## Section 69

[069-001]At the end of the fortnight, on the morning of the day on which

[069-002]Brooke Burgess was expected back, Dorothy, slowly moving into the

[069-003]sitting room with her usual melancholy air, found Mr. Gibson talking

[069-004]to her aunt. "There she is herself," said Miss Stanbury, jumping

[069-005]up briskly, "and now you can speak to her. Of course I have no

[069-006]authority,--none in the least. But she knows what my wishes are."

[069-007]And, having so spoken, Miss Stanbury left the room.

[069-008]It will be remembered that hitherto no word of affection had been

[069-009]whispered by Mr. Gibson into Dorothy's ears. When he came before to

[069-010]press his suit, she had been made aware of his coming, and had fled,

[069-011]leaving her answer with her aunt. Mr. Gibson had then expressed

[069-012]himself as somewhat injured, in that no opportunity of pouring forth

[069-013]his own eloquence had been permitted to him. On that occasion Miss

[069-014]Stanbury, being in a snubbing humour, had snubbed him. She had in

[069-015]truth scolded him almost as much as she had scolded Dorothy, telling

[069-016]him that he went about the business in hand as though butter wouldn't

[069-017]melt in his mouth. "You're stiff as a chair-back," she had said to

[069-018]him, with a few other compliments, and these amenities had for a

[069-019]while made him regard the establishment at Heavitree as being, at

[069-020]any rate, pleasanter than that in the Close. But since that cool

[069-021]reflection had come. The proposal was not that he should marry Miss

[069-022]Stanbury, senior, who certainly could be severe on occasions, but

[069-023]Miss Stanbury, junior, whose temper was as sweet as primroses in

[069-024]March. That which he would have to take from Miss Stanbury, senior,

[069-025]was a certain sum of money, as to which her promise was as good as

[069-026]any bond in the world. Things had come to such a pass with him in

[069-027][[exeter|Exeter]],--from the hints of his friend the Prebend, from a word or two

[069-028]which had come to him from the Dean, from certain family arrangements

[069-029]proposed to him by his mother and sisters,--things had come to such

[069-030]a pass that he was of a mind that he had better marry some one. He

[069-031]had, as it were, three strings to his bow. There were the two French

[069-032]strings, and there was Dorothy. He had not breadth of genius enough

[069-033]to suggest to himself that yet another woman might be found. There

[069-034]was a difficulty on the French score even about Miss Stanbury; but

[069-035]it was clear to him that, failing her, he was due to one of the

[069-036]two Miss Frenches. Now it was not only that the Miss Frenches were

[069-037]empty-handed, but he was beginning to think himself that they were

[069-038]not as nice as they might have been in reference to the arrangement

[069-039]of their head-gear. Therefore, having given much thought to the

[069-040]matter, and remembering that he had never yet had play for his own

[069-041]eloquence with Dorothy, he had come to Miss Stanbury asking that he

[069-042]might have another chance. It had been borne in upon him that he had

[069-043]perhaps hitherto regarded Dorothy as too certainly his own since she

[069-044]had been offered to him by her aunt,--as being a prize that required

[069-045]no eloquence in the winning; and he thought that if he could have an

[069-046]opportunity of amending that fault, it might even yet be well with

[069-047]his suit. So he prepared himself, and asked permission, and now found

[069-048]himself alone with the young lady.

[069-049]"When last I was in this house, Miss Stanbury," he began, "I was not

[069-050]fortunate enough to be allowed an opportunity of pleading my cause to

[069-051]yourself." Then he paused, and Dorothy was left to consider how best

[069-052]she might answer him. All that her aunt had said to her had not been

[069-053]thrown away upon her. The calls upon that slender meal-tub at home

[069-054]she knew were quite sufficient. And Mr. Gibson was, she believed, a

[069-055]good man. And how better could she dispose of herself in life? And

[069-056]what was she that she should scorn the love of an honest [[social-class|gentleman]]?

[069-057]She would take him, she thought,--if she could. But then there came

[069-058]upon her, unconsciously, without work of thought, by instinct rather

[069-059]than by intelligence, a feeling of the closeness of a wife to her

[069-060]husband. Looking at it in general she could not deny that it would

[069-061]be very proper that she should become Mrs. Gibson. But when there

[069-062]came upon her a remembrance that she would be called upon for

[069-063]demonstration of her love,--that he would embrace her, and hold her

[069-064]to his heart, and kiss her,--she revolted and shuddered. She believed

[069-065]that she did not want to marry any man, and that such a state of

[069-066]things would not be good for her. "Dear young lady," continued Mr.

[069-067]Gibson, "you will let me now make up for the loss which I then

[069-068]experienced?"

[069-069]"I thought it was better not to give you trouble," said Dorothy.

[069-070]"Trouble, Miss Stanbury! How could it be trouble? The labour we

[069-071]delight in physics pain. But to go back to the subject-matter. I hope

[069-072]you do not doubt that my affection for you is true and honest, and

[069-073]genuine."

[069-074]"I don't want to doubt anything, Mr. Gibson; but--"

[069-075]"You needn't, dearest Miss Stanbury; indeed you needn't. If you

[069-076]could read my heart you would see written there true love very

[069-077]plainly;--very plainly. And do you not think it a duty that people

[069-078]should marry?" It may be surmised that he had here forgotten some

[069-079]connecting link which should have joined without abruptness the

[069-080]declaration of his own love, and his social view as to the general

[069-081]expediency of [[marriage|matrimony]]. But Dorothy did not discover the hiatus.

[069-082]"Certainly,--when they like each other, and if their friends think it

[069-083]proper."

[069-084]"Our friends think it proper, Miss Stanbury,--may I say Dorothy?--all

[069-085]of them. I can assure you that on my side you will be welcomed by a

[069-086]mother and sisters only too anxious to receive you with open arms.

[069-087]And as regards your own relations, I need hardly allude to your

[069-088]revered aunt. As to your own mother and sister,--and your brother,

[069-089]who, I believe, gives his mind chiefly to other things,--I am assured

[069-090]by Miss Stanbury that no opposition need be feared from them. Is that

[069-091]true, dearest Dorothy?"

[069-092]"It is true."

[069-093]"Does not all that plead in my behalf? Tell me, Dorothy."

[069-094]"Of course it does."

[069-095]"And you will be mine?" As far as eloquence could be of service, Mr.

[069-096]Gibson was sufficiently eloquent. To Dorothy his words appeared good,

[069-097]and true, and affecting. All their friends did wish it. There were

[069-098]many reasons why it should be done. If talking could have done it,

[069-099]his talking was good enough. Though his words were in truth cold,

[069-100]and affected, and learned by rote, they did not offend her; but his

[069-101]face offended her; and the feeling was strong within her that if she

[069-102]yielded, it would soon be close to her own. She couldn't do it. She

[069-103]didn't love him, and she wouldn't do it. Priscilla would not grudge

[069-104]her her share out of that meagre meal-tub. Had not Priscilla told her

[069-105]not to marry the man if she did not love him? She found that she was

[069-106]further than ever from loving him. She would not do it. "Say that you

[069-107]will be mine," pleaded Mr. Gibson, coming to her with both his hands

[069-108]outstretched.

[069-109]"Mr. Gibson, I can't," she said. She was sobbing now, and was half

[069-110]choked by tears.

[069-111]"And why not, Dorothy?"

[069-112]"I don't know, but I can't. I don't feel that I want to be married at

[069-113]all."

[069-114]"But it is honourable."

[069-115]"It's no use, Mr. Gibson; I can't, and you oughtn't to ask me any

[069-116]more."

[069-117]"Must this be your very last answer?"

[069-118]"What's the good of going over it all again and again? I can't do

[069-119]it."

[069-120]"Never, Miss Stanbury?"

[069-121]"No;--never."

[069-122]"That is cruel, very cruel. I fear that you doubt my love."

[069-123]"It isn't cruel, Mr. Gibson. I have a right to have my own feelings,

[069-124]and I can't. If you please, I'll go away now." Then she went, and

[069-125]he was left [[reputation|standing]] alone in the room. His first feeling was one

[069-126]of anger. Then there came to be mixed with that a good deal of

[069-127]wonder,--and then a certain amount of doubt. He had during the last

[069-128]fortnight discussed the matter at great length with a friend, a

[069-129]gentleman who knew the world, and who took upon himself to say that

[069-130]he specially understood female nature. It was by advice from this

[069-131]friend that he had been instigated to plead his own cause. "Of course

[069-132]she means to accept you," the friend had said. "Why the mischief

[069-133]shouldn't she? But she has some flimsy, old-fashioned country idea

[069-134]that it isn't maidenly to give in at first. You tell her roundly that

[069-135]she must marry you." Mr. Gibson was just reaching that roundness

[069-136]which his friend had recommended when the lady left him and he was

[069-137]alone.

[069-138]Mr. Gibson was no doubt very much in love with Dorothy Stanbury. So

[069-139]much, we may take for granted. He, at least, believed that he was in

[069-140]love with her. He would have thought it wicked to propose to her had

[069-141]he not been in love with her. But with his love was mingled a certain

[069-142]amount of contempt which had induced him to look upon her as an easy

[069-143]conquest. He had been perhaps a little ashamed of himself for being

[069-144]in love with Dorothy, and had almost believed the Frenches when they

[069-145]had spoken of her as a poor creature, a dependant, one born to be

[069-146]snubbed,--as a young woman almost without an identity of her own.

[069-147]When, therefore, she so pertinaciously refused him, he could not but

[069-148]be angry. And it was natural that he should be surprised. Though he

[069-149]was to have received a [[money|fortune]] with Dorothy, the money was not hers.

[069-150]It was to be hers,--or rather theirs,--only if she would accept him.

[069-151]Mr. Gibson thoroughly understood this point. He knew that Dorothy had

[069-152]nothing of her own. The proposal made to her was as rich as though

[069-153]he had sought her down at [[nuncombe-putney|Nuncombe Putney]], with his preferment, plus

[069-154]the L2,000, in his own pocket. And his other advantages were not

[069-155]hidden from his own eyes. He was a clergyman, well thought of, not

[069-156]bad-looking certainly, considerably under forty,--a man, indeed, who

[069-157]ought to have been, in the eyes of Dorothy, such an Orlando as she

[069-158]would have most desired. He could not therefore but wonder. And then

[069-159]came the doubt. Could it be possible that all those refusals were

[069-160]simply the early pulses of hesitating compliance produced by maidenly

[069-161]reserve? Mr. Gibson's friend had expressed a strong opinion that

[069-162]almost any young woman would accept any young man if he put his

[069-163]"com 'ether" upon her strong enough. For Mr. Gibson's friend was an

[069-164]Irishman. As to Dorothy the friend had not a doubt in the world. Mr.

[069-165]Gibson, as he stood alone in the room after Dorothy's departure,

[069-166]could not share his friend's [[truth|certainty]]; but he thought it just

[069-167]possible that the pulsations of maidenly reserve were yet at work. As

[069-168]he was revolving these points in his mind, Miss Stanbury entered the

[069-169]room.

[069-170]"It's all over now," she said.

[069-171]"As how, Miss Stanbury?"

[069-172]"As how! She's given you an answer; hasn't she?"

[069-173]"Yes, Miss Stanbury, she has given me an answer. But it has occurred

[069-174]to me that young ladies are sometimes,--perhaps a little--"

[069-175]"She means it, Mr. Gibson; you may take my word for that. She is

[069-176]quite in earnest. She can take the bit between her teeth as well as

[069-177]another, though she does look so mild and gentle. She's a Stanbury

[069-178]all over."

[069-179]"And must this be the last of it, Miss Stanbury?"

[069-180]"Upon my word, I don't know what else you can do,--unless you send

[069-181]the Dean and Chapter to talk her over. She's a pig-headed, foolish

[069-182]young woman;--but I can't help that. The truth is, you didn't make

[069-183]enough of her at first, Mr. Gibson. You thought the plum would tumble

[069-184]into your mouth."
