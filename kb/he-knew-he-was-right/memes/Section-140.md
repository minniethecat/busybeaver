## Section 140

[140-001]"Mamma," she said, the day before they went to Florence, "has papa

[140-002]fixed about your leaving England yet? I suppose you'll go now on the

[140-003]last Saturday in July?"

[140-004]"I suppose we shall, my dear."

[140-005]"Has not papa written about the berths?"

[140-006]"I believe he has, my dear."

[140-007]"Because he ought to know who are going. I will not go."

[140-008]"You will not, Nora. Is that a proper way of speaking?"

[140-009]"Dear mamma, I mean it to be proper. I hope it is proper. But is it

[140-010]not best that we should understand each other? All my life depends on

[140-011]my going or my staying now. I must decide."

[140-012]"After what has passed, you do not, I suppose, mean to live in Mr.

[140-013]Glascock's house?"

[140-014]"Certainly not. I mean to live with,--with,--with my husband. Mamma,

[140-015]I promised not to write, and I have not written. And he has not

[140-016]written,--because I told him not. Therefore, nothing is settled. But

[140-017]it is not fair to throw it in my teeth that nothing is settled."

[140-018]"I have thrown nothing in your teeth, Nora."

[140-019]"Papa talks sneeringly about chairs and tables. Of course, I know

[140-020]what he is thinking of. As I cannot go with him to the Mandarins, I

[140-021]think I ought to be allowed to look after the chairs and tables."

[140-022]"What do you mean, my dear?"

[140-023]"That you should absolve me from my promise, and let me write to Mr.

[140-024]Stanbury. I do not want to be left without a home."

[140-025]"You cannot wish to write to a [[social-class|gentleman]] and ask him to marry you!"

[140-026]"Why not? We are engaged. I shall not ask him to marry me,--that is

[140-027]already settled; but I shall ask him to make arrangements."

[140-028]"Your papa will be very angry if you break your word to him."

[140-029]"I will write, and show you the letter. Papa may see it, and if he

[140-030]will not let it go, it shall not go. He shall not say that I broke my

[140-031]word. But, mamma, I will not go out to the Islands. I should never

[140-032]get back again, and I should be broken-hearted." Lady Rowley had

[140-033]nothing to say to this; and Nora went and wrote her letter. "Dear

[140-034]Hugh," the letter ran, "Papa and mamma leave England on the last

[140-035]Saturday in July. I have told mamma that I cannot return with them.

[140-036]Of course, you know why I stay. Mr. Glascock is to be married the day

[140-037]after to-morrow, and they have asked me to go with them to Monkhams

[140-038]some time in August. I think I shall do so, unless Emily wants me to

[140-039]remain with her. At any rate, I shall try to be with her till I go

[140-040]there. You will understand why I tell you all this. Papa and mamma

[140-041]know that I am writing. It is only a business letter, and, therefore,

[140-042]I shall say no more, except that I am ever and always yours,--NORA."

[140-043]"There," she said, handing her letter to her mother, "I think that

[140-044]that ought to be sent. If papa chooses to prevent its going, he can."

[140-045]Lady Rowley, when she handed the letter to her husband, recommended

[140-046]that it should be allowed to go to its destination. She admitted

[140-047]that, if they sent it, they would thereby signify their consent to

[140-048]her engagement;--and she alleged that Nora was so strong in her will,

[140-049]and that the circumstances of their journey out to the Antipodes were

[140-050]so peculiar, that it was of no avail for them any longer to oppose

[140-051]the match. They could not force their daughter to go with them.

[140-052]"But I can cast her off from me, if she be disobedient," said Sir

[140-053]Marmaduke. Lady Rowley, however, had no desire that her daughter

[140-054]should be cast off, and was aware that Sir Marmaduke, when it came

[140-055]to the point of casting off, would be as little inclined to be stern

[140-056]as she was herself. Sir Marmaduke, still hoping that firmness would

[140-057]carry the day, and believing that it behoved him to maintain his

[140-058]parental authority, ended the discussion by keeping possession of the

[140-059]letter, and saying that he would take time to consider the matter.

[140-060]"What security have we that he will ever marry her, if she does

[140-061]stay?" he asked the next morning. Lady Rowley had no doubt on this

[140-062]score, and protested that her opposition to [[hugh-stanbury|Hugh Stanbury]] arose

[140-063]simply from his want of [[money|income]]. "I should never be justified," said

[140-064]Sir Marmaduke, "if I were to go and leave my girl as it were in the

[140-065]hands of a penny-a-liner." The letter, in the end, was not sent; and

[140-066]Nora and her father hardly spoke to each other as they made their

[140-067]journey back to Florence together.

[140-068][[emily-trevelyan|Emily Trevelyan]], before the arrival of that letter from her husband,

[140-069]had determined that she would not leave Italy. It had been her

[140-070]purpose to remain somewhere in the neighbourhood of her husband and

[140-071]child; and to overcome her difficulties,--or be overcome by them, as

[140-072]circumstances might direct. Now her plans were again changed,--or,

[140-073]rather, she was now without a plan. She could form no plan till

[140-074]she should again see Mr. Glascock. Should her child be restored to

[140-075]her, would it not be her duty to remain near her husband? All this

[140-076]made Nora's line of conduct the more difficult for her. It was

[140-077]acknowledged that she could not remain in Italy. Mrs. Trevelyan's

[140-078]position would be most embarrassing; but as all her efforts were to

[140-079]be used towards a reconciliation with her husband, and as his state

[140-080]utterly precluded the idea of a mixed household,--of any such a

[140-081]family arrangement as that which had existed in Curzon Street,--Nora

[140-082]could not remain with her. Mrs. Trevelyan herself had declared that

[140-083]she would not wish it. And, in that case, where was Nora to bestow

[140-084]herself when Sir Marmaduke and Lady Rowley had sailed? Caroline

[140-085]offered to curtail those honeymoon weeks in Switzerland, but it was

[140-086]impossible to listen to an offer so magnanimous and so unreasonable.

[140-087]Nora had a dim romantic idea of sharing Priscilla's bed-room in that

[140-088]small cottage near [[nuncombe-putney|Nuncombe Putney]], of which she had heard, and of

[140-089]there learning lessons in strict economy;--but of this she said

[140-090]nothing. The short journey from the Baths of Lucca to Florence was

[140-091]not a pleasant one, and the Rowley family were much disturbed as they

[140-092]looked into the future. Lodgings had now been taken for them, and

[140-093]there was the great additional doubt whether Mrs. Trevelyan would

[140-094]find her child there on her arrival.

[140-095]The Spaldings went one way from the Florence station, and the Rowleys

[140-096]another. The American Minister had returned to the city some days

[140-097]previously,--drawn there nominally by pleas of business, but, in

[140-098]truth, by the necessities of the wedding breakfast,--and he met them

[140-099]at the station. "Has Mr. Glascock come back?" Nora was the first to

[140-100]ask. Yes;--he had come. He had been in the city since two o'clock,

[140-101]and had been up at the American Minister's house for half a minute.

[140-102]"And has he brought the child?" asked Caroline, relieved of doubt

[140-103]on her own account. Mr. Spalding did not know;--indeed, he had not

[140-104]interested himself quite so intently about Mrs. Trevelyan's little

[140-105]boy, as had all those who had just returned from the Baths. Mr.

[140-106]Glascock had said nothing to him about the child, and he had not

[140-107]quite understood why such a man should have made a journey to Siena,

[140-108]leaving his sweetheart behind him, just on the eve of his marriage.

[140-109]He hurried his women-kind into their carriage, and they were driven

[140-110]away; and then Sir Marmaduke was driven away with his women-kind.

[140-111]Caroline Spalding had perhaps thought that Mr. Glascock might have

[140-112]been there to meet her.

[140-113]CHAPTER LXXXVI.

[140-114]MR. GLASCOCK AS NURSE.

[140-115]A message had been sent by the wires to Trevelyan, to let him know

[140-116]that Mr. Glascock was himself coming for the boy. Whether such

[140-117]message would or would not be sent out to Casalunga Mr. Glascock had

[140-118]been quite ignorant;--but it could, at any rate, do no harm. He did

[140-119]feel it hard as in this hot weather he made the journey, first to

[140-120]Florence, and then on to Siena. What was he to the Rowleys, or to

[140-121]Trevelyan himself, that such a job of work should fall to his lot at

[140-122]such a period of his life? He had been very much in love with Nora,

[140-123]no doubt; but, luckily for him, as he thought, Nora had refused him.

[140-124]As for Trevelyan,--Trevelyan had never been his friend. As for Sir

[140-125]Marmaduke,--Sir Marmaduke was nothing to him. He was almost angry

[140-126]even with Mrs. Trevelyan as he arrived tired, heated, and very dusty,

[140-127]at Siena. It was his purpose to sleep at Siena that night, and to

[140-128]go out to Casalunga early the next morning. If the telegram had not

[140-129]been forwarded, he would send a message on that evening. On inquiry,

[140-130]however, he found that the message had been sent, and that the paper

[140-131]had been put into the Signore's own hand by the Sienese messenger.

[140-132]Then he got into some discourse with the landlord about the strange

[140-133]gentleman at Casalunga. Trevelyan was beginning to become the subject

[140-134]of [[scandal|gossip]] in the town, and people were saying that the stranger was

[140-135]very strange indeed. The landlord thought that if the Signore had

[140-136]any friends at all, it would be well that such friends should come

[140-137]and look after him. Mr. Glascock asked if Mr. Trevelyan was ill. It

[140-138]was not only that the Signore was out of health,--so the landlord

[140-139]heard,--but that he was also somewhat-- And then the landlord touched

[140-140]his head. He eat nothing, and went nowhere, and spoke to no one; and

[140-141]the people at the hospital to which Casalunga belonged were beginning

[140-142]to be uneasy about their tenant. Perhaps Mr. Glascock had come to

[140-143]take him away. Mr. Glascock explained that he had not come to take

[140-144]Mr. Trevelyan away,--but only to take away a little boy that was with

[140-145]him. For this reason he was travelling with a maid-servant,--a fact

[140-146]for which Mr. Glascock clearly thought it necessary that he should

[140-147]give an intelligible and credible explanation. The landlord seemed to

[140-148]think that the people at the hospital would have been much rejoiced

[140-149]had Mr. Glascock intended to take Mr. Trevelyan away also.

[140-150]He started after a very early breakfast, and found himself walking

[140-151]up over the stone ridges to the house between nine and ten in the

[140-152]morning. He himself had sat beside the driver and had put the maid

[140-153]inside the carriage. He had not deemed it wise to take an undivided

[140-154]charge of the boy even from Casalunga to Siena. At the door of the

[140-155]house, as though waiting for him, he found Trevelyan, not dirty as he

[140-156]had been before, but dressed with much appearance of smartness. He

[140-157]had a brocaded cap on his head, and a shirt with a laced front, and a

[140-158]worked waistcoat, and a frock coat, and coloured bright trousers. Mr.

[140-159]Glascock knew at once that all the clothes which he saw before him

[140-160]had been made for Italian and not for English wear; and could almost

[140-161]have said that they had been bought in Siena and not in Florence.

[140-162]"I had not intended to impose this labour on you, Mr. Glascock,"

[140-163]Trevelyan said, raising his cap to salute his visitor.

[140-164]"For fear there might be mistakes, I thought it better to come

[140-165]myself," said Mr. Glascock. "You did not wish to see Sir Marmaduke?"

[140-166]"Certainly not Sir Marmaduke," said Trevelyan, with a look of anger

[140-167]that was almost grotesque.

[140-168]"And you thought it better that Mrs. Trevelyan should not come."

[140-169]"Yes;--I thought it better;--but not from any feeling of anger

[140-170]towards her. If I could welcome my wife here, Mr. Glascock, without

[140-171]a risk of wrath on her part, I should be very happy to receive her.

[140-172]I love my wife, Mr. Glascock. I love her dearly. But there have been

[140-173]misfortunes. Never mind. There is no reason why I should trouble you

[140-174]with them. Let us go in to breakfast. After your drive you will have

[140-175]an appetite."
