# Reconciliation

In *He Knew He Was Right*, reconciliation between Louis and Emily Trevelyan is repeatedly attempted and repeatedly frustrated. The novel traces a series of moments when the marriage might have been repaired — and shows how pride, jealousy, and the interventions of others consistently prevent the healing that both parties intermittently desire.

## Overview

The possibility of reconciliation hangs over the Trevelyan separation from its earliest days. Friends and family urge it; both Louis and Emily, at different moments, wish for it. Yet each attempt at reconciliation founders, not on any single insurmountable obstacle, but on the accumulated weight of wounded pride, mutual suspicion, and the structural inequality of Victorian marriage (003-101).

## Context in Victorian Society

In Victorian England, marital separation carried profound social stigma. Unlike modern divorce, a separation did not dissolve the marriage — it suspended it in a state of legal and social ambiguity. Reconciliation was the preferred outcome, urged by clergy, family, and social convention alike. Yet the very conditions that made reconciliation desirable also made it difficult: a wife who returned to her husband returned to his authority, and a husband who accepted his wife's return implicitly acknowledged that the separation had been unjustified.

## Manifestation in the Novel

The novel's treatment of reconciliation is notable for its psychological realism. Trollope does not offer a simple narrative of estrangement followed by reunion. Instead, he depicts reconciliation as a fragile, contingent process — one that almost succeeds on several occasions before failing definitively.

Early in the separation, "before dinner-time a reconciliation had been effected" (009-138), only to collapse again when the underlying issues resurface. The pattern repeats throughout the novel: moments of emotional openness are followed by renewed suspicion, and each failed reconciliation makes the next less likely.

## Key Examples

### The First Reconciliation

After the initial breach, a reconciliation is achieved through the intervention of family members: "They were brought together" and a reconciliation was made (009-148). The illustration captioned "Showing how reconciliation was made" (009-152) marks this as a key turning point — but the reconciliation proves temporary, demonstrating that formal reconciliation without resolved grievances is fragile.

### Failed Interventions

Lady Milborough and others attempt to bring about a reconciliation, but their interventions are ineffective. Trevelyan's suspicion and Emily's pride combine to "render any steps taken for a reconciliation ineffectual" (022-002). The novel suggests that reconciliation cannot be imposed from outside — it requires a mutual surrender that neither party is willing to make.
