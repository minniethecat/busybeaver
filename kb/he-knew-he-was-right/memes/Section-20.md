## Section 20

[020-001]"It will be better that she should go," said Trevelyan, when he found

[020-002]himself alone. And so it came to pass that that blessing of a rich

[020-003]marriage, which had as it were fallen upon them at the Mandarins from

[020-004]out of heaven, had become, after an interval of but two short years,

[020-005]anything but an unmixed blessing.

[020-006]CHAPTER XII.

[020-007]MISS STANBURY'S GENEROSITY.

[020-008]On one Wednesday morning early in June, great preparations were being

[020-009]made at the brick house in the Close at [[exeter|Exeter]] for an event which can

[020-010]hardly be said to have required any preparation at all. Mrs. Stanbury

[020-011]and her elder daughter were coming into Exeter from [[nuncombe-putney|Nuncombe Putney]]

[020-012]to visit Dorothy. The reader may perhaps remember that when Miss

[020-013]Stanbury's invitation was sent to her niece, she was pleased to

[020-014]promise that such visits should be permitted on a Wednesday morning.

[020-015]Such a visit was now to be made, and old Miss Stanbury was quite

[020-016]moved by the occasion. "I shall not see them, you know, Martha," she

[020-017]had said, on the afternoon of the preceding day.

[020-018]"I suppose not, ma'am."

[020-019]"Certainly not. Why should I? It would do no good."

[020-020]"It is not for me to say, ma'am, of course."

[020-021]"No, Martha, it is not. And I am sure that I am right. It's no good

[020-022]going back and undoing in ten minutes what twenty years have done.

[020-023]She's a poor harmless creature, I believe."

[020-024]"The most harmless in the world, ma'am."

[020-025]"But she was as bad as poison to me when she was young, and what's

[020-026]the good of trying to change it now? If I was to tell her that I

[020-027]loved her, I should only be lying."

[020-028]"Then, ma'am, I would not say it."

[020-029]"And I don't mean. But you'll take in some wine and cake, you know."

[020-030]"I don't think they'll care for wine and cake."

[020-031]"Will you do as I tell you? What matters whether they care for it or

[020-032]not? They need not take it. It will look better for Miss Dorothy.

[020-033]If Dorothy is to remain here I shall choose that she should be

[020-034]respected." And so the question of the cake and wine had been decided

[020-035]overnight. But when the morning came Miss Stanbury was still in

[020-036]a twitter. Half-past ten had been the hour fixed for the visit,

[020-037]in consequence of there being a train in from Lessboro', due at

[020-038]the Exeter [[social-class|station]] at ten. As Miss Stanbury breakfasted always

[020-039]at half-past eight, there was no need of hurry on account of the

[020-040]expected visit. But, nevertheless, she was in a fuss all the morning;

[020-041]and spoke of the coming period as one in which she must necessarily

[020-042]put herself into solitary confinement.

[020-043]"Perhaps your mamma will be cold," she said, "and will expect a

[020-044]fire."

[020-045]"Oh, dear, no, Aunt Stanbury."

[020-046]"It could be lighted of course. It is a pity they should come just so

[020-047]as to prevent you from going to morning service; is it not?"

[020-048]"I could go with you, aunt, and be back very nearly in time. They

[020-049]won't mind waiting a quarter of an hour."

[020-050]"What; and have them here all alone! I wouldn't think of such a

[020-051]thing. I shall go up-stairs. You had better come to me when they are

[020-052]gone. Don't hurry them. I don't want you to hurry them at all; and

[020-053]if you require anything, Martha will wait upon you. I have told the

[020-054]girls to keep out of the way. They are so giddy, there's no knowing

[020-055]what they might be after. Besides,--they've got their work to mind."

[020-056]All this was very terrible to poor Dorothy, who had not as yet quite

[020-057]recovered from the original fear with which her aunt had inspired

[020-058]her,--so terrible that she was almost sorry that her mother and

[020-059]sister were coming to her. When the knock was heard at the door,

[020-060]precisely as the cathedral clock was striking half-past ten,--to

[020-061]secure which punctuality, and thereby not to offend the owner of the

[020-062]mansion, Mrs. Stanbury and Priscilla had been walking about the Close

[020-063]for the last ten minutes,--Miss Stanbury was still in the parlour.

[020-064]"There they are!" she exclaimed, jumping up. "They haven't given

[020-065]a body much time to run away, have they, my dear? Half a minute,

[020-066]Martha,--just half a minute!" Then she gathered up her things as

[020-067]though she had been ill-treated in being driven to make so sudden a

[020-068]retreat, and Martha, as soon as the last hem of her mistress's dress

[020-069]had become invisible on the stairs, opened the front door for the

[020-070]visitors.

[020-071]"Do you mean to say you like it?" said Priscilla, when they had been

[020-072]there about a quarter of an hour.

[020-073]"H--u--sh," whispered Mrs. Stanbury.

[020-074]"I don't suppose she's listening at the door," said Priscilla.

[020-075]"Indeed, she's not," said Dorothy. "There can't be a truer, honester

[020-076]woman, than Aunt Stanbury."

[020-077]"But is she kind to you, Dolly?" asked the mother.

[020-078]"Very kind; too kind. Only I don't understand her quite, and then

[020-079]she gets angry with me. I know she thinks I'm a fool, and that's the

[020-080]worst of it."

[020-081]"Then, if I were you, I would come home," said Priscilla.

[020-082]"She'll never forgive you if you do," said Mrs. Stanbury.

[020-083]"And who need care about her forgiveness?" said Priscilla.

[020-084]"I don't mean to go home yet, at any rate," said Dorothy. Then there

[020-085]was a knock at the door, and Martha entered with the cake and wine.

[020-086]"Miss Stanbury's compliments, ladies, and she hopes you'll take a

[020-087]glass of sherry." Whereupon she filled out the glasses and carried

[020-088]them round.

[020-089]"Pray give my compliments and thanks to my sister Stanbury," said

[020-090]Dorothy's mother. But Priscilla put down the glass of wine without

[020-091]touching it, and looked her sternest at the maid.

[020-092]Altogether, the visit was not very successful, and poor Dorothy

[020-093]almost felt that if she chose to remain in the Close she must lose

[020-094]her mother and sister, and that without really making a friend of

[020-095]her aunt. There had as yet been no quarrel,--nothing that had been

[020-096]plainly recognised as disagreeable; but there had not as yet come to

[020-097]be any sympathy, or assured signs of comfortable love. Miss Stanbury

[020-098]had declared more than once that it would do, but had not succeeded

[020-099]in showing in what the success consisted. When she was told that the

[020-100]two ladies were gone, she desired that Dorothy might be sent to her,

[020-101]and immediately began to make anxious inquiries.

[020-102]"Well, my dear, and what do they think of it?"

[020-103]"I don't know, aunt, that they think very much."

[020-104]"And what do they say about it?"

[020-105]"They didn't say very much, aunt. I was very glad to see mamma and

[020-106]Priscilla. Perhaps I ought to tell you that mamma gave me back the

[020-107]money I sent her."

[020-108]"What did she do that for?" asked Miss Stanbury very sharply.

[020-109]"Because she says that Hugh sends her now what she wants." Miss

[020-110]Stanbury, when she heard this, looked very sour. "I thought it best

[020-111]to tell you, you know."

[020-112]"It will never come to any good, got in that way,--never."

[020-113]"But, Aunt Stanbury, isn't it good of him to send it?"

[020-114]"I don't know. I suppose it's better than drinking, and smoking, and

[020-115]gambling. But I dare say he gets enough for that too. When a man,

[020-116]born and bred like a gentleman, condescends to let out his talents

[020-117]and education for such purposes, I dare say they are willing enough

[020-118]to pay him. The devil always does pay high wages. But that only makes

[020-119]it so much the worse. One almost comes to doubt whether any one ought

[020-120]to learn to write at all, when it is used for such vile purposes.

[020-121]I've said what I've got to say, and I don't mean to say anything

[020-122]more. What's the use? But it has been hard upon me,--very. It was my

[020-123]money did it, and I feel I've misused it. It's a disgrace to me which

[020-124]I don't deserve."

[020-125]For a couple of minutes Dorothy remained quite silent, and Miss

[020-126]Stanbury did not herself say anything further. Nor during that time

[020-127]did she observe her niece, or she would probably have seen that the

[020-128]subject was not to be dropped. Dorothy, though she was silent, was

[020-129]not calm, and was preparing herself for a crusade in her brother's

[020-130]defence.

[020-131]"Aunt Stanbury, he's my brother, you know."

[020-132]"Of course he's your brother. I wish he were not."

[020-133]"I think him the best brother in the world,--and the best son."

[020-134]"Why does he sell himself to write sedition?"

[020-135]"He doesn't sell himself to write sedition. I don't see why it should

[020-136]be sedition, or anything wicked, because it's sold for a penny."

[020-137]"If you are going to cram him down my throat, Dorothy, you and I had

[020-138]better part."

[020-139]"I don't want to say anything about him, only you ought--not--to

[020-140]abuse him--before me." By this time Dorothy was beginning to sob,

[020-141]but Miss Stanbury's countenance was still very grim and very stern.

[020-142]"He's coming home to Nuncombe Putney, and I want to--see--see him,"

[020-143]continued Dorothy.

[020-144]"[[hugh-stanbury|Hugh Stanbury]] coming to Exeter! He won't come here."

[020-145]"Then I'd rather go home, Aunt Stanbury."

[020-146]"Very well, very well," said Miss Stanbury, and she got up and left

[020-147]the room.

[020-148]Dorothy was in dismay, and began to think that there was nothing for

[020-149]her to do but to pack up her clothes and prepare for her departure.

[020-150]She was very sorry for what had occurred, being fully alive to the

[020-151]importance of the aid not only to herself, but to her mother and

[020-152]sister, which was afforded by the present arrangement, and she felt

[020-153]very angry with herself, in that she had already driven her aunt to

[020-154]quarrel with her. But she had found it to be impossible to hear her

[020-155]own brother abused without saying a word on his behalf. She did not

[020-156]see her aunt again till dinner-time, and then there was hardly a word

[020-157]uttered. Once or twice Dorothy made a little effort to speak, but

[020-158]these attempts failed utterly. The old woman would hardly reply even

[020-159]by a monosyllable, but simply muttered something, or shook her head

[020-160]when she was addressed. Jane, who waited at table, was very demure

[020-161]and silent, and Martha, who once came into the room during the meal,

[020-162]merely whispered a word into Miss Stanbury's ear. When the cloth

[020-163]was removed, and two glasses of port had been poured out by Miss

[020-164]Stanbury herself, Dorothy felt that she could endure this treatment

[020-165]no longer. How was it possible that she could drink wine under such

[020-166]circumstances?

[020-167][Illustration: Aunt Stanbury at dinner will not speak.]

[020-168]"Not for me, Aunt Stanbury," said she, with a deploring tone.

[020-169]"Why not?"

[020-170]"I couldn't drink it to-day."

[020-171]"Why didn't you say so before it was poured out? And why not to-day?

[020-172]Come, drink it. Do as I bid you." And she stood over her niece, as a

[020-173]tragedy queen in a play with a bowl of poison. Dorothy took it and

[020-174]sipped it from mere force of obedience. "You make as many bones about

[020-175]a glass of port wine as though it were senna and salts," said Miss

[020-176]Stanbury. "Now I've got something to say to you." By this time the

[020-177]servant was gone, and the two were seated alone together in the

[020-178]parlour. Dorothy, who had not as yet swallowed above half her wine,

[020-179]at once put the glass down. There was an importance in her aunt's

[020-180]tone which frightened her, and made her feel that some evil was

[020-181]coming. And yet, as she had made up her mind that she must return

[020-182]home, there was no further evil that she need dread. "You didn't

[020-183]write any of those horrid articles?" said Miss Stanbury.

[020-184]"No, aunt; I didn't write them. I shouldn't know how."

[020-185]"And I hope you'll never learn. They say women are to vote, and

[020-186]become doctors, and if so, there's no knowing what devil's tricks

[020-187]they mayn't do. But it isn't your fault about that filthy newspaper.

[020-188]How he can let himself down to write stuff that is to be printed on

[020-189]straw is what I can't understand."
