## Section 125

[125-001]"He is between thirty and forty," said Lady Rowley. "Of course we

[125-002]know that from the peerage." Lady Rowley, however, was wrong. Had she

[125-003]consulted the peerage, she would have seen that Mr. Glascock was over

[125-004]forty.

[125-005]Nora, as soon as she was alone and could think about it all, felt

[125-006]quite sure that Mr. Glascock would never make her another offer. This

[125-007]ought not to have caused her any sorrow, as she was very well aware

[125-008]that she would not accept him, should he do so. Yet, perhaps, there

[125-009]was a moment of some feeling akin to disappointment. Of course she

[125-010]would not have accepted him. How could she? Her faith was so plighted

[125-011]to [[hugh-stanbury|Hugh Stanbury]] that she would be a by-word among women for ever,

[125-012]were she to be so false. And as she told herself, she had not the

[125-013]slightest feeling of affection for Mr. Glascock. It was quite out of

[125-014]the question, and a matter simply for speculation. Nevertheless it

[125-015]would have been a very grand thing to be Lady Peterborough, and she

[125-016]almost regretted that she had a heart in her bosom.

[125-017]She had become fully aware during that interview that her mother

[125-018]still entertained hopes, and almost suspected that Lady Rowley had

[125-019]known something of Mr. Glascock's residence in Florence. She had

[125-020]seen that her mother had met Mr. Glascock almost as though some such

[125-021]meeting had been expected, and had spoken to him almost as though she

[125-022]had expected to have to speak to him. Would it not be better that she

[125-023]should at once make her mother understand that all this could be of

[125-024]no avail? If she were to declare plainly that nothing could bring

[125-025]about such a marriage, would not her mother desist? She almost made

[125-026]up her mind to do so; but as her mother said nothing to her before

[125-027]they started for Mr. Spalding's house, neither did she say anything

[125-028]to her mother. She did not wish to have angry words if they could be

[125-029]avoided, and she felt that there might be anger and unpleasant words

[125-030]were she to insist upon her devotion to Hugh Stanbury while this rich

[125-031]prize was in sight. If her mother should speak to her, then, indeed,

[125-032]she would declare her own settled purpose; but she would do nothing

[125-033]to accelerate the evil hour.

[125-034]There were but few people in Mrs. Spalding's drawing-room when they

[125-035]were announced, and Mr. Glascock was not among them. Miss Wallachia

[125-036]Petrie was there, and in the confusion of the introduction was

[125-037]presumed by Lady Rowley to be one of the nieces introduced. She had

[125-038]been distinctly told that Mr. Glascock was to marry the eldest, and

[125-039]this lady was certainly older than the other two. In this way Lady

[125-040]Rowley decided that Miss Wallachia Petrie was her daughter's hated

[125-041]rival, and she certainly was much surprised at the [[social-class|gentleman]]'s taste.

[125-042]But there is nothing,--nothing in the way of an absurd matrimonial

[125-043]engagement,--into which a man will not allow himself to be entrapped

[125-044]by pique. Nora would have a great deal to answer for, Lady Rowley

[125-045]thought, if the unfortunate man should be driven by her cruelty to

[125-046]marry such a woman as this one now before her.

[125-047]It happened that Lady Rowley soon found herself seated by Miss

[125-048]Petrie, and she at once commenced her questionings. She intended to

[125-049]be very discreet, but the subject was too near her heart to allow her

[125-050]to be altogether silent. "I believe you know Mr. Glascock?" she said.

[125-051]"Yes," said Wallachia, "I do know him." Now the peculiar nasal twang

[125-052]which our cousins over the water have learned to use, and which

[125-053]has grown out of a certain national instinct which coerces them to

[125-054]express themselves with self-assertion;--let the reader go into his

[125-055]closet and talk through his nose for awhile with steady attention

[125-056]to the effect which his own voice will have, and he will find that

[125-057]this theory is correct;--this intonation, which is so peculiar among

[125-058]intelligent Americans, had been adopted con amore, and, as it were,

[125-059]taken to her bosom by Miss Petrie. Her ears had taught themselves

[125-060]to feel that there could be no vitality in speech without it, and

[125-061]that all utterance unsustained by such tone was effeminate, vapid,

[125-062]useless, unpersuasive, unmusical,--and English. It was a complaint

[125-063]frequently made by her against her friends Caroline and Olivia that

[125-064]they debased their voices, and taught themselves the puling British

[125-065]mode of speech. "I do know the gentleman," said Wallachia;--and Lady

[125-066]Rowley shuddered. Could it be that such a woman as this was to reign

[125-067]over Monkhams, and become the future Lady Peterborough?

[125-068]"He told me that he is acquainted with the family," said Lady Rowley.

[125-069]"He is staying at our hotel, and my daughter knew him very well when

[125-070]he was living in [[london|London]]."

[125-071]"I dare say. I believe that in London the titled aristocrats do hang

[125-072]pretty much together." It had never occurred to poor Lady Rowley,

[125-073]since the day in which her husband had been made a knight, at the

[125-074]advice of the Colonial Minister, in order that the inhabitants of

[125-075]some island might be gratified by the opportunity of using the

[125-076]title, that she and her children had thereby become aristocrats.

[125-077]Were her daughter Nora to marry Mr. Glascock, Nora would become an

[125-078]aristocrat,--or would, rather, be ennobled,--all which Lady Rowley

[125-079]understood perfectly.

[125-080]"I don't know that London society is very exclusive in that respect,"

[125-081]said Lady Rowley.

[125-082]"I guess you are pretty particular," said Miss Petrie, "and it seems

[125-083]to me you don't have much regard to intellect or erudition,--but fix

[125-084]things up straight according to birth and money."

[125-085]"I hope we are not quite so bad as that," said Lady Rowley. "I do not

[125-086]know London well myself, as I have passed my life in very distant

[125-087]places."

[125-088]"The distant places are, in my estimation, the best. The further the

[125-089]mind is removed from the contamination incidental to the centres of

[125-090]long-established luxury, the more chance it has of developing itself

[125-091]according to the intention of the Creator, when he bestowed his gifts

[125-092]of intellect upon us." Lady Rowley, when she heard this eloquence,

[125-093]could hardly believe that such a man as Mr. Glascock should really be

[125-094]intent upon marrying such a lady as this who was sitting next to her.

[125-095]In the meantime, Nora and the real rival were together, and they also

[125-096]were talking of Mr. Glascock. Caroline Spalding had said that Mr.

[125-097]Glascock had spoken to her of [[nora-rowley|Nora Rowley]], and Nora acknowledged that

[125-098]there had been some acquaintance between them in London. "Almost more

[125-099]than that, I should have thought," said Miss Spalding, "if one might

[125-100]judge by his manner of speaking of you."

[125-101][Illustration: The rivals.]

[125-102]"He is a little given to be enthusiastic," said Nora, laughing.

[125-103]"The least so of all mankind, I should have said. You must know he is

[125-104]very intimate in this house. It begun in this way;--Olivia and I were

[125-105]travelling together, and there was--a difficulty, as we say in our

[125-106]country when three or four gentlemen shoot each other. Then there

[125-107]came up Mr. Glascock and another gentleman. By-the-bye, the other

[125-108]gentleman was your brother-in-law."

[125-109]"Poor Mr. Trevelyan!"

[125-110]"He is very ill;--is he not?"

[125-111]"We think so. My sister is with us, you know. That is to say, she is

[125-112]at Siena to-day."

[125-113]"I have heard about him, and it is so sad. Mr. Glascock knows him. As

[125-114]I said, they were travelling together, when Mr. Glascock came to our

[125-115]assistance. Since that, we have seen him very frequently. I don't

[125-116]think he is enthusiastic,--except when he talks of you."

[125-117]"I ought to be very proud," said Nora.

[125-118]"I think you ought,--as Mr. Glascock is a man whose good opinion is

[125-119]certainly worth having. Here he is. Mr. Glascock, I hope your ears

[125-120]are tingling. They ought to do so, because we are saying all manner

[125-121]of fine things about you."

[125-122]"I could not be well spoken of by two on whose good word I should set

[125-123]a higher value," said he.

[125-124]"And whose do you value the most?" said Caroline.

[125-125]"I must first know whose eulogium will run the highest."

[125-126]Then Nora answered him. "Mr. Glascock, other people may praise

[125-127]you louder than I can do, but no one will ever do so with more

[125-128]sincerity." There was a pretty earnestness about her as she spoke,

[125-129]which Lady Rowley ought to have heard. Mr. Glascock bowed, and Miss

[125-130]Spalding smiled, and Nora blushed.

[125-131]"If you are not overwhelmed now," said Miss Spalding, "you must be so

[125-132]used to flattery, that it has no longer any effect upon you. You must

[125-133]be like a drunkard, to whom wine is as water, and who thinks that

[125-134]brandy is not strong enough."

[125-135]"I think I had better go away," said Mr. Glascock, "for fear the

[125-136]brandy should be watered by degrees." And so he left them.

[125-137]Nora had become quite aware, without much process of thinking about

[125-138]it, that her former lover and this American young lady were very

[125-139]intimate with each other. The tone of the conversation had shewn that

[125-140]it was so;--and, then, how had it come to pass that Mr. Glascock had

[125-141]spoken to this American girl about her,--Nora Rowley? It was evident

[125-142]that he had spoken of her with warmth, and had done so in a manner to

[125-143]impress his hearer. For a minute or two they sat together in silence

[125-144]after Mr. Glascock had left them, but neither of them stirred. Then

[125-145]Caroline Spalding turned suddenly upon Nora, and took her by the

[125-146]hand. "I must tell you something," said she, "only it must be a

[125-147]secret for awhile."

[125-148]"I will not repeat it."

[125-149]"Thank you, dear. I am engaged to him,--as his wife. He asked me this

[125-150]very afternoon, and nobody knows it but my aunt. When I had accepted

[125-151]him, he told me all the story about you. He had very often spoken

[125-152]of you before, and I had guessed how it must have been. He wears

[125-153]his heart so open for those whom he loves, that there is nothing

[125-154]concealed. He had seen you just before he came to me. But perhaps I

[125-155]am wrong to tell you that now. He ought to have been thinking of you

[125-156]again at such a time."

[125-157]"I did not want him to think of me again."

[125-158]"Of course you did not. Of course I am joking. You might have been

[125-159]his wife if you wished it. He has told me all that. And he especially

[125-160]wants us to be friends. Is there anything to prevent it?"

[125-161]"On my part? Oh, dear, no;--except that you will be such grand folk,

[125-162]and we shall be so poor."

[125-163]"We!" said Caroline, laughing. "I am so glad that there is a 'we.'"

[125-164]CHAPTER LXXVII.

[125-165]THE FUTURE LADY PETERBOROUGH.

[125-166]"If you have not sold yourself for British gold, and for British

[125-167]acres, and for British rank, I have nothing to say against it,"

[125-168]said Miss Wallachia Petrie that same evening to her friend Caroline

[125-169]Spalding.

[125-170]"You know that I have not sold myself, as you call it," said

[125-171]Caroline. There had been a long friendship between these two ladies,

[125-172]and the younger one knew that it behoved her to bear a good deal

[125-173]from the elder. Miss Petrie was honest, clever, and in earnest. We

[125-174]in England are not usually favourably disposed to women who take a

[125-175]pride in a certain antagonism to men in general, and who are anxious

[125-176]to shew the world that they can get on very well without male

[125-177]assistance; but there are many such in America who have noble

[125-178]aspirations, good intellects, much energy, and who are by no means

[125-179]unworthy of friendship. The hope in regard to all such women,--the

[125-180]hope entertained not by themselves, but by those who are solicitous

[125-181]for them,--is that they will be cured at last by a husband and

[125-182]half-a-dozen children. In regard to Wallachia Petrie there was not,

[125-183]perhaps, much ground for such hope. She was so positively wedded to

[125-184]women's rights in general, and to her own rights in particular, that

[125-185]it was improbable that she should ever succumb to any man;--and where

[125-186]would be the man brave enough to make the effort? From circumstances

[125-187]Caroline Spalding had been the beloved of her heart since Caroline

[125-188]Spalding was a very little girl; and she had hoped that Caroline

[125-189]would through life have borne arms along with her in that contest

[125-190]which she was determined to wage against man, and which she always

[125-191]waged with the greatest animosity against men of the British race.

[125-192]She hated rank; she hated riches; she hated monarchy;--and with

[125-193]a true woman's instinct in battle, felt that she had a specially

[125-194]strong point against Englishmen, in that they submitted themselves

[125-195]to dominion from a woman monarch. And now the chosen friend of her

[125-196]youth,--the friend who had copied out all her poetry, who had learned

[125-197]by heart all her sonnets, who had, as she thought, reciprocated all

[125-198]her ideas, was going to be married,--and to be married to an English

[125-199]lord! She had seen that it was coming for some time, and had spoken

[125-200]out very plainly, hoping that she might still save the brand from

[125-201]the burning. Now the evil was done; and Caroline Spalding, when she

[125-202]told her news, knew well that she would have to bear some heavy

[125-203]reproaches.
