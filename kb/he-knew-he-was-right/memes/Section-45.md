## Section 45

[045-001]Stanbury endeavoured with all his eloquence to prove that this latter

[045-002]piece of information from the spy must be incorrect. If such a letter

[045-003]had been written by Mrs. Trevelyan to [[colonel-osborne|Colonel Osborne]], it must have

[045-004]been done while he, Stanbury, was staying at the [[clock-house|Clock House]]. This

[045-005]seemed to him to be impossible; but he could hardly explain why

[045-006]it should be impossible. She had written to the man before, and

[045-007]had received him when he came to [[nuncombe-putney|Nuncombe Putney]]. Why was it even

[045-008]improbable that she should have written to him again? Nevertheless,

[045-009]Stanbury felt sure that she had sent no such letter. "I think I

[045-010]understand her feelings and her mind," said he; "and if so, any such

[045-011]correspondence would be incompatible with her previous conduct."

[045-012]Trevelyan only smiled at this,--or pretended to smile. He would

[045-013]not discuss the question; but believed implicitly what Bozzle had

[045-014]told him in spite of all Stanbury's arguments. "I can say nothing

[045-015]further," said Stanbury.

[045-016]"No, my dear fellow. There is nothing further to be said, except

[045-017]this, that I will have my unfortunate wife removed from the decent

[045-018]protection of your mother's roof with the least possible delay. I

[045-019]feel that I owe Mrs. Stanbury the deepest apology for having sent

[045-020]such an inmate to trouble her repose."

[045-021]"Nonsense!"

[045-022]"That is what I feel."

[045-023]"And I say that it is nonsense. If you had never sent that wretched

[045-024]blackguard down to fabricate lies at Nuncombe Putney, my mother's

[045-025]repose would have been all right. As it is, Mrs. Trevelyan can remain

[045-026]where she is till after Christmas. There is not the least necessity

[045-027]for removing her at once. I only meant to say that the arrangement

[045-028]should not be regarded as altogether permanent. I must go to my work

[045-029]now. Good-bye."

[045-030]"Good-bye, Stanbury."

[045-031]Stanbury paused at the door, and then once more turned round. "I

[045-032]suppose it is of no use my saying anything further; but I wish you to

[045-033]understand fully that I regard your wife as a woman much ill-used,

[045-034]and I think you are punishing her, and yourself, too, with a cruel

[045-035]severity for an indiscretion of the very slightest kind."

[045-036]CHAPTER XXVII.

[045-037]MR. TREVELYAN'S LETTER TO HIS WIFE.

[045-038]Trevelyan, when he was left alone, sat for above a couple of hours

[045-039]contemplating the misery of his position, and endeavouring to teach

[045-040]himself by thinking what ought to be his future conduct. It never

[045-041]occurred to him during these thoughts that it would be well that he

[045-042]should at once take back his wife, either as a matter of duty, or of

[045-043]welfare, for himself or for her. He had taught himself to believe

[045-044]that she had disgraced him; and, though this feeling of disgrace

[045-045]made him so wretched that he wished that he were dead, he would

[045-046]allow himself to make no attempt at questioning the correctness of

[045-047]his conviction. Though he were to be shipwrecked for ever, even

[045-048]that seemed to be preferable to supposing that he had been wrong.

[045-049]Nevertheless, he loved his wife dearly, and, in the white heat of his

[045-050]anger endeavoured to be merciful to her. When Stanbury accused him

[045-051]of severity, he would not condescend to defend himself; but he told

[045-052]himself then of his great mercy. Was he not as fond of his own boy

[045-053]as any other father, and had he not allowed her to take the child

[045-054]because he had felt that a mother's love was more imperious, more

[045-055]craving in its nature, than the love of a father? Had that been

[045-056]severe? And had he not resolved to allow her every comfort which

[045-057]her unfortunate position,--the self-imposed misfortune of her

[045-058]position,--would allow her to enjoy? She had come to him without

[045-059]a shilling; and yet, bad as her treatment of him had been, he was

[045-060]willing to give enough not only to support her, but her sister also,

[045-061]with every comfort. Severe! No; that, at least, was an undeserved

[045-062]accusation. He had been anything but severe. Foolish he might have

[045-063]been, in taking a wife from a home in which she had been unable to

[045-064]learn the discretion of a matron; too trusting he had been, and too

[045-065]generous,--but certainly not severe. But, of course, as he said to

[045-066]himself, a young man like Stanbury would take the part of a woman

[045-067]with whose sister he was in love. Then he turned his thoughts upon

[045-068]Bozzle, and there came over him a crushing feeling of ignominy,

[045-069]shame, moral dirt, and utter degradation, as he reconsidered his

[045-070]dealings with that ingenious [[social-class|gentleman]]. He was paying a rogue to

[045-071]watch the steps of a man whom he hated, to pry into the home secrets,

[045-072]to read the letters, to bribe the servants, to record the movements

[045-073]of this rival, this successful rival, in his wife's affections! It

[045-074]was a filthy thing,--and yet what could he do? Gentlemen of old, his

[045-075]own grandfather, or his father, would have taken such a fellow as

[045-076]Colonel Osborne by the throat and have caned him, and afterwards

[045-077]would have shot him, or have stood to be shot. All that was changed

[045-078]now,--but it was not his fault that it was changed. He was willing

[045-079]enough to risk his life, could any opportunity of risking it in this

[045-080]cause be obtained for him. But were he to cudgel Colonel Osborne,

[045-081]he would be simply arrested, and he would then be told that he had

[045-082]disgraced himself foully by striking a man old enough to be his

[045-083]father!

[045-084]How was he to have avoided the employment of some such man as Bozzle?

[045-085]He had also employed a gentleman, his friend, Stanbury; and what was

[045-086]the result? The facts were not altered. Even Stanbury did not attempt

[045-087]to deny that there had been a correspondence, and that there had been

[045-088]a visit. But Stanbury was so blind to all impropriety, or pretended

[045-089]such blindness, that he defended that which all the world agreed

[045-090]in condemning. Of what use had Stanbury been to him? He had wanted

[045-091]facts, not advice. Stanbury had found out no facts for him; but

[045-092]Bozzle, either by fair means or foul, did get at the truth. He did

[045-093]not doubt but that Bozzle was right about that letter written only

[045-094]yesterday, and received on that very morning. His wife, who had

[045-095]probably been complaining of her wrongs to Stanbury, must have

[045-096]retired from that conversation to her chamber, and immediately have

[045-097]written this letter to her lover! With such a woman as that what can

[045-098]be done in these days otherwise than by the aid of such a one as

[045-099]Bozzle? He could not confine his wife in a dungeon. He could not

[045-100]save himself from the disgrace of her misconduct, by any rigours of

[045-101]surveillance on his own part. As wives are managed now-a-days, he

[045-102]could not forbid to her the use of the post-office,--could not hinder

[045-103]her from seeing this hypocritical scoundrel, who carried on his

[045-104]wickedness under the false guise of family friendship. He had given

[045-105]her every chance to amend her conduct: but, if she were resolved

[045-106]on disobedience, he had no means of enforcing obedience. The facts,

[045-107]however, it was necessary that he should know.

[045-108]And now, what should he do? How should he go to work to make her

[045-109]understand that she could not write even a letter without his knowing

[045-110]it; and that if she did either write to the man or see him he would

[045-111]immediately take the child from her, and provide for her only in such

[045-112]fashion as the law should demand from him? For himself, and for his

[045-113]own life, he thought that he had determined what he would do. It was

[045-114]impossible that he should continue to live in [[london|London]]. He was ashamed

[045-115]to enter a club. He had hardly a friend to whom it was not an agony

[045-116]to speak. They who knew him, knew also of his disgrace, and no longer

[045-117]asked him to their houses. For days past he had eaten alone, and sat

[045-118]alone, and walked alone. All study was impossible to him. No pursuit

[045-119]was open to him. He spent his time in thinking of his wife, and of

[045-120]the disgrace which she had brought upon him. Such a life as this, he

[045-121]knew, was unmanly and shameful, and it was absolutely necessary for

[045-122]him that he should in some way change it. He would go out of England,

[045-123]and would travel,--if only he could so dispose of his wife that she

[045-124]might be safe from any possible communication with Colonel Osborne.

[045-125]If that could be effected, nothing that money could do should be

[045-126]spared for her. If that could not be effected he would remain at

[045-127]home,--and crush her.

[045-128]That night before he went to bed he wrote a letter to his wife, which

[045-129]was as follows;--

[045-130]DEAR EMILY,

[045-131]I have learned, beyond the shadow of a doubt, that you

[045-132]have corresponded with Colonel Osborne since you have been

[045-133]at Nuncombe Putney, and also that you have seen him there.

[045-134]This has been done in direct opposition to my expressed

[045-135]wishes, and I feel myself compelled to tell you that such

[045-136]conduct is disgraceful to you, and disgracing to me. I am

[045-137]quite at a loss to understand how you can reconcile to

[045-138]yourself so flagrant a disobedience of my instructions,

[045-139]and so perverse a disregard to the opinion of the world at

[045-140]large.

[045-141]But I do not write now for the sake of finding fault with

[045-142]you. It is too late for me to have any hope that I can do

[045-143]so with good effect, either as regards your credit or my

[045-144]happiness. Nevertheless, it is my duty to protect both you

[045-145]and myself from further shame; and I wish to tell you what

[045-146]are my intentions with that view. In the first place, I

[045-147]warn you that I keep a watch on you. The doing so is very

[045-148]painful to me, but it is absolutely necessary. You cannot

[045-149]see Colonel Osborne, or write to him, without my knowing

[045-150]it. I pledge you my word that in either case,--that is, if

[045-151]you correspond with him or see him,--I will at once take

[045-152]our boy away from you. I will not allow him to remain,

[045-153]even with a mother, who shall so misconduct herself.

[045-154]Should Colonel Osborne address a letter to you, I desire

[045-155]that you will put it under an envelope addressed to me.

[045-156]If you obey my commands on this head I will leave our boy

[045-157]with you nine months out of every year till he shall be

[045-158]six years old. Such, at least, is my present idea, though

[045-159]I will not positively bind myself to adhere to it. And I

[045-160]will allow you L800 per year for your own maintenance and

[045-161]that of your sister. I am greatly grieved to find from

[045-162]my friend Mr. Stanbury that your conduct in reference to

[045-163]Colonel Osborne has been such as to make it necessary that

[045-164]you should leave Mrs. Stanbury's house. I do not wonder

[045-165]that it should be so. I shall immediately seek for a

[045-166]future home for you, and when I have found one that is

[045-167]suitable, I will have you conveyed to it.

[045-168]I must now further explain my purposes,--and I must beg

[045-169]you to remember that I am driven to do so by your direct

[045-170]disobedience to my expressed wishes. Should there be any

[045-171]further communication between you and Colonel Osborne,

[045-172]not only will I take your child away from you, but I will

[045-173]also limit the allowance to be made to you to a bare

[045-174]sustenance. In such case, I shall put the matter into the

[045-175]hands of a lawyer, and shall probably feel myself driven

[045-176]to take steps towards freeing myself from a connection

[045-177]which will be disgraceful to my name.

[045-178]For myself, I shall live abroad during the greater part of

[045-179]the year. London has become to me uninhabitable, and all

[045-180]English pleasures are distasteful.
