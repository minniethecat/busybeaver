## Section 130

[130-001]"I am so thankful to you for bringing him to me," she said.

[130-002]"I told you that you should see him," he said. "Perhaps it might have

[130-003]been better that I should have sent him by a servant; but there are

[130-004]circumstances which make me fear to let him out of my sight."

[130-005]"Do you think that I did not wish to see you also? Louis, why do

[130-006]you do me so much wrong? Why do you treat me with such cruelty?"

[130-007]Then she threw her arms round his neck, and before he could repulse

[130-008]her,--before he could reflect whether it would be well that he should

[130-009]repulse her or not,--she had covered his brow and cheeks and lips

[130-010]with kisses. "Louis," she said; "Louis, speak to me!"

[130-011]"It is hard to speak sometimes," he said.

[130-012][Illustration: "It is hard to speak sometimes."]

[130-013]"You love me, Louis?"

[130-014]"Yes;--I love you. But I am afraid of you!"

[130-015]"What is it that you fear? I would give my life for you, if you would

[130-016]only come back to me and let me feel that you believed me to be

[130-017]true." He shook his head, and began to think,--while she still clung

[130-018]to him. He was quite sure that her father and mother had intended to

[130-019]bring a mad doctor down upon him, and he knew that his wife was in

[130-020]her mother's hands. Should he yield to her now,--should he make her

[130-021]any promise,--might not the result be that he would be shut up in

[130-022]dark rooms, robbed of his [[personal-freedom|liberty]], robbed of what he loved better

[130-023]than his liberty,--his power as a man. She would thus get the better

[130-024]of him and take the child, and the world would say that in this

[130-025]contest between him and her he had been the sinning one, and she the

[130-026]one against whom the sin had been done. It was the chief object of

[130-027]his mind, the one thing for which he was eager, that this should

[130-028]never come to pass. Let it once be conceded to him from all sides

[130-029]that he had been right, and then she might do with him almost as she

[130-030]willed. He knew well that he was ill. When he thought of his child,

[130-031]he would tell himself that he was dying. He was at some moments of

[130-032]his miserable existence fearfully anxious to come to terms with his

[130-033]wife, in order that at his death his boy might not be without a

[130-034]protector. Were he to die, then it would be better that his child

[130-035]should be with its mother. In his happy days, immediately after

[130-036]his marriage, he had made a will, in which he had left his entire

[130-037][[money|property]] to his wife for her life, providing for its subsequent

[130-038]descent to his child,--or children. It had never even occurred to his

[130-039]poor shattered brain that it would be well for him to alter his will.

[130-040]Had he really believed that his wife had betrayed him, doubtless he

[130-041]would have done so. He would have hated her, have distrusted her

[130-042]altogether, and have believed her to be an evil thing. He had no such

[130-043]belief. But in his desire to achieve empire, and in the sorrows which

[130-044]had come upon him in his unsuccessful struggle, his mind had wavered

[130-045]so frequently, that his spoken words were no true indicators of his

[130-046]thoughts; and in all his arguments he failed to express either his

[130-047]convictions or his desires. When he would say something stronger than

[130-048]he intended, and it would be put to him by his wife, by her father or

[130-049]mother, or by some friend of hers, whether he did believe that she

[130-050]had been untrue to him, he would recoil from the answer which his

[130-051]heart would dictate, lest he should seem to make an acknowledgment

[130-052]that might weaken the ground upon which he stood. Then he would

[130-053]satisfy his own conscience by assuring himself that he had never

[130-054]accused her of such sin. She was still clinging to him now as his

[130-055]mind was working after this fashion. "Louis," she said, "let it all

[130-056]be as though there had been nothing."

[130-057]"How can that be, my dear?"

[130-058]"Not to others;--but to us it can be so. There shall be no word

[130-059]spoken of the past." Again he shook his head. "Will it not be best

[130-060]that there should be no word spoken?"

[130-061]"'Forgiveness may be spoken with the tongue,'" he said, beginning to

[130-062]quote from a poem which had formerly been frequent in his hands.

[130-063]"Cannot there be real forgiveness between you and me,--between

[130-064]husband and wife who, in truth, love each other? Do you think that I

[130-065]would tell you of it again?" He felt that in all that she said there

[130-066]was an assumption that she had been right, and that he had been

[130-067]wrong. She was promising to forgive. She was undertaking to forget.

[130-068]She was willing to take him back to the warmth of her love, and the

[130-069]comfort of her kindness,--but was not asking to be taken back. This

[130-070]was what he could not and would not endure. He had determined that

[130-071]if she behaved well to him, he would not be harsh to her, and he

[130-072]was struggling to keep up to his resolve. He would accuse her of

[130-073]nothing,--if he could help it. But he could not say a word that would

[130-074]even imply that she need forget,--that she should forgive. It was for

[130-075]him to forgive;--and he was willing to do it, if she would accept

[130-076]forgiveness. "I will never speak a word, Louis," she said, laying her

[130-077]head upon his shoulder.

[130-078]"Your heart is still hardened," he replied slowly.

[130-079]"Hard to you?"

[130-080]"And your mind is dark. You do not see what you have done. In our

[130-081]religion, Emily, forgiveness is sure, not after penitence, but with

[130-082]repentance."

[130-083]"What does that mean?"

[130-084]"It means this, that though I would welcome you back to my arms with

[130-085]joy, I cannot do so, till you have--confessed your fault."

[130-086]"What fault, Louis? If I have made you unhappy, I do, indeed, grieve

[130-087]that it has been so."

[130-088]"It is of no use," said he. "I cannot talk about it. Do you suppose

[130-089]that it does not tear me to the very soul to think of it?"

[130-090]"What is it that you think, Louis?" As she had been travelling

[130-091]thither, she had determined that she would say anything that he

[130-092]wished her to say,--make any admission that might satisfy him. That

[130-093]she could be happy again as other women are happy, she did not

[130-094]expect; but if it could be conceded between them that bygones should

[130-095]be bygones, she might live with him and do her duty, and, at least,

[130-096]have her child with her. Her father had told her that her husband was

[130-097]mad; but she was willing to put up with his madness on such terms as

[130-098]these. What could her husband do to her in his madness that he could

[130-099]not do also to the child? "Tell me what you want me to say, and I

[130-100]will say it," she said.

[130-101]"You have sinned against me," he said, raising her head gently from

[130-102]his shoulder.

[130-103]"Never!" she exclaimed. "As God is my judge, I never have!" As she

[130-104]said this, she retreated and took the sobbing boy again into her

[130-105]arms.

[130-106]He was at once placed upon his guard, telling himself that he saw the

[130-107]necessity of holding by his child. How could he tell? Might there not

[130-108]be a policeman down from Florence, ready round the house, to seize

[130-109]the boy and carry him away? Though all his remaining life should be

[130-110]a torment to him, though infinite plagues should be poured upon his

[130-111]head, though he should die like a dog, alone, unfriended, and in

[130-112]despair, while he was fighting this battle of his, he would not give

[130-113]way. "That is sufficient," he said. "Louey must return now to his own

[130-114]chamber."

[130-115]"I may go with him?"

[130-116]"No, Emily. You cannot go with him now. I will thank you to release

[130-117]him, that I may take him." She still held the little fellow closely

[130-118]pressed in her arms. "Do not reward me for my courtesy by further

[130-119]disobedience," he said.

[130-120]"You will let me come again?" To this he made no reply. "Tell me that

[130-121]I may come again."

[130-122]"I do not think that I shall remain here long."

[130-123]"And I may not stay now?"

[130-124]"That would be impossible. There is no accommodation for you."

[130-125]"I could sleep on the boards beside his cot," said Mrs. Trevelyan.

[130-126]"That is my place," he replied. "You may know that he is not

[130-127]disregarded. With my own hands I tend him every morning. I take him

[130-128]out myself. I feed him myself. He says his prayers to me. He learns

[130-129]from me, and can say his letters nicely. You need not fear for him.

[130-130]No mother was ever more tender with her child than I am with him."

[130-131]Then he gently withdrew the boy from her arms, and she let her child

[130-132]go, lest he should learn to know that there was a quarrel between

[130-133]his father and his mother. "If you will excuse me," he said, "I will

[130-134]not come down to you again to-day. My servant will see you to your

[130-135]carriage."

[130-136]So he left her; and she, with an Italian girl at her heels, got into

[130-137]her vehicle, and was taken back to Siena. There she passed the night

[130-138]alone at the inn, and on the next morning returned to Florence by the

[130-139]railway.

[130-140]CHAPTER LXXX.

[130-141]"WILL THEY DESPISE HIM?"

[130-142]Gradually the news of the intended marriage between Mr. Glascock and

[130-143]Miss Spalding spread itself over Florence, and people talked about

[130-144]it with that energy which subjects of such moment certainly deserve.

[130-145]That Caroline Spalding had achieved a very great triumph, was, of

[130-146]course, the verdict of all men and of all women; and I fear that

[130-147]there was a corresponding feeling that poor Mr. Glascock had been

[130-148]triumphed over, and as it were, subjugated. In some respects he had

[130-149]been remiss in his duties as a bachelor visitor to Florence,--as a

[130-150]visitor to Florence who had manifestly been much in want of a wife.

[130-151]He had not given other girls a fair chance, but had thrown himself

[130-152]down at the feet of this American female in the weakest possible

[130-153]manner. And then it got about the town that he had been refused over

[130-154]and over again by [[nora-rowley|Nora Rowley]]. It is too probable that Lady Rowley

[130-155]in her despair and dismay had been indiscreet, and had told secrets

[130-156]which should never have been mentioned by her. And the wife of

[130-157]the English minister, who had some grudges of her own, lifted her

[130-158]eyebrows and shook her head and declared that all the Glascocks at

[130-159]home would be outraged to the last degree. "My dear Lady Rowley,"

[130-160]she said, "I don't know whether it won't become a question with them

[130-161]whether they should issue a commission de lunatico." Lady Rowley did

[130-162]not know what a commission de lunatico meant, but was quite willing

[130-163]to regard poor Mr. Glascock as a lunatic. "And there is poor Lord

[130-164]Peterborough at Naples just at death's door," continued the British

[130-165]Minister's wife. In this she was perhaps nearly correct; but as Lord

[130-166]Peterborough had now been in the same condition for many months, as

[130-167]his mind had altogether gone, and as the doctor declared that he

[130-168]might live in his present condition for a year, or for years, it

[130-169]could not fairly be said that Mr. Glascock was acting without due

[130-170]filial feeling in engaging himself to marry a young lady. "And she

[130-171]such a creature!" said Lady Rowley, with emphasis. This the British

[130-172]Minister's wife noticed simply by shaking her head. Caroline Spalding

[130-173]was undoubtedly a pretty girl; but, as the British Minister's wife

[130-174]said afterwards, it was not surprising that poor Lady Rowley should

[130-175]be nearly out of her mind.
