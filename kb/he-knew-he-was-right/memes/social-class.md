# Social Class

The Victorian class hierarchy as depicted in *He Knew He Was Right*, structuring characters' identities, constraining their choices, and providing the social vocabulary through which status is claimed, challenged, and defended.

## Overview

Class permeates every relationship in Trollope's novel. Sir Rowley is introduced as "a high-minded gentleman" (001-199) who has fallen on harder times, his "neither an exalted position nor a large fortune" (001-157) establishing the novel's interest in the gap between class status and economic reality. The Trevelyans move in the upper reaches of London society; the Rowleys are respectable gentry under financial pressure; Hugh Stanbury occupies the ambiguous class position of the professional journalist — educated as a gentleman but earning his living by trade, constrained by "those imposed upon him by his position as a member of Parliament" (002-041) and his professional identity.

## Context in Victorian Society

Victorian England was a society in flux, with traditional landed aristocracy losing ground to industrial and professional wealth. The category of "gentleman" was increasingly contested: did it require landed property, a classical education, or merely respectable conduct? The novel engages this debate through Hugh Stanbury, who claims the status of gentleman while pursuing a profession — journalism — that many of his contemporaries considered beneath that station.

## Manifestation in the Novel

### The Stanbury Class Drama

Miss Jemima Stanbury embodies the old class order: she values inherited wealth, proper connections, and social exclusivity. Her disapproval of Hugh's journalism reflects a class-based distinction between professions (law, church, medicine) and mere "trade." Yet the novel ultimately sides with Hugh, suggesting that professional competence and personal integrity matter more than inherited status.

### The Rowley Predicament

Sir Rowley's financial difficulties place his daughters in an awkward class position: they are gently born but poorly dowered. Nora's courtship with Hugh is shadowed by this class ambiguity — she is marrying "down" in social terms but "up" in terms of character. The novel treats this tension with sympathy, refusing to either condemn or endorse class endogamy.

## Key Examples

### The Gentleman Question

The novel repeatedly raises the question of what makes a gentleman. Trevelyan, by birth and wealth, is unquestionably a gentleman — yet his conduct toward his wife is ungentlemanly. Hugh Stanbury works for a living, yet his integrity makes him the novel's moral standard-bearer. The implicit argument is that character, not class, is the true measure of gentlemanliness.

### Professional Identity

Hugh's journalism places him at the intersection of class meanings: he is educated, articulate, and moves easily in polite society, yet he earns his living by writing for newspapers — an activity his aunt considers degrading. The novel uses this tension to interrogate Victorian class categories without wholly rejecting them.
