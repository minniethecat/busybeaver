# Virtue

The Victorian ideal of moral virtue that structures the ethical landscape of *He Knew He Was Right*, distinguishing characters who act with integrity from those who merely claim it.

## Overview

Virtue in Trollope's novel is not an abstract quality but a demonstrated capacity for fair dealing, self-restraint, and concern for others. The novel's moral judgments are enacted through plotting: virtuous characters like Hugh Stanbury and Nora Rowley are rewarded with happy marriages; vicious characters suffer the consequences of their actions. Yet Trollope's moral vision is characteristically nuanced — Trevelyan is not simply vicious but tragically unable to live up to the virtue he claims (016-160).

## Context in Victorian Society

Victorian moral discourse placed enormous emphasis on virtue, particularly female virtue. A woman's reputation for virtue was her most precious possession, and its loss — even through unfounded suspicion — could be socially fatal. The novel critiques this gendered distribution of virtue's burdens while affirming the value of genuine moral character.

## Manifestation in the Novel

### Virtue as Action

The novel's moral judgments are embedded in narrative outcomes. Characters who act with generosity, honesty, and self-restraint prosper; characters who indulge jealousy, pride, or selfishness suffer. This is not crude moralizing but the structural logic of Trollope's realism.

### The Appearance of Virtue

The novel distinguishes between the appearance and substance of virtue. Trevelyan presents himself as a man defending his honour — a virtuous stance by Victorian standards — but his actions reveal selfishness and cruelty. Emily, by contrast, is suspected of impropriety but behaves with consistent integrity.
