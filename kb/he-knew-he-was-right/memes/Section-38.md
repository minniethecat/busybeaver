## Section 38

[038-001]The party went off very much as usual. There were two whist tables,

[038-002]for Miss Stanbury could not bear to cut out. At other houses than her

[038-003]own, when there was cutting out, it was quite understood that Miss

[038-004]Stanbury was to be allowed to keep her place. "I'll go away, and sit

[038-005]out there by myself, if you like," she would say. But she was never

[038-006]thus banished; and at her own house she usually contrived that

[038-007]there should be no system of banishment. She would play dummy whist,

[038-008]preferring it to the four-handed game; and, when hard driven, and

[038-009]with a meet opponent, would not even despise double-dummy. It was

[038-010]told of her and of Mrs. MacHugh that they had played double-dummy

[038-011]for a whole evening together; and they who were given to calumny

[038-012]had declared that the candles on that evening had been lighted very

[038-013]early. On the present occasion a great many sixpenny points were

[038-014]scored, and much tea and cake were consumed. Mr. Gibson never played

[038-015]whist,--nor did Dorothy. That young John Wright and Mary Cheriton

[038-016]should do nothing but talk to each other was a thing of course,

[038-017]as they were to be married in a month or two. Then there was Ida

[038-018]Cheriton, who could not very well be left at home; and Mr. Gibson

[038-019]made himself pleasant to Dorothy and Ida Cheriton, instead of making

[038-020]himself pleasant to the two Miss Frenches. Gentlemen in provincial

[038-021]towns quite understand that, from the nature of social circumstances

[038-022]in the provinces, they should always be ready to be pleasant at least

[038-023]to a pair at a time. At a few minutes before twelve they were all

[038-024]gone, and then came the shock.

[038-025]"Dolly, my dear, what do you think of Mr. Gibson?"

[038-026]"Think of him, Aunt Stanbury?"

[038-027]"Yes; think of him;--think of him. I suppose you know how to think?"

[038-028]"He seems to me always to preach very drawling sermons."

[038-029]"Oh, bother his sermons! I don't care anything about his sermons now.

[038-030]He is a very good clergyman, and the Dean thinks very much about

[038-031]him."

[038-032]"I am glad of that, Aunt Stanbury."

[038-033]Then came the shock. "Don't you think it would be a very good thing

[038-034]if you were to become Mrs. Gibson?"

[038-035]It may be presumed that Miss Stanbury had assured herself that she

[038-036]could not make progress with Dorothy by "beating about the bush."

[038-037]There was an inaptitude in her niece to comprehend the advantages

[038-038]of the situations, which made some direct explanation absolutely

[038-039]necessary. Dorothy stood half-smiling, half-crying, when she heard

[038-040]the proposition, her cheeks suffused with that pink colour, and with

[038-041]both her hands extended with surprise.

[038-042]"I've been thinking about it ever since you've been here," said Miss

[038-043]Stanbury.

[038-044]"I think he likes Miss French," said Dorothy, in a whisper.

[038-045]"Which of them? I don't believe he likes them at all. Maybe, if they

[038-046]go on long enough, they may be able to toss up for him. But I don't

[038-047]think it of him. Of course they're after him, but he'll be too wise

[038-048]for them. And he's more of a fool than I take him to be if he don't

[038-049]prefer you to them." Dorothy remained quite silent. To such an

[038-050]address as this it was impossible that she should reply a word. It

[038-051]was incredible to her that any man should prefer herself to either of

[038-052]the young women in question; but she was too much confounded for the

[038-053]expression even of her humility. "At any rate you're wholesome, and

[038-054]pleasant and modest," said Miss Stanbury.

[038-055]Dorothy did not quite like being told that she was wholesome; but,

[038-056]nevertheless, she was thankful to her aunt.

[038-057]"I'll tell you what it is," continued Miss Stanbury; "I hate all

[038-058]mysteries, especially with those I love. I've saved two thousand

[038-059]pounds, which I've put you down for in my will. Now, if you and he

[038-060]can make it up together, I'll give you the money at once. There's no

[038-061]knowing how often an old woman may alter her will; but when you've

[038-062]got a thing, you've got it. Mr. Gibson would know the meaning of a

[038-063]bird in the hand as well as anybody. Now those girls at Heavitree

[038-064]will never have above a few hundreds each, and not that while

[038-065]their mother lives." Dorothy made one little attempt at squeezing

[038-066]her aunt's hand, wishing to thank her aunt for this affectionate

[038-067]generosity; but she had hardly accomplished the squeeze, when she

[038-068]desisted, feeling strangely averse to any acknowledgment of such a

[038-069]boon as that which had been offered to her. "And now, good night, my

[038-070]dear. If I did not think you a very sensible young woman, I should

[038-071]not trust you by saying all this." Then they parted, and Dorothy soon

[038-072]found herself alone in her bedroom.

[038-073]To have a husband of her own, a perfect [[social-class|gentleman]] too, and a

[038-074]clergyman;--and to go to him with a [[money|fortune]]! She believed that two

[038-075]thousand pounds represented nearly a hundred a year. It was a large

[038-076]fortune in those parts,--according to her understanding of ladies'

[038-077]fortunes. And that she, the humblest of the humble, should be

[038-078]selected for so honourable a position! She had never quite known,

[038-079]quite understood as yet, whether she had made good her footing in

[038-080]her aunt's house in a manner pleasant to her aunt. More than once or

[038-081]twice she had spoken even of going back to her mother, and things

[038-082]had been said which had almost made her think that her aunt had been

[038-083]angry with her. But now, after a month or two of joint residence, her

[038-084]aunt was offering to her--two thousand pounds and a husband!

[038-085]But was it within her aunt's power to offer to her the husband? Mr.

[038-086]Gibson had always been very civil to her. She had spoken more to Mr.

[038-087]Gibson than to any other man in [[exeter|Exeter]]. But it had never occurred to

[038-088]her for a moment that Mr. Gibson had any special liking for her. Was

[038-089]it probable that he would ever entertain any feeling of that kind

[038-090]for her? It certainly had occurred to her before now that Mr. Gibson

[038-091]was sometimes bored by the Miss Frenches;--but then gentlemen do get

[038-092]bored by ladies.

[038-093]And at last she asked herself another question,--had she any special

[038-094]liking for Mr. Gibson? As far as she understood such matters

[038-095]everything was blank there. Thinking of that other question, she went

[038-096]to sleep.

[038-097]CHAPTER XXIII.

[038-098]COLONEL OSBORNE AND MR. BOZZLE RETURN TO LONDON.

[038-099][[hugh-stanbury|Hugh Stanbury]] went down on the Saturday, by the early express to

[038-100]Exeter, on his road to Lessboro'. He took his ticket through to

[038-101]Lessboro', not purposing to stay at Exeter; but, from the exigencies

[038-102]of the various trains, it was necessary that he should remain for

[038-103]half an hour at the Exeter Station. This took place on the Saturday,

[038-104]and [[colonel-osborne|Colonel Osborne]]'s visit to the [[clock-house|Clock House]] had been made on the

[038-105]Friday. Colonel Osborne had returned to Lessboro', had slept again

[038-106]at Mrs. Clegg's house, and returned to [[london|London]] on the Saturday. It so

[038-107]happened that he also was obliged to spend half an hour at the Exeter

[038-108]Station, and that his half-hour, and Hugh Stanbury's half-hour, were

[038-109]one and the same. They met, therefore, as a matter of course, upon

[038-110]the platform. Stanbury was the first to see the other, and he found

[038-111]that he must determine on the spur of the moment what he would say,

[038-112]and what he would do. He had received no direct commission from

[038-113]Trevelyan as to his meeting with Colonel Osborne. Trevelyan had

[038-114]declared that, as to the matter of quarrelling, he meant to retain

[038-115]the privilege of doing that for himself; but Stanbury had quite

[038-116]understood that this was only the vague expression of an angry man.

[038-117]The Colonel had taken a glass of sherry, and had lighted a cigar,

[038-118]and was quite comfortable,--having thrown aside, for a time, that

[038-119]consciousness of the futility of his journey which had perplexed

[038-120]him,--when Stanbury accosted him.

[038-121]"What! Mr. Stanbury,--how do you do? Fine day, isn't it? Are you

[038-122]going up or down?"

[038-123]"I'm going to see my own people at [[nuncombe-putney|Nuncombe Putney]], a village beyond

[038-124]Lessboro'," said Hugh.

[038-125]"Ah;--indeed." Colonel Osborne of course perceived at once that as

[038-126]this man was going to the house at which he had just been visiting,

[038-127]it would be better that he should himself explain what he had done.

[038-128]If he were to allow this mention of Nuncombe Putney to pass without

[038-129]saying that he himself had been there, he would be convicted of

[038-130]at least some purpose of secrecy in what he had been doing. "Very

[038-131]strange," said he; "I was at Nuncombe Putney myself yesterday."

[038-132]"I know you were," said Stanbury.

[038-133]"And how did you know it?" There had been a tone of anger in

[038-134]Stanbury's voice which Colonel Osborne had at once appreciated, and

[038-135]which made him assume a similar tone. As they spoke there was a man

[038-136][[reputation|standing]] in a corner close by the bookstall, with his eye upon them,

[038-137]and that man was Bozzle, the ex-policeman,--who was doing his duty

[038-138]with sedulous activity by seeing "the Colonel" back to London. Now

[038-139]Bozzle did not know Hugh Stanbury, and was angry with himself that he

[038-140]should be so ignorant. It is the pride of a detective ex-policeman to

[038-141]know everybody that comes in his way.

[038-142]"Well, I had been so informed. My friend Trevelyan knew that you were

[038-143]there,--or that you were going there."

[038-144]"I don't care who knew that I was going there," said the Colonel.

[038-145]"I won't pretend to understand how that may be, Colonel Osborne; but

[038-146]I think you must be aware, after what took place in Curzon Street,

[038-147]that it would have been better that you should not have attempted to

[038-148]see Mrs. Trevelyan. Whether you have seen her I do not know."

[038-149]"What business is it of yours, Mr. Stanbury, whether I have seen that

[038-150]lady or not?"

[038-151]"Unhappily for me, her husband has made it my business."

[038-152]"Very unhappily for you, I should say."

[038-153]"And the lady is staying at my mother's house."

[038-154]"I presume the lady is not a prisoner in your mother's house, and

[038-155]that your mother's hospitality is not so restricted but that her

[038-156]guest may see an old friend under her roof." This Colonel Osborne

[038-157]said with an assumed look of almost righteous indignation, which

[038-158]was not at all lost upon Bozzle. They had returned back towards the

[038-159]bookstall, and Bozzle, with his eyes fixed on a copy of the "D. R."

[038-160]which he had just bought, was straining his ears to the utmost to

[038-161]catch what was being said.

[038-162]"You best know whether you have seen her or not."

[038-163]"I have seen her."

[038-164]"Then I shall take leave to tell you, Colonel Osborne, that you have

[038-165]acted in a most unfriendly way, and have done that which must tend to

[038-166]keep an affectionate husband apart from his wife."

[038-167]"Sir, I don't at all understand this kind of thing addressed to me.

[038-168]The father of the lady you are speaking of has been my most intimate

[038-169]friend for thirty years." After all, the Colonel was a mean man when

[038-170]he could take pride in his youth, and defend himself on the score of

[038-171]his age, in one and the same proceeding.

[038-172]"I have nothing further to say," replied Stanbury.

[038-173]"You have said too much already, Mr. Stanbury."

[038-174]"I think not, Colonel Osborne. You have, I fear, done an incredible

[038-175]deal of mischief by going to Nuncombe Putney; and, after all that

[038-176]you have heard on the subject, you must have known that it would be

[038-177]mischievous. I cannot understand how you can force yourself about a

[038-178]man's wife against the man's expressed wish."

[038-179]"Sir, I didn't force myself upon anybody. Sir, I went down to see an

[038-180]old friend,--and a remarkable piece of antiquity. And, when another

[038-181]old friend was in the neighbourhood, close by,--one of the oldest

[038-182]friends I have in the world,--wasn't I to go and see her? God bless

[038-183]my soul! What business is it of yours? I never heard such impudence

[038-184]in my life!" Let the charitable reader suppose that Colonel Osborne

[038-185]did not know that he was lying,--that he really thought, when he

[038-186]spoke, that he had gone down to Lessboro' to see the remarkable piece

[038-187]of antiquity.
