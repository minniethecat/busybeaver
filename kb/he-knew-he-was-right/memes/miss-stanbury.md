# Miss Stanbury

Miss Jemima Stanbury is the wealthy, autocratic aunt of the Stanbury siblings, living in the cathedral close at Exeter. A woman of strong opinions, considerable fortune, and an imperious manner, she exerts a gravitational pull on the lives of her relations — summoning Dorothy to live with her, designating Brooke Burgess as her heir, and dispensing judgments on all matters of family and society.

## Overview

Miss Stanbury first enters the narrative as the aunt whose wealth and residence in Exeter shape the Stanbury family's circumstances (007-013). She is a self-made woman of property: "Miss Stanbury was a very rich lady" (012-064), but her wealth was not inherited — "Miss Stanbury had not been born to this wealth" (012-039). Her acquisition of fortune, through inheritance from a man she was engaged to marry in her youth (012-048), is one of the novel's pointed commentaries on the role of chance and attachment in Victorian women's economic lives.

## Role in the Novel

Miss Stanbury functions as a structural pivot connecting the novel's geographical and social worlds. Her Exeter home provides an alternative domestic sphere to both the Trevelyan household and the Stanbury cottage at Nuncombe Putney. Her wealth and social standing make her a figure of consequence, while her strong opinions — on family loyalty, proper conduct, and matrimonial suitability — drive several of the novel's minor plot lines.

She also embodies a particular type of Victorian woman: the unmarried woman of independent means who exercises power through property rather than through marriage. Her authority is not patriarchal but matriarchal — she rules her domain absolutely, and even the men in her orbit must accommodate themselves to her will.

## Relationships

### Hugh Stanbury

Miss Stanbury's relationship with her nephew Hugh is marked by both affection and disapproval. She disapproves of his career in journalism and his marriage to Nora Rowley, yet the family bond persists. Hugh navigates his aunt's expectations with a mixture of independence and respect.

### Dorothy Stanbury

Miss Stanbury summons Dorothy from Nuncombe Putney to live with her in Exeter, presenting her to society as "my niece, Miss Dorothy Stanbury" (052-038). She takes an active interest in Dorothy's marital prospects, and her approval is a significant factor in the courtship plots that surround her niece.

### Brooke Burgess

Miss Stanbury designates Brooke Burgess as her heir, a decision that places him at the centre of Exeter's matrimonial calculations. Her approval of his eventual attachment to Dorothy Stanbury is one of the novel's quiet satisfactions.

### Priscilla Stanbury

Priscilla Stanbury's attitude toward her aunt is characterised by resentment: "her hatred for her aunt" (030-159) is noted as a defining feature of her character. This antipathy provides a counterpoint to Dorothy's more dutiful relationship with Miss Stanbury.

## Key Scenes

### The Exeter Household

Miss Stanbury's establishment in the cathedral close is a world unto itself. From this seat of authority, she dispenses judgments on family matters and manages the lives of those within her orbit (012-001).

### Summoning Dorothy

Miss Stanbury's decision to bring Dorothy to Exeter — "I have asked Mrs. Stanbury to send one of the girls over" (013-098) — sets in motion the courtship plot that will dominate Dorothy's narrative.

## Character Development

Miss Stanbury undergoes no dramatic transformation; her function is to be a fixed point around which other characters move. Her consistency — in wealth, in opinion, in the exercise of authority — makes her one of Trollope's memorable portraits of the older generation's grip on Victorian social life.
