[035-005]"And there is a copy of my answer," said Mrs. Trevelyan. "I shall

[035-006]keep both, because I know so well what ill-natured things people will

[035-008]"Dear Emily, do not send it," said Nora.

[035-014]husband wished to guide me in such matters, why did he not stay with

[035-016]Then she went out into the village and posted the letter. Nora

[035-017]meanwhile was thinking whether she would call in the assistance of

[035-018]Priscilla Stanbury; but she did not like to take any such a step in

[035-019]opposition to her sister.

[035-020]CHAPTER XXI.

[035-021]SHEWING HOW COLONEL OSBORNE WENT TO NUNCOMBE PUTNEY.

[035-022]Colonel Osborne was expected at [[nuncombe-putney|Nuncombe Putney]] on the Friday, and

[035-023]it was Thursday evening before either Mrs. Stanbury or Priscilla was

[035-024]told of his coming. Emily had argued the matter with Nora, declaring

[035-025]that she would make the communication herself, and that she would

[035-026]make it when she pleased and how she pleased. "If Mrs. Stanbury

[035-027]thinks," said she, "that I am going to be treated as a prisoner, or

[035-028]that I will not judge myself as to whom I may see, or whom I may not

[035-029]see, she is very much mistaken." Nora felt that were she to give

[035-030]information to those ladies in opposition to her sister's wishes,

[035-031]she would express suspicion on her own part by doing so; and she was

[035-032]silent. On that same Thursday Priscilla had written her last defiant

[035-033]letter to her aunt,--that letter in which she had cautioned her aunt

[035-034]to make no further accusations without being sure of her facts. To

[035-035]Priscilla's imagination that coming of Lucifer in person, of which

[035-036]Mrs. Trevelyan had spoken, would hardly have been worse than the

[035-037]coming of Colonel Osborne. When, therefore, Mrs. Trevelyan declared

[035-038]the fact on the Thursday evening, vainly endeavouring to speak of

[035-039]the threatened visit in an ordinary voice, and as of an ordinary

[035-040]circumstance, it was as though a thunderbolt had fallen upon them.

[035-041]"Colonel Osborne coming here!" said Priscilla, mindful of the

[035-042]Stanbury correspondence,--mindful of the evil tongues of the world.

[035-043]"And why not?" demanded Mrs. Trevelyan, who had heard nothing of the

[035-044]Stanbury correspondence.

[035-045]"Oh dear, oh dear!" ejaculated Mrs. Stanbury, who, of course, was

[035-046]aware of all that had passed between the [[clock-house|Clock House]] and the house in

[035-047]the Close, though the letters had been written by her daughter.

[035-048]Nora was determined to stand up for her sister, whatever might be the

[035-049]circumstances of the case. "I wish Colonel Osborne were not coming,"

[035-050]said she, "because it makes a foolish fuss; but I cannot understand

[035-051]how anybody can suppose it to be wrong that Emily should see papa's

[035-052]very oldest friend in the world."

[035-053]"But why is he coming?" demanded Priscilla.

[035-054]"Because he wants to see an acquaintance at Cockchaffington," said

[035-055]Mrs. Trevelyan; "and there is a wonderful church-door there."

[035-056]"A church-fiddlestick!" said Priscilla.

[035-057]The matter was debated throughout all the evening. At one time

[035-058]there was a great quarrel between the ladies, and then there was a

[035-059]reconciliation. The point on which Mrs. Trevelyan stood with the

[035-060]greatest firmness was this,--that it did not become her, as a married

[035-061]woman whose conduct had always been good and who was more careful as

[035-062]to that than she was even of her name, to be ashamed to meet any man.

[035-063]"Why should I not see Colonel Osborne, or Colonel anybody else who

[035-064]might call here with the same justification for calling which his old

[035-065]friendship gives him?" Priscilla endeavoured to explain to her that

[035-066]her husband's known wishes ought to hinder her from doing so. "My

[035-067]husband should have remained with me to express his wishes," Mrs.

[035-068]Trevelyan replied.

[035-069]Neither could Mrs. Stanbury nor could Priscilla bring herself to say

[035-070]that the man should not be admitted into the house. In the course of

[035-071]the debate, in the heat of her anger, Mrs. Trevelyan declared that

[035-072]were any such threat held out to her, she would leave the house and

[035-073]see Colonel Osborne in the street, or at the inn.

[035-074]"No, Emily; no," said Nora.

[035-075]"But I will. I will not submit to be treated as a guilty woman, or as

[035-076]a prisoner. They may say what they like; but I won't be shut up."

[035-077]"No one has tried to shut you up," said Priscilla.

[035-078]"You are afraid of that old woman at [[exeter|Exeter]]," said Mrs. Trevelyan;

[035-079]for by this time the facts of the Stanbury correspondence had

[035-080]all been elicited in general conversation; "and yet you know how

[035-081]uncharitable and malicious she is."

[035-082]"We are not afraid of her," said Priscilla. "We are afraid of nothing

[035-083]but of doing wrong."

[035-084]"And will it be wrong to let an old [[social-class|gentleman]] come into the house,"

[035-085]said Nora, "who is nearly sixty, and who has known us ever since we

[035-086]were born?"

[035-087]"If he is nearly sixty, Priscilla," said Mrs. Stanbury, "that does

[035-088]seem to make a difference." Mrs. Stanbury herself was only just

[035-089]sixty, and she felt herself to be quite an old woman.

[035-090]"They may be devils at eighty," said Priscilla.

[035-091]"Colonel Osborne is not a devil at all," said Nora.

[035-092]"But mamma is so foolish," said Priscilla. "The man's age does not

[035-093]matter in the least."

[035-094]"I beg your pardon, my dear," said Mrs. Stanbury, very humbly.

[035-095]At that time the quarrel was raging, but afterwards came the

[035-096]reconciliation. Had it not been for the Stanbury correspondence the

[035-097]fact of Colonel Osborne's threatened visit would have been admitted

[035-098]as a thing necessary--as a disagreeable necessity; but how was

[035-099]the visit to be admitted and passed over in the teeth of that

[035-100]correspondence? Priscilla felt very keenly the peculiar cruelty

[035-101]of her position. Of course Aunt Stanbury would hear of the visit.

[035-102]Indeed, any secrecy in the matter was not compatible with Priscilla's

[035-103]ideas of honesty. Her aunt had apologised humbly for having said

[035-104]that Colonel Osborne had been at Nuncombe. That apology, doubtless,

[035-105]had been due. Colonel Osborne had not been at Nuncombe when the

[035-106]accusation had been made, and the accusation had been unjust and

[035-107]false. But his coming had been spoken of by Priscilla in her own

[035-108]letters as an occurrence which was quite out of the question. Her

[035-109]anger against her aunt had been for saying that the man had come,

[035-110]not for objecting to such a visit. And now the man was coming, and

[035-111]Aunt Stanbury would know all about it. How great, how terrible, how

[035-112]crushing would be Aunt Stanbury's triumph!

[035-113]"I must write and tell her," said Priscilla.

[035-114]"I am sure I shall not object," said Mrs. Trevelyan.

[035-115]"And Hugh must be told," said Mrs. Stanbury.

[035-116]"You may tell all the world, if you like," said Mrs. Trevelyan.

[035-117]In this way it was settled among them that Colonel Osborne was to

[035-118]be received. On the next morning, Friday morning, Colonel Osborne,

[035-119]doubtless having heard something of Mrs. Crocket from his friend

[035-120]at Cockchaffington, was up early, and had himself driven over to

[035-121]Nuncombe Putney before breakfast. The ever-watchful Bozzle was, of

[035-122]course, at his heels,--or rather, not at his heels on the first two

[035-123]miles of the journey; for Bozzle, with painful zeal, had made himself

[035-124]aware of all the facts, and had started on the Nuncombe Putney road

[035-125]half an hour before the Colonel's fly was in motion. And when the

[035-126]fly passed him he was lying discreetly hidden behind an old oak. The

[035-127]driver, however, had caught a glimpse of him as he was topping a

[035-128]hill, and having seen him about on the previous day, and perceiving

[035-129]that he was dressed in a decent coat and trousers, and that,

[035-130]nevertheless, he was not a gentleman, began to suspect that he

[035-131]was--somebody. There was a great deal said afterwards about Bozzle in

[035-132]Mrs. Clegg's yard at Lessboro'; but the Lessboro' mind was never able

[035-133]to satisfy itself altogether respecting Bozzle and his mission. As

[035-134]to Colonel Osborne and his mission, the Lessboro' mind did satisfy

[035-135]itself with much [[truth|certainty]]. The horse was hardly taken from out of

[035-136]Colonel Osborne's fly in Mrs. Crocket's yard when Bozzle stepped

[035-137]into the village by a path which he had already discovered, and soon

[035-138]busied himself among the tombs in the churchyard. Now, one corner of

[035-139]the churchyard was immediately opposite to the iron gate leading into

[035-140]the Clock House. "Drat 'un," said the wooden-legged postman, still

[035-141]sitting on his donkey, to Mrs. Crocket's ostler, "if there be'ant the

[035-142]chap as was here yesterday when I was a starting, and I zeed 'un in

[035-143]Lezbro' street thick very morning." "He be'ant arter no good, that

[035-144]'un," said the ostler. After that a close watch was kept upon the

[035-145]watcher.

[035-146][Illustration: The wooden-legged postman of Nuncombe Putney.]

[035-147]In the meantime, Colonel Osborne had ordered his breakfast at the

[035-148]Stag and Antlers, and had asked questions as to the position of the

[035-149]Clock House. He was altogether ignorant of Mr. Bozzle, although Mr.

[035-150]Bozzle had been on his track now for two days and two nights. He had

[035-151]determined, as he came on to Nuncombe Putney, that he would not be

[035-152]shame-faced about his visit to Mrs. Trevelyan. It is possible that

[035-153]he was not so keen in the matter as he had been when he planned his

[035-154]journey in [[london|London]]; and, it may be, that he really tried to make

[035-155]himself believe that he had come all the way to the confines of

[035-156]Dartmoor to see the porch of Cockchaffington Church. The session

[035-157]in London was over, and it was necessary for such a man as Colonel

[035-158]Osborne that he should do something with himself before he went down

[035-159]to the Scotch grouse. He had long desired to see something of the

[035-160]most picturesque county in England; and now, as he sat eating his

[035-161]breakfast in Mrs. Crocket's parlour, he almost looked upon his dear

[035-162]Emily as a subsidiary attraction. "Oh, that's the Clock House,"

[035-163]he said to Mrs. Crocket. "No, I have not the pleasure of knowing

[035-164]Mrs. Stanbury; very respectable lady, so I have heard; widow of a

[035-165]clergyman; ah, yes; son up in London; I know him;--always writing

[035-166]books is he? Very clever, I dare say. But there's a lady,--indeed two

[035-167]ladies,--whom I do know. Mrs. Trevelyan is there, I think,--and Miss

[035-168]Rowley."

[035-169]"You be'ant Muster Trevelyan, be you?" said Mrs. Crocket, looking at

[035-170]him very hard.

[035-171]"No, I'm not Mr. Trevelyan."

[035-172]"Nor yet 'the Colonel' they doo be talking about?"

[035-173]"Well, yes, I am a colonel. I don't know why anybody should talk

[035-174]about me. I'll just step out now, however, and see my friends."

[035-175]"It's madam's lover," said Mrs. Crocket to herself, "as sure as eggs

[035-176]is eggs." As she said so, Colonel Osborne boldly walked across the

[035-177]village and pulled the bell at the iron gate, while Bozzle, crouching

[035-178]among the tombs, saw the handle in his hand. "There he is," said

[035-179]Priscilla. Everybody in the Clock House had known that the fly,

[035-180]which they had seen, had brought "the Colonel" into Nuncombe Putney.

[035-181]Everybody had known that he had breakfasted at the Stag and Antlers.

[035-182]And everybody now knew that he was at the gate ringing the bell.

[035-183]"Into the drawing-room," said Mrs. Stanbury, with a fearful,

[035-184]tremulous whisper, to the girl who went across the little garden

[035-185]in front to open the iron gate. The girl felt as though Apollyon

[035-186]were there, and as though she were called upon to admit Apollyon.

[035-187]Mrs. Stanbury having uttered her whisper, hurried away up-stairs.

[035-188]Priscilla held her ground in the parlour, determined to be near the

[035-189]scene of action if there might be need. And it must be acknowledged

[035-190]that she peeped from behind the curtain, anxious to catch a glimpse

[035-191]of the terrible man, whose coming to Nuncombe Putney she regarded as

[035-192]so severe a misfortune.
