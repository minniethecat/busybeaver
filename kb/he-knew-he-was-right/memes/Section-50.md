## Section 50

[050-001]"Gone; are they? As it is past wishing that they hadn't come, it's

[050-002]the best thing they could do now. And who is to pay the rent of the

[050-003]house, now they have gone?" As this was a point on which Dorothy was

[050-004]not prepared to trouble herself at present, she made no answer to the

[050-005]question.

[050-006]Dorothy at this time was in a state of very great perturbation on her

[050-007]own account. The reader may perhaps remember that she had been much

[050-008]startled by a proposition that had been made to her in reference

[050-009]to her future life. Her aunt had suggested to her that she should

[050-010]become--Mrs. Gibson. She had not as yet given any answer to that

[050-011]proposition, and had indeed found it to be quite impossible to speak

[050-012]about it at all. But there can be no doubt that the suggestion had

[050-013]opened out to her altogether new views of life. Up to the moment

[050-014]of her aunt's speech to her, the idea of her becoming a married

[050-015]woman had never presented itself to her. In her humility it had

[050-016]not occurred to her that she should be counted as one among the

[050-017]candidates for [[marriage|matrimony]]. Priscilla had taught her to regard

[050-018]herself,--indeed, they had both so regarded themselves,--as born to

[050-019]eat and drink, as little as might be, and then to die. Now, when she

[050-020]was told that she could, if she pleased, become Mrs. Gibson, she was

[050-021]almost lost in a whirl of new and confused ideas. Since her aunt had

[050-022]spoken, Mr. Gibson himself had dropped a hint or two which seemed to

[050-023]her to indicate that he also must be in the secret. There had been

[050-024]a party, with a supper, at Mrs. Crumbie's, at which both the Miss

[050-025]Frenches had been present. But Mr. Gibson had taken her, Dorothy

[050-026]Stanbury, out to supper, leaving both Camilla and Arabella behind

[050-027]him in the drawing-room! During the quarter of an hour afterwards

[050-028]in which the ladies were alone while the gentlemen were eating

[050-029]and drinking, both Camilla and Arabella continued to wreak their

[050-030]vengeance. They asked questions about Mrs. Trevelyan, and suggested

[050-031]that Mr. Gibson might be sent over to put things right. But Miss

[050-032]Stanbury had heard them, and had fallen upon them with a heavy hand.

[050-033]"There's a good deal expected of Mr. Gibson, my dears," she said,

[050-034]"which it seems to me Mr. Gibson is not inclined to perform."

[050-035]"It is quite indifferent to us what Mr. Gibson may be inclined to

[050-036]perform," said Arabella. "I'm sure we shan't interfere with Miss

[050-037]Dorothy."

[050-038]As this was said quite out loud before all the other ladies, Dorothy

[050-039]was overcome with shame. But her aunt comforted her when they were

[050-040]again at home.

[050-041]"Laws, my dear; what does it matter? When you're Mrs. Gibson, you'll

[050-042]be proud of it all."

[050-043]Was it then really written in the book of the Fates that she, Dorothy

[050-044]Stanbury, was to become Mrs. Gibson? Poor Dorothy began to feel

[050-045]that she was called upon to exercise an amount of thought and

[050-046]personal decision to which she had not been accustomed. Hitherto,

[050-047]in the things which she had done, or left undone, she had received

[050-048]instructions which she could obey. Had her mother and Priscilla

[050-049]told her positively not to go to her aunt's house, she would have

[050-050]remained at Nuncombe without complaint. Had her aunt since her coming

[050-051]given her orders as to her mode of life,--enjoined, for instance,

[050-052]additional church attendances, or desired her to perform menial

[050-053]services in the house,--she would have obeyed, from custom, without a

[050-054]word. But when she was told that she was to marry Mr. Gibson, it did

[050-055]seem to her to be necessary to do something more than obey. Did she

[050-056]love Mr. Gibson? She tried hard to teach herself to think that she

[050-057]might learn to love him. He was a nice-looking man enough, with sandy

[050-058]hair, and a head rather bald, with thin lips, and a narrow nose, who

[050-059]certainly did preach drawling sermons; but of whom everybody said

[050-060]that he was a very excellent clergyman. He had a house and an [[money|income]],

[050-061]and all [[exeter|Exeter]] had long since decided that he was a man who would

[050-062]certainly marry. He was one of those men of whom it may be said that

[050-063]they have no possible claim to remain unmarried. He was fair game,

[050-064]and unless he surrendered himself to be bagged before long, would

[050-065]subject himself to just and loud complaint. The Miss Frenches had

[050-066]been aware of this, and had thought to make sure of him among them.

[050-067]It was a little hard upon them that the old maid of the Close, as

[050-068]they always called Miss Stanbury, should interfere with them when

[050-069]their booty was almost won. And they felt it to be the harder because

[050-070]Dorothy Stanbury was, as they thought, so poor a creature. That

[050-071]Dorothy herself should have any doubt as to accepting Mr. Gibson, was

[050-072]an idea that never occurred to them. But Dorothy had her doubts. When

[050-073]she came to think of it, she remembered that she had never as yet

[050-074]spoken a word to Mr. Gibson, beyond such little trifling remarks as

[050-075]are made over a tea-table. She might learn to love him, but she did

[050-076]not think that she loved him as yet.

[050-077]"I don't suppose all this will make any difference to Mr. Gibson,"

[050-078]said Miss Stanbury to her niece, on the morning after the receipt of

[050-079]Priscilla's note stating that the Trevelyans had left Nuncombe.

[050-080]Dorothy always blushed when Mr. Gibson's name was mentioned, and she

[050-081]blushed now. But she did not at all understand her aunt's allusion.

[050-082]"I don't know what you mean, aunt," she said.

[050-083]"Well, you know, my dear, what they say about Mrs. Trevelyan and the

[050-084][[clock-house|Clock House]] is not very nice. If Mr. Gibson were to turn round and

[050-085]say that the connection wasn't pleasant, no one would have a right to

[050-086]complain."

[050-087]The faint customary blush on Dorothy's cheeks which Mr. Gibson's name

[050-088]had produced now covered her whole face even up to the roots of her

[050-089]hair. "If he believes bad of mamma, I'm sure, Aunt Stanbury, I don't

[050-090]want to see him again."

[050-091]"That's all very fine, my dear, but a man has to think of himself,

[050-092]you know."

[050-093]"Of course he thinks of himself. Why shouldn't he? I dare say he

[050-094]thinks of himself more than I do."

[050-095]"Dorothy, don't be a fool. A good husband isn't to be caught every

[050-096]day."

[050-097]"Aunt Stanbury, I don't want to catch any man."

[050-098]"Dorothy, don't be a fool."

[050-099]"I must say it. I don't suppose Mr. Gibson thinks of me the least in

[050-100]the world."

[050-101]"Psha! I tell you he does."

[050-102]"But as for mamma and Priscilla, I never could like anybody for a

[050-103]moment who would be ashamed of them."

[050-104]She was most anxious to declare that, as far as she knew herself

[050-105]and her own wishes at present, she entertained no partiality for Mr.

[050-106]Gibson,--no feeling which could become partiality even if Mr. Gibson

[050-107]was to declare himself willing to accept her mother and her sister

[050-108]with herself. But she did not dare to say so. There was an instinct

[050-109]within her which made it almost impossible to her to express an

[050-110]objection to a suitor before the suitor had declared himself to be

[050-111]one. She could speak out as touching her mother and her sister,--but

[050-112]as to her own feelings she could express neither assent nor dissent.

[050-113]"I should like to have it settled soon," said Miss Stanbury, in a

[050-114]melancholy voice. Even to this Dorothy could make no reply. What did

[050-115]soon mean? Perhaps in the course of a year or two. "If it could be

[050-116]arranged by the end of this week, it would be a great comfort to me."

[050-117]Dorothy almost fell off her chair, and was stricken altogether dumb.

[050-118]"I told you, I think, that Brooke Burgess is coming here?"

[050-119]"You said he was to come some day."

[050-120]"He is to be here on Monday. I haven't seen him for more than twelve

[050-121]years; and now he's to be here next week? Dear, dear! When I think

[050-122]sometimes of all the hard words that have been spoken, and the harder

[050-123]thoughts that have been in people's minds, I often regret that the

[050-124]money ever came to me at all. I could have done without it, very

[050-125]well,--very well."

[050-126]"But all the unpleasantness is over now, aunt."

[050-127]"I don't know about that. Unpleasantness of that kind is apt to

[050-128]rankle long. But I wasn't going to give up my rights. Nobody but a

[050-129]coward does that. They talked of going to law and trying the will,

[050-130]but they wouldn't have got much by that. And then they abused me for

[050-131]two years. When they had done and got sick of it, I told them they

[050-132]should have it all back again as soon as I am dead. It won't be long

[050-133]now. This Burgess is the elder nephew, and he shall have it all."

[050-134]"Is not he grateful?"

[050-135]"No. Why should he be grateful? I don't do it for special love of

[050-136]him. I don't want his gratitude; nor anybody's gratitude. Look at

[050-137]Hugh. I did love him."

[050-138]"I am grateful, Aunt Stanbury."

[050-139]"Are you, my dear? Then show it by being a good wife to Mr. Gibson,

[050-140]and a happy wife. I want to get everything settled while Burgess is

[050-141]here. If he is to have it, why should I keep him out of it whilst I

[050-142]live? I wonder whether Mr. Gibson would mind coming and living here,

[050-143]Dolly?"

[050-144]The thing was coming so near to her that Dorothy began to feel that

[050-145]she must, in truth, make up her mind, and let her aunt know also how

[050-146]it had been made up. She was sensible enough to perceive that if

[050-147]she did not prepare herself for the occasion she would find herself

[050-148]hampered by an engagement simply because her aunt had presumed that

[050-149]it was out of the question that she should not acquiesce. She would

[050-150]drift into marriage with Mr. Gibson against her will. Her greatest

[050-151]difficulty was the fact that her aunt clearly had no doubt on the

[050-152]subject. And as for herself, hitherto her feelings did not, on either

[050-153]side, go beyond doubts. Assuredly it would be a very good thing for

[050-154]her to become Mrs. Gibson, if only she could create for herself some

[050-155]attachment for the man. At the present moment her aunt said nothing

[050-156]more about Mr. Gibson, having her mind much occupied with the coming

[050-157]of Mr. Brooke Burgess.

[050-158]"I remember him twenty years ago and more; as nice a boy as you would

[050-159]wish to see. His father was the fourth of the brothers. Dear, dear!

[050-160]Three of them are gone; and the only one remaining is old Barty, whom

[050-161]no one ever loved."

[050-162]The Burgesses had been great people in Exeter, having been both

[050-163]bankers and brewers there, but the light of the family had paled;

[050-164]and though Bartholomew Burgess, of whom Miss Stanbury declared that

[050-165]no one had ever loved him, still had a share in the bank, it was

[050-166]well understood in the city that the real wealth in the firm of

[050-167]Cropper and Burgess belonged to the Cropper family. Indeed the most

[050-168]considerable portion of the fortune that had been realised by old

[050-169]Mr. Burgess had come into the possession of Miss Stanbury herself.

[050-170]Bartholomew Burgess had never forgiven his brother's will, and

[050-171]between him and Jemima Stanbury the feud was irreconcileable. The

[050-172]next brother, Tom Burgess, had been a solicitor at Liverpool, and had

[050-173]done well there. But Miss Stanbury knew nothing of the Tom Burgesses

[050-174]as she called them. The fourth brother, Harry Burgess, had been a

[050-175]clergyman, and this Brooke Burgess, Junior, who was now coming to

[050-176]the Close, had been left with a widowed mother, the eldest of a

[050-177]large family. It need not now be told at length how there had been

[050-178]ill-blood also between this clergyman and the heiress. There had been

[050-179]attempts at friendship, and at one time Miss Stanbury had received

[050-180]the Rev. Harry Burgess and all his family at the Close;--but the

[050-181]attempts had not been successful; and though our old friend had never

[050-182]wavered in her determination to leave the money all back to some one

[050-183]of the Burgess family, and with this view had made a pilgrimage to

[050-184][[london|London]] some twelve years since, and had renewed her acquaintance

[050-185]with the widow and the children, still there had been no comfortable

[050-186]relations between her and any of the Burgess family. Old Barty

[050-187]Burgess, whom she met in the Close, or saw in the High Street every

[050-188]day of her life, was her great enemy. He had tried his best,--so at

[050-189]least she was convinced,--to drive her out of the pale of society,

[050-190]years upon years ago, by saying evil things of her. She had conquered

[050-191]in that combat. Her victory had been complete, and she had triumphed

[050-192]after a most signal fashion. But this triumph did not silence Barty's

[050-193]tongue, nor soften his heart. When she prayed to be forgiven, as she

[050-194]herself forgave others, she always exempted Barty Burgess from her

[050-195]prayers. There are things which flesh and blood cannot do. She had

[050-196]not liked Harry Burgess' widow, nor for the matter of that, Harry

[050-197]Burgess himself. When she had last seen the children she had not

[050-198]liked any of them much, and had had her doubts even as to Brooke. But

[050-199]with that branch of the family she was willing to try again. Brooke

[050-200]was now coming to the Close, having received, however, an intimation,

[050-201]that if, during his visit to Exeter, he chose to see his Uncle Barty,

[050-202]any such intercourse must be kept quite in the background. While he

[050-203]remained in Miss Stanbury's house he was to remain there as though

[050-204]there were no such person as Mr. Bartholomew Burgess in Exeter.
