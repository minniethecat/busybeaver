## Section 108

[108-001]"But I don't know that that will suit Camilla at all."

[108-002]"She can name any other day she pleases, of course;--that is, in

[108-003]May."

[108-004]"But why is this to be?"

[108-005]"There are things about money, Mrs. French, which I cannot arrange

[108-006]sooner. And I find that unfortunately I must go up to [[london|London]]." Though

[108-007]many other questions were asked, nothing further was got out of

[108-008]Mr. Gibson on that occasion; and he left the house with a perfect

[108-009]understanding on his own part,--and on that of Mrs. French,--that the

[108-010]marriage was postponed till some day still to be fixed, but which

[108-011]could not and should not be before the 29th of April. Mrs. French

[108-012]asked him why he did not come up and see Camilla. He replied,--false

[108-013]man that he was,--that he had hoped to have seen her this morning,

[108-014]and that he would come again before the week was over.

[108-015]Then it was that Camilla spoke her mind out plainly. "I shall go to

[108-016]his house at once," she said, "and find out all about it. I don't

[108-017]understand it. I don't understand it at all; and I won't put up with

[108-018]it. He shall know who he has to deal with, if he plays tricks upon

[108-019]me. Mamma, I wonder you let him out of the house, till you had made

[108-020]him come back to his old day."

[108-021]"What could I do, my dear?"

[108-022]"What could you do? Shake him out of it,--as I would have done. But

[108-023]he didn't dare to tell me,--because he is a coward."

[108-024]Camilla in all this showed her spirit; but she allowed her anger

[108-025]to hurry her away into an indiscretion. Arabella was present, and

[108-026]Camilla should have repressed her rage.

[108-027]"I don't think he's at all a coward," said Arabella.

[108-028]"That's my business. I suppose I'm entitled to know what he is better

[108-029]than you."

[108-030]"All the same I don't think Mr. Gibson is at all a coward," said

[108-031]Arabella, again pleading the cause of the man who had misused her.

[108-032]"Now, Arabella, I won't take any interference from you; mind that.

[108-033]I say it was cowardly, and he should have come to me. It's my

[108-034]concern, and I shall go to him. I'm not going to be stopped by any

[108-035]shilly-shally nonsense, when my future respectability, perhaps, is

[108-036]at stake. All [[exeter|Exeter]] knows that the marriage is to take place on the

[108-037]31st of this month."

[108-038]On the next day Camilla absolutely did go to Mr. Gibson's house at

[108-039]an early hour, at nine, when, as she thought, he would surely be at

[108-040]breakfast. But he had flown. He had left Exeter that morning by an

[108-041]early train, and his servant thought that he had gone to London. On

[108-042]the next morning Camilla got a note from him, written in London.

[108-043]It affected to be very cheery and affectionate, beginning "Dearest

[108-044]Cammy," and alluding to the postponement of his wedding as though

[108-045]it were a thing so fixed as to require no further question. Camilla

[108-046]answered this letter, still in much wrath, complaining, protesting,

[108-047]expostulating;--throwing in his teeth the fact that the day had been

[108-048]fixed by him, and not by her. And she added a postscript in the

[108-049]following momentous words:--"If you have any respect for the name of

[108-050]your future wife, you will fall back upon your first arrangement." To

[108-051]this she got simply a line of an answer, declaring that this falling

[108-052]back was impossible, and then nothing was heard of him for ten days.

[108-053]He had gone from Tuesday to Saturday week;--and the first that

[108-054]Camilla saw of him was his presence in the reading desk when he

[108-055]chaunted the cathedral service as priest-vicar on the Sunday.

[108-056]At this time Arabella was very ill, and was confined to her bed.

[108-057]Mr. Martin declared that her system had become low from over

[108-058]anxiety,--that she was nervous, weak, and liable to hysterics,--that

[108-059]her feelings were in fact too many for her,--and that her efforts to

[108-060]overcome them, and to face the realities of the world, had exhausted

[108-061]her. This was, of course, not said openly, at the town-cross of

[108-062]Exeter; but such was the opinion which Mr. Martin gave in confidence

[108-063]to the mother. "Fiddle-de-dee!" said Camilla, when she was told of

[108-064]feelings, susceptibilities, and hysterics. At the present moment she

[108-065]had a claim to the undivided interest of the family, and she believed

[108-066]that her sister's illness was feigned in order to defraud her of her

[108-067]rights. "My dear, she is ill," said Mrs. French. "Then let her have

[108-068]a dose of salts," said the stern Camilla. This was on the Sunday

[108-069]afternoon. Camilla had endeavoured to see Mr. Gibson as he came out

[108-070]of the cathedral, but had failed. Mr. Gibson had been detained within

[108-071]the building,--no doubt by duties connected with the choral services.

[108-072]On that evening he got a note from Camilla, and quite early on the

[108-073]Monday morning he came up to Heavitree.

[108-074]"You will find her in the drawing-room," said Mrs. French, as she

[108-075]opened the hall-door for him. There was a smile on her face as she

[108-076]spoke, but it was a forced smile. Mr. Gibson did not smile at all.

[108-077]"Is it all right with her?" he asked.

[108-078]"Well;--you had better go to her. You see, Mr. Gibson, young ladies,

[108-079]when they are going to be married, think that they ought to have

[108-080]their own way a little, just for the last time, you know." He took no

[108-081]notice of the joke, but went with slow steps up to the drawing-room.

[108-082]It would be inquiring too curiously to ask whether Camilla, when

[108-083]she embraced him, discerned that he had fortified his courage that

[108-084]morning with a glass of curacoa.

[108-085]"What does all this mean, Thomas?" was the first question that

[108-086]Camilla asked when the embrace was over.

[108-087]"All what mean, dear?"

[108-088]"This untoward delay. Thomas, you have almost broken my heart. You

[108-089]have been away, and I have not heard from you."

[108-090]"I wrote twice, Camilla."

[108-091]"And what sort of letters? If there is anything the matter, Thomas,

[108-092]you had better tell me at once." She paused, but Thomas held his

[108-093]tongue. "I don't suppose you want to kill me."

[108-094]"God forbid," said Thomas.

[108-095]"But you will. What must everybody think of me in the city when

[108-096]they find that it is put off? Poor mamma has been dreadful;--quite

[108-097]dreadful! And here is Arabella now laid up on a bed of sickness."

[108-098]This, too, was indiscreet. Camilla should have said nothing about her

[108-099]sister's sickness.

[108-100]"I have been so sorry to hear about dear Bella," said Mr. Gibson.

[108-101]"I don't suppose she's very bad," said Camilla, "but of course we all

[108-102]feel it. Of course we're upset. As for me, I bear up; because I've

[108-103]that spirit that I won't give way if it's ever so; but, upon my word,

[108-104]it tries me hard. What is the meaning of it, Thomas?"

[108-105]But Thomas had nothing to say beyond what he had said before to Mrs.

[108-106]French. He was very particular, he said, about money; and certain

[108-107]money matters made it incumbent on him not to marry before the 29th

[108-108]of April. When Camilla suggested to him that as she was to be his

[108-109]wife, she ought to know all about his money matters, he told her that

[108-110]she should,--some day. When they were married, he would tell her all.

[108-111]Camilla talked a great deal, and said some things that were very

[108-112]severe. Mr. Gibson did not enjoy his morning, but he endured the

[108-113]upbraidings of his fair one with more firmness than might perhaps

[108-114]have been expected from him. He left all the talking to Camilla; but

[108-115]when he got up to leave her, the 29th of April had been fixed, with

[108-116]some sort of assent from her, as the day on which she was really to

[108-117]become Mrs. Gibson.

[108-118]When he left the room, he again met Mrs. French on the landing-place.

[108-119]She hesitated a moment, waiting to see whether the door would be

[108-120]shut; but the door could not be shut, as Camilla was [[reputation|standing]] in

[108-121]the entrance. "Mr. Gibson," said Mrs. French, in a voice that was

[108-122]scarcely a whisper, "would you mind stepping in and seeing poor Bella

[108-123]for a moment?"

[108-124]"Why;--she is in bed," said Camilla.

[108-125]"Yes;--she is in bed; but she thinks it would be a comfort to her.

[108-126]She has seen nobody these four days except Mr. Martin, and she thinks

[108-127]it would comfort her to have a word or two with Mr. Gibson." Now

[108-128]Mr. Gibson was not only going to be Bella's brother-in-law, but

[108-129]he was also a clergyman. Camilla in her heart believed that the

[108-130]half-clerical aspect which her mother had given to the request was

[108-131]false and hypocritical. There were special reasons why Bella should

[108-132]not have wished to see Mr. Gibson in her bedroom, at any rate till

[108-133]Mr. Gibson had become her brother-in-law. The expression of such a

[108-134]wish at the present moment was almost indecent.

[108-135]"You'll be there with them?" said Camilla. Mr. Gibson blushed up to

[108-136]his ears as he heard the suggestion. "Of course you'll be there with

[108-137]them, mamma."

[108-138]"No, my dear, I think not. I fancy she wishes him to read to her,--or

[108-139]something of that sort." Then Mr. Gibson, without speaking a word,

[108-140]but still blushing up to his ears, was taken to Arabella's room; and

[108-141]Camilla, flouncing into the drawing-room, banged the door behind her.

[108-142]She had hitherto fought her battle with considerable skill and with

[108-143]great courage;--but her very success had made her imprudent. She

[108-144]had become so imperious in the great position which she had reached,

[108-145]that she could not control her temper or wait till her power was

[108-146]confirmed. The banging of that door was heard through the whole

[108-147]house, and every one knew why it was banged. She threw herself on to

[108-148]a sofa, and then, instantly rising again, paced the room with quick

[108-149]step. Could it be possible that there was treachery? Was it on the

[108-150]cards that that weak, poor creature, Bella, was intriguing once again

[108-151]to defraud her of her husband? There were different things that she

[108-152]now remembered. Arabella, in that moment of bliss in which she had

[108-153]conceived herself to be engaged to Mr. Gibson, had discarded her

[108-154]chignon. Then she had resumed it,--in all its monstrous proportions.

[108-155]Since that it had been lessened by degrees, and brought down, through

[108-156]various interesting but abnormal shapes, to a size which would hardly

[108-157]have drawn forth any anathema from Miss Stanbury. And now, on this

[108-158]very morning, Arabella had put on a clean nightcap, with muslin

[108-159]frills. It is perhaps not unnatural that a sick lady, preparing

[108-160]to receive a clergyman in her bedroom, should put on a clean

[108-161]nightcap,--but to suspicious eyes small causes suffice to create

[108-162]alarm. And if there were any such hideous wickedness in the wind, had

[108-163]Arabella any colleague in her villainy? Could it be that the mother

[108-164]was plotting against her daughter's happiness and respectability?

[108-165]Camilla was well aware that her mamma would at first have preferred

[108-166]to give Arabella to Mr. Gibson, had the choice in the matter been

[108-167]left to her. But now, when the thing had been settled before all

[108-168]the world, would not such treatment on a mother's part be equal to

[108-169]infanticide? And then as to Mr. Gibson himself! Camilla was not

[108-170]prone to think little of her own charms, but she had been unable

[108-171]not to perceive that her lover had become negligent in his personal

[108-172]attentions to her. An accepted lover, who deserves to have been

[108-173]accepted, should devote every hour at his command to his mistress.

[108-174]But Mr. Gibson had of late been so chary of his presence at

[108-175]Heavitree, that Camilla could not but have known that he took no

[108-176]delight in coming thither. She had acknowledged this to herself; but

[108-177]she had consoled herself with the reflection that marriage would make

[108-178]this all right. Mr. Gibson was not the man to stray from his wife,

[108-179]and she could trust herself to obtain a sufficient hold upon her

[108-180]husband hereafter, partly by the strength of her tongue, partly by

[108-181]the ascendency of her spirit, and partly, also, by the comforts which

[108-182]she would provide for him. She had not doubted but that it would be

[108-183]all well when they should be married;--but how if, even now, there

[108-184]should be no marriage for her? Camilla French had never heard of

[108-185]Creusa and of Jason, but as she paced her mother's drawing-room that

[108-186]morning she was a Medea in spirit. If any plot of that kind should be

[108-187]in the wind, she would do such things that all Devonshire should hear

[108-188]of her wrongs and of her revenge!
