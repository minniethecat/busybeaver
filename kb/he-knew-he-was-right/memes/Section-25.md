## Section 25

[025-001]"They will be together again before next April," Mrs. Fairfax had

[025-002]replied. But Mrs. Fairfax was a jolly dame who made the best of

[025-003]everything. Lady Milborough raised her hands in despair, and shook

[025-004]her head. "I don't suppose, though, that Mr. Glascock will go to

[025-005]Devonshire after his lady love," said Mrs. Fairfax. Lady Milborough

[025-006]again raised her hands, and again shook her head.

[025-007]Mrs. Stanbury had given an easy assent when her son proposed to her

[025-008]this new mode of life, but Priscilla had had her doubts. Like all

[025-009]women, she thought that when a man was to be separated from his wife,

[025-010]the woman must be in the wrong. And though it must be doubtless

[025-011]comfortable to go from the cottage to the [[clock-house|Clock House]], it

[025-012]would, she said, with much prudence, be very uncomfortable

[025-013]to go back from the Clock House to the cottage. Hugh replied

[025-014]very cavalierly,--generously, that is, rashly, and somewhat

[025-015]impetuously,--that he would guarantee them against any such

[025-016]degradation.

[025-017]"We don't want to be a burden upon you, my dear," said the mother.

[025-018]"You would be a great burden on me," he replied, "if you were living

[025-019]uncomfortably while I am able to make you comfortable."

[025-020]Mrs. Stanbury was soon won over by Mrs. Trevelyan, by Nora, and

[025-021]especially by the baby; and even Priscilla, after a week or two,

[025-022]began to feel that she liked their company. Priscilla was a young

[025-023]woman who read a great deal, and even had some gifts of understanding

[025-024]what she read. She borrowed books from the clergyman, and paid a

[025-025]penny a week to the landlady of the Stag and Antlers for the hire

[025-026]during half a day of the weekly newspaper. But now there came a box

[025-027]of books from [[exeter|Exeter]], and a daily paper from [[london|London]], and,--to improve

[025-028]all this,--both the new comers were able to talk with her about the

[025-029]things she read. She soon declared to her mother that she liked

[025-030]Miss Rowley much the best of the two. Mrs. Trevelyan was too fond

[025-031]of having her own way. She began to understand, she would say to

[025-032]her mother, that a man might find it difficult to live with Mrs.

[025-033]Trevelyan. "She hardly ever yields about anything," said Priscilla.

[025-034]As Miss Priscilla Stanbury was also very fond of having her own way,

[025-035]it was not surprising that she should object to that quality in this

[025-036]lady, who had come to live under the same roof with her.

[025-037]The country about [[nuncombe-putney|Nuncombe Putney]] is perhaps as pretty as any in

[025-038]England. It is beyond the river Teign, between that and Dartmoor,

[025-039]and is so lovely in all its variations of rivers, rivulets, broken

[025-040]ground, hills and dales, old broken, battered, time-worn timber,

[025-041]green knolls, rich pastures, and heathy common, that the wonder is

[025-042]that English lovers of scenery know so little of it. At the Stag and

[025-043]Antlers old Mrs. Crocket, than whom no old woman in the public line

[025-044]was ever more generous, more peppery, or more kind, kept two clean

[025-045]bed-rooms, and could cook a leg of Dartmoor mutton and make an apple

[025-046]pie against any woman in Devonshire. "Drat your fish!" she would say,

[025-047]when some self-indulgent and exacting traveller would wish for more

[025-048]than these accustomed viands. "Cock you up with dainties! If you

[025-049]can't eat your victuals without fish, you must go to Exeter. And

[025-050]then you'll get it stinking mayhap." Now Priscilla Stanbury and Mrs.

[025-051]Crocket were great friends, and there had been times of deep want,

[025-052]in which Mrs. Crocket's friendship had been very serviceable to the

[025-053]ladies at the cottage. The three young women had been to the inn one

[025-054]morning to ask after a conveyance from Nuncombe Putney to Princetown,

[025-055]and had found that a four-wheeled open carriage with an old horse

[025-056]and a very young driver could be hired there. "We have never dreamed

[025-057]of such a thing," Priscilla Stanbury had said, "and the only time

[025-058]I was at Princetown I walked there and back." So they had called at

[025-059]the Stag and Antlers, and Mrs. Crocket had told them her mind upon

[025-060]several matters.

[025-061]"What a dear old woman!" said Nora, as they came away, having made

[025-062]their bargain for the open carriage.

[025-063]"I think she takes quite enough upon herself, you know," said Mrs.

[025-064]Trevelyan.

[025-065]"She is a dear old woman," said Priscilla, not attending at all to

[025-066]the last words that had been spoken. "She is one of the best friends

[025-067]I have in the world. If I were to say the best out of my own family,

[025-068]perhaps I should not be wrong."

[025-069]"But she uses such very odd language for a woman," said Mrs.

[025-070]Trevelyan. Now Mrs. Crocket had certainly "dratted" and "darned" the

[025-071]boy, who wouldn't come as fast as she had wished, and had laughed

[025-072]at Mrs. Trevelyan very contemptuously, when that lady had suggested

[025-073]that the urchin, who was at last brought forth, might not be a safe

[025-074]charioteer down some of the hills.

[025-075]"I suppose I'm used to it," said Priscilla. "At any rate I know I

[025-076]like it. And I like her."

[025-077]"I dare say she's a good sort of woman," said Mrs. Trevelyan,

[025-078]"only--"

[025-079]"I am not saying anything about her being a good woman now," said

[025-080]Priscilla, interrupting the other with some vehemence, "but only that

[025-081]she is my friend."

[025-082]"I liked her of all things," said Nora. "Has she lived here always?"

[025-083]"Yes; all her life. The house belonged to her father and to her

[025-084]grandfather before her, and I think she says she has never slept

[025-085]out of it a dozen times in her life. Her husband is dead, and her

[025-086]daughters are married away, and she has the great grief and trouble

[025-087]of a ne'er-do-well son. He's away now, and she's all alone." Then

[025-088]after a pause, she continued; "I dare say it seems odd to you, Mrs.

[025-089]Trevelyan, that we should speak of the innkeeper as a dear friend;

[025-090]but you must remember that we have been poor among the poorest--and

[025-091]are so indeed now. We only came into our present house to receive

[025-092]you. That is where we used to live," and she pointed to the tiny

[025-093]cottage, which now that it was dismantled and desolate, looked to be

[025-094]doubly poor. "There have been times when we should have gone to bed

[025-095]very hungry if it had not been for Mrs. Crocket."

[025-096]Later in the day Mrs. Trevelyan, finding Priscilla alone, had

[025-097]apologized for what she had said about the old woman. "I was very

[025-098]thoughtless and forgetful, but I hope you will not be angry with me.

[025-099]I will be ever so fond of her if you will forgive me."

[025-100]"Very well," said Priscilla, smiling; "on those conditions I will

[025-101]forgive you." And from that time there sprang up something like

[025-102]a feeling of friendship between Priscilla and Mrs. Trevelyan.

[025-103]Nevertheless Priscilla was still of opinion that the Clock House

[025-104]arrangement was dangerous, and should never have been made; and Mrs.

[025-105]Stanbury, always timid of her own nature, began to fear that it must

[025-106]be so, as soon as she was removed from the influence of her son. She

[025-107]did not see much even of the few neighbours who lived around her, but

[025-108]she fancied that people looked at her in church as though she had

[025-109]done that which she ought not to have done, in taking herself to a

[025-110]big and comfortable house for the sake of lending her protection to a

[025-111]lady who was separated from her husband. It was not that she believed

[025-112]that Mrs. Trevelyan had been wrong; but that, knowing herself to be

[025-113]weak, she fancied that she and her daughter would be enveloped in the

[025-114]danger and suspicion which could not but attach themselves to the

[025-115]lady's condition, instead of raising the lady out of the cloud,--as

[025-116]would have been the case had she herself been strong. Mrs. Trevelyan,

[025-117]who was sharpsighted and clear-witted, soon saw that it was so, and

[025-118]spoke to Priscilla on the subject before she had been a fortnight in

[025-119]the house. "I am afraid your mother does not like our being here,"

[025-120]she said.

[025-121]"How am I to answer that?" Priscilla replied.

[025-122]"Just tell the truth."

[025-123]"The truth is so uncivil. At first I did not like it. I disliked it

[025-124]very much."

[025-125]"Why did you give way?"

[025-126]"I didn't give way. Hugh talked my mother over. Mamma does what I

[025-127]tell her, except when Hugh tells her something else. I was afraid,

[025-128]because, down here, knowing nothing of the world, I didn't wish

[025-129]that we, little people, should be mixed up in the quarrels and

[025-130]disagreements of those who are so much bigger."

[025-131]"I don't know who it is that is big in this matter."

[025-132]"You are big,--at any rate by comparison. But now it must go on. The

[025-133]house has been taken, and my fears are over as regards you. What you

[025-134]observe in mamma is only the effect, not yet quite worn out, of what

[025-135]I said before you came. You may be quite sure of this,--that we

[025-136]neither of us believe a word against you. Your position is a very

[025-137]unfortunate one; but if it can be remedied by your staying here with

[025-138]us, pray stay with us."

[025-139]"It cannot be remedied," said Emily; "but we could not be anywhere

[025-140]more comfortable than we are here."

[025-141]CHAPTER XV.

[025-142]WHAT THEY SAID ABOUT IT IN THE CLOSE.

[025-143]When Miss Stanbury, in the Close at Exeter, was first told of the

[025-144]arrangement that had been made at Nuncombe Putney, she said some very

[025-145]hard words as to the thing that had been done. She was quite sure

[025-146]that Mrs. Trevelyan was no better than she should be. Ladies who were

[025-147]separated from their husbands never were any better than they should

[025-148]be. And what was to be thought of any woman, who, when separated from

[025-149]her husband, would put herself under the protection of such a Paladin

[025-150]as [[hugh-stanbury|Hugh Stanbury]]? She heard the tidings of course from Dorothy, and

[025-151]spoke her mind even to Dorothy plainly enough; but it was to Martha

[025-152]that she expressed herself with her fullest vehemence.

[025-153]"We always knew," she said, "that my brother had married an

[025-154]addle-pated, silly woman, one of the most unsuited to be the mistress

[025-155]of a clergyman's house that ever a man set eyes on; but I didn't

[025-156]think she'd allow herself to be led into such a stupid thing as

[025-157]this."

[025-158]"I don't suppose the lady has done anything amiss,--any more than

[025-159]combing her husband's hair, and the like of that," said Martha.

[025-160]"Don't tell me! Why, by their own story, she has got a lover."

[025-161]"But he ain't to come after her down here, I suppose. And as for

[025-162]lovers, ma'am, I'm told that the most of 'em have 'em up in London.

[025-163]But it don't mean much, only just idle talking and gallivanting."

[025-164]"When women can't keep themselves from idle talking with strange

[025-165]gentlemen, they are very far gone on the road to the devil. That's

[025-166]my notion. And that was everybody's notion a few years ago. But now,

[025-167]what with divorce bills, and women's rights, and penny papers, and

[025-168]false hair, and married women being just like giggling girls, and

[025-169]giggling girls knowing just as much as married women, when a woman

[025-170]has been married a year or two she begins to think whether she mayn't

[025-171]have more fun for her money by living apart from her husband."

[025-172]"Miss Dorothy says--"

[025-173]"Oh, bother what Miss Dorothy says! Miss Dorothy only knows what

[025-174]it has suited that scamp, her brother, to tell her. I understand

[025-175]this woman has come away because of a lover; and if that's so, my

[025-176]sister-in-law is very wrong to receive her. The temptation of the

[025-177]Clock House has been too much for her. It's not my doing; that's

[025-178]all."

[025-179]That evening Miss Stanbury and Dorothy went out to tea at the house

[025-180]of Mrs. MacHugh, and there the matter was very much discussed. The

[025-181]family of the Trevelyans was known by name in these parts, and the

[025-182]fact of Mrs. Trevelyan having been sent to live in a Devonshire

[025-183]village, with Devonshire ladies who had a relation in Exeter so

[025-184]well esteemed as Miss Stanbury of the Close, were circumstances of

[025-185]themselves sufficient to ensure a considerable amount of prestige

[025-186]at the city tea-table for the tidings of this unfortunate family

[025-187]quarrel. Some reticence was of course necessary because of the

[025-188]presence of Miss Stanbury and of Dorothy. To Miss Stanbury herself

[025-189]Mrs. MacHugh and Mrs. Crumbie, of Cronstadt House, did not scruple

[025-190]to express themselves very plainly, and to whisper a question as to

[025-191]what was to be done should the lover make his appearance at Nuncombe

[025-192]Putney; but they who spoke of the matter before Dorothy, were at

[025-193]first more charitable, or, at least, more forbearing. Mr. Gibson,

[025-194]who was one of the minor canons, and the two Miss Frenches from

[025-195]Heavitree, who had the [[honour|reputation]] of hunting unmarried clergymen in

[025-196]couples, seemed to have heard all about it. When Mrs. MacHugh and

[025-197]Miss Stanbury, with Mr. and Mrs. Crumbie, had seated themselves at

[025-198]their whist-table, the younger people were able to express their

[025-199]opinions without danger of interruption or of rebuke. It was known

[025-200]to all Exeter by this time, that Dorothy Stanbury's mother had gone

[025-201]to the Clock House, and that she had done so in order that Mrs.

[025-202]Trevelyan might have a home. But it was not yet known whether anybody

[025-203]had called upon them. There was Mrs. Merton, the wife of the present

[025-204]parson of Nuncombe, who had known the Stanburys for the last twenty

[025-205]years; and there was Mrs. Ellison of Lessboro', who lived only four

[025-206]miles from Nuncombe, and who kept a pony-carriage. It would be a

[025-207]great thing to know how these ladies had behaved in so difficult and

[025-208]embarrassing a position. Mrs. Trevelyan and her sister had now been

[025-209]at Nuncombe Putney for more than a fortnight, and something in that

[025-210]matter of calling must have been done,--or have been left undone. In

[025-211]answer to an ingeniously-framed question asked by Camilla French,

[025-212]Dorothy at once set the matter at rest. "Mrs. Merton," said Camilla

[025-213]French, "must find it a great thing to have two new ladies come to

[025-214]the village, especially now that she has lost you, Miss Stanbury?"
