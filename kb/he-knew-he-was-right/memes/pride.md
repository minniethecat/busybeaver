# Pride

The double-edged concept of pride in *He Knew He Was Right*, operating as both a legitimate sense of self-worth and the destructive arrogance that prevents Louis Trevelyan from recognizing his error. The novel suggests that pride, when untempered by humility, becomes the engine of tragedy.

## Overview

Pride in Trollope's novel is the psychological mechanism through which jealousy becomes catastrophe. Trevelyan's jealous suspicion is fueled by wounded pride: the thought that his wife might prefer another man's company is an injury to his masculine self-regard. The narrator identifies this as "manly pride" — a gendered form of self-regard that proves particularly resistant to correction (064-027). Rather than confronting his insecurity, Trevelyan doubles down on his authority, and pride prevents him from admitting error once his suspicions prove groundless. The novel asks whether "manly pride had deceived him" (064-032), suggesting that what presents as strength may in fact be self-destructive delusion.

## Context in Victorian Society

Victorian masculinity was closely bound up with pride. A gentleman's pride was understood as a positive quality — the foundation of honour, integrity, and public standing. But Victorian moralists also warned against the sin of pride, drawing on Christian traditions that ranked pride as the deadliest of the seven deadly sins. Trollope's portrayal of Trevelyan navigates this ambiguity: his pride is both the source of his social standing and the cause of his downfall.

## Manifestation in the Novel

### Trevelyan's Fatal Pride

Trevelyan cannot admit that he might be wrong because his entire identity is invested in being right. The novel's title encodes this tragic irony: "he knew he was right" is simultaneously a statement of epistemological confidence and a diagnosis of his fatal flaw. His pride makes correction impossible — to admit error would be to annihilate his sense of self.

### Pride and Isolation

Pride isolates Trevelyan from the social networks that might correct his delusion. He cannot confide in friends because to do so would be to admit vulnerability. He cannot accept his wife's reassurance because to need reassurance is itself a wound to pride. The novel shows how pride, which presents itself as strength, is in fact the mechanism of Trevelyan's progressive isolation and weakening.

### Dignified Pride

The novel does not treat all pride as destructive. Emily Trevelyan's refusal to admit guilt she does not bear is a form of pride that the narrative endorses — the legitimate self-respect of an innocent person. Miss Stanbury's pride in her social position, while sometimes comically rigid, is also the foundation of her independence and moral authority. The novel thus distinguishes between pride as self-respect and pride as self-idolatry.

## Key Examples

### The Refusal to Yield

At every point where Trevelyan might step back from the precipice — accepting Emily's explanation, tolerating Osborne's visits, reconciling after a quarrel — pride intervenes. He makes "a sacrifice of his political pride which he would make for no other" consideration (004-073), revealing how pride structures his decision-making even in non-domestic contexts. Each refusal to yield makes the next more necessary: having asserted his authority once, he must assert it again to maintain consistency. Pride thus generates its own escalating logic.

### The Contrast with Hugh Stanbury

Hugh Stanbury's willingness to admit uncertainty, to change his mind, and to pursue an unconventional career all reflect a relationship to pride fundamentally different from Trevelyan's. Hugh has self-respect without self-idolatry, and the novel holds him up as the normative standard against which Trevelyan's pathological pride is measured.
