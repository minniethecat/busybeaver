## Section 123

[123-001]And now I must tell you something about myself,--because

[123-002]if you are to be my sister you ought to know it all. I

[123-003]also am going to be married to a man whom I love,--oh, so

[123-004]dearly! His name is Mr. Brooke Burgess, and he is a great

[123-005]friend of my aunt's. At first she did not like our being

[123-006]engaged, because of some family reason;--but she has got

[123-007]over that, and nothing can be kinder and nicer than she

[123-008]is. We are to be married here, some day in June,--the 11th

[123-009]I think it will be. How I do wish you could have been here

[123-010]to be my bridesmaid. It would have been so nice to have

[123-011]had Hugh's sweetheart with me. He is a friend of Hugh's,

[123-012]and no doubt you will hear all about him. The worst of it

[123-013]is that we must live in [[london|London]], because my husband as will

[123-014]be,--you see I call him mine already,--is in an office

[123-015]there. And so poor Aunt Stanbury will be left all alone.

[123-016]It will be very sad, and she is so wedded to [[exeter|Exeter]] that I

[123-017]fear we shall not get her up to London.

[123-018]I would describe Mr. Burgess to you, only I do not suppose

[123-019]you would care to hear about him. He is not so tall as

[123-020]Hugh, but he is a great deal better looking. With you two

[123-021]the good looks are to be with the wife; but, with us, with

[123-022]the husband. Perhaps you think Hugh is handsome. We used

[123-023]to declare that he was the ugliest boy in the country.

[123-024]I don't suppose it makes very much difference. Brooke is

[123-025]handsome, but I don't think I should like him the less if

[123-026]he were ever so ugly.

[123-027]Do you remember hearing about the Miss Frenches when you

[123-028]were in Devonshire? There has come up such a terrible

[123-029]affair about them. A Mr. Gibson, a clergyman, was going to

[123-030]marry the younger; but has changed his mind and wants to

[123-031]take the elder. I think he was in love with her first.

[123-032]Dorothy did not say a word about the little intermediate stage of

[123-033]attachment to herself.

[123-034]All this is making a great noise in the city, and some

[123-035]people think he should be punished severely. It seems to

[123-036]me that a [[social-class|gentleman]] ought not to make such a mistake; but

[123-037]if he does, he ought to own it. I hope they will let him

[123-038]marry the elder one. Aunt Stanbury says it all comes from

[123-039]their wearing chignons. I wish you knew Aunt Stanbury,

[123-040]because she is so good. Perhaps you wear a chignon. I

[123-041]think Priscilla said that you did. It must not be large,

[123-042]if you come to see Aunt Stanbury.

[123-043]Pray write to me,--and believe that I hope to be your most

[123-044]affectionate sister,

[123-045]DOROTHY STANBURY.

[123-046]P.S.--I am so happy, and I do so hope that you will be the

[123-047]same.

[123-048]This was received only a day before the departure of the Rowleys for

[123-049]Italy, and was answered by a short note promising that Nora would

[123-050]write to her correspondent from Florence.

[123-051]There could be no doubt that Trevelyan had started with his boy,

[123-052]fearing the result of the medical or legal interference with his

[123-053]affairs which was about to be made at Sir Marmaduke's instance. He

[123-054]had written a few words to his wife, neither commencing nor ending

[123-055]his note after any usual fashion, telling her that he thought it

[123-056]expedient to travel, that he had secured the services of a nurse for

[123-057]the little boy, and that during his absence a certain [[money|income]] would,

[123-058]as heretofore, be paid to her. He said nothing as to his probable

[123-059]return, or as to her future life; nor was there anything to indicate

[123-060]whither he was going. Stanbury, however, had learned from the

[123-061]faithless and frightened Bozzle that Trevelyan's letters were to be

[123-062]sent after him to Florence. Mr. Bozzle, in giving this information,

[123-063]had acknowledged that his employer was "becoming no longer quite

[123-064]himself under his troubles," and had expressed his opinion that he

[123-065]ought to be "looked after." Bozzle had made his money; and now,

[123-066]with a grain of humanity mixed with many grains of faithlessness,

[123-067]reconciled it to himself to tell his master's secrets to his master's

[123-068]enemies. What would a counsel be able to say about his conduct

[123-069]in a court of law? That was the question which Bozzle was always

[123-070]asking himself as to his own business. That he should be abused

[123-071]by a barrister to a jury, and exposed as a spy and a fiend, was,

[123-072]he thought, a matter of course. To be so abused was a part of his

[123-073]profession. But it was expedient for him in all cases to secure some

[123-074]loop-hole of apparent duty by which he might in part escape from such

[123-075]censures. He was untrue to his employer now, because he thought that

[123-076]his employer ought to be "looked after." He did, no doubt, take a

[123-077]five-pound note from [[hugh-stanbury|Hugh Stanbury]]; but then it was necessary that

[123-078]he should live. He must be paid for his time. In this way Trevelyan

[123-079]started for Florence, and within a week afterwards the Rowleys were

[123-080]upon his track.

[123-081]Nothing had been said by Sir Marmaduke to Nora as to her lover since

[123-082]that stormy interview in which both father and daughter had expressed

[123-083]their opinions very strongly, and very little had been said by Lady

[123-084]Rowley. Lady Rowley had spoken more than once of Nora's return to

[123-085]the Mandarins, and had once alluded to it as a [[truth|certainty]]. "But I do

[123-086]not know that I shall go back," Nora had said. "My dear," the mother

[123-087]had replied, "unless you are married, I suppose your home must be

[123-088]with your parents." Nora, having made her protest, did not think it

[123-089]necessary to persevere, and so the matter was dropped. It was known,

[123-090]however, that they must all come back to London before they started

[123-091]for their seat of government, and therefore the subject did not at

[123-092]present assume its difficult aspect. There was a tacit understanding

[123-093]among them that everything should be done to make the journey

[123-094]pleasant to the young mother who was in search of her son; and, in

[123-095]addition to this, Lady Rowley had her own little understanding, which

[123-096]was very tacit indeed, that in Mr. Glascock might be found an escape

[123-097]from one of their great family difficulties.

[123-098]"You had better take this, papa," Mrs. Trevelyan had said, when she

[123-099]received from the office of Mr. Bideawhile a cheque payable to her

[123-100]order for the money sent to her by her husband's direction.

[123-101]"I do not want the man's money," said Sir Marmaduke.

[123-102]"But you are going to this place for my sake, papa;--and it is right

[123-103]that he should bear the expense for his own wife. And, papa, you must

[123-104]remember always that though his mind is distracted on this horrible

[123-105]business, he is not a bad man. No one is more liberal or more just

[123-106]about money." Sir Marmaduke's feelings on the matter were very

[123-107]much the same as those which had troubled Mr. Outhouse, and he,

[123-108]personally, refused to touch the money; but his daughter paid her own

[123-109]share of the expenses of the journey.

[123-110]They travelled at their ease, stopping at Paris, and at Geneva, and

[123-111]at Milan. Lady Rowley thought that she was taken very fast, because

[123-112]she was allowed to sleep only two nights at each of these places,

[123-113]and Sir Rowley himself thought that he had achieved something of a

[123-114]Hannibalian enterprise in taking five ladies and two maids over the

[123-115]Simplon and down into the plains of Lombardy, with nobody to protect

[123-116]him but a single courier. He had been a little nervous about it,

[123-117]being unaccustomed to European travelling, and had not at first

[123-118]realised the fact that the journey is to be made with less trouble

[123-119]than one from the Marble Arch to Mile End. "My dears," he said to his

[123-120]younger daughters, as they were rattling round the steep downward

[123-121]twists and turns of the great road, "you must sit quite still on

[123-122]these descents, or you do not know where you may go. The least thing

[123-123]would overset us." But Lucy and Sophy soon knew better, and became

[123-124]so intimate with the mountain, under the friendly guidance of their

[123-125]courier, that before the plains were reached, they were in and out,

[123-126]and here and there, and up and down, as though they had been bred

[123-127]among the valleys of the pass. There would come a ringing laugh from

[123-128]some rock above their head, and Lady Rowley looking up would see

[123-129]their dresses fluttering on a pinnacle which appeared to her to be

[123-130]fit only for a bird; and there would be the courier behind them, with

[123-131]two parasols, and a shawl, and a cloak, and an eye-glass, and a fine

[123-132]pair of grizzled whiskers. They made an Alpine club of their own,

[123-133]refusing to admit their father because he would not climb up a rock,

[123-134]and Nora thought of the letters about it which she would write to

[123-135]her lover,--only that she had determined that she would not write to

[123-136]him at all without telling her mother,--and Mrs. Trevelyan would for

[123-137]moments almost forget that she had been robbed of her child.

[123-138]From Milan they went on to Florence, and though they were by that

[123-139]time quite at home in Italy, and had become critical judges of

[123-140]Italian inns and Italian railways, they did not find that journey

[123-141]to be quite so pleasant. There is a romance to us still in the name

[123-142]of Italy which a near view of many details in the country fails to

[123-143]realise. Shall we say that a journey through Lombardy is about as

[123-144]interesting as one through the flats of Cambridgeshire and the fens

[123-145]of Norfolk? And the station of Bologna is not an interesting spot

[123-146]in which to spend an hour or two, although it may be conceded that

[123-147]provisions may be had there much better than any that can be procured

[123-148]at our own railway stations. From thence they went, still by rail,

[123-149]over the Apennines, and unfortunately slept during the whole time.

[123-150]The courier had assured them that if they would only look out they

[123-151]would see the castles of which they had read in novels; but the day

[123-152]had been very hot, and Sir Marmaduke had been cross, and Lady Rowley

[123-153]had been weary, and so not a castle was seen. "Pistoia, me lady,

[123-154]this," said the courier opening the door;--"to stop half an hour."

[123-155]"Oh, why was it not Florence?" Another hour and a half! So they

[123-156]all went to sleep again, and were very tired when they reached the

[123-157]beautiful city.

[123-158]During the next day they rested at their inn, and sauntered through

[123-159]the Duomo, and broke their necks looking up at the inimitable glories

[123-160]of the campanile. Such a one as Sir Marmaduke had of course not come

[123-161]to Florence without introductions. The Foreign Office is always very

[123-162]civil to its next-door neighbour of the colonies,--civil and cordial,

[123-163]though perhaps a little patronising. A minister is a bigger man than

[123-164]a governor; and the smallest of the diplomatic fry are greater swells

[123-165]than even secretaries in quite important dependencies. The attache,

[123-166]though he be unpaid, dwells in a capital, and flirts with a countess.

[123-167]The governor's right-hand man is confined to an island, and dances

[123-168]with a planter's daughter. The distinction is quite understood, but

[123-169]is not incompatible with much excellent good feeling on the part of

[123-170]the superior department. Sir Marmaduke had come to Florence fairly

[123-171]provided with passports to Florentine society, and had been mentioned

[123-172]in more than one letter as the distinguished Governor of the

[123-173]Mandarins, who had been called home from his seat of government on a

[123-174]special mission of great importance. On the second day he went out

[123-175]to call at the embassy and to leave his cards. "Have you been able

[123-176]to learn whether he is here?" asked Lady Rowley of her husband in a

[123-177]whisper, as soon as they were alone.
