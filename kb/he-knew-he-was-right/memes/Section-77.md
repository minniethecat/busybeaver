## Section 77

[077-001]But the cause of Miss Stanbury's sharpest anger was not to be found

[077-002]in Mr. Gibson's conduct either before Dorothy's refusal of his offer,

[077-003]or on the occasion of his being turned out of the house. A base

[077-004]rumour was spread about the city that Dorothy Stanbury had been

[077-005]offered to Mr. Gibson, that Mr. Gibson had civilly declined the

[077-006]offer,--and that hence had arisen the wrath of the Juno of the Close.

[077-007]Now this was not to be endured by Miss Stanbury. She had felt even

[077-008]in the moment of her original anger against Mr. Gibson that she was

[077-009]bound in honour not to tell the story against him. She had brought

[077-010]him into the little difficulty, and she at least would hold her

[077-011]tongue. She was quite sure that Dorothy would never boast of her

[077-012]triumph. And Martha had been strictly cautioned,--as indeed, also,

[077-013]had Brooke Burgess. The man had behaved like an idiot, Miss Stanbury

[077-014]said; but he had been brought into a little dilemma, and nothing

[077-015]should be said about it from the house in the Close. But when the

[077-016]other rumour reached Miss Stanbury's ears, when Mrs. Crumbie condoled

[077-017]with her on her niece's misfortune, when Mrs. MacHugh asked whether

[077-018]Mr. Gibson had not behaved rather badly to the young lady, then our

[077-019]Juno's celestial mind was filled with a divine anger. But even then

[077-020]she did not declare the truth. She asked a question of Mrs. Crumbie,

[077-021]and was enabled, as she thought, to trace the falsehood to the

[077-022]Frenches. She did not think that Mr. Gibson could on a sudden have

[077-023]become so base a liar. "Mr. Gibson fast and loose with my niece!" she

[077-024]said to Mrs. MacHugh. "You have not got the story quite right, my

[077-025]dear friend. Pray, believe me;--there has been nothing of that sort."

[077-026]"I dare say not," said Mrs. MacHugh, "and I'm sure I don't care. Mr.

[077-027]Gibson has been going to marry one of the French girls for the last

[077-028]ten years, and I think he ought to make up his mind and do it at

[077-029]last."

[077-030]"I can assure you he is quite welcome as far as Dorothy is

[077-031]concerned," said Miss Stanbury.

[077-032]Without a doubt the opinion did prevail throughout [[exeter|Exeter]] that Mr.

[077-033]Gibson, who had been regarded time out of mind as the [[money|property]] of

[077-034]the Miss Frenches, had been angled for by the ladies in the Close,

[077-035]that he had nearly been caught, but that he had slipped the hook

[077-036]out of his mouth, and was now about to subside quietly into the net

[077-037]which had been originally prepared for him. Arabella French had not

[077-038]spoken loudly on the subject, but Camilla had declared in more than

[077-039]one house that she had most direct authority for stating that the

[077-040][[social-class|gentleman]] had never dreamed of offering to the young lady. "Why he

[077-041]should not do so if he pleases, I don't know," said Camilla. "Only

[077-042]the fact is that he has not pleased. The rumour of course has reached

[077-043]him, and, as we happen to be very old friends, we have authority for

[077-044]denying it altogether." All this came round to Miss Stanbury, and she

[077-045]was divine in her wrath.

[077-046]"If they drive me to it," she said to Dorothy, "I'll have the whole

[077-047]truth told by the bellman through the city, or I'll publish it in the

[077-048]County Gazette."

[077-049]"Pray don't say a word about it, Aunt Stanbury."

[077-050]"It is those odious girls. He's there now every day."

[077-051]"Why shouldn't he go there, Aunt Stanbury?"

[077-052]"If he's fool enough, let him go. I don't care where he goes. But

[077-053]I do care about these lies. They wouldn't dare to say it only they

[077-054]think my mouth is closed. They've no honour themselves, but they

[077-055]screen themselves behind mine."

[077-056]"I'm sure they won't find themselves mistaken in what they trust to,"

[077-057]said Dorothy, with a spirit that her aunt had not expected from her.

[077-058]Miss Stanbury at this time had told nobody that the offer to her

[077-059]niece had been made and repeated and finally rejected;--but she found

[077-060]it very difficult to hold her tongue.

[077-061]In the meantime Mr. Gibson spent a good deal of his time at

[077-062]Heavitree. It should not perhaps be asserted broadly that he had made

[077-063]up his mind that marriage would be good for him; but he had made up

[077-064]his mind, at least, to this, that it was no longer to be postponed

[077-065]without a balance of disadvantage. The Charybdis in the Close drove

[077-066]him helpless into the whirlpool of the Heavitree Scylla. He had no

[077-067]longer an escape from the perils of the latter shore. He had been so

[077-068]mauled by the opposite waves, that he had neither spirit nor skill

[077-069]left to him to keep in the middle track. He was almost daily at

[077-070]Heavitree, and did not attempt to conceal from himself the approach

[077-071]of his doom.

[077-072]But still there were two of them. He knew that he must become a prey,

[077-073]but was there any choice left to him as to which siren should have

[077-074]him? He had been quite aware in his more gallant days, before he had

[077-075]been knocked about on that Charybdis rock, that he might sip, and

[077-076]taste, and choose between the sweets. He had come to think lately

[077-077]that the younger young lady was the sweeter. Eight years ago indeed

[077-078]the passages between him and the elder had been tender; but Camilla

[077-079]had then been simply a romping girl, hardly more than a year or two

[077-080]beyond her teens. Now, with her matured charms, Camilla was certainly

[077-081]the more engaging as far as outward form went. Arabella's cheeks

[077-082]were thin and long, and her front teeth had come to show themselves.

[077-083]Her eyes were no doubt still bright, and what she had of hair was

[077-084]soft and dark. But it was very thin in front, and what there was of

[077-085]supplemental mass behind,--the bandbox by which Miss Stanbury was

[077-086]so much aggrieved,--was worn with an indifference to the lines of

[077-087]beauty, which Mr. Gibson himself found to be very depressing. A man

[077-088]with a fair burden on his back is not a grievous sight; but when

[077-089]we see a small human being attached to a bale of goods which he

[077-090]can hardly manage to move, we feel that the poor fellow has been

[077-091]cruelly overweighted. Mr. Gibson certainly had that sensation about

[077-092]Arabella's chignon. And as he regarded it in a nearer and a dearer

[077-093]light,--as a chignon that might possibly become his own, as a burden

[077-094]which in one sense he might himself be called upon to bear, as a

[077-095]domestic utensil which he himself might be called upon to inspect,

[077-096]and perhaps to aid the shifting on and the shifting off, he did begin

[077-097]to think that that side of the Scylla gulf ought to be avoided if

[077-098]possible. And probably this propensity on his part, this feeling that

[077-099]he would like to reconsider the matter dispassionately before he

[077-100]gave himself up for good to his old love, may have been increased by

[077-101]Camilla's apparent withdrawal of her claims. He felt mildly grateful

[077-102]to the Heavitree household in general for accepting him in this time

[077-103]of his affliction, but he could not admit to himself that they had a

[077-104]right to decide upon him in private conclave, and allot him either to

[077-105]the one or to the other nuptials without consultation with himself.

[077-106]To be swallowed up by Scylla he now recognised as his doom; but he

[077-107]thought he ought to be asked on which side of the gulf he would

[077-108]prefer to go down. The way in which Camilla spoke of him as a thing

[077-109]that wasn't hers, but another's; and the way in which Arabella looked

[077-110]at him, as though he were hers and could never be another's, wounded

[077-111]his manly pride. He had always understood that he might have his

[077-112]choice, and he could not understand that the little mishap which had

[077-113]befallen him in the Close was to rob him of that privilege.

[077-114]He used to drink tea at Heavitree in those days. On one evening on

[077-115]going in he found himself alone with Arabella. "Oh, Mr. Gibson," she

[077-116]said, "we weren't sure whether you'd come. And mamma and Camilla have

[077-117]gone out to Mrs. Camadge's." Mr. Gibson muttered some word to the

[077-118]effect that he hoped he had kept nobody at home; and, as he did so,

[077-119]he remembered that he had distinctly said that he would come on

[077-120]this evening. "I don't know that I should have gone," said Arabella,

[077-121]"because I am not quite,--not quite myself at present. No, not ill;

[077-122]not at all. Don't you know what it is, Mr. Gibson, to be,--to be,--to

[077-123]be,--not quite yourself?" Mr. Gibson said that he had very often felt

[077-124]like that. "And one can't get over it;--can one?" continued Arabella.

[077-125]"There comes a presentiment that something is going to happen, and

[077-126]a kind of belief that something has happened, though you don't know

[077-127]what; and the heart refuses to be light, and the spirit becomes

[077-128]abashed, and the mind, though it creates new thoughts, will not

[077-129]settle itself to its accustomed work. I suppose it's what the novels

[077-130]have called Melancholy."

[077-131]"I suppose it is," said Mr. Gibson. "But there's generally some cause

[077-132]for it. Debt for instance--"

[077-133]"It's nothing of that kind with me. It's no debt, at least, that can

[077-134]be written down in the figures of ordinary arithmetic. Sit down, Mr.

[077-135]Gibson, and we will have some tea." Then, as she stretched forward to

[077-136]ring the bell, he thought that he never in his life had seen anything

[077-137]so unshapely as that huge wen at the back of her head. "Monstrum

[077-138]horrendum, informe, ingens!" He could not help quoting the words to

[077-139]himself. She was dressed with some attempt at being smart, but her

[077-140]ribbons were soiled, and her lace was tawdry, and the fabric of her

[077-141]dress was old and dowdy. He was quite sure that he would feel no

[077-142]pride in calling her Mrs. Gibson, no pleasure in having her all to

[077-143]himself at his own hearth. "I hope we shall escape the bitterness of

[077-144]Miss Stanbury's tongue if we drink tea tete-a-tete," she said, with

[077-145]her sweetest smile.

[077-146]"I don't suppose she'll know anything about it."

[077-147]"She knows about everything, Mr. Gibson. It's astonishing what she

[077-148]knows. She has eyes and ears everywhere. I shouldn't care, if she

[077-149]didn't see and hear so very incorrectly. I'm told now that she

[077-150]declares--; but it doesn't signify."

[077-151]"Declares what?" asked Mr. Gibson.

[077-152]"Never mind. But wasn't it odd how all Exeter believed that you were

[077-153]going to be married in that house, and to live there all the rest of

[077-154]your life, and be one of Miss Stanbury's slaves. I never believed

[077-155]it, Mr. Gibson." This she said with a sad smile, that ought to have

[077-156]brought him on his knees, in spite of the chignon.

[077-157]"One can't help these things," said Mr. Gibson.

[077-158]"I never could have believed it;--not even if you had not given me an

[077-159]assurance so solemn, and so sweet, that there was nothing in it." The

[077-160]poor man had given the assurance, and could not deny the solemnity

[077-161]and the sweetness. "That was a happy moment for us, Mr. Gibson;

[077-162]because, though we never believed it, when it was dinned into our

[077-163]ears so frequently, when it was made such a triumph in the Close, it

[077-164]was impossible not to fear that there might be something in it." He

[077-165]felt that he ought to make some reply, but he did not know what to

[077-166]say. He was thoroughly ashamed of the lie he had told, but he could

[077-167]not untell it. "Camilla reproached me afterwards for asking you,"

[077-168]whispered Arabella, in her softest, tenderest voice. "She said

[077-169]that it was unmaidenly. I hope you did not think it unmaidenly, Mr.

[077-170]Gibson?"

[077-171]"Oh dear no;--not at all," said he.
