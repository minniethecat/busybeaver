## Section 2

[002-001]"If I am suspected," said Mrs. Trevelyan to her sister one morning,

[002-002]as they sat together in the little back drawing-room, "life will not

[002-003]be worth having."

[002-004]"How can you talk of being suspected, Emily?"

[002-005]"What does he mean then by saying that he would rather not have

[002-006][[colonel-osborne|Colonel Osborne]] here? A man older than my own father, who has known

[002-007]me since I was a baby!"

[002-008]"He didn't mean anything of that kind, Emily. You know he did not,

[002-009]and you should not say so. It would be too horrible to think of."

[002-010]"It was a great deal too horrible to be spoken, I know. If he does

[002-011]not beg my pardon, I shall,--I shall continue to live with him, of

[002-012]course, as a sort of upper servant, because of baby. But he shall

[002-013]know what I think and feel."

[002-014]"If I were you I would forget it."

[002-015]"How can I forget it? Nothing that I can do pleases him. He is civil

[002-016]and kind to you because he is not your master; but you don't know

[002-017]what things he says to me. Am I to tell Colonel Osborne not to come?

[002-018]Heavens and earth! How should I ever hold up my head again if I were

[002-019]driven to do that? He will be here to-day I have no doubt; and Louis

[002-020]will sit there below in the library, and hear his step, and will not

[002-021]come up."

[002-022]"Tell Richard to say you are not at home."

[002-023]"Yes; and everybody will understand why. And for what am I to deny

[002-024]myself in that way to the best and oldest friend I have? If any such

[002-025]orders are to be given, let him give them and then see what will come

[002-026]of it."

[002-027]Mrs. Trevelyan had described Colonel Osborne truly as far as words

[002-028]went, in saying that he had known her since she was a baby, and that

[002-029]he was an older man than her father. Colonel Osborne's age exceeded

[002-030]her father's by about a month, and as he was now past fifty, he might

[002-031]be considered perhaps, in that respect, to be a safe friend for a

[002-032]young married woman. But he was in every respect a man very different

[002-033]from Sir Marmaduke. Sir Marmaduke, blessed and at the same time

[002-034]burdened as he was with a wife and eight daughters, and condemned as

[002-035]he had been to pass a large portion of his life within the tropics,

[002-036]had become at fifty what many people call quite a middle-aged man.

[002-037]That is to say, he was one from whom the effervescence and elasticity

[002-038]and salt of youth had altogether passed away. He was fat and slow,

[002-039]thinking much of his wife and eight daughters, thinking much also of

[002-040]his dinner. Now Colonel Osborne was a bachelor, with no burdens but

[002-041]those imposed upon him by his position as a member of Parliament,--a

[002-042]man of [[money|fortune]] to whom the world had been very easy. It was not

[002-043]therefore said so decidedly of him as of Sir Marmaduke, that he

[002-044]was a middle-aged man, although he had probably already lived more

[002-045]than two-thirds of his life. And he was a good-looking man of his

[002-046]age, bald indeed at the top of his head, and with a considerable

[002-047]sprinkling of grey hair through his bushy beard; but upright in his

[002-048]carriage, active, and quick in his step, who dressed well, and was

[002-049]clearly determined to make the most he could of what remained to him

[002-050]of the advantages of youth. Colonel Osborne was always so dressed

[002-051]that no one ever observed the nature of his garments, being no doubt

[002-052]well aware that no man after twenty-five can afford to call special

[002-053]attention to his coat, his hat, his cravat, or his trousers; but

[002-054]nevertheless the matter was one to which he paid much attention, and

[002-055]he was by no means lax in ascertaining what his tailor did for him.

[002-056]He always rode a pretty horse, and mounted his groom on one at any

[002-057]rate as pretty. He was known to have an excellent stud down in the

[002-058]shires, and had the [[honour|reputation]] of going well with hounds. Poor Sir

[002-059]Marmaduke could not have ridden a hunt to save either his government

[002-060]or his credit. When, therefore, Mrs. Trevelyan declared to her sister

[002-061]that Colonel Osborne was a man whom she was entitled to regard with

[002-062]semi-parental feelings of veneration because he was older than her

[002-063]father, she made a comparison which was more true in the letter than

[002-064]in the spirit. And when she asserted that Colonel Osborne had known

[002-065]her since she was a baby, she fell again into the same mistake.

[002-066]Colonel Osborne had indeed known her when she was a baby, and had in

[002-067]old days been the very intimate friend of her father; but of herself

[002-068]he had seen little or nothing since those baby days, till he had met

[002-069]her just as she was about to become Mrs. Trevelyan; and though it was

[002-070]natural that so old a friend should come to her and congratulate her

[002-071]and renew his friendship, nevertheless it was not true that he made

[002-072]his appearance in her husband's house in the guise of the useful old

[002-073]family friend, who gives silver cups to the children and kisses the

[002-074]little girls for the sake of the old affection which he has borne for

[002-075]the parents. We all know the appearance of that old [[social-class|gentleman]], how

[002-076]pleasant and dear a fellow he is, how welcome is his face within the

[002-077]gate, how free he makes with our wine, generally abusing it, how he

[002-078]tells our eldest daughter to light his candle for him, how he gave

[002-079]silver cups when the girls were born, and now bestows tea-services as

[002-080]they get married,--a most useful, safe, and charming fellow, not a

[002-081]year younger-looking or more nimble than ourselves, without whom life

[002-082]would be very blank. We all know that man; but such a man was not

[002-083]Colonel Osborne in the house of Mr. Trevelyan's young bride.

[002-084]Emily Rowley, when she was brought home from the [[mandarin-islands|Mandarin Islands]]

[002-085]to be the wife of [[louis-trevelyan|Louis Trevelyan]], was a very handsome young woman,

[002-086]tall, with a bust rather full for her age, with dark eyes--eyes that

[002-087]looked to be dark because her eye-brows and eye-lashes were nearly

[002-088]black, but which were in truth so varying in colour, that you could

[002-089]not tell their hue. Her brown hair was very dark and very soft; and

[002-090]the tint of her complexion was brown also, though the colour of her

[002-091]cheeks was often so bright as to induce her enemies to say falsely of

[002-092]her that she painted them. And she was very strong, as are some girls

[002-093]who come from the tropics, and whom a tropical climate has suited.

[002-094]She could sit on her horse the whole day long, and would never be

[002-095]weary with dancing at the Government House balls. When Colonel

[002-096]Osborne was introduced to her as the baby whom he had known, he

[002-097]thought it would be very pleasant to be intimate with so pleasant a

[002-098]friend,--meaning no harm indeed, as but few men do mean harm on such

[002-099]occasions,--but still, not regarding the beautiful young woman whom

[002-100]he had seen as one of a generation succeeding to that of his own, to

[002-101]whom it would be his duty to make himself useful on account of the

[002-102]old friendship which he bore to her father.

[002-103]It was, moreover, well known in [[london|London]],--though not known at all

[002-104]to Mrs. Trevelyan,--that this ancient Lothario had before this

[002-105]made himself troublesome in more than one family. He was fond of

[002-106]intimacies with married ladies, and perhaps was not averse to the

[002-107]excitement of marital hostility. It must be remembered, however, that

[002-108]the hostility to which allusion is here made was not the hostility

[002-109]of the pistol or the horsewhip,--nor, indeed, was it generally the

[002-110]hostility of a word of spoken anger. A young husband may dislike

[002-111]the too-friendly bearing of a friend, and may yet abstain from that

[002-112]outrage on his own [[pride|dignity]] and on his wife, which is conveyed by a

[002-113]word of suspicion. Louis Trevelyan having taken a strong dislike to

[002-114]Colonel Osborne, and having failed to make his wife understand that

[002-115]this dislike should have induced her to throw cold water upon the

[002-116]Colonel's friendship, had allowed himself to speak a word which

[002-117]probably he would have willingly recalled as soon as spoken. But

[002-118]words spoken cannot be recalled, and many a man and many a woman who

[002-119]has spoken a word at once regretted, are far too proud to express

[002-120]that regret. So it was with Louis Trevelyan when he told his wife

[002-121]that he did not wish Colonel Osborne to come so often to his house.

[002-122]He had said it with a flashing eye and an angry tone; and though she

[002-123]had seen the eye flash before, and was familiar with the angry tone,

[002-124]she had never before felt herself to be insulted by her husband. As

[002-125]soon as the word had been spoken Trevelyan had left the room, and had

[002-126]gone down among his books. But when he was alone, he knew that he

[002-127]had insulted his wife. He was quite aware that he should have spoken

[002-128]to her gently, and have explained to her, with his arm round her

[002-129]waist, that it would be better for both of them that this friend's

[002-130]friendship should be limited. There is so much in a turn of the eye

[002-131]and in the tone given to a word when such things have to be said,--so

[002-132]much more of importance than in the words themselves. As Trevelyan

[002-133]thought of this, and remembered what his manner had been, how much

[002-134]anger he had expressed, how far he had been from having his arm round

[002-135]his wife's waist as he spoke to her, he almost made up his mind to

[002-136]go up-stairs and to apologise. But he was one to whose nature the

[002-137]giving of any apology was repulsive. He could not bear to have to own

[002-138]himself to have been wrong. And then his wife had been most provoking

[002-139]in her manner to him. When he had endeavoured to make her understand

[002-140]his wishes by certain disparaging hints which he had thrown out as to

[002-141]Colonel Osborne, saying that he was a dangerous man, one who did not

[002-142]show his true [[reputation|character]], a snake in the grass, a man without settled

[002-143]principles, and such like, his wife had taken up the cudgels for her

[002-144]friend, and had openly declared that she did not believe a word of

[002-145]the things that were alleged against him. "But still, for all that,

[002-146]it is true," the husband had said. "I have no doubt that you think

[002-147]so," the wife had replied. "Men do believe evil of one another, very

[002-148]often. But you must excuse me if I say that I think you are mistaken.

[002-149]I have known Colonel Osborne much longer than you have done, Louis,

[002-150]and papa has always had the highest opinion of him." Then Mr.

[002-151]Trevelyan had become very angry, and had spoken those words which

[002-152]he could not recall. As he walked to and fro among his books

[002-153]down-stairs, he almost felt that he ought to beg his wife's pardon.

[002-154]He knew his wife well enough to be sure that she would not forgive

[002-155]him unless he did so. He would do so, he thought, but not exactly

[002-156]now. A moment would come in which it might be easier than at present.

[002-157]He would be able to assure her when he went up to dress for dinner,

[002-158]that he had meant no harm. They were going out to dine at the house

[002-159]of a lady of rank, the Countess Dowager of Milborough, a lady

[002-160][[reputation|standing]] high in the world's esteem, of whom his wife stood a little

[002-161]in awe; and he calculated that this feeling, if it did not make his

[002-162]task easy would yet take from it some of its difficulty. Emily would

[002-163]be, not exactly cowed, by the prospect of Lady Milborough's dinner,

[002-164]but perhaps a little reduced from her usual self-assertion. He would

[002-165]say a word to her when he was dressing, assuring her that he had not

[002-166]intended to animadvert in the slightest degree upon her own conduct.
