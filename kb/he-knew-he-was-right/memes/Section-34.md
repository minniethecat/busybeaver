## Section 34

[034-001]It was thus with no settled scheme that the Colonel went to work,

[034-002]and made inquiries, and ascertained Mrs. Trevelyan's address in

[034-003]Devonshire. When he learned it, he thought that he had done much;

[034-004]though, in truth, there had been no secrecy in the matter. Scores

[034-005]of people knew Mrs. Trevelyan's address besides the newsvendor who

[034-006]supplied her paper, from whose boy [[colonel-osborne|Colonel Osborne]]'s servant obtained

[034-007]the information. But when the information had been obtained, it was

[034-008]expedient that it should be used; and therefore Colonel Osborne wrote

[034-009]the following letter:--

[034-010]Acrobats Club, July 31, 186--.

[034-011]DEAR EMILY,

[034-012]Twice the Colonel wrote Dearest Emily, and twice he tore the sheet on

[034-013]which the words were written. He longed to be ardent, but still it

[034-014]was so necessary to be prudent! He was not quite sure of the lady.

[034-015]Women sometimes tell their husbands, even when they have quarrelled

[034-016]with them. And, although ardent expressions in writing to pretty

[034-017]women are pleasant to male writers, it is not pleasant for a

[034-018][[social-class|gentleman]] to be asked what on earth he means by that sort of thing at

[034-019]his time of life. The Colonel gave half an hour to the consideration,

[034-020]and then began the letter, Dear Emily. If prudence be the soul

[034-021]of valour, may it not be considered also the very mainspring, or,

[034-022]perhaps, the pivot of love?

[034-023]DEAR EMILY,

[034-024]I need hardly tell you with what dismay I have heard of

[034-025]all that has taken place in Curzon Street. I fear that you

[034-026]must have suffered much, and that you are suffering now.

[034-027]It is an inexpressible relief to me to hear that you have

[034-028]your child with you, and Nora. But, nevertheless, to

[034-029]have your home taken away from you, to be sent out of

[034-030][[london|London]], to be banished from all society! And for what?

[034-031]The manner in which the minds of some men work is quite

[034-032]incomprehensible.

[034-033]As for myself, I feel that I have lost the company of

[034-034]a friend, whom indeed I can very ill spare. I have a

[034-035]thousand things to say to you, and among them one or

[034-036]two which I feel that I must say,--that I ought to say.

[034-037]As it happens, an old schoolfellow of mine is Vicar of

[034-038]Cockchaffington, a village which I find by the map is

[034-039]very near to [[nuncombe-putney|Nuncombe Putney]]. I saw him in town last

[034-040]spring, and he then asked me to pay him a visit. There is

[034-041]something in his church which people go to see, and though

[034-042]I don't understand churches much, I shall go and see it.

[034-043]I shall run down on Wednesday, and shall sleep at the inn

[034-044]at Lessboro'. I see that Lessboro' is a market town, and

[034-045]I suppose there is an inn. I shall go over to my friend on

[034-046]the Thursday, but shall return to Lessboro'. Though a man

[034-047]be ever so eager to see a church door-way, he need not

[034-048]sleep at the parsonage. On the following day, I will get

[034-049]over to Nuncombe Putney, and I hope that you will see me.

[034-050]Considering my long friendship with you, and my great

[034-051]attachment to your father and mother, I do not think

[034-052]that the strictest martinet would tell you that you need

[034-053]hesitate in the matter.

[034-054]I have seen Mr. Trevelyan twice at the club, but he has

[034-055]not spoken to me. Under such circumstances I could not of

[034-056]course speak to him. Indeed, I may say that my feelings

[034-057]towards him just at present are of such a nature as

[034-058]to preclude me from doing so with any appearance of

[034-059]cordiality.

[034-060]Dear Emily,

[034-061]Believe me now, as always, your affectionate friend,

[034-062]FREDERIC OSBORNE.

[034-063]When he read that letter over to himself a second time he felt quite

[034-064]sure that he had not committed himself. Even if his friend were to

[034-065]send the letter to her husband, it could not do him any harm. He was

[034-066]aware that he might have dilated more on the old friendship between

[034-067]himself and Sir Marmaduke, but he experienced a certain distaste to

[034-068]the mention of things appertaining to years long past. It did not

[034-069]quite suit him in his present frame of mind to speak of his regard in

[034-070]those quasi-paternal terms which he would have used had it satisfied

[034-071]him to represent himself simply as her father's friend. His language

[034-072]therefore had been a little doubtful, so that the lady might, if

[034-073]she were so minded, look upon him in that tender light in which her

[034-074]husband had certainly chosen to regard him.

[034-075]When the letter was handed to Mrs. Trevelyan, she at once took it

[034-076]with her up to her own room, so that she might be alone when she read

[034-077]it. The handwriting was quite familiar to her, and she did not choose

[034-078]that even her sister should see it. She had told herself twenty times

[034-079]over that, while living at Nuncombe Putney, she was not living under

[034-080]the guardianship of Mrs. Stanbury. She would consent to live under

[034-081]the guardianship of no one, as her husband did not choose to remain

[034-082]with her and protect her. She had done no wrong, and she would submit

[034-083]to no other authority, than that of her legal lord and master. Nor,

[034-084]according to her views of her own position, was it in his power to

[034-085]depute that authority to others. He had caused the separation, and

[034-086]now she must be the sole judge of her own actions. In itself, a

[034-087]correspondence between her and her father's old friend was in no

[034-088]degree criminal or even faulty. There was no reason, moral, social,

[034-089]or religious, why an old man, over fifty, who had known her all her

[034-090]life, should not write to her. But yet she could not say aloud before

[034-091]Mrs. Stanbury, and Priscilla, and her sister, that she had received a

[034-092]letter from Colonel Osborne. She felt that the colour had come to her

[034-093]cheek, and that she could not even walk out of the room as though the

[034-094]letter had been a matter of indifference to her.

[034-095]And would it have been a matter of indifference had there been nobody

[034-096]there to see her? Mrs. Trevelyan was certainly not in love with

[034-097]Colonel Osborne. She was not more so now than she had been when her

[034-098]father's friend, purposely dressed for the occasion, had kissed her

[034-099]in the vestry of the church in which she was married, and had given

[034-100]her a blessing, which was then intended to be semi-paternal,--as from

[034-101]an old man to a young woman. She was not in love with him,--never

[034-102]would be, never could be in love with him. Reader, you may believe

[034-103]in her so far as that. But where is the woman, who, when she is

[034-104]neglected, thrown over, and suspected by the man that she loves, will

[034-105]not feel the desire of some sympathy, some solicitude, some show of

[034-106]regard from another man? This woman's life, too, had not hitherto

[034-107]been of such a nature that the tranquillity of the [[clock-house|Clock House]] at

[034-108]Nuncombe Putney afforded to her all that she desired. She had been

[034-109]there now a month, and was almost sick from the want of excitement.

[034-110]And she was full of wrath against her husband. Why had he sent her

[034-111]there to break her heart in a disgraceful retirement, when she had

[034-112]never wronged him? From morning to night she had no employment, no

[034-113]amusement, nothing to satisfy her cravings. Why was she to be doomed

[034-114]to such an existence? She had declared that as long as she could

[034-115]have her boy with her, she would be happy. She was allowed to have

[034-116]her boy; but she was anything but happy. When she received Colonel

[034-117]Osborne's letter,--while she held it in her hand still unopened, she

[034-118]never for a moment thought that that could make her happy. But there

[034-119]was in it something of excitement. And she painted the man to herself

[034-120]in brighter colours now than she had ever given to him in her former

[034-121]portraits. He cared for her. He was gracious to her. He appreciated

[034-122]her talents, her beauty, and her conduct. He knew that she deserved

[034-123]a treatment very different from that accorded to her by her husband.

[034-124]Why should she reject the sympathy of her father's oldest friend,

[034-125]because her husband was madly jealous about an old man? Her husband

[034-126]had chosen to send her away, and to leave her, so that she must act

[034-127]on her own judgment. Acting on her own judgment, she read Colonel

[034-128]Osborne's letter from first to last. She knew that he was wrong to

[034-129]speak of coming to Nuncombe Putney; but yet she thought that she

[034-130]would see him. She had a dim perception that she was [[reputation|standing]] on the

[034-131]edge of a precipice, on broken ground which might fall under her

[034-132]without a moment's warning, and yet she would not retreat from the

[034-133]danger. Though Colonel Osborne was wrong, very wrong in coming to see

[034-134]her, yet she liked him for coming. Though she would be half afraid

[034-135]to tell her news to Mrs. Stanbury, and more than half afraid to tell

[034-136]Priscilla, yet she liked the excitement of the fear. Nora would scold

[034-137]her; but Nora's scolding she thought she could answer. And then it

[034-138]was not the fact that Colonel Osborne was coming down to Devonshire

[034-139]to see her. He was coming as far as Lessboro' to see his friend at

[034-140]Cockchaffington. And when at Lessboro', was it likely that he should

[034-141]leave the neighbourhood without seeing the daughter of his old ally?

[034-142]And why should he do so? Was he to be unnatural in his conduct,

[034-143]uncivil and unfriendly, because Mr. Trevelyan had been foolish,

[034-144]suspicious, and insane?

[034-145]So arguing with herself, she answered Colonel Osborne's letter before

[034-146]she had spoken on the subject to any one in the house,--and this was

[034-147]her answer:--

[034-148]MY DEAR COLONEL OSBORNE,

[034-149]I must leave it to your own judgment to decide whether you

[034-150]will come to Nuncombe Putney or not. There are reasons

[034-151]which would seem to make it expedient that you should stay

[034-152]away,--even though circumstances are bringing you into

[034-153]the immediate neighbourhood. But of these reasons I will

[034-154]leave you to be the judge. I will never let it be said

[034-155]that I myself have had cause to dread the visit of any old

[034-156]friend. Nevertheless, if you stay away, I shall understand

[034-157]why you do so.

[034-158]Personally, I shall be glad to see you,--as I have always

[034-159]been. It seems odd to me that I cannot write in warmer

[034-160]tones to my father's and mother's oldest friend. Of

[034-161]course, you will understand that though I shall readily

[034-162]see you if you call, I cannot ask you to stay. In the

[034-163]first place, I am not now living in my own house. I am

[034-164]staying with Mrs. Stanbury, and the place is called the

[034-165]Clock House.

[034-166]Yours very sincerely,

[034-167]EMILY TREVELYAN.

[034-168]The Clock House, Nuncombe Putney, Monday.

[034-169]Soon after she had written it, Nora came into her room, and at once

[034-170]asked concerning the letter which she had seen delivered to her

[034-171]sister that morning.

[034-172]"It was from Colonel Osborne," said Mrs. Trevelyan.

[034-173]"From Colonel Osborne! How very wrong!"

[034-174]"I don't see that it is wrong at all. Because Louis is foolish and

[034-175]mad, that cannot make another man wrong for doing the most ordinary

[034-176]thing in the world."

[034-177]"I had hoped it had been from Louis," said Nora.

[034-178]"Oh dear, no. He is by no means so considerate. I do not suppose I

[034-179]shall hear from him, till he chooses to give some fresh order about

[034-180]myself or my child. He will hardly trouble himself to write to me,

[034-181]unless he takes up some new freak to show me that he is my master."

[034-182]"And what does Colonel Osborne say?"

[034-183]"He is coming here."

[034-184]"Coming here?" almost shouted Nora.

[034-185]"Yes; absolutely here. Does it sound to you as if Lucifer himself

[034-186]were about to show his face? The fact is, he happens to have a friend

[034-187]in the neighbourhood whom he has long promised to visit; and as he

[034-188]must be at Lessboro', he does not choose to go away without the

[034-189]compliment of a call. It will be as much to you as to me."
