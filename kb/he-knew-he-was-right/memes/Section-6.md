## Section 6

[006-001]"My dear Louis," she said, "I want to speak a word to you. Come

[006-002]here." And then she led him into a distant corner, Mrs. Trevelyan

[006-003]watching her all the while, and guessing why her husband was thus

[006-004]carried away. "I just want to give you a little hint, which I am sure

[006-005]I believe is quite unnecessary," continued Lady Milborough. Then she

[006-006]paused, but Trevelyan would not speak. She looked into his face, and

[006-007]saw that it was black. But the man was the only child of her dearest

[006-008]friend, and she persevered. "Do you know I don't quite like that

[006-009][[colonel-osborne|Colonel Osborne]] coming so much to your house." The face before her

[006-010]became still blacker, but still the man said nothing. "I dare say it

[006-011]is a prejudice on my part, but I have always disliked him. I think he

[006-012]is a dangerous friend;--what I call a snake in the grass. And though

[006-013]Emily's high good sense, and love for you, and general feelings on

[006-014]such a subject, are just what a husband must desire--Indeed, I am

[006-015]quite sure that the possibility of anything wrong has never entered

[006-016]into her head. But it is the very purity of her innocence which makes

[006-017]the danger. He is a bad man, and I would just say a word to her, if I

[006-018]were you, to make her understand that his coming to her of a morning

[006-019]is not desirable. Upon my word, I believe there is nothing he likes

[006-020]so much as going about and making mischief between men and their

[006-021]wives."

[006-022]Thus she delivered herself; and [[louis-trevelyan|Louis Trevelyan]], though he was sore

[006-023]and angry, could not but feel that she had taken the part of a

[006-024]friend. All that she had said had been true; all that she had said

[006-025]to him he had said to himself more than once. He too hated the man.

[006-026]He believed him to be a snake in the grass. But it was intolerably

[006-027]bitter to him that he should be warned about his wife's conduct by

[006-028]any living human being; that he, to whom the world had been so full

[006-029]of good [[money|fortune]],--that he, who had in truth taught himself to think

[006-030]that he deserved so much good fortune, should be made the subject of

[006-031]care on behalf of his friend, because of danger between himself and

[006-032]his wife! On the spur of the moment he did not know what answer to

[006-033]make. "He is not a man whom I like myself," he said.

[006-034]"Just be careful, Louis, that is all," said Lady Milborough, and then

[006-035]she was gone.

[006-036]To be cautioned about his wife's conduct cannot be pleasant to any

[006-037]man, and it was very unpleasant to Louis Trevelyan. He, too, had been

[006-038]asked a question about Sir Marmaduke's expected visit to England

[006-039]after the ladies had left the room. All the town had heard of it

[006-040]except himself. He hardly spoke another word that evening till the

[006-041]brougham was announced; and his wife had observed his silence. When

[006-042]they were seated in the carriage, he together with his wife and Nora

[006-043]Rowley, he immediately asked a question about Sir Marmaduke. "Emily,"

[006-044]he said, "is there any truth in a report I hear that your father is

[006-045]coming home?" No answer was made, and for a moment or two there was

[006-046]silence. "You must have heard of it, then," he said. "Perhaps you can

[006-047]tell me, Nora, as Emily will not reply. Have you heard anything of

[006-048]your father's coming?"

[006-049]"Yes; I have heard of it," said Nora slowly.

[006-050]"And why have I not been told?"

[006-051]"It was to be kept a secret," said Mrs. Trevelyan boldly.

[006-052]"A secret from me; and everybody else knows it! And why was it to be

[006-053]a secret?"

[006-054]"Colonel Osborne did not wish that it should be known," said Mrs.

[006-055]Trevelyan.

[006-056]"And what has Colonel Osborne to do between you and your father in

[006-057]any matter with which I may not be made acquainted? I will have

[006-058]nothing more between you and Colonel Osborne. You shall not see

[006-059]Colonel Osborne. Do you hear me?"

[006-060]"Yes, I hear you, Louis."

[006-061]"And do you mean to obey me? By G----, you shall obey me. Remember

[006-062]this, that I lay my positive order upon you, that you shall not see

[006-063]Colonel Osborne again. You do not know it, perhaps, but you are

[006-064]already forfeiting your [[honour|reputation]] as an honest woman, and bringing

[006-065]disgrace upon me by your familiarity with Colonel Osborne."

[006-066]"Oh, Louis, do not say that!" said Nora.

[006-067]"You had better let him speak it all at once," said Emily.

[006-068]"I have said what I have got to say. It is now only necessary that

[006-069]you should give me your solemn assurance that you will obey me."

[006-070]"If you have said all that you have to say, perhaps you will listen

[006-071]to me," said his wife.

[006-072]"I will listen to nothing till you have given me your promise."

[006-073]"Then I certainly shall not give it you."

[006-074]"Dear Emily, pray, pray do what he tells you," said Nora.

[006-075]"She has yet to learn that it is her duty to do as I tell her," said

[006-076]Trevelyan. "And because she is obstinate, and will not learn from

[006-077]those who know better than herself what a woman may do, and what she

[006-078]may not, she will ruin herself, and destroy my happiness."

[006-079]"I know that you have destroyed my happiness by your unreasonable

[006-080]jealousy," said the wife. "Have you considered what I must feel in

[006-081]having such words addressed to me by my husband? If I am fit to be

[006-082]told that I must promise not to see any man living, I cannot be fit

[006-083]to be any man's wife." Then she burst out into an hysterical fit of

[006-084]tears, and in this condition she got out of the carriage, entered her

[006-085]house, and hurried up to her own room.

[006-086]"Indeed, she has not been to blame," said Nora to Trevelyan on the

[006-087]staircase.

[006-088]"Why has there been a secret kept from me between her and this man;

[006-089]and that too, after I had cautioned her against being intimate with

[006-090]him? I am sorry that she should suffer; but it is better that she

[006-091]should suffer a little now, than that we should both suffer much

[006-092]by-and-by."

[006-093]Nora endeavoured to explain to him the truth about the committee, and

[006-094]Colonel Osborne's promised influence, and the reason why there was to

[006-095]be a secret. But she was too much in a hurry to get to her sister to

[006-096]make the matter plain, and he was too much angered to listen to her.

[006-097]He shook his head when she spoke of Colonel Osborne's dislike to have

[006-098]his name mentioned in connection with the matter. "All the world

[006-099]knows it," he said with scornful laughter.

[006-100]It was in vain that Nora endeavoured to explain to him that though

[006-101]all the world might know it, Emily herself had only heard of the

[006-102]proposition as a thing quite unsettled, as to which nothing at

[006-103]present should be spoken openly. It was in vain to endeavour to make

[006-104]peace on that night. Nora hurried up to her sister, and found that

[006-105]the hysterical tears had again given place to anger. She would not

[006-106]see her husband, unless he would beg her pardon; and he would not see

[006-107]her unless she would give the promise he demanded. And the husband

[006-108]and wife did not see each other again on that night.

[006-109]CHAPTER IV.

[006-110]HUGH STANBURY.

[006-111][Illustration]

[006-112]It has been already stated that [[nora-rowley|Nora Rowley]] was not quite so well

[006-113]disposed as perhaps she ought to have been, to fall in love with the

[006-114]Honourable Charles Glascock, there having come upon her the habit of

[006-115]comparing him with another [[social-class|gentleman]] whenever this duty of falling in

[006-116]love with Mr. Glascock was exacted from her. That other gentleman was

[006-117]one with whom she knew that it was quite out of the question that she

[006-118]should fall in love, because he had not a shilling in the world; and

[006-119]the other gentleman was equally aware that it was not open to him to

[006-120]fall in love with Nora Rowley--for the same reason. In regard to such

[006-121]matters Nora Rowley had been properly brought up, having been made

[006-122]to understand by the best and most cautious of mothers, that in that

[006-123]matter of falling in love it was absolutely necessary that bread and

[006-124]cheese should be considered. "Romance is a very pretty thing," Lady

[006-125]Rowley had been wont to say to her daughters, "and I don't think life

[006-126]would be worth having without a little of it. I should be very sorry

[006-127]to think that either of my girls would marry a man only because he

[006-128]had money. But you can't even be romantic without something to eat

[006-129]and drink." Nora thoroughly understood all this, and being well aware

[006-130]that her fortune in the world, if it ever was to be made at all,

[006-131]could only be made by marriage, had laid down for herself certain

[006-132]hard lines,--lines intended to be as fast as they were hard. Let

[006-133]what might come to her in the way of likings and dislikings, let the

[006-134]temptation to her be ever so strong, she would never allow her heart

[006-135]to rest on a man who, if he should ask her to be his wife, would not

[006-136]have the means of supporting her. There were many, she knew, who

[006-137]would condemn such a resolution as cold, selfish, and heartless. She

[006-138]heard people saying so daily. She read in books that it ought to be

[006-139]so regarded. But she declared to herself that she would respect the

[006-140]judgment neither of the people nor of the books. To be poor alone, to

[006-141]have to live without a husband, to look forward to a life in which

[006-142]there would be nothing of a career, almost nothing to do, to await

[006-143]the vacuity of an existence in which she would be useful to no one,

[006-144]was a destiny which she could teach herself to endure, because it

[006-145]might probably be forced upon her by necessity. Were her father to

[006-146]die there would hardly be bread for that female flock to eat. As it

[006-147]was, she was eating the bread of a man in whose house she was no more

[006-148]than a visitor. The lot of a woman, as she often told herself, was

[006-149]wretched, unfortunate, almost degrading. For a woman such as herself

[006-150]there was no path open to her energy, other than that of getting a

[006-151]husband. Nora Rowley thought of all this till she was almost sick of

[006-152]the prospect of her life,--especially sick of it when she was told

[006-153]with much authority by the Lady Milboroughs of her acquaintance that

[006-154]it was her bounden duty to fall in love with Mr. Glascock. As to

[006-155]falling in love with Mr. Glascock, she had not as yet quite made up

[006-156]her mind. There was so much to be said on that side of the question,

[006-157]if such falling in love could only be made possible. But she had

[006-158]quite made up her mind that she would never fall in love with a poor

[006-159]man. In spite, however, of all that, she felt herself compelled to

[006-160]make comparisons between Mr. Glascock and one Mr. [[hugh-stanbury|Hugh Stanbury]], a

[006-161]gentleman who had not a shilling.

[006-162]Mr. Hugh Stanbury had been at college the most intimate friend of

[006-163]Louis Trevelyan, and at Oxford had been, in spite of Trevelyan's

[006-164]successes, a bigger man than his friend. Stanbury had not taken so

[006-165]high a degree as Trevelyan,--indeed had not gone out in honours at

[006-166]all. He had done little for the credit of his college, and had never

[006-167]put himself in the way of wrapping himself up for life in the scanty

[006-168]lambswool of a fellowship. But he had won for himself reputation as

[006-169]a clever speaker, as a man who had learned much that college tutors

[006-170]do not profess to teach, as a hard-headed, ready-witted fellow, who,

[006-171]having the world as an oyster before him, which it was necessary that

[006-172]he should open, would certainly find either a knife or a sword with

[006-173]which to open it.
