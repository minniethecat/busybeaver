## Section 21

[021-001]"I don't see how it can make a difference as he writes it."

[021-002]"It would make a great deal of difference to me. And I'm told that

[021-003]what they call ink comes off on your fingers like lamp-black. I never

[021-004]touched one, thank God; but they tell me so. All the same; it isn't

[021-005]your fault."

[021-006]"I've nothing to do with it, Aunt Stanbury."

[021-007]"Of course you've not. And as he is your brother it wouldn't be

[021-008]natural that you should like to throw him off. And, my dear, I like

[021-009]you for taking his part. Only you needn't have been so fierce with an

[021-010]old woman."

[021-011]"Indeed--indeed I didn't mean to be--fierce, Aunt Stanbury."

[021-012]"I never was taken up so short in my life. But we won't mind that.

[021-013]There; he shall come and see you. I suppose he won't insist on

[021-014]leaving any of his nastiness about."

[021-015]"But is he to come here, Aunt Stanbury?"

[021-016]"He may if he pleases."

[021-017]"Oh, Aunt Stanbury!"

[021-018]"When he was here last he generally had a pipe in his mouth, and I

[021-019]dare say he never puts it down at all now. Those things grow upon

[021-020]young people so fast. But if he could leave it on the door-step just

[021-021]while he's here I should be obliged to him."

[021-022]"But, dear aunt, couldn't I see him in the street?"

[021-023]"Out in the street! No, my dear. All the world is not to know that

[021-024]he's your brother; and he is dressed in such a rapscallion manner

[021-025]that the people would think you were talking to a house-breaker."

[021-026]Dorothy's face became again red as she heard this, and the angry

[021-027]words were very nearly spoken. "The last time I saw him," continued

[021-028]Miss Stanbury, "he had on a short, rough jacket, with enormous

[021-029]buttons, and one of those flipperty-flopperty things on his head,

[021-030]that the butcher-boys wear. And, oh, the smell of tobacco! As he had

[021-031]been up in [[london|London]] I suppose he thought [[exeter|Exeter]] was no better than a

[021-032]village, and he might do just as he pleased. But he knew that if

[021-033]I'm particular about anything, it is about a [[social-class|gentleman]]'s hat in

[021-034]the streets. And he wanted me--me!--to walk with him across to Mrs.

[021-035]MacHugh's! We should have been hooted about the Close like a pair of

[021-036]mad dogs;--and so I told him."

[021-037]"All the young men seem to dress like that now, Aunt Stanbury."

[021-038]"No, they don't. Mr. Gibson doesn't dress like that."

[021-039]"But he's a clergyman, Aunt Stanbury."

[021-040]"Perhaps I'm an old fool. I dare say I am, and of course that's what

[021-041]you mean. At any rate I'm too old to change, and I don't mean to try.

[021-042]I like to see a difference between a gentleman and a house-breaker.

[021-043]For the matter of that I'm told that there is a difference, and that

[021-044]the house-breakers all look like gentlemen now. It may be proper to

[021-045]make us all stand on our heads, with our legs sticking up in the air;

[021-046]but I for one don't like being topsy-turvey, and I won't try it. When

[021-047]is he to reach Exeter?"

[021-048]"He is coming on Tuesday next, by the last train."

[021-049]"Then you can't see him that night. That's out of the question. No

[021-050]doubt he'll sleep at the Nag's Head, as that's the lowest radical

[021-051]public-house in the city. Martha shall try to find him. She knows

[021-052]more about his doings than I do. If he chooses to come here the

[021-053]following morning before he goes down to [[nuncombe-putney|Nuncombe Putney]], well and

[021-054]good. I shall wait up till Martha comes back from the train on

[021-055]Tuesday night, and hear." Dorothy was of course full of gratitude and

[021-056]thanks; but yet she felt almost disappointed by the result of her

[021-057]aunt's clemency on the matter. She had desired to take her brother's

[021-058]part, and it had seemed to her as though she had done so in a very

[021-059]lukewarm manner. She had listened to an immense number of accusations

[021-060]against him, and had been unable to reply to them because she had

[021-061]been conquered by the promise of a visit. And now it was out of the

[021-062]question that she should speak of going. Her aunt had given way to

[021-063]her, and of course had conquered her.

[021-064]Late on the Tuesday evening, after ten o'clock, [[hugh-stanbury|Hugh Stanbury]] was

[021-065]walking round the Close with his aunt's old servant. He had not put

[021-066]up at that dreadfully radical establishment of which Miss Stanbury

[021-067]was so much afraid, but had taken a bed-room at the Railway Inn. From

[021-068]there he had walked up to the Close with Martha, and now was having a

[021-069]few last words with her before he would allow her to return to the

[021-070]house.

[021-071]"I suppose she'd as soon see the devil as see me," said Hugh.

[021-072]"If you speak in that way, Mr. Hugh, I won't listen to you."

[021-073]"And yet I did everything I could to please her; and I don't think

[021-074]any boy ever loved an old woman better than I did her."

[021-075]"That was while she used to send you cakes, and ham, and jam to

[021-076]school, Mr. Hugh."

[021-077]"Of course it was, and while she sent me flannel waistcoats to

[021-078]Oxford. But when I didn't care any longer for cakes or flannel then

[021-079]she got tired of me. It is much better as it is, if she'll only be

[021-080]good to Dorothy."

[021-081]"She never was bad to anybody, Mr. Hugh. But I don't think an old

[021-082]lady like her ever takes to a young woman as she does to a young man,

[021-083]if only he'll let her have a little more of her own way than you

[021-084]would. It's my belief that you might have had it all for your own

[021-085]some day, if you'd done as you ought."

[021-086]"That's nonsense, Martha. She means to leave it all to the Burgesses.

[021-087]I've heard her say so."

[021-088]"Say so; yes. People don't always do what they say. If you'd managed

[021-089]rightly you might have it all;--and so you might now."

[021-090]"I'll tell you what, old girl; I shan't try. Live for the next twenty

[021-091]years under her apron strings, that I may have the chance at the end

[021-092]of it of cutting some poor devil out of his money! Do you know the

[021-093]meaning of making a score off your own bat, Martha?"

[021-094]"No, I don't; and if it's anything you're like to do, I don't think I

[021-095]should be the better for learning,--by all accounts. And now if you

[021-096]please, I'll go in."

[021-097]"Good night, Martha. My love to them both, and say I'll be there

[021-098]to-morrow exactly at half-past nine. You'd better take it. It won't

[021-099]turn to slate-stone. It hasn't come from the old gentleman."

[021-100]"I don't want anything of that kind, Mr. Hugh;--indeed I don't."

[021-101]"Nonsense. If you don't take it you'll offend me. I believe you think

[021-102]I'm not much better than a schoolboy still."

[021-103]"I don't think you're half so good, Mr. Hugh," said the old servant,

[021-104]sticking the sovereign which Hugh had given her in under her glove as

[021-105]she spoke.

[021-106]On the next morning that other visit was made at the brick house, and

[021-107]Miss Stanbury was again in a fuss. On this occasion, however, she was

[021-108]in a much better humour than before, and was full of little jokes as

[021-109]to the nature of the visitation. Of course, she was not to see her

[021-110]nephew herself, and no message was to be delivered from her, and none

[021-111]was to be given to her from him. But an accurate report was to be

[021-112]made to her as to his appearance, and Dorothy was to be enabled to

[021-113]answer a variety of questions respecting him after he was gone. "Of

[021-114]course, I don't want to know anything about his money," Miss Stanbury

[021-115]said, "only I should like to know how much these people can afford

[021-116]to pay for their penny trash." On this occasion she had left the

[021-117]room and gone up-stairs before the knock came at the door, but she

[021-118]managed, by peeping over the balcony, to catch a glimpse of the

[021-119]"flipperty-flopperty" hat which her nephew certainly had with him on

[021-120]this occasion.

[021-121]Hugh Stanbury had great news for his sister. The cottage in which

[021-122]Mrs. Stanbury lived at Nuncombe Putney, was the tiniest little

[021-123]dwelling in which a lady and her two daughters ever sheltered

[021-124]themselves. There was, indeed, a sitting-room, two bed-rooms, and a

[021-125]kitchen; but they were all so diminutive in size that the cottage was

[021-126]little more than a cabin. But there was a house in the village, not

[021-127]large indeed, but eminently respectable, three stories high, covered

[021-128]with ivy, having a garden behind it, and generally called the Clock

[021-129]House, because there had once been a clock upon it. This house

[021-130]had been lately vacated, and Hugh informed his sister that he was

[021-131]thinking of taking it for his mother's accommodation. Now, the

[021-132]late occupants of the [[clock-house|Clock House]], at Nuncombe Putney, had been

[021-133]people with five or six hundred a year. Had other matters been in

[021-134]accordance, the house would almost have entitled them to consider

[021-135]themselves as county people. A gardener had always been kept

[021-136]there,--and a cow!

[021-137]"The Clock House for mamma!"

[021-138]"Well, yes. Don't say a word about it as yet to Aunt Stanbury, as

[021-139]she'll think that I've sold myself altogether to the old gentleman."

[021-140]"But, Hugh, how can mamma live there?"

[021-141]"The fact is, Dorothy, there is a secret. I can't tell you quite

[021-142]yet. Of course, you'll know it, and everybody will know it, if the

[021-143]thing comes about. But as you won't talk, I will tell you what most

[021-144]concerns ourselves."

[021-145]"And am I to go back?"

[021-146]"Certainly not,--if you will take my advice. Stick to your aunt. You

[021-147]don't want to smoke pipes, and wear Tom-and-Jerry hats, and write for

[021-148]the penny newspapers."

[021-149]Now Hugh Stanbury's secret was this;--that [[louis-trevelyan|Louis Trevelyan]]'s wife and

[021-150]sister-in-law were to leave the house in Curzon Street, and come and

[021-151]live at Nuncombe Putney, with Mrs. Stanbury and Priscilla. Such, at

[021-152]least, was the plan to be carried out, if Hugh Stanbury should be

[021-153]successful in his present negotiations.

[021-155]THE HONOURABLE MR. GLASCOCK.

[021-157]By the end of July Mrs. Trevelyan with her sister was established in

[021-158]the Clock House, at Nuncombe Putney, under the protection of Hugh's

[021-159]mother; but before the reader is made acquainted with any of the

[021-160]circumstances of their life there, a few words must be said of an

[021-161]occurrence which took place before those two ladies left Curzon

[021-162]Street.

[021-163]As to the quarrel between Trevelyan and his wife things went from bad

[021-164]to worse. Lady Milborough continued to interfere, writing letters

[021-165]to Emily which were full of good sense, but which, as Emily said

[021-166]herself, never really touched the point of dispute. "Am I, who am

[021-167]altogether unconscious of having done anything amiss, to confess that

[021-168]I have been in the wrong? If it were about a small matter, I would

[021-169]not mind, for the sake of peace. But when it concerns my conduct in

[021-170]reference to another man I would rather die first." That had been

[021-171]Mrs. Trevelyan's line of thought and argument in the matter; but

[021-172]then old Lady Milborough in her letters spoke only of the duty of

[021-173]obedience as promised at the altar. "But I didn't promise to tell a

[021-174]lie," said Mrs. Trevelyan. And there were interviews between Lady

[021-175]Milborough and Trevelyan, and interviews between Lady Milborough and

[021-176][[nora-rowley|Nora Rowley]]. The poor dear old dowager was exceedingly busy and full

[021-177]of groans, prescribing Naples, prescribing a course of extra prayers,

[021-178]prescribing a general course of letting by-gones be by-gones,--to

[021-179]which, however, Trevelyan would by no means assent without some

[021-180]assurance, which he might regard as a guarantee,--prescribing

[021-181]retirement to a small town in the west of France if Naples would not

[021-182]suffice; but she could effect nothing.
