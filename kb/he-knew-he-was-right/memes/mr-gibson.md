# Mr. Gibson

Mr. Gibson is a minor but sharply drawn character in the Exeter social circle, notable chiefly for his prolonged and indecisive courtship of Dorothy Stanbury. A man of respectable standing but no particular force of character, he serves as a comic foil to the earnest and decisive Brooke Burgess — and as a thematic illustration of the damage that masculine irresolution can inflict on women's lives.

## Overview

Mr. Gibson moves in the Exeter society centred on Miss Stanbury's circle. He appears at social gatherings, plays no cards — "Mr. Gibson never played cards" (038-014) — and conducts himself with a gentlemanly vagueness that masks a fundamental inability to commit. His courtship of Dorothy Stanbury has become a standing joke in Exeter: "a seven years of flirtation" (060-076) that has produced neither a proposal nor a withdrawal, only an endless, draining ambiguity.

His character is revealed primarily through the commentary of others. Miss Stanbury speaks of him with increasing exasperation: "There's a good deal expected of Mr. Gibson, my dears" (050-033), she remarks, adding acidly that "it seems to me Mr. Gibson is not inclined to perform" (050-034). The Miss Frenches — Arabella and Camilla — pursue him with an energy that he neither encourages nor definitively rebuffs, and their manoeuvrings around him provide some of the novel's most pointed comic scenes.

## Role in the Novel

Mr. Gibson's narrative function is to embody a particular kind of masculine failure — not the catastrophic, destructive failure of a Louis Trevelyan, but the quieter, attritional failure of a man who simply cannot decide. His seven-year flirtation with Dorothy consumes years of her eligible youth and leaves her with nothing to show for it. It is only through the arrival of Brooke Burgess — a man willing to act on his feelings — that Dorothy escapes the limbo into which Gibson's indecision has placed her.

His simultaneous involvement with the Miss Frenches — "Don't you think so, Mr. Gibson?" Arabella French presses him repeatedly (026-014, 026-033, 026-041) — demonstrates that his indecision is not merely romantic but structural. He drifts between potential partners without the courage to choose or the honesty to withdraw, leaving a trail of social awkwardness and feminine disappointment.

## Relationships

### Dorothy Stanbury

Mr. Gibson's relationship with Dorothy Stanbury is the defining failure of his character. The Exeter community widely expects a marriage: "They say he is to marry Dorothy Stanbury" (059-094). But expectation never hardens into action. He visits, he flirts, he hovers — and he never proposes. Dorothy, a young woman of limited means with no father to press her case, is left in a position of painful dependency on his intentions — a position that the novel presents as both unjust and all too common.

Dorothy herself reaches a point of clarity about him. "She tried hard to teach herself to love Mr. Gibson" (050-056), the narrator observes — the operative word being "tried." Her inability to manufacture affection for a man who cannot manufacture a decision is one of the novel's quiet victories of self-knowledge.

### Arabella and Camilla French

The Miss Frenches — Arabella and Camilla — pursue Mr. Gibson with a determination that borders on the predatory. They invite him to tea, consult him on matters of fashion and propriety, and whisper Colonel Osborne's name into his ear at parties (026-059). Their attentions to him are partly romantic interest and partly a reflection of the limited options available to unmarried women in provincial society. Gibson receives their advances with the same passive ambiguity that characterises all his dealings, neither encouraging nor discouraging — and thereby encouraging by default.

### Miss Stanbury

Miss Stanbury's view of Mr. Gibson evolves from guarded approval to frank exasperation. "There's a good deal expected of Mr. Gibson, my dears," she tells her nieces, "which it seems to me Mr. Gibson is not inclined to perform" (050-033, 050-034). Her sharp tongue cuts through the social niceties that protect Gibson from accountability, and her judgment — that he is a man who will not do what is expected of him — is ultimately vindicated.

## Key Scenes

### The Northernhay Gathering

A social gathering in the Northernhay brings together Mr. Gibson and the Miss Frenches in a scene that exemplifies the Exeter social whirl (037-180). Gibson circulates, is consulted by Arabella, flattered by Camilla, and observed by all — a man perpetually at the centre of romantic speculation without ever becoming the centre of romantic action.

### Tea with the Miss Frenches

The tea-party scene in which Arabella French presses Mr. Gibson for his opinions on marriage, propriety, and Colonel Osborne is a minor comic masterpiece (026-009–026-072). "Don't you think so, Mr. Gibson?" — the question recurs with variations, each iteration drawing him deeper into conversational complicity with women he has no intention of marrying. His replies — "Mr. Gibson replied that perhaps there might be something in it" (026-015), "Indeed!" said Mr. Gibson (026-062) — are masterpieces of non-committal evasion.

### The Collapse of Expectations

The moment at which it becomes clear that Mr. Gibson will not marry Dorothy is rendered not as a dramatic scene but as a gradual dawning. Miss Stanbury's pronouncement — "which it seems to me Mr. Gibson is not inclined to perform" (050-034) — crystallises what the community has long suspected. Dorothy's quiet acceptance of this outcome, and her subsequent openness to Brooke Burgess's genuine affection, represents her liberation from Gibson's paralysing ambiguity.

## Character Development

Mr. Gibson does not develop; he persists. That is precisely the point. In a novel that traces multiple forms of masculine failure — Trevelyan's pathological jealousy, Colonel Osborne's manipulative vanity — Gibson represents the failure of simple indecision. His character is a study in the moral consequences of not choosing: the years he consumes, the expectations he raises and dashes, the women he keeps in a state of suspended uncertainty. His punishment is not dramatic but social — he fades from the narrative as Dorothy moves on to a real marriage, leaving Gibson to the Miss Frenches and the tea-parties of Exeter, a man who will never quite perform what is expected of him.
