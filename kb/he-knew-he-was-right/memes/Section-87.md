[087-017]all." The wife did all that she could to comfort him, reminding him

[087-018]that Sir Marmaduke would be home soon, and that then the burden would

[087-019]be taken from his shoulders. But she was forced to admit that it was

[087-020]very hard.

[087-021]CHAPTER LIII.

[087-022]HUGH STANBURY IS SHEWN TO BE NO CONJUROR.

[087-023][Illustration]

[087-024]Many weeks had now passed since [[hugh-stanbury|Hugh Stanbury]] had paid his visit to

[087-025]St. Diddulph's, and [[nora-rowley|Nora Rowley]] was beginning to believe that her

[087-026]rejection of her lover had been so firm and decided that she would

[087-027]never see him or hear from him more; and she had long since confessed

[087-028]to herself that if she did not see him or hear from him soon, life

[087-029]would not be worth a straw to her. To all of us a single treasure

[087-030]counts for much more when the outward circumstances of our life are

[087-031]dull, unvaried, and melancholy, than it does when our days are full

[087-032]of pleasure, or excitement, or even of business. With Nora Rowley at

[087-033]St. Diddulph's life at present was very melancholy. There was little

[087-034]or no society to enliven her. Her sister was sick at heart, and

[087-035]becoming ill in health under the burden of her troubles. Mr. Outhouse

[087-036]was moody and wretched; and Mrs. Outhouse, though she did her best

[087-037]to make her house comfortable to her unwelcome inmates, could not

[087-038]make it appear that their presence there was a pleasure to her.

[087-039]Nora understood better than did her sister how distasteful the

[087-040]present arrangement was to their uncle, and was consequently very

[087-041]uncomfortable on that score. And in the midst of that unhappiness,

[087-042]she of course told herself that she was a young woman miserable and

[087-043]unfortunate altogether. It is always so with us. The heart when it is

[087-044]burdened, though it may have ample strength to bear the burden, loses

[087-045]its buoyancy and doubts its own power. It is like the springs of a

[087-046]carriage which are pressed flat by the superincumbent weight. But,

[087-047]because the springs are good, the weight is carried safely, and they

[087-048]are the better afterwards for their required purposes because of the

[087-049]trial to which they have been subjected.

[087-050]Nora had sent her lover away, and now at the end of three months

[087-051]from the day of his dismissal she had taught herself to believe that

[087-052]he would never come again. Amidst the sadness of her life at St.

[087-053]Diddulph's some confidence in a lover expected to come again would

[087-054]have done much to cheer her. The more she thought of Hugh Stanbury,

[087-055]the more fully she became convinced that he was the man who as a

[087-056]lover, as a husband, and as a companion, would just suit all her

[087-057]tastes. She endowed him liberally with a hundred good gifts in the

[087-058]disposal of which Nature had been much more sparing. She made for

[087-059]herself a mental portrait of him more gracious in its flattery than

[087-060]ever was canvas coming from the hand of a Court limner. She gave

[087-061]him all gifts of manliness, honesty, truth, and energy, and felt

[087-062]regarding him that he was a Paladin,--such as Paladins are in this

[087-063]age, that he was indomitable, sure of success, and fitted in all

[087-064]respects to take the high position which he would certainly win

[087-065]for himself. But she did not presume him to be endowed with such a

[087-066]constancy as would make him come to seek her hand again. Had Nora at

[087-067]this time of her life been living at the West-end of [[london|London]], and

[087-068]going out to parties three or four times a week, she would have been

[087-069]quite easy about his coming. The springs would not have been weighted

[087-070]so heavily, and her heart would have been elastic.

[087-071]No doubt she had forgotten many of the circumstances of his visit

[087-072]and of his departure. Immediately on his going she had told her

[087-073]sister that he would certainly come again, but had said at the same

[087-074]time that his coming could be of no use. He was so poor a man; and

[087-075]she,--though poorer than he,--had been so little accustomed to

[087-076]poverty of life, that she had then acknowledged to herself that she

[087-077]was not fit to be his wife. Gradually, as the slow weeks went by her,

[087-078]there had come a change in her ideas. She now thought that he never

[087-079]would come again; but that if he did she would confess to him that

[087-080]her own views about life were changed. "I would tell him frankly

[087-081]that I could eat a crust with him in any garret in London." But this

[087-082]was said to herself;--never to her sister. Emily and Mrs. Outhouse

[087-083]had determined together that it would be wise to abstain from all

[087-084]mention of Hugh Stanbury's name. Nora had felt that her sister had so

[087-085]abstained, and this reticence had assisted in producing the despair

[087-086]which had come upon her. Hugh, when he had left her, had certainly

[087-087]given her encouragement to expect that he would return. She had been

[087-088]sure then that he would return. She had been sure of it, though she

[087-089]had told him that it would be useless. But now, when these sad weeks

[087-090]had slowly crept over her head, when during the long hours of the

[087-091]long days she had thought of him continually,--telling herself that

[087-092]it was impossible that she should ever become the wife of any man if

[087-093]she did not become his,--she assured herself that she had seen and

[087-094]heard the last of him. She must surely have forgotten his hot words

[087-095]and that daring embrace.

[087-096]Then there came a letter to her. The question of the management of

[087-097]letters for young ladies is handled very differently in different

[087-098]houses. In some establishments the post is as free to young ladies

[087-099]as it is to the reverend seniors of the household. In others it is

[087-100]considered to be quite a matter of course that some experienced

[087-101]discretion should sit in judgment on the correspondence of the

[087-102]daughters of the family. When Nora Rowley was living with her sister

[087-103]in Curzon Street, she would have been very indignant indeed had it

[087-104]been suggested to her that there was any authority over her letters

[087-105]vested in her sister. But now, circumstanced as she was at St.

[087-106]Diddulph's, she did understand that no letter would reach her

[087-107]without her aunt knowing that it had come. All this was distasteful

[087-108]to her,--as were indeed all the details of her life at St.

[087-109]Diddulph's;--but she could not help herself. Had her aunt told her

[087-110]that she should never be allowed to receive a letter at all, she must

[087-111]have submitted till her mother had come to her relief. The letter

[087-112]which reached her now was put into her hands by her sister, but it

[087-113]had been given to Mrs. Trevelyan by Mrs. Outhouse. "Nora," said

[087-114]Mrs. Trevelyan, "here is a letter for you. I think it is from Mr.

[087-115]Stanbury."

[087-116]"Give it me," said Nora greedily.

[087-117]"Of course I will give it you. But I hope you do not intend to

[087-118]correspond with him."

[087-119]"If he has written to me I shall answer him of course," said Nora,

[087-120]holding her treasure.

[087-121]"Aunt Mary thinks that you should not do so till papa and mamma have

[087-122]arrived."

[087-123]"If Aunt Mary is afraid of me let her tell me so, and I will contrive

[087-124]to go somewhere else." Poor Nora knew that this threat was futile.

[087-125]There was no house to which she could take herself.

[087-126]"She is not afraid of you at all, Nora. She only says that she thinks

[087-127]you should not write to Mr. Stanbury." Then Nora escaped to the cold

[087-128]but solitary seclusion of her bed-room and there she read her letter.

[087-129]The reader may remember that Hugh Stanbury when he last left St.

[087-130]Diddulph's had not been oppressed by any of the gloomy reveries of a

[087-131]despairing lover. He had spoken his mind freely to Nora, and had felt

[087-132]himself justified in believing that he had not spoken in vain. He had

[087-133]had her in his arms, and she had found it impossible to say that she

[087-134]did not love him. But then she had been quite firm in her purpose to

[087-135]give him no encouragement that she could avoid. She had said no word

[087-136]that would justify him in considering that there was any engagement

[087-137]between them; and, moreover, he had been warned not to come to the

[087-138]house by its mistress. From day to day he thought of it all, now

[087-139]telling himself that there was nothing to be done but to trust in

[087-140]her fidelity till he should be in a position to offer her a fitting

[087-141]home, and then reflecting that he could not expect such a girl as

[087-142]Nora Rowley to wait for him, unless he could succeed in making her

[087-143]understand that he at any rate intended to wait for her. On one day

[087-144]he would think that good faith and proper consideration for Nora

[087-145]herself required him to keep silent; on the next he would tell

[087-146]himself that such maudlin chivalry as he was proposing to himself was

[087-147]sure to go to the wall and be neither rewarded nor recognised. So at

[087-148]last he sat down and wrote the following letter:--

[087-149]Lincoln's Inn Fields, January, 186--.

[087-150]DEAREST NORA,

[087-151]Ever since I last saw you at St. Diddulph's, I have been

[087-152]trying to teach myself what I ought to do in reference

[087-153]to you. Sometimes I think that because I am poor I ought

[087-154]to hold my tongue. At others I feel sure that I ought to

[087-155]speak out loud, because I love you so dearly. You may

[087-156]presume that just at this moment the latter opinion is in

[087-157]the ascendant.

[087-158]As I do write I mean to be very bold; so bold that if I am

[087-159]wrong you will be thoroughly disgusted with me and will

[087-160]never willingly see me again. But I think it best to be

[087-161]true, and to say what I think. I do believe that you love

[087-162]me. According to all precedent I ought not to say so;--but

[087-163]I do believe it. Ever since I was at St. Diddulph's that

[087-164]belief has made me happy,--though there have been moments

[087-165]of doubt. If I thought that you did not love me, I would

[087-166]trouble you no further. A man may win his way to love when

[087-167]social circumstances are such as to throw him and the girl

[087-168]together; but such is not the case with us; and unless you

[087-169]love me now, you never will love me.

[087-170]"I do--I do!" said Nora, pressing the letter to her bosom.

[087-171]If you do, I think that you owe it me to say so, and

[087-172]to let me have all the joy and all the feeling of

[087-173][[duty|responsibility]] which such an assurance will give me.
