## Section 129

[129-001]"Do you mean, sir, to take away your wife's [[honour|character]]?" said Sir

[129-002]Marmaduke, coming up in wrath. "Remember that she is my daughter, and

[129-003]that there are things which flesh and blood cannot stand."

[129-004]"She is my wife, sir, and that is ten times more. Do you think that

[129-005]you would do more for her than I would do,--drink more of Esill? You

[129-006]had better go away, Sir Marmaduke. You can do no good by coming here

[129-007]and talking of your daughter. I would have given the world to save

[129-008]her;--but she would not be saved."

[129-009]"You are a slanderer!" said Sir Marmaduke, in his wrath.

[129-010]Mr. Glascock turned round to the father, and tried to quiet him. It

[129-011]was so manifest to him that the balance of the poor man's mind was

[129-012]gone, that it seemed to him to be ridiculous to upbraid the sufferer.

[129-013]He was such a piteous sight to behold, that it was almost impossible

[129-014]to feel indignation against him. "You cannot wonder," said Mr.

[129-015]Glascock, advancing close to the master of the house, "that the

[129-016]mother should want to see her only child. You do not wish that your

[129-017]wife should be the most wretched woman in the world."

[129-018]"Am not I the most wretched of men? Can anything be more wretched

[129-019]than this? Is her life worse than mine? And whose fault was it? Had

[129-020]I any friend to whom she objected? Was I untrue to her in a single

[129-021]thought?"

[129-022]"If you say that she was untrue, it is a falsehood," said Sir

[129-023]Marmaduke.

[129-024]"You allow yourself a [[personal-freedom|liberty]] of expression, sir, because you are my

[129-025]wife's father," said Trevelyan, "which you would not dare to take in

[129-026]other circumstances."

[129-027]"I say that it is a false calumny,--a lie! and I would say so to any

[129-028]man on earth who should dare to slander my child's name."

[129-029]"Your child, sir! She is my wife;--my wife;--my wife!" Trevelyan, as

[129-030]he spoke, advanced close up to his father-in-law; and at last hissed

[129-031]out his words, with his lips close to Sir Marmaduke's face. "Your

[129-032]right in her is gone, sir. She is mine,--mine,--mine! And you see the

[129-033]way in which she has treated me, Mr. Glascock. Everything I had was

[129-034]hers; but the words of a grey-haired sinner were sweeter to her than

[129-035]all my love. I wonder whether you think that it is a pleasant thing

[129-036]for such a one as I to come out here and live in such a place as

[129-037]this? I have not a friend,--a companion,--hardly a book. There is

[129-038]nothing that I can eat or drink. I do not stir out of the house,--and

[129-039]I am ill;--very ill! Look at me. See what she has brought me to! Mr.

[129-040]Glascock, on my honour as a man, I never wronged her in a thought or

[129-041]a word."

[129-042]Mr. Glascock had come to think that his best chance of doing any good

[129-043]was to get Trevelyan into conversation with himself, free from the

[129-044]interruption of Sir Marmaduke. The father of the injured woman could

[129-045]not bring himself to endure the hard words that were spoken of his

[129-046]daughter. During this last speech he had broken out once or twice;

[129-047]but Trevelyan, not heeding him, had clung to Mr. Glascock's arm. "Sir

[129-048]Marmaduke," said he, "would you not like to see the boy?"

[129-049]"He shall not see the boy," said Trevelyan. "You may see him. He

[129-050]shall not. What is he that he should have control over me?"

[129-051]"This is the most fearful thing I ever heard of," said Sir Marmaduke.

[129-052]"What are we to do with him?"

[129-053]Mr. Glascock whispered a few words to Sir Marmaduke, and then

[129-054]declared that he was ready to be taken to the child. "And he will

[129-055]remain here?" asked Trevelyan. A pledge was then given by Sir

[129-056]Marmaduke that he would not force his way farther into the house,

[129-057]and the two other men left the chamber together. Sir Marmaduke,

[129-058]as he paced up and down the room alone, perspiring at every pore,

[129-059]thoroughly uncomfortable and ill at ease, thought of all the hard

[129-060]positions of which he had ever read, and that his was harder than

[129-061]them all. Here was a man married to his daughter, in possession of

[129-062]his daughter's child, manifestly mad,--and yet he could do nothing

[129-063]to him! He was about to return to the seat of his government, and

[129-064]he must leave his own child in this madman's power! Of course, his

[129-065]daughter could not go with him, leaving her child in this madman's

[129-066]hands. He had been told that even were he to attempt to prove the man

[129-067]to be mad in Italy, the process would be slow; and, before it could

[129-068]be well commenced, Trevelyan would be off with the child elsewhere.

[129-069]There never was an embarrassment, thought Sir Marmaduke, out of which

[129-070]it was so impossible to find a clear way.

[129-071]In the meantime, Mr. Glascock and Trevelyan were visiting the child.

[129-072]It was evident that the father, let him be ever so mad, had discerned

[129-073]the expediency of allowing some one to see that his son was alive

[129-074]and in health. Mr. Glascock did not know much of children, and could

[129-075]only say afterwards that the boy was silent and very melancholy, but

[129-076]clean, and apparently well. It appeared that he was taken out daily

[129-077]by his father in the cool hours of the morning, and that his father

[129-078]hardly left him from the time that he was taken up till he was put to

[129-079]bed. But Mr. Glascock's desire was to see Trevelyan alone, and this

[129-080]he did after they had left the boy. "And now, Trevelyan," he said,

[129-081]"what do you mean to do?"

[129-082]"To do?"

[129-083]"In what way do you propose to live? I want you to be reasonable with

[129-084]me."

[129-085]"They do not treat me reasonably."

[129-086]"Are you going to measure your own conduct by that of other people?

[129-087]In the first place, you should go back to England. What good can you

[129-088]do here?" Trevelyan shook his head, but remained silent. "You cannot

[129-089]like this life."

[129-090]"No, indeed. But whither can I go now that I shall like to live?"

[129-091]"Why not home?"

[129-092]"I have no home."

[129-093]"Why not go back to England? Ask your wife to join you, and return

[129-094]with her. She would go at a word." The poor wretch again shook

[129-095]his head. "I hope you think that I speak as your friend," said Mr.

[129-096]Glascock.

[129-097]"I believe you do."

[129-098]"I will say nothing of any imprudence; but you cannot believe that

[129-099]she has been untrue to you?" Trevelyan would say nothing to this, but

[129-100]stood silent waiting for Mr. Glascock to continue. "Let her come back

[129-101]to you--here; and then, as soon as you can arrange it, go to your own

[129-102]home."

[129-103]"Shall I tell you something?" said Trevelyan.

[129-104]"What is it?"

[129-105]He came up close to Mr. Glascock, and put his hand upon his visitor's

[129-106]shoulder. "I will tell you what she would do at once. I dare say that

[129-107]she would come to me. I dare say that she would go with me. I am

[129-108]sure she would. And directly she got me there, she would--say that I

[129-109]was--mad! She,--my wife, would do it! He,--that furious, ignorant old

[129-110]man below, tried to do it before. His wife said that I was mad." He

[129-111]paused a moment, as though waiting for a reply; but Mr. Glascock had

[129-112]none to make. It had not been his object, in the advice which he had

[129-113]given, to entrap the poor fellow by a snare, and to induce him so to

[129-114]act that he should deliver himself up to keepers; but he was well

[129-115]aware that wherever Trevelyan might be, it would be desirable that he

[129-116]should be placed for awhile in the charge of some physician. He could

[129-117]not bring himself at the spur of the moment to repudiate the idea by

[129-118]which Trevelyan was actuated. "Perhaps you think that she would be

[129-119]right?" said Trevelyan.

[129-120]"I am quite sure that she would do nothing that is not for the best,"

[129-121]said Mr. Glascock.

[129-122]"I can see it all. I will not go back to England, Mr. Glascock. I

[129-123]intend to travel. I shall probably leave this and go to--to--to

[129-124]Greece, perhaps. It is a healthy place, this, and I like it for that

[129-125]reason; but I shall not stay here. If my wife likes to travel with

[129-126]me, she can come. But,--to England I will not go."

[129-127]"You will let the child go to his mother?"

[129-128]"Certainly not. If she wants to see the child, he is here. If she

[129-129]will come,--without her father,--she shall see him. She shall not

[129-130]take him from hence. Nor shall she return to live with me, without

[129-131]full acknowledgment of her fault, and promises of an amended life. I

[129-132]know what I am saying, Mr. Glascock, and have thought of these things

[129-133]perhaps more than you have done. I am obliged to you for coming to

[129-134]me; but now, if you please, I would prefer to be alone."

[129-135]Mr. Glascock, seeing that nothing further could be done, joined Sir

[129-136]Marmaduke, and the two walked down to their carriage at the bottom of

[129-137]the hill. Mr. Glascock, as he went, declared his conviction that the

[129-138]unfortunate man was altogether mad, and that it would be necessary

[129-139]to obtain some interference on the part of the authorities for the

[129-140]protection of the child. How this could be done, or whether it

[129-141]could be done in time to intercept a further flight on the part of

[129-142]Trevelyan, Mr. Glascock could not say. It was his idea that Mrs.

[129-143]Trevelyan should herself go out to Casalunga, and try the force of

[129-144]her own persuasion.

[129-145]"I believe that he would murder her," said Sir Marmaduke.

[129-146]"He would not do that. There is a glimmer of sense in all his

[129-147]madness, which will keep him from any actual violence."

[129-148]CHAPTER LXXIX.

[129-149]"I CAN SLEEP ON THE BOARDS."

[129-150]Three days after this there came another carriage to the bottom of

[129-151]the hill on which Casalunga stood, and a lady got out of it all

[129-152]alone. It was [[emily-trevelyan|Emily Trevelyan]], and she had come thither from Siena

[129-153]in quest of her husband and her child. On the previous day Sir

[129-154]Marmaduke's courier had been at the house with a note from the

[129-155]wife to the husband, and had returned with an answer, in which

[129-156]Mrs. Trevelyan was told that, if she would come quite alone, she

[129-157]should see her child. Sir Marmaduke had been averse to any further

[129-158]intercourse with the man, other than what might be made in accordance

[129-159]with medical advice, and, if possible, with government authority.

[129-160]Lady Rowley had assented to her daughter's wish, but had suggested

[129-161]that she should at least be allowed to go also,--at any rate, as

[129-162]far as the bottom of the hill. But Emily had been very firm, and Mr.

[129-163]Glascock had supported her. He was confident that the man would do no

[129-164]harm to her, and he was indisposed to believe that any interference

[129-165]on the part of the Italian Government could be procured in such a

[129-166]case with sufficient celerity to be of use. He still thought it might

[129-167]be possible that the wife might prevail over the husband, or the

[129-168]mother over the father. Sir Marmaduke was at last obliged to yield,

[129-169]and Mrs. Trevelyan went to Siena with no other companion but the

[129-170]courier. From Siena she made the journey quite alone; and having

[129-171]learned the circumstances of the house from Mr. Glascock, she got out

[129-172]of the carriage, and walked up the hill. There were still the two men

[129-173]coopering at the vats, but she did not stay to speak to them. She

[129-174]went through the big gates, and along the slanting path to the door,

[129-175]not doubting of her way;--for Mr. Glascock had described it all to

[129-176]her, making a small plan of the premises, and even explaining to her

[129-177]the position of the room in which her boy and her husband slept. She

[129-178]found the door open, and an Italian maid-servant at once welcomed

[129-179]her to the house, and assured her that the signor would be with her

[129-180]immediately. She was sure that the girl knew that she was the boy's

[129-181]mother, and was almost tempted to ask questions at once as to the

[129-182]state of the household; but her [[truth|knowledge]] of Italian was slight, and

[129-183]she felt that she was so utterly a stranger in the land that she

[129-184]could dare to trust no one. Though the heat was great, her face was

[129-185]covered with a thick veil. Her dress was black, from head to foot,

[129-186]and she was as a woman who mourned for her husband. She was led into

[129-187]the room which her father had been allowed to enter through the

[129-188]window; and here she sat, in her husband's house, feeling that in no

[129-189]position in the world could she be more utterly separated from the

[129-190]interests of all around her. In a few minutes the door was opened,

[129-191]and her husband was with her, bringing the boy in his hand. He had

[129-192]dressed himself with some care; but it may be doubted whether the

[129-193]garments which he wore did not make him appear thinner even and more

[129-194]haggard than he had looked to be in his old dressing-gown. He had not

[129-195]shaved himself, but his long hair was brushed back from his forehead,

[129-196]after a fashion quaint and very foreign to his former ideas of

[129-197]dress. His wife had not expected that her child would come to her

[129-198]at once,--had thought that some entreaties would be necessary, some

[129-199]obedience perhaps exacted from her, before she would be allowed to

[129-200]see him; and now her heart was softened, and she was grateful to her

[129-201]husband. But she could not speak to him till she had had the boy in

[129-202]her arms. She tore off her bonnet, and then clinging to the child,

[129-203]covered him with kisses. "Louey, my darling! Louey; you remember

[129-204]mamma?" The child pressed himself close to the mother's bosom, but

[129-205]spoke never a word. He was cowed and overcome, not only by the

[129-206]incidents of the moment, but by the terrible melancholy of his

[129-207]whole life. He had been taught to understand, without actual spoken

[129-208]lessons, that he was to live with his father, and that the former

[129-209]woman-given happinesses of his life were at an end. In this second

[129-210]visit from his mother he did not forget her. He recognised the luxury

[129-211]of her love; but it did not occur to him even to hope that she might

[129-212]have come to rescue him from the evil of his days. Trevelyan was

[129-213][[reputation|standing]] by, the while, looking on; but he did not speak till she

[129-214]addressed him.
