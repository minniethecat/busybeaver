## Section 127

[127-001]"I should have thought it was very dreadful," said Lady Rowley.

[127-002]"Full of dread, of awe, and of horror, were those fiery days of

[127-003]indiscriminate slaughter; but they were not days of desolation,

[127-004]because hope was always there by our side. There was a hope in

[127-005]which the soul could trust, and the trusting soul is ever light and

[127-006]buoyant."

[127-007]"I dare say it is," said Lady Rowley.

[127-008]"But apathy, and serfdom, and kinghood, and dominion, drain the

[127-009]fountain of its living springs, and the soul becomes like the plummet

[127-010]of lead, whose only tendency is to hide itself in subaqueous mud and

[127-011]unsavoury slush."

[127-012]Subaqueous mud and unsavoury slush! Lady Rowley repeated the words to

[127-013]herself as she made good her escape, and again expressed to herself

[127-014]her conviction that it could not possibly be so. The "subaqueous mud

[127-015]and unsavoury slush," with all that had gone before it about the

[127-016]soul was altogether unintelligible to her; but she knew that it was

[127-017]American buncom of a high order of eloquence, and she told herself

[127-018]again and again that it could not be so. She continued to keep her

[127-019]eyes upon Mr. Glascock, and soon saw him again talking to Nora. It

[127-020]was hardly possible, she thought, that Nora should speak to him

[127-021]with so much animation, or he to her, unless there was some feeling

[127-022]between them which, if properly handled, might lead to a renewal of

[127-023]the old tenderness. She went up to Nora, having collected the other

[127-024]girls, and said that the carriage was then waiting for them. Mr.

[127-025]Glascock immediately offered Lady Rowley his arm, and took her

[127-026]down to the hall. Could it be that she was leaning upon a future

[127-027]son-in-law? There was something in the thought which made her lay

[127-028]her weight upon him with a freedom which she would not otherwise

[127-029]have used. Oh!--that her Nora should live to be Lady Peterborough!

[127-030]We are apt to abuse mothers for wanting high husbands for their

[127-031]daughters;--but can there be any point in which the true maternal

[127-032]instinct can shew itself with more affectionate enthusiasm? This poor

[127-033]mother wanted nothing for herself from Mr. Glascock. She knew very

[127-034]well that it was her fate to go back to the Mandarins, and probably

[127-035]to die there. She knew also that such men as Mr. Glascock, when they

[127-036]marry beneath themselves in [[social-class|rank]] and [[money|fortune]], will not ordinarily

[127-037]trouble themselves much with their mothers-in-law. There was nothing

[127-038]desired for herself. Were such a match accomplished, she might,

[127-039]perhaps, indulge herself in talking among the planters' wives of her

[127-040]daughter's coronet; but at the present moment there was no idea even

[127-041]of this in her mind. It was of Nora herself, and of Nora's sisters,

[127-042]that she was thinking,--for them that she was plotting,--that the

[127-043]one might be rich and splendid, and the others have some path opened

[127-044]for them to riches and splendour. Husband-hunting mothers may be

[127-045]injudicious; but surely they are maternal and unselfish. Mr. Glascock

[127-046]put her into the carriage, and squeezed her hand;--and then he

[127-047]squeezed Nora's hand. She saw it, and was sure of it. "I am so glad

[127-048]you are going to be happy," Nora had said to him before this. "As

[127-049]far as I have seen her, I like her so much." "If you do not come

[127-050]and visit her in her own house, I shall think you have no spirit of

[127-051]friendship," he said. "I will," Nora had replied;--"I will." This had

[127-052]been said up-stairs, just as Lady Rowley was coming to them, and on

[127-053]this understanding, on this footing, Mr. Glascock had pressed her

[127-054]hand.

[127-055]As she went home, Lady Rowley's mind was full of doubt as to the

[127-056]course which it was best that she should follow with her daughter.

[127-057]She was not unaware how great was the difficulty before her. Hugh

[127-058]Stanbury's name had not been mentioned since they left [[london|London]], but at

[127-059]that time Nora was obstinately bent on throwing herself away upon the

[127-060]"penny-a-liner." She had never been brought to acknowledge that such

[127-061]a marriage would be even inappropriate, and had withstood gallantly

[127-062]the expression of her father's displeasure. But with such a spirit as

[127-063]Nora's, it might be easier to prevail by silence than by many words.

[127-064]Lady Rowley was quite sure of this,--that it would be far better to

[127-065]say nothing further of [[hugh-stanbury|Hugh Stanbury]]. Let the cure come, if it might

[127-066]be possible, from absence and from her daughter's good sense. The

[127-067]only question was whether it would be wise to say any word about Mr.

[127-068]Glascock. In the carriage she was not only forbearing but flattering

[127-069]in her manner to Nora. She caressed her girl's hand and spoke to

[127-070]her,--as mothers know how to speak when they want to make much of

[127-071]their girls, and to have it understood that those girls are behaving

[127-072]as girls should behave. There was to be nobody to meet them to-night,

[127-073]as it had been arranged that Sir Marmaduke and Mrs. Trevelyan should

[127-074]sleep at Siena. Hardly a word had been spoken in the carriage; but

[127-075]up-stairs, in their drawing-room, there came a moment in which Lucy

[127-076]and Sophie had left them, and Nora was alone with her mother. Lady

[127-077]Rowley almost knew that it would be most prudent to be silent;--but a

[127-078]word spoken in season;--how good it is! And the thing was so near to

[127-079]her that she could not hold her peace. "I must say, Nora," she began,

[127-080]"that I do like your Mr. Glascock."

[127-081]"He is not my Mr. Glascock, mamma," said Nora, smiling.

[127-082]"You know what I mean, dear." Lady Rowley had not intended to utter a

[127-083]word that should appear like pressure on her daughter at this moment.

[127-084]She had felt how imprudent it would be to do so. But now Nora seemed

[127-085]to be leading the way herself to such discourse. "Of course, he is

[127-086]not your Mr. Glascock. You cannot eat your cake and have it, nor can

[127-087]you throw it away and have it."

[127-088]"I have thrown my cake away altogether, and certainly I cannot have

[127-089]it." She was still smiling as she spoke, and seemed to be quite merry

[127-090]at the idea of regarding Mr. Glascock as the cake which she had

[127-091]declined to eat.

[127-092]"I can see one thing quite plainly, dear."

[127-093]"What is that, mamma?"

[127-094]"That in spite of what you have done, you can still have your cake

[127-095]whenever you choose to take it."

[127-096]"Why, mamma, he is engaged to be married!"

[127-097]"Mr. Glascock?"

[127-098]"Yes, Mr. Glascock. It's quite settled. Is it not sad?"

[127-099]"To whom is he engaged?" Lady Rowley's solemnity as she asked this

[127-100]question was piteous to behold.

[127-101]"To Miss Spalding,--Caroline Spalding."

[127-102]"The eldest of those nieces?"

[127-103]"Yes;--the eldest."

[127-104]"I cannot believe it."

[127-105]"Mamma, they both told me so. I have sworn an eternal friendship with

[127-106]her already."

[127-107]"I did not see you speaking to her."

[127-108]"But I did talk to her a great deal."

[127-109]"And he is really going to marry that dreadful woman?"

[127-110]"Dreadful, mamma!"

[127-111]"Perfectly awful! She talked to me in a way that I have read about

[127-112]in books, but which I did not before believe to be possible. Do you

[127-113]mean that he is going to be married to that hideous old maid,--that

[127-114]bell-clapper?"

[127-115]"Oh, mamma, what slander! I think her so pretty."

[127-116]"Pretty!"

[127-117]"Very pretty. And, mamma, ought I not to be happy that he should

[127-118]have been able to make himself so happy? It was quite, quite, quite

[127-119]impossible that I should have been his wife. I have thought about it

[127-120]ever so much, and I am so glad of it! I think she is just the girl

[127-121]that is fit for him."

[127-122]Lady Rowley took her candle and went to bed, professing to herself

[127-123]that she could not understand it. But what did it signify? It was,

[127-124]at any rate, certain now that the man had put himself out of Nora's

[127-125]reach, and if he chose to marry a republican virago, with a red nose,

[127-126]it could now make no difference to Nora. Lady Rowley almost felt

[127-127]a touch of satisfaction in reflecting on the future misery of his

[127-128]married life.

[127-129]CHAPTER LXXVIII.

[127-130]CASALUNGA.

[127-131]Sir Marmaduke had been told at the Florence post-office that he would

[127-132]no doubt be able to hear tidings of Trevelyan, and to learn his

[127-133]address, from the officials in the post-office at Siena. At Florence

[127-134]he had been introduced to some gentleman who was certainly of

[127-135]importance,--a superintendent who had clerks under him and who was a

[127-136]big man. This person had been very courteous to him, and he had gone

[127-137]to Siena thinking that he would find it easy to obtain Trevelyan's

[127-138]address,--or to learn that there was no such person there. But at

[127-139]Siena he and his courier together could obtain no information. They

[127-140]rambled about the huge cathedral and the picturesque market-place

[127-141]of that quaint old city for the whole day, and on the next morning

[127-142]after breakfast they returned to Florence. They had learned nothing.

[127-143]The young man at the post-office had simply protested that he knew

[127-144]nothing of the name of Trevelyan. If letters should come addressed to

[127-145]such a name, he would keep them till they were called for; but, to

[127-146]the best of his [[truth|knowledge]], he had never seen or heard the name. At

[127-147]the guard-house of the gendarmerie they could not, or would not, give

[127-148]him any information, and Sir Marmaduke came back with an impression

[127-149]that everybody at Siena was ignorant, idiotic, and brutal. Mrs.

[127-150]Trevelyan was so dispirited as to be ill, and both Sir Marmaduke and

[127-151]Lady Rowley were disposed to think that the world was all against

[127-152]them. "You have no conception of the sort of woman that man is going

[127-153]to marry," said Lady Rowley.

[127-154]"What man?"

[127-155]"Mr. Glascock! A horrid American female, as old almost as I am, who

[127-156]talks through her nose, and preaches sermons about the rights of

[127-157]women. It is incredible! And Nora might have had him just for lifting

[127-158]up her hand." But Sir Marmaduke could not interest himself much about

[127-159]Mr. Glascock. When he had been told that his daughter had refused the

[127-160]heir to a great estate and a peerage, it had been matter of regret;

[127-161]but he had looked upon the affair as done, and cared nothing now

[127-162]though Mr. Glascock should marry a transatlantic Xantippe. He was

[127-163]angry with Nora because by her obstinacy she was adding to the

[127-164]general perplexities of the family, but he could not make comparisons

[127-165]on Mr. Glascock's behalf between her and Miss Spalding,--as his wife

[127-166]was doing, either mentally or aloud, from hour to hour. "I suppose it

[127-167]is too late now," said Lady Rowley, shaking her head.

[127-168]"Of course it is too late. The man must marry whom he pleases. I am

[127-169]beginning to wonder that anybody should ever want to get married. I

[127-170]am indeed."

[127-171]"But what are the girls to do?"

[127-172]"I don't know what anybody is to do. Here is a man as mad as a March

[127-173]hare, and yet nobody can touch him. If it was not for the child, I

[127-174]should advise Emily to put him out of her head altogether."

[127-175]But though Sir Marmaduke could not bring himself to take any interest

[127-176]in Mr. Glascock's affairs, and would not ask a single question

[127-177]respecting the fearful American female whom this unfortunate man

[127-178]was about to translate to the position of an English peeress, yet

[127-179]circumstances so fell out that before three days were over he and

[127-180]Mr. Glascock were thrown together in very intimate relations. Sir

[127-181]Marmaduke had learned that Mr. Glascock was the only Englishman in

[127-182]Florence to whom Trevelyan had been known, and that he was the only

[127-183]person with whom Trevelyan had been seen to speak while passing

[127-184]through the city. In his despair, therefore, Sir Marmaduke had gone

[127-185]to Mr. Glascock, and it was soon arranged that the two gentlemen

[127-186]should renew the search at Siena together, without having with them

[127-187]either Mrs. Trevelyan or the courier. Mr. Glascock knew the ways

[127-188]of the people better than did Sir Marmaduke, and could speak the

[127-189]language. He obtained a passport to the good offices of the police of

[127-190]Siena, and went prepared to demand rather than to ask for assistance.

[127-191]They started very early, before breakfast, and on arriving at Siena

[127-192]at about noon, first employed themselves in recruiting exhausted

[127-193]nature. By the time that they had both declared that the hotel at

[127-194]Siena was the very worst in all Italy, and that a breakfast without

[127-195]eatable butter was not to be considered a breakfast at all, they

[127-196]had become so intimate that Mr. Glascock spoke of his own intended

[127-197]marriage. He must have done this with the conviction on his mind that

[127-198][[nora-rowley|Nora Rowley]] would have told her mother of his former intention, and

[127-199]that Lady Rowley would have told Sir Marmaduke; but he did not feel

[127-200]it to be incumbent on himself to say anything on that subject. He had

[127-201]nothing to excuse. He had behaved fairly and honourably. It was not

[127-202]to be expected that he should remain unmarried for ever for the sake

[127-203]of a girl who had twice refused him. "Of course there are very many

[127-204]in England," he said, "who will think me foolish to marry a girl from

[127-205]another country."
