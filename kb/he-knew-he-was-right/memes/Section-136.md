## Section 136

[136-001]There came back an answer from Mr. Crump, in which that [[social-class|gentleman]]

[136-002]expressed a very strong idea that Mr. Gibson should be prosecuted for

[136-003]damages with the utmost virulence, and with the least possible delay.

[136-004]No compromise should be accepted. Mr. Crump would himself come to

[136-005][[exeter|Exeter]] and see the lawyer as soon as he should be told that there

[136-006]was a lawyer to be seen. As to the carving knife, Mr. Crump was of

[136-007]opinion that it did not mean anything. Mr. Crump was a gentleman who

[136-008]did not believe in strong romance, but who had great trust in all

[136-009]pecuniary claims. The Frenches had always been genteel. The late

[136-010]Captain French had been an officer in the army, and at ordinary times

[136-011]and seasons the Frenches were rather ashamed of the Crump connection.

[136-012]But now the timber merchant might prove himself to be a useful

[136-013]friend.

[136-014]Mrs. French shewed her brother's letter to Bella,--and poor Bella was

[136-015]again sore-hearted, seeing that nothing was said in it of her claims.

[136-016]"It will be dreadful scandal to have it all in the papers!" said

[136-017]Bella.

[136-018]"But what can we do?"

[136-019]"Anything would be better than that," said Bella. "And you don't want

[136-020]to punish Mr. Gibson, mamma."

[136-021]"But, my dear, you see what your uncle says. What can I do, except go

[136-022]to him for advice?"

[136-023]"Why don't you go to Mr. Gibson yourself, mamma?"

[136-024]But nothing was said to Camilla about Mr. Crump;--nothing as yet.

[136-025]Camilla did not love Mr. Crump, but there was no other house except

[136-026]that of Mr. Crump's at Gloucester to which she might be sent, if

[136-027]it could be arranged that Mr. Gibson and Bella should be made one.

[136-028]Mrs. French took her eldest daughter's advice, and went to Mr.

[136-029]Gibson;--taking Mr. Crump's letter in her pocket. For herself she

[136-030]wanted nothing,--but was it not the duty of her whole life to fight

[136-031]for her daughters? Poor woman! If somebody would only have taught her

[136-032]how that duty might best be done, she would have endeavoured to obey

[136-033]the teaching. "You know I do not want to threaten you," she said to

[136-034]Mr. Gibson; "but you see what my brother says. Of course I wrote to

[136-035]my brother. What could a poor woman do in such circumstances except

[136-036]write to her brother?"

[136-037]"If you choose to set the bloodhounds of the law at me, of course you

[136-038]can," said Mr. Gibson.

[136-039]"I do not want to go to law at all;--God knows I do not!" said Mrs.

[136-040]French. Then there was a pause. "Poor dear Bella!" ejaculated Mrs.

[136-041]French.

[136-042]"Dear Bella!" echoed Mr. Gibson.

[136-043]"What do you mean to do about Bella?" asked Mrs. French.

[136-044]"I sometimes think that I had better take poison and have done with

[136-045]it!" said Mr. Gibson, feeling himself to be very hard pressed.

[136-046]CHAPTER LXXXIII.

[136-047]BELLA VICTRIX.

[136-048]Mr. Crump arrived at Exeter. Camilla was not told of his coming till

[136-049]the morning of the day on which he arrived; and then the tidings were

[136-050]communicated, because it was necessary that a change should be made

[136-051]in the bed-rooms. She and her sister had separate rooms when there

[136-052]was no visitor with them, but now Mr. Crump must be accommodated.

[136-053]There was a long consultation between Bella and Mrs. French, but at

[136-054]last it was decided that Bella should sleep with her mother. There

[136-055]would still be too much of the lioness about Camilla to allow of her

[136-056]being regarded as a safe companion through the watches of the night.

[136-057]"Why is Uncle Jonas coming now?" she asked.

[136-058]"I thought it better to ask him," said Mrs. French.

[136-059]After a long pause, Camilla asked another question. "Does Uncle Jonas

[136-060]mean to see Mr. Gibson?"

[136-061]"I suppose he will," said Mrs. French.

[136-062]"Then he will see a low, mean fellow;--the lowest, meanest fellow

[136-063]that ever was heard of! But that won't make much difference to Uncle

[136-064]Jonas. I wouldn't have him now, if he was to ask me ever so;--that I

[136-065]wouldn't!"

[136-066]Mr. Crump came, and kissed his sister and two nieces. The embrace

[136-067]with Camilla was not very affectionate. "So your Joe has been and

[136-068]jilted you?" said Uncle Jonas;--"it's like one of them clergymen.

[136-069]They say so many prayers, they think they may do almost anything

[136-070]afterwards. Another man would have had his head punched."

[136-071]"The less talk there is about it the better," said Camilla.

[136-072]On the following day Mr. Crump called by appointment on Mr. Gibson,

[136-073]and remained closeted with that gentleman for the greater portion of

[136-074]the morning. Camilla knew well that he was going, and went about the

[136-075]house like a perturbed spirit during his absence. There was a look

[136-076]about her that made them all doubt whether she was not, in truth,

[136-077]losing her mind. Her mother more than once went to the pantry to

[136-078]see that the knives were right; and, as regarded that sharp-pointed

[136-079]weapon, was careful to lock it up carefully out of her daughter's

[136-080]way. Mr. Crump had declared himself willing to take Camilla back to

[136-081]Gloucester, and had laughed at the obstacles which his niece might,

[136-082]perhaps, throw in the way of such an arrangement. "She mustn't have

[136-083]much luggage;--that is all," said Mr. Crump. For Mr. Crump had been

[136-084]made aware of the circumstances of the trousseau. [[About]] three o'clock

[136-085]Mr. Crump came back from Mr. Gibson's, and expressed a desire to be

[136-086]left alone with Camilla. Mrs. French was prepared for everything; and

[136-087]Mr. Crump soon found himself with his younger niece.

[136-088]"Camilla, my dear," said he, "this has been a bad business."

[136-089]"I don't know what business you mean, Uncle Jonas."

[136-090]"Yes, you do, my dear;--you know. And I hope it won't come too late

[136-091]to prove to you that young women shouldn't be too keen in setting

[136-092]their caps at the gentlemen. It's better for them to be hunted, than

[136-093]to hunt."

[136-094]"Uncle Jonas, I will not be insulted."

[136-095]"Stick to that, my dear, and you won't get into a scrape again. Now,

[136-096]look here. This man can never be made to marry you, anyhow."

[136-097]"I wouldn't touch him with a pair of tongs, if he were kneeling at my

[136-098]feet!"

[136-099]"That's right; stick to that. Of course, you wouldn't now, after all

[136-100]that has come and gone. No girl with any spirit would."

[136-101]"He's a coward and a thief, and he'll be--damned for what he has

[136-102]done, some of these days!"

[136-103]"T-ch, t-ch, t-ch! That isn't a proper way for a young lady to talk.

[136-104]That's cursing and swearing."

[136-105]"It isn't cursing and swearing;--it's what the Bible says."

[136-106]"Then we'll leave him to the Bible. In the meantime, Mr. Gibson wants

[136-107]to marry some one else, and that can't hurt you."

[136-108]"He may marry whom he likes;--but he shan't marry Bella--that's all!"

[136-109]"It is Bella that he means to marry."

[136-110]"Then he won't. I'll forbid the banns. I'll write to the bishop. I'll

[136-111]go to the church and prevent its being done. I'll make such a noise

[136-112]in the town that it can't be done. It's no use your looking at me

[136-113]like that, Uncle Jonas. I've got my own feelings, and he shall never

[136-114]marry Bella. It's what they have been intending all through, and it

[136-115]shan't be done!"

[136-116]"It will be done."

[136-117]"Uncle Jonas, I'll stab her to the heart, and him too, before I'll

[136-118]see it done! Though I were to be killed the next day, I would. Could

[136-119]you bear it?"

[136-120]"I'm not a young woman. Now, I'll tell you what I want you to do."

[136-121]"I'll not do anything."

[136-122]"Just pack up your things, and start with me to Gloucester

[136-123]to-morrow."

[136-124]"I--won't!"

[136-125]"Then you'll be carried, my dear. I'll write to your aunt, to say

[136-126]that you're coming; and we'll be as jolly as possible when we get you

[136-127]home."

[136-128]"I won't go to Gloucester, Uncle Jonas. I won't go away from Exeter.

[136-129]I won't let it be done. She shall never, never, never be that man's

[136-130]wife!"

[136-131]Nevertheless, on the day but one after this, Camilla French did go to

[136-132]Gloucester. Before she went, however, things had to be done in that

[136-133]house which almost made Mrs. French repent that she had sent for so

[136-134]stern an assistant. Camilla was at last told, in so many words, that

[136-135]the things which she had prepared for her own wedding must be given

[136-136]up for the wedding of her sister; and it seemed that this item in

[136-137]the list of her sorrows troubled her almost more than any other. She

[136-138]swore that whither she went there should go the dresses, and the

[136-139]handkerchiefs, and the hats, the bonnets, and the boots. "Let her

[136-140]have them," Bella had pleaded. But Mr. Crump was inexorable. He had

[136-141]looked into his sister's affairs, and found that she was already in

[136-142]debt. To his practical mind, it was an absurdity that the unmarried

[136-143]sister should keep things that were wholly unnecessary, and that the

[136-144]sister that was to be married should be without things that were

[136-145]needed. There was a big trunk, of which Camilla had the key, but

[136-146]which, unfortunately for her, had been deposited in her mother's

[136-147]room. Upon this she sat, and swore that nothing should move her but a

[136-148]promise that her plunder should remain untouched. But there came this

[136-149]advantage from the terrible question of the wedding raiments,--that

[136-150]in her energy to keep possession of them, she gradually abandoned her

[136-151]opposition to her sister's marriage. She had been driven from one

[136-152]point to another till she was compelled at last to stand solely upon

[136-153]her possessions. "Perhaps we had better let her keep them," said Mrs.

[136-154]French. "Trash and nonsense!" said Mr. Crump. "If she wants a new

[136-155]frock, let her have it; as for the sheets and tablecloths, you'd

[136-156]better keep them yourself. But Bella must have the rest."

[136-157]It was found on the eve of the day on which she was told that she was

[136-158]to depart that she had in truth armed herself with a dagger or clasp

[136-159]knife. She actually displayed it when her uncle told her to come away

[136-160]from the chest on which she was sitting. She declared that she would

[136-161]defend herself there to the last gasp of her life; but of course the

[136-162]knife fell from her hand the first moment that she was touched. "I

[136-163]did think once that she was going to make a poke at me," Mr. Crump

[136-164]said afterwards; "but she had screamed herself so weak that she

[136-165]couldn't do it."

[136-166]When the morning came, she was taken to the fly and driven to

[136-167]the station without any further serious outbreak. She had even

[136-168]condescended to select certain articles, leaving the rest of

[136-169]the hymeneal [[money|wealth]] behind her. Bella, early on that morning of

[136-170]departure, with great humility, implored her sister to forgive her;

[136-171]but no entreaties could induce Camilla to address one gracious word

[136-172]to the proposed bride. "You've been cheating me all along!" she said;

[136-173]and that was the last word she spoke to poor Bella.

[136-174]She went, and the field was once more open to the amorous Vicar

[136-175]of St. Peter's-cum-Pumpkin. It is astonishing how the greatest

[136-176]difficulties will sink away, and become as it were nothing, when

[136-177]they are encountered face to face. It is certain that Mr. Gibson's

[136-178]position had been one most trying to the nerves. He had speculated on

[136-179]various modes of escape;--a curacy in the north of England would be

[136-180]welcome, or the duties of a missionary in New Zealand,--or death. To

[136-181]tell the truth, he had, during the last week or two, contemplated

[136-182]even a return to the dominion of Camilla. That there should ever

[136-183]again be things pleasant for him in Exeter seemed to be quite

[136-184]impossible. And yet, on the evening of the day but one after the

[136-185]departure of Camilla, he was seated almost comfortably with his own

[136-186]Arabella! There is nothing that a man may not do, nothing that he may

[136-187]not achieve, if he have only pluck enough to go through with it.
