## Section 98

[098-001]The letter, which was still in the ex-policeman's hand, had reached

[098-002]Stony Walk on the previous day; but the master of the house had been

[098-003]absent, finding out facts, following up his profession, and earning

[098-004]an honest penny. Trevelyan had followed his letter quicker than

[098-005]he had intended when it was written, and was now with his prime

[098-006]minister, before his prime minister had been able to take any action

[098-007]on the last instruction received. "Does one Mr. Samuel Bozzle live

[098-008]here?" asked Trevelyan. Then Bozzle came forward and introduced his

[098-009]wife. There was no one else present except the baby, and Bozzle

[098-010]intimated that let matters be as delicate as they might, they could

[098-011]be discussed with perfect security in his wife's presence. But

[098-012]Trevelyan was of a different opinion, and he was disgusted and

[098-013]revolted,--most unreasonably,--by the appearance of his minister's

[098-014]domestic arrangements. Bozzle had always waited upon him with a

[098-015]decent coat, and a well-brushed hat, and clean shoes. It is very much

[098-016]easier for such men as Mr. Bozzle to carry decency of appearance

[098-017]about with them than to keep it at home. Trevelyan had never believed

[098-018]his ally to be more than an ordinary ex-policeman, but he had not

[098-019]considered how unattractive might be the interior of a private

[098-020]detective's private residence. Mrs. Bozzle had set a chair for

[098-021]him, but he had declined to sit down. The room was dirty, and very

[098-022]close,--as though no breath of air was ever allowed to find entrance

[098-023]there. "Perhaps you could put on your coat, and walk out with me for

[098-024]a few minutes," said Trevelyan. Mrs. Bozzle, who well understood that

[098-025]business was business, and that wives were not business, felt no

[098-026]anger at this, and handed her husband his best coat. The well-brushed

[098-027]hat was fetched from a cupboard, and it was astonishing to see

[098-028]how easily and how quickly the outer respectability of Bozzle was

[098-029]restored.

[098-030]"Well?" said Trevelyan, as soon as they were together in the middle

[098-031]of Stony Walk.

[098-032]"There hasn't been nothing to be done, sir," said Bozzle.

[098-033]"Why not?" Trevelyan could perceive at once that the authority which

[098-034]he had once respected had gone from the man. Bozzle away from his own

[098-035]home, out on business, with his coat buttoned over his breast, and

[098-036]his best hat in his hand, was aware that he commanded respect,--and

[098-037]he could carry himself accordingly. He knew himself to be

[098-038]somebody, and could be easy, self-confident, confidential, severe,

[098-039]authoritative, or even arrogant, as the circumstances of the moment

[098-040]might demand. But he had been found with his coat off, and a baby in

[098-041]his arms, and he could not recover himself. "I do not suppose that

[098-042]anybody will question my right to have the care of my own child,"

[098-043]said Trevelyan.

[098-044]"If you would have gone to Mr. Skint, sir--," suggested Bozzle.

[098-045]"There ain't no smarter gent in all the profession, sir, than Mr.

[098-046]Skint."

[098-047]Mr. Trevelyan made no reply to this, but walked on in silence, with

[098-048]his minister at his elbow. He was very wretched, understanding well

[098-049]the degradation to which he was subjecting himself in discussing his

[098-050]wife's conduct with this man;--but with whom else could he discuss

[098-051]it? The man seemed to be meaner now than he had been before he had

[098-052]been seen in his own home. And Trevelyan was conscious too that he

[098-053]himself was not in outward appearance as he used to be; that he was

[098-054]ill-dressed, and haggard, and worn, and visibly a wretched being. How

[098-055]can any man care to dress himself with attention who is always alone,

[098-056]and always miserable when alone? During the months which had passed

[098-057]over him since he had sent his wife away from him, his very nature

[098-058]had been altered, and he himself was aware of the change. As he went

[098-059]about, his eyes were ever cast downwards, and he walked with a quick

[098-060]shuffling gait, and he suspected others, feeling that he himself was

[098-061]suspected. And all work had ceased with him. Since she had left him

[098-062]he had not read a single book that was worth the reading. And he knew

[098-063]it all. He was conscious that he was becoming disgraced and degraded.

[098-064]He would sooner have shot himself than have walked into his club,

[098-065]or even have allowed himself to be seen by daylight in Pall Mall,

[098-066]or Piccadilly. He had taken in his misery to drinking little drops

[098-067]of brandy in the morning, although he knew well that there was no

[098-068]shorter road to the devil than that opened by such a habit. He looked

[098-069]up for a moment at Bozzle, and then asked him a question. "Where is

[098-070]he now?"

[098-071]"You mean the Colonel, sir. He's up in town, sir, a minding of his

[098-072]parliamentary duties. He have been up all this month, sir."

[098-073]"They haven't met?"

[098-074]Bozzle paused a moment before he replied, and then smiled as he

[098-075]spoke. "It is so hard to say, sir. Ladies is so cute and cunning.

[098-076]I've watched as sharp as watching can go, pretty near. I've put a

[098-077]youngster on at each hend, and both of 'em 'd hear a mouse stirring

[098-078]in his sleep. I ain't got no [[truth|evidence]], Mr. Trevelyan. But if you ask

[098-079]me my opinion, why in course they've been together somewhere. It

[098-080]stands to reason, Mr. Trevelyan; don't it?" And Bozzle as he said

[098-081]this smiled almost aloud.

[098-082]"D----n and b----t it all for ever!" said Trevelyan, gnashing his

[098-083]teeth, and moving away into Union Street as fast as he could walk.

[098-084]And he did go away, leaving Bozzle [[reputation|standing]] in the middle of Stony

[098-085]Walk.

[098-086]"He's disturbed in his mind,--quite 'orrid," Bozzle said when he got

[098-087]back to his wife. "He cursed and swore as made even me feel bad."

[098-088]"B.," said his wife, "do you listen to me. Get in what's a howing,

[098-089]and don't you have nothing more to do with it."

[098-090]CHAPTER LX.

[098-091]ANOTHER STRUGGLE.

[098-092]Sir Marmaduke and Lady Rowley were to reach England about the end of

[098-093]March or the beginning of April, and both Mrs. Trevelyan and Nora

[098-094]Rowley were almost sick for their arrival. Both their uncle and aunt

[098-095]had done very much for them, had been true to them in their need, and

[098-096]had submitted to endless discomforts in order that their nieces might

[098-097]have respectable shelter in their great need; but nevertheless their

[098-098]conduct had not been of a kind to produce either love or friendship.

[098-099]Each of the sisters felt that she had been much better off at

[098-100][[nuncombe-putney|Nuncombe Putney]], and that either the weakness of Mrs. Stanbury,

[098-101]or the hardness of Priscilla, was preferable to the repulsive

[098-102]forbearance of their clerical host. He did not scold them. He never

[098-103]threw it in Mrs. Trevelyan's teeth that she had been separated

[098-104]from her husband by her own fault; he did not tell them of his own

[098-105]discomfort. But he showed it in every gesture, and spoke of it in

[098-106]every tone of his voice;--so that Mrs. Trevelyan could not refrain

[098-107]from apologising for the misfortune of her presence.

[098-108]"My dear," he said, "things can't be pleasant and unpleasant at the

[098-109]same time. You were quite right to come here. I am glad for all our

[098-110]sakes that Sir Marmaduke will be with us so soon."

[098-111]She had almost given up in her mind the hope that she had long

[098-112]cherished, that she might some day be able to live again with her

[098-113]husband. Every step which he now took in reference to her seemed to

[098-114]be prompted by so bitter an hostility, that she could not but believe

[098-115]that she was hateful to him. How was it possible that a husband and

[098-116]his wife should again come together, when there had been between

[098-117]them such an emissary as a detective policeman? Mrs. Trevelyan

[098-118]had gradually come to learn that Bozzle had been at Nuncombe

[098-119]Putney, watching her, and to be aware that she was still under the

[098-120]surveillance of his eye. For some months past now she had neither

[098-121]seen [[colonel-osborne|Colonel Osborne]], nor heard from him. He had certainly by his

[098-122]folly done much to produce the ruin which had fallen upon her; but it

[098-123]never occurred to her to blame him. Indeed she did not know that he

[098-124]was liable to blame. Mr. Outhouse always spoke of him with indignant

[098-125]scorn, and Nora had learned to think that much of their misery was

[098-126]due to his imprudence. But Mrs. Trevelyan would not see this, and,

[098-127]not seeing it, was more widely separated from her husband than

[098-128]she would have been had she acknowledged that any excuse for his

[098-129]misconduct had been afforded by the vanity and folly of the other

[098-130]man.

[098-131]Lady Rowley had written to have a furnished house taken for them

[098-132]from the first of April, and a house had been secured in Manchester

[098-133]Street. The situation in question is not one which is of itself very

[098-134]charming, nor is it supposed to be in a high degree fashionable; but

[098-135]Nora looked forward to her escape from St. Diddulph's to Manchester

[098-136]Street as though Paradise were to be re-opened to her as soon as she

[098-137]should be there with her father and mother. She was quite clear now

[098-138]as to her course about [[hugh-stanbury|Hugh Stanbury]]. She did not doubt but that she

[098-139]could so argue the matter as to get the consent of her father and

[098-140]mother. She felt herself to be altogether altered in her views of

[098-141]life, since experience had come upon her, first at Nuncombe Putney,

[098-142]and after that, much more heavily and seriously, at St. Diddulph's.

[098-143]She looked back as though to a childish dream to the ideas which had

[098-144]prevailed with her when she had told herself, as she used to do so

[098-145]frequently, that she was unfit to be a poor man's wife. Why should

[098-146]she be more unfit for such a position than another? Of course there

[098-147]were many thoughts in her mind, much of memory if nothing of regret,

[098-148]in regard to Mr. Glascock and the splendour that had been offered

[098-149]to her. She had had her chance of being a rich man's wife, and had

[098-150]rejected it,--had rejected it twice, with her eyes open. Readers

[098-151]will say that if she loved Hugh Stanbury with all her heart, there

[098-152]could be nothing of regret in her reflections. But we are perhaps

[098-153]accustomed in judging for ourselves and of others to draw the

[098-154]lines too sharply, and to say that on this side lie vice, folly,

[098-155]heartlessness, and greed,--and on the other honour, love, truth, and

[098-156]wisdom,--the good and the bad each in its own domain. But the good

[098-157]and the bad mix themselves so thoroughly in our thoughts, even in our

[098-158]aspirations, that we must look for excellence rather in overcoming

[098-159]evil than in freeing ourselves from its influence. There had been

[098-160]many moments of regret with Nora;--but none of remorse. At the very

[098-161]moment in which she had sent Mr. Glascock away from her, and had

[098-162]felt that he had now been sent away for always, she had been full of

[098-163]regret. Since that there had been many hours in which she had thought

[098-164]of her own self-lesson, of that teaching by which she had striven to

[098-165]convince herself that she could never fitly become a poor man's wife.

[098-166]But the upshot of it all was a healthy pride in what she had done,

[098-167]and a strong resolution that she would make shirts and hem towels for

[098-168]her husband if he required it. It had been given her to choose, and

[098-169]she had chosen. She had found herself unable to tell a man that she

[098-170]loved him when she did not love him,--and equally unable to conceal

[098-171]the love which she did feel. "If he wheeled a barrow of turnips about

[098-172]the street, I'd marry him to-morrow," she said to her sister one

[098-173]afternoon as they were sitting together in the room which ought to

[098-174]have been their uncle's study.
