## Section 44

[044-001]"And take the boy?"

[044-002]"No;--I could not permit that. What I intend is this. I will give

[044-003]her L800 a year, as long as I have reason to believe that she has no

[044-004]communication whatever, either by word of mouth or by letter, with

[044-005]that man. If she does, I will put the case immediately into the hands

[044-006]of my lawyer, with instructions to him to ascertain from counsel what

[044-007]severest steps I can take."

[044-008]"How I hate that word severe, when applied to a woman."

[044-009]"I dare say you do,--when applied to another man's wife. But there

[044-010]will be no severity in my first proposition. As for the child,--if

[044-011]I approve of the place in which she lives, as I do at present,--he

[044-012]shall remain with her for nine months in the year till he is

[044-013]six years old. Then he must come to me. And he shall come to me

[044-014]altogether if she sees or hears from that man. I believe that L800

[044-015]a year will enable her to live with all comfort under your mother's

[044-016]roof."

[044-017]"As to that," said Stanbury, slowly, "I suppose I had better tell you

[044-018]at once, that the [[nuncombe-putney|Nuncombe Putney]] arrangement cannot be considered as

[044-019]permanent."

[044-020]"Why not?"

[044-021]"Because my mother is timid and nervous, and altogether unused to the

[044-022]world."

[044-023]"That unfortunate woman is to be sent away,--even from Nuncombe

[044-024]Putney!"

[044-025]"Understand me, Trevelyan."

[044-026]"I understand you. I understand you most thoroughly. Nor do I wonder

[044-027]at it in the least. Do not suppose that I am angry with your mother,

[044-028]or with you, or with your sister. I have no right to expect that they

[044-029]should keep her after that man has made his way into their house. I

[044-030]can well conceive that no honest, high-minded lady would do so."

[044-031]"It is not that at all."

[044-032]"But it is that. How can you tell me that it isn't? And yet you would

[044-033]have me believe that I am not disgraced!" As he said this Trevelyan

[044-034]got up, and walked about the room, tearing his hair with his hands.

[044-035]He was in truth a wretched man, from whose mind all expectation of

[044-036]happiness was banished, who regarded his own position as one of

[044-037]incurable ignominy, looking upon himself as one who had been made

[044-038]unfit for society by no fault of his own. What was he to do with the

[044-039]wretched woman who could be kept from the evil of her pernicious

[044-040]vanity by no gentle custody, whom no most distant retirement

[044-041]would make safe from the effects of her own ignorance, folly, and

[044-042]obstinacy? "When is she to go?" he asked in a low, sepulchral

[044-043]tone,--as though these new tidings that had come upon him had been

[044-044]fatal--laden with doom, and finally subversive of all chance even of

[044-045]tranquillity.

[044-046]"When you and she may please."

[044-047]"That is all very well;--but let me know the truth. I would not have

[044-048]your mother's house--contaminated; but may she remain there for a

[044-049]week?"

[044-050]Stanbury jumped from his seat with an oath. "I tell you what it

[044-051]is, Trevelyan;--if you speak of your wife in that way, I will not

[044-052]listen to you. It is unmanly and untrue to say that her presence

[044-053]can--contaminate any house."

[044-054]"That is very fine. It may be chivalrous in you to tell me on her

[044-055]behalf that I am a liar,--and that I am not a man."

[044-056]"You drive me to it."

[044-057]"But what am I to think when you are forced to declare that this

[044-058]unfortunate woman can not be allowed to remain at your mother's

[044-059]house,--a house which has been especially taken with reference to a

[044-060]shelter for her? She has been received,--with the idea that she would

[044-061]be discreet. She has been indiscreet, past belief, and she is to be

[044-062]turned out,--most deservedly. Heaven and earth! Where shall I find

[044-063]a roof for her head?" Trevelyan as he said this was walking about

[044-064]the room with his hands stretched up towards the ceiling; and as

[044-065]his friend was attempting to make him comprehend that there was no

[044-066]intention on the part of any one to banish Mrs. Trevelyan from the

[044-067][[clock-house|Clock House]], at least for some months to come,--not even till after

[044-068]Christmas unless some satisfactory arrangement could be sooner

[044-069]made,--the door of the room was opened by the boy, who called himself

[044-070]a clerk, and who acted as Trevelyan's servant in the chambers, and

[044-071]a third person was shown into the room. That third person was Mr.

[044-072]Bozzle. As no name was given, Stanbury did not at first know Mr.

[044-073]Bozzle, but he had not had his eye on Mr. Bozzle for half a minute

[044-074]before he recognised the ex-policeman by the outward attributes

[044-075]and signs of his profession. "Oh, is that you, Mr. Bozzle?" said

[044-076]Trevelyan, as soon as the great man had made his bow of salutation.

[044-077]"Well;--what is it?"

[044-078][Illustration: That third person was Mr. Bozzle.]

[044-079]"Mr. [[hugh-stanbury|Hugh Stanbury]], I think," said Bozzle, making another bow to the

[044-080]young barrister.

[044-081]"That's my name," said Stanbury.

[044-082]"Exactly so, Mr. S. The identity is one as I could prove on oath in

[044-083]any court in England. You was on the railway platform at [[exeter|Exeter]] on

[044-084]Saturday when we was waiting for the 12 express 'buss;--wasn't you

[044-085]now, Mr. S.?"

[044-086]"What's that to you?"

[044-087]"Well;--as it do happen, it is something to me. And, Mr. S., if you

[044-088]was asked that question in hany court in England or before even one

[044-089]of the metropolitan bekes, you wouldn't deny it."

[044-090]"Why the devil should I deny it? What's all this about, Trevelyan?"

[044-091]"Of course you can't deny it, Mr. S. When I'm down on a fact, I am

[044-092]down on it. Nothing else wouldn't do in my profession."

[044-093]"Have you anything to say to me, Mr. Bozzle?" asked Trevelyan.

[044-094]"Well;--I have; just a word."

[044-095]"[[About]] your journey to Devonshire?"

[044-096]"Well;--in a way it is about my journey to Devonshire. It's all along

[044-097]of the same job, Mr. Trewillian."

[044-098]"You can speak before my friend here," said Trevelyan. Bozzle had

[044-099]taken a great dislike to Hugh Stanbury, regarding the barrister with

[044-100]a correct instinct as one who was engaged for the time in the same

[044-101]service with himself, and who was his rival in that service. When

[044-102]thus instigated to make as it were a party of three in this delicate

[044-103]and most confidential matter, and to take his rival into his

[044-104]confidence, he shook his head slowly and looked Trevelyan hard in the

[044-105]face,--"Mr. Stanbury is my particular friend," said Trevelyan, "and

[044-106]knows well the circumstances of this unfortunate affair. You can say

[044-107]anything before him."

[044-108]Bozzle shook his head again. "I'd rayther not, Mr. Trewillian," said

[044-109]he. "Indeed I'd rayther not. It's something very particular."

[044-110]"If you take my advice," said Stanbury, "you will not hear him

[044-111]yourself."

[044-112]"That's your advice, Mr. S.?" asked Mr. Bozzle.

[044-113]"Yes;--that's my advice. I'd never have anything to do with such a

[044-114]fellow as you as long as I could help it."

[044-115]"I dare say not, Mr. S.; I dare say not. We're hexpensive, and we're

[044-116]haccurate;--neither of which is much in your line, Mr. S., if I

[044-117]understand about it rightly."

[044-118]"Mr. Bozzle, if you've got anything to tell, tell it," said Trevelyan

[044-119]angrily.

[044-120]"A third party is so objectionable," pleaded Bozzle.

[044-121]"Never mind. That is my affair."

[044-122]"It is your affair, Mr. Trewillian. There's not a doubt of that. The

[044-123]lady is your wife."

[044-124]"Damnation!" shouted Trevelyan.

[044-125]"But the credit, sir," said Bozzle. "The credit is mine. And here

[044-126]is Mr. S. has been down a interfering with me, and doing no 'varsal

[044-127]good, as I'll undertake to prove by [[truth|evidence]] before the affair is

[044-128]over."

[044-129]"The affair is over," said Stanbury.

[044-130]"That's as you think, Mr. S. That's where your information goes to,

[044-131]Mr. S. Mine goes a little beyond that, Mr. S. I've means as you can

[044-132]know nothing about, Mr. S. I've irons in the fire, what you're as

[044-133]ignorant on as the babe as isn't born."

[044-134]"No doubt you have, Mr. Bozzle," said Stanbury.

[044-135]"I has. And now if it be that I must speak before a third party, Mr.

[044-136]Trewillian, I'm ready. It ain't that I'm no ways ashamed. I've done

[044-137]my duty, and knows how to do it. And let a counsel be ever so sharp,

[044-138]I never yet was so 'posed but what I could stand up and hold my own.

[044-139]The Colonel, Mr. Trewillian, got,--a letter,--from your lady,--this

[044-140]morning."

[044-141]"I don't believe it," said Stanbury, sharply.

[044-142]"Very likely not, Mr. S. It ain't in my power to say anything

[044-143]whatever about you believing or not believing. But Mr. T.'s lady

[044-144]has wrote the letter; and the Colonel,--he has received it. You

[044-145]don't look after these things, Mr. S. You don't know the ways of

[044-146]'em. But it's my business. The lady has wrote the letter, and the

[044-147]Colonel,--why, he has received it." Trevelyan had become white with

[044-148]rage when Bozzle first mentioned this continued correspondence

[044-149]between his wife and [[colonel-osborne|Colonel Osborne]]. It never occurred to him to

[044-150]doubt the correctness of the policeman's information, and he regarded

[044-151]Stanbury's assertion of incredulity as being simply of a piece with

[044-152]his general obstinacy in the matter. At this moment he began to

[044-153]regret that he had called in the assistance of his friend, and that

[044-154]he had not left the affair altogether in the hands of that much more

[044-155]satisfactory, but still more painful, agent, Mr. Bozzle. He had again

[044-156]seated himself, and for a moment or two remained silent on his chair.

[044-157]"It ain't my fault, Mr. Trewillian," continued Bozzle, "if this

[044-158]little matter oughtn't never to have been mentioned before a third

[044-159]party."

[044-160]"It is of no moment," said Trevelyan, in a low voice. "What does it

[044-161]signify who knows it now?"

[044-162]"Do not believe it, Trevelyan," said Stanbury.

[044-163]"Very well, Mr. S. Very well. Just as you like. Don't believe it.

[044-164]Only it's true, and it's my business to find them things out. It's

[044-165]my business, and I finds 'em out. Mr. Trewillian can do as he likes

[044-166]about it. If it's right, why, then it is right. It ain't for me to

[044-167]say nothing about that. But there's the fact. The lady, she has wrote

[044-168]another letter; and the Colonel,--why, he has received it. There

[044-169]ain't nothing wrong about the post-office. If I was to say what was

[044-170]inside of that billydou,--why, then I should be proving what I didn't

[044-171]know; and when it came to [[reputation|standing]] up in court, I shouldn't be able

[044-172]to hold my own. But as for the letter, the lady wrote it, and the

[044-173]Colonel,--he received it."

[044-174]"That will do, Mr. Bozzle," said Trevelyan.

[044-175]"Shall I call again, Mr. Trewillian?"

[044-176]"No;--yes. I'll send to you, when I want you. You shall hear from

[044-177]me."

[044-178]"I suppose I'd better be keeping my eyes open about the Colonel's

[044-179]place, Mr. Trewillian?"

[044-180]"For God's sake, Trevelyan, do not have anything more to do with this

[044-181]man!"

[044-182]"That's all very well for you, Mr. S.," said Bozzle. "The lady ain't

[044-183]your wife."

[044-184]"Can you imagine anything more disgraceful than all this?" said

[044-185]Stanbury.

[044-186]"Nothing; nothing; nothing!" answered Trevelyan.

[044-187]"And I'm to keep stirring, and be on the move?" again suggested

[044-188]Bozzle, who prudently required to be fortified by instructions before

[044-189]he devoted his time and talents even to so agreeable a pursuit as

[044-190]that in which he had been engaged.

[044-191]"You shall hear from me," said Trevelyan.

[044-192]"Very well;--very well. I wish you good-day, Mr. Trewillian. Mr. S.,

[044-193]yours most obedient. There was one other point, Mr. Trewillian."

[044-194]"What point?" asked Trevelyan, angrily.

[044-195]"If the lady was to join the Colonel--"

[044-196]"That will do, Mr. Bozzle," said Trevelyan, again jumping up from

[044-197]his chair. "That will do." So saying, he opened the door, and Bozzle,

[044-198]with a bow, took his departure. "What on earth am I to do? How am I

[044-199]to save her?" said the wretched husband, appealing to his friend.
