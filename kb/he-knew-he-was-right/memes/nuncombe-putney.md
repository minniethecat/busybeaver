# Nuncombe Putney

**Nuncombe Putney** is a fictional rural village in Devonshire that serves as a crucial setting in [[Anthony Trollope]]'s *He Knew He Was Right* (1869). It is the home of [[Hugh Stanbury]]'s family — his mother Mrs. Stanbury and his sister [[Dorothy Stanbury|Dorothy]] — and becomes the place of refuge where [[Emily Trevelyan]] flees with her infant son after her separation from [[Louis Trevelyan]]. In the novel's moral geography, Nuncombe Putney represents honesty, simplicity, and authentic feeling, set against the corruption and gossip of [[London]] society where the Trevelyan marriage unravels.

## Location and Character

Nuncombe Putney is a quintessential Trollopian rural community: small, socially interconnected, and governed by the quiet authority of figures like Miss Stanbury of [[Exeter]]. Its distance from London — both geographical and moral — makes it an ideal sanctuary for Emily Trevelyan when she can no longer remain in her husband's house. The village's name, combining the religious "Nuncombe" with the humble "Putney," suggests both retreat and ordinary English life.

## Significance in the Novel

### Refuge for Emily Trevelyan

When [[The Trevelyan Separation|the Trevelyan separation]] becomes unavoidable, Emily's departure to Nuncombe Putney is both practical and symbolic. She leaves behind the fashionable London square where her marriage collapsed and enters a world of reduced circumstances but greater moral clarity:

> She was determined to bear it all, to bear the poverty, the solitude, the scorn, the degradation, rather than to return to her husband under the stigma of an admission that she had been wrong. [011-028]

At Nuncombe Putney, Emily lives with her infant son under the protection of the Stanbury family, experiencing a form of domestic peace that contrasts sharply with the psychological warfare of her married life.

### The Stanbury Household

The village is inseparable from the Stanbury family who inhabit it. Mrs. Stanbury's modest cottage becomes the stage for some of the novel's most intimate scenes — the growing attachment between [[Nora Rowley]] and [[Hugh Stanbury]], the quiet dignity of Dorothy Stanbury's constrained life, and the network of female solidarity that sustains Emily through her ordeal.
