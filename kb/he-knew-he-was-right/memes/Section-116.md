## Section 116

[116-001]"I'm afraid you will think when it is too late."

[116-002]"It isn't that I don't like carriages, papa. I do like them; and

[116-003]pretty dresses, and brooches, and men and women who have nothing to

[116-004]do, and balls, and the opera; but--I love this man, and that is more

[116-005]to me than all the rest. I cannot help myself, if it were ever so.

[116-006]Papa, you mustn't be angry with me. Pray, pray, pray do not say that

[116-007]horrid word again."

[116-008]This was the end of the interview. Sir Marmaduke found that he had

[116-009]nothing further to say. Nora, when she reached her last prayer to her

[116-010]father, referring to that curse with which he had threatened her, was

[116-011]herself in tears, and was leaning on him with her head against his

[116-012]shoulder. Of course he did not say a word which could be understood

[116-013]as sanctioning her engagement with Stanbury. He was as strongly

[116-014]determined as ever that it was his duty to save her from the perils

[116-015]of such a marriage as that. But, nevertheless, he was so far overcome

[116-016]by her as to be softened in his manners towards her. He kissed her as

[116-017]he left her, and told her to go to her mother. Then he went out and

[116-018]thought of it all, and felt as though Paradise had been opened to his

[116-019]child and she had refused to enter the gate.

[116-020]CHAPTER LXXI.

[116-021]SHEWING WHAT HUGH STANBURY THOUGHT ABOUT THE DUTY OF MAN.

[116-022]In the conference which took place between Sir Marmaduke and his

[116-023]wife after the interview between him and Nora, it was his idea that

[116-024]nothing further should be done at all. "I don't suppose the man will

[116-025]come here if he be told not," said Sir Marmaduke, "and if he does,

[116-026]Nora of course will not see him." He then suggested that Nora would

[116-027]of course go back with them to the Mandarins, and that when once

[116-028]there she would not be able to see Stanbury any more. "There must be

[116-029]no correspondence or anything of that sort, and so the thing will die

[116-030]away." But Lady Rowley declared that this would not quite suffice.

[116-031]Mr. Stanbury had made his offer in due form, and must be held to be

[116-032]entitled to an answer. Sir Marmaduke, therefore, wrote the following

[116-033]letter to the "penny-a-liner," mitigating the asperity of his

[116-034]language in compliance with his wife's counsels.

[116-035]Manchester Street, April 20th, 186--.

[116-036]MY DEAR SIR,--

[116-037]Lady Rowley has told me of your proposal to my daughter

[116-038]Nora; and she has told me also what she learned from you

[116-039]as to your circumstances in life. I need hardly point out

[116-040]to you that no father would be justified in giving his

[116-041]daughter to a [[social-class|gentleman]] upon so small an [[money|income]], and upon

[116-042]an income so very insecure.

[116-043]I am obliged to refuse my consent, and I must therefore

[116-044]ask you to abstain from visiting and from communicating

[116-045]with my daughter.

[116-046]Yours faithfully,

[116-047]MARMADUKE ROWLEY.

[116-048][[hugh-stanbury|Hugh Stanbury]], Esq.

[116-049]This letter was directed to Stanbury at the office of the D. R.,

[116-050]and Sir Marmaduke, as he wrote the pernicious address, felt himself

[116-051]injured in that he was compelled to write about his daughter to a man

[116-052]so circumstanced. Stanbury, when he got the letter, read it hastily

[116-053]and then threw it aside. He knew what it would contain before he

[116-054]opened it. He had heard enough from Lady Rowley to be aware that Sir

[116-055]Marmaduke would not welcome him as a son-in-law. Indeed, he had never

[116-056]expected such welcome. He was half-ashamed of his own suit because of

[116-057]the lowliness of his position,--half-regretful that he should have

[116-058]induced such a girl as [[nora-rowley|Nora Rowley]] to give up for his sake her hopes

[116-059]of magnificence and splendour. But Sir Marmaduke's letter did not add

[116-060]anything to this feeling. He read it again, and smiled as he told

[116-061]himself that the father would certainly be very weak in the hands

[116-062]of his daughter. Then he went to work again at his article with a

[116-063]persistent resolve that so small a trifle as such a note should have

[116-064]no effect upon his daily work. Of course Sir Marmaduke would refuse

[116-065]his consent. Of course it would be for him, Stanbury, to marry the

[116-066]girl he loved in opposition to her father. Her father indeed! If Nora

[116-067]chose to take him,--and as to that he was very doubtful as to Nora's

[116-068]wisdom,--but if Nora would take him, what was any father's opposition

[116-069]to him? He wanted nothing from Nora's father. He was not looking

[116-070]for money with his wife;--nor for fashion, nor countenance. Such a

[116-071]Bohemian was he that he would be quite satisfied if his girl would

[116-072]walk out to him, and become his wife, with any morning-gown on and

[116-073]with any old hat that might come readiest to hand. He wanted neither

[116-074]cards, nor breakfast, nor carriages, nor fine clothes. If his Nora

[116-075]should choose to come to him as she was, he having had all previous

[116-076]necessary arrangements duly made,--such as calling of banns or

[116-077]procuring of licence if possible,--he thought that a father's

[116-078]opposition would almost add something to the pleasure of the

[116-079]occasion. So he pitched the letter on one side, and went on with his

[116-080]article. And he finished his article; but it may be doubted whether

[116-081]it was completed with the full strength and pith needed for moving

[116-082]the pulses of the national mind,--as they should be moved by leading

[116-083]articles in the D. R. As he was writing he was thinking of Nora,--and

[116-084]thinking of the letter which Nora's father had sent to him. Trivial

[116-085]as was the letter, he could not keep himself from repeating the words

[116-086]of it to himself. "'Need hardly point out,'--oh; needn't he. Then

[116-087]why does he? Refusing his consent! I wonder what the old buffers

[116-088]think is the meaning of their consent, when they are speaking of

[116-089]daughters old enough to manage for themselves? Abstain from visiting

[116-090]or communicating with her! But if she visits and communicates with

[116-091]me;--what then? I can't force my way into the house, but she can

[116-092]force her way out. Does he imagine that she can be locked up in the

[116-093]nursery or put into the corner?" So he argued with himself, and by

[116-094]such arguments he brought himself to the conviction that it would

[116-095]be well for him to answer Sir Marmaduke's letter. This he did at

[116-096]once,--before leaving the office of the D. R.

[116-097]250, Fleet Street, 20th April.

[116-098]MY DEAR SIR MARMADUKE ROWLEY,--

[116-099]I have just received your letter, and am indeed sorry that

[116-100]its contents should be so little favourable to my hopes.

[116-101]I understand that your objection to me is simply in regard

[116-102]to the smallness and insecurity of my income. On the first

[116-103]point I may say that I have fair hopes that it may be

[116-104]at once increased. As to the second, I believe I may

[116-105]assert that it is as sure at least as the income of other

[116-106]professional men, such as barristers, merchants, and

[116-107]doctors. I cannot promise to say that I will not see your

[116-108]daughter. If she desires me to do so, of course I shall

[116-109]be guided by her views. I wish that I might be allowed an

[116-110]opportunity of seeing you, as I think I could reverse or

[116-111]at least mitigate some of the objections which you feel to

[116-112]our marriage.

[116-113]Yours most faithfully,

[116-114]HUGH STANBURY.

[116-115]On the next day but one Sir Marmaduke came to him. He was sitting

[116-116]at the office of the D. R., in a very small and dirty room at the

[116-117]back of the house, and Sir Marmaduke found his way thither through

[116-118]a confused crowd of compositors, pressmen, and printers' boys. He

[116-119]thought that he had never before been in a place so foul, so dark,

[116-120]so crowded, and so comfortless. He himself was accustomed to do his

[116-121]work, out in the Islands, with many of the appanages of vice-royalty

[116-122]around him. He had his secretary, and his private secretary, and his

[116-123]inner-room, and his waiting-room; and not unfrequently he had the

[116-124]honour of a dusky sentinel walking before the door through which he

[116-125]was to be approached. He had an idea that all gentlemen at their

[116-126]work had comfortable appurtenances around them,--such as carpets,

[116-127]dispatch-boxes, unlimited stationery, easy chairs for temporary

[116-128]leisure, big table-space, and a small world of books around them

[116-129]to give at least a look of erudition to their pursuits. There was

[116-130]nothing of the kind in the miserably dark room occupied by Stanbury.

[116-131]He was sitting at a wretched little table on which there was nothing

[116-132]but a morsel of blotting paper, a small ink-bottle, and the paper

[116-133]on which he was scribbling. There was no carpet there, and no

[116-134]dispatch-box, and the only book in the room was a little dog's-eared

[116-135]dictionary. "Sir Marmaduke, I am so much obliged to you for coming,"

[116-136]said Hugh. "I fear you will find this place a little rough, but we

[116-137]shall be all alone."

[116-138]"The place, Mr. Stanbury, will not signify, I think."

[116-139]"Not in the least,--if you don't mind it. I got your letter, you

[116-140]know, Sir Marmaduke."

[116-141]"And I have had your reply. I have come to you because you have

[116-142]expressed a wish for an interview;--but I do not see that it will do

[116-143]any good."

[116-144]"You are very kind for coming, indeed, Sir Marmaduke;--very kind. I

[116-145]thought I might explain something to you about my income."

[116-146]"Can you tell me that you have any permanent income?"

[116-147]"It goes on regularly from month to month;"--Sir Marmaduke did not

[116-148]feel the slightest respect for an income that was paid monthly.

[116-149]According to his ideas, a gentleman's income should be paid

[116-150]quarterly, or perhaps half-yearly. According to his view, a monthly

[116-151]salary was only one degree better than weekly wages;--"and I suppose

[116-152]that is permanence," said Hugh Stanbury.

[116-153]"I cannot say that I so regard it."

[116-154]"A barrister gets his, you know, very irregularly. There is no saying

[116-155]when he may have it."

[116-156]"But a barrister's profession is recognised as a profession among

[116-157]gentlemen, Mr. Stanbury."

[116-158]"And is not ours recognised? Which of us, barristers or men of

[116-159]literature, have the most effect on the world at large? Who is most

[116-160]thought of in [[london|London]], Sir Marmaduke,--the Lord Chancellor or the

[116-161]Editor of the 'Jupiter?'"

[116-162]"The Lord Chancellor a great deal," said Sir Marmaduke, quite

[116-163]dismayed by the audacity of the question.

[116-164]"By no means, Sir Marmaduke," said Stanbury, throwing out his hand

[116-165]before him so as to give the energy of action to his words. "He has

[116-166]the higher rank. I will admit that."

[116-167]"I should think so," said Sir Marmaduke.

[116-168]"And the larger income."

[116-169]"Very much larger, I should say," said Sir Marmaduke, with a smile.

[116-170]"And he wears a wig."

[116-171]"Yes;--he wears a wig," said Sir Marmaduke, hardly knowing in what

[116-172]spirit to accept this assertion.

[116-173]"And nobody cares one brass button for him or his opinions," said

[116-174]Stanbury, bringing down his hand heavily on the little table for the

[116-175]sake of emphasis.

[116-176]"What, sir?"

[116-177]"If you'll think of it, it is so."

[116-178]"Nobody cares for the Lord Chancellor!" It certainly is the fact that

[116-179]gentlemen living in the [[mandarin-islands|Mandarin Islands]] do think more of the Lord

[116-180]Chancellor, and the Lord Mayor, and the Lord-Lieutenant, and the Lord

[116-181]Chamberlain, than they whose spheres of life bring them into closer

[116-182]contact with those august functionaries. "I presume, Mr. Stanbury,

[116-183]that a connection with a penny newspaper makes such opinions as these

[116-184]almost a necessity."

[116-185]"Quite a necessity, Sir Marmaduke. No man can hold his own in print,

[116-186]now-a-days, unless he can see the difference between tinsel and

[116-187]gold."

[116-188]"And the Lord Chancellor, of course, is tinsel."

[116-189]"I do not say so. He may be a great lawyer,--and very useful. But his

[116-190]lordship, and his wig, and his woolsack, are tinsel in comparison

[116-191]with the real power possessed by the editor of a leading newspaper.

[116-192]If the Lord Chancellor were to go to bed for a month, would he be

[116-193]much missed?"
