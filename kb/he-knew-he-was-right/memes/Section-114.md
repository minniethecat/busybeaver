## Section 114

[114-001]"I have come here, sir, to do the duty of a parent to his child;

[114-002]to protect my poor girl against the cruelty of a husband who in

[114-003]an unfortunate hour was allowed to take her from her home. I will

[114-004]know the reason why my daughter has been treated as though,--as

[114-005]though,--as though--"

[114-006]"Listen to me for a minute," said Trevelyan.

[114-007]"I am listening."

[114-008]"I will tell you nothing; I will answer you not a word."

[114-009]"You will not answer me?"

[114-010]"Not when you come to me in this fashion. My wife is my wife, and

[114-011]my claim to her is nearer and closer than is yours, who are her

[114-012]father. She is the mother of my child, and the only being in the

[114-013]world,--except that child,--whom I love. Do you think that with

[114-014]such motives on my part for tenderness towards her, for loving care,

[114-015]for the most anxious solicitude, that I can be made more anxious,

[114-016]more tender, more loving by coarse epithets from you? I am the

[114-017]most miserable being under the sun because our happiness has been

[114-018]interrupted, and is it likely that such misery should be cured by

[114-019]violent words and gestures? If your heart is wrung for her, so is

[114-020]mine. If she be much to you, she is more to me. She came here the

[114-021]other day, almost as a stranger, and I thought that my heart would

[114-022]have burst beneath its weight of woe. What can you do that can add

[114-023]an ounce to the burden that I bear? You may as well leave me,--or at

[114-024]least be quiet."

[114-025]Sir Marmaduke had stood and listened to him, and he, too, was so

[114-026]struck by the altered appearance of the man that the violence of his

[114-027]indignation was lessened by the pity which he could not suppress.

[114-028]When Trevelyan spoke of his wretchedness, it was impossible not to

[114-029]believe him. He was as wretched a being to look at as it might have

[114-030]been possible to find. His contracted cheeks, and lips always open,

[114-031]and eyes glowing in their sunken caverns, told a tale which even

[114-032]Sir Marmaduke, who was not of nature quick in deciphering such

[114-033]stories, could not fail to read. And then the twitching motion of

[114-034]the man's hands, and the restless shuffling of his feet, produced a

[114-035]nervous feeling that if some remedy were not applied quickly, some

[114-036]alleviation given to the misery of the suffering wretch, human power

[114-037]would be strained too far, and the man would break to pieces,--or

[114-038]else the mind of the man. Sir Marmaduke, during his journey in the

[114-039]cab, had resolved that, old as he was, he would take this sinner

[114-040]by the throat, this brute who had striven to stain his daughter's

[114-041]name,--and would make him there and then acknowledge his own

[114-042]brutality. But it was now very manifest to Sir Marmaduke that there

[114-043]could be no taking by the throat in this case. He could not have

[114-044]brought himself to touch the poor, weak, passionate creature before

[114-045]him. Indeed, even the fury of his words was stayed, and after that

[114-046]last appeal he stormed no more. "But what is to be the end of it?" he

[114-047]said.

[114-048]"Who can tell? Who can say? She can tell. She can put an end to it

[114-049]all. She has but to say a word, and I will devote my life to her. But

[114-050]that word must be spoken." As he said this, he dashed his hand upon

[114-051]the table, and looked up with an air that would have been comic with

[114-052]its assumed magnificence had it not been for the true tragedy of the

[114-053]occasion.

[114-054]"You had better, at any rate, let her have her child for the

[114-055]present."

[114-056]"No;--my boy shall go with me. She may go, too, if she pleases, but

[114-057]my boy shall certainly go with me. If I had put her from me, as you

[114-058]said just now, it might have been otherwise. But she shall be as

[114-059]welcome to me as flowers in May,--as flowers in May! She shall be as

[114-060]welcome to me as the music of heaven."

[114-061]Sir Marmaduke felt that he had nothing more to urge. He had

[114-062]altogether abandoned that idea of having his revenge at the cost of

[114-063]the man's throat, and was quite convinced that reason could have

[114-064]no power with him. He was already thinking that he would go away,

[114-065]straight to his lawyer, so that some step might be taken at once to

[114-066]stop, if possible, the taking away of the boy to America, when the

[114-067]lock of the door was gently turned, and the landlady entered the

[114-068]room.

[114-069]"You will excuse me, sir," said the woman, "but if you be anything to

[114-070]this [[social-class|gentleman]]--"

[114-071]"Mrs. Fuller, leave the room," said Trevelyan. "I and the gentleman

[114-072]are engaged."

[114-073]"I see you be engaged, and I do beg pardon. I ain't one as would

[114-074]intrude wilful, and, as for listening, or the likes of that, I scorn

[114-075]it. But if this gentleman be anything to you, Mr. Trevelyan--"

[114-076]"I am his wife's father," said Sir Marmaduke.

[114-077]"Like enough. I was thinking perhaps so. His lady was down here on

[114-078]Thursday,--as sweet a lady as any gentleman need wish to stretch by

[114-079]his side."

[114-080]"Mrs. Fuller," said Trevelyan, marching up towards her, "I will not

[114-081]have this, and I desire that you will retire from my room."

[114-082]But Mrs. Fuller escaped round the table, and would not be banished.

[114-083]She got round the table, and came closely opposite to Sir Marmaduke.

[114-084]"I don't want to say nothing out of my place, sir," said she, "but

[114-085]something ought to be done. He ain't fit to be left to hisself,--not

[114-086]alone,--not as he is at present. He ain't, indeed, and I wouldn't be

[114-087]doing my duty if I didn't say so. He has them sweats at night as'd be

[114-088]enough to kill any man; and he eats nothing, and he don't do nothing;

[114-089]and as for that poor little boy as is now in my own bed upstairs, if

[114-090]it wasn't that I and my Bessy is fond of children, I don't know what

[114-091]would become of that boy."

[114-092]Trevelyan, finding it impossible to get rid of her, had stood

[114-093]quietly, while he listened to her. "She has been good to my child,"

[114-094]he said. "I acknowledge it. As for myself, I have not been well. It

[114-095]is true. But I am told that travel will set me on my feet again.

[114-096]Change of air will do it." Not long since he had been urging the

[114-097]wretchedness of his own bodily health as a reason why his wife

[114-098]should yield to him; but now, when his sickness was brought as a

[114-099]charge against him,--was adduced as a reason why his friends should

[114-100]interfere, and look after him, and concern themselves in his affairs,

[114-101]he saw at once that it was necessary that he should make little of

[114-102]his ailments.

[114-103]"Would it not be best, Trevelyan, that you should come with me to a

[114-104]doctor?" said Sir Marmaduke.

[114-105]"No;--no. I have my own doctor. That is, I know the course which

[114-106]I should follow. This place, though it is good for the boy, has

[114-107]disagreed with me, and my life has not been altogether pleasant;--I

[114-108]may say, by no means pleasant. Troubles have told upon me, but change

[114-109]of air will mend it all."

[114-110]"I wish you would come with me, at once, to [[london|London]]. You shall come

[114-111]back, you know. I will not detain you."

[114-112]"Thank you,--no. I will not trouble you. That will do, Mrs. Fuller.

[114-113]You have intended to do your duty, no doubt, and now you can go."

[114-114]Whereupon Mrs. Fuller did go. "I am obliged for your care, Sir

[114-115]Marmaduke, but I can really do very well without troubling you."

[114-116]"You cannot suppose, Trevelyan, that we can allow things to go on

[114-117]like this."

[114-118]"And what do you mean to do?"

[114-119]"Well;--I shall take advice. I shall go to a lawyer,--and to a

[114-120]doctor, and perhaps to the Lord Chancellor, and all that kind of

[114-121]thing. We can't let things go on like this."

[114-122]"You can do as you please," said Trevelyan, "but as you have

[114-123]threatened me, I must ask you to leave me."

[114-124]Sir Marmaduke could do no more, and could say no more, and he took

[114-125]his leave, shaking hands with the man, and speaking to him with a

[114-126]courtesy which astonished himself. It was impossible to maintain

[114-127]the strength of his indignation against a poor creature who was so

[114-128]manifestly unable to guide himself. But when he was in London he

[114-129]drove at once to the house of Dr. Trite Turbury, and remained there

[114-130]till the doctor returned from his round of visits. According to

[114-131]the great authority, there was much still to be done before even

[114-132]the child could be rescued out of the father's hands. "I can't act

[114-133]without the lawyers," said Dr. Turbury. But he explained to Sir

[114-134]Marmaduke what steps should be taken in such a matter.

[114-135]Trevelyan, in the mean time, clearly understanding that hostile

[114-136]measures would now be taken against him, set his mind to work to

[114-137]think how best he might escape at once to America with his boy.

[114-138]CHAPTER LXX.

[114-139]SHEWING WHAT NORA ROWLEY THOUGHT ABOUT CARRIAGES.

[114-140]Sir Marmaduke, on his return home from Dr. Turbury's house, found

[114-141]that he had other domestic troubles on hand over and above those

[114-142]arising from his elder daughter's position. Mr. [[hugh-stanbury|Hugh Stanbury]] had

[114-143]been in Manchester Street during his absence, and had asked for him,

[114-144]and, finding that he was away from home, had told his story to Lady

[114-145]Rowley. When he had been shown up-stairs all the four daughters had

[114-146]been with their mother; but he had said a word or two signifying his

[114-147]desire to speak to Lady Rowley, and the three girls had left the

[114-148]room. In this way it came to pass that he had to plead his cause

[114-149]before Nora's mother and her elder sister. He had pleaded it well,

[114-150]and Lady Rowley's heart had been well disposed towards him; but when

[114-151]she asked of his house and his home, his answer had been hardly more

[114-152]satisfactory than that of Alan-a-Dale. There was little that he

[114-153]could call his own beyond "The blue vault of heaven." Had he saved

[114-154]any money? No,--not a shilling;--that was to say,--as he himself

[114-155]expressed it,--nothing that could be called money. He had a few

[114-156]pounds by him, just to go on with. What was his [[money|income]]? Well--last

[114-157]year he had made four hundred pounds, and this year he hoped to make

[114-158]something more. He thought he could see his way plainly to five

[114-159]hundred a year. Was it permanent; and if not, on what did it depend?

[114-160]He believed it to be as permanent as most other professional incomes,

[114-161]but was obliged to confess that, as regarded the source from whence

[114-162]it was drawn at the present moment, it might be brought to an abrupt

[114-163]end any day by a disagreement between himself and the editor of the

[114-164]D. R. Did he think that this was a fixed income? He did think that if

[114-165]he and the editor of the D. R. were to fall out, he could come across

[114-166]other editors who would gladly employ him. Would he himself feel safe

[114-167]in giving his own sister to a man with such an income? In answer to

[114-168]this question, he started some rather bold doctrines on the subject

[114-169]of [[marriage|matrimony]] in general, asserting that safety was not desirable,

[114-170]that energy, patience, and mutual confidence would be increased by

[114-171]the excitement of risk, and that in his opinion it behoved young men

[114-172]and young women to come together and get themselves married, even

[114-173]though there might be some not remote danger of distress before them.

[114-174]He admitted that starvation would be disagreeable,--especially for

[114-175]children, in the eyes of their parents,--but alleged that children

[114-176]as a rule were not starved, and quoted the Scripture to prove that

[114-177]honest laborious men were not to be seen begging their bread in

[114-178]the streets. He was very eloquent, but his eloquence itself was

[114-179]against him. Both Lady Rowley and Mrs. Trevelyan were afraid of such

[114-180]advanced opinions; and, although everything was of course to be left,

[114-181]nominally, to the decision of Sir Marmaduke, they both declared that

[114-182]they could not recommend Sir Marmaduke to consent. Lady Rowley said

[114-183]a word as to the expediency of taking Nora back with her to the

[114-184]Mandarins, pointing out what appeared to her then to be the necessity

[114-185]of taking Mrs. Trevelyan with them also; and in saying this she

[114-186]hinted that if Nora were disposed to stand by her engagement, and

[114-187]Mr. Stanbury equally so disposed, there might be some possibility

[114-188]of a marriage at a future period. Only in such case, there must be

[114-189]no correspondence. In answer to this Hugh declared that he regarded

[114-190]such a scheme as being altogether bad. The Mandarins were so very far

[114-191]distant that he might as well be engaged to an angel in heaven. Nora,

[114-192]if she were to go away now, would perhaps never come back again; and

[114-193]if she did come back, would be an old woman, with hollow cheeks. In

[114-194]replying to this proposition, he let fall an opinion that Nora was

[114-195]old enough to judge for herself. He said nothing about her actual

[114-196]age, and did not venture to plead that the young lady had a legal

[114-197]right to do as she liked with herself; but he made it manifest that

[114-198]such an idea was in his mind. In answer to this, Lady Rowley asserted

[114-199]that Nora was a good girl, and would do as her father told her; but

[114-200]she did not venture to assert that Nora would give up her engagement.

[114-201]Lady Rowley at last undertook to speak to Sir Rowley, and to speak

[114-202]also to her daughter. Hugh was asked for his address, and gave that

[114-203]of the office of the D. R. He was always to be found there between

[114-204]three and five; and after that, four times a week, in the reporters'

[114-205]gallery of the House of Commons. Then he was at some pains to explain

[114-206]to Lady Rowley that though he attended the reporters' gallery, he

[114-207]did not report himself. It was his duty to write leading political

[114-208]articles, and, to enable him to do so, he attended the debates.
