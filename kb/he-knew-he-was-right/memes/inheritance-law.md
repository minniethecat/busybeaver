# Inheritance Law

Inheritance law — the body of statutes and customs governing the transmission of property, title, and wealth — shapes the material circumstances of nearly every character in *He Knew He Was Right*. From Miss Stanbury's eccentric will to the Rowley sisters' lack of portions, the novel repeatedly shows how legal rules about property succession determine social destinies.

## Overview

Victorian inheritance was governed by a complex interplay of common law, equity, and statute. The default rule was primogeniture: real property passed to the eldest male heir. Married women could not own property independently until the Married Women's Property Act of 1870 (passed the year after the novel's publication). Wills could deviate from default rules, creating the possibility of eccentric distributions — and the family conflicts that attended them. Trollope uses inheritance as both plot mechanism and social commentary, showing how legal rules about money and property distribute power within families.

## Context in Victorian Society

The Victorian period saw ongoing reform of inheritance and property law. The Wills Act of 1837 had standardised testamentary formalities. The issue of married women's property was fiercely debated throughout the 1860s. Primogeniture remained the default for landed estates, but increasing amounts of wealth took the form of personal (movable) property, which could be more freely devised. The tension between traditional landed inheritance and new-money flexibility runs through the novel's treatment of property.

## Manifestation in the Novel

### Miss Stanbury's Will

Miss Stanbury of Exeter controls substantial wealth, which she uses as an instrument of social power. She tells her nephew Hugh that "the money was, at her death, to go back to the people from whom it had come to her" — a statement that keeps her relatives in a state of permanent uncertainty. Her will, and the threat of disinheritance it carries, governs the behaviour of everyone around her. The Exeter gossip about "inheritance" (088-168) reflects how wills were a matter of intense public speculation.

### The Rowley Sisters' Portionlessness

Sir Marmaduke Rowley, governor of the Mandarin Islands, has eight daughters and insufficient means to portion them for marriage. In a society where a woman's marriageability depended substantially on her dowry, this is a quiet catastrophe. Trevelyan's remark that "girls should not have fortunes" (001-195) is spoken from the comfortable position of a man who has already married a portionless girl — but the comment glosses over the structural problem that the other seven daughters face.

### The Heir of Property

The novel's secondary plots repeatedly involve questions of succession. Sir Peter Mancrudy, "the heir of property" (051-016), represents the landed-interest side of inheritance. The Gibson sisters are "regarded time out of mind as the property of" their social circle (077-033), a phrase that collapses the distinction between owning property and being defined by one's relation to it.

## Key Examples

### Disinheritance as Threat

The threat of disinheritance — "so-called disinheritance" (149-151) — functions as a recurring plot device. Characters fear being cut off from expected inheritances, and this fear constrains their choices. The fear that Dorothy "should rob him of his inheritance" (144-158) illustrates how inheritance expectations could poison family relationships before any death had occurred.

### Property as Identity

When a character declares "the property was mine" (058-074), the claim is not merely financial. Property in Trollope's world is identity: it determines social standing, marriageability, and personal autonomy. The novel's attention to wills, settlements, and portions reflects a society in which legal title to property was the primary determinant of individual freedom.
