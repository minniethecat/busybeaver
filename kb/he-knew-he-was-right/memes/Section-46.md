## Section 46

[046-001]Yours affectionately,

[046-002]LOUIS TREVELYAN.

[046-003]When he had finished this he read it twice, and believed that he had

[046-004]written, if not an affectionate, at any rate a considerate letter.

[046-005]He had no bounds to the pity which he felt for himself in reference

[046-006]to the injury which was being done to him, and he thought that the

[046-007]offers which he was making, both in respect to his child and the

[046-008]money, were such as to entitle him to his wife's warmest gratitude.

[046-009]He hardly recognised the force of the language which he used when he

[046-010]told her that her conduct was disgraceful, and that she had disgraced

[046-011]his name. He was quite unable to look at the whole question between

[046-012]him and his wife from her point of view. He conceived it possible

[046-013]that such a woman as his wife should be told that her conduct would

[046-014]be watched, and that she should be threatened with the Divorce Court,

[046-015]with an effect that should, upon the whole, be salutary. There

[046-016]be men, and not bad men either, and men neither uneducated, or

[046-017]unintelligent, or irrational in ordinary matters, who seem to be

[046-018]absolutely unfitted by nature to have the custody or guardianship of

[046-019]others. A woman in the hands of such a man can hardly save herself or

[046-020]him from endless trouble. It may be that between such a one and his

[046-021]wife, events shall flow on so evenly that no ruling, no constraint

[046-022]is necessary,--that even the giving of advice is never called for

[046-023]by the circumstances of the day. If the man be happily forced to

[046-024]labour daily for his living till he be weary, and the wife be laden

[046-025]with many ordinary cares, the routine of life may run on without

[046-026]storms;--but for such a one, if he be without work, the management

[046-027]of a wife will be a task full of peril. The lesson may be learned at

[046-028]last; he may after years come to perceive how much and how little of

[046-029]guidance the partner of his life requires at his hands; and he may be

[046-030]taught how that guidance should be given;--but in the learning of the

[046-031]lesson there will be sorrow and gnashing of teeth. It was so now with

[046-032]this man. He loved his wife. To a certain extent he still trusted

[046-033]her. He did not believe that she would be faithless to him after the

[046-034]fashion of women who are faithless altogether. But he was jealous of

[046-035]authority, fearful of slights, self-conscious, afraid of the world,

[046-036]and utterly ignorant of the nature of a woman's mind.

[046-037]He carried the letter with him in his pocket throughout the next

[046-038]morning, and in the course of the day he called upon Lady Milborough.

[046-039]Though he was obstinately bent on acting in accordance with his own

[046-040]views, yet he was morbidly desirous of discussing the grievousness of

[046-041]his position with his friends. He went to Lady Milborough, asking for

[046-042]her advice, but desirous simply of being encouraged by her to do that

[046-043]which he was resolved to do on his own judgment.

[046-044]"Down,--after her,--to [[nuncombe-putney|Nuncombe Putney]]!" said Lady Milborough,

[046-045]holding up both her hands.

[046-046]"Yes; he has been there. And she has been weak enough to see him."

[046-047]"My dear Louis, take her to Naples at once,--at once."

[046-048]"It is too late for that now, Lady Milborough."

[046-049]"Too late! Oh, no. She has been foolish, indiscreet,

[046-050]disobedient,--what you will of that kind. But, Louis, don't send her

[046-051]away; don't send your young wife away from you. Those whom God has

[046-052]joined together, let no man put asunder."

[046-053]"I cannot consent to live with a wife with whom neither my wishes

[046-054]nor my word have the slightest effect. I may believe of her what I

[046-055]please, but, think what the world will believe! I cannot disgrace

[046-056]myself by living with a woman who persists in holding intercourse

[046-057]with a man whom the world speaks of as her lover."

[046-058]"Take her to Naples," said Lady Milborough, with all the energy of

[046-059]which she was capable.

[046-060]"I can take her nowhere, nor will I see her, till she has given [[truth|proof]]

[046-061]that her whole conduct towards me has been altered. I have written a

[046-062]letter to her, and I have brought it. Will you excuse me if I ask you

[046-063]to take the trouble to read it?"

[046-064]Then he handed Lady Milborough the letter, which she read very

[046-065]slowly, and with much care.

[046-066]"I don't think I would--would--would--"

[046-067]"Would what?" demanded Trevelyan.

[046-068]"Don't you think that what you say is a little,--just a little prone

[046-069]to make,--to make the breach perhaps wider?"

[046-070]"No, Lady Milborough. In the first place, how can it be wider?"

[046-071]"You might take her back, you know; and then if you could only get to

[046-072]Naples!"

[046-073]"How can I take her back while she is corresponding with this man?"

[046-074]"She wouldn't correspond with him at Naples."

[046-075]Trevelyan shook his head and became cross. His old friend would not

[046-076]at all do as old friends are expected to do when called upon for

[046-077]advice.

[046-078]"I think," said he, "that what I have proposed is both just and

[046-079]generous."

[046-080]"But, Louis, why should there be any separation?"

[046-081]"She has forced it upon me. She is headstrong, and will not be

[046-082]ruled."

[046-083]"But this about disgracing you. Do you think that you must say that?"

[046-084]"I think I must, because it is true. If I do not tell her the truth,

[046-085]who is there that will do so? It may be bitter now, but I think that

[046-086]it is for her welfare."

[046-087]"Dear, dear, dear!"

[046-088]"I want nothing for myself, Lady Milborough."

[046-089]"I am sure of that, Louis."

[046-090]"My whole happiness was in my home. No man cared less for going out

[046-091]than I did. My child and my wife were everything to me. I don't

[046-092]suppose that I was ever seen at a club in the evening once throughout

[046-093]a season. And she might have had anything that she liked,--anything!

[046-094]It is hard, Lady Milborough; is it not?"

[046-095]Lady Milborough, who had seen the angry brow, did not dare to suggest

[046-096]Naples again. But yet, if any word might be spoken to prevent this

[046-097]utter wreck of a home, how good a thing it would be! He had got up to

[046-098]leave her, but she stopped him by holding his hand. "For better, for

[046-099]worse, Louis; remember that."

[046-100]"Why has she forgotten it?"

[046-101]"She is flesh of your flesh, bone of your bone. And for the boy's

[046-102]sake! Think of your boy, Louis. Do not send that letter. Sleep on it,

[046-103]Louis, and think of it."

[046-104]"I have slept on it."

[046-105]"There is no promise in it of forgiveness after a while. It is

[046-106]written as though you intended that she should never come back to

[046-107]you."

[046-108]"That shall be as she behaves herself."

[046-109]"But tell her so. Let there be some one bright spot in what you say

[046-110]to her, on which her mind may fix itself. If she be not altogether

[046-111]hardened, that letter will drive her to despair."

[046-112]But Trevelyan would not give up the letter, nor indicate by a word

[046-113]that he would reconsider the question of its [[social-custom|propriety]]. He escaped as

[046-114]soon as he could from Lady Milborough's room, and almost declared as

[046-115]he did so, that he would never enter her doors again. She had utterly

[046-116]failed to see the matter in the proper light. When she talked of

[046-117]Naples she must surely have been unable to comprehend the extent

[046-118]of the ill-usage to which he, the husband, had been subjected. How

[046-119]was it possible that he should live under the same roof with a wife

[046-120]who claimed to herself the right of receiving visitors of whom he

[046-121]disapproved,--a visitor,--a [[social-class|gentleman]],--one whom the world called her

[046-122]lover? He gnashed his teeth and clenched his fist as he thought of

[046-123]his old friend's ignorance of the very first law in a married man's

[046-124]code of laws.

[046-125]But yet when he was out in the streets he did not post his letter at

[046-126]once; but thought of it throughout the whole day, trying to prove

[046-127]the weight of every phrase that he had used. Once or twice his heart

[046-128]almost relented. Once he had the letter in his hand, that he might

[046-129]tear it. But he did not tear it. He put it back into his pocket, and

[046-130]thought again of his grievance. Surely it was his first duty in such

[046-131]an emergency to be firm!

[046-132]It was certainly a wretched life that he was leading. In the evening

[046-133]he went all alone to an eating-house for his dinner, and then,

[046-134]sitting with a miserable glass of sherry before him, he again read

[046-135]and re-read the epistle which he had written. Every harsh word that

[046-136]it contained was, in some sort, pleasant to his ear. She had hit

[046-137]him hard, and should he not hit her again? And then, was it not his

[046-138]bounden duty to let her know the truth? Yes; it was his duty to be

[046-139]firm.

[046-140]So he went out and posted the letter.

[046-141]CHAPTER XXVIII.

[046-142]GREAT TRIBULATION.

[046-143][Illustration]

[046-144]Trevelyan's letter to his wife fell like a thunderbolt among them at

[046-145]Nuncombe Putney. Mrs. Trevelyan was altogether unable to keep it to

[046-146]herself;--indeed she made no attempt at doing so. Her husband had

[046-147]told her that she was to be banished from the [[clock-house|Clock House]] because her

[046-148]present hostess was unable to endure her misconduct, and of course

[046-149]she demanded the reasons of the charge that was thus brought against

[046-150]her. When she first read the letter, which she did in the presence of

[046-151]her sister, she towered in her passion.

[046-152]"Disgraced him! I have never disgraced him. It is he that has

[046-153]disgraced me. Correspondence! Yes;--he shall see it all. Unjust,

[046-154]ignorant, foolish man! He does not remember that the last

[046-155]instructions he really gave me, were to bid me see [[colonel-osborne|Colonel Osborne]].

[046-156]Take my boy away! Yes. Of course, I am a woman and must suffer. I

[046-157]will write to Colonel Osborne, and will tell him the truth, and will

[046-158]send my letter to Louis. He shall know how he has ill-treated me! I

[046-159]will not take a penny of his money;--not a penny. Maintain you! I

[046-160]believe he thinks that we are beggars. Leave this house because of my

[046-161]conduct! What can Mrs. Stanbury have said? What can any of them have

[046-162]said? I will demand to be told. Free himself from the connection!

[046-163]Oh, Nora, Nora! that it should come to this!--that I should be thus

[046-164]threatened, who have been as innocent as a baby! If it were not for

[046-165]my child, I think that I should destroy myself!"

[046-166]Nora said what she could to comfort her sister, insisting chiefly on

[046-167]the promise that the child should not be taken away. There was no

[046-168]doubt as to the husband's power in the mind of either of them; and

[046-169]though, as regarded herself, Mrs. Trevelyan would have defied her

[046-170]husband, let his power be what it might, yet she acknowledged to

[046-171]herself that she was in some degree restrained by the fear that she

[046-172]would find herself deprived of her only comfort.

[046-173]"We must just go where he bids us,--till papa comes," said Nora.

[046-174]"And when papa is here, what help will there be then? He will not let

[046-175]me go back to the islands,--with my boy. For myself I might die, or

[046-176]get out of his way anywhere. I can see that. Priscilla Stanbury is

[046-177]right when she says that no woman should trust herself to any man.

[046-178]Disgraced! That I should live to be told by my husband that I had

[046-179]disgraced him,--by a lover!"

[046-180]There was some sort of agreement made between the two sisters as to

[046-181]the manner in which Priscilla should be interrogated respecting the

[046-182]sentence of banishment which had been passed. They both agreed that

[046-183]it would be useless to make inquiry of Mrs. Stanbury. If anything had

[046-184]really been said to justify the statement made in Mr. Trevelyan's

[046-185]letter, it must have come from Priscilla, and have reached Trevelyan

[046-186]through Priscilla's brother. They, both of them, had sufficiently

[046-187]learned the ways of the house to be sure that Mrs. Stanbury had not

[046-188]been the person active in the matter. They went down, therefore,

[046-189]together, and found Priscilla seated at her desk in the parlour.

[046-190]Mrs. Stanbury was also in the room, and it had been presumed between

[046-191]the sisters that the interrogations should be made in that lady's

[046-192]absence; but Mrs. Trevelyan was too hot in the matter for restraint,

[046-193]and she at once opened out her budget of grievance.
