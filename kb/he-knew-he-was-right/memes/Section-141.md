## Section 141

[141-001]Poor Mr. Glascock was afraid to decline to sit down to the meal which

[141-002]was prepared for him. He did mutter something about having already

[141-003]eaten, but Trevelyan put this aside with a wave of his hand as he led

[141-004]the way into a spacious room, in which had been set out a table with

[141-005]almost a sumptuous banquet. The room was very bare and comfortless,

[141-006]having neither curtains nor matting, and containing not above half

[141-007]a dozen chairs. But an effort had been made to give it an air of

[141-008]Italian luxury. The windows were thrown open, down to the ground, and

[141-009]the table was decorated with fruits and three or four long-necked

[141-010]bottles. Trevelyan waved with his hand towards an arm-chair, and Mr.

[141-011]Glascock had no alternative but to seat himself. He felt that he was

[141-012]sitting down to breakfast with a madman; but if he did not sit down,

[141-013]the madman might perhaps break out into madness. Then Trevelyan went

[141-014]to the door and called aloud for Catarina. "In these remote places,"

[141-015]said he, "one has to do without the civilisation of a bell. Perhaps

[141-016]one gains as much in quiet as one loses in comfort." Then Catarina

[141-017]came with hot meats and fried potatoes, and Mr. Glascock was

[141-018]compelled to help himself.

[141-019]"I am but a bad trencherman myself," said Trevelyan, "but I shall

[141-020]lament my misfortune doubly if that should interfere with your

[141-021]appetite." Then he got up and poured out wine into Mr. Glascock's

[141-022]glass. "They tell me that it comes from the Baron's vineyard," said

[141-023]Trevelyan, alluding to the wine-farm of Ricasoli, "and that there is

[141-024]none better in Tuscany. I never was myself a judge of the grape, but

[141-025]this to me is as palatable as any of the costlier French wines. How

[141-026]grand a thing would wine really be, if it could make glad the heart

[141-027]of man. How truly would one worship Bacchus if he could make one's

[141-028]heart to rejoice. But if a man have a real sorrow, wine will not wash

[141-029]it away,--not though a man were drowned in it, as Clarence was."

[141-030]Mr. Glascock hitherto had spoken hardly a word. There was an attempt

[141-031]at joviality about this breakfast,--or, at any rate, of the usual

[141-032]comfortable luxury of hospitable entertainment,--which, coming as it

[141-033]did from Trevelyan, almost locked his lips. He had not come there to

[141-034]be jovial or luxurious, but to perform a most melancholy mission; and

[141-035]he had brought with him his saddest looks, and was prepared for a

[141-036]few sad words. Trevelyan's speech, indeed, was sad enough, but Mr.

[141-037]Glascock could not take up questions of the worship of Bacchus at

[141-038]half a minute's warning. He eat a morsel, and raised his glass to his

[141-039]lips, and felt himself to be very uncomfortable. It was necessary,

[141-040]however, that he should utter a word. "Do you not let your little boy

[141-041]come in to breakfast?" he said.

[141-042]"He is better away," said Trevelyan gloomily.

[141-043]"But as we are to travel together," said Mr. Glascock, "we might as

[141-044]well make acquaintance."

[141-045]"You have been a little hurried with me on that score," said

[141-046]Trevelyan. "I wrote certainly with a determined mind, but things have

[141-047]changed somewhat since then."

[141-048]"You do not mean that you will not send him?"

[141-049]"You have been somewhat hurried with me, I say. If I remember

[141-050]rightly, I named no time, but spoke of the future. Could I have

[141-051]answered the message which I received from you, I would have

[141-052]postponed your visit for a week or so."

[141-053]"Postponed it! Why,--I am to be married the day after to-morrow.

[141-054]It was just as much as I was able to do, to come here at all." Mr.

[141-055]Glascock now pushed his chair back from the table, and prepared

[141-056]himself to speak up. "Your wife expects her child now, and you will

[141-057]break her heart by refusing to send him."

[141-058]"Nobody thinks of my heart, Mr. Glascock."

[141-059]"But this is your own offer."

[141-060]"Yes, it was my own offer, certainly. I am not going to deny my own

[141-061]words, which have no doubt been preserved in testimony against me."

[141-062]"Mr. Trevelyan, what do you mean?" Then, when he was on the point of

[141-063]boiling over with passion, Mr. Glascock remembered that his companion

[141-064]was not responsible for his expressions. "I do hope you will let

[141-065]the child go away with me," he said. "You cannot conceive the state

[141-066]of his mother's anxiety, and she will send him back at once if you

[141-067]demand it."

[141-068]"Is that to be in good faith?"

[141-069]"Certainly, in good faith. I would lend myself to nothing, Mr.

[141-070]Trevelyan, that was not said and done in good faith."

[141-071]"She will not break her word, excusing herself, because I am--mad?"

[141-072]"I am sure that there is nothing of the kind in her mind."

[141-073]"Perhaps not now; but such things grow. There is no iniquity, no

[141-074]breach of promise, no treason that a woman will not excuse to

[141-075]herself,--or a man either,--by the comfortable self-assurance that

[141-076]the person to be injured is--mad. A hound without a friend is not so

[141-077]cruelly treated. The outlaw, the murderer, the perjurer has surer

[141-078]privileges than the man who is in the way, and to whom his friends

[141-079]can point as being--mad!" Mr. Glascock knew or thought that he knew

[141-080]that his host in truth was mad, and he could not, therefore, answer

[141-081]this tirade by an assurance that no such idea was likely to prevail.

[141-082]"Have they told you, I wonder," continued Trevelyan, "how it was

[141-083]that, driven to force and an ambuscade for the recovery of my own

[141-084]child, I waylaid my wife and took him from her? I have done nothing

[141-085]to forfeit my right as a man to the control of my own family. I

[141-086]demanded that the boy should be sent to me, and she paid no attention

[141-087]to my words. I was compelled to vindicate my own authority; and then,

[141-088]because I claimed the right which belongs to a father, they said that

[141-089]I was--mad! Ay, and they would have proved it, too, had I not fled

[141-090]from my country and hidden myself in this desert. Think of that, Mr.

[141-091]Glascock! Now they have followed me here,--not out of love for me;

[141-092]and that man whom they call a governor comes and insults me; and my

[141-093]wife promises to be good to me, and says that she will forgive and

[141-094]forget! Can she ever forgive herself her own folly, and the cruelty

[141-095]that has made shipwreck of my life? They can do nothing to me here;

[141-096]but they would entice me home because there they have friends, and

[141-097]can fee doctors,--with my own money,--and suborn lawyers, and put me

[141-098]away,--somewhere in the dark, where I shall be no more heard of among

[141-099]men! As you are a man of honour, Mr. Glascock,--tell me; is it not

[141-100]so?"

[141-101]"I know nothing of their plans,--beyond this, that you wrote me word

[141-102]that you would send them the boy."

[141-103]"But I know their plans. What you say is true. I did write you

[141-104]word,--and I meant it. Mr. Glascock, sitting here alone from

[141-105]morning to night, and lying down from night till morning, without

[141-106]companionship, without love, in utter misery, I taught myself to feel

[141-107]that I should think more of her than of myself."

[141-108]"If you are so unhappy here, come back yourself with the child. Your

[141-109]wife would desire nothing better."

[141-110]"Yes;--and submit to her, and her father, and her mother. No,--Mr.

[141-111]Glascock; never, never. Let her come to me."

[141-112]"But you will not receive her."

[141-113]"Let her come in a proper spirit, and I will receive her. She is the

[141-114]wife of my bosom, and I will receive her with joy. But if she is to

[141-115]come to me and tell me that she forgives me,--forgives me for the

[141-116]evil that she has done,--then, sir, she had better stay away. Mr.

[141-117]Glascock, you are going to be married. Believe me,--no man should

[141-118]submit to be forgiven by his wife. Everything must go astray if that

[141-119]be done. I would rather encounter their mad doctors, one of them

[141-120]after another till they had made me mad;--I would encounter anything

[141-121]rather than that. But, sir, you neither eat nor drink, and I fear

[141-122]that my speech disturbs you."

[141-123]It was like enough that it may have done so. Trevelyan, as he had

[141-124]been speaking, had walked about the room, going from one extremity to

[141-125]the other with hurried steps, gesticulating with his arms, and every

[141-126]now and then pushing back with his hands the long hair from off his

[141-127]forehead. Mr. Glascock was in truth very much disturbed. He had come

[141-128]there with an express object; but, whenever he mentioned the child,

[141-129]the father became almost rabid in his wrath. "I have done very well,

[141-130]thank you," said Mr. Glascock. "I will not eat any more, and I

[141-131]believe I must be thinking of going back to Siena."

[141-132]"I had hoped you would spend the day with me, Mr. Glascock."

[141-133]"I am to be married, you see, in two days; and I must be in Florence

[141-134]early to-morrow. I am to meet my--wife, as she will be, and the

[141-135]Rowleys, and your wife. Upon my word I can't stay. Won't you just say

[141-136]a word to the young woman and let the boy be got ready?"

[141-137]"I think not;--no, I think not."

[141-138]"And am I to have had all this journey for nothing? You will have

[141-139]made a fool of me in writing to me."

[141-140]"I intended to be honest, Mr. Glascock."

[141-141]"Stick to your honesty, and send the boy back to his mother. It will

[141-142]be better for you, Trevelyan."

[141-143]"Better for me! Nothing can be better for me. All must be worst. It

[141-144]will be better for me, you say; and you ask me to give up the last

[141-145]drop of cold water wherewith I can touch my parched lips. Even in my

[141-146]hell I had so much left to me of a limpid stream, and you tell me

[141-147]that it will be better for me to pour it away. You may take him, Mr.

[141-148]Glascock. The woman will make him ready for you. What matters it

[141-149]whether the fiery furnace be heated seven times, or only six;--in

[141-150]either degree the flames are enough! You may take him;--you may take

[141-151]him." So saying, Trevelyan walked out of the window, leaving Mr.

[141-152]Glascock seated in his chair. He walked out of the window and went

[141-153]down among the olive trees. He did not go far, however, but stood

[141-154]with his arm round the stem of one of them, playing with the shoots

[141-155]of a vine with his hand. Mr. Glascock followed him to the window and

[141-156]stood looking at him for a few moments. But Trevelyan did not turn

[141-157]or move. There he stood gazing at the pale, cloudless, heat-laden,

[141-158]motionless sky, thinking of his own sorrows, and remembering too,

[141-159]doubtless, with the vanity of a madman, that he was probably being

[141-160]watched in his reverie.

[141-161]Mr. Glascock was too practical a man not to make the most of the

[141-162]offer that had been made to him, and he went back among the passages

[141-163]and called for Catarina. Before long he had two or three women with

[141-164]him, including her whom he had brought from Florence, and among them

[141-165]Louey was soon made to appear, dressed for his journey, together with

[141-166]a small trunk in which were his garments. It was quite clear that

[141-167]the order for his departure had been given before that scene at the

[141-168]breakfast-table, and that Trevelyan had not intended to go back from

[141-169]his promise. Nevertheless Mr. Glascock thought it might be as well to

[141-170]hurry his departure, and he turned back to say the shortest possible

[141-171]word of farewell to Trevelyan in the garden. But when he got to the

[141-172]window, Trevelyan was not to be found among the olive trees. Mr.

[141-173]Glascock walked a few steps down the hill, looking for him, but

[141-174]seeing nothing of him, returned to the house. The elder woman said

[141-175]that her master had not been there, and Mr. Glascock started with his

[141-176]charge. Trevelyan was manifestly mad, and it was impossible to treat

[141-177]him as a sane man would have been treated. Nevertheless, Mr. Glascock

[141-178]felt much compunction in carrying the child away without a final kiss

[141-179]or word of farewell from its father. But it was not to be so. He

[141-180]had got into the carriage with the child, having the servant seated

[141-181]opposite to him,--for he was moved by some undefinable fear which

[141-182]made him determine to keep the boy close to him, and he had not,

[141-183]therefore, returned to the driver's seat,--when Trevelyan appeared

[141-184][[reputation|standing]] by the road-side at the bottom of the hill. "Would you take

[141-185]him away from me without one word!" said Trevelyan bitterly.
