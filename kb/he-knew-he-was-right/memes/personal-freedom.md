# Personal Freedom

The contested ideal of personal autonomy in *He Knew He Was Right*, explored through characters' struggles to assert individual will against the claims of marriage, custom, and social obligation.

## Overview

Personal freedom in Trollope's novel is never absolute but always negotiated within relationships and social structures. Emily Trevelyan's insistence on choosing her own social circle is a claim to personal freedom that her husband interprets as insubordination. Hugh Stanbury's choice of journalism over a conventional profession is a claim to professional freedom that his aunt interprets as social degradation. The novel tracks these competing claims, suggesting that freedom in Victorian society is always constrained — but that some constraints are more legitimate than others (003-084).

## Context in Victorian Society

Victorian liberalism elevated individual freedom as a political ideal, but this freedom was unevenly distributed. Married women, under the doctrine of coverture, had severely limited legal autonomy. Working-class people had little economic freedom. The novel registers these asymmetries: Emily's freedom is constrained by marriage law, Nora's by economic dependency, while Trevelyan enjoys the freedom of a wealthy gentleman — a freedom he uses to tyrannize his wife.

## Manifestation in the Novel

### Freedom and Gender

The novel's most pointed critique concerns the gendered distribution of freedom. Trevelyan demands freedom of action for himself — the right to dictate his wife's social circle, to remove his child, to exile himself — while denying Emily the freedom to make even the most ordinary social choices. The novel exposes this asymmetry without resolving it.

### Professional Freedom

Hugh Stanbury's journalism represents a positive model of freedom: the freedom to choose meaningful work, to support oneself honestly, and to live according to one's own values. His professional freedom is earned through competence and integrity, not claimed as a birthright.

## Key Examples

### Emily's Stand

Emily's refusal to end her friendship with Colonel Osborne is the novel's central act of personal freedom. She is not asserting a right to infidelity — she is asserting a right to choose her own associates, to be trusted, and to be treated as a moral agent rather than a possession. The novel supports her claim even as it shows its costs.

### Nora's Choice

Nora Rowley's decision to marry Hugh Stanbury despite his uncertain financial prospects is an exercise of personal freedom in the romantic domain. She chooses character over security, and the novel endorses her choice.
