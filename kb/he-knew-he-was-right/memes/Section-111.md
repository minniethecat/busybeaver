## Section 111

[111-001]"Why should she not take him home with her?" said Lady Rowley.

[111-002]"Because I will not have it so," replied Trevelyan. "Because I choose

[111-003]that it should be understood that I am to be the master of my own

[111-004]affairs."

[111-005]Mrs. Trevelyan had now thrown aside her bonnet and her veil, and was

[111-006]covering her child with caresses. The poor little fellow, whose mind

[111-007]had been utterly dismayed by the events which had occurred to him

[111-008]since his capture, though he returned her kisses, did so in fear

[111-009]and trembling. And he was still sobbing, rubbing his eyes with his

[111-010]knuckles, and by no means yielding himself with his whole heart to

[111-011]his mother's tenderness,--as she would have had him do. "Louey,"

[111-012]she said, whispering to him, "you know mamma; you haven't forgotten

[111-013]mamma?" He half murmured some little infantine word through his sobs,

[111-014]and then put his cheek up to be pressed against his mother's face.

[111-015]"Louey will never, never forget his own mamma; will he, Louey?" The

[111-016]poor boy had no assurances to give, and could only raise his cheek

[111-017]again to be kissed. In the meantime Lady Rowley and Trevelyan were

[111-018][[reputation|standing]] by, not speaking to each other, regarding the scene in

[111-019]silence.

[111-020][Illustration: "You haven't forgotten Mamma?"]

[111-021]She,--Lady Rowley,--could see that he was frightfully altered in

[111-022]appearance, even since the day on which she had so lately met him

[111-023]in the City. His cheeks were thin and haggard, and his eyes were

[111-024]deep and very bright,--and he moved them quickly from side to side,

[111-025]as though ever suspecting something. He seemed to be smaller in

[111-026]stature,--withered, as it were, as though he had melted away. And

[111-027]though he stood looking upon his wife and child, he was not for a

[111-028]moment still. He would change the posture of his hands and arms,

[111-029]moving them quickly with little surreptitious jerks, and would

[111-030]shuffle his feet upon the floor, almost without altering his

[111-031]position. His clothes hung about him, and his linen was soiled and

[111-032]worn. Lady Rowley noticed this especially, as he had been a man

[111-033]peculiarly given to neatness of apparel. He was the first to speak.

[111-034]"You have come down here in a cab?" said he.

[111-035]"Yes,--in a cab, from [[london|London]]," said Lady Rowley.

[111-036]"Of course you will go back in it? You cannot stay here. There is no

[111-037]accommodation. It is a wretched place, but it suits the boy. As for

[111-038]me, all places are now alike."

[111-039]"Louis," said his wife, springing up from her knees, coming to him,

[111-040]and taking his right hand between both her own, "you will let me take

[111-041]him with me. I know you will let me take him with me."

[111-042]"I cannot do that, Emily; it would be wrong."

[111-043]"Wrong to restore a child to his mother? Oh, Louis, think of it. What

[111-044]must my life be without him,--or you?"

[111-045]"Don't talk of me. It is too late for that."

[111-046]"Not if you will be reasonable, Louis, and listen to me. Oh, heavens,

[111-047]how ill you are!" As she said this she drew nearer to him, so that

[111-048]her face was almost close to his. "Louis, come back; come back, and

[111-049]let it all be forgotten. It shall be a dream, a horrid dream, and

[111-050]nobody shall speak of it." He left his hand within hers and stood

[111-051]looking into her face. He was well aware that his life since he had

[111-052]left her had been one long hour of misery. There had been to him no

[111-053]alleviation, no comfort, no consolation. He had not a friend left to

[111-054]him. Even his satellite, the policeman, was becoming weary of him and

[111-055]manifestly suspicious. The woman with whom he was now lodging, and

[111-056]whose resources were infinitely benefited by his payments to her, had

[111-057]already thrown out hints that she was afraid of him. And as he looked

[111-058]at his wife, he knew that he loved her. Everything for him now was

[111-059]hot and dry and poor and bitter. How sweet would it be again to sit

[111-060]with her soft hand in his, to feel her cool brow against his own, to

[111-061]have the comfort of her care, and to hear the music of loving words!

[111-062]The companionship of his wife had once been to him everything in the

[111-063]world; but now, for many months past, he had known no companion. She

[111-064]bade him come to her, and look upon all this trouble as a dream not

[111-065]to be mentioned. Could it be possible that it should be so, and that

[111-066]they might yet be happy together,--perhaps in some distant country,

[111-067]where the story of all their misery might not be known? He felt all

[111-068]this truly and with a keen accuracy. If he were mad, he was not all

[111-069]mad. "I will tell you of nothing that is past," said she, hanging to

[111-070]him, and coming still nearer to him, and embracing his arm.

[111-071]Could she have condescended to ask him not to tell her of the

[111-072]past;--had it occurred to her so to word her request,--she might

[111-073]perhaps have prevailed. But who can say how long the tenderness of

[111-074]his heart would have saved him from further outbreak;--and whether

[111-075]such prevailing on her part would have been of permanent service? As

[111-076]it was, her words wounded him in that spot of his inner self which

[111-077]was most sensitive,--on that spot from whence had come all his fury.

[111-078]A black cloud came upon his brow, and he made an effort to withdraw

[111-079]himself from her grasp. It was necessary to him that she should in

[111-080]some fashion own that he had been right, and now she was promising

[111-081]him that she would not tell him of his fault! He could not thus

[111-082]swallow down all the convictions by which he had fortified himself to

[111-083]bear the misfortunes which he had endured. Had he not quarrelled with

[111-084]every friend he possessed on this score; and should he now stultify

[111-085]himself in all those quarrels by admitting that he had been cruel,

[111-086]unjust, and needlessly jealous? And did not truth demand of him that

[111-087]he should cling to his old assurances? Had she not been disobedient,

[111-088]ill-conditioned, and rebellious? Had she not received the man, both

[111-089]him personally and his letters, after he had explained to her that

[111-090]his honour demanded that it should not be so? How could he come into

[111-091]such terms as those now proposed to him, simply because he longed

[111-092]to enjoy the rich sweetness of her soft hand, to feel the fragrance

[111-093]of her breath, and to quench the heat of his forehead in the cool

[111-094]atmosphere of her beauty? "Why have you driven me to this by your

[111-095]intercourse with that man?" he said. "Why, why, why did you do it?"

[111-096]She was still clinging to him. "Louis," she said, "I am your wife."

[111-097]"Yes; you are my wife."

[111-098]"And will you still believe such evil of me without any cause?"

[111-099]"There has been cause,--horrible cause. You must

[111-100]repent,--repent,--repent."

[111-101]"Heaven help me," said the woman, falling back from him, and

[111-102]returning to the boy who was now seated in Lady Rowley's lap. "Mamma,

[111-103]do you speak to him. What can I say? Would he think better of me were

[111-104]I to own myself to have been guilty, when there has been no guilt, no

[111-105]slightest fault? Does he wish me to purchase my child by saying that

[111-106]I am not fit to be his mother?"

[111-107]"Louis," said Lady Rowley, "if any man was ever wrong, mad, madly

[111-108]mistaken, you are so now."

[111-109]"Have you come out here to accuse me again, as you did before in

[111-110]London?" he asked. "Is that the way in which you and she intend to

[111-111]let the past be, as she says, like a dream? She tells me that I am

[111-112]ill. It is true. I am ill,--and she is killing me, killing me, by her

[111-113]obstinacy."

[111-114]"What would you have me do?" said the wife, again rising from her

[111-115]child.

[111-116]"Acknowledge your transgressions, and say that you will amend your

[111-117]conduct for the future."

[111-118]"Mamma, mamma,--what shall I say to him?"

[111-119]"Who can speak to a man that is beside himself?" replied Lady Rowley.

[111-120]"I am not so beside myself as yet, Lady Rowley, but that I know how

[111-121]to guard my own honour and to protect my own child. I have told you,

[111-122]Emily, the terms on which you can come back to me. You had better now

[111-123]return to your mother's house; and if you wish again to have a house

[111-124]of your own, and your husband, and your boy, you know by what means

[111-125]you may acquire them. For another week I shall remain here;--after

[111-126]that I shall remove far from hence."

[111-127]"And where will you go, Louis?"

[111-128]"As yet I know not. To Italy I think,--or perhaps to America. It

[111-129]matters little where for me."

[111-130]"And will Louey be taken with you?"

[111-131]"Certainly he will go with me. To strive to bring him up so that he

[111-132]may be a happier man than his father is all that there is now left

[111-133]for me in life." Mrs. Trevelyan had now got the boy in her arms, and

[111-134]her mother was seated by her on the sofa. Trevelyan was [[reputation|standing]] away

[111-135]from them, but so near the door that no sudden motion on their part

[111-136]would enable them to escape with the boy without his interposition.

[111-137]It now again occurred to the mother to carry off her prize in

[111-138]opposition to her husband;--but she had no scheme to that effect laid

[111-139]with her mother, and she could not reconcile herself to the idea of

[111-140]a contest with him in which personal violence would be necessary.

[111-141]The woman of the house had, indeed, seemed to sympathise with her,

[111-142]but she could not dare in such a matter to trust to assistance from a

[111-143]stranger. "I do not wish to be uncourteous," said Trevelyan, "but if

[111-144]you have no assurance to give me, you had better--leave me."

[111-145]Then there came to be a bargaining about time, and the poor woman

[111-146]begged almost on her knees that she might be allowed to take her

[111-147]child up-stairs and be with him alone for a few minutes. It seemed

[111-148]to her that she had not seen her boy till she had had him to herself,

[111-149]in absolute privacy, till she had kissed his limbs, and had her hand

[111-150]upon his smooth back, and seen that he was white and clean and bright

[111-151]as he had ever been. And the bargain was made. She was asked to

[111-152]pledge her word that she would not take him out of the house,--and

[111-153]she pledged her word, feeling that there was no strength in her for

[111-154]that action which she had meditated. He, knowing that he might still

[111-155]guard the passage at the bottom of the stairs, allowed her to go with

[111-156]the boy to his bedroom, while he remained below with Lady Rowley. A

[111-157]quarter of an hour was allowed to her, and she humbly promised that

[111-158]she would return when that time was expired.

[111-159]Trevelyan held the door open for her as she went, and kept it open

[111-160]during her absence. There was hardly a word said between him and Lady

[111-161]Rowley, but he paced from the passage into the room and from the room

[111-162]into the passage with his hands behind his back. "It is cruel," he

[111-163]said once. "It is very cruel."

[111-164]"It is you that are cruel," said Lady Rowley.

[111-165]"Of course;--of course. That is natural from you. I expect that from

[111-166]you." To this she made no answer, and he did not open his lips again.

[111-167]After a while Mrs. Trevelyan called to her mother, and Lady Rowley

[111-168]was allowed to go up-stairs. The quarter of an hour was of course

[111-169]greatly stretched, and all the time Trevelyan continued to pace in

[111-170]and out of the room. He was patient, for he did not summon them; but

[111-171]went on pacing backwards and forwards, looking now and again to see

[111-172]that the cab was at its place,--that no deceit was being attempted,

[111-173]no second act of kidnapping being perpetrated. At last the two ladies

[111-174]came down the stairs, and the boy was with them,--and the woman of

[111-175]the house.
