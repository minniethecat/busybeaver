## Section 97

[097-001]Both Priscilla and Mrs. Stanbury read it, and for awhile they sat

[097-002]with the two letters among them without much speech about them.

[097-003]Mrs. Stanbury was endeavouring to make herself believe that her

[097-004]sister-in-law's opposition might be overcome, and that then Dorothy

[097-005]might be married. Priscilla was inquiring of herself whether it would

[097-006]be well that Dorothy should defy her aunt,--so much, at any rate,

[097-007]would be well,--and marry the man, even to his deprivation of the

[097-008]old woman's [[money|fortune]]. Priscilla had her doubts about this, being very

[097-009]strong in her ideas of self-denial. That her sister should put up

[097-010]with the bitterest disappointment rather than injure the man she

[097-011]loved was right;--but then it would also be so extremely right to

[097-012]defy Aunt Stanbury to her teeth! But Dorothy, in whose [[honour|character]] was

[097-013]mixed with her mother's softness much of the old Stanbury strength,

[097-014]had no doubt in her mind. It was very sweet to be so loved. What

[097-015]gratitude did she not owe to a man who was so true to her! What was

[097-016]she that she should stand in his way? To lay herself down that she

[097-017]might be crushed in his path was no more than she owed to him. Mrs.

[097-018]Stanbury was the first to speak.

[097-019]"I suppose he is a very good young man," she said.

[097-020]"I am sure he is;--a noble, true-hearted man," said Priscilla.

[097-021]"And why shouldn't he marry whom he pleases, as long as she is

[097-022]respectable?" said Mrs. Stanbury.

[097-023]"In some people's eyes poverty is more disreputable than vice," said

[097-024]Priscilla.

[097-025]"Your aunt has been so fond of Dorothy," pleaded Mrs. Stanbury.

[097-026]"Just as she is of her servants," said Priscilla.

[097-027]But Dorothy said nothing. Her heart was too full to enable her to

[097-028]defend her aunt; nor at the present moment was she strong enough to

[097-029]make her mother understand that no hope was to be entertained. In the

[097-030]course of the day she walked out with her sister on the road towards

[097-031]Ridleigh, and there, [[reputation|standing]] among the rocks and ferns, looking down

[097-032]upon the river, with the buzz of the little mill within her ears,

[097-033]she explained the feelings of her heart and her many thoughts with a

[097-034]flow of words stronger, as Priscilla thought, than she had ever used

[097-035]before.

[097-036]"It is not what he would suffer now, Pris, or what he would feel, but

[097-037]what he would feel ten, twenty years hence, when he would know that

[097-038]his children would have been all provided for, had he not lost his

[097-039]fortune by marrying me."

[097-040]"He must be the only judge whether he prefers you to the old woman's

[097-041]money," said Priscilla.

[097-042]"No, dear; not the only judge. And it isn't that, Pris,--not which

[097-043]he likes best now, but which it is best for him that he should have.

[097-044]What could I do for him?"

[097-045]"You can love him."

[097-046]"Yes;--I can do that." And Dorothy paused a moment, to think how

[097-047]exceedingly well she could do that one thing. "But what is that? As

[097-048]you said the other day, a dog can do that. I am not clever. I can't

[097-049]play, or talk French, or do things that men like their wives to do.

[097-050]And I have lived here all my life; and what am I, that for me he

[097-051]should lose a great fortune?"

[097-052]"That is his look out."

[097-053]"No, dearest;--it is mine, and I will look out. I shall be able, at

[097-054]any rate, to remember always that I have loved him, and have not

[097-055]injured him. He may be angry with me now,"--and there was a feeling

[097-056]of pride at her heart, as she thought that he would be angry with

[097-057]her, because she did not go to him,--"but he will know at last that I

[097-058]have been as good to him as I knew how to be."

[097-059]Then Priscilla wound her arms round Dorothy, and kissed her.

[097-060]"My sister," she said; "my own sister!" They walked on further,

[097-061]discussing the matter in all its bearings, talking of the act of

[097-062]self-denial which Dorothy was called on to perform, as though it were

[097-063]some abstract thing, the performance of which was, or perhaps was

[097-064]not, imperatively demanded by the laws which should govern humanity;

[097-065]but with no idea on the mind of either of them that there was any

[097-066]longer a doubt as to this special matter in hand. They were away

[097-067]from home over three hours; and, when they returned, Dorothy at once

[097-068]wrote her two letters. They were very simple, and very short. She

[097-069]told Brooke, whom she now addressed as "Dear Mr. Burgess," that

[097-070]it could not be as he would have it; and she told her aunt,--with

[097-071]some terse [[personal-freedom|independence]] of expression, which Miss Stanbury quite

[097-072]understood,--that she had considered the matter, and had thought it

[097-073]right to refuse Mr. Burgess's offer.

[097-074]"Don't you think she is very much changed?" said Mrs. Stanbury to her

[097-075]eldest daughter.

[097-076]"Not changed in the least, mother; but the sun has opened the bud,

[097-077]and now we see the fruit."

[097-078]CHAPTER LIX.

[097-079]MR. BOZZLE AT HOME.

[097-080][Illustration]

[097-081]It had now come to pass that Trevelyan had not a friend in the world

[097-082]to whom he could apply in the matter of his wife and family. In the

[097-083]last communication which he had received from Lady Milborough she

[097-084]had scolded him, in terms that were for her severe, because he had

[097-085]not returned to his wife and taken her off with him to Naples. Mr.

[097-086]Bideawhile had found himself obliged to decline to move in the matter

[097-087]at all. With [[hugh-stanbury|Hugh Stanbury]], Trevelyan had had a direct quarrel. Mr.

[097-088]and Mrs. Outhouse he regarded as bitter enemies, who had taken the

[097-089]part of his wife without any regard to the decencies of life. And now

[097-090]it had come to pass that his sole remaining ally, Mr. Samuel Bozzle,

[097-091]the ex-policeman, was becoming weary of his service. Trevelyan

[097-092]remained in the north of Italy up to the middle of March, spending a

[097-093]fortune in sending telegrams to Bozzle, instigating Bozzle by all the

[097-094]means in his power to obtain possession of the child, desiring him at

[097-095]one time to pounce down upon the parsonage of St. Diddulph's with a

[097-096]battalion of policemen armed to the teeth with the law's authority,

[097-097]and at another time suggesting to him to find his way by stratagem

[097-098]into Mr. Outhouse's castle and carry off the child in his arms. At

[097-099]last he sent word to say that he himself would be in England before

[097-100]the end of March, and would see that the majesty of the law should be

[097-101]vindicated in his favour.

[097-102]Bozzle had in truth made but one personal application for the child

[097-103]at St. Diddulph's. In making this he had expected no success, though,

[097-104]from the energetic nature of his disposition, he had made the attempt

[097-105]with some zeal. But he had never applied again at the parsonage,

[097-106]disregarding the letters, the telegrams, and even the promises which

[097-107]had come to him from his employer with such frequency. The truth was

[097-108]that Mrs. Bozzle was opposed to the proposed separation of the mother

[097-109]and the child, and that Bozzle was a man who listened to the words of

[097-110]his wife. Mrs. Bozzle was quite prepared to admit that Madame T.,--as

[097-111]Mrs. Trevelyan had come to be called at No. 55, Stony Walk,--was

[097-112]no better than she should be. Mrs. Bozzle was disposed to think

[097-113]that ladies of quality, among whom Madame T. was entitled in her

[097-114]estimation to take [[social-class|rank]], were seldom better than they ought to be,

[097-115]and she was quite willing that her husband should earn his bread

[097-116]by watching the lady or the lady's lover. She had participated in

[097-117]Bozzle's triumph when he had discovered that the Colonel had gone to

[097-118]Devonshire, and again when he had learned that the Lothario had been

[097-119]at St. Diddulph's. And had the case been brought before the judge

[097-120]ordinary by means of her husband's exertions, she would have taken

[097-121]pleasure in reading every word of the [[truth|evidence]], even though her

[097-122]husband should have been ever so roughly handled by the lawyers. But

[097-123]now, when a demand was made upon Bozzle to violate the sanctity of

[097-124]the clergyman's house, and withdraw the child by force or stratagem,

[097-125]she began to perceive that the palmy days of the Trevelyan affair

[097-126]were over for them, and that it would be wise on her husband's part

[097-127]gradually to back out of the gentleman's employment. "Just put it on

[097-128]the fire-back, Bozzle," she said one morning, as her husband stood

[097-129]before her reading for the second time a somewhat lengthy epistle

[097-130]which had reached him from Italy, while he held the baby over his

[097-131]shoulder with his left arm. He had just washed himself at the sink,

[097-132]and though his face was clean, his hair was rough, and his shirt

[097-133]sleeves were tucked up.

[097-134][Illustration: "Put it on the fire-back, Bozzle."]

[097-135]"That's all very well, Maryanne; but when a party has took a gent's

[097-136]money, a party is bound to go through with the job."

[097-137]"Gammon, Bozzle."

[097-138]"It's all very well to say gammon; but his money has been took,--and

[097-139]there's more to come."

[097-140]"And ain't you worked for the money,--down to Hexeter one time,

[097-141]across the water pretty well day and night watching that ere

[097-142]clergyman's 'ouse like a cat? What more'd he have? As to the child,

[097-143]I won't hear of it, B. The child shan't come here. We'd all be

[097-144]shewed up in the papers as that black, that they'd hoot us along the

[097-145]streets. It ain't the regular line of business, Bozzle; and there

[097-146]ain't no good to be got, never, by going off the regular line."

[097-147]Whereupon Bozzle scratched his head and again read the letter. A

[097-148]distinct promise of a hundred pounds was made to him, if he would

[097-149]have the child ready to hand over to Trevelyan on Trevelyan's arrival

[097-150]in England.

[097-151]"It ain't to be done, you know," said Bozzle.

[097-152]"Of course it ain't," said Mrs. Bozzle.

[097-153]"It ain't to be done anyways;--not in my way of business. Why didn't

[097-154]he go to Skint, as I told him, when his own lawyer was too dainty for

[097-155]the job? The paternal parent has a right to his infants, no doubt."

[097-156]That was Bozzle's law.

[097-157]"I don't believe it, B."

[097-158]"But he have, I tell you."

[097-159]"He can't suckle 'em;--can he? I don't believe a bit of his rights."

[097-160]"When a married woman has followers, and the husband don't go the

[097-161]wrong side of the post too, or it ain't proved again him that he do,

[097-162]they'll never let her have nothing to do with the children. It's been

[097-163]before the court a hundred times. He'll get the child fast enough if

[097-164]he'll go before the court."

[097-165]"Anyways it ain't your business, Bozzle, and don't you meddle nor

[097-166]make. The money's good money as long as it's honest earned; but when

[097-167]you come to rampaging and breaking into a gent's house, then I say

[097-168]money may be had a deal too hard." In this special letter, which had

[097-169]now come to hand, Bozzle was not instructed to "rampage." He was

[097-170]simply desired to make a further official requisition for the boy at

[097-171]the parsonage, and to explain to Mr. Outhouse, Mrs. Outhouse, and

[097-172]Mrs. Trevelyan, or to as many of them as he could contrive to see,

[097-173]that Mr. Trevelyan was immediately about to return to [[london|London]], and

[097-174]that he would put the law into execution if his son were not given

[097-175]up to him at once. "I'll tell you what it is, B.," exclaimed Mrs.

[097-176]Bozzle, "it's my belief as he ain't quite right up here;" and Mrs.

[097-177]Bozzle touched her forehead.

[097-178]"It's love for her as has done it then," said Bozzle, shaking his

[097-179]head.

[097-180]"I'm not a taking of her part, B. A woman as has a husband as finds

[097-181]her with her wittels regular, and with what's decent and comfortable

[097-182]beside, ought to be contented. I've never said no other than that.

[097-183]I ain't no patience with your saucy madames as can't remember as

[097-184]they're eating an honest man's bread. Drat 'em all; what is it they

[097-185]wants? They don't know what they wants. It's just hidleness,--cause

[097-186]there ain't a ha'porth for 'em to do. It's that as makes 'em--, I

[097-187]won't say what. But as for this here child, B.--." At that moment

[097-188]there came a knock at the door. Mrs. Bozzle going into the passage,

[097-189]opened it herself, and saw a strange gentleman. Bozzle, who had stood

[097-190]at the inner door, saw that the gentleman was Mr. Trevelyan.
