## Section 72

[072-001]"Where do you think the money came from that she has got? Did

[072-002]you ever hear that she had anything of her own? She never had a

[072-003]penny,--never a penny. It came out of this house. It is the capital

[072-004]on which this business was founded, and on which it ought to be

[072-005]carried on to this day. My brother had thrown her off; by heavens,

[072-006]yes;--had thrown her off. He had found out what she was, and had got

[072-007]rid of her."

[072-008]"But he left her his money."

[072-009]"Yes;--she got near him when he was dying, and he did leave her his

[072-010]money;--his money, and my money, and your father's money."

[072-011]"He could have given her nothing, Uncle Barty, that wasn't his own."

[072-012]"Of course that's true;--it's true in one way. You might say the same

[072-013]of a man who was cozened into leaving every shilling away from his

[072-014]own children. I wasn't in [[exeter|Exeter]] when the will was made. We none

[072-015]of us were here. But she was here; and when we came to see him die,

[072-016]there we found her. She had had her revenge upon him, and she means

[072-017]to have it on all of us. I don't believe she'll ever leave you a

[072-018]shilling, Brooke. You'll find her out yet, and you'll talk of her to

[072-019]your nephews as I do to you."

[072-020]Brooke made some ordinary answer to this, and bade his uncle adieu.

[072-021]He had allowed himself to entertain a half chivalrous idea that he

[072-022]could produce a reconciliation between Miss Stanbury and his uncle

[072-023]Barty; and since he had been at Exeter he had said a word, first to

[072-024]the one and then to the other, hinting at the subject; but his hints

[072-025]had certainly not been successful. As he walked from the bank into

[072-026]the High Street he could not fail to ask himself whether there were

[072-027]any grounds for the terrible accusations which he had just heard from

[072-028]his uncle's lips. Something of the same kind, though in form much

[072-029]less violent, had been repeated to him very often by others of the

[072-030]family. Though he had as a boy known Miss Stanbury well, he had been

[072-031]taught to regard her as an ogress. All the Burgesses had regarded

[072-032]Miss Stanbury as an ogress since that unfortunate will had come

[072-033]to light. But she was an ogress from whom something might be

[072-034]gained,--and the ogress had still persisted in saying that a Burgess

[072-035]should be her heir. It had therefore come to pass that Brooke had

[072-036]been brought up half to revere her and half to abhor her. "She is a

[072-037]dreadful woman," said his branch of the family, "who will not scruple

[072-038]at anything evil. But as it seems that you may probably reap the

[072-039]advantage of the evil that she does, it will become you to put up

[072-040]with her iniquity." As he had become old enough to understand the

[072-041]nature of her position, he had determined to judge for himself;--but

[072-042]his judgment hitherto simply amounted to this,--that Miss Stanbury

[072-043]was a very singular old woman, with a kind heart and good instincts,

[072-044]but so capricious withal that no sensible man would risk his

[072-045]happiness on expectations formed on her promises. Guided by this

[072-046]opinion, he had resolved to be attentive to her and, after a certain

[072-047]fashion, submissive; but certainly not to become her slave. She had

[072-048]thrown over her nephew. She was constantly complaining to him of her

[072-049]niece. Now and again she would say a very bitter word to him about

[072-050]himself. When he had left Exeter on his little excursion, no one was

[072-051]so much in favour with her as Mr. Gibson. On his return he found that

[072-052]Mr. Gibson had been altogether discarded, and was spoken of in terms

[072-053]of almost insolent abuse. "If I were ever so humble to her," he had

[072-054]said to himself, "it would do no good; and there is nothing I hate so

[072-055]much as humility." He had thus determined to take the goods the gods

[072-056]provided, should it ever come to pass that such godlike provision was

[072-057]laid before him out of Miss Stanbury's coffers;--but not to alter

[072-058]his mode of life or put himself out of his way in obedience to her

[072-059]behests, as a man might be expected to do who was destined to receive

[072-060]so rich a legacy. Upon this idea he had acted, still believing the

[072-061]old woman to be good, but believing at the same time that she was

[072-062]very capricious. Now he had heard what his Uncle Bartholomew Burgess

[072-063]had had to say upon the matter, and he could not refrain from asking

[072-064]himself whether his uncle's accusations were true.

[072-065]In a narrow passage between the High Street and the Close he met Mr.

[072-066]Gibson. There had come to be that sort of intimacy between the two

[072-067]men which grows from closeness of position rather than from any

[072-068]social desire on either side, and it was natural that Burgess should

[072-069]say a word of farewell. On the previous evening Miss Stanbury

[072-070]had relieved her mind by turning Mr. Gibson into ridicule in her

[072-071]description to Brooke of the manner in which the clergyman had

[072-072]carried on his love affair; and she had at the same time declared

[072-073]that Mr. Gibson had been most violently impertinent to herself. He

[072-074]knew, therefore, that Miss Stanbury and Mr. Gibson had become two,

[072-075]and would on this occasion have passed on without a word relative

[072-076]to the old lady had Mr. Gibson allowed him to do so. But Mr. Gibson

[072-077]spoke his mind freely.

[072-078]"Off to-morrow, are you?" he said. "Good-bye. I hope we may meet

[072-079]again; but not in the same house, Mr. Burgess."

[072-080]"There or anywhere I shall be very happy," said Brooke.

[072-081]"Not there, certainly. While you were absent Miss Stanbury treated me

[072-082]in such a way that I shall certainly never put my foot in her house

[072-083]again."

[072-084]"Dear me! I thought that you and she were such great friends."

[072-085]"I knew her very well, of course;--and respected her. She is a good

[072-086]churchwoman, and is charitable in the city; but she has got such a

[072-087]tongue in her head that there is no bearing it when she does what she

[072-088]calls giving you a bit of her mind."

[072-089]"She has been indulgent to me, and has not given me much of it."

[072-090]"Your time will come, I've no doubt," continued Mr. Gibson.

[072-091]"Everybody has always told me that it would be so. Even her oldest

[072-092]friends knew it. You ask Mrs. MacHugh, or Mrs. French, at Heavitree."

[072-093]"Mrs. French!" said Brooke, laughing. "That would hardly be fair

[072-094][[truth|evidence]]."

[072-095]"Why not? I don't know a better judge of [[honour|character]] in all Exeter than

[072-096]Mrs. French. And she and Miss Stanbury have been intimate all their

[072-097]lives. Ask your uncle at the bank."

[072-098]"My uncle and Miss Stanbury never were friends," said Brooke.

[072-099]"Ask [[hugh-stanbury|Hugh Stanbury]] what he thinks of her. But don't suppose I want

[072-100]to say a word against her. I wouldn't for the world do such a thing.

[072-101]Only, as we've met there and all that, I thought it best to let you

[072-102]know that she had treated me in such a way, and has been altogether

[072-103]so violent, that I never will go there again." So saying, Mr.

[072-104]Gibson passed on, and was of opinion that he had spoken with great

[072-105]generosity of the old woman who had treated him so badly.

[072-106]In the afternoon Brooke Burgess went over to the further end of the

[072-107]Close, and called on Mrs. MacHugh; and from thence he walked across

[072-108]to Heavitree, and called on the Frenches. It may be doubted whether

[072-109]he would have been so well behaved to these ladies had they not been

[072-110]appealed to by Mr. Gibson as witnesses to the [[reputation|character]] of Miss

[072-111]Stanbury. He got very little from Mrs. MacHugh. That lady was kind

[072-112]and cordial, and expressed many wishes that she might see him again

[072-113]in Exeter. When he said a few words about Mr. Gibson, Mrs. MacHugh

[072-114]only laughed, and declared that the [[social-class|gentleman]] would soon find a

[072-115]plaister for that sore. "There are more fishes than one in the sea,"

[072-116]she said.

[072-117]"But I'm afraid they've quarrelled, Mrs. MacHugh."

[072-118]"So they tell me. What should we have to talk about here if somebody

[072-119]didn't quarrel sometimes? She and I ought to get up a quarrel for the

[072-120]good of the public;--only they know that I never can quarrel with

[072-121]anybody. I never see anybody interesting enough to quarrel with." But

[072-122]Mrs. MacHugh said nothing about Miss Stanbury, except that she sent

[072-123]over a message with reference to a rubber of whist for the next night

[072-124]but one.

[072-125]He found the two French girls sitting with their mother, and they all

[072-126]expressed their great gratitude to him for coming to say good-bye

[072-127]before he went. "It's so very nice of you, Mr. Burgess," said

[072-128]Camilla, "and particularly just at present."

[072-129]"Yes, indeed," said Arabella, "because you know things have been so

[072-130]unpleasant."

[072-131]"My dears, never mind about that," said Mrs. French. "Miss Stanbury

[072-132]has meant everything for the best, and it is all over now."

[072-133]"I don't know what you mean by its being all over, mamma," said

[072-134]Camilla. "As far as I can understand, it has never been begun."

[072-135]"My dear, the least said the soonest mended," said Mrs. French.

[072-136]"That's of course, mamma," said Camilla; "but yet one can't hold

[072-137]one's tongue altogether. All the city is talking about it, and I dare

[072-138]say Mr. Burgess has heard as much as anybody else."

[072-139]"I've heard nothing at all," said Brooke.

[072-140]"Oh yes, you have," continued Camilla. Arabella conceived herself

[072-141]at this moment to be situated in so delicate a position, that it

[072-142]was best that her sister should talk about it, and that she herself

[072-143]should hold her tongue,--with the exception, perhaps, of a hint here

[072-144]and there which might be of assistance; for Arabella completely

[072-145]understood that the prize was now to be hers, if the prize could be

[072-146]rescued out of the Stanbury clutches. She was aware,--no one better

[072-147]aware,--how her sister had interfered with her early hopes, and was

[072-148]sure, in her own mind, that all her disappointment had come from

[072-149]fratricidal rivalry on the part of Camilla. It had never, however,

[072-150]been open to her to quarrel with Camilla. There they were, linked

[072-151]together, and together they must fight their battles. As two pigs may

[072-152]be seen at the same trough, each striving to take the delicacies of

[072-153]the banquet from the other, and yet enjoying always the warmth of

[072-154]the same dunghill in amicable contiguity, so had these young ladies

[072-155]lived in sisterly friendship, while each was striving to take a

[072-156]husband from the other. They had understood the position, and,

[072-157]though for years back they had talked about Mr. Gibson, they had

[072-158]never quarrelled; but now, in these latter days of the Stanbury

[072-159]interference, there had come tacitly to be something of an

[072-160]understanding between them that, if any fighting were still possible

[072-161]on the subject, one must be put forward and the other must yield.

[072-162]There had been no spoken agreement, but Arabella quite understood

[072-163]that she was to be put forward. It was for her to take up the

[072-164]running, and to win, if possible, against the Stanbury filly. That

[072-165]was her view, and she was inclined to give Camilla credit for acting

[072-166]in accordance with it with honesty and zeal. She felt, therefore,

[072-167]that her words on the present occasion ought to be few. She sat back

[072-168]in her corner of the sofa, and was intent on her work, and shewed by

[072-169]the pensiveness of her brow that there were thoughts within her bosom

[072-170]of which she was not disposed to speak. "You must have heard a great

[072-171]deal," said Camilla, laughing. "You must know how poor Mr. Gibson has

[072-172]been abused, because he wouldn't--"
