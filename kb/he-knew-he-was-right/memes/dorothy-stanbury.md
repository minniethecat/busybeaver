# Dorothy Stanbury

Dorothy Stanbury is the younger sister of Hugh Stanbury and the niece of the formidable Miss Stanbury of Exeter. A quietly principled young woman of limited means, she becomes the romantic focus of two suitors — the vacillating Mr. Gibson and the earnest Brooke Burgess — while navigating the social currents of both rural Nuncombe Putney and the more demanding society of Exeter.

## Overview

Dorothy Stanbury first appears in the context of the Stanbury family at Nuncombe Putney, where she lives with her mother and sister Priscilla. She is described as resembling her brother Hugh in feature: "in face Dorothy Stanbury was very like her brother Hugh" (014-070). Light-haired and possessed of "the sweetest temper in the world" (060-081), Dorothy embodies a type of modest, unassuming virtue that Trollope presents as genuinely admirable rather than merely conventional.

Her narrative arc centres on courtship. She becomes the object of Mr. Gibson's prolonged but indecisive attentions — a "seven years of flirtation" (060-076) that ultimately goes nowhere — before attracting the genuine affection of Brooke Burgess, Miss Stanbury's heir. Dorothy's story offers a quieter counterpoint to the destructive jealousy that consumes the Trevelyan marriage.

## Role in the Novel

Dorothy functions as a moral and romantic foil within the novel's broader examination of love and marriage. While the Trevelyan plot traces the catastrophic failure of a marriage built on suspicion, Dorothy's courtship plot demonstrates the possibility of quiet, steady affection leading to a sound union. She represents the domestic ideal that the novel ultimately endorses — not the passionate intensity of Louis Trevelyan's possessive love, but the patient, reciprocal regard that grows between herself and Brooke Burgess (069-138).

Her removal from Nuncombe Putney to Exeter — summoned by her aunt Miss Stanbury — also serves to connect the novel's two principal geographical settings and to bring the Stanbury family's domestic economy into focus.

## Relationships

### Hugh Stanbury

Dorothy shares a close bond with her brother Hugh, to whom she bears a physical resemblance (014-070). Hugh's career as a journalist and his marriage to Nora Rowley place him at the centre of the novel's main plot, while Dorothy remains in the domestic sphere. Their relationship exemplifies the Stanbury family's quiet mutual support.

### Miss Stanbury

Dorothy's aunt Jemima Stanbury — "Miss Stanbury" of Exeter — is a formidable presence who summons Dorothy from Nuncombe Putney to live with her: "This is my niece, Miss Dorothy Stanbury, from Nuncombe Putney" (052-038). Miss Stanbury's imperious character provides both opportunities and constraints for Dorothy, who must navigate her aunt's strong opinions on everything, including matrimony.

### Mr. Gibson

Mr. Gibson's courtship of Dorothy is notable chiefly for its protracted indecision. The community of Exeter widely expects the match: "They say he is to marry Dorothy Stanbury" (059-094). But after "seven years of flirtation" (060-076), Gibson's attentions come to nothing, revealing him as a man incapable of commitment — a minor but pointed contrast to the decisive action that Dorothy's eventual suitor, Brooke Burgess, displays.

### Brooke Burgess

Brooke Burgess, Miss Stanbury's designated heir, falls genuinely in love with Dorothy. The narrator observes that he is "very much in love with Dorothy Stanbury" (069-138), and he seriously contemplates "taking Dorothy Stanbury for his wife" (073-027). Their eventual union represents one of the novel's few successful romantic resolutions.

## Key Scenes

### Arrival in Exeter

Dorothy's introduction to Exeter society occurs when Miss Stanbury presents her: "This is my niece, Miss Dorothy Stanbury, from Nuncombe Putney" (052-038). This moment marks her transition from the rural domesticity of Nuncombe Putney to the more complex social world of Exeter.

### Mr. Gibson's Indecision

The prolonged and ultimately fruitless courtship by Mr. Gibson occupies a significant portion of Dorothy's narrative. The community's expectation — "They say he is to marry Dorothy Stanbury" (059-094) — creates dramatic irony, as the reader increasingly perceives Gibson's inadequacy as a suitor.

### Brooke Burgess's Proposal

Brooke Burgess's earnest courtship of Dorothy provides the resolution to her romantic plot. Unlike Gibson's vacillation, Burgess's intentions are clear and honourable: the idea of "taking Dorothy Stanbury for his wife" (073-027) is one he pursues with determination.

## Character Development

Dorothy's arc is one of quiet self-possession rather than dramatic transformation. She begins the novel as a modest young woman of limited prospects and ends it with her character essentially unchanged — but her circumstances transformed through marriage. Trollope presents this stability as a virtue: Dorothy does not need to change because her values — patience, loyalty, sweet temper — are already exactly what the novel's moral framework endorses (060-081).
