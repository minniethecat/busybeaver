## Section 19

[019-001]"He has a right to expect obedience, Lady Milborough."

[019-002]"Of course; that is all one wants."

[019-003]"And I will obey Mr. Trevelyan--in anything reasonable."

[019-004]"But, my dear, who is to say what is reasonable? That, you see, is

[019-005]always the difficulty. You must allow that your husband is the person

[019-006]who ought to decide that."

[019-007]"Has he told you that I have refused to obey him, Lady Milborough?"

[019-008]The Countess paused a moment before she replied. "Well, yes; I think

[019-009]he has," she said. "He asked you to do something about a letter,--a

[019-010]letter to that [[colonel-osborne|Colonel Osborne]], who is a man, my dear, really to be

[019-011]very much afraid of; a man who has done a great deal of harm,--and

[019-012]you declined. Now in a matter of that kind of course the husband--"

[019-013]"Lady Milborough, I must ask you to listen to me. You have listened

[019-014]to Mr. Trevelyan, and I must ask you to listen to me. I am sorry

[019-015]to trouble you, but as you have come here about this unpleasant

[019-016]business, you must forgive me if I insist upon it."

[019-017]"Of course I will listen to you, my dear."

[019-018]"I have never refused to obey my husband, and I do not refuse now.

[019-019]The [[social-class|gentleman]] of whom you have been speaking is an old friend of my

[019-020]father's, and has become my friend. Nevertheless, had Mr. Trevelyan

[019-021]given me any plain order about him, I should have obeyed him. A

[019-022]wife does not feel that her chances of happiness are increased when

[019-023]she finds that her husband suspects her of being too intimate with

[019-024]another man. It is a thing very hard to bear. But I would have

[019-025]endeavoured to bear it, knowing how important it is for both our

[019-026]sakes, and more especially for our child. I would have made excuses,

[019-027]and would have endeavoured to think that this horrid feeling on his

[019-028]part is nothing more than a short delusion."

[019-029]"But my dear--"

[019-030]"I must ask you to hear me out, Lady Milborough. But when he tells me

[019-031]first that I am not to meet the man, and so instructs the servants;

[019-032]then tells me that I am to meet him, and go on just as I was going

[019-033]before, and then again tells me that I am not to see him, and again

[019-034]instructs the servants,--and, above all, the cook!--that Colonel

[019-035]Osborne is not to come into the house, then obedience becomes rather

[019-036]difficult."

[019-037]"Just say now that you will do what he wants, and then all will be

[019-038]right."

[019-039]"I will not say so to you, Lady Milborough. It is not to you that

[019-040]I ought to say it. But as he has chosen to send you here, I will

[019-041]explain to you that I have never disobeyed him. When I was free, in

[019-042]accordance with Mr. Trevelyan's wishes, to have what intercourse I

[019-043]pleased with Colonel Osborne, I received a note from that gentleman

[019-044]on a most trivial matter. I answered it as trivially. My husband saw

[019-045]my letter, closed, and questioned me about it. I told him that the

[019-046]letter was still there, and that if he chose to be a spy upon my

[019-047]actions he could open it and read it."

[019-048]"My dear, how could you bring yourself to use the word spy to your

[019-049]husband?"

[019-050]"How could he bring himself to accuse me as he did? If he cares for

[019-051]me let him come and beg my pardon for the insult he has offered me."

[019-052]"Oh, Mrs. Trevelyan,--"

[019-053]"Yes; that seems very wrong to you, who have not had to bear it. It

[019-054]is very easy for a stranger to take a husband's part, and help to put

[019-055]down a poor woman who has been ill-used. I have done nothing wrong,

[019-056]nothing to be ashamed of; and I will not say that I have. I never

[019-057]have spoken a word to Colonel Osborne that all the world might not

[019-058]hear."

[019-059]"Nobody has accused you, my dear."

[019-060]"Yes; he has accused me, and you have accused me, and you will make

[019-061]all the world accuse me. He may put me out of his house if he likes,

[019-062]but he shall not make me say I have been wrong, when I know I have

[019-063]been right. He cannot take my child from me."

[019-064]"But he will."

[019-065]"No," shouted Mrs. Trevelyan, jumping up from her chair, "no; he

[019-066]shall never do that. I will cling to him so that he cannot separate

[019-067]us. He will never be so wicked,--such a monster as that. I would go

[019-068]about the world saying what a monster he had been to me." The passion

[019-069]of the interview was becoming too great for Lady Milborough's power

[019-070]of moderating it, and she was beginning to feel herself to be in a

[019-071]difficulty. "Lady Milborough," continued Mrs. Trevelyan, "tell him

[019-072]from me that I will bear anything but that. That I will not bear."

[019-073]"Dear Mrs. Trevelyan, do not let us talk about it."

[019-074]"Who wants to talk about it? Why do you come here and threaten me

[019-075]with a thing so horrible? I do not believe you. He would not dare to

[019-076]separate me and my--child."

[019-077]"But you have only to say that you will submit yourself to him."

[019-078]"I have submitted myself to him, and I will submit no further. What

[019-079]does he want? Why does he send you here? He does not know what he

[019-080]wants. He has made himself miserable by an absurd idea, and he wants

[019-081]everybody to tell him that he has been right. He has been very wrong;

[019-082]and if he desires to be wise now, he will come back to his home,

[019-083]and say nothing further about it. He will gain nothing by sending

[019-084]messengers here."

[019-085]Lady Milborough, who had undertaken a most disagreeable task from

[019-086]the purest motives of old friendship, did not like being called a

[019-087]messenger; but the woman before her was so strong in her words, so

[019-088]eager, and so passionate, that she did not know how to resent the

[019-089]injury. And there was coming over her an idea, of which she herself

[019-090]was hardly conscious, that after all, perhaps, the husband was not in

[019-091]the right. She had come there with the general idea that wives, and

[019-092]especially young wives, should be submissive. She had naturally taken

[019-093]the husband's part; and having a preconceived dislike to Colonel

[019-094]Osborne, she had been willing enough to think that precautionary

[019-095]measures were necessary in reference to so eminent, and notorious,

[019-096]and experienced a Lothario. She had never altogether loved Mrs.

[019-097]Trevelyan, and had always been a little in dread of her. But she had

[019-098]thought that the authority with which she would be invested on this

[019-099]occasion, the manifest right on her side, and the undeniable truth of

[019-100]her grand argument, that a wife should obey, would carry her, if not

[019-101]easily, still successfully through all difficulties. It was probably

[019-102]the case that Lady Milborough when preparing for her visit, had

[019-103]anticipated a triumph. But when she had been closeted for an hour

[019-104]with Mrs. Trevelyan, she found that she was not triumphant. She was

[019-105]told that she was a messenger, and an unwelcome messenger; and she

[019-106]began to feel that she did not know how she was to take herself away.

[019-107]"I am sure I have done everything for the best," she said, getting up

[019-108]from her chair.

[019-109]"The best will be to send him back, and make him feel the truth."

[019-110]"The best for you, my dear, will be to consider well what should be

[019-111]the duty of a wife."

[019-112]"I have considered, Lady Milborough. It cannot be a wife's duty to

[019-113]acknowledge that she has been wrong in such a matter as this."

[019-114]Then Lady Milborough made her curtsey and got herself away in some

[019-115]manner that was sufficiently awkward, and Mrs. Trevelyan curtseyed

[019-116]also as she rang the bell; and, though she was sore and wretched,

[019-117]and, in truth, sadly frightened, she was not awkward. In that

[019-118]encounter, so far as it had gone, she had been the victor.

[019-119]As soon as she was alone and the carriage had been driven well away

[019-120]from the door, Mrs. Trevelyan left the drawing-room and went up to

[019-121]the nursery. As she entered she clothed her face with her sweetest

[019-122]smile. "How is his own mother's dearest, dearest, darling duck?"

[019-123]she said, putting out her arms and taking the boy from the nurse.

[019-124]The child was at this time about ten months old, and was a strong,

[019-125]hearty, happy infant, always laughing when he was awake and always

[019-126]sleeping when he did not laugh, because his little limbs were

[019-127]free from pain and his little stomach was not annoyed by internal

[019-128]troubles. He kicked, and crowed, and sputtered, when his mother took

[019-129]him, and put up his little fingers to clutch her hair, and was to her

[019-130]as a young god upon the earth. Nothing in the world had ever been

[019-131]created so beautiful, so joyous, so satisfactory, so divine! And they

[019-132]told her that this apple of her eye was to be taken away from her!

[019-133]No;--that must be impossible. "I will take him into my own room,

[019-134]nurse, for a little while--you have had him all the morning," she

[019-135]said; as though the "having baby" was a privilege over which there

[019-136]might almost be a quarrel. Then she took her boy away with her,

[019-137]and when she was alone with him, went through such a service in

[019-138]baby-worship as most mothers will understand. Divide these two! No;

[019-139]nobody should do that. Sooner than that, she, the mother, would

[019-140]consent to be no more than a servant in her husband's house. Was not

[019-141]her baby all the world to her?

[019-142]On the evening of that day the husband and wife had an interview

[019-143]together in the library, which, unfortunately, was as unsatisfactory

[019-144]as Lady Milborough's visit. The cause of the failure of them all

[019-145]lay probably in this,--that there was no decided point which, if

[019-146]conceded, would have brought about a reconciliation. Trevelyan asked

[019-147]for general submission, which he regarded as his right, and which in

[019-148]the existing circumstances he thought it necessary to claim, and

[019-149]though Mrs. Trevelyan did not refuse to be submissive she would make

[019-150]no promise on the subject. But the truth was that each desired that

[019-151]the other should acknowledge a fault, and that neither of them would

[019-152]make that acknowledgment. [[emily-trevelyan|Emily Trevelyan]] felt acutely that she

[019-153]had been ill-used, not only by her husband's suspicion, but by the

[019-154]manner in which he had talked of his suspicion to others,--to Lady

[019-155]Milborough and the cook, and she was quite convinced that she was

[019-156]right herself, because he had been so vacillating in his conduct

[019-157]about Colonel Osborne. But Trevelyan was equally sure that justice

[019-158]was on his side. Emily must have known his real wishes about Colonel

[019-159]Osborne; but when she had found that he had rescinded his verbal

[019-160]orders about the admission of the man to the house,--which he had

[019-161]done to save himself and her from slander and [[scandal|gossip]],--she had taken

[019-162]advantage of this and had thrown herself more entirely than ever into

[019-163]the intimacy of which he disapproved! When they met, each was so sore

[019-164]that no approach to terms was made by them.

[019-165]"If I am to be treated in that way, I would rather not live with

[019-166]you," said the wife. "It is impossible to live with a husband who is

[019-167]jealous."

[019-168]"All I ask of you is that you shall promise me to have no further

[019-169]communication with this man."

[019-170]"I will make no promise that implies my own disgrace."

[019-171]"Then we must part; and if that be so, this house will be given up.

[019-172]You may live where you please,--in the country, not in [[london|London]]; but I

[019-173]shall take steps that Colonel Osborne does not see you."

[019-174]"I will not remain in the room with you to be insulted thus," said

[019-175]Mrs. Trevelyan. And she did not remain, but left the chamber,

[019-176]slamming the door after her as she went.
