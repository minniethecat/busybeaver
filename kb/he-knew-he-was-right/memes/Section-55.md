## Section 55

[055-001]Stanbury went back to the Full Moon, and retraced his steps with his

[055-002]friend towards Lincoln's Inn. Two minutes took him from the parsonage

[055-003]to the public-house, but during these two minutes he resolved that

[055-004]he would speak his mind roundly to Trevelyan as they returned home.

[055-005]Trevelyan should either take his wife back again at once, or else he,

[055-006]Stanbury, would have no more to do with him. He said nothing till

[055-007]they had threaded together the maze of streets which led them from

[055-008]the neighbourhood of the Church of St. Diddulph's into the straight

[055-009]way of the Commercial Road. Then he began. "Trevelyan," said he, "you

[055-010]are wrong in all this from beginning to end."

[055-011]"What do you mean?"

[055-012]"Just what I say. If there was anything in what your wife did to

[055-013]offend you, a soft word from you would have put it all right."

[055-014]"A soft word! How do you know what soft words I used?"

[055-015]"A soft word now would do it. You have only to bid her come back to

[055-016]you, and let bygones be bygones, and all would be right. Can't you be

[055-017]man enough to remember that you are a man?"

[055-018]"Stanbury, I believe you want to quarrel with me."

[055-019]"I tell you fairly that I think that you are wrong."

[055-020]"They have talked you over to their side."

[055-021]"I know nothing about sides. I only know that you are wrong."

[055-022]"And what would you have me do?"

[055-023]"Go and travel together for six months." Here was Lady Milborough's

[055-024]receipt again! "Travel together for a year if you will. Then come

[055-025]back and live where you please. People will have forgotten it;--or if

[055-026]they remember it, what matters? No sane person can advise you to go

[055-027]on as you are doing now."

[055-028]But it was of no avail. Before they had reached the Bank the two

[055-029]friends had quarrelled and had parted. Then Trevelyan felt that there

[055-030]was indeed no one left to him but Bozzle. On the following morning he

[055-031]saw Bozzle, and on the evening of the next day he was in Paris.

[055-032]CHAPTER XXXIII.

[055-033]HUGH STANBURY SMOKES ANOTHER PIPE.

[055-034]Trevelyan was gone, and Bozzle alone knew his address. During the

[055-035]first fortnight of her residence at St. Diddulph's Mrs. Trevelyan

[055-036]received two letters from Lady Milborough, in both of which she

[055-037]was recommended, indeed tenderly implored, to be submissive to

[055-038]her husband. "Anything," said Lady Milborough, "is better than

[055-039]separation." In answer to the second letter Mrs. Trevelyan told the

[055-040]old lady that she had no means by which she could shew any submission

[055-041]to her husband, even if she were so minded. Her husband had gone

[055-042]away, she did not know whither, and she had no means by which she

[055-043]could communicate with him. And then came a packet to her from her

[055-044]father and mother, despatched from the islands after the receipt by

[055-045]Lady Rowley of the melancholy tidings of the journey to Nuncombe

[055-046]Putney. Both Sir Marmaduke and Lady Rowley were full of anger against

[055-047]Trevelyan, and wrote as though the husband could certainly be brought

[055-048]back to a sense of his duty, if they only were present. This packet

[055-049]had been at [[nuncombe-putney|Nuncombe Putney]], and contained a sealed note from Sir

[055-050]Marmaduke addressed to Mr. Trevelyan. Lady Rowley explained that it

[055-051]was impossible that they should get to England earlier than in the

[055-052]spring. "I would come myself at once and leave papa to follow," said

[055-053]Lady Rowley, "only for the children. If I were to bring them, I

[055-054]must take a house for them, and the expense would ruin us. Papa has

[055-055]written to Mr. Trevelyan in a way that he thinks will bring him to

[055-056]reason."

[055-057]But how was this letter, by which the husband was to be brought to

[055-058]reason, to be put into the husband's hands? Mrs. Trevelyan applied

[055-059]to Mr. Bideawhile and to Lady Milborough, and to Stanbury, for

[055-060]Trevelyan's address; but was told by each of them that nothing was

[055-061]known of his whereabouts. She did not apply to Mr. Bozzle, although

[055-062]Mr. Bozzle was more than once in her neighbourhood; but as yet she

[055-063]knew nothing of Mr. Bozzle. The replies from Mr. Bideawhile and from

[055-064]Lady Milborough came by the post; but [[hugh-stanbury|Hugh Stanbury]] thought that duty

[055-065]required him to make another journey to St. Diddulph's and carry his

[055-066]own answer with him.

[055-067]And on this occasion Fortune was either very kind to him,--or very

[055-068]unkind. Whichever it was, he found himself alone for a few seconds

[055-069]in the parsonage parlour with [[nora-rowley|Nora Rowley]]. Mr. Outhouse was away at

[055-070]the time. Emily had gone up-stairs for the boy; and Mrs. Outhouse,

[055-071]suspecting nothing, had followed her. "Miss Rowley," said he, getting

[055-072]up from his seat, "if you think it will do any good I will follow

[055-073]Trevelyan till I find him."

[055-074]"How can you find him? Besides, why should you give up your own

[055-075]business?"

[055-076]"I would do anything--to serve your sister." This he said with

[055-077]hesitation in his voice, as though he did not dare to speak all that

[055-078]he desired to have spoken.

[055-079]"I am sure that Emily is very grateful," said Nora; "but she would

[055-080]not wish to give you such trouble as that."

[055-081]"I would do anything for your sister," he repeated, "--for your sake,

[055-082]Miss Rowley." This was the first time that he had ever spoken a word

[055-083]to her in such a strain, and it would be hardly too much to say that

[055-084]her heart was sick for some such expression. But now that it had

[055-085]come, though there was a sweetness about it that was delicious to

[055-086]her, she was absolutely silenced by it. And she was at once not only

[055-087]silent, but stern, rigid, and apparently cold. Stanbury could not but

[055-088]feel as he looked at her that he had offended her. "Perhaps I ought

[055-089]not to say as much," said he; "but it is so."

[055-090]"Mr. Stanbury," said she, "that is nonsense. It is of my sister, not

[055-091]of me, that we are speaking."

[055-092]Then the door was opened and Emily came in with her child, followed

[055-093]by her aunt. There was no other opportunity, and perhaps it was well

[055-094]for Nora and for Hugh that there should have been no other. Enough

[055-095]had been said to give her comfort; and more might have led to his

[055-096]discomposure. As to that matter on which he was presumed to have come

[055-097]to St. Diddulph's, he could do nothing. He did not know Trevelyan's

[055-098]address, but did know that Trevelyan had abandoned the chambers in

[055-099]Lincoln's Inn. And then he found himself compelled to confess that he

[055-100]had quarrelled with Trevelyan, and that they had parted in anger on

[055-101]the day of their joint visit to the East. "Everybody who knows him

[055-102]must quarrel with him," said Mrs. Outhouse. Hugh when he took his

[055-103]leave was treated by them all as a friend who had been gained. Mrs.

[055-104]Outhouse was gracious to him. Mrs. Trevelyan whispered a word to him

[055-105]of her own trouble. "If I can hear anything of him, you may be sure

[055-106]that I will let you know," he said. Then it was Nora's turn to bid

[055-107]him adieu. There was nothing to be said. No word could be spoken

[055-108]before others that should be of any avail. But as he took her hand

[055-109]in his he remembered the reticence of her fingers on that former day,

[055-110]and thought that he was sure there was a difference.

[055-111]On this occasion he made his journey back to the end of Chancery Lane

[055-112]on the top of an omnibus; and as he lit his little pipe, disregarding

[055-113]altogether the scrutiny of the public, thoughts passed through his

[055-114]mind similar to those in which he had indulged as he sat smoking on

[055-115]the corner of the churchyard wall at Nuncombe Putney. He declared to

[055-116]himself that he did love this girl; and as it was so, would it not be

[055-117]better, at any rate more manly, that he should tell her so honestly,

[055-118]than go on groping about with half-expressed words when he saw her,

[055-119]thinking of her and yet hardly daring to go near her, bidding himself

[055-120]to forget her although he knew that such forgetting was impossible,

[055-121]hankering after the sound of her voice and the touch of her hand, and

[055-122]something of the tenderness of returned affection,--and yet regarding

[055-123]her as a prize altogether out of his reach! Why should she be out

[055-124]of his reach? She had no money, and he had not a couple of hundred

[055-125]pounds in the world. But he was earning an [[money|income]] which would give

[055-126]them both shelter and clothes and bread and cheese.

[055-127]What reader is there, male or female, of such stories as is this, who

[055-128]has not often discussed in his or her own mind the different sides of

[055-129]this question of love and marriage? On either side enough may be said

[055-130]by any arguer to convince at any rate himself. It must be wrong for a

[055-131]man, whose income is both insufficient and precarious also, not only

[055-132]to double his own cares and burdens, but to place the weight of that

[055-133]doubled burden on other shoulders besides his own,--on shoulders that

[055-134]are tender and soft, and ill adapted to the carriage of any crushing

[055-135]weight. And then that doubled burden,--that burden of two mouths to

[055-136]be fed, of two backs to be covered, of two minds to be satisfied, is

[055-137]so apt to double itself again and again. The two so speedily become

[055-138]four, and six! And then there is the feeling that that kind of

[055-139]semi-poverty, which has in itself something of the pleasantness of

[055-140][[personal-freedom|independence]], when it is borne by a man alone, entails the miseries

[055-141]of a draggle-tailed and querulous existence when it is imposed on a

[055-142]woman who has in her own home enjoyed the comforts of affluence. As a

[055-143]man thinks of all this, if he chooses to argue with himself on that

[055-144]side, there is enough in the argument to make him feel that not only

[055-145]as a wise man but as an honest man, he had better let the young lady

[055-146]alone. She is well as she is, and he sees around him so many who have

[055-147]tried the chances of marriage and who are not well! Look at Jones

[055-148]with his wan, worn wife and his five children, Jones who is not yet

[055-149]thirty, of whom he happens to know that the wretched man cannot look

[055-150]his doctor in the face, and that the doctor is as necessary to the

[055-151]man's house as is the butcher! What heart can Jones have for his work

[055-152]with such a burden as this upon his shoulders? And so the thinker,

[055-153]who argues on that side, resolves that the young lady shall go her

[055-154]own way for him.

[055-155]But the arguments on the other side are equally cogent, and so much

[055-156]more alluring! And they are used by the same man with reference to

[055-157]the same passion, and are intended by him to put himself right in his

[055-158]conduct in reference to the same dear girl. Only the former line of

[055-159]thoughts occurred to him on a Saturday, when he was ending his week

[055-160]rather gloomily, and this other way of thinking on the same subject

[055-161]has come upon him on a Monday, as he is beginning his week with

[055-162]renewed hope. Does this young girl of his heart love him? And if so,

[055-163]their affection for each other being thus reciprocal, is she not

[055-164]entitled to an expression of her opinion and her wishes on this

[055-165]difficult subject? And if she be willing to run the risk and to

[055-166]encounter the dangers,--to do so on his behalf, because she is

[055-167]willing to share everything with him,--is it becoming in him, a man,

[055-168]to fear what she does not fear? If she be not willing let her say so.

[055-169]If there be any speaking, he must speak first;--but she is entitled,

[055-170]as much as he is, to her own ideas respecting their great outlook

[055-171]into the affairs of the world. And then is it not manifestly God's

[055-172]ordinance that a man should live together with a woman? How poor

[055-173]a creature does the man become who has shirked his duty in this

[055-174]respect, who has done nothing to keep the world going, who has been

[055-175]willing to ignore all affection so that he might avoid all burdens,

[055-176]and who has put into his own belly every good thing that has come to

[055-177]him, either by the earning of his own hands or from the bounty and

[055-178]industry of others! Of course there is a risk; but what excitement is

[055-179]there in anything in which there is none? So on the Tuesday he speaks

[055-180]his mind to the young lady, and tells her candidly that there will be

[055-181]potatoes for the two of them,--sufficient, as he hopes, of potatoes,

[055-182]but no more. As a matter of course the young lady replies that

[055-183]she for her part will be quite content to take the parings for her

[055-184]own eating. Then they rush deliciously into each other's arms and

[055-185]the matter is settled. For, though the convictions arising from

[055-186]the former line of argument may be set aside as often as need be,

[055-187]those reached from the latter are generally conclusive. That such

[055-188]a settlement will always be better for the young [[social-class|gentleman]] and the

[055-189]young lady concerned than one founded on a sterner prudence is more

[055-190]than one may dare to say; but we do feel sure that that country will

[055-191]be most prosperous in which such leaps in the dark are made with the

[055-192]greatest freedom.
