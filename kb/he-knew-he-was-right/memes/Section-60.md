## Section 60

[060-001]"You are only joking, of course," he said.

[060-002]"Indeed, I am not joking. I can assure you, Mr. Gibson, that the

[060-003]welfare of the friends whom I really love can never be a matter of

[060-004]joke to me. Mrs. Crumbie says that you positively are engaged to

[060-005]marry Dorothy Stanbury."

[060-006]"What does Mrs. Crumbie know about it?"

[060-007]"I dare say, nothing. It is not so;--is it?"

[060-008]"Certainly not."

[060-009]"And there is nothing in it;--is there?"

[060-010]"I wonder why people make these reports," said Mr. Gibson,

[060-011]prevaricating.

[060-012][Illustration: "I wonder why people make these reports."]

[060-013]"It is a fabrication from beginning to end then," said Arabella,

[060-014]pressing the matter quite home. At this time she was very close to

[060-015]him, and though her words were severe, the glance from her eyes was

[060-016]soft. And the scent from her hair was not objectionable to him, as

[060-017]it would have been to Miss Stanbury. And the mode of her head-dress

[060-018]was not displeasing to him. And the folds of her dress, as they fell

[060-019]across his knee, were welcome to his feelings. He knew that he was as

[060-020]one under temptation, but he was not strong enough to bid the tempter

[060-021]avaunt. "Say that it is so, Mr. Gibson!"

[060-022]"Of course, it is not so," said Mr. Gibson--lying.

[060-023]"I am so glad. For of course, Mr. Gibson, when we heard it we thought

[060-024]a great deal about it. A man's happiness depends so much on whom he

[060-025]marries;--doesn't it? And a clergyman's more than anybody else's. And

[060-026]we didn't think she was quite the sort of woman that you would like.

[060-027]You see, she has had no advantages, poor thing. She has been shut up

[060-028]in a little country cottage all her life;--just a labourer's hovel,

[060-029]no more;--and though it wasn't her fault, of course, and we all

[060-030]pitied her, and were so glad when Miss Stanbury brought her to

[060-031]the Close;--still, you know, though one was very glad of her as

[060-032]an acquaintance, yet, you know, as a wife,--and for such a dear,

[060-033]dear friend--" She went on, and said many other things with equal

[060-034]enthusiasm, and then wiped her eyes, and then smiled and laughed.

[060-035]After that she declared that she was quite happy,--so happy; and so

[060-036]she left him. The poor man, after the falsehood had been extracted

[060-037]from him, said nothing more; but sat, in patience, listening to the

[060-038]raptures and enthusiasm of his friend. He knew that he had disgraced

[060-039]himself, and he knew also that his disgrace would be known, if

[060-040]Dorothy Stanbury should accept his offer on the morrow. And yet how

[060-041]hardly he had been used! What answer could he have given compatible

[060-042]both with the truth and with his own personal [[pride|dignity]]?

[060-043][[About]] half an hour afterwards he was walking back to [[exeter|Exeter]] with

[060-044]Brooke Burgess, and then Brooke did ask him a question or two.

[060-045]"Nice girls those Frenches, I think," said Brooke.

[060-046]"Very nice," said Mr. Gibson.

[060-047]"How Miss Stanbury does hate them," says Brooke.

[060-048]"Not hate them, I hope," said Mr. Gibson.

[060-049]"She doesn't love them;--does she?"

[060-050]"Well, as for love;--yes; in one sense,--I hope she does. Miss

[060-051]Stanbury, you know, is a woman who expresses herself strongly."

[060-052]"What would she say, if she were told that you and I were going to

[060-053]marry those two girls? We are both favourites, you know."

[060-054]"Dear me! What a very odd supposition," said Mr. Gibson.

[060-055]"For my part, I don't think I shall," said Brooke.

[060-056]"I don't suppose I shall either," said Mr. Gibson, with a gravity

[060-057]which was intended to convey some smattering of rebuke.

[060-058]"A fellow might do worse, you know," said Brooke. "For my part, I

[060-059]rather like girls with chignons, and all that sort of get-up. But the

[060-060]worst of it is, one can't marry two at a time."

[060-061]"That would be bigamy," said Mr. Gibson.

[060-062]"Just so," said Brooke.

[060-063]CHAPTER XXXVI.

[060-064]MISS STANBURY'S WRATH.

[060-065]Punctually at eleven o'clock on the Friday morning Mr. Gibson

[060-066]knocked at the door of the house in the Close. The reader must not

[060-067]imagine that he had ever wavered in his intention with regard to

[060-068]Dorothy Stanbury, because he had been driven into a corner by the

[060-069]pertinacious ingenuity of Miss French. He never for a moment thought

[060-070]of being false to Miss Stanbury the elder. Falseness of that nature

[060-071]would have been ruinous to him,--would have made him a marked man in

[060-072]the city all his days, and would probably have reached even to the

[060-073]bishop's ears. He was neither bad enough, nor audacious enough, nor

[060-074]foolish enough, for such perjury as that. And, moreover, though the

[060-075]wiles of Arabella had been potent with him, he very much preferred

[060-076]Dorothy Stanbury. Seven years of flirtation with a young lady is more

[060-077]trying to the affection than any duration of [[marriage|matrimony]]. Arabella had

[060-078]managed to awaken something of the old glow, but Mr. Gibson, as soon

[060-079]as he was alone, turned from her mentally in disgust. No! Whatever

[060-080]little trouble there might be in his way, it was clearly his duty

[060-081]to marry Dorothy Stanbury. She had the sweetest temper in the world,

[060-082]and blushed with the prettiest blush! She would have, moreover, two

[060-083]thousand pounds on the day she married, and there was no saying what

[060-084]other and greater pecuniary advantages might follow. His mind was

[060-085]quite made up; and during the whole morning he had been endeavouring

[060-086]to drive all disagreeable reminiscences of Miss French from his

[060-087]memory, and to arrange the words with which he would make his offer

[060-088]to Dorothy. He was aware that he need not be very particular about

[060-089]his words, as Dorothy, from the bashfulness of her nature, would be

[060-090]no judge of eloquence at such a time. But still, for his own sake,

[060-091]there should be some form of expression, some [[social-custom|propriety]] of diction.

[060-092]Before eleven o'clock he had it all by heart, and had nearly freed

[060-093]himself from the uneasiness of his falsehood to Arabella. He had

[060-094]given much serious thought to the matter, and had quite resolved that

[060-095]he was right in his purpose, and that he could marry Dorothy with a

[060-096]pure conscience, and with a true promise of a husband's love. "Dear

[060-097]Dolly!" he said to himself, with something of enthusiasm as he walked

[060-098]across the Close. And he looked up to the house as he came to it.

[060-099]There was to be his future home. There was not one of the prebends

[060-100]who had a better house. And there was a dove-like softness about

[060-101]Dorothy's eyes, and a winning obedience in her manner, that were

[060-102]charming. His lines had fallen to him in very pleasant places.

[060-103]Yes;--he would go up to her, and take her at once by the hand, and

[060-104]ask her whether she would be his, now and for ever. He would not

[060-105]let go her hand till he had brought her so close to him that she

[060-106]could hide her blushes on his shoulder. The whole thing had been so

[060-107]well conceived, had become so clear to his mind, that he felt no

[060-108]hesitation or embarrassment as he knocked at the door. Arabella

[060-109]French would, no doubt, hear of it soon. Well;--she must hear of it.

[060-110]After all she could do him no injury.

[060-111]He was shown up at once into the drawing-room, and there he

[060-112]found--Miss Stanbury the elder. "Oh, Mr. Gibson!" she said at once.

[060-113]"Is anything the matter with--dear Dorothy?"

[060-114]"She is the most obstinate, pig-headed young woman I ever came across

[060-115]since the world began."

[060-116]"You don't say so! But what is it, Miss Stanbury?"

[060-117]"What is it? Why just this. Nothing on earth that I can say to her

[060-118]will induce her to come down and speak to you."

[060-119]"Have I offended her?"

[060-120]"Offended a fiddlestick! Offence indeed! An offer from an honest man,

[060-121]with her friends' approval, and a [[money|fortune]] at her back, as though she

[060-122]had been born with a gold spoon in her mouth! And she tells me that

[060-123]she can't, and won't, and wouldn't, and shouldn't, as though I were

[060-124]asking her to walk the streets. I declare I don't know what has come

[060-125]to the young women;--or what it is they want. One would have thought

[060-126]that butter wouldn't melt in her mouth."

[060-127]"But what is the reason, Miss Stanbury?"

[060-128]"Oh, reason! You don't suppose people give reasons in these days.

[060-129]What reason have they when they dress themselves up with bandboxes on

[060-130]their sconces? Just simply the old reason--'I do not like thee, Dr.

[060-131]Fell;--why I cannot tell.'"

[060-132]"May I not see her myself, Miss Stanbury?"

[060-133]"I can't make her come down-stairs to you. I've been at her the whole

[060-134]morning, Mr. Gibson. Ever since daylight, pretty nearly. She came

[060-135]into my room before I was up, and told me she had made up her mind.

[060-136]I've coaxed, and scolded, and threatened, and cried;--but if she'd

[060-137]been a milestone it couldn't have been of less use. I told her she

[060-138]might go back to Nuncombe, and she just went off to pack up."

[060-139]"But she's not to go?"

[060-140]"How can I say what such a young woman will do? I'm never allowed a

[060-141]way of my own for a moment. There's Brooke Burgess been scolding me

[060-142]at that rate I didn't know whether I stood on my head or my heels.

[060-143]And I don't know now."

[060-144]Then there was a pause, while Mr. Gibson was endeavouring to decide

[060-145]what would now be his best course of action. "Don't you think she'll

[060-146]ever come round, Miss Stanbury?"

[060-147]"I don't think she'll ever come any way that anybody wants her to

[060-148]come, Mr. Gibson."

[060-149]"I didn't think she was at all like that," said Mr. Gibson, almost in

[060-150]tears.

[060-151]"No,--nor anybody else. I've been seeing it come all the same. It's

[060-152]just the Stanbury perversity. If I'd wanted to keep her by herself,

[060-153]to take care of me, and had set my back up at her if she spoke to

[060-154]a man, and made her understand that she wasn't to think of getting

[060-155]married, she'd have been making eyes at every man that came into the

[060-156]house. It's just what one gets for going out of one's way. I did

[060-157]think she'd be so happy, Mr. Gibson, living here as your wife. She

[060-158]and I between us could have managed for you so nicely."

[060-159]Mr. Gibson was silent for a minute or two, during which he walked up

[060-160]and down the room,--contemplating, no doubt, the picture of married

[060-161]life which Miss Stanbury had painted for him,--a picture which, as

[060-162]it seemed, was not to be realised. "And what had I better do, Miss

[060-163]Stanbury?" he asked at last.

[060-164]"Do! I don't know what you're to do. I'm groom enough to bring a mare

[060-165]to water, but I can't make her drink."

[060-166]"Will waiting be any good?"

[060-167]"How can I say? I'll tell you one thing not to do. Don't go and

[060-168]philander with those girls at Heavitree. It's my belief that Dorothy

[060-169]has been thinking of them. People talk to her, of course."

[060-170]"I wish people would hold their tongues. People are so indiscreet.

[060-171]People don't know how much harm they may do."

[060-172]"You've given them some excuse, you know, Mr. Gibson."

[060-173]This was very ill-natured, and was felt by Mr. Gibson to be so rude,

[060-174]that he almost turned upon his patroness in anger. He had known Dolly

[060-175]for not more than three months, and had devoted himself to her, to

[060-176]the great anger of his older friends. He had come this morning true

[060-177]to his appointment, expecting that others would keep their promises

[060-178]to him, as he was ready to keep those which he had made;--and now he

[060-179]was told that it was his fault! "I do think that's rather hard, Miss

[060-180]Stanbury," he said.

[060-181]"So you have," said she;--"nasty, slatternly girls, without an idea

[060-182]inside their noddles. But it's no use your scolding me."

[060-183]"I didn't mean to scold, Miss Stanbury."
