## Overview

Caroline Spalding is an American woman who appears in the novel's Italian chapters as part of the expatriate society that forms around the Trevelyan and Rowley parties. She is the elder of two American sisters traveling in Europe, and her romance with [[charles-glascock|Charles Glascock]] provides a transatlantic subplot that broadens the novel's social canvas beyond English provincial life.

## Role in the Novel

Caroline Spalding enters the narrative in the Italian chapters, when [[nora-rowley|Nora Rowley]] and her party encounter the Spalding sisters in Turin (063-108). Her presence introduces an American perspective into the novel's otherwise English social world, and Trollope uses her to explore cultural differences between American and English sensibilities — particularly regarding courtship, independence, and the position of women.

Her central plot function is her romance with Charles Glascock, the heir to a title and estate. The relationship is complicated by Glascock's simultaneous attraction to Nora Rowley, creating a quiet rivalry between the two women that is handled with Trollope's characteristic delicacy and emotional intelligence (065-165). Caroline is aware of this competition, and her suffering during the period of uncertainty is depicted with genuine sympathy (090-122).

Caroline eventually wins Glascock's love and his proposal. The match is significant because it crosses national lines — an American woman becoming an English aristocrat — and because it represents, in the novel's terms, a successful and mutually respectful courtship, in contrast to the disasters and compromises that characterize many of the novel's other romantic relationships (130-145).

## Character and Disposition

Caroline Spalding is characterized by intelligence, dignity, and a "spirit of her own" (091-155). Unlike the French sisters, who pursue marriage with anxious calculation, Caroline maintains her self-respect throughout her romantic uncertainty. When she perceives that Glascock may be interested in Nora, her back rises "as the back of a thoroughbred horse will rise at the touch of an inexperienced rider" (092-107) — but she does not scheme or complain. She waits, and her patience is rewarded.

The narrator's admiration for Caroline is evident. She possesses what Trollope most values in his heroines: moral clarity, emotional honesty, and the courage to be unhappy rather than compromise her principles. Her American nationality is not merely incidental but is presented as part of what makes her distinctive — a frankness and independence that English conventions do not always encourage.

> Caroline Spalding knew that her happiness hung in the balance, and she did what she could — nothing. She would not angle for a proposal, would not manipulate, would not perform. If Glascock wanted her, he must want her for what she was: an American woman with a mind of her own and no talent for deception. (126-028)

## Key Scenes

Caroline's most significant scenes occur in the Italian setting, where the expatriate social world creates an atmosphere of heightened romantic possibility. Her interactions with Glascock in Turin and later in England reveal the slow, sometimes painful progress of a courtship based on genuine compatibility rather than theatrical passion.

The scene in which Glascock finally declares himself — addressing his letter to "Miss Spalding, — Caroline Spalding" — is one of the novel's quiet triumphs. It is not a grand romantic gesture but a steady, considered act of commitment, and Caroline's response is equally measured: she accepts him not with ecstasy but with a deep and quiet satisfaction that the narrator presents as a truer form of happiness (127-101).

## Significance

Caroline Spalding represents the possibility of a modern, companionate marriage based on mutual respect rather than social convenience or reckless passion. In a novel dominated by the catastrophic failure of the Trevelyan marriage, Caroline's story offers an alternative model — one in which reason, dignity, and genuine affection prevail. Her American identity adds a cosmopolitan dimension to the novel's social vision and suggests that the virtues Trollope most admires are not confined to any single nationality.
