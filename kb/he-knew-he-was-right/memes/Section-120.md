## Section 120

[120-001]"I meant only to stay one night, aunt."

[120-002]"That is nonsense. If I am to part with either of you, I will part

[120-003]with him. You are dearer to me than he is. Dorothy, you do not know

[120-004]how dear to me you are."

[120-005]Dorothy immediately fell on her knees at her aunt's feet, and hid her

[120-006]face in her aunt's lap. Miss Stanbury twined round her fingers the

[120-007]soft hair which she loved so well,--because it was a grace given by

[120-008]God and not bought out of a shop,--and caressed the girl's head, and

[120-009]muttered something that was intended for a prayer. "If he will let

[120-010]me, aunt, I will give him up," said Dorothy, looking up into her

[120-011]aunt's face. "If he will say that I may, though I shall love him

[120-012]always, he may go."

[120-013]"He is his own master," said Miss Stanbury. "Of course he is his own

[120-014]master."

[120-015]"Will you let me return to-morrow,--just for a few days,--and then

[120-016]you can talk to him as you please. I did not mean to come to stay. I

[120-017]wished him good-bye because I knew that I should not meet him here."

[120-018]"You always talk of going away, Dorothy, as soon as ever you are in

[120-019]the house. You are always threatening me."

[120-020]"I will come again, the moment you tell me. If he goes in the

[120-021]morning, I will be here the same evening. And I will write to him,

[120-022]Aunt Stanbury, and tell him,--that he is--quite free,--quite

[120-023]free,--quite free."

[120-024]Miss Stanbury made no reply to this, but sat, still playing with her

[120-025]niece's hair. "I think I will go to bed," she said at last. "It is

[120-026]past ten. You need not go to Nuncombe, Dorothy. Martha shall meet

[120-027]him, and he can see me here. But I do not wish him to stay in the

[120-028]house. You can go over and call on Mrs. MacHugh. Mrs. MacHugh will

[120-029]take it well of you that you should call on her." Dorothy made no

[120-030]further opposition to this arrangement, but kissed her aunt, and went

[120-031]to her chamber.

[120-032]How was it all to be for her? For the last two days she had been

[120-033]radiant with new happiness. Everything had seemed to be settled. Her

[120-034]lover, in his high-handed way, had declared that in no important

[120-035]crisis of life would he allow himself to be driven out of his way

[120-036]by the fear of what an old woman might do in her will. When Dorothy

[120-037]assured him that not for worlds would she, though she loved him

[120-038]dearly, injure his material prospects, he had thrown it all aside,

[120-039]after a grand fashion, that had really made the girl think that

[120-040]all Miss Stanbury's money was as nothing to his love for her. She

[120-041]and Priscilla and her mother had been carried away so entirely by

[120-042]Brooke's oratory as to feel for the time that the difficulties were

[120-043]entirely conquered. But now the aspect of things was so different!

[120-044]Whatever Brooke might owe to Miss Stanbury, she, Dorothy, owed her

[120-045]aunt everything. She would immolate herself,--if Brooke would only

[120-046]let her. She did not quite understand her aunt's stubborn opposition;

[120-047]but she knew that there was some great cause for her aunt's feeling

[120-048]on the matter. There had been a promise made, or an oath sworn, that

[120-049]the [[money|property]] of the Burgess family should not go into the hands of

[120-050]any Stanbury. Dorothy told herself that, were she married, she would

[120-051]be a Stanbury no longer;--that her aunt would still comply with the

[120-052][[duty|obligation]] she had fixed for herself; but, nevertheless, she was

[120-053]ready to believe that her aunt might be right. Her aunt had always

[120-054]declared that it should be so; and Dorothy, knowing this, confessed

[120-055]to herself that she should have kept her heart under better control.

[120-056]Thinking of these things, she went to the table, where paper and ink

[120-057]and pens had all been prepared for her so prettily, and began her

[120-058]letter to Brooke. "Dearest, dearest Brooke." But then she thought

[120-059]that this was not a fair keeping of her promise, and she began again.

[120-060]"My dear Brooke." The letter, however, did not get itself written

[120-061]that night. It was almost impossible for her to write it. "I think it

[120-062]will be better for you," she had tried to say, "to be guided by my

[120-063]aunt." But how could she say this when she did not believe it? It was

[120-064]her wish to make him understand that she would never think ill of

[120-065]him, for a moment, if he would make up his mind to abandon her;--but

[120-066]she could not find the words to express herself,--and she went, at

[120-067]last, to bed, leaving the half-covered paper upon the table.

[120-068]She went to bed, and cried herself to sleep. It had been so sweet

[120-069]to have a lover,--a man of her own, to whom she could say what she

[120-070]pleased, from whom she had a right to ask for counsel and protection,

[120-071]a man who delighted to be near her, and to make much of her. In

[120-072]comparison with her old mode of living, her old ideas of life, her

[120-073]life with such a lover was passed in an elysium. She had entered from

[120-074]barren lands into so rich a paradise! But there is no paradise, as

[120-075]she now found, without apples which must be eaten, and which lead

[120-076]to sorrow. She regretted in this hour that she had ever seen Brooke

[120-077]Burgess. After all, with her aunt's love and care for her, with

[120-078]her mother and sister near her, with the respect of those who knew

[120-079]her, why should the lands have been barren, even had there been no

[120-080]entrance for her into that elysium? And did it not all result in

[120-081]this,--that the elysium to be desired should not be here; that the

[120-082]paradise, without the apples, must be waited for till beyond the

[120-083]grave? It is when things go badly with us here, and for most of us

[120-084]only then, that we think that we can see through the dark clouds into

[120-085]the joys of heaven. But at last she slept, and in her dreams Brooke

[120-086]was sitting with her in Niddon Park with his arm tight clasped round

[120-087]her waist.

[120-088]She slept so soundly, that when a step crept silently into her room,

[120-089]and when a light was held for awhile over her face, neither the step

[120-090]nor the light awakened her. She was lying with her head back upon the

[120-091]pillow, and her arm hung by the bedside, and her lips were open, and

[120-092]her loose hair was spread upon the pillow. The person who stood there

[120-093]with the light thought that there never had been a fairer sight.

[120-094]Everything there was so pure, so sweet, so good! She was one whose

[120-095]only selfish happiness could come to her from the belief that others

[120-096]loved her. The step had been very soft, and even the breath of the

[120-097]intruder was not allowed to pass heavily into the air, but the light

[120-098]of the candle shone upon the eyelids of the sleeper, and she moved

[120-099]her head restlessly on the pillow. "Dorothy, are you awake? Can you

[120-100]speak to me?"

[120-101]Then the disturbed girl gradually opened her eyes and gazed upwards,

[120-102]and raised herself in her bed, and sat wondering. "Is anything the

[120-103]matter, aunt?" she said.

[120-104]"Only the vagaries of an old woman, my pet,--of an old woman who

[120-105]cannot sleep in her bed."

[120-106][Illustration: "Only the vagaries of an old woman."]

[120-107]"But what is it, aunt?"

[120-108]"Kiss me, dearest." Then with something of slumber still about her,

[120-109]Dorothy raised herself in her bed, and placed her arm on her aunt's

[120-110]shoulder and embraced her. "And now for my news," said Miss Stanbury.

[120-111]"What news, aunt? It isn't morning yet; is it?"

[120-112]"No;--it is not morning. You shall sleep again presently. I have

[120-113]thought of it, and you shall be Brooke's wife, and I will have it

[120-114]here, and we will all be friends."

[120-115]"What!"

[120-116]"You will like that;--will you not?"

[120-117]"And you will not quarrel with him? What am I to say? What am I to

[120-118]do?" She was, in truth, awake now, and, not knowing what she did, she

[120-119]jumped out of bed, and stood holding her aunt by the arm.

[120-120]"It is not a dream," said Miss Stanbury.

[120-121]"Are you sure that it is not a dream? And may he come here

[120-122]to-morrow?"

[120-123]"Of course he will come to-morrow."

[120-124]"And may I see him, Aunt Stanbury?"

[120-125]"Not if you go home, my dear."

[120-126]"But I won't go home. And will you tell him? Oh dear, oh dear! Aunt

[120-127]Stanbury, I do not think that I believe it yet."

[120-128]"You will catch cold, my dear, if you stay there trying to believe

[120-129]it. You have nothing on. Get into bed and believe it there. You will

[120-130]have time to think of it before the morning." Then Miss Stanbury went

[120-131]back to her own chamber, and Dorothy was left alone to realise her

[120-132]bliss.

[120-133]She thought of all her life for the last twelve months,--of the

[120-134]first invitation to [[exeter|Exeter]], and the doubts of the family as to

[120-135]its acceptance, of her arrival and of her own doubts as to the

[120-136]possibility of her remaining, of Mr. Gibson's courtship and her

[120-137]aunt's disappointment, of Brooke's coming, of her love and of

[120-138]his,--and then of her departure back to Nuncombe. After that had come

[120-139]the triumph of Brooke's visit, and then the terrible sadness of her

[120-140]aunt's displeasure. But now everything was good and glorious. She

[120-141]did not care for money herself. She thought that she never could

[120-142]care much for being rich. But had she made Brooke poor by marrying

[120-143]him, that must always have been to her matter of regret, if not of

[120-144]remorse. But now it was all to be smooth and sweet. Now a paradise

[120-145]was to be opened to her, with no apples which she might not eat;--no

[120-146]apples which might not, but still must, be eaten. She thought that it

[120-147]would be impossible that she should sleep again that night; but she

[120-148]did sleep, and dreamed that Brooke was holding her in Niddon Park,

[120-149]tighter than ever.

[120-150]When the morning came she trembled as she walked down into the

[120-151]parlour. Might it not still be possible that it was all a dream? Or

[120-152]what if her aunt should again have changed her purpose? But the first

[120-153]moment of her aunt's presence told her that there was nothing to

[120-154]fear. "How did you sleep, Dorothy?" said the old lady.

[120-155]"Dear aunt, I do not know. Was it all sleep?"

[120-156]"What shall we say to Brooke when he comes?"

[120-157]"You shall tell him."

[120-158]"No, dearest, you must tell him. And you must say to him that if he

[120-159]is not good to my girl, and does not love her always, and cling to

[120-160]her, and keep her from harm, and be in truth her loving husband, I

[120-161]will hold him to be the most ungrateful of human beings." And before

[120-162]Brooke came, she spoke again. "I wonder whether he thinks you as

[120-163]pretty as I do, Dolly?"

[120-164]"He never said that he thought me pretty at all."

[120-165]"Did he not? Then he shall say so, or he shall not have you. It was

[120-166]your looks won me first, Dolly,--like an old fool as I am. It is so

[120-167]pleasant to have a little nature after such a deal of artifice." In

[120-168]which latter remarks it was quite understood that Miss Stanbury was

[120-169]alluding to her enemies at Heavitree.

[120-170]CHAPTER LXXIV.

[120-171]THE LIONESS AROUSED.

[120-172]Brooke Burgess had been to Exeter and had gone,--for he only remained

[120-173]there one night,--and everything was apparently settled. It was not

[120-174]exactly told through Exeter that Miss Stanbury's heir was to be

[120-175]allowed to marry Miss Stanbury's niece; but Martha knew it, and Giles

[120-176]Hickbody guessed it, and Dorothy was allowed to tell her mother and

[120-177]sister, and Brooke himself, in his own careless way, had mentioned

[120-178]the matter to his uncle Barty. As Miss Stanbury had also told the

[120-179]secret in confidence to Mrs. MacHugh, it cannot be said that it was

[120-180]altogether well kept. Four days after Brooke's departure the news

[120-181]reached the Frenches at Heavitree. It was whispered to Camilla by

[120-182]one of the shopmen with whom she was still arranging her marriage

[120-183]trousseau, and was repeated by her to her mother and sister with some

[120-184]additions which were not intended to be good-natured. "He gets her

[120-185]and the money together as a bargain--of course," said Camilla. "I

[120-186]only hope the money won't be found too dear."
