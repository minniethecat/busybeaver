## Section 91

[091-001]But the American Minister hardly knew how to behave himself when he

[091-002]met Mr. Glascock, or even when he was called upon to speak of him.

[091-003]Florence no doubt is a large city, and is now the capital of a great

[091-004]kingdom; but still people meet in Florence much more frequently

[091-005]than they do in Paris or in [[london|London]]. It may almost be said that they

[091-006]whose habit it is to go into society, and whose circumstances bring

[091-007]them into the same circles, will see each other every day. Now the

[091-008]American Minister delighted to see and to be seen in all places

[091-009]frequented by persons of a certain [[social-class|rank]] and position in Florence.

[091-010]Having considered the matter much, he had convinced himself that

[091-011]he could thus best do his duty as minister from the great Republic

[091-012]of Free States to the newest and,--as he called it,--"the free-est

[091-013]of the European kingdoms." The minister from France was a marquis;

[091-014]he from England was an earl; from Spain had come a count,--and so

[091-015]on. In the domestic privacy of his embassy Mr. Spalding would be

[091-016]severe enough upon the sounding brasses and the tinkling cymbals,

[091-017]and was quite content himself to be the Honourable Jonas G.

[091-018]Spalding,--Honourable because selected by his country for a post of

[091-019]honour; but he liked to be heard among the cymbals and seen among

[091-020]the brasses, and to feel that his position was as high as theirs. Mr.

[091-021]Glascock also was frequently in the same circles, and thus it came

[091-022]to pass that the two gentlemen saw each other almost daily. That Mr.

[091-023]Spalding knew well how to bear himself in his high place no one could

[091-024]doubt; but he did not quite know how to carry himself before Mr.

[091-025]Glascock. At home at Boston he would have been more completely master

[091-026]of the situation.

[091-027]He thought too that he began to perceive that Mr. Glascock avoided

[091-028]him, though he would hear on his return home that that gentleman had

[091-029]been at the embassy, or had been walking in the Cascine with his

[091-030]nieces. That their young ladies should walk in public places with

[091-031]unmarried gentlemen is nothing to American fathers and guardians.

[091-032]American young ladies are accustomed to choose their own companions.

[091-033]But the minister was tormented by his doubts as to the ways of

[091-034]Englishmen, and as to the phase in which English habits might most

[091-035]properly exhibit themselves in Italy. He knew that people were

[091-036]talking about Mr. Glascock and his niece. Why then did Mr. Glascock

[091-037]avoid him? It was perhaps natural that Mr. Spalding should have

[091-038]omitted to observe that Mr. Glascock was not delighted by those

[091-039]lectures on the American constitution which formed so large a part of

[091-040]his ordinary conversation with Englishmen.

[091-041]It happened one afternoon that they were thrown together so closely

[091-042]for nearly an hour that neither could avoid the other. They were both

[091-043]at the old palace in which the Italian parliament is held, and were

[091-044]kept waiting during some long delay in the ceremonies of the place.

[091-045]They were seated next to each other, and during such delay there was

[091-046]nothing for them but to talk. On the other side of each of them was a

[091-047]stranger, and not to talk in such circumstances would be to quarrel.

[091-048]Mr. Glascock began by asking after the ladies.

[091-049]"They are quite well, sir, thank you," said the minister. "I hope

[091-050]that Lord Peterborough was pretty well when last you heard from

[091-051]Naples, Mr. Glascock." Mr. Glascock explained that his father's

[091-052]condition was not much altered, and then there was silence for a

[091-053]moment.

[091-054]"Your nieces will remain with you through the spring I suppose?" said

[091-055]Mr. Glascock.

[091-056]"Such is their intention, sir."

[091-057]"They seem to like Florence, I think."

[091-058]"Yes;--yes; I think they do like Florence. They see this capital,

[091-059]sir, perhaps under more favourable circumstances than are accorded

[091-060]to most of my countrywomen. Our republican simplicity, Mr. Glascock,

[091-061]has this drawback, that away from home it subjects us somewhat to the

[091-062]cold shade of unobserved obscurity. That it possesses merits which

[091-063]much more than compensate for this trifling evil I should be the last

[091-064]man in Europe to deny." It is to be observed that American citizens

[091-065]are always prone to talk of Europe. It affords the best counterpoise

[091-066]they know to that other term, America,--and America and the United

[091-067]States are of course the same. To speak of France or of England as

[091-068]weighing equally against their own country seems to an American to be

[091-069]an absurdity,--and almost an insult to himself. With Europe he can

[091-070]compare himself, but even this is done generally in the style of the

[091-071]Republican Browning when she addressed the Ancient Marbles.

[091-072]"Undoubtedly," said Mr. Glascock, "the family of a minister

[091-073]abroad has great advantages in seeing the country to which he is

[091-074]accredited."

[091-075]"That is my meaning, sir. But, as I was remarking, we carry with us

[091-076]as a people no external symbols of our [[reputation|standing]] at home. The wives

[091-077]and daughters, sir, of the most honoured of our citizens have no

[091-078]nomenclature different from that which belongs to the least noted

[091-079]among us. It is perhaps a consequence of this that Europeans who are

[091-080]accustomed in their social intercourse to the assistance of titles,

[091-081]will not always trouble themselves to inquire who and what are the

[091-082]American citizens who may sit opposite to them at table. I have

[091-083]known, Mr. Glascock, the wife and daughter of a gentleman who has

[091-084]been thrice sent as senator from his native State to Washington,

[091-085]to remain as disregarded in the intercourse of a European city, as

[091-086]though they had formed part of the family of some grocer from your

[091-087]Russell Square!"

[091-088]"Let the Miss Spaldings go where they will," said Mr. Glascock, "they

[091-089]will not fare in that way."

[091-090]"The Miss Spaldings, sir, are very much obliged to you," said the

[091-091]minister with a bow.

[091-092]"I regard it as one of the luckiest chances of my life that I was

[091-093]thrown in with them at St. Michael as I was," said Mr. Glascock with

[091-094]something like warmth.

[091-095]"I am sure, sir, they will never forget the courtesy displayed by you

[091-096]on that occasion," said the minister bowing again.

[091-097]"That was a matter of course. I and my friend would have done the

[091-098]same for the grocer's wife and daughter of whom you spoke. Little

[091-099]services such as that do not come from appreciation of merit, but are

[091-100]simply the payment of the debt due by all men to all women."

[091-101]"Such is certainly the rule of living in our country, sir," said Mr.

[091-102]Spalding.

[091-103]"The chances are," continued the Englishman, "that no further

[091-104]observation follows the payment of such a debt. It has been a thing

[091-105]of course."

[091-106]"We delight to think it so, Mr. Glascock, in our own cities."

[091-107]"But in this instance it has given rise to one of the pleasantest,

[091-108]and as I hope most enduring friendships that I have ever formed,"

[091-109]said Mr. Glascock with enthusiasm. What could the American Minister

[091-110]do but bow again three times? And what other meaning could he attach

[091-111]to such words than that which so many of his friends had been

[091-112]attributing to Mr. Glascock for some weeks past? It had occurred to

[091-113]Mr. Spalding, even since he had been sitting in his present close

[091-114]proximity to Mr. Glascock, that it might possibly be his duty as an

[091-115]uncle having to deal with an Englishman, to ask that gentleman what

[091-116]were his intentions. He would do his duty let it be what it might;

[091-117]but the asking of such a question would be very disagreeable to him.

[091-118]For the present he satisfied himself with inviting his neighbour to

[091-119]come and drink tea with Mrs. Spalding on the next evening but one.

[091-120]"The girls will be delighted, I am sure," said he, thinking himself

[091-121]to be justified in this friendly familiarity by Mr. Glascock's

[091-122]enthusiasm. For Mr. Spalding was clearly of opinion that, let the

[091-123]value of republican simplicity be what it might, an alliance with the

[091-124]crumbling marbles of Europe would in his niece's circumstances be not

[091-125]inexpedient. Mr. Glascock accepted the invitation with alacrity, and

[091-126]the minister when he was closeted with his wife that evening declared

[091-127]his opinion that after all the Britisher meant fighting. The aunt

[091-128]told the girls that Mr. Glascock was coming, and in order that it

[091-129]might not seem that a net was being specially spread for him, others

[091-130]were invited to join the party. Miss Petrie consented to be there,

[091-131]and the Italian, Count Buonarosci, to whose presence, though she

[091-132]could not speak to him, Mrs. Spalding was becoming accustomed. It

[091-133]was painful to her to feel that she could not communicate with those

[091-134]around her, and for that reason she would have avoided Italians. But

[091-135]she had an idea that she could not thoroughly realise the advantages

[091-136]of foreign travel unless she lived with foreigners; and, therefore,

[091-137]she was glad to become intimate at any rate with the outside of Count

[091-138]Buonarosci.

[091-139]"I think your uncle is wrong, dear," said Miss Petrie early in the

[091-140]day to her friend.

[091-141]"But why? He has done nothing more than what is just civil."

[091-142]"If Mr. Glascock kept a store in Broadway he would not have thought

[091-143]it necessary to shew the same civility."

[091-144]"Yes;--if we all liked the Mr. Glascock who kept the store."

[091-145]"Caroline," said the poetess with severe eloquence, "can you put your

[091-146]hand upon your heart and say that this inherited title, this tinkling

[091-147]cymbal as I call it, has no attraction for you or yours? Is it the

[091-148]unadorned simple man that you welcome to your bosom, or a thing of

[091-149]stars and garters, a patch of parchment, the minion of a throne,

[091-150]the lordling of twenty descents, in which each has been weaker than

[091-151]that before it, the hero of a scutcheon, whose glory is in his

[091-152]quarterings, and whose worldly [[money|wealth]] comes from the sweat of serfs

[091-153]whom the euphonism of an effete country has learned to decorate with

[091-154]the name of tenants?"

[091-155]But Caroline Spalding had a spirit of her own, and had already made

[091-156]up her mind that she would not be talked down by Miss Petrie. "Uncle

[091-157]Jonas," said she, "asks him because we like him; and would do so

[091-158]too if he kept the store in Broadway. But if he did keep the store

[091-159]perhaps we should not like him."

[091-160]"I trow not," said Miss Petrie.

[091-161]Livy was much more comfortable in her tactics, and without consulting

[091-162]anybody sent for a hairdresser. "It's all very well for Wallachia,"

[091-163]said Livy,--Miss Petrie's name was Wallachia,--"but I know a nice

[091-164]sort of man when I see him, and the ways of the world are not to be

[091-165]altered because Wally writes poetry."

[091-166]When Mr. Glascock was announced Mrs. Spalding's handsome rooms were

[091-167]almost filled, as rooms in Florence are filled,--obstruction in every

[091-168]avenue, a crowd in every corner, and a block at every doorway, not

[091-169]being among the customs of the place. Mr. Spalding immediately caught

[091-170]him,--intercepting him between the passages and the ladies,--and

[091-171]engaged him at once in conversation.

[091-172]"Your John S. Mill is a great man," said the minister.

[091-173]"They tell me so," said Mr. Glascock. "I don't read what he writes

[091-174]myself."

[091-175]This acknowledgment seemed to the minister to be almost disgraceful,

[091-176]and yet he himself had never read a word of Mr. Mill's writings.

[091-177]"He is a far-seeing man," continued the minister. "He is one of

[091-178]the few Europeans who can look forward, and see how the rivers of

[091-179]civilization are running on. He has understood that women must at

[091-180]last be put upon an equality with men."

[091-181]"Can he manage that men shall have half the babies?" said Mr.

[091-182]Glascock, thinking to escape by an attempt at playfulness.

[091-183]But the minister was down upon him at once,--had him by the lappet

[091-184]of his coat, though he knew how important it was for his dear niece

[091-185]that he should allow Mr. Glascock to amuse himself this evening after

[091-186]another fashion. "I have an answer ready, sir, for that difficulty,"

[091-187]he said. "Step aside with me for a moment. The question is important,

[091-188]and I should be glad if you would communicate my ideas to your great

[091-189]philosopher. Nature, sir, has laid down certain laws, which are

[091-190]immutable; and, against them,--"
