## Section 119

[119-001]"I'll tell her the truth; and if she be very angry, I'll just come

[119-002]home again. But I think I'll come home to-morrow any way, so that

[119-003]I'll pass you on the road. That will be best. She won't want us both

[119-004]together. Only then, Brooke, I shan't see you again."

[119-005]"Not till June."

[119-006]"And is it to be really in June?"

[119-007]"You say you don't like May."

[119-008]"You are such a goose, Brooke. It will be May almost to-morrow. I

[119-009]shall be such a poor wife for you, Brooke. As for getting my things

[119-010]ready, I shall not bring hardly any things at all. Have you thought

[119-011]what it is to take a body so very poor?"

[119-012]"I own I haven't thought as much about it, Dolly,--as I ought to have

[119-013]done, perhaps."

[119-014]"It is too late now, Brooke."

[119-015]"I suppose it is."

[119-016]"Quite too late. A week ago I could have borne it. I had almost got

[119-017]myself to think that it would be better that I should bear it. But

[119-018]you have come, and banished all the virtue out of my head. I am

[119-019]ashamed of myself, because I am so unworthy; but I would put up with

[119-020]that shame rather than lose you now. Brooke, Brooke, I will so try to

[119-021]be good to you!"

[119-022]In the afternoon Martha and Dorothy started together for [[exeter|Exeter]],

[119-023]Brooke and Priscilla accompanying them as far as Mrs. Crocket's,

[119-024]where the Lessboro' fly was awaiting them. Dorothy said little or

[119-025]nothing during the walk, nor, indeed, was she very communicative

[119-026]during the journey into Exeter. She was going to her aunt, instigated

[119-027]simply by the affection of her full heart; but she was going with a

[119-028]tale in her mouth which she knew would be very unwelcome. She could

[119-029]not save herself from feeling that, in having accepted Brooke, and in

[119-030]having not only accepted him but even fixed the day for her marriage,

[119-031]she had been ungrateful to her aunt. Had it not been for her aunt's

[119-032]kindness and hospitality, she would never have seen Brooke Burgess.

[119-033]And as she had been under her aunt's care at Exeter, she doubted

[119-034]whether she had not been guilty of some great fault in falling in

[119-035]love with this man, in opposition as it were to express orders.

[119-036]Should her aunt still declare that she would in no way countenance

[119-037]the marriage, that she would still oppose it and use her influence

[119-038]with Brooke to break it off, then would Dorothy return on the morrow

[119-039]to her mother's cottage at [[nuncombe-putney|Nuncombe Putney]], so that her lover might

[119-040]be free to act with her aunt as he might think fit. And should he

[119-041]yield, she would endeavour,--she would struggle hard, to think that

[119-042]he was still acting for the best. "I must tell her myself, Martha,"

[119-043]said Dorothy, as they came near to Exeter.

[119-044]"Certainly, miss;--only you'll do it to-night."

[119-045]"Yes;--at once. As soon after I get there as possible."

[119-046]CHAPTER LXXIII.

[119-047]DOROTHY RETURNS TO EXETER.

[119-048]Miss Stanbury perfectly understood that Martha was to come back by

[119-049]the train reaching Exeter at 7 p.m., and that she might be expected

[119-050]in the Close about a quarter-of-an-hour after that time. She had been

[119-051]nervous and anxious all day,--so much so that Mr. Martin had told

[119-052]her that she must be very careful. "That's all very well," the old

[119-053]woman had said, "but you haven't got any medicine for my complaint,

[119-054]Mr. Martin." The apothecary had assured her that the worst of her

[119-055]complaint was in the east wind, and had gone away begging her to be

[119-056]very careful. "It is not God's breezes that are hard to any one," the

[119-057]old lady had said to herself,--"but our own hearts." After her lonely

[119-058]dinner she had fidgeted about the room, and had rung twice for the

[119-059]girl, not knowing what order to give when the servant came to her.

[119-060]She was very anxious about her tea, but would not have it brought to

[119-061]her till after Martha should have arrived. She was half-minded to

[119-062]order that a second cup and saucer should be placed there, but she

[119-063]had not the courage to face the disappointment which would fall upon

[119-064]her, should the cup and saucer stand there for no purpose. And yet,

[119-065]should she come, how nice it would be to shew her girl that her old

[119-066]aunt had been ready for her. Thrice she went to the window after the

[119-067]cathedral clock had struck seven, to see whether her ambassador was

[119-068]returning. From her window there was only one very short space of

[119-069]pathway on which she could have seen her,--and, as it happened, there

[119-070]came the ring at the door, and no ambassador had as yet been viewed.

[119-071]Miss Stanbury was immediately off her seat, and out upon the landing.

[119-072]"Here we are again, Miss Dorothy," said Martha. Then Miss Stanbury

[119-073]could not restrain herself,--but descended the stairs, moving as she

[119-074]had never moved since she had first been ill. "My bairn," she said;

[119-075]"my dearest bairn! I thought that perhaps it might be so. Jane,

[119-076]another tea-cup and saucer up-stairs." What a pity that she had not

[119-077]ordered it before! "And get a hot cake, Jane. You will be ever so

[119-078]hungry, my darling, after your journey."

[119-079]"Are you glad to see me, Aunt Stanbury?" said Dorothy.

[119-080]"Glad, my pretty one!" Then she put up her hands, and smoothed down

[119-081]the girl's cheeks, and kissed her, and patted Martha on the back, and

[119-082]scolded her at the same time for not bringing Miss Dorothy from the

[119-083][[social-class|station]] in a cab. "And what is the meaning of that little bag?" she

[119-084]said. "You shall go back for the rest yourself, Martha, because it is

[119-085]your own fault." Martha knew that all this was pleasant enough;--but

[119-086]then her mistress's moods would sometimes be changed so suddenly! How

[119-087]would it be when Miss Stanbury knew that Brooke Burgess had been left

[119-088]behind at Nuncombe Putney?

[119-089]"You see I didn't stay to eat any of the lamb," said Dorothy,

[119-090]smiling.

[119-091]"You shall have a calf instead, my dear," said Miss Stanbury,

[119-092]"because you are a returned prodigal."

[119-093]All this was very pleasant, and Miss Stanbury was so happy dispensing

[119-094]her tea, and the hot cake, and the clotted cream, and was so intent

[119-095]upon her little methods of caressing and petting her niece, that

[119-096]Dorothy had no heart to tell her story while the plates and cups were

[119-097]still upon the table. She had not, perhaps, cared much for the hot

[119-098]cake, having such a weight upon her mind, but she had seemed to care,

[119-099]understanding well that she might so best conduce to her aunt's

[119-100]comfort. Miss Stanbury was a woman who could not bear that the good

[119-101]things which she had provided for a guest should not be enjoyed. She

[119-102]could taste with a friend's palate, and drink with a friend's throat.

[119-103]But when debarred these vicarious pleasures by what seemed to her to

[119-104]be the caprice of her guests, she would be offended. It had been one

[119-105]of the original sins of Camilla and Arabella French that they would

[119-106]declare at her tea-table that they had dined late and could not eat

[119-107]tea-cake. Dorothy knew all this,--and did her duty;--but with a

[119-108]heavy heart. There was the story to be told, and she had promised

[119-109]Martha that it should be told to-night. She was quite aware, too,

[119-110]independently of her promise, that it was necessary that it should

[119-111]be told to-night. It was very sad,--very grievous that the dear old

[119-112]lady's happiness should be disturbed so soon; but it must be done.

[119-113]When the tea-things were being taken away her aunt was still purring

[119-114]round her, and saying gentle, loving words. Dorothy bore it as well

[119-115]as she could,--bore it well, smiling and kissing her aunt's hand, and

[119-116]uttering now and then some word of affection. But the thing had to be

[119-117]done; and as soon as the room was quiet for a moment, she jumped up

[119-118]from her chair and began. "Aunt Stanbury, I must tell you something

[119-119]at once. Who, do you think, is at Nuncombe Putney?"

[119-120]"Not Brooke Burgess?"

[119-121]"Yes, he is. He is there now, and is to be here with you to-morrow."

[119-122]The whole colour and [[honour|character]] of Miss Stanbury's face was changed in

[119-123]a moment. She had been still purring up to the moment in which this

[119-124]communication had been made to her. Her gratification had come to her

[119-125]from the idea that her pet had come back to her from love of her,--as

[119-126]in very truth had been the case; but now it seemed that Dorothy had

[119-127]returned to ask for a great favour for herself. And she reflected at

[119-128]once that Brooke had passed through Exeter without seeing her. If

[119-129]he was determined to marry without reference to her, he might at

[119-130]any rate have had the grace to come to her and say so. She, in the

[119-131]fulness of her heart, had written words of affection to Dorothy;--and

[119-132]both Dorothy and Brooke had at once taken advantage of her

[119-133]expressions for their own purposes. Such was her reading of the story

[119-134]of the day. "He need not trouble himself to come here now," she said.

[119-135]"Dear aunt, do not say that."

[119-136]"I do say it. He need not trouble himself to come now. When I said

[119-137]that I should be glad to see you, I did not intend that you should

[119-138]meet Mr. Burgess under my roof. I did not wish to have you both

[119-139]together."

[119-140]"How could I help coming, when you wrote to me like that?"

[119-141]"It is very well,--but he need not come. He knows the way from

[119-142]Nuncombe to [[london|London]] without stopping at Exeter."

[119-143]"Aunt Stanbury, you must let me tell it you all."

[119-144]"There is no more to tell, I should think."

[119-145]"But there is more. You knew what he thought about me, and what he

[119-146]wished."

[119-147]"He is his own master, my dear;--and you are your own mistress."

[119-148]"If you speak to me like that you will kill me, Aunt Stanbury. I did

[119-149]not think of coming; only when Martha brought your dear letter I

[119-150]could not help it. But he was coming. He meant to come to-morrow,

[119-151]and he will. Of course he must defend himself, if you are angry with

[119-152]him."

[119-153]"He need not defend himself at all."

[119-154]"I told them, and I told him, that I would only stay one night,--if

[119-155]you did not wish that we should be here together. You must see him,

[119-156]Aunt Stanbury. You would not refuse to see him."

[119-157]"If you please, my dear, you must allow me to judge whom I will see."

[119-158]After that the discussion ceased between them for awhile, and Miss

[119-159]Stanbury left the room that she might hold a consultation with

[119-160]Martha. Dorothy went up to her chamber, and saw that everything had

[119-161]been prepared for her with most scrupulous care. Nothing could be

[119-162]whiter, neater, cleaner, nicer than was everything that surrounded

[119-163]her. She had perceived while living under her aunt's roof, how,

[119-164]gradually, small, delicate feminine comforts had been increased for

[119-165]her. Martha had been told that Miss Dorothy ought to have this, and

[119-166]that Miss Dorothy ought to have that; till at last she, who had

[119-167]hitherto known nothing of the small luxuries that come from an easy

[119-168][[money|income]], had felt ashamed of the prettinesses that had been added to

[119-169]her. Now she could see at once that infinite care had been used to

[119-170]make her room bright and smiling,--only in the hope that she would

[119-171]return. As soon as she saw it all, she sat down on her bed and burst

[119-172]out into tears. Was it not hard upon her that she should be forced

[119-173]into such ingratitude! Every comfort prepared for her was a coal of

[119-174]hot fire upon her head. And yet what had she done that she ought not

[119-175]to have done? Was it unreasonable that she should have loved this

[119-176]man, when they two were brought together? And had she even dared to

[119-177]think of him otherwise than as an acquaintance till he had compelled

[119-178]her to confess her love? And after that had she not tried to separate

[119-179]herself from him, so that they two,--her aunt and her lover,--might

[119-180]be divided by no quarrel? Had not Priscilla told her that she was

[119-181]right in all that she was doing? Nevertheless, in spite of all this,

[119-182]she could not refrain from accusing herself of ingratitude towards

[119-183]her aunt. And she began to think it would have been better for her

[119-184]now to have remained at home, and have allowed Brooke to come alone

[119-185]to Exeter than to have obeyed the impulse which had arisen from the

[119-186]receipt of her aunt's letter. When she went down again she found

[119-187]herself alone in the room, and she was beginning to think that it was

[119-188]intended that she should go to bed without again seeing her aunt;

[119-189]but at last Miss Stanbury came to her, with a sad countenance, but

[119-190]without that look of wrath which Dorothy knew so well. "My dear,"

[119-191]she said, "it will be better that Mr. Burgess should go up to London

[119-192]to-morrow. I will see him, of course, if he chooses to come, and

[119-193]Martha shall meet him at the station and explain it. If you do not

[119-194]mind, I would prefer that you should not meet him here."
