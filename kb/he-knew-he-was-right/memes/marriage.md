# Marriage

**Marriage** is the central thematic institution of [[Anthony Trollope]]'s *He Knew He Was Right* (1869). The novel examines marriage not as a stable given but as a precarious negotiation — of power, autonomy, trust, and legal obligation — set against the rigid conventions of Victorian England. Through the catastrophic dissolution of the [[Louis Trevelyan|Trevelyan]] union, set alongside the successful courtships of [[Nora Rowley]] and [[Hugh Stanbury]], and of [[Dorothy Stanbury]] and [[Brooke Burgess|Mr. Brooke Burgess]], Trollope conducts an extended inquiry into what makes a marriage viable and what destroys it.

## Victorian Marriage Law and Custom

The novel's marital crisis unfolds within a specific legal framework. Under Victorian law, a wife had no independent legal existence — her property, her children, and her body belonged to her husband. The Matrimonial Causes Act of 1857 had made divorce possible but remained profoundly asymmetrical: a husband could divorce a wife for adultery alone, while a wife had to prove adultery *plus* cruelty, desertion, or incest.

This legal context makes [[Emily Trevelyan]]'s resistance to her husband's demands all the more striking. She has no legal recourse; she can only appeal to his reason and, failing that, to public opinion — a gamble that proves devastating.

## The Trevelyan Marriage

The [[Louis Trevelyan|Trevelyan]] marriage serves as the novel's central case study in marital failure. What makes the portrait devastating is its ordinariness: the Trevelyans begin as an ideal couple, deeply in love, with every material advantage. Their destruction comes not from external catastrophe but from internal dynamics — jealousy, pride, and the fatal inability of either partner to yield.

> "But Emily likes her way too," said Lady Rowley. [001-231]

This brief observation from Emily's mother captures the novel's diagnosis: the Trevelyan marriage fails not because one party is villainous and the other innocent, but because both are constitutionally incapable of the mutual accommodation that marriage requires. Louis insists on absolute obedience; Emily insists on absolute vindication. Between these two absolutes, the marriage is crushed.

## Counterpoint: Successful Unions

Trollope structures the novel so that healthy marriages provide contrast to the Trevelyan catastrophe:

| Couple | Dynamic | Lesson |
|--------|---------|--------|
| [[Nora Rowley]] & [[Hugh Stanbury]] | Mutual respect, shared values, patience | Love flourishes where neither partner demands submission |
| [[Dorothy Stanbury]] & Brooke Burgess | Gentleness, gratitude, domestic harmony | Marriage as partnership, not conquest |
| Sir Marmaduke & Lady Rowley | Established, stable, mutually supportive | The "normal" Victorian marriage that the Trevelyans fail to achieve |

## Themes

### Power and Autonomy

The novel's marriage critique centres on power. Louis Trevelyan's jealousy is, at root, a demand for total control — over his wife's friendships, movements, and inner life. Emily's resistance is a refusal to cede her autonomy. The novel asks whether marriage, as constituted in Victorian England, can accommodate two autonomous selves, and finds the answer deeply troubling.

### Trust and Jealousy

Marriage in the novel cannot survive without trust. Trevelyan's suspicion, once kindled, consumes everything — his love for Emily, his relationship with his son, his friendships, his sanity. The novel argues that the legal powers of a Victorian husband, when combined with a jealous temperament, produce a tyranny from which the wife has no escape.

### Public vs. Private

The Trevelyan marriage is destroyed as much by its exposure to public scrutiny as by its internal dynamics. Gossip, newspaper commentary, and the interventions of well-meaning relatives and friends all accelerate the catastrophe. The novel is sceptical about whether marriage can be a truly private institution in a society that polices it so intensely.
