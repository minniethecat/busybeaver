## Section 156

[156-001]On that other matter, touching [[emily-trevelyan|Emily Trevelyan]], Lady Milborough

[156-002]frankly owned that she had seen early in the day that he was the one

[156-003]most in fault. "I must say, my dear," she said, "that I very greatly

[156-004]dislike your friend, [[colonel-osborne|Colonel Osborne]]."

[156-005]"I am sure that he meant not the slightest harm,--no more than she

[156-006]did."

[156-007]"He was old enough, and ought to have known better. And when the

[156-008]first hint of an uneasiness in the mind of Louis was suggested to

[156-009]him, his feelings as a [[social-class|gentleman]] should have prompted him to remove

[156-010]himself. Let the suspicion have been ever so absurd, he should

[156-011]have removed himself. Instead of that, he went after her,--into

[156-012]Devonshire."

[156-013]"He went to see other friends, Lady Milborough."

[156-014]"I hope it may have been so;--I hope it may have been so. But he

[156-015]should have cut off his hand before he rang at the door of the house

[156-016]in which she was living. You will understand, my dear, that I acquit

[156-017]your sister altogether. I did so all through, and said the same to

[156-018]poor Louis when he came to me. But Colonel Osborne should have known

[156-019]better. Why did he write to her? Why did he go to St. Diddulph's? Why

[156-020]did he let it be thought that,--that she was especially his friend.

[156-021]Oh dear; oh dear; oh dear! I am afraid he is a very bad man."

[156-022]"We had known him so long, Lady Milborough."

[156-023]"I wish you had never known him at all. Poor Louis! If he had only

[156-024]done what I told him at first, all might have been well. 'Go to

[156-025]Naples, with your wife,' I said. 'Go to Naples.' If he had gone to

[156-026]Naples, there would have been no journeys to Siena, no living at

[156-027]Casalunga, no separation. But he didn't seem to see it in the same

[156-028]light. Poor dear Louis. I wish he had gone to Naples when I told

[156-029]him."

[156-030]While they were going backwards and forwards, looking at the cottage

[156-031]at Twickenham and trying to make things comfortable there for the

[156-032]sick man, Lady Milborough hinted to Nora that it might be distasteful

[156-033]to Trevelyan, in his present condition, to have even a sister-in-law

[156-034]staying in the house with him. There was a little chamber which Nora

[156-035]had appropriated to herself, and at first it seemed to be taken

[156-036]for granted that she should remain there at least till the 10th of

[156-037]August, on which day Lady Peterborough had signified that she and her

[156-038]husband would be ready to receive their visitor. But Lady Milborough

[156-039]slept on the suggestion, and on the next morning hinted her

[156-040]disapprobation. "You shall take them down in the carriage, and their

[156-041]luggage can follow in a cab;--but the carriage can bring you back.

[156-042]You will see how things are then."

[156-043]"Dear Lady Milborough, you would go out of town at once if I left

[156-044]you."

[156-045]"And I shall not go out of town if you don't leave me. What

[156-046]difference does it make to an old woman like me? I have got no

[156-047]lover coming to look for me, and all I have to do is to tell my

[156-048]daughter-in-law that I shall not be there for another week or so.

[156-049]Augusta is very glad to have me, but she is the wisest woman in the

[156-050]world, and can get on very well without me."

[156-051]"And as I am the silliest, I cannot."

[156-052]"You shall put it in that way if you like it, my dear. Girls in your

[156-053]position often do want assistance. I dare say you think me very

[156-054]straight-laced, but I am quite sure Mr. Stanbury will be grateful to

[156-055]me. As you are to be married from Monkhams, it will be quite well

[156-056]that you should pass thither through my house as an intermediate

[156-057]resting-place, after leaving your father and mother." By all which

[156-058]Lady Milborough intended to express an opinion that the value of

[156-059]the article which [[hugh-stanbury|Hugh Stanbury]] would receive at the altar would be

[156-060]enhanced by the distinguished purity of the hands through which it

[156-061]had passed before it came into his possession;--in which opinion she

[156-062]was probably right as regarded the price put upon the article by

[156-063]the world at large, though it may perhaps be doubted whether the

[156-064]recipient himself would be of the same opinion.

[156-065]"I hope you know that I am grateful, whatever he may be," said Nora,

[156-066]after a pause.

[156-067]"I think that you take it as it is meant, and that makes me quite

[156-068]comfortable."

[156-069]"Lady Milborough, I shall love you for ever and ever. I don't think I

[156-070]ever knew anybody so good as you are,--or so nice."

[156-071]"Then I shall be more than comfortable," said Lady Milborough. After

[156-072]that there was an embrace, and the thing was settled.

[156-073]CHAPTER XCV.

[156-074]TREVELYAN BACK IN ENGLAND.

[156-075]Nora, with Lady Milborough's carriage, and Lady Milborough's coach

[156-076]and footman, and with a cab ready for the luggage close behind the

[156-077]carriage, was waiting at the railway station when the party from

[156-078]Dover arrived. She soon saw Hugh upon the platform, and ran to

[156-079]him with her news. They had not a word to say to each other of

[156-080]themselves, so anxious were they both respecting Trevelyan. "We got a

[156-081]bed-carriage for him at Dover," said Hugh; "and I think he has borne

[156-082]the journey pretty well;--but he feels the heat almost as badly as

[156-083]in Italy. You will hardly know him when you see him." Then, when the

[156-084]rush of passengers was gone, Trevelyan was brought out by Hugh and

[156-085]the courier, and placed in Lady Milborough's carriage. He just smiled

[156-086]as his eye fell upon Nora, but he did not even put out his hand to

[156-087]greet her.

[156-088]"I am to go in the carriage with him," said his wife.

[156-089]"Of course you are,--and so will I and Louey. I think there will be

[156-090]room: it is so large. There is a cab for all the things. Dear Emily,

[156-091]I am so glad to see you."

[156-092]"Dearest Nora! I shall be able to speak to you by-and-by, but you

[156-093]must not be angry with me now. How good you have been."

[156-094]"Has not she been good? I don't understand about the cottage. It

[156-095]belongs to some friend of hers; and I have not been able to say a

[156-096]word about the rent. It is so nice;--and looks upon the river. I hope

[156-097]that he will like it."

[156-098]"You will be with us?"

[156-099]"Not just at first. Lady Milborough thinks I had better not,--that he

[156-100]will like it better. I will come down almost every day, and will stay

[156-101]if you think he will like it."

[156-102]These few words were said while the men were putting Trevelyan

[156-103]into the carriage. And then another arrangement was made. Hugh

[156-104]hired a second cab, in which he and the courier made a part of the

[156-105]procession; and so they all went to Twickenham together. Hugh had not

[156-106]yet learned that he would be rewarded by coming back alone with Nora

[156-107]in the carriage.

[156-108]The cottage by the River Thames, which, as far as the party knew, was

[156-109]nameless, was certainly very much better than the house on the top of

[156-110]the hill at Casalunga. And now, at last, the wife would sleep once

[156-111]more under the same roof with her husband, and the separation would

[156-112]be over. "I suppose that is the Thames," said Trevelyan; and they

[156-113]were nearly the only words he spoke in Nora's hearing that evening.

[156-114]Before she started on her return journey, the two sisters were

[156-115]together for a few minutes, and each told her own budget of news in

[156-116]short, broken fragments. There was not much to tell. "He is so weak,"

[156-117]said Mrs. Trevelyan, "that he can do literally nothing. He can hardly

[156-118]speak. When we give him wine, he will say a few words, and his mind

[156-119]seems then to be less astray than it was. I have told him just simply

[156-120]that it was all my doing,--that I have been in fault all through, and

[156-121]every now and then he will say a word, to shew me that he remembers

[156-122]that I have confessed."

[156-123]"My poor Emily!"

[156-124]"It was better so. What does it all matter? He had suffered so, that

[156-125]I would have said worse than that to give him relief. The pride has

[156-126]gone out of me so, that I do not regard what anybody may say. Of

[156-127]course, it will be said that I--went astray, and that he forgave me."

[156-128]"Nobody will say that, dearest; nobody. Lady Milborough is quite

[156-129]aware how it all was."

[156-130]"What does it signify? There are things in life worse even than a bad

[156-131]name."

[156-132]"But he does not think it?"

[156-133]"Nora, his mind is a mystery to me. I do not know what is in it.

[156-134]Sometimes I fancy that all facts have been forgotten, and that he

[156-135]merely wants the childish gratification of being assured that he is

[156-136]the master. Then, again, there come moments, in which I feel sure

[156-137]that suspicion is lurking within him, that he is remembering the

[156-138]past, and guarding against the future. When he came into this house,

[156-139]a quarter of an hour ago, he was fearful lest there was a mad doctor

[156-140]lurking about to pounce on him. I can see in his eye that he had some

[156-141]such idea. He hardly notices Louey,--though there was a time, even at

[156-142]Casalunga, when he would not let the child out of his sight."

[156-143]"What will you do now?"

[156-144]"I will try to do my duty;--that is all."

[156-145]"But you will have a doctor?"

[156-146]"Of course. He was content to see one in Paris, though he would not

[156-147]let me be present. Hugh saw the gentleman afterwards, and he seemed

[156-148]to think that the body was worse than the mind." Then Nora told her

[156-149]the name of a doctor whom Lady Milborough had suggested, and took her

[156-150]departure along with Hugh in the carriage.

[156-151]In spite of all the sorrow that they had witnessed and just left,

[156-152]their journey up to [[london|London]] was very pleasant. Perhaps there is no

[156-153]period so pleasant among all the pleasant periods of love-making as

[156-154]that in which the intimacy between the lovers is so assured, and the

[156-155]coming event so near, as to produce and to endure conversation about

[156-156]the ordinary little matters of life;--what can be done with the

[156-157]limited means at their mutual disposal; how that life shall be begun

[156-158]which they are to lead together; what idea each has of the other's

[156-159]duties; what each can do for the other; what each will renounce for

[156-160]the other. There was a true sense of the delight of intimacy in the

[156-161]girl who declared that she had never loved her lover so well as when

[156-162]she told him how many pairs of stockings she had got. It is very

[156-163]sweet to gaze at the stars together; and it is sweet to sit out

[156-164]among the haycocks. The reading of poetry together, out of the same

[156-165]book, with brows all close, and arms all mingled, is very sweet. The

[156-166]pouring out of the whole heart in written words, which the writer

[156-167]knows would be held to be ridiculous by any eyes, and any ears, and

[156-168]any sense, but the eyes and ears and sense of the dear one to whom

[156-169]they are sent, is very sweet;--but for the girl who has made a shirt

[156-170]for the man that she loves, there has come a moment in the last

[156-171]stitch of it, sweeter than any that stars, haycocks, poetry, or

[156-172]superlative epithets have produced. [[nora-rowley|Nora Rowley]] had never as yet

[156-173]been thus useful on behalf of Hugh Stanbury. Had she done so, she

[156-174]might perhaps have been happier even than she was during this

[156-175]journey;--but, without the shirt, it was one of the happiest moments

[156-176]of her life. There was nothing now to separate them but their own

[156-177]prudential scruples;--and of them it must be acknowledged that Hugh

[156-178]Stanbury had very few. According to his shewing, he was as well

[156-179]provided for [[marriage|matrimony]] as the gentleman in the song, who came out

[156-180]to woo his bride on a rainy night. In live stock he was not so well

[156-181]provided as the Irish gentleman to whom we allude; but in regard to

[156-182]all other provisions for comfortable married life, he had, or at a

[156-183]moment's notice could have, all that was needed. Nora could live just

[156-184]where she pleased;--not exactly in Whitehall Gardens or Belgrave

[156-185]Square; but the New Road, Lupus Street, Montague Place, the North

[156-186]Bank, or Kennington Oval, with all their surrounding crescents,

[156-187]terraces, and rows, offered, according to him, a choice so wide,

[156-188]either for lodgings or small houses, that their only embarrassment

[156-189]was in their riches. He had already insured his life for a thousand

[156-190]pounds, and, after paying yearly for that, and providing a certain

[156-191]surplus for saving, five hundred a year was the [[money|income]] on which they

[156-192]were to commence the world. "Of course, I wish it were five thousand

[156-193]for your sake," he said; "and I wish I were a Cabinet Minister, or a

[156-194]duke, or a brewer; but, even in heaven, you know all the angels can't

[156-195]be archangels." Nora assured him that she would be quite content with

[156-196]virtues simply angelic. "I hope you like mutton-chops and potatoes; I

[156-197]do," he said. Then she told him of her ambition about the beef-steak,

[156-198]acknowledging that, as it must now be shared between two, the

[156-199]glorious idea of putting a part of it away in a cupboard must be

[156-200]abandoned. "I don't believe in beef-steaks," he said. "A beef-steak

[156-201]may mean anything. At our club, a beef-steak is a sumptuous and

[156-202]expensive luxury. Now, a mutton-chop means something definite, and

[156-203]must be economical."
