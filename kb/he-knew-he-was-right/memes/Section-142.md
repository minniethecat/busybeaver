## Section 142

[142-001]"I went to look for you but you were gone," said Mr. Glascock.

[142-002]"No, sir, I was not gone. I am here. It is the last time that I shall

[142-003]ever gladden my eyes with his brightness. Louey, my love, will you

[142-004]come to your father?" Louey did not seem to be particularly willing

[142-005]to leave the carriage, but he made no loud objection when Mr.

[142-006]Glascock held him up to the open space above the door. The child had

[142-007]realised the fact that he was to go, and did not believe that his

[142-008]father would stop him now; but he was probably of opinion that the

[142-009]sooner the carriage began to go on the better it would be for him.

[142-010]Mr. Glascock, thinking that his father intended to kiss him over the

[142-011]door, held him by his frock; but the doing of this made Trevelyan

[142-012]very angry. "Am I not to be trusted with my own child in my arms?"

[142-013]said he. "Give him to me, sir. I begin to doubt now whether I am

[142-014]right to deliver him to you." Mr. Glascock immediately let go his

[142-015]hold of the boy's frock and leaned back in the carriage. "Louey will

[142-016]tell papa that he loves him before he goes?" said Trevelyan. The poor

[142-017]little fellow murmured something, but it did not please his father,

[142-018]who had him in his arms. "You are like the rest of them, Louey," he

[142-019]said; "because I cannot laugh and be gay, all my love for you is

[142-020]nothing;--nothing! You may take him. He is all that I have;--all that

[142-021]I have;--and I shall never see him again!" So saying he handed the

[142-022]child into the carriage, and sat himself down by the side of the road

[142-023]to watch till the vehicle should be out of sight. As soon as the last

[142-024]speck of it had vanished from his sight, he picked himself up, and

[142-025]dragged his slow footsteps back to the house.

[142-026]Mr. Glascock made sundry attempts to amuse the child, with whom he

[142-027]had to remain all that night at Siena; but his efforts in that line

[142-028]were not very successful. The boy was brisk enough, and happy, and

[142-029]social by nature; but the events, or rather the want of events of

[142-030]the last few months, had so cowed him, that he could not recover his

[142-031]spirits at the bidding of a stranger. "If I have any of my own," said

[142-032]Mr. Glascock to himself, "I hope they will be of a more cheerful

[142-033]disposition."

[142-034]As we have seen, he did not meet Caroline at the [[social-class|station]],--thereby

[142-035]incurring his lady-love's displeasure for the period of

[142-036]half-a-minute; but he did meet Mrs. Trevelyan almost at the door of

[142-037]Sir Marmaduke's lodgings. "Yes, Mrs. Trevelyan; he is here."

[142-038]"How am I ever to thank you for such [[virtue|goodness]]?" said she. "And Mr.

[142-039]Trevelyan;--you saw him?"

[142-040]"Yes:--I saw him."

[142-041]Before he could answer her further she was up-stairs, and had her

[142-042]child in her arms. It seemed to be an age since the boy had been

[142-043]stolen from her in the early spring in that unknown, dingy street

[142-044]near Tottenham Court Road. Twice she had seen her darling since

[142-045]that,--twice during his captivity; but on each of these occasions

[142-046]she had seen him as one not belonging to herself, and had seen him

[142-047]under circumstances which had robbed the greeting of almost all its

[142-048]pleasure. But now he was her own again, to take whither she would,

[142-049]to dress and to undress, to feed, to coax, to teach, and to caress.

[142-050]And the child lay close up to her as she hugged him, putting up his

[142-051]little cheek to her chin, and burying himself happily in her embrace.

[142-052]He had not much as yet to say, but she could feel that he was

[142-053]contented.

[142-054]Mr. Glascock had promised to wait for her a few minutes,--even at the

[142-055]risk of Caroline's displeasure,--and Mrs. Trevelyan ran down to him

[142-056]as soon as the first craving of her mother's love was satisfied. Her

[142-057]boy would at any rate be safe with her now, and it was her duty to

[142-058]learn something of her husband. It was more than her duty;--if only

[142-059]her services might be of avail to him. "And you say he was well?"

[142-060]she asked. She had taken Mr. Glascock apart, and they were alone

[142-061]together, and he had determined that he would tell her the truth.

[142-062]"I do not know that he is ill,--though he is pale and altered beyond

[142-063]belief."

[142-064]"Yes;--I saw that."

[142-065]"I never knew a man so thin and haggard."

[142-066]"My poor Louis!"

[142-067]"But that is not the worst of it."

[142-068]"What do you mean, Mr. Glascock?"

[142-069]"I mean that his mind is astray, and that he should not be left

[142-070]alone. There is no knowing what he might do. He is so much more alone

[142-071]there than he would be in England. There is not a soul who could

[142-072]interfere."

[142-073]"Do you mean that you think--that he is in danger--from himself?"

[142-074]"I would not say so, Mrs. Trevelyan; but who can tell? I am sure of

[142-075]this,--that he should not be left alone. If it were only because of

[142-076]the misery of his life, he should not be left alone."

[142-077]"But what can I do? He would not even see papa."

[142-078]"He would see you."

[142-079]"But he would not let me guide him in anything. I have been to him

[142-080]twice, and he breaks out,--as if I were--a bad woman."

[142-081]"Let him break out. What does it matter?"

[142-082]"Am I to own to a falsehood,--and such a falsehood?"

[142-083]"Own to anything, and you will conquer him at once. That is what I

[142-084]think. You will excuse what I say, Mrs. Trevelyan."

[142-085]"Oh, Mr. Glascock, you have been such a friend! What should we have

[142-086]done without you!"

[142-087]"You cannot take to heart the words that come from a disordered

[142-088]reason. In truth, he believes no ill of you."

[142-089]"But he says so."

[142-090]"It is hard to know what he says. Declare that you will submit to

[142-091]him, and I think that he will be softened towards you. Try to bring

[142-092]him back to his own country. It may be that were he to--die there,

[142-093]alone, the memory of his loneliness would be heavy with you in after

[142-094]days." Then, having so spoken, he rushed off, declaring, with a

[142-095]forced laugh, that Caroline Spalding would never forgive him.

[142-096]The next day was the day of the wedding, and [[emily-trevelyan|Emily Trevelyan]] was

[142-097]left all alone. It was of course out of the question that she should

[142-098]join any party the purport of which was to be festive. Sir Marmaduke

[142-099]went with some grumbling, declaring that wine and severe food in the

[142-100]morning were sins against the plainest rules of life. And the three

[142-101]Rowley girls went, Nora officiating as one of the bridesmaids. But

[142-102]Mrs. Trevelyan was left with her boy, and during the day she was

[142-103]forced to resolve what should be the immediate course of her life.

[142-104]Two days after the wedding her family would return to England. It was

[142-105]open to her to go with them, and to take her boy with her. But a few

[142-106]days since how happy she would have been could she have been made to

[142-107]believe that such a mode of returning would be within her power! But

[142-108]now she felt that she might not return and leave that poor, suffering

[142-109]wretch behind her. As she thought of him she tried to interrogate

[142-110]herself in regard to her feelings. Was it love, or duty, or

[142-111]compassion which stirred her? She had loved him as fondly as any

[142-112]bright young woman loves the man who is to take her away from

[142-113]everything else, and make her a part of his house and of himself.

[142-114]She had loved him as Nora now loved the man whom she worshipped and

[142-115]thought to be a god, doing godlike work in the dingy recesses of the

[142-116]D. R. office. Emily Trevelyan was forced to tell herself that all

[142-117]that was over with her. Her husband had shown himself to be weak,

[142-118]suspicious, unmanly,--by no means like a god. She had learned to feel

[142-119]that she could not trust her comfort in his hands,--that she could

[142-120]never know what his thoughts of her might be. But still he was her

[142-121]husband, and the father of her child; and though she could not dare

[142-122]to look forward to happiness in living with him, she could understand

[142-123]that no comfort would be possible to her were she to return to

[142-124]England and to leave him to perish alone at Casalunga. Fate seemed

[142-125]to have intended that her life should be one of misery, and she must

[142-126]bear it as best she might.

[142-127]The more she thought of it, however, the greater seemed to be her

[142-128]difficulties. What was she to do when her father and mother should

[142-129]have left her? She could not go to Casalunga if her husband would not

[142-130]give her entrance; and if she did go, would it be safe for her to

[142-131]take her boy with her? Were she to remain in Florence she would be

[142-132]hardly nearer to him for any useful purpose than in England; and even

[142-133]should she pitch her tent at Siena, occupying there some desolate

[142-134]set of huge apartments in a deserted palace, of what use could she

[142-135]be to him? Could she stay there if he desired her to go; and was it

[142-136]probable that he would be willing that she should be at Siena while

[142-137]he was living at Casalunga,--no more than two leagues distant? How

[142-138]should she begin her work; and if he repulsed her, how should she

[142-139]then continue it?

[142-140]But during these wedding hours she did make up her mind as to what

[142-141]she would do for the present. She would certainly not leave Italy

[142-142]while her husband remained there. She would for a while keep her

[142-143]rooms in Florence, and there should her boy abide. But from time

[142-144]to time,--twice a week perhaps,--she would go down to Siena and

[142-145]Casalunga, and there form her plans in accordance with her husband's

[142-146]conduct. She was his wife, and nothing should entirely separate her

[142-147]from him, now that he so sorely wanted her aid.

[142-148]CHAPTER LXXXVII.

[142-149]MR. GLASCOCK'S MARRIAGE COMPLETED.

[142-150][Illustration]

[142-151]The Glascock marriage was a great affair in Florence;--so much

[142-152]so, that there were not a few who regarded it as a strengthening

[142-153]of peaceful relations between the United States and the United

[142-154]Kingdom, and who thought that the Alabama claims and the question

[142-155]of naturalisation might now be settled with comparative ease. An

[142-156]English lord was about to marry the niece of an American Minister

[142-157]to a foreign court. The bridegroom was not, indeed, quite a lord as

[142-158]yet, but it was known to all men that he must be a lord in a very

[142-159]short time, and the bride was treated with more than usual bridal

[142-160]honours because she belonged to a legation. She was not, indeed, an

[142-161]ambassador's daughter, but the niece of a daughterless ambassador,

[142-162]and therefore almost as good as a daughter. The wives and daughters

[142-163]of other ambassadors, and the other ambassadors themselves, of

[142-164]course, came to the wedding; and as the palace in which Mr. Spalding

[142-165]had apartments stood alone, in a garden, with a separate carriage

[142-166]entrance, it seemed for all wedding purposes as though the

[142-167]whole palace were his own. The English Minister came, and his

[142-168]wife,--although she had never quite given over turning up her nose at

[142-169]the American bride whom Mr. Glascock had chosen for himself. It was

[142-170]such a pity, she said, that such a man as Mr. Glascock should marry

[142-171]a young woman from Providence, Rhode Island. Who in England would

[142-172]know anything of Providence, Rhode Island? And it was so expedient,

[142-173]in her estimation, that a man of family should strengthen himself

[142-174]by marrying a woman of family. It was so necessary, she declared,

[142-175]that a man when marrying should remember that his child would have

[142-176]two grandfathers, and would be called upon to account for four

[142-177]great-grandfathers. Nevertheless Mr. Glascock was--Mr. Glascock;

[142-178]and, let him marry whom he would, his wife would be the future Lady

[142-179]Peterborough. Remembering this, the English Minister's wife gave up

[142-180]the point when the thing was really settled, and benignly promised to

[142-181]come to the breakfast with all the secretaries and attaches belonging

[142-182]to the legation, and all the wives and daughters thereof. What may

[142-183]a man not do, and do with eclat, if he be heir to a peer and have

[142-184]plenty of money in his pocket?
