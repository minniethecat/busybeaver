## Section 57

[057-001]He sat next to me almost all the evening yesterday; but

[057-002]he didn't say anything about it, except that he hoped I

[057-003]agreed with him about going to church, and all that. I

[057-004]suppose I do; and I am quite sure that if I were to be a

[057-005]clergyman's wife, I should endeavour to do whatever my

[057-006]husband thought right about religion. One ought to try

[057-007]to do so, even if the clergyman is not one's husband.

[057-008]Mr. Burgess has come, and he was so very amusing all the

[057-009]evening, that perhaps that was the reason Mr. Gibson said

[057-010]so little. Mr. Burgess is a very nice man, and I think

[057-011]Aunt Stanbury is more fond of him than of anybody. He is

[057-012]not at all the sort of person that I expected.

[057-013]But if Mr. Gibson does come on Friday, and does really

[057-014]mean it, what am I to say to him? Aunt Stanbury will be

[057-015]very angry if I do not take her advice. I am quite sure

[057-016]that she intends it all for my happiness; and then, of

[057-017]course, she knows so much more about the world than I do.

[057-018]She asks me what it is that I expect. Of course, I do not

[057-019]expect anything. It is a great compliment from Mr. Gibson,

[057-020]who is a clergyman, and thought well of by everybody. And

[057-021]nothing could be more respectable. Aunt Stanbury says

[057-022]that with the money she would give us we should be quite

[057-023]comfortable; and she wants us to live in this house. She

[057-024]says that there are thirty girls round [[exeter|Exeter]] who would

[057-025]give their eyes for such a chance; and, looking at it

[057-026]in that light, of course, it is a very great thing for

[057-027]me. Only think how poor we have been! And then, dear

[057-028]Priscilla, perhaps he would let me be good to you and dear

[057-029]mamma!

[057-030]But of course he will ask me whether I--love him; and what

[057-031]am I to say? Aunt Stanbury says that I am to love him.

[057-032]"Begin to love him at once," she said this morning. I

[057-033]would if I could, partly for her sake, and because I do

[057-034]feel that it would be so respectable. When I think of it,

[057-035]it does seem such a pity that poor I should throw away

[057-036]such a chance. And I must say that Mr. Gibson is very

[057-037]good and most obliging; and everybody says that he has

[057-038]an excellent temper, and that he is a most prudent,

[057-039]well-dispositioned man. I declare, dear Priscilla, when I

[057-040]think of it, I cannot bring myself to believe that such a

[057-041]man should want me to be his wife.

[057-042]But what ought I to do? I suppose when a girl is in love

[057-043]she is very unhappy if the [[social-class|gentleman]] does not propose to

[057-044]her. I am sure it would not make me at all unhappy if I

[057-045]were told that Mr. Gibson had changed his mind.

[057-046]Dearest Priscilla, you must write at once, because he is

[057-047]to be here on Friday. Oh, dear; Friday does seem to be so

[057-048]near! And I shall never know what to say to him, either

[057-049]one way or the other.

[057-050]Your most affectionate sister,

[057-051]DOROTHY STANBURY.

[057-052]P.S.--Give my kindest love to mamma; but you need not tell

[057-053]her unless you think it best.

[057-054]Priscilla received this letter on the Wednesday morning, and felt

[057-055]herself bound to answer it on that same afternoon. Had she postponed

[057-056]her reply for a day, it would still have been in Dorothy's hands

[057-057]before Mr. Gibson could have come to her on the dreaded Friday

[057-058]morning. But still that would hardly give her time enough to consider

[057-059]the matter with any degree of deliberation after she should have been

[057-060]armed with what wisdom Priscilla might be able to send her. The post

[057-061]left [[nuncombe-putney|Nuncombe Putney]] at three; and therefore the letter had to be

[057-062]written before their early dinner.

[057-063]So Priscilla went into the garden and sat herself down under an old

[057-064]cedar that she might discuss the matter with herself in all its

[057-065]bearings. She felt that no woman could be called upon to write a

[057-066]letter that should be of more importance. The whole welfare in life

[057-067]of the person who was dearest to her would probably depend upon it.

[057-068]The weight upon her was so great that she thought for a while she

[057-069]would take counsel with her mother; but she felt sure that her mother

[057-070]would recommend the marriage; and that if she afterwards should find

[057-071]herself bound to oppose it, then her mother would be a miserable

[057-072]woman. There could be no use to her in taking counsel with her

[057-073]mother, because her mother's mind was known to her beforehand. The

[057-074][[duty|responsibility]] was thrown upon her, and she alone must bear it.

[057-075]She tried hard to persuade herself to write at once and tell her

[057-076]sister to marry the man. She knew her sister's heart so well as to be

[057-077]sure that Dorothy would learn to love the man who was her husband. It

[057-078]was almost impossible that Dorothy should not love those with whom

[057-079]she lived. And then her sister was so well adapted to be a wife and

[057-080]a mother. Her temper was so sweet, she was so pure, so unselfish, so

[057-081]devoted, and so healthy withal! She was so happy when she was acting

[057-082]for others; and so excellent in action when she had another one to

[057-083]think for her! She was so trusting and trustworthy that any husband

[057-084]would adore her! Then Priscilla walked slowly into the house, got

[057-085]her prayer-book, and returning to her seat under the tree read the

[057-086]marriage service. It was one o'clock when she went up-stairs to write

[057-087]her letter, and it had not yet struck eleven when she first seated

[057-088]herself beneath the tree. Her letter, when written, was as follows:--

[057-089]Nuncombe Putney, August 25, 186--.

[057-090]DEAREST DOROTHY,

[057-091]I got your letter this morning, and I think it is better

[057-092]to answer it at once, as the time is very short. I have

[057-093]been thinking about it with all my mind, and I feel almost

[057-094]awe-stricken lest I should advise you wrongly. After all,

[057-095]I believe that your own dear sweet truth and honesty would

[057-096]guide you better than anybody else can guide you. You may

[057-097]be sure of this, that whichever way it is, I shall think

[057-098]that you have done right. Dearest sister, I suppose there

[057-099]can be no doubt that for most women a married life is

[057-100]happier than a single one. It is always thought so, as we

[057-101]may see by the anxiety of others to get married; and when

[057-102]an opinion becomes general, I think that the world is most

[057-103]often right. And then, my own one, I feel sure that you

[057-104]are adapted both for the cares and for the joys of married

[057-105]life. You would do your duty as a married woman happily,

[057-106]and would be a comfort to your husband;--not a thorn in

[057-107]his side, as are so many women.

[057-108]But, my pet, do not let that reasoning of Aunt Stanbury's

[057-109]about the thirty young girls who would give their eyes for

[057-110]Mr. Gibson, have any weight with you. You should not take

[057-111]him because thirty other young girls would be glad to have

[057-112]him. And do not think too much of that respectability

[057-113]of which you speak. I would never advise my Dolly to

[057-114]marry any man unless she could be respectable in her new

[057-115]position; but that alone should go for nothing. Nor should

[057-116]our poverty. We shall not starve. And even if we did, that

[057-117]would be but a poor excuse.

[057-118]I can find no escape from this,--that you should love him

[057-119]before you say that you will take him. But honest, loyal

[057-120]love need not, I take it, be of that romantic kind which

[057-121]people write about in novels and poetry. You need not

[057-122]think him to be perfect, or the best or grandest of men.

[057-123]Your heart will tell you whether he is dear to you. And

[057-124]remember, Dolly, that I shall remember that love itself

[057-125]must begin at some precise time. Though you had not

[057-126]learned to love him when you wrote on Tuesday, you may

[057-127]have begun to do so when you get this on Thursday.

[057-128]If you find that you love him, then say that you will be

[057-129]his wife. If your heart revolts from such a declaration

[057-130]as being false;--if you cannot bring yourself to feel

[057-131]that you prefer him to others as the partner of your

[057-132]life,--then tell him, with thanks for his courtesy, that

[057-133]it cannot be as he would have it.

[057-134]Yours always and ever most affectionately,

[057-135]PRISCILLA.

[057-136]CHAPTER XXXV.

[057-137]MR. GIBSON'S GOOD FORTUNE.

[057-138]"I'll bet you half-a-crown, my lad, you're thrown over at last, like

[057-139]the rest of them. There's nothing she likes so much as taking some

[057-140]one up in order that she may throw him over afterwards." It was thus

[057-141]that Mr. Bartholomew Burgess cautioned his nephew Brooke.

[057-142]"I'll take care that she shan't break my heart, Uncle Barty. I will

[057-143]go my way and she may go hers, and she may give her money to the

[057-144]hospital if she pleases."

[057-145]On the morning after his arrival Brooke Burgess had declared aloud

[057-146]in Miss Stanbury's parlour that he was going over to the bank to see

[057-147]his uncle. Now there was in this almost a breach of contract. Miss

[057-148]Stanbury, when she invited the young man to Exeter, had stipulated

[057-149]that there should be no intercourse between her house and the bank.

[057-150]"Of course, I shall not need to know where you go or where you don't

[057-151]go," she had written; "but after all that has passed there must not

[057-152]be any positive intercourse between my house and the bank." And now

[057-153]he had spoken of going over to C and B, as he called them, with the

[057-154]utmost indifference. Miss Stanbury had looked very grave, but had

[057-155]said nothing. She had determined to be on her guard, so that she

[057-156]should not be driven to quarrel with Brooke if she could avoid it.

[057-157]Bartholomew Burgess was a tall, thin, ill-tempered old man, as

[057-158]well-known in Exeter as the cathedral, and respected after a fashion.

[057-159]No one liked him. He said ill-natured things of all his neighbours,

[057-160]and had never earned any [[honour|reputation]] for doing good-natured acts. But

[057-161]he had lived in Exeter for nearly seventy years, and had achieved

[057-162]that sort of esteem which comes from long tenure. And he had

[057-163]committed no great iniquities in the course of his fifty years of

[057-164]business. The bank had never stopped payment, and he had robbed no

[057-165]one. He had not swallowed up widows and orphans, and had done his

[057-166]work in the firm of Cropper and Burgess after the old-fashioned safe

[057-167]manner, which leads neither to riches nor to ruin. Therefore he was

[057-168]respected. But he was a discontented, sour old man, who believed

[057-169]himself to have been injured by all his own friends, who disliked

[057-170]his own partners because they had bought that which had, at any rate,

[057-171]never belonged to him;--and whose strongest passion it was to hate

[057-172]Miss Stanbury of the Close.

[057-173]"She's got a parson by the hand, now," said the uncle, as he

[057-174]continued his caution to the nephew.

[057-175]"There was a clergyman there last night."

[057-176]"No doubt, and she'll play him off against you, and you against him;

[057-177]and then she'll throw you both over. I know her."

[057-178]"She has got a right to do what she likes with her own, Uncle Barty."

[057-179]"And how did she get it? Never mind. I'm not going to set you against

[057-180]her, if you're her favourite for the moment. She has a niece with her

[057-181]there,--hasn't she?"

[057-182]"One of her brother's daughters."

[057-183]"They say she's going to make that clergyman marry her."

[057-184]"What;--Mr. Gibson?"

[057-185]"Yes. They tell me he was as good as engaged to another girl,--one of

[057-186]the Frenches of Heavitree. And therefore dear Jemima could do nothing

[057-187]better than interfere. When she has succeeded in breaking the girl's

[057-188]heart--"
