## Section 43

[043-001]"Oh, yes;--if it were only for myself I should want nothing."

[043-002]"I will do the best I can with Trevelyan."

[043-003]"The best will be to make him, and every one, understand that the

[043-004]fault is altogether his, and not Emily's."

[043-005]"The best will be to make each think that there has been no real

[043-006]fault," said Hugh.

[043-007]"There should be no talking of faults," said Priscilla. "Let the

[043-008]husband take his wife back,--as he is bound to do."

[043-009]These words occupied hardly a minute in the saying, but during that

[043-010]minute [[hugh-stanbury|Hugh Stanbury]] held Nora by the hand. He held it fast. She

[043-011]would not attempt to withdraw it, but neither would she return his

[043-012]pressure by the muscle of a single finger. What right had he to press

[043-013]her hand; or to make any sign of love, any pretence of loving, when

[043-014]he had gone out of his way to tell her that she was not good enough

[043-015]for him? Then he started, and Nora and Priscilla put on their hats

[043-016]and left the house.

[043-017]"Let us go to Niddon Park," said Nora.

[043-018]"To Niddon Park again?"

[043-019]"Yes; it is so beautiful! And I should like to see it by the morning

[043-020]light. There is plenty of time."

[043-021]So they walked to Niddon Park in the morning, as they had done on the

[043-022]preceding evening. Their conversation at first regarded Trevelyan and

[043-023]his wife, and the old trouble; but Nora could not keep herself from

[043-024]speaking of Hugh Stanbury.

[043-025]"He would not have come," she said, "unless Louis had sent him."

[043-026]"He would not have come now, I think."

[043-027]"Of course not;--why should he?--before Parliament was hardly over,

[043-028]too? But he won't remain in town now,--will he?"

[043-029]"He says somebody must remain,--and I think he will be in [[london|London]] till

[043-030]near Christmas."

[043-031]"How disagreeable! But I suppose he doesn't care. It's all the same

[043-032]to a man like him. They don't shut the clubs up, I dare say. Will he

[043-033]come here at Christmas?"

[043-034]"Either then or for the New Year;--just for a day or two."

[043-035]"We shall be gone then, I suppose?" said Nora.

[043-036]"That must depend on Mr. Trevelyan," said Priscilla.

[043-037]"What a life for two women to lead;--to depend upon the caprice of a

[043-038]man who must be mad! Do you think that Mr. Trevelyan will care for

[043-039]what your brother says to him?"

[043-040]"I do not know Mr. Trevelyan."

[043-041]"He is very fond of your brother, and I suppose men friends do listen

[043-042]to each other. They never seem to listen to women. Don't you think

[043-043]that, after all, they despise women? They look on them as dainty,

[043-044]foolish things."

[043-045]"Sometimes women despise men," said Priscilla.

[043-046]"Not very often;--do they? And then women are so dependent on men. A

[043-047]woman can get nothing without a man."

[043-048]"I manage to get on somehow," said Priscilla.

[043-049]"No, you don't, Miss Stanbury,--if you think of it. You want mutton.

[043-050]And who kills the sheep?"

[043-051]"But who cooks it?"

[043-052]"But the men-cooks are the best," said Nora; "and the men-tailors,

[043-053]and the men to wait at table, and the men-poets, and the

[043-054]men-painters, and the men-nurses. All the things that women do, men

[043-055]do better."

[043-056]"There are two things they can't do," said Priscilla.

[043-057]"What are they?"

[043-058]"They can't suckle babies, and they can't forget themselves."

[043-059]"[[About]] the babies, of course not. As for forgetting themselves,--I am

[043-060]not quite so sure that I can forget myself.--That is just where your

[043-061]brother went down last night."

[043-062]They had at this moment reached the top of the steep slope below

[043-063]which the river ran brawling among the rocks, and Nora seated herself

[043-064]exactly where she had sat on the previous evening.

[043-065]"I have been down scores of times," said Priscilla.

[043-066]"Let us go now."

[043-067]"You wouldn't go when Hugh asked you yesterday."

[043-068]"I didn't care then. But do come now,--if you don't mind the climb."

[043-069]Then they went down the slope and reached the spot from whence Hugh

[043-070]Stanbury had jumped from rock to rock across the stream. "You have

[043-071]never been out there, have you?" said Nora.

[043-072]"On the rocks? Oh, dear, no! I should be sure to fall."

[043-073]"But he went; just like a goat."

[043-074]"That's one of the things that men can do, I suppose," said

[043-075]Priscilla. "But I don't see any great glory in being like a goat."

[043-076]"I do. I should like to be able to go, and I think I'll try. It is so

[043-077]mean to be dainty and weak."

[043-078]"I don't think it at all dainty to keep dry feet."

[043-079]"But he didn't get his feet wet," said Nora. "Or if he did, he didn't

[043-080]mind. I can see at once that I should be giddy and tumble down if I

[043-081]tried it."

[043-082]"Of course you would."

[043-083]"But he didn't tumble down."

[043-084]"He has been doing it all his life," said Priscilla.

[043-085]"He can't do it up in London. When I think of myself, Miss Stanbury,

[043-086]I am so ashamed. There is nothing that I can do. I couldn't write an

[043-087]article for a newspaper."

[043-088]"I think I could. But I fear no one would read it."

[043-089]"They read his," said Nora, "or else he wouldn't be paid for writing

[043-090]them." Then they climbed back again up the hill, and during the

[043-091]climbing there were no words spoken. The slope was not much of a

[043-092]hill,--was no more than the fall from the low ground of the valley to

[043-093]the course which the river had cut for itself; but it was steep while

[043-094]it lasted; and both the young women were forced to pause for a minute

[043-095]before they could proceed upon their journey. As they walked home

[043-096]Priscilla spoke of the scenery, and of the country, and of the

[043-097]nature of the life which she and her mother and sister had passed at

[043-098][[nuncombe-putney|Nuncombe Putney]]. Nora said but little till they were just entering

[043-099]the village, and then she went back to the subject of her thoughts.

[043-100]"I would sooner," said she, "write for a newspaper than do anything

[043-101]else in the world."

[043-102]"Why so?"

[043-103]"Because it is so noble to teach people everything! And then a man

[043-104]who writes for a newspaper must know so many things himself! I

[043-105]believe there are women who do it, but very few. One or two have done

[043-106]it, I know."

[043-107]"Go and tell that to Aunt Stanbury, and hear what she will say about

[043-108]such women."

[043-109]"I suppose she is very,--prejudiced."

[043-110]"Yes; she is; but she is a clever woman. I am inclined to think women

[043-111]had better not write for newspapers."

[043-112]"And why not?" Nora asked.

[043-113]"My reasons would take me a week to explain, and I doubt whether I

[043-114]have them very clear in my own head. In the first place there is that

[043-115]difficulty about the babies. Most of them must get married you know."

[043-116]"But not all," said Nora.

[043-117]"No; thank God; not all."

[043-118]"And if you are not married you might write for a newspaper. At any

[043-119]rate, if I were you, I should be very proud of my brother."

[043-120]"Aunt Stanbury is not at all proud of her nephew," said Priscilla, as

[043-121]they entered the house.

[043-122]CHAPTER XXVI.

[043-123]A THIRD PARTY IS SO OBJECTIONABLE.

[043-124]Hugh Stanbury went in search of Trevelyan immediately on his return

[043-125]to London, and found his friend at his rooms in Lincoln's Inn.

[043-126]"I have executed my commission," said Hugh, endeavouring to speak of

[043-127]what he had done in a cheery voice.

[043-128]"I am much obliged to you, Stanbury; very much;--but I do not know

[043-129]that I need trouble you to tell me anything about it."

[043-130]"And why not?"

[043-131]"I have learned it all from that--man."

[043-132]"What man?"

[043-133]"From Bozzle. He has come back, and has been with me, and has learned

[043-134]everything."

[043-135]"Look here, Trevelyan;--when you asked me to go down to Devonshire,

[043-136]you promised me that there should be nothing more about Bozzle. I

[043-137]expect you to put that rascal, and all that he has told you, out of

[043-138]your head altogether. You are bound to do so for my sake, and you

[043-139]will be very wise to do so for your own."

[043-140]"I was obliged to see him when he came."

[043-141]"Yes, and to pay him, I do not doubt. But that is all done, and

[043-142]should be forgotten."

[043-143]"I can't forget it. Is it true or untrue that he found that man down

[043-144]there? Is it true or untrue that my wife received [[colonel-osborne|Colonel Osborne]] at

[043-145]your mother's house? Is it true or untrue that Colonel Osborne went

[043-146]down there with the express object of seeing her? Is it true or

[043-147]untrue that they had corresponded? It is nonsense to bid me to forget

[043-148]all this. You might as well ask me to forget that I had desired her

[043-149]neither to write to him, nor to see him."

[043-150]"If I understand the matter," said Trevelyan, "you are incorrect in

[043-151]one of your assertions."

[043-152]"In which?"

[043-153]"You must excuse me if I am wrong, Trevelyan; but I don't think you

[043-154]ever did tell your wife not to see this man, or not to write to him?"

[043-155]"I never told her! I don't understand what you mean."

[043-156]"Not in so many words. It is my belief that she has endeavoured to

[043-157]obey implicitly every clear instruction that you have given her."

[043-158]"You are wrong;--absolutely and altogether wrong. Heaven and earth!

[043-159]Do you mean to tell me now, after all that has taken place, that she

[043-160]did not know my wishes?"

[043-161]"I have not said that. But you have chosen to place her in such a

[043-162]position, that though your word would go for much with her, she

[043-163]cannot bring herself to respect your wishes."

[043-164]"And you call that being dutiful and affectionate!"

[043-165]"I call it human and reasonable; and I think that it is compatible

[043-166]with duty and affection. Have you consulted her wishes?"

[043-167]"Always!"

[043-168]"Consult them now then, and bid her come back to you."

[043-169]"No;--never! As far as I can see, I will never do so. The moment she

[043-170]is away from me this man goes to her, and she receives him. She must

[043-171]have known that she was wrong,--and you must know it."

[043-172]"I do not think that she is half so wrong as you yourself," said

[043-173]Stanbury. To this Trevelyan made no answer, and they both remained

[043-174]silent some minutes. Stanbury had a communication to make before he

[043-175]went, but it was one which he wished to delay as long as there was a

[043-176]chance that his friend's heart might be softened;--one which he need

[043-177]not make if Trevelyan would consent to receive his wife back to his

[043-178]house. There was the day's paper lying on the table, and Stanbury had

[043-179]taken it up and was reading it,--or pretending to read it.

[043-180]"I will tell you what I propose to do," said Trevelyan.

[043-181]"Well."

[043-182]"It is best both for her and for me that we should be apart."

[043-183]"I cannot understand how you can be so mad as to say so."

[043-184]"You don't understand what I feel. Heaven and earth! To have a man

[043-185]coming and going--. But, never mind. You do not see it, and nothing

[043-186]will make you see it. And there is no reason why you should."

[043-187]"I certainly do not see it. I do not believe that your wife cares

[043-188]more for Colonel Osborne, except as an old friend of her father's,

[043-189]than she does for the fellow that sweeps the crossing. It is a matter

[043-190]in which I am bound to tell you what I think."

[043-191]"Very well. Now, if you have freed your mind, I will tell you my

[043-192]purpose. I am bound to do so, because your people are concerned in

[043-193]it. I shall go abroad."

[043-194]"And leave her in England?"

[043-195]"Certainly. She will be safer here than she can be abroad,--unless

[043-196]she should choose to go back with her father to the islands."
