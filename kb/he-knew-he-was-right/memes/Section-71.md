## Section 71

[071-001]If the truth had been told on that occasion, [[hugh-stanbury|Hugh Stanbury]] would have

[071-002]had to own that he had written lately two or three rather stinging

[071-003]articles in the "Daily Record," as "to the assumed merits and actual

[071-004]demerits of the clergy of the Church of England." It is astonishing

[071-005]how fluent a man is on a subject when he has lately delivered himself

[071-006]respecting it in this fashion.

[071-007]Nothing on that evening was said about the [[clock-house|Clock House]], or about

[071-008]Priscilla's intentions. Priscilla was up early on the next morning,

[071-009]intending to discuss it in the garden with Hugh before breakfast; but

[071-010]Hugh was aware of her purpose and avoided her. It was his intention

[071-011]to speak first to his mother; and though his mother was, as he knew,

[071-012]very much in awe of her daughter, he thought that he might carry his

[071-013]point, at any rate for the next three months, by forcing an assent

[071-014]from the elder lady. So he managed to waylay Mrs. Stanbury before she

[071-015]descended to the parlour.

[071-016]"We can't afford it, my dear;--indeed we can't," said Mrs. Stanbury.

[071-017]"That's not the question, mother. The rent must be paid up to

[071-018]Christmas, and you can live here as cheap as you can anywhere."

[071-019]"But Priscilla--"

[071-020]"Oh, Priscilla! Of course we know what Priscilla says. Priscilla has

[071-021]been writing to me about it in the most sensible manner in the world;

[071-022]but what does it all come to? If you are ashamed of taking assistance

[071-023]from me, I don't know who is to do anything for anybody. You are

[071-024]comfortable here?"

[071-025]"Very comfortable; only Priscilla feels--"

[071-026]"Priscilla is a tyrant, mother; and a very stern one. Just make up

[071-027]your mind to stay here till Christmas. If I tell you that I can

[071-028]afford it, surely that ought to be enough." Then Dorothy entered the

[071-029]room, and Hugh appealed to her. Dorothy had come to Nuncombe only on

[071-030]the day before, and had not been consulted on the subject. She had

[071-031]been told that the Clock House was to be abandoned, and had been

[071-032]taken down to inspect the cottage in which old Soames had lived;--but

[071-033]her opinion had not been asked. Priscilla had quite made up her mind,

[071-034]and why should she ask an opinion of any one? But now Dorothy's

[071-035]opinion was demanded. "It's what I call the rhodomontade of

[071-036][[personal-freedom|independence]]," said Hugh.

[071-037]"I suppose it is very expensive," suggested Dorothy.

[071-038]"The house must be paid for," said Hugh;--"and if I say that I've got

[071-039]the money, is not that enough? A miserable, dirty little place, where

[071-040]you'll catch your death of lumbago, mother."

[071-041]"Of course it's not a comfortable house," said Mrs. Stanbury,--who,

[071-042]of herself, was not at all indifferent to the comforts of her present

[071-043]residence.

[071-044]"And it is very dirty," said Dorothy.

[071-045]"The nastiest place I ever saw in my life. Come, mother; if I say

[071-046]that I can afford it, ought not that to be enough for you? If you

[071-047]think you can't trust me, there's an end of everything, you know."

[071-048]And Hugh, as he thus expressed himself, assumed an air of injured

[071-049]virtue.

[071-050]Mrs. Stanbury had very nearly yielded, when Priscilla came in among

[071-051]them. It was impossible not to continue the conversation, though Hugh

[071-052]would much have preferred to have forced an assent from his mother

[071-053]before he opened his mouth on the subject to his sister. "My mother

[071-054]agrees with me," said he abruptly, "and so does Dolly, that it will

[071-055]be absurd to move away from this house at present."

[071-056]"Mamma!" exclaimed Priscilla.

[071-057]"I don't think I said that, Hugh," murmured Dorothy, softly.

[071-058]"I'm sure I don't want anything for myself," said Mrs. Stanbury.

[071-059]"It's I that want it," said Hugh. "And I think that I've a right to

[071-060]have my wishes respected, so far as that goes."

[071-061]"My dear Hugh," said Priscilla, "the cottage is already taken, and we

[071-062]shall certainly go into it. I spoke to Mrs. Crocket yesterday about

[071-063]a cart for moving the things. I'm sure mamma agrees with me. What

[071-064]possible business can people have to live in such a house as this

[071-065]with about twenty-four shillings a week for everything? I won't do

[071-066]it. And as the thing is settled, it is only making trouble to disturb

[071-067]it."

[071-068]"I suppose, Priscilla," said Hugh, "you'll do as your mother

[071-069]chooses?"

[071-070]"Mamma chooses to go. She has told me so already."

[071-071]"You have talked her into it."

[071-072]"We had better go, Hugh," said Mrs. Stanbury. "I'm sure we had better

[071-073]go."

[071-074]"Of course we shall go," said Priscilla. "Hugh is very kind and very

[071-075]generous, but he is only giving trouble for nothing about this. Had

[071-076]we not better go down to breakfast?"

[071-077]And so Priscilla carried the day. They went down to breakfast, and

[071-078]during the meal Hugh would speak to nobody. When the gloomy meal

[071-079]was over he took his pipe and walked out to the cottage. It was an

[071-080]untidy-looking, rickety place, small and desolate, with a pretension

[071-081]about it of the lowest order, a pretension that was evidently ashamed

[071-082]of itself. There was a porch. And the one sitting-room had what the

[071-083]late Mr. Soames had always called his bow window. But the porch

[071-084]looked as though it were tumbling down, and the bow window looked

[071-085]as though it were tumbling out. The parlour and the bedroom over it

[071-086]had been papered;--but the paper was torn and soiled, and in sundry

[071-087]places was hanging loose. There was a miserable little room called a

[071-088]kitchen to the right as you entered the door, in which the grate was

[071-089]worn out, and behind this was a shed with a copper. In the garden

[071-090]there remained the stumps and stalks of Mr. Soames's cabbages, and

[071-091]there were weeds in plenty, and a damp hole among some elder bushes

[071-092]called an arbour. It was named Laburnum Cottage, from a shrub that

[071-093]grew at the end of the house. Hugh Stanbury shuddered as he stood

[071-094]smoking among the cabbage-stalks. How could a man ask such a girl

[071-095]as [[nora-rowley|Nora Rowley]] to be his wife, whose mother lived in a place like

[071-096]this? While he was still [[reputation|standing]] in the garden, and thinking of

[071-097]Priscilla's obstinacy and his own ten guineas a week, and the sort of

[071-098]life which he lived in [[london|London]],--where he dined usually at his club,

[071-099]and denied himself nothing in the way of pipes, beer, and beefsteaks,

[071-100]he heard a step behind him, and turning round, saw his elder sister.

[071-101]"Hugh," she said, "you must not be angry with me."

[071-102]"But I am angry with you."

[071-103]"I know you are; but you are unjust. I am doing what I am sure is

[071-104]right."

[071-105]"I never saw such a beastly hole as this in all my life."

[071-106]"I don't think it beastly at all. You'll find that I'll make it nice.

[071-107]Whatever we want here you shall give us. You are not to think that I

[071-108]am too proud to take anything at your hands. It is not that."

[071-109]"It's very like it."

[071-110]"I have never refused anything that is reasonable, but it is quite

[071-111]unreasonable that we should go on living in such a place as that, as

[071-112]though we had three or four hundred a year of our own. If mamma got

[071-113]used to the comfort of it, it would be hard then upon her to move.

[071-114]You shall give her what you can afford, and what is reasonable; but

[071-115]it is madness to think of living there. I couldn't do it."

[071-116]"You're to have your way at any rate, it seems."

[071-117]"But you must not quarrel with me, Hugh. Give me a kiss. I don't have

[071-118]you often with me; and yet you are the only man in the world that I

[071-119]ever speak to, or even know. I sometimes half think that the bread is

[071-120]so hard and the water so bitter, that life will become impossible. I

[071-121]try to get over it; but if you were to go away from me in anger, I

[071-122]should be so beaten for a week or two that I could do nothing."

[071-123]"Why won't you let me do anything?"

[071-124]"I will;--whatever you please. But kiss me." Then he kissed her, as

[071-125]he stood among Mr. Soames's cabbage-stalks. "Dear Hugh; you are such

[071-126]a god to me!"

[071-127]"You don't treat me like a divinity."

[071-128]"But I think of you as one when you are absent. The gods were never

[071-129]obeyed when they showed themselves. Let us go and have a walk.

[071-130]Come;--shall we get as far as Ridleigh Mill?" Then they started

[071-131]together, and all unpleasantness was over between them when they

[071-132]returned to the Clock House.

[071-133]CHAPTER XLIV.

[071-134]BROOKE BURGESS TAKES LEAVE OF EXETER.

[071-135][Illustration]

[071-136]The time had arrived at which Brooke Burgess was to leave [[exeter|Exeter]]. He

[071-137]had made his tour through the county, and returned to spend his two

[071-138]last nights at Miss Stanbury's house. When he came back Dorothy was

[071-139]still at Nuncombe, but she arrived in the Close the day before his

[071-140]departure. Her mother and sister had wished her to stay at Nuncombe.

[071-141]"There is a bed for you now, and a place to be comfortable in,"

[071-142]Priscilla had said, laughing, "and you may as well see the last of

[071-143]us." But Dorothy declared that she had named a day to her aunt, and

[071-144]that she would not break her engagement. "I suppose you can stay if

[071-145]you like," Priscilla had urged. But Dorothy was of opinion that she

[071-146]ought not to stay. She said not a word about Brooke Burgess; but it

[071-147]may be that it would have been matter of regret to her not to shake

[071-148]hands with him once more. Brooke declared to her that had she not

[071-149]come back he would have gone over to Nuncombe to see her; but Dorothy

[071-150]did not consider herself entitled to believe that.

[071-151]On the morning of the last day Brooke went over to his uncle's

[071-152]office. "I've come to say good-bye, Uncle Barty," he said.

[071-153]"Good-bye, my boy. Take care of yourself."

[071-154]"I mean to try."

[071-155]"You haven't quarrelled with the old woman,--have you?" said Uncle

[071-156]Barty.

[071-157]"Not yet;--that is to say, not to the knife."

[071-158]"And you still believe that you are to have her money?"

[071-159]"I believe nothing one way or the other. You may be sure of this,--I

[071-160]shall never count it mine till I've got it; and I shall never make

[071-161]myself so sure of it as to break my heart because I don't get it. I

[071-162]suppose I've got as good a right to it as anybody else, and I don't

[071-163]see why I shouldn't take it if it come in my way."

[071-164]"I don't think it ever will," said the old man, after a pause.

[071-165]"I shall be none the worse," said Brooke.

[071-166]"Yes, you will. You'll be a broken-hearted man. And she means to

[071-167]break your heart. She does it on purpose. She has no more idea of

[071-168]leaving you her money than I have. Why should she?"

[071-169]"Simply because she takes the fancy."

[071-170]"Fancy! Believe me, there is very little fancy about it. There isn't

[071-171]one of the name she wouldn't ruin if she could. She'd break all our

[071-172]hearts if she could get at them. Look at me and my position. I'm

[071-173]little more than a clerk in the concern. By God;--I'm not so well off

[071-174]as a senior clerk in many a bank. If there came a bad time, I must

[071-175]lose as the others would lose;--but a clerk never loses. And my share

[071-176]in the business is almost a nothing. It's just nothing,--compared to

[071-177]what it would have been, only for her."

[071-178]Brooke had known that his uncle was a disappointed, or at least

[071-179]a discontented man; but he had never known much of the old man's

[071-180]circumstances, and certainly had not expected to hear him speak in

[071-181]the strain that he had now used. He had heard often that his Uncle

[071-182]Barty disliked Miss Stanbury, and had not been surprised at former

[071-183]sharp, biting little words spoken in reference to that lady's

[071-184][[honour|character]]. But he had not expected such a tirade of abuse as the

[071-185]banker had now poured out. "Of course I know nothing about the bank,"

[071-186]said he; "but I did not suppose that she had had anything to do with

[071-187]it."
