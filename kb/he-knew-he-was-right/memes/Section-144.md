## Section 144

[144-001]They sat there discussing the matter for some time through the long,

[144-002]cool, evening hours, but nothing could be settled further,--except

[144-003]that Nora would write to her friend as soon as her affairs had begun

[144-004]to shape themselves after her return to England. At last Caroline

[144-005]went into the house, and for a few minutes Mr. Glascock was alone

[144-006]with Nora. He had remained, determining that the moment should come,

[144-007]but now that it was there he was for awhile unable to say the words

[144-008]that he wished to utter. At last he spoke. "Miss Rowley, Caroline is

[144-009]so eager to be your friend."

[144-010]"I know she is, and I do love her so dearly. But, without joke, Mr.

[144-011]Glascock, there will be as it were a great gulf between us."

[144-012]"I do not know that there need be any gulf, great or little. But

[144-013]I did not mean to allude to that. What I want to say is this. My

[144-014]feelings are not a bit less warm or sincere than hers. You know of

[144-015]old that I am not very good at expressing myself."

[144-016]"I know nothing of the kind."

[144-017]"There is no such gulf as what you speak of. All that is mostly

[144-018]gone by, and a nobleman in England, though he has advantages as a

[144-019][[social-class|gentleman]], is no more than a gentleman. But that has nothing to

[144-020]do with what I am saying now. I shall never forget my journey to

[144-021]Devonshire. I won't pretend to say now that I regret its result."

[144-022]"I am quite sure you don't."

[144-023]"No; I do not;--though I thought then that I should regret it always.

[144-024]But remember this, Miss Rowley,--that you can never ask me to do

[144-025]anything that I will not, if possible, do for you. You are in some

[144-026]little difficulty now."

[144-027]"It will disappear, Mr. Glascock. Difficulties always do."

[144-028]"But we will do anything that we are wanted to do; and should a

[144-029]certain event take place--"

[144-030]"It will take place some day."

[144-031]"Then I hope that we may be able to make Mr. Stanbury and his wife

[144-032]quite at home at Monkhams." After that he took Nora's hand and kissed

[144-033]it, and at that moment Caroline came back to them.

[144-034]"To-morrow, Mr. Glascock," she said, "you will, I believe, be at

[144-035][[personal-freedom|liberty]] to kiss everybody; but to-day you should be more discreet."

[144-036]It was generally admitted among the various legations in Florence

[144-037]that there had not been such a wedding in the City of Flowers since

[144-038]it had become the capital of Italia. Mr. Glascock and Miss Spalding

[144-039]were married in the chapel of the legation,--a legation chapel on the

[144-040]ground floor having been extemporised for the occasion. This greatly

[144-041]enhanced the pleasantness of the thing, and saved the necessity of

[144-042]matrons and bridesmaids packing themselves and their finery into

[144-043]close fusty carriages. A portion of the guests attended in the

[144-044]chapel, and the remainder, when the ceremony was over, were found

[144-045]strolling about the shady garden. The whole affair of the breakfast

[144-046]was very splendid and lasted some hours. In the midst of this the

[144-047]bride and bridegroom were whisked away with a pair of grey horses to

[144-048]the railway station, and before the last toast of the day had been

[144-049]proposed by the Belgian Councillor of Legation, they were half way up

[144-050]the Apennines on their road to Bologna. Mr. Spalding behaved himself

[144-051]like a man on the occasion. Nothing was spared in the way of expense,

[144-052]and when he made that celebrated speech, in which he declared that

[144-053]the republican virtue of the New World had linked itself in a happy

[144-054]alliance with the aristocratic splendour of the Old, and went on with

[144-055]a simile about the lion and the lamb, everybody accepted it with good

[144-056]humour in spite of its being a little too long for the occasion.

[144-057]"It has gone off very well, mamma; has it not?" said Nora, as she

[144-058]returned home with her mother to her lodgings.

[144-059]"Yes, my dear; much, I fancy, as these things generally do."

[144-060]"I thought it was so nice. And she looked so very well. And he was so

[144-061]pleasant, and so much like a gentleman;--not noisy, you know,--and

[144-062]yet not too serious."

[144-063]"I dare say, my love."

[144-064]"It is easy enough, mamma, for a girl to be married, for she has

[144-065]nothing to do but to wear her clothes and look as pretty as she can.

[144-066]And if she cries and has a red nose it is forgiven her. But a man has

[144-067]so difficult a part to play. If he tries to carry himself as though

[144-068]it were not a special occasion, he looks like a fool that way; and if

[144-069]he is very special, he looks like a fool the other way. I thought Mr.

[144-070]Glascock did it very well."

[144-071]"To tell you the truth, my dear, I did not observe him."

[144-072]"I did,--narrowly. He hadn't tied his cravat at all nicely."

[144-073]"How you could think of his cravat, Nora, with such memories as you

[144-074]must have, and such regrets, I cannot understand."

[144-075]"Mamma, my memories of Mr. Glascock are pleasant memories, and as for

[144-076]regrets,--I have not one. Can I regret, mamma, that I did not marry a

[144-077]man whom I did not love,--and that I rejected him when I knew that I

[144-078]loved another? You cannot mean that, mamma."

[144-079]"I know this;--that I was thinking all the time how proud I should

[144-080]have been, and how much more fortunate he would have been, had you

[144-081]been [[reputation|standing]] there instead of that American young woman." As she

[144-082]said this Lady Rowley burst into tears, and Nora could only answer

[144-083]her mother by embracing her. They were alone together, their party

[144-084]having been too large for one carriage, and Sir Marmaduke having

[144-085]taken his two younger daughters. "Of course I feel it," said Lady

[144-086]Rowley, through her tears. "It would have been such a position for

[144-087]my child! And that young man,--without a shilling in the world; and

[144-088]writing in that way, just for bare bread!" Nora had nothing more

[144-089]to say. A feeling that in herself would have been base, was simply

[144-090]affectionate and maternal in her mother. It was impossible that she

[144-091]should make her mother see it as she saw it.

[144-092]There was but one intervening day and then the Rowleys returned to

[144-093]England. There had been, as it were, a tacit agreement among them

[144-094]that, in spite of all their troubles, their holiday should be a

[144-095]holiday up to the time of the Glascock marriage. Then must commence

[144-096]at once the stern necessity of their return home,--home, not only

[144-097]to England, but to those antipodean islands from which it was

[144-098]too probable that some of them might never come back. And the

[144-099]difficulties in their way seemed to be almost insuperable. First

[144-100]of all there was to be the parting from [[emily-trevelyan|Emily Trevelyan]]. She had

[144-101]determined to remain in Florence, and had written to her husband

[144-102]saying that she would do so, and declaring her willingness to go out

[144-103]to him, or to receive him in Florence at any time and in any manner

[144-104]that he might appoint. She had taken this as a first step, intending

[144-105]to go to Casalunga very shortly, even though she should receive

[144-106]no answer from him. The parting between her and her mother and

[144-107]father and sisters was very bitter. Sir Marmaduke, as he had become

[144-108]estranged from Nora, had grown to be more and more gentle and loving

[144-109]with his elder daughter, and was nearly overcome at the idea of

[144-110]leaving her in a strange land, with a husband near her, mad, and yet

[144-111]not within her custody. But he could do nothing,--could hardly say a

[144-112]word,--toward opposing her. Though her husband was mad, he supplied

[144-113]her with the means of living; and when she said that it was her duty

[144-114]to be near him, her father could not deny it. The parting came. "I

[144-115]will return to you the moment you send to me," were Nora's last words

[144-116]to her sister. "I don't suppose I shall send," said Emily. "I shall

[144-117]try to bear it without assistance."

[144-118]Then the journey from Italy to England was made without much

[144-119]gratification or excitement, and the Rowley family again found

[144-120]themselves at Gregg's Hotel.

[144-121]CHAPTER LXXXVIII.

[144-122]CROPPER AND BURGESS.

[144-123]We must now go back to [[exeter|Exeter]] and look after Mr. Brooke Burgess and

[144-124]Miss Dorothy Stanbury. It is rather hard upon readers that they

[144-125]should be thus hurried from the completion of hymeneals at Florence,

[144-126]to the preparations for other hymeneals in Devonshire; but it is the

[144-127]nature of a complex story to be entangled with many weddings towards

[144-128]its close. In this little history there are, we fear, three or four

[144-129]more to come. We will not anticipate by alluding prematurely to Hugh

[144-130]Stanbury's treachery, or death,--or the possibility that he after all

[144-131]may turn out to be the real descendant of the true Lord Peterborough

[144-132]and the actual inheritor of the title and estate of Monkhams, nor

[144-133]will we speak of Nora's certain fortitude under either of these

[144-134]emergencies. But the instructed reader must be aware that Camilla

[144-135]French ought to have a husband found for her; that [[colonel-osborne|Colonel Osborne]]

[144-136]should be caught in some matrimonial trap,--as, how otherwise

[144-137]should he be fitly punished?--and that something should be at least

[144-138]attempted for Priscilla Stanbury, who from the first has been

[144-139]intended to be the real heroine of these pages. That Martha should

[144-140]marry Giles Hickbody, and Barty Burgess run away with Mrs. MacHugh,

[144-141]is of course evident to the meanest novel-expounding capacity; but

[144-142]the fate of Brooke Burgess and of Dorothy will require to be evolved

[144-143]with some delicacy and much detail.

[144-144]There was considerable difficulty in fixing the day. In the first

[144-145]place Miss Stanbury was not very well,--and then she was very

[144-146]fidgety. She must see Brooke again before the day was fixed, and

[144-147]after seeing Brooke she must see her lawyer. "To have a lot of money

[144-148]to look after is more plague than profit, my dear," she said to

[144-149]Dorothy one day; "particularly when you don't quite know what you

[144-150]ought to do with it." Dorothy had always avoided any conversation

[144-151]with her aunt about money since the first moment in which she had

[144-152]thought of accepting Brooke Burgess as her husband. She knew that

[144-153]her aunt had some feeling which made her averse to the idea that any

[144-154]portion of the [[money|property]] which she had inherited should be enjoyed by

[144-155]a Stanbury after her death, and Dorothy, guided by this [[truth|knowledge]],

[144-156]had almost convinced herself that her love for Brooke was treason

[144-157]either against him or against her aunt. If, by engaging herself to

[144-158]him, she should rob him of his inheritance, how bitter a burden to

[144-159]him would her love have been! If, on the other hand, she should

[144-160]reward her aunt for all that had been done for her by forcing

[144-161]herself, a Stanbury, into a position not intended for her, how base

[144-162]would be her ingratitude! These thoughts had troubled her much, and

[144-163]had always prevented her from answering any of her aunt's chance

[144-164]allusions to the property. For her, things had at last gone very

[144-165]right. She did not quite know how it had come about, but she was

[144-166]engaged to marry the man she loved. And her aunt was, at any rate,

[144-167]reconciled to the marriage. But when Miss Stanbury declared that she

[144-168]did not know what to do about the property, Dorothy could only hold

[144-169]her tongue. She had had plenty to say when it had been suggested to

[144-170]her that the marriage should be put off yet for a short while, and

[144-171]that, in the meantime, Brooke should come again to Exeter. She swore

[144-172]that she did not care for how long it was put off,--only that she

[144-173]hoped it might not be put off altogether. And as for Brooke's coming,

[144-174]that, for the present, would be very much nicer than being married

[144-175]out of hand at once. Dorothy, in truth, was not at all in a hurry to

[144-176]be married, but she would have liked to have had her lover always

[144-177]coming and going. Since the courtship had become a thing permitted,

[144-178]she had had the privilege of welcoming him twice at the house in the

[144-179]Close; and that running down to meet him in the little front parlour,

[144-180]and the getting up to make his breakfast for him as he started in

[144-181]the morning, were among the happiest epochs of her life. And then,

[144-182]as soon as ever the breakfast was eaten, and he was gone, she would

[144-183]sit down to write him a letter. Oh, those letters, so beautifully

[144-184]crossed, more than one of which was copied from beginning to end

[144-185]because some word in it was not thought to be sweet enough;--what a

[144-186]heaven of happiness they were to her! The writing of the first had

[144-187]disturbed her greatly, and she had almost repented of the privilege

[144-188]before it was ended; but with the first and second the difficulties

[144-189]had disappeared; and, had she not felt somewhat ashamed of the

[144-190]occupation, she could have sat at her desk and written him letters

[144-191]all day. Brooke would answer them, with fair regularity, but in a

[144-192]most cursory manner,--sending seven or eight lines in return for two

[144-193]sheets fully crossed; but this did not discompose her in the least.

[144-194]He was worked hard at his office, and had hundreds of other things

[144-195]to do. He, too, could say,--so thought Dorothy,--more in eight lines

[144-196]than she could put into as many pages.
