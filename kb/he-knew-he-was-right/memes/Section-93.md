## Section 93

[093-001]But nevertheless Mr. Glascock had not quite made up his mind to ask

[093-002]the elder sister to be his wife. He was one of those men to whom

[093-003]love-making does not come very easy, although he was never so much at

[093-004]his ease as when he was in company with ladies. He was sorely in want

[093-005]of a wife, but he was aware that at different periods during the last

[093-006]fifteen years he had been angled for as a fish. Mothers in England

[093-007]had tried to catch him, and of such mothers he had come to have the

[093-008]strongest possible detestation. He had seen the hooks,--or perhaps

[093-009]had fancied that he saw them when they were not there. Lady Janes and

[093-010]Lady Sarahs had been hard upon him, till he learned to buckle himself

[093-011]into triple armour when he went amongst them, and yet he wanted

[093-012]a wife;--no man more sorely wanted one. The reader will perhaps

[093-013]remember how he went down to [[nuncombe-putney|Nuncombe Putney]] in quest of a wife, but

[093-014]all in vain. The lady in that case had been so explicit with him that

[093-015]he could not hope for a more favourable answer; and, indeed, he would

[093-016]not have cared to marry a girl who had told him that she preferred

[093-017]another man to himself, even if it had been possible for him to do

[093-018]so. Now he had met a lady very different from those with whom he had

[093-019]hitherto associated,--but not the less manifestly a lady. Caroline

[093-020]Spalding was bright, pleasant, attractive, very easy to talk to, and

[093-021]yet quite able to hold her own. But the American Minister was--a

[093-022]bore; and Miss Petrie was--unbearable. He had often told himself that

[093-023]in this matter of marrying a wife he would please himself altogether,

[093-024]that he would allow himself to be tied down by no consideration of

[093-025]family pride,--that he would consult nothing but his own heart and

[093-026]feelings. As for [[social-class|rank]], he could give that to his wife. As for money,

[093-027]he had plenty of that also. He wanted a woman that was not blasee

[093-028]with the world, that was not a fool, and who would respect him. The

[093-029]more he thought of it, the more sure he was that he had seen none who

[093-030]pleased him so well as Caroline Spalding; and yet he was a little

[093-031]afraid of taking a step that would be irrevocable. Perhaps the

[093-032]American Minister might express a wish to end his days at Monkhams,

[093-033]and might think it desirable to have Miss Petrie always with him as a

[093-034]private secretary in poetry!

[093-035]"Between you and us, Mr. Glascock, the spark of sympathy does not

[093-036]pass with a strong flash," said a voice in his ear. As he turned

[093-037]round rapidly to face his foe, he was quite sure, for the moment,

[093-038]that under no possible circumstances would he ever take an American

[093-039]woman to his bosom as his wife.

[093-040]"No," said he; "no, no. I rather think that I agree with you."

[093-041]"The antipathy is one," continued Miss Petrie, "which has been

[093-042]common on the face of the earth since the clown first trod upon the

[093-043]courtier's heels. It is the instinct of fallen man to hate equality,

[093-044]to desire ascendancy, to crush, to oppress, to tyrannise, to

[093-045]enslave. Then, when the slave is at last free, and in his freedom

[093-046]demands--equality, man is not great enough to take his enfranchised

[093-047]brother to his bosom."

[093-048]"You mean negroes," said Mr. Glascock, looking round and planning for

[093-049]himself a mode of escape.

[093-050]"Not negroes only,--not the enslaved blacks, who are now enslaved no

[093-051]more,--but the rising nations of white men wherever they are to be

[093-052]seen. You English have no sympathy with a people who claim to be at

[093-053]least your equals. The clown has trod upon the courtier's heels till

[093-054]the clown is clown no longer, and the courtier has hardly a court in

[093-055]which he may dangle his sword-knot."

[093-056]"If so the clown might as well spare the courtier," not meaning the

[093-057]rebuke which his words implied.

[093-058]"Ah--h,--but the clown will not spare the courtier, Mr. Glascock. I

[093-059]understand the gibe, and I tell you that the courtier shall be spared

[093-060]no longer;--because he is useless. He shall be cut down together with

[093-061]the withered grasses and thrown into the oven, and there shall be an

[093-062]end of him." Then she turned round to appeal to an American gentleman

[093-063]who had joined them, and Mr. Glascock made his escape. "I hold it to

[093-064]be the holiest duty which I owe to my country never to spare one of

[093-065]them when I meet him."

[093-066]"They are all very well in their way," said the American gentleman.

[093-067]"Down with them, down with them!" exclaimed the poetess, with a

[093-068]beautiful enthusiasm. In the meantime Mr. Glascock had made up his

[093-069]mind that he could not dare to ask Caroline Spalding to be his wife.

[093-070]There were certain forms of the American female so dreadful that no

[093-071]wise man would wilfully come in contact with them. Miss Petrie's

[093-072]ferocity was distressing to him, but her eloquence and enthusiasm

[093-073]were worse even than her ferocity. The personal incivility of which

[093-074]she had been guilty in calling him a withered grass was distasteful

[093-075]to him, as being opposed to his ideas of the customs of society; but

[093-076]what would be his fate if his wife's chosen friend should be for ever

[093-077]dinning her denunciation of withered grasses into his ear?

[093-078]He was still thinking of all this when he was accosted by Mrs.

[093-079]Spalding. "Are you going to dear Lady Banbury's to-morrow?" she

[093-080]asked. Lady Banbury was the wife of the English Minister.

[093-081]"I suppose I shall be there in the course of the evening."

[093-082]"How very nice she is; is she not? I do like Lady Banbury;--so soft,

[093-083]and gentle, and kind."

[093-084]"One of the pleasantest old ladies I know," said Mr. Glascock.

[093-085]"It does not strike you so much as it does me," said Mrs. Spalding,

[093-086]with one of her sweetest smiles. "The truth is, we all value what

[093-087]we have not got. There are no Lady Banburys in our country, and

[093-088]therefore we think the more of them when we meet them here. She is

[093-089]talking of going to Rome for the Carnival, and has asked Caroline

[093-090]to go with her. I am so pleased to find that my dear girl is such a

[093-091]favourite."

[093-092]Mr. Glascock immediately told himself that he saw the hook. If he

[093-093]were to be fished for by this American aunt as he had been fished

[093-094]for by English mothers, all his pleasure in the society of Caroline

[093-095]Spalding would be at once over. It would be too much, indeed, if

[093-096]in this American household he were to find the old vices of an

[093-097]aristocracy superadded to young republican sins! Nevertheless Lady

[093-098]Banbury was, as he knew well, a person whose opinion about young

[093-099]people was supposed to be very good. She noticed those only who were

[093-100]worthy of notice; and to have been taken by the hand by Lady Banbury

[093-101]was acknowledged to be a passport into good society. If Caroline

[093-102]Spalding was in truth going to Rome with Lady Banbury, that fact

[093-103]was in itself a great confirmation of Mr. Glascock's good opinion

[093-104]of her. Mrs. Spalding had perhaps understood this; but had not

[093-105]understood that having just hinted that it was so, she should have

[093-106]abstained from saying a word more about her dear girl. Clever and

[093-107]well-practised must, indeed, be the hand of the fisherwoman in

[093-108]matrimonial waters who is able to throw her fly without showing

[093-109]any glimpse of the hook to the fish for whom she angles. Poor Mrs.

[093-110]Spalding, though with kindly instincts towards her niece she did on

[093-111]this occasion make some slight attempt at angling, was innocent of

[093-112]any concerted plan. It seemed to her to be so natural to say a good

[093-113]word in praise of her niece to the man whom she believed to be in

[093-114]love with her niece.

[093-115]Caroline and Mr. Glascock did not meet each other again till late in

[093-116]the evening, and just as he was about to take his leave. As they came

[093-117]together each of them involuntarily looked round to see whether Miss

[093-118]Petrie was near. Had she been there nothing would have been said

[093-119]beyond the shortest farewell greeting. But Miss Petrie was afar

[093-120]off, electrifying some Italian by the vehemence of her sentiments,

[093-121]and the audacious volubility of a language in which all arbitrary

[093-122]restrictions were ignored. "Are you going?" she asked.

[093-123]"Well;--I believe I am. Since I saw you last I've encountered Miss

[093-124]Petrie again, and I'm rather depressed."

[093-125]"Ah;--you don't know her. If you did you wouldn't laugh at her."

[093-126]"Laugh at her! Indeed I do not do that; but when I'm told that I'm to

[093-127]be thrown into the oven and burned because I'm such a worn-out old

[093-128]institution--"

[093-129]"You don't mean to say that you mind that!"

[093-130]"Not much, when it comes up in the ordinary course of conversation;

[093-131]but it palls upon one when it is asserted for the fourth or fifth

[093-132]time in an evening."

[093-133]"Alas, alas!" exclaimed Miss Spalding, with mock energy.

[093-134]"And why, alas?"

[093-135]"Because it is so impossible to make the oil and vinegar of the old

[093-136]world and of the new mix together and suit each other."

[093-137]"You think it is impossible, Miss Spalding?"

[093-138]"I fear so. We are so terribly tender, and you are always pinching us

[093-139]on our most tender spot. And we never meet you without treading on

[093-140]your gouty toes."

[093-141]"I don't think my toes are gouty," said he.

[093-142]"I apologise to your own, individually, Mr. Glascock; but I must

[093-143]assert that nationally you are subject to the gout."

[093-144]"That is, when I'm told over and over again that I'm to be cut down

[093-145]and thrown into the oven--"

[093-146]"Never mind the oven now, Mr. Glascock. If my friend has been

[093-147]over-zealous I will beg pardon for her. But it does seem to me,

[093-148]indeed it does, with all the reverence and partiality I have

[093-149]for everything European,"--the word European was an offence to

[093-150]him, and he shewed that it was so by his countenance,--"that the

[093-151]idiosyncrasies of you and of us are so radically different, that

[093-152]we cannot be made to amalgamate and sympathise with each other

[093-153]thoroughly."

[093-154]He paused for some seconds before he answered her, but it was so

[093-155]evident by his manner that he was going to speak, that she could

[093-156]neither leave him nor interrupt him. "I had thought that it might

[093-157]have been otherwise," he said at last, and the tone of his voice was

[093-158]so changed as to make her know that he was in earnest.

[093-159]But she did not change her voice by a single note. "I'm afraid it

[093-160]cannot be so," she said, speaking after her old fashion--half in

[093-161]earnest, half in banter. "We may make up our minds to be very civil

[093-162]to each other when we meet. The threats of the oven may no doubt be

[093-163]dropped on our side, and you may abstain from expressing in words

[093-164]your sense of our inferiority."

[093-165]"I never expressed anything of the kind," he said, quite in anger.

[093-166]"I am taking you simply as the sample Englishman, not as Mr.

[093-167]Glascock, who helped me and my sister over the mountains. Such of

[093-168]us as have to meet in society may agree to be very courteous; but

[093-169]courtesy and cordiality are not only not the same, but they are

[093-170]incompatible."

[093-171]"Why so?"

[093-172]"Courtesy is an effort, and cordiality is free. I must be allowed

[093-173]to contradict the friend that I love; but I assent,--too often

[093-174]falsely,--to what is said to me by a passing acquaintance. In spite

[093-175]of what the Scripture says, I think it is one of the greatest

[093-176]privileges of a brother that he may call his brother a fool."

[093-177]"Shall you desire to call your husband a fool?"
