## Section 62

[062-001]Upon this, of course, they spoke to each other. In [[london|London]] they had

[062-002]been well acquainted, each having been an intimate guest at the house

[062-003]of old Lady Milborough. And each knew something of the other's recent

[062-004]history. Mr. Glascock was aware, as was all the world, that Trevelyan

[062-005]had quarrelled with his wife; and Trevelyan was aware that Mr.

[062-006]Glascock had been spoken of as a suitor to his own sister-in-law. Of

[062-007]that visit which Mr. Glascock had made to [[nuncombe-putney|Nuncombe Putney]], and of

[062-008]the manner in which Nora had behaved to her lover, Trevelyan knew

[062-009]nothing. Their greetings spoken, their first topic of conversation

[062-010]was, of course, the injury proposed to be done to the American

[062-011]ladies, and which would now fall upon them. They went into the

[062-012]waiting-room together, and during such toilet as they could make

[062-013]there, grumbled furiously. They would take post horses over the

[062-014]mountain, not from any love of solitary grandeur, but in order that

[062-015]they might make the company pay for its iniquity. But it was soon

[062-016]apparent to them that they themselves had no ground of complaint, and

[062-017]as everybody was very civil, and as a seat in the banquette over the

[062-018]heads of the American ladies was provided for them, and as the man

[062-019]from the bureau came and apologised, they consented to be pacified,

[062-020]and ended, of course, by tipping half-a-dozen of the servants about

[062-021]the yard. Mr. Glascock had a man of his own with him, who was very

[062-022]nearly being put on to the same seat with his master as an extra

[062-023]civility; but this inconvenience was at last avoided. Having settled

[062-024]these little difficulties, they went into breakfast in the buffet.

[062-025]There could be no better breakfast than used to be given in the

[062-026]buffet at the railway terminus at St. Michael. The company might

[062-027]occasionally be led into errors about that question of coupe seats,

[062-028]but in reference to their provisions, they set an example which might

[062-029]be of great use to us here in England. It is probably the case that

[062-030]breakfasts for travellers are not so frequently needed here as they

[062-031]are on the Continent; but, still, there is often to be found a crowd

[062-032]of people ready to eat if only the wherewithal were there. We are

[062-033]often told in our newspapers that England is disgraced by this and

[062-034]by that; by the unreadiness of our army, by the unfitness of our

[062-035]navy, by the irrationality of our laws, by the immobility of our

[062-036]prejudices, and what not; but the real disgrace of England is the

[062-037]railway sandwich,--that whited sepulchre, fair enough outside, but

[062-038]so meagre, poor, and spiritless within, such a thing of shreds and

[062-039]parings, such a dab of food, telling us that the poor bone whence it

[062-040]was scraped had been made utterly bare before it was sent into the

[062-041]kitchen for the soup pot. In France one does get food at the railway

[062-042]stations, and at St. Michael the breakfast was unexceptional.

[062-043]Our two friends seated themselves near to the American ladies, and

[062-044]were, of course, thanked for their politeness. American women are

[062-045]taught by the habits of their country to think that men should give

[062-046]way to them more absolutely than is in accordance with the practices

[062-047]of life in Europe. A seat in a public conveyance in the States, when

[062-048]merely occupied by a man, used to be regarded by any woman as being

[062-049]at her service as completely as though it were vacant. One woman

[062-050]indicating a place to another would point with equal freedom to a man

[062-051]or a space. It is said that this is a little altered now, and that

[062-052]European views on this subject are spreading themselves. Our two

[062-053]ladies, however, who were pretty, clever-looking, and attractive even

[062-054]after the night's journey, were manifestly more impressed with the

[062-055]villainy of the French officials than they were with the kindness of

[062-056]their English neighbours.

[062-057]"And nothing can be done to punish them?" said the younger of them to

[062-058]Mr. Glascock.

[062-059]"Nothing, I should think," said he. "Nothing will, at any rate."

[062-060]"And you will not get back your money?" said the elder,--who, though

[062-061]the elder, was probably not much above twenty.

[062-062]"Well;--no. Time is money, they say. It would take thrice the value

[062-063]of the time in money, and then one would probably fail. They have

[062-064]done very well for us, and I suppose there are difficulties."

[062-065]"It couldn't have taken place in our country," said the younger lady.

[062-066]"All the same, we are very much obliged to you. It would not have

[062-067]been nice for us to have to go up into the banquette."

[062-068]"They would have put you into the interior."

[062-069]"And that would have been worse. I hate being put anywhere,--as if I

[062-070]were a sheep. It seems so odd to us, that you here should be all so

[062-071]tame."

[062-072]"Do you mean the English or the French, or the world in general on

[062-073]this side of the Atlantic?"

[062-074]"We mean Europeans," said the younger lady, who was better after

[062-075]her breakfast. "But then we think that the French have something

[062-076]of compensation, in their manners, and their ways of life, their

[062-077]climate, the beauty of their cities, and their general management of

[062-078]things."

[062-079]"They are very great in many ways, no doubt," said Mr. Glascock.

[062-080]"They do understand living better than you do," said the elder.

[062-081]"Everything is so much brighter with them," said the younger.

[062-082]"They contrive to give a grace to every-day existence," said the

[062-083]elder.

[062-084]"There is such a welcome among them for strangers," said the younger.

[062-085]"Particularly in reference to places taken in the coupe," said

[062-086]Trevelyan, who had hardly spoken before.

[062-087]"Ah, that is an affair of honesty," said the elder. "If we want

[062-088]honesty, I believe we must go back to the stars and stripes."

[062-089]Mr. Glascock looked up from his plate almost aghast. He said nothing,

[062-090]however, but called for the waiter, and paid for his breakfast.

[062-091]Nevertheless, there was a considerable amount of travelling

[062-092]friendship engendered between the ladies and our two friends

[062-093]before the diligence had left the railway yard. They were two Miss

[062-094]Spaldings, going on to Florence, at which place they had an uncle,

[062-095]who was minister from the States to the kingdom of Italy; and they

[062-096]were not at all unwilling to receive such little civilities as

[062-097]gentlemen can give to ladies when travelling. The whole party

[062-098]intended to sleep at Turin that night, and they were altogether on

[062-099]good terms with each other when they started on the journey from St.

[062-100]Michael.

[062-101]"Clever women those," said Mr. Glascock, as soon as they had arranged

[062-102]their legs and arms in the banquette.

[062-103]"Yes, indeed."

[062-104]"American women always are clever,--and are almost always pretty."

[062-105]"I do not like them," said Trevelyan,--who in these days was in a

[062-106]mood to like nothing. "They are exigeant;--and then they are so hard.

[062-107]They want the weakness that a woman ought to have."

[062-108]"That comes from what they would call your insular prejudice. We

[062-109]are accustomed to less self-assertion on the part of women than is

[062-110]customary with them. We prefer women to rule us by seeming to yield.

[062-111]In the States, as I take it, the women never yield, and the men have

[062-112]to fight their own battles with other tactics."

[062-113]"I don't know what their tactics are."

[062-114]"They keep their distance. The men live much by themselves, as though

[062-115]they knew they would not have a chance in the presence of their wives

[062-116]and daughters. Nevertheless they don't manage these things badly. You

[062-117]very rarely hear of an American being separated from his wife."

[062-118]The words were no sooner out of his mouth, than Mr. Glascock knew,

[062-119]and remembered, and felt what he had said. There are occasions in

[062-120]which a man sins so deeply against fitness and the circumstances

[062-121]of the hour, that it becomes impossible for him to slur over his

[062-122]sin as though it had not been committed. There are certain little

[062-123]peccadilloes in society which one can manage to throw behind

[062-124]one,--perhaps with some difficulty, and awkwardness; but still they

[062-125]are put aside, and conversation goes on, though with a hitch. But

[062-126]there are graver offences, the gravity of which strikes the offender

[062-127]so seriously that it becomes impossible for him to seem even to

[062-128]ignore his own iniquity. Ashes must be eaten publicly, and sackcloth

[062-129]worn before the eyes of men. It was so now with poor Mr. Glascock. He

[062-130]thought about it for a moment,--whether or no it was possible that

[062-131]he should continue his remarks about the American ladies, without

[062-132]betraying his own consciousness of the thing that he had done; and

[062-133]he found that it was quite impossible. He knew that he was red up to

[062-134]his hairs, and hot, and that his blood tingled. His blushes, indeed,

[062-135]would not be seen in the seclusion of the banquette; but he could not

[062-136]overcome the heat and the tingling. There was silence for about three

[062-137]minutes, and then he felt that it would be best for him to confess

[062-138]his own fault. "Trevelyan," he said, "I am very sorry for the

[062-139]allusion that I made. I ought to have been less awkward, and I beg

[062-140]your pardon."

[062-141]"It does not matter," said Trevelyan. "Of course I know that

[062-142]everybody is talking of it behind my back. I am not to expect that

[062-143]people will be silent because I am unhappy."

[062-144]"Nevertheless I beg your pardon," said the other.

[062-145]There was but little further conversation between them till they

[062-146]reached Lanslebourg, at the foot of the mountain, at which place they

[062-147]occupied themselves with getting coffee for the two American ladies.

[062-148]The Miss Spaldings took their coffee almost with as much grace as

[062-149]though it had been handed to them by Frenchmen. And indeed they were

[062-150]very gracious,--as is the nature of American ladies in spite of that

[062-151]hardness of which Trevelyan had complained. They assume an intimacy

[062-152]readily, with no appearance of impropriety, and are at their ease

[062-153]easily. When, therefore, they were handed out of their carriage by

[062-154]Mr. Glascock, the bystanders at Lanslebourg might have thought that

[062-155]the whole party had been travelling together from New York. "What

[062-156]should we have done if you hadn't taken pity on us?" said the elder

[062-157]lady. "I don't think we could have climbed up into that high place;

[062-158]and look at the crowd that have come out of the interior. A man has

[062-159]some advantages after all."

[062-160]"I am quite in the dark as to what they are," said Mr. Glascock.

[062-161]"He can give up his place to a lady, and can climb up into a

[062-162]banquette."

[062-163]"And he can be a member of Congress," said the younger. "I'd sooner

[062-164]be senator from Massachusetts than be the Queen of England."

[062-165]"So would I," said Mr. Glascock. "I'm glad we can agree about one

[062-166]thing."

[062-167]The two gentlemen agreed to walk up the mountain together, and with

[062-168]some trouble induced the conductor to permit them to do so. Why

[062-169]conductors of diligences should object to such relief to their horses

[062-170]the ordinary Englishman can hardly understand. But in truth they

[062-171]feel so deeply the [[duty|responsibility]] which attaches itself to their

[062-172]shepherding of their sheep, that they are always fearing lest some

[062-173]poor lamb should go astray on the mountain side. And though the road

[062-174]be broad and very plainly marked, the conductor never feels secure

[062-175]that his passenger will find his way safely to the summit. He likes

[062-176]to know that each of his flock is in his right place, and disapproves

[062-177]altogether of an erratic spirit. But Mr. Glascock at last prevailed,

[062-178]and the two men started together up the mountain. When the permission

[062-179]has been once obtained the walker may be sure that his guide and

[062-180]shepherd will not desert him.

[062-181]"Of course I know," said Trevelyan, when the third twist up the

[062-182]mountain had been overcome, "that people talk about me and my wife.

[062-183]It is a part of the punishment for the mistake that one makes."
