# Brooke Burgess

Brooke Burgess is the great-nephew and designated heir of Miss Jemima Stanbury of Exeter. A young man of just thirty, he arrives in Exeter expecting to secure his inheritance — and instead finds himself genuinely in love with Dorothy Stanbury, in a courtship that represents one of the novel's few uncomplicated romantic successes.

## Overview

Brooke Burgess first appears in the novel as a figure of expectation. Miss Stanbury announces his impending visit with considerable fanfare: "I told you, I think, that Brooke Burgess is coming here?" (050-118). His background places him in the banking firm of Cropper and Burgess — "Brooke Burgess was in the firm" (012-051) — giving him a respectable commercial standing that complements his expectations as Miss Stanbury's heir. At the time of his arrival in Exeter, "Brooke Burgess was a man just turned thirty" (051-001), unmarried and, in Miss Stanbury's view, in need of settling.

His introduction to Exeter society is an immediate success. At Miss Stanbury's dinner table he proves himself affable and engaging: "Brooke Burgess was a complete success" (052-108). His warmth and good humour win over both his formidable aunt and the wider Stanbury circle, setting the stage for his romantic attachment to Dorothy.

## Role in the Novel

Brooke Burgess serves a dual function in the novel's structure. On the plot level, he is the instrument by which Miss Stanbury's fortune will be transmitted to the next generation — a matter of considerable concern to the Stanbury family. On the thematic level, his courtship of Dorothy Stanbury provides a counter-model to the toxic jealousy that destroys the Trevelyan marriage (069-138). Where Louis Trevelyan's love curdles into suspicion and control, Burgess's affection is characterised by honesty, patience, and genuine regard (073-027).

His presence in Exeter also brings the Stanbury family into sharper focus. As an outsider who is nevertheless connected by blood and inheritance, he can observe the family dynamics — Miss Stanbury's imperiousness, Dorothy's quiet virtue, Hugh's independence — with a clarity that insiders cannot.

## Relationships

### Miss Stanbury

Brooke Burgess is the great-nephew of Miss Jemima Stanbury and her designated heir. Their relationship is affectionate but shaped by the inherent tension of inheritance: "Shall I call you Aunt Stanbury," said Brooke Burgess to the old woman (052-072). Miss Stanbury is genuinely fond of him — she pokes him in the ribs, laughs with him, and treats him as the centre of attention (052-165, 052-179) — but she also expects to control the terms of his inheritance, including whom he marries. Burgess navigates this pressure with diplomacy, maintaining his aunt's affection while ultimately following his own heart.

### Dorothy Stanbury

Burgess's courtship of Dorothy Stanbury is the emotional heart of his character arc. Initially introduced to Dorothy as Miss Stanbury's niece and companion, Burgess quickly perceives her qualities: she has "the sweetest temper in the world" (060-081). The narrator confirms the depth of his feeling: "he was very much in love with Dorothy Stanbury" (069-138). Unlike Mr. Gibson's vacillating seven-year flirtation (060-076), Burgess proceeds with earnest intent, seriously contemplating "taking Dorothy Stanbury for his wife" (073-027). Their eventual marriage represents one of the novel's few unequivocally happy resolutions.

### Hugh Stanbury

Burgess and Hugh Stanbury develop a cordial relationship through their shared connection to the Stanbury family. When Burgess asks Hugh for a private conversation, the encounter is amicable and direct: "Brooke Burgess, and then Brooke did ask him" (060-044). Hugh, as Dorothy's brother and a man of independent principles, serves as a natural sounding-board for Burgess's intentions.

## Key Scenes

### Arrival in Exeter

Burgess's arrival at Miss Stanbury's house in Exeter is a set-piece of domestic comedy and social observation. "The hour at which Mr. Brooke Burgess was to arrive had come round" (051-148), and Miss Stanbury's nervous anticipation — combined with her determination not to show it — creates a memorable introduction. His immediate warmth and easy manner — "Very glad to see you," said Mr. Brooke Burgess (052-030) — disarm even his formidable aunt.

### The Successful Dinner

At Miss Stanbury's dinner, Burgess proves himself socially adept: "Brooke Burgess was a complete success" (052-108). His ability to charm the assembled company — including his aunt, who ends the evening "poking Brooke Burgess in the ribs" in high good humour (052-179) — establishes him as a man who can hold his own in Exeter society.

### Falling in Love with Dorothy

Burgess's growing attachment to Dorothy is depicted with subtlety and warmth. The morning after his arrival, "Brooke Burgess had declared aloud" his admiration (057-145), and the narrator later confirms he is "very much in love with Dorothy Stanbury" (069-138). His courtship, conducted under the watchful and not entirely approving eye of Miss Stanbury, is a test of both his sincerity and his diplomacy.

## Character Development

Burgess does not undergo a dramatic transformation; rather, the novel reveals the steadiness of his character through action. He arrives in Exeter as a man with expectations — of inheritance, of position — and leaves it as a man who has earned what he receives. His choice of Dorothy, a woman of limited means but genuine virtue, over a more financially advantageous match, demonstrates a moral independence that the novel endorses. In a story dominated by pathological jealousy and marital breakdown, Brooke Burgess embodies the possibility of straightforward, honourable love.
