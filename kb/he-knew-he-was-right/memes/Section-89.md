## Section 89

[089-001]Miss Stanbury understood it all as well as though she were in perfect

[089-002]health. She knew her own failings, was conscious of her worldly

[089-003]tendencies, and perceived that her old servant was thinking of it.

[089-004]And then sundry odd thoughts, half-digested thoughts, ideas too

[089-005]difficult for her present strength, crossed her brain. Had it been

[089-006]wicked of her when she was well to hope that a scheming woman should

[089-007]not succeed in betraying a man by her schemes into an ill-assorted

[089-008]marriage; and if not wicked then, was it wicked now because she was

[089-009]ill? And from that thought her mind travelled on to the ordinary

[089-010]practices of death-bed piety. Could an assumed devotion be of use to

[089-011]her now,--such a devotion as Martha was enjoining upon her from hour

[089-012]to hour, in pure and affectionate solicitude for her soul? She had

[089-013]spoken one evening of a game of cards, saying that a game of cribbage

[089-014]would have consoled her. Then Martha, with a shudder, had suggested

[089-015]a hymn, and had had recourse at once to a sleeping draught. Miss

[089-016]Stanbury had submitted, but had understood it all. If cards were

[089-017]wicked, she had indeed been a terrible sinner. What hope could there

[089-018]be now, on her death-bed, for one so sinful? And she could not repent

[089-019]of her cards, and would not try to repent of them, not seeing the

[089-020]evil of them; and if they were innocent, why should she not have the

[089-021]consolation now,--when she so much wanted it? Yet she knew that the

[089-022]whole household, even Dorothy, would be in arms against her, were she

[089-023]to suggest such a thing. She took the hymn and the sleeping draught,

[089-024]telling herself that it would be best for her to banish such ideas

[089-025]from her mind. Pastors and masters had laid down for her a mode of

[089-026]living, which she had followed, but indifferently perhaps, but still

[089-027]with an intention of obedience. They had also laid down a mode of

[089-028]dying, and it would be well that she should follow that as closely

[089-029]as possible. She would say nothing more about cards. She would

[089-030]think nothing more of Camilla French. But, as she so resolved, with

[089-031]intellect half asleep, with her mind wandering between fact and

[089-032]dream, she was unconsciously comfortable with an assurance that if

[089-033]Mr. Gibson did marry Camilla French, Camilla French would lead him

[089-034]the very devil of a life.

[089-035]During three days Dorothy went about the house as quiet as a mouse,

[089-036]sitting nightly at her aunt's bedside, and tending the sick woman

[089-037]with the closest care. She, too, had been now and again somewhat

[089-038]startled by the seeming worldliness of her aunt in her illness. Her

[089-039]aunt talked to her about rents, and gave her messages for Brooke

[089-040]Burgess on subjects which seemed to Dorothy to be profane when spoken

[089-041]of on what might perhaps be a death-bed. And this struck her the more

[089-042]strongly, because she had a matter of her own on which she would have

[089-043]much wished to ascertain her aunt's opinion, if she had not thought

[089-044]that it would have been exceedingly wrong of her to trouble her

[089-045]aunt's mind at such a time by any such matter. Hitherto she had said

[089-046]not a word of Brooke's proposal to any living being. At present it

[089-047]was a secret with herself, but a secret so big that it almost caused

[089-048]her bosom to burst with the load that it bore. She could not, she

[089-049]thought, write to Priscilla till she had told her aunt. If she were

[089-050]to write a word on the subject to any one, she could not fail to make

[089-051]manifest the extreme longing of her own heart. She could not have

[089-052]written Brooke's name on paper, in reference to his words to herself,

[089-053]without covering it with epithets of love. But all that must be known

[089-054]to no one if her love was to be of no avail to her. And she had an

[089-055]idea that her aunt would not wish Brooke to marry her,--would think

[089-056]that Brooke should do better; and she was quite clear that in such a

[089-057]matter as this her aunt's wishes must be law. Had not her aunt the

[089-058]power of disinheriting Brooke altogether? And what then if her aunt

[089-059]should die,--should die now,--leaving Brooke at [[personal-freedom|liberty]] to do as he

[089-060]pleased? There was something so distasteful to her in this view of

[089-061]the matter that she would not look at it. She would not allow herself

[089-062]to think of any success which might possibly accrue to herself by

[089-063]reason of her aunt's death. Intense as was the longing in her heart

[089-064]for permission from those in authority over her to give herself to

[089-065]Brooke Burgess, perfect as was the earthly Paradise which appeared to

[089-066]be open to her when she thought of the good thing which had befallen

[089-067]her in that matter, she conceived that she would be guilty of the

[089-068]grossest ingratitude were she in any degree to curtail even her own

[089-069]estimate of her aunt's prohibitory powers because of her aunt's

[089-070]illness. The remembrance of the words which Brooke had spoken to her

[089-071]was with her quite perfect. She was entirely conscious of the joy

[089-072]which would be hers, if she might accept those words as properly

[089-073]sanctioned; but she was a creature in her aunt's hands,--according to

[089-074]her own ideas of her own duties; and while her aunt was ill she could

[089-075]not even learn what might be the behests which she would be called on

[089-076]to obey.

[089-077]She was sitting one evening alone, thinking of all this, having left

[089-078]Martha with her aunt, and was trying to reconcile the circumstances

[089-079]of her life as it now existed with the circumstances as they had been

[089-080]with her in the old days at [[nuncombe-putney|Nuncombe Putney]], wondering at herself in

[089-081]that she should have a lover, and trying to convince herself that for

[089-082]her this little episode of romance could mean nothing serious, when

[089-083]Martha crept down into the room to her. Of late days,--the alteration

[089-084]might perhaps be dated from the rejection of Mr. Gibson,--Martha, who

[089-085]had always been very kind, had become more respectful in her manner

[089-086]to Dorothy than had heretofore been usual with her. Dorothy was quite

[089-087]aware of it, and was not unconscious of a certain rise in the world

[089-088]which was thereby indicated. "If you please, miss," said Martha, "who

[089-089]do you think is here?"

[089-090]"But there is nobody with my aunt?" said Dorothy.

[089-091]"She is sleeping like a babby, and I came down just for a moment.

[089-092]Mr. Gibson is here, miss,--in the house! He asked for your aunt, and

[089-093]when, of course, he could not see her, he asked for you." Dorothy for

[089-094]a few minutes was utterly disconcerted, but at last she consented to

[089-095]see Mr. Gibson. "I think it is best," said Martha, "because it is bad

[089-096]to be fighting, and missus so ill. 'Blessed are the peace-makers,'

[089-097]miss, 'for they shall be called the children of God.'" Convinced by

[089-098]this argument, or by the working of her own mind, Dorothy directed

[089-099]that Mr. Gibson might be shewn into the room. When he came, she found

[089-100]herself unable to address him. She remembered the last time in which

[089-101]she had seen him, and was lost in wonder that he should be there. But

[089-102]she shook hands with him, and went through some form of greeting in

[089-103]which no word was uttered.

[089-104]"I hope you will not think that I have done wrong," said he, "in

[089-105]calling to ask after my old friend's state of health?"

[089-106]"Oh dear, no," said Dorothy, quite bewildered.

[089-107]"I have known her for so very long, Miss Dorothy, that now in the

[089-108]hour of her distress, and perhaps mortal malady, I cannot stop to

[089-109]remember the few harsh words that she spoke to me lately."

[089-110]"She never means to be harsh, Mr. Gibson."

[089-111]"Ah; well; no,--perhaps not. At any rate, I have learned to forgive

[089-112]and forget. I am afraid your aunt is very ill, Miss Dorothy."

[089-113]"She is ill, certainly, Mr. Gibson."

[089-114]"Dear, dear! We are all as the grass of the field, Miss

[089-115]Dorothy,--here to-day and gone to-morrow, as sparks fly upwards. Just

[089-116]fit to be cut down and cast into the oven. Mr. Jennings has been with

[089-117]her, I believe?" Mr. Jennings was the other minor canon.

[089-118]"He comes three times a week, Mr. Gibson."

[089-119]"He is an excellent young man,--a very good young man. It has been a

[089-120]great comfort to me to have Jennings with me. But he's very young,

[089-121]Miss Dorothy; isn't he?" Dorothy muttered something, purporting to

[089-122]declare that she was not acquainted with the exact circumstances of

[089-123]Mr. Jennings' age. "I should be so glad to come if my old friend

[089-124]would allow me," said Mr. Gibson, almost with a sigh. Dorothy was

[089-125]clearly of opinion that any change at the present would be bad for

[089-126]her aunt, but she did not know how to express her opinion; so she

[089-127]stood silent and looked at him. "There needn't be a word spoken, you

[089-128]know, about the ladies at Heavitree," said Mr. Gibson.

[089-129]"Oh dear, no," said Dorothy. And yet she knew well that there would

[089-130]be such words spoken if Mr. Gibson were to make his way into her

[089-131]aunt's room. Her aunt was constantly alluding to the ladies at

[089-132]Heavitree, in spite of all the efforts of her old servant to restrain

[089-133]her.

[089-134]"There was some little misunderstanding," said Mr. Gibson; "but all

[089-135]that should be over now. We both intended for the best, Miss Dorothy;

[089-136]and I'm sure nobody here can say that I wasn't sincere." But Dorothy,

[089-137]though she could not bring herself to answer Mr. Gibson plainly,

[089-138]could not be induced to assent to his proposition. She muttered

[089-139]something about her aunt's weakness, and the great attention which

[089-140]Mr. Jennings shewed. Her aunt had become very fond of Mr. Jennings,

[089-141]and she did at last express her opinion, with some clearness, that

[089-142]her aunt should not be disturbed by any changes at present. "After

[089-143]that I should not think of pressing it, Miss Dorothy," said Mr.

[089-144]Gibson; "but, still, I do hope that I may have the privilege of

[089-145]seeing her yet once again in the flesh. And touching my approaching

[089-146]marriage, Miss Dorothy--" He paused, and Dorothy felt that she

[089-147]was blushing up to the roots of her hair. "Touching my marriage,"

[089-148]continued Mr. Gibson, "which however will not be solemnized till the

[089-149]end of March;"--it was manifest that he regarded this as a point that

[089-150]would in that household be regarded as an argument in his favour,--"I

[089-151]do hope that you will look upon it in the most favourable light,--and

[089-152]your excellent aunt also, if she be spared to us."

[089-153]"I am sure we hope that you will be happy, Mr. Gibson."

[089-154]"What was I to do, Miss Dorothy? I know that I have been very much

[089-155]blamed;--but so unfairly! I have never meant to be untrue to a mouse,

[089-156]Miss Dorothy." Dorothy did not at all understand whether she were the

[089-157]mouse, or Camilla French, or Arabella. "And it is so hard to find

[089-158]that one is ill-spoken of because things have gone a little amiss."

[089-159]It was quite impossible that Dorothy should make any answer to this,

[089-160]and at last Mr. Gibson left her, assuring her with his last word that

[089-161]nothing would give him so much pleasure as to be called upon once

[089-162]more to see his old friend in her last moments.

[089-163]Though Miss Stanbury had been described as sleeping "like a babby,"

[089-164]she had heard the footsteps of a strange man in the house, and had

[089-165]made Martha tell her whose footsteps they were. As soon as Dorothy

[089-166]went to her, she darted upon the subject with all her old keenness.

[089-167]"What did he want here, Dolly?"

[089-168]"He said he would like to see you, aunt,--when you are a little

[089-169]better, you know. He spoke a good deal of his old friendship and

[089-170]respect."
