## Section 139

[139-001]It was very sad, because it was necessary that some decision

[139-002]should be made as to the future residence of Mrs. Trevelyan and of

[139-003]Nora. Emily had declared that nothing should induce her to go to

[139-004]the Islands with her father and mother unless her boy went with

[139-005]her. Since her journey to Casalunga she had also expressed her

[139-006]unwillingness to leave her husband. Her heart had been greatly

[139-007]softened towards him, and she had declared that where he remained,

[139-008]there would she remain,--as near to him as circumstances would admit.

[139-009]It might be that at last her care would be necessary for his comfort.

[139-010]He supplied her with means of living, and she would use these means

[139-011]as well as she might be able in his service.

[139-012]Then there had arisen the question of Nora's future residence. And

[139-013]there had come troubles and storms in the family. Nora had said that

[139-014]she would not go back to the Mandarins, but had not at first been

[139-015]able to say where or how she would live. She had suggested that

[139-016]she might stay with her sister, but her father had insisted that

[139-017]she could not live on the [[money|income]] supplied by Trevelyan. Then, when

[139-018]pressed hard, she had declared that she intended to live on Hugh

[139-019]Stanbury's income. She would marry him at once,--with her father's

[139-020]leave, if she could get it, but without it if it needs must be so.

[139-021]Her mother told her that [[hugh-stanbury|Hugh Stanbury]] was not himself ready for her;

[139-022]he had not even proposed so hasty a marriage, nor had he any home

[139-023]fitted for her. Lady Rowley, in arguing this, had expressed no assent

[139-024]to the marriage, even as a distant arrangement, but had thought

[139-025]thus to vanquish her daughter by suggesting small but insuperable

[139-026]difficulties. On a sudden, however, Lady Rowley found that all

[139-027]this was turned against her, by an offer that came direct from Mr.

[139-028]Glascock. His Caroline, he said, was very anxious that Nora should

[139-029]come to them at Monkhams as soon as they had returned home from

[139-030]Switzerland. They intended to be there by the middle of August, and

[139-031]would hurry there sooner, if there was any intermediate difficulty

[139-032]about finding a home for Nora. Mr. Glascock said nothing about Hugh

[139-033]Stanbury; but, of course, Lady Rowley understood that Nora had told

[139-034]all her troubles and hopes to Caroline, and that Caroline had told

[139-035]them to her future husband. Lady Rowley, in answer to this, could

[139-036]only say that she would consult her husband.

[139-037]There was something very grievous in the proposition to Lady Rowley.

[139-038]If Nora had not been self-willed and stiff-necked beyond the usual

[139-039]self-willedness and stiff-neckedness of young women she might have

[139-040]been herself the mistress of Monkhams. It was proposed now that she

[139-041]should go there to wait till a poor man should have got together

[139-042]shillings enough to buy a few chairs and tables, and a bed to lie

[139-043]upon! The thought of this was very bitter. "I cannot think, Nora, how

[139-044]you could have the heart to go there," said Lady Rowley.

[139-045]"I cannot understand why not, mamma. Caroline and I are friends, and

[139-046]surely he and I need not be enemies. He has never injured me; and if

[139-047]he does not take offence, why should I?"

[139-048]"If you don't see it, I can't help it," said Lady Rowley.

[139-049]And then Mrs. Spalding's triumph was terrible to Lady Rowley. Mrs.

[139-050]Spalding knew nothing of her future son-in-law's former passion, and

[139-051]spoke of her Caroline as having achieved triumphs beyond the reach of

[139-052]other girls. Lady Rowley bore it, never absolutely telling the tale

[139-053]of her daughter's fruitless victory. She was too good at heart to

[139-054]utter the boast;--but it was very hard to repress it. Upon the whole

[139-055]she would have preferred that Mr. Glascock and his bride should not

[139-056]have become the fast friends of herself and her family. There was

[139-057]more of pain than of pleasure in the alliance. But circumstances

[139-058]had been too strong for her. Mr. Glascock had been of great use in

[139-059]reference to Trevelyan, and Caroline and Nora had become attached

[139-060]to each other almost on their first acquaintance. Here they were

[139-061]together at the Baths of Lucca, and Nora was to be one of the four

[139-062]bridesmaids. When Sir Marmaduke was consulted about this visit to

[139-063]Monkhams, he became fretful, and would give no answer. The marriage,

[139-064]he said, was impossible, and Nora was a fool. He could give her no

[139-065]allowance more than would suffice for her clothes, and it was madness

[139-066]for her to think of stopping in England. But he was so full of cares

[139-067]that he could come to no absolute decision on this matter. Nora,

[139-068]however, had come to a very absolute decision.

[139-069]"Caroline," she said, "if you will have me, I will go to Monkhams."

[139-070]"Of course we will have you. Has not Charles said how delighted he

[139-071]would be?"

[139-072]"Oh yes,--your Charles," said Nora, laughing.

[139-073]"He is mine now, dear. You must not expect him to change his mind

[139-074]again. I gave him the chance, you know, and he would not take it.

[139-075]But, Nora, come to Monkhams, and stay as long as it suits. I have

[139-076]talked it all over with him, and we both agree that you shall have

[139-077]a home there. You shall be just like a sister. Olivia is coming too

[139-078]after a bit; but he says there is room for a dozen sisters. Of course

[139-079]it will be all right with Mr. Stanbury after a while." And so it was

[139-080]settled among them that [[nora-rowley|Nora Rowley]] should find a home at Monkhams,

[139-081]if a home in England should be wanted for her.

[139-082]It wanted but four days to that fixed for the marriage at Florence,

[139-083]and but six to that on which the Rowleys were to leave Italy for

[139-084]England, when Mr. Glascock received Trevelyan's letter. It was

[139-085]brought to him as he was sitting at a late breakfast in the garden

[139-086]of the hotel; and there were present at the moment not only all the

[139-087]Spalding family, but the Rowleys also. Sir Marmaduke was there and

[139-088]Lady Rowley, and the three unmarried daughters; but Mrs. Trevelyan,

[139-089]as was her wont, had remained alone in her own room. Mr. Glascock

[139-090]read the letter, and read it again, without attracting much

[139-091]attention. Caroline, who was of course sitting next to him, had her

[139-092]eyes upon him, and could see that the letter moved him; but she was

[139-093]not curious, and at any rate asked no question. He himself understood

[139-094]fully how great was the offer made,--how all-important to the

[139-095]happiness of the poor mother,--and he was also aware, or thought

[139-096]that he was aware, how likely it might be that the offer would be

[139-097]retracted. As regarded himself, a journey from the Baths at Lucca to

[139-098]Casalunga and back before his marriage, would be a great infliction

[139-099]on his patience. It was his plan to stay where he was till the day

[139-100]before his marriage, and then to return to Florence with the rest

[139-101]of the party. All this must be altered, and sudden changes must be

[139-102]made, if he decided on going to Siena himself. The weather now was

[139-103]very hot, and such a journey would be most disagreeable to him. Of

[139-104]course he had little schemes in his head, little amatory schemes

[139-105]for praenuptial enjoyment, which, in spite of his mature years,

[139-106]were exceedingly agreeable to him. The chestnut woods round the

[139-107]Baths of Lucca are very pleasant in the early summer, and there

[139-108]were excursions planned in which Caroline would be close to his

[139-109]side,--almost already his wife. But, if he did not go, whom could he

[139-110]send? It would be necessary at least that he should consult her, the

[139-111]mother of the child, before any decision was formed.

[139-112]At last he took Lady Rowley aside, and read to her the letter. She

[139-113]understood at once that it opened almost a heaven of bliss to her

[139-114]daughter;--and she understood also how probable it might be that that

[139-115]wretched man, with his shaken wits, should change his mind. "I think

[139-116]I ought to go," said Mr. Glascock.

[139-117]"But how can you go now?"

[139-118]"I can go," said he. "There is time for it. It need not put off my

[139-119]marriage,--to which of course I could not consent. I do not know whom

[139-120]I could send."

[139-121]"Monnier could go," said Lady Rowley, naming the courier.

[139-122]"Yes;--he could go. But it might be that he would return without

[139-123]the child, and then we should not forgive ourselves. I will go,

[139-124]Lady Rowley. After all, what does it signify? I am a little old, I

[139-125]sometimes think, for this philandering. You shall take this letter to

[139-126]your daughter, and I will explain it all to Caroline."

[139-127]Caroline had not a word to say. She could only kiss him, and promise

[139-128]to make him what amends she could when he came back. "Of course you

[139-129]are right," she said. "Do you think that I would say a word against

[139-130]it, even though the marriage were to be postponed?"

[139-131]"I should;--a good many words. But I will be back in time for that,

[139-132]and will bring the boy with me."

[139-133]Mrs. Trevelyan, when her husband's letter was read to her, was almost

[139-134]overcome by the feelings which it excited. In her first paroxysm of

[139-135]joy she declared that she would herself go to Siena, not for her

[139-136]child's sake, but for that of her husband. She felt at once that the

[139-137]boy was being given up because of the father's weakness,--because

[139-138]he felt himself to be unable to be a protector to his son,--and

[139-139]her woman's heart was melted with softness as she thought of the

[139-140]condition of the man to whom she had once given her whole heart.

[139-141]Since then, doubtless, her heart had revolted from him. Since that

[139-142]time there had come hours in which she had almost hated him for his

[139-143]cruelty to her. There had been moments in which she had almost cursed

[139-144]his name because of the aspersion which it had seemed that he had

[139-145]thrown upon her. But this was now forgotten, and she remembered only

[139-146]his weakness. "Mamma," she said, "I will go. It is my duty to go to

[139-147]him." But Lady Rowley withheld her, explaining that were she to go,

[139-148]the mission might probably fail in its express purpose. "Let Louey be

[139-149]sent to us first," said Lady Rowley, "and then we will see what can

[139-150]be done afterwards."

[139-151]And so Mr. Glascock started, taking with him a maid-servant who might

[139-152]help him with the charge of the child. It was certainly very hard

[139-153]upon him. In order to have time for his journey to Siena and back,

[139-154]and time also to go out to Casalunga, it was necessary that he should

[139-155]leave the Baths at five in the morning. "If ever there was a hero of

[139-156]romance, you are he!" said Nora to him.

[139-157]"The heroes of life are so much better than the heroes of romance,"

[139-158]said Caroline.

[139-159]"That is a lesson from the lips of the American Browning," said Mr.

[139-160]Glascock. "Nevertheless, I think I would rather ride a charge against

[139-161]a Paynim knight in Palestine than get up at half-past four in the

[139-162]morning."

[139-163]"We will get up too, and give the knight his coffee," said Nora.

[139-164]They did get up, and saw him off; and when Mr. Glascock and Caroline

[139-165]parted with a lover's embrace, Nora stood by as a sister might have

[139-166]done. Let us hope that she remembered that her own time was coming.

[139-167]There had been a promise given by Nora, when she left [[london|London]], that

[139-168]she would not correspond with Hugh Stanbury while she was in Italy,

[139-169]and this promise had been kept. It may be remembered that Hugh had

[139-170]made a proposition to his lady-love, that she should walk out of the

[139-171]house one fine morning, and get herself married without any reference

[139-172]to her father's or her mother's wishes. But she had not been willing

[139-173]to take upon herself as yet [[personal-freedom|independence]] so complete as this would

[139-174]have required. She had assured her lover that she did mean to marry

[139-175]him some day, even though it should be in opposition to her father,

[139-176]but that she thought that the period for filial persuasion was not

[139-177]yet over; and then, in explaining all this to her mother, she had

[139-178]given a promise neither to write nor to receive letters during the

[139-179]short period of her sojourn in Italy. She would be an obedient child

[139-180]for so long;--but, after that, she must claim the right to fight her

[139-181]own battle. She had told her lover that he must not write; and, of

[139-182]course, she had not written a word herself. But now, when her mother

[139-183]threw it in her teeth that Stanbury would not be ready to marry her,

[139-184]she thought that an unfair advantage was being taken of her,--and of

[139-185]him. How could he be expected to say that he was ready,--deprived as

[139-186]he was of the power of saying anything at all?
