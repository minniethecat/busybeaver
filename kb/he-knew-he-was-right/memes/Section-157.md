## Section 157

[157-001]"Then we will have the mutton-chops at home," said Nora, "and you

[157-002]shall go to your club for the beef-steak."

[157-003]When they reached Eccleston Square, Nora insisted on taking Hugh

[157-004]Stanbury up to Lady Milborough. It was in vain that he pleaded that

[157-005]he had come all the way from Dover on a very dusty day,--all the way

[157-006]from Dover, including a journey in a Hansom cab to Twickenham and

[157-007]back, without washing his hands and face. Nora insisted that Lady

[157-008]Milborough was such a dear, good, considerate creature, that she

[157-009]would understand all that, and Hugh was taken into her presence. "I

[157-010]am delighted to see you, Mr. Stanbury," said the old lady, "and hope

[157-011]you will think that Nora is in good keeping."

[157-012]"She has been telling me how very kind you have been to her. I do not

[157-013]know where she could have bestowed herself if you had not received

[157-014]her."

[157-015]"There, Nora;--I told you he would say so. I won't tell tales, Mr.

[157-016]Stanbury; but she had all manner of wild plans which I knew you

[157-017]wouldn't approve. But she is very amiable, and if she will only

[157-018]submit to you as well as she does to me--"

[157-019]"I don't mean to submit to him at all, Lady Milborough;--of course

[157-020]not. I am going to marry for [[personal-freedom|liberty]]."

[157-021]"My dear, what you say, you say in joke; but a great many young women

[157-022]of the present day do, I really believe, go up to the altar and

[157-023]pronounce their marriage vows, with the simple idea that as soon as

[157-024]they have done so, they are to have their own way in everything. And

[157-025]then people complain that young men won't marry! Who can wonder at

[157-026]it?"

[157-027]"I don't think the young men think much about the obedience," said

[157-028]Nora. "Some marry for money, and some for love. But I don't think

[157-029]they marry to get a slave."

[157-030]"What do you say, Mr. Stanbury?" asked the old lady.

[157-031]"I can only assure you that I shan't marry for money," said he.

[157-032]Two or three days after this Nora left her friend in Eccleston

[157-033]Square, and domesticated herself for awhile with her sister. Mrs.

[157-034]Trevelyan declared that such an arrangement would be comfortable

[157-035]for her, and that it was very desirable now, as Nora would so soon

[157-036]be beyond her reach. Then Lady Milborough was enabled to go to

[157-037]Dorsetshire, which she did not do, however, till she had presented

[157-038]Nora with the veil which she was to wear on the occasion of her

[157-039]wedding. "Of course I cannot see it, my dear, as it is to take place

[157-040]at Monkhams; but you must write and tell me the day;--and I will

[157-041]think of you. And you, when you put on the veil, must think of me."

[157-042]So they parted, and Nora knew that she had made a friend for life.

[157-043][Illustration: Nora's veil.]

[157-044]When she first took her place in the house at Twickenham as a

[157-045]resident, Trevelyan did not take much notice of her;--but, after

[157-046]awhile, he would say a few words to her, especially when it might

[157-047]chance that she was with him in her sister's absence. He would speak

[157-048]of dear Emily, and poor Emily, and shake his head slowly, and talk of

[157-049]the pity of it. "The pity of it, Iago; oh, the pity of it," he said

[157-050]once. The allusion to her was so terrible that she almost burst out

[157-051]in anger, as she would have done formerly. She almost told him that

[157-052]he had been as wrong throughout as was the jealous husband in the

[157-053]play whose words he quoted, and that his jealousy, if continued, was

[157-054]likely to be as tragical. But she restrained herself, and kept close

[157-055]to her needle,--making, let us hope, an auspicious garment for Hugh

[157-056]Stanbury. "She has seen it now," he continued; "she has seen it now."

[157-057]Still she went on with her hemming in silence. It certainly could not

[157-058]be her duty to upset at a word all that her sister had achieved. "You

[157-059]know that she has confessed?" he asked.

[157-060]"Pray, pray do not talk about it, Louis."

[157-061]"I think you ought to know," he said. Then she rose from her seat and

[157-062]left the room. She could not stand it, even though he were mad,--even

[157-063]though he were dying!

[157-064]She went to her sister and repeated what had been said. "You had

[157-065]better not notice it," said Emily. "It is only a [[truth|proof]] of what I told

[157-066]you. There are times in which his mind is as active as ever it was,

[157-067]but it is active in so terrible a direction!"

[157-068]"I cannot sit and hear it. And what am I to say when he asks me a

[157-069]question as he did just now? He said that you had confessed."

[157-070]"So I have. Do none confess but the guilty? What is all that we have

[157-071]read about the Inquisition and the old tortures? I have had to learn

[157-072]that torturing has not gone out of the world;--that is all."

[157-073]"I must go away if he says the same thing to me so again."

[157-074]"That is nonsense, Nora. If I can bear it, cannot you? Would you have

[157-075]me drive him into violence again by disputing with him upon such a

[157-076]subject?"

[157-077]"But he may recover;--and then he will remember what you have said."

[157-078]"If he recovers altogether he will suspect nothing. I must take my

[157-079]chance of that. You cannot suppose that I have not thought about it.

[157-080]I have often sworn to myself that though the world should fall around

[157-081]me, nothing should make me acknowledge that I had ever been untrue

[157-082]to my duty as a married woman, either in deed, or word, or thought.

[157-083]I have no doubt that the poor wretches who were tortured in their

[157-084]cells used to make the same resolutions as to their confessions. But

[157-085]yet, when their nails were dragged out of them, they would own to

[157-086]anything. My nails have been dragged out, and I have been willing to

[157-087]confess anything. When he talks of the pity of it, of course I know

[157-088]what he means. There has been something, some remainder of a feeling,

[157-089]which has still kept him from asking me that question. May God, in

[157-090]his mercy, continue to him that feeling!"

[157-091]"But you would answer truly?"

[157-092]"How can I say what I might answer when the torturer is at my nails?

[157-093]If you knew how great was the difficulty to get him away from that

[157-094]place in Italy and bring him here; and what it was to feel that one

[157-095]was bound to stay near him, and that yet one was impotent,--and to

[157-096]know that even that refuge must soon cease for him, and that he might

[157-097]have gone out and died on the road-side, or have done anything which

[157-098]the momentary strength of madness might have dictated,--if you could

[157-099]understand all this, you would not be surprised at my submitting to

[157-100]any degradation which would help to bring him here."

[157-101]Stanbury was often down at the cottage, and Nora could discuss the

[157-102]matter better with him than with her sister. And Stanbury could learn

[157-103]more thoroughly from the physician who was now attending Trevelyan

[157-104]what was the state of the sick man, than Emily could do. According

[157-105]to the doctor's idea there was more of ailment in the body than in

[157-106]the mind. He admitted that his patient's thoughts had been forced

[157-107]to dwell on one subject till they had become distorted, untrue,

[157-108]jaundiced, and perhaps mono-maniacal; but he seemed to doubt whether

[157-109]there had ever been a time at which it could have been decided that

[157-110]Trevelyan was so mad as to make it necessary that the law should

[157-111]interfere to take care of him. A man,--so argued the doctor,--need

[157-112]not be mad because he is jealous, even though his jealousy be ever

[157-113]so absurd. And Trevelyan, in his jealousy, had done nothing cruel,

[157-114]nothing wasteful, nothing infamous. In all this Nora was very little

[157-115]inclined to agree with the doctor, and thought nothing could be more

[157-116]infamous than Trevelyan's conduct at the present moment,--unless,

[157-117]indeed, he could be screened from infamy by that plea of madness.

[157-118]But then there was more behind. Trevelyan had been so wasted by the

[157-119]kind of life which he had led, and possessed by nature stamina so

[157-120]insufficient to resist such debility, that it was very doubtful

[157-121]whether he would not sink altogether before he could be made to

[157-122]begin to rise. But one thing was clear. He should be contradicted in

[157-123]nothing. If he chose to say that the moon was made of green cheese,

[157-124]let it be conceded to him that the moon was made of green cheese.

[157-125]Should he make any other assertion equally removed from the truth,

[157-126]let it not be contradicted. Who would oppose a man with one foot in

[157-127]the grave?

[157-128]"Then, Hugh, the sooner I am at Monkhams the better," said Nora, who

[157-129]had again been subjected to inuendoes which had been unendurable to

[157-130]her. This was on the 7th of August, and it still wanted three days to

[157-131]that on which the journey to Monkhams was to be made.

[157-132]"He never says anything to me on the subject," said Hugh.

[157-133]"Because you have made him afraid of you. I almost think that Emily

[157-134]and the doctor are wrong in their treatment, and that it would be

[157-135]better to stand up to him and tell him the truth." But the three days

[157-136]passed away, and Nora was not driven to any such vindication of her

[157-137]sister's [[honour|character]] towards her sister's husband.

[157-138]CHAPTER XCVI.

[157-139]MONKHAMS.

[157-140][Illustration]

[157-141]On the 10th of August [[nora-rowley|Nora Rowley]] left the cottage by the river-side

[157-142]at Twickenham, and went down to Monkhams. The reader need hardly be

[157-143]told that Hugh brought her up from Twickenham and sent her off in the

[157-144]railway carriage. They agreed that no day could be fixed for their

[157-145]marriage till something further should be known of Trevelyan's state.

[157-146]While he was in his present condition such a marriage could not have

[157-147]been other than very sad. Nora, when she left the cottage, was still

[157-148]very bitter against her brother-in-law, quoting the doctor's opinion

[157-149]as to his sanity, and expressing her own as to his conduct under that

[157-150]supposition. She also believed that he would rally in health, and was

[157-151]therefore, on that account, less inclined to pity him than was his

[157-152]wife. [[emily-trevelyan|Emily Trevelyan]] of course saw more of him than did her sister,

[157-153]and understood better how possible it was that a man might be in such

[157-154]a condition as to be neither mad nor sane;--not mad, so that all

[157-155]power over his own actions need be taken from him; nor sane, so

[157-156]that he must be held to be accountable for his words and thoughts.

[157-157]Trevelyan did nothing, and attempted to do nothing, that could injure

[157-158]his wife and child. He submitted himself to medical advice. He did

[157-159]not throw away his money. He had no Bozzle now waiting at his heels.

[157-160]He was generally passive in his wife's hands as to all outward

[157-161]things. He was not violent in rebuke, nor did he often allude to

[157-162]their past unhappiness. But he still maintained, by a word spoken

[157-163]every now and then, that he had been right throughout in his contest

[157-164]with his wife,--and that his wife had at last acknowledged that it

[157-165]was so. She never contradicted him, and he became bolder and bolder

[157-166]in his assertions, endeavouring on various occasions to obtain some

[157-167]expression of an assent from Nora. But Nora would not assent, and he

[157-168]would scowl at her, saying words, both in her presence and behind her

[157-169]back, which implied that she was his enemy. "Why not yield to him?"

[157-170]her sister said the day before she went. "I have yielded, and your

[157-171]doing so cannot make it worse."

[157-172]"I can't do it. It would be false. It is better that I should go

[157-173]away. I cannot pretend to agree with him, when I know that his mind

[157-174]is working altogether under a delusion." When the hour for her

[157-175]departure came, and Hugh was waiting for her, she thought that it

[157-176]would be better that she should go, without seeing Trevelyan. "There

[157-177]will only be more anger," she pleaded. But her sister would not be

[157-178]contented that she should leave the house in this fashion, and urged

[157-179]at last, with tears running down her cheeks, that this might possibly

[157-180]be the last interview between them.
