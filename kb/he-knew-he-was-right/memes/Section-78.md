## Section 78

[078-001]Arabella French was painfully alive to the fact that she must do

[078-002]something. She had her fish on the hook; but of what use is a fish

[078-003]on your hook, if you cannot land him? When could she have a better

[078-004]opportunity than this of landing the scaly darling out of the fresh

[078-005]and free waters of his bachelor stream, and sousing him into the pool

[078-006]of domestic life, to be ready there for her own household purposes?

[078-007]"I had known you so long, Mr. Gibson," she said, "and had valued your

[078-008]friendship so--so deeply." As he looked at her he could see nothing

[078-009]but the shapeless excrescence to which his eyes had been so painfully

[078-010]called by Miss Stanbury's satire. It is true that he had formerly

[078-011]been very tender with her, but she had not then carried about with

[078-012]her that distorted monster. He did not believe himself to be at all

[078-013]bound by anything which had passed between them in circumstances

[078-014]so very different. But yet he ought to say something. He ought to

[078-015]have said something; but he said nothing. She was patient, however,

[078-016]very patient; and she went on playing him with her hook. "I am so

[078-017]glad that I did not go out to-night with mamma. It has been such a

[078-018]pleasure to me to have this conversation with you. Camilla, perhaps,

[078-019]would say that I am--unmaidenly."

[078-020]"I don't think so."

[078-021]"That is all that I care for, Mr. Gibson. If you acquit me, I do

[078-022]not mind who accuses. I should not like to suppose that you thought

[078-023]me unmaidenly. Anything would be better than that; but I can throw

[078-024]all such considerations to the wind when true--true--friendship is

[078-025]concerned. Don't you think that one ought, Mr. Gibson?"

[078-026]If it had not been for the thing at the back of her head, he would

[078-027]have done it now. Nothing but that gave him courage to abstain.

[078-028]It grew bigger and bigger, more shapeless, monstrous, absurd, and

[078-029]abominable, as he looked at it. Nothing should force upon him the

[078-030]necessity of assisting to carry such an abortion through the world.

[078-031]"One ought to sacrifice everything to friendship," said Mr. Gibson,

[078-032]"except [[pride|self-respect]]."

[078-033]He meant nothing personal. Something special, in the way of an

[078-034]opinion, was expected of him; and, therefore, he had striven to

[078-035]say something special. But she was in tears in a moment. "Oh, Mr.

[078-036]Gibson," she exclaimed; "oh, Mr. Gibson!"

[078-037]"What is the matter, Miss French?"

[078-038]"Have I lost your respect? Is it that that you mean?"

[078-039]"Certainly not, Miss French."

[078-040]"Do not call me Miss French, or I shall be sure that you condemn me.

[078-041]Miss French sounds so very cold. You used to call me--Bella." That

[078-042]was quite true; but it was long ago, thought Mr. Gibson,--before the

[078-043]monster had been attached. "Will you not call me Bella now?"

[078-044]He thought that he had rather not; and yet, how was he to avoid it?

[078-045]On a sudden he became very crafty. Had it not been for the sharpness

[078-046]of his mother wit, he would certainly have been landed at that

[078-047]moment. "As you truly observed just now," he said, "the tongues

[078-048]of people are so malignant. There are little birds that hear

[078-049]everything."

[078-050]"I don't care what the little birds hear," said Miss French, through

[078-051]her tears. "I am a very unhappy girl;--I know that; and I don't care

[078-052]what anybody says. It is nothing to me what anybody says. I know what

[078-053]I feel." At this moment there was some dash of truth about her. The

[078-054]fish was so very heavy on hand that, do what she would, she could

[078-055]not land him. Her hopes before this had been very low,--hopes that

[078-056]had once been high; but they had been depressed gradually; and, in

[078-057]the slow, dull routine of her daily life, she had learned to bear

[078-058]disappointment by degrees, without sign of outward suffering, without

[078-059]consciousness of acute pain. The task of her life had been weary,

[078-060]and the wished-for goal was ever becoming more and more distant;

[078-061]but there had been still a chance, and she had fallen away into a

[078-062]lethargy of lessening expectation, from which joy, indeed, had been

[078-063]banished, but in which there had been nothing of agony. Then had

[078-064]come upon the whole house at Heavitree the great Stanbury peril, and,

[078-065]arising out of that, had sprung new hopes to Arabella, which made her

[078-066]again capable of all the miseries of a foiled ambition. She could

[078-067]again be patient, if patience might be of any service; but in such a

[078-068]condition an eternity of patience is simply suicidal. She was willing

[078-069]to work hard, but how could she work harder than she had worked. Poor

[078-070]young woman,--perishing beneath an incubus which a false idea of

[078-071]fashion had imposed on her!

[078-072]"I hope I have said nothing that makes you unhappy," pleaded Mr.

[078-073]Gibson. "I'm sure I haven't meant it."

[078-074]"But you have," she said. "You make me very unhappy. You condemn me.

[078-075]I see you do. And if I have done wrong it has been all because-- Oh

[078-076]dear, oh dear, oh dear!"

[078-077]"But who says you have done wrong?"

[078-078]"You won't call me Bella,--because you say the little birds will hear

[078-079]it. If I don't care for the little birds, why should you?"

[078-080]There is no question more difficult than this for a [[social-class|gentleman]] to

[078-081]answer. Circumstances do not often admit of its being asked by a lady

[078-082]with that courageous simplicity which had come upon Miss French in

[078-083]this moment of her agonising struggle; but nevertheless it is one

[078-084]which, in a more complicated form, is often put, and to which some

[078-085]reply, more or less complicated, is expected. "If I, a woman, can

[078-086]dare, for your sake, to encounter the public tongue, will you, a man,

[078-087]be afraid?" The true answer, if it could be given, would probably

[078-088]be this; "I am afraid, though a man, because I have much to lose

[078-089]and little to get. You are not afraid, though a woman, because you

[078-090]have much to get and little to lose." But such an answer would be

[078-091]uncivil, and is not often given. Therefore men shuffle and lie, and

[078-092]tell themselves that in love,--love here being taken to mean all

[078-093]antenuptial contests between man and woman,--everything is fair. Mr.

[078-094]Gibson had the above answer in his mind, though he did not frame

[078-095]it into words. He was neither sufficiently brave nor sufficiently

[078-096]cruel to speak to her in such language. There was nothing for him,

[078-097]therefore, but that he must shuffle and lie.

[078-098]"I only meant," said he, "that I would not for worlds do anything to

[078-099]make you uneasy."

[078-100]She did not see how she could again revert to the subject of her own

[078-101]Christian name. She had made her little tender, loving request, and

[078-102]it had been refused. Of course she knew that it had been refused as a

[078-103]matter of caution. She was not angry with him because of his caution,

[078-104]as she had expected him to be cautious. The barriers over which

[078-105]she had to climb were no more than she had expected to find in her

[078-106]way;--but they were so very high and so very difficult! Of course she

[078-107]was aware that he would escape if he could. She was not angry with

[078-108]him on that account. Anger could not have helped her. Indeed, she did

[078-109]not price herself highly enough to make her feel that she would be

[078-110]justified in being angry. It was natural enough that he shouldn't

[078-111]want her. She knew herself to be a poor, thin, vapid, tawdry

[078-112]creature, with nothing to recommend her to any man except a sort

[078-113]of second-rate, provincial-town fashion which,--infatuated as she

[078-114]was,--she attributed in a great degree to the thing she carried on

[078-115]her head. She knew nothing. She could do nothing. She possessed

[078-116]nothing. She was not angry with him because he so evidently wished to

[078-117]avoid her. But she thought that if she could only be successful she

[078-118]would be good and loving and obedient,--and that it was fair for her

[078-119]at any rate to try. Each created animal must live and get its food

[078-120]by the gifts which the Creator has given to it, let those gifts be

[078-121]as poor as they may,--let them be even as distasteful as they may to

[078-122]other members of the great created family. The rat, the toad, the

[078-123]slug, the flea, must each live according to its appointed mode of

[078-124]existence. Animals which are parasites by nature can only live by

[078-125]attaching themselves to life that is strong. To Arabella Mr. Gibson

[078-126]would be strong enough, and it seemed to her that if she could fix

[078-127]herself permanently upon his strength, that would be her proper

[078-128]mode of living. She was not angry with him because he resisted

[078-129]the attempt, but she had nothing of conscience to tell her that

[078-130]she should spare him as long as there remained to her a chance of

[078-131]success. And should not her plea of excuse, her justification be

[078-132]admitted? There are tormentors as to which no man argues that they

[078-133]are iniquitous, though they be very troublesome. He either rids

[078-134]himself of them, or suffers as quiescently as he may.

[078-135]"We used to be such--great--friends," she said, still crying, "and I

[078-136]am afraid you don't like me a bit now."

[078-137]"Indeed I do;--I have always liked you. But--"

[078-138]"But what? Do tell me what the but means. I will do anything that you

[078-139]bid me."

[078-140]Then it occurred to him that if, after such a promise, he were to

[078-141]confide to her his feeling that the chignon which she wore was ugly

[078-142]and unbecoming, she would probably be induced to change her mode of

[078-143]head-dress. It was a foolish idea, because, had he followed it out,

[078-144]he would have seen that compliance on her part in such a matter could

[078-145]only be given with the distinct understanding that a certain reward

[078-146]should be the consequence. When an unmarried gentleman calls upon an

[078-147]unmarried lady to change the fashion of her personal adornments, the

[078-148]unmarried lady has a right to expect that the unmarried gentleman

[078-149]means to make her his wife. But Mr. Gibson had no such meaning; and

[078-150]was led into error by the necessity for sudden action. When she

[078-151]offered to do anything that he might bid her do, he could not take up

[078-152]his hat and go away. She looked up into his face, expecting that he

[078-153]would give her some order;--and he fell into the temptation that was

[078-154]spread for him.

[078-155]"If I might say a word,--" he began.

[078-156]"You may say anything," she exclaimed.

[078-157]"If I were you I don't think--"

[078-158]"You don't think what, Mr. Gibson?"

[078-159]He found it to be a matter very difficult of approach. "Do you know,

[078-160]I don't think the fashion that has come up about wearing your hair

[078-161]quite suits you,--not so well as the way you used to do it." She

[078-162]became on a sudden very red in the face, and he thought that she was

[078-163]angry. Vexed she was, but still, accompanying her vexation, there

[078-164]was a remembrance that she was achieving victory even by her own

[078-165]humiliation. She loved her chignon; but she was ready to abandon even

[078-166]that for him. Nevertheless she could not speak for a moment or two,

[078-167]and he was forced to continue his criticism. "I have no doubt those

[078-168]things are very becoming and all that, and I dare say they are

[078-169]comfortable."

[078-170]"Oh, very," she said.

[078-171]"But there was a simplicity that I liked about the other."

[078-172]Could it be then that for the last five years he had stood aloof from

[078-173]her because she had arrayed herself in fashionable attire? She was

[078-174]still very red in the face, still suffering from wounded vanity,

[078-175]still conscious of that soreness which affects us all when we are

[078-176]made to understand that we are considered to have failed there, where

[078-177]we have most thought that we excelled. But her womanly art enabled

[078-178]her quickly to conceal the pain. "I have made a promise," she said,

[078-179]"and you will find that I will keep it."
