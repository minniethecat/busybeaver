## Section 99

[099-001]"If he wheeled a big barrow, you'd have to wheel a little one," said

[099-002]her sister.

[099-003]"Then I'd do it. I shouldn't mind. There has been this advantage in

[099-004]St. Diddulph's, that nothing can be triste, nothing dull, nothing

[099-005]ugly after it."

[099-006]"It may be so with you, Nora;--that is in imagination."

[099-007]"What I mean is that living here has taught me much that I never

[099-008]could have learned in Curzon Street. I used to think myself such a

[099-009]fine young woman,--but, upon my word, I think myself a finer one

[099-010]now."

[099-011]"I don't quite know what you mean."

[099-012]"I don't quite know myself; but I nearly know. I do know this, that

[099-013]I've made up my own mind about what I mean to do."

[099-014]"You'll change it, dear, when mamma is here, and things are

[099-015]comfortable again. It's my belief that Mr. Glascock would come to you

[099-016]again to-morrow if you would let him." Mrs. Trevelyan was, naturally,

[099-017]in complete ignorance of the experience of transatlantic excellence

[099-018]which Mr. Glascock had encountered in Italy.

[099-019]"But I certainly should not let him. How would it be possible after

[099-020]what I wrote to Hugh?"

[099-021]"All that might pass away," said Mrs. Trevelyan,--slowly, after a

[099-022]long pause.

[099-023]"All what might pass away? Have I not given him a distinct promise?

[099-024]Have I not told him that I loved him, and sworn that I would be true

[099-025]to him? Can that be made to pass away,--even if one wished it?"

[099-026]"Of course it can. Nothing need be fixed for you till you have stood

[099-027]at the altar with a man and been made his wife. You may choose still.

[099-028]I can never choose again."

[099-029]"I never will, at any rate," said Nora.

[099-030]Then there was another pause. "It seems strange to me, Nora," said

[099-031]the elder sister, "that after what you have seen you should be so

[099-032]keen to be married to any one."

[099-033]"What is a girl to do?"

[099-034]"Better drown herself than do as I have done. Only think what there

[099-035]is before me. What I have gone through is nothing to it. Of course I

[099-036]must go back to the Islands. Where else am I to live? Who else will

[099-037]take me?"

[099-038]"Come to us," said Nora.

[099-039]"Us, Nora! Who are the us? But in no way would that be possible.

[099-040]Papa will be here, perhaps, for six months." Nora thought it quite

[099-041]possible that she might have a home of her own before six months were

[099-042]passed,--even though she might be wheeling the smaller barrow,--but

[099-043]she would not say so. "And by that time everything must be decided."

[099-044]"I suppose it must."

[099-045]"Of course papa and mamma must go back," said Mrs. Trevelyan.

[099-046]"Papa might take a pension. He's entitled to a pension now."

[099-047]"He'll never do that as long as he can have employment. They'll go

[099-048]back, and I must go with them. Who else would take me in?"

[099-049]"I know who would take you in, Emily."

[099-050]"My darling, that is romance. As for myself, I should not care where

[099-051]I went. If it were even to remain here, I could bear it."

[099-052]"I could not," said Nora, decisively.

[099-053]"It is so different with you, dear. I don't suppose it is possible

[099-054]I should take my boy with me to the Islands; and how--am I--to

[099-055]go--anywhere--without him?" Then she broke down, and fell into a

[099-056]paroxysm of sobs, and was in very truth a broken-hearted woman.

[099-057]Nora was silent for some minutes, but at last she spoke. "Why do you

[099-058]not go back to him, Emily?"

[099-059]"How am I to go back to him? What am I to do to make him take me

[099-060]back?" At this very moment Trevelyan was in the house, but they did

[099-061]not know it.

[099-062]"Write to him," said Nora.

[099-063]"What am I to say? In very truth I do believe that he is mad. If I

[099-064]write to him, should I defend myself or accuse myself? A dozen times

[099-065]I have striven to write such a letter,--not that I might send it, but

[099-066]that I might find what I could say should I ever wish to send it. And

[099-067]it is impossible. I can only tell him how unjust he has been, how

[099-068]cruel, how mad, how wicked!"

[099-069]"Could you not say to him simply this?--'Let us be together, wherever

[099-070]it may be; and let bygones be bygones.'"

[099-071]"While he is watching me with a policeman? While he is still thinking

[099-072]that I entertain a--lover? While he believes that I am the base thing

[099-073]that he has dared to think me?"

[099-074]"He has never believed it."

[099-075]"Then how can he be such a villain as to treat me like this? I could

[099-076]not go to him, Nora;--not unless I went to him as one who was known

[099-077]to be mad, over whom in his wretched condition it would be my duty

[099-078]to keep watch. In no other way could I overcome my abhorrence of the

[099-079]outrages to which he has subjected me."

[099-080]"But for the child's sake, Emily."

[099-081]"Ah, yes! If it were simply to grovel in the dust before him it

[099-082]should be done. If humiliation would suffice,--or any self-abasement

[099-083]that were possible to me! But I should be false if I said that I look

[099-084]forward to any such possibility. How can he wish to have me back

[099-085]again after what he has said and done? I am his wife, and he has

[099-086]disgraced me before all men by his own words. And what have I done,

[099-087]that I should not have done;--what left undone on his behalf that

[099-088]I should have done? It is hard that the foolish workings of a weak

[099-089]man's mind should be able so completely to ruin the prospects of a

[099-090]woman's life!"

[099-091]Nora was beginning to answer this by attempting to shew that the

[099-092]husband's madness was, perhaps, only temporary, when there came a

[099-093]knock at the door, and Mrs. Outhouse was at once in the room. It

[099-094]will be well that the reader should know what had taken place at the

[099-095]parsonage while the two sisters had been together up-stairs, so that

[099-096]the nature of Mrs. Outhouse's mission to them may explain itself. Mr.

[099-097]Outhouse had been in his closet down-stairs, when the maid-servant

[099-098]brought word to him that Mr. Trevelyan was in the parlour, and was

[099-099]desirous of seeing him.

[099-100]"Mr. Trevelyan!" said the unfortunate clergyman, holding up both his

[099-101]hands. The servant understood the tragic importance of the occasion

[099-102]quite as well as did her master, and simply shook her head. "Has your

[099-103]mistress seen him?" said the master. The girl again shook her head.

[099-104]"Ask your mistress to come to me," said the clergyman. Then the girl

[099-105]disappeared; and in a few minutes Mrs. Outhouse, equally imbued with

[099-106]the tragic elements of the day, was with her husband.

[099-107]Mr. Outhouse began by declaring that no consideration should induce

[099-108]him to see Trevelyan, and commissioned his wife to go to the man and

[099-109]tell him that he must leave the house. When the unfortunate woman

[099-110]expressed an opinion that Trevelyan had some legal rights upon which

[099-111]he might probably insist, Mr. Outhouse asserted roundly that he could

[099-112]have no legal right to remain in that parsonage against the will

[099-113]of the rector. "If he wants to claim his wife and child, he must

[099-114]do it by law,--not by force; and thank God, Sir Marmaduke will be

[099-115]here before he can do that." "But I can't make him go," said Mrs.

[099-116]Outhouse. "Tell him that you'll send for a policeman," said the

[099-117]clergyman.

[099-118]It had come to pass that there had been messages backwards and

[099-119]forwards between the visitor and the master of the house, all carried

[099-120]by that unfortunate lady. Trevelyan did not demand that his wife and

[099-121]child should be given up to him;--did not even, on this occasion,

[099-122]demand that his boy should be surrendered to him,--now, at once.

[099-123]He did say, very repeatedly, that of course he must have his boy,

[099-124]but seemed to imply that, under certain circumstances, he would

[099-125]be willing to take his wife to live with him again. This appeared

[099-126]to Mrs. Outhouse to be so manifestly the one thing that was

[099-127]desirable,--to be the only solution of the difficulty that could be

[099-128]admitted as a solution at all,--that she went to work on that hint,

[099-129]and ventured to entertain a hope that a reconciliation might be

[099-130]effected. She implored her husband to lend a hand to the work;--by

[099-131]which she intended to imply that he should not only see Trevelyan,

[099-132]but consent to meet the sinner on friendly terms. But Mr. Outhouse

[099-133]was on the occasion even more than customarily obstinate. His wife

[099-134]might do what she liked. He would neither meddle nor make. He would

[099-135]not willingly see Mr. Trevelyan in his own house;--unless, indeed,

[099-136]Mr. Trevelyan should attempt to force his way up into the nursery.

[099-137]Then he said that which left no doubt on his wife's mind that, should

[099-138]any violence be attempted, her husband would manfully join the melee.

[099-139]But it soon became evident that no such attempt was to be made on

[099-140]that day. Trevelyan was lachrymose, heartbroken, and a sight pitiable

[099-141]to behold. When Mrs. Outhouse loudly asserted that his wife had not

[099-142]sinned against him in the least,--"not in a tittle, Mr. Trevelyan,"

[099-143]she repeated over and over again,--he began to assert himself,

[099-144]declaring that she had seen the man in Devonshire, and corresponded

[099-145]with him since she had been at St. Diddulph's; and when the lady had

[099-146]declared that the latter assertion was untrue, he had shaken his

[099-147]head, and had told her that perhaps she did not know all. But the

[099-148]misery of the man had its effect upon her, and at last she proposed

[099-149]to be the bearer of a message to his wife. He had demanded to see his

[099-150]child, offering to promise that he would not attempt to take the boy

[099-151]by force on this occasion,--saying, also, that his claim by law was

[099-152]so good, that no force could be necessary. It was proposed by Mrs.

[099-153]Outhouse that he should first see the mother,--and to this he at last

[099-154]assented. How blessed a thing would it be if these two persons could

[099-155]be induced to forget the troubles of the last twelve months, and once

[099-156]more to love and trust each other! "But, sir," said Mrs. Outhouse,

[099-157]putting her hand upon his arm;--"you must not upbraid her, for

[099-158]she will not bear it." "She knows nothing of what is due to a

[099-159]husband," said Trevelyan, gloomily. The task was not hopeful; but,

[099-160]nevertheless, the poor woman resolved to do her best.

[099-161]And now Mrs. Outhouse was in her niece's room, asking her to go down

[099-162]and see her husband. Little Louis had at the time been with the

[099-163]nurse, and the very moment that the mother heard that the child's

[099-164]father was in the house, she jumped up and rushed away to get

[099-165]possession of her treasure. "Has he come for baby?" Nora asked in

[099-166]dismay. Then Mrs. Outhouse, anxious to obtain a convert to her

[099-167]present views, boldly declared that Mr. Trevelyan had no such

[099-168]intention. Mrs. Trevelyan came back at once with the boy, and then

[099-169]listened to all her aunt's arguments. "But I will not take baby with

[099-170]me," she said. At last it was decided that she should go down alone,

[099-171]and that the child should afterwards be taken to his father in the

[099-172]drawing-room; Mrs. Outhouse pledging herself that the whole household

[099-173]should combine in her defence if Mr. Trevelyan should attempt to take

[099-174]the child out of that room. "But what am I to say to him?" she asked.

[099-175]"Say as little as possible," said Mrs. Outhouse,--"except to make him

[099-176]understand that he has been in error in imputing fault to you."

[099-177]"He will never understand that," said Mrs. Trevelyan.

[099-178]A considerable time elapsed after that before she could bring herself

[099-179]to descend the stairs. Now that her husband was so near her, and that

[099-180]her aunt had assured her that she might reinstate herself in her

[099-181]position, if she could only abstain from saying hard words to him,

[099-182]she wished that he was away from her again, in Italy. She knew that

[099-183]she could not refrain from hard words. How was it possible that she

[099-184]should vindicate her own honour, without asserting with all her

[099-185]strength that she had been ill-used; and, to speak truth on the

[099-186]matter, her love for the man, which had once been true and eager, had

[099-187]been quelled by the treatment she had received. She had clung to her

[099-188]love in some shape, in spite of the accusations made against her,

[099-189]till she had heard that the policeman had been set upon her heels.

[099-190]Could it be possible that any woman should love a man, or at least

[099-191]that any wife should love a husband, after such usage as that? At

[099-192]last she crept gently down the stairs, and stood at the parlour-door.

[099-193]She listened, and could hear his steps, as he paced backwards and

[099-194]forwards through the room. She looked back, and could see the face

[099-195]of the servant peering round from the kitchen-stairs. She could not

[099-196]endure to be watched in her misery, and, thus driven, she opened the

[099-197]parlour-door. "Louis," she said, walking into the room, "Aunt Mary

[099-198]has desired me to come to you."
