## Section 76

[076-001]Everybody meets everybody in Florence every day. Carry and Livy

[076-002]Spalding had met Mr. Glascock twice before the dinner at their

[076-003]uncle's house, so that they met at dinner quite as intimate friends.

[076-004]Mrs. Spalding had very large rooms, up three flights of stairs, on

[076-005]the Lungarno. The height of her abode was attributed by Mrs. Spalding

[076-006]to her dread of mosquitoes. She had not yet learned that people in

[076-007]Florence require no excuse for being asked to walk up three flights

[076-008]of stairs. The rooms, when they were reached, were very lofty,

[076-009]floored with what seemed to be marble, and were of a nature almost to

[076-010]warrant Mrs. Spalding in feeling that nature had made her more akin

[076-011]to an Italian countess than to a matron of Nubbly Creek, State of

[076-012]Illinois, where Mr. Spalding had found her and made her his own.

[076-013]There was one other Englishman present, Mr. Harris Hyde Granville

[076-014]Gore, from the Foreign Office, now serving temporarily at the English

[076-015]Legation in Florence; and an American, Mr. Jackson Unthank, a man of

[076-016][[money|wealth]] and taste, who was resolved on having such a collection of

[076-017]pictures at his house in Baltimore that no English private collection

[076-018]should in any way come near to it; and a Tuscan, from the Italian

[076-019]Foreign Office, to whom nobody could speak except Mr. Harris Hyde

[076-020]Granville Gore,--who did not indeed seem to enjoy the efforts of

[076-021]conversation which were expected of him. The Italian, who had a

[076-022]handle to his name,--he was a Count Buonarosci,--took Mrs. Spalding

[076-023]in to dinner. Mrs. Spalding had been at great trouble to ascertain

[076-024]whether this was proper, or whether she should not entrust herself

[076-025]to Mr. Glascock. There were different points to be considered in

[076-026]the matter. She did not quite know whether she was in Italy or in

[076-027]America. She had glimmerings on the subject of her privilege to carry

[076-028]her own nationality into her own drawing-room. And then she was

[076-029]called upon to deal between an Italian Count with an elder brother,

[076-030]and an English Honourable, who had no such incumbrance. Which of the

[076-031]two was possessed of the higher [[social-class|rank]]? "I've found it all out, Aunt

[076-032]Mary," said Livy. "You must take the Count." For Livy wanted to give

[076-033]her sister every chance. "How have you found it out?" said the aunt.

[076-034]"You may be sure it is so," said Livy. And the lady in her doubt

[076-035]yielded the point. Mrs. Spalding, as she walked along the passage on

[076-036]the Count's arm, determined that she would learn Italian. She would

[076-037]have given all Nubbly Creek to have been able to speak a word to

[076-038]Count Buonarosci. To do her justice, it must be admitted that she had

[076-039]studied a few words. But her courage failed her, and she could not

[076-040]speak them. She was very careful, however, that Mr. H. H. G. Gore was

[076-041]placed in the chair next to the Count.

[076-042]"We are very glad to see you here," said Mr. Spalding, addressing

[076-043]himself especially to Mr. Glascock, as he stood up at his own seat at

[076-044]the round table. "In leaving my own country, sir, there is nothing

[076-045]that I value more than the privilege of becoming acquainted with

[076-046]those whose historic names and existing positions are of such

[076-047]inestimable value to the world at large." In saying this, Mr.

[076-048]Spalding was not in the least insincere, nor did his conscience at

[076-049]all prick him in reference to that speech at Nubbly Creek. On both

[076-050]occasions he half thought as he spoke,--or thought that he thought

[076-051]so. Unless it be on subjects especially endeared to us the thoughts

[076-052]of but few of us go much beyond this.

[076-053]Mr. Glascock, who sat between Mrs. Spalding and her niece, was soon

[076-054]asked by the elder lady whether he had been in the States. No; he

[076-055]had not been in the States. "Then you must come, Mr. Glascock," said

[076-056]Mrs. Spalding, "though I will not say, dwelling as we now are in the

[076-057]metropolis of the world of art, that we in our own homes have as much

[076-058]of the outer beauty of form to charm the stranger as is to be found

[076-059]in other lands. Yet I think that the busy lives of men, and the

[076-060]varied institutions of a free country, must always have an interest

[076-061]peculiarly their own." Mr. Glascock declared that he quite agreed

[076-062]with her, and expressed a hope that he might some day find himself in

[076-063]New York.

[076-064]"You wouldn't like it at all," said Carry; "because you are an

[076-065]aristocrat. I don't mean that it would be your fault."

[076-066]"Why should that prevent my liking it,--even if I were an

[076-067]aristocrat?"

[076-068]"One half of the people would run after you, and the other half would

[076-069]run away from you," said Carry.

[076-070]"Then I'd take to the people who ran after me, and would not regard

[076-071]the others."

[076-072]"That's all very well,--but you wouldn't like it. And then you would

[076-073]become unfair to what you saw. When some of our speechifying people

[076-074]talked to you about our institutions through their noses, you would

[076-075]think that the institutions themselves must be bad. And we have

[076-076]nothing to show except our institutions."

[076-077]"What are American institutions?" asked Mr. Glascock.

[076-078]"Everything is an institution. Having iced water to drink in every

[076-079]room of the house is an institution. Having hospitals in every town

[076-080]is an institution. Travelling altogether in one class of railway

[076-081]cars is an institution. Saying sir, is an institution. Teaching all

[076-082]the children mathematics is an institution. Plenty of food is an

[076-083]institution. Getting drunk is an institution in a great many towns.

[076-084]Lecturing is an institution. There are plenty of them, and some are

[076-085]very good;--but you wouldn't like it."

[076-086]"At any rate, I'll go and see," said Mr. Glascock.

[076-087]"If you do, I hope we may be at home," said Miss Spalding.

[076-088]Mr. Spalding, in the mean time, with the assistance of his

[076-089]countryman, the man of taste, was endeavouring to explain a certain

[076-090]point in American politics to the Count. As, in doing this, they

[076-091]called upon Mr. Gore to translate every speech they made into

[076-092]Italian, and as Mr. Gore had never offered his services as an

[076-093]interpreter, and as the Italian did not quite catch the subtle

[076-094]meanings of the Americans in Mr. Gore's Tuscan version, and did not

[076-095]in the least wish to understand the things that were explained to

[076-096]him, Mr. Gore and the Italian began to think that the two Americans

[076-097]were bores. "The truth is, Mr. Spalding," said Mr. Gore, "I've got

[076-098]such a cold in my head, that I don't think I can explain it any

[076-099]more." Then Livy Spalding laughed aloud, and the two American

[076-100]gentlemen began to eat their dinner. "It sounds ridiculous, don't

[076-101]it?" said Mr. Gore, in a whisper.

[076-102]"I ought not to have laughed, I know," said Livy.

[076-103]"The very best thing you could have done. I shan't be troubled any

[076-104]more now. The fact is, I know just nine words of Italian. Now there

[076-105]is a difficulty in having to explain the whole theory of American

[076-106]politics to an Italian, who doesn't want to know anything about it,

[076-107]with so very small a repertory of words at one's command."

[076-108]"How well you did it!"

[076-109]"Too well. I felt that. So well that, unless I had stopped it, I

[076-110]shouldn't have been able to say a word to you all through dinner.

[076-111]Your laughter clenched it, and Buonarosci and I will be grateful to

[076-112]you for ever."

[076-113]After the ladies went there was rather a bad half hour for Mr.

[076-114]Glascock. He was button-holed by the minister, and found it

[076-115]oppressive before he was enabled to escape into the drawing-room.

[076-116]"Mr. Glascock," said the minister, "an English gentleman, sir,

[076-117]like you, who has the privilege of an hereditary seat in your

[076-118]parliament,"--Mr. Glascock was not quite sure whether he were being

[076-119]accused of having an hereditary seat in the House of Commons, but he

[076-120]would not stop to correct any possible error on that point,--"and who

[076-121]has been born to all the gifts of fortune, rank, and social eminence,

[076-122]should never think that his education is complete till he has visited

[076-123]our great cities in the west." Mr. Glascock hinted that he by no

[076-124]means conceived his education to be complete; but the minister went

[076-125]on without attending to this. "Till you have seen, sir, what men

[076-126]can do who are placed upon the earth with all God's gifts of free

[076-127]intelligence, free air, and a free soil, but without any of those

[076-128]other good things which we are accustomed to call the gifts of

[076-129]fortune, you can never become aware of the infinite ingenuity of

[076-130]man." There had been much said before, but just at this moment Mr.

[076-131]Gore and the American left the room, and the Italian followed them

[076-132]briskly. Mr. Glascock at once made a decided attempt to bolt; but the

[076-133]minister was on the alert, and was too quick for him. And he was by

[076-134]no means ashamed of what he was doing. He had got his guest by the

[076-135]coat, and openly declared his intention of holding him. "Let me keep

[076-136]you for a few minutes, sir," said he, "while I dilate on this point

[076-137]in one direction. In the drawing-room female spells are too potent

[076-138]for us male orators. In going among us, Mr. Glascock, you must not

[076-139]look for luxury or refinement, for you will find them not. Nor must

[076-140]you hope to encounter the highest order of erudition. The lofty

[076-141]summits of acquired [[truth|knowledge]] tower in your country with an altitude

[076-142]we have not reached yet."

[076-143]"It's very good of you to say so," said Mr. Glascock.

[076-144]"No, sir. In our new country and in our new cities we still lack the

[076-145]luxurious perfection of fastidious civilisation. But, sir, regard our

[076-146]level. That is what I say to every unprejudiced Britisher that comes

[076-147]among us; look at our level. And when you have looked at our level,

[076-148]I think that you will confess that we live on the highest table-land

[076-149]that the world has yet afforded to mankind. You follow my meaning,

[076-150]Mr. Glascock?" Mr. Glascock was not sure that he did, but the

[076-151]minister went on to make that meaning clear. "It is the multitude

[076-152]that with us is educated. Go into their houses, sir, and see how they

[076-153]thumb their books. Look at the domestic correspondence of our helps

[076-154]and servants, and see how they write and spell. We haven't got the

[076-155]mountains, sir, but our table-lands are the highest on which the

[076-156]bright sun of our Almighty God has as yet shone with its illuminating

[076-157]splendour in this improving world of ours! It is because we are a

[076-158]young people, sir,--with nothing as yet near to us of the decrepitude

[076-159]of age. The weakness of age, sir, is the penalty paid by the folly of

[076-160]youth. We are not so wise, sir, but what we too shall suffer from its

[076-161]effects as years roll over our heads." There was a great deal more,

[076-162]but at last Mr. Glascock did escape into the drawing-room.

[076-163]"My uncle has been saying a few words to you perhaps," said Carry

[076-164]Spalding.

[076-165]"Yes; he has," said Mr. Glascock.

[076-166]"He usually does," said Carry Spalding.

[076-170]The feud between Miss Stanbury and Mr. Gibson raged violently in

[076-171][[exeter|Exeter]], and produced many complications which were very difficult

[076-172]indeed of management. Each belligerent party felt that a special

[076-173]injury had been inflicted upon it. Mr. Gibson was quite sure that he

[076-174]had been grossly misused by Miss Stanbury the elder, and strongly

[076-175]suspected that Miss Stanbury the younger had had a hand in this

[076-176]misconduct. It had been positively asserted to him,--at least so he

[076-177]thought, but in this was probably in error,--that the lady would

[076-178]accept him if he proposed to her. All Exeter had been made aware of

[076-179]the intended compact. He, indeed, had denied its existence to Miss

[076-180]French, comforting himself, as best he might, with the reflection

[076-181]that all is fair in love and war; but when he counted over his

[076-182]injuries he did not think of this denial. All Exeter, so to say, had

[076-183]known of it. And yet, when he had come with his proposal, he had been

[076-184]refused without a moment's consideration, first by the aunt, and then

[076-185]by the niece;--and, after that, had been violently abused, and at

[076-186]last turned out of the house! Surely, no gentleman had ever before

[076-187]been subjected to ill-usage so violent! But Miss Stanbury the elder

[076-188]was quite as assured that the injury had been done to her. As to the

[076-189]matter of the compact itself, she knew very well that she had been as

[076-190]true as steel. She had done everything in her power to bring about

[076-191]the marriage. She had been generous in her offers of money. She had

[076-192]used all her powers of persuasion on Dorothy, and she had given

[076-193]every opportunity to Mr. Gibson. It was not her fault if he had not

[076-194]been able to avail himself of the good things which she had put in

[076-195]his way. He had first been, as she thought, ignorant and arrogant,

[076-196]fancying that the good things ought to be made his own without any

[076-197]trouble on his part;--and then awkward, not knowing how to take the

[076-198]trouble when trouble was necessary. And as to that matter of abusive

[076-199]language and turning out of the house, Miss Stanbury was quite

[076-200]convinced that she was sinned against, and not herself the sinner.

[076-201]She declared to Martha, more than once, that Mr. Gibson had used such

[076-202]language to her that, coming out of a clergyman's mouth, it had quite

[076-203]dismayed her. Martha, who knew her mistress, probably felt that Mr.

[076-204]Gibson had at least received as good as he gave; but she had made no

[076-205]attempt to set her mistress right on that point.
