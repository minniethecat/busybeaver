## Section 104

[104-001]"I do not think, Louis, that she has ever denied your right to be

[104-002]master."

[104-003]"To be master in my own house, and to be paramount in my influence

[104-004]over her. So much I had a right to demand."

[104-005]"Who has denied your right?"

[104-006]"She has submitted herself to the counsels and to the influences of a

[104-007]man who has endeavoured to undermine me in her affection. In saying

[104-008]that I make my accusation as light against her as is possible. I

[104-009]might make it much heavier, and yet not sin against the truth."

[104-010]"This is an illusion, Louis."

[104-011]"Ah;--well. No doubt it becomes you to defend your child. Was it

[104-012]an illusion when he went to Devonshire? Was it an illusion when he

[104-013]corresponded with her,--contrary to my express orders,--both before

[104-014]and after that unhallowed journey? Lady Rowley, there must be no more

[104-015]such illusions. If my wife means to come back to me, and to have her

[104-016]child in her own hands, she must be penitent as regards the past, and

[104-017]obedient as regards the future."

[104-018]There was a wicked bitterness in that word penitent which almost

[104-019]maddened Lady Rowley. She had come to this meeting believing that

[104-020]Trevelyan would be rejoiced to take back his wife, if details could

[104-021]be arranged for his doing so which should not subject him to the

[104-022]necessity of crying, peccavi; but she found him speaking of his wife

[104-023]as though he would be doing her the greatest possible favour in

[104-024]allowing her to come back to him dressed in sackcloth, and with ashes

[104-025]on her head. She could understand from what she had heard that his

[104-026]tone and manner were much changed since he obtained possession of

[104-027]the child, and that he now conceived that he had his wife within his

[104-028]power. That he should become a tyrant because he had the power to

[104-029]tyrannise was not in accordance with her former conception of the

[104-030]man's [[honour|character]];--but then he was so changed, that she felt that

[104-031]she knew nothing of the man who now stood before her. "I cannot

[104-032]acknowledge that my daughter has done anything that requires

[104-033]penitence," said Lady Rowley.

[104-034]"I dare say not; but my view is different."

[104-035]"She cannot admit herself to be wrong when she knows herself to be

[104-036]right. You would not have her confess to a fault, the very idea of

[104-037]which has always been abhorrent to her?"

[104-038]"She must be crushed in spirit, Lady Rowley, before she can again

[104-039]become a pure and happy woman."

[104-040]"This is more than I can bear," said Lady Rowley, now, at last,

[104-041]worked up to a fever of indignation. "My daughter, sir, is as pure a

[104-042]woman as you have ever known, or are likely to know. You, who should

[104-043]have protected her against the world, will some day take blame to

[104-044]yourself as you remember that you have so cruelly maligned her." Then

[104-045]she walked away to the door, and would not listen to the words which

[104-046]he was hurling after her. She went down the stairs, and out of the

[104-047]house, and at the end of Poulter's Alley found the cab which was

[104-048]waiting for her.

[104-049]Trevelyan, as soon as he was alone, rang the bell, and sent for

[104-050]Bozzle. And while the waiter was coming to him, and until his

[104-051]myrmidon had appeared, he continued to stalk up and down the room,

[104-052]waving his hand in the air as though he were continuing his speech.

[104-053]"Bozzle," said he, as soon as the man had closed the door, "I have

[104-054]changed my mind."

[104-055]"As how, Mr. Trewillian?"

[104-056]"I shall make no further attempt. I have done all that man can do,

[104-057]and have done it in vain. Her father and mother uphold her in her

[104-058]conduct, and she is lost to me,--for ever."

[104-059]"But the boy, Mr. T.?"

[104-060]"I have my child. Yes,--I have my child. Poor infant. Bozzle, I look

[104-061]to you to see that none of them learn our retreat."

[104-062]"As for that, Mr. Trewillian,--why facts is to be come at by one

[104-063]party pretty well as much as by another. Now, suppose the things

[104-064]was changed, wicey warsey,--and as I was hacting for the Colonel's

[104-065]party."

[104-066]"D---- the Colonel!" exclaimed Trevelyan.

[104-067]"Just so, Mr. Trewillian; but if I was hacting for the other party,

[104-068]and they said to me, 'Bozzle,--where's the boy?' why, in three days

[104-069]I'd be down on the facts. Facts is open, Mr. Trewillian, if you knows

[104-070]where to look for them."

[104-071]"I shall take him abroad,--at once."

[104-072]"Think twice of it, Mr. T. The boy is so young, you see, and a

[104-073]mother's 'art is softer and lovinger than anything. I'd think twice

[104-074]of it, Mr. T., before I kept 'em apart." This was a line of thought

[104-075]which Mr. Bozzle's conscience had not forced him to entertain to the

[104-076]prejudice of his professional arrangements; but now, as he conversed

[104-077]with his employer, and became by degrees aware of the failure of

[104-078]Trevelyan's mind, some shade of remorse came upon him, and made him

[104-079]say a word on behalf of the "other party."

[104-080]"Am I not always thinking of it? What else have they left me to think

[104-081]of? That will do for to-day. You had better come down to me to-morrow

[104-082]afternoon." Bozzle promised obedience to these instructions, and as

[104-083]soon as his patron had started he paid the bill, and took himself

[104-084]home.

[104-085]Lady Rowley, as she travelled back to her house in Manchester Street,

[104-086]almost made up her mind that the separation between her daughter and

[104-087]her son-in-law had better be continued. It was a very sad conclusion

[104-088]to which to come, but she could not believe that any high-spirited

[104-089]woman could long continue to submit herself to the caprices of a

[104-090]man so unreasonable and dictatorial as he to whom she had just been

[104-091]listening. Were it not for the boy, there would, she felt, be no

[104-092]doubt upon the matter. And now, as matters stood, she thought that

[104-093]it should be their great object to regain possession of the child.

[104-094]Then she endeavoured to calculate what would be the result to her

[104-095]daughter, if in very truth it should be found that the wretched

[104-096]man was mad. To hope for such a result seemed to her to be very

[104-097]wicked;--and yet she hardly knew how not to hope for it.

[104-098]"Well, mamma," said [[emily-trevelyan|Emily Trevelyan]], with a faint attempt at a smile,

[104-099]"you saw him?"

[104-100]"Yes, dearest, I saw him. I can only say that he is a most

[104-101]unreasonable man."

[104-102]"And he would tell you nothing of Louey?"

[104-103]"No dear,--not a word."

[104-104]CHAPTER LXIII.

[104-105]SIR MARMADUKE AT HOME.

[104-106][[nora-rowley|Nora Rowley]] had told her lover that there was to be no further

[104-107]communication between them till her father and mother should be

[104-108]in England; but in telling him so, had so frankly confessed her

[104-109]own affection for him and had so sturdily promised to be true to

[104-110]him, that no lover could have been reasonably aggrieved by such an

[104-111]interdiction. Nora was quite conscious of this, and was aware that

[104-112][[hugh-stanbury|Hugh Stanbury]] had received such encouragement as ought at any rate to

[104-113]bring him to the new Rowley establishment, as soon as he should learn

[104-114]where it had fixed itself. But when at the end of ten days he had

[104-115]not shown himself, she began to feel doubts. Could it be that he had

[104-116]changed his mind, that he was unwilling to encounter refusal from her

[104-117]father, or that he had found, on looking into his own affairs more

[104-118]closely, that it would be absurd for him to propose to take a wife to

[104-119]himself while his means were so poor and so precarious? Sir Marmaduke

[104-120]during this time had been so unhappy, so fretful, so indignant,

[104-121]and so much worried, that Nora herself had become almost afraid of

[104-122]him; and, without much reasoning on the matter, had taught herself

[104-123]to believe that Hugh might be actuated by similar fears. She had

[104-124]intended to tell her mother of what had occurred between her and

[104-125]Stanbury the first moment that she and Lady Rowley were together; but

[104-126]then there had fallen upon them that terrible incident of the loss

[104-127]of the child, and the whole family had become at once so wrapped up

[104-128]in the agony of the bereaved mother, and so full of rage against the

[104-129]unreasonable father, that there seemed to Nora to be no possible

[104-130]opportunity for the telling of her own love-story. Emily herself

[104-131]appeared to have forgotten it in the midst of her own misery, and had

[104-132]not mentioned Hugh Stanbury's name since they had been in Manchester

[104-133]Street. We have all felt how on occasions our own hopes and fears,

[104-134]nay, almost our own individuality, become absorbed in and obliterated

[104-135]by the more pressing cares and louder voices of those around us. Nora

[104-136]hardly dared to allude to herself while her sister's grief was still

[104-137]so prominent, and while her father was daily complaining of his own

[104-138]personal annoyances at the Colonial Office. It seemed to her that at

[104-139]such a moment she could not introduce a new matter for dispute, and

[104-140]perhaps a new subject of dismay.

[104-141]Nevertheless, as the days passed by, and as she saw nothing of Hugh

[104-142]Stanbury, her heart became sore and her spirit vexed. It seemed to

[104-143]her that if she were now deserted by him, all the world would be

[104-144]over for her. The Glascock episode in her life had passed by,--that

[104-145]episode which might have been her history, which might have been a

[104-146]history so prosperous, so magnificent, and probably so happy. As she

[104-147]thought of herself and of circumstances as they had happened to her,

[104-148]of the resolutions which she had made as to her own career when she

[104-149]first came to [[london|London]], and of the way in which she had thrown all

[104-150]those resolutions away in spite of the wonderful success which had

[104-151]come in her path, she could not refrain from thinking that she had

[104-152]brought herself to shipwreck by her own indecision. It must not be

[104-153]imagined that she regretted what she had done. She knew very well

[104-154]that to have acted otherwise than she did when Mr. Glascock came

[104-155]to her at [[nuncombe-putney|Nuncombe Putney]] would have proved her to be heartless,

[104-156]selfish, and unwomanly. Long before that time she had determined that

[104-157]it was her duty to marry a rich man,--and, if possible, a man in

[104-158]high position. Such a one had come to her,--one endowed with all the

[104-159]good things of the world beyond her most sanguine expectation,--and

[104-160]she had rejected him! She knew that she had been right because she

[104-161]had allowed herself to love the other man. She did not repent what

[104-162]she had done, the circumstances being as they were, but she almost

[104-163]regretted that she had been so soft in heart, so susceptible of the

[104-164]weakness of love, so little able to do as she pleased with herself.

[104-165]Of what use to her was it that she loved this man with all her

[104-166]strength of affection when he never came to her, although the time at

[104-167]which he had been told that he might come was now ten days past?

[104-168]She was sitting one afternoon in the drawing-room listlessly

[104-169]reading, or pretending to read, a novel, when, on a sudden, Hugh

[104-170]Stanbury was announced. The circumstances of the moment were most

[104-171]unfortunate for such a visit. Sir Marmaduke, who had been down at

[104-172]Whitehall in the morning, and from thence had made a journey to St.

[104-173]Diddulph's-in-the-East and back, was exceedingly cross and out of

[104-174]temper. They had told him at his office that they feared he would not

[104-175]suffice to carry through the purpose for which he had been brought

[104-176]home. And his brother-in-law, the parson, had expressed to him an

[104-177]opinion that he was in great part responsible for the misfortune of

[104-178]his daughter, by the encouragement which he had given to such a man

[104-179]as [[colonel-osborne|Colonel Osborne]]. Sir Marmaduke had in consequence quarrelled both

[104-180]with the chief clerk and with Mr. Outhouse, and had come home surly

[104-181]and discontented. Lady Rowley and her eldest daughter were away,

[104-182]closeted at the moment with Lady Milborough, with whom they were

[104-183]endeavouring to arrange some plan by which the boy might at any

[104-184]rate be given back. Poor Emily Trevelyan was humble enough now to

[104-185]Lady Milborough,--was prepared to be humble to any one, and in any

[104-186]circumstances, so that she should not be required to acknowledge that

[104-187]she had entertained Colonel Osborne as her lover. The two younger

[104-188]girls, Sophy and Lucy, were in the room when Stanbury was announced,

[104-189]as was also Sir Marmaduke, who at that very moment was uttering angry

[104-190]growls at the obstinacy and want of reason with which he had been

[104-191]treated by Mr. Outhouse. Now Sir Marmaduke had not so much as heard

[104-192]the name of Hugh Stanbury as yet; and Nora, though her listlessness

[104-193]was all at an end, at once felt how impossible it would be to explain

[104-194]any of the circumstances of her case in such an interview as this.

[104-195]While, however, Hugh's dear steps were heard upon the stairs,

[104-196]her feminine mind at once went to work to ascertain in what best

[104-197]mode, with what most attractive reason for his presence, she might

[104-198]introduce the young man to her father. Had not the girls been then

[104-199]present, she thought that it might have been expedient to leave Hugh

[104-200]to tell his own story to Sir Marmaduke. But she had no opportunity of

[104-201]sending her sisters away; and, unless chance should remove them, this

[104-202]could not be done.
