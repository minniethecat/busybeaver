## Section 37

[037-001]"Poor Priscilla!" Dorothy did not mean to be severe on her aunt,

[037-002]either in regard to the letters which had not been written, or to the

[037-003]one letter which now had been written. But Dorothy pitied her sister,

[037-004]whom she felt to be in trouble.

[037-005]"Well; I don't know about her being so poor. Priscilla, I'll be

[037-006]bound, thinks as well of herself as any of us do."

[037-007]"She'd cut her fingers off before she'd mean to do wrong," said

[037-008]Dorothy.

[037-009]"But what does that come to? What's the good of that? It isn't

[037-010]meaning to do right that will save us. For aught I know, the Radicals

[037-011]may mean to do right. Mr. Beales means to do right--perhaps."

[037-012]"But, aunt,--if everybody did the best they could?"

[037-013]"Tush, my dear! you are getting beyond your depth. There are such

[037-014]things still, thank God! as spiritual pastors and masters. Entrust

[037-015]yourself to them. Do what they think right." Now if aught were known

[037-016]in [[exeter|Exeter]] of Miss Stanbury, this was known,--that if any clergyman

[037-017]volunteered to give to her, unasked and uninvited, counsel, either

[037-018]ghostly or bodily, that clergyman would be sent from her presence

[037-019]with a wigging which he would not soon forget. The thing had been

[037-020]tried more than once, and the wigging had been complete. There was no

[037-021]more attentive listener in church than Miss Stanbury; and she would,

[037-022]now and again, appeal to a clergyman on some knotty point. But for

[037-023]the ordinary authority of spiritual pastors and masters she shewed

[037-024]more of abstract reverence than of practical obedience.

[037-025]"I'm sure Priscilla does the best she can," said Dorothy, going back

[037-026]to the old subject.

[037-027]"Ah,--well,--yes. What I want to say about Priscilla is this. It is a

[037-028]thousand pities she is so obstinate, so pig-headed, so certain that

[037-029]she can manage everything for herself better than anybody else can

[037-030]for her." Miss Stanbury was striving to say something good of her

[037-031]niece, but found the task to be difficult and distasteful to her.

[037-032]"She has managed for mamma ever so many years; and since she took it

[037-033]we have hardly ever been in debt," said Dorothy.

[037-034]"She'll do all that, I don't doubt. I don't suppose she cares much

[037-035]for ribbons and false hair for herself."

[037-036]"Who? Priscilla! The idea of Priscilla with false hair!"

[037-037]"I dare say not;--I dare say not. I do not think she'd spend her

[037-038]mother's money on things of that kind."

[037-039]"Aunt Stanbury, you don't know her."

[037-040]"Ah; very well. Perhaps I don't. But, come, my dear, you are very

[037-041]hard upon me, and very anxious to take your sister's part. And

[037-042]what is it all about? I've just written to her as civil a letter

[037-043]as one woman ever wrote to another. And if I had chosen, I could

[037-044]have,--could have,--h--m--m." Miss Stanbury, as she hesitated for

[037-045]words in which to complete her sentence, revelled in the strength of

[037-046]the vituperation which she could have poured upon her niece's head,

[037-047]had she chosen to write her last letter about [[colonel-osborne|Colonel Osborne]] in her

[037-048]severe strain.

[037-049]"If you have written kindly to her, I am so much obliged to you,"

[037-050]said Dorothy.

[037-051]"The truth is, Priscilla has meant to be right. Meaning won't go

[037-052]for much when the account is taken, unless the meaning comes from a

[037-053]proper source. But the poor girl has done as well as she has known

[037-054]how. I believe it is Hugh's fault more than anybody else's." This

[037-055]accusation was not pleasant to Dorothy, but she was too intent just

[037-056]now on Priscilla's case to defend her brother. "That man never ought

[037-057]to have been there; and that woman never ought to have been there.

[037-058]There cannot be a doubt about that. If Priscilla were sitting there

[037-059]opposite to me, she would own as much. I am sure she would." Miss

[037-060]Stanbury was quite right if she meant to assert that Priscilla had

[037-061]owned as much to herself. "And because I think so, I am willing to

[037-062]forgive her part in the matter. To me, personally, she has always

[037-063]been rude,--most uncourteous,--and,--and,--and unlike a younger woman

[037-064]to an older one, and an aunt, and all that. I suppose it is because

[037-065]she hates me."

[037-066]"Oh, no, Aunt Stanbury!"

[037-067]"My dear, I suppose it is. Why else should she treat me in such a

[037-068]way? But I do believe of her that she would rather eat an honest, dry

[037-069]crust, than dishonest cake and ale."

[037-070]"She would rather starve than pick up a crumb that was dishonest,"

[037-071]said Dorothy, fairly bursting out into tears.

[037-072]"I believe it. I do believe it. There; what more can I say? Clock

[037-073]House, indeed! What matter what house you live in, so that you can

[037-074]pay the rent of it honestly?"

[037-075]"But the rent is paid--honestly," said Dorothy, amidst her sobs.

[037-076]"It's paid, I don't doubt. I dare say the woman's husband and your

[037-077]brother see to that among them. Oh, that my boy, Hugh, as he used

[037-078]to be, should have brought us all to this! But there's no knowing

[037-079]what they won't do among them. Reform, indeed! Murder, sacrilege,

[037-080]adultery, treason, atheism;--that's what Reform means; besides every

[037-081]kind of nastiness under the sun." In which latter category Miss

[037-082]Stanbury intended especially to include bad printer's ink, and paper

[037-083]made of straw.

[037-084]The reader may as well see the letter which was as civil a letter

[037-085]as ever one woman wrote to another, so that the collection of the

[037-086]Stanbury correspondence may be made perfect.

[037-087]The Close, August 6, 186--.

[037-088]MY DEAR NIECE,

[037-089]Your letter has not astonished me nearly as much as you

[037-090]expected it would. I am an older woman than you, and,

[037-091]though you will not believe it, I have seen more of the

[037-092]world. I knew that the [[social-class|gentleman]] would come after the

[037-093]lady. Such gentlemen always do go after their ladies.

[037-094]As for yourself, I can see all that you have done, and

[037-095]pretty nearly hear all that you have said, as plain as a

[037-096]pike-staff. I do you the credit of believing that the plan

[037-097]is none of your making. I know who made the plan, and a

[037-098]very bad plan it is.

[037-099]As to my former letters and the other man, I understand

[037-100]all about it. You were very angry that I should accuse you

[037-101]of having this man at the house; and you were right to be

[037-102]angry. I respect you for having been angry. But what does

[037-103]all that say as to his coming,--now that he has come?

[037-104]If you will consent to take an old woman's advice, get rid

[037-105]of the whole boiling of them. I say it in firm love and

[037-106]friendship, for I am,--

[037-107]Your affectionate aunt,

[037-108]JEMIMA STANBURY.

[037-109]The special vaunted courtesy of this letter consisted, no doubt, in

[037-110]the expression of respect which it contained, and in that declaration

[037-111]of affection with which it terminated. The epithet was one which

[037-112]Miss Stanbury would by no means use promiscuously in writing to her

[037-113]nearest relatives. She had not intended to use it when she commenced

[037-114]her letter to Priscilla. But the respect of which she had spoken

[037-115]had glowed, and had warmed itself into something of temporary love;

[037-116]and feeling at the moment that she was an affectionate aunt, Miss

[037-117]Stanbury had so put herself down in her letter. Having done such a

[037-118]deed she felt that Dorothy, though Dorothy knew nothing about it,

[037-119]ought in her gratitude to listen patiently to anything that she might

[037-120]now choose to say against Priscilla.

[037-121]But Dorothy was in truth very miserable, and in her misery wrote a

[037-122]long letter that afternoon to her mother,--which, however, it will

[037-123]not be necessary to place entire among the Stanbury records,--begging

[037-124]that she might be informed as to the true circumstances of the case.

[037-125]She did not say a word of censure in regard either to her mother or

[037-126]sister; but she expressed an opinion in the mildest words which she

[037-127]could use, that if anything had happened which had compromised their

[037-128]names since their residence at the [[clock-house|Clock House]], she, Dorothy, had

[037-129]better go home and join them. The meaning of which was that it would

[037-130]not become her to remain in the house in the Close, if the house in

[037-131]the Close would be disgraced by her presence. Poor Dorothy had taught

[037-132]herself to think that the iniquity of roaring lions spread itself

[037-133]very widely.

[037-134]In the afternoon she made some such proposition to her aunt in

[037-135]ambiguous terms. "Go home!" said Miss Stanbury. "Now?"

[037-136]"If you think it best, Aunt Stanbury."

[037-137]"And put yourself in the middle of all this iniquity and abomination!

[037-138]I don't suppose you want to know the woman?"

[037-139]"No, indeed!"

[037-140]"Or the man?"

[037-141]"Oh, Aunt Stanbury!"

[037-142]"It's my belief that no decent gentleman in Exeter would look at you

[037-143]again if you were to go and live among them at [[nuncombe-putney|Nuncombe Putney]] while

[037-144]all this is going on. No, no. Let one of you be saved out of it, at

[037-145]least."

[037-146]Aunt Stanbury had more than once made use of expressions which

[037-147]brought the faintest touch of gentle pink up to her niece's cheeks.

[037-148]We must do Dorothy the justice of saying that she had never dreamed

[037-149]of being looked at by any gentleman, whether decent or indecent. Her

[037-150]life at Nuncombe Putney had been of such a nature, that though she

[037-151]knew that other girls were looked at, and even made love to, and that

[037-152]they got married and had children, no dim vision of such a career

[037-153]for herself had ever presented itself to her eyes. She had known

[037-154]very well that her mother and sister and herself were people

[037-155]apart,--ladies, and yet so extremely poor, that they could only

[037-156]maintain their rank by the most rigid seclusion. To live, and work

[037-157]unseen, was what the world had ordained for her. Then her call

[037-158]to Exeter had come upon her, and she had conceived that she was

[037-159]henceforth to be the humble companion of a very imperious old aunt.

[037-160]Her aunt, indeed, was imperious, but did not seem to require humility

[037-161]in her companion. All the good things that were eaten and drunk were

[037-162]divided between them with the strictest impartiality. Dorothy's

[037-163]cushion and hassock in the church and in the cathedral were the same

[037-164]as her aunt's. Her bed-room was made very comfortable for her. Her

[037-165]aunt never gave her any orders before company, and always spoke of

[037-166]her before the servants as one whom they were to obey and respect.

[037-167]Gradually Dorothy came to understand the meaning of this;--but her

[037-168]aunt would sometimes say things about young men which she did not

[037-169]quite understand. Could it be that her aunt supposed that any young

[037-170]man would come and wish to marry her,--her, Dorothy Stanbury? She

[037-171]herself had not quite so strong an aversion to men in general as that

[037-172]which Priscilla felt, but she had not as yet found that any of those

[037-173]whom she had seen at Exeter were peculiarly agreeable to her. Before

[037-174]she went to bed that night her aunt said a word to her which startled

[037-175]her more than she had ever been startled before. On that evening Miss

[037-176]Stanbury had a few friends to drink tea with her. There were Mr. and

[037-177]Mrs. Crumbie, and Mrs. MacHugh of course, and the Cheritons from

[037-178]Alphington, and the Miss Apjohns from Helion Villa, and old Mr. Powel

[037-179]all the way from Haldon, and two of the Wrights from their house

[037-180]in the Northernhay, and Mr. Gibson;--but the Miss Frenches from

[037-181]Heavitree were not there. "Why don't you have the Miss Frenches,

[037-182]aunt?" Dorothy had asked.

[037-183]"Bother the Miss Frenches! I'm not bound to have them every time.

[037-184]There's Camilla has been and got herself a band-box on the back of

[037-185]her head a great deal bigger than the place inside where her brains

[037-186]ought to be." But the band-box at the back of Camilla French's head

[037-187]was not the sole cause of the omission of the two sisters from the

[037-188]list of Miss Stanbury's visitors on this occasion.
