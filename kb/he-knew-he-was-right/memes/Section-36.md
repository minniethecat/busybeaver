## Section 36

[036-001]The plan of the campaign had all been arranged. Mrs. Trevelyan and

[036-002]Nora together received [[colonel-osborne|Colonel Osborne]] in the drawing-room. It was

[036-003]understood that Nora was to remain there during the whole visit. "It

[036-004]is horrible to think that such a precaution should be necessary,"

[036-005]Mrs. Trevelyan had said, "but perhaps it may be best. There is no

[036-006]knowing what the malice of people may not invent."

[036-007]"My dear girls," said the Colonel, "I am delighted to see you," and

[036-008]he gave a hand to each.

[036-009]"We are not very cheerful here," said Mrs. Trevelyan, "as you may

[036-010]imagine."

[036-011]"But the scenery is beautiful," said Nora, "and the people we are

[036-012]living with are kind and nice."

[036-013]"I am very glad of that," said the Colonel. Then there was a pause,

[036-014]and it seemed, for a moment or two, that none of them knew how to

[036-015]begin a general conversation. Colonel Osborne was quite sure, by this

[036-016]time, that he had come down to Devonshire with the express object of

[036-017]seeing the door of the church at Cockchaffington, and Mrs. Trevelyan

[036-018]was beginning to think that he certainly had not come to see her.

[036-019]"Have you heard from your father since you have been here?" asked the

[036-020]Colonel.

[036-021]Then there was an explanation about Sir Marmaduke and Lady Rowley.

[036-022]Mr. Trevelyan's name was not mentioned; but Mrs. Trevelyan stated

[036-023]that she had explained to her mother all the painful circumstances of

[036-024]her present life. Sir Marmaduke, as Colonel Osborne was aware, was

[036-025]expected to be in England in the spring, and Lady Rowley would, of

[036-026]course, come with him. Nora thought that they might probably now come

[036-027]before that time; but Mrs. Trevelyan declared that it was out of the

[036-028]question that they should do so. She was sure that her father could

[036-029]not leave the islands except when he did so in obedience to official

[036-030]orders. The expense of doing so would be ruinous to him. And what

[036-031]good would he do? In this way there was a great deal of family

[036-032]conversation, in which Colonel Osborne was able to take a part; but

[036-033]not a word was said about Mr. Trevelyan.

[036-034]Nor did "the Colonel" find an opportunity of expressing a spark of

[036-035]that sentiment, for the purpose of expressing which he had made

[036-036]this journey to Devonshire. It is not pleasant to make love in the

[036-037]presence of a third person, even when that love is all fair and above

[036-038]board; but it is quite impracticable to do so to a married lady, when

[036-039]that married lady's sister is present. No more futile visit than

[036-040]this of Colonel Osborne's to the [[clock-house|Clock House]] was ever made. And yet,

[036-041]though not a word was spoken to which Mr. Trevelyan himself could

[036-042]have taken the slightest exception, the visit, futile as it was,

[036-043]could not but do an enormous deal of harm. Mrs. Crocket had already

[036-044]guessed that the fine [[social-class|gentleman]] down from [[london|London]] was the lover of the

[036-045]married lady at the Clock House, who was separated from her husband.

[036-046]The wooden-legged postman and the ostler were not long in connecting

[036-047]the man among the tombstones with the visitor to the house.

[036-048]Trevelyan, as we are aware, already knew that Colonel Osborne was in

[036-049]the neighbourhood. And poor Priscilla Stanbury was now exposed to the

[036-050]terrible necessity of owning the truth to her aunt. "The Colonel,"

[036-051]when he had sat an hour with his young friends, took his leave; and,

[036-052]as he walked back to Mrs. Crocket's, and ordered that his fly might

[036-053]be got ready for him, his mind was heavy with the disagreeable

[036-054]feeling that he had made an ass of himself. The whole affair had

[036-055]been a failure; and though he might be able to pass off the porch at

[036-056]Cockchaffington among his friends, he could not but be aware himself

[036-057]that he had spent his time, his trouble, and his money for nothing.

[036-058]He became aware, as he returned to Lessboro', that had he intended to

[036-059]make any pleasant use whatever of his position in reference to Mrs.

[036-060]Trevelyan, the tone of his letter and his whole mode of proceeding

[036-061]should have been less patriarchal. And he should have contrived a

[036-062]meeting without the presence of [[nora-rowley|Nora Rowley]].

[036-063]As soon as he had left them, Mrs. Trevelyan went to her own room, and

[036-064]Nora at once rejoined Priscilla.

[036-065]"Is he gone?" asked Priscilla.

[036-066]"Oh, yes;--he has gone."

[036-067]"What would I have given that he had never come!"

[036-068]"And yet," said Nora, "what harm has he done? I wish he had not come,

[036-069]because, of course, people will talk! But nothing was more natural

[036-070]than that he should come over to see us when he was so near us."

[036-071]"Nora!"

[036-072]"What do you mean?"

[036-073]"You don't believe all that? In the neighbourhood! I believe he came

[036-074]on purpose to see your sister, and I think that it was a dastardly

[036-075]and most ungentleman-like thing to do."

[036-076]"I am quite sure you are wrong, then,--altogether wrong," said Nora.

[036-077]"Very well. We must have our own opinions. I am glad you can be so

[036-078]charitable. But he should not have come here,--to this house, even

[036-079]though imperative business had brought him into the very village.

[036-080]But men in their vanity never think of the injury they may do to a

[036-081]woman's name. Now I must go and write to my aunt. I am not going to

[036-082]have it said hereafter that I deceived her. And then I shall write to

[036-083]Hugh. Oh dear; oh dear!"

[036-084]"I am afraid we are a great trouble to you."

[036-085]"I will not deceive you, because I like you. This is a great trouble

[036-086]to me. I have meant to be so prudent, and with all my prudence I have

[036-087]not been able to keep clear of rocks. And I have been so indignant

[036-088]with Aunt Stanbury! Now I must go and eat humble-pie."

[036-089]Then she eat humble-pie,--after the following fashion:--

[036-090]DEAR AUNT STANBURY,

[036-091]After what has passed between us, I think it right to tell

[036-092]you that Colonel Osborne has been at [[nuncombe-putney|Nuncombe Putney]], and

[036-093]that he called at the Clock House this morning. We did not

[036-094]see him. But Mrs. Trevelyan and Miss Rowley, together, did

[036-095]see him. He remained here perhaps an hour.

[036-096]I should not have thought it necessary to mention this to

[036-097]you, the matter being one in which you are not concerned,

[036-098]were it not for our former correspondence. When I last

[036-099]wrote, I had no idea that he was coming,--nor had mamma.

[036-100]And when you first wrote, he was not even expected by

[036-101]Mrs. Trevelyan. The man you wrote about was another

[036-102]gentleman;--as I told you before. All this is most

[036-103]disagreeable and tiresome;--and would be quite

[036-104]nonsensical, but that circumstances seem to make it

[036-105]necessary.

[036-106]As for Colonel Osborne, I wish he had not been here; but

[036-107]his coming would do no harm,--only that it will be talked

[036-108]about.

[036-109]I think you will understand how it is that I feel myself

[036-110]constrained to write to you. I do hope that you will spare

[036-111]mamma, who is disturbed and harassed when she gets angry

[036-112]letters. If you have anything to say to myself, I don't

[036-113]mind it.

[036-114]Yours truly,

[036-115]PRISCILLA STANBURY.

[036-116]The Clock House, Friday, August 5.

[036-117]She wrote also to her brother Hugh; but Hugh himself reached Nuncombe

[036-118]Putney before the letter reached him.

[036-119]Mr. Bozzle watched the Colonel out of the house, and watched him

[036-120]out of the village. When the Colonel was fairly started, Mr. Bozzle

[036-121]walked back to Lessboro'.

[036-122]CHAPTER XXII.

[036-123]SHEWING HOW MISS STANBURY BEHAVED TO HER TWO NIECES.

[036-124][Illustration]

[036-125]The triumph of Miss Stanbury when she received her niece's letter was

[036-126]certainly very great,--so great that in its first flush she could not

[036-127]restrain herself from exhibiting it to Dorothy. "Well,--well,--what

[036-128]do you think, Dolly?"

[036-129]"[[About]] what, aunt? I don't know who the letter is from."

[036-130]"Nobody writes to me now so constant as your sister Priscilla. The

[036-131]letter is from Priscilla. Colonel Osborne has been at the Clock

[036-132]House, after all. I knew that he would be there. I knew it! I knew

[036-133]it!"

[036-134]Dorothy, when she heard this, was dumbfounded. She had rested her

[036-135]defence of her mother and sister on the impossibility of any such

[036-136]visit being admitted. According to her lights the coming of Colonel

[036-137]Osborne, after all that had been said, would be like the coming of

[036-138]Lucifer himself. The Colonel was, to her imagination, a horrible

[036-139]roaring lion. She had no idea that the erratic manoeuvres of such

[036-140]a beast might be milder and more innocent than the wooing of any

[036-141]turtle-dove. She would have asked whether the roaring lion had gone

[036-142]away again, and, if so, whether he had taken his prey with him,

[036-143]were it not that she was too much frightened at the moment to ask

[036-144]any question. That her mother and sister should have been wilfully

[036-145]concerned in such iniquity was quite incredible to her, but yet she

[036-146]did not know how to defend them. "But are you quite sure of it, Aunt

[036-147]Stanbury? May there not be another mistake?"

[036-148]"No mistake this time, I think, my dear. Any way, Priscilla says that

[036-149]he is there." Now in this there was a mistake. Priscilla had said

[036-150]nothing of the kind.

[036-151]"You don't mean that he is staying at the Clock House, Aunt

[036-152]Stanbury?"

[036-153]"I don't know where he is now. I'm not his keeper. And, I'm glad to

[036-154]say, I'm not the lady's keeper either. Ah, me! It's a bad business.

[036-155]You can't touch pitch and not be defiled, my dear. If your mother

[036-156]wanted the Clock House, I would sooner have taken it for her myself

[036-157]than that all this should have happened,--for the family's sake."

[036-158]But Miss Stanbury, when she was alone, and when she had read her

[036-159]niece's three letters again and again, began to understand something

[036-160]of Priscilla's honesty, and began also to perceive that there might

[036-161]have been a great difficulty respecting the Colonel, for which

[036-162]neither her niece nor her sister-in-law could fairly be held to

[036-163]be responsible. It was perhaps the plainest characteristic of all

[036-164]the Stanburys that they were never wilfully dishonest. Ignorant,

[036-165]prejudiced, and passionate they might be. In her anger Miss Stanbury,

[036-166]of [[exeter|Exeter]], could be almost malicious; and her niece at Nuncombe

[036-167]Putney was very like her aunt. Each could say most cruel things, most

[036-168]unjust things, when actuated by a mistaken consciousness of perfect

[036-169]right on her own side. But neither of them could lie,--even by

[036-170]silence. Let an error be brought home to either of them,--so as to

[036-171]be acknowledged at home,--and the error would be assuredly confessed

[036-172]aloud. And, indeed, with differences in the shades, Hugh and Dorothy

[036-173]were of the same nature. They were possessed of sweeter tempers than

[036-174]their aunt and sister, but they were filled with the same eager

[036-175]readiness to believe themselves to be right,--and to own themselves

[036-176]to others to be wrong, when they had been constrained to make such

[036-177]confession to themselves. The chances of life, and something probably

[036-178]of inner nature, had made Dorothy mild and obedient; whereas, in

[036-179]regard to Hugh, the circumstances of his life and disposition had

[036-180]made him obstinate and self-reliant. But in all was to be found the

[036-181]same belief in self,--which amounted almost to conceit,--the same

[036-182]warmth of affection, and the same love of justice.

[036-183]When Miss Stanbury had again perused the correspondence, and had come

[036-184]to see, dimly, how things had gone at Nuncombe Putney,--when the

[036-185]conviction came upon her mind that Priscilla had entertained a horror

[036-186]as to the coming of this Colonel equal to that which she herself

[036-187]had felt,--when her imagination painted to her all that her niece

[036-188]had suffered, her heart was softened somewhat. She had declared to

[036-189]Dorothy that pitch, if touched, would certainly defile; and she had,

[036-190]at first, intended to send the same opinion, couched in very forcible

[036-191]words, to her correspondents at the Clock House. They should not

[036-192]continue to go astray for want of being told that they were going

[036-193]astray. It must be acknowledged, too, that there was a certain

[036-194]amount of ignoble wrath in the bosom of Miss Stanbury because her

[036-195]sister-in-law had taken the Clock House. She had never been told, and

[036-196]had not even condescended to ask Dorothy, whether the house was taken

[036-197]and paid for by her nephew on behalf of his mother, or whether it

[036-198]was paid for by Mr. Trevelyan on behalf of his wife. In the latter

[036-199]case, Mrs. Stanbury would, she thought, be little more than an

[036-200]upper servant, or keeper,--as she expressed it to herself. Such an

[036-201]arrangement appeared to her to be quite disgraceful in a Stanbury;

[036-202]but yet she believed that such must be the existing arrangement, as

[036-203]she could not bring herself to conceive that [[hugh-stanbury|Hugh Stanbury]] could keep

[036-204]such an establishment over his mother's head out of money earned by

[036-205]writing for a penny newspaper. There would be a triumph of democracy

[036-206]in this which would vanquish her altogether. She had, therefore, been

[036-207]anxious enough to trample on Priscilla and upon all the affairs of

[036-208]the Clock House; but yet she had been unable to ignore the nobility

[036-209]of Priscilla's truth, and having acknowledged it to herself she found

[036-210]herself compelled to acknowledge it aloud. She sat down to think in

[036-211]silence, and it was not till she had fortified herself by her first

[036-212]draught of beer, and till she had finished her first portion of bread

[036-213]and cheese, that she spoke. "I have written to your sister herself,

[036-214]this time," she said. "I don't know that I ever wrote a line to her

[036-215]before in my life."
