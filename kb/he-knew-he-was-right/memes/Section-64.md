## Section 64

[064-001]He had not considered, when his resolutions to this effect were first

[064-002]forming themselves, that a separation between a man and his wife once

[064-003]effected cannot be annulled, and as it were cured, so as to leave no

[064-004]cicatrice behind. Gradually, as he spent day after day in thinking on

[064-005]this one subject, he came to feel that even were his wife to submit,

[064-006]to own her fault humbly, and to come back to him, this very coming

[064-007]back would in itself be a new wound. Could he go out again with

[064-008]his wife on his arm to the houses of those who knew that he had

[064-009]repudiated her because of her friendship with another man? Could

[064-010]he open again that house in Curzon Street, and let things go on

[064-011]quietly as they had gone before? He told himself that it was

[064-012]impossible;--that he and she were ineffably disgraced;--that, if

[064-013]reunited, they must live buried out of sight in some remote distance.

[064-014]And he told himself, also, that he could never be with her again

[064-015]night or day without thinking of the separation. His happiness had

[064-016]been shipwrecked.

[064-017]Then he had put himself into the hands of Mr. Bozzle, and Mr. Bozzle

[064-018]had taught him that women very often do go astray. Mr. Bozzle's idea

[064-019]of female virtue was not high, and he had opportunities of implanting

[064-020]his idea on his client's mind. Trevelyan hated the man. He was filled

[064-021]with disgust by Bozzle's words, and was made miserable by Bozzle's

[064-022]presence. Yet he came gradually to believe in Bozzle. Bozzle alone

[064-023]believed in him. There were none but Bozzle who did not bid him to

[064-024]submit himself to his disobedient wife. And then, as he came to

[064-025]believe in Bozzle, he grew to be more and more assured that no one

[064-026]but Bozzle could tell him facts. His chivalry, and love, and sense of

[064-027]woman's honour, with something of manly pride on his own part,--so

[064-028]he told himself,--had taught him to believe it to be impossible that

[064-029]his wife should have sinned. Bozzle, who knew the world, thought

[064-030]otherwise. Bozzle, who had no interest in the matter, one way or the

[064-031]other, would find out facts. What if his chivalry, and love, and

[064-032]manly pride had deceived him? There were women who sinned. Then he

[064-033]prayed that his wife might not be such a woman; and got up from his

[064-034]prayers almost convinced that she was a sinner.

[064-035]His mind was at work upon it always. Could it be that she was so base

[064-036]as this--so vile a thing, so abject, such dirt, pollution, filth? But

[064-037]there were such cases. Nay, were they not almost numberless? He found

[064-038]himself reading in the papers records of such things from day to

[064-039]day, and thought that in doing so he was simply acquiring experience

[064-040]necessary for himself. If it were so, he had indeed done well to

[064-041]separate himself from a thing so infamous. And if it were not so,

[064-042]how could it be that that man had gone to her in Devonshire? He had

[064-043]received from his wife's hands a short note addressed to the man, in

[064-044]which the man was desired by her not to go to her, or to write to

[064-045]her again, because of her husband's commands. He had shown this to

[064-046]Bozzle, and Bozzle had smiled. "It's just the sort of thing they

[064-047]does," Bozzle had said. "Then they writes another by post." He had

[064-048]consulted Bozzle as to the sending on of that letter, and Bozzle had

[064-049]been strongly of opinion that it should be forwarded, a copy having

[064-050]been duly taken and attested by himself. It might be very pretty

[064-051][[truth|evidence]] by-and-by. If the letter were not forwarded, Bozzle thought

[064-052]that the omission to do so might be given in evidence against his

[064-053]employer. Bozzle was very careful, and full of "evidence." The letter

[064-054]therefore was sent on to [[colonel-osborne|Colonel Osborne]]. "If there's billy-dous

[064-055]going between 'em we shall nobble 'em," said Bozzle. Trevelyan tore

[064-056]his hair in despair, but believed that there would be billy-dous.

[064-057]He came to believe everything; and, though he prayed fervently that

[064-058]his wife might not be led astray, that she might be saved at any

[064-059]rate from utter vice, yet he almost came to hope that it might be

[064-060]otherwise;--not, indeed, with the hope of the sane man, who desires

[064-061]that which he tells himself to be for his advantage; but with the

[064-062]hope of the insane man, who loves to feed his grievance, even though

[064-063]the grief should be his death. They who do not understand that a man

[064-064]may be brought to hope that which of all things is the most grievous

[064-065]to him, have not observed with sufficient closeness the perversity of

[064-066]the human mind. Trevelyan would have given all that he had to save

[064-067]his wife; would, even now, have cut his tongue out before he would

[064-068]have expressed to anyone,--save to Bozzle,--a suspicion that she

[064-069]could in truth have been guilty; was continually telling himself that

[064-070]further life would be impossible to him, if he, and she, and that

[064-071]child of theirs, should be thus disgraced;--and yet he expected it,

[064-072]believed it, and, after a fashion, he almost hoped it.

[064-073]He was to wait at Turin till tidings should come from Bozzle, and

[064-074]after that he would go on to Venice; but he would not move from Turin

[064-075]till he should have received his first communication from England.

[064-076]When he had been three days at Turin they came to him, and, among

[064-077]other letters in Bozzle's packet, there was a letter addressed in his

[064-078]wife's handwriting. The letter was simply directed to Bozzle's house.

[064-079]In what possible way could his wife have found out ought of his

[064-080]dealings with Bozzle,--where Bozzle lived, or could have learned that

[064-081]letters intended for him should be sent to the man's own residence?

[064-082]Before, however, we inspect the contents of Mr. Bozzle's dispatch, we

[064-083]will go back and see how Mrs. Trevelyan had discovered the manner of

[064-084]forwarding a letter to her husband.

[064-085]The matter of the address was, indeed, very simple. All letters for

[064-086]Trevelyan were to be redirected from the house in Curzon Street, and

[064-087]from the chambers in Lincoln's Inn, to the Acrobats' Club; to the

[064-088]porter of the Acrobats' Club had been confided the secret, not of

[064-089]Bozzle's name, but of Bozzle's private address, No. 55, Stony Walk,

[064-090]Union Street, Borough. Thus all letters reaching the Acrobats' were

[064-091]duly sent to Mr. Bozzle's house. It may be remembered that Hugh

[064-092]Stanbury, on the occasion of his last visit to the parsonage of St.

[064-093]Diddulph's, was informed that Mrs. Trevelyan had a letter from her

[064-094]father for her husband, and that she knew not whither to send it.

[064-095]It may well be that, had the matter assumed no other interest in

[064-096]Stanbury's eyes than that given to it by Mrs. Trevelyan's very

[064-097]moderate anxiety to have the letter forwarded, he would have thought

[064-098]nothing about it; but having resolved, as he sat upon the knife-board

[064-099]of the omnibus,--the reader will, at any rate, remember those

[064-100]resolutions made on the top of the omnibus while Hugh was smoking his

[064-101]pipe,--having resolved that a deed should be done at St. Diddulph's,

[064-102]he resolved also that it should be done at once. He would not allow

[064-103]the heat of his purpose to be cooled by delay. He would go to St.

[064-104]Diddulph's at once, with his heart in his hand. But it might, he

[064-105]thought, be as well that he should have an excuse for his visit.

[064-106]So he called upon the porter at the Acrobats', and was successful

[064-107]in learning Mr. Trevelyan's address. "Stony Walk, Union Street,

[064-108]Borough," he said to himself, wondering; then it occurred to him

[064-109]that Bozzle, and Bozzle only among Trevelyan's friends, could

[064-110]live at Stony Walk in the Borough. Thus armed, he set out for St.

[064-111]Diddulph's;--and, as one of the effects of his visit to the East, Sir

[064-112]Marmaduke's note was forwarded to [[louis-trevelyan|Louis Trevelyan]] at Turin.

[064-113]CHAPTER XXXIX.

[064-114]MISS NORA ROWLEY IS MALTREATED.

[064-115][[hugh-stanbury|Hugh Stanbury]], when he reached the parsonage, found no difficulty

[064-116]in making his way into the joint presence of Mrs. Outhouse, Mrs.

[064-117]Trevelyan, and Nora. He was recognised by the St. Diddulph's party

[064-118]as one who had come over to their side, as a friend of Trevelyan

[064-119]who had found himself constrained to condemn his friend in spite of

[064-120]his friendship, and was consequently very welcome. And there was

[064-121]no difficulty about giving the address. The ladies wondered how it

[064-122]came to pass that Mr. Trevelyan's letters should be sent to such

[064-123]a locality, and Hugh expressed his surprise also. He thought it

[064-124]discreet to withhold his suspicions about Mr. Bozzle, and simply

[064-125]expressed his conviction that letters sent in accordance with the

[064-126]directions given by the club-porter would reach their destination.

[064-127]Then the boy was brought down, and they were all very confidential

[064-128]and very unhappy together. Mrs. Trevelyan could see no end to the

[064-129]cruelty of her position, and declared that her father's anger against

[064-130]her husband was so great that she anticipated his coming with almost

[064-131]more of fear than of hope. Mrs. Outhouse expressed an opinion that

[064-132]Mr. Trevelyan must surely be mad; and Nora suggested that the

[064-133]possibility of such perversity on the part of a man made it almost

[064-134]unwise in any woman to trust herself to the power of a husband. "But

[064-135]there are not many like him, thank God," said Mrs. Outhouse, bridling

[064-136]in her wrath. Thus they were very friendly together, and Hugh

[064-137]was allowed to feel that he stood upon comfortable terms in the

[064-138]parsonage;--but he did not as yet see how he was to carry out his

[064-139]project for the present day.

[064-140]At last Mrs. Trevelyan went away with the child. Hugh felt that he

[064-141]ought to go, but stayed courageously. He thought he could perceive

[064-142]that Nora suspected the cause of his assiduity; but it was quite

[064-143]evident that Mrs. Outhouse did not do so. Mrs. Outhouse, having

[064-144]reconciled herself to the young man, was by no means averse to his

[064-145]presence. She went on talking about the wickedness of Trevelyan, and

[064-146]her brother's anger, and the fate of the little boy, till at last the

[064-147]little boy's mother came back into the room. Then Mrs. Outhouse went.

[064-148]They must excuse her for a few minutes, she said. If only she would

[064-149]have gone a few minutes sooner, how well her absence might have been

[064-150]excused. Nora understood it all now; and though she became almost

[064-151]breathless, she was not surprised, when Hugh got up from his chair

[064-152]and asked her sister to go away. "Mrs. Trevelyan," he said, "I want

[064-153]to speak a few words to your sister. I hope you will give me the

[064-154]opportunity."

[064-155]"Nora!" exclaimed Mrs. Trevelyan.

[064-156]"She knows nothing about it," said Hugh.

[064-157]"Am I to go?" said Mrs. Trevelyan to her sister. But Nora said never

[064-158]a word. She sat perfectly fixed, not turning her eyes from the object

[064-159]on which she was gazing.

[064-160][Illustration: "Am I to go?"]

[064-161]"Pray,--pray do," said Hugh.

[064-162]"I cannot think that it will be for any good," said Mrs. Trevelyan;

[064-163]"but I know that she may be trusted. And I suppose it ought to be so,

[064-164]if you wish it."

[064-165]"I do wish it, of all things," said Hugh, still [[reputation|standing]] up, and

[064-166]almost turning the elder sister out of the room by the force of his

[064-167]look and voice. Then, with another pause of a moment, Mrs. Trevelyan

[064-168]rose from her chair and left the room, closing the door after her.

[064-169]Hugh, when he found that the coast was clear for him, immediately

[064-170]began his task with a conviction that not a moment was to be lost.

[064-171]He had told himself a dozen times that the matter was hopeless,

[064-172]that Nora had shown him by every means in her power that she was

[064-173]indifferent to him, that she with all her friends would know that

[064-174]such a marriage was out of the question; and he had in truth come

[064-175]to believe that the mission which he had in hand was one in which

[064-176]success was not possible. But he thought that it was his duty to go

[064-177]on with it. "If a man love a woman, even though it be the king and

[064-178]the beggar-woman reversed,--though it be a beggar and a queen, he

[064-179]should tell her of it. If it be so, she has a right to know it and to

[064-180]take her choice. And he has a right to tell her, and to say what he

[064-181]can for himself." Such was Hugh's doctrine in the matter; and, acting

[064-182]upon it, he found himself alone with his mistress.
