## Section 59

[059-001]But circumstances did give him an opportunity of speaking to Mr.

[059-002]Gibson. On the Wednesday afternoon both he and Mr. Gibson were

[059-003]invited to drink tea at Mrs. French's house on that evening. Such

[059-004]invitations at [[exeter|Exeter]] were wont to be given at short dates, and both

[059-005]the gentlemen had said that they would go. Then Arabella French had

[059-006]called in the Close and had asked Miss Stanbury and Dorothy. It was

[059-007]well understood by Arabella that Miss Stanbury herself would not

[059-008]drink tea at Heavitree. And it may be that Dorothy's company was not

[059-009]in truth desired. The ladies both declined. "Don't you stay at home

[059-010]for me, my dear," Miss Stanbury said to her niece. But Dorothy had

[059-011]not been out without her aunt since she had been at Exeter, and

[059-012]understood perfectly that it would not be wise to commence the

[059-013]practice at the house of the Frenches. "Mr. Brooke is coming, Miss

[059-014]Stanbury; and Mr. Gibson," Miss French said. And Miss Stanbury had

[059-015]thought that there was some triumph in her tone. "Mr. Brooke can go

[059-016]where he pleases, my dear," Miss Stanbury replied. "And as for Mr.

[059-017]Gibson, I am not his keeper." The tone in which Miss Stanbury

[059-018]spoke would have implied great imprudence, had not the two ladies

[059-019]understood each other so thoroughly, and had not each known that it

[059-020]was so.

[059-021]There was the accustomed set of people in Mrs. French's

[059-022]drawing-room;--the Crumbies, and the Wrights, and the Apjohns. And

[059-023]Mrs. MacHugh came also,--knowing that there would be a rubber. "Their

[059-024]naked shoulders don't hurt me," Mrs. MacHugh said, when her friend

[059-025]almost scolded her for going to the house. "I'm not a young man. I

[059-026]don't care what they do to themselves." "You might say as much if

[059-027]they went naked altogether," Miss Stanbury had replied in anger. "If

[059-028]nobody else complained, I shouldn't," said Mrs. MacHugh. Mrs. MacHugh

[059-029]got her rubber; and as she had gone for her rubber, on a distinct

[059-030]promise that there should be a rubber, and as there was a rubber, she

[059-031]felt that she had no right to say ill-natured things. "What does it

[059-032]matter to me," said Mrs. MacHugh, "how nasty she is? She's not going

[059-033]to be my wife." "Ugh!" exclaimed Miss Stanbury, shaking her head both

[059-034]in anger and disgust.

[059-035]Camilla French was by no means so bad as she was painted by Miss

[059-036]Stanbury, and Brooke Burgess rather liked her than otherwise. And it

[059-037]seemed to him that Mr. Gibson did not at all dislike Arabella, and

[059-038]felt no repugnance at either the lady's noddle or shoulders now that

[059-039]he was removed from Miss Stanbury's influence. It was clear enough

[059-040]also that Arabella had not given up the attempt, although she must

[059-041]have admitted to herself that the claims of Dorothy Stanbury were

[059-042]very strong. On this evening it seemed to have been specially

[059-043]permitted to Arabella, who was the elder sister, to take into her own

[059-044]hands the management of the case. Beholders of the game had hitherto

[059-045]declared that Mr. Gibson's safety was secured by the constant

[059-046]coupling of the sisters. Neither would allow the other to hunt

[059-047]alone. But a common sense of the common danger had made some special

[059-048]strategy necessary, and Camilla hardly spoke a word to Mr. Gibson

[059-049]during the evening. Let us hope that she found some temporary

[059-050]consolation in the presence of the stranger.

[059-051]"I hope you are going to stay with us ever so long, Mr. Burgess?"

[059-052]said Camilla.

[059-053]"A month. That is ever so long;--isn't it? Why I mean to see all

[059-054]Devonshire within that time. I feel already that I know Exeter

[059-055]thoroughly and everybody in it."

[059-056]"I'm sure we are very much flattered."

[059-057]"As for you, Miss French, I've heard so much about you all my life,

[059-058]that I felt that I knew you before I came here."

[059-059]"Who can have spoken to you about me?"

[059-060]"You forget how many relatives I have in the city. Do you think my

[059-061]Uncle Barty never writes to me?"

[059-062]"Not about me."

[059-063]"Does he not? And do you suppose I don't hear from Miss Stanbury?"

[059-064]"But she hates me. I know that."

[059-065]"And do you hate her?"

[059-066]"No, indeed. I've the greatest respect for her. But she is a little

[059-067]odd; isn't she, now, Mr. Burgess? We all like her ever so much; and

[059-068]we've known her ever so long, six or seven years,--since we were

[059-069]quite young things. But she has such queer notions about girls."

[059-070]"What sort of notions?"

[059-071]"She'd like them all to dress just like herself; and she thinks that

[059-072]they should never talk to young men. If she was here she'd say I was

[059-073]flirting with you, because we're sitting together."

[059-074]"But you are not; are you?"

[059-075]"Of course I am not."

[059-076]"I wish you would," said Brooke.

[059-077]"I shouldn't know how to begin. I shouldn't indeed. I don't know what

[059-078]flirting means, and I don't know who does know. When young ladies and

[059-079]gentlemen go out, I suppose they are intended to talk to each other."

[059-080]"But very often they don't, you know."

[059-081]"I call that stupid," said Camilla. "And yet, when they do, all the

[059-082]old maids say that the girls are flirting. I'll tell you one thing,

[059-083]Mr. Burgess. I don't care what any old maid says about me. I always

[059-084]talk to people that I like, and if they choose to call me a flirt,

[059-085]they may. It's my opinion that still waters run the deepest."

[059-086]"No doubt the noisy streams are very shallow," said Brooke.

[059-087]"You may call me a shallow stream if you like, Mr. Burgess."

[059-088]"I meant nothing of the kind."

[059-089]"But what do you call Dorothy Stanbury? That's what I call still

[059-090]water. She runs deep enough."

[059-091]"The quietest young lady I ever saw in my life."

[059-092]"Exactly. So quiet, but so--clever. What do you think of Mr. Gibson?"

[059-093]"Everybody is asking me what I think of Mr. Gibson."

[059-094]"You know what they say. They say he is to marry Dorothy Stanbury.

[059-095]Poor man! I don't think his own consent has ever been asked

[059-096]yet;--but, nevertheless, it's settled."

[059-097]"Just at present he seems to me to be,--what shall I say?--I oughtn't

[059-098]to say flirting with your sister; ought I?"

[059-099]"Miss Stanbury would say so if she were here, no doubt. But the fact

[059-100]is, Mr. Burgess, we've known him almost since we were infants, and

[059-101]of course we take an interest in his welfare. There has never been

[059-102]anything more than that. Arabella is nothing more to him than I am.

[059-103]Once, indeed--; but, however--; that does not signify. It would be

[059-104]nothing to us, if he really liked Dorothy Stanbury. But as far as we

[059-105]can see,--and we do see a good deal of him,--there is no such feeling

[059-106]on his part. Of course we haven't asked. We should not think of such

[059-107]a thing. Mr. Gibson may do just as he likes for us. But I am not

[059-108]quite sure that Dorothy Stanbury is just the girl that would make

[059-109]him a good wife. Of course when you've known a person seven or eight

[059-110]years you do get anxious about his happiness. Do you know, we think

[059-111]her,--perhaps a little,--sly."

[059-112]In the meantime, Mr. Gibson was completely subject to the individual

[059-113]charms of Arabella. Camilla had been quite correct in a part of

[059-114]her description of their intimacy. She and her sister had known Mr.

[059-115]Gibson for seven or eight years; but nevertheless the intimacy could

[059-116]not with truth be said to have commenced during the infancy of the

[059-117]young ladies, even if the word were used in its legal sense. Seven or

[059-118]eight years, however, is a long acquaintance; and there was, perhaps,

[059-119]something of a real grievance in this Stanbury intervention. If it

[059-120]be a recognised fact in society that young ladies are in want of

[059-121]husbands, and that an effort on their part towards [[marriage|matrimony]] is not

[059-122]altogether impossible, it must be recognised also that failure will

[059-123]be disagreeable, and interference regarded with animosity. Miss

[059-124]Stanbury the elder was undoubtedly interfering between Mr. Gibson

[059-125]and the Frenches; and it is neither manly nor womanly to submit

[059-126]to interference with one's dearest prospects. It may, perhaps, be

[059-127]admitted that the Miss Frenches had shown too much open ardour in

[059-128]their pursuit of Mr. Gibson. Perhaps there should have been no ardour

[059-129]and no pursuit. It may be that the theory of womanhood is right which

[059-130]forbids to women any such attempts,--which teaches them that they

[059-131]must ever be pursued, never the pursuers. As to that there shall be

[059-132]no discourse at present. But it must be granted that whenever the

[059-133]pursuit has been attempted, it is not in human nature to abandon it

[059-134]without an effort. That the French girls should be very angry with

[059-135]Miss Stanbury, that they should put their heads together with the

[059-136]intention of thwarting her, that they should think evil things of

[059-137]poor Dorothy, that they should half despise Mr. Gibson, and yet

[059-138]resolve to keep their hold upon him as a chattel and a thing of value

[059-139]that was almost their own, was not perhaps much to their discredit.

[059-140]"You are a good deal at the house in the Close now," said Arabella,

[059-141]in her lowest voice,--in a voice so low that it was almost

[059-142]melancholy.

[059-143]"Well; yes. Miss Stanbury, you know, has always been a staunch friend

[059-144]of mine. And she takes an interest in my little church." People say

[059-145]that girls are sly; but men can be sly, too, sometimes.

[059-146]"It seems that she has taken you so much away from us, Mr. Gibson."

[059-147]"I don't know why you should say that, Miss French."

[059-148]"Perhaps I am wrong. One is apt to be sensitive about one's friends.

[059-149]We seem to have known you so well. There is nobody else in Exeter

[059-150]that mamma regards as she does you. But, of course, if you are happy

[059-151]with Miss Stanbury that is everything."

[059-152]"I am speaking of the old lady," said Mr. Gibson, who, in spite of

[059-153]his slyness, was here thrown a little off his guard.

[059-154]"And I am speaking of the old lady too," said Arabella. "Of whom else

[059-155]should I be speaking?"

[059-156]"No;--of course not."

[059-157]"Of course," continued Arabella, "I hear what people say about the

[059-158]niece. One cannot help what one hears, you know, Mr. Gibson; but I

[059-159]don't believe that, I can assure you." As she said this, she looked

[059-160]into his face, as though waiting for an answer; but Mr. Gibson had no

[059-161]answer ready. Then Arabella told herself that if anything was to be

[059-162]done it must be done at once. What use was there in beating round

[059-163]the bush, when the only chance of getting the game was to be had by

[059-164]dashing at once into the thicket. "I own I should be glad," she said,

[059-165]turning her eyes away from him, "if I could hear from your own mouth

[059-166]that it is not true."

[059-167]Mr. Gibson's position was one not to be envied. Were he willing to

[059-168]tell the very secrets of his soul to Miss French with the utmost

[059-169]candour, he could not answer her question either one way or the

[059-170]other, and he was not willing to tell her any of his secrets. It was

[059-171]certainly the fact, too, that there had been tender passages between

[059-172]him and Arabella. Now, when there have been such passages, and the

[059-173][[social-class|gentleman]] is cross-examined by the lady, as Mr. Gibson was being

[059-174]cross-examined at the present moment,--the gentleman usually teaches

[059-175]himself to think that a little falsehood is permissible. A gentleman

[059-176]can hardly tell a lady that he has become tired of her, and has

[059-177]changed his mind. He feels the matter, perhaps, more keenly even than

[059-178]she does; and though, at all other times he may be a very Paladin in

[059-179]the cause of truth, in such strait as this he does allow himself some

[059-180]latitude.
