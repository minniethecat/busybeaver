## Section 155

[155-001]Throughout all the Trevelyan troubles Lady Milborough had continued

[155-002]to shew a friendly anxiety on behalf of [[emily-trevelyan|Emily Trevelyan]]. She had

[155-003]called once or twice on Lady Rowley, and Lady Rowley had of course

[155-004]returned the visits. She had been forward in expressing her belief

[155-005]that in truth the wife had been but little if at all to blame,

[155-006]and had won her way with Lady Rowley, though she had never been

[155-007]a favourite with either of Lady Rowley's daughters. Now, in her

[155-008]difficulty, Lady Rowley went to Lady Milborough, and returned with

[155-009]an invitation that Nora should come to Eccleston Square, either till

[155-010]such time as she might think fit to go to Monkhams, or till Mrs.

[155-011]Trevelyan should have returned, and should be desirous of having her

[155-012]sister with her. When Nora first heard of this she almost screamed

[155-013]with surprise, and, if the truth must be told, with disappointment

[155-014]also.

[155-015]"She never liked me, mamma."

[155-016]"Then she is so much more good-natured."

[155-017]"But I don't want to go to her merely because she is good-natured

[155-018]enough to receive a person she dislikes. I know she is very good. I

[155-019]know she would sacrifice herself for anything she thought right. But,

[155-020]mamma, she is such a bore!"

[155-021]But Lady Rowley would not be talked down, even by Nora, in this

[155-022]fashion. Nora was somewhat touched with an idea that it would be a

[155-023]fine independent thing to live alone, if it were only for a week or

[155-024]two, just because other young ladies never lived alone. Perhaps there

[155-025]was some half-formed notion in her mind that permission to do so was

[155-026]part of the reward due to her for having refused to marry a lord.

[155-027]Stanbury was in some respects a Bohemian, and it would become her,

[155-028]she thought, to have a little practice herself in the Bohemian

[155-029]line. She had, indeed, declined a Bohemian marriage, feeling

[155-030]strongly averse to encounter the loud displeasure of her father and

[155-031]mother;--but as long as everything was quite proper, as long as there

[155-032]should be no running away, or subjection of her name to scandal, she

[155-033]considered that a little [[personal-freedom|independence]] would be useful and agreeable.

[155-034]She had looked forward to sitting up at night alone by a single

[155-035]tallow candle, to stretching a beefsteak so as to last her for two

[155-036]days' dinners, and perhaps to making her own bed. Now, there would

[155-037]not be the slightest touch of romance in a visit to Lady Milborough's

[155-038]house in Eccleston Square, at the end of July. Lady Rowley, however,

[155-039]was of a different opinion, and spoke her mind plainly. "Nora, my

[155-040]dear, don't be a fool. A young lady like you can't go and live in

[155-041]lodgings by herself. All manner of things would be said. And this is

[155-042]such a very kind offer! You must accept it,--for Hugh's sake. I have

[155-043]already said that you would accept it."

[155-044]"But she will be going out of town."

[155-045]"She will stay till you can go to Monkhams,--if Emily is not back

[155-046]before then. She knows all about Emily's affairs; and if she does

[155-047]come back,--which I doubt, poor thing,--Lady Milborough and you will

[155-048]be able to judge whether you should go to her." So it was settled,

[155-049]and Nora's Bohemian Castle in the Air fell into shatters.

[155-050]The few remaining days before the departure to Southampton passed

[155-051]quickly, but yet sadly. Sir Marmaduke had come to England expecting

[155-052]pleasure,--and with that undefined idea which men so employed always

[155-053]have on their return home that something will turn up which will make

[155-054]their going back to that same banishment unnecessary. What Governor

[155-055]of Hong-Kong, what Minister to Bogota, what General of the Forces

[155-056]at the Gold Coast, ever left the scene of his official or military

[155-057]labours without a hope, which was almost an expectation, that a

[155-058]grateful country would do something better for him before the period

[155-059]of his return should have arrived? But a grateful country was doing

[155-060]nothing better for Sir Marmaduke, and an ungrateful Secretary of

[155-061]State at the Colonial Office would not extend the term during which

[155-062]he could regard himself as absent on special service. How thankful he

[155-063]had been when first the tidings reached him that he was to come home

[155-064]at the expense of the Crown, and without diminution of his official

[155-065][[money|income]]! He had now been in England for five months, with a per diem

[155-066]allowance, with his very cabs paid for him, and he was discontented,

[155-067]sullen, and with nothing to comfort him but his official grievance,

[155-068]because he could not be allowed to extend his period of special

[155-069]service more than two months beyond the time at which those special

[155-070]services were in truth ended! There had been a change of Ministry in

[155-071]the last month, and he had thought that a Conservative Secretary of

[155-072]State would have been kinder to him. "The Duke says I can stay three

[155-073]months with leave of absence;--and have half my pay stopped. I wonder

[155-074]whether it ever enters into his august mind that even a Colonial

[155-075]Governor must eat and drink." It was thus he expressed his great

[155-076]grievance to his wife. "The Duke," however, had been as inexorable

[155-077]as his predecessor, and Sir Rowley, with his large family, was too

[155-078]wise to remain to the detriment of his pocket. In the meantime the

[155-079]clerks in the office, who had groaned in spirit over the ignorance

[155-080]displayed in his [[truth|evidence]] before the committee, were whispering

[155-081]among themselves that he ought not to be sent back to his seat of

[155-082]government at all.

[155-083]Lady Rowley also was disappointed and unhappy. She had expected so

[155-084]much pleasure from her visit to her daughter, and she had received so

[155-085]little! Emily's condition was very sad, but in her heart of hearts

[155-086]perhaps she groaned more bitterly over all that Nora had lost, than

[155-087]she did over the real sorrows of her elder child. To have had the cup

[155-088]at her lip, and then not to have tasted it! And she had the solace of

[155-089]no communion in this sorrow. She had accepted [[hugh-stanbury|Hugh Stanbury]] as her

[155-090]son-in-law, and not for worlds would she now say a word against him

[155-091]to any one. She had already taken him to her heart, and she loved

[155-092]him. But to have had it almost within her grasp to have had a lord,

[155-093]the owner of Monkhams, for her son-in-law! Poor Lady Rowley!

[155-094]Sophie and Lucy, too, were returning to their distant and dull

[155-095]banishment without any realisation of their probable but unexpressed

[155-096]ambition. They made no complaint, but yet it was hard on them

[155-097]that their sister's misfortune should have prevented them from

[155-098]going,--almost to a single dance. Poor Sophie and poor Lucy! They

[155-099]must go, and we shall hear no more about them. It was thought well

[155-100]that Nora should not go down with them to Southampton. What good

[155-101]would her going do? "God bless you, my darling," said the mother, as

[155-102]she held her child in her arms.

[155-103]"Good-bye, dear mamma."

[155-104]"Give my best love to Hugh, and tell him that I pray him with my

[155-105]last word to be good to you." Even then she was thinking of Lord

[155-106]Peterborough, but the memory of what might have been was buried deep

[155-107]in her mind.

[155-108]"Nora, tell me all about it," said Lucy.

[155-109]"There will be nothing to tell," said Nora.

[155-110]"Tell it all the same," said Lucy. "And bring Hugh out to write a

[155-111]book of travels about the Mandarins. Nobody has ever written a book

[155-112]about the Mandarins." So they parted; and when Sir Marmaduke and his

[155-113]party were taken off in two cabs to the Waterloo Station, Nora was

[155-114]taken in one cab to Eccleston Square.

[155-115]It may be doubted whether any old lady since the world began ever did

[155-116]a more thoroughly Christian and friendly act than this which was now

[155-117]being done by Lady Milborough. It was the end of July, and she would

[155-118]already have been down in Dorsetshire, but for her devotion to this

[155-119]good deed. For, in truth, what she was doing was not occasioned by

[155-120]any express love for [[nora-rowley|Nora Rowley]]. Nora Rowley was all very well, but

[155-121]Nora Rowley towards her had been flippant, impatient, and, indeed,

[155-122]not always so civil as a young lady should be to the elderly friends

[155-123]of her married sister. But to Lady Milborough it had seemed to be

[155-124]quite terrible that a young girl should be left alone in the world,

[155-125]without anybody to take care of her. Young ladies, according to her

[155-126]views of life, were fragile plants that wanted much nursing before

[155-127]they could be allowed to be planted out in the gardens of the

[155-128]world as married women. When she heard from Lady Rowley that Nora

[155-129]was engaged to marry Hugh Stanbury,--"You know all about Lord

[155-130]Peterborough, Lady Milborough; but it is no use going back to that

[155-131]now,--is it? And Mr. Stanbury has behaved so exceedingly well

[155-132]in regard to poor Louis,"--when Lady Milborough heard this, and

[155-133]heard also that Nora was talking of going to live by herself--in

[155-134]lodgings--she swore to herself, like a goodly Christian woman, as

[155-135]she was, that such a thing must not be. Eccleston Square in July

[155-136]and August is not pleasant, unless it be to an inhabitant who

[155-137]is interested in the fag-end of the parliamentary session. Lady

[155-138]Milborough had no interest in politics,--had not much interest even

[155-139]in seeing the social season out to its dregs. She ordinarily remained

[155-140]in [[london|London]] till the beginning or middle of July, because the people

[155-141]with whom she lived were in the habit of doing so;--but as soon as

[155-142]ever she had fixed the date of her departure, that day to her was

[155-143]a day of release. On this occasion the day had been fixed,--and it

[155-144]was unfixed, and changed, and postponed, because it was manifest

[155-145]to Lady Milborough that she could do good by remaining for another

[155-146]fortnight. When she made the offer she said nothing of her previous

[155-147]arrangements. "Lady Rowley, let her come to me. As soon as her friend

[155-148]Lady Peterborough is at Monkhams, she can go there."

[155-149]Thus it was that Nora found herself established in Eccleston Square.

[155-150]As she took her place in Lady Milborough's drawing-rooms, she

[155-151]remembered well a certain day, now two years ago, when she had first

[155-152]heard of the glories of Monkhams in that very house. Lady Milborough,

[155-153]as good-natured then as she was now, had brought Mr. Glascock and

[155-154]Nora together, simply because she had heard that the [[social-class|gentleman]]

[155-155]admired the young lady. Nora, in her pride, had resented this as

[155-156]interference,--had felt that the thing had been done, and, though she

[155-157]had valued the admiration of the man, had ridiculed the action of the

[155-158]woman. As she thought of it now she was softened by gratitude. She

[155-159]had not on that occasion been suited with a husband, but she had

[155-160]gained a friend. "My dear," said Lady Milborough, as at her request

[155-161]Nora took off her hat, "I am afraid that the parties are mostly

[155-162]over,--that is, those I go to; but we will drive out every day, and

[155-163]the time won't be so very long."

[155-164]"It won't be long for me, Lady Milborough;--but I cannot but know how

[155-165]terribly I am putting you out."

[155-166]"I am never put out, Miss Rowley," said the old lady, "as long as I

[155-167]am made to think that what I do is taken in good part."

[155-168]"Indeed, indeed it shall be taken in good part," said Nora,--"indeed

[155-169]it shall." And she swore a solemn silent vow of friendship for the

[155-170]dear old woman.

[155-171]Then there came letters and telegrams from Chambery, Dijon, and

[155-172]Paris, and the joint expedition in search of the cottage was made

[155-173]to Twickenham. It was astonishing how enthusiastic and how loving

[155-174]the elder and the younger lady were together before the party from

[155-175]Italy had arrived in England. Nora had explained everything about

[155-176]herself,--how impossible it had been for her not to love Hugh

[155-177]Stanbury; how essential it had been for her happiness and self-esteem

[155-178]that she should refuse Mr. Glascock; how terrible had been the

[155-179]tragedy of her sister's marriage. Lady Milborough spoke of the former

[155-180]subject with none of Lady Rowley's enthusiasm, but still with an

[155-181]evident partiality for her own rank, which almost aroused Nora to

[155-182]indignant eloquence. Lady Milborough was contented to acknowledge

[155-183]that Nora might be right, seeing that her heart was so firmly fixed;

[155-184]but she was clearly of opinion that Mr. Glascock, being Mr. Glascock,

[155-185]had possessed a better right to the prize in question than could

[155-186]have belonged to any man who had no recognised position in the world.

[155-187]Seeing that her heart had been given away, Nora was no doubt right

[155-188]not to separate her hand from her heart; but Lady Milborough was of

[155-189]opinion that young ladies ought to have their hearts under better

[155-190]control, so that the men entitled to the prizes should get them. It

[155-191]was for the welfare of England at large that the eldest sons of good

[155-192]families should marry the sweetest, prettiest, brightest, and most

[155-193]lovable girls of their age. It is a doctrine on behalf of which very

[155-194]much may be said.
