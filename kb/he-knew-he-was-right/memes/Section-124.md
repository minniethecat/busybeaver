## Section 124

[124-001]"Who;--Trevelyan?"

[124-002]"I did not suppose you could learn about him, because he would be

[124-003]hiding himself. But is Mr. Glascock here?"

[124-004]"I forgot to ask," said Sir Marmaduke.

[124-005]Lady Rowley did not reproach him. It is impossible that any father

[124-006]should altogether share a mother's anxiety in regard to the marriage

[124-007]of their daughters. But what a thing it would be! Lady Rowley thought

[124-008]that she could compound for all misfortunes in other respects, if she

[124-009]could have a daughter married to the future Lord Peterborough. She

[124-010]had been told in England that he was faultless,--not very clever, not

[124-011]very active, not likely to be very famous; but, as a husband, simply

[124-012]faultless. He was very rich, very good-natured, easily managed,

[124-013]more likely to be proud of his wife than of himself, addicted to no

[124-014]jealousies, afflicted by no vices, so respectable in every way that

[124-015]he was sure to become great as an English nobleman by the very weight

[124-016]of his virtues. And it had been represented also to Lady Rowley

[124-017]that this paragon among men had been passionately attached to her

[124-018]daughter! Perhaps she magnified a little the romance of the story;

[124-019]but it seemed to her that this greatly endowed lover had rushed away

[124-020]from his country in despair, because her daughter Nora would not

[124-021]smile upon him. Now they were, as she hoped, in the same city with

[124-022]him. But it was indispensable to her success that she should not seem

[124-023]to be running after him. To Nora, not a word had been said of the

[124-024]prospect of meeting Mr. Glascock at Florence. Hardly more than a

[124-025]word had been said to her sister Emily, and that under injunction

[124-026]of strictest secrecy. It must be made to appear to all the world

[124-027]that other motives had brought them to Florence,--as, indeed, other

[124-028]motives had brought them. Not for worlds would Lady Rowley have run

[124-029]after a man for her daughter; but still, still,--still, seeing that

[124-030]the man was himself so unutterably in love with her girl, seeing that

[124-031]he was so fully justified by his position to be in love with any

[124-032]girl, seeing that such a maximum of happiness would be the result of

[124-033]such a marriage, she did feel that, even for his sake, she must be

[124-034]doing a good thing to bring them together! Something, though not much

[124-035]of all this, she had been obliged to explain to Sir Marmaduke;--and

[124-036]yet he had not taken the trouble to inquire whether Mr. Glascock was

[124-037]in Florence!

[124-038]On the third day after their arrival, the wife of the British

[124-039]minister came to call upon Lady Rowley, and the wife of the British

[124-040]minister was good-natured, easy-mannered, and very much given to

[124-041]conversation. She preferred talking to listening, and in the course

[124-042]of a quarter of an hour had told Lady Rowley a good deal about

[124-043]Florence; but she had not mentioned Mr. Glascock's name. It would

[124-044]have been so pleasant if the requisite information could have been

[124-045]obtained without the asking of any direct question on the subject!

[124-046]But Lady Rowley, who from many years' practice of similar, though

[124-047]perhaps less distinguished, courtesies on her part, knew well the

[124-048]first symptom of the coming end of her guest's visit, found that the

[124-049]minister's wife was about to take her departure without an allusion

[124-050]to Mr. Glascock. And yet the names had been mentioned of so many

[124-051]English residents in Florence, who neither in [[money|wealth]], [[social-class|rank]], or

[124-052]virtue, were competent to hold a candle to that phoenix! She was

[124-053]forced, therefore, to pluck up courage, and to ask the question.

[124-054]"Have you had a Mr. Glascock here this spring?" said Lady Rowley.

[124-055]"What;--Lord Peterborough's son? Oh, dear, yes. Such a singular

[124-056]being!"

[124-057]Lady Rowley thought that she could perceive that her phoenix had

[124-058]not made himself agreeable at the embassy. It might perhaps be that

[124-059]he had buried himself away from society because of his love. "And is

[124-060]here now?" asked Lady Rowley.

[124-061]"I cannot say at all. He is sometimes here and sometimes with his

[124-062]father at Naples. But when here, he lives chiefly with the Americans.

[124-063]They say he is going to marry an American girl,--their minister's

[124-064]niece. There are three of them, I think, and he is to take the

[124-065]eldest." Lady Rowley asked no more questions, and let her august

[124-066]visitor go, almost without another word.

[124-067]CHAPTER LXXVI.

[124-068]"WE SHALL BE SO POOR."

[124-069]Mr. Glascock at that moment was not only in Florence, but was

[124-070]occupying rooms in the very hotel in which the Rowleys were staying.

[124-071]Lady Rowley, when she heard that he was engaged to marry an American

[124-072]lady, became suddenly very sick at heart,--sick with a sickness

[124-073]that almost went beyond her heart. She felt ill, and was glad to be

[124-074]alone. The rumour might be untrue. Such rumours generally are untrue.

[124-075]But then, as Lady Rowley knew very well, they generally have some

[124-076]foundation in truth. Mr. Glascock, if he were not actually engaged

[124-077]to the American girl, had probably been flirting with her;--and, if

[124-078]so, where was that picture which Lady Rowley had been painting for

[124-079]herself of a love-lorn swain to be brought back to the pleasures and

[124-080]occupations of the world only by the girl of whom he was enamoured?

[124-081]But still she would not quite give up the project. Mr. Glascock,

[124-082]if he was in Italy, would no doubt see by the newspapers that Sir

[124-083]Marmaduke and his family were in Florence,--and would probably come

[124-084]to them. Then, if Nora would only behave herself, the American girl

[124-085]might still be conquered.

[124-086]During two or three days after this nothing was seen or heard of

[124-087]Mr. Glascock. Had Lady Rowley thought of mentioning the name to the

[124-088]waiter at the hotel, she would have learned that he was living in the

[124-089]next passage; but it did not occur to her to seek information in that

[124-090]fashion. Nor did she ask direct questions in other quarters about

[124-091]Mr. Glascock himself. She did, however, make inquiry about Americans

[124-092]living in Florence,--especially about the American Minister,--and,

[124-093]before a week had passed overhead, had been introduced to the

[124-094]Spaldings. Mrs. Spalding was very civil, and invited Lady Rowley and

[124-095]all the girls and Sir Marmaduke to come to her on her "Fridays." She

[124-096]received her friends every Friday, and would continue to do so till

[124-097]the middle of June. She had nieces who would, she said, be so happy

[124-098]to make the acquaintance of the Miss Rowleys.

[124-099]By this time the picture galleries, the churches, and the palaces in

[124-100]Florence had nearly all been visited. Poor Lady Rowley had dragged

[124-101]herself wearily from sight to sight, hoping always to meet with

[124-102]Mr. Glascock, ignorant of the fact that residents in a town do not

[124-103]pass their mornings habitually in looking after pictures. During

[124-104]this time inquiries were being made through the police, respecting

[124-105]Trevelyan; and Sir Marmaduke had obtained information that an English

[124-106]gentleman, with a little boy, had gone on to Siena, and had located

[124-107]himself there. There seemed to be but little doubt that this was

[124-108]Trevelyan,--though nothing had been learned with [[truth|certainty]] as to the

[124-109]gentleman's name. It had been decided that Sir Marmaduke, with his

[124-110]courier and Mrs. Trevelyan, should go on to Siena, and endeavour

[124-111]to come upon the fugitive, and they had taken their departure on a

[124-112]certain morning. On that same day Lady Rowley was walking with Nora

[124-113]and one of the other girls through the hall of the hotel, when they

[124-114]were met in full face--by Mr. Glascock! Lady Rowley and Lucy were in

[124-115]front, and they, of course, did not know the man. Nora had seen him

[124-116]at once, and in her confusion hardly knew how to bear herself. Mr.

[124-117]Glascock was passing by her without recognising her,--had passed her

[124-118]mother and sister, and had so far gone on, that Nora had determined

[124-119]to make no sign, when he chanced to look up and see who it was that

[124-120]was so close to him. "Miss Rowley," he said, "who thought of meeting

[124-121]you in Florence!" Lady Rowley, of course, turned round, and there was

[124-122]an introduction. Poor Nora, though she knew nothing of her mother's

[124-123]schemes, was confused and ill at ease. Mr. Glascock was very civil,

[124-124]but at the same time rather cold. Lady Rowley was all smiles and

[124-125]courtesy. She had, she said, heard his name from her daughters, and

[124-126]was very happy to make his acquaintance. Lucy looked on somewhat

[124-127]astonished to find that the lover whom her sister had been blamed for

[124-128]rejecting, and who was spoken of with so many encomiums, was so old a

[124-129]man. Mr. Glascock asked after Mrs. Trevelyan; and Lady Rowley, in a

[124-130]low, melancholy whisper, told him that they were now all in Florence,

[124-131]in the hope of meeting Mr. Trevelyan. "You have heard the sad story,

[124-132]I know, Mr. Glascock,--and therefore I do not mind telling you." Mr.

[124-133]Glascock acknowledged that he did know the story, and informed her

[124-134]that he had seen Mr. Trevelyan in Florence within the last ten days.

[124-135]This was so interesting, that, at Lady Rowley's request, he went with

[124-136]them up to their rooms, and in this way the acquaintance was made. It

[124-137]turned out that Mr. Glascock had spoken to Mr. Trevelyan, and that

[124-138]Trevelyan had told him that he meant for the present to take up his

[124-139]residence in some small Italian town. "And how was he looking, Mr.

[124-140]Glascock?"

[124-141]"Very ill, Lady Rowley;--very ill, indeed."

[124-142]"Do not tell her so, Mr. Glascock. She has gone now with her father

[124-143]to Siena. We think that he is there, with the boy,--or, at least,

[124-144]that he may be heard of there. And you;--you are living here?" Mr.

[124-145]Glascock said that he was living between Naples and Florence,--going

[124-146]occasionally to Naples, a place that he hated, to see his father,

[124-147]and coming back at intervals to the capital. Nora sat by, and hardly

[124-148]spoke a word. She was nicely dressed, with an exquisite little

[124-149]bonnet, which had been bought as they came through Paris; and Lady

[124-150]Rowley, with natural pride, felt that if he was ever in love with her

[124-151]child, that love must come back upon him now. American girls, she had

[124-152]been told, were hard, and dry, and sharp, and angular. She had seen

[124-153]some at the Mandarins, with whom she thought it must be impossible

[124-154]that any Englishman should be in love. There never, surely, had

[124-155]been an American girl like her Nora. "Are you fond of pictures, Mr.

[124-156]Glascock?" she asked. Mr. Glascock was not very fond of pictures,

[124-157]and thought that he was rather tired of them. What was he fond of?

[124-158]Of sitting at home and doing nothing. That was his reply, at least;

[124-159]and a very unsatisfactory reply it was, as Lady Rowley could hardly

[124-160]propose that they should come and sit and do nothing with him. Could

[124-161]he have been lured into churches or galleries, Nora might have been

[124-162]once more thrown into his company. Then Lady Rowley took courage,

[124-163]and asked him whether he knew the Spaldings. They were going to Mrs.

[124-164]Spalding's that very evening,--she and her daughters. Mr. Glascock

[124-165]replied that he did know the Spaldings, and that he also should be at

[124-166]their house. Lady Rowley thought that she discovered something like a

[124-167]blush about his cheekbones and brow, as he made his answer. Then he

[124-168]left them, giving his hand to Nora as he went;--but there was nothing

[124-169]in his manner to justify the slightest hope.

[124-170]"I don't think he is nice at all," said Lucy.

[124-171]"Don't be so foolish, Lucy," said Lady Rowley angrily.

[124-172]"I think he is very nice," said Nora. "He was only talking nonsense

[124-173]when he said that he liked to sit still and do nothing. He is not at

[124-174]all an idle man;--at least I am told so."

[124-175]"But he is as old as Methuselah," said Lucy.
