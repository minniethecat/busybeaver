## Section 5

[005-001]Such was the secrecy of [[colonel-osborne|Colonel Osborne]]'s secret! He had been so

[005-002]chary of having his name mentioned in connection with a political

[005-003]job, that he had found it necessary to impose on his young friend

[005-004]the burden of a secret from her husband, and yet the husband heard

[005-005]the whole story told openly at his club on the same day! There was

[005-006]nothing in the story to anger Trevelyan had he not immediately felt

[005-007]that there must be some plan in the matter between his wife and

[005-008]Colonel Osborne, of which he had been kept ignorant. Hitherto,

[005-009]indeed, his wife, as the reader knows, could not have told him. He

[005-010]had not seen her since the matter had been discussed between her and

[005-011]her friend. But he was angry because he first learned at his club

[005-012]that which he thought he ought to have learned at home.

[005-013]As soon as he reached his house he went at once to his wife's room,

[005-014]but her maid was with her, and nothing could be said at that moment.

[005-015]He then dressed himself, intending to go to Emily as soon as the girl

[005-016]had left her; but the girl remained,--was, as he believed, kept in

[005-017]the room purposely by his wife, so that he should have no moment of

[005-018]private conversation. He went down-stairs, therefore, and found Nora

[005-019][[reputation|standing]] by the drawing-room fire.

[005-020]"So you are dressed first to-day?" he said. "I thought your turn

[005-021]always came last."

[005-022]"Emily sent Jenny to me first to-day because she thought you would be

[005-023]home, and she didn't go up to dress till the last minute."

[005-024]This was intended well by Nora, but it did not have the desired

[005-025]effect. Trevelyan, who had no command over his own features, frowned,

[005-026]and showed that he was displeased. He hesitated a moment, thinking

[005-027]whether he would ask Nora any question as to this report about her

[005-028]father and mother; but, before he had spoken, his wife was in the

[005-029]room.

[005-030]"We are all late, I fear," said Emily.

[005-031]"You, at any rate, are the last," said her husband.

[005-032]"[[About]] half a minute," said the wife.

[005-033]Then they got into the hired brougham which was [[reputation|standing]] at the door.

[005-034]Trevelyan, in the sweet days of his early confidence with his wife,

[005-035]had offered to keep a carriage for her, explaining to her that the

[005-036]luxury, though costly, would not be beyond his reach. But she had

[005-037]persuaded him against the carriage, and there had come to be an

[005-038]agreement that instead of the carriage there should always be an

[005-039]autumn tour. "One learns something from going about; but one learns

[005-040]nothing from keeping a carriage," Emily had said. Those had been

[005-041]happy days, in which it had been intended that everything should

[005-042]always be rose-coloured. Now he was meditating whether, in lieu of

[005-043]that autumn tour, it would not be necessary to take his wife away to

[005-044]Naples altogether, so that she might be removed from the influence

[005-045]of--of--of--; no, not even to himself would he think of Colonel

[005-046]Osborne as his wife's lover. The idea was too horrible! And yet, how

[005-047]dreadful was it that he should have, for any reason, to withdraw her

[005-048]from the influence of any man!

[005-049]Lady Milborough lived ever so far away, in Eccleston Square, but

[005-050]Trevelyan did not say a single word to either of his companions

[005-051]during the journey. He was cross and vexed, and was conscious that

[005-052]they knew that he was cross and vexed. Mrs. Trevelyan and her sister

[005-053]talked to each other the whole way, but they did so in that tone

[005-054]which clearly indicates that the conversation is made up, not for any

[005-055]interest attached to the questions asked or the answers given, but

[005-056]because it is expedient that there should not be silence. Nora said

[005-057]something about Marshall and Snellgrove, and tried to make believe

[005-058]that she was very anxious for her sister's answer. And Emily said

[005-059]something about the opera at Covent Garden, which was intended

[005-060]to show that her mind was quite at ease. But both of them failed

[005-061]altogether, and knew that they failed. Once or twice Trevelyan

[005-062]thought that he would say a word in token, as it were, of repentance.

[005-063]Like the naughty child who knew that he was naughty, he was trying to

[005-064]be good. But he could not do it. The fiend was too strong within him.

[005-065]She must have known that there was a proposition for her father's

[005-066]return through Colonel Osborne's influence. As that man at the club

[005-067]had heard it, how could she not have known it? When they got out at

[005-068]Lady Milborough's door he had spoken to neither of them.

[005-069]There was a large dull party, made up mostly of old people. Lady

[005-070]Milborough and Trevelyan's mother had been bosom friends, and

[005-071]Lady Milborough had on this account taken upon herself to be much

[005-072]interested in Trevelyan's wife. But [[louis-trevelyan|Louis Trevelyan]] himself, in

[005-073]discussing Lady Milborough with Emily, had rather turned his mother's

[005-074]old friend into ridicule, and Emily had, of course, followed her

[005-075]husband's mode of thinking. Lady Milborough had once or twice given

[005-076]her some advice on small matters, telling her that this or that air

[005-077]would be good for her baby, and explaining that a mother during a

[005-078]certain interesting portion of her life, should refresh herself

[005-079]with a certain kind of malt liquor. Of all counsel on such domestic

[005-080]subjects Mrs. Trevelyan was impatient,--as indeed it was her nature

[005-081]to be in all matters, and consequently, authorized as she had been

[005-082]by her husband's manner of speaking of his mother's friend, she

[005-083]had taken a habit of quizzing Lady Milborough behind her back,

[005-084]and almost of continuing the practice before the old lady's face.

[005-085]Lady Milborough, who was the most affectionate old soul alive,

[005-086]and good-tempered with her friends to a fault, had never resented

[005-087]this, but had come to fear that Mrs. Trevelyan was perhaps a little

[005-088]flighty. She had never as yet allowed herself to say anything worse

[005-089]of her young friend's wife than that. And she would always add that

[005-090]that kind of thing would cure itself as the nursery became full. It

[005-091]must be understood therefore that Mrs. Trevelyan was not anticipating

[005-092]much pleasure from Lady Milborough's party, and that she had accepted

[005-093]the invitation as a matter of duty.

[005-094]There was present among the guests a certain Honourable Charles

[005-095]Glascock, the eldest son of Lord Peterborough, who made the

[005-096]affair more interesting to Nora than it was to her sister. It had

[005-097]been whispered into Nora's ears, by more than one person,--and

[005-098]among others by Lady Milborough, whose own daughters were all

[005-099]married,--that she might, if she thought fit, become the Honourable

[005-100]Mrs. Charles Glascock. Now, whether she might think fit, or

[005-101]whether she might not, the presence of the [[social-class|gentleman]] under such

[005-102]circumstances, as far as she was concerned, gave an interest to the

[005-103]evening. And as Lady Milborough took care that Mr. Glascock should

[005-104]take Nora down to dinner, the interest was very great. Mr. Glascock

[005-105]was a good-looking man, just under forty, in Parliament, heir to

[005-106]a peerage, and known to be well off in respect to [[money|income]]. Lady

[005-107]Milborough and Mrs. Trevelyan had told [[nora-rowley|Nora Rowley]] that should

[005-108]encouragement in that direction come in her way, she ought to allow

[005-109]herself to fall in love with Mr. Glascock. A certain amount of

[005-110]encouragement had come in her way, but she had not as yet allowed

[005-111]herself to fall in love with Mr. Glascock. It seemed to her that Mr.

[005-112]Glascock was quite conscious of the advantages of his own position,

[005-113]and that his powers of talking about other matters than those with

[005-114]which he was immediately connected were limited. She did believe that

[005-115]he had in truth paid her the compliment of falling in love with her,

[005-116]and this is a compliment to which few girls are indifferent. Nora

[005-117]might perhaps have tried to fall in love with Mr. Glascock, had she

[005-118]not been forced to make comparisons between him and another. This

[005-119]other one had not fallen in love with her, as she well knew; and she

[005-120]certainly had not fallen in love with him. But still, the comparison

[005-121]was forced upon her, and it did not result in favour of Mr. Glascock.

[005-122]On the present occasion Mr. Glascock as he sat next to her almost

[005-123]proposed to her.

[005-124]"You have never seen Monkhams?" he said. Monkhams was his father's

[005-125]seat, a very grand place in Worcestershire. Of course he knew very

[005-126]well that she had never seen Monkhams. How should she have seen it?

[005-127]"I have never been in that part of England at all," she replied.

[005-128]"I should so like to show you Monkhams. The oaks there are the finest

[005-129]in the kingdom. Do you like oaks?"

[005-130]"Who does not like oaks? But we have none in the islands, and nobody

[005-131]has ever seen so few as I have."

[005-132]"I'll show you Monkhams some day. Shall I? Indeed, I hope that some

[005-133]day I may really show you Monkhams."

[005-134]Now when an unmarried man talks to a young lady of really showing her

[005-135]the house in which it will be his destiny to live, he can hardly mean

[005-136]other than to invite her to live there with him. It must at least be

[005-137]his purpose to signify that, if duly encouraged, he will so invite

[005-138]her. But Nora Rowley did not give Mr. Glascock much encouragement on

[005-139]this occasion.

[005-140]"I'm afraid it is not likely that anything will ever take me into

[005-141]that part of the country," she said. There was something perhaps in

[005-142]her tone which checked Mr. Glascock, so that he did not then press

[005-143]the invitation.

[005-144]When the ladies were up-stairs in the drawing-room, Lady Milborough

[005-145]contrived to seat herself on a couch intended for two persons only,

[005-146]close to Mrs. Trevelyan. Emily, thinking that she might perhaps hear

[005-147]some advice about Guinness's stout, prepared herself to be saucy. But

[005-148]the matter in hand was graver than that. Lady Milborough's mind was

[005-149]uneasy about Colonel Osborne.

[005-150]"My dear," said she, "was not your father very intimate with that

[005-151]Colonel Osborne?"

[005-152]"He is very intimate with him, Lady Milborough."

[005-153]"Ah, yes; I thought I had heard so. That makes it of course natural

[005-154]that you should know him."

[005-155]"We have known him all our lives," said Emily, forgetting probably

[005-156]that out of the twenty-three years and some months which she had

[005-157]hitherto lived, there had been a consecutive period of more than

[005-158]twenty years in which she had never seen this man whom she had known

[005-159]all her life.

[005-160]"That makes a difference, of course; and I don't mean to say anything

[005-161]against him."

[005-162]"I hope not, Lady Milborough, because we are all especially fond of

[005-163]him." This was said with so much of purpose, that poor, dear old Lady

[005-164]Milborough was stopped in her good work. She knew well the terrible

[005-165]strait to which Augustus Poole had been brought with his wife,

[005-166]although nobody supposed that Poole's wife had ever entertained a

[005-167]wrong thought in her pretty little heart. Nevertheless he had been

[005-168]compelled to break up his establishment, and take his wife to Naples,

[005-169]because this horrid Colonel would make himself at home in Mrs.

[005-170]Poole's drawing-room in Knightsbridge. Augustus Poole, with courage

[005-171]enough to take any man by the beard, had taking by the beard been

[005-172]possible, had found it impossible to dislodge the Colonel. He could

[005-173]not do so without making a row which would have been disgraceful to

[005-174]himself and injurious to his wife; and therefore he had taken Mrs.

[005-175]Poole to Naples. Lady Milborough knew the whole story, and thought

[005-176]that she foresaw that the same thing was about to happen in the

[005-177]drawing-room in Curzon Street. When she attempted to say a word to

[005-178]the wife, she found herself stopped. She could not go on in that

[005-179]quarter after the reception with which the beginning of her word had

[005-180]been met. But perhaps she might succeed better with the husband.

[005-181]After all, her friendship was with the Trevelyan side, and not with

[005-182]the Rowleys.
