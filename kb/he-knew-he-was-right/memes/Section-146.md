## Section 146

[146-001]"I am damned, if I do!" said Barty Burgess, rising up from his chair.

[146-002]But before he had left the room he had agreed to consider the

[146-003]proposition. Miss Stanbury had of course known that any such

[146-004]suggestion coming from her without an adequate reason assigned, would

[146-005]have been mere idle wind. She was prepared with such adequate reason.

[146-006]If Mr. Burgess could see his way to make the proposed transfer of his

[146-007]share of the bank business, she, Miss Stanbury, would hand over to

[146-008]him, for his life, a certain proportion of the Burgess [[money|property]] which

[146-009]lay in the city, the income of which would exceed that drawn by him

[146-010]from the business. Would he, at his time of life, take that for doing

[146-011]nothing which he now got for working hard? That was the meaning of

[146-012]it. And then, too, as far as the portion of the property went,--and

[146-013]it extended to the houses owned by Miss Stanbury on the bank side

[146-014]of the Close,--it would belong altogether to Barty Burgess for his

[146-015]life. "It will simply be this, Mr. Burgess;--that Brooke will be your

[146-016]heir,--as would be natural."

[146-017]"I don't know that it would be at all natural," said he. "I should

[146-018]prefer to choose my own heir."

[146-019]"No doubt, Mr. Burgess,--in respect to your own property," said Miss

[146-020]Stanbury.

[146-021]At last he said that he would think of it, and consult his partner;

[146-022]and then he got up to take his leave. "For myself," said Miss

[146-023]Stanbury, "I would wish that all animosities might be buried."

[146-024]"We can say that they are buried," said the grim old man,--"but

[146-025]nobody will believe us."

[146-026]"What matters,--if we could believe it ourselves?"

[146-027]"But suppose we didn't. I don't believe that much good can come from

[146-028]talking of such things, Miss Stanbury. You and I have grown too old

[146-029]to swear a friendship. I will think of this thing, and if I find

[146-030]that it can be made to suit without much difficulty, I will perhaps

[146-031]entertain it." Then the interview was over, and old Barty made

[146-032]his way down-stairs, and out of the house. He looked over to the

[146-033]tenements in the Close which were offered to him, every circumstance

[146-034]of each one of which he knew, and felt that he might do worse. Were

[146-035]he to leave the bank, he could not take his entire income with him,

[146-036]and it had been long said of him that he ought to leave it. The

[146-037]Croppers, who were his partners,--and whom he had never loved,--would

[146-038]be glad to welcome in his place one of the old family who would have

[146-039]money; and then the name would be perpetuated in [[exeter|Exeter]], which, even

[146-040]to Barty Burgess, was something.

[146-041]On that night the scheme was divulged to Dorothy, and she was in

[146-042]ecstasies. [[london|London]] had always sounded bleak and distant and terrible

[146-043]to her; and her heart had misgiven her at the idea of leaving her

[146-044]aunt. If only this thing might be arranged! When Brooke spoke the

[146-045]next morning of returning at once to his office, he was rebuked by

[146-046]both the ladies. What was the Ecclesiastical Commission Office to any

[146-047]of them, when matters of such importance were concerned? But Brooke

[146-048]would not be talked out of his prudence. He was very willing to be

[146-049]made a banker at Exeter, and to go to school again and learn banking

[146-050]business; but he would not throw up his occupation in London till he

[146-051]knew that there was another ready for him in the country. One day

[146-052]longer he spent in Exeter, and during that day he was more than once

[146-053]with his uncle. He saw also the Messrs. Cropper, and was considerably

[146-054]chilled by the manner in which they at first seemed to entertain the

[146-055]proposition. Indeed, for a couple of hours he thought that the scheme

[146-056]must be abandoned. It was pointed out to him that Mr. Barty Burgess's

[146-057]life would probably be short, and that he--Barty--had but a small

[146-058]part of the business at his disposal. But gradually a way to terms

[146-059]was seen,--not quite so simple as that which Miss Stanbury had

[146-060]suggested; and Brooke, when he left Exeter, did believe it possible

[146-061]that he, after all, might become the family representative in the old

[146-062]banking-house of the Burgesses.

[146-063]"And how long will it take, Aunt Stanbury?" Dorothy asked.

[146-064]"Don't you be impatient, my dear."

[146-065]"I am not the least impatient; but of course I want to tell mamma and

[146-066]Priscilla. It will be so nice to live here and not go up to London.

[146-067]Are we to stay here,--in this very house?"

[146-068]"Have you not found out yet that Brooke will be likely to have an

[146-069]opinion of his own on such things?"

[146-070]"But would you wish us to live here, aunt?"

[146-071]"I hardly know, dear. I am a foolish old woman, and cannot say what I

[146-072]would wish. I cannot bear to be alone."

[146-073]"Of course we will stay with you."

[146-074]"And yet I should be jealous if I were not mistress of my own house."

[146-075]"Of course you will be mistress."

[146-076]"I believe, Dolly, that it would be better that I should die. I have

[146-077]come to feel that I can do more good by going out of the world than

[146-078]by remaining in it." Dorothy hardly answered this in words, but sat

[146-079]close by her aunt, holding the old woman's hand and caressing it, and

[146-080]administering that love of which Miss Stanbury had enjoyed so little

[146-081]during her life and which had become so necessary to her.

[146-082]The news about the bank arrangements, though kept of course as a

[146-083]great secret, soon became common in Exeter. It was known to be a good

[146-084]thing for the firm in general that Barty Burgess should be removed

[146-085]from his share of the management. He was old-fashioned, unpopular,

[146-086]and very stubborn; and he and a certain Mr. Julius Cropper, who was

[146-087]the leading man among the Croppers, had not always been comfortable

[146-088]together. It was at first hinted that old Miss Stanbury had been

[146-089]softened by sudden twinges of conscience, and that she had confessed

[146-090]to some terrible crime in the way of forgery, perjury, or perhaps

[146-091]worse, and had relieved herself at last by making full restitution.

[146-092]But such a rumour as this did not last long or receive wide credence.

[146-093]When it was hinted to such old friends as Sir Peter Mancrudy and Mrs.

[146-094]MacHugh, they laughed it to scorn,--and it did not exist even in the

[146-095]vague form of an undivulged mystery for above three days. Then it

[146-096]was asserted that old Barty had been found to have no real claim

[146-097]to any share in the bank, and that he was to be turned out at Miss

[146-098]Stanbury's instance;--that he was to be turned out, and that Brooke

[146-099]had been acknowledged to be the owner of the Burgess share of her

[146-100]business. Then came the fact that old Barty had been bought out, and

[146-101]that the future husband of Miss Stanbury's niece was to be the junior

[146-102]partner. A general feeling prevailed at last that there had been

[146-103]another great battle between Miss Stanbury and old Barty, and that

[146-104]the old maid had prevailed now as she had done in former days.

[146-105]Before the end of July the papers were in the lawyer's hands, and

[146-106]all the terms had been fixed. Brooke came down again and again, to

[146-107]Dorothy's great delight, and displayed considerable firmness in the

[146-108]management of his own interest. If Fate intended to make him a banker

[146-109]in Exeter instead of a clerk in the Ecclesiastical Commission Office,

[146-110]he would be a banker after a respectable fashion. There was more

[146-111]than one little struggle between him and Mr. Julius Cropper, which

[146-112]ended in accession of respect on the part of Mr. Cropper for his new

[146-113]partner. Mr. Cropper had thought that the establishment might best

[146-114]be known to the commercial world of the West of England as "Croppers'

[146-115]Bank;" but Brooke had been very firm in asserting that if he was to

[146-116]have anything to do with it the old name should be maintained.

[146-117]"It's to be 'Cropper and Burgess,'" he said to Dorothy one afternoon.

[146-118]"They fought hard for 'Cropper, Cropper, and Burgess;'--but I

[146-119]wouldn't stand more than one Cropper."

[146-120]"Of course not," said Dorothy, with something almost of scorn in

[146-121]her voice. By this time Dorothy had gone very deeply into banking

[146-122]business.

[146-123]CHAPTER LXXXIX.

[146-124]"I WOULDN'T DO IT, IF I WAS YOU."

[146-125]Miss Stanbury at this time was known all through Exeter to be very

[146-126]much altered from the Miss Stanbury of old;--or even from the Miss

[146-127]Stanbury of two years since. The Miss Stanbury of old was a stalwart

[146-128]lady who would play her rubber of whist five nights a week, and could

[146-129]hold her own in conversation against the best woman in Exeter,--not

[146-130]to speak of her acknowledged superiority over every man in that city.

[146-131]Now she cared little for the glories of debate; and though she still

[146-132]liked her rubber, and could wake herself up to the old fire in the

[146-133]detection of a revoke or the claim for a second trick, her rubbers

[146-134]were few and far between, and she would leave her own house on an

[146-135]evening only when all circumstances were favourable, and with many

[146-136]precautions against wind and water. Some said that she was becoming

[146-137]old, and that she was going out like the snuff of a candle. But Sir

[146-138]Peter Mancrudy declared that she might live for the next fifteen

[146-139]years, if she would only think so herself. "It was true," Sir Peter

[146-140]said, "that in the winter she had been ill, and that there had been

[146-141]danger as to her throat during the east winds of the spring;--but

[146-142]those dangers had passed away, and, if she would only exert herself,

[146-143]she might be almost as good a woman as ever she had been." Sir Peter

[146-144]was not a man of many words, or given to talk frequently of his

[146-145]patients; but it was clearly Sir Peter's opinion that Miss Stanbury's

[146-146]mind was ill at ease. She had become discontented with life, and

[146-147]therefore it was that she cared no longer for the combat of tongues,

[146-148]and had become cold even towards the card-table. It was so in truth;

[146-149]and yet perhaps the lives of few men or women had been more innocent,

[146-150]and few had struggled harder to be just in their dealings and

[146-151]generous in their thoughts.

[146-152]There was ever present to her mind an idea of failure and a fear lest

[146-153]she had been mistaken in her views throughout her life. No one had

[146-154]ever been more devoted to peculiar opinions, or more strong in the

[146-155]use of language for their expression; and she was so far true to

[146-156]herself, that she would never seem to retreat from the position she

[146-157]had taken. She would still scorn the new fangles of the world around

[146-158]her, and speak of the changes which she saw as all tending to evil.

[146-159]But, through it all, there was an idea present to herself that

[146-160]it could not be God's intention that things should really change

[146-161]for the worse, and that the fault must be in her, because she had

[146-162]been unable to move as others had moved. She would sit thinking of

[146-163]the circumstances of her own life and tell herself that with her

[146-164]everything had failed. She had loved, but had quarrelled with her

[146-165]lover; and her love had come to nothing--but barren wealth. She had

[146-166]fought for her wealth and had conquered;--and had become hard in the

[146-167]fight, and was conscious of her own hardness. In the early days of

[146-168]her riches and power she had taken her nephew by the hand,--and had

[146-169]thrown him away from her because he would not dress himself in her

[146-170]mirror. She had believed herself to be right, and would not, even

[146-171]now, tell herself that she had been wrong; but there were doubts, and

[146-172]qualms of conscience, and an uneasiness,--because her life had been

[146-173]a failure. Now she was seeking to appease her self-accusations by

[146-174]sacrificing everything for the happiness of her niece and her chosen

[146-175]hero; but as she went on with the work she felt that all would be in

[146-176]vain, unless she could sweep herself altogether from off the scene.

[146-177]She had told herself that if she could bring Brooke to Exeter, his

[146-178]prospects would be made infinitely brighter than they would be in

[146-179]London, and that she in her last days would not be left utterly

[146-180]alone. But as the prospect of her future life came nearer to her, she

[146-181]saw, or thought that she saw, that there was still failure before

[146-182]her. Young people would not want an old woman in the house with

[146-183]them;--even though the old woman would declare that she would be no

[146-184]more in the house than a tame cat. And she knew herself also too well

[146-185]to believe that she could make herself a tame cat in the home that

[146-186]had so long been subject to her dominion. Would it not be better that

[146-187]she should go away somewhere,--and die?
