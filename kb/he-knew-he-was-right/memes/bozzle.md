## Overview

Bozzle is a private detective — or "inquiry agent" in the Victorian parlance — hired by [[louis-trevelyan|Louis Trevelyan]] to investigate his wife [[emily-trevelyan|Emily]]'s suspected infidelity with [[colonel-osborne|Colonel Osborne]]. Bozzle is a minor character with a major impact on the plot: his surveillance reports, ambiguous by nature and inflammatory in Trevelyan's jealous reading, accelerate the destruction of the Trevelyan marriage. He is one of Trollope's most pointed creations — a figure who embodies the corrosive intersection of money, suspicion, and the emerging Victorian surveillance economy.

## Role in the Novel

Bozzle's entrance into the narrative marks a decisive turn in Trevelyan's psychological decline. Until the moment Trevelyan engages Bozzle's services, his jealousy has been a private torment — destructive but contained within the domestic sphere. The hiring of a detective transforms private suspicion into public machinery: letters are intercepted, movements are tracked, and the intimate life of a marriage becomes the subject of paid surveillance (032-059).

Trevelyan's engagement of Bozzle is presented as both a symptom and a cause of his mental deterioration. He knows, even as he hires the detective, that he is crossing a line from which there can be no return. The narrator makes this explicit: "He knew that he was having recourse to a measure which was in itself disgraceful" — but the jealousy has so consumed his reason that disgrace seems a lesser evil than uncertainty (032-112).

Bozzle's reports are a masterful narrative device. They are factually accurate but contextually misleading — Osborne and Emily did travel on the same train, did stay in the same town — and Trevelyan, primed by jealousy, reads them as confirmation of his worst fears. Bozzle himself is not malicious; he is merely a professional providing the service for which he is paid. His neutrality is, in its way, more damning than malice would be: it reveals that the destruction of the Trevelyan marriage is, from a certain angle, simply a business transaction (032-115).

The turning point comes when Trevelyan, too late, recognizes the instrument he has employed. "I don't see what Bozzle can do," Emily says, and Trevelyan's reply — "I could do nothing, except dismiss Bozzle" — is an admission of defeat. The detective has become an embarrassment, a symbol of how far Trevelyan has fallen from the dignity of a gentleman (032-145, 032-150).

## Character and Disposition

Bozzle is drawn with economy rather than depth — he is a function more than a personality, and this is precisely Trollope's point. He is the man "who had not lived without a stain upon his character" yet who has found, in the burgeoning detective industry, a niche in which his moral ambiguity is a professional asset (032-059). He is efficient, incurious about the justice of his assignments, and entirely untroubled by the human consequences of his work.

The narrator does not condemn Bozzle outright — to do so would elevate him to a moral stature he does not possess. Instead, Bozzle is presented as a symptom of a larger social pathology: the Victorian gentleman's willingness to delegate his ugliest impulses to paid subordinates, and the market's ready supply of men willing to accept that delegation.

> The man was one Bozzle, who had not lived without a stain upon his character, and who had found, in the growing demand for private inquiry, a profession suited to his talents — talents which consisted principally in an absolute indifference to the feelings of those whom he was paid to watch. (032-059)

## Key Scenes

Bozzle's most significant contribution to the narrative occurs through his reports rather than his presence. The letter he sends from Exeter, detailing Osborne's movements and Emily's whereabouts, is read by Trevelyan with the desperate credulity of a man who has already decided what he believes (032-062). Bozzle's surveillance of the railway journey to Lessboro' — in which Osborne and the detective travel on the same train, the watcher and the watched occupying adjacent carriages in a grotesque parody of social intimacy — is one of the novel's most darkly comic sequences (032-065).

The final mention of Bozzle — Trevelyan's decision to dismiss him — is notably anticlimactic. The detective who has done so much damage simply disappears from the narrative, his work done, his bill presumably paid. There is no reckoning, no moment of accountability. The machinery of surveillance, once engaged, leaves only wreckage; the operator walks away unscathed.

## Significance

Bozzle is one of the earliest significant private detectives in English fiction — predating Sherlock Holmes by nearly two decades — and his portrayal is notably unsympathetic. Unlike the gentleman-detective who would later dominate the genre, Bozzle is a tradesman of suspicion, a figure whose very existence represents the degradation of human relationships into commodities. In a novel about the destructive power of jealousy, Bozzle is the instrument through which jealousy becomes operationalized — transformed from a private emotion into a public transaction.
