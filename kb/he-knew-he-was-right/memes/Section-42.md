## Section 42

[042-001]"Do you wish her to be taken away at once?" asked Hugh, almost in an

[042-002]angry tone.

[042-003]"Certainly not. That would be impossible. We have agreed to take her,

[042-004]and must bear with it. And I would not have her moved from this, if I

[042-005]thought that if she stayed awhile it might be arranged that she might

[042-006]return from us direct to her husband."

[042-007]"I shall try that, of course;--now."

[042-008]"But if he will not have her;--if he be so obstinate, so foolish, and

[042-009]so wicked, do not leave her here longer than you can help." Then Hugh

[042-010]explained that Sir Marmaduke and Lady Rowley were to be in England in

[042-011]the spring, and that it would be very desirable that the poor woman

[042-012]should not be sent abroad to look for a home before that. "If it must

[042-013]be so, it must," said Priscilla. "But eight months is a long time."

[042-014]Hugh went out to smoke his pipe on the church-wall in a moody,

[042-015]unhappy state of mind. He had hoped to have done so well in regard

[042-016]to Mrs. Trevelyan! Till he had met [[colonel-osborne|Colonel Osborne]], he felt sure,

[042-017]almost sure, that she would have refused to see that pernicious

[042-018]troubler of the peace of families. In this he found that he had been

[042-019]disappointed; but he had not expected that Priscilla would have been

[042-020]so much opposed to the arrangement which he had made about the house,

[042-021]and then he had been buoyed up by the anticipation of some delight in

[042-022]meeting [[nora-rowley|Nora Rowley]]. There was, at any rate, the excitement of seeing

[042-023]her to keep his spirits from flagging. He had seen her, and had had

[042-024]the opportunity of which he had so long been thinking. He had seen

[042-025]her, and had had every possible advantage on his side. What could any

[042-026]man desire better than the privilege of walking home with the girl

[042-027]he loved through country lanes of a summer evening? They had been an

[042-028]hour together,--or might have been, had he chosen to prolong the

[042-029]interview. But the words which had been spoken between them had had

[042-030]not the slightest interest,--unless it were that they had tended to

[042-031]make the interval between him and her wider than ever. He had asked

[042-032]her,--he thought that he had asked,--whether it would grieve her to

[042-033]abandon that delicate, dainty mode of life to which she had been

[042-034]accustomed; and she had replied, that she would never abandon it of

[042-035]her own accord. Of course she had intended him to take her at her

[042-036]word.

[042-037]He blew forth quick clouds of heavy smoke, as he attempted to make

[042-038]himself believe that this was all for the best. What would such a one

[042-039]as he was do with a wife? Or, seeing as he did see, that marriage

[042-040]itself was quite out of the question, how could it be good either for

[042-041]him or her that they should be tied together by a long engagement?

[042-042]Such a future would not at all suit the purpose of his life. In his

[042-043]life absolute freedom would be needed;--freedom from unnecessary

[042-044]ties, freedom from unnecessary burdens. His [[money|income]] was most

[042-045]precarious, and he certainly would not make it less so by submission

[042-046]to any closer literary thraldom. And he believed himself to be a

[042-047]Bohemian,--too much of a Bohemian to enjoy a domestic fireside with

[042-048]children and slippers. To be free to go where he liked, and when he

[042-049]liked; to think as he pleased; to be driven nowhere by conventional

[042-050]rules; to use his days, Sundays as well as Mondays, as he pleased

[042-051]to use them; to turn Republican, if his mind should take him that

[042-052]way,--or Quaker, or Mormon, or Red Indian, if he wished it, and in so

[042-053]turning to do no damage to any one but himself;--that was the life

[042-054]which he had planned for himself. His Aunt Stanbury had not read

[042-055]his [[honour|character]] altogether wrongly, as he thought, when she had once

[042-056]declared that decency and godliness were both distasteful to him.

[042-057]Would it not be destruction to such a one as he was, to fall into an

[042-058]interminable engagement with any girl, let her be ever so sweet?

[042-059]But yet, he felt as he sat there, filling pipe after pipe, smoking

[042-060]away till past midnight, that though he could not bear the idea of

[042-061]trammels, though he was totally unfit for [[marriage|matrimony]], either present

[042-062]or in prospect,--he felt that he had within his breast a double

[042-063]identity, and that that other division of himself would be utterly

[042-064]crushed if it were driven to divest itself of the idea of love.

[042-065]Whence was to come his poetry, the romance of his life, the springs

[042-066]of clear water in which his ignoble thoughts were to be dipped till

[042-067]they should become pure, if love was to be banished altogether from

[042-068]the list of delights that were possible to him? And then he began

[042-069]to speculate on love,--that love of which poets wrote, and of which

[042-070]he found that some sparkle was necessary to give light to his life.

[042-071]Was it not the one particle of divine breath given to man, of which

[042-072]he had heard since he was a boy? And how was this love to be come

[042-073]at, and was it to be a thing of reality, or merely an idea? Was

[042-074]it a pleasure to be attained, or a mystery that charmed by the

[042-075]difficulties of the distance,--a distance that never could be so

[042-076]passed that the thing should really be reached? Was love to be

[042-077]ever a delight, vague as is that feeling of unattainable beauty

[042-078]which far-off mountains give, when you know that you can never

[042-079]place yourself amidst their unseen valleys? And if love could be

[042-080]reached,--the love of which the poets sing, and of which his own

[042-081]heart was ever singing,--what were to be its pleasures? To press a

[042-082]hand, to kiss a lip, to clasp a waist, to hear even the low voice of

[042-083]the vanquished, confessing loved one as she hides her blushing cheek

[042-084]upon your shoulder,--what is it all but to have reached the once

[042-085]mysterious valley of your far-off mountain, and to have found that it

[042-086]is as other valleys,--rocks and stones, with a little grass, and a

[042-087]thin stream of running water? But beyond that pressure of the hand,

[042-088]and that kissing of the lips,--beyond that short-lived pressure of

[042-089]the plumage which is common to birds and men,--what could love do

[042-090]beyond that? There were children with dirty faces, and household

[042-091]bills, and a wife who must, perhaps, always darn the stockings,--and

[042-092]be sometimes cross. Was love to lead only to this,--a dull life, with

[042-093]a woman who had lost the beauty from her cheeks, and the gloss from

[042-094]her hair, and the music from her voice, and the fire from her eye,

[042-095]and the grace from her step, and whose waist an arm should no longer

[042-096]be able to span? Did the love of the poets lead to that, and that

[042-097]only? Then, through the cloud of smoke, there came upon him some

[042-098]dim idea of self-abnegation,--that the mysterious valley among

[042-099]the mountains, the far-off prospect of which was so charming to

[042-100]him,--which made the poetry of his life, was, in fact, the capacity

[042-101]of caring more for other human beings than for himself. The beauty of

[042-102]it all was not so much in the thing loved as in the loving. "Were she

[042-103]a cripple, hunchbacked, eyeless," he said to himself, "it might be

[042-104]the same. Only she must be a woman." Then he blew off a great cloud

[042-105]of smoke, and went into bed lost amidst poetry, philosophy, love, and

[042-106]tobacco.

[042-107]It had been arranged over-night that he was to start the next morning

[042-108]at half-past seven, and Priscilla had promised to give him his

[042-109]breakfast before he went. Priscilla, of course, kept her word. She

[042-110]was one of those women who would take a grim pleasure in coming

[042-111]down to make the tea at any possible hour,--at five, at four, if it

[042-112]were needed,--and who would never want to go to bed again when the

[042-113]ceremony was performed. But when Nora made her appearance,--Nora, who

[042-114]had been called dainty,--both Priscilla and Hugh were surprised. They

[042-115]could not say why she was there,--nor could Nora tell herself. She

[042-116]had not forgiven him. She had no thought of being gentle and loving

[042-117]to him. She declared to herself that she had no wish of saying

[042-118]good-bye to him once again. But yet she was in the room, waiting

[042-119]for him, when he came down to his breakfast. She had been unable to

[042-120]sleep, and had reasoned with herself as to the absurdity of lying in

[042-121]bed awake, when she preferred to be up and out of the house. It was

[042-122]true that she had not been out of her bed at seven any morning since

[042-123]she had been at [[nuncombe-putney|Nuncombe Putney]]; but that was no reason why she

[042-124]should not be more active on this special morning. There was a noise

[042-125]in the house, and she never could sleep when there was a noise. She

[042-126]was quite sure that she was not going down because she wished to see

[042-127][[hugh-stanbury|Hugh Stanbury]], but she was equally sure that it would be a disgrace

[042-128]to her to be deterred from going down, simply because the man was

[042-129]there. So she descended to the parlour, and was [[reputation|standing]] near the

[042-130]open window when Stanbury bustled into the room, some quarter of

[042-131]an hour after the proper time. Priscilla was there also, guessing

[042-132]something of the truth, and speculating whether these two young

[042-133]people, should they love each other, would be the better or the worse

[042-134]for such love. There must be marriages,--if only that the world

[042-135]might go on in accordance with the Creator's purpose. But, as far

[042-136]as Priscilla could see, blessed were they who were not called upon

[042-137]to assist in the scheme. To her eyes all days seemed to be days of

[042-138]wrath, and all times, times of tribulation. And it was all mere

[042-139]vanity and vexation of spirit. To go on and bear it till one

[042-140]was dead,--helping others to bear it, if such help might be of

[042-141]avail,--that was her theory of life. To make it pleasant by eating,

[042-142]and drinking, and dancing, or even by falling in love, was, to her

[042-143]mind, a vain crunching of ashes between the teeth. Not to have ill

[042-144]things said of her and of hers, not to be disgraced, not to be

[042-145]rendered incapable of some human effort, not to have actually to

[042-146]starve,--such was the extent of her ambition in this world. And for

[042-147]the next,--she felt so assured of the [[virtue|goodness]] of God that she could

[042-148]not bring herself to doubt of happiness in a world that was to be

[042-149]eternal. Her doubt was this, whether it was really the next world

[042-150]which would be eternal. Of eternity she did not doubt;--but might

[042-151]there not be many worlds? These things, however, she kept almost

[042-152]entirely to herself. "You down!" Priscilla had said.

[042-153]"Well, yes; I could not sleep when I heard you all moving. And the

[042-154]morning is so fine, and I thought that perhaps you would go out and

[042-155]walk after your brother has gone." Priscilla promised that she would

[042-156]walk, and then the tea was made.

[042-157]"Your sister and I are going out for an early walk," said Nora, when

[042-158]she was greeted by Stanbury. Priscilla said nothing, but thought she

[042-159]understood it all.

[042-160]"I wish I were going with you," said Hugh. Nora, remembering how very

[042-161]little he had made of his opportunity on the evening before, did not

[042-162]believe him.

[042-163]The eggs and fried bacon were eaten in a hurry, and very little was

[042-164]said. Then there came the moment for parting. The brother and sister

[042-165]kissed each other, and Hugh took Nora by the hand. "I hope you make

[042-166]yourself happy here," he said.
