## Section 58

[058-001]"Which girl's heart, Uncle Barty?"

[058-002]"The girl the man was to have married; when that's done she'll throw

[058-003]Gibson over. You'll see. She'll refuse to give the girl a shilling.

[058-004]She took the girl's brother by the hand ever so long, and then she

[058-005]threw him over. And she'll throw the girl over too, and send her back

[058-006]to the place she came from. And then she'll throw you over."

[058-007]"According to you, she must be the most malicious old woman that ever

[058-008]was allowed to live!"

[058-009]"I don't think there are many to beat her, as far as malice goes. But

[058-010]you'll find out for yourself. I shouldn't be surprised if she were to

[058-011]tell you before long that you were to marry the niece."

[058-012]"I shouldn't think that such very hard lines either," said Brooke

[058-013]Burgess.

[058-014]"I've no doubt you may have her if you like," said Barty, "in spite

[058-015]of Mr. Gibson. Only I should recommend you to take care and get the

[058-016]money first."

[058-017]When Brooke went back to the house in the Close, Miss Stanbury was

[058-018]quite fussy in her silence. She would have given much to have been

[058-019]told something about Barty, and, above all, to have learned what

[058-020]Barty had said about herself. But she was far too proud even to

[058-021]mention the old man's name of her own accord. She was quite sure that

[058-022]she had been abused. She guessed, probably with tolerable accuracy,

[058-023]the kind of things that had been said of her, and suggested to

[058-024]herself what answer Brooke would make to such accusations. But she

[058-025]had resolved to cloak it all in silence, and pretended for a while

[058-026]not to remember the young man's declared intention when he left the

[058-027]house. "It seems odd to me," said Brooke, "that Uncle Barty should

[058-028]always live alone as he does. He must have a dreary time of it."

[058-029]"I don't know anything about your Uncle Barty's manner of living."

[058-030]"No;--I suppose not. You and he are not friends."

[058-031]"By no means, Brooke."

[058-032]"He lives there all alone in that poky bank-house, and nobody ever

[058-033]goes near him. I wonder whether he has any friends in the city?"

[058-034]"I really cannot tell you anything about his friends. And, to tell

[058-035]you the truth, Brooke, I don't want to talk about your uncle. Of

[058-036]course, you can go to see him when you please, but I'd rather you

[058-037]didn't tell me of your visits afterwards."

[058-038]"There is nothing in the world I hate so much as a secret," said he.

[058-039]He had no intention in this of animadverting upon Miss Stanbury's

[058-040]secret enmity, nor had he purposed to ask any question as to her

[058-041]relations with the old man. He had alluded to his dislike of having

[058-042]secrets of his own. But she misunderstood him.

[058-043]"If you are anxious to know--" she said, becoming very red in the

[058-044]face.

[058-045]"I am not at all curious to know. You quite mistake me."

[058-046]"He has chosen to believe,--or to say that he believed,--that I

[058-047]wronged him in regard to his brother's will. I nursed his brother

[058-048]when he was dying,--as I considered it to be my duty to do. I cannot

[058-049]tell you all that story. It is too long, and too sad. Romance is very

[058-050]pretty in novels, but the romance of a life is always a melancholy

[058-051]matter. They are most happy who have no story to tell."

[058-052]"I quite believe that."

[058-053]"But your Uncle Barty chose to think,--indeed, I hardly know what

[058-054]he thought. He said that the will was a will of my making. When it

[058-055]was made I and his brother were apart; we were not even on speaking

[058-056]terms. There had been a quarrel, and all manner of folly. I am not

[058-057]very proud when I look back upon it. It is not that I think myself

[058-058]better than others; but your Uncle Brooke's will was made before we

[058-059]had come together again. When he was ill it was natural that I should

[058-060]go to him,--after all that had passed between us. Eh, Brooke?"

[058-061]"It was womanly."

[058-062]"But it made no difference about the will. Mr. Bartholomew Burgess

[058-063]might have known that at once, and must have known it afterwards. But

[058-064]he has never acknowledged that he was wrong;--never even yet."

[058-065]"He could not bring himself to do that, I should say."

[058-066]"The will was no great triumph to me. I could have done without it.

[058-067]As God is my judge, I would not have lifted up my little finger to

[058-068]get either a part or the whole of poor Brooke's money. If I had known

[058-069]that a word would have done it, I would have bitten my tongue out

[058-070]before it should have been spoken." She had risen from her seat, and

[058-071]was speaking with a solemnity that almost filled her listener with

[058-072]awe. She was a woman short of stature; but now, as she stood over

[058-073]him, she seemed to be tall and majestic. "But when the man was dead,"

[058-074]she continued, "and the will was there,--the [[money|property]] was mine,

[058-075]and I was bound in duty to exercise the privileges and bear the

[058-076]responsibilities which the dead man had conferred upon me. It was

[058-077]Barty, then, who sent a low attorney to me, offering me a compromise.

[058-078]What had I to compromise? Compromise! No. If it was not mine by all

[058-079]the right the law could give, I would sooner have starved than have

[058-080]had a crust of bread out of the money." She had now clenched both her

[058-081]fists, and was shaking them rapidly as she stood over him, looking

[058-082]down upon him.

[058-083]"Of course it was your own."

[058-084]"Yes. Though they asked me to compromise, and sent messages to me to

[058-085]frighten me;--both Barty and your Uncle Tom; ay, and your father too,

[058-086]Brooke; they did not dare to go to law. To law, indeed! If ever there

[058-087]was a good will in the world, the will of your Uncle Brooke was good.

[058-088]They could talk, and malign me, and tell lies as to dates, and strive

[058-089]to make my name odious in the county; but they knew that the will was

[058-090]good. They did not succeed very well in what they did attempt."

[058-091]"I would try to forget it all now, Aunt Stanbury."

[058-092]"Forget it! How is that to be done? How can the mind forget the

[058-093]history of its own life? No,--I cannot forget it. I can forgive it."

[058-094]"Then why not forgive it?"

[058-095]"I do. I have. Why else are you here?"

[058-096]"But forgive old Uncle Barty also!"

[058-097]"Has he forgiven me? Come now. If I wished to forgive him, how should

[058-098]I begin? Would he be gracious if I went to him? Does he love me,

[058-099]do you think,--or hate me? Uncle Barty is a good hater. It is the

[058-100]best point about him. No, Brooke, we won't try the farce of a

[058-101]reconciliation after a long life of enmity. Nobody would believe us,

[058-102]and we should not believe each other."

[058-103]"Then I certainly would not try."

[058-104]"I do not mean to do so. The truth is, Brooke, you shall have it

[058-105]all when I'm gone, if you don't turn against me. You won't take to

[058-106]writing for penny newspapers, will you, Brooke?" As she asked the

[058-107]question she put one of her hands softly on his shoulder.

[058-108]"I certainly shan't offend in that way."

[058-109]"And you won't be a Radical?"

[058-110]"No, not a Radical."

[058-111]"I mean a man to follow Beales and Bright, a republican, a

[058-112]putter-down of the Church, a hater of the Throne. You won't take up

[058-113]that line, will you, Brooke?"

[058-114]"It isn't my way at present, Aunt Stanbury. But a man shouldn't

[058-115]promise."

[058-116]"Ah me! It makes me sad when I think what the country is coming

[058-117]to. I'm told there are scores of members of Parliament who don't

[058-118]pronounce their h's. When I was young, a member of Parliament used to

[058-119]be a [[social-class|gentleman]];--and they've taken to ordaining all manner of people.

[058-120]It used to be the case that when you met a clergyman you met a

[058-121]gentleman. By-the-bye, Brooke, what do you think of Mr. Gibson?"

[058-122]"Mr. Gibson! To tell the truth, I haven't thought much about him."

[058-123]"But you must think about him. Perhaps you haven't thought about my

[058-124]niece, Dolly Stanbury?"

[058-125]"I think that she's an uncommonly nice girl."

[058-126]"She's not to be nice for you, young man. She's to be married to Mr.

[058-127]Gibson."

[058-128]"Are they engaged?"

[058-129]"Well, no; but I intend that they shall be. You won't begrudge that I

[058-130]should give my little savings to one of my own name?"

[058-131]"You don't know me, Aunt Stanbury, if you think that I should

[058-132]begrudge anything that you might do with your money."

[058-133]"Dolly has been here a month or two. I think it's three months since

[058-134]she came, and I do like her. She's soft and womanly, and hasn't taken

[058-135]up those vile, filthy habits which almost all the girls have adopted.

[058-136]Have you seen those Frenches with the things they have on their

[058-137]heads?"

[058-138]"I was speaking to them yesterday."

[058-139]"Nasty sluts! You can see the grease on their foreheads when they try

[058-140]to make their hair go back in the dirty French fashion. Dolly is not

[058-141]like that;--is she?"

[058-142]"She is not in the least like either of the Miss Frenches."

[058-143]"And now I want her to become Mrs. Gibson. He is quite taken."

[058-144]"Is he?"

[058-145]"Oh dear, yes. Didn't you see him the other night at dinner and

[058-146]afterwards? Of course he knows that I can give her a little bit of

[058-147]money, which always goes for something, Brooke. And I do think it

[058-148]would be such a nice thing for Dolly."

[058-149]"And what does Dolly think about it?"

[058-150]"There's the difficulty. She likes him well enough; I'm sure of that.

[058-151]And she has no stuck-up ideas about herself. She isn't one of those

[058-152]who think that almost nothing is good enough for them. But--"

[058-153]"She has an objection."

[058-154]"I don't know what it is. I sometimes think she is so bashful and

[058-155]modest she doesn't like to talk of being married,--even to an old

[058-156]woman like me."

[058-157]"Dear me! That's not the way of the age;--is it, Aunt Stanbury?"

[058-158]"It's coming to that, Brooke, that the girls will ask the men soon.

[058-159]Yes,--and that they won't take a refusal either. I do believe that

[058-160]Camilla French did ask Mr. Gibson."

[058-161]"And what did Mr. Gibson say?"

[058-162]"Ah;--I can't tell you that. He knows too well what he's about to

[058-163]take her. He's to come here on Friday at eleven, and you must be out

[058-164]of the way. I shall be out of the way too. But if Dolly says a word

[058-165]to you before that, mind you make her understand that she ought to

[058-166]accept Gibson."

[058-167]"She's too good for him, according to my thinking."

[058-168]"Don't you be a fool. How can any young woman be too good for

[058-169]a gentleman and a clergyman? Mr. Gibson is a gentleman. Do you

[058-170]know,--only you must not mention this,--that I have a kind of idea

[058-171]that we could get [[nuncombe-putney|Nuncombe Putney]] for him. My father had the living,

[058-172]and my brother; and I should like it to go on in the family."

[058-173]No opportunity came in the way of Brooke Burgess to say anything in

[058-174]favour of Mr. Gibson to Dorothy Stanbury. There did come to be very

[058-175]quickly a sort of intimacy between her and her aunt's favourite; but

[058-176]she was one not prone to talk about her own affairs. And as to such

[058-177]an affair as this,--a question as to whether she should or should not

[058-178]give herself in marriage to her suitor,--she, who could not speak

[058-179]of it even to her own sister without a blush, who felt confused

[058-180]and almost confounded when receiving her aunt's admonitions and

[058-181]instigations on the subject, would not have endured to hear Brooke

[058-182]Burgess speak on the matter. Dorothy did feel that a person easier to

[058-183]know than Brooke had never come in her way. She had already said as

[058-184]much to him as she had spoken to Mr. Gibson in the three months that

[058-185]she had made his acquaintance. They had talked about [[exeter|Exeter]], and

[058-186]about Mrs. MacHugh, and the cathedral, and Tennyson's poems, and the

[058-187][[london|London]] theatres, and Uncle Barty, and the family quarrel. They had

[058-188]become quite confidential with each other on some matters. But on

[058-189]this heavy subject of Mr. Gibson and his proposal of marriage not

[058-190]a word had been said. When Brooke once mentioned Mr. Gibson on the

[058-191]Thursday morning, Dorothy within a minute had taken an opportunity of

[058-192]escaping from the room.
