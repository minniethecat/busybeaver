[110-001]"Yes, ma'am."

[110-002]"You can say that you know her old aunt wouldn't,--wouldn't refuse

[110-003]to have her back again. You can put it your own way, you know. You

[110-005]"But she won't, ma'am."

[110-006]"Won't what?"

[110-007]"Won't say anything about returning."

[110-008]"Yes, she will, Martha, if you talk to her rightly." The servant

[110-009]didn't reply for awhile, but stood looking out of the window. "You

[110-010]might as well go about the lamb at once, Martha."

[110-011]"So I will, ma'am, when I've got it out, all clear."

[110-012]"What do you mean by that?"

[110-013]"Why,--just this, ma'am. May I tell Miss Dolly straight out that you

[110-014]want her to come back, and that I've been sent to say so?"

[110-015]"No, Martha."

[110-016]"Then how am I to do it, ma'am?"

[110-017]"Do it out of your own head, just as it comes up at the moment."

[110-018]"Out of my own head, ma'am?"

[110-019]"Yes;--just as you feel, you know."

[110-020]"Just as I feel, ma'am?"

[110-021]"You understand what I mean, Martha."

[110-022]"I'll do my best, ma'am, and I can't say no more. And if you scolds

[110-023]me afterwards, ma'am,--why, of course, I must put up with it."

[110-024]"But I won't scold you, Martha."

[110-025]"Then I'll go out to Winslow's about the lamb at once, ma'am."

[110-026]"Very nice, and not too small, Martha."

[110-027]Martha went out and ordered the lamb, and packed it as desired quite

[110-028]clean in a napkin, and fitted it into the basket, and arranged with

[110-029]Giles Hickbody to carry it down for her early in the morning to the

[110-030][[social-class|station]], so that she might take the first train to Lessborough. It

[110-031]was understood that she was to hire a fly at Lessborough to take her

[110-032]to [[nuncombe-putney|Nuncombe Putney]]. Now that she understood the importance of her

[110-033]mission and was aware that the present she took with her was only

[110-034]the customary accompaniment of an ambassadress entrusted with a

[110-035]great mission, Martha said nothing even about the expense. The train

[110-036]started for Lessborough at seven, and as she was descending from her

[110-037]room at six, Miss Stanbury, in her flannel dressing-gown, stepped

[110-038]out of the door of her own room. "Just put this in the basket," said

[110-039]she, handing a note to her servant. "I thought last night I'd write a

[110-040]word. Just put it in the basket and say nothing about it." The note

[110-041]which she sent was as follows:--

[110-042]The Close, 8th April, 186--.

[110-043]MY DEAR DOROTHY,--

[110-044]As Martha talks of going over to pay you a visit, I've

[110-045]thought that I'd just get her to take you a quarter of

[110-046]lamb, which is coming in now very nice. I do envy her

[110-047]going to see you, my dear, for I had gotten somehow to

[110-048]love to see your pretty face. I'm getting almost strong

[110-049]again; but Sir Peter, who was here this afternoon, just

[110-050]calling as a friend, was uncivil enough to say that I'm

[110-051]too much of an old woman to go out in the east wind. I

[110-052]told him it didn't much matter;--for the sooner old women

[110-053]made way for young ones, the better.

[110-054]I am very desolate and solitary here. But I rather think

[110-055]that women who don't get married are intended to be

[110-056]desolate; and perhaps it is better for them, if they

[110-057]bestow their time and thoughts properly,--as I hope you

[110-058]do, my dear. A woman with a family of children has almost

[110-059]too many of the cares of this world, to give her mind as

[110-060]she ought to the other. What shall we say then of those

[110-061]who have no such cares, and yet do not walk uprightly?

[110-062]Dear Dorothy, be not such a one. For myself, I acknowledge

[110-063]bitterly the extent of my shortcomings. Much has been

[110-064]given to me; but if much be expected, how shall I answer

[110-065]the demand?

[110-066]I hope I need not tell you that whenever it may suit you

[110-067]to pay a visit to [[exeter|Exeter]], your room will be ready for you,

[110-068]and there will be a warm welcome. Mrs. MacHugh always asks

[110-069]after you; and so has Mrs. Clifford. I won't tell you

[110-070]what Mrs. Clifford said about your colour, because it

[110-071]would make you vain. The Heavitree affair has all been put

[110-072]off;--of course you have heard that. Dear, dear, dear! You

[110-073]know what I think, so I need not repeat it.

[110-074]Give my respects to your mamma and Priscilla,--and for

[110-075]yourself, accept the affectionate love of

[110-076]Your loving old aunt,

[110-077]JEMIMA STANBURY.

[110-078]P.S.--If Martha should say anything to you, you may feel

[110-079]sure that she knows my mind.

[110-080]Poor old soul. She felt an almost uncontrollable longing to have her

[110-081]niece back again, and yet she told herself that she was bound not

[110-082]to send a regular invitation, or to suggest an unconditional return.

[110-083]Dorothy had herself decided to take her departure, and if she chose

[110-084]to remain away,--so it must be. She, Miss Stanbury, could not demean

[110-085]herself by renewing her invitation. She read her letter before she

[110-086]added to it the postscript, and felt that it was too solemn in its

[110-087]tone to suggest to Dorothy that which she wished to suggest. She had

[110-088]been thinking much of her own past life when she wrote those words

[110-089]about the state of an unmarried woman, and was vacillating between

[110-090]two minds,--whether it were better for a young woman to look forward

[110-091]to the cares and affections, and perhaps hard usage, of a married

[110-092]life; or to devote herself to the easier and safer course of an

[110-093]old maid's career. But an old maid is nothing if she be not kind

[110-094]and good. She acknowledged that, and, acknowledging it, added the

[110-095]postscript to her letter. What though there was a certain blow

[110-096]to her pride in the writing of it! She did tell herself that in

[110-097]thus referring her niece to Martha for an expression of her own

[110-098]mind,--after that conversation which she and Martha had had in the

[110-099]parlour,--she was in truth eating her own words. But the postscript

[110-100]was written, and though she took the letter up with her to her own

[110-101]room in order that she might alter the words if she repented of them

[110-102]in the night, the letter was sent as it was written,--postscript and

[110-103]all.

[110-104]She spent the next day with very sober thoughts. When Mrs. MacHugh

[110-105]called upon her and told her that there were rumours afloat in

[110-106]Exeter that the marriage between Camilla French and Mr. Gibson would

[110-107]certainly be broken off, in spite of all purchases that had been

[110-108]made, she merely remarked that they were two poor, feckless things,

[110-109]who didn't know their own minds. "Camilla knows hers plain enough,"

[110-110]said Mrs. MacHugh sharply; but even this did not give Miss Stanbury

[110-111]any spirit. She waited, and waited patiently, till Martha should

[110-112]return, thinking of the sweet pink colour which used to come and

[110-113]go in Dorothy's cheeks,--which she had been wont to observe so

[110-114]frequently, not knowing that she had observed it and loved it.

[110-115]CHAPTER LXVII.

[110-116]RIVER'S COTTAGE.

[110-117]Three days after [[hugh-stanbury|Hugh Stanbury]]'s visit to Manchester Street, he wrote

[110-118]a note to Lady Rowley, telling her of the address at which might be

[110-119]found both Trevelyan and his son. As Bozzle had acknowledged, facts

[110-120]are things which may be found out. Hugh had gone to work somewhat

[110-121]after the Bozzlian fashion, and had found out this fact. "He lives at

[110-122]a place called River's Cottage, at Willesden," wrote Stanbury. "If

[110-123]you turn off the Harrow Road to the right, about a mile beyond the

[110-124]cemetery, you will find the cottage on the left hand side of the lane

[110-125]about a quarter of a mile from the Harrow Road. I believe you can go

[110-126]to Willesden by railway, but you had better take a cab from [[london|London]]."

[110-127]There was much consultation respecting this letter between Lady

[110-128]Rowley and Mrs. Trevelyan, and it was decided that it should not

[110-129]be shown to Sir Marmaduke. To see her child was at the present

[110-130]moment the most urgent necessity of the poor mother, and both the

[110-131]ladies felt that Sir Marmaduke in his wrath might probably impede

[110-132]rather than assist her in this desire. If told where he might find

[110-133]Trevelyan, he would probably insist on starting in quest of his

[110-134]son-in-law himself, and the distance between the mother and her child

[110-135]might become greater in consequence, instead of less. There were many

[110-136]consultations; and the upshot of these was, that Lady Rowley and her

[110-137]daughter determined to start for Willesden without saying anything to

[110-138]Sir Marmaduke of the purpose they had in hand. When Emily expressed

[110-139]her conviction that if Trevelyan should be away from home they would

[110-140]probably be able to make their way into the house,--so as to see the

[110-141]child, Lady Rowley with some hesitation acknowledged that such might

[110-142]be the case. But the child's mother said nothing to her own mother of

[110-143]a scheme which she had half formed of so clinging to her boy that no

[110-144]human power should separate them.

[110-145]They started in a cab, as advised by Stanbury, and were driven to a

[110-146]point on the road from which a lane led down to Willesden, passing by

[110-147]River's Cottage. They asked as they came along, and met no difficulty

[110-148]in finding their way. At the point on the road indicated, there was

[110-149]a country inn for hay-waggoners, and here Lady Rowley proposed that

[110-150]they should leave their cab, urging that it might be best to call at

[110-151]the cottage in the quietest manner possible; but Mrs. Trevelyan, with

[110-152]her scheme in her head for the recapture of their child, begged that

[110-153]the cab might go on;--and thus they were driven up to the door.

[110-154]River's Cottage was not a prepossessing abode. It was a new building,

[110-155]of light-coloured bricks, with a door in the middle and one window

[110-156]on each side. Over the door was a stone tablet, bearing the

[110-157]name,--River's Cottage. There was a little garden between the

[110-158]road and the house, across which there was a straight path to the

[110-159]door. In front of one window was a small shrub, generally called

[110-160]a puzzle-monkey, and in front of the other was a variegated laurel.

[110-161]There were two small morsels of green turf, and a distant view round

[110-162]the corner of the house of a row of cabbage stumps. If Trevelyan were

[110-163]living there, he had certainly come down in the world since the days

[110-164]in which he had occupied the house in Curzon Street. The two ladies

[110-165]got out of the cab, and slowly walked across the little garden. Mrs.

[110-166]Trevelyan was dressed in black, and she wore a thick veil. She had

[110-167]altogether been unable to make up her mind as to what should be her

[110-168]conduct to her husband should she see him. That must be governed by

[110-169]circumstances as they might occur. Her visit was made not to him, but

[110-170]to her boy.

[110-171]The door was opened before they knocked, and Trevelyan himself was

[110-172][[reputation|standing]] in the narrow passage. Lady Rowley was the first to speak.

[110-173]"Louis," she said, "I have brought your wife to see you."

[110-174]"Who told you that I was here?" he asked, still [[reputation|standing]] in the

[110-175]passage.

[110-176]"Of course a mother would find out where was her child," said Lady

[110-177]Rowley.

[110-178]"You should not have come here without notice," he said. "I was

[110-179]careful to let you know the conditions on which you should come."

[110-180]"You do not mean that I shall not see my child," said the mother.

[110-181]"Oh, Louis, you will let me see him."

[110-182]Trevelyan hesitated a moment, still keeping his position firmly in

[110-183]the doorway. By this time an old woman, decently dressed and of

[110-184]comfortable appearance, had taken her place behind him, and behind

[110-185]her was a slip of a girl about fifteen years of age. This was the

[110-186]owner of River's Cottage and her daughter, and all the inhabitants

[110-187]of the cottage were now there, standing in the passage. "I ought not

[110-188]to let you see him," said Trevelyan; "you have intruded upon me in

[110-189]coming here! I had not wished to see you here,--till you had complied

[110-190]with the order I had given you." What a meeting between a husband and

[110-191]a wife who had not seen each other now for many months,--between a

[110-192]husband and a wife who were still young enough not to have outlived

[110-193]the first impulses of their early love! He still stood there guarding

[110-194]the way, and had not even put out his hand to greet her. He was

[110-195]guarding the way lest she should, without his permission, obtain

[110-196]access to her own child! She had not removed her veil, and now she

[110-197]hardly dared to step over the threshold of her husband's house. At

[110-198]this moment, she perceived that the woman behind was pointing to

[110-199]the room on the left, as the cottage was entered, and Emily at once

[110-200]understood that her boy was there. Then at that moment she heard her

[110-201]son's voice, as, in his solitude, the child began to cry. "I must go

[110-202]in," she said; "I will go in;" and rushing on she tried to push aside

[110-203]her husband. Her mother aided her, nor did Trevelyan attempt to stop

[110-204]her with violence, and in a moment she was kneeling at the foot of a

[110-205]small sofa, with her child in her arms. "I had not intended to hinder

[110-206]you," said Trevelyan, "but I require from you a promise that you will

[110-207]not attempt to remove him."
