## Section 31

[031-001]Yours truly,

[031-002]PRISCILLA STANBURY.

[031-003]P.S.--Another [[social-class|gentleman]] did call here,--not to see Mrs.

[031-004]Trevelyan; but I suppose mamma's house need not be closed

[031-005]against all visitors.

[031-006]Poor Dorothy had passed evil hours from the moment in which her

[031-007]aunt had so far certified herself as to [[colonel-osborne|Colonel Osborne]]'s visit to

[031-008]Nuncombe as to make her feel it to be incumbent on her to interfere.

[031-009]After much consideration Miss Stanbury had told her niece the

[031-010]dreadful news, and had told also what she intended to do. Dorothy,

[031-011]who was in truth horrified at the iniquity of the fact which was

[031-012]related, and who never dreamed of doubting the truth of her aunt's

[031-013]information, hardly knew how to interpose. "I am sure mamma won't let

[031-014]there be anything wrong," she had said.

[031-015]"And you don't call this wrong?" said Miss Stanbury, in a tone of

[031-016]indignation.

[031-017]"But perhaps mamma will tell them to go."

[031-018]"I hope she will. I hope she has. But he was allowed to be there

[031-019]for hours. And now three days have passed and there is no sign of

[031-020]anything being done. He came and went and may come again when he

[031-021]pleases." Still Dorothy pleaded. "I shall do my duty," said Miss

[031-022]Stanbury.

[031-023]"I am quite sure mamma will do nothing wrong," said Dorothy. But the

[031-024]letter was written and sent, and the answer to the letter reached the

[031-025]house in the Close in due time.

[031-026]When Miss Stanbury had read and re-read the very short reply which

[031-027]her niece had written, she became at first pale with dismay, and

[031-028]then red with renewed vigour and obstinacy. She had made herself, as

[031-029]she thought, quite certain of her facts before she had acted on her

[031-030]information. There was some equivocation, some most unworthy deceit

[031-031]in Priscilla's letter. Or could it be possible that she herself had

[031-032]been mistaken? Another gentleman had been there;--not, however, with

[031-033]the object of seeing Mrs. Trevelyan! So said Priscilla. But she had

[031-034]made herself sure that the man in question was a man from [[london|London]],

[031-035]a middle-aged man from London, who had specially asked for Mrs.

[031-036]Trevelyan, and who had at once been known to Mrs. Clegg, at the

[031-037]Lessboro' inn, to be Mrs. Trevelyan's lover. Miss Stanbury was

[031-038]very unhappy, and at last sent for Giles Hickbody. Giles Hickbody

[031-039]had never pretended to know the name. He had seen the man and had

[031-040]described him, "Quite a swell, ma'am; and a Lon'oner, and one as'd

[031-041]be up to anything; but not a young 'un; no, not just a young 'un,

[031-042]zartainly." He was cross-examined again now, and said that all he

[031-043]knew about the man's name was that there was a handle to it. This was

[031-044]ended by Miss Stanbury sending him down to Lessboro' to learn the

[031-045]very name of the gentleman, and by his coming back with that of the

[031-046]Honourable George Glascock written on a piece of paper. "They says

[031-047]now as he was arter the other young 'ooman," said Giles Hickbody.

[031-048]Then was the confusion of Miss Stanbury complete.

[031-049]It was late when Giles returned from Lessboro', and nothing could

[031-050]be done that night. It was too late to write a letter for the

[031-051]next morning's post. Miss Stanbury, who was as proud of her own

[031-052]discrimination as she was just and true, felt that a day of

[031-053]humiliation had indeed come for her. She hated Priscilla almost as

[031-054]vigorously as Priscilla hated her. To Priscilla she would not write

[031-055]to own her fault; but it was incumbent on her to confess it to Mrs.

[031-056]Stanbury. It was incumbent on her also to confess it to Dorothy. All

[031-057]that night she did not sleep, and the next morning she went about

[031-058]abashed, wretched, hardly mistress of her own maids. She must confess

[031-059]it also to Martha, and Martha would be very stern to her. Martha had

[031-060]pooh-poohed the whole story of the lover, seeming to think that there

[031-061]could be no reasonable objection to a lover past fifty.

[031-062]"Dorothy," she said at last, about noon, "I have been over

[031-063]hasty about your mother and this man. I am sorry for it, and

[031-064]must--beg--everybody's--pardon."

[031-065]"I knew mamma would do nothing wrong," said Dorothy.

[031-066]"To do wrong is human, and she, I suppose, is not more free than

[031-067]others; but in this matter I was misinformed. I shall write and beg

[031-068]her pardon; and now I beg your pardon."

[031-069]"Not mine, Aunt Stanbury."

[031-070]"Yes, yours and your mother's, and the lady's also,--for against her

[031-071]has the fault been most grievous. I shall write to your mother and

[031-072]express my contrition." She put off the evil hour of writing as long

[031-073]as she could, but before dinner the painful letter had been written,

[031-074]and carried by herself to the post. It was as follows:--

[031-075]The Close, August 3, 186--.

[031-076]DEAR SISTER STANBURY,

[031-077]I have now learned that the information was false on which

[031-078]my former letter was based. I am heartily sorry for any

[031-079]annoyance I may have given you. I can only inform you

[031-080]that my intentions were good and upright. Nevertheless, I

[031-081]humbly beg your pardon.

[031-082]Yours truly,

[031-083]JEMIMA STANBURY.

[031-084]Mrs. Stanbury, when she received this, was inclined to let the matter

[031-085]drop. That her sister-in-law should express such abject contrition

[031-086]was to her such a lowering of the great ones of the earth, that the

[031-087]apology conveyed to her more pain than pleasure. She could not hinder

[031-088]herself from sympathising with all that her sister-in-law had felt

[031-089]when she had found herself called upon to humiliate herself. But

[031-090]it was not so with Priscilla. Mrs. Stanbury did not observe that

[031-091]her daughter's name was scrupulously avoided in the apology; but

[031-092]Priscilla observed it. She would not let the matter drop, without

[031-093]an attempt at the last word. She therefore wrote back again as

[031-094]follows;--

[031-095][[nuncombe-putney|Nuncombe Putney]], August 4, 186--.

[031-096]DEAR AUNT STANBURY,

[031-097]I am glad you have satisfied yourself about the gentleman

[031-098]who has so much disquieted you. I do not know that the

[031-099]whole affair would be worth a moment's consideration, were

[031-100]it not that mamma and I, living as we do so secluded a

[031-101]life, are peculiarly apt to feel any attack upon our good

[031-102]name,--which is pretty nearly all that is left to us. If

[031-103]ever there were women who should be free from attack,

[031-104]at any rate from those of their own family, we are such

[031-105]women. We never interfere with you, or with anybody; and I

[031-106]think you might abstain from harassing us by accusations.

[031-107]Pray do not write to mamma in such a strain again, unless

[031-108]you are quite sure of your ground.

[031-109]Yours truly,

[031-110]PRISCILLA STANBURY.

[031-111]"Impudent!" said Miss Stanbury to Martha, when she had read the

[031-112]letter. "Ill-conditioned, impudent vixen!"

[031-113]"She was provoked, miss," said Martha.

[031-114]"Well; yes; yes;--and I suppose it is right that you should tell me

[031-115]of it. I dare say it is part of what I ought to bear for being an old

[031-116]fool, and too cautious about my own flesh and blood. I will bear it.

[031-117]There. I was wrong, and I will say that I have been justly punished.

[031-118]There,--there!"

[031-119]How very much would Miss Stanbury's tone have been changed had

[031-120]she known that at that very moment Colonel Osborne was eating his

[031-121]breakfast at Mrs. Crocket's inn, in Nuncombe Putney!

[031-122]CHAPTER XIX.

[031-123]BOZZLE, THE EX-POLICEMAN.

[031-124][Illustration]

[031-125]When Mr. Trevelyan had gone through the miserable task of breaking up

[031-126]his establishment in Curzon Street, and had seen all his furniture

[031-127]packed, including his books, his pictures, and his pet Italian

[031-128]ornaments, it was necessary that he should go and live somewhere. He

[031-129]was very wretched at this time,--so wretched that life was a burden

[031-130]to him. He was a man who loved his wife;--to whom his child was very

[031-131]dear; and he was one too to whom the ordinary comforts of domestic

[031-132]life were attractive and necessary. There are men to whom release

[031-133]from the constraint imposed by family ties will be, at any rate for

[031-134]a time, felt as a release. But he was not such a man. There was no

[031-135]delight to him in being able to dine at his club, and being free to

[031-136]go whither he pleased in the evening. As it was, it pleased him to

[031-137]go no whither in the evenings; and his mornings were equally blank

[031-138]to him. He went so often to Mr. Bideawhile, that the poor old lawyer

[031-139]became quite tired of the Trevelyan family quarrel. Even Lady

[031-140]Milborough, with all her power of sympathising, began to feel that

[031-141]she would almost prefer on any morning that her dear young friend,

[031-142][[louis-trevelyan|Louis Trevelyan]], should not be announced. Nevertheless, she always

[031-143]saw him when he came, and administered comfort according to her

[031-144]light. Of course he would have his wife back before long. That was

[031-145]the only consolation she was able to offer; and she offered it so

[031-146]often that he began gradually to feel that something might be done

[031-147]towards bringing about so desirable an event. After what had occurred

[031-148]they could not live again in Curzon Street,--nor even in London for

[031-149]awhile; but Naples was open to them. Lady Milborough said so much to

[031-150]him of the advantages which always came in such circumstances from

[031-151]going to Naples, that he began to regard such a trip as almost the

[031-152]natural conclusion of his adventure. But then there came that very

[031-153]difficult question;--what step should be first taken? Lady Milborough

[031-154]proposed that he should go boldly down to Nuncombe Putney, and make

[031-155]the arrangement. "She will only be too glad to jump into your arms,"

[031-156]said Lady Milborough. Trevelyan thought that if he went to Nuncombe

[031-157]Putney, his wife might perhaps jump into his arms; but what would

[031-158]come after that? How would he stand then in reference to his

[031-159]authority? Would she own that she had been wrong? Would she promise

[031-160]to behave better in future? He did not believe that she was yet

[031-161]sufficiently broken in spirit to make any such promise. And he told

[031-162]himself again and again that it would be absurd in him to allow her

[031-163]to return to him without such subjection, after all that he had gone

[031-164]through in defence of his marital rights. If he were to write to her

[031-165]a long letter, argumentative, affectionate, exhaustive, it might be

[031-166]better. He was inclined to believe of himself that he was good at

[031-167]writing long, affectionate, argumentative, and exhaustive letters.

[031-168]But he would not do even this as yet. He had broken up his house, and

[031-169]scattered all his domestic gods to the winds, because she had behaved

[031-170]badly to him; and the thing done was too important to allow of

[031-171]redress being found so easily.

[031-172]So he lived on a wretched life in London. He could hardly endure to

[031-173]show himself at his club, fearing that every one would be talking of

[031-174]him as the man who was separated from his wife,--perhaps as the man

[031-175]of whose wife Colonel Osborne was the dear friend. No doubt for a day

[031-176]or two there had been much of such conversation; but it had died away

[031-177]from the club long before his consciousness had become callous. At

[031-178]first he had gone into a lodging in Mayfair; but this had been but

[031-179]for a day or two. After that he had taken a set of furnished chambers

[031-180]in Lincoln's Inn, immediately under those in which Stanbury lived;

[031-181]and thus it came to pass that he and Stanbury were very much thrown

[031-182]together. As Trevelyan would always talk of his wife this was rather

[031-183]a bore; but our friend bore with it, and would even continue to

[031-184]instruct the world through the columns of the D. R. while Trevelyan

[031-185]was descanting on the peculiar cruelty of his own position.

[031-186]"I wish to be just, and even generous; and I do love her with all my

[031-187]heart," he said one afternoon, when Hugh was very hard at work.
