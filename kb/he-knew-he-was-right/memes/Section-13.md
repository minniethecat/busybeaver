## Section 13

[013-001]She believed in [[exeter|Exeter]], thinking that there was no other provincial

[013-002]town in England in which a maiden lady could live safely and

[013-003]decently. [[london|London]] to her was an abode of sin; and though, as we have

[013-004]seen, she delighted to call herself one of the county set, she did

[013-005]not love the fields and lanes. And in Exeter the only place for a

[013-006]lady was the Close. Southernhay and Northernhay might be very well,

[013-007]and there was doubtless a respectable neighbourhood on the Heavitree

[013-008]side of the town; but for the new streets, and especially for the

[013-009]suburban villas, she had no endurance. She liked to deal at dear

[013-010]shops; but would leave any shop, either dear or cheap, in regard to

[013-011]which a printed advertisement should reach her eye. She paid all her

[013-012]bills at the end of each six months, and almost took a delight in

[013-013]high prices. She would rejoice that bread should be cheap, and grieve

[013-014]that meat should be dear, because of the poor; but in regard to other

[013-015]matters no reduction in the cost of an article ever pleased her.

[013-016]She had houses as to which she was told by her agent that the rents

[013-017]should be raised; but she would not raise them. She had others which

[013-018]it was difficult to let without lowering the rents, but she would not

[013-019]lower them. All change was to her hateful and unnecessary.

[013-020]She kept three maid-servants, and a man came in every day to clean

[013-021]the knives and boots. Service with her was well requited, and much

[013-022]labour was never exacted. But it was not every young woman who could

[013-023]live with her. A rigidity as to hours, as to religious exercises,

[013-024]and as to dress, was exacted, under which many poor girls altogether

[013-025]broke down; but they who could stand this rigidity came to know that

[013-026]their places were very valuable. No one belonging to them need want

[013-027]for aught, when once the good opinion of Miss Stanbury had been

[013-028]earned. When once she believed in her servant there was nobody like

[013-029]that servant. There was not a man in Exeter could clean a boot except

[013-030]Giles Hickbody,--and if not in Exeter, then where else? And her own

[013-031]maid Martha, who had lived with her now for twenty years, and who had

[013-032]come with her to the brick house when she first inhabited it, was

[013-033]such a woman that no other servant anywhere was fit to hold a candle

[013-034]to her. But then Martha had great gifts,--was never ill, and really

[013-035]liked having sermons read to her.

[013-036]Such was Miss Stanbury, who had now discarded her nephew Hugh. She

[013-037]had never been tenderly affectionate to Hugh, or she would hardly

[013-038]have asked him to live in London on a hundred a year. She had never

[013-039]really been kind to him since he was a boy, for although she had paid

[013-040]for him, she had been almost penurious in her manner of doing so,

[013-041]and had repeatedly given him to understand, that in the event of her

[013-042]death not a shilling would be left to him. Indeed, as to that matter

[013-043]of bequeathing her money, it was understood that it was her purpose

[013-044]to let it all go back to the Burgess family. With the Burgess family

[013-045]she had kept up no sustained connection, it being quite understood

[013-046]that she was never to be asked to meet the only one of them now left

[013-047]in Exeter. Nor was it as yet known to any one in what manner the

[013-048]money was to go back, how it was to be divided, or who were to be the

[013-049]recipients. But she had declared that it should go back, explaining

[013-050]that she had conceived it to be a duty to let her own relations know

[013-051]that they would not inherit her [[money|wealth]] at her death.

[013-052][[About]] a week after she had sent back poor Hugh's letter with the

[013-053]endorsement on it as to unworthy bread, she summoned Martha to the

[013-054]back parlour in which she was accustomed to write her letters. It was

[013-055]one of the theories of her life that different rooms should be used

[013-056]only for the purposes for which they were intended. She never allowed

[013-057]pens and ink up into the bed-rooms, and had she ever heard that any

[013-058]guest in her house was reading in bed, she would have made an instant

[013-059]personal attack upon that guest, whether male or female, which would

[013-060]have surprised that guest. Poor Hugh would have got on better with

[013-061]her had he not been discovered once smoking in the garden. Nor would

[013-062]she have writing materials in the drawing-room or dining-room. There

[013-063]was a chamber behind the dining-room in which there was an inkbottle,

[013-064]and if there was a letter to be written, let the writer go there

[013-065]and write it. In the writing of many letters, however, she put no

[013-066]confidence, and regarded penny postage as one of the strongest

[013-067]evidences of the coming ruin.

[013-068]"Martha," she said, "I want to speak to you. Sit down. I think I am

[013-069]going to do something." Martha sat down, but did not speak a word.

[013-070]There had been no question asked of her, and the time for speaking

[013-071]had not come. "I am writing to Mrs. Stanbury, at [[nuncombe-putney|Nuncombe Putney]]; and

[013-072]what do you think I am saying to her?"

[013-073]Now the question had been asked, and it was Martha's duty to reply.

[013-074]"Writing to Mrs. Stanbury, ma'am?"

[013-075]"Yes, to Mrs. Stanbury."

[013-076]"It ain't possible for me to say, ma'am, unless it's to put Mr. Hugh

[013-077]from going on with the newspapers."

[013-078]"When my nephew won't be controlled by me, I shan't go elsewhere

[013-079]to look for control over him; you may be sure of that, Martha. And

[013-080]remember, Martha, I don't want to have his name mentioned again in

[013-081]the house. You will tell them all so, if you please."

[013-082]"He was a very nice [[social-class|gentleman]], ma'am."

[013-083]"Martha, I won't have it; and there's an end of it. I won't have it.

[013-084]Perhaps I know what goes to the making of a nice gentleman as well as

[013-085]you do."

[013-086]"Mr. Hugh, ma'am,--"

[013-087]"I won't have it, Martha. And when I say so, let there be an end

[013-088]of it." As she said this, she got up from her chair, and shook her

[013-089]head, and took a turn about the room. "If I'm not mistress here, I'm

[013-090]nobody."

[013-091]"Of course you're mistress here, ma'am."

[013-092]"And if I don't know what's fit to be done, and what's not fit, I'm

[013-093]too old to learn; and, what's more, I won't be taught. I'm not going

[013-094]to have my house crammed with radical incendiary stuff, printed with

[013-095]ink that stinks, on paper made out of straw. If I can't live without

[013-096]penny literature, at any rate I'll die without it. Now listen to me."

[013-097]"Yes, ma'am."

[013-098]"I have asked Mrs. Stanbury to send one of the girls over here."

[013-099]"To live, ma'am?" Martha's tone as she asked the question, showed how

[013-100]deeply she felt its importance.

[013-101]"Yes, Martha; to live."

[013-102]"You'll never like it, ma'am."

[013-103]"I don't suppose I shall."

[013-104]"You'll never get on with it, ma'am; never. The young lady'll be out

[013-105]of the house in a week; or if she ain't, somebody else will."

[013-106]"You mean yourself."

[013-107]"I'm only a servant, ma'am, and it don't signify about me."

[013-108]"You're a fool."

[013-109]"That's true, ma'am, I don't doubt."

[013-110]"I've sent for her, and we must do the best we can. Perhaps she won't

[013-111]come."

[013-112]"She'll come fast enough," said Martha. "But whether she'll stay,

[013-113]that's a different thing. I don't see how it's possible she's to

[013-114]stay. I'm told they're feckless, idle young ladies. She'll be so

[013-115]soft, ma'am, and you,--"

[013-116]"Well; what of me?"

[013-117]"You'll be so hard, ma'am!"

[013-118]"I'm not a bit harder than you, Martha; nor yet so hard. I'll do my

[013-119]duty, or at least I'll try. Now you know all about it, and you may go

[013-120]away. There's the letter, and I mean to go out and post it myself."

[013-121]CHAPTER VIII.

[013-122]"I KNOW IT WILL DO."

[013-123]Miss Stanbury carried her letter all the way to the chief post-office

[013-124]in the city, having no faith whatever in those little subsidiary

[013-125]receiving houses which are established in different parts of the

[013-126]city. As for the iron pillar boxes which had been erected of late

[013-127]years for the receipt of letters, one of which,--a most hateful thing

[013-128]to her,--stood almost close to her own hall door, she had not the

[013-129]faintest belief that any letter put into one of them would ever reach

[013-130]its destination. She could not understand why people should not walk

[013-131]with their letters to a respectable post-office instead of chucking

[013-132]them into an iron stump,--as she called it,--out in the middle of the

[013-133]street with nobody to look after it. Positive orders had been given

[013-134]that no letter from her house should ever be put into the iron post.

[013-135]Her epistle to her sister-in-law, of whom she never spoke otherwise

[013-136]than as Mrs. Stanbury, was as follows:--

[013-137]The Close, Exeter, 22nd April, 186--.

[013-138]MY DEAR SISTER STANBURY,

[013-139]Your son, Hugh, has taken to courses of which I do not

[013-140]approve, and therefore I have put an end to my connection

[013-141]with him. I shall be happy to entertain your daughter

[013-142]Dorothy in my house if you and she approve of such a plan.

[013-143]Should you agree to this, she will be welcome to receive

[013-144]you or her sister,--_not her brother_,--in my house any

[013-145]Wednesday morning between half-past nine and half-past

[013-146]twelve. I will endeavour to make my house pleasant to her

[013-147]and useful, and will make her an allowance of L25 per

[013-148]annum for her clothes as long as she may remain with me. I

[013-149]shall expect her to be regular at meals, to be constant in

[013-150]going to church, and not to read modern novels.

[013-151]I intend the arrangement to be permanent, but of course I

[013-152]must retain the power of closing it if, and when, I shall

[013-153]see fit. Its permanence must be contingent on my life. I

[013-154]have no power of providing for any one _after my death_.

[013-155]Yours truly,

[013-156]JEMIMA STANBURY.

[013-157]I hope the young lady does not have any false hair about

[013-158]her.

[013-159]When this note was received at Nuncombe Putney the amazement which it

[013-160]occasioned was extreme. Mrs. Stanbury, the widow of the late vicar,

[013-161]lived in a little morsel of a cottage on the outskirts of the

[013-162]village, with her two daughters, Priscilla and Dorothy. Their whole

[013-163]income, out of which it was necessary that they should pay rent for

[013-164]their cottage, was less than L70 per annum. During the last few

[013-165]months a five-pound note now and again had found its way to Nuncombe

[013-166]Putney out of the coffers of the "D. R.;" but the ladies there were

[013-167]most unwilling to be so relieved, thinking that their brother's

[013-168]career was of infinitely more importance than their comforts or even

[013-169]than their living. They were very poor, but they were accustomed

[013-170]to poverty. The elder sister was older than Hugh, but Dorothy, the

[013-171]younger, to whom this strange invitation was now made, was two years

[013-172]younger than her brother, and was now nearly twenty-six. How they had

[013-173]lived, and dressed themselves, and had continued to be called ladies

[013-174]by the inhabitants of the village was, and is, and will be a mystery

[013-175]to those who have had the spending of much larger incomes, but have

[013-176]still been always poor. But they had lived, had gone to church every

[013-177]Sunday in decent apparel, and had kept up friendly relations with the

[013-178]family of the present vicar, and with one or two other neighbours.

[013-179]When the letter had been read first by the mother, and then aloud,

[013-180]and then by each of them separately, in the little sitting-room in

[013-181]the cottage, there was silence among them,--for neither of them

[013-182]desired to be the first to express an opinion. Nothing could be more

[013-183]natural than the proposed arrangement, had it not been made unnatural

[013-184]by a quarrel existing nearly throughout the whole life of the person

[013-185]most nearly concerned. Priscilla, the elder daughter, was the one of

[013-186]the family who was generally the ruler, and she at last expressed an

[013-187]opinion adverse to the arrangement. "My dear, you would never be able

[013-188]to bear it," said Priscilla.
