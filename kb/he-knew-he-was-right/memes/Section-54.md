## Section 54

[054-001]"Yes;--he has been told. She did not write to her mother till after

[054-002]she had got to [[nuncombe-putney|Nuncombe Putney]]. She did not like to speak about her

[054-003]troubles while there was a hope that things might be made smooth."

[054-004]"You can't blame her for that, my dear."

[054-005]"But there was a month lost, or nearly. Letters go only once a month.

[054-006]And now they can't hear from Marmaduke or Bessy,"--Lady Rowley's name

[054-007]was Bessy,--"till the beginning of September."

[054-008]"That will be in a fortnight."

[054-009]"But what can my brother say to them? He will suppose that they are

[054-010]still down in Devonshire."

[054-011]"You don't think he will come at once?"

[054-012]"How can he, my dear? He can't come without leave, and the expense

[054-013]would be ruinous. They would stop his pay, and there would be all

[054-014]manner of evils. He is to come in the spring, and they must stay

[054-015]here till he comes." The parson of St. Diddulph's sighed and groaned.

[054-016]Would it not have been almost better that he should have put his

[054-017]pride in his pocket, and have consented to take Mr. Trevelyan's

[054-018]money?

[054-019]On the second morning [[hugh-stanbury|Hugh Stanbury]] called at the parsonage, and was

[054-020]closeted for a while with the parson. Nora had heard his voice in the

[054-021]passage, and every one in the house knew who it was that was talking

[054-022]to Mr. Outhouse, in the little back parlour that was called a study.

[054-023]Nora was full of anxiety. Would he ask to see them,--to see her? And

[054-024]why was he there so long? "No doubt he has brought a message from Mr.

[054-025]Trevelyan," said her sister. "I dare say he will send word that I

[054-026]ought not to have come to my uncle's house." Then, at last, both Mr.

[054-027]Outhouse and Hugh Stanbury came into the room in which they were all

[054-028]sitting. The greetings were cold and unsatisfactory, and Nora barely

[054-029]allowed Hugh to touch the tip of her fingers. She was very angry with

[054-030]him, and yet she knew that her anger was altogether unreasonable.

[054-031]That he had caused her to refuse a marriage that had so much to

[054-032]attract her was not his sin;--not that; but that, having thus

[054-033]overpowered her by his influence, he should then have stopped. And

[054-034]yet Nora had told herself twenty times that it was quite impossible

[054-035]that she should become Hugh Stanbury's wife;--and that, were Hugh

[054-036]Stanbury to ask her, it would become her to be indignant with him,

[054-037]for daring to make a proposition so outrageous. And now she was sick

[054-038]at heart, because he did not speak to her!

[054-039]He had, of course, come to St. Diddulph's with a message from

[054-040]Trevelyan, and his secret was soon told to them all. Trevelyan

[054-041]himself was up-stairs in the sanded parlour of the Full Moon

[054-042]public-house, round the corner. Mrs. Trevelyan, when she heard this,

[054-043]clasped her hands and bit her lips. What was he there for? If he

[054-044]wanted to see her, why did he not come boldly to the parsonage? But

[054-045]it soon appeared that he had no desire to see his wife. "I am to take

[054-046]Louey to him," said Hugh Stanbury, "if you will allow me."

[054-047]"What;--to be taken away from me!" exclaimed the mother. But Hugh

[054-048]assured her that no such idea had been formed; that he would have

[054-049]concerned himself in no such stratagem, and that he would himself

[054-050]undertake to bring the boy back again within an hour. Emily was, of

[054-051]course, anxious to be informed what other message was to be conveyed

[054-052]to her; but there was no other message--no message either of love or

[054-053]of instruction.

[054-054]"Mr. Stanbury," said the parson, "has left something in my hands for

[054-055]you." This "something" was given over to her as soon as Stanbury

[054-056]had left the house, and consisted of cheques for various small sums,

[054-057]amounting in all to L200. "And he hasn't said what I am to do with

[054-058]it?" Emily asked of her uncle. Mr. Outhouse declared that the cheques

[054-059]had been given to him without any instructions on that head. Mr.

[054-060]Trevelyan had simply expressed his satisfaction that his wife should

[054-061]be with her uncle and aunt, had sent the money, and had desired to

[054-062]see the child.

[054-063]The boy was got ready, and Hugh walked with him in his arms round the

[054-064]corner, to the Full Moon. He had to pass by the bar, and the barmaid

[054-065]and the potboy looked at him very hard. "There's a young 'ooman has

[054-066]to do with that ere little game," said the potboy. "And it's two to

[054-067]one the young 'ooman has the worst of it," said the barmaid. "They

[054-068]mostly does," said the potboy, not without some feeling of pride in

[054-069]the immunities of his sex. "Here he is," said Hugh, as he entered

[054-070]the parlour. "My boy, there's papa." The child at this time was more

[054-071]than a year old, and could crawl about and use his own legs with the

[054-072]assistance of a finger to his little hand, and could utter a sound

[054-073]which the fond mother interpreted to mean papa; for with all her hot

[054-074]anger against her husband, the mother was above all things anxious

[054-075]that her child should be taught to love his father's name. She

[054-076]would talk of her separation from her husband as though it must be

[054-077]permanent; she would declare to her sister how impossible it was that

[054-078]they should ever again live together; she would repeat to herself

[054-079]over and over the tale of the injustice that had been done to her,

[054-080]assuring herself that it was out of the question that she should ever

[054-081]pardon the man; but yet, at the bottom of her heart, there was a hope

[054-082]that the quarrel should be healed before her boy would be old enough

[054-083]to understand the nature of quarrelling. Trevelyan took the child on

[054-084]to his knee, and kissed him; but the poor little fellow, startled by

[054-085]his transference from one male set of arms to another, confused by

[054-086]the strangeness of the room, and by the absence of things familiar

[054-087]to his sight, burst out into loud tears. He had stood the journey

[054-088]round the corner in Hugh's arms manfully, and, though he had looked

[054-089]about him with very serious eyes, as he passed through the bar,

[054-090]he had borne that, and his carriage up the stairs; but when he

[054-091]was transferred to his father, whose air, as he took the boy, was

[054-092]melancholy and lugubrious in the extreme, the poor little fellow

[054-093]could endure no longer a mode of treatment so unusual, and, with a

[054-094]grimace which for a moment or two threatened the coming storm, burst

[054-095]out with an infantine howl. "That's how he has been taught," said

[054-096]Trevelyan.

[054-097][Illustration: The "Full Moon" at St. Diddulph's.]

[054-098]"Nonsense," said Stanbury. "He's not been taught at all. It's

[054-099]Nature."

[054-100]"Nature that he should be afraid of his own father! He did not cry

[054-101]when he was with you."

[054-102]"No;--as it happened, he did not. I played with him when I was at

[054-103]Nuncombe; but, of course, one can't tell when a child will cry, and

[054-104]when it won't."

[054-105]"My darling, my dearest, my own son!" said Trevelyan, caressing the

[054-106]child, and trying to comfort him; but the poor little fellow only

[054-107]cried the louder. It was now nearly two months since he had seen his

[054-108]father, and, when age is counted by months only, almost everything

[054-109]may be forgotten in six weeks. "I suppose you must take him back

[054-110]again," said Trevelyan, sadly.

[054-111]"Of course I must take him back again. Come along, Louey, my boy."

[054-112]"It is cruel;--very cruel," said Trevelyan. "No man living could love

[054-113]his child better than I love mine;--or, for the matter of that, his

[054-114]wife. It is very cruel."

[054-115]"The remedy is in your own hands, Trevelyan," said Stanbury, as he

[054-116]marched off with the boy in his arms.

[054-117]Trevelyan had now become so accustomed to being told by everybody

[054-118]that he was wrong, and was at the same time so convinced that he was

[054-119]right, that he regarded the perversity of his friends as a part of

[054-120]the persecution to which he was subjected. Even Lady Milborough,

[054-121]who objected to [[colonel-osborne|Colonel Osborne]] quite as strongly as did Trevelyan

[054-122]himself, even she blamed him now, telling him that he had done

[054-123]wrong to separate himself from his wife. Mr. Bideawhile, the old

[054-124]family lawyer, was of the same opinion. Trevelyan had spoken to Mr.

[054-125]Bideawhile as to the expediency of making some lasting arrangement

[054-126]for a permanent maintenance for his wife; but the attorney had told

[054-127]him that nothing of the kind could be held to be lasting. It was

[054-128]clearly the husband's duty to look forward to a reconciliation, and

[054-129]Mr. Bideawhile became quite severe in the tone of rebuke which he

[054-130]assumed. Stanbury treated him almost as though he were a madman. And

[054-131]as for his wife herself--when she wrote to him she would not even

[054-132]pretend to express any feeling of affection. And yet, as he thought,

[054-133]no man had ever done more for a wife. When Stanbury had gone with the

[054-134]child, he sat waiting for him in the parlour of the public-house, as

[054-135]miserable a man as one could find. He had promised himself something

[054-136]that should be akin to pleasure in seeing his boy;--but it had been

[054-137]all disappointment and pain. What was it that they expected him to

[054-138]do? What was it that they desired? His wife had behaved with such

[054-139]indiscretion as almost to have compromised his honour; and in return

[054-140]for that he was to beg her pardon, confess himself to have done

[054-141]wrong, and allow her to return in triumph! That was the light in

[054-142]which he regarded his own position; but he promised to himself that

[054-143]let his own misery be what it might he would never so degrade him.

[054-144]The only person who had been true to him was Bozzle. Let them all

[054-145]look to it. If there were any further intercourse between his wife

[054-146]and Colonel Osborne, he would take the matter into open court, and

[054-147]put her away publicly, let Mr. Bideawhile say what he might. Bozzle

[054-148]should see to that;--and as to himself, he would take himself out of

[054-149]England and hide himself abroad. Bozzle should know his address, but

[054-150]he would give it to no one else. Nothing on earth should make him

[054-151]yield to a woman who had ill-treated him,--nothing but confession

[054-152]and promise of amendment on her part. If she would acknowledge and

[054-153]promise, then he would forgive all, and the events of the last four

[054-154]months should never again be mentioned by him. So resolving he sat

[054-155]and waited till Stanbury should return to him.

[054-156]When Stanbury got back to the parsonage with the boy he had nothing

[054-157]to do but to take his leave. He would fain have asked permission to

[054-158]come again, could he have invented any reason for doing so. But the

[054-159]child was taken from him at once by its mother, and he was left alone

[054-160]with Mr. Outhouse. [[nora-rowley|Nora Rowley]] did not even show herself, and he

[054-161]hardly knew how to express sympathy and friendship for the guests at

[054-162]the parsonage, without seeming to be untrue to his friend Trevelyan.

[054-163]"I hope all this may come to an end soon," he said.

[054-164]"I hope it may, Mr. Stanbury," said the clergyman; "but to tell you

[054-165]the truth, it seems to me that Mr. Trevelyan is so unreasonable a

[054-166]man, so much like a madman indeed, that I hardly know how to look

[054-167]forward to any future happiness for my niece." This was spoken with

[054-168]the utmost severity that Mr. Outhouse could assume.

[054-169]"And yet no man loves his wife more tenderly."

[054-170]"Tender love should show itself by tender conduct, Mr. Stanbury. What

[054-171]has he done to his wife? He has blackened her name among all his

[054-172]friends and hers, he has turned her out of his house, he has reviled

[054-173]her,--and then thinks to prove how good he is by sending her money.

[054-174]The only possible excuse is that he must be mad."
