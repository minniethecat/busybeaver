## Section 154

[154-001]On the next day Stanbury went out to Casalunga alone. He had

[154-002]calculated, on leaving England, that if any good might be done

[154-003]at Siena it could be done in three days, and that he would have

[154-004]been able to start on his return on the Wednesday morning,--or on

[154-005]Wednesday evening at the latest. But now there did not seem to be any

[154-006]chance of that;--and he hardly knew how to guess when he might get

[154-007]away. He had sent a telegram to Lady Rowley after his first visit,

[154-008]in which he had simply said that things were not at all changed at

[154-009]Casalunga, and he had written to Nora each day since his arrival. His

[154-010]stay was prolonged at great expense and inconvenience to himself;

[154-011]and yet it was impossible that he should go and leave his work half

[154-012]finished. As he walked up the hill to the house he felt very angry

[154-013]with Trevelyan, and prepared himself to use hard words and dreadful

[154-014]threats. But at the very moment of his entrance on the terrace,

[154-015]Trevelyan professed himself ready to go to England. "That's right,

[154-016]old fellow," said Hugh. "I am so glad." But in expressing his joy he

[154-017]had hardly noticed Trevelyan's voice and appearance.

[154-018]"I might as well go," he said. "It matters little where I am, or

[154-019]whether they say that I am mad or sane."

[154-020]"When we have you over there, nobody shall say a word that is

[154-021]disagreeable."

[154-022]"I only hope that you may not have the trouble of burying me on the

[154-023]road. You don't know, Stanbury, how ill I am. I cannot eat. If I

[154-024]were at the bottom of that hill, I could no more walk up it than I

[154-025]could fly. I cannot sleep, and at night my bed is wet through with

[154-026]perspiration. I can remember nothing,--nothing but what I ought to

[154-027]forget."

[154-028]"We'll put you on to your legs again when we get you to your own

[154-029]climate."

[154-030]"I shall be a poor traveller,--a poor traveller; but I will do my

[154-031]best."

[154-032]When would he start? That was the next question. Trevelyan asked for

[154-033]a week, and Stanbury brought him down at last to three days. They

[154-034]would go to Florence by the evening train on Friday, and sleep there.

[154-035]Emily should come out and assist him to arrange his things on the

[154-036]morrow. Having finished so much of his business, Stanbury returned to

[154-037]Siena.

[154-038]They both feared that he might be found on the next day to have

[154-039]departed from his intention; but no such idea seemed to have occurred

[154-040]to him. He gave instructions as to the notice to be served on the

[154-041]agent from the Hospital as to his house, and allowed Emily to go

[154-042]among his things and make preparations for the journey. He did not

[154-043]say much to her; and when she attempted, with a soft half-uttered

[154-044]word, to assure him that the threat of Italian interference, which

[154-045]had come from Stanbury, had not reached Stanbury from her, he simply

[154-046]shook his head sadly. She could not understand whether he did not

[154-047]believe her, or whether he simply wished that the subject should be

[154-048]dropped. She could elicit no sign of affection from him, nor would he

[154-049]willingly accept such from her;--but he allowed her to prepare for

[154-050]the journey, and never hinted that his purpose might again be liable

[154-051]to change. On the Friday, Emily with her child, and Hugh with all

[154-052]their baggage, travelled out on the road to Casalunga, thinking it

[154-053]better that there should be no halt in the town on their return.

[154-054]At Casalunga, Hugh went up the hill with the driver, leaving Mrs.

[154-055]Trevelyan in the carriage. He had been out at the house before in the

[154-056]morning, and had given all necessary orders;--but still at the last

[154-057]moment he thought that there might be failure. But Trevelyan was

[154-058]ready, having dressed himself up with a laced shirt, and changed his

[154-059]dressing-gown for a blue frock-coat, and his brocaded cap for a Paris

[154-060]hat, very pointed before and behind, and closely turned up at the

[154-061]sides. But Stanbury did not in the least care for his friend's dress.

[154-062]"Take my arm," he said, "and we will go down, fair and easy. Emily

[154-063]would not come up because of the heat." He suffered himself to be

[154-064]led, or almost carried down the hill; and three women, and the

[154-065]coachman, and an old countryman who worked on the farm, followed with

[154-066]the luggage. It took about an hour and a half to pack the things; but

[154-067]at last they were all packed, and corded, and bound together with

[154-068]sticks, as though it were intended that they should travel in that

[154-069]form to Moscow. Trevelyan the meanwhile sat on a chair which had been

[154-070]brought out for him from one of the cottages, and his wife stood

[154-071]beside him with her boy. "Now then we are ready," said Stanbury. And

[154-072]in that way they bade farewell to Casalunga. Trevelyan sat speechless

[154-073]in the carriage, and would not even notice the child. He seemed to be

[154-074]half dreaming and to fix his eyes on vacancy. "He appears to think of

[154-075]nothing now," Emily said that evening to Stanbury. But who can tell

[154-076]how busy and how troubled are the thoughts of a madman!

[154-077]They had now succeeded in their object of inducing their patient to

[154-078]return with them to England; but what were they to do with him when

[154-079]they had reached home with him? They rested only a night at Florence;

[154-080]but they found their fellow-traveller so weary, that they were unable

[154-081]to get beyond Bologna on the second day. Many questions were asked

[154-082]of him as to where he himself would wish to take up his residence in

[154-083]England; but it was found almost impossible to get an answer. Once

[154-084]he suggested that he would like to go back to Mrs. Fuller's cottage

[154-085]at Willesden, from whence they concluded that he would wish to live

[154-086]somewhere out of [[london|London]]. On his first day's journey, he was moody

[154-087]and silent,--wilfully assuming the airs of a much-injured person. He

[154-088]spoke hardly at all, and would notice nothing that was said to him by

[154-089]his wife. He declared once that he regarded Stanbury as his keeper,

[154-090]and endeavoured to be disagreeable and sullenly combative; but on

[154-091]the second day, he was too weak for this, and accepted, without

[154-092]remonstrance, the attentions that were paid to him. At Bologna they

[154-093]rested a day, and from thence both Stanbury and Mrs. Trevelyan wrote

[154-094]to Nora. They did not know where she might be now staying, but the

[154-095]letters, by agreement, were addressed to Gregg's Hotel. It was

[154-096]suggested that lodgings, or, if possible, a small furnished house,

[154-097]should be taken in the neighbourhood of Mortlake, Richmond, or

[154-098]Teddington, and that a telegram as well as a letter should be sent to

[154-099]them at the Paris hotel. As they could not travel quick, there might

[154-100]be time enough for them in this way to know whither they should go on

[154-101]their reaching London.

[154-102]They stayed a day at Bologna, and then they went on again,--to Turin,

[154-103]over the mountains to Chambery, thence to Dijon, and on to Paris. At

[154-104]Chambery they remained a couple of days, fancying that the air there

[154-105]was cool, and that the delay would be salutary to the sick man. At

[154-106]Turin, finding that they wanted further assistance, they had hired

[154-107]a courier, and at last Trevelyan allowed himself to be carried in

[154-108]and out of the carriages and up and down the hotel stairs almost as

[154-109]though he were a child. The delay was terribly grievous to Stanbury,

[154-110]and Mrs. Trevelyan, perceiving this more than once, begged him to

[154-111]leave them, and to allow her to finish the journey with the aid

[154-112]of the courier. But this he could not do. He wrote letters to

[154-113]his friends at the D. R. office, explaining his position as well

[154-114]as he could, and suggesting that this and that able assistant

[154-115]should enlighten the British people on this and that subject,

[154-116]which would,--in the course of nature, as arranged at the D. R.

[154-117]office,--have fallen into his hands. He and Mrs. Trevelyan became

[154-118]as brother and sister to each other on their way home,--as, indeed,

[154-119]it was natural that they should do. Were they doing right or wrong

[154-120]in this journey that they were taking? They could not conceal from

[154-121]themselves that the labour was almost more than the poor wretch could

[154-122]endure; and that it might be, as he himself had suggested, that they

[154-123]would be called on to bury him on the road. But that residence at

[154-124]Casalunga had been so terrible,--the circumstances of it, including

[154-125]the solitude, sickness, madness, and habits of life of the wretched

[154-126]hermit, had been so dangerous,--the probability of interference on

[154-127]the part of some native authority so great, and the chance of the

[154-128]house being left in Trevelyan's possession so small, that it had

[154-129]seemed to him that they had no other alternative; and yet, how would

[154-130]it be if they were killing him by the toil of travelling? From

[154-131]Chambery, they made the journey to Paris in two days, and during that

[154-132]time Trevelyan hardly opened his mouth. He slept much, and ate better

[154-133]than he had done in the hotter climate on the other side of the Alps.

[154-134]They found a telegram at Paris, which simply contained the promise

[154-135]of a letter for the next day. It had been sent by Nora, before she

[154-136]had gone out on her search. But it contained one morsel of strange

[154-137]information; "Lady Milborough is going with me." On the next day

[154-138]they got a letter, saying that a cottage had been taken, furnished,

[154-139]between Richmond and Twickenham. Lady Milborough had known of the

[154-140]cottage, and everything would be ready then. Nora would herself meet

[154-141]them at the [[social-class|station]] in London, if they would, as she proposed, stay

[154-142]a night at Dover. They were to address to her at Lady Milborough's

[154-143]house, in Eccleston Square. In that case, she would have a carriage

[154-144]for them at the Victoria Station, and would go down with them at once

[154-145]to the cottage.

[154-146]There were to be two days more of weary travelling, and then they

[154-147]were to be at home again. She and he would have a house together as

[154-148]husband and wife, and the curse of their separation would, at any

[154-149]rate, be over. Her mind towards him had changed altogether since

[154-150]the days in which she had been so indignant, because he had set a

[154-151]policeman to watch over her. All feeling of anger was over with her

[154-152]now. There is nothing that a woman will not forgive a man, when he is

[154-153]weaker than she is herself.

[154-154]The journey was made first to Dover, and then to London. Once, as

[154-155]they were making their way through the Kentish hop-fields, he put

[154-156]out his hand feebly, and touched hers. They had the carriage to

[154-157]themselves, and she was down on her knees before him instantly. "Oh,

[154-158]Louis! Oh, Louis! say that you forgive me!" What could a woman do

[154-159]more than that in her mercy to a man?

[154-160]"Yes;--yes; yes," he said; "but do not talk now; I am so tired."

[154-161]CHAPTER XCIV.

[154-162]A REAL CHRISTIAN.

[154-163]In the meantime the Rowleys were gone. On the Monday after the

[154-164]departure of Stanbury for Italy, Lady Rowley had begun to look the

[154-165]difficulty about Nora in the face, and to feel that she must do

[154-166]something towards providing the poor girl with a temporary home.

[154-167]Everybody had now agreed that she was to marry [[hugh-stanbury|Hugh Stanbury]] as soon

[154-168]as Hugh Stanbury could be ready, and it was not to be thought of that

[154-169]she should be left out in the world as one in disgrace or under a

[154-170]cloud. But what was to be done? Sir Marmaduke was quite incapable of

[154-171]suggesting anything. He would make her an allowance, and leave her a

[154-172]small sum of ready money;--but as to residence, he could only suggest

[154-173]again and again that she should be sent to Mrs. Outhouse. Now Lady

[154-174]Rowley was herself not very fond of Mrs. Outhouse, and she was aware

[154-175]that Nora herself was almost as averse to St. Diddulph's as she was

[154-176]to the Mandarins. Nora already knew that she had the game in her

[154-177]own hands. Once when in her presence her father suggested the near

[154-178]relationship and prudent [[honour|character]] and intense respectability of Mrs.

[154-179]Outhouse, Nora, who was sitting behind Sir Marmaduke, shook her head

[154-180]at her mother, and Lady Rowley knew that Nora would not go to St.

[154-181]Diddulph's. This was the last occasion on which that proposition was

[154-182]discussed.
