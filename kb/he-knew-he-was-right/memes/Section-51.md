## Section 51

[051-001]At this time Brooke Burgess was a man just turned thirty, and was

[051-002]a clerk in the Ecclesiastical Record Office, in Somerset House. No

[051-003]doubt the peculiar nature and name of the public department to which

[051-004]he was attached had done something to recommend him to Miss Stanbury.

[051-005]Ecclesiastical records were things greatly to be reverenced in her

[051-006]eyes, and she felt that a [[social-class|gentleman]] who handled them and dealt with

[051-007]them would probably be sedate, gentlemanlike, and conservative.

[051-008]Brooke Burgess, when she had last seen him, was just about to enter

[051-009]upon the duties of the office. Then there had come offence, and she

[051-010]had in truth known nothing of him from that day to this. The visitor

[051-011]was to be at [[exeter|Exeter]] on the following Monday, and very much was done

[051-012]in preparation of his coming. There was to be a dinner party on that

[051-013]very day, and dinner parties were not common with Miss Stanbury. She

[051-014]had, however, explained to Martha that she intended to put her best

[051-015]foot forward. Martha understood perfectly that Mr. Brooke Burgess

[051-016]was to be received as the heir of [[money|property]]. Sir Peter Mancrudy, the

[051-017]great Devonshire chemist, was coming to dinner, and Mr. and Mrs.

[051-018]Powel from Haldon,--people of great distinction in that part of the

[051-019]county,--Mrs. MacHugh of course; and, equally of course, Mr. Gibson.

[051-020]There was a deep discussion between Miss Stanbury and Martha

[051-021]as to asking two of the Cliffords, and Mr. and Mrs. Noel from

[051-022]Doddiscombeleigh. Martha had been very much in favour of having

[051-023]twelve. Miss Stanbury had declared that with twelve she must have two

[051-024]waiters from the greengrocer's, and that two waiters would overpower

[051-025]her own domesticities below stairs. Martha had declared that she

[051-026]didn't care about them any more than if they were puppy dogs. But

[051-027]Miss Stanbury had been quite firm against twelve. She had consented

[051-028]to have ten,--for the sake of artistic arrangement at the table;

[051-029]"They should be pantaloons and petticoats alternate, you know," she

[051-030]had said to Martha,--and had therefore asked the Cliffords. But the

[051-031]Cliffords could not come, and then she had declined to make any

[051-032]further attempt. Indeed, a new idea had struck her. Brooke Burgess,

[051-033]her guest, should sit at one end of the table, and Mr. Gibson, the

[051-034]clergyman, at the other. In this way the proper alternation would be

[051-035]effected. When Martha heard this, Martha quite understood the extent

[051-036]of the good fortune that was in store for Dorothy. If Mr. Gibson was

[051-037]to be welcomed in that way, it could only be in preparation of his

[051-038]becoming one of the family.

[051-039]And Dorothy herself became aware that she must make up her mind. It

[051-040]was not so declared to her, but she came to understand that it was

[051-041]very probable that something would occur on the coming Monday which

[051-042]would require her to be ready with her answer on that day. And she

[051-043]was greatly tormented by feeling that if she could not bring herself

[051-044]to accept Mr. Gibson,--should Mr. Gibson propose to her, as to which

[051-045]she continued to tell herself that the chance of such a thing must

[051-046]be very remote indeed,--but that if he should propose to her, and if

[051-047]she could not accept him, her aunt ought to know that it would be so

[051-048]before the moment came. But yet she could not bring herself to speak

[051-049]to her aunt as though any such proposition were possible.

[051-050]It happened that during the week, on the Saturday, Priscilla came

[051-051]into Exeter. Dorothy met her sister at the railway station, and then

[051-052]the two walked together two miles and back along the Crediton Road.

[051-053]Aunt Stanbury had consented to Priscilla coming to the Close, even

[051-054]though it was not the day appointed for such visits; but the walk

[051-055]had been preferred, and Dorothy felt that she would be able to ask

[051-056]for counsel from the only human being to whom she could have brought

[051-057]herself to confide the fact that a gentleman was expected to ask her

[051-058]to marry him. But it was not till they had turned upon their walk,

[051-059]that she was able to open her mouth on the subject even to her

[051-060]sister. Priscilla had been very full of their own cares at Nuncombe,

[051-061]and had said much of her determination to leave the [[clock-house|Clock House]] and

[051-062]to return to the retirement of some small cottage. She had already

[051-063]written to Hugh to this effect, and during their walk had said much

[051-064]of her own folly in having consented to so great a change in their

[051-065]mode of life. At last Dorothy struck in with her story.

[051-066]"Aunt Stanbury wants me to make a change too."

[051-067]"What change?" asked Priscilla anxiously.

[051-068]"It is not my idea, Priscilla, and I don't think that there can be

[051-069]anything in it. Indeed, I'm sure there isn't. I don't see how it's

[051-070]possible that there should be."

[051-071]"But what is it, Dolly?"

[051-072]"I suppose there can't be any harm in my telling you."

[051-073]"If it's anything concerning yourself, I should say not. If it

[051-074]concerns Aunt Stanbury, I dare say she'd rather you held your

[051-075]tongue."

[051-076]"It concerns me most," said Dorothy.

[051-077]"She doesn't want you to leave her, does she?"

[051-078]"Well; yes; no. By what she said last,--I shouldn't leave her at all

[051-079]in that way. Only I'm sure it's not possible."

[051-080]"I am the worst hand in the world, Dolly, at guessing a riddle."

[051-081]"You've heard of that Mr. Gibson, the clergyman;--haven't you?"

[051-082]"Of course I have."

[051-083]"Well--. Mind, you know, it's only what Aunt Stanbury says. He has

[051-084]never so much as opened his lips to me himself, except to say, 'How

[051-085]do you do?' and that kind of thing."

[051-086]"Aunt Stanbury wants you to marry him?"

[051-087]"Yes!"

[051-088]"Well?"

[051-089]"Of course it's out of the question," said Dorothy, sadly.

[051-090]"I don't see why it should be out of the question," said Priscilla

[051-091]proudly. "Indeed, if Aunt Stanbury has said much about it, I should

[051-092]say that Mr. Gibson himself must have spoken to her."

[051-093]"Do you think he has?"

[051-094]"I do not believe that my aunt would raise false hopes," said

[051-095]Priscilla.

[051-096]"But I haven't any hopes. That is to say, I had never thought about

[051-097]such a thing."

[051-098]"But you think about it now, Dolly?"

[051-099]"I should never have dreamed about it, only for Aunt Stanbury."

[051-100]"But, dearest, you are dreaming of it now, are you not?"

[051-101]"Only because she says that it is to be so. You don't know how

[051-102]generous she is. She says that if it should be so, she will give me

[051-103]ever so much money;--two thousand pounds!"

[051-104]"Then I am quite sure that she and Mr. Gibson must understand each

[051-105]other."

[051-106]"Of course," said Dorothy, sadly, "if he were to think of such a

[051-107]thing at all, it would only be because the money would be

[051-108]convenient."

[051-109]"Not at all," said Priscilla, sternly,--with a sternness that was

[051-110]very comfortable to her listener. "Not at all. Why should not Mr.

[051-111]Gibson love you as well as any man ever loved any woman? You are

[051-112]nice-looking,"--Dorothy blushed beneath her hat even at her sister's

[051-113]praise,--"and good-tempered, and lovable in every way. And I think

[051-114]you are just fitted to make a good wife. And you must not suppose,

[051-115]Dolly, that because Mr. Gibson wouldn't perhaps have asked you

[051-116]without the money, that therefore he is mercenary. It so often

[051-117]happens that a gentleman can't marry unless the lady has some money!"

[051-118]"But he hasn't asked me at all."

[051-119]"I suppose he will, dear."

[051-120]"I only know what Aunt Stanbury says."

[051-121]"You may be sure that he will ask you."

[051-122]"And what must I say, Priscilla?"

[051-123]"What must you say? Nobody can tell you that, dear, but yourself. Do

[051-124]you like him?"

[051-125]"I don't dislike him."

[051-126]"Is that all?"

[051-127]"I know him so very little, Priscilla. Everybody says he is very

[051-128]good;--and then it's a great thing, isn't it, that he should be a

[051-129]clergyman?"

[051-130]"I don't know about that."

[051-131]"I think it is. If it were possible that I should ever marry any one,

[051-132]I should like a clergyman so much the best."

[051-133]"Then you do know what to say to him."

[051-134]"No, I don't, Priscilla. I don't know at all."

[051-135]"Look here, dearest. What my aunt offers to you is a very great

[051-136]step in life. If you can accept this gentleman I think you would be

[051-137]happy;--and I think, also, which should be of more importance for

[051-138]your consideration, that you would make him happy. It is a brighter

[051-139]prospect, dear Dolly, than to live either with us at Nuncombe, or

[051-140]even with Aunt Stanbury as her niece."

[051-141]"But if I don't love him, Priscilla?"

[051-142]"Then give it up, and be as you are, my own own, dearest sister."

[051-143]"So I will," said Dorothy, and at that time her mind was made up.

[051-145]CHAPTER XXXI.

[051-146]MR. BROOKE BURGESS.

[051-147][Illustration]

[051-148]The hour at which Mr. Brooke Burgess was to arrive had come round,

[051-149]and Miss Stanbury was in a twitter, partly of expectation, and

[051-150]partly, it must be confessed, of fear. Why there should be any fear

[051-151]she did not herself know, as she had much to give and nothing to

[051-152]expect. But she was afraid, and was conscious of it, and was out

[051-153]of temper because she was ashamed of herself. Although it would be

[051-154]necessary that she should again dress for dinner at six, she had put

[051-155]on a clean cap at four, and appeared at that early hour in one of

[051-156]her gowns which was not customarily in use for home purposes at that

[051-157]early hour. She felt that she was "an old fool" for her pains, and

[051-158]was consequently cross to poor Dorothy. And there were other reasons

[051-159]for some display of harshness to her niece. Mr. Gibson had been at

[051-160]the house that very morning, and Dorothy had given herself airs. At

[051-161]least, so Miss Stanbury thought. And during the last three or four

[051-162]days, whenever Mr. Gibson's name had been mentioned, Dorothy had

[051-163]become silent, glum, and almost obstructive. Miss Stanbury had been

[051-164]at the trouble of explaining that she was specially anxious to have

[051-165]that little matter of the engagement settled at once. She knew that

[051-166]she was going to behave with great generosity;--that she was going to

[051-167]sacrifice, not her money only, of which she did not think much, but a

[051-168]considerable portion of her authority, of which she did think a great

[051-169]deal; and that she was about to behave in a manner which demanded

[051-170]much gratitude. But it seemed to her that Dorothy was not in

[051-171]the least grateful. Hugh had proved himself to be "a mass of

[051-172]ingratitude," as she was in the habit of saying. None of the

[051-173]Burgesses had ever shown to her any gratitude for promises made to

[051-174]them, or, indeed, for any substantial favours conferred upon them.

[051-175]And now Dorothy, to whom a very seventh heaven of happiness had been

[051-176]opened,--a seventh heaven, as it must be computed in comparison with

[051-177]her low expectations,--now Dorothy was already shewing how thankless

[051-178]she could become. Mr. Gibson had not yet declared his passion, but he

[051-179]had freely admitted to Miss Stanbury that he was prepared to do so.

[051-180]Priscilla had been quite right in her suggestion that there was a

[051-181]clear understanding between the clergyman and her aunt.

[051-182]"I don't think he is come after all," said Miss Stanbury, looking

[051-183]at her watch. Had the train arrived at the moment that it was due,

[051-184]had the expectant visitor jumped out of the railway carriage into

[051-185]a fly, and had the driver galloped up to the Close, it might have

[051-186]been possible that the wheels should have been at the door as Miss

[051-187]Stanbury spoke.

[051-188]"It's hardly time yet, aunt."

[051-189]"Nonsense; it is time. The train comes in at four. I dare say he

[051-190]won't come at all."
