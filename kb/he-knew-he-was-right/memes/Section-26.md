## Section 26

[026-001]"Mamma tells me," said Dorothy, "that Mrs. Trevelyan and Miss Rowley

[026-002]do not mean to know anybody. They have given it out quite plainly, so

[026-003]that there should be no mistake."

[026-004]"Dear, dear," said Camilla French.

[026-005]"I dare say it's for the best," said Arabella French, who was the

[026-006]elder, and who looked very meek and soft. Miss French almost always

[026-007]looked meek and soft.

[026-008]"I'm afraid it will make it very dull for your mother,--not seeing

[026-009]her old friends," said Mr. Gibson.

[026-010]"Mamma won't feel that at all," said Dorothy.

[026-011]"Mrs. Stanbury, I suppose, will see her own friends at her own house

[026-012]just the same," said Camilla.

[026-013]"There would be great difficulty in that, when there is a lady who is

[026-014]to remain unknown," said Arabella. "Don't you think so, Mr. Gibson?"

[026-015]Mr. Gibson replied that perhaps there might be a difficulty, but he

[026-016]wasn't sure. The difficulty, he thought, might be got over if the

[026-017]ladies did not always occupy the same room.

[026-018]"You have never seen Mrs. Trevelyan, have you, Miss Stanbury?" asked

[026-019]Camilla.

[026-020]"Never."

[026-021]"She is not an old family friend, then,--or anything of that sort?"

[026-022]"Oh, dear, no."

[026-023]"Because," said Arabella, "it is so odd how different people get

[026-024]together sometimes." Then Dorothy explained that Mr. Trevelyan and

[026-025]her brother Hugh had long been friends.

[026-026]"Oh!--of Mr. Trevelyan," said Camilla. "Then it is he that has sent

[026-027]his wife to Nuncombe, not she that has come there?"

[026-028]"I suppose there has been some agreement," said Dorothy.

[026-029]"Just so; just so," said Arabella, the meek. "I should like to see

[026-030]her. They say that she is very beautiful; don't they?"

[026-031]"My brother says that she is handsome."

[026-032]"Exceedingly lovely, I'm told," said Camilla. "I should like to see

[026-033]her,--shouldn't you, Mr. Gibson?"

[026-034]"I always like to see a pretty woman," said Mr. Gibson, with a polite

[026-035]bow, which the sisters shared between them.

[026-036]"I suppose she'll go to church," said Camilla.

[026-037]"Very likely not," said Arabella. "Ladies of that sort very often

[026-038]don't go to church. I dare say you'll find that she'll never stir

[026-039]out of the place at all, and that not a soul in Nuncombe will ever

[026-040]see her except the gardener. It is such a thing for a woman to be

[026-041]separated from her husband! Don't you think so, Mr. Gibson?"

[026-042]"Of course it is," said he, with a shake of his head, which was

[026-043]intended to imply that the censure of the church must of course

[026-044]attend any sundering of those whom the church had bound together; but

[026-045]which implied also by the absence from it of any intense clerical

[026-046]severity, that as the separated wife was allowed to live with so very

[026-047]respectable a lady as Mrs. Stanbury, there must probably be some

[026-048]mitigating circumstances attending this special separation.

[026-049]"I wonder what he is like?" said Camilla, after a pause.

[026-050]"Who?" asked Arabella.

[026-051]"The [[social-class|gentleman]]," said Camilla.

[026-052]"What gentleman?" demanded Arabella.

[026-053]"I don't mean Mr. Trevelyan," said Camilla.

[026-054]"I don't believe there really is,--eh,--is there?" said Mr. Gibson,

[026-055]very timidly.

[026-056]"Oh, dear, yes," said Arabella.

[026-057]"I'm afraid there's something of the kind," said Camilla. "I've heard

[026-058]that there is, and I've heard his name." Then she whispered very

[026-059]closely into the ear of Mr. Gibson the words, "[[colonel-osborne|Colonel Osborne]]," as

[026-060]though her lips were by far too pure to mention aloud any sound so

[026-061]full of iniquity.

[026-062]"Indeed!" said Mr. Gibson.

[026-063]"But he's quite an old man," said Dorothy, "and knew her father

[026-064]intimately before she was born. And, as far as I can understand, her

[026-065]husband does not suspect her in the least. And it's only because

[026-066]there's a misunderstanding between them, and not at all because of

[026-067]the gentleman."

[026-068]"Oh!" exclaimed Camilla.

[026-069]"Ah!" exclaimed Arabella.

[026-070]"That would make a difference," said Mr. Gibson.

[026-071]"But for a married woman to have her name mentioned at all with a

[026-072]gentleman,--it is so bad; is it not, Mr. Gibson?" And then Arabella

[026-073]also had her whisper into the clergyman's ear,--very closely. "I'm

[026-074]afraid there's not a doubt about the Colonel. I'm afraid not. I am

[026-075]indeed."

[026-076]"Two by honours and the odd, and it's my deal," said Miss Stanbury,

[026-077]briskly, and the sharp click with which she put the markers down

[026-078]upon the table was heard all through the room. "I don't want anybody

[026-079]to tell me," she said, "that when a young woman is parted from her

[026-080]husband, the chances are ten to one that she has been very foolish."

[026-081]"But what's a woman to do, if her husband beats her?" said Mrs.

[026-082]Crumbie.

[026-083]"Beat him again," said Mrs. MacHugh.

[026-084]"And the husband will be sure to have the worst of it," said Mr.

[026-085]Crumbie. "Well, I declare, if you haven't turned up an honour again,

[026-086]Miss Stanbury!"

[026-087]"It was your wife that cut it to me, Mr. Crumbie." Then they were

[026-088]again at once immersed in the play, and the name neither of Trevelyan

[026-089]nor Osborne was heard till Miss Stanbury was marking her double under

[026-090]the candlestick; but during all pauses in the game the conversation

[026-091]went back to the same topic, and when the rubber was over they

[026-092]who had been playing it lost themselves for ten minutes in the

[026-093]allurements of the interesting subject. It was so singular a

[026-094]coincidence that the lady should have gone to [[nuncombe-putney|Nuncombe Putney]] of

[026-095]all villages in England, and to the house of Mrs. Stanbury of all

[026-096]ladies in England. And then was she innocent, or was she guilty; and

[026-097]if guilty, in what degree? That she had been allowed to bring her

[026-098]baby with her was considered to be a great point in her favour. Mr.

[026-099]Crumbie's opinion was that it was "only a few words." Mrs. Crumbie

[026-100]was afraid that she had been a little light. Mrs. MacHugh said that

[026-101]there was never fire without smoke. And Miss Stanbury, as she took

[026-102]her departure, declared that the young women of the present day

[026-103]didn't know what they were after. "They think that the world should

[026-104]be all frolic and dancing, and they have no more idea of doing their

[026-105]duty and earning their bread than a boy home for the holidays has of

[026-106]doing lessons."

[026-107]Then, as she went home with Dorothy across the Close, she spoke a

[026-108]word which she intended to be very serious. "I don't mean to say

[026-109]anything against your mother for what she has done as yet. Somebody

[026-110]must take the woman in, and perhaps it was natural. But if that

[026-111]Colonel What's-his-name makes his way down to Nuncombe Putney, your

[026-112]mother must send her packing, if she has any respect either for

[026-113]herself or for Priscilla."

[026-114]CHAPTER XVI.

[026-115]DARTMOOR.

[026-116][Illustration]

[026-117]The well-weighed decision of Miss Stanbury respecting the

[026-118]Stanbury-Trevelyan arrangement at Nuncombe Putney had been

[026-119]communicated to Dorothy as the two walked home at night across the

[026-120]Close from Mrs. MacHugh's house, and it was accepted by Dorothy

[026-121]as being wise and proper. It amounted to this. If Mrs. Trevelyan

[026-122]should behave herself with [[social-custom|propriety]] in her retirement at the Clock

[026-123]House, no further blame in the matter should be attributed to Mrs.

[026-124]Stanbury for receiving her,--at any rate in Dorothy's hearing. The

[026-125]existing scheme, whether wise or foolish, should be regarded as an

[026-126]accepted scheme. But if Mrs. Trevelyan should be indiscreet,--if,

[026-127]for instance, Colonel Osborne should show himself at Nuncombe

[026-128]Putney,--then, for the sake of the family, Miss Stanbury would speak

[026-129]out, and would speak out very loudly. All this Dorothy understood,

[026-130]and she could perceive that her aunt had strong suspicion that there

[026-131]would be indiscretion.

[026-132]"I never knew one like her," said Miss Stanbury, "who, when she'd got

[026-133]away from one man, didn't want to have another dangling after her."

[026-134]A week had hardly passed after the party at Mrs. MacHugh's, and Mrs.

[026-135]Trevelyan had hardly been three weeks at Nuncombe Putney, before the

[026-136]tidings which Miss Stanbury almost expected reached her ears.

[026-137]"The Colonel's been at the [[clock-house|Clock House]], ma'am," said Martha.

[026-138]Now, it was quite understood in the Close by this time that "the

[026-139]Colonel" meant Colonel Osborne.

[026-140]"No!"

[026-141]"I'm told he has though, ma'am, for sure and certain."

[026-142]"Who says so?"

[026-143]"Giles Hickbody was down at Lessboro', and see'd him hisself,--a

[026-144]portly, middle-aged man,--not one of your young scampish-like

[026-145]lovers."

[026-146]"That's the man."

[026-147]"Oh, yes. He went over to Nuncombe Putney, as sure as

[026-148]anything;--hired Mrs. Clegg's chaise and pair, and asked for Mrs.

[026-149]Trevelyan's house as open as anything. When Giles asked in the yard,

[026-150]they told him as how that was the married lady's young man."

[026-151]"I'd like to be at his tail,--so I would,--with a mop-handle," said

[026-152]Miss Stanbury, whose hatred for those sins by which the comfort and

[026-153]respectability of the world are destroyed, was not only sincere, but

[026-154]intense. "Well; and what then?"

[026-155]"He came back and slept at Mrs. Clegg's that night,--at least, that

[026-156]was what he said he should do."

[026-157]Miss Stanbury, however, was not so precipitate or uncharitable as

[026-158]to act strongly upon information such as this. Before she even said

[026-159]a word to Dorothy, she made further inquiry. She made very minute

[026-160]inquiry, writing even to her very old and intimate friend Mrs.

[026-161]Ellison, of Lessboro',--writing to that lady a most cautious and

[026-162]guarded letter. At last it became a fact proved to her mind that

[026-163]Colonel Osborne had been at the Clock House, had been received there,

[026-164]and had remained there for hours,--had been allowed access to Mrs.

[026-165]Trevelyan, and had slept the night at the inn at Lessboro'. The thing

[026-166]was so terrible to Miss Stanbury's mind, that even false hair, Dr.

[026-167]Colenso, and penny newspapers did not account for it.

[026-168]"I shall begin to believe that the Evil One has been allowed to come

[026-169]among us in person because of our sins," she said to Martha;--and she

[026-170]meant it.

[026-171]In the meantime, Mrs. Trevelyan, as may be remembered, had hired Mrs.

[026-172]Crocket's open carriage, and the three young women, Mrs. Trevelyan,

[026-173]Nora, and Priscilla, made a little excursion to Princetown, somewhat

[026-174]after the fashion of a picnic. At Princetown, in the middle of

[026-175]Dartmoor, about nine miles from Nuncombe Putney, is the prison

[026-176]establishment at which are kept convicts undergoing penal servitude.

[026-177]It is regarded by all the country round with great interest, chiefly

[026-178]because the prisoners now and again escape, and then there comes a

[026-179]period of interesting excitement until the escaped felon shall have

[026-180]been again taken. How can you tell where he may be, or whether it may

[026-181]not suit him to find his rest in your own cupboard, or under your own

[026-182]bed? And then, as escape without notice will of course be the felon's

[026-183]object, to attain that he will probably cut your throat, and the

[026-184]throat of everybody belonging to you. All which considerations give

[026-185]an interest to Princetown, and excite in the hearts of the Devonians

[026-186]of these parts a strong affection for the Dartmoor prison. Of those

[026-187]who visit Princetown comparatively few effect an entrance within the

[026-188]walls of the gaol. They look at the gloomy place with a mysterious

[026-189]interest, feeling something akin to envy for the prisoners who have

[026-190]enjoyed the privilege of solving the mysteries of prison life, and

[026-191]who know how men feel when they have their hair cut short, and are

[026-192]free from moral [[duty|responsibility]] for their own conduct, and are moved

[026-193]about in gangs, and treated like wild beasts.

[026-194]But the journey to Princetown, from whatever side it is approached,

[026-195]has the charm of wild and beautiful scenery. The spot itself is ugly

[026-196]enough; but you can go not thither without breathing the sweetest,

[026-197]freshest air, and encountering that delightful sense of romance which

[026-198]moorland scenery always produces. The idea of our three friends was

[026-199]to see the Moor rather than the prison, to learn something of the

[026-200]country around, and to enjoy the excitement of eating a sandwich

[026-201]sitting on a hillock, in exchange for the ordinary comforts of a good

[026-202]dinner with chairs and tables. A bottle of sherry and water and a

[026-203]paper of sandwiches contained their whole banquet; for ladies, though

[026-204]they like good things at picnics, and, indeed, at other times, almost

[026-205]as well as men like them, very seldom prepare dainties for themselves

[026-206]alone. Men are wiser and more thoughtful, and are careful to have the

[026-207]good things, even if they are to be enjoyed without companionship.
