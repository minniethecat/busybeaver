## Section 32

[032-001]"'It is all very well for gentlemen to call themselves reformers,'"

[032-002]Hugh was writing, "'but have these gentlemen ever realised to

[032-003]themselves the meaning of that word? We think that they have never

[032-004]done so as long as--' Of course you love her," said Hugh, with his

[032-005]eyes still on the paper, still leaning on his pen, but finding by the

[032-006]cessation of sound that Trevelyan had paused, and therefore knowing

[032-007]that it was necessary that he should speak.

[032-008]"As much as ever," said Trevelyan, with energy.

[032-009]"'As long as they follow such a leader, in such a cause, into

[032-010]whichever lobby he may choose to take them--' Exactly so,--exactly,"

[032-011]said Stanbury; "just as much as ever."

[032-012]"You are not listening to a word," said Trevelyan.

[032-013]"I haven't missed a single expression you have used," said Stanbury.

[032-014]"But a fellow has to do two things at a time when he's on the daily

[032-015]press."

[032-016]"I beg your pardon for interrupting you," said Trevelyan, angrily,

[032-017]getting up, taking his hat, and stalking off to the house of Lady

[032-018]Milborough. In this way he became rather a bore to his friends. He

[032-019]could not divest his mind of the injury which had accrued to him from

[032-020]his wife's conduct, nor could he help talking of the grief with which

[032-021]his mind was laden. And he was troubled with sore suspicions, which,

[032-022]as far as they concerned his wife, had certainly not been merited. It

[032-023]had seemed to him that she had persisted in her intimacy with Colonel

[032-024]Osborne in a manner that was not compatible with that wife-like

[032-025]indifference which he regarded as her duty. Why had she written to

[032-026]him and received letters from him when her husband had plainly told

[032-027]her that any such communication was objectionable? She had done

[032-028]so, and as far as Trevelyan could remember her words, had plainly

[032-029]declared that she would continue to do so. He had sent her away into

[032-030]the most remote retirement he could find for her; but the post was

[032-031]open to her. He had heard much of Mrs. Stanbury, and of Priscilla,

[032-032]from his friend Hugh, and thoroughly believed that his wife was in

[032-033]respectable hands. But what was to prevent [[colonel-osborne|Colonel Osborne]] from

[032-034]going after her, if he chose to do so? And if he did so choose,

[032-035]Mrs. Stanbury could not prevent their meeting. He was racked with

[032-036]jealousy, and yet he did not cease to declare to himself that he knew

[032-037]his wife too well to believe that she would sin. He could not rid

[032-038]himself of his jealousy, but he tried with all his might to make the

[032-039]man whom he hated the object of it, rather than the woman whom he

[032-040]loved.

[032-041]He hated Colonel Osborne with all his heart. It was a regret to him

[032-042]that the days of duelling were over, so that he could not shoot the

[032-043]man. And yet, had duelling been possible to him, Colonel Osborne had

[032-044]done nothing that would have justified him in calling his enemy out,

[032-045]or would even have enabled him to do so with any chance of inducing

[032-046]his enemy to fight. Circumstances, he thought, were cruel to him

[032-047]beyond compare, in that he should have been made to suffer so great

[032-048]torment without having any of the satisfaction of revenge. Even Lady

[032-049]Milborough, with all her horror as to the Colonel, could not tell

[032-050]him that the Colonel was amenable to any punishment. He was advised

[032-051]that he must take his wife away and live at Naples because of this

[032-052]man,--that he must banish himself entirely if he chose to repossess

[032-053]himself of his wife and child;--and yet nothing could be done to

[032-054]the unprincipled rascal by whom all his wrongs and sufferings were

[032-055]occasioned! Thinking it very possible that Colonel Osborne would

[032-056]follow his wife, he had a watch set upon the Colonel. He had found a

[032-057]retired policeman,--a most discreet man, as he was assured,--who, for

[032-058]a consideration, undertook the management of interesting jobs of this

[032-059]kind. The man was one Bozzle, who had not lived without a certain

[032-060][[honour|reputation]] in the police courts. In these days of his madness,

[032-061]therefore, he took Mr. Bozzle into his pay; and after a while he got

[032-062]a letter from Bozzle with the [[exeter|Exeter]] post-mark. Colonel Osborne had

[032-063]left [[london|London]] with a ticket for Lessboro'. Bozzle also had taken a

[032-064]place by the same train for that small town. The letter was written

[032-065]in the railway carriage, and, as Bozzle explained, would be posted by

[032-066]him as he passed through Exeter. A further communication should be

[032-067]made by the next day's post, in a letter which Mr. Bozzle proposed to

[032-068]address to Z. A., Post-office, Waterloo Place.

[032-069]On receiving this first letter, Trevelyan was in an agony of

[032-070]doubt, as well as misery. What should he do? Should he go to Lady

[032-071]Milborough, or to Stanbury; or should he at once follow Colonel

[032-072]Osborne and Mr. Bozzle to Lessboro'? It ended in his resolving at

[032-073]last to wait for the letter which was to be addressed to Z. A. But he

[032-074]spent an interval of horrible suspense, and of insane rage. Let the

[032-075]laws say what they might, he would have the man's blood, if he found

[032-076]that the man had even attempted to wrong him. Then, at last, the

[032-077]second letter reached him. Colonel Osborne and Mr. Bozzle had each of

[032-078]them spent the day in the neighbourhood of Lessboro', not exactly in

[032-079]each other's company, but very near to each other. "The Colonel" had

[032-080]ordered a gig, on the day after his arrival at Lessboro', for the

[032-081]village of Cockchaffington; and, for all Mr. Bozzle knew, the Colonel

[032-082]had gone to Cockchaffington. Mr. Bozzle was ultimately inclined

[032-083]to think that the Colonel had really spent his day in going to

[032-084]Cockchaffington. Mr. Bozzle himself, knowing the wiles of such

[032-085]men as Colonel Osborne, and thinking at first that that journey

[032-086]to Cockchaffington might only be a deep ruse, had walked over to

[032-087][[nuncombe-putney|Nuncombe Putney]]. There he had had a pint of beer and some bread and

[032-088]cheese at Mrs. Crocket's house, and had asked various questions, to

[032-089]which he did not receive very satisfactory answers. But he inspected

[032-090]the [[clock-house|Clock House]] very minutely, and came to a decided opinion as to

[032-091]the point at which it would be attacked, if burglary were the object

[032-092]of the assailants. And he observed the iron gates, and the steps,

[032-093]and the shape of the trees, and the old pigeon-house-looking fabric

[032-094]in which the clock used to be placed. There was no knowing when

[032-095]information might be wanted, or what information might not be of use.

[032-096]But he made himself tolerably sure that Colonel Osborne did not visit

[032-097]Nuncombe Putney on that day; and then he walked back to Lessboro'.

[032-098]Having done this, he applied himself to the little memorandum book in

[032-099]which he kept the records of these interesting duties, and entered a

[032-100]claim against his employer for a conveyance to Nuncombe Putney and

[032-101]back, including driver and ostler; and then he wrote his letter.

[032-102]After that he had a hot supper, with three glasses of brandy and

[032-103]water, and went to bed with a thorough conviction that he had earned

[032-104]his bread on that day.

[032-105]The letter to Z. A. did not give all these particulars, but it

[032-106]did explain that Colonel Osborne had gone off, apparently, to

[032-107]Cockchaffington, and that he,--Bozzle,--had himself visited Nuncombe

[032-108]Putney. "The hawk hasn't been nigh the dovecot as yet," said Mr.

[032-109]Bozzle in his letter, meaning to be both mysterious and facetious.

[032-110]It would be difficult to say whether the wit or the mystery disgusted

[032-111]Trevelyan the most. He had felt that he was defiling himself with

[032-112]dirt when he first went to Mr. Bozzle. He knew that he was having

[032-113]recourse to means that were base and low,--which could not be other

[032-114]than base or low, let the circumstances be what they might. But Mr.

[032-115]Bozzle's conversation had not been quite so bad as Mr. Bozzle's

[032-116]letters; as it may have been that Mr. Bozzle's successful activity

[032-117]was more insupportable than his futile attempts. But, nevertheless,

[032-118]something must be done. It could not be that Colonel Osborne should

[032-119]have gone down to the close neighbourhood of Nuncombe Putney without

[032-120]the intention of seeing the lady whom his obtrusive pertinacity had

[032-121]driven to that seclusion. It was terrible to Trevelyan that Colonel

[032-122]Osborne should be there, and not the less terrible because such a one

[032-123]as Mr. Bozzle was watching the Colonel on his behalf. Should he go to

[032-124]Nuncombe Putney himself? And if so, when he got to Nuncombe Putney

[032-125]what should he do there? At last, in his suspense and his grief, he

[032-126]resolved that he would tell the whole to [[hugh-stanbury|Hugh Stanbury]].

[032-127]"Do you mean," said Hugh, "that you have put a policeman on his

[032-128]track?"

[032-129]"The man was a policeman once."

[032-130]"What we call a private detective. I can't say I think you were

[032-131]right."

[032-132]"But you see that it was necessary," said Trevelyan.

[032-133]"I can't say that it was necessary. To speak out, I can't understand

[032-134]that a wife should be worth watching who requires watching."

[032-135]"Is a man to do nothing then? And even now it is not my wife whom I

[032-136]doubt."

[032-137]"As for Colonel Osborne, if he chooses to go to Lessboro', why

[032-138]shouldn't he? Nothing that you can do, or that Bozzle can do, can

[032-139]prevent him. He has a perfect right to go to Lessboro'."

[032-140]"But he has not a right to go to my wife."

[032-141]"And if your wife refuses to see him; or having seen him,--for a man

[032-142]may force his way in anywhere with a little trouble,--if she sends

[032-143]him away with a flea in his ear, as I believe she would--"

[032-144]"She is so frightfully indiscreet."

[032-145]"I don't see what Bozzle can do."

[032-146]"He has found out at any rate that Osborne is there," said Trevelyan.

[032-147]"I am not more fond of dealing with such fellows than you are

[032-148]yourself. But I think it is my duty to know what is going on. What

[032-149]ought I to do now?"

[032-150]"I should do nothing,--except dismiss Bozzle."

[032-151]"You know that that is nonsense, Stanbury."

[032-152]"Whatever I did I should dismiss Bozzle." Stanbury was now quite in

[032-153]earnest, and, as he repeated his suggestion for the dismissal of the

[032-154]policeman, pushed his writing things away from him. "If you ask my

[032-155]opinion, you know, I must tell you what I think. I should get rid of

[032-156]Bozzle as a beginning. If you will only think of it, how can your

[032-157]wife come back to you if she learns that you have set a detective to

[032-158]watch her?"

[032-159]"But I haven't set the man to watch her."

[032-160]"Colonel Osborne is nothing to you, except as he is concerned with

[032-161]her. This man is now down in her neighbourhood; and, if she learns

[032-162]that, how can she help feeling it as a deep insult? Of course the man

[032-163]watches her as a cat watches a mouse."

[032-164]"But what am I to do? I can't write to the man and tell him to come

[032-165]away. Osborne is down there, and I must do something. Will you go

[032-166]down to Nuncombe Putney yourself, and let me know the truth?"

[032-167]After much debating of the subject, Hugh Stanbury said that he would

[032-168]himself go down to Nuncombe Putney alone. There were difficulties

[032-169]about the D. R.; but he would go to the office of the newspaper and

[032-170]overcome them. How far the presence of [[nora-rowley|Nora Rowley]] at his mother's

[032-171]house may have assisted in bringing him to undertake the journey,

[032-172]perhaps need not be accurately stated. He acknowledged to himself

[032-173]that the claims of friendship were strong upon him; and that as he

[032-174]had loudly disapproved of the Bozzle arrangement, he ought to lend a

[032-175]hand to some other scheme of action. Moreover, having professed his

[032-176]conviction that no improper visiting could possibly take place under

[032-177]his mother's roof, he felt bound to shew that he was not afraid to

[032-178]trust to that conviction himself. He declared that he would be ready

[032-179]to proceed to Nuncombe Putney to-morrow;--but only on condition that

[032-180]he might have plenary power to dismiss Bozzle.
