# Mr. Bideawhile

Mr. Bideawhile is the elderly family lawyer to Louis Trevelyan, a figure of professional rectitude and humane concern who is drawn into the Trevelyan marital crisis in the hope that legal authority might succeed where social counsel has failed. His failure to arrest Trevelyan's descent into paranoid isolation is one of the novel's most pointed illustrations of the limits of institutional intervention in private tragedy.

## Overview

Mr. Bideawhile first enters the narrative when Trevelyan, seeking a confidential outlet for his suspicions, turns to the legal profession. "Old Mr. Bideawhile had been his father's lawyer" (007-164), the narrator notes, establishing a familial connection that predates the current crisis. This history of trusted service makes Bideawhile a natural confidant — and his inability to help Trevelyan all the more significant.

His professional manner is characterised by caution and propriety. When Emily seeks his assistance, she finds him "full of doubt,—full of fear" (024-133), a man who understands the gravity of the situation but struggles to find a legal remedy for what is fundamentally a psychological and moral breakdown. His office becomes a site of repeated, increasingly desperate consultations as Trevelyan's condition worsens.

## Role in the Novel

Mr. Bideawhile functions as the novel's representative of the legal establishment and its relationship to domestic crisis. Through his interactions with both Trevelyan and Emily, Trollope explores what the law can and cannot do in the context of a marriage collapsing under the weight of irrational jealousy. The answer, as the novel demonstrates, is: very little.

Trevelyan goes to Bideawhile "so often" that "the poor old lawyer was worn out with the case" (031-138). He wants Bideawhile to sanction a separation — to provide legal cover for what is, in essence, an act of marital cruelty driven by delusion. Bideawhile resists. "Mr. Bideawhile became quite severe in the tone he assumed" (054-129), refusing to lend professional legitimacy to Trevelyan's irrational demands. But refusal is not the same as prevention, and Trevelyan ultimately acts on his own authority regardless of legal counsel.

Bideawhile also serves as an intermediary between the estranged spouses, handling financial arrangements and communicating positions that the parties can no longer communicate directly. He arranges for Emily to receive "a proper sum quarterly through Mr. Bideawhile for her own clothes" (024-159), a detail that underscores both the practical dependencies of separated wives and the lawyer's role as a conduit for transactions that personal estrangement has made impossible.

## Relationships

### Louis Trevelyan

The relationship between Trevelyan and his lawyer is one of client and counsel that gradually becomes one of supplicant and resistant authority. Trevelyan wants Bideawhile to validate his position; Bideawhile wants Trevelyan to see reason. Neither gets what he wants. Trevelyan's visits become so frequent and so fruitless that "the poor old lawyer was worn out with the case" (031-138) — a phrase that captures both the emotional toll of the matter and its fundamental intractability.

Trevelyan's suspicion eventually extends even to his own lawyer. He becomes "somewhat afraid that Mr. Bideawhile might not be zealous on his behalf" (075-043), and when Bideawhile's answer to some demand comes back "very short" (075-068), Trevelyan concludes — without evidence — that "Mr. Bideawhile had probably been corrupted by the other side" (075-073). The paranoid logic that destroyed his marriage now begins to corrode his professional relationships as well.

### Emily Trevelyan

Mr. Bideawhile's dealings with Emily are marked by professional correctness and a degree of personal sympathy that his position constrains him from expressing fully. When he visits her, she finds him "full of doubt,—full of fear" (024-133) — a man who sees the injustice of her situation but can offer only the limited remedies of the law. He explains practical arrangements — "Mr. Bideawhile explained to her that Nuncombe Putney was very pretty" (024-172) — with a gentleness that does not quite conceal the inadequacy of what he can actually do for her.

His most human moment comes in a small aside: asked about the village where Emily is staying, he replies "'Lovely!' said Mr. Bideawhile, who had a weakness for young women" (024-177). The narrator's wry observation humanises the elderly lawyer, reminding the reader that behind the professional facade is a man with ordinary feelings and sympathies.

### Lady Milborough

Bideawhile and Lady Milborough function as parallel figures — she representing social authority, he legal authority — and Trevelyan consults both, to equally limited effect. The narrative links them explicitly: Trevelyan sends letters "to Mr. Bideawhile and to Lady Milborough" (055-059) and receives "the replies from Mr. Bideawhile and from Lady Milborough" (055-063). Their failure, taken together, represents the novel's judgment that neither social convention nor legal process can cure what ails Louis Trevelyan.

## Key Scenes

### The Initial Consultation

Trevelyan's first approach to Bideawhile is a scene of suppressed confession and professional caution. Trevelyan cannot bring himself to state his suspicions plainly even to his lawyer (007-166), and Bideawhile, for his part, cannot offer legal remedies for grievances that remain unspoken. The scene establishes the pattern that will persist throughout: a client who cannot be honest and a lawyer who cannot be effective.

### Emily's Interview

When Emily consults Mr. Bideawhile after the separation, the meeting is a study in constrained sympathy. "Mr. Bideawhile called upon her, her husband's lawyer" (024-107), and she strives "so to speak that Mr. Bideawhile should find no fault with her" (024-131). The power imbalance — a separated wife appealing to her husband's lawyer — is palpable, and Bideawhile's courtesy cannot quite bridge the gap between what is just and what is legally available.

### The Short Answer

Bideawhile's eventual refusal to continue indulging Trevelyan is conveyed in a letter described as "very short" (075-068). This terse communication represents the lawyer's final judgment: that further engagement is futile, that legal process has reached its limit, and that Trevelyan must be left to the consequences of his own choices. The brevity of the reply is itself a moral statement.

## Character Development

Mr. Bideawhile does not undergo character development so much as he undergoes character exhaustion. He begins the novel as a competent, humane professional and ends it worn out, his authority spent, his counsel ignored. His arc is one of depletion rather than transformation — an honest man worn down by a client whose problems are beyond the reach of honest professional advice.

His failure is not personal but systemic. The novel suggests, through Bideawhile's experience, that the Victorian legal framework for marriage was fundamentally inadequate to address the kind of psychological crisis that Trevelyan represents. A man could not be legally restrained from destroying his marriage through irrational jealousy, and a woman could not be legally protected from the consequences — a gap that Bideawhile, for all his goodwill, is powerless to close.
