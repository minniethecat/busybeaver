## Section 67

[067-001]She had been so wretched at [[nuncombe-putney|Nuncombe Putney]] when she had felt herself

[067-002]constrained to admit to herself that this man for whom she had

[067-003]sacrificed herself did not care for her, that she could not now but

[067-004]enjoy her triumph. After she had sobbed upon the bed, she got up and

[067-005]walked about the room smiling; and she would now press her hands to

[067-006]her forehead, and then shake her tresses, and then clasp her own left

[067-007]hand with her right, as though he were still holding it. Wicked man!

[067-008]Why had he been so wicked and so violent? And why, why, why had she

[067-009]not once felt his lips upon her brow?

[067-010]And she was pleased with herself. Her sister had rebuked her because

[067-011]she had refused to make her [[money|fortune]] by marrying Mr. Glascock; and,

[067-012]to own the truth, she had rebuked herself on the same score when she

[067-013]found that [[hugh-stanbury|Hugh Stanbury]] had not had a word of love to say to her. It

[067-014]was not that she regretted the grandeur which she had lost, but that

[067-015]she should, even within her own thoughts, with the consciousness of

[067-016]her own bosom, have declared herself unable to receive another man's

[067-017]devotion because of her love for this man who neglected her. Now

[067-018]she was proud of herself. Whether it might be accounted as good or

[067-019]ill-fortune that she had ever seen Hugh Stanbury, it must at any rate

[067-020]be right that she should be true to him now that she had seen him

[067-021]and had loved him. To know that she loved and that she was not loved

[067-022]again had nearly killed her. But such was not her lot. She too had

[067-023]been successful with her quarry, and had struck her game, and brought

[067-024]down her dear. He had been very violent with her, but his violence

[067-025]had at least made the matter clear. He did love her. She would

[067-026]be satisfied with that, and would endeavour so to live that that

[067-027]alone should make life happy for her. How should she get his

[067-028]photograph,--and a lock of his hair?--and when again might she have

[067-029]the pleasure of placing her own hand within his great, rough, violent

[067-030]grasp? Then she kissed the hand which he had held, and opened the

[067-031]door of her room, at which her sister was now knocking.

[067-032]"Nora, dear, will you not come down?"

[067-033]"Not yet, Emily. Very soon I will."

[067-034]"And what has happened, dearest?"

[067-035]"There is nothing to tell, Emily."

[067-036]"There must be something to tell. What did he say to you?"

[067-037]"Of course you know what he said."

[067-038]"And what answer did you make?"

[067-039]"I told him that it could not be."

[067-040]"And did he take that,--as final, Nora?"

[067-041]"Of course not. What man ever takes a No as final?"

[067-042]"When you said No to Mr. Glascock he took it."

[067-043]"That was different, Emily."

[067-044]"But how different? I don't see the difference, except that if you

[067-045]could have brought yourself to like Mr. Glascock, it would have been

[067-046]the greatest thing in the world for you, and for all of them."

[067-047]"Would you have me take a man, Emily, that I didn't care one straw

[067-048]for, merely because he was a lord? You can't mean that."

[067-049]"I'm not talking about Mr. Glascock now, Nora."

[067-050]"Yes, you are. And what's the use? He is gone, and there's an end of

[067-051]it."

[067-052]"And is Mr. Stanbury gone?"

[067-053]"Of course."

[067-054]"In the same way?" asked Mrs. Trevelyan.

[067-055]"How can I tell about his ways? No; it is not in the same way. There!

[067-056]He went in a very different way."

[067-057]"How was it different, Nora?"

[067-058]"Oh, so different. I can't tell you how. Mr. Glascock will never come

[067-059]back again."

[067-060]"And Mr. Stanbury will?" said the elder sister. Nora made no reply,

[067-061]but after a while nodded her head. "And you want him to come back?"

[067-062]She paused again, and again nodded her head. "Then you have accepted

[067-063]him?"

[067-064]"I have not accepted him. I have refused him. I have told him that it

[067-065]was impossible."

[067-066]"And yet you wish him back again!" Nora again nodded her head.

[067-067]"That is a state of things I cannot at all understand," said Mrs.

[067-068]Trevelyan, "and would not believe unless you told me so yourself."

[067-069]"And you think me very wrong, of course. I will endeavour to do

[067-070]nothing wrong, but it is so. I have not said a word of encouragement

[067-071]to Mr. Stanbury; but I love him with all my heart. Ought I to tell

[067-072]you a lie when you question me? Or is it natural that I should never

[067-073]wish to see again a person whom I love better than all the world? It

[067-074]seems to me that a girl can hardly be right if she have any choice of

[067-075]her own. Here are two men, one rich and the other poor. I shall fall

[067-076]to the ground between them. I know that. I have fallen to the ground

[067-077]already. I like the one I can't marry. I don't care a straw for the

[067-078]one who could give me a grand house. That is falling to the ground.

[067-079]But I don't see that it is hard to understand, or that I have

[067-080]disgraced myself."

[067-081]"I said nothing of disgrace, Nora."

[067-082]"But you looked it."

[067-083]"I did not intend to look it, dearest."

[067-084]"And remember this, Emily, I have told you everything because you

[067-085]asked me. I do not mean to tell anybody else, at all. Mamma would not

[067-086]understand me. I have not told him, and I shall not."

[067-087]"You mean Mr. Stanbury?"

[067-088]"Yes; I mean Mr. Stanbury. As to Mr. Glascock, of course I shall tell

[067-089]mamma that. I have no secret there. That is his secret, and I suppose

[067-090]mamma should know it. But I will have nothing told about the other.

[067-091]Had I accepted him, or even hinted to him that I cared for him, I

[067-092]would tell mamma at once."

[067-093]After that there came something of a lecture, or something, rather,

[067-094]of admonition, from Mrs. Outhouse. That lady did not attempt to

[067-095]upbraid, or to find any fault; but observed that as she understood

[067-096]that Mr. Stanbury had no means whatever, and as Nora herself had

[067-097]none, there had better be no further intercourse between them, till,

[067-098]at any rate, Sir Marmaduke and Lady Rowley should be in [[london|London]]. "So

[067-099]I told him that he must not come here any more, my dear," said Mrs.

[067-100]Outhouse.

[067-101]"You are quite right, aunt. He ought not to come here."

[067-102]"I am so glad that you agree with me."

[067-103]"I agree with you altogether. I think I was bound to see him when he

[067-104]asked to see me; but the thing is altogether out of the question. I

[067-105]don't think he'll come any more, aunt." Then Mrs. Outhouse was quite

[067-106]satisfied that no harm had been done.

[067-107]A month had now passed since anything had been heard at St.

[067-108]Diddulph's from Mr. Trevelyan, and it seemed that many months might

[067-109]go on in the same dull way. When Mrs. Trevelyan first found herself

[067-110]in her uncle's house, a sum of two hundred pounds had been sent to

[067-111]her; and since that she had received a letter from her husband's

[067-112]lawyer saying that a similar amount would be sent to her every three

[067-113]months, as long as she was separated from her husband. A portion

[067-114]of this she had given over to Mr. Outhouse; but this pecuniary

[067-115]assistance by no means comforted that unfortunate [[social-class|gentleman]] in his

[067-116]trouble. "I don't want to get into debt," he said, "by keeping a lot

[067-117]of people whom I haven't the means to feed. And I don't want to board

[067-118]and lodge my nieces and their family at so much a head. It's very

[067-119]hard upon me either way." And so it was. All the comfort of his home

[067-120]was destroyed, and he was driven to sacrifice his [[personal-freedom|independence]] by

[067-121]paying his tradesmen with a portion of Mrs. Trevelyan's money. The

[067-122]more he thought of it all, and the more he discussed the matter with

[067-123]his wife, the more indignant they became with the truant husband. "I

[067-124]can't believe," he said, "but what Mr. Bideawhile could make him come

[067-125]back, if he chose to do his duty."

[067-126]"But they say that Mr. Trevelyan is in Italy, my dear."

[067-127]"And if I went to Italy, might I leave you to starve, and take my

[067-128]income with me?"

[067-129]"He doesn't leave her quite to starve, my dear."

[067-130]"But isn't a man bound to stay with his wife? I never heard of such a

[067-131]thing,--never. And I'm sure that there must be something wrong. A man

[067-132]can't go away and leave his wife to live with her uncle and aunt. It

[067-133]isn't right."

[067-134]"But what can we do?"

[067-135]Mr. Outhouse was forced to acknowledge that nothing could be done. He

[067-136]was a man to whom the quiescence of his own childless house was the

[067-137]one pleasure of his existence. And of that he was robbed because this

[067-138]wicked madman chose to neglect all his duties, and leave his wife

[067-139]without a house to shelter her. "Supposing that she couldn't have

[067-140]come here, what then?" said Mr. Outhouse. "I did tell him, as plain

[067-141]as words could speak, that we couldn't receive them." "But here they

[067-142]are," said Mrs. Outhouse, "and here they must remain till my brother

[067-143]comes to England." "It's the most monstrous thing that I ever heard

[067-144]of in all my life," said Mr. Outhouse. "He ought to be locked

[067-145]up;--that's what he ought."

[067-146]It was hard, and it became harder, when a gentleman, whom Mr.

[067-147]Outhouse certainly did not wish to see, called upon him about the

[067-148]latter end of September. Mr. Outhouse was sitting alone, in the

[067-149]gloomy parlour of his parsonage,--for his own study had been given

[067-150]up to other things, since this great inroad had been made upon his

[067-151]family;--he was sitting alone on one Saturday morning, preparing for

[067-152]the duties of the next day, with various manuscript sermons lying on

[067-153]the table around him, when he was told that a gentleman had called

[067-154]to see him. Had Mr. Outhouse been an incumbent at the West-end of

[067-155]London, or had his maid been a West-end servant, in all probability

[067-156]the gentleman's name would have been demanded; but Mr. Outhouse was a

[067-157]man who was not very ready in foreseeing and preventing misfortunes,

[067-158]and the girl who opened the door was not trained to discreet

[067-159]usages in such matters. As she announced the fact that there was a

[067-160]gentleman, she pointed to the door, to show that the gentleman was

[067-161]there; and before Mr. Outhouse had been able to think whether it

[067-162]would be prudent for him to make some preliminary inquiry, Colonel

[067-163]Osborne was in the room. Now, as it happened, these two men had never

[067-164]hitherto met each other, though one was the brother-in-law of Sir

[067-165]Marmaduke Rowley, and the other had been his very old friend. "My

[067-166]name, Mr. Outhouse, is [[colonel-osborne|Colonel Osborne]]," said the visitor, coming

[067-167]forward, with his hand out. The clergyman, of course, took his hand,

[067-168]and asked him to be seated. "We have known each other's names very

[067-169]long," continued the Colonel, "though I do not think we have ever yet

[067-170]had an opportunity of becoming acquainted."

[067-171][Illustration: At St. Diddulph's.]

[067-172]"No," said Mr. Outhouse; "we have never been acquainted, I believe."

[067-173]He might have added, that he had no desire whatever to make such

[067-174]acquaintance; and his manner, over which he himself had no control,

[067-175]did almost say as much. Indeed, this coming to his house of the

[067-176]suspected lover of his niece appeared to him to be a heavy addition

[067-177]to his troubles; for, although he was disposed to take his niece's

[067-178]part against her husband to any possible length,--even to the locking

[067-179]up of the husband as a madman, if it were possible,--nevertheless, he

[067-180]had almost as great a horror of the Colonel, as though the husband's

[067-181]allegation as to the lover had been true as gospel. Because Trevelyan

[067-182]had been wrong altogether, Colonel Osborne was not the less wrong.

[067-183]Because Trevelyan's suspicions were to Mr. Outhouse wicked and

[067-184]groundless, he did not the less regard the presumed lover to be an

[067-185]iniquitous roaring lion, going about seeking whom he might devour.

[067-186]Elderly unmarried men of fashion generally, and especially colonels,

[067-187]and majors, and members of parliament, and such like, were to him

[067-188]as black sheep or roaring lions. They were "fruges consumere nati;"

[067-189]men who stood on club doorsteps talking naughtily and doing nothing,

[067-190]wearing sleek clothing, for which they very often did not pay, and

[067-191]never going to church. It seemed to him,--in his ignorance,--that

[067-192]such men had none of the burdens of this world upon their shoulders,

[067-193]and that, therefore, they stood in great peril of the burdens of the

[067-194]next. It was, doubtless, his special duty to deal with men in such

[067-195]peril;--but those wicked ones with whom he was concerned were those

[067-196]whom he could reach. Now, the Colonel Osbornes of the earth were

[067-197]not to be got at by any clergyman, or, as far as Mr. Outhouse could

[067-198]see, by any means of grace. That story of the rich man and the camel

[067-199]seemed to him to be specially applicable to such people. How was such

[067-200]a one as Colonel Osborne to be shewn the way through the eye of a

[067-201]needle? To Mr. Outhouse, his own brother-in-law, Sir Marmaduke, was

[067-202]almost of the same class,--for he frequented clubs when in London,

[067-203]and played whist, and talked of the things of the world,--such as the

[067-204]Derby, and the levees, and West-end dinner parties,--as though they

[067-205]were all in all to him. He, to be sure, was weighted with so large

[067-206]a family that there might be hope for him. The eye of the needle

[067-207]could not be closed against him as a rich man; but he savoured of

[067-208]the West-end, and was worldly, and consorted with such men as this

[067-209]Colonel Osborne. When Colonel Osborne introduced himself to Mr.

[067-210]Outhouse, it was almost as though Apollyon had made his way into the

[067-211]parsonage of St. Diddulph's.
