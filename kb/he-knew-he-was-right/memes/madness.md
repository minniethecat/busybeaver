# Madness

The progressive mental deterioration of Louis Trevelyan in *He Knew He Was Right*, portrayed by Trollope as a condition that is simultaneously psychological, moral, and social. Trevelyan's "insane jealousy" (022-017) functions as the novel's central tragic mechanism.

## Overview

Madness in Trollope's novel is not a sudden break but a gradual descent. Trevelyan begins as a rational if anxious husband and ends as a man whose grip on reality has been entirely consumed by jealous delusion. The term "insane jealousy" recurs throughout the novel, marking the transition from ordinary marital suspicion to pathological obsession. The novel traces this trajectory with a clinical precision that anticipates later psychological realism while remaining grounded in Victorian moral and medical discourse.

## Context in Victorian Society

Victorian psychiatry was in a period of rapid development, with increasing attention to conditions that fell short of total insanity. The concept of "monomania" — a partial delusion in an otherwise rational mind — was widely discussed in medical and legal circles. Trevelyan's condition maps closely onto this category: he remains capable of managing his affairs, conversing rationally, and even recognizing his own condition, yet he is utterly in the grip of one fixed false belief. The novel also engages with the Victorian moral framework that treated madness as a form of moral failure — a weakness of will rather than a medical condition deserving of treatment.

## Manifestation in the Novel

### The Stages of Decline

Trevelyan's mental deterioration follows a discernible progression. Initially, he experiences ordinary jealousy that he attempts to control. The narrator notes that he was "ashamed of what he had done" (015-111) in confronting his wife, suggesting self-awareness of his irrationality. But the jealousy proves stronger than shame. He becomes increasingly isolated, withdrawing from friends, abandoning his London life, and eventually exiling himself to Italy. The physical deterioration mirrors the mental: by the novel's end, Trevelyan is a broken man in both mind and body.

### Self-Awareness and Helplessness

One of the novel's most psychologically acute features is Trevelyan's intermittent recognition of his own condition. He "did not cease to declare to himself that he knew himself to be a man blasted by jealousy, and yet he did not cease" to act on it (032-036). This self-awareness without self-control is the hallmark of Trollope's psychological realism. Trevelyan is not simply mad; he is a man watching himself go mad, unable to intervene.

### Social Madness

The novel also suggests that Trevelyan's madness is not purely internal but is produced and amplified by social conditions. Victorian marriage law gave husbands near-absolute authority; Victorian honour culture made a wife's perceived infidelity an existential threat to masculine identity. Trevelyan's madness, in this reading, is the logical endpoint of a social system that granted men power without requiring them to earn trust.

## Key Examples

### "Insane Jealousy"

The phrase "insane jealousy" appears at a crucial moment in the novel (022-017), marking the narrator's diagnostic judgment. The term bridges moral and medical discourse: "insane" suggests a clinical condition beyond the sufferer's control, while "jealousy" names a moral failing. This ambiguity — is Trevelyan sick or sinful? — runs throughout the novel and is never fully resolved.

### The Italian Exile

Trevelyan's self-imposed exile to Italy represents the spatial dimension of his mental deterioration. He removes himself from the social world that might correct his delusions, choosing isolation that feeds his condition. The Italian setting — traditionally associated with passion and irrationality in the English novel — reinforces the thematic association between foreignness and madness.

### The Failed Self-Cure

Trevelyan's attempts at self-mastery are among the novel's most poignant passages. He "tried with all his might to make the evidence of things disenchant him of his jealousy, but he tried with all his might in vain" (032-038). The repetition of "with all his might" underscores the paradox: the will that might cure him is the very faculty that has been compromised.
