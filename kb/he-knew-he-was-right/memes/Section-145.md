## Section 145

[145-001]She was quite happy when she was told that the marriage could not

[145-002]take place till August, but that Brooke must come again in July.

[145-003]Brooke did come in the first week of July, and somewhat horrified

[145-004]Dorothy by declaring to her that Miss Stanbury was unreasonable.

[145-005]"If I insist upon leaving [[london|London]] so often for a day or two," said he,

[145-006]"how am I to get anything like leave of absence when the time comes?"

[145-007]In answer to this Dorothy tried to make him understand that business

[145-008]should not be neglected, and that, as far as she was concerned, she

[145-009]could do very well without that trip abroad which he had proposed for

[145-010]her. "I'm not going to be done in that way," said Brooke. "And now

[145-011]that I am here she has nothing to say to me. I've told her a dozen

[145-012]times that I don't want to know anything about her will, and that

[145-013]I'll take it all for granted. There is something to be settled on

[145-014]you, that she calls her own."

[145-015]"She is so generous, Brooke."

[145-016]"She is generous enough, but she is very whimsical. She is going

[145-017]to make her whole will over again, and now she wants to send some

[145-018]message to Uncle Barty. I don't know what it is yet, but I am to take

[145-019]it. As far as I can understand, she has sent all the way to London

[145-020]for me, in order that I may take a message across the Close."

[145-021]"You talk as though it were very disagreeable, coming to [[exeter|Exeter]],"

[145-022]said Dorothy, with a little pout.

[145-023]"So it is,--very disagreeable."

[145-024]"Oh, Brooke!"

[145-025]"Very disagreeable if our marriage is to be put off by it. I think

[145-026]it will be so much nicer making love somewhere on the Rhine than

[145-027]having snatches of it here, and talking all the time about wills and

[145-028]tenements and settlements." As he said this, with his arm round her

[145-029]waist and his face quite close to hers,--shewing thereby that he was

[145-030]not altogether averse even to his present privileges,--she forgave

[145-031]him.

[145-032]On that same afternoon, just before the banking hours were over,

[145-033]Brooke went across to the house of Cropper and Burgess, having first

[145-034]been closeted for nearly an hour with his aunt,--and, as he went,

[145-035]his step was sedate and his air was serious. He found his uncle

[145-036]Barty, and was not very long in delivering his message. It was to

[145-037]this effect,--that Miss Stanbury particularly wished to see Mr.

[145-038]Bartholomew Burgess on business, at some hour on that afternoon

[145-039]or that evening. Brooke himself had been made acquainted with the

[145-040]subject in regard to which this singular interview was desired;

[145-041]but it was not a part of his duty to communicate any information

[145-042]respecting it. It had been necessary that his consent to certain

[145-043]arrangements should be asked before the invitation to Barty Burgess

[145-044]could be given; but his present mission was confined to an authority

[145-045]to give the invitation.

[145-046]Old Mr. Burgess was much surprised, and was at first disposed to

[145-047]decline the proposition made by the "old harridan," as he called her.

[145-048]He had never put any restraint on his language in talking of Miss

[145-049]Stanbury with his nephew, and was not disposed to do so now, because

[145-050]she had taken a new vagary into her head. But there was something in

[145-051]his nephew's manner which at last induced him to discuss the matter

[145-052]rationally.

[145-053]"And you don't know what it's all about?" said Uncle Barty.

[145-054]"I can't quite say that. I suppose I do know pretty well. At any

[145-055]rate, I know enough to think that you ought to come. But I must not

[145-056]say what it is."

[145-057]"Will it do me or anybody else any good?"

[145-058]"It can't do you any harm. She won't eat you."

[145-059]"But she can abuse me like a pickpocket, and I should return it, and

[145-060]then there would be a scolding match. I always have kept out of her

[145-061]way, and I think I had better do so still."

[145-062]Nevertheless Brooke prevailed,--or rather the feeling of curiosity

[145-063]which was naturally engendered prevailed. For very, very many years

[145-064]Barty Burgess had never entered or left his own house of business

[145-065]without seeing the door of that in which Miss Stanbury lived,--and

[145-066]he had never seen that door without a feeling of detestation for the

[145-067]owner of it. It would, perhaps, have been a more rational feeling on

[145-068]his part had he confined his hatred to the memory of his brother, by

[145-069]whose will Miss Stanbury had been enriched, and he had been, as he

[145-070]thought, impoverished. But there had been a contest, and litigation,

[145-071]and disputes, and contradictions, and a long course of those

[145-072]incidents in life which lead to rancour and ill blood, after the

[145-073]death of the former Brooke Burgess; and, as the result of all this,

[145-074]Miss Stanbury held the [[money|property]] and Barty Burgess held his hatred.

[145-075]He had never been ashamed of it, and had spoken his mind out to all

[145-076]who would hear him. And, to give Miss Stanbury her due, it must be

[145-077]admitted that she had hardly been behind him in the warmth of her

[145-078]expression,--of which old Barty was well aware. He hated, and knew

[145-079]that he was hated in return. And he knew, or thought that he knew,

[145-080]that his enemy was not a woman to relent because old age and weakness

[145-081]and the fear of death were coming on her. His enemy, with all her

[145-082]faults, was no coward. It could not be that now at the eleventh hour

[145-083]she should desire to reconcile him by any act of tardy justice,--nor

[145-084]did he wish to be reconciled at this the eleventh hour. His hatred

[145-085]was a pleasant excitement to him. His abuse of Miss Stanbury was a

[145-086]chosen recreation. His unuttered daily curse, as he looked over to

[145-087]her door, was a relief to him. Nevertheless he would go. As Brooke

[145-088]had said,--no harm could come of his going. He would go, and at least

[145-089]listen to her proposition.

[145-090][[About]] seven in the evening his knock was heard at the door. Miss

[145-091]Stanbury was sitting in the small up-stairs parlour, dressed in her

[145-092]second best gown, and was prepared with considerable stiffness and

[145-093]state for the occasion. Dorothy was with her, but was desired in a

[145-094]quick voice to hurry away the moment the knock was heard, as though

[145-095]old Barty would have jumped from the hall door into the room at

[145-096]a bound. Dorothy collected herself with a little start, and went

[145-097]without a word. She had heard much of Barty Burgess, but had never

[145-098]spoken to him, and was subject to a feeling of great awe when she

[145-099]would remember that the grim old man of whom she had heard so much

[145-100]evil would soon be her uncle. According to arrangement, Mr. Burgess

[145-101]was shewn up-stairs by his nephew. Barty Burgess had been born in

[145-102]this very house, but had not been inside the walls of it for more

[145-103]than thirty years. He also was somewhat awed by the occasion, and

[145-104]followed his nephew without a word. Brooke was to remain at hand,

[145-105]so that he might be summoned should he be wanted; but it had been

[145-106]decided by Miss Stanbury that he should not be present at the

[145-107]interview. As soon as her visitor entered the room she rose in a

[145-108]stately way, and curtseyed, propping herself with one hand upon the

[145-109]table as she did so. She looked him full in the face meanwhile, and

[145-110]curtseying a second time asked him to seat himself in a chair which

[145-111]had been prepared for him. She did it all very well, and it may be

[145-112]surmised that she had rehearsed the little scene, perhaps more than

[145-113]once, when nobody was looking at her. He bowed, and walked round to

[145-114]the chair and seated himself; but finding that he was so placed that

[145-115]he could not see his neighbour's face, he moved his chair. He was not

[145-116]going to fight such a duel as this with the disadvantage of the sun

[145-117]in his eyes.

[145-118][Illustration: Barty Burgess.]

[145-119]Hitherto there had hardly been a word spoken. Miss Stanbury had

[145-120]muttered something as she was curtseying, and Barty Burgess had made

[145-121]some return. Then she began: "Mr. Burgess," she said, "I am indebted

[145-122]to you for your complaisance in coming here at my request." To this

[145-123]he bowed again. "I should not have ventured thus to trouble you were

[145-124]it not that years are dealing more hardly with me than they are

[145-125]with you, and that I could not have ventured to discuss a matter

[145-126]of deep interest otherwise than in my own room." It was her room

[145-127]now, certainly, by law; but Barty Burgess remembered it when it was

[145-128]his mother's room, and when she used to give them all their meals

[145-129]there,--now so many, many years ago! He bowed again, and said not a

[145-130]word. He knew well that she could sooner be brought to her point by

[145-131]his silence than by his speech.

[145-132]She was a long time coming to her point. Before she could do so she

[145-133]was forced to allude to times long past, and to subjects which she

[145-134]found it very difficult to touch without saying that which would

[145-135]either belie herself, or seem to be severe upon him. Though she had

[145-136]prepared herself, she could hardly get the words spoken, and she was

[145-137]greatly impeded by the obstinacy of his silence. But at last her

[145-138]proposition was made to him. She told him that his nephew, Brooke,

[145-139]was about to be married to her niece, Dorothy; and that it was her

[145-140]intention to make Brooke her heir in the bulk of the property which

[145-141]she had received under the will of the late Mr. Brooke Burgess.

[145-142]"Indeed," she said, "all that I received at your brother's hands

[145-143]shall go back to your brother's family unimpaired." He only bowed,

[145-144]and would not say a word. Then she went on to say that it had at

[145-145]first been a matter to her of deep regret that Brooke should have set

[145-146]his affections upon her niece, as there had been in her mind a strong

[145-147]desire that none of her own people should enjoy the reversion of the

[145-148]wealth, which she had always regarded as being hers only for the

[145-149]term of her life; but that she had found that the young people had

[145-150]been so much in earnest, and that her own feeling had been so near

[145-151]akin to a prejudice, that she had yielded. When this was said Barty

[145-152]smiled instead of bowing, and Miss Stanbury felt that there might be

[145-153]something worse even than his silence. His smile told her that he

[145-154]believed her to be lying. Nevertheless she went on. She was not fool

[145-155]enough to suppose that the whole nature of the man was to be changed

[145-156]by a few words from her. So she went on. The marriage was a thing

[145-157]fixed, and she was thinking of settlements, and had been talking to

[145-158]lawyers about a new will.

[145-159]"I do not know that I can help you," said Barty, finding that a

[145-160]longer pause than usual made some word from him absolutely necessary.

[145-161]"I am going on to that, and I regret that my story should detain

[145-162]you so long, Mr. Burgess." And she did go on. She had, she said,

[145-163]made some saving out of her income. She was not going to trouble Mr.

[145-164]Burgess with this matter,--only that she might explain to him that

[145-165]what she would at once give to the young couple, and what she would

[145-166]settle on Dorothy after her own death, would all come from such

[145-167]savings, and that such gifts and bequests would not diminish the

[145-168]family property. Barty again smiled as he heard this, and Miss

[145-169]Stanbury in her heart likened him to the devil in person. But still

[145-170]she went on. She was very desirous that Brooke Burgess should

[145-171]come and live at Exeter. His property would be in the town and

[145-172]the neighbourhood. It would be a seemly thing,--such were her

[145-173]words,--that he should occupy the house that had belonged to his

[145-174]grandfather and his great-grandfather; and then, moreover,--she

[145-175]acknowledged that she spoke selfishly,--she dreaded the idea of being

[145-176]left alone for the remainder of her own years. Her proposition at

[145-177]last was uttered. It was simply this, that Barty Burgess should give

[145-178]to his nephew, Brooke, his share in the bank.
