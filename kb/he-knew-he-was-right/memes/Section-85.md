## Section 85

[085-001]"I don't suppose you regard yourself as an Arabella French," said he.

[085-002]"How angry Miss French would be if she heard you. She considers

[085-003]herself to be one of the reigning beauties of [[exeter|Exeter]]."

[085-004]"She has had a very long reign, and dominion of that sort to be

[085-005]successful ought to be short."

[085-006]"That is spiteful, Mr. Burgess."

[085-007]"I don't feel spiteful against her, poor woman. I own I do not love

[085-008]Camilla. Not that I begrudge Camilla her present prosperity."

[085-009]"Nor I either, Mr. Burgess."

[085-010]"She and Mr. Gibson will do very well together, I dare say."

[085-011]"I hope they will," said Dorothy, "and I do not see any reason

[085-012]against it. They have known each other a long time."

[085-013]"A very long time," said Brooke. Then he paused for a minute,

[085-014]thinking how he might best tell her that which he had now resolved

[085-015]should be told on this occasion. Dorothy finished her tea and got up

[085-016]as though she were about to go to her duty up-stairs. She had been as

[085-017]yet hardly an hour in the room, and the period of her relief was not

[085-018]fairly over. But there had come something of a personal flavour in

[085-019]their conversation which prompted her, unconsciously, to leave him.

[085-020]She had, without any special indication of herself, included herself

[085-021]among that company of old maids who are born and live and die without

[085-022]that vital interest in the affairs of life which nothing but family

[085-023]duties, the care of children, or at least of a husband, will give to

[085-024]a woman. If she had not meant this she had felt it. He had understood

[085-025]her meaning, or at least her feeling, and had taken upon himself to

[085-026]assure her that she was not one of the company whose privations she

[085-027]had endeavoured to describe. Her instinct rather than her reason put

[085-028]her at once upon her guard, and she prepared to leave the room. "You

[085-029]are not going yet," he said.

[085-030]"I think I might as well. Martha has so much to do, and she comes to

[085-031]me again at five in the morning."

[085-032]"Don't go quite yet," he said, pulling out his watch. "I know all

[085-033]about the hours, and it wants twenty minutes to the proper time."

[085-034]"There is no proper time, Mr. Burgess."

[085-035]"Then you can remain a few minutes longer. The fact is, I've got

[085-036]something I want to say to you."

[085-037]He was now [[reputation|standing]] between her and the door, so that she could not

[085-038]get away from him; but at this moment she was absolutely ignorant of

[085-039]his purpose, expecting nothing of love from him more than she would

[085-040]from Sir Peter Mancrudy. Her face had become flushed when she made

[085-041]her long speech, but there was no blush on it as she answered him

[085-042]now. "Of course, I can wait," she said, "if you have anything to say

[085-043]to me."

[085-044]"Well;--I have. I should have said it before, only that that other

[085-045]man was here." He was blushing now,--up to the roots of his hair, and

[085-046]felt that he was in a difficulty. There are men, to whom such moments

[085-047]of their lives are pleasurable, but Brooke Burgess was not one of

[085-048]them. He would have been glad to have had it done and over,--so that

[085-049]then he might take pleasure in it.

[085-050]"What man?" asked Dorothy, in perfect innocence.

[085-051]"Mr. Gibson, to be sure. I don't know that there is anybody else."

[085-052]"Oh, Mr. Gibson. He never comes here now, and I don't suppose he will

[085-053]again. Aunt Stanbury is so very angry with him."

[085-054]"I don't care whether he comes or not. What I mean is this. When I

[085-055]was here before, I was told that you were going--to marry him."

[085-056]"But I wasn't."

[085-057]"How was I to know that, when you didn't tell me? I certainly did

[085-058]know it after I came back from Dartmoor." He paused a moment, as

[085-059]though she might have a word to say. She had no word to say, and

[085-060]did not in the least know what was coming. She was so far from

[085-061]anticipating the truth, that she was composed and easy in her mind.

[085-062]"But all that is of no use at all," he continued. "When I was here

[085-063]before Miss Stanbury wanted you to marry Mr. Gibson; and, of course,

[085-064]I had nothing to say about it. Now I want you--to marry me."

[085-065]"Mr. Burgess!"

[085-066]"Dorothy, my darling, I love you better than all the world. I do,

[085-067]indeed." As soon as he had commenced his protestations he became

[085-068]profuse enough with them, and made a strong attempt to support them

[085-069]by the action of his hands. But she retreated from him step by step,

[085-070]till she had regained her chair by the tea-table, and there she

[085-071]seated herself,--safely, as she thought; but he was close to her,

[085-072]over her shoulder, still continuing his protestations, offering up

[085-073]his vows, and imploring her to reply to him. She, as yet, had not

[085-074]answered him by a word, save by that one half-terrified exclamation

[085-075]of his name. "Tell me, at any rate, that you believe me, when I

[085-076]assure you that I love you," he said. The room was going round with

[085-077]Dorothy, and the world was going round, and there had come upon her

[085-078]so strong a feeling of the disruption of things in general, that she

[085-079]was at the moment anything but happy. Had it been possible for her to

[085-080]find that the last ten minutes had been a dream, she would at this

[085-081]moment have wished that it might become one. A trouble had come upon

[085-082]her, out of which she did not see her way. To dive among the waters

[085-083]in warm weather is very pleasant; there is nothing pleasanter. But

[085-084]when the young swimmer first feels the thorough immersion of his

[085-085]plunge, there comes upon him a strong desire to be quickly out again.

[085-086]He will remember afterwards how joyous it was; but now, at this

[085-087]moment, the dry land is everything to him. So it was with Dorothy.

[085-088]She had thought of Brooke Burgess as one of those bright ones of the

[085-089]world, with whom everything is happy and pleasant, whom everybody

[085-090]loves, who may have whatever they please, whose lines have been laid

[085-091]in pleasant places. She thought of him as a man who might some day

[085-092]make some woman very happy as his wife. To be the wife of such a man

[085-093]was, in Dorothy's estimation, one of those blessed chances which come

[085-094]to some women, but which she never regarded as being within her own

[085-095]reach. Though she had thought much about him, she had never thought

[085-096]of him as a possible possession for herself; and now that he was

[085-097]offering himself to her, she was not at once made happy by his love.

[085-098]Her ideas of herself and of her life were all dislocated for the

[085-099]moment, and she required to be alone, that she might set herself in

[085-100]order, and try herself all over, and find whether her bones were

[085-101]broken. "Say that you believe me," he repeated.

[085-102][Illustration: The world was going round with Dorothy.]

[085-103]"I don't know what to say," she whispered.

[085-104]"I'll tell you what to say. Say at once that you will be my wife."

[085-105]"I can't say that, Mr. Burgess."

[085-106]"Why not? Do you mean that you cannot love me?"

[085-107]"I think, if you please, I'll go up to Aunt Stanbury. It is time for

[085-108]me; indeed it is; and she will be wondering, and Martha will be put

[085-109]out. Indeed I must go up."

[085-110]"And will you not answer me?"

[085-111]"I don't know what to say. You must give me a little time to

[085-112]consider. I don't quite think you're serious."

[085-113]"Heaven and earth!" began Brooke.

[085-114]"And I'm sure it would never do. At any rate, I must go now. I must,

[085-115]indeed."

[085-116]And so she escaped, and went up to her aunt's room, which she reached

[085-117]at ten minutes after her usual time, and before Martha had begun

[085-118]to be put out. She was very civil to Martha, as though Martha had

[085-119]been injured; and she put her hand on her aunt's arm, with a soft,

[085-120]caressing, apologetic touch, feeling conscious that she had given

[085-121]cause for offence. "What has he been saying to you?" said her aunt,

[085-122]as soon as Martha had closed the door. This was a question which

[085-123]Dorothy, certainly, could not answer. Miss Stanbury meant nothing

[085-124]by it,--nothing beyond a sick woman's desire that something of the

[085-125]conversation of those who were not sick should be retailed to her;

[085-126]but to Dorothy the question meant so much! How should her aunt have

[085-127]known that he had said anything? She sat herself down and waited,

[085-128]giving no answer to the question. "I hope he gets his meals

[085-129]comfortably," said Miss Stanbury.

[085-130]"I am sure he does," said Dorothy, infinitely relieved. Then, knowing

[085-131]how important it was that her aunt should sleep, she took up the

[085-132]volume of Jeremy Taylor, and, with so great a burden on her mind,

[085-133]she went on painfully and distinctly with the second sermon on the

[085-134][[marriage|Marriage]] Ring. She strove valiantly to keep her mind to the godliness

[085-135]of the discourse, so that it might be of some possible service to

[085-136]herself; and to keep her voice to the tone that might be of service

[085-137]to her aunt. Presently she heard the grateful sound which indicated

[085-138]her aunt's repose, but she knew of experience that were she to stop,

[085-139]the sound and the sleep would come to an end also. For a whole hour

[085-140]she persevered, reading the sermon of the Marriage Ring with such

[085-141]attention to the godly principles of the teaching as she could

[085-142]give,--with that terrible burden upon her mind.

[085-143]"Thank you;--thank you; that will do, my dear. Shut it up," said

[085-144]the sick woman. "It's time now for the draught." Then Dorothy moved

[085-145]quietly about the room, and did her nurse's work with soft hand, and

[085-146]soft touch, and soft tread. After that her aunt kissed her, and bade

[085-147]her sit down and sleep.

[085-148]"I'll go on reading, aunt, if you'll let me," said Dorothy. But

[085-149]Miss Stanbury, who was not a cruel woman, would have no more of the

[085-150]reading, and Dorothy's mind was left at [[personal-freedom|liberty]] to think of the

[085-151]proposition that had been made to her. To one resolution she came

[085-152]very quickly. The period of her aunt's illness could not be a proper

[085-153]time for marriage vows, or the amenities of love-making. She did not

[085-154]feel that he, being a man, had offended; but she was quite sure that

[085-155]were she, a woman, the niece of so kind an aunt, the nurse at the

[085-156]bed-side of such an invalid,--were she at such a time to consent to

[085-157]talk of love, she would never deserve to have a lover. And from this

[085-158]resolve she got great comfort. It would give her an excuse for making

[085-159]no more assured answer at present, and would enable her to reflect

[085-160]at leisure as to the reply she would give him, should he ever, by

[085-161]any chance, renew his offer. If he did not,--and probably he would

[085-162]not,--then it would have been very well that he should not have been

[085-163]made the victim of a momentary generosity. She had complained of the

[085-164]dulness of her life, and that complaint from her had produced his

[085-165]noble, kind, generous, dear, enthusiastic benevolence towards her.

[085-166]As she thought of it all,--and by degrees she took great pleasure in

[085-167]thinking of it,--her mind bestowed upon him all manner of eulogies.

[085-168]She could not persuade herself that he really loved her, and yet she

[085-169]was full at heart of gratitude to him for the expression of his love.

[085-170]And as for herself, could she love him? We who are looking on of

[085-171]course know that she loved him;--that from this moment there was

[085-172]nothing belonging to him, down to his shoe-tie, that would not be

[085-173]dear to her heart and an emblem so tender as to force a tear from

[085-174]her. He had already become her god, though she did not know it. She

[085-175]made comparisons between him and Mr. Gibson, and tried to convince

[085-176]herself that the judgment, which was always pronounced very clearly

[085-177]in Brooke's favour, came from anything but her heart. And thus

[085-178]through the long watches of the night she became very happy, feeling

[085-179]but not knowing that the whole aspect of the world was changed to her

[085-180]by those few words which her lover had spoken to her. She thought now

[085-181]that it would be consolation enough to her in future to know that

[085-182]such a man as Brooke Burgess had once asked her to be the partner of

[085-183]his life, and that it would be almost ungenerous in her to push her

[085-184]advantage further and attempt to take him at his word. Besides, there

[085-185]would be obstacles. Her aunt would dislike such a marriage for him,

[085-186]and he would be bound to obey her aunt in such a matter. She would

[085-187]not allow herself to think that she could ever become Brooke's wife,

[085-188]but nothing could rob her of the treasure of the offer which he had

[085-189]made her. Then Martha came to her at five o'clock, and she went to

[085-190]her bed to dream for an hour or two of Brooke Burgess and her future

[085-191]life.
