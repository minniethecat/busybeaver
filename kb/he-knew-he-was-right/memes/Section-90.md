[090-001]"He should have thought of that before. How am I to see people now?"

[090-002]"But when you are better, aunt--?"

[090-003]"How do I know that I shall ever be better? He isn't off with those

[090-004]people at Heavitree,--is he?"

[090-005]"I hope not, aunt."

[090-006]"Psha! A poor, weak, insufficient creature;--that's what he is.

[090-007]Mr. Jennings is worth twenty of him." Dorothy, though she put the

[090-008]question again in its most alluring form of Christian charity and

[090-009]forgiveness, could not induce her aunt to say that she would see

[090-010]Mr. Gibson. "How can I see him, when you know that Sir Peter has

[090-011]forbidden me to see anybody except Mrs. Clifford and Mr. Jennings?"

[090-012]Two days afterwards there was an uncomfortable little scene at

[090-013]Heavitree. It must, no doubt, have been the case, that the same train

[090-014]of circumstances which had produced Mr. Gibson's visit to the Close,

[090-015]produced also the scene in question. It was suggested by some who

[090-016]were attending closely to the matter that Mr. Gibson had already come

[090-017]to repent his engagement with Camilla French; and, indeed, there were

[090-018]those who pretended to believe that he was induced, by the prospect

[090-019]of Miss Stanbury's demise, to transfer his allegiance yet again, and

[090-020]to bestow his hand upon Dorothy at last. There were many in the city

[090-021]who could never be persuaded that Dorothy had refused him,--these

[090-022]being, for the most part, ladies in whose estimation the value of a

[090-023]husband was counted so great, and a beneficed clergyman so valuable

[090-024]among suitors, that it was to their thinking impossible that Dorothy

[090-025]Stanbury should in her sound senses have rejected such an offer. "I

[090-026]don't believe a bit of it," said Mrs. Crumbie to Mrs. Apjohn; "is

[090-027]it likely?" The ears of all the French family were keenly alive

[090-028]to rumours, and to rumours of rumours. Reports of these opinions

[090-029]respecting Mr. Gibson reached Heavitree, and had their effect.

[090-030]As long as Mr. Gibson was behaving well as a suitor, they were

[090-031]inoperative there. What did it matter to them how the prize might

[090-032]have been struggled for,--might still be struggled for elsewhere,

[090-033]while they enjoyed the consciousness of possession? But when the

[090-034]consciousness of possession became marred by a cankerous doubt, such

[090-035]rumours were very important. Camilla heard of the visit in the Close,

[090-036]and swore that she would have justice done her. She gave her mother

[090-037]to understand that, if any trick were played upon her, the diocese

[090-038]should be made to ring of it, in a fashion that would astonish them

[090-039]all, from the bishop downwards. Whereupon Mrs. French, putting much

[090-040]faith in her daughter's threats, sent for Mr. Gibson.

[090-041]"The truth is, Mr. Gibson," said Mrs. French, when the civilities of

[090-042]their first greeting had been completed, "my poor child is pining."

[090-043]"Pining, Mrs. French!"

[090-044]"Yes;--pining, Mr. Gibson. I am afraid that you little understand

[090-045]how sensitive is that young heart. Of course, she is your own now.

[090-046]To her thinking, it would be treason to you for her to indulge in

[090-047]conversation with any other [[social-class|gentleman]]; but, then, she expects that

[090-048]you should spend your evenings with her,--of course!"

[090-049]"But, Mrs. French,--think of my engagements, as a clergyman."

[090-050]"We know all about that, Mr. Gibson. We know what a clergyman's calls

[090-051]are. It isn't like a doctor's, Mr. Gibson."

[090-052]"It's very often worse, Mrs. French."

[090-053]"Why should you go calling in the Close, Mr. Gibson?" Here was the

[090-054]gist of the accusation.

[090-055]"Wouldn't you have me make my peace with a poor dying sister?"

[090-056]pleaded Mr. Gibson.

[090-057]"After what has occurred," said Mrs. French, shaking her head at him,

[090-058]"and while things are just as they are now, it would be more like an

[090-059]honest man of you to stay away. And, of course, Camilla feels it. She

[090-060]feels it very much;--and she won't put up with it neither."

[090-061]"I think this is the cruellest, cruellest thing I ever heard," said

[090-062]Mr. Gibson.

[090-063]"It is you that are cruel, sir."

[090-064]Then the wretched man turned at bay. "I tell you what it is, Mrs.

[090-065]French;--if I am treated in this way, I won't stand it. I won't,

[090-066]indeed. I'll go away. I'm not going to be suspected, nor yet blown

[090-067]up. I think I've behaved handsomely, at any rate to Camilla."

[090-068]"Quite so, Mr. Gibson, if you would come and see her on evenings,"

[090-069]said Mrs. French, who was falling back into her usual state of

[090-070]timidity.

[090-071]"But, if I'm to be treated in this way, I will go away. I've thought

[090-072]of it as it is. I've been already invited to go to Natal, and if I

[090-073]hear anything more of these accusations, I shall certainly make up

[090-074]my mind to go." Then he left the house, before Camilla could be down

[090-075]upon him from her perch on the landing-place.

[090-076]CHAPTER LV.

[090-077]THE REPUBLICAN BROWNING.

[090-078]Mr. Glascock had returned to Naples after his sufferings in the

[090-079]dining-room of the American Minister, and by the middle of February

[090-080]was back again in Florence. His father was still alive, and it was

[090-081]said that the old lord would now probably live through the winter.

[090-082]And it was understood that Mr. Glascock would remain in Italy. He

[090-083]had declared that he would pass his time between Naples, Rome, and

[090-084]Florence; but it seemed to his friends that Florence was, of the

[090-085]three, the most to his taste. He liked his room, he said, at the York

[090-086]Hotel, and he liked being in the capital. That was his own statement.

[090-087]His friends said that he liked being with Carry Spalding, the

[090-088]daughter of the American Minister; but none of them, then in Italy,

[090-089]were sufficiently intimate with him to express that opinion to

[090-090]himself.

[090-091]It had been expressed more than once to Carry Spalding. The world in

[090-092]general says such things to ladies more openly than it does to men,

[090-093]and the probability of a girl's success in [[marriage|matrimony]] is canvassed

[090-094]in her hearing by those who are nearest to her with a freedom which

[090-095]can seldom be used in regard to a man. A man's most intimate friend

[090-096]hardly speaks to him of the prospect of his marriage till he himself

[090-097]has told that the engagement exists. The lips of no living person had

[090-098]suggested to Mr. Glascock that the American girl was to become his

[090-099]wife; but a great deal had been said to Carry Spalding about the

[090-100]conquest she had made. Her uncle, her aunt, her sister, and her great

[090-101]friend Miss Petrie, the poetess,--the Republican Browning as she

[090-102]was called,--had all spoken to her about it frequently. Olivia had

[090-103]declared her conviction that the thing was to be. Miss Petrie had,

[090-104]with considerable eloquence, explained to her friend that that

[090-105]English title, which was but the clatter of a sounding brass, should

[090-106]be regarded as a drawback rather than as an advantage. Mrs. Spalding,

[090-107]who was no poetess, would undoubtedly have welcomed Mr. Glascock as

[090-108]her niece's husband with all an aunt's energy. When told by Miss

[090-109]Petrie that old Lord Peterborough was a tinkling cymbal she snapped

[090-110]angrily at her gifted countrywoman. But she was too honest a woman,

[090-111]and too conscious also of her niece's strength, to say a word to urge

[090-112]her on. Mr. Spalding as an American minister, with full powers at the

[090-113]court of a European sovereign, felt that he had full as much to give

[090-114]as to receive; but he was well inclined to do both. He would have

[090-115]been much pleased to talk about his nephew Lord Peterborough, and

[090-116]he loved his niece dearly. But by the middle of February he was

[090-117]beginning to think that the matter had been long enough in training.

[090-118]If the Honourable Glascock meant anything, why did he not speak

[090-119]out his mind plainly? The American Minister in such matters was

[090-120]accustomed to fewer ambages than were common in the circles among

[090-121]which Mr. Glascock had lived.

[090-122]In the meantime Caroline Spalding was suffering. She had allowed

[090-123]herself to think that Mr. Glascock intended to propose to her, and

[090-124]had acknowledged to herself that were he to do so she would certainly

[090-125]accept him. All that she had seen of him, since the day on which he

[090-126]had been courteous to her about the seat in the diligence, had been

[090-127]pleasant to her. She had felt the charm of his manner, his education,

[090-128]and his gentleness; and had told herself that with all her love for

[090-129]her own country, she would willingly become an Englishwoman for the

[090-130]sake of being that man's wife. But nevertheless the warnings of her

[090-131]great friend, the poetess, had not been thrown away upon her. She

[090-132]would put away from herself as far as she could any desire to become

[090-133]Lady Peterborough. There should be no bias in the man's favour on

[090-134]that score. The tinkling cymbal and the sounding brass should be

[090-135]nothing to her. But yet,--yet what a chance was there here for her?

[090-136]"They are dishonest, and rotten at the core," said Miss Petrie,

[090-137]trying to make her friend understand that a free American should

[090-138]under no circumstances place trust in an English aristocrat. "Their

[090-139]country, Carry, is a game played out, while we are still breasting

[090-140]the hill with our young lungs full of air." Carry Spalding was proud

[090-141]of her intimacy with the Republican Browning; but nevertheless she

[090-142]liked Mr. Glascock; and when Mr. Glascock had been ten days in

[090-143]Florence, on his third visit to the city, and had been four or five

[090-144]times at the embassy without expressing his intentions in the proper

[090-145]form, Carry Spalding began to think that she had better save herself

[090-146]from a heartbreak while salvation might be within her reach. She

[090-147]perceived that her uncle was gloomy and almost angry when he spoke of

[090-148]Mr. Glascock, and that her aunt was fretful with disappointment. The

[090-149]Republican Browning had uttered almost a note of triumph; and had it

[090-150]not been that Olivia persisted, Carry Spalding would have consented

[090-151]to go away with Miss Petrie to Rome. "The old stones are rotten too,"

[090-152]said the poetess; "but their dust tells no lies." That well known

[090-153]piece of hers--"Ancient Marbles, while ye crumble," was written at

[090-154]this time, and contained an occult reference to Mr. Glascock and her

[090-155]friend.

[090-156]But Livy Spalding clung to the alliance. She probably knew her

[090-157]sister's heart better than did the others; and perhaps also had a

[090-158]clearer insight into Mr. Glascock's [[honour|character]]. She was at any rate

[090-159]clearly of opinion that there should be no running away. "Either you

[090-160]do like him, or you don't. If you do, what are you to get by going to

[090-161]Rome?" said Livy.

[090-162]"I shall get quit of doubt and trouble."

[090-163]"I call that cowardice. I would never run away from a man, Carry.

[090-164]Aunt Sophie forgets that they don't manage these things in England

[090-165]just as we do."

[090-166]"I don't know why there should be a difference."

[090-167]"Nor do I;--only that there is. You haven't read so many of their

[090-168]novels as I have."

[090-169]"Who would ever think of learning to live out of an English novel?"

[090-170]said Carry.

[090-171]"I am not saying that. You may teach him to live how you like

[090-172]afterwards. But if you have anything to do with people it must be

[090-173]well to know what their manners are. I think the richer sort of

[090-174]people in England slide into these things more gradually than we do.

[090-175]You stand your ground, Carry, and hold your own, and take the goods

[090-176]the gods provide you." Though Caroline Spalding opposed her sister's

[090-177]arguments, and was particularly hard upon that allusion to "the

[090-178]richer sort of people,"--which, as she knew, Miss Petrie would have

[090-179]regarded as [[truth|evidence]] of reverence for sounding brasses and tinkling

[090-180]cymbals,--nevertheless she loved Livy dearly for what she said, and

[090-181]kissed the sweet counsellor, and resolved that she would for the

[090-182]present decline the invitation of the poetess. Then was Miss Petrie

[090-183]somewhat indignant with her friend, and threw out her scorn in those

[090-184]lines which have been mentioned.
