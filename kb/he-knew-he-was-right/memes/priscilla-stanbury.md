# Priscilla Stanbury

Priscilla Stanbury is the elder sister of Hugh and Dorothy Stanbury, living with her widowed mother at Nuncombe Putney. Sharp-tongued, fiercely independent, and unimpressed by the social pretensions that govern Exeter society, she provides one of the novel's most distinctive minor voices.

## Overview

Priscilla is introduced in the context of the Nuncombe Putney household, where she lives with her mother and, initially, her sister Dorothy. Unlike Dorothy's sweet temper, Priscilla's character is defined by strong opinions and an unwillingness to dissemble. "As Miss Priscilla Stanbury was also very fond of having her own way" (025-034) — this mild observation understates a personality that Trollope renders with evident relish.

## Role in the Novel

Priscilla serves as a foil to both her sister Dorothy and her aunt Miss Stanbury. Where Dorothy is compliant, Priscilla is resistant; where Miss Stanbury exercises authority through wealth, Priscilla asserts independence through refusal. Her most notable characteristic is her undisguised antipathy toward her wealthy aunt: "the chief characteristic of Priscilla Stanbury was her hatred for her aunt in Exeter" (030-159).

## Relationships

### Dorothy Stanbury

Priscilla's relationship with Dorothy is sisterly but marked by temperamental contrast. Where Dorothy accommodates, Priscilla challenges. When Dorothy is summoned to Exeter, Priscilla remains at Nuncombe Putney, and the separation underscores their different paths.

### Miss Stanbury

Priscilla's "hatred for her aunt" (030-159) is a defining feature. Unlike Dorothy, who submits to Miss Stanbury's authority, Priscilla refuses to court favour. This resistance is presented not as villainy but as a form of integrity — however prickly.

### Mrs. Stanbury

Priscilla lives with her mother at Nuncombe Putney, sharing the domestic burdens of the reduced Stanbury household. Their relationship is one of practical coexistence rather than dramatic tension.

## Key Scenes

### The Nuncombe Putney Household

The Stanbury cottage at Nuncombe Putney is the setting for Priscilla's most characteristic moments. Her plain speaking and domestic competence define the household's atmosphere (025-050).

### Rejecting Exeter

Priscilla's refusal to seek her aunt's favour — and her open hostility toward Miss Stanbury — marks her as one of the few characters in the novel who refuses to be governed by money.

## Character Development

Priscilla is a static character by design: she does not change because she refuses to be changed. Her consistency — in opinion, in loyalty to her mother, in hostility to her aunt — is her defining trait. Trollope presents this stubbornness as admirable, a form of authenticity in a social world that rewards performance.
