## Section 84

[084-001]Brooke Burgess came, and had an interview with Sir Peter, and to

[084-002]him Sir Peter was under some [[duty|obligation]] to speak plainly, as being

[084-003]the person whom Miss Stanbury recognised as her heir. So Sir Peter

[084-004]declared that his patient might perhaps live, and perhaps might die.

[084-005]"The truth is, Mr. Burgess," said Sir Peter, "a doctor doesn't know

[084-006]so very much more about these things than other people." It was

[084-007]understood that Brooke was to remain three days in [[exeter|Exeter]], and then

[084-008]return to [[london|London]]. He would, of course, come again if--if anything

[084-009]should happen. Sir Peter had been quite clear in his opinion, that no

[084-010]immediate result was to be anticipated,--either in the one direction

[084-011]or the other. His patient was doomed to a long illness; she might get

[084-012]over it, or she might succumb to it.

[084-013]Dorothy and Brooke were thus thrown much together during these three

[084-014]days. Dorothy, indeed, spent most of her hours beside her aunt's bed,

[084-015]instigating sleep by the reading of a certain series of sermons in

[084-016]which Miss Stanbury had great faith; but nevertheless, there were

[084-017]some minutes in which she and Brooke were necessarily together. They

[084-018]eat their meals in each other's company, and there was a period in

[084-019]the evening, before Dorothy began her night-watch in her aunt's room,

[084-020]at which she took her tea while Martha was nurse in the room above.

[084-021]At this time of the day she would remain an hour or more with Brooke;

[084-022]and a great deal may be said between a man and a woman in an hour

[084-023]when the will to say it is there. Brooke Burgess had by no means

[084-024]changed his mind since he had declared it to [[hugh-stanbury|Hugh Stanbury]] under the

[084-025]midnight lamps of Long Acre, when warmed by the influence of oysters

[084-026]and whisky toddy. The whisky toddy had in that instance brought out

[084-027]truth and not falsehood,--as is ever the nature of whisky toddy and

[084-028]similar dangerous provocatives. There is no saying truer than that

[084-029]which declares that there is truth in wine. Wine is a dangerous

[084-030]thing, and should not be made the exponent of truth, let the truth

[084-031]be good as it may; but it has the merit of forcing a man to show his

[084-032]true colours. A man who is a [[social-class|gentleman]] in his cups may be trusted to

[084-033]be a gentleman at all times. I trust that the severe censor will not

[084-034]turn upon me, and tell me that no gentleman in these days is ever to

[084-035]be seen in his cups. There are cups of different degrees of depth;

[084-036]and cups do exist, even among gentlemen, and seem disposed to hold

[084-037]their own let the censor be ever so severe. The gentleman in his cups

[084-038]is a gentleman always; and the man who tells his friend in his cups

[084-039]that he is in love, does so because the fact has been very present

[084-040]to himself in his cooler and calmer moments. Brooke Burgess, who

[084-041]had seen Hugh Stanbury on two or three occasions since that of the

[084-042]oysters and toddy, had not spoken again of his regard for Hugh's

[084-043]sister; but not the less was he determined to carry out his plan and

[084-044]make Dorothy his wife if she would accept him. But could he ask her

[084-045]while the old lady was, as it might be, dying in the house? He put

[084-046]this question to himself as he travelled down to Exeter, and had told

[084-047]himself that he must be guided for an answer by circumstances as

[084-048]they might occur. Hugh had met him at the station as he started for

[084-049]Exeter, and there had been a consultation between them as to the

[084-050][[social-custom|propriety]] of bringing about, or of attempting to bring about, an

[084-051]interview between Hugh and his aunt. "Do whatever you like," Hugh had

[084-052]said. "I would go down to her at a moment's warning, if she should

[084-053]express a desire to see me."

[084-054]On the first night of Brooke's arrival this question had been

[084-055]discussed between him and Dorothy. Dorothy had declared herself

[084-056]unable to give advice. If any message were given to her she would

[084-057]deliver it to her aunt, but she thought that anything said to her

[084-058]aunt on the subject had better come from Brooke himself. "You

[084-059]evidently are the person most important to her," Dorothy said, "and

[084-060]she would listen to you when she would not let any one else say a

[084-061]word." Brooke promised that he would think of it; and then Dorothy

[084-062]tripped up to relieve Martha, dreaming nothing at all of that other

[084-063]doubt to which the important personage downstairs was now subject.

[084-064]Dorothy was, in truth, very fond of the new friend she had made; but

[084-065]it had never occurred to her that he might be a possible suitor to

[084-066]her. Her old conception of herself,--that she was beneath the notice

[084-067]of any man,--had only been partly disturbed by the absolute fact of

[084-068]Mr. Gibson's courtship. She had now heard of his engagement with

[084-069]Camilla French, and saw in that complete [[truth|proof]] that the foolish man

[084-070]had been induced to offer his hand to her by the promise of her

[084-071]aunt's money. If there had been a moment of exaltation,--a period in

[084-072]which she had allowed herself to think that she was, as other women,

[084-073]capable of making herself dear to a man,--it had been but a moment.

[084-074]And now she rejoiced greatly that she had not acceded to the wishes

[084-075]of one to whom it was so manifest that she had not made herself in

[084-076]the least dear.

[084-077]On the second day of his visit, Brooke was summoned to Miss

[084-078]Stanbury's room at noon. She was forbidden to talk, and during a

[084-079]great portion of the day could hardly speak without an effort; but

[084-080]there would be half hours now and again in which she would become

[084-081]stronger than usual, at which time nothing that Martha and Dorothy

[084-082]could say would induce her to hold her tongue. When Brooke came to

[084-083]her on this occasion he found her sitting up in bed with a great

[084-084]shawl round her; and he at once perceived she was much more like her

[084-085]own self than on the former day. She told him that she had been an

[084-086]old fool for sending for him, that she had nothing special to say

[084-087]to him, that she had made no alteration in her will in regard to

[084-088]him,--"except that I have done something for Dolly that will have to

[084-089]come out of your pocket, Brooke." Brooke declared that too much could

[084-090]not be done for a person so good, and dear, and excellent as Dorothy

[084-091]Stanbury, let it come out of whose pocket it might. "She is nothing

[084-092]to you, you know," said Miss Stanbury.

[084-093]"She is a great deal to me," said Brooke.

[084-094]"What is she?" asked Miss Stanbury.

[084-095]"Oh;--a friend; a great friend."

[084-096]"Well; yes. I hope it may be so. But she won't have anything that

[084-097]I haven't saved," said Miss Stanbury. "There are two houses at St.

[084-098]Thomas's; but I bought them myself, Brooke;--out of the [[money|income]]."

[084-099]Brooke could only declare that as the whole property was hers, to do

[084-100]what she liked with it as completely as though she had inherited it

[084-101]from her own father, no one could have any right to ask questions

[084-102]as to when or how this or that portion of the property had accrued.

[084-103]"But I don't think I'm going to die yet, Brooke," she said. "If it is

[084-104]God's will, I am ready. Not that I'm fit, Brooke. God forbid that I

[084-105]should ever think that. But I doubt whether I shall ever be fitter. I

[084-106]can go without repining if He thinks best to take me." Then he stood

[084-107]up by her bedside, with his hand upon hers, and after some hesitation

[084-108]asked her whether she would wish to see her nephew Hugh. "No," said

[084-109]she, sharply. Brooke went on to say how pleased Hugh would have been

[084-110]to come to her. "I don't think much of death-bed reconciliations,"

[084-111]said the old woman, grimly. "I loved him dearly, but he didn't love

[084-112]me, and I don't know what good we should do each other." Brooke

[084-113]declared that Hugh did love her; but he could not press the matter,

[084-114]and it was dropped.

[084-115]On that evening at eight Dorothy came down to her tea. She had dined

[084-116]at the same table with Brooke that afternoon, but a servant had been

[084-117]in the room all the time and nothing had been said between them. As

[084-118]soon as Brooke had got his tea he began to tell the story of his

[084-119]failure about Hugh. He was sorry, he said, that he had spoken on the

[084-120]subject, as it had moved Miss Stanbury to an acrimony which he had

[084-121]not expected.

[084-122]"She always declares that he never loved her," said Dorothy. "She has

[084-123]told me so twenty times."

[084-124]"There are people who fancy that nobody cares for them," said Brooke.

[084-125]"Indeed there are, Mr. Burgess; and it is so natural."

[084-126]"Why natural?"

[084-127]"Just as it is natural that there should be dogs and cats that are

[084-128]petted and loved and made much of, and others that have to crawl

[084-129]through life as they can, cuffed and kicked and starved."

[084-130]"That depends on the accident of possession," said Brooke.

[084-131]"So does the other. How many people there are that don't seem to

[084-132]belong to anybody,--and if they do, they're no good to anybody.

[084-133]They're not cuffed exactly, or starved; but--"

[084-134]"You mean that they don't get their share of affection?"

[084-135]"They get perhaps as much as they deserve," said Dorothy.

[084-136]"Because they're cross-grained, or ill-tempered, or disagreeable?"

[084-137]"Not exactly that."

[084-138]"What then?" asked Brooke.

[084-139]"Because they're just nobodies. They are not anything particular to

[084-140]anybody, and so they go on living till they die. You know what I

[084-141]mean, Mr. Burgess. A man who is a nobody can perhaps make himself

[084-142]somebody,--or, at any rate, he can try; but a woman has no means of

[084-143]trying. She is a nobody, and a nobody she must remain. She has her

[084-144]clothes and her food, but she isn't wanted anywhere. People put up

[084-145]with her, and that is about the best of her luck. If she were to die

[084-146]somebody perhaps would be sorry for her, but nobody would be worse

[084-147]off. She doesn't earn anything or do any good. She is just there and

[084-148]that's all."

[084-149]Brooke had never heard her speak after this fashion before, had never

[084-150]known her to utter so many consecutive words, or to put forward any

[084-151]opinion of her own with so much vigour. And Dorothy herself, when she

[084-152]had concluded her speech, was frightened by her own energy and grew

[084-153]red in the face, and showed very plainly that she was half ashamed

[084-154]of herself. Brooke thought that he had never seen her look so pretty

[084-155]before, and was pleased by her enthusiasm. He understood perfectly

[084-156]that she was thinking of her own position, though she had entertained

[084-157]no idea that he would so read her meaning; and he felt that it was

[084-158]incumbent on him to undeceive her, and make her know that she was not

[084-159]one of those women who are "just there and that's all." "One does see

[084-160]such a woman as that now and again," he said.

[084-161]"There are hundreds of them," said Dorothy. "And of course it can't

[084-162]be helped."

[084-163]"Such as Arabella French," said he, laughing.

[084-164]"Well,--yes; if she is one. It is very easy to see the difference.

[084-165]Some people are of use and are always doing things. There are others,

[084-166]generally women, who have nothing to do, but who can't be got rid of.

[084-167]It is a melancholy sort of feeling."

[084-168]"You at least are not one of them."

[084-169]"I didn't mean to complain about myself," she said. "I have got a

[084-170]great deal to make me happy."
