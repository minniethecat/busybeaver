## Section 159

[159-001][[About]] the middle of September there came a very happy time for her,

[159-002]when Hugh was asked down to shoot partridges,--in the doing of which,

[159-003]however, all his brightness did not bring him near in excellence to

[159-004]his host. Lord Peterborough had been shooting partridges all his

[159-005]life, and shot them with a precision which excited Hugh's envy. To

[159-006]own the truth, Stanbury did not shoot well, and was treated rather

[159-007]with scorn by the gamekeeper; but in other respects he spent three or

[159-008]four of the happiest days of his life. He had his work to do, and

[159-009]after the second day over the stubbles, declared that the exigencies

[159-010]of the D. R. were too severe to enable him to go out with his gun

[159-011]again; but those rambles about the park with Nora, for which, among

[159-012]the exigencies of the D. R., he did find opportunity, were never to

[159-013]be forgotten.

[159-014]"Of course I remember that it might have been mine," she said,

[159-015]sitting with him under an old, hollow, withered sloping stump of an

[159-016]oak, which still, however, had sufficient of a head growing from one

[159-017]edge of the trunk to give them the shade they wanted; "and if you

[159-018]wish me to own to regrets,--I will."

[159-019]"It would kill me, I think, if you did; and yet I cannot get it out

[159-020]of my head that if it had not been for me your [[social-class|rank]] and position in

[159-021]life might have been so--so suitable to you."

[159-022]"No, Hugh; there you're wrong. I have thought about it a good deal,

[159-023]too; and I know very well that the cold beef-steak in the cupboard is

[159-024]the thing for me. Caroline will do very well here. She looks like a

[159-025]peeress, and bears her honours grandly; but they will never harden

[159-026]her. I, too, could have been magnificent with fine feathers. Most

[159-027]birds are equal to so much as that. I fancy that I could have

[159-028]looked the part of the fine English lady, and could have patronised

[159-029]clergymen's wives in the country, could have held my own among my

[159-030]peers in [[london|London]], and could have kept Mrs. Crutch in order; but it

[159-031]would have hardened me, and I should have learned to think that to be

[159-032]a lady of fashion was everything."

[159-033]"I do not believe a bit of it."

[159-034]"It is better as it is, Hugh;--for me at least. I had always a sort

[159-035]of conviction that it would be better, though I had a longing to play

[159-036]the other part. Then you came, and you have saved me. Nevertheless,

[159-037]it is very nice, Hugh, to have the oaks to sit under." Stanbury

[159-038]declared that it was very nice.

[159-039][Illustration: Monkhams.]

[159-040]But still nothing was settled about the wedding. Trevelyan's

[159-041]condition was so uncertain that it was very difficult to settle

[159-042]anything. Though nothing was said on the subject between Stanbury

[159-043]and Mrs. Trevelyan, and nothing written between Nora and her sister,

[159-044]it could not but be remembered that should Trevelyan die, his widow

[159-045]would require a home with them. They were deterred from choosing a

[159-046]house by this reflection, and were deterred from naming a day also by

[159-047]the consideration that were they to do so, Trevelyan's state might

[159-048]still probably prevent it. But this was arranged, that if Trevelyan

[159-049]lived through the winter, or even if he should not live, their

[159-050]marriage should not be postponed beyond the end of March. Till

[159-051]that time Lord Peterborough would remain at Monkhams, and it was

[159-052]understood that Nora's invitation extended to that period.

[159-053]"If my wife does not get tired of you, I shall not," Lord

[159-054]Peterborough said to Nora. "The thing is that when you do go we shall

[159-055]miss you so terribly." In September, too, there happened another

[159-056]event which took Stanbury to [[exeter|Exeter]], and all needful particulars as

[159-057]to that event shall be narrated in the next chapter.

[159-058]CHAPTER XCVII.

[159-059]MRS. BROOKE BURGESS.

[159-060]It may be doubted whether there was a happier young woman in England

[159-061]than Dorothy Stanbury when that September came which was to make

[159-062]her the wife of Mr. Brooke Burgess, the new partner in the firm of

[159-063]Cropper and Burgess. Her early aspirations in life had been so low,

[159-064]and of late there had come upon her such a succession of soft showers

[159-065]of success,--mingled now and then with slight threatenings of storms

[159-066]which had passed away,--that the Close at Exeter seemed to her to

[159-067]have become a very Paradise. Her aunt's temper had sometimes been to

[159-068]her as the threat of a storm, and there had been the Gibson marriage

[159-069]treaty, and the short-lived opposition to the other marriage treaty

[159-070]which had seemed to her to be so very preferable; but everything had

[159-071]gone at last as though she had been Fortune's favourite,--and now

[159-072]had come this beautiful arrangement about Cropper and Burgess, which

[159-073]would save her from being carried away to live among strangers in

[159-074]London! When she first became known to us on her coming to Exeter,

[159-075]in compliance with her aunt's suggestion, she was timid, silent,

[159-076]and altogether without self-reliance. Even they who knew her best

[159-077]had never guessed that she possessed a keen sense of humour, a nice

[159-078]appreciation of [[honour|character]], and a quiet reticent wit of her own, under

[159-079]that staid and frightened demeanour. Since her engagement with Brooke

[159-080]Burgess it seemed to those who watched her that her [[reputation|character]] had

[159-081]become changed, as does that of a flower when it opens itself in its

[159-082]growth. The sweet gifts of nature within became visible, the petals

[159-083]sprang to view, and the leaves spread themselves, and the sweet scent

[159-084]was felt upon the air. Had she remained at Nuncombe, it is probable

[159-085]that none would ever have known her but her sister. It was necessary

[159-086]to this flower that it should be warmed by the sun of life, and

[159-087]strengthened by the breezes of opposition, and filled by the showers

[159-088]of companionship, before it could become aware of its own loveliness.

[159-089]Dorothy was one who, had she remained ever unseen in the retirement

[159-090]of her mother's village cottage, would have lived and died ignorant

[159-091]of even her own capabilities for enjoyment. She had not dreamed that

[159-092]she could win a man's love,--had hardly dreamed till she had lived at

[159-093]Exeter that she had love of her own to give back in return. She had

[159-094]not known that she could be firm in her own opinion, that she could

[159-095]laugh herself and cause others to laugh, that she could be a lady and

[159-096]know that other women were not so, that she had good looks of her own

[159-097]and could be very happy when told of them by lips that she loved. The

[159-098]flower that blows the quickest is never the sweetest. The fruit that

[159-099]ripens tardily has ever the finest flavour. It is often the same with

[159-100]men and women. The lad who talks at twenty as men should talk at

[159-101]thirty, has seldom much to say worth the hearing when he is forty;

[159-102]and the girl who at eighteen can shine in society with composure, has

[159-103]generally given over shining before she is a full-grown woman. With

[159-104]Dorothy the scent and beauty of the flower, and the flavour of the

[159-105]fruit, had come late; but the fruit will keep, and the flower will

[159-106]not fall to pieces with the heat of an evening.

[159-107]"How marvellously your bride has changed since she has been here,"

[159-108]said Mrs. MacHugh to Miss Stanbury. "We thought she couldn't say boo

[159-109]to a goose at first; but she holds her own now among the best of

[159-110]'em."

[159-111]"Of course she does;--why shouldn't she? I never knew a Stanbury yet

[159-112]that was a fool."

[159-113]"They are a wonderful family, of course," said Mrs. MacHugh; "but I

[159-114]think that of all of them she is the most wonderful. Old Barty said

[159-115]something to her at my house yesterday that wasn't intended to be

[159-116]kind."

[159-117]"When did he ever intend to be kind?"

[159-118]"But he got no change out of her. 'The Burgesses have been in Exeter

[159-119]a long time,' she said, 'and I don't see why we should not get on at

[159-120]any rate as well as those before us.' Barty grunted and growled and

[159-121]slunk away. He thought she would shake in her shoes when he spoke to

[159-122]her."

[159-123]"He has never been able to make a Stanbury shake in her shoes yet,"

[159-124]said the old lady.

[159-125]Early in September, Dorothy went to [[nuncombe-putney|Nuncombe Putney]] to spend a week

[159-126]with her mother and sister at the cottage. She had insisted on this,

[159-127]though Priscilla had hinted, somewhat unnecessarily, that Dorothy,

[159-128]with her past comforts and her future prospects, would find the

[159-129]accommodation at the cottage very limited. "I suppose you and I,

[159-130]Pris, can sleep in the same bed, as we always did," she said, with a

[159-131]tear in each eye. Then Priscilla had felt ashamed of herself, and had

[159-132]bade her come.

[159-133]"The truth is, Dolly," said the elder sister, "that we feel so unlike

[159-134]marrying and giving in marriage at Nuncombe, that I'm afraid you'll

[159-135]lose your brightness and become dowdy, and grim, and misanthropic, as

[159-136]we are. When mamma and I sit down to what we call dinner, I always

[159-137]feel that there is a grace hovering in the air different to that

[159-138]which she says."

[159-139]"And what is it, Pris?"

[159-140]"Pray, God, don't quite starve us, and let everybody else have

[159-141]indigestion. We don't say it out loud, but there it is; and the

[159-142]spirit of it might damp the orange blossoms."

[159-143]She went of course, and the orange blossoms were not damped. She had

[159-144]long walks with her sister round by Niddon and Ridleigh, and even as

[159-145]far distant as Cockchaffington, where much was said about that wicked

[159-146]Colonel as they stood looking at the porch of the church. "I shall be

[159-147]so happy," said Dorothy, "when you and mother come to us. It will be

[159-148]such a joy to me that you should be my guests."

[159-149]"But we shall not come."

[159-150]"Why not, Priscilla?"

[159-151]"I know it will be so. Mamma will not care for going, if I do not

[159-152]go."

[159-153]"And why should you not come?"

[159-154]"For a hundred reasons, all of which you know, Dolly. I am stiff,

[159-155]impracticable, ill-conditioned, and very bad at going about visiting.

[159-156]I am always thinking that other people ought to have indigestion, and

[159-157]perhaps I might come to have some such feeling about you and Brooke."

[159-158]"I should not be at all afraid of that."

[159-159]"I know that my place in the world is here, at Nuncombe Putney. I

[159-160]have a pride about myself, and think that I never did wrong but

[159-161]once,--when I let mamma go into that odious [[clock-house|Clock House]]. It is a bad

[159-162]pride, and yet I'm proud of it. I haven't got a gown fit to go and

[159-163]stay with you, when you become a grand lady in Exeter. I don't doubt

[159-164]you'd give me any sort of gown I wanted."

[159-165]"Of course I would. Ain't we sisters, Pris?"

[159-166]"I shall not be so much your sister as he will be your husband.

[159-167]Besides, I hate to take things. When Hugh sends money, and for

[159-168]mamma's sake it is accepted, I always feel uneasy while it lasts, and

[159-169]think that that plague of an indigestion ought to come upon me also.

[159-170]Do you remember the lamb that came when you went away? It made me so

[159-171]sick."

[159-172]"But, Priscilla;--isn't that morbid?"

[159-173]"Of course it is. You don't suppose I really think it grand. I am

[159-174]morbid. But I am strong enough to live on, and not get killed by the

[159-175]morbidity. Heaven knows how much more there may be of it;--forty

[159-176]years, perhaps, and probably the greater portion of that absolutely

[159-177]alone;--"

[159-178]"No;--you'll be with us then,--if it should come."

[159-179]"I think not, Dolly. Not to have a hole of my own would be

[159-180]intolerable to me. But, as I was saying, I shall not be unhappy. To

[159-181]enjoy life, as you do, is I suppose out of the question for me. But

[159-182]I have a satisfaction when I get to the end of the quarter and find

[159-183]that there is not half-a-crown due to any one. Things get dearer and

[159-184]dearer, but I have a comfort even in that. I have a feeling that I

[159-185]should like to bring myself to the straw a day." Of course there

[159-186]were offers made of aid,--offers which were rather prayers,--and

[159-187]plans suggested of what might be done between Brooke and Hugh; but

[159-188]Priscilla declared that all such plans were odious to her. "Why

[159-189]should you be unhappy about us?" she continued. "We will come and see

[159-190]you,--at least I will,--perhaps once in six months, and you shall pay

[159-191]for the railway ticket; only I won't stay, because of the gown."
