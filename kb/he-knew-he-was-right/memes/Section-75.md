## Section 75

[075-001]For your wife's sake, knowing that she is in a great

[075-002]degree still in your power, I will condescend to tell

[075-003]you what has happened. When Mrs. Trevelyan found herself

[075-004]constrained to leave [[nuncombe-putney|Nuncombe Putney]] by your aspersions

[075-005]on her [[honour|character]], she came here, to the protection of her

[075-006]nearest relatives within reach, till her father and mother

[075-007]should be in England. Sorely against my will I received

[075-008]them into my home, because they had been deprived of other

[075-009]shelter by the cruelty or madness of him who should have

[075-010]been their guardian. Here they are, and here they shall

[075-011]remain till Sir Marmaduke Rowley arrives. The other day,

[075-012]on the 29th of September, [[colonel-osborne|Colonel Osborne]], who is their

[075-013]father's old friend, called, not on them, but on me. I may

[075-014]truly say that I did not wish to see Colonel Osborne. They

[075-015]did not see him, nor did he ask to see them. If his coming

[075-016]was a fault,--and I think it was a fault,--they were

[075-017]not implicated in it. He came, remained a few minutes,

[075-018]and went without seeing any one but myself. That is the

[075-019]history of Colonel Osborne's visit to my house.

[075-020]I have not thought fit to show your letter to your wife,

[075-021]or to make her acquainted with this further [[truth|proof]] of your

[075-022]want of reason. As to the threats which you hold out

[075-023]of removing her child from her, you can of course do

[075-024]nothing except by law. I do not think that even you will

[075-025]be sufficiently audacious to take any steps of that

[075-026]description. Whatever protection the law may give her

[075-027]and her child from your tyranny and misconduct cannot be

[075-028]obtained till her father shall be here.

[075-029]I have only further to request that you will not address

[075-030]any further communication to me. Should you do so, it will

[075-031]be refused.

[075-032]Yours in deep indignation,

[075-033]OLIPHANT OUTHOUSE.

[075-034]Trevelyan had also written two other letters to England, one to Mr.

[075-035]Bideawhile and the other to Bozzle. In the former he acquainted the

[075-036]lawyer that he had discovered that his wife still maintained her

[075-037]intercourse with Colonel Osborne, and that he must therefore remove

[075-038]his child from her custody. He then inquired what steps would be

[075-039]necessary to enable him to obtain possession of his little boy.

[075-040]In the letter to Bozzle he sent a cheque, and his thanks for the

[075-041]ex-policeman's watchful care. He desired Bozzle to continue his

[075-042]precautions, and explained his intentions about his son. Being

[075-043]somewhat afraid that Mr. Bideawhile might not be zealous on his

[075-044]behalf, and not himself understanding accurately the extent of his

[075-045]power with regard to his own child, or the means whereby he might

[075-046]exercise it, he was anxious to obtain assistance from Bozzle also on

[075-047]this point. He had no doubt that Bozzle knew all about it. He had

[075-048]great confidence in Bozzle. But still he did not like to consult the

[075-049]ex-policeman. He knew that it became him to have some regard for his

[075-050]own [[pride|dignity]]. He therefore put the matter very astutely to Bozzle,

[075-051]asking no questions, but alluding to his difficulty in a way that

[075-052]would enable Bozzle to offer advice.

[075-053]And where was he to get a woman to take charge of his child? If Lady

[075-054]Milborough would do it, how great would be the comfort! But he was

[075-055]almost sure that Lady Milborough would not do it. All his friends had

[075-056]turned against him, and Lady Milborough among the number. There was

[075-057]nobody left to him, but Bozzle. Could he entrust Bozzle to find some

[075-058]woman for him who would take adequate charge of the little fellow,

[075-059]till he himself could see to the child's education? He did not put

[075-060]this question to Bozzle in plain terms; but he was very astute, and

[075-061]wrote in such a fashion that Bozzle could make a proposal, if any

[075-062]proposal were within his power.

[075-063]The answer from Mr. Outhouse came first. To this Mr. Trevelyan paid

[075-064]very little attention. It was just what he expected. Of course Mr.

[075-065]Outhouse's assurance about Colonel Osborne went for nothing. A man

[075-066]who would permit intercourse in his house between a married lady and

[075-067]her lover would not scruple to deny that he had permitted it. Then

[075-068]came Mr. Bideawhile's answer, which was very short. Mr. Bideawhile

[075-069]said that nothing could be done about the child till Mr. Trevelyan

[075-070]should return to England;--and that he could give no opinion as to

[075-071]what should be done then till he knew more of the circumstances. It

[075-072]was quite clear to Trevelyan that he must employ some other lawyer.

[075-073]Mr. Bideawhile had probably been corrupted by Colonel Osborne. Could

[075-074]Bozzle recommend a lawyer?

[075-075]From Bozzle himself there came no other immediate reply than, "his

[075-076]duty, and that he would make further inquiries."

[075-077]CHAPTER XLVI.

[075-078]THE AMERICAN MINISTER.

[075-079]In the second week in October, Mr. Glascock returned to Florence,

[075-080]intending to remain there till the weather should have become

[075-081]bearable at Naples. His father was said to be better, but was in

[075-082]such a condition as hardly to receive much comfort from his son's

[075-083]presence. His mind was gone, and he knew no one but his nurse; and,

[075-084]though Mr. Glascock was unwilling to put himself altogether out of

[075-085]the reach of returning at a day's notice, he did not find himself

[075-086]obliged to remain in Naples during the heat of the autumn. So Mr.

[075-087]Glascock returned to the hotel at Florence, accompanied by the tall

[075-088]man who wore the buttons. The hotel-keeper did not allow such a light

[075-089]to remain long hidden under a bushel, and it was soon spread far and

[075-090]wide that the Honourable Charles Glascock and his suite were again in

[075-091]the beautiful city.

[075-092]And the fact was soon known to the American Minister and his family.

[075-093]Mr. Spalding was a man who at home had been very hostile to English

[075-094]interests. Many American gentlemen are known for such hostility. They

[075-095]make anti-English speeches about the country, as though they thought

[075-096]that war with England would produce certain triumph to the States,

[075-097]certain increase to American trade, and certain downfall to a tyranny

[075-098]which no Anglo-Saxon nation ought to endure. But such is hardly their

[075-099]real opinion. There, in the States, as also here in England, you

[075-100]shall from day to day hear men propounding, in very loud language,

[075-101]advanced theories of political action, the assertion of which

[075-102]is supposed to be necessary to the end which they have in view.

[075-103]Men whom we know to have been as mild as sucking doves in the

[075-104]political aspiration of their whole lives, suddenly jump up,

[075-105]and with infuriated gestures declare themselves the enemies

[075-106]of everything existing. When they have attained their little

[075-107]purpose,--or have failed to do so,--they revert naturally into their

[075-108]sucking-dove elements. It is so with Americans as frequently as

[075-109]with ourselves,--and there is no political subject on which it is

[075-110]considered more expedient to express pseudo-enthusiasm than on that

[075-111]of the sins of England. It is understood that we do not resent it.

[075-112]It is presumed that we regard it as the Irishman regarded his wife's

[075-113]cuffs. In the States a large party, which consists chiefly of those

[075-114]who have lately left English rule, and who are keen to prove to

[075-115]themselves how wise they have been in doing so, is pleased by this

[075-116]strong language against England; and, therefore, the strong language

[075-117]is spoken. But the speakers, who are, probably, men knowing something

[075-118]of the world, mean it not at all; they have no more idea of war with

[075-119]England than they have of war with all Europe; and their respect for

[075-120]England and for English opinion is unbounded. In their political

[075-121]tones of speech and modes of action they strive to be as English as

[075-122]possible. Mr. Spalding's aspirations were of this nature. He had

[075-123]uttered speeches against England which would make the hair stand on

[075-124]end on the head of an uninitiated English reader. He had told his

[075-125]countrymen that Englishmen hugged their chains, and would do so until

[075-126]American hammers had knocked those chains from off their wounded

[075-127]wrists and bleeding ankles. He had declared that, if certain American

[075-128]claims were not satisfied, there was nothing left for Americans to do

[075-129]but to cross the ferry with such a sheriff's officer as would be able

[075-130]to make distraint on the great English household. He had declared

[075-131]that the sheriff's officer would have very little trouble. He had

[075-132]spoken of Canada as an outlying American territory, not yet quite

[075-133]sufficiently redeemed from savage life to be received into the Union

[075-134]as a State. There is a multiplicity of subjects of this kind ready

[075-135]to the hand of the American orator. Mr. Spalding had been quite

[075-136]successful, and was now Minister at Florence; but, perhaps, one of

[075-137]the greatest pleasures coming to him from his prosperity was the

[075-138]enjoyment of the society of well-bred Englishmen, in the capital to

[075-139]which he had been sent. When, therefore, his wife and nieces pointed

[075-140]out to him the fact that it was manifestly his duty to call upon Mr.

[075-141]Glascock after what had passed between them on that night under the

[075-142]Campanile, he did not rebel for an instant against the order given

[075-143]to him. His mind never reverted for a moment to that opinion which

[075-144]had gained for him such a round of applause, when expressed on the

[075-145]platform of the Temperance Hall at Nubbly Creek, State of Illinois,

[075-146]to the effect that the English aristocrat, thorough-born and

[075-147]thorough-bred, who inherited acres and titles from his father, could

[075-148]never be fitting company for a thoughtful Christian American citizen.

[075-149]He at once had his hat brushed, and took up his best gloves and

[075-150]umbrella, and went off to Mr. Glascock's hotel. He was strictly

[075-151]enjoined by the ladies to fix a day on which Mr. Glascock would come

[075-152]and dine at the American embassy.

[075-153]"'C. G.' has come back to see you," said Olivia to her elder sister.

[075-154]They had always called him "C. G." since the initials had been seen

[075-155]on the travelling bag.

[075-156]"Probably," said Carry. "There is so very little else to bring people

[075-157]to Florence, that there can hardly be any other reason for his

[075-158]coming. They do say it's terribly hot at Naples just now; but that

[075-159]can have had nothing to do with it."

[075-160]"We shall see," said Livy. "I'm sure he's in love with you. He looked

[075-161]to me just like a proper sort of lover for you, when I saw his long

[075-162]legs creeping up over our heads into the banquette."

[075-163]"You ought to have been very much obliged to his long legs;--so sick

[075-164]as you were at the time."

[075-165]"I like him amazingly," said Livy, "legs and all. I only hope Uncle

[075-166]Jonas won't bore him, so as to prevent his coming."

[075-167]"His father is very ill," said Carry, "and I don't suppose we shall

[075-168]see him at all."

[075-169]But the American Minister was successful. He found Mr. Glascock

[075-170]sitting in his dressing-gown, smoking a cigar, and reading a

[075-171]newspaper. The English aristocrat seemed very glad to see his

[075-172]visitor, and assumed no airs at all. The American altogether forgot

[075-173]his speech at Nubbly Creek, and found the aristocrat's society to

[075-174]be very pleasant. He lit a cigar, and they talked about Naples,

[075-175]Rome, and Florence. Mr. Spalding, when the marbles of old Rome were

[075-176]mentioned, was a little too keen in insisting on the merits of Story,

[075-177]Miss Hosmer, and Hiram Powers, and hardly carried his listener with

[075-178]him in the parallel which he drew between Greenough and Phidias; and

[075-179]he was somewhat repressed by the apathetic curtness of Mr. Glascock's

[075-180]reply, when he suggested that the victory gained by the gunboats at

[075-181]Vicksburg, on the Mississippi, was vividly brought to his mind by

[075-182]an account which he had just been reading of the battle of Actium;

[075-183]but he succeeded in inducing Mr. Glascock to accept an invitation to

[075-184]dinner for the next day but one, and the two gentlemen parted on the

[075-185]most amicable terms.
