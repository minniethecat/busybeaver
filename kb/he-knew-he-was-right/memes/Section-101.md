## Section 101

[101-001]"Why did you turn your wife out of doors, Mr. Trevelyan?" asked Mrs.

[101-002]Outhouse boldly.

[101-003]"I did not turn her out of doors. I provided a fitting shelter

[101-004]for her. I gave her everything that she could want. You know what

[101-005]happened. That man went down and was received there. I defy you, Mrs.

[101-006]Outhouse, to say that it was my fault."

[101-007]Mrs. Outhouse did attempt to show him that it was his fault; but

[101-008]while she was doing so he left the house. "I don't think she could go

[101-009]back to him," said Mrs. Outhouse to her husband. "He is quite insane

[101-010]upon this matter."

[101-011]"I shall be insane, I know," said Mr. Outhouse, "if Sir Marmaduke

[101-012]does not come home very quickly." Nevertheless he quite ignored any

[101-013]legal power that might be brought to bear against him as to the

[101-014]restitution of the child to its father.

[101-015]CHAPTER LXI.

[101-016]PARKER'S HOTEL, MOWBRAY STREET.

[101-017]Within a week of the occurrence which is related in the last chapter,

[101-018]there came a telegram from Southampton to the parsonage at St.

[101-019]Diddulph's, saying that Sir Marmaduke and Lady Rowley had reached

[101-020]England. On the evening of that day they were to lodge at a small

[101-021]family hotel in Baker Street, and both Mrs. Trevelyan and Nora were

[101-022]to be with them. The leave-taking at the parsonage was painful, as

[101-023]on both sides there existed a feeling that affection and sympathy

[101-024]were wanting. The uncle and aunt had done their duty, and both Mrs.

[101-025]Trevelyan and Nora felt that they ought to have been demonstrative

[101-026]and cordial in their gratitude;--but they found it impossible to

[101-027]become so. And the rector could not pretend but that he was glad to

[101-028]be rid of his guests. There were, too, some last words about money to

[101-029]be spoken, which were grievous thorns in the poor man's flesh. Two

[101-030]bank notes, however, were put upon his table, and he knew that unless

[101-031]he took them he could not pay for the provisions which his unwelcome

[101-032]visitors had consumed. Surely there never was a man so cruelly

[101-033]ill-used as had been Mr. Outhouse in all this matter. "Another such

[101-034]winter as that would put me in my grave," he said, when his wife

[101-035]tried to comfort him after they were gone. "I know that they have

[101-036]both been very good to us," said Mrs. Trevelyan, as she and her

[101-037]sister, together with the child and the nurse, hurried away towards

[101-038]Baker Street in a cab, "but I have never for a moment felt that

[101-039]they were glad to have us." "But how could they have been glad to

[101-040]have us," she added afterwards, "when we brought such trouble with

[101-041]us?" But they to whom they were going now would receive her with

[101-042]joy;--would make her welcome with all her load of sorrows, would give

[101-043]to her a sympathy which it was impossible that she should receive

[101-044]from others. Though she might not be happy now,--for in truth how

[101-045]could she be ever really happy again,--there would be a joy to her in

[101-046]placing her child in her mother's arms, and in receiving her father's

[101-047]warm caresses. That her father would be very vehement in his anger

[101-048]against her husband she knew well,--for Sir Marmaduke was a vehement

[101-049]man. But there would be some support for her in the very violence

[101-050]of his wrath, and at this moment it was such support that she most

[101-051]needed. As they journeyed together in the cab, the married sister

[101-052]seemed to be in the higher spirits of the two. She was sure, at any

[101-053]rate, that those to whom she was going would place themselves on her

[101-054]side. Nora had her own story to tell about [[hugh-stanbury|Hugh Stanbury]], and was

[101-055]by no means so sure that her tale would be received with cordial

[101-056]agreement. "Let me tell them myself," she whispered to her sister.

[101-057]"Not to-night, because they will have so much to say to you; but I

[101-058]shall tell mamma to-morrow."

[101-059]The train by which the Rowleys were to reach [[london|London]] was due at the

[101-060][[social-class|station]] at 7.30 p.m., and the two sisters timed their despatch from

[101-061]St. Diddulph's so as to enable them to reach the hotel at eight. "We

[101-062]shall be there now before mamma," said Nora, "because they will have

[101-063]so much luggage, and so many things, and the trains are always late."

[101-064]When they started from the door of the parsonage, Mr. Outhouse gave

[101-065]the direction to the cabman, "Gregg's Hotel, Baker Street." Then at

[101-066]once he began to console himself in that they were gone.

[101-067]It was a long drive from St. Diddulph's in the east, to Marylebone in

[101-068]the west, of London. None of the party in the cab knew anything of

[101-069]the region through which they passed. The cabman took the line by the

[101-070]back of the Bank, and Finsbury Square and the City Road, thinking it

[101-071]best, probably, to avoid the crush at Holborn Hill, though at the

[101-072]expense of something of a circuit. But of this Mrs. Trevelyan and

[101-073]Nora knew nothing. Had their way taken them along Piccadilly, or

[101-074]through Mayfair, or across Grosvenor Square, they would have known

[101-075]where they were; but at present they were not thinking of those once

[101-076]much-loved localities. The cab passed the Angel, and up and down the

[101-077]hill at Pentonville, and by the King's Cross stations, and through

[101-078]Euston Square,--and then it turned up Gower Street. Surely the man

[101-079]should have gone on along the New Road, now that he had come so far

[101-080]out of his way. But of this the two ladies knew nothing,--nor did the

[101-081]nurse. It was a dark, windy night, but the lamps in the streets had

[101-082]given them light, so that they had not noticed the night. Nor did

[101-083]they notice it now as the streets became narrower and darker. They

[101-084]were hardly thinking that their journey was yet at an end, and the

[101-085]mother was in the act of covering her boy's face as he lay asleep on

[101-086]the nurse's lap, when the cab was stopped. Nora looking out through

[101-087]the window, saw the word "Hotel" over a doorway, and was satisfied.

[101-088]"Shall I take the child, ma'am?" said a man in black, and the child

[101-089]was handed out. Nora was the first to follow, and she then perceived

[101-090]that the door of the hotel was not open. Mrs. Trevelyan followed;

[101-091]and then they looked round them,--and the child was gone. They heard

[101-092]the rattle of another cab as it was carried away at a gallop round a

[101-093]distant corner;--and then some inkling of what had happened came upon

[101-094]them. The father had succeeded in getting possession of his child.

[101-095]It was a narrow, dark street, very quiet, having about it a certain

[101-096]air of poor respectability,--an obscure, noiseless street, without

[101-097]even a sign of life. Some unfortunate one had endeavoured here to

[101-098]keep an hotel;--but there was no hotel kept there now. There had

[101-099]been much craft in selecting the place in which the child had been

[101-100]taken from them. As they looked around them, perceiving the terrible

[101-101]misfortune which had befallen them, there was not a human being near

[101-102]them save the cabman, who was occupied in unchaining, or pretending

[101-103]to unchain the heavy mass of luggage on the roof. The windows of

[101-104]the house before which they were stopping, were closed, and Nora

[101-105]perceived at once that the hotel was not inhabited. The cabman must

[101-106]have perceived it also. As for the man who had taken the child, the

[101-107]nurse could only say that he was dressed in black, like a waiter,

[101-108]that he had a napkin under his arm, and no hat on his head. He had

[101-109]taken the boy tenderly in his arms,--and then she had seen nothing

[101-110]further. The first thing that Nora had seen, as she stood on the

[101-111]pavement, was the other cab moving off rapidly.

[101-112]Mrs. Trevelyan had staggered against the railings, and was soon

[101-113]screaming in her wretchedness. Before long there was a small crowd

[101-114]around them, comprising three or four women, a few boys, an old man

[101-115]or two,--and a policeman. To the policeman Nora had soon told the

[101-116]whole story, and the cabman was of course attacked. But the cabman

[101-117]played his part very well. He declared that he had done just what

[101-118]he had been told to do. Nora was indeed sure that she had heard her

[101-119]uncle desire him to drive to Gregg's Hotel in Baker Street. The

[101-120]cabman in answer to this, declared that he had not clearly heard the

[101-121]old gentleman's directions; but that a man whom he had conceived to

[101-122]be a servant, had very plainly told him to drive to Parker's Hotel,

[101-123]Mowbray Street, Gower Street. "I comed ever so far out of my way,"

[101-124]said the cabman, "to avoid the rumpus with the homnibuses at the

[101-125]hill,--cause the ladies' things is so heavy we'd never got up if the

[101-126]'orse had once jibbed." All which, though it had nothing to do with

[101-127]the matter, seemed to impress the policeman with the idea that the

[101-128]cabman, if not a true man, was going to be too clever for them on

[101-129]this occasion. And the crafty cabman went on to declare that his

[101-130]horse was so tired with the load that he could not go on to Baker

[101-131]Street. They must get another cab. Take his number! Of course they

[101-132]could take his number. There was his number. His fare was four and

[101-133]six,--that is if the ladies wouldn't pay him anything extra for the

[101-134]terrible load; and he meant to have it. It would be sixpence more if

[101-135]they kept him there many minutes longer. The number was taken, and

[101-136]another cab was got, and the luggage was transferred, and the money

[101-137]was paid, while the unhappy mother was still screaming in hysterics

[101-138]against the railings. What had been done was soon clear enough to all

[101-139]those around her. Nora had told the policeman, and had told one of

[101-140]the women, thinking to obtain their sympathy and assistance. "It's

[101-141]the kid's dada as has taken it," said one man, "and there ain't

[101-142]nothing to be done." There was nothing to be done;--nothing at any

[101-143]rate then and there.

[101-144]Nora had been very eager that the cabman should be arrested; but the

[101-145]policeman assured her that such an arrest was out of the question,

[101-146]and would have been useless had it been possible. The man would be

[101-147]forthcoming if his presence should be again desired, but he had

[101-148]probably,--so said the policeman,--really been desired to drive to

[101-149]Mowbray Street. "They knows where to find me if they wants me,--only

[101-150]I must be paid my time," said the cabman confidently. And the

[101-151]policeman was of opinion that as the boy had been kidnapped on behalf

[101-152]of the father, no legal steps could be taken either for the recovery

[101-153]of the child or for the punishment of the perpetrators of the act. He

[101-154]got up, however, on the box of the cab, and accompanied the party to

[101-155]the hotel in Baker Street. They reached it almost exactly at the same

[101-156]time with Sir Marmaduke and Lady Rowley, and the reader must imagine

[101-157]the confusion, the anguish, and the disappointment of that meeting.

[101-158]Mrs. Trevelyan was hardly in possession of her senses when she

[101-159]reached her mother, and could not be induced to be tranquil even when

[101-160]she was assured by her father that her son would suffer no immediate

[101-161]evil by being transferred to his father's hands. She in her frenzy

[101-162]declared that she would never see her little one again, and seemed to

[101-163]think that the father might not improbably destroy the child. "He is

[101-164]mad, papa, and does not know what he does. Do you mean to say that a

[101-165]madman may do as he pleases?--that he may rob my child from me in the

[101-166]streets?--that he may take him out of my very arms in that way?" And

[101-167]she was almost angry with her father because no attempt was made that

[101-168]night to recover the boy.
