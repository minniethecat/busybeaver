## Section 65

[065-001]"Nora," he said, speaking perhaps with more energy than the words

[065-002]required, "I have come here to tell you that I love you, and to ask

[065-003]you to be my wife."

[065-004]Nora, for the last ten minutes, had been thinking that this would

[065-005]come,--that it would come at once; and yet she was not at all

[065-006]prepared with an answer. It was now weeks since she had confessed to

[065-007]herself frankly that nothing else but this,--this one thing which was

[065-008]now happening, this one thing which had now happened,--that nothing

[065-009]else could make her happy, or could touch her happiness. She had

[065-010]refused a man whom she otherwise would have taken, because her heart

[065-011]had been given to [[hugh-stanbury|Hugh Stanbury]]. She had been bold enough to tell

[065-012]that other suitor that it was so, though she had not mentioned the

[065-013]rival's name. She had longed for some expression of love from this

[065-014]man when they had been at Nuncombe together, and had been fiercely

[065-015]angry with him because no such expression had come from him. Day

[065-016]after day, since she had been with her aunt, she had told herself

[065-017]that she was a broken-hearted woman, because she had given away all

[065-018]that she had to give and had received nothing in return. Had he said

[065-019]a word that might have given her hope, how happy could she have been

[065-020]in hoping. Now he had come to her with a plain-spoken offer, telling

[065-021]her that he loved her, and asking her to be his wife,--and she was

[065-022]altogether unable to answer. How could she consent to be his wife,

[065-023]knowing as she did that there was no [[truth|certainty]] of an [[money|income]] on which

[065-024]they could live? How could she tell her father and mother that she

[065-025]had engaged herself to marry a man who might or might not make L400 a

[065-026]year, and who already had a mother and sister depending on him?

[065-027]In truth, had he come more gently to her, his chance of a happy

[065-028]answer,--of an answer which might be found to have in it something

[065-029]of happiness,--would have been greater. He might have said a word

[065-030]which she could not but have answered softly;--and then from that

[065-031]constrained softness other gentleness would have followed, and so

[065-032]he would have won her in spite of her discretion. She would have

[065-033]surrendered gradually, accepting on the score of her great love all

[065-034]the penalties of a long and precarious engagement. But when she

[065-035]was asked to come and be his wife, now and at once, she felt that

[065-036]in spite of her love it was impossible that she could accede to a

[065-037]request so sudden, so violent, so monstrous. He stood over her as

[065-038]though expecting an instant answer; and then, when she had sat dumb

[065-039]before him for a minute, he repeated his demand. "Tell me, Nora, can

[065-040]you love me? If you knew how thoroughly I have loved you, you would

[065-041]at least feel something for me."

[065-042]To tell him that she did not love him was impossible to her. But how

[065-043]was she to refuse him without telling him either a lie, or the truth?

[065-044]Some answer she must give him; and as to that matter of marrying him,

[065-045]the answer must be a negative. Her education had been of that nature

[065-046]which teaches girls to believe that it is a crime to marry a man

[065-047]without an assured income. Assured [[virtue|morality]] in a husband is a great

[065-048]thing. Assured good temper is very excellent. Assured talent,

[065-049]religion, amiability, truth, honesty, are all desirable. But an

[065-050]assured income is indispensable. Whereas, in truth, the income may

[065-051]come hereafter; but the other things, unless they be there already,

[065-052]will hardly be forthcoming. "Mr. Stanbury," she said, "your

[065-053]suddenness has quite astounded me."

[065-054]"Ah, yes; but how should I not be sudden? I have come here on purpose

[065-055]to say this to you. If I do not say it now--"

[065-056]"You heard what Emily said."

[065-057]"No;--what did she say?"

[065-058]"She said that it would not be for good that you should speak to me

[065-059]thus."

[065-060]"Why not for good? But she is unhappy, and looks gloomily at things."

[065-061]"Yes, indeed."

[065-062]"But all the world need not be sad for ever because she has been

[065-063]unfortunate."

[065-064]"Not all the world, Mr. Stanbury;--but you must not be surprised if

[065-065]it affects me."

[065-066]"But would that prevent your loving me,--if you did love me? But,

[065-067]Nora, I do not expect you to love me,--not yet. I do not say that I

[065-068]expect it,--ever. But if you would--. Nora, I can do no more than

[065-069]tell you the simple truth. Just listen to me for a minute. You know

[065-070]how I came to be intimate with you all in Curzon Street. The first

[065-071]day I saw you I loved you; and there has come no change yet. It is

[065-072]months now since I first knew that I loved you. Well; I told myself

[065-073]more than once,--when I was down at Nuncombe for instance,--that I

[065-074]had no right to speak to you. What right can a poor devil like me

[065-075]have, who lives from hand to mouth, to ask such a girl as you to be

[065-076]his wife? And so I said nothing,--though it was on my lips every

[065-077]moment that I was there." Nora remembered at the moment how she had

[065-078]looked to his lips, and had not seen the words there. "But I think

[065-079]there is something unmanly in this. If you cannot give me a grain

[065-080]of hope;--if you tell me that there never can be hope, it is my

[065-081]misfortune. It will be very grievous, but I will bear it. But that

[065-082]will be better than puling and moping about without daring to tell my

[065-083]tale. I am not ashamed of it. I have fallen in love with you, Nora,

[065-084]and I think it best to come for an answer."

[065-085]He held out his arms as though he thought that she might perhaps come

[065-086]to him. Indeed he had no idea of any such coming on her part; but

[065-087]she, as she looked at him, almost thought that it was her duty to go.

[065-088]Had she a right to withhold herself from him, she who loved him so

[065-089]dearly? Had he stepped forward and taken her in his arms it might be

[065-090]that all power of refusal would soon have been beyond her power.

[065-091]"Mr. Stanbury," she said, "you have confessed yourself that it is

[065-092]impossible."

[065-093]"But do you love me;--do you think that it is possible that you

[065-094]should ever love me?"

[065-095]"You know, Mr. Stanbury, that you should not say anything further.

[065-096]You know that it cannot be."

[065-097]"But do you love me?"

[065-098]"You are ungenerous not to take an answer without driving me to be

[065-099]uncourteous."

[065-100]"I do not care for courtesy. Tell me the truth. Can you ever love me?

[065-101]With one word of hope I will wait, and work, and feel myself to be a

[065-102]hero. I will not go till you tell me that you cannot love me."

[065-103]"Then I must tell you so."

[065-104]"What is it you will tell me, Nora? Speak it. Say it. If I knew that

[065-105]a girl disliked me, nothing should make me press myself upon her. Am

[065-106]I odious to you, Nora?"

[065-107]"No; not odious,--but very, very unfair."

[065-108]"I will have the truth if I be ever so unfair," he said. And by this

[065-109]time probably some inkling of the truth had reached his intelligence.

[065-110]There was already a tear in Nora's eye, but he did not pity her. She

[065-111]owed it to him to tell him the truth, and he would have it from her

[065-112]if it was to be reached. "Nora," he said, "listen to me again. All my

[065-113]heart and soul are in this. It is everything to me. If you can love

[065-114]me you are bound to say so. By Jove, I will believe you do unless you

[065-115]swear to me that it is not so!" He was now holding her by the hand

[065-116]and looking closely into her face.

[065-117]"Mr. Stanbury," she said, "let me go; pray, pray let me go."

[065-118]"Not till you say that you love me. Oh, Nora, I believe that you love

[065-119]me. You do; yes; you do love me. Dearest, dearest Nora, would you not

[065-120]say a word to make me the happiest man in the world?" And now he had

[065-121]his arm round her waist.

[065-122]"Let me go," she said, struggling through her tears and covering her

[065-123]face with her hands. "You are very, very wicked. I will never speak

[065-124]to you again. Nay, but you shall let me go!" And then she was out of

[065-125]his arms and had escaped from the room before he had managed to touch

[065-126]her face with his lips.

[065-127]As he was thinking how he also might escape now,--might escape

[065-128]and comfort himself with his triumph,--Mrs. Outhouse returned to

[065-129]the chamber. She was very demure, and her manner towards him was

[065-130]considerably changed since she had left the chamber. "Mr. Stanbury,"

[065-131]she said, "this kind of thing mustn't go any further indeed;--at

[065-132]least not in my house."

[065-133]"What kind of thing, Mrs. Outhouse?"

[065-134]"Well;--what my elder niece has told me. I have not seen Miss Rowley

[065-135]since she left you. I am quite sure she has behaved with discretion."

[065-136]"Indeed she has, Mrs. Outhouse."

[065-137]"The fact is my nieces are in grief and trouble, and this is no time

[065-138]or place for love-making. I am sorry to be uncivil, but I must ask

[065-139]you not to come here any more."

[065-140]"I will stay away from this house, certainly, if you bid me."

[065-141]"I am very sorry; but I must bid you. Sir Marmaduke will be home in

[065-142]the spring, and if you have anything to say to him of course you can

[065-143]see him."

[065-144]Then Hugh Stanbury took his leave of Mrs. Outhouse; but as he went

[065-145]home, again on the knifeboard of an omnibus, he smoked the pipe of

[065-146]triumph rather than the pipe of contemplation.

[065-147]CHAPTER XL.

[065-148]"C. G."

[065-149]The Miss Spaldings were met at the [[social-class|station]] at Florence by their

[065-150]uncle, the American Minister, by their cousin, the American Secretary

[065-151]of Legation, and by three or four other dear friends and relations,

[065-152]who were there to welcome the newcomers to sunny Italy. Mr. Glascock,

[065-153]therefore, who ten minutes since had been, and had felt himself to

[065-154]be, quite indispensable to their comfort, suddenly became as though

[065-155]he were nothing and nobody. Who is there that has not felt these

[065-156]sudden disruptions to the intimacies and friendships of a long

[065-157]journey? He bowed to them, and they to him, and then they were

[065-158]whirled away in their grandeur. He put himself into a small, open

[065-159]hackney-carriage, and had himself driven to the York Hotel, feeling

[065-160]himself to be deserted and desolate. The two Miss Spaldings were

[065-161]the daughters of a very respectable lawyer at Boston, whereas Mr.

[065-162]Glascock was heir to a peerage, to an enormous fortune, and to one of

[065-163]the finest places in England. But he thought nothing of this at the

[065-164]time. As he went he was meditating which young woman was the most

[065-165]attractive, [[nora-rowley|Nora Rowley]] or Caroline Spalding. He had no doubt but

[065-166]that Nora was the prettier, the pleasanter in manner, the better

[065-167]dressed, the more engaging in all that concerned the outer woman;

[065-168]but he thought that he had never met any lady who talked better than

[065-169]Caroline Spalding. And what was Nora Rowley's beauty to him? Had she

[065-170]not told him that she was the property of some one else; or, for the

[065-171]matter of that, what was Miss Spalding to him? They had parted, and

[065-172]he was going on to Naples in two days. He had said some half-defined

[065-173]word as to calling at the American Embassy, but it had not been taken

[065-174]up by either of the ladies. He had not pressed it, and so they had

[065-175]parted without an understanding as to a future meeting.
