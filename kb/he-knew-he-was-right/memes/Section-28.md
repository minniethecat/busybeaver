## Section 28

[028-001]"How can I know? He did once."

[028-002]"And you love him?"

[028-003]"Yes. I love him certainly."

[028-004]"I don't see how you can have a doubt. Here is Jack with the

[028-005]carriage, and if we don't mind he'll pass us by without seeing us."

[028-006]Then Mrs. Trevelyan got up, and when they had succeeded in diverting

[028-007]Jack's attention for a moment from the horse, they called to Nora,

[028-008]who was still moving about from one knoll to another, and who showed

[028-009]no desire to abandon the contemplations in which she had been

[028-010]engaged.

[028-011]It had been mid-day before they left home in the morning, and they

[028-012]were due to be at home in time for tea,--which is an epoch in the

[028-013]day generally allowed to be more elastic than some others. When Mrs.

[028-014]Stanbury lived in the cottage her hour for tea had been six; this had

[028-015]been stretched to half-past seven when she received Mrs. Trevelyan at

[028-016]the [[clock-house|Clock House]]; and it was half-past eight before Jack landed them

[028-017]at their door. It was manifest to them all as they entered the house

[028-018]that there was an air of mystery in the face of the girl who had

[028-019]opened the door for them. She did not speak, however, till they were

[028-020]all within the passage. Then she uttered a few words very solemnly.

[028-021]"There be a [[social-class|gentleman]] come," she said.

[028-022]"A gentleman!" said Mrs. Trevelyan, thinking in the first moment of

[028-023]her husband, and in the second of [[colonel-osborne|Colonel Osborne]].

[028-024]"He be for you, miss," said the girl, bobbing her head at Nora.

[028-025]Upon hearing this Nora sank speechless into the chair which stood in

[028-026]the passage.

[028-027]CHAPTER XVII.

[028-028]A GENTLEMAN COMES TO NUNCOMBE PUTNEY.

[028-029]It soon became known to them all as they remained clustered in the

[028-030]hall that Mr. Glascock was in the house. Mrs. Stanbury came out to

[028-031]them and informed them that he had been at [[nuncombe-putney|Nuncombe Putney]] for the

[028-032]last five hours, and that he had asked for Mrs. Trevelyan when he

[028-033]called. It became evident as the affairs of the evening went on, that

[028-034]Mrs. Stanbury had for a few minutes been thrown into a terrible state

[028-035]of amazement, thinking that "the Colonel" had appeared. The strange

[028-036]gentleman, however, having obtained admittance, explained who he was,

[028-037]saying that he was very desirous of seeing Mrs. Trevelyan,--and Miss

[028-038]Rowley. It may be presumed that a glimmer of light did make its way

[028-039]into Mrs. Stanbury's mind on the subject; but up to the moment at

[028-040]which the three travellers arrived, she had been in doubt on the

[028-041]subject. Mr. Glascock had declared that he would take a walk, and

[028-042]in the course of the afternoon had expressed high approval of Mrs.

[028-043]Crocket's culinary skill. When Mrs. Crocket heard that she had

[028-044]entertained the son of a lord, she was very loud in her praise of the

[028-045]manner in which he had eaten two mutton chops and called for a third.

[028-046]He had thought it no disgrace to apply himself to the second half

[028-047]of an apple pie, and had professed himself to be an ardent admirer

[028-048]of Devonshire cream. "It's them counter-skippers as turns up their

[028-049]little noses at the victuals as is set before them," said Mrs.

[028-050]Crocket.

[028-051]After his dinner Mr. Glascock had returned to the Clock House, and

[028-052]had been sitting there for an hour with Mrs. Stanbury, not much to

[028-053]her delight or to his, when the carriage was driven up to the door.

[028-054]"He is to go back to Lessboro' to-night," said Mrs. Stanbury in a

[028-055]whisper.

[028-056]"Of course you must see him before he goes," said Mrs. Trevelyan to

[028-057]her sister. There had, as was natural, been very much said between

[028-058]the two sisters about Mr. Glascock. Nora had abstained from asserting

[028-059]in any decided way that she disliked the man, and had always

[028-060]absolutely refused to allow [[hugh-stanbury|Hugh Stanbury]]'s name to be mixed up with

[028-061]the question. Whatever might be her own thoughts about Hugh Stanbury

[028-062]she had kept them even from her sister. When her sister had told her

[028-063]that she had refused Mr. Glascock because of Hugh, she had shown

[028-064]herself to be indignant, and had since that said one or two fine

[028-065]things as to her capacity to refuse a brilliant offer simply because

[028-066]the man who had made it was indifferent to her. Mrs. Trevelyan had

[028-067]learned from her that her suitor had declared his intention to

[028-068]persevere; and here was perseverance with a vengeance! "Of course you

[028-069]must see him,--at once," said Mrs. Trevelyan. Nora for a few seconds

[028-070]had remained silent, and then had run up to her room. Her sister

[028-071]followed her instantly.

[028-072]"What is the meaning of it all?" said Priscilla to her mother.

[028-073]"I suppose he is in love with Miss Rowley," said Mrs. Stanbury.

[028-074]"But who is he?"

[028-075]Then Mrs. Stanbury told all that she knew. She had seen from his

[028-076]card that he was an Honourable Mr. Glascock. She had collected from

[028-077]what he had said that he was an old friend of the two ladies. Her

[028-078]conviction was strong in Mr. Glascock's favour,--thinking, as she

[028-079]expressed herself, that everything was right and proper,--but she

[028-080]could hardly explain why she thought so.

[028-081]"I do wish that they had never come," said Priscilla, who could not

[028-082]rid herself of an idea that there must be danger in having to do with

[028-083]women who had men running after them.

[028-084]"Of course I'll see him," said Nora to her sister. "I have not

[028-085]refused to see him. Why do you scold me?"

[028-086]"I have not scolded you, Nora; but I do want you to think how

[028-087]immensely important this is."

[028-088]"Of course it is important."

[028-089]"And so much the more so because of my misfortunes! Think how good he

[028-090]must be, how strong must be his attachment, when he comes down here

[028-091]after you in this way."

[028-092]"But I have to think of my own feelings."

[028-093]"You know you like him. You have told me so. And only fancy what

[028-094]mamma will feel! Such a position! And the man so excellent! Everybody

[028-095]says that he hasn't a fault in any way."

[028-096]"I hate people without faults."

[028-097]"Oh, Nora, Nora, that is foolish! There, there; you must go down.

[028-098]Pray,--pray do not let any absurd fancy stand in your way, and

[028-099]destroy everything. It will never come again, Nora. And, only think;

[028-100]it is all now your own, if you will only whisper one word."

[028-101]"Ah!--one word,--and that a falsehood!"

[028-102]"No,--no. Say you will try to love him, and that will be enough. And

[028-103]you do love him?"

[028-104]"Do I?"

[028-105]"Yes, you do. It is only the opposition of your nature that makes you

[028-106]fight against him. Will you go now?"

[028-107]"Let me be for two minutes by myself," said Nora, "and then I'll come

[028-108]down. Tell him that I'm coming." Mrs. Trevelyan stooped over her,

[028-109]kissed her, and then left her.

[028-110]Nora, as soon as she was alone, stood upright in the middle of the

[028-111]room and held her hands up to her forehead. She had been far from

[028-112]thinking, when she was considering the matter easily among the

[028-113]hillocks, that the necessity for an absolute decision would come upon

[028-114]her so instantaneously. She had told herself only this morning that

[028-115]it would be wise to accept the man, if he should ever ask a second

[028-116]time;--and he had come already. He had been waiting for her in the

[028-117]village while she had been thinking whether he would ever come across

[028-118]her path again. She thought that it would have been easier for her

[028-119]now to have gone down with a "yes" in her mouth, if her sister had

[028-120]not pressed her so hard to say that "yes." The very pressure from her

[028-121]sister seemed to imply that such pressure ought to be resisted. Why

[028-122]should there have been pressure, unless there were reasons against

[028-123]her marrying him? And yet, if she chose to take him, who would have

[028-124]a right to complain of her? Hugh Stanbury had never spoken to her a

[028-125]word that would justify her in even supposing that he would consider

[028-126]himself to be ill-used. All others of her friends would certainly

[028-127]rejoice, would applaud her, pat her on the back, cover her with

[028-128]caresses, and tell her that she had been born under a happy star. And

[028-129]she did like the man. Nay;--she thought she loved him. She withdrew

[028-130]her hands from her brow, assured herself that her lot in life was

[028-131]cast, and with hurrying fingers attempted to smooth her hair and to

[028-132]arrange her ribbons before the glass. She would go to the encounter

[028-133]boldly and accept him honestly. It was her duty to do so. What might

[028-134]she not do for brothers and sisters as the wife of Lord Peterborough

[028-135]of Monkhams? She saw that that arrangement before the glass could be

[028-136]of no service, and she stepped quickly to the door. If he did not

[028-137]like her as she was, he need not ask her. Her mind was made up, and

[028-138]she would do it. But as she went down the stairs to the room in which

[028-139]she knew that he was waiting for her, there came over her a cold

[028-140]feeling of self-accusation,--almost of disgrace. "I do not care,"

[028-141]she said. "I know that I'm right." She opened the door quickly, that

[028-142]there might be no further doubt, and found that she was alone with

[028-143]him.

[028-144]"Miss Rowley," he said, "I am afraid you will think that I am

[028-145]persecuting you."

[028-146]"I have no right to think that," she answered.

[028-147]"I'll tell you why I have come. My dear father, who has always been

[028-148]my best friend, is very ill. He is at Naples, and I must go to him.

[028-149]He is very old, you know,--over eighty; and will never live to come

[028-150]back to England. From what I hear, I think it probable that I may

[028-151]remain with him till everything is over."

[028-152]"I did not know that he was so old as that."

[028-153]"They say that he can hardly live above a month or two. He will never

[028-154]see my wife,--if I can have a wife; but I should like to tell him, if

[028-155]it were possible,--that,--that--"

[028-156]"I understand you, Mr. Glascock."

[028-157]"I told you that I should come to you again, and as I may possibly

[028-158]linger at Naples all the winter, I could not go without seeing you.

[028-159]Miss Rowley, may I hope that you can love me?"

[028-160]She did not answer him a word, but stood looking away from him with

[028-161]her hands clasped together. Had he asked her whether she would be his

[028-162]wife, it is possible that the answer which she had prepared would

[028-163]have been spoken. But he had put the question in another form. Did

[028-164]she love him? If she could only bring herself to say that she could

[028-165]love him, she might be lady of Monkhams before the next summer had

[028-166]come round.

[028-167]"Nora," he said, "do you think that you can love me?"

[028-168]"No," she said, and there was something almost of fierceness in the

[028-169]tone of her voice as she answered him.

[028-170]"And must that be your final answer to me?"

[028-171]"Mr. Glascock, what can I say?" she replied. "I will tell you the

[028-172]honest truth:--I will tell you everything. I came into this room

[028-173]determined to accept you. But you are so good, and so kind, and so

[028-174]upright, that I cannot tell you a falsehood. I do not love you. I

[028-175]ought not to take what you offer me. If I did, it would be because

[028-176]you are rich, and a lord; and not because I love you. I love some one

[028-177]else. There;--pray, pray do not tell of me; but I do." Then she flung

[028-178]away from him and hid her face in a corner of the sofa out of the

[028-179]light.

[028-180]Her lover stood silent, not knowing how to go on with the

[028-181]conversation, not knowing how to bring it to an end. After what

[028-182]she had now said to him it was impossible that he should press her

[028-183]further. It was almost impossible that he should wish to do so. When

[028-184]a lady is frank enough to declare that her heart is not her own to

[028-185]give, a man can hardly wish to make further prayer for the gift. "If

[028-186]so," he said, "of course I have nothing to hope."
