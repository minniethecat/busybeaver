## Section 134

[134-001]"It is not done at all," said Caroline.

[134-002]"I feel quite satisfied you will not jilt me, and as I shall insist

[134-003]on having the ceremony performed, I choose to regard it as a

[134-004][[truth|certainty]]. Passing that by, then, I will go on to the results. My

[134-005]uncles, and aunts, and cousins, and the people you talk of, were very

[134-006]reasonable folk when I last saw them, and quite sufficiently alive to

[134-007]the fact that they had to regard me as the head of their family. I

[134-008]do not doubt that we shall find them equally reasonable when we get

[134-009]home; but should they be changed, should there be any sign shewn that

[134-010]my choice of a wife had occasioned displeasure,--such displeasure

[134-011]would not affect you."

[134-012]"But it would affect you."

[134-013]"Not at all. In my own house I am master,--and I mean to continue to

[134-014]be so. You will be mistress there, and the only fear touching such

[134-015]a position is that it may be recognised by others too strongly. You

[134-016]have nothing to fear, Carry."

[134-017]"It is of you I am thinking."

[134-018]"Nor have I. What if some old women, or even some young women, should

[134-019]turn up their noses at the wife I have chosen, because she has not

[134-020]been chosen from among their own countrywomen, is that to be a cause

[134-021]of suffering to us? Can not we rise above that,--lasting as it would

[134-022]do for a few weeks, a month or two perhaps,--say a year,--till my

[134-023]Caroline shall have made herself known? I think that we are strong

[134-024]enough to live down a trouble so light." He had come close to her

[134-025]as he was speaking, and had again put his arm round her waist. She

[134-026]tried to escape from his embrace,--not with persistency, not with the

[134-027]strength which always suffices for a woman when the embrace is in

[134-028]truth a thing to be avoided, but clutching at his fingers with hers,

[134-029]pressing them rather than loosening their grasp. "No, Carry," he

[134-030]continued; "we have got to go through with it now, and we will try

[134-031]and make the best of it. You may trust me that we shall not find it

[134-032]difficult,--not, at least, on the ground of your present fears. I can

[134-033]bear a heavier burden than you will bring upon me."

[134-034]"I know that I ought to prove to you that I am right," she said,

[134-035]still struggling with his hand.

[134-036]"And I know that you can prove nothing of the kind. Dearest, it is

[134-037]fixed between us now, and do not let us be so silly as to raise

[134-038]imaginary difficulties. Of course you would have to marry me, even if

[134-039]there were cause for such fears. If there were any great cause, still

[134-040]the game would be worth the candle. There could be no going back, let

[134-041]the fear be what it might. But there need be no fear if you will only

[134-042]love me." She felt that he was altogether too strong for her,--that

[134-043]she had mistaken his [[honour|character]] in supposing that she could be more

[134-044]firm than he. He was so strong that he treated her almost as a

[134-045]child;--and yet she loved him infinitely the better for so treating

[134-046]her. Of course, she knew now that her objection, whether true or

[134-047]unsubstantial, could not avail. As he stood with his arm round her,

[134-048]she was powerless to contradict him in anything. She had so far

[134-049]acknowledged this that she no longer struggled with him, but allowed

[134-050]her hand to remain quietly within his. If there was no going back

[134-051]from this bargain that had been made,--why, then, there was no need

[134-052]for combating. And when he stooped over and kissed her lips, she had

[134-053]not a word to say. "Be good to me," he said, "and tell me that I am

[134-054]right."

[134-055]"You must be master, I suppose, whether you are right or wrong. A man

[134-056]always thinks himself entitled to his own way."

[134-057]"Why, yes. When he has won the battle, he claims his captive. Now,

[134-058]the truth is this, I have won the battle, and your friend, Miss

[134-059]Petrie, has lost it. I hope she will understand that she has been

[134-060]beaten at last out of the field." As he said this, he heard a step

[134-061]behind them, and turning round saw Wallachia there almost before he

[134-062]could drop his arm.

[134-063]"I am sorry that I have intruded on you," she said very grimly.

[134-064]"Not in the least," said Mr. Glascock. "Caroline and I have had a

[134-065]little dispute, but we have settled it without coming to blows."

[134-066]"I do not suppose that an English [[social-class|gentleman]] ever absolutely strikes a

[134-067]lady," said Wallachia Petrie.

[134-068]"Not except on strong provocation," said Mr. Glascock. "In reference

[134-069]to wives, a stick is allowed as big as your thumb."

[134-070]"I have heard that it is so by the laws of England," said Wallachia.

[134-071]"How can you be so ridiculous, Wally!" said Caroline. "There is

[134-072]nothing that you would not believe."

[134-073]"I hope that it may never be true in your case," said Wallachia.

[134-074]A couple of days after this Miss Spalding found that it was

[134-075]absolutely necessary that she should explain the circumstances of her

[134-076]position to Nora. She had left Nora with the purpose of performing

[134-077]a very high-minded action, of sacrificing herself for the sake of

[134-078]her lover, of giving up all her golden prospects, and of becoming

[134-079]once again the bosom friend of Wallachia Petrie, with this simple

[134-080]consolation for her future life,--that she had refused to marry

[134-081]an English nobleman because the English nobleman's condition was

[134-082]unsuited to her. It would have been an episode in female life in

[134-083]which pride might be taken;--but all that was now changed. She had

[134-084]made her little attempt,--had made it, as she felt, in a very languid

[134-085]manner, and had found herself treated as a child for doing so. Of

[134-086]course she was happy in her ill success; of course she would have

[134-087]been broken-hearted had she succeeded. But, nevertheless, she was

[134-088]somewhat lowered in her own esteem, and it was necessary that she

[134-089]should acknowledge the truth to the friend whom she had consulted. A

[134-090]day or two had passed before she found herself alone with Nora, but

[134-091]when she did so she confessed her failure at once.

[134-092]"You told him all, then?" said Nora.

[134-093]"Oh yes, I told him all. That is, I could not really tell him. When

[134-094]the moment came I had no words."

[134-095]"And what did he say?"

[134-096]"He had words enough. I never knew him to be eloquent before."

[134-097]"He can speak out if he likes," said Nora.

[134-098]"So I have found,--with a vengeance. Nobody was ever so put down as I

[134-099]was. Don't you know that there are times when it does not seem to be

[134-100]worth your while to put out your strength against an adversary? So it

[134-101]was with him. He just told me that he was my master, and that I was

[134-102]to do as he bade me."

[134-103]"And what did you say?"

[134-104]"I promised to be a good girl," said Caroline, "and not to pretend

[134-105]to have any opinion of my own ever again. And so we kissed, and were

[134-106]friends."

[134-107]"I dare say there was a kiss, my dear."

[134-108]"Of course there was;--and he held me in his arms, and comforted me,

[134-109]and told me how to behave;--just as you would do a little girl. It's

[134-110]all over now, of course; and if there be a mistake, it is his fault.

[134-111]I feel that all [[duty|responsibility]] is gone from myself, and that for all

[134-112]the rest of my life I have to do just what he tells me."

[134-113]"And what says the divine Wallachia?"

[134-114]"Poor Wally! She says nothing, but she thinks that I am a castaway

[134-115]and a recreant. I am a recreant, I know;--but yet I think that I was

[134-116]right. I know I could not help myself."

[134-117]"Of course you were right, my dear," said the sage Nora. "If you had

[134-118]the notion in your head, it was wise to get rid of it; but I knew how

[134-119]it would be when you spoke to him."

[134-120]"You were not so weak when he came to you."

[134-121]"That was altogether another thing. It was not arranged in heaven

[134-122]that I was to become his captive."

[134-123]After that Wallachia Petrie never again tried her influence on her

[134-124]former friend, but admitted to herself that the evil was done, and

[134-125]that it could not be remedied. According to her theory of life,

[134-126]Caroline Spalding had been wrong, and weak,--had shewn herself to

[134-127]be comfort-loving and luxuriously-minded, had looked to get her

[134-128]happiness from soft effeminate pleasures rather than from rational

[134-129]work and the useful, independent exercise of her own intelligence.

[134-130]In the privacy of her little chamber Wallachia Petrie shed,--not

[134-131]absolute tears,--but many tearful thoughts over her friend. It was

[134-132]to her a thing very terrible that the chosen one of her heart should

[134-133]prefer the career of an English lord's wife to that of an American

[134-134]citizeness, with all manner of capability for female voting, female

[134-135]speech-making, female poetising, and, perhaps, female political

[134-136]action before her. It was a thousand pities! "You may take a horse

[134-137]to water,"--said Wallachia to herself, thinking of the ever-freshly

[134-138]springing fountain of her own mind, at which Caroline Spalding would

[134-139]always have been made welcome freely to quench her thirst,--"but you

[134-140]cannot make him drink if he be not athirst." In the future she would

[134-141]have no friend. Never again would she subject herself to the disgrace

[134-142]of such a failure. But the sacrifice was to be made, and she knew

[134-143]that it was bootless to waste her words further on Caroline Spalding.

[134-144]She left Florence before the wedding, and returned alone to the land

[134-145]of [[personal-freedom|liberty]]. She wrote a letter to Caroline explaining her conduct,

[134-146]and Caroline Spalding shewed the letter to her husband,--as one that

[134-147]was both loving and eloquent.

[134-148]"Very loving and very eloquent," he said. "But, nevertheless, one

[134-149]does think of sour grapes."

[134-150]"There I am sure you wrong her," said Caroline.

[134-151]CHAPTER LXXXII.

[134-152]MRS. FRENCH'S CARVING KNIFE.

[134-153]During these days there were terrible doings at [[exeter|Exeter]]. Camilla had

[134-154]sworn that if Mr. Gibson did not come to, there should be a tragedy,

[134-155]and it appeared that she was inclined to keep her word. Immediately

[134-156]after the receipt of her letter from Mr. Gibson she had had an

[134-157]interview with that gentleman in his lodgings, and had asked him his

[134-158]intentions. He had taken measures to fortify himself against such an

[134-159]attack; but, whatever those measures were, Camilla had broken through

[134-160]them. She had stood before him as he sat in his arm-chair, and he had

[134-161]been dumb in her presence. It had perhaps been well for him that the

[134-162]eloquence of her indignation had been so great that she had hardly

[134-163]been able to pause a moment for a reply. "Will you take your letter

[134-164]back again?" she had said. "I should be wrong to do that," he had

[134-165]lisped out in reply, "because it is true. As a Christian minister

[134-166]I could not stand with you at the altar with a lie in my mouth."

[134-167]In no other way did he attempt to excuse himself,--but that, twice

[134-168]repeated, filled up all the pause which she made for him.

[134-169][Illustration: Camilla's wrath.]

[134-170]There never had been such a case before,--so impudent, so cruel, so

[134-171]gross, so uncalled for, so unmanly, so unnecessary, so unjustifiable,

[134-172]so damnable,--so sure of eternal condemnation! All this she

[134-173]said to him with loud voice, and clenched fist, and starting

[134-174]eyes,--regardless utterly of any listeners on the stairs, or of

[134-175]outside passers in the street. In very truth she was moved to a

[134-176]sublimity of indignation. Her low nature became nearly poetic under

[134-177]the wrong inflicted upon her. She was almost tempted to tear him with

[134-178]her hands, and inflict upon him at the moment some terrible vengeance

[134-179]which should be told of for ever in the annals of Exeter. A man so

[134-180]mean as he, so weak, so cowardly, one so little of a hero;--that he

[134-181]should dare to do it, and dare to sit there before her, and to say

[134-182]that he would do it! "Your gown shall be torn off your back, sir, and

[134-183]the very boys of Exeter shall drag you through the gutters!" To this

[134-184]threat he said nothing, but sat mute, hiding his face in his hands.

[134-185]"And now tell me this, sir;--is there anything between you and

[134-186]Bella?" But there was no voice in reply. "Answer my question, sir.

[134-187]I have a right to ask it." Still he said not a word. "Listen to me.

[134-188]Sooner than that you and she should be man and wife, I would stab

[134-189]her! Yes, I would;--you poor, paltry, lying, cowardly creature!" She

[134-190]remained with him for more than half an hour, and then banged out of

[134-191]the room flashing back a look of scorn at him as she went. Martha,

[134-192]before that day was over, had learned the whole story from Mr.

[134-193]Gibson's cook, and had told her mistress.
