## Section 11

[011-001][[louis-trevelyan|Louis Trevelyan]] felt that he had disgraced himself. He had meant to

[011-002]have been strong, and he had, as he knew, been very weak. He had

[011-003]meant to have acted in a high-minded, honest, manly manner; but

[011-004]circumstances had been so untoward with him, that on looking at his

[011-005]own conduct, it seemed to him to have been mean, and almost false

[011-006]and cowardly. As the order for the exclusion of this hated man from

[011-007]his house had been given, he should at any rate have stuck to the

[011-008]order. At the moment of his vacillation he had simply intended to

[011-009]make things easy for his wife; but she had taken advantage of his

[011-010]vacillation, and had now clearly conquered him. Perhaps he respected

[011-011]her more than he had done when he was resolving, three or four days

[011-012]since, that he would be the master in his own house; but it may be

[011-013]feared that the tenderness of his love for her had been impaired.

[011-014]Late in the afternoon his wife and sister-in-law came down dressed

[011-015]for walking, and, finding Trevelyan in the library, they asked him to

[011-016]join them,--it was a custom with them to walk in the park on a Sunday

[011-017]afternoon,--and he at once assented, and went out with them. Emily,

[011-018]who had had her triumph, was very gracious. There should not be a

[011-019]word more said by her about [[colonel-osborne|Colonel Osborne]]. She would avoid that

[011-020][[social-class|gentleman]], never receiving him in Curzon Street, and having as little

[011-021]to say to him as possible elsewhere; but she would not throw his name

[011-022]in her husband's teeth, or make any reference to the injury which had

[011-023]so manifestly been done to her. Unless Louis should be indiscreet,

[011-024]it should be as though it had been forgotten. As they walked by

[011-025]Chesterfield House and Stanhope Street into the park, she began to

[011-026]discuss the sermon they had heard that morning, and when she found

[011-027]that that subject was not alluring, she spoke of a dinner to which

[011-028]they were to go at Mrs. Fairfax's house. Louis Trevelyan was quite

[011-029]aware that he was being treated as a naughty boy, who was to be

[011-030]forgiven.

[011-031]They went across Hyde Park into Kensington Gardens, and still the

[011-032]same thing was going on. Nora found it to be almost impossible to say

[011-033]a word. Trevelyan answered his wife's questions, but was otherwise

[011-034]silent. Emily worked very hard at her mission of forgiveness, and

[011-035]hardly ceased in her efforts at conciliatory conversation. Women

[011-036]can work so much harder in this way than men find it possible to do!

[011-037]She never flagged, but continued to be fluent, conciliatory, and

[011-038]intolerably wearisome. On a sudden they came across two men together,

[011-039]who, as they all knew, were barely acquainted with each other. These

[011-040]were Colonel Osborne and [[hugh-stanbury|Hugh Stanbury]].

[011-041]"I am glad to find you are able to be out," said the Colonel.

[011-042]"Thanks; yes. I think my seclusion just now was almost as much due to

[011-043]baby as to anything else. Mr. Stanbury, how is it we never see you

[011-044]now?"

[011-045]"It is the D. R., Mrs. Trevelyan;--nothing else. The D. R. is a most

[011-046]grateful mistress, but somewhat exacting. I am allowed a couple of

[011-047]hours on Sundays, but otherwise my time is wholly passed in Fleet

[011-048]Street."

[011-049]"How very unpleasant."

[011-050]"Well; yes. The unpleasantness of this world consists chiefly in the

[011-051]fact that when a man wants wages, he must earn them. The Christian

[011-052]philosophers have a theory about it. Don't they call it the primeval

[011-053]fall, original sin, and that kind of thing?"

[011-054]"Mr. Stanbury, I won't have irreligion. I hope that doesn't come from

[011-055]writing for the newspapers."

[011-056]"Certainly not with me, Mrs. Trevelyan. I have never been put on

[011-057]to take that branch yet. Scrubby does that with us, and does it

[011-058]excellently. It was he who touched up the Ritualists, and then the

[011-059]Commission, and then the Low Church bishops, till he didn't leave one

[011-060]of them a leg to stand upon."

[011-061]"What is it, then, that the Daily Record upholds?"

[011-062]"It upholds the Daily Record. Believe in that and you will surely be

[011-063]saved." Then he turned to Miss Rowley, and they two were soon walking

[011-064]on together, each manifestly interested in what the other was saying,

[011-065]though there was no word of tenderness spoken between them.

[011-066]Colonel Osborne was now between Mr. and Mrs. Trevelyan. She would

[011-067]have avoided the position had it been possible for her to do so.

[011-068]While they were falling into their present places, she had made a

[011-069]little mute appeal to her husband to take her away from the spot, to

[011-070]give her his arm and return with her, to save her in some way from

[011-071]remaining in company with the man to whose company for her he had

[011-072]objected; but he took no such step. It had seemed to him that he

[011-073]could take no such step without showing his hostility to Colonel

[011-074]Osborne.

[011-075]They walked on along the broad path together, and the Colonel was

[011-076]between them.

[011-077]"I hope you think it satisfactory,--about Sir Rowley," he said.

[011-078]"Beggars must not be choosers, you know, Colonel Osborne. I felt a

[011-079]little disappointed when I found that we were not to see them till

[011-080]February next."

[011-081]"They will stay longer then, you know, than they could now."

[011-082]"I have no doubt when the time comes we shall all believe it to be

[011-083]better."

[011-084]"I suppose you think, Emily, that a little pudding to-day is better

[011-085]than much to-morrow."

[011-086]Colonel Osborne certainly had a caressing, would-be affectionate mode

[011-087]of talking to women, which, unless it were reciprocated and enjoyed,

[011-088]was likely to make itself disagreeable. No possible words could have

[011-089]been more innocent than those he had now spoken; but he had turned

[011-090]his face down close to her face, and had almost whispered them. And

[011-091]then, too, he had again called her by her Christian name. Trevelyan

[011-092]had not heard the words. He had walked on, making the distance

[011-093]between him and the other man greater than was necessary, anxious to

[011-094]show to his wife that he had no jealousy at such a meeting as this.

[011-095]But his wife was determined that she would put an end to this state

[011-096]of things, let the cost be what it might. She did not say a word to

[011-097]Colonel Osborne, but addressed herself at once to her husband.

[011-098]"Louis," she said, "will you give me your arm? We will go back, if

[011-099]you please." Then she took her husband's arm, and turned herself and

[011-100]him abruptly away from their companion.

[011-101]The thing was done in such a manner that it was impossible that

[011-102]Colonel Osborne should not perceive that he had been left in anger.

[011-103]When Trevelyan and his wife had gone back a few yards, he was obliged

[011-104]to return for Nora. He did so, and then rejoined his wife.

[011-105]"It was quite unnecessary, Emily," he said, "that you should behave

[011-106]like that."

[011-107]"Your suspicions," she said, "have made it almost impossible for me

[011-108]to behave with [[social-custom|propriety]]."

[011-109]"You have told him everything now," said Trevelyan.

[011-110]"And it was requisite that he should be told," said his wife. Then

[011-111]they walked home without interchanging another word. When they

[011-112]reached their house, Emily at once went up to her own room, and

[011-113]Trevelyan to his. They parted as though they had no common interest

[011-114]which was worthy of a moment's conversation. And she by her step,

[011-115]and gait, and every movement of her body showed to him that she was

[011-116]not his wife now in any sense that could bring to him a feeling of

[011-117]domestic happiness. Her compliance with his command was of no use

[011-118]to him unless she could be brought to comply in spirit. Unless she

[011-119]would be soft to him he could not be happy. He walked about his room

[011-120]uneasily for half-an-hour, trying to shake off his sorrow, and then

[011-121]he went up to her room. "Emily," he said, "for God's sake let all

[011-122]this pass away."

[011-123]"What is to pass away?"

[011-124]"This feeling of rancour between you and me. What is the world to us

[011-125]unless we can love one another? At any rate it will be nothing to

[011-126]me."

[011-127]"Do you doubt my love?" said she.

[011-128]"No; certainly not."

[011-129]"Nor I yours. Without love, Louis, you and I can not be happy. But

[011-130]love alone will not make us so. There must be trust, and there must

[011-131]also be forbearance. My feeling of annoyance will pass away in time;

[011-132]and till it does, I will shew it as little as may be possible."

[011-133]He felt that he had nothing more to say, and then he left her; but he

[011-134]had gained nothing by the interview. She was still hard and cold, and

[011-135]still assumed a tone which seemed to imply that she had manifestly

[011-136]been the injured person.

[011-137]Colonel Osborne, when he was left alone, stood for a few moments on

[011-138]the spot, and then with a whistle, a shake of the head, and a little

[011-139]low chuckle of laughter, rejoined the crowd.

[011-140]CHAPTER VII.

[011-141]MISS JEMIMA STANBURY, OF EXETER.

[011-142][Illustration]

[011-143]Miss Jemima Stanbury, the aunt of our friend Hugh, was a maiden lady,

[011-144]very much respected, indeed, in the city of [[exeter|Exeter]]. It is to be

[011-145]hoped that no readers of these pages will be so un-English as to be

[011-146]unable to appreciate the difference between county society and town

[011-147]society,--the society, that is, of a provincial town, or so ignorant

[011-148]as not to know also that there may be persons so privileged, that

[011-149]although they live distinctly within a provincial town, there

[011-150]is accorded to them, as though by brevet rank, all the merit of

[011-151]living in the county. In reference to persons so privileged, it is

[011-152]considered that they have been made free from the contamination of

[011-153]contiguous bricks and mortar by certain inner gifts, probably of

[011-154]birth, occasionally of profession, possibly of merit. It is very

[011-155]rarely, indeed, that money alone will bestow this acknowledged

[011-156]rank; and in Exeter, which by the stringency and excellence of its

[011-157]well-defined rules on such matters, may perhaps be said to take the

[011-158]lead of all English provincial towns, money alone has never availed.

[011-159]Good blood, especially if it be blood good in Devonshire, is rarely

[011-160]rejected. Clergymen are allowed within the pale,--though by no means

[011-161]as certainly as used to be the case; and, indeed, in these days of

[011-162]literates, clergymen have to pass harder examinations than those ever

[011-163]imposed upon them by bishops' chaplains, before they are admitted ad

[011-164]eundem among the chosen ones of the city of Exeter. The wives and

[011-165]daughters of the old prebendaries see well to that. And, as has been

[011-166]said, special merit may prevail. Sir Peter Mancrudy, the great Exeter

[011-167]physician, has won his way in,--not at all by being Sir Peter, which

[011-168]has stood in his way rather than otherwise,--but by the acknowledged

[011-169]excellence of his book about saltzes. Sir Peter Mancrudy is supposed

[011-170]to have quite a metropolitan, almost a European [[honour|reputation]],--and

[011-171]therefore is acknowledged to belong to the county set, although he

[011-172]never dines out at any house beyond the limits of the city. Now, let

[011-173]it be known that no inhabitant of Exeter ever achieved a clearer

[011-174]right to be regarded as "county," in opposition to "town," than had

[011-175]Miss Jemima Stanbury. There was not a tradesman in Exeter who was not

[011-176]aware of it, and who did not touch his hat to her accordingly. The

[011-177]men who drove the flies, when summoned to take her out at night,

[011-178]would bring oats with them, knowing how probable it was that they

[011-179]might have to travel far. A distinct apology was made if she was

[011-180]asked to drink tea with people who were simply "town." The Noels of

[011-181]Doddescombe Leigh, the Cliffords of Budleigh Salterton, the Powels of

[011-182]Haldon, the Cheritons of Alphington,--all county persons, but very

[011-183]frequently in the city,--were greeted by her, and greeted her, on

[011-184]terms of equality. Her most intimate friend was old Mrs. MacHugh,

[011-185]the widow of the last dean but two, who could not have stood higher

[011-186]had she been the widow of the last bishop. And then, although Miss

[011-187]Stanbury was intimate with the Frenches of Heavitree, with the

[011-188]Wrights of Northernhay, with the Apjohns of Helion Villa,--a really

[011-189]magnificent house, two miles out of the city on the Crediton

[011-190]Road, and with the Crumbies of Cronstadt House, Saint Ide's,--who

[011-191]would have been county people, if living in the country made the

[011-192]difference;--although she was intimate with all these families,

[011-193]her manner to them was not the same, nor was it expected to be the

[011-194]same, as with those of her own acknowledged set. These things are

[011-195]understood in Exeter so well!
