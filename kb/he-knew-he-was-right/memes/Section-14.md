## Section 14

[014-001]"I suppose not," said Mrs. Stanbury, plaintively.

[014-002]"I could try," said Dorothy.

[014-003]"My dear, you don't know that woman," said Priscilla.

[014-004]"Of course I don't know her," said Dorothy.

[014-005]"She has always been very good to Hugh," said Mrs. Stanbury.

[014-006]"I don't think she has been good to him at all," said Priscilla.

[014-007]"But think what a saving it would be," said Dorothy. "And I could

[014-008]send home half of what Aunt Stanbury says she would give me."

[014-009]"You must not think of that," said Priscilla, "because she expects

[014-010]you to be dressed."

[014-011]"I should like to try," she said, before the morning was over,--"if

[014-012]you and mamma don't think it would be wrong."

[014-013]The conference that day ended in a written request to Aunt Stanbury

[014-014]that a week might be allowed for consideration,--the letter being

[014-015]written by Priscilla, but signed with her mother's name,--and with a

[014-016]very long epistle to Hugh, in which each of the ladies took a part,

[014-017]and in which advice and decision were demanded. It was very evident

[014-018]to Hugh that his mother and Dorothy were for compliance, and that

[014-019]Priscilla was for refusal. But he never doubted for a moment. "Of

[014-020]course she will go," he said in his answer to Priscilla; "and she

[014-021]must understand that Aunt Stanbury is a most excellent woman, as true

[014-022]as the sun, thoroughly honest, with no fault but this, that she likes

[014-023]her own way. Of course Dolly can go back again if she finds the house

[014-024]too hard for her." Then he sent another five-pound note, observing

[014-025]that Dolly's journey to [[exeter|Exeter]] would cost money, and that her

[014-026]wardrobe would want some improvement.

[014-027]"I'm very glad that it isn't me," said Priscilla, who, however, did

[014-028]not attempt to oppose the decision of the man of the family. Dorothy

[014-029]was greatly gratified by the excitement of the proposed change in

[014-030]her life, and the following letter, the product of the wisdom of the

[014-031]family, was written by Mrs. Stanbury:--

[014-032][[nuncombe-putney|Nuncombe Putney]], 1st May, 186--.

[014-033]MY DEAR SISTER STANBURY,

[014-034]We are all very thankful for the kindness of your offer,

[014-035]which my daughter Dorothy will accept with feelings of

[014-036]affectionate gratitude. I think you will find her docile,

[014-037]good-tempered, and amiable; but a mother, of course,

[014-038]speaks well of her own child. She will endeavour to comply

[014-039]with your wishes in all things reasonable. She, of course,

[014-040]understands that should the arrangement not suit, she will

[014-041]come back home on the expression of your wish that it

[014-042]should be so. And she will, of course, do the same, if she

[014-043]should find that living in Exeter does not suit herself.

[014-044][This sentence was inserted at the instance of Priscilla,

[014-045]after much urgent expostulation.] Dorothy will be ready

[014-046]to go to you on any day you may fix after the 7th of this

[014-047]month.

[014-048]Believe me to remain,

[014-049]Your affectionate sister-in-law,

[014-050]P. STANBURY.

[014-051]"She's going to come," said Miss Stanbury to Martha, holding the

[014-052]letter in her hand.

[014-053]"I never doubted her coming, ma'am," said Martha.

[014-054]"And I mean her to stay, unless it's her own fault. She'll have the

[014-055]small room up-stairs, looking out front, next to mine. And you must

[014-056]go and fetch her."

[014-057]"Go and fetch her, ma'am?"

[014-058]"Yes. If you won't, I must."

[014-059]"She ain't a child, ma'am. She's twenty-five years old, and surely

[014-060]she can come to Exeter by herself, with a railroad all the way from

[014-061]Lessboro'."

[014-062]"There's no place a young woman is insulted in so bad as those

[014-063]railway carriages, and I won't have her come by herself. If she is to

[014-064]live with me, she shall begin decently at any rate."

[014-065]Martha argued the matter, but was of course beaten, and on the day

[014-066]fixed started early in the morning for Nuncombe Putney, and returned

[014-067]in the afternoon to the Close with her charge. By the time that she

[014-068]had reached the house she had in some degree reconciled herself to

[014-069]the dangerous step that her mistress had taken, partly by perceiving

[014-070]that in face Dorothy Stanbury was very like her brother Hugh, and

[014-071]partly, perhaps, by finding that the young woman's manner to herself

[014-072]was both gentle and sprightly. She knew well that gentleness alone,

[014-073]without some back-bone of strength under it, would not long succeed

[014-074]with Miss Stanbury. "As far as I can judge, ma'am, she's a sweet

[014-075]young lady," said Martha, when she reported her arrival to her

[014-076]mistress, who had retired up-stairs to her own room, in order that

[014-077]she might thus hear a word of tidings from her lieutenant, before she

[014-078]showed herself on the field of action.

[014-079]"Sweet! I hate your sweets," said Miss Stanbury.

[014-080]"Then why did you send for her, ma'am?"

[014-081]"Because I was an old fool. But I must go down and receive her, I

[014-082]suppose."

[014-083]Then Miss Stanbury went down, almost trembling as she went. The

[014-084]matter to her was one of vital importance. She was going to change

[014-085]the whole tenour of her life for the sake,--as she told herself,--of

[014-086]doing her duty by a relative whom she did not even know. But we may

[014-087]fairly suppose that there had in truth been a feeling beyond that,

[014-088]which taught her to desire to have some one near her to whom she

[014-089]might not only do her duty as guardian, but whom she might also love.

[014-090]She had tried this with her nephew; but her nephew had been too

[014-091]strong for her, too far from her, too unlike to herself. When he came

[014-092]to see her he had smoked a short pipe,--which had been shocking to

[014-093]her,--and he had spoken of Reform, and Trades' Unions, and meetings

[014-094]in the parks, as though they had not been Devil's ordinances. And he

[014-095]was very shy of going to church,--utterly refusing to be taken there

[014-096]twice on the same Sunday. And he had told his aunt that owing to a

[014-097]peculiar and unfortunate weakness in his constitution he could not

[014-098]listen to the reading of sermons. And then she was almost certain

[014-099]that he had once kissed one of the maids! She had found it impossible

[014-100]to manage him in any way; and when he positively declared himself as

[014-101]permanently devoted to the degrading iniquities of penny newspapers,

[014-102]she had thought it best to cast him off altogether. Now, thus late in

[014-103]life, she was going to make another venture, to try an altogether new

[014-104]mode of living,--in order, as she said to herself, that she might be

[014-105]of some use to somebody,--but, no doubt, with a further unexpressed

[014-106]hope in her bosom, that the solitude of her life might be relieved by

[014-107]the companionship of some one whom she might love. She had arrayed

[014-108]herself in a clean cap and her evening gown, and she went down-stairs

[014-109]looking sternly, with a fully-developed idea that she must initiate

[014-110]her new duties by assuming a mastery at once. But inwardly she

[014-111]trembled, and was intensely anxious as to the first appearance of

[014-112]her niece. Of course there would be a little morsel of a bonnet.

[014-113]She hated those vile patches,--dirty flat daubs of millinery as

[014-114]she called them; but they had become too general for her to refuse

[014-115]admittance for such a thing within her doors. But a chignon, a

[014-116]bandbox behind the noddle,--she would not endure. And then there were

[014-117]other details of feminine gear, which shall not be specified, as to

[014-118]which she was painfully anxious,--almost forgetting in her anxiety

[014-119]that the dress of this young woman whom she was about to see must

[014-120]have ever been regulated by the closest possible economy.

[014-121]The first thing she saw on entering the room was a dark straw hat,

[014-122]a straw hat with a strong penthouse flap to it, and her heart was

[014-123]immediately softened.

[014-124]"My dear," she said, "I am glad to see you."

[014-125]Dorothy, who, on her part, was trembling also, whose position was one

[014-126]to justify most intense anxiety, murmured some reply.

[014-127]"Take off your hat," said the aunt, "and let me give you a kiss."

[014-128]The hat was taken off and the kiss was given. There was certainly no

[014-129]chignon there. Dorothy Stanbury was light haired, with almost flaxen

[014-130]ringlets, worn after the old-fashioned way which we used to think so

[014-131]pretty when we were young. She had very soft grey eyes, which ever

[014-132]seemed to beseech you to do something when they looked at you, and

[014-133]her mouth was a beseeching mouth. There are women who, even amidst

[014-134]their strongest efforts at giving assistance to others, always look

[014-135]as though they were asking aid themselves, and such a one was Dorothy

[014-136]Stanbury. Her complexion was pale, but there was always present in

[014-137]it a tint of pink running here and there, changing with every word

[014-138]she spoke, changing indeed with every pulse of her heart. Nothing

[014-139]ever was softer than her cheek; but her hands were thin and hard,

[014-140]and almost fibrous with the working of the thread upon them. She

[014-141]was rather tall than otherwise, but that extreme look of feminine

[014-142]dependence which always accompanied her, took away something even

[014-143]from the appearance of her height.

[014-144]"These are all real, at any rate," said her aunt, taking hold of the

[014-145]curls, "and won't be hurt by a little cold water."

[014-146]Dorothy smiled but said nothing, and was then taken up to her

[014-147]bed-room. Indeed, when the aunt and niece sat down to dinner together

[014-148]Dorothy had hardly spoken. But Miss Stanbury had spoken, and things

[014-149]upon the whole had gone very well.

[014-150]"I hope you like roast chicken, my dear?" said Miss Stanbury.

[014-151]"Oh, thank you."

[014-152]"And bread sauce? Jane, I do hope the bread sauce is hot."

[014-153]If the reader thinks that Miss Stanbury was indifferent to

[014-154]considerations of the table, the reader is altogether ignorant of

[014-155]Miss Stanbury's [[honour|character]]. When Miss Stanbury gave her niece the

[014-156]liver-wing, and picked out from the attendant sausages one that had

[014-157]been well browned and properly broken in the frying, she meant to do

[014-158]a real kindness.

[014-159]"And now, my dear, there are mashed potatoes and bread sauce. As for

[014-160]green vegetables, I don't know what has become of them. They tell me

[014-161]I may have green peas from France at a shilling a quart; but if I

[014-162]can't have English green peas, I won't have any."

[014-163]Miss Stanbury was [[reputation|standing]] up as she said this,--as she always did on

[014-164]such occasions, liking to have a full mastery over the dish.

[014-165]"I hope you like it, my dear?"

[014-166]"Everything is so very nice."

[014-167]"That's right. I like to see a young woman with an appetite. Remember

[014-168]that God sends the good things for us to eat; and as long as we

[014-169]don't take more than our share, and give away something to those who

[014-170]haven't a fair share of their own, I for one think it quite right to

[014-171]enjoy my victuals. Jane, this bread sauce isn't hot. It never is hot.

[014-172]Don't tell me; I know what hot is!"

[014-173]Dorothy thought that her aunt was very angry; but Jane knew Miss

[014-174]Stanbury better, and bore the scolding without shaking in her shoes.

[014-175]"And now, my dear, you must take a glass of port wine. It will do you

[014-176]good after your journey."

[014-177]Dorothy attempted to explain that she never did drink any wine, but

[014-178]her aunt talked down her scruples at once.

[014-179]"One glass of port wine never did anybody any harm, and as there is

[014-180]port wine, it must be intended that somebody should drink it."

[014-181]Miss Stanbury, as she sipped hers out very slowly, seemed to enjoy it

[014-182]much. Although May had come, there was a fire in the grate, and she

[014-183]sat with her toes on the fender, and her silk dress folded up above

[014-184]her knees. She sat quite silent in this position for a quarter of an

[014-185]hour, every now and then raising her glass to her lips. Dorothy sat

[014-186]silent also. To her, in the newness of her condition, speech was

[014-187]impossible.
