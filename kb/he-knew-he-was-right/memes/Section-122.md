## Section 122

[122-001]But something must be done. He had proceeded so far in forming a

[122-002]resolution, as he reached St. David's Church on his return homewards.

[122-003]His sagacious friend had told him that as soon as he had altered

[122-004]his mind, he was bound to let the lady know of it without delay.

[122-005]"You must remember," said the sagacious friend, "that you will owe

[122-006]her much,--very much." Mr. Gibson was perplexed in his mind when he

[122-007]reflected how much he might possibly be made to owe her if she should

[122-008]decide on appealing to a jury of her countrymen for justice. But

[122-009]anything would be better than his home at St. Peter's-cum-Pumpkin

[122-010]with Camilla sitting opposite to him as his wife. Were there not

[122-011]distant lands in which a clergyman, unfortunate but still energetic,

[122-012]might find work to do? Was there not all America?--and were there

[122-013]not Australia, New Zealand, Natal, all open to him? Would not a

[122-014]missionary career among the Chinese be better for him than St.

[122-015]Peter's-cum-Pumpkin with Camilla French for his wife? By the time he

[122-016]had reached home his mind was made up. He would write a letter to

[122-017]Camilla at once; and he would marry Arabella at once,--on any day

[122-018]that might be fixed,--on condition that Camilla would submit to her

[122-019]defeat without legal redress. If legal redress should be demanded, he

[122-020]would put in [[truth|evidence]] the fact that her own mother had been compelled

[122-021]to caution the tradesmen of the city in regard to her extravagance.

[122-022]He did write his letter,--in an agony of spirit. "I sit down,

[122-023]Camilla, with a sad heart and a reluctant hand," he said,

[122-024]to communicate to you a fatal truth. But truth should be

[122-025]made to prevail, and there is nothing in man so cowardly,

[122-026]so detrimental, and so unmanly as its concealment. I have

[122-027]looked into myself, and have inquired of myself, and have

[122-028]assured myself, that were I to become your husband, I

[122-029]should not make you happy. It would be of no use for me

[122-030]now to dilate on the reasons which have convinced me;--but

[122-031]I am convinced, and I consider it my duty to inform you so

[122-032]at once. I have been closeted with your mother, and have

[122-033]made her understand that it is so.

[122-034]I have not a word to say in my own justification but

[122-035]this,--that I am sure I am acting honestly in telling you

[122-036]the truth. I would not wish to say a word animadverting

[122-037]on yourself. If there must be blame in this matter, I am

[122-038]willing to take it all on my own shoulders. But things

[122-039]have been done of late, and words have been spoken, and

[122-040]habits have displayed themselves, which would not, I am

[122-041]sure, conduce to our mutual comfort in this world, or to

[122-042]our assistance to each other in our struggles to reach the

[122-043]happiness of the world to come.

[122-044]I think that you will agree with me, Camilla, that when

[122-045]a man or a woman has fallen into such a mistake as

[122-046]that which I have now made, it is best that it should

[122-047]be acknowledged. I know well that such a change of

[122-048]arrangements as that which I now propose will be regarded

[122-049]most unfavourably. But will not anything be better than

[122-050]the binding of a matrimonial knot which cannot be again

[122-051]unloosed, and which we should both regret?

[122-052]I do not know that I need add anything further. What can

[122-053]I add further? Only this;--that I am inflexible. Having

[122-054]resolved to take this step,--and to bear the evil things

[122-055]that may be said of me,--for your happiness and for my own

[122-056]tranquility,--I shall not now relinquish my resolution. I

[122-057]do not ask you to forgive me. I doubt much whether I shall

[122-058]ever be quite able to forgive myself. The mistake which

[122-059]I have made is one which should not have been committed.

[122-060]I do not ask you to forgive me; but I do ask you to pray

[122-061]that I may be forgiven.

[122-062]Yours, with feelings of the truest friendship,

[122-063]THOMAS GIBSON.

[122-064]The letter had been very difficult, but he was rather proud of it

[122-065]than otherwise when it was completed. He had felt that he was writing

[122-066]a letter which not improbably might become public [[money|property]]. It was

[122-067]necessary that he should be firm, that he should accuse himself a

[122-068]little in order that he might excuse himself much, and that he should

[122-069]hint at causes which might justify the rupture, though he should

[122-070]so veil them as not to appear to defend his own delinquency by

[122-071]ungenerous counter-accusation. When he had completed the letter,

[122-072]he thought that he had done all this rather well, and he sent the

[122-073]despatch off to Heavitree by the clerk of St. Peter's Church, with

[122-074]something of that feeling of expressible relief which attends the

[122-075]final conquest over some fatal and all but insuperable misfortune. He

[122-076]thought that he was sure now that he would not have to marry Camilla

[122-077]on the 29th of the month,--and there would probably be a period of

[122-078]some hours before he would be called upon to hear or read Camilla's

[122-079]reply.

[122-080]Camilla was alone when she received the letter, but she rushed at

[122-081]once to her mother. "There," said she; "there--I knew that it was

[122-082]coming!" Mrs. French took the paper into her hands, and gasped, and

[122-083]gazed at her daughter without speaking. "You knew of it, mother."

[122-084]"Yesterday,--when he told me, I knew of it."

[122-085]"And Bella knows it."

[122-086]"Not a word of it."

[122-087]"She does. I am sure she does. But it is all nothing. I will not

[122-088]accept it. He cannot treat me so. I will drag him there;--but he

[122-089]shall come."

[122-090]"You can't make him, my dear."

[122-091]"I will make him. And you would help me, mamma, if you had any

[122-092]spirit. What,--a fortnight before the time, when the things are all

[122-093]bought! Look at the presents that have been sent! Mamma, he doesn't

[122-094]know me. And he never would have done it, if it had not been for

[122-095]Bella,--never. She had better take care, or there shall be such a

[122-096]tragedy that nobody ever heard the like. If she thinks that she is

[122-097]going to be that man's wife,--she is--mistaken." Then there was a

[122-098]pause for a moment. "Mamma," she said, "I shall go to him at once.

[122-099]I do not care in the least what anybody may say. I shall--go to

[122-100]him,--at once." Mrs. French felt that at this moment it was best that

[122-101]she should be silent.

[122-102]CHAPTER LXXV.

[122-103]THE ROWLEYS GO OVER THE ALPS.

[122-104][Illustration]

[122-105]By the thirteenth of May the Rowley family had established itself in

[122-106]Florence, purposing to remain either there or at the baths of Lucca

[122-107]till the end of June, at which time it was thought that Sir Marmaduke

[122-108]should begin to make preparations for his journey back to the

[122-109]Islands. Their future prospects were not altogether settled. It was

[122-110]not decided whether Lady Rowley should at once return with him,

[122-111]whether Mrs. Trevelyan should return with him,--nor was it settled

[122-112]among them what should be the fate of [[nora-rowley|Nora Rowley]]. Nora Rowley was

[122-113]quite resolved herself that she would not go back to the Islands, and

[122-114]had said as much to her mother. Lady Rowley had not repeated this

[122-115]to Sir Marmaduke, and was herself in doubt as to what might best be

[122-116]done. Girls are understood by their mothers better than they are by

[122-117]their fathers. Lady Rowley was beginning to be aware that Nora's

[122-118]obstinacy was too strong to be overcome by mere words, and that other

[122-119]steps must be taken if she were to be weaned from her pernicious

[122-120]passion for [[hugh-stanbury|Hugh Stanbury]]. Mr. Glascock was still in Florence. Might

[122-121]she not be cured by further overtures from Mr. Glascock? The chance

[122-122]of securing such a son-in-law was so important, so valuable, that no

[122-123]trouble was too great to be incurred, even though the probability of

[122-124]success might not be great.

[122-125]It must not, however, be supposed that Lady Rowley carried off all

[122-126]the family to Italy, including Sir Marmaduke, simply in chase of

[122-127]Mr. Glascock. Anxious as she was on the subject, she was too proud,

[122-128]and also too well-conditioned, to have suggested to herself such a

[122-129]journey with such an object. Trevelyan had escaped from Willesden

[122-130]with the child, and they had heard,--again through Stanbury,--that

[122-131]he had returned to Italy. They had all agreed that it would be well

[122-132]that they should leave [[london|London]] for awhile, and see something of

[122-133]the Continent; and when it was told to them that little Louis was

[122-134]probably in Florence, that alone was reason enough for them to go

[122-135]thither. They would go to the city till the heat was too great and

[122-136]the mosquitoes too powerful, and then they would visit the baths

[122-137]of Lucca for a month. This was their plan of action, and the cause

[122-138]for their plan; but Lady Rowley found herself able to weave into it

[122-139]another little plan of her own of which she said nothing to anybody.

[122-140]She was not running after Mr. Glascock; but if Mr. Glascock should

[122-141]choose to run after them,--or her, who could say that any harm had

[122-142]been done?

[122-143]Nora had answered that proposition of her lover's to walk out of the

[122-144]house in Manchester Street, and get married at the next church, in a

[122-145]most discreet manner. She had declared that she would be true and

[122-146]firm, but that she did not wish to draw upon herself the displeasure

[122-147]of her father and mother. She did not, she said, look upon a

[122-148]clandestine marriage as a happy resource. But,--this she added at the

[122-149]end of a long and very sensible letter,--she intended to abide by her

[122-150]engagement, and she did not intend to go back to the Mandarins. She

[122-151]did not say what alternative she would choose in the event of her

[122-152]being unable to obtain her father's consent before his return. She

[122-153]did not suggest what was to become of her when Sir Marmaduke's leave

[122-154]of absence should be expired. But her statement that she would not go

[122-155]back to the islands was certainly made with more substantial vigour,

[122-156]though, perhaps, with less of reasoning, than any other of the

[122-157]propositions made in her letter. Then, in her postscript, she told

[122-158]him that they were all going to Italy. "Papa and mamma think that we

[122-159]ought to follow poor Mr. Trevelyan. The lawyer says that nothing can

[122-160]be done while he is away with the boy. We are therefore all going to

[122-161]start to Florence. The journey is delightful. I will not say whose

[122-162]presence will be wanting to make it perfect."

[122-163]Before they started there came a letter to Nora from Dorothy, which

[122-164]shall be given entire, because it will tell the reader more of

[122-165]Dorothy's happiness than would be learned from any other mode of

[122-166]narrative.

[122-167]The Close, Thursday.

[122-168]DEAREST NORA,

[122-169]I have just had a letter from Hugh, and that makes me feel

[122-170]that I should like to write to you. Dear Hugh has told me

[122-171]all about it, and I do so hope that things may come right

[122-172]and that we may be sisters. He is so good that I do not

[122-173]wonder that you should love him. He has been the best son

[122-174]and the best brother in the world, and everybody speaks

[122-175]well of him,--except my dear aunt, who is prejudiced

[122-176]because she does not like newspapers. I need not praise

[122-177]him to you, for I dare say you think quite as well of him

[122-178]as I do. I cannot tell you all the beautiful things he

[122-179]says about you, but I dare say he has told them to you

[122-180]himself.

[122-181]I seem to know you so well because Priscilla has talked

[122-182]about you so often. She says that she knew that you and

[122-183]my brother were fond of each other because you growled at

[122-184]each other when you were together at the [[clock-house|Clock House]], and

[122-185]never had any civil words to say before people. I don't

[122-186]know whether growling is a sign of love, but Hugh does

[122-187]growl sometimes when he is most affectionate. He growls at

[122-188]me, and I understand him, and I like to be growled at. I

[122-189]wonder whether you like him to growl at you.
