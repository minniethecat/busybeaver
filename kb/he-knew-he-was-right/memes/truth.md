# Truth

The contested concept of truth that underlies the central tragedy of *He Knew He Was Right*. The novel's title ironizes Trevelyan's conviction: he "knew" he was right about his wife's infidelity, and this certainty, rather than any actual infidelity, destroys him.

## Overview

Trollope's novel is fundamentally an examination of the gap between subjective conviction and objective truth. Louis Trevelyan is absolutely certain that his wife Emily is conducting an improper relationship with Colonel Osborne. The reader, the narrator, and every other character in the novel know this certainty to be false. The narrator states directly that "the reader may be quite certain that Colonel Osborne had no" improper intentions (003-087), establishing an objective truth against which Trevelyan's subjective conviction is measured. The tragedy lies not in what is true but in what Trevelyan believes to be true. "But when he was alone, he knew that he" was wrong, yet this knowledge could not dislodge his conviction (002-126). The novel's title functions as bitter dramatic irony: he "knew" he was right, and that knowledge was precisely his ruin.

## Context in Victorian Society

The Victorian period saw intense debate about the nature of evidence and proof, influenced by developments in legal theory, natural science, and biblical criticism. The legal standard of "reasonable doubt" was being refined, and the emerging social sciences were developing new theories about delusion and monomania. Trevelyan's condition — absolute certainty in the face of contrary evidence — maps onto contemporary medical discourse about "monomania," a form of partial insanity in which the sufferer is rational in all matters except one fixed delusion.

## Manifestation in the Novel

### The Certainty That Destroys

Trevelyan's tragedy is that his conviction grows in inverse proportion to the evidence. The more Emily protests her innocence, the more certain he becomes of her guilt. The narrator traces this logic with clinical precision: Trevelyan demands proof of innocence, but no proof can satisfy him because his suspicion has become its own self-validating system. His jealousy operates despite his own intermittent recognition of its irrationality — "in truth he did so because he" could not help himself (003-104). Trevelyan "knew himself to be a man blasted by jealousy, and yet he did not cease" to act on it (032-036). The novel thus dramatizes what modern psychology would call confirmation bias — the tendency to interpret all evidence as supporting one's pre-existing belief.

### Truth and Gender

The novel also examines how truth claims are gendered in Victorian society. Trevelyan's word carries legal weight; Emily's does not. When he accuses, she must prove her innocence — a reversal of the legal principle that the accuser bears the burden of proof. The novel exposes the structural injustice of a system in which a husband's suspicion carries more evidentiary weight than a wife's denial.

### Narrative Truth

Trollope's narrator occupies a unique position as the arbiter of truth. The narrator tells us directly that Emily is innocent and that Osborne's intentions are harmless. This narrative omniscience creates a peculiar readerly experience: we know Trevelyan is wrong, yet we watch him destroy himself with unshakeable conviction. The gap between narrative truth and character belief generates the novel's distinctive tragic irony.

## Key Examples

### The Demand for Proof

Trevelyan repeatedly demands that Emily "prove" her innocence, but the demand itself is impossible to satisfy. How does one prove the absence of an affair? The structure of accusation makes exoneration structurally impossible — a dynamic the novel exposes with considerable psychological sophistication.

### The Epistemic Asymmetry

Emily's position is epistemically helpless: she cannot know what Trevelyan thinks he knows, and she cannot disprove what exists only in his imagination. The narrator captures this asymmetry in scenes where Emily attempts to reason with a husband who has abandoned reason, creating some of the novel's most painful dramatic moments.

### The Reader's Knowledge

The novel's management of reader knowledge is crucial to its effect. By establishing Emily's innocence early and unequivocally, Trollope transforms the plot from a mystery ("is she guilty?") into a tragedy ("how will his false certainty destroy them?"). The narrator's direct assertion that "the reader may be quite certain that Colonel Osborne had no" improper intent (003-087) removes ambiguity and elevates the reader to a position of superior knowledge. This dramatic irony intensifies as Trevelyan's delusion deepens.
