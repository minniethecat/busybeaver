## Section 4

[004-001]"If I were to manage it, as you call it,--which I can't do at

[004-002]all,--it would be a gross job."

[004-003]"That's all nonsense to us, [[colonel-osborne|Colonel Osborne]]. Ladies always like

[004-004]political jobs, and think that they,--and they only,--make politics

[004-005]bearable. But this would not be a job at all. Papa could do it better

[004-006]than anybody else. Think how long he has been at it!"

[004-007]The matter in discussion was the chance of an order being sent out to

[004-008]Sir Marmaduke to come home from his islands at the public expense,

[004-009]to give [[truth|evidence]], respecting colonial government in general, to a

[004-010]committee of the House of Commons which was about to sit on the

[004-011]subject. The committee had been voted, and two governors were to be

[004-012]brought home for the purpose of giving evidence. What arrangement

[004-013]could be so pleasant to a governor living in the [[mandarin-islands|Mandarin Islands]],

[004-014]who had had a holiday lately, and who could but ill afford to

[004-015]take any holidays at his own expense? Colonel Osborne was on this

[004-016]committee, and, moreover, was on good terms at the Colonial Office.

[004-017]There were men in office who would be glad to do Colonel Osborne a

[004-018]service, and then if this were a job, it would be so very little of

[004-019]a job! Perhaps Sir Marmaduke might not be the very best man for the

[004-020]purpose. Perhaps the government of the Mandarins did not afford the

[004-021]best specimen of that colonial lore which it was the business of the

[004-022]committee to master. But then two governors were to come, and it

[004-023]might be as well to have one of the best sort, and one of the second

[004-024]best. No one supposed that excellent old Sir Marmaduke was a paragon

[004-025]of a governor, but then he had an infinity of experience! For over

[004-026]twenty years he had been from island to island, and had at least

[004-027]steered clear of great scrapes.

[004-028]"We'll try it, at any rate," said the Colonel.

[004-029]"Do, Colonel Osborne. Mamma would come with him, of course?"

[004-030]"We should leave him to manage all that. It's not very likely that he

[004-031]would leave Lady Rowley behind."

[004-032]"He never has. I know he thinks more of mamma than he ever does of

[004-033]himself. Fancy having them here in the autumn! I suppose if he came

[004-034]for the end of the session, they wouldn't send him back quite at

[004-035]once?"

[004-036]"I rather fancy that our foreign and colonial servants know how to

[004-037]stretch a point when they find themselves in England."

[004-038]"Of course they do, Colonel Osborne; and why shouldn't they? Think of

[004-039]all that they have to endure out in those horrible places. How would

[004-040]you like to live in the Mandarins?"

[004-041]"I should prefer [[london|London]], certainly."

[004-042]"Of course you would; and you mustn't begrudge papa a month or two

[004-043]when he comes. I never cared about your being in Parliament before,

[004-044]but I shall think so much of you now if you can manage to get papa

[004-045]home."

[004-046]There could be nothing more innocent than this,--nothing more

[004-047]innocent at any rate as regarded any offence against Mr. Trevelyan.

[004-048]But just then there came a word which a little startled Mrs.

[004-049]Trevelyan, and made her feel afraid that she was doing wrong.

[004-050]"I must make one stipulation with you, Emily," said the Colonel.

[004-051]"What is that?"

[004-052]"You must not tell your husband."

[004-053]"Oh, dear! and why not?"

[004-054]"I am sure you are sharp enough to see why you should not. A word of

[004-055]this repeated at any club would put an end at once to your project,

[004-056]and would be very damaging to me. And, beyond that, I wouldn't wish

[004-057]him to know that I had meddled with it at all. I am very chary of

[004-058]having my name connected with anything of the kind; and, upon my

[004-059]word, I wouldn't do it for any living human being but yourself.

[004-060]You'll promise me, Emily?"

[004-061]She gave the promise, but there were two things in the matter, as it

[004-062]stood at present, which she did not at all like. She was very averse

[004-063]to having any secret from her husband with Colonel Osborne; and she

[004-064]was not at all pleased at being told that he was doing for her a

[004-065]favour that he would not have done for any other living human being.

[004-066]Had he said so to her yesterday, before those offensive words had

[004-067]been spoken by her husband, she would not have thought much about it.

[004-068]She would have connected the man's friendship for herself with his

[004-069]very old friendship for her father, and she would have regarded the

[004-070]assurance as made to the Rowleys in general, and not to herself in

[004-071]particular. But now, after what had occurred, it pained her to be

[004-072]told by Colonel Osborne that he would make, specially on her behalf,

[004-073]a sacrifice of his political pride which he would make for no other

[004-074]person living. And then, as he had called her by her Christian name,

[004-075]as he had exacted the promise, there had been a tone of affection in

[004-076]his voice that she had almost felt to be too warm. But she gave the

[004-077]promise; and when he pressed her hand at parting, she pressed his

[004-078]again, in token of gratitude for the kindness to be done to her

[004-079]father and mother.

[004-080]Immediately afterwards Colonel Osborne went away, and Mrs. Trevelyan

[004-081]was left alone in her drawing-room. She knew that her husband was

[004-082]still down-stairs, and listened for a moment to hear whether he would

[004-083]now come up to her. And he, too, had heard the Colonel's step as he

[004-084]went, and for a few moments had doubted whether or no he would at

[004-085]once go to his wife. Though he believed himself to be a man very firm

[004-086]of purpose, his mind had oscillated backwards and forwards within the

[004-087]last quarter of an hour between those two purposes of being round

[004-088]with his wife, and of begging her pardon for the words which he

[004-089]had already spoken. He believed that he would best do his duty by

[004-090]that plan of being round with her; but then it would be so much

[004-091]pleasanter--at any rate, so much easier, to beg her pardon. But of

[004-092]one thing he was quite certain, he must by some means exclude Colonel

[004-093]Osborne from his house. He could not live and continue to endure the

[004-094]feelings which he had suffered while sitting down-stairs at his desk,

[004-095]with the knowledge that Colonel Osborne was closeted with his wife

[004-096]up-stairs. It might be that there was nothing in it. That his wife

[004-097]was innocent he was quite sure. But nevertheless, he was himself so

[004-098]much affected by some feeling which pervaded him in reference to this

[004-099]man, that all his energy was destroyed, and his powers of mind and

[004-100]body were paralysed. He could not, and would not, stand it. Rather

[004-101]than that he would follow Mr. Poole, and take his wife to Naples. So

[004-102]resolving, he put his hat on his head and walked out of the house. He

[004-103]would have the advantage of the afternoon's consideration before he

[004-104]took either the one step or the other.

[004-105]As soon as he was gone [[emily-trevelyan|Emily Trevelyan]] went up-stairs to her baby.

[004-106]She would not stir as long as there had been a chance of his coming

[004-107]to her. She very much wished that he would come, and had made up her

[004-108]mind, in spite of the fierceness of her assertion to her sister, to

[004-109]accept any slightest hint at an apology which her husband might offer

[004-110]to her. To this state of mind she was brought by the consciousness of

[004-111]having a secret from him, and by a sense not of impropriety on her

[004-112]own part, but of conduct which some people might have called improper

[004-113]in her mode of parting from the man against whom her husband had

[004-114]warned her. The warmth of that hand-pressing, and the affectionate

[004-115]tone in which her name had been pronounced, and the promise made to

[004-116]her, softened her heart towards her husband. Had he gone to her now

[004-117]and said a word to her in gentleness all might have been made right.

[004-118]But he did not go to her.

[004-119]"If he chooses to be cross and sulky, he may be cross and sulky,"

[004-120]said Mrs. Trevelyan to herself as she went up to her baby.

[004-121]"Has Louis been with you?" Nora asked, as soon as Mrs. Fairfax had

[004-122]brought her home.

[004-123]"I have not seen him since you left me," said Mrs. Trevelyan.

[004-124]"I suppose he went out before Colonel Osborne?"

[004-125]"No, indeed. He waited till Colonel Osborne had gone, and then he

[004-126]went himself; but he did not come near me. It is for him to judge of

[004-127]his own conduct, but I must say that I think he is very foolish."

[004-128]This the young wife said in a tone which clearly indicated that she

[004-129]had judged her husband's conduct, and had found it to be very foolish

[004-130]indeed.

[004-131]"Do you think that papa and mamma will really come?" said Nora,

[004-132]changing the subject of conversation.

[004-133]"How can I tell? How am I to know? After all that has passed I am

[004-134]afraid to say a word lest I should be accused of doing wrong. But

[004-135]remember this, Nora, you are not to speak of it to any one."

[004-136]"You will tell Louis?"

[004-137]"No; I will tell no one."

[004-138]"Dear, dear Emily; pray do not keep anything secret from him."

[004-139]"What do you mean by secret? There isn't any secret. Only in such

[004-140]matters as that,--about politics,--no [[social-class|gentleman]] likes to have his

[004-141]name talked about!"

[004-142]A look of great distress came upon Nora's face as she heard this. To

[004-143]her it seemed to be very bad that there should be a secret between

[004-144]her sister and Colonel Osborne to be kept from her brother-in-law.

[004-145]"I suppose you will suspect me next?" said Mrs. Trevelyan, angrily.

[004-146]"Emily, how can you say anything so cruel?"

[004-147]"You look as if you did."

[004-148]"I only mean that I think it would be wiser to tell all this to

[004-149]Louis."

[004-150]"How can I tell him Colonel Osborne's private business, when Colonel

[004-151]Osborne has desired me not to do so. For whose sake is Colonel

[004-152]Osborne doing this? For papa's and mamma's! I suppose Louis won't

[004-153]be--jealous, because I want to have papa and mamma home. It would not

[004-154]be a bit less unreasonable than the other."

[004-155]CHAPTER III.

[004-156]LADY MILBOROUGH'S DINNER PARTY.

[004-157][[louis-trevelyan|Louis Trevelyan]] went down to his club in Pall Mall, the Acrobats, and

[004-158]there heard a rumour that added to his anger against Colonel Osborne.

[004-159]The Acrobats was a very distinguished club, into which it was now

[004-160]difficult for a young man to find his way, and almost impossible

[004-161]for a man who was no longer young, and therefore known to many. It

[004-162]had been founded some twenty years since with the idea of promoting

[004-163]muscular exercise and gymnastic amusements; but the promoters had

[004-164]become fat and lethargic, and the Acrobats spent their time mostly in

[004-165]playing whist, and in ordering and eating their dinners. There were

[004-166]supposed to be, in some out-of-the-way part of the building, certain

[004-167]poles and sticks and parallel bars with which feats of activity might

[004-168]be practised, but no one ever asked for them now-a-days, and a man,

[004-169]when he became an Acrobat, did so with a view either to the whist or

[004-170]the cook, or possibly to the social excellences of the club. Louis

[004-171]Trevelyan was an Acrobat;--as was also Colonel Osborne.

[004-172]"So old Rowley is coming home," said one distinguished Acrobat to

[004-173]another in Trevelyan's hearing.

[004-174]"How the deuce is he managing that? He was here a year ago?"

[004-175]"Osborne is getting it done. He is to come as a witness for this

[004-176]committee. It must be no end of a lounge for him. It doesn't count as

[004-177]leave, and he has every shilling paid for him, down to his cab-fares

[004-178]when he goes out to dinner. There's nothing like having a friend at

[004-179]Court."
