# Trevelyan Household

The **Trevelyan Household** is the domestic establishment of [[Louis Trevelyan|Louis]] and [[Emily Trevelyan]] in [[Anthony Trollope]]'s *He Knew He Was Right* (1869). Located in a fashionable London square, the household begins the novel as an image of ideal Victorian domesticity — a beautiful wife, a wealthy husband, an infant son, and the material comforts appropriate to their station. Its dissolution over the course of the novel mirrors and materializes the collapse of the [[Marriage|marriage]] at its centre, making the household itself a symbolic entity in the novel's critique of Victorian domestic ideology.

## Structure

The Trevelyan household follows the classic Victorian domestic hierarchy:

| Position | Occupant | Authority |
|----------|---------|-----------|
| Head of household | [[Louis Trevelyan]] | Legal and financial authority; sole control of property, children, and domicile |
| Wife and mistress | [[Emily Trevelyan]] | Domestic management; no independent legal standing |
| Child | Louis Trevelyan (infant) | Object of custody disputes; legally belongs to the father |
| Servants | Unnamed | Execute domestic labour; answerable ultimately to the master |

This structure is both typical and, as the novel demonstrates, potentially lethal. Because Victorian law gave the husband absolute control over the household — including the power to expel his wife and retain their child — Trevelyan's jealousy has institutional force behind it. The household is not merely a setting but a legal apparatus that enables his tyranny.

## Dissolution

The household's breakdown proceeds in stages:

1. **Intact** (Chapters 1–5): The Trevelyans live together in apparent harmony, though jealousy festers beneath the surface
2. **Contested** (Chapters 6): Trevelyan orders Emily to leave; she refuses, asserting her right to remain in the marital home
3. **Broken** (Chapters 6–11): Emily departs for [[Nuncombe Putney]]; the household ceases to function as a domestic unit
4. **Dissolved** (Chapters 12+): Trevelyan lives alone, then abandons the house entirely for Italy; the physical household is gone, and only the legal shell remains

## Significance

The Trevelyan Household embodies the novel's argument about the vulnerability of Victorian wives. Because a married woman had no independent legal existence, her access to shelter, income, and her own children depended entirely on her husband's will. The household's dissolution is therefore not merely a consequence of marital breakdown but a demonstration of the structural inequality that made such breakdowns catastrophic for women.
