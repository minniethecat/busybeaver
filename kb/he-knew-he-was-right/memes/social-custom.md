# Social Custom

The unwritten code of Victorian social convention that governs behaviour in *He Knew He Was Right*, shaping the possibilities and constraints within which characters navigate marriage, friendship, and reputation.

## Overview

Social custom in Trollope's novel functions as both a stabilizing framework and a repressive force. Characters who conform to custom — observing proper visiting hours, maintaining appropriate friendships, respecting class boundaries — are rewarded with social approval. Characters who transgress custom, even innocently, face suspicion and censure. Emily Trevelyan's insistence on receiving Colonel Osborne's visits is, by the standards of Victorian custom, a breach of propriety — not because anything improper occurs, but because the appearance of impropriety is itself a social offense (003-087).

## Context in Victorian Society

Victorian society was governed by an elaborate code of unwritten rules: who could visit whom, for how long, with what degree of intimacy, under what circumstances. These rules were particularly stringent for married women, whose reputation depended on strict observance of propriety. The novel registers the suffocating force of these conventions while also acknowledging their social function: custom provides predictability and stability in a society undergoing rapid change.

## Manifestation in the Novel

Custom operates most powerfully in the novel through the mechanism of gossip and public opinion. The "certain ladies about the" Trevelyans' social circle (003-075) function as a chorus of customary judgment, interpreting every action through the lens of convention. Trevelyan himself is partly driven by fear of what these customary arbiters will think — his jealousy is inseparable from his concern for social appearance. A husband must navigate "the advantages of his own position" within this customary matrix (005-112), and failure to do so invites social penalty.

## Key Examples

### Visiting Etiquette

The entire plot turns on a question of visiting etiquette: is it proper for a married woman to receive visits from an unmarried male friend of her father? By Victorian standards, the answer was ambiguous — and this ambiguity is precisely what Trevelyan's jealousy exploits. The narrator explicitly flags "the danger of Mrs. Trevelyan's position" (003-084), indicating that custom could condemn even innocent behaviour. The novel uses this customary gray zone to generate its central conflict.

### Custom and Courtship

The novel's courtship plots are all structured by customary expectations about how men and women should meet, court, and marry. Mr. Gibson's comic failure as a suitor partly reflects his inability to navigate the customary rituals of courtship with appropriate grace.
