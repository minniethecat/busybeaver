## Section 131

[131-001]This had occurred a full week after the evening spent at Mr.

[131-002]Spalding's house; and even yet Lady Rowley had never been put right

[131-003]as to that mistake of hers about Wallachia Petrie. That other trouble

[131-004]of hers, and her eldest daughter's journey to Siena, had prevented

[131-005]them from going out; and though the matter had often been discussed

[131-006]between Lady Rowley and Nora, there had not as yet come between them

[131-007]any proper explanation. Nora would declare that the future bride

[131-008]was very pretty and very delightful; and Lady Rowley would throw up

[131-009]her hands in despair and protest that her daughter was insane. "Why

[131-010]should he not marry whom he likes, mamma?" Nora once said, almost

[131-011]with indignation.

[131-012]"Because he will disgrace his family."

[131-013]"I cannot understand what you mean, mamma. They are, at any rate, as

[131-014]good as we are. Mr. Spalding stands quite as high as papa does."

[131-015]"She is an American," said Lady Rowley.

[131-016]"And her family might say that he is an Englishman," said Nora.

[131-017]"My dear, if you do not understand the incongruity between an English

[131-018]peer and a Yankee--female, I cannot help you. I suppose it is because

[131-019]you have been brought up within the limited society of a small

[131-020]colony. If so, it is not your fault. But I had hoped you had been in

[131-021]Europe long enough to have learned what was what. Do you think, my

[131-022]dear, that she will look well when she is presented to her Majesty as

[131-023]Lord Peterborough's wife?"

[131-024]"Splendid," said Nora. "She has just the brow for a coronet."

[131-025]"Heavens and earth!" said Lady Rowley, throwing up her hands. "And

[131-026]you believe that he will be proud of her in England?"

[131-027]"I am sure he will."

[131-028]"My belief is that he will leave her behind him, or that they

[131-029]will settle somewhere in the wilds of America,--out in Mexico, or

[131-030]Massachusetts, or the Rocky Mountains. I do not think that he will

[131-031]have the courage to shew her in [[london|London]]."

[131-032]The marriage was to take place in the Protestant church at Florence

[131-033]early in June, and then the bride and bridegroom were to go over the

[131-034]Alps, and to remain there subject to tidings as to the health of the

[131-035]old man at Naples. Mr. Glascock had thrown up his seat in Parliament,

[131-036]some month or two ago, knowing that he could not get back to his

[131-037]duties during the present session, and feeling that he would shortly

[131-038]be called upon to sit in the other House. He was thus free to use his

[131-039]time and to fix his days as he pleased; and it was certainly clear to

[131-040]those who knew him, that he was not ashamed of his American bride.

[131-041]He spent much of his time at the Spaldings' house, and was always

[131-042]to be seen with them in the Cascine and at the Opera. Mrs. Spalding,

[131-043]the aunt, was, of course, in great glory. A triumphant, happy, or

[131-044]even simply a splendid marriage, for the rising girl of a family is

[131-045]a great glory to the maternal mind. Mrs. Spalding could not but be

[131-046]aware that the very air around her seemed to breathe congratulations

[131-047]into her ears. Her friends spoke to her, even on indifferent

[131-048]subjects, as though everything was going well with her,--better with

[131-049]her than with anybody else; and there came upon her in these days

[131-050]a dangerous feeling, that in spite of all the preachings of the

[131-051]preachers, the next world might perhaps be not so very much better

[131-052]than this. She was, in fact, the reverse of the medal of which

[131-053]poor Lady Rowley filled the obverse. And the American Minister was

[131-054]certainly an inch taller than before, and made longer speeches, being

[131-055]much more regardless of interruption. Olivia was delighted at her

[131-056]sister's success, and heard with rapture the description of Monkhams,

[131-057]which came to her second-hand through her sister. It was already

[131-058]settled that she was to spend her next Christmas at Monkhams, and

[131-059]perhaps there might be an idea in her mind that there were other

[131-060]eldest sons of old lords who would like American brides. Everything

[131-061]around Caroline Spalding was pleasant,--except the words of Wallachia

[131-062]Petrie.

[131-063]Everything around her was pleasant till there came to her a touch of

[131-064]a suspicion that the marriage which Mr. Glascock was going to make

[131-065]would be detrimental to her intended husband in his own country.

[131-066]There were many in Florence who were saying this besides the wife of

[131-067]the English Minister and Lady Rowley. Of course Caroline Spalding

[131-068]herself was the last to hear it, and to her the idea was brought

[131-069]by Wallachia Petrie. "I wish I could think you would make yourself

[131-070]happy,--or him," Wallachia had said, croaking.

[131-071]"Why should I fail to make him happy?"

[131-072]"Because you are not of the same blood, or race, or manners as

[131-073]himself. They say that he is very wealthy in his own country, and

[131-074]that those who live around him will look coldly on you."

[131-075]"So that he does not look coldly, I do not care how others may look,"

[131-076]said Caroline proudly.

[131-077]"But when he finds that he has injured himself by such a marriage in

[131-078]the estimation of all his friends,--how will it be then?"

[131-079]This set Caroline Spalding thinking of what she was doing. She began

[131-080]to realise the feeling that perhaps she might not be a fit bride for

[131-081]an English lord's son, and in her agony she came to [[nora-rowley|Nora Rowley]] for

[131-082]counsel. After all, how little was it that she knew of the home and

[131-083]the country to which she was to be carried! She might not, perhaps,

[131-084]get adequate advice from Nora, but she would probably learn something

[131-085]on which she could act. There was no one else among the English at

[131-086]Florence to whom she could speak with freedom. When she mentioned her

[131-087]fears to her aunt, her aunt of course laughed at her. Mrs. Spalding

[131-088]told her that Mr. Glascock might be presumed to know his own business

[131-089]best, and that she, as an American lady of high [[reputation|standing]],--the niece

[131-090]of a minister!--was a fitting match for any Englishman, let him be

[131-091]ever so much a lord. But Caroline was not comforted by this, and in

[131-092]her suspense she went to Nora Rowley. She wrote a line to Nora, and

[131-093]when she called at the hotel, was taken up to her friend's bed-room.

[131-094]She found great difficulty in telling her story, but she did tell it.

[131-095]"Miss Rowley," she said, "if this is a silly thing that he is going

[131-096]to do, I am bound to save him from his own folly. You know your own

[131-097]country better than I do. Will they think that he has disgraced

[131-098]himself?"

[131-099]"Certainly not that," said Nora.

[131-100]"Shall I be a load round his neck? Miss Rowley, for my own sake I

[131-101]would not endure such a position as that, not even though I love him.

[131-102]But for his sake! Think of that. If I find that people think ill of

[131-103]him,--because of me--!"

[131-104]"No one will think ill of him."

[131-105]"Is it esteemed needful that such a one as he should marry a woman of

[131-106]his own [[social-class|rank]]? I can bear to end it all now; but I shall not be able

[131-107]to bear his humiliation, and my own despair, if I find that I have

[131-108]injured him. Tell me plainly,--is it a marriage that he should not

[131-109]make?" Nora paused for a while before she answered, and as she sat

[131-110]silent the other girl watched her face carefully. Nora on being thus

[131-111]consulted, was very careful that her tongue should utter nothing that

[131-112]was not her true opinion as best she knew how to express it. Her

[131-113]sympathy would have prompted her to give such an answer as would

[131-114]at once have made Caroline happy in her mind. She would have been

[131-115]delighted to have been able to declare that these doubts were utterly

[131-116]groundless, and this hesitation needless. But she conceived that she

[131-117]owed it as a duty from one woman to another to speak the truth as she

[131-118]conceived it on so momentous an occasion, and she was not sure but

[131-119]that Mr. Glascock would be considered by his friends in England to be

[131-120]doing badly in marrying an American girl. What she did not remember

[131-121]was this,--that her very hesitation was in fact an answer, and such

[131-122]an answer as she was most unwilling to give. "I see that it would be

[131-123]so," said Caroline Spalding.

[131-124]"No;--not that."

[131-125]"What then? Will they despise him,--and me?"

[131-126]"No one who knows you can despise you. No one who sees you can fail

[131-127]to admire you." Nora, as she said this, thought of her mother, but

[131-128]told herself at once that in this matter her mother's judgment had

[131-129]been altogether destroyed by her disappointment. "What I think will

[131-130]take place will be this. His family, when first they hear of it, will

[131-131]be sorry."

[131-132]"Then," said Caroline, "I will put an end to it."

[131-133]"You can't do that, dear. You are engaged, and you haven't a right.

[131-134]I am engaged to a man, and all my friends object to it. But I shan't

[131-135]put an end to it. I don't think I have a right. I shall not do it any

[131-136]way, however."

[131-137]"But if it were for his good?"

[131-138]"It couldn't be for his good. He and I have got to go along together

[131-139]somehow."

[131-140]"You wouldn't hurt him," said Caroline.

[131-141]"I won't if I can help it, but he has got to take me along with him

[131-142]any how; and Mr. Glascock has got to take you. If I were you, I

[131-143]shouldn't ask any more questions."

[131-144]"It isn't the same. You said that you were to be poor, but he is very

[131-145]rich. And I am beginning to understand that these titles of yours are

[131-146]something like kings' crowns. The man who has to wear them can't do

[131-147]just as he pleases with them. Noblesse oblige. I can see the meaning

[131-148]of that, even when the [[duty|obligation]] itself is trumpery in its nature.

[131-149]If it is a man's duty to marry a Talbot because he's a Howard, I

[131-150]suppose he ought to do his duty." After a pause she went on again. "I

[131-151]do believe that I have made a mistake. It seemed to be absurd at the

[131-152]first to think of it, but I do believe it now. Even what you say to

[131-153]me makes me think it."

[131-154]"At any rate you can't go back," said Nora enthusiastically.

[131-155]"I will try."

[131-156]"Go to himself and ask him. You must leave him to decide it at last.

[131-157]I don't see how a girl when she is engaged, is to throw a man over

[131-158]unless he consents. Of course you can throw yourself into the Arno."

[131-159]"And get the water into my shoes,--for it wouldn't do much more at

[131-160]present."

[131-161]"And you can--jilt him," said Nora.

[131-162]"It would not be jilting him."

[131-163]"He must decide that. If he so regards it, it will be so. I advise

[131-164]you to think no more about it; but if you speak to anybody it should

[131-165]be to him." This was at last the result of Nora's wisdom, and then

[131-166]the two girls descended together to the room in which Lady Rowley was

[131-167]sitting with her other daughters. Lady Rowley was very careful in

[131-168]asking after Miss Spalding's sister, and Miss Spalding assured her

[131-169]that Olivia was quite well. Then Lady Rowley made some inquiry about

[131-170]Olivia and Mr. Glascock, and Miss Spalding assured her that no two

[131-171]persons were ever such allies, and that she believed that they were

[131-172]together at this moment investigating some old church. Lady Rowley

[131-173]simpered, and declared that nothing could be more proper, and

[131-174]expressed a hope that Olivia would like England. Caroline Spalding,

[131-175]having still in her mind the trouble that had brought her to Nora,

[131-176]had not much to say about this. "If she goes again to England I am

[131-177]sure she will like it," replied Miss Spalding.
