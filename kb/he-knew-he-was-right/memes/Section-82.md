[082-011]CHAPTER L.

[082-012]CAMILLA TRIUMPHANT.

[082-013][Illustration]

[082-014]It was now New Year's day, and there was some grief and perhaps more

[082-015]excitement in [[exeter|Exeter]],--for it was rumoured that Miss Stanbury lay

[082-016]very ill at her house in the Close. But in order that our somewhat

[082-017]uneven story may run as smoothly as it may be made to do, the little

[082-018]history of the French family for the intervening months shall be told

[082-019]in this chapter, in order that it may be understood how matters were

[082-020]with them when the tidings of Miss Stanbury's severe illness first

[082-021]reached their house at Heavitree.

[082-022]After that terrible scene in which Miss Stanbury had so dreadfully

[082-023]confounded Mr. Gibson by declaring the manner in which he had been

[082-024]rebuffed by Dorothy, the unfortunate clergyman had endeavoured to

[082-025]make his peace with the French family by assuring the mother that in

[082-026]very truth it was the dearest wish of his heart to make her daughter

[082-027]Camilla his wife. Mrs. French, who had ever been disposed to favour

[082-028]Arabella's ambition, well knowing its priority and ancient right,

[082-029]and who of late had been taught to consider that even Camilla had

[082-030]consented to waive any claim that she might have once possessed,

[082-031]could not refrain from the expression of some surprise. That he

[082-032]should be recovered at all out of the Stanbury clutches was very

[082-033]much to Mrs. French,--was so much that, had time been given her for

[082-034]consideration, she would have acknowledged to herself readily that

[082-035]the [[money|property]] had best be secured at once to the family, without

[082-036]incurring that amount of risk which must unquestionably attend any

[082-037]attempt on her part to direct Mr. Gibson's purpose hither or thither.

[082-038]But the proposition came so suddenly that time was not allowed to her

[082-039]to be altogether wise. "I thought it was poor Bella," she said, with

[082-040]something of a piteous whine in her voice. At the moment Mr. Gibson

[082-041]was so humble, that he was half inclined to give way even on that

[082-042]head. He felt himself to have been brought so low in the market by

[082-043]that terrible story of Miss Stanbury's,--which he had been unable

[082-044]either to contradict or to explain,--that there was but little power

[082-045]of fighting left in him. He was, however, just able to speak a word

[082-046]for himself, and that sufficed. "I hope there has been no mistake,"

[082-047]he said; "but really it is Camilla that has my heart." Mrs. French

[082-048]made no rejoinder to this. It was so much to her to know that Mr.

[082-049]Gibson's heart was among them at all after what had occurred in

[082-050]the Close, that she acknowledged to herself after that moment of

[082-051]reflection that Arabella must be sacrificed for the good of the

[082-052]family interests. Poor, dear, loving, misguided, and spiritless

[082-053]mother! She would have given the blood out of her bosom to get

[082-054]husbands for her daughters, though it was not of her own experience

[082-055]that she had learned that of all worldly goods a husband is the best.

[082-056]But it was the possession which they had from their earliest years

[082-057]thought of acquiring, which they first expected, for which they had

[082-058]then hoped, and afterwards worked and schemed and striven with every

[082-059]energy,--and as to which they had at last almost despaired. And now

[082-060]Arabella's fire had been rekindled with a new spark, which, alas,

[082-061]was to be quenched so suddenly! "And am I to tell them?" asked

[082-062]Mrs. French, with a tremor in her voice. To this, however, Mr.

[082-063]Gibson demurred. He said that for certain reasons he should like a

[082-064]fortnight's grace; and that at the end of the fortnight he would

[082-065]be prepared to speak. The interval was granted without further

[082-066]questions, and Mr. Gibson was allowed to leave the house.

[082-067]After that Mrs. French was not very comfortable at home. As soon as

[082-068]Mr. Gibson had departed, Camilla at once returned to her mother and

[082-069]desired to know what had taken place. Was it true that the perjured

[082-070]man had proposed to that young woman in the Close? Mrs. French was

[082-071]not clever at keeping a secret, and she could not keep this by her

[082-072]own aid. She told all that happened to Camilla, and between them

[082-073]they agreed that Arabella should be kept in ignorance till the fatal

[082-074]fortnight should have passed. When Camilla was interrogated as to

[082-075]her own purpose, she said she should like a day to think of it. She

[082-076]took the twenty-four hours, and then made the following confession

[082-077]of her passion to her mother. "You see, mamma, I always liked Mr.

[082-078]Gibson,--always."

[082-079]"So did Arabella, my dear,--before you thought of such things."

[082-080]"I dare say that may be true, mamma; but that is not my fault. He

[082-081]came here among us on such sweetly intimate terms that the feeling

[082-082]grew up with me before I knew what it meant. As to any idea of

[082-083]cutting out Arabella, my conscience is quite clear. If I thought

[082-084]there had been anything really between them I would have gone

[082-085]anywhere,--to the top of a mountain,--rather than rob my sister of a

[082-086]heart that belonged to her."

[082-087]"He has been so slow about it," said Mrs. French.

[082-088]"I don't know about that," said Camilla. "Gentlemen have to be

[082-089]slow, I suppose, when they think of their incomes. He only got St.

[082-090]Peter's-cum-Pumpkin three years ago, and didn't know for the first

[082-091]year whether he could hold that and the minor canonry together. Of

[082-092]course a [[social-class|gentleman]] has to think of these things before he comes

[082-093]forward."

[082-094]"My dear, he has been very backward."

[082-095]"If I'm to be Mrs. Gibson, mamma, I beg that I mayn't hear anything

[082-096]said against him. Then there came all this about that young woman;

[082-097]and when I saw that Arabella took on so,--which I must say was very

[082-098]absurd,--I'm sure I put myself out of the way entirely. If I'd buried

[082-099]myself under the ground I couldn't have done it more. And it's my

[082-100]belief that what I've said, all for Arabella's sake, has put the old

[082-101]woman into such a rage that it has made a quarrel between him and the

[082-102]niece; otherwise that wouldn't be off. I don't believe a word of her

[082-103]refusing him, and never shall. Is it in the course of things, mamma?"

[082-104]Mrs. French shook her head. "Of course not. Then when you question

[082-105]him,--very properly,--he says that he's devoted to--poor me. If I was

[082-106]to refuse him, he wouldn't put up with Bella."

[082-107]"I suppose not," said Mrs. French.

[082-108]"He hates Bella. I've known it all along, though I wouldn't say

[082-109]so. If I were to sacrifice myself ever so it wouldn't be of any

[082-110]good,--and I shan't do it." In this way the matter was arranged.

[082-111]At the end of the fortnight, however, Mr. Gibson did not come,--nor

[082-112]at the end of three weeks. Inquiries had of course been made, and it

[082-113]was ascertained that he had gone into Cornwall for a parson's holiday

[082-114]of thirteen days. That might be all very well. A man might want the

[082-115]recruiting vigour of some change of air after such scenes as those

[082-116]Mr. Gibson had gone through with the Stanburys, and before his

[082-117]proposed encounter with new perils. And he was a man so tied by the

[082-118]leg that his escape could not be for any long time. He was back

[082-119]on the appointed Sunday, and on the Wednesday Mrs. French, under

[082-120]Camilla's instruction, wrote to him a pretty little note. He replied

[082-121]that he would be with her on the Saturday. It would then be nearly

[082-122]four weeks after the great day with Miss Stanbury, but no one would

[082-123]be inclined to quarrel with so short a delay as that. Arabella in the

[082-124]meantime had become fidgety and unhappy. She seemed to understand

[082-125]that something was expected, being quite unable to guess what that

[082-126]something might be. She was true throughout these days to the

[082-127]simplicity of head-gear which Mr. Gibson had recommended to her,

[082-128]and seemed in her questions to her mother and to Camilla to be more

[082-129]fearful of Dorothy Stanbury than of any other enemy. "Mamma, I think

[082-130]you ought to tell her," said Camilla more than once. But she had not

[082-131]been told when Mr. Gibson came on the Saturday. It may truly be said

[082-132]that the poor mother's pleasure in the prospects of one daughter was

[082-133]altogether destroyed by the anticipation of the other daughter's

[082-134]misery. Had Mr. Gibson made Dorothy Stanbury his wife they could

[082-135]have all comforted themselves together by the heat of their joint

[082-136]animosity.

[082-137]He came on the Saturday, and it was so managed that he was closeted

[082-138]with Camilla before Arabella knew that he was in the house. There

[082-139]was a quarter of an hour during which his work was easy, and perhaps

[082-140]pleasant. When he began to explain his intention, Camilla, with the

[082-141]utmost frankness, informed him that her mother had told her all about

[082-142]it. Then she turned her face on one side and put her hand in his; he

[082-143]got his arm round her waist, gave her a kiss, and the thing was done.

[082-144]Camilla was fully resolved that after such a betrothal it should not

[082-145]be undone. She had behaved with sisterly forbearance, and would not

[082-146]now lose the reward of virtue. Not a word was said of Arabella at

[082-147]this interview till he was pressed to come and drink tea with them

[082-148]all that night. He hesitated a moment; and then Camilla declared,

[082-149]with something perhaps of imperious roughness in her manner, that he

[082-150]had better face it all at once. "Mamma will tell her, and she will

[082-151]understand," said Camilla. He hesitated again, but at last promised

[082-152]that he would come.

[082-153]Whilst he was yet in the house Mrs. French had told the whole story

[082-154]to her poor elder daughter. "What is he doing with Camilla?" Arabella

[082-155]had asked with feverish excitement.

[082-156]"Bella, darling;--don't you know?" said the mother.

[082-157]"I know nothing. Everybody keeps me in the dark, and I am badly used.

[082-158]What is it that he is doing?" Then Mrs. French tried to take the

[082-159]poor young woman in her arms, but Arabella would not submit to be

[082-160]embraced. "Don't!" she exclaimed. "Leave me alone. Nobody likes me,

[082-161]or cares a bit about me! Why is Cammy with him there, all alone?"

[082-162]"I suppose he is asking her--to be--his wife." Then Arabella threw

[082-163]herself in despair upon the bed, and wept without any further attempt

[082-164]at control over her feelings. It was a death-blow to her last hope,

[082-165]and all the world, as she looked upon the world then, was over for

[082-166]her. "If I could have arranged it the other way, you know that I

[082-167]would," said the mother.

[082-168]"Mamma," said Arabella, jumping up, "he shan't do it. He hasn't a

[082-169]right. And as for her,-- Oh, that she should treat me in this way!

[082-170]Didn't he tell me the other night, when he drank tea here with me

[082-171]alone--"

[082-172]"What did he tell you, Bella?"

[082-173]"Never mind. Nothing shall ever make me speak to him again;--not if

[082-174]he married her three times over; nor to her. She is a nasty, sly,

[082-175]good-for-nothing thing!"

[082-176]"But, Bella--"

[082-177]"Don't talk to me, mamma. There never was such a thing done before

[082-178]since people--were--people at all. She has been doing it all the

[082-179]time. I know she has."
