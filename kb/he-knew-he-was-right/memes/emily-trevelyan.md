# Emily Trevelyan

**Emily Trevelyan** (née Rowley) is the co-protagonist of [[Anthony Trollope]]'s 1869 novel *He Knew He Was Right*. The daughter of Sir Marmaduke Rowley, Governor of the [[Mandarin Islands]], Emily marries [[Louis Trevelyan]] in what appears to be an ideal union — only to find herself at the centre of a devastating marital conflict when her husband's pathological [[Jealousy|jealousy]] demands she sacrifice her autonomy. Emily's refusal to obey what she considers an unjust command — ending her innocent friendship with [[Colonel Osborne]] — becomes the novel's central moral argument about the rights of wives within [[Marriage|Victorian marriage]]. Unlike her husband, who descends into madness and death, Emily endures, embodying Trollope's conviction that principle grounded in truth outlasts tyranny grounded in suspicion.

## Background

Emily Rowley is the eldest daughter of Sir Marmaduke Rowley, a colonial administrator serving as Governor of the Mandarin Islands. She meets Louis Trevelyan during his travels as a young man, and their courtship is swift and seemingly ideal:

> Emily Rowley, when she was brought home from the islands, was acknowledged to be a pearl indeed. [002-085]

Her upbringing in the colonial periphery, away from the intricate social codes of London society, shapes both her character and her vulnerability. She enters English society as an outsider — beautiful, intelligent, and spirited — precisely the qualities that will later make her unwilling to bend to her husband's unreasonable demands.

## Character and Personality

Emily Trevelyan is defined by her moral courage and her stubborn adherence to principle. When her husband demands she end her friendship with Colonel Osborne — a demand she considers a slur on her honour — she refuses absolutely:

> "I think you are so wrong, Emily. I do indeed." [003-036]

Her position is not one of defiance for its own sake, but of principle: to submit to an unjust accusation, she believes, would be to concede its justice. This makes her both admirable and, in the eyes of some Victorian readers, dangerously intransigent. Trollope deliberately complicates the question of whether Emily's principled resistance is heroic or self-destructive — or both.

Her strength of character is tested severely: she endures public scandal, separation from her husband, the loss of her social position, and — most devastatingly — the abduction of her infant son. Through all of this, she maintains her dignity and her conviction that she has done nothing wrong.

## Key Relationships

### Louis Trevelyan

Emily's relationship with [[Louis Trevelyan]] is the novel's tragic centre. What begins as genuine love and mutual admiration disintegrates as Trevelyan's jealousy transforms him into a domestic tyrant. Emily's response — principled resistance — is simultaneously her greatest strength and the proximate cause of her suffering. The novel refuses to resolve the ambiguity: Emily is right to resist, but her resistance deepens the tragedy.

### Colonel Osborne

[[Colonel Osborne]] is the unwitting instrument of Emily's marital catastrophe. A bachelor in his fifties who had known her father in the Colonial Service, Osborne's visits to Emily are entirely proper — but Trevelyan's jealousy recasts them as evidence of infidelity. Emily's friendship with Osborne is genuine but naive; she underestimates how her husband's mind will twist it.

### Nora Rowley

[[Nora Rowley]], Emily's younger sister, is her most steadfast ally. Nora's own romantic plot — her courtship with [[Hugh Stanbury]] — runs parallel to Emily's tragedy, providing the novel with a counterpoint of successful love against the backdrop of marital collapse.

### Hugh Stanbury

[[Hugh Stanbury]] becomes Emily's champion in the public sphere. As a journalist, he uses his position at the Daily Record to defend Emily's reputation when Trevelyan's allies spread rumours against her. His friendship with Emily also complicates his romance with Nora.

## Significance

Emily Trevelyan is one of Victorian fiction's most complex portraits of a wronged wife. Unlike the passive victims of much sensation fiction, Emily is active, principled, and — crucially — implicated in her own tragedy. Trollope neither sentimentalizes her suffering nor condemns her resistance. Her story raises questions that the novel refuses to answer definitively: When is resistance to marital authority justified? What does a wife owe her husband when his demands are unreasonable? Can principle coexist with love?

Feminist literary criticism has revisited Emily Trevelyan as a case study in Victorian gender politics, noting that her "crime" — refusing to obey an unjust command — is precisely what the nineteenth-century conduct books warned against, and precisely what modern readers most admire.
