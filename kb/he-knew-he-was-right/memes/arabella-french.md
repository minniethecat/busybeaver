## Overview

Arabella French is the elder of the two French sisters, minor comic characters in *He Knew He Was Right* who represent the anxious, unfulfilled single woman of the Victorian middle class. Together with her sister [[camilla-french|Camilla]], she circulates in the social orbit of the novel's principal characters, providing a satirical counterpoint to the central marital drama. Arabella is distinguished by a more practical temperament than her sister, and she eventually achieves the marital settlement that both sisters desperately seek — she marries [[mr-gibson|Mr. Gibson]].

## Role in the Novel

Arabella French's primary narrative function is to illustrate, through comic contrast, the precarious position of unmarried women in Victorian society. While the novel's central plot concerns the catastrophic breakdown of a marriage, the French sisters represent the opposite predicament: women for whom any marriage, however unromantic, would be a social salvation (026-005).

Arabella is drawn with a combination of satire and pathos. She is "painfully alive" to the fact that she and her sister are regarded as husband-hunters, yet this self-awareness does not free her from the role — it only makes her more acutely conscious of every social opportunity and every perceived slight (078-001).

Her eventual marriage to Mr. Gibson provides one of the novel's few conventional happy endings. The narrator reports this union in an almost offhand manner, as if to underline that Arabella's story, while individually satisfying, is not the novel's true concern (147-091). Her marriage — and the domestic contentment it presumably brings — is a footnote to the larger tragedy of the Trevelyans.

## Character and Disposition

Arabella is portrayed as the more sensible of the two sisters — less prone to [[camilla-french|Camilla]]'s enthusiastic effusions, more calculating in her social strategies, and ultimately more successful in achieving her goal of matrimony. Where Camilla is impulsive, Arabella is watchful; where Camilla hopes, Arabella plans.

Yet Trollope's treatment of both sisters is fundamentally compassionate. If Arabella is calculating, it is because Victorian society has given her no honorable alternative. Her "anxiety" about her position is not merely personal vanity but a rational response to a system in which a woman's worth is measured entirely by her marital status (085-001).

## Key Scenes

Arabella appears in several social gatherings throughout the novel. In chapter 26, she and Camilla are introduced as fixtures of the Heavitree social landscape. Their conversations with the novel's principals — including a telling exchange in which [[louis-trevelyan|Louis Trevelyan]] mockingly compares someone to "an Arabella French" — reveal the sisters' ambiguous social position: tolerated but not fully accepted, present but not central (084-163).

> "Such as Arabella French," said he, laughing. It was a cruel comparison, for Arabella French was precisely what no woman wished to be — known, discussed, and yet not quite respectable in the way that truly mattered. (084-163)

## Significance

Arabella French and her sister serve as structural foils in the novel's social economy. They embody the fate that Nora Rowley might have faced had she not found love with Hugh Stanbury, and the fate that all Victorian women of limited means must fear. Their story is comic in tone but serious in implication: the marriage market is a real market, and women who fail to secure a place in it face a life of genteel obscurity.
