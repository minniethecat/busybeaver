## Section 24

[024-001][Illustration: To have been the mother of a future peer!]

[024-002]As she was thinking of this Mrs. Trevelyan came into the room. Nora

[024-003]felt that though she might dread to meet her mother, she could be

[024-004]bold enough on such an occasion before her sister. Emily had not

[024-005]done so well with her own affairs, as to enable her to preach with

[024-006]advantage about marriage.

[024-007]"He has gone?" said Mrs. Trevelyan, as she opened the door.

[024-008]"Yes, he has gone."

[024-009]"Well? Do not pretend, Nora, that you will not tell me."

[024-010]"There is nothing worth the telling, Emily."

[024-011]"What do you mean? I am sure he has proposed. He told me in so many

[024-012]words that it was his intention."

[024-013]"Whatever has happened, dear, you may be quite sure that I shall

[024-014]never be Mrs. Glascock."

[024-015]"Then you have refused him,--because of [[hugh-stanbury|Hugh Stanbury]]!"

[024-016]"I have refused him, Emily, because I did not love him. Pray let that

[024-017]be enough."

[024-018]Then she walked out of the room with something of stateliness in her

[024-019]gait,--as might become a girl who had had it in her power to be the

[024-020]future Lady Peterborough; but as soon as she reached the sacredness

[024-021]of her own chamber, she gave way to an agony of tears. It would,

[024-022]indeed, be much to be a Lady Peterborough. And she had, in truth,

[024-023]refused it all because of Hugh Stanbury! Was Hugh Stanbury worth so

[024-024]great a sacrifice?

[024-025]CHAPTER XIV.

[024-026]THE CLOCK HOUSE AT NUNCOMBE PUTNEY.

[024-027]It was not till a fortnight had passed after the transaction recorded

[024-028]in the last chapter, that Mrs. Trevelyan and [[nora-rowley|Nora Rowley]] first heard

[024-029]the proposition that they should go to live at [[nuncombe-putney|Nuncombe Putney]]. From

[024-030]bad to worse the quarrel between the husband and the wife had gone

[024-031]on, till Trevelyan had at last told his friend Lady Milborough

[024-032]that he had made up his mind that they must live apart. "She is so

[024-033]self-willed,--and perhaps I am the same," he had said, "that it

[024-034]is impossible that we should live together." Lady Milborough had

[024-035]implored and called to witness all testimonies, profane and sacred,

[024-036]against such a step,--had almost gone down on her knees. Go to

[024-037]Naples,--why not Naples? Or to the quiet town in the west of France,

[024-038]which was so dull that a wicked roaring lion, fond of cities and

[024-039]gambling, and eating and drinking, could not live in such a place!

[024-040]Oh, why not go to the quiet town in the west of France? Was not

[024-041]anything better than this flying in the face of God and man? Perhaps

[024-042]Trevelyan did not himself like the idea of the quiet dull French

[024-043]town. Perhaps he thought that the flying in the face of God and man

[024-044]was all done by his wife, not by him; and that it was right that his

[024-045]wife should feel the consequences. After many such entreaties, many

[024-046]such arguments, it was at last decided that the house in Curzon

[024-047]Street should be given up, and that he and his wife live apart.

[024-048]"And what about Nora Rowley?" asked Lady Milborough, who had become

[024-049]aware by this time of Nora's insane folly in having refused Mr.

[024-050]Glascock.

[024-051]"She will go with her sister, I suppose."

[024-052]"And who will maintain her? Dear, dear, dear! It does seem as though

[024-053]some young people were bent upon cutting their own throats, and all

[024-054]their family's."

[024-055]Poor Lady Milborough just at this time went as near to disliking the

[024-056]Rowleys as was compatible with her nature. It was not possible to her

[024-057]to hate anybody. She thought that she hated the Colonel Osbornes; but

[024-058]even that was a mistake. She was very angry, however, with both Mrs.

[024-059]Trevelyan and her sister, and was disposed to speak of them as though

[024-060]they had been born to create trouble and vexation.

[024-061]Trevelyan had not given any direct answer to that question about Nora

[024-062]Rowley's maintenance, but he was quite prepared to bear all necessary

[024-063]expense in that direction, at any rate till Sir Marmaduke should have

[024-064]arrived. At first there had been an idea that the two sisters should

[024-065]go to the house of their aunt, Mrs. Outhouse. Mrs. Outhouse was the

[024-066]wife,--as the reader may perhaps remember,--of a clergyman living

[024-067]in the east of [[london|London]]. St. Diddulph's-in-the-East was very much in

[024-068]the east indeed. It was a parish outside the City, lying near the

[024-069]river, very populous, very poor, very low in [[honour|character]], and very

[024-070]uncomfortable. There was a rectory-house, queerly situated at the

[024-071]end of a little blind lane, with a gate of its own, and a so-called

[024-072]garden about twenty yards square. But the rectory of St. Diddulph's

[024-073]cannot be said to have been a comfortable abode. The neighbourhood

[024-074]was certainly not alluring. Of visiting society within a distance

[024-075]of three or four miles there was none but what was afforded by the

[024-076]families of other East-end clergymen. And then Mr. Outhouse himself

[024-077]was a somewhat singular man. He was very religious, devoted to

[024-078]his work, most kind to the poor; but he was unfortunately a

[024-079]strongly-biased man, and at the same time very obstinate withal. He

[024-080]had never allied himself very cordially with his wife's brother,

[024-081]Sir Marmaduke, allowing himself to be carried away by a prejudice

[024-082]that people living at the West-end, who frequented clubs, and

[024-083]were connected in any way with fashion, could not be appropriate

[024-084]companions for himself. The very title which Sir Marmaduke had

[024-085]acquired was repulsive to him, and had induced him to tell his

[024-086]wife more than once that Sir this or Sir that could not be fitting

[024-087]associates for a poor East-end clergyman. Then his wife's niece had

[024-088]married a man of fashion,--a man supposed at St. Diddulph's to be

[024-089]very closely allied to fashion; and Mr. Outhouse had never been

[024-090]induced even to dine in the house in Curzon Street. When, therefore,

[024-091]he heard that Mr. and Mrs. Trevelyan were to be separated within two

[024-092]years of their marriage, it could not be expected that he should be

[024-093]very eager to lend to the two sisters the use of his rectory.

[024-094]There had been interviews between Mr. Outhouse and Trevelyan, and

[024-095]between Mrs. Outhouse and her niece; and then there was an interview

[024-096]between Mr. Outhouse and Emily, in which it was decided that Mrs.

[024-097]Trevelyan would not go to the parsonage of St. Diddulph's. She had

[024-098]been very outspoken to her uncle, declaring that she by no means

[024-099]intended to carry herself as a disgraced woman. Mr. Outhouse had

[024-100]quoted St. Paul to her; "Wives, obey your husbands." Then she had

[024-101]got up and had spoken very angrily. "I look for support from you,"

[024-102]she said, "as the man who is the nearest to me, till my father

[024-103]shall come." "But I cannot support you in what is wrong," said the

[024-104]clergyman. Then Mrs. Trevelyan had left the room, and would not see

[024-105]her uncle again.

[024-106]She carried things altogether with a high hand at this time. When old

[024-107]Mr. Bideawhile called upon her, her husband's ancient family lawyer,

[024-108]she told that [[social-class|gentleman]] that if it was her husband's will that they

[024-109]should live apart, it must be so. She could not force him to remain

[024-110]with her. She could not compel him to keep up the house in Curzon

[024-111]Street. She had certain rights, she believed. She spoke then, she

[024-112]said, of pecuniary rights,--not of those other rights which her

[024-113]husband was determined, and was no doubt able, to ignore. She did not

[024-114]really know what those pecuniary rights might be, nor was she careful

[024-115]to learn their exact extent. She would thank Mr. Bideawhile to see

[024-116]that things were properly arranged. But of this her husband, and Mr.

[024-117]Bideawhile, might be quite sure;--she would take nothing as a favour.

[024-118]She would not go to her uncle's house. She declined to tell Mr.

[024-119]Bideawhile why she had so decided; but she had decided. She was ready

[024-120]to listen to any suggestion that her husband might make as to her

[024-121]residence, but she must claim to have some choice in the matter. As

[024-122]to her sister, of course she intended to give Nora a home as long as

[024-123]such a home might be wanted. It would be very sad for Nora, but in

[024-124]existing circumstances such an arrangement would be expedient. She

[024-125]would not go into details as to expense. Her husband was driving

[024-126]her away from him, and it was for him to say what proportion of his

[024-127][[money|income]] he would choose to give for her maintenance,--for hers and

[024-128]for that of their child. She was not desirous of anything beyond the

[024-129]means of decent living, but of course she must for the present find

[024-130]a home for her sister as well as for herself. When speaking of her

[024-131]baby she had striven hard so to speak that Mr. Bideawhile should find

[024-132]no trace of doubt in the tones of her voice. And yet she had been

[024-133]full of doubt,--full of fear. As Mr. Bideawhile had uttered nothing

[024-134]antagonistic to her wishes in this matter,--had seemed to agree

[024-135]that wherever the mother went thither the child would go also,--Mrs.

[024-136]Trevelyan had considered herself to be successful in this interview.

[024-137]The idea of a residence at Nuncombe Putney had occurred first to

[024-138]Trevelyan himself, and he had spoken of it to Hugh Stanbury. There

[024-139]had been some difficulty in this, because he had snubbed Stanbury

[024-140]grievously when his friend had attempted to do some work of gentle

[024-141]interference between him and his wife; and when he began the

[024-142]conversation, he took the trouble of stating, in the first instance,

[024-143]that the separation was a thing fixed,--so that nothing might be

[024-144]urged on that subject. "It is to be. You will understand that,"

[024-145]he said; "and if you think that your mother would agree to the

[024-146]arrangement, it would be satisfactory to me, and might, I think,

[024-147]be made pleasant to her. Of course, your mother would be made to

[024-148]understand that the only fault with which my wife is charged is that

[024-149]of indomitable disobedience to my wishes."

[024-150]"Incompatibility of temper," suggested Stanbury.

[024-151]"You may call it that if you please;--though I must say for myself

[024-152]that I do not think that I have displayed any temper to which a

[024-153]woman has a right to object." Then he had gone on to explain what

[024-154]he was prepared to do about money. He would pay, through Stanbury's

[024-155]hands, so much for maintenance and so much for house rent, on the

[024-156]understanding that the money was not to go into his wife's hands.

[024-157]"I shall prefer," he said, "to make myself, on her behalf, what

[024-158]disbursements may be necessary. I will take care that she receives a

[024-159]proper sum quarterly through Mr. Bideawhile for her own clothes,--and

[024-160]for those of our poor boy." Then Stanbury had told him of the Clock

[024-161]House, and there had been an agreement made between them;--an

[024-162]agreement which was then, of course, subject to the approval of

[024-163]the ladies at Nuncombe Putney. When the suggestion was made to Mrs.

[024-164]Trevelyan,--with a proposition that the [[clock-house|Clock House]] should be taken

[024-165]for one year, and that for that year, at least, her boy should remain

[024-166]with her,--she assented to it. She did so with all the calmness that

[024-167]she was able to assume; but, in truth, almost everything seemed to

[024-168]have been gained, when she found that she was not to be separated

[024-169]from her baby. "I have no objection to living in Devonshire if Mr.

[024-170]Trevelyan wishes it," she said, in her most stately manner; "and

[024-171]certainly no objection to living with Mr. Stanbury's mother." Then

[024-172]Mr. Bideawhile explained to her that Nuncombe Putney was not a large

[024-173]town,--was, in fact, a very small and a very remote village. "That

[024-174]will make no difference whatsoever as far as I am concerned," she

[024-175]answered; "and as for my sister, she must put up with it till my

[024-176]father and my mother are here. I believe the scenery at Nuncombe

[024-177]Putney is very pretty." "Lovely!" said Mr. Bideawhile, who had a

[024-178]general idea that Devonshire is supposed to be a picturesque

[024-179]county. "With such a life before me as I must lead," continued Mrs.

[024-180]Trevelyan, "an ugly neighbourhood, one that would itself have had

[024-181]no interest for a stranger, would certainly have been an additional

[024-182]sorrow." So it had been settled, and by the end of July, Mrs.

[024-183]Trevelyan, with her sister and baby, was established at the Clock

[024-184]House, under the protection of Mrs. Stanbury. Mrs. Trevelyan had

[024-185]brought down her own maid and her own nurse, and had found that the

[024-186]arrangements made by her husband had, in truth, been liberal. The

[024-187]house in Curzon Street had been given up, the furniture had been sent

[024-188]to a warehouse, and Mr. Trevelyan had gone into lodgings. "There

[024-189]never were two young people so insane since the world began," said

[024-190]Lady Milborough to her old friend, Mrs. Fairfax, when the thing was

[024-191]done.
