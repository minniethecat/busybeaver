## Section 147

[147-001]"If Mr. Brooke is to come here," Martha said to her one day, "we

[147-002]ought to begin and make the changes, ma'am."

[147-003]"What changes? You are always wanting to make changes."

[147-004]"If they was never made till I wanted them they'd never be made,

[147-005]ma'am. But if there is to be a married couple there should be things

[147-006]proper. Anyways, ma'am, we ought to know;--oughtn't we?"

[147-007]The truth of this statement was so evident that Miss Stanbury could

[147-008]not contradict it. But she had not even yet made up her mind. Ideas

[147-009]were running through her head which she knew to be very wild, but

[147-010]of which she could not divest herself. "Martha," she said, after a

[147-011]while, "I think I shall go away from this myself."

[147-012]"Leave the house, ma'am?" said Martha, awestruck.

[147-013]"There are other houses in the world, I suppose, in which an old

[147-014]woman can live and die."

[147-015]"There is houses, ma'am, of course."

[147-016]"And what is the difference between one and another?"

[147-017]"I wouldn't do it, ma'am, if I was you. I wouldn't do it if it was

[147-018]ever so. Sure the house is big enough for Mr. Brooke and Miss Dorothy

[147-019]along with you. I wouldn't go and make such change as that;--I

[147-020]wouldn't indeed, ma'am." Martha spoke out almost with eloquence, so

[147-021]much expression was there in her face. Miss Stanbury said nothing

[147-022]more at the moment, beyond signifying her indisposition to make up

[147-023]her mind to anything at the present moment. Yes;--the house was big

[147-024]enough as far as rooms were concerned; but how often had she heard

[147-025]that an old woman must always be in the way, if attempting to live

[147-026]with a newly-married couple? If a mother-in-law be unendurable, how

[147-027]much more so one whose connection would be less near? She could keep

[147-028]her own house no doubt, and let them go elsewhere; but what then

[147-029]would come of her old dream, that Burgess, the new banker in the

[147-030]city, should live in the very house that had been inhabited by the

[147-031]Burgesses, the bankers of old? There was certainly only one way out

[147-032]of all these troubles, and that way would be that she should--go from

[147-033]them and be at rest.

[147-034]Her will had now been drawn out and completed for the third or fourth

[147-035]time, and she had made no secret of its contents either with Brooke

[147-036]or Dorothy. The whole estate she left to Brooke, including the houses

[147-037]which were to become his after his uncle's death; and in regard to

[147-038]the [[money|property]] she had made no further stipulation. "I might have

[147-039]settled it on your children," she said to him, "but in doing so I

[147-040]should have settled it on hers. I don't know why an old woman should

[147-041]try to interfere with things after she has gone. I hope you won't

[147-042]squander it, Brooke."

[147-043]"I shall be a steady old man by that time," he said.

[147-044]"I hope you'll be steady at any rate. But there it is, and God must

[147-045]direct you in the use of it, if He will. It has been a burthen to

[147-046]me; but then I have been a solitary old woman." Half of what she had

[147-047]saved she proposed to give Dorothy on her marriage, and for doing

[147-048]this arrangements had already been made. There were various other

[147-049]legacies, and the last she announced was one to her nephew, Hugh. "I

[147-050]have left him a thousand pounds," she said to Dorothy,--"so that he

[147-051]may remember me kindly at last." As to this, however, she exacted a

[147-052]pledge that no intimation of the legacy was to be made to Hugh. Then

[147-053]it was that Dorothy told her aunt that Hugh intended to marry Nora

[147-054]Rowley, one of the ladies who had been at the [[clock-house|Clock House]] during the

[147-055]days in which her mother had lived in grandeur; and then it was also

[147-056]that Dorothy obtained leave to invite Hugh to her own wedding. "I

[147-057]hope she will be happier than her sister," Miss Stanbury said, when

[147-058]she heard of the intended marriage.

[147-059]"It wasn't Mrs. Trevelyan's fault, you know, aunt."

[147-060]"I say nothing about anybody's fault; but this I do say, that it was

[147-061]a very great misfortune. I fought all that battle with your sister

[147-062]Priscilla, and I don't mean to fight it again, my dear. If Hugh

[147-063]marries the young lady, I hope she will be more happy than her

[147-064]sister. There can be no harm in saying that."

[147-065]Dorothy's letter to her brother shall be given, because it will

[147-066]inform the reader of all the arrangements as they were made up to

[147-067]that time, and will convey the [[exeter|Exeter]] news respecting various persons

[147-068]with whom our story is concerned.

[147-069]The Close, July 20th, 186--.

[147-070]DEAR HUGH,--

[147-071]The day for my marriage is now fixed, and I wish with all

[147-072]my heart that it was the same with you. Pray give my love

[147-073]to Nora. It seems so odd that, though she was living for a

[147-074]while with mamma at [[nuncombe-putney|Nuncombe Putney]], I never should have

[147-075]seen her yet. I am very glad that Brooke has seen her, and

[147-076]he declares that she is quite _magnificently beautiful_.

[147-077]Those are his own words.

[147-078]We are to be married on the 10th of August, a Wednesday,

[147-079]and now comes my great news. Aunt Stanbury says that you

[147-080]are to come and stay in the house. She bids me tell you so

[147-081]with her love; and that you can have a room as long as you

[147-082]like. _Of course you must come._ In the first place, you

[147-083]must because you are to give me away, and Brooke wouldn't

[147-084]have me if I wasn't given away properly; and then it will

[147-085]make me so happy that you and Aunt Stanbury should be

[147-086]friends again. You can stay as long as you like, but, of

[147-087]course, you must come the day before the wedding. We are

[147-088]to be married in the Cathedral, and there are to be two

[147-089]clergymen, but I don't yet know who they will be;--not Mr.

[147-090]Gibson, certainly, as you were good enough to suggest.

[147-091]Mr. Gibson is married to Arabella French, and they have

[147-092]gone away somewhere into Cornwall. Camilla has come back,

[147-093]and I have seen her once. She looked ever so fierce, as

[147-094]though she intended to declare that she didn't mind what

[147-095]anybody may think. They say that she still protests that

[147-096]she never will speak to her sister again.

[147-097]I was introduced to Mr. Barty Burgess the other day.

[147-098]Brooke was here, and we met him in the Close. I hardly

[147-099]knew what he said to me, I was so frightened; but Brooke

[147-100]said that he meant to be civil, and that he is going

[147-101]to send me a present. I have got a quantity of things

[147-102]already, and yesterday Mrs. MacHugh sent me such a

[147-103]beautiful cream-jug. If you'll come in time on the 9th,

[147-104]you shall see them all before they are put away.

[147-105]Mamma and Priscilla are to be here, and they will come

[147-106]on the 9th also. Poor, dear mamma is, I know, terribly

[147-107]flurried about it, and so is Aunt Stanbury. It is so long

[147-108]since they have seen each other. I don't think Priscilla

[147-109]feels it the same way, because she is so brave. Do you

[147-110]remember when it was first proposed that I should come

[147-111]here? I am so glad I came,--because of Brooke. He will

[147-112]come on the 9th, quite early, and I do so hope you will

[147-113]come with him.

[147-114]Yours most affectionately,

[147-115]DOROTHY STANBURY.

[147-116]Give my best, best love to Nora.

[147-117]CHAPTER XC.

[147-118]LADY ROWLEY CONQUERED.

[147-119][Illustration]

[147-120]When the Rowleys were back in [[london|London]], and began to employ themselves

[147-121]on the terrible work of making ready for their journey to the

[147-122]Islands, Lady Rowley gradually gave way about [[hugh-stanbury|Hugh Stanbury]]. She had

[147-123]become aware that Nora would not go back with them,--unless under an

[147-124]amount of pressure which she would find it impossible to use. And if

[147-125]Nora did not go out to the Islands, what was to become of her unless

[147-126]she married this man? Sir Marmaduke, when all was explained to him,

[147-127]declared that a girl must do what her parents ordered her to do.

[147-128]"Other girls live with their fathers and mothers, and so must she."

[147-129]Lady Rowley endeavoured to explain that other girls lived with their

[147-130]fathers and mothers, because they found themselves in established

[147-131]homes from which they are not disposed to run away; but Nora's

[147-132]position was, as she alleged, very different. Nora's home had

[147-133]latterly been with her sister, and it was hardly to be expected

[147-134]that the parental authority should not find itself impaired by the

[147-135]interregnum which had taken place. Sir Marmaduke would not see the

[147-136]thing in the same light, and was disposed to treat his daughter with

[147-137]a high hand. If she would not do as she was bidden, she should no

[147-138]longer be daughter of his. In answer to this Lady Rowley could only

[147-139]repeat her conviction that Nora would not go out to the Mandarins;

[147-140]and that as for disinheriting her, casting her off, cursing her, and

[147-141]the rest,--she had no belief in such doings at all. "On the stage

[147-142]they do such things as that," she said; "and, perhaps, they used to

[147-143]do it once in reality. But you know that it's out of the question,

[147-144]now. Fancy your [[reputation|standing]] up and cursing at the dear girl, just as we

[147-145]are all starting from Southampton!" Sir Marmaduke knew as well as his

[147-146]wife that it would be impossible, and only muttered something about

[147-147]the "dear girl" behaving herself with great impropriety.

[147-148]They were all aware that Nora was not going to leave England, because

[147-149]no berth had been taken for her on board the ship, and because, while

[147-150]the other girls were preparing for their long voyage, no preparations

[147-151]were made for her. Of course she was not going. Sir Marmaduke would

[147-152]probably have given way altogether immediately on his return to

[147-153]London, had he not discussed the matter with his friend Colonel

[147-154]Osborne. It became, of course, his duty to make some inquiry as

[147-155]to the Stanbury family, and he knew that Osborne had visited Mrs.

[147-156]Stanbury when he made his unfortunate pilgrimage to the porch of

[147-157]Cockchaffington Church. He told Osborne the whole story of Nora's

[147-158]engagement, telling also that other most heart-breaking tale of

[147-159]her conduct in regard to Mr. Glascock, and asked the Colonel what

[147-160]he thought about the Stanburys. Now the Colonel did not hold the

[147-161]Stanburys in high esteem. He had met Hugh, as the reader may perhaps

[147-162]remember, and had had some intercourse with the young man, which

[147-163]had not been quite agreeable to him, on the platform of the railway

[147-164][[social-class|station]] at Exeter. And he had also heard something of the ladies

[147-165]at Nuncombe Putney during his short sojourn at the house of Mrs.

[147-166]Crocket. "My belief is, they are beggars," said [[colonel-osborne|Colonel Osborne]].

[147-167]"I suppose so," said Sir Marmaduke, shaking his head.

[147-168]"When I went over to call on Emily,--that time I was at

[147-169]Cockchaffington, you know, when Trevelyan made himself such a d----

[147-170]fool,--I found the mother and sister living in a decentish house

[147-171]enough; but it wasn't their house."

[147-172]"Not their own, you mean?"

[147-173]"It was a place that Trevelyan had got this young man to take for

[147-174]Emily, and they had merely gone there to be with her. They had been

[147-175]living in a little bit of a cottage; a sort of a place that any--any

[147-176]ploughman would live in. Just that kind of cottage."

[147-177]"Goodness gracious!"

[147-178]"And they've gone to another just like it;--so I'm told."

[147-179]"And can't he do anything better for them than that?" asked Sir

[147-180]Marmaduke.

[147-181]"I know nothing about him. I have met him, you know. He used to be

[147-182]with Trevelyan;--that was when Nora took a fancy for him, of course.

[147-183]And I saw him once down in Devonshire, when I must say he behaved

[147-184]uncommonly badly,--doing all he could to foster Trevelyan's stupid

[147-185]jealousy."
