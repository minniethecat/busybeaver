## Section 70

[070-001]This did seem cruel to the poor man. From the first day in which

[070-002]the project had been opened to him by Miss Stanbury, he had yielded

[070-003]a ready acquiescence,--in spite of those ties which he had at

[070-004]Heavitree,--and had done his very best to fall into her views. "I

[070-005]don't think that is at all fair, Miss Stanbury," he said, with some

[070-006]tone of wrath in his voice.

[070-007]"It's true,--quite true. You always treated her as though she were

[070-008]something beneath you." Mr. Gibson stood speechless, with his mouth

[070-009]open. "So you did. I saw it all. And now she's had spirit enough to

[070-010]resent it. I don't wonder at it; I don't, indeed. It's no good your

[070-011][[reputation|standing]] there any longer. The thing is done."

[070-012]Such intolerable ill-usage Mr. Gibson had never suffered in his life.

[070-013]Had he been untrue, or very nearly untrue, to those dear girls at

[070-014]Heavitree for this? "I never treated her as anything beneath me," he

[070-015]said at last.

[070-016]"Yes, you did. Do you think that I don't understand? Haven't I eyes

[070-017]in my head, and ears? I'm not deaf yet, nor blind. But there's an

[070-018]end of it. If any young woman ever meant anything, she means it. The

[070-019]truth is, she don't like you."

[070-020]Was ever a lover despatched in so uncourteous a way! Then, too, he

[070-021]had been summoned thither as a lover, had been specially encouraged

[070-022]to come there as a lover, had been assured of success in a peculiar

[070-023]way, had had the plum actually offered to him! He had done all that

[070-024]this old woman had bidden him,--something, indeed, to the prejudice

[070-025]of his own heart; he had been told that the wife was ready for

[070-026]him; and now, because this foolish young woman didn't know her own

[070-027]mind,--this was Mr. Gibson's view of the matter,--he was reviled

[070-028]and abused, and told that he had behaved badly to the lady. "Miss

[070-029]Stanbury," he said, "I think that you are forgetting yourself."

[070-030]"Highty, tighty!" said Miss Stanbury. "Forgetting myself! I shan't

[070-031]forget you in a hurry, Mr. Gibson."

[070-032]"Nor I you, Miss Stanbury. Good morning, Miss Stanbury." Mr. Gibson,

[070-033]as he went from the hall-door into the street, shook the dust off his

[070-034]feet, and resolved that for the future he and Miss Stanbury should be

[070-035]two. There would arise great trouble in [[exeter|Exeter]], but, nevertheless, he

[070-036]and Miss Stanbury must be two. He could justify himself in no other

[070-037]purpose after such conduct as he had received.

[070-038]CHAPTER XLIII.

[070-039]LABURNUM COTTAGE.

[070-040]There had been various letters passing, during the last six weeks,

[070-041]between Priscilla Stanbury and her brother, respecting the Clock

[070-042]House at [[nuncombe-putney|Nuncombe Putney]]. The ladies at Nuncombe had, certainly, gone

[070-043]into the [[clock-house|Clock House]] on the clear understanding that the expenses of

[070-044]the establishment were to be incurred on behalf of Mrs. Trevelyan.

[070-045]Priscilla had assented to the movement most doubtingly. She had

[070-046]disliked the idea of taking the charge of a young married woman who

[070-047]was separated from her husband, and she had felt that a going down

[070-048]after such an uprising,--a fall from the Clock House back to a

[070-049]cottage,--would be very disagreeable. She had, however, allowed her

[070-050]brother's arguments to prevail, and there they were. The annoyance

[070-051]which she had anticipated from the position of their late guest had

[070-052]fallen upon them: it had been felt grievously, from the moment in

[070-053]which [[colonel-osborne|Colonel Osborne]] called at the house; and now that going back

[070-054]to the cottage must be endured. Priscilla understood that there had

[070-055]been a settlement between Trevelyan and Stanbury as to the cost of

[070-056]the establishment so far;--but that must now be at an end. In their

[070-057]present circumstances she would not continue to live there, and had

[070-058]already made inquiries as to some humble roof for their shelter. For

[070-059]herself she would not have cared had it been necessary for her to

[070-060]hide herself in a hut,--for herself, as regarded any feeling as to

[070-061]her own [[reputation|standing]] in the village. For herself, she was ashamed of

[070-062]nothing. But her mother would suffer, and she knew what Aunt Stanbury

[070-063]would say to Dorothy. To Dorothy at the present moment, if Dorothy

[070-064]should think of accepting her suitor, the change might be very

[070-065]deleterious; but still it should be made. She could not endure

[070-066]to live there on the very hard-earned proceeds of her brother's

[070-067]pen,--proceeds which were not only hard-earned, but precarious. She

[070-068]gave warning to the two servants who had been hired, and consulted

[070-069]with Mrs. Crocket as to a cottage, and was careful to let it be known

[070-070]throughout Nuncombe Putney that the Clock House was to be abandoned.

[070-071]The Clock House had been taken furnished for six months, of which

[070-072]half were not yet over; but there were other expenses of living there

[070-073]much greater than the rent, and go she would. Her mother sighed and

[070-074]assented; and Mrs. Crocket, having strongly but fruitlessly advised

[070-075]that the Clock House should be inhabited at any rate for the six

[070-076]months, promised her assistance. "It has been a bad business, Mrs.

[070-077]Crocket," said Priscilla; "and all we can do now is to get out of

[070-078]it as well as we can. Every mouthful I eat chokes me while I stay

[070-079]there." "It ain't good, certainly, miss, not to know as you're all

[070-080]straight the first thing as you wakes in the morning," said Mrs.

[070-081]Crocket,--who was always able to feel when she woke that everything

[070-082]was straight with her.

[070-083]Then there came the correspondence between Priscilla and Hugh.

[070-084]Priscilla was at first decided, indeed, but mild in the expression

[070-085]of her decision. To this, and to one or two other missives

[070-086]couched in terms of increasing decision, Hugh answered with manly,

[070-087]self-asserting, overbearing arguments. The house was theirs till

[070-088]Christmas; between this and then he would think about it. He could

[070-089]very well afford to keep the house on till next Midsummer, and then

[070-090]they might see what had best be done. There was plenty of money, and

[070-091]Priscilla need not put herself into a flutter. In answer to that word

[070-092]flutter, Priscilla wrote as follows:--

[070-093]Clock House, September 16, 186--.

[070-094]DEAR HUGH,

[070-095]I know very well how good you are, and how generous, but

[070-096]you must allow me to have feelings as well as yourself. I

[070-097]will not consent to have myself regarded as a grand lady

[070-098]out of your earnings. How should I feel when some day I

[070-099]heard that you had run yourself into debt? Neither mamma

[070-100]nor I could endure it. Dorothy is provided for now, at any

[070-101]rate for a time, and what we have is enough for us. You

[070-102]know I am not too proud to take anything you can spare to

[070-103]us, when we are ourselves placed in a proper position: but

[070-104]I could not live in this great house, while you are paying

[070-105]for everything,--and I will not. Mamma quite agrees with

[070-106]me, and we shall go out of it on Michaelmas-day. Mrs.

[070-107]Crocket says she thinks she can get you a tenant for the

[070-108]three months, out of Exeter,--if not for the whole rent,

[070-109]at least for part of it. I think we have already got a

[070-110]small place for eight shillings a week, a little out of

[070-111]the village, on the road to Cockchaffington. You will

[070-112]remember it. Old Soames used to live there. Our old

[070-113]furniture will be just enough. There is a mite of a

[070-114]garden, and Mrs. Crocket says she thinks we can get it for

[070-115]seven shillings, or perhaps for six and sixpence, if we

[070-116]stay there. We shall go in on the 29th. Mrs. Crocket will

[070-117]see about having somebody to take care of the house.

[070-118]Your most affectionate sister,

[070-119]PRISCILLA.

[070-120]On the receipt of this letter, Hugh proceeded to Nuncombe. At this

[070-121]time he was making about ten guineas a week, and thought that

[070-122]he saw his way to further work. No doubt the ten guineas were

[070-123]precarious;--that is, the "Daily Record" might discontinue his

[070-124]services to-morrow, if the "Daily Record" thought fit to do so. The

[070-125]greater part of his earnings came from the "D. R.," and the editor

[070-126]had only to say that things did not suit any longer, and there would

[070-127]be an end of it. He was not as a lawyer or a doctor with many clients

[070-128]who could not all be supposed to withdraw their custom at once; but

[070-129]leading articles were things wanted with at least as much regularity

[070-130]as physic or law, and [[hugh-stanbury|Hugh Stanbury]], believing in himself, did not

[070-131]think it probable that an editor, who knew what he was about, would

[070-132]withdraw his patronage. He was proud of his weekly ten guineas,

[070-133]feeling sure that a weekly ten guineas would not as yet have been

[070-134]his had he stuck to the Bar as a profession. He had calculated, when

[070-135]Mrs. Trevelyan left the Clock House, that two hundred a year would

[070-136]enable his mother to continue to reside there, the rent of the place

[070-137]furnished, or half-furnished, being only eighty; and he thought

[070-138]that he could pay the two hundred easily. He thought so still, when

[070-139]he received Priscilla's last letter; but he knew something of the

[070-140]stubbornness of his dear sister, and he, therefore, went down to

[070-141]Nuncombe Putney, in order that he might use the violence of his logic

[070-142]on his mother.

[070-143]He had heard of Mr. Gibson from both Priscilla and from Dorothy,

[070-144]and was certainly desirous that "dear old Dolly," as he called her,

[070-145]should be settled comfortably. But when dear old Dolly wrote to him

[070-146]declaring that it could not be so, that Mr. Gibson was a very nice

[070-147][[social-class|gentleman]], of whom she could not say that she was particularly

[070-148]fond,--"though I really do think that he is an excellent man, and if

[070-149]it was any other girl in the world, I should recommend her to take

[070-150]him,"--and that she thought that she would rather not get married, he

[070-151]wrote to her the kindest brotherly letter in the world, telling her

[070-152]that she was "a brick," and suggesting to her that there might come

[070-153]some day some one who would suit her taste better than Mr. Gibson.

[070-154]"I'm not very fond of parsons myself," said Hugh, "but you must not

[070-155]tell that to Aunt Stanbury." Then he suggested that as he was going

[070-156]down to Nuncombe, Dorothy should get leave of absence and come over

[070-157]and meet him at the Clock House. Dorothy demanded the leave of

[070-158]absence somewhat imperiously, and was at home at the Clock House when

[070-159]Hugh arrived.

[070-160]"And so that little affair couldn't come off?" said Hugh at their

[070-161]first family meeting.

[070-162]"It was a pity," said Mrs. Stanbury, plaintively. She had been very

[070-163]plaintive on the subject. What a thing it would have been for her,

[070-164]could she have seen Dorothy so well established!

[070-165]"There's no help for spilt milk, mother," said Hugh. Mrs. Stanbury

[070-166]shook her head.

[070-167]"Dorothy was quite right," said Priscilla.

[070-168]"Of course she was right," said Hugh. "Who doubts her being right?

[070-169]Bless my soul! What's any girl to do if she don't like a man except

[070-170]to tell him so? I honour you, Dolly,--not that I ever should have

[070-171]doubted you. You're too much of a chip of the old block to say you

[070-172]liked a man when you didn't."

[070-173]"He is a very excellent young man," said Mrs. Stanbury.

[070-174]"An excellent fiddlestick, mother. Loving and liking don't go by

[070-175]excellence. Besides, I don't know about his being any better than

[070-176]anybody else, just because he's a clergyman."

[070-177]"A clergyman is more likely to be steady than other men," said the

[070-178]mother.

[070-179]"Steady, yes; and as selfish as you please."

[070-180]"Your father was a clergyman, Hugh."

[070-181]"I don't mean to say that they are not as good as others; but I won't

[070-182]have it that they are better. They are always dealing with the Bible,

[070-183]till they think themselves apostles. But when money comes up, or

[070-184]comfort, or, for the matter of that either, a pretty woman with a

[070-185]little money, then they are as human as the rest of us."
