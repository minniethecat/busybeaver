## Section 49

[049-001]"If Emily and Nora come here they must come as our guests," said Mrs.

[049-002]Outhouse.

[049-003]"Certainly," said the clergyman. "And if I am told they are in want

[049-004]of a home they shall find one here till their father comes. But I am

[049-005]bound to say that as regards the elder I think her home should be

[049-006]elsewhere."

[049-007]"Of course it should," said Mrs. Outhouse. "I don't know anything

[049-008]about the law, but it seems to me very odd that a young woman should

[049-009]be turned out in this way. You say she has done nothing?"

[049-010]"I will not argue the matter," said Trevelyan.

[049-011]"That's all very well, Mr. Trevelyan," said the lady, "but she's my

[049-012]own niece, and if I don't stand up for her I don't know who will. I

[049-013]never heard such a thing in my life as a wife being sent away after

[049-014]such a fashion as that. We wouldn't treat a cookmaid so; that we

[049-015]wouldn't. As for coming here, she shall come if she pleases, but I

[049-016]shall always say that it's the greatest shame I ever heard of."

[049-017]Nothing came of this visit at last. The lady grew in her anger; and

[049-018]Mr. Trevelyan, in his own defence, was driven to declare that his

[049-019]wife's obstinate intimacy with [[colonel-osborne|Colonel Osborne]] had almost driven

[049-020]him out of his senses. Before he left the parsonage he was brought

[049-021]even to tears by his own narration of his own misery;--whereby Mr.

[049-022]Outhouse was considerably softened, although Mrs. Outhouse became

[049-023]more and more stout in the defence of her own sex. But nothing at

[049-024]last came of it. Trevelyan insisted on paying for his wife, wherever

[049-025]she might be placed; and when he found that this would not be

[049-026]permitted to him at the parsonage, he was very anxious to take some

[049-027]small furnished house in the neighbourhood, in which the two sisters

[049-028]might live for the next six months under the wings of their uncle

[049-029]and aunt. But even Mr. Outhouse was moved to pleasantry by this

[049-030]suggestion, as he explained the nature of the tenements which were

[049-031]common at St. Diddulph's. Two rooms, front and back, they might

[049-032]have for about five-and-sixpence a week in a house with three other

[049-033]families. "But perhaps that is not exactly what you'd like," said Mr.

[049-034]Outhouse. The interview ended with no result, and Mr. Trevelyan took

[049-035]his leave, declaring to himself that he was worse off than the foxes,

[049-036]who have holes in which to lay their heads;--but it must be presumed

[049-037]that his sufferings in this respect were to be by attorney; as it was

[049-038]for his wife, and not for himself, that the necessary hole was now

[049-039]required.

[049-040]As soon as he was gone Mrs. Outhouse answered Nora's letter, and

[049-041]without meaning to be explicit, explained pretty closely what had

[049-042]taken place. The spare bedroom at the parsonage was ready to receive

[049-043]either one or both of the sisters till Sir Marmaduke should be in

[049-044][[london|London]], if one or both of them should choose to come. And though

[049-045]there was no nursery at the parsonage,--for Mr. and Mrs. Outhouse had

[049-046]been blessed with no children,--still room should be made for the

[049-047]little boy. But they must come as visitors,--"as our own nieces,"

[049-048]said Mrs. Outhouse. And she went on to say that she would have

[049-049]nothing to do with the quarrel between Mr. Trevelyan and his wife.

[049-050]All such quarrels were very bad,--but as to this quarrel she could

[049-051]take no part either one side or the other. Then she stated that Mr.

[049-052]Trevelyan had been at the parsonage, but that no arrangement had been

[049-053]made, because Mr. Trevelyan had insisted on paying for their board

[049-054]and lodging.

[049-055]This letter reached [[nuncombe-putney|Nuncombe Putney]] before any reply was received by

[049-056]Mrs. Trevelyan from her husband. This was on the Saturday morning,

[049-057]and Mrs. Trevelyan had pledged herself to Mrs. Stanbury that she

[049-058]would leave the [[clock-house|Clock House]] on the Monday. Of course, there was no

[049-059]need that she should do so. Both Mrs. Stanbury and Priscilla would

[049-060]now have willingly consented to their remaining till Sir Marmaduke

[049-061]should be in England. But Mrs. Trevelyan's high spirit revolted

[049-062]against this after all that had been said. She thought that she

[049-063]should hear from her husband on the morrow, but the post on Sunday

[049-064]brought no letter from Trevelyan. On the Saturday they had finished

[049-065]packing up,--so certain was Mrs. Trevelyan that some instructions as

[049-066]to her future destiny would be sent to her by her lord.

[049-067]At last they decided on the Sunday that they would both go at once

[049-068]to St. Diddulph's; or perhaps it would be more correct to say that

[049-069]this was the decision of the elder sister. Nora would willingly have

[049-070]yielded to Priscilla's entreaties, and have remained. But Emily

[049-071]declared that she could not, and would not, stay in the house. She

[049-072]had a few pounds,--what would suffice for her journey; and as Mr.

[049-073]Trevelyan had not thought proper to send his orders to her, she would

[049-074]go without them. Mrs. Outhouse was her aunt, and her nearest relative

[049-075]in England. Upon whom else could she lean in this time of her great

[049-076]affliction? A letter, therefore, was written to Mrs. Outhouse, saying

[049-077]that the whole party, including the boy and nurse, would be at St.

[049-078]Diddulph's on the Monday evening, and the last cord was put to the

[049-079]boxes.

[049-080]"I suppose that he is very angry," Mrs. Trevelyan said to her sister,

[049-081]"but I do not feel that I care about that now. He shall have nothing

[049-082]to complain of in reference to any gaiety on my part. I will see no

[049-083]one. I will have no--correspondence. But I will not remain here after

[049-084]what he has said to me, let him be ever so angry. I declare, as I

[049-085]think of it, it seems to me that no woman was ever so cruelly treated

[049-086]as I have been." Then she wrote one further line to her husband.

[049-087]Not having received any orders from you, and having

[049-088]promised Mrs. Stanbury that I would leave this house

[049-089]on Monday, I go with Nora to my aunt, Mrs. Outhouse,

[049-090]to-morrow.

[049-091]E. T.

[049-092]On the Sunday evening the four ladies drank tea together, and they

[049-093]all made an effort to be civil, and even affectionate, to each other.

[049-094]Mrs. Trevelyan had at last allowed Priscilla to explain how it had

[049-095]come to pass that she had told her brother that it would be better

[049-096]both for her mother and for herself that the existing arrangements

[049-097]should be brought to an end, and there had come to be an agreement

[049-098]between them that they should all part in amity. But the conversation

[049-099]on the Sunday evening was very difficult.

[049-100]"I am sure we shall always think of you both with the greatest

[049-101]kindness," said Mrs. Stanbury.

[049-102]"As for me," said Priscilla, "your being with us has been a delight

[049-103]that I cannot describe;--only it has been wrong."

[049-104]"I know too well," said Mrs. Trevelyan, "that in our present

[049-105]circumstances we are unable to carry delight with us anywhere."

[049-106]"You hardly understand what our life has been," said Priscilla; "but

[049-107]the truth is that we had no right to receive you in such a house as

[049-108]this. It has not been our way of living, and it cannot continue to be

[049-109]so. It is not wonderful that people should talk of us. Had it been

[049-110]called your house, it might have been better."

[049-111]"And what will you do now?" asked Nora.

[049-112]"Get out of this place as soon as we can. It is often hard to go

[049-113]back to the right path; but it may always be done,--or at least

[049-114]attempted."

[049-115]"It seems to me that I take misery with me wherever I go," said Mrs.

[049-116]Trevelyan.

[049-117]"My dear, it has not been your fault," said Mrs. Stanbury.

[049-118]"I do not like to blame my brother," said Priscilla, "because he has

[049-119]done his best to be good to us all;--and the punishment will fall

[049-120]heaviest upon him, because he must pay for it."

[049-121]"He should not be allowed to pay a shilling," said Mrs. Trevelyan.

[049-122]Then the morning came, and at seven o'clock the two sisters, with the

[049-123]nurse and child, started for Lessboro' Station in Mrs. Crocket's open

[049-124]carriage, the luggage having been sent on in a cart. There were many

[049-125]tears shed, and any one looking at the party would have thought that

[049-126]very dear friends were being torn asunder.

[049-127]"Mother," said Priscilla, as soon as the parlour door was shut, and

[049-128]the two were alone together, "we must take care that we never are

[049-129]brought again into such a mistake as that. They who protect the

[049-130]injured should be strong themselves."

[049-131]CHAPTER XXX.

[049-132]DOROTHY MAKES UP HER MIND.

[049-133]It was true that most ill-natured things had been said at Lessboro'

[049-134]and at Nuncombe Putney about Mrs. Stanbury and the visitors at the

[049-135]Clock House, and that these ill-natured things had spread themselves

[049-136]to [[exeter|Exeter]]. Mrs. Ellison of Lessboro', who was not the most

[049-137]good-natured woman in the world, had told Mrs. Merton of Nuncombe

[049-138]that she had been told that the Colonel's visit to the lady had been

[049-139]made by express arrangement between the Colonel and Mrs. Stanbury.

[049-140]Mrs. Merton, who was very good-natured, but not the wisest woman

[049-141]in the world, had declared that any such conduct on the part of

[049-142]Mrs. Stanbury was quite impossible. "What does it matter which it

[049-143]is,--Priscilla or her mother?" Mrs. Ellison had said. "These are the

[049-144]facts. Mrs. Trevelyan has been sent there to be out of the way of

[049-145]this Colonel; and the Colonel immediately comes down and sees her at

[049-146]the Clock House. But when people are very poor they do get driven to

[049-147]do almost anything."

[049-148]Mrs. Merton, not being very wise, had conceived it to be her duty

[049-149]to repeat this to Priscilla; and Mrs. Ellison, not being very

[049-150]good-natured, had conceived it to be hers to repeat it to Mrs.

[049-151]MacHugh at Exeter. And then Bozzle's coming had become known.

[049-152]"Yes, Mrs. MacHugh, a policeman in mufti down at Nuncombe! I wonder

[049-153]what our friend in the Close here will think about it! I have always

[049-154]said, you know, that if she wanted to keep things straight at

[049-155]Nuncombe, she should have opened her purse-strings."

[049-156]From all which it may be understood, that Priscilla Stanbury's desire

[049-157]to go back to their old way of living had not been without reason.

[049-158]It may be imagined that Miss Stanbury of the Close did not receive

[049-159]with equanimity the reports which reached her. And, of course, when

[049-160]she discussed the matter either with Martha or with Dorothy, she fell

[049-161]back upon her own early appreciation of the folly of the Clock House

[049-162]arrangement. Nevertheless, she had called Mrs. Ellison very bad

[049-163]names, when she learned from her friend Mrs. MacHugh what reports

[049-164]were being spread by the lady from Lessboro'.

[049-165]"Mrs. Ellison! Yes; we all know Mrs. Ellison. The bitterest tongue in

[049-166]Devonshire, and the falsest! There are some people at Lessboro' who

[049-167]would be well pleased if she paid her way there as well as those poor

[049-168]women do at Nuncombe. I don't think much of what Mrs. Ellison says."

[049-169]"But it is bad about the policeman," said Mrs. MacHugh.

[049-170]"Of course it's bad. It's all bad. I'm not saying that it's not bad.

[049-171]I'm glad I've got this other young woman out of it. It's all that

[049-172]young man's doing. If I had a son of my own, I'd sooner follow him to

[049-173]the grave than hear him call himself a Radical."

[049-174]Then, on a sudden, there came to the Close news that Mrs. Trevelyan

[049-175]and her sister were gone. On the very Monday on which they went,

[049-176]Priscilla sent a note on to her sister, in which no special allusion

[049-177]was made to Aunt Stanbury, but which was no doubt written with the

[049-178]intention that the news should be communicated.
