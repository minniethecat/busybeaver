## Section 143

[143-001]Mr. and Mrs. Spalding were covered with glory on the occasion; and

[143-002]perhaps they did not bear their glory as meekly as they should have

[143-003]done. Mrs. Spalding laid herself open to some ridicule from the

[143-004]British Minister's wife because of her inability to understand with

[143-005]absolute clearness the condition of her niece's husband in respect to

[143-006]his late and future seat in Parliament, to the fact of his being a

[143-007]commoner and a nobleman at the same time, and to certain information

[143-008]which was conveyed to her, surely in a most unnecessary manner, that

[143-009]if Mr. Glascock were to die before his father her niece would never

[143-010]become Lady Peterborough, although her niece's son, if she had

[143-011]one, would be the future lord. No doubt she blundered, as was most

[143-012]natural; and then the British Minister's wife made the most of the

[143-013]blunders; and when once Mrs. Spalding ventured to speak of Caroline

[143-014]as her ladyship, not to the British Minister's wife, but to the

[143-015]sister of one of the secretaries, a story was made out of it which

[143-016]was almost as false as it was ill-natured. Poor Caroline was spoken

[143-017]of as her ladyship backward and forwards among the ladies of the

[143-018]legation in a manner which might have vexed her had she known

[143-019]anything about it; but, nevertheless, all the ladies prepared their

[143-020]best flounces to go to the wedding. The time would soon come when she

[143-021]would in truth be a "ladyship," and she might be of social use to any

[143-022]one of the ladies in question.

[143-023]But Mr. Spalding was, for the time, the most disturbed of any of

[143-024]the party concerned. He was a tall, thin, clever Republican of the

[143-025]North,--very fond of hearing himself talk, and somewhat apt to take

[143-026]advantage of the courtesies of conversation for the purpose of making

[143-027]unpardonable speeches. As long as there was any give and take going

[143-028]on in the melee of words he would speak quickly and with energy,

[143-029]seizing his chances among others; but the moment he had established

[143-030]his right to the floor,--as soon as he had won for himself the

[143-031]position of having his turn at the argument, he would dole out his

[143-032]words with considerable slowness, raise his hand for oratorial

[143-033]effect, and proceed as though Time were annihilated. And he would go

[143-034]further even than this, for,--fearing by experience the escape of his

[143-035]victims,--he would catch a man by the button-hole of his coat, or

[143-036]back him ruthlessly into the corner of a room, and then lay on to him

[143-037]without quarter. Since the affair with Mr. Glascock had been settled,

[143-038]he had talked an immensity about England,--not absolutely taking

[143-039]honour to himself because of his intended connection with a lord, but

[143-040]making so many references to the aristocratic side of the British

[143-041]constitution as to leave no doubt on the minds of his hearers as

[143-042]to the source of his arguments. In old days, before all this was

[143-043]happening, Mr. Spalding, though a courteous man in his personal

[143-044]relations, had constantly spoken of England with the bitter

[143-045]indignation of the ordinary American politician. England must be made

[143-046]to disgorge. England must be made to do justice. England must be

[143-047]taught her place in the world. England must give up her claims. In

[143-048]hot moments he had gone further, and had declared that England must

[143-049]be--whipped. He had been specially loud against that aristocracy of

[143-050]England which, according to a figure of speech often used by him,

[143-051]was always feeding on the vitals of the people. But now all this was

[143-052]very much changed. He did not go the length of expressing an opinion

[143-053]that the House of Lords was a valuable institution; but he discussed

[143-054]questions of primogeniture and hereditary legislation, in reference

[143-055]to their fitness for countries which were gradually emerging

[143-056]from feudal systems, with an equanimity, an impartiality, and a

[143-057]perseverance which soon convinced those who listened to him where he

[143-058]had learned his present lessons, and why. "The conservative nature of

[143-059]your institutions, sir," he said to poor Sir Marmaduke at the Baths

[143-060]of Lucca a very few days before the marriage, "has to be studied

[143-061]with great care before its effects can be appreciated in reference

[143-062]to a people who, perhaps, I may be allowed to say, have more in

[143-063]their composition of constitutional reverence than of educated

[143-064]intelligence." Sir Marmaduke, having suffered before, had endeavoured

[143-065]to bolt; but the American had caught him and pinned him, and the

[143-066]Governor of the Mandarins was impotent in his hands. "The position

[143-067]of the great peer of Parliament is doubtless very splendid, and

[143-068]may be very useful," continued Mr. Spalding, who was intending to

[143-069]bring round his argument to the evil doings of certain scandalously

[143-070]extravagant young lords, and to offer a suggestion that in such

[143-071]cases a committee of aged and respected peers should sit and decide

[143-072]whether a second son, or some other heir should not be called to the

[143-073][[money|inheritance]] both of the title and the property. But Mrs. Spalding

[143-074]had seen the sufferings of Sir Marmaduke, and had rescued him. "Mr.

[143-075]Spalding," she had said, "it is too late for politics, and Sir

[143-076]Marmaduke has come out here for a holiday." Then she took her husband

[143-077]by the arm, and led him away helpless.

[143-078]In spite of these drawbacks to the success,--if ought can be

[143-079]said to be a drawback on success of which the successful one is

[143-080]unconscious,--the marriage was prepared with great splendour, and

[143-081]everybody who was anybody in Florence was to be present. There

[143-082]were only to be four bridesmaids, Caroline herself having strongly

[143-083]objected to a greater number. As Wallachia Petrie had fled at

[143-084]the first note of preparation for these trivial and unpalatable

[143-085]festivities, another American young lady was found; and the sister of

[143-086]the English secretary of legation, who had so maliciously spread that

[143-087]report about her "ladyship," gladly agreed to be the fourth.

[143-088]As the reader will remember, the whole party from the Baths of Lucca

[143-089]reached Florence only the day before the marriage, and Nora at

[143-090]the [[social-class|station]] promised to go up to Caroline that same evening. "Mr.

[143-091]Glascock will tell me about the little boy," said Caroline; "but I

[143-092]shall be so anxious to hear about your sister." So Nora crossed the

[143-093]bridge after dinner, and went up to the American Minister's palatial

[143-094]residence. Caroline was then in the loggia, and Mr. Glascock was

[143-095]with her; and for a while they talked about [[emily-trevelyan|Emily Trevelyan]] and her

[143-096]misfortunes. Mr. Glascock was clearly of opinion that Trevelyan would

[143-097]soon be either in an asylum or in his grave. "I could not bring

[143-098]myself to tell your sister so," he said; "but I think your father

[143-099]should be told,--or your mother. Something should be done to put an

[143-100]end to that fearful residence at Casalunga." Then by degrees the

[143-101]conversation changed itself to Nora's prospects; and Caroline, with

[143-102]her friend's hand in hers, asked after [[hugh-stanbury|Hugh Stanbury]].

[143-103]"You will not mind speaking before him,--will you?" said Caroline,

[143-104]putting her hand on her own lover's arm.

[143-105]"Not unless he should mind it," said Nora, smiling. She had meant

[143-106]nothing beyond a simple reply to her friend's question, but he took

[143-107]her words in a different sense, and blushed as he remembered his

[143-108]visit to [[nuncombe-putney|Nuncombe Putney]].

[143-109]"He thinks almost more of your happiness than he does of mine," said

[143-110]Caroline; "which isn't fair, as I am sure that Mr. Stanbury will not

[143-111]reciprocate the attention. And now, dear, when are we to see you?"

[143-112]"Who on earth can say?"

[143-113]"I suppose Mr. Stanbury would say something,--only he is not here."

[143-114]"And papa won't send my letter," said Nora.

[143-115]"You are sure that you will not go out to the Islands with him?"

[143-116]"Quite sure," said Nora. "I have made up my mind so far as that."

[143-117]"And what will your sister do?"

[143-118]"I think she will stay. I think she will say good-bye to papa and

[143-119]mamma here in Florence."

[143-120]"I am quite of opinion that she should not leave her husband alone in

[143-121]Italy," said Mr. Glascock.

[143-122]"She has not told us with [[truth|certainty]]," said Nora; "but I feel sure

[143-123]that she will stay. Papa thinks she ought to go with them to [[london|London]]."

[143-124]"Your papa seems to have two very intractable daughters," said

[143-125]Caroline.

[143-126]"As for me," declared Nora, solemnly, "nothing shall make me go back

[143-127]to the Islands,--unless Mr. Stanbury should tell me to do so."

[143-128]"And they start at the end of July?"

[143-129]"On the last Saturday."

[143-130]"And what will you do then, Nora?"

[143-131]"I believe there are casual wards that people go to."

[143-132]"Casual wards!" said Caroline.

[143-133]"Miss Rowley is condescending to poke her fun at you," said Mr.

[143-134]Glascock.

[143-135]"She is quite welcome, and shall poke as much as she likes; only we

[143-136]must be serious now. If it be necessary, we will get back by the end

[143-137]of July;--won't we, Charles?"

[143-138]"You will do nothing of the kind," said Nora. "What!--give up your

[143-139]honeymoon to provide me with board and lodgings! How can you suppose

[143-140]that I am so selfish or so helpless? I would go to my aunt, Mrs.

[143-141]Outhouse."

[143-142]"We know that that wouldn't do," said Caroline. "You might as well be

[143-143]in Italy as far as Mr. Stanbury is concerned."

[143-144]"If Miss Rowley would go to Monkhams, she might wait for us,"

[143-145]suggested Mr. Glascock. "Old Mrs. Richards is there; and though of

[143-146]course she would be dull--"

[143-147]"It is quite unnecessary," said Nora. "I shall take a two-pair back

[143-148]in a respectable feminine quarter, like any other young woman who

[143-149]wants such accommodation, and shall wait there till my young man can

[143-150]come and give me his arm to church. That is about the way we shall do

[143-151]it. I am not going to give myself any airs, Mr. Glascock, or make any

[143-152]difficulties. Papa is always talking to me about chairs and tables

[143-153]and frying-pans, and I shall practise to do with as few of them as

[143-154]possible. As I am headstrong about having my young man,--and I own

[143-155]that I am headstrong about that,--I guess I've got to fit myself for

[143-156]that sort of life." And Nora, as she said this, pronounced her words

[143-157]with something of a nasal twang, imitating certain countrywomen of

[143-158]her friend's.

[143-159]"I like to hear you joking about it, Nora; because your voice is so

[143-160]cheery and you are so bright when you joke. But, nevertheless, one

[143-161]has to be reasonable, and to look the facts in the face. I don't see

[143-162]how you are to be left in London alone, and you know that your aunt

[143-163]Mrs. Outhouse,--or at any rate your uncle,--would not receive you

[143-164]except on receiving some strong anti-Stanbury pledge."

[143-165]"I certainly shall not give an anti-Stanbury pledge."

[143-166]"And, therefore, that is out of the question. You will have a

[143-167]fortnight or three weeks in London, in all the bustle of their

[143-168]departure, and I declare I think that at the last moment you will go

[143-169]with them."

[143-170]"Never!--unless he says so."

[143-171]"I don't see how you are even to meet--'him,' and talk it over."

[143-172]"I'll manage that. My promise not to write lasts only while we are in

[143-173]Italy."

[143-174]"I think we had better get back to England, Charles, and take pity on

[143-175]this poor destitute one."

[143-176]"If you talk of such a thing I will swear that I will never go to

[143-177]Monkhams. You will find that I shall manage it. It may be that I

[143-178]shall do something very shocking,--so that all your patronage will

[143-179]hardly be able to bring me round afterwards; but I will do something

[143-180]that will serve my purpose. I have not gone so far as this to be

[143-181]turned back now." Nora, as she spoke of having "gone so far," was

[143-182]looking at Mr. Glascock, who was seated in an easy arm-chair close

[143-183]to the girl whom he was to make his wife on the morrow, and she

[143-184]was thinking, no doubt, of the visit which he had made to Nuncombe

[143-185]Putney, and of the first irretrievable step which she had taken when

[143-186]she told him that her love was given to another. That had been her

[143-187]Rubicon. And though there had been periods with her since the passing

[143-188]of it, in which she had felt that she had crossed it in vain, that

[143-189]she had thrown away the splendid security of the other bank without

[143-190]obtaining the perilous object of her ambition,--though there had been

[143-191]moments in which she had almost regretted her own courage and noble

[143-192]action, still, having passed the river, there was nothing for her

[143-193]but to go on to Rome. She was not going to be stopped now by the

[143-194]want of a house in which to hide herself for a few weeks. She was

[143-195]without money, except so much as her mother might be able, almost

[143-196]surreptitiously, to give her. She was without friends to help

[143-197]her,--except these who were now with her, whose friendship had come

[143-198]to her in so singular a manner, and whose power to aid her at the

[143-199]present moment was cruelly curtailed by their own circumstances.

[143-200]Nothing was settled as to her own marriage. In consequence of

[143-201]the promise that had been extorted from her that she should not

[143-202]correspond with Stanbury, she knew nothing of his present wishes or

[143-203]intention. Her father was so offended by her firmness that he would

[143-204]hardly speak to her. And it was evident to her that her mother,

[143-205]though disposed to yield, was still in hopes that her daughter, in

[143-206]the press and difficulty of the moment, would allow herself to be

[143-207]carried away with the rest of the family to the other side of the

[143-208]world. She knew all this,--but she had made up her mind that she

[143-209]would not be carried away. It was not very pleasant, the thought that

[143-210]she would be obliged at last to ask her young man, as she called

[143-211]him, to provide for her; but she would do that and trust herself

[143-212]altogether in his hands sooner than be taken to the Antipodes. "I

[143-213]can be very resolute if I please, my dear," she said, looking at

[143-214]Caroline. Mr. Glascock almost thought that she must have intended to

[143-215]address him.
