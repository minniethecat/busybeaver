## Section 150

[150-001]"Papa, that is unfair, most unfair,--and ungenerous."

[150-002]"Nora," said her mother, "do not speak in that way to your father."

[150-003]"Mamma, it is unfair. Papa accuses Mr. Stanbury of being,--being

[150-004]lukewarm and untrue,--of not being in earnest."

[150-005]"I would rather that he were not in earnest," said Sir Marmaduke.

[150-006]"Mr. Stanbury is ready at any time," continued Nora. "He would have

[150-007]the banns at once read, and marry me in three weeks,--if I would let

[150-008]him."

[150-009]"Good gracious, Nora!" exclaimed Lady Rowley.

[150-010]"But I have refused to name any day, or to make any arrangement,

[150-011]because I did not wish to do so before papa had given his consent.

[150-012]That is why things are in this way. If papa will but let me take a

[150-013]room till I can go to Monkhams, I will have everything arranged from

[150-014]there. You can trust Mr. Glascock for that, and you can trust her."

[150-015]"I suppose your papa will make you some allowance," said Lady Rowley.

[150-016]"She is entitled to nothing, as she has refused to go to her proper

[150-017]home," said Sir Marmaduke.

[150-018]The conversation, which had now become very disagreeable, was

[150-019]not allowed to go any further. And it was well that it should be

[150-020]interrupted. They all knew that Sir Marmaduke must be brought round

[150-021]by degrees, and that both Nora and Lady Rowley had gone as far as was

[150-022]prudent at present. But all trouble on this head was suddenly ended

[150-023]for this evening by the entrance of the waiter with a telegram.

[150-024]It was addressed to Lady Rowley, and she opened it with trembling

[150-025]hands,--as ladies always do open telegrams. It was from Emily

[150-026]Trevelyan. "Louis is much worse. Let somebody come to me. Hugh

[150-027]Stanbury would be the best."

[150-028]In a few minutes they were so much disturbed that no one quite knew

[150-029]what should be done at once. Lady Rowley began by declaring that she

[150-030]would go herself. Sir Marmaduke of course pointed out that this was

[150-031]impossible, and suggested that he would send a lawyer. Nora professed

[150-032]herself ready to start immediately on the journey, but was stopped by

[150-033]a proposition from her sister Lucy that in that case [[hugh-stanbury|Hugh Stanbury]]

[150-034]would of course go with her. Lady Rowley asked whether Hugh would go,

[150-035]and Nora asserted that he would go immediately as a matter of course.

[150-036]She was sure he would go, let the people at the D. R. say what they

[150-037]might. According to her there was always somebody at the call of the

[150-038]editor of the D. R. to do the work of anybody else, when anybody else

[150-039]wanted to go away. Sir Marmaduke shook his head, and was very uneasy.

[150-040]He still thought that a lawyer would be best, feeling, no doubt, that

[150-041]if Stanbury's services were used on such an occasion, there must be

[150-042]an end of all opposition to the marriage. But before half-an-hour was

[150-043]over Stanbury was sent for. The boots of the hotel went off in a cab

[150-044]to the office of the D. R. with a note from Lady Rowley. "Dear Mr.

[150-045]Stanbury,--We have had a telegram from Emily, and want to see you,

[150-046]_at once_. Please come. We shall sit up and wait for you till you do

[150-047]come.--E. R."

[150-048]It was very distressing to them because, let the result be what it

[150-049]might, it was all but impossible that Mrs. Trevelyan should be with

[150-050]them before they had sailed, and it was quite out of the question

[150-051]that they should now postpone their journey. Were Stanbury to start

[150-052]by the morning train on the following day, he could not reach Siena

[150-053]till the afternoon of the fourth day; and let the result be what

[150-054]it might when he arrived there, it would be out of the question

[150-055]that [[emily-trevelyan|Emily Trevelyan]] should come back quite at once, or that she

[150-056]should travel at the same speed. Of course they might hear again by

[150-057]telegram, and also by letter; but they could not see her, or have any

[150-058]hand in her plans. "If anything were to happen, she might have come

[150-059]with us," said Lady Rowley.

[150-060]"It is out of the question," said Sir Marmaduke gloomily. "I could

[150-061]not give up the places I have taken."

[150-062]"A few days more would have done it."

[150-063]"I don't suppose she would wish to go," said Nora. "Of course she

[150-064]would not take Louey there. Why should she? And then I don't suppose

[150-065]he is so ill as that."

[150-066]"There is no saying," said Sir Marmaduke. It was very evident

[150-067]that, whatever might be Sir Marmaduke's opinion, he had no

[150-068]strongly-developed wish for his son-in-law's recovery.

[150-069]They all sat up waiting for Hugh Stanbury till eleven, twelve, one,

[150-070]and two o'clock at night. The "boots" had returned, saying that Mr.

[150-071]Stanbury had not been at the office of the newspaper, but that,

[150-072]according to information received, he certainly would be there that

[150-073]night. No other address had been given to the man, and the note had

[150-074]therefore of necessity been left at the office. Sir Marmaduke became

[150-075]very fretful, and was evidently desirous of being liberated from his

[150-076]night watch. But he could not go himself, and shewed his impatience

[150-077]by endeavouring to send the others away. Lady Rowley replied for

[150-078]herself that she should certainly remain in her corner on the sofa

[150-079]all night, if it were necessary; and as she slept very soundly in her

[150-080]corner, her comfort was not much impaired. Nora was pertinacious in

[150-081]refusing to go to bed. "I should only go to my own room, papa, and

[150-082]remain there," she said. "Of course I must speak to him before he

[150-083]goes." Sophie and Lucy considered that they had as much right to sit

[150-084]up as Nora, and submitted to be called geese and idiots by their

[150-085]father.

[150-086]Sir Marmaduke had arisen with a snort from a short slumber, and

[150-087]had just sworn that he and everybody else should go to bed, when

[150-088]there came a ring at the front-door bell. The trusty boots had also

[150-089]remained up, and in two minutes Hugh Stanbury was in the room. He

[150-090]had to make his excuses before anything else could be said. When he

[150-091]reached the D. R. office between ten and eleven, it was absolutely

[150-092]incumbent on him to write a leading article before he left it. He

[150-093]had been in the reporter's gallery of the House all the evening,

[150-094]and he had come away laden with his article. "It was certainly

[150-095]better that we should remain up, than that the whole town should be

[150-096]disappointed," said Sir Marmaduke, with something of a sneer.

[150-097]"It is so very, very good of you to come," said Nora.

[150-098]"Indeed, it is," said Lady Rowley; "but we were quite sure you would

[150-099]come." Having kissed and blessed him as her son-in-law, Lady Rowley

[150-100]was now prepared to love him almost as well as though he had been

[150-101]Lord Peterborough.

[150-102]"Perhaps, Mr. Stanbury, we had better show you this telegram," said

[150-103]Sir Marmaduke, who had been [[reputation|standing]] with the scrap of paper in his

[150-104]hand since the ring of the bell had been heard. Hugh took the message

[150-105]and read it. "I do not know what should have made my daughter mention

[150-106]your name," continued Sir Marmaduke;--"but as she has done so, and as

[150-107]perhaps the unfortunate invalid himself may have alluded to you, we

[150-108]thought it best to send for you."

[150-109]"No doubt it was best, Sir Marmaduke."

[150-110]"We are so situated that I cannot go. It is absolutely necessary that

[150-111]we should leave town for Southampton on Friday week. The ship sails

[150-112]on Saturday."

[150-113]"I will go as a matter of course," said Hugh. "I will start at

[150-114]once,--at any time. To tell the truth, when I got Lady Rowley's note,

[150-115]I thought that it was to be so. Trevelyan and I were very intimate at

[150-116]one time, and it may be that he will receive me without displeasure."

[150-117]There was much to be discussed, and considerable difficulty in

[150-118]the discussion. This was enhanced, too, by the feeling in the

[150-119]minds of all of them that Hugh and Sir Marmaduke would not meet

[150-120]again,--probably for many years. Were they to part now on terms of

[150-121]close affection, or were they to part almost as strangers? Had Lucy

[150-122]and Sophie not persistently remained up, Nora would have faced the

[150-123]difficulty, and taken the bull by the horns, and asked her father to

[150-124]sanction her engagement in the presence of her lover. But she could

[150-125]not do it before so many persons, even though the persons were her

[150-126]own nearest relatives. And then there arose another embarrassment.

[150-127]Sir Marmaduke, who had taught himself to believe that Stanbury

[150-128]was so poor as hardly to have the price of a dinner in his

[150-129]pocket,--although, in fact, our friend Hugh was probably the richer

[150-130]man of the two,--said something about defraying the cost of the

[150-131]journey. "It is taken altogether on our behalf," said Sir Marmaduke.

[150-132]Hugh became red in the face, looked angry, and muttered a word or two

[150-133]about Trevelyan being the oldest friend he had in the world,--"even

[150-134]if there were nothing else." Sir Marmaduke felt ashamed of

[150-135]himself,--without cause, indeed, for the offer was natural,--said

[150-136]nothing further about it; but appeared to be more stiff and ungainly

[150-137]than ever.

[150-138]The Bradshaw was had out and consulted, and nearly half an hour

[150-139]was spent in poring over that wondrous volume. It is the fashion

[150-140]to abuse Bradshaw,--we speak now especially of Bradshaw the

[150-141]Continental,--because all the minutest details of the autumn tour,

[150-142]just as the tourist thinks that it may be made, cannot be made patent

[150-143]to him at once without close research amidst crowded figures. After

[150-144]much experience we make bold to say that Bradshaw knows more, and

[150-145]will divulge more in a quarter of an hour, of the properest mode of

[150-146]getting from any city in Europe to any other city more than fifty

[150-147]miles distant, than can be learned in that first city in a single

[150-148]morning with the aid of a courier, a carriage, a pair of horses, and

[150-149]all the temper that any ordinary tourist possesses. The Bradshaw was

[150-150]had out, and it was at last discovered that nothing could be gained

[150-151]in the journey from [[london|London]] to Siena by starting in the morning.

[150-152]Intending as he did to travel through without sleeping on the road,

[150-153]Stanbury could not do better than leave London by the night mail

[150-154]train, and this he determined to do. But when that was arranged, then

[150-155]came the nature of his commission. What was he to do? No commission

[150-156]could be given to him. A telegram should be sent to Emily the next

[150-157]morning to say that he was coming; and then he would hurry on and

[150-158]take his orders from her.

[150-159]They were all in doubt, terribly in doubt, whether the aggravated

[150-160]malady of which the telegram spoke was malady of the mind or of the

[150-161]body. If of the former nature then the difficulty might be very great

[150-162]indeed; and it would be highly expedient that Stanbury should have

[150-163]some one in Italy to assist him. It was Nora who suggested that he

[150-164]should carry a letter of introduction to Mr. Spalding, and it was she

[150-165]who wrote it. Sir Marmaduke had not foregathered very closely with

[150-166]the English Minister, and nothing was said of assistance that should

[150-167]be peculiarly British. Then, at last, about three or four in the

[150-168]morning came the moment for parting. Sir Marmaduke had suggested that

[150-169]Stanbury should dine with them on the next day before he started, but

[150-170]Hugh had declined, alleging that as the day was at his command it

[150-171]must be devoted to the work of providing for his absence. In truth,

[150-172]Sir Marmaduke had given the invitation with a surly voice, and Hugh,

[150-173]though he was ready to go to the North Pole for any others of the

[150-174]family, was at the moment in an aggressive mood of mind towards Sir

[150-175]Marmaduke.
