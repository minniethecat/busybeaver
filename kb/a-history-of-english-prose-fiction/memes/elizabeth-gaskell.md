# Elizabeth Gaskell

## Life

Elizabeth Cleghorn Gaskell (1810–1865), known as Mrs. Gaskell, was born in [[london|London]] and raised in Knutsford, Cheshire. She married William Gaskell, a Unitarian minister in Manchester, and it was her life in that industrial city that gave her intimate knowledge of the conditions she would later depict in her fiction. Her early writing was a response to personal tragedy—she began her first novel after the death of her infant son. Gaskell became closely associated with the literary circle that included [[charles-dickens|Charles Dickens]] (who published much of her work in his periodicals) and Charlotte Brontë, whose biography she was commissioned to write after Brontë's death.

## Literary Significance

Gaskell belongs to the school of [[novel|English novel]]ists who used fiction as a vehicle for social commentary. Her most celebrated novel, *Mary Barton*, is "a more powerful novel of purpose" than [[charles-kingsley|Charles Kingsley]]'s *Alton Locke*, one which "enlists the sympathies of the reader very strongly with the trials of the manufacturing classes" (046-010). In this work "Mrs. Gaskell described the life and trials of the manufacturing classes with great ability" (042-010). The novel was a landmark of industrial fiction, addressing the grievances of workers and the widening gap between rich and poor in Victorian [[england|England]]. Beyond her novels of social purpose, Gaskell also made a significant contribution to literary biography. "The pathetic story of the lives of the Brontë sisters, supplied by Mrs. Gaskell, has deepened the interest excited by the early popularity of 'Jane Eyre'" (042-009). Her *Life of Charlotte Brontë* (1857) remains a classic of literary biography, though its frankness generated controversy and even legal threats at the time of its publication.

## Major Works

- **Mary Barton** (1848) — Gaskell's first novel and her most influential work of social purpose. It "enlists the sympathies of the reader very strongly with the trials of the manufacturing classes" (046-010). Tuckerman ranks it, alongside *Uncle Tom's Cabin*, as a leading example of the novel of purpose. The novel describes "the life and trials of the manufacturing classes with great ability" (042-010).
- **Cranford** (1851–1853) — A gentle, humorous depiction of life in a small Cheshire town, based on her memories of Knutsford.
- **Ruth** (1853) — A controversial novel treating the subject of the "fallen woman" with sympathy.
- **North and South** (1854–1855) — An industrial novel contrasting life in the rural South and the manufacturing North of [[england|England]].
- **The Life of Charlotte Brontë** (1857) — The biography that "deepened the interest" in the Brontë sisters' lives and works (042-009).
- **Wives and Daughters** (1864–1866) — Her last and unfinished novel, considered by many her finest.
