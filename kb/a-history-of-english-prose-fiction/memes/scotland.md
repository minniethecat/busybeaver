# Scotland

## Overview

Scotland holds a distinctive place in the history of English prose fiction. From [[tobias-smollett|Tobias Smollett]] in the eighteenth century through Sir [[walter-scott|Walter Scott]] and his successors, Scottish writers, settings, and traditions have enriched English fiction immeasurably. The peculiarities of the language of Scotland, along with its dramatic history and landscape, provided material for a literary tradition that achieved world-wide influence. (040-024)

## Literary Significance

Scotland's contribution to English fiction is inseparable from the career of Sir [[walter-scott|Walter Scott]], who made the [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]] of Scottish history and the character of Scottish life familiar to readers across Europe and [[america|America]]. (041-008) Scott's intimate knowledge of Scottish law, traditions, and landscape, combined with his extraordinary narrative gifts, transformed both the novel as a form and the perception of Scotland as a cultural entity.

Before Scott, Smollett had already brought Scottish characters and perspectives into the eighteenth-century novel. After Scott, a succession of [[scottish-fiction|Scottish novel]]ists — including [[john-galt|John Galt]], [[susan-ferrier|Susan Ferrier]], and later Robert Louis Stevenson and James Barrie — continued to explore Scottish themes.

## Key Locations

- **Edinburgh**: Scotland's literary capital, home to Scott, Stevenson, and the *Edinburgh Review*
- **The Highlands**: Setting for Scott's *Waverley* and *Rob Roy*, representing the romantic Scotland of clan and mountain
- **The Borders**: Scott's beloved border country, rich in ballad tradition and historical association
- **Glasgow**: Commercial and industrial Scotland, increasingly represented in [[the-nineteenth-century-novel|nineteenth-century fiction]]

## Role in Fiction

Scotland appears in English fiction in several distinct roles: as a source of romantic history and landscape (Scott's Waverley novels), as a region of distinctive manners and dialect (Galt, Ferrier), and as a contrast to English social norms. The [[scottish-fiction|Scottish novel]] tradition established the viability of regional fiction within English literature, paving the way for later regionalist movements.
