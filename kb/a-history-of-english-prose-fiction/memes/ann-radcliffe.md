## Life

Ann Radcliffe (née Ward) was born in [[london|London]] in 1764. She married William Radcliffe, an [[oxford|Oxford]] graduate and journalist, in 1787. The marriage was a supportive one, and her husband encouraged her literary pursuits. Radcliffe led a notably private life, avoiding the publicity that accompanied her considerable literary success. She ceased publishing novels in 1797, though she lived until 1823, and became the subject of many rumours and posthumous myths, including a false report that she had died of terror induced by her own imaginings.

## Literary Significance

Ann Radcliffe was the greatest contributor to the [[Gothic Fiction|Gothic]] romantic revival in English fiction. Along with [[Horace Walpole]] and [[clara-reeve|Clara Reeve]], she took a chief part in originating and sustaining this movement (038-009). [[clara-reeve|Clara Reeve]] and the celebrated Mrs. Radcliffe did much to sustain the prominent position which women were taking in fictitious composition (038-005).

Radcliffe's method differed markedly from her predecessors. Her plan was not to summon a spectre from his resting-place and to make him move among flesh and blood personages (039-010). Instead, she simply described the superstitious fears of her heroes and heroines, and sought to make her reader share in them. She excited the imagination by highly wrought scenes of horror, but rather than ascribing those scenes to the intervention of supernatural beings, she showed them to proceed from natural causes (039-011). The terror felt by her fictitious characters and shared by the reader was not so much inspired by real dangers from without as by superstitious fear within (039-011).

Her scenes of supernatural suspense were executed with remarkable skill. The following passage illustrates her method: "From the disturbed slumber into which she then sunk, she was soon awakened by a noise, which seemed to arise within her chamber; but the silence that prevailed, as she tearfully listened, inclined her to believe that she had been alarmed by such sounds as sometimes occur in dreams" (039-012). Such passages are excellent examples of Mrs. Radcliffe's power of depicting and exciting fear (039-014). The loneliness of her heroines, their dread of real dangers inclining their minds to expect the unreal, are shown with an art of which neither Walpole nor Reeve were capable (039-014).

But what Mrs. Radcliffe attempted, she carried out with very great skill (039-016). She placed the scenes of her narratives in Sicily, [[italy|Italy]], or the south of [[france|France]], and made good use of the warm natures and vivid imaginations born of southern climates. Every aid which an effective *mise en scène* could supply to her supernatural effects was most skilfully brought into play — lonely castles, secret passages, gloomy churches, and monkish superstitions (039-016). In her descriptions of scenery, she chose nature in its most awe-inspiring forms, and instilled into the reader's mind the same sense of the insignificance of man (039-016). Buckle's description of the effect of nature upon human imagination and credulity is recalled by the striking manner in which Mrs. Radcliffe moulded the surroundings of her heroes and heroines (039-017).

The Radcliffe school had a lasting influence, though its limitations were evident. Lewis in *The Monk* and Maturin in *The Family of Montorio* carried the principles of the Radcliffe school beyond the verge of absurdity (040-001). The works of Walpole, [[clara-reeve|Clara Reeve]], and particularly of Mrs. Radcliffe, contain very decided merits. They made a school which has found many admirers and has given a vast deal of pleasure (039-015).

## Major Works

- **The Mysteries of Udolpho** (1794) — Radcliffe's masterpiece, set in the Apennines, following the orphaned Emily St. Aubert through terrifying adventures and seemingly supernatural phenomena.
- **The Romance of the Forest** (1791) — An early [[gothic-fiction|Gothic novel]] that established Radcliffe's reputation for atmospheric suspense.
- **The [[italy|Italian]]** (1797) — A dark tale of the Inquisition featuring the villainous monk Schedoni; considered by some critics her finest work.
- **A Sicilian Romance** (1790) — Her first published novel, introducing many of the Gothic conventions she would later perfect.
