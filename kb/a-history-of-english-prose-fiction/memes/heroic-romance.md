# Heroic Romance

## Definition

Heroic [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]] is a genre of long-form prose fiction that flourished in seventeenth-century [[france|France]] and found imitators in [[england|England]] after the Restoration. The French termed these works "les romans de longue haleine" — literally, [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]]s of long breath — reflecting their prodigious length and elaborate narrative construction. The genre was created by Marin le Roy de Gomberville, Madeleine de Scudéry, and Gauthier de Costes de La Calprenède.

## Historical Context

With the [[the-restoration|Restoration of Charles II]] in 1660, "the pensions and fashions which were sent across the Channel from the court of Louis XIV brought with them a curious species of fiction which had a temporary vogue in [[england|England]]." (018-001) The heroic [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]] arrived as part of a broader current of French cultural influence that shaped English taste in the latter half of the seventeenth century.

Tuckerman situates the heroic [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]] as a descendant of earlier narrative traditions: "This was the bulky but enervated descendant of chivalric and pastoral [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]]." (018-002) Unlike its predecessors, however, the heroic [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]] was not rooted in authentic social ideals: "The tales of chivalry and of pastoral life had their *raison d'être*. The feudal knighthood found in the tournaments, in the adventures of knight-errantry, and in the supernatural agencies which filled their volumes of [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]], the reflection of their own aspirations and beliefs." (018-002)

## Key Characteristics

The heroic [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]] has several defining features that distinguish it from earlier and later forms of fiction:

- **Improbable classical settings**: The narratives unfold among Greek warriors, Parthian princesses, and Carthaginian soldiers, though these ancient figures "converse in the affected style of the French court." (018-005) "Artaxerxes appears on one page, the queen of the Amazons on the next, then the king of Lacedæmon, Alexander the Great, even a prince of Mexico." (018-005)

- **Love subordinated to martial valor — in theory**: While imitating the martial character of chivalric tales, the heroic [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]] "subordinated that character to love." (018-003) Warriors are perpetually "distracted by a pure, all-absorbing, and never-dying love as foreign to their nature as to that of the readers of heroic [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]]." (018-005)

- **Roman à clef**: The genre's success depended largely on "the circumstance, that under the disguise of Greek warriors or Parthian princesses, there were really described contemporary beauties and courtiers, who fondly believed that they had attained, through the genius of Calprenède and Scudéri, an enviable immortality." (018-005)

- **Extreme length**: The works are "monumental" in scale, built on interlocking plots and digressions that extend across thousands of pages.

- **Artificiality**: Tuckerman judges the heroic [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]] as "unnatural and artificial, rather than ideal." (018-003) The artificial passion "which seemed appropriate to ideal shepherds tuning their pipes under a perpetual sunshine, became absurd when applied to Greek or Carthaginian soldiers." (018-003)

## Notable Examples

**French originals**: The genre was established by Gomberville, Scudéry, and Calprenède. Their major works — including Scudéry's *Artamène, ou le Grand Cyrus* and Calprenède's *Cléopâtre* — were widely read across Europe.

**English imitations**:

- *Eliana* (1661): A "bona-fide imitation" that "would have served much better as a caricature." Tuckerman notes that "to the absurdity of incident is added an absurdity of language which gives the book almost a comic aspect." (018-006)

- *Parthenissa* (1664) by Roger Boyle, later Earl of Orrery: "Dunlop considered the best work of this description to be the 'Parthenissa.'" Though "marked by the faults of prolixity and incongruity characteristic of the heroic style," it is "not without narrative interest or literary merit." (018-006)

- *Argenis* (1621): A "partly political and partly heroic volume" by a Scotchman named Barclay. Written in Latin, it is "hardly to be included in English fiction." (015-005 footnote 81)

The genre proved "as ephemeral in [[england|England]] as the cloaks and feathers with which it had crossed the Channel." (018-006)
