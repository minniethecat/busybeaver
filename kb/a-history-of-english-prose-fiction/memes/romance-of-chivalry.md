# Romance of Chivalry

(001-020)The romance of chivalry emerged in the [[middle-ages|Middle Ages]] as a distinctive form of fiction, closely bound to the feudal system and the ideals of knighthood. As Tuckerman observes, "In the midst of an age of gloom and anarchy, when Feudalism was slowly building up a new social organization on the ruins of the Roman Empire, arose that spirit of chivalry, which, in its connection with the Christian religion, forms so sharp a division between the sentiments of ancient and modern times."

## Origins and Character

(001-021)The chivalric romances drew from diverse sources across cultures and traditions. From Northern Europe came "dark and terrible fancies, malignant enchanters, and death-dealing spirits"; from Arabia and the East came "gorgeous pictures of palaces built of gold and precious stones, magic rings" and fantastical creatures; from ancient Greece and Rome came "memories of the heroes and mysteries of mythology." This fusion of traditions produced a unique literary form that reflected the character of the medieval world.

(001-022)Tuckerman identifies two dominant ideas that shaped the romances and their readers: "One religious, which had fashioned the gigantic cathedrals, and swept the masses from their native soil to hurl them upon the Holy Land; the other secular, which had built feudal fortresses, and set the man of courage erect and armed within his own domain." These two forces — the Roman Church and the Feudal System — formed the framework within which the romance of chivalry flourished.

## The Spirit of Chivalry

(001-023)The chivalric ideal was fundamentally a military one. Under feudalism, "every man was compelled to look upon war as his natural occupation." Tuckerman describes how the feudal imagination was moved "through the senses, and especially through the eye" — by "shining armor, horses with brilliant trappings, gorgeous dress, and martial show." The spirit of adventure manifested in tournaments, private feuds, and the extravagances of knight-errantry.

(001-024)The influence of the Roman Church was equally profound. For centuries, the Church was "the sole teacher and the sole external source of moral elevation," shaping the moral landscape within which the romances were written and read. The supernatural elements that pervade chivalric romance — enchanters, spirits, and divine interventions — reflect the deep religious sensibility of the age.

## Themes and Content

(001-026)The romances typically centered on the legendary figures of Arthur and [[charlemagne|Charlemagne]], weaving together chivalric adventure with supernatural elements. They served as both entertainment and moral instruction, holding "the mirror up to nature" to show the age its own character and ideals.

## Legacy

(047-002)The romance of chivalry left an enduring mark on English prose fiction. Though the form declined with the passing of the feudal age, its influence persisted in the development of the novel—particularly in the emphasis on individual adventure, the exploration of romantic love, and the narrative structures that would later be adapted by authors from Richardson to Scott.
