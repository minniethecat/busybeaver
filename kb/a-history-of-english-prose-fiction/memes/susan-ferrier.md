# Susan Ferrier

## Life

Susan Edmonstone Ferrier (1782–1854) was born in Edinburgh, the youngest daughter of James Ferrier, a Writer to the Signet and a colleague of Sir [[walter-scott|Walter Scott]]. She grew up in Edinburgh's legal and literary circles, where she formed an acquaintance with Scott himself. Ferrier never married and lived quietly with her family, writing her novels in private. Her literary output was small in quantity—only three completed novels—but each met with considerable success and critical regard.

## Literary Significance

Ferrier belongs to the tradition of [[scotland|Scottish]] domestic fiction that flourished in the wake of Scott's Waverley novels. Tuckerman places her among the women novelists who gave the form "a position of respectability and dignity" (040-028), alongside Austen, Edgeworth, and Stowe. Her novels are noted for their lively sketches of [[scotland|Scottish]] society. "Miss Ferrier's novels, 'Inheritance' and 'Marriage,' were greatly admired by Scott, and now, some sixty years later, are still widely read" (042-009). Her particular strengths lay in plot construction and naturalistic character drawing: "Miss Ferrier's skill in the construction of a plot, her natural studies of character and the liveliness of her descriptions have kept her works popular, notwithstanding great changes in the public taste" (042-009). She is mentioned in the context of [[scotland|Scottish]] fiction alongside [[john-galt|John Galt]] and [[jane-porter|Jane Porter]] as one of the writers who maintained public interest in "the land which Sir [[walter-scott|Walter Scott]] taught it to know and love so well" (041-015). Tuckerman also includes her among those novelists "whose works are to be ranked among the best works of fiction in any language" (040-026) and among those whose works have "afforded to hundreds of thousands, young and old, a never failing source of healthful entertainment" (047-006).

## Major Works

- **Marriage** (1818) — Ferrier's first novel, a satirical comedy of manners contrasting English and [[scotland|Scottish]] society through the story of a young Englishwoman who marries a [[scotland|Scottish]] laird. Admired by Scott and widely popular throughout the nineteenth century.
- **Inheritance** (1824) — Her second novel, noted for its intricate plotting and vivid gallery of [[scotland|Scottish]] characters. Praised by Scott and considered by some critics superior to *Marriage*.
- **Destiny** (1831) — Her final novel, dedicated to Sir [[walter-scott|Walter Scott]].
