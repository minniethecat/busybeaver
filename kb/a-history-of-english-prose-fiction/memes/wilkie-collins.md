# Wilkie Collins

## Life

Wilkie Collins (1824–1889) was an [[novel|English novel]]ist and playwright, a close friend and collaborator of [[charles-dickens|Charles Dickens]]. He was the son of the landscape painter William Collins. Collins studied law at Lincoln's Inn but soon turned to writing as his primary occupation. His work appeared alongside that of Mortimer Collins, G. H. Lewes, and Shirley Brooks in the mid-Victorian literary scene. (044-016)

## Literary Significance

Collins is recognized as a pioneer of sensation fiction, a genre that combined elements of the [[gothic-fiction|Gothic novel]] with realistic contemporary settings. Tuckerman observes that Collins' "greatest power lies in the construction of his plot, in the mystery in which he can shroud his characters, and in the surprisingly real and effective, though dark and unusual, stroke-like delineations of character." (042-020)

Beyond sensation fiction, Collins also contributed to the historical novel. His work *Antonina* studied the life of ancient Rome, placing him among [[the-nineteenth-century-novel|Victorian novel]]ists who used historical settings for fictional purposes. (046-004) In this he stood alongside [[john-gibson-lockhart|John Gibson Lockhart]], whose *Valerius* treated a similar Roman theme, and other historical novelists of the period who sought to recreate the atmosphere of antiquity through imaginative reconstruction. (046-004)

## Major Works

- **Antonina** (1850) — A historical novel set in ancient Rome, studying the life and decline of the Roman Empire through fictional narrative.
- **The Woman in White** (1859–60) — A landmark sensation novel featuring mystery, mistaken identity, and psychological suspense, serialized in Dickens' *All the Year Round*.
- **The Moonstone** (1868) — Often considered the first detective novel in English, featuring a complex plot centered on a stolen Indian diamond.
