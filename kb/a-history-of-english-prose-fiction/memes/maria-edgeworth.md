## Life

Maria Edgeworth was born in [[oxford|Oxford]]shire in 1768, the daughter of [[Richard Lovell Edgeworth]], an Anglo-[[ireland|Irish]] inventor and educational reformer. In 1782 she moved to the family estate at Edgeworthstown, County Longford, [[ireland|Ireland]], where she spent most of her adult life managing the estate and writing. Her father's influence on her early intellectual development was considerable, and together they produced *Practical Education* (1798), a treatise reflecting their progressive educational ideas.

Living in [[ireland|Ireland]] gave Edgeworth intimate knowledge of [[ireland|Irish]] society at a time of great political turbulence. She observed the lives of both landlords and tenants, and these observations supplied the material for her most significant fiction. Edgeworth maintained an active literary correspondence with contemporaries including [[Sir Walter Scott]] and [[Jane Austen]]. She remained unmarried throughout her life and continued writing until her death in 1849.

## Literary Significance

First among the contributors to the novel of [[ireland|Irish]] life and manners may be mentioned Maria Edgeworth, by whose successful labors Scott was first inspired to undertake his own (042-003). This acknowledgment by Scott himself places Edgeworth at the fountainhead of the regional novel tradition. In Miss Edgeworth's works, [[ireland|Ireland]] found a true exposition of her wrongs and her virtues, and also of her follies and errors (042-003).

The evils of absenteeism were powerfully illustrated in her novel of the same name. In *Castle Rackrent*, the trials and difficulties of landlord and tenant were described with genuine sympathy and dramatic force. The peculiarities of [[ireland|Irish]] temper and character were studied by Miss Edgeworth with a fidelity which has given her novels the same national stamp and value which belong to those of Scott (042-003). Like him, too, she did much to raise fiction in character, scope, and influence.

Whether describing [[ireland|Irish]], English, or fashionable life, she is always true to nature, always pure and elevated in tone. Her works are neither marred by the coarseness of the past, nor by the false delicacy of the present (042-003). She studiously avoids error and exaggeration in every form. Sentimentality and mock heroism have no place in her pages. While she is wanting in poetry, she is singularly rich in the scenes and characters of every-day life, and her novels are marked by a common-sense knowledge of the world which never degenerates into commonplace (042-003).

Edgeworth's power to move readers emotionally is attested by her own response to literature. She was deeply moved by the *Simple Story* of [[elizabeth-inchbald|Mrs. Inchbald]]: "Its effect upon my feelings," she said after reading it for the fourth time, "was as powerful as at the first reading; I never read any novel that affected me so strongly, or that so completely possessed me with the belief in the real existence of all the persons it represents" (038-002). This capacity for deep emotional engagement mirrors the quality she sought to achieve in her own fiction.

Maria Edgeworth stands among the fifteen distinguished women — from herself and [[jane-austen|Jane Austen]] to [[george-eliot|George Eliot]] — who contributed to the single department of fiction concerned with English domestic life (042-018). Her influence extended beyond her own generation: the enduring popularity of such women writers is sufficient commentary on the success with which woman's talent has been directed toward fiction (042-019).

## Major Works

- **Castle Rackrent** (1800) — A landmark novel tracing four generations of an [[ireland|Irish]] landlord family through the unreliable narration of an old family steward, Thady Quirk. Considered the first regional novel and the first [[irish-fiction|Irish novel]] of significance.
- **The Absentee** (1812) — A novel illustrating the damaging effects of absentee landlordism on [[ireland|Irish]] society and economy.
- **Belinda** (1801) — A novel of English fashionable life exploring questions of marriage, female education, and social conduct.
- **Practical Education** (1798, with Richard Lovell Edgeworth) — An influential educational treatise based on the Edgeworths' observations of child development.
- **Tales of Fashionable Life** (1809–1812) — A series of moral tales depicting the consequences of social ambition and folly.
