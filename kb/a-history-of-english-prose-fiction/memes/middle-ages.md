# Middle Ages

## Definition

The Middle Ages is the historical period spanning roughly from the fifth to the fifteenth century, bridging the fall of the Roman Empire and the dawn of the Renaissance. In the context of English prose fiction, the Middle Ages is the crucible in which the [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]] of chivalry — the earliest form of extended narrative fiction in English — was forged.

## Historical Context

Tuckerman opens his history by situating the [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]] of chivalry squarely within the medieval world: "In the midst of an age of gloom and anarchy, when Feudalism was slowly building up a new social organization on the ruins of the Roman Empire, arose that spirit of chivalry, which, in its connection with the Christian religion, forms so sharp a division between the sentiments of ancient and modern times." (001-020)

The social conditions of the Middle Ages directly shaped the literature it produced. "During the anarchy of the Middle Ages, every man was compelled to look to himself alone for protection." (001-023) This circumstance created the social institution of feudalism, with its hierarchy of lord and vassal, and its code of military honor — the raw material for [[romance-of-chivalry|chivalric romance]].

The intellectual world of the Middle Ages was dominated by the Church. "When the critical spirit of inquiry began to show itself in the Middle Ages, it found in the theological writings handed down from earlier ages an obstacle which it took centuries to overcome." (001-025) Between "the vague ignorance of the Middle Ages and the new growth of learning" lay the transitional period that produced the first English prose fiction. (007-003)

## Key Characteristics

The Middle Ages as a literary epoch is defined by several features that shaped early fiction:

- **Feudalism and chivalry**: "The chivalry of the Middle Ages is essentially the chivalry of the feudal system." (003-001) The knight's code of honor, courage, and courtly love became the central subject matter of [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]].

- **The elevation of woman**: Tuckerman notes it is "remarkable that in the Middle Ages woman should for the first time be raised to a position of influence and supremacy." (003-002) This social shift drove the courtly love tradition central to [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]].

- **Martial enthusiasm**: Tales of warfare and tournaments dominated: "the military enthusiasm of the Middle Ages could thoroughly appreciate" the detailed descriptions of combat that fill [[romance-of-chivalry|chivalric romance]]s. (005-002)

- **Moral seriousness**: "Life in the Middle Ages was a very serious affair, and in the [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]]s there was almost no attempt at wit or humor." (006-012) The [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]]s reflected a world where survival depended on strength and virtue.

- **Absence of character delineation**: "The authors of the [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]]s of chivalry never attempted delineation of character. Their heroes are good knights or bad knights, and in either case possess only the particular qualities which would place them in one of these categories." (006-012)

## The Transition to Modernity

As the Middle Ages waned, new social forces emerged. The [[elizabethan-literature|Elizabethan age]] "had worked itself free from the intellectual sloth of the Middle Ages, although it was familiarizing itself with an almost unknown world abroad, and creating a new world at home." (011-001)

Yet the imaginative legacy of the Middle Ages endured. Malory's *Morte d'Arthur*, a compendium of Arthurian [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]] written at the close of the medieval period, preserved the chivalric tradition for later centuries. As Tuckerman observes, "the [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]]s of chivalry, which reflected with great faithfulness the character of the age, and having formed for three centuries the principal literary entertainment of the knighthood of Europe, left on the new civilization, and the new literature which had outgrown and discarded it, lasting traces of its natural beauty." (001-020)

By the time Spenser wrote *The Faerie Queene*, the Middle Ages were "becoming a part of the past." (007-005) The transition was complete: chivalry had given way to allegory, and the [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]] to the novel.
