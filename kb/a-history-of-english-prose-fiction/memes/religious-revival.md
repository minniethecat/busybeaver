**The Religious Revival** of the eighteenth century was a powerful spiritual movement that reshaped English society and influenced the moral development of prose fiction.

Led by figures such as John Wesley and George Whitefield, the revival injected a new seriousness into public life and provided a counterweight to the worldly cynicism inherited from [[The Restoration]]. Its impact on fiction was indirect but substantial—it created a reading public with strong moral expectations and inspired novelists to take ethical questions seriously. Tuckerman notes the "power and influence of the religious revival" in shaping the cultural climate in which the [[Novel]] matured (033-001).

## Overview

The religious revival, often called the Evangelical or Methodist awakening, began in the 1730s as a reaction against the perceived spiritual deadness of the established Church of [[england|England]].

It emphasized personal conversion, emotional preaching, and the moral reform of society. The movement spread rapidly among the working and middle classes, creating new networks of literacy and book distribution. Its methods proved "singularly adapted to the work of the religious revivalist" in reaching audiences that the traditional church had neglected (033-007).

## Historical Context

The revival emerged from a society still recovering from the moral legacy of the Restoration.

By the early eighteenth century, many observers felt that the religious establishment had grown complacent and worldly. The revival offered an alternative—a religion of the heart that appealed to emotion rather than intellect, to personal experience rather than doctrinal subtlety.

The movement had different effects on different social classes. The upper classes, Tuckerman observes, were "much less influenced by the religious revival than the lower," preserving a greater distance from its emotional intensity (034-002). But the middle and working classes embraced it, creating a vast new reading public with distinctive moral sensibilities.

## Key Figures

| Figure | Role | Contribution |
|--------|------|-------------|
| John Wesley | Founder of Methodism | Preached to vast outdoor congregations; organized Methodist societies |
| George Whitefield | Evangelist | Powerful open-air preacher who reached audiences across [[england|Britain]] and [[america|America]] |
| William Law | Theologian | His *Serious Call* (1728) influenced the early revival and later writers |

These figures, while not themselves novelists, created an ethical climate that shaped the expectations of the novel-reading public. The demand for fiction that combined entertainment with moral instruction was a direct legacy of the revival's emphasis on personal righteousness.

## Impact on Fiction

The religious revival influenced English prose fiction in several lasting ways.

It strengthened the moral seriousness that characterized the novels of [[Samuel Richardson]], whose *Pamela* and *Clarissa* placed virtue and temptation at the center of their narratives. It helped create a female reading public that expected fiction to uphold rather than undermine religious values. And it contributed to the tradition of the novel as a form of moral inquiry—a vehicle for exploring questions of character, choice, and consequence.

The revival's emphasis on individual spiritual experience also paralleled the novel's growing interest in the inner lives of ordinary people. Both movements—religious and literary—valued personal testimony, emotional authenticity, and the drama of moral decision.
