# William Makepeace Thackeray

William Makepeace Thackeray (1811–1863) stands with [[charles-dickens|Charles Dickens]]
and [[george-eliot|George Eliot]] as one of the three giants of Victorian fiction.
His work is distinguished by its satirical edge, penetrating moral commentary,
and deep sympathy for human frailty.

Tuckerman groups him among the novelists who defined the moral vision
of the nineteenth-century [[england|English novel]] (040-028).

## Life

Born in Calcutta, India, in 1811, Thackeray was sent to [[england|England]] for his education
after his father's death. He attended Charterhouse and Trinity College, Cambridge,
though he left without a degree. The loss of his inherited fortune forced him to earn his living by his pen.

An anecdote recorded by Tuckerman captures the early struggle:
Macaulay once told Thackeray that during a hot season in India
he passed the time reading [[Henry Fielding|Fielding]]'s novels (029-007).
This locates Thackeray within the lineage of eighteenth-century comic fiction
he would come to transform.

Thackeray's career began in journalism, contributing to *Fraser's Magazine* and *Punch*.
He later edited the *Cornhill Magazine*, where he published "Nil Nisi Bonum,"
a reflective tribute to the eighteenth-century novelists he admired (032-007).

## Literary Significance

Thackeray's declared "expressed object" was a fundamentally moral one:
to expose vanity and hypocrisy while celebrating goodness wherever it appeared (041-002).
His fiction is characterized by a gift, "in common with [[charles-dickens|Dickens]],"
for making very ordinary people and events compelling through the alchemy of style (042-016).

Among his contemporaries, Thackeray alone was said to dispute the pre-eminence
of [[jane-austen|Jane Austen]], whom Tuckerman rates as unsurpassed by any novelist (042-012).
Yet an admirer of Thackeray and an admirer of Zola would find themselves in sharp disagreement,
so different were their conceptions of what fiction ought to be (041-003).

Thackeray considered [[Henry Fielding|Fielding]]'s Amelia "the most charming" heroine
in English fiction (030-007), revealing his deep debt to the eighteenth-century comic tradition.
His own heroines both inherit and subvert that tradition,
combining comic breadth with Victorian moral seriousness.

## Major Works

- **Vanity Fair** (1847–1848) — "A [[novel|Novel]] Without a Hero,"
  a panoramic satire of social climbing in Regency [[england|England]].
- **Pendennis** (1848–1850) — A semi-autobiographical *Bildungsroman*.
- **Henry Esmond** (1852) — A historical novel set in the reign of Queen Anne.
- **The Newcomes** (1853–1855) — A multi-generational family saga.
