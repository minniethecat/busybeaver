# Henry Mackenzie

## Life

Henry Mackenzie (1745–1831) was born in Edinburgh, the son of a physician. Educated at the University of Edinburgh, he trained for the law and became a solicitor, eventually rising to the position of Comptroller of Taxes for [[scotland|Scotland]]. His literary career was pursued alongside his legal profession, and he became a central figure in the Edinburgh literary scene. He was the editor of *The Mirror* and *The Lounger*, two influential periodicals, and a prominent member of the Edinburgh literati.

## Literary Significance

"The novels of Henry Mackenzie have a charm of their own, which may be largely attributed to the fact that their author was a gentleman" (035-012). This observation by Tuckerman points to a distinctive quality of Mackenzie's fiction: a refinement of tone and elevation of sentiment that set it apart from much [[the-eighteenth-century-novel|eighteenth-century fiction]]. As Tuckerman notes, "whoever has read, to any extent, the works of fiction of the eighteenth century, must have observed how perpetually he was kept in low company, how rarely he met with a character who had the instincts as well as the social position of a gentleman" (035-013). In *The Man of Feeling*, the hero Harley is "a sensitive, retiring man, with feelings too fine for his surroundings. The author places him in various scenes, and traces the effect which each produces upon his character" (035-013). Mackenzie's aim "was less to weave a complicated plot, than to study and move the heart; and to the lover of sentiment his novels may still be attractive" (036-001). His second novel, *The Man of the World*, presented the counterpart of Harley—Sir Thomas Sindall, a villain of "ceaseless persistency" who "labors... to seduce a single woman" with "a resolution which years do not diminish" (036-001). In *Julia de Roubigné*, Mackenzie illustrated the idea that "in actual life calamities were more often brought about by the innocent errors of the sufferers" than by the designing villainy of others (036-001). Sir [[walter-scott|Walter Scott]] "ranked this novel among the 'most heart-wringing histories' that ever were written" (036-001). Tuckerman also observes that, in contrast to Goldsmith, "Mackenzie does not think of visiting any legal retribution on his 'Man of the World'" (033-012), a feature characteristic of [[the-eighteenth-century-novel|eighteenth-century fiction]]'s treatment of crime and villainy.

## Major Works

- **The Man of Feeling** (1771) — Mackenzie's most celebrated work. "A tone of refined sentiment and dignity pervades 'The Man of Feeling,' which recalls the 'Vicar of Wakefield,' and introduces the reader to better company and more elevated thoughts than the novels of the time usually afford" (035-013). The narrative is "hardly a narrative" in the conventional sense, following the sensitive Harley through various scenes that test his delicate sensibilities; "the early death of Harley completes the delineation of a man too gentle and too sensitive to battle with life" (035-013).
- **The Man of the World** (1773) — A deliberate contrast to *The Man of Feeling*, depicting Sir Thomas Sindall as a villain who pursues his objects with "ceaseless persistency" and "without any apparent passion" (036-001).
- **Julia de Roubigné** (1777) — An epistolary novel exploring the tragic consequences of innocent indiscretion; Scott ranked it among the "most heart-wringing histories" ever written (036-001).
