## Life

Clara Reeve was born in Ipswich, [[england|England]], in 1729, the daughter of a clergyman. She lived a quiet, provincial life and began her literary career relatively late. Her most significant contributions to English fiction came during the late eighteenth century, when the Gothic and romantic revival was gaining momentum in English literature.

## Literary Significance

Clara Reeve took a chief part in originating and sustaining the romantic revival in English fiction, alongside [[Horace Walpole]] and [[Ann Radcliffe]] (038-009). Along with the celebrated [[ann-radcliffe|Mrs. Radcliffe]], she did much to sustain the prominent position which women were taking in fictitious composition (038-005).

Reeve's contribution to the Gothic tradition was distinctive. She hoped to attain success in the attempt to unite the [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]] and the novel by limiting all supernatural occurrences to the verge of probability (039-006). But to the average mind of the present day, Clara Reeve's ghost is not less improbable and incredible than Walpole's gigantic helmet (039-009). Miss Reeve introduced far less of the incredible than her predecessor, but she did not approach Walpole in the adaptation of her scenes to supernatural effects (039-009).

Reeve's sense of congruity was shocked by the strong contrast between the usual and the extraordinary found in Walpole's works, and she therefore limited herself to a single supernatural effect, which might inspire fear while yet remaining within the bounds of superstitious credulity (039-010). Her works, alongside those of Walpole and particularly of [[ann-radcliffe|Mrs. Radcliffe]], contain very decided merits and made a school which has found many admirers (039-015).

Reeve also made an important scholarly contribution with her work *The Progress of Romance* (1785), which catalogued many now-forgotten novels and provided an early history of prose fiction (038-008).

## Major Works

- **The Old English Baron** (1778) — Originally published as *The Champion of Virtue*, this [[gothic-fiction|Gothic novel]] was Reeve's most famous work, written in response to Walpole's *The Castle of Otranto* with a more restrained approach to the supernatural.
- **The Progress of Romance** (1785) — A critical dialogue surveying the history of prose fiction from ancient times to the eighteenth century; an important early work of literary history that documented many now-forgotten novels.
