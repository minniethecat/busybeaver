# Philip Sidney

Sir Philip Sidney (1554–1586) was the embodiment of the Elizabethan ideal:
courtier, soldier, poet, and prose [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]]r.
His posthumously published *Arcadia* stands as one of the most important works
of English prose fiction before the eighteenth-century [[novel]].

Tuckerman describes Sidney's [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]] as occupying a permanent place
in the history of English literature (015-004),
noting that "the same enthusiastic praise" continued to surround his name
long after his early death (013-012).

## Life

Sidney was born at Penshurst Place, Kent, into a family deeply connected to the Elizabethan court.
His education at Shrewsbury School and Christ Church, [[oxford|Oxford]],
equipped him with the classical and Continental learning that would inform his literary work.

At court, Sidney was a figure of exceptional promise.
Tuckerman records that "Master Philip Sidney" was among the select group
who gathered around the Earl of Arundel to form a literary academy (011-002).
Raleigh and Sidney, Tuckerman notes, were at once among "courtiers, soldiers, and thinkers" (010-004).

Sidney's life was cut short at thirty-one, when he died of a wound received at the Battle of Zutphen.
The combination of "patriotism, the accomplishments of a scholar, and gallantry in battle,
combined with Sidney's genial disposition, to win him" the enduring admiration of his countrymen (013-014).

## Literary Significance

Sidney's *Arcadia* is the supreme example of the pastoral [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]] in English.
Though described by one contemporary as a "vain amatorious poem" (014-004),
the work has endured because of its prose qualities.

The plot turns on the adventures of two princes who disguise themselves to gain access
to the Arcadian princesses they love (014-001).
Sidney's delineation of character and scene is rendered in
"language unsurpassed for delicacy and accuracy" (014-003).
One passage describes a forest where "the trees more happily described than by Sidney's pen" (014-002).

The *Arcadia* bridges the medieval [[romance-of-chivalry|romance tradition]]
and the [[novel|modern novel]]'s concern with psychological states (008-011).
It was widely read throughout the seventeenth century and influenced the development
of English prose style for generations.

## Major Works

- **Arcadia** (1590, revised 1593) — A pastoral prose [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]] interwoven with verse.
- **Astrophel and Stella** (1591) — Sonnet sequence that inaugurated the English tradition.
- **The Defence of Poesy** (1595) — The first major work of English literary criticism.
