## Life

William Godwin was born in Wisbech, Cambridgeshire, in 1756. He was trained as a Dissenting minister but abandoned religious faith for rationalist philosophy. Godwin became a central figure in radical [[london|London]] intellectual circles in the 1790s. He married the feminist writer Mary Wollstonecraft in 1797; she died shortly after giving birth to their daughter, Mary Godwin (later [[mary-shelley|Mary Shelley]], author of *Frankenstein*). Godwin's radical political views and unconventional personal life made him both influential and controversial.

## Literary Significance

The novelist most deeply embued with the doctrines of the French Revolution was William Godwin—a man of great literary ambition, and less literary capacity (036-010). His *Life of Chaucer* has the merits of a compilation, but not those of an original literary work. His political and social writings were merely reproductions of French revolutionary views, and were entirely discredited by Malthus' attacks upon them (036-010). The same lack of originality and of independent power characterized Godwin's novels, which all have a patch-work effect, and in all may be found the traces of imitation (036-010).

His most notable novel, *Caleb Williams* (1794), was conceived with grand ambitions. Godwin tells us that, when thinking over *Caleb Williams*, he said to himself a thousand times: "I will write a tale, that shall constitute an epoch in the mind of the reader, that no one, after he has read it, shall ever be exactly the same man that he was before" (037-002). The main idea of the story is certainly a striking one, but it is feebly carried out. The constitution of society cannot be effectively attacked by so improbable and exceptional an illustration of tyranny as the persecution of Caleb Williams (037-002).

Among Godwin's other novels, *St. Leon* and *Mandeville* are dull attempts in the direction of the historical novel. *Fleetwood, or the New Man of Feeling* embodies some of the author's social views, and contains evidence of an imitation of Fielding and Smollett, in which only their coarseness is successfully copied (036-010).

## Major Works

- **An Enquiry Concerning Political Justice** (1793) — Godwin's most influential work of political philosophy, arguing for rational anarchism and the perfectibility of human society.
- **Caleb Williams** (1794) — Godwin's most famous novel; a psychological thriller that dramatizes the tyranny of the powerful over the powerless, widely considered the first English crime novel.
- **St. Leon** (1799) — A historical novel blending Gothic elements with Godwin's philosophical preoccupations.
- **Fleetwood, or the New Man of Feeling** (1805) — A novel of sentiment and social criticism, imitative of Fielding and Smollett.
