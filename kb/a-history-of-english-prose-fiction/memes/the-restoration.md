**The Restoration** (1660–1688) marked a decisive turning point in English literary history.

The return of Charles II ended the Puritan Commonwealth and reopened the theaters. This transformed the cultural landscape of [[England]] and inaugurated a new spirit of rational inquiry. Tuckerman traces a continuous tendency "since the Restoration" toward the modern [[Novel]] (016-011).

## Overview

The Restoration of the monarchy in 1660 brought an end to the austere cultural regime of the Puritan Interregnum.

The reopening of the theaters and relaxation of censorship created new literary opportunities. But the period was also marked by a prevailing spirit of licentiousness. The drama of the Restoration reflected the same tendencies visible in contemporary fiction (017-013).

## Historical Context

The Restoration was a reaction against the severity of Puritan rule, but it overshot into its own excesses.

The court of Charles II set a tone of cynicism that permeated literary culture. The idea that vice was associated with wit shaped fiction for generations (027-001).

The moral reaction against Restoration licentiousness would eventually fuel the [[Religious Revival]] of the eighteenth century. French literary models from [[France]] exerted a powerful influence on English taste during this period.

## Key Figures

The Restoration period witnessed the work of important transitional figures in prose fiction:

| Figure | Role | Significance |
|--------|------|-------------|
| [[aphra-behn|Aphra Behn]] | [[novel|Novel]]ist and playwright | One of the first English women to earn a living by writing |
| [[john-bunyan|John Bunyan]] | Allegorist | *Pilgrim's Progress* gave prose fiction new resources |
| Samuel Pepys | Diarist | Demonstrated the narrative potential of observation |

Behn's novels "were as currently upon the tables of the great as the works of the best-known writers" (019-011).

## Impact on Fiction

The Restoration's most important legacy for prose fiction was the shift from [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]] to realism.

History shows a continuous tendency since the Restoration toward the representation of ordinary life (016-011). The displacement of heroic [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]] by narratives grounded in contemporary society defined the period (018-006).

The moral reaction gave the early novel its strong ethical purpose. The morning of the eighteenth century was "to the period of the Restoration like the morning after a debauch" (024-010).
