## Life

Nathaniel Hawthorne was born in Salem, Massachusetts, in 1804, a descendant of Puritan settlers including one of the judges of the Salem witch trials. He attended Bowdoin College alongside [[Henry Wadsworth Longfellow]] and future president Franklin Pierce. After a period of secluded literary apprenticeship, he published *Twice-Told Tales* (1837) and later served as surveyor of the Salem Custom House. His greatest novels appeared between 1850 and 1852, during a remarkable creative period. He died in 1864 while traveling with Pierce.

## Literary Significance

At the head of New [[england|England]], and, indeed, of [[america|American]] writers of fiction, stands Nathaniel Hawthorne. His three great works, "The Scarlet Letter," "The House of the Seven Gables," and "The Blithedale Romance," are the finest specimens of imaginative writing which [[america|American]] genius has yet produced (045-004).

The interest of Hawthorne's novels lies almost entirely in their subtle and astute studies of the hidden workings of the human mind. His fictions are remarkable for their want of action: "The Scarlet Letter" can hardly be said to have a plot. But narrative excitement was never Hawthorne's object, and the want of it is never felt by his reader. Each scene is an appropriate sequel to the last, and a natural introduction to the next. Each chapter has its special interest — the analysis of a condition of mind, a dramatic situation, or a highly finished domestic picture (045-004).

It is in the delineation of character and the study of human motives that Hawthorne's chief excellence as a novelist consists. Nothing can exceed the penetration and vividness with which such persons as Zenobia, in "The Blithedale Romance," and Holgrave, in "The House of the Seven Gables," are described. The homeward walk of the fallen young minister, in "The Scarlet Letter," when he had resolved to desert his flock and to connect himself again with Hester Prynne, is an unsurpassed delineation of sudden moral degeneration (045-004).

There is nothing of modern realism in Hawthorne's novels, and yet they leave a realistic impression behind them. The greater number of his characters appear to us rather as representatives of certain mental conditions than as real flesh and blood (045-004). When the reader lays down "The Scarlet Letter" or "The House of the Seven Gables," he insensibly feels himself imbued with the spirit and atmosphere of Puritan New [[england|England]]. Hawthorne was so intensely a New [[england|England]]er in his sympathies, prejudices, and habits of mind, that his writings were always colored by the thought and sentiment of his native land (045-005).

In "The Scarlet Letter," there is little evidence of the use of historical researches, and yet in that volume, colonial life has been made real and actual to us by the very intensity of the author's national feeling (045-005). Hawthorne also reproduced the spirit of Puritan New [[england|England]] in "The Scarlet Letter" (046-005).

## Major Works

- **Twice-Told Tales** (1837) — A collection of short stories that first brought Hawthorne critical notice.
- **The Scarlet Letter** (1850) — His greatest novel, set in Puritan Boston, exploring sin, guilt, and redemption through the story of Hester Prynne; a work that reproduced the spirit of Puritan New [[england|England]] (045-004, 046-005).
- **The House of the Seven Gables** (1851) — A novel of ancestral guilt and redemption, centered on the Pyncheon family of Salem (045-004).
- **The Blithedale Romance** (1852) — Based on Hawthorne's experience at the Brook Farm utopian community, featuring the memorable character Zenobia (045-004).
- **The Marble Faun** (1860) — A [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]] set in [[italy|Italy]], exploring themes of art, sin, and the fall of man.
