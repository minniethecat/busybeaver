## Life

Charlemagne (Charles the Great, 742–814) was King of the Franks from 768 and Emperor of the Romans from 800, ruling over a vast European empire. While his historical achievements were substantial, his significance for English prose fiction lies primarily in the legends and [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]]s that grew up around his name during the [[middle-ages|Middle Ages]]. In the [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]]s of chivalry, the historical Charlemagne was transformed into an idealized figure — a great Christian emperor surrounded by twelve peers or paladins, whose adventures became the subject of countless tales (004-002).

The Charlemagne [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]]s originated in [[france|France]] and formed the body of literature known as the *chansons de geste* (songs of deeds), of which the *Chanson de Roland* is the most famous example. These tales, which celebrated martial glory and feudal loyalty, were transmitted across Europe by minstrels and [[minstrels|troubadours]], eventually influencing English romantic literature.

## Literary Significance

The [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]]s of chivalry sprang to life as a logical production of their times. Their authors seized on the character of a king and a warrior — their highest conception of greatness, in the persons of Charlemagne and Arthur. Regardless of anachronism, they represented their heroes as the centre of a chivalric court, accoutred in the arms and practising the customs of later centuries; they created, in fact, a new Charlemagne adapted to the new times (004-002).

The [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]]s of chivalry fall naturally into three general classes: those relating to Charlemagne and his peers; those relating to classical and mythological heroes; and, finally, the tales connected with King Arthur (004-005). The Charlemagne cycle thus represents one of the three foundational pillars of medieval romantic fiction. While the Arthurian legends proved more influential in English literary history — being brought to their culmination in [[Thomas Malory]]'s "Morte d'Arthur" — the Charlemagne [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]]s contributed significantly to the development of the chivalric ideal that pervaded European literature for centuries.

The mass of fiction floating in the imaginations of medieval men — dark fancies from the North, gorgeous Eastern imagery from Arabia, and faded memories of classical mythology — was gathered up by the [[romance-of-chivalry|medieval romance]]rs, who interwove it with the traditions of Arthur and Charlemagne to produce those strange compositions which are so entirely the product of the habits, superstitions, and sympathies of the [[middle-ages|Middle Ages]] (001-021).

In later periods, the ideal characters of Charlemagne and Arthur continued to be admired as embodiments of the qualities most valued in a martial age — a theme that persisted even as the [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]] form evolved through the heroic [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]] of the seventeenth century into the [[novel|modern novel]] (018-001).

## Major Works (Associated Romances)

- **_Chanson de Roland_** (c. 1100) — The preeminent French epic of the Charlemagne cycle, recounting the heroism of Roland at the Battle of Roncevaux Pass. Though a French work, its influence extended to English literature through translations and adaptations.
- **_Orlando Furioso_** (1516, Ariosto) — [[italy|Italian]] epic continuing the Charlemagne [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]] tradition, widely read and translated in [[england|England]].
- **_Orlando Innamorato_** (1495, Boiardo) — [[italy|Italian]] precursor to Ariosto's work, further developing the Charlemagne legends.
