# Ireland

## Overview

Ireland occupies a unique position in English prose fiction: politically part of the United Kingdom after 1800, yet culturally and linguistically distinct. In [[maria-edgeworth|Maria Edgeworth]]'s works, Ireland found a true exposition of her national character and customs. (042-003) The [[irish-fiction|Irish novel]] tradition that followed gave English literature some of its most distinctive regional fiction.

## Literary Significance

The [[irish-fiction|Irish novel]] emerged as a distinct form in the early nineteenth century, inaugurated by [[maria-edgeworth|Maria Edgeworth]]'s *Castle Rackrent* (1800). Unlike the [[novel|English novel]] of manners, Irish fiction was marked by political consciousness, oral storytelling traditions, and a characteristic blend of humor and pathos. The peculiarities of language of Ireland, along with its troubled history, gave Irish fiction a distinctive voice. (040-024)

After Edgeworth came the Banim brothers with their *Tales of the O'Hara Family*, [[william-carleton|William Carleton]] with his unparalleled knowledge of Irish peasant life, and the more popular novelists [[samuel-lover|Samuel Lover]] and [[charles-lever|Charles Lever]], who presented a lighter, more comic vision of Irish character.

## Key Locations

- **Dublin**: The political and cultural capital, setting for much [[irish-fiction|Anglo-Irish fiction]]
- **The Irish countryside**: Rural Ireland as depicted by Carleton and the Banims
- **Connaught**: Wild western Ireland, associated with the more romantic Irish tales

## Role in Fiction

Ireland served English fiction as a source of exotic subject matter close to home: a land of eloquence, passion, poverty, and political unrest that could be rendered in English while retaining its foreign character. Irish fiction mediated between English readers and Irish reality, sometimes reinforcing stereotypes and sometimes challenging them.
