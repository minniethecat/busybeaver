**Satire** was one of the most powerful literary modes in eighteenth-century English prose fiction.

It was wielded with devastating effect by masters such as [[Jonathan Swift]] and [[Henry Fielding]]. Unlike the straightforward realism of the emerging [[Novel]], satire exposed the vices and follies of society through indirection. The satirical impulse was inseparable from the moral ferment of post-[[The Restoration]] [[England]] (021-006).

## Definition

Satire is a mode of writing that seeks to reform or ridicule through wit, irony, and exaggeration.

In prose fiction, it holds up a distorted mirror to society. The satirist's tools include parody, burlesque, and fantastical allegorical worlds. Tuckerman notes that [[jonathan-swift|Swift]]'s general satirical object stays constant while its application varies with circumstances (026-001).

## Historical Context

Satire's prominence in eighteenth-century English fiction had roots in the political conflicts of the previous century.

After the Restoration, the collapse of censorship and the rise of party politics created a public sphere where satirical writing could flourish. The periodical essay provided venues for gentler satire aimed at reforming manners. The novel offered a longer narrative form for satirical vision (021-006).

## Key Characteristics

Satire in English prose fiction has these defining features:

| Feature | Description | Example |
|---------|------------|---------|
| Allegorical displacement | Real targets shown through fictional analogues | Lilliput stands for [[england|England]] |
| Structural irony | Gap between stated and actual meaning | [[jonathan-swift|Swift]]'s modest proposer |
| Moral seriousness | Comic surface conceals ethical purpose | Houyhnhnms vs. Yahoos |
| Caricature | Characters embody vices or absurdities | Thackeray's social types |

Tuckerman observes that [[jonathan-swift|Swift]]'s satire "gathered strength and bitterness as it went on" (026-006).

> "Whether read for the satire or the story, the adventures of Gulliver have always retained their popularity" (025-011).

## Notable Examples

The corpus highlights these signal achievements of satire in English fiction:

| Work | Author | Primary Target | Mode |
|------|--------|---------------|------|
| *Gulliver's Travels* | [[jonathan-swift|Jonathan Swift]] | Human nature, politics | Fantasy voyage |
| *Joseph Andrews* | [[henry-fielding|Henry Fielding]] | Social pretension | Comic epic |
| *A Tale of a Tub* | [[jonathan-swift|Jonathan Swift]] | Religious enthusiasm | Layered allegory |
| *Vanity Fair* | [[william-thackeray|William Thackeray]] | Social ambition | Ironic panorama |

[[jonathan-swift|Swift]]'s satire is noted for its comprehensive scope. In the voyage to Laputa, the satire targets the vanity of philosophers (026-005). Thackeray continued the tradition in the nineteenth century (044-005).
