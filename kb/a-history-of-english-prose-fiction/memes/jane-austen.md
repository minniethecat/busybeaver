# Jane Austen

Jane Austen (1775–1817) is, in Tuckerman's account, one of the two most powerful female novelists of the early nineteenth century, linked with [[samuel-richardson|Maria Edgeworth]] as a founding figure of the English domestic novel (042-009). Her works—*Pride and Prejudice*, *Emma*, *Mansfield Park*, *Sense and Sensibility*—established a mode of fiction grounded in the close observation of everyday life among the country gentry.

Tuckerman ranks "the writings of Miss Austen, of [[maria-edgeworth|Miss Edgeworth]], of [[susan-ferrier|Miss Ferrier]]" among "the best works of fiction in any language" (040-026). Her novels, together with those of [[walter-scott|Sir Walter Scott]], [[charles-dickens|Dickens]], [[William Thackeray|Thackeray]], and [[samuel-richardson|George Eliot]], have "afforded to hundreds of thousands, young and old, a never failing source of healthful entertainment" (047-006).

## Life

Austen was born in 1775 in Steventon, Hampshire, where her father served as rector. She lived a quiet, outwardly uneventful life within a close-knit family, never marrying. Her novels were published anonymously—"By a Lady"—beginning with *Sense and Sensibility* in 1811. By the time of her death in 1817 at the age of forty-one, she had published four novels, with *Northanger Abbey* and *Persuasion* appearing posthumously.

Unlike many of her contemporaries, Austen derived no material from travel, adventure, or public life. Her experience was confined to the domestic circle she depicted, yet from this narrow ground she produced fiction of enduring depth.

## Literary Significance

Austen "depended neither on exciting scenes, nor on highly wrought effects of human passion for the interest of her stories, but studied every-day life and ordinary people with a sympathy and power of observation which imparted a deep interest to all her works" (042-009). This commitment to the ordinary—to the moral drama of drawing-rooms and country walks—was her innovation.

Where [[Henry Fielding|Fielding]] gave the broad canvas of the road and the inn, and [[samuel-richardson|Richardson]] the intense interiority of the letter, Austen gave the domestic circle: conversations over tea, the anxieties of inheritance, the slow revelation of character through social intercourse. Tuckerman notes that she described "the country gentry and middle classes of society" (042-009).

Her gift for observation aligns her with the greatest novelists. Tuckerman writes of the faculty "of making very ordinary people interesting"—"the observing sympathy of a Thackeray, a Miss Austen, or a [[george-eliot|George Eliot]] is necessary to light up the unnoticed figures which throng the path" (042-016). The novelist's task, as Austen practiced it, was not to invent extraordinary events but to reveal the significance that already inheres in ordinary life.

Tuckerman traces a lineage of female novelists "from [[maria-edgeworth|Maria Edgeworth]] and Jane Austen to [[george-eliot|George Eliot]], who have contributed to the single department of fiction concerned with English domestic life" (042-018)—fifteen women who, between them, transformed the novel into the dominant literary form of the century.

## Major Works

- **Sense and Sensibility** (1811) — A study of two sisters embodying the rival claims of prudence and passion.
- **Pride and Prejudice** (1813) — Her most celebrated novel; the courtship of Elizabeth Bennet and Mr. Darcy.
- **Mansfield Park** (1814) — The moral education of Fanny Price within a wealthy country household.
- **Emma** (1815) — The comic errors of a heroine "whom no one but myself will much like."
- **Northanger Abbey** (1817, posthumous) — A satire of Gothic fiction and a defense of the novel.
- **Persuasion** (1817, posthumous) — A story of second chances and enduring love.
