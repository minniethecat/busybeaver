# Euphuism

## Definition

Euphuism is an elaborate and highly artificial prose style that flourished in Elizabethan [[england|England]] during the late sixteenth century. Named after [[john-lyly|John Lyly]]'s [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]] *Euphues, the Anatomy of Wit* (1579), the style is characterized by balanced antithetical phrases, extended similes drawn from classical mythology and natural history, alliteration, and a profusion of rhetorical ornament.

## Historical Context

Euphuism emerged during the reign of Elizabeth I, a period when English prose was still developing its literary identity. The [[elizabethan-literature|age of Elizabeth]] "had worked itself free from the intellectual sloth of the [[middle-ages|Middle Ages]]" and was "familiarizing itself with an almost unknown world abroad, and creating a new world at home." (011-001) In this atmosphere of intellectual ferment, style was prized as a mark of cultivation. As Tuckerman notes, "the most striking feature of Elizabethan fiction is the great importance attached to style. The writer cared more to excite admiration by the turn of his phrases and the ornaments of his language, than to interest his reader by plot or incident." (011-004)

[[john-lyly|John Lyly]] published *Euphues* in 1579, and the work "was so much admired at Court that courtiers and ladies studied its pages as the perfection of breeding." (011-006) For many years Lyly "lingered about the court waiting for a place which should reward him for his labors, solacing his disappointment by sanguine hopes," though he died in 1606 unfulfilled. (011-007)

## Key Characteristics

The euphuistic style is distinguished by several defining features:

- **Antithetical balance**: Phrases are arranged in parallel opposition. Lyly's style is "well illustrated by a speech in which Euphues exhorts his friend Philautus to constancy in love." (012-006)

- **Classical and natural allusions**: Euphuists drew heavily on Pliny's natural history and classical mythology for similes and comparisons. These allusions served to display the author's learning and wit.

- **Rhetorical ornamentation**: The style favors elaborate figures of speech over plain narration. Tuckerman observes that beneath the stylistic excess, Lyly's work contained genuine insight: "The deeper meaning of Lyly's work, which lies beneath the artificial trick of style, is a serious attempt to portray the fashionable life of the time." (012-001)

- **Emotional artifice**: As shown in the dialogue between Euphues and Philautus: "Although hereto Euphues, I have shrined thee in my heart for a trustie friende, I will shunne thee hereafter as a friendlesse foe." (012-002)

## Notable Examples

The primary exemplar of euphuism is Lyly's *Euphues, the Anatomy of Wit* (1579) and its sequel *Euphues and his [[england|England]]* (1580). The title itself suggests the analytical, moralizing character of the work — "as is suggested by the title, 'Euphues, The Anatomy of Wit.'" (012-001)

The popularity of *Euphues* "excited much imitation, and for many years traces of its influence appear in all varieties of English prose." (012-006) Shakespeare himself engaged with the fashion, "ridiculing the affectations of contemporary language in 'Love's Labour Lost.'" (012-006 footnote 62) Ben Jonson also portrayed euphuistic characters, as in *Every Man out of his Humour*, where a character declares: "O, Master Brisk, as 'tis said in Euphues, Hard is the choice, when one is compelled, either by silence to die with grief, or by speaking to live with shame." (012-006 footnote 62)

The influence of *Euphues* extended beyond its immediate imitators: "occasionally, the influence of 'Euphues' is manifest" even in later works of the period. (013-004)
