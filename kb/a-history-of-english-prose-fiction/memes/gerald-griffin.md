Gerald Griffin (1803–1840) was an [[irish-fiction|Irish novel]]ist, poet, and playwright. Tuckerman lists him among the notable novelists of [[ireland|Irish]] life and manners, alongside [[lady-morgan|Lady Morgan]], Mrs. S. C. Hall, and others (042-006).

Griffin's most celebrated work is *The Collegians* (1829), a novel based on a real-life murder case in County Limerick. The story follows the tragic consequences of a secret marriage between Hardress Cregan, a young Anglo-[[ireland|Irish]] gentleman, and Eily O'Connor, a poor but beautiful peasant girl. The novel was praised for its vivid depiction of [[ireland|Irish]] rural society and its dramatic, suspenseful narrative.

Beyond *The Collegians*, Griffin wrote several other works including *Tales of the Munster Festivals* (1827) and *The Invasion* (1832). Disillusioned with literary life, Griffin destroyed many of his unpublished manuscripts and joined the Christian Brothers, a Catholic religious order, in 1838. He died of typhus two years later at the age of thirty-six, leaving behind a small but enduring body of work that captures the complexities of [[ireland|Irish]] life in the early nineteenth century.
