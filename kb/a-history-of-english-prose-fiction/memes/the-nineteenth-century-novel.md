# The Nineteenth-Century Novel

## Overview

The novels of the nineteenth century are so numerous and so generally familiar that they form the bulk of what we now consider the [[novel|English novel]] tradition. (001-006) The century witnessed an extraordinary expansion of fiction in both quantity and variety, establishing the novel as the dominant literary form of the age. From [[walter-scott|Walter Scott]]'s historical [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]]s through Dickens's social panoramas, Thackeray's satires, [[george-eliot|George Eliot]]'s psychological realism, and Hardy's tragic vision, the nineteenth-century novel mapped the full range of human experience.

## Historical Context

The nineteenth century inherited the novel form perfected in the eighteenth, but transformed it through new social conditions: industrialization, urbanization, the expansion of the reading public, and the rise of circulating libraries and serial publication. (021-008) The century was marked by rapid material progress, political reform, and intense moral and religious debate. These forces shaped fiction directly, as novelists engaged with the great questions of the age: class, gender, faith, science, and social justice.

The three-volume novel (triple-decker) and serial publication in periodicals created new economic models for fiction writing. Authors like Dickens reached unprecedented audiences through monthly installments, making the novelist a public figure of considerable influence.

## Key Figures

- **Sir [[walter-scott|Walter Scott]]** (1771–1832): Created the historical novel and made [[scotland|Scottish]] life a subject of world literature.
- **[[jane-austen|Jane Austen]]** (1775–1817): Perfected the novel of manners with exquisite irony and moral precision.
- **[[charles-dickens|Charles Dickens]]** (1812–1870): The central figure of Victorian fiction, combining social reform, comedy, and unforgettable characters.
- **[[william-thackeray|William Makepeace Thackeray]]** (1811–1863): Master of social satire and panoramic narrative.
- **[[george-eliot|George Eliot]]** (1819–1880): Brought profound intellectual and psychological depth to the novel.
- **[[anthony-trollope|Anthony Trollope]]** (1815–1882): Chronicled English society with unmatched breadth and consistency.
- **[[thomas-hardy|Thomas Hardy]]** (1840–1928): Brought tragic vision and rural Wessex to the late Victorian novel.

## Impact on Fiction

The nineteenth century expanded the novel's scope enormously:

- **The [[historical-novel|Historical Novel]]**: Scott's *Waverley* (1814) created a new genre that spread across Europe.
- **Social Fiction**: Dickens, Gaskell, and Kingsley used fiction to expose social evils and advocate reform.
- **Psychological Realism**: [[george-eliot|George Eliot]] deepened the novel's capacity for moral and psychological analysis.
- **Regional Fiction**: [[scotland|Scottish]], [[ireland|Irish]], and provincial English traditions flourished alongside the [[london|London]]-centered novel.
- **The Woman [[novel|Novel]]ist**: Austen, the Brontës, Gaskell, and Eliot established women as central figures in literary production.

The century also saw the development of distinct subgenres: the sensation novel, the novel of purpose, and the beginnings of detective and science fiction. (020-002)
