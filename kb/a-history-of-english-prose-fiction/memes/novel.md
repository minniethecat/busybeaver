The **Novel** is the central literary form of modern English prose fiction.

It emerged in the eighteenth century, prioritizing realistic representation over the fantastical conventions of [[romance-of-chivalry|medieval romance]]. The rise of the novel is inseparable from the social transformations of [[England]] after [[The Restoration]] (001-004).

Tracing its development from [[Daniel Defoe]] to the Victorian masters offers a comprehensive view of English literature. The novel grew from experiments in verisimilitude into the dominant genre of modern literary culture.

## Definition

A novel is an extended work of prose fiction that depicts characters and events approximating real life.

Unlike the earlier [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]]—which dealt in marvels and chivalric adventures—the novel grounds its action in recognizable social settings. It explores the inner lives of individuals through narrative techniques that create the illusion of reality.

Tuckerman's history traces the transformation "from the early [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]] to the novel of the present day" (001-004). The novel came to be defined by its commitment to circumstantial particularity and psychological depth.

## Historical Context

The English novel took shape during a period of profound social and intellectual change.

The decline of feudalism, the expansion of the middle class, and the growth of literacy created demand for new narrative forms. Tuckerman notes that Elizabethan [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]] was "to Elizabeth's time what the novel is to our own" (011-003).

The prose [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]]s of the seventeenth century introduced psychological analysis that paved the way (018-006). After the Restoration, rational inquiry created the intellectual climate for the novel's emergence.

## Key Characteristics

| Characteristic | Description |
|---------------|-------------|
| **Realism** | Depiction of ordinary life and plausible events |
| **Character Development** | Focus on individual psychology and moral growth |
| **Prose Medium** | Extended narrative in prose |
| **Social Scope** | Representation of diverse classes and manners |
| **Narrative Coherence** | Unified plot with causal connections |

The realistic novel is distinguished by "its study of human thought and action" (011-004). This emphasis on the particular marks a decisive break with idealized [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]] conventions.

## Notable Examples

The corpus identifies several landmark works and authors.

[[daniel-defoe|Daniel Defoe]] established verisimilitude through concrete detail. "[[daniel-defoe|Defoe]]'s power of giving life to fictitious narrative has never been surpassed" (016-007).

[[samuel-richardson|Samuel Richardson]] introduced sustained psychological exploration in *Pamela* and *Clarissa*, deepening the novel's capacity for interior experience.

[[henry-fielding|Henry Fielding]] developed the realistic novel with a broader comic vision of society (020-012). His *Tom Jones* exemplified narrative unity across a social panorama.

[[walter-scott|Walter Scott]] extended the novel's historical range through antiquarian research and vivid storytelling, making the past accessible to modern imagination.

> "Fielding developed the realistic novel. A prosaic and conventional treatment of life succeeded to the romantic and the marvellous" (020-012).
