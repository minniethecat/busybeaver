# Charles Dickens

Charles Dickens (1812–1870) was the central figure of Victorian fiction and the most widely read [[novel|English novel]]ist of the nineteenth century. Tuckerman records that "the works of no writer in the English language, except those of Scott, have attained so immediate a reputation and have won so wide-spread a popularity as the novels of Charles Dickens" (043-005).

Together with [[William Thackeray|William Makepeace Thackeray]] and [[samuel-richardson|George Eliot]], Dickens defined the [[the-nineteenth-century-novel|Victorian novel]]. He brought to English fiction an unprecedented breadth of character, a fierce social conscience, and an imagination so vivid that his creations entered the common language.

## Life

Dickens was born in Portsmouth in 1812 and experienced the hardships of lower-middle-class life when his father was imprisoned for debt. These early experiences—the blacking factory, the debtor's prison, the scramble for respectability—furnished the material for his most autobiographical novel, *David Copperfield*.

His literary career began with the *Sketches by Boz* (1836), but it was *The Pickwick Papers* (1836–37) that made him, almost overnight, the most celebrated author in [[england|England]]. Tuckerman quotes the *[[london|London]] Quarterly Review* of 1837: "In less than six months from the appearance of the first number of the 'Pickwick Papers,' the whole reading public were talking about them … and Mr. Dickens was the grand object of interest to the whole tribe of 'Leo-hunters,' male and female, of the metropolis" (043-005).

For forty years, Dickens held his place in public esteem "without any sensible diminution" (043-005). He died in 1870, leaving *The Mystery of Edwin Drood* unfinished.

## Literary Significance

Dickens' most remarkable achievement lay in "the number of his creations, and in the distinctness with which he could impress them on the memory of his readers" (043-005). Characters like Micawber ("Something will turn up"), Mark Tapley, Pecksniff, and Fagin remain permanently stamped on the English imagination.

His great object was "to celebrate the virtues of the humbler ranks of life, and to expose the acts of injustice or tyranny to which they are subjected" (043-005). This he did "in a spirit of the truest philanthropy and most universal benevolence" (043-005).

Dickens was, in Tuckerman's assessment, "essentially a national writer in the variety of the characters with whom he brought his readers into communion"—he was "essentially popular, from the fact that he dealt with the masses," and "essentially English, in that he was the apostle of home" (043-008). No novelist before him had so thoroughly caught the spirit of domestic life.

> Dickens hated hypocrisy as Thackeray hated snobbism. (043-005)

His faults—exaggeration and caricature—sprang from "the exuberance and power of his imagination" (043-007). Yet considering "the number and variety of the figures conjured up by his creative mind … it is extraordinary that to so few this criticism will apply" (043-007).

Dickens shares with [[samuel-richardson|George Eliot]] and [[William Thackeray|Thackeray]] the faculty of "making very ordinary people interesting"—a talent "characteristic of the best minds which have contributed to fiction or the drama" (042-016).

## Major Works

- **The Pickwick Papers** (1836–37) — The work that made Dickens famous; a comic picaresque of the Pickwick Club.
- **Oliver Twist** (1837–39) — A novel of purpose exposing the workhouse system and criminal underworld (046-008).
- **Nicholas Nickleby** (1838–39) — An attack on the Yorkshire boarding schools, featuring the brutal Dotheboys Hall (046-008).
- **David Copperfield** (1849–50) — Dickens' most autobiographical novel.
- **Bleak House** (1852–53) — A sustained satire on the Court of Chancery.
- **A Tale of Two Cities** (1859) — Historical novel of the French Revolution, praised for its "never-failing power" (046-001).
- **Barnaby Rudge** (1841) — Historical novel set during the Gordon Riots (046-001).
