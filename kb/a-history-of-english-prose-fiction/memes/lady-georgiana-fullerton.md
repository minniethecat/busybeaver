Lady Georgiana Fullerton (1812–1885), born Georgiana Charlotte Leveson-Gower, was an [[novel|English novel]]ist and philanthropist. Tuckerman lists her among the women who contributed to the English domestic novel, alongside [[mary-russell-mitford|Mary K. Mitford]], Mrs. Crowe, Mrs. Marsh, and others (044-013). Her name also appears in the index to the volume (048-001).

Lady Georgiana was the daughter of a prominent political family—her father, Lord Granville Leveson-Gower, was a diplomat and statesman. In 1833 she married Alexander Fullerton, a former officer in the British army who later became a country gentleman. The Fullertons converted to Roman Catholicism in 1846, a decision that profoundly influenced Lady Georgiana's literary career.

Her novels include *Ellen Middleton* (1844), *Grantley Manor* (1847), and *Too Strange Not to Be True* (1864). *Grantley Manor*, published after her conversion, was notable for its sympathetic treatment of Catholicism at a time when anti-Catholic sentiment was strong in [[england|England]]. Many of her later works, such as *Constance Sherwood* (1865), were historical novels with Catholic themes. Lady Georgiana also devoted herself to charitable work with the poor, combining her literary activity with active religious philanthropy.
