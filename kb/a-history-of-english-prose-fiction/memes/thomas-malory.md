## Life

Sir Thomas Malory was a [[london|London]] knight of the fifteenth century who was well read in chivalric literature (004-006). Little is definitively known about his life, though he is generally identified with Sir Thomas Malory of Newbold Revel in Warwickshire, who served in the French wars under the Earl of Warwick and was imprisoned at various times. It was during a period of imprisonment that Malory is believed to have completed his great work, the "Morte d'Arthur."

## Literary Significance

By the middle of the fifteenth century, a vast body of Arthurian [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]] had accumulated, including the [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]]s of Merlin the Enchanter, of Launcelot du Lac, of Tristram, of Perceval le Gallois, and the Saint Gréal. Sir Thomas Malory combined these tales in the volume he called the "Morte d'Arthur," an excellent specimen of a [[romance-of-chivalry|chivalric romance]], which was printed by Caxton in 1485, and has since appeared in many editions down to the present day (004-006).

The literary form of the "Morte d'Arthur" is characterized by a noble and forcible simplicity of expression that pervades the old Norman French in which the [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]]s of chivalry were first written, a quality well reflected in the English of Sir Thomas Malory (006-012). Of plot there is none; the same vagueness pervades the course of the narrative that is characteristic of the historical groundwork, the geography, and the time of action. Most of the incidents depend on chance (006-012).

Malory's work represents the culmination and summation of the medieval [[romance-of-chivalry|chivalric romance]] tradition. The authors of these [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]]s never attempted delineation of character — their heroes are simply good knights or bad knights, possessing only the particular qualities that would place them in one of these categories (006-012). However, Malory's prose achieved a dignity and pathos that elevated him above his sources. The famous passage on the death of Arthur captures the noble simplicity of his style: "And thou were the goodelyest persone that euer came among prees of knyghtes. And thou were the mekest man & the gentylest that euer ete in halle amonge ladyes. And thou were the sternest knyghte to thy mortall foo that euer put spere in the reyst" (006-012).

The "Morte d'Arthur" holds a unique position as the bridge between the [[romance-of-chivalry|medieval romance]] tradition and the later development of English prose fiction. After Malory, the [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]] of chivalry declined — killed not by [[Miguel de Cervantes]]'s "Don Quixote," as is sometimes supposed, but by the natural exhaustion of its materials and the changing taste of readers.

## Major Works

- **Le Morte d'Arthur** (completed c. 1470, printed 1485) — The definitive English prose compilation of Arthurian legends, combining the major French and English [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]] cycles into a single coherent narrative from Arthur's birth to his death. Printed by William Caxton, it has remained continuously in print and has inspired countless adaptations and retellings (004-006, 006-012).
