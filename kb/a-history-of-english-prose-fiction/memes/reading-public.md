# Reading Public

## Definition

The reading public refers to the collective audience for printed fiction, whose expansion in size, diversity, and sophistication was a decisive force in the development of the [[novel|English novel]]. As literacy spread, printing became cheaper, and leisure time increased, the composition of the reading public changed — and with it, the character of the fiction written to satisfy it. Tuckerman describes the reading public as the ultimate arbiter of literary success: a novel's ability to sustain "a place in the favor of the reading public" was the measure of its achievement. (040-028)

## Historical Context

The growth of the reading public in eighteenth-century [[england|England]] was fueled by rising literacy rates, the expansion of the middle class, and the establishment of circulating libraries that made books affordable to a broader audience. The novel, as a form, was uniquely positioned to serve this new readership. Unlike poetry or drama, it required no special education to enjoy and addressed the concerns of ordinary life.

By the early nineteenth century, the reading public had become a powerful force shaping literary production. The sheer scale of the audience was unprecedented. Tuckerman notes that "the lives and thoughts of the reading public" became so central to literary production that they demanded systematic treatment. (040-020) The public's tastes and expectations directly influenced what authors wrote and publishers printed.

The Victorian period saw the reading public grow even larger and more diverse. "The whole reading public were talking about them," Tuckerman writes of the sensation caused by certain popular novels in 1837, capturing the intensity of the relationship between fiction and its audience. (043-005) The reading public had become both more complex and more demanding: "the lives of men and women had become far more complex and the reading public far more exacting, varied, and critical." (040-022)

## Key Characteristics

- **Expanding scale**: The reading public grew from a small elite in the seventeenth century to a mass audience by the mid-nineteenth century.
- **Social diversity**: The audience expanded beyond the aristocracy to include the middle classes, women, and eventually working-class readers.
- **Market influence**: The tastes and purchasing power of the reading public directly shaped what was published. Popular demand could elevate a novelist to fame or consign them to obscurity.
- **Segmentation**: By the nineteenth century, the reading public had segmented into distinct audiences with different tastes — the "lower tastes of the reading public" were catered to by sensational fiction, while a more refined audience sought literary quality. (047-002)

Tuckerman observes that the power of the reading public extended beyond mere sales. The "minds of the reading public" were shaped by what they read, and in turn, "was attracted by qualities" that no earlier age would have valued. (043-008) This reciprocal relationship — the public shaping fiction, and fiction shaping the public — was one of the distinctive features of the modern literary marketplace.

## Notable Examples

The power of the reading public is illustrated by the career of Sir [[walter-scott|Walter Scott]], whose Waverley [[novel|Novel]]s achieved unprecedented popularity across all social classes. Tuckerman notes the scale of Scott's success: "thirteen different translations were issued in Germany, four in [[france|France]]," demonstrating that the reading public was now international. (045-008)

[[charles-dickens|Charles Dickens]]'s relationship with his audience exemplified the Victorian reading public at its most engaged. The serial publication of his novels created a shared reading experience across the nation. As Tuckerman notes of the sensation caused by *Pickwick Papers*: "the whole reading public were talking about them." (043-005)

The phenomenon also had its critics. Some authors "urged in their own defense" that they merely catered to "the lower tastes of the reading public," disclaiming responsibility for the moral quality of their work. (047-002) This tension between artistic integrity and popular appeal was a defining feature of the nineteenth-century literary marketplace.
