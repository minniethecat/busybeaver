# Translation in Literature

## Definition

Translation in literature refers to the practice of rendering foreign literary works into English, a process that served as one of the primary channels through which continental literary forms, styles, and ideas entered the development of English prose fiction. From the [[middle-ages|medieval period]] through the nineteenth century, translations introduced English readers and writers to works they could not otherwise access, shaping native literary production in fundamental ways.

## Historical Context

The role of translation in English literary history begins with the [[middle-ages|medieval period]]. The [[romance-of-chivalry|chivalric romance]]s that formed the earliest stratum of English prose fiction were largely translations and adaptations of French originals. "Although many imitations and translations appeared in [[england|England]] for more than a century," Tuckerman writes, English fiction only gradually developed its own distinctive character. (004-006)

The Elizabethan period marked a high point of translation activity. "Translations without number of the classical and [[italy|Italian]] authors appeared," as [[england|England]] eagerly absorbed the literature of the continental Renaissance. (011-003) "The volumes of translations issued in Elizabeth's time" exposed English writers to a vast range of models — from [[italy|Italian]] novelle to classical histories — that would shape the development of English prose style and narrative technique. (015-010)

Translation continued to serve as a conduit for literary innovation throughout the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries. Individual authors contributed to the practice: [[aphra-behn|Aphra Behn]]'s "literary productions include a translation of Fontenelle's 'Plurality of Worlds.'" (018-017) Chaucer, in his earlier work, produced a "translation of the 'Romaunt of the Rose'" that demonstrated the close relationship between translation and original creation in the [[middle-ages|medieval period]]. (007-003)

By the nineteenth century, the flow had reversed: [[novel|English novel]]s were now being translated into other languages on an unprecedented scale. "When we consider the number of translations issued in Europe and the editions they ran through," Tuckerman observes, the international reach of the [[novel|English novel]] becomes apparent. (041-014) [[walter-scott|Walter Scott]]'s works appeared in "thirteen different translations in Germany, four in [[france|France]]." (045-008) Cooper's novels similarly found "in almost every European land, translations" that spread [[america|American]] fiction across the continent. (045-001)

## Key Characteristics

- **Cross-cultural transmission**: Translation imports foreign literary forms, techniques, and ideas into the native tradition.
- **Adaptation as well as rendering**: Early translations often freely adapted their sources rather than producing literal renderings, blending translation with original creation.
- **Market expansion**: By the nineteenth century, translations of [[novel|English novel]]s into European languages represented a major publishing industry and a sign of English fiction's international dominance.
- **Bidirectional flow**: Translation both imported foreign models into English (medieval–Renaissance) and exported English models abroad (nineteenth century).

## Notable Examples

The medieval *Morte d'Arthur* tradition itself was deeply shaped by translation from French sources. The [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]]s of chivalry that "delighted the chivalric reader of the [[middle-ages|Middle Ages]]" were largely French in origin, entering English through translation and adaptation. (004-002)

In the Elizabethan period, the translation of [[italy|Italian]] *novelle* provided models for the short prose fiction that flourished in [[england|England]]. Classical translations — of Heliodorus, Apuleius, and others — offered alternative narrative paradigms to the native [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]] tradition.

The nineteenth century saw the phenomenon of the [[novel|English novel]] conquering Europe through translation. Scott's Waverley [[novel|Novel]]s appeared across the continent; "thirteen different translations were issued in Germany, four in [[france|France]]." (045-008) This international circulation marked the emergence of English fiction as a global literary force.
