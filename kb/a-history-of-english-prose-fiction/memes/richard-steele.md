## Life

Richard Steele was born in Dublin in 1672 and educated at Charterhouse School and Merton College, [[oxford|Oxford]]. He left [[oxford|Oxford]] without a degree to pursue a military career, eventually rising to the rank of captain. His early literary efforts included several comedies for the stage, but it was in periodical journalism that he found his true vocation.

In 1709 Steele founded *The Tatler*, a tri-weekly periodical that combined news, social commentary, and moral essays. He was soon joined by his friend [[Joseph Addison]], and together they transformed English periodical writing. After *The Tatler* ceased in 1711, the two men launched *The Spectator*, which became one of the most influential periodicals of the eighteenth century. Steele served as a Member of Parliament and was knighted in 1715. He died in 1729.

## Literary Significance

Steele, along with Addison and [[Samuel Johnson]], made the essay one of the most attractive and popular forms of literature in the eighteenth century (020-012). His prose style was conversational and warm, contrasting with the more formal manner of Addison. Steele brought a humane and sympathetic tone to periodical writing, focusing on domestic life, manners, and moral improvement.

Steele's influence extended beyond journalism into the development of English prose fiction. The character sketches and narrative vignettes that appeared in *The Tatler* and *The Spectator* — including the famous Sir Roger de Coverley papers — helped establish techniques of characterization and social observation that later novelists would adopt.

[[philip-sidney|Sir Philip Sidney]]'s description of an envious man in the second book of the *Arcadia* suggested to Sir Richard Steele his essay in the nineteenth number of the *Spectator* (014-004), demonstrating Steele's engagement with earlier English prose writers as a source of inspiration.

Steele's judgment of literary matters was acute. Of the novelist [[Aphra Behn]], Steele observed that she "understood the practic part of love better than the speculative" (019-009), a succinct assessment that captures both Behn's strengths and the moral concerns of Steele's own era.

## Major Works

- **The Tatler** (1709–1711) — Tri-weekly periodical founded by Steele, covering news, society, and moral commentary.
- **The Spectator** (1711–1712, with [[joseph-addison|Joseph Addison]]) — Daily periodical featuring the Sir Roger de Coverley papers and essays on literature and manners.
- **The Conscious Lovers** (1722) — A sentimental comedy that exemplified Steele's moral approach to drama.
- **The Christian Hero** (1701) — A moral treatise urging virtue as the foundation of heroic action.
- **The Guardian** (1713) — A short-lived periodical continuing in the Addison-Steele tradition.
