# The Eighteenth-Century Novel

## Overview

The eighteenth century was the period of the growth and perfection of English fiction. Beginning with the works of [[daniel-defoe|Defoe]] and [[jonathan-swift|Swift]], the novel developed through Richardson, Fielding, Smollett, and Sterne into a mature literary form. By the end of the century, fiction had become the dominant literary genre in [[england|England]], absorbing an increasing share of literary talent and readership.

The period saw fiction evolve from episodic adventure narratives and moral allegories into psychologically complex studies of character and society. (020-008) The novel became, in Tuckerman's phrase, "the exponent" of national development, reflecting and shaping the social and moral character of the age.

## Historical Context

The eighteenth century in [[england|England]] was an era of reforms, marked by the progressive dismantling of abuses and corruption inherited from previous centuries. (020-009) Population and wealth had outgrown existing laws and customs. While the earlier part of the period exhibited corruption in public and private life, indifference in religion, inadequate education, and coarseness throughout the social system, a solid foundation of good sense and an earnest desire for improvement gradually introduced one reform after another.

The reading public expanded dramatically during this period. Circulating libraries, coffee-houses, and an increasingly literate middle class created a new market for prose fiction. (024-010) The novel, unlike poetry or drama, appealed directly to this growing audience with stories of contemporary life.

## Key Figures

- **[[daniel-defoe|Daniel Defoe]]** (c. 1660–1731): Established the realistic narrative tradition with *Robinson Crusoe* (1719) and *Moll Flanders* (1722).
- **[[jonathan-swift|Jonathan Swift]]** (1667–1745): Brought satirical power to prose narrative with *Gulliver's Travels* (1726).
- **[[samuel-richardson|Samuel Richardson]]** (1689–1761): Pioneered the epistolary novel and psychological realism with *Pamela* (1740) and *Clarissa* (1748).
- **[[henry-fielding|Henry Fielding]]** (1707–1754): Developed the comic epic in prose with *Joseph Andrews* (1742) and *Tom Jones* (1749).
- **[[tobias-smollett|Tobias Smollett]]** (1721–1771): Extended the picaresque tradition with *Roderick Random* (1748) and *Humphry Clinker* (1771).
- **[[laurence-sterne|Laurence Sterne]]** (1713–1768): Revolutionized narrative form with *Tristram Shandy* (1759–1767).
- **[[oliver-goldsmith|Oliver Goldsmith]]** (1728–1774): Contributed the domestic idyll *The Vicar of Wakefield* (1766).
- **[[frances-burney|Frances Burney]]** (1752–1840): Brought the feminine perspective to the novel of manners with *Evelina* (1778).

## Impact on Fiction

The eighteenth century established the novel as a recognized literary form. Key innovations included:

- **Realism**: [[daniel-defoe|Defoe]]'s insistence on credible, detailed narration created the expectation that fiction should represent ordinary life truthfully.
- **Psychological Depth**: Richardson's epistolary technique allowed unprecedented access to characters' inner lives.
- **Narrative Structure**: Fielding's architectonic plotting demonstrated that the novel could achieve the formal coherence of classical epic.
- **[[satire|Satire]] and Social Commentary**: The novel became a vehicle for moral and social criticism, from [[jonathan-swift|Swift]]'s savage irony to Goldsmith's gentle humor.

The century also saw the emergence of the female novelist. [[aphra-behn|Aphra Behn]], [[eliza-haywood|Eliza Haywood]], [[charlotte-lennox|Charlotte Lennox]], and [[frances-burney|Frances Burney]] were among the women who established a tradition of fiction written by and for women. (021-009)
