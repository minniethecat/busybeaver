**England** is the central geographic and cultural setting of the history of English prose fiction.

From the [[romance-of-chivalry|medieval romance]]s to the Victorian [[Novel]], the social and political life of England provided both the subject matter and the readership for prose narrative. Tuckerman's history is fundamentally a story about England—its institutions, class structure, and transformation from a feudal to a modern society (002-003).

## Relevance to Fiction

England's centrality to prose fiction is inseparable from its role as the birthplace of the [[novel|modern novel]].

The nation's stable political development after 1688, its commercial printing industry, and its literate middle class created ideal conditions for prose fiction. The Hundred Years' struggle between England and [[France]] stimulated a sense of national identity (007-006).

The new era in English history that developed under Elizabeth contributed directly to the flourishing of literature (009-005).

## Associated Authors

England produced virtually every major figure in the history of English prose fiction:

| Author | Connection to England | Key Works |

| [[Daniel Defoe]] | [[london|London]]-born; chronicled English trade | *Robinson Crusoe* |

These writers drew on the full range of English life—from the fashionable [[london|London]] of [[The Restoration]] to the rural villages of the countryside.

## Literary Works

England features not only as the setting but as a central subject of English fiction.

[[daniel-defoe|Defoe]]'s narratives recreate the social geography of England from [[london|London]] streets to provincial towns. Fielding's *Tom Jones* is a panoramic survey of English society. Richardson's novels explore the domestic interiors of the English middle class.

English fiction's international influence was immense. Translations and imitations of [[novel|English novel]]s appeared throughout Europe.

> "The growth of the universities at Oxford and Cambridge was a potent factor in spreading knowledge" (002-003).
