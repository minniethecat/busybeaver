# Laurence Sterne

Laurence Sterne (1713–1768) occupies, in Tuckerman's judgment, "a position in the history of fiction separate and unique" (034-008). *The Life and Opinions of Tristram Shandy, Gentleman* (1759–67) abandoned the plotted novel perfected by [[samuel-richardson|Samuel Richardson]] and [[Henry Fielding|Henry Fielding]] in favor of digression, whim, and the eccentric play of a singular mind.

Tuckerman's portrait is unsparing: "Laurence Sterne was a profligate clergyman, a dishonest author, and an unfaithful husband" (034-005). Yet the same pen that records these vices also acknowledges his gift: "He wrote 'Tristram Shandy,' and he wrote a great many sermons. He descended to the indulgence of low tastes, and rose to an elevated strain of thought, with equal facility" (034-005).

## Life

Sterne was born in Clonmel, [[ireland|Ireland]], in 1713, the son of an army officer. He attended Cambridge, took holy orders, and spent most of his life as vicar of Sutton-on-the-Forest in Yorkshire—an obscure country clergyman until the publication of the first two volumes of *Tristram Shandy* in 1759 made him, at forty-six, a sudden celebrity.

The fame that followed was intense but brief. Sterne traveled to [[london|London]] and Paris, enjoying his literary success, and died of tuberculosis in 1768. His *Sermons of Mr. Yorick* (1760–69) were published under the name of the witty parson from his novel, blurring the line between fiction and biography in a manner characteristic of the man.

## Literary Significance

*Tristram Shandy* defies classification. Tuckerman describes it as "a singular and brilliant medley of wit, sentiment, indecency, and study of character" (034-005). Sterne "has no story to tell. His aim is to amuse the reader by odd and whimsical remarks on every subject and on every personage whose peculiarities promise material for humor and satire" (034-009).

> Sterne is perpetually digressing, moralizing, commenting on every trivial topic which enters into his story, until the story itself is completely lost, if, indeed, it can ever be said to have been begun. (034-009)

Despite his borrowings from Rabelais—and Tuckerman records the damning instance in which Sterne plagiarized even Burton's protest against plagiarism (037-002)—"Sterne had originality, a gift at all times rare, and always, perhaps, becoming rarer" (034-008). As a humorist he belongs with Fielding and Smollett, but as a novelist his approach is entirely his own.

### Uncle Toby and Captain Shandy

The novel's greatest creations are Sterne's characters: Mr. Shandy, the philosopher of hobby-horses whose mind is revealed in a single question—"What is the character of a family to an hypothesis?" (034-010); Yorick, the witty parson whose epitaph, *Alas! Poor Yorick!*, expresses "so tenderly the amiable faults for which he suffered" (034-010); and above all Captain Toby Shandy, "that combination of simplicity, gentleness, humanity, and modesty" (034-010).

The deathbed scene of Lieutenant Lefevre is, in Tuckerman's judgment, "probably the finest passage which ever fell from the skilful pen of Laurence Sterne" (034-011). The exchange between Uncle Toby and Corporal Trim—"He shall not die, by G—, cried my uncle Toby"—ends with one of the most famous sentences in English fiction: "The *accusing spirit*, which flew up to Heaven's chancery with the oath, blushed as he gave it in;—and the *recording angel*, as he wrote it down, dropped a tear upon the word, and blotted it out for ever" (034-011).

Sterne's method is one of indirect revelation: he "makes a character known to the reader by a succession of delicate touches rather than by description" (034-010).

## Major Works

- **The Life and Opinions of Tristram Shandy, Gentleman** (1759–67) — Nine volumes of digressive, character-driven narrative.
- **A Sentimental Journey Through [[france|France]] and [[italy|Italy]]** (1768) — A travel narrative continued by the Shandean sensibility.
- **The Sermons of Mr. Yorick** (1760–69) — Sermons published under the name of Sterne's fictional parson.
