## Life

George Eliot was born Mary Ann Evans in Warwickshire, [[england|England]], in 1819. She received an unusually broad education for a woman of her time and developed a deep interest in philosophy, theology, and languages. After a period of religious skepticism, she moved to [[london|London]] and became a translator and editor. Her unconventional personal life—including a long partnership with the married writer George Henry Lewes—placed her outside respectable Victorian society, but her literary genius could not be ignored.

## Literary Significance

By far the greatest female novelist who has devoted her talents to the English domestic novel, and by far the greatest female writer in the language is undeniably George Eliot (042-011). Women almost invariably leave the stamp of their sex upon their work, but George Eliot took and held a man's position in literature from the outset of her career (042-011). She brought to her work a woman's sympathy and a woman's attention to detail, but in breadth of conception and comprehensiveness of thought, her mind was essentially masculine (042-011). Her appreciation of varieties and shades of character was almost Shakespearian (042-011).

In the study of the inward workings of the human mind, George Eliot is unsurpassed by any novelist (042-012). Thackeray alone can dispute her pre-eminence in this respect (042-012). However much the reader may recoil from the horror of Hetty's crime in *Adam Bede*, he cannot deny that it follows as a natural consequence; and although Dorothea's marriages in *Middlemarch* are extremely disappointing, the train of thought which led her to enter into them is traced with unerring clearness (042-012).

George Eliot's writing has a distinctive quality. An obstacle to the popularity of George Eliot's novels lies in the slowness of their movement (042-013). The author's soliloquies, comments, and reflections, which are so much valued by her especial admirers, constantly interrupt the course of the narrative, and prove cumbersome to such readers as enjoy a rapid, flowing story (042-013). Yet without these interruptions, how much of George Eliot's best wisdom would be lost (042-013).

A faculty which George Eliot possessed in common with Dickens and Thackeray was that of making very ordinary people interesting (042-016). This talent is characteristic of the best minds which have contributed to fiction or the drama (042-016). George Eliot is particularly happy in drawing a really ordinary person, especially when a little pretension is added (042-016).

She was not without her limits. So long as George Eliot confined herself to her own sphere of action, she exhibited remarkable powers. But even her great name could not command admiration for *The [[spain|Spanish]] Gypsy*, and her limitations clearly appeared in *Daniel Deronda* (042-017).

In historical fiction, George Eliot's *Romola* must always retain a high place (046-002). But its author's great creative power led her to bestow more pains on such of the characters as proceeded from her own imagination, than on those whom history provided ready-made (046-002).

The novels of George Eliot, along with those of Scott, Dickens, Thackeray, Austen, and Ferrier, have afforded to hundreds of thousands a never-failing source of healthful entertainment, and her conceptions remain among the dearest possessions of all English-speaking people (047-006) (047-013).

## Major Works

- **Adam Bede** (1859) — Eliot's first full-length novel, a tragedy of seduction and infanticide set in rural Warwickshire; praised for its sympathetic portrayal of ordinary working people.
- **The Mill on the Floss** (1860) — A semi-autobiographical novel tracing the lives of siblings Tom and Maggie Tulliver; celebrated for its psychological depth.
- **Silas Marner** (1861) — A compact tale of a reclusive weaver redeemed through the love of a foundling child; one of Eliot's most accessible works.
- **Middlemarch** (1871–72) — Widely regarded as Eliot's masterpiece and one of the greatest [[novel|English novel]]s; a panoramic study of provincial life, idealistic ambition, and marital disappointment.
- **Daniel Deronda** (1876) — Eliot's final novel, notable for its sympathetic treatment of Jewish identity and its critique of English high society.
