# Thomas More

## Life

Sir Thomas More (1478–1535) was an English lawyer, scholar, and statesman who served as Lord Chancellor under Henry VIII. A devout Catholic, More wore a hair shirt and practiced self-flagellation, yet he was also a Christian in a wider sense — he appreciated the importance of peace and material welfare alongside religious orthodoxy. (009-005) As a lawyer, More saw clearly into the temper of his time and discerned the new tendencies that were forming the opinions and influencing the actions of his countrymen. (009-005) He was executed for refusing to acknowledge Henry VIII as head of the English church, dying a martyr's death for principles he held throughout his life. (009-005)

## Literary Significance

More's most important contribution to English prose fiction was *[[utopia|Utopia]]* (1516), a work that belongs to the earliest period of English narrative. According to Tuckerman, "there is no better exposition" of the New Learning — the dawn of material prosperity, the rule of law, and a new secular intelligence — than More's *[[utopia|Utopia]]*. (009-001) The book used the device of a fictional traveler, a mariner who described to More the nature of a distant island's government, to present a vision of an [[utopia|ideal commonwealth]] governed by reason rather than theological authority. (009-002)

In *[[utopia|Utopia]]*, More imagined a society without a priesthood, where religion was left to individual conscience and war was considered an unmitigated evil. (009-003) (009-004) The citizens of [[utopia|Utopia]] focused on practical questions: making good laws, dealing with crime and labor, and promoting public health, education, and general well-being. (009-004) Tuckerman notes that More's use of fiction to urge political and social reforms established a precedent — the didactic purpose in English fiction — that would become a prominent characteristic of the [[novel|English novel]]. (019-001)

## Major Works

- **[[utopia|Utopia]]** (1516) — A philosophical fiction describing an ideal island commonwealth. Written in Latin and translated into English by Ralph Robinson. The work presents a vision of religious toleration, rational governance, and social justice that was centuries ahead of its time.
