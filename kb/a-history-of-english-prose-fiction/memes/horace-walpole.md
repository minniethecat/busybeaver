# Horace Walpole

Horace Walpole (1717–1797) was an English writer, art historian, and man of letters.
The fourth son of Prime Minister Sir Robert Walpole, he authored *The Castle of Otranto* (1764),
the work that initiated the [[gothic-fiction|Gothic tradition]] in English prose fiction.

Tuckerman notes that "posterity has cause to regret that Horace Walpole, of all men best fitted
by personality and position to enrich the literature of romantic fiction,
should have written but one [[novel]]" (038-010).

## Life

Walpole was born into the heart of English political power as the son of Sir Robert Walpole,
the dominant political figure of the early Hanoverian era (022-001).
He was educated at Eton and Cambridge, and traveled extensively on the Continent.

Through his family connections and personal charm, Walpole moved easily in the highest circles
of [[england|English society]], observing court life with a sharp eye (021-002).
In 1742, he wrote to Sir Horace Mann on the state of English letters,
demonstrating the epistolary fluency for which he would become famous (020-014).

Walpole was an indefatigable letter-writer whose correspondence provides a vivid chronicle
of eighteenth-century social and literary life (020-012).
His letters helped corroborate the extraordinary popular reception of [[frances-burney|Frances Burney]]'s *Evelina* (037-006).

## Literary Significance

Walpole's enduring contribution rests on a single work: *The Castle of Otranto*, published in 1764.
The novel was "an addition of no little value" to English literature (038-010).

Its declared object "was to unite the characteristics of ancient and modern [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]]" (038-011),
blending supernatural elements with the psychological [[realism-in-fiction|realism]]
of the emerging [[novel]] form.

Walpole, together with [[clara-reeve|Clara Reeve]] and [[ann-radcliffe|Ann Radcliffe]], was central to the revival of romantic
elements in English fiction (038-009). Where [[Samuel Richardson|Richardson]]
and [[Henry Fielding|Fielding]] had moved toward domestic realism,
Walpole deliberately reached backward to medieval atmospherics and forward to psychological terror.

The very merits of *Otranto* "sustain this decision" (038-012).
Its influence is evident in the later [[gothic-fiction|Gothic school]]
from Radcliffe through to the Victorian sensation novel (039-005).

## Major Works

- **The Castle of Otranto** (1764) — The first [[gothic-fiction|Gothic novel]], combining supernatural
  apparitions with psychological suspense (039-003).
- **The Mysterious Mother** (1768) — A Gothic tragedy in blank verse.
- **Anecdotes of Painting in [[england|England]]** (1762–1771) — Foundational work of English art history.
- **Letters** — Valued for their wit and historical observation.
