# Allegory

## Definition

Allegory is a narrative form in which characters, events, and settings function as symbols representing abstract ideas, moral qualities, or spiritual truths. Unlike simple metaphor, allegory sustains its symbolic correspondences across the entire structure of a work. The literal story operates on one level, while the deeper, often moral or religious, meaning operates on another. Tuckerman describes it as a form that "raised romantic fiction into the region of allegory, and gave to English literature a work which has ever been read with delight by men of every class and condition." (004-005)

## Historical Context

Allegory has ancient roots in classical and medieval literature, but its most celebrated expression in English prose fiction came in the late seventeenth century with [[john-bunyan|John Bunyan]]'s *The Pilgrim's Progress* (1678). Before the rise of the [[novel|realistic novel]], allegory and [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]] were the dominant narrative modes: "there had been the [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]] and the allegory. There had been the short love story, and the tale of roguery and adventure." (027-007)

Bunyan's work emerged from the Puritan religious culture of the seventeenth century. His allegory transformed the spiritual pilgrimage of a Christian soul into a compelling narrative that "appealed to all classes and ages." (027-011) Tuckerman judges it "destined ever to remain, the finest allegory in the English language." (016-002)

The form continued to influence later fiction. [[daniel-defoe|Daniel Defoe]], writing in the early eighteenth century, drew on allegorical techniques: "[[daniel-defoe|Defoe]] claimed that it was an allegory of his own life." (028-013) [[jonathan-swift|Jonathan Swift]] employed allegory in a satirical mode in *A Tale of a Tub*, described by Tuckerman as "the wonderful allegory of the 'Tale of a Tub.'" (025-010)

## Key Characteristics

- **Sustained symbolic correspondence**: Characters represent abstract qualities (e.g., Christian in *The Pilgrim's Progress* represents every believer; Giant Despair represents spiritual despondency).
- **Moral or spiritual purpose**: Allegory is inherently didactic, aiming to convey ethical or theological truths through narrative.
- **Universalized characters**: Characters are often named for the qualities they embody rather than functioning as individualized portraits.
- **Journey or quest structure**: Many allegories employ the metaphor of a journey or pilgrimage to structure the protagonist's moral development.

Bunyan's particular genius, as Tuckerman explains, was "to accomplish the extraordinary task of making abstractions living realities," achieving "the first place among writers of allegory." (016-003) His characters, though embodying abstract qualities, possess vividness and psychological reality.

## Notable Examples

[[john-bunyan|John Bunyan]]'s **The Pilgrim's Progress** (1678) stands as the supreme English prose allegory. Tuckerman devotes extended attention to its achievement, noting that it gave English literature a work "which has ever been read with delight by men of every class and condition." (004-005) Its combination of spiritual seriousness with narrative momentum made it one of the most widely read books in English.

[[jonathan-swift|Jonathan Swift]]'s **A Tale of a Tub** (1704) represents allegory in a satirical key. Tuckerman calls it "the wonderful allegory of the 'Tale of a Tub,'" in which [[jonathan-swift|Swift]] deployed allegorical form to satirize religious controversy. (025-010) The work demonstrated that allegory could serve comic and polemical ends as well as spiritual ones.

[[jonathan-swift|Swift]]'s **Gulliver's Travels** (1726) also employs allegorical elements, though Tuckerman notes that [[jonathan-swift|Swift]] was "at pains to maintain the probability of the allegory" within the fantastical framework. (026-011) [[daniel-defoe|Daniel Defoe]]'s fictions, meanwhile, drew on allegorical conventions even as they pioneered realist technique. (028-013)

## Related Concepts

- [[Puritanism]] — the religious culture that produced Bunyan's allegorical vision
- [[English Novel]] — the later realist form that partly evolved from and reacted against allegory
- [[Satire]] — a closely related mode that [[jonathan-swift|Swift]] made overlap with allegory in *A Tale of a Tub*
- [[Romance of Chivalry]] — the other major pre-novel narrative form alongside allegory
