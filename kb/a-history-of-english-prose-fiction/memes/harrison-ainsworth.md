# Harrison Ainsworth

## Life

William Harrison Ainsworth (1805–1882) was an English historical novelist who began his literary career as a lawyer before turning to fiction. He became one of the most popular novelists of the Victorian era, known particularly for his vivid depictions of historical settings and criminal underworlds.

## Literary Significance

Ainsworth's fiction belongs to the tradition of the historical novel that flourished in [[england|England]] under the influence of Sir [[walter-scott|Walter Scott]]. Tuckerman notes that he "has made use of historical material in 'The Tower of [[london|London]],' and similar writings" (046-005), placing him among a group of well-known historical novelists who worked in the wake of Scott's achievement. Ainsworth also contributed to the tradition of the criminal novel, a genre Tuckerman describes with considerable skepticism. "From Lytton's 'Paul Clifford,' and Ainsworth's 'Jack Sheppard,' down to 'Merciless Ben, the Hair-Lifter,' criminal narrative has been occupied with endowing burglars and murderers with the graces of gentlemen and the moral worth of Christian missionaries" (044-007). Tuckerman judges this species of fiction harshly, observing that "in its celebration of successful crime, and its representation under a heroic aspect of villains and blacklegs, no species of fiction is more false to nature or more injurious to youthful readers" (044-007). Ainsworth's dual engagement with historical [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]] and criminal narrative reflects the expanding range of subject matter in nineteenth-century English fiction, even as it drew criticism for its moral implications.

## Major Works

- **The Tower of [[london|London]]** — A historical novel drawing on Ainsworth's use of English historical material, set around the famous fortress and its storied past (046-005).
- **Jack Sheppard** — A criminal novel centered on the notorious eighteenth-century thief, emblematic of the genre Tuckerman condemns for romanticizing villainy (044-007).
