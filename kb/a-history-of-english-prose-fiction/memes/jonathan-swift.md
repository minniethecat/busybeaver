**Jonathan Swift** (1667–1745) was the supreme master of prose [[Satire]] in the English language.

His works, particularly *Gulliver's Travels* and *A Tale of a Tub*, combined savage indignation with unparalleled imaginative power. Swift's bitterness toward humanity was rooted in his experience of political and religious conflict in post-[[The Restoration]] [[England]] and [[Ireland]]. Tuckerman's history presents him as a figure of great apparent contradictions—a misanthrope capable of loyal friendship, a clergyman who wrote bawdy verse, a defender of reason who depicted humanity as Yahoos (025-009).

## Life

Swift was born in Dublin in 1667 to English parents and educated at Trinity College.

His early career as secretary to Sir William Temple gave him access to the world of politics and letters. He received a deanery under Queen Anne and became the most feared pamphleteer of his age. The bitter lines that introduce Swift in Tuckerman's pages reflect a man shaped by disappointed ambition and a deep pessimism about human nature (025-005).

## Literary Significance

Swift's greatest contribution to English prose fiction was the perfection of satirical narrative as a vehicle for comprehensive social criticism.

In *Gulliver's Travels*, he created a work that functioned simultaneously as a children's adventure story and a devastating anatomy of human corruption. The satire "gathered strength and bitterness as it went on," moving from the playful diminution of Lilliput to the cosmic misanthropy of the Houyhnhnms (026-006). In *A Tale of a Tub*, his earlier allegory of religious enthusiasm, Swift's talents in "caricature and in satire" were already fully formed (025-010).

Together with [[Daniel Defoe]], Addison, and Steele, Swift helped forge the clear, forceful English prose that characterized the eighteenth century (020-012).

## Major Works

| Work | Year | Significance |
|------|------|-------------|
| *A Tale of a Tub* | 1704 | Allegorical satire of religious excess and modern learning |
| *Gulliver's Travels* | 1726 | Universal satire of human nature through fantasy voyages |
| *A Modest Proposal* | 1729 | Devastating ironic pamphlet on [[ireland|Irish]] poverty |
| *The Battle of the Books* | 1704 | Mock-heroic defense of the ancients against the moderns |

Swift's satire in the voyage to Laputa is directed "against the vanity of philosophers and projectors" (026-005). The final voyage to the land of the Houyhnhnms remains one of the most uncompromising critiques of humanity ever written.

> "The character of Swift presents great apparent contradictions" (025-009).
