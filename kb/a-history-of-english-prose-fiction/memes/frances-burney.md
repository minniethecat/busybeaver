# Frances Burney

## Life

Frances Burney (1752–1840), later Madame d'Arblay, was born in King's Lynn, Norfolk, the daughter of Dr. Charles Burney, a distinguished music historian. Her father's house in [[london|London]] was a gathering place for the leading literary and artistic figures of the day, including [[samuel-johnson|Samuel Johnson]], Edmund Burke, and Hester Thrale. As a young girl, she observed these visitors with keen attention, studying "the characters and manners of those who talked and moved about her" (037-003). She began writing fiction secretly at the age of sixteen, but was discouraged by her stepmother. Her breakthrough came in 1778, when at the age of twenty-five, she published *Evelina* anonymously—even the publisher did not know the author's identity. The novel's immediate success brought her into literary celebrity. In 1793 she married General Alexandre d'Arblay, a French émigré, and spent several years in [[france|France]]. She lived into her eighty-eighth year, long enough "to see the works of her early friends enrolled among the classics or consigned to oblivion, and to recognize that the approval of posterity had been added to the early fame of her own writings" (037-003).

## Literary Significance

Burney occupies a pivotal position in the development of the [[novel|English novel]]. Her first novel, *Evelina*, "made a sensation which the merits of the work fully justified" (037-003). She was among the first novelists to make the daily experiences of a young woman—the "difficulties and sensations of a young girl just entering life" (037-004)—the central subject of serious fiction. In the painting of manners, Burney "was eminently successful" (037-007), and her skill in plot construction was regarded as exceptional: "Fielding alone, of the eighteenth century novelists, can be said to surpass Miss Burney in this respect" (037-007). Her social range was remarkable—*Evelina* takes the reader through theatres, Vauxhall, Ranelagh, and the fashionable assemblies of Bath (037-004). [[samuel-johnson|Samuel Johnson]] considered Burney "equal to Fielding," though Tuckerman notes that "although she possessed qualities similar to his—constructive power and picturesqueness—she possessed them in a lesser degree" (037-010). In managing the epistolary form, however, "she surpassed Richardson in verisimilitude and concentration" (037-010). Her novels bridged the gap between the satirical tradition of Fielding and Smollett and the domestic realism that [[jane-austen|Jane Austen]] would later perfect. Johnson once declared that he had "dined at Mrs. Garrick's with Mrs. Carter, Miss [[hannah-more|Hannah More]], and Miss Fanny Burney: three such women are not to be found" (037-013).

## Major Works

- **Evelina** (1778) — Burney's first and most celebrated novel. An epistolary novel tracing a young woman's entrance into [[london|London]] society, notable for its vivid depiction of fashionable life and its skillful plot construction. The heroine is "one whose circumstances particularly well suited her to form the centre of a varied collection of characters and of a comprehensive picture of contemporary society" (037-004).
- **Cecilia** (1782) — Her second novel, which "has much the same merits which distinguished *Evelina*" (037-010). The novel gave rise to the phrase "pride and prejudice," later used by [[jane-austen|Jane Austen]].
- **Camilla** (1796) — A novel of manners and education.
- **The Wanderer** (1814) — Her final novel, set partly during the French Revolution.
- **Diaries and Letters** — Posthumously published, these provide a vivid record of eighteenth-century literary and court life.
