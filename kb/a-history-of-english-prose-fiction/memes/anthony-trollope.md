## Life

Anthony Trollope was born in [[london|London]] in 1815, the son of a failed barrister and a successful novelist mother, [[frances-trollope|Frances Trollope]]. He worked for the General Post Office for over thirty years, combining his postal career with a prodigious literary output. Trollope famously rose early each morning to write before going to his office, producing a set number of words per hour with mechanical regularity. His disciplined work habits allowed him to publish forty-seven novels, along with numerous short stories, travel books, and biographies.

## Literary Significance

Anthony Trollope was among the novelists who elevated the form to a position of respectability and dignity in the nineteenth century, alongside Scott, Austen, Dickens, Thackeray, and [[charles-kingsley|Charles Kingsley]] (040-028). His works exemplified the strong tendency among novelists since Scott to devote themselves to the common characters and incidents of every-day life, describing the world as it appears to the ordinary observer; such was the general character of the works of [[george-eliot|George Eliot]] and of Mr. Anthony Trollope (041-002).

With a few exceptions, Mr. Anthony Trollope has confined himself to the novel of English social life, but that mine he has worked with wonderful assiduity and success (043-004). His clerical studies in *The Warden* and *Barchester Towers* won him a special reputation, and *The Small House at Allington* is a love story of particular fascination (043-004). Few writers have described the manifestations of love in the acts and thoughts of a modest, sweet girl as delicately as Mr. Trollope has done (043-004).

Mr. Anthony Trollope and several other novelists have shown how fallacious is the idea that the imagination is a fickle mistress to be courted and waited for; they have proved that she can be made to settle down and accustomed by habit to working at stated hours and for regular periods (043-003).

Trollope's novels will have a special value for the future student of English social life in the nineteenth century (043-004). The race-course, the hunting field, the country seat, Piccadilly, Hyde Park, the life of clubs and parliament, are described by him with photographic minuteness (043-004).

In an article published in *The Nineteenth Century*, Mr. Anthony Trollope expressed his views on the good and evil influences exerted by works of fiction, arguing that the novelist's moral influence is all the more powerful because it is insinuated through amusement rather than applied with declared intention; the novelist creeps in closer than the schoolmaster, closer than the father, closer almost than the mother (046-013).

## Major Works

- **The Warden** (1855) — The first of the Barsetshire novels; a gentle satire of ecclesiastical politics and reform.
- **Barchester Towers** (1857) — The most celebrated of the Barsetshire series, featuring the unforgettable characters of Mrs. Proudie, Mr. Slope, and Archdeacon Grantly.
- **The Small House at Allington** (1864) — A love story of particular fascination, featuring the delicately drawn character of Lily Dale.
- **Phineas Finn** (1869) — A parliamentary novel following the career of a young [[ireland|Irish]] MP, the first of the Palliser series.
- **The Way We Live Now** (1875) — Often considered Trollope's masterpiece, a sweeping satire of financial speculation and moral corruption in Victorian society.
