# Sentiment in Fiction

## Definition

Sentiment in fiction refers to the deliberate cultivation and expression of refined emotion, moral feeling, and sympathetic sensibility within narrative prose. Distinguished from mere emotional display, literary sentiment involves the portrayal of characters' inner moral lives and the evocation of sympathetic responses in readers. In the context of eighteenth-century English fiction, sentiment became both a narrative technique and a measure of a work's moral seriousness.

Tuckerman identifies sentiment as one of the defining qualities that [[romance-of-chivalry|chivalric romance]] bequeathed to later fiction, noting that early [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]] reflected "the first budding of the chivalric sentiment." (003-005) The concept evolved from medieval ideals of courtly feeling into a central concern of the eighteenth-century novel.

## Historical Context

The rise of sentiment in English fiction is inseparable from the broader eighteenth-century "culture of sensibility" — a philosophical and literary movement that valued emotional responsiveness as a sign of moral character. Tuckerman describes the preconditions for this development in his account of the late seventeenth and early eighteenth centuries, when religious sentiment had declined and "the absence of sentiment so characteristic of the time" created a vacuum that the novel would fill. (024-004)

The shift from Restoration libertinism to eighteenth-century moral seriousness created the conditions for sentiment to flourish. After the revolution of 1688, a new tone entered English life and letters. Tuckerman notes that this period saw "a tone of healthy sentiment which insensibly elevates the reader" begin to displace the coarser sensibilities of the Restoration. (036-006) The novel, as a new literary form uniquely capable of representing interior experience, became the natural vehicle for this cultural shift.

## Key Characteristics

- **Moral purpose**: Sentimental fiction consistently subordinates entertainment to moral instruction. Characters' emotional trials serve as vehicles for ethical reflection, a pattern Tuckerman traces through the major sentimental novelists. (020-011)
- **Sympathetic identification**: The sentimental novel invites readers to feel with rather than merely observe characters. This technique of sympathetic engagement was refined across the eighteenth century.
- **Elevation of domestic feeling**: Sentimental fiction locates profound emotion in ordinary domestic experience rather than in heroic or aristocratic settings. This democratization of feeling was a significant departure from earlier [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]] conventions. (034-001)
- **Combination of pathos and humor**: In the hands of its greatest practitioners — particularly Sterne — sentiment achieves its effects through an unstable mixture of tears and laughter. Tuckerman observes that "the combination of sentiment, pathos, and humor which Sterne sometimes reached with remarkable skill" represents one of the form's highest achievements. (034-011)

## Notable Examples

**[[samuel-richardson|Samuel Richardson]]** established the emotional template for the [[novel|English novel]]. His epistolary technique — having characters write their feelings in the moment of experiencing them — created an unprecedented intimacy between reader and character. Tuckerman notes that "Richardson had known how to move his readers," a capacity for emotional effect that was central to his enormous influence. (029-006)

**[[laurence-sterne|Laurence Sterne]]** carried the play of sentiment to its most sophisticated expression. His work demonstrates "the combination of sentiment, pathos, and humor" in a form that continually defies reader expectations. (034-011) Sterne's digressive, self-conscious narrative method made sentiment itself a subject of ironic scrutiny even as it evoked genuine feeling. "Sterne is perpetually digressing," Tuckerman observes, "mocking at the same time both his reader and himself." (034-009)

**[[henry-mackenzie|Henry Mackenzie]]** represents the late eighteenth-century flowering of the sentimental tradition. Tuckerman observes that "the novels of [[henry-mackenzie|Henry Mackenzie]] have a charm of their own," and his *Man of Feeling* (1771) became the period's definitive portrait of refined masculine sensibility. (035-012) A "tone of refined sentiment and dignity pervades 'The Man of Feeling,'" marking the culmination of the sentimental novel before it gave way to new forms. (035-013)
