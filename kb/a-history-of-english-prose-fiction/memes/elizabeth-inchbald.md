# Elizabeth Inchbald

## Life

Elizabeth Inchbald (1753–1821) was born Elizabeth Simpson in Suffolk. "She was overcome in early life by an enthusiasm for the stage; ran away from home to find theatrical employment, and remained for many years a popular [[london|London]] actress" (037-014). Despite "great and durable beauty, and the object of constant attention from aristocratic admirers, it is believed that her reputation continued unsullied" (037-015). Her early life was one of considerable hardship. Largely caused by a worthless husband, her poverty "obliged her to perform the most menial labors" (037-015). She wrote to a friend: "Last Thursday I finished scouring my bed-chamber, while a coach with a coronet and two footmen waited at the door to take me an airing" (037-015). "The same courage and industry were carried by Mrs. Inchbald into her literary labors, the profits of which enabled her to live with considerable comfort toward the end of her life" (038-001).

## Literary Significance

Inchbald was a writer of considerable natural ability, though not a polished one. Tuckerman notes that "neither of these works has much merit from a critical point of view. They are faulty in construction, and give frequent evidence of the authoress' lack of education" (038-001). Yet her real strength lay elsewhere: "in her ability to excite the interest and to move the feelings of her reader, Mrs. Inchbald met with great success. Her novels are of the pathetic order, and appeal to the sympathies with a sometimes powerful effect" (038-002). This gift for emotional connection earned her a remarkable tribute from [[maria-edgeworth|Maria Edgeworth]], who wrote of *A Simple Story*: "I never read *any* novel—I except none,—I never read any novel that affected me so strongly, or that so completely possessed me with the belief in the real existence, of all the persons it represents" (038-002). Inchbald's career as a playwright complemented her fiction; she "left a large number of plays, many of which had been acted with success" (038-001).

## Major Works

- **A Simple Story** (1791) — Inchbald's first and most acclaimed novel. A tale of tangled family relationships, forbidden love, and the conflict between passion and duty. [[maria-edgeworth|Maria Edgeworth]] was "deeply moved" by it even on her fourth reading (038-002).
- **Nature and Art** (1796) — Her second novel, contrasting the natural goodness of an unschooled young man with the corruption of his worldly cousin.
- **Plays** — Inchbald wrote and adapted numerous plays for the [[london|London]] stage, many performed successfully; she also edited a multi-volume collection of British plays.
