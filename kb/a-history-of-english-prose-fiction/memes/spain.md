# Spain

## Overview

Spain exercised a significant literary influence on the development of English prose fiction, particularly through the [[romance-of-chivalry|chivalric romance]]s of the [[middle-ages|Middle Ages]] and the picaresque tradition of the Renaissance. The Spanish [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]]s of *Amadis of Gaul* and *Palmerin* were among the most widely read works of fiction in sixteenth-century Europe, shaping the expectations of English readers and writers alike. (010-004)

## Literary Significance

The Spanish contribution to English fiction came through several channels. The [[romance-of-chivalry|chivalric romance]]s, with their elaborate adventures, idealized love, and fantastic settings, provided models for Elizabethan prose fiction and were both imitated and satirized by later writers. The fashions of [[france|France]], of Spain, and of Turkey, even of the Moors, all contributed to the eclectic mix of influences that shaped sixteenth-century English taste. (010-004)

More profoundly, Cervantes's *Don Quixote* (1605, 1615) transformed European fiction by parodying the excesses of [[romance-of-chivalry|chivalric romance]] while simultaneously creating the first [[novel|modern novel]]. English eighteenth-century novelists — particularly Fielding, who described *Joseph Andrews* as "written in imitation of the manner of Cervantes" — acknowledged the Spanish master as a foundational figure.

## Key Locations

- **Madrid**: The capital of Spanish literary culture
- **La Mancha**: The setting of *Don Quixote*, iconic landscape of the novel tradition
- **Seville**: Setting for picaresque tales and later Romantic fiction

## Role in Fiction

Spain represented for English fiction the land of [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]] par excellence: a country of chivalry, passion, and adventure, set against which English realism and restraint could be measured. The Spanish picaresque tradition — episodic narratives following a roguish hero through a corrupt society — provided a structural model that [[novel|English novel]]ists from [[daniel-defoe|Defoe]] through Smollett adapted to native materials. (012-006)
