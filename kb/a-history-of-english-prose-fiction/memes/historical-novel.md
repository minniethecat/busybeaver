# Historical Novel

## Definition

The historical novel is a subgenre of fiction in which the plot is set in a historically defined period, often incorporating real historical events, settings, and figures into an imaginative narrative. It attempts to reconstruct the spirit, manners, and social conditions of a past age. As Tuckerman notes, "the historical novel is obviously a subdivision of the novel of manners." (045-012)

## Historical Context

The historical novel rose to prominence in the early nineteenth century, largely through the works of Sir [[walter-scott|Walter Scott]]. The earlier eighteenth-century novel had concentrated on contemporary life and manners; Scott opened a vast new territory by turning to the past. Tuckerman observes that Scott's novels "deal with periods extending from the [[middle-ages|Middle Ages]] to the eighteenth century" and are noteworthy for "their truth to human nature, their accurate pictures of social life in other days, their poetical appreciation of the beauties of nature." (045-014)

The form found a receptive audience in an age increasingly conscious of historical change. Scott's success inspired numerous followers, and "G.P.R. James are well-known historical novelists who have written under the influence of Scott." (046-005) The genre continued to develop through the Victorian period and beyond.

## Key Characteristics

- **Historical setting**: The narrative is placed in a recognizable past era, often with specific dates and events.
- **Blend of fact and fiction**: Real historical figures and events intermingle with invented characters and plots. "Many inaccuracies of fact may be pointed out," Tuckerman admits of Scott, but what matters is "their truth to human nature." (045-013, 045-014)
- **Period manners and atmosphere**: The author recreates the social customs, speech, and material culture of the period. The "special merit of Scott's historical novels" lies in capturing the lived texture of past societies. (045-013)
- **Sweeping scope**: Historical novels often encompass broad social panoramas, from aristocracy to common people.

## Notable Examples

The foundational works of the historical novel are Sir [[walter-scott|Walter Scott]]'s Waverley [[novel|Novel]]s (1814–1832), which include *Ivanhoe*, *Waverley*, and *The Heart of Midlothian*. These established the template for the genre across European literature.

Earlier experiments in historical fiction include [[william-godwin|William Godwin]]'s *Fleetwood* (1805), which Tuckerman notes among "attempts in the direction of the historical novel." (036-010) [[edward-bulwer|Edward Bulwer-Lytton]] contributed *The Last Days of Pompeii* and *The Last of the Barons*, which Tuckerman classifies as historical novels alongside his other fiction. (043-002)

Among recent practitioners at the time of Tuckerman's writing, "the most prominent of recent historical novels" attempted to describe specific historical moments with scholarly attention to detail. (046-003) G.P.R. James, a prolific follower of Scott, produced numerous historical [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]]s. (046-005)
