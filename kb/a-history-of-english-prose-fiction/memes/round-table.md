# Round Table

## Definition

The Round Table is the legendary central symbol of King Arthur's court — the table at which the king and his knights gathered as equals, none occupying a position of precedence. Unlike the hierarchical seating of feudal halls, the Round Table's circular form signified that all who sat at it were peers in honor and fellowship. In Malory's *Morte d'Arthur*, it serves as the organizing principle of Arthurian chivalry, the physical and symbolic center around which the adventures of the knights revolve. Its destruction marks the end of Arthur's kingdom and of the chivalric world itself.

## Historical Context

The Round Table has both literary and historical dimensions. Historically, Edward I held a "Round Table" tournament at Kenilworth in 1279, evidence that the Arthurian ideal exercised a powerful hold on the medieval aristocratic imagination. (004-003) This fusion of historical practice and literary ideal is characteristic of chivalric culture, in which life imitated art as much as art reflected life.

In literary terms, the Round Table achieved its definitive form in [[thomas-malory|Sir Thomas Malory]]'s *Morte d'Arthur* (1485), which gathered and synthesized the scattered Arthurian [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]]s into a coherent narrative. Tuckerman notes that the *Morte d'Arthur* made "the knights of the Round Table familiar in almost every household." (004-005) It was Malory who gave the Round Table its tragic arc — from the glory of its founding through the slow corruption of its ideals to its final, catastrophic dissolution.

The Round Table had a significant afterlife in English fiction. Even as the [[romance-of-chivalry|chivalric romance]] declined, its central symbol persisted. Tuckerman observes that "the knights of the Round Table, Robin Hood and his merry men" continued to inhabit the English literary imagination well after their historical moment had passed. (016-009)

## Key Characteristics

- **Equality of fellowship**: The circular form of the table abolishes rank among those seated at it. All knights are equal in honor; the king is first among equals rather than a hierarchical superior. This ideal of chivalric brotherhood was central to the Round Table's appeal.
- **The adventure-seeking imperative**: The knights of the Round Table are defined by their perpetual quest for adventure. Tuckerman notes that "the knights of the Round Table had much more difficulty in discovering adventures" as the cycle progresses, suggesting a world in which chivalric opportunities are gradually exhausted. (005-012)
- **The tragic arc**: The Round Table is defined as much by its fall as by its founding. The love affair of Launcelot and Guenever brings "calamity upon the Round Table," and the narrative concludes with "the deserted Round Table, the separation of life-long friends, the end of all that was glorious." (006-005, 006-006)
- **Contrast with popular heroism**: Tuckerman draws a revealing contrast between the aristocratic chivalry of the Round Table and the popular heroism of Robin Hood. The Robin Hood ballads "differ from those of the Round Table in their entire freedom from the exclusive and knightly spirit." (007-008) This contrast illuminates the class character of Arthurian chivalry — it is an aristocratic ideal, however broadly appealing.

## Notable Examples

[[thomas-malory|Sir Thomas Malory]]'s **Morte d'Arthur** is the definitive treatment of the Round Table in English prose. Tuckerman describes it as "an excellent specimen of a mediæval [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]]," and notes how Malory wove the disparate threads of the Arthurian tradition into a unified narrative organized around the rise and fall of the Round Table. (004-006) "In the very beginning of the *Morte d'Arthur*," Tuckerman observes, "and throughout we trace its influence" — the Round Table is the work's governing symbol. (004-007)

The **Arthurian passages of the *Morte d'Arthur*** that most fully realize the Round Table's significance include the Roman war episode — "a curious illustration of the extent and variety of the ground which the *Morte d'Arthur* covers" (006-004) — and the final tragedy of Launcelot and Guenever, after which "the old glory of the Round Table passes away forever." (006-008)
