## Life

John Lyly was born in Kent in 1554 and studied at Magdalen College, [[oxford|Oxford]], where he received the degree of Master of Arts. Not a very diligent scholar, he disliked the "crabbed studies" of logic and philosophy, "his genie being naturally bent to the pleasant paths of poetry" (011-005). At [[oxford|Oxford]], as later at Elizabeth's court, he was reputed "a rare poet, witty, comical, and facetious" (011-005).

During his life in [[london|London]] he produced a number of plays and poems which have given his name a not inconsiderable place in the list of Elizabethan poets and dramatists (011-005). For many years Lyly lingered about the court waiting for a promised position to reward his labors and support his declining years (011-006). He died in 1606, having left a lasting mark on English prose style.

## Literary Significance

Lyly is now best known, where known at all, by his prose work *Euphues*, which attained great popularity at Elizabeth's court, where all the ladies knew his phrases by heart and to "parley [[euphuism|Euphuism]]" was a sign of breeding (011-006). The word [[euphuism|Euphuism]] became an abstract term in the language to express the ornate and antithetical style of which this book is the most marked example (011-005).

In Lyly's own day it was said by Edward Blount that the nation was "in his debt for a new English which hee taught them" (011-005). Since then, the verdict of posterity has been that Lyly corrupted the public taste and introduced an affected and overloaded manner of writing. A careful examination of Lyly's work, however, does not sustain this view. The [[euphuism|Euphuistic style]] was not of Lyly's invention — he acquired it from the men about him and merely gave it, through his writings, a distinct character and individuality (011-005).

Lyly characterized his own time with the observation: "It is a world to see how Englishmen desire to hear finer speech than the language will allow, to eat finer bread than is made of wheat, or wear finer cloth than is made of wool" (011-003). These were the words of one who expressed in his own writings the tendency to fanciful exaggeration which was so strong among the men about him (011-003).

In the [[euphuism|Euphuistic style]], tedious and grotesque as it often is, appear the first serious efforts among English prose writers to attain a better mode of expression (011-005). Scott's attempt to portray a euphuist in *The Monastery* bears no resemblance whatever to the followers of John Lyly (045-013), indicating the distinctive nature of the style Lyly embodied.

## Major Works

- **Euphues, the Anatomy of Wit** (1579) — A prose [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]] that established the [[euphuism|Euphuistic style]], characterized by elaborate antitheses, similes drawn from natural history, and ornate language.
- **Euphues and His [[england|England]]** (1580) — A sequel continuing the adventures of the hero.
- **Plays** — Several dramatic works including *Campaspe*, *Sapho and Phao*, *Endymion*, and *Gallathea*, which contributed to the development of Elizabethan comedy.
