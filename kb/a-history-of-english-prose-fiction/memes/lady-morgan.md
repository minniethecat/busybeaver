Lady Morgan (c. 1781–1859), born Sydney Owenson, was an [[irish-fiction|Irish novel]]ist and one of the most prominent literary figures in early nineteenth-century [[ireland|Ireland]]. Tuckerman names her among the notable novelists of [[ireland|Irish]] life and manners, together with Mrs. S. C. Hall, [[gerald-griffin|Gerald Griffin]], and others (042-006).

Her most famous novel, *The Wild [[ireland|Irish]] Girl* (1806), was a national tale that introduced English readers to the culture, history, and landscape of [[ireland|Ireland]]. Written in the form of letters from a young Englishman visiting [[ireland|Ireland]], the novel championed [[ireland|Irish]] identity and contributed to the vogue for national tales in the Romantic period. The book was a remarkable success and made its author a celebrity.

Lady Morgan wrote numerous other novels including *O'Donnel* (1814), *Florence Macarthy* (1818), and *The O'Briens and the O'Flahertys* (1827). She also published travel writings on [[france|France]] and [[italy|Italy]]. In 1812 she married Sir Charles Morgan, a physician, and thereafter wrote under the name Lady Morgan. Her career was marked by both popular success and controversy, as her bold opinions and [[ireland|Irish]] patriotism often provoked criticism from conservative reviewers.
