## Works

Ouida published over 40 novels during her career. Her most famous works include *Under Two Flags* (1867), *Moths* (1880), and *Strathmore* (1865). She was known for extravagant, romantic narratives featuring aristocratic settings, dashing heroes, and sensational plots.

## Reception in Tuckerman

Tuckerman identifies Ouida, alongside [[George A. Lawrence]], as a principal originator of the "Muscular [[novel|Novel]]" — a genre combining the worst elements of fashionable and criminal fiction (044-009). In works like *Strathmore* and *Guy Livingstone*, "the reader is introduced to men of extraordinary physical development, whose strength is proof against the wildest dissipation; to women of extraordinary beauty, whose charms are enhanced in proportion to their coarseness and lack of modesty" (044-009).

Tuckerman acknowledges that Ouida brought "a literary power which has given them considerable notoriety; and has placed them at the head of their particular school; but it is a school whose distinctive characteristics consist in extravagance, unhealthiness of tone, and falseness to nature" (044-010).

## Criticism

Tuckerman's assessment of Ouida is sharply negative. He groups her with writers who "prostituted their talents in pandering to the low tastes of their readers" (047-008), arguing that such authors "have hidden under an outward respectability of words the most immoral and degrading thoughts" (047-008). Her novel *Strathmore* is cited among a list of "objectionable works" that represent "vulgarity and pruriency" in contemporary fiction (047-013).
