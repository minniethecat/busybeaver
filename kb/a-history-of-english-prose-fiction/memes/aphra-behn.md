# Aphra Behn

## Life

Aphra Behn (c. 1640–1689) was born Aphra Johnson, "a gentlewoman by birth, of a good family in the city of Canterbury" (018-015). Her father was appointed to a colonial office in the West Indies, where he took his family while Aphra was still a young girl. There she began "a chequered life by living on a plantation among rough and lawless colonists," and there she made the acquaintance of the enslaved African prince Oroonoko, "whose sad story she afterward made known to the world" (018-016). After returning to [[england|England]], she married a merchant of Dutch extraction named Behn and went to live in the Netherlands, where she served as a British spy. She conveyed intelligence to the English government of a Dutch plan to sail up the Thames and destroy the English fleet, but her warnings were disregarded (018-016). Abandoning espionage, she returned to [[london|London]] and devoted herself to literature. She died in 1689 and was buried in the cloisters of Westminster Abbey, "covered only with a marble stone, with two wretched verses on it" (018-017).

## Literary Significance

Behn holds a unique place in English literary history as the first professional female writer in the language. Her most important work, *Oroonoko* (1688), is "worthy of notice as one of the earliest attempts on the part of an [[novel|English novel]]ist to deal with characters which had come under the writer's observation in actual life" (019-001). It is also notable for containing a didactic purpose—a characteristic "which for good or for evil has been a prominent feature of the [[novel|English novel]]" (019-001). Through the story of the enslaved African prince, "Mrs. Behn protested against the evils of the slave trade through the medium of a story which may be considered a forerunner of *Uncle Tom's Cabin*" (019-002). Tuckerman observes that the novel form gave Behn an advantage a treatise could not: "the novel has the advantage over the treatise, that it deals with the particular and not the general, with the individual and not the nation" (019-003). Behn endowed Oroonoko with "beauty of person and nobility of character" (019-003) and made him "as capable even of reigning well... as any Prince civilized in the most refined Schools of Humanity and Learning" (019-008). Beyond *Oroonoko*, her other fiction consists of short tales "usually founded on fact, which describe in unrestrained language the passion and adventures of a pair of very ardent lovers" (019-008). They "show the prevailing inclination in narrative fiction toward characters and scenes taken from actual life" but "contain no studies of character, and no information of importance concerning contemporary manners" (019-009). Her plays were commercially successful but "characterized by a licentiousness which won for her the title of 'the female Wycherley'" (018-017). Sir [[walter-scott|Walter Scott]] recorded that an old lady of his acquaintance assured him that "in her younger days, Mrs. Behn's novels were as currently upon the toilette as the works of [[maria-edgeworth|Miss Edgeworth]] at present" (019-011). Tuckerman cautions that in judging Behn we "must compare it with the standard of the age to which it belongs, and not with our own" (019-012), noting that "the nineteenth century, with all its well-deserved pride in an advanced refinement and morality, has produced a large number of novelists, both male and female, whose works are as immoral as those of Mrs. Behn, without her excuse" (020-002).

## Major Works

- **Oroonoko; or, The Royal Slave** (1688) — Behn's masterpiece and one of the earliest [[novel|English novel]]s. Based on her experiences in Surinam, it tells the tragic story of an enslaved African prince. The novel was dramatized by Thomas Southern.
- **The Fair Jilt** (1688) — A short novel of passion and intrigue; the most licentious of her tales, its opening lines contain Behn's famous meditation on love.
- **The Lucky Mistake** — Described by Tuckerman as "the best in narrative interest, and the most attractive in tone" of her short fictions; "without the grossness characteristic of Mrs. Behn's works" (019-009).
- **The Lover's Watch** — "The Art of making Love: being Rules for Courtship for every Hour of the Day and Night" (018-017).
- Numerous plays, poems, and translations, including a translation of Fontenelle's *Plurality of Worlds*.
