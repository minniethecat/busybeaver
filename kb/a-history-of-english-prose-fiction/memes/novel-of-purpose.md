# Novel of Purpose

## Definition

The novel of purpose is a work of fiction in which the primary aim is to advocate for a particular social, political, or moral cause. Unlike novels that merely reflect the society they depict, the novel of purpose deliberately uses narrative to argue a position and influence the reader's views. Tuckerman defines it concisely: "The novel of purpose may be defined as a work of fiction designed to advocate some special cause, political or social." (046-006)

## Historical Context

The novel of purpose emerged as a distinct form in the nineteenth century, when the novel had become the dominant literary medium and social reform was a pressing public concern. The expansion of the reading public and the urgency of issues such as industrial poverty, political reform, and religious controversy created conditions in which fiction could serve as an instrument of persuasion.

The form drew on the didactic tradition in English literature while adapting it to the realistic conventions of the nineteenth-century novel. Tuckerman notes that the novel of purpose represented a conscious effort to harness the popularity of fiction for extra-literary ends, though this very purposefulness created artistic risks: a work like [[charles-kingsley|Charles Kingsley]]'s *Alton Locke* "has all the drawbacks of a novel of purpose in an exaggerated form." (036-006)

## Key Characteristics

- **Argumentative design**: The plot and characters are constructed to illustrate and advance a specific thesis or reform agenda.
- **Social or political focus**: The novel addresses identifiable public issues — industrial conditions, religious controversies, political reforms.
- **Representative characters**: Characters often embody social positions or classes rather than functioning as fully individualized portraits.
- **Tension between art and argument**: The form faces an inherent artistic challenge: the didactic impulse can overwhelm the narrative. Tuckerman observes that "the didactic" element can weaken a novel when it comes at the expense of storytelling. (046-010)

A distinctive sub-form is the religious novel of purpose, "written in the interest of some religious body," which Tuckerman identifies as "a curious form of the novel of purpose." (046-007) These works used fiction to advocate for particular theological positions or denominational perspectives.

## Notable Examples

[[harriet-beecher-stowe|Harriet Beecher Stowe]]'s **Uncle Tom's Cabin** (1852) is perhaps the most consequential novel of purpose in English-language fiction, though [[america|American]] in origin. It demonstrated the extraordinary power of fiction to shape public opinion on a major social issue.

Among English examples, [[charles-kingsley|Charles Kingsley]]'s **Alton Locke** (1850) addressed the condition of the working class, though Tuckerman judges it as suffering from "all the drawbacks of a novel of purpose in an exaggerated form." (036-006) The work illustrates both the potential and the peril of the form — its ability to bring serious issues before a wide readership, and its tendency toward oversimplification.

[[elizabeth-gaskell|Elizabeth Gaskell]]'s **Mary Barton** (1848) represented a more artistically successful approach. Tuckerman calls it "a more powerful novel of purpose," suggesting that Gaskell achieved a better balance between social argument and novelistic craft. (046-010) Her work addressed industrial poverty and class conflict without sacrificing character and narrative.

The most important specimens of the form, Tuckerman observes, "are those written in the interest of the people," focusing on social and economic justice rather than narrower sectarian concerns. (046-008) Alongside the novel of purpose, "there has been the novel of utopia," which also uses fiction for social argument but through the construction of imaginary ideal societies. (040-025)
