## Life

James Fenimore Cooper was born in Burlington, New Jersey, in 1789 and grew up in Cooperstown, New York, a frontier settlement founded by his father. He attended Yale College and served in the [[america|United States]] Navy before turning to literature. His early works imitated English models, but he soon found his true subject in the [[america|American]] landscape, history, and national character.

## Literary Significance

With the publication of "The Spy," Cooper opened a thoroughly national vein, and began a literary career which showed how little native genius need rely on foreign influence or on foreign subjects. He described the stirring events and the moral heroism of the [[america|American]] Revolution with patriotic sympathy and original literary power. He touched the romantic chords of that great struggle with a delicacy which met with a world-wide response (045-001).

Not only did [[america|American]]s feel that in Cooper's novels the picturesque and characteristic features of their country were delineated by a master-hand, but in almost every European land, translations of his works testified to the universal interest excited by the examples of simplicity, endurance, and sagacity which formed the subjects of his pen (045-001).

The scenes among which his hero Leatherstocking lived — the primeval forest, the great inland lakes, the hunter's camp, and Indian wigwam — were described by Cooper with a fidelity and picturesqueness which will always give to his works a national value. Now that farms and manufacturing towns cover what a century ago was a trackless wilderness, it would be almost impossible for us to realize the previous condition of the country were it not for the novels of Cooper (045-002).

Cooper painted with equal skill the life of the [[america|American]] sailor, at a time when that life had an interest and excitement it no longer possesses. No marine novelist has given a more life-like impression of a ship than Cooper, and none have excelled him in descriptions of the sea and in studies of those peculiar forms of human nature produced by life on the ocean (045-002).

He also threw into bold relief the stirring incidents of [[america|American]] colonial and revolutionary times in "The Spy" and "The Pioneers" (046-004). So long as Cooper confined himself to purely national subjects, his success was brilliant and continuous; but many of his works show the effect of misdirected talent, and have fallen into neglect (045-002).

## Major Works

- **The Spy** (1821) — A novel of the [[america|American]] Revolution that opened a national vein for [[america|American]] fiction (045-001, 046-004).
- **The Pioneers** (1823) — The first published of the Leatherstocking Tales, depicting frontier life (045-001, 046-004).
- **The Last of the Mohicans** (1826) — The most famous of the Leatherstocking Tales, set during the French and Indian War (045-001).
- **The Prairie** (1827) — Continues the Leatherstocking saga in the Great Plains.
- **The Pathfinder** (1840) and **The Deer-slayer** (1841) — Further Leatherstocking Tales featuring Natty Bumppo (045-001).
- Naval novels including **The Pilot** (1824) and **The Red Rover** (1827) — Marine fiction that captured the excitement of [[america|American]] seafaring life (045-002).
