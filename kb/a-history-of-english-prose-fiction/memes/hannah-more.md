# Hannah More

## Life

Hannah More (1745–1833) was an English religious writer, poet, and philanthropist. She was part of the Bluestocking circle of intellectual women in [[london|London]] and was acquainted with leading literary figures of her time, including [[samuel-johnson|Samuel Johnson]] and David Garrick. Tuckerman groups More with Mrs. Carter and Miss [[frances-burney|Fanny Burney]] as prominent literary women of the period. (037-013) Later in life she devoted herself to philanthropic and educational work, establishing schools for poor children in Somerset.

## Literary Significance

More was a prolific author whose works spanned poetry, drama, and religious and moral essays. Her significance in the history of English fiction lies primarily in her opposition to novels and her influence on the debate about fiction's moral effects. According to Tuckerman, More "in her essays on female education, strenuously objected to novel-reading, and yet she herself was the author of a religious novel." (038-008) This contradiction — condemning fiction while practicing it — illustrates the complex status of the novel in the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries, when fiction was simultaneously enormously popular and widely suspect on moral grounds.

More's *Coelebs in Search of a Wife* (1809), her religious novel, attempted to provide a morally safe alternative to the secular novels she criticized. It exemplified the didactic tradition in English fiction, using narrative form to convey religious and moral instruction.

## Major Works

- **Coelebs in Search of a Wife** (1809) — A didactic religious novel presenting an idealized vision of Christian domestic life, written as an alternative to secular fiction.
- **Essays on Female Education** — Moral and educational essays in which More argued against novel-reading while advocating for women's intellectual development.
- **Strictures on the Modern System of Female Education** (1799) — A treatise on women's education that was highly influential in its time.
