# Washington Irving

## Life

Washington Irving (1783–1859) was an [[america|American]] author, essayist, biographer, and diplomat. Named after George Washington, he began his literary career writing satirical essays before traveling extensively in Europe. He spent seventeen years abroad, during which he produced his most famous works. Irving later served as the U.S. minister to [[spain|Spain]], where he researched and wrote biographical and historical works.

## Literary Significance

Irving was one of the first [[america|American]] writers to achieve an international literary reputation. His praise of English fiction carried particular weight as an [[america|American]] perspective on the English literary tradition. Tuckerman quotes Irving's enthusiastic assessment of a major [[novel|English novel]]ist: "So wrote Washington Irving; and if the reader is inclined to consider these words as the extravagant eulogy of a partial friend, it is only necessary to examine the works of the author in question to be convinced of their entire justice." (035-008)

Irving's treatment of the Hudson River region demonstrated how [[america|American]] settings could be rendered with "all the richness of imagination" that had previously been associated with European landscapes and traditions. (045-003) His use of [[america|American]] settings and folklore helped establish a distinctively [[america|American]] voice in English-language fiction, influencing subsequent [[america|American]] authors like [[james-fenimore-cooper|James Fenimore Cooper]] and [[nathaniel-hawthorne|Nathaniel Hawthorne]].

## Major Works

- **The Sketch Book of Geoffrey Crayon, Gent.** (1819–20) — A collection of essays and short stories including "Rip Van Winkle" and "The Legend of Sleepy Hollow," which adapted European folk tales to [[america|American]] settings.
- **A History of New York** (1809) — A satirical history purportedly written by Diedrich Knickerbocker, establishing Irving's comic voice.
- **Tales of the Alhambra** (1832) — Stories and essays inspired by Irving's residence in the Alhambra palace in Granada, [[spain|Spain]].
