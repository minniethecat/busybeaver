# Samuel Johnson

Samuel Johnson (1709–1784), universally known as Dr. Johnson, was the preeminent literary figure
of eighteenth-century [[england|England]].
His philosophical novel *Rasselas* (1759) made a significant contribution to English prose fiction,
alongside his greater fame as lexicographer and critic.

Tuckerman records how Johnson "lived in penury and drudgery" during his early [[london|London]] years,
a formative experience that shaped his sympathy for struggling writers (020-015).

## Life

Johnson was born in Lichfield, Staffordshire, the son of a bookseller.
After a brief and financially interrupted period at Pembroke College, [[oxford|Oxford]],
he moved to [[london|London]] in 1737 to make his way as a writer.

His early [[london|London]] years were marked by severe poverty (020-015).
By the 1760s, he was the undisputed arbiter of English letters,
the man to whom the first volume of a new [[novel]] was sent "for his opinion" (035-009).

He was counted among the major prose stylists of the age,
grouped with Addison and [[jonathan-swift|Swift]] as one of those
in whom English prose "had become the apt instrument" of expression (020-012).

## Literary Significance

Johnson's single work of fiction, *Rasselas*, was written in the evenings of a single week
to pay for his mother's funeral. The tale begins:
"Ye who listen with credulity to the whispers of fancy...
attend to the history of Rasselas, Prince of Abyssinia" (034-011).

Dr. Johnson "made the repository of so much truth" from this simple framework (034-011).
The narrative follows Rasselas and his sister Nekayah as they escape the Happy Valley
and search the world for a "choice of life" that yields lasting satisfaction (034-013).

Tuckerman notes the resemblance between *Rasselas* and Voltaire's *Candide*,
both published in 1759 and both skeptical of philosophical optimism (035-002).

Johnson was "an enthusiastic admirer" of [[Samuel Richardson|Richardson]]'s fiction (029-006).
His critical judgment carried immense weight in the development of the [[novel|English novel]].

## Major Works

- **Rasselas** (1759) — A philosophical novel exploring the impossibility
  of finding perfect happiness in any human condition.
- **A Dictionary of the English Language** (1755) — The definitive English dictionary.
- **The Lives of the Poets** (1779–1781) — Critical biographies of fifty-two poets.
