# William Beckford

## Life

William Beckford (1760–1844) was born at Fonthill Abbey in Wiltshire, the son of a wealthy alderman who had twice been Lord Mayor of [[london|London]]. He inherited an immense fortune at the age of ten, making him "[[england|England]]'s wealthiest son," as Lord Byron would later call him in *Childe Harold* (036-007). His education was conducted by private tutors, and he showed early gifts for music, art, and languages. Beckford's extravagant tastes and enormous wealth made him a legendary figure, but his life was marked by scandal, including a controversial relationship with William Courtenay that led to his social exile. He spent many years abroad, particularly in Portugal, and devoted his later life to collecting art and building an extraordinary Gothic structure at Fonthill.

## Literary Significance

Beckford's literary reputation rests almost entirely on a single work: *Vathek*, an oriental [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]] written in French in 1782 when the author was only twenty years old, and later translated into English. Tuckerman notes that the work "excited great attention at the time" and that it was "composed in three days and two nights, during which the author never took off his clothes" (036-007). Byron considered this tale "superior to 'Rasselas'" (036-007). The novel "represented the downward career of an oriental prince, who had given himself up to sensual indulgence, and who is allured by a Giaour into the commission of crimes which lead him to everlasting and horrible punishments" (036-007). Tuckerman remarks that "'Vathek' gives evidence of a familiarity with oriental customs, and a vividness of imagination which are remarkable in so youthful an author. The descriptions of the Caliph and of the Hall of Eblis are full of power" (036-007). However, he also judges that "in depth of meaning, and in that intrinsic worth which gives endurance to a literary work, it bears no comparison to 'Rasselas.' The one affords an hour's amusement; the other retains its place among those volumes which are read and re-read with constant pleasure and satisfaction" (036-007). Beckford stands, alongside [[samuel-johnson|Samuel Johnson]] and his *Rasselas*, as one of the eighteenth-century authors who "had drawn on the romantic features of Eastern life" (044-012), contributing to what later became "a recognized department of English fiction" (044-012): the Oriental novel.

## Major Works

- **Vathek** (1786, English translation) — An oriental [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]] following the Caliph Vathek's descent into sensuality, crime, and damnation. Praised by Byron as "superior to 'Rasselas'" and noted for its "vividness of imagination" (036-007). The Hall of Eblis sequence is often cited as a masterpiece of Gothic-influenced Orientalism.
- **Dreams, Waking Thoughts, and Incidents** (1783) — An epistolary travel narrative based on Beckford's European tours.
- **Recollections of an Excursion to the Monasteries of Alcobaça and Batalha** (1835) — A late travel memoir.
