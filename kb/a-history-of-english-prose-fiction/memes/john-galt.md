# John Galt

## Life

John Galt (1779–1839) was born in Irvine, Ayrshire, the son of a sea captain. After an unsuccessful attempt at a commercial career in [[london|London]], he traveled extensively in the Mediterranean and the Levant, experiences he later drew upon in his travel writing and fiction. Galt was also involved in the Canada Company, a colonization venture in Upper Canada, and founded the town of Guelph, Ontario. His later years were marked by financial difficulties, and he returned to literary work to support himself.

## Literary Significance

John Galt belongs to the generation of [[scottish-fiction|Scottish novel]]ists who followed in the wake of Sir [[walter-scott|Walter Scott]]'s extraordinary achievement. Tuckerman places him "following the great Sir Walter in the description of [[scotland|Scottish]] life and manners" alongside a distinguished group of writers (041-015). Galt's particular contribution is noted with precision: "John Galt, in the 'Annals of the Parish,' gave many humorous descriptions of national character" (041-015). This work, which chronicles the life of a rural [[scotland|Scottish]] parish through the eyes of its minister, exemplifies the post-Scott tradition of regional fiction — fiction rooted in the particularities of [[scotland|Scottish]] life, manners, and character that Scott had made familiar to readers throughout the world. Tuckerman observes that the talents of writers like Galt "have kept up the interest which the world has learned to take in every thing appertaining to the land which Sir [[walter-scott|Walter Scott]] taught it to know and love so well" (041-015).

## Major Works

- **Annals of the Parish** (1821) — Galt's most enduring work, a humorous and sympathetic chronicle of [[scotland|Scottish]] parish life narrated by the Reverend Micah Balwhidder; noted by Tuckerman for its "humorous descriptions of national character" (041-015).
- **The Ayrshire Legatees** (1820) — An epistolary novel recounting a [[scotland|Scottish]] family's journey to [[london|London]], notable for its comic observation of cultural differences.
- **The Entail** (1823) — A novel of [[scotland|Scottish]] life exploring family inheritance and obsession with land.
