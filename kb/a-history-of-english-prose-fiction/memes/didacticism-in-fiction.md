# Didactic Purpose in Fiction

## Definition

The didactic purpose in fiction refers to the intention of prose narratives to instruct, reform, or morally educate readers.

From its earliest English examples, prose fiction carried a "didactic purpose; a characteristic which has clung to it more or less closely ever since" (019-001).

This instructive function persists across centuries of English fiction, manifesting in different forms: moral exemplars, social criticism, political argument, and religious instruction.

## Historical Context

The didactic impulse in English fiction emerged as early as the seventeenth century, when prose narratives were frequently justified on moral grounds.

Puritan influence in the seventeenth century reinforced the view that fiction should serve moral instruction rather than mere entertainment (016-002).

By the late seventeenth century, even writers of popular fiction like [[aphra-behn|Aphra Behn]] occasionally produced stories with "a didactic aim or a special interest" (019-008), though such works were exceptions in her oeuvre.

The eighteenth century saw the didactic function become central to the novel's social role, with novelists like Richardson explicitly framing their work as moral instruction.

In the nineteenth century, as the novel matured, writers increasingly sought "to combine a didactic aim with artistic excellence" (046-006), though the balance often tilted toward instruction.

## Key Characteristics

The didactic function in English fiction operates through several modes. It may take the form of explicit moral commentary by the narrator, exemplification through virtuous or vicious characters, or the embedding of sermons and moral essays within narrative structure.

The didactic element distinguishes English fiction from traditions that prioritize pure entertainment or aesthetic achievement.

However, the tension between instruction and art is a recurring challenge: when didactic aims dominate, "the characters and the incidents are much subordinated to the didactic" (046-010), potentially weakening the work as fiction.

## Notable Examples

The most prominent vehicle for didactic fiction in the nineteenth century was the novel of purpose—a genre in which social, political, or religious arguments drive the narrative.

As the corpus notes, "a more powerful novel of purpose" emerged in the Victorian period, addressing issues such as industrial conditions, parliamentary reform, and religious controversy (046-010).

Earlier, [[samuel-richardson|Samuel Richardson]]'s novels exemplify the didactic tradition in the eighteenth century, combining "didactic and artistic purpose" (037-001) through epistolary narratives that dramatize moral choices.
