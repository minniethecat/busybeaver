# Charles Reade

## Life

Charles Reade (1814–1884) was an [[novel|English novel]]ist and playwright. He studied at Magdalen College, [[oxford|Oxford]], and was called to the bar at Lincoln's Inn, though he practiced law only briefly before devoting himself to literature.

Reade began his literary career as a dramatist, and his theatrical experience influenced the dramatic construction of his later novels. He remained a fellow of Magdalen College throughout his life.

## Literary Significance

According to Tuckerman, Charles Reade "began a long series of popular novels" that dealt with a wide range of subjects, generally from the standpoint of the social reformer. (042-020) His first novels included *Peg Woffington* and *Christie Johnstone*. (042-020)

Reade's best-known work, *It Is Never Too Late to Mend*, criticized prison discipline and described the striking scenes of the Australian gold-fields. (042-020) Tuckerman ranks it highly: "Few novels of the present day contain a more interesting story or more lifelike delineations of character." (042-020)

This places Reade within the novel-of-purpose tradition — fiction with explicit reformist intent — a defining characteristic of English prose fiction identified by Tuckerman.

Reade's method was distinguished by extensive documentary research. He accumulated notebooks of newspaper clippings and factual material, weaving documented abuses into fictional narratives. His narrative style retained the vivid, dramatic quality of the popular novelist while serving reformist aims.

## Major Works

Reade's first novels were *Peg Woffington* (1853) and *Christie Johnstone* (1853). (042-020) His most celebrated work, *It Is Never Too Late to Mend* (1856), exposed prison abuses and depicted the Australian gold-fields. (042-020) His historical masterpiece *The Cloister and the Hearth* (1861) offered a panoramic depiction of 15th-century European life.
