# Literary Sketch

## Definition

The literary sketch is a short prose form that offers a concise, vivid portrait of a character, a scene, or a social type. Unlike the full-scale novel, the sketch aims at intensity and immediacy rather than narrative development. It is at once a standalone minor genre and an essential technique within longer works of fiction. As Tuckerman's repeated use of the term suggests, the "sketch of character" was a recognized category of literary achievement throughout the period covered by his history. (027-002)

## Historical Context

The literary sketch has deep roots in English prose. The seventeenth-century "character" — a short prose portrait of a social or moral type — was a direct ancestor of the fictional sketch. The eighteenth-century periodical essay, particularly in the hands of Addison and Steele, further developed the form by embedding brief character sketches within essays on manners and society.

In the nineteenth century, the sketch became a staple of magazine and periodical literature. Authors like [[charles-dickens|Charles Dickens]] published collections of sketches (*Sketches by [[charles-dickens|Boz]]*, 1836) before turning to full-length novels. The form suited the demands of serial publication and a reading public hungry for variety.

Within the novel, the ability to produce memorable sketches of character became a mark of a novelist's skill. Tuckerman repeatedly evaluates novelists by the quality of their character sketches, making it one of his primary criteria of literary merit.

## Key Characteristics

- **Concision**: The sketch captures its subject in a few paragraphs or pages rather than through extended development.
- **Vivid particularity**: The best sketches fix a character or scene in the reader's mind through concrete, sensory detail.
- **Typicality**: Sketches often portray social or moral types — the miser, the coquette, the pedant — as well as unique individuals.
- **Observational quality**: The sketch draws its power from the author's eye for telling detail and characteristic behavior.
- **Standalone and embedded forms**: Sketches could be published independently (in periodicals or collections) or serve as episodes within longer works.

Tuckerman describes Sir [[walter-scott|Walter Scott]]'s characters as "such original sketches of character" that they "have so greatly enriched the literature of the world." (045-010) The phrase captures the sketch's dual nature: it is both a technique of representation and a contribution to the broader literary culture.

## Notable Examples

The Elizabethan period produced what Tuckerman calls "the first specimen of a fine prose style" in which "the life-like sketches of character which will often appear in the course of the narrative" demonstrated the sketch's power even before the novel proper had developed. (015-004) These early sketches showed that English prose could capture individual character with precision and grace.

[[john-bunyan|John Bunyan]]'s **The Pilgrim's Progress** (1678) contains a celebrated example: "the sketch of the pilgrim, which opens the book, is one of the most striking passages in English literature." (016-005) Bunyan's concision — his ability to make a character vivid in a few strokes — prefigured the novelistic character sketch of later centuries.

In the nineteenth century, the character sketch reached its highest development within the novel. Tuckerman singles out a novelist — referred to in terms that suggest [[george-eliot|George Eliot]] — for producing "a more perfect sketch of character than that of Sir Roger de Coverley," the famous character from Addison's *Spectator*. (027-002) The comparison to Addison's celebrated creation shows how the novel had absorbed and surpassed the periodical sketch tradition.

Maritime novelist [[frederick-marryat|Frederick Marryat]] produced "well-drawn and entertaining characters sketched by his hand" in works like *Peter Simple*, demonstrating the sketch's versatility across genres. (044-011) [[james-morier|James Morier]]'s novels contained "highly humorous sketches of Persian life," showing how the form could extend beyond English characters to capture foreign types and manners. (044-012)
