# John Bunyan

John Bunyan (1628–1688) was a Puritan preacher and writer whose *The Pilgrim's Progress* (1678) is the supreme English allegory. Tuckerman marvels "that John Bunyan, a poor, illiterate tinker, was able to take the first place among writers of allegory" (016-003). His work forms a bridge between the medieval allegorical tradition and the realist fiction that would follow.

Along with [[daniel-defoe|Daniel Defoe]] and [[jonathan-swift|Jonathan Swift]], Bunyan gave to English fiction the quality of intense reality: "In the allegory of 'The Pilgrim's Progress,' the sense of reality was produced by the intense realization of the subject by the author, unassisted by any literary device" (027-011).

## Life

Bunyan was born in 1628 near Bedford, the son of a tinker. He served in the Parliamentary army during the Civil War and afterward returned to his trade. His spiritual autobiography, *Grace Abounding in the Chief of Sinners* (1666), records the torments of a conscience struggling toward assurance of salvation.

Tuckerman notes that Bunyan "possessed an imagination the activity of which would have unsettled the reason of any less powerfully constituted man" (016-003). As a child he was "afflicted with thoughts of the Day of Judgment, night and day, trembling at the thoughts of the fearful torments of hell fire" (016-003).

A pivotal moment came during a game of cat (a precursor to cricket), when "a voice did suddenly dart from heaven into my soul, which said, 'Wilt thou leave thy sins and go to Heaven, or have thy sins and go to Hell?'" (016-003). After his conversion, his conscience grew so tender that "I durst not take up a pin or a stick, though but so big as a straw, for my conscience now was sore, and would smart at every touch" (016-004).

Bunyan spent twelve years in Bedford prison for preaching without a license. It was during this imprisonment that he wrote much of *The Pilgrim's Progress*. Of this separation he wrote movingly: "The parting with my wife and poor children hath often been to me in this place as the pulling the flesh from my bones … especially my poor blind child, who lay nearer to my heart than all I had beside" (016-008).

## Literary Significance

Bunyan's genius lay in his ability to give living personality to abstractions. "With a conscience so tender, and a longing so intense for what he considered a condition of grace, Bunyan described the journey of Christian with the minuteness and fidelity of one who had trod the same path" (016-005).

The characters of *The Pilgrim's Progress*—Evangelist, Pliable, Help, Mr. Worldly Wiseman, Timorous, Talkative, Vain Confidence, Giant Despair—are "not mere personifications, but distinct human beings with whom every reader of the 'Pilgrim's Progress' feels an intimate acquaintance" (016-007). The places through which Christian passes—the Slough of Despond, the Wicket Gate, the House of the Interpreter, the Hill Difficulty, the Valley of Humiliation, the Valley of the Shadow of Death, Vanity Fair—have become "familiar localities to many generations of men" (016-007).

> Other allegorists have pleased the fancy or gratified the understanding, but Bunyan occupies at once the imagination, the reason and the heart of his reader. (016-007)

Tuckerman notes that *The Pilgrim's Progress* contains "evidences of a strong human sympathy, and a kindly indulgence on the part of the author for the weak and erring among his fellow-men" (016-008)—a quality that Bunyan's narrow [[puritanism|Puritanism]] had "failed to impair" (016-008).

With Bunyan's allegories, Tuckerman writes, "we leave ideality behind us as a characteristic feature of English fiction" (016-009). The ideal heroes of [[romance-of-chivalry|chivalric romance]] give way to the pilgrim Christian, and after him to the realistic characters of [[daniel-defoe|Defoe]] and the eighteenth-century novelists.

## Major Works

- **Grace Abounding in the Chief of Sinners** (1666) — Spiritual autobiography; a classic of Puritan self-examination (016-003, 028-011).
- **The Pilgrim's Progress** (1678) — [[allegory|Allegory]] of the Christian life; the most widely read English book after the Bible for two centuries.
- **The Holy War** (1682) — [[allegory|Allegory]] of the soul besieged by the forces of Diabolus.
- **The Life and Death of Mr. Badman** (1680) — A dialogue-narrative tracing the career of an unrepentant sinner.
