# Scottish Fiction

## Overview

[[scotland|Scottish]] life and manners have been made familiar to the world by a series of brilliant novelists, first among whom stands the greatest figure in the history of English fiction. (041-008) The Scottish novel tradition, running from [[tobias-smollett|Tobias Smollett]] in the eighteenth century through Sir [[walter-scott|Walter Scott]] and Robert Louis Stevenson in the nineteenth, brought a distinctive regional voice to English-language fiction and exercised an influence far beyond [[scotland|Scotland]]'s borders.

## Historical Context

Previous to the publication of *Waverley* in 1814, [[scotland|Scotland]] was a comparatively unknown land. Even Englishmen had little knowledge of its national habits, traditions, or scenery. To Scotchmen, the history of their country was little more than a skeleton until Scott filled it with flesh and blood, giving it new life and animation. (041-009)

Scott's achievement built on earlier foundations. Smollett had already brought [[scotland|Scottish]] characters and settings into the eighteenth-century novel. But it was Scott who transformed [[scotland|Scottish]] history, landscape, and character into world literature, creating a vogue for [[scotland|Scottish]] subjects that persisted throughout the nineteenth century.

## Key Figures

- **[[tobias-smollett|Tobias Smollett]]** (1721–1771): [[scotland|Scottish]]-born pioneer of the picaresque novel, brought [[scotland|Scottish]] perspectives to [[the-eighteenth-century-novel|eighteenth-century fiction]].
- **Sir [[walter-scott|Walter Scott]]** (1771–1832): The central figure of [[scotland|Scottish]] fiction; created the historical novel with the Waverley series and made [[scotland|Scotland]] a literary landscape of world significance.
- **[[john-galt|John Galt]]** (1779–1839): Chronicler of [[scotland|Scottish]] rural life in novels like *Annals of the Parish*.
- **[[susan-ferrier|Susan Ferrier]]** (1782–1854): [[novel|Novel]]ist of [[scotland|Scottish]] manners, sometimes called the "[[scotland|Scottish]] [[jane-austen|Jane Austen]]."
- **Robert Louis Stevenson** (1850–1894): Brought [[scotland|Scottish]] themes into adventure fiction and psychological [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]].
- **James Barrie** (1860–1937): Depicted [[scotland|Scottish]] rural life with sentimental realism.

## Impact on Fiction

The [[scotland|Scottish]] contribution to English fiction extends beyond individual authors:

- **The [[historical-novel|Historical Novel]]**: Scott's invention of the historical novel transformed fiction across Europe and [[america|America]].
- **Regional Voice**: [[scotland|Scottish]] fiction demonstrated that local traditions, dialect, and landscape could achieve universal literary significance.
- **Dialogue and Dialect**: The use of Scots dialect in fiction enriched English prose with new registers and rhythms.
- **National Identity**: [[scotland|Scottish]] fiction preserved and celebrated national identity within the broader British literary tradition.

Scott's influence on the description of [[scotland|Scottish]] life and manners was so profound that later novelists inevitably worked in his shadow. (041-015)
