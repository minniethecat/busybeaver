# Italy

## Overview

Italy has been a land of peculiar fascination for English writers from the Renaissance onward. In the sixteenth century, Italy was the literary center of Europe — the source of the pastoral [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]], the novella, and the ideals of Renaissance learning that shaped Elizabethan fiction. (011-005) Throughout the subsequent centuries, Italy continued to serve English fiction as a setting for [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]], a destination for the Grand Tour, and a repository of classical and Renaissance culture.

## Literary Significance

The Italian influence on English prose fiction began with translation and imitation. Elizabethan writers like William Painter and Geoffrey Fenton translated Italian *novelle* into English, providing plot material and narrative models. [[john-lyly|John Lyly]]'s *Euphues* and [[philip-sidney|Philip Sidney]]'s *Arcadia* drew heavily on Italian pastoral and courtly traditions.

In the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries, Italy became a favored setting for English fiction. [[gothic-fiction|Gothic novel]]ists like [[ann-radcliffe|Ann Radcliffe]] set their tales of mystery in Italian castles and convents. For the Romantic generation, Italy represented the union of natural beauty, artistic achievement, and historical depth that [[england|England]] seemed to lack after the Industrial Revolution.

## Key Locations

- **Rome**: The eternal city, setting for countless [[novel|English novel]]s of the Grand Tour
- **Venice**: City of mystery and decadence in English fiction
- **Florence**: Center of Renaissance art and Anglo-Italian literary culture
- **The Italian countryside**: Setting for Radcliffe's [[gothic-fiction|Gothic romance]]s and later travel fiction

## Role in Fiction

For English fiction, Italy has typically served as an imaginative counterpoint to [[england|England]]: a land of passion, art, and history against which English character and values could be defined and tested. The Italian landscape and architectural sublime provided Gothic fiction with its essential vocabulary of terror and wonder. (013-001)
