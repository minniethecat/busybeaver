# Charlotte Lennox

## Life

Charlotte Lennox (1720–1804) was born in New York, the daughter of Sir James Ramsay, Lieutenant-Governor of New York. Tuckerman records that "when fifteen years of age she was sent to [[london|London]], and there supported herself by her pen" (037-012). Her literary career was sustained by the patronage of [[samuel-johnson|Samuel Johnson]], who admired her talents and helped her secure support from the literary establishment. Despite Johnson's advocacy, Lennox struggled financially throughout her life, and her later years were marked by poverty. She died in [[london|London]] in 1804, having produced a varied body of work including novels, poetry, translations, and a periodical.

## Literary Significance

Charlotte Lennox occupies a modest but distinctive place in the history of [[the-eighteenth-century-novel|eighteenth-century fiction]]. Her literary reputation was championed by [[samuel-johnson|Samuel Johnson]], who once declared, "I have dined at Mrs. Garrick's with Mrs. Carter, Miss [[hannah-more|Hannah More]], and Miss [[frances-burney|Fanny Burney]]: three such women are not to be found. I know not where I could find a fourth, except Mrs. Lennox, who is superior to them all" (037-013). Johnson's high opinion placed her in the company of the most distinguished literary women of the century. Her most enduring work, *The Female Quixote*, "is an entertaining satire on the old French [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]]s" (037-013). In this novel, the heroine Arabella has absorbed the conventions of [[heroic-romance|French heroic romance]]s so completely that she interprets every event in her life through their lens, much as Cervantes's Don Quixote had done with [[romance-of-chivalry|chivalric romance]]s. The novel was praised by [[henry-fielding|Henry Fielding]], who wrote a favorable review, and it influenced the development of the satirical novel of manners. However, Tuckerman also notes that her other novels, including "Sophia" and "Euphemia," are "without any special interest" (037-013), suggesting that her talent was not sustained across her career.

## Major Works

- **The Female Quixote; or, The Adventures of Arabella** (1752) — Lennox's most significant novel. "An entertaining satire on the old French [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]]s" (037-013) in which the heroine, raised in seclusion and nourished on the heroic [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]]s of Scudéry and La Calprenède, misinterprets the world around her through the lens of [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]] conventions. The novel is a landmark in the satirical tradition bridging Cervantes and [[jane-austen|Jane Austen]].
- **The Life of Harriot Stuart** (1750) — Lennox's first novel, semi-autobiographical.
- **Sophia** (1762) — Published serially in Johnson's *The Lady's Magazine*; Tuckerman judges it "without any special interest" (037-013).
- **Euphemia** (1790) — Her final novel, likewise dismissed by Tuckerman as lacking lasting interest (037-013).
