# America

## Overview

America played a growing role in the world of English prose fiction throughout the nineteenth century: as a market for English books, as a source of distinctively American fiction, and as a subject of fascination for English readers. By the end of the century, American novelists like Hawthorne, Stowe, and James had achieved recognition on both sides of the Atlantic, and the American edition had become an important consideration for English publishers. (028-014)

## Literary Significance

The American contribution to English-language fiction began with [[washington-irving|Washington Irving]], whose *Sketch Book* (1819–1820) was admired in [[england|England]] for its graceful style and gentle humor. [[james-fenimore-cooper|James Fenimore Cooper]]'s Leatherstocking tales brought the American frontier into fiction with epic scope. Later, [[nathaniel-hawthorne|Nathaniel Hawthorne]]'s psychological [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]]s and [[harriet-beecher-stowe|Harriet Beecher Stowe]]'s *Uncle Tom's Cabin* demonstrated the moral power American fiction could command.

The relationship between English and American fiction was reciprocal: [[novel|English novel]]s found an enormous readership in America, while American themes and characters — the frontier, the Puritan heritage, the conflict over slavery — enriched the common literary tradition. (041-009)

## Key Locations

- **New [[england|England]]**: The Puritan heartland, setting for Hawthorne's fiction
- **New York**: Irving's Hudson Valley and the emerging American literary marketplace
- **The Frontier**: Cooper's wilderness, representing American distinctiveness

## Role in Fiction

For English readers, America represented both the familiar (a shared language and literary tradition) and the foreign (democratic institutions, frontier conditions, racial conflict). American fiction brought new settings, new moral questions, and a new democratic sensibility into English-language literature.
