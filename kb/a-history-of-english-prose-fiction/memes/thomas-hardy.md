Thomas Hardy (1840–1928) was an [[novel|English novel]]ist and poet whose work bridges the Victorian and modern periods. Tuckerman lists him among the notable novelists of the later nineteenth century, alongside Trollope and [[james-payn|James Payn]] (044-016). Hardy's name also appears in the index to the volume, referenced with a footnote (048-001).

Hardy is best known for his "Wessex novels," including *Tess of the d'Urbervilles* (1891), *Jude the Obscure* (1895), and *The Mayor of Casterbridge* (1886). These works are set in the fictional county of Wessex, based on the rural region of southwestern [[england|England]] where Hardy was born and lived much of his life. The novels are characterized by their tragic vision, their deep sympathy with ordinary people, and their unflinching portrayal of the constraints imposed by social convention and fate.

After the hostile reception of *Jude the Obscure*, Hardy abandoned novel-writing and devoted the remainder of his career to poetry. He published several volumes of verse, including *Wessex Poems* (1898) and *The Dynasts* (1904–1908), an epic drama of the Napoleonic Wars. Hardy's stature as both novelist and poet has continued to grow, and he is now regarded as one of the central figures of English literature.
