# Imagination in Fiction

## Definition

Imagination in fiction refers to the creative faculty by which authors invent characters, settings, and events that transcend literal reality. In the history of English prose fiction, the imagination has played a shifting role — sometimes allowed free rein, other times subordinated to reason and probability. Tuckerman traces this arc from the [[romance-of-chivalry|medieval romance]]s, which "afforded unlimited range to the imagination," to the [[novel|modern novel]], in which the imagination operates under the discipline of realism. (004-002)

## Historical Context

In the earliest English prose [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]]s, imagination operated without constraint. Tuckerman describes the [[middle-ages|medieval period]] as "a period of uncontrolled imagination, of unsubstantial creations, of the supernatural and the marvelous made familiar." (007-003) The [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]]s of chivalry — with their giants, enchanters, and miraculous interventions — represent "the view of an active but ungoverned imagination." (007-004)

The great turning point came with the Renaissance and the gradual triumph of rationalism. The Protestant Reformation, the rise of experimental science, and the Enlightenment's commitment to reason all worked to subordinate the imagination to more disciplined modes of thought. Tuckerman marks this shift clearly: the modern tendency has been "to subordinate the imagination to the reason of man, to withdraw the attention from the supernatural and to concentrate it on the real." (016-011)

Yet the imagination was never wholly banished from fiction. It found legitimate expression in allegory — Bunyan's *Pilgrim's Progress* — and in the psychological interiority of the eighteenth-century novel. The question was no longer whether imagination had a place in fiction, but how it should be governed.

## Key Characteristics

- **Unfettered creation in [[romance-of-chivalry|medieval romance]]**: The earliest prose fictions exercised imagination without regard for probability. Knights encounter dragons, wizards cast spells, and the laws of nature are routinely suspended. (004-002, 007-003)
- **Allegorical imagination**: In works like *Pilgrim's Progress*, the imagination operates within a structured symbolic framework. Tuckerman notes that "a good allegory requires an imagination of unusual power," since it must sustain a coherent symbolic narrative while remaining accessible to ordinary readers. (016-003)
- **The disciplined imagination of the novel**: The eighteenth-century novel did not abolish the imagination but redirected it toward the representation of probable characters and plausible events. This is what Tuckerman calls "the subordination of the imagination to reason." (020-009)
- **Imagination and sympathy**: The novel's greatest innovation was to harness the imagination for sympathetic identification — enabling readers to enter the consciousness of characters unlike themselves. This moral dimension of the literary imagination became central to the [[the-nineteenth-century-novel|Victorian novel]].

## Notable Examples

The **[[romance-of-chivalry|medieval romance]]s** — particularly the Arthurian cycle collected in Malory's *Morte d'Arthur* — represent the imagination in its most unconstrained form. Tuckerman describes how "the imagination of the peasantry had peopled every mountain and valley with supernatural beings," and the [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]]s drew freely on this folkloric inheritance. (008-009) The *Morte d'Arthur* itself is "the work of an active but ungoverned imagination," creating a world in which the marvelous is accepted without question. (007-004)

**[[john-bunyan|John Bunyan]]'s *The Pilgrim's Progress*** (1678) demonstrates how the imagination could be disciplined to serve allegorical and moral purposes. Bunyan's achievement, Tuckerman writes, is that he "occupies at once the imagination, the reason and the heart of his reader." (016-007) This simultaneous engagement of multiple faculties set a pattern for the [[novel|English novel]]'s ambition to be at once entertaining, instructive, and moving.

**[[daniel-defoe|Daniel Defoe]]** represents the transition to the [[novel|modern novel]]istic imagination. *Robinson Crusoe* (1719) was "a work of imagination, but so matter-of-fact" in its method that readers accepted it as authentic travel narrative. (027-011) [[daniel-defoe|Defoe]]'s genius was to disguise the work of the imagination so thoroughly that it became invisible.
