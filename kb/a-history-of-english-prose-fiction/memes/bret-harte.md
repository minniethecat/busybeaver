## Life

Francis Bret Harte (1836–1902) was an [[america|American]] author and poet born in Albany, New York. He moved to California in 1854, where he worked variously as a teacher, miner, and journalist before achieving literary fame. His experiences in the California mining camps during the Gold Rush provided the raw material for his most celebrated stories. After achieving international recognition, Harte spent his later years in Europe, serving as a [[america|United States]] consul in Germany and living in [[london|London]] until his death.

## Literary Significance

[[novel|Novel]]s descriptive of Western life had been written by several [[america|American]] authors, but none of these writers gave such original sketches of character, or so graphically portrayed the spirit of life in the far West as Mr. Bret Harte. "The Luck of Roaring Camp" and the other stories of this talented writer opened a vein of [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]] where it was least expected (045-010). Harte's stories brought the rough, colorful world of the California mining frontier into [[america|American]] literary consciousness with a distinctive blend of humor, sentiment, and regional authenticity that established the local-color movement in [[america|American]] fiction.

## Major Works

- **_The Luck of Roaring Camp, and Other Sketches_** (1870) — The collection that made Harte internationally famous, including the title story about a baby born in a mining camp.
- **_The Outcasts of Poker Flat_** (1869) — A classic Western story of exiles trapped together by a snowstorm.
- **_Plain Language from Truthful James_** (1870, also known as "The Heathen Chinee") — A satirical poem that became a national sensation.
- **_Gabriel Conroy_** (1876) — A novel of California life, less successful than his short fiction.
