## Life

Henry Brooke was born in County Cavan, [[ireland|Ireland]], in 1703. He was a young [[ireland|Irish]]man of a fine figure, a well-stored mind, and a disposition of particular gentleness. He was loved by [[Alexander Pope]] and Lyttleton, caressed by the Prince of Wales, and honored by the friendly interest of [[Jonathan Swift]]. Married before he was twenty-one to a young girl who presented him with three children before she was eighteen, his life was a constant struggle to provide for a family which increased with every year. After a long period of active life, passed in literary occupations, he retired to an obscure part of [[ireland|Ireland]], and there died, attended by a daughter, the only survivor of twenty-two children (036-002).

## Literary Significance

Brooke's literary reputation rests almost entirely on a single novel: "The Fool of Quality," first published in 1766. The work received a moderate share of public attention upon publication. Its narrative was extremely slight: Harry, the future Earl of Moreland, was stolen from his parents by an uncle in disguise, and the five volumes of the work consist almost entirely of an account of the education of the child and the various incidents which affected or illustrated his mental growth (036-002, 036-003).

The novel achieved an unusual second life when John Wesley chanced to meet with it. Although Wesley required his followers "to read only such books as tend to the knowledge and love of God," he became so enthusiastic over the moral excellence of the work that he expunged some offensive passages it contained and republished it for the benefit of the Methodists. Wesley declared it "the most excellent in its kind that I have seen either in the English or any other language" and praised it for "perpetually aiming at inspiring and increasing every right affection" (036-003).

A century later, [[Charles Kingsley]] revived interest in the novel with an edition carrying his preface. But Kingsley's own critical assessment was conflicted. He praised its "deep and grand ethics" and "broad and genial humanity" (036-004), yet his own description of its faults was devastating: "The plot is extravagant as well as ill-woven, and broken, besides, by episodes as extravagant as itself. The morality is quixotic, and practically impossible. The sermonizing, whether theological or social, is equally clumsy and obtrusive" (036-005). It is singular that Kingsley should have expected that a book with so many and so evident faults could have remained popular simply because its moral was a good one (036-005).

"The Fool of Quality" has all the drawbacks of a novel of purpose in an exaggerated form. Whatever merit it may have is buried under a mass of dulness almost impossible to penetrate, and a silliness pervades the characters and the conversations which makes even the lighter portions unreadable (036-005, 036-006).

## Major Works

- **Gustavus Vasa** (1739) — A tragedy that was banned from the [[london|London]] stage for its perceived political content.
- **The Fool of Quality** (1766–1770) — A five-volume novel of purpose recounting the education of Harry, Earl of Moreland; praised by Wesley for its moral excellence and by Kingsley for its ethics, but criticized for its extravagant plot and obtrusive sermonizing (036-002–036-006).
- **Juliet Grenville** (1774) — A later novel continuing in the sentimental vein.
