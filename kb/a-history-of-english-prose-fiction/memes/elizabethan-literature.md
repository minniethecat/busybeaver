# Elizabethan Literature

## Overview

In the rapidity and scope of intellectual and material progress, the age of Elizabeth is unequaled in English history. The nation seemed to pass from the darkness of night into a sunshine which would never end. This extraordinary period, spanning the reign of Elizabeth I (1558–1603), saw the flourishing of English drama, poetry, and the first sustained experiments in English prose fiction.

## Historical Context

The Elizabethan age was shaped by multiple converging forces: the English Reformation, the expansion of trade and exploration, the defeat of the [[spain|Spanish]] Armada (1588), and a renaissance of classical learning. The printing press made books accessible to a wider audience than ever before, while translations of [[italy|Italian]], French, and [[spain|Spanish]] works introduced new literary models.

The most striking feature of Elizabethan fiction is the great importance attached to style and rhetorical elaboration. (011-004) Writers like [[john-lyly|John Lyly]], with his *Euphues* (1578), developed an ornate, highly figured prose that influenced a generation. [[philip-sidney|Philip Sidney]]'s *Arcadia* (1590) brought pastoral [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]] to English readers with unprecedented artistic ambition.

## Key Figures

- **[[john-lyly|John Lyly]]** (c. 1553–1606): Author of *Euphues: The Anatomy of Wit* (1578), which gave its name to the euphuistic style and established prose fiction as a vehicle for wit and moral instruction.
- **[[philip-sidney|Sir Philip Sidney]]** (1554–1586): His *Arcadia* is the greatest prose [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]] of the Elizabethan period, combining pastoral, chivalric, and political themes with exquisite prose. (011-005)
- **[[robert-greene|Robert Greene]]** (c. 1558–1592): Prolific writer of prose [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]]s and pamphlets, notable for his euphuistic style and pastoral settings.
- **[[thomas-deloney|Thomas Deloney]]** (c. 1543–1600): Brought prose fiction to the merchant and artisan classes with realistic tales of trade and domestic life.
- **Thomas Nashe** (1567–c. 1601): Author of *The Unfortunate Traveller* (1594), a picaresque narrative that anticipated later novelistic realism.

## Impact on Fiction

The Elizabethan period established several enduring foundations for English fiction:

- **Prose as Art**: Lyly and Sidney demonstrated that English prose could achieve the rhetorical sophistication previously reserved for poetry and classical languages.
- **The Romance Tradition**: Sidney's *Arcadia* and the pastoral [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]]s established a tradition of idealized fiction that persisted through the eighteenth century.
- **Realism and Everyday Life**: Deloney's tales of craftsmen and merchants introduced the subject matter of ordinary English life into prose fiction.
- **Translation and Adaptation**: The Elizabethan practice of translating and adapting Continental works enriched the English fictional vocabulary. (013-012)

While Elizabethan prose fiction was later eclipsed by the rise of the novel in the eighteenth century, it established the fundamental premise that prose narrative could be a serious artistic medium.
