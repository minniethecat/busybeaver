# Pastoral Romance

## Definition

The pastoral [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]] is a genre of narrative fiction that depicts an idealized vision of rural life, typically featuring shepherds, nymphs, and noble characters disguised as rustics in a landscape of forests, streams, and meadows. It combines the conventions of [[romance-of-chivalry|chivalric romance]] with the classical pastoral tradition inherited from Greek and Latin poetry. As Tuckerman describes it, the pastoral [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]] was one of the two major tributaries — alongside the [[romance-of-chivalry|chivalric romance]] — that flowed into the development of English prose fiction. (018-001)

## Historical Context

The pastoral [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]] flourished in [[england|England]] during the Elizabethan period, reaching its highest expression in [[philip-sidney|Sir Philip Sidney]]'s *Arcadia*, written around 1580. Sidney "was familiar with the best [[italy|Italian]] models," and his work synthesized "the characteristics of both the chivalric and the pastoral [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]]." (014-005)

The form answered a cultural need in the Renaissance: it provided an idealized literary space where courtly values of love, honor, and refined sensibility could be explored away from the complexities of actual political life. The pastoral setting offered both escape and a subtle mirror for court society.

Both the chivalric and pastoral [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]]s shared a common impulse: "the embodiment of ideals." (018-002) But where [[romance-of-chivalry|chivalric romance]] idealized martial valor, pastoral [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]] idealized contemplative love and the harmony of nature with art.

## Key Characteristics

- **Idealized rural setting**: The action unfolds in an Arcadian landscape — a stylized countryside of shepherds, flocks, and natural beauty.
- **Noble characters in disguise**: Princes and princesses appear as shepherds and shepherdesses, creating a tension between rustic simplicity and courtly sophistication.
- **Love as central theme**: The narrative revolves around romantic courtship, often complicated by disguise, misunderstanding, and rival suitors.
- **Blend of prose and verse**: Pastoral [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]]s frequently intersperse poems and songs within the prose narrative.
- **Moral and philosophical digressions**: The leisurely pace allows for extended discussions of love, honor, and virtue.

Sidney's *Arcadia*, as Tuckerman notes, drew on "the best [[italy|Italian]] models" to create a distinctively English version of the form. (014-005) His work combined the intricate plotting of continental [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]] with a Renaissance humanist's interest in ethics and statecraft.

## Notable Examples

[[philip-sidney|Sir Philip Sidney]]'s **Arcadia** (c. 1580, published 1590) is the preeminent English pastoral [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]]. Written for the entertainment of Sidney's sister, the Countess of Pembroke, it narrates the adventures of two princes, Musidorus and Pyrocles, who disguise themselves to gain access to the princesses they love. The work "was much admired" in its time and went through numerous editions. (014-005)

The pastoral mode also influenced later fiction. Elements of the pastoral [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]] survived in the idealized rural interludes of eighteenth- and nineteenth-century novels, though the pure form declined after the Elizabethan period. Tuckerman notes that the pastoral [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]], along with [[romance-of-chivalry|chivalric romance]], was a direct ancestor of the [[novel|modern novel]]: the novel is "the direct descendant of chivalric and pastoral [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]]." (018-001)
