# Puritanism

## Definition

Puritanism was a Protestant reform movement that arose within the Church of [[england|England]] during the late sixteenth century and reached its peak of influence in the seventeenth century. In the history of English prose fiction, Puritanism is significant not merely as a religious force but as a cultural current whose moral seriousness, introspective habits, and allegorical imagination left a profound mark on narrative literature — above all in the work of [[john-bunyan|John Bunyan]].

## Historical Context

The [[elizabethan-literature|age of Elizabeth]] had been one of intellectual expansion and dramatic brilliance, but it had also inherited "with little qualification the violence, the cruelty, and the unbridled passions of the centuries which had gone before." (011-001) The stage reflected "all that was most base in human nature," and "ecclesiastical discipline had been laid aside." (011-001)

In response, "the Puritans brought about reformation and self-restraint, by enforcing a new code of morals all the more rigid from the looseness which on every side they found to combat." (011-001) Their closing of the theatres in 1642 was driven by "the hatred of God-fearing men against the foulest depravity presented in a poetic and attractive form." (011-001)

Tuckerman devotes an entire chapter to "The Puritans, The Pilgrim's Progress," underscoring the movement's centrality to the development of English fiction. (001-011)

## Key Characteristics

**Moral seriousness**: Puritanism brought a new gravity to English prose. The light, courtly [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]]s of the Elizabethan period gave way to works of earnest moral purpose.

**Allegorical imagination**: The Puritan habit of reading the world as a spiritual allegory — every event a sign, every journey a pilgrimage — found its supreme literary expression in Bunyan's *The Pilgrim's Progress*. Tuckerman ranks it as "the finest allegory in the English language," noting that Spenser had earlier "transferred romantic fiction into the region of allegory, and gave to English literature the immortal 'Faery Queen.'" (004-005, 016-002)

**Introspection**: Puritanism cultivated intense self-examination. Bunyan's *Grace Abounding in the Chief of Sinners* exemplifies this inward turn, recording his spiritual autobiography with a psychological depth that anticipates the novel's interest in interior life.

**Democratic reach**: Bunyan's works reached readers across all social classes. "The child or the laborer reads the 'Pilgrim's Progress' as a record of adventure," while the learned found equal depth. (016-007)

## Notable Examples

**[[john-bunyan|John Bunyan]], *The Pilgrim's Progress*** (1678): "That [[john-bunyan|John Bunyan]], a poor, illiterate tinker, with no education except the reading of the Bible and a few religious works, should have attained the first place among writers of allegory, and to accomplish the extraordinary feat of composing a prose narrative which has ever remained the finest allegory in the English language, is one of the wonders of literature." (016-003, 016-002)

Bunyan's achievement was to fuse the allegorical mode with vivid narrative. "Bunyan occupies at once the imagination and the understanding." Unlike many allegorists who sacrifice story to symbol, Bunyan created characters — Christian, Faithful, Giant Despair — who live as real figures in the reader's mind. (016-007)

**[[john-bunyan|John Bunyan]], *The Holy War*** (1682): Bunyan's second major allegory, also noted alongside *The Pilgrim's Progress* by Tuckerman. (016-008)

Bunyan's influence extended across the centuries: "Bunyan, more than a century later, used the same simple and direct language to reach the common people." (019-002) The introspective tradition he established — the "self-communings" later echoed in [[daniel-defoe|Defoe]] and Richardson (028-011) — became a permanent strand of the [[novel|English novel]].
