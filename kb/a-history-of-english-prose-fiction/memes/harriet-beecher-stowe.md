# Harriet Beecher Stowe

## Life

Harriet Beecher Stowe (1811–1896) was born in Litchfield, Connecticut, into the prominent Beecher family.

Her father, Lyman Beecher, was a well-known Presbyterian minister. She taught at Hartford Female Seminary before marrying Calvin Ellis Stowe, a biblical scholar.

Her exposure to slavery through visits to Kentucky provided material for her most famous novel. By the 1850s she was, alongside [[george-eliot|George Eliot]] and [[jane-austen|Jane Austen]], among the women "ranked among the best works of fiction in any language" (040-026).

## Literary Significance

Stowe's contribution is the novel of purpose—fiction that enlists narrative art in service of a moral cause. *Uncle Tom's Cabin* exemplifies "the capacities of fiction for moving the public mind" (046-010).

The novel's impact was extraordinary. "Within a year, more than two hundred thousand copies were sold in the [[america|United States]], and fully a million in [[england|England]]" (045-008). The book was "dramatized in not less than twenty different forms" (045-008).

Tuckerman notes Mrs. Stowe "gave to her characters a very life-like effect" and placed negro life before the reader "with genuine sympathy and truth" (045-008). Uncle Tom and Topsy are "fine and original creations" (045-009).

Her success "resulted less from literary excellence than from the fact that when one great subject rose pre-eminent in the public mind, she was able to embody it in a popular and easily comprehended form" (045-009).

## Major Works

- **Uncle Tom's Cabin** (1852) — The most prominent Southern-life novel, its circulation "the most remarkable phenomenon in the annals of literature" (045-007).
- **Dred** (1856) — A second anti-slavery novel.
- **The Minister's Wooing** (1859) — Historical novel set in New [[england|England]].
- **Oldtown Folks** (1869) — Regional novel of New [[england|England]] life.
