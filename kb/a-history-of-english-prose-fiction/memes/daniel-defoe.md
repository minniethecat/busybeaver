**Daniel Defoe** (c. 1660–1731) was the first great [[novel|English novel]]ist and a foundational figure in the development of realistic prose fiction.

His narratives established the novel's capacity for verisimilitude through the accumulation of concrete, circumstantial detail. Defoe's career spanned journalism, political pamphleteering, and fiction, with *Robinson Crusoe* (1719) becoming one of the most influential works in English literature. He wrote during the formative period of the [[Novel]] in [[England]] after [[The Restoration]].

## Life

Defoe was born in [[london|London]] around 1660 to a family of Dissenters. His early life was shaped by the religious and political conflicts of Restoration [[england|England]].

He worked as a merchant, journalist, and government agent before turning to fiction in his late fifties. Tuckerman presents Defoe as a figure of remarkable versatility who appears "under the various phases of dissenter, tradesman, pamphleteer, convict, and secret agent" (027-008). His experience in commerce and politics gave him an intimate knowledge of English society that informed his fiction.

## Literary Significance

Defoe's greatest contribution was the development of a narrative technique that made fictional events feel like documented reality.

His power of giving life to fictitious narrative "has never been surpassed by that of any other novelist" (016-007). Unlike earlier [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]]rs who dealt in marvels, Defoe grounded his stories in the ordinary world of trade, travel, and survival. The market price of everyday goods, the practicalities of shelter and food—these concrete details created what Tuckerman identifies as an unprecedented impression of reality in English fiction (021-009).

Defoe, together with [[jonathan-swift|Swift]] and Addison, helped establish the clear, plain English prose style that characterized the eighteenth century (020-012).

## Major Works

| Work | Year | Significance |
|------|------|-------------|
| *Robinson Crusoe* | 1719 | The archetypal survival narrative and novel of individual experience |
| *Moll Flanders* | 1722 | Pioneering first-person narrative of a woman's life in the criminal underworld |
| *A Journal of the Plague Year* | 1722 | Fictionalized documentary of the 1665 plague, blurring fact and fiction |
| *Roxana* | 1724 | Complex study of a woman's moral decline and social ambition |

Defoe's narratives often took the form of authentic memoirs or journals, a technique that gave his fictions the authority of true accounts. His pickpockets and tradesmen are presented with a documentary precision that made the boundary between invention and reportage difficult to discern (024-007).

> "Defoe's power of giving life to fictitious narrative has never been surpassed by that of any other novelist" (016-007).
