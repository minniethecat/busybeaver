## Life

Oliver Goldsmith was born in [[ireland|Ireland]] in 1728 (or 1730 by some accounts), the son of a clergyman. After a somewhat erratic education at Trinity College, Dublin, and a brief study of medicine at Edinburgh and Leiden, he traveled through Europe on foot, supporting himself by playing the flute and disputing at universities.

Settling in [[london|London]], Goldsmith turned to writing as a means of subsistence. He contributed to numerous periodicals and became a member of the celebrated Literary Club alongside [[Samuel Johnson]], [[Joshua Reynolds]], and [[Edmund Burke]]. Johnson was a steadfast friend to Goldsmith. All readers of Boswell know how the *Vicar of Wakefield* found a publisher: how Goldsmith's landlady arrested him for his rent, and how he wrote to Johnson in his distress; how the kind lexicographer sent a guinea at once, and followed to find the guinea already changed and a bottle of Madeira before the persecuted but philosophical author; how Johnson put the cork in the bottle, and after a hasty glance at the manuscript, went out and sold it for sixty pounds (035-002). Goldsmith died in 1774, his last years clouded by debt and illness.

## Literary Significance

Goldsmith's literary achievement spans multiple genres, and he is as much valued for his prose as for his verse (020-012). His novel *The Vicar of Wakefield* stands as one of the enduring works of eighteenth-century English fiction. No novelist has more deeply imbued his work with his own genius and spirit, and none have had a more beneficent genius, nor a more beautiful spirit to impart than the author of "The Deserted Village" (035-006). The exquisite style, the delicate choice of words, the amiability of sentiment, so peculiarly his own, and so well suited to express the simple beauty of his thoughts, give a charm to the work which familiarity can only endear (035-006).

Dr. Primrose, the hero of the novel, preserving his simplicity, his modesty, and his nobility of character alike when surrounded by the pleasures of his prosperous home, when struggling with the hardships of his ruined fortune, and when rewarded by the surfeit of good-fortune which follows his trial, stands high among the most noble conceptions of English fiction (035-007). [[Sir Walter Scott]] declared: "We read the 'Vicar of Wakefield' in youth and in age. We return to it again and again, and bless the memory of an author who contrives so well to reconcile us to human nature" (035-007).

Goethe, in his eighty-first year, declared that Goldsmith's novel was his delight at the age of twenty, that it had in a manner formed a part of his education, influencing his tastes and feelings throughout life (035-008). [[washington-irving|Washington Irving]] recorded that Rogers, the Nestor of British literature, declared that of all the books which, through the fitful changes of three generations he had seen rise and fall, the charm of the *Vicar of Wakefield* had alone continued as at first (035-008).

In the hands of [[daniel-defoe|Defoe]], Richardson, Fielding, Goldsmith, and Miss Burney, the novel reached a high position as a work of art (040-006). Such books as these are to be cherished as precious legacies from the years that have gone before (040-006). For more than a hundred years *The Vicar of Wakefield* has held its own, while hundreds of novels which created more sensation at the time of their appearance have fallen into everlasting oblivion (040-007). The conceptions of a Goldsmith, a Scott, a Dickens, a Thackeray, a [[george-eliot|George Eliot]], remain among the dearest possessions of all English-speaking people (047-013).

## Major Works

- **The Vicar of Wakefield** (1766) — A novel of family life recounting the trials and eventual restoration of Dr. Primrose and his family; celebrated for its charm, gentle humour, and moral beauty.
- **The Deserted Village** (1770) — A pastoral poem lamenting rural depopulation, widely regarded as one of the finest poems of the eighteenth century.
- **The Traveller** (1764) — A philosophical poem reflecting on national character across Europe; the work that first established Goldsmith's poetic reputation.
- **She Stoops to Conquer** (1773) — A landmark comedy of manners that remains a staple of the English stage.
- **The Citizen of the World** (1762) — A series of essays in the form of letters from a Chinese philosopher visiting [[london|London]], offering satirical observations of English society.
