# Humor in Fiction

## Definition

Humor in fiction is the use of wit, comic incident, and humorous characterization to entertain, satirize, and illuminate human nature within narrative prose. In the [[novel|English novel]] tradition, humor serves not merely as ornament but as a mode of perception — a way of seeing and representing the incongruities of social life and individual character. Tuckerman draws a sharp contrast between the humorous and the sentimental traditions, observing of Richardson and Fielding that it was the former's gift "to make his reader weep, and Fielding to make him laugh." (030-005)

## Historical Context

Humor had deep roots in English popular culture — in the jest-books, broadside ballads, and folk tales that predate the novel — but it entered prose fiction proper through the work of the eighteenth-century masters. Before Fielding, Tuckerman notes that the prose [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]]s of the sixteenth and early seventeenth centuries contained "almost no attempt at wit or humor." (006-012) The *Morte d'Arthur*, for all its narrative richness, proceeds with high seriousness throughout.

The decisive turn came with the rise of the novel in the 1740s. [[henry-fielding|Henry Fielding]], drawing on his experience as a playwright and satirist, brought to prose fiction a sophisticated comic vision. Fielding's humor was grounded in his intimate knowledge of [[london|London]] life and his professional experience as a magistrate. Tuckerman observes that "Fielding, himself a police justice, made the corruptions of the age his peculiar study." (024-006) This exposure to the full range of human folly — from the petty to the criminal — gave his humor its characteristic blend of amusement and moral seriousness.

## Key Characteristics

- **Comic characterization**: The humor of Fielding and Smollett arises from vividly drawn characters whose speech, mannerisms, and actions reveal their essential natures. Tuckerman notes that in Smollett "there is an abundance of clever portraiture, and an exuberance of broad humor which inspire interest even when the narrative flags." (031-011)
- **Satirical intent**: Humor in the eighteenth-century novel is rarely an end in itself. It serves a moral purpose, exposing vice and folly to corrective laughter. Fielding, Smollett, and Sterne all wrote within this Horatian tradition of comic moral instruction.
- **Vitality and excess**: The comic mode in [[the-eighteenth-century-novel|eighteenth-century fiction]] is characterized by an energy that sometimes overflows formal boundaries. Tuckerman describes Smollett's world as one of "coarse adventures and coarse humor," yet insists that "he is yet always true to nature." (032-001)
- **Integration of humor and pathos**: In Sterne especially, humor and sentiment are inseparable. The same passage that provokes laughter may, a paragraph later, move the reader to tears. This unstable mixture became one of the distinctive achievements of eighteenth-century comic fiction. (034-011)

## Notable Examples

**[[henry-fielding|Henry Fielding]]** established the comic novel as a major literary form. His *Tom Jones* (1749) represents the fullest expression of his comic vision — a panoramic survey of English society organized around the adventures of a high-spirited hero. Fielding's humor is inseparable from his moral intelligence: "Fielding had a quick eye for the ridiculous, and humor that it is impossible not to enjoy." (032-006)

**[[tobias-smollett|Tobias Smollett]]** brought to the novel a coarser but equally vital comic energy. His *Humphrey Clinker* (1771) is, in Tuckerman's judgment, "the most humorous" of his works, while *Roderick Random* is "the simplest and most direct." (031-013) Smollett's humor derives from his vivid rendering of eccentric characters and his unflinching depiction of the body and its indignities.

**[[laurence-sterne|Laurence Sterne]]** transformed the possibilities of literary humor. In *Tristram Shandy* (1759–1767), humor becomes a structural principle rather than a local effect — the novel's digressions, its typographical experiments, its refusal of conventional chronology are all expressions of a fundamentally comic view of human experience. Tuckerman identifies Sterne as "a humorist" to be "classed with the greatest," whose originality transformed the resources of comic fiction. (034-008)
