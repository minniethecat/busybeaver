# Literary Imitation

## Definition

Literary imitation is the practice by which authors deliberately model their works on earlier or foreign literary examples. Unlike plagiarism, which conceals its sources, imitation openly adopts established forms, styles, and conventions as a means of learning, adaptation, and creative development. Throughout the history of English prose fiction, imitation has functioned as a primary mechanism of literary transmission and evolution.

## Historical Context

Imitation was central to the formation of English prose fiction. Medieval [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]] writers imitated French and [[italy|Italian]] models; Elizabethan authors imitated classical and continental works; eighteenth-century novelists imitated each other and their predecessors. Tuckerman notes that "although many imitations and translations appeared in [[england|England]] for more than a century after the 'Morte d'Arthur' was produced," the native tradition only gradually found its own voice. (004-006)

In the Elizabethan period, the success of [[john-lyly|John Lyly]]'s *Euphues* "excited much imitation, and its influence is strongly felt throughout the literature of the period." (012-006) The euphuistic style became a fashion because other writers saw its popularity and sought to reproduce its effects. Imitation was not merely copying but a form of participation in a shared literary culture.

The practice persisted through the eighteenth century. [[daniel-defoe|Daniel Defoe]]'s *Robinson Crusoe* spawned "calculated to attract imitation," as other writers recognized the appeal of the realistic adventure narrative. (027-001) Smollett, Sterne, and other mid-century novelists each had their imitators. As Tuckerman observes of Sterne, "his borrowed plumage and his imitation of Rabelais' style apart," his original genius still shone through. (034-008)

## Key Characteristics

- **Deliberate modeling**: Authors consciously adopt forms, styles, or conventions from admired predecessors or foreign models.
- **Creative adaptation**: Successful imitation transforms its source rather than merely reproducing it. Tuckerman distinguishes between mechanical copying and genuine creative engagement.
- **Fashion and popularity**: Imitation often follows commercial success. When a work becomes popular, others rush to reproduce its winning formula.
- **Cross-cultural transmission**: Imitation of foreign works (French, [[italy|Italian]], [[spain|Spanish]]) was crucial to the development of English fiction.

Tuckerman observes that even the greatest authors participated in imitative relationships. "He must have been suspected of imitation," he writes of one major novelist, adding: "But while both these great men were undoubtedly original in the highest sense, the similarity of their methods suggests a common inheritance of literary tradition." (035-002)

## Notable Examples

The influence of *Euphues* (1579) generated a wave of euphuistic writing across Elizabethan [[england|England]]. Lyly's antithetical style "is well illustrated by the following speech," Tuckerman notes, and "for many years traces of its influence appear in all varieties of English prose." (012-006)

In the eighteenth century, imitation became a recognized literary strategy. [[henry-fielding|Henry Fielding]]'s *Joseph Andrews* (1742) began as an imitation — and parody — of [[samuel-richardson|Samuel Richardson]]'s *Pamela*. Tuckerman notes that even *Robinson Crusoe* inspired numerous imitations: [[daniel-defoe|Defoe]]'s "creations were calculated to attract imitation." (027-001)

The [[gothic-fiction|Gothic novel]], too, illustrates the power of imitation. [[horace-walpole|Horace Walpole]]'s *The Castle of Otranto* spawned countless imitations: [[clara-reeve|Clara Reeve]], [[ann-radcliffe|Ann Radcliffe]], and their followers each built upon and transformed the Gothic template. Even later, "traces of imitation" could be found in works like Godwin's *St. Leon* and *Mandeville*. (036-010)

The tradition of imitation declined as Romantic ideals of originality took hold, but the practice remained a fundamental force in literary history. As late as the nineteenth century, Thackeray's *The History of Pendennis* and other novels contained characters who were "clever sketches" in the imitative tradition. (042-019) Some works verged on the derivative: one novel was judged "a poor imitation of 'Don Quixote'" used "as a satire on the state of [[ireland|Ireland]]." (044-020)
