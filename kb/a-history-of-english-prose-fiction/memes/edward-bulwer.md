## Life

Edward Bulwer-Lytton was born in [[london|London]] in 1803 into a family of high rank. Educated at Cambridge, he began his literary career with poetry before turning to fiction. Born in a high rank, he was perfectly at home in his descriptions of the upper classes, and never slow in exposing their vices (043-003). His political career included serving as a Member of Parliament and as Secretary of State for the Colonies. He was created Baron Lytton in 1866 and died in 1873.

## Literary Significance

Among the most famous writers of fiction of the nineteenth century will always be mentioned the name of Sir Bulwer Lytton. More than any other writer, he studied and developed the novel as a form of literature (043-001). While almost every novelist took some special field and confined himself to that, Lytton attempted, and successfully, every department of fiction (043-002).

His studies of men took so universal a form that he became familiar even with the slang terms of pickpockets and house-breakers (043-003). Sir Bulwer Lytton is one of the most voluminous writers of a very prolific class, and yet he has never repeated himself. He not only forced his imagination to continuous labor, but he was able to insure an unending novelty of conception. In each one of his novels we are introduced to an entirely new set of characters inhabiting quite unfamiliar scenes (043-003).

In the field of historical fiction, his "Last Days of Pompeii" and "Harold, the Last of the Saxon Kings" are both powerful, ingenious, and interesting narratives, giving as definite an idea of the times of which they treat as is possible after so long a lapse of time (046-001). The former work required far more historical research and a far greater effort of the imagination than [[William Makepeace Thackeray]]'s "Henry Esmond," though the reader cannot divest himself of a certain sense of unreality in Lytton's novel (046-001).

Lytton's "Zanoni," a peculiarly fanciful work, unfolds the mysteries of the Rosicrucians (046-011). His novel "Paul Clifford" occupies a notable place among the eminent criminal novels (044-007). Alongside [[Maria Edgeworth]], [[Anthony Trollope]], and others, Lytton's works include novels which deal with fashionable life (044-006).

## Major Works

- **Pelham** (1828) — A fashionable novel that established Lytton's early reputation and influenced the style of fashionable society.
- **Paul Clifford** (1830) — A criminal novel that criticized the penal system, famously beginning with the line "It was a dark and stormy night."
- **Eugene Aram** (1832) — A novel based on the true story of a scholar convicted of murder.
- **The Last Days of Pompeii** (1834) — A powerful and ingenious historical narrative set in ancient Rome at the time of the eruption of Vesuvius (046-001).
- **Rienzi** (1835) — A historical novel set in medieval Rome that leaves a great impression of verisimilitude (046-001).
- **Harold, the Last of the Saxon Kings** (1848) — A historical novel of the Norman Conquest (046-001).
- **Zanoni** (1842) — A peculiarly fanciful work that unfolds the mysteries of the Rosicrucians (046-011).
- **The Last of the Barons** (1843) — A striking view of declining feudalism, though somewhat clogged by its superabundance of historic incident (046-001).
- **What Will He Do With It?** (1858) — Combines examples of the heroic, the humorous, the pathetic, and the villainous, affording perhaps the best general view of the author's varied talents (043-003).
