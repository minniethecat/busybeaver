# Joseph Addison

Joseph Addison (1672–1719) was an English essayist, poet, and statesman whose contributions to *The Spectator* (1711–12) mark, in Tuckerman's view, the true beginning of the [[novel|English novel]]. "With Addison," he writes, "nature becomes the subject of fiction, and the novel is begun" (027-007).

Together with [[samuel-richardson|Richard Steele]], Addison transformed the periodical essay into a vehicle for character study and moral instruction. His creation of Sir Roger de Coverley—a fully realized fictional character—represents a crucial step from the abstract types of earlier literature toward the psychological particularity of the [[novel|modern novel]].

## Life

Addison was born in Milston, Wiltshire, in 1672 and educated at Charterhouse and [[oxford|Oxford]]. His literary gifts attracted the attention of the Whig leadership, and under Queen Anne he rose to the position of Secretary of State (020-015). Unlike many of his literary contemporaries—[[jonathan-swift|Swift]] received a deanery, Steele became a member of Parliament—Addison achieved both political and literary eminence during his lifetime.

Tuckerman contrasts Addison with [[jonathan-swift|Jonathan Swift]]: "[[jonathan-swift|Swift]], misanthropic, miserable, bitter; Addison, happy, loving mankind, admired alike by ally and opponent … The same contrast is in their works" (026-011). Where [[jonathan-swift|Swift]] dwelt on evil and exposed it "in more than its own deformity," Addison "exerted all his talents to correct it" (026-011).

Addison died in 1719 at the age of forty-seven—calm and conscious, employing his last moments "to ask pardon of one he had offended" (026-011).

## Literary Significance

Addison's declared aim in *The Spectator* was "to banish vice and ignorance out of the territories of Great [[england|Britain]]" (027-001). He attacked the licentiousness, swearing, gambling, duelling, and drunkenness of the age with both "solemn reproof and delicate raillery" (027-001). His particular achievement, as Tuckerman notes through the French critic Taine, was that he "made morality fashionable" (027-001).

To accomplish this, Addison did more than expose vice—"he held up to the public view characters who exemplified his teachings, and were calculated to attract imitation" (027-001). This representational turn was the decisive step toward the novel.

> Addison's knowledge of human nature, and his skill in delineating it in single touches, place him in the front rank of writers of fiction, notwithstanding the limit of his contributions to this department of literature. (027-003)

The Quaker who reproves the insolent captain—"Thy mirth, friend, savoureth of folly"—and Will Wimble, the poor relation, are rendered in a few words with full individuality.

### Sir Roger de Coverley

Sir Roger is Addison's greatest creation—"not to be described by any pen but that of Addison" (027-004). He embodies "a perfect simplicity" joined to "the qualities of a just, honest, useful man, and delightful companion" (027-004). The reader's acquaintance with him is personal rather than literary: we know his ancestry, his estate, his long-standing love affair with the beautiful widow, "all his thoughts, opinions, and surroundings" (027-005). Tuckerman notes that Addison "dwelt with tenderness on every detail regarding him, and finally described Sir Roger's death to prevent any less reverential pen from trifling with his hero" (027-005).

## Major Works

- **The Spectator** (1711–12) — The periodical co-authored with Steele, containing the De Coverley papers and moral essays.
- **The Tatler** (1709–11) — Predecessor to *The Spectator*, founded by Steele with Addison's contributions.
- **Cato** (1712) — A tragedy on the Roman republican hero.
- **The Campaign** (1704) — A poem celebrating Marlborough's victory at Blenheim.
