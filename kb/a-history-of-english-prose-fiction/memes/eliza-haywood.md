# Eliza Haywood

## Life

Eliza Haywood (c. 1693–1756), born Elizabeth Fowler, was an [[novel|English novel]]ist, playwright, and actress. She was one of the most prolific writers of the early eighteenth century and, alongside [[Aphra Behn]] and Delarivier Manley, is counted among the earliest female novelists in English literature. (031-001)

## Literary Significance

Haywood's fiction belongs to the tradition of amatory fiction established by her predecessors. Tuckerman notes that "[[aphra-behn|Mrs. Behn]], Mrs. Manley, and Mrs. Heywood, the earliest female novelists, produced the most inflammatory and licentious novels of their time" (038-006). Her early work shared the coarseness characteristic of the period: "In the days of [[aphra-behn|Mrs. Behn]], Mrs. Heywood, Fielding, or Smollett, coarseness of thought and language was so general that it naturally had a prominent place in novels" (040-027).

## Major Works

Haywood's most significant contribution to the development of English fiction was her later novel **Miss Betsey Thoughtless** (1751). Tuckerman describes it as "among the earliest specimens of a domestic novel; the plot has interest, and the characters are life-like" (028-014). The novel "was exceedingly popular, and had some merit" and "is believed to have suggested to Miss Burney some of the incidents in 'Evelina'" (028-014). However, it also "illustrates, if any illustration were needed, the prevailing absence of any elevated view, either of love, or of the relations between men and women" (028-014).
