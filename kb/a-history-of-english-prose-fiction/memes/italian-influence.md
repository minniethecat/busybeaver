# Italian Influence on English Fiction

## Definition

[[italy|Italian]] influence on English fiction refers to the literary and cultural impact of [[italy|Italian]] narrative traditions on the development of English prose fiction.

This influence, described in the corpus as "the beginning of an extensive [[italy|Italian]] influence" (011-005), shaped English fiction from the Elizabethan period through the eighteenth century, affecting narrative forms, subject matter, and literary taste.

## Historical Context

The [[italy|Italian]] influence on English fiction began in earnest during the [[elizabethan-literature|Elizabethan era]], when English literature was transformed by continental models.

[[italy|Italian]] tales and [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]]s circulated widely: "The [[italy|Italian]] tales were issued in various forms" (015-005), serving as source material for English writers.

This influence coincided with a broader European cultural orientation—Elizabethan fiction reflected a cosmopolitan literary culture (011-003).

By the eighteenth century, [[italy|Italian]] writers had become "familiar in [[england|England]]" (031-001), and their narrative conventions were absorbed into the emerging [[novel|English novel]].

## Key Characteristics

[[italy|Italian]] influence manifests in English fiction through several distinct channels.

First, [[italy|Italian]] narrative forms—particularly the novella and the [[romance-of-chivalry|romance]]—provided structural models for English prose.

Second, [[italy|Italian]] settings and characters became stock elements of English fiction, as seen in stories drawing on "the [[italy|Italian]] origin of the story" (013-001).

The influence was not merely imitative; English writers adapted [[italy|Italian]] models to native tastes and moral frameworks.

However, Puritan critics viewed "the volumes of classical and [[italy|Italian]] writers" (016-002) with suspicion, associating continental literature with moral laxity.

## Notable Examples

The [[italy|Italian]] novella tradition, particularly the works of Boccaccio, provided plot material and narrative techniques for English writers.

In the eighteenth century, the influence of [[italy|Italian]] models persisted even as the [[novel|English novel]] developed its own identity.

Writers such as [[ann-radcliffe|Ann Radcliffe]] adapted [[italy|Italian]] settings to create atmospheric narratives of suspense (032-005).
