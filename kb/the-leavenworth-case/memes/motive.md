# Motive

In criminal law and detective work, motive is the underlying reason that impels a person to commit a crime. It is distinct from means and opportunity — one may have the ability and the chance to commit a crime, but without motive, the case remains incomplete. The search for motive drives much of the investigation in The Leavenworth Case.

## The Centrality of Motive

From the very beginning, the investigators recognize that the murder of Horatio Leavenworth must have a discernible motive. The victim was a wealthy and benevolent man, making the crime initially baffling:

> Mr. Leavenworth was too benevolent a man, too much respected and beloved, to have enemies. Still, if he had been murdered, there must have been some motive. (001-024)

This becomes the recurring refrain of the investigation. Detective Gryce systematically pursues motive as the key to identifying the killer, building a chain of reasoning from financial and personal circumstances.

## Financial Motive — The Will

The primary motive explored in the novel is financial gain through inheritance. The will of Horatio Leavenworth becomes a central piece of evidence:

> I can show the existence of motive; and I can likewise show it was of a character both emphatic and deep. (024-074)

When the altered will is discovered — leaving the bulk of the estate to Mary Leavenworth rather than Eleanore — the financial motive shifts from one sister to the other. The detective acknowledges the power of this motive:

> "The motive was the usual one of self-interest," he said quietly. (024-076)

## Personal and Emotional Motives

Beyond financial gain, the novel explores more complex emotional motives. The secret marriage between Eleanore Leavenworth and Henry Clavering complicates the motive analysis. Mr. Gryce explains:

> Would constitute an adequate motive for so great a crime. (016-006)

And later:

> The immediate motive of the tragedy; a bit of proof that would settle the whole business. (026-065)

The narrator, Mr. Raymond, wrestles with the moral implications of Mary's silence, trying to find a charitable explanation:

> She does it only from motives of kindness towards one less fortunate than herself. (017-038)

## The Detective's Method

Mr. Gryce's investigative method treats motive as one element in a triad with means and opportunity. He explains this framework to Raymond:

> The existence of motive is not enough, even though taken with the fact of opportunity. (026-046)

This principle — that motive alone does not convict — echoes the novel's broader theme about the danger of circumstantial reasoning. The detective must establish not only that a person had reason to kill, but that they actually did so.
