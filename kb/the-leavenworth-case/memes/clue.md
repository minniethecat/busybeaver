# Clue

A piece of information or physical evidence that suggests a direction for investigation. In detective methodology, clues are the raw material from which theories are built, tested, and either confirmed or discarded. The Leavenworth Case follows the detective Ebenezer Gryce as he assembles and follows clues toward the truth.

## The Detective's Method

Detective Gryce articulates the importance of early clues to the investigation:

> Everything depends upon getting the right clue at the start. (001-058)

This principle guides the entire investigation. The initial clues — the position of the body, the missing key, the circumstances of the locked room — establish the framework within which all subsequent reasoning occurs.

When a significant clue finally emerges during the inquest, the response is immediate:

> Sensation. A clue, then, at last! Something had been discovered which gave promise of a speedy solution to the mystery. (004-074)

## Physical Clues

The novel's physical clues include the murder weapon (a pistol), the key to the library, and various domestic objects that acquire evidentiary significance. The detective examines each with care:

> There must be some mystery in regard to this key which must be looked into. (003-197)

Physical evidence is treated not as conclusive in itself but as a starting point for inference — a doorway rather than a destination.

## Following the Clues

The investigative narrative traces the detective's method of following clues from one to the next:

> "Why, next I followed up the clue by going to the Hoffman House." (022-058)

> "If I should succeed in getting at the clue of his connection with this family." (022-060)

Gryce pursues unpromising leads with professional persistence:

> I have been, for the last two months, sifting evidence, studying facts, and following unpromising clues. (036-018)

## Clues as Narrative Device

In the structure of the novel, clues serve a dual purpose: they advance the detective's investigation and also guide the reader through the mystery. Anna Katharine Green pioneered the technique of placing clues in plain view while concealing their significance — a device later used by Conan Doyle, Christie, and the entire detective fiction tradition.

The detective explains the intellectual challenge of clue interpretation:

> "The clue to this murder is supplied by the paper on which the confession is written." (035-004)

And earlier:

> "If you can explain this fact, it will supply us with the clue we want." (035-010)

## The Limits of Clues

The novel is careful to show that clues alone do not solve crimes — they require interpretation. Mr. Gryce warns:

> "No; clues such as are given here must be followed by the mind." (034-208)

A clue may point in many directions; only careful reasoning can determine which direction is correct. The detective who merely collects clues without understanding them is no better than a cataloguer of unrelated facts.
