# New York City

The metropolitan setting of *The Leavenworth Case*. Anna Katharine Green situates her detective story in the specific social geography of 1870s New York — its Fifth Avenue mansions, downtown law offices, hotels, churches, and the neighborhoods where wealth and poverty intersect. Unlike the abstract settings of some earlier mysteries, Green's New York is concrete and mappable: each location carries specific social meaning that the detective [[ebenezer-gryce]] must navigate to solve the [[the-murder-of-horatio-leavenworth|murder of Horatio Leavenworth]].

## The Leavenworth Mansion

The mansion on Fifth Avenue is the gravitational center of the novel. It represents the world of inherited wealth, but also the isolation that wealth can create:

> The house was one of the finest on the avenue, a large, double-fronted mansion of dark stone, with a pillared entrance. (001-008)

The mansion's internal geography — the library where Horatio Leavenworth dies, the parlors where the inquest is held, the servants' quarters — becomes a map of the investigation itself.

## Historical Context

The New York of the 1870s was a city of explosive growth and stark contrasts. The post-Civil War economic boom had created immense fortunes, while immigration and industrialization transformed the urban landscape. Green's novel captures a city where old certainties about class and character are being tested by the anonymity and mobility of modern urban life.

The detective novel, as a genre, is closely tied to the city. Detection requires anonymity, mobility, and the mixing of social worlds that only the modern metropolis provides. In Green's hands, New York is both a realistic setting and a symbolic space — a labyrinth where truth must be discovered through methodical investigation.
