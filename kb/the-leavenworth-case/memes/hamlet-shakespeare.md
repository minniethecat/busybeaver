# Hamlet

*Hamlet* is William Shakespeare's tragedy of the Prince of Denmark, written circa 1600. The play is referenced in chapter epigraphs throughout *The Leavenworth Case*, where its exploration of truth-seeking, feigned and real madness, and the moral weight of revenge provides a literary framework for Anna Katharine Green's detective narrative.

## Relevance to the Novel

*Hamlet* is, in many ways, a precursor to the detective story — a narrative structured around the investigation of a murder, the testing of evidence, and the psychological toll of knowledge. Green's use of *Hamlet* epigraphs signals her novel's participation in this tradition and invites the reader to consider the Leavenworth murder through the lens of Shakespearean tragedy.

## Thematic Parallels

### Investigation as Moral Burden

Both Hamlet and [[ebenezer-gryce]] carry the weight of discovering and proving a hidden truth. Hamlet's famous hesitation — his need to be certain before acting — parallels Gryce's methodical approach to gathering evidence before making an accusation.

### Women and Suspicion

Ophelia's disintegration under the pressure of male suspicion and manipulation echoes the experience of [[eleanore-leavenworth]], whose silence and suffering are misinterpreted as guilt by those investigating her.

### The Problem of Appearance

The play's central question — how to distinguish truth from appearance — is also the detective's fundamental challenge. Hamlet's "antic disposition" and the novel's suspects' strategic silences both illustrate the difficulty of reading human behavior, a theme central to [[detection]] as a discipline.

## Epigraph Function

Green's use of Shakespeare epigraphs at chapter heads serves multiple purposes. They establish literary pedigree for a genre (detective fiction) that was still establishing its cultural legitimacy. They provide thematic commentary on the action. And they create a conversation between the Elizabethan and the contemporary, suggesting that the moral questions raised by murder — guilt, justice, the possibility of redemption — transcend historical period.
