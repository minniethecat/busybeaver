# Discovery of the Altered Will

The revelation, during the murder investigation, that Horatio Leavenworth had changed his will shortly before his death. The original will named Mary as his sole heiress; the altered will shifted the bulk of the estate to Eleanore. This discovery fundamentally recasts the question of motive.

## The Original Will

At the start of the novel, Mary Leavenworth is publicly understood to be her uncle's heiress:

> She had already been declared his heiress. (001-033)

This fact provides a clear financial motive for the crime: if Mary killed her uncle, she would inherit. But the discovery of the altered will complicates this reasoning.

## The Changed Document

The altered will was drafted shortly before Horatio Leavenworth's death — a timing that is itself suspicious. The document shifts the inheritance from Mary to Eleanore, suggesting that Leavenworth had learned something that changed his intentions.

The question of when Mary knew of the changed will becomes central. If she knew she had been disinherited, her motive for murder evaporates. If she did not know — if she killed believing she would inherit — the altered will represents a bitter irony.

## Investigation and Implications

The will becomes a question of first importance:

> The will of Horatio Leavenworth became a question of the first importance. (016-001)

The investigation must determine not only the contents of the will but the timeline of its alteration and the knowledge that various parties had of it. The will is both a legal document and a key to understanding motive.
