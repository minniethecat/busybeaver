# Q

A subordinate agent in Ebenezer Gryce's investigative team, Q is memorably described by his superior as "a living interrogation point." His role in the investigation involves covert surveillance and intelligence gathering.

## Character and Role

Gryce introduces Q with a characteristic quip:

> He is an agent of mine who is a living interrogation point; so we call him Q. (014-109)

The name is both a joke and a description: Q's function is to ask questions — to probe, to follow, to extract information. Unlike Gryce, who operates through direct examination and intellectual synthesis, Q works in the field, tracking suspects and gathering the raw intelligence that Gryce then analyzes.

## Investigative Function

Q represents the operational arm of the investigation. While Gryce theorizes, Q executes. His surveillance of Henry Clavering and other figures provides Gryce with the factual basis for his deductions. The division of labor — Gryce as the intellectual detective, Q as the field agent — anticipates the later convention of the detective-with-assistant pairing that characterizes Holmes and Watson, Poirot and Hastings, and many subsequent duos.

## Narrative Significance

Though a minor character, Q embodies the institutional dimension of detection. The investigation is not merely Gryce's intellectual exercise — it is a police operation with resources, personnel, and a chain of command. Q's presence reminds the reader that detection is a profession, not merely a hobby.
