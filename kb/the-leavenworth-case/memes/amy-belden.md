# Amy Belden

The proprietress of a boarding house whose connection to the case emerges in the later stages of the investigation. Her testimony provides crucial evidence connecting the movements and actions of key figures in the mystery.

## The Boarding House

Mrs. Belden's establishment serves as a waypoint in the investigation. The detective's interest in her premises is connected to the movements of Henry Clavering and other figures on the night of the murder. The boarding house — a liminal space between public and private — is an appropriate setting for revelations that bridge these two spheres.

## Her Testimony

Mrs. Belden's narrative, delivered to the investigators, provides one of the novel's key expository sequences. Her account fills gaps in the timeline and explains connections that had been obscure. As a witness, she is notable for her directness and the specificity of her recollections.

The boarding-house keeper — like the servant — occupies a social position that allows her to observe without being observed in return. Her testimony is valuable because she was present at crucial moments without being suspected of involvement.

## Role in the Resolution

Mrs. Belden's evidence becomes important in the final stages of the investigation. Her testimony helps establish the sequence of events that led to the murder and confirms the detective's deductions about the movements of the principal figures.
