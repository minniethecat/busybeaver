# Horatio Leavenworth

A wealthy and respected New York gentleman whose murder by pistol shot in his own library is the crime that drives the entire narrative of The Leavenworth Case. Though dead before the novel begins, his character — and the disposition of his fortune — shapes every aspect of the investigation.

## The Victim

Horatio Leavenworth was a man of substantial wealth and social standing. He was known as a benefactor to his two nieces, Mary and Eleanore, whom he raised after the death of their parents. His character is described as benevolent:

> Mr. Leavenworth was too benevolent a man, too much respected and beloved, to have enemies. Still, if he had been murdered, there must have been some motive. (001-024)

The apparent contradiction between his benevolent character and his violent death drives the investigation: who could wish harm to such a man?

## The Will

Leavenworth's will is the novel's central document. Its provisions — and the question of whether it was altered shortly before his death — provide the financial motive that underlies suspicion of both nieces. The will was drafted by the firm Veeley, Carr & Raymond, connecting the narrator professionally to the case.

The discovery that the will had been changed — shifting the bulk of the inheritance from Mary to Eleanore — fundamentally alters the investigation's understanding of motive and redirects suspicion.

## The Death

Leavenworth was killed by a single pistol shot to the back of the head while seated at his desk in his library. The locked door, the missing key, and the position of the body create the locked-room elements of the mystery. The murder weapon — a small Smith & Wesson pistol — is traced through the firearms dealer Bohn & Co.

## Posthumous Influence

Though dead, Leavenworth exerts a powerful influence over the narrative. His testamentary choices, his relationships with his nieces, and his possible knowledge of Eleanore's secret marriage all become subjects of investigation. The resolution of the mystery requires understanding not only who killed him, but why — and the answer lies in the complexities of his personal and financial affairs.
