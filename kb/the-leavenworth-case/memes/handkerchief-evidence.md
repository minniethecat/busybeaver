# Handkerchief Evidence

A physical object introduced as evidence at the coroner's inquest — a handkerchief found to be soiled with pistol residue — that becomes one of the most contested pieces of circumstantial evidence in The Leavenworth Case. The handkerchief exemplifies the novel's treatment of physical evidence: it is real, it is incriminating, and it may not prove what it seems to prove.

## Discovery and Presentation

The handkerchief is introduced dramatically during the inquest proceedings. Detective Gryce produces it as a key piece of physical evidence:

> He immediately drew from his breast a handkerchief curiously soiled. "It is strange," he remarked, "that we have not been able to find the owner of this." (008-169)

The handkerchief's significance lies not merely in its existence but in its condition — stained with residue consistent with the firing of a pistol. This links it directly to the murder weapon.

## The Confrontation

Gryce presents the handkerchief to Eleanore Leavenworth for identification:

> "First, let me see if it is my handkerchief." And she held out her hand. (008-176)

Her reaction is telling:

> She let the handkerchief fall convulsively from her hand. (008-180)

The detective's questioning of the household staff tightens the net:

> "Can you tell when she last washed the handkerchief?" (008-182)

The servant replies:

> "This, sir; this handkerchief? Oh, some time this week, sir." (008-183)

## Evidentiary Value

The handkerchief connects the murder weapon (the pistol) to a specific household member. Pistol grease stains on a recently laundered handkerchief suggest that the owner was in proximity to the fired weapon.

But Mary Leavenworth immediately questions its probative value:

> The pistol grease on the handkerchief is certainly no proof that she herself was connected with the crime. (009-005)

And she protests the detective's tactics:

> "I object to your bringing out that wretched handkerchief, which she may or may not have dropped there." (009-005)

## The Handkerchief as a Narrative Device

In the structure of the novel, the handkerchief serves multiple functions. It is:
1. A physical clue that advances the investigation
2. A dramatic device that heightens tension at the inquest
3. A contested item that illustrates the ambiguity of circumstantial evidence
4. A focusing object that concentrates suspicion on a particular individual

The handkerchief demonstrates how a small domestic object can acquire enormous evidentiary significance in a murder investigation, and how that significance can be argued from both incriminating and exculpatory perspectives.
