# Everett Raymond

A young lawyer and junior partner at the firm Veeley, Carr & Raymond, Mr. Raymond serves as the narrator and a central participant in The Leavenworth Case. His dual role as legal professional and emotionally involved observer creates the novel's distinctive perspective — part legal procedural, part romantic drama.

## The Lawyer-Narrator

Raymond is introduced as a rising young attorney whose senior partner, Mr. Veeley, sends him to the Leavenworth mansion to represent the family during the coroner's inquest:

> I was at that time a young man, just admitted to the bar after a course of study in the office of Veeley, Carr & Raymond. (001-001)

His legal training shapes his narration. He observes the investigation with professional understanding, noting procedural details and evaluating evidence with a lawyer's analytical eye. Yet his narrative voice is also personal and emotional, particularly as he becomes increasingly involved with the Leavenworth sisters.

## Relationship with the Leavenworth Sisters

Raymond's involvement deepens from professional obligation to personal attachment. He is drawn to both Mary and Eleanore Leavenworth, and his shifting sympathies mirror the reader's own uncertainty about their guilt or innocence. His emotional investment creates a productive tension with his professional detachment.

His presence at the inquest, his private conversations with the sisters, and his eventual role in the resolution of the case place him at the center of events he is uniquely qualified to narrate.

## Role in the Investigation

Though not a detective himself, Raymond serves as an essential partner to Ebenezer Gryce's investigation. He provides legal knowledge, access to the Leavenworth household, and a sympathetic ear to the suspects. His observations supplement Gryce's more clinical approach, and his narration makes the detective's reasoning accessible to the reader.

The novel's structure — a lawyer narrating a detective's investigation — allows Green to present both the legal and investigative dimensions of the case through a single, coherent perspective.

## Narrative Function

As narrator, Raymond serves several functions:
1. **Observer**: He reports events with professional precision
2. **Mediator**: He translates Gryce's cryptic methods for the reader
3. **Emotional anchor**: His feelings for the sisters give the investigation personal stakes
4. **Conscience**: His lawyer's ethics provide a moral framework for evaluating the pursuit of justice
