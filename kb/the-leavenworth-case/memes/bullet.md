# Bullet

The bullet is the projectile fired from a pistol that killed [[horatio-leavenworth]], making it the central piece of physical evidence in *The Leavenworth Case*. Its examination at the [[coroners-inquest|coroner's inquest]], its caliber and characteristics, and the tracing of the weapon from which it was fired form a critical thread in [[ebenezer-gryce]]'s investigation.

## Definition

In the context of the novel, the bullet is both a literal object — the projectile extracted from the victim's body — and a symbolic link between the crime and its perpetrator. Unlike [[circumstantial-evidence]], which requires inference, the bullet is direct physical proof that a murder was committed and that a specific weapon was used.

## Application in the Investigation

The bullet's role in the investigation proceeds through several stages:

| Stage | Action | Significance |
|-------|--------|--------------|
| Discovery | Found lodged in the victim's head | Confirms the cause of death |
| Extraction | Removed by the examining physician | Becomes a physical exhibit |
| Examination | Analyzed at [[the-coroners-inquest-event|the inquest]] | Establishes the weapon type |
| Tracing | Linked to a specific pistol | Narrows the field of suspects |
| Reconstruction | Used to establish the shooting position | Maps the crime scene geometry |

The bullet is introduced as evidence early in the novel, immediately establishing the violent nature of the death:

> They found the body of Mr. Leavenworth still in the same position, but with a bullet-hole in his head as large as the end of my little finger. (001-019)

The physician's extraction and presentation of the bullet transforms it from a wound into evidence:

> The physician stated that he had found and extracted the bullet which he now handed to the jury. (002-007)

## Textual Evidence

The physical examination of the bullet at the inquest is a moment of forensic theater, with the coroner using the object to focus the jury's attention:

> The physician's testimony being ended, the coroner picked up the bullet which had been laid on the table. (002-010)

The expert witness's handling of the bullet is observed with professional interest:

> Q, with an expressive eyebrow, took the bullet carelessly in his hand. (005-009)

The coroner's introduction of the bullet to the expert is formal and pointed:

> "We have here a bullet, which we must ask you to examine." (005-008)

## Forensic Context

In the 1870s, ballistic analysis was rudimentary compared to modern forensic science. There was no systematic rifling-pattern matching, no ballistic fingerprinting. The bullet could reveal its caliber — narrowing the possible weapons — but could not be definitively matched to a specific pistol. This limitation is important to the novel's plot: the bullet establishes the fact of murder and the type of weapon, but the identification of the murderer requires the full apparatus of [[detection]] — testimony, motive, opportunity, and the methodical assembly of [[circumstantial-evidence]].
