# Mr. Clavering Sr.

The father of Henry Clavering, mentioned in connection with the secret marriage between his son and Eleanore Leavenworth. His social position as an English gentleman and his knowledge of the marriage provide important context for understanding the motivations and constraints of the principal characters.

## Family and Status

As the father of Henry Clavering, the elder Mr. Clavering represents the English social dimension of the novel. His family's standing and expectations inform the secrecy of his son's marriage to Eleanore — a marriage contracted without parental knowledge and potentially against parental wishes.

The Clavering family's Englishness places them outside the immediate social world of New York, adding an international dimension to the domestic crime. The question of whether Henry Clavering acted with or without his father's knowledge becomes relevant to understanding his movements and motives.

## Connection to the Plot

Though the elder Clavering never appears directly, his existence shapes the actions of his son and, indirectly, of Eleanore. The secrecy of the marriage suggests that it would not have been approved — a motive for concealment that extends beyond the immediate crime and into the social dynamics of Victorian transatlantic society.
