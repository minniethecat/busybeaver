# Henry Clavering

An English gentleman whose secret marriage to Eleanore Leavenworth places him at the intersection of several crucial questions in the murder investigation. His nationality, his concealed relationship, and his movements on the night of the crime make him a figure of intense investigative interest.

## The Mysterious Foreigner

Clavering is introduced to the reader through inference and investigation rather than direct appearance. He is first mentioned as a mysterious man whose presence was noted near the Leavenworth mansion. His English nationality marks him as an outsider — someone whose motives and loyalties are, to American eyes, inherently suspect.

The narrator eventually meets him and notes his gentlemanly bearing, but Clavering's character remains elusive through much of the novel. He is a cipher — a figure whose significance is clear (he is married to Eleanore) but whose nature is opaque.

## The Secret Marriage

Clavering's marriage to Eleanore is the novel's central secret. Performed without the knowledge of Horatio Leavenworth or of society, the marriage carries serious legal and social implications. The question of when the marriage occurred relative to the murder becomes one of the investigation's key factual determinations.

The existence of the marriage supplies a motive for the crime: if Horatio Leavenworth discovered the secret union, his reaction might have precipitated violence. Alternatively, the marriage might be entirely unrelated to the murder — a private matter that becomes entangled with a public crime through unfortunate timing.

## Role in the Investigation

Clavering's role shifts as the investigation progresses. Initially, he is a suspect — his secret marriage and his foreign status make him a natural target of suspicion. Later, as evidence accumulates, his position becomes more ambiguous. The detection of his connection to the key and his presence at the Hoffman House provides Gryce with essential investigative material:

> "I followed up the clue by going to the Hoffman House," Gryce reported. (022-058)

## Narrative Significance

Clavering functions as a catalyst in the plot — his existence forces Eleanore into silence, which in turn generates suspicion. He is also a representative of the world beyond New York, introducing an international dimension to what might otherwise be a purely local mystery. His Englishness places the novel in dialogue with the British detective fiction tradition that Green both drew upon and innovated against.
