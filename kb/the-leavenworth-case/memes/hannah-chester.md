# Hannah Chester

A servant in the Leavenworth household who provides important testimony about domestic arrangements and the movements of household members on the night of Horatio Leavenworth's murder.

## Role in the Household

As a domestic servant in the Leavenworth mansion, Hannah Chester had access to the private quarters and daily routines of the household. Her position allowed her to observe details that other witnesses — more removed from the domestic sphere — might miss. Servants in Victorian households were often the repositories of family secrets precisely because they were treated as invisible.

## Testimony

Hannah's testimony at the coroner's inquest focuses on the physical details of the household: the laundry, the handkerchief, the comings and goings of the family members. Her evidence about when she last washed a particular handkerchief becomes unexpectedly significant when that handkerchief is found stained with pistol residue:

> "Can you tell when she last washed the handkerchief?" (008-182)

> "This, sir; this handkerchief? Oh, some time this week, sir." (008-183)

This domestic detail — a laundered handkerchief — connects the physical evidence of the pistol to the household routine, becoming a link in the chain of circumstantial evidence.

## Significance

Hannah Chester represents the role of domestic witnesses in detective fiction. Her evidence, like that of many servants in Victorian novels, is valuable precisely because of its mundane character. She notices what others overlook because her work requires her to attend to the physical details of household life.
