# Secret Marriage

A marriage performed without public notice, often without the knowledge of family members or the community. In The Leavenworth Case, the discovery of a secret marriage between Eleanore Leavenworth and Henry Clavering fundamentally reshapes the investigation by providing an explanation for Eleanore's otherwise inexplicable silence.

## Legal Status in the 19th Century

In 19th-century American law, secret or clandestine marriages were legally valid if they met the basic requirements of contract — mutual consent and solemnization by an authorized officiant. However, they carried significant social stigma and could affect inheritance rights, particularly if the marriage contradicted the expectations of a testator.

The Leavenworth fortune was at stake. A secret marriage could be interpreted as an attempt to circumvent the deceased's wishes regarding his nieces' marital choices, providing a potential motive for murder.

## Discovery in the Novel

The marriage between Eleanore Leavenworth and Henry Clavering is revealed only gradually. The narrator, Mr. Raymond, himself a lawyer, pursues verification:

> "Has the gentleman himself no proofs of this marriage?" (019-038)

The establishment of the marriage's existence — and the determination of when it occurred relative to the murder — becomes a critical factual question in the investigation.

## The Marriage as Motive

Once discovered, the secret marriage provides a powerful lens through which to view the entire case. It supplies:

1. **A motive for Eleanore's silence** — She could not explain her actions without revealing the marriage
2. **A connection between suspects** — The marriage links Eleanore to Clavering, whose involvement had been suspected
3. **A narrative explanation** — The marriage transforms a series of inexplicable behaviors into a coherent story

The inheritance implications of the marriage add financial weight to the personal drama. The Leavenworth will's provisions regarding marriage could determine which niece inherited the fortune.

## Secret Marriage and Narrative Form

The secret marriage is a conventional element of Victorian fiction — Dickens, Collins, and Braddon all employed it. Green's use of the device is distinctive in that the marriage functions not merely as a melodramatic revelation but as a legal fact with specific evidentiary consequences. The novel treats the question of proof of marriage with the same rigor it applies to proof of murder.
