# Mrs. Belden

The proprietress of a boarding house who provides one of the novel's most important witness statements. Her detailed narrative account — delivered to the investigators — fills critical gaps in the timeline and establishes connections between figures whose relationships had been only suspected.

## The Boarding House

Mrs. Belden's establishment — a boarding house — is a location of significant activity in the later stages of the investigation. It was here that certain figures connected to the case stayed or visited, and Mrs. Belden's observations of their movements and behavior provide the investigators with essential evidence.

## Her Narrative

Mrs. Belden's testimony takes the form of an extended narrative — a story within the story — that illuminates events that occurred outside the direct observation of the narrator and the detective. Her account is notable for its detail and specificity: she recalls conversations, timings, and physical details with a clarity that suggests both good observation and the practiced memory of a landlady who notes everything.

## Evidentiary Value

Her evidence is valuable because it comes from a neutral party — a woman with no stake in the inheritance, no personal connection to the Leavenworth family, and no reason to fabricate or conceal. Her testimony provides the kind of independent corroboration that is essential in a case built largely on the statements of interested parties.
