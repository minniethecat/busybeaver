# The Coroner

The public official charged with investigating the death of Horatio Leavenworth and presiding over the inquest that follows. Though never named, the coroner is a figure of considerable professional authority whose conduct of the inquest shapes the entire investigation.

## Professional Authority

The coroner arrives at the Leavenworth mansion shortly after the discovery of the body and immediately takes charge of the scene:

> The coroner is here, then? (001-039)

His presence transforms the private tragedy into a public legal proceeding. The narrator observes his professional competence:

> He was capable of conducting an important examination, with the necessary skill and judgment. (002-004)

> The face of the coroner, sternly intelligent and attentive. (002-003)

## Conduct of the Inquest

The coroner's methodical questioning of witnesses establishes the factual record upon which the entire investigation depends. He examines the secretary, the household staff, the expert witnesses, and the Leavenworth nieces, maintaining order and focus throughout:

> Mr. Gryce put a stop to any attempt at levity or badinage into an examination the seriousness of which we were all beginning to feel. (003-128)

## Decision

At the conclusion of the testimony, the coroner makes the crucial determination that the death was murder — not accident, not suicide. This decision, rendered through the jury's verdict, authorizes the criminal investigation that follows and transforms the case from a matter of private misfortune to a public prosecution.
