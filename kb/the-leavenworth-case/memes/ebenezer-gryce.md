# Ebenezer Gryce

Ebenezer Gryce is the lead detective investigating the murder of [[horatio-leavenworth]] in Anna Katharine Green's *The Leavenworth Case* (1878). A professional investigator attached to the [[new-york-police-department]], Gryce is one of the earliest detective characters in American fiction — predating Sherlock Holmes by nearly a decade — and embodies a methodical, evidence-based approach to [[detection]] that was innovative for its time. He is assisted throughout the case by the narrator, [[everett-raymond]], a junior lawyer who observes Gryce's methods with professional admiration.

## Role and Character

Mr. Gryce is introduced as a detective of considerable reputation who arrives at the [[leavenworth-mansion]] shortly after the body is discovered and immediately takes charge of the investigation. The narrator describes him with a mixture of respect and curiosity:

> Mr. Gryce, the detective, was not the thin, wiry, and painfully attentive person of the French detective, nor the burly, red-faced, and wordy individual of the English. He was a portly, comfortable-looking man, whose sturdy frame and jovial countenance were relieved by a pair of eyes so keen and searching that they seemed to pierce through the person they looked at. (001-054)

Gryce's approach is characterized by patience, careful observation, and a willingness to follow unpromising leads. He is not given to dramatic pronouncements; his deductions emerge gradually from the accumulation of facts:

> Ebenezer Gryce is not the man I have always believed him if he cannot pull the wool over the eyes of a pair of amateurs. (010-053)

## Investigative Method

Gryce's method is systematic and intellectually rigorous. At the crime scene, he immediately establishes a working theory based on the position of physical evidence:

> And Mr. Gryce planted his feet firmly upon a certain spot in the carpet: "The bullet came from somewhere in this direction." (001-062)

He values witness testimony but subjects it to careful scrutiny, comparing statements for consistency and probing for hidden truths. His questioning at [[the-coroners-inquest-event|the coroner's inquest]] reveals his ability to extract information without revealing his own theories. He examines the physical evidence — the pistol, the handkerchief, the key — and interviews all the principal witnesses.

His confidence, when finally earned, is formidable:

> Think I am deceived. Ha, ha! Ebenezer Gryce deceived after a month of hard work! (036-020)

## Key Actions

Gryce attends the coroner's inquest and immediately begins developing his theory of the case. A notable moment occurs when he produces the key — a crucial piece of evidence — at a dramatic moment:

> He glanced at Mr. Gryce, who immediately drew from his breast pocket a small package. Unwrapping it, he disclosed a key. (008-169)

His investigation takes him from the Leavenworth mansion to the Hoffman House and beyond. The discovery of the altered will, the revelation of a secret marriage, and the final confrontation with the murderer all bear the impress of Gryce's methodical approach.

> I saw Mr. Gryce's form suddenly stiffen — "for a person to be summoned to your presence." (009-049)

## Narrative Function

As the detective figure, Gryce serves as the engine of the plot. His investigation structures the novel's forward movement, and his revelations provide the narrative's primary source of suspense and surprise. He is, characteristically for the early detective genre, more a function than a fully rounded personality — the reader learns little about his personal life — but his professional competence is vividly rendered. His partnership with the narrator [[everett-raymond]] establishes the detective-assistant dynamic that would become a staple of the genre.
