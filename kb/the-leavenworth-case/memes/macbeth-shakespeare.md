# Macbeth

*Macbeth* is William Shakespeare's tragedy of ambition, murder, and guilt, first performed circa 1606. The play is referenced in chapter epigraphs throughout *The Leavenworth Case*, where its treatment of murder, conscience, and the impossibility of concealing guilt provides a literary counterpoint to Anna Katharine Green's detective plot.

## Relevance to the Novel

Anna Katharine Green uses Shakespeare quotations as chapter epigraphs to establish thematic resonance between the Elizabethan tragedy and her contemporary detective story. *Macbeth* in particular — with its intimate portrait of murderers and their psychological unraveling — offers a darker, more interior perspective on crime than the detective genre alone can provide:

| Macbeth Element | Leavenworth Parallel |
|-----------------|---------------------|
| The act of murder transforming the murderer | [[mary-leavenworth]]'s psychological journey |
| Guilt manifesting in behavior | The inconsistencies that alert [[ebenezer-gryce]] |
| A woman driving the action | The central role of female characters in the crime |
| Blood that cannot be washed away | The [[handkerchief-evidence]] and other physical traces |
| The impossibility of escape from consequences | The inexorable closing of [[circumstantial-evidence]] |

## Thematic Parallels

### Murder and Transformation

*Macbeth* explores how the act of murder transforms the murderer — a theme that resonates with the novel's portrayal of [[mary-leavenworth]]. The psychological consequences of violence, in both works, are as inevitable as the legal ones.

### Guilt and Exposure

Lady Macbeth's sleepwalking confession — "Out, damned spot!" — dramatizes the impossibility of fully concealing guilt. In *The Leavenworth Case*, this principle operates through the accumulation of [[circumstantial-evidence]]: the traces that crime leaves behind, the inconsistencies in testimony that [[ebenezer-gryce]] methodically collects, and the psychological pressure that eventually forces [[marys-confession|confession]].

### Ambition and Violence

The relationship between ambition and violence that drives *Macbeth* finds its echo in the financial motive at the heart of the Leavenworth case — the [[inheritance]] that provides the reason for murder. But where Macbeth's ambition is for power, the ambition in Green's novel is more domestic: the desire for financial security, social position, and freedom from an unwanted marriage.

## Epigraph Function

The *Macbeth* epigraphs function as a literary commentary on the action, inviting the reader to consider the Leavenworth murder not merely as a puzzle to be solved but as a moral drama with deep roots in the Western literary tradition. By associating her contemporary detective story with Shakespearean tragedy, Green claims for her genre a seriousness that the 19th-century "sensation novel" was often denied.
