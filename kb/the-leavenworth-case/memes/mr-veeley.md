# Mr. Veeley

The senior partner at Veeley, Carr & Raymond, the law firm that represents the Leavenworth family and drafted Horatio Leavenworth's will. It is Mr. Veeley who dispatches the narrator, Everett Raymond, to the Leavenworth mansion to represent the family during the coroner's inquest.

## Professional Role

As the head of the firm, Mr. Veeley was Horatio Leavenworth's personal attorney. He drafted the will whose provisions become central to the investigation. His professional relationship with the deceased gives him knowledge of the testamentary arrangements that is essential to understanding the case.

The firm's connection to the Leavenworth family is well established:

> As Mr. Veeley was their uncle's best friend, they naturally turned to him for guidance. (001-013)

## The Summons

Veeley's decision to send Raymond to the inquest — rather than attending himself — sets the novel's events in motion. His trust in his junior partner reflects the professional hierarchy of the 19th-century law office and provides the narrative device by which the lawyer-narrator becomes involved in the case.

Raymond notes Veeley's reaction to the news:

> "Mr. Veeley," I repeated, "will be overwhelmed by it." (001-012)

## Legal and Narrative Function

Mr. Veeley's primary function is structural: he provides the professional connection that brings Raymond to the Leavenworth mansion and the legal expertise that frames the testamentary issues. As a character, he appears only briefly, but his institutional role — as the lawyer who knows the dead man's final wishes — is central to the novel's legal dimension.
