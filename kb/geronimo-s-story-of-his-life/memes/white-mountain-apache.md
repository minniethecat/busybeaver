## Overview

The White Mountain Apaches were a distinct Apache division living north of [[geronimo]]'s people. Their relationship with the Bedonkohe was characterized by cautious distance rather than the close alliance that existed among the four southern bands. Their reservation became the site of Geronimo's confinement in the early 1880s and the starting point of his famous 1885 escape.

## Identity & Territory

The White Mountain Apaches occupied the high country of eastern Arizona. Geronimo locates them: "North of us lived the White Mountain Apaches. They were not always on the best of terms with our tribe, yet we seldom had any war with them" (002-003). Their territory bordered that of the [[navajo|Navajo Indians]] to the north — a significant distinction, as Geronimo notes the Navajo "were not of the same blood as the Apaches" and councils were held "with all Apache tribes, but never with the Navajo Indians" (002-003).

## Role in the Apache Wars

The White Mountain Apaches played a more peripheral but tactically significant role:

- **Arms Supply** — During the 1885 breakout from the reservation, Geronimo's band "obtained from the White Mountain Apaches" guns and ammunition (017-003), suggesting a degree of material support despite political distance.

- **The Reservation** — The San Carlos Apache Indian Reservation, established on White Mountain territory, became the U.S. government's concentration point for multiple Apache bands, including Geronimo's. The 1885 breakout — when "about fifty of the Chiricahua prisoners, headed by [[geronimo]], [[naiche]], and other chiefs, escaped from the White Mountain Reserve" (017-009) — launched the final phase of the Apache Wars.

- **Chief Hash-ka-ai-la** — Geronimo knew their chief personally and "considered him a good warrior" (002-003), indicating mutual respect despite the lack of deep alliance.

## Notable Members

- [[hash-ka-ai-la]] — Chief of the White Mountain Apaches, "a good warrior" (002-003)
