## Overview

The esadadedne (buckskin-on-a-hoop) was the principal musical instrument of the [[apache-family|Apaches]], used to accompany the singing that drove all ceremonial and social dances. It appears at multiple points in [[geronimo|Geronimo]]'s narrative, always in connection with communal gatherings that marked the tribe's most significant occasions.

## Description

[[geronimo|Geronimo]] describes the esadadedne in the context of tribal festivities: "The music for our dance was singing led by the warriors, and accompanied by beating the *esadadedne* (buckskin-on-a-hoop). No words were sung — only the tones" (004-001). The instrument was a simple frame drum, likely constructed by stretching a piece of treated buckskin over a circular hoop and securing it.

Unlike the complex musical traditions of some other Native American tribes, Apache dance music was minimalist: warriors led the singing with wordless tones, and the esadadedne provided the rhythmic foundation. The emphasis was on collective participation rather than individual performance.

## Uses in Ceremony

The esadadedne was used across multiple types of gatherings. After feast days came "horse races, foot races, wrestling, jumping, and all sorts of games" (004-001), all preceded by the music of the esadadedne. In the war dance, "singing led by the warriors and accompanied with the beating of the *esadadene*" joined with "yells and war whoops" that "sometimes almost drown the music" (020-017).

The instrument's presence at both celebratory feasts and solemn war preparations underscores its role as a unifying element of [[apache-family|Apache]] ceremonial life.

Geronimo describes the music that accompanied Apache festivities:
