## Overview

The war bonnet (or war band) was a ceremonial headdress worn by Apache warriors preparing for battle. In Geronimo's description, it was "fastened upon their brows" as part of the war preparation ritual, serving both as a practical identifier in combat and as a symbol of the warrior's spiritual readiness to face death. Unlike the feathered headdress traditions of Plains Indian cultures, the Apache war bonnet was worn as a band or cap affixed to the forehead, intimately connected to the warrior's personal preparation for the warpath.

## Description

According to the narrative, the war bands were worn in conjunction with other battle preparations. When the three-tribe coalition assembled on the Mexican border for the revenge raid of 1859, Geronimo describes the warriors thus:

> "Their faces were painted, the war bands fastened upon their brows, their long scalp-locks ready for the hand and knife of the warrior who could overcome them" (006-016).

The war band was worn alongside face paint and the traditional scalp-lock — the long lock of hair that an enemy warrior would seek to take as a trophy, symbolizing the warrior's willingness to risk death in combat. This combination of adornments transformed the warrior's body into a declaration: painted for battle, bound with the war band, and wearing the scalp-lock as an open challenge to any opponent.

The wearing of the war bonnet was an exclusively male practice tied to the warpath. Geronimo himself is described as wearing a war bonnet when he first met the narrator S. M. Barrett in 1904, where Barrett acted "as interpreter of English into Spanish, and vice versa, in selling a war bonnet" (001-001). This encounter — a commercial transaction involving a war bonnet — became the genesis of Geronimo's entire autobiography.

## Cultural Significance

The war bonnet functioned as more than decoration. It marked the transition from everyday life to the warpath — a visual signal to the community and to the spirits that the wearer was prepared for combat and for death. In a culture where warfare was both a practical necessity and a spiritual obligation, the war bonnet served as the warrior's compact with his purpose.

The ritual preparation for war, including the fastening of the war band, was part of a broader system of Apache war customs that included the war dance and the council of war. Together, these customs formed a complete ceremonial framework governing when and how Apache men went to war, ensuring that war parties operated with both spiritual sanction and collective deliberation.

## Examples in the Narrative

- **The 1859 Revenge Raid** — All warriors of the three Apache tribes assembled on the Mexican border with "war bands fastened upon their brows" (006-016) before crossing into Sonora to avenge the Kas-ki-yeh massacre. This was the largest coordinated Apache war party Geronimo describes.

- **Geronimo's Introduction to Barrett** — The first meeting between Geronimo and the narrator occurred when Barrett "acted for him as interpreter... in selling a war bonnet" (001-001), making the war bonnet the literal entry point into Geronimo's story and the collaboration that produced his autobiography.

- **War Paint and Readiness** — Apache warriors in war paint, including war bands, were a recognizable sight to both enemies and allies. When Mexican troops encountered Apaches "in war paint" (010-015), the visual ensemble — of which the war band was a key element — signaled immediate hostile intent.
