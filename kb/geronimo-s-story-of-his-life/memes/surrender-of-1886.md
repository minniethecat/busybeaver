# Surrender of 1886

The Surrender of 1886 marked the end of the Apache Wars, the final chapter in decades of armed resistance by [[geronimo|Geronimo]] and his [[bedonkohe-apache|Bedonkohe]] band against both Mexican and United States forces. The surrender took place at [[skeleton-canyon|Skeleton Canyon]] in southeastern Arizona Territory in September 1886, when Geronimo and [[naiche|Naiche]], exhausted by relentless pursuit under [[general-miles|General Nelson A. Miles]], agreed to lay down their arms under terms they believed guaranteed a return to their homeland. Instead, the Apaches were sent to [[fort-sill|Florida]] as prisoners of war — a breach that Geronimo would spend the remaining twenty-three years of his life decrying.

## Background

### The Pursuit

By early 1886, the Apache resistance had been reduced to a small band. After General Crook's failed negotiations, General Miles was appointed commander of all western posts and intensified the campaign. Geronimo records:

> Soon General Miles was made commander of all the western posts, and troops trailed us continually. They were led by Captain Lawton, who had good scouts. The Mexican soldiers also became more active and more numerous. We had skirmishes almost every day. (017-003)

The combined pressure of American troops, Mexican forces, and Apache scouts proved effective. Geronimo describes how the band was worn down: "We were reckless of our lives, because we felt every man's hand was against us" (017-010). Captain Lawton's scouts tracked the Apaches relentlessly across the rugged terrain of the Sierra Madre.

### The Approach

Eventually, Geronimo decided to seek terms. He recounts the decision:

> I knew that General Miles was the chief of the American troops, and I decided to send a message to him. I sent my brother, White Horse, and Ki-e-ta to go to Fort Bowie to see General Miles, and to tell him that we wanted to make a treaty of peace. (017-008, 017-010)

The messengers returned with word that Miles would negotiate. Geronimo and his remaining warriors — including Naiche, the son of the late chief [[cochise|Cochise]] and "the only chief with me" (016-011) — proceeded to Skeleton Canyon for the meeting.

## The Surrender

### The Meeting at Skeleton Canyon

The exact date of the surrender in September 1886 is recorded in military dispatches. Geronimo's own account emphasizes the conditional nature of what he agreed to:

| Party | Representatives | Role |
|-------|----------------|------|
| Apache | Geronimo, Naiche, remaining warriors | Surrendering party |
| United States | General Nelson A. Miles, Captain Lawton, Lieutenant Gatewood | Receiving party |
| Witnesses | Various officers and scouts | Documentation |

Geronimo recounts that Miles made specific promises. According to Geronimo, the general told him:

> "Geronimo, if you will agree to a few words of a treaty, we need have no more trouble." (017-012)

The terms, as Geronimo understood them, included a period of separation from families followed by reunification and eventual return to Arizona. The reality was starkly different.

### The Terms and Their Violation

Immediately after the surrender, the Apaches were transported by rail to Florida — first to Fort Marion and then to Fort Pickens — and later to Mount Vernon Barracks in Alabama. Families were separated, and many Apaches, including children, died of disease in the unfamiliar climate. Geronimo remained a prisoner of war until his death in 1909 at Fort Sill, Oklahoma.

The editor of Geronimo's autobiography, S. M. Barrett, carefully documents the controversy:

> In several places throughout the manuscript Geronimo accuses General Miles of bad faith. … I have simply been granted the opportunity to give the old Indian a chance to state his own case. (P03-018, P03-019)

- **[[general-miles|General Nelson A. Miles]]** — Commanding American officer who negotiated the terms
- **Captain Henry Lawton** — Led the scouts who tracked Geronimo's band through the Sierra Madre
- **Lieutenant Charles B. Gatewood** — Officer who convinced Geronimo to meet with Miles

## Aftermath

### Prisoner of War Status

The Apaches were classified as prisoners of war rather than defeated combatants entitled to treaty protections. This classification enabled the government to hold them indefinitely. Geronimo describes the journey:

> We were not allowed to take anything with us except our clothing. … We were put on a train and sent to Florida. (019-001, 019-004)

Over the following years, many Apaches died from disease — tuberculosis and pneumonia were particularly devastating in the humid Florida climate, so different from the Arizona mountains.

### Historical Dispute

The question of whether Geronimo surrendered unconditionally or conditionally remains historically contested. Military records from 1886 support the government position of an unconditional surrender. Geronimo's first-person account, recorded twenty years later, insists on the conditional nature of the agreement. Barrett, as editor, notes that Geronimo's manuscript "criticised General Crook" and "accuses General Miles of bad faith," but that the War Department granted permission for publication on the understanding that the manuscript would be submitted for review (P03-017, P03-018).

> The fact that Geronimo has told the story in his own way is doubtless the only excuse necessary for offering it to the public. (P03-020)

## Significance

The Surrender of 1886 marked the definitive end of armed Native American resistance in the United States. Geronimo was the last Native American leader to formally surrender to the U.S. Army, and his band was among the last to be removed from their ancestral lands. The controversy surrounding the terms of the surrender — and the twenty-three years of imprisonment that followed — has made the event a lasting symbol of the contested history between the United States government and Native American peoples.
