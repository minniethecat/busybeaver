## Overview

**Kas-ki-yeh** (also spelled Kaskiyeh) was a Mexican town in [[sonora|Sonora]] where, in the summer of 1858, Mexican troops attacked an [[apache-family|Apache]] encampment and killed [[geronimo|Geronimo]]'s mother, his young wife [[alope]], and his three small children. This massacre became the defining tragedy of Geronimo's life, the event that ignited his lifelong vendetta against Mexican forces and shaped his identity as a warrior.

## The Massacre (1858)

In the summer of 1858, the Bedonkohe Apaches — at peace with both Mexican towns and neighboring Indian tribes — traveled south into Old Mexico to trade. Their whole tribe went through [[sonora|Sonora]] toward [[casa-grande|Casa Grande]], stopping at Kas-ki-yeh to camp for several days just outside the town. Each day the Apaches went into town to trade, leaving their camp under the protection of a small guard (006-001).

Late one afternoon, as the trading party returned from town, they were met by women and children who reported that Mexican troops had attacked the camp:

> Mexican troops from some other town had attacked our camp, killed all the warriors of the guard, captured all our ponies, secured our arms, destroyed our supplies, and killed many of our women and children.
>
> (006-002)

When the warriors reassembled in silence at their rendezvous point by the river, Geronimo learned the full extent of his personal loss:

> I found that my aged mother, my young wife, and my three small children were among the slain.
>
> (006-002)

## Aftermath

The tribe — reduced to only eighty warriors and without arms or supplies, surrounded by Mexicans far inside their territory — could not fight. Chief [[mangus-colorado|Mangus-Colorado]] ordered an immediate retreat to Arizona, leaving the dead upon the field. Geronimo followed in silence (006-003, 006-004).

> I stood until all had passed, hardly knowing what I would do — I had no weapon, nor did I hardly wish to fight, neither did I contemplate recovering the bodies of my loved ones, for that was forbidden. I did not pray, nor did I resolve to do anything in particular, for I had no purpose left.
>
> (006-004)

For two days and three nights the tribe made forced marches toward the Mexican border. Geronimo did not eat and spoke to no one: "there was nothing to say" (006-005). Upon reaching their home settlement, he burned all of his family's possessions — the decorations Alope had made, the playthings of his children, even his teepee and his mother's teepee (006-007).

## Geronimo's Vow of Vengeance

The massacre transformed Geronimo. He later wrote:

> I was never again contented in our quiet home. True, I could visit my father's grave, but I had vowed vengeance upon the Mexican troopers who had wronged me, and whenever I came near his grave or saw anything to remind me of former happy days my heart would ache for revenge upon Mexico.
>
> (006-008)

## The Revenge Campaign (1859)

Almost a year after the massacre, in the summer of 1859, Geronimo — acting as intermediary — assembled a coalition of three Apache tribes on the Mexican border: the Bedonkohe led by [[mangus-colorado|Mangus-Colorado]], the Chokonen ([[chiricahua-apache|Chiricahua]]) led by [[cochise]], and the Nedni led by Whoa (006-011 through 006-016).

Geronimo addressed the gathered warriors:

> "Kinsman, you have heard what the Mexicans have recently done without cause. You are my relatives — uncles, cousins, brothers. We are men the same as the Mexicans are — we can do to them what they have done to us. Let us go forward and trail them — I will lead you to their city — we will attack them in their homes."
>
> (006-013)

At [[arispe]], the Apache force drew out the Mexican garrison by killing eight mounted scouts, then engaged in a two-day battle. Geronimo identified the Mexican cavalry as the same soldiers who had perpetrated the Kas-ki-yeh massacre and was granted command of the battle (006-024).

> In all the battle I thought of my murdered mother, wife, and babies — of my father's grave and my vow of vengeance, and I fought with fury. Many fell by my hand, and constantly I led the advance.
>
> (006-025)

After a desperate two-hour engagement in which Geronimo fought hand-to-hand, killing opponents with spear, saber, and knife, the Apaches were victorious. Covered in the blood of his enemies, Geronimo was made war chief of all the Apaches on the battlefield (006-026, 006-027). He later reflected:

> I could not call back my loved ones, I could not bring back the dead Apaches, but I could rejoice in this revenge. The Apaches had avenged the massacre of "Kas-ki-yeh."
>
> (006-028)

## Significance

Kas-ki-yeh represents the pivotal turning point in Geronimo's life. Before the massacre, he was a young husband and father living quietly with his family. After it, he became the most feared Apache war leader of his generation. The massacre and its aftermath — the revenge campaign, the elevation to war chief — set in motion the decades of conflict that defined the latter half of the Apache Wars.
