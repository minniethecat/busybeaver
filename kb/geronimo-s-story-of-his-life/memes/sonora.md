# Sonora

Sonora, the northern Mexican state bordering Arizona, was the primary theater of Geronimo's raiding campaigns and the site of his most intense combat experiences. For decades, Geronimo and other Apache warriors crossed into Sonora to raid Mexican settlements, seize livestock, and exact vengeance for the [[kas-ki-yeh-massacre|Kas-Ki-Yeh massacre]] that killed Geronimo's mother, wife, and children.

## Geography

Sonora stretches from the Gulf of California eastward to the Sierra Madre Occidental, a mountain range that provided the Apaches with concealment and defensive positions. Geronimo describes entering Sonora through multiple routes: "on the north line of Sonora" following the Sierra de Antunez Mountains (009-002), through the Nedni Apache range, and via the Sierra Madre Mountains (009-004). The terrain — canyon-cut mountains, arid plains, and isolated settlements — was perfectly suited to Apache guerrilla tactics.

Geronimo often refers to Sonora interchangeably with "Old Mexico" in his narrative, reflecting the Apache understanding that Sonora represented the Mexican heartland they raided.

## Role in the Narrative

### Raiding Territory

Sonora was the destination of Geronimo's first raid as a war leader in the summer of 1858, when "our whole tribe (Bedonkohe Apaches) went through Sonora toward Casa Grande" (006-001). This expedition ended with the Kas-Ki-Yeh massacre, which transformed Geronimo's relationship with Mexico into one of permanent war.

In subsequent years, Geronimo led dozens of expeditions into Sonora. Typical was the raid of summer 1865:

> In the summer of 1865, with four warriors, I went again into Mexico. ... We entered Sonora at a point southwest from Tombstone, Arizona, and followed the Sierra de Antunez Mountains to the southern limit, then crossed the country as far south as the mouth of Yaqui River. Here we saw a great lake extending beyond the limit of sight. Then we turned north, attacked several settlements, and secured plenty of supplies. (010-025)

### The Military Pursuit

By 1885–1886, Sonora had become the site of the final military campaign against Geronimo. Captain Lawton's command operated deep in Sonora, and Geronimo records the official dispatches documenting their pursuit:

> Captain Lawton reports, through Colonel Royall, commanding at Fort Huachuca, that his camp surprised Geronimo's camp on Yongi River, about 130 miles south and east of Campas, Sonora, or nearly 300 miles south of Mexican boundary. (018-SMG-3)

Another dispatch from Hermosillo, the state capital, confirmed: "Geronimo with forty Indians is endeavoring to make terms of peace with Mexican authorities of Fronteraz district" (018-SMG-4).

### The Yaqui River

In one of the most striking passages of his narrative, Geronimo describes reaching the mouth of the Yaqui River, where he "saw a great lake extending beyond the limit of sight" (010-025). For a man whose entire life had been spent in the arid mountains of the Southwest, the Gulf of California represented an encounter with a world beyond his experience.

## Significance

Sonora was, for Geronimo, the landscape of both trauma and triumph. The state held the graves of his family and the memories of countless battles. It was the proving ground where he earned his reputation as a war leader, and it was the territory where the United States Army finally cornered him. The dispatches from Sonora in 1886 — from Hermosillo, from Fronteraz, from the Yongi River — mark the end of Apache armed resistance in North America.
