# Fort Bowie

Fort Bowie, situated at Apache Pass in what is now southeastern Arizona, was a central military post throughout the Apache Wars. For Geronimo and the Chiricahua Apaches, Fort Bowie represented both the possibility of peace and the reality of American military power — a place where treaties were made, trust was broken, and the final chapter of Apache armed resistance concluded.

## Geography

Fort Bowie was built at Apache Pass, a strategic gap in the Dos Cabezas Mountains that served as a natural travel corridor between the Sonoran Desert and the Chiricahua Mountains. The pass provided a reliable water source at Apache Springs, making it a critical waypoint for both Apache bands and military expeditions. Geronimo refers to the fort interchangeably as both "Fort Bowie" and "Apache Pass" throughout his narrative, reflecting the Apache understanding that the fort was defined by its location at this ancient pass.

## Role in the Narrative

### Early Encounters

Geronimo's first direct encounter with Fort Bowie came around 1872, when United States troops used the post as a staging ground for operations against Apache bands. On one occasion, troops arrived at the Apache camp, "paid for the cattle the Apaches had eaten, took the Indians and their stock, and moved on toward Fort Bowie" (010-020).

### Treaty and Betrayal

The most significant early event at Fort Bowie was the treacherous attack on Apache leaders during supposed peace negotiations. Geronimo recounts:

> Not long after this some of the officers of the United States troops invited our leaders to hold a conference at Apache Pass (Fort Bowie). Just before noon the Indians were shown into a tent and told that they would be given something to eat. When in the tent they were attacked by soldiers. (013-005)

Chief [[mangus-colorado|Mangus-Colorado]] escaped by cutting through the tent, but most of the warriors present were killed or captured. Among the Bedonkohe dead were Sanza, Kladetahe, Niyokahe, and Gopi (013-005). Geronimo notes that he did not believe the Indian agent was complicit, attributing the treachery entirely to the soldiers.

### General Howard's Command

A more positive chapter in Fort Bowie's history came under General Oliver O. Howard's command. Geronimo describes this period with unusual warmth:

> When I went to Apache Pass (Fort Bowie) I found General Howard in command, and made a treaty with him. This treaty lasted until long after General Howard had left our country. He always kept his word with us and treated us as brothers. We never had so good a friend among the United States officers as General Howard. (015-004)

Under Howard's administration, the Apaches received regular rations — Geronimo's band of Bedonkohe received twelve steers, as did [[cochise|Cochise's]] Chokonen band. Geronimo contrasts this treatment with his later life as a prisoner: "Now, as prisoners of war in this Reservation, we do not get such good rations" (015-004).

### The Final Surrender

Fort Bowie was the destination to which Geronimo was brought after his final surrender in 1886. Geronimo sent his brother Porico with George Wratton to Fort Bowie to request a return to Arizona (017-010), but his ultimate encounter was with [[general-miles|General Nelson A. Miles]], who was then in command at the post.

The aftermath of the surrender is recorded in Naiche's account, preserved in Geronimo's narrative:

> General Miles said to them: "You go with me to Fort Bowie and at a certain time you will go to see your relatives in Florida." (018-SMG)

Captain Thompson, acting assistant adjutant general, assured the Apache prisoners at Fort Bowie: "Don't be afraid; no harm shall come to you. You will go to your friends all right" (018-NCH). However, the Apaches were soon transported by rail eastward, never to return to Arizona as free people.
