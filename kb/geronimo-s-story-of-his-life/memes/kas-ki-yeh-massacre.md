# Kas-Ki-Yeh Massacre

The Kas-Ki-Yeh massacre of 1858 was the single most formative event in [[geronimo|Geronimo's]] life — the moment when a young Apache trader returned from a peaceful visit to town to find his mother, his wife [[alope|Alope]], and his three small children slaughtered by Mexican troops. The massacre transformed Geronimo from a young warrior into a determined avenger, shaping the course of the Apache Wars for the next three decades.

> I was never again contented in our quiet home. … I had no purpose in life beyond revenge. (006-007)

## Background

### The Mexican–Apache Relationship

By the mid-19th century, Apache–Mexican relations had deteriorated into a cycle of raid and reprisal. [[mangus-colorado|Mangus-Colorado]], chief of the [[bedonkohe-apache|Bedonkohe Apaches]], had led multiple campaigns against Mexican settlements. The Mexican government of Sonora had authorized military action against Apache encampments, including those of non-combatants.

### The Camp at Kas-Ki-Yeh

In the summer of 1858, the Bedonkohe Apaches under Mangus-Colorado were camped near a Mexican town the Indians called Kas-ki-yeh, in the state of Sonora. Geronimo describes the location:

> We went south into Mexico. Troops followed us, so we went west and camped in the mountains north of Arispe. There we camped for a short time, and then moved to a Mexican town called by the Indians Kas-ki-yeh. Here we stayed for several days, trading with the Mexicans. (006-001)

The warriors — including Geronimo — went into the town to trade. The women, children, and elders remained at the camp. It was during this absence that Mexican troops attacked.

## The Massacre

### What Happened

While the warriors were away trading, a company of Mexican troops from Sonora descended on the Apache camp. The soldiers killed everyone they found — women, children, and the elderly — targeting the most vulnerable members of the tribe while the fighting men were absent. When Geronimo and the other warriors returned, they found a scene of devastation.

Geronimo's account is spare but devastating:

> Late one afternoon, when we returned from the town, we were met by a few women and children who told us that Mexican troops from some other town had attacked our camp, killed all the warriors of the guard, captured all our ponies, secured all our arms, destroyed our supplies, and killed many of our women and children. (006-002)

### The Dead

Among the slain were:

| Victim | Relationship to Geronimo |
|--------|--------------------------|
| Juana (Geronimo's mother) | Mother |
| Alope | Young wife |
| Child 1 | Son |
| Child 2 | Son |
| Child 3 | Daughter (infant) |

Geronimo's mother, his young wife Alope, and his three small children — the entire family he had built — were killed in the attack. He says simply:

> When all were counted, I found that my aged mother, my young wife, my three small children were among the slain. (006-005)

No other Apache leader carried the same burden of personal loss into the coming decades of warfare. This singular trauma distinguished Geronimo from his contemporaries and gave his fighting an intensity that was remarked upon by both allies and enemies.

## Aftermath

### Geronimo's Revenge

The massacre transformed Geronimo. He describes the aftermath with a directness that conveys the depth of his transformation:

> I was never again contented in our quiet home. … I had no purpose in life beyond revenge. If I could but kill one more Mexican — I did not care for my own life. (006-007)

Geronimo sought and received spiritual authorization for his campaign. He describes receiving a "power" — a vision or spiritual experience — that told him no bullet, arrow, or lance could kill him. Armed with this belief, he became one of the most feared Apache warriors:

> I was no longer a boy. … I had been given the power to avenge the wrongs of my people. When I went on the warpath, I thought only of killing Mexicans. (006-007)

### The Council of War

Mangus-Colorado called a council of the warriors. All agreed that they would take the warpath against Mexico. Geronimo was appointed to carry the appeal for aid to [[cochise|Cochise]] and the [[chokonen-apache|Chokonen Apaches]] (006-011). Cochise's response at a formal dawn council was favorable — the Chokonen joined the war, as did the [[nedni-apache|Nedni Apaches]] under [[whoa|Whoa]] (006-018).

### The War of Revenge

The campaign that followed was devastating. Geronimo led warriors against Mexican settlements with a singular focus on vengeance. After the battle that avenged the massacre, Geronimo records:

> Still covered with the blood of the battle, I was made war chief, and I gave orders for scalping the slain. … Thus we avenged the massacre of "Kas-ki-yeh." (006-027, 006-028)

The revenge campaign united the three Apache bands — Bedonkohe, Chokonen, and Nedni — in coordinated warfare against Mexico that continued for years. The alliance forged in the wake of Kas-Ki-Yeh would become the foundation of Apache resistance throughout the remainder of the 19th century.

## Significance

The Kas-Ki-Yeh massacre is the central pivot of Geronimo's autobiography. Everything that follows — the decades of warfare, the raids, the battles, the final surrender, the twenty-three years of imprisonment — traces back to this single afternoon when Geronimo returned from trading to find his family slaughtered. The massacre is not merely a biographical detail; it is the emotional and spiritual engine of the entire narrative.

> I cannot think that we are useless or that Usen would have created a people so that they should be destroyed. … It is my land, my home, my father's land, to which I now ask to be allowed to return. (023-005)
