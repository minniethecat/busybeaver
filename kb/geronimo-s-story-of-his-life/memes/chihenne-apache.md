# Chihenne Apache

The Chihenne (also spelled Chi-hen-ne), known as the Ojo Caliente or Hot Springs Apaches, were one of the principal bands of the Apache people. They lived to the east of [[geronimo|Geronimo]]'s [[bedonkohe-apache|Bedonkohe]] band and were led by Chief [[victoria|Victoria]], whom Geronimo describes as a loyal friend and ally.

## Location and Identity

The Chihenne territory centered on the Ojo Caliente (Hot Springs) area in present-day New Mexico. Geronimo's autobiography places them geographically in relation to his own people:

> East of us lived the Chi-hen-ne (Ojo Caliente), (Hot Springs) Apaches. (002-003)

The alternative names — Chihenne, Chi-hen-ne, Ojo Caliente Apache, and Hot Springs Apache — all refer to the same band, reflecting the different naming conventions used by the Apache themselves, Spanish-speaking settlers, and English-speaking Americans.

## Relationship with the Bedonkohe

Geronimo describes consistently peaceful and cooperative relations between the Chihenne and his own [[bedonkohe-apache|Bedonkohe]] band. This stands in notable contrast to the more complex relationships among some other Apache subdivisions:

> Our tribe never had any difficulty with them. Victoria, their chief, was always a friend to me. He always helped our tribe when we asked him for help. (002-003)

This alliance proved practically significant when Geronimo's band, displaced from their own territory, sought refuge among Victoria's people near Hot Springs. The Chihenne welcomed them for a four-day gathering that brought together approximately four hundred Indians:

> We had heard that Chief Victoria of the Chihenne (Oje Caliente) Apaches was holding a council with the white men near Hot Springs in New Mexico, and that he had plenty of provisions. (015-002)

## Place in Apache Tribal Structure

The Chihenne were one of the Apache subdivisions that, along with the [[bedonkohe-apache|Bedonkohe]], [[chokonen-apache|Chokonen]], and [[nedni-apache|Nedni]], were described by Geronimo as "fast friends in the days of freedom" (002-008). This quadripartite alliance represented a core bloc within the broader Apache tribal structure during the period of Geronimo's active leadership.

## Leadership and Fate

Chief Victoria led the Chihenne during the turbulent period of the Apache Wars. Geronimo records that Victoria "lost his life in the defense of the rights of his people" and characterizes him as "a good man" (002-003). Victoria's son, Charley, is listed among the next generation of Apache chieftains alongside [[naiche|Naiche]] (son of Coche) and Asa (son of [[whoa|Whoa]]) (009-004).

The Chihenne band's fate was intertwined with that of the other Apache groups who were ultimately removed from their homelands and confined to reservations. Geronimo's narrative records that his own band had "quietly withdrawn, expecting to live with Victoria's band, where we thought we would not be molested" (016-002) — a hope that was ultimately not to be realized as the military pressure on all Apache bands intensified.
