# Apache Revenge

Revenge was a central moral imperative in Apache culture — when a family member was killed, vengeance was not merely permitted but obligatory, forming the driving motivation behind Geronimo's decades-long campaign against Mexican forces after the massacre of his family at Kas-Ki-Yeh.

## Overview

The Apache code of revenge operated independently of religion. Apaches were taught that Usen did not concern himself with "the petty quarrels of men" — if they had a grievance against any individual, they took vengeance themselves (003-005). This created a clear division between spiritual life (prayer was for positive goods) and personal justice (which was a human responsibility executed through warfare). Geronimo's narrative is fundamentally structured around revenge: the massacre of his mother, wife, and children at Kas-Ki-Yeh drives him to "desire more revenge" even after achieving a great victory (007-001).

## Description

### Revenge as Moral Obligation

When Mexican troops massacred Geronimo's family at Kas-Ki-Yeh, his response was not grief alone but a vow of vengeance upon the Mexican troopers who had wronged him (006-008). This vow drove him through battle after battle: "Still covered with the blood of my enemies, still holding my conquering weapon, I turned away to revisit my father's grave" — the private act of mourning was inseparable from the public act of vengeance (006-027). Even after a great victory, Geronimo "still desired more revenge" (007-001).

### Distinction from Religious Prayer

Apache theology established a crucial boundary: one never prayed against any person. If someone had a grievance, they acted directly — "we ourselves took vengeance" (003-005). This separated the religious sphere (prayer to Usen for strength, health, wisdom) from the sphere of justice and warfare. Vengeance was a human responsibility executed through direct action, not divine intervention.

### Collective and Individual Dimensions

While Geronimo's revenge was intensely personal, it also served collective purposes. His revenge against the Mexican troops avenged not only his own family but the broader Apache community. The raid on Kas-Ki-Yeh produced a tribal war authorized by the council of warriors (006-011), blending personal vengeance with collective military action.

## Examples in the Narrative

- **The vow after Kas-Ki-Yeh** (006-008) — After discovering his murdered family, Geronimo vowed vengeance upon the Mexican troopers, a vow that defined the subsequent decades of his life.
- **Battle fury driven by revenge** (006-025) — Geronimo describes fighting "with fury" as he thought of his murdered mother, wife, and babies and his vow of vengeance.
- **Revenge satisfied but not exhausted** (006-027–007-001) — After a major victory, Geronimo found satisfaction in revenge against the slain, yet still desired more, launching a campaign of several months against Mexican settlements.
- **The theological basis** (003-005) — The teaching that Usen does not care for the petty quarrels of men, requiring individuals to take vengeance themselves.
