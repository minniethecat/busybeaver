# Chief Election

[[apache-family|Apache]] chieftainship combined hereditary succession with formal election by the council of warriors — hereditary chiefs inherited their position through family lineage, but the council's vote was the only formal electoral mechanism in Apache governance, used exclusively for the election of the chief.

## Overview

Leadership among the [[apache-family|Apaches]] followed a hereditary model: [[geronimo|Geronimo]]'s grandfather [[maco|Maco]] had been chief of the [[bedonkohe-apache|Bedonkohe Apaches]], and after Maco's death, [[mangus-colorado|Mangus-Colorado]] became chief (005-002). [[naiche|Naiche]] is described as "the last hereditary chief of the hostile Apaches" (018-011), confirming the hereditary principle. However, the council of warriors retained the formal authority to elect the chief — this was, in Geronimo's words, "the only election by the council in formal assembly" beyond the initial admission of warriors through the four-expedition process (020-011).

## Description

### Hereditary Succession

Chiefs typically came from chiefly lineages. [[geronimo|Geronimo]]'s grandfather [[maco|Maco]] had been chief, and leadership passed through recognized family lines (005-001). [[mangus-colorado|Mangus-Colorado]], who was not directly in Geronimo's lineage, became chief after Maco's death, suggesting that while heredity was important, it was not absolute — the council's confirmation or election could place a different leader in power (005-002).

### Council Election

The council of warriors elected the chief in formal assembly. This was the sole electoral function of the council beyond the admission of new warriors (020-011). The election process balanced hereditary claims against demonstrated leadership ability, ensuring that the chief had the confidence of the warrior class he would lead in battle.

### Leadership Limitations

Old men, while their advice was respected, were not permitted to lead in battle — old age meant loss of physical power, which was "fatal to active leadership" (020-012). This limitation on elderly leadership ensured that war chiefs maintained the physical capability to lead combat operations.

### Multiple Chiefs

The [[apache-family|Apaches]] recognized multiple chiefs simultaneously, each governing different bands. [[geronimo|Geronimo]] mentions [[hash-ka-ai-la]], chief of a separate Apache group (002-004), [[cochise|Cochise]] as chief of the Chokonen Apaches (006-012), and [[mangus-colorado|Mangus-Colorado]] as chief of the [[bedonkohe-apache|Bedonkohe]] (005-002). This distributed leadership structure reflected the segmented nature of Apache tribal organization.

## Examples in the Narrative

- **Maco's chieftainship** (005-001) — [[geronimo|Geronimo]]'s grandfather [[maco|Maco]] served as chief of the [[bedonkohe-apache|Bedonkohe Apaches]], establishing the family's chiefly lineage.
- **Mangus-Colorado's succession** (005-002) — After Maco's death, [[mangus-colorado|Mangus-Colorado]] became chief, demonstrating the combination of hereditary expectation and council selection.
- **Naiche as hereditary chief** (018-011) — [[naiche|Naiche]] is described as the last hereditary chief of the hostile Apaches, confirming the hereditary principle endured into the final period of Apache resistance.
- **Cochise's council at dawn** (006-012) — [[cochise|Cochise]] as chief of the Chokonen convened the council in formal assembly, demonstrating chiefly authority over the warrior council.
