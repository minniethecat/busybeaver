# Council of Warriors

The council of warriors was the central governing institution in Apache society, serving as both a legislative body that authorized war expeditions and a gatekeeping organization that controlled admission to the warrior class through a rigorous process of apprenticeship and peer review.

## Overview

The council of warriors functioned as the collective decision-making body of Apache fighting men. It determined when the tribe would take the warpath, who would lead expeditions, and which young men had proven themselves worthy of warrior status. Geronimo describes his own admission to the council at age seventeen (005-004) as a transformative moment that granted him the privileges of the warpath. The council operated through formal assemblies where warriors sat arranged by rank, deliberated in silence before speaking, and reached decisions that bound the entire tribe.

## Description

### Admission to the Warrior Class

Becoming a warrior and gaining admission to the council required completing four war expeditions as a novice or apprentice (020-010). During these expeditions, the youth served as a servant to the experienced warriors — caring for horses, cooking food, and performing duties without being told (020-008). He was not permitted to speak to any warrior except in answer to questions or when instructed to speak. During this apprenticeship, he was expected to learn the sacred names of everything used in warfare, as after the tribe entered the warpath no common names were used — war was considered a solemn religious matter (020-009).

After four expeditions, the council of warriors voted on the youth's admission. The criteria were exacting: the youth had to demonstrate industry, discretion in speech, courage in battle, uncomplaining endurance of hardship, and an absence of any cowardice or weakness (020-010). A single warrior's objection was sufficient to block admission.

### Council Procedure and Deliberation

The council convened at the call of the chief. Geronimo describes Cochise, chief of the Chokonen Apaches, calling a council at early dawn where warriors assembled silently in a mountain dell, seated on the ground in rows according to their ranks (006-012). They sat smoking in silence until the chief signaled for the proceedings to begin — at which point the speaker rose to present his case. This formal, hierarchical procedure underscored the gravity with which the Apaches treated collective decision-making about war.

### Role in Authorizing War

The council held the sole authority to authorize war expeditions. After deliberating and preparing for the warpath, the council would initiate the war dance — a violent ceremony accompanied by singing, the beating of the *esadadene* (drum), and war whoops that sometimes nearly drowned the music (020-017). Only warriors participated in this dance. When Mangus-Colorado wished to wage war against Mexico, he first called a council and confirmed that all warriors were willing to take the warpath (006-011). Geronimo was then appointed to solicit aid from other Apache tribes.

### Election of Chiefs

Beyond the initial admission of warriors through the four-expedition apprenticeship system, the only formal election conducted by the council was the election of the chief (020-011). Old men, while their advice was always respected, were not permitted to lead in battle — old age meant loss of physical power, which was considered fatal to active leadership (020-012).

## Examples in the Narrative

- **Geronimo's admission (005-004)** — At age seventeen in 1846, Geronimo was admitted to the council of warriors, granting him the right to join war parties and the freedom to act independently. He describes this as a moment of great happiness.
- **Mangus-Colorado's war council (006-011)** — After the massacre of Geronimo's family at Kas-Ki-Yeh, Mangus-Colorado called a council that confirmed all warriors were willing to take the warpath against Mexico. Geronimo was dispatched to the Chokonen Apaches to solicit Cochise's aid.
- **Cochise's dawn council (006-012)** — Geronimo's appeal to the Chokonen Apaches was received in a formal council at early dawn. Warriors assembled in silence, arranged by rank in a mountain dell, and Geronimo presented his case for war against the Mexican troops.

> If, after four expeditions, all the warriors are satisfied that the youth has been industrious, has not spoken out of order, has been discreet in all things, has shown courage in battle, has borne all hardships uncomplainingly, and has exhibited no color of cowardice, or weakness of any kind, he may by vote of the council be admitted as a warrior; but if any warrior objects to him upon any account he is not accepted.
> — (020-010)

In this dance there is the usual singing led by the warriors and accompanied with the beating of the esadadene, but the dancing is more violent, and yells and war whoops sometimes almost drown the music. Only warriors participated in this dance.
> — (020-017)
