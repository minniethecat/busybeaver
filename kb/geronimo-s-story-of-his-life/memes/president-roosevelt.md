## Overview

Theodore Roosevelt (1858–1919) was the 26th President of the United States, serving from 1901 to 1909. In [[geronimo]]'s narrative, Roosevelt appears not as a military adversary but as the highest authority to whom the old Apache warrior appealed for the right to tell his story. Geronimo dedicated his autobiography to Roosevelt and participated in the President's 1905 inaugural parade (P01-001, 021-002).

## Life & Role

Geronimo dedicated his life story to Theodore Roosevelt, calling him "chief of a great people" and expressing his belief that Roosevelt was "fair-minded and will cause my people to receive justice in the future" (P01-001). The dedication was made with the permission Roosevelt had personally granted.

When [[s-m-barrett|S. M. Barrett]], the book's editor, was initially denied permission by the officer in charge at [[fort-sill]] (Lieutenant Purington) to write Geronimo's life story, Barrett appealed directly to President Roosevelt. Barrett wrote to the President arguing that Geronimo "had been held a prisoner of war for twenty years and had never been given a chance to tell his side of the story" (P03-005). Roosevelt granted the authorization by return mail, and shortly thereafter the War Department issued instructions permitting the project to proceed (P03-005).

After the manuscript was completed, it was submitted to President Roosevelt for review. Roosevelt responded with a letter advising that the editor "disclaim responsibility in all cases where the reputation of an individual is assailed" (P03-011). Barrett complied by appending notes throughout the book disclaiming responsibility for Geronimo's criticisms of named individuals (P03-011).

Barrett later acknowledged that "without the kindly advice and assistance of President Theodore Roosevelt this book could not have been written" (P02-004).

## Relationship to Geronimo

Roosevelt's relationship to Geronimo was that of a distant but decisive authority figure. Geronimo appealed to him as the highest chief of the United States, and Roosevelt responded by granting the old warrior a platform to tell his story. When [[general-miles|General Miles]] came to negotiate Geronimo's surrender in 1886, Miles told Geronimo that "The President of the United States has sent for you" (017-012), underscoring the symbolic weight of presidential authority in Geronimo's understanding of his captors. Geronimo later noted with approval that "the President of the United States is a Christian" (022-009), reflecting the importance he placed on the President's moral character.

General Miles invoked presidential authority during the surrender negotiations:

> General Miles said to me: "The President of the United States has sent me to speak to you. He has heard of your trouble with the white men, and says that if you will agree to a few words of treaty all will be satisfactorily arranged."
>
> (017-012)

## Key Events

- **Dedication of autobiography** — Geronimo dedicated his life story to Roosevelt as "chief of a great people" (P01-001)
- **Permission to publish** — Roosevelt personally authorized Geronimo to tell his story for publication after Barrett's appeal (P03-005)
- **Manuscript review** — Roosevelt read the completed manuscript and advised that a disclaimer be included for criticisms of individuals (P03-011)
- **1905 Inaugural Parade** — Geronimo rode in Roosevelt's inaugural parade; "Every Sunday the President of the Fair sent for me to go to a wild west show" (021-004)
