# Treaty Making

Treaty making among the Apaches ranged from inter-tribal peace councils conducted through traditional diplomatic protocols to formal agreements with the United States government — a practice that Geronimo describes as operating under fundamentally different understandings between the two parties.

## Overview

The Apaches had their own traditions of making peace agreements with other tribes, convening councils at neutral locations and sealing treaties through symbolic acts such as shaking hands (013-001). When dealing with the United States government, these traditional practices met a very different system of written treaties, military negotiations, and — from Geronimo's perspective — promises that were not kept. Geronimo expresses deep skepticism about American treaty-making: "General Miles made the treaty with the Apaches, but we know very well that the Government subsequently treated the prisoners of war" in ways inconsistent with the agreement (P03-018).

## Description

### Inter-Tribal Peace Treaties

Apache chiefs convened peace councils with other tribes at designated locations. Geronimo describes the chiefs of several tribes assembling at Casa Grande to make a treaty of peace (011-010). These councils followed established protocols — the parties met, deliberated, and sealed agreements through ritual acts. Traditional Apache treaties were made face-to-face, with the spoken word and handshake serving as bonds (013-001).

### Treaties with Military Outposts

Geronimo made local agreements with military posts, such as the treaty he negotiated at Apache Pass while stationed there (013-007). These local arrangements differed from the grand treaties negotiated by generals — they were practical, situational agreements between Apache leaders and post commanders.

### The Treaty with General Miles

The definitive treaty that ended Geronimo's warfare was made with General Miles in 1886. From Geronimo's perspective, this treaty was not honored: the terms under which he surrendered — particularly regarding the treatment of Apaches as prisoners of war — were, in his view, violated by the government afterward (P03-018). This experience colored Apache attitudes toward American treaty-making as inherently unreliable.

### Contrast in Understanding

A fundamental gap existed between Apache and American conceptions of treaties. Apache agreements were personal, oral, and situational, rooted in the honor of the chiefs who made them. American treaties were written documents interpreted by a distant government that could override the promises made by its military officers. Geronimo's narrative reflects this gap — the American officers who made the treaty were not the same people as the government that subsequently determined the prisoners' fate.

## Examples in the Narrative

- **Casa Grande peace council** (011-010) — Chiefs of multiple tribes assembled to make a treaty of peace, following traditional Apache diplomatic protocols.
- **Treaty at Apache Pass** (013-007) — Geronimo made a treaty with the military post at Apache Pass, a local arrangement with practical terms.
- **The General Miles treaty** (P03-018) — The surrender treaty of 1886, which Geronimo believed was not honored by the United States government regarding the treatment of Apache prisoners of war.
