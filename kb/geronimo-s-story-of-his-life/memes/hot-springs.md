# Hot Springs

Hot Springs (Ojo Caliente), located in what is now New Mexico, was the homeland of the Chihenne (Ojo Caliente) Apache band and one of the few places where Geronimo experienced genuine peace and hospitality during his long years of conflict. Chief [[victoria|Victoria]] of the Chihenne Apaches extended friendship and material support to Geronimo's Bedonkohe band, making Hot Springs a sanctuary — until American military authority turned it into a place of imprisonment.

## Geography

Geronimo locates Hot Springs relative to other Apache territories: "East of us lived the Chi-hen-ne (Ojo Caliente), (Hot Springs) Apaches" (002-004). The settlement was situated near the Gila River drainage, approximately "two miles above Hot Springs" (015-005) where the Chihenne held their celebrations. The hot springs themselves gave the location its name in both English and Spanish (Ojo Caliente).

## Role in the Narrative

### Friendship with Victoria

Geronimo's relationship with Chief Victoria and the Chihenne band is one of the few consistently positive themes in his narrative:

> Victoria, their chief, was always a friend to me. He always helped our tribe when we asked him for help. He lost his life in the defense of the rights of his people. He was a good man and a brave warrior. (002-004)

### A Year of Peace

After fleeing from Apache Pass, Geronimo brought his depleted band to Hot Springs seeking refuge:

> We had heard that Chief Victoria of the Chihenne (Oje Caliente) Apaches was holding a council with the white men near Hot Springs in New Mexico, and that he had plenty of provisions. ... We went to Hot Springs. We easily found Victoria and his band, and they gave us supplies for the winter. We stayed with them for about a year, and during this stay we had perfect peace. We had not the least trouble with Mexicans, white men, or Indians. (015-003)

### The Four-Day Feast

When Geronimo decided to leave Victoria's band, the chief insisted on a farewell celebration:

> The festivities were held about two miles above Hot Springs, and lasted for four days. There were about four hundred Indians at this celebration. I do not think we ever spent a more pleasant time than upon this occasion. No one ever treated our tribe more kindly than Victoria and his band. We are still proud to say that he and his people were our friends. (015-005)

This passage is remarkable in Geronimo's narrative — a moment of pure joy and community, unmarred by violence or betrayal.

### Arrest at Hot Springs

The sanctuary of Hot Springs was violated when military authorities from San Carlos arrived:

> Soon after we arrived in New Mexico two companies of scouts were sent from San Carlos. When they came to Hot Springs they sent word for me and Victoria to come to town. ... As soon as we arrived in town soldiers met us, disarmed us, and took us both to headquarters, where we were tried by court-martial. ... Victoria was released and I was sentenced to the guardhouse. Scouts conducted me to the guardhouse and put me in chains.

Geronimo was imprisoned for the act of leaving Apache Pass and returning to Hot Springs — a decision he considered entirely justified. He notes that other Indians who had followed him were also chained, though he insisted: "If it was wrong (and I do not think it was wrong) for us to go to Hot Springs, I alone was to blame".

### Return During the Final Campaign

During the final pursuit of 1885–1886, Geronimo again sought the familiar terrain near Hot Springs:

> With six men and four women I made for the range of mountains near Hot Springs, New Mexico. We passed many cattle ranches, but had no trouble with the cowboys. We killed cattle to eat whenever we were in need of food, but we frequently suffered greatly for water. (017-001)

## Significance

Hot Springs represents the tragedy of Apache-American relations in microcosm. It was a place of genuine peace and inter-band Apache solidarity, yet it became the site of Geronimo's arrest and imprisonment — punishment for the simple act of returning to a place he considered home. Victoria's friendship stands in Geronimo's memory as a counterpoint to the betrayals committed by American authorities.
