# San Carlos Reservation

The San Carlos Apache Indian Reservation in southeastern Arizona was a central location in the later chapters of [[geronimo|Geronimo]]'s life. It was the site of multiple confinements, confrontations with military authorities, and the place where a railroad station was named "Geronimo" after him.

## Confinement and Release

Geronimo records spending time at San Carlos between his campaigns: "We remained in Arizona for some time, living in San Carlos Reservation, at a place now called Geronimo" (012-002). This period followed the Casa Grande massacre, and from San Carlos he launched another expedition into Mexico in 1883, remaining in the mountain ranges "for about fourteen months" (012-002), skirmishing with Mexican troops before returning to Arizona in 1884 to recruit more Apaches (012-002).

## Imprisonment and Trial

San Carlos was also where Geronimo endured one of his imprisonments. After being disarmed and taken at Hot Springs, he records: "I was kept a prisoner for four months, during which time I was transferred to San Carlos" (013-010). He was subjected to a trial — "Then I think I had another trial, although I was not present" (013-010) — and eventually released, though he "never felt at ease any longer at the Post" (013-011). After his release, "We were allowed to live above San Carlos at a place now called Geronimo" (013-011), under an agent the Indians called Nick Golee, where "All went well here for a period of two years, but we were not satisfied" (013-011).

## Confrontation with General Crook

One of the most dramatic episodes at San Carlos involved General Crook's confiscation of Apache livestock. After returning to San Carlos with a herd of cattle and horses taken from Mexicans during their wars, Geronimo protested when Crook seized the animals: "I told him that these were not white men's cattle, but belonged to us, for we had taken them from the Mexicans during our wars. I also told him that we did not intend to kill these animals, but that we wished to keep them and raise stock on our range" (014-005). Crook refused, and when Geronimo went near Fort Apache, "General Crook ordered officers, soldiers, and scouts to see that I was arrested; if I offered resistance they were instructed to kill me" (014-005).

Later, when Crook tracked him to the Sierra de Antunez Mountains in Mexico and asked why he had left the reservation, Geronimo replied: "You told me that I might live in the reservation the same as white people lived. One year I raised a crop of corn, and gathered and stored it, and the next year I put in a crop of oats, and when the crop was almost ready to harvest, you told your soldiers to put me in prison, and if I resisted to kill me. If I had been let alone I would now have been in good circumstances, but instead of that you and the Mexicans are hunting me with soldiers" (014-006). Crook denied giving such orders and Geronimo agreed to return to San Carlos (014-006).

> Soon after we arrived at San Carlos the officer in charge, General Crook, took the horses and cattle away from us. I told him that these were not white men's cattle, but belonged to us, for we had taken them from the Mexicans during our wars. I also told him that we did not intend to kill these animals, but that we wished to keep them and raise stock on our range. He would not listen to me, but took the stock.

## A Place Called Geronimo

The naming of a location "Geronimo" on the Southern Pacific Railroad line through the reservation reflects the complicated legacy of the Apache leader — simultaneously a prisoner of the U.S. government and a figure whose name became embedded in the geography of the region. Geronimo mentions this twice: "living in San Carlos Reservation, at a place now called Geronimo" (012-002) and "We were allowed to live above San Carlos at a place now called Geronimo" (013-011).

## The Reservation as Epicenter

San Carlos was the gravitational center of Geronimo's reservation-era experience. Military patrols repeatedly "went back toward San Carlos" after engagements (011-003, 011-004). After internal conflicts among the Apache bands — including four warriors killed in a drunken brawl — "Some of them went to San Carlos and some to Old Mexico" (013-006), while Geronimo himself returned to Hot Springs (013-006). The reservation appears repeatedly as the destination for military escorts, the source of scout companies, and the place from which Geronimo repeatedly departed for campaigns in Mexico.

## After the Surrender

Following the final surrender, Geronimo and other Apache prisoners of war were not returned to San Carlos but were sent to [[fort-sill|Fort Sill]], Oklahoma, and other locations far from their Arizona homeland. The reservation system represented a fundamental transformation of Apache life — from the free-ranging existence of the pre-reservation era to the constrained circumstances that Geronimo's autobiography both documents and resists.
