# Chief Victoria

Chief Victoria was the leader of the [[chihenne-apache|Chihenne]] (Ojo Caliente, or Hot Springs) Apache band who lived to the east of [[geronimo|Geronimo]]'s [[bedonkohe-apache|Bedonkohe]] people. Victoria is portrayed in Geronimo's autobiography as a steadfast friend and ally — a chief distinguished by his kindness, generosity, and ultimate sacrifice in defense of his people's rights.

## Relationship with Geronimo

Geronimo describes Victoria in unequivocally positive terms. Unlike the complex and sometimes adversarial relationships Geronimo had with other Apache leaders, Victoria was consistently a friend:

> Our tribe never had any difficulty with them. Victoria, their chief, was always a friend to me. He always helped our tribe when we asked him for help. He lost his life in the defense of the rights of his people. He was a good man. (002-003)

This tribute — appearing early in the narrative, in the chapter on Apache tribal subdivisions — establishes Victoria as one of the few Apache chiefs Geronimo praises without reservation.

## Hospitality at Hot Springs

One of the most significant episodes involving Victoria occurred when [[geronimo|Geronimo]]'s band, displaced and seeking refuge, traveled to Hot Springs in New Mexico where Victoria was holding council with American authorities. The Chihenne chief welcomed them warmly:

> We had heard that Chief Victoria of the Chihenne (Oje Caliente) Apaches was holding a council with the white men near Hot Springs in New Mexico, and that he had plenty of provisions. We had always been on friendly terms with this tribe, and Victoria was especially kind to my people. (015-002)

The gathering at Hot Springs lasted four days and brought together approximately four hundred Indians in what Geronimo remembered as one of the most pleasant occasions of his life:

> No one ever treated our tribe more kindly than Victoria and his band. We are still proud to say that Victoria was our friend. (015-003)

## Shared Captivity

After the festivities at Hot Springs, Geronimo's band remained in the area, expecting to live peaceably alongside Victoria's people. When two companies of scouts arrived from San Carlos, they summoned both Geronimo and Victoria to town (016-001). Geronimo's band had "quietly withdrawn" from Apache Pass, "expecting to live with Victoria's band, where we thought we would not be molested" (016-002). This shared experience of displacement and military scrutiny bound the two leaders together.

## Family

Victoria had at least one son, Charley, who is listed alongside [[naiche|Naiche]] (son of Coche) and Asa (son of [[whoa|Whoa]]) as one of three Apache chieftains of the next generation (009-004).

## Key Events

- **Pre-1880s** — Victoria maintains friendly relations with Geronimo's Bedonkohe band (002-003)
- **Council at Hot Springs** — Victoria hosts a four-day gathering of approximately 400 Indians near Hot Springs, New Mexico, providing provisions and hospitality to Geronimo's people (015-002, 015-003)
- **Arrival of Scouts** — Victoria and Geronimo are summoned together by scouts from San Carlos (016-001)
- **Death** — Victoria "lost his life in the defense of the rights of his people" (002-003)
