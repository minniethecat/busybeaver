# Alope

Alope was Geronimo's first wife, the daughter of No-po-so, whom Geronimo married shortly after being admitted to the council of warriors — and whose murder along with their three small children in the Mexican massacre at Kas-Ki-Yeh became the driving force behind Geronimo's decades-long campaign of vengeance.

## Overview

Alope appears in Geronimo's narrative in three concentrated passages: his courtship and marriage to her after gaining warrior status (005-005), their brief domestic life together (005-006), and the discovery of her murder at Kas-Ki-Yeh (006-007). She is described as "a slender, delicate girl" and "the fair Alope." Though she appears in only a few paragraphs of the autobiography, her death is the emotional and narrative fulcrum of the entire book — Geronimo's vow of vengeance against the Mexican troopers who killed her and their children drives nearly every subsequent military action he describes.

## Life & Role

### Courtship and Marriage

After Geronimo was admitted to the council of warriors at age seventeen, he gained the right to marry. He had long loved Alope, the daughter of No-po-so. Geronimo describes her as "a slender, delicate girl, but we had been lovers for a long time" (005-005). He approached her father to negotiate the marriage, but No-po-so asked "many ponies" for her — perhaps hoping to keep his dutiful daughter at home. Geronimo made no reply but returned days later with a herd of ponies and took Alope as his wife.

### Family Life

Geronimo and Alope established their home not far from his mother's teepee. Alope made decorations for their dwelling — the "little decorations that Alope had made" that Geronimo would later burn in grief (006-007). They had three children together during their brief marriage.

### Death at Kas-Ki-Yeh

In the summer of approximately 1851, while Geronimo and the other warriors were away on a trading expedition, Mexican troops attacked the Apache settlement at Kas-Ki-Yeh. When Geronimo returned, he found his aged mother, his young wife Alope, and his three small children all murdered. He burned their teepee, the decorations Alope had made, and his mother's property, then "stood by the grave of my wife and children" and vowed vengeance (006-008).

## Relationship to Geronimo

Alope was Geronimo's first and deepest love. His description of their courtship is one of the few tender passages in the autobiography: "Perhaps our love was of no interest to him; perhaps he wanted to keep Alope with him, for she was a dutiful daughter" (005-005). Her death transforms Geronimo from a young warrior seeking status and adventure into a man driven by grief and rage. His vow at her grave — "I had vowed vengeance upon the Mexican troopers who had wronged me" — echoes through every subsequent chapter.
