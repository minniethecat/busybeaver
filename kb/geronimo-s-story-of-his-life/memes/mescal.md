## Overview

Mescal was a distilled spirit obtained by Apaches through trade or capture from Mexican sources. Unlike the traditional fermented corn beverage [[tiswin]], mescal was an external intoxicant, stronger in its effects and associated with Mexican commerce. Its consumption by Apache war parties led to near-catastrophic loss of discipline on at least two occasions in Geronimo's account, revealing both the dangers of alcohol in a warrior culture and the strategic exploitation of intoxicants in the Apache–Mexican conflict.

## Description

Mescal was typically transported in bottles held in wicker baskets. It was not produced by the Apaches themselves but acquired from Mexicans — either through trade at towns like Casa Grande or as booty captured from pack trains. Its potency was significantly greater than tiswin, the traditional Apache fermented corn drink.

Geronimo's narrative reveals an ambivalent attitude toward alcohol: he acknowledges drinking it himself but describes the severe tactical dangers it posed when warriors lost discipline. His accounts of mescal-related incidents demonstrate his understanding of alcohol as both a social lubricant in cross-cultural trade and a dangerous substance that could undermine Apache military effectiveness. Crucially, mescal was not part of Apache material culture — it arrived through the same cross-border economy that brought firearms, ammunition, and other trade goods.


> As soon as we made camp the Indians began to get drunk and fight each other. I, too, drank enough mescal to feel the effect of it, but I was not drunk. I ordered the fighting stopped, but the order was disobeyed. Finally the camp became comparatively still, for the Indians were too drunk to walk or even to fight. While they were in this stupor I poured out all the mescal, then I put out all the fires and moved the pack mules to a considerable distance from camp.
>
> (008-003)

## Examples in the Narrative

- **The Captured Mescal Train (1861)** — Geronimo's raiding party captured a mule pack train loaded with provisions, including mescal. "As soon as we made camp the Indians began to get drunk and fight each other" (008-002). Geronimo himself "drank enough mescal to feel the effect of it" but remained sober enough to recognize the danger. When the warriors became "too drunk to walk or even to fight," Geronimo "poured out all the mescal" and moved the pack mules to safety, then kept guard alone all night (008-002). His decision to destroy the mescal rather than let it destroy his war party demonstrates the leadership qualities that, despite his lack of formal chiefly status, earned him the trust of warriors across multiple Apache bands.

- **The Casa Grande Treaty Ambush** — After making a peace treaty at Casa Grande, "the Mexicans gave us mescal. Soon nearly all the Indians were drunk. While they were drunk two companies of Mexican troops, from another town, attacked us, killed twenty Indians, and captured many more" (012-003). This incident reveals mescal used as a weapon — Mexican authorities exploited Apache drinking to incapacitate warriors before a coordinated attack. The treaty turned out to be a pretext for ambush, and the mescal was the instrument that made the ambush possible.

## Cultural Significance

The contrast between mescal and tiswin illuminates a broader pattern in Apache–Mexican relations. Tiswin, produced by Apache women from Apache corn, was embedded in Apache social life and ceremony. Mescal, by contrast, arrived through the cross-border economy — a commodity that brought with it not only intoxication but also the asymmetrical power relations of colonial exchange. When Mexicans gave mescal to Apaches, it was sometimes a gesture of trade or diplomacy; at other times, as at Casa Grande, it was a deliberate stratagem. Geronimo's wariness of mescal — his willingness to pour it out rather than let it destroy his warriors — reflects his recognition of this dual nature.
