# Warrior Initiation

Warrior initiation was the formal process by which Apache youths became recognized warriors, requiring completion of four war expeditions as an apprentice — serving the experienced warriors, learning sacred war names, and proving courage and character — before being admitted by vote of the council of warriors.

## Overview

Becoming a warrior was not automatic for Apache males. A youth had to complete a demanding four-expedition apprenticeship during which he served as a servant to experienced warriors, performing whatever duties were required without being told, while remaining silent except when spoken to (020-008). Only after all warriors were satisfied with his industry, discretion, courage, endurance, and absence of any cowardice could he be admitted by vote (020-010). Geronimo himself underwent this process and was admitted to the council of warriors at age seventeen in 1846 (005-004).

## Description

### The Four-Expedition Requirement

To be admitted as a warrior, a youth had to go with the warriors on the warpath four times as an apprentice (020-006). On each expedition, he acted as a servant — caring for horses, cooking food, and performing whatever duties were needed without waiting to be told (020-008). He was not allowed to speak to any warrior except when answering questions or when specifically instructed to speak.

### Learning the Sacred Names

During these four expeditions, the apprentice was expected to learn the sacred names of everything used in warfare. After the tribe entered upon the warpath, no common names were used for anything related to war — warfare was considered a solemn religious matter (020-009). This linguistic training embedded the apprentice in the sacred dimension of Apache martial culture.

### The Council Vote

After completion of four expeditions, the warriors would convene to vote on the youth's admission. The criteria were comprehensive: the youth must have been industrious, discreet in speech, courageous in battle, uncomplaining in hardship, and free of any cowardice or weakness. A single warrior's objection was sufficient to deny admission (020-010). This demanding standard ensured that only those who had thoroughly proven themselves could join the warrior class.

### Geronimo's Initiation

Geronimo describes his own admission at age seventeen as a moment of transformation. The customs of the tribe had previously prohibited him from sharing the glories of the warpath until the council admitted him (005-004). Once admitted, he could go wherever he wanted, join war parties freely, and act as an independent warrior. He describes this as making him "very happy."

## Examples in the Narrative

- **Geronimo's admission** (005-004) — At seventeen years old in 1846, Geronimo was admitted to the council of the warriors after completing his apprenticeship. He describes the freedom and status this conferred.
- **The apprentice's duties** (020-008) — A detailed description of the servant role apprentices played on expeditions — caring for horses, cooking, anticipating needs without instruction, and maintaining silence except when addressed.
- **The sacred war language** (020-009) — During apprenticeship, the youth learned the sacred names that replaced common speech for all things related to war once the tribe was on the warpath.
- **The final vote** (020-010) — The council's vote required unanimous consent; any warrior could object, and a single objection blocked admission.
