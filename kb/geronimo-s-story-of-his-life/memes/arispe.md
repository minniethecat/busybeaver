# Arispe

Arispe, a Mexican town in northern Sonora, appears repeatedly in Geronimo's narrative as a focal point of Apache raiding activity. Located near the strategic corridor between the Sierra Madre Mountains and the Arizona border, Arispe was the site of several significant engagements during Geronimo's decades-long campaign against Mexican settlements.

## Geography

Geronimo situates Arispe in the mountains north of the main travel routes through Sonora. In one expedition, he describes camping "north of Arispe" (012-001) after a series of successful raids. The town's position made it a natural target for Apaches moving south from Arizona toward the richer settlements of central Sonora, and it served as a staging point for Mexican military responses.

## Role in the Narrative

### The Arispe Ambush

One of Geronimo's most decisive engagements occurred near Arispe:

> When we were almost at Arispe we camped, and eight men rode out from the city to parley with us. These we captured, killed, and scalped. This was to draw the troops from the city, and the next day they came. The skirmishing lasted all day without a general engagement, but just at night we captured their supply train, so we had plenty of provisions and some more guns. (008-008)

The tactic of killing the parley party to draw out Mexican troops — and then capturing their supply train rather than engaging directly — exemplifies Geronimo's strategic approach to warfare.

### The Cattle Raid

In the summer of 1865, Geronimo led a particularly successful expedition that culminated near Arispe:

> When we had come back northwest of Arispe we secured about sixty head of cattle, and drove them to our homes in Arizona. We did not go directly home, but camped in different valleys with our cattle. We were not trailed. (010-025)

### The Ambush Reversed

Not all encounters at Arispe ended in Apache victory. In fall 1865, Geronimo was ambushed near the town:

> When near Arispe we made camp one evening, and thinking that we were not being trailed, turned loose the whole herd, even those we had been riding. ... Just as we had begun to eat our supper our scouts came in and announced Mexican troops coming toward our camp. We started for the horses, but troops that our scouts had not seen were on the cliffs above us, and opened fire. We scattered in all directions, and the troops recovered all our booty. (011-002)

The loss of the entire herd — captured after weeks of raiding — was a significant setback. The Apaches reassembled three days later "at our appointed place of rendezvous in the Sierra Madre Mountains in northern Sonora" (011-002) and returned to Arizona empty-handed.

### The 1867 Defeat

The most stinging defeat near Arispe came under [[mangus-colorado|Mangus-Colorado's]] leadership in 1867:

> When we were not far from Arispe, Mexican troops rode upon us. They were well armed and well mounted, and when we first saw them they were not half a mile away from us. We left the cattle and rode as hard as we could toward the mountains, but they gained on us rapidly. (011-004)

Geronimo took command of the subsequent expedition, leading six warriors on foot directly toward Arispe and successfully raiding several settlements at night, capturing horses, mules, saddles, and blankets before returning safely to Arizona (011-005).

### A Besieged Camp

During the final campaigns, Geronimo found himself cornered near Arispe:

> We had lost about fifteen warriors in Arizona, and had gained no recruits. With our reduced number we camped in the mountains north of Arispe. Mexican troops were seen by our scouts in several directions. The United States troops were coming down from the north. We were well armed with guns and supplied with ammunition, but we did not care to be surrounded by the troops of two governments, so we started to move our camp southward. (016-003)

This passage captures the closing vise of the Apache Wars — Mexican troops to the south, American troops to the north, and Geronimo's dwindling band caught between them.

## Significance

Arispe serves as a microcosm of Geronimo's raiding career: moments of brilliant tactical success alternating with devastating losses, ambushes both given and received, and the gradual tightening of military pressure that would ultimately force his surrender. The town's repeated appearance in the narrative — across chapters 8 through 16 — underscores its strategic importance in the geography of Apache-Mexican conflict.
