# Prisoner Customs

Apache customs governing prisoners of war reflected a warrior code that distinguished between killing enemies in battle and the treatment of captives — prisoners were never chained or confined, and captured individuals could be adopted into the tribe, though enemy combatants were often killed rather than taken alive.

## Overview

The Apaches maintained clear customs regarding prisoners. Geronimo states explicitly: "We never chained prisoners or kept them in confinement" (007-016). When captives were taken, they might be adopted into the tribe — particularly women and children. However, explicit orders were sometimes given to take no prisoners, as when Mexican forces declared "take no prisoners; dead Indians are what we want" (012-007). Geronimo himself was held as a prisoner of war for over twenty years, and his narrative reflects the Apache experience of being on both sides of captivity.

## Description

### Apache Treatment of Captives

Apache warriors did not chain or imprison captives. Geronimo records that "we never chained prisoners or kept them in confinement, but they seldom attempted to escape" (007-016) — suggesting that captives were integrated into the community rather than incarcerated. The practice of adopting captives, particularly women and children, was common. When raiding, warriors might "take them prisoners" if they did not resist (011-001), indicating a distinction between combatants (who were killed) and non-combatants (who could be captured).

### Enemy Policies Toward Apache Prisoners

Mexican and American forces had different approaches. Mexican troops sometimes operated under explicit "no prisoners" orders — Geronimo records that Mexican soldiers were told "take no prisoners; dead Indians are what we want" (012-007). American forces, by contrast, held Apaches as prisoners of war under treaty arrangements. Geronimo spent over twenty years as a prisoner of war, first in Florida, then at Fort Sill (003-003).

### The Prisoner-of-War Experience

Geronimo's narrative is framed by his status as a prisoner of war. The preface notes that the book is "the record of the private life of the Apache Indians" and extends "to Geronimo as a prisoner of war the courtesy due any captive" (P02-001). Geronimo himself states simply: "We are held as prisoners of war in this Military Reserve" (003-003). This status colored every aspect of his later life and the book itself.

## Examples in the Narrative

- **No chains, no confinement** (007-016) — Geronimo emphasizes that Apaches never chained prisoners, a point of honor in contrast to their own treatment as prisoners of war.
- **Mexican no-prisoner orders** (012-007) — Mexican commanders explicitly ordered their troops to take no prisoners, reflecting the total-war character of the Apache-Mexican conflict.
- **Geronimo's captivity** (003-003, P02-001) — The framing of the entire autobiography within Geronimo's status as a prisoner of war held for over two decades.
