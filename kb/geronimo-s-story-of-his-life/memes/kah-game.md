## Overview

The Kah (foot) game was the most noted gambling game of the [[apache-family|Apache]] tribe, played at night around a campfire after feasts and dances. [[geronimo|Geronimo]] describes it as "seldom played except as a gambling game, but for that purpose it is the most popular game known to the tribe" (004-003). The game reenacted the creation legend in which the eagle slew a nameless monster with a white rock.

## Description

The game was set up with four moccasins placed in holes dug in two parallel rows on opposite sides of the camp (004-002). A campfire burned between the rows. The score was kept by a bundle of sticks, with each side taking a stick for every point won.

One side took the bone — a symbol of the white rock used by the eagle to slay the nameless monster from the [[apache-family|Apache]] creation story — and hid it in one of the four moccasins behind blankets, while singing the legends of creation (004-002). The side holding the bone represented the feathered tribe (birds); the opposing side represented the beasts.

When the blankets were thrown down, a chosen player from the opposing team approached with a war club and struck the moccasin believed to contain the bone (004-002). If correct, that side gained the bone and the right to sing. The game consisted of only four plays: three that lose and one that wins. When all sticks were gone from the bundle, the side with more sticks won.

A typical game lasted four or five hours and was never played in daytime (004-003).

Geronimo describes the game's ritual setup:

> Four moccasins are placed about four feet apart in holes in the ground, dug in a row on one side of the camp, and on the opposite side a similar parallel row. At night a campfire is started between these two rows of moccasins, and the players are arranged on sides, one or any number on each side.
>
> (004-002)
