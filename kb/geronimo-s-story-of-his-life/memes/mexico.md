## Overview

Mexico (referred to as "Old Mexico" in the narrative) is the country bordering the Apache homeland to the south. Throughout Geronimo's autobiography, Mexico serves as both the site of profound personal tragedy — the massacre of his family at [[kas-ki-yeh-massacre|Kas-ki-yeh]] — and the theater of decades of retaliatory raids. The Apache–Mexican conflict forms the central thread of the book's middle chapters.

## Geography

Mexico lies south of Arizona, with the Mexican states of [[sonora]] and Chihuahua bordering Apache territory. The narrative describes the [[sierra-madre|Sierra Madre]] Mountains running north–south through the country, the Sierra de Antunez range in northern Sonora, and the Sierra de Sahuaripa Mountains. Geronimo's war parties traveled southward "past Quitaco, Nacozari, and many smaller settlements," following "river courses and mountain ranges" to conceal their movements. The Mexican border region is described as a zone of constant crossing, with Apache families hidden "in a mountain rendezvous near the Mexican border" (006-016) during wartime.

## Role in the Narrative

Mexico is the dominant external setting in Geronimo's story, appearing in nearly every chapter from VI through XVII. Key episodes include:

- **The Kas-ki-yeh Massacre (1858)** — Mexican troops attacked Geronimo's camp while the warriors were away trading in a Mexican town, killing his mother, wife, and three children (006-002). This event became the driving motivation for Geronimo's lifelong war against Mexicans.

- **The Battle of Kas-ki-yeh (1859)** — Geronimo led a coalition of three Apache tribes in a revenge battle against the Mexican troops responsible for the massacre. He was given the honor of directing the battle "because I had been more deeply wronged than others" (006-012). The Apaches killed the entire Mexican force.

- **Repeated Raids (1860–1886)** — Geronimo led numerous expeditions into [[sonora]] and Chihuahua, raiding villages, ambushing Mexican troops, and seizing supplies. Many of these raids resulted in Apache casualties, but Geronimo's "feelings toward the Mexicans did not change" (007-004).

- **Mexican Counterattacks** — Mexican troops repeatedly pursued the Apaches into Arizona, attacking their settlements at daybreak. On one occasion "three companies of Mexican troops who had surrounded our settlement in the night opened fire" (007-013), killing women and children and burning teepees.

- **Captives and Slavery** — The narrative records that captured Apache women were "compelled to work for the Mexicans" and that "many women and children were carried away at different times by Mexicans", while captured Mexicans "were compelled to cut wood and herd horses" — though Geronimo emphasizes Apaches "never chained prisoners."

> I was never again contented in our quiet home. True, I could visit my father's grave, but I had vowed vengeance upon the Mexican troopers who had wronged me, and whenever I came near his grave or saw anything to remind me of former happy days my heart would ache for revenge upon Mexico.
>
> (006-008)
