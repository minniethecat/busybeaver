# Mangus-Colorado

Mangus-Colorado was the chief of the Bedonkohe Apaches who succeeded Geronimo's grandfather Maco as leader, commanded Apache forces through the central years of the Mexican conflict, and was treacherously killed in 1863 after being captured while under a flag of truce — an event that Geronimo cites as one of the greatest wrongs done to his people.

## Overview

Mangus-Colorado ("Red Sleeve") was the dominant Apache military and political leader during the middle decades of the 19th century. He became chief of the Bedonkohe Apaches after the death of Maco (Geronimo's grandfather) and led the tribe through years of warfare against Mexican forces. Geronimo served under his command through multiple campaigns, describing Mangus-Colorado as a leader who called councils, authorized war parties, divided commands among subordinate chiefs, and negotiated treaties. His death — killed by American soldiers after being captured while approaching under a white flag — is recounted by Geronimo as one of the signal betrayals that shaped Apache distrust of American authorities (016-006, 018-011).

## Life & Role

### Rise to Chieftainship

Mangus-Colorado became chief of the Bedonkohe Apaches after the death of Maco, when Geronimo's father was still a young warrior (005-002). By the time Geronimo reached warrior age in 1846, Mangus-Colorado was the established leader who called councils, authorized war parties, and commanded the tribe's military operations.

### Military Leadership

Mangus-Colorado led numerous campaigns against Mexican forces. He gave the order to march immediately after the Kas-Ki-Yeh massacre (006-003), called the council that confirmed all warriors were willing to take the warpath against Mexico, and appointed Geronimo to solicit aid from other Apache tribes (006-011). He took personal command of divisions in major battles, including an engagement where he "took command of one division" while the warriors were assembled (008-004). He led eight warriors on a raid into Mexico in 1867 (009-005), and his warriors remained eager to follow him on subsequent expeditions (009-007).

### Treaty Making

Mangus-Colorado engaged in diplomatic efforts with both Mexicans and Americans. He went to make a treaty of peace with Mexican authorities, accompanied by three other warriors (014-001, 014-002). He also made a treaty with the American post at Apache Pass, as did Cochise — though Geronimo notes he does not know the terms (013-007).

### Death

Mangus-Colorado's death is treated by Geronimo as one of the greatest wrongs inflicted on the Apaches. He "had, years ago, been foully murdered" (018-011). Geronimo specifically lists "the fate of Mangus-Colorado" alongside his own unjust imprisonment as the grievances that drove the final period of Apache resistance (016-006). The chief was killed after being taken prisoner while approaching under a flag of truce — a violation of the most fundamental norms of warfare.

## Relationship to Geronimo

Mangus-Colorado was Geronimo's chief and commander through the formative and most active years of Geronimo's warrior life. Geronimo served under him from his admission to the warrior council in 1846 through multiple campaigns against Mexico. Mangus-Colorado appointed Geronimo as an envoy to other Apache tribes to solicit military aid (006-011), indicating trust in the younger warrior's diplomatic abilities. Geronimo's account of Mangus-Colorado is consistently respectful — he portrays him as a legitimate, capable leader whose treacherous death was a defining injustice.
