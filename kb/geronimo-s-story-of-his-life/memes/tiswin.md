## Overview

Tiswin (sometimes spelled "tizwin") was a fermented beverage made from corn by [[apache-family|Apache]] women and children. [[geronimo|Geronimo]] describes it as "very highly prized by the Indians" (003-003) for its intoxicating effects. Its consumption — and abuse — appears at multiple significant junctures in the narrative, sometimes with fatal consequences that shaped the course of Apache–U.S. relations.

## Description

The production of tiswin is described in the chapter on tribal customs:

> "Besides grinding the corn (by hand with stone mortars and pestles) for bread, we sometimes crushed it and soaked it, and after it had fermented made from this juice a *tiswin*, which had the power of intoxication, and was very highly prized by the Indians" (003-003).

The work of making tiswin was "done by the squaws and children," part of the broader division of labor in [[apache-family|Apache]] society where women managed food production while men hunted and fought. Corn, the base ingredient, was a staple crop cultivated by Apache women in mountain clearings and along streams.

The drink was consumed socially and ceremonially. Unlike [[mescal]] — a distilled spirit acquired through trade with [[mexico|Mexicans]] — tiswin was an indigenous product, made entirely within Apache communities from locally grown corn. This distinction mattered: tiswin belonged to Apache culture, while mescal was an external intoxicant that arrived through cross-cultural exchange.

## Examples in the Narrative

- **Post-Reservation Violence** — After the departure of [[general-howard|General Howard]] from Apache Pass, "some Indians at the Post were drunk on *tiswin*, which they had made from corn. They fought among themselves and four of them were killed" (016-009). This incident contributed directly to [[geronimo|Geronimo]]'s decision to separate his band from the other Apache groups and return to [[hot-springs|Hot Springs]], a move that escalated tensions with U.S. authorities and helped precipitate the later outbreaks.

- **Cultural Continuity Under Pressure** — The continued production of tiswin on the reservation demonstrates how [[apache-family|Apache]] women maintained traditional foodways even under the constraints of reservation life. The fact that corn was available for fermentation — rather than being reserved entirely for food — suggests that Apache domestic economy retained some autonomy in the early reservation period.

- **Contrast with Mescal** — Unlike [[mescal]], which was used by [[mexico|Mexicans]] as a weapon to incapacitate Apache warriors before ambush (012-003), tiswin-related violence was internal to Apache communities, arising from disputes among Apache men rather than from external manipulation.
