## Overview

The Comanche appear in [[geronimo]]'s narrative as a Plains tribe associated with the Kiowa, sharing territory in Oklahoma where the [[apache-family|Apache]] prisoners of war were ultimately settled. Geronimo mentions a prominent Comanche leader and describes the tribe's participation in the Fort Sill community alongside the Apache (013-011, 020-019, 023-003).

## Identity & Territory

The Comanche were a powerful Plains tribe whose traditional territory covered parts of present-day Texas, Oklahoma, and surrounding regions. By the time of Geronimo's narrative, they shared lands with the Kiowa in Oklahoma. Geronimo states that his people were "now held on Comanche and Kiowa lands, which are not suited to our needs," reflecting the post-war relocation of the Apache to territory that belonged to these Plains tribes (023-003).

## Role in the Apache Wars

Like the Kiowa, the Comanche did not participate in the Apache Wars — they occupied a different geographic and cultural sphere. Their significance in Geronimo's narrative is limited to the post-war period at [[fort-sill]], where they were neighbors to the Apache prisoners of war.

## Notable Members

- **Quanna Parker** (Quanah Parker) — Described by Geronimo as "chief of Comanche Indians" (013-011). Quanah Parker was a historically significant Comanche leader who advocated for his people's adaptation to reservation life. A photograph of him appears in Geronimo's autobiography.

## Relations with Other Tribes

The Comanche were close allies of the [[kiowa|Kiowa]], sharing lands and participating jointly in social events. When Geronimo's daughter Eva held her coming-of-age dance at Medicine Creek, invitations were sent to "all Apaches, and many Comanches and Kiowas" (020-019), indicating amicable intertribal relations during the Fort Sill period.

Geronimo expresses his dissatisfaction with the assigned reservation lands:

> We are now held on Comanche and Kiowa lands, which are not suited to our needs—these lands and this climate are suited to the Indians who originally inhabited this country, of course, but our people are decreasing in numbers here, and will continue to decrease unless they are allowed to return to their native land.
>
> (023-003)
