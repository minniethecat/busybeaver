# War Dance

The war dance was the central ceremonial practice of Apache [[warrior-initiation|warrior]] culture, performed both before war expeditions as a preparation ritual and after them as a celebration and public accounting of battle results, including the display of [[scalping-custom|scalps]]. Authorized by the [[council-of-warriors|Council of Warriors]] (020-017), it served as the ceremonial bridge between deliberation and action — marking the transition from peace to warpath, and from warpath back to camp.

## Overview

The war dance served multiple functions in Apache society: it was a spiritual preparation for combat, a communal celebration of victory, and a public ritual for displaying battle trophies. Only warriors participated in the war dance proper (020-017), though other dances — such as the social scalp dance after a war party's return — involved the wider community including women. The dance was accompanied by singing led by warriors, the beating of the *esadadene* (drum), and war whoops that sometimes nearly drowned the music.

## Description

### Pre-War Dance

After the [[council-of-warriors|council of warriors]] had deliberated and prepared for the warpath, the war dance would commence. This dance was violent in character, with yells and war whoops that sometimes nearly drowned the music. Only warriors participated (020-017). The dance served to steel the warriors for combat and to invoke spiritual protection for the expedition. The pre-war war dance was conducted with ritual seriousness befitting war's status as "a solemn religious matter" (020-009).

### Post-War Scalp Dance

After a war party returned, a modified version of the war dance was held. Warriors who had taken scalps exhibited them to the tribe on elevated poles or spears, which were carried around the campfires during the dance. This post-war dance retained some of the solemnity of the pre-war version but included more levity than would be permitted at the war dance itself (020-018). The scalp dance served as a public accounting — the tribe could see and celebrate what each warrior had accomplished.

### Social Dances

Beyond the purely martial dances, the Apaches also held social dances after feasts celebrating noted events. These festivities typically lasted about four days, with feasting by day and dancing by night (004-001). The music consisted of singing led by warriors accompanied by drum beats. In these social dances, women participated — at certain occasions, ladies would dance forward two-by-two and designate a warrior to dance with them, in a back-and-forth pattern on a line from the center to the outer edge of the circle (020-024).

### Spiritual Significance

War was considered a solemn religious matter in Apache culture (020-009). The war dance was not merely entertainment or morale-building — it was an act of worship and spiritual preparation under the authority of [[usen|Usen]], the Apache life-giver. The dancing, singing, and display of trophies were conducted with ritual seriousness, particularly in the pre-war context. The dance concluded with each warrior giving an account of his deeds (020-025).

| Aspect | Pre-War Dance | Post-War (Scalp) Dance |
|--------|--------------|----------------------|
| Participants | Warriors only | Warriors + scalps displayed to community |
| Tone | Solemn, violent | Celebratory, some levity |
| Purpose | Spiritual preparation for battle | Public accounting of results |
| Women's role | None in the dance proper | Observers; participate in social dances after |
| Duration | Night-long | Often extended across days of feasting |

## Examples in the Narrative

- **Dance after Kas-Ki-Yeh revenge** (006-027) — After the massacre of Geronimo's family, he was made war chief and gave orders for scalping the slain Mexican troops. The subsequent dance celebrated this vengeance.
- **Night-long celebration after a raid** (008-002, 008-013) — After successful raids, the Apaches feasted, divided spoils, and danced all night. Geronimo notes that on one occasion "the dance began, and it did not cease until morning."
- **Feast and dance after dividing spoils** (008-020) — Following another successful expedition, the warriors "gave a feast and dance, and divided the spoils."
- **Scalp exhibition** (020-018) — Warriors displayed scalps elevated on poles carried around campfires during the post-war dance.
