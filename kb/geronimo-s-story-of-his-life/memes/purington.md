# Lieutenant Purington

Lieutenant Purington was a United States Army officer stationed at [[fort-sill|Fort Sill]], Oklahoma, during the period of [[geronimo|Geronimo]]'s imprisonment there. He appears in Geronimo's autobiography in two significant roles: as the officer who granted permission for the autobiography project, and as an authority figure to whom Geronimo brought grievances on behalf of his people.

## The Autobiography Project

In the summer of 1905, when [[george-wratton|S. M. Barrett]], the Superintendent of Schools at Lawton, Oklahoma, sought to record Geronimo's life story, it was Lieutenant Purington who held the authority to grant or deny permission. Barrett describes approaching Purington at Fort Sill:

> I immediately called at the fort (Fort Sill) and asked the officer in charge, Lieutenant Purington, for permission to write the old chief's life story. (P03-005)

Purington, after consulting with Geronimo and confirming the old chief's willingness, granted the request. This administrative decision — seemingly routine at the time — enabled the creation of one of the most important first-person accounts of the Apache Wars and Native American history. The autobiography project proceeded with the understanding that it would need further approval from higher authorities, but Purington's initial authorization at the local level was the essential first step.

> Geronimo — Apache Chief — S. M. Barrett, Supt. Schools. Letter to the President stating that above-mentioned desires to tell his life story that it may be published, and requests permission to tell it in his own way, and also desires assurance that what he has said shall in no way work to his harm. (P03-005)

## Advocate for Apache Grievances

Purington also served as a conduit for Geronimo's complaints about the treatment of his people at Fort Sill. Geronimo records that he "complained and told Lieutenant Purington that I intended to report the matter to the President" regarding the handling of Apache funds and property (019-008). This places Purington in the role of a local authority to whom the Apache prisoners of war brought their concerns — a position that required both administrative competence and the interpersonal skills to manage relations with the captive Apache population.

## The Apache Fund Controversy

The specific grievance Geronimo brought to Purington concerned the "Apache Fund" — money from the sale of Apache cattle and grain that the Indians believed should have been given directly to them, but which was instead partially deposited into a government-managed fund (019-008). Purington's position as officer in charge made him the first point of contact for such complaints, placing him at the intersection of military administration and Apache welfare.

## Key Events

- **Summer 1905** — Grants S. M. Barrett permission to record Geronimo's autobiography (P03-005)
- **Post-1905** — Receives Geronimo's complaints about the Apache Fund and the treatment of Apache prisoners (019-008)
