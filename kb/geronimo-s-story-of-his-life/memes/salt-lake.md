## Overview

The [[apache-family|Apaches]] obtained their salt from a small lake in the Gila Mountains, which [[geronimo|Geronimo]] describes as having special rules of peace. The site was treated as neutral ground where even enemies could travel without fear, making it one of the few places in Apache territory governed by a universal truce rather than by tribal boundaries.

## Description

The lake is described as "a very small lake of clear, shallow water" with a small mound rising from the center (020-004). The water was too salty to drink, and the lake bottom was covered with a brown crust. When the crust was broken, cakes of salt adhered to it.

The salt cakes could be washed clean in the lake's own water but would dissolve if washed in any other water (020-004). This unique property made the lake an irreplaceable resource for the [[apache-family|Apaches]], who needed salt for food preparation and preservation.

## Sacred Truce

When visiting this lake, the [[apache-family|Apaches]] observed a strict prohibition: "our people were not allowed to even kill game or attack an enemy" (020-005). All creatures were free to come and go without molestation. This truce transformed the salt-gathering expedition into something resembling a religious pilgrimage, where practical subsistence and spiritual discipline intersected.
