## Overview

Fort Pickens, located on Santa Rosa Island near Pensacola, Florida, was one of the detention facilities where the U.S. government held Apache prisoners of war following their removal from the Southwest in 1886. Geronimo was held there for nearly two years under conditions he described as a direct treaty violation.

## Geography

Fort Pickens is situated on the western end of Santa Rosa Island, a barrier island in the Gulf of Mexico near Pensacola, Florida. Its remote coastal location, like that of [[fort-marion]] on the Atlantic coast, placed the Apache prisoners in an unfamiliar maritime environment radically different from the arid mountains of Arizona and Sonora. The hot, humid Florida climate proved devastating to Apache health.

## Geronimo's Imprisonment

[[geronimo]] describes his transfer to Fort Pickens in stark terms: "In forty days they took me from there to Fort Pickens (Pensacola), Florida" (019-007). Upon arrival, he was put to hard labor: "Here they put me to sawing up large logs. There were several other Apache warriors with me, and all of us had to work every day" (019-007). The duration was substantial — "For nearly two years we were kept at hard labor in this place and we did not see our families until May, 1887" (019-007). Geronimo explicitly charged that "This treatment was in direct violation of our treaty made at [[skeleton-canyon|Skeleton Canyon]]" (019-007), referring to the surrender terms negotiated with [[general-miles|General Miles]] (019-007).

## Role in the Narrative

Fort Pickens represents the post-surrender disposition of Geronimo and his people after the military campaign ended. The U.S. government's policy involved removing the Apaches far from their traditional territory, separating families, and moving prisoners between different forts — Fort Marion, Fort Pickens, Mount Vernon Barracks, and ultimately [[fort-sill]] — over decades. The dispersion of Apache prisoners across multiple Florida forts reflected the government's determination to end Apache resistance through geographic isolation. The separation from families was particularly acute: Geronimo notes he "did not see our families until May, 1887" (019-007), roughly two years after his arrival.
