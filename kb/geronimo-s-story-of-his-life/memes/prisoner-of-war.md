## Overview

The status of "prisoner of war" defined the final decades of Geronimo's life and those of his people. Following his surrender in 1886, Geronimo and the Chiricahua Apaches were held as prisoners of war — not on a reservation as ordinary detainees but under military custody — at a succession of forts across Florida, Alabama, and Oklahoma. Geronimo's narrative offers a rare firsthand account of captivity from the Apache perspective.

## Description

The prisoner-of-war experience involved forced relocation across vast distances, family separation, and confinement in unfamiliar environments. After surrender, Geronimo was sent to Fort Marion, Florida, then to Mount Vernon Barracks, Alabama, and finally to Fort Sill, Oklahoma — each move taking him further from his Arizona homeland and deeper into prolonged captivity.

Geronimo's account emphasizes the distinction between the terms of surrender and the reality of captivity. The original understanding was that the Apaches "should be sent to Fort Marion, Florida, where their tribe, including their families, had already been ordered" (019-008). However:

> "They regarded the separation of themselves from their families as a violation of the terms of their treaty of surrender" (019-008).

The relocation was illustrated by General Miles in a striking visual metaphor: "He took a stone and placed it on the ground... and said to me: 'This represents the Indians about Fort Bowie... Just here the Indians of the reservation and the soldiers may be placed'" (019-010). This stone map showed the consolidation of all Apache groups at Fort Marion — a policy of concentration that treated the Chiricahuas as a military problem to be solved through removal.

## Captivity Conditions

The conditions of captivity varied across locations and administrations. Geronimo contrasts the treatment during General Howard's administration, when "if we ran out we only had to ask and we were supplied," with life as a prisoner: "Now, as prisoners of war in this Reservation, we do not get such good rations" (016-008).

At Fort Sill, the prisoners were permitted to raise crops and livestock, and some degree of economic self-sufficiency was restored. However, the fundamental condition — confinement far from the Apache homeland, under military authority rather than civil law — remained unchanged until Geronimo's death in 1909.

## Examples in the Narrative

- **The Surrender Terms (1886)** — General Miles illustrated the relocation plan by placing stones on the ground representing different Apache groups to be consolidated at Fort Marion (019-010), a visual negotiation that Geronimo remembered in precise detail decades later.

- **Ration Complaint** — Geronimo's detailed comparison of ration quality under different administrators (016-008) reflects the prisoner's acute awareness of material conditions as a measure of treatment.

- **Family Separation** — The broken promise of family reunification at Fort Marion is central to Geronimo's sense of betrayal. The separation was not merely logistical but a violation of the fundamental Apache social structure in which family ties were paramount.

- **Apache Treatment of Captives** — By contrast, Geronimo notes that when Apaches took captives, "Mexican men when captured were compelled to cut wood and herd horses. Mexican women and children were treated as our own people", and "we never chained prisoners or kept them in confinement." This comparison serves as an implicit critique of U.S. treatment of Apache prisoners.
