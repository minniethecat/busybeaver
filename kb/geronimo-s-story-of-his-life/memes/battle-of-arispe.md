The **Battle of Arispe** (also known as **Geronimo's Mightiest Battle**) was fought around 1884 near Arispe, in Sonora, Mexico, between a reduced band of Apache warriors led by [[geronimo]] (Geronimo) and a large force of Mexican troops. Geronimo himself considered this his greatest battle — it was the last engagement he ever fought against Mexicans and the one in which he personally shot and killed the opposing Mexican general (012-001).

## Background

The battle occurred in the aftermath of the Casa Grande massacre, in which Mexican troops had attacked the Apaches during what was supposed to be a peace negotiation, killing twenty Indians and capturing many more. After this treachery, Geronimo's band did not reassemble for a long period. They eventually returned to Arizona and lived for a time at the San Carlos Reservation (012-001).

In 1883 they went into Mexico again, remaining in the mountains for about fourteen months and fighting many skirmishes with Mexican troops. In 1884 they returned briefly to Arizona seeking recruits but found none. During this Arizona trip they also had trouble with United States soldiers and lost about fifteen warriors. With their reduced numbers, they camped in the mountains north of Arispe (012-002).

> We had lost about fifteen warriors in Arizona, and had gained no recruits. With our reduced number we camped in the mountains north of Arispe. Mexican troops were seen by our scouts in several directions. The United States troops were coming down from the north [012-002].

## The Battle

The Apaches made camp some distance from the mountains beside a stream with a deep channel and small trees growing along its banks. As was their custom, they placed scouts around the camp. At daybreak the next morning, the scouts rushed in to warn that Mexican troops were approaching. Within five minutes the Mexicans opened fire (012-003).

Geronimo ordered his people — including the women and children — into the ditches formed by the stream, and had them deepen these defensive positions. He gave strict orders to conserve ammunition and remain under cover. The battle continued all day, with the Mexicans repeatedly charging at different points, being repulsed, rallying, and charging again. Both sides suffered heavy casualties (012-004).

## Geronimo's Personal Attack

In the afternoon, the Mexican commanding general arrived on the field and the fighting intensified. Geronimo gave orders for his warriors to target Mexican officers. Around three o'clock, the general assembled his officers for a council near the main stream. A small ditch ran close to where they stood, and Geronimo crawled along it until he was within earshot (012-005).

> The general was an old warrior. The wind was blowing in my direction, so that I could hear all he said, and I understood most of it. This is about what he told them: "Officers, yonder in those ditches is the red devil Geronimo and his hated band. This must be his last day. Ride on him from both sides of the ditches; kill men, women, and children; take no prisoners; dead Indians are what we want. Do not spare your own men; exterminate this band at any cost; I will post the wounded to shoot all deserters; go back to your companies and advance" [012-005].

As the general gave the order to advance, Geronimo took deliberate aim and shot him dead. Instantly the ground around Geronimo was filled with bullets, but he was untouched. The Apaches, seeing what had happened, rose from the ditches with their war cry. The Mexican columns wavered briefly but continued their advance until Apache fire destroyed the front ranks (012-006).

The Mexicans continued to rally and advance throughout the afternoon. That night, before the firing ceased, a dozen Apaches crawled out of the ditches and set fire to the long prairie grass behind the Mexican troops. In the confusion that followed, Geronimo's band escaped into the mountains (012-007).

## Aftermath and Significance

This was the last battle Geronimo ever fought with the Mexicans. From this time forward, United States troops pursued his band continually until the final surrender to [[general-miles]] (General Miles) in Skeleton Canyon. In his many wars with the Mexicans, Geronimo received eight wounds: a bullet in the right leg above the knee (which he still carried), a shot through the left forearm, a saber wound below the right knee, a musket butt wound on the head, a bullet wound below the left eye, a shot in the left side, and a shot in the back (012-008).

Geronimo's successful defense against overwhelming odds, his personal assassination of the enemy commander, and the ingenious escape by setting fire to the prairie grass all contributed to the battle's legendary status among the Apaches. It represented the apex of Geronimo's military career against the Mexicans (012-009).
