## Overview

The White Mountain region in eastern Arizona was the homeland of the [[white-mountain-apache|White Mountain Apaches]] and the site of the [[san-carlos-reservation|San Carlos Apache Indian Reservation]] where [[geronimo|Geronimo]] and his band were confined in the early 1880s. From this reservation, [[geronimo|Geronimo]] led his famous breakout in May 1885, beginning the final phase of the Apache Wars.

## Geography

The White Mountain region lies in eastern Arizona, bordering the lands of the [[navajo|Navajo]] Indians to the north. The White Mountain Apaches' territory is described as being "next to that of the Navajo Indians, who were not of the same blood as the Apaches" (002-003). The terrain is mountainous and rugged, providing cover for resistance. The [[san-carlos-reservation|San Carlos Reservation]] within this region served as the U.S. government's designated concentration point for multiple Apache bands.

## Role in the Narrative

White Mountain appears at two critical junctures in [[geronimo|Geronimo]]'s story:

- **Relations with the White Mountain Apaches** — Geronimo describes the White Mountain Apaches as neighbors "not always on the best of terms with our tribe, yet we seldom had any war with them" (002-003). Their chief, [[hash-ka-ai-la]], was considered "a good warrior" by Geronimo, though the two tribes maintained cautious relations. During the 1885 breakout, Geronimo's band "obtained from the White Mountain Apaches" (017-003) guns and ammunition to support their flight.

- **The 1885 Breakout** — On May 17, 1885, [[geronimo|Geronimo]], [[naiche]], and other chiefs led approximately fifty [[chiricahua-apache|Chiricahua]] prisoners in an escape from the White Mountain Reserve. As described in contemporary accounts, this breakout launched "a career of murder and robbery unparalleled in the history of Indian raids" (017-009). The escape marked the beginning of [[geronimo|Geronimo]]'s final period of freedom before his ultimate surrender in 1886.

Geronimo describes the White Mountain Apaches and their chief:

> They were not always on the best of terms with our tribe, yet we seldom had any war with them. I knew their chief, Hash-ka-ai-la, personally, and I considered him a good warrior.
>
> (002-004)
