## Overview

The [[apache-family|Apaches]] had a formal trial system for resolving disputes within the tribe. When a member was wronged and did not wish to settle the matter personally, they could bring a complaint to the chieftain for investigation and judgment (020-001). This system coexisted with the right to personal vengeance — but choosing trial meant forfeiting that right.

## Description

Both the accused and the accuser were entitled to present witnesses. Critically, witnesses were "not interrupted in any way by questions, but simply say what they wish to say in regard to the matter" (020-001). Witnesses were not placed under oath, because the [[apache-family|Apaches]] did not believe their own people would give false testimony against one another.

The chief presided alone for minor matters, but for serious offenses he asked "two or three leaders to sit with him" as a panel (020-002). The panel's role was solely to determine guilt. If the accused was found not guilty, the matter ended and the complaining party forfeited the right to personal vengeance — an important check against double jeopardy through both formal and informal channels.

If the accused was found guilty, the injured party fixed the penalty, which was "generally confirmed by the chief and his associates" (020-002). This gave the victim agency in sentencing while preserving the chief's authority to moderate excessive punishments.

Geronimo describes the witness testimony rule:

> Both the accused and the accuser are entitled to witnesses, and their witnesses are not interrupted in any way by questions, but simply say what they wish to say in regard to the matter. The witnesses are not placed under oath, because it is not believed that they will give false testimony in a matter relating to their own people.
>
> (020-001)
