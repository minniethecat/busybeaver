The **Battle of White Hill** was a decisive engagement fought around 1873–74 between a band of approximately sixty Apache warriors led by [[geronimo]] (Geronimo) and two companies of Mexican cavalry in the Sierra Madre Mountains of Mexico, near the village of Nacori. It was one of Geronimo's most complete tactical victories: the Apaches killed every Mexican soldier without suffering a single fatality (011-006).

## Background

In about 1873, Geronimo's band had been ranging in the Sierra Madre Mountains for over a year, raiding Mexican settlements for supplies but avoiding any general engagement with Mexican troops. After briefly returning to their homes in Arizona for about a year, they went back into Mexico and established a camp near Nacori. They had just organized bands of warriors for raiding the country when their scouts discovered Mexican troops advancing toward their camp to attack them (011-005).

## The Battle

The Apache warriors marched out to meet the Mexican troops at a location about five miles from their camp. Upon sighting the Apaches, the Mexican soldiers quickly rode to the top of a round hill and dismounted, placing their horses on the outside as breastworks. The hill was described as very steep and rocky, with no timber on its sides (011-006).

The chief of the [[nedni-apache]] (Nedni Apaches), [[whoa]] (Whoa), was with Geronimo and commanded one division of the Apache force. The Apaches crept up the hill behind rocks while the Mexicans maintained a constant but ineffective fire. Geronimo specifically cautioned his warriors not to expose themselves, knowing that the Mexican troopers would exhaust their ammunition. Soon the Apaches had killed all the Mexican horses, though the soldiers continued to fire from behind the carcasses (011-006).

> I knew that the troopers would waste their ammunition. Soon we had killed all their horses, but the soldiers would lie behind these and shoot at us. While we had killed several Mexicans, we had not yet lost a man [011-006].

## The Final Charge

The engagement lasted from approximately one o'clock in the afternoon until mid-afternoon. Realizing that further sniping would not dislodge the defenders, Geronimo ordered a direct charge:

> We had been fighting ever since about one o'clock, and about the middle of the afternoon, seeing that we were making no further progress, I gave the sign for the advance. The war-whoop sounded and we leaped forward from every stone over the Mexicans' dead horses, fighting hand to hand [011-007].

The suddenness of the charge threw the Mexicans into confusion. Within minutes, the Apaches had killed every soldier. After the battle, the warriors scalped the slain, carried away their own dead, and secured all the weapons they needed. That night they moved their camp eastward through the Sierra Madre Mountains into Chihuahua, where they remained unmolested for about a year before returning to Arizona (011-007).

## Significance

The Battle of White Hill demonstrated the tactical effectiveness of Apache warfare against conventional Mexican cavalry. Geronimo's patience in exhausting the enemy's ammunition and terrain before committing to a charge, and the complete destruction of two cavalry companies without Apache losses, reinforced his reputation as a formidable war leader.
