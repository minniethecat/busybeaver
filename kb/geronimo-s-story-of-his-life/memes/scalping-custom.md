# Scalping Custom

Scalping was a battlefield practice among the Apaches in which warriors took scalps from slain enemies as trophies of combat, later displaying them publicly at post-war dances — a custom governed by warrior honor codes that distinguished between warriors (who could be scalped) and non-combatants (who generally were not).

## Overview

The taking of scalps served both as proof of a warrior's combat achievements and as a ritual element of the post-war scalp dance, where trophies were exhibited to the tribe on elevated poles. Geronimo describes scalping as a regular part of warfare (006-027, 007-005), but the custom was not indiscriminate — warriors distinguished between combatants and non-combatants. When Geronimo's party killed four men driving cattle, they "did not scalp them; they were not warriors" (014-004). The practice was also adopted by some Mexican forces and irregular troops who fought against the Apaches.

## Description

### Battlefield Practice

Scalping occurred immediately after combat. Geronimo records giving orders "for scalping the slain" after a battle, then setting a watch and returning to camp with the scalps (006-027). After another engagement, he notes that four Indians were killed "and their enemies had been scalped" as a matter of course in the aftermath of battle (007-005). The scalp-lock hairstyle worn by Apache warriors — a long lock of hair ready for an enemy's hand — acknowledged the reciprocal nature of the practice (006-016).

### The Scalp Dance

The post-war scalp dance, described in detail in the Unwritten Laws chapter, was the public ritual for displaying battle trophies. Warriors who had taken scalps exhibited them to the tribe elevated on poles or spears, which were carried around the campfires during the dance (020-018). This dance had more levity than the solemn pre-war war dance, functioning as both a celebration and a public accounting of each warrior's deeds.

### Honor Code and Distinctions

The Apaches distinguished between warriors and non-warriors in the application of scalping. Geronimo's party killed four men but "did not scalp them; they were not warriors" (014-004), indicating that scalping was reserved for combatants. However, Geronimo also notes that Mexican irregulars "were instructed to scalp non-combatants" (010-014), suggesting that the honor code was not universally observed by all parties in the conflict.

## Examples in the Narrative

- **Scalping order after battle** (006-027) — Geronimo gave orders for scalping the slain Mexican troops after a major engagement, then set watches and returned to camp.
- **Enemies scalped** (007-005) — After finding four dead Indians, Geronimo notes their enemies had been scalped — a routine observation of battlefield aftermath.
- **Non-warriors spared from scalping** (014-004) — Four men killed while driving cattle were not scalped because they were not warriors.
- **Scalp-locks as preparation** (006-016) — Warriors going on the warpath wore long scalp-locks, a hairstyle that acknowledged the reciprocal danger of being scalped.
