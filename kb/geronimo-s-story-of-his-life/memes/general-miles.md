# General Miles

General Nelson Appleton Miles (1839–1925) was the [[general-miles|United States Army]] commander who accepted [[geronimo|Geronimo's]] final [[surrender-of-1886|surrender]] in 1886 at [[skeleton-canyon|Skeleton Canyon]], and whom Geronimo later accused of bad faith regarding the treatment of Apache [[prisoner-customs|prisoners of war]] under the terms of the treaty. As "the chief of the American troops" (017-008), Miles's actions closed a chapter of the [[apache-pass|Apache Wars]] that had lasted decades — but the peace he brokered proved deeply contested.

## Overview

General Miles appears in Geronimo's narrative as the American commander who brought the Apache Wars to an end. Geronimo describes him as "the chief of the American troops" (017-008). The surrender to Miles at Skeleton Canyon in September 1886 marked the conclusion of Geronimo's decades of warfare, but the peace was immediately clouded by disagreement over the treaty's terms. Geronimo specifically criticizes Miles, and the editor notes that "in several places throughout the manuscript Geronimo accuses General Miles of bad faith" regarding the government's subsequent treatment of Apache prisoners (P03-018).

Miles had a distinguished Civil War record, receiving the Medal of Honor, before being appointed to command the western posts. His pursuit of Geronimo's band in 1886 was relentless, utilizing Captain Lawton's scouts and coordinated Mexican forces to wear down the Apache resistance through constant skirmishing (017-003).

## Life & Role

### Commander of American Forces

General Miles was appointed commander of American forces pursuing the Apache resistance. Geronimo records that "soon General Miles was made commander" of the troops (017-003), signaling an intensification of the military campaign. Captain Lawton "had good scouts" under Miles's command, and the "Mexican soldiers also became more active and more numerous" (017-003), creating a coordinated encirclement that made Apache movement increasingly difficult.

Geronimo acknowledges that Miles's strategy of constant pressure proved effective. "We had skirmishes almost every day" (017-003), he writes, describing how the relentless pursuit "wore us down" until further resistance became militarily untenable. Despite the enmity, Geronimo knew that "General Miles was the chief of the American troops" — the recognized authority with whom a treaty could be made (017-008).

### The Surrender Treaty

After months of pursuit, Geronimo sent word that he wished to meet. His messengers went "on to Fort Bowie to see General Miles, and to tell him that we wanted to make a treaty of peace" (017-010). The treaty was negotiated at Skeleton Canyon, where Miles reportedly offered terms that Geronimo accepted in good faith.

According to Geronimo's account, the terms included a period of separation followed by reunification with families and return to Arizona. The reality was far different. The Apaches were transported to [[fort-sill|Florida]] as prisoners, then to Alabama, and finally to Fort Sill, Oklahoma, where Geronimo remained incarcerated for over twenty years (019-001).

### Co-signers of the Treaty

The surrender was witnessed by several parties. Geronimo specifically names [[naiche|Naiche]], "the only chief with me" after the death of [[whoa|Whoa]] (016-011), as present at the negotiations. The treaty also involved [[asa-deklugie|Asa Deklugie]], Geronimo's niece, and other Apache leaders who surrendered alongside the main band.

### Legacy of the Treaty

The editor of Geronimo's autobiography notes carefully that he was "in no way responsible for what Geronimo says" (P03-019), but also acknowledges the gravity of the accusation: Geronimo "accuses General Miles of bad faith" (P03-018). The specific complaint concerns the treatment of Apaches as prisoners of war — Geronimo believed the treaty promised conditions that the government did not honor. Twenty years of imprisonment at Fort Sill were the consequence.

> The fact that Geronimo has told the story in his own way is doubtless the only excuse necessary for offering it to the public. (P03-020)

## Relationship to Geronimo

General Miles was Geronimo's final military adversary and the man who accepted his surrender. Their relationship was defined by the treaty and its aftermath. Geronimo respected Miles as the legitimate American commander — "I knew that General Miles was the chief of the American troops" (017-008) — but believed himself betrayed by the government Miles represented. The relationship represents the central tension of the Apache-American conflict: the gap between the promises made in surrender negotiations and the reality of prisoner-of-war existence.
