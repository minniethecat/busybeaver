# Captain Crawford

Captain Emmet Crawford was a United States Army officer whose death during the Geronimo campaign of 1886 is recorded in the official military correspondence reproduced in [[geronimo|Geronimo]]'s autobiography. His assassination by Mexican forces while pursuing Geronimo's band in the Sierra Madre mountains represented one of the most controversial incidents of the Apache Wars.

## The Assassination

Crawford was killed on January 11, 1886, under circumstances that generated significant diplomatic tension between the United States and Mexico. The official report by [[general-miles|General Nelson A. Miles]] to the Adjutant General in Washington, D.C. references the incident in the context of recounting the operations that led to Geronimo's final surrender:

> The assassination of Captain Crawford, on January 11, by the Mexicans, is a matter of history. (018-016)

The phrasing — "assassination" rather than "death in combat" — reflects the U.S. Army's official position that Crawford was killed deliberately by Mexican forces rather than in a legitimate military engagement. This was not merely a battlefield casualty but a politically charged event that complicated the already-fraught cross-border pursuit of Apache bands between the United States and Mexico.

## The Geronimo Campaign Context

Crawford was operating in Mexico as part of the U.S. Army's campaign to capture or force the surrender of [[geronimo|Geronimo]] and his band, who had fled across the border after breaking out of the San Carlos Reservation. American forces under [[general-crook|General George Crook]] and later [[general-miles|General Miles]] pursued the Apaches into the Sierra Madre, operating under agreements — often strained — with Mexican authorities.

Crawford's death occurred during this cross-border campaign and became a significant footnote in the diplomatic and military history of the operation. The incident is mentioned in Miles's report alongside the surrender negotiations conducted through [[gatewood|Lieutenant Gatewood]], framing it as one of the costs and complications of the final push to end Apache resistance (018-016).

## Significance in Geronimo's Narrative

Crawford does not appear directly in Geronimo's personal narrative — his name enters the autobiography through the official documents that [[george-wratton|S. M. Barrett]] incorporated into the published volume. His inclusion in the historical record preserved within Geronimo's book serves as a reminder that the Apache Wars were not a simple binary conflict but a complex three-sided affair involving Apache, American, and Mexican forces, with violence sometimes erupting between the latter two.
