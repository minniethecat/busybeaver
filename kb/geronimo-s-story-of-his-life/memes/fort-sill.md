# Fort Sill

Fort Sill, established in 1869 in what was then Indian Territory (now Oklahoma), was the final place of imprisonment for [[geronimo|Geronimo]] and the surviving Chiricahua Apaches. Initially a frontier cavalry post, Fort Sill became the Apaches' home — and prison — from 1894 until Geronimo's death there in 1909. It was at Fort Sill that Geronimo dictated his life story to [[general-miles|S. M. Barrett]] in 1905, producing the autobiography that stands as the definitive Apache account of the wars that shaped the American Southwest.

## Location and Function

Fort Sill is located near Lawton, in the southwestern part of what is now Oklahoma, on the Medicine Creek valley. Geronimo describes the setting simply: "in this Military Reservation (Fort Sill)" (003-003). The fort was originally established to control the Kiowa and Comanche, but by 1894 it had been designated as the place where the Chiricahua Apache prisoners of war — who had been moved from [[skeleton-canyon|Arizona]] to Florida to Alabama — would finally settle.

## Life as a Prisoner of War

### Arrival

Geronimo and the surviving Apaches arrived at Fort Sill in 1894, eight years after the [[surrender-of-1886|surrender of 1886]]. They were classified as prisoners of war, a status that meant they could be held indefinitely without trial or formal charges — a legal framework that the United States government used to keep Geronimo and his people confined for a total of twenty-three years.

> When we first came to Fort Sill, Captain Scott was in charge. (019-007)

The Apaches were granted a measure of autonomy within the reservation. They were permitted to build teepees, raise crops, and maintain many of their traditional customs — but they could not leave. The contradiction of being simultaneously a "prisoner" and a resident of a functioning community defined Geronimo's last two decades.

### Daily Life

Despite his prisoner status, Geronimo became something of a public figure at Fort Sill. He raised cattle, grew crops, and participated in the economic life of the post. He attended the Omaha Exposition (1898), the Pan-American Exposition (1901), and the St. Louis World's Fair (1904), selling autographs and photographs of himself (021-001). These trips were supervised by military authorities and required special permission, but they provided Geronimo with a glimpse of the world beyond the reservation.

### The Barrett Interviews

In the summer of 1905, S. M. Barrett, superintendent of schools at Lawton, Oklahoma, began visiting Geronimo at Fort Sill. Barrett's account of their meetings describes the setting:

> I visited him at his teepee in the Fort Sill Military reservation. (P03-002)

The teepee became the site of a months-long collaboration. Geronimo dictated his story through an interpreter — Asa Deklugie, his niece — while Barrett transcribed. The project required clearance from the highest levels of government. Barrett wrote to President Theodore Roosevelt, and the War Department authorized the project through an elaborate chain of military endorsements that ultimately carried the approval of "the commanding officer of Fort Sill" (P03-013).

The process was not without friction. Geronimo "refused to talk when a stenographer was present" (P03-007), preferring the informality of conversation. He was also suspicious of military oversight. Barrett describes how he built trust: "I came alone, and said that Geronimo was very sick with cold and fever" (P03-008) — suggesting the intimacy of the relationship that developed.

### The Final Years

Geronimo spent his final years at Fort Sill as a prisoner who had outlasted his captors. He had seen the Apache Wars begin and end, had buried most of his family, had survived decades of imprisonment, and had told his story. In February 1909, he contracted pneumonia while riding home in a storm. He died at Fort Sill on February 17, 1909, and was buried in the Apache prisoner-of-war cemetery on the post.

## Significance

Fort Sill represents the final chapter of Geronimo's life and, in a broader sense, the conclusion of the Indian Wars. The fort that once housed Geronimo as a prisoner now serves as the U.S. Army Field Artillery School. Geronimo's grave remains there, visited by those who seek to understand the man and the conflicts he embodied.

> I cannot think that we are useless or that Usen would have created a people so that they should be destroyed. … It is my land, my home, my father's land, to which I now ask to be allowed to return. (023-005)
