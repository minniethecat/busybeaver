# Apache Family

Apache family life was structured around the mother's central role in child-rearing and cultural transmission, polygamous marriage arrangements among warriors and chiefs, and well-defined customs governing widowhood, divorce, and child custody.

## Overview

The Apache family was the primary institution for cultural transmission and social identity. Mothers taught children the legends of the people, religious practices such as prayer to Usen, and the skills necessary for survival (003-005). Warriors could take multiple wives — Geronimo himself had more than one (019-005), and Naiche is noted as traveling with "his mother, his two wives, and his children" (006-021). Widowhood, separation, and child custody were governed by established customs that balanced the needs of the surviving family members with tribal norms.

## Description

### The Mother's Role

Apache mothers bore primary responsibility for transmitting cultural knowledge. Geronimo describes his mother teaching him "the legends of our people; taught me of the sun and sky, the moon and stars, the clouds and storms" and teaching him to kneel and pray to Usen (003-005). This transmission of oral tradition, cosmology, and religious practice through the maternal line was the foundation of Apache education.

### Polygamy

Multiple wives were accepted in Apache society, particularly among warriors and chiefs. Geronimo records having more than one wife (019-005). Naiche traveled with "his mother, his two wives, and his children" (006-021). The practice appears to have been unexceptional — it is mentioned in passing rather than as a subject of special note.

### Family Size

Apache families were large. Geronimo was "fourth in a family of eight children — four boys and four girls" (003-003). Children played around the family teepee and participated in games that taught practical skills (003-009).

### Widowhood and Custody

A widow with children typically remained single for two to three years after her husband's death before remarrying, while a childless widow could remarry immediately (005-003). In cases of separation, the wife retained custody of small children — Geronimo's wife who separated from him "kept our two small children, which she had a right to do" (019-005).

### The Teepee as Family Center

The teepee served as the physical center of family life. Geronimo describes being "rolled on the dirt floor of my father's teepee, hung in my tsoch (Apache name for cradle)" as an infant (003-004). When he married Alope, he made a home "not far from my mother's teepee" (005-006).

## Examples in the Narrative

- **Geronimo's childhood** (003-003) — Fourth of eight children, Geronimo grew up in a large family where his mother taught him legends and prayer.
- **Polygamy in Geronimo's own life** (019-005) — Geronimo consented to let one of his wives go to Mescalero, an arrangement recognized as divorce under Apache custom.
- **Naiche's family** (006-021) — Naiche traveled with his mother, two wives, and children, demonstrating the multi-generational, polygamous family structure.
- **Widowhood of Geronimo's mother** (005-003) — After his father's death, Geronimo's mother chose not to remarry, though custom permitted it.
