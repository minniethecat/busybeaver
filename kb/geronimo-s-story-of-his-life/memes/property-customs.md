# Property Customs

Apache property customs governed inheritance after death, gift exchange at ceremonial gatherings, and the community's relationship to material possessions — reflecting a society where wealth was redistributed rather than accumulated.

## Overview

When an Apache warrior died, his property was given away to others as was customary in the tribe (005-002). This practice of redistribution contrasted with accumulation. The Apaches observed with curiosity the greater variety and quantity of material possessions kept by Mexicans, noting that Mexicans "kept many more kinds of property than the Apaches did" (008-012). At ceremonial dances, gift exchange between warriors and their dance partners followed established protocols, with disputes over appropriate gifts resolved by medicine men or chiefs (020-025).

## Description

### Inheritance and Death Customs

Upon a warrior's death, his property was distributed to others according to tribal custom. Geronimo records that when his father died, "we gave away all of his other property, as was customary in our tribe" (005-002). This practice prevented the accumulation of inherited wealth and ensured that material goods circulated through the community. The widow returned to her own people after the husband's death (005-003).

### Comparative Material Culture

The Apaches maintained a relatively minimal material culture. Geronimo notes that when raiding Mexican settlements, the Apaches encountered "many curious things" they "could not understand" — the Mexicans possessed a greater variety and quantity of goods (008-012). This observation highlights the deliberate simplicity of Apache material life, consistent with a nomadic warrior society.

### Gift Exchange at Dances

Social dances included a formal gift exchange. When a dance concluded, each warrior gave a present to the lady who had selected him as her partner. If the lady was satisfied, they parted; if not, the matter was referred to a medicine man or chief who determined the appropriate gift (020-025). This custom regulated social interactions and established expectations of generosity.

### Destruction of Property in Mourning

In extreme grief, Apaches sometimes destroyed possessions. After the massacre of his family, Geronimo burned their teepee, his mother's teepee, and destroyed all her property (006-007). This act of ritual destruction marked the depth of loss and severed ties to a place of tragedy.

## Examples in the Narrative

- **Father's death and property distribution** (005-002) — When Geronimo's father died, his property was given away to others according to custom. He was buried with his best clothes, painted face, rich blanket, saddled horse, and arms.
- **Geronimo's mourning destruction** (006-007) — After Kas-Ki-Yeh, Geronimo burned the family teepee, his mother's teepee, and all her property in an act of grief.
- **Mexican material wealth observed** (008-012) — Apaches noted the contrast between their minimal possessions and the variety of goods in Mexican houses and stores.
