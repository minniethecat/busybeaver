## Overview

The Chiricahua Apaches (referred to in the narrative as "Cho-kon-en" or "Chokonen") were one of the four closely allied Apache tribes — alongside the Bedonkohe, Chihenne, and Nedni — that formed the core of Geronimo's world. Led by the legendary chief Cochise and later by his son Naiche, the Chiricahua were Geronimo's most steadfast allies throughout the Apache Wars.

## Identity & Territory

The Chiricahua lived south of the Bedonkohe, in the mountains of southeastern Arizona. Geronimo describes them: "South of us lived the Cho-kon-en (Chiricahua) Apaches, whose chief in the old days was Cochise, and later his son, Naiche" (002-004). Their territory extended into what is now the Chiricahua Mountains and the surrounding basin and range country. Their range bordered the Bedonkohe to the north and the Mexican states of Sonora and Chihuahua to the south.

## Role in the Apache Wars

The Chiricahua were central participants in every major phase of the conflict:

- **The Revenge Raid of 1859** — Cochise personally led the Chokonen contingent in the three-tribe coalition that Geronimo organized to avenge the Kas-ki-yeh massacre. "We traveled in three divisions: the Bedonkohe Apaches led by Mangus-Colorado, the Chokonen Apaches by Cochise, and the Nedni Apaches by Whoa" (007-008).

- **Apache Pass Engagements** — After the Bascom Affair, "Cochise and Mangus-Colorado did likewise" in making a treaty with the soldiers at Apache Pass. When that treaty was broken, "the Bedonkohe and the Chokonen Apaches, both commanded by Cochise" fought the soldiers together (016-003).

- **The 1885 Breakout** — When Geronimo fled the reservation, "about four hundred Indians went with me. They were the Bedonkohe, Chokonen, and Nedni Apaches" (017-002).

- **The Final Surrender** — It was Kayitah, "a Chokonen Apache," and Marteen, a Nedni Apache, serving as scouts for Captain Lawton, who found Geronimo and delivered General Miles's message (018-006). Naiche, "the son of Cochise, and the hereditary chief of the Apaches," surrendered alongside Geronimo on September 4, 1886.

## Relations with Other Tribes

The Chiricahua were "always on the most friendly terms" with the Bedonkohe: "We were often in camp and on the trail together" (002-004). Geronimo describes the four tribes — Bedonkohe, Chokonen, Chihenne, and Nedni — as "fast friends in the days of freedom" who "cling together as they decrease in number. Only the destruction of all our people would dissolve our bonds of friendship" (002-005).

> South of us lived the Cho-kon-en (Chiricahua) Apaches, whose chief in the old days was Cochise, and later his son, Naiche. This tribe was always on the most friendly terms with us. We were often in camp and on the trail together. Naiche, who was my companion in arms, is now my companion in bondage.
>
> (002-006)
