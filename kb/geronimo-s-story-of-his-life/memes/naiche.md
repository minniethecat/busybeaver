# Naiche

Naiche was the hereditary chief of the Chokonen Apaches, the son of Cochise, who fought alongside Geronimo in the final campaigns of Apache resistance, escaped with him from the White Mountain reservation, and surrendered together with him to General Miles in 1886.

## Overview

Naiche (also spelled Natches) was the son and successor of the great Chokonen chief Cochise. As the "last hereditary chief of the hostile Apaches" (018-011), Naiche represented the continuity of Apache leadership from the era of Cochise and Mangus-Colorado through to the final surrender. Geronimo describes him as "wild and suspicious" after years of betrayal (018-011), yet he remained Geronimo's steadfast companion through the final campaigns, the escape from reservation, and the ultimate surrender. His portrait appears among the illustrations in Geronimo's autobiography (002-010).

## Life & Role

### Hereditary Succession

Naiche inherited the chieftainship of the Chokonen Apaches from his father Cochise. The Chokonen had always been on the most friendly terms with Geronimo's Bedonkohe band (002-006), and this alliance continued under Naiche's leadership. His hereditary status is emphasized by Geronimo — he is "the son of Cochise, and the hereditary chief" (018-016).

### Final Campaigns

Naiche fought alongside Geronimo through the final period of Apache resistance. After the death of Whoa, Naiche was "the only chief with me" (016-011), making him Geronimo's sole remaining peer in leadership. The two leaders moved in parallel during the final military operations, with their bands operating in coordination against American and Mexican forces (018-010).

### Surrender of 1886

Naiche surrendered together with Geronimo in 1886. On September 4, "Naiche, the son of Cochise, and the hereditary chief of the hostile Apaches" was among those who escaped from the White Mountain reservation with Geronimo (018-016). Shortly after, both leaders surrendered to General Miles. Naiche was "wild and suspicious and evaporated whenever a stranger appeared" — traumatized by years of betrayal and the fate of leaders like Mangus-Colorado (018-011).

## Relationship to Geronimo

Naiche was Geronimo's most trusted peer and companion in the final years of Apache resistance. Their relationship was one of equals — both were chiefs of their respective bands who coordinated military operations and made joint decisions about war and peace. When Geronimo describes being "alone" in leadership after Whoa's death, Naiche is the exception — the one remaining chief who stood with him.
