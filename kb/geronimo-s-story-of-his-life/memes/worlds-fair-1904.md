# 1904 St. Louis World's Fair

The 1904 Louisiana Purchase Exposition, commonly known as the St. Louis World's Fair, was a pivotal event in [[geronimo|Geronimo]]'s later life as a prisoner of war. Invited — or, in his description, summoned — to appear at the fair, Geronimo spent the summer of 1904 in St. Louis as both an exhibit and an entrepreneur, selling autographs, photographs, and handmade crafts to thousands of fairgoers. He devoted an entire chapter of his autobiography to the experience.

## Summons to St. Louis

Geronimo's account suggests reluctance about attending the fair. The decision was not entirely his own:

> At the World's Fair. When I was at first asked to attend the St. Louis World's Fair I did not wish to go. Later, when I was told that I would receive good attention and protection, and that the President of the United States said that it would be all right, I consented. (021-001, 021-002)

The invocation of presidential authority — and the implicit understanding that refusal was not truly an option — reflects the constrained circumstances of Geronimo's captivity. Even his appearance at a public exhibition was subject to the approval and direction of his captors.

## Life at the Fair

Once at the fair, Geronimo adapted with characteristic pragmatism. He was put on display in an "Indian Village" alongside other Native Americans:

> When these people first came to the World's Fair they did nothing but parade up and down the streets. (021-005)

But Geronimo distinguished himself from the other Native American participants. Rather than merely being exhibited, he turned the situation to his advantage, selling autographed photographs of himself at premium prices and handcrafted items including bows and arrows and beadwork:

> Many people in St. Louis invited me to come to their homes, but my health would not permit me to go much into society. (021-003)

His description of the fair includes a note of wonder at its scale — he observed the Ferris wheel, noting that "the people down in the Fair Grounds looked no larger than ants" (021-014) — but also a critical awareness of the political dimensions of his presence there.

## Critical Observations

Geronimo's account of the fair is not merely descriptive but pointedly critical. He notes the disparity between how Native Americans were displayed and how they were treated:

> There were some little brown people at the Fair that United States troops captured. (021-019)

He observes that while the "President sent them to the Fair so that they could learn something of the civilization of the white man," they were shown only the fair's amusements rather than anything truly educational (021-021). Yet his final assessment is characteristically balanced:

> I am glad I went to the Fair. I saw many interesting things and learned much of the white man's ways. (021-022)

## Aftermath

Geronimo's appearance at the 1904 World's Fair became one of the most publicized episodes of his captivity. The image of the aged Apache warrior — once the most feared fighting leader in the Southwest — selling autographs at a world exposition captured the profound transformation of his circumstances from warrior to spectacle. The fair also provided Geronimo with one of his few opportunities to earn money independently, a small measure of agency within the larger framework of his imprisonment.
