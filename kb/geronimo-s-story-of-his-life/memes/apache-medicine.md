# Apache Medicine

Apache medicine was a sophisticated system of herbal healing taught — according to Apache belief — by the supreme being Usen at the time of creation, and preserved across generations by medicine men skilled in the identification, preparation, and administration of medicinal plants.

## Overview

The Apaches possessed extensive knowledge of herbal medicine. This knowledge was considered a divine gift: Usen created healing herbs specifically for the Apaches when he established their homeland in the West, teaching them where to find these plants and how to prepare them (002-013). Each generation produced men skilled in the art of healing who carried this knowledge forward (003-019). The practice of medicine combined practical herbal application with spiritual elements — faith in prayer was held to be as important as the physical effect of the medicine itself (003-020).

## Description

### Divine Origin

According to Apache tradition, Usen taught the first man — named Apache — how to prepare herbs for medicine alongside hunting and fighting (001-019). When Usen created the Apache homeland, he populated it with healing herbs and instructed the people in their use, establishing medicine as one of the three foundational skills of Apache life alongside hunting and warfare.

### Knowledge and Practice

The Apaches knew which herbs to use for specific ailments, how to prepare them, and how to administer the medicine. This knowledge was transmitted from generation to generation through a lineage of skilled practitioners (003-019). The medicine men held a respected position in Apache society — Geronimo describes social dances as being "presided over by a chief and medicine men" (020-013), and disputes at ceremonial gatherings were referred to "someone in authority (medicine man or chief)" for resolution (020-025).

### Role of Faith

Apache medicine integrated spiritual belief with physical treatment. In gathering herbs, preparing them, and administering medicine, as much faith was held in prayer as in the actual effect of the herbs (003-020). This holistic approach — treating both the spiritual and physical dimensions of illness — characterized the practice. When disease or pestilence spread through the community, leaders would assemble the people and question them to determine what evil had been committed and how Usen could be satisfied, sometimes requiring sacrifice or punishment (004-006).

### Medicine Men in Ceremony

Medicine men played significant roles beyond healing. During ceremonial dances, medicine men entered stripped to the waist and participated in ritual proceedings (020-022). They assisted chiefs in directing dances (020-021) and served as arbiters in disputes that arose during social gatherings (020-025).

## Examples in the Narrative

- **Geronimo's mother's wounds healed** (003-018) — A woman named Cho-ko-le suffered severe scalp wounds, but under the treatment of medicine men, all her wounds were healed.
- **Prayer and medicine combined** (003-020) — Geronimo describes how faith in prayer was held to be as important as the herbs' physical effects, illustrating the spiritual dimension of Apache healing.
- **Disease as moral failing** (004-006) — When pestilence struck, the community was assembled and questioned to identify wrongdoing and determine what sacrifice or punishment would satisfy Usen.
- **Medicine men at the social dance** (020-021–020-025) — Geronimo, Naiche, and medicine men directed ceremonial dances, with medicine men playing key roles in ritual proceedings and dispute resolution.
