# Sierra Madre Mountains

The Sierra Madre mountain range in northern Mexico — including the Sierra de Antunez, Sierra de Sahuaripa, and the broader Sierra Madre proper — was the primary theater of Geronimo's Mexican campaigns. For decades, these mountains served as refuge, raiding base, and battleground for Apache warriors operating against Mexican settlements.

## A Raiding Base

The Sierra Madre was the launching point for countless Apache raids. Geronimo entered these mountains through the country of the Chokonen Apaches and "entered the Sierra Madre Mountains" (008-007) on an early raid. The mountains provided cover and strategic advantage — from them, the Apaches could descend on villages and pack trains, then vanish back into the rugged terrain. In 1861, Geronimo "entered Chihuahua and followed south on the east side of the Sierra Madre Mountains four days' journey; then crossed over to the Sierra de Sahuaripa range, not far east of Casa Grande" (009-003), where his band attacked a pack train. This pattern repeated through the decades: "We ranged in these mountains for over a year, raiding the Mexican settlements for our supplies" (012-001).

## Battleground

The mountains witnessed some of Geronimo's bloodiest engagements. After a day-long fight where "the Mexicans, running first this way and then that, became so confused that in a few minutes we had killed them all" (012-006), Geronimo "moved our camp eastward through the Sierra Madre Mountains into Chihuahua" (012-006). In the summer of 1860, in the Sierra de Sahuaripa Mountains, Geronimo suffered a near-fatal head wound when his foot slipped "in a pool of blood" and he fell under a Mexican trooper who "struck me over the head with the butt of his gun, knocking me senseless" (009-002). A warrior following behind killed the Mexican with a spear, and Geronimo was found "lying unconscious where I had fallen" (009-002). He notes: "I still wear the scar given me by that musketeer" (009-002).

## Refuge and Rendezvous

When scattered by enemy attacks, the Sierra Madre served as the reassembly point. After a raid went wrong in 1865, with Mexican troops recovering all the Apache booty, "In three days we reassembled at our appointed place of rendezvous in the Sierra Madre Mountains in northern Sonora" (010-003). Again, after an engagement at Nokode where "Mexican troops were coming from many quarters, we disbanded" only to reassemble "in the Sierra Madre Mountains" where "a council was held" (012-009). The mountains' vastness gave Apache bands the ability to disappear and regroup at will.


> In three days we reassembled at our appointed place of rendezvous in the Sierra Madre Mountains in northern Sonora. We then entered our camp in the Sierra Madre Mountains, and the next day began to move south.
>
> (010-003)

## The Final Campaign

The Sierra Madre figured in Geronimo's final campaigns as well. After leaving the San Carlos Reservation, "That night we went east into the foothills of the Sierra Madre Mountains and made another camp" (015-002). Mexican troops pursued and attacked again, but Geronimo records that "the Mexicans had a very large army, and we avoided a general engagement. It is senseless to fight when you cannot hope to win" (015-002). The Sierra Madre's terrain, which had enabled decades of Apache resistance, could not indefinitely protect dwindling bands against overwhelming force.
