# Lawton

Lawton, Oklahoma, was the town adjacent to [[fort-sill|Fort Sill]], where [[geronimo|Geronimo]] spent his final years as a prisoner of war. It was from Lawton that S. M. Barrett, superintendent of schools, launched the project to record Geronimo's life story — a collaboration that produced the definitive Apache account of the wars that shaped the American Southwest.

## Geography

Lawton was founded in 1901 in what was then Oklahoma Territory, situated in the southwestern part of the territory near the Fort Sill Military Reservation. The town grew rapidly as a civilian complement to the military post, providing housing, commerce, and services to the area. Its proximity to Fort Sill — roughly three miles from the Apache prisoner-of-war camp — made it the natural base for Barrett's interviews with Geronimo.

## Role in the Narrative

### Barrett's Base of Operations

The preface to Geronimo's autobiography, written by Barrett, is dated "Lawton, Oklahoma, Aug. 12th, 1905" (P03-001). Barrett describes how the project was conceived and executed from Lawton, where he held his position as superintendent of schools. The final page identifies the location simply: "Lawton, Oklahoma" (023-001).

From Lawton, Barrett made repeated visits to Geronimo's teepee at Fort Sill, conducting the interviews that became the autobiography. The process required navigating military bureaucracy — Barrett corresponded with the War Department and President [[president-roosevelt|Theodore Roosevelt]] from Lawton, securing the authorization to publish Geronimo's words.

### Captain Lawton

Lawton, Oklahoma, shares its name with Captain Henry W. Lawton, the U.S. Army officer who led the pursuit of Geronimo's band during the final Apache campaign of 1885–1886. Geronimo describes Lawton's relentless tracking: "They were led by Captain Lawton, who had good scouts" (017-001). Captain Lawton's command was "organized for a long pursuit" (018-SMG-2) and proved to be one of the most effective military operations against the Apache resistance.

The scouts serving under Captain Lawton — including Kayitah, a Chokonen Apache, and Marteen, a Nedni Apache — were the ones who finally arranged the meeting between Geronimo and [[general-miles|General Miles]] that led to the surrender (017-010). The town of Lawton, founded after the Apache Wars, was named for this officer.

> Captain Lawton reports, through Colonel Royall, commanding at Fort Huachuca, that his camp surprised Geronimo's camp on Yongi River, about 130 miles south and east of Campas, Sonora, or nearly 300 miles south of Mexican boundary, capturing all the Indian property, including hundreds of pounds of dried meat and nineteen riding animals. (018-SMG-3)

## Significance

Lawton represents the intersection of military conquest and historical preservation. The town named for the officer who helped capture Geronimo became the place from which Geronimo's story was told to the world. Barrett's work from Lawton ensured that the Apache perspective — not just the military reports of Captain Lawton — would survive.
