# Cochise

Cochise was the chief of the Chokonen (Chiricahua) Apaches, described by Geronimo as a disciplined and ceremonious leader who convened councils at dawn with warriors seated in silence by rank — and who allied his band with Mangus-Colorado's Bedonkohe in the war against Mexico.

## Overview

Cochise led the Chokonen Apaches during the period of Geronimo's early warrior years. His son Naiche would later become the hereditary chief and Geronimo's companion in the final campaigns. Geronimo's most detailed portrait of Cochise comes from a diplomatic mission: when Geronimo was dispatched by Mangus-Colorado to solicit Chokonen aid against Mexico, Cochise received him in a formal dawn council where warriors sat in silence arranged by rank, smoking, until the chief signaled for Geronimo to present his case (006-012).

## Life & Role

### Leadership of the Chokonen

Cochise was the established chief of the Chokonen Apaches "in the old days," succeeded later by his son Naiche (002-006). The Chokonen were among the Apache bands that maintained distinct leadership while cooperating militarily with the Bedonkohe and Nedni under a combined command structure.

### Alliance Against Mexico

When Geronimo came to Cochise seeking aid for the war against Mexico after the Kas-Ki-Yeh massacre, Cochise called a council at early dawn. The warriors assembled silently in a mountain dell, seated on the ground in rows according to rank, smoking in silence until the chief gave a signal for Geronimo to rise and present his appeal (006-012). Cochise committed his warriors to the combined Apache force that included Mangus-Colorado's Bedonkohe and Whoa's Nedni (006-018).

### Treaty Making

Cochise, like Mangus-Colorado, entered into treaty arrangements with American military posts. Geronimo records that "Cochise and Mangus-Colorado did likewise" in making treaties at Apache Pass (013-007), though Geronimo notes he does not know the exact terms. Cochise also received provisions from American authorities — at one point obtaining twelve steers for his tribe (015-004).

### Military Command

During combined campaigns, Cochise took command of Chokonen forces. In one engagement, a band of Chokonen Indians under Cochise "took command of both divisions" when the tactical situation required unified leadership (014-007).

## Relationship to Geronimo

Cochise was an ally and fellow chief. Geronimo served as Mangus-Colorado's envoy to Cochise, presenting the case for war against Mexico in a formal tribal council. Their relationship appears to have been one of mutual respect between allied leaders. Cochise's son Naiche would later become Geronimo's close companion in the final campaigns and surrender.
