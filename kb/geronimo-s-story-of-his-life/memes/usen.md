# Usen

Usen is the supreme being and creator in Apache religion, described by Geronimo as the divine entity who created the Apache people, gave them their homeland, taught them medicine and survival skills, and remained the central object of Apache worship and prayer.

## Overview

Usen occupies the central place in Apache spiritual life. He is the creator of all tribes of men, each with their own homeland purpose-built for their welfare (002-012). For the Apaches specifically, Usen created their homes in the West, provided grain, fruits, and game, taught them herbal medicine, and gave them a pleasant climate with everything needed for clothing and shelter (002-013). Geronimo frames the Apache relationship to Usen as one of direct, unmediated worship — there were no churches, no formal religious organizations, and no designated sabbath (004-005).

## Description

### Creation of the Apaches

In the Apache creation account, Usen taught the first man — named Apache — how to prepare herbs for medicine, how to hunt, and how to fight. Apache became the first chief of the Indians and wore the eagle's feathers as the sign of justice, wisdom, and power (001-019). Usen gave the Apaches their homeland in the land of the West, and Geronimo expresses a profound sense of displacement when separated from it: "When they are taken from these homes they sicken and die" (002-014).

### Creator of All Tribes

Apache theology holds that Usen created all tribes of men with a righteous purpose for each (002-011). For each tribe, Usen made a home tailored to its needs, placing whatever would be best for that tribe's welfare in its appointed land (002-012). This universalist view — that Usen's creation encompassed all peoples, not just the Apaches — is a distinctive feature of Apache religious thought.

### Prayer and Direct Worship

Apache worship was direct and personal. Geronimo's mother taught him as a child to kneel and pray to Usen for strength, health, wisdom, and protection (003-005). Significantly, Apaches never prayed against any person — if they had a grievance, they took vengeance themselves, believing Usen did not concern himself with the petty quarrels of men (003-005). Communal worship involved the whole tribe assembling to sing and pray, though smaller gatherings of two or three were also common. Prayers could be silent, spoken aloud individually, or led by an elder speaking for all (004-005).

### Source of Medical Knowledge

Usen was believed to be the source of all Apache medical knowledge. He created healing herbs and taught the people where to find them and how to prepare them (002-013). Each generation produced men skilled in the art of healing, continuing the knowledge that Usen had taught "in the beginning" (003-019).

### Moral Authority and Sacrifice

When disease or pestilence struck, the community would assemble and leaders would question the people to determine what evil had been done and how Usen could be satisfied. Sacrifice was sometimes deemed necessary, and offending individuals could be punished (004-006). This established Usen not only as creator but as a moral authority whose displeasure manifested in collective misfortune.

## Examples in the Narrative

- **Geronimo's childhood prayer** (003-005) — His mother taught him the legends of the Apache people and to kneel and pray to Usen for strength, health, wisdom, and protection.
- **Communal worship gatherings** (004-005) — The whole tribe would assemble to sing and pray, with songs that were not formal — singers could insert their own words. Sometimes an elder would speak about their duties to each other and to Usen.
- **Yucca harvest thanksgiving** (020-014) — Every summer the tribe gathered yucca fruit, ground it into cakes, and assembled to feast, sing, and give praises to Usen. Prayers of Thanksgiving were said by all participants.
- **Disease and sacrifice** (004-006) — When pestilence spread, leaders questioned the community to identify wrongdoing and determine how to satisfy Usen, sometimes requiring sacrifice or punishment.
