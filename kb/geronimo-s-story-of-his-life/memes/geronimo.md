# Geronimo

Geronimo (Mescalero-Chiricahua: *Goyahkla*, "One Who Yawns"; June 1829 – February 17, 1909) was a prominent war leader and medicine man of the [[bedonkohe-apache|Bedonkohe]] band of the Apache people. His autobiography, *Geronimo's Story of His Life*, dictated to [[general-miles|S. M. Barrett]] and published in 1906, stands as one of the most significant first-person accounts of the Apache Wars and the forced removal of Native Americans. Geronimo led his people through decades of conflict with [[mangus-colorado|Mexican]] and American forces, participating in numerous raids, battles, and ultimately the final Apache [[surrender-of-1886|surrender]] in 1886 at [[skeleton-canyon|Skeleton Canyon]].

## Early Life

Geronimo was born in No-doyohn Canyon, Arizona, in June 1829, the fourth of eight children — four boys and four girls — in a [[bedonkohe-apache|Bedonkohe Apache]] family (003-001). He describes his homeland as the country around the headwaters of the Gila River:

> This range was our fatherland; among these mountains our wigwams were hidden; the scattered valleys contained our fields; the boundless prairies, stretching away on every side, were our pastures; the rocky caverns were our burying places. (003-002)

As a child, Geronimo was taught the legends of his people by his mother, and the arts of war and hunting by his father (003-003). His grandfather, [[mangus-colorado|Maco]], was chief of the Bedonkohe Apaches before being succeeded by [[mangus-colorado|Mangus-Colorado]] (005-002). Geronimo's early life followed the traditional patterns of Apache upbringing: he "rolled on the dirt floor of my father's teepee, hung in my *tsoch* at my mother's back, or suspended from the bough of a tree" (003-003).

## Warrior Years

Geronimo became a warrior while still a young man, serving under the leadership of [[mangus-colorado|Mangus-Colorado]]. He participated in numerous campaigns against Mexican forces throughout the middle decades of the nineteenth century. His tactical skill and spiritual power as a medicine man grew steadily, and he earned a reputation as a fierce and resourceful fighter.

### The Kas-Ki-Yeh Massacre

The [[kas-ki-yeh-massacre|massacre at Kas-Ki-Yeh]] stands as the defining trauma of Geronimo's life. Mexican troops under the government of Sonora attacked the Apache camp while the warriors were away trading in [[kas-ki-yeh|the town]]. Geronimo returned to find his mother, his young wife [[alope|Alope]], and his three small children among the slain (006-005). He describes the aftermath:

> I was never again contented in our quiet home. … I had no purpose in life beyond revenge. If I could but kill one more Mexican — I did not care for my own life. (006-007)

This event drove Geronimo's decades-long campaign of revenge against Mexican forces, and the "power" he received at this time was understood by him to be a spiritual authorization for warfare.

### Geronimo's Mightiest Battle

The battle that Geronimo considered his greatest took place in 1883 in Mexico. With only a small band of warriors, he faced a combined force of Mexican infantry and cavalry. The Apaches were surrounded and outnumbered, and their ammunition was nearly exhausted (012-001). Geronimo describes the desperate charge:

> There was only one way out — to charge straight through the Mexican lines toward the mountains. We made breastworks of our dead horses, and there fought until our ammunition gave out. (012-004)

The battle was fought at a place that was later named "Geronimo" in his honor, on the Southern Pacific Railroad in Arizona (012-002). The engagement cemented his reputation as one of the most formidable Apache war leaders.

### Service Under Other Chiefs

Throughout his warrior career, Geronimo served alongside other prominent Apache leaders. He fought under [[cochise|Cochise]] of the [[chokonen-apache|Chokonen]] band, was a contemporary of [[whoa|Whoa]] of the [[nedni-apache|Nedni]], and later led alongside [[naiche|Naiche]], son of Cochise, who served as Geronimo's lieutenant during the protracted resistance of the 1880s (002-010).

## The Final Struggle

By the mid-1880s, the Apache resistance was under intense pressure. General Crook had attempted to negotiate a return to the reservation, but Geronimo "feared treachery and decided to remain in Mexico" (017-001). When [[general-miles|General Nelson A. Miles]] was made commander of all western posts, the pursuit intensified:

> Soon General Miles was made commander of all the western posts, and troops trailed us continually. They were led by Captain Lawton, who had good scouts. We had skirmishes almost every day. (017-003)

The campaign was relentless. Geronimo records that "we were reckless of our lives, because we felt every man's hand was against us" (017-010). The band was eventually worn down to a small remnant.

## Surrender

The [[surrender-of-1886|surrender at Skeleton Canyon]] in September 1886 marked the end of the Apache Wars. Geronimo and [[naiche|Naiche]] approached [[general-miles|General Miles]] under terms that Geronimo understood as a conditional surrender. According to Geronimo's account, Miles promised that after a period of detention the Apaches would be reunited with their families and allowed to return to Arizona (017-012).

These promises were not kept. Geronimo specifically "accuses General Miles of bad faith" regarding the government's subsequent treatment of Apache prisoners (P03-018). The Apaches were sent to Florida, then to Alabama, and finally to [[fort-sill|Fort Sill, Oklahoma]], where Geronimo remained a prisoner of war for over twenty years.

## Prisoner of War

At Fort Sill, Geronimo lived under military supervision but was allowed a measure of autonomy. He married several times, raised crops, and in his later years became a public figure. He participated in the [[ch-21|Omaha Exposition (1898)]], the [[ch-21|Pan-American Exposition (1901)]], and the [[ch-21|Louisiana Purchase Exposition (1904)]], where he sold autographs and photographs of himself (021-001).

In 1905, Geronimo agreed to tell his life story to [[general-miles|S. M. Barrett]], superintendent of schools at Lawton, Oklahoma. The project required personal authorization from President Theodore Roosevelt, secured through Barrett's petition that "here was an old Indian who had been held a prisoner of war for twenty years and had never been given a chance to tell his side of the story" (P03-005).

## Beliefs and Character

Geronimo's religious beliefs centered on [[usen|Usen]], the Apache life-giver, whom he addresses throughout the narrative. He describes Usen as the creator of "the sun, the moon, the stars, the mountains, the rivers, and the plains" and asserts that Usen "does not change his work" (003-012). Geronimo placed great faith in prayer and spiritual power, receiving war medicine that he believed made him invulnerable in battle.

His personal code emphasized truth-telling, loyalty to his people, and the warrior's obligation to protect the weak. He was also a pragmatist: "I have killed many Mexicans; I do not know how many … Some of them were not worth counting" (010-014).

## Legacy

Geronimo died of pneumonia at Fort Sill on February 17, 1909, still a prisoner of war. He was buried in the Apache prisoner-of-war cemetery at Fort Sill. His autobiography, published three years before his death, remains the definitive Apache account of the wars that shaped the American Southwest. Geronimo's name has entered the English language as a battle cry, and his life story continues to be read as both a war narrative and a testament to Apache endurance.

> The fact that Geronimo has told the story in his own way is doubtless the only excuse necessary for offering it to the public. (P03-020)
