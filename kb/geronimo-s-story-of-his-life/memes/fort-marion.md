## Overview

Fort Marion (now known as Castillo de San Marcos) in St. Augustine, Florida, was one of the detention sites where the U.S. government held [[geronimo]] and other [[chiricahua-apache|Chiricahua Apache]] prisoners of war following their surrender in 1886. The narrative describes the terms of surrender as including a promise that Geronimo would be reunited with his family and other Apaches at Fort Marion.

## Geography

Fort Marion is located on the Atlantic coast of Florida, in the city of St. Augustine. The fort, originally built by the Spanish as Castillo de San Marcos, sits on the western shore of Matanzas Bay. Its remote location in Florida, far from the Apache homeland in the Southwest, represented a deliberate strategy of separation and isolation of Apache prisoners from their traditional territory.

## Role in the Narrative

Fort Marion appears in the final chapters of Geronimo's story as part of the surrender agreement negotiated with [[general-miles|General Nelson A. Miles]]. According to the narrative:

- **Surrender Terms** — General Miles reportedly promised that Geronimo and his band would be "united with their families at Fort Marion" (019-008). The General drew a diagram on the ground with stones to illustrate the plan: placing the stone representing Geronimo's band "beside the one which represented [[chihuahua]] at Fort Marion" (019-010).

- **Broken Promises** — Geronimo states that the promise of family reunification at Fort Marion was not honored: "They regarded the separation of themselves from their families as a violation of the terms of their treaty of surrender" (019-008). The Apaches interpreted the guarantee of joining their families at Fort Marion as "the most positive manner conceivable to their minds."

- **Subsequent Transfers** — After Fort Marion, the Apache prisoners were moved to Mount Vernon Barracks in Alabama, and later to [[fort-sill]], Oklahoma. Fort Marion represents the beginning of a decades-long period of captivity for Geronimo and his people.
