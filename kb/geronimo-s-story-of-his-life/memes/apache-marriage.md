# Apache Marriage

Apache marriage customs combined formal negotiation between the suitor and the bride's father — typically involving a bride-price paid in ponies — with social rituals including the young woman's debut at a tribal dance, after which warriors could seek her hand.

## Overview

Marriage among the Apaches was a structured social institution governed by well-defined customs. A young woman's coming of age was publicly announced, typically at a tribal dance where she made her debut (020-019). A warrior seeking marriage would then approach the woman's father to negotiate, offering ponies or other valuable goods as a bride-price (005-005). Widowhood and separation also followed established customs — a widow with children typically remained single for two to three years, while a childless widow could remarry immediately (005-003).

## Description

### Coming of Age and Social Debut

When an Apache girl reached womanhood, her family announced it publicly. Geronimo describes announcing in 1905 that his daughter Eva had "attained womanhood" and would "put away childish things and assume her station as a young lady." At a dance of the tribe she would make her debut, after which it would be proper for a warrior to seek her hand in marriage (020-019). The debut involved an elaborate ceremonial dance in which the young woman advanced around the campfire, first alone, then with increasing numbers of companions, while medicine men participated with painted bodies (020-022).

### The Proposal and Bride-Price

When a warrior wished to marry, he approached the woman's father. The father could set a bride-price — Geronimo's courtship of Alope, daughter of No-po-so, required "many ponies" (005-005). Geronimo made no reply to the demand but returned days later with a herd of ponies, demonstrating the suitor's obligation to meet the father's terms. The transaction established the marriage bond and the husband's obligations to his wife's family.

### Widowhood Customs

After a warrior's death, his widow returned to her own people (005-003). Widows with children typically remained single for two to three years after the husband's death, while childless widows could remarry immediately. Geronimo's own mother never remarried after his father's death, though tribal custom permitted it.

### Separation and Divorce

Apache custom recognized forms of marital separation. Geronimo describes consenting to let one of his wives go to live at the Mescalero Agency — a separation "equivalent to what the white people call divorce" (019-005). The wife retained custody of their two small children, which she had a right to do under Apache custom, and soon remarried.

### Dance Partnership and Gift Exchange

At social dances, women designated warriors as dance partners. After the dance, each warrior gave a present to the lady who had selected him. If she was satisfied with the gift, they parted; if not, the matter was referred to an authority (medicine man or chief) who determined what constituted a proper gift (020-025). This custom created structured social interactions between unmarried men and women.

## Examples in the Narrative

- **Geronimo's marriage to Alope** (005-005) — After being admitted to the warrior council, Geronimo sought to marry Alope. Her father No-po-so demanded many ponies; Geronimo returned with a herd and secured the marriage.
- **Eva's debut** (020-019) — In 1905, Geronimo announced his daughter Eva's coming of age at a grand dance attended by Apaches, Comanches, and Kiowas near Medicine Creek.
- **Widowhood of Geronimo's mother** (005-003) — After his father's death, Geronimo assumed care of his mother, who chose not to remarry despite tribal custom permitting it.
- **Divorce at Mescalero** (019-005) — Geronimo's wife separated and moved to the Mescalero Agency, taking their two children — an arrangement recognized as divorce under Apache custom.
