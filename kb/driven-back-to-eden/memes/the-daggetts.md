# The Daggetts

The Daggetts are the Durham family's upstairs neighbors in the New York tenement — a shadow family whose presence defines, by negative example, what the Durhams are fleeing. They appear only briefly but their influence pervades the novel's opening: their scarlet fever threatens physical contagion, and Melissa Daggett's "cheap, vile journal" threatens moral contagion.

## Role in the Story

The Daggetts are introduced in the novel's first chapter as part of Robert Durham's inventory of urban dangers. Winnie has been upstairs with Melissa Daggett, from whom she has obtained a "cheap, vile journal, full of flashy pictures that so often offend the eye on newsstands" (001-019). The narrator's reaction reveals the novel's hierarchies of danger: "The Daggett children had had the scarlet fever a few months before. 'But here's a worse infection,' I reflected" — the moral infection of sensational print outweighing even the physical infection of disease.

The Daggetts' flat, like the Durhams', is cramped and unwholesome, but unlike the Durhams they lack the means or the will to escape. Their apartment is described in pointed contrast to the Durhams': "Our rooms would not have satisfied a Dutch housewife, but if 'order is heaven's first law' a little of Paradise was in them as compared to the Daggetts' apartments" (001-018). The Daggetts thus serve as a cautionary image — what the Durhams risk becoming if they remain in the city.

## Key Passages

- **"The Daggett children had had the scarlet fever... 'But here's a worse infection'"** (001-019) — physical and moral contagion linked
- **"A little of Paradise was in them as compared to the Daggetts' apartments"** (001-018) — the Daggetts as the floor below which the Durhams must not sink
- **Melissa Daggett's "cheap, vile journal"** — the novel's emblem of urban moral pollution

## Relationships

- **Melissa Daggett**: The daughter who lends Winnie the sensational newspaper, serving as a vector of moral contagion
- **The Durham children**: Winnie is drawn to Melissa's company; the Daggett children more broadly represent the peer influences the Durham parents fear
- **[[the-ricketts]]**: The family on the floor below, completing the vertical sandwich of urban crowding
