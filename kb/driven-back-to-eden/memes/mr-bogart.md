# Mr. Bogart

## Character Overview

Mr. Bogart is the New York City commission merchant who handles the sale of the Durham family's farm produce. Though he appears only indirectly — through the checks that arrive regularly and the narrator's references to "his good management" (036-005) — he is essential to the family's economic survival. He represents the commercial bridge between rural production and urban consumption, embodying the market relationships that make subsistence farming viable.

## Role in the Story

Bogart is introduced when the narrator explains commerce to his children on the Hudson River steamer: "Just as soon as we can bring down a crate of berries and send it to Mr. Bogart we shall be adding to the commerce of the world" (010-007). Throughout the summer and autumn, Bogart receives shipments of raspberries, potatoes, sweet corn, tomatoes, and chickens, sending back "checks that came regularly, the fruit bringing a fair price under his good management" (036-005). His reliability — sending returns every Monday — allows the narrator to pay off debts to Mr. Jones and begin saving for winter. Bogart is never physically present in the country scenes; his role is entirely functional, but his function is indispensable: without a market connection, the family's production would have no economic value.

## Key Passages

- "Just as soon as we can bring down a crate of berries and send it to Mr. Bogart we shall be adding to the commerce of the world" (010-007) — the family's introduction to commercial agriculture
- "Checks from Mr. Bogart came regularly, the fruit bringing a fair price under his good management" (036-005) — Bogart's reliability as commercial partner
- "On Monday he made his returns and sent me the proceeds of the fruit shipped previously" (036-005) — the rhythm of market exchange
