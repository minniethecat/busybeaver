## Character Overview

Mr. Rollins is a neighbor farmer near the Durham farm in Maizeville. He is a practical, community-minded man who appears at key moments of the novel: helping move the Durham family into the farm, joining the confrontation with the thieving Bagley, and supporting Robert Durham after the barn fire destroys the family's new building.

## Role in the Story

Rollins first appears as one of the neighbors who assists with moving the Durhams' furniture from the Maizeville Landing to the farm, driving one of the two wagon teams (014-044, 015-012). He emerges as a more prominent figure when he joins Robert Durham and Mr. Jones in confronting Bagley about stolen chickens — "His hen roost was robbed t'other night" (033-013), and during the confrontation he steps forward "excitedly" demanding justice (033-021). After Bagley's reformation begins, Rollins agrees to employ Bagley in exchange for restitution (034-063, 035-002). When lightning destroys the Durhams' new barn, Rollins is among the first neighbors to arrive and help (037-008), and it is the "warmhearted and impulsive Rollins" who declares, "when you are ready to build again, there are plenty who will help you" (037-014). He later sells some land to Robert Durham (042-023).

## Key Passages

- **The move**: Rollins drives one of the wagon teams bringing the Durhams' belongings from the landing to the farm (014-044, 015-012).
- **Confronting Bagley**: Rollins joins the posse to Bagley's house, stepping forward "excitedly" to demand justice for his stolen chickens (033-013, 033-021).
- **After the barn fire**: The "warmhearted and impulsive Rollins" promises the community will help rebuild (037-014).
- **Reconciliation with Bagley**: Later, Rollins and Bagley walk "in a companionable way down the road" together, the chicken theft repaid and forgiven (037-021).

## Relationships

Rollins is a neighbor to **[[robert-durham]]** and **[[john-jones]]**, joining them in both practical farm labor and community discipline. His relationship with **[[mr-bagley]]** evolves from victim of theft to employer and eventual companionable neighbor, reflecting the novel's theme of community reformation. He also sells land to Robert Durham, deepening the Durhams' stake in Maizeville.
