## Character Overview

Old Mr. Jacox is an aged farm laborer hired by Robert Durham during the critical haying season in late June. He is described as "too aged to hold his own in other harvest fields" yet proves himself a faithful worker, plodding "faithfully over the prostrate grass" (036-008). Jacox represents the Durhams' willingness to employ those whom others overlook — part of Robert Durham's "queer lot of helpers" (035-006).

## Role in the Story

Mr. Jacox first appears in "the last days of June," hired at "moderate wages" to help with the hay harvest (032-016). Robert Durham wryly describes him as part of a "queer lot of helpers" that includes the reformed Bagley — Jacox "is too aged to hold his own in other harvest fields" but serves well wielding a scythe in the Durhams' meadow (035-006). During the rush before a thunderstorm, "old Jacox was told to get the grass he had cut into as good shape as possible before the shower" (035-030). He later "plodded faithfully over the prostrate grass" (036-008) and is evacuated along with the children when the storm threatens (036-009).

## Key Passages

- **Hired at the hay harvest**: Jacox first appears, hired at moderate wages in late June (032-016).
- **Part of a "queer lot"**: Durham lists Jacox among his unusual helpers — "too aged to hold his own in other harvest fields" but valuable on the Durham farm (035-006).
- **Faithful labor**: "Old Jacox had plodded faithfully over the prostrate grass" through the busy season (036-008).

## Relationships

Jacox works for **[[robert-durham]]** alongside **[[mr-bagley]]** in an improbable workforce that Durham himself calls a "queer lot of helpers" (035-006). His presence reinforces the theme of inclusive community that runs through the novel.
