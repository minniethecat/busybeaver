## Character Overview

Mr. Jamison is the deceased previous owner of the farm near Maizeville that the Durham family purchases and renames "Eden." Although he never appears as a living character in the narrative, his presence pervades the first half of the novel through the memories of his neighbor John Jones and the physical traces he left behind — the farmhouse, the garden, the raspberry patches, the orchard, and the potato fields. He and his wife were the last of an old-fashioned farming family, and his death marks the end of an era that the Durhams inherit.

## Role in the Story

Mr. Jamison functions as an unseen benchmark against which the Durhams' farming efforts are measured. John Jones frequently refers to "old man Jamison" when explaining the farm's history and potential to Robert Durham (015-006). Jamison had been a competent farmer who "allus had the best of everything" (017-017), but in his final years he was "too poorly to take much care of things" (020-013), allowing the property to decline. His death precipitated the sale of the farm, creating the opportunity for the Durham family's move from the city.

Jones describes Jamison as a "great crony" (020-015) — a close friend and fellow farmer — and his testimony establishes Jamison's character as a hardworking, old-fashioned agriculturalist who "held on" when others were giving up on farming (020-023). The raspberry bushes, potato varieties, and chicken coops that the Durhams discover on the property are all legacies of Jamison's stewardship.

## Key Passages

- **First mention — the previous occupants**: John Jones tells Robert Durham that "Old Mr. and Mrs. Jamison lived here till lately," establishing the farm's history (007-013).
- **Jamison's decline**: Jones notes that Jamison "was poorly last year and didn't" tend to the garden properly, explaining the farm's neglected state at the time of sale (015-006).
- **The Jamison estate**: Robert must negotiate with the Jamison estate to purchase remaining equipment — "they belonged to the Jamison estate" (017-021).
- **A farmer of quality**: Jones remarks that "Jamison allus had the best of everything," establishing a standard that the Durhams aspire to match (017-017).
- **Raspberry legacy**: Jones explains that Jamison planted the raspberry bushes that become one of the Durhams' first successful crops — "Jamison, you see, grew these raspberries" (020-017).
