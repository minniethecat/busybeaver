# Driven Back to Eden

## Work Overview

*Driven Back to Eden* is a first-person domestic novel published in 1885 by the American author Edward Payson Roe (1838–1888). Originally serialized in *St. Nicholas* magazine — a leading children's periodical of the era — the novel follows Robert Durham, a New York City bookkeeper, who moves his wife and five children to a run-down farm in the Hudson River Valley. The novel's title encapsulates its central metaphor: the family is not merely moving to the country but returning to a state of original wholeness, "driven back" by circumstances they cannot control (002-021).

## Publication Context

Roe was a popular novelist in the domestic-realist tradition, often compared to Harriet Beecher Stowe and Susan Warner. *Driven Back to Eden* was serialized in *St. Nicholas* (founded 1873), a magazine that published work by Louisa May Alcott, Mark Twain, and Rudyard Kipling. The novel's didactic content — agricultural instruction woven into narrative — reflects *St. Nicholas*'s educational mission for young readers. The Standard Ebooks edition used for this wiki is based on the 1885 Dodd, Mead and Company book publication.

## Narrative Features

The novel is narrated in the first person by Robert Durham, whose voice is characterized by paternal warmth, self-deprecating humor, and the conventional pieties of 19th-century domestic fiction. The narrative is structured around the agricultural year, with each chapter organized around a seasonal task or event. The novel's 44 chapters run from late winter through the following New Year, covering the family's first complete year of country life.
