# The City Flat

The Durham family's New York City flat is less a home than a rhetorical foil — the negative pole against which the novel measures every subsequent domestic space. Located in a densely populated but "respectable" neighborhood not far from Robert Durham's place of business, the flat is described with a bitterness unusual in the otherwise genial narrator: "We called the narrow flat we lived in 'home.' The idea!" (001-024).

## Physical Description

The flat is "a long, narrow line of rooms, reaching from a crowded street to a little backyard" (001-024). Its dimensions mirror the family's constrained life: Bobsey treats it "like a cage to a wild bird" (001-027), and Mrs. Durham is "hot and flushed from too near acquaintance with the stove" (001-028). The backyard, far from offering relief, is "full of noisy children by day, and noisier cats by night" (001-024).

## Narrative Function

The city flat serves three narrative purposes. First, it establishes the problem the novel must solve: urban domestic space cannot sustain the family's physical or moral health. Second, it introduces the Daggetts above and the Ricketts below — neighbors whose proximity makes privacy impossible and whose influence threatens the children (Melissa Daggett's "cheap, vile journal," the scarlet fever from the Daggett children). Third, each inadequacy of the flat — noise, crowding, lack of outdoor space, moral contamination — prefigures the corresponding virtue of the farmhouse: quiet, spaciousness, the garden, moral sanctuary.

## Key Passages

- **"We called the narrow flat we lived in 'home.' The idea!"** (001-024) — the narrator's definitive rejection of urban domesticity
- **"It was not a home, and was scarcely a fit camping ground for such a family squad as ours"** (001-024) — the military metaphor that will be inverted when the farmhouse becomes a "citadel"
- **"Our living room was to him like a cage to a wild bird"** (001-027) — Bobsey's restlessness as an index of the flat's inadequacy
