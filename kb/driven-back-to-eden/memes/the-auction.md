## Overview

In late March of their first country spring, the Durham family attends a "vandoo" — a country farm auction — at a nearby farmhouse. Mr. Jones alerts them to the sale and offers to bid on their behalf (017-004). The event marks the family's first significant engagement with the rural community, providing both practical acquisitions and cultural immersion.

## Narrative Account

Mr. Jones informs Robert Durham of the upcoming auction: "there's to be a vandoo the first of the week" (017-004). A family heading West is selling everything, indoors and out. On auction day, vehicles of all kinds converge on the muddy roads — "stylish-looking people drove up in top buggies, and there were many heavy, springless wagons driven by rusty-looking countrymen" (021-007).

The auctioneer is "a rustic humorist" who entertains the crowd while driving up bids (021-007). The Durham children are fascinated: Merton observes the bidding, Winnie eyes household items, and Bobsey enjoys the free lunch of coffee, crackers, and cheese. Robert purchases essential furniture and farm tools at reasonable prices. The second day after the auction, the family returns to collect their purchases, integrating them into their home (022-002).

## Significance

The auction represents the Durhams' practical and social initiation into country life. Economically, it allows them to furnish their home and equip their farm at minimal cost — a crucial advantage for a family of limited means. Socially, it is their first public appearance as community members rather than city outsiders. The event also demonstrates the value of Mr. Jones's guidance: without his knowledge of the auction and willingness to bid, the family would have missed a vital opportunity. The auction thus reinforces the novel's themes of neighborly assistance and rural resourcefulness.
