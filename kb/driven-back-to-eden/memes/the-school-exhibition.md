# The School Exhibition

The winter exhibition at the district schoolhouse is the novel's emblematic community event — a gathering that brings together the scattered farm families of the Maizeville district for an evening of recitations, spelling competitions, and shared culture. For the Durham children, it is a rite of passage: their first public demonstration of what they have learned since leaving the city.

## The Event

Country school exhibitions of the period typically featured students reciting memorized pieces (poems, speeches, dialogues), competing in spelling bees, and displaying copybooks, drawings, and other schoolwork. Families from miles around would attend, making the exhibition as much a social occasion as an academic one. The event combined education, entertainment, and community bonding in a single evening.

## Narrative Function

The exhibition marks the Durham children's full integration into the country community. When Merton, Winnie, or Bobsey stands before the assembled district families and performs creditably, it is public confirmation that the city children belong. The event also allows the novel to contrast country education — communal, celebratory, connected to community life — with the anonymous, institutional schooling of the city.
