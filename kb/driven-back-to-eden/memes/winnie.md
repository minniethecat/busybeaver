# Winnie

## Character Overview

Winnie is one of the Durham daughters, distinguished by her affinity for animal care. When the family buys agricultural books at Christmas, "to Winnie was given a book on poultry, and the cuts representing the various birds were even more to her taste than cuts from the fowls themselves at a Christmas dinner" (004-002). This early identification of her natural interest — poultry husbandry — proves prescient: on the farm, she takes charge of the chickens and becomes a reliable contributor to the family economy.

## Role in the Story

Winnie appears less frequently than Mousie or Bobsey, reflecting her position as a middle child who is neither sickly nor troublesome — she is, in the narrator's implicit taxonomy, the easy child. Her Christmas gift marks her as having a clear, practical bent, and on the farm she fulfills that promise. She is often paired with Bobsey in lessons (024-009) and in play — she helps him gather prunings (020-002) — serving as a bridge between the older and younger children. Her poultry-keeping contributes directly to the family's subsistence economy through egg production. She appears in collective family scenes (the steamboat journey, the first night in the farmhouse, Thanksgiving) as a steady, untroubled presence.

## Key Passages

- "To Winnie was given a book on poultry, and the cuts representing the various birds were even more to her taste than cuts from the fowls themselves" (004-002) — natural aptitude revealed
- "She and Mousie began giving Winnie and Bobsey daily lessons" (024-009) — Winnie as student in the family school
- "Gathering and piling up my prunings would be as good fun as anything else" (020-002) — Winnie and Bobsey finding play in work
