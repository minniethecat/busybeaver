## Overview

On an early April day, the Durham family gathers brush and dead wood from their clearing work, piling it for a bonfire that becomes an impromptu celebration. The burning transforms labor into festivity: the children exult in the flames, and a simple outdoor meal elevates the occasion into a feast. The bonfire encapsulates the novel's vision of country life, where work and joy intertwine.

## Narrative Account

Robert Durham begins the day with garden preparation, turning soil while his children carry armfuls of brush "to our prospective bonfire" (019-014). The promise of the bonfire motivates the younger children through the labor: "they soon wished to see the great pile on fire" (019-017). Robert promises the bonfire will not be lit until sundown, building anticipation throughout the day.

By late afternoon, Mr. Jones arrives to help ready the ground for plowing. As dusk falls, Robert applies a match to the brush pile. The children "set up shouts of exultation" as flames leap upward (022-023). The fire transmutes the "unsightly rubbish" of clearing work into warmth and spectacle (023-004). The family gathers around the flames and shares a simple but satisfying meal — roasted potatoes from the cellar, bread, and milk — which the narrator calls "a feast" (023-018).

## Significance

The bonfire represents a pivotal emotional moment in the family's country transition. After weeks of hard labor, the burning of the brush symbolizes the clearing away of their former city life and the kindling of their new rural identity. The event requires no money and no special preparation — it arises naturally from necessary work — yet produces more genuine joy than any city entertainment the family had known. The bonfire also reinforces the novel's argument that family unity is strengthened through shared labor and simple pleasures. Robert notes that "nothing greater than a bonfire was in prospect, yet few costly pleasures could have excited more anticipation" (023-002).
