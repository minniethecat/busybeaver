# Worship and Religion

Religious faith permeates *Driven Back to Eden* not as doctrine but as domestic practice. The Durhams' Protestant piety is woven into the texture of daily life: morning and evening prayers, Sunday church attendance, and the sanctification of labor as a form of devotion. The novel's religion is practical, familial, and inseparable from the rhythms of the agricultural year.

## Expression in the Text

The novel's key religious moment occurs on the family's first night in the farmhouse. Exhausted, cold, and disoriented after a stormy arrival, the Durhams nevertheless "knelt around our hearth, and made it an altar to God" (013-032). This single act fuses three of the novel's core values — home, family, and worship — in a single ritual. The hearth is simultaneously fireplace, gathering place, and altar; the family is simultaneously household and congregation.

This pattern continues throughout the novel. Dr. Lyman, the country minister, models a faith that engages rather than withdraws from the world — he is a naturalist and physician as well as preacher. Bobsey provides the novel's most direct theological statement when he declares that "God put the first people in a garden and they was very good, better'n any folks afterwards. God oughter know the best place for people" — a child's Edenic logic that sanctions the family's entire country project.

## Key Passages

- **"We knelt around our hearth, and made it an altar to God"** (013-032) — the founding religious act of the family's country life
- **"God put the first people in a garden... God oughter know the best place for people"** — Bobsey's child-theology that gives divine warrant to the move
- **Dr. Lyman's ministry** — a model of engaged faith that bridges the spiritual and the practical
