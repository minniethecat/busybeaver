# Planting Season

The first planting season is the family's rite of passage from city people to country people. For weeks in April and May, the entire household — father, mother, and all four children — works the soil of [[the-garden]] and the surrounding fields, putting in potatoes, corn, peas, beans, onions, radishes, beets, and strawberries. The planting is both a practical necessity (the family must grow food to live) and a symbolic act: each seed placed in the ground is a wager on the country experiment.

## Narrative Account

The planting season begins in earnest once the ground thaws. The narrator consults his "garden book" (022-021) — a constant companion through these weeks — and the family pores over seed catalogues from "several dealers in seeds" (006-003). The seed packets become objects of almost talismanic significance: "Onion seed that looked like gunpowder" (022-012), "two quarts of red and two of white onion sets" (022-007), radish and beet seeds, pea seeds, and strawberry plants ordered by mail.

Mr. Jones brings his horse and plow for the heavy work: "I'll have the rest of the garden plowed" (022-009). With the ground turned, the narrator stretches a garden line "across it, and, with a sharp-pointed hoe, made a shallow drill" (022-010). The children take their assigned roles — Mousie dropping seeds at careful intervals, Winnie covering them, Merton handling the heavier work — and the planting becomes a family ritual.

The scale is ambitious for beginners. Beyond the kitchen garden, the narrator plants "the other acre of ground" (028-008) with corn and potatoes, describing it as "our great undertaking." The work stretches across weeks: early potatoes and hardy vegetables first (022-006), then corn on "the warm garden slope" (028-003), then successive plantings of beans and late vegetables. By late May, "our first plantings of potatoes and early vegetables were making rapid growth" (028-015), and the family can look across their work with the satisfaction of creators.

## Significance

The planting season dramatizes the novel's most fundamental argument: that direct engagement with the soil transforms character. The narrator frames this explicitly: the children are learning not just agriculture but "the secret of the world's wealth — that it comes from the soil" (023-022). Each seed dropped is a lesson in causation — effort now, reward later — that city life never taught.

The planting also establishes the family's economic model. They plant for subsistence (potatoes, vegetables for the table) and for cash (the strawberry patch, extra potatoes to sell). The narrator's careful calculations — seed spacing, planting depth, succession planting — model the shift from urban consumption to rural production that the novel advocates.

The communal dimension is equally important. Mr. Jones's plowing, his advice about planting distances, and the children's shared labor all reinforce that country life is collaborative, not solitary. The planting season is when the Durhams stop being visitors and start being farmers.

## Key Moments

- **The seed catalogues** (006-003–006): On winter evenings, the family studies seed catalogues "by the fire" — imagination preparing the ground for action.
- **Mousie's seedlings** (022-004): "Cabbage, lettuce, and tomato seeds were sprouting beside Mousie's window" — the indoor start that precedes the outdoor campaign.
- **Plowing day** (022-009–016): Mr. Jones brings his horse: "I'll plow the garden" — the moment the garden moves from plan to reality.
- **The first seeds** (022-010–012): The narrator stretches a garden line, makes a drill, and drops the first onion seeds — the precise, almost ceremonial beginning.
- **"The other acre"** (028-008): The narrator describes "our great undertaking — the planting of the other acre of ground" — ambition exceeding the kitchen garden.
- **Succession planting** (028-004): "When this planting is up a few inches high, we will plant another" — the narrator adopting the farmer's long view, thinking in seasons rather than days.
- **The first shoots** (028-015): "Our first plantings of potatoes and early vegetables were making rapid growth" — the reward for weeks of labor, and the promise of the harvest to come.
