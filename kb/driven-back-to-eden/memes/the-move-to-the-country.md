# The Move to the Country

## Event Overview

The move to the country spans chapters 9–13, from the family's final preparations in New York to their first night in the frozen farmhouse. It comprises "Breaking Camp" (chapter 9), the wharf scenes and steamboat voyage up the Hudson (chapters 10–11), and the arrival at Maizeville Landing on "A March Evening in Eden" (chapter 12). The move transforms the novel's spatial and emotional coordinates: everything before it is city, everything after is country.

## Narrative Significance

The move is the novel's structural hinge. The narrator frames it as both a practical relocation and a providential journey — the family is being "driven back to Eden" by forces beyond their control. The steamboat voyage provides a suspended space where the narrator can educate his children about commerce (010-007) and where each family member's anticipation is dramatized: Mousie's quiet hope, Merton's sportsman's eagerness, Bobsey's excitement. The arrival — "a half-frozen and badly frightened family" (013-013) — is deliberately underwhelming, contrasting the Edenic ideal with the harsh reality of a March night in an unheated farmhouse.

## Key Passages

- "Breaking Camp" (chapter 9) — the family's final departure from the city
- "Scenes on the Wharf" (chapter 10) — the Hudson River as threshold
- "This is different from the Eden of which we have been dreaming" (012-047) — reality confronts expectation
- "Never did the word mean more to a half-frozen and badly frightened family" (013-013) — the farmhouse as refuge
