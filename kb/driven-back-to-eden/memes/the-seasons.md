# The Seasons

## Definition

In *Driven Back to Eden*, the seasons are not merely a backdrop but the novel's fundamental organizing principle. The Durham family arrives in late winter (March), and the narrative follows the agricultural year through spring planting, summer harvest, autumn gathering, and winter consolidation. This seasonal structure replaces the abstract, uniform time of city wage work — "the lightning speed with which things are done nowadays" (002-021) — with the cyclical, task-driven time of rural life.

## Expression in the Text

The novel opens in winter, with the family still in their city flat, but the move to the country coincides with late March, "the best month for pruning trees" (020-002). Spring brings the first planting: onions, peas, potatoes, and garden vegetables. Summer is the season of intensive labor and reward — raspberry harvests, fishing trips, the children growing "as brown as little Indians" (036-005). Summer also brings dangers: a violent thunderstorm in chapter 36 threatens both crops and family. Autumn is the season of gathering and preservation — "Hoarding for Winter" (041-001), the penultimate chapter, describes the family's efforts to store food, cut firewood, and prepare for cold months. Winter returns in the final chapters as a time of accounting, Thanksgiving celebration (043-005), and hopeful planning for the coming year.

## Narrative Function

The seasonal structure serves multiple narrative purposes. It provides the novel's episodic rhythm, with each chapter organized around a seasonal task. It marks the family's progress: the narrator's growing competence is measured by his ability to anticipate and meet each season's demands. It also embodies the novel's central contrast between city and country time — the former is linear, stressful, and externally dictated; the latter is cyclical, purposeful, and tied to natural processes. The final chapter's New Year's resolution (044-012) closes the seasonal cycle with the promise of its renewal.

## Key Passages

- "You grow one thing up here in all seasons, I fancy — an appetite" (007-025) — John Jones's seasonal humor on the narrator's first visit
- "The old canes that had borne fruit the previous season were still standing, ragged and unsightly" (020-003) — the seasonal work of renewal, visualized
- "If we have a moist season, like enough they'll do well" (022-014) — farming as negotiation with seasonal uncertainty
- "Early in April, therefore, our country life was passing into a quiet routine" (024-009) — the settling into seasonal rhythm
- "Hoarding for Winter" (041-001) — the chapter title that marks autumn's task
