# Community

## Definition

In *Driven Back to Eden*, community is the web of rural relationships that makes country life sustainable. It is embodied most fully in John Jones, the bluff, honest neighbor farmer who sells the Durhams their farm, advises them on everything from pruning to planting, and treats the narrator with a rough friendliness that the city never offered: "You're amazin' refreshin' for a city chap" (007-011). Community is presented as an advantage of rural life that the narrator did not anticipate — the city flat offered proximity without connection, while the country offers distance with genuine mutual aid.

## Expression in the Text

The novel's community is centered on the Jones family. John Jones is introduced as a blunt, practical man whose local knowledge is invaluable: he knows which slopes catch the morning sun, which soil drains well, and what a "city chap" needs to survive his first year (007-018). His friendship is both personal and economic — he holds the mortgage on the farm but also shows up to advise on raspberry pruning and onion planting. The relationship matures over the year: by winter, the narrator reflects on the neighbor's maxim that "as the farmer's land grows rich he grows poor" (019-019), absorbing local wisdom. The community extends beyond the Joneses to include the Irish washerwoman, the summer boarders who buy produce, and the broader network of growers and marketmen who form the economic ecosystem the Durhams enter.

## Narrative Function

Community provides the novel's social argument for country life. The city is characterized by anonymous proximity — the Daggetts upstairs, the Ricketts downstairs, the "low roar of the city" — while the country offers named, meaningful relationships. The Jones family functions as a foil to the Durhams: established, competent, and generous, they demonstrate what the narrator's family might become. Community also serves a practical narrative function: John Jones is the source of the agricultural knowledge the narrator lacks, making the family's rapid learning curve plausible.

## Key Passages

- "You're amazin' refreshin' for a city chap" (007-011) — John Jones's characteristic blend of humor and welcome
- "Don't you see? The house is on a slope facing the southeast. You get the morning sun and the southern breeze" (007-018) — local knowledge freely shared
- "As the farmer's land grows rich he grows poor" (019-019) — rural wisdom transmitted across the fence line
- "I'll crack on another hundred to the price" (007-011) — neighborly teasing as social bonding
- "My good old mother taught me that it was God's plan to give us a chance, and help us make the most of it" (043-005) — community wisdom passed through generations
