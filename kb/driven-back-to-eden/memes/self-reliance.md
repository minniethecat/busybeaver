# Self-Reliance

## Definition

Self-reliance in *Driven Back to Eden* means the family's capacity to sustain itself through direct labor on the land, rather than through the narrator's city salary as a bookkeeper. It is both a practical economic calculation — "what does my salary give us but food and clothing and shelter" (002-021) — and a moral aspiration toward the dignity of being among "what are called the 'producers'" (010-007). The concept fuses Emersonian individualism with the concrete realities of growing food, selling produce, and managing household accounts.

## Expression in the Text

The narrator's proposal is starkly material: "I propose that we go to the country and get our living out of the soil" (002-019). Throughout the winter before the move, he studies farming "during all my waking hours not devoted to business" (005-005), haunting Washington Market to learn from "plain, honest, sensible producers" what crops sell and at what profit. Once on the farm, self-reliance takes concrete form: pruning orchards, planting gardens, raising chickens, and selling raspberries through Mr. Bogart. By late summer, the narrator can pay off debts and begin hoarding for winter (036-005). The final chapter title — "We Can Make a Living in Eden" (044-001) — declares the project's success.

## Narrative Function

Self-reliance provides the novel's economic plot arc: the family moves from precarious wage-dependence to grounded subsistence. It also functions as a moral proving ground. Characters reveal their nature through their relationship to work: Merton's "Nimrod instincts" channeled into productive sport, Mousie's recovery tied to garden tasks, Bobsey's immaturity marked by "the tendencies of a consumer, and not of a producer or saver" (037-037). The concept organizes the novel's implicit critique of urban wage labor — work that separates the father from his family — against the ideal of integrated rural labor where "all could do something, even down to pale Mousie and little Bobsey" (002-021).

## Key Passages

- "I propose that we go to the country and get our living out of the soil" (002-019) — the novel's economic thesis
- "What does my salary give us but food and clothing and shelter" (002-021) — the insufficiency of wage labor as family support
- "We shall become what are called the 'producers,' and but for this class the world would soon come to an end" (010-007) — the moral dignity of productive labor
- "Bobsey's tendencies were those of a consumer, and not of a producer or saver" (037-037) — character measured by economic role
- "We Can Make a Living in Eden" (044-001) — the concluding affirmation of achieved self-reliance
