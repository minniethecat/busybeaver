# The Barn

The barn on the Durham farm stands as the most symbolically freighted structure in the novel — and for much of the story, it does not stand at all. Built new by the previous owner shortly before the Durhams' arrival, it burns to the ground in the thunderstorm of Chapter XXXVI, then rises again through a winter of neighborly labor. Its arc from construction to destruction to reconstruction mirrors the family's own journey: loss met by resilience, individual effort reinforced by [[community]].

## Description in the Text

The barn first appears during the Durhams' inspection of the farm: "nothing new except a barn that had recently been built on a hillside" (007-015). This hillside construction is deliberate — the basement level opens at grade on the lower side, providing stables for the horse and cow, while the upper level serves as the hay floor and wagon entrance. The "old barn" (016-008), by contrast, is a dilapidated structure slated for conversion into a chicken house for Winnie's flock. When the narrator first examines it, he notes that "the old barn showed the need of a good many repairs" (016-008), but sees in its south-facing basement the perfect "place for Winnie's chickens" (016-011).

John Jones shows the narrator a practical country trick for lighting the barn interior: "I don't b'lieve in matches around a barn, but I'll show you something" (017-010), using a fragment of looking-glass to reflect sunlight — a small detail that embodies the novel's celebration of rural ingenuity.

By midsummer, the barn is well-stocked: "the barn was now so well filled that I began to calculate how many more tons I would try to store away" (036-008). This prosperity makes its destruction all the more devastating.

## Narrative Significance

The barn serves three overlapping roles in the novel's thematic architecture.

**First, as a site of work.** The barn and barnyard are where the family's agricultural labor concentrates: cleaning out manure for fertilizer (022-007), storing hay (032-016), housing the horse and cow (021-030–031), and drying the raspberry harvest (035-031). The barn anchors the farm's daily rhythm and makes visible the family's growing competence.

**Second, as a symbol of providential testing.** The thunderstorm of Chapter XXXVI strikes the barn directly: "a stream of fire was bursting from the roof of our barn" (036-014). The loss is total — "the hay and the things in the barn at the time will prove a total loss" (037-027) — and the narrator confesses that "the absence of the barn made a great gap in our family circle" (037-029). The barn's destruction is the novel's darkest moment, testing the family's faith in their country enterprise.

**Third, as a demonstration of community.** The rebuilding becomes a collective effort. Neighbors contribute labor, and the narrator resolves that "the burning of the barn should add to our success in the end" (037-043). The new barn is designed better than the old — connected to a sty for pigs and a poultry house for chickens (041-002–004) — embodying the novel's argument that adversity, properly met, yields improvement. By the final chapters, "our new barn, now about completed, gave my wife and me a sense of security" (042-041).

## Key Scenes

- **First inspection** (007-015): The narrator notes "nothing new except a barn that had recently been built" — the barn as promise of the farm's potential.
- **Converting the old barn for chickens** (016-008–017): "We are going to clean barn, and make a place for Winnie's chickens" — the children's first farm project, linking the barn to the family's domestic economy.
- **The looking-glass lantern** (017-010): John Jones's improvised sunlight-reflector — rural ingenuity in miniature.
- **Loading the hay** (034-058): "By six o'clock, we had in the barn three loads of hay" — the satisfaction of harvest stored safely.
- **The lightning strike** (036-014): "A stream of fire was bursting from the roof of our barn" — the novel's most dramatic single image.
- **The morning after** (037-029): "The absence of the barn made a great gap in our family circle" — the barn anthropomorphized as a lost family member.
- **The resolution** (037-043): "The burning of the barn should add to our success in the end" — the novel's ethic of resilience crystallized.
- **Rebuilding begins** (040-021): "Around the foundations of the barn, and before night the first logs were in place" — the community at work.
- **The new barn completed** (042-041): "Our new barn, now about completed, gave my wife and me a sense of security" — restoration and the closing of the wound.
