# Mrs. Jones

## Character Overview

Mrs. Jones is the wife of John Jones and the Durham family's nearest female neighbor. Where her husband provides agricultural mentorship and bluff humor, Mrs. Jones offers domestic warmth, practical assistance, and neighborly goodwill. She is introduced in pointed contrast to her tall, angular husband — "short, red, and round" — but her bustling energy and generous table quickly win the narrator's lasting regard. Throughout the novel, she embodies the feminine dimension of country neighborliness: the ready meal, the made-up bed, the steady presence at moments of both celebration and crisis.

## Role in the Story

Mrs. Jones first appears when the narrator visits the Jamison farm with her husband, serving "a great dish of fricasseed chicken" that establishes her as a figure of abundance and welcome (007-027). When the Durham family arrives at Maizeville Landing in a March snowstorm, it is Mrs. Jones who has prepared the one remaining bed and made their cold farmhouse habitable (013-024). The narrator observes that "Mr. and Mrs. Jones's kind offices" sustain the family through their first disorienting days in the country (013-025).

Her practical support continues through the spring. She furnishes a dinner for the family when Mrs. Durham's strength is taxed (015-012) and makes regular neighborly visits — "Mrs. Jones had been over to see my wife" (018-022). In summer she joins the Durhams on their trip to the seashore, where "plump Mrs. Jones dozed and smiled, and wondered at the restless waves" but could not be induced to venture beyond ankle-deep water (039-006, 039-013). At Thanksgiving she helps prepare the feast, remembers to include the Bagley family, and assists with clearing away — her last quiet acts of neighborly completion before the novel's final scenes (043-006, 043-009). She stands beside Mrs. Durham through the pig-slaughtering that closes the narrative year (044-002).

## Key Passages

- First impression: "Mrs. Jones made a striking contrast to her husband, for she first impressed me as being short, red, and round; but her friendly, bustling ways and hearty welcome soon added other and very pleasant impressions" (007-027)
- "Mrs. Jones has made up the one bed left in the house, and with a great fire in the kitchen stove had managed to give the place a faint aspect of comfort" (013-024)
- "Mrs. Jones came over, and we made her run the gauntlet of our questions" (019-007) — the family's eager dependence on her local knowledge
- Seashore episode: "Plump Mrs. Jones dozed and smiled, and wondered at the restless waves" (039-006); "Mrs. Jones could not be induced to go beyond ankle-deep in the surf" (039-013)
- Thanksgiving: "You've remembered the Bagleys today," Mrs. Jones remarked (043-006) — her quiet thoughtfulness for others
