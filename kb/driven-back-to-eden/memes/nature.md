# Nature

In *Driven Back to Eden*, nature is neither a picturesque backdrop nor a wilderness to be conquered, but a normative order. The novel presents the natural world as the context in which human beings — and especially children — were designed to flourish. The city, by contrast, is an "unnatural" environment whose chief defect is that it severs people from "a natural and wholesome out-of-door life" (001-032).

## Nature as Normative Order

The novel's title announces its argument: the Durhams are *driven back* to Eden — not to a pristine wilderness but to a cultivated landscape where nature and human labor meet. The natural world the novel celebrates is the nature of the Hudson Valley: farms, orchards, brooks, meadows, and woodlands. It is nature as *home* rather than nature as *wilderness*. The seasons provide a divinely ordained rhythm; the soil provides a divinely ordained classroom. When Bobsey declares that "God put the first people in a garden... God oughter know the best place for people," he articulates the novel's natural theology in its simplest form.

## Expression in the Text

The narrator's critique of city life pivots on the absence of nature: "It is the curse of crowded city life that there is so little of a natural and wholesome out-of-door life" (001-032). Every aspect of the country — the brook, the garden, the orchard, the meadow, the changing seasons — is presented as a restorative force. Mousie's health improves not primarily through medicine but through exposure to fresh air, sunlight, and the rhythms of farm life. The children's education is advanced not primarily through books but through direct engagement with the natural world.

## Key Passages

- **"It is the curse of crowded city life that there is so little of a natural and wholesome out-of-door life"** (001-032) — the novel's thesis statement on nature vs. city
- **Bobsey's Edenic logic** — "God put the first people in a garden... God oughter know the best place for people"
- **Mousie's recovery through outdoor life** — nature as medicine
