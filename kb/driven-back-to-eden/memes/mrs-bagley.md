## Character Overview

Mrs. Bagley is the wife of Bagley, the shiftless and troublesome neighbor near the Durham farm in Maizeville. She is a largely silent figure, mentioned primarily as part of "the Bagleys" — the family that the Durhams and their neighbors work to reform from thievery and idleness into decent, productive members of the community. Though her direct appearances are few, she represents the unseen beneficiary of the Durhams' charitable intervention.

## Role in the Story

Mrs. Bagley enters the narrative obliquely, first through Robert Durham's confrontation with her husband Bagley at their "dilapidated hovel" where "all his family" is present (033-017). She is the silent recipient of the Durhams' practical charity: Mrs. Durham brings food and clothing to the Bagley household, and by the novel's close, the Bagley children are attending school in respectable clothes that Mrs. Bagley has "been able to buy and make" (042-037). Her trajectory mirrors her husband's reformation — from the wife of a "rapscallion" thief (033-013) to a member of a family that the Joneses feel "ought to be encouraged" (043-006).

## Key Passages

- **The Bagley family at home**: Robert Durham, Mr. Jones, and Rollins confront Bagley at his "dilapidated hovel" with "all his family" present (033-017), marking the beginning of the reformation effort that Mrs. Bagley benefits from.
- **Mrs. Durham's charity**: After the confrontation, Mrs. Durham is asked to "go down and see" the Bagley household, bringing food and presence that begin the family's transformation (034-020).
- **School clothes**: By December, Mrs. Bagley has acquired and made clothes so that "all three of the young Bagleys make a very respectable appearance" for school (042-037).
- **Thanksgiving encouragement**: Mrs. Jones remembers the Bagleys at Thanksgiving and sends them food, noting "they ought to be encouraged" (043-006).

## Relationships

The Bagley family — husband **[[mr-bagley]]** and their children — are the primary context for Mrs. Bagley's existence in the novel. She is indirectly connected to **[[mrs-durham]]**, whose charitable visits help transform the Bagley household, and to **[[mrs-jones]]**, who also reaches out to the family.
