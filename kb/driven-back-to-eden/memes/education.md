# Education

## Definition

In *Driven Back to Eden*, education is a lifelong, family-centered process that blends formal study with practical experience. The narrator explicitly rejects the notion that schooling and learning are synonymous. The family's preparation for country life itself becomes an educational project: they study agricultural books together, learn geography through map-reading, and absorb economics at Washington Market. Once on the farm, the mother and Mousie conduct daily lessons (024-009) while the children's farm work provides an education in responsibility, natural science, and self-discipline that no city school could offer.

## Expression in the Text

The novel's educational philosophy is dramatized in the Christmas book-buying expedition, where the family replaces candy and toys with agricultural manuals: "we all had agreed to do without candy, toys, and knickknacks, and to buy books that would tell us how to live in the country" (004-002). Each child's book choice reveals a natural aptitude — Winnie's poultry guide, Merton's hunting and fishing manual — that formal schooling had not recognized. The narrator treats the entire winter of preparation as "so much education out of school hours" (005-005). Once settled, education continues through structured lessons and unstructured farm experience. The children's practical competency — identifying plants, caring for animals, understanding weather — is presented as a more valuable education than city schooling could provide.

## Narrative Function

Education serves the novel's broader argument that country life is superior to city life not merely in health but in human formation. The city school is an institution the family leaves behind; country education is an ongoing family project. This framing also allows the narrator to position himself as both father and teacher, reinforcing the paternal authority that structures the novel. The children's growth in knowledge and skill parallels the family's growth in economic security — both are products of the same integrated rural life.

## Key Passages

- "We all had agreed to do without candy, toys, and knickknacks, and to buy books that would tell us how to live in the country" (004-002) — education prioritized over consumption
- "All this was so much education out of school hours" (005-005) — learning reframed as continuous rather than institutional
- "She and Mousie began giving Winnie and Bobsey daily lessons, for we had decided that the children should not go to school until the coming autumn" (024-009) — homeschooling as deliberate choice
- "There is no crop in the country so important as that of boys and girls" (001-024) — the novel's statement on education as primary agricultural product
- "You shall have the book and a breech-loading shotgun also" (004-003) — combining book learning with practical skill
