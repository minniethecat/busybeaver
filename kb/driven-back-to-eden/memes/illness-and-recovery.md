# Illness and Recovery

Illness is the novel's most intimate argument for leaving the city. The Durham family's move is motivated not primarily by economics or aesthetics but by the visible decline of its most vulnerable member: Mousie, the fourteen-year-old daughter whose pallor and frailty — "she appeared to be scarcely more than twelve, so diminutive was her frame" — haunt the novel's opening chapters. Her recovery in the country provides the novel's medical thesis: nature heals.

## The City Illness

The novel opens with a diagnostic gaze. Robert Durham asks Mousie, "Do you feel stronger tonight?" and receives the answer he always receives: "Yes, papa" — but "she always said she was better, but never got well." The city flat is not merely uncomfortable; it is pathogenic. The tenement's poor air, lack of sunlight, and confinement are presented as causes of the children's physical decline. The Daggett children have had scarlet fever; the Ricketts below are another vector of urban contagion. The city is not just morally but medically dangerous.

## The Country Cure

Mousie's recovery — gradual, then dramatic — is the novel's most sustained proof of country life's superiority. Fresh air, outdoor activity, wholesome food, and the rhythms of farm life accomplish what city medicine could not. The transformation is both physical (color returns to her cheeks, strength to her limbs) and psychological (her quiet passivity gives way to active engagement with the farm's life). The novel's medical argument is not subtle: nature is the physician the city cannot provide.

## The Daggetts' Scarlet Fever

The Daggett children's scarlet fever, mentioned early in the novel, serves as a synecdoche for urban contagion. Unlike the Durhams, the Daggetts cannot leave — and their children's health suffers accordingly. The episode also introduces the novel's concern with moral contagion: Melissa Daggett's "cheap, vile journal" represents a spiritual infection as dangerous as the physical one.

## Key Passages

- **"She always said she was better, but never got well"** — Mousie's city condition, the novel's opening diagnostic
- **Mousie's recovery through country life** — the novel's medical thesis enacted
- **The Daggetts' scarlet fever** — urban illness as a structural condition
