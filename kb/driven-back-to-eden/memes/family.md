# Family

## Definition

In *Driven Back to Eden*, the Durham family is not merely the cast of characters but the novel's organizational and moral center. Every decision — from the initial proposal to leave New York to the daily allocation of farm tasks — is evaluated by its effect on "the little group for whom I was responsible" (001-024). The family operates as both an economic unit (shared labor produces shared sustenance) and a moral community (character is formed through mutual obligation).

## Expression in the Text

The narrator, Robert Durham, explicitly frames himself as "commander in chief of the little band" (001-024), a paternalistic metaphor that runs throughout the novel. The family's city life is marked by fragmentation: children scatter into dangerous streets, the wife grows thin with overwork, and the narrator is absent at his office all day. Country life promises reintegration — "my work, instead of being away from them all day, could be with them" (002-021). This reunification is realized in passages like the family's arrival at the frozen farmhouse, where "never did the word mean more to a half-frozen and badly frightened family" (013-013), and in the settled routine of April, when the narrator reflects that "if all were well in the citadel of home, the chances of the outdoor campaign were greatly improved" (024-009).

## Narrative Function

The family serves as the novel's moral compass and plot engine. The narrator's detailed accounting of his children's needs — Mousie's pallor, Bobsey's wildness, Merton's "Nimrod instincts" — provides the justification for the radical move to the country. The family's collective labor (each child contributing according to ability) models an ideal of cooperative self-sufficiency. The family also structures the novel's emotional arc: the anxieties of city life give way to the satisfactions of shared rural work, culminating in the triumphant declaration "We Can Make a Living in Eden" (044-001).

## Key Passages

- "I sat at the head of the table, commander in chief of the little band" (001-024) — the narrator's statement of paternal authority
- "I propose that we go to the country and get our living out of the soil" (002-019) — the family's founding decision
- "It wouldn't be so if beyond the door lay a garden and fields and woods" (002-021) — the vision of family integration through rural life
- "Never did the word mean more to a half-frozen and badly frightened family" (013-013) — the family's first night in the farmhouse
- "If all were well in the citadel of home, the chances of the outdoor campaign were greatly improved" (024-009) — the domestic sphere as strategic base
