# Rural Economy

*Driven Back to Eden* is unusual among pastoral novels for the seriousness with which it treats economics. Robert Durham is not a wealthy man fleeing the city on a whim; he is a clerk with "very limited means" who must make the farm pay. The novel's attention to prices, markets, crops, and labor is not incidental but central to its argument: country life is not merely more virtuous than city life but potentially more *profitable*, if approached with the same systematic intelligence one would bring to any business.

## The Economic Model

The Durhams' economic model is diversification. Rather than relying on a single cash crop — the vulnerability of many real-world farms — they produce a portfolio: garden vegetables for the table and market, fruit from the orchard, maple sugar from the sugaring operation, eggs and poultry, preserved berries, and the products of the barn (milk, butter, possibly meat). This diversification spreads risk and provides year-round income. The model is explicitly family-based: the children's labor is not exploitation but education — they learn the economic logic of production while contributing to the family enterprise.

## Washington Market as Economic Anchor

The narrator's systematic study of Washington Market — "I haunted Washington Market in the gray dawn and learned from much inquiry what products found a ready and certain sale" — establishes the economic rationality of the move. The farm is not a retreat from commerce but a reorientation of it: instead of earning a salary and purchasing food, the family produces food and sells the surplus. The market becomes the bridge between country production and urban consumption.

## Key Passages

- **Washington Market research** — the narrator's systematic economic investigation before the move
- **The diversified farm economy** — vegetables, fruit, maple sugar, poultry, preserves
- **The children's economic education** — labor as both contribution and learning
