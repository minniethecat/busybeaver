# Subsistence

## Definition

Subsistence in *Driven Back to Eden* denotes the practical economic basis of family life: growing, gathering, and selling enough to sustain the household. It encompasses both the family's internal economy (producing food for their own table) and their market participation (selling raspberries, potatoes, and chickens through the commission merchant Mr. Bogart). The concept is defined against the narrator's former life as a salaried bookkeeper — a "consumer" rather than a "producer" (037-037).

## Expression in the Text

The narrator approaches subsistence as a subject for systematic study. During the winter before the move, he "haunted Washington Market in the gray dawn" and "learned from much inquiry what products found a ready and certain sale" (005-005). He rejects get-rich-quick fantasies in favor of "common sense" and the "humble circumstances of the vast majority living from the soil" (005-005). Once on the farm, subsistence takes concrete form: the family sells raspberries, corn, tomatoes, potatoes, and chickens. The narrator explains the logic of commerce to his children on the Hudson River steamer: "commerce is made by people supplying themselves and each other with what they need" (010-007). By late summer, the family's subsistence economy is functioning well enough that the narrator can distinguish between consumption and investment: if cashing a crop "would not go further toward our support than the things themselves," the family must sell rather than eat (037-037).

## Narrative Function

Subsistence provides the novel's economic structure. Each chapter after the move is organized around a seasonal economic task — pruning, planting, berry-picking, harvesting — and each task is evaluated by its contribution to the family's bottom line. The concept also provides the novel's implicit social critique: the dignity of the "producer" is contrasted with the precarious dependency of the salaried city worker. The monthly checks from Mr. Bogart become a narrative device for measuring progress, and the ability to pay off debts and "hoard for winter" (036-005) marks the novel's economic climax before the final affirmation.

## Key Passages

- "I haunted Washington Market in the gray dawn and learned from much inquiry" (005-005) — the narrator's systematic approach to subsistence economics
- "Commerce is made by people supplying themselves and each other with what they need" (010-007) — the narrator's lesson in basic economics
- "We shall become what are called the 'producers,' and but for this class the world would soon come to an end" (010-007) — the moral elevation of productive labor
- "Bobsey's tendencies were those of a consumer, and not of a producer or saver" (037-037) — economic role as character index
- "We Can Make a Living in Eden" (044-001) — the chapter title that declares subsistence achieved
