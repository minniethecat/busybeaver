# Boyhood

*Driven Back to Eden* is, at its core, a book about the problem of raising boys. Robert Durham's initial "problem" — stated in the novel's first chapter — is existential before it is economic: "What would I do if I were in Merton's place?" The answer the novel provides is that boyhood requires space, meaningful work, and moral purpose — none of which the city can supply.

## The City Boy

Merton, at fifteen, embodies the city boy's predicament. After school, he smokes cigarettes "made from the sweepings of the manufacturer's floor" and answers his father's reproach with the novel's most devastating question: "I'd like to know what there is for a boy to do in this street." The street offers only mischief; the flat offers only confinement. Bobsey, at seven, is already showing the pattern — "squaring off" at bigger boys, treating the living room "like a cage to a wild bird" (001-027), his restless energy finding no healthy outlet.

## The Country Transformation

The move to the country transforms boyhood by giving it purpose. Merton, once idle and sullen, becomes an active participant in the family's agricultural enterprise — planting, harvesting, caring for animals. Bobsey's dangerous street energy is channeled into exploration, berry-picking, and supervised outdoor play. The novel's thesis is explicit: boys need "something to do that would not harm them, for do *something* they certainly would" — and the country provides that something in the form of meaningful work integrated into family life.

## Key Passages

- **"I'd like to know what there is for a boy to do in this street"** — Merton's indictment of urban boyhood
- **"Our living room was to him like a cage to a wild bird"** (001-027) — Bobsey's city confinement
- **The country work that fills Merton's after-school hours** — the novel's answer to the boyhood problem
