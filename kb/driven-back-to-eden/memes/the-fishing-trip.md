## Overview

After weeks of relentless farm labor, Robert Durham arranges a day of fishing and sailing on the Hudson River for the family. The outing, narrated in Chapter 29 ("We Go A-Fishing"), is the family's first purely recreational experience in the country — a deliberate departure from work that reveals the pleasures of rural life.

## Narrative Account

The family drives to the village to purchase fishing tackle, then proceeds to the mouth of the Moodna Creek, where a weathered boathouse stands (029-004). They hire a boat and first explore up the creek with the tide, then float down with the ebb. "This, to the children, was like a voyage of discovery," the narrator notes, as each "little bay, point, and gulf" receives an improvised name (029-004).

The fishing yields modest results — enough for pride but not a feast. Bobsey nearly falls into a deep pool in his eagerness to handle a minnow, and the spot is christened "Bobsey's Luck." The day ends with the family sailing on the broad Hudson, surrounded by the grandeur of the Highlands. Robert reflects that such a day would have been impossible in their city life, where recreation always cost money and yielded less satisfaction.

## Significance

The fishing trip marks the family's emotional arrival in the country. Until this point, country life has meant only work — clearing, planting, building. The outing demonstrates that the rural world offers restorative pleasures as well as demanding labor. It also deepens the family's bond: the shared adventure creates memories distinct from the shared toil. Robert's recognition that "a day's fishing and sailing on the river" (028-015) costs little but enriches greatly reinforces the novel's economic argument for country living — that genuine recreation need not be purchased.
