# Health and Nature

## Definition

In *Driven Back to Eden*, health is inseparable from nature. The novel presents a straightforward medical theory: city life — with its confined spaces, "poisoned" air, and nervous overstrain — sickens people, while country life — with its "pure air," physical labor, and natural rhythms — restores them. This is not presented as metaphor but as a practical observation confirmed by the visible improvement of each family member over the course of the year.

## Expression in the Text

The narrator's initial diagnosis is explicit: his wife is "growing thin" with a "sharpness" in her voice that is new; Mousie is "growing paler and thinner every day" (002-019); and Bobsey's wildness is a symptom of confinement — "Our living room was to him like a cage to a wild bird" (001-024). The proposed cure is environmental: "outdoor life and pure air, instead of that breathed over and over, would bring quiet to your nerves and the roses back to your cheeks" (002-021). The novel tracks the fulfillment of this promise: Mousie's anticipation alone — "the hope of flowers and a garden had fed her life like a deep, hidden spring" (010-011) — begins her transformation. By summer, "the children were as brown as little Indians, and we daily thanked God for health" (036-005). The narrator's declaration that Mousie "belongs to nature" and "would always be an exile in the city" (026-005) makes health a matter of environmental fit rather than individual constitution.

## Narrative Function

Health provides the novel's most emotionally immediate argument for country life. While economic self-reliance is abstract, Mousie's pallor and the wife's nervous exhaustion are visceral. The health narrative also individualizes each family member's arc: Mousie's transformation is the most dramatic, Bobsey's wild energy is redirected rather than suppressed, and the narrator himself finds that even a failed hunting expedition "would bring back increased health and strength if nothing else" (008-002). Health functions as the novel's proof that the country experiment is working, even before economic results arrive.

## Key Passages

- "Mousie is growing paler and thinner every day" (002-019) — the medical crisis that triggers the move
- "Outdoor life and pure air, instead of that breathed over and over, would bring quiet to your nerves and the roses back to your cheeks" (002-021) — the therapeutic promise of country life
- "He would bring back increased health and strength if nothing else" (008-002) — health as the baseline benefit of outdoor activity
- "The hope of flowers and a garden had fed her life like a deep, hidden spring" (010-011) — psychological health through anticipatory hope
- "The children were as brown as little Indians, and we daily thanked God for health" (036-005) — the fulfilled promise of restored health
