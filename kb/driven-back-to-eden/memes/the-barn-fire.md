## Overview

In mid-summer, during a violent thunderstorm, lightning strikes the Durhams' newly built barn, setting it ablaze. The fire destroys the structure and its contents — a devastating material loss for a family of limited means. The event, titled "A Thunderbolt" in Chapter 36, tests the family's faith, resilience, and their commitment to country life.

## Narrative Account

The storm arrives suddenly. Robert describes "the thunder and lightning" as "simply terrific" (036-011), and the family huddles indoors as the tempest rages. A bolt strikes the barn, igniting the dry timber. The family watches helplessly as months of labor and their best agricultural structure go up in flames. The loss includes not just the building but stored hay, tools, and equipment essential for the coming harvest.

In the aftermath, the family faces a choice: abandon their country experiment or rebuild. Robert's determination, bolstered by his wife's steady faith and Mr. Jones's practical assistance, prevails. The narrator reflects that "the shock of the thunderbolt" could have "disabled us beyond a moment's rallying" (037-002), yet the family endures. Neighbors arrive to help clear the wreckage and offer materials for rebuilding.

## Significance

The barn fire is the novel's central crisis — the moment when the Durhams' idyllic vision of country life collides with its harsh realities. It serves as a crucible: the family's decision to rebuild rather than retreat to the city proves their transformation is genuine, not merely a fair-weather experiment. The event also validates the novel's argument about neighborliness, as the rural community rallies around the family in their hour of need, offering exactly the kind of practical support that was absent in their city life.
