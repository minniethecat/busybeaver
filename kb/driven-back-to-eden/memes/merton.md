# Merton

Merton is the eldest son of [[robert-durham]] and [[mrs-durham]], and the older brother of [[mousie]], [[winnie]], and [[bobsey]]. At "little over fifteen" (001-019), he embodies the novel's central argument: that city life starves a boy's natural impulses while [[country-life]] channels them into healthy growth. The narrator's decision to move the family to [[the-farm-eden]] is in part driven by concern for Merton's future — the "influence of the street" (002-003) threatens to pull him into trouble, while the farm offers "sturdiness to his limbs and the right kind of color into his face" (004-002).

## Character Overview

Merton enters the novel in Chapter I as a source of parental anxiety. In the city flat, he is restless, sometimes sullen, and susceptible to the "influence of the street" that his father fears "involved more danger than I could provide against" (002-003). His schoolbooks arrive, but his heart is elsewhere — he "set his heart on a book that gave an account of game, fish, birds, and mammals" (004-002), revealing the outdoor yearnings that city life cannot satisfy. The move to the country transforms him: the same impulses that made him troublesome become productive, and he emerges as his father's most reliable farmhand, a capable hunter, and a young man learning to balance sport with responsibility.

## Role in the Story

In the novel's opening chapters, Merton is introduced as a boy on the edge of trouble. His father finds him "in mischief" (001-018) with a paper that contains "an account of a burglary" (001-018), and the street outside their flat represents an ominous pull. Yet Merton is also warmhearted — "the idea of your being cross, mamma, seems all wrong!" (001-032) — suggesting that his difficulties stem from environment rather than character.

The decision to move to the country is explicitly framed around Merton's welfare. In Chapter II, the narrator tells his wife: "I wish to forget every sad or painful thing that has happened in this flat, so far as I am your teacher, Merton" (002-013), linking the move to a fresh start for the boy.

Once the decision is made, Merton's transformation begins even before the family leaves the city. "The Nimrod instincts of the race were awakened in Merton" (004-002) when he discovers books on hunting and fishing at a Christmas bookstall. His father promises him a breech-loading shotgun — but with an important condition: "he must earn his fishing tackle during the coming summer" (004-003), establishing the link between sport and economic responsibility that defines his country education.

On the farm, Merton matures rapidly. He works alongside his father pruning trees and clearing brush, patrols the property against predators with his gun, and takes charge of the raspberry patch. His friendship with [[junior-jones]] provides the peer companionship city life lacked, and the two boys hunt rabbits together — Merton's first kill becoming a moment of "exultation" (014-048) that his father frames carefully as earned achievement rather than mere sport.

Merton's arc reaches its fullest expression in the balance he learns: "they could take their guns and be absent two or three hours if they would promise to help faithfully the rest of the day" (015-004). This negotiation — sport earned through work — is the novel's model of boyhood properly channeled. By the final chapters, Merton has become a competent young farmer who can be trusted with significant responsibilities, from managing the maple sugaring to preparing the soil for spring planting.

## Key Passages

- **First appearance and the burglary paper** (001-017–019): Merton is found "in mischief" reading a newspaper account of a burglary, establishing the city's negative pull on an impressionable boy.
- **"The Nimrod instincts of the race"** (004-002): Merton discovers hunting and fishing books at Christmas, and his natural outdoor impulses are awakened — the novel's key statement about innate boyhood energy finding its proper channel.
  > "The Nimrod instincts of the race were awakened in Merton, and I soon found that he had set his heart on a book that gave an account of game, fish, birds, and mammals." (004-002)
- **The shotgun promise** (004-003): His father promises him a breech-loading shotgun — "you shall have the book and a breech-loading shotgun also" — with the stipulation that he must earn his fishing tackle, linking sport to economic responsibility.
- **Arrival at the farm** (012-008): Merton shows "much manly spirit" helping to carry the family's baggage into their new home, his first display of the responsibility country life demands.
- **Friendship with Junior Jones** (014-012–048): Merton's rabbit hunt with Junior becomes a rite of passage — "Merton was a picture of exultation" (014-048) — and the beginning of his most important peer friendship.
- **The rabbit-pie and work ethic** (015-012): The rabbit Merton kills becomes "a rabbit-pie" for the family table, and he learns that hunting provides food, not just sport — a key lesson in the novel's subsistence ethic.
- **Learning the balance** (015-004): "They could take their guns and be absent two or three hours if they would promise to help faithfully the rest of the day" — the negotiation that defines Merton's growth from restless boy to responsible young man.
- **Vigilance against predators** (025-002–006): Merton patrols the farm at dawn with his gun, protecting the crops from marauding birds and animals — his hunting skills now serving the family's livelihood.
- **Planting season** (027-021–023): "Merton and I can take them up in the morning" — Merton takes on the planting of potatoes, working alongside his father as an equal partner in the farm's spring work.

## Relationships

- [[robert-durham]] — father and moral guide, who frames Merton's country education around earned reward
- [[mrs-durham]] — mother, whose "solicitude" (026-008) balances the father's expectations
- [[mousie]] — sister; Merton is warmhearted toward her frailty
- [[winnie]] — sister and frequent work companion on the farm
- [[bobsey]] — youngest brother, whose peril in the butternut tree (Ch. XVIII) Merton helps avert
- [[junior-jones]] — country friend and peer, his most important relationship outside the family
- [[mrs-jones]] — neighbor and provider of the rabbit-pie that rewards Merton's first hunt
