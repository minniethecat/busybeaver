## Overview

The orchard is one of the most valuable assets on the Durham farm, inherited from the previous owner, old Mr. Jamison. Spanning part of the farm's acreage alongside the plowable land and woodlot, the orchard contains apple and fruit trees that John Jones estimates took "the best part of a century to build up" (007-016). It becomes a major source of income for the Durhams in their first year of farming.

## Description in the Text

Robert Durham's first impression of the orchard comes during John Jones's tour of the property, when his "gaze lingered longest on the orchard and fruit trees that surrounded the dwelling" (007-015). Jones emphasizes its value immediately, noting that the farm includes "twenty acres of plowable land and orchard, and a five-acre woodlot" (007-013). The trees are mature and established — not something a new owner could quickly replicate.

By the time the Durhams take possession, however, the orchard shows signs of neglect from Jamison's final years. Robert observes "unpruned growth" and ground "strewn with fallen fruit" (019-012), reflecting the transition period between owners. Jones "shook his head over the orchard" (027-006) — a gesture of concern about the work required to restore it. Yet the orchard remains productive: an old chicken coop in the orchard (016-010) is repurposed, and by late summer Robert records "the beginning of our chief labor in the orchard" (042-043), selling barrels of apples as a commercial crop.

## Narrative Significance

The orchard embodies the theme of inherited agricultural wealth — the idea that the best farming assets are those built by previous generations. Unlike the vegetable garden, which the Durhams can establish in a single season, the orchard represents long-term capital that requires stewardship rather than creation. Its neglected state on arrival parallels the family's own transition: both orchard and family need care and attention to return to full productivity.

The orchard also marks the farm's seasonal rhythm, appearing from the spring inspection through the autumn harvest, and serves as a minor setting for domestic scenes — the old coop becomes a play area for the children (037-029).

## Key Scenes

- **First sight**: Robert's gaze "lingered longest on the orchard and fruit trees" during his initial tour of the property (007-015).
- **Jones's assessment**: John Jones notes it took "the best part of a century to build up an orchard like that," emphasizing its irreplaceable value (007-016).
- **Spring neglect**: Robert finds the orchard ground "strewn with fallen fruit" and branches overgrown from a year without pruning (019-012).
- **The old coop**: An abandoned chicken coop in the orchard becomes a point of interest during exploration (016-010).
- **Harvest season**: Robert sells "a number of barrels" of apples, beginning the orchard's return to commercial productivity (042-043).
