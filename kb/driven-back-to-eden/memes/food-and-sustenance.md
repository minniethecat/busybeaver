# Food and Sustenance

Food in *Driven Back to Eden* is never merely fuel. It is an index of the family's moral and economic condition. The city chapters present food under the sign of scarcity and strain: Mrs. Durham, "hot and flushed from too near acquaintance with the stove" (001-028), pushes away "her almost untasted supper" with the exhausted cry "Broiling the steak was enough for me." The country chapters, by contrast, present food under the sign of abundance, pleasure, and communal participation — the garden's produce, the orchard's fruit, the sugaring operation's maple syrup, the Thanksgiving feast.

## From Deprivation to Abundance

The novel's food arc traces the family's transformation. In the city, meals are prepared in a cramped kitchen by an exhausted mother; food is consumed in haste amid tension; appetite is diminished by the very conditions of its satisfaction. In the country, food production becomes a family enterprise: the children plant, tend, harvest, and preserve. The garden yields vegetables; the orchard yields fruit; the sugaring operation yields maple sugar; the berry patches yield preserves. Food is no longer merely purchased and consumed but *produced* — and the act of production transforms the family's relationship to what they eat.

## The Communal Table

The country table is a site of family gathering, gratitude, and shared labor's reward. Unlike the city supper — fragmented by exhaustion and tension — the country meal integrates the day's work into a ritual of renewal. The narrator's emphasis on specific foods (the first garden vegetables, the Thanksgiving turkey, the maple sugar) underscores the novel's argument that authentic sustenance requires authentic production — that food tastes different when you have grown it yourself.

## Key Passages

- **"Broiling the steak was enough for me"** (001-031) — Mrs. Durham's city exhaustion, measured in uneaten food
- **The first garden harvest meal** — abundance as the reward of labor
- **The Thanksgiving feast** — food as communal celebration and religious gratitude
