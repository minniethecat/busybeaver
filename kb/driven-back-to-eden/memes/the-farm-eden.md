# The Farm (Eden)

## Location Overview

The Durham farm — the old Jamison place — is a run-down property on a southeast-facing slope near Maizeville Landing on the Hudson River. It comprises a cottage with a wide fireplace, an overgrown orchard, a neglected garden, raspberry patches, meadows, and a wood lot. John Jones, the neighbor who sells it, describes its advantages with characteristic bluntness: "the house is on a slope facing the southeast. You get the morning sun and the southern breeze" (007-018). The farm is the novel's physical and symbolic center — the "Eden" that gives the book its title.

## Role in the Story

The farm is introduced in chapter 7 when the narrator first visits, finding "the cottage, and saw the wide, old-fashioned fireplace, with its crackling blaze" (007-011). The family arrives in March to a frozen landscape (chapter 12), and the farm's transformation over the seasons parallels the family's own recovery. Each area of the farm becomes associated with specific family members: the garden with Mousie's recovery (010-011), the orchard with the narrator's pruning work (020-002), the raspberry patches with the family's first cash crop (chapter 32). By the novel's end, the farm has become a productive property that "can make a living" (044-001), fulfilling its Edenic promise.

## Key Passages

- "The house is on a slope facing the southeast. You get the morning sun and the southern breeze" (007-018) — John Jones on the farm's natural advantages
- "I entered the main room of the cottage, and saw the wide, old-fashioned fireplace, with its crackling blaze" (007-011) — first sight of the farmhouse
- "We Can Make a Living in Eden" (044-001) — the novel's concluding affirmation
- "Never did the word mean more to a half-frozen and badly frightened family" (013-013) — the farmhouse as refuge
