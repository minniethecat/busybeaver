# Moral Character

## Definition

In *Driven Back to Eden*, moral character is not innate but cultivated through environment and habit. The novel presents a clear environmental determinism: city conditions — crowding, anonymity, wage dependence — erode character, while country conditions — fresh air, honest labor, family integration — build it. The narrator's goal is not merely economic but moral: to raise children who are not merely fed but formed, who become "producers" rather than "consumers" (037-037) in both economic and moral senses.

## Expression in the Text

The novel treats character as observable through behavior. The narrator distinguishes between "plain, honest, sensible producers" and those who give "careless, random, or ignorant answers" at Washington Market (005-005). John Jones's rough honesty — "You're amazin' refreshin' for a city chap" (007-011) — is presented as the natural product of rural life. The children's moral development is tracked through their relationship to work: Bobsey's immaturity is marked by "the tendencies of a consumer, and not of a producer or saver" (037-037), while Merton's growing responsibility is measured by his willingness to "promise to help faithfully" (015-004). The narrator's mother's teaching — "it was God's plan to give us a chance, and help us make the most of it" (043-005) — frames character as the proper response to providential opportunity.

## Narrative Function

Moral character provides the novel's ethical argument for country life, complementing the economic (self-reliance) and physical (health) arguments. It allows the novel to address the reader directly through the narrator's reflections on what kind of people the Durhams are becoming. Character also provides the novel's standard for judging other people — marketmen, neighbors, even the unseen previous owner of the farm — creating a moral taxonomy that maps onto the urban/rural divide.

## Key Passages

- "Plain, honest, sensible producers in the country" (005-005) — moral taxonomy of economic actors
- "Honesty" as the narrator's compensating virtue for not being "remarkably quick" (002-021) — character as economic strategy
- "Bobsey's tendencies were those of a consumer, and not of a producer or saver" (037-037) — consumption as moral category
- "It was God's plan to give us a chance, and help us make the most of it" (043-005) — character as response to providence
- "Promise to help faithfully the rest of the day" (015-004) — faithfulness as practiced virtue
