# Faith and Providence

## Definition

In *Driven Back to Eden*, faith is the quiet but persistent conviction that the Durham family's fortunes are guided by "a kindly and divine hand." It is not doctrinal or theological — there are no ministers, sermons, or church scenes of any significance. Instead, faith is domestic and practical: the family kneels around the hearth and "made it an altar to God" (013-032), gives thanks for health daily (036-005), and draws courage from the belief that "we are in God's hands, and He will take care of us" (037-003).

## Expression in the Text

The novel's religious sensibility is established early through the children. Bobsey's innocent theology — "God put the first people in a garden and they was very good" (003-005) — provides a child's-eye version of the Eden metaphor that titles the book. The narrator's gratitude is frequently directed heavenward: "I thanked God for the look on her face" (008-017). In moments of crisis, faith is explicitly invoked as reassurance: during a violent thunderstorm, the narrator tells his terrified family, "We are in God's hands, and He will take care of us" (037-003). The novel's religious climax comes at Thanksgiving, when the narrator recalls his mother's teaching "that it was God's plan to give us a chance, and help us make the most of it" (043-005). The final chapter closes with a New Year's resolution: "Let us make the best and most of it, and ask God to help us" (044-012).

## Narrative Function

Faith functions as the novel's ultimate explanatory framework. While the narrator makes practical arguments for country life — economics, health, child development — faith provides the metaphysical warrant: the family is not merely making a shrewd move but following a providential path. This framing allows the novel to take risks seriously (the storm, the financial precarity) without descending into despair, because the outcome is never solely in human hands. Faith also ties the novel to its 19th-century domestic-literature context, where religious sentiment was an expected element of moral fiction.

## Key Passages

- "God put the first people in a garden and they was very good" (003-005) — Bobsey's child theology, linking the Eden metaphor to Genesis
- "We knelt around our hearth, and made it an altar to God" (013-032) — domestic religion on the first night in the farmhouse
- "We are in God's hands, and He will take care of us" (037-003) — faith in crisis during the thunderstorm
- "The children were as brown as little Indians, and we daily thanked God for health" (036-005) — gratitude as daily religious practice
- "Let us make the best and most of it, and ask God to help us" (044-012) — the novel's closing religious resolution
