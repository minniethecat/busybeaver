# The Hudson River

## Location Overview

The Hudson River is the waterway along which the Durham family travels from New York City to Maizeville Landing. In the novel, it serves as both a geographical feature and a narrative device — the journey upriver marks the family's transition from their old life to their new one. The steamboat voyage in chapters 10–11 provides a suspended, transitional space: neither city nor country, but the passage between them.

## Role in the Story

The Hudson River steamboat journey occupies chapters 10 and 11. "A Voyage Up the Hudson" (chapter 11) depicts the family's movement through a landscape that grows progressively more rural — a visual education in the world they are entering. The narrator uses the voyage as a teaching opportunity, explaining commerce to his children as they watch produce being loaded: "See, youngsters, all this means commerce" (010-007). The river also serves as the family's economic lifeline thereafter, carrying their produce downstream to Mr. Bogart's market.
