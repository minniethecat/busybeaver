## Overview

The brook is a smaller tributary watercourse on the Durham farm, described as a "mountain brook" (003-011) that feeds into the larger creek. Its most notable feature is a stand of sugar maple trees along its bank, which Robert Durham identifies as a potential source of maple syrup — one of the many small-scale agricultural opportunities the farm offers.

## Description in the Text

Before the Durhams ever see their farm, the brook exists in Robert's imagination as part of the idealized rural landscape — "a mountain brook that foams into a rocky basin" (003-011). This image, painted by a friend describing country life, is part of what draws the family toward their move. Once on the property, the brook becomes a tangible reality: Robert recognizes the sugar maples "from a previous visit" and notes they stand "down by the brook" (017-023).

During the spring thaw, the brook joins the creek in making what Robert calls "genuine March music" — "the roar of the creek and the gurgle of the brook" (018-010). It is a smaller, more intimate water feature than the creek: Robert describes "what was now a good-sized brook" (018-009), suggesting it swells with the seasons. The children draw water from it — "you filled the pails from the brook!" (018-022).

## Narrative Significance

The brook represents the small-scale, manageable bounty of the farm — unlike the dramatic creek with its swimming holes and near-drownings, the brook is gentle and useful. The sugar maples beside it promise a domestic industry (maple sugaring) that never quite materializes in the narrative but symbolizes the farm's latent potential. The brook's "gurgle," paired with the creek's "roar," forms part of the sensory soundscape that distinguishes country life from the city's noise.
