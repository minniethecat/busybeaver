# The Schoolhouse

The country schoolhouse serves as the novel's institutional counterpoint to the city's educational deficiencies. Where the city offered Merton only "the street" after school hours, the country schoolhouse is embedded in a community that extends learning beyond the classroom into the fields, woods, and farmyard.

## Role in the Narrative

The schoolhouse appears primarily in the novel's middle chapters, after the Durhams have settled into the farm. The children's enrollment in the district school marks their integration into the rural community — a transition from city outsiders to country participants. The school becomes a site of social connection as well as formal learning: the children form friendships, encounter the country children who serve as foils and models, and participate in the communal rituals (spelling bees, recitations, winter exhibitions) that distinguish rural education from its urban counterpart.

## Key Passages

- **The children's first day at the country school** — integration into the rural community
- **The winter exhibition** — the schoolhouse as a site of community gathering and shared culture
- **The walk to and from school** — the daily journey that, unlike the city street, is itself educational
