# The First Harvest

## Event Overview

The first raspberry harvest occupies chapters 32 and 36–37. After months of preparation — pruning, weeding, cultivating — the Durham family finally has a product to sell. The berries are picked, crated, and shipped down the Hudson to Mr. Bogart, whose checks "came regularly, the fruit bringing a fair price" (036-005). The harvest transforms the family's economic position: the narrator can pay off debts and begin "hoarding for winter" (036-005). The harvest also tests the children's character, as they must learn to sell what they would rather eat (037-037).

## Narrative Significance

The first harvest is the novel's economic climax. It provides proof that the country experiment is not merely healthy and morally uplifting but financially viable. The narrator's satisfaction is palpable: "The results of our labor were cheering" (036-005). The harvest also individualizes each child's relationship to the family economy — Bobsey's "tendencies" as a "consumer, and not of a producer or saver" (037-037) mark him as the family member least transformed by the country experience.

## Key Passages

- "The results of our labor were cheering" (036-005) — the harvest's emotional payoff
- "Checks from Mr. Bogart came regularly" (036-005) — economic validation
- "Bobsey's tendencies were those of a consumer, and not of a producer or saver" (037-037) — the harvest as character test
- "Then we began to hoard for winter" (036-005) — the transition from production to preservation
