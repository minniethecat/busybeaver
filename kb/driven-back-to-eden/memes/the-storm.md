# The Storm

On a hot July evening, with the barn full of hay and the family's first harvest secure, a thunderstorm sweeps down the Hudson Valley and lightning strikes the Durham barn. The fire that follows consumes the building and everything in it — a season's labor reduced to ash in a single night. The storm is the novel's dramatic fulcrum: the moment when the family's country enterprise faces its severest test, and the moment when their new community proves its worth.

## Narrative Account

The day had been oppressively hot, and the narrator, "like the captain of a vessel before a storm," had worked to make everything "snug and safe" (036-009). The hay was in the barn — "the barn was now so well filled that I began to calculate how many more tons I would try to store away" (036-008) — and the family retired feeling secure in their prosperity.

The lightning struck without warning. "A stream of fire was bursting from the roof of our barn" (036-014). The narrator's first instinct was to save the animals: "Lead the horse out of the barn basement, Merton" (037-005). But the hay — dry July hay packed to the rafters — was beyond saving. "I opened the barn doors and saw that the hay was already ablaze" (037-007).

The neighbors arrived within minutes. Mr. Jones and other men from Maizeville formed a bucket brigade, but their efforts could only contain the fire, not extinguish it. "The hay and the things in the barn at the time will prove a total loss" (037-027). By morning, "the absence of the barn made a great gap in our family circle" (037-029) — the narrator's language, anthropomorphizing the lost building as a family member, measures the depth of the blow.

Yet the morning also brought the first intimations of recovery. The narrator observed that "the garden has been helped rather than injured by the storm" (037-031), and the neighbors pledged help for rebuilding. Mr. Jones declared: "By that time you'll have a new barn up, I s'pose; and after you have it up, I'll help you fill it" (037-035). The narrator made his resolve explicit: "I inwardly resolved that the burning of the barn should add to our success in the end" (037-043).

## Significance

The storm serves the novel's providential theology. It is a test — of the family's faith in their country enterprise, of their neighbors' goodwill, and of the narrator's ability to lead his family through crisis without despair. The loss is real and total: a season's hay, the building itself, and the sense of security the barn represented. But the response transforms the loss into a demonstration of community.

The neighbors' immediate assistance — fighting the fire through the night, pledging labor for the rebuilding — validates the narrator's faith in country people. Mr. Jones's practical optimism ("I'll help you fill it") contrasts with the tenement isolation the family left behind. The storm, in destroying the barn, reveals the social capital the family has accumulated through months of neighborly relations.

The narrator's resolution — "that the burning of the barn should add to our success in the end" (037-043) — is the novel's thesis statement on adversity. The new barn, when it rises in the autumn, is better designed than the old: connected to a sty and poultry house, reflecting lessons learned. The storm does not end the country experiment; it deepens it.

## Key Moments

- **The gathering tension** (036-008–009): The narrator works "like the captain of a vessel before a storm" to secure the hay — dramatic irony for the reader, who senses what is coming.
- **The lightning strike** (036-014): "A stream of fire was bursting from the roof of our barn" — the novel's most vivid single image, rendered in one devastating sentence.
- **Saving the livestock** (037-005): "Lead the horse out of the barn basement, Merton" — the narrator's first clear command in the crisis, prioritizing life over property.
- **The neighbors arrive** (037-014): Mr. Jones and others form a bucket brigade — "you've made all our barns safer, and we owe you a good turn" (037-014) — community solidarity in action.
- **The morning after** (037-029): "The absence of the barn made a great gap in our family circle" — loss measured in the language of family.
- **The resolution** (037-043): "The burning of the barn should add to our success in the end" — the novel's ethic of resilient optimism crystallized.
