## Definition

Christmas in *Driven Back to Eden* marks the turning point in the Durham family's story. The holiday serves as the emotional catalyst for their decision to leave the city, contrasting the poverty of their flat with the dream of rural life. It is both a literal event and a symbol of hope and renewal.

## Expression in the Text

The family's decision to move to the country crystallizes during Christmas week. The narrator recalls that "by the time Christmas week began we all had agreed to do without everything which was not absolutely essential" (004-002), saving money for the country venture. Chapter 5, titled "A Country Christmas in a City Flat," depicts their humble celebration: a simple Christmas morning exchange of small, practical gifts (005-002) and a dinner "consisting almost wholly of turkey" (005-003), their one indulgence.

Later, Christmas reappears as a marker of country prosperity. Mr. Jones speaks of "choppin' and readin' from now to Christmas" (030-006), framing the holiday as the culmination of a productive year. By Chapter 44, the children receive skates as "premature Christmas presents" (044-004), and "Christmas brought us more than its wonted joys" (044-006) — a measure of how far the family has come.

## Narrative Function

Christmas serves a dual narrative purpose. In the opening chapters, it dramatizes the family's urban poverty and fuels their resolve to change. The meager celebration sharpens the contrast between their cramped flat and the imagined abundance of Eden. In the closing chapters, Christmas returns as a symbol of achieved prosperity, closing the arc from deprivation to fulfillment. The holiday bookends the novel's central argument: that country life offers not just subsistence but genuine joy.
