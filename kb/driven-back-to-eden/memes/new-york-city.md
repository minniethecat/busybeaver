# New York City

## Location Overview

New York City in the 1880s, as depicted in the novel, is a place of crowded tenements, dangerous streets, and anonymous neighbors. The Durham family occupies "a long, narrow line of rooms, reaching from a crowded street to a little backyard full of noisy children by day, and noisier cats by night" (001-024). The city is characterized by its "low roar" (002-014), its "poisoned" air, and the constant threat of moral contamination from the Daggetts above, the Ricketts below, and the street outside.

## Role in the Story

New York City appears primarily in the novel's first five chapters, establishing the conditions that make the move to the country necessary. It is never revisited after the family boards the Hudson River steamer in chapter 10, but it remains the permanent negative reference point — every country pleasure is implicitly measured against its city absence. The narrator's specific locations include his place of business (an unspecified office), Washington Market (where he researches agricultural prices (005-005)), and the Broadway agricultural bookstore (004-002). The city is also the destination for the family's produce shipments to Mr. Bogart, maintaining an economic link even after the physical departure.

## Key Passages

- "A long, narrow line of rooms, reaching from a crowded street to a little backyard" (001-024) — the Durham flat's spatial description
- "The low roar of the city, a roar so deep as to make one feel as if standing on the brink of a maelstrom" (002-014) — sensory assault of urban life
- "I haunted Washington Market in the gray dawn" (005-005) — the narrator's research in the city's commercial heart
- "There is danger for them just outside that door" (002-021) — the moral peril of city streets
