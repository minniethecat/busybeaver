# Home

In *Driven Back to Eden*, "home" is not merely a dwelling but a moral category. The narrator draws an explicit distinction between the city flat — which "was not a home, and was scarcely a fit camping ground for such a family squad as ours" (001-024) — and the [[the-farm-eden]] farmhouse that becomes the center of family life. Home is where work, education, worship, and rest converge; it is the "citadel" (024-009) from which the family's outdoor campaign is launched and to which they return each evening. The novel's central spatial argument is that a true home requires ground beneath it — that the tenement, however well-intentioned its occupants, cannot be made into a home because it severs the family from the soil.

## Definition

The novel distinguishes three levels of dwelling. The **city flat** is an anti-home — "a long, narrow line of rooms, reaching from a crowded street to a little backyard" (001-024) — that the narrator regards with bitter irony: "We called the narrow flat we lived in 'home.' The idea!" (001-024). The **farmhouse**, by contrast, is a genuine home: centered on the "wide, old-fashioned fireplace, with its crackling blaze" (007-011), it provides space for the family to gather, work, and worship together. Beyond even the farmhouse, the novel gestures toward a third level: **Eden itself** as humanity's original and intended home, from which city life represents an exile and to which country life is a return.

## Expression in the Text

The novel's treatment of home follows a clear arc across three decisive moments. In the city, the Durhams occupy suffocating quarters that the narrator refuses to honor with the word "home" — "It was not a home, and was scarcely a fit camping ground" (001-024). The flat's inadequacy is measured in its effects: [[mousie]] growing paler, [[bobsey]] treating it "like a cage," the parents' nerves frayed by proximity without privacy.

> "It was not a home, and was scarcely a fit camping ground for such a family squad as ours. We called the narrow flat we lived in 'home.' The idea!" (001-024)

The farmhouse reverses this. The narrator's first glimpse — "the wide, old-fashioned fireplace, with its crackling blaze" (007-011) — is the novel's turning point from deprivation to abundance. On the first night, "we knelt around our hearth, and made it an altar to God" (013-032), sanctifying the domestic space and fusing the concepts of home, family, and worship.

By spring, the home has become operational: "If all were well in the citadel of home, the chances of the outdoor campaign were greatly improved" (024-009). The military metaphor — citadel, campaign — captures the novel's vision of home as both refuge and base of operations. Home is not a place of retreat from the world but the secure center from which the family engages the world.

## Narrative Function

Home anchors the novel's spatial imagination through a clear transformation:

| Phase | Location | Quality | Key Image |
|-------|----------|---------|-----------|
| **City** (Ch. I–IX) | The tenement flat | Anti-home: confinement, noise, illness | "A long, narrow line of rooms" (001-024) |
| **Arrival** (Ch. XII–XIII) | The farmhouse | Home discovered: warmth, space, sanctuary | "The wide, old-fashioned fireplace" (007-011) |
| **Settlement** (Ch. XIV–XLIV) | The "citadel" | Home operational: base for work, worship, family life | "The citadel of home" (024-009) |

The hearth serves as the novel's symbolic center — the place of family prayer, evening reading, winter planning, and the domestic rituals that bind the family. Its warmth answers the city's cold; its space answers the flat's confinement; its gathered family answers the tenement's isolation.

## Key Passages

- **"It was not a home, and was scarcely a fit camping ground"** (001-024) — the city flat's definitive rejection as domestic space.
- **"I entered the main room of the cottage, and saw the wide, old-fashioned fireplace, with its crackling blaze"** (007-011) — the first glimpse of the real home, rendered with almost sacramental attention.
- **"We knelt around our hearth, and made it an altar to God"** (013-032) — the sanctification of domestic space; home and worship fused in a single act.
- **"Never did the word mean more to a half-frozen and badly frightened family"** (013-013) — home's meaning crystallized in the crisis of the stormy arrival.
- **"If all were well in the citadel of home, the chances of the outdoor campaign were greatly improved"** (024-009) — home as strategic base, the domestic and the agricultural linked in a single military metaphor.
