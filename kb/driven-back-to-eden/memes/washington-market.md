# Washington Market

Washington Market is the economic linchpin of Robert Durham's country enterprise. Before committing to the move, the narrator "haunted Washington Market in the gray dawn and learned from much inquiry what products found a ready and certain sale at some price, and what appeared to yield the largest profit" (Chapter V). The market is where urban demand meets rural supply — and the narrator's systematic study of it transforms what might have been a romantic flight into a calculated economic venture.

## Narrative Function

Washington Market appears at key moments when the novel needs to establish the economic rationality of the Durhams' move. Robert Durham is not a Thoreauvian dropout but a practical businessman who approaches country life as he would any other enterprise: by studying the market. His dawn visits to Washington Market — questioning vendors, noting prices, identifying reliable demand — ground the novel's pastoralism in the hard facts of commerce. The market thus bridges the novel's two worlds: it is physically in the city but economically connected to the country, and the narrator's mastery of its logic is what makes the move viable.

## Key Passages

- **"I haunted Washington Market in the gray dawn"** (Chapter V) — the narrator's economic research
- **Inquiries about "what products found a ready and certain sale"** — the market as the rational basis for country enterprise
- **The contrast between "specimens appearing in our market stalls" and the farm's produce** — the market as a standard of quality
