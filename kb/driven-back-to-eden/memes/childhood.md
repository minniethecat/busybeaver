# Childhood

## Definition

In *Driven Back to Eden*, childhood is both a condition to be protected and a capacity to be developed. The novel measures the quality of life by its effect on children's "body, mind, and soul" (002-021). The narrator's central argument for leaving the city is not economic ambition but pedagogical conviction: city life stunts children, while country life — with its "pure air," "garden and fields and woods," and meaningful work — allows them to "grow sturdy and strong" (002-021).

## Expression in the Text

The novel opens with the narrator's assessment of his children's deteriorating condition in the city flat, which Bobsey treats "like a cage to a wild bird" (001-024). Each child exemplifies a different relationship to growth: Mousie is physically fragile, her "listless, weary look" giving way to animation only with "the hope of flowers and a garden" (010-011); Winnie receives a poultry book and Merton a hunting manual, their reading choices expressing natural talents that city schooling had suppressed (004-002); and Bobsey, the youngest, must learn to channel his wild energy into productive farm work rather than street mischief. In the country, the children receive daily lessons from their mother and Mousie (024-009), combining formal education with practical skill-building. The narrator's observation that Mousie "belongs to nature" and "would always be an exile in the city" (026-005) encapsulates the novel's environmental determinism regarding child development.

## Narrative Function

Childhood serves as the novel's evaluative lens: the success or failure of country life is measured in children's health, happiness, and character. The children's individual arcs — Mousie's recovery, Merton's maturation into a sportsman and worker, Bobsey's gradual domestication — provide the story's emotional texture and its moral argument. The narrator's famous line — "There is no crop in the country so important as that of boys and girls" (001-024) — explicitly frames child-rearing as the family's primary agricultural product.

## Key Passages

- "There is no crop in the country so important as that of boys and girls" (001-024) — the novel's thesis on child-rearing
- "Pure air for them to breathe, and space for them to grow healthfully in body, mind, and soul" (002-021) — the tripartite vision of child development
- "The Nimrod instincts of the race were awakened in Merton" (004-002) — natural talents emerging through country opportunity
- "The hope of flowers and a garden had fed her life like a deep, hidden spring" (010-011) — Mousie's psychological recovery through anticipatory hope
- "That child belongs to nature, and would always be an exile in the city" (026-005) — the novel's environmental philosophy of childhood
