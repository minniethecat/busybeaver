# Bobsey

## Character Overview

Bobsey is the Durham family's youngest child, a boy of about seven whose restless energy provides both comic relief and a recurring domestic challenge. In the city flat, he is essentially uncontainable:

> Our living room was to him like a cage to a wild bird. Slip away he would, to his mother's alarm; for he was almost certain to get into mischief or trouble. (001-024)

His arc across the country year is one of gradual domestication: his wildness, destructive in the confined city, becomes a resource on the farm, where he piles prunings, gathers eggs, and learns the rhythms of productive work. He remains, however, the family's most ungovernable member, and his "tendencies" are explicitly those of "a consumer, and not of a producer or saver" (037-037) — a judgment that marks his incomplete moral formation.

## Role in the Story

Bobsey serves as the novel's barometer of domestic order. When he is uncontrollable, the household is in crisis; when he is absorbed in useful work, the country experiment is succeeding. His most dramatic moment comes early, when he slips away from the flat and must be retrieved from the street — an event that crystallizes the narrator's fear:

> Outdoor life and pure air, in my estimation, constituted the all-sufficient panacea, provided they could be combined with right moral influences. I had felt this even more strongly when I looked down to pale Mousie and little Bobsey. (002-021)

The move to the country is framed as much for Bobsey's safety as for anyone's health.

The first night after deciding on the move sets the pattern. Bobsey is put into his little crib while the parents talk (002-002), but the city's dangers — represented by the street where the narrator anxiously listens for Merton and Bobsey (002-003) — remain immediate. Even the language the narrator uses to describe the children's future life is inflected by Bobsey's needs: "As for Bobsey, he should have a pig which he could call his own and look after" (003-012) — the pig representing productive occupation for restless hands.

On the farm, Bobsey's energy is gradually redirected. The narrator observes that "gathering and piling up my prunings would be as good fun as anything else" (020-002) — a pragmatic understanding that farm work can match a child's appetite for play. Yet Bobsey's moral development is incomplete. At the raspberry harvest, when the melons on which "the children had set their hearts" must be sold rather than eaten, the narrator judges:

> Bobsey's tendencies were those of a consumer, and not of a producer or saver. (037-037)

This is the novel's clearest articulation of what the country life is meant to teach: that the children must learn to be producers, not consumers. Bobsey is the test case — the one for whom this lesson is hardest.

His religious education comes through his mother, and the innocence of his child's theology adds a layer of grace to the Eden metaphor:

> "Mamma says that God put the first people in a garden and they was very good." (003-005)

Thus Bobsey gives a kind of divine sanction to the country project: if God began with a garden, the Durhams' return to the land is not escape but restoration.
