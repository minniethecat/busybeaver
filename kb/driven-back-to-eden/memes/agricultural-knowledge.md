# Agricultural Knowledge

## Definition

In *Driven Back to Eden*, agricultural knowledge is the practical expertise that makes country life possible. It encompasses book learning (agricultural manuals, journals "devoted to rural interests" (004-013)), oral transmission (John Jones's advice on pruning, planting, and soil), and experiential learning (the narrator's trial-and-error education over the course of the year). The novel treats this knowledge as a legitimate and demanding body of expertise, on par with the narrator's former profession of bookkeeping.

## Expression in the Text

The narrator approaches agricultural knowledge with the systematic mindset of a clerk. During the winter before the move, he "read, thought, and studied on the problem of supporting my family in the country" (005-005), haunting Washington Market to gather data from experienced growers. The Christmas expedition to the agricultural bookstore represents the family's collective investment in knowledge: "we felt like a long vista leading toward our unknown home was opening before us, illumined by alluring pictures" (004-002).

Once on the farm, knowledge flows through two channels: the neighbor John Jones, who dispenses practical wisdom on everything from soil drainage to onion cultivation (022-014), and direct experience, as the narrator learns by doing — often painfully. His wry observation that "our modern Eden will cost a great many backaches" (030-026) acknowledges the gap between book knowledge and embodied skill.

## Narrative Function

Agricultural knowledge provides the novel's learning arc. The narrator's growing competence — from the anxious city father who "didn't know much besides bookkeeping" (002-021) to the confident farmer who can anticipate each season's demands and pay off his debts — is the novel's primary character development. The transmission of knowledge also reinforces community bonds: John Jones's willingness to share expertise models rural neighborliness, and the children's absorption of practical skills represents the novel's educational ideal.

## Key Passages

- "All my waking hours not devoted to business, I read, thought, and studied" (005-005) — systematic knowledge acquisition
- "We all had agreed to buy books that would tell us how to live in the country" (004-002) — knowledge as family investment
- "I haunted Washington Market in the gray dawn and learned from much inquiry" (005-005) — empirical research as knowledge method
- "If we have a moist season, like enough they'll do well" (022-014) — John Jones's experiential knowledge
- "Our modern Eden will cost a great many backaches" (030-026) — the gap between knowledge and embodiment
