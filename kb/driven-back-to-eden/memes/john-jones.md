# John Jones

## Character Overview

John Jones is the Durham family's nearest neighbor and their most important country ally. A lifelong resident of the Maizeville area, he is introduced as the seller of the old Jamison farm — a "bluff friend" (chapter 6 title) whose rough manner conceals deep generosity. He is characterized by practical agricultural knowledge, a teasing sense of humor, and an instinctive neighborliness that the narrator, accustomed to the anonymity of city life, finds remarkable. Jones embodies the novel's ideal of rural character: honest, capable, and fundamentally decent.

## Role in the Story

Jones appears at critical junctures throughout the novel, always with practical advice or material help. He first meets the narrator at the steamboat landing, driving "his box sleigh to see the farm he had written of in his laconic letter" (007-002). His local knowledge proves invaluable: he explains which slopes catch the morning sun (007-018), advises on raspberry pruning (020-003), and teaches onion cultivation (022-014). His humor is a running motif — "You're amazin' refreshin' for a city chap. I guess I'll crack on another hundred to the price" (007-011) — but it never obscures his fundamental fairness. He holds the mortgage on the Durham farm, accepting early interest payments without pressure, and his maxim that "as the farmer's land grows rich he grows poor" (019-019) passes into the narrator's store of rural wisdom. Jones also provides a family contrast: his son Junior Jones appears as a peer for Merton.

## Key Passages

- "You're amazin' refreshin' for a city chap. I guess I'll crack on another hundred to the price" (007-011) — characteristic humor on first meeting
- "Don't you see? The house is on a slope facing the southeast. You get the morning sun and the southern breeze" (007-018) — local knowledge freely shared
- "I wouldn't take a section of the garden of Eden if there was malary around" (007-018) — blunt practicality about land quality
- "As the farmer's land grows rich he grows poor" (019-019) — rural wisdom the narrator absorbs
- "If we have a moist season, like enough they'll do well" (022-014) — agricultural advice delivered casually
