# The Garden

The garden on the Durham farm is where country ideals meet dirt. When the family arrives in March, the garden is a neglected patch of weeds and exhausted soil; by summer's end it has become the source of their table, the subject of Mr. Jones's admiration, and the proving ground for the novel's argument that intelligent labor transforms both land and character. More than any other single location in the novel, the garden dramatizes the Durhams' progression from urban ignorance to rural competence.

## Description in the Text

The garden first appears indirectly, as a dream. Mrs. Durham imagines "a garden and fields and woods" (002-021) beyond the tenement door, while Mousie "clapped her hands at the prospect of a flower garden that should be as big as our flat" (003-010). When the family actually arrives, the reality is less romantic: "All through the garden were dry, unsightly weeds, about which were scattered dead potato-vines, blackened by the frost" (019-012). The narrator wastes no time — "In this open space in the garden we will begin our brush pile" (019-013) — and declares the children "gardeners now, and I'm head gardener" (019-015).

The garden's transformation is documented in granular detail: plowing (022-009), garden lines and drills (022-010), seed catalogues studied by the fire (006-003), onion seed "that looked like gunpowder" (022-012), radish and beet seeds planted "only an inch deep" (022-022). By late spring, "the soil was brown and clean" (030-002); by midsummer, the narrator can walk through it at evening "inhaling the fresh wholesomeness of the soil with pleasure" (023-021).

## Narrative Significance

The garden functions as the novel's primary classroom. Every agricultural lesson — plowing depth, seed spacing, manure application, weed control, crop rotation — is taught in its furrows. The narrator's "garden book" (022-021) serves as the family's textbook, consulted before each new planting: "I think I'll get my garden book and be sure I'm right about the distance" (022-021).

The garden also redistributes labor across the family. Mousie tends seedlings in the kitchen window (022-004); Winnie learns to distinguish crops from weeds; Merton and the narrator handle the heavy work of plowing and furrowing; even Bobsey participates in the planting. The garden is the one place where every family member contributes, making it the spatial expression of the novel's communal work ethic.

By late summer, the garden's productivity validates the entire country experiment. The narrator sells "garden potatoes, at a dollar a bushel" (037-037), and the family dines on vegetables "picked a few moments before from our own garden" (031-004). The garden transforms from promise to proof.

## Key Scenes

- **The dream of a garden** (002-021, 003-010): Before the move, Mrs. Durham imagines "a garden and fields and woods" and Mousie dreams of a flower garden "as big as our flat" — the garden as imaginative catalyst for the family's decision.
- **First encounter with the real garden** (019-012–015): "Dry, unsightly weeds" and dead potato vines greet the family, but the narrator immediately begins organizing labor — "you are gardeners now."
- **The garden book and the first seeds** (022-006–022): "I'll get out my treasure of seeds" — the narrator consults his garden book, lines are stretched, onion seeds like gunpowder are sown.
- **Plowing day** (022-009–016): Mr. Jones brings his horse and plow: "I'll have the rest of the garden plowed" — the garden's scale demands horsepower, not just hand labor.
- **The weed war** (030-002–031): "Invading, not only the garden and fields, but the raspberries" — weeds become a moral enemy, requiring relentless daily attention.
- **Early strawberries** (031-011–012): The family "picked our way around the garden, exclaiming in pleased wonder" at the strawberry blossoms — the first fruits of their labor.
- **The garden after the barn fire** (037-031–032): "The garden has been helped rather than injured by the storm" — the garden's resilience contrasts with the barn's destruction.
- **Selling the harvest** (037-037): "I found a purchaser for my garden potatoes, at a dollar a bushel" — the garden becomes a source of cash income, not just subsistence.
