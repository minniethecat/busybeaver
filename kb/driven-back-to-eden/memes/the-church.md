# The Church

The country church, presided over by Dr. Lyman, is the spiritual anchor of the Durham family's new life. Unlike the urban churches from which the family came — unnamed, presumably large, abstracted from daily life — the country church is intimate, practical, and integrated into the rhythms of the agricultural community.

## Role in the Narrative

The Durhams' search for a church home is one of the first acts of settlement. "At last there came a mild, sunny morning, and we resolved to find a church home. I had heard that Dr. Lyman, who preached in the nearest village, had the faculty of keeping young people awake" (Chapter XIV). The choice of minister is revealing: the narrator values not doctrinal precision but the capacity to engage young minds — a practical, domestic criterion that matches the novel's overall approach to religion.

The church becomes a site of community integration. Sunday attendance brings the Durhams into contact with their country neighbors; the rhythms of the liturgical year (Christmas, Easter, Thanksgiving) provide temporal structure; Dr. Lyman's ministry — bridging natural science, medicine, and faith — models the integrated life the novel endorses.

## Key Passages

- **"I had heard that Dr. Lyman... had the faculty of keeping young people awake"** — the practical criterion for choosing a church
- **Sunday worship as community ritual** — the church as social as well as spiritual center
- **Dr. Lyman's sermons** — practical Christianity that engages rather than withdraws
