## Definition

Neighborliness in *Driven Back to Eden* is the practice of mutual aid and goodwill between rural neighbors, presented as one of country life's essential virtues. The novel draws a sharp contrast between the anonymous, sometimes hostile proximity of tenement neighbors in New York City and the generous, practical assistance offered by country neighbors like Mr. Jones.

## Expression in the Text

The Durhams' city neighbors — the Daggetts on the floor above and the Ricketts below — represent urban alienation. They are heard through thin walls and characterized by suspicion, notably Melissa Daggett's "sly" nature (002-019). By contrast, Mr. Jones embodies rural neighborliness from the first encounter. He offers blunt, generous advice about the farm: "I don't intend havin' any neighbors around blamin' me for a fraud" (007-004), grounding his neighborly ethic in personal accountability and long-term reputation.

Neighbor Jones proves his goodwill repeatedly: helping the family settle in (014-004–006), driving them through storms in his sleigh (014-044), and advising on everything from raspberry cultivation to maple sugaring. His wife, too, exemplifies "neighborly kindness" that "has steadily increased" (007-027). The novel frames this as the rural norm — neighbors who lend tools, share knowledge, and provide emergency aid. Mr. Rollins, another neighbor, assists the family's move without being asked (014-044–047).

## Narrative Function

Neighborliness functions as both theme and plot mechanism. The contrast between city and country neighbors reinforces the novel's central argument that rural life fosters moral character and community. Practically, Mr. Jones's guidance makes the Durhams' survival possible — without his agricultural knowledge, their farming venture would have failed. Neighborliness thus becomes the human infrastructure of the country ideal: self-reliance, the novel suggests, is sustained by a network of willing neighbors.
