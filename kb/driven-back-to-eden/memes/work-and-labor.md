# Work and Labor

## Definition

In *Driven Back to Eden*, physical work is presented as a moral good rather than an unfortunate necessity. The narrator frames the family's transition from city to country explicitly in terms of labor: instead of the father working alone at an office while the family fragments at home, "my work, instead of being away from them all day, could be with them. And all could do something, even down to pale Mousie and little Bobsey" (002-021). Work, in this vision, integrates the family, builds character, and produces tangible results.

## Expression in the Text

The novel is structured around the work of each season: pruning in March (020-002), planting in April, weeding and cultivating through summer, harvesting berries and vegetables, gathering firewood for winter. Each family member contributes according to ability — Merton prunes and hunts, Winnie tends poultry, Mousie gathers vegetables, and even Bobsey helps pile prunings. The narrator explicitly treats labor as pedagogy: "they could take their guns and be absent two or three hours if they would promise to help faithfully the rest of the day" (015-004). The work is not romanticized — the narrator wryly observes that "our modern Eden will cost a great many backaches" (030-026) — but it is consistently valued. The chapter title "We Shall All Earn Our Salt" (035-001) makes labor the measure of membership in the family economy.

## Narrative Function

Work provides the novel's episodic structure and its implicit argument. Each chapter's farm task is a small narrative arc: learning, attempting, struggling, and (usually) succeeding. This rhythm replaces the monotonous grind of city office work with varied, seasonal, and visible labor. The narrator's satisfaction comes not from leisure but from productive achievement — paying off debts, filling the cellar for winter, seeing the children brown and healthy from outdoor work. Work is the mechanism by which the novel's values — self-reliance, family cohesion, health — are realized.

## Key Passages

- "My work, instead of being away from them all day, could be with them" (002-021) — the integration of labor and family life
- "Our modern Eden will cost a great many backaches" (030-026) — honest acknowledgment of labor's physical cost
- "They could take their guns and be absent two or three hours if they would promise to help faithfully the rest of the day" (015-004) — labor as negotiated reciprocity
- "We Shall All Earn Our Salt" (035-001) — labor as the condition of belonging
- "The results of our labor were cheering" (036-005) — the satisfaction of productive work
