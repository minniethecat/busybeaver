# Themis (Divine Custom)

*Themis* (Greek: Θέμις) is the Greek concept of correct tribal custom — "the thing that is always done" — and, together with [[moira|Moira]] and the [[erinyes|Erinyes]], one of the three foundational forces that uphold the law of *Dikê* in Aeschylus' *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]*. Themis represents the ancestral wisdom accessible through oracles and elders, the body of ritual precedent that governs human conduct in the absence of written law.

## Definition

As explained in Murray's Introduction, Themis is "the correct tribal custom, the thing that is always done—always, that is, by the people who really know" (P01-003).

It is not a written code but the living tradition of a community, transmitted orally through elders and ritual specialists.

When crises arise — "prodigies or bewildering emergencies" — and ordinary knowledge fails, "you consult the elders or other authorities on ritual and precedent" (P01-003).

If they cannot help, "you go to an oracle and consult the great ancestors, the '[[mother-earth|Earth]]y' or '[[chthonian|Underworld]]' people, called *Chthonioi*, lying in the bosom of the earth" (P01-003).

These ancestors "tell you what is Themis; and that is why Themis presides at [[delphi|Delphi]]" (P01-003).


Unlike [[moira|Moira]] (the impersonal law of portion) or the [[erinyes|Erinyes]] (the wrathful enforcers), Themis is intimately connected with [[delphi|Delphi]] and the oracular tradition. She is, in the genealogy presented by the [[pythian-prophetess|Pythian Prophetess]] at the opening of the play, the second occupant of the [[delphi|Delphi]]c throne, succeeding [[mother-earth|Earth]] and preceding [[phoebe|Phoebe]] and [[apollo|Apollo]] (001-001).

## In The Eumenides

Themis appears in *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]* primarily as part of the conceptual framework that the play both invokes and transcends.

She is invoked in the Prophetess's opening prayer as the second holder of the [[delphi|Delphi]]c oracle: "First of all Gods I worship in this prayer [[mother-earth|Earth]], the primeval prophet; after her Themis, the Wise, who on her mother's throne — So runs the tale — sat second" (001-001).

This genealogy places Themis within the older order of earth-based oracles that [[apollo|Apollo]]'s [[delphi|Delphi]]c priesthood has supplanted.


The play's central theological argument — that the mechanical law of *Dikê* must be corrected by the personal wisdom of [[zeus|Zeus]] — necessarily involves a reevaluation of Themis.

The "correct custom" that Themis represents is, in its primitive form, the blood-feud: the unquestioned obligation of kin to avenge kin.

As Murray notes, this is "the law of Themis, of [[moira|Moira]], of the [[erinyes|Erinyes]], and ultimately of [[mother-earth|Mother Earth]]" (P01-002).

When [[orestes|Orestes]] kills his mother, he acts in accordance with one aspect of Themis (the son's duty to avenge his father) while violating another (the absolute prohibition against matricide).

The play's resolution — the acquittal at the [[areopagus|Areopagus]] and the transformation of the [[erinyes|Furies]] into [[erinyes|Eumenides]] — represents not the abolition of Themis but its elevation: the old custom is not discarded but integrated into a new civic order.


That Themis was understood by Aeschylus as capable of evolution is suggested by the Prophetess's description of the [[delphi|Delphi]]c succession: Themis yielded the throne to [[phoebe|Phoebe]] "by whose own Accepted will, with never strife nor stress" (001-001). Unlike the violent transitions that characterize the succession myths of Hesiod, the [[delphi|Delphi]]c succession is peaceful and consensual — a model, perhaps, for the reconciliation [[athena|Athena]] achieves with the [[erinyes|Furies]] at the play's end.

## In Greek Thought

Themis occupies a distinctive place in Greek religious thought as the personification of established social order. In Hesiod's *Theogony*, she is a Titaness, the daughter of [[mother-earth|Earth]] and Heaven, and the second consort of [[zeus|Zeus]] after Metis; her children include the Seasons (*Horae*) and the [[moira|Fate]]s (*[[moira|Moira]]i*). This genealogy associates her with cosmic order and the rhythms of nature and time.

The Athenian connection is particularly significant. Themis was worshipped in [[athens|Athens]], and her association with oracles and the proper ordering of society made her a natural patron of the city's legal institutions — a role that *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]* transfers to [[athena|Athena]] and the [[areopagus|Areopagus]]. The play can be read as tracing the historical development from Themis-governed custom (ancestral, oral, [[delphi|Delphi]]c) to [[athena|Athena]]-governed law (civic, institutional, Athenian).
