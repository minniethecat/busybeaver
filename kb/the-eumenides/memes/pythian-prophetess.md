# The Pythian Prophetess

The Pythian Prophetess (the [[delphic-oracle|Pythia]]) is the priestess of [[apollo|Apollo]] at [[delphi|Delphi]], whose opening speech and terrified flight from the inner shrine launch the action of Aeschylus' *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]*. She is the first character to speak and the first to witness the polluted [[orestes|Orestes]] surrounded by the sleeping [[erinyes|Furies]] — a sight so horrifying that it drives her from the temple.

## Role in the Play

The Prophetess opens the play with a solemn prayer that traces the genealogy of the [[delphi|Delphi]]c oracle: [[mother-earth|Earth]], [[themis|Themis]], [[phoebe|Phoebe]], [[apollo|Apollo]], and the subsidiary deities of the region — [[athena|Pallas Athena]], the [[the-nymphs|Corycian Nymphs]], [[dionysus|Bromios]] ([[dionysus|Dionysus]]), [[poseidon|Poseidon]], and [[zeus|Zeus]]. The prayer establishes the theological history that the play will transform: the peaceful succession of prophetic authority, culminating in [[apollo|Apollo]]'s rule and [[zeus|Zeus]]'s ultimate sovereignty.

She then enters the inner shrine to perform her office — and the play's tone shifts from reverent to terrifying. She returns "grasping at the wall for support," her description tumbling out in broken, horrified phrases: "Ah! Horrors, horrors, dire to speak or see, From [[apollo|Loxias]]' chamber drive me reeling back. My knees are weak beneath me, and I lack The strength to fly" (001-001).

Her description of what she has seen provides the play's first visual image of its central figures: [[orestes|Orestes]] "at the inmost Altar of the world, A man abhorred of God, his body hurled [[mother-earth|Earth]]ward in desperate prayer; blood on his hand Yet reeking, and a naked new-drawn brand Wreathed in beseeching wool" (001-001).

And surrounding him, the [[erinyes|Furies]] — "a rout unknown Of women sleepeth, flung from throne to throne" — beings she struggles to categorize: "Women? Nay, never women!

Gorgons more: And yet not like the Gorgon shapes of yore" (001-001).


Her flight from the shrine and her final prayer — "Let [[apollo|Apollo]] great in power Take to his care the peril of this hour: Being Helper, Prophet, Seer of things unseen, The stainèd hearth he knoweth to make clean" (001-001) — hands the dramatic action to [[apollo|Apollo]] and the divine figures who will resolve it. She does not appear again.

## Mythological Background

The Pythia was the historical priestess of [[apollo|Apollo]] at [[delphi|Delphi]], one of the most important religious offices in the Greek world. Selected from among the women of [[delphi|Delphi]], she delivered [[apollo|Apollo]]'s oracles from the *adyton* (inner shrine) of the temple, seated on a tripod over a chasm that emitted intoxicating vapors. Her utterances, often cryptic or fragmentary, were interpreted by the temple's priests and delivered to consultants in verse or prose.

Aeschylus' portrayal of the [[delphic-oracle|Pythia]] as an elderly woman ("An old woman, and in fear, A thing of naught, a babe in helplessness!") humanizes the office, contrasting her vulnerability with the cosmic powers — [[apollo|Apollo]], the [[erinyes|Furies]], [[zeus|Zeus]] — who will determine the play's outcome. She is the mortal witness to a divine drama.

## Relationships

- **[[apollo|Apollo]]**: the god she serves; his power and knowledge far exceed hers
- **[[orestes|Orestes]]**: the suppliant she discovers at the altar
- **The [[erinyes|Furies]]**: the terrifying beings she cannot identify
