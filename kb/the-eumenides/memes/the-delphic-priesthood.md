# The Delphic Priesthood

The Delphic Priesthood is the prophetic institution at [[apollo|Apollo]]'s shrine on [[mount-parnassus|Mount Parnassus]], centered on the office of the [[delphic-oracle|Pythia]] — the priestess through whom [[apollo|Apollo]] delivered oracles. In *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]*, the [[delphi|Delphi]]c priesthood is the human instrument through which the divine genealogy of the oracle ([[mother-earth|Earth]] → [[themis|Themis]] → [[phoebe|Phoebe]] → [[apollo|Apollo]]) is narrated and maintained.

## Role in the Play

The play opens at [[delphi|Delphi]] with the [[pythian-prophetess|Pythian Prophetess]] standing before the temple doors (001-001).

Her opening prayer (001-003) is the priesthood's foundational text within the play — a ritual invocation that traces the sacred succession of the [[delphi|Delphi]]c oracle through four generations, establishing [[apollo|Apollo]] as the fourth prophet and declaring that "[[apollo|Loxias]]' lips reveal the thought of [[zeus|Zeus]]" (001-003).

After entering the shrine and discovering the sleeping [[erinyes|Furies]] surrounding [[orestes|Orestes]], she flees in terror, leaving the stage to [[apollo|Apollo]].


## The Pythia's Office

[[pythian-prophetess|The Pythia]] — an elderly woman selected from the local population — delivered [[apollo|Apollo]]'s responses while in a state of ecstatic possession. Temple priests interpreted her utterances and managed the sanctuary's extensive political and economic affairs. The priesthood was responsible for the operation of the most important oracular institution in the Greek world.

## Theological Function

The [[delphi|Delphi]]c priesthood serves a crucial theological function in Aeschylus' play. Through the [[delphic-oracle|Pythia]]'s prayer, the audience learns that the prophetic succession at [[delphi|Delphi]] occurred peacefully — "with never strife nor stress" (001-003) — in contrast to the violent cosmic power struggles of earlier mythology. This peaceful transmission of divine authority prefigures the play's central theme: the replacement of the blood-feud with civic justice through persuasion rather than violence.
