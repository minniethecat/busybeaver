# Athena Persuades the Furies

[[athena|Athena]]'s persuasion of the [[erinyes|Furies]] is the pivotal act of political theology in Aeschylus' *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]*: after the trial's equal verdict acquits [[orestes|Orestes]], the defeated [[erinyes|Furies]] threaten to destroy [[athens|Athens]] with blight and barrenness, and [[athena|Athena]], rather than driving them out by force, deploys a sustained campaign of persuasion — acknowledgment, honor, and promise — to transform their destructive rage into protective blessing.

## Overview

When [[athena|Athena]]'s tie-breaking vote acquits [[orestes|Orestes]], the [[erinyes|Furies]] explode with rage. Their great choral ode — "Woe on you, woe, ye younger gods!

Ye have trampled the great Laws of old Beneath your chariots!" (001-133) — threatens to poison the land.

[[athena|Athena]] responds not with the thunderbolt (which, as keeper of [[zeus|Zeus]]'s keys, she could deploy) but with a three-stage persuasion: she first acknowledges the dignity of their ancient role; then offers them a permanent home and cult in [[athens|Athens]]; and finally promises that their function — enforcing moral order — will be elevated, not abolished.

The [[erinyes|Furies]], gradually, accept.


## Context

The [[erinyes|Furies]] are the ancient enforcers of the blood-feud, "the Virgins without love, So grey, so old, whom never god Above hath kissed" (001-003).

At the trial, [[apollo|Apollo]]'s arguments and [[athena|Athena]]'s tie-breaking vote have defeated their claim on [[orestes|Orestes]].

From their perspective, this is not merely a legal loss but a cosmic rupture: the "younger gods" have overruled the "great Laws of old." Their threat to blight [[athens|Athens]] is not capricious — it is the logical consequence of their function: thwarted justice must manifest as collective suffering (001-022).


## The Event

[[athena|Athena]]'s persuasion proceeds through three distinct stages, each reflected in the text's dramatic rhythm.

### Stage 1: Acknowledgment (001-134)

When the [[erinyes|Furies]] first threaten [[athens|Athens]] with "Seed-ravening blight and mildews merciless, Till all the land lie waste in fruitlessness" (001-134), [[athena|Athena]]'s opening move is not rebuttal but recognition: "Ye are not conquered. Equal, stone for stone, The judgement fell, in honesty of thought, Not scorn of thee" (001-134). She refuses to frame the outcome as victory-versus-defeat, instead validating the integrity of the process and the legitimacy of their grievance.

### Stage 2: Offer of Honor (001-134)

[[athena|Athena]] moves from acknowledgment to concrete offer: "I promise: here shall be A home your own, a caverned mystery, Where alway ye shall sit, enthroned in pride And shining, by my people glorified" (001-134). The offer is not merely to tolerate the [[erinyes|Furies]] but to honor them — permanent cult, chthonian worship by the Athenians, and a seat of dignity beneath the [[areopagus|Areopagus]]. She reframes their function: from wandering punishers to established guardians.

### Stage 3: The Pragmatic Appeal (001-136)

When the [[erinyes|Furies]] remain resistant, [[athena|Athena]] adds a practical dimension: she holds "the keys of that great treasure-house... Where sleep the lightnings" (001-136) but chooses not to use them.

The implicit threat is paired with explicit generosity: "From many miles of land to thee shall come First-fruits for maidens wed, for children born; Then shall ye bless this peace that we have sworn" (001-136).

The persuasion completes when the [[erinyes|Furies]] shift from cursing to blessing, their ritual dress changing from black to crimson.


## Aftermath

The persuasion succeeds where force would have failed. The [[erinyes|Furies]] become the [[erinyes|Eumenides]] ("[[erinyes|Kindly Ones]]"), their function preserved but reoriented: what was once a source of terror becomes a source of civic blessing — fertility, health, and concord. The play's title marks this transformation as its central theological achievement.

## In The Eumenides

The persuasion scene is the dramatic culmination of the play's political argument.

Murray's interpretive framework emphasizes that [[athena|Athena]]'s method — persuasion over coercion, incorporation over expulsion — models the transition from the archaic blood-feud to civic justice: "the Law that can pardon and understand can itself be understood and loved.

Its ministers are no longer alien and hostile beings...

They are accepted by [[athena|Athena]] as fellow-citizens, and their Law recognized as an inward aspiration, a standard of right living which men consciously need and seek" (P01-018).
