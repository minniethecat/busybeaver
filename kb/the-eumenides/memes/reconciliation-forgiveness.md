Reconciliation and forgiveness are the central theological themes of Aeschylus' *[[erinyes|Eumenides]]*. The play asks: how can the endless chain of vengeance be broken? Aeschylus' answer — that [[zeus|Zeus]], having himself learned through suffering, can forgive the penitent suppliant — represents a revolutionary moment in Greek religious thought, anticipating elements of later theological systems while remaining firmly rooted in the language of Greek civic religion.

## Definition

The forgiveness dramatised in the *[[erinyes|Eumenides]]* is not the cancellation of the law but its fulfillment at a higher level.

As the introduction explains: "The mechanical and automatic operation of the Law is corrected by the will of the Father. It is not broken, but more truly and perfectly fulfilled" (P01-019).

Forgiveness does not mean that [[orestes|Orestes]]' act was not wrong; it means that the wrong has been understood in its full context and the doer, having suffered and been purified, can be released from the [[curse|curse]].


The key to Aeschylean forgiveness is the capacity of [[zeus|Zeus]] to learn. Unlike the [[erinyes|Erinyes]], who "neither understand nor forgive" (P01-015), [[zeus|Zeus]] "made a road to Thought" and "established [[learning-by-suffering|Learning by Suffering]] to be an abiding law" (P01-013). He himself "in the distant past won his throne by violence, but now he has learned and his heart is changed" (P01-013). A god who can learn is a god who can forgive.

## In The Eumenides

The play dramatises reconciliation at two levels. At the personal level, [[orestes|Orestes]] is forgiven. He has committed matricide — the most terrible violation of the natural order — but he acted at [[apollo|Apollo]]'s command, he has suffered, he has been purified, and he comes as a suppliant to [[athena|Athena]]'s altar. "Perfect by suffering," he is received and pardoned (001-107).

At the cosmic level, the [[erinyes|Erinyes]] themselves are reconciled with the Olympian order. Their rage at being defeated is immense: "Woe on you, woe, ye younger gods! Ye have trampled the great Laws of old / Beneath your chariots!" [paraphrase]. [[athena|Athena]] does not argue them into submission; she persuades them. She offers them honour, a permanent home in [[athens|Athens]], and a continuing role in the cosmic order: "Accept my word, and cast not here the hot / Fruits of a passion that turns all to ill" [paraphrase].

The crucial test is whether the [[erinyes|Erinyes]] believe in [[peitho|Persuasion]]: "Do they believe in [[peitho|Persuasion]], which is the gift of [[zeus|Zeus]] and the office of [[athena|Athena]]? That is, do they admit that there is a place in the Law for reason and understanding?" (002-044). When they accept, the reconciliation is complete: "The Law that is [[moira|Fate]] and the Father the All-Comprehending / Are here met together as one" [paraphrase].

The endnotes draw an illuminating comparison with St. Paul: "the suppression of the Law by a personal relation to a divine person" (P01-023). But Aeschylus goes further than Paul in one crucial respect: "When [[orestes|Orestes]] is pardoned by the will of [[zeus|Zeus]], the [[erinyes|Furies]] yield; the Law is deemed to be satisfied; there is no talk of its demanding to be paid off with another victim" (P01-023). Aeschylean forgiveness is absolute — no substitute victim is required.

## Political Reconciliation

The play was performed in 458 BCE, three years after the democratic reformer [[ephialtes|Ephialtes]] was murdered and during a period of intense factional strife in [[athens|Athens]] (002-048). The reconciliation of the [[erinyes|Erinyes]] is also a political allegory: the old aristocratic powers (symbolised by the ancient [[the-chthonian-people|chthonian]] goddesses) must be incorporated into the democratic order rather than destroyed.

The crimson robes given to the [[erinyes|Erinyes]] — the mark of a *metoikos* or resident alien in [[athens|Athens]] (002-051) — symbolise this civic incorporation. They become honoured inhabitants of the city, not outcasts. The poet's "gospel of forgiveness" (002-048) is as much a political programme as a theological one.

## Key Passages

> The Zeus who learns and understands is also the Zeus who can forgive the sinner. He can forgive just because he understands. The Law of the Moirai and the Erinyes neither understands nor forgives. It simply operates.
> (P01-015)

> Thus at last the offender who deserves pardon can be pardoned. But that is not all. The Law that can pardon and understand can itself be understood and loved. Its ministers are no longer alien and hostile beings … They are accepted by Athena as fellow-citizens, and their Law recognized as an inward aspiration, a standard of right living which men consciously need and seek. The "Furies" have become "Eumenides."
> (P01-027)
