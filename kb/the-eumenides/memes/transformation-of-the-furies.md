# The Transformation of the Furies

The Transformation of the Furies is the culminating act of Aeschylus' *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]*: [[athena|Athena]] persuades the defeated [[erinyes|Erinyes]] to abandon their wrath against [[athens|Athens]], accept a permanent home beneath the [[areopagus|Areopagus]] hill, and become the [[erinyes|Eumenides]] ("[[erinyes|Kindly Ones]]") — guardians of the city who bless rather than curse. It is the event that gives the play its name.

## Overview

After [[athena|Athena]] casts the deciding vote for [[orestes|Orestes]]' acquittal, the [[erinyes|Furies]] erupt in rage. Their great choral ode of defeat — "Woe on you, woe, ye younger gods!

Ye have trampled the great Laws of old Beneath your chariots!" (001-060) — threatens to destroy [[athens|Athens]] with blight, barrenness, and plague.

[[athena|Athena]], rather than dismissing or destroying them, engages in an extended act of persuasion: she acknowledges their ancient dignity, offers them honor and a permanent home in [[athens|Athens]], and gradually transforms their destructive rage into protective blessing.


## Context

The [[erinyes|Furies]] are the ancient enforcers of the blood-feud — "the Virgins without love, So grey, so old, whom never god Above hath kissed" (001-002). Their defeat at the trial is, from their perspective, the overthrow of cosmic law by the "younger gods" who "have trampled the great Laws of old." [[athena|Athena]]'s task is not merely to calm their anger but to convince them that the new order honors rather than abolishes their function.

## The Event

[[athena|Athena]]'s persuasion proceeds through several stages: she first acknowledges their suffering (they have been defeated, and defeat is bitter);

she then reminds them of her own authority (she holds the keys to [[zeus|Zeus]]'s thunderbolt but chooses not to use it); she offers them a permanent home beneath the [[areopagus|Areopagus]], with chthonian worship from the Athenians;

and finally, she promises that their function — the enforcement of moral order — will be not abolished but elevated: their blessings will ensure the city's fertility, health, and concord.


The [[erinyes|Furies]], gradually, accept. Their transformation is marked by a change in their ritual dress (they exchange black robes for crimson) and in their speech: their final choral ode blesses [[athens|Athens]] with images of fertility, peace, and prosperity.

## Aftermath

The [[erinyes|Eumenides]] become permanent guardians of [[athens|Athens]], worshipped at a shrine beneath the [[areopagus|Areopagus]] hill. Their transformation is the model for all subsequent reconciliations: the old order is not destroyed but incorporated, and what was once a source of terror becomes a source of blessing.

## In The Eumenides

The transformation completes the play's theological arc. Murray summarizes: "the Law that can pardon and understand can itself be understood and loved. Its ministers are no longer alien and hostile beings... They are accepted by [[athena|Athena]] as fellow-citizens, and their Law recognized as an inward aspiration, a standard of right living which men consciously need and seek. The '[[erinyes|Furies]]' have become '[[erinyes|Eumenides]]'" (P01-018).
