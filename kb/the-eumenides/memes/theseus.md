# Theseus

Theseus, the legendary king of [[athens|Athens]], appears in *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]* only through two brief references in [[athena|Athena]]'s speeches. These allusions anchor the play's mythic action in Athenian legendary history and establish the connection between the goddess [[athena|Athena]] and the city she protects.

## Role in the Play

### Athena's Entrance (001-048)

[[athena|Athena]]'s first reference to Theseus comes in her entrance speech: she has been away at the river [[scamander-river|Scamander]], receiving land from "the sons of Theseus" as a prize of war (001-048). This establishes [[athena|Athena]]'s ancient relationship with the Athenian people — "Theseus' sons" being a poetic term for the Athenians themselves. The goddess is not a distant Olympian intervening arbitrarily in human affairs; she is the city's patron, with a history of receiving gifts from its legendary kings.

### The Founding of the Areopagus (001-113)

[[athena|Athena]]'s second reference to Theseus occurs in her founding ordinance for the [[areopagus|Areopagus]] court. She establishes the tribunal on the [[areopagus-hill|Hill of Ares]] — the very place where the Amazons once camped when they came "in wrath for Theseus' wrongs of old" (001-113). This reference to Theseus' mythological conflict with the Amazons provides the aetiology for the name "[[areopagus|Areopagus]]" ([[areopagus-hill|Hill of Ares]]) and connects the new civic institution to the city's most ancient legendary history.

## Mythological Significance

Theseus was the great national hero of [[athens|Athens]], credited with the synoecism (political unification) of Attica, the slaying of the Minotaur, and numerous other exploits. By the time of Aeschylus, Theseus had become a symbol of Athenian identity and democratic values. His brief appearances in *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]* — through [[athena|Athena]]'s words rather than in person — serve to remind the audience that the events unfolding on stage are rooted in their own city's sacred history.
