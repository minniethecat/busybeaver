# The Erinyes (Furies)

The Erinyes (Furies, also called *Eumenides* — "Kindly Ones" — after their transformation) are the chorus of Aeschylus' *The Eumenides* and the play's most complex characters. They begin as the implacable enforcers of the blood-feud — ancient, terrifying, unloved by gods or mortals — and end as the honored guardians of [[athens|Athens]], the *Semnai Theai* ("Revered Goddesses"), whose blessings ensure the city's fertility and peace.

## Role in the Play

The Erinyes are the collective protagonist-antagonist of *The Eumenides*. They appear first as a sleeping chorus surrounding [[orestes|Orestes]] at [[apollo|Apollo]]'s altar — a "rout unknown of women" that the [[pythian-prophetess|Pythian Prophetess]] cannot identify: "Women? Nay, never women! Gorgons more: And yet not like the Gorgon shapes of yore" (001-001). Their appearance is deliberately uncategorizable: neither women nor Gorgons, neither living nor dead, "all shadows, black, abominable."

[[apollo|Apollo]]'s description is contemptuous: "The Virgins without love, So grey, so old, whom never god Above hath kissed, nor man, nor from the wilderness One wild beast. They were born for wickedness And sorrow; for in evil night they dwell, And feed on the great darkness that is Hell, Most hated by the Gods and human thought" (001-002).

Yet the play grants the Erinyes a powerful counter-argument. When [[apollo|Apollo]] dismisses their claim to pursue [[orestes|Orestes]], their Leader retorts: "We be by Law eternal what we be" (001-012). Their function is not arbitrary or malevolent; it is the enforcement of an ancient and legitimate law — the law that kindred bloodshed must be avenged. [[apollo|Apollo]] has broken that law by stealing [[orestes|Orestes]] away.

Their great choral ode (the "binding song") asserts their cosmic function: "This is our office, this our right, Apportioned by eternal Night" (001-045). They are not evil; they are the mechanism by which the universe corrects transgressions. Without them, there is no justice — only chaos.

After their defeat at the trial, their rage is immense: "Woe on you, woe, ye younger gods! Ye have trampled the great Laws of old Beneath your chariots! Ye have broke the rods Of justice, yea and torn them from my hold!" (001-060). [[athena|Athena]]'s response — persuasion, the offer of honor and a permanent home — transforms them. When they accept, they become the Eumenides, the "Kindly Ones," and pronounce blessings on [[athens|Athens]] that conclude the play.

## Mythological Background

The Erinyes were among the most ancient powers in Greek religion, born, according to Hesiod, from the blood of Ouranos when Kronos castrated him. They were associated with the enforcement of oaths, the punishment of kin-murder, and the protection of the rights of parents and elders. In Homer, they "check the voice" of [[achilles|Achilles]]' horse when it speaks beyond its [[moira|Moira]]; in the *[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]*, they avenge the robbed vulture's nest.

Their transformation into the Eumenides at the end of Aeschylus' play reflects Athenian cult practice: the *Semnai Theai* had a shrine beneath the [[areopagus|Areopagus]] hill, where they received chthonian offerings. Aeschylus' myth provides an aetiology for this cult, explaining how the terrifying Furies became the benevolent guardians of [[athens|Athens]].

- **[[mother-earth|Mother Earth (Gaia)]]**: the supreme chthonian authority, whose wrath the Erinyes channel
- **[[the-chthonian-people|The Dead]]**: their primary constituency; they avenge the wrongs of the murdered
- **[[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]**: the murdered mother who summons them to pursue [[orestes|Orestes]]
- **[[apollo|Apollo]]**: their antagonist, who despises them and protects the suppliant
- **[[athena|Athena]]**: the goddess who transforms them from Furies into Eumenides
- **[[orestes|Orestes]]**: their quarry, whose acquittal they ultimately accept

## Significance in Aeschylean Thought

The Erinyes embody the old law — necessary, legitimate, but incomplete. Their transformation into Eumenides is the play's central act: the law is not abolished but fulfilled, and its enforcers become its guardians. The "Furies" have become "Eumenides" — and, as Murray observes, the Law they represent is "no longer an alien and hostile" force but "an inward aspiration, a standard of right living which men consciously need and seek" (P01-018).

## In Later Tradition

The Eumenides appear in later Greek literature primarily as the *Semnai Theai* of Athenian cult.

In modern thought, they have been interpreted through multiple lenses: Freudian (the Furies as the return of the repressed, the *Eumenides* as the therapeutic transformation of guilt), feminist (the suppression of the maternal principle by patriarchal Olympian order), and political (the founding of the rule of law).

Aeschylus' achievement is to have created characters flexible enough to sustain all these readings while remaining dramatically compelling.
