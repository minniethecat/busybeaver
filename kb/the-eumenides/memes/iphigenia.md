# Iphigenia

Iphigenia, daughter of [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] and [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]], is the original victim whose sacrifice at [[aulis|Aulis]] sets the entire chain of vengeance in the *Oresteia* in motion. Though she does not appear in *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]* and is mentioned only indirectly, her death is the first wrong in the sequence that culminates in [[orestes|Orestes]]' trial: [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] sacrifices Iphigenia → [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] kills [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] → [[orestes|Orestes]] kills [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] → the [[erinyes|Furies]] pursue [[orestes|Orestes]] → the [[areopagus|Areopagus]] is founded.

## Role in the Play

Iphigenia is referenced in Murray's Introduction as part of the chain of vengeance that Aeschylus describes in the *[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]*: "The prophet [[calchas|Calchas]] has been describing the ravenous feast of the two Eagles; the wrath of [[artemis|Artemis]] thereat and the vengeance exacted in the death of Iphigenia; the future vengeance to be exacted for that death; and beyond a yet further vista of vengeances re-avenged" (P01-008). Her death is the pivot on which the entire cycle turns.

The moral complexity is acute. [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s sacrifice of Iphigenia was commanded by [[artemis|Artemis]], who had stilled the winds at [[aulis|Aulis]].

He was trapped between two divine obligations: to lead the Greek fleet to [[troy|Troy]] (his duty as commander) and to protect his daughter (his duty as father). Whatever choice he made would be wrong.

The tragedy is that his wrong choice — sacrificing Iphigenia — generated [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]'s legitimate grievance, which generated her murder of [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]], which generated [[orestes|Orestes]]' obligation to avenge, ad infinitum.


## Mythological Background

The myth of Iphigenia's sacrifice exists in multiple versions. In the most common, [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] offended [[artemis|Artemis]] (by killing a sacred deer or boasting of his hunting skill), and the goddess demanded the sacrifice of his daughter as compensation. In some versions, [[artemis|Artemis]] substituted a deer for Iphigenia at the last moment and transported the girl to [[tauris|Tauris]], where she became a priestess of [[artemis|Artemis]]. This version is the basis of Euripides' *Iphigenia in [[tauris|Tauris]]*.

Aeschylus' treatment in the *[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]* is more ambiguous about whether the sacrifice actually occurred, but its moral weight — the horror of a father killing his own child for a military expedition — is the same regardless.

## Relationships

- **[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]** (father): her sacrificer, whose act condemns him and the entire house
- **[[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]** (mother): whose grief and rage at the sacrifice drive her to murder [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]
- **[[artemis|Artemis]]**: the goddess who demanded (or accepted) the sacrifice
- **[[orestes|Orestes]]** (brother): who must avenge his father's death without denying his mother's legitimate grievance
