# Kindred Blood

**Kindred blood** (Greek: αἷμα συγγενές) is the blood shared by members of the same family or lineage. In the Greek religious system that [[the-eumenides|The Eumenides]] engages with, the shedding of kindred blood is the gravest form of transgression, generating a unique and terrible [[blood-pollution|pollution (miasma)]].

## Murray's Analysis

Murray's Introduction carefully distinguishes between two kinds of homicide:

"When a man kills one of another tribe. There is no 'sin' or 'pollution' in this, though of course the kindred of the stranger will duly try to avenge him. Suppose, next, that a man kills, not a stranger, but his own kinsman. Then he has shed the 'kindred blood,' and committed a real sin. He is polluted, and pollutes all who associate with him." (P01-001)

## Kinship and Obligation

The shedding of kindred blood creates an inescapable obligation on the victim's kin. If the dead have left children or male representatives, "they are bound in honour to take up the blood-feud and do vengeance, even though, if the relationship is very close, it may bring pollution on them." (P01-001)

This is the trap in which [[orestes|Orestes]] finds himself: his father's kindred blood demands vengeance against [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]], but in killing his mother he sheds kindred blood again — and incurs pollution anew.

## Resolution in The Eumenides

The resolution of *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]* does not deny the gravity of kindred blood. Rather, it establishes a new mechanism — the [[the-areopagus-court|Areopagus Court]] — for adjudicating cases where the demands of kindred blood conflict. The court does not abolish the concept of kindred blood; it civilizes the response to its shedding.

> So far we can follow the ancient ideas without much difficulty. But it is rather a surprise to the modern to find Mother Earth among the supreme authorities for executing this law.
>
> (P01-006)
