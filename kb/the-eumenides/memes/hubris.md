# Hubris (Overstepping / Arrogance)

*Hubris* (Greek: Ὕβρις) is the act of overstepping one's allotted portion (*[[moira|Moira]]*) — the arrogant transgression that, in the moral universe of Aeschylus' *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]*, inevitably calls down the retributive law of *Dikê*. It is not merely pride but a cosmological violation: the refusal to remain within one's proper bounds, whether as a mortal challenging the gods, a kinsman shedding kindred blood, or even the sun exceeding its seasonal zenith.

## Definition

In the framework established by Murray's Introduction, Hubris is defined relationally against [[moira|Moira]]: each being has a "portion" allotted, and "he must not exceed it nor trespass on the [[moira|Moira]] of another" (P01-004). Hubris is the act of exceeding that portion. The [[erinyes|Erinyes]], as enforcers of Dikê, can be "roused by any *Hubris* or overstepping of [[moira|Moira]]" (P01-005). Their function is not limited to avenging kin-murder; they respond to any violation of the cosmic order.

The natural-world parallel is instructive. Murray observes that the annual decline of the sun's power "is really a punishment due to the *Hubris* which the Sun committed when at his height" (P01-007).

This is not metaphor: in the primitive Greek worldview, the same law that governs human conduct governs the physical universe. The sun at its zenith exceeds its [[moira|Moira]]; its subsequent decline is Dikê in action.

The "rule that blood calls for blood, that *Hubris* goes before a fall, or that sin brings punishment, stands as an unbroken natural law" (P01-007).


## In The Eumenides

Hubris is the engine of the entire [[the-oresteia|Oresteia trilogy]]. [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s sacrifice of [[iphigenia|Iphigenia]] is an act of Hubris; [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]'s murder of [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] is both an act of Hubris and a consequence of Hubris; [[orestes|Orestes]]' murder of [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] is both the fulfillment of divine command and an act of Hubris against the mother-son bond. Each act is simultaneously justified (by the law of the blood-feud) and transgressive (against the bonds it violates).

This is the moral trap from which only [[zeus|Zeus]] can deliver.

The old law — enforced by the [[erinyes|Erinyes]] — can only perpetuate the chain: each act of Hubris calls for vengeance, which itself constitutes a further act of Hubris, which calls for further vengeance, ad infinitum.

Aeschylus' solution is not to deny that Hubris has occurred but to introduce a higher authority capable of breaking the chain.

As Murray puts it, "the mechanical and automatic operation of the Law is corrected by the will of the Father. It is not broken, but more truly and perfectly fulfilled" (P01-012).


The play's resolution is transformative: the [[erinyes|Furies]] are persuaded to become [[erinyes|Eumenides]], and the law they enforce is no longer merely punitive but educative. Hubris is still punished, but the punishment is now embedded within a civic order that can weigh intent and circumstance — the [[areopagus|Areopagus]] — rather than a blind natural mechanism.

## In Greek Thought

Hubris is one of the most persistent concepts in Greek ethical thought, appearing in Homer, Hesiod, the tragedians, the historians, and the philosophers. In Aristotle's *Nicomachean Ethics*, hubris is defined as doing and saying things that cause shame to the victim, not for any advantage to oneself but for the pleasure of asserting superiority. This psychological dimension — hubris as a disposition of character — complements Aeschylus' cosmological understanding.

The pre-Socratic philosopher Anaximander expressed the same intuition in cosmological terms: things "pay penalty and retribution to each other for their injustice according to the ordinance of time." The cosmos itself is a balance of opposing forces, each respecting the boundaries of the others; Hubris is the violation of that balance, and Dikê its restoration.
