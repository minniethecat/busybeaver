# The Chorus of Furies

The Chorus of the [[erinyes|Furies]] is the choral collective of *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]* — the dramatic voice of the [[erinyes|Erinyes]], ancient goddesses of the blood-feud, who pursue [[orestes|Orestes]] from [[delphi|Delphi]] to [[athens|Athens]], perform the terrifying Binding Song, lament their defeat in the trial, and ultimately accept [[athena|Athena]]'s persuasion to become the [[erinyes|Eumenides]], blessing [[athens|Athens]] in the play's final procession.

As the chorus of a Greek tragedy, they are both characters within the drama and the ritual-dramatic voice through which the play's theological argument is enacted.


## Overview

The Furies' Chorus is the most active and dramatically central chorus in the surviving Aeschylean corpus.

Unlike the chorus of *[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]* (elders of [[argos|Argos]], who observe and comment) or *The Libation Bearers* (slave women, who conspire), the [[erinyes|Furies]] are the antagonists of the play — they drive the action, perform ritual songs, and undergo a transformation that is the play's central event.

They enter the stage as terrifying hunters and exit as honored guardians, their choral function shifting from curse to blessing.


## Role in The Eumenides

### The Binding Song (001-039)

The Chorus's first great set-piece is the Binding Song, a ritual incantation performed as they surround [[orestes|Orestes]] at [[athena|Athena]]'s statue in [[athens|Athens]].

The song is both a [[curse|curse]] and a claim of cosmic authority: "But our sacrifice to bind, Lo, the music that we wind, How it dazeth and amazeth And the will it maketh blind, As it moves without a lyre To the throb of my desire" (001-039).

The absence of the lyre — the instrument of [[apollo|Apollo]], of Olympian order — marks the Chorus as [[the-chthonian-people|chthonian]], pre-Olympian, belonging to a world before the ordering of music into harmony.


The Binding Song is a ritual of pursuit and capture. The Chorus dances around [[orestes|Orestes]], tightening their circle, performing the entrapment they intend to enact in reality. It is both theater and magic — a dramatic representation of their cosmic function.

### The Defeat Ode (001-133)

After [[athena|Athena]]'s tie-breaking vote acquits [[orestes|Orestes]], the Chorus erupts into a song of cosmic despair: "Woe on you, woe, ye younger gods! Ye have trampled the great Laws of old Beneath your chariots!" (001-133). This ode is the theological crisis of the play: the [[erinyes|Furies]] experience the verdict not as a legal defeat but as cosmic rupture, the overthrow of the "great Laws of old" by the "younger gods." Their choral form — broken, staccato, repetitive — mirrors the fragmentation of their world.

### The Blessing (001-153)

After [[athena|Athena]]'s persuasion succeeds, the Chorus's final song transforms from curse to blessing. They pray for Athenian fertility, concord, and prosperity — their [[the-chthonian-people|chthonian]] power now directed toward civic flourishing. The ritual function of the chorus is completed: what began as curse-magic ends as civic religion.

## Historical and Mythological Context

In Greek tragedy, the chorus represents the collective voice of the community — elders, women, citizens — whose perspective frames the action.

Aeschylus's innovation in *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]* is to make the chorus both the embodiment of a cosmic principle (the blood-feud) and the dramatic antagonist.

The transformation of the chorus thus enacts the transformation of the principle it represents: the [[transformation-of-the-furies|Furies become Eumenides]], the curse becomes a blessing, and the chorus exits in a torchlight procession that was likely one of the most spectacular stage effects in fifth-century Athenian theater.
