# Chthonian (Underworld Divinity)

The term *chthonian* (Greek: χθόνιος, *chthonios*, "of the earth" or "under the earth") describes deities, spirits, and powers associated with the earth, the underworld, and the dead. In Aeschylus' *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]*, the chthonian realm is the source of the old law — the wisdom of the ancestors, the wrath of the [[erinyes|Erinyes]], and the punitive power of [[mother-earth|Mother Earth]] — that the new Olympian order must acknowledge, respect, and ultimately transform.

## Definition

Greek religion distinguished between *Olympian* deities (associated with the sky, Mount Olympus, and the established order of [[zeus|Zeus]]) and *chthonian* deities (associated with the earth, the underworld, fertility, and the dead). Olympian worship typically involved白天rituals, burnt offerings rising upward, and temples elevated above the ground. Chthonian worship involved nighttime rituals, libations poured into the earth, and shrines at ground level or in caves.

The distinction was not absolute — many deities had both Olympian and chthonian aspects — but it was fundamental to Greek religious practice. [[zeus|Zeus]] himself had chthonian manifestations ([[zeus|Zeus]] *Chthonios*, [[zeus|Zeus]] of the underworld), and the hero cults that were central to Greek civic religion were essentially chthonian in character: the dead hero, buried in the earth, received offerings and in return protected the community.

Murray's Introduction makes the chthonian dimension of the play's theology explicit: "[[mother-earth|Earth]] is the home of the dead, the Chthonian people, both the great ancestors who know what is [[themis|Themis]] and 'the wronged ones in the darkness' who cry for justice on their oppressors" (P01-006).

The ancestors, the [[erinyes|Erinyes]], and [[mother-earth|Mother Earth]] together form the chthonian foundation of the law — ancient, inexorable, and terrifying, but also a source of blessing: "blessing as well as cursing lies in the power of the Chthonian people, the dead, the [[erinyes|Erinyes]], and collectively of [[mother-earth|Mother Earth]]" (P01-006).


## In The Eumenides

The chthonian nature of the [[erinyes|Erinyes]] is established in the play's opening scene. [[pythian-prophetess|The Pythian Prophetess]] describes them as dwelling "in evil night" and feeding "on the great darkness that is Hell" (001-002). They are explicitly identified with the underworld — beings that "god nor devil dares to love" (001-002). [[apollo|Apollo]]'s contempt for them — "Out, ye wild goats unherded! Out, ye drove Accursed" (001-002) — expresses the Olympian disdain for the chthonian powers.

Yet the play's resolution does not reject the chthonian but transforms it. [[athena|Athena]] persuades the [[erinyes|Erinyes]] to accept a permanent home beneath the [[areopagus|Areopagus]] hill — a chthonian location, in the earth, but now integrated into the civic order of [[athens|Athens]]. They become the *[[erinyes|Semnai Theai]]* ("Revered Goddesses"), receiving worship that is recognizably chthonian (libations poured into the earth, nighttime processions) but now directed toward blessing rather than cursing.

This transformation is the play's deepest theological insight: the chthonian is not false or evil but incomplete. The old gods — the [[erinyes|Erinyes]], the dead, the earth — possess real power and real wisdom. What they lack is understanding, mercy, the capacity to learn and to forgive. By integrating them into [[zeus|Zeus]]'s order — by giving them a place under [[athens|Athens]], not banishing them — [[athena|Athena]] creates a world in which "the Law that can pardon and understand can itself be understood and loved" (P01-018).

## In Greek Thought

The Olympian/chthonian distinction has been central to modern scholarship on Greek religion, though the Greeks themselves did not systematize it as rigorously as scholars once assumed. Walter Burkert's *Greek Religion* treats the distinction as a "polarity" rather than a rigid taxonomy, and recent scholarship emphasizes the fluidity and overlap between the two spheres.

What Aeschylus dramatizes, however, is not scholarly taxonomy but a lived theological drama.

The Olympian gods of Homer — anthropomorphic, sky-dwelling, politically organized — represented a relatively recent development in Greek religious history, superimposed upon an older stratum of earth-based, ancestral, and chthonian cults.

The *Oresteia* can be read as mythologizing this historical process: the Olympian order does not destroy the chthonian but incorporates it, transforming its destructive power into a force for civic blessing.
