# Artemis

**Artemis** (Greek: Ἄρτεμις) is the goddess of the hunt, wild animals, childbirth, and virginity. Though she does not appear directly in [[the-eumenides|The Eumenides]], her role in the backstory of the [[the-oresteia|Oresteia]] trilogy is crucial.

## In the Oresteia Backstory

As Gilbert Murray's Introduction explains, the prophet [[calchas|Calchas]] in the *[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]* describes how two eagles — representing [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] and [[menelaus|Menelaus]] — feasted ravenously on a pregnant hare. [[artemis|Artemis]], as protectress of wild creatures, was enraged by this sacrilege. Her wrath fell upon the Greek fleet at [[aulis|Aulis]], preventing the winds from carrying the ships to [[troy|Troy]]. (P01-008)

> The prophet Calchas has been describing the ravenous feast of the two Eagles; the wrath of Artemis thereat and the vengeance exacted in the death of Iphigenia; the future vengeance to be exacted for that death; and beyond a yet further vista of vengeances re-avenged.
>
> (P01-008)

The price of Artemis' forgiveness was the sacrifice of [[iphigenia|Iphigenia]], [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s daughter. [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]], torn between his duties as a king and as a father, chose to sacrifice [[iphigenia|Iphigenia]] — a decision that sets in motion the entire chain of vengeance that drives the trilogy.

## The Chain of Vengeance

Artemis' demand for [[iphigenia|Iphigenia]]'s sacrifice is the first link in the chain:
1. Artemis demands [[iphigenia|Iphigenia]]'s death
2. [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] sacrifices [[iphigenia|Iphigenia]]
3. [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] kills [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] in revenge
4. [[orestes|Orestes]] kills [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] in revenge
5. The [[erinyes|Furies]] pursue [[orestes|Orestes]]

Thus the goddess of the hunt becomes, paradoxically, the hunter who sets the entire tragic cycle in motion.

## Aeschylean Theology

The mention of Artemis in the Oresteia reflects Aeschylus' complex theology. The gods, even the Olympians, are not always just by human standards. Artemis' demand is harsh, even cruel — yet it must be obeyed. This tension between divine command and human morality is one of the deep problems that *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]* ultimately resolves through [[athena|Athena]]'s institution of the [[the-areopagus-court|Areopagus Court]] and the appeal from law to a person.
