# The Oresteia

The **Oresteia** (Greek: Ὀρέστεια) is a trilogy of Greek tragedies written by Aeschylus in 458 BCE (002-048). It is the only complete trilogy to survive from ancient Greek theater. The three plays are:

1. **[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]** — The return of King [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] from [[troy|Troy]] and his murder by his wife [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] and her lover [[aegisthus|Aegisthus]]
2. **The Libation Bearers** (Choephoroe) — [[orestes|Orestes]] returns from exile and avenges his father by killing his mother and [[aegisthus|Aegisthus]]
3. **[[the-eumenides|The Eumenides]]** — [[orestes|Orestes]] is pursued by the [[erinyes|Furies]] to [[delphi|Delphi]] and [[athens|Athens]], where he is tried and acquitted

## Thematic Arc

The trilogy traces a grand movement from the primitive law of the [[the-blood-feud|blood-feud]] to the establishment of civic justice. In the *[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]*, the law of "the doer must suffer" operates with mechanical inevitability. In the *Choephoroe*, [[orestes|Orestes]] obeys the same law but is destroyed by it. In *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]*, the cycle is broken through the intervention of [[athena|Athena]] and the establishment of the [[the-areopagus-court|Areopagus Court]].

## Murray's Introduction

As Gilbert Murray notes in his Introduction to the play, "We have seen in the *[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]* how the law that 'The doer must suffer,' or that Sin must bring Punishment, is mainly envisaged by the most primitive Greek tradition as a form of the blood-feud" (P01-001). The trilogy as a whole dramatizes the [[justice-dike|evolution of Justice]] from automatic retribution to reasoned judgment.

## The Eumenides as Conclusion

*The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]* provides the resolution that the first two plays deny. Where the *[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]* ends with [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] standing over her husband's body and the *Choephoroe* ends with [[orestes|Orestes]] fleeing his mother's [[erinyes|Furies]], *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]* ends with reconciliation — the [[transformation-of-the-furies|transformation of the Furies]] into the [[erinyes|Eumenides]] ("[[erinyes|Kindly Ones]]"), guardians of [[athens|Athens]] and its [[civic-order|civic order]].

## Historical Context

The trilogy was produced in 458 BCE, just a few years after the democratic reforms of [[ephialtes|Ephialtes]] (462 BCE) that transferred powers from the aristocratic [[the-areopagus-court|Areopagus]] Council to the popular courts (002-041). The play's celebration of the [[areopagus|Areopagus]] as a homicide court — rather than a political body — has been read by scholars as Aeschylus' nuanced response to these contemporary political events.

> We have seen in the Agamemnon how the law that “The doer must suffer,” or that Sin must bring Punishment, is mainly envisaged by the most primitive Greek tradition as a form of the blood-feud. It is blood calling for blood.
>
> (P01-001)
