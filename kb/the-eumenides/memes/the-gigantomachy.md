# The Gigantomachy

The **Gigantomachy** (Greek: Γιγαντομαχία, "Battle of the Giants") was the cosmic battle in which the [[olympian-order|Olympian Gods]], led by [[zeus|Zeus]], defeated the Giants ([[mother-earth|Ge]]geneis), earth-born beings who attempted to overthrow the divine order.

## In The Eumenides

[[athena|Athena]] references the Gigantomachy in her speech to the [[erinyes|Furies]], reminding them of their shared history of fighting on the side of the Olympians against the Giants. By invoking this common victory, [[athena|Athena]] seeks to persuade the [[erinyes|Furies]] that they belong not in opposition to the Olympian order but within it — as honored and essential members of the divine community. The battleground of [[phlegra|Phlegra]] was, as the endnotes explain, "the scene of the Battle between the Gods and Giants" (002-024).

## Mythological Significance

The Gigantomachy was one of the defining conflicts of Greek mythology. It followed the Titanomachy (war against the Titans) and represented the final consolidation of Olympian rule. The Giants, born from the blood of Uranus that fell upon [[mother-earth|Gaia]], were monstrous beings of immense strength who sought to avenge the Titans by overthrowing [[zeus|Zeus]] and the Olympians.

A prophecy declared that the Giants could only be defeated with the aid of a mortal. [[zeus|Zeus]] therefore enlisted [[hera|Hera]]cles, and [[athena|Athena]] played a crucial role in the battle — a fact she proudly recalls in the play.

## Artistic Depictions

The Gigantomachy was one of the most popular subjects in Greek art. It appeared on:
- The east metopes of the Parthenon in [[athens|Athens]]
- The Great Altar of [[pergamon|Pergamon]]
- Numerous vase paintings and sculptural groups throughout the Greek world

## Role in the Play's Argument

[[athena|Athena]]'s reference to the Gigantomachy serves a rhetorical purpose. She is reminding the [[erinyes|Furies]] that they have always been allies of the Olympian order, not enemies. The battle against the Giants was a battle for cosmic order itself — and the [[erinyes|Furies]] fought on the right side. Her argument implies: accept incorporation into the new civic order of [[athens|Athens]] just as you once accepted incorporation into the Olympian order.

Murray captures the theological significance of this mythological reference:

> "the world is not ruled by fabulous Typhons and Giants"—nor, we may add, by blind mechanical laws—"but by One who is a wise Father to all."
>
> (P01-015)
