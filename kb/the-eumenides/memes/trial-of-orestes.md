# The Trial of Orestes

The [[trial-of-orestes]] is the central event of Aeschylus' *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]* and the founding myth of the [[the-areopagus-court|Areopagus court]].

Presided over by [[athena|Athena]], with [[apollo|Apollo]] as [[orestes|Orestes]]' advocate and the [[erinyes|Furies]] as prosecutors, the trial acquits [[orestes|Orestes]] of the charge of matricide and — in doing so — transforms the ancient law of the [[the-blood-feud|blood-feud]] into the [[civic-order|civic justice]] of the Athenian polis.

The event marks the historical transition from [[justice-dike|primitive Dikê]] to deliberative law.


## Overview

The trial occupies the third act of *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]*. [[orestes|Orestes]], having been purified by [[apollo|Apollo]] at [[delphi|Delphi]] and guided by [[hermes|Hermes]], has reached [[athens|Athens]] and supplicated at [[athena|Athena]]'s statue on the [[acropolis|Acropolis]]. [[athena|Athena]], returning from [[troy|Troy]], hears his prayer and the [[erinyes|Furies]]' counter-claim, and determines that the case requires a formal trial. She empanels twelve Athenian citizens as jurors, establishing the [[areopagus|Areopagus]] as a permanent court for homicide cases.

The trial proceeds as a formal legal proceeding: the [[erinyes|Furies]] argue that matricide is the unpardonable crime, violating the most sacred bond of kinship. [[apollo|Apollo]] argues that the father's claim is prior to the mother's — that [[orestes|Orestes]] acted justly in avenging [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]], and that the command of [[zeus|Zeus]] legitimizes the act. The jury divides evenly. [[athena|Athena]] casts the deciding vote for acquittal, establishing the principle that a tied vote favors the defendant.

## Context

The trial is the culmination of the entire *Oresteia*. The *[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]* depicted the murder of the king by his wife; the *Choëphoroe* depicted [[orestes|Orestes]]' revenge-killing of [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] and [[aegisthus|Aegisthus]]. *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]* opens with [[orestes|Orestes]] as a polluted fugitive, pursued by the [[erinyes|Furies]]. The trial is the mechanism by which the cycle of vengeance — endlessly perpetuated by the old law of *Dikê* — is finally broken.

## The Event

[[athena|Athena]] establishes the court, hears the arguments of both sides, and casts her deciding vote. [[apollo|Apollo]] serves as [[orestes|Orestes]]' advocate (and, notably, as a witness to the matricide, since he commanded it). The [[erinyes|Furies]] serve as prosecutors, arguing that the ancient law cannot be set aside. The twelve [[athenian-citizens|Athenian jurors]] cast the first votes in the first jury trial — the founding act of citizen justice.

## Aftermath

[[orestes|Orestes]] is acquitted and returns to [[argos|Argos]], freed from the [[erinyes|Furies]]' pursuit. But the trial's more significant aftermath is institutional: the [[founding-of-the-areopagus|Areopagus is established]] as a permanent court, and the [[erinyes|Furies]] — initially enraged at their defeat — are persuaded by [[athena|Athena]] to accept a new role as the [[the-eumenides]], guardians of [[athens|Athens]]. The cosmic order is reconfigured: the mechanical law yields to a justice that "can understand and therefore sympathize" [Murray, Introduction].

## In The Eumenides

The trial is the play's dramatic and theological climax. Murray observes: "the acquittal of [[orestes|Orestes]] does not depend on the arguments used in the trial, but on the Will of [[zeus|Zeus]], which is an ultimate fact not dependent on argument" [Murray, Introduction]. The trial's purpose is not to determine guilt — [[apollo|Apollo]]'s command already established [[orestes|Orestes]]' moral position — but to create the institution through which divine will becomes civic justice.

## Key Passages

> The interest lies in the foundation of the Court of the Areopagus, as a tribunal superseding the blood-feud, the ordeal by oath, and all the rigid and unreasoning practices of primitive justice, by a justice which can understand and therefore sympathize.
> [Murray, Introduction]

> For I am utterly the Father's.
> [Athena, Eumenides]

> This Council I appoint, this court of law, Untainted by desire of gain, revered, Quick to strike down, yet quick to bless, a guard Of the land's peace, unsleeping, for the sake Of sleeping men.
