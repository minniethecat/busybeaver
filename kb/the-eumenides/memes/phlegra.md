# Phlegra

**Phlegra** is the mythical site of the Gigantomachy — the great battle between the Olympian gods and the Giants. In *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]*, [[orestes|Orestes]] mentions Phlegra in his prayer to [[athena|Athena]], imagining that she might be "brooding upon Phlegra's field" as a commander in cosmic warfare.

## Mythological Significance

[[the-gigantomachy|The Gigantomachy]] was one of the defining conflicts of Greek mythology, in which the Olympian gods led by [[zeus|Zeus]] defeated the Giants ([[mother-earth|Ge]]geneis), earth-born beings who attempted to overthrow the divine order. [[athena|Athena]] played a crucial role in this battle, and the event was frequently depicted in Greek art — most famously on the east metopes of the Parthenon and the altar of [[pergamon|Pergamon]].

The endnotes observe that Aeschylus "seems to conceive it as a continuous battle, not an incident in the past" (002-024). This is significant: in Aeschylus's conception, the cosmic struggle between order and chaos is ongoing, not a completed historical event. The gods must continually maintain the order they established.

## Role in *The Eumenides*

[[orestes|Orestes]], clinging to [[athena|Athena]]'s statue on the [[areopagus|Areopagus]], calls on the goddess wherever she might be:

> "Or is it Phlegra's field thou brood'st upon, Guiding the storm, like some bold Lord of War, Oh, hear! A goddess heareth though afar" (001-035)

The image of [[athena|Athena]] "guiding the storm" at Phlegra portrays her as a war goddess actively engaged in cosmic battle. This martial characterization creates dramatic irony: the [[athena|Athena]] who arrives is not a warrior on Phlegra's field but a civic founder who will institute the world's first homicide court. The transformation from warrior goddess to judicial founder is one of the play's central movements.

[[orestes|Orestes]] lists Phlegra alongside [[lake-tritonis|Lake Tritonis]] ([[athena|Athena]]'s Libyan birthplace) and the [[scamander-river|Scamander River]] near [[troy|Troy]] — the three locations span the mythological birth, cosmic battle, and historical conquest dimensions of [[athena|Athena]]'s divine identity.

## Geography

Phlegra was traditionally located in the [[pallene|Pallene]] peninsula of [[chalcidice|Chalcidice]] (northern Greece), though some ancient sources place it in Thessaly or Thrace. The name derives from the Greek *phlego* ("to burn"), evoking the fiery nature of the battle.
