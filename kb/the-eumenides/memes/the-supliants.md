# The Suppliant Tradition

The **Suppliant** (Greek: ἱκέτης, *hiketês*) is one who seeks protection through ritual [[supplication|supplication (hikesia)]]. The figure of the suppliant is central to [[the-eumenides|The Eumenides]] — [[orestes|Orestes]] is the archetypal suppliant, and [[zeus|Zeus]] himself is called the Eternal Suppliant. (P01-009)

## Ritual Form

The suppliant approached a sacred place — an altar, a temple, or the hearth of a powerful person — bearing ritual tokens: an olive branch wound with wool. By sitting at the altar and clasping it (or a sacred image), the suppliant placed themselves under divine protection. To harm a suppliant at the altar was to commit sacrilege.

## Zeus the Suppliant

Murray's Introduction makes the striking claim that [[zeus|Zeus]] "is not only the protector of suppliants, he is himself the Eternal Suppliant, the God and Master of all things, who forgives because he also craves for forgiveness." (P01-016)

This provocative theological statement — from Aeschylus' *Supplices* — suggests that the very capacity for mercy arises from shared vulnerability. The god who can forgive is the god who understands what it means to need forgiveness.

> Nay, he is not only the protector of suppliants, he is himself the Eternal Suppliant, the God and Master of all things, who forgives because he also craves for forgiveness.
>
> (P01-009)

## In The Eumenides

[[orestes|Orestes]] arrives at [[delphi|Delphi]] as a suppliant, bearing the ritual tokens described by the [[pythian-prophetess|Pythia]]: "a naked new-drawn brand / Wreathed in beseeching wool, a suppliant's weed / Of snow-white fleece." He then travels to [[athens|Athens]] and supplicates the image of [[athena|Athena]]. The entire action of the play is framed by the ritual grammar of supplication — the claim of the vulnerable upon the powerful. (002-031)
