# Python

**Python** (Greek: Πύθων) was the great serpent or dragon that, according to the traditional Greek myth, guarded the sanctuary at [[delphi|Delphi]] before [[apollo|Apollo]]'s arrival. [[apollo|Apollo]] slew Python and claimed the oracle for himself.

## Aeschylus' Revision

The traditional myth told of [[apollo|Apollo]]'s violent conquest of [[delphi|Delphi]] — his battle with the dragon born of [[mother-earth|Earth]], and [[mother-earth|Earth]]'s anger thereafter. But Aeschylus deliberately departs from this tradition. As the endnotes to the Murray translation explain:

"The ordinary legend told of [[apollo|Apollo]]'s battle with a dragon, born of [[mother-earth|Earth]], and the anger of [[mother-earth|Earth]] thereafter; Aeschylus insists that there was never any strife among the gods at [[delphi|Delphi]]."

In Aeschylus' version, the succession is peaceful: [[mother-earth|Earth]] → [[themis|Themis]] → [[phoebe|Phoebe]] → [[apollo|Apollo]], "with never strife nor stress." Python is erased from the narrative.

## Theological Significance

Aeschylus' suppression of Python is central to the play's argument. By removing the violence from [[delphi|Delphi]]'s foundation, the poet creates a model of peaceful divine succession that prefigures the play's resolution. Just as [[apollo|Apollo]] received the oracle peacefully from [[phoebe|Phoebe]], so the [[erinyes|Furies]] will be peacefully incorporated into the [[olympian-order|Olympian order]] by [[athena|Athena]].
