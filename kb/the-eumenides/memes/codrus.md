# Codrus

**Codrus** (Greek: Κόδρος) was the legendary last king of [[athens|Athens]]. Murray's Introduction to [[the-eumenides|The Eumenides]] mentions him as an example of the sacrificial logic that Aeschylus' theology transcends.

## Murray's Reference

Murray writes that Aeschylus "knew how Codrus died, and Menoikeus and Macaria, how [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] and [[erechtheus|Erechtheus]] and other kings had given their children to die. But for him such practices belonged to that primitive and barbaric world which Hellenic [[zeus|Zeus]] had swept away, so he hoped, forever." (P01-016)

> He knew how Codrus died, and Menoikeus and Macaria, how Agamemnon and Erechtheus and other kings had given their children to die. But for him such practices belonged to that primitive and barbaric world which Hellenic Zeus had swept away, so he hoped, forever.
>

According to legend, the Dorians were told by an oracle that they would conquer [[athens|Athens]] only if they did not kill its king. Codrus, learning of this, disguised himself as a peasant, provoked a Dorian soldier into killing him, and thus saved his city.

## The Logic of Substitutionary Sacrifice

The story of Codrus represents a primitive religious logic: one life must be paid for another. Murray contrasts this with the Aeschylean resolution in *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]*, where [[orestes|Orestes]] is forgiven without any substitute victim being offered. The law is satisfied not by another death but by the establishment of a new kind of justice — the justice of the [[the-areopagus-court|Areopagus Court]].
