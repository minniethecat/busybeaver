# The Olympian Gods

The [[the-olympian-gods|The Olympian Gods]] are the divine order under [[zeus|Zeus]] that, in Aeschylus' *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]*, supersedes and transforms the older [[chthonian|chthonian order]] of [[mother-earth|Earth]], the [[erinyes|Erinyes]], and the ancestral dead.

They are the "younger gods" whom the [[erinyes|Furies]] accuse of breaking the ancient laws — and whose capacity to learn, forgive, and establish institutions is the play's central theological claim.

As Murray notes, the [[olympian-order|Olympian religion]] "brought to man the Good News that...'the world is not ruled by fabulous Typhons and Giants'...'but by One who is a wise Father to all'" (P01-015).


## Role

The Olympian gods operate as a hierarchy under [[zeus|Zeus]]. [[apollo|Apollo]] is the *Prophêtês Dios*, the revealer who speaks [[zeus|Zeus]]'s words. [[athena|Athena]] is "pure, undiluted [[zeus|Zeus]]" (P01-012), whose judgment is identical with his. [[hermes|Hermes]] is the guide who shepherds the suppliant. Together, they execute a divine will that is personal ([[zeus|Zeus]]'s), intelligent ([[athena|Athena]]'s), and effective ([[apollo|Apollo]]'s ritual power, [[hermes|Hermes]]' guidance).

| God | Olympian Role | Function in The [[erinyes|Eumenides]] |
|-----|--------------|--------------------------|
| [[zeus|Zeus]] | Supreme ruler | Source of all authority; never appears directly |
| [[athena|Athena]] | Judge, persuader, founder | Casts deciding vote; persuades [[erinyes|Furies]]; founds [[the-areopagus-court|Areopagus]] |
| [[apollo|Apollo]] | Prophet, purifier, advocate | Purifies [[orestes|Orestes]]; advocates at trial; speaks [[zeus|Zeus]]'s will |
| [[hermes|Hermes]] | Guide, messenger | Shepherds [[orestes|Orestes]] from [[delphi|Delphi]] to [[athens|Athens]] |

## In The Eumenides

The Olympian gods do not appear as a collective body but act through their individual members. [[apollo|Apollo]] purifies and advocates. [[athena|Athena]] judges and persuades. [[hermes|Hermes]] guides. [[zeus|Zeus]] — the supreme authority — never appears but is present in every action: [[apollo|Apollo]] speaks his words, [[athena|Athena]] executes his will.

The [[erinyes|Furies]]' accusation — "These be the deeds ye do, ye Gods of the younger race: Ye break the Law at your will" (001-016) — states the conflict directly. The Olympian response is not denial but transformation: the old law is not broken but fulfilled, and its enforcers (the [[erinyes|Furies]]) are not destroyed but honored. The [[transformation-of-the-furies|transformation]] of the [[erinyes|Furies]] into the [[erinyes|Eumenides]] is the Olympian solution: incorporate rather than eliminate.

Murray emphasizes the novelty of Aeschylus' Olympian theology: the Olympian religion represented a humanizing advance over "the welter of primitive taboos and terrors" (P01-015). The Olympian gods are not merely more powerful than the chthonian powers; they are morally superior because they can understand and forgive — a quality the old law entirely lacks.

## Significance

The Olympian order, as Aeschylus presents it, represents the humanizing of the divine. It is a "Good News" — a theological revolution in which the gods become intelligible, merciful, and institutionally engaged with human communities. The [[the-areopagus-court|Areopagus]] is the earthly expression of this Olympian theology: a court where divine will becomes civic law, and where the [[justice-dike|justice]] that once mechanically demanded blood now can weigh, deliberate, and forgive.
