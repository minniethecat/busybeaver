The oath (Greek ὅρκος, *horkos*) was the decisive instrument of pre-democratic Greek justice: the accused swore innocence, and if he could not swear, he was guilty. In Aeschylus' *[[erinyes|Eumenides]]*, [[athena|Athena]] explicitly rejects this "ordeal by oath" and replaces it with trial by jury — a foundational moment in the mythological history of law.

## Definition

In primitive Greek legal practice, the oath was not evidence but a verdict. "The [[erinyes|Furies]] follow the old ordeal by oath: the only trial permitted to the accused man is that both parties can be made to swear.

If the accused can swear that he did not commit the crime, well and good. If he cannot, he is guilty. This leaves out of account any inquiry into justification or extenuating circumstances or even intention" (002-030).

The oath presumed divine enforcement: a false oath would bring the wrath of the gods upon the swearer.


[[athena|Athena]] condemns this system precisely because it precludes understanding: "Let no bare oath the deeper right subdue" (001-135). An oath can only establish whether an act was committed, not whether it was justified. In [[orestes|Orestes']] case, he freely admits to killing [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] — under the oath system, he would be condemned without any consideration of [[apollo|Apollo]]'s command or [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]'s own crime.

## In The Eumenides

The oath appears throughout the trial as both a procedural mechanism and a theological argument. [[apollo|Apollo]] frames his advocacy in the language of oaths and bonds: "The fate-ordainèd meeting, breast to breast, / Of man and woman is a tie more sure / Than oath or pact, if [[justice-dike|Justice]] guards it pure" (001-099). He argues that the marriage bond of [[hera|Hera]] and [[zeus|Zeus]] is itself a sacred oath that the [[erinyes|Erinyes]] dishonour by pursuing a husband's avenger while ignoring a wife's murderer.

When [[athena|Athena]] establishes the [[the-areopagus-court|Areopagus]], she explicitly replaces the oath-challenge with a new procedure: "I will judges find / In mine own City, who will make no blind / Oath-challenge to pursuer and pursued, / But follow this new rule, by me indued / As law for ever. Proofs and witnesses / Call ye on either side, and set to these / Your oaths" (001-139). The oath is demoted from verdict to testimony — evidence to be weighed, not an automatic determination of guilt.

The judges themselves take an oath before casting their votes: "I charge you, rise / And lift your stones of doom and judge, alway / Your oath remembering" [paraphrase]. But this is a different kind of oath — an oath to judge fairly, not an oath of innocence. It inaugurates the civic oath of the juror rather than the archaic oath of the accused.

[[orestes|Orestes]] himself concludes with a solemn oath of alliance between [[argos|Argos]] and [[athens|Athens]]: "I swear, ere I depart to mine own land, / This oath. No captain of an Argive band / Shall ever against [[athens|Athens]] raise his spear" [paraphrase]. The play thus traces the evolution of the oath from mechanical ordeal to civic covenant.

## In Greek Thought

The transition from oath-based to inquiry-based justice corresponds to the broader Greek movement from archaic to classical institutions. The [[the-areopagus-court|Areopagus court]], as the endnotes observe, "supersed[es] the blood-feud, the ordeal by oath, and all the rigid and unreasoning practices of primitive justice, by a justice which can understand and therefore sympathize" (002-035).

Hesiod's *Theogony* makes Horkos (Oath) a divine being, the child of Eris (Strife), who "brings the most grief to men upon the earth, whenever one willingly swears a false oath." Aeschylus' innovation is not to abolish the oath but to subordinate it to a higher form of inquiry — a movement parallel to the transformation of the [[erinyes|Erinyes]] from pursuers to guardians.
