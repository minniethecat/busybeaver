# Pan

**Pan** (Greek: Πάν) is the god of the wild, shepherds, flocks, and rustic music. He was worshipped at the [[corycian-cave|Corycian Cave]] on [[mount-parnassus|Mount Parnassus]], alongside the [[the-nymphs|Nymphs]] and [[dionysus|Dionysus]].

## In The Eumenides

Pan is not directly named in [[the-eumenides|The Eumenides]], but his presence is implicit in the sacred landscape of [[mount-parnassus|Parnassus]]. The [[corycian-cave|Corycian Cave]] was a shared cult site for the [[the-nymphs|Nymphs]] and Pan, and archaeological evidence — including thousands of votive offerings — confirms the importance of his worship there. The prophetess's reference to the "[[the-nymphs|Nymphs]] who keep yon old Corycian bird-belovèd steep" includes Pan within the circle of Parnassian deities.

## Nature and Worship

Pan was a god of untamed nature — half-man, half-goat — associated with panic, wild music, and the fertile energy of the wilderness. His worship at [[delphi|Delphi]] represents the integration of rustic, pre-Olympian religion into the [[apollo|Apollo]]nian sanctuary. This integration mirrors the play's central theme: the peaceful incorporation of older, chthonian powers into the new Olympian order.
