# Agamemnon

Agamemnon, king of [[argos|Mycenae]] and commander of the Greek forces at [[troy|Troy]], is the absent center of the *Oresteia*. Though dead before *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]* begins, his murder by [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] and the obligation it imposes on his son [[orestes|Orestes]] to avenge him drive the entire trilogy. He is the father whose blood calls for blood — the original victim whose claim sets the chain of vengeance in motion.

## Role in the Play

Agamemnon does not appear in *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]*, but his presence pervades the play. [[apollo|Apollo]]'s command to [[orestes|Orestes]] — to avenge his father by killing his mother — is the act whose consequences the trial must adjudicate. The [[erinyes|Furies]] argue that matricide is the unpardonable crime; [[apollo|Apollo]] argues that the murder of a father — a husband — by his wife is the greater wrong, and that [[orestes|Orestes]] acted justly in avenging it.

Murray frames the moral complexity: "[[aegisthus|Aegisthus]], as son of [[thyestes|Thyestes]], acted quite correctly in killing Agamemnon, and [[orestes|Orestes]] in killing him. Similarly, when [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] killed Agamemnon the eternal law compelled [[orestes|Orestes]] to avenge him, however reluctantly" (P01-001). Agamemnon is at once a victim (murdered by his wife), a perpetrator (he sacrificed their daughter [[iphigenia|Iphigenia]]), and the object of a legitimate claim for vengeance.

His absence from the stage is significant: the dead cannot speak for themselves. Their claims must be voiced by others — by [[apollo|Apollo]], who speaks for the father; by the [[erinyes|Furies]], who speak for the murdered mother. The trial is, in part, a contest over who speaks for the dead.

## Mythological Background

Agamemnon was the son of Atreus (or, in some versions, Pleisthenes) and the brother of [[menelaus|Menelaus]]. As king of [[argos|Mycenae]], he led the Greek expedition against [[troy|Troy]] to recover Helen, [[menelaus|Menelaus]]'s wife. To obtain favorable winds for the fleet at [[aulis|Aulis]], he sacrificed his daughter [[iphigenia|Iphigenia]] to [[artemis|Artemis]] — an act that earned him [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]'s undying hatred.

On his return from [[troy|Troy]], he was murdered by [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] and her lover [[aegisthus|Aegisthus]]. Aeschylus' *Agamemnon*, the first play of the *Oresteia*, depicts this murder in detail: [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] traps him in a net as he bathes and strikes him down. His death is the crime that sets the trilogy's cycle of vengeance in motion.

## Relationships

- **[[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]** (wife): his murderer, whom he wronged by sacrificing their daughter
- **[[orestes|Orestes]]** (son): his avenger, commanded by [[apollo|Apollo]] to kill [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]
- **[[iphigenia|Iphigenia]]** (daughter): sacrificed by Agamemnon for favorable winds at [[aulis|Aulis]]
- **[[menelaus|Menelaus]]** (brother): his partner in the [[the-trojan-war|Trojan War]]
- **[[aegisthus|Aegisthus]]** (cousin): his usurper and [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]'s lover

## Significance in Aeschylean Thought

Agamemnon represents the original wrong — or rather, the chain of wrongs — that the old law of *Dikê* can only perpetuate. He sacrificed [[iphigenia|Iphigenia]] (wrong), so [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] killed him (wrong), so [[orestes|Orestes]] must kill [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] (wrong). Each act is both justified (by the blood-feud) and criminal (by the bonds it violates). Only the new justice of [[zeus|Zeus]], administered by [[athena|Athena]] at the [[areopagus|Areopagus]], can break this chain.
