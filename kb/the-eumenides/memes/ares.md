# Ares

[[ares]] is the Olympian god of war, son of [[zeus|Zeus]] and [[hera|Hera]], and — in Aeschylus' *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]* — the divine namesake of the [[areopagus-hill|Areopagus]] ("[[areopagus-hill|Hill of Ares]]"), where [[athena|Athena]] founds the homicide court. Though Ares does not appear as a character in the play, his name and mythological presence are woven into the sacred geography of Athenian justice.

## Role in the Play

Ares enters *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]* through etymology and topography. [[athena|Athena]], in her founding speech for the [[the-areopagus-court|Areopagus court]], explains the origin of the hill's name: "Here in the Amazons' most virgin hold, Who came in wrath for [[theseus|Theseus]]' wrongs of old Embattled, and this fortress against ours, Hill against hill, towers against soaring towers, Built, and to Ares on the rock with flame Gave sacrifice: whence comes its awful name, The Rock, the Mount, of Ares" (001-077).

The myth [[athena|Athena]] recounts — the Amazons sacrificing to Ares on the hill during their war against [[theseus|Theseus]] — establishes the [[areopagus|Areopagus]] as a site of ancient martial sanctity, predating and legitimizing its later civic function. The god of war presides, in name, over the institution that ends the blood-feud — a paradoxical but characteristically Aeschylean synthesis.

In the play's final blessing, the transformed [[erinyes|Furies]] invoke Ares alongside [[zeus|Zeus]] as a guardian of [[athens|Athens]]: "The Fort of Heaven, which [[zeus|Zeus]] divine And faithful Ares hold in sway, A shining loveliness to enfold The altars of the gods of old" (001-077). Ares and [[zeus|Zeus]] together hold the city's divine fortress, integrating the god of war into the civic order.

## Mythological Background

Ares was one of the twelve Olympian gods, the personification of war's violence and chaos. Unlike [[athena|Athena]], who represented strategic warfare and wisdom in battle, Ares embodied war's destructive fury. In Homer, he is often depicted as brutal and somewhat contemptible; [[zeus|Zeus]] tells him in the *Iliad* that he is "most hateful" of all the Olympians.

Aeschylus' treatment of Ares in *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]* is notably different. By associating Ares with the [[areopagus-hill|Areopagus]] — an institution of deliberative justice — the playwright domesticates the war god, making him a guardian of civic order rather than its destroyer. The hill where Amazons once sacrificed to Ares becomes the seat of measured, lawful judgment.

## Relationship to the Areopagus

The [[areopagus-hill|Areopagus]] takes its name from Ares ([[areopagus-hill|Areios Pagos]] = "[[areopagus-hill|Hill of Ares]]"). In Athenian tradition, the hill was the site of Ares' trial for the murder of Halirrhothius, son of [[poseidon|Poseidon]] — a myth that, like Aeschylus' narrative of [[orestes|Orestes]]' trial, establishes the hill as the primordial site of homicide adjudication. By grounding the court's authority in Ares' name, Aeschylus connects his foundation narrative to an older layer of Athenian sacred topography.
