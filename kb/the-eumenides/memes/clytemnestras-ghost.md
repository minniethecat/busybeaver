# The Appearance of Clytemnestra's Ghost

The Appearance of Clytemnestra's Ghost is the dramatic turning-point in the opening scene of Aeschylus' *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]*: the ghost of the murdered queen appears to the sleeping [[erinyes|Furies]] at [[delphi|Delphi]], rebuking them for allowing [[orestes|Orestes]] to escape, and goading them to resume their relentless pursuit. It is one of the most visceral ghost-scenes in Greek tragedy, in which the dead speak directly to their supernatural avengers and set the play's central conflict in motion.

## Overview

The scene opens after [[apollo|Apollo]] has instructed [[orestes|Orestes]] to flee to [[athens|Athens]] and placed the [[erinyes|Furies]] into a magical sleep (001-005). The stage is empty but for the sleeping [[erinyes|Furies]]. Enter the ghost of [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] — visible only to the [[erinyes|Furies]] and the audience — who delivers an extended rebuke, showing the wound in her throat and shaming the [[erinyes|Furies]] for their slumber. Her appearance and goading trigger the [[erinyes|Furies]]' waking and the resumption of their hunt, reversing the brief moment of [[orestes|Orestes]]' escape.

## Context

[[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] was murdered by her son [[orestes|Orestes]], in revenge for her murder of his father [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]].

In the world of archaic Greek justice, the unavenged dead suffer perpetual dishonor — wandering in shame among the shades, hissed at and rebuked.

[[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]'s ghost makes this explicit: "'Tis you, have left me among all the dead Dishonoured. Alway, for that blood I shed, Rebuke and hissing cease not, and I go Wandering in shame" (001-006).

The [[erinyes|Furies]], as enforcers of the blood-feud, have allowed [[orestes|Orestes]] to escape while asleep — a betrayal of their cosmic function and a personal injury to [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]].


## The Event

### The Ghost's Accusation

[[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]'s speech is a masterwork of rhetorical inversion: she, the murderer, presents herself as the wronged party and the [[erinyes|Furies]] as her betrayers.

She reminds them of the sacrifices she offered them in life — "Oft of my bounty ye have lapt your fill; Oft the sad peace of wineless cups to earth I have poured, and midmurk feastings on your hearth Burned" (001-006) — and contrasts this with their current inaction: "And all these things ye have cast beneath your feet, And he is fled, fled lightly like a fawn Out of your nets!" (001-006).


### The Wound

The stage direction — "She shows the wound in her throat" (001-006) — makes the violence physically present on stage. The wound is both accusation and evidence: the [[erinyes|Furies]] are supposed to pursue those who shed kindred blood, yet the visible sign of that blood-shedding is paraded before their sleeping eyes while they do nothing.

### The Goading

[[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] shifts from rebuke to provocation: "Thou feelst my scorn? Aye, feel and agonize Within; such words are scourges to the wise. Thy blood-mist fold about him, like a doom. Waste him with vapour from thy burning womb. A second chase is death!" (001-006). The ghost does not merely ask for vengeance — she whips the [[erinyes|Furies]] into a frenzy, turning their sleep into torment until they wake.

## Aftermath

The ghost vanishes as the [[erinyes|Furies]] gradually wake, crying out in their sleep before rising. Their waking — treated in the separate event [[waking-of-the-furies]] — leads directly to the discovery of [[orestes|Orestes]]' escape, [[apollo|Apollo]]'s expulsion of them from [[delphi|Delphi]], and their renewed pursuit to [[athens|Athens]]. The ghost scene thus serves as the dramatic pivot that transforms a moment of potential resolution ([[orestes|Orestes]] cleansed at [[delphi|Delphi]]) into renewed crisis.

## In The Eumenides

The [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] ghost scene is unique in Aeschylean drama.

Unlike the ghost of Darius in *The Persians* (who rises from the dead through ritual invocation), [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]'s ghost appears unbidden, visible only to the chthonic [[erinyes|Furies]] and the audience.

The scene bridges the world of the dead (where [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] suffers dishonor) and the world of the living (where [[orestes|Orestes]] flees), making the [[erinyes|Furies]] — beings who belong to both realms — the pivot point.

The ghost's rhetoric also prefigures the play's central tension: the clash between the old law (blood for blood, enforced by the [[erinyes|Furies]]) and the new (justice through trial, embodied by [[apollo|Apollo]] and [[athena|Athena]]).
