# Peitho (Persuasion)

**Peitho** (Greek: Πειθώ) is both a minor goddess and a central principle in Greek culture: the power of persuasion through speech. In [[the-eumenides|The Eumenides]], Peitho is the means by which [[athena|Athena]] resolves the conflict with the [[erinyes|Furies]].

## Persuasion vs. Force

[[athena|Athena]]'s strategy is distinctive: she does not command the [[erinyes|Furies]] to yield; she persuades them. She appeals to their honor, offers them a permanent home in [[athens|Athens]], and promises them eternal worship. The [[erinyes|Furies]] are not conquered — they consent.

This is the play's deepest political argument: legitimate order is established through persuasion, not force. The [[erinyes|Furies]]' consent legitimizes the new [[civic-order|civic order]] in a way that their mere submission never could. (002-044)

## Persuasion and Democracy

[[athena|Athena]]'s use of Peitho prefigures the democratic ideal of governance through reasoned debate rather than violence. The [[the-areopagus-court|Areopagus Court]] itself is an institution of persuasion: both sides are heard, evidence is weighed, and judgment is rendered through deliberation. The play thus celebrates Peitho as the foundation of civilized politics.

## Athena and Peitho

[[the-furies-chorus|The Chorus of Furies]] acknowledges the power of [[athena|Athena]]'s persuasion:

"For my part, I will trust in Persuasion's power, / For thou hast spoken, and the iron flower / Of anger melts."

This transformation — from rage to acceptance through speech — is the dramatic climax of the play's argument for the civilizing power of persuasion.
