# The Council of the Areopagus

The Council of the [[areopagus|Areopagus]] is the body of twelve Athenian citizens whom [[athena|Athena]] selects and empanels to judge [[orestes|Orestes]] in *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]*. They are "the noblest of the breed of [[athens|Athens]]" (001-060), chosen by the goddess to constitute the first homicide court in human history. The Council represents the human component of divine justice: [[zeus|Zeus]]'s will is executed through [[athena|Athena]], but the actual act of judgment — the weighing of evidence, the casting of stones — is performed by mortal men.

## Overview

[[athena|Athena]] announces the Council's formation in her founding speech: "Myself not judging, I will judges find In mine own City, who will make no blind Oath-challenge to pursuer and pursued, But follow this new rule, by me indued As law for ever" (001-060). The key word is "new rule" — the Council is not merely a jury but the institutional embodiment of a new form of justice. Where the old law required oaths and ordeals, the new law requires deliberation and judgment by peers.

## Role in The Eumenides

The Council's role is both procedural and symbolic.

Procedurally, the twelve jurors hear arguments from [[apollo|Apollo]] (for [[orestes|Orestes]]) and the [[erinyes|Furies]] (for [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]), then cast their stones into two urns — one for guilt, one for acquittal.

The votes are equal (six to six), and [[athena|Athena]]'s tie-breaking vote acquits [[orestes|Orestes]].

[[athena|Athena]]'s announcement of the tie-breaking rule — "if equal be The votes ye cast, [[orestes|Orestes]] shall go free" (001-060) — establishes the principle of mercy as a structural feature of the court, not merely a contingent outcome.


Symbolically, the Council represents the transition from divine fiat to human deliberation. [[athena|Athena]] refuses to judge alone: "This is a mystery graver to decide Than mortal dreameth. Nor for me 'twere good To sift the passionate punishments of blood" (001-060). By delegating judgment to mortal men, she creates an institution that can persist beyond any single divine act — a court that will judge all future homicides, generation after generation.

The Council's composition — twelve citizens, "the noblest of the breed" — emphasizes that justice requires both divine charter and human virtue. The [[areopagus|Areopagus]] is not a theocracy; it is a civic institution under divine patronage.

## Historical and Mythological Context

The historical [[areopagus|Areopagus]] Council was composed of ex-archons (former chief magistrates) who served for life.

It held jurisdiction over homicide cases and certain religious offenses, and before the democratic reforms of [[ephialtes|Ephialtes]] (462 BCE), it exercised broad political oversight.

Aeschylus's dramatic founding of the court in the mythic past would have resonated deeply with his Athenian audience: the play was produced in 458 BCE, just four years after [[ephialtes|Ephialtes]]' reforms, and the play's defense of the [[areopagus|Areopagus]]'s judicial role has been read as a conservative response to democratic challenges to the court's authority.


The Play's endnotes confirm the historical context: "The [[areopagus|Areopagus]] is celebrated by Aeschylus as a homicide court, just after its political powers had been abolished by the radical democracy" [Murray, Introduction].
