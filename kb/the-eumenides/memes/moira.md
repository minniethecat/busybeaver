# Moira (Fate / Allotted Portion)

*Moira* (Greek: Μοῖρα) is the "portion" allotted to every person, god, or city — the boundary of one's proper sphere that must be neither abandoned nor exceeded. In Aeschylus' *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]*, Moira is one of the three pillars (alongside [[themis|Themis]] and the [[erinyes|Erinyes]]) that uphold the law of *Dikê*, and its transgression — *[[hubris|Hubris]]* — is the act that calls down punishment.

## Definition

Murray translates Moira as "Fate" but immediately qualifies: "more strictly it is the 'portion' allotted to every man, god, or city.

Each of us must fulfil his portion; he cannot escape it: he must not exceed it nor trespass on the Moira of another" (P01-004).

This is a profoundly relational concept: Moira defines not only what one is entitled to but also the limits beyond which one encroaches on others.

The Moirai, when personified, are "the Assigners or Apportioners of man's lot in life" (P01-004).


The concept has a natural affinity with Plato's vision of justice in the *Republic*, where "every man fulfils his 'portion' of service to the community" (P01-004). In both formulations, justice is not abstract equality but proper differentiation — each element of the whole performing its assigned function without overreaching.

## In The Eumenides

Moira operates throughout *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]* as the invisible architecture of the moral universe. When [[orestes|Orestes]] kills [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]], he acts within his Moira as his father's avenger, yet simultaneously transgresses the Moira of a son toward his mother. This double bind is the predicament that the old, mechanical *Dikê* cannot resolve — and the reason a higher justice, administered by [[athena|Athena]] with the wisdom of [[zeus|Zeus]], is necessary.

The [[erinyes|Erinyes]] themselves acknowledge the binding force of Moira. In the Iliad, Murray notes, when [[achilles|Achilles]]' horse miraculously speaks to warn his master of approaching death, "the [[erinyes|Erinyes]] 'check his voice'" because "the horse, however well-intentioned, was obviously transgressing his Moira" (P01-005). This episode — and the [[erinyes|Erinyes]]' role in it — demonstrates that Moira is not merely a human constraint but a cosmic one, applying to animals, nature, and even the gods themselves.

Murray's analysis also reveals that Moira is dynamic rather than static: "the growth and diminishing every year of the power of the Sun" is a "punishment due to the *[[hubris|Hubris]]* which the Sun committed when at his height" (P01-007). Even the sun has its Moira, and its seasonal decline is a correction of its summer excess. This naturalization of divine justice — making punishment as inevitable as the seasons — is central to Aeschylus' worldview.

## In Greek Thought

The Moirai (Fates) appear throughout Greek literature, most famously as the three sisters — Clotho (the spinner), Lachesis (the allotter), and Atropos (the inevitable) — who control the thread of each mortal life. In Homer, even [[zeus|Zeus]] cannot contravene the Moirai; when his son Sarpedon is fated to die at [[troy|Troy]], [[zeus|Zeus]] can only weigh the consequences of intervention before reluctantly accepting the decree of fate.

Aeschylus' treatment of Moira is more theological than Homer's.

By making Moira one element of a larger system — alongside [[themis|Themis]] and the [[erinyes|Erinyes]], all ultimately subordinate to the learning-and-forgiving [[zeus|Zeus]] — he transforms fate from an arbitrary external constraint into part of a coherent moral order.

The question the play asks is not whether Moira binds (it does) but whether the binding itself can be understood as benevolent, as the expression of a divine Father's will rather than a blind mechanical process.


## Key Passages

> The law that "The doer shall suffer" is a natural law like the maturing of seed, or the return of spring; most of all like the growth and diminishing every year of the power of the Sun.
> (P01-007)
