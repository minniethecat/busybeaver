# Learning by Suffering (Pathei Mathos)

"Learning by Suffering" (*pathei mathos*, Greek: πάθει μάθος) is the Aeschylean doctrine — articulated in the *[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]* and central to the theology of *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]* — that [[zeus|Zeus]] "made a road to Thought" and "established Learning by Suffering to be an abiding law." It is the principle that enables [[zeus|Zeus]] to forgive: because he has learned through suffering, he can understand the sinner, and because he understands, he can forgive.

## Definition

Murray's Introduction presents this doctrine as the theological heart of the *Oresteia*. In the *[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]*, after describing the endless chain of vengeance — each act of blood calling for further blood — Aeschylus asks how humanity can escape. His answer: "Only in the thought of [[zeus|Zeus]], whatever [[zeus|Zeus]] may be." The [[zeus|Zeus]] of Aeschylus is "He who made a road to Thought, who established Learning by Suffering to be an abiding law" (P01-008).

Crucially, this is not a law that applies only to humans. [[zeus|Zeus]] himself "in the distant past won his throne by violence, but now he has learned and his heart is changed" (P01-008).

[[zeus|Zeus]] is not intrinsically perfect; he becomes perfect through learning — and what he learns comes through suffering, through the experience of his own offenses and their consequences.

Murray develops this through the *[[prometheus|Prometheus]]* trilogy, where [[zeus|Zeus]] "beginning as a conqueror and a tyrant, seems at first like the villain of the piece.

But he possesses this peculiar secret: he can learn by his own offences; so the end is reconciliation" (P01-009).


## In The Eumenides

[[zeus|Zeus]] does not appear on stage in *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]*, but the doctrine of *pathei mathos* governs the play's entire action. [[apollo|Apollo]] and [[athena|Athena]] act as [[zeus|Zeus]]'s agents, and their wisdom — [[apollo|Apollo]]'s prophetic knowledge, [[athena|Athena]]'s judicial intelligence — is the expression of [[zeus|Zeus]]'s own learning. As [[athena|Athena]] states, [[zeus|Zeus]] has given her "that power of thinking and understanding (*τὸ φρονεῖν*) to which we were told in the *[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]* that he was guiding mankind" (P01-012).

The trial of [[orestes|Orestes]] is both a test and a demonstration of the doctrine. [[orestes|Orestes]] has suffered — pursued by the [[erinyes|Furies]] across the earth, an outcast from human community — and through that suffering has reached [[athens|Athens]] as a suppliant, purified and repentant. [[athena|Athena]]'s verdict does not deny that he killed his mother; it acknowledges the act and the suffering it entailed, and through that acknowledgment arrives at forgiveness.

The [[erinyes|Furies]], by contrast, cannot learn because they cannot acknowledge suffering as anything other than a demand for punishment. Their transformation into [[erinyes|Eumenides]] is itself an act of *pathei mathos*: they learn, through [[athena|Athena]]'s persuasion, that their role can be one of blessing rather than cursing, and this learning changes their nature.

## In Greek Thought

The idea that suffering teaches wisdom is common in Greek literature — from Homer's [[achilles|Achilles]], who learns compassion through the loss of Patroclus, to Sophocles' Oedipus, who gains insight only through blindness and exile. What distinguishes Aeschylus is his application of the principle to the divine realm. The gods themselves — even [[zeus|Zeus]] — are subject to *pathei mathos*, and their capacity to learn is what makes them worthy of worship.

Later Greek philosophy developed related ideas: Plato's concept of philosophical education as a turning of the soul toward the Good, achieved through the painful recognition of one's own ignorance; Aristotle's theory of tragic *katharsis*, in which the audience learns through the pity and fear aroused by the hero's suffering. Both can be seen as intellectual descendants of Aeschylus' *pathei mathos*.
