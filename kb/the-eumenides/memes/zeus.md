# Zeus

Zeus, the supreme god of the Greek pantheon, never appears on stage in *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]*, yet he is the theological center around which the entire play revolves. Aeschylus presents a Zeus radically transformed from the Homeric king of gods: a deity who "made a road to Thought," who learned through his own offenses how to forgive, and whose will — executed through [[apollo|Apollo]] and [[athena|Athena]] — corrects the mechanical law of *Dikê* without abolishing it.

## Role in the Play

Zeus's absence from the stage is itself meaningful. As Murray explains, "The Greek convention, like our own, did not easily represent the Supreme Father in bodily form on the stage" (P01-010).

Instead, Zeus is represented by "his son and daughter, [[apollo|Apollo]] and [[athena|Athena]]" (P01-010).

[[apollo|Apollo]] is the *Prophêtês Dios* — "the revealer of Zeus" — who declares: "Never have I spoken on my throne of prophecy any word concerning man, woman or city, which was not commanded by Zeus the Father" (P01-011).

[[athena|Athena]] is "pure, undiluted Zeus" (P01-012), born from his head alone, whose judgment is identical with his.


Zeus's will operates at every level of the play's action. It was by his command that [[orestes|Orestes]] killed [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]; it is by his will that [[orestes|Orestes]] is now to be set free. The trial before the [[areopagus|Areopagus]] does not determine [[orestes|Orestes]]' guilt independently of Zeus; rather, it is the mechanism through which Zeus's will becomes effective in the world of human institutions.

## Mythological Background

In the Homeric tradition, Zeus is the king of gods and men, the wielder of the thunderbolt, the enforcer of oaths and hospitality.

He is powerful, majestic, and often arbitrary — a glorified Achaean chieftain projected onto the cosmic scale. Aeschylus' Zeus is different.

Murray traces the development: in the *[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]*, Zeus is "He who made a road to Thought, who established [[learning-by-suffering|Learning by Suffering]] to be an abiding law" (P01-008).

In the *[[prometheus|Prometheus]]* trilogy, Zeus "beginning as a conqueror and a tyrant, seems at first like the villain of the piece.

But he possesses this peculiar secret: he can learn by his own offences; so the end is reconciliation" (P01-009).


This Zeus is not intrinsically perfect but perfectible — a god who grows, learns, and becomes worthy of worship through the same process of suffering and understanding that he imposes on humanity. He "is not only the protector of suppliants, he is himself the Eternal Suppliant, the God and Master of all things, who forgives because he also craves for forgiveness" (P01-009).

## Relationships

- **[[apollo|Apollo]]** (son): the *Prophêtês Dios*, the revealer who speaks Zeus's words
- **[[athena|Athena]]** (daughter): "pure, undiluted Zeus," his thought made manifest
- **[[hermes|Hermes]]** (son): the guide who shepherds [[orestes|Orestes]] to [[athens|Athens]]
- **The [[erinyes|Erinyes]]**: the old powers whose law Zeus corrects but does not destroy
- **[[orestes|Orestes]]**: the suppliant whose acquittal expresses Zeus's will

## Key Passages

> Zeus is "He who made a road to Thought, who established Learning by Suffering to be an abiding law."
> (P01-008)

> The Zeus who learns and understands is also the Zeus who can forgive the sinner. He can forgive just because he understands.
> (P01-009)

## Significance in Aeschylean Thought

Zeus is the answer to the problem the *Oresteia* poses. The old law — the blood-feud, the [[erinyes|Erinyes]], the mechanical operation of *Dikê* — is incapable of breaking the chain of vengeance. Only a personal God, one who can learn and forgive, can do that. Aeschylus' Zeus is that God: the Father whose understanding corrects the law, whose forgiveness liberates the sinner, and whose will founds the institutions — the [[areopagus|Areopagus]] — through which justice becomes civic rather than mechanical.
