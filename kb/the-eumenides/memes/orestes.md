# Orestes

Orestes, son of [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] and [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]], is the central figure of Aeschylus' *Oresteia* and the protagonist of *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]*. Having killed his mother to avenge his father — acting on the command of [[apollo|Apollo]] and the will of [[zeus|Zeus]] — he arrives at [[delphi|Delphi]] as a polluted suppliant, pursued by the [[erinyes|Furies]], seeking [[purification|purification]] and ultimately justice before the [[areopagus|Areopagus]].

## Role in the Play

Orestes appears in *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]* as a suppliant from the opening scene.

[[pythian-prophetess|The Pythian Prophetess]] discovers him at the central altar of [[apollo|Apollo]]'s temple at [[delphi|Delphi]]: "a man abhorred of God, his body hurled [[mother-earth|Earth]]ward in desperate prayer; blood on his hand yet reeking, and a naked new-drawn brand wreathed in beseeching wool, a suppliant's weed of snow-white fleece" (001-001).

He is at once pitiable and terrifying — a murderer whose act was divinely commanded, a suppliant entitled to protection, a source of pollution that contaminates the sacred space.


[[apollo|Apollo]]'s instruction to Orestes — "fly thou and falter not. For these shall hunt thee, ever on through earth Unwandered, through the vast lands of the North, The sea-ways and the cities ringed with sea" (001-002) — sends him on a journey from [[delphi|Delphi]] to [[athens|Athens]]. This journey is both geographical and theological: from [[apollo|Apollo]]'s ritual [[purification|purification]] to [[athena|Athena]]'s civic justice, from the old order of the blood-feud to the new order of the [[areopagus|Areopagus]].

At the trial, Orestes speaks only briefly (his case is argued by [[apollo|Apollo]]), but his presence — the polluted suppliant clinging to [[athena|Athena]]'s statue — is the human center of the legal and theological debate. His acquittal, enabled by [[athena|Athena]]'s deciding vote, is both a personal deliverance and a cosmic event: the moment when the mechanical law of *Dikê* yields to the higher justice of [[zeus|Zeus]].

## Mythological Background

Orestes belongs to the [[argos|House of Atreus]], the most cursed dynasty in Greek mythology. His father [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]], king of [[argos|Mycenae]], sacrificed his daughter [[iphigenia|Iphigenia]] to obtain favorable winds for the [[the-trojan-war|Trojan War]]. His mother [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]], with her lover [[aegisthus|Aegisthus]], murdered [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] on his return from [[troy|Troy]]. Orestes, commanded by [[apollo|Apollo]], avenged his father by killing his mother — an act that the *Choëphoroe* (*Libation Bearers*), the second play of the *Oresteia*, depicts in full.

The story of Orestes was treated by all three major tragedians: Aeschylus in the *Oresteia* (458 BCE), Sophocles in his *[[electra|Electra]]*, and Euripides in his *[[electra|Electra]]* and *Orestes*. Each version makes different choices about Orestes' culpability, the role of the gods, and the nature of the resolution. Aeschylus' version is the most theologically ambitious, transforming a story of familial vengeance into a founding myth of Athenian civic justice.

## Relationships

- **[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]** (father): murdered by [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]; his death is the wrong Orestes must avenge
- **[[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]** (mother): killed by Orestes at [[apollo|Apollo]]'s command; her ghost appears in *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]* to rouse the sleeping [[erinyes|Furies]]
- **[[apollo|Apollo]]**: divine patron and protector; commands the matricide, purifies Orestes at [[delphi|Delphi]], and serves as his advocate at the trial
- **[[athena|Athena]]**: divine judge who founds the [[areopagus|Areopagus]] and casts the deciding vote for acquittal
- **The [[erinyes|Furies]] ([[erinyes|Erinyes]])**: the pursuers; transformed by [[athena|Athena]] into the [[erinyes|Eumenides]], guardians of [[athens|Athens]]

## Significance in Aeschylean Thought

Orestes is the human vessel through whom Aeschylus works out his central theological argument.

His predicament — commanded by one divine authority ([[apollo|Apollo]]/[[zeus|Zeus]]) to commit an act that violates another divine law (the prohibition against matricide enforced by the [[erinyes|Furies]]) — exposes the inadequacy of a purely mechanical justice.

The resolution of his case — acquittal through [[athena|Athena]]'s wisdom, accepted by the [[erinyes|Furies]]' transformation — demonstrates that the law can be fulfilled more perfectly through understanding and forgiveness than through endless retribution.


## In Later Tradition

The story of Orestes continued to fascinate Greek and Roman writers. Euripides' *Orestes* (408 BCE) explores the psychological aftermath of the matricide; his *[[iphigenia|Iphigenia]] in [[tauris|Tauris]]* depicts Orestes' later adventures retrieving a sacred statue of [[artemis|Artemis]]. In Roman literature, Ovid and Seneca both treat the story, and the character became a standard example in rhetorical exercises (*controversiae*) about justified homicide.
