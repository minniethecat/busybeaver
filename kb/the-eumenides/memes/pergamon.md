# Pergamon

**Pergamon** (Greek: Πέργαμον; Latin: Pergamum) was an ancient Greek city in the region of Mysia in Asia Minor (modern-day Bergama, Turkey). It is mentioned in [[the-eumenides|The Eumenides]] scholarship in connection with the Gigantomachy — the cosmic battle in which the [[olympian-order|Olympian Gods]] defeated the Giants.

## The Gigantomachy Connection

The battle of the [[phlegra|Phlegra]] plain — the Gigantomachy — was one of the defining conflicts of Greek mythology. (002-024) [[athena|Athena]] played a crucial role in this battle, and the event was frequently depicted in Greek art. The most famous artistic representation of the Gigantomachy adorned the Great Altar of Pergamon, built in the 2nd century BCE. Its monumental frieze depicts the Olympian gods, including [[athena|Athena]], locked in combat with the earth-born Giants ([[mother-earth|Ge]]geneis).

## Historical Significance

Pergamon was the capital of the Attalid dynasty and one of the great cultural centers of the Hellenistic world. Its library was said to rival that of Alexandria, and its sculptural school produced some of the most dramatic and dynamic works of Greek art. The Great Altar, now reconstructed in the Pergamon Museum in Berlin, remains one of the most celebrated monuments of antiquity.

## Role in The Eumenides

Pergamon is not directly mentioned in the text of the play, but its altar provides important visual evidence for the Gigantomachy tradition — a myth that [[athena|Athena]] herself references in her speech to the [[erinyes|Furies]] when she speaks of their shared victory over the Giants. The artistic depictions at Pergamon help illuminate the mythological world that Aeschylus' audience would have known.
