# Ixion

**Ixion** (Greek: Ἰξίων) was a legendary king of the Lapiths. Murray's Introduction to [[the-eumenides|The Eumenides]] cites him as a key example of [[zeus|Zeus]]'s capacity for forgiveness.

## The First Murderer Purified

Ixion committed the first murder of a kinsman in Greek mythology, killing his father-in-law to avoid paying a bride-price. Stricken with pollution and shunned by all, he became the archetypal suppliant. [[zeus|Zeus]], in an unprecedented act, purified Ixion — the first time a god had cleansed a murderer.

Murray writes: "[[[zeus|Zeus]]] forgave the bloodstained Ixion because of his suffering and prayer. Nay, he is not only the protector of suppliants, he is himself the Eternal Suppliant, the God and Master of all things, who forgives because he also craves for forgiveness." (P01-009)

## The Two Ixions

The mythological Ixion notoriously repaid [[zeus|Zeus]]'s mercy with ingratitude — attempting to seduce [[hera|Hera]] and being punished by eternal rotation on a fiery wheel. But Murray distinguishes the archaic story from the Aeschylean theological point: what matters is [[zeus|Zeus]]'s act of purification, which establishes the precedent that even the shedding of kindred blood can be forgiven by a god who understands suffering. (002-033)
