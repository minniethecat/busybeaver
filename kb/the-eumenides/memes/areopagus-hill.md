# Areopagus Hill (Hill of Ares)

[[the-areopagus-court|The Areopagus]] Hill (Greek: Ἄρειος Πάγος, "Hill of Ares") is a rocky outcrop northwest of the Athenian [[acropolis|Acropolis]]. In Aeschylus' *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]*, it is the site where [[athena|Athena]] establishes the homicide court that bears its name — and beneath which the transformed [[erinyes|Furies]] receive their permanent shrine as the [[erinyes|Eumenides]]. (001-072)

## Location

The hill rises between the [[acropolis|Acropolis]] (to the southeast) and the Agora (to the north). In historical [[athens|Athens]], the [[the-areopagus-court|Council of the Areopagus]] met on this hill, and a cleft or cave beneath it housed the shrine of the *[[erinyes|Semnai Theai]]* (the "Revered Goddesses"), the Athenian cult-name for the [[erinyes|Eumenides]].

## In The Eumenides

[[the-areopagus-court|The Areopagus]] Hill is the setting for the trial of [[orestes|Orestes]] and the subsequent transformation of the [[erinyes|Furies]]. [[athena|Athena]] establishes the court on this site, explaining that it is the place where the Amazons once camped when they invaded [[athens|Athens]] — a military association that gives the hill its name ([[ares|Ares]] being the god of war).

After the trial, [[athena|Athena]] persuades the [[erinyes|Furies]] to accept a permanent home beneath the hill. This location is significant: the [[erinyes|Eumenides]], as chthonian powers, belong under the earth, not in the sky like the Olympians. Their shrine beneath the [[areopagus|Areopagus]] integrates the old order into the new — the chthonian below, the Olympian above, the human institution (the court) between them.

## Religious Significance

The shrine of the [[erinyes|Semnai Theai]] beneath the [[areopagus|Areopagus]] was an important site of Athenian chthonian worship. Offerings were poured into the earth, and the goddesses were approached with a mixture of reverence and fear — they could bless or curse, and their power, though channeled into civic protection, remained formidable.

Aeschylus' foundation myth gives this cult a specific origin: it was [[athena|Athena]] herself who established the shrine, and the [[erinyes|Eumenides]] who first received worship there are the same beings who pursued [[orestes|Orestes]]. The myth thus links the terrifying chthonian powers to the benevolent civic guardians through a single narrative of transformation.
