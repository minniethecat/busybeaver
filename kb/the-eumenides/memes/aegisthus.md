# Aegisthus

Aegisthus, son of [[thyestes|Thyestes]] and lover of [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]], is a figure from the backstory of *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]*: he conspired with [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] to murder [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] and was in turn killed by [[orestes|Orestes]]. Though he does not appear in the play, his role in the chain of vengeance — and the moral complexity of his claim — is essential to understanding the cycle that the [[areopagus|Areopagus]] is founded to break.

## Role in the Play

Aegisthus is mentioned in Murray's Introduction as an example of the blood-feud's paradox: "Aegisthus, as son of [[thyestes|Thyestes]], acted quite correctly in killing [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]], and [[orestes|Orestes]] in killing him" (P01-001).

The feud between the houses of Atreus and [[thyestes|Thyestes]] is a multigenerational cycle of crime and vengeance: Atreus ([[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s father) fed [[thyestes|Thyestes]] (Aegisthus's father) his own children at a banquet; [[thyestes|Thyestes]] cursed the house of Atreus; Aegisthus, as [[thyestes|Thyestes]]'s surviving son, was duty-bound to avenge this atrocity by killing [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]].


Thus Aegisthus's murder of [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]], like [[orestes|Orestes]]'s murder of Aegisthus and [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]], is both a crime and a fulfillment of sacred obligation. The blood-feud makes every avenger also a perpetrator, every act of justice also a new injustice. This is the system that *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]* depicts as superseded by the [[areopagus|Areopagus]].

## Mythological Background

The feud between Atreus and [[thyestes|Thyestes]] is one of the darkest stories in Greek mythology. Atreus, king of [[argos|Mycenae]], discovered that his brother [[thyestes|Thyestes]] had seduced his wife. In revenge, Atreus killed [[thyestes|Thyestes]]'s sons and served them to their father at a reconciliation banquet. When [[thyestes|Thyestes]] realized what he had eaten, he cursed Atreus and his descendants. Aegisthus, the sole surviving son of [[thyestes|Thyestes]] (in most versions), was raised to avenge his father and brothers.

Aegisthus's role in the *Oresteia* is treated in the earlier plays of the trilogy. In the *[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]*, he appears after [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] has killed her husband and boasts of his vengeance; the chorus despises him as an unmanly schemer who let a woman do the killing. In the *Choëphoroe*, [[orestes|Orestes]] kills him alongside [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]].

## Relationships

- **[[thyestes|Thyestes]]** (father): the victim of Atreus's atrocity, whose curse drives the feud
- **[[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]** (lover): his partner in murdering [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] and ruling [[argos|Mycenae]]
- **[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]** (victim): killed to avenge the crimes against his father
- **[[orestes|Orestes]]** (killer): the son of [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] who avenges his father by killing Aegisthus
