# The Death of Agamemnon

The **Death of [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]** is the murder of King [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] of [[argos|Argos]] by his wife [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] and her lover [[aegisthus|Aegisthus]] upon the king's return from the [[the-trojan-war|Trojan War]]. This event, dramatized in the first play of the [[the-oresteia|Oresteia]] trilogy (*[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]*), is the essential background of [[the-eumenides|The Eumenides]].

## The Murder

[[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] killed [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] in his bath, trapping him in a net and striking him with an axe or sword. [[apollo|Apollo]], in his testimony at the [[areopagus|Areopagus]], vividly describes the murder:

> He came from battle after sufferings sore But greater glories, and she stood before The gate to greet and praise him, strewed his path With crimson robes and led him to his bath— A marble bed!—and o'er the end thereof Laid the great web and curtained it above, To ensnare him as he rose; then, in the wide Unending folds, she smote him and he died! So died a man, ye hear it from my lips, All-honoured, War-Lord of a thousand ships; And such a wife was she!
>
> (001-104)

The murder was both a personal act of revenge — for [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s sacrifice of their daughter [[iphigenia|Iphigenia]] at [[aulis|Aulis]] — and a political act that transferred power in [[argos|Argos]] to [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] and [[aegisthus|Aegisthus]].

## Justifications

The death of [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] is morally complex. From the perspective of the [[justice-dike|blood-feud]], it was justified: [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] had killed [[iphigenia|Iphigenia]], and blood demands blood. From [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]'s perspective, it was a mother's righteous vengeance. But from the perspective of the city and of [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s son [[orestes|Orestes]], it was murder — a crime that demanded its own vengeance.

The law that "the doer must suffer" is mainly envisaged by the most primitive Greek tradition as a form of the blood-feud: it is blood calling for blood. [[aegisthus|Aegisthus]], as son of [[thyestes|Thyestes]], acted correctly in killing [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]], and [[orestes|Orestes]] in killing him. Similarly, when [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] killed [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] the eternal law compelled [[orestes|Orestes]] to avenge him, however reluctantly. (P01-001)

## The Chain Continues

The murder of [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] is the second link in the chain of vengeance (after [[iphigenia|Iphigenia]]'s sacrifice). It demands the third: [[orestes|Orestes]]' murder of [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]. This chain of reciprocal violence is the problem that *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]* must solve — not by declaring one killing just and another unjust, but by replacing the entire logic of the blood-feud with the logic of civic justice.
