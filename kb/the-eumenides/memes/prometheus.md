# Prometheus

**Prometheus** (Greek: Προμηθεύς, "Forethought") is the Titan who defied [[zeus|Zeus]] by giving fire to humanity. Murray's Introduction to [[the-eumenides|The Eumenides]] discusses Prometheus at length as a parallel to the play's theology.

## Murray's Analysis

Murray explains that the idea of [[zeus|Zeus]] as a god who learns and grows was developed by Aeschylus in the *Prometheus*-trilogy (P01-009):

"He himself in the distant past won his throne by violence, but now he has learned and his heart is changed. This idea of a supreme Ruler who, though inscrutably wise, is not perfect but only working his way towards perfection, was developed by Aeschylus in the *Prometheus*-trilogy, where [[zeus|Zeus]], beginning as a conqueror and a tyrant, seems at first like the villain of the piece. But he possesses this peculiar secret: he can learn by his own offences; so the end is reconciliation."
>
> (P01-009)

## Parallel with The Eumenides

Prometheus' story parallels [[orestes|Orestes]]': both suffer for offenses that were, in some sense, divinely willed; both are ultimately reconciled through [[zeus|Zeus]]'s capacity for growth and forgiveness. The [[zeus|Zeus]] who bound Prometheus is the same [[zeus|Zeus]] who, having learned, sets him free — just as the [[zeus|Zeus]] who commanded [[orestes|Orestes]] to kill is the same [[zeus|Zeus]] who, through [[athena|Athena]], acquits him (P01-017).
