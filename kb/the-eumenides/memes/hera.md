# Hera

[[hera]] is the queen of the Olympian gods, wife and sister of [[zeus|Zeus]], and the goddess of marriage and childbirth. Though she does not appear as a character in *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]*, her name is invoked at a pivotal moment in the [[trial-of-orestes|trial of Orestes]] — when [[apollo|Apollo]] argues that the marriage bond sanctified by Hera carries greater weight than the claims of maternal kinship.

## Role in the Play

[[apollo|Apollo]]'s invocation of Hera comes during his defense of [[orestes|Orestes]] before the [[the-areopagus-court|Areopagus]].

The [[erinyes|Furies]] have argued that matricide is the unpardonable crime, violating the most sacred bond of blood.

[[apollo|Apollo]] counters by appealing to a higher bond — marriage — and to the divine authority that sanctions it: "How? Would ye count as a light thing and vain The perfect bond of Hera and high [[zeus|Zeus]]?

Yea, and thy word dishonoured too the use Of Cypris, whence love groweth to his best.

The fate-ordainèd meeting, breast to breast, Of man and woman is a tie more sure Than oath or pact, if [[justice-dike|Justice]] guards it pure" (001-010).


This argument is central to [[apollo|Apollo]]'s case. [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] violated the marriage bond by murdering [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] — her husband and the father of her children. Hera, as the divine guardian of marriage (*Hera Teleia*, "Hera the Fulfiller"), sanctifies this bond. By appealing to Hera's authority alongside [[zeus|Zeus]]'s, [[apollo|Apollo]] asserts that the marriage tie — and the father's claim it establishes — is prior to and stronger than the mother's claim.

## Mythological Background

Hera was one of the twelve Olympian gods, daughter of Cronus and Rhea. As [[zeus|Zeus]]'s wife, she was the queen of Olympus and the goddess who presided over marriage, women, childbirth, and family. Her cult titles reflect her domains: *Hera Teleia* (of marriage), *Hera Eileithyia* (of childbirth), and *Hera Parthenos* (the maiden).

In Greek myth, Hera is often portrayed as jealous and vengeful toward [[zeus|Zeus]]'s lovers and illegitimate children — a characterization largely absent from Aeschylus' brief but dignified invocation. Here, Hera is simply the divine guarantor of the marriage bond, the cosmic principle that makes the union of man and woman "a tie more sure than [[oath|oath]] or pact."

## Significance in Aeschylean Thought

[[apollo|Apollo]]'s appeal to Hera is part of the play's larger argument about the nature of kinship and justice. The old law — enforced by the [[erinyes|Furies]] — recognizes only the blood tie between mother and child.

The new law — represented by the Olympian gods — recognizes marriage as a prior and more binding relationship.

This theological claim has obvious patriarchal implications, but Aeschylus' purpose is not primarily to subordinate women; it is to establish that justice requires a hierarchy of obligations, and that the marriage bond, sanctified by Hera and [[zeus|Zeus]], stands at its apex.
