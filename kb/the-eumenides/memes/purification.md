# Purification (Katharmos)

Purification (*katharmos*, Greek: Καθαρμός) is the ritual cleansing that removes the stain of blood-pollution (*miasma*) in Greek religion. In Aeschylus' *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]*, [[apollo|Apollo]]'s purification of [[orestes|Orestes]] at [[delphi|Delphi]] is the essential precondition for the trial at [[athens|Athens]] — the act that transforms [[orestes|Orestes]] from a polluted fugitive into a suppliant entitled to a hearing before the [[areopagus|Areopagus]].

## Definition

In Greek religious practice, purification rituals were required after birth, death, and especially after bloodshed. The most common form of purification from homicide involved the blood of a piglet (*choiros*) being poured over the killer's hands, symbolically washing away the stain. More elaborate rituals might include exile for a prescribed period, followed by readmission to the community through further rites.

Murray's Introduction emphasizes that purification is not merely ritual hygiene but a theological necessity. When kindred blood is shed, "the intolerable stain falls first and most directly on the face of [[mother-earth|Mother Earth]]. It pollutes her, and she sends up her punishments from below" (P01-006). Purification is thus an act that addresses not only the individual killer but the cosmic order itself — it cleanses the earth of the pollution that would otherwise bring blight, barrenness, and plague.

## In The Eumenides

[[apollo|Apollo]]'s role as purifier is established from the play's first scene.

[[pythian-prophetess|The Pythian Prophetess]], having witnessed the polluted [[orestes|Orestes]] at the altar, leaves the matter to [[apollo|Apollo]]: "Being Helper, Prophet, Seer of things unseen, The stainèd hearth he knoweth to make clean" (001-001).

[[apollo|Apollo]] himself confirms that the purification has been performed when he addresses the [[erinyes|Furies]]: "I bade him turn for cleansing to my door" (001-011).

And at the trial, he states explicitly: "Therefore the cleansing of his stain is mine" (001-076).


The [[erinyes|Furies]], however, reject the efficacy of [[apollo|Apollo]]'s purification. From their perspective, the pollution is permanent — "The earth may open and hide, but never shall he be freed" (001-015). This disagreement is not merely a dispute about ritual competence but about the nature of wrongdoing itself. For the [[erinyes|Furies]], the act of kindred bloodshed is ontologically irrevocable; for [[apollo|Apollo]] (and [[zeus|Zeus]]), purification is real because forgiveness is real.

The play's resolution resolves this tension through synthesis rather than the victory of one side. The [[erinyes|Furies]] are not told that purification was meaningless; instead, they are persuaded that their own role — enforcing the law — is compatible with a world in which purification and forgiveness are also possible. [[the-areopagus-court|The Areopagus]], as an institution, makes this synthesis permanent: it is a court that judges guilt but also a place where the judged can be cleansed and reintegrated.

## In Greek Thought

The concept of purification was central to Greek religion from the earliest period. The Homeric poems already show awareness of the need for cleansing after bloodshed. The [[delphic-oracle|Delphic Oracle]] itself was a major source of purification rituals and requirements, prescribing specific acts and periods of exile for those who consulted it about blood-guilt.

The mystery cults — particularly the Eleusinian Mysteries and Orphism — developed the concept of purification beyond the physical into the spiritual realm.

In these traditions, purification was not only from specific acts of pollution but from an inherited or existential condition, and the purified soul could hope for a better fate after death.

Aeschylus' treatment of purification, while focused on the legal and civic dimensions, gestures toward this broader spiritual significance: the transformation of the [[erinyes|Furies]] into [[erinyes|Eumenides]] is itself a kind of cosmic purification, a cleansing of the moral order.
