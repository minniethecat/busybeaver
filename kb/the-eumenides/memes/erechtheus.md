# Erechtheus

**Erechtheus** (Greek: Ἐρεχθεύς) was a legendary king of [[athens|Athens]], sometimes identified with [[erichthonius|Erichthonius]]. Murray's Introduction to [[the-eumenides|The Eumenides]] mentions him as an example of the sacrificial logic that Aeschylus' theology transcends.

## The Sacrifice of His Daughters

According to myth, when [[athens|Athens]] was threatened by Eleusis, an oracle declared that Erechtheus must sacrifice one of his daughters to secure victory. He sacrificed his youngest daughter (or, in some versions, multiple daughters), and [[athens|Athens]] was saved. Euripides dramatized this story in his lost tragedy *Erechtheus*.

## Murray's Citation

Murray lists Erechtheus among the figures who belong to a superseded moral world: "He knew how [[codrus|Codrus]] died, and Menoikeus and Macaria, how [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] and Erechtheus and other kings had given their children to die. But for him such practices belonged to that primitive and barbaric world which Hellenic [[zeus|Zeus]] had swept away, so he hoped, forever." (P01-016)

The key point is that Aeschylus' [[zeus|Zeus]] does not demand substitute victims. [[orestes|Orestes]] is forgiven not because another life is paid but because [[zeus|Zeus]], through [[athena|Athena]], wills forgiveness.
