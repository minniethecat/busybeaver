# Helen of Troy

**Helen** (Greek: Ἑλένη) was the daughter of [[zeus|Zeus]] and [[leda|Leda]], sister of [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]], and wife of [[menelaus|Menelaus]]. Her abduction by Paris of [[troy|Troy]] triggered the [[the-trojan-war|Trojan War]], which forms the background of [[the-eumenides|The Eumenides]].

## Sister of Clytemnestra

Helen and [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] are mirror images: both are daughters of [[leda|Leda]], both are queens who betray their husbands, and both are blamed for catastrophic wars. Yet their fates diverge. Helen, in the Homeric tradition, is a figure of ambiguous guilt — part victim, part agent. [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]], by contrast, is portrayed as deliberate and masculine in her violence.

## The Face That Launched a Thousand Ships

[[the-trojan-war|The Trojan War]], fought to recover Helen, is the world from which the characters of the Oresteia return. [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] spent ten years at [[troy|Troy]], during which [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] ruled [[argos|Argos]] and took [[aegisthus|Aegisthus]] as her lover. The war thus created the conditions for [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s murder and the entire subsequent chain of vengeance.

## In Literature

Helen appears in multiple Greek tragedies beyond the Oresteia. Euripides' *Helena* presents an alternative version in which Helen never went to [[troy|Troy]] — a phantom was sent in her place while the real Helen waited faithfully in Egypt (002-021). This version challenges the traditional narrative and complicates Helen's culpability for the war.
