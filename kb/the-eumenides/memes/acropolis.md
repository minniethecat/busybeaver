# The Acropolis

The Acropolis (Greek: Ἀκρόπολις, "high city") is the rocky citadel of [[athens|Athens]], crowned with the temple of [[athena|Athena]] Parthenos. In *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]*, it is the site of [[orestes|Orestes]]' supplication — "Pallas' Rock" where he embraces [[athena|Athena]]'s ancient image and claims her protection.

## Location

The Acropolis rises abruptly from the Athenian plain, a flat-topped rock approximately 150 meters above sea level. In Aeschylus' day, it housed the archaic temple of [[athena|Athena]] (the "old image" [[orestes|Orestes]] clasps) and would soon be crowned with the Parthenon. [[the-areopagus-court|The Areopagus]] Hill lies to the northwest, visible from the Acropolis.

## In The Eumenides

[[apollo|Apollo]] instructs [[orestes|Orestes]]: "On till thou come to Pallas' Rock, and fold Thine arms in prayer about her image old" (001-002). The Acropolis is thus the terminus of [[orestes|Orestes]]' sacred journey — from the chthonian oracle at [[delphi|Delphi]], across the earth under [[hermes|Hermes]]' guidance, to the Olympian citadel of [[athens|Athens]].

[[athena|Athena]] discovers [[orestes|Orestes]] at her statue and acknowledges his supplication. But she does not grant automatic pardon. Instead, she creates the [[areopagus|Areopagus]] — expanding the sacred geography from the Acropolis (divine presence) to the [[areopagus-hill|Areopagus Hill]] (divine-human institution). The movement from one to the other is the play's spatial logic: the goddess is present on the Acropolis, but justice is done on the [[areopagus|Areopagus]].

## Religious Significance

The Acropolis was the religious heart of [[athens|Athens]]. The Erechtheion housed the ancient olive-wood statue of [[athena|Athena]] Polias, the city's most sacred object. The [[pan|Pan]]athenaic procession, culminating at the Acropolis, was the city's central religious festival. Aeschylus' choice to place [[orestes|Orestes]]' supplication here — at the most sacred spot in [[athens|Athens]] — emphasizes the gravity of his claim and the significance of its resolution.
