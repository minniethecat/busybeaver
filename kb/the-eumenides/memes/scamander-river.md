# Scamander River

The Scamander River (Greek: Σκάμανδρος) is the river of the Trojan plain, flowing past the city of [[troy|Troy]] to the Hellespont. In *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]*, the Scamander appears at the moment of [[athena|Athena]]'s entrance: she has been at Scamander, receiving the Achaeans' land-grant to the sons of [[theseus|Theseus]], and has returned swiftly to [[athens|Athens]] upon hearing [[orestes|Orestes]]' prayer. The river thus serves as a geographic hinge connecting the world of the [[the-trojan-war|Trojan War]] to the Athenian courtroom.

## Overview

The Scamander (modern Karamenderes River) is the principal river of the Troad in northwestern Asia Minor, flowing from Mount Ida to the Hellespont. In Homeric epic it is both a geographic feature and a divine being — the river-god Scamander (Xanthus) who fights [[achilles|Achilles]] in *Iliad* 21. In Aeschylus, the Scamander appears not as a battlefield but as the site of a diplomatic transaction: the Achaean lords' grant of land to [[athena|Athena]].

## In The Eumenides

[[athena|Athena]]'s entrance speech begins: "Far off I heard the calling of my name, Beside Scamander, where I took in claim The new land which the Achaean lords and kings, In royal spoil for many warfarings, Gave, root and fruit for ever, as mine own Exempted prize, to [[theseus|Theseus]]' sons alone" (001-048).

The Scamander is the place where [[athena|Athena]] receives her τιμή (honor/portion) — a grant of Trojan land dedicated to the Athenians.

Her journey from Scamander to [[athens|Athens]] maps a geographical arc from the world of war ([[troy|Troy]], the *Iliad*) to the world of law ([[athens|Athens]], the *[[erinyes|Eumenides]]*), framing the trial of [[orestes|Orestes]] within the broader aftermath of the [[the-trojan-war|Trojan War]].


Murray's stage direction notes: "Enter [[athena|Athena]]. Far off I heard the calling of my name, Beside Scamander... Thence came I speeding, while behind me rolled My wingless aegis, floating fold on fold" (001-048). The image of [[athena|Athena]] flying from the Troad to [[athens|Athens]] — her aegis streaming behind her — is one of the play's most vivid mythological transitions.

## Mythological Associations

In Homer, the river-god Scamander (also called Xanthus by the gods) is a son of Oceanus and Tethys, and an ally of the Trojans.

When [[achilles|Achilles]] fills the river with Trojan corpses, Scamander rises against him in rage (*Iliad* 21.211–382).

Aeschylus's reference to Scamander at [[athena|Athena]]'s entrance implicitly invokes this Homeric background: the goddess who once aided the Achaeans at [[troy|Troy]] now returns to establish a court of law at [[athens|Athens]].

The river, a site of divine wrath in Homer, becomes in Aeschylus a site of divine generosity — the place where [[athena|Athena]] receives a gift of land for her people.
