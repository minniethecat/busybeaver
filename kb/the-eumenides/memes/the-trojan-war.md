# The Trojan War

The **Trojan War** was the legendary ten-year conflict between a coalition of Greek kingdoms and the city of [[troy|Troy]]. It forms the essential background of [[the-eumenides|The Eumenides]] and the entire [[the-oresteia|Oresteia]] trilogy.

## Origins

The war began when Paris, a prince of [[troy|Troy]], abducted [[helen-of-troy|Helen]], wife of [[menelaus|Menelaus]] of [[sparta|Sparta]]. Bound by the Oath of [[tyndareus|Tyndareus]], the Greek kings assembled at [[aulis|Aulis]] under the command of [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]], [[menelaus|Menelaus]]' brother and the most powerful Greek ruler.

## Sacrifice at Aulis

At [[aulis|Aulis]], [[artemis|Artemis]] stilled the winds, demanding the sacrifice of [[iphigenia|Iphigenia]], [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s daughter. (P01-008) [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s decision to sacrifice his child to enable the war is the original crime that sets the Oresteia's chain of vengeance in motion.

## Agamemnon's Return

After ten years at [[troy|Troy]], [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] returned to [[argos|Argos]] with the Trojan princess [[cassandra|Cassandra]] as his concubine. [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]], who had ruled in his absence and taken [[aegisthus|Aegisthus]] as her lover, murdered him in his bath — partly in revenge for [[iphigenia|Iphigenia]], partly for her own political and personal motives.

## Aftermath

The war's end brought no peace for its heroes. The Greek fleet scattered on the return voyage; many veterans wandered for years before reaching home. The gods rewarded some cities and condemned others "for their services in the Trojan War" (002-028). [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s own homecoming, celebrated in tragedy, became the deadliest of all.

## In The Eumenides

The Trojan War is never directly depicted in *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]*, but its shadow hangs over every scene. The war created the conditions for [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s murder, which required [[orestes|Orestes]]' vengeance, which brought the [[erinyes|Furies]] upon him. The play's resolution — the establishment of civic justice at [[athens|Athens]] — can be read as a repudiation of the heroic violence that the Trojan War represents.
