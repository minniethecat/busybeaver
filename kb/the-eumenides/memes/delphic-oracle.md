# Delphic Oracle

The Delphic Oracle — the shrine of [[apollo|Apollo]] at [[delphi|Delphi]] on [[mount-parnassus|Mount Parnassus]] — was the most important prophetic institution in the Greek world. In Aeschylus' *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]*, it is both the literal setting of the play's opening scene and a symbol of the theological transition from the old chthonic order to the new Olympian dispensation under [[zeus|Zeus]] and [[apollo|Apollo]].

## Definition

[[delphi|Delphi]] was believed by the Greeks to be the center (*omphalos*, "navel") of the world. Pilgrims from across the Greek world consulted the Pythia — the priestess of [[apollo|Apollo]] who, seated on a tripod over a chasm that emitted intoxicating vapors, delivered the god's oracles in a state of ecstatic possession. The oracle's pronouncements, often cryptic or ambiguous, guided decisions on war, colonization, religious practice, and personal morality for over a thousand years.

The opening speech of *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]* — the [[pythian-prophetess|Pythian Prophetess]]'s prayer — provides a theological genealogy of the [[delphi|Delphi]]c shrine.

The oracle passed through four holders: first [[mother-earth|Earth]] ([[mother-earth|Gaia]]), the primeval prophet; then [[themis|Themis]], the personification of correct custom; then [[phoebe|Phoebe]], another Titaness; and finally [[apollo|Apollo]], to whom [[phoebe|Phoebe]] gave the shrine "as a birthtide gift" (001-001).

Significantly, each transition is peaceful — "by whose own Accepted will, with never strife nor stress" (001-001).

This peaceful succession contrasts with the violent divine successions of Hesiod's *Theogony* and prefigures the play's theme of reconciliation.


## In The Eumenides

The play opens at the doors of [[apollo|Apollo]]'s temple at [[delphi|Delphi]]. [[pythian-prophetess|The Pythian Prophetess]] enters the inner shrine to perform her office and emerges in terror at what she has seen: [[orestes|Orestes]], polluted with his mother's blood, at the central altar, surrounded by the sleeping [[erinyes|Furies]]. Her description — "Horrors, horrors, dire to speak or see, From [[apollo|Loxias]]' chamber drive me reeling back" (001-001) — establishes the central conflict visually before any character speaks.

[[apollo|Apollo]]'s role at [[delphi|Delphi]] combines prophecy, purification, and advocacy. He is *[[apollo|Loxias]]* (the oracular god), the purifier who cleanses [[orestes|Orestes]] of his stain, and the advocate who will later defend him before the [[areopagus|Areopagus]]. Murray notes that [[apollo|Apollo]]'s prophetic authority derives entirely from [[zeus|Zeus]]: "Never have I spoken on my throne of prophecy any word concerning man, woman or city, which was not commanded by [[zeus|Zeus the Father]]" (P01-011).

The [[delphi|Delphi]]c shrine thus functions in the play as the site where the old and new orders meet. The oracular tradition descends from [[mother-earth|Earth]], through [[themis|Themis]] and [[phoebe|Phoebe]], to [[apollo|Apollo]] — and [[apollo|Apollo]], unlike his predecessors, speaks not on his own authority but as the *Prophêtês Dios*, the revealer of [[zeus|Zeus]]. The oracle is not abolished but transformed: what was once the voice of the earth becomes the voice of the Father.

## In Greek Thought

[[delphi|Delphi]]'s influence on Greek civilization was immense. The oracle's pronouncements shaped colonization, legislation, and religious reform throughout the archaic and classical periods. The [[delphi|Delphi]]c maxims — "Know thyself" (γνῶθι σεαυτόν) and "Nothing in excess" (μηδὲν ἄγαν) — became foundational texts of Greek ethical thought.

Politically, [[delphi|Delphi]] was a panhellenic institution, neutral in theory (though often partisan in practice) and consulted by both Greek and non-Greek powers. The Amphictyonic League, a religious association of tribes that administered the sanctuary, waged several "Sacred Wars" to protect [[delphi|Delphi]]c autonomy — conflicts that played a significant role in the power politics of the 4th century BCE.

Aeschylus, by locating his play at [[delphi|Delphi]] and tracing the oracle's genealogy, claims [[delphi|Delphi]]c authority for his own theological vision. The play's argument — that the mechanical law of Dikê must be corrected by the personal wisdom of [[zeus|Zeus]] — is presented as the teaching of the oracle itself.
