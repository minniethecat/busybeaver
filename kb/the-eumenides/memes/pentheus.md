# Pentheus

**Pentheus** (Greek: Πενθεύς) was a legendary king of Thebes. He is mentioned in [[the-eumenides|The Eumenides]] by the [[pythian-prophetess|Pythian Prophetess]] in her opening prayer.

## In The Eumenides

The prophetess invokes [[dionysus|Bromios (Dionysus)]] and recalls:

"Since first he led his Maenad host on high / And doomed King Pentheus like a hare to die."

This is a reference to Euripides' *Bacchae*, in which Pentheus — who refused to acknowledge [[dionysus|Dionysus]] as a god — was torn apart by the god's female followers (Maenads), led by his own mother Agave. The image is one of violent, ecstatic destruction — the fate of those who resist divine power.

## Function in the Prophetess's Speech

The Pentheus reference serves as a dark counterpoint to the peaceful succession at [[delphi|Delphi]]. Where the prophetic throne passed peacefully from [[mother-earth|Earth]] to [[themis|Themis]] to [[phoebe|Phoebe]] to [[apollo|Apollo]], Pentheus met a horrific death for opposing a god's arrival. The contrast underscores Aeschylus' argument: order through incorporation is preferable to order through destruction.
