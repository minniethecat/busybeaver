# The Areopagus Court

The [[the-areopagus-court|The Areopagus Court]] is the ancient Athenian homicide court and council, whose founding is dramatized in Aeschylus' *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]*.

According to the play's aetiological myth, it was established by [[athena|Athena]] herself to try [[orestes|Orestes]] for the murder of [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] — making it the first homicide court in human history and the institution through which the [[the-blood-feud|blood-feud]] is superseded by [[civic-order|civic justice]].

As the earthly expression of [[zeus|Zeus]]'s will via [[athena|Athena]], the [[areopagus|Areopagus]] is the institutional pivot around which the entire *Oresteia* turns.


## Role

The Areopagus had jurisdiction over homicide cases — the most serious crimes in Athenian law because they generated [[blood-pollution|pollution]] (*miasma*) that threatened the entire community. Its historical composition of ex-archons ensured that its members were experienced and respected citizens. In the 5th century BCE, the democratic reforms of [[ephialtes|Ephialtes]] (462 BCE) reduced its political powers, but it retained its homicide jurisdiction and religious prestige.

## In The Eumenides

Aeschylus presents the [[areopagus|Areopagus]] as a divine institution — founded by [[athena|Athena]], with [[zeus|Zeus]]'s authority, accepted by the [[erinyes|Furies]]. The twelve [[athenian-citizens|Athenian jurors]] who cast the first votes are the human component of a structure that is fundamentally divine in origin. Murray summarizes: "the interest lies in the foundation of the [[areopagus|Court of the Areopagus]], as a tribunal superseding the blood-feud, the ordeal by oath, and all the rigid and unreasoning practices of primitive justice, by a justice which can understand and therefore sympathize" [Murray, Introduction].

## Significance

The Areopagus represents the institutional expression of Aeschylus' theology. The old law — the blood-feud, enforced by the [[erinyes|Erinyes]] — is mechanical, impersonal, and endless. The new law — administered by the [[areopagus|Areopagus]] — is deliberative, human, and capable of resolution. The court does not abolish the old law but incorporates it: the [[erinyes|Furies]] are not banished but given a permanent home beneath the court, and their function (the enforcement of moral order) continues, but now within a structure that can weigh, deliberate, and forgive.

The Areopagus is also the institutional bridge between divine will and human justice. [[zeus|Zeus]]'s will is absolute but abstract; the [[areopagus|Areopagus]] is its concrete, institutional realization. [[athena|Athena]]'s founding act creates not just a court but a permanent structure through which [[justice-dike|justice]] can be continually administered, generation after generation.
