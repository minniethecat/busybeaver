# Ephialtes

[[ephialtes]] (Greek: Ἐφιάλτης) was an Athenian politician and democratic reformer whose legislation in 462 BCE severely curtailed the political powers of the [[the-areopagus-court|Areopagus]] — the ancient council and court whose founding is dramatized in Aeschylus' *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]*.

Though named only in the play's endnotes (not the dramatic text itself), Ephialtes is the political ghost haunting the entire work: Aeschylus' portrayal of the [[the-areopagus-court|Areopagus]] as a divinely instituted, noble, and indispensable guardian of [[athens|Athens]] is a direct response to the democratic attack on the court's authority.


## Historical Context

In 462–461 BCE, Ephialtes led the democratic faction in [[athens|Athens]] in stripping the [[the-areopagus-court|Areopagus]] of its political powers — its general supervision of the state, its oversight of magistrates, and its role as "guardian of the constitution." The reforms transferred these powers to the democratic Assembly (*Ekklēsia*), the Council of Five Hundred (*Boulē*), and the popular courts (*Dikastēria*). The [[areopagus|Areopagus]] retained only its traditional jurisdiction over homicide cases and certain religious matters.

Just three years later, in 458 BCE, Aeschylus produced the *Oresteia*, in which the [[areopagus|Areopagus]] is founded by [[athena|Athena]] herself, with [[zeus|Zeus]]'s authority and the consent of the transformed [[erinyes|Furies]]. The play is, among other things, an impassioned defense of the [[areopagus|Areopagus]]'s traditional dignity and a call for civic reconciliation in a time of bitter factional strife.

Ephialtes himself was murdered shortly after his reforms — a victim of the very political violence that Aeschylus' gospel of forgiveness sought to transcend.

## In The Eumenides (Endnotes)

Murray's endnotes make Ephialtes' relevance explicit.

Note 41 explains that "at the time of the Persian invasion [the [[areopagus|Areopagus]]] seems to have been the only organ of the constitution which held firm," but that in "461–460, three years before this play, its political powers were severely cut down by the democratic party led by Ephialtes." The endnotes further ask: "What the political bearing of the [[erinyes|Eumenides]] was, in a time of fierce party feeling, the answer is not hard to find.

Aeschylus preaches reconciliation and magnifies the original function of the [[areopagus|Areopagus]], which it still retains" (002-041).


Note 48 deepens the historical picture: "461 Kimon, the aristocratic leader, banished: 460 the [[areopagus|Areopagus]] attacked and reduced in power: in return Ephialtes, the democratic leader, murdered... War, faction and reconciliation in abundance!" The scholarly observation that "this feud-torn atmosphere must have had an influence in leading Aeschylus to his gospel of forgiveness" (002-048) connects Ephialtes' reform and murder to the play's central theological theme.

## Significance

Ephialtes is not a character in *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]*, but his shadow falls across the entire play. Aeschylus does not name him — that would be too crude, too partisan — but the play's vision of the [[the-areopagus-court|Areopagus]] as a divine institution whose "reverence and fear" (001-077) safeguard [[athens|Athens]] is unmistakably a response to Ephialtes' attack. The play urges [[athens|Athens]] not to "cast away Fear, yet cast not all" — a statement that, in its historical context, is a warning against Ephialtes' radical democratic program.

> (002-041)
