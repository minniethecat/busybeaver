# Io

**Io** (Greek: Ἰώ) was a mortal priestess of [[hera|Hera]] who was loved by [[zeus|Zeus]] and transformed into a heifer to hide her from [[hera|Hera]]'s jealousy. Murray's Introduction to [[the-eumenides|The Eumenides]] cites Io as a key example of Aeschylus' theology of suffering and redemption.

## Murray's Analysis

Murray notes that in Aeschylus' *Supplices* (The Suppliant Women), "we hear how Io, once the persecuted victim of [[[zeus|Zeus]]'s] lust, is at last led to peace and blessedness and becomes the Virgin Mother of the Deliverer of [[prometheus|Prometheus]]." (P01-009)

Io's story follows the Aeschylean pattern: she suffers unjustly, wanders in exile and torment, but is ultimately redeemed. This pattern — suffering → wandering → purification → reconciliation — is the same arc that [[orestes|Orestes]] follows in *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]*. Murray observes that "in the end, after suffering and struggle, after cleansing and supplication, that union is achieved between the Law which acts like blind fate and the Father who understands" — a pattern first established through Io. (P01-017)

## The Suppliant Parallel

Io is the archetypal suppliant, and her descendants (the Danaids) are the suppliants of the *Supplices* play. Murray draws a direct parallel to [[orestes|Orestes]], who also comes as a [[supplication|suppliant]] to [[athens|Athens]], seeking the same divine mercy that Io ultimately received.

> A modern reader is more likely to ask why, if Orestes only fulfilled the command of Zeus, he should be punished at all. Why is there any talk of suffering and forgiveness? The answer is quite straightforward.
>
> (P01-017)
