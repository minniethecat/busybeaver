# Blood-Pollution (Miasma)

[[blood-pollution|Blood-Pollution]] (*miasma*, Greek: Μίασμα) is the ritual stain that falls upon a person who sheds kindred blood — and, by extension, upon all who associate with them. In Aeschylus' *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]*, it is the central mechanism by which the law of [[justice-dike|Dikê]] operates: the pollution is both the automatic consequence of the act and the object of [[purification]] that enables [[orestes|Orestes]]' eventual acquittal at the [[trial-of-orestes|Areopagus trial]].

## Definition

Murray's Introduction establishes the logic of blood-pollution with precision. When a man kills a stranger, "there is no 'sin' or 'pollution' in this, though of course the kindred of the stranger will duly try to avenge him." But when a man "kills, not a stranger, but his own kinsman," he has "shed the 'kindred blood,' and committed a real sin. He is polluted, and pollutes all who associate with him" (P01-001).

Crucially, the pollution is not a metaphor or a psychological state but a material reality in the Greek worldview — a stain that physically contaminates the earth itself.

As Murray explains, "whenever kindred blood is shed, the intolerable stain falls first and most directly on the face of [[mother-earth|Mother Earth]].

It pollutes her, and she sends up her punishments from below, blight and barrenness and plague" (P01-006).

This is why [[mother-earth|Mother Earth]] — [[mother-earth|Gaia]] — is among the supreme authorities for executing the law of Dikê: she is the direct victim of the pollution.


The pollution is also inherently social. Because the polluted person "pollutes all who associate with him," they are cut off from human community — from the hearth, the temple, the feast. This is the state in which the [[pythian-prophetess|Pythian Prophetess]] finds [[orestes|Orestes]] at the opening of *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]*: "a man abhorred of God, his body hurled [[mother-earth|Earth]]ward in desperate prayer; blood on his hand yet reeking" (001-001). He is at once a [[supplication|suppliant]] and a source of contagion.

## In The Eumenides

The entire action of *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]* turns on the tension between [[orestes|Orestes]]' pollution and his purification. [[apollo|Apollo]], as the god of [[delphi|Delphi]], is also the god of ritual cleansing: "The stainèd hearth he knoweth to make clean" (001-001). When [[apollo|Apollo]] defends [[orestes|Orestes]], he claims to have performed the necessary purification: "Therefore the cleansing of his stain is mine" (001-076).

Yet the [[erinyes|Furies]] refuse to recognize [[apollo|Apollo]]'s purification. For them, the pollution is permanent and ineradicable — the blood once shed can never be un-shed.

Their objection is not merely legal but ontological: the pollution is a fact about the world, and no ritual can change the facts.

This is why the trial requires not just a ritual solution (purification at [[delphi|Delphi]]) but an institutional one (the [[the-areopagus-court|Areopagus]]).

The old gods recognize only pollution and punishment; the new gods — [[zeus|Zeus]], [[apollo|Apollo]], [[athena|Athena]] — recognize that purification and forgiveness are also possible.


The play's resolution represents a profound transformation in the concept of purification. The [[erinyes|Furies]] are persuaded to bless rather than curse; the pollution is not denied but absorbed into a larger order. As Murray puts it, "the Law that can pardon and understand can itself be understood and loved" (P01-018). The "[[erinyes|Furies]]" have become "[[erinyes|Eumenides]]" — and what was once an external stain becomes "an inward aspiration, a standard of right living which men consciously need and seek" (P01-018).

## In Greek Thought

The concept of *miasma* was central to Greek religion and law. [[purification|Purification]] rituals (*katharmoi*) were essential to both private and public life: birth, death, and bloodshed all generated pollution that required ritual cleansing. The Athenian legal system distinguished between types of homicide based partly on the pollution incurred: intentional homicide polluted more severely than unintentional, and killing a kinsman polluted most severely of all.

| Type of Homicide | Pollution Severity | Legal Consequence |
|-----------------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Killing a stranger | None (ritually) | Kin may seek revenge |
| Unintentional homicide | Moderate | Exile until purified |
| Intentional homicide (non-kin) | Severe | Death or permanent exile |
| Kindred bloodshed | Most severe | Pursued by [[erinyes|Erinyes]]; permanent pollution |

Aeschylus' innovation is to spiritualize the concept. By the end of *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]*, pollution is no longer a physical stain that can be washed away with pig's blood (the traditional ritual) but a moral condition that can be addressed only through justice, persuasion, and the [[transformation-of-the-furies|transformation]] of the community's relationship to the wrongdoer.
