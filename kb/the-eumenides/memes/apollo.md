# Apollo

[[apollo]] (Phoebus Apollo, also called Loxias) is the god of prophecy, purification, music, and healing — the son of [[zeus|Zeus]] and the presiding deity of the [[delphic-oracle|Delphic Oracle]].

In Aeschylus' *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]*, he plays a triple role: he commands the matricide, purifies [[orestes|Orestes]] of its [[blood-pollution|pollution]], and serves as his advocate at the [[trial-of-orestes|trial]] before the [[the-areopagus-court|Areopagus]].

As the *Prophêtês Dios* — the "Prophet of [[zeus|Zeus]]" — Apollo is the conduit through which divine will enters the human world.


## Role in the Play

Apollo dominates the play's opening. As the [[pythian-prophetess|Pythian Prophetess]] reels from the shrine in terror, Apollo appears in the inner sanctuary, standing over the sleeping [[erinyes|Furies]] and the suppliant [[orestes|Orestes]] at his altar. His first words establish his protective role: "I fail thee not. For ever more I stay, Or watching at thy side or far away, Thy guard, and iron against thine enemies" (001-002).

He sends [[orestes|Orestes]] to [[athens|Athens]] with a promise — "I shall set thee free At last from all this woe" (001-002) — and commissions [[hermes|Hermes]] to guide him. When the [[erinyes|Furies]] wake and confront him, a sharp exchange reveals the theological stakes. The [[erinyes|Furies]] accuse: "Thou spakest and this man his mother slew." Apollo replies: "I spoke, and he avenged his father. True" (001-010). The [[erinyes|Furies]] pursue the matricide; Apollo, the avenger of a father. Both claim divine authority.

At the trial, Apollo speaks as both witness and advocate. Murray notes that "Apollo appears both as a witness and as a patron or sympathizer: quite an important character in ancient justice" (002-035).

His theological argument — that the father's claim outweighs the mother's, since the father is the true parent, the mother merely the nourisher of the seed — has struck modern readers as bizarre.

But as Murray observes, "the acquittal of [[orestes|Orestes]] does not depend on the arguments used in the trial, but on the Will of [[zeus|Zeus]], which is an ultimate fact not dependent on argument" [Murray, Introduction].


Apollo's most significant role may be his identity as the *Prophêtês Dios* — "the revealer of [[zeus|Zeus]]." As he tells the court: "Never have I spoken on my throne of prophecy any word concerning man, woman or city, which was not commanded by [[zeus|Zeus the Father]]" [paraphrase]. Apollo's authority derives entirely from [[zeus|Zeus]]; he is the conduit through which [[zeus|Zeus]]'s will enters the world.

## Mythological Background

Apollo was one of the most widely worshipped gods in the Greek world. Son of [[zeus|Zeus]] and [[lete|Leto]], twin brother of [[artemis|Artemis]], he was associated with prophecy ([[delphi|Delphi]]), music (the lyre), healing and plague, archery, and the sun. His oracle at [[delphi|Delphi]] was the most important prophetic institution in the ancient Mediterranean.

Aeschylus' Apollo is particularly [[delphi|Delphi]]c — the god of purification (*katharsios*) and prophecy (*prophêtês*). The play's opening prayer traces the [[delphi|Delphi]]c succession — [[mother-earth|Earth]], [[themis|Themis]], [[phoebe|Phoebe]], Apollo — and emphasizes the peaceful transfer of prophetic authority. Apollo inherits the chthonian oracle but operates as the mouthpiece of [[zeus|Olympian Zeus]].

## Relationships

- **[[zeus|Zeus]]** (father): the source of all Apollo's prophetic authority
- **[[athena|Athena]]** (half-sister): together they execute [[zeus|Zeus]]'s will — he purifies and advocates, she judges
- **[[orestes|Orestes]]**: his suppliant and protégé
- **[[erinyes|The Furies (Erinyes)]]**: his antagonists; he despises them as "wild goats unherded"
- **[[hermes|Hermes]]** (half-brother): commissioned to guide [[orestes|Orestes]] to [[athens|Athens]]

## Significance in Aeschylean Thought

Apollo is the instrument through whom [[zeus|Zeus]]'s will becomes actionable in the world. He commands, purifies, and advocates — but always as the *Prophêtês Dios*, never on his own authority. This subordination of the traditional Olympian god to [[zeus|Zeus]]'s higher purpose is characteristic of Aeschylus' theology, in which all divine action converges toward [[justice-dike|justice]] and [[civic-order|civic order]].

## In Later Tradition

Apollo remained central to Greek and Roman religion. Nietzsche's *The Birth of Tragedy* made the Apollonian/Dionysian dichotomy a foundational concept of modern aesthetics, with Apollo representing form, clarity, individuation, and the principium individuationis. The [[delphic-oracle|Delphic Oracle]] continued to function into the Roman imperial period, a testament to Apollo's enduring cultural authority.
