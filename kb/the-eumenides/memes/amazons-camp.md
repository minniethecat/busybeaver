# The Amazons' Camp

The Amazons' Camp is the name given in *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]* to the fortress the Amazons built on the [[areopagus|Areopagus]] hill during their mythic invasion of [[athens|Athens]]. In [[athena|Athena]]'s founding speech at the conclusion of [[orestes|Orestes]]' trial, she recounts how the hill received its name — the [[areopagus-hill|Hill of Ares]] — because the Amazons sacrificed to [[ares|Ares]] there before battling [[theseus|Theseus]]. The camp is thus the mythic antecedent of the court, linking the hill's martial past to its judicial future.

## Overview

[[the-areopagus-court|The Areopagus]] (Άρειος Πάγος, "[[areopagus-hill|Hill of Ares]]") is a rocky outcrop northwest of the Athenian [[acropolis|Acropolis]].

In historical [[athens|Athens]] it was the seat of the [[the-areopagus-court|Council of the Areopagus]], the ancient aristocratic court that tried homicide cases.

In *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]*, Aeschylus provides the mythological aetiology of both the hill's name and the court's foundation: the Amazons, invading [[athens|Athens]] to punish [[theseus|Theseus]] for abducting their queen Antiope, built a fortress on the hill and offered sacrifice to [[ares|Ares]] there.

[[athena|Athena]] transforms this site of war into a site of law.


## In The Eumenides

[[athena|Athena]]'s founding speech directly connects the Amazons' camp to the court she establishes: "On this mountain shall it be, Here in the Amazons' most virgin hold, Who came in wrath for [[theseus|Theseus]]' wrongs of old Embattled, and this fortress against ours, Hill against hill, towers against soaring towers, Built, and to [[ares|Ares]] on the rock with flame Gave sacrifice: whence comes its awful name, The Rock, the Mount, of [[ares|Ares]]" (001-060).

The etymology is both martial and sacred. The name "[[areopagus|Areopagus]]" derives not from a battle — the Amazons were defeated — but from their act of sacrifice: they gave offerings to [[ares|Ares]], god of war, on the summit. The hill that will become the seat of [[athens|Athens]]'s highest homicide court was first consecrated by its invaders, and [[athena|Athena]]'s founding of the court continues that consecration under a new dispensation.

The speech then pivots to [[athena|Athena]]'s vision of the court: "All things here Being holy, Reverence and her sister, Fear, In darkness as in daylight shall restrain From all unrighteousness the sons of men" (001-060). The Amazons' sacrifice to [[ares|Ares]] — an act of war — is transformed into the citizens' reverence and fear — the psychological foundations of law.

## Mythological Associations

The myth of the Amazon invasion of [[athens|Athens]] belongs to the [[theseus|Theseus]] cycle. After [[theseus|Theseus]] abducted the Amazon queen Antiope (or Hippolyta), the Amazons marched on [[athens|Athens]] in retaliation and besieged the [[acropolis|Acropolis]].

They were defeated, but their presence on the [[areopagus|Areopagus]] left a permanent mark on the city's sacred topography.

Aeschylus uses this myth to give the newly-founded court the weight of antiquity: the [[areopagus|Areopagus]] was sacred before it was judicial, consecrated by enemies before it was claimed by citizens.


## Cult and Ritual

[[the-areopagus-court|The Areopagus]] was associated with [[ares|Ares]] from earliest times. Aeschylus's aetiology — the Amazons' sacrifice — is one variant of a broader tradition connecting the hill to the god of war. By the classical period, the hill housed not only the homicide court but also shrines to the [[erinyes|Erinyes]] (the [[erinyes|Semnai Theai]], "August Goddesses"), who received their new home beneath the hill after their transformation into the [[erinyes|Eumenides]].
