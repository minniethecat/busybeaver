# The Trial of Orestes

The **[[trial-of-orestes|Trial of Orestes]]** is the climactic event of [[the-eumenides|The Eumenides]], in which [[athena|Athena]] establishes the [[the-areopagus-court|Areopagus Court]] on [[areopagus-hill|Areopagus Hill]] and [[orestes|Orestes]] is tried for the murder of his mother [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]. As the endnotes observe, the interest lies in "the foundation of the [[areopagus|Court of the Areopagus]], as a tribunal superseding the blood-feud, the ordeal by [[oath|oath]], and all the rigid and unreasoning practices of primitive justice." (002-033)

## The Proceedings

[[athena|Athena]] summons "the noblest of the breed" — twelve Athenian citizens — to serve as jurors. [[apollo|Apollo]] appears as advocate and witness for [[orestes|Orestes]]; the [[erinyes|Furies]] serve as prosecutors, arguing the case for the dead [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]. The trial is the first homicide trial in Athenian myth, and it establishes the precedent for all subsequent judgments.

## Apollo's Arguments

[[apollo|Apollo]] makes two principal arguments for [[orestes|Orestes]]' acquittal:
1. [[orestes|Orestes]] killed at [[apollo|Apollo]]'s command — "If thou didst kill thy mother, was it not my word and will?" — and [[apollo|Apollo]] speaks as the *Prophêtês Dios*, the revealer of [[zeus|Zeus]]'s will
2. The mother is not truly a parent — she merely "nourishes the seed" planted by the father. As proof, [[apollo|Apollo]] cites [[athena|Athena]] herself, born from [[zeus|Zeus]]'s head alone, with no mother

## Athena's Judgment

The jury's vote is evenly divided. [[athena|Athena]], as president of the court, casts the deciding vote for acquittal — "for I am utterly the Father's" (738). Her vote is not a legal argument but an act of alignment with [[zeus|Zeus]]'s will.

The tied vote and [[athena|Athena]]'s intervention have been interpreted in many ways. Some see it as a recognition of the moral complexity of [[orestes|Orestes]]' situation — the impossibility of a clear verdict. Others read it as a demonstration that justice sometimes requires more than mechanical application of law: it requires the wisdom of a person.

## The Furies' Response

The [[erinyes|Furies]] initially threaten to blight [[athens|Athens]] in revenge for their defeat. The [[erinyes|Furies]], bewildered by this new form of trial, "begin to realize that there are coming into the world new forces which are destined to supplant them." (002-031) But [[athena|Athena]] persuades them to accept a new role — as honored guardians of the city, receiving worship and offerings. Their [[transformation-of-the-furies|transformation]] from [[erinyes|Furies]] to [[erinyes|Eumenides]] ("[[erinyes|Kindly Ones]]") is the resolution of the play.
