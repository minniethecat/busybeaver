# The Blood-Feud

The blood-feud is the primitive institution of kin-vengeance that forms the background and the dramatic engine of Aeschylus' *Oresteia*. In *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]*, the blood-feud is the old order — the system in which "the doer must suffer" through a chain of retaliatory killings — that is superseded by the civic justice of the [[areopagus|Areopagus]]. The play's central achievement is to dramatize this transition from private vengeance to public law.

## Definition

Murray's Introduction provides the classic account. The law that "the doer must suffer" was "mainly envisaged by the most primitive Greek tradition as a form of the blood-feud. It is blood calling for blood" (P01-001).

At its simplest, when a man kills someone of another tribe, "there is no 'sin' or 'pollution' in this, though of course the kindred of the stranger will duly try to avenge him" (P01-001).

The blood-feud is thus, in its simplest form, not a moral matter but a social obligation — the duty of kin to avenge kin.


When the killing is of a kinsman rather than a stranger, the blood-feud becomes entangled with pollution (*miasma*). The killer "has shed the 'kindred blood,' and committed a real sin.

He is polluted, and pollutes all who associate with him" (P01-001).

Yet the obligation to avenge still holds: "If the kinsman has left any children or male representatives, they are bound in honour to take up the blood-feud and do vengeance, even though, if the relationship is very close, it may bring pollution on them" (P01-001).


This paradox is the engine of the entire *Oresteia*: [[aegisthus|Aegisthus]] acts correctly in killing [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] (avenging his father [[thyestes|Thyestes]]), [[orestes|Orestes]] acts correctly in killing [[aegisthus|Aegisthus]] (avenging his father [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]), and [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] acts correctly in killing [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] (avenging her daughter [[iphigenia|Iphigenia]]). Every act of vengeance is both required (by the blood-feud) and polluting (because it sheds kindred blood). The chain is endless.

## In The Eumenides

*The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]* is the play in which the blood-feud is finally broken. The trial before the [[areopagus|Areopagus]] does not deny the validity of the blood-feud — both sides present legitimate claims — but establishes a higher court of appeal. The [[erinyes|Furies]], as the supernatural embodiment of the blood-feud, are not defeated but transformed.

Murray frames this as the foundation of civilization itself: "the interest lies in the foundation of the [[areopagus|Court of the Areopagus]], as a tribunal superseding the blood-feud, the ordeal by oath, and all the rigid and unreasoning practices of primitive justice, by a justice which can understand and therefore sympathize" [Murray, Introduction].

The blood-feud's logic is mechanical: if A kills B, B's kin must kill A. [[the-areopagus-court|The Areopagus]] introduces deliberation: was the killing justified? What were the circumstances? What does [[zeus|Zeus]] will? The court does not abolish vengeance but transforms it into judgment — a decision rendered collectively, by citizens, under divine guidance.

## In Greek Thought

The blood-feud was a historical reality in early Greece, traces of which survive in Homer (the *Iliad*'s references to *poinē*, blood-price) and in the legal codes of Draco and Solon, which sought to limit rather than eliminate private vengeance. The Athenian homicide courts, of which the [[areopagus|Areopagus]] was the oldest, represented the city's gradual monopolization of the right to punish — a process that continued throughout the classical period.

Aeschylus' myth of the [[areopagus|Areopagus]]'s founding is thus an aetiology not only of a specific court but of the very principle of public justice. The blood-feud is not wrong; it is incomplete. What the [[areopagus|Areopagus]] adds is the capacity to weigh, to deliberate, to distinguish — and, when appropriate, to forgive.
