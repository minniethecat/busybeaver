# Agamemnon (Character Analysis)

**[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]** is the absent center of [[the-eumenides|The Eumenides]]. Though he never appears on stage, his murder — and the question of its justice — drives every aspect of the play's action.

## The Absent King

[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s absence from the stage is dramatically significant. He is the dead father whose blood cries out for vengeance, yet also the husband whose own crime — the [[sacrifice-of-iphigenia|sacrifice of Iphigenia]] — makes him a morally compromised victim. His silence allows his legacy to be contested: is he the rightful king murdered by a treacherous wife, or the child-killer who got what he deserved?

In the *Choëphoroe*, the second play of the trilogy, [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s presence is felt through his grave — which dominates the stage as the focal point of [[orestes|Orestes]]' and [[electra|Electra]]'s invocation of their father's spirit (002-004). The grave scene transforms [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] from an absent victim into an active force demanding vengeance from beyond death.

## Murray's Analysis

Murray notes that the death of [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] was, by the logic of the blood-feud, justified: [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] was avenging her daughter. And [[aegisthus|Aegisthus]], "as son of [[thyestes|Thyestes]], acted quite correctly in killing [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]." Yet the same logic also demands that [[orestes|Orestes]] avenge [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]. The problem is not that anyone acted wrongly — each act of vengeance was "correct" — but that the logic itself is endless. (P01-001)

> The law that "The doer must suffer," or that Sin must bring Punishment, is mainly envisaged by the most primitive Greek tradition as a form of the blood-feud. It is blood calling for blood.
>
> (P01-001)

## The Father's Claim

[[apollo|Apollo]]'s defense of [[orestes|Orestes]] rests on the claim that [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] was a "godlike king, a scepter-bearer," murdered not in war but in his own home, by treachery. The father's blood, [[apollo|Apollo]] argues, is more sacred than the mother's — a gendered argument that [[athena|Athena]]'s deciding vote endorses.
