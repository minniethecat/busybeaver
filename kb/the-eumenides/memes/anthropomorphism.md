# Anthropomorphism in Greek Religion

**Anthropomorphism** (Greek: ἀνθρωπομορφισμός, "human-shaped") is the representation of gods in human form. Murray's Introduction to [[the-eumenides|The Eumenides]] identifies anthropomorphism as a crucial advance in Greek religion — and as central to Aeschylus' theological project.

## Murray's Analysis

Murray argues that the "great advance made by [the Olympian] system as compared with the welter of primitive taboos and terrors" lies in the "humanizing of the nonhuman":

"It brought to man the Good News that, as Plutarch expresses it, 'the world is not ruled by fabulous Typhons and Giants' — nor, we may add, by blind mechanical laws — 'but by One who is a wise Father to all.' It sought to make religion humane at the expense of making it anthropomorphic." (P01-015)

## From Mechanical Law to Personal Judgment

This anthropomorphism is essential to the Oresteia's resolution. The [[justice-dike|law of the blood-feud]] operates mechanically: blood demands blood, without understanding or mercy. But [[zeus|Zeus]], as a person — as a Father — can learn, understand, and forgive. The appeal from law to a person, which [[athena|Athena]] facilitates, is possible only because the Olympian gods are persons, not impersonal forces.

## Critique of Anthropomorphism

Murray acknowledges the critique — that anthropomorphism makes religion "too human," reducing the divine to human proportions. But for Aeschylus, this was the great achievement: the gods could be approached, reasoned with, and reconciled. The "mechanical and automatic operation of the Law is corrected by the will of the Father." (P01-012)

> Athena likewise, we are told with emphasis, is the daughter of Zeus alone, with no mother. She is pure, undiluted Zeus (664 ff.).
