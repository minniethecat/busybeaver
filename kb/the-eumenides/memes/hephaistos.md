# Hephaistos

**Hephaistos** (Greek: Ἥφαιστος; Latin: Hephaestus) is the god of fire, metalworking, and craftsmanship. He is mentioned in [[the-eumenides|The Eumenides]] in connection with the foundation myth of [[delphi|Delphi]].

## In The Eumenides

The [[pythian-prophetess|Pythian Prophetess]] recounts in her opening speech how the people who first escorted [[apollo|Apollo]] to [[delphi|Delphi]] were "a folk born of Hephaistos." These people, led by King [[delphos|Delphos]], served as pioneers who prepared the sacred way for the god, "making sweet a bitter land" (001-033).

## Mythological Significance

In Greek mythology, Hephaistos is the divine craftsman who forges the weapons and armor of the gods. He is particularly associated with [[athens|Athens]] — the Athenians claimed descent from Hephaistos through their legendary king [[erichthonius|Erichthonius]]. This Athenian connection is significant given the play's movement toward [[athens|Athens]] as the site of resolution.

Hephaistos was lame, having been thrown from Olympus by either [[zeus|Zeus]] or [[hera|Hera]], depending on the version of the myth. Despite his physical imperfection, he was married to Aphrodite and was honored as the patron of artisans and craftsmen.

## Role in the Play

The reference to Hephaistos' people serves to establish the peaceful, constructive nature of [[apollo|Apollo]]'s arrival at [[delphi|Delphi]]. They are pioneers and road-builders, not conquerors. This detail reinforces Aeschylus' deliberate revision of the traditional myth in which [[apollo|Apollo]] seized [[delphi|Delphi]] by violence — here, the god arrives attended by Hephaistos' craftsmen, transforming a wilderness into a civilized sanctuary.
