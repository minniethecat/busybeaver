# Hermes

Hermes, the messenger of the gods and the guide of travelers and souls, appears in *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]* only through a single command from [[apollo|Apollo]]: "Do thou, O Hermes, brother of my blood, Watch over him. Thou guide of man, make good The name thou bearest, shepherding again My suppliant" (001-002). Though he never appears on stage, his commission as [[orestes|Orestes]]' guide establishes the divine infrastructure that enables the suppliant's journey from [[delphi|Delphi]] to [[athens|Athens]].

## Role in the Play

[[apollo|Apollo]]'s invocation of Hermes is brief but theologically significant. Murray comments: "Him who pitieth suffering men [[zeus|Zeus]] pitieth, and his ways are sweet on earth" (001-002).

Hermes' role as guide — *psychopompos*, the conductor of souls — extends here to the living: he shepherds the suffering suppliant across the earth, just as he shepherds the dead to the underworld.

The journey from [[delphi|Delphi]] to [[athens|Athens]], from pollution to purification, from the old law to the new, is itself sacred — and Hermes is its guardian.


## Mythological Background

Hermes was the son of [[zeus|Zeus]] and Maia, the messenger of the gods, the protector of travelers, merchants, and thieves, and the guide of souls to the underworld (*psychopompos*). His distinctive attributes — winged sandals, the herald's staff (*kerykeion*), a traveler's hat — marked him as the god of boundaries and transitions: between gods and mortals, between the living and the dead, between one place and another.
