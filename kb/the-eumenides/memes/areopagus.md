# Areopagus (Court of Athens)

[[the-areopagus-court|The Areopagus]] (Greek: Ἄρειος Πάγος, "[[areopagus-hill|Hill of Ares]]") is the ancient Athenian council and homicide court that, in Aeschylus' *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]*, is founded by [[athena|Athena]] to try [[orestes|Orestes]] for the murder of his mother [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]. The play presents this founding as the pivotal moment in the transition from the blood-feud — the old, mechanical law of *Dikê* — to civic justice administered by a human court under divine guidance.

## Definition

In historical [[athens|Athens]], the Areopagus was the oldest council, composed of ex-archons, and held jurisdiction over homicide cases, religious matters, and certain political offenses. It met on the [[areopagus-hill|Hill of Ares]], northwest of the [[acropolis|Acropolis]]. By Aeschylus' time (the 5th century BCE), its powers had been reduced by the democratic reforms of [[ephialtes|Ephialtes]] (462 BCE), though it retained its homicide jurisdiction and religious prestige. (002-041)

Aeschylus' account in *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]* is an aetiological myth — a story that explains the origin of an existing institution. By tracing the court's founding to [[athena|Athena]] herself, with the participation of [[apollo|Apollo]] and the acquiescence of the [[erinyes|Furies]], Aeschylus gives the Areopagus a divine charter. This was politically significant: the play was produced in 458 BCE, just four years after [[ephialtes|Ephialtes]]' reforms, and can be read as an argument for preserving the Areopagus's traditional authority. (002-041)

## In The Eumenides

The trial before the Areopagus occupies the central third of the play. [[athena|Athena]], having heard [[orestes|Orestes]]' supplication at her statue on the [[acropolis|Acropolis]], establishes the court as a permanent institution. In her founding speech, she declares: "I will judges find in mine own City, who will make no blind Oath-challenge to pursuer and pursued, but follow this new rule, by me indued as law for ever" (001-070). She later proclaims: "This Council and this Judgement Seat by me are stablisht" (001-113).

Murray summarizes the significance: "the interest lies in the foundation of the Court of the Areopagus, as a tribunal superseding the blood-feud, the ordeal by oath, and all the rigid and unreasoning practices of primitive justice, by a justice which can understand and therefore sympathize" (002-033).

The trial itself is structured as a formal legal proceeding: the [[erinyes|Furies]] serve as prosecutors, [[apollo|Apollo]] as witness and advocate for the defense, and [[athena|Athena]] as presiding judge. Twelve Athenian citizens — the first jury — cast votes. [[athena|Athena]]'s deciding vote for acquittal, cast after the jury divides evenly, establishes the precedent that a tied vote favors the defendant — the origin of the Athenian legal principle later known as "the vote of [[athena|Athena]]."

Crucially, the Areopagus does not replace the divine order but integrates it. The [[erinyes|Furies]] are not banished but given a permanent home beneath the Areopagus hill, becoming the [[erinyes|Eumenides]] ("[[erinyes|Kindly Ones]]") — guardians of [[athens|Athens]] who bless the land rather than curse it. The court thus embodies the synthesis of the old law (chthonic, retributive, mechanical) and the new (Olympian, merciful, intelligent). (P01-012)

## In Greek Thought

[[the-areopagus-court|The Areopagus]] remained a central institution in Athenian civic identity throughout the classical period. Aristotle, in the *Athenian Constitution*, traces its history from the aristocratic council of the pre-democratic period to its reduced but still prestigious role in the 4th century. The orator Demosthenes invokes it as a symbol of [[athens|Athens]]' ancestral piety and legal wisdom. (002-041)

Aeschylus' myth of the court's founding had lasting cultural influence. The idea that law courts represent a civilizing advance over private vengeance — that justice requires institutions, not just principles — is one of the play's most enduring contributions to Western political thought.
