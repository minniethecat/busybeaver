# Dionysus

**Dionysus** (Greek: Διόνυσος), also called **Bromios** ("the Roarer"), is the god of wine, ecstasy, ritual madness, and theater. He is mentioned in [[the-eumenides|The Eumenides]] in connection with the sacred landscape of [[mount-parnassus|Mount Parnassus]], where his Maenadic rites were practiced.

## In The Eumenides

The [[pythian-prophetess|Pythian Prophetess]] invokes Dionysus immediately after describing the [[corycian-cave|Corycian Cave]] in her opening prayer (001-001):

"And Bromios walks the mountain, well I know, Since first he led his Maenad host on high And doomed King [[pentheus|Pentheus]] like a hare to die."

The reference places Dionysus within the chain of sacred powers occupying [[mount-parnassus|Parnassus]] — a chain that runs from the [[chthonian|chthonian]] powers ([[mother-earth|Earth]], the [[the-nymphs|Nymphs]]) through the ecstatic (Dionysus) to the [[olympian-order|Olympian]] ([[apollo|Apollo]]). This sequence is central to the play's theological geography.

## Maenadic Rites

The Maenads (Bacchae) were female followers of Dionysus who performed ecstatic dances on the mountainside. The prophetess's reference to the death of King [[pentheus|Pentheus]] — torn apart by Maenads in Euripides' *Bacchae* — signals the dangerous, untamed power of Dionysiac worship.

## Role in the Play

Though Dionysus is not a character in *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]*, his invocation situates the [[delphi|Delphi]]c sanctuary within a broader sacred landscape. The prophetess acknowledges all the powers of [[mount-parnassus|Parnassus]] before taking her seat — a gesture of comprehensive piety that reflects the play's concern with the proper ordering of divine powers, old and new.
