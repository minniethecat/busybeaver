# Aulis

**Aulis** (Greek: Αὐλίς) was a harbor in Boeotia, on the Euripus Strait. It is a crucial location in the backstory of [[the-eumenides|The Eumenides]], for it was here that [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] sacrificed his daughter [[iphigenia|Iphigenia]]. (P01-008)

## The Sacrifice at Aulis

As Murray's Introduction explains, the Greek fleet assembled at Aulis to sail for [[troy|Troy]]. But [[artemis|Artemis]], angered by [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s killing of a sacred animal, stilled the winds. The prophet [[calchas|Calchas]] declared that only the sacrifice of [[iphigenia|Iphigenia]], [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s daughter, would appease the goddess and allow the fleet to sail. (P01-008)

[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]], faced with the choice between his duty as commander and his love as a father, lured [[iphigenia|Iphigenia]] to Aulis under the pretext of marriage to [[achilles|Achilles]] — and sacrificed her. This act, the first murder in the chain, sets the entire Oresteia in motion.

## In the Chain of Vengeance

The sacrifice at Aulis is the origin point of the trilogy's cycle of blood:
1. **Aulis**: [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] kills [[iphigenia|Iphigenia]]
2. **[[argos|Argos]]**: [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] kills [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]
3. **[[argos|Argos]]**: [[orestes|Orestes]] kills [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]]
4. **[[delphi|Delphi]] → [[athens|Athens]]**: The [[erinyes|Furies]] pursue [[orestes|Orestes]]

Each crime demands another, and the chain seems unbreakable — until [[athena|Athena]]'s intervention at [[athens|Athens]].

## Aeschylean Ambiguity

Aeschylus' treatment of [[iphigenia|Iphigenia]]'s death is characteristically ambiguous. In the *[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]*, the chorus describes the sacrifice in harrowing detail but does not clearly condemn [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]. The question — was he right to obey the goddess? — is left open, and it is this very ambiguity that the trial in *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]* must ultimately resolve.
