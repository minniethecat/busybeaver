# Clytemnestra

Clytemnestra, the murdered queen of [[argos|Mycenae]] and mother of [[orestes|Orestes]], appears in *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]* only as a ghost — a specter who rises from the underworld to rouse the sleeping [[erinyes|Furies]] and demand that they pursue her son for the matricide. Though she speaks only a single scene, her ghostly presence drives the play's first movement: it is her wrath that wakes the [[erinyes|Furies]], and their pursuit of [[orestes|Orestes]] is in service of her claim.

## Role in the Play

Clytemnestra's ghost enters after [[orestes|Orestes]] has departed and [[apollo|Apollo]] has exited, finding the [[erinyes|Furies]] asleep at [[delphi|Delphi]]. Her address to them is bitter, urgent, and accusatory: "Ye sleep. O God, and what are sleepers worth?

'Tis you, have left me among all the dead Dishonoured" (001-007).

She contrasts her own dishonored state — "Alway, for that blood I shed, Rebuke and hissing cease not, and I go Wandering in shame" — with the absence of any pursuit of [[orestes|Orestes]]: "for his Who smote me, being of my blood, there is No wrath in all the darkness: there is none Cares for a mother murdered by her son" (001-007).


She shows them the wound in her throat — "Open thine heart to see this gash!" — and reminds them of the offerings she made to them in life: "Oft of my bounty ye have lapt your fill; Oft the sad peace of wineless cups to earth I have poured, and midmurk feastings on your hearth Burned" (001-007). The [[erinyes|Furies]], she argues, owe her their service.

When they moan in their sleep but do not wake, her frustration escalates: "Moan on, but he is vanished and forgot. So strong the prayers of them that love me not!" (001-007). Finally, as they stir: "Awake! Be strong!

And faithful keep Thy lust of pain through all the drugs of sleep. Thou feelst my scorn? Aye, feel and agonize Within; such words are scourges to the wise. Thy blood-mist fold about him, like a doom.

Waste him with vapour from thy burning womb. A second chase is death! Pursue! Pursue!" (001-007).


Her role is paradoxical: she is a victim demanding justice, but she is also a murderer (of [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]) who was herself killed justly (as vengeance for her crime). The [[erinyes|Furies]]' commitment to her cause, while [[apollo|Apollo]] champions [[orestes|Orestes]], perfectly captures the moral impasse that only the [[areopagus|Areopagus]] can resolve.

## Mythological Background

Clytemnestra was the daughter of [[tyndareus|Tyndareus of Sparta]] and [[leda|Leda]], and the sister of Helen. She married [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]], king of [[argos|Mycenae]], and bore him several children, including [[iphigenia|Iphigenia]], [[electra|Electra]], and [[orestes|Orestes]].

When [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] sacrificed [[iphigenia|Iphigenia]] to obtain favorable winds for the [[the-trojan-war|Trojan War]], Clytemnestra's resentment festered.

During his ten-year absence at [[troy|Troy]], she took [[aegisthus|Aegisthus]] ([[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s cousin and hereditary enemy) as her lover.

On [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s return, she murdered him — in some versions with an axe, in Aeschylus' *[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]* with a net and a sword — and ruled [[argos|Mycenae]] with [[aegisthus|Aegisthus]] until [[orestes|Orestes]], commanded by [[apollo|Apollo]], avenged his father by killing them both.


## Relationships

- **[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]** (husband): murdered by Clytemnestra in revenge for [[iphigenia|Iphigenia]]'s sacrifice
- **[[orestes|Orestes]]** (son): her murderer, whose acquittal she opposes from the grave
- **[[iphigenia|Iphigenia]]** (daughter): sacrificed by [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]; the original wrong that set the chain of vengeance in motion
- **[[aegisthus|Aegisthus]]** (lover): her partner in ruling [[argos|Mycenae]] after [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s death
- **The [[erinyes|Furies]] ([[erinyes|Erinyes]])**: the chthonic powers she summons to pursue [[orestes|Orestes]]

## Significance in Aeschylean Thought

Clytemnestra's ghost embodies the unbreakable claim of the murdered dead. Her demand for vengeance is legitimate — she was indeed murdered by her son — but her own status as a murderer complicates that legitimacy. The play does not resolve this complexity by declaring one claim valid and the other invalid; rather, it establishes a court that can hear both claims and render a judgment that is just without being mechanical.

## In Later Tradition

Clytemnestra has been one of the most compelling figures in Greek tragedy for later artists and thinkers. She is the protagonist of several modern adaptations, including Eugene O'Neill's *Mourning Becomes [[electra|Electra]]* and Jean-Paul Sartre's *The Flies*. Feminist readings have reclaimed her as a figure of resistance against patriarchal authority, while psychoanalytic readings have emphasized the primal horror of the mother-son bond she represents.
