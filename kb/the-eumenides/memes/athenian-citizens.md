# Chorus of Athenian Citizens

The Chorus of Athenian Citizens — the jury of twelve Athenians empaneled by [[athena|Athena]] to judge [[orestes|Orestes]] — are the silent witnesses to the founding of the [[areopagus|Areopagus]]. Though they appear in the Dramatis Personae of *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]* (001-072), they speak no words; their presence alone constitutes the civic body in whose name [[athena|Athena]] renders judgment. They are the first jury in the first homicide court — the human component of an institution that marries divine authority with citizen deliberation.

## Role in the Play

The Athenian citizens are present at the trial but take no active role beyond casting their votes. Their silence is dramatically significant: the trial is argued by the divine powers ([[apollo|Apollo]] for the defense, the [[erinyes|Furies]] for the prosecution) and judged by [[athena|Athena]]. The human jurors witness and ratify — they are the civic body on whose behalf the court exists.

Murray notes that the [[areopagus|Areopagus]] "supersed[es] the blood-feud, the ordeal by oath, and all the rigid and unreasoning practices of primitive justice, by a justice which can understand and therefore sympathize" [Murray, Introduction]. The Athenian jurors embody this transformation: they are not avengers but judges, not enforcers of custom but deliberators of law. Their silent vote is the mechanism through which divine will becomes civic judgment.

> The foundation of the Court of the Areopagus, as a tribunal superseding the blood-feud, the ordeal by oath, and all the rigid and unreasoning practices of primitive justice, by a justice which can understand and therefore sympathize.
