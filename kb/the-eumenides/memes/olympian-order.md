# Olympian Order

The Olympian Order is the new divine dispensation under [[zeus|Zeus]] and his children — [[apollo|Apollo]], [[athena|Athena]], [[hermes|Hermes]] — that, in Aeschylus' *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]*, supersedes and transforms the older chthonian order of [[mother-earth|Earth]], the [[erinyes|Erinyes]], and the ancestral dead. Where the old order operates through mechanical retribution — "the doer must suffer" — the Olympian order operates through understanding, persuasion, and the establishment of civic institutions.

## Definition

Murray frames the Olympian/chthonian contrast as the central theological drama of the play.

The old gods — the [[erinyes|Erinyes]] — represent "the wrath of the dead or the injured acting as a curse and pursuing the transgressor" (P01-005). They are the "especial guardians" (P01-007) of the law that blood calls for blood.

The Olympian gods — [[zeus|Zeus]], [[apollo|Apollo]], [[athena|Athena]] — represent a different principle: not the abolition of the law but its correction by a personal wisdom that can "understand and therefore sympathize" [Murray, Introduction].


The Olympian advance, Murray argues, is fundamentally a humanizing one. The Homeric Olympian religion "brought to man the Good News that... 'the world is not ruled by fabulous Typhons and Giants'... 'but by One who is a wise Father to all.' It sought to make religion humane at the expense of making it anthropomorphic" (P01-015). Aeschylus develops this insight: his [[zeus|Zeus]] is not merely the Homeric king of gods but a being capable of growth, learning, and moral development.

## In The Eumenides

The conflict between Olympian and chthonian is staged directly. The [[erinyes|Furies]] explicitly name it: "These be the deeds ye do, ye Gods of the younger race: Ye break the Law at your will; your high throne drips with gore" (001-016). From their perspective, the Olympian order is lawless innovation — the "younger gods" who "have trampled the great Laws of old beneath your chariots" (001-060).

[[apollo|Apollo]]'s contemptuous reply — "Out, ye wild goats unherded! Out, ye drove Accursed, that god nor devil dares to love!" (001-002) — expresses the Olympian disdain for the chthonian powers. Yet the play's resolution is not a simple Olympian victory. [[athena|Athena]] does not banish the [[erinyes|Furies]]; she persuades them. She offers them a permanent home in [[athens|Athens]], honors rather than suppresses them, and transforms their destructive power into a force for civic blessing.

This is the Olympian innovation: not the destruction of the old but its incorporation and transformation. "The mechanical and automatic operation of the Law is corrected by the will of the Father. It is not broken, but more truly and perfectly fulfilled" (P01-012).

## In Greek Thought

The historical development Aeschylus dramatizes — from chthonian to Olympian worship — corresponds to a real religious evolution in archaic Greece. The Homeric poems (8th century BCE) present an already-established Olympian pantheon, but traces of older, earth-based cults persist throughout Greek religion. The Eleusinian Mysteries, the hero cults, and the worship of Demeter and [[dionysus|Dionysus]] all preserved chthonian elements within the Olympian framework.

Aeschylus' achievement is to transform this historical process into a theological argument. The Olympian order is not merely newer or more powerful; it is morally superior because it can learn, forgive, and establish institutions — the [[areopagus|Areopagus]] — that embody justice without perpetuating vengeance.
