# The Law of the Moirai

The **Law of the [[moira|Moira]]i** ([[moira|Fate]]s) is the mechanical, impersonal law of retribution — "the doer must suffer" — that governs the world of the [[the-blood-feud|blood-feud]]. Murray's Introduction to [[the-eumenides|The Eumenides]] identifies this law as the central problem that the play must resolve.

## Murray's Analysis

Murray describes the law as operating with the inevitability of a natural force:

"The Law of the Moirai and the [[erinyes|Erinyes]] neither understands nor forgives. It simply operates." (P01-009)

This law is not evil — it maintains cosmic order — but it is mechanical. It cannot distinguish between just and unjust killing, between the murder of [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] and the matricide of [[orestes|Orestes]]. Both shed [[kindred-blood|kindred blood]]; both must suffer. The law is blind.

## Contrast with Zeus

The Aeschylean innovation is to subordinate this mechanical law to the will of [[zeus|Zeus]]. [[zeus|Zeus]], unlike the [[moira|Moira]]i, can learn and understand: "He himself in the distant past won his throne by violence, but now he has learned and his heart is changed." (P01-008) The [[zeus|Zeus]] who understands can forgive — and forgiveness, from the perspective of the [[moira|Moira]]i, is "a sort of monstrosity, a wanton breach in the law of Cause and Effect." (P01-008)

## The Resolution

[[athena|Athena]]'s verdict does not abolish the Law of the [[moira|Moira]]i. Rather, it establishes a higher court of appeal — the judgment of a person ([[zeus|Zeus]], acting through [[athena|Athena]]) over the automatic operation of law. This is the theological parallel to the legal principle of [[epieikeia|epieikeia]] (equity): the correction of law where it fails owing to its generality.

> That being so, how can there be any forgiveness? Would not forgiveness be a sort of monstrosity, a wanton breach in the law of Cause and Effect? Aeschylus, in the Agamemnon, gives his answer in unusually clear language (161–182).
>
> (P01-008)
