# Supplication (Hikesia)

Supplication (*hikesia*, Greek: Ἱκεσία) is the ancient Greek religious institution by which a person in desperate need — a fugitive, an exile, a polluted killer — could seek refuge at a god's altar and, by performing the ritual of supplication, place themselves under divine protection. In Aeschylus' *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]*, [[orestes|Orestes]]' supplication at [[delphi|Delphi]] and then at [[athena|Athena]]'s statue on the [[acropolis|Acropolis]] drives the entire plot.

## Definition

Supplication was one of the most sacred obligations in Greek religion. The ritual involved the suppliant (*hiketês*) approaching an altar or a person of authority, assuming a posture of humility (often kneeling or sitting at the altar), and holding a suppliant branch — a wool-wreathed olive bough. The person or god approached was religiously obligated to offer protection; to harm a suppliant was to commit an act of impiety that could bring divine punishment.

Murray's Introduction traces the theological significance of supplication to [[zeus|Zeus]] himself: "It is also he who instituted the law of the suppliant. He forgave the bloodstained [[ixion|Ixion]] because of his suffering and prayer.

Nay, he is not only the protector of suppliants, he is himself the Eternal Suppliant, the God and Master of all things, who forgives because he also craves for forgiveness" (P01-009).

This identification of [[zeus|Zeus]] as both the protector of suppliants and himself a kind of suppliant — one who forgives because he understands the need for forgiveness — is at the heart of Aeschylus' theology.


## In The Eumenides

The play opens with [[orestes|Orestes]] already at the altar of [[apollo|Apollo]] at [[delphi|Delphi]]: "a man abhorred of God, his body hurled [[mother-earth|Earth]]ward in desperate prayer; blood on his hand yet reeking, and a naked new-drawn brand wreathed in beseeching wool, a suppliant's weed of snow-white fleece" (001-001). The suppliant's wool-wreathed branch is explicitly described — Murray's translation captures the ritual object that marks [[orestes|Orestes]] as under divine protection.

[[apollo|Apollo]] honors the supplication and performs the ritual cleansing.

He then sends [[orestes|Orestes]] on to [[athens|Athens]], to supplicate at [[athena|Athena]]'s statue: "On till thou come to [[acropolis|Pallas' Rock]], and fold Thine arms in prayer about her image old.

In [[athens|Athens]] there be hearts to judge, there be Words that bring peace; and I shall set thee free" (001-002).

The journey from [[delphi|Delphi]] to [[athens|Athens]] is a journey from one form of supplication (ritual purification at [[apollo|Apollo]]'s shrine) to another (supplication before [[athena|Athena]]'s civic justice).


[[athena|Athena]]'s response to finding [[orestes|Orestes]] at her statue is significant.

She does not automatically grant his prayer but also does not dismiss it: "If truly in the justice of thy cause Trusting, thou clingest here in need so dire To mine own shape, hard by my deathless fire, In fearful prayer, as lost [[ixion|Ixion]] prayed, Make to all these thine answer unafraid" (001-030).

She respects the supplication but also requires — as [[zeus|Zeus]] would — that the suppliant's case be heard and judged. This is the innovation: supplication is not an automatic pardon but an entitlement to a hearing.


## In Greek Thought

Supplication was a recurring theme in Greek literature, from Homer (where Odysseus supplicates Nausicaa and later Penelope's suitors must be judged for violating the laws of hospitality and supplication) to the historians (where Herodotus and Thucydides record supplications violated as acts of impiety with political consequences). The tension between the religious obligation to protect suppliants and the political or military reasons to harm them is a persistent ethical dilemma.

Aeschylus himself wrote a play titled *Supplices* (*The [[the-supliants|Suppliants]]*), which deals with the Danaids' supplication for protection from forced marriage. In that play, as in *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]*, the institution of supplication becomes a vehicle for exploring the relationship between divine law, human justice, and the obligations of the community.
