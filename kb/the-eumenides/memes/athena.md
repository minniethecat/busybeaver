# Athena

[[athena]] (Pallas Athena) is the goddess of wisdom, the patron deity of [[athens|Athens]], and — in Aeschylus' *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]* — the divine founder of the [[the-areopagus-court|Areopagus court]] and the agent of [[zeus|Zeus]]'s higher justice.

She is, as Murray's Introduction emphasizes, "pure, undiluted [[zeus|Zeus]]" (P01-012): born from [[zeus|Zeus]]'s head alone, with no mother, she embodies his thought and executes his will through persuasion rather than force.

Her role in the play bridges the Olympian order and the chthonic powers, transforming the [[erinyes|Furies]] into the [[erinyes|Eumenides]] — the "[[erinyes|Kindly Ones]]" who bless [[athens|Athens]].


## Role in the Play

Athena enters *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]* at its midpoint, responding to [[orestes|Orestes]]' supplication at her statue on the [[acropolis|Acropolis]]. Her first words establish her dual identity: she has been away at [[troy|Troy]], receiving land from the sons of [[theseus|Theseus]], but she has heard [[orestes|Orestes]]' cry and come to investigate. She is at once a goddess of war (returning from [[troy|Troy]]) and a goddess of justice (responding to a suppliant).

Her role is tripartite: judge, persuader, and founder. As judge, she presides over the [[trial-of-orestes|trial]], hears both sides, and casts the deciding vote for acquittal.

As persuader, she convinces the [[erinyes|Furies]] — ancient, implacable, and furious at their defeat — to accept a new role as guardians of [[athens|Athens]].

As founder, she establishes the [[areopagus|Areopagus]] as a permanent court, creating the institution through which "the mechanical and automatic operation of the Law is corrected by the will of the Father" (P01-012).


Her deciding vote is the dramatic climax of the trial. She explains: "for I am utterly the Father's" [Athena, [[erinyes|Eumenides]]]. Her judgment is not independent of [[zeus|Zeus]] but identical with his — she is "pure, undiluted [[zeus|Zeus]]" — and yet her method is her own: persuasion, reasoning, the offer of honor and a permanent home to the defeated [[erinyes|Furies]].

Murray observes that Athena's persuasion of the [[erinyes|Furies]] is the play's deepest theological act: "the Law that can pardon and understand can itself be understood and loved. Its ministers are no longer alien and hostile beings... They are accepted by Athena as fellow-citizens, and their Law recognized as an inward aspiration, a standard of right living which men consciously need and seek" (P01-018).

## Mythological Background

Athena's birth from [[zeus|Zeus]]'s head — fully armed, with no mother — is one of the most distinctive myths in the Greek pantheon. In Hesiod's *Theogony*, [[zeus|Zeus]] swallows Metis (Wisdom) while she is pregnant with Athena, and Athena is subsequently born from his head. This genealogy is central to Aeschylus' use of Athena in *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]*: she is "pure, undiluted [[zeus|Zeus]]" precisely because she has no maternal inheritance, no chthonian element, no connection to the old order of [[mother-earth|Earth]] and the [[erinyes|Erinyes]].

Athena was the patron deity of [[athens|Athens]], and her temple — the Parthenon — dominated the [[acropolis|Acropolis]]. Her festival, the [[pan|Pan]]athenaea, was the city's most important religious celebration. Aeschylus, by making Athena the founder of the [[areopagus|Areopagus]], gives [[athens|Athens]]'s oldest court a divine charter tied to the city's patron goddess.

## Relationships

- **[[zeus|Zeus]]** (father): the source of her wisdom and authority; she is "utterly the Father's"
- **[[apollo|Apollo]]** (half-brother): both children of [[zeus|Zeus]]; [[apollo|Apollo]] purifies and advocates, Athena judges
- **[[orestes|Orestes]]**: the suppliant whose case she adjudicates
- **[[erinyes|The Furies (Erinyes)]]**: the ancient powers she persuades to become the [[erinyes|Eumenides]]
- **[[athens|Athens]]**: the city she protects and for which she establishes the [[areopagus|Areopagus]]

## Significance in Aeschylean Thought

Athena embodies the Aeschylean synthesis. She is neither the blind enforcer of the old law (the [[erinyes|Furies]]) nor the remote source of divine command ([[zeus|Zeus]]) but the active, intelligent mediator between them. Her method — persuasion, honor, institutional creation — demonstrates that [[justice-dike|justice]] need not be imposed by force; it can be achieved through reason and the [[transformation-of-the-furies|transformation]] of relationships. This synthesis is the theological heart of *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]*, connecting the [[olympian-order|Olympian]] and [[chthonian]] realms.

## In Later Tradition

Athena remained the preeminent symbol of [[athens|Athens]] throughout antiquity. In Plato's *Timaeus*, an Egyptian priest tells Solon that [[athens|Athens]] was founded by Athena, who "chose the place in which you were born because she saw that the well-tempered climate would produce men of the greatest wisdom." [[the-areopagus-court|The Areopagus]], too, retained its cultural prestige long after its political powers were reduced — a testament to the enduring influence of Aeschylus' [[founding-of-the-areopagus|foundation myth]].
