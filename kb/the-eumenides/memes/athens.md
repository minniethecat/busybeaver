# Athens

[[athens]] is the city of [[athena|Athena]], the patron goddess of wisdom and civic justice.

In Aeschylus' *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]*, Athens is the destination of [[orestes|Orestes]]' journey from [[delphi|Delphi]] and the site of the play's culminating action: the [[trial-of-orestes|trial]] before the [[the-areopagus-court|Areopagus]], the [[founding-of-the-areopagus|founding of the homicide court]], and the [[transformation-of-the-furies|transformation of the Furies]] into the [[erinyes|Eumenides]].

The city is not merely a backdrop but the play's political subject — the place where divine [[justice-dike|justice]] becomes [[civic-order|civic justice]].


## Location

Athens lies in the region of Attica, on a plain surrounded by mountains and open to the sea. Its heart is the [[acropolis|Acropolis]] — the rocky hill crowned with [[athena|Athena]]'s temple — and nearby, the [[areopagus-hill|Hill of Ares]] ([[areopagus|Areopagus]]), where the ancient homicide court met. In Aeschylus' time, Athens was the leading city of the Delian League and the cultural center of the Greek world.

## In The Eumenides

Athens is more than a setting in *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]*; it is the play's political subject. The city is presented as the place where divine justice becomes civic justice, where the [[olympian-order|Olympian order]] establishes its earthly institution. [[athena|Athena]]'s connection to the city is personal and ancient: she has received land from the sons of [[theseus|Theseus]] at [[troy|Troy]], and she returns to the [[acropolis|Acropolis]] as to her home.

[[the-areopagus-court|The Areopagus]], founded on the [[areopagus-hill|Hill of Ares]], is specifically an Athenian institution. The twelve jurors are [[athenian-citizens|Athenian citizens]]. The blessings the [[erinyes|Eumenides]] pronounce at the play's end are explicitly for Athens — fertility for the land, health for the people, concord among citizens, victory in war. The city is not merely the beneficiary of the cosmic reconciliation; it is its embodiment.

## Religious Significance

Athens was the primary cult center of [[athena|Athena]], whose temple — the Parthenon — dominated the [[acropolis|Acropolis]]. The city's religious calendar was organized around her festivals, particularly the [[pan|Pan]]athenaea. The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]' shrine beneath the [[areopagus|Areopagus]] was an important site of chthonian worship, and the [[areopagus|Areopagus]] court itself retained religious authority throughout antiquity.

Aeschylus, by making the founding of the [[areopagus|Areopagus]] the culmination of the Oresteia, claims divine sanction for Athens's most venerable institution. The play is, among other things, a work of civic theology — a myth that explains how Athens became, by the will of [[zeus|Zeus]] and the agency of [[athena|Athena]], the city where cosmic justice became human law.
