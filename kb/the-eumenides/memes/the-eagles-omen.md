# The Eagles' Omen

The **Eagles' Omen** was the portent interpreted by the seer [[calchas|Calchas]] at [[aulis|Aulis]], as described in the *[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]* and discussed at length in Murray's Introduction to [[the-eumenides|The Eumenides]].

## The Portent

Two eagles — representing [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]] and [[menelaus|Menelaus]] — were seen devouring a pregnant hare. The hare, heavy with young, symbolized both fertility and vulnerability. [[artemis|Artemis]], as protectress of wild creatures and patron of childbirth, was enraged by this sacrilege.

## Calchas's Interpretation

[[calchas|Calchas]] declared that [[artemis|Artemis]] demanded the sacrifice of [[iphigenia|Iphigenia]], [[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]'s daughter, in atonement. He also foresaw the chain of vengeance that would follow: the death of [[iphigenia|Iphigenia]], the vengeance of [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]], and beyond "a yet further vista of vengeances re-avenged."

## Murray's Analysis

Murray cites the eagles' omen as the moment when Aeschylus poses the fundamental question of the trilogy: "How man can find escape from this endless chain and 'cast off from his mind the burden of futility.'" The answer — "Only in the thought of [[zeus|Zeus]], whatever [[zeus|Zeus]] may be" — is the theological core of *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]*.
