# Dikê (Justice)

*[[justice-dike|Dikê]]* (Greek: Δίκη) is the personified concept of justice in ancient Greek thought, understood in Aeschylus' *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]* as the inexorable natural law that "the doer must suffer" — a law enforced by [[themis|Themis]], [[moira|Moira]], the [[erinyes|Erinyes]], and ultimately [[mother-earth|Mother Earth]].

The play traces its transformation from a mechanical, impersonal force into a justice tempered by understanding and the will of [[zeus|Zeus]], culminating in the founding of the [[the-areopagus-court|Areopagus court]].


## Definition

In the primitive Greek tradition, *Dikê* is not a moral aspiration but a description of how the cosmos actually operates: sin brings punishment as inevitably as seed matures or spring returns.

As Gilbert Murray's Introduction explains, it operates as "a natural law like the maturing of seed, or the return of spring" (P01-007).

The term is closely bound to a network of related concepts: *[[themis|Themis]]* (correct tribal custom, the thing that is always done), *[[moira|Moira]]* (each being's allotted portion, which must not be exceeded), and the *[[erinyes|Erinyes]]* (the wrath of the wronged dead, pursuing transgressors as a [[curse|curse]]).

Together these form what Murray calls "the Law of the [[moira|Moira]]i and the [[erinyes|Erinyes]]" — a law that "neither understands nor forgives. It simply operates" (P01-009).


The law manifests most starkly in the [[the-blood-feud|blood-feud]]: when kindred blood is shed, the doer is polluted, and the [[blood-pollution|pollution]] demands vengeance. This creates an endless chain — "the doer must suffer" — with each act of vengeance requiring its own retaliation. As Murray notes, the [[erinyes|Erinyes]] are "its especial guardians" (P01-007), pursuing transgressors with an impersonal fury that knows no mercy and no exception.

## In The Eumenides

Aeschylus presents the entire trilogy as a working-out of the tension between the old, mechanical *Dikê* and a new, higher justice. In the *[[agamemnon|Agamemnon]]*, [[calchas|Calchas]] describes a chain of vengeances stretching into an indefinite future; Aeschylus then asks how humanity can escape this "endless chain" and "cast off from his mind the burden of futility" (P01-008). His answer — "Only in the thought of [[zeus|Zeus]], whatever [[zeus|Zeus]] may be" — introduces the play's central theological innovation.

[[zeus|Zeus]], in Aeschylus' conception, is not merely the Homeric king of gods but "He who made a road to Thought, who established [[learning-by-suffering|Learning by Suffering]] to be an abiding law" (P01-008).

He is a god who can learn and therefore forgive — a quality the old *Dikê* entirely lacks.

Crucially, [[zeus|Zeus]] does not appear directly in *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]*; instead, he is "represented by his son and daughter, [[apollo|Apollo]] and [[athena|Athena]]" (P01-010).

[[apollo|Apollo]], as the *Prophêtês Dios* (revealer of [[zeus|Zeus]]), commands [[orestes|Orestes]] to kill [[clytemnestra|Clytemnestra]] and then defends him; [[athena|Athena]], "pure, undiluted [[zeus|Zeus]]" (P01-012), casts the deciding vote for acquittal.


The [[trial-of-orestes|trial of Orestes]] before the [[the-areopagus-court|Areopagus]] thus represents the historical transition from blood-feud justice to civic justice — from a law that mechanically demands blood for blood to a justice administered by a court that can weigh circumstances, intent, and the will of the gods. As Murray observes, "the mechanical and automatic operation of the Law is corrected by the will of the Father. It is not broken, but more truly and perfectly fulfilled" (P01-012).

The resolution is embodied in [[athena|Athena]]'s persuasion of the [[erinyes|Furies]] to become the [[erinyes|Eumenides]] ("[[erinyes|Kindly Ones]]") — guardians of [[athens|Athens]] who bless rather than curse. The Law, once alien and hostile, is "recognized as an inward aspiration, a standard of right living which men consciously need and seek" (P01-018).

## In Greek Thought

The Aeschylean treatment of *Dikê* prefigures a major theme in subsequent Greek philosophy.

Murray draws explicit parallels to Plato's *Statesman*, where "the best of all is not that a law should rule, but a man, if the man be wise and of royal nature," because a law "can never comprehend exactly what is noblest and most just for all cases" (P01-013).

He cites Aristotle's concept of *Epieikeia* ([[epieikeia|Equity]]) — "the correction of the law where it fails owing to its generality" — which must be applied by the "wise man" (P01-014).

In both philosophers, as in Aeschylus, "the ultimate justice is to be found in an appeal from a law to a person" (P01-014).


This idea, Murray argues, had far-reaching historical consequences: the Hellenistic philosophical schools (Cynic, Stoic, Epicurean) made the Wise Man the judge of right conduct; Hellenistic rulers were deified to cut juridical knots through the fiction of divine will; and the Olympian religion itself represented a humanizing advance over "the welter of primitive taboos and terrors" (P01-015).

Murray also draws a striking parallel to Pauline theology: Aeschylus' doctrine is "an early and less elaborate stage of the theological system which we associate with St. Paul: the suppression of the Law by a personal relation to a divine person" (P01-016). Notably, however, Aeschylus rejected the idea that the Law must be paid off with another victim — in Paul, sin still demands blood (Christ's), whereas in Aeschylus, "when [[orestes|Orestes]] is pardoned by the will of [[zeus|Zeus]], the [[erinyes|Furies]] yield; the Law is deemed to be satisfied" without a substitute sacrifice (P01-016).

## Key Passages

> The doer must suffer. That is *Dikê*, Justice. It is the law of Themis, of Moira, of the Erinyes, and ultimately of Mother Earth.
> (P01-002)

> (P01-009)
