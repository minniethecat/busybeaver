# The Ephetae

The **Ephetae** (Greek: οἱ Ἐφέται, "the Instructors") were a body of fifty-one judges in ancient [[athens|Athens]] associated with the court of the [[the-areopagus-court|Areopagus]]. They were traditionally responsible for trying homicide cases.

## The Fifty-One

The number fifty-one is significant: the Ephetae constituted a jury large enough to represent the community but small enough to deliberate. In Athenian tradition, they were established by [[athena|Athena]] herself when she instituted the [[the-areopagus-court|Areopagus Court]] to try [[orestes|Orestes]].

## In The Eumenides

The play does not name the Ephetae directly, but [[athena|Athena]]'s institution of the court implies a jury of Athenian citizens. [[athena|Athena]] selects "the noblest of the breed" — twelve citizens — to hear the case. This number differs from the traditional fifty-one, but the principle is the same: a body of citizens, acting as representatives of the community, exercises the power of judgment that in the old system belonged to the [[erinyes|Furies]] alone.

## From Blood-Vengeance to Jury Trial

The Ephetae represent the institutional replacement of the [[the-blood-feud|blood-feud]]. Where the old system left vengeance to the victim's kin, the new system entrusts judgment to a panel of citizens acting on behalf of the community. This transition — from private vengeance to public justice — is the central achievement celebrated in *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]*.
