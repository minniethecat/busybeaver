# The Supplication at Athens

The Supplication at Athens is the ritual act by which [[orestes|Orestes]], having been purified at [[delphi|Delphi]] and guided by [[hermes|Hermes]], claims [[athena|Athena]]'s protection by embracing her statue on the Athenian [[acropolis|Acropolis]]. It is the event that brings the goddess into the drama and initiates the judicial process that culminates in the founding of the [[areopagus|Areopagus]].

## Overview

[[orestes|Orestes]] arrives at [[athens|Athens]] after his long journey from [[delphi|Delphi]], "through the vast lands of the North, The sea-ways and the cities ringed with sea" (001-002). He finds [[athena|Athena]]'s ancient image on the [[acropolis|Acropolis]] and performs the ritual of supplication — "fold Thine arms in prayer about her image old" (001-002) — placing himself under the goddess's protection.

[[athena|Athena]], returning from [[troy|Troy]], discovers him. Her response is measured: she acknowledges the validity of his supplication but does not grant automatic acquittal. Instead, she establishes a process — the trial before the [[areopagus|Areopagus]] — through which his claim will be adjudicated. This is the innovation: supplication guarantees a hearing, not a pardon.

## Context

[[supplication|Supplication]] (*hikesia*) was one of the most sacred obligations in Greek religion. A person who performed the ritual — kneeling at an altar, holding a wool-wreathed branch — placed themselves under divine protection. To harm a suppliant was an act of impiety. Yet the [[erinyes|Furies]]' claim is also legitimate: they pursue a matricide, and the pollution he bears is real. [[athena|Athena]]'s challenge is to honor both the suppliant's right and the prosecutors' claim.

## The Event

[[athena|Athena]] enters, hears [[orestes|Orestes]]' prayer, and — critically — also hears the [[erinyes|Furies]]. She refuses to decide the case herself: "The matter is too great for mortal man To judge, nor is it right that I should try A cause of bloodshed with my single voice" (001-030 ff.). Instead, she establishes the [[areopagus|Areopagus]] — a court of Athenian citizens, under her presidency — to adjudicate the case. The supplication thus becomes the catalyst for an institutional innovation.

## Aftermath

The trial proceeds, [[orestes|Orestes]] is acquitted, and the [[areopagus|Areopagus]] becomes a permanent institution. The supplication at [[athens|Athens]], like the purification at [[delphi|Delphi]], is a necessary but not sufficient condition for the resolution: ritual cleansing and ritual supplication must be completed by civic judgment.

## In The Eumenides

The supplication marks the play's transition from [[delphi|Delphi]] to [[athens|Athens]], from the old oracular order to the new civic order, from [[apollo|Apollo]]'s ritual cleansing to [[athena|Athena]]'s institutional justice.
