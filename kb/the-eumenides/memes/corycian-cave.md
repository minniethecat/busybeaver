# Corycian Cave

The Corycian Cave (Greek: Κωρύκιον ἄντρον) is a limestone cave on the southern slopes of [[mount-parnassus|Mount Parnassus]], above [[delphi|Delphi]].

In *The [[erinyes|Eumenides]]*, it is invoked in the [[pythian-prophetess|Pythian Prophetess]]'s opening prayer as part of the sacred geography surrounding [[apollo|Apollo]]'s shrine: "the [[the-nymphs|Nymphs]] who keep Yon old Corycian bird-belovèd steep, Deep-caverned, where things blessèd come and go" (001-001).

The cave represents the chthonian face of [[delphi|Delphi]] — a place of nymphs, ecstasy, and primordial presence that coexists with the ordered Olympian oracle.


## Overview

The Corycian Cave is a large natural cavern approximately 1,400 meters above sea level on [[mount-parnassus|Mount Parnassus]], about two hours' climb from [[delphi|Delphi]]. In antiquity it was sacred to the [[the-nymphs|Corycian Nymphs]] (daughters of the river-god [[pleistos-river|Pleistos]]) and to [[pan|Pan]]. Archaeological excavations have revealed offerings from the Neolithic period through the Roman era — figurines, pottery, and bones — indicating continuous ritual use spanning millennia.

## In The Eumenides

The Prophetess includes the cave among the deities and powers she honors before entering the shrine: "Next, She before the shrine, [[athena|Pallas]], is praisèd, and the [[the-nymphs|Nymphs]] who keep Yon old Corycian bird-belovèd steep, Deep-caverned, where things blessèd come and go" (001-001).

The juxtaposition is significant: [[athena|Pallas Athena]] (Olympian, civic, the goddess of the city) and the [[the-nymphs|Corycian Nymphs]] (chthonian, wild, of the mountain) are honored in the same breath.

This pairing prefigures the play's central reconciliation — the Olympian order does not abolish the chthonian but incorporates it.


The "things blessèd" (μάκαρες) that "come and go" through the cave likely refer to the ecstatic rites of [[dionysus|Dionysus]], whose Maenads roamed [[mount-parnassus|Parnassus]].

The Prophetess mentions [[dionysus|Bromios]] ([[dionysus|Dionysus]]) immediately after the cave: "And [[dionysus|Bromios]] walks the mountain, well I know, Since first he led his Maenad host on high And doomed King [[pentheus|Pentheus]] like a hare to die" (001-001).

The cave is thus part of a chain of sacred sites that runs from the chthonian ([[mother-earth|Earth]], the [[the-nymphs|Nymphs]]) through the ecstatic ([[dionysus|Dionysus]]) to the Olympian ([[apollo|Apollo]]).


## Mythological Associations

The [[the-nymphs|Corycian Nymphs]] — Corycia, Cleodora, and Melaina — were daughters of the river [[pleistos-river|Pleistos]]. The cave was also associated with [[pan|Pan]], whose presence on [[mount-parnassus|Parnassus]] is attested in cult. The cave's location above [[delphi|Delphi]] placed it within the sacred landscape of the most important oracular site in the Greek world: pilgrims to [[delphi|Delphi]] would pass the cave on their ascent, experiencing the full spectrum of sacred geography from mountain wildness to ordered sanctuary.

## Cult and Ritual

Worship at the Corycian Cave centered on the [[the-nymphs|Nymphs]] and [[pan|Pan]] as deities of the wild, fertility, and ecstatic possession. Ritual activity included the offering of figurines (particularly of women and animals), libations, and possibly ecstatic dancing associated with Dionysiac rites on [[mount-parnassus|Parnassus]]. The cave's "things blessèd" — perhaps spirits or daimones — suggest a numinous presence independent of Olympian temple ritual.
