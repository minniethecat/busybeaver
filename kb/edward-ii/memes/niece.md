# The Niece

The **Niece** of [[edward-ii|King Edward II]] (historically Margaret de Clare, daughter of Gilbert de Clare, Earl of Gloucester) appears in *Edward II* primarily as a dynastic instrument. Edward arranges her marriage to [[piers-gaveston|Gaveston]] to secure his favourite's place within the [[nobility]], transforming her from a royal relative into a political bargaining chip. Her few scenes reveal a woman navigating a role she did not choose with grace and loyalty.

## A Marriage of Convenience

Edward announces the marriage to the assembled nobles as a fait accompli:

> And then his marriage shall be solemnised; / For wot you not that I have made him sure / Unto my cousin, the Earl of Gloucester's heir? (004-181)

The Niece is not consulted—she is "made sure" to Gaveston as one might secure a property deed. Her identity is defined entirely by her relationships: niece to the king, daughter to Gloucester, wife to Gaveston. She has no independent dramatic existence.

## Voice and Agency

Despite her objectification by the political men around her, the Niece speaks with warmth and concern. Her first words express grief for Gaveston's exile:

> The grief for his exile was not so much / As is the joy of his returning home (005-017)

When Edward is threatened, she urges: "Sweet uncle, speak more kindly to the lords" (006-118). Her intervention is gentle but pointed—she recognises the political danger that Edward's confrontational style invites. Her voice, though rarely heard, consistently counsels conciliation and care.

## Dramatic Erasure

The Niece disappears from the play after Act 2, her dramatic function exhausted once Gaveston's noble status has been secured through their marriage. Her erasure mirrors the play's broader treatment of women—Isabella excepted—as resources to be deployed and discarded by the men who control power. Yet her brief presence lingers as a reminder of the human costs exacted by dynastic politics.
