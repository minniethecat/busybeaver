# Younger Spenser

The Younger Spenser is a study in courtly ambition — a man who attaches himself to power not out of loyalty but calculation, and whose rise and fall trace the arc of fortune that governs the play.

## The Careerist

Spenser's philosophy is stated with brutal clarity: "he that hath the favour of a king May with one word advance us while we live" (005-004). This is not loyalty to a person or principle but an investment strategy — the king's favour is a commodity to be acquired and leveraged. His advice to Baldock to "cast the scholar off, And learn to court it like a gentleman" (005-012) reveals the court as a school of dissimulation.

## Instrument and Victim

Spenser rises with Gaveston and falls with him. His career is parasitic — entirely dependent on the favour of those above him — and this dependence makes his fall inevitable. The play offers no redemption for the careerist; Spenser is swept away by the same forces that elevated him.
