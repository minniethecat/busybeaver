# Rebellion

Rebellion in *[[edward-ii|Edward II]]* is both political action and dramatic structure. The barons' revolt against Edward's misrule drives the plot from its first scenes to the final catastrophe.

## Causes

The rebellion is provoked by Edward's excessive favouritism toward [[piers-gaveston|Gaveston]]. [[lancaster|Lancaster]] warns the king directly: "Look for rebellion, look to be deposed" (006-094), linking the king's personal attachments to political consequences. The barons frame their rising not as [[treason|treason]] but as correction -- a response to a monarch who has abandoned his obligations.

> Look for rebellion, look to be deposed:
> Thy garrisons are beaten out of [[france-as-setting|France]],
> And, lame and poor, lie groaning at the gates;
> The wild Oneil, with swarms of Irish kerns,
> Lives uncontrolled within the English pale;
> Unto the walls of York the Scots make road,
> And, unresisted, drive away rich spoils.

Lancaster presents rebellion not as a choice but as an inevitability -- the kingdom is already in rebellion against itself through neglect and misrule. The barons' armed resistance merely formalises a condition that Edward's governance has already created.

## The Baronial Coalition

The rebels are not a monolithic force but a coalition of powerful nobles: Lancaster, [[warwick|Warwick]], [[pembroke|Pembroke]], and the two Mortimers. Their unity is fragile, held together by shared opposition to Gaveston rather than a common vision. Once Gaveston is eliminated, the coalition fractures, and the [[younger-mortimer|Younger Mortimer]] emerges as a new threat -- more dangerous because he aims at the crown itself rather than merely its favourite.

> Then may we with some colour rise in arms;
> For, howsoever we have borne it out,
> 'Tis treason to be up against the king;
> So shall we have the people of our side,
> Which, for his father's sake, lean to the king,
> But cannot brook a night-grown mushroom,
> Such a one as my Lord of Cornwall is,
> Should bear us down of the [[nobility|nobility]].

Younger Mortimer's speech reveals the calculated nature of baronial rebellion. He seeks "some colour" -- a legal pretext -- for armed resistance. The candid admission that "'Tis treason to be up against the king" demonstrates that the barons understand the gravity of what they contemplate. Yet they proceed, calculating that popular support will protect them.

## Escalation

Edward's response to rebellion is characteristically personal: "Have at the rebels and their complices!" (006-138). He treats political opposition as personal betrayal, escalating rather than defusing conflict. The rebellion metastasizes from a targeted action against Gaveston into a full-scale challenge to Edward's legitimacy.

> The headstrong barons shall not limit me;
> He that I list to favour shall be great.
> Come, let's away; and, when the [[marriage|marriage]] ends,
> Have at the rebels and their complices!

Edward's declaration -- "He that I list to favour shall be great" -- is an assertion of royal prerogative that makes negotiation impossible. By framing every concession as an intolerable limit on his will, Edward transforms his barons from discontented counsellors into armed rebels exactly as Mortimer predicted.

[[queen-isabella|The Queen]]'s alignment with the rebels marks a decisive turning point. When Isabella asks the barons "would the rebels deny me that?" (011-028), she reveals that even the king's marriage has become a theatre of rebellion.

## Nature of the Rebel Force

Mortimer describes his followers as "a ranker rout of rebels never was" (011-043), acknowledging the motley nature of the insurrection. The rebellion draws strength from its breadth -- commons and nobles united against misrule -- but this breadth also makes it politically unstable.

> So wish not they, I wis, that sent thee hither.
> Thou com'st from Mortimer and his complices:
> A ranker rout of rebels never was.
> Well, say thy message.

Edward's dismissal of the rebel force as a "ranker rout" reveals his inability to take the rebellion seriously even at its most dangerous moment. His aristocratic contempt for the mixed composition of the rebel army blinds him to its political significance: support from the commons makes the barons' rebellion a national movement rather than a factional squabble.

## Dramatic Function

Rebellion serves as the primary engine of plot. Each act brings a new phase of conflict: threat, [[exile|exile]], return, execution, and ultimately deposition. The play traces a complete arc from the barons' initial complaint to the collapse of legitimate authority. Rebellion is not merely a theme but the structural principle that organises the action -- every scene advances or resists the logic of revolt.
