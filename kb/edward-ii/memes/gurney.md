# Gurney

**Gurney** (historically Thomas Gurney) appears in the final act of [[edward-ii]] as [[younger-mortimer|Younger Mortimer]]'s agent, summoned alongside [[Matrevis]] to assume custody of the deposed king and facilitate his murder. Though his role is secondary to Matrevis's, Gurney's presence completes the portrait of institutionalised violence—tyranny distributed across obedient functionaries.

## Shared Commission

Mortimer summons both men in a single breath: "Who's there? Call hither, Gurney and Matrevis" (020-021). Gurney and Matrevis function as a pair throughout their scenes, their individuality subsumed into their shared function. When Mortimer addresses Gurney directly:

> Younger Mortimer: Gurney— / Gurney: My lord? (020-028–029)

The exchange is minimal—a name and an acknowledgment—yet it establishes the chain of command with brutal economy. Gurney's "I warrant you, my lord" (020-031) is the language of feudal service repurposed for murder.

## The King's Custodian

In the prison scenes, Gurney participates in the systematic degradation of Edward. When the king asks for water to cool his thirst, Matrevis offers only "channel-water" (021-007), and Gurney mocks Edward's distress:

> Gurney: No, but wash your face, and shave away your beard, / Lest you be known, and so be rescued. (021-009)

| Action | Agent | PN |
|--------|-------|-----|
| Summoned by Mortimer | Gurney + Matrevis | (020-021) |
| Receives commission | Gurney | (020-028) |
| Guards the imprisoned king | Gurney | (021-002) |

| Challenges intruders | Gurney | (021-015) |

The mockery of the title "my liege" applied to a deposed and imprisoned king is characteristic of the play's late-phase cruelty. Gurney's refusal of basic comfort—clean water—represents the petty dimensions of tyranny: not merely the grand act of regicide but the daily humiliations that precede it.

## Instrument of Tyranny

Like Matrevis, Gurney never questions his orders or displays independent moral judgment. He is the perfect functionary—present when summoned, obedient when commanded, and absent from the play's moral reckoning. His final appearance, challenging an intruder with "How now! who comes there?" (021-015), suggests a man alert to threat but blind to guilt.

Gurney and Matrevis together represent the banality of political murder: it requires not monsters but reliable men who do as they are told. The tragedy of Edward's death lies partly in this—that a king can be destroyed not by great villains but by ordinary functionaries following orders.
