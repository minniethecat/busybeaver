# Pride

Pride — the overestimation of one's own authority, status, or invulnerability — afflicts every major character in *Edward II*. It is the tragic flaw that transforms manageable conflicts into irreparable catastrophes.

## Edward's Pride

Edward's pride is absolute: "Were he a peasant, being my minion, I'll make the proudest of you stoop to him" (004-023). This is pride of station taken to its extreme — the king claims the right to invert the entire social hierarchy by mere declaration. His pride is not personal vanity but constitutional overreach: the belief that royal will supersedes all other considerations.

## Gaveston's Pride

Gaveston's pride is the pride of the parvenu — the favourite who has risen beyond his birth and cannot resist flaunting his elevation. His fantasy of the court as an aesthetic playground, where he will "draw the pliant king which way I please" (001-018), reveals a contempt for power that he does not actually possess. His pride is parasitic on Edward's, and like all parasites, it dies with its host.

## The Barons' Pride

The barons are not immune. Warwick, Lancaster, and Mortimer each display the pride of ancient lineage — the conviction that birth alone constitutes a claim to power. Their objection to Gaveston is not merely political but social: "What man of noble birth can brook this sight?" (004-011). Pride of birth meets pride of station, and the collision destroys the realm.

## Pride and Tragedy

In classical tragedy, pride (*hubris*) is the flaw that brings down the great. Marlowe distributes this flaw across his entire cast. No single character monopolizes pride, and no single fall resolves it. The play's tragic vision is democratic: pride destroys everyone, the low-born favourite and the anointed king alike.
