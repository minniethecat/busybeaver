# Edward II's Deposition

The deposition of Edward II is the play's constitutional and theological crisis point. An anointed monarch — believed by contemporaries to rule by divine mandate — is forced to surrender his crown to his enemies. The event transforms political conflict into cosmic violation.

## The Abdication Scene

Marlowe's deposition scene is among the most powerful in Elizabethan drama. Edward is brought before his enemies and ordered to yield the crown. His resistance gives way to exhausted submission in a speech that oscillates between defiance, self-pity, and tragic self-awareness:

> Ah, [[leicester|Leicester]], weigh how hardly I can brook To lose my crown and kingdom without cause; To give ambitious Mortimer my right, That, like a mountain, overwhelms my bliss; In which extreme my mind here murdered is! (019-008)

The crown-and-un crown sequence that follows is a theatrical tour de force. Edward takes off the crown, puts it back on, takes it off again — unable to complete the act that will unmake him as king:

> Here, take my crown; the life of Edward too: Two kings in England cannot reign at once. But stay a while: let me be king till night, That I may gaze upon this glittering crown. (019-008)

The physical gesture is the meaning: Edward cannot let go of the thing that defines him, even as he knows he must.

## The Crown Transferred

The physical transfer of the crown to his son — the future Edward III — is carefully orchestrated to preserve the appearance of legitimacy. When Edward finally surrenders it, the language is both ritualistic and deeply personal:

> Here, receive my crown. Receive it? no, these innocent hands of mine Shall not be guilty of so foul a crime; He of you all that most desires my blood, And will be called the murderer of a king, Take it. (019-016)

The crown passes not to Mortimer but to the legitimate heir. But the form cannot disguise the substance: Mortimer and Isabella rule, the prince is a figurehead, and the deposition is a coup dressed in constitutional language.

## The King's Lament

Before the surrender, Edward delivers a meditation on the nature of kingship stripped of power:

> But what are kings, when regiment is gone, But perfect shadows in a sunshine day? My [[nobility|nobles]] rule; I bear the name of king, I wear the crown; but am controlled by them. (019-004)

This is the play's most lucid statement of the gap between the office and the man. Edward has always been a king in name; the deposition merely makes visible what has been true since the first act — that his authority was never as absolute as he imagined.

## Consequences

The deposition unleashes forces that Mortimer cannot control. Edward's imprisonment and [[murder|murder]] follow with grim inevitability. The young Edward III, once he comes of age, will execute Mortimer for the very act of deposition that made Mortimer's power possible. The cycle of violence does not end with the crown's transfer — it merely changes hands.
