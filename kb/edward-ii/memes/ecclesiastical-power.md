# Ecclesiastical Power

The church in *Edward II* is not a spiritual refuge but a political actor — the Archbishop of Canterbury is among the first to sign Gaveston's exile order, and church authority provides institutional legitimacy to the baronial cause.

## The Church as Political Actor

Warwick's opening complaint frames the conflict in religious terms: "will they tyrannise upon the church? Ah, wicked King! accursed Gaveston!" (002-004). The barons position themselves as defenders of ecclesiastical privilege against royal encroachment. When "violence is offered to the church" (002-023), even secular lords claim a duty to resist.

## The Archbishop's Authority

The Archbishop of Canterbury lends constitutional weight to the baronial proceedings. He is the first to subscribe his name to Gaveston's exile order, transforming a factional demand into a proceeding with institutional legitimacy. His presence signals that the conflict is not merely personal — it engages the fundamental ordering of the realm.

## Church and Crown in Conflict

Edward's response to ecclesiastical opposition is characteristically personal: he threatens to "fire thy crazed buildings" where "thy antichristian churches blaze" (004-058). This language — equating church buildings with Antichrist — would have resonated with Reformation-era audiences familiar with debates about ecclesiastical corruption. Edward's rhetoric, however, only confirms the church's alignment with his enemies.

## Structural Role

The ecclesiastical dimension of the conflict reflects the medieval constitution in which church and crown were interdependent. By alienating the church, Edward loses not merely spiritual sanction but a crucial pillar of institutional order. The Archbishop's participation in the exile proceedings demonstrates that Edward has lost the confidence of every estate of the realm.
