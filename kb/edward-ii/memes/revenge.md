# Revenge

Revenge courses through *Edward II* as both personal passion and political logic. From the opening scene to the final executions, characters justify violence through the language of revenge, transforming raw ambition into righteous action.

## Personal Revenge

The play opens with revenge in the air. Mortimer the Elder demands: "revenge it, and let these their heads Preach upon poles for trespass of their tongues" (001-031). From the first confrontation, violence is framed as response to injury, never as initiation. Every character who acts does so in the name of a prior wrong.

## Edward's Vengeance

Edward embraces revenge as royal prerogative. He swears "I'll be revenged on him for my exile" (001-058), treating the barons' constitutional action as personal affront. His promise to "reign to be revenged of them" (004-061) reveals revenge not as momentary passion but as governing principle — he will rule for the purpose of retaliation.

## Baronial Retaliation

The barons match Edward's language: "And courage too, to be revenged at full" (002-030). Their revenge is collective and institutional, channeled through the forms of law and council. But the forms cannot disguise the content — revenge, not justice, drives the escalating cycle of action and reaction.

## The Revenge Cycle

Marlowe structures the play as a revenge spiral. Gaveston's execution provokes Edward's revenge on the barons; Edward's deposition provokes Mortimer's ascendancy; Mortimer's rule provokes Edward III's revenge. Each act of revenge claims to close the cycle but in fact perpetuates it. Only the final executions — of Mortimer and his allies — offer the possibility of closure, and even this is shadowed by the knowledge of what came before.

## Revenge and Tragedy

Revenge tragedy was a thriving Elizabethan genre, and *Edward II* draws on its conventions while subtly subverting them. Unlike the revenge protagonist who acts in righteous cause, Edward's pursuit of revenge is self-destructive — each retaliatory act weakens rather than restores his authority.
