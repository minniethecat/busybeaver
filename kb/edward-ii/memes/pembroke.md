# Pembroke

Pembroke represents a political path not taken — moderation in a world that has made moderation impossible. His presence among the [[nobility|barons]] signals that opposition to Edward was not monolithic, but his inability to prevent [[gavestons-execution|Gaveston's execution]] reveals the limits of moderate influence in a polarised political landscape.

## Key Speeches

> Can kingly lions fawn on creeping ants?
> — Act 1, Scene 4

Pembroke's single line at the New Temple captures the baronial perspective in its most measured form. It is not hatred but a sense of violated proportion that motivates his opposition — a lion that fawns on ants has forgotten what it is. The metaphor is hierarchical rather than personal: the problem is not who Gaveston is but what Edward has become by favouring him. Among the barons' insults at the New Temple — Warwick's "Phaeton," Mortimer's "base peasant," Lancaster's eagerness to sign — Pembroke's is the most philosophical and the least vindictive.

> My Lord Mortimer, and you, my lords, each one,
> To gratify the king's request therein,
> Touching the sending of this Gaveston,
> Because his majesty so earnestly
> Desires to see the man before his death,
> I will upon mine honour undertake
> To carry him, and bring him back again;
> Provided this, that you, my Lord of [[arundel|Arundel]],
> Will join with me.
> — Act 2, Scene 5

This is Pembroke's most consequential speech — and his greatest failure. He stakes his honour on Gaveston's safe return, offering himself as guarantor of a procedure that would satisfy both the king's [[love-and-desire|desire]] to see his favourite and the barons' determination to execute him. The language is formal, balanced, procedural — Pembroke at his most characteristic. He is attempting to construct a middle path where honour is preserved on both sides.

The speech's irony is devastating. Pembroke's honour, so carefully pledged, will be destroyed not by his own failure but by Warwick's ambush. "My Lord of Pembroke, we deliver him you: / Return him on your honour" — Mortimer's words seal Pembroke's [[mortality|fate]]. When Warwick seizes Gaveston, Pembroke's honour becomes worthless because the baronial faction has rendered it so. The moderate is destroyed by his own allies.

> My lord, you shall go with me:
> My house is not far hence; out of the way
> A little; but our men shall go along.
> We that have pretty wenches to our wives,
> Sir, must not come so near to balk their lips.
> — Act 2, Scene 5

This unexpected domestic turn — Pembroke declining to accompany Gaveston home because he wishes to see his wife — is a moment of startling ordinariness in a scene of political crisis. Pembroke's household, his "pretty wench," his desire to go home — these details humanise a character who might otherwise be merely the baronial faction's conscience. The line also underscores the tragedy of what follows: Pembroke's domestic contentment is purchased at the cost of his public honour. He goes home to his wife, and Warwick goes to kill Gaveston.

## The Failure of Moderation

Pembroke's moderate position proves untenable at every turn. He signs the [[exile|exile]] [[natural-order|order]] but does not demand Gaveston's death. He supports the baronial cause but treats Edward with residual deference. He guarantees Gaveston's safe conduct — and watches his guarantee destroyed. Pembroke's word, his honour, is demolished by the very faction to which he belongs.

The episode demonstrates that in polarized politics, the middle ground is a [[murder|killing]] field. Pembroke's proceduralism — his belief that honour, oaths, and formal guarantees can manage political conflict — is exposed as naive. In a world where Warwick will ambush prisoners and Mortimer will suborn murderers, Pembroke's honour is not a shield but a vulnerability.

His survival past the play's midpoint (he is not among those executed at [[boroughbridge|Boroughbridge]]) is itself a kind of judgment. Pembroke outlives Lancaster, Warwick, and the [[elder-mortimer|Elder Mortimer]] — the principled, the passionate, and the paternal — but his survival is purchased at the cost of his political relevance. After the Gaveston episode, Pembroke recedes from the play's centre. Moderation, having failed, becomes invisible.

## Dramatic Arc

Pembroke's arc is a parabola of diminishing influence. He enters as a peer among equals at the New Temple, participates in [[gavestons-exile|Gaveston's exile]], and stakes his honour on Gaveston's safe conduct. After Warwick's ambush destroys that honour, Pembroke's role contracts. By Act 3, he is a secondary figure in the baronial army; by Acts 4 and 5, he has disappeared entirely. The arc traces the fate of moderation in revolutionary times: not destruction but irrelevance.

## Historical Figure

The historical Aymer de Valence (c.1275–1324) was a prominent nobleman who served both Edward I and [[edward-ii|Edward II]]. His career exemplifies the difficulties faced by moderates during the reign: he participated in the baronial opposition but later returned to royal service, only to be marginalized by the rise of the Despensers.
