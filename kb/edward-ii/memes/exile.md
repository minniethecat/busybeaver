# Exile

Exile — the forcible expulsion of a subject from the realm — is the barons' primary weapon against Gaveston and the event that most clearly reveals Edward's political weakness. The play cycles through exile, return, and re-exile, each iteration escalating the conflict.

## Exile as Political Weapon

The barons use exile as a constitutional remedy: "Are you content to banish him the realm?" (004-047). By securing the Archbishop of Canterbury's signature, they frame Gaveston's removal as lawful, not merely factional. The paper they circulate for signatures transforms personal animosity into institutional action.

## Gaveston's Experience

For Gaveston, England without Edward is meaningless: "The sight of London to my exiled eyes Is as Elysium to a new-come soul" (001-003). His first speech frames exile as a kind of death — the loss not merely of place but of the king's presence, which alone gives his life meaning. The promise of recall — "share the kingdom with thy dearest friend" (001-003) — is the gravitational center of his entire existence.

## Edward's Response

Edward experiences his favourite's exile as personal violation. He rages against Mortimer as "the only cause of his exile" (001-052) and swears "I'll be revenged on him for my exile" (001-058), collapsing the distinction between Gaveston's punishment and his own injury. This identification of self with favourite is precisely what the barons find intolerable.

## The Cycle of Banishment

Exile in *Edward II* is never final — it is part of a cycle: "banish or behead that Gaveston?" (002-024). The barons recognize that exile alone cannot solve their problem; the king will always recall his favourite. Each return from exile deepens Edward's defiance and the barons' resentment, making violent resolution inevitable.

## Exile and Sovereignty

The exile conflict exposes the central constitutional question: can the barons lawfully compel the king to act against his will? When Edward asks "Am I a king, and must be overruled?" (001-028), he frames exile not as policy but as sovereignty crisis. The barons' success in exiling Gaveston proves that Edward's authority is conditional, not absolute.

## Dramatic Function

Exile serves as the play's ratchet mechanism. Each cycle — banishment → recall → confrontation → re-banishment — raises the stakes. The impossibility of permanent resolution through exile drives the plot toward the only remaining option: execution.
