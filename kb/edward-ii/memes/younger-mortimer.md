# Younger Mortimer

The Younger Mortimer is *Edward II*'s most complex antagonist — a character who begins as a defender of legitimate baronial interests and ends as a regicide whose ambition exceeds anything Gaveston attempted. His trajectory from principled opposition to tyrannical overreach mirrors and inverts Edward's own tragic arc, making him not merely a villain but a second tragic protagonist whose fall completes the play's political argument.

> "The prince I rule, the queen I do command, / And with a lowly congé to the ground / The proudest lords salute me as I pass" (022-016–022-020)

## The Defender of Order

Mortimer initially presents himself as a defender of constitutional order against Edward's misrule. His objection to Gaveston is framed in terms of rank and birth: "Thou, villain! wherefore talk'st thou of a king, That hardly art a gentleman by birth?" (004-022). In the early acts, Mortimer claims to act on behalf of the realm rather than personal ambition, positioning himself as the voice of aristocratic corporate identity against royal absolutism.

This self-presentation is not wholly insincere. Mortimer's early arguments carry genuine weight, and his diagnosis of Edward's misrule is substantially correct. The play's tragedy lies partly in the fact that Mortimer's initial cause is just, making his subsequent corruption all the more disturbing.

## The Ascent

Mortimer's rise follows Gaveston's elimination. With the favourite dead, Mortimer becomes the dominant political force — first as Isabella's ally in her French embassy, then as her military partner in the invasion of England, then as her (probable) lover, and finally as the effective ruler of England. Each step in his ascent is marked by an expansion of ambition and a contraction of principle.

> "The king must die, or Mortimer goes down" (022-010)

The relationship with Isabella is central to Mortimer's ascent and his corruption. What begins as a political alliance — two figures wronged by Edward joining forces — becomes something more intimate and more dangerous. Isabella provides Mortimer with legitimacy (she is the queen) and Mortimer provides Isabella with military capacity (he commands the invasion force). Together they form a partnership that is simultaneously political, personal, and homicidal.

## The Regicide

Mortimer's decision to order Edward's murder marks his moral tipping point. The act transforms him from a political actor into a criminal, and the play's judgement is unambiguous. Where Edward's deposition could be defended on constitutional grounds, his murder cannot — it is a crime that taints everything Mortimer has achieved.

The manner of Edward's murder — degrading, brutal, conducted in secret — reflects on its author. Mortimer does not grant his enemy a noble death but a sordid one, and this choice reveals the poverty of his moral imagination. He has become what he claimed to oppose: a tyrant who treats human life as an instrument of political convenience.

## Tragic Mirror

Mortimer functions as Edward's tragic mirror. Both are undone by the same flaw — the inability to moderate desire — but expressed in opposite directions: Edward through excessive love, Mortimer through excessive ambition. Their parallel falls suggest that power itself is the corrupting agent, regardless of the character who wields it. The wheel of fortune that casts Edward down also raises Mortimer up, and having reached its apex, casts him down in turn.

The symmetry is exact: Edward is murdered on Mortimer's orders; Mortimer is executed on Edward III's. The cycle of violence completes itself, and the young king's act of justice restores the moral order that both Edward II and Mortimer had, in their different ways, shattered.

## Others' Eyes: How Mortimer Is Seen

**Queen Isabella** sees Mortimer as protector and liberator — the man who rescues her from a loveless marriage and enables her return to England as a political actor rather than a neglected wife. Her dependence on Mortimer is both personal and political, and her inability to protect him from Edward III's justice marks the limit of her power.

**The barons** see Mortimer with increasing unease. Lancaster's old-guard principled opposition gives way to Mortimer's ruthless ambition, and the baronial cause is hollowed out from within. By the time of Edward's murder, Mortimer has become what the barons originally claimed to oppose: a figure whose personal will overrides all institutional constraint.

**Edward III** sees Mortimer as a usurper who must be destroyed. The young king's swift and decisive action against Mortimer — no trial, no negotiation, no delay — is simultaneously an act of filial justice and a declaration that the cycle of extrajudicial violence ends here. Mortimer's execution restores the monarchy, but it does so by mirroring the very ruthlessness it condemns.

## Historical Figure

The historical Roger Mortimer (1287–1330) was a powerful Marcher lord who became the lover of Queen Isabella and the de facto ruler of England after Edward II's deposition. He was created Earl of March in 1328 and exercised power with a ruthlessness that alienated even his former allies. His execution by Edward III in 1330 — without trial, at the moment he believed himself most secure — is one of the most dramatic reversals in medieval English history. Marlowe compresses the chronology but preserves the essential shape of Mortimer's rise and fall.
