# Warwick

Warwick is the sharpest edge of baronial opposition — more aggressive than Lancaster, more personal in his hatred of Gaveston than the [[elder-mortimer|Elder Mortimer]]. His implacable hostility helps drive the conflict from political disagreement to violent confrontation, and his execution of Gaveston is the act that makes civil war inevitable.

## Key Speeches

> Ignoble vassal, that, like Phaeton,
> Aspir'st unto the guidance of the sun!
> — Act 1, Scene 4

Warwick's mythological insult at the New Temple distills the baronial critique of Gaveston into two lines. Phaeton, who stole Apollo's chariot and scorched the earth before Zeus struck him down, is the archetype of misplaced ambition — a mortal who reached for divine prerogative and destroyed the world in the attempt. The image captures both Warwick's contempt and his warning: Gaveston's rise will burn the realm.

> Upon him, soldiers! take away his weapons!
> ...
> Lancaster, why talk'st thou to the slave? —
> Go, soldiers, take him hence; for, by my sword,
> His head shall off. — Gaveston, short warning
> Shall serve thy turn: it is our country's cause
> That here severely we will execute
> Upon thy person. — Hang him at a bough.
> — Act 2, Scene 5

Warwick's capture of Gaveston is the play's pivotal act of baronial violence. The shift from "His head shall off" to "Hang him at a bough" — beheading to hanging — is a deliberate degradation: hanging is the [[mortality|death]] of common criminals, not noblemen. Warwick is not merely executing an enemy but stripping him of status. "Short warning / Shall serve thy turn" dismisses the forms of noble execution as luxuries Gaveston does not deserve.

> *Aside.* Yet not perhaps,
> If Warwick's wit and policy prevail.
> — Act 2, Scene 5

This aside, spoken while [[pembroke|Pembroke]] guarantees Gaveston's safe conduct, reveals Warwick's defining characteristic: he is the baron who acts when others deliberate. His "wit and policy" are not arguments but ambushes. Where Lancaster speaks and Mortimer schemes, Warwick seizes. The aside is a promise the audience knows he will keep — and he does, intercepting Gaveston from Pembroke's men and beheading him in a ditch.

> A desperate and unnatural resolution! —
> Alarum to the fight!
> Saint George for England, and the barons' right!
> — Act 3, Scene 3

Warwick's battle cry at [[boroughbridge|Boroughbridge]] frames the civil war as a holy cause. The invocation of Saint George — England's patron — aligns baronial arms with national identity. "The barons' right" asserts that the [[nobility|nobility]]'s cause is England's cause. It is Warwick's last significant speech before his capture and execution, and it distills his character: a man for whom political argument always terminates in military action.

## The Gaveston Execution

Warwick's seizure and execution of Gaveston is the act for which he is most remembered — and the act that defines his dramatic function. Pembroke, a man of [[honour-code|honour]], guarantees Gaveston's safe conduct to see Edward one last time. Warwick, a man of action, ambushes the prisoner on the road and beheads him in a trench. The contrast between Pembroke's procedural integrity and Warwick's extrajudicial violence is the contrast between two models of baronial resistance — one that operates within the norms of aristocratic conduct and one that abandons those norms for results.

Warwick's justification — "it is my country's cause I follow" — is the language of patriotic necessity. But the act itself is a violation of the law of arms, as Spencer notes: "A bloody part, flatly 'gainst law of arms!" Warwick has won the barons their [[revenge|revenge]] but at the cost of their procedural legitimacy. The execution of Gaveston transforms the barons from constitutional reformers into something closer to what Edward has always claimed they were: rebels.

## The Hater of Gaveston

Warwick's opposition to Gaveston is visceral rather than constitutional. "But I long more to see him banished hence" — the language is personal, almost gleeful. Where Lancaster speaks of the realm and Mortimer of noble birth, Warwick speaks of satisfaction. His hatred is the id of baronial politics — the raw emotion beneath the constitutional rhetoric.

This visceral quality distinguishes Warwick from his allies. Lancaster's opposition is structural (Gaveston as symptom of misrule); Mortimer's is instrumental (Gaveston as obstacle to power). Warwick's is personal — he simply loathes the man. This makes him both the most reliable baronial soldier (he never wavers, never negotiates, never compromises) and the most dangerous (his hatred cannot be satisfied by [[exile|exile]] or demotion, only by death).

## Death and Defiance

Warwick's execution at Boroughbridge provides him with a fitting exit. "Tyrant, I scorn thy threats and menaces; / It is but temporal that thou canst inflict" — his final words are defiance. The distinction between temporal and eternal punishment positions Warwick as a martyr to the baronial cause, suffering earthly death at Edward's hands while reserving his soul for divine judgment. His last line — "Farewell, vain world!" — is the contemptus mundi of a man who has never valued anything as much as his hatred of Gaveston.

## Historical Figure

The historical Guy de Beauchamp (c.1272–1315) was a powerful magnate and one of the Lords Ordainers who sought to reform [[edward-ii|Edward II]]'s government. He was among those responsible for [[gavestons-execution|Gaveston's execution]] in 1312, an act for which he later received a royal pardon. His death in 1315 — possibly by [[poison|poisoning]] — removed one of Edward's most determined opponents.
