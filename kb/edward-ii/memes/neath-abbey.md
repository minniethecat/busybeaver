# Neath Abbey

Neath Abbey represents the last threshold between Edward's freedom and his captivity. It is at Neath that the king is finally captured by Mortimer's forces, passing from fugitive monarch to prisoner and, eventually, to martyr.

## The Final Refuge

Edward's flight to Neath is his last attempt to find sanctuary — significantly, in an ecclesiastical rather than a secular location. The abbey represents spiritual authority as final resort when political authority has collapsed. But the sanctuary fails; Mortimer's men violate the abbey's protection with the same contempt for institutional boundaries that has characterized the entire conflict.

## Threshold

Neath functions as a dramatic threshold — the point of no return after which Edward's fate is sealed. What happens at Berkeley is the consequence of what happens at Neath. The abbey's failure to protect the king signals the complete collapse of every institution — crown, church, nobility — that should have sustained legitimate rule.

## Historical Counterpart

The historical Neath Abbey was a Cistercian monastery in Glamorgan, Wales. Edward II was captured there (or nearby) in November 1326 after attempting to flee the invasion of Isabella and Mortimer. The abbey ruins survive today as a significant medieval monument.
