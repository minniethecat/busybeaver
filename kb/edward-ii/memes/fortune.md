# Fortune

Fortune — the medieval conception of a capricious goddess who turns her wheel, raising some as she casts down others — is the metaphysical framework within which *Edward II* unfolds. Characters who ascend today face destruction tomorrow, and no one in the play is exempt from Fortune's revolution.

## The Wheel Turns

Gaveston's opening soliloquy enacts Fortune's logic in miniature: he rises from exile to "share the kingdom" (001-003), imagining himself at the wheel's summit. His fantasy of unlimited favour contains within it the seed of his fall — Fortune's wheel turns inexorably, and those at the top are positioned only to descend.

## Fortune and Ambition

Spenser's career advice — "he that hath the favour of a king May with one word advance us while we live" (005-004) — captures the relationship between Fortune and court ambition. Advancement depends not on merit but on Fortune's unpredictable favour, mediated through the even more unpredictable favour of the king.

## The Fall

The play's imagery persistently evokes Fortune's mechanism: "threaten'st death whether he rise or fall" (006-016). Rise and fall are not alternatives but sequential certainties. Every character who rises — Gaveston, Edward, Mortimer — experiences a corresponding fall. The pattern is so consistent that it suggests not chance but law.

## Fortune vs. Providence

Marlowe's treatment of Fortune is notably secular. Unlike medieval morality plays where Fortune's wheel is ultimately governed by divine providence, *Edward II* offers no theological comfort. The wheel turns, characters fall, and no higher purpose redeems the pattern. This secular Fortune is more terrifying than its medieval predecessor — it offers meaninglessness rather than mystery.

## Dramatic Function

The Fortune motif provides the play's structural rhythm. Each act corresponds to a quarter-turn of the wheel: Gaveston rises (Act I), the barons resist (Act II), Gaveston falls (Act III), Edward falls (Act IV-V). The symmetry is almost mechanical, suggesting that political catastrophe is not contingent but structural.
