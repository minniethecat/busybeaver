# Rice ap Howel

Rice ap Howel is the military arm of Isabella and Mortimer's victory. His Welsh troops — equipped with their distinctive hooks — provide the muscle that finishes the civil war. His capture of Elder Spenser and his later role in escorting the deposed Edward II make him one of the play's most efficient agents of regime change.

## The Welsh Commander

Rice ap Howel enters with Elder Spenser as his prisoner, declaring loyalty to the new regime: "God save Queen Isabel and her princely son!" (017-021). He reports his military success crisply — Younger Spenser has fled, created Earl of Gloucester by Edward's patent (017-024). His Welsh identity is signalled by the stage direction "Enter, with Welsh hooks" (018-010), marking him as an outsider to the English political order who nonetheless serves its new masters.

## The Custodian of Edward II

After Edward's capture, Rice ap Howel is assigned to the grim work of custody. His exchanges with Leicester and the Mower in the killing scene (018-012 through 018-035) show a man who is practical, not cruel: "My lord, be going: care not for these villains" (018-028). He urges Leicester to leave the scene of Edward's degradation at Kenilworth: "As good be gone, as stay and be abus'd" (018-026). His final words to the Mower — "Remember thee, fellow!" (018-035) — carry a note of dark recognition: they have both been instruments in something monstrous.

## Dramatic Function

Rice ap Howel embodies the unglamorous but essential work of military enforcement. He does not debate policy, does not speechify about justice, does not participate in the factional intrigues at court. He captures enemies, escorts prisoners, and keeps order. His presence on stage reminds the audience that the great political drama of Edward II's reign ultimately resolves into the physical custody of a broken king.
