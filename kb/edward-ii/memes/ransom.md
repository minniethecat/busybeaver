# Ransom

Ransom — the exchange of money for the release of captives — appears in *Edward II* as a flashpoint that exposes deeper conflicts over royal finance, military obligation, and baronial power. The ransom dispute in Act 6 is not merely a financial transaction but a staging ground for competing claims about who bears the costs of the king's wars and who enjoys the profits of captivity.

## The Ransom Dispute in Act 6

The ransom theme centres on Act 6, where the barons hold a captive and demand payment. Lancaster opens the exchange with casual confidence: "We'll have him ransomed, man: be of good cheer" [006-062], treating the captive as a straightforward financial asset. Younger Mortimer names the price — "They rate his ransom at five thousand pound" [006-063] — a substantial sum that demonstrates the barons' economic leverage over the crown.

The demand quickly escalates into a power struggle. A baron threatens: "Cousin, an if he will not ransom him, I'll thunder such a peal" [006-069], using the threat of withheld payment to challenge royal authority directly. King Edward capitulates — "Then ransom him" [006-083] — but his command is immediately undercut. Younger Mortimer retorts: "'Twas in your wars; you should ransom him" [006-084], insisting that Edward bears the financial obligation for a captive taken in royal service.

A second voice reinforces the point: "And you will ransom him, or else—" [006-089], the unfinished threat hanging in the air. The exchange reveals that ransom is not a neutral transaction but a site where the barons can both extract money from the king and publicly demonstrate his dependence on their goodwill.

## Ransom and Royal Finance

The ransom dispute is symptomatic of a broader fiscal crisis. Edward's wars require funding, his favourites consume resources, and the barons — who control local revenues and military manpower — are positioned to make the king pay for his own policies. The five thousand pound ransom is both a specific demand and a symbol of the structural financial imbalance between crown and nobility.

In the economy of medieval warfare, ransom was a legitimate source of income for captors. By insisting that Edward pay, the barons are simultaneously asserting a customary right and exposing the king's inability to fund his own wars without baronial cooperation. The episode thus dramatises the fiscal dimension of the constitutional crisis that runs through the play.

## Dramatic Function

The ransom scene compresses multiple political tensions into a single exchange: the question of who pays for war, the barons' capacity to withhold cooperation, and the king's vulnerability to economic pressure. It serves as a microcosm of the larger power struggle, translating abstract constitutional conflict into a concrete and humiliating financial demand.
