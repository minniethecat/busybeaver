# Imprisonment

Imprisonment in *[[edward-ii|Edward II]]* operates on multiple registers: as a literal punishment, as a political tool of control, and most powerfully as a metaphor for the condition of kingship itself. The play moves from Gaveston's casual threat of imprisonment to the wrenching image of Edward locked in a dungeon, tracing an arc in which imprisonment becomes the defining experience of the tragic king.

## Gaveston's Cruelty

Gaveston's first use of power upon his return from exile is to threaten imprisonment. Confronting the [[bishop-of-coventry|Bishop of Coventry]], he declares:

> He shall to prison, and there die in bolts.

And follows with a sneer:

> A prison may beseem his holiness. [001-011]

The line is darkly ironic in retrospect. Gaveston, who dispenses imprisonment so casually against the bishop, will himself be taken prisoner by [[warwick|Warwick]] and executed in a trench. The weapon he wielded against others becomes the fate he cannot escape.

## Prisoners of War

War creates prisoners, and the play's treatment of wartime captivity exposes the political economy of ransom. [[elder-mortimer|Elder Mortimer]] is taken prisoner by the Scots, and [[younger-mortimer|Younger Mortimer]] demands that the king pay his uncle's ransom:

> They rate his ransom at five thousand pound.
> Who should defray the money but the king,
> Seeing he is taken prisoner in his wars? [008-062]

The demand is reasonable in feudal terms — a lord captured in the king's service should be ransomed by the king — but Edward's financial mismanagement and obsession with Gaveston make the payment impossible. The ransom becomes another grievance in the accumulating case against the king.

## The Prisoner Gaveston

The pivotal event of the play's middle section is Gaveston's capture. [[pembroke|Pembroke]] offers his honour as surety:

> Upon mine oath, I will return him back. [012-020]

But Warwick ambushes Pembroke's men while their lord is absent, seizing Gaveston and executing him without trial. The episode turns on the status of "prisoner" — Pembroke's word should have protected Gaveston, but honour proves a fragile shield against political violence. [[arundel|Arundel]] reports the aftermath:

> The Earl of Warwick seized him on his way;
> For, being delivered unto Pembroke's men,
> Their lord rode home, thinking his prisoner safe;
> But, ere he came, Warwick in ambush lay,
> And bare him to his death. [012-058]

The violation of Pembroke's custodianship is a breach of chivalric law, a further sign that the realm is descending into extra-legal violence.

## The King's Imprisonment

Edward's transition from monarch to prisoner is the play's central tragic movement. The king who once imprisoned a bishop now finds himself confined by his own nobles. At [[kenilworth|Kenilworth Castle]], Edward articulates the paradox of imprisoned kingship:

> But what are kings, when regiment is gone,
> But perfect shadows in a sunshine day?
> My [[nobility|nobles]] rule; I bear the name of king,
> I wear the [[sovereignty|crown]]; but am controlled by them. [019-004]

The language of imprisonment here is not physical but political — Edward is confined not by walls but by the collapse of his authority. Yet even as he speaks, he is being moved from castle to castle, his physical freedom shrinking as his political power evaporates.

[[kent|Kent]]'s lament crystallises the theme:

> O, miserable is that commonweal,
> Where lords keep courts, and kings are locked in prison! [021-014]

The line inverts the natural order — lords holding court while kings rot in dungeons — and serves as both political commentary and tragic recognition. The "miserable commonweal" is the polity in which hierarchy has collapsed.

## The Mobile Prison

[[younger-mortimer|Mortimer]]'s instructions for Edward's custody reveal imprisonment as psychological torture:

> Remove him still from place to place by night,
> Till at the last he come to Killingworth,
> And then from thence to Berkeley back again. [020-022]

The constant movement — Kenilworth to Berkeley and back — denies Edward even the stability of a fixed prison. He is not merely imprisoned but unmoored, perpetually in transit, deprived of the rest that even a dungeon might afford. The mobile prison is Mortimer's refinement of cruelty: a torment of perpetual dislocation.

## Berkeley's Dungeon

The play's final prison is [[berkeley-castle|Berkeley Castle]], where Edward meets his death. The dungeon is both physical space and metaphysical condition. [[lightborn|Lightborn]] arrives as the agent of that death, and the prison becomes a tomb. Edward's last moments are staged in the enclosed space of his cell — the king who once commanded palaces dies in a dungeon.

The arc is complete: the king who imprisoned a bishop is himself imprisoned; the man who ordered others confined is himself confined unto death. Imprisonment in *Edward II* is not merely a plot device but the play's central metaphor for the condition of fallen sovereignty.
