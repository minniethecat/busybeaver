# Council

The **council** in [[edward-ii]] is the institutional space where governance is conducted—and contested. Distinct from [[parliament]] (which represents the broader realm), the council operates as an executive body, closer to the monarch's person and therefore more susceptible to factional capture. As the play progresses, control of the council becomes synonymous with control of the kingdom.

## The Queen's Council

After Edward's deposition, the council emerges as an instrument of the new regime. Leicester announces:

> My lord, the council of the queen command / That I resign my charge (019-026)

The formulation is revealing: it is "the council of the queen," not the council of the realm. The possessive marks the council as an extension of [[Queen Isabella]] and Mortimer's power rather than an independent deliberative body. The council that once advised the king now commands in the queen's name—the institution persists, but its allegiance has shifted.

## The Council-Table

Mortimer Junior's description of himself at the "council-table, grave enough" (022-019) captures the performance of governance. The council-table is a stage where authority is enacted through gravity and decorum. Mortimer's "grave" demeanour at the council-table masks his private scheming, revealing the gap between the council's public function and the private ambitions it serves.

## The Council-Chamber

In the play's final scene, Mortimer retreats to the "council-chamber" (024-013), seeking the institutional protection of the space. But the council-chamber cannot shield him from the consequences of his actions. The young Edward III, asserting the authority that properly belongs to the crown, pursues Mortimer even into this sanctum:

> Into the council-chamber he is gone, / To crave the aid and succour of his peers (024-013)

| Institution | Function | Vulnerability |
|-------------|----------|---------------|
| Council | Executive governance | Factional capture by Mortimer |
| [[parliament\|Parliament]] | Representative deliberation | Manipulation by nobles |
| Crown | Sovereign authority | Personal weakness of Edward II |

The council's trajectory mirrors the play's larger arc: an institution designed to check power becomes a vehicle for its abuse, and only the reassertion of legitimate royal authority—in the person of Edward III—can restore its proper function.
