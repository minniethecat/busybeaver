# Treason

Treason in *[[edward-ii|Edward II]]* is less a stable legal category than a political weapon wielded by every faction. The crown charges the barons with treason for rising against the king; the barons charge the king's favourites with treason for corrupting the realm; and the king himself is ultimately deposed on grounds that amount to treason against the office he embodies. The play thus exposes treason not as a fixed crime but as a rhetorical instrument whose meaning shifts with political power.

## The Charge of Treason

The word "treason" is shouted at moments of maximum political tension. Young Mortimer acknowledges the structural paradox early in the conflict:

> Then may we with some colour rise in arms;
> For, howsoever we have borne it out,
> 'Tis treason to be up against the king;
> So shall we have the people of our side,
> Which, for his father's sake, lean to the king,
> But cannot brook a night-grown mushroom,
> Such a one as my Lord of Cornwall is,
> Should bear us down of the [[nobility|nobility]].

This admission reveals the barons' awareness that their resistance is technically treasonous, yet they proceed anyway -- a measure of how thoroughly they believe the king has forfeited his legitimacy. Mortimer seeks "some colour" -- a legal pretext or justification -- precisely because he knows the act is treasonous under existing law. His solution is to secure popular opinion, transforming treason into patriotism through sheer force of consensus.

When the barons capture Gaveston, Edward immediately deploys the charge in the opposite direction:

> Ah, traitors, have they put my friend to death?
> Tell me, [[arundel|Arundel]], died he ere thou cam'st,
> Or didst thou see my friend to take his death?

> Yea, Spenser, traitors all.

Edward's accusation -- "traitors all" -- encompasses the entire baronial party. Yet [[warwick|Warwick]] has already pre-emptively rejected the label: "We are no traitors; therefore threaten not" (004-020). The symmetrical exchange of treason accusations reveals the word's emptiness as a legal category; each side simply denies the label while applying it to the other.

The Spensers are executed as traitors in a scene that dispenses with legal form entirely. When the [[young-spenser|Younger Spenser]] hears that Warwick seized Gaveston, his response is immediate: "Some treason, or some villainy, was the cause" (011-033). The accusation of treason serves as a pre-emptive justification for whatever violence follows, transforming extrajudicial execution into an act of political purification.

## The King as Traitor

The most radical move in the play's treatment of treason is the implication that the king himself may be guilty of it. By favouring Gaveston over the realm's welfare, by squandering resources, and by making war on his own nobles, Edward commits what the barons construe as treason against the office of [[sovereignty|kingship]].

> Proud traitor, Mortimer, why dost thou chase
> Thy lawful king, thy sovereign, with thy sword?
> Vile wretch, and why hast thou, of all unkind,
> Borne arms against thy brother and thy king?

[[kent|Kent]]'s soliloquy at the battle's turning point reverses the treason charge -- Mortimer is the traitor for making war on his "lawful king." But Kent's language also hints at the complexity: Mortimer is a "proud traitor" because he is both legally in the wrong and politically in the ascendant. The soliloquy captures the moment when the legal category of treason and the political reality of power diverge completely.

The [[deposition|deposition]] crystallises this logic: Edward is forced to surrender the crown not for a specific statutory crime but for a pattern of behaviour deemed treasonous to the commonwealth. This was an explosively dangerous argument in the 1590s -- the notion that a king could commit treason against his own realm threatened the entire architecture of Tudor political theology. Marlowe stages the argument without fully endorsing or condemning it, leaving the audience to reckon with its implications.

## The Treason-Accuser Accused

The cyclical pattern of treason accusation finds its fullest expression in Mortimer's [[mortality|fate]]. Having wielded the charge against Gaveston, the Spensers, and implicitly against Edward himself, Mortimer is ultimately condemned for treason against the young [[prince-edward|Edward III]].

> Traitor, in me my loving father speaks,
> And plainly saith, 'twas thou that murderedst him.

> Hence with the traitor, with the murderer!

Edward III's judgment is direct and unambiguous. The boy king needs no legal subtlety; the murdered father speaks through the living son. Mortimer's head is brought on stage, and Edward III offers it to his father's ghost:

> Sweet father, here unto thy murdered ghost
> I offer up the wicked traitor's head;
> And let these tears, distilling from mine eyes,
> Be witness of my [[grief|grief]] and innocency.

The ritualistic offering of the traitor's head restores a kind of [[natural-order|order]], but it is an order purchased through violence. Treason has been punished, but the cycle of accusation and counter-accusation has claimed the king himself, his brother Kent, his favourites, and the man who orchestrated his fall. The play suggests that treason is not a property of specific acts but of losing. Victory determines who is called traitor and who is called loyal.

## Treason as Dramatic Engine

Accusations of treason structure the play's major turning points. [[gavestons-recall|Gaveston's return]] is met with charges of treason; the Spensers are executed as traitors; Kent is arrested as a traitor for attempting to rescue the imprisoned king; Mortimer himself, having wielded the charge against others, is ultimately condemned for treason against the young Edward III. The cyclical pattern -- accuser becomes accused -- suggests that in a world without settled constitutional authority, the label of traitor adheres to whoever lacks the power to resist it.
