# Trussel

Trussel is the procedural face of Edward II's deposition. In a play filled with grand passions — love, vengeance, ambition — Trussel speaks the language of parliamentary process. His demand for the crown is impersonal, constitutional, and for that very reason, devastating.

## The Parliamentary Demand

Trussel enters alongside Leicester and the Bishop of Winchester, bearing the authority of Parliament. His words are formal and uncompromising: "My lord, the parliament must have present news; And therefore say, will you resign or no?" (019-009). The question brooks no negotiation — Trussel does not accuse, does not condemn; he merely executes the will of the assembled realm. When Edward refuses to resign, Trussel prepares to depart with the crown (019-011), his duty discharged if the answer is not forthcoming.

## The Abdication Ceremony

In the deposition scene proper, Trussel speaks the formal words of surrender: "And thus, most humbly do we take our leave" (019-019). His language is ritualised, almost liturgical — the crown is not seized but "received" through constitutional forms. He exits with the crown alongside the Bishop of Winchester (019-020), having physically transferred the symbol of sovereignty from one king to the next.

## Constitutional Significance

Trussel's role embodies the principle that deposition is not murder but legal process — a distinction the play insistently blurs. Where Lightborn's murder of Edward is secret, physical, and shameful, Trussel's removal of the crown is public, verbal, and ceremonious. The juxtaposition of these two modes of political violence — the constitutional and the carceral — is one of the play's darkest ironies.

## Historical Basis

The historical William Trussel (d.1347) was a knight and parliamentary official who played a prominent role in Edward II's deposition. As speaker of the Parliament of 1327, he formally renounced homage to Edward on behalf of the realm — the constitutional act that dissolved the bond between king and subjects. His speech was a landmark in English constitutional history, establishing that the realm could withdraw its allegiance from an unfit monarch.
