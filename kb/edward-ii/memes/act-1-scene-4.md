[004-001] The New Temple.

[004-002] |  | *Enter **[[lancaster|Lancaster]]**, **[[warwick|Warwick]]**, **[[pembroke|Pembroke]]**, the **[[elder-mortimer|Elder Mortimer]]**, the **[[younger-mortimer|Younger Mortimer]]**, the **[[archbishop-of-canterbury|Archbishop of Canterbury]]**, and Attendants.* |

[004-003] | [[earl-of-lancaster|Lancaster]] | Here is the form of [[piers-gaveston|Gaveston]]’s [[exile]]; May it please your lordship to subscribe your name. |

[004-004] | Archbishop of [[ecclesiastical-power|Canterbury]] | Give me the paper. *He subscribes, as the others do after him.* |

[004-005] | Lancaster | Quick, quick, my lord; I long to write my name. |

[004-006] | Warwick | But I long more to see him banished hence. |

[004-007] | Younger Mortimer | The name of Mortimer shall fright the king, Unless he be declined from that base peasant. |

[004-008] |  | *Enter **[[edward-ii|King Edward]]**, **Gaveston**, and **[[kent|Kent]]**.* |

[004-009] | King Edward | What, are you moved that Gaveston sits here? It is our pleasure; we will have it so. |

[004-010] | Lancaster | Your grace doth well to place him by your side, For nowhere else the new earl is so safe. |

[004-011] | Elder Mortimer | What man of noble birth can brook this sight? *Quam male conveniunt!—* See, what a scornful look the peasant casts! |

[004-012] | Pembroke | Can kingly lions fawn on creeping ants? |

[004-013] | Warwick | Ignoble vassal, that, like Phaeton, Aspir’st unto the guidance of the sun! |

[004-014] | Younger Mortimer | Their downfall is at hand, their forces down: We will not thus be faced and over-peered. |

[004-015] | King Edward | Lay hands on that [[treason|traitor]] Mortimer! |

[004-016] | Elder Mortimer | Lay hands on that [[treason|traitor]] Gaveston! |

[004-017] | Kent | Is this the duty that you owe your king? |

[004-018] | Warwick | We know our duties; let him know his peers. |

[004-019] | King Edward | Whither will you bear him? stay, or ye shall die. |

[004-020] | Elder Mortimer | We are no traitors; therefore threaten not. |

[004-021] | Gaveston | No, threaten not, my lord, but pay them home. Were I a king— |

[004-022] | Younger Mortimer | Thou, villain! wherefore talk’st thou of a king, That hardly art a gentleman by birth? |

[004-023] | King Edward | Were he a peasant, being my [[favoritism|minion]], I’ll make the proudest of you stoop to him. |

[004-024] | Lancaster | My lord—you may not thus disparage us.— Away, I say, with hateful Gaveston! |

[004-025] | Elder Mortimer | And with the [[earl-of-kent|Earl of Kent]] that favours him. |

[004-026] |  | *Attendants remove **Gaveston** and **Kent**.* |

[004-027] | King Edward | Nay, then, lay violent hands upon your king: Here, Mortimer, sit thou in Edward’s throne; Warwick and Lancaster, wear you my [[sovereignty|crown]]. Was ever king thus overruled as I? |

[004-028] | Lancaster | Learn, then, to rule us better, and the realm. |

[004-029] | Younger Mortimer | What we have done, our heart-blood shall maintain. |

[004-030] | Warwick | Think you that we can brook this upstart’s [[pride]]? |

[004-031] | King Edward | Anger and wrathful [[wrath|fury]] stops my speech. |

[004-032] | Archbishop of Canterbury | Why are you not moved? be patient, my lord, And see what we your counsellors have done. |

[004-033] | Younger Mortimer | My lords, now let us all be resolute, And either have our wills, or lose our lives. |

[004-034] | King Edward | Meet you for this, proud over-daring peers! Ere my sweet Gaveston shall part from me, This isle shall fleet upon the ocean, And wander to the unfrequented Inde. |

[004-035] | Archbishop of Canterbury | You know that I am legate to the Pope: On your [[duty|allegiance]] to the see of Rome, Subscribe, as we have done, to his exile. |

[004-036] | Younger Mortimer | Curse him, if he refuse; and then may we Depose him, and elect another king. |

[004-037] | King Edward | Ay, there it goes! but yet I will not yield: Curse me, depose me, do the worst you can. |

[004-038] | Lancaster | Then linger not, my lord, but do it straight. |

[004-039] | Archbishop of Canterbury | Remember how the bishop was abused: Either banish him that was the cause thereof, Or I will presently discharge these lords Of duty and allegiance due to thee. |

[004-040] | King Edward | *Aside.* It boots me not to threat; I must speak fair: The legate of the Pope will be obeyed.— My lord, you shall be Chancellor of the realm; Thou, Lancaster, High-Admiral of our fleet; Young Mortimer and his uncle shall be earls; And you, Lord Warwick, President of the North; And thou of Wales. If this content you not, Make several kingdoms of this monarchy, And share it equally amongst you all, So I may have some nook or corner left, To frolic with my dearest Gaveston. |

[004-041] | Archbishop of Canterbury | Nothing shall alter us; we are resolved. |

[004-042] | Lancaster | Come, come, subscribe. |

[004-043] | Younger Mortimer | Why should you [[love-and-desire|love]] him whom the world hates so? |

[004-044] | King Edward | Because he loves me more than all the world. Ah, none but rude and savage-minded men Would seek the ruin of my Gaveston! You that be noble-born should pity him. |

[004-045] | Warwick | You that are princely-born should shake him off: For shame, subscribe, and let the clown depart. |

[004-046] | Elder Mortimer | Urge him, my lord. |

[004-047] | Archbishop of Canterbury | Are you content to banish him the realm? |

[004-048] | King Edward | I see I must, and therefore am content: Instead of ink, I’ll write it with my tears. *Subscribes.* |

[004-049] | Younger Mortimer | The king is lovesick for his minion. |

[004-050] | King Edward | ’Tis done: and now, accursed hand, fall off! |

[004-051] | Lancaster | Give it me: I’ll have it published in the streets. |

[004-052] | Younger Mortimer | I’ll see him presently despatched away. |

[004-053] | Archbishop of Canterbury | Now is my heart at ease. |

[004-054] | Warwick | And so is mine. |

[004-055] | Pembroke | This will be good news to the common sort. |

[004-056] | Elder Mortimer | Be it or no, he shall not linger here. |

[004-057] |  | *Exeunt all except **King Edward**.* |

[004-058] | King Edward | How fast they run to banish him I love! They would not stir, were it to do me good. Why should a king be subject to a priest? Proud Rome, that hatchest such imperial grooms, With these thy superstitious taper-lights, Wherewith thy antichristian churches blaze, I’ll fire thy crazed buildings, and enforce The papal towers to kiss the lowly ground! With slaughtered priests make Tiber’s channel swell, And banks raised higher with their sepulchres! As for the peers, that back the clergy thus, If I be king, not one of them shall live. |

[004-059] |  | *Reenter **Gaveston**.* |

[004-060] | Gaveston | My lord, I hear it whispered everywhere, That I am banished and must fly the land. |

[004-061] | King Edward | ’Tis true, sweet Gaveston: O were it false! The legate of the Pope will have it so, And thou must hence, or I shall be [[deposition|deposed]]. But I will reign to be revenged of them; And therefore, sweet friend, take it patiently. Live where thou wilt, I’ll send thee gold enough; And long thou shalt not stay; or, if thou dost, I’ll come to thee; my love shall ne’er decline. |

[004-062] | Gaveston | Is all my hope turned to this hell of [[grief]]? |

[004-063] | King Edward | Rend not my heart with thy too-piercing words: Thou from this land, I from myself am banished. |

[004-064] | Gaveston | To go from hence grieves not poor Gaveston; But to forsake you, in whose gracious looks The blessedness of Gaveston remains; For nowhere else seeks he felicity. |

[004-065] | King Edward | And only this torments my wretched soul, That, whether I will or no, thou must depart. Be governor of Ireland in my stead, And there abide till [[fortune]] call thee home. Here, take my picture, and let me wear thine: |

[004-066] |  | *They exchange pictures.* |

[004-067] |  | O, might I keep thee here, as I do this, Happy were I! but now most miserable. |

[004-068] | Gaveston | ’Tis something to be pitied of a king. |

[004-069] | King Edward | Thou shalt not hence; I’ll hide thee, Gaveston. |

[004-070] | Gaveston | I shall be found, and then ’twill grieve me more. |

[004-071] | King Edward | Kind words and mutual talk makes our grief greater: Therefore, with dumb embracement, let us part— Stay, Gaveston; I cannot leave thee thus. |

[004-072] | Gaveston | For every look, my love drops down a tear: Seeing I must go, do not renew my sorrow. |

[004-073] | King Edward | The time is little that thou hast to stay, And, therefore, give me leave to look my fill. But, come, sweet friend; I’ll bear thee on thy way. |

[004-074] | Gaveston | The peers will frown. |

[004-075] | King Edward | I pass not for their anger. Come, let’s go: O, that we might as well return as go! |

[004-076] |  | *Enter **[[queen-isabella|Queen Isabella]]**.* |

[004-077] | Queen Isabella | Whither goes my lord? |

[004-078] | King Edward | Fawn not on me, French strumpet; get thee gone! |

[004-079] | Queen Isabella | On whom but on my husband should I fawn? |

[004-080] | Gaveston | On Mortimer; with whom, ungentle queen— I judge no more—judge you the rest, my lord. |

[004-081] | Queen Isabella | In saying this, thou wrong’st me, Gaveston: Is’t not enough that thou corrupt’st my lord, And art a bawd to his affections, But thou must call mine [[honour-code|honour]] thus in question? |

[004-082] | Gaveston | I mean not so; your grace must pardon me. |

[004-083] | King Edward | Thou art too familiar with that Mortimer, And by thy means is Gaveston exiled: But I would wish thee reconcile the lords, Or thou shalt ne’er be reconciled to me. |

[004-084] | Queen Isabella | Your highness knows, it lies not in my power. |

[004-085] | King Edward | Away, then! touch me not.—Come, Gaveston. |

[004-086] | Queen Isabella | Villain, ’tis thou that robb’st me of my lord. |

[004-087] | Gaveston | Madam, ’tis you that rob me of my lord. |

[004-088] | King Edward | Speak not unto her: let her droop and pine. |

[004-089] | Queen Isabella | Wherein, my lord, have I deserved these words? Witness the tears that Isabella sheds, Witness this heart, that, sighing for thee, breaks, How dear my lord is to poor Isabel! |

[004-090] | King Edward | And witness heaven how dear thou art to me: There weep; for, till my Gaveston be repealed, Assure thyself thou com’st not in my sight. |

[004-091] |  | *Exeunt **King Edward** and **Gaveston**.* |

[004-092] | Queen Isabella | O miserable and distressed queen! Would, when I left sweet [[france-as-setting|France]], and was embarked, That charming Circe, walking on the waves, Had changed my shape! or at the marriage-day The cup of Hymen had been full of poison! Or with those arms, that twined about my neck, I had been stifled, and not lived to see The king my lord thus to abandon me! Like frantic Juno, will I fill the earth With ghastly murmur of my sighs and cries; For never doted Jove on Ganymede So much as he on cursed Gaveston: But that will more exasperate his wrath; I must entreat him, I must speak him fair, And be a means to call home Gaveston: And yet he’ll ever dote on Gaveston; And so am I forever miserable. |

[004-093] |  | *Reenter **Lancaster**, **Warwick**, **Pembroke**, the **Elder Mortimer**, and the **Younger Mortimer**.* |

[004-094] | Lancaster | Look, where the sister of the king of France Sits wringing of her hands and beats her breast! |

[004-095] | Warwick | The king, I fear, hath ill-treated her. |

[004-096] | Pembroke | Hard is the heart that injures such a saint. |

[004-097] | Younger Mortimer | I know ’tis ’long of Gaveston she weeps. |

[004-098] | Elder Mortimer | Why, he is gone. |

[004-099] | Younger Mortimer | Madam, how fares your grace? |

[004-100] | Queen Isabella | Ah, Mortimer, now breaks the king’s hate forth, And he confesseth that he loves me not! |

[004-101] | Younger Mortimer | Cry quittance, madam, then, and love not him. |

[004-102] | Queen Isabella | No, rather will I die a thousand deaths: And yet I love in vain; he’ll ne’er love me. |

[004-103] | Lancaster | Fear ye not, madam; now his minion’s gone, His wanton humour will be quickly left. |

[004-104] | Queen Isabella | O, never, Lancaster! I am enjoined, To sue unto you all for his repeal: This wills my lord, and this must I perform, Or else be banished from his highness’ presence. |

[004-105] | Lancaster | For his repeal, madam! he comes not back, Unless the sea cast up his shipwrecked body. |

[004-106] | Warwick | And to behold so sweet a sight as that, There’s none here but would run his horse to [[mortality|death]]. |

[004-107] | Younger Mortimer | But, madam, would you have us call him home? |

[004-108] | Queen Isabella | Ay, Mortimer, for, till he be restored, The angry king hath banished me [[westminster-london|the court]]; And, therefore, as thou lov’st and tender’st me, Be thou my advocate unto these peers. |

[004-109] | Younger Mortimer | What, would you have me plead for Gaveston? |

[004-110] | Elder Mortimer | Plead for him that will, I am resolved. |

[004-111] | Lancaster | And so am I, my lord: dissuade the queen. |

[004-112] | Queen Isabella | O, Lancaster, let him dissuade the king! For ’tis against my will he should return. |

[004-113] | Warwick | Then speak not for him; let the peasant go. |

[004-114] | Queen Isabella | ’Tis for myself I speak, and not for him. |

[004-115] | Pembroke | No speaking will prevail; and therefore cease. |

[004-116] | Younger Mortimer | Fair queen, forbear to angle for the fish Which, being caught, strikes him that takes it dead; I mean that vile torpedo, Gaveston, That now, I hope, floats on the Irish seas. |

[004-117] | Queen Isabella | Sweet Mortimer, sit down by me a while, And I will tell thee reasons of such weight As thou wilt soon subscribe to his repeal. |

[004-118] | Younger Mortimer | It is impossible: but speak your mind. |

[004-119] | Queen Isabella | Then, thus;—but none shall hear it but ourselves. *Talks to the **Younger Mortimer**, apart.* |

[004-120] | Lancaster | My lords, albeit the queen win Mortimer, Will you be resolute and hold with me? |

[004-121] | Elder Mortimer | Not I, against my nephew. |

[004-122] | Pembroke | Fear not; the queen’s words cannot alter him. |

[004-123] | Warwick | No? do but mark how earnestly she pleads! |

[004-124] | Lancaster | And see how coldly his looks make denial! |

[004-125] | Warwick | She smiles: now, for my life, his mind is changed! |

[004-126] | Lancaster | I’ll rather lose his friendship, I, than grant. |

[004-127] | Younger Mortimer | Well, of necessity it must be so.— My lords, that I abhor base Gaveston I hope your honours make no question. And therefore, though I plead for his repeal, ’Tis not for his sake, but to our avail; Nay, for the realm’s behoof, and for the king’s. |

[004-128] | Lancaster | Fie, Mortimer, dishonour not thyself! Can this be true, ’twas good to banish him? And is this true, to call him home again? Such reasons make white black, and dark night day. |

[004-129] | Younger Mortimer | My Lord of Lancaster, mark the respect. |

[004-130] | Lancaster | In no respect can contraries be true. |

[004-131] | Queen Isabella | Yet, good my lord, hear what he can allege. |

[004-132] | Warwick | All that he speaks is nothing; we are resolved. |

[004-133] | Younger Mortimer | Do you not wish that Gaveston were dead? |

[004-134] | Pembroke | I would he were! |

[004-135] | Younger Mortimer | Why, then, my lord, give me but leave to speak. |

[004-136] | Elder Mortimer | But, nephew, do not play the sophister. |

[004-137] | Younger Mortimer | This which I urge is of a burning zeal To mend the king and do our country good. Know you not Gaveston hath store of gold, Which may in Ireland purchase him such friends As he will front the mightiest of us all? And whereas he shall live and be beloved, ’Tis hard for us to work his overthrow. |

[004-138] | Warwick | Mark you but that, my lord of Lancaster. |

[004-139] | Younger Mortimer | But, were he here, detested as he is, How easily might some base slave be suborned To greet his lordship with a poniard, And none so much as blame the murderer, But rather praise him for that brave attempt, And in the chronicle enrol his name For purging of the realm of such a plague! |

[004-140] | Pembroke | He saith true. |

[004-141] | Lancaster | Ay, but how chance this was not done before? |

[004-142] | Younger Mortimer | Because, my lords, it was not thought upon. Nay, more, when he shall know it lies in us To banish him, and then to call him home, ’Twill make him vail the top flag of his pride, And fear to offend the meanest nobleman. |

[004-143] | Elder Mortimer | But how if he do not, nephew? |

[004-144] | Younger Mortimer | Then may we with some colour rise in arms; For, howsoever we have borne it out, ’Tis [[rebellion|treason]] to be up against the king; So shall we have the people of our side, Which, for his father’s sake, lean to the king, But cannot brook a night-grown mushroom, Such a one as my Lord of Cornwall is, Should bear us down of the nobility: And, when the commons and the [[nobility|nobles]] join, ’Tis not the king can buckler Gaveston; We’ll pull him from the strongest hold he hath. My lords, if to perform this I be slack, Think me as base a groom as Gaveston. |

[004-145] | Lancaster | On that condition Lancaster will grant. |

[004-146] | Warwick | And so will Pembroke and I. |

[004-147] | Elder Mortimer | And I. |

[004-148] | Younger Mortimer | In this I count me highly gratified, And Mortimer will rest at your command. |

[004-149] | Queen Isabella | And when this favour Isabel forgets, Then let her live abandoned and forlorn.— But see, in happy time, my lord the king, Having brought the Earl of Cornwall on his way, Is new returned. This news will glad him much: Yet not so much as me; I love him more Than he can Gaveston: would he loved me But half so much! then were I treble-blest. |

[004-150] |  | *Reenter **King Edward**, mourning.* |

[004-151] | King Edward | He’s gone, and for his absence thus I mourn: Did never sorrow go so near my heart As doth the want of my sweet Gaveston; And, could my crown’s revenue bring him back, I would freely give it to his enemies, And think I gained, having bought so dear a friend. |

[004-152] | Queen Isabella | Hark, how he harps upon his minion! |

[004-153] | King Edward | My heart is as an anvil unto sorrow, Which beats upon it like the Cyclops’ hammers, And with the noise turns up my giddy brain, And makes me frantic for my Gaveston. Ah, had some bloodless Fury rose from hell, And with my kingly sceptre struck me dead, When I was forced to leave my Gaveston! |

[004-154] | Lancaster | *Diablo*, what passions call you these? |

[004-155] | Queen Isabella | My gracious lord, I come to bring you news. |

[004-156] | King Edward | That you have parled with your Mortimer? |

[004-157] | Queen Isabella | That Gaveston, my lord, shall be repealed. |

[004-158] | King Edward | Repealed! the news is too sweet to be true. |

[004-159] | Queen Isabella | But will you love me, if you find it so? |

[004-160] | King Edward | If it be so, what will not Edward do? |

[004-161] | Queen Isabella | For Gaveston, but not for Isabel. |

[004-162] | King Edward | For thee, fair queen, if thou lov’st Gaveston; I’ll hang a golden tongue about thy neck, Seeing thou hast pleaded with so good success. |

[004-163] | Queen Isabella | No other jewels hang about my neck Than these, my lord; nor let me have more wealth Than I may fetch from this rich treasury. O, how a kiss revives poor Isabel! |

[004-164] | King Edward | Once more receive my hand; and let this be A second marriage ’twixt thyself and me. |

[004-165] | Queen Isabella | And may it prove more happy than the first! My gentle lord, bespeak these nobles fair, That wait attendance for a gracious look, And on their knees salute your majesty. |

[004-166] | King Edward | Courageous Lancaster, embrace thy king; And, as gross vapours perish by the sun, Even so let hatred with thy sovereign’s smile: Live thou with me as my companion. |

[004-167] | Lancaster | This salutation overjoys my heart. |

[004-168] | King Edward | Warwick shall be my chiefest counsellor: These silver hairs will more adorn my court Than gaudy silks or rich embroidery. Chide me, sweet Warwick, if I go astray. |

[004-169] | Warwick | Slay me, my lord, when I offend your grace. |

[004-170] | King Edward | In solemn triumphs and in public shows Pembroke shall bear the sword before the king. |

[004-171] | Pembroke | And with this sword Pembroke will fight for you. |

[004-172] | King Edward | But wherefore walks young Mortimer aside? Be thou commander of our royal fleet; Or, if that lofty office like thee not, I make thee here Lord Marshal of the realm. |

[004-173] | Younger Mortimer | My lord, I’ll marshal so your enemies, As England shall be quiet, and you safe. |

[004-174] | King Edward | And as for you, Lord Mortimer of Chirke, Whose great achievements in our foreign war Deserve no common place nor mean reward, Be you the general of the levied troops That now are ready to assail the Scots. |

[004-175] | Elder Mortimer | In this your grace hath highly honoured me, For with my nature war doth best agree. |

[004-176] | Queen Isabella | Now is the king of England rich and strong, Having the love of his renowned peers. |

[004-177] | King Edward | Ay, Isabel, ne’er was my heart so light.— Clerk of the crown, direct our warrant forth, For Gaveston, to Ireland! |

[004-178] |  | *Enter **[[beaumont|Beaumont]]** with warrant.* |

[004-179] |  | Beaumont, fly As fast as Iris or Jove’s Mercury. |

[004-180] | Beaumont | It shall be done, my gracious lord. *Exit.* |

[004-181] | King Edward | Lord Mortimer, we leave you to your charge. Now let us in, and feast it royally. Against our friend the Earl of Cornwall comes We’ll have a general tilt and tournament; And then his marriage shall be solemnised; For wot you not that I have made him sure Unto our cousin, the [[earl-of-gloucester|Earl of Gloucester]]’s heir? |

[004-182] | Lancaster | Such news we hear, my lord. |

[004-183] | King Edward | That day, if not for him, yet for my sake, Who in the triumph will be challenger, Spare for no cost; we will requite your love. |

[004-184] | Warwick | In this or aught your highness shall command us. |

[004-185] | King Edward | Thanks, gentle Warwick. Come, let’s in and revel. |

[004-186] |  | *Exeunt all except the **Elder Mortimer** and the **Younger Mortimer**.* |

[004-187] | Elder Mortimer | Nephew, I must to Scotland; thou stay’st here. Leave now to oppose thyself against the king: Thou seest by nature he is mild and calm; And, seeing his mind so dotes on Gaveston, Let him without controlment have his will. The mightiest kings have had their minions; Great Alexander loved Hephaestion, The conquering Hercules for Hylas wept, And for Patroclus stern Achilles drooped. And not kings only, but the wisest men; The Roman Tully loved Octavius, Grave Socrates wild Alcibiades. Then let his grace, whose youth is flexible, And promiseth as much as we can wish, Freely enjoy that vain lightheaded earl; For riper years will wean him from such toys. |

[004-188] | Younger Mortimer | Uncle, his wanton humour grieves not me; But this I scorn, that one so basely-born Should by his sovereign’s favour grow so pert, And riot it with the treasure of the realm, While soldiers mutiny for want of pay. He wears a lord’s revenue on his back, And, Midas-like, he jets it in the court, With base outlandish cullions at his heels, Whose proud fantastic liveries make such show As if that Proteus, god of shapes, appeared. I have not seen a dapper Jack so brisk: He wears a short Italian hooded cloak, Larded with pearl, and in his Tuscan cap A jewel of more value than the crown. While others walk below, the king and he, From out a window, laugh at such as we, And flout our train, and jest at our attire. Uncle, ’tis this that makes me impatient. |

[004-189] | Elder Mortimer | But, nephew, now you see the king is changed. |

[004-190] | Younger Mortimer | Then so I am, and live to do him service: But, whiles I have a sword, a hand, a heart, I will not yield to any such upstart. You know my mind: come, uncle, let’s away. |

[004-191] |  | *Exeunt.* |
