# Natural Order

The concept of natural order — the belief that the universe is structured in hierarchies of degree, from God through angels and monarchs to the lowliest creatures — is the unspoken premise against which *Edward II* measures its political catastrophe. When Edward elevates Gaveston above his natural station, he violates not merely custom but the cosmic order itself.

## Degree and Its Violation

The Elizabethan doctrine of degree held that every being occupies a fixed place in a divinely ordained hierarchy. The barons' outrage at Gaveston is rooted in this doctrine: "What man of noble birth can brook this sight?" (004-011). Gaveston's elevation is not merely politically inadvisable — it is ontologically wrong, a tear in the fabric of reality that must be repaired.

## The Upstart's Crime

Mortimer articulates the charge precisely: "Thou, villain! wherefore talk'st thou of a king, That hardly art a gentleman by birth?" (004-022). Gaveston's crime is not any action but his existence in a position that contradicts the natural order. The barons' violence is framed as restoration — returning the hierarchy to its proper configuration by removing the element that has been unnaturally elevated.

## Edward's Inversion

Edward's response — "Were he a peasant, being my minion, I'll make the proudest of you stoop to him" (004-023) — is not merely defiant but revolutionary. He claims the royal prerogative to redefine degree itself, to remake the natural order by sovereign will. This claim is what transforms the barons' grievance from a dispute about personnel into a constitutional crisis.

## Consequences of Disorder

The play traces the consequences of natural order's violation: Gaveston's elevation → baronial resistance → civil war → deposition → regicide. The chain of catastrophe suggests that violation of degree is not a victimless transgression — it unleashes forces that cannot be controlled. The restoration of order at the play's end (Edward III's execution of Mortimer) comes only after the realm has been devastated.
