# James

James is a minor figure who attends the imprisoned [[Edward II]] at [[Berkeley Castle]]. He first appears as an attendant to [[Pembroke]] in Act 8, where he is entrusted with guarding [[Gaveston]]: "Come hither, James: / I do commit this Gaveston to thee; / Be thou this night his keeper" [008-060]. Though his role is small, his presence carries significant dramatic weight: he is one of the few characters who treats the deposed king with ordinary human compassion, offering small kindnesses that contrast sharply with the calculated cruelty of Edward's captors. In a play dominated by grand political gestures and violent reversals, James's quiet decency provides a reminder of the human scale on which political tragedy is ultimately experienced.

## Compassion Amidst Cruelty

James's treatment of Edward is notable precisely because it is unremarkable — he offers water, speaks respectfully, and refrains from cruelty. In the context of Edward's imprisonment, where every interaction is a potential instrument of torment, James's ordinary humanity becomes extraordinary. His presence suggests that the moral universe of the play is not uniformly bleak: even in the depths of deposition and impending murder, there exist individuals who refuse to participate in the logic of violence.

## Dramatic Function

James functions as a moral witness. His presence at Edward's imprisonment allows the audience to see the king's suffering through eyes that register it as suffering rather than as political necessity. His earlier role as [[Gaveston]]'s keeper [008-060] creates a subtle dramatic parallel: the same man entrusted with guarding Edward's favourite becomes, in the play's final movement, a witness to the king's own degradation. This perspective is crucial for the play's tragic effect: without figures like James, Edward's downfall might register merely as a political lesson; with them, it becomes a human catastrophe. James thus embodies the play's insistence that political action has moral consequences that cannot be evaded by appeals to raison d'état.
