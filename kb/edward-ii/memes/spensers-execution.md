# Execution of the Spensers

The execution of the Spenser family — both Elder and Younger — marks the decisive collapse of Edward II's regime. These two men, father and son, had become the architects and symbols of Edward's misrule. Their deaths at the hands of the victorious baronial faction are both a military necessity and a symbolic cleansing of the body politic.

## The Capture

Elder Spenser is taken prisoner first, captured at Boroughbridge by Rice ap Howel: "Enter Rice ap Howel with the Elder Spenser prisoner, and Attendants" (017-020). Brought before Queen Isabella and Mortimer, he is denounced as "this traitor to the state, Spenser, the father to that wanton Spenser" (017-021). Mortimer orders his immediate execution: "Meanwhile have hence this rebel to the block" (017-030). Elder Spenser's defiance — "Rebel is he that fights against his sovereign" (017-031) — is the last voice of loyalist resistance before his removal.

## Younger Spenser's Fate

Younger Spenser — created Earl of Gloucester by Edward's patent — flees the battlefield but is hunted down. Rice ap Howel reports: "Spenser the son, created Earl of Gloucester, is likewise fled" (017-024). His capture and execution shortly follow, stripping Edward of his last political ally. The younger Spenser's ambitious hunger for titles and land, dramatised throughout Acts 3 and 4, ends in the brutal logic of regime change.

## Symbolic Significance

The paired executions of the Spensers close the chapter of Edward's personal rule. Where Gaveston was the favourite of the heart, the Spensers were the favourites of ambition — men who translated Edward's weakness into political power. Their deaths are not tragic but functional: the new regime requires their removal to secure its legitimacy. The execution of the father before the son, and the son after his flight, dramatises the thoroughness with which Mortimer and Isabella dismantle the old order.

## Historical Event

The historical executions of the Despensers in 1326 were among the most brutal political killings of the Middle Ages. Hugh Despenser the Elder, Earl of Winchester, was captured at Bristol and executed immediately on 27 October 1326. Hugh Despenser the Younger was given a show trial and publicly executed on 24 November 1326: he was hanged from a gallows fifty feet high, cut down while still alive, castrated, disembowelled, and finally beheaded and quartered. His head was displayed on London Bridge. Marlowe's play omits the gruesome details but captures the political logic: removing the king's favourites was the necessary prelude to removing the king himself.
