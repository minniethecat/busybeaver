# Mortimer's Execution

The execution of Younger Mortimer is the play's final event — the moment when the cycle of political violence closes and legitimate authority reasserts itself. The young Edward III, barely crowned, orders Mortimer's arrest for the murder of his father. Mortimer's fall, as swift as his rise, confirms the play's bleak vision of power: what violence builds, violence destroys.

## Edward III's Assertion of Authority

The newly crowned Edward III — a figure barely seen in the preceding acts — emerges in the final scene as the agent of justice. He enters "with Queen Isabella, the Archbishop of Canterbury, Champion, and Nobles" (022-020) as a monarch already acting with royal dignity: "Champion, here's to thee" (022-024). His attempts to save his uncle Kent reveal both his youth and his emerging resolve: "Sweet mother, if I cannot pardon him, entreat my Lord Protector for his life" (022-038). But when Mortimer overrules him, the king's restraint cracks: "What safety may I look for at his hands, if that my uncle shall be murdered thus?" (022-046). This question hangs over the scene — and over Mortimer's fate.

## Mortimer's Downfall

Mortimer's execution is anticipated rather than staged. His soliloquy at the opening of the scene — "The king must die, or Mortimer goes down" (022-003) — is fatefully prophetic. He dispatches Lightborn to kill Edward, orders Kent's execution over Edward III's protests, and presumes his power absolute: "I seal, I cancel, I do what I will" (022-019). But the young king's silent witness to these abuses accumulates the moral authority that will, in the historical event, authorise Mortimer's arrest and execution.

## Dramatic Closure

Mortimer's execution provides the formal closure that the play requires. Edward II's murder must be avenged, and the agent of that murder must be punished, for the moral order to be restored. Although Marlowe does not stage the execution — it occurs offstage, between the play's final scenes or after its close — the dramatic logic is inexorable. Mortimer who says "The king must die, or Mortimer goes down" (022-003) has written his own sentence. The wheel of Fortune that he invokes at his end has already begun its downward turn from the moment Edward III first questions his authority.

## Historical Event

The historical Roger Mortimer was arrested at Nottingham Castle in October 1330 on the orders of the seventeen-year-old Edward III. He was taken to London, given a summary trial, and hanged at Tyburn on 29 November 1330 — the first nobleman to be executed at the common place of execution. Unlike Marlowe's defiant Mortimer, the historical Mortimer reportedly confessed his sins before death. His body was left on the gallows for two days as a warning, and his estates were forfeited to the crown. The execution marked the true beginning of Edward III's personal rule and is often seen as the moment when the instability of Edward II's reign was finally laid to rest.
