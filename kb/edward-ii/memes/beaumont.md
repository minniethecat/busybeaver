# Beaumont

Beaumont is one of the play's most minor characters, appearing in a single scene as a messenger bearing a warrant. His brief moment on stage nevertheless illuminates the machinery of royal authority: Edward's commands, however arbitrary, are transmitted through loyal agents who ask no questions and offer no resistance.

## The Warrant Scene

Beaumont enters to receive Edward's urgent command: "Beaumont, fly as fast as Iris or Jove's Mercury" (004-179). The mythological comparison — Iris, messenger of the gods; Mercury, the swift-footed herald — elevates a routine delivery into a moment of classical grandeur. Beaumont's reply is the model of unquestioning obedience: "It shall be done, my gracious lord" (004-180). He exits immediately, his function discharged.

## Function in the Royalist Apparatus

Beaumont represents the silent machinery of royal administration. Unlike Gaveston, the Spensers, or the baronial lords, he has no agenda, no faction, no ambition. He is the embodiment of delegated authority — a figure who exists solely to execute the king's will. In a play dominated by factional struggle and competing interests, his blank obedience is almost unsettling.

## Historical Basis

The historical Beaumont family was a prominent noble house in medieval England. Louis de Beaumont (d.1333) served as Bishop of Durham under Edward II, while Henry de Beaumont (c.1280–1340) was a key royalist who fought at Bannockburn and remained loyal to Edward II throughout his reign. Marlowe's Beaumont may be based on one of these figures, reduced to a walk-on role that symbolises rather than dramatises royal service.
