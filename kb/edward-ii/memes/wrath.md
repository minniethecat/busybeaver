# Wrath

Wrath — the intense anger of kings and nobles — is a recurring emotional and political force in *Edward II*. It operates on two registers: the personal fury of a monarch whose authority is challenged, and the quasi-divine anger invoked to legitimise political violence. Edward's wrath is consistently framed in cosmic terms, yet it repeatedly fails to achieve its object, staging a tension between the rhetoric of absolute royal anger and the political reality of its limits.

## Wrath in Edward II

King Edward's wrath is both a sign of his royal authority and a political weakness. In Act 4, he declares: "Anger and wrathful fury stops my speech" [004-031], showing how his temper overwhelms his capacity for political communication. His courtiers recognise the danger of provoking him, noting that certain actions "will more exasperate his wrath" [004-092], and that they must manage him with careful entreaty.

The barons also experience and deploy wrath. In Act 6, a curse is invoked: "may he be, and perish in his wrath!" [006-051], weaponising Edward's own temper against him. The same act warns that "the wrathful nobles threaten wars" [006-106], explicitly linking noble anger to the structural threat of civil conflict.

In Act 12, a political settlement is attempted "to appease the wrath of their offended king" [012-017], recognising that royal wrath must be managed through negotiation — an implicit acknowledgment that the king's anger is a political fact to be navigated rather than a divine prerogative to be obeyed.

## Wrath as Political Force

The play repeatedly pairs wrath with war. Act 15 opens: "after many threats of wrathful war, Triumpheth England's Edward" [015-003], framing the entire conflict as driven by anger. The imagery of wrath culminates in Act 19 with a predatory simile: "he rends and tears it with his wrathful paw" [019-004], comparing the king's rage to a lion's violence — simultaneously majestic and destructive.

## Divine Wrath and Cosmic Frame

Edward consistently frames his anger in mythological and cosmic language. His comparison of himself to Jove — "Never doted Jove on Ganymede / So much as he on cursèd Gaveston" — elevates a political dispute over a favourite into a conflict of divine proportions. This rhetorical strategy treats royal wrath not as a personal failing but as a manifestation of cosmic order: to defy the king's will is to defy the gods themselves.

Queen Isabella offers a counter-theology of wrath. Her formulation in Act 18 — "Successful battle gives the God of kings / To them that fight in right, and fear in wrath" — decouples divine favour from royal identity and reassigns it to the righteousness of the cause. In her account, wrath is not the king's prerogative but a moral category determined by the justice of one's actions.

## Dramatic Function

Wrath serves as both catalyst and accelerant throughout the play. Edward's anger at Gaveston's first exile drives the initial political crisis; his fury at the bishops and barons escalates the confrontation; and his impotent rage in imprisonment marks the dissolution of his royal identity. Wrath is the emotional engine of the plot, yet it consistently fails to achieve its object — a pattern that underscores the play's sceptical treatment of absolute power and the passions that sustain it.

## Marlovian Ambiguity

Where orthodox Tudor political theology insisted that royal anger was an instrument of divine justice, Marlowe's play suggests a more unsettling possibility: that the king's wrath is indistinguishable from petulance, and that the cosmic frame invoked to justify it is mere rhetoric. The play neither endorses Edward's claims to divine anger nor wholly dismisses them, leaving the status of royal wrath — and by extension, of royal authority itself — radically uncertain. This ambiguity is characteristic of Marlowe's broader scepticism toward received political theology.
