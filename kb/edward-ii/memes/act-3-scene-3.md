[012-001] The battlefield, [[battle-of-boroughbridge|Boroughbridge]].

[012-002] |  | *Enter **[[edward-ii|King Edward]]**, the **Elder [[young-spenser|Spenser]]**, the **Younger Spenser**, **[[baldock|Baldock]]**, and Noblemen of the King’s side.* |

[012-003] | King Edward | Why do we sound retreat? upon them, lords! This day I shall your [[revenge|vengeance]] with my sword On those proud rebels that are up in arms, And do confront and countermand their king. |

[012-004] | Younger Spenser | I doubt it not, my lord; right will prevail. |

[012-005] | [[elder-spenser|Elder Spenser]] | ’Tis not amiss, my liege, for either part To breathe a while; our men, with sweat and dust All choked well near, begin to faint for heat; And this retire refresheth horse and man. |

[012-006] | Younger Spenser | Here come the rebels. |

[012-007] |  | *Enter the **[[younger-mortimer|Younger Mortimer]]**, **[[lancaster|Lancaster]]**, **[[warwick|Warwick]]**, **[[pembroke|Pembroke]]**, and others.* |

[012-008] | Younger Mortimer | Look, [[earl-of-lancaster|Lancaster]], yonder is Edward Among his flatterers. |

[012-009] | Lancaster | And there let him be, Till he pay dearly for their company. |

[012-010] | Warwick | And shall, or Warwick’s sword shall smite in vain. |

[012-011] | King Edward | What, rebels, do you shrink and sound retreat? |

[012-012] | Younger Mortimer | No, Edward, no; thy flatterers faint and fly. |

[012-013] | Lancaster | They’d best betimes forsake thee and their trains, For they’ll betray thee, traitors as they are. |

[012-014] | Younger Spenser | Traitor on thy face, rebellious Lancaster! |

[012-015] | Pembroke | Away, base upstart! brav’st thou [[nobility|nobles]] thus? |

[012-016] | Elder Spenser | A noble attempt and honourable deed, Is it not, trow ye, to assemble aid And levy arms against your lawful king? |

[012-017] | King Edward | For which, ere long, their heads shall satisfy To appease the wrath of their offended king. |

[012-018] | Younger Mortimer | Then, Edward, thou wilt fight it to the last, And rather bathe thy sword in subjects’ blood Than [[exile|banish]] that pernicious company? |

[012-019] | King Edward | Ay, traitors all, rather than thus be braved, Make England’s civil towns huge heaps of stones, And ploughs to go about our palace-gates. |

[012-020] | Warwick | A desperate and unnatural resolution!— Alarum to the fight! Saint George for England, and the barons’ right! |

[012-021] | King Edward | Saint George for England, and King Edward’s right! |

[012-022] |  | *Alarums. Exeunt the two parties severally.* |

[012-023] |  | *Reenter **King Edward** and his followers, with the Barons and **[[kent|Kent]]** captive.* |

[012-024] | King Edward | Now, lusty lords, now not by chance of war, But justice of the quarrel and the cause, Vailed is your [[pride]]: methinks you hang the heads But we’ll advance them, traitors: now ’tis time To be avenged on you for all your braves, And for the murder of my dearest friend, To whom right well you knew our soul was knit, Good Pierce of [[piers-gaveston|Gaveston]], my sweet favourite: Ah, rebels, recreants, you made him away! |

[012-025] | Kent | Brother, in regard of thee and of thy land, Did they remove that flatterer from thy throne. |

[012-026] | King Edward | So, sir, you have spoke: away, avoid our presence! *Exit **Kent**.* Accursed wretches, was’t in regard of us, When we had sent our messenger to request He might be spared to come to speak with us, And Pembroke undertook for his return, That thou, proud Warwick, watched the prisoner, Poor Pierce, and headed him ’gainst law of arms? For which thy head shall overlook the rest As much as thou in [[wrath|rage]] outwent’st the rest. |

[012-027] | Warwick | Tyrant, I scorn thy threats and menaces; It is but temporal that thou canst inflict. |

[012-028] | Lancaster | The worst is [[mortality|death]]; and better die to live Than live in infamy under such a king. |

[012-029] | King Edward | Away with them, my lord of Winchester! These lusty leaders, Warwick and Lancaster, I charge you roundly, off with both their heads! Away! |

[012-030] | Warwick | Farewell, vain world! |

[012-031] | Lancaster | Sweet Mortimer, farewell! |

[012-032] | Younger Mortimer | England, unkind to thy nobility, Groan for this [[grief]]! behold how thou art maimed! |

[012-033] | King Edward | Go, take that haughty Mortimer to [[tower-of-london|the Tower]]; There see him safe bestowed; and, for the rest, Do speedy execution on them all. Be gone! |

[012-034] | Younger Mortimer | What, Mortimer! can ragged stony walls Immure thy virtue that aspires to heaven? No, Edward, England’s scourge, it may not be; Mortimer’s hope surmounts his [[fortune]] far. |

[012-035] |  | *The captive Barons are led off.* |

[012-036] | King Edward | Sound, drums and trumpets! March with me, my friends. Edward this day hath crowned him king anew. |

[012-037] |  | *Exeunt all except the **Younger Spenser**, **[[levune|Levune]]** and **Baldock**.* |

[012-038] | Younger Spenser | Levune, the trust that we repose in thee Begets the quiet of King Edward’s land: Therefore be gone in haste, and with advice Bestow that treasure on the lords of [[france-as-setting|France]], That, therewith all enchanted, like the guard That suffered Jove to pass in showers of gold To Danae, all aid may be denied To Isabel the queen, that now in France Makes friends, to cross the seas with her young son, And step into his father’s regiment. |

[012-039] | Levune | That’s it these barons and the subtle queen Long levelled at. |

[012-040] | Baldock | Yea, but, Levune, thou seest, These barons lay their heads on blocks together: What they intend, the hangman frustrates clean. |

[012-041] | Levune | Have you no doubt, my lords, I’ll clap so close Among the lords of France with England’s gold, That Isabel shall make her plaints in vain, And France shall be obdurate with her tears. |

[012-042] | Younger Spenser | Then make for France amain; Levune, away! Proclaim King Edward’s wars and victories. |

[012-043] |  | *Exeunt.* |
