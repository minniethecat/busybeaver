# Marriage

**Marriage** in [[edward-ii]] is stripped of romance and exposed as a purely political instrument. The union of Edward and [[Queen Isabella]]—announced, discussed, and celebrated throughout the early acts—becomes the lens through which Marlowe examines the collision between personal desire and dynastic obligation. Far from a private bond, marriage in the play is a public transaction that shapes the kingdom's political trajectory.

## The Marriage of Edward and Isabella

Isabella's opening lament sets the tone for the play's treatment of marriage. She wishes that "at the marriage-day / The cup of Hymen had been full of poison" (004-092), imagining her wedding as a death rather than a union. This startling image establishes marriage not as a source of joy but as the origin of her suffering.

Edward's neglect of his wife in favour of [[Gaveston]] transforms the marriage from a personal wound into a political crisis. The barons urge Edward to "let this be / A second marriage 'twixt thyself and me" (004-164), seeking to re-establish the proper bond between king and kingdom through the metaphor of remarriage.

## Marriage and Legitimacy

The play links marriage to questions of political legitimacy. Edward arranges for Gaveston's marriage to his niece as a strategic move:

> And then his marriage shall be solemnised; / For wot you not that I have made him sure / Unto my cousin, the Earl of Gloucester's heir? (004-181)

| Aspect | Function in the Play |
|--------|---------------------|
| Dynastic alliance | Gaveston's marriage to the Earl of Gloucester's heir secures his noble status |
| Legitimacy marker | Edward's own marriage to Isabella anchors his claim to the French alliance |
| Betrayal site | Edward's neglect of Isabella's bed is both personal and political betrayal |
| Political metaphor | The barons frame reconciliation with Edward as a "second marriage" |

## The Politicisation of the Marriage Bed

Marlowe transforms the marriage bed into a political space. Gaveston's description of the marriage feast—"this day shall be your marriage feast" (006-136)—is immediately followed by plans for military action: "when the marriage ends, / Have at the rebels" (006-138). The juxtaposition reveals how marriage celebrations serve as cover for political violence.

Isabella's suffering as a neglected wife becomes the play's most potent political critique. Her complaint that her "marriage-day" (005-017) means nothing to Edward is not merely personal pathos—it is a challenge to the legitimacy of a king who fails in his dynastic duties.
