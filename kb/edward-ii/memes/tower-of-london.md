# Tower of [[westminster-london|London]]

The Tower of London is where the play's political violence reaches its terrible terminus. Edward II is taken there after his capture, imprisoned in the dungeon, and murdered by Lightborn in one of the most harrowing scenes in early modern drama. The Tower's transformation from a symbol of royal power into the site of the king's degradation epitomises the play's relentless inversion of [[natural-order|hierarchy]].

## Dramatic Function

The Tower appears in the final movement of Act 5, signalled by the stage direction "Near the Tower of London" (013-001). It is here that Mortimer dispatches Lightborn with the unspoken command to kill the king. The scene that follows — Edward in the castle sewer, Lightborn's arrival, the red-hot spit — occurs within the Tower's walls. The fortress that was built by William the Conqueror to project royal authority becomes the instrument of royal annihilation.

Lightborn's practical request for his [[murder|murder]] instruments makes the Tower's transformation concrete:

> I know what I must do. Get you away: Yet be not far off; I shall need your help: See that in the next room I have a fire, And get me a spit, and let it be red-hot. (023-016)

The domesticity of the request — a fire, a spit, a table, a featherbed — is what makes it horrifying. These are household objects, not instruments of state. The Tower, the realm's great fortress, has become a kitchen where a king will be cooked.

## Symbol of Inverted [[sovereignty|Sovereignty]]

Throughout the play, locations shift their symbolic charge. [[tynemouth|Tynemouth]] is a refuge that becomes a trap; Kenilworth is a site of imprisonment that becomes a site of [[deposition|abdication]]. The Tower completes this pattern. The ultimate symbol of monarchical power — the fortress where kings kept their treasures and their prisoners — becomes the place where a king is reduced to the most abject of deaths.

Lightborn's parting words to the gaolers capture the utter degradation of the scene:

> So, now. Must I [[About|about]] this gear: ne'er was there any So finely handled as this king shall be. — Foh, here's a place indeed with all my heart! (023-018)

The professional assassin's disgust — "Foh, here's a place indeed" — at the sewer where Edward is kept is a grim joke. The place that disgusts Lightborn is the Tower of London itself, now befouled by the very prisoner it was built to hold.

## Historical Geography

The Tower of London was indeed the site of significant political imprisonments and deaths in the medieval period, though Edward II was not actually murdered there — he died at Berkeley Castle. Marlowe relocates the murder to the Tower, perhaps because it carried greater symbolic weight for a London audience familiar with its reputation. The historical Tower was a royal palace, a prison, a treasury, and a menagerie — a concentration of royal power that Marlowe's audience would have immediately recognised.
