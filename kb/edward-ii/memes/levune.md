# Levune

Levune is the diplomatic linchpin of Queen Isabella's return to England. As a Frenchman in her service, he bridges the gap between the exiled queen's aspirations and the material support she needs from the French court. His role is entirely functional — he carries messages, negotiates passage, and facilitates the logistics of invasion — yet without him, Isabella's counter-coup would have no international dimension.

## Diplomatic Function

Levune first appears accompanying Isabella and Prince Edward in France (011-015). The Younger Spenser immediately identifies him as a trusted agent: "Our friend Levune, faithful and full of trust, Informeth us of many a different plot" (011-017). Edward himself greets him warmly: "Welcome, Levune" (011-018). The trust placed in him by both royalist and queen's factions suggests a figure of unusual diplomatic skill.

## The French Connection

The Spensers delegate sensitive tasks to Levune: "Levune, the trust that we repose in thee concerns us nearly" (012-038). He is entrusted with escorting Prince Edward and coordinating with the French: "Then make for France amain; Levune, away!" (012-042). His French identity gives him access and credibility in a court where English nobles would be viewed with suspicion. The Younger Spenser later receives word from him by letter: "from Levune" (015-012), signed with the loyal formula "Your honour's in all service, Levune" (015-014).

## Dramatic Significance

Levune's dramatic function is to make the international dimension of the conflict tangible. Without him, Isabella's escape to France and return with an army would happen entirely offstage. His presence on stage — receiving orders, carrying messages, appearing in multiple scenes across acts — converts a geopolitical abstraction into a human operation. He is the play's answer to the question: how does a deposed queen actually coordinate an invasion?
