# Baldock

Baldock is a minor figure who illuminates a major theme: the corruption of intellect into instrument of power. A scholar who serves as tutor to the king's niece, his transformation into a courtier traces the path by which learning becomes servility.

## The Scholar-Courtier

Baldock's most revealing moment is his self-description as a hypocrite by training: "Mine old lord, whiles he lived, was so precise, That he would take exceptions at my buttons" (005-013). His education has taught him to dissemble — to appear virtuous while preparing for "any kind of villainy." The scholar, in Marlowe's world, is not the opposite of the courtier but the courtier's most sophisticated form.

## Instrument of Power

Baldock attaches himself to the Younger Spenser, who serves Gaveston, who serves the king. His career is a chain of dependencies, each link requiring the suppression of whatever scruples remain from his scholarly past. His eventual execution alongside Spenser is the logical conclusion of a life lived as another man's instrument.
