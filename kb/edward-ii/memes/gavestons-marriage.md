# Gaveston's Marriage

Edward II arranges Gaveston's marriage to his niece — the daughter of the Duke of Gloucester — as a gesture of supreme favour that places the favourite within the royal family itself. The marriage is both a personal bond and a political provocation, transforming Gaveston from a mere court favourite into a member of the blood royal.

## Royal Favour Made Flesh

The marriage is Edward's most audacious move in his campaign to elevate Gaveston above all other subjects. By wedding his favourite to a Plantagenet princess, Edward signals that the normal hierarchies of birth and rank do not apply where Gaveston is concerned. The barons, who see noble marriage as a matter of family alliance and territorial strategy, are outraged that the king's niece — a royal bride — should be given to an obscure Gascon knight.

## The Niece's Role

The niece herself is a largely silent figure in the play, her consent assumed rather than dramatised. She functions as a token of exchange in the economy of royal favour — a human bond that Edward deploys to tie Gaveston more tightly to himself. Her presence in the play, brief as it is, illustrates the costs that Edward's obsession imposes on those around him.

## Political Consequences

The marriage accelerates the baronial rebellion. It is one thing for the king to shower a favourite with gifts and titles; it is quite another to give him a princess. The marriage confirms the barons' worst fear: that Edward will stop at nothing to advance Gaveston, even at the expense of the nobility's traditional prerogatives and the royal family's dignity.

## Historical Event

The historical Piers Gaveston married Margaret de Clare (c.1293–1342) in 1307, shortly after Edward II's accession. Margaret was the daughter of Gilbert de Clare, Earl of Gloucester, and Joan of Acre — making her Edward II's niece through his sister Joan. The marriage was a spectacular elevation for a Gascon knight and was deeply resented by the English baronage. After Gaveston's execution in 1312, Margaret remarried and lived until 1342, long outliving both her first husband and the political world that had destroyed him.
