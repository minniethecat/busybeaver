[006-001] Before [[tynemouth|Tynemouth Castle]].

[006-002] |  | *Enter **[[edward-ii|King Edward]]**, **[[queen-isabella|Queen Isabella]]**, **[[kent|Kent]]**, **[[lancaster|Lancaster]]**, the **[[younger-mortimer|Younger Mortimer]]**, **[[warwick|Warwick]]**, **[[pembroke|Pembroke]]**, and Attendants.* |

[006-003] | King Edward | The wind is good; I wonder why he stays: I fear me he is wrecked upon the sea. |

[006-004] | Queen Isabella | Look, [[earl-of-lancaster|Lancaster]], how passionate he is, And still his mind runs on his [[favoritism|minion]]! |

[006-005] | Lancaster | My lord— |

[006-006] | King Edward | How now! what news? is [[piers-gaveston|Gaveston]] arrived? |

[006-007] | Younger Mortimer | Nothing but Gaveston! what means your grace? You have matters of more weight to think upon: The [[king-of-france|King of France]] sets foot in Normandy. |

[006-008] | King Edward | A trifle! we’ll expel him when we please. But tell me, Mortimer, what’s thy device Against the stately triumph we decreed? |

[006-009] | Younger Mortimer | A homely one, my lord, not worth the telling. |

[006-010] | King Edward | Pray thee, let me know it. |

[006-011] | Younger Mortimer | But, seeing you are so desirous, thus it is; A lofty cedar tree, fair flourishing, On whose top branches kingly eagles perch, And by the bark a canker creeps me up, And gets unto the highest bough of all; The motto, *Aeque tandem.* |

[006-012] | King Edward | And what is yours, my Lord of Lancaster? |

[006-013] | Lancaster | My lord, mine’s more obscure than Mortimer’s. Pliny reports there is a flying-fish Which all the other fishes deadly hate, And therefore, being pursued, it takes the air: No sooner is it up, but there’s a fowl That seizeth it: this fish, my lord, I bear; The motto this, *Undique mors est.* |

[006-014] | Kent | Proud Mortimer! ungentle Lancaster! Is this the [[love-and-desire|love]] you bear your sovereign? Is this the fruit your reconcilement bears? Can you in words make show of amity, And in your shields display your rancorous minds? What call you this but private libelling Against the Earl of Cornwall and my brother? |

[006-015] | Queen Isabella | Sweet husband, be content; they all love you. |

[006-016] | King Edward | They love me not that hate my Gaveston. I am that cedar; shake me not too much; And you the eagles; soar ye ne’er so high, I have the jesses that will pull you down; And *Aeque tandem* shall that canker cry Unto the proudest peer of Britainy. Thou that compar’st him to a flying-fish, And threaten’st [[mortality|death]] whether he rise or fall, ’Tis not the hugest monster of the sea, Nor foulest harpy, that shall swallow him. |

[006-017] | Younger Mortimer | If in his absence thus he favours him, What will he do whenas he shall be present? |

[006-018] | Lancaster | That shall we see: look, where his lordship comes! |

[006-019] |  | *Enter **Gaveston**.* |

[006-020] | King Edward | My Gaveston! Welcome to Tynmouth! welcome to thy friend! Thy absence made me droop and pine away; For, as the lovers of fair Danae, When she was locked up in a brazen tower, Desired her more, and waxed outrageous, So did it fare with me: and now thy sight Is sweeter far than was thy parting hence Bitter and irksome to my sobbing heart. |

[006-021] | Gaveston | Sweet lord and king, your speech preventeth mine; Yet have I words left to express my joy: The shepherd, nipt with biting winter’s [[wrath|rage]], Frolics not more to see the painted spring Than I do to behold your majesty. |

[006-022] | King Edward | Will none of you salute my Gaveston? |

[006-023] | Lancaster | Salute him! yes.—Welcome, Lord Chamberlain! |

[006-024] | Younger Mortimer | Welcome is the good Earl of Cornwall! |

[006-025] | Warwick | Welcome, Lord Governor of the Isle of Man! |

[006-026] | Pembroke | Welcome, Master Secretary! |

[006-027] | Kent | Brother, do you hear them? |

[006-028] | King Edward | Still will these earls and [[nobility|barons]] use me thus? |

[006-029] | Gaveston | My lord, I cannot brook these injuries. |

[006-030] | Queen Isabella | *Aside.* Ay me, poor soul, when these begin to jar! |

[006-031] | King Edward | Return it to their throats; I’ll be thy warrant. |

[006-032] | Gaveston | Base, leaden earls, that glory in your birth, Go sit at home, and eat your tenants’ beef; And come not here to scoff at Gaveston, Whose mounting thoughts did never creep so low As to bestow a look on such as you. |

[006-033] | Lancaster | Yet I disdain not to do this for you. *Draws his sword, and offers to stab **Gaveston**.* |

[006-034] | King Edward | [[treason|Treason]]! [[rebellion|treason]]! where’s the [[treason|traitor]]? |

[006-035] | Pembroke | Here, here! |

[006-036] | King Edward | Convey hence Gaveston; they’ll murder him. |

[006-037] | Gaveston | The life of thee shall salve this foul disgrace. |

[006-038] | Younger Mortimer | Villain, thy life! unless I miss mine aim. *Wounds **Gaveston**.* |

[006-039] | Queen Isabella | Ah, furious Mortimer, what hast thou done? |

[006-040] | Younger Mortimer | No more than I would answer, were he slain. |

[006-041] |  | *Exit **Gaveston** with Attendants.* |

[006-042] | King Edward | Yes, more than thou canst answer, though he live: Dear shall you both abide this riotous deed: Out of my presence! come not near [[westminster-london|the court]]. |

[006-043] | Younger Mortimer | I’ll not be barred the court for Gaveston. |

[006-044] | Lancaster | We’ll hale him by the ears unto the block. |

[006-045] | King Edward | Look to your own heads; his is sure enough. |

[006-046] | Warwick | Look to your own [[sovereignty|crown]], if you back him thus. |

[006-047] | Kent | Warwick, these words do ill beseem thy years. |

[006-048] | King Edward | Nay, all of them conspire to cross me thus: But, if I live, I’ll tread upon their heads That think with high looks thus to tread me down. Come, [[earl-of-kent|Edmund]], let’s away, and levy men: ’Tis war that must abate these barons’ [[pride]]. |

[006-049] |  | *Exeunt **King Edward**, **Queen Isabella**, and **Kent**.* |

[006-050] | Warwick | Let’s to our castles, for the king is moved. |

[006-051] | Younger Mortimer | Moved may he be, and perish in his wrath! |

[006-052] | Lancaster | Cousin, it is no dealing with him now; He means to make us stoop by force of arms: And therefore let us jointly here protest To prosecute that Gaveston to the death. |

[006-053] | Younger Mortimer | By heaven, the abject villain shall not live! |

[006-054] | Warwick | I’ll have his blood, or die in seeking it. |

[006-055] | Pembroke | The like oath Pembroke takes. |

[006-056] | Lancaster | And so doth Lancaster. Now send our heralds to defy the king; And make the people swear to put him down. |

[006-057] |  | *Enter a **Messenger**.* |

[006-058] | Younger Mortimer | Letters! from whence? |

[006-059] | Messenger | *Giving letters to **Mortimer**.* From Scotland, my lord. |

[006-060] | Lancaster | Why, how now, cousin! how fare all our friends? |

[006-061] | Younger Mortimer | My uncle’s taken prisoner by the Scots. |

[006-062] | Lancaster | We’ll have him ransomed, man: be of good cheer. |

[006-063] | Younger Mortimer | They rate his ransom at five thousand pound. Who should defray the money but the king, Seeing he is taken prisoner in his wars? I’ll to the king. |

[006-064] | Lancaster | Do, cousin, and I’ll bear thee company. |

[006-065] | Warwick | Meantime my Lord of Pembroke and myself Will to Newcastle here, and gather head. |

[006-066] | Younger Mortimer | [[About]] it, then, and we will follow you. |

[006-067] | Lancaster | Be resolute and full of secrecy. |

[006-068] | Warwick | I warrant you. *Exit with **Pembroke**.* |

[006-069] | Younger Mortimer | Cousin, an if he will not ransom him, I’ll thunder such a peal into his ears As never subject did unto his king. |

[006-070] | Lancaster | Content; I’ll bear my part.—Hollo! who’s there? |

[006-071] |  | *Enter **Guard**.* |

[006-072] | Younger Mortimer | Ay, marry, such a guard as this doth well. |

[006-073] | Lancaster | Lead on the way. |

[006-074] | Guard | Whither will your lordships? |

[006-075] | Younger Mortimer | Whither else but to the king? |

[006-076] | Guard | His highness is disposed to be alone. |

[006-077] | Lancaster | Why, so he may; but we will speak to him. |

[006-078] | Guard | You may not in, my lord. |

[006-079] | Younger Mortimer | May we not? |

[006-080] |  | *Enter **King Edward** and **Kent**.* |

[006-081] | King Edward | How now! What noise is this? who have we here? is’t you? *Going.* |

[006-082] | Younger Mortimer | Nay, stay, my lord; I come to bring you news; Mine uncle’s taken prisoner by the Scots. |

[006-083] | King Edward | Then ransom him. |

[006-084] | Lancaster | ’Twas in your wars; you should ransom him. |

[006-085] | Younger Mortimer | And you will ransom him, or else— |

[006-086] | Kent | What, Mortimer, you will not threaten him? |

[006-087] | King Edward | Quiet yourself; you shall have the broad seal, To gather for him throughout the realm. |

[006-088] | Lancaster | Your minion Gaveston hath taught you this. |

[006-089] | Younger Mortimer | My lord, the family of the Mortimers Are not so poor, but, would they sell their land, ’Twould levy men enough to anger you. We never beg, but use such prayers as these. |

[006-090] | King Edward | Shall I still be haunted thus? |

[006-091] | Younger Mortimer | Nay, now you are here alone, I’ll speak my mind. |

[006-092] | Lancaster | And so will I; and then, my lord, farewell. |

[006-093] | Younger Mortimer | The idle triumphs, masks, lascivious shows, And prodigal gifts bestowed on Gaveston, Have drawn thy treasury dry, and made thee weak; The murmuring commons, overstretched, break. |

[006-094] | Lancaster | Look for rebellion, look to be [[deposition|deposed]]: Thy garrisons are beaten out of [[france-as-setting|France]], And, lame and poor, lie groaning at the gates; The wild Oneil, with swarms of Irish kerns, Lives uncontrolled within the English pale; Unto the walls of York the Scots make road, And, unresisted, drive away rich spoils. |

[006-095] | Younger Mortimer | The haughty Dane commands the narrow seas, While in the harbour ride thy ships unrigged. |

[006-096] | Lancaster | What foreign prince sends thee ambassadors? |

[006-097] | Younger Mortimer | Who loves thee, but a sort of flatterers? |

[006-098] | Lancaster | Thy gentle queen, sole sister to Valois, Complains that thou hast left her all forlorn. |

[006-099] | Younger Mortimer | Thy court is naked, being bereft of those That make a king seem glorious to the world, I mean the peers, whom thou shouldst dearly love; Libels are cast against thee in the street; Ballads and rhymes made of thy overthrow. |

[006-100] | Lancaster | The northern borderers, seeing their houses burnt, Their wives and children slain, run up and down, Cursing the name of thee and Gaveston. |

[006-101] | Younger Mortimer | When wert thou in the field with banner spread, But once? and then thy soldiers marched like players, With garish robes, not armour; and thyself, Bedaubed with gold, rode laughing at the rest, Nodding and shaking of thy spangled crest, Where women’s favours hung like labels down. |

[006-102] | Lancaster | And thereof came it that the fleering Scots, To England’s high disgrace, have made this jig; “Maids of England, sore may you mourn, For your lemans you have lost at Bannocksbourn— With a heave and a ho! What weeneth the king of England So soon to have won Scotland!— With a rombelow!” |

[006-103] | Younger Mortimer | Wigmore shall fly, to set my uncle free. |

[006-104] | Lancaster | And, when ’tis gone, our swords shall purchase more. If you be moved, [[revenge]] it as you can: Look next to see us with our ensigns spread. *Exit with the **Younger Mortimer**.* |

[006-105] | King Edward | My swelling heart for very anger breaks: How oft have I been baited by these peers, And dare not be revenged, for their power is great! Yet, shall the crowning of these cockerels Affright a lion? Edward, unfold thy paws, And let their lives’-blood slake thy fury’s hunger. If I be cruel and grow tyrannous, Now let them thank themselves, and rue too late. |

[006-106] | Kent | My lord, I see your love to Gaveston Will be the ruin of the realm and you, For now the wrathful nobles threaten wars; And therefore, brother, [[exile|banish]] him forever. |

[006-107] | King Edward | Art thou an enemy to my Gaveston? |

[006-108] | Kent | Ay; and it grieves me that I favoured him. |

[006-109] | King Edward | Traitor, be gone! whine thou with Mortimer. |

[006-110] | Kent | So will I, rather than with Gaveston. |

[006-111] | King Edward | Out of my sight, and trouble me no more! |

[006-112] | Kent | No marvel though thou scorn thy noble peers, When I thy brother am rejected thus. |

[006-113] | King Edward | Away! *Exit **Kent**.* Poor Gaveston, thou hast no friend but me! Do what they can, we’ll live in Tynmouth here; And, so I walk with him about the walls, What care I though the earls begirt us round? Here comes she that is cause of all these jars. |

[006-114] |  | *Enter **Queen Isabella**, with Edward’s **Niece**, two **Ladies**, **Gaveston**, **[[baldock|Baldock]]**, and the **[[young-spenser|Younger Spenser]]**.* |

[006-115] | Queen Isabella | My lord, ’tis thought the earls are up in arms. |

[006-116] | King Edward | Ay, and ’tis likewise thought you favour ’em. |

[006-117] | Queen Isabella | Thus do you still suspect me without cause. |

[006-118] | Niece | Sweet uncle, speak more kindly to the queen. |

[006-119] | Gaveston | My lord, dissemble with her; speak her fair. |

[006-120] | King Edward | Pardon me, sweet; I forgot myself. |

[006-121] | Queen Isabella | Your pardon is quickly got of Isabel. |

[006-122] | King Edward | The younger Mortimer is grown so brave, That to my face he threatens civil wars. |

[006-123] | Gaveston | Why do you not commit him to [[tower-of-london|the Tower]]? |

[006-124] | King Edward | I dare not, for the people love him well. |

[006-125] | Gaveston | Why, then, we’ll have him privily made away. |

[006-126] | King Edward | Would Lancaster and he had both caroused A bowl of poison to each other’s health! But let them go, and tell me what are these. |

[006-127] | Niece | Two of my father’s servants whilst he lived: May’t please your grace to entertain them now. |

[006-128] | King Edward | Tell me, where wast thou born? what is thine arms? |

[006-129] | Baldock | My name is Baldock, and my gentry I fetch from Oxford, not from heraldry. |

[006-130] | King Edward | The fitter art thou, Baldock, for my turn. Wait on me, and I’ll see thou shalt not want. |

[006-131] | Baldock | I humbly thank your majesty. |

[006-132] | King Edward | Knowest thou him, Gaveston. |

[006-133] | Gaveston | Ay, my lord; His name is Spenser; he is well allied: For my sake let him wait upon your grace; Scarce shall you find a man of more desert. |

[006-134] | King Edward | Then, Spenser, wait upon me for his sake: I’ll grace thee with a higher style ere long. |

[006-135] | Younger Spenser | No greater titles happen unto me Than to be favoured of your majesty! |

[006-136] | King Edward | Cousin, this day shall be your marriage feast:— And, Gaveston, think that I love thee well, To wed thee to our niece, the only heir Unto the [[earl-of-gloucester|Earl of Gloucester]] late deceased. |

[006-137] | Gaveston | I know, my lord, many will stomach me; But I respect neither their love nor hate. |

[006-138] | King Edward | The headstrong barons shall not limit me; He that I list to favour shall be great. Come, let’s away; and, when the marriage ends, Have at the rebels and their complices! |

[006-139] |  | *Exeunt.* |
