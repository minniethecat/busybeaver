# Parliament

**Parliament** in [[edward-ii]] functions as the institutional voice of the [[nobility]] and the realm against the king's personal rule. Introduced in the play's opening scene, parliament is the mechanism through which the barons challenge Edward's authority over [[Gaveston]]'s banishment and recall, and it remains a persistent counterweight to royal prerogative throughout the drama.

## Parliament and Gaveston's Banishment

The play's central political conflict is framed from the outset in parliamentary terms. Gaveston reads the articles of his banishment as the work of "the king, and the nobles / From the parliament" (001-018), establishing parliament as the forum where noble opposition to royal favourites is formalised. This is no mere advisory body—parliament has the power to compel the king's actions.

Mortimer Senior boasts of his role in "incensing" the parliament against Gaveston:

> As then I did incense the parliament, / So will I now, and thou shalt back to France (001-054)

The verb "incense" reveals parliament as a body to be inflamed and manipulated rather than a rational deliberative assembly. Political passion, not reasoned debate, drives parliamentary action.

## Constitutional Tension

The play dramatises the unresolved constitutional question of medieval England: what are the limits of royal authority, and what power does parliament hold to constrain the king?

| Position | Representative | Claim |
|----------|---------------|-------|
| Royal prerogative | Edward II | The king's will is absolute; parliament cannot bind him |
| Parliamentary authority | Mortimer Junior | Parliament speaks for the realm and can compel the king |
| Negotiated governance | Kent | Balance between royal dignity and noble counsel |

When Edward is deposed, the legal formula acknowledges parliament's role: "as the realm and parliament shall please, / So shall your brother be disposed of" (017-014). The king's fate is placed in parliament's hands, completing the institutional victory that began with Gaveston's banishment.

## Parliament in Crisis

By the final acts, parliament has become an instrument of Mortimer's personal power rather than a genuine deliberative body. Trussel announces that "the parliament must have present news" (019-009) of the king's fate, but the news it receives is predetermined by Mortimer's machinations. The institution that began as a check on royal tyranny becomes itself a tool of baronial tyranny.

## Dramatic Significance

Parliament in Edward II serves as more than historical backdrop—it is the structural antagonist to Edward's personal rule. Every major political decision in the play is filtered through or contested by parliamentary authority. The tragedy of Edward's reign is in part the tragedy of a king who cannot accept that his will must answer to an institution beyond himself.
