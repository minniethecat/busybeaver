# Sovereignty

Sovereignty in *[[edward-ii|Edward II]]* is not an abstract principle but a bitterly contested question: what are the limits of royal authority, and who may check a king who governs by personal passion rather than political reason?

## The King's Claim

Edward asserts an absolutist vision of sovereignty: "Am I a king, and must be overruled?" (001-028). His conception of kingship is personal and affective rather than institutional. He treats the crown as an extension of his will, declaring that [[piers-gaveston|Gaveston]] shall "share the kingdom with thy dearest friend" (001-003) as though the realm were private property.

> I cannot brook these haughty menaces:
> Am I a king, and must be overruled?--
> Brother, display my ensigns in the field:
> I'll bandy with the barons and the earls,
> And either die or live with Gaveston.

When challenged by the barons, Edward frames every limitation as an existential threat: "Was ever king thus overruled as I?" (004-027). He sees no distinction between counsel and usurpation, between lawful restraint and [[treason|treason]].

> Nay, then, lay violent hands upon your king:
> Here, Mortimer, sit thou in Edward's throne;
> [[warwick|Warwick]] and [[lancaster|Lancaster]], wear you my crown.
> Was ever king thus overruled as I?

Edward's language is consistently theatrical — sovereignty for him is a performance of majesty rather than a practice of rule. He confuses the symbols of kingship (the throne, the crown, the title) with its substance (consent, [[duty|obligation]], and the web of feudal loyalty).

## Baronial Counter-Claim

The [[nobility|nobility]] advances a competing theory: sovereignty is mediated through the peers of the realm. Lancaster's retort -- "Learn, then, to rule us better, and the realm" (004-028) -- asserts that kingship carries obligations, not merely prerogatives. The barons do not reject monarchy but insist it must operate within a framework of counsel and consent.

> What we have done, our heart-blood shall maintain.

[[younger-mortimer|Younger Mortimer]]'s reply to Edward's outcry is the clearest articulation of baronial constitutionalism. The nobles do not claim the right to displace the king at will; they claim the right to enforce the understood terms of kingship itself -- terms Edward has systematically violated through his favouritism, fiscal recklessness, and contempt for noble counsel.

This tension escalates to open crisis: "Is this the [[love-and-desire|love]] you bear your sovereign?" (006-014) becomes a question the barons answer by force. The Younger Mortimer frames violence as constitutional correction -- "We would but rid the realm of Gaveston" (008-025) -- claiming to act for the good of the kingdom rather than personal ambition.

> Proud Mortimer! ungentle Lancaster!
> Is this the love you bear your sovereign?
> Is this the fruit your reconcilement bears?
> Can you in words make show of amity,
> And in your shields display your rancorous minds?

[[kent|Kent]]'s anguished question captures the crisis of sovereignty in a single question. The barons' emblems -- a cedar tree consumed by a canker, a flying-fish caught between sea and sky with the motto *Undique mors est* -- reveal that their quarrel has moved beyond Gaveston to a fundamental challenge of Edward's kingship itself.

## The People's Voice

Marlowe introduces a third dimension through the commons. Younger Mortimer calculates that "when the commons and the nobles join, / 'Tis not the king can buckler Gaveston" (004-144). The play suggests that sovereignty ultimately rests not on divine right but on a broader consensus that Edward has fatally undermined.

> Then may we with some colour rise in arms;
> For, howsoever we have borne it out,
> 'Tis treason to be up against the king;
> So shall we have the people of our side,
> Which, for his father's sake, lean to the king,
> But cannot brook a night-grown mushroom,
> Such a one as my Lord of Cornwall is,
> Should bear us down of the nobility:
> And, when the commons and the nobles join,
> 'Tis not the king can buckler Gaveston;
> We'll pull him from the strongest hold he hath.

Mortimer's speech is a master-class in political calculation. He acknowledges that [[rebellion|rebellion]] is technically treason -- "howsoever we have borne it out, / 'Tis treason to be up against the king" -- yet proceeds because he can claim the people's support. The speech exposes sovereignty as a calculus of power rather than a fixed principle: the king's authority depends on a coalition of consent that Edward has shattered.

## Dramatic Arc

The sovereignty question follows a tragic arc: absolute claim yields to baronial resistance, which escalates to military conflict, and culminates in deposition. Edward's inability to distinguish between personal attachment and political obligation destroys the very authority he insists is absolute. The play does not resolve the constitutional crisis it stages -- it leaves the audience to contemplate whether sovereignty can ever be separated from the person who wears the crown.
