# Duty

**Duty** in [[edward-ii]] is a fractured concept. Characters invoke it to demand obedience, to justify rebellion, and to mourn betrayal—yet no single definition of duty commands universal assent. The collision between duty to the [[sovereignty|crown]], duty to the [[nobility|noble order]], and duty to personal bonds drives the play's central conflicts.

## Competing Duties

The play's opening political confrontation is framed as a question of duty. Kent challenges the barons:

> Is this the duty that you owe your king? (004-017)

Kent's question assumes a single, unambiguous duty of obedience to the monarch. But the play immediately complicates this assumption. The barons believe their duty to the realm—to resist a corrupting favourite—overrides their duty to the king's personal will. Edward, in turn, demands that the lords be discharged "of duty and allegiance due to thee" (004-039), attempting to dissolve the bonds that constrain him.

## Duty and Social Rank

Duty is inextricable from hierarchy. Younger Spenser's letter to Edward promises "My duty to your honour" (015-014), employing the language of feudal obligation. The word "duty" in the play almost always appears in contexts that reinforce or challenge social rank—it is never a purely personal commitment.

## The Failure of Duty

The play's tragic arc traces the progressive failure of duty at every level. Edward fails in his duty as king by neglecting the realm for [[Gaveston]]. The [[nobility]] fail in their duty as subjects by deposing and murdering their sovereign. Isabella fails in her duty as wife by conspiring against her husband. No character who invokes duty emerges with their obligations fulfilled.

Marlowe's pessimism is structural: the play presents a world in which duties are multiple, overlapping, and contradictory, making faithful performance of all obligations impossible. The character who tries to honour every duty—Kent—is the character most visibly torn apart by conflicting allegiances.
