# Elder Mortimer

The Elder Mortimer functions as the play's voice of baronial gravity — a figure of established authority whose opposition to Edward's misrule carries the weight of age and experience. He is the generational counterweight to his nephew, representing a political culture of counsel and consent that the Younger Mortimer will ultimately abandon.

## Key Speeches

> If you [[love-and-desire|love]] us, my lord, hate [[piers-gaveston|Gaveston]].
> — Act 1, Scene 1

The Elder Mortimer's first line is a political ultimatum compressed into eight words. "If you love us" makes baronial loyalty conditional on royal action — a radical inversion of the feudal relationship. The starkness of "hate Gaveston" reduces complex constitutional grievances to a single demand: choose. It is the logic of polarisation in its most distilled form, spoken not by the hot-headed younger Mortimer but by the grave elder — which makes it more ominous, not less.

> What man of noble birth can brook this sight?
> *Quam male conveniunt!* —
> See, what a scornful look the peasant casts!
> — Act 1, Scene 4

The Elder Mortimer's Latin exclamation — "How badly they go together!" — elevates baronial outrage into classical precedent. The tag suggests that Gaveston's elevation offends not merely political prudence but [[natural-order|natural order]] itself. "What man of noble birth can brook this sight?" is a question that expects no answer because none is needed: the sight of a peasant seated beside a king is self-evidently intolerable. The Elder Mortimer's conservatism is ontological — he believes in a world where birth determines place, and Gaveston's rise is an assault on the structure of reality.

> Nephew, I must to Scotland; thou stay'st here.
> Leave now to oppose thyself against the king:
> Thou seest by nature he is mild and calm;
> And, seeing his mind so dotes on Gaveston,
> Let him without controlment have his will.
> The mightiest kings have had their minions;
> Great Alexander loved Hephaestion,
> The conquering Hercules for Hylas wept,
> And for Patroclus stern Achilles drooped.
> And not kings only, but the wisest men;
> The Roman Tully loved Octavius,
> Grave Socrates wild Alcibiades.
> Then let his grace, whose youth is flexible,
> And promiseth as much as we can wish,
> Freely enjoy that vain lightheaded earl;
> For riper years will wean him from such toys.
> — Act 1, Scene 4

This is the Elder Mortimer's most significant speech, and it represents a political philosophy his nephew will reject. The catalogue of classical precedents — Alexander and Hephaestion, Hercules and Hylas, Achilles and Patroclus — frames Edward's love for Gaveston not as aberration but as tradition. The great have always had favourites. The Elder Mortimer's counsel is patience: Edward is young, and "riper years will wean him from such toys."

The speech reveals both the Elder Mortimer's wisdom and its limits. He understands Edward's psychology — the king's "youth is flexible" — but misunderstands the political dynamics of favouritism. Gaveston is not Hylas; he is a figure who concentrates [[royal-patronage|royal patronage]], distributes offices, and threatens baronial interests materially as well as symbolically. The Elder Mortimer's classical humanism cannot account for the structural threat that Gaveston represents. His nephew's reply — cataloguing Gaveston's wardrobe, his jewels, his "proud fantastic liveries" — is a materialist rebuttal: this is not [[About|about]] love but about power.

> In this your grace hath highly honoured me,
> For with my nature war doth best agree.
> — Act 1, Scene 4

When Edward appoints him general of the forces against Scotland, the Elder Mortimer's response combines gratitude with self-knowledge. "War doth best agree" with his nature — he is a soldier, not a politician. The appointment simultaneously honours and neutralises him: sent to Scotland, he is removed from the domestic political arena where his nephew will operate unchecked. The Elder Mortimer's military competence is his political limitation.

## Generational Contrast

The relationship between the two Mortimers sketches a generational shift in political culture. The Elder Mortimer opposes Gaveston within the established framework of counsel and consent. He signs the [[exile|exile]] order, argues for patience, counsels restraint, and accepts military command as an honourable alternative to political conflict. His political imagination is bounded by the conventions of aristocratic governance — oaths, councils, the forms of legitimate opposition.

The Younger Mortimer watches his uncle's approach fail. The recall of Gaveston proves the Elder's patience futile. The military command to Scotland removes the Elder from the arena precisely when his presence is most needed. The capture and [[ransom|ransom]] demand — "They rate his ransom at five thousand pound. / Who should defray the money but the king" — becomes the Younger Mortimer's casus belli. The Elder Mortimer is transformed from counsellor into hostage, his wisdom rendered irrelevant by his captivity.

The generational argument is the play's implicit political thesis: the old order of counsel, consent, and institutional restraint cannot contain the forces that Edward's misrule has unleashed. The Elder Mortimer's failure is not personal but structural — he is a figure from a world that no longer exists, and his nephew inherits the ruins.

## Capture and Ransom

The Elder Mortimer's capture by the Scots is a turning point in the play's politics. The Younger Mortimer's demand that Edward ransom his uncle — "Seeing he is taken prisoner in his wars" — transforms a military misfortune into a political crisis. Edward's reluctance to pay (he offers only "the broad seal / To gather for him throughout the realm" — permission to beg) is interpreted by the Younger Mortimer as abandonment. The Elder Mortimer, who counselled patience and accommodation, becomes the instrument through which his nephew's radicalisation is completed.

The ransom dispute also exposes a structural weakness in the baronial position: the Elder Mortimer was captured fighting Edward's war, and Edward's refusal to ransom him demonstrates that the king's obligations to his [[nobility|nobility]] are entirely discretionary. A king who will not ransom a captured baron is a king who has abandoned the most fundamental [[duty|duty]] of feudal lordship. The Elder Mortimer's captivity is the proof his nephew needs that Edward cannot be reformed — only removed.

## Dramatic Arc

The Elder Mortimer's dramatic arc is one of displacement. He enters as a peer among equals, voices baronial outrage with classical authority, counsels patience, and is dispatched to Scotland — where he is captured, ransomed (or not), and removed from the play. His disappearance after Act 1 (he is mentioned but not seen again) is itself a dramatic statement: the old order exits the stage, leaving the Younger Mortimer to reshape the world in his own image.

## Historical Figure

The historical Mortimer the elder (Roger Mortimer, 1st Baron Mortimer of Chirk, c.1256–1326) was a Marcher lord and uncle to the more famous Roger Mortimer. He fought against Edward II's forces and died in the [[tower-of-[[westminster-london|london]]|Tower of London]]. Marlowe compresses and simplifies this biography, using the Elder Mortimer primarily as a generational foil rather than a fully realised historical figure.
