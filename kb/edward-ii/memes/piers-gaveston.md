# Piers Gaveston

Piers Gaveston is the catalyst of [[edward-ii|Edward II]] — the figure whose relationship with the king sets every major conflict in motion. His rise from [[exile|exile]] to earldom, his dominance of the court through [[favoritism|favouritism]], and his fall from [[royal-patronage|royal patronage]] to execution at the hands of the barons trace the play's tragic arc in miniature. His relationship with Edward also raises fundamental questions about [[love-and-desire|love and desire]] and political power in Marlowe's world.

## Character

Gaveston is ambitious, witty, and utterly dependent on Edward's favour. His opening soliloquy reveals a man who understands power entirely in personal terms:

> "What greater bliss can hap to Gaveston / Than live and be the favourite of a king!" (001-003)

He plans to manipulate Edward through pleasure — "Music and poetry is his delight; / Therefore I'll have Italian masks by night" (001-018) — treating the court as a stage for aestheticized power.

## The Favourite's Rise

Gaveston's trajectory from exile to the earldom of Cornwall is breathtaking in its speed. Edward's father had banished him on pain of death; Edward's first act as king is to recall him and "share the kingdom with thy dearest friend" (001-003). This elevation of a man of obscure birth to the highest ranks of nobility outrages the established barons and sets the political crisis in motion. The [[gavestons-recall|recall from exile]] is the inciting incident from which all subsequent conflict flows.

## Relationship with Edward

The nature of Edward and Gaveston's relationship — passionate friendship, political dependency, or homoerotic attachment — has been debated by critics for centuries. Marlowe leaves the exact nature ambiguous while making the intensity unmistakable. Edward's grief at Gaveston's death is absolute and politically destabilizing:

> "I shall never see / My lovely Pierce of Gaveston again!" (011-003)

The relationship exemplifies the play's central theme: when private [[love-and-desire|desire]] occupies a public throne, the consequences are catastrophic.

## Death

Gaveston is captured by the barons at [[tynemouth|Tynemouth]] and executed without trial — a violation of law that mirrors Edward's violation of custom in elevating him. The [[gavestons-execution|execution of Gaveston]] is the play's first irreversible act, the point after which reconciliation between Edward and the barons becomes impossible. The barons who execute him believe they are restoring order; in fact, they have initiated the cycle of violence that will consume them all.

## Historical Figure

The historical Piers Gaveston was a Gascon knight who became the favourite of Edward II. He was made Earl of Cornwall in 1307, exiled three times, and executed by the baronial opposition at Blacklow Hill in 1312. Contemporary chroniclers often depicted him in hostile terms, emphasizing his influence over the king and his alleged arrogance toward the nobility. Marlowe's portrayal draws on this tradition while investing Gaveston with considerable theatrical vitality.
