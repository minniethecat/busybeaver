[014-001] [[france-as-setting|Paris]].

[014-002] |  | *Enter **[[queen-isabella|Queen Isabella]]** and **[[prince-edward|Prince Edward]]**.* |

[014-003] | Queen Isabella | Ah, boy, our friends do fail us all in France! The lords are cruel, and the king unkind. What shall we do? |

[014-004] | Prince Edward | Madam, return to England, And please my father well; and then a fig For all my uncle’s friendship here in France! I warrant you, I’ll win his highness quickly; ’A loves me better than a thousand Spensers. |

[014-005] | Queen Isabella | Ah, boy, thou art deceived, at least in this, To think that we can yet be tuned together! No, no, we jar too far.—Unkind Valois! Unhappy Isabel, when France rejects, Whither, O, whither dost thou bend thy steps? |

[014-006] |  | *Enter **[[sir-john-of-hainault|Sir John of Hainault]]**.* |

[014-007] | Sir John | Madam, what cheer? |

[014-008] | Queen Isabella | Ah, good Sir John of [[hainault|Hainault]], Never so cheerless nor so far distrest! |

[014-009] | Sir John | I hear, sweet lady, of the king’s unkindness: But droop not, madam; noble minds contemn Despair. Will your grace with me to Hainault, And there stay time’s advantage with your son?— How say you, my lord! will you go with your friends, And shake off all our fortunes equally? |

[014-010] | Prince Edward | So pleaseth the queen my mother, me it likes: The king of England, not [[westminster-london|the court]] of France, Shall have me from my gracious mother’s side, Till I be strong enough to break a staff; And then have at the proudest [[young-spenser|Spenser]]’s head! |

[014-011] | Sir John | Well said, my lord! |

[014-012] | Queen Isabella | O my sweet heart, how do I moan thy wrongs, Yet triumph in the hope of thee, my joy!— Ah, sweet Sir John, even to the utmost verge Of Europe, on the shore of Tanais, Will we with thee to Hainault—so we will: The marquis is a noble gentleman; His grace, I dare presume, will welcome me.— But who are these? |

[014-013] |  | *Enter **[[kent|Kent]]** and the **[[younger-mortimer|Younger Mortimer]]**.* |

[014-014] | Kent | Madam, long may you live, Much happier than your friends in England do! |

[014-015] | Queen Isabella | Lord [[earl-of-kent|Edmund]] and Lord Mortimer alive! Welcome to France! the news was here, my lord, That you were dead, or very near your [[mortality|death]]. |

[014-016] | Younger Mortimer | Lady, the last was truest of the twain: But Mortimer, reserved for better hap, Hath shaken off the thraldom of [[tower-of-london|the Tower]], And lives to advance your standard, good my lord. |

[014-017] | Prince Edward | How mean you, and the king my father lives? No, my Lord Mortimer, not I, I trow. |

[014-018] | Queen Isabella | Not, son! Why not? I would it were no worse!— But, gentle lords, friendless we are in France. |

[014-019] | Younger Mortimer | Monsieur Le Grand, a noble friend of yours, Told us, at our arrival, all the news— How hard the [[nobility|nobles]], how unkind the king Hath showed himself: but, madam, right makes room Where weapons want; and, though a many friends Are made away, as [[warwick|Warwick]], [[lancaster|Lancaster]], And others of our part and faction, Yet have we friends, assure your grace, in England, Would cast up caps, and clap their hands for joy, To see us there, appointed for our foes. |

[014-020] | Kent | Would all were well, and Edward well reclaimed, For England’s [[honour-code|honour]], peace, and quietness! |

[014-021] | Younger Mortimer | But by the sword, my lord, ’t must be deserved: The king will ne’er forsake his flatterers. |

[014-022] | Sir John | My lords of England, sith the ungentle king Of France refuseth to give aid of arms To this distressed queen, his sister, here, Go you with her to Hainault: doubt ye not We will find comfort, money, men, and friends, Ere long to bid the English king a base.— How say’st, young prince, what think you of the match? |

[014-023] | Prince Edward | I think [[edward-ii|King Edward]] will outrun us all. |

[014-024] | Queen Isabella | Nay, son, not so; and you must not discourage Your friends that are so forward in your aid. |

[014-025] | Kent | Sir John of Hainault, pardon us, I pray: These comforts that you give our woeful queen Bind us in kindness all at your command. |

[014-026] | Queen Isabella | Yea, gentle brother:—and the God of heaven Prosper your happy motion, good Sir John! |

[014-027] | Younger Mortimer | This noble gentleman, forward in arms, Was born, I see, to be our anchor-hold.— Sir John of Hainault, be it thy renown, That England’s queen and nobles in distress Have been by thee restored and comforted. |

[014-028] | Sir John | Madam, along; and you, my lords, with me, That England’s peers may Hainault’s welcome see. |

[014-029] |  | *Exeunt.* |
