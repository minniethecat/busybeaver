# The [[murder|Murder]] of Edward II

The murder of Edward II is the catastrophe toward which the entire play has been moving — the act that transforms political conflict into irrevocable crime and sets the stage for Mortimer's own destruction.

## Mortimer's [[natural-order|Order]]

Mortimer's decision to kill the king is presented as a matter of political calculation rather than [[love-and-desire|passion]]. The Latin formula he employs captures the brutal pragmatism of the act:

> "Edwardum occidere nolite timere, bonum est, Fear not to kill the king, 'tis good he die." But read it thus, and that's another sense; "Edwardum occidere nolite, timere bonum est, Kill not the king, 'tis good to fear the worst." Unpointed as it is, thus shall it go. (022-003)

The unpunctuated Latin — capable of meaning both "kill the king" and "kill not the king" — provides Mortimer with plausible deniability. Should the letter be discovered, its ambiguity allows him to claim he forbade the murder. The device is as cynical as it is ingenious: Mortimer commissions a regicide while preserving the appearance of clean hands.

> The king must die, or Mortimer goes down; The commons now begin to pity him: Yet he that is the cause of Edward's death, Is sure to pay for it when his son's of age; And therefore will I do it cunningly. (022-003)

Mortimer acknowledges the risk: whoever kills Edward will eventually be punished by [[prince-edward|Edward III]]. His solution is to delegate the murder through layers of intermediaries — Lightborn, [[matrevis|Matrevis]], [[gurney|Gurney]] — so that guilt cannot be traced back to him.

## The Murderer

Lightborn, Mortimer's hired assassin, approaches his task with professional detachment. His self-introduction is a catalogue of murder techniques delivered with chilling [[pride|pride]]:

> I learned in Naples how to [[poison|poison]] flowers; To strangle with a lawn thrust down the throat; To pierce the wind pipe with a needle's point; Or, whilst one is asleep, to take a quill, And blow a little powder in his ears; Or open his mouth, and pour quicksilver down. But yet I have a braver way than these. (022-012)

The "braver way" Lightborn alludes to — the red-hot spit — is never named directly. Marlowe's restraint here is more powerful than description: the horror is left to the audience's imagination, made worse by Lightborn's casual request for "a spit, and let it be red-hot" and "a table and a featherbed" (023-016). Professional equipment for a professional killing.

## The Victim

Edward's final moments strip [[sovereignty|kingship]] to its bare human substance. The man who once declared "Am I a king, and must be overruled?" dies in darkness, his royal identity irrelevant to the men who hold him down and the instrument that kills him. Even Lightborn, the remorseless professional, acknowledges the pathos:

> Relent! ha, ha! I use much to relent. (022-010)

The line is sardonic — Lightborn has no intention of relenting — but its very utterance acknowledges that a normal human being *would* relent at the sight of a king in such abjection. Lightborn's immunity to that response marks him as something less than human.

## The Aftermath

The murder does not end the cycle of violence. Kent's attempt to rescue his brother leads to his own execution. Mortimer's triumph is brief; Edward III's [[revenge|revenge]] is swift. The murder, intended to secure Mortimer's power, ensures his destruction. The play's final scene — [[mortimers-execution|Mortimer's execution]], Edward III's ascension — offers the possibility of closure, but the knowledge of what happened at Berkeley darkens any restoration.
