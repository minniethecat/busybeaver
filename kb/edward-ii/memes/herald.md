# Herald

The Herald in *Edward II* is an official messenger who delivers formal communications between the warring factions. Unlike ordinary messengers, the Herald operates within a specific code of political protocol: his announcements carry the weight of formal declaration, and his person is theoretically protected by the conventions of diplomatic communication. His entrance — "Enter **Herald** with his coat of arms" [010-002] — visually marks the shift to formal political theatre. His presence on stage signals moments when the conflict shifts from private conspiracy to public declaration — when political positions must be stated openly and formally rather than plotted in secret.

## Formal Communication and Political Theatre

The Herald's function is inherently theatrical. His announcements transform private political decisions into public performances, compelling each faction to state its position in terms that can be heard, recorded, and contested. [[Lancaster]] signals this shift explicitly: "Now send our heralds to defy the king; / And make the people swear to put him down" [006-103], treating the Herald not as a neutral messenger but as an instrument of political mobilisation. This transformation has political consequences: once a demand or refusal is formally declared through the Herald, it becomes harder to disown or negotiate around. The Herald thus serves as an instrument of political accountability, forcing the play's actors to commit publicly to courses of action they might prefer to keep ambiguous.

## Dramatic Function

As a stock figure of Elizabethan drama, the Herald provides exposition and advances the plot through formal announcement. His declaration — "Long live King Edward, England's lawful lord!" [010-003] — performs the ritual affirmation of sovereignty even as the political reality of that sovereignty collapses. Each heraldic appearance marks a stage in the escalation of conflict, transforming private grievances into public ultimatums. The Herald is the mechanism through which political conflict becomes political theatre — a process that lies at the heart of Marlowe's dramaturgy.
