# Flattery

Flattery — the strategic deployment of praise to manipulate the powerful — is the essential court skill in *Edward II*. Gaveston masters it; Edward is undone by it; the barons despise it yet cannot escape its logic.

## Gaveston's Art

Gaveston's opening soliloquy is a masterclass in flattery as political method. He plans entertainments — "Italian masks by night, Sweet speeches, comedies, and pleasing shows" (001-018) — not for their own sake but as instruments of control. Flattery is the mechanism by which the favourite "draw[s] the pliant king which way I please" (001-018). Aesthetic pleasure becomes political manipulation.

## The Flatterer's Cynicism

Gaveston reveals his true attitude toward those he flatters: "it is no pain to speak men fair; I'll flatter these, and make them live in hope" (001-014). Flattery costs nothing — it is the cheapest currency in the court economy — yet it buys access, favour, and ultimately power. The flatterer's cynicism is the clearest index of the court's corruption.

## Edward's Vulnerability

Edward's susceptibility to flattery is his defining weakness. He cannot distinguish between genuine loyalty and strategic praise. The play suggests that this vulnerability is inherent in monarchy itself — the isolation of power makes the powerful uniquely susceptible to those who tell them what they wish to hear.

## Flattery and Truth

The play contrasts flattery with plain speech. The barons, whatever their faults, speak directly — "My lord—you may not thus disparage us" (004-024). But plain speech proves politically impotent against flattery's seductions. The play offers the uncomfortable suggestion that in a court where power is absolute, flattery is rational and truth-telling is self-destructive.
