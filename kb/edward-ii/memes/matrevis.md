# Matrevis

**Matrevis** (historically John Maltravers) appears in the final act of [[edward-ii]] as one of the two agents—alongside [[Gurney]]—whom [[younger-mortimer|Younger Mortimer]] commissions to imprison and ultimately murder the deposed king. Though he speaks fewer than two dozen lines, Matrevis embodies the play's descent from political conflict into calculated brutality.

## Commission and Obedience

Mortimer summons Matrevis and Gurney with chilling efficiency:

> Who's there? Call hither, Gurney and Matrevis.— / To dash the heavy-headed Edmund's drift (020-021)

The verb "dash" captures the casual violence of the commission. Matrevis's response—"It shall be done, my lord" (020-027)—is the language of unquestioning obedience. He asks no questions about the morality of the task; his function is purely instrumental.

Mortimer instructs Matrevis to "write a letter presently / Unto our deputy" (020-026), making him not merely an executioner but an active participant in the administrative machinery of murder.

## The Custody of the King

Matrevis appears with soldiers to take custody of Edward, assuring him with false solicitude:

> My lord, be not pensive; we are your friends (021-003)

The gap between Matrevis's words ("friends") and his intentions (murder) exemplifies the play's late-phase moral collapse. When Edward asks for water to wash, Matrevis offers "channel-water" (021-007)—ditch water—a deliberate humiliation disguised as solicitude.

When Edward physically resists, Matrevis drops all pretence: "Why strive you thus? your labour is but lost" (021-010). The line is both a practical observation and a metaphysical pronouncement: resistance to the machinery of power is futile.

## Dramatic Function

Matrevis serves as Mortimer's willing instrument, demonstrating how tyranny operates through intermediaries. He never questions his orders, never expresses remorse, and never appears independently of his commission. His final line—"'Twixt theirs and yours shall be no enmity" (021-014)—promises a peace that will only come through death.
