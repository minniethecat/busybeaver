# Deposition

The deposition of [[edward-ii|Edward II]] -- the forced removal of an anointed king -- represents the play's constitutional and theological crisis point. No earlier action, however violent, approaches the radicalism of deposing a monarch believed to rule by divine mandate.

## The Threat Emerges

Deposition enters the play's vocabulary early. Edward himself introduces the possibility: "Curse me, depose me, do the worst you can" (004-037). His defiant invitation is rhetorical [[pride|overreach]] -- he does not believe deposition possible. But the word, once spoken, becomes thinkable. The [[nobility|barons]], who began by seeking only [[piers-gaveston|Gaveston]]'s removal, are gradually radicalised by Edward's intransigence.

> Ay, there it goes! but yet I will not yield:
> Curse me, depose me, do the worst you can.

Edward's exclamation -- "Ay, there it goes!" -- reveals that he recognises the direction of the barons' threat before he even names it. The [[archbishop-of-[[ecclesiastical-power|canterbury]]|Archbishop of Canterbury]], wielding papal authority, has linked Gaveston's [[exile|exile]] to the king's continued legitimacy, and [[younger-mortimer|Younger Mortimer]] has spelled out the logic: "Curse him, if he refuse; and then may we / Depose him, and elect another king" (004-036). Deposition has become a thinkable political instrument.

## The King's Warning

Edward recognizes the danger before his enemies do. He warns Gaveston: "thou must hence, or I shall be deposed" (004-061), acknowledging that the favourite's presence has become incompatible with the king's survival.

> 'Tis true, sweet Gaveston: O were it false!
> The legate of the Pope will have it so,
> And thou must hence, or I shall be deposed.
> But I will reign to be revenged of them;
> And therefore, sweet friend, take it patiently.

Even this recognition does not produce changed behaviour -- Edward's attachment to Gaveston outweighs his instinct for self-preservation. His promise of [[revenge|revenge]] reveals that he understands exile as a tactical retreat, not a genuine concession. The pattern is prophetic: Edward consistently treats deposition as a reversible insult rather than a structural threat to his kingship.

## Rebellion's Logical Conclusion

[[lancaster|Lancaster]] makes the connection explicit: "Look for rebellion, look to be deposed" (006-094). Deposition is not a separate crime but the natural terminus of resistance. Once the barons have taken up arms against the king's favourite, the logic of conflict drives inexorably toward challenging the king himself.

> Look for rebellion, look to be deposed:
> Thy garrisons are beaten out of [[france-as-setting|France]],
> And, lame and poor, lie groaning at the gates;
> The wild Oneil, with swarms of Irish kerns,
> Lives uncontrolled within the English pale;
> Unto the walls of York the Scots make road,
> And, unresisted, drive away rich spoils.

Lancaster frames deposition as a consequence not merely of political misrule but of imperial collapse. The catalogue of disasters -- French losses, Irish rebellion, Scottish raids -- makes [[edwards-deposition|Edward's deposition]] appear simultaneously as punishment and as national necessity. Kingship is measured by its capacity to protect the realm; a king who cannot do so forfeits his claim.

## The Deed

By Act V, the unthinkable has become routine. Mortimer awaits "news he were deposed" (020-005) with bureaucratic calm, as though awaiting a shipment. The deposition scene itself reveals the theological depth of the crisis: Edward must be forced to resign a crown he believes is conferred by God.

> I'll not resign, not whilst I live.
> Traitors, be gone, and join you with Mortimer.
> Elect, conspire, install, do what you will:
> Their blood and yours shall seal these treacheries!

Edward's resistance is magnificent and futile. He cannot conceive of kingship as a revocable office; for him it is identity itself. The extended vacillation of the deposition scene -- putting on the crown, taking it off, putting it on again -- dramatises the impossibility of the act. Kingship is not a garment to be removed; it is the self.

## The Aftermath

The younger Mortimer's casualness [[About|about]] regicide reveals how thoroughly the moral landscape has been transformed. [[kent|Kent]]'s report -- "I hear of late he hath deposed himself" (020-045) -- offers a face-saving formula that fools no one. The king has been deposed not by his own will but by force.

> First would I hear news he were deposed,
> And then let me alone to handle him.

Mortimer's chilling efficiency exposes the gap between the constitutional fiction and the political reality. Deposition was sold to [[parliament|parliament]] as a voluntary resignation; what follows is secret imprisonment, psychological torture, and [[murder|murder]]. The play does not flinch from showing that deposition is merely the anteroom to regicide.

## Constitutional Precedent

Marlowe's treatment of deposition draws on recent English history (the deposition of Richard II in 1399) and contemporary anxieties about succession. The play offers no comfortable resolution: Mortimer, who engineered the deposition, is himself executed by [[prince-edward|Edward III]]. Deposition solves nothing -- it only transfers the crisis to a new generation, leaving the young king to mourn a murdered father while holding a traitor's severed head.
