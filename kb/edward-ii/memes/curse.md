# Curse

Cursing in *[[edward-ii|Edward II]]* is a form of dramatic speech-act — words meant not merely to describe but to wound, to damn, to bring ruin upon their target. The play is saturated with curses, from the opening scenes to the final judgment, and the pattern of cursing traces an arc from political defiance to spiritual despair.

## The Bishop's Anathema

The first curse of the play carries institutional weight. When Gaveston abuses the [[bishop-of-coventry|Bishop of Coventry]], the bishop invokes the Church's power of anathema:

> For this offence be thou accursed of God! [001-013]

This is not a personal outburst but a formal ecclesiastical curse, backed by the institutional authority of the medieval Church. The bishop's curse is answered by Edward's counter-curse upon the clergy:

> Proud Rome, that hatchest such imperial grooms,
> With these thy superstitious taper-lights,
> Wherewith thy antichristian churches blaze,
> I'll fire thy crazed buildings, and enforce
> The papal towers to kiss the lowly ground! [004-058]

Edward's anti-clerical tirade is itself a form of counter-curse, substituting royal violence for ecclesiastical authority. The mutual cursing between crown and Church establishes the [[ecclesiastical-power|ecclesiastical power]] struggle that runs through the play.

## Lancaster's Curse

[[lancaster|Lancaster]] is the baron most given to the language of cursing. His first appearance in the play includes a curse directed at Gaveston:

> Ah, wicked King! accursed Gaveston!
> This ground, which is corrupted with their steps,
> Shall be their timeless sepulchre or mine. [002-025]

The curse is both condemnation and prophecy. The "accursed" Gaveston will indeed find a "timeless sepulchre" — an early grave — though Lancaster's own death will also fulfil the "or mine" alternative. Lancaster later reports how the northern borderers suffer from the king's neglect:

> The northern borderers, seeing their houses burnt,
> Their wives and children slain, run up and down,
> Cursing the name of thee and Gaveston. [008-041]

The common people's curse against the king is a powerful political sign — a monarch who has lost the love of his subjects is a monarch whose legitimacy is eroding from below.

## Isabella's Lament

[[queen-isabella|Isabella]]'s curse is directed not at a person but at her own fate. In her first soliloquy, she wishes she had never come to England:

> Would, when I left sweet France, and was embarked,
> That charming Circe, walking on the waves,
> Had changed my shape! or at the marriage-day
> The cup of Hymen had been full of poison! [005-057]

The mythological language — Circe, Hymen — elevates the curse from personal complaint to tragic lament. Isabella's curse is notable for what it does not do: she does not curse Gaveston directly but instead curses the circumstances that bind her. Her rage is displaced onto mythological figures, a sign of her political powerlessness at this stage of the play.

## Edward's Fury

Edward's curses escalate with his losses. After Gaveston's [[gavestons-execution|execution]], the king's language becomes apocalyptic:

> Treacherous Warwick! traitorous Mortimer!
> If I be England's king, in lakes of gore
> Your headless trunks, your bodies will I trail,
> That you may drink your fill, and quaff in blood...
> On your accursed traitorous progeny,
> You villains that have slain my Gaveston! [011-062]

The curse on "accursed traitorous progeny" extends the king's wrath beyond the present generation to the children of his enemies. This is the language of biblical curse — the iniquity of the fathers visited upon the sons. But the curse is also impotent; even as Edward speaks it, the barons are consolidating their military advantage.

## Mortimer's Cruel Instructions

[[younger-mortimer|Mortimer]] deploys cursing as a deliberate tactic of psychological torment against the imprisoned Edward:

> Speak curstly to him; and in any case
> Let no man comfort him, if he chance to weep,
> But amplify his grief with bitter words. [020-022]

The instruction to "speak curstly" transforms cursing from spontaneous emotion into calculated cruelty. Mortimer weaponises harsh language as part of a systematic programme of degradation. The curse is no longer a dramatic outburst but an instrument of torture.

## Edward III's Judgment

The play's final curse is also its most authoritative. [[prince-edward|Edward III]], newly crowned, condemns Mortimer:

> And thou shalt die, and on his mournful hearse
> Thy hateful and accursed head shall lie. [023-010]

The young king's curse carries the force of law. Unlike the curses traded between Edward II and his barons — which were expressions of anger and political defiance — Edward III's condemnation is a judicial sentence. The "accursed head" of Mortimer, displayed on the hearse, enacts the curse as physical fact. The cycle of cursing closes with the restoration of legitimate authority, but the play's accumulated weight of malediction — from bishop to baron to king to queen to commoner — testifies to a world in which language is the most accessible weapon.
