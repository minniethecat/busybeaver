# Edward II

Edward II is Marlowe's most fully realized tragic protagonist — a king whose private desires are fatally incompatible with public office. Unlike the overreaching conquerors of *Tamburlaine* or the diabolical scholar of *Doctor Faustus*, Edward's tragedy is fundamentally human: he loves the wrong person too much, and his kingdom cannot accommodate that love. His arc from absolute monarch to deposed and murdered prisoner is the emotional and structural spine of the play.

> "Come, Gaveston, / And share the kingdom with thy dearest friend" (001-001–001-003)

## Character

Edward is defined by the intensity of his attachments. His love for Gaveston is absolute and uncalculating, and this very absoluteness makes it politically catastrophic. He cannot moderate, cannot compromise, cannot distinguish between personal feeling and political obligation. His question — "Am I a king, and must be overruled?" (001-028) — is genuine bewilderment: he genuinely does not understand why kingship should involve constraint.

Edward's character is built on contradiction. He is simultaneously the most powerful man in England and the most emotionally vulnerable; capable of both tyrannical rage and profound tenderness; a king who speaks the language of divine right while acting with the impetuosity of a thwarted lover. These contradictions make him dramatically compelling in ways that simpler tragic protagonists are not.

## Political Failure

Edward's tragedy is not that he is evil but that he is incapable of kingship as the barons conceive it. He treats the crown as an extension of his personal will, distributing titles, wealth, and authority as expressions of affection rather than instruments of governance. The barons' objection is not merely to Gaveston personally but to what Gaveston represents: a monarchy unmoored from the consultative norms that constrain royal power.

> "I'll bandy with thee, tribe of lords, thy king / In spite of all thy threats and threatening words" (004-018–004-020)

Edward's political failure is thus structural rather than merely personal. His conception of kingship — absolutist, personal, affective — is incompatible with the barons' conception — consultative, corporate, constrained by custom and counsel. The play does not simply condemn Edward; it stages the collision of two incompatible political theories and traces the catastrophe that results.

## Relationship with Gaveston

The relationship between Edward and Gaveston is the play's emotional and political centre. Edward offers everything — kingdom, crown, the obedience of nobles — in exchange for Gaveston's presence. His language with Gaveston is the language of complete emotional surrender, utterly unlike the rhetoric of command he deploys toward the barons.

> "Fear'st thou thy person? Thou shalt have a guard. / Want'st thou gold? Go to my treasury. / Wouldst thou be loved and feared? Receive my seal" (001-026–001-030)

Gaveston's execution is not merely a political setback but an existential wound from which Edward never recovers. His grief is total and unmeasured — a king weeping openly, cursing the nobles who have taken his friend, abandoning the composure that kingship demands. In this grief, Edward becomes something more than a failed king: he becomes a figure of radical human vulnerability.

## Tragic Arc

Edward's arc follows a classical tragic trajectory: height (absolute monarch) → reversal (Gaveston's death at the barons' hands) → suffering (deposition, imprisonment at Berkeley Castle) → recognition (too late, in the darkness of his cell). His final scenes at Berkeley achieve a pathos rare in Elizabethan drama — a king stripped of every trapping of majesty, reduced to the bare fact of his humanity.

> "But what are kings, when regiment is gone, / But perfect shadows in a sunshine day?" (020-056–020-060)

The murder scene is among the most horrifying in the period's drama. Edward is not granted a noble death but a degrading one — killed in a manner that literalises the inversion of his authority. The physical brutality of his murder is the final statement of the play's political vision: when the forms of legitimacy are stripped away, what remains is mere violence.

## Others' Eyes: How Edward Is Seen

Edward is perceived in radically different ways by those around him, and these competing perspectives structure the play's political argument.

**Gaveston** sees Edward as the source of all value — the king whose favour transforms a commoner into a virtual co-sovereign. Gaveston's soliloquies frame Edward in the language of romantic devotion, treating political power as an extension of personal affection.

**Queen Isabella** sees Edward as a husband who has abandoned her. Her early complaints register personal hurt — "For now my lord the king regards me not" (001-050) — but this personal grievance gradually transforms into political alliance with Mortimer. Isabella's shift from wounded wife to political actor is one of the play's most consequential developments, and it is driven by her changing perception of Edward: from beloved husband to political obstacle.

**The barons**, particularly Lancaster and the younger Mortimer, see Edward as a structural threat — a king whose personal attachments have corrupted the machinery of governance. Their language is the language of institutional concern, but it masks personal ambition, particularly in Mortimer's case. The gap between the barons' public rationale and their private motives is one of the play's central ironies.

**The audience** is invited to see all these perspectives simultaneously. Edward is at once a failed king, a devoted lover, a neglectful husband, a tragic victim, and a political catastrophe. Marlowe refuses to resolve these contradictions, leaving Edward as a figure of irreducible complexity.

## Historical Figure

The historical Edward II (1284–1327) was the son of Edward I, the "Hammer of the Scots." His reign was marked by conflict with the baronage, military defeat at Bannockburn (1314), and eventual deposition by his wife Isabella and Roger Mortimer. He was murdered at Berkeley Castle in 1327, probably on Mortimer's orders.

Marlowe compresses and dramatises these events, making Edward's personal relationships the primary engine of historical catastrophe. Where chronicle sources distribute causation across multiple factors — fiscal mismanagement, military failure, baronial resentment — Marlowe concentrates the causal weight in Edward's love for Gaveston, transforming a complex political crisis into a streamlined tragic narrative of desire and its consequences.
