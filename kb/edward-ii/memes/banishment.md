# Banishment

Banishment in *[[edward-ii|Edward II]]* is both a political instrument and a deeply personal wound. The play opens with Gaveston's return from exile, and the conflict between king and nobles turns repeatedly on the question of who may be banished — and who has the power to recall. Banishment is never merely geographical removal; it is a public declaration of who belongs to the polity and who must be expelled from it.

## Gaveston's Exile and Return

The play begins in the shadow of banishment. Gaveston has been exiled by Edward I, and Edward II's first act as king is to repeal that exile:

> My father is deceased. Come, Gaveston,
> And share the kingdom with thy dearest friend.

Edward's recall of Gaveston is simultaneously an act of royal prerogative and a political provocation. By reversing his father's decree, Edward signals that the new reign will operate by favour rather than by counsel. The barons read the recall as the first move in a campaign to concentrate power in the hands of an unworthy favourite.

The bishops and barons respond by demanding Gaveston's banishment anew. The Archbishop of Canterbury presents it as a collective decision of the council:

> We and the rest, that are his counsellors,
> Will meet, and with a general consent
> Confirm his banishment with our hands and seals. [004-035]

The "general consent" is a fiction — Edward is coerced — but the form matters. Banishment dressed in conciliar language acquires the appearance of law rather than rebellion.

## The King's Lament

Edward's response to Gaveston's banishment is the play's most intimate expression of royal suffering:

> Rend not my heart with thy too-piercing words:
> Thou from this land, I from myself am banished. [005-060]

The line collapses the political and the personal. Gaveston is banished from England; Edward is banished from himself. The king's identity is so bound up with his favourite that separation becomes a form of existential exile. This is more than rhetorical excess — it establishes the emotional logic that will drive Edward to make ever more reckless political decisions to reclaim his companion.

Edward also resents the clerical power behind the banishment decree:

> Why should a king be subject to a priest?
> Proud Rome, that hatchest such imperial grooms... [004-058]

The king's anti-clerical fury is directed at the institutional framework that can banish a royal favourite. Banishment thus becomes entangled with questions of [[sovereignty|sovereignty]] — who has ultimate authority to determine membership in the political community?

## Isabella's Banishment

The theme extends to the queen. Isabella is herself banished from the king's presence when she fails to secure Gaveston's repeal:

> The angry king hath banished me the court. [005-108]

The queen's banishment mirrors Gaveston's — the king uses the same tool against his wife that the barons used against his favourite. The symmetry is cruel: Isabella pleads for Gaveston's return, and her reward is to share his condition. Her banishment from court also enables her political transformation. Removed from Edward's orbit, she becomes an independent political actor who will eventually ally with [[younger-mortimer|Mortimer]] against her husband.

## Banishment as Political Logic

The barons' shifting position on banishment reveals its instrumental nature. [[lancaster|Lancaster]] exposes the contradiction when Mortimer argues for Gaveston's recall:

> Can this be true, 'twas good to banish him?
> And is this true, to call him home again?
> Such reasons make white black, and dark night day. [005-128]

Lancaster's objection is logical: if banishment was right, how can repeal be right? Mortimer's counter — that the power to banish and recall will make Gaveston more pliable — reveals banishment as a mechanism of control rather than a legal judgment. The threat of renewed exile is meant to discipline the favourite, not to serve any principle.

## Kent's Exile

[[kent|Kent]] himself is banished by his brother Edward for opposing the war against the barons:

> Proud Edward, dost thou banish me thy presence?
> But I'll to France, and cheer the wronged queen,
> And certify what Edward's looseness is. [013-003]

Kent's banishment is the play's most consequential act of exile. The king expels his own brother — his closest remaining ally — for speaking unwelcome truth. Kent's journey to France sets in motion the invasion that will ultimately depose Edward. Banishment, intended as a tool of royal control, becomes the instrument of the king's destruction.
