# Lancaster

Thomas, Earl of Lancaster is the voice of established nobility in *Edward II* — proud, principled within the limits of his caste, and ultimately doomed by the very forces he sets in motion.

## The Baronial Voice

Lancaster speaks for an order that Edward's favouritism has disrupted. His opening exchange with the king establishes the terms of conflict: "Lancaster!" "My lord?" (001-020–021). The terse exchange captures the baronial expectation of reciprocal obligation — the king commands, the noble responds, and the realm is governed through this mutual recognition. Edward's elevation of Gaveston shatters this reciprocity.

## Defender of Noble Privilege

Lancaster frames his opposition in constitutional language: "Learn, then, to rule us better, and the realm" (004-028). He presents himself not as a rebel but as a counsellor — one who speaks truth to power precisely because his own position is secure. His warning to Edward — "Look for rebellion, look to be deposed" (006-094) — is both prophecy and threat.

## Limits

Lancaster's limitations are those of his class. He cannot imagine a political order different from the one he defends, and his defence of noble privilege ultimately serves his own interests as much as the realm's. When Mortimer moves from opposition to regicide, Lancaster's principles provide no guide — he is a man of the old order, helpless before the new.

## Historical Figure

The historical Thomas of Lancaster (c.1278–1322) was a grandson of Henry III and one of the wealthiest magnates in England. He led the baronial opposition to Edward II and was executed after the Battle of Boroughbridge in 1322. His death made him a popular martyr, with miracles reported at his tomb — an ironic posthumous elevation for a man who spent his life defending hierarchy against royal overreach.
