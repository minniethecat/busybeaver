# Berkeley

Lord Berkeley appears only in the final act of *[[edward-ii|Edward II]]*, but his brief role is pivotal: he is appointed custodian of the imprisoned king, and his humanity — his reluctance to be cruel — costs him the position. Berkeley serves as a foil to the ruthless gaolers who replace him, embodying the possibility of compassion in a world whose political logic demands cruelty.

## The Reluctant Gaoler

Berkeley first enters at [[kenilworth|Kenilworth Castle]], delivering the order that transfers custody of Edward from [[leicester|Leicester]] to himself:

> My lord, think not a thought so villainous
> Can harbour in a man of noble birth.
> To do your highness service and devoir,
> And save you from your foes, Berkeley would die. [019-025]

Berkeley's pledge of service is significant. Unlike the other barons, he addresses Edward not as a prisoner to be managed but as a king to be served. His language — "devoir" (duty), "highness" — preserves the forms of loyalty even as he participates in the king's confinement. The gesture is small but humanising; in a play of hardening political positions, Berkeley offers a moment of residual decency.

Edward, however, is past hope. Tearing Mortimer's order, he grants Berkeley permission to take him:

> Whither you will: all places are alike,
> And every earth is fit for burial. [019-031]

Edward's resignation transforms Berkeley's role from gaoler to funeral escort. The king no longer distinguishes between palaces and prisons, between life and death.

## Dismissed for Pity

Berkeley's compassion undoes him. [[bishop-of-winchester|The Bishop of Winchester]] reports that Berkeley is too gentle for the work of political incarceration:

> The Lord of Berkeley is so pitiful
> As Leicester that had charge of him before. [020-016]

"Pitiful" here means "full of pity" — a virtue in most contexts, but a liability in the [[younger-mortimer|Mortimer]] regime. Mortimer immediately acts:

> Berkeley shall be discharged, the king removed,
> And none but we shall know where he lieth. [020-018]

Berkeley is replaced by [[gurney|Gurney]] and [[matrevis|Matrevis]], gaolers who will not hesitate to torment their prisoner. Mortimer's detailed instructions — move Edward constantly, speak "curstly" to him, "amplify his grief with bitter words" (020-022) — are framed in explicit opposition to Berkeley's gentleness. Pity, in Mortimer's calculus, is weakness; cruelty is policy.

## The Significance of Berkeley's Failure

Berkeley's dismissal marks a threshold in the play's moral descent. Before this point, Edward's gaolers had shown some measure of decency — Leicester comforted him at Kenilworth. After Berkeley's removal, the king is placed in the hands of men who will torture and murder him. Berkeley's failure is not personal but structural: a political system that demands absolute control cannot tolerate the unpredictability of human compassion.

The irony is that Berkeley, by failing to be cruel, may have sealed Edward's [[edwards-murder|fate]]. Had Berkeley remained custodian, the king might have lived — but Mortimer could not risk it. The mechanism of power requires instruments willing to execute its ugliest commands, and Berkeley, by refusing that role, makes way for those who will.
