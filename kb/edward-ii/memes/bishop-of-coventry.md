# Bishop of Coventry

The Bishop of Coventry appears in a single but pivotal scene in Act 1, where his arrest and physical abuse at Edward's command crystallises the king's contempt for the church hierarchy. His brief role functions as an early warning of Edward's self-destructive appetite for revenge against those who oppose Gaveston.

## Dramatic Role

The Bishop enters having just celebrated the funeral rites for Edward I: "To celebrate your father's exequies" (001-051). Edward's response is not gratitude but fury — the Bishop was among those who signed the document consenting to Gaveston's exile during the previous reign. Edward orders his arrest and house to be seized as a mint for Gaveston's use (001-062). The scene establishes Edward's vindictiveness and disregard for institutional norms.

## Ecclesiastical Status and Abuse

The assault on a bishop's person and property was a grave offence in the medieval world — bishops were consecrated princes of the church, and their persons were held sacrosanct. When Edward commands "why do you not throw him in the Tower?" (001-062), he violates this sanctity. The Bishop's protest — "I did no more than I was bound to do" (001-054) — frames the action as a lawful exercise of duty rather than personal malice, making Edward's retaliation appear all the more tyrannical.

## Political Significance

By attacking the Bishop of Coventry, Edward signals that no institution — not even the church — will be permitted to obstruct his will concerning Gaveston. This act alienates the ecclesiastical establishment, foreshadowing the broader coalition of church and barons that will eventually depose him. The incident is one of the first steps in the chain of provocations that leads to civil war.

## Historical Basis

The historical Bishop of Coventry during this period was Walter Langton (d.1321), who served as treasurer under Edward I. Langton had clashed with the young Edward over financial matters, and Edward II did indeed seize his temporalities early in his reign. Marlowe compresses and dramatises this history into the single vivid scene of the Bishop's humiliation.
