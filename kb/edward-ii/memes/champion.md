# Champion

The Champion is a ceremonial figure in *Edward II*, appearing in the coronation pageantry that attends Prince Edward's accession as [[Edward III]]. He enters alongside the new king and the [[Archbishop of Canterbury]] [020-001], a configuration that unites martial, ecclesiastical, and monarchical authority in a single ritual tableau. Though a minor role in terms of lines, the Champion embodies the ritual and symbolic dimension of kingship — the ceremonies, oaths, and public performances through which royal authority is constituted and displayed. His presence at the coronation serves as a reminder that kingship is not merely a matter of political power but of symbolic legitimacy, sustained through ritual even when the person of the king has been violently displaced.

## Ceremonial Kingship

The Champion's function is purely ceremonial: he represents the martial dimension of kingship — the tradition of trial by combat that symbolically demonstrated divine favour toward the monarch. His challenge — "If any Christian, Heathen, Turk, or Jew, / Dares but affirm that Edward's not true king, / And will avouch his saying with the sword, / I am the Champion that will combat him" [020-003] — is a ritualised defence of the new king's legitimacy, its universal scope ("Christian, Heathen, Turk, or Jew") asserting that [[Edward III]]'s title is valid against all possible challengers. In the context of Edward II's deposition and his son's accession, the Champion's ritual role takes on added significance. The ceremony of the coronation, with its traditional offices and symbolic acts, works to legitimise the new reign and to assert continuity with the past even as the rupture of deposition threatens to undermine the institution of monarchy itself.

## Dramatic Function

The Champion's brief appearance serves a structural purpose: it marks the transition from the chaotic violence of Edward's reign to the promised order of his son's. [[Edward III]]'s response — "Champion, here's to thee" [020-005], accompanied by a purse — is a gesture of royal patronage that performs the new king's competence in the rituals of monarchy. By including the ceremonial figure of the Champion, Marlowe gestures toward the rituals that sustain monarchy as an institution, suggesting that while individual kings may fall, the symbolic apparatus of kingship endures — for better or worse.
