# Nobility

The **nobility** in [[edward-ii]] is more than a social rank—it is a political identity rooted in martial honour, ancestral right, and the expectation of royal respect. Edward's elevation of [[Gaveston]] above the established nobility triggers the central conflict of the play, as the [[barons]] perceive his favouritism as an assault on the natural order of the realm.

## The Nobility's Grievance

The barons' opposition to Gaveston is fundamentally a defence of noble privilege. Mortimer Junior articulates the core complaint when he warns Edward that the nobles "Should bear us down of the nobility" (004-144)—the commons and nobility together should support the king, but Edward's policies alienate both estates. The nobility see themselves as the backbone of the kingdom, and Edward's neglect of their counsel is an affront to their station.

Mortimer Junior's language is consistently that of injured aristocratic honour:

> Be counterbuffed of your nobility. / Strike off their heads, and let them preach on poles! (011-004)

The verb "counterbuffed" (rebuffed, contradicted) captures the nobility's sense of humiliation—Edward has allowed his favourites to insult and displace the natural leaders of the realm.

## Nobility and Virtue

The play presents two competing visions of nobility: one based on birth and tradition, the other on personal virtue and service. Edward urges Mortimer to:

> To cherish virtue and nobility, / And have old servitors in high esteem (011-044)

| Conception | Advocate | Basis |
|------------|----------|-------|
| Nobility of birth | Mortimer Junior | Ancestral right, martial honour, inherited status |
| Nobility of service | Edward II | Loyalty, personal merit, service to the crown |
| Nobility of virtue | Kent | Moral character, wisdom, moderation |

Edward's coupling of "virtue and nobility" as separate qualities reveals the tension—nobility of birth does not guarantee virtue, and those born outside the nobility (like Gaveston) may possess qualities the king values more highly.

## The Cost of Alienating the Nobility

Mortimer Junior's lament for England after Gaveston's death—"England, unkind to thy nobility, / Groan for this grief!" (012-032)—frames the kingdom's suffering as a consequence of its mistreatment of its natural leaders. The irony is that it is the nobility themselves, through civil war and regicide, who inflict the greatest suffering on England.

The play traces a tragic arc: the nobility begin as defenders of constitutional order against royal tyranny, but their defence of their privileges leads them to commit crimes that undermine the very order they claimed to protect. By the end, Mortimer's tyranny has replaced Edward's, and the nobility's moral authority is destroyed along with the king.

## Dramatic Function

The nobility serves as the collective antagonist to Edward's personal rule, but Marlowe refuses to make them simple villains. Their grievances are legitimate, their methods increasingly illegitimate, and their ultimate fate—Mortimer's execution by the young Edward III—restores order at the cost of revealing how thoroughly noble honour has been compromised.
