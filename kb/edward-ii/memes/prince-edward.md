# Prince Edward (Edward III)

The young Prince Edward is the play's figure of renewal — the son who restores the [[natural-order|order]] his father could not maintain. His decisive [[mortimers-execution|execution of Mortimer]] in the final scene signals the return of legitimate authority after years of chaos.

## Key Speeches

> Commit not to my youth things of more weight
> Than fits a prince so young as I to bear;
> And fear not, lord and father — heaven's great beams
> On Atlas' shoulder shall not lie more safe
> Than shall your charge committed to my trust.
> — Act 3, Scene 2

The Prince's first speech in the play is remarkable for its poise. Sent to [[france-as-setting|France]] as a diplomatic envoy, the boy-prince deploys classical allusion (Atlas bearing the heavens) with the confidence of an adult. The speech is both deferential — "fits a prince so young as I to bear" — and assertive, claiming competence beyond his years. The audience sees, in miniature, the ruler Edward II might have been.

> Madam, return to England,
> And please my father well; and then a fig
> For all my uncle's friendship here in France!
> I warrant you, I'll win his highness quickly;
> 'A loves me better than a thousand Spensers.
> — Act 4, Scene 2

In Paris with Isabella, the Prince resists his mother's political despair with a child's confidence in paternal [[love-and-desire|love]]. "'A loves me better than a thousand Spensers" is simultaneously naive and shrewd — the Prince understands that his father's affections are transferable, even if he overestimates the value of filial love against political favouritism. The line is heartbreaking in retrospect: Edward II will never see his son again.

> Mother, persuade me not to wear the [[sovereignty|crown]]:
> Let him be king; I am too young to reign.
> — Act 5, Scene 2

At the moment of his father's deposition, the Prince resists the crown. His refusal is not petulance but moral clarity — he understands that wearing the crown while his father lives makes him complicit in usurpation. The simplicity of "I am too young to reign" masks a sophisticated political judgment: legitimacy cannot be borrowed from Mortimer.

> Villain!—
> ...
> My father's murdered through thy [[treason|treachery]];
> And thou shalt die, and on his mournful hearse
> Thy hateful and accursed head shall lie,
> To witness to the world that by thy means
> His kingly body was too soon interred.
> — Act 5, Scene 6

The Prince's first word as king is "Villain!" — a monosyllable that reverses years of Mortimer's dominance. The speech that follows is formal, prosecutorial, and irrevocable. He names the crime ("my father's murdered through thy treachery"), pronounces the sentence ("thou shalt die"), and specifies the symbolic geography of justice (Mortimer's head on Edward's hearse). The boy who was too young to reign has become, in an instant, a king who judges.

## Dramatic Arc

The Prince's arc is one of emergence. He appears first as a diplomatic instrument in Act 3, dispatched to France with his mother — a child performing adult functions in a collapsing court. In Act 4, he is a refugee, moving from Paris to [[hainault|Hainault]] with Isabella, his presence lending legitimacy to the invasion force. In Act 5, he is simultaneously victim (Mortimer's prisoner-protégé) and agent (the new king whose mere existence threatens Mortimer's power). The final scene transforms him into the play's moral authority — a king who executes the murderer of his father and imprisons his own mother for complicity.

This arc of emergence is deliberately compressed. Marlowe gives the Prince only a handful of scenes, but each one advances his development. The effect is of a character who has been watching, learning, and waiting — whose patience, unlike his father's impulsiveness, is a form of power.

## The Silent Heir

Prince Edward appears only briefly, and his few lines carry disproportionate weight. His judgment on Mortimer — swift, final, and irreversible — demonstrates the very qualities his father lacked: decisiveness, clarity of purpose, the ability to distinguish personal feeling from political action. Where Edward II governed through favour, Edward III governs through justice.

## Restoration

The play's final movement is one of restoration. Edward III's execution of Mortimer re-establishes the moral and political order that his father's reign had shattered. The son succeeds where the father failed, suggesting that the institution of monarchy can survive the individual monarch's inadequacy — but only if the successor possesses virtues the predecessor lacked.

Yet the restoration is shadowed by irony. The Prince's justice is retrospective — it can punish the [[murder|murder]] of his father but cannot undo it. His imprisonment of Isabella signals that the new order is founded on the same familial ruptures that destroyed the old one. The crown passes from father to son, but it passes through blood.

## Dramatic Function

Prince Edward functions as the play's deus ex machina — the unexpected agent who resolves an apparently irresolvable crisis. His sudden assertiveness in the final scene provides closure while leaving open questions. Is the restoration genuine, or merely a pause before the next cycle of ambition and violence? The historical answer — Edward III's fifty-year reign of conquest and consolidation — suggests the former, but Marlowe's play ends before that answer is given.

## Historical Figure

The historical Edward III (1312–1377) was one of England's most successful medieval kings. His fifty-year reign saw the beginning of the Hundred Years' War, the victories of Crecy and Poitiers, and the establishment of the Order of the Garter. His early assumption of power — executing Mortimer at age seventeen — established a pattern of decisive action that would characterize his reign.
