# Favoritism

Favoritism — the king's disproportionate advancement of a personal favourite — is the central political fault line of *Edward II*. Edward's devotion to Gaveston alienates the nobility, triggers civil war, and ultimately leads to his own deposition.

## The King's Favourite

Gaveston defines himself as "the favourite of a king" (001-003), framing royal favour as the ultimate form of personal advancement. Upon his father's death, Edward immediately recalls Gaveston from exile, promising to "share the kingdom with thy dearest friend" (001-003). This elevation of a man "that hardly art a gentleman by birth" (004-022) enrages the established nobility.

Edward's favouritism is not merely political but deeply emotional. Gaveston's influence allows him to "draw the pliant king which way I please" (001-018) through the manipulation of Edward's tastes for music, poetry, and spectacle. The relationship is described by contemporaries as the king being "lovesick for his minion" (004-049).

## Baronial Opposition

The barons frame their opposition to Edward explicitly in terms of his favouritism. Lancaster, Warwick, and Mortimer object not to the king's authority in principle but to its misdirection toward an unworthy favourite. Edward's declaration — "Were he a peasant, being my minion, I'll make the proudest of you stoop to him" (004-023) — confirms their worst fears: that personal affection overrides all considerations of rank and merit.

## Historical Precedent

Mortimer the Elder attempts to normalize the criticism by appealing to classical precedent: "Mightiest kings have had their minions; Great Alexander loved Hephaestion" (004-187). But this rhetorical move backfires — it implicitly compares Gaveston to a figure whose excessive favour from Alexander was itself a source of political instability, underscoring rather than defusing the threat.

## Dramatic Function

Favoritism in *Edward II* functions as more than a character flaw — it is the structural engine of the plot. Every major conflict flows from Edward's inability to moderate his attachment to Gaveston. The barons' revolt, Gaveston's execution, the French alliance, and ultimately Edward's deposition all trace back to this single source of political imbalance.
