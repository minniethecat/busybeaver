# Gaveston's Exile

Gaveston's exile is the spark that ignites the play's entire political conflagration. Before the play begins, Edward I had banished Gaveston to France. On the old king's death, Edward II immediately recalls his favourite — and the barons immediately demand his removal again. The exile, imposed by the assembled lords and bishops, is the first open clash between Edward's personal attachments and his political obligations.

## The Exile Document

The mechanism of exile is bureaucratic and collective. The Archbishop of Canterbury, the Bishop of Coventry, and the secular lords — Warwick, Lancaster, Mortimer — each sign the document demanding Gaveston's removal. The Archbishop's "Give me the paper" (004-004) transforms baronial grievance into formal constitutional action. Edward is outraged not only by the exile itself but by the institutional machinery behind it: the church and nobility acting in concert to constrain the crown.

## Edward's Response

Edward's fury at the exile drives the first act's accelerating violence. He assaults the Bishop of Coventry — "Throw him in the Tower!" (001-062) — and schemes to recall Gaveston despite his sworn word. The exile does not resolve the conflict; it intensifies it. By giving Edward a concrete grievance, the barons transform a diffuse tension over royal favouritism into a focused political crisis. Every subsequent event — Gaveston's return, the civil war, the deposition — traces back to this originating act of institutional coercion.

## Historical Event

The historical exile of Piers Gaveston occurred in 1308, shortly after Edward II's coronation. The barons, led by Thomas of Lancaster, demanded Gaveston's removal on grounds of his excessive influence over the king and his control of royal patronage. The exile was initially to Ireland (where Gaveston served as the king's lieutenant), not France as in Marlowe's play. The historical exile and recall were both repeated in 1311 under the Ordinances, reflecting the cyclical nature of the conflict that Marlowe compresses into a single dramatic arc.
