# Poison

**Poison** in [[edward-ii]] functions as both a literal instrument of death and a master metaphor for the corruption that spreads through the body politic. From [[Queen Isabella]]'s wish that her wedding cup had been poisoned to Lightborn's expert knowledge of toxic flowers, the language of poison traces the play's movement from personal grievance to political assassination.

## Poison as Metaphor

Isabella's first extended speech establishes poison as the play's governing metaphor for [[marriage]] gone wrong:

> or at the marriage-day / The cup of Hymen had been full of poison! (004-092)

The image is strikingly intimate—poison at the wedding cup, the very symbol of union and celebration. Isabella imagines her marriage not as a bond broken but as a bond that was toxic from its inception. The metaphor suggests that the political order itself has been poisoned at its source.

Edward extends the metaphor of shared poison to his relationship with [[Gaveston]]. Mortimer Junior reports that the king and his favourite "had both caroused / A bowl of poison to each other's health" (006-126), transforming their bond into a mutual intoxication that is simultaneously a mutual destruction.

## The Technology of Poison

In the play's final act, the metaphorical becomes literal. Lightborn, Edward's assassin, boasts of his expertise:

> I learned in Naples how to poison flowers; / To strangle with a lawn (022-012)

| Aspect | Example | PN |
|--------|---------|-----|
| Metaphorical poison | Isabella's wedding cup | 004-092 |
| Shared intoxication | Edward and Gaveston's bond | 006-126 |
| Technical expertise | Lightborn's Neapolitan training | 022-012 |
| Psychological poison | "enough to poison any man" | 023-003 |

The shift from metaphor to method is chilling: what began as a rhetorical figure in Isabella's lament becomes the literal means of Edward's destruction. The play refuses to keep poison safely contained in the realm of language—it insists on its material reality.

## Poison and the Body Politic

The final reference to poison explicitly links the physical and the political. Lightborn reflects that Edward's grief "were enough to poison any man, / Much more a king" (023-003). The king's body is not merely a biological entity but the embodiment of the realm; to poison the king is to poison the kingdom. The regicide is thus both a physical act and a constitutional catastrophe—a poison that will work its way through the body politic long after Edward's death.
