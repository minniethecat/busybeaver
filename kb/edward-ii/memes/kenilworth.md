# Kenilworth Castle

Kenilworth (called "Killingworth" in the play) is the baronial stronghold par excellence — a fortress that represents the military power of the nobility and serves as the stage for Edward's accelerating downfall.

## The Baronial Fortress

Kenilworth's function in the play is primarily military. It is where the barons gather their forces and where Edward's diminishing authority is most starkly contrasted with the barons' armed strength. The castle's massive fortifications — among the largest in medieval England — embody the independent power of the nobility that Edward has failed to accommodate.

## Site of Confrontation

The scenes at Kenilworth mark the transition from political conflict to military action. What began as counsel and remonstrance in Westminster has become armed confrontation at a baronial castle. The journey from London to Kenilworth traces Edward's decline from sovereign to fugitive.

## Historical Counterpart

The historical Kenilworth Castle in Warwickshire was one of the greatest fortresses in medieval England. It was held by Thomas of Lancaster and later passed to the crown. Edward II was briefly held there after his capture in 1326 before being moved to Berkeley Castle.
