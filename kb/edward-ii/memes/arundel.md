# Earl of Arundel

Arundel is one of the few nobles who remain loyal to Edward II throughout the crisis — a choice that costs him his life and demonstrates that in the world of the play, virtue offers no protection.

## Unwavering Loyalty

Where other royalist supporters — even Kent — eventually break with Edward, Arundel remains steadfast. His loyalty is not conditional on Edward's competence or virtue; it is owed to the office, not the man. This distinction makes him the play's most principled figure, and also its most tragic.

## The Cost of Principle

Arundel's execution is one of the play's quieter atrocities — less spectacular than Edward's murder but equally damning of Mortimer's regime. His death demonstrates that the new order is no more just than the old; it simply punishes different loyalties.

## Historical Figure

The historical Edmund FitzAlan (1285–1326) was a prominent supporter of Edward II who served as justice of Wales and was executed after the Marcher rebellion against the king failed. His son Richard would later have his father's attainder reversed and the earldom restored.
