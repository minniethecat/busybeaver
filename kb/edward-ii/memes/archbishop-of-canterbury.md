# Archbishop of Canterbury

The Archbishop of Canterbury appears only in the first act but carries immense symbolic weight. His willingness to sign Gaveston's [[exile|exile]] [[natural-order|order]] transforms baronial faction politics into constitutionally sanctioned action, and his reappearance at [[prince-edward|Edward III]]'s coronation in Act 5 closes the arc of ecclesiastical legitimacy.

## Key Speeches

> First, were his sacred garments rent and torn;
> Then laid they violent hands upon him; next,
> Himself imprisoned, and his goods asseized:
> This certify the Pope: away, take horse.
> — Act 1, Scene 2

The Archbishop's first speech catalogues the abuses against the [[bishop-of-coventry|Bishop of Coventry]] with forensic precision. The anaphoric "First... Then... next" constructs a legal narrative of escalating violence. His immediate response — "This certify the Pope" — escalates the conflict from a local dispute to a matter of papal concern. The Archbishop transforms the assault on Coventry into an assault on the church universal, and his dispatch of the messenger is the first institutional response to Edward's tyranny.

> What need I? God himself is up in arms
> When violence is offered to the church.
> — Act 1, Scene 2

When Lancaster asks if the Archbishop will take arms against the king, this reply is a masterpiece of ecclesiastical politics. "God himself is up in arms" means the Archbishop need not raise secular forces — divine authority is sufficient. But in context, the line also legitimises the barons' resort to arms. If God is "up in arms" against Edward, then the barons who arm themselves are acting as divine instruments. The Archbishop does not call for violence, but he provides its theological justification.

> My lords, to ease all this, but hear me speak:
> We and the rest, that are his counsellors,
> Will meet, and with a general consent
> Confirm his banishment with our hands and seals.
> — Act 1, Scene 2

The Archbishop proposes a constitutional solution to a political crisis. "General consent" and "hands and seals" are the mechanisms of legitimate governance — procedures that distinguish lawful exile from arbitrary expulsion. The speech positions the Archbishop as a mediator between Isabella's distress and Mortimer's aggression, offering institutional process as an alternative to civil war.

> Give me the paper.
> — Act 1, Scene 4

The Archbishop's subscription to the exile order is the act for which he is most remembered. Four words, no rhetoric — the brevity is itself a statement. The Archbishop does not explain, justify, or moralise. He simply signs, and his signature, placed first on the document, transforms the barons' demand into a constitutional act. The church has withdrawn its consent from Edward's governance of Gaveston.

> Long live King Edward, by the grace of God
> King of England and Lord of Ireland!
> — Act 5, Scene 4

The Archbishop returns at the coronation of Edward III, speaking the formal words of acclamation. The reappearance is structurally significant: the church that withdrew its support from Edward II now blesses Edward III. The Archbishop's presence at the coronation provides ecclesiastical continuity across the rupture of [[deposition|deposition]] — the same office that legitimised resistance to the father now legitimises the son's reign.

## Institutional Authority

The Archbishop's authority is institutional rather than personal. He speaks for an office that predates the current king and will outlast him. When he threatens to "discharge these lords / Of [[duty|duty]] and allegiance due to thee" as papal legate, he is deploying the most powerful constitutional weapon available — the authority to dissolve the bonds of fealty that constitute political order. Edward's submission to the exile demand is a submission not to the barons but to the church, which is why his subsequent [[wrath|rage]] is directed at "Proud Rome" rather than at Lancaster or Mortimer.

The Archbishop's institutional authority also limits him. He can force [[gavestons-exile|Gaveston's exile]] but cannot prevent [[gavestons-recall|Gaveston's recall]]. He can threaten deposition but cannot execute it. His power is conditional — dependent on the barons' willingness to enforce ecclesiastical judgments with secular arms. When the baronial cause shifts from constitutional resistance (Lancaster) to personal ambition (Mortimer), the Archbishop's institutional framework becomes irrelevant. He disappears from the play after Act 1, returning only at the coronation — a bookend to an ecclesiastical authority that shaped the opening crisis but could not control its outcome.

## Church and State

The Archbishop embodies the interdependence of spiritual and temporal authority in medieval political theory. His participation in Gaveston's exile reflects the constitutional reality that the king governs not alone but in concert with church and [[nobility|nobility]]. Edward's alienation of the Archbishop — through the abuse of the Bishop of Coventry — represents the loss of an entire pillar of legitimate rule.

Edward's response to the Archbishop's authority is revealing. He rages against "Proud Rome" and threatens to "fire thy crazed buildings," but he submits. The king who defies his barons obeys his archbishop. The distinction is crucial: Edward recognises ecclesiastical authority as legitimate in a way he does not recognise baronial authority. He can call Mortimer a [[treason|traitor]] but cannot call the papal legate one. The Archbishop's power is, in this sense, the limit-case of Edward's political imagination — the one form of authority he cannot reject without rejecting the foundations of his own kingship.

## Historical Figure

The historical Robert Winchelsey (c.1245–1313) was a theologian and churchman who served as Archbishop of Canterbury from 1294 to 1313. He had a contentious relationship with both Edward I and Edward II, often siding with the baronial opposition on matters of taxation and royal authority. His resistance to Edward I's demands for clerical taxation established precedents that shaped the constitutional crises of Edward II's reign.
