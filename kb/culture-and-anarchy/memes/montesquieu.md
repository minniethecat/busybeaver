# Montesquieu

Charles-Louis de Secondat, Baron de Montesquieu (1689–1755), was a French
political philosopher, jurist, and man of letters whose major works — *The
Spirit of the Laws* (1748) and the *Persian Letters* (1721) — established
him as one of the foundational thinkers of the European Enlightenment. In
*Culture and Anarchy*, [[matthew-arnold]] does not engage Montesquieu's
political theory directly; instead, he enlists the French philosopher's
words as the intellectual keynote of [[Chapter-I]], opening the book with a
quotation that defines the motive for [[culture]] itself: the disinterested
pursuit of knowing for its own sake — the [[hellenism|Hellenic]] passion for
seeing things as they really are — conceived not as idle curiosity but as
the enlargement of human nature.

## Identity

Montesquieu was born into the provincial nobility at the Chateau de la Brede
near Bordeaux. He studied law, inherited a judicial office in the Parlement
of Bordeaux, and rose to prominence as a man of letters with the publication
of the *Persian Letters*, a satirical portrait of French society seen
through the eyes of two Persian travellers. His masterpiece, *The Spirit of
the Laws* (1748), developed the theory of the separation of powers —
legislative, executive, and judicial — that would profoundly influence the
framers of the American constitution and the course of modern political
thought. He was elected to the Academie Francaise in 1728 and spent
extensive periods travelling in England, whose constitutional arrangements
he admired. For Arnold, however, Montesquieu matters not as a constitutional
theorist but as a witness to the true ground of the intellectual life.

## Role in Arnold's Argument

Arnold deploys Montesquieu at two structurally decisive moments in Chapter I,
"Sweetness and Light":

**Opening the chapter.** Arnold begins not with his own voice but with
Montesquieu's. The first substantive passage of the book's argument proper
— after the Preface — is a block quotation in which Montesquieu declares
that the first motive for study is the desire to "augment the excellence of
our nature." Arnold uses this to rescue the word "curiosity" from its
English pejorative sense and to establish that there is a genuine scientific
passion — "natural and proper in an intelligent being" — that is the soil
in which culture grows [001-001].

**Closing the framework.** At the end of the same paragraph-arc, Arnold
returns to Montesquieu and places his words alongside those of [[bishop-wilson]]
to form the twin mottos of culture. Where Montesquieu supplies the motto for
culture viewed as the scientific passion for pure knowledge ("To render an
intelligent being yet more intelligent!"), Bishop Wilson supplies the motto
for culture viewed as the moral and social passion for doing good ("To make
reason and the will of God prevail") [001-002].

This paired structure is the organizing principle of Arnold's entire concept
of [[culture]] — the conviction that neither the intellectual nor the moral
impulse alone is sufficient, and that [[sweetness-and-light]] requires both:
knowing and doing, seeing and acting, the Hellenic love of truth and the
Hebraic love of righteousness.

## Key Passages

### The Opening Quotation

At the very outset of Chapter I, Arnold quotes Montesquieu to distinguish
between frivolous and genuine intellectual curiosity:

> Montesquieu says:—"The first motive which ought to impel us to study is
> the desire to augment the excellence of our nature, and to render an
> intelligent being yet more intelligent." This is the true ground to assign
> for the genuine scientific passion, however manifested, and for culture,
> viewed simply as a fruit of this passion; and it is a worthy ground, even
> though we let the term curiosity stand to describe it.
> [001-001]

### The Motto of Culture's First Aspect

Arnold returns to the quotation to frame the dual nature of culture:

> As, in the first view of it, we took for its worthy motto Montesquieu's
> words: "To render an intelligent being yet more intelligent!" so, in the
> second view of it, there is no better motto which it can have than these
> words of Bishop Wilson: "To make reason and the will of God prevail!"
> [001-002]

## Relationship to Arnold's Framework

Montesquieu occupies a distinctive position in Arnold's intellectual
economy. Unlike [[thomas-carlyle]] or [[goethe]], whom Arnold cites as
authoritative modern voices who model the life of culture, Montesquieu
appears as a more remote but foundational source — the Enlightenment
spokesman for a principle Arnold regards as timeless. His words authenticate
the "scientific passion" side of culture: the sheer desire to see things as
they really are, regardless of where the seeing leads.

This is significant because Arnold's English audience was predisposed to
suspect that "culture" meant either dilettantism or subversion. By anchoring
his argument in an unimpeachable figure of the French Enlightenment — a
thinker universally respected among the educated classes of Europe — Arnold
establishes that the intellectual ambition of culture is not a modern
novelty or a German eccentricity but a principle rooted in the mainstream of
European thought.

Structurally, Montesquieu and [[bishop-wilson]] form a deliberate French-English
pair. Montesquieu (the French Catholic philosopher) supplies the [[hellenism]]
half — the passion for knowing — while Bishop Wilson (the English Anglican
bishop) supplies the [[hebraism]] half — the passion for doing good. That
Arnold opens with the Frenchman and then immediately incorporates the
Englishman signals his central argument: that the two traditions must be
combined, and that England in particular needs the Continental intellectual
strain to complete its moral seriousness [001-002].
