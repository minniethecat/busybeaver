# Joseph Smith

Joseph Smith (1805–1844) was the founder of Mormonism and the Latter Day Saint movement. In *Culture and Anarchy*, Matthew Arnold references Smith through the lens of Hepworth Dixon's contemporary account of the Mormons, using him as a case study in the Victorian English habit of treating religious enthusiasm with either dismissive ridicule or uncritical sympathy.

## Identity

Smith was born in Sharon, Vermont, and grew up in upstate New York during the Second Great Awakening — a period of intense religious ferment that produced numerous new religious movements. In 1830, he published the Book of Mormon, which he claimed to have translated from golden plates revealed by an angel. He led his followers westward through Ohio, Missouri, and Illinois, founding the city of Nauvoo before being killed by a mob in Carthage, Illinois, in 1844.

By the 1860s, when Arnold was writing, Mormonism was a subject of intense fascination in England. Hepworth Dixon's *New America* (1867) provided English readers with a widely-read account of Mormon beliefs and practices, including polygamy. It is through Dixon's book that Arnold encounters Smith.

## In Culture and Anarchy

Arnold discusses Joseph Smith in Chapter III ("[[barbarians|Barbarians]], [[philistines|Philistines]], [[populace|Populace]]") in the context of a sustained critique of the English failure to exercise discriminating judgment in matters of religion. He quotes Dixon's work on the Mormons:

> "It is easy to say," he writes of the Mormons, "that these saints are dupes and fanatics, to laugh at Joe Smith and his church, but what then?" [003-013]

The rhetorical question — "but what then?" — captures what Arnold regards as the essential poverty of the English approach to religious movements. To dismiss Smith as a fraud is insufficiently serious; but to treat him with unearned respect is, in Arnold's view, equally inadequate. What is missing is a third option: the exercise of critical intelligence — the application of "right reason" to distinguish genuine spiritual insight from religious enthusiasm.

## Arnold's Critique

Arnold places Smith alongside other figures of popular religious enthusiasm — [[william-murphy|William Murphy]], the Mormon prophets, and various Nonconformist preachers — as examples of what [[hebraism|Hebraism]] unchecked by [[hellenism|Hellenism]] produces. His point is not to mock Smith personally (the easy route he criticises others for taking) but to criticise the intellectual culture that treats such figures as authorities without applying discriminating standards of reason.

The deeper argument is this: English society, having rejected the intellectual discipline that [[culture]] provides, is unable to distinguish between the authentic and the spurious in religion. In the absence of a properly functioning intellectual authority — what Arnold calls the "[[best-self]]" exercising "right reason" — any person claiming religious inspiration is treated as equally valid. This is the "[[doing-as-one-likes]]" of the religious sphere.

Arnold's specific warning is striking:

> "we shall never get rid of our natural taste for the bathos in religion — never get access to a best self and right reason which may stand as a serious authority — by treating Mr. Murphy as his own disciples treat him, seriously, and as if he was as much an authority as any one else: so we shall never get rid of it while our able and popular writers treat their Joe Smiths and Deborah Butlers..." [003-013]

"Bathos in religion" is a revealing phrase. Arnold is not accusing Smith of bathos; he is accusing the English reading public of a taste for it — a predisposition to find spiritual profundity in the extravagant and the crude.

## Arnold and American Religion

Smith's appearance in *Culture and Anarchy* is part of Arnold's broader treatment of American religion, which he regards as English Puritanism carried to its logical extreme. In the absence of an [[establishment|Established Church]] — of any connection between religion and the main current of national life — American religion fragments into a chaos of competing sects, each claiming divine authority. Mormonism, as the most successful of these new movements, represents for Arnold the *reductio ad absurdum* of the Nonconformist principle.

This is not a sympathetic reading of Mormonism, and Arnold's condescension toward Smith is palpable. But it is a reading that takes Smith seriously as a cultural phenomenon — as evidence about the spiritual condition of the Anglo-American world — rather than dismissing him as a mere fraud.

## Relationship to Arnold's Framework

Smith serves Arnold as an exhibit in the case against uncritical religious liberty. The argument is characteristically Arnoldian in its double movement: Smith represents something genuinely problematic (religious enthusiasm without intellectual discipline), and the English response to Smith represents something equally problematic (the inability to distinguish the genuine from the spurious). The solution, as always, is [[culture]] — the cultivation of that "best self" that can exercise discriminating judgment.
