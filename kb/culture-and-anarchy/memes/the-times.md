# The Times

The leading British newspaper of the Victorian era and Arnold's primary exhibit of middle-class [[philistinism|Philistinism]] in action. Throughout *Culture and Anarchy*, Arnold cites *The Times* as the authentic voice of the English middle class — confident, doctrinaire, satisfied with itself, and utterly indifferent to the "free play of the mind upon all subjects" that genuine culture demands. In Arnold's rhetorical strategy, "says *The Times*" functions as shorthand for "the English middle class thinks" (P03-013).

## Identity

Founded in 1785 as *The Daily Universal Register* and renamed *The Times* in 1788, the newspaper had by the 1860s become the most influential publication in Britain. Under the long editorship of John Thadeus Delane (1841–1877), *The Times* established itself as the voice of respectable middle-class opinion — liberal in politics, Protestant in religion, and deeply invested in the narrative of British progress and superiority. It commanded a circulation of around 60,000, far larger than any rival, and its leading articles were reproduced and discussed across the country.

## Arnold's Critique

Arnold's engagement with *The Times* is extensive and pointed. He quotes the newspaper across multiple chapters, always to illustrate the same point: that the English middle class, however well-intentioned, lacks the intellectual breadth and flexibility that genuine culture requires.

### On Religious Establishments

Arnold quotes *The Times* on the question of the Irish Church — the great political-religious controversy of the day — to expose what he sees as the newspaper's shallow liberalism:

> "In the colonies," says The Times, "we see religious communities unfettered by state endowments and thriving wonderfully." (P03-013)

For Arnold, this is typical *Times* reasoning: reduce a complex question of national identity and spiritual life to a mechanical principle ("no establishment = more religion") and call the matter settled. The newspaper's confident empiricism masks a failure to think deeply about what religion actually requires.

### On America

*The Times*'s coverage of [[america|America]] provides another target. The newspaper holds up America as proof that religious establishments are unnecessary:

> The Times triumphantly shows us America without religious establishments, and asks why we cannot do the same. (P03-020)

Arnold's response is that the American example cuts both ways. America may have thriving religion without an establishment, but it also exhibits — in Arnold's view — a coarsening of public life, a worship of the "average man," and a lack of the "high best self" that genuine culture seeks. *The Times* sees only what confirms its prejudices.

### On Social Necessity

In perhaps his most sustained critique, Arnold attacks *The Times* for its doctrine of "social necessitarianism" — the fatalistic acceptance that things must be as they are:

> The melancholy doctrine is preached in The Times with great clearness and force: "The alternative is between anarchy and a despotism which Englishmen will not endure." (003-017)

This either/or thinking, Arnold argues, is precisely what prevents England from developing a genuine idea of the State. *The Times* frames every question as a choice between existing alternatives, never asking whether those alternatives might themselves be inadequate.

### On Culture

*The Times*'s attitude toward culture itself draws Arnold's sharpest rebuke:

> "Art is long," says The Times, "and life is short; for the present, at any rate, we must content ourselves with the best we have." (006-020)

Arnold replies: "So it is of no use to say to The Times, and to our Liberal friends repeating after The Times, that the best we have is not good enough" (006-022). The newspaper's complacent pragmatism — "we must content ourselves with the best we have" — is the very antithesis of the cultural ideal, which insists on pursuing perfection without settling for what is merely available.

## Arnold's Rhetorical Strategy

| *Times* Position | Arnold's Rebuttal |
|-----------------|-------------------|
| "Religious communities thrive without endowments" | You have not understood what religion *is* — you reduce it to a mechanical outcome |
| "America proves disestablishment works" | America also proves the coarsening effect of losing all centres of authority |
| "The choice is anarchy or despotism" | Those are not the only alternatives — there is the ideal of the State as right reason |
| "We must content ourselves with the best we have" | The best we have is not good enough; culture pursues perfection, not adequacy |

## The Function of The Times in Arnold's Argument

*The Times* serves Arnold as a concrete, recognisable embodiment of the Philistine mentality he seeks to diagnose and cure. Rather than attacking abstractions, Arnold lets *The Times* condemn itself in its own words — always confident, always certain, always mistaking its own limitations for the boundaries of the possible.

The newspaper's great sin, in Arnold's eyes, is not that it is wrong about particular questions. It is that it refuses to acknowledge that there *are* questions it has not considered. *The Times* exemplifies what Arnold calls "the want of flexibility which is the great defect of the English middle class" — the inability to see that one's own certainties might be parochial rather than universal.
