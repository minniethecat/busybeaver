# Dissenters

The Dissenters are the English Nonconformists — those Protestants who, since the
Act of Uniformity of 1662, had worshipped outside the established Church of
England. Arnold's treatment of the Dissenters is characteristically double: he
respects their earnestness while deploring their narrowness, and he insists that
their political project — the disestablishment of the Church — is a distraction
from the deeper work of culture.

## Arnold's Definition

Arnold defines the Dissenters not by their theology (which he rarely engages)
but by their temper. The Dissenter is "earnest, moral, and narrow" — the
adjectives always travel together in Arnold's prose. His Hebraising preoccupation
with conduct makes him "invaluable" to English national life, but his
indifference to "sweetness and light" makes him, in Arnold's view, an
insufficient guide to what a nation ought to become [P03-016, 002-009].

## Usage Across the Text

### The Political Dissenters

In Chapter II, Arnold identifies "the political dissenters in the middle-class"
as a key constituency of Victorian liberalism. Their demand for "religious
equality" through disestablishment is, in Arnold's analysis, an instance of the
confusion of machinery with the end. The Dissenter "conceives of no higher
object for his ambition than to get rid of the Establishment" — and in so doing
mistakes an institutional arrangement for a spiritual achievement [002-009].

### The Hebraising Middle Class

In the Preface, Arnold locates the Dissenters within the larger framework of
"the Puritan and Hebraising middle-class." The Dissenters, he argues, are the
purest expression of the Hebraic temper in English life — strict in conduct,
suspicious of beauty, and convinced that "the main thing" is obedience to divine
law. Arnold does not wish to extinguish this temper but to balance it with the
Hellenic "free play of consciousness" [P03-016].

## Historical Context

The Dissenters had been a force in English life since the seventeenth century,
but their political significance reached a peak in the mid-Victorian period.
The Liberation Society, founded in 1844, campaigned for the disestablishment of
the Church of England — a cause Arnold regarded as a classic instance of
mistaking machinery for the end [P03-016, 002-009].
