The *Daily News* was a London daily newspaper founded in 1846 by Charles Dickens, who served as its first editor. By the 1860s, it had established itself as a leading Liberal paper closely associated with Nonconformist and reformist opinion. Arnold engages with the *Daily News* in Chapter III of *Culture and Anarchy* as part of his broader critique of the press's role in shaping Philistine public opinion.

## Nature

The *Daily News* was a morning newspaper aligned with the Liberal Party and sympathetic to reform causes including free trade, religious liberty, and the extension of the suffrage. It positioned itself as a serious alternative to *The Times* and a more intellectual counterpart to the popular *Daily Telegraph*. Its editorial line reflected the Nonconformist conscience that Arnold saw as so influential in Victorian public life.

## Role in Arnold's Argument

Arnold discusses the *Daily News* primarily in Chapter III, where he examines the reactions of the press to his cultural criticism. The *Daily News* had taken up the *Daily Telegraph*'s cause against Arnold, and he treats its response as characteristic of the Liberal press's inability to grasp what he is trying to say. The *Daily News*, Arnold observes, "eloquently says" that the Nonconformists have the "will, and the power" for reform (PN 003-014), but this very confidence is, for Arnold, the problem: it substitutes moral energy for intellectual clarity.

Arnold notes with some amusement that the *Daily News* accuses him of wanting to "do away with the Hyde Park railings" and other misunderstood positions (PN 003-020). The paper's misreading of his argument becomes, in Arnold's hands, evidence of the poverty of public discourse — a discourse conducted through slogans and stock responses rather than through genuine intellectual engagement. The *Daily News* is Arnold's example of how even the more serious Liberal press cannot escape the mechanical habits of mind that characterize Philistine culture.

## Key Passages

> "The *Daily News* eloquently says, [the Nonconformists have] the will, and the power, requisite for carrying through a great and noble work" (PN 003-014)

> "The *Daily News* has, at first, something very sensible to say about culture" — but quickly falls back into misunderstanding (PN 003-018)

> "The *Daily News* says, I observe, that all my views about culture are quite unsound" — Arnold's wry summary of the paper's critique (PN 003-020)

## Historical Context

The *Daily News* was founded by Charles Dickens in January 1846 as a radical alternative to the *Morning Chronicle*. Dickens edited the paper for only 17 issues before handing over to John Forster. Under subsequent editors, including Charles Wentworth Dilke and later H. W. Massingham, the paper maintained its Liberal and reformist character through the Victorian period. It was particularly associated with the platform of Richard Cobden and John Bright — the very "Liberal practitioners" whom Arnold subjects to critique. The paper merged with the *Morning Post* in 1930.
