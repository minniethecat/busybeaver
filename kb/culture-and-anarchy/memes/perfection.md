# Perfection

The telos of Arnold's entire project — the state of complete and harmonious human development that culture pursues, encompassing both the individual's inner growth and the perfection of society as a whole. Perfection is the answer to every question Arnold poses: "What is culture for?" — Perfection. "What is the state for?" — Perfection. "Why resist machinery?" — Because it substitutes means for the end, which is perfection (P03-003).

## Arnold's Definition

Arnold defines perfection not as a static state but as an ongoing process:

> Not a having and a resting, but a growing and a becoming, is the character of perfection as culture conceives it. (001-005)

This dynamic conception distinguishes Arnold's perfectionism from both religious quietism (which imagines perfection as a state to be passively received) and utilitarian complacency (which imagines it as a condition to be achieved by material accumulation). Perfection is not something you arrive at; it is something you pursue — and the pursuit itself is transformative.

Perfection has two essential structural qualities for Arnold:

1. **Harmonious**: It must develop all sides of human nature — moral, intellectual, aesthetic, social — rather than elevating one faculty at the expense of others. "Perfection is an harmonious expansion of all the powers which make the beauty and worth of human nature, and is not consistent with the over-development of any one power at the expense of the rest" (001-005).

2. **General**: It must extend to all members of society, not just a privileged few. "Because men are all members of one great whole, and the sympathy which is in human nature will not allow one member to be indifferent to the rest, perfection is not possible while the individual remains isolated" (001-005).

### The Greek Inheritance

Arnold's concept of perfection draws heavily on the Greek ideal of *eudaimonia* — the flourishing life in which all human capacities are developed in balanced harmony. The phrase "harmonious expansion of all the powers" is almost a direct translation of the Aristotelian conception of virtue as the mean between extremes. Arnold's innovation is to insist that this classical ideal is not at odds with Christianity but is, in fact, its completion — that the Christian aspiration to holiness and the Greek aspiration to harmonious development are, properly understood, the same aspiration.

## Culture as the Study of Perfection

The identification of culture with the study of perfection is Arnold's foundational move:

> Culture is then properly described not as having its origin in curiosity, but as having its origin in the love of perfection; it is a study of perfection. (001-002)

This formulation elevates culture from a merely intellectual pursuit to a moral and social one. Culture is driven not by idle curiosity — not by the desire to know interesting things — but by "the moral and social passion for doing good" (001-002). Its motto is Bishop Wilson's phrase: "To make reason and the will of God prevail" (001-002).

### The Implications of the Definition

Several consequences follow from defining culture as "the study of perfection":

1. **Culture is normative, not descriptive**: It is not concerned with what people happen to think or do but with what they *ought* to think and do. "The best that has been thought and said" is a standard, not a survey.

2. **Culture is moral, not merely intellectual**: The pursuit of knowledge for its own sake may be admirable, but it is not culture in Arnold's sense. Culture is knowledge placed in the service of human improvement.

3. **Culture is practical, not contemplative**: "The moral and social passion for doing good" makes culture into a force that acts upon the world rather than merely observing it. The scholar who knows everything and does nothing fails the test of culture.

### The Controversy

This definition has been controversial from Arnold's own day to ours. Critics — from John Stuart Mill to contemporary cultural studies — have objected that Arnold's perfectionism is elitist, prescriptive, and insufficiently attentive to the diversity of human goods. Arnold's reply, implicit throughout *Culture and Anarchy*, is that the charge of elitism mistakes the *source* of the standard (the cultural tradition Arnold inherited) for the standard itself. Perfection is not what Arnold happens to prefer; it is what human nature, properly understood, demands.

## Harmonious Perfection

Arnold insists that true perfection cannot involve the over-development of any single human faculty. This principle — the principle of harmony — is the basis of his critique of Puritan Hebraism (which develops conscience at the expense of intellect and beauty), of industrial capitalism (which develops the commercial faculty at the expense of all others), and of political liberalism (which develops the love of liberty at the expense of asking what liberty is for).

The image Arnold uses is of a body in which one limb has grown monstrously large while the others have atrophied. Such a body is not stronger than a proportionate one; it is deformed. The same is true of the soul. A person of impeccable moral conduct who has never learned to see beauty or to think freely is not a perfected person but a mutilated one.

### The Therapeutic Function

Harmonious perfection has a therapeutic function in Arnold's thought. It names a condition toward which we can strive even as we acknowledge its unattainability. No one achieves perfect harmony; everyone can pursue it. The pursuit itself — the effort to develop neglected faculties, to round out a one-sided character, to see what one has been trained not to see — is the substance of the cultural life.

## General Perfection

Equally important is the social dimension:

> Because men are all members of one great whole, and the sympathy which is in human nature will not allow one member to be indifferent to the rest, perfection is not possible while the individual remains isolated. (001-005)

The individual is "obliged, under pain of being stunted and enfeebled in his own development," to carry others along in the march toward perfection (001-005). This is Arnold's answer to those who accuse culture of being selfish or elitist — true culture is inherently social and democratic. The man who pursues his own perfection while ignoring the condition of his neighbors has not understood what perfection requires.

### The Democratic Imperative

Arnold's insistence on generality gives his perfectionism a democratic edge that is easily overlooked. Perfection is not the preserve of a cultivated elite; it is a universal human possibility — and a universal human obligation. The working man who seeks "sweetness and light" is pursuing perfection as genuinely as the Oxford don. What matters is not the starting point but the direction of travel. "The many," Arnold writes, "will never understand the few unless the few make the effort to communicate" (001-005). Perfection is not something to be hoarded but something to be shared.

## Perfection and Religion

Arnold takes pains to show that culture's idea of perfection coincides with religion's. Both place perfection "in an internal condition, in the growth and predominance of our humanity proper, as distinguished from our animality" (001-005). The difference is one of emphasis, not substance. Where culture goes beyond religion, as ordinarily conceived, is in insisting that perfection must be *harmonious* — that no single faculty, however exalted, can be developed at the expense of others.

This is Arnold's most radical theological claim: that culture completes religion, and that religion without culture is incomplete. The Puritan who is morally scrupulous but aesthetically dead is not a good Christian but an incomplete one. Christianity, properly understood, aims at the full development of human nature — and that is precisely what culture, in Arnold's sense, pursues.

### Bishop Wilson as Exemplar

Arnold's use of Bishop Wilson as a recurring authority is instructive. Wilson is not a Hellenist — he is a plain, old-fashioned Anglican bishop — but his maxims ("To make reason and the will of God prevail") express an inwardness and a practical orientation toward perfection that Arnold finds exemplary. Wilson shows that the love of perfection is not the property of any particular intellectual tradition; it is available to anyone who seeks it sincerely.

## Perfection vs. Machinery

A crucial distinction Arnold draws is between perfection itself and the external means often mistaken for it. Wealth, industry, population, freedom — these are "machinery," not perfection. To worship them as ends in themselves is to mistake the instrument for the goal:

> The whole civilisation of the modern world is machinery; and the more machinery we have, the more we need culture, to save us from being overpowered by it. (001-006)

True perfection is inward and spiritual, consisting in "the growth and predominance of our humanity proper" (001-005). Everything external — laws, institutions, railways, coal — is valuable only insofar as it serves this inward end. The confusion of machinery with perfection is, for Arnold, the defining error of the modern age.

## Perfection as the Answer to Anarchy

Arnold's concept of perfection does political work. The problem of anarchy — "doing as one likes" without reference to any standard beyond individual preference — is, at bottom, a problem about ends. People do as they like because they have no conception of what they *ought* to like. Perfection supplies the missing end. When individuals and classes orient themselves toward perfection — toward the harmonious development of human nature — they discover a common standard that transcends their private interests. "By our best self," as Arnold puts it, "we are united, impersonal, at one" (002-018).

## Historical Context

Arnold's perfectionism belongs to a tradition that runs from Plato through Aristotle, the Stoics, and the Christian contemplative tradition, to the German *Bildung* ideal that Arnold encountered through Goethe and Wilhelm von Humboldt. His distinctive contribution is to marry this classical tradition to a democratic politics — to insist that perfection is not for the few but for all, and that the state has a positive obligation to create the conditions in which all can pursue it.

The political resonance of this claim in the 1860s was explosive. In an England that had just extended the franchise and was debating compulsory education, Arnold's assertion that the state should actively promote "perfection" was a direct challenge to the liberal orthodoxy of minimal government. It was also a challenge to the working-class movement, which Arnold feared was more interested in marching and breaking windows than in the arduous inward labor that perfection requires.
