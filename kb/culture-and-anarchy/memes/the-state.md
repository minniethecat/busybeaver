# The State

The State, in Arnold's political vocabulary, is not the apparatus of
coercion, taxation, and administration that the English middle class
mistrusted. It is something closer to what the Continental tradition calls
*der Staat* — the community organised as a rational whole, the institutional
form of the nation's best collective judgment. Arnold's rehabilitation of
the concept of the State is one of the most original and politically
significant moves in *Culture and Anarchy*.

## Arnold's Definition

Arnold defines the State by reference to the Continent. On the Continent,
"the idea of the State is a serious and important idea," whereas "our
English habit, on the contrary, is to use the State as a mere
administrative machine" [002-023]. The English have lost the sense — which
the ancient world possessed and the Continental nations retained through
Roman law — that "the State — the nation, in its collective and corporate
character" should exercise "paramount authority" over individual and class
interests [002-004]. Arnold's project is to recover this richer
conception: "culture suggests the idea of the State" [002-024], and
specifically the idea that "the State is the organ of our collective best
self, of our national right reason" [002-026].

## Usage Across the Text

### The English Problem: No Notion of the State

Arnold's diagnosis is blunt: the English have no adequate idea of the
State. They understand the State as a set of practical arrangements — "a
mere administrative machine" for managing public business — but not as the
bearer of a normative standard higher than private interest [002-023]. This
deficiency is the political consequence of the triumph of middle-class
liberalism, which identified the State with external constraint and
therefore regarded its restriction as an unconditional good [002-017].

### The State as the Antidote to Anarchy

Arnold presents the State as the institutional answer to the disorder
produced by "doing as one likes." Where the ordinary self, left to itself,
produces the "anarchy" of contending appetites, the State embodies a
standard that stands above appetite. "We want an authority," Arnold writes,
and "the State is the organ of our collective best self" [002-026]. The
State is not an external imposition on freedom but "the action of the
collective nation" — that is, a form of freedom exercised at a higher level
of organisation [003-018]. "The action of the State being the action of the
collective nation, and the action of the collective nation being the action
of all its individuals," the apparent opposition between State action and
individual liberty dissolves [003-018].

### State Action and Its Enemies

Arnold devotes substantial energy to rebutting the objections to State
action that prevailed in Victorian England. The objection that State action
stifles individual energy he answers by distinguishing between "the energy
of the individual" and "the energy of individuals pursuing their own
private ends without regard to the common weal" [002-017–018]. The
objection that the English are too individualistic for State action he
turns on its head: the very "relaxed habits of government" that
individualism has produced are a reason for, not against, a stronger
conception of the State [002-009]. Arnold's position is not that the State
should displace individual initiative but that individual initiative
requires a framework of collective reason — and this framework is what the
English lack.

### The State and the Church

Arnold's discussion of the State is entangled with his discussion of the
Church — the institution that, in the English historical experience, most
closely approximated the idea of a corporate bearer of right reason. But
the Church had been captured by sectarian interests (the Nonconformists on
one side, the ritualists on the other), and Arnold looks to the State as
the institutional form capable of carrying the ideal of "a best self and
right reason which may stand as a serious authority" beyond the ruins of
ecclesiastical consensus [003-013–014].

## Historical Context

Arnold's theory of the State draws on German idealist philosophy —
particularly Hegel's *Philosophy of Right* (1821), which Arnold would have
encountered through his extensive reading in German literature and thought.
The idea of the State as the actualisation of rational freedom was a
commonplace of nineteenth-century German political thought, but it was
almost entirely absent from English discourse, which remained locked in the
utilitarian and individualist assumptions Arnold set out to challenge.
Arnold's achievement was to translate this Continental idea into an English
idiom and to connect it with the practical political questions raised by
the Reform agitation of the 1860s [002-004, 002-023–026, 003-014].
