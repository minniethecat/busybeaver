**Hyde Park** is a large royal park in central London, significant in [[culture-and-anarchy]] as the site of the 1866 Reform League demonstrations that [[matthew-arnold]] uses as a recurring example of popular political action and the challenges it poses to public order.

## The 1866 Hyde Park Riots

In July 1866, the Reform League organized a mass meeting in Hyde Park to demand extension of the franchise. When the gates were locked by authorities, demonstrators tore down railings and entered the park. The event became known as the "Hyde Park riots" and served as a flashpoint in the broader [[reform-crisis-1866-1867]] [002-028].

Arnold references the riots at several points in [[culture-and-anarchy]], consistently using them to illustrate his argument that popular political action, lacking the guidance of culture and [[right-reason]], tends toward anarchy. He describes the Hyde Park rioter as fundamentally different from the philanthropic middle-class reformer [002-009].

## Arnold's Treatment

Arnold's treatment of Hyde Park reflects his ambivalence about democracy and popular movements. He acknowledges the legitimacy of political grievances while condemning what he sees as the destructive methods of redress. He writes of the readiness of the crowd "the other day in Hyde Park to take upon themselves all the functions of government" [002-022].

For Arnold, the Hyde Park demonstrations exemplify the tendency of English [[individualism]] to manifest as "doing as one likes" — the central problem he diagnoses in Victorian society. The rioter, like the [[philistines|Philistine]] middle-class worshipper of [[free-trade]], acts from untransformed impulse rather than cultivated reason [003-018].

## In the Context of Reform

The Hyde Park events occurred during the wider [[reform-crisis-1866-1867]], which culminated in the Second Reform Act of 1867. Arnold's discussion of Hyde Park is thus part of his engagement with the most pressing political question of his day: how to extend political participation while preserving social order and elevating public discourse [005-010].
