# Charles Spurgeon

Charles Haddon Spurgeon (1834–1892) was an English Particular Baptist
preacher whose sermons at the Metropolitan Tabernacle drew crowds of
thousands and earned him the title "Prince of Preachers." Arnold invokes
him eight times in *Culture and Anarchy* (1869) as the pre-eminent
spokesman for Nonconformist "voluntaryism" — the principle that
religious bodies should be self-governing and free from state
establishment — which Arnold treats as a theological variant of the
general Liberal doctrine of "doing as one likes."

## Role in *Culture and Anarchy*

Spurgeon appears more frequently than any other religious figure in
the book except St. Paul. Arnold's treatment is notable for its tone:
less satirical than his portraits of Murphy or Cattle, but more
sustained in its critique, reflecting Spurgeon's genuine stature as
a public intellectual.

### "The Dissidence of Dissent"

In the Conclusion, Arnold gives Spurgeon his most concentrated
attention:

> because Mr. Spurgeon, in his eloquent and memorable sermon at the
> opening of a great new chapel, has set forth very finely …
> (PN 006-002)

Arnold quotes Spurgeon's argument that the Nonconformist conscience
requires disestablishment not as a political convenience but as a
theological imperative. The logic, as Arnold presents it, runs:

> this mechanical maxim of Mr. Spurgeon and the Nonconformists, that
> "the State should only do that which is the State's duty and
> province to do" (PN 006-005)

Arnold's critique is not that Spurgeon is wrong about theology but
that his theological reasoning, however "eloquent and memorable,"
is a species of the same mechanical thinking that governs the
Philistine manufacturer and the Barbarian aristocrat. Each class
finds its own rationale for "doing as one likes"; Spurgeon merely
provides the Dissenting version.

### The "Setting Up of the Roman Empire"

In the Preface, Arnold notes:

> Mr. Spurgeon says, "a setting up of the Roman Empire"
> (PN P03-028)

Spurgeon's fear that disestablishment might lead to a new ecclesiastical
tyranny is, for Arnold, an ironic confirmation of his own argument:
that the Dissenters' passion for liberty ends in a new form of
constraint, because liberty without "right reason" is merely the
exchange of one master for another.

## Connection to Arnold's Argument

Spurgeon matters to Arnold because he is not a fringe figure but the
most popular preacher in Victorian England. His voluntaryism represents
the mainstream of Dissenting opinion, and Arnold's critique of it is
therefore a critique of English Protestantism at its most influential.
The argument is not that Spurgeon is insincere — Arnold concedes his
eloquence — but that even the most sincere Hebraism, when it refuses
the light of Hellenism, cannot see beyond its own mechanical categories.
