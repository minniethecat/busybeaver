The **Book of Leviticus** is the third book of the Hebrew Bible and the Christian [[old-testament|Old Testament]], containing detailed prescriptions for ritual, legal, and moral conduct. In [[culture-and-anarchy]], [[matthew-arnold]] refers to Leviticus in the context of the English Puritan interpretation of Mosaic law, particularly around marriage regulations — using it to illustrate what he sees as the limitations of a purely [[hebraism|Hebraic]] approach to religion.

## The Book and Its Contents

Leviticus (from the Greek *Leuitikon*, "pertaining to the Levites") takes its name from the priestly tribe of Israel and consists primarily of laws and instructions governing sacrifice, ritual purity, dietary practice, moral conduct, and the observance of holy days. Its central theme is holiness — the separation of the people of Israel from surrounding nations through obedience to divine law.

In the Christian tradition, Leviticus has often been treated as the paradigmatic example of "the law" that the New Testament gospel supersedes. Its detailed ritual prescriptions — governing everything from leprosy to mildew, from sexual relations to the treatment of slaves — have made it a challenging text for modern readers, and a frequent focus of debates about biblical literalism and the relationship between Old Testament law and Christian ethics.

## Arnold's Reference

Arnold mentions the Book of Leviticus in [[Chapter-VI|Chapter VI]] of *Culture and Anarchy* ("Our Liberal Practitioners"), in a discussion of the rigidity with which English [[puritanism]] approached biblical law. He notes the controversy over marriage to a deceased wife's sister — a question that preoccupied Victorian ecclesiastical and parliamentary debate — and observes that [[john-william-colenso|Bishop Colenso]] had questioned the traditional reading of the Levitical prohibition:

> The name he always gave to the Book of Leviticus [006-014]

This reference serves Arnold's broader argument about the limitations of [[hebraism]] when it is not balanced by [[hellenism]]. The Puritan fixation on the literal text of Leviticus exemplifies what Arnold sees as the Hebraic tendency to reduce religion to a code of conduct, divorced from the free play of intelligence and the pursuit of [[perfection]] in its fullest human sense.

## The Marriage Question

The specific controversy Arnold references concerned the prohibition in Leviticus 18:16 and 20:21 against marrying a brother's wife — a text that English ecclesiastical law had extended to prohibit marriage with a deceased wife's sister. The campaign to legalize such marriages was one of the long-running reform causes of Victorian England, championed by Liberals and opposed by the established church on scriptural grounds.

Arnold's interest is not in the legal question itself but in what it reveals about the English religious temper. He asks:

> Does God's law (that is, the Book of Leviticus) forbid us to marry our wife's sister? [006-017]

For Arnold, the very form of the question reveals the problem. To ask whether Leviticus "forbids" a particular act is to treat scripture as a legal code rather than as a source of spiritual wisdom — to substitute the letter for the spirit, the rule for the life. The person who can think of God's law only as prohibition, Arnold implies, has already lost the capacity to think of religion as the pursuit of human perfection.

## Hebraism and Legalism

Arnold's use of Leviticus illustrates the central distinction he draws between two modes of religious thought. [[hebraism]], in its narrowest form, treats scripture as a repository of rules to be obeyed — "doing" without "knowing." [[hellenism]], by contrast, treats scripture (and all cultural productions) as objects of understanding, to be approached with the free play of intelligence.

The Book of Leviticus, in Arnold's telling, becomes a test case. One can read it as the Puritans did — as a code of conduct whose every clause demands literal compliance — or one can read it as Arnold urges, with an appreciation for its place in the development of human religious consciousness, without mistaking its ritual prescriptions for the essence of religion itself.

This is not, for Arnold, an argument against the Old Testament or against moral seriousness. It is an argument against what he calls "machinery" — the substitution of external forms and literal prescriptions for the inward work of [[perfection]] that true religion, like true culture, demands.
