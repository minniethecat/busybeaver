The Established Church — the Church of England in its legal character as the state church — is a recurring presence in Arnold's *Culture and Anarchy*. Arnold's attitude toward the Establishment is characteristically nuanced: he recognizes the value of a national church as an organ of culture and social cohesion, while acknowledging that in its current form it has become a partisan institution that excites the antipathy of Nonconformists and fails to embody the comprehensive ideal of which it is the historic custodian.

## Nature

The Church of England was established as the national church at the Reformation, with the monarch as its Supreme Governor and its bishops sitting in the House of Lords. By Arnold's time in the 1860s, it remained legally privileged — funded by tithes and endowments, its clergy enjoying social status, its doctrines embedded in the constitution — but its claim to national comprehensiveness had been eroded by the growth of Nonconformity, Roman Catholic emancipation, and secular liberalism.

## Role in Arnold's Argument

Arnold's treatment of the Established Church reflects his broader project of seeking a principle of authority beyond class interest. He does not defend the Establishment in the partisan spirit of a Church-and-King Tory; rather, he sees in the idea of a national church — an organ of the State devoted to the cultivation of the best self — a vision that the present Establishment has failed to realize.

The Nonconformists' opposition to the Establishment troubles Arnold not because he thinks the Establishment is perfect but because he sees the Nonconformist critique as intellectually shallow: it proceeds from "antipathy" rather than from a genuine vision of what a national organ of culture might be. Arnold's own position, hinted at in the Preface, is that the Establishment might be reformed rather than abolished — that "the present Anglican Establishment" might be given "a character the most latitudinarian" so as to serve as a genuine "centre of good" for the nation (PN P03-021).

## Key Passages

> "The Nonconformists, blind partisans of the Anglican Establishment" — Arnold's opening characterization in the Preface (PN P03-006)

> "The English Established Church and Episcopacy" — discussed in the context of historical Nonconformist separation (PN P03-022)

> "A character the most latitudinarian" — Arnold's suggested reform for the Establishment, making it broad enough to serve as a national centre (PN P03-021)

Arnold also contrasts the Establishment with Dissenting bodies, noting that the Establishment does not "differ from the Established Church by having worthier or more philosophical views" — the point being that the critique should be about the quality of culture, not merely about institutional arrangements (PN 006-007).

## Historical Context

The Church of England's established status was a central issue in Victorian religious politics. The 1828 repeal of the Test and Corporation Acts and the 1829 Catholic Emancipation Act had already broken the Anglican monopoly on public office. The 1830s and 1840s saw the disestablishment of the Church of Ireland as a live political question (achieved in 1869), and Nonconformists increasingly agitated for disestablishment in England itself — a cause that would gain strength through the Liberation Society and the campaigns of Edward Miall. Arnold wrote *Culture and Anarchy* at a moment when the future of the Establishment was genuinely uncertain. The Church of England was not disestablished in England, and remains the established church to this day.
