# Lord Elcho

Francis Charteris, styled Lord Elcho (1818–1914), was a Scottish
Conservative MP who sat in the House of Commons from 1841 to 1883 before
succeeding as the 10th Earl of Wemyss. In *Culture and Anarchy*, Lord
Elcho occupies a privileged position in Arnold's sociological typology:
he is the "perfection, or happy mean, or virtue, of aristocracy"
[002-016] — the Barbarian at his best, and therefore the most instructive
case for demonstrating the insufficiency of even the best Barbarian as a
source of social authority.

## Identity

Elcho was the eldest son of the 9th Earl of Wemyss and a prominent figure
in mid-Victorian Conservative politics. He took an active interest in
military affairs, serving as a lieutenant-colonel in the London Scottish
Rifle Volunteers, and was known as an independent-minded MP who did not
always follow the party line. Arnold encountered him during the Reform
debates and was struck by a particular combination of qualities: high
spirit tempered by ease, serenity, and politeness — the very qualities
that Thomas Carlyle identified as the distinctive virtues of aristocracy.

## Role in Arnold's Argument

Elcho serves as the indispensable "mean" against which Arnold measures
both the excess (Sir Thomas Bateson) and the defect (left unnamed) of the
aristocratic class. Arnold's Aristotelian framework depends on Elcho: it
is because Elcho is genuinely admirable — "showing plenty of high spirit,
but remarkable, far above and beyond his gift of high spirit, for the fine
tempering of his high spirit, for ease, serenity, politeness" [002-016] —
that his insufficiency carries such critical weight.

The crucial turn in Arnold's argument is that even this "beautiful and
virtuous mean" is not enough. "In this beautiful and virtuous mean, there
seemed evidently some insufficiency of light" [002-016]. Elcho has the
exterior culture of the Barbarian — the graces, the bearing, the high
spirit — but lacks the inward transformation that Arnold's "culture"
demands. If even the best Barbarian cannot supply the principle of
authority the nation needs, then the entire class is disqualified.

## Key Passages

**On the perfect mean of aristocracy**: In Arnold's most extended treatment
of Elcho, he writes: "Lord Elcho was exactly the perfection, or happy
mean, or virtue, of aristocracy… On the one hand, in Lord Elcho, showing
plenty of high spirit, but remarkable, far above and beyond his gift of
high spirit, for the fine tempering of his high spirit, for ease, serenity,
politeness" [002-016]. The repetition of "tempering" is deliberate — Elcho
represents the aristocratic spirit moderated, not raw.

**On the insufficiency of light**: Arnold's diagnosis is precise: even
Elcho's excellence consists of "the great virtues, as Mr. Carlyle says, of
aristocracy," yet "in this beautiful and virtuous mean, there seemed
evidently some insufficiency of light" [002-016]. The phrase "insufficiency
of light" recurs throughout the book as the signature failing of the
Barbarian class.

**On the Barbarian genealogy**: In a striking passage, Arnold traces the
aristocracy's virtues back to their supposed Barbarian ancestors: "The
chivalry of the Barbarians, with its characteristics of high spirit,
choice manners, and distinguished bearing, — what is this but the
beautiful commencement of the politeness of our aristocratic class? In
some Barbarian noble, no doubt, one would have admired, if one could have
been then alive to see it, the rudiments of Lord Elcho" [003-005].

**On Elcho and Odger**: Arnold draws a parallel between the aristocratic
mean and the working-class mean: "Mr. Odger… may very well stand for the
beautiful and virtuous mean of our present working-class; and I think
everybody will admit that in Mr. Odger, as in Lord Elcho, there is
manifestly, with all his good points, some insufficiency of light"
[002-022]. The structural equivalence is the point: every class, at its
best, still lacks what only culture can supply.

## Relationship to Arnold's Framework

Lord Elcho is Arnold's most carefully ambivalent portrait. He is never
mocked or dismissed; the respect Arnold accords him is genuine. This is
what makes Elcho so effective as a critical device: Arnold demonstrates
that the problem with the aristocracy is not that it is wicked or
incompetent, but that even at its very best — in the person of a man who
exemplifies "ease, serenity, politeness" — it lacks the intellectual depth
and inward transformation that culture alone can provide.

The Elcho-Bateson pair is one of the book's most elegant analytical
moves. By naming actual, recognisable figures as the mean and excess of
aristocracy, Arnold gives his abstract classification of the three classes
a concreteness that pure theory could never achieve. The reader who has
seen Elcho and Bateson in the House of Commons or the newspapers
immediately grasps what Arnold means by the Barbarian range.
