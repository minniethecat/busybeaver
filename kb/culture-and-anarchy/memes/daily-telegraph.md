The *Daily Telegraph* was a London daily newspaper established in 1855, which by the 1860s had become one of the most widely circulated newspapers in Britain. In *Culture and Anarchy*, Arnold treats the *Telegraph* as the quintessential organ of Philistine opinion — loud, self-satisfied, and hostile to the cultivation of "sweetness and light" that Arnold advocates.

## Nature

The *Daily Telegraph* was a penny newspaper aimed at a broad middle-class readership. Under its founding editor Thornton Leigh Hunt and its owner Joseph Moses Levy, it pioneered a lively, accessible style of journalism that broke with the more sober tone of the established press. By the time Arnold wrote *Culture and Anarchy*, the *Telegraph* claimed the largest circulation in the world, a claim Arnold notes with irony.

## Role in Arnold's Argument

Arnold's engagement with the *Daily Telegraph* is one of the most sustained press critiques in *Culture and Anarchy*. He singles out the paper as the embodiment of Philistine self-congratulation, quoting and mocking its claim to have "the largest circulation in the whole world" (PN 001-015). The *Telegraph*'s writers — whom Arnold calls "the young lions of the *Daily Telegraph*" — represent for him the confident ignorance of a class that believes its commercial success validates its opinions (PN 001-016).

The *Telegraph* had taken Arnold to task for his cultural criticism, and Arnold's reply forms a central strand of Chapter I. The paper's attack on Arnold is, in his view, perfectly characteristic of the Philistine hostility to ideas: it substitutes bluster for argument and treats the pursuit of culture as an affectation rather than a serious endeavor. Arnold's famous description of culture as "sweetness and light" is, in part, a response to the *Telegraph*'s dismissal of his project.

## Key Passages

Arnold repeatedly returns to the *Daily Telegraph* as a touchstone:

> "I say that when our religious organizations — which I admit to be the greatest beauty and glory of the nation — and the *Daily Telegraph*, with its largest circulation in the whole world, is the *Daily Telegraph*!" (PN 001-015)

> "The young lions of the *Daily Telegraph* in the meanwhile are sacrificed" — Arnold's ironic comment on the paper's self-presentation as representing the public (PN 001-016)

The paper is discussed in the Preface as well, where Arnold notes its coupling of praise for Nonconformity with attacks on culture (PN P04-002).

## Historical Context

The *Daily Telegraph* was founded in 1855 following the repeal of the newspaper stamp duty, which made penny newspapers economically viable. It rapidly established itself as a popular competitor to *The Times*, offering a more accessible and sensational style. By the 1860s, its circulation exceeded 240,000, reportedly the largest in the world at the time. Arnold's critique reflects a broader Victorian anxiety about the demotic character of the popular press and its influence on public opinion. The *Telegraph* merged with *The Sunday Telegraph* in the 20th century and continues publication today.
