# John Worth Edmonds

John Worth Edmonds (1799–1874) was an American judge who served on the New
York Supreme Court and became one of the most prominent American spiritualists
of the mid-nineteenth century. In *Culture and Anarchy* (1869), Arnold
references Edmonds twice as an illustration of the indiscriminate reverence
with which certain popular writers treat religious figures of no intellectual
substance.

## Role in Arnold's Argument

Edmonds appears in Chapter III, in a sustained passage where Arnold critiques
the contemporary English writer Hepworth Dixon for treating obscure American
religious figures with the solemnity due to Plato or St. Paul.

### The Indiscriminate Appeal to Authority

Arnold's first reference places Edmonds in a list of authorities whom Dixon
cites as if their endorsement conferred intellectual legitimacy:

> "Well, but," says Mr. Hepworth Dixon, "a theory which has been accepted
> by men like Judge Edmonds, Dr. Hare, Elder Frederick, and Professor Bush!"
> … But the question is, have personages like Judge Edmonds, and Newman
> Weeks, and Elderess Polly … anything of the weight and significance for
> the best reason and spirit of man that Plato and St. Paul have?
> Evidently they, at present, have not. (PN 003-013)

Arnold's point is not that Edmonds is a fraud, but that the mere fact of
his acceptance of a doctrine — or his judicial eminence — does not confer
intellectual weight upon it. The habit of treating all authorities as equal
is, for Arnold, a symptom of the "natural taste for the bathos" that
afflicts modern democratic culture.

### Edmonds Among the Explorers

Later in the same chapter, Arnold returns to Edmonds as part of a catalogue
of the "infinite variety of experiments" that proliferate when no
authoritative standard of right reason is recognised:

> even in this short paper I have enumerated many: the British Banner,
> Judge Edmonds, Newman Weeks, Deborah Butler, Elderess Polly, Brother
> Noyes, the Rev. W. Cattle, the Licensed Victuallers, the Commercial
> Travellers, and I know not how many more; and the numbers of this noble
> army are swelling every day. (PN 003-019)

The phrase "this noble army" drips with Arnoldian irony. Edmonds and his
fellow "explorers" are not villains — Arnold's tone is more bemused than
hostile — but they represent the chaotic proliferation of intellectual and
spiritual authorities that results when society lacks a principle of
discrimination.

## Connection to Arnold's Framework

Edmonds serves Arnold's larger argument about authority and the need for a
"best self" that can stand as a genuine standard. In a culture where Judge
Edmonds and Plato are cited with equal reverence, there can be no
authoritative "right reason" to guide individual conduct. Edmonds thus
exemplifies what Arnold sees as the intellectual consequence of doing as
one likes: a flattening of all distinctions, a democracy of ideas in which
nothing is truly excellent because nothing is truly judged.
