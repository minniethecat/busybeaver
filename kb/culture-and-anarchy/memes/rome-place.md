Ancient Rome appears in *Culture and Anarchy* primarily as a point of comparison with Greece. Arnold's judgment of Rome reflects the 19th-century classical education's standard hierarchy: Greece represents the creative, intellectual, and aesthetic achievement of the ancient world; Rome represents the organizational, administrative, and practical. Rome is not dismissed, but it is subordinated to Greece as the archetype of the Hellenic ideal.

## Location

Ancient Rome was a city-state on the Tiber River that grew into an empire spanning the Mediterranean world. At its height in the 2nd century CE, the Roman Empire extended from Britain to Mesopotamia, from the Rhine to the Sahara. For Arnold, "Rome" means primarily the civilization of the late Republic and early Empire — the world of Cicero, Virgil, and the Stoics.

## Role in Arnold's Argument

Arnold mentions Rome in two contexts. The first is the historical judgment that both classical civilizations — "the civilisation of Greece and Rome" — were "mechanical and external" in their cultural reach, failing to penetrate deeply into the lives of the populations they governed (PN 001-006). This is a candid acknowledgment that neither classical civilization achieved the universal cultivation of the best self that Arnold posits as the ideal.

The second context is Rome's relationship to Christianity. Arnold notes the "antipathy of Puritanism toward the Church of Rome" and judges it "wrong and false" — an instance of the provincialism that prevents English Protestants from recognizing whatever is genuinely valuable in the Roman Catholic tradition (PN P03-027). Arnold's interest here is not in ancient Rome but in its Christian successor, which he sees as having preserved elements of the classical inheritance that Protestantism lost.

## Key Passages

> "The civilisation of Greece and Rome, mechanical and external, and hardly more than external to those whom it did not reach" (PN 001-006)

> "The antipathy of Puritanism towards the Church of Rome is wrong and false" — a rare Arnoldian defense of Roman Catholicism as a cultural force (PN P03-027)

Arnold also invokes Rome in Chapter I through a Cato quotation about Roman greatness (PN 001-015).

## Historical Context

The Victorian classical education placed Greece and Rome at the center of the curriculum, but the relative valuation of the two civilizations was a matter of active debate. The 19th century saw a marked shift toward the idealization of Greece (particularly Athens) as the supreme achievement of the ancient world, with Rome increasingly seen as derivative and practical rather than creative. Arnold's treatment of Rome reflects this Hellenophilic tendency, though his acknowledgment of Rome's cultural value — and his defense of the Roman Catholic Church against Protestant prejudice — introduces a more balanced judgment than was common among English Hellenists.
