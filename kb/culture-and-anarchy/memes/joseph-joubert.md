# Joseph Joubert

Joseph Joubert (1754–1824) is one of the few continental thinkers whom [[matthew-arnold]] cites with consistent and unreserved approval throughout *Culture and Anarchy*. A French moralist who published almost nothing in his lifetime, Joubert belongs to that strand of French thought Arnold identifies with the [[hellenism|Hellenic]] spirit — the pursuit of inward perfection through the love of form, clarity, and beauty. His *Pensées*, collected and published posthumously by Chateaubriand, became for Arnold a touchstone of practical wisdom, a model of how [[culture]] works upon the individual soul through reflection and refinement rather than through dogma or system.

## Identity

Joubert was a French moralist and essayist of the late Enlightenment and early Romantic periods. He moved in the literary circles of Paris, corresponded with Chateaubriand and other leading figures, but deliberately refrained from publishing during his lifetime. His reputation rests entirely on the *Pensées* — maxims, aphorisms, and fragmentary reflections on ethics, religion, literature, and education — which were gathered and published posthumously in 1838. The work reveals a mind of exceptional delicacy and spiritual refinement, qualities that Arnold would later celebrate as exemplary of the Hellenic temperament.

## Role in Arnold's Argument

Arnold references Joubert at three strategic points in *Culture and Anarchy*, and each citation underscores a core element of Arnold's own argument. Joubert functions in the text not as a remote authority but as a kindred spirit — a writer who grasped, with French lucidity, precisely what Arnold wished to urge upon his English audience: that the pursuit of perfection must be inward and practical, that religious worship requires public and visible expression, and that the best spiritual language approaches simplicity and reticence.

## Key Passages

Arnold's first appeal to Joubert comes early in the book, where he recommends Joubert's approach to reading as a model for his own readers:

> They should be read, as Joubert says Nicole should be read, with a direct aim at practice. [P03-002]

Here Joubert supplies Arnold with a hermeneutic principle: wisdom literature — whether Nicole's essays or Joubert's own maxims — is not meant for idle speculation but for the active shaping of conduct. This is Arnold's own conception of culture as a practical, transforming force.

The second and most developed reference appears in the discussion of worship and the social fabric:

> Just what makes worship impressive, says Joubert, is its publicity, its external manifestation, its sound, its splendour, its observance universally and visibly holding its way through all the details both of our outward and of our inward life. [006-006]

This passage anchors Arnold's insistence that religion and culture are not merely private, inward affairs but require visible, communal forms — an argument central to his critique of Nonconformist individualism.

A third, quieter reference in Chapter VI invokes Joubert on prayer:

> The best prayers are those which have nothing distinct about them, and which are thus of the nature of simple adoration.

Joubert's ascetic, almost negative theology of prayer — the idea that the purest spiritual language approaches wordlessness — resonates with Arnold's preference for the suggestive over the dogmatic, for the poetic over the doctrinal.

## Relationship to Arnold's Framework

Joubert embodies the [[hellenism|Hellenic]] ideal as Arnold defines it: the commitment to seeing things as they really are, to the discipline of inward clarity, and to the harmonising of thought and conduct through the steady, unobtrusive work of self-culture. Unlike the Hebraic emphasis on strictness of conscience and obedience to law, the Hellenic impulse — and Joubert's own method — proceeds by way of light, intelligence, and the love of perfection for its own sake. Arnold's repeated recourse to Joubert signals his conviction that the English mind, prone to Hebraic narrowness and provinciality, has much to learn from the French tradition of moral reflection that Joubert represents.
