# Democracy

Democracy is the horizon of Arnold's social thought — the political fact toward
which England was moving, and the condition that made culture more necessary than
ever. Arnold's attitude toward democracy is neither the enthusiasm of the
radical nor the alarm of the conservative: it is the concern of the humanist who
recognizes that the enlargement of political power without the enlargement of
intelligence is a formula for anarchy.

## Arnold's Definition

Arnold does not offer a systematic definition of democracy; his treatment is
contextual, shaped by the debates of the 1860s. But his position is clear:
democracy, understood as the extension of political rights, is machinery — "what
is population but machinery?" [001-007]. The real question is not whether
democracy will come but whether the newly enfranchised will possess the "best
self" that makes self-government something more than self-assertion. Democracy
without culture is "the world of democracy" without the means to inhabit it
wisely [001-020–021].

## Usage Across the Text

### Democracy as Historical Necessity

Arnold accepts democracy as an irreversible fact. The Reform Act of 1867, which
extended the franchise to urban working-class men, was the culmination of a
process that began in 1832. Arnold does not waste energy opposing what cannot be
opposed; his project is to ensure that democracy is "not provincialised, not
materialised, not brutalised" [001-020–021].

### The Peril and Promise of Popular Government

The peril of democracy, in Arnold's view, is that it may simply replace one form
of "doing as one likes" with another: the aristocracy's self-assertion gives way
to the middle class's, which gives way to the working class's, and the cycle of
anarchy continues at a lower level. The promise of democracy is that it may, for
the first time, make "the best self" a genuinely national possession — but only
if culture intervenes to elevate what democracy otherwise leaves at the level of
opinion [001-021].

### Democracy and the State

Arnold's advocacy of "a strong State-power" [002-009] is intimately connected to
his understanding of democracy. The State, in his conception, is the organ of
"the best self" — the collective reason that stands above the anarchy of
individual and class interests. Democracy needs the State, and the State needs
culture; without both, democracy is merely the rule of "the ordinary self"
writ large.

## Historical Context

The Reform Act of 1867 — passed while Arnold was composing the essays that
became *Culture and Anarchy* — is the immediate political context for his
reflections on democracy. The Act roughly doubled the electorate, enfranchising
many urban working-class men for the first time. Arnold's question — what kind
of culture will democracy produce, or be produced by? — was the defining
intellectual question of the moment [001-020–021].
