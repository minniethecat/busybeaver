# William Edward Baxter

William Edward Baxter (1825–1890) was a British Liberal politician and writer. [[matthew-arnold]] repeatedly pairs him with [[charles-buxton]] as an example of the "philosophical politician" who swims with the stream of popular opinion rather than standing for [[right-reason]] [P03-008].

## Identity

Baxter was a Liberal MP and writer on political subjects. He belonged to the tradition of Nonconformist liberalism that Arnold engages with throughout *Culture and Anarchy*. Arnold groups him — alongside Charles Buxton and the Dean of Canterbury — among those who lend a philosophical veneer to what is essentially popular prejudice.

## Role in Arnold's Argument

Arnold deploys Baxter as an illustration of how the [[ordinary-self]] dresses itself in intellectual clothing. Rather than being guided by the [[best-self]] and the pursuit of [[culture]], Baxter represents the tendency to rationalise what is already popular:

> "And we see philosophical politicians, with a turn for swimming with the stream, like Mr. Baxter or Mr. Charles Buxton, and philosophical divines with the same turn, like the Dean of Canterbury, seeking to give a sort of grand stamp of generality and solemnity to this antipathy of the Nonconformists." [P03-008]

Arnold notes acidly that "nothing can be pleasanter than swimming with the stream" [P03-008].

## The "Political Philosopher"

In Chapter VI, Arnold describes Baxter and Buxton as "political philosophers" who gave the appearance of intellectual depth to the national mind's aversion to religious endowments:

> "There were found political philosophers, like Mr. Baxter and Mr. Charles Buxton, to give it a look of more general and philosophical character." [006-002]

This characterisation is central to Arnold's critique: Baxter represents the kind of thinker who, instead of challenging popular opinion, supplies it with intellectual justification.

## Distinction from Richard Baxter

Arnold also references a different [[richard-baxter|Richard Baxter]] — the seventeenth-century Puritan divine — in a passage praising "the fruitful men of English Puritanism and Nonconformity" such as "Milton, Baxter, Wesley" [P03-009]. This is a distinct figure from the nineteenth-century politician, and Arnold's contrasting treatment of the two Baxters underscores his distinction between creative Nonconformist energy and its modern reduction to political convenience.

## Relationship to Arnold's Framework

Baxter personifies the alliance between [[philistines|philistinism]] and intellectual pretension. He is a "philosophical politician" only in the sense of providing post-hoc rationalisations for doing what the [[middle-class]] already wishes to do. For Arnold, this is the opposite of genuine [[culture]], which requires resistance to the stream rather than accommodation to it.
