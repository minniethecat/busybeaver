# Populace

The "Populace" completes Arnold's tripartite social taxonomy alongside the
Barbarians (aristocracy) and Philistines (middle class). It is the most
sympathetically treated class in Arnold's scheme — "raw and half-developed,"
possessed of "an immense amount of human nature," but also "long since deeply
sunk" in poverty and neglect [006-032]. Arnold's concern is that democracy,
extending political power to the Populace without the elevating discipline of
culture, will produce not liberation but a deeper anarchy.

## Arnold's Definition

Arnold defines the Populace not by its occupations or income but by its relation
to culture. The Populace is the class that "has not yet been touched by
sweetness and light" — not because it is incapable of being touched but because
English society has failed to make "the best that has been thought and known"
available to it. The Populace is thus defined by privation: it "has not yet the
idea of the State" [003-005], and it "conceives of no higher object for its
ambition" than material improvement [006-032].

## Usage Across the Text

### The Third Estate

Arnold's treatment of the Populace is shaped by the Reform crisis of 1866–67.
The Hyde Park riots of 1866 — in which working-class demonstrators clashed with
police over the exclusion of the Populace from the franchise — had dramatized
the question Arnold addresses: what happens when the "raw and unkindled masses"
enter political life? [003-005]

### Sympathy Without Sentimentality

Arnold's attitude toward the Populace is distinctive: he is genuinely
sympathetic without being sentimental. He acknowledges that the Populace "has
been flattered and put forward as the new power," but he insists that "the
Populace has not yet the idea of the State" — the conception of a common good
that transcends class interest. The solution is not to exclude the Populace from
politics but to educate it [003-005, 006-032].

## Historical Context

The Reform Act of 1867 gave the vote to urban working-class men for the first
time, fundamentally altering the political landscape Arnold addressed. His
treatment of the Populace reflects both the optimism of those who believed in
"the people" and the anxiety of those who feared "the mob" [003-005, 006-032].
