# Jeremy Bentham

Jeremy Bentham (1748–1832) was an English philosopher, jurist, and social
reformer, best known as the founder of modern utilitarianism — the ethical
doctrine that the right action is that which produces the greatest happiness
for the greatest number. Arnold refers to Bentham twice in *Culture and
Anarchy* (1869), each time as an exemplar of the systematic thinker whose
influence, however real, carries inherent limitations.

## Role in Arnold's Argument

Arnold's treatment of Bentham operates on two levels. On the surface, he
acknowledges Bentham's genuine contribution to social thought. But beneath
this, he deploys Bentham as a case study in the danger of allowing any single
thinker to become the "Rabbi" of a movement.

### Benthamism and the Inadequacy of System

In the Preface, Arnold discusses the dangers of an "inadequate conception"
of various dimensions of human nature:

> culture teaches us how out of place is this language, and that to use it
> shows an inadequate conception of human nature, and that no Church will
> thank a man for taking its part in this fashion, but will leave him with
> indifference to the tender mercies of his Benthamite friends. But
> avoiding Benthamism, or an inadequate conception of the religious side in
> man, culture makes us also avoid Mialism, or an inadequate conception of
> man's totality. (PN P03-027)

Here "Benthamism" serves as shorthand for a worldview that reduces human
nature to its calculable, utilitarian dimension — neglecting the religious
impulse that Arnold regards as an essential element of human perfection.
The phrase "the tender mercies of his Benthamite friends" is deeply
ironic; Arnold suggests that Benthamite rationalism, for all its reforming
zeal, lacks tenderness for the non-rational dimensions of human experience.

### The Bentham-Comte Parallel: Rabbi and Current

In Chapter I, Arnold develops a more general argument about the relationship
between intellectual currents and the individuals who become identified
with them:

> some man, some Bentham or Comte, who has the real merit of having early
> and strongly felt and helped the new current, but who brings plenty of
> narrownesses and mistakes of his own into his feeling and help of it, is
> credited with being the author of the whole current, the fit person to be
> entrusted with its regulation and to guide the human race. (PN 001-021)

Arnold then recounts his personal liberation from Bentham's intellectual
authority. Opening Bentham's *Deontology*, he finds Bentham dismissing
Socrates and Plato as having "talked nonsense under pretence of talking
wisdom and morality." Arnold's response is immediate:

> From the moment of reading that, I am delivered from the bondage of
> Bentham! the fanaticism of his adherents can touch me no longer; I feel
> the inadequacy of his mind and ideas for being the rule of human society,
> for perfection. (PN 001-021)

This is Arnold's key move: he does not reject Bentham's usefulness — he
acknowledges Bentham's "real merit" — but refuses to grant any single
thinker the status of final authority. Culture, Arnold argues, "tends
always thus to deal with the men of a system, of disciples, of a school."

## Connection to Arnold's Framework

Bentham represents, for Arnold, the Philistine spirit at its most
intellectually formidable: a rational, reforming, utilitarian cast of mind
that measures everything by practical consequences and remains indifferent
to the Hellenic ideals of "sweetness and light." Benthamism, in Arnold's
taxonomy, is a species of machinery-worship — the belief that institutional
reform and rational calculation can substitute for the inward work of
perfection. Arnold's "deliverance from the bondage of Bentham" is thus not
merely personal but paradigmatic: it models the liberation that culture
offers from all intellectual systems that claim finality.
