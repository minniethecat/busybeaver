# Bishop Wilson

Thomas Wilson (1663–1755), Anglican bishop of Sodor and Man, is the most frequently cited authority in *Culture and Anarchy* after Scripture. Arnold returns to Wilson's *Maxims of Piety and Christianity* throughout the book, finding in this eighteenth-century prelate a model of the Christian life that combines Hebraic moral seriousness with an unexpected openness to intellectual light — precisely the synthesis of [[hebraism]] and [[hellenism]] that Arnold advocates. "To me and to the members of the Society for the Promotion of Christian Knowledge," Arnold writes, "Bishop Wilson's Maxims deserve to be circulated far more widely than they are" (P03-002).

## Identity

Thomas Wilson served as Bishop of Sodor and Man from 1697 until his death in 1755, making him one of the longest-serving bishops in Anglican history. His diocese — the Isle of Man — was remote and modest, but Wilson's pastoral writings, particularly the *Maxims of Piety and Christianity* (originally published as part of his larger work *Sacra Privata*), attained a wide readership among devout Anglicans. He was known for his practical piety, his attention to the moral and spiritual life of ordinary people, and a certain breadth of sympathy unusual in eighteenth-century churchmen.

## Role in Arnold's Argument

Arnold deploys Bishop Wilson as a rhetorical anchor throughout *Culture and Anarchy*. Wilson serves not as a historical figure to be analysed but as an authoritative voice — a "guide" whose maxims illuminate the right path. Arnold quotes him in every chapter:

### The Key Maxim

The phrase Arnold returns to most frequently is Wilson's maxim:

> "To make reason and the will of God prevail." (001-002)

Arnold elevates this to the very definition of culture's purpose. "I have been trying to show," he writes, "that culture is, or ought to be, the study and pursuit of perfection; and that of perfection as being an increased energy, and a harmonious expansion, of all the powers which make the beauty and worth of human nature" (001-002). Wilson's phrase captures this ambition in religious language, bridging the gap between secular culture and Christian devotion.

## Wilson's Teachings

Arnold extracts several principles from Wilson's *Maxims* that shape his own argument:

**"First, never go against the best light you have"** (004-002). This maxim, Arnold argues, is the common ground of both Hebraism and Hellenism — the obligation to act according to one's best understanding. It is the starting point for all genuine improvement.

**"Take care that the light be not darkness"** (004-009). Arnold quotes this as a warning against the complacency of "doing as one likes." Having a "best light" is not enough; one must actively ensure that one's understanding is genuinely illuminated, not merely conventional prejudice dressed as conviction.

**"Riches are very dangerous things"** (006-011). Arnold deploys Wilson's suspicion of wealth against the materialistic complacency of the Victorian middle class. The bishop's ascetic streak serves Arnold's critique of Philistine worship of prosperity.

**On the moral and intellectual life combined**: "Bishop Wilson," Arnold observes, "is full of examples of this combination of the moral with the intellectual life" (006-017). This is the heart of Wilson's appeal for Arnold — the bishop represents a Christianity that is morally strenuous without being intellectually narrow.

## Wilson as Exemplar of Hellenised Hebraism

Arnold's most ambitious use of Wilson comes in Chapter VI, where he presents the bishop as proof that Hebraism need not be hostile to intellectual light:

> Bishop Wilson, who is full of examples of this combination of the moral with the intellectual life, of that Hellenising within the limits of Hebraism itself, of that renewing of the Hebraic spirit by contact with the Hellenic spirit — Bishop Wilson speaks of "the light of the understanding" as the very "eye of the soul." (006-017)

This "Hellenising within the limits of Hebraism" is Arnold's ultimate formula for cultural renewal. Wilson demonstrates that one can be deeply Christian, morally serious, and committed to conduct — while also prizing intellectual clarity, openness, and the free play of mind. He is, in Arnold's hands, the living refutation of the claim that Hebraism and Hellenism are incompatible.

## Key Passages

> The reader will often find Bishop Wilson quoted. To me and to the members of the Society for the Promotion of Christian Knowledge, his Maxims deserve to be circulated far more widely than they are. (P03-001–002)

> I cannot forbear quoting one more passage of this good bishop:—"Do not think that you have any right to judge others, unless you are prepared to be judged by the same rule." (002-022)

> Bishop Wilson excellently says: "Riches are very dangerous things; and God give us all grace to see our danger, and to be upon our guard." (006-011)

> Bishop Wilson has some striking words:—"It is not enough that we believe the Gospel to be true; we must also believe it to be good; we must love it as well as believe it." (006-030)

## Relationship to Arnold's Framework

Bishop Wilson functions in Arnold's argument as the living synthesis of the two forces that the book's dialectic distinguishes. He represents the possibility — crucial to Arnold's entire project — that religious seriousness and intellectual culture are not enemies but allies. Wilson's Hebraism (his insistence on conduct, obedience, and conscience) is genuine, but it is a Hebraism that has been "Hellenised" — opened to light, flexibility, and the free play of mind. In Arnold's rhetorical strategy, Wilson is the answer to the Puritan who suspects that "culture" means moral laxity.
