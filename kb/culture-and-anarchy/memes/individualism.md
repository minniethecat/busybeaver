# Individualism

Individualism is, in Arnold's anatomy of English society, the temperament that
underlies all the others. It is "our strong individualism, our hatred of all
limits to the unrestricted swing of the individual's personality" [001-006] —
a national disposition so deep that it shapes every institution the English
build and every argument they make for freedom.

## Arnold's Definition

Arnold defines individualism not as a philosophical doctrine but as a
psychological fact about the English people. They distrust the State; they
resent external authority; they insist, with "energy and honesty," upon the
right to go their own way [003-005]. This is, he grants, a genuine source of
national strength — the "staunch individualism" that has made England
politically stable — but it is also, in his view, the root of England's
inability to conceive of perfection as a common enterprise [001-006, 002-009].

## Usage Across the Text

### Individualism and Doing as One Likes

Individualism provides the philosophical ground for "doing as one likes." The
Englishman's conviction that he has an absolute right to "the freeplay of his
own personality" [001-006] is, Arnold argues, a secularized form of the Puritan
insistence on private judgment. It is admirable in its honesty, intolerable in
its consequences: a nation of individuals each doing as he likes produces not
freedom but anarchy.

### The Paradox of English Liberty

Arnold identifies a paradox at the heart of English individualism: the very
energy with which the English insist upon their personal liberty prevents them
from building the institutions — particularly an effective State — that could
secure it. British individualism and industrialism together, he writes with
irony, "can bring a man to" material comfort [002-009], but they cannot bring
him to "sweetness and light."

### The Reformation's Legacy

Arnold traces English individualism to the Reformation, which in England took a
peculiarly individualist form. The Dissenting tradition — with its insistence on
private judgment, its suspicion of ecclesiastical authority, and its
identification of conscience with individual conviction — is, in Arnold's
reading, the religious expression of the same temper that produced laissez-faire
economics and the cult of personal liberty [003-005].

## Historical Context

Arnold's critique of individualism reflects the mid-Victorian debate over the
role of the State. The tradition of laissez-faire, associated with figures like
John Bright and Richard Cobden, held that individual initiative — not state
action — was the engine of national progress. Arnold's insistence on the
necessity of "a strong State-power" [002-009] was a direct challenge to this
tradition, and it aligned him, uncomfortably, with continental traditions of
administrative centralisation that the English temperament instinctively
rejected [001-006].
