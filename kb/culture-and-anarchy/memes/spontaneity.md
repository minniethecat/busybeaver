# Spontaneity

The essential quality of the Hellenic mind — what Arnold calls "spontaneity of consciousness," the capacity to think freely and freshly that [[hebraism|Hebraism]]'s emphasis on conduct and obedience tends to suppress [004-004].

## Arnold's Definition

Spontaneity of consciousness is Arnold's term for the mind's natural and free activity — thinking that is not constrained by dogma, habit, or the pressure of practical action. It is the intellectual counterpart to the moral energy of [[conscience]].

Where Hebraism insists on right acting and develops the faculty of conscience to that end, Hellenism insists on right thinking and develops the faculty of spontaneity. The difference is not that one is good and the other bad, but that a complete human being needs both. Arnold's famous formulation captures this complementarity:

> "The governing idea of Hellenism is *spontaneity of consciousness*; that of Hebraism, *strictness of conscience*." [004-004]

## The Greek Heritage

The term "spontaneity" carries for Arnold the entire weight of the Greek intellectual tradition. It is the quality he finds in [[sophocles]], in [[plato]], in the Athenian democracy — a disposition to look at things directly and freshly, without the mediation of fixed systems. This is what he means by "seeing things as they really are" — the core Hellenistic discipline that modern England has forgotten.

Arnold's Hellenism is not an argument for the study of Greek in schools (though he did advocate that elsewhere). It is an argument for a certain temper of mind — flexible, undogmatic, curious, open to experience — that he associates with the Greek achievement and finds largely absent from Victorian intellectual life.

## Spontaneity and Flexibility

The practical fruit of spontaneity is flexibility — the ability to see a question from more than one side. This is precisely what the English Puritan tradition lacks. Arnold praises [[oxford]] for fostering this quality:

> "The flexibility which sweetness and light give, and which is the opposite of that rigidity which is the besetting danger of the Hebraising mind." [001-016]

For Arnold, Oxford's contribution to English life is not primarily intellectual (he is candid about its scholarly deficiencies) but spiritual and temperamental. The University's beauty and its traditions of humane learning foster a cast of mind that is the opposite of the commercial and utilitarian spirit of the age.

## The Suppression of Spontaneity

Arnold argues that modern English life systematically suppresses spontaneity of consciousness. The pressure to act, to take a position, to be "practical" — all the characteristic virtues of the commercial and industrial middle class — work against the free play of mind that Hellenism cultivates. The result is a culture that is energetic but shallow, morally serious but intellectually rigid.

The [[philistines|Philistine]] is Arnold's type of this suppression. The Philistine is not stupid or wicked; he is earnest, hardworking, and morally upright. What he lacks is spontaneity — the capacity to step outside his own assumptions and see the world from a different angle. This is why Arnold insists that the Philistine's moral seriousness, however admirable, is not enough: without the intellectual flexibility that spontaneity provides, moral energy hardens into fanaticism.

## Spontaneity in Balance

Arnold is careful not to exalt spontaneity above conscience. His ideal is not the pure Hellenist — the aesthete who pursues intellectual refinement without moral purpose — but the balanced human being in whom spontaneity and conscience cooperate. This is the point of his famous insistence that [[hellenism]] and [[hebraism]] are not antagonists but complements: "the aim and end of both Hebraism and Hellenism is, as I have said, one and the same, and this aim and end is man's perfection or salvation" [004-005].

The tragedy of English history, as Arnold tells it, is that the balance was lost — that the Reformation, the Civil War, and the triumph of Puritanism tilted the national character decisively toward Hebraism, leaving spontaneity to wither.
