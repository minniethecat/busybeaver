# Sir Thomas Bateson

Sir Thomas Bateson (1819–1890), later first Baron Deramore, was an
Anglo-Irish Conservative MP and baronet who served in the House of Commons
from 1844 to 1885. In *Culture and Anarchy*, Bateson appears not as a
biographical subject but as a sociological type: the "excess" of the
aristocratic spirit, the man in whom "the high spirit of aristocracy, its
impenetrability, defiant courage, and pride of resistance, were developed
even in excess" [002-016].

## Identity

Bateson, the son of a baronet from County Londonderry, represented
Londonderry in Parliament for much of the mid-Victorian period. He was a
staunch Conservative and a defender of Protestant and landlord interests.
Arnold, however, is not interested in Bateson's political career as such;
he takes him as a specimen. Having heard Bateson speak during the Reform
debates, Arnold applies Aristotle's doctrine of the mean to the English
aristocracy, finding Bateson the extreme case of everything the Barbarian
class embodies when untempered by intelligence.

## Role in Arnold's Argument

Bateson serves a precise structural function in Arnold's analysis of the
three great classes. If Lord Elcho represents the "beautiful and virtuous
mean" of aristocracy — high-spirited, serene, polite, yet insufficient in
light — then Bateson represents its defect carried to the point of danger.
Arnold's Aristotelian framework requires both the norm and the deviation
to make the classification work: "by observing these two we might see both
the inadequacy of aristocracy to supply the principle of authority needful
for our present wants, and the danger of its trying to supply it when it
was not really competent for the business" [002-016, 003-002].

The key move Arnold makes with Bateson is to insist that the excess is
still a kind of compliment. "To be chosen to exemplify the happy mean of a
good quality, or set of good qualities, is evidently a praise to a man;
nay, to be chosen to exemplify even their excess, is a kind of praise"
[003-004]. Bateson's resistance is not a vice per se — it is the
aristocratic virtue of independence pushed beyond its proper limit.
Arnold thus manages to anatomise the class without simple condemnation.

## Key Passages

**On the Aristotelian mean**: Arnold describes attending the Reform debates
in the House of Commons, "having heard a number of interesting speakers,
and among them Lord Elcho and Sir Thomas Bateson." Applying Aristotle, he
concludes that "Lord Elcho was exactly the perfection, or happy mean, or
virtue, of aristocracy, and Sir Thomas Bateson the excess." Bateson,
Arnold warns, was "manifestly capable, if he had his way given him, of
causing us great danger, and, indeed, of throwing the whole commonwealth
into confusion" [002-016].

**On the aristocratic spirit**: Arnold refines the typology in a later
chapter: the "perfect and virtuous mean of that fine spirit which is the
distinctive quality of aristocracies, is to be found in Lord Elcho's
chivalrous style, and its excess in Sir Thomas Bateson's turn for
resistance" [003-002].

**On Bateson and the Populace**: In a striking parallel, Arnold groups
Bateson with working-class radicals: "Mr. Bradlaugh, like Sir Thomas
Bateson and the Rev. W. Cattle, is evidently capable, if he had his head
given him, of running us all into great dangers and confusion" [002-022].
The excess of aristocracy and the excess of democracy converge on the same
point — the refusal to submit to any authority but one's own.

**On nomination and naming**: When Arnold introduces the term "Barbarians"
for the aristocratic class, he describes them as "conceived mainly as a
body moving between the two cardinal points of Lord Elcho and Sir Thomas
Bateson, but as a whole nearer to the latter than the former" [003-004].
The class as a whole, Arnold suggests, leans more toward Bateson's
resistance than Elcho's tempering — a sobering judgment on the
aristocracy's capacity for cultural leadership.

## Relationship to Arnold's Framework

Bateson is the living illustration of the Barbarian class's defining
weakness: a "turn for resistance" that, however admirable as independence,
becomes mere obstruction when unilluminated by ideas. In Arnold's tripartite
typology, the Barbarians (aristocracy) are not wicked but inadequate —
they lack the "light" that culture alone can supply. Bateson, as the
extreme case, shows what happens when this inadequacy is allowed free
rein. He is, in effect, the reductio ad absurdum of doing-as-one-likes in
its aristocratic register.

The pairing of Bateson with the Rev. W. Cattle (the excess of the
Philistine middle class) and Mr. Bradlaugh (the excess of the Populace)
creates a structural symmetry across Arnold's three classes. Each class
has its "mean" and its "excess"; Bateson's prominence in this scheme
testifies to Arnold's conviction that the aristocratic version of the
problem — the inability to receive ideas — is the form that most directly
threatens social order.
