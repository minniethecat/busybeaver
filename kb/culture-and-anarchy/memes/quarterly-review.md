The *Quarterly Review* was a conservative periodical founded in 1809 as a Tory counterpart to the Whig *Edinburgh Review*. Arnold refers to it twice in *Culture and Anarchy* — once in the Preface, where it defends Eton against his criticism, and once in Chapter I, where it serves as evidence of the "unedifying activity" of contemporary intellectual life. The *Quarterly Review* represents, for Arnold, the voice of the Barbarian class in its literary mode: well-bred, confident, and intellectually complacent.

## Nature

The *Quarterly Review* was a quarterly periodical of politics, literature, and general culture. Throughout the 19th century, it was one of the most influential periodicals in Britain, associated with the Tory Party and the established interests of Church and State. Its contributors included some of the most prominent conservative intellectuals of the period, including John Wilson Croker, Robert Southey, and Walter Scott. By the 1860s, its influence had declined somewhat from its early 19th-century peak, but it remained a significant voice of establishment opinion.

## Role in Arnold's Argument

In the Preface, Arnold notes that the *Quarterly Review* "takes up the cudgels for Eton" in response to his criticism of aristocratic education (PN P03-005). Arnold had argued that the classical education offered at Eton, while valuable in its way, failed to provide the kind of intellectual cultivation that a modern governing class required. The *Quarterly Review*'s defense of Eton is, for Arnold, entirely predictable: it is the instinctive reaction of the Barbarian class to any questioning of its institutions.

In Chapter I, Arnold returns to the *Quarterly Review* in a broader survey of contemporary intellectual life. He describes an article in the *Quarterly* on a historical subject as an example of the "unedifying activity" that passes for intellectual work in England (PN 001-001). The piece is learned, competent, and entirely without significance — it adds nothing to the stock of human perfection. This is Arnold's critique of the Barbarian intellect in its most characteristic form: technically accomplished but spiritually and culturally inert.

## Key Passages

> "The *Quarterly Review* takes up the cudgels for Eton" — defending aristocratic education against Arnold's critique (PN P03-005)

> "In the *Quarterly Review*, some little time ago, was an article of this character" — an example of unedifying intellectual activity (PN 001-001)

## Historical Context

The *Quarterly Review* was founded in 1809 by John Murray and edited for many years by John Gibson Lockhart. It was the principal Tory rival to the Whig *Edinburgh Review*, and its early contributors included some of the most famous literary names of the period. The *Quarterly* was particularly associated with the defense of the established order — the monarchy, the Church of England, the landed interest, and the ancient universities — against the forces of reform. Arnold's critique of the *Quarterly Review* is thus a critique of the intellectual culture of the Barbarian class in its most articulate form: it is learned, polished, and entirely without the "sweetness and light" that comes from the genuine pursuit of perfection. The *Quarterly Review* ceased publication in 1967.
