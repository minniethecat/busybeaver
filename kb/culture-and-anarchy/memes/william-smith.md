# Dr. William Smith

Sir William Smith (1813–1893) was a distinguished English lexicographer, classical scholar, and editor. He is best known for his series of educational reference works — particularly the *Dictionary of Greek and Roman Antiquities* (1842), the *Dictionary of Greek and Roman Biography and Mythology* (1849), and the *Dictionary of Greek and Roman Geography* (1854) — which became standard texts in Victorian education. His Latin and Greek school-books, widely adopted in English public schools and grammar schools, made him one of the most influential figures in nineteenth-century English classical education.

In *Culture and Anarchy*, [[matthew-arnold]] mentions Smith in the Introduction, in a passage that simultaneously praises his learning and satirises the self-promotional practices of Victorian publishing.

## Career

Smith began his career as a classical master at University College School, London, before turning to lexicography and educational publishing. His major dictionaries — produced with the collaboration of leading scholars of the day — established the model for the scholarly reference work in English. He served as editor of the *[[quarterly-review|Quarterly Review]]* from 1867 until his death, giving him a significant platform in Victorian intellectual life.

Smith was knighted in 1892, the year before his death, in recognition of his services to classical scholarship and education.

## The Quarterly Review Episode

Arnold's reference to Smith occurs in the Introduction to *Culture and Anarchy* (the original 1869 Preface), where Arnold is responding to critics of his remarks on English education. One such critic was [[oscar-browning|Oscar Browning]], an assistant master at Eton, who had taken issue with Arnold's observations about the "charlatanism and extravagance" in the manufacture and supply of English school-books [P03-005].

Arnold's response is characteristic in its deployment of ironic praise. Browning, Arnold notes, has argued that Dr. William Smith's school-books are "meritorious and many" — a claim Arnold does not dispute. But the comedy of the situation, as Arnold deftly points out, lies in the fact that Smith, as editor of the *Quarterly Review*, was effectively reviewing and commending his own publications:

> And as to school-books he adds, finally, that Dr. William Smith, the learned and distinguished editor of the Quarterly Review, is, as we all know, the compiler of school-books meritorious and many. [P03-005]

The italics on "we all know" are Arnold's — the irony is unmistakable. A man who edits the journal in which his own works are praised is not, Arnold implies, in the best position to offer an impartial assessment of their merit.

## Arnold's Satirical Portrait

Arnold's treatment of the Smith episode serves a larger purpose in his argument. The Introduction is concerned, in part, with the way English institutions — schools, churches, journals, political parties — tend to become closed systems of mutual congratulation. The *Quarterly Review* praising Dr. Smith's books, and Dr. Smith editing the *Quarterly Review* that does the praising, exemplifies what Arnold elsewhere calls "machinery" — the substitution of external forms and institutional relationships for genuine critical [[right-reason]].

Yet Arnold's tone is not hostile. He notes, with evident sincerity, that Smith's school-books are indeed "meritorious and many." The problem is not the books; it is the loss of critical independence that occurs when the same person occupies both the role of producer and the role of reviewer. This, Arnold suggests, is the English disease: a tendency toward complacent self-congratulation that substitutes mutual admiration for genuine [[perfection|self-examination]].

## Connection to Oscar Browning

The Smith reference is embedded in Arnold's engagement with [[oscar-browning]], whom Arnold treats with amused respect. Browning, Arnold observes, exhibits "that frankness and manly self-confidence which our great public schools, and none of them so much as Eton, are supposed to inspire" [P03-005]. This self-confidence is "contagious," Arnold adds — so much so that it has "managed to fire Dr. William Smith (himself, no doubt, the modestest man alive, and never trained at Eton) with the same spirit."

The entire passage is a miniature demonstration of Arnold's method: identifying a genuine good (Browning's frankness, Smith's scholarship) while exposing the institutional arrangements that compromise it (a journal editor reviewing his own books).
