# The Reformation

The sixteenth-century religious revolution that Arnold identifies as the pivotal moment when [[hebraism|Hebraism]] reasserted its dominance over [[hellenism|Hellenism]] in Western Christianity. For Arnold, the Reformation was both a legitimate return to the Hebraic core of the faith — a protest against the Hellenized accretions of medieval Catholicism — and the beginning of a one-sided development whose consequences England was still living with in the nineteenth century (004-012).

## Historical Background

The Reformation began with Martin Luther's Ninety-Five Theses in 1517 and unfolded over more than a century of religious conflict across Europe, culminating in the Thirty Years' War (1618–1648). In England, the Reformation took a distinct path: Henry VIII's break with Rome (1534) was primarily political, but under Edward VI and Elizabeth I a distinctly Protestant settlement emerged. The "Puritan" wing of English Protestantism — which Arnold treats as the purest expression of Hebraism — sought to purge the Church of England of all remaining "popish" elements and to establish a godly commonwealth on strictly biblical lines.

## Arnold's Historical Narrative

Arnold situates the Reformation within his grand dialectic of Hebraism and Hellenism. His narrative runs as follows:

**1. The Renaissance as Hellenic Revival.** The Renaissance of the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries brought "a return to Greek art and literature" — a revival of the Hellenic spirit after centuries of Hebraic dominance (004-006). This was "the re-entry of Hellenism into the world" (004-013), promising a balanced cultivation of all human powers.

**2. The Reformation as Hebraic Reaction.** But the Renaissance's "bright promise" was cut short. "The Reformation has been often called a Hebraising revival, a return to the spirit of the Old Testament" (004-012). That characterisation, Arnold agrees, captures something essential:

> The Reformation was the triumph of Hebraism. It was a return to the earnestness, the intensity, the concentration upon conduct, which characterised primitive Christianity. (004-012)

**3. The Cost of Triumph.** The Reformation "stamped out" the Hellenic dimension of Christianity, producing a religion of pure moral strenuousness:

> The bright promise of Hellenism faded, and Hebraism ruled the world. Then was seen the amazing spectacle of the human spirit, strictly obeying the law of its being, but chained, nevertheless, to a mechanical routine. (004-008)

## Arnold's Ambivalent Assessment

Arnold's treatment of the Reformation is notably double-edged. He does not dismiss it — the Reformation was a "legitimate" return to the Hebraic core of Christianity against a Church that had become corrupted by worldliness:

> The Protestantism which made the Reformation and which has such strength in England still, is a great and living force. (P03-024)

Yet Arnold insists that the Reformation was also a narrowing. By focusing exclusively on conduct and right belief, it lost the Renaissance vision of the whole human being — body, mind, and spirit — developing in harmony. The Puritan who emerged from the Reformation was morally serious but aesthetically impoverished, intellectually suspicious, and incapable of "the free play of the mind upon all subjects" (004-007).

## Victorian Echoes

Arnold is particularly interested in the Reformation's continuing hold on the English imagination. In the nineteenth century, English Protestants still mobilised the rhetoric of the Reformation as a political tool:

| Victorian Usage | Arnold's Response |
|----------------|-------------------|
| "The lamp of the Reformation put out!" (006-003) | Dismissed as "stock cry" — a mechanical slogan substituting for thought |
| "No Popery!" (006-003) | A "routine" objection that blocks genuine inquiry |
| Defence of the Protestant Establishment | Arnold argues that establishments can become "machinery" — means mistaken for ends |

For Arnold, these Reformation slogans exemplify the Hebraic fixation on "conduct and obedience" at the expense of fresh thinking. The English middle class, he complains, still judges every question by the standard of the sixteenth century.

## The Reformation in Arnold's European Context

Arnold contrasts the English Reformation's legacy with that of continental Protestantism. [[germany|Germany]], the original home of the Reformation, had managed — in Arnold's view — to temper its Hebraism with Hellenic breadth. German universities, German philosophy, and German literature represented a Protestant culture that had not "stamped out" the intellectual and aesthetic dimensions of life. England, by contrast, had produced "the strong, Puritan, Hebraising movement" without the counterbalancing Hellenic revival that Germany enjoyed (004-006).

## Significance in Arnold's Argument

The Reformation functions in *Culture and Anarchy* as:

- **Historical pivot**: The moment when the Renaissance's balanced vision collapsed into one-sided Hebraism
- **Genealogy of the present**: The source of England's continuing Puritan-Hebraic character
- **Cautionary tale**: A necessary reform that became an unnecessary narrowing
- **Analogy for Arnold's project**: Just as the Reformation renewed Christianity by returning to its sources, Arnold's "culture" seeks to renew English life by returning to the Hellenic ideal of balanced human development
