# John Stuart Mill

John Stuart Mill (1806–1873) was an English philosopher, political
economist, and Member of Parliament, and the most influential liberal
thinker of the Victorian era. [[matthew-arnold]] mentions Mill only
once in *Culture and Anarchy*, but the reference is charged: Arnold
groups Mill with the historian Henry Thomas Buckle as an instance of
the rationalist, system-building intelligence that "culture" exists
precisely to temper and correct [001-021].

## Identity

Mill was the eldest son of James Mill, the Scottish philosopher and
disciple of [[jeremy-bentham|Bentham]], who educated his son according to
a rigorous programme of classical and philosophical study from the age
of three. By his teens Mill had mastered Greek and Latin, logic,
political economy, and the calculus. The intellectual intensity of this
upbringing produced a crisis in his early twenties — a nervous breakdown
that he later described in his *Autobiography* (1873) as a discovery
that the utilitarian system in which he had been raised could not
account for the emotional and aesthetic dimensions of human life. His
recovery, aided by the poetry of Wordsworth and the music of Mozart,
marked a decisive turn in his thought: he became, in his own phrase, a
"reformed utilitarian," committed to refining and enlarging the narrow
rationalism of his inheritance rather than discarding it.

His major works include *A System of Logic* (1843), *Principles of
Political Economy* (1848), *On Liberty* (1859), *Utilitarianism* (1861),
and *The Subjection of Women* (1869) — the last appearing in the same
year Arnold published *Culture and Anarchy*. He served as Member of
Parliament for Westminster from 1865 to 1868, where he advocated for
women's suffrage, proportional representation, and the rights of
labour.

## Role in Arnold's Argument

Mill appears in Arnold's text not as a target of direct attack but
as a limiting case — an example of the kind of liberal rationalism
that culture must resist without wholly repudiating. Arnold writes:

> A current in people's minds sets towards new ideas; people are
> dissatisfied with their old narrow stock of Philistine ideas,
> Anglo-Saxon ideas, or any other; and some man, some Bentham or
> Comte, who has the real merit of having early and strongly felt
> and helped the new current, but who brings plenty of narrownesses
> and mistakes of his own into his feeling and help of it, is
> credited with being the author of the whole current, the fit
> person to be entrusted with its regulation and to guide the human
> race. [001-021]

Mill is not named in this sentence, but the context makes his presence
felt. Arnold has just mentioned "the late Mr. Buckle, or Mr. Mill,"
and the passage that follows — describing the tendency of system-makers
to be "credited with being the author of the whole current" — applies
with particular force to Mill, whose *System of Logic* and *Principles
of Political Economy* had shaped a generation of liberal thought.
Arnold's objection is not to Mill's intelligence or good faith but to
the danger of mistaking a partial and time-bound system for a complete
account of human flourishing. Culture, for Arnold, "is always assigning
to system-makers and systems a smaller share in the bent of human
destiny than their friends like" [001-021].

## Key Passages

The single reference to Mill in *Culture and Anarchy* occurs in a
passage where Arnold discusses the forces pressing upon the new
democratic spirit in England:

> There are many friends of the people. There are friends of the
> people for old-established parties, mixed motives, and various
> reasons. There are friends of the people whose feeling for the
> people is a mere Jacobinism, abstract, fierce, and blind. ...
> However much it may find to say in its praise — and I am far from
> denying that it has done great things in this country and
> elsewhere — there is, nevertheless, no doubt that it is a rationalism
> which is a great deal too narrow, and that it has a great deal too
> much the character of a fixed and final doctrine, for the late
> Mr. Buckle, or Mr. Mill. [001-021]

The phrase "fixed and final doctrine" is the hinge of Arnold's
criticism. For him, culture is anti-doctrinal by its nature — it is
"a study of perfection" that "moves by the force, not merely or
primarily of the scientific passion for pure knowledge, but also of
the moral and social passion for doing good" [001-002]. Against this
open-ended pursuit, any system — however noble — that presents itself
as "fixed and final" is an obstacle rather than an aid.
