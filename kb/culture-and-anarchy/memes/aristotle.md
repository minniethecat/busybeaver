# Aristotle

Aristotle (384–322 BCE) was a Greek philosopher, student of Plato, and
tutor of Alexander the Great. His works span logic, metaphysics, ethics,
politics, natural science, and poetics. Arnold invokes him three times
in *Culture and Anarchy* (1869), drawing on Aristotle's ethical doctrine
of the mean and on his observation about the fit audience for the life of
the mind.

## Role in *Culture and Anarchy*

Arnold's references to Aristotle serve two distinct rhetorical functions:
a self-deprecating appeal to classical authority, and a sombre meditation
on the natural constituency of culture.

### The "Lumber of Phrases" from Aristotle

In Chapter II, Arnold recalls his Oxford education:

> having been brought up at Oxford in the bad old times, when we were
> stuffed with Greek and Aristotle, and thought nothing of preparing
> ourselves … my head is still full of a lumber of phrases we learnt
> at Oxford from Aristotle, about virtue being in a mean, and about
> excess and defect, and so on. (PN 002-016)

The passage is characteristically ironic. Arnold appears to dismiss his
classical learning as useless "lumber" while simultaneously deploying
it to frame his argument. The reference to Aristotle's doctrine of the
mean — that virtue lies between excess and defect — is central to
Arnold's own method: culture as the pursuit of "a harmonious perfection,
a perfection in which the characters of beauty and intelligence are both
present" (Preface). Arnold seeks a mean between the excesses of Hebraism
(moral rigidity) and Hellenism (detached aestheticism), even as he
refuses to name Aristotle as the source.

### "Sweetness and Light"

In the Conclusion, Arnold quotes Aristotle to temper the expectations of
culture's advocates:

> Aristotle says that those for whom ideas and the pursuit of the
> intelligible law of things can have much attraction, are principally
> the young, filled with generous spirit and with a passion for
> perfection; but the mass of mankind, he says, follow seeming goods
> for real, bestowing hardly a thought upon true sweetness and
> light;— "and to their lives," he adds mournfully, "who can give
> another and a better rhythm?" (PN 006-042)

This passage, drawn from the *Nicomachean Ethics*, supplies Arnold with
his closing note — a melancholy realism that the love of perfection will
always be the property of a minority. Arnold does not dispute this; he
accepts it as a permanent condition that makes the work of culture all
the more necessary.

## Connection to Arnold's Argument

Aristotle represents, for Arnold, the Hellenic spirit in its purest
philosophical form: the pursuit of knowing as an end in itself. Arnold's
triangulation between Aristotle, the Old Testament prophets, and the
modern Philistine is the structural engine of the book's final chapters.
Aristotle provides a classical warrant for the proposition that "sweetness
and light" — Arnold's shorthand for the Hellenic ideal of intellectual
and aesthetic perfection — is not a modern fancy but one of the oldest
strands in Western thought.
