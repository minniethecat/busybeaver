The Church of Ireland was the established Protestant (Anglican) church in Ireland from the Reformation until its disestablishment by Gladstone's Irish Church Act 1869. In *Culture and Anarchy*, Arnold refers to the Church of Ireland primarily in the context of the movement for its disestablishment — a cause he treats as an illustration of how English Nonconformists pursue reform through moral antipathy rather than through the intellectual cultivation of Hellenism.

## Nature

The Church of Ireland was a Protestant episcopal church, part of the Anglican communion, and from the time of Henry VIII was the legally established church of Ireland. Despite its privileged position, it represented only a small minority of the Irish population — approximately 12% — with the majority being Roman Catholic and a significant minority Presbyterian. It was funded by tithes levied on the entire population, a source of deep resentment among non-Anglicans.

## Role in Arnold's Argument

Arnold discusses the Church of Ireland in the context of the Nonconformist campaign for disestablishment. His interest is not in the Church as an institution per se but in what the campaign against it reveals about the character of English reform. The Nonconformists seek to "abolish the Irish Church by the power of the Nonconformists' antipathy to religious establishments" (PN P03-014), and Arnold sees this antipathy — however morally motivated — as falling short of the Hellenic ideal of seeing things as they truly are.

Arnold does not defend the Irish establishment on its merits. His critique is directed at the intellectual quality of the reform movement, not at the institution it attacks. He contrasts what he sees as the mechanical, passion-driven pursuit of disestablishment with the kind of disinterested inquiry that culture demands.

## Historical Context

The Church of Ireland was established as the state church in 1536 during the English Reformation. Throughout its history, it struggled with the anomaly of being a minority church legally privileged over the majority Catholic population. The Tithe War of the 1830s and the growing strength of Irish nationalism made its position increasingly untenable. The Irish Church Act 1869, one of Gladstone's first major reforms, disestablished the church effective 1 January 1871, removed its entitlement to tithes, and redistributed much of its property. The disestablished church continues today as an autonomous province of the Anglican Communion.
