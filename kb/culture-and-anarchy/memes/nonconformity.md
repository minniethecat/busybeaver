**Nonconformity** refers to the tradition of English Protestant churches and communities that refused to conform to the doctrine and governance of the established [[church-of-england|Church of England]]. In [[culture-and-anarchy]], [[matthew-arnold]] subjects Nonconformity to sustained critique as the quintessential expression of English [[hebraism]] — a religious culture that emphasizes strict moral conscience at the expense of intellectual and aesthetic development.

## Historical Background

English Nonconformity emerged from the Puritan movement of the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries, crystallizing as a distinct tradition after the Act of Uniformity of 1662, which required all clergy to conform to the Book of Common Prayer. The roughly two thousand ministers who refused — known as the "Ejected" or "Nonconformist" ministers — formed the nucleus of what became the Congregational, Presbyterian, and Baptist denominations.

By Arnold's time, Nonconformity had become a substantial social and political force. The [[dissenters|Nonconformist]] conscience, particularly in the manufacturing districts of the Midlands and the North, shaped Victorian morality, politics, and public life. The Liberal Party relied heavily on Nonconformist support, and Nonconformist agitation drove many of the era's reform movements — from the abolition of church rates to the disestablishment of the [[irish-church]].

## Arnold's Critique

Arnold's relationship to Nonconformity is complex. He acknowledges the historical achievement of English Nonconformity in breaking "the yoke of ecclesiastical domination" and contributing to the moral fibre of the nation [001-016]. The men of English [[puritanism]] and Nonconformity, he concedes, "were men who were trained" in a demanding spiritual discipline [P03-009].

Yet Arnold finds Nonconformity ultimately inadequate as a cultural force. He argues that the Nonconformist churches, like the churches of English Nonconformity "after its founders departed," have narrowed into mere sectarianism, losing the intellectual breadth that would allow them to engage productively with modern life [P03-026].

> The great works by which, not only in literature, art, and science generally, but in religion itself, the human spirit has manifested its approaches to totality, and a full, harmonious perfection, and by which it stimulates and helps forward the world's general perfection, come, not from Nonconformists, but from men who either belong to Establishments or have been trained in them. [P03-009]

## Hebraism Without Hellenism

For Arnold, Nonconformity represents [[hebraism]] detached from its necessary complement, [[hellenism]]. The Nonconformist emphasis on conscience, conduct, and biblical authority — while valuable — becomes one-sided when not balanced by the Hellenic pursuit of knowledge, beauty, and "seeing things as they really are."

He writes:

> I cannot at once accept the Nonconformity any more than the industrialism which I see around me. [002-018]

Both, in his view, represent the triumph of narrow practicality over the full development of human capacities that true culture demands. The Nonconformist, Arnold suggests, has "worshipped his fetish of separatism so long that he is likely to wish still to remain, like Ephraim, 'a wild ass alone by himself'" [P03-029] — a biblical allusion that captures Arnold's diagnosis of Nonconformity as wilful isolation from the broader currents of intellectual life.

## The "Essential Part"

Arnold identifies what he calls "the essential part of Nonconformity" as "a popular church-discipline" [P03-027]. This democratic element — the insistence on the right of ordinary believers to organize their own religious life — was historically valuable. But Arnold argues that Nonconformity has failed to evolve beyond this initial insight, remaining fixated on ecclesiastical questions when it should be contributing to the broader culture.

The tragedy of Nonconformity, in Arnold's view, is that it began as a movement for spiritual freedom but ended as a party of "machinery" — substituting the external forms of church governance for the inner work of [[perfection]]. Having broken one yoke, it simply erected another.

## Nonconformity and the State

Arnold's critique of Nonconformity is inseparable from his argument for the [[state]] as a centre of authority and [[right-reason]]. The Nonconformist antipathy to state establishments of religion, Arnold argues, is not grounded in a commitment to reason and justice but in a narrow sectarian interest — "the power of the Nonconformists' antipathy to Church establishments" [006-002]. Liberal politicians, dependent on Nonconformist votes, elevate this sectarian interest into a principle of national policy, calling it "the decision of the national mind."

This analysis points toward Arnold's larger thesis: that only the state, acting as the organ of "our best self," can transcend the partialities of class and sect. Nonconformity, for all its historical virtues, remains trapped in what Arnold calls the "ordinary self" — the self of faction, interest, and unexamined prejudice.
