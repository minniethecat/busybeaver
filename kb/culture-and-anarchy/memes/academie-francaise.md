# Académie Française

The Académie Française (French Academy), founded in 1635 by Cardinal Richelieu, is the pre-eminent French learned body on matters pertaining to the French language and literature. Matthew Arnold invokes it throughout *Culture and Anarchy* as the paradigmatic example of a centralized cultural authority — something England conspicuously lacks — while simultaneously arguing against simply importing such an institution.

## Nature

The Académie is a state-sanctioned literary institution with official authority over the French language. Its forty members (les immortels) are elected for life and charged with producing an official dictionary and maintaining standards of linguistic and literary taste. For Arnold, it represents "an authoritative centre" — a concept England has no equivalent for.

## Role in Arnold's Argument

Arnold uses the French Academy as a foil in his broader argument about culture and machinery. His position is characteristically dialectical: he acknowledges that the absence of any institution like the Academy has real consequences for English intellectual life, but he equally insists that simply establishing an English Academy would be a mechanical solution that fails to address the deeper problem (003-012).

The Academy serves two functions in Arnold's rhetoric:

1. **Diagnostic**: The absence of an English Academy reveals England's deeper problem — "the want of sensitiveness of intellectual conscience, the disbelief in right reason, the dislike of authority" — not merely a missing institution (P03-004).

2. **Cautionary**: An English Academy would inevitably reproduce English defects: "One can see the happy family in one's mind's eye as distinctly as if it was already constituted. Lord Stanhope, the Bishop of Oxford, Mr. Gladstone, the Dean of Westminster, Mr. Froude, Mr. Henry Reeve" — a "brilliant and select coterie" that would collapse into factionalism (P03-004).

## Key Passages

Arnold discusses the Academy most extensively in the Preface to the 1869 edition, where he connects it to his critique of "machinery" — the English tendency to fix problems by creating external institutions rather than cultivating inward perfection:

> I have indeed expressly declared that I wanted no such thing; but let us notice how it is just our worship of machinery, and of external doing, which leads to this charge being brought; and how the inwardness of culture makes us seize, for watching and cure, the faults to which our want of an Academy inclines us, and yet prevents us from trusting to an arm of flesh, as the Puritans say,—from blindly flying to this outward machinery of an Academy, in order to help ourselves. (P03-004)

In Chapter III, he returns to the theme of authority, connecting the Academy question to the larger problem of English resistance to any standard beyond individual preference:

> I have formerly pointed out how in literature the absence of any authoritative centre, like an Academy, tends to do this; each section of the public has its own literary organ, and the mass of the public is without any suspicion that the value of these organs is relative to their being nearer a certain ideal centre of correct information, taste, and intelligence, or farther away from it. (003-012)

## Historical Context

By Arnold's time, the Académie Française had been the standard-bearer of French linguistic authority for over two centuries. Its role as arbiter of literary taste was widely recognized across Europe, making it a natural reference point for discussions about cultural authority. Arnold's contemporary readers — especially those familiar with French intellectual life — would have immediately understood what was at stake in the comparison.
