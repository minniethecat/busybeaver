# Robert Lowe

Robert Lowe (1811–1892), later Viscount Sherbrooke, was a Liberal statesman
who served as Chancellor of the Exchequer under Gladstone (1868–1873). An
opponent of the 1867 Reform Act — he famously warned that extending the
franchise would require "to prevail on our future masters to learn their
letters" — Lowe was one of the most intellectually formidable parliamentarians
of his generation. In *Culture and Anarchy*, Arnold repeatedly invokes Lowe
not to dispute his ability but to expose the cultural assumptions underlying
his political rhetoric. Lowe is Arnold's exemplary Philistine statesman:
energetic, able, and entirely unaware of his own limitations.

## Identity

Lowe was an albino with severely impaired eyesight, yet he rose to the top
of British politics through sheer intellectual force. Educated at Winchester
and University College, Oxford, he was a fellow of Magdalen College before
emigrating to Australia, where he practised law and served in the New South
Wales Legislative Council. Returning to England in 1850, he entered
Parliament as a Liberal and quickly established himself as a formidable
debater. His opposition to the 1867 Reform Bill cost him his seat, but
Gladstone brought him into the Cabinet as Chancellor the following year.

## Role in Arnold's Argument

Arnold uses Lowe as a specimen of what happens when intelligence is
detached from culture. Lowe is manifestly clever; his speeches are
"celebrated" and widely admired. But his cleverness operates entirely
within the framework of middle-class assumptions — it never questions the
value of the machinery it celebrates. "Mr. Lowe talks to us of this strong
middle-class, with an admiration that is perfectly sincere," Arnold
observes, "and even with a sort of wonder" [002-017]. The problem is not
insincerity but shallowness. Lowe genuinely believes that material
prosperity and political freedom are sufficient ends, and because he
believes this so intelligently, he is a more dangerous spokesman for
Philistinism than a less able advocate would be.

### The Edinburgh Speech

Arnold pays particular attention to Lowe's speech at Edinburgh, in which
the Chancellor celebrated the achievements of the middle class. "After
Mr. Lowe's great speech at Edinburgh we were all, I remember, talking about
it and applauding it" [002-016]. Arnold's response is to ask what the
speech left out — everything, he suggests, that makes life worth living
beyond the counting-house.

### Lowe and "Doing as One Likes"

In Chapter III, Arnold quotes Lowe's description of the working class as
"drunken and venal" [003-014]. Arnold does not dispute the factual accuracy
of this characterisation; what troubles him is that Lowe offers no standard
by which the working class might be judged beyond the standard of
middle-class respectability. Lowe's dismissal of the populace is the mirror
image of his celebration of the middle class — both reflect the same
incapacity to see beyond class interest.

## Key Passages

**On middle-class achievement**: "The achievements which fill Mr. Lowe
with such inexpressible admiration" [001-019] are, Arnold suggests,
achievements of a very limited kind — material and political rather than
spiritual and intellectual.

**On the Edinburgh speech**: Arnold recalls "the general chorus of
laudation with which Mr. Roebuck or Mr. Lowe debauch the minds of the
middle classes" [001-020]. The word "debauch" is significant: it implies
that flattery of the middle class is not merely inaccurate but morally
corrupting.

**On the strong middle-class**: "Mr. Lowe talks to us of this strong
middle-class … with his never-failing ingenuity" [002-017]. The phrase
"never-failing ingenuity" is barbed — it suggests that Lowe's cleverness
is deployed in the service of an inadequate ideal.

## Relationship to Arnold's Framework

Lowe is the political embodiment of Philistinism at its most intellectually
respectable. He is not ignorant or crude; he is a man of genuine ability.
But his ability is wholly absorbed by the world of machinery — Free Trade,
Parliamentary Reform, material prosperity — and he has no sense that any
higher standard might exist. He represents precisely the deficiency that
Arnold's "culture" is designed to remedy: the absence of "sweetness and
light" in the governing class.
