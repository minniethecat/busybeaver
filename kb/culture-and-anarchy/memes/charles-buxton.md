# Charles Buxton

Charles Buxton (1822–1871) was a British Liberal Member of Parliament and author. [[matthew-arnold]] repeatedly names him alongside [[william-edward-baxter]] as a quintessential example of the politician who lends philosophical respectability to popular sentiment rather than challenging it with [[right-reason]] [P03-008].

## Identity

Buxton was the son of Sir Thomas Fowell Buxton, the noted anti-slavery campaigner. He served as Liberal MP for several constituencies and wrote on political and social questions. Arnold's references to him are always in the context of his role as a "philosophical politician" aligned with the Nonconformist antipathy to church establishments.

## Role in Arnold's Argument

Arnold uses Buxton as shorthand for a particular type of Victorian public intellectual — one who philosophises not to discover truth but to dignify what people already believe:

> "And we see philosophical politicians, with a turn for swimming with the stream, like Mr. Baxter or Mr. Charles Buxton... seeking to give a sort of grand stamp of generality and solemnity to this antipathy of the Nonconformists." [P03-008]

The metaphor of "swimming with the stream" recurs throughout Arnold's references to Buxton. For Arnold, this is the antithesis of [[culture]], which requires the courage to resist the current.

## The Political Philosopher

In Chapter VI, Arnold groups Buxton among those who give intellectual colouring to the national mood:

> "There were found political philosophers, like Mr. Baxter and Mr. Charles Buxton, to give it a look of more general and philosophical character." [006-002]

In the Preface, Arnold returns to this pairing, observing that there will always be thinkers like Buxton "to swim with the stream, but to swim with it philosophically; to call the desires of the ordinary self of any great section of the community the 'general conscience'" [P03-029].

## Relationship to Arnold's Framework

Buxton represents for Arnold the problem of the [[ordinary-self]] in politics. Rather than elevate public discourse toward the [[best-self]], Buxton and his kind validate the inclinations that people already have. This is the characteristic failing of [[philistines|philistinism]] in its intellectual form: not ignorance, but the misuse of intelligence in service of comfortable conclusions.

Buxton also illustrates the alliance between [[liberalism]] and [[nonconformists]] that Arnold saw as a defining feature of Victorian political culture — an alliance he regarded as energetic but spiritually and intellectually shallow.
