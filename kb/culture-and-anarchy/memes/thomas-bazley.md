# Thomas Bazley

Sir Thomas Bazley (1797–1885) was a Manchester cotton manufacturer and
Liberal Member of Parliament who served as president of the Manchester
Chamber of Commerce. Arnold invokes him as a representative of the
industrial middle class — prosperous, earnest, and, in Arnold's
estimation, entirely devoid of the "sweetness and light" that
distinguishes true culture from mere material success.

## Role in *Culture and Anarchy*

Bazley appears in *Culture and Anarchy* (1869) as a specimen of
Philistinism in its most concentrated form. Arnold assigns him to a
structural role in the book's typology of English classes.

### "The Representation of Manchester"

Arnold introduces Bazley with devastating brevity:

> the actual representation of Manchester, Mr. Bazley. Mr. Bazley
> sums up for us, in fact, the middle-class, its spirit and its
> character (PN 002-019)

To be "the representation of Manchester" is, in Arnold's lexicon, to
be the embodiment of industrial England at its most philistine —
energetic, practical, wealthy, and blind to the life of the mind.
Bazley's parliamentary speeches on behalf of free trade and
manufacturing interests are, for Arnold, exercises in the mechanical
reasoning of the "ordinary self": they calculate profit but never ask
what profit is *for*.

### "Two Cardinal Points"

In Chapter III, Arnold identifies what Bazley and his class hold
sacred:

> We see in Mr. Bazley … the two cardinal points of middle-class
> Liberalism: Free-trade, and unrestricted competition (PN 003-004)

The phrase "two cardinal points" is ironic — Arnold substitutes the
theological language of compass bearings for what are, in his view,
merely the economic interests of a single class elevated to the
status of universal principles. Bazley's Liberalism is not a
philosophy but a rationalisation of class advantage.

### "They Shall Sit on Thrones"

Arnold's most pointed observation comes in the same chapter:

> they will sit on thrones with Mr. Bazley and other middle-class
> potentates (PN 003-007)

The image of Bazley enthroned is deliberately incongruous — the
cotton manufacturer as king. Arnold's point is that the middle class,
having achieved economic dominance, now seeks cultural and political
dominion without having first acquired the inward cultivation that
would make such dominion legitimate.

## Connection to Arnold's Argument

Bazley is the Philistine par excellence: industrious, wealthy,
politically engaged, and yet — in Arnold's judgment — unfit to
govern, because he mistakes the machinery of prosperity for the
substance of civilisation. His career illustrates Arnold's central
claim that the middle class, left to its "ordinary self," cannot
supply the "centre of light and authority" that a healthy society
requires.
