# Deborah Butler

Deborah Butler was an American religious figure associated with the mid-
nineteenth-century spiritualist movement in the United States. Very little
is known of her life outside Arnold's references in *Culture and Anarchy*
(1869), where she appears twice as an emblem of the uncritical reverence
that democratic culture accords to minor sectarian figures.

## Role in Arnold's Argument

Butler appears solely within Arnold's critique of Hepworth Dixon's writings
on American religious movements. Arnold's concern is not with Butler
herself — about whom he offers no biographical detail — but with the cultural
habit of treating marginal religious figures as if they carried the weight
of intellectual and spiritual authorities.

### Proclaiming "the New Covenant"

In Chapter III, Arnold quotes Dixon's breathless description of a religious
gathering:

> "Such are, in brief, the bases of what Newman Weeks, Sarah Horton,
> Deborah Butler, and the associated brethren, proclaimed in Rolt's Hall as
> the new covenant!" If he was summing up an account of the teaching of
> Plato or St. Paul, Mr. Hepworth Dixon could not be more earnestly
> reverential. (PN 003-013)

Arnold's objection is to proportionality. To announce a new religious
dispensation from "Rolt's Hall" — a rented meeting room — with the same
solemnity one would use for the *Republic* or the Epistles is, for Arnold,
a category error. It is not that Butler and her associates are insincere;
it is that the culture has lost the capacity to distinguish between the
weighty and the trivial.

### Butler Among the Bathos

Arnold names Butler again in his catalogue of the "infinite variety of
experiments" that characterises modern religious life:

> the British Banner, Judge Edmonds, Newman Weeks, Deborah Butler, Elderess
> Polly, Brother Noyes, the Rev. W. Cattle, the Licensed Victuallers, the
> Commercial Travellers, and I know not how many more; and the numbers of
> this noble army are swelling every day. (PN 003-019)

Arnold's irony in calling this "a noble army" is unmistakable. Butler is
swept into a list that juxtaposes religious enthusiasts with publicans and
salesmen — the implication being that, in the absence of authoritative
standards, all claims to spiritual truth become indistinguishable.

## Connection to Arnold's Framework

Deborah Butler is a minor figure in Arnold's argument, but she serves an
important structural function. She is one of the concrete instances through
which Arnold diagnoses what he calls "the natural taste for the bathos" —
the democratic tendency to treat all opinions, all leaders, and all
doctrines as being of equal weight. Arnold's remedy — the cultivation of "a
best self and right reason which may stand as a serious authority" — is
precisely what would allow a culture to distinguish between a Deborah Butler
and a St. Paul, and to assign each the proportionate attention they deserve.
