# Frederick Robertson

Frederick William Robertson (1816–1853), known as "Robertson of Brighton," was an English Anglican divine and one of the most influential preachers of the Victorian era. In *Culture and Anarchy*, [[matthew-arnold|Matthew Arnold]] praises Robertson's rhetorical gifts while using one of his sermons to illustrate a characteristic error in Victorian thinking about [[hellenism|Hellenism]] [004-003].

## Identity

Robertson was educated at Brasenose College, Oxford, and ordained into the Church of England in 1840. After a curacy in Winchester and a brief, troubled incumbency in Cheltenham — where his Broad Church sympathies alienated his Evangelical congregation — he was appointed to Trinity Chapel, Brighton, in 1847. There he developed the style of preaching that made him famous: earnest, psychologically acute, and unafraid to engage with contemporary intellectual questions.

His early death at 37 from a brain disease gave his reputation a romantic cast. His sermons, published posthumously, went through numerous editions and were read throughout the English-speaking world. By the 1860s, when Arnold was writing, Robertson was widely regarded as one of the great preachers of the age.

## In Culture and Anarchy

Arnold discusses Robertson in Chapter IV ("[[hebraism-and-hellenism|Hebraism and Hellenism]]"), citing a sermon on Greece as an example of how even gifted Victorian thinkers misunderstand the Greek spirit:

> "There is a sermon on Greece and the Greek spirit by a man never to be mentioned without interest and respect, Frederick Robertson, in which this rhetorical use of Greece and the Greek spirit, and the inadequate exhibition of them, is almost ludicrous..." [004-003]

Arnold's criticism is specific and carefully framed. The word "rhetorical" is the key: Robertson treats Greece as a mere rhetorical foil — a symbol of aesthetic refinement — rather than engaging with the genuine Hellenistic ideal of "seeing things as they really are." For Arnold, this superficial treatment of Greece exemplifies the Victorian failure to grasp Hellenism as a serious intellectual and spiritual discipline.

## Arnold's Admiration and Critique

Arnold's tone is notably respectful — Robertson is "never to be mentioned without interest and respect" — even as the criticism is sharp. This dual register reflects Arnold's broader method: acknowledging the moral seriousness of Victorian religious culture (Robertson's sermons were widely admired for their earnestness) while insisting that it falls short of the full human ideal.

The structure of Arnold's judgment is characteristic. Robertson is praised for what he is (a serious, gifted preacher) and criticised for what he does not have (genuine understanding of the Greek spirit). This is the same pattern Arnold applies to the [[philistines|Philistines]] as a class: they are not wicked or stupid, but they lack [[culture]] — the discipline of seeing things as they really are.

## Robertson and the Victorian Religious Mind

Robertson's case is instructive because it shows that Arnold's critique of Victorian religion is not a simple matter of "Hebraism bad, Hellenism good." Robertson, a Broad Churchman with genuine intellectual interests, was precisely the sort of clergyman Arnold might have been expected to approve. His failure was not dogmatic narrowness (he was widely accused of doctrinal unsoundness by Evangelicals) but intellectual shallowness — the inability to treat Greek thought as anything more than a source of rhetorical ornament.

This is perhaps the most devastating form of Arnold's critique: not that Victorians are wrong about Hellenism, but that they do not even understand what it would mean to be right about it. Robertson's sermon on Greece is "almost ludicrous" not because it is false but because it misses the point entirely — it uses Greece as decoration when Arnold wants it used as discipline.

## Significance

Robertson serves Arnold's argument in two ways. First, he represents the best of Victorian religious culture — earnest, eloquent, and morally serious. Second, his failure to truly understand Greece demonstrates Arnold's central thesis: that [[hebraism|Hebraism]] without [[hellenism|Hellenism]], however admirable, remains incomplete.

The Robertson passage also illustrates Arnold's characteristic generosity toward serious error. He does not dismiss Robertson; he respects him while showing his limitations. This is the temper of the critic rather than the polemicist — and it is precisely this temper that Arnold associates with Hellenism itself.
