# Plato

The ancient Greek philosopher whom Arnold repeatedly invokes as the supreme representative of the [[hellenism|Hellenic]] spirit — the thinker who first articulated the ideal of seeing "the true, firm, intelligible law of things" that stands at the heart of Arnold's concept of culture. Arnold places Plato in a lineage that runs from Athens through the Renaissance to modern [[germany|Germany]], presenting him as the classical counterweight to the Hebraic dominance of English life (004-006).

## Identity

Plato (c. 428–348 BCE) was a student of Socrates and the teacher of Aristotle, the founder of the Academy in Athens, and the author of some thirty philosophical dialogues that laid the foundations of Western philosophy. For Arnold, Plato represents not a specific doctrine but a disposition — the Hellenic instinct to subject every question to the free play of intelligence, to seek "the true, firm, intelligible law of things" (005-002) rather than to rest content with conventional answers.

## Arnold's Plato

Arnold's Plato is a figure whose significance is more cultural than doctrinal. He cites Plato not as a source of metaphysical propositions but as an embodiment of the Hellenic attitude toward life — the commitment to seeing things as they really are, regardless of where that seeing leads:

> The great concern of Hellenism is the free play of the mind upon all subjects, the flexibility which keeps a man from losing sight of the real aim of life. (004-007)

Plato's dialogues, with their restless questioning, their refusal to settle for easy answers, and their insistence that the unexamined life is not worth living, exemplify this Hellenic commitment.

## Key References in Arnold's Text

### The Transition from Hellenism to Hebraism

Arnold identifies the historical shift from classical philosophy to Christianity — "from Plato to St. Paul" — as the decisive moment in the relationship between the two spiritual forces:

> From Hellenism to Hebraism, from Plato to St. Paul, one feels inclined to quote the saying of the ancient philosopher: "The friend of both is the friend of neither." (004-007)

This is not a rejection of either tradition but an acknowledgement of their differing orientations. Plato and Paul represent distinct, equally legitimate responses to the human condition.

### The True, Firm, Intelligible Law

Arnold finds in Plato the classical expression of the Hellenic instinct:

> The instinct for what Plato calls the true, firm, intelligible law of things — the instinct for knowing. (005-002)

This Platonic formula captures Arnold's own understanding of what culture seeks: not mere information or taste, but genuine insight into the nature of things.

### The Desire That Runs Through All Reality

In one of his most lyrical passages, Arnold borrows from Plato to describe the deepest human longing:

> The desire which, as Plato says, "for ever through all the universe pursues the good." (005-010)

This eternal aspiration toward the good is, for Arnold, the common root of both Hebraism and Hellenism — the shared ground on which a synthesis might be built.

### Plato and Socrates in English Misunderstanding

Arnold uses a reference to the popular perception of Socrates and Plato to illustrate English intellectual narrowness:

> The Englishman, in his parliamentary debates, thinks that Socrates and Plato were talking nonsense under pretence of teaching geometry. (001-021)

This wry observation captures Arnold's critique of English Philistinism — the tendency to dismiss as impractical nonsense anything that does not yield immediate, measurable results.

## Plato and Bishop Wilson: An Unlikely Parallel

Arnold draws an implicit but significant parallel between Plato and [[bishop-wilson|Bishop Wilson]]. Both represent, in their different idioms, the commitment to following "the best light one has." Where Plato speaks of the intelligible law of things, Wilson speaks of "the light of the understanding" as "the eye of the soul" (006-017). Arnold's pairing of the pagan philosopher with the Christian bishop is a deliberate demonstration that the Hellenic and Hebraic spirits can converge.

## Plato in the German Tradition

Arnold associates the modern revival of Platonic-Hellenic thinking with Germany. He invokes "Lessing and Herder in Germany, at the end of the last century" (001-024) as figures who renewed the free play of intelligence against dogma. [[germany|Germany]] represents, for Arnold, the nineteenth-century home of the Hellenic spirit — the nation where Plato's legacy of critical, disinterested inquiry has been most fully taken up. This German connection is central to Arnold's argument that England needs a dose of continental intellectual seriousness.

## Significance in Arnold's Framework

Plato serves Arnold as:

| Role | Significance |
|------|-------------|
| **Source of the Hellenic ideal** | "The true, firm, intelligible law of things" is the classical statement of culture's goal |
| **Symbol of intellectual freedom** | Plato's dialogues model the free play of mind against which Arnold measures English narrowness |
| **Bridge to Germany** | Plato → Renaissance → Lessing/Herder/Goethe → modern culture |
| **Contrast to English Philistinism** | The English dismissal of Plato as "talking nonsense" exemplifies the Hebraic suspicion of free thought |
| **Ally of Hebraism at its best** | Plato's pursuit of the good parallels Wilson's pursuit of God's will |
