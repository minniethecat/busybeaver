# Ordinary Self

The "ordinary self" is the foil to Arnold's "best self" — it is the self
we inhabit by default, shaped by appetite, class position, and the
"commonness" of everyday desire. Arnold's political psychology turns on the
distinction between these two selves, and his indictment of Victorian
England is that it has mistaken the ordinary self for the whole of human
nature, building its institutions on a foundation of sand.

## Arnold's Definition

Arnold defines the ordinary self negatively, as what remains when culture
has not yet done its work. "We find no basis for a firm State-power in our
ordinary selves; culture suggests one to us in our best self" [002-024].
The ordinary self is not evil — it is simply personal rather than
impersonal, partial rather than whole. It likes and dislikes, wants and
fears, but it cannot supply "a serious principle of authority" because it
is always the self of someone in particular — a Barbarian, a Philistine, a
Populace — never the self that is "united, impersonal, at harmony" [002-024].

## Usage Across the Text

### The Class-Coloured Self

Arnold's most sustained analysis of the ordinary self comes in his
discussion of class. Each class, he argues, projects its ordinary self
onto the political stage. The aristocracy asserts its "exquisite
externalness of manners"; the middle class its "industry and energy"; the
working class its raw collective force — and each mistakes its ordinary
self for a universal standard [003-007, 003-010]. "In almost all who have
it," Arnold writes of the best self, "it is mixed with some infusion of
the spirit of an ordinary self, some quantity of class-instinct, and even,
as has been shown, of more than one class-instinct at the same time"
[003-011].

### The Ordinary Self and "Doing as One Likes"

The ordinary self is the psychological substrate of Arnold's master-target:
"doing as one likes." "What one's ordinary self likes," Arnold observes
dryly, "is what one's ordinary self in general likes, — the common
tendency of one's class" [003-010]. The Victorian cult of liberty is, on
this reading, simply the elevation of the ordinary self into a political
principle — the belief that freedom consists in letting each person's
ordinary self do whatever it happens to desire [003-012].

### The Difficulty of Transcending It

Arnold is not optimistic about the ease with which the ordinary self can be
overcome. "It is clear," he writes, "that the very absence of any powerful
authority amongst us, and the prevalent doctrine of the duty and happiness
of doing as one likes, and asserting our personal liberty, must tend to
prevent the erection" of the best self into an effective authority
[003-012]. The ordinary self is self-reinforcing: the more it is indulged,
the harder it becomes to see any alternative to it. "We do not attempt to
give to our ordinary self, as we have it in action, predominant authority,
and to impose its rule upon other people; but it is not certain that our
ordinary self would not benefit by taking this advice" [003-012].

### The Ordinary Self in Politics

The practical consequence, Arnold argues, is that English politics operates
without any standard higher than the ordinary self of whichever class
happens to be ascendant. "We get no suggestions of right reason, and no
rebukes of our ordinary self, from our governors," he complains [003-017].
Instead, a "philosophical theory" has grown up — the doctrine of
laissez-faire and representative government — that makes a virtue of
leaving the ordinary self unchallenged [003-017–019].

## Historical Context

Arnold's ordinary self/best self distinction belongs to a nineteenth-century
debate about the nature of the self under conditions of democracy. John
Stuart Mill's "individuality" (in *On Liberty*, 1859) and Ralph Waldo
Emerson's "Self-Reliance" (1841) both asserted the sufficiency of the
ordinary self; Arnold's innovation was to argue that the ordinary self was
constitutionally incapable of supplying its own standard. The distinction
also echoes the Pauline "old man"/"new man" contrast, which Arnold, as the
son of a clergyman, would have absorbed from childhood [002-024–026].
