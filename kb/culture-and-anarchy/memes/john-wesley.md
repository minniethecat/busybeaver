# John Wesley

John Wesley (1703–1791) was an English cleric, theologian, and evangelist who founded Methodism. [[matthew-arnold]] cites him in the Preface to *Culture and Anarchy* as a key witness to the argument that the most fruitful figures of English [[nonconformists|Nonconformity]] were men trained within the Established Church [P03-009]. Arnold's interest is structural: Wesley illustrates the thesis that [[culture]] and totality depend on contact with the main current of national life.

## Identity

Wesley was an ordained priest of the [[church-of-england|Church of England]], educated at Christ Church, Oxford. His evangelical conversion in 1738 and his itinerant preaching produced a revival that eventually separated from the Establishment he never formally left. For Arnold, Wesley belonged to an earlier generation of Nonconformist energy: not the sectarian separatism of Victorian Dissenters, but a movement still drawing its vitality from the national Establishment.

## Role in Arnold's Argument

Arnold invokes Wesley to argue that Nonconformity severed from the Establishment loses the capacity to produce men of national significance. Wesley appears alongside Milton and Baxter to clinch the case: the most "fruitful men of English Puritanism and Nonconformity" were all reared within the pale of the Church of England [P03-009].

Arnold's second reference concerns religious sincerity. He observes that the Nonconformist assumption of superiority over Roman Catholic devotion is unwarranted, remarking that "Whitfield and Wesley were not at all better than St. Francis" in religious devotion [P03-027]. The point is not to diminish Wesley but to humble sectarian complacency.

## Key Passages

Arnold's primary reference to Wesley is in his enumeration of the exceptions to Nonconformist provincialism:

> The fruitful men of English Puritanism and Nonconformity are men who were trained within the pale of the Establishment,—Milton, Baxter, Wesley. A generation or two outside the Establishment, and Puritanism produces men of national mark no more. [P03-009]

The Establishment is the soil in which such figures grow; outside it the same tradition yields only narrowness.

A second passage groups Wesley with his fellow evangelist George Whitefield in a broader argument about religious humility:

> One of the best wishes one could form for Mr. Spurgeon or Father Jackson is, that they might be permitted to learn on this side the grave (for if they do not, a considerable surprise is certainly reserved for them on the other) that Whitfield and Wesley were not at all better than St. Francis, and that they themselves are not at all better than Lacordaire. [P03-027]

Religious devotion, Arnold insists, confers no monopoly on truth; the Methodist evangelist and the Roman Catholic saint stand on equal ground.

## Relationship to Arnold's Framework

Wesley embodies the tension between [[hebraism|Hebraism]] and [[hellenism|Hellenism]] at the centre of Arnold's argument. Profoundly Hebraic — driven by conscience and the imperative of conduct — his fruitfulness nonetheless depended on formation within an Establishment that kept him in contact with the broader currents of national life. The Establishment is a safeguard against the tendency of Hebraism to become "absorbing and tyrannous" [P03-011]. Once Methodism separated from the Church of England, it descended into the "hole-and-corner" existence of sectarian religion.

Wesley thus stands as a witness to Arnold's conviction that the [[establishment|Establishment]] principle is essential to the cultivation of totality. To follow Wesley, the Nonconformist must reckon with the fact that Wesley himself was made by the very institution his spiritual descendants repudiate.
