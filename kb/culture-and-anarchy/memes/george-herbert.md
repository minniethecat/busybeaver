# George Herbert

George Herbert (1593–1633) was an English metaphysical poet and Anglican priest. Matthew Arnold quotes him in *Culture and Anarchy* to illustrate the self-denying character of Christian Hebraism — the ideal of renunciation that Christianity offered to the ancient world.

## Identity

Herbert was a Welsh-born poet and orator who served as a parish priest in Bemerton, near Salisbury. His major work, *The Temple* (1633), is a collection of devotional poems known for their spiritual depth, formal ingenuity, and the tension between worldly ambition and religious devotion.

## Role in Arnold's Argument

Arnold invokes Herbert's line "my Saviour banished joy" as a literary monument to the Hebraistic ideal of self-sacrifice that conquered the Greco-Roman world (154-155). In Arnold's framework, Herbert's verse captures the essential spirit of Hebraism: the rejection of the "alma Venus, the life-giving and joy-giving power of nature" in favor of "conformity to the image of a self-sacrificing example."

## Key Passages

Arnold writes that "to a world stricken with moral enervation Christianity offered its spectacle of an inspired self-sacrifice; to men who refused themselves nothing, it showed one who refused himself everything — 'my Saviour banished joy' says George Herbert" (154-155). This passage appears in Chapter IV, where Arnold traces the historical triumph of Hebraism over Hellenism.

## Relationship to Arnold's Framework

Herbert represents the spiritual and attractive side of Hebraism — not the narrow, mechanical rule-following that Arnold critiques, but the genuine impulse toward self-conquest and moral seriousness. Arnold places Herbert's line alongside the Epistles of St. Paul, St. Augustine's *Confessions*, and the *Imitation of Christ* as one of the "literary monuments" to the Hebraistic pursuit of perfection through self-renunciation.
