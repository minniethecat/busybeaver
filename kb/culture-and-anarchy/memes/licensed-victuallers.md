The Licensed Victuallers were the organized trade of publicans — proprietors of licensed drinking establishments — who feature memorably in Chapter III of *Culture and Anarchy* as one of Arnold's examples of Philistine organizations attempting to shape public life. Arnold uses them, alongside the Commercial Travellers and other interest groups, to illustrate the fragmentation of English society into competing class interests, each pursuing its own narrow ends under the banner of reform.

## Nature

The Licensed Victuallers were a trade association representing the interests of publicans and the licensed drinks trade. In an era when temperance movements were gaining strength and the social role of the public house was increasingly contested, the Licensed Victuallers organized to protect their members' commercial interests and to project an image of social responsibility through educational and charitable initiatives.

## Role in Arnold's Argument

Arnold places the Licensed Victuallers in a satirical catalogue of organizations that, in his view, mistake their own class or trade interest for a universal interest. He describes the spectacle of the "Licensed Victuallers or the Commercial Travellers" proposing to "make a school for their children" — an ostensibly good intention that, examined closely, reveals the self-interested character of Philistine philanthropy (PN 003-015). Each trade group, Arnold implies, wants a school that will reproduce its own values rather than one that will cultivate the best self through genuine culture.

The Licensed Victuallers reappear in Arnold's roll-call of the strange coalition of interests that characterizes modern English life: "Judge Edmonds, Newman Weeks, Deborah Butler, Elderess Polly, Brother Noyes, the Rev. W. Cattle, the Licensed Victuallers, the Commercial Travellers, and I know not how many more personages" (PN 003-019). In this catalogue, the Licensed Victuallers sit alongside religious enthusiasts and spiritualists — all manifestations, in Arnold's eyes, of the "anarchy" of doing as one likes.

## Key Passages

> "The Licensed Victuallers or the Commercial Travellers propose to make a school for their children — and what things are accustomed to play!" (PN 003-015)

## Historical Context

The licensed victualling trade was a significant economic and political force in Victorian Britain. Public houses were central to working-class social life, and the trade's organization — formalized in bodies like the Licensed Victuallers' Protection Society and the Licensed Victuallers' National Defence League — exercised considerable political influence, particularly in resisting temperance legislation and licensing restrictions. Arnold's satirical treatment reflects the educated class's suspicion of trade-based political organization, seeing in it the replacement of disinterested public reason by the clash of private interests.
