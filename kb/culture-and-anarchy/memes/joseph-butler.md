# Joseph Butler

Joseph Butler (1692–1752) was an English bishop, theologian, and moral
philosopher whose *Analogy of Religion* (1736) and *Fifteen Sermons
Preached at the Rolls Chapel* (1726) established him as one of the most
penetrating intellects in the Anglican tradition. [[matthew-arnold]]
references Butler only once in *Culture and Anarchy*, but the reference
occurs at a pivotal moment in the Preface — Arnold's extended defence
of the Established Church against its Nonconformist critics — and carries
structural weight far beyond its brevity. Butler appears as part of a
triad with Richard Hooker and Isaac Barrow: three Anglican thinkers
whose existence is, for Arnold, sufficient to settle the argument about
whether an Establishment of religion serves the higher life of a nation.
Arnold's appeal to Butler is an appeal to the idea that the pursuit of
[[culture]], understood as the harmonious perfection of our whole being,
has historically been better served by the comprehensive, disinterested
institutions of a national church than by the "hole-and-corner"
communities of separatism.

## Identity

Butler was born in Wantage, Berkshire, into a Presbyterian family —
a detail that sharpens Arnold's point, since Butler himself was formed
by the Established Church he entered rather than the Nonconformity of
his upbringing. He studied at Oriel College, Oxford, and rose through the
Anglican hierarchy to become Bishop of Bristol (1738) and later Bishop
of Durham (1750). His philosophical reputation rests principally on two
works: the *Fifteen Sermons* (1726), which develop a sophisticated moral
psychology built around the concept of conscience as a reflective
principle superior to self-love and benevolence; and the *Analogy of
Religion* (1736), a reasoned defence of natural and revealed religion
against deist critics, which remained a standard text at Oxford and
Cambridge for more than a century. Butler's prose is dense and exacting —
he was said to have refused to publish anything he had not rewritten
at least seven times — and his work influenced later moralists from
David Hume to John Henry Newman.

## Role in Arnold's Argument

Arnold deploys Butler in a single but structurally important passage
in the Preface. Having established that the Nonconformists suffer from
"provincialism" — a narrowness and one-sidedness that "thwarts the full
perfection of our humanity" — Arnold turns to the question of whether
religious establishments, for all their defects, have nevertheless
contributed something indispensable to the intellectual and moral life
of the nation [P03-008–009]. His argument is that "the great works by
which, not only in literature, art, and science generally, but in
religion itself, the human spirit has manifested its approaches to
totality" come not from separatist communities but "from men who either
belong to Establishments or have been trained in them" [P03-009].

Butler is the culminating name in this argument. Arnold arrays him
alongside Richard Hooker (the great apologist of the Elizabethan
Church) and Isaac Barrow (the Cambridge mathematician and divine) as
irrefutable evidence that an Establishment has "done more to moralise
and ennoble English statesmen and their conduct" than the communities
that produced the Nonconformist divines [P03-009]. The three names
ascend chronologically and rhetorically — Hooker for the sixteenth
century, Barrow for the seventeenth, Butler for the eighteenth — so
that the Established Church is seen to have produced across three
centuries a continuous tradition of thinkers whose weight and
comprehensiveness Nonconformity cannot match. Where Hooker provides
the architectural defence of the Church's order, and Barrow supplies
the mathematical and homiletic brilliance, Butler furnishes the moral
and philosophical depth: the reflective, disinterested examination of
conscience that is, for Arnold, the very temper of culture.

Arnold's argument gains additional force from Butler's biography. Butler
was born a Presbyterian but educated and elevated within the Church
of England. He exemplifies, along with Milton, Baxter, and Wesley —
all "trained within the pale of the Establishment" [P03-009] — the
pattern Arnold perceives: that the Establishment nurtures men of
national significance, while Nonconformity, after a generation or two
outside the Establishment's embrace, ceases to produce them.

## Key Passages

Arnold's only direct reference to Butler in *Culture and Anarchy*
appears in the Preface, in the context of his sustained reply to the
Rev. Edward White's claim that Nonconformist communities exert as much
"moral and ennobling influence upon the conduct of statesmen" as the
Established Church:

> A lover of perfection, who looks to inward ripeness for the true
> springs of conduct, will surely think that as Shakspeare has done
> more for the inward ripeness of our statesmen than Dr. Watts, and
> has, therefore, done more to moralise and ennoble them, so an
> Establishment which has produced Hooker, Barrow, Butler, has done
> more to moralise and ennoble English statesmen and their conduct
> than communities which have produced the Nonconformist divines.
> [P03-009]

The passage is remarkable for its rhetorical structure. The analogy
between literature (Shakespeare vs. Watts) and religion (the Anglican
triad vs. Nonconformist divines) establishes a common standard across
domains: it is inward ripeness, not outward machinery, that ennobles
conduct. Butler's place as the third term in the ascending triad
suggests that the Establishment's intellectual productivity is not a
historical accident but an enduring pattern, culminating in the
philosophical sophistication of eighteenth-century Anglicanism.

## Relationship to Arnold's Framework

Butler's significance for Arnold's framework extends well beyond the
single sentence that names him. Several connections merit attention.

First, Butler represents the intellectual fruit of the Establishment
principle. Arnold's case for a national Church is not primarily
theological — he is notably indifferent to disputes about episcopacy
versus Presbyterianism, calling them "such silly things, that very
easiness doth make them hard to be disputed of in serious manner"
[P03-021], quoting Hooker. Rather, Arnold's case is cultural: the
Establishment puts men "in contact with the main current of national
life" [P03-010], and this contact fosters the "totality" of development
that sectarianism, by its very nature, precludes. Butler, the
Presbyterian-born bishop, the rigorous moral philosopher writing
within the comprehensive fold of the national Church, is a living
example of this principle.

Second, Butler's philosophical method resonates with Arnold's conception
of culture. Butler's *Sermons* develop an account of conscience as a
reflective faculty that surveys and adjudicates among the competing
claims of self-love and benevolence — a process that bears a striking
resemblance to Arnold's own description of culture as "a free and fresh
stream of thought" turned upon our "stock notions and habits" [P03-003].
Both Butler and Arnold insist on the necessity of disinterested
reflection as the precondition of right action. Butler's famous dictum
that "there is a superior principle of reflection or conscience in
every man" might be read as a theological antecedent to Arnold's
conviction that each person harbours a "best self" capable of
transcending class interest, sectarian prejudice, and the mere
machinery of "doing as one likes."

Third, Butler's placement in the Preface is strategic. The Preface sets
out Arnold's whole programme: the defence of culture, the critique of
"machinery," the diagnosis of Hebraism's tendency toward one-sidedness,
and the argument that Establishments foster totality while separatism
breeds provincialism. Butler appears at the precise juncture where
Arnold moves from diagnosing the Nonconformists' defects to asserting
the positive claim of the Establishment — and he functions as
evidentiary capstone. Arnold could have named any number of Anglican
divines; that he chose Butler, the most philosophically rigorous of
the three, suggests that what Arnold values in the Establishment is
not mere institutional continuity but the capacity to produce thought
of lasting intellectual weight.
