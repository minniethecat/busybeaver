# Sir Daniel Gooch

Sir Daniel Gooch (1816–1889) was an English railway engineer, industrialist,
and Conservative politician, who served as the first chief mechanical
engineer of the Great Western Railway and later as its chairman. In
*Culture and Anarchy* (1869), Arnold invokes Gooch twice — not for his
engineering achievements, but for a single maxim attributed to his mother,
which Arnold treats as the perfect distillation of the Philistine worldview.

## Role in Arnold's Argument

Gooch appears in Arnold's text not as a thinker or writer, but as a cultural
symptom — a man whose entire moral horizon is captured by a sentence his
mother repeated to him each morning.

### Mrs. Gooch's Golden Rule

In Chapter II, discussing the Hyde Park riots and the outlook of the English
working class, Arnold introduces Gooch's maxim:

> That beautiful sentence Sir Daniel Gooch quoted to the Swindon workmen,
> and which I treasure as Mrs. Gooch's Golden Rule, or the Divine
> Injunction "Be ye Perfect" done into British,—the sentence Sir Daniel
> Gooch's mother repeated to him every morning when he was a boy going to
> work: "Ever remember, my dear Dan, that you should look forward to being
> some day manager of that concern!" — this fruitful maxim is perfectly
> fitted to shine forth in the heart of the Hyde Park rough also, and to be
> his guiding-star through life. (PN 002-009)

The irony is layered. Arnold calls the maxim "beautiful" and "fruitful" —
but his real point is devastating. The Christian command "Be ye perfect"
(Matt. 5:48), a call to inward spiritual transformation, has been translated
into the language of industrial ambition: aspire to become a manager. This
is Arnold's definition of Philistinism: the substitution of external,
mechanical goods for inward perfection.

### Working by the Light of the Golden Rule

In Chapter III, Arnold returns to Gooch's maxim to define which part of
the working class belongs to the Philistines:

> It is obvious that that part of the working-class which, working
> diligently by the light of Mrs. Gooch's Golden Rule, looks forward to the
> happy day when it will sit on thrones with Mr. Bazley and other
> middle-class potentates, to survey, as Mr. Bright beautifully says, "the
> cities it has built, the railroads it has made, the manufactures it has
> produced" … is, or is in a fair way to be, one in spirit with the
> industrial middle-class. (PN 003-007)

Arnold's classification is sociological: those working men who internalize
the Goochian aspiration to rise through industrial success have aligned
themselves spiritually with middle-class Philistinism. They seek "its class
and its class-instinct," not "its best self."

## The Maxim as Cultural Diagnosis

Gooch's significance for Arnold lies entirely in this one sentence. It
encapsulates three features of the Philistine outlook:

1. **The reduction of perfection to position**: "Be ye perfect" becomes
   "become manager" — spiritual aspiration collapses into career ambition.
2. **The generational transmission of Philistine values**: The maxim is a
   mother's daily counsel to her son — Philistinism is not merely adopted
   but inherited and lovingly imparted.
3. **The universality of the appeal**: Arnold insists the maxim is "perfectly
   fitted" to inspire not only an engineer's son but "the Hyde Park rough
   also" — Philistinism cuts across class lines.
