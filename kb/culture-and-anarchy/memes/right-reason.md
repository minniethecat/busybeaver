# Right Reason

"Right reason" is Arnold's term for the intellectual faculty that discerns
the true from the merely popular, the permanent from the ephemeral. It is
the cognitive dimension of the best self — the capacity to see things as
they really are, undistorted by class interest, partisan passion, or the
"commonness" of unreflective opinion. Arnold borrows the phrase from the
Stoic and Scholastic traditions but gives it a distinctively modern,
democratic application.

## Arnold's Definition

Arnold introduces right reason as what the English lack. "We have a strong
belief in freedom, and a very weak belief in right reason," he writes
[002-008]. The English habit is to trust the outcome of contending
interests — markets, elections, the free play of opinion — rather than to
ask what reason, impersonally exercised, would dictate. "The blessed thing
is to like what right reason ordains, and to follow her authority," Arnold
insists, and this is the discipline that "culture" exists to cultivate
[002-011].

## Usage Across the Text

### Right Reason and the State

The political function of right reason is to supply a standard for State
action. Arnold imagines the State as "summing up the right reason of the
community, and giving it a form and a force" [002-012]. The best regime is
"the power most representing the right reason of the nation, and most
worthy, therefore, of ruling — of exercising, in the language of popular
writers, the most important of the functions of government" [002-013].
This is Arnold's answer to the objection that State action is a form of
class tyranny: the State, properly understood, is not the agent of any
class but the institutional embodiment of right reason itself.

### Right Reason Against Anarchy

In Arnold's scheme, right reason is the positive alternative to anarchy.
"Plainly, a defence against anarchy is right reason, ideas, light," he
declares [002-015]. The more a society trusts the mere collision of
interests to produce order, the more it drifts toward the condition Arnold
calls anarchy — not chaos but a deeper disorder in which no standard
higher than appetite is recognised. "The more, therefore, we have of
right reason, the more we are saved from anarchy" [002-015].

### Right Reason and the Best Self

Arnold consistently pairs right reason with the best self. The best self
is the psychological locus of impersonality; right reason is its cognitive
operation. "Right reason, or our best self, appearing alone to offer such
a sound centre of authority," he writes [003-016]. The two concepts are so
closely linked that Arnold sometimes uses them interchangeably: "the State,
or organ of our collective best self, of our national right reason"
[002-026]. This pairing is central to Arnold's political philosophy:
authority requires both a motivational ground (the best self) and an
intellectual standard (right reason).

### The Weak Belief in Right Reason

Arnold is pessimistic about the English capacity for right reason.
"Accustomed as we are to the spectacle of our ordinary self, of our common
humanity, doing as it likes," he observes, the idea of a rational standard
above appetite seems exotic and perhaps dangerous [003-012]. When
"W. Cattle comes at his best self by Papist-baiting, or Newman Weeks and
Deborah Butler at right reason by following their noses," the claim that
right reason exists at all "does certainly appear over-sanguine" [003-019].
Yet Arnold insists that the difficulty of accessing right reason does not
make it any less necessary.

## Historical Context

The phrase "right reason" (*recta ratio*) has a long philosophical
pedigree: it appears in Cicero's Stoic writings, in Aquinas's natural-law
theory, and in Hooker's *Laws of Ecclesiastical Polity* (1594), a text
Arnold knew well from his Anglican upbringing. Arnold's innovation is to
democratise the concept: right reason is not the property of a
philosophical elite but a capacity latent in every person and collectively
expressed through the State. This move parallels T. H. Green's
contemporary effort to reconcile idealist philosophy with liberal
democracy [002-011–013, 003-023].
