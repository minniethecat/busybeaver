# William Chambers

William Chambers was a British Liberal Member of Parliament whom [[matthew-arnold|Matthew Arnold]] describes as a "rigid Hebraiser," noted for introducing legislation to permit marriage with a deceased wife's sister — a bill that Arnold uses to illustrate the mechanical application of Hebraic legalism in politics [006-014].

## Identity

Chambers served in the House of Commons as a Liberal MP. He is known to history primarily through Arnold's account of his Deceased Wife's Sister Bill, which sought to legalise marriage between a man and his deceased wife's sister — a union that was prohibited under English law because of the interpretation of Levitical kinship rules. The bill became for Arnold a vivid illustration of how certain Victorian reformers approached moral questions through narrow legalistic frameworks.

Little is recorded of Chambers beyond Arnold's account, and his parliamentary career was not distinguished. This is itself significant: Arnold chooses as his example not a major political figure but an obscure MP whose bill exemplifies a larger pattern of reasoning. Chambers's obscurity is part of Arnold's argument — the mechanical Hebraism he criticises is not the property of exceptional individuals but a habit of mind distributed throughout the political class.

## In Culture and Anarchy

Arnold presents Chambers as an exemplar of what he calls **rigid Hebraism** — the tendency to treat scripture as a legal code and to decide moral questions purely by whether a specific text forbids an action. Chambers's reform effort was, in Arnold's view, well-intentioned but conducted in the wrong spirit: rather than asking what conduces to human perfection, Chambers asked only whether the [[book-of-leviticus|Book of Leviticus]] explicitly prohibited the marriage [006-017].

Arnold contrasts this approach with the wisdom of [[bishop-wilson|Bishop Wilson]], who "gives an admirable lesson to rigid Hebraisers, like Mr. Chambers" [006-017]. Wilson's maxim — "to make reason and the will of God prevail" — represents the liberal, humanised Hebraism that Arnold endorses, as opposed to Chambers's mechanical, text-bound version.

## Key Passages

Arnold recounts being present at Chambers's speech in the House of Commons:

> "I was lucky enough to be present when Mr. Chambers, I think, brought forward in the House of Commons his bill for enabling a man to marry his deceased wife's sister, and I heard the speech which Mr. Chambers then made in support of his bill." [006-014]

This is a rare moment of first-person reportage in *Culture and Anarchy*. Arnold positions himself as an eyewitness, lending the account an immediacy that the more abstract passages lack. The effect is to make Chambers's position seem not merely mistaken but absurd — the reader is invited to imagine Arnold in the gallery, listening with polite incredulity.

During the debate, a Liberal supporter of Chambers offered a formula that Arnold found revealing:

> "A distinguished Liberal supporter of Mr. Chambers... produced a formula of much beauty and neatness for conveying in brief the Liberal notions on this head: 'Liberty,' said he, 'is the law of human life.' And, therefore, the moment it is ascertained that God's law, the Book of Leviticus, does not stop the way, man's law, the law of liberty, asserts its right." [006-014]

Arnold's irony is devastating. The formula "of much beauty and neatness" is precisely what he mocks — the Liberal conviction that a slogan can settle a moral question. "Liberty is the law of human life" is the sort of phrase that sounds conclusive without meaning anything specific.

## The Book of Leviticus

Arnold's treatment of Chambers connects to a larger argument about the [[book-of-leviticus|Book of Leviticus]] and its authority. The Deceased Wife's Sister controversy turned on whether the Levitical prohibition of marriage to a brother's wife extended to a wife's sister. For Chambers and his allies, the question was purely textual: does the Book of Leviticus forbid it or not? For Arnold, this was exactly the wrong question.

The deeper issue is the status of scriptural authority in modern life. Arnold, despite his reputation as a religious liberal, does not advocate disregarding scripture. His point is subtler: scripture should be interpreted in light of reason and the pursuit of human perfection, not treated as a legal code whose prohibitions can be mechanically applied or circumvented.

## Arnold's Deeper Point

The Chambers episode is not merely about the Deceased Wife's Sister Bill — a reform that eventually passed in 1907, long after Arnold's death. It is about the poverty of a certain kind of liberal reasoning. The Liberal formula — "liberty is the law of human life" — reduces moral deliberation to a single principle and then applies that principle mechanically. This is, in Arnold's terms, [[machinery]] — the worship of a means (liberty) as if it were an end (human perfection).

The Chambers case thus becomes a parable about the intellectual poverty of Victorian [[liberalism]]. Chambers is not corrupt or stupid; he is, by his own lights, doing good. But his frame of reference is so narrow — his conception of moral reasoning so impoverished — that even his good intentions cannot lead to good results.

## Relationship to Arnold's Framework

Chambers embodies the intersection of [[hebraism|Hebraism]] and political [[liberalism]] that Arnold critiques throughout *Culture and Anarchy*. Arnold's point is not that Chambers's bill was wrong in substance but that the reasoning behind it — reducing moral questions to whether a scriptural prohibition could be found and then appealing to "liberty" as a catch-all justification — represented a failure of [[culture]] and [[right-reason|right reason]].

Arnold also uses Chambers to connect his critique to the broader problem of mechanical Hebraism, citing the biblical command "Be fruitful and multiply" as another instance of "the edict of God's law, as Mr. Chambers would say" [006-031]. The phrase "as Mr. Chambers would say" has become, by this point in the chapter, a shorthand for a whole approach to moral reasoning that Arnold finds inadequate.
