# Establishment

The institutional face of English life that Arnold both critiques and defends — an established order whose authority is legitimate in principle but mechanical and class-bound in practice, requiring the transformative influence of culture to become the organ of the nation's "best self" [002-016].

## Arnold's Ambivalent Position

Arnold's stance toward the Establishment is characteristically nuanced. On one hand, he is a sharp critic of the aristocracy ("Barbarians") and the class system that gives them unearned authority. On the other, he is deeply suspicious of the liberal assault on established institutions, which he sees as driven by the narrow interests of the middle class ("[[philistines|Philistines]]") rather than by a genuine vision of the common good.

The Establishment, for all its flaws, at least embodies the principle of authority — and [[authority]], Arnold insists, is necessary. The alternative is not freedom but [[anarchy]]: the dissolution of all standards into the chaos of "[[doing-as-one-likes]]."

## The Church of England as Paradigm

The [[church-of-england|Church of England]] is the paradigmatic Establishment institution for Arnold. His Preface expands at length on the history of the Church, tracing the tension between Episcopalianism and Presbyterianism from Hooker through the Reformation to the nineteenth century [P03-021 through P03-024]. Arnold's treatment of the Church is entirely consistent with his treatment of the Establishment more broadly: he defends the principle of an established Church — the idea that religion should be connected to the main current of national life — while criticising the Church's actual performance as mechanical, class-bound, and spiritually narrow.

This is the argument that so irritated Arnold's Nonconformist critics. They read his defence of Establishment as a defence of Anglican privilege. Arnold insisted it was a defence of a principle: that the national life, including its religious life, should have organs of authority and collective reason — what he calls the "best self" of the nation [002-016].

## Culture's Role

[[Culture]]'s task is not to destroy the Establishment but to transform it — to replace the mechanical authority of class privilege with the rational authority of the "[[best-self]]." This is the argument of Chapter II, where Arnold develops his theory of the State not as the instrument of any particular class but as the organ of the nation's collective reason:

> "We want an authority, and we find nothing but jealous classes, checks, and a deadlock; culture suggests the idea of the State. We find no basis for a firm State-power in our ordinary selves; culture suggests one to us in our best self." [002-016]

## The Establishment and Machinery

For Arnold, the great danger of Establishment is that it becomes [[machinery]] — an end in itself rather than a means to human perfection. The aristocracy worships the Establishment because it guarantees their privileges; the middle class resents it for the same reason. Neither response engages with the deeper question: what kind of institutional order would genuinely serve the pursuit of human perfection?

Arnold's answer is that the Establishment, properly understood and transformed by culture, is indispensable to that pursuit. But the transformation required is radical: it means replacing the class principle with the principle of reason, and the mechanical operation of inherited privilege with the living authority of the "best self."

## Contemporary Resonance

Arnold's ambivalent posture toward the Establishment has proved remarkably durable as a political stance. The position — critical of existing institutions in their actual operation, but deeply conservative about the principle they embody — recurs in Anglo-American political thought from [[edmund-burke|Burke]] through [[t-s-eliot|T.S. Eliot]] to the present. It is the position of someone who believes that institutions are necessary for human flourishing but finds none of the actually existing ones satisfactory.
