# Nonconformists

The contemporary embodiment of the Puritan tradition — the Protestant dissenters whom Arnold treats with a mixture of genuine respect and sharp critique, seeing in them both the moral backbone of England and the chief obstacle to the diffusion of "sweetness and light." The Nonconformists are not merely an example in Arnold's argument; they are its primary audience — the people he most urgently hopes to persuade (P03-007).

## Arnold's Ambivalent Assessment

Arnold's attitude toward the Nonconformists is one of the most carefully calibrated positions in *Culture and Anarchy*. He opens his discussion with a declaration that is at once conciliatory and uncompromising:

> Certainly we are no enemies of the Nonconformists; for, on the contrary, what we aim at is their perfection. (P03-007)

The sentence is pure Arnold: the genial tone masks a radical claim. To say that one aims at the Nonconformists' "perfection" is to say, politely but unmistakably, that they are not yet perfect — that their present condition, however admirable in certain respects, falls short of what human beings can and should be.

### Respect and Its Limits

Arnold's respect for the Nonconformists is genuine but bounded. He admires their moral seriousness, their industry, their conviction that life should be lived according to principle rather than appetite. These are real virtues, and Arnold never retracts his acknowledgment of them. What he cannot accept is the Nonconformist assumption that moral seriousness is sufficient — that being right with God excuses one from the larger task of becoming fully human.

### "That Light Be Not Darkness"

His central criticism is characteristically compressed into a single image: the Nonconformists, as "the successors and representatives of the Puritans," have "not enough added to their care for walking staunchly by the best light they have, a care that that light be not darkness" (P03-007). The image is devastating in its precision. The Nonconformists are walking — they are not idle or indifferent — and they are walking "staunchly," with conviction and determination. The problem is not their energy but their direction. The light they follow may be darkness, and their very staunchness prevents them from noticing.

## The Nonconformist Type

Arnold's Nonconformist is a recognizable social type: earnest, serious, industrious, convinced of his own righteousness, and utterly indifferent — even hostile — to the claims of beauty, intellect, and "sweetness and light." This type is the product of a religious culture that has elevated conscience above consciousness, conduct above contemplation, and moral certainty above intellectual flexibility.

### The Anatomy of the Type

Several features define Arnold's Nonconformist:

1. **Moral self-sufficiency**: The conviction that sincere religious belief, combined with upright conduct, exhausts the demands of human perfection. The Nonconformist does not feel the lack of anything that culture might supply.

2. **Suspicion of intellect**: "The free play of the mind upon all subjects" — Arnold's definition of Hellenism — is, to the Nonconformist, a temptation rather than a duty. Thinking too freely courts heresy, and heresy imperils the soul.

3. **Indifference to beauty**: The arts, literature, and the life of aesthetic pleasure are, at best, permissible diversions and, at worst, worldly snares. The Nonconformist's world is morally serious but aesthetically impoverished.

4. **Provincial certainty**: The Nonconformist lives within a closed circle of conviction, untroubled by alternatives. "The British Banner" — some Dissenting newspaper — is as authoritative as the *Saturday Review*, because the Nonconformist has no "notion that there was a scale of value for judgments on these topics" (003-012).

### Why This Matters

What Arnold objects to is not the Nonconformist's moral seriousness but his complacency about it. The Nonconformist is convinced that his religious sincerity excuses him from the larger task of human perfection — that being right with God is sufficient, and that culture is, at best, a distraction and, at worst, a temptation. This is, for Arnold, a tragic error: the Nonconformist has turned a partial good (moral earnestness) into a complete one, and in doing so has closed off the possibility of the very perfection he claims to seek.

## The Social Consequences

The political power of Nonconformity in the 1860s makes this more than a theological quibble. The Nonconformists, as a key Liberal constituency, were shaping English legislation on education, temperance, and the church. Their cultural narrowness was becoming national policy.

Arnold's essay is, in part, an argument that the Nonconformist conscience is not a sufficient basis for governing a modern nation. A political program driven by Dissenting morality — however sincerely held — will produce a nation that is morally earnest without being wise, industrious without being cultivated, and free without knowing what freedom is for. The Nonconformist vision of England, in Arnold's view, is an England of machinery.

### The Education Question

The Nonconformists' opposition to state-supported education — rooted in their fear of Anglican indoctrination — was, for Arnold, a particularly damaging example of sectarian narrowness blocking national improvement. The children of the working class needed "sweetness and light" more than anyone, but the Nonconformist insistence on denominational schooling was preventing the state from providing it. The irony was cruel: the most morally serious element of English society was, by its very seriousness, obstructing the diffusion of the culture it most needed.

## Beyond the Nonconformists: A Structural Critique

Arnold's engagement with Nonconformity is of more than historical interest because it exemplifies a structural pattern that recurs throughout his thought: the collision between Hebraism (moral seriousness, conviction, obedience) and Hellenism (intellectual flexibility, curiosity, the free play of mind). The Nonconformists are not wrong to be morally serious; they are wrong to be *only* morally serious. The error is not in the content of their conviction but in its exclusiveness.

This structural pattern makes Arnold's critique transferable beyond its Victorian context. Any community that elevates moral certainty above intellectual openness — whether religious, political, or cultural — reproduces the Nonconformist error. Arnold's prescription — "sweetness and light" as a supplement, not a replacement — is a standing rebuke to all forms of moral complacency, secular as well as religious.

## The Remedy

Arnold's prescription is not that the Nonconformists should abandon their faith but that they should supplement it. What they need is "sweetness and light" — the flexibility, intellectual curiosity, and aesthetic sensibility that their tradition has neglected. The aim is not to destroy their moral energy but to "develop their full humanity more perfectly" (P03-007).

This is the characteristic Arnoldian move: not "either/or" but "both/and." Hebraism is not wrong; it is incomplete. The Nonconformist is not an enemy; he is a potential ally who has mistaken a part for the whole. The argument is at once generous and uncompromising: generous in its refusal to condemn, uncompromising in its insistence that more is required.

## Historical Context

By the 1860s, Nonconformists constituted roughly half of English churchgoers. They were the backbone of the Liberal Party in the provinces, the driving force behind the campaign for the disestablishment of the Church of Ireland (achieved in 1869), and the most vocal advocates of temperance legislation and Sabbatarian restrictions. The Liberation Society, founded in 1844, had made disestablishment a live political issue. Arnold's critique was thus both a theological argument and a direct intervention in contemporary politics — an attempt to persuade the Nonconformists that their political program, however well-intentioned, was inadequate to the needs of a modern nation.

The irony that Arnold's own father, Thomas Arnold of Rugby, had been a broad-church Anglican deeply sympathetic to Dissent adds a biographical dimension to the argument. Arnold was not attacking an alien tradition but critiquing a tendency within his own religious inheritance.
