# Conscience

The characteristic virtue of Hebraism — the inward monitor of right and wrong that drives moral conduct, which Arnold both honors as essential and critiques as insufficient when isolated from the life of the mind (004-004).

## Arnold's Definition

Conscience is the faculty that Hebraism prizes above all others. Where Hellenism prizes consciousness (the capacity to see things clearly), Hebraism prizes conscience (the capacity to feel moral obligation). Arnold describes the difference in terms of the primary danger each tradition addresses:

> The Greek quarrel with the body and its desires is, that they hinder right thinking, the Hebrew quarrel with them is, that they hinder right acting. (004-004)

Conscience is what makes this Hebrew quarrel possible — it is the internal voice that says "thou shalt not" and generates the energy of moral effort. Arnold never denies the value of conscience; he denies only its sufficiency.

## The Strength of Conscience

Arnold is careful to acknowledge the genuine moral power of conscience. The Puritan tradition, for all its narrowness, produced men and women of extraordinary moral seriousness. This moral energy — "the care and discipline received from Hebraism" — is "an eternal possession" of the human race (P03-032).

The strength of conscience lies in its ability to generate action. Unlike the Hellenic love of contemplation, which can slide into passivity, conscience "sets doing above knowing" and drives people to act on their convictions (004-005). This is why Hebraism has historically been the more politically effective of the two forces.

## The Weakness of Conscience Alone

The crucial weakness of conscience, Arnold argues, is that it tells us to act but does not tell us *what* to do. A person with a strong conscience but a weak understanding may act with great energy in the service of error:

> The Puritan's very strength of conscience, his sense of the burden of sin, may drive him to narrow and uncharitable judgments of others. (004-007)

What conscience lacks is the Hellenic virtue of *seeing clearly*. Without the check of intelligence and the tempering influence of "sweetness and light," conscience becomes rigid, judgmental, and ultimately self-defeating.

## Conscience, Sin, and Perfection

For the Hebraic tradition, the great obstacle to perfection is sin — the failure of conduct. For the Hellenic tradition, it is ignorance — the failure of understanding. Arnold's synthesis is that both diagnoses are correct but incomplete. The truly cultured person needs both a strong conscience (to feel the urgency of right action) and a clear intelligence (to know what right action consists in).

## Historical Context

Arnold's treatment of conscience reflects the Victorian crisis of religious authority. By the 1860s, the biblical criticism of German scholars and the challenge of Darwinism had made traditional religious certainty increasingly difficult to sustain. Arnold's response is characteristic: he retains the moral seriousness of Christianity (conscience) while opening it to intellectual modernity (consciousness).
