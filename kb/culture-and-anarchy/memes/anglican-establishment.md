The Anglican Establishment denotes the Church of England in its full institutional, legal, and social character — not merely as a religious body but as a legally established church woven into the fabric of the English constitution, with bishops in the House of Lords, tithes levied on the entire population, and a privileged position in education and public ceremony. Arnold's discussion of the Establishment in the Preface to *Culture and Anarchy* is notably ambivalent: he criticizes the Nonconformists' "blind partisanship" against it while suggesting that the Establishment might be reformed into something genuinely comprehensive and culturally valuable.

## Nature

The Anglican Establishment was, in Arnold's day, a complex of legal privileges, social customs, and institutional powers. The monarch was its Supreme Governor; its bishops sat in Parliament; its clergy were supported by mandatory tithes; its liturgy was prescribed by Act of Parliament; its doctrines were embedded in the Thirty-Nine Articles to which Oxford and Cambridge dons were required to subscribe. This interweaving of church and state made the Establishment a uniquely potent symbol — for its defenders, of national identity and moral order; for its critics, of social injustice and religious coercion.

## Role in Arnold's Argument

Arnold's discussion of the Anglican Establishment in the Preface is strategic. He opens by acknowledging that he must say more than "a few words" about it (PN P03-006), immediately positioning the question as central to his project. The Nonconformists, he observes, are "blind partisans of the Anglican Establishment" — meaning that their opposition to it is passionate rather than reasoned, a matter of sectarian feeling rather than of intellectual judgment (PN P03-006).

Arnold's own position is neither the Tory defense of the Establishment as it stands nor the Nonconformist demand for its abolition. He sketches a third possibility: that "the present Anglican Establishment" might be given "a character the most latitudinarian" — made broad enough to serve as a genuine "centre of good" for the nation (PN P03-021). This is one of the few constructive political proposals in *Culture and Anarchy*: instead of abolishing the Establishment (the Nonconformist solution) or defending it unchanged (the Tory solution), Arnold imagines reforming it into a genuinely comprehensive national organ of culture — a church that could speak for the best self of the nation rather than for a sectarian interest.

## Key Passages

> "The Nonconformists, blind partisans of the Anglican Establishment. More than a few words we must spend on this matter" (PN P03-006)

> "The Anglican Establishment here in England — sufficient and popular" in its own way, yet "it is in some respects insufficient" (PN P03-008)

> Arnold's proposal: "to give the present Anglican Establishment a character the most latitudinarian" so that it might become "a centre of good" (PN P03-021)

## Historical Context

The "latitudinarian" tradition Arnold invokes was a strand within the Church of England that emphasized reason, toleration, and breadth of doctrine over strict confessional orthodoxy. Originating in the 17th-century Cambridge Platonists and influential through the 18th-century Broad Church movement, latitudinarianism had, by Arnold's time, become associated with liberal Anglican thinkers like Thomas Arnold (Matthew's father) and F. D. Maurice. Arnold's suggestion that the Establishment be reformed along latitudinarian lines reflects this family and intellectual inheritance: the belief that the national church could be made broad enough to encompass reasonable men of differing views, and thereby serve as a genuine organ of national culture rather than a party of one religious faction.
