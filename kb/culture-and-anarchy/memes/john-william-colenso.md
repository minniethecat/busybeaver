# John William Colenso

John William Colenso (1814–1883) was a British mathematician, theologian,
and the first Anglican Bishop of Natal in South Africa. He became one of
the most controversial religious figures of the Victorian era through his
biblical criticism, particularly *The Pentateuch and Book of Joshua
Critically Examined* (1862–1879), which questioned the Mosaic authorship
and historical accuracy of the first books of the Bible. Arnold refers to
Colenso once in *Culture and Anarchy* (1869), using his name as a cultural
shorthand for intellectual revolution in matters of received authority.

## Role in Arnold's Argument

Colenso appears in Chapter VI, in Arnold's discussion of the changing
Victorian attitudes toward love and marriage. The reference is brief but
telling:

### The Colenso of Love and Marriage

> And this exactly falls in with what Mr. Hepworth Dixon, who may almost
> be called the Colenso of love and marriage,—such a revolution does he
> make in our ideas on these matters, just as Dr. Colenso does in our ideas
> on religion,—tells us of the notions and proceedings of our kinsmen in
> America. (PN 006-015)

The comparison is precise. Just as Colenso had subjected the Pentateuch to
rational, critical examination — unsettling traditional beliefs about the
authorship and authority of Scripture — so Hepworth Dixon, in Arnold's
view, was subjecting the received ideas about marriage and sexual relations
to a similarly rationalising revision. Arnold is not necessarily endorsing
Colenso's conclusions; he is using Colenso as a recognisable cultural
reference point to characterise a mode of intellectual operation.

### Colenso and the Philistine Pattern

The Colenso reference also illustrates what Arnold sees as a characteristic
Philistine pattern: the combination of intellectual daring with a certain
narrowness of vision. Colenso's critical method, like Dixon's, proceeds by
applying rational scrutiny to traditional authority. But Arnold's larger
argument in Chapter VI is that this rationalism, however liberating, remains
incomplete — it satisfies the "craving for forbidden fruit and the craving
for legality" without cultivating the "delicacy of perception" that
constitutes true Hellenic culture.

## Connection to Arnold's Framework

Colenso exemplifies, for Arnold, the ambiguous legacy of the critical spirit
in Victorian England. On one hand, Colenso's willingness to subject received
tradition to rational examination represents a genuine advance over mere
conformity. On the other, Arnold's juxtaposition of Colenso with Hepworth
Dixon — a popular writer whom Arnold treats with sustained irony — suggests
that rational criticism alone, unaccompanied by what Arnold calls "sweetness
and light," is insufficient. The "Colenso of love and marriage" is not a
compliment; it is a diagnosis of a cultural moment in which revolution in
ideas has outpaced the refinement of sensibility.
