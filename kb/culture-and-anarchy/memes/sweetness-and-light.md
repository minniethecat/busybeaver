# Sweetness and Light

Arnold's master-metaphor for culture itself — borrowed from Jonathan Swift's *The Battle of the Books* — designating the ideal union of beauty and intelligence that constitutes true human perfection (001-010).

## Arnold's Definition

Arnold takes the phrase "sweetness and light" from Swift, who called them "the two noblest of things" (001-010). For Arnold, sweetness represents the moral and aesthetic dimension of perfection — beauty, temper, flexibility of mind. Light represents the intellectual dimension — reason, clear seeing, the ability to know things as they truly are. Together they form what Arnold calls "the character of perfection," which is the object of culture:

> Culture, which is the study of perfection, leads us to conceive of true human perfection as a harmonious perfection, developing all sides of our humanity; and as a general perfection, developing all parts of our society. (P03-007)

The defining trait of the person who possesses sweetness and light is what the Greeks called *euphyia* — "the man who tends towards sweetness and light" — a natural well-grownness and flexibility of character (001-010).

## Usage Across the Text

### Chapter I: Sweetness and Light

The first chapter takes its title from the concept itself and develops it most fully. Arnold contrasts "sweetness and light" with the narrowness of English Puritanism, which pursues moral rectitude without the tempering influence of beauty and intelligence (001-013). He argues that the Protestant religion, despite its seriousness, has failed to produce "an ideal of complete harmonious human perfection" (001-014).

The crucial practical benefit of sweetness and light is **flexibility** — the capacity to see a thing from more than one side, to resist the rigidity of dogma and party spirit (001-016). Arnold illustrates this through the example of Oxford, whose "beauty and sweetness" formed men capable of this flexibility even amid the "hideous manufactures" of modern industrial England (001-017).

Arnold's key formulation: "the essential character of human perfection is sweetness and light. It is of use because, as pursuing perfection, it pursues sweetness and light" (001-022). He explicitly identifies culture with this pursuit: "Culture, then, is the pursuit of sweetness and light. He who works for sweetness and light, works to make reason and the will of God prevail" (001-023).

### Chapter IV: Hebraism and Hellenism

In the fourth chapter, sweetness and light reappears as the characteristic fruit of Hellenism, contrasted with the moral strenuousness of Hebraism (004-004). Where Hebraism prioritizes conduct and obedience, Hellenism prioritizes seeing things as they really are — the precondition for both sweetness and light.

### Preface

Arnold returns to the phrase in his Preface, emphasizing that the critic's task is to add sweetness and light to the Nonconformists' zeal, developing their "full humanity more perfectly" (P03-007). He dismisses the notion "that culture, and sweetness, and light, are all moonshine" as a misunderstanding of what culture truly offers (P03-020).

## Historical Context

Arnold appropriates Swift's phrase from *The Battle of the Books* (1704), where the bee (representing the ancients) produces "sweetness and light" while the spider (representing the moderns) spins only from itself. Arnold transforms this literary metaphor into a comprehensive ideal of human perfection, infusing it with the Greek concept of *euphyia* (well-grownness) and Coleridgean ideas about the harmonious development of human faculties (001-010 through 001-017).
