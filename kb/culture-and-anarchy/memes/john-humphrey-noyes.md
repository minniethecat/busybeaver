# John Humphrey Noyes

John Humphrey Noyes (1811–1886) was an American utopian socialist and religious leader who founded the Oneida Community in New York. [[matthew-arnold]] references him — as "Brother Noyes" — as an emblem of the provincialism he saw in the more extravagant forms of American Nonconformist religious experimentation [P03-018].

## Identity

Noyes was a radical religious thinker who broke from conventional Christianity to establish a perfectionist commune based on the principle of "Bible Communism," which included communal property and complex marriage. The Oneida Community was among the most famous American utopian experiments of the nineteenth century. Arnold groups Noyes alongside other unorthodox American religious figures such as the revivalist [[charles-spurgeon|Spurgeon]] as examples of what he considered the eccentric products of disestablishment.

## Role in Arnold's Argument

Arnold invokes Noyes as evidence that religious disestablishment, left to itself, can produce not only earnestness but also eccentricity and provincial narrowness. In the Preface, he lists the "free churches of Mr. Beecher or Brother Noyes" alongside their "provincialism" [P03-018].

In Chapter III, Arnold satirically juxtaposes Noyes with a catalogue of contemporary religious curiosities:

> "Deborah Butler, Elderess Polly, Brother Noyes, the Rev. W. Cattle, the Licensed Victuallers..." [003-019]

This rhetorical move — placing Noyes in a list of seeming eccentrics — illustrates Arnold's concern that [[dissent]] without the discipline of [[culture]] can descend into the merely idiosyncratic.

## Key Passages

In Chapter VI, Arnold draws a theological contrast:

> "The Patriarchs and the Old Testament, Brother Noyes to St. Paul and the New." [006-015]

This pairing suggests that Noyes, in Arnold's view, represented the extreme of New Testament primitivism — bypassing tradition and [[establishment]] in favour of a direct, and to Arnold's mind unseasoned, return to apostolic Christianity.

## Relationship to Arnold's Framework

Noyes embodies for Arnold the dangers of what he calls "doing as one likes" in religion. Without the moderating influence of [[the-state]] and the discipline of [[culture]], religious energy produces not [[perfection]] but eccentricity. Noyes is thus a cautionary figure in Arnold's broader argument about the need for a national framework of [[right-reason]].
