# Industrialism

Industrialism, in Arnold's vocabulary, names more than an economic system. It is
an entire orientation of the national mind — a faith that material production
constitutes the chief end of human life. Arnold treats this faith not as an
error of fact but as an error of category: a confusion of machinery with the
perfection it is meant to serve [001-016].

## Arnold's Definition

Arnold does not offer a dictionary definition of industrialism; his method is to
expose it by juxtaposition. Wealth and industrial production belong to the
"material civilization" that England has pursued with unrivaled success. But
"culture," Arnold insists, "places human perfection in an internal condition,"
not in the accumulation of external goods [001-016]. Industrialism is thus
defined negatively: it is whatever a nation pursues when it has forgotten that
"the Kingdom of God is within you."

## Usage Across the Text

### Industrialism as Machinery

Industrialism is the paradigmatic instance of what Arnold calls "machinery":
a means — industrial production, commercial expansion, Free Trade — that
nineteenth-century England had transformed into an end. "Faith in machinery is,"
Arnold writes, "our besetting danger" [002-018], and industrialism is the
machinery most seductive because most visibly productive.

### Industrialism and Individualism

Arnold links industrialism with the Victorian cult of individualism [002-009].
Together they form a creed: that each person pursuing his own material
betterment — what Arnold elsewhere condemns as "doing as one likes" — will
collectively produce the national good. Arnold regards this as a fundamental
confusion of liberty with perfection: "British industrialism and individualism
can bring about a paradise," he writes with evident irony, but the paradise
they promise is material, not spiritual [002-009].

### The Limits of Material Prosperity

Throughout the text, Arnold insists that the question is not whether
industrialism produces wealth — it manifestly does — but whether wealth
constitutes the end of human life. "The idea of perfection as an inward
condition of the mind and spirit," he writes, "is at variance with the
mechanical and material civilization in esteem with us" [001-016]. The
manufacturing towns of the North may be prosperous, but Arnold sees in them no
"sweetness and light."

## Historical Context

Arnold wrote during the high tide of the Industrial Revolution, when Britain's
manufacturing supremacy was unquestioned. The Great Exhibition of 1851 had
displayed British industrial prowess to the world; the Reform Act of 1867 was
partly a response to the new urban working class industrialism had created.
Arnold's critique of industrialism thus cuts against the grain of national
self-congratulation [001-016, 002-018].
