# Richard Baxter

Richard Baxter (1615–1691) was an English Puritan church leader, theologian, and prolific writer, best known for his devotional classic *The Saints' Everlasting Rest* (1650). In *Culture and Anarchy*, [[matthew-arnold]] references Baxter in two distinct roles: first, as one of the "fruitful men" of English Puritanism trained within the [[church-of-england|Established Church]]; and second, as a "philosophical politician" who swims with the stream of popular opinion rather than standing for [[right-reason]].

## Life and Work

Baxter was born in Rowton, Shropshire, and ordained in the [[church-of-england|Church of England]] in 1638. He served as a chaplain in the Parliamentary army during the Civil War but remained a moderate, opposing both the extremes of the Cromwellian regime and the rigid episcopalianism of the Restoration church. After the Restoration of 1660, Baxter was among the Puritan ministers ejected from their livings by the Act of Uniformity (1662), becoming a leading voice of English [[nonconformity]].

His literary output was extraordinary — he authored over 160 works, spanning theology, ethics, devotional writing, and pastoral counsel. *The Saints' Everlasting Rest* and *The Reformed Pastor* (1656) remain his most enduring contributions to English religious literature.

## Arnold's View of Baxter

Arnold's treatment of Baxter in *Culture and Anarchy* is double-edged. In the Preface, Arnold groups Baxter with [[john-milton]] and [[john-wesley]] as evidence that the Established Church, for all its defects, has historically nurtured men of spiritual and intellectual stature:

> The fruitful men of English Puritanism and Nonconformity are men who were trained within the pale of the Establishment,—Milton, Baxter, Wesley. A generation or two outside the Establishment, and Puritanism produces men of national mark no more. [P03-009]

This argument serves Arnold's broader case for the cultural value of national religious establishments. The establishment, Arnold contends, provides the intellectual breadth and institutional stability necessary to produce figures of "national mark" — a claim he supports by observing that equivalent figures emerge from established churches in Scotland, Germany, Switzerland, and France.

## Swimming with the Stream

Later in the same essay, however, Arnold turns the critical side of his argument toward Baxter. In the context of the movement to disestablish the [[irish-church]], Arnold observes that "philosophical politicians" take the path of least resistance, lending intellectual respectability to whatever cause happens to enjoy popular support:

> We see philosophical politicians, with a turn for swimming with the stream, like Mr. Baxter or Mr. Charles Buxton, and philosophical divines with the same turn, like the Dean of Canterbury, seeking to give a sort of grand stamp of generality and solemnity to this antipathy of the Nonconformists, and to dress it out as a law of human progress in the future. [P03-008]

The phrase "swimming with the stream" becomes a recurring motif in Arnold's critique. He returns to it again in the essay's conclusion, grouping Baxter with [[charles-buxton]] and the Dean of Canterbury as thinkers who "swim with the stream, but swim with it philosophically" — lending intellectual cover to what is, in Arnold's view, merely the "ordinary self" of a political faction [P03-029].

## Baxter and the Nonconformists

Arnold's use of Baxter illuminates a central tension in his argument about [[nonconformity]]. By placing Baxter alongside Milton and Wesley as products of the establishment, Arnold suggests that Nonconformity's own best figures owe their formation to the very institution Nonconformists seek to dismantle. Yet by also casting Baxter as a "swimmer with the stream," Arnold implies that even the most distinguished Nonconformist thinkers have succumbed to the temptation of dressing partisan interests in philosophical language.

This dual treatment reflects Arnold's characteristic method: acknowledging the virtues of his opponents while exposing what he sees as their deeper intellectual failure. Baxter is not condemned; he is diagnosed — as one who, despite his genuine gifts, has allowed himself to become an instrument of the "ordinary self" rather than a voice for [[best-self|the best self]].

## Distinction from William Edward Baxter

Richard Baxter is entirely distinct from [[william-edward-baxter]], the nineteenth-century Liberal Member of Parliament for Montrose Burghs, whom Arnold similarly critiques as a "philosophical politician" caught up in the current of popular opinion. The two Baxters, separated by two centuries, serve parallel roles in Arnold's argument: both represent the type of thinker who lends intellectual legitimacy to movements that, in Arnold's judgment, fall short of the ideal of [[perfection]].
