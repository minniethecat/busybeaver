# Johann Gottfried Herder

Johann Gottfried Herder (1744–1803) was a German philosopher, theologian,
and literary critic whose ideas on language, history, and national
culture shaped the intellectual landscape of late-eighteenth-century
Germany. [[matthew-arnold]] names him alongside
[[gotthold-ephraim-lessing|Lessing]] in Chapter I of *Culture and
Anarchy* as an exemplar of the "great men of culture": those who labour
to humanise knowledge and carry the best ideas of their time across the
whole breadth of society [001-024].

## Identity

Herder was born in Mohrungen, East Prussia, and studied under Kant at
the University of Königsberg before entering the Lutheran ministry.
He was a pupil and protégé of Johann Georg Hamann, from whom he absorbed
a deep conviction that reason is always embodied in language, history,
and communal life — never abstract, never detachable from the particular
people and traditions that give it flesh. His major works include the
*Fragments on Recent German Literature* (1767–1768), the *Treatise on
the Origin of Language* (1772), and the monumental *Outlines of a
Philosophy of the History of Man* (1784–1791), in which he traced the
development of human civilisation from the natural world through
successive national cultures, each with its own irreducible character
and contribution. In his later years he produced influential studies of
Hebrew poetry, folk song, and Shakespeare, arguing that the truest
poetry springs not from rule-bound imitation of classical models but
from the living voice of a people — an argument that helped liberate
Goethe and the young Romantics from French neo-classical orthodoxy.

## Role in Arnold's Argument

Herder appears in Arnold's text in a single but structurally significant
passage. Arnold is defining what he means by "the men of culture":
those who possess "a passion for diffusing, for making prevail, for
carrying from one end of society to the other, the best knowledge, the
best ideas of their time" — who have "laboured to divest knowledge of
all that was harsh, uncouth, difficult, abstract, professional,
exclusive; to humanise it" [001-024].

Arnold names three historical personifications of this type: Abelard
in the Middle Ages, and Lessing and Herder in eighteenth-century
Germany. The pairing of Lessing and Herder is precise and deliberate.
Lessing represents the critical, discriminating dimension of culture —
the edge that cuts away false authority. Herder represents the
sympathetic, historical dimension — the capacity to enter into the life
of other peoples and other ages and to find there a value that is not
one's own. Together they stand for the double movement that Arnold
calls "sweetness and light": Herder supplies the sweetness — the warmth
of historical imagination and the love of the particular — just as
Lessing supplies the light of critical reason.

## Key Passages

Arnold's direct reference is brief but emphatic:

> Such were Lessing and Herder in Germany, at the end of the last
> century; and their services to Germany were in this way inestimably
> precious. Generations will pass, and literary monuments will
> accumulate, and works far more perfect than the works of Lessing
> and Herder will be produced in Germany; and yet the names of these
> two men will fill a German with a reverence and enthusiasm such as
> the names of the most gifted masters will hardly awaken. Because
> they humanised knowledge; because they broadened the basis of life
> and intelligence; because they worked powerfully to diffuse
> sweetness and light, to make reason and the will of God prevail.
> [001-024]

The passage is notable for the priority it gives to the *social effect*
of a thinker over the *intrinsic perfection* of his works. Arnold
concedes that "works far more perfect" than those of Herder will be
produced, but insists that Herder's name will still command a reverence
that "the most gifted masters will hardly awaken." The argument is at
the heart of Arnold's conception of culture: the value of a thinker
is measured not by the flawlessness of his productions but by his
power to "broaden the basis of life and intelligence" — to make more
people capable of receiving and using ideas.
