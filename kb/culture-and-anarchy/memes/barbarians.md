# Barbarians

The "Barbarians" are, in Arnold's adaptation of Heine's tripartite social
classification, the English aristocracy. The term is provocative but precise:
Arnold means to suggest that the aristocracy, for all its outward polish,
remains inwardly uncivilized — possessed of "sweetness" without "light."

## Arnold's Definition

Arnold borrows the term from Heine, who used "Barbarians" to describe the
English upper class's combination of physical vigour and intellectual
indifference. Arnold develops the idea: the Barbarian's "exterior culture" — his
field sports, his social ease, his "serenity and dignified freedom from petty
cares" — is genuine but incomplete. The Barbarian possesses what Arnold calls
"sweetness" (outward amenity) without "light" (inward intelligence) [003-002].
He is agreeable, even admirable, but he is not interested in "the best that has
been thought and said."

## Usage Across the Text

### The Taxonomy of Classes

Arnold's use of "Barbarians" is most fully developed in Chapter III, where he
systematically applies the Heine-derived categories: Barbarians (aristocracy),
Philistines (middle class), and Populace (working class). The Barbarian is
characterized not by savagery but by a defensive investment in the status quo —
"no aristocracy likes the system which tells it that its own class is not
sufficient for the wants of the community" [002-005].

### Sweetness Without Light

The Barbarian's limitation is not moral but intellectual. He has the "innate
faculty" for sweetness — the grace of manner that distinguishes the aristocrat
from the tradesman — but he has no corresponding faculty for the critical
examination of his own position. His "exterior culture" is, in Arnold's
judgment, a "dress" rather than a discipline [003-002].

## Historical Context

Arnold's use of "Barbarians" reflects the post-1832 political landscape, in
which aristocratic dominance had been challenged but not displaced. The Reform
Act of 1867, extending the franchise to working-class men, sharpened the
question of whether the aristocracy could continue to claim the right to govern
[002-005].
