## Coverage Notes

Matthew Arnold's *Culture and Anarchy*, though primarily a work of social criticism, ranges across a distinctive geography. The places referenced in the text span the institutions of English life — [[hyde-park|Hyde Park]], the [[east-of-london|East of London]], the manufacturing city of [[birmingham-place|Birmingham]] — and extend outward to the continental and classical reference points that structure Arnold's comparative imagination: [[france-place|France]], [[greece-place|Greece]], [[rome-place|Rome]], and [[ireland-place|Ireland]] as a site of English political anxiety. Each place serves Arnold's argument not merely as a location but as a symbol — of culture, of anarchy, of the possibilities and failures of civilisation.
