# Charles Bradlaugh

Charles Bradlaugh (1833–1891) was an English political activist,
secularist, and republican who served as Member of Parliament for
Northampton. A prominent free-thinker and founder of the National
Secular Society, Bradlaugh appears throughout *Culture and Anarchy*
(1869) as Arnold's recurring example of working-class political energy
in its "excess" — earnest, iconoclastic, and unguided by "right reason."

## Role in *Culture and Anarchy*

Bradlaugh is one of the most frequently cited working-class figures in
the book, mentioned nine times. Arnold uses him to illustrate the
argument that the working-class, like the middle and aristocratic
classes, is incapable of supplying an adequate centre of authority from
its own resources.

### The "Excess" of the Working-Class

In Chapter II, Arnold frames Bradlaugh through the Aristotelian
doctrine of the mean:

> Mr. Odger … may serve to give us the notion of defect, and Mr.
> Bradlaugh of excess, in the essential quality of a workman
> (PN 002-022)

Arnold continues:

> the virtue of the working-class is, as I have said, honesty; the
> virtuous mean of honesty is perhaps best shown in Mr. Odger … and
> the excess of that virtue is perhaps best shown in Mr. Bradlaugh,
> the iconoclast, who seems to me to be almost nothing else but
> honesty run to seed. (PN 002-022)

The phrase "honesty run to seed" captures Arnold's ambivalent
assessment: Bradlaugh's sincerity is not in question, but his
political expression of it lacks the "sweetness and light" of
culture.

### A Figure of Anarchy

In Chapter III, Arnold sharpens the contrast:

> we see in Mr. Odger the virtuous mean, and in Mr. Bradlaugh the
> excess. (PN 003-003)

Later in the same chapter, Bradlaugh becomes a symbol of disorder:

> Mr. Bradlaugh in his Hyde Park anarchy-mongering (PN 003-018)

This reference to the Hyde Park Riots of 1866 positions Bradlaugh as
a participant in the "anarchy" that Arnold's title names. Bradlaugh
represents the working-class acting on its "ordinary self" — the
untutored impulse that mistakes political agitation for genuine
improvement.

### Rhetorical Function

Bradlaugh appears consistently as a foil. Arnold pairs him with the
Rev. W. Cattle (a Protestant firebrand) as twin symbols of undisciplined
political passion:

> the Rev. W. Cattle on this side, Mr. Bradlaugh on that!—pull devil,
> pull baker! (PN 003-017)

The rhetorical strategy is clear: by placing a secularist republican
and a Protestant agitator on the same level, Arnold argues that
political energy, whatever its ideological content, is equally empty
without the guidance of "our best self."

## Connection to Arnold's Argument

Bradlaugh functions as the personification of Arnold's thesis that
"freedom to do as one likes" is insufficient. Bradlaugh's secularism,
his republicanism, his free-thought — all are, for Arnold, expressions
of liberty without culture. Arnold does not dispute Bradlaugh's right
to speak; he disputes the assumption that speaking forcefully is the
same as speaking wisely. Bradlaugh thus illustrates the core argument
of *Culture and Anarchy*: that political machinery, however democratic,
cannot substitute for the inward transformation that culture alone
can effect.
