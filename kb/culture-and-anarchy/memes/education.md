# Education

Education is, for Arnold, not merely a topic among others but the practical
expression of his entire cultural programme. If culture is "the pursuit of our
total perfection by means of getting to know the best that has been thought and
said," then education is the institutional form of that pursuit. And because it
is too important to be left to individual preference, education is also the
arena in which Arnold most vigorously insists on the necessity of State action.

## Arnold's Definition

Arnold's understanding of education is inseparable from his definition of
culture. The purpose of education is not vocational training or the transmission
of useful knowledge; it is the formation of "the best self" — the cultivation of
"all the powers which make the beauty and worth of human nature" [001-010].
Education is therefore the concrete answer to the question culture poses: how
shall a society organize itself so that every member may approach perfection?

## Usage Across the Text

### Education and the State

Arnold's most controversial argument about education is that it requires State
intervention. The middle-class Englishman's instinctive hostility to "State
action" — his conviction that education is a private matter — is, for Arnold,
a surrender to anarchy. He points to continental models, particularly Prussia,
where "the State" (not the Church, not private enterprise) "has the control of
education" [003-021]. The English reluctance to follow this model is, in
Arnold's view, a failure of nerve disguised as a defence of liberty.

### Middle-Class Education and Its Failures

Arnold's critique of middle-class education is withering. Mr. Bazley, a
Manchester manufacturer, had declared that "the middle classes had a most
admirable education" — a claim Arnold treats as evidence of the very defect it
denies [002-019]. The middle class, he argues, confuses the possession of useful
knowledge with the cultivation of intelligence. The result is a class that is
industrious and prosperous but incapable of "seeing things as they really are."

### Education as Antidote to Anarchy

Throughout the book, Arnold presents education as the alternative to anarchy. If
"doing as one likes" is the English disease, education is the cure: it
substitutes the authority of "the best self" for the anarchy of individual
preference [001-010]. The "machinery of our education" — the institutional
apparatus of schools, curricula, and inspection — is the mechanism by which
society transmits its ideal of perfection to each new generation [001-010].

## Historical Context

Arnold wrote at a watershed moment in English educational history. The Forster
Education Act of 1870 (published shortly after *Culture and Anarchy*) would
establish the framework for universal elementary education in England. Arnold
himself had served as a school inspector for nearly two decades by the time he
wrote the book, and his arguments about education are informed by direct
experience of the deficiencies of English schooling [P03-013, 001-020,
003-021–022].
