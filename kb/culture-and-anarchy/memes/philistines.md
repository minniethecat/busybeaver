# Philistines

The Philistines are, in Arnold's Heine-derived taxonomy, the English middle
class. The term is borrowed from German student slang, where *Philister* denoted
the unenlightened townspeople who stood outside the university's circle of
culture. Arnold's adaptation is both more precise and more generous: the
Philistine is earnest, industrious, and moral — but his notion of "the good" is
"narrow and inadequate," confined to the material and the sectarian [003-001].

## Arnold's Definition

Arnold's Philistine is defined by what he lacks: "sweetness and light." He
possesses energy, honesty, and practical intelligence in abundance; what he does
not possess is the "free play of consciousness" that characterizes the Hellenic
mind. The Philistine is Hebraising without being Hellenic — moral without being
intelligent, earnest without being cultivated [001-021].

| Attribute | Barbarians (Aristocracy) | Philistines (Middle Class) | Populace (Working Class) |
|-----------|--------------------------|---------------------------|--------------------------|
| **Defining virtue** | Courage, high spirit | Industry, earnestness | Energy, raw vigour |
| **Defining vice** | Inaccessibility to ideas | Resistance to "sweetness and light" | Unformed rawness |
| **Relation to authority** | Traditional deference | "Doing as one likes" | Emerging, unguided |
| **Cultural potential** | Low (entrenched) | Moderate (has energy + conscience) | High (unformed, malleable) |
| **Arnold's prescription** | Hellenise | Hellenise + broaden conscience | Hellenise + form |

Arnold's taxonomy is deliberately imprecise — he calls it a "humble attempt at a
scientific nomenclature" — because the boundaries are porous. Every class contains
"*aliens*": individuals who transcend their class character by virtue of curiosity
or cultivation. These aliens are, for Arnold, the seed of a genuinely common
culture [003-001].

## Usage Across the Text

### The Philistine Triumph

The Philistines, in Arnold's account, have become the dominant force in English
life. Their values — trade, respectability, Dissenting religion, "doing as one
likes" — set the tone of the nation. Arnold regards this as a national problem
because the Philistine "has not the idea of human perfection." He "makes
sweetness and light secondary" and "gives the main stress to what he calls
conduct" [003-001].

### Philistinism as a Cultural Attitude

Arnold distinguishes between Philistines (the class) and Philistinism (the
attitude). Philistinism is a temper of mind — "the strong, dogged,
unintelligent, Philistine way of doing business" — that can, in principle,
affect any class. But in England it has found its natural home in the middle
class, whose commercial success has convinced it that its way of seeing the
world is the only way [001-021].

### The Philistine and Religion

The Philistine's religion is, for Arnold, the key to both his strength and his
limitation. It is predominantly Nonconformist — Dissenting, Puritan, centred on
"the intense conviction of the preciousness of personal salvation." This gives
the Philistine a moral seriousness that Arnold genuinely respects. But it also
narrows his conception of human life to the "Hebraising" pole, excluding the
"spontaneity of consciousness" that Hellenism cultivates.

Arnold's critique here is deliberately subtle. He does not attack Dissent on
theological grounds. His objection is cultural: Nonconformity, because it has
no establishment to connect it to the broad stream of national life, produces
what he calls "a life of holes and corners" — intense but inward-turning,
passionate but provincial. The Philistine's religion is the religion of a class,
not of a nation; and this, for Arnold, is its fatal limitation [P03-008].

> "The great works by which, not only in literature, art, and science generally,
> but in religion itself, the human spirit has manifested its approaches to
> totality and to a full, harmonious perfection, and not in literature, art,
> and science only, but in religion too, come, not from Nonconformists, but from
> men who either belong to Establishments or have been trained in them"
> [P03-009].

## Historical Context

Arnold's use of "Philistines" entered English usage through his writing and
became one of his most durable contributions to the language. The term
resonated because it named something real: the cultural ascendancy of the
commercial middle class in mid-Victorian England, and the corresponding
marginalization of the classical and literary ideals Arnold called "culture."

Arnold borrowed the term from Heinrich Heine, who in turn took it from German
student slang. In the German universities, *Philister* was the townsman — solid,
respectable, and profoundly indifferent to the life of the mind. Arnold
transplanted the term to English soil and gave it a specifically Victorian
inflection: the Philistine was not merely the uneducated man but the man who,
having achieved material comfort and social respectability, mistakes these for
the whole of human flourishing.

The concept proved remarkably durable. By the end of the nineteenth century,
"Philistine" had become a standard term of English cultural criticism, used by
writers from John Ruskin to Oscar Wilde. It persists in contemporary usage as
a shorthand for hostility or indifference to the arts and intellectual life —
a testament to the precision and fertility of Arnold's original coinage.
