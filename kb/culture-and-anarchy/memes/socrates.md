# Socrates

Socrates (c. 470–399 BCE) was an Athenian philosopher whose method of
relentless questioning and commitment to self-knowledge made him the
founding figure of the Western philosophical tradition. In *Culture and
Anarchy*, Arnold invokes Socrates more often than any other classical
figure save Plato, and for a specific purpose: Socrates embodies the
Hellenic ideal of seeing things as they really are — the disinterested
play of mind that Arnold sets against the Hebraic impulse toward moral
earnestness and the modern impulse toward practical action.

## Identity

Socrates wrote nothing; his thought survives through the dialogues of
Plato and the memoirs of Xenophon. Arnold draws on both sources, but
favours Xenophon's Socrates — the "true Socrates of the Memorabilia"
[004-007] — precisely because Xenophon's version lacks the literary
polish of Plato's and presents a simpler, more direct portrait. This
preference is itself an Arnoldian move: the Xenophontic Socrates is less
a system-builder than a man who demonstrates a way of being, a
"disinterested play of consciousness upon stock notions and habits"
[006-047].

## Role in Arnold's Argument

Socrates appears at five strategic points in *Culture and Anarchy*, each
time serving a distinct argumentative function:

First, in the opening of the 1869 Preface, Arnold invokes Socrates's
maxim — "Know thyself!" — as the proper speech for a man of culture
addressing his countrymen [P04-002]. This is Arnold's declaration of
method: culture begins not with a political programme but with
self-knowledge.

Second, in Chapter I, Arnold groups Socrates and Plato (and classical
learning generally) with the "sweetness and light" of Hellenism, which
English Philistinism has systematically undervalued [001-021].

Third, in the crucial Hellenism-Hebraism chapter, Arnold uses Socrates to
mark the deepest difference between the two tendencies. "To St. Paul it
appears possible to 'hold the truth in unrighteousness,' which is just
what Socrates judged impossible" [004-006]. For Socrates, to know the good
is to do it; for Hebraism, knowing and doing are radically distinct. This
single contrast encapsulates the entire argument of the book.

Fourth, Arnold dwells on the Xenophontic Socrates's definition of the best
life: "The best man is he who most tries to perfect himself, and the
happiest man is he who most feels that he is perfecting himself." This,
Arnold says, "has something so simple, spontaneous, and unsophisticated
about it, that it seems to fill us with clearness and hope" [004-007]. But
he immediately balances this with the Hebraic objection — attributed to
Carlyle — that "Socrates is terribly at ease in Zion." The Hellenic
confidence in reason's sufficiency must contend with the Hebraic
consciousness of sin.

Fifth, in the Conclusion, Arnold returns to Socrates as the model of
influence without office. "Socrates is poisoned and dead; but in his own
breast does not every man carry about with him a possible Socrates, in
that power of a disinterested play of consciousness upon his stock notions
and habits?" [006-047]. Culture operates not through institutions or
programmes but through the awakening of this inward Socratic capacity.

## Key Passages

**On self-knowledge as cultural method**: "I do not wish to see men of
culture asking to be entrusted with power… the speech most proper, at
present, for a man of culture to make to a body of his fellow-countrymen
who get him into a committee-room, is Socrates's: Know thyself!"
[P04-002].

**On the divergence of Hellenism and Hebraism**: "To St. Paul it appears
possible to 'hold the truth in unrighteousness,' which is just what
Socrates judged impossible. The moral virtues, on the other hand, are with
Aristotle but the porch and access to the intellectual" [004-006].

**On the Xenophontic Socrates**: "This account of the matter by Socrates,
the true Socrates of the Memorabilia, has something so simple,
spontaneous, and unsophisticated about it, that it seems to fill us with
clearness and hope when we hear it" [004-007].

**On the inward Socrates**: "In his own breast does not every man carry
about with him a possible Socrates, in that power of a disinterested play
of consciousness upon his stock notions and habits, of which this wise
and admirable man gave all through his lifetime the great example?"
[006-047].

## Relationship to Arnold's Framework

Socrates is the living embodiment of Arnold's central value: the free play
of consciousness. He is not a systematic philosopher (Arnold explicitly
distinguishes culture from "a philosophic system"), nor a political actor,
nor a religious teacher. He is, instead, the man who asks questions — who
insists that we examine our "stock notions and habits" before we act on
them. This is precisely what Arnold means by "culture" as opposed to
machinery, Hebraism, or Jacobinism.

The Socratic inheritance also allows Arnold to negotiate his relationship
to Christianity. By using Socrates as a counterpoint to St. Paul, Arnold
neither rejects Christianity nor subordinates reason to faith; instead,
he insists that the two currents — Hellenism and Hebraism — are both
necessary and must be held in fruitful tension. Socrates makes it possible
to be a Victorian liberal and a serious moralist without collapsing either
into the other.
