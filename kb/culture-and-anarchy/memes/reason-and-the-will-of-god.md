# Reason and the Will of God

The motto of Arnoldian culture — "to make reason and the will of God prevail" — combining the intellectual and moral dimensions of the pursuit of perfection into a single commanding imperative (001-002).

## Arnold's Source: Bishop Wilson

Arnold takes the phrase from Bishop Thomas Wilson (1663–1755), an Anglican divine whose *Maxims of Piety and Christianity* provided Arnold with a language for expressing the religious dimension of culture without denominational narrowness:

> Only, whereas the passion for doing good is apt to be overhasty in determining what reason and the will of God say, because its turn is for acting rather than thinking, what distinguishes culture is that it is possessed by the scientific passion as well as by the passion of doing good. (001-002)

Wilson's phrase serves Arnold's purpose perfectly because it fuses two things that modern thought tends to separate: reason (the classical, Hellenic, intellectual standard) and the will of God (the biblical, Hebraic, moral standard). In Arnold's hands, the phrase becomes a way of saying that true religion and true culture converge on the same point.

## Reason and the Will of God in Practice

The practical meaning of the phrase is that culture is not merely the pursuit of knowledge for its own sake. It is knowledge in the service of right action:

> Culture, then, is the pursuit of sweetness and light. He who works for sweetness and light, works to make reason and the will of God prevail. (001-023)

This formulation answers the charge that culture is selfish or merely aesthetic. Culture is action-oriented: it seeks to reshape the world according to the best understanding available.

## The Distinction Between Right Reason and Mere Opinion

A crucial function of Arnold's standard is to distinguish between what people *think* is reason and the will of God and what actually *is*. The danger, Arnold warns, is that people "take their own conceptions, which proceed from their own state of development and share in all the imperfections and immaturities of this, for a basis of action" (001-002). Culture provides the critical tools to prevent this confusion.

## Historical Context

Bishop Wilson was a figure of the late Stuart and early Hanoverian church, known for his pastoral devotion and his *Maxims*, which were widely read in the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries. Arnold's choice of Wilson rather than a more contemporary theologian was strategic: Wilson represented a moderate, reasonable Anglicanism that could appeal across denominational lines.
