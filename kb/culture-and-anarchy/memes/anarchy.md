# Anarchy

The titular antagonist of Arnold's essay — not a revolutionary movement but the pervasive social condition in which private inclination has displaced public reason as the organizing principle of English life (002-005).

## Arnold's Definition

Anarchy, for Arnold, is not primarily a political phenomenon. It is a cultural and spiritual condition. The anarchic principle is "doing as one likes" — the belief that each individual's private judgment is sufficient, that no external standard (of reason, of tradition, of authority) need be consulted:

> The anarchy which culture seeks to cure is not the anarchy of the streets — though it may lead to that — but the anarchy of the spirit. (002-019)

Arnold's anarchy, then, is not about bombs and barricades. It is about the refusal to acknowledge any standard beyond personal preference. It is what happens when Hebraism loses its moral content and Hellenism is entirely absent.

## Anarchy and the English Character

Arnold's diagnosis is specifically English. The Englishman's "right to do what he likes" is, he says, "in fact, his religion" (002-019). This is anarchy in Arnold's special sense: not chaos, but the principled rejection of principle. Every Englishman becomes his own pope, his own parliament, his own arbiter of truth.

The consequences, Arnold argues, are everywhere visible: in the violence of the Hyde Park riots, in the shallowness of English public discourse, in the worship of "machinery" as though it were an end in itself, in the class warfare that prevents any conception of the common good.

## Culture as the Antidote

Culture is Arnold's answer to anarchy. Where anarchy asserts the sufficiency of private judgment, culture insists on the discipline of the best that has been thought and said. Where anarchy is the rule of the "ordinary self," culture cultivates the "best self":

> Culture suggests the idea of the State. We find no basis for a firm State-power in our ordinary selves; culture suggests one to us in our best self. (002-017)

The state, properly understood, is the institutional embodiment of our collective best self — and thus the practical remedy for anarchy.

## Anarchy and the Classes

Arnold is careful to note that anarchy is not the fault of any one class. The aristocracy practices it by hereditary right, the middle class by philosophical conviction, and the working class by sheer numbers. All three contribute to the "anarchy" that blocks the emergence of a genuine common good:

> Our governing classes have not the notion of the State, of the nation's collective best self, which culture begets. (002-016)

## Historical Context

Arnold wrote *Culture and Anarchy* in the aftermath of the Hyde Park Riots of 1866 and during the debates over the Second Reform Bill (1867). His contemporaries feared that extending the franchise would unleash mob rule. Arnold's argument is more subtle: the real danger is not the mob but the absence of any standard by which to judge the mob's demands — or anyone else's.
