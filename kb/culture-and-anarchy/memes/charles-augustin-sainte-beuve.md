# Charles Augustin Sainte-Beuve

Charles Augustin Sainte-Beuve (1804–1869) was a celebrated French literary critic whose critical method [[matthew-arnold|Matthew Arnold]] discusses at length in the opening pages of *Culture and Anarchy*. Arnold uses Sainte-Beuve to explore the meaning of **curiosity** as a motive for [[culture]] [001-001].

## Identity

Sainte-Beuve was the dominant literary critic of nineteenth-century France, known for his biographical method of criticism — the *portrait littéraire* — and his weekly essays collected as *Causeries du Lundi* ("Monday Chats"). His approach sought to understand literary works through the life, psychology, and social circumstances of their authors, a method that placed him at the centre of French intellectual life for three decades.

Sainte-Beuve's own career had a complicated relationship to institutions. He began as a Romantic poet associated with Victor Hugo's circle, moved through a period of Saint-Simonian socialism, and eventually established himself as the voice of critical authority in the Second Empire. By the time Arnold wrote *Culture and Anarchy*, Sainte-Beuve was the model of what a critic could be in a culture that took criticism seriously — a position that had no equivalent in England.

## The Curiosity Distinction

Arnold opens *Culture and Anarchy* by invoking Sainte-Beuve to draw a distinction between two senses of curiosity — the frivolous and the noble:

> "In the Quarterly Review, some little time ago, was an estimate of the celebrated French critic, Monsieur Sainte-Beuve, and a very inadequate estimate it, in my judgment, was. And its inadequacy consisted chiefly in this: that in our English way it left out of sight the double sense really involved in the word curiosity, thinking enough was said to stamp Monsieur Sainte-Beuve with blame if it was said that he was impelled in his operations as a critic by curiosity." [001-001]

The *Quarterly Review*'s error is characteristically English: it applies the English sense of "curiosity" (idle inquisitiveness, prying into private matters) to a French critic whose practice was animated by the French sense of *curiosité* — a "liberal and intelligent eagerness about the things of the mind." Arnold's point is that the English language itself is an obstacle to understanding: its vocabulary lacks the resources to name certain kinds of intellectual virtue.

## The Opening Gambit

The Sainte-Beuve passage is strategically placed at the very beginning of Arnold's argument — it is the first substantive point he makes in the entire book. This placement is significant: before Arnold discusses machinery, Hebraism, or the State, he first establishes what genuine intellectual curiosity looks like.

The opening move is a defence not of Sainte-Beuve specifically but of the intellectual life itself. By showing that the English cannot even recognise intellectual virtue when they see it, Arnold prepares the ground for his larger argument about the poverty of English intellectual culture. The *Quarterly Review*'s attack on Sainte-Beuve is a miniature of everything Arnold finds wrong with English public discourse: provincial, incurious, and incapable of distinguishing the frivolous from the serious.

## Curiosity and Culture

The term *curiosity* — in its elevated, Sainte-Beuvian sense — names for Arnold "the desire after the things of the mind simply for their own sakes and for the pleasure of seeing them as they are" [001-001]. This is the motive force of [[culture]]: the disinterested love of knowledge that distinguishes the genuinely cultivated mind from the mere accumulator of information.

Sainte-Beuve thus serves as the positive exemplar of the intellectual disposition that Arnold wishes to cultivate in his English readers. He is, in Arnold's scheme, the embodiment of [[hellenism|Hellenism]] in critical practice — seeing things as they really are, without the distortion of dogma, party, or practical calculation.

## The French Model

Sainte-Beuve's presence at the opening of *Culture and Anarchy* signals another of Arnold's persistent themes: the value of looking beyond England for intellectual models. Throughout his career, Arnold held up French intellectual culture — and particularly the French tradition of the critic — as a reproach to English provincialism. The French Academy, the *Revue des Deux Mondes*, Sainte-Beuve's *Lundis*: these were institutions and practices that made serious intellectual life possible in ways that had no English counterpart.

Arnold's francophilia is not uncritical — he is well aware of French faults — but it is strategic. By invoking a French critic as his opening example, Arnold immediately establishes that his frame of reference is European, not merely English. The reader who opens *Culture and Anarchy* is being invited to see English life from the outside.

## Relationship to Arnold's Framework

Sainte-Beuve represents the [[hellenism|Hellenic]] spirit of free intellectual inquiry that Arnold contrasts with English [[hebraism|Hebraism]]. He is one of the few figures in the book whom Arnold cites with unqualified admiration. Sainte-Beuve's critical practice — seeing things as they really are, without prejudice or practical motive — is precisely what Arnold means by [[culture]].

The Sainte-Beuve passage also introduces a method that Arnold employs throughout the book: the close reading of a critical judgment to reveal the assumptions behind it. Arnold does not simply assert that the *Quarterly Review* is wrong about Sainte-Beuve; he analyses the error to show what it reveals about English intellectual habits. This is the method of the critic as educator.
