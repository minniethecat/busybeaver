The Reform League was a working-class political organization established in 1865 to agitate for the extension of the suffrage to all adult men. It played a central role in the reform agitation of 1866–1867, including the Hyde Park Riots of July 1866, and is discussed by Arnold in *Culture and Anarchy* as a manifestation of the Populace's entry into political life — characterized by passionate action and class interest rather than by the disinterested pursuit of perfection.

## Nature

The Reform League was a political pressure group organized on a national scale, with branches across England and strong ties to trade unions and working-men's associations. It stood in contrast to the more middle-class Reform Union, advocating for universal manhood suffrage rather than the more limited household-suffrage proposals. Its leaders included Edmond Beales, who served as its president, and its methods combined mass demonstrations, petitioning, and public oratory.

## Role in Arnold's Argument

Arnold treats the Reform League as emblematic of the Populace's political character. When he discusses the League's orators, he emphasizes that their rhetoric — "inveighing against our cruel and bloated aristocracy" — reflects the "passions and point of view of the Populace" rather than any genuine appeal to right reason or the best self (PN 003-014). The League, in Arnold's framework, operates from a class standpoint: it is "the working-class" that speaks through the League, with what Arnold grants are its "brightest powers of sympathy and readiest powers of action" (PN 002-013).

Yet Arnold also sees the Reform League as inseparable from what he calls "anarchy" — the tendency to do as one likes in the political sphere. The Hyde Park affair, in which Reform Leaguers tore down the park railings to hold their meeting, is the most dramatic instance of this tendency in the book. Arnold's point is not that the League's goals are wrong but that its methods and motives lack the Hellenic element of intellectual cultivation.

## Key Passages

Arnold discusses the Reform League in Chapter II, "Doing as One Likes," where it exemplifies the "anarchy" of unregulated popular action:

> "For the Reform League, it is the working-class, with its 'brightest powers of sympathy and readiest powers of action'" (PN 002-013)

The Hyde Park episode is central:

> "Reform Leaguers bent on assembling to promote universal suffrage broke through the iron rails encompassing the Park" (PN 002-028)

In Chapter III, Arnold analyzes the League's rhetoric and its class character:

> "When the Reform League orators inveigh against our cruel and bloated aristocracy, these invectives so evidently show the passions and point of view of the Populace" (PN 003-014)

## Historical Context

The Reform League was founded in February 1865 by a coalition of trade unionists and radicals, with Edmond Beales as president. Its great moment came during the reform crisis of 1866–1867, culminating in the Hyde Park demonstration of 23 July 1866, when the government prohibited a planned mass meeting and the League's supporters tore down the park railings in protest. The League dissolved in 1869 after the passage of the Reform Act 1867, which, while falling short of universal manhood suffrage, significantly expanded the electorate. Arnold's treatment of the League reflects the widespread anxiety among the educated classes about working-class political mobilization.
