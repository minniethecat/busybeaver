## Arnold's Definition

*Culture and Anarchy* is a collection of essays by [[matthew-arnold]], first published as a book in 1869. Its full title — *Culture and Anarchy: An Essay in Political and Social Criticism* — signals Arnold's central purpose: to advance culture as a corrective to the social and political disorders of mid-Victorian England. The essays originally appeared in the *[[british-banner|British Banner]]*, gathered and substantially revised for book publication with a new Preface and Preamble [P01-004].

The work is structured as a sustained critique of what Arnold termed "doing as one likes" — the prevailing English instinct to elevate individual liberty above all other goods. Against this, Arnold sets culture as "the study of perfection," a pursuit of the harmonious development of all sides of human nature that requires the state to serve as a centre of authority and right reason [P02-001].

## Central Arguments

### Sweetness and Light

Arnold borrows the phrase "sweetness and light" from Jonathan Swift, re-purposing it to describe the twofold character of culture: beauty (sweetness) and intelligence (light). Together, these constitute the Hellenic ideal of human perfection — the harmonious and complete development of human faculties [001-001].

### The Three Classes

Arnold divides English society into three classes, none of which he finds adequate to the task of governance:

- **Barbarians** — the [[aristocracy]], preoccupied with external graces and field sports, lacking in ideas
- **Philistines** — the [[middle-class]], self-satisfied, narrowly religious, devoted to machinery and external prosperity
- **Populace** — the working class, raw and undeveloped, alternately servile and violent

Each class, Arnold argues, is blinded by its own characteristic defects. Only culture, operating through an ideal centre of authority, can transcend class partiality [003-001–003-005].

### Hebraism and Hellenism

The distinction between [[hebraism]] and [[hellenism]] forms the philosophical core of the work. Hebraism prizes conduct and obedience — "doing" — while Hellenism prizes knowledge and vision — "knowing." Arnold contends that English civilisation has over-emphasised Hebraism, producing a culture of strict moral conscience but impoverished intellect. The remedy is a renewed attention to Hellenism: seeing things as they really are [004-001].

## Usage Across the Text

Arnold uses "culture and anarchy" as the organising polarity of his argument. Culture represents the pursuit of total human perfection through "getting to know, on all the matters which most concern us, the best which has been thought and said in the world" [P02-002]. Anarchy represents the consequences of unguided individual liberty — the Hyde Park riots of 1866, [[hyde-park|the Reform League demonstrations]], and the broader tendency of English society to mistake freedom for licence [002-009].

## Historical Context

*Culture and Anarchy* appeared at a pivotal moment in Victorian history. The [[reform-crisis-1866-1867|Reform crisis of 1866–67]] had raised the prospect of working-class enfranchisement; the Hyde Park demonstrations had dramatised the fragility of public order. Arnold wrote against the background of these events, positioning his argument as a response to both the complacency of the middle class and the perceived threat of popular democracy.

The work engages with numerous contemporary figures, including [[frederic-harrison]] (Arnold's liberal critic), [[john-bright]] (who mocked culture as "a smattering of the two dead languages"), and [[john-henry-newman]] (whose Oxford Movement Arnold treats as a precursor to his own cultural critique).

## Reception and Influence

*Culture and Anarchy* has exercised a lasting influence on English literary and social criticism. The book's central terms — "sweetness and light," "[[philistines]]," "[[barbarians]]," "[[hebraism]]" and "[[hellenism]]" — have entered the vocabulary of cultural commentary, even as the specifics of Arnold's argument have been contested.

Contemporary reviewers were divided. [[frederic-harrison]] praised Arnold's style while rejecting his political conclusions; others, particularly among Nonconformist readers, took offence at Arnold's characterisation of English Dissent as narrow and provincial. In the twentieth century, critics such as [[t-s-eliot]] and [[f-r-leavis]] engaged seriously with Arnold's vision of culture as a corrective to materialism, while more recent scholarship has examined the work's relationship to Victorian imperialism, class ideology, and the emergence of English as an academic discipline.

The phrase "sweetness and light" — borrowed from Swift's *The Battle of the Books* (1704), where it describes the bees' productions of honey and wax — became so closely associated with Arnold that it is now remembered as his coinage. Similarly, Arnold's use of "Philistine" to denote the culturally indifferent middle class has become the standard English meaning of the term, displacing its original biblical reference.
