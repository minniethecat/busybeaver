## Arnold's Definition

In *[[culture-and-anarchy]]*, [[matthew-arnold]] invokes the Old Testament as the foundational text of [[hebraism]] — the tradition of moral rigour and strict conscience that has shaped English religious culture. Arnold treats the Old Testament not primarily as a revealed scripture but as the expression of a particular orientation towards human life: one that prioritises conduct over knowledge, obedience over intellectual inquiry, and the consciousness of sin over the apprehension of beauty [004-007].

## Usage Across the Text

### The Discipline of the Old Testament

In Chapter IV, Arnold draws a sharp distinction between the Old Testament and the New Testament as representative of two modes of moral discipline:

> The discipline of the Old Testament may be summed up as a discipline teaching us to abhor and flee from sin; the discipline of the New Testament, as a discipline teaching us to die to it. [004-007]

This formulation is central to Arnold's analysis of Hebraism. The Old Testament represents the initial stage of moral development — the awakening to sin as a positive, active entity. The New Testament represents the advanced stage — the interiorisation of moral discipline to the point of dying to sin itself. Arnold presents both as necessary, but argues that English culture has remained arrested at the Old Testament stage, fixated on external rules and prohibitions rather than achieving the inward transformation that the New Testament demands [004-007].

### American Biblical Interpretation

In Chapter VI, Arnold returns to the Old Testament in his critique of contemporary liberal reform. He observes that his American contemporaries — particularly the Mormons — appeal directly to the Old Testament patriarchs as models for their marital practices. Arnold presents this as a characteristic example of what happens when Hebraism is detached from the moderating influence of [[hellenism]]:

> With that affinity of genius to the Hebrew genius which we have already noticed, and with the strong belief of our race that liberty is the law of human life, so far as a fixed, perfect, and paramount rule of conscience, the Bible, does not expressly control it, our American kinsmen go again, Mr. Hepworth Dixon tells us, to their Bible, the Mormons to the patriarchs and the Old Testament. [006-015]

Arnold's point is that the unchecked Hebraic impulse — reading the Old Testament without the guidance of culture, historical criticism, or Hellenic reason — produces grotesque results. Liberty, ungoverned by [[right-reason]], becomes licence [006-015].

## Relationship to Hebraism

The Old Testament functions in Arnold's argument as the textual embodiment of the Hebraic spirit. Hebraism, as Arnold defines it, is not identical with the Old Testament, but the Old Testament provides its characteristic language, imagery, and moral vocabulary. The "energy of faith" and the "intense and convinced energy" with which the Hebrew throws himself upon his ideal — these are qualities Arnold associates specifically with the Old Testament tradition [004-006–004-007].

Arnold's Hebraism is essentially an Old Testament religion: a religion of law, conscience, and the strenuous pursuit of righteousness. It is the religion of the prophets rather than the psalmists — of ethical demand rather than contemplative wonder. This is both its strength and, in Arnold's analysis, its limitation.

## The Puritan Reading Tradition

Arnold's critique of the Old Testament's role in English culture is inseparable from his critique of [[puritanism]] and [[nonconformity]]. The English Puritan tradition, he argues, read the Old Testament as a legal code rather than as a spiritual document. The result was a religious culture that excelled at moral discipline but atrophied in intellectual and aesthetic development.

This reading tradition explains, for Arnold, why English [[hebraism]] proved so resistant to [[hellenism]]. The Old Testament, read literally and legalistically, provides a complete system of conduct that leaves no room for the free play of intelligence. The Puritan who knows what Leviticus forbids has no need to ask what beauty requires.

## Historical Context

Arnold's treatment of the Old Testament reflects the broader Victorian engagement with biblical criticism. The mid-nineteenth century saw the rise of German higher criticism, geological challenges to biblical chronology, and the publication of works such as [[john-william-colenso|Bishop Colenso]]'s critical examination of the Pentateuch. Arnold, while not a biblical scholar himself, participated in this climate of inquiry by treating the Old Testament as a human document — a record of the Hebraic genius — rather than as a repository of inerrant propositions.

At the same time, Arnold's deep familiarity with the Old Testament — evident in his easy movement between the prophets, the historical books, and the wisdom literature — reflects his own formation in a culture saturated with the language of the [[king-james-bible|King James Bible]]. His argument is not against the Old Testament but against a particular way of reading it.
