# Culture

The master-concept of Arnold's entire social and critical project — not mere cultivation or learning, but the systematic pursuit of total human perfection through the free play of consciousness upon all available knowledge (001-002).

## Arnold's Definition

Arnold offers multiple formulations of culture, each emphasizing a different dimension. Culture is "the study of perfection" (001-002). It "places human perfection in an internal condition, in the growth and predominance of our humanity proper" (001-005). Its motto, from Bishop Wilson, is "to make reason and the will of God prevail" (001-002).

Perhaps the most enduring definition is the one that appears in the Preface: culture is the pursuit of "total perfection by means of getting to know, on all the matters which most concern us, the best which has been thought and said in the world, and, through this knowledge, turning a stream of fresh and free thought upon our stock notions and habits" (P03-003).

## Curiosity vs. Perfection

Arnold distinguishes his conception of culture from mere intellectual curiosity. The disparagers of culture, he notes, "make its motive curiosity; sometimes, indeed, they make its motive mere exclusiveness and vanity" (001-001). But true culture is driven by something deeper — "the love of perfection" (001-002).

This means culture has both a scientific and a moral dimension. It seeks to know things as they truly are (the scientific passion), but it also seeks to make things better (the moral and social passion). The two are inseparable: one cannot improve the world without first understanding it.

## Culture as Criticism

A key function of culture is critical — it subjects all ideas, institutions, and habits to the test of reason and the best thinking of humanity. Arnold calls this "the free play of consciousness" or "the free play of the mind upon all subjects" (001-006). Culture refuses to accept any idea simply because it is familiar, convenient, or endorsed by authority. It insists on seeing things as they really are.

This critical function makes culture inherently unsettling to those invested in the status quo. Arnold acknowledges that culture "seeks to do away with classes" and "to make the best that has been thought and known in the world current everywhere" (P03-003). It is a democratizing force.

## The Social Dimension

Arnold insists that culture is not selfish or individualistic. Because "men are all members of one great whole," individual perfection is impossible without general perfection (001-005). The cultured person is obliged "to carry others along with him in his march towards perfection" (001-005). This social imperative distinguishes Arnoldian culture from mere aestheticism or intellectual dandyism.

## Culture's Enemies

Arnold identifies several forces that oppose or caricature culture: "machinery" (the worship of external means like wealth and industry), "doing as one likes" (the Englishman's insistence on personal liberty without regard for reason or the common good), and the various forms of class spirit and party zeal that substitute prejudice for thought.

> The whole scope of the essay is to recommend culture as the great help out of our present difficulties; culture being a pursuit of our total perfection by means of getting to know the best which has been thought and said. (P03-003)
