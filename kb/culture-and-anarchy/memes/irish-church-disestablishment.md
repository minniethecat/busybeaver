The disestablishment of the Church of Ireland was a major legislative reform carried out by William Ewart Gladstone's first government in 1869, severing the legal connection between the Church of Ireland (the Anglican church in Ireland) and the British state. Matthew Arnold discusses the disestablishment proceedings extensively in Chapter VI of *Culture and Anarchy*, using it alongside the Real Estate Intestacy Bill as an example of reform driven by mechanical impulses rather than right reason.

## Overview

The Church of Ireland, though it served only a small minority of the Irish population (approximately 12%), was the established state church, funded by tithes levied on the entire population, including the Roman Catholic majority and Presbyterian minority. The movement for disestablishment gained momentum throughout the 1860s, driven by Irish nationalism, Nonconformist opposition to religious establishments, and Gladstone's conviction that the arrangement was unjust. The Irish Church Act 1869, passed after Gladstone's 1868 electoral victory, disestablished the church effective 1 January 1871.

## Arnold's Treatment

Arnold opens Chapter VI, "Our Liberal Practitioners," by acknowledging the widespread enthusiasm for the disestablishment proceedings, which he calls "the operation of the present day which most attracts the general attention and excites people's feelings" (PN 006-001). However, he quickly pivots to a characteristic critique: the enthusiasm for disestablishment comes not from a genuine love of reason or perfection, but from the Nonconformists' "antipathy to religious establishments and endowments" (PN 006-018).

For Arnold, the disestablishment campaign exemplifies the Hebraic tendency in English life — a concern with right conduct and moral duty that operates without the balancing influence of Hellenic intelligence. The Nonconformists pursue disestablishment because they feel it is morally right, not because they have examined the question through the lens of "sweetness and light." Arnold does not oppose disestablishment itself; rather, he critiques the intellectual poverty of the motives driving it.

## Key Passages

Arnold's treatment of disestablishment in Chapter VI is interwoven with his discussion of the Real Estate Intestacy Bill, presenting both as operations that need "Hellenising":

> "The proceedings for the disestablishment of the Irish Church, which we are now witnessing" — these excite the general attention most among present-day operations (PN 006-001)

> "We Hellenised with the Real Estate Intestacy Bill, and with the disestablishment of the Irish Church by the power of the Nonconformists' antipathy to religious establishments and endowments, and see whether what our reprovers beautifully call ministering to the diseased spirit of our time is best done by the Hellenising method of proceeding, or by the other" (PN 006-018)

He returns to the theme later in the chapter, acknowledging that the "present actual operation for the disestablishment of the Irish Church is fruitful and solid" (PN 006-037), while still insisting that the progressive approach of "our Liberal friends" lacks the intellectual depth that culture would provide.

## Historical Significance

The Irish Church Act 1869 was one of the landmark reforms of Gladstone's first ministry and a significant step in the long process of dismantling the Anglican confessional state in the United Kingdom. It removed the Church of Ireland's entitlement to tithes, ended its representation in the House of Lords, and significantly reduced its property holdings while providing compensation. The Act was a crucial victory for Irish nationalism and for Nonconformist political influence in British politics.

In Arnold's cultural criticism, the disestablishment serves as a test case for what he saw as the central weakness of Victorian liberalism: the substitution of moral earnestness (Hebraism) for intellectual cultivation (Hellenism) as the guiding principle of social reform.
