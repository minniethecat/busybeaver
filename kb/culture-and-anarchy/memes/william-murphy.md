# William Murphy

William Murphy (1834–1872) was an Irish-born Protestant lecturer who
gained notoriety in the 1860s for anti-Catholic lectures delivered in
Birmingham and other English towns, frequently provoking riots. Arnold
invokes him seven times in *Culture and Anarchy* (1869) as an exemplar
of religious passion ungoverned by "right reason."

## Role in *Culture and Anarchy*

Arnold pairs Murphy with the Rev. W. Cattle as twin examples of
Hebraism run amok — moral earnestness severed from the "free play of
consciousness" that Hellenism provides.

### "Something Primary and Sacred"

Arnold's first reference is characteristically ironic:

> Mr. Murphy lectures at Birmingham, and showers on the Catholic
> population of that town words which blow against them in sparks
> and smoke (PN 002-007)

The metaphor of "sparks and smoke" suggests that Murphy's lectures
generate heat without light — passion without understanding. Arnold
immediately connects this to his larger argument about the inadequacy
of "doing as one likes":

> we want to know whether Mr. Murphy's notion of his right to do as
> he likes (PN 002-008)

### The Magnetic Power of Unreason

In Chapter III, Arnold observes the effect Murphy has on his audience:

> the magnetic power which Mr. Murphy exercises on Birmingham
> Protestants (PN 003-013)

The word "magnetic" is carefully chosen — it suggests an irrational
attraction, the kind of force that operates below the level of reasoned
argument. Murphy's popularity is precisely what condemns him in
Arnold's eyes, because it demonstrates the public's incapacity to
distinguish genuine spiritual authority from mere vehemence.

### Murphy and the Failure of Hebraism

In Chapter V, Arnold returns to Murphy as evidence that Hebraism, when
isolated from Hellenism, degenerates into sectarian rancour:

> Mr. Murphy, the Birmingham lecturer … cannot possibly see that
> Protestantism is not the whole of human life (PN 005-004)

This is Arnold's central critique: Murphy's Protestantism is not wrong
in its convictions but in its narrowness. It mistakes a part of human
life for the whole. The Hebraic impulse to moral earnestness, when not
balanced by the Hellenic impulse to see things as they really are,
produces not righteousness but bigotry.

## Connection to Arnold's Argument

Murphy and [[charles-bradlaugh|Charles Bradlaugh]] form a matched pair
in Arnold's rhetoric: Murphy represents religious passion without
culture; Bradlaugh represents secular passion without culture. By
showing that both the Protestant agitator and the atheist republican
produce the same intellectual emptiness, Arnold argues that the fault
lies not in any particular creed but in the absence of "sweetness and
light" — the disciplined pursuit of perfection that culture alone
provides.
