# Middle Class

The middle class occupies the center of Arnold's social critique in *Culture and
Anarchy*. It is, in his view, the "great representative of trade and
Dissent" (002-004) — the class whose values had come to dominate Victorian
England, and whose limitations therefore constituted a national problem of the
first order. Arnold's analysis of the middle class is arguably the most
influential social diagnosis in nineteenth-century English letters: it gave the
language the term "Philistine" as a sociological category, and it established a
pattern of middle-class self-criticism that would echo through E.M. Forster,
George Orwell, and beyond.

## Arnold's Definition

Arnold adapts Heine's term "Philistines" — originally a dismissive label used by
German university students for the unenlightened townspeople — to name the
English middle class. For Arnold, the Philistine is defined not by want of
intelligence but by want of "sweetness and light": he is earnest, industrious,
and moral, but his notion of perfection is "narrow and inadequate" (002-014).
The middle-class Dissenter, in particular, embodies the Hebraising impulse
divorced from Hellenic balance (P03-016).

### The Hebraic Core

The middle class's defining characteristic, in Arnold's analysis, is its
Hebraism — its intense preoccupation with moral conduct, its suspicion of
intellectual play, its conviction that "sweetness and light" are, at best,
optional luxuries and, at worst, sinful distractions. This Hebraism is the
product of the Puritan inheritance, transmitted through the Dissenting chapels
and evangelical movements that shaped middle-class religious culture (P03-016).
The middle-class Philistine is not immoral; he is *merely* moral — moral without
being intelligent, earnest without being cultivated.

### The Structural Position

Arnold's analysis of the middle class is structural, not personal. He is not
criticizing individual middle-class people; he is diagnosing the position of a
class that has achieved economic and political dominance without having
cultivated the intelligence that dominance requires. The middle class "has by
this time grown conscious" of its political power, but its idea of reform is
confined to institutional adjustments — Free Trade, Parliamentary
representation, the abolition of church rates — that never touch the inward
condition of the nation (002-017–018).

## Usage Across the Text

### The Ascendancy of Trade and Dissent

Arnold traces the middle class's rise to the Reformation, which in England
produced a peculiar alliance between the Puritan temper and commercial
prosperity. The result was a class "with many virtues, but without the idea of
human perfection" (001-019–020). Trade became the national religion, and the
middle class, having made England materially prosperous, claimed the right to
determine what England ought to be (002-004).

### Middle-Class Liberalism and Its Limits

Arnold's critique of middle-class liberalism — "the great middle-class
liberalism" of his youth (001-019) — is that it mistakes machinery for the end.
The middle class achieved its political victories (the Reform Act, the repeal of
the Corn Laws, the disestablishment of the Irish Church) and assumed that these
victories constituted moral progress. Arnold's reply is that they constitute
only the *conditions* for moral progress, and that a class that confuses the two
will govern badly (002-017–018).

### The Hebraising Impulse

The middle class, shaped by Puritanism, exhibits what Arnold calls the
Hebraising impulse at its most intense: a preoccupation with conduct and
obedience, coupled with suspicion of the free play of mind. This makes the
middle class "Hebraising" without being truly Hellenic (P03-016) — moral without
being intelligent, earnest without being cultivated.

## Counter-Arguments

Arnold's middle-class readers were not, on the whole, persuaded. The *Saturday
Review* — Arnold's "old adversary" — accused him of being an "elegant Jeremiad"
who had mistaken his own aesthetic preferences for universal truths. Later
critics, from Raymond Williams to Stefan Collini, have noted that Arnold's
category of "Philistine" is suspiciously capacious — it covers everyone from the
factory owner to the Dissenting minister to the provincial journalist, and the
very breadth of the category suggests it may be more a rhetorical device than an
empirical observation. Arnold's defenders reply that the category's breadth is
its strength: "Philistinism" names a disposition, not a demographic, and the
disposition is real — and recognizable — regardless of how many people exhibit
it.

## Historical Context

Arnold writes in the aftermath of the 1867 Reform Act, which extended the
franchise to urban working-class men and threatened to dilute middle-class
political dominance. The middle class's anxiety about its position — its
"incomparable Parliament" (002-013) — informs Arnold's diagnosis. He sees the
middle class as having won the political argument without ever having cultivated
the intelligence to govern well (002-004, 002-009).

The deeper context is the transformation of English society by industrial
capitalism. The middle class that Arnold anatomizes was a historical novelty: a
class that had made its fortune in trade and manufacture, that worshipped at
Dissenting chapels rather than Anglican churches, and that saw in Free Trade and
Parliamentary reform the instruments of its own liberation. Arnold's question —
"liberated for what?" — was one that the middle class's own intellectual
tradition was poorly equipped to answer.
