# Queen Elizabeth I

Queen Elizabeth I (1533–1603) was the Tudor monarch of England from 1558 to 1603. [[matthew-arnold|Matthew Arnold]] invokes her reign in the Preface to *Culture and Anarchy* as an example of the temper and largeness of spirit that characterised England's great epochs — a temper he contrasts with the narrowness of Victorian sectarian politics [P03-023].

## Identity

Elizabeth I presided over the Elizabethan Religious Settlement, which established the [[church-of-england|Church of England]] as a *via media* between Roman Catholicism and Protestant Nonconformity. Her reign coincided with the flourishing of English literature — Shakespeare, Spenser, Bacon — that Arnold regarded as the high-water mark of English cultural achievement and, implicitly, as the historical vindication of his argument that national greatness is measured by spiritual and intellectual achievement rather than material prosperity.

The Elizabethan Settlement was the historical origin of the [[establishment|Establishment]] that Arnold both critiques and defends throughout *Culture and Anarchy*. By establishing a national Church that was neither Roman Catholic nor Calvinist, Elizabeth created the institutional framework within which Arnold's argument about culture and the State operates.

## Role in Arnold's Argument

Arnold references the "temper observed in Queen Elizabeth's time" as a quality that "tended to diminish" the scope of what he calls [[machinery]] — the preoccupation with institutional and organisational questions at the expense of spiritual substance [P03-023]. The Elizabethan age, in Arnold's view, possessed a breadth of vision that Victorian England had lost.

This invocation of the Elizabethan temper is strategically placed in the Preface, where Arnold is defending himself against the charge of being insufficiently enthusiastic about [[liberalism]]. His point is that the Liberal obsession with dismantling the [[church-of-england|Church Establishment]] is precisely the sort of machinery-worship that the Elizabethans — with their larger view of things — would have recognised as petty:

> "In that time the temper which tended to diminish the scope of machinery, to make men look beyond it to the end for which it existed, was a temper observed in Queen Elizabeth's time" [P03-023]

The argument is audacious: Arnold is positioning himself as the heir of Elizabethan breadth, and his Liberal critics as the heirs of Puritan narrowness.

## The Economic Contrast

In Chapter VI, Arnold makes an incidental reference that places Elizabeth's England as a baseline against which modern [[industrialism]] is measured:

> "The increase of population since Queen Elizabeth's time, the production of silk..." [006-021]

The casualness of the reference is itself significant. Elizabeth's reign is invoked not as an object of nostalgia but as a fixed point — a known quantity against which the vast economic and demographic changes of the nineteenth century can be calibrated. Arnold is not arguing for a return to Elizabethan conditions (he has no illusions about the material poverty of the sixteenth century) but for a recovery of Elizabethan largeness of spirit.

## The Elizabethan Ideal

The Elizabethan age represented for Arnold a moment when English national life achieved a rare harmony of [[hebraism|Hebraism]] and [[hellenism|Hellenism]] — moral energy combined with intellectual freedom. The nineteenth century, by contrast, had abundant energy but lacked the [[sweetness-and-light]] that characterised the Renaissance.

This idealisation of the Elizabethan period was a commonplace of Victorian cultural criticism, shared by figures as different as [[john-ruskin|Ruskin]] and [[thomas-carlyle|Carlyle]]. What distinguishes Arnold's version is its specific connection to his argument about the State and the Establishment. For Arnold, Elizabeth's greatness was not merely personal but institutional: she created the conditions — the national Church, the connection between religion and the main current of national life — in which a great culture could flourish.

## The Elizabethan Legacy and Victorian Decline

Arnold's elegiac treatment of the Elizabethan legacy is inseparable from his critique of Victorian [[nonconformity]]. The Puritans whom Elizabeth contained — and whose spiritual descendants dominate Victorian religious life — represent, in Arnold's historical schema, the narrowing of the English spirit. The Elizabethan Settlement was a moment of balance that subsequent history has progressively undone.

This reading of English history — as a long declension from Elizabethan breadth to Victorian narrowness — provides the historical depth beneath Arnold's surface argument about culture and anarchy. The struggle he describes in the 1860s is, in his view, the latest episode in a drama that has been playing out since the Reformation.

## Relationship to Arnold's Framework

Elizabeth I serves Arnold as a historical benchmark. Her reign demonstrates that England has, at certain moments, achieved something close to the ideal of [[culture]] that Arnold advocates. The contrast between Elizabethan expansiveness and Victorian provincialism is implicit throughout his argument, surfacing explicitly in the Preface and functioning as a submerged criterion of judgment elsewhere.

The figure of Elizabeth also allows Arnold to make a political point without appearing to make one: by associating himself with the Elizabethan temper, he positions himself above the sectarian battles of his own day — the critic of both [[philistines|Philistine]] Liberalism and [[barbarians|Barbarian]] Conservatism.
