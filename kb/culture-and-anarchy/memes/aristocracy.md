# Aristocracy

In *Culture and Anarchy*, the aristocracy represents England's traditional ruling
order — a class Arnold treats with characteristic ambivalence: outwardly gifted
with the graces of "sweetness and light" yet inwardly hostile to the critical
spirit that genuine culture requires [002-004, 002-014].

## Arnold's Definition

Arnold defines the aristocracy (whom he also calls "Barbarians") not by wealth
or title alone but by a particular cast of mind. They possess, he grants, an
"innate faculty" for "sweetness and light" [002-015] — a natural ease and
polish that the middle class conspicuously lacks. Yet this very endowment proves
their limitation: the aristocracy deploys its graces defensively, to preserve
its position rather than to pursue perfection [002-014–015]. Arnold draws on the
example of Lord Elcho, who embodied for him the aristocratic resistance to ideas
that threaten established order [002-016].

## Usage Across the Text

### The Barbarian Temperament

Arnold adapts Heine's tripartite division of society, identifying the
aristocracy with "Barbarians" — a label that, for Arnold, carries less moral
censure than ironic precision. The aristocratic cultivation of the exterior
(self-possession, field sports, social charm) substitutes for the inward
discipline of reason. This is the "exterior culture" that Arnold contrasts with
the Hellenic ideal of the examined life [003-002].

### The Aristocratic Critique of Democracy

Writing in the shadow of the 1867 Reform Act (the "Reform Crisis"), Arnold
observes that the aristocracy's political argument — that it alone possesses the
"dignity" and "elevation" to govern [002-014] — collapses under the weight of
actual aristocratic performance. The aristocracy "likes the system which
flatters its self-importance" [002-013], a charge Arnold levels with equal force
at the middle class.

### Sweetness Without Light

Arnold's most penetrating critique of the aristocracy is that it achieves
"sweetness" (outward amenity) without "light" (inward intelligence). The
aristocrat may be agreeable, even admirable, in manner; but his resistance to
the free play of mind — to the "best that has been thought and said" — renders
him an obstacle to national perfection [002-015, 003-014].

## Historical Context

Arnold's treatment of the aristocracy reflects the post-1832 Reform Act
landscape, in which aristocratic political monopoly had been breached but not
broken. The 1867 Reform Act (the "leap in the dark," as Arnold's contemporaries
called it) extended the franchise to urban working-class men, sharpening the
question Arnold poses throughout the book: who shall govern, and on what
authority [002-004, 002-009]?
