# Doing as One Likes

Arnold's diagnosis of the central disorder of English life — the elevation of personal liberty into an absolute principle, detached from any conception of right reason or the general good (002-005).

## Arnold's Definition

The phrase "doing as one likes" appears throughout *Culture and Anarchy* as Arnold's shorthand for the peculiarly English understanding of freedom. It is not freedom in the classical or philosophical sense — the capacity to govern oneself according to reason — but rather the mere absence of external constraint:

> The Englishman's right to do what he likes is, in fact, his religion. (002-019)

Arnold uses the term with deliberate irony, echoing the language of the very people he criticizes. The point is that "doing as one likes" is not a principle at all — it is the absence of principle, the refusal to submit one's inclinations to the judgment of reason.

## The Hyde Park Riots

Arnold's most vivid illustration of "doing as one likes" is the Hyde Park Riots of 1866, when a crowd protesting the Reform Bill tore down the park railings:

> The Hyde Park demonstration affords a good example of our manner of dealing with the right of doing as one likes. (002-011)

What Arnold finds remarkable is not the violence itself but the official response: the government capitulated, effectively teaching the crowd that "doing as one likes" pays. Arnold is unimpressed by the argument that only a few railings were broken — for him the principle is everything.

## Doing as One Likes and the Middle Class

Arnold identifies the middle class as the primary carrier of the "doing as one likes" ethos. What the aristocracy does by inherited privilege, and what the working class does by force of numbers, the middle class does by conviction. The classic Victorian liberal — self-made, individualistic, suspicious of the state — makes "doing as one likes" into a philosophy:

> Our prevalent notion is that it is a most happy and important thing for a man merely to be able to do as he likes. (002-005)

## Anarchy as Consequence

The logical endpoint of "doing as one likes" is anarchy — not necessarily political anarchy in the revolutionary sense, but a general dissolution of order and authority. This is Arnold's argument for why culture is needed: culture provides the standard of reason and perfection against which mere personal preference can be judged and restrained.

> The difficulty for democracy is, how to find and keep high ideals. The individuals who compose it are, the bulk of them, persons who need to follow an ideal, not to set one. (002-019)

## Historical Context

Arnold's critique of "doing as one likes" engages with the central political debates of the 1860s: the agitation for the Second Reform Bill (passed in 1867), the Hyde Park disturbances, and the broader question of whether extending the franchise would lead to mob rule or responsible citizenship. Arnold's position cuts against both the reactionary defense of privilege and the liberal celebration of individual rights — culture, not class interest, must be the guiding principle.
