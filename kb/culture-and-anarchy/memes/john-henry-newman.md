# John Henry Newman

John Henry Newman (1801–1890) was an English theologian, poet, and
cardinal who was first an Anglican priest and later converted to Roman
Catholicism. He was a central figure in the Oxford Movement, a mid-19th-
century effort to reconnect the Church of England with its Catholic
patrimony. Arnold invokes Newman twice in the Introduction to *Culture
and Anarchy* as a figure synonymous with the Oxford Movement's collision
with mid-Victorian liberalism.

## Role in *Culture and Anarchy*

Arnold references Newman as both a diagnostician and a casualty of the
liberalism that the Oxford Movement struggled against. He writes:

> …as any one who reads Dr. Newman's *Apology* may see (PN 001-017)

and:

> what was it, this liberalism, as Dr. Newman saw it, and as it really
> broke the Oxford movement? It was the great middle-class liberalism,
> which had for the cardinal points of its belief the Reform Bill of
> 1832, and local self-government, in politics; in the social sphere,
> free-trade, unrestricted competition, and the making of large
> industrial fortunes; in the religious sphere, the Dissidence of
> Dissent and the Protestantism of the Protestant religion.
> (PN 001-019)

For Arnold, Newman's experience crystallises the central conflict of
Victorian culture: religious seriousness arrayed against the irresistible
force of middle-class economic and political liberalism. Newman "felt
himself fighting with" this liberalism, which "till only the other day
seemed to be the paramount force in this country" (PN 001-019).

## The Oxford Movement Context

The Oxford Movement (1833–1845), also known as Tractarianism, sought to
reassert the catholic heritage of the Church of England through a series
of *Tracts for the Times*, many authored by Newman. The movement
emphasised apostolic succession, the authority of the Church Fathers, and
the sacramental life — all positions that brought its adherents into
conflict with the Erastian, latitudinarian temper of the age.

Newman's 1845 conversion to Rome effectively ended the movement's
Anglican phase. His *Apologia Pro Vita Sua* (1864), published only five
years before *Culture and Anarchy*, was a spiritual autobiography
occasioned by a public dispute with Charles Kingsley and had recently
made Newman a figure of broad public interest.

## Connection to Arnold's Argument

Arnold's use of Newman is strategic. By invoking a figure of
unquestionable intellectual and spiritual weight, Arnold demonstrates
that his own critique of middle-class liberalism is not merely an
"Oxford" sentiment or a reactionary aestheticism — it partakes of a
deeper religious tradition. Newman and Arnold share an Oxford pedigree,
a distrust of the "machinery" of political economy, and a conviction
that the perfection of the human spirit cannot be reduced to material
prosperity.

However, Arnold's position differs from Newman's in a crucial respect:
where Newman sought refuge in Rome, Arnold seeks to reconstruct culture
as a "study of perfection" that encompasses both Hebraism (moral
earnestness) and Hellenism (the free play of consciousness). Arnold
invokes Newman not to endorse Tractarian theology but to illustrate that
liberalism, however triumphant as a political force, leaves unanswered
the deepest questions about human flourishing.
