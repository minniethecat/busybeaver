# Henry Thomas Buckle

Henry Thomas Buckle (1821–1862) was an English historian, best known for his unfinished *History of Civilisation in England*, which attempted to apply scientific methods to the study of history. [[matthew-arnold]] references him twice in *Culture and Anarchy* as an example of rationalist and scientific dogmatism — the intellectual vice of those who reduce human life to measurable laws [001-021, 005-003].

## Identity

Buckle was a self-taught scholar who sought to establish history as a science governed by discoverable laws of climate, soil, food supply, and social statistics. His work was enormously influential in mid-Victorian intellectual circles, representing the high tide of positivism in English historical writing. Arnold groups Buckle with Auguste Comte and [[frederic-harrison|Mr. Frederic Harrison]] as thinkers who claimed for science an authority it did not possess over the full range of human experience.

## Role in Arnold's Argument

In Chapter I, Arnold lists Buckle among those who represent the dogmatic spirit in its modern rationalist form:

> "Men like Comte, or the late Mr. Buckle, or Mr. Mill. However much it..." [001-021]

The fragmentary nature of the surviving corpus reference is itself suggestive: Arnold's engagement with Buckle is brief but pointed. Buckle stands for the claim that everything important about human life can be reduced to scientific law — a claim Arnold regards as a form of intellectual [[philistines|philistinism]].

In Chapter V, Arnold returns to Buckle with greater sharpness:

> "So remarkable, of the late Mr. Buckle. Such a fanaticism carries it..." [005-003]

The word "fanaticism" is Arnold's definitive judgement. Buckle's rationalism, far from being the opposite of religious fanaticism, is in Arnold's view simply another form of it — the [[hebraism|Hebraic]] zeal for righteousness transferred from the religious to the scientific sphere.

## Relationship to Arnold's Framework

Buckle represents for Arnold the danger of intellectual specialisation without the balancing influence of [[culture]]. His attempt to make history a science exemplifies what Arnold calls "machinery" — the substitution of method for wisdom. Buckle was in earnest, but his earnestness was of the kind that Arnold diagnoses as characteristically English: energetic, systematic, and spiritually incomplete.
