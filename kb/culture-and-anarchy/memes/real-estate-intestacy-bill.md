The Real Estate Intestacy Bill was a legislative proposal in the mid-1860s aimed at abolishing primogeniture by providing for the equal division of a deceased person's real property among their children when no will existed. Matthew Arnold uses the Bill extensively in Chapter VI of *Culture and Anarchy* as a case study in how "stock notions" and "mechanical maxims" drive English reform without the guidance of right reason.

## Overview

The Bill sought to change the law of intestate succession for real estate, replacing the traditional rule of primogeniture (inheritance by the eldest son) with equal division among all children. It was debated in the House of Commons during the reform-intensive period of the mid-1860s. The measure reflected the liberal and Dissenting impulse toward equality and the dismantling of aristocratic legal privileges.

## Arnold's Treatment

Arnold devotes a substantial portion of Chapter VI, "Our Liberal Practitioners," to analyzing the Real Estate Intestacy Bill. He treats it not primarily as a legal question but as an illustration of how English reform operates through unexamined assumptions rather than through the Hellenic process of seeing things as they truly are. The "stock notion" underlying the Bill — that all children have a natural right to an equal share of their parent's property — is, in Arnold's view, a mechanical maxim that has never been genuinely examined by reason (PN 006-010).

Arnold contrasts the Bill's approach with what he calls "Hellenising" the question: examining the actual effects of the proposed change, considering whether it would truly produce better social outcomes, and testing the maxim itself against experience and right reason (PN 006-013). He presents the Bill as a perfect specimen of the Philistine reform impulse: well-intentioned, equality-driven, but intellectually shallow.

## Key Passages

Arnold's most concentrated discussion of the Bill appears in Chapter VI. He introduces it as a parallel case to the disestablishment of the Irish Church — both are operations that excite popular feeling but which, when examined closely, reveal the limitations of the English reform mentality (PN 006-009).

> "The stock notion on which the Real Estate Intestacy Bill was based — the notion that in the nature and fitness of things all a man's children have a right to an equal share in the enjoyment of what he leaves — really has" no rational foundation when tested by the "way of right reason" (PN 006-010)

He explicitly frames his analysis as an exercise in "Hellenising" the question:

> "We Hellenised with the Real Estate Intestacy Bill, and with the disestablishment of the Irish Church by the power of the Nonconformists' antipathy to religious establishments" (PN 006-018)

## Historical Significance

The Real Estate Intestacy Bill was part of the broader Victorian movement to reform inheritance law, which had long been dominated by aristocratic principles of primogeniture and entail. The 1833 Inheritance Act had already made some changes, and the Intestacy Bill represented the continuing push toward egalitarian legal reform. The Bill did not become law in the 1860s, but the principle of equal division among children was eventually established in English intestacy law by the Administration of Estates Act 1925.

In Arnold's framework, the Bill is significant not for its legislative outcome but for what it reveals about the character of English liberalism: a tendency to proceed from abstract equality-maxims without the "sweetness and light" of genuine intellectual cultivation.
