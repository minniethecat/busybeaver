# Robert Buchanan

Robert Williams Buchanan (1841–1901) was a Scottish poet, novelist, and
critic who achieved notoriety in the 1870s for his attack on the
Pre-Raphaelite poets in "The Fleshly School of Poetry." In *Culture and
Anarchy* (1869), Arnold quotes Buchanan's poetry at length in the
Conclusion, treating it as symptomatic of the Victorian taste for
"the bathos" — the sentimental preference for emotional effect over
intellectual clarity.

## Role in *Culture and Anarchy*

Buchanan appears exclusively in the book's Conclusion, where Arnold
quotes him six times across nine pages. This concentrated attention
is unusual, and it signals that Arnold regards Buchanan's verse as
exemplary of a broader cultural failing.

### Poetry of the "Bathos"

Arnold introduces Buchanan as:

> a young Scotch writer, Mr. Robert Buchanan, because he invests with
> so much tenderness the spectacle of the poor children
> (PN 006-024)

The word "invests" is carefully chosen — it suggests that Buchanan
clothes a thin observation in rich sentiment. Arnold quotes Buchanan's
description of "white-faced" children who are "sent into the streets
to play" — a passage Arnold treats not as bad poetry but as poetry
that mistakes pathos for truth:

> the poetry of Mr. Robert Buchanan may say to persuade us
> (PN 006-030)

### The Critique of Sentimentalism

Arnold's objection is not that Buchanan feels sympathy for poor
children — Arnold would share that sympathy — but that Buchanan's
verse presents emotional response as a substitute for the disciplined
thought that would actually improve children's lives. The poetry
indulges feeling; it does not seek "to see the object as in itself
it really is."

This is Arnold's definition of "the bathos": the preference for
emotional satisfaction over intellectual rigour. Buchanan's verse
makes readers feel virtuous without asking them to think.

### "Never The Times and Mr. Robert Buchanan"

Arnold's climactic use of Buchanan pairs him with *The Times*
newspaper:

> whatever The Times and Mr. Robert Buchanan may say
> (PN 006-032)

The coupling is strategic: *The Times* represents middle-class
opinion at its most self-confident; Buchanan represents middle-brow
culture at its most self-congratulatory. Both, Arnold argues,
reinforce the "ordinary self" rather than challenging it.

## Connection to Arnold's Argument

Buchanan functions as the aesthetic counterpart to [[thomas-bazley|Bazley]]
in politics and [[william-murphy|Murphy]] in religion. Each figure
represents a class or domain operating in the absence of culture:
Bazley gives us Philistine economics, Murphy gives us Hebraism without
Hellenism, and Buchanan gives us poetry that touches the heart without
engaging the mind. Arnold's argument is that all three are symptoms of
the same disease — the failure to cultivate "our best self."
