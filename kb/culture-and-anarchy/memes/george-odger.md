# George Odger

George Odger (1813–1877) was a prominent English trade unionist and radical politician, a shoemaker by trade who became one of the most visible working-class leaders of the 1860s. Matthew Arnold selects him in *Culture and Anarchy* as representative of the best qualities — and characteristic limitations — of the emerging working class.

## Identity

Odger was a founder of the London Trades Council and a leading figure in the Reform League alongside Edmond Beales. He stood unsuccessfully for Parliament several times as a working-class candidate. His speeches and organising work made him one of the most recognisable faces of labour politics in Victorian England.

## Role in Arnold's Argument

Arnold's treatment of Odger is notably measured. In his taxonomy of English classes, Arnold seeks a "virtuous mean" for each — a figure who represents the class's best qualities without its excesses. For the working class, he selects Odger:

> Mr. Odger, whose speeches we have all read, and of whom his friends relate, besides, much that is favourable, may very well stand for the beautiful and virtuous mean of our present working-class; and I think everybody will admit that in Mr. Odger, as in Lord Elcho, there is manifestly, with all his good points, some insufficiency of light. (003-086)

This judgment — praise for Odger's character, regret for his want of "light" — encapsulates Arnold's view of the working class as an "embryo" possessing "honesty in an inchoate and untrained state" (003-085). The deficiency is not moral but cultural: Odger has "bright powers of sympathy and ready powers of action" but lacks the intellectual equipment culture provides.

## Key Passages

Arnold contrasts Odger with Charles Bradlaugh, who represents the "excess" of working-class politics — the iconoclast "almost for baptizing us all in blood and fire" (003-087). Between them stands Odger as the balanced figure, yet even he lacks sufficiency of light.

Later, Arnold groups Odger with Bradlaugh as figures who will eventually claim the political stage for the Populace: "Presently, perhaps, Mr. Odger and Mr. Bradlaugh will be there with their mission to oust both Barbarians and Philistines, and to get the heritage for the Populace" (006-269). Arnold's response is not to oppose this but to insist that "the transformation of each and all of these according to the law of perfection" matters more than which class holds power.

In a lighter vein, Arnold notes that for one variety of Philistine, "the relaxed self" enjoys "deputations, or hearing Mr. Odger speak" (004-109), suggesting Odger had become a familiar figure on the political lecture circuit.

## Relationship to Arnold's Framework

Odger is the most sympathetically drawn member of the Populace in *Culture and Anarchy*. He represents what Arnold sees as the genuine moral sincerity of the working class — its "honesty" and "sympathy" — while also illustrating the limits of that sincerity without culture. Odger is not lampooned like Bradlaugh or dismissed like Beales; he is held up as exactly the kind of person whom culture could elevate from honest action to enlightened perfection.
