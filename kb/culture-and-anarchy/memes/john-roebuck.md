# John Arthur Roebuck

John Arthur Roebuck (1802–1879) was a Radical Liberal MP for Bath and later Sheffield. Matthew Arnold repeatedly invokes him in *Culture and Anarchy* as the exemplar of the English tendency to mistake external freedoms for inward perfection — the quintessential "faith in machinery."

## Identity

Roebuck was born in Madras, India, and educated in Canada and England. He was called to the bar in 1832 and entered Parliament in 1832 as MP for Bath. Known as "Tear 'em" for his aggressive debating style, he was a radical reformer who later became more conservative. He chaired the Sebastopol Committee during the Crimean War.

## Role in Arnold's Argument

Arnold uses Roebuck as his central illustration of how Englishmen worship "machinery" — external institutions and freedoms — as if they had value in themselves. Roebuck's "stock argument for proving the greatness and happiness of England" (002-017) is that every Englishman may say what he likes. Arnold counters that the aspirations of culture are not satisfied "unless what men say, when they may say what they like, is worth saying."

Roebuck appears as the spokesman for a mechanical conception of freedom: the fact of liberty matters more than its content. The "famous definition of happiness" (004-130) — "I look around me and ask what is the state of England? Is not every man able to say what he likes?" — represents for Arnold the intellectual poverty of treating means as ends.

## Key Passages

Arnold introduces Roebuck at the heart of his argument about machinery in Chapter I:

> "May not every man in England say what he likes?" — Mr. Roebuck perpetually asks; and that, he thinks, is quite sufficient, and when every man may say what he likes, our aspirations ought to be satisfied. But the aspirations of culture, which is the study of perfection, are not satisfied, unless what men say, when they may say what they like, is worth saying. (002-017)

In Chapter III, Arnold returns to Roebuck's definition of happiness, connecting it to the problem of anarchy:

> This is the old story of our system of checks and every Englishman doing as he likes, which we have already seen to have been convenient enough so long as there were only the Barbarians and the Philistines to do what they liked, but to be getting inconvenient, and productive of anarchy, now that the Populace wants to do what it likes too. (004-131)

In Chapter II, Arnold groups Roebuck with Robert Lowe as those who "debauch the minds of the middle classes, and make such Philistines of them" (003-040), teaching men to value themselves on what they have achieved materially rather than on what they have become spiritually.

## Relationship to Arnold's Framework

Roebuck embodies the Philistine faith in machinery — the belief that external freedoms (speech, trade, industry) constitute human perfection in themselves. Arnold uses him to demonstrate why culture is necessary: it provides the critical standard that distinguishes the *fact* of liberty from the *quality* of what is done with it. Roebuck's argument that England is great because Englishmen may say what they like is, for Arnold, the perfect specimen of Hebraism without Hellenism: strenuousness without light.
