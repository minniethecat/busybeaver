# Hellenism

One pole of Arnold's dialectic of civilisation — the force that drives humanity toward clear seeing, intellectual spontaneity, and the harmonious perfection of all human powers. Together with [[hebraism]], it anchors the argument of *Culture and Anarchy*. Arnold traces its lineage from ancient Greek philosophy through the Renaissance to the modern ideal of "culture" itself, finding its classical voice in [[plato|Plato]] and its modern echo in the intellectual traditions of [[germany|Germany]] (004-004).

## Arnold's Definition

Arnold defines Hellenism and Hebraism as the two great spiritual disciplines that have shaped Western civilisation. The governing idea of Hellenism, he writes, is "spontaneity of consciousness" — the impulse to see things as they really are, without prejudice or preconception:

> The uppermost idea with Hellenism is to see things as they really are; the uppermost idea with Hebraism is conduct and obedience. (004-004)

Hellenism prizes knowing above doing. Its characteristic excellence is what the Greeks called *theoria* — the disinterested contemplation of things for the sake of understanding them. This is not intellectual laziness, Arnold insists, but a distinct and necessary form of human effort. "The great concern of Hellenism is the free play of the mind upon all subjects" (004-007).

## The Hellenic Inheritance

Hellenism draws its name and character from the ancient Greek spirit. Arnold associates its revival with the Renaissance — that "return to Greek art and literature" which reawakened Europe from the spiritual domination of the Middle Ages (004-006). The Renaissance demonstrated that Hellenism is "profound and admirable," no less serious than Hebraism, despite Puritan suspicions of its worldliness.

The key Greek concept Arnold invokes is *euphyia* — "the man who tends towards sweetness and light" — a natural well-grownness that makes the Hellenic ideal fundamentally aesthetic and holistic (001-010). This ideal finds expression in the Greek instinct "for what Plato calls the true, firm, intelligible law of things" (005-002).

## Hellenism vs. Hebraism

Arnold's great insight is that Hellenism and Hebraism are not enemies but complementary forces with a shared end:

> The final aim of both Hellenism and Hebraism, as of all great spiritual disciplines, is no doubt the same: man's perfection or salvation. (004-003)

Yet their methods diverge radically:

> Both Hellenism and Hebraism arise out of the wants of human nature, and both address themselves to satisfying those wants. But they differ in the direction they take. Hebraism addresses itself to the obvious, palpable wants of our nature; Hellenism, to its higher, more spiritual wants. (004-007)

The tragedy of Western history, Arnold argues, is that these two forces have rarely been in balance. One or the other has dominated each era. Arnold traces this rhythm: primitive Christianity saw the "defeat inflicted on Hellenism by Christianity" (004-015); the Renaissance brought Hellenism's "re-entry into the world" in the sixteenth century (004-013); the Reformation then reasserted Hebraic dominance; and the modern age, particularly in England, remains Hebraic in temper.

## Hellenism and the Idea of Culture

Arnold's concept of "culture" is essentially Hellenism in modern dress. When he defines culture as "the pursuit of our total perfection by means of getting to know, on all the matters which most concern us, the best which has been thought and said in the world," he is restating the Hellenic ideal of comprehensive, disinterested understanding. [[sweetness-and-light]] is the fruit of this Hellenic disposition — the union of beauty and intelligence that Arnold sets against English narrowness.

> The great men of culture are those who have had a passion for diffusing, for making prevail, for carrying from one end of society to the other, the best knowledge, the best ideas of their time. (P03-032)

## The English Problem

England, Arnold contends, suffers from an excess of Hebraism and a deficiency of Hellenism. The Puritan inheritance has made the English zealous for conduct but indifferent to beauty, suspicious of free thought, and hostile to "sweetness and light":

> Hebraism rampant in us and Hellenism stamped out. (P03-017)

This one-sided development produces "incomplete and mutilated men" who cannot achieve true perfection because they have developed only part of their humanity (P03-007). The Englishman's characteristic suspicion of "the free play of the mind" means that in England, "Hellenism is always treated as an enemy" (005-005).

The solution Arnold proposes is not to abandon Hebraism — "the care and discipline received from Hebraism remain for our race an eternal possession" (P03-032) — but to supplement it with the Hellenic virtues of flexibility, intellectual curiosity, and love of beauty. "What we want is Hellenism, the letting our consciousness play freely on the objects that come before it" (006-032).

## The German Connection

Arnold notably associates the modern revival of Hellenism with [[germany|Germany]]. He repeatedly invokes Goethe as a Hellenic figure — one who pursued the free development of all human powers — and contrasts German intellectual seriousness favourably with English Philistinism. "The great works by which, of modern times, the human spirit has been formed," Arnold writes, pointing to Germany's philosophical and literary achievement (P03-009). Germany represents, for Arnold, the possibility that a modern, Protestant nation might temper its Hebraism with Hellenic breadth.

## Historical Context

Arnold's Hellenism-Hebraism distinction draws on a long tradition of contrasting Athens with Jerusalem, going back at least to Heinrich Heine and ultimately to early Christian thought. Arnold was also influenced by his father Thomas Arnold's educational reforms at Rugby, which sought to infuse English public schooling with classical ideals. The immediate polemical context was the Victorian debate about national education, religious dissent, and the cultural credentials of the middle class — Arnold's Hellenism was an argument for intellectual breadth against the narrowness of sectarian education.
