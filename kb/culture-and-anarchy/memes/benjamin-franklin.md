# Benjamin Franklin

Benjamin Franklin (1706–1790) was an American statesman, philosopher, inventor, and one of the Founding Fathers of the United States. In *Culture and Anarchy*, [[matthew-arnold|Matthew Arnold]] invokes Franklin as a representative of the utilitarian spirit — the "[[machinery]]" mindset that privileges practical outcomes over the perfection of human nature itself [001-010].

## Identity

Franklin was the prototypical self-made man of the Enlightenment: printer, scientist, diplomat, and moralist. His *Autobiography* established a model of American self-improvement that was still influential in Arnold's day, and his maxims — "early to bed, early to rise," "a penny saved is a penny earned" — had passed into the common stock of Anglo-American wisdom.

Arnold would have encountered Franklin through a double lens: as the most famous American of the eighteenth century, and as the embodiment of a certain kind of practical philosophy that Arnold associated with [[philistines|Philistinism]]. Franklin's famous project of moral perfection — his chart of thirteen virtues with daily marks for each transgression — was precisely the sort of mechanical approach to human improvement that Arnold found both admirable and inadequate.

## In Culture and Anarchy

Arnold quotes Franklin in Chapter I ("[[sweetness-and-light|Sweetness and Light]]") to illustrate the utilitarian tendency to reduce all human goods to material and practical measures:

> "the utilitarian Franklin says just as explicitly:—'Eat and drink such an exact quantity as suits the constitution of thy body, in reference to the services of thy mind.'" [001-010]

The word "utilitarian" is the key. Arnold is not dismissing Franklin's practical wisdom — the advice is sensible enough — but placing it in a hierarchy. Franklin's counsel is about the efficient management of the self as an instrument; what it lacks is any sense that the self might have purposes beyond efficiency.

## Franklin and Machinery

Franklin appears as part of Arnold's critique of what he calls "machinery" — the English tendency to worship external goods (wealth, industry, population, political freedom) as ends in themselves rather than as means to human perfection. Franklin's advice about eating and drinking "in reference to the services of thy mind" treats the mind as a tool to be maintained rather than as a faculty to be cultivated.

The contrast Arnold draws is between Franklin and the Greek ideal of [[hellenism]] — the pursuit of perfection for its own sake. Franklin wants to make the mind serviceable; Arnold wants to make it excellent. The difference is not trivial: it is the difference between a culture that asks "what is this good for?" and one that asks "what is good?"

## Arnold's Ambivalence

Arnold's treatment of Franklin is more nuanced than it might appear. He places Franklin alongside voices he respects — the passage follows a discussion of the [[bishop-wilson|Bishop Wilson]], whom Arnold admires — and the critique is embedded in a larger argument about the coexistence of [[hebraism|Hebraism]] and [[hellenism|Hellenism]].

Franklin's moral seriousness — his genuine commitment to self-improvement — is the Hebraic element that Arnold acknowledges even as he insists on its insufficiency. The problem with Franklin is not that he is wrong about conduct but that he has no conception of anything beyond conduct: no sense of beauty, of intellectual joy, of the free play of mind that Arnold calls [[spontaneity|spontaneity of consciousness]].

## Franklin and America

Franklin's presence in *Culture and Anarchy* also carries a political charge. By the 1860s, Franklin had become an icon of [[america|American]] identity — the self-made man who embodied democratic virtues. Arnold's willingness to treat this icon critically is part of his larger argument about the inadequacy of American culture.

In Chapter III, Arnold discusses America's cultural dependence on England and the limited significance of American thought in the world of ideas [003-013]. Franklin, for all his achievements, belongs to this pattern: he is an important figure in the history of practical philosophy, but he does not represent the kind of intellectual achievement that Arnold associates with the great tradition of European culture.

## Significance

Franklin serves an important function in Arnold's argument: he stands for a certain strand of modern thought that Arnold both respects (for its discipline and clarity) and critiques (for its inability to rise above utility). By placing Franklin alongside other voices in the tradition of machinery thinking, Arnold builds his case for culture as the harmonious expansion of all human powers.

The Franklin passage also illustrates Arnold's characteristic method of argument by juxtaposition. Rather than directly refuting utilitarianism, he places a utilitarian maxim alongside the Greek ideal and lets the contrast do the work. The reader is invited to see that Franklin's wisdom, however sensible, belongs to a lower order of achievement than the wisdom of Sophocles or Plato.
