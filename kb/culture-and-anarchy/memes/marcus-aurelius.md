# Marcus Aurelius

Marcus Aurelius (121–180 CE) was a Roman emperor and Stoic philosopher
whose *Meditations* — a series of private moral reflections written in
Greek during his military campaigns — has remained for nearly two
millennia one of the most widely read works of ethical self-examination
in the Western tradition. [[matthew-arnold]] invokes Marcus Aurelius in
the Preface to *Culture and Anarchy* as one of a select group of
pre-Christian or non-Christian writers — alongside Epictetus, Plato,
and the author of the Book of Job — in whom he finds a "peculiarly
sincere" inwardness that throws the merely formal Christianity of his
own age into unflattering relief [P03-002].

## Identity

Marcus Aurelius was born into a senatorial family and adopted by the
Emperor Antoninus Pius, whom he succeeded in 161 CE. He spent most of
his reign on the Danube frontier, defending the empire against Germanic
tribes, and it was in the camp, amid the hardships of military
life, that he composed the twelve books of the *Meditations* (the
Greek title, *Ta Eis Heauton*, means simply "To Himself"). The work
was never intended for publication; it is a notebook of spiritual
exercises, written in a terse, unadorned Greek, in which the emperor
exhorts himself to live according to reason, to accept the transience
of all things, and to meet every event — whether pleasant or painful —
with the equanimity of a mind aligned with the Stoic conception of a
rational universe. He died, probably of the plague, at Vindobona
(Vienna) or Sirmium in 180 CE.

## Role in Arnold's Argument

Arnold's reference to Marcus Aurelius occurs in the Preface, in one
of the most carefully constructed passages in *Culture and Anarchy*.
Arnold is defending himself against the charge that he is insufficiently
respectful toward the "great texts" of Puritanism — that by commending
the Hellenic ideal of "spontaneity of consciousness" he is slighting
the Hebraic virtues of obedience and righteousness. His reply is that
the real test of a religion or philosophy is not what it professes but
the inward quality of life it produces:

> And although an Epictetus or a Marcus Aurelius, a Plato or a
> Socrates, may have lived and worked in a way that puts to shame
> the ordinary Christian of our own day, the Christian whom one
> meets in the streets, still the genuine Christian consciousness,
> as it is to be found in the best of the saints and martyrs, is
> something higher than the consciousness of the philosopher.
> [P03-002]

The movement of the passage is dialectical. Arnold first acknowledges
that a pagan like Marcus Aurelius can put the "ordinary Christian" to
shame — an acknowledgement that gives the Nonconformist critic
everything he fears. But he immediately shifts the ground: the highest
reach of Christian inwardness, found in "the best of the saints and
martyrs," surpasses anything the pagan philosophers achieved. The
argument is a characteristic Arnoldian triangulation: he uses the
pagan to rebuke the Philistine Christian, then uses the saint to
check the complacency of the merely rational pagan, and the whole
operation serves to elevate "the genuine Christian consciousness"
above all its rivals.

## Key Passages

Arnold's direct reference appears in a single concentrated sentence in
the Preface:

> I was surprised to see the *Saturday Review* recently speak of the
> *Meditations* of Marcus Aurelius as utterly worthless, and to find
> Mr. Hepworth Dixon joining in the cry. The *Meditations* of Marcus
> Aurelius seem to me to have, for their main scope, the excitation
> of our faculties of seriousness and self-recollection; and in this
> respect they are, for many of us, one of the most precious books
> in the world.
> [P03-002]

The phrase "seriousness and self-recollection" captures what Arnold
values in the Stoic tradition. These are not doctrinal virtues — Marcus
Aurelius has no revelation, no church, no promise of personal
immortality — but they are *cultural* virtues: the habits of mind
that make it possible for a person to stand apart from "the rush and
glare of the modern" and to judge things by a measure deeper than
fashion or utility.
