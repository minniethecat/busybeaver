# The Reform Crisis (1866–1867)

The political upheaval surrounding the extension of the parliamentary franchise in 1866–1867 provides Arnold with his most vivid contemporary illustration of the conflict between authority and "doing as one likes." The crisis — which included mass demonstrations, the Hyde Park Riots, Reform League agitation, and the eventual passage of Disraeli's Reform Act — becomes, in *Culture and Anarchy*, a parable about the dangers of anarchy and the need for a strong [[authority|principle of authority]] (002-028).

## The Political Context

The Reform Crisis unfolded in two phases:

**Phase 1: The Liberal Bill Fails (1866).** Lord Russell's Liberal government introduced a moderate Reform Bill to extend the franchise. It was defeated by a coalition of Conservative opponents and anti-Reform Liberals ("Adullamites"), bringing down the government and installing a Conservative ministry under Lord Derby, with Benjamin Disraeli as Chancellor of the Exchequer and leader in the [[house-of-commons|House of Commons]].

**Phase 2: The Hyde Park Riots and the Conservative Bill (1866–1867).** The defeat of the Liberal bill triggered widespread popular agitation. The Reform League, led by Edmond Beales, organised mass meetings. When the authorities banned a planned meeting in [[hyde-park-riots-1866|Hyde Park]] in July 1866, protesters tore down the railings and occupied the park for three days. The following year, Disraeli — in a remarkable political manoeuvre — introduced a Conservative Reform Bill that was even more radical than the one the Liberals had proposed, enfranchising a substantial portion of the urban working class.

## Arnold's Account

Arnold witnessed the Reform agitation first-hand. His description of listening to the Reform debates in Parliament and observing the Hyde Park Riots is central to his argument:

> I had the advantage of listening to the Reform debates in the House of Commons, having heard a number of intelligent speakers, and having read the leading articles of the newspapers about them. (002-016)

His verdict is withering. Both the parliamentary debates and the street agitation displayed, in Arnold's view, the same fundamental defect: the absence of any genuine appeal to reason or authority. The Reform League orators and the parliamentary debaters alike were simply asserting their will — "doing as one likes" — without reference to any higher standard.

### The Reform League

Arnold treats the Reform League as a case study in the dangers of unchecked popular politics:

> For the Reform League, it is the working-class, the basis of our national greatness, which claims recognition. (002-013)

While Arnold does not oppose the working class's claim to political representation, he objects to the *manner* in which the claim is advanced — not through reasoned argument but through force and threat:

> The Reform League orators inveigh against the upper classes as the disturbers of our national harmony. (003-014)

### The Hyde Park Riots

The Hyde Park Riots of July 1866 crystallised Arnold's concerns:

| Arnold's Observation | His Interpretation |
|---------------------|-------------------|
| "London's Hyde Park riots occurred in 1866" (002-028) | Evidence that English liberty has degenerated into mere absence of restraint |
| "Reform Leaguers bent on assembling there" (002-028) | The assertion of will against lawful authority |
| "Mr. Bradlaugh in his Hyde Park anarchy-mongering" (003-018) | The descent of political agitation into "anarchy" |
| "The right of meeting in Hyde Park" (005-010) | A genuine right, but one that must be exercised within the framework of authority |

Arnold's point is not that the working class should be excluded from political life. It is that the Reform agitation, like so much else in English life, exemplified the absence of "a high best self, a centre of light and authority" that could guide public life beyond the mere collision of interests (003-014).

## The Parliamentary Dimension

Arnold was equally critical of the parliamentary handling of the crisis. The debates in the House of Commons struck him as displays of party manoeuvring rather than genuine deliberation about the public good:

> The Liberal majority in the House of Commons was broken up; and, therefore, the leaders of the Liberal party could not carry their measure. (006-002)

Disraeli's "leap in the dark" — pushing through a more radical Reform Bill than the Liberals had proposed — was, for Arnold, not statesmanship but a cynical political calculation. The entire crisis revealed the poverty of a political culture that had no language for discussing the common good beyond the competition of interests.

## The Reform Crisis and Arnold's Theory of the State

The Reform Crisis is the concrete occasion for Arnold's most important political argument: that English life lacks an adequate idea of the State as a centre of authority and right reason. The crisis demonstrates, in Arnold's view, that when political conflict is reduced to the clash of wills — "doing as one likes" on a national scale — the result is not liberty but anarchy:

> We want an authority, and we find nothing but jealous classes, checks, and a dead-lock. (003-014)

The Reform Crisis thus becomes the negative image of Arnold's positive ideal: a State that represents the "best self" of the nation, embodying reason rather than mere will, and capable of guiding public life toward the common good rather than merely registering the outcome of sectarian struggles.

## Key Figures

| Figure | Role | Arnold's View |
|--------|------|---------------|
| **Benjamin Disraeli** | Conservative leader, architect of the 1867 Reform Act | Cynical pragmatist; the "leap in the dark" |
| **Edmond Beales** | President of the Reform League | Representative of working-class assertion without intellectual culture |
| **Charles Bradlaugh** | Radical orator, secularist | "Hyde Park anarchy-mongering" — the dangerous extreme |
