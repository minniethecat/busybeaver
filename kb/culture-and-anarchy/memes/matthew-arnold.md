# Matthew Arnold

Matthew Arnold (1822–1888) was the son of the great Rugby headmaster Thomas Arnold and became one of the most influential Victorian critics of culture and society. *Culture and Anarchy* (1869) is his most sustained work of social criticism, developing the ideas about Hellenism and Hebraism and the social function of culture that he had first explored in his earlier essays.

## Identity

Arnold was simultaneously a poet, a literary critic, a social critic, and — for thirty-five years — a government school inspector. This combination of roles is crucial to understanding *Culture and Anarchy*: Arnold writes with the sensibility of a poet, the analytical habits of a critic, and the practical experience of a civil servant who had spent decades observing English society at close range.

He held the Professorship of Poetry at Oxford (1857–1867), the first person to deliver his lectures in English rather than Latin. His major poems include "Dover Beach," "The Scholar-Gipsy," and "Thyrsis."

## Role in Arnold's Argument

As the author, Arnold's presence permeates every page. He speaks in the first person, addresses the reader directly, and frequently cites his own earlier work. The essay's characteristic tone — urbane, ironic, self-deprecating but also deeply serious — is Arnold's own creation and an essential part of its persuasive strategy.

Arnold presents himself not as a system-builder but as a man of culture trying to bring "a stream of fresh and free thought" to bear on the problems of his time (P03-003).

## Key Passages

Arnold's preface is his most sustained autobiographical statement within the book, defending the essay against its critics and restating his central arguments. He answers the charge that culture is impractical by pointing to his own career as a school inspector — a man who has spent his life in practical work but who insists that practical work is worthless without a guiding ideal (P03-011).

## Relationship to Arnold's Framework

Arnold is the exemplar of the cultured critic — the person who combines Hebraic moral seriousness with Hellenic intelligence, who seeks to "make reason and the will of God prevail" not through partisan advocacy but through the disinterested pursuit of perfection.
