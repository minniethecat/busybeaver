The *British Banner* was a Nonconformist weekly newspaper published in London from 1848 to 1858, edited by the Congregationalist minister John Campbell. Arnold mentions it in Chapter III of *Culture and Anarchy* as one of the organs of religious opinion that took hostile notice of his lectures on culture — an example, in Arnold's view, of how the Nonconformist press substitutes sectarian vigilance for genuine intellectual engagement.

## Nature

The *British Banner* was a religious newspaper with a strong Nonconformist editorial line. It was particularly associated with Congregationalism and with the campaign against state interference in religion. More combative than the *Daily News* and more explicitly denominational, the *British Banner* represented the assertive, politically engaged Nonconformity that Arnold saw as a powerful and problematic force in Victorian public life.

## Role in Arnold's Argument

Arnold's reference to the *British Banner* is brief but pointed. He places it in a list of the "personages" who have taken against him: "Not only does the *Daily News* abuse the lecture, but the *British Banner*" (PN 003-012). The *British Banner*'s hostility, Arnold implies, is perfectly predictable: any cultural criticism that does not affirm Protestant orthodoxy will be treated by the Nonconformist press as an enemy of religion. This is the "provincialism" Arnold attributes to Nonconformity — the inability to see beyond its own sectarian horizon to the larger question of human perfection.

Arnold does not engage with the *British Banner*'s arguments in detail (if indeed they were arguments rather than denunciations). The paper serves in his text as a shorthand for a certain kind of religious journalism: earnest, vigilant, and intellectually narrow.

## Key Passages

> "Not only does the *Daily News* abuse the lecture, but the *British Banner*" — Arnold's roll-call of press hostility (PN 003-012)

Arnold lists the *British Banner* among the catalogue of entities that have risen up against him: "the *British Banner*, Judge Edmonds, Newman Weeks, Deborah Butler" — a satirical grouping that mixes the Nonconformist press with spiritualist enthusiasts (PN 003-019).

## Historical Context

The *British Banner* was founded in 1848 by John Campbell (1794–1867), a prominent Congregationalist minister who had previously edited the *Christian Witness*. The paper was weekly and its circulation, while modest by the standards of the daily press, was significant within Nonconformist circles. Campbell was a controversial figure, known for his combative style and his willingness to engage in public polemics with secularists, Roman Catholics, and liberal Anglicans. The paper ceased publication shortly after Campbell's retirement, and its brief existence means that Arnold's mention of it is one of the few surviving public records of its editorial line. Arnold's reference suggests that the *British Banner* was sufficiently well-known in the 1860s to serve as a recognizable symbol of the Nonconformist press.
