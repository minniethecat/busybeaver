# Liberalism

Liberalism, for Arnold, is the political expression of the Victorian faith in
machinery. It is the creed that supposes the removal of external constraints —
on trade, on worship, on the press — will of itself produce human perfection.
Arnold's quarrel with liberalism is not that it is wrong but that it is
"inadequate": it addresses "material civilisation" without ever touching
the "inward condition of the mind and spirit" (001-017, 001-019). This critique,
delivered at the high-water mark of liberal ascendancy in Victorian England, is
one of the most radical moves in *Culture and Anarchy* — an argument that the
triumphant political philosophy of the age has mistaken its means for its end.

## Arnold's Definition

Arnold takes his definition of liberalism from Cardinal Newman, who saw it as
"the great anti-ecclesiastical, anti-dogmatic, anti-supernatural principle" of
the age (001-019). Arnold accepts the description but reverses the valuation:
liberalism did prevail, and its triumph produced a nation of "doers" who had
lost the sense of what doing is for. Liberalism is, in this reading, the
philosophical expression of "doing as one likes" raised to the level of a
political programme (001-017).

### Liberalism as Machinery

Arnold's central insight about liberalism is that it represents machinery at its most seductive. Liberalism is not wrong about freedom — freedom is genuinely valuable — but it systematically confuses the instrument (liberty) with the end (perfection). The liberal says: "Remove the constraints, and people will flourish." Arnold replies: "Flourish toward what?" The liberal framework has no answer because it has deliberately excluded the question of ends from political discourse. This is the liberal achievement and the liberal failure in a single stroke.

### Levels of Liberal Inadequacy

Arnold identifies inadequacy at three levels:

1. **Conceptual**: Liberalism treats freedom as a terminal value rather than an instrumental one. "We have got the maxim of 'liberty' as a fixed principle, and we are satisfied with it; we do not ask what we are to do with our liberty when we have got it" (001-008).

2. **Institutional**: Liberalism concentrates on institutional reform — Free Trade, Parliamentary representation, the removal of religious disabilities — while neglecting the inward condition that gives institutions their value. The Reform Act may be a triumph of liberal politics, but it does not make anyone more cultivated (002-017).

3. **Cultural**: Liberalism, in its middle-class form, is indifferent — even hostile — to the claims of "sweetness and light." The liberal temper is pragmatic, instrumental, and suspicious of anything that cannot be cashed out in terms of institutional change or material improvement (001-020).

### The Philosopher's Liberalism

Arnold distinguishes the popular liberalism of the middle class from the intellectual tradition represented by figures like John Stuart Mill and Wilhelm von Humboldt — "men of the highest order of culture" whose liberalism was informed by a genuine vision of human perfection (001-017–019). This distinction is crucial: Arnold does not reject the liberal tradition wholesale; he insists it must be deepened by the Hellenic ideal of the free play of intelligence. The philosopher's liberalism *can* answer the question "flourish toward what?"; the popular liberalism cannot.

## Usage Across the Text

### The Triumph of Middle-Class Liberalism

Arnold traces the arc of liberalism from a liberating force to a spent one.
"Liberalism prevailed," he writes; "it was the business of the hour" (001-017).
But this very success concealed a defect: liberalism had become identified with
the interests and the mental habits of the middle class, and "the great
middle-class liberalism" could not see beyond its own machinery — Free Trade,
Parliamentary seats, the reform of church rates (002-017).

### Liberalism and Its Discontents

The liberalism of Arnold's youth was already, he sensed, inadequate to the needs
of a mature democracy. "A current of new ideas is gradually superseding our old
middle-class liberalism," he observes, though he acknowledges the new current
"cannot yet be rightly judged" (001-020). Arnold positions "culture" not as the
enemy of liberalism but as its fulfilment — the completion that liberalism
itself cannot supply. Culture asks the question that liberalism suppresses: to what end?

## A Critique from the Left and the Right

Arnold's critique of liberalism has attracted criticism from both directions. From the left, critics argue that Arnold's perfectionism is a retreat from the concrete political struggles — for suffrage, for workers' rights, for religious equality — that liberalism at its best advanced. To say that "freedom is not the end" is, in this view, to dismiss the very real oppression that the liberal tradition fought against.

From the right, critics — particularly in the Tory tradition — have argued that Arnold's critique of liberalism inadvertently makes the case for authority at the expense of liberty, and that his vision of the state as "the organ of our collective best self" is uncomfortably close to continental authoritarianism.

Arnold's defenders reply that both critiques miss the dialectical character of his argument. He is not proposing to replace liberalism with something else — not with socialism, not with Tory paternalism — but to *complete* it. Liberalism has given us freedom; culture asks what we should do with it. The two are not enemies but necessary partners.

## Historical Context

Arnold's engagement with liberalism reflects the political ferment of the 1860s.
The Reform Act of 1867 had been preceded by decades of liberal agitation;
Gladstone's first ministry (1868–1874) was underway as Arnold wrote. But
Arnold's target is less the Liberal Party than the liberal temper — the
assumption, pervasive in Victorian public life, that institutional reform was
identical with moral progress (001-017, 002-017).

The philosophical context is equally important. John Stuart Mill's *On Liberty* (1859) had appeared just a decade before, arguing that the only justification for state coercion was the prevention of harm to others. Arnold's insistence that the state should actively promote "perfection" was a direct challenge to this framework — and one that Mill's followers regarded as a dangerous step toward paternalism.
