# Edmond Beales

Edmond Beales (1803–1881) was an English barrister and radical political activist who served as president of the Reform League during the 1860s. Matthew Arnold references him in *Culture and Anarchy* as a representative figure of middle-class political activism — earnest but lacking in "light."

## Identity

Beales was called to the bar in 1830 and became a county court judge. He was a leading figure in the Reform League, which campaigned for universal manhood suffrage. He was instrumental in organising the Hyde Park demonstrations of 1866–67, which brought the reform agitation onto the streets of London.

## Role in Arnold's Argument

Arnold uses Beales to illustrate two related points. First, Beales exemplifies how individuals are "sacrificed" to political machinery — the causes they serve consume them without delivering the inward perfection culture promises. In a passage about how movements demand sacrifices, Arnold writes: "A voice for every man in his country's government is necessary for the society of the future, but meanwhile Mr. Beales and Mr. Bradlaugh are sacrificed" (002-035).

Second, Beales serves in Arnold's taxonomy of English classes. Arnold classifies him as belonging "to our solid middle-class" who "perhaps, if he had not been a great popular leader, would have been a Philistine" (003-086). This places Beales among the Philistines — honest and energetic but devoted to external machinery rather than inward perfection.

## Key Passages

In Chapter I, Arnold discusses how culture admits the necessity of industrial and political movements while insisting that the present generation is sacrificed to them:

> Freedom of speech is necessary for the society of the future, but the young lions of the Daily Telegraph in the meanwhile are sacrificed. A voice for every man in his country's government is necessary for the society of the future, but meanwhile Mr. Beales and Mr. Bradlaugh are sacrificed. (002-035)

In Chapter III, Arnold places Beales within his class taxonomy, noting his middle-class roots and his role as a popular leader who, despite genuine qualities, lacks the "light" of culture.

By the conclusion, Arnold groups Beales with other educators who claim to be guiding society: "Mr. Disraeli educates, Mr. Bright educates, Mr. Beales educates" (006-272) — a list that implies all are merely reinforcing existing machinery rather than genuinely cultivating inward perfection.

## Relationship to Arnold's Framework

Beales represents the Philistine activist — sincere, energetic, and effective in organising for external reform, but ultimately a worshipper of political machinery. Arnold's treatment of Beales is less harsh than his treatment of Bradlaugh (the "excess" of the working class), but Beales remains trapped in the Philistine worldview, mistaking institutional change for human perfection. The Hyde Park demonstrations, which Beales led, exemplify the "anarchy" of Arnold's title — each class doing as it likes without reference to right reason.
