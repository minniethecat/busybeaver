# Authority

Arnold's solution to the problem of anarchy — not the arbitrary rule of class or force, but the legitimate authority that represents the collective "best self" of the nation. The concept of authority is the positive pole of Arnold's political argument, the constructive counterpart to his critique of machinery, Philistinism, and "doing as one likes" (002-011).

## Arnold's Definition

Authority, in Arnold's framework, is not the same as the power of the aristocracy (which is mere class rule) or the power of the state in its ordinary sense (which risks becoming machinery). True authority is grounded in right reason — it is the embodiment of "the best that has been thought and said" applied to the governance of society. This is a thoroughly normative concept: authority is not what happens to rule but what *deserves* to rule.

Arnold's key move is to locate authority not in any existing institution but in an ideal: the "best self." Every individual, he argues, has an "ordinary self" (the seat of class interest, prejudice, and the impulse to "do as one likes") and a "best self" (the seat of reason, culture, and the pursuit of perfection). Authority is the collective expression of this best self — what the nation would will if it willed rationally rather than from class interest or private appetite.

### The Two-Self Framework

The ordinary self / best self distinction is the philosophical foundation of Arnold's theory of authority, and it deserves careful attention:

- **Ordinary self**: The self as it actually operates in daily life — driven by class loyalty, personal interest, sectarian prejudice, and what Arnold calls "stock notions and habits." When the ordinary self rules, the result is anarchy — each class and faction pursuing its own ends without reference to any common standard (002-016).

- **Best self**: The self as it would be if informed by culture and right reason — impartial, disinterested, oriented toward perfection. "By our best self," Arnold writes, "we are united, impersonal, at one" (002-018). The best self transcends class because it is defined not by social position but by rational pursuit of the good.

### The Aristotelian Echo

Arnold's two-self framework echoes Aristotle's distinction between the desiring part of the soul and the rational part, and more distantly Plato's analogy of the charioteer (reason) governing the horses (appetite and spirit). Arnold would have recognized this genealogy: his Hellenism is, at bottom, a recovery of the Greek conviction that reason can and should govern the passions — and that political authority is merely this psychic governance writ large.

## Authority and the State

Arnold makes the provocative argument that the state — properly conceived — is the organ of our collective best self:

> We want an authority, and we find nothing but jealous classes, checks, and a deadlock. Culture suggests the idea of the State. We find no basis for a firm State-power in our ordinary selves; culture suggests one to us in our best self. (002-017)

This is a radical redefinition of the state, far removed from both the continental absolutist tradition (where the state is the monarch's possession) and the English laissez-faire tradition (where the state is a necessary evil to be minimized). Arnold's state is not the enemy of freedom but its precondition — the institutional framework within which rational freedom is possible.

### Not Hobbes's Leviathan

Arnold's authority is not Hobbesian. Hobbes grounds sovereignty in fear and the contract that ends the war of all against all; Arnold grounds it in reason and the cultivation that lifts individuals out of their ordinary selves. The difference is fundamental: Hobbesian authority is a *modus vivendi*; Arnoldian authority is an *ideal*. Where Hobbes asks what men will actually submit to, Arnold asks what they *ought* to submit to — and answers, "their own best selves, collectively organized" (002-017).

### The Continental Comparison

Arnold's enthusiasm for the state drew on continental sources that were largely unfamiliar to his English audience. The German idealist tradition — particularly Hegel's conception of the state as the actualization of rational freedom — and the French tradition of centralized administration both provided models of what a state *could* be when it was not merely the arena of competing class interests. Arnold does not advocate importing either model wholesale, but he uses them as foils to expose the poverty of the English conception of the state as nothing more than a night-watchman (P03-014).

## The English Resistance to Authority

The great obstacle to authority in England, Arnold argues, is the national obsession with "doing as one likes." The Englishman's instinctive resistance to being told what to do — his conviction that his own judgment is sufficient — makes the establishment of rational authority nearly impossible. This resistance is not, in Arnold's view, a sign of sturdy independence but of provincial self-satisfaction:

> Our governing classes have not the notion of the State, of the nation's collective best self, which culture begets. (002-016)

Each class pursues its own interests and calls the result "freedom." The aristocracy wants to hunt and govern counties; the middle class wants Free Trade and Dissenting chapels; the working class wants to march and break windows. What none of them wants — what the English temperament seems constitutionally incapable of wanting — is a common standard of reason by which all claims might be adjudicated. "We are in a deadlock," Arnold writes, and the deadlock is not accidental: it is the logical consequence of a political culture that has elevated "doing as one likes" to a principle (002-011).

### Authority and Freedom: The Dialectic

Arnold's most subtle political move is to refuse the opposition between authority and freedom. For Arnold, true freedom is not the absence of constraint but the capacity to act in accordance with one's best self. The ordinary self may experience authority as constraint; the best self recognizes it as liberation. This is a classical move — it echoes Plato's argument in the *Republic* that the just man is the freest man — and it places Arnold squarely in the tradition of positive liberty that Isaiah Berlin would later critique. Arnold would reply that Berlin's "negative liberty" is simply machinery-worship in philosophical dress.

## The Best Self

The concept of the "best self" is crucial to Arnold's theory of authority. It is not an external standard imposed by force but an internal standard discovered by culture:

> By our best self we are united, impersonal, at one. (002-018)

The best self is what remains when we strip away class prejudice, personal interest, and the various "stock notions and habits" that culture subjects to criticism. It is "impersonal" — not in the sense of being cold or detached, but in the sense of transcending the particular interests that divide us. The best self of the aristocrat and the best self of the trade unionist are, Arnold insists, identical: both seek perfection, both recognize right reason, both desire sweetness and light. What divides them is not their best selves but their ordinary selves — the selves shaped by class position and sectarian loyalty.

### The Epistemological Problem

A significant difficulty with Arnold's concept of the best self is epistemological: how do we know what the best self wills? Arnold's answer — "culture" — is elegant but circular. Culture teaches us to recognize our best self, but our best self is what recognizes the authority of culture. The circle is not necessarily vicious — it may be the hermeneutic circle that characterizes all self-knowledge — but it leaves Arnold's theory vulnerable to the charge that "the best self" is simply a name for whatever Arnold happens to approve of. His contemporaries were not slow to make this charge.

## Authority's Limits

Arnold is careful to distinguish legitimate authority from two related but distinct concepts:

1. **Establishment** — the existing institutional order, which may or may not embody right reason. Arnold's respect for the Establishment is pragmatic ("it has governed us well") rather than principled. When the Establishment merely perpetuates class privilege, it forfeits the claim to authority (002-013).

2. **Machinery** — authority pursued as an end in itself rather than as a means to perfection. Even a genuinely rational state can become machinery if its citizens forget *why* it deserves obedience and treat it as self-justifying. The French Academy, in Arnold's analysis, illustrates this danger: a legitimate institution that, in the absence of the inward condition of intellectual conscience, becomes just another piece of machinery (P03-004).

## Historical Context

Arnold's theory of the state draws on continental sources largely unfamiliar to his English audience: the German idealist tradition (Hegel, Wilhelm von Humboldt) and French statist thought. His insistence that the state could be a positive moral force ran directly counter to the dominant English liberal tradition. John Stuart Mill's *On Liberty* (1859) had appeared just a decade before, arguing that the only justification for state coercion was the prevention of harm to others. Arnold's claim that the state should actively promote "perfection" struck Mill's followers as a dangerous step toward continental authoritarianism. Arnold's reply — implicit throughout *Culture and Anarchy* — is that Mill's harm principle, for all its elegance, cannot answer the prior question: what kind of life is worth living?

The political context of 1867–69 is crucial. The Second Reform Act (1867) had just dramatically expanded the franchise, and the Hyde Park Riots (1866) had demonstrated that the working class was prepared to use extra-parliamentary means to press its claims. Arnold's call for "authority" was, in part, a response to this moment of democratic transition — an argument that extending the franchise without cultivating the intelligence to use it well was a recipe for anarchy.
