# Henry Sidgwick

Henry Sidgwick (1838–1900) was an English philosopher and one of the founders of the Cambridge school of moral philosophy. In *Culture and Anarchy*, Matthew Arnold engages with Sidgwick as a thoughtful critic — one who challenges Arnold's emphasis on "sweetness and light" by arguing that the world needs "fire and strength" from religion even more.

## Identity

Sidgwick was educated at Rugby and Trinity College, Cambridge, where he became a fellow and later Knightbridge Professor of Moral Philosophy. He was a founder of the Society for Psychical Research and author of *The Methods of Ethics* (1874), a landmark of utilitarian thought. Though deeply religious in sensibility, he resigned his Trinity fellowship in 1869 because he could no longer subscribe to the Thirty-Nine Articles of the Church of England.

## Role in Arnold's Argument

Sidgwick serves as Arnold's most intellectually serious interlocutor in *Culture and Anarchy*. Arnold quotes him directly: "Culture diffuses sweetness and light. I do not undervalue these blessings, but religion gives fire and strength, and the world wants fire and strength even more than sweetness and light" (005-172).

Arnold's response is nuanced. He concedes that "manful walking by the best light one has — fire and strength as he calls it — has its high value as well as culture" (005-173). But he argues that Sidgwick's generalisation fails to account for circumstance: the English, Arnold insists, already dwell "too exclusively" on fire and strength through their Puritan inheritance. What they need now is not more Hebraism but more Hellenism — more "sweetness and light."

## Key Passages

Arnold develops his rebuttal across several passages in Chapter V. He asks whether Sidgwick's claim holds for all times and all peoples:

> When Mr. Sidgwick says so broadly, that the world wants fire and strength even more than sweetness and light, is he not carried away by a turn for powerful generalisation? does he not forget that the world is not all of one piece, and every piece with the same needs at the same time? (005-174)

Arnold's central counter-argument is historical: the Roman world, Leo X's court, and eighteenth-century France may have needed fire and strength more, but the English Puritans, the Barbarians, and the Birmingham lecturers of Arnold's day already have fire and strength in abundance. What they lack is sweetness and light.

In the final chapter, Arnold returns to Sidgwick's conception of "social usefulness" as "losing oneself in a mass of disagreeable, hard, mechanical details" (006-267), arguing that the priority should be clear ideas rather than mechanical execution — "to act is easy, to think is hard," as Goethe said.

## Relationship to Arnold's Framework

Sidgwick is Arnold's foil for the Hebraism-versus-Hellenism argument that structures the entire book. He represents the most defensible version of the position Arnold opposes: that moral strenuousness and religious "fire" are more urgent than cultural refinement. Arnold does not dismiss this position but contextualises it, arguing that England's specific historical situation — a long Puritan ascendancy — means it needs the corrective of Hellenism, not more Hebraism. Sidgwick thus allows Arnold to demonstrate that culture is not hostile to religion but rather insists on their proper balance.
