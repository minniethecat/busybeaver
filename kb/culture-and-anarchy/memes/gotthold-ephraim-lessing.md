# Gotthold Ephraim Lessing

Gotthold Ephraim Lessing (1729–1781) was a German dramatist, literary
critic, and philosopher of the Enlightenment. [[matthew-arnold]] names
him — together with [[johann-gottfried-herder|Johann Gottfried Herder]] —
in Chapter I of *Culture and Anarchy* as one of the defining figures of
the German cultural awakening at the close of the eighteenth century.
For Arnold, Lessing and Herder are exemplary "men of culture": thinkers
who laboured to strip knowledge of its professional hardness and
exclusivity and to carry the best ideas of their age from one end of
society to the other [001-024].

## Identity

Lessing was born in Kamenz, Saxony, the son of a Lutheran pastor.
He studied theology and medicine at the University of Leipzig before
turning definitively to literature. His most celebrated dramatic works
include *Miss Sara Sampson* (1755), *Minna von Barnhelm* (1767),
*Emilia Galotti* (1772), and the verse-play *Nathan the Wise* (1779),
a plea for religious tolerance that drew on the parable of the three
rings. As a critic he reshaped German letters through his *Laocoön*
(1766), which defined the boundaries between poetry and the visual arts,
and the *Hamburg Dramaturgy* (1767–1769), a series of theatre reviews
that dismantled the authority of French neo-classical models and pointed
German drama toward Shakespeare and the Greeks. His theological writings —
including the *Wolfenbüttel Fragments* and *The Education of the Human
Race* (1780) — applied the same critical intelligence to revealed
religion, arguing that religious truths develop progressively through
history rather than descending complete and unalterable from above.

## Role in Arnold's Argument

Arnold presents Lessing not as an object of literary criticism but as
an instance of a cultural type. In the passage where he appears, Arnold
is defining the "great men of culture" as those "who have had a passion
for diffusing, for making prevail, for carrying from one end of society
to the other, the best knowledge, the best ideas of their time" — who
have "laboured to divest knowledge of all that was harsh, uncouth,
difficult, abstract, professional, exclusive; to humanise it, to make
it efficient outside the clique of the cultivated and learned"
[001-024]. Peter Abelard is the medieval instance; Lessing and Herder
are the modern German instances.

The structural move is important. Arnold is arguing that culture is not
a private cultivation of the self but a social force — it "seeks to do
away with classes; to make all live in an atmosphere of sweetness and
light" [001-024]. Lessing, who spent his career bringing philosophical
and theological questions out of the university and into the public
theatre and the periodical press, embodies this diffusion. Arnold's
praise of Lessing is at bottom a praise of translation in the broadest
sense: the work of taking what is difficult and making it available
without making it false.

## Key Passages

Arnold's only direct reference to Lessing in *Culture and Anarchy*
appears in the context of his definition of the men of culture, in
a single sustained paragraph that links Abelard, Lessing, Herder, and
Saint Augustine:

> Such were Lessing and Herder in Germany, at the end of the last
> century; and their services to Germany were in this way inestimably
> precious. Generations will pass, and literary monuments will
> accumulate, and works far more perfect than the works of Lessing
> and Herder will be produced in Germany; and yet the names of these
> two men will fill a German with a reverence and enthusiasm such as
> the names of the most gifted masters will hardly awaken.
> [001-024]

The prediction has proved accurate: although Goethe and Schiller
overshadow Lessing in literary reputation, Lessing's role as the
awakener of German critical consciousness remains unrivalled. Arnold,
who elsewhere in his criticism writes at length on Heine and Goethe,
knew this tradition well, and his pairing of Lessing with Herder —
the critic with the historicist, the dramatist with the philologist —
suggests that culture, as Arnold understands it, requires both the
sharp edge of discrimination and the broad sweep of historical sympathy.
