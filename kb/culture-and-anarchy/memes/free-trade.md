# Free Trade

Free Trade is, for Arnold, the economic talisman of the Victorian middle class —
a doctrine believed in with "religious faith" rather than examined with critical
intelligence [006-023]. It is the most conspicuous instance of what Arnold means
by "machinery": a practical policy, genuinely beneficial in its sphere, that has
been elevated to the status of an absolute good, blinding its adherents to the
deeper questions of human perfection it cannot answer.

## Arnold's Definition

Arnold does not argue against Free Trade as an economic policy; he argues
against the cult of Free Trade as a substitute for thought. The Liberal
free-trader, in his account, possesses a "talisman" that relieves him of the
need to think about what trade is *for* [006-021–022]. Free Trade, like
parliamentary reform and the abolition of church rates, belongs to the machinery
of national life — necessary but not sufficient. The question Arnold insists
upon is: what kind of human being does Free Trade produce? [002-017–018]

## Usage Across the Text

### Free Trade as Machinery

In Chapter II, Free Trade appears alongside parliamentary reform and voluntary
churchism as one of the "great works" of modern English liberalism. Arnold
grants that these are genuine achievements but insists that they are "machinery"
— "not the real ends" of human life but "means" mistaken for ends [002-017–019].
The middle class has "done its great works of free-trade" and now rests
contentedly, never asking whether material prosperity alone constitutes
perfection [002-019].

### The Talisman Mentality

Arnold's most sustained treatment of Free Trade appears in Chapter VI, where he
analyzes what might be called the epistemology of political economy. The
free-trader's "two axioms" — that Free Trade produces wealth and that wealth
produces well-being — are accepted as self-evident truths, when in fact both
require scrutiny [006-021]. Arnold notes dryly that "about one in nineteen" of
the English population benefits from Free Trade in the way its advocates claim,
and that the "religious faith" in the doctrine prevents its adherents from
seeing the actual conditions of English life [006-022–023].

### The Limits of Economic Thinking

Arnold's critique of Free Trade is ultimately a critique of a mode of thinking
that reduces human flourishing to material prosperity. The "practical Liberal
free-traders" and "the British Philistine" share the assumption that "our
greatness and welfare are proved by our being so very rich" [006-030]. Against
this, Arnold insists that the true measure of a nation is not its trade balances
but the quality of its "sweetness and light."

## Historical Context

The repeal of the Corn Laws in 1846 had made Free Trade the defining economic
achievement of mid-Victorian liberalism. Richard Cobden and John Bright, the
leaders of the Anti-Corn Law League, were national heroes; the doctrine of Free
Trade was widely regarded as self-evidently true. Arnold's critique is
accordingly audacious: he is attacking not a fringe position but the central
orthodoxy of the age [001-019, 006-018–023].
