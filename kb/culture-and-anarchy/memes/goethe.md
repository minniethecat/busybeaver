# Johann Wolfgang von Goethe

Johann Wolfgang von Goethe (1749–1832) was the towering figure of German
literature — poet, dramatist, novelist, scientist, and statesman — whose
works including *Faust*, *Wilhelm Meister*, and *The Sorrows of Young
Werther* established him as the supreme representative of European
humanism in the age of revolution. In *Culture and Anarchy*, Arnold
invokes Goethe three times, each invocation serving to authorise the
primacy of thought over action, of inward cultivation over outward
machinery.

## Identity

Goethe lived through the French Revolution, the Napoleonic Wars, and the
dawn of the industrial age, yet maintained throughout his long life a
commitment to the ideal of *Bildung* — the harmonious cultivation of the
whole self through literature, art, science, and experience. This ideal
closely parallels Arnold's "culture," and it is no accident that Arnold —
whose father knew Goethe's friend Carlyle — turns to Goethe as the modern
voice of Hellenism. Where the Greeks embodied the free play of mind in
their civic life, Goethe embodied it in the life of the individual,
demonstrating that the classical ideal could survive in the modern world.

## Role in Arnold's Argument

Arnold cites Goethe on three occasions, each at a critical juncture in his
argument:

First, in the 1869 Preface, Arnold uses Goethe's phrase *eigene grosse
Erfindungen* — "precious discoveries of his own" — to characterise the
self-invented theological systems of Nonconformist sects [P03-011]. The
irony is that what the sectary takes as a mark of authenticity (having
chosen his own creed) Goethe and Arnold see as a mark of provinciality:
the self-made religionist, absorbed in his own inventions, neglects the
larger culture that would liberate him from them.

Second, at the end of Chapter IV — the great Hellenism-Hebraism chapter —
Arnold returns to Goethe for the climactic claim that the Bible, for all
its Hebraic particularity, is "not only a national book, but the Book of
the Nations" [P03-032]. Goethe's verdict on the Bible is the highest
endorsement Arnold can offer: it comes from the supreme modern Hellenist,
not from a Hebraist, and it validates Arnold's insistence that Hebraism
and Hellenism are ultimately complementary, not contradictory.

Third, in the Conclusion, Arnold deploys Goethe's most famous maxim — "to
act is easy, to think is hard" [006-044] — as the definitive reply to the
"believers in action" who demand that culture produce practical results.
The maxim encapsulates Arnold's entire case for the priority of inward
transformation: action without thought is not merely ineffective but
positively dangerous, and the difficulty of thinking — of seeing things
as they really are — is precisely why culture deserves our first and most
sustained attention.

## Key Passages

**On sectarian invention**: Arnold describes how the Nonconformist's
"eigene grosse Erfindungen, as Goethe calls them, — the precious
discoveries of himself and his friends for expressing the inexpressible
and defining the undefinable in peculiar forms of their own, cannot but,
as he has voluntarily chosen them, and is personally responsible for them,
fill his whole mind" [P03-011].

**On the Bible as the Book of the Nations**: "The Bible, which preaches
this word, will forever remain, as Goethe called it, not only a national
book, but the Book of the Nations" [P03-032].

**On the difficulty of thinking**: "Because the believers in action,
forgetting Goethe's maxim, 'to act is easy, to think is hard,' imagine
there is some wonderful virtue in losing oneself in a mass of mechanical
details, therefore they excuse themselves from much thought about the
clear ideas which ought to govern these details" [006-044].

## Relationship to Arnold's Framework

Goethe is, for Arnold, the indispensable modern witness: the man who
proves that the Hellenic ideal is not merely an antiquarian fancy but a
living possibility. When Arnold wants to authorise the primacy of thought
over action, he quotes Goethe. When he wants to show that the highest
culture can honour Hebraism without being subordinated to it, he quotes
Goethe. When he wants a term — *eigene grosse Erfindungen* — for the
self-deception of provincial sincerity, he borrows it from Goethe.

Goethe thus functions as Arnold's secular saint: the modern man who
achieved what Socrates achieved in antiquity — a life governed by the
free play of consciousness — and who thereby demonstrates that the
pursuit of perfection is not an academic exercise but the most serious
business of human existence.
