# Martin Luther

Martin Luther (1483–1546) was the German theologian and reformer who initiated the Protestant Reformation. [[matthew-arnold]] invokes Luther at a pivotal moment in Chapter II, characterising him as a **Philistine of genius** — a figure of immense moral energy who was yet constitutionally incapable of the balance that [[culture]] requires [002-020].

## Identity

Luther was an Augustinian friar, theologian, and professor at the University of Wittenberg whose Ninety-five Theses (1517) launched the Reformation. His translation of the Bible into German shaped the modern German language. For Arnold, Luther represented the archetypal reformer: a man of powerful conviction whose religious passion reshaped Europe, yet whose limitations illustrated the dangers of [[hebraism]] unmoderated by [[hellenism]].

## Role in Arnold's Argument

Arnold deploys Luther in one of the book's most striking critical formulations:

> "Among whom, said he, 'there was a Luther and also a Melancthon,' who had not been trained in episcopal palaces." [002-020]

The pairing of Luther with his more conciliatory colleague Philipp Melanchthon is significant. Arnold uses the distinction to argue that the Reformation — for all its greatness — was ultimately a movement of the [[ordinary-self]], not the [[best-self]]. Luther's energy was heroic, but his narrowness was the narrowness of [[philistines|philistinism]].

## The Philistine of Genius

Arnold's characterisation of Luther as a "Philistine of genius" is one of his most memorable critical formulations. The phrase captures the paradox Arnold sees at the heart of the Reformation: a movement of immense historical significance that was led by men who were, from the standpoint of [[culture]], incomplete. Luther's greatness lay in his moral passion; his limitation lay in his indifference to [[sweetness-and-light]].

In the Preface, Arnold notes the institutional consequences of Luther's work, observing that "the Calvinist and Lutheran Churches are established" as state churches in parts of Europe [P03-025] — a fact that complicates the simple Nonconformist narrative of disestablishment as progress.

## Relationship to Arnold's Framework

Luther is the paradigmatic case of Arnold's thesis that [[hebraism]] and [[hellenism]] must be balanced. Luther had Hebraism in abundance — the passion for righteousness, the submission to the will of God — but lacked the Hellenic qualities of spontaneity, play of mind, and the desire to see things as they are. His historical importance is undeniable; his incompleteness as a human being is equally so.
