The **King James Bible**, also known as the Authorized Version, is the English translation of the Bible commissioned by King James I and published in 1611. In [[culture-and-anarchy]], [[matthew-arnold]] draws extensively upon it as the authoritative source for scriptural quotations, particularly in his discussions of Hebraism, [[right-reason]], and the relationship between religious and secular culture.

## Cultural Significance in Victorian England

By Arnold's time, the King James Bible had been the dominant English translation for over two and a half centuries. Its language — the cadences of its prose, the imagery of its poetry — had penetrated every layer of English culture, from Parliament to the cottage hearth. For Arnold, this universality was a cultural resource of the first importance. The King James Bible provided a shared vocabulary that could, in principle, unite [[barbarians]], [[philistines]], and [[populace]] in a common conversation about human [[perfection]].

Yet Arnold also recognised that this shared vocabulary had become a source of division. The [[nonconformity|Nonconformist]] reading of the King James Bible — literal, legalistic, fixated on [[book-of-leviticus|Levitical]] prohibitions — had turned a text of spiritual and literary richness into a manual of conduct. Arnold's project, in part, was to recover the King James Bible for culture: to read it not as a sectarian rulebook but as one of the great achievements of the human spirit.

## Role in Culture and Anarchy

Arnold's argument in [[culture-and-anarchy]] relies heavily on biblical language and concepts. Throughout the work, he quotes from the King James Bible to support his central thesis about the complementary roles of [[hebraism]] and [[hellenism]]. The most concentrated use of scriptural quotation occurs in Chapter IV, where Arnold develops his concept of [[right-reason]] and the idea that "the kingdom of God is within you" [004-020].

Arnold's approach to biblical citation is distinctive. Rather than treating scripture as an object of theological dispute, he draws on it as a source of moral and cultural wisdom accessible to all educated readers. This aligns with his broader project of making religion serve culture rather than sectarianism [004-016].

## In Arnold's Concept of Hebraism

For Arnold, the King James Bible represents the foundation of [[hebraism]] — the tradition of strict moral conscience derived from the Hebrew scriptures and carried into English life through [[puritanism]] and [[nonconformity]]. He writes:

> "The fruit of the righteous is a tree of life; and he that winneth souls is wise" [004-017]

This and similar quotations underpin Arnold's argument that Hebraism emphasizes conduct and obedience, which must be balanced by the Hellenic pursuit of "seeing things as they really are" [004-018].

## Quotations and Allusions

Arnold's use of the King James Bible extends beyond direct quotation to pervasive allusion. Key passages he cites include:

> "I am the light of the world" [004-020]

> "The truth shall make you free" [004-021]

> "Be ye doers of the word, and not hearers only" [004-022]

These citations serve to ground Arnold's cultural argument in the shared textual heritage of his Victorian audience. By appealing to the King James Bible rather than to sectarian theological works, Arnold positions culture as a unifying force that transcends denominational divisions [004-023].

## The Bible as Literature

Implicit in Arnold's use of the King James Bible is a claim about the relationship between scripture and literature. Arnold approaches the biblical text with the same critical sensibility he brings to [[sophocles]] or [[goethe]] — as a work of human genius whose value lies not in its doctrinal content but in its power to illuminate the human condition. This literary approach to scripture, anticipating elements of twentieth-century biblical criticism, is one of the most quietly radical aspects of *Culture and Anarchy*.
