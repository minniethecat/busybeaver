# Zephaniah Diggs

Zephaniah Diggs is a minor, possibly fictional figure mentioned by Matthew Arnold in Chapter III of *Culture and Anarchy*. Arnold introduces him as his "poor old poaching friend" to humorously illustrate the defect of the working class in his tripartite analysis of English society [003-013].

## Identity

Diggs is a rural labourer and poacher — a representative of that substratum of the English working class that Arnold, in his public-school paternalism, regarded with a mixture of affection and condescension. The name "Zephaniah" — drawn from the Old Testament minor prophet — was a common name among Dissenting communities in rural England, placing Diggs within the Nonconformist world that Arnold anatomises throughout *Culture and Anarchy*. Whether Diggs existed in reality or was an invention of Arnold's is uncertain; the text suggests he is a type rather than a documented historical individual.

## Role in Arnold's Argument

Arnold uses Diggs as a representative of the working-class defect. After analysing the aristocratic defect — represented by [[lord-elcho|Lord Elcho]] and Sir Thomas Bateson — and the middle-class defect — represented by Mr. Bazley and the Rev. W. Cattle — Arnold arrives at the working class. The working class, he argues, has a characteristic failing: "the falling short in what Mr. [[frederic-harrison]] calls those 'bright powers of sympathy and ready powers of action'" [003-013].

Diggs embodies this failing. Arnold describes him as a man who, "between his hare-snaring and his gin-drinking, has got his powers of sympathy quite dulled and his powers of action in any great movement of his class hopelessly impaired" [003-013]. This is Arnold at his most patrician: the poacher's manual skill and resourcefulness, which in other contexts might be admired, are here made to stand for a narrowing of moral and political capacity.

## Key Passages

Arnold introduces Diggs through a characteristic rhetorical move — an apologetic preface that sharpens rather than softens the critique:

> "I have a friend, who is very familiar with the ways of them, the poor labouring people, a poacher in fact, though he is alive in the flesh, he is dead to all heed of criticism, and as I know he will never read what I now write, I am free to name him freely, my poor old poaching friend, Zephaniah Diggs." [003-013]

The phrase "though he is alive in the flesh, he is dead to all heed of criticism" is revealing. It echoes the Pauline language that Arnold deploys throughout *Culture and Anarchy* to discuss the tension between Hebraism and Hellenism. Diggs's literal physical aliveness contrasts with his intellectual deadness — a miniature version of Arnold's larger argument about the spiritual state of England.

Arnold quickly adds that "examples of this defect belong, as I have said, to a bygone age rather than to the present" [003-013], suggesting Diggs represents an older, fading type of working-class character. This temporal distancing allows Arnold to criticise the working class while simultaneously claiming that the criticism is already obsolete.

## Rhetorical Function

Diggs serves a precise rhetorical purpose in Arnold's tripartite analysis. The structure is symmetrical:

| Class | Mean | Excess | Defect |
|-------|------|--------|--------|
| Aristocracy | [[lord-elcho|Lord Elcho]] | Sir Thomas Bateson | — |
| Middle Class | Mr. Bazley | Rev. W. Cattle | — |
| Working Class | — | — | Zephaniah Diggs |

The asymmetry is telling: the working class lacks both the "mean" and "excess" columns. Arnold provides no positive exemplar of working-class virtue to balance Diggs's defect. This structural absence reflects the broader pattern of *Culture and Anarchy*, in which the working class appears primarily as a problem to be managed — through [[culture]], through the [[the-state|State]], through the tutelage of the cultivated classes.

## Relationship to Arnold's Framework

Diggs belongs to the world of the "[[populace]]," the third of Arnold's three great classes. His humourous inclusion allows Arnold to complete his analytical framework without directly criticising a living public figure who might answer back. In this, Diggs functions as a rhetorical safety valve: by making the working-class defect both personalised and comic, Arnold can address it without appearing to attack the working class as a whole.

The passage also illustrates Arnold's method of argument by anecdote. Rather than presenting empirical evidence about working-class political capacity, Arnold offers a story about a named individual whose characteristics are taken to be representative. This is consistent with the essayistic mode of *Culture and Anarchy*, which proceeds by accumulation of examples rather than systematic demonstration.
