# Ernest Renan

Ernest Renan (1823–1892) was a French philologist, historian of religion,
and philosopher whose work on the historical Jesus (*Vie de Jésus*, 1863)
made him one of the most celebrated — and controversial — European
intellectuals of his generation. In *Culture and Anarchy*, Arnold invokes
Renan as a witness from the Continent: a thinker who understands what the
English do not — that culture, in the sense of "liberal intelligence," is
not an ornament but a political necessity.

## Identity

Renan trained for the priesthood at the seminary of Saint-Sulpice before
losing his faith and devoting himself to secular scholarship. His
historical-critical study of the New Testament, which treated Jesus as a
human figure rather than a divine person, caused an international scandal
and led to his suspension from the chair of Hebrew at the Collège de
France (he was reinstated in 1871). He was elected to the Académie
Française in 1878. Arnold, who read French fluently and followed French
intellectual life closely, regarded Renan as the pre-eminent example of
the cultivated European mind.

## Role in Arnold's Argument

Arnold uses Renan in three distinct ways: as a diagnostician of
Anglo-American cultural poverty, as an exemplar of "liberal intelligence,"
and as a supporter — albeit a qualified one — of State action. Renan
functions as a Continental counterweight to the English parochialism Arnold
seeks to overcome.

### As Diagnostician of America

Arnold quotes Renan's observation about the United States at length:
"the natural truth of things, Monsieur Renan says of America, in a book
he has recently published, is that the work of universal levelling is
proceeding there with a rapidity and a completeness elsewhere unknown"
[P03-014]. Renan's point — which Arnold endorses — is that American
democracy, for all its energy, produces a "universal *bassesse*," a
lowering of the human type to the common measure. This is precisely the
danger Arnold sees for England if the Philistine middle class is not
leavened by culture.

### As Bearer of "Liberal Intelligence"

Arnold borrows Renan's phrase "la raison, l'intelligence libérale"
to name the faculty that the English lack. "Liberal intelligence, as
Monsieur Renan calls it, or, in our own words, reason and intelligence"
[P03-015] — this is what Arnold's "culture" aims to cultivate. Renan
supplies the Continental vocabulary for what Arnold is trying to name in
English.

### On State Action

In Chapter III, Arnold enlists Renan in support of the idea of the State:
"the most powerful of these friends, Monsieur Renan, on State action"
[003-022]. Renan's French republicanism, grounded in the strong-state
tradition of post-revolutionary France, serves Arnold as a model for what
England has lost — the capacity to conceive of the State as something other
than a neutral referee among private interests.

## Key Passages

**On America**: "The work of universal levelling is proceeding there with
a rapidity and a completeness elsewhere unknown … the condition of culture
is, as Monsieur Renan's words show, just what America, in its present
state, least possesses" [P03-014, P03-019].

**On liberal intelligence**: Arnold quotes Renan's concept of "liberal
intelligence" as the standard against which English middle-class culture
must be measured [P03-015].

## Relationship to Arnold's Framework

Renan represents the intellectual tradition Arnold wants England to learn
from. Unlike the English liberals Arnold criticises, Renan understands that
the free play of mind is not a luxury but the condition of genuine
political freedom. Unlike the English positivists (Frederic Harrison and
his school), Renan does not demand that intelligence produce a systematic
programme; he models the disposition — open, curious, undogmatic — that
Arnold calls "Hellenism."
