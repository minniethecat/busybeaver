# Hepworth Dixon

William Hepworth Dixon (1821–1879) was an English historian, travel
writer, and editor of *The Athenaeum*. Arnold invokes him twice in
*Culture and Anarchy* (1869) as an illustration of the Victorian public's
credulity before authors who command a large following without possessing
genuine intellectual or spiritual authority.

## Role in *Culture and Anarchy*

Arnold's treatment of Hepworth Dixon is satirical but carefully poised.
He does not deny Dixon's ability or interest; he uses Dixon's popularity
to diagnose a cultural pathology.

### "The Mormons, by One of Themselves"

In Chapter III, Arnold writes of the British habit of deference to
whoever "says them decisively, and has a large following of some strong
kind":

> This habit of ours is very well shown in that able and interesting
> work of Mr. Hepworth Dixon's, which we were all reading lately, *The
> Mormons, by One of Themselves*. … the well-known book in which Mr.
> Hepworth Dixon described the Mormons, and other similar religious
> bodies in America, with so much detail and such warm sympathy.
> (PN 003-013)

Arnold's point is not that Dixon's book is worthless — he calls it
"able and interesting" — but that the public takes Dixon for an
authority on religion simply because he writes vividly and has a
following. The cultural failure is the inability to distinguish
between "a great and profound spirit, which is an authority" and
"inferior spirits" who are merely popular.

### Dixon as "the Colenso of Love and Marriage"

In the Conclusion, Arnold returns to Dixon with sharper satire:

> Mr. Hepworth Dixon, who may almost be called the Colenso of love
> and marriage,—such a revolution does he make in our ideas on
> these matters, just as Dr. Colenso does in our ideas on religion,—
> tells us of the notions and proceedings of our kinsmen in America.
> (PN 006-015)

The comparison with [[john-william-colenso|Bishop Colenso]] is pointed:
both men, in Arnold's view, represent the tendency to treat every social
experiment as equally worthy of attention, surrendering the critical
function of "right reason." Dixon's sympathetic accounts of Mormon
polygamy and American free-love communities are, for Arnold, symptoms
of a culture that has lost its grip on the idea of a standard of
perfection.

## Connection to Arnold's Argument

Dixon functions in *Culture and Anarchy* as a case study in the failure
of what Arnold calls the "ordinary self" — the unregenerate taste that
gravitates toward sensation rather than perfection. Arnold's objection
to Dixon is less about Dixon's content than about the public's inability
to read him critically. The passage illustrates Arnold's broader
argument that Hebraism, left to itself and disconnected from Hellenism's
"free play of consciousness," produces not moral rigour but a peculiar
kind of Victorian prurience dressed in biblical language.
