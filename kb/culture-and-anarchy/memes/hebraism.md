# Hebraism

The pole of moral strenuousness in Arnold's dialectic — the force that drives humanity toward right conduct, obedience to divine law, and the conquest of sinful desire. Together with [[hellenism]], it forms the central organizing dichotomy of *Culture and Anarchy*. Arnold traces Hebraism's influence through the [[the-reformation|Reformation]], English Puritanism, and the Victorian moral temper exemplified by figures such as [[bishop-wilson|Bishop Wilson]] (004-004).

## Arnold's Definition

Arnold identifies Hebraism as one of the two great spiritual disciplines that have shaped Western civilization, alongside Hellenism. Its governing idea, he writes, is "conduct and obedience":

> The uppermost idea with Hebraism is conduct and obedience. Nothing can do away with this ineffaceable difference. The Greek quarrel with the body and its desires is, that they hinder right thinking, the Hebrew quarrel with them is, that they hinder right acting. (004-004)

For Hebraism, the great danger is sin — the failure to act rightly and obey divine commandment. Its characteristic virtue is conscience, the internal monitor that warns against moral transgression. "The word conscience," Arnold notes, "comes from a root meaning knowledge, but it has come to mean, in Hebraism, something quite different from knowing — it has come to mean the sense of sin" (004-005).

## Hebraism and Hellenism: A Comparative View

Arnold's distinction between the two forces can be summarised across several dimensions:

| Dimension | Hebraism | Hellenism |
|-----------|----------|-----------|
| **Governing idea** | Conduct and obedience | Seeing things as they really are |
| **Cardinal danger** | Sin | Ignorance |
| **Central virtue** | Conscience | Spontaneity of consciousness |
| **Primary mode** | Doing | Knowing |
| **Historical expression** | Christianity, Puritanism | Greek philosophy, Renaissance |
| **Relation to body** | Quarrel with desires (hinder right acting) | Quarrel with desires (hinder right thinking) |
| **Temporal focus** | Fire and strength — immediate action | Light and flexibility — patient understanding |
| **Excess produces** | Narrowness, Philistinism, "incomplete and mutilated men" | Moral indifference, ineffectual aestheticism |
| **Motto** | "The law of the Lord is perfect" | "Know thyself" |

> As we do not forget that both Hellenism and Hebraism are profound and admirable manifestations of man's life, so we must not forget that they require to be adjusted one to the other. (004-006)

## The Essential Bent Toward Doing

Arnold emphasises that the fundamental disposition of Hebraism is to "set doing above knowing" (004-005). This is not a defect but a defining characteristic. Hebraism addresses a real and permanent human need — the need for moral discipline, for a clear standard of right and wrong, and for the energy to act on that standard:

> Stoicism, which was the true and great representative, in the Pagan world, of the Hebraising spirit, worked and taught, in like manner, with its eyes fixed straight upon conduct. And so it was a great and admirable school of morals. (004-005)

This "bent to fire and strength" made Hebraism the driving force behind the Protestant Reformation, which Arnold regards as a legitimate return to the Hebraic core of Christianity against the Hellenized accretions of medieval Catholicism (004-006).

## Hebraism and Puritanism

The English Puritans are Arnold's primary exhibit of Hebraism in action. They represent "the strong, Puritan, Hebraising movement" that has dominated English life since the seventeenth century (004-006). Arnold acknowledges the genuine moral seriousness of Puritanism — its "zeal for walking staunchly by the best light they have" (P03-007).

Yet he argues that this zealous Hebraism has become one-sided. The Puritan fixation on conduct has produced what Arnold calls "incomplete and mutilated men" — people who are morally serious but intellectually narrow, aesthetically impoverished, and incapable of the flexibility that comes from seeing things from more than one side (P03-007). The social consequence is the "Hebraising of Britain" that leaves English life dour, suspicious of beauty, and hostile to free play of mind.

## The Hebraism-Hellenism Balance

Arnold's crucial insight is that Hebraism is not wrong — it is incomplete without [[hellenism]]. The great ages of civilisation have been those where the two forces achieved some kind of balance. The tragedy of Hebraism unchecked, Arnold argues, is that its very moral seriousness can become a form of narrowness:

> The bright promise of Hellenism faded, and Hebraism ruled the world. Then was seen the amazing spectacle of the human spirit, strictly obeying the law of its being, but chained, nevertheless, to a mechanical routine. (004-008)

Arnold traces this pattern through Western history: primitive Christianity was "altogether the hour of Hebraism" (004-015); the Renaissance brought a temporary revival of Hellenism; the Reformation restored Hebraic dominance; and the nineteenth century remains under Hebraism's "exclusive predominance" (005-010).

## The English Problem

England's particular affliction, in Arnold's diagnosis, is "Hebraism rampant" — a culture so obsessed with conduct and conscience that it has "stamped out" the Hellenic virtues of beauty, intelligence, and flexibility (P03-017). The Victorian middle class, Arnold's "Philistines," represent the social type of unchecked Hebraism: earnest, industrious, morally self-satisfied, and deeply suspicious of "culture" as a foreign, effeminate import.

Yet Arnold does not call for the abandonment of Hebraism. His critique is a call for balance, not for replacement:

> The care and discipline received from Hebraism remain for our race an eternal possession. And, as we are all of us, in various degrees, the offspring of both Hebraism and Hellenism, we are naturally inclined to regard them both as part of our inheritance. (P03-032)

## Hebraism in Arnold's Own Religious Vision

In his later chapters, Arnold develops a more constructive view. He envisions a "Hellenising within the limits of Hebraism itself" — "that renewing of the Hebraic spirit by contact with the Hellenic" (006-017). The goal is not to replace Jerusalem with Athens, but to let the light of Greek intelligence illuminate the Hebraic commitment to righteousness. Bishop Wilson, with his combination of moral seriousness and intellectual openness, becomes Arnold's model of this synthesis.

## Historical Context

Arnold's Hebraism-Hellenism dichotomy draws on a long tradition, including Heine's distinction between "Hebrew" and "Greek" tendencies, and ultimately on the early Christian contrast between Athens and Jerusalem articulated by Tertullian. The immediate context was the Victorian crisis of faith: Arnold sought to preserve the moral seriousness of Christianity (its Hebraic core) while opening it to the intellectual and aesthetic currents of modern thought (the Hellenic dimension). The formula was controversial — religious conservatives saw it as secularising Christianity, while secular radicals found it too accommodating to religion.
