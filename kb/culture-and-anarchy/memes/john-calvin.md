# John Calvin

John Calvin (1509–1564) was the French theologian, reformer, and principal architect of the Reformed tradition within Protestantism. [[matthew-arnold]] invokes Calvin twice in the Preface to *Culture and Anarchy* — once through Lord Bolingbroke's phrase "friends of Calvin's discipline," and once in a comparative observation about the institutional settlement of the [[the-reformation|Reformation]] in continental Europe. In Arnold's framework, Calvin represents the disciplinary intensity of [[hebraism|Hebraism]] in its Protestant form: morally serious, institutionally coherent, yet incomplete without the balance of [[hellenism|Hellenism]].

## Identity

Calvin was born in Noyon, Picardy, and educated in law and the humanities at Orléans and Bourges before breaking with Rome and devoting himself to the cause of reform. His *Institutes of the Christian Religion* (first edition 1536, final edition 1559) is the foundational systematic theology of the Reformed tradition. From 1536 until his death he dominated the religious and civic life of Geneva, establishing a theocratic order governed by consistorial discipline and a rigorous moral code. Calvin's influence radiated from Geneva across Europe — through the French Huguenots, the Dutch Reformed Church, the Church of Scotland, and the English Puritans — making him, alongside [[martin-luther|Martin Luther]], one of the two most consequential figures of the Protestant Reformation.

## Role in Arnold's Argument

Arnold invokes Calvin in the Preface to *Culture and Anarchy* as part of his extended argument about religious [[establishment|establishments]] and the provincialism of English [[nonconformists|Nonconformity]]. Unlike [[martin-luther|Luther]], whom Arnold characterises directly as a "Philistine of genius," Calvin is cited less as a personality than as the organisational and disciplinary pole of the Reformed tradition — the figure whose church order gave institutional shape to the Puritan impulse that Arnold both respects and critiques.

Calvin enters Arnold's argument at two strategic points. First, through Lord Bolingbroke's historical observation about the Elizabethan Settlement: if "the friends of Calvin's discipline" — the English Presbyterians and Puritan reformers — had been incorporated into the Established Church, the fissiparous sectarianism that Arnold diagnosed in Victorian Nonconformity might have been averted [P03-023]. Second, as a comparative political point: in France and Germany, "the Calvinist and Lutheran Churches are established" side by side with the episcopal church, demonstrating that establishment need not mean uniformity — and that the provincialism of English Dissent is a contingent historical outcome rather than an inevitable consequence of Reformed doctrine [P03-025].

## Key Passages

Arnold's first reference to Calvin comes through the voice of Lord Bolingbroke, whom he quotes at length as an impartial historical witness to the missed opportunity of the Elizabethan age:

> "Lord Bolingbroke, on a matter of this kind a very clear-judging and impartial witness, says, in a work far too little read, his Remarks on English History: — 'The measures pursued and the temper observed in Queen Elizabeth's time tended to diminish the religious opposition by a slow, a gentle, and for that very reason an effectual progression. There was even room to hope that when the first fire of the Dissenters' zeal was passed, reasonable terms of union with the Established Church might be accepted by such of them as were not intoxicated with fanaticism. These were friends to order, though they disputed about it. If these friends of Calvin's discipline had been once incorporated with the Established Church, the remaining sectaries would have been of little moment, either for numbers or reputation; and the very means which were proper to gain these friends, were likewise the most effectual to hinder the increase of them, and of the other sectaries in the meantime.'" [P03-023]

The second reference situates Calvin as part of the continental model for a plural establishment that Arnold proposes as an alternative to the stark choice between Anglican monopoly and Nonconformist voluntaryism:

> "Why should not a Presbyterian or Congregational Church, based on this considerable and important, though not essential principle, of the congregation's power in the church management, be established, — with equal rank for its chiefs with the chiefs of Episcopacy, and with admissibility of its ministers, under a revised system of patronage and preferment, to benefices, — side by side with the Episcopal Church, as the Calvinist and Lutheran Churches are established side by side in France and Germany?" [P03-025]

## Relationship to Arnold's Framework

Calvin occupies a more ambivalent position in Arnold's argument than [[martin-luther|Luther]], who receives the direct and memorable characterisation of "Philistine of genius." Luther is the figure Arnold critiques; Calvin is the figure whose institutional legacy he grapples with. This difference reflects the deeper structure of Arnold's analysis: Luther represents the originating spiritual impulse of the Reformation — heroic, one-sided, incomplete; Calvin represents its crystallisation into a church order that, for all its moral intensity, created the structural conditions for the separatist tendency Arnold deplores in English Nonconformity.

In Arnold's conceptual vocabulary, Calvin stands squarely within [[hebraism|Hebraism]] — the tendency to make the moral law and the will of God the consuming centre of human life, to the neglect of the free play of mind and the pursuit of [[culture]]. Yet Arnold's proposal in the Preface is not to reject Calvin's legacy but to reintegrate it: to restore the Presbyterian and Congregational principle — "the popular authority of elders," the power of the congregation — to the National Church, reconnecting the heirs of Calvin's discipline with "the main current of national life" [P03-025]. The institutional pluralism Arnold sketches — Episcopalian and Presbyterian side by side, within a single establishment — is his most concrete proposal for healing the rift between [[hebraism|Hebraism]] and [[hellenism|Hellenism]] at the level of church polity.

This makes Calvin, in Arnold's argument, the symbolic representative of an alternative historical path: not the path of separation and provincialism that English Dissent took, but a path of incorporation and breadth that might have been — and that might still, Arnold suggests, be charted. The "friends of Calvin's discipline" were not, in Bolingbroke's telling, fanatics; they were "friends to order, though they disputed about it" [P03-023]. Their tragedy was not their doctrine but their extrusion — a structural failure of the English Reformation, not a doctrinal failure of Calvinism.
