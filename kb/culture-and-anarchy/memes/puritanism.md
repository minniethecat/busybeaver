# Puritanism

The living force of Hebraism in English life — a tradition Arnold both honors for its moral seriousness and critiques for its resistance to "sweetness and light," making it the central case study of his argument about the insufficiency of Hebraism without Hellenism. Puritanism, for Arnold, is not merely a historical movement but a permanent possibility of the English soul (001-013).

## Arnold's Definition

Puritanism, in Arnold's usage, is broader than the historical movement of the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries. It names a permanent disposition of the English character: the tendency to prioritize moral conduct over intellectual cultivation, to value righteousness above beauty, and to regard "the life of the mind" with suspicion as a distraction from the more urgent business of salvation.

> The strong, Puritan, Hebraising movement which has been so great a force in English history has left its mark on all our national habits. (004-006)

The historical Puritans are Arnold's primary exhibits, but his real target is the living Puritan inheritance — the Dissenters, the Nonconformists, the evangelical middle class — that continues to shape English culture in the nineteenth century. Arnold's genius as a social critic is to see that Puritanism is not a spent force but a living one, and that its power is precisely what makes its defects a matter of national concern.

### Puritanism as a Spiritual Type

Arnold identifies several constitutive features of the Puritan spiritual type:

1. **The primacy of conscience**: Puritanism elevates moral self-scrutiny above all other forms of attention. The Puritan is always asking "Am I right with God?" — and never asking "Am I fully human?" (004-006).

2. **Suspicion of the aesthetic**: Beauty, art, literature — these are, for the Puritan, at best permissible and at worst dangerous. They distract the soul from its one essential business.

3. **Resistance to intellectual play**: "The free play of the mind upon all subjects" — Arnold's definition of Hellenism — is, to the Puritan temper, a form of spiritual frivolity. Thinking should serve conviction, not question it.

4. **Provincial certainty**: The Puritan lives within a closed circle of conviction and experiences no discomfort at its closure. What lies outside the circle is not interesting but threatening.

### The Greatness and the Limitation

Arnold's treatment of Puritanism is distinguished by its refusal of simple condemnation. He recognizes — more generously than many of his contemporaries — that the Puritan achievement was real and valuable. The Puritans brought moral energy, seriousness of purpose, and a deep sense of individual responsibility to English life. Their "zeal for walking staunchly by the best light they have" is genuinely admirable (P03-007).

The problem is structural rather than substantive: it is not that the Puritan light is false but that it is narrow. The Puritan has "developed one side of their humanity at the expense of all others" (P03-007). A complete human being needs more than moral seriousness, just as a healthy body needs more than a strong heart. The heart is essential, but a body that is all heart is a monstrosity.

## The Puritan Achievement

Arnold's acknowledgment of what Puritanism accomplished is essential to understanding his argument, because it establishes that his critique is not dismissive but dialectical. He is not saying "Puritanism is wrong" but "Puritanism is incomplete." The distinction matters because it determines the character of the remedy:

1. **Moral energy**: The Puritans injected into English life a seriousness about conduct that the easygoing Anglicanism of the eighteenth century had largely lost. The Evangelical Revival, with its roots in Puritan spirituality, was arguably the most significant religious movement in England between the Reformation and the Oxford Movement.

2. **Individual responsibility**: Puritanism's insistence that each soul stands directly before God — without the mediation of priest or sacrament — fostered a culture of personal accountability that Arnold, for all his critiques, never proposed to dismantle.

3. **Democratic instincts**: The Puritan conviction that every soul is equally precious in God's sight contained, in embryo, a democratic politics. Arnold's own democratic commitments — his insistence that perfection must be "general," extending to all — are, in part, a secularization of Puritan egalitarianism.

### The Key Passages

Arnold's most concentrated discussions of Puritanism occur in the Preface and Chapter IV. In the Preface, he confronts the Puritan inheritance directly through the figure of the Nonconformists:

> Puritanism was the great stronghold of the Hebraising spirit in England, and the Nonconformists are the successors and representatives of the Puritans. Certainly we are no enemies of the Nonconformists; for, on the contrary, what we aim at is their perfection. (P03-007)

In Chapter IV, he places Puritanism within the larger dialectic of Hebraism and Hellenism, arguing that the Puritan ascendancy after the Civil War gave English culture its distinctive bias toward conduct over consciousness:

> The strong, Puritan, Hebraising movement which has been so great a force in English history has left its mark on all our national habits, on our gait and bearing, on our literature and our art. (004-006)

## The Puritan Narrowness

Arnold's critique of Puritanism is that it produces "incomplete and mutilated men" — people who have "developed one side of their humanity at the expense of all others" (P03-007). The metaphor of mutilation is deliberate and harsh: a body with one overdeveloped limb is not a stronger body but a deformed one.

### What Puritanism Lacks

The Puritan is morally serious but intellectually narrow, zealous for righteousness but indifferent to beauty. A society dominated by the Puritan spirit will be suspicious of art, hostile to free intellectual inquiry, and resistant to the "flexibility" that Arnold identifies as the great practical benefit of "sweetness and light" (001-016). The specific deficits are:

- **Aesthetic poverty**: The Puritan world is morally rich but sensorily impoverished. There is no place in it for the contemplation of beauty as an end in itself.

- **Intellectual rigidity**: The Puritan mind moves within fixed categories and experiences deviation as threat rather than as opportunity for growth.

- **Social joylessness**: The Puritan suspicion of pleasure extends beyond the obviously sinful to the innocent delights of art, music, and conviviality. A culture shaped by Puritanism will be a culture without "sweetness."

### The Diagnostic Function

Arnold's critique of Puritanism serves a diagnostic function within his larger argument. Puritanism is not merely wrong; it reveals something about the structure of human perfection. A tradition that develops one faculty at the expense of all others demonstrates, by its failure, the necessity of harmony. The Puritan error is the most instructive error in Arnold's intellectual universe because it shows, with painful clarity, what happens when a partial good is mistaken for a complete one.

## Puritanism and the Middle Class

Arnold identifies Puritanism with the English middle class. The Dissenting tradition is the spiritual expression of the class that made the Industrial Revolution. Their commercial virtues — thrift, industry, self-reliance — are the secular expression of Puritan discipline. Their cultural vices — narrowness, smugness, indifference to beauty — are the secular expression of Puritan one-sidedness.

This identification of a religious tradition with a social class is one of Arnold's most influential moves. It transforms Puritanism from a theological category into a sociological one, and in doing so makes it available for a kind of critique that would have been impossible if Puritanism were treated purely as a matter of doctrine. The middle-class Dissenter is not wrong about God; he is wrong about what a human life should be. And that error is visible not in his theology but in his furniture, his newspapers, his tone of voice — in all the "stock notions and habits" that culture subjects to criticism (001-013).

### The Paradox of Puritan Success

There is a deep irony in Arnold's analysis: the very success of the Puritan middle class is what makes its narrowness so dangerous. A marginalized sect can afford to be narrow; a ruling class cannot. The Puritans had won — politically, economically, culturally — and their victory had made their limitations into national limitations. The Dissenter who once suffered under the Clarendon Code now shaped the legislation of the Liberal Party. What had been a sectarian defect had become a national problem.

## Remedy: The Hellenic Supplement

Arnold's prescription follows logically from his diagnosis: Puritanism does not need to be replaced but supplemented. What it lacks is Hellenism — the free play of mind, the love of beauty, the capacity for intellectual flexibility. The aim is not to extinguish the Puritan moral energy but to broaden it, to make it "harmonious" by adding what it has neglected. "Sweetness and light" are not alternatives to moral seriousness; they are its completion.

## Historical Context

The immediate context was the political power of Dissent in the 1860s. Nonconformists were a major Liberal constituency and the driving force behind campaigns for disestablishment and temperance. Arnold's attack on "Puritanism" was in part an intervention in these contemporary battles — an attempt to persuade the Dissenters that their political program, however principled, was founded on an incomplete understanding of human nature.

The deeper context was the long shadow of the English Civil War and the Restoration. Puritanism had been defeated politically in 1660 but had triumphed culturally in the centuries that followed. The Evangelical Revival of the eighteenth century, the rise of Methodism, the spread of Dissenting chapels across industrial England — all represented the continuing vitality of the Puritan spirit. Arnold's question was whether that spirit, having remade English religion and English politics, was capable of remaking itself.
