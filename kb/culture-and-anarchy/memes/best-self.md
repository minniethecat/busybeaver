# Best Self

The "best self" is Arnold's central philosophical coinage — the element in
each person that is not personal at all but impersonal, rational, and shared.
It is the ground on which genuine authority can stand, and it is the answer
Arnold gives to the anarchic principle of "doing as one likes." The best self
is what remains when class interest, personal appetite, and the "ordinary
self" are set aside.

## Arnold's Definition

Arnold introduces the best self as a necessary counter-concept to the ordinary
self. "By our best self we are united, impersonal, at harmony," he writes;
"we find no basis for a firm State-power in our ordinary selves; culture
suggests one to us in our best self" [002-024]. The best self is not a
supernatural entity but a fact of human nature accessible through culture —
the discipline of seeing things as they are. It is "capable of affording a
serious principle of authority" [002-026], and the State, properly conceived,
is nothing other than "the organ of our collective best self, of our national
right reason" [002-026].

## Usage Across the Text

### The Best Self as the Foundation of Authority

Arnold's entire argument for State action rests on the concept of the best
self. Without it, there is no answer to the objection that the State is merely
the rule of one class over another. "The great thing," Arnold insists, "is to
find our best self, and to seek to affirm nothing but that" [002-025]. This
is not mysticism but a practical political principle: if there is no
trans-personal standard to which individuals and classes can appeal, then
politics reduces to a contest of interests and force — which is precisely the
condition Arnold diagnoses in Victorian England [002-025–026].

### The Ordinary Self and Its Limits

The best self is defined in opposition to the "ordinary self" — the bundle of
appetites, class habits, and personal preferences that each individual carries
and that each class projects onto the political stage. Arnold observes that
"in almost all who have it, it is mixed with some infusion of the spirit of
an ordinary self, some quantity of class-instinct" [003-011]. The difficulty
of politics is that the ordinary self is vivid, tangible, and clamorous while
the best self is quiet, impersonal, and easily ignored. "It is clear," Arnold
writes, "that the very absence of any powerful authority amongst us, and the
prevalent doctrine of the duty and happiness of doing as one likes, and
asserting our personal liberty, must tend to prevent the erection" of the
best self into an effective authority [003-012].

### The Best Self in Each Class

Arnold is careful to locate the best self across all social classes. In each
class, "there are born a certain number of natures with a curiosity about
their best self, with a bent for seeing things as they are, for disentangling
themselves from machinery" [003-010]. These "aliens," as Arnold calls them,
are the carriers of culture in every station — they are "sown more abundantly
than one might think" and "appear where and when one least expects it"
[003-010]. This is a democratic element in Arnold's thought: the best self is
not the property of an elite but a human capacity distributed across the
social field, though it requires cultivation to become effective.

### The State as Organ of the Best Self

The political conclusion Arnold draws is that the State should be the
institutional expression of the collective best self. "We see, then, how
indispensable to that human perfection which we seek is, in the opinion of
good judges, some public recognition and establishment of our best self, or
right reason" [003-023]. This is Arnold's alternative both to laissez-faire
individualism (which trusts the ordinary self) and to class-based governance
(which enshrines one class's ordinary self as law). The State, on this view,
is "the organ of our collective best self" [002-026] — not an external
imposition but the institutional form of the very authority that culture
discovers within each person.

## Historical Context

Arnold's concept of the best self draws on a long tradition of philosophical
idealism — from Plato's rational soul through Kant's transcendental ego to
the Victorian idealist revival then underway at Oxford (T. H. Green's
*Prolegomena to Ethics* would appear in 1883, shortly before Arnold's death).
But Arnold's formulation is distinctively political: the best self is not a
metaphysical postulate but a practical answer to the crisis of authority in a
democratising society [002-024, 003-014–016].
