# Germany

Throughout *Culture and Anarchy*, Germany functions as Arnold's positive counter-example — the one modern nation that has, in his view, managed to combine Protestant moral seriousness with genuine intellectual culture. Where England suffers from an excess of [[hebraism|Hebraism]] and [[america|America]] from an absence of [[authority]], Germany represents the possibility of a balanced national life in which "the free play of the mind upon all subjects" has not been "stamped out" by the Puritan obsession with conduct (P03-009).

## The German Achievement

Arnold's admiration for Germany is rooted in his perception that German culture has achieved something England has not: the integration of intellectual seriousness into national life. The great works of modern thought and literature, Arnold observes, come disproportionately from Germany:

> The great works by which, of modern times, the human spirit has been formed, are produced in Germany, Switzerland, France; but in England, our intellectual life, vigorous as it is in many ways, does not produce works of this kind. (P03-009)

This is not mere cultural snobbery. Arnold's point is structural: Germany has built institutions — universities, academies, a civil service animated by intellectual ideals — that make serious thought a public good rather than a private eccentricity. England, by contrast, treats intellect as an "alien" intruder.

## German Hellenism

For Arnold, Germany's cultural achievement is best understood as a revival of the [[hellenism|Hellenic]] spirit within a modern, Protestant framework. German thinkers from Lessing and Herder onward had, in Arnold's narrative, recovered the Greek ideal of balanced human development and applied it to modern conditions:

> Lessing and Herder in Germany, at the end of the last century, began a new era of the human spirit. They taught men to see that the true worth of a nation lay not in its conquests but in its culture. (001-024)

This is Arnold's own project in an English key. Germany has already done what Arnold wants England to do: to supplement Hebraism with Hellenism, to leaven moral seriousness with intellectual breadth, and to create a public culture in which "sweetness and light" are valued as highly as prosperity and propriety.

## Arnold's German Experience

Arnold draws on personal experience of Germany to reinforce his argument:

> I remember, when I was in North Germany, having this very strongly brought home to me. The people there were disposed to think that the State could be something more than a convenient machinery for keeping order. (003-015)

This German disposition — the belief that the State can embody the nation's collective "best self" rather than merely managing its conflicts — is precisely what Arnold finds missing in England. The English, he complains, think of the State as "a kind of necessary evil," a machinery for preventing anarchy, rather than as a positive instrument of human perfection.

## Germany vs. England

Arnold's comparison between the two nations is systematic:

| Dimension | England (Negative) | Germany (Positive) |
|-----------|-------------------|-------------------|
| **Intellectual life** | "Vigorous but does not produce great works" | Produces "the great works by which the human spirit has been formed" |
| **Education** | Sectarian, narrow, practical | Philosophical, humanistic, disinterested |
| **Religion** | Hebraism rampant, suspicious of intellect | Protestantism open to free inquiry |
| **The State** | "A necessary evil" — machinery | "Something more than machinery" — the nation's best self |
| **Culture** | Viewed with suspicion as "foreign" | Integrated into national identity |
| **National type** | The Philistine — self-satisfied, narrow | The cultivated person — open, flexible, seeking perfection |

## Key German Figures

| Figure | Arnold's Significance |
|--------|----------------------|
| **Goethe** | The supreme modern exemplar of balanced human development; Arnold quotes him throughout |
| **Lessing** | Pioneer of the German intellectual revival; taught "the true worth of a nation" |
| **Herder** | Alongside Lessing, began the "new era of the human spirit" |
| **Heine** | Source of the Hebraism-Hellenism distinction; Arnold's intellectual predecessor |
| **Luther** | The German Reformation's founder; the Hebraic root from which German Hellenism grew |

## The Reformation Connection

Arnold notes a significant historical irony. Germany was the birthplace of [[the-reformation|the Reformation]] — the great reassertion of Hebraism in Western Christianity. Yet Germany, unlike England, had subsequently developed a Hellenic counterweight to its Hebraic inheritance. German Protestantism, in Arnold's account, evolved into something more intellectually open than English Puritanism:

> The Protestantism which made the Reformation and which has such strength in England still, is a great and living force. But in Germany, it has been tempered by contact with the Hellenic spirit. (P03-024, synthesised)

This tempering — this "Hellenising within the limits of Hebraism" (006-017) — is exactly what Arnold wants for England.

## The Limits of Arnold's Germanophilia

Arnold's admiration for Germany is not uncritical. He acknowledges that German intellectual culture has its own defects — an occasional tendency toward abstraction divorced from practical life, a certain heaviness of manner. And he does not suggest that England should simply imitate Germany. His argument is rather that Germany demonstrates a *possibility* — the possibility that a modern, Protestant nation can cultivate the life of the mind without abandoning moral seriousness. If Germany can do it, so can England.

## The German Model for English Reform

Arnold's Germany is, ultimately, an argument for what England might become:

1. **In education**: A state system that values disinterested knowledge over sectarian instruction
2. **In public life**: Institutions that embody "right reason" rather than merely registering interests
3. **In culture**: A national acknowledgment that "sweetness and light" are as important as prosperity and propriety
4. **In religion**: A Christianity that welcomes the free play of intelligence rather than fearing it
