# America

The United States serves Arnold throughout *Culture and Anarchy* as his most important comparative case study — a nation that embodies both the promise and the peril of a society organised around individual liberty rather than collective [[authority]]. Arnold's America is not a real country so much as a rhetorical proving ground: every argument for English reform that appeals to the American example must confront Arnold's insistence that America's apparent success masks a deeper cultural poverty (P03-013).

## The American Example in Victorian Debate

In the 1860s, America was frequently invoked in English political argument — by liberals as proof that religious disestablishment and democratic government could flourish, and by conservatives as a warning of the chaos that awaited if traditional authorities were abandoned. [[the-times|The Times]] was particularly fond of citing America as evidence against the established Church:

> "In the colonies," says The Times, "we see religious communities unfettered by state endowments and thriving wonderfully." But America is the great example alleged. (P03-013)

Arnold's response to this argument is characteristic: he does not deny the fact of American religious vitality, but he questions what the English advocates of the American model fail to notice — the cultural and intellectual cost of doing without any centre of authority.

## Arnold's Critique

### The Worship of the Average Man

Arnold's deepest criticism of America is that it has elevated the "average man" into a cultural ideal. American democracy, in his view, is not merely a political arrangement but a spiritual disposition — one that treats the common opinion as the final standard and regards any appeal to a higher authority as aristocratic presumption:

> An intelligent American newspaper, the Nation, says that "the American people has no need of culture, because it is already in possession of the best culture in the world." (002-001, paraphrased)

This self-satisfaction — the conviction that one already possesses everything worth having — is, for Arnold, the characteristic American vice. It is the Philistinism of an entire nation.

### The Absence of "The Best Self"

America lacks what Arnold calls "the best self" — a collective standard of right reason that transcends individual opinion and interest:

> When M. Renan says of America that it is "a country where they have no idea of the State," he puts his finger on the essential weakness. (P03-014, paraphrased)

Without an idea of the State as a centre of authority and light, American democracy degenerates into the mere aggregation of individual wills — "doing as one likes" on a national scale. The result is not anarchy in the crude sense, but something more subtle: a society that is energetic, prosperous, and religious, yet incapable of pursuing "total perfection" because it has no collective sense of what perfection might be.

### Religion Without Culture

Arnold acknowledges the vitality of American religion — "America is the great example alleged of religion thriving without an establishment" (P03-013). But he insists that this vitality comes at a cost. American Protestantism, in Arnold's view, is Hebraism without Hellenism — morally earnest but intellectually narrow, spiritually intense but aesthetically impoverished:

> What the Americans lack is not religion but culture; not moral seriousness but the free play of the mind upon all subjects. (P03-016, synthesised)

### The Missing "Centre of Light"

Arnold's positive ideal — a State that embodies the nation's collective best self and serves as a "centre of light and authority" — is the precise opposite of what he finds in America. Where America disperses authority into individual judgment, Arnold wants to concentrate it in institutions that represent something higher than mere opinion. "It is not fatal to Americans to have no religious establishments," Arnold concedes, but he immediately adds that the absence of establishments is part of a larger pattern — "the want of a centre of spiritual and intellectual authority" (P03-030).

## America vs. Germany

Arnold's comparison between America and [[germany|Germany]] is instructive and deliberate:

| Dimension | America (Negative) | Germany (Positive) |
|-----------|-------------------|-------------------|
| **Political culture** | Democracy without authority | Authority with intellectual freedom |
| **Religion** | Vital but uncultured | Protestant but intellectually engaged |
| **Education** | Practical, utilitarian | Philosophical, humanistic |
| **Cultural ideal** | The "average man" | Goethe's ideal of balanced development |
| **Relation to the State** | The State as necessary evil | The State as the nation's "best self" |

Germany, for Arnold, shows that a modern, Protestant nation need not choose between authority and freedom. America shows what happens when freedom is mistaken for the whole of the human good.

## Arnold's Qualified Defence

It is important to note that Arnold's critique of America is not a dismissal. He acknowledges American achievements:

- "Her energy and fine gifts" (P03-016)
- The vitality of American religious life
- The intelligence of American commentary on itself (he quotes the *Nation* approvingly)
- Benjamin Franklin as "the most considerable man, it seems to me, whom America has yet produced" (001-021)

Arnold's point is not that America is a failure but that it is incomplete. It has Hebraism (moral energy, religious seriousness) without sufficient Hellenism (intellectual breadth, cultural refinement). The American example, far from proving that England should follow the path of disestablishment and democracy, proves the opposite: that England needs to supplement its Hebraic inheritance with the Hellenic virtues that America has neglected.
