# Heinrich Heine

Heinrich Heine (1797–1856) was a German poet, journalist, and critic
whose lyric poetry, political satire, and cultural journalism made him
one of the most consequential — and controversial — figures in
nineteenth-century German letters. [[matthew-arnold]] knew Heine's
work well: he devoted a substantial essay to him in *Essays in
Criticism* (1865) and lectured on him at Oxford. In *Culture and
Anarchy* itself, Heine appears only in a single glancing allusion,
but the allusion is telling: Arnold invokes Heine's mockery of the
[[philistine|Philistine]] to sharpen his own anatomy of English
middle-class narrowness [003-017].

## Identity

Heine was born in Düsseldorf to a Jewish family and studied law at
Bonn, Göttingen, and Berlin, where he attended Hegel's lectures.
He converted to Lutheranism in 1825 — a "ticket of admission to
European culture," as he bitterly put it — but the conversion did
little to protect him from the anti-Semitic hostility that dogged
his career. His early lyric collections, especially the *Buch der
Lieder* (*Book of Songs*, 1827), established him as the greatest
German poet after [[goethe|Goethe]]; poems such as "Die Lorelei"
and "Du bist wie eine Blume" entered the common stock of German
song. But Heine was never content to be a lyric poet alone. His
journalism — collected in the four volumes of *Reisebilder* (*Travel
Pictures*, 1826–1831) — combined travelogue, political polemic,
and literary criticism in a prose of extraordinary lightness and
bite, and his long verse satires *Atta Troll* (1843) and
*Deutschland: Ein Wintermärchen* (*Germany: A Winter's Tale*, 1844)
turned the same weapons on the political and cultural pieties of
the German Restoration. He spent the last eight years of his life
bedridden in Paris with a spinal disease, during which he produced
some of his most trenchant poetry and prose.

## Role in Arnold's Argument

Heine does not appear in *Culture and Anarchy* by name. His presence
is felt indirectly, through the concept of the Philistine — a term
Arnold borrows from German usage, where it had been popularised in
part by Heine's satirical portraits of the German *Bürger*. When
Arnold writes of "the Philistine" as the personification of
"the vulgarity and commonness of the middle class," he is drawing
on a tradition of German cultural criticism that runs from Goethe
and Schiller through Heine to Arnold's own contemporaries.

The deeper connection, however, lies in Arnold's essay on Heine
published two years before *Culture and Anarchy*. There Arnold
describes Heine as "the most important German successor and
continuator of Goethe in Goethe's most important line of activity" —
the line of the "liberator," the writer who "wages war against
Philistinism." Heine, Arnold writes, "dissolved much, he awakened
much, he set men's minds in motion; he opened up new paths of
thought." But Arnold also faults Heine for lacking "high seriousness"
— for being, in the end, too much the wit and too little the
moralist. This double judgement — admiration for the liberating
impulse, reservation about the absence of positive moral content —
exactly mirrors Arnold's treatment of the Hellenic element in
culture, which must be completed by Hebraism if it is not to
dissolve into mere cleverness.

## Key Passages

Although Heine is not named in *Culture and Anarchy*, the central
passage on Philistinism in Chapter III carries his imprint:

> For Philistine gives the notion of something particularly
> stiff-necked and perverse in the resistance to light and its
> children; and therein it specially suits our middle class, who
> not only do not pursue sweetness and light, but who even prefer
> to them that sort of machinery of business, chapels, tea-meetings,
> and addresses from Mr. Murphy and the Rev. W. Cattle, which makes
> up the dismal and illiberal life of the Puritan middle class.
> [003-017]

The tone — the sudden shift from analytical precision to satirical
particularity, the naming of names — is Heine's inheritance, even
if the moral framework within which Arnold deploys it is his own.
