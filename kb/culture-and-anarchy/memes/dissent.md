# Dissent

Dissent, in Arnold's vocabulary, names something larger than religious
nonconformity. It is the spiritual temper of the English middle class — the
Hebraising impulse at its most intense and most English. The Dissenter's
insistence on private judgment, his suspicion of ecclesiastical authority, and
his identification of religion with conduct rather than intelligence make him,
in Arnold's view, the paradigmatic Englishman: "earnest, narrow, and
unconverted" by "sweetness and light."

## Arnold's Definition

Arnold treats Dissent as the religious expression of the English individualism
he critiques throughout the book. The Dissenter "has a strong sense of the
importance of conduct, of the necessity of sound morals" — this is his genuine
virtue. But he "has not the idea of human perfection as a harmonious expansion
of all the powers which make the beauty and worth of human nature." Conduct,
divorced from intelligence, becomes a form of machinery — the means mistaken for
the end [002-009].

## Usage Across the Text

### Dissent and the Middle Class

Dissent is, for Arnold, inseparable from the middle class. The "political
dissenters in the middle-class" represent the fusion of religious earnestness
with political liberalism that had defined English public life since the
Reformation [002-009]. Arnold does not wish to suppress Dissent — "the
Dissidence of Dissent" is, he grants, a genuine expression of English liberty —
but he insists that Dissent alone cannot supply the "sweetness and light" that
a nation needs [002-009].
