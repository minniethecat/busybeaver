# Modern Movement

## Overview

The Modern Movement in architecture was the early 20th-century attempt to create a building art adequate to the conditions of machine-age civilization. Rejecting the historical revival styles that had dominated the 19th century, modernist architects sought forms that expressed structure, function, and the materials of industrial production. Mumford, writing in 1924, treats the modern movement as an emergent force -- promising but not yet realized, caught between the genuine achievements of engineering and the superficiality of mere stylistic innovation.

## Core Ideas

### A Universal Language of Building

Mumford's assessment of the modern movement is dialectical. He grants its fundamental premise: "The modernist is correct in saying that the mass of building ought to speak the same language" [(007-036)]. The [[medieval-building-tradition|medieval tradition]] had been a common language, understood by builders and users alike; the modern movement sought to recover that universality through the vocabulary of steel, glass, and concrete.

### The Dictionary Versus the Literature

But Mumford warns against a naive identification of newness with goodness. "Where the modernist becomes confused... is in regarding the *dictionary* of modern forms, whose crude elements are exhibited in our factories and skyscrapers and grain elevators, as in any sense equivalent for their creative expression" [(007-036)]. The modern movement's reduction of architecture to a few standard forms, while understandable as a corrective to 19th-century eclecticism, risks becoming a new orthodoxy as limiting as the academic classicism it replaced.

> "So far our mechanical architecture is a sort of structural Esperanto: it has a vocabulary without a literature, and when it steps beyond the elements of its grammar it can only translate badly into its own tongue the noble poems and epics that the Romans and Greeks and medieval builders left behind them." [(007-036)]

### Beyond Form to Civilization

Mumford's prescription is that the modern movement must go beyond form to function -- to the reorganization of building production, land tenure, and community planning: "The leaders of modernism do not, indeed, make the mistake that some of their admirers have made" [(007-037)]. [[frank-lloyd-wright|Frank Lloyd Wright]], in Mumford's account, exemplifies the right path: an architecture that uses machine-age means for human ends.

> "In sum, the best modern work does not merely respect the machine: it respects the people who use it. It is the lesser artists and architects who, unable to control and mold the products of the machine, have glorified it in its nakedness, much as the producer of musical comedies, in a similar mood of helpless adulation, has 'glorified' the American girl -- as if either the machine or the girl needed it." [(007-038)]

## Historical Context

### Converging Currents

The modern movement emerged from several converging currents: the Arts and Crafts movement's critique of industrial production, the structural innovations of the [[chicago-school-city|Chicago]] School, the theoretical manifestos of European architects (Loos, Gropius, Le Corbusier), and the social reform movements that saw architecture as a tool for improving working-class life.

### Mumford's Unusual Position

Mumford's position is unusual: he shares the modernists' rejection of historical styles and their enthusiasm for engineering, but he is skeptical of the formalist tendency within modernism -- the idea that a new style, in itself, constitutes a solution to architecture's problems. For Mumford, the problem is not primarily stylistic; it is social, economic, and institutional. A new architecture requires a new civilization.

> "It is not sufficient, with Mr. Louis Sullivan, to say that we must accept and enthrone the virtues of democracy; still less is there any meaning in the attempt of the Educational Committee of the American Institute of Architects to educate public taste in the arts. Nor is there any genuine esthetic salvation in the demand of the modernists that we embrace in more wholehearted fashion the machine. Our architecture has been full of false starts and unfulfilled promises, precisely because the ground has not been worked enough beforehand to receive the new seeds." [(008-012)]

### Key Figures

- **[[louis-sullivan|Louis Sullivan]]** (1856--1924): The American architect whose search for "a rule so broad as to admit of no exceptions" made him, in Mumford's view, the most serious American contributor to the modern movement [(007-036)].
- **[[frank-lloyd-wright|Frank Lloyd Wright]]** (1867--1959): [[louis-sullivan|Sullivan]]'s disciple, whom Mumford singles out as the modern architect whose work does not "resemble either factories or garages or grain elevators" -- whose buildings demonstrate that modern means can serve humane ends [(007-037)].

## In the Text

### Ambivalence and Assessment

Mumford's treatment of the modern movement is concentrated in Chapter VII, but his ambivalence runs through the entire book. He sees modernism as the most promising architectural development of his time -- the only movement seriously attempting to engage with the realities of machine-age civilization. But he also sees its limitations: its formal reductionism, its neglect of regional and ecological context, its tendency to treat buildings as isolated objects rather than parts of a larger urban fabric.

> "The error with regard to these new forms of building is the attempt to universalize the mere process or form, instead of attempting to universalize the scientific spirit in which they have been conceived. The design for a dwelling-house which ignores everything but the physical necessities of the occupants is the product of a limited conception of science which stops short at physics and mechanics, and neglects biology, psychology, and sociology." [(007-034)]

### Achievement and Weakness

The modern movement's greatest achievement, in Mumford's view, is its insistence that architecture must be of its time -- that it cannot retreat into historical styles and must find forms adequate to modern materials, modern construction methods, and modern ways of life. Its greatest weakness is its failure to see that form alone cannot solve the problems Mumford has diagnosed: the dissolution of the crafts, the degradation of the urban environment, the gap between art and life.

> "To fancy that any widespread improvement of architecture lies principally with the architects is an esthetic delusion: in a barren soil the most fertile geniuses are cut off from their full growth. We have not lacked architects of boldness and originality, from Latrobe to Louis H. Sullivan: nor have we lacked men of great ability, from Thomas Jefferson to Bertram Goodhue; nor yet have we lacked men who stood outside the currents of their time and kept their own position, from Richardson to Dr. Cram. With all these capacities at our disposal, our finest efforts in building remain chaotic and undisciplined and dispersed -- the reflection of our accumulated civilization." [(008-003)]

## Legacy

The modern movement transformed 20th-century architecture. Its formal vocabulary -- the flat roof, the strip window, the open plan, the free facade -- became the international language of building in the postwar period. But Mumford would likely argue that this very success confirmed his critique: modernism became a style, not a transformation of civilization.

The questions Mumford posed to the modern movement -- can architecture be universal without being monotonous? Can it be of the machine age without being machinelike? Can it serve human needs rather than merely expressing structural logic? -- remain live questions for contemporary practice. The modern movement's formal achievements are secure; its social and ecological ambitions remain unfinished business.
