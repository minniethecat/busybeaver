# American Academy in Rome

The American Academy in Rome, founded in 1894 on the Janiculum Hill, was the institutional capstone of the [[beaux-arts|Beaux-Arts]] ascendancy in American architecture.

Conceived by [[charles-mckim|Charles Follen McKim]] and sustained by [[daniel-burnham|Daniel Burnham]] and their circle, the Academy was modeled — in spirit if not in precise structure — on the French Academy's Villa Medici, which housed the winners of the Prix de Rome.

Its purpose was to give American architects, artists, and scholars sustained contact with classical antiquity, providing what the [[ecole-des-beaux-arts|École des Beaux-Arts]] had given an earlier generation: immersion in "the Roman precedent" [(006-017)].

For Mumford, the Academy was more than an educational institution; it was a symptom. It embodied the cultural logic of an age in which, as he writes, "all roads lead literally to Rome" [(006-030)].

## Founding and Mission

The Academy was incorporated in 1894 as the American School of Architecture in Rome, expanding to include painting and sculpture shortly afterward. [[charles-mckim|McKim]], who had studied at the [[ecole-des-beaux-arts|École des Beaux-Arts]] and traveled extensively in Italy, was its driving force. [[daniel-burnham|Burnham]], the impresario of the [[worlds-columbian-exposition-1893|World's Columbian Exposition]], provided organizational backing.

The original board included Richard Morris Hunt, the first American to study at the [[ecole-des-beaux-arts|École]], and a roster of the leading architects, painters, and sculptors of the imperial period.

The mission was explicit: to bring American practitioners into direct contact with the monuments of classical antiquity and the [[renaissance-architecture|Renaissance]].

Rome was not chosen for its contemporary vitality but for its historical authority — for "its stereotyped canons and rules" and "its skill in conspicuous waste" [(006-013)].

The Academy was the institutional embodiment of a conviction Mumford summarizes with characteristic ambivalence: that American architecture required Roman models to achieve dignity.

## Mumford's Diagnosis: The Imperial Home

Mumford's reference to the Academy is brief but concentrated.

It occurs at the close of his survey of the imperial period's monuments, immediately after his discussion of the great railroad stations — the [[pennsylvania-station|Pennsylvania Station]] in [[new-york-city|New York]] and Union Station in [[washington-dc|Washington, D.C.]] — which he identifies as the major architectural achievements of the era.

The passage is worth quoting at length:

> It was no accident that the great triumphs of American architecture during the imperial period were the railroad stations; particularly the Pennsylvania and the Grand Central in New York, and the Union Station in Washington. Nor is it by mere chance that the Washington and the Pennsylvania stations are the monuments to two architects, McKim and Burnham, who worshiped most wholeheartedly at the imperial shrine. With capital insight, these men established the American Academy at Rome: they recognized their home.

[(006-030)]

The irony is layered. [[charles-mckim|McKim]] and [[daniel-burnham|Burnham]] "recognized their home" in Rome because the architecture they practiced was, in Mumford's analysis, Roman architecture — the architecture of baths, basilicas, and circuses, adapted to the purposes of a modern imperial capital.

The Academy was not an escape from American conditions but their confirmation. It institutionalized a backward gaze at precisely the moment when, as Mumford argues, "a living art is a fresh gesture of the spirit" [(006-039)].

## Rome as Precedent and Problem

Rome functions in *Sticks and Stones* as both model and warning. Mumford's extended analogy between imperial Rome and Gilded Age America — drawing on the Italian historian Guglielmo Ferrero's concept of a "*véritable recommencement d'histoire*" [(006-009)] — runs through the entire book:

> These years witnessed what the Roman historian, Ferrero, has called a "véritable recommencement d'histoire." In the new centers of privilege there arose a scale of living and a mode of architecture which, with all its attendant miseries, depletions, and exploitations, recalled the Rome of the first and second centuries after Christ.

[(006-009)]

The Academy, by this logic, was the architectural profession's formal acknowledgment of the parallel. It anchored American classicism not to indigenous traditions or regional materials but to an imported authority. The consequence, Mumford argues, was a systematic neglect of the local.

Vermont marble and Indiana limestone — the materials of [[worlds-columbian-exposition|the White City]] — supplanted regional stone. "Even architects whose place of work was nearer to the site of their buildings were, nevertheless, compelled to copy the style of the more successful practitioners in [[new-york-city|New York]] and [[chicago-school-city|Chicago]]" [(006-029)].

## The Institutional Network

The Academy was not an isolated institution but the hub of an interlocking system.

It was linked to the [[ecole-des-beaux-arts|École des Beaux-Arts]] through the Prix de Rome tradition, to the [[architectural-competition|architectural competition]] system through the Rome Prize, and to the major firms — [[charles-mckim|McKim]], Mead & White; [[daniel-burnham|Burnham]] and Bennett — that dominated American institutional commissions.

Its alumni staffed the architecture schools and shaped the curricula that perpetuated classical training.

### The Beaux-Arts Institutional Complex

| Institution | Role | Relationship to Academy |
|-------------|------|------------------------|
| [[ecole-des-beaux-arts|École des Beaux-Arts]] (Paris) | Training | Source of the Prix de Rome model; trained Academy founders |
| [[american-academy-in-rome|American Academy in Rome]] | Immersion | Hub: classical residency for American architects and artists |
| [[architectural-competition|Architectural Competitions]] | Selection | Rome Prize competitions; [[town-planning|civic design]] competitions judged by Academy affiliates |
| Major firms ([[charles-mckim|McKim]], Mead & White; Carrère & Hastings) | Practice | Staffed by Academy alumni; executed the imperial program |
| Architecture schools (Columbia, MIT, Penn) | Reproduction | Taught by Academy affiliates; perpetuated the classical curriculum |

This network constituted what Mumford called the "imperial" architectural culture. It was, in its own terms, remarkably successful. It produced buildings of great technical refinement and a professional culture of high competence.

But it was also, Mumford insists, a closed circuit: "the one thing the museum cannot attempt to do is to supply a soil for living art: all that it can present is a pattern for reproduction" [(006-039)].

The Academy was, in effect, the museum's extension into education — a place where architects learned to reproduce the perfected past.

## Legacy

The Academy's influence on American architecture was at once deep and bounded. It trained the architects who built the civic centers, libraries, museums, and railroad stations of the early twentieth century. It sustained a standard of classical literacy that enriched American public architecture.

But its limitations were those of the imperial culture it served.

The [[modern-movement|modern movement]], when it arrived, defined itself in deliberate opposition to everything the Academy represented — and found its symbols not in Rome but in the grain elevator, the factory, and the [[the-machine-age|machine]].

Mumford's judgment is not dismissive but diagnostic. The Academy was, in his reading, a truthful expression of its age.

The architects who founded it did so "with capital insight" [(006-030)] — they understood that their architecture, for all its American sites and functions, took Rome as its spiritual home. The question Mumford leaves open is whether a living architecture can have its home in the remote past.
