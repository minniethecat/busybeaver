# Gothic Architecture

## Overview

Gothic architecture originated in 12th-century France as a structural innovation combining pointed arches, ribbed vaults, and flying buttresses to achieve unprecedented height and light in ecclesiastical buildings. In the 18th and 19th centuries, the Gothic Revival movement resurrected medieval forms as a reaction against classical restraint, becoming — as Lewis Mumford documents — one of the principal stylistic currents in American architecture between 1820 and the Civil War. The Gothic mode was adopted both for its picturesque romanticism and, later, for its claimed organic relationship to Christian civilization.

> The coincidence of industrialism and romanticism is just as emphatic in America as in England; and it is not without historic justice that the architect who in 1807 designed the chapel of St. Mary's Seminary in Baltimore used a mode that had been known as the Carpenter's Gothic. [004-004]

## Characteristics

The Gothic style is defined by several distinctive formal and structural features:

- **Pointed arch**: The defining element, distributing weight more efficiently than the round Roman arch and allowing greater flexibility in proportion.
- **Ribbed vault**: A skeletal framework of intersecting stone ribs supporting a thinner web of masonry, reducing weight and allowing clerestory windows.
- **Flying buttress**: External arched supports transmitting lateral thrust from the nave vault to external piers, freeing wall surfaces for large windows.
- **Vertical emphasis**: Towers, spires, and pinnacles draw the eye upward, expressing spiritual aspiration.
- **Tracery and stained glass**: Elaborate stone window tracery filled with colored glass, transforming interior light.

In its 19th-century revival form, these structural principles were often reduced to decorative appliqué — pointed arches and tracery applied to otherwise conventional buildings. Mumford describes the result as "a collection of tags, thrown at random against a building" [004-007].

> Gothic touches about doors and the exterior of windows, and a heap of bric-a-brac and curios on the inside, softened the gauntness and bareness of this architecture, or rather, distracted attention from the skinny awkwardness of the design. [004-007]

## Historical Context

Gothic architecture developed in the Île-de-France around 1140, with the Abbey Church of Saint-Denis recognized as the first fully Gothic building. The style spread across Europe over the next three centuries, producing the great cathedrals of Chartres, Notre-Dame, Amiens, and Cologne.

The Gothic Revival began as an 18th-century antiquarian and literary interest. Horace Walpole's Strawberry Hill (1749) launched the domestic Gothic fashion in England. Mumford's verdict on Walpole is withering:

> What attracted Walpole to the Gothic style was little more than the phosphorescence of decay: he summoned up the ghosts of the Middle Ages but not the guilds; and instead of admiring the soundness of medieval workmanship, he admired only the irregularities and accidents of age. [004-005]

In America, the style arrived through pattern books and the work of architects like Alexander Jackson Davis and Andrew Jackson Downing, who promoted Gothic cottages as the appropriate style for picturesque rural settings.

> For a while it seemed as if the Gothic revival might give the prevailing cast to nineteenth-century building; for if this mode was adopted at first because it was picturesque and historic it was later advocated on engineering principles by Viollet-le-Duc. [004-019]

The symbols of the organic world pervaded the thought of this period: "biology was supplanting physics, and Gothic architecture was supposed peculiarly to be in the line of organic growth" [004-019]. This organic analogy — the idea that Gothic architecture grew naturally from medieval society like a plant from soil — lent the Revival intellectual respectability beyond mere fashion.

The Gothic revival competed with Greek Revival, Italianate, and other modes throughout the antebellum period. Washington Irving's Sunnyside and James Renwick's Smithsonian Institution Building (the "Castle") were prominent early American examples [004-022].

## Key Figures

- **Richard Upjohn** (1802–1878): English-born architect who designed Trinity Church, New York (1846), the most influential American Gothic Revival church. Upjohn brought a more archaeologically correct understanding of medieval precedent than earlier practitioners [004-028].
- **James Renwick Jr.** (1818–1895): Architect of St. Patrick's Cathedral, New York (1858–1879) and the Smithsonian "Castle" (1855). Renwick brought French Gothic training to American practice [004-028].
- **Alexander Jackson Davis** (1803–1892): Popularized Gothic Revival domestic architecture through pattern books and built works like Lyndhurst.

> There are a number of honorable exceptions to this rule, for architects like Renwick, who designed St. Patrick's Cathedral, and Upjohn, who built Trinity Church, had a more sincere understanding of the medieval builder's aims and methods. [004-028]

## Key Examples

- **Trinity Church, New York** (Richard Upjohn, 1846) — considered the masterpiece of American Gothic Revival, with a 281-foot spire dominating lower Manhattan.
- **St. Patrick's Cathedral, New York** (James Renwick Jr., 1858–1879) — the largest Gothic Revival cathedral in the United States, modeled on French precedents.
- **Smithsonian Institution Building** (James Renwick Jr., 1855) — a castellated "Norman" Gothic design that established the Washington Mall's architectural character.
- **Lyndhurst** (Alexander Jackson Davis, 1838–1842) — a Hudson River Gothic Revival country house.

## In the Text

Mumford treats the Gothic Revival as an expression of the Romantic movement's reaction against classical rationalism, but ultimately as a failed attempt to recover organic community through architectural means. The movement's fundamental contradiction was its attempt to revive the forms of medieval architecture without the social and economic conditions that had produced them.

> To hold to Gothic precedent in the hope of recreating the medieval community is to hope that an ancient bottle will turn potassium permanganate into claret. [005-030]

Mumford's critique is that the Gothic Revival treated architecture as costume rather than as an organic expression of its time. The American version was particularly thin — a guide book of 1826 referred to a Masonic Hall "somewhat in the Gothic style"; and Mumford can "characterize all the buildings of the period by saying that they were 'somewhat' like architecture — a little more architecture than a mask on an empty face" [004-018].

> Unfortunately the process of disintegration had gone so far that no one current of thought had the power to dominate; and the Gothic style proved to be only the first of a number of discordant influences. [004-019]

## Decline and Legacy

The Gothic Revival declined after the Civil War as eclecticism and the Richardsonian Romanesque eclipsed it. H.H. Richardson began his career working in a "Free Gothic" mode — "he had come under the influence of Viollet-le-Duc; and for about ten years he struggled with incongruous forms and materials in the anomalous manner known as Free Gothic" [005-006] — before arriving at his mature Romanesque language.

> Richardson was not a decorator, but a builder: in going back to Romanesque precedent, with its round arches and massive stone members, he was following out a dictum of Viollet-le-Duc's: "only primitive sources furnish the energy for a real renovation." [005-006]

The style's lasting contribution lies in the institutional architecture of American churches and universities, where collegiate Gothic became the standard idiom for campuses like Princeton and Yale well into the 20th century.
