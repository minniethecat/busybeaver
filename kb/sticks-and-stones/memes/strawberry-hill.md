## Overview

Strawberry Hill occupies a pivotal position in Lewis Mumford's history of architectural decline. Horace Walpole's Gothic villa at Twickenham, begun in 1749 and expanded over the following decades, was the first domestic building in England to adopt the Gothic style not for structural or liturgical reasons but for its picturesque and associational qualities. It was, Mumford argues, the fountainhead of the [[picturesque-movement|Picturesque movement]] — and therefore, in a sense, the beginning of the end of architectural honesty.

Mumford's treatment of Strawberry Hill is characteristically compressed but devastating: "It is not a great step from building a ruin to building a mansion that is little better than a ruin" [003-017]. The line connects Walpole's villa to the entire tradition of Picturesque domestic architecture — the cottage orné, the Gothic cottage, the suburban medievalism that proliferated through England and America in the 19th century.

## Walpole's Defense

Walpole was not unaware of the criticism his house attracted. He defended Strawberry Hill, Mumford notes, "by saying he did not aim to make his house so Gothic as to exclude convenience" [003-017]. This defense, in Mumford's view, concedes the essential point: Walpole's Gothic was a matter of surface and association, not structural conviction. The pointed arches, the battlements, the fan-vaulted gallery — all were applied to a modern plan, a modern structure, and a modern way of living.

The contrast with genuine medieval Gothic is instructive. The medieval builder used the pointed arch, the flying buttress, and the ribbed vault because they solved structural problems — because they were the most efficient way to enclose space in stone. Walpole used them because they evoked a mood, because they conjured an atmosphere of chivalric romance and monastic contemplation. The difference is the difference between structure and scenery.

## The Gothic as Costume

Strawberry Hill established a pattern that would be repeated countless times in the architecture of the 19th century: the historical style worn as costume. The Gothic of Strawberry Hill was not the Gothic of [[chartres-cathedral|Chartres]] or [[notre-dame|Notre-Dame]] but the Gothic of the pattern book — a collection of motifs that could be applied to any building, regardless of its structure, site, or purpose.

Mumford's critique is not merely aesthetic but moral. Architecture, in his view, is a form of truth-telling: a building should express its structure, its materials, and its purpose honestly. Strawberry Hill, by this standard, was a lie — a modern house masquerading as a medieval abbey. The damage it did was not to architecture alone but to the entire culture of building, which it habituated to the acceptance of architectural falsehood.

## The Picturesque Legacy

The legacy of Strawberry Hill extended far beyond the Gothic Revival. The Picturesque habit of mind that Walpole exemplified — the preference for visual effect over structural honesty, for historical association over functional clarity — became, Mumford argues, the dominant mode of 19th-century domestic architecture. The suburban cottage, the collegiate Gothic campus, the rustic country house: all descend, directly or indirectly, from Walpole's villa at Twickenham.

> While Walpole defended Strawberry Hill by saying he did not aim to make his house so Gothic as to exclude convenience, it happened again and again that the picturesque was the enemy of simple honesty in design.

[003-017]

This is the heart of Mumford's case against the Picturesque. It was not merely a style among styles but a systematic deformation of architectural judgment — a training in the acceptance of appearance in place of reality.
