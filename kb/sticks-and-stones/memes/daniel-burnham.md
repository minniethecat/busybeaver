# Daniel Burnham

## Overview

Daniel Burnham (1846–1912) was the organizational genius of American architecture at the turn of the century — the figure who commanded the [[worlds-columbian-exposition|World's Columbian Exposition]] of 1893 and gave form to the commercial and civic ambitions of the Gilded Age. Mumford presents Burnham as a transitional figure: his early [[chicago-school-city|Chicago]] work belonged to the structurally honest tradition of the [[chicago-school-city|Chicago]] School, but the Exposition marked a turn toward academic classicism that would dominate American public architecture for a generation. His famous dictum — "Make no little plans" — captures both his ambition and, in Mumford's implicit critique, his failure to distinguish scale from quality.

> Messrs. McKim, White, Hunt, and Burnham divined that they were fated to serve Renaissance despots and emperors with more than Roman power, and unerringly they chose the proper form for their activities. Whereas Rome had cast its spell over the architects of the early Renaissance because they wished once more to enter into its life, the life of its sages and poets and artists, it attracted the architects of the White City because of its external features — because of its stereotyped canons and rules — because of the relatively small number of choices it offered for a lapse in taste — because of its skill in conspicuous waste, and because of that very noncommittal quality in its massive forms which permitted the basilica to become a church, or the temple to become a modern bank. [(006-008)]

## Biography

Burnham rose from draftsman to partner in Burnham & Root, one of [[chicago-school-city|Chicago]]'s most innovative firms. After John Root's early death in 1891, Burnham assumed sole leadership and was appointed Director of Works for the [[worlds-columbian-exposition|World's Columbian Exposition]]. His organizational skill in coordinating hundreds of architects, artists, and builders for the Fair established him as the preeminent architectural manager in America. He subsequently led major planning projects for [[washington-dc|Washington]], D.C., [[chicago-school-city|Chicago]], and Manila.

Mumford records that [[charles-mckim|McKim]], Burnham, Carrere, and Hastings were the "great professors of the classic style" — and notes that "the age shaped them and chose them and used them for its ends. Their mode of building was almost unescapably determined by the milieu in which they worked" [(006-018)].

## Architectural Philosophy

Burnham's architectural philosophy was pragmatic rather than theoretical. He believed in the power of large-scale organization to produce architectural results that no individual architect could achieve alone. Mumford's assessment is ambivalent: Burnham's Exposition demonstrated "the enterprise and capacity for organization which the architects of [[chicago-school-city|Chicago]] had applied to the construction of the skyscraper" [(006-007)], but it also turned American architecture decisively toward academic classicism and away from the structural honesty of the [[chicago-school-city|Chicago]] School.

> In creating this fair, the enterprise and capacity for organization which the architects of Chicago had applied to the construction of the skyscraper transformed the unkempt wilderness of Jackson Park into the Great White City in the space of two short years. Here the architects of the country, particularly of New York and Chicago, appeared for the first time as a united profession, or, to speak more accurately, as a college. [(006-007)]

Burnham's own view of the Fair — recorded by his biographers Charles Moore and Edward Bennett — was that "it is what the Romans would have wished to create in permanent form" [(006-031)]. Mumford's retort is sharp: "One may say of our imperial cities that they are what the Romans did create — but whether the form will be permanent or not is a matter we may leave to the sardonic attentions of history" [(006-031)].

## Influence and Legacy

Burnham's influence was organizational rather than formal. The [[city-beautiful|City Beautiful]] movement he helped inspire produced grand civic plans across America, though few were realized as completely as Burnham's own [[chicago-school-city|Chicago]] Plan of 1909. Mumford counts Burnham among the architects who "worshiped most wholeheartedly at the imperial shrine" and notes that, with [[charles-mckim|McKim]], "these men established the American Academy at Rome: they recognized their home" [(006-021)].

> In justice to the great professors of the classic style, Messrs. McKim and Burnham and Carrere and Hastings, one must admit that the age shaped them and chose them and used them for its ends. Their mode of building was almost unescapably determined by the milieu in which they worked. [(006-018)]

For Mumford, Burnham represents the moment when American architecture traded structural innovation for stylistic respectability — a bargain whose consequences would be felt for decades. The ephemeral nature of Burnham's own work underscores Mumford's critique, as demonstrated by the fate of his Illinois Trust and Savings Bank in [[chicago-school-city|Chicago]]. [[charles-mckim|McKim]] had predicted it "would long be a monument to his genius," but "unfortunately for Mr. [[charles-mckim|McKim]]'s reputation as a prophet, he was unappreciative of the rapid growth of [[chicago-school-city|Chicago]], the consequent appreciation in the value of real estate in the Loop district, and the expansive force of a great bank. This beautiful building is doomed to be replaced by one which will tower into the air" [(007-006)].

The [[city-beautiful|City Beautiful]], in Mumford's analysis, was a "municipal cosmetic" — an attempt "to seek a remedy on paper which could be purchased only by a thorough reorganization of the community's life" [(006-015)]. Burnham's genuine achievements in organization and large-scale planning were, in this view, in the service of a vision that mistook monumental facades for urban health.

## In Mumford's Text

Mumford discusses Burnham in Chapters VI and VII, where he is a central figure in the narrative of imperial architecture and its consequences. In Chapter VI, Burnham appears as the organizer of [[worlds-columbian-exposition|the White City]], the impresario who united the profession around a classical vision and thereby redirected the course of American architecture. Mumford's analysis is dialectical: he grants the organizational achievement while indicting its effects.

In Chapter VII, Burnham appears more obliquely — his buildings among those "designed for the most part by men who thought in terms of established architectural forms" [(007-020)], his Illinois Trust bank demolished by the very commercial forces his architecture sought to dignify. The Monadnock Building, designed with Root, stands as an exception — a building Mumford singles out for its influence on German modernists precisely because it "did not employ the [[steel-frame-construction|steel skeleton]]" [(007-020)].

Burnham's most quoted dictum does not appear in Mumford's text directly, but it haunts the margins of Mumford's critique: "Make no little plans; they have no magic to stir men's blood." The question Mumford implicitly poses is whether any plan, however grand, can compensate for architecture's severance from the life of the community.
