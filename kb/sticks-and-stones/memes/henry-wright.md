# Henry Wright

Henry Wright (1878–1936) appears in Lewis Mumford's *Sticks and Stones* as an architect-planner engaged with the practical application of regional planning and Garden City principles in America. His work with [[clarence-stein|Clarence Stein]] on planned communities represented one of the most significant American attempts to translate [[ebenezer-howard|Ebenezer Howard]]'s ideas into built form.

## Planned Communities

Wright's most notable contributions include Sunnyside Gardens in Queens, New York, and Radburn, New Jersey — the latter pioneering the "Radburn Plan" of separating pedestrian and vehicular circulation. These projects embodied the regional planning philosophy that Mumford championed throughout his career.

## Regional Planning Context

Wright's work intersected with the intellectual circle that included Mumford, [[benton-mackaye|Benton MacKaye]], and [[patrick-geddes|Patrick Geddes]]. Together they formed the Regional Planning Association of America, which sought to counter metropolitan sprawl through planned decentralization — a theme central to Mumford's critique of the [[machine-age|Machine Age]] city.

## Economic Analysis of Housing

Mumford cited Wright's economic calculations to illustrate the shifting cost structure of American housing:

> According to an estimate by Mr. Henry Wright in the *Journal of the American Institute of Architects*, the structure of the dwelling house represented over ninety percent of the cost in 1800. Throughout the century there was a slow, steady increase in the amount necessary for site, fixtures, and appliances, until, in 1900, the curve takes a sharp upward rise; with the result that in 1920 the cost of site and mechanical equipment has risen to almost one-half the total cost of the house. [007-012]

This economic analysis supported Mumford's argument that the industrial city had rendered the individual home an increasingly marginal element in the manufactured environment.

Wright further observed that site-costs were rising at such a rate that the house itself would eventually disappear into the pavement:

> Mr. Henry Wright has in fact humorously suggested that at the present increasing ratio of site-costs — roads, sewers, and so forth — to house-costs, the house itself will disappear in favor of the first item by 1970. [007-043]

This pointed critique underscored the absurdity of unplanned metropolitan expansion and the shifting economic balance between house and infrastructure.
