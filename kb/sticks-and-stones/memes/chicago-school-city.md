# Chicago

## Overview

Chicago is the city that Mumford presents as the architectural laboratory of industrial America. Its explosive growth after the Great Fire of 1871, its position as the hub of the nation's railroad network, and its concentration of capital and entrepreneurial energy made it the natural birthplace of the skyscraper and the Chicago School. "Hardly had the process of concentration and consolidation begun before the proper form manifested itself" [(006-007)] — the tall steel-frame office building that would transform the skyline of every American city.

## History

Chicago's architectural significance is inseparable from its economic geography. As the terminus of the transcontinental rail network and the processing center for the agricultural wealth of the Midwest, the city attracted [[washington-dc|the capital]] and talent that made architectural innovation possible. The great fire of 1871 cleared the downtown and created the opportunity for a building boom. By the 1880s, Chicago had become the most architecturally progressive city in America — the place where the elevator, the steel frame, and the speculative office building were brought together into a new building type.

## Architecture

The Chicago School is defined by its structural honesty. Architects like William Le Baron Jenney, John Root, and [[louis-sullivan|Louis Sullivan]] developed the steel-frame skyscraper — a building type in which the structure determined the form, with the historical styles reduced to surface treatment. The characteristic Chicago window — a large fixed central pane flanked by operable double-hung sash — maximized light and ventilation within the steel-frame grid. [[louis-sullivan|Sullivan]]'s work added a dimension of artistic ambition, seeking "a rule so broad as to admit of no exceptions" [(007-036)] that would unite structure, function, and ornament.

The [[worlds-columbian-exposition|World's Columbian Exposition]] of 1893 marked a turning point. The Fair's classical architecture, organized by [[daniel-burnham|Daniel Burnham]], demonstrated "the enterprise and capacity for organization which the architects of Chicago had applied to the construction of the skyscraper" [(006-007)] but channeled it toward academic classicism rather than structural innovation. In Mumford's account, the Fair both crowned and corrupted the Chicago School.

## Significance

Mumford treats Chicago as the architectural proving ground of industrial civilization. Here, more clearly than anywhere else, the forces that shaped modern building — the concentration of capital, the division of labor, the application of engineering to construction, the [[speculative-building|speculative development]] of urban land — produced their characteristic forms. The Chicago School demonstrated that the machine age could produce an architecture of genuine power; the [[worlds-columbian-exposition-1893|Columbian Exposition]] demonstrated that this power could be diverted into stylistic display.

## In the Text

Chicago appears most prominently in Chapters V and VI of Sticks and Stones. Mumford traces the arc from the post-Fire building boom through the development of the skyscraper to the [[worlds-columbian-exposition-1893|Columbian Exposition]], seeing in this trajectory the larger pattern of American architecture: structural innovation followed by stylistic retreat.
