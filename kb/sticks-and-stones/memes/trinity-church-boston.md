# Trinity Church (Boston)

## Overview

Trinity Church in Boston's Copley Square is [[henry-hobson-richardson|H. H. Richardson]]'s most famous work and, in Mumford's account, the building that proves his genius as a builder rather than merely a designer. "The proof of [[henry-hobson-richardson|Richardson]]'s genius as a builder lies in the difference between the accepted drawings for Trinity Church and the finished building. His ideas altered with the progress of the work, and in almost every case the building itself is a vast improvement over the paper design" [(005-007)]. The church established [[henry-hobson-richardson|Richardson]]'s national reputation and became the prototype for the [[romanesque-revival|Richardsonian Romanesque]] style that dominated American public architecture for a generation.

## History

Trinity Church was commissioned to replace an earlier church destroyed in the Great Boston Fire of 1872. The site, in the newly created Back Bay district, was a challenging irregular lot in what was then the western edge of the city. [[henry-hobson-richardson|Richardson]] won the commission in a limited competition and developed the design through multiple revisions as construction progressed. The building was consecrated in 1877 and immediately recognized as a landmark of American architecture.

## Architecture

The church is a massive cruciform building in rough-faced granite and brownstone, with a dominant central tower that anchors the composition. Its style — now known as [[romanesque-revival|Richardsonian Romanesque]] — adapts the heavy massing and round arches of Southern French Romanesque to the scale and materials of the American city. The interior is richly decorated with mural paintings by John La Farge and stained glass by La Farge and others, creating what Mumford might describe as a total architectural environment — building, painting, and sculpture integrated into a single work.

## Significance

Mumford uses Trinity Church as his prime exhibit for the argument that architecture is a living process, not a paper exercise. [[henry-hobson-richardson|Richardson]]'s willingness to revise and improve as the building took physical form — to treat construction as a continuation of design — represents the builder's tradition at its best. The church also demonstrates the potential of romanticism when guided by a master: "[[henry-hobson-richardson|Richardson]]'s brand of romanticism was a genuine attempt to embrace the age" [(005-008)], producing buildings that were historically informed but not historically trapped.

## In the Text

Mumford notes that "probably most people who know [[henry-hobson-richardson|Richardson]]'s name vaguely associate him with ecclesiastical work; but [[henry-hobson-richardson|Richardson]]'s long list of public works there are but five churches" [(005-008)]. Trinity Church is the most famous of those five — but Mumford's point is that [[henry-hobson-richardson|Richardson]] was not a church architect but an architect of the whole age, whose public buildings, libraries, and railroad stations were as significant as his churches. Trinity is the work that made his name, but it represents only one facet of his ambition.
