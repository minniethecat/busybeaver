# Washington D.C.

Washington, D.C. occupies a unique position in Mumford's architectural history: it is the American city that was designed, not merely accreted. Unlike [[new-york-city|New York City]], whose gridiron was imposed on [[new-york-city|Manhattan]] with speculative indifference, Washington received from its founding a plan of genuine spatial imagination — Major Pierre L'Enfant's grand vision of radiating avenues, ceremonial axes, and symbolic relationships between the institutions of government. For Mumford, Washington is the laboratory where the classical tradition in American architecture faced its most complete test, and where the [[beaux-arts|Beaux-Arts]] ideals of the [[ecole-des-beaux-arts|Ecole des Beaux-Arts]] found their most comprehensive expression. Yet the capital also illustrates, more clearly than any other American city, the abstractness and social limitation of purely formal planning — a framework "excellent, if cities could live by government alone."

## The L'Enfant Plan

### A Vision of Republican Grandeur

Major Pierre Charles L'Enfant, a French engineer who had served in the Continental Army, was commissioned by George Washington in 1791 to design the new federal capital. Mumford presents L'Enfant's plan as the culmination of the classical tradition in American city design — a direct descendant of the grand planning of Paris under Louis XIV, with "radiating avenues that cut through the city in the way that riding lanes cut through the hunting forest." By placing major public buildings in key positions, establishing proper physical relationships between departments of government, and planning spacious avenues culminating in squares, triangles, and round-points, L'Enfant "gave great dignity to the new capital city."

The plan was, in Mumford's view, the final flowering of classical spatial order — and simultaneously its "last gasp." Its ambition was to create a city that embodied republican ideals in monumental form, but the forces that would shape 19th-century American cities — commercial speculation, industrial expansion, the gridiron plan — had little use for L'Enfant's carefully orchestrated vistas. The plan was often ignored and overridden in the decades that followed, though its monumental framework persisted.

> "One further effect of the classic mode has still to be noted: the introduction of formal city design, by the French engineer, Major L'Enfant, in the laying out of Washington. Stirred by the memory of the grand design of Paris under Louis XIV, with its radiating avenues that cut through the city in the way that riding lanes cut through the hunting forest, L'Enfant sought to superimpose a dignified pattern upon the rectangular plan provided by the commissioners of Washington." [(003-043)]

### Features of the Plan

| Element | Description | Fate |
|---|---|---|
| Radiating Avenues | Diagonal avenues cutting across the orthogonal grid, modeled on Versailles and Haussmann's Paris | Partially realized; revived under McMillan Commission |
| Ceremonial Axes | Extended sightlines connecting key public buildings across the city | Realigned in 1901 to pass through Washington Monument |
| Squares and Round-Points | Intersections of avenues and grid streets designed as public gathering spaces | Many degraded or overrun by traffic |
| Key Building Sites | Capitol, President's House, and departmental buildings placed in commanding positions | Maintained; expanded through 19th and 20th centuries |
| Public Mall | Grand ceremonial green space extending from Capitol to Potomac | Restored and extended by McMillan Plan, 1901 |
| Federal-Only Focus | Plan addressed government buildings exclusively, omitting commerce and housing | DC developed organically around the federal core |

## The Capitol and Early Public Architecture

The United States Capitol, built and rebuilt by multiple hands over more than a century, is Mumford's primary example of the strengths and weaknesses of American public architecture in the classical mode. He notes that "in spite of the many hands that worked upon it the Capitol at Washington is still a fairly coherent structure," crediting the strength of the early republican architectural tradition. [[benjamin-latrobe|Benjamin Henry Latrobe]], the British-born architect whose contributions to the Capitol included a unique American order of corn stalks and tobacco leaves, represents for Mumford the creative adaptation of classical forms to American conditions — a genuine architectural inventiveness that the later [[beaux-arts|Beaux-Arts]] revivalists would sacrifice to academic correctness.

[[benjamin-latrobe|Latrobe]] is also a figure of symbolic importance: his disillusion with revolutionary idealism, recorded in his autobiography after "a dozen years' residence at the Republican court of Washington," parallels Mumford's own narrative of the decline from republican vigor to imperial formalism. The Capitol that emerged from successive reconstructions and expansions became, in Mumford's account, less a living building than a monument to accumulated official taste.

> "Benjamin Latrobe, the British architect who contributed so much to the Capitol at Washington — including a new order of corn stalks and tobacco leaves — was a witness to the disintegration of the age and the dissolution of its world of ideas." [(003-060)]

## The Abstractness of the Formal Plan

For all its spatial dignity, Washington's plan suffered from a fundamental limitation that Mumford identifies as characteristic of the classical tradition: it was conceived to serve government alone. "Contrived to set off and serve the buildings of the government, it exercised no control over domestic building, over business, over the manifold economic functions of the developing city." The L'Enfant plan was a diagram of power, not a framework for life. Its radiating avenues and ceremonial squares had nothing to say about housing, commerce, industry, or the everyday rhythms of urban existence.

This abstractness had consequences. The plan that was too pure for everyday life was also too formal for the merchants of [[new-york-city|New York]], who "could not understand L'Enfant's plan for Washington" and "seized upon that part of it which was intelligible: its regularity, its appearance of order." The American compromise, in Mumford's reading, was to borrow the rigid geometry of formal planning while abandoning its spatial imagination — producing not Washington's ceremonial order but [[new-york-city|New York]]'s speculative grid.

> "Unfortunately, if Washington has the coherence of a formal plan, it also has its abstractness: contrived to set off and serve the buildings of the government, it exercised no control over domestic building, over business, over the manifold economic functions of the developing city. The framework was excellent, if cities could live by government alone." [(003-044)]

## The McMillan Commission and the Imperial Revival

### Restoration of the L'Enfant Vision

The restoration of L'Enfant's plan, initiated in 1901 by the McMillan Commission (chaired by Senator James McMillan with [[daniel-burnham|Daniel Burnham]], [[charles-mckim|Charles McKim]], Frederick Law Olmsted Jr., and Augustus Saint-Gaudens among its members), represents the moment when "the realities of the imperial regime at length caught up with the dreamer born out of his due time." The Commission extended the great axis through the Washington Monument, assigned the site for Henry Bacon's Lincoln Memorial, and established the template for a city of national temples — "the first of a whole series of temples devoted to the national deities."

The McMillan Plan gave Washington the coherent monumental core it had never quite achieved under L'Enfant's scattered vision. Union Station (1907), designed by [[daniel-burnham|Daniel Burnham]], anchored the city's railroad gateway in appropriate imperial grandeur. Yet Mumford's treatment of the revival is deeply critical. The Lincoln Memorial, though architecturally refined, struck him as a betrayal of Lincoln's "homespun and humane and humorous America." The temples of the imperial capital, he argues, were architecture of compensation — "grandiloquent stones for people who have been deprived of bread and sunlight and all that keeps man from becoming vile."

> "In the restoration of the original plan of Washington, which began in 1901, the axis of the plan was so altered as to make it pass through the Washington Monument; and at the same time the place of the Lincoln Memorial, designed by the late Mr. Henry Bacon, a pupil of Mr. McKim's, was assigned. This was the first of a whole series of temples devoted to the national deities." [(006-040)]

### The Architecture of Imperial Compensation

In his final assessment of Washington's imperial architecture, Mumford develops a broader thesis about the relationship between monuments and social conditions. The railroad stations, memorials, and civic buildings of the capital are, he argues, "an architecture of compensation: it provides grandiloquent stones for people who have been deprived of bread and sunlight and all that keeps man from becoming vile." Behind the monumental facades trudged a landless proletariat; beyond the capital's ceremonial core lay a countryside whose resources were drained and whose children were uprooted.

This critique extends to specific buildings. The Lincoln Memorial, Grant's Tomb (in [[new-york-city|New York]], but of the same spirit), and the McKinley Memorial at Niles, Ohio, all belong to the same imperial typology: temples in which "one feels not the living beauty of our American past, but the mortuary air of archaeology." The Temple of the Scottish Rite, designed by John Russell Pope, completes the imperial ensemble — a city of white classical forms that spoke the language of eternal order while the nation's social fabric unraveled.

> "For my own part, I think we have at last acquired a criterion which will enable us to sum up the architecture of the imperial age, and deal justly with these railroad stations and stadiums, these sewers and circuses, these aqueducts and parkways and grand avenues. Our imperial architecture is an architecture of compensation: it provides grandiloquent stones for people who have been deprived of bread and sunlight and all that keeps man from becoming vile." [(008-026)]
