# Fiske Kimball

Fiske Kimball (1888–1955) appears in Lewis Mumford's *Sticks and Stones* as a scholarly authority on American architectural history. Kimball's research on [[thomas-jefferson|Thomas Jefferson]]'s architecture and on the broader development of American building provided Mumford with historical documentation for his narrative of American architecture's evolution. Mumford drew upon Kimball's scholarship in two distinct contexts: as a source for understanding the Palladian tradition in colonial Virginia, and — with reservations — as a representative of the classicist revival position that Mumford himself contested.

## Architectural Scholarship

Kimball's most significant contributions include his studies of Jefferson's architectural work — particularly [[monticello|Monticello]] and the [[university-of-virginia|University of Virginia]] — and his broader survey *American Architecture* (1928). His scholarship helped establish the documentary foundation for understanding the transmission of European architectural ideas to America through figures like Jefferson and [[robert-adam|Robert Adam]].

## *Domestic Architecture of the American Colonies*

Mumford specifically cited Kimball's compendious study *Domestic Architecture of the American Colonies and of the Early Republic* as part of a new wave of rigorous, critical scholarship on colonial architecture.

> Ever since colonial architecture was reappreciated after the Civil War, a large amount of material has appeared on the early architecture of the colonies. Before 1900 the greater part of this was uncritical. Isham and Brown's work on the early architecture of Connecticut and Rhode Island made a new departure, which Messrs. Cousins and Riley's studies of the architecture of Salem and Philadelphia have carried on. Mr. Fiske Kimball's compendious study of the *Domestic Architecture of the Colonies and the Early Republic* brings together a large amount of authenticated data. [010-008]

Before 1900, the greater part of writing on early American architecture had been uncritical — appreciative but lacking in scholarly method. Kimball's work, along with that of scholars like Isham and Brown on the early architecture of Connecticut and Rhode Island, and Cousins and Riley's studies of Salem and Philadelphia, represented what Mumford saw as a necessary documentary turn. This emerging scholarly tradition provided the architectural history that complemented Mumford's own cultural interpretation of American building.

## Kimball on the Classical Tradition in Virginia

Mumford drew on Kimball's analysis of the Palladian villa tradition as it developed in the manors of Virginia and Maryland. In discussing the relationship between plantation society and classical architecture, Mumford quoted Kimball's observation that the country house provided a more natural setting for Palladian forms than the urban palace or church:

> "In the town palaces and churches," as Mr. Fiske Kimball justly says, "there was a strong contradiction between modern conditions and ancient forms, so that it was only in the country that Palladio's ideas of domestic architecture could come to a clear and successful expression." [003-005]

This insight supported Mumford's broader argument that the classical tradition in America was shaped by social and economic conditions — specifically, the land monopoly and slavery that characterized the plantation system, which Mumford compared to the conditions that enabled the Roman villa. Kimball's documentary research thus provided historical grounding for Mumford's cultural critique.

## Mumford's Disagreement with Kimball

Despite drawing on Kimball's scholarship, Mumford diverged sharply from his conclusions. Kimball was among those architectural historians who "urge the acceptance of the classic tradition in America as a foundation for a general modern style" [003-006] — a position Mumford explicitly rejected:

> For all its minor felicities, however, we must not make the mistake of the modern revivalists, like Mr. Fiske Kimball, who urge the acceptance of the classic tradition in America as a foundation for a general modern style. Form and function are too far divorced in the classic mode to permit the growth of an architecture which will proceed on all fours in houses and public buildings, and factories and barns. [003-006]

For Mumford, Kimball represented a scholarly tradition that documented the classical achievement in American architecture without sufficiently reckoning with its limitations. The classical mode, Mumford argued, "achieves order and dignity, not by composing differences, but by canceling them" [003-006] — its standards "do not inhere in the building, but are laid on outside of it" [003-006]. Where Kimball saw a tradition to be revived, Mumford saw a formal language that could not accommodate the diversity of modern building types.

## Role in Mumford's Narrative

In *Sticks and Stones*, Kimball represents the emerging discipline of American architectural history — a scholarly tradition that complemented Mumford's own more polemical and critical approach. Where Mumford offered sweeping cultural interpretation, Kimball and his contemporaries provided the archival groundwork. Their relationship illustrates a productive tension in architectural discourse: the documentarian who assembles the facts, and the critic who interprets their meaning for the present.
