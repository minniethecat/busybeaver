# New York & Boston Architecture

New York and Boston: two cities, two architectural cultures. In *Sticks and Stones*, Boston represents the cultivated tradition — Richardson's Trinity Church, the Back Bay brownstones, the institutional gravity of Harvard and MIT. New York represents commercial energy — the skyscraper race, the speculative grid, the restless pace of demolition and rebuilding. Together they frame the Eastern establishment against which the Chicago School and other regional movements defined themselves.

## New York City

New York's architecture is the architecture of capital and density. From the cast-iron fronts of SoHo to the Woolworth Building and the great skyscrapers of the early twentieth century, New York's builders pushed upward because they could not push outward. Mumford, a New Yorker himself, was keenly aware of the city's contradictions: its energy, its chaos, its occasional moments of monumental achievement amid the commercial racket.

## Boston & New England

Boston's architectural identity is bound up with history, education, and the Puritan legacy. The city's architectural contributions — Richardson's Romanesque, the Boston Public Library, the Back Bay — reflect a culture that valued permanence and propriety. New England's town commons, meeting houses, and mill buildings form a distinct regional tradition that Mumford treats with evident affection.

## New England Regional Tradition

The New England village — its green, its white church, its surrounding farms — is one of the enduring images of American settlement. Mumford saw in the New England town a model of regional order, a counterpoint to the metropolitan chaos of New York and Chicago. The colonial and Federal architecture of the Northeast provided the baseline from which all later American architecture departed.
