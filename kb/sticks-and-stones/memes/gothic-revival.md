# Gothic Revival

## Overview

The Gothic Revival was a major architectural movement of the 19th century in both Europe and America. Emerging first as a romantic and picturesque interest in medieval forms, it developed into one of the dominant styles of the Victorian era. In Lewis Mumford's account, the Gothic Revival represents a pivotal moment in American architectural history — an attempt to reclaim a pre-industrial building tradition in the face of rapid mechanization and speculative construction.

Mumford situates the Gothic Revival within the broader "romantic movement" of the early 19th century. The style's trajectory, from superficial picturesque decoration to a more earnest, theoretically grounded mode of building, parallels the maturation of American architecture from frontier pragmatism toward professional self-consciousness.

## Characteristics

Gothic Revival architecture is distinguished by several hallmark features:

- **Pointed arches**: The defining structural motif, replacing the round arch of classical and Romanesque traditions
- **Ribbed vaults and flying buttresses**: Adapted from medieval cathedrals, often more decorative than structural in American applications
- **Vertical emphasis**: Towers, spires, and pinnacles drawing the eye upward — symbolizing spiritual aspiration
- **Asymmetrical massing**: Irregular, picturesque silhouettes, particularly in domestic architecture
- **Tracery and crenellation**: Decorative window tracery; battlements and turrets that, as Mumford notes, "no one ever climbed" and "no one rose to defend" [(004-006)]

In its American incarnation, the Gothic Revival initially served as little more than applied decoration — what Mumford calls "Gothic touches about doors and the exterior of windows" [(004-007)] — before maturing into a genuinely structural approach in the hands of architects like Richard Upjohn and James Renwick Jr.

## Historical Context

The Gothic Revival's origins lie in 18th-century England. Horace Walpole's transformation of his country house, Strawberry Hill (begun 1749), launched the style as a self-conscious aesthetic choice. Mumford is pointed in his assessment of Walpole's motivations:

> What attracted Walpole to the Gothic style was little more than the phosphorescence of decay: he summoned up the ghosts of the Middle Ages but not the guilds; and instead of admiring the soundness of medieval masonry, those who followed directly in his path were affected rather by the spectacle of its dilapidation, so that the production of authentic ruins became one of the chief efforts of the eighteenth-century landscape gardener. [(004-005)]

The style crossed the Atlantic early in the 19th century. Mumford notes that by 1807, an architect had already "designed the chapel of St. Mary's Seminary in Baltimore, after the Gothic fashion" [(004-004)]. Over the following decades, the Gothic Revival became one of several competing revival styles that gave American cities what Mumford describes as "the aspect of a museum and the American countryside a touch of the picture-book" [(004-022)].

The movement was later reinforced by theoretical arguments. Augustus Pugin's *Contrasts* (1836) and [[john-ruskin|John Ruskin]]'s writings provided intellectual foundations, arguing that Gothic was "a natural and scientific mode of construction, that it stood for growth and function, as against the arbitrary character of the classic work" [(004-019)]. This organic rationale — drawing on the biological metaphors then supplanting Newtonian physics in intellectual life — elevated the Gothic Revival beyond mere antiquarianism.

## Key Examples

- **Strawberry Hill** (Twickenham, England, 1749–1776): The prototype. Horace Walpole's "Gothic" mansion, which Mumford treats as the origin point of the romantic architectural movement.
- **[[trinity-church-boston|Trinity Church]]** ([[new-york-city|New York]], 1846): Richard Upjohn's masterpiece. Mumford singles out Upjohn and Renwick as a "saving remnant" who "had a more sincere understanding of the conventional task" [(004-028)].
- **St. Patrick's Cathedral** ([[new-york-city|New York]], 1858–1878): James Renwick Jr.'s French Gothic cathedral, the most ambitious American Gothic Revival project.
- **Smithsonian Institution Building** ([[washington-dc|Washington]], D.C., 1855): James Renwick Jr.'s "Castle" — one of the buildings Mumford lists as in "the predominant Gothic mode" [(004-022)].

## In the Text

Mumford's treatment of the Gothic Revival in *Sticks and Stones* is characteristically ambivalent. He recognizes the style's cultural significance while exposing its structural dishonesty. Chapter IV, "The Diaspora of the Pioneer," contains the most concentrated discussion. Mumford presents the Gothic Revival as fundamentally a reaction against industrialization:

> For a while it seemed as if the Gothic revival might give the prevailing cast to nineteenth-century building. [(004-019)]

But he is sharply critical of its superficial application. The style too often served as:

> a disguise for mean or thoughtless workmanship. [(004-007)]

A guidebook of 1826 described a Masonic Hall as "somewhat in the Gothic style," and Mumford seizes on this phrase: "we can characterize all the buildings of the period by saying that they were 'somewhat' like architecture — a little more than scenery, a little less than solids" [(004-018)]. This judgment — that the Gothic Revival was more scenography than substance — runs through Mumford's entire assessment.

The most successful practitioners — Upjohn with [[trinity-church-boston|Trinity Church]], Renwick with St. Patrick's — transcended the merely decorative approach. Mumford acknowledges them as an "honorable exception" to the general rule [(004-028)].

## Decline / Legacy

By the late 19th century, the Gothic Revival had fragmented into competing eclectic modes — "the rage for the Mansard roof, or the introduction of German Gothic, and, a little later, the taste for Queen Anne domesticity" [(005-011)]. Mumford sees this as the "dispersion of taste and the collapse of judgment which marked the Gilded Age" [(005-011)].

The style's ultimate failure, in Mumford's view, was its attempt to solve modern architectural problems by retreating into the past:

> To hold to Gothic precedent in the hope of recreating the medieval community is to hope that an ancient bottle will turn potassium permanganate into claret. [(005-030)]

Nevertheless, the Gothic Revival's legacy endures. It established the principle that architecture should be historically informed; it gave American cities some of their most recognizable landmarks; and it laid the groundwork for the more rigorous historicism of [[henry-hobson-richardson|Henry Hobson Richardson]] and the [[romanesque-revival|Romanesque Revival]]. In Mumford's narrative, the Gothic Revival is a necessary phase — a flawed but earnest attempt to recover meaning in building at a moment when industrialization had stripped architecture of both craft and conviction.
