# Colonial Architecture

## Overview

Colonial architecture refers to the building tradition of the American colonies from the earliest European settlements through the late 18th century. Mumford describes it as a [[vernacular-architecture|vernacular tradition]] shaped by carpenter-builders who worked with local materials and gradually incorporated classical decorative motifs at a provincial remove. "We have learned in recent years to appreciate the felicities of eighteenth-century colonial architecture, and even the earlier seventeenth-century style is now coming into its own" [(001-019)]. Yet Mumford warns against nostalgic revival: what made colonial architecture valuable was its embeddedness in a specific way of life — and that life is irrecoverable.

## Characteristics

The essential characteristic of colonial architecture is its whiteness. "Indeed, the whiteness of our colonial architecture is an essential characteristic; it dazzled Dickens on his first visit to America, and made him think that all the houses had been built only yesterday" [(002-021)]. Mumford provides an aesthetic rationale: "white and white alone fully reflects the surrounding lights; white and white alone gives a pure blue or lavender shadow" [(002-021)].

Structurally, colonial architecture was a wooden tradition. The carpenter-builder — not the professional architect — was its creator, working with framing techniques brought from England and adapted to American forests. Classical motifs appeared as surface decoration: fanlights, moldings, portico pillars — details that "served to fill the blank in provincial architecture" rather than determining the building's fundamental form.

## Historical Context

Colonial building emerged from two converging traditions: the medieval [[medieval-building-tradition|craft tradition]] of European village communities and the specific conditions of the American frontier. Mumford traces the carpenter-builder's lineage from the medieval guild system: the same collective craft knowledge that built the cathedrals, scaled down and adapted to the modest needs of colonial settlers.

The colonial period was, in Mumford's framing, a brief moment of balance between tradition and innovation, between European inheritance and American adaptation. It was "a happy accident that made the carpenter-builders and cabinet makers of America see their China, their Paris, their Rome through a distance, dimly" [(002-023)]. This distance was productive: it filtered out the excesses of European fashion while allowing the essential dignity of classical proportion to inform the everyday building of farmhouses, meeting houses, and town halls.

By the late 18th century, this balance was breaking down. The rise of professional architects, the increasing availability of pattern books, and the growing wealth and ambition of the planter and merchant classes pushed colonial building toward a more self-conscious classicism. "Once we have genuinely appreciated the influence that created early colonial architecture, we see that it is irrecoverable: what we call a revival is really a second burial" [(002-024)].

## Key Figures

- **Peter Harrison** (1716–1775): Self-taught architect whose work in Newport and Boston represented the most sophisticated colonial adaptation of English Palladianism.
- **Samuel McIntire** (1757–1811): Salem carver and builder whose Federal-style houses represent the late colonial transition toward professional architectural practice.
- **The anonymous carpenter-builder**: Mumford's true hero of the colonial period — the craftsman who "keeps on building... in the rural districts of New England, from Maine to Connecticut" well into the 19th century, maintaining the [[vernacular-architecture|vernacular tradition]] against the encroachments of academic fashion.

## Key Examples

- **Faneuil Hall**, Boston (1742): "Not the worst of Boston's buildings" in Mumford's appraisal — a public market and meeting hall that exemplifies colonial civic architecture at its most functional and dignified.
- **Mount Vernon**, Virginia (1757–1778): [[washington-dc|Washington]]'s plantation house, expanded over decades, showing the accumulation of colonial building wisdom without the imposition of a single academic design.
- **Independence Hall**, Philadelphia (1753): The most accomplished public building of the colonial period, combining Georgian dignity with the modesty and proportion characteristic of colonial work at its best.

## In the Text

Mumford's treatment of colonial architecture is affectionately critical. He values it as the last expression of an integrated building culture before the fragmentation introduced by the [[renaissance-architecture|Renaissance]]-architect, the machine, and the speculative builder. The colonial house, in his account, is architecture that still knew its place — literally and figuratively — in community, landscape, and tradition.

But he is equally clear about the impossibility of revival. The colonial way of building depended on specific economic and social conditions: the availability of timber, the modesty of the carpenter-builder's ambition, the absence of sharp class distinctions in the early settlements. "What those who admire the eighteenth century style do not, perhaps, see is that an accident cannot be recovered" [(002-023)]. The colonial architecture revival that began after the Civil War — and continues to the present day — is, in Mumford's view, a symptom of cultural nostalgia, not a genuine architectural tradition.

## Legacy

The legacy of colonial architecture is paradoxical. It is the most persistently imitated of American building traditions, reproduced in countless suburban developments and "colonial revival" houses. Yet Mumford would argue that these imitations precisely miss what made the original valuable: its unselfconsciousness, its adaptation to local conditions, its integration of building and life.

The deeper legacy is methodological. Colonial architecture demonstrates that good building does not require professional architects, elaborate theories, or expensive materials. It requires a living [[medieval-building-tradition|craft tradition]], a sense of proportion, and buildings designed for use rather than display. These are lessons that Mumford believes the modern movement, for all its technological sophistication, has yet to fully absorb.
