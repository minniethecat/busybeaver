# Architectural Competition

The architectural competition is among the most consequential institutions in modern architectural practice. Inherited from the [[ecole-des-beaux-arts|École des Beaux-Arts]], where students progressed through a graded sequence of competition exercises culminating in the Prix de Rome, the competition system became the default mechanism for awarding major civic and institutional commissions in America. Mumford engages with competitions throughout *Sticks and Stones* not as a singular topic but as a recurrent motif — a lens through which the tensions between professional culture, public taste, and commercial interest are brought into focus. The competition could elevate architecture to a matter of public deliberation, but it could also, Mumford suggests, reinforce a formalism disconnected from "the new materials, the new processes, the new functions and the new forms of community life" [(007-035)].

## The Beaux-Arts Competition Tradition

The competition was central to the pedagogical method of the [[ecole-des-beaux-arts|École des Beaux-Arts]]. Students competed in a hierarchy of exercises — from the *esquisse* (sketch problem) to the *grand projet* — developing mastery of classical composition before being permitted to design original work. The ultimate prize, the Grand Prix de Rome, carried a residency at the [[american-academy-in-rome|American Academy in Rome]] and the promise of a state career.

This system, transplanted to America through the [[ecole-des-beaux-arts|École]]-trained architects who returned after the Civil War — [[henry-hobson-richardson|Richardson]], Hunt, and later [[charles-mckim|McKim]] — shaped the new professional culture. The competition promised meritocracy in an era of patronage: a young architect could win a reputation without family connections. But it also, Mumford argues, institutionalized a particular kind of architectural thinking — one in which "the paper plan" [(005-017)] could supplant the craft of building.

## The 1879 Tenement-House Competition

One of the most sobering episodes in the history of American architectural competitions appears in Mumford's account of the Gilded Age. The model tenement-house competition of 1879, sponsored by the journal *Plumber and Sanitary Engineer*, invited designs for improved multiple-family housing in [[new-york-city|New York City]].

> To the disgrace of the architectural profession in America, the worst features of tenement-house construction were standardized in the so-called dumbbell tenement which won the first prize in the model tenement-house competition of 1879; and the tenements which were designed after this pattern in the succeeding years combined a maximum lack of privacy with a minimum of light and air.

[(005-027)]

The "dumbbell" tenement, with its pinched waist designed to admit minimal light and air to interior rooms, was thereafter reproduced thousands of times across the city. The episode revealed a structural failure: a competition intended to reform housing had instead codified its defects. "The gridiron street-design, the narrow frontage, the deep lot, all conspired to make good housing difficult" [(005-027)], and the competition system, far from overcoming these constraints, had dignified them with a prize.

## The Chicago Tribune Tower Competition (1922)

The most celebrated American architectural competition of the early twentieth century was held in 1922 for the new headquarters of the *[[chicago-school-city|Chicago]] Tribune*. Although it lies beyond the strict temporal bounds of Mumford's 1924 text, it appears in the closing chapter as a touchstone of [[the-machine-age|the machine age]]. The competition drew 263 entries from 23 countries, including designs by Walter Gropius, Adolf Loos, and Eliel Saarinen — whose second-place entry influenced American skyscraper design more profoundly than the winning Gothic tower by Raymond Hood and John Mead Howells.

The competition demonstrated both the promise and the peril of the open competition. It brought international [[modern-movement|modernism]] before an American public; it also showed that a corporate patron could use the competition to generate publicity while selecting a conservative design. Mumford's closing reflections on architecture's subordination to commercial values — on the way "the forms of business take precedence over the forms of humanism" [(007-008)] — resonate with the Tribune Tower's uneasy marriage of Gothic dress and steel frame.

The [[new-york-city|New York]] Public Library episode is especially telling. Mumford writes:

> Mr. Charles McKim, for example, indignantly withdrew from the competition over the New York Public Library because the demands of the librarian for a convenient and expeditious administration of his business interfered with the full-blown conception which Mr. McKim had in mind.

[(006-032)]

Here the competition's implicit promise — that it would select the design best suited to the institution's needs — was subordinated to the architect's formal ambition. The [[beaux-arts|Beaux-Arts]] method, "founded on the conviction that architecture was a learned discipline, not an intuitive art" [(007-035)], had produced a professional culture in which established canons could override the demands of function.

## Competition as Mechanism: Promise and Critique

Competitions served multiple roles in the architectural ecology Mumford describes. They were a means of public engagement, a platform for young talent, and a mechanism for selecting among competing visions for civic monuments. The [[worlds-columbian-exposition-1893|World's Columbian Exposition of 1893]] was itself a kind of meta-competition — a collaboration among competing firms orchestrated by [[daniel-burnham|Daniel Burnham]] into a unified whole.

Yet Mumford's judgment is tempered. The competition, like the exposition, tended to privilege "the mask" [(006-001)] over the substance of building. It rewarded the paper architecture of elevation and plan while slighting the real conditions of site, material, climate, and use. It belonged to an architectural culture in which, as Mumford writes of the imperial period's chief failing:

> the classic style served well enough only when the building to be erected had some direct relation to the needs and interests of the Roman world — the concourse of idlers in the baths or the tiers of spectators in the circuses and hippodromes. When it came face to face with our own day, it had but little to say.

[(006-033)]

The competition, like the architecture it produced, flourished when the problem resembled something the Romans had already solved. When it confronted the genuinely new — the skyscraper, the tenement, the factory — its record was more ambiguous.
