# Romanticism

## Overview

Romanticism was the dominant aesthetic orientation of 19th-century architecture, characterized by a turn toward the historical past for forms, motifs, and meanings. Mumford identifies romanticism and industrialism as "the two faces of the new civilization, one looking towards the past, and the other towards the future; one glorifying the new, the other clinging to the old" [(004-003)]. For architecture, romanticism provided a vocabulary of historical styles that served both as escape from and decoration for the realities of industrial production.

## Characteristics

The central mechanism of [[romantic-movement|architectural romanticism]] was the application of historical "style" as a surface treatment divorced from structure and function. Mumford defines this as romanticism's specific contribution: "As a disguise for mean or thoughtless workmanship, the application of antique 'style' was the romantic contribution to architecture; and it served very handily during the period of speculative building and selling that accompanied the growth of the new industrial towns" [(004-007)].

The romantic approach was fundamentally literary and associative rather than structural. Horace Walpole's Strawberry Hill exemplified the attitude: "What attracted Walpole to the Gothic style was little more than the phosphorescence of decay: he sought in architecture the same element that he prized in the novel — surprise and terror" [(004-005)]. Architecture became a vehicle for mood and historical allusion rather than a direct expression of construction and use.

## Historical Context

Romanticism emerged in the late 18th century as a reaction against Enlightenment rationalism. Mumford emphasizes that its appearance alongside industrialism was no accident: both were responses to the dissolution of the older, integrated civilization [(004-003)]. The "dream that is dying and the dream that is coming to birth do not stand in sequence, but mingle as do the images in a dissolving view" [(004-004)].

In America, romanticism arrived in the context of a landscape shaped by pioneer settlement and industrial exploitation. "The name pioneer has a romantic color; but in America the land pioneer mined the forests and the soil, and the industry pioneer almost as ruthlessly mined the human resources, and when the pay-dirt got sallow and thin, they both moved on" [(004-011)]. Romantic architecture provided a cultural counterweight — a decorative screen — for an increasingly unromantic reality.

By mid-century, the divisions within American culture were becoming evident. "By 1860 the halcyon day of American civilization was over; the spirit had lingered in letters and scholarship... but the sun had already sunk below the horizon, and what seemed a promise was in reality an afterglow" [(004-030)].

## Key Figures

- **Horace Walpole** (1717–1797): Author of The Castle of Otranto and builder of Strawberry Hill, whose "perverse and wayward interest in the past" exemplified the romantic attitude everywhere [(004-005)].
- **A. W. N. Pugin** (1812–1852): English architect who gave the Gothic revival a moral and religious foundation, arguing for the superiority of medieval Christian architecture.
- **[[john-ruskin|John Ruskin]]** (1819–1900): Critic whose writings on Venetian Gothic and the moral dimensions of architecture deeply influenced the romantic movement in both England and America.
- **Andrew Jackson Downing** (1815–1852): American landscape architect and writer who popularized the Gothic cottage and picturesque suburban ideal.

## Key Examples

- **Strawberry Hill**, Twickenham (1749–1776): Walpole's "Gothic" villa, the architectural manifesto of early romanticism, where historical forms were deployed for their picturesque and associative effects.
- **[[crystal-palace|The Crystal Palace]]**, London (1851): Represents "the extreme of industrial art" — a building of pure engineering that stood in stark contrast to romantic historicism [(004-025)].
- **Armsmear**, Hartford (1855–1862): Colonel Colt's mansion, which Mumford presents as the opposite of the [[crystal-palace|Crystal Palace]] — "untutored romanticism," a "lonely example of the Italian villa style" in an industrial setting [(004-025)].

## In the Text

Mumford's treatment of romanticism is fundamentally critical. He sees it not as a genuine creative movement but as a symptom of cultural disintegration. "From the standpoint of architecture, the early part of the nineteenth century was a period of disintegration. The gap between sheer utility and art, which the [[renaissance-architecture|Renaissance]] had emphasized, was widened with the coming of machinery" [(004-002)]. Romanticism papered over this gap with historical decoration rather than addressing it structurally.

The romantic impulse contributed to architecture primarily through the proliferation of revival styles — Greek, Gothic, Romanesque, Egyptian, Italian villa — each applied to building types for which they had no organic connection. "Even where style did not conceal the reality of the structure, it served to divert attention from the absence of any coherent plan" [(004-007)]. Buildings became stage sets, and architecture lost touch with both construction and community life.

Mumford's deeper critique is that romanticism represented a failure of nerve — a refusal to engage with the realities of industrial civilization in order to shape them. Instead of finding an architectural language for the machine age, the romantics retreated into historical fantasy. The task Mumford sets for [[modern-movement|modern architecture]] is precisely to overcome this breach: to create forms as honest and integrated as those of the medieval builders, but suited to the conditions and materials of the modern world.

## Decline / Legacy

The romantic impulse in architecture did not so much end as become institutionalized. The academic eclecticism of the late 19th century — the [[beaux-arts|Beaux-Arts]] system, the "Battle of the Styles" — was romanticism systematized into a curriculum. Even the modern movement, in its rejection of historical ornament, defined itself against the romantic legacy.

Yet romanticism left a permanent mark on architectural thought. Its insistence that buildings carry meaning beyond utility, that they connect occupants to history and nature, and that architecture has a spiritual dimension — these concerns, however badly served by the stylistic revivals, remain valid architectural questions. Mumford's own vision of an organic architecture that reconciles art and life is, in a sense, a transmuted romanticism — one that looks forward rather than backward.
