## Overview

Victor Branford was a British sociologist whose intellectual partnership with Patrick Geddes shaped the civic and regional framework that underlies Mumford's architectural thinking. Mumford acknowledges his "intellectual debt to Messrs. Victor Branford and Patrick Geddes" in the Acknowledgments to *Sticks and Stones*, and cites Branford's work throughout the book's sociological apparatus.

> My intellectual debt to Messrs. Victor Branford and Patrick Geddes will be apparent to those who have followed their work. [P03-001]

## The Geddes-Branford Intellectual Partnership

Branford and Geddes collaborated on a wide-ranging program of sociological and civic research centered on the concept of the "regional survey" — a comprehensive method of studying cities and regions through the integrated lenses of geography, history, economics, and social organization. Branford brought organizational and institutional acumen to Geddes's more visionary temperament. Together they edited the *Making of the Future* series, published through the Sociological Society and later Le Play House in London.

> For the civic and sociological background of this study, consult Professor Patrick Geddes's *Cities in Evolution*, London: 1915, likewise his *Principles of Sociology in Relation to Economics*. The latter can be obtained through Le Play House, 65 Belgrave Road, London, S. W. 1. The chapter on Westminster, by Mr. Victor Branford, in *Our Social Inheritance*, London: 1919, is a unique introduction to the direct study of social institutions and their architectural forms. The other volumes in *The Making of the Future* series, edited by Messrs. Geddes and Branford, should also have an important place on the student's shelf. [014-002]

## Branford's Specific Contribution: The Westminster Chapter

Mumford specifically recommends Branford's chapter on Westminster in *Our Social Inheritance* (1919) as "a unique introduction to the direct study of social institutions and their architectural forms." [014-002] This recommendation is significant: it reveals Mumford's conviction that architecture cannot be understood apart from the social institutions that produce it — a theme that runs through every chapter of *Sticks and Stones*. Branford's method of tracing the relationship between civic institutions and the buildings that house them is precisely the approach Mumford himself adopts.

## The "Miner vs. Peasant" Framework

In the body of *Sticks and Stones*, Mumford deploys a sociological framework derived from Geddes and Branford to characterize 19th-century industrial development:

> Both land pioneering and industrial pioneering were essentially subdivisions of one occupation, mining; and, following the clue opened by Messrs. Geddes and Branford, one may say with Professor Adshead that the nineteenth century witnessed "the great attack of the miner on the peasant." [004-019]

This framework — the "miner" representing extractive, temporary, exploitative modes of settlement, and the "peasant" representing rooted, cyclical, nurturing relationships with the land — becomes a recurring analytical device throughout *Sticks and Stones*, used to explain everything from the jerry-built houses of industrial towns to the ecological blindness of City Beautiful planning.
