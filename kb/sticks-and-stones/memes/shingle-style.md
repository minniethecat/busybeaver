## Overview

The Shingle Style was a short-lived but significant American architectural movement of the late 1870s and 1880s, characterized by the continuous wood shingle cladding of walls and roofs, open and informal floor plans, and a free adaptation of colonial and vernacular forms. The style emerged from the work of [[henry-hobson-richardson|Henry Hobson Richardson]] and [[stanford-white|Stanford White]], who developed it primarily for seaside and suburban estates along the New England coast.

Lewis Mumford's assessment of the Shingle Style is both admiring and elegiac. "By far the finest things in the late 'eighties," he writes, "are the shingled houses which Richardson and Stanford White and a few others developed for seaboard estates: they recovered the spirit of the early vernacular work, and continued the colonial tradition without even faintly recalling colonial forms" [005-026]. For Mumford, the Shingle Style represented a rare moment in late 19th-century American architecture when a genuinely organic building tradition seemed within reach — a moment that, tragically, "was scarcely sounded before it died out" [005-026].

## The Recovery of Vernacular Spirit

What distinguished the Shingle Style from the dominant eclecticism of the period was its relationship to the American building tradition. While the architects of the [[gothic-revival|Gothic Revival]] and the various Renaissance revivals imported their forms from Europe, the Shingle Style architects drew on the indigenous vocabulary of the colonial house — the gambrel roof, the continuous shingle surface, the asymmetrical massing — and developed these elements into a modern architectural language.

The shingled surface, Mumford notes, was particularly well-suited to the American landscape. Unlike the masonry walls of European tradition, which assert a clear boundary between building and nature, the weathered shingle blended the house into its surroundings — a visual continuity between the built and the natural that the early colonial builders had understood intuitively. Richardson's shingled houses, like the Stoughton House in Cambridge (1882–1883) or the numerous seaside cottages he designed for the Eastern elite, seemed to grow from their sites rather than to have been imposed upon them.

## A Brief Flowering

Mumford dates the Shingle Style's flourishing to a narrow window in the mid-1880s: between the opening of the Brooklyn Bridge in 1884 and Richardson's death in 1886. "There was a short period during which the echoes of Richardson's style resounded in the work of the Western architects," he writes, "and then in New York two of Richardson's own pupils, Messrs. McKim and White, who had caught the spirit of the period that was to follow the passing of the frontier, prepared an appropriate mold for its activities" [005-026].

The Shingle Style was thus bracketed, at one end, by Richardson's death and, at the other, by the rise of the [[imperial-facade|Imperial Façade]] — the classical revival that McKim, Mead & White would champion after 1893. "This new note, however, was scarcely sounded before it died out," Mumford laments, "and in the twenty years that followed the conflict between industrialism and romanticism was swallowed up and finally forgotten in the rise of a new mode. Richardson had not died too soon. The quality of mind and culture which shines through his work was opposed to nearly every manifestation of the period that succeeded him" [005-026].

## McKim, Mead & White and the Shift to Classicism

The career of [[mckim-mead-and-white|McKim, Mead & White]] illustrates the trajectory of the Shingle Style and its abandonment. The firm produced some of the finest shingled houses of the period — most notably the William G. Low House in Bristol, Rhode Island (1887), with its great triangular gable sweeping from ridge to ground. Yet the same architects who had mastered the shingled surface would, within a decade, become the principal exponents of the classical revival, designing buildings like the Boston Public Library (1888–1895) and Pennsylvania Station (1902–1910) in a Beaux-Arts idiom that owed nothing to the American vernacular.

For Mumford, this transition was not merely a change of style but a betrayal of architectural principle. The Shingle Style had promised an architecture that grew organically from American conditions — from the materials, climate, and building traditions of the Eastern seaboard. The classical revival, by contrast, imposed an imported vocabulary of columns, pediments, and domes that belonged, in Mumford's view, to an entirely different cultural and political order.

## The Legacy

Despite its brevity, the Shingle Style exerted a lasting influence on American architecture. Its principles — the open plan, the integration of interior and exterior space, the use of natural materials, and the free interpretation of vernacular forms — would resurface in the work of Frank Lloyd Wright and the Prairie School, and again in the post-World War II period, when architects like Charles Moore and Robert Venturi rediscovered the shingled surface as a medium of architectural expression.

Mumford's lament for the Shingle Style is, in the end, a lament for a road not taken: a modern American architecture that might have grown from native roots rather than being imported wholesale from Europe. "If we are to have a fine architecture," he writes, "we must begin at the other end from that where our sumptuously illustrated magazines on home-building and architecture begin — not with the building itself, but with the whole complex out of which architect, builder, and patron spring, and into which the finished building, whether it be a cottage or a skyscraper, is set" [008-048]. The Shingle Style, in its brief moment, had glimpsed what such an architecture might look like.
