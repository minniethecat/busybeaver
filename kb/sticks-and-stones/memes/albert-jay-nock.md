## Overview

Albert Jay Nock was an American essayist, editor, and social critic whose journal *The Freeman* (1920–1924) served as the original publishing venue for the early chapters of *Sticks and Stones*. Mumford acknowledges Nock's role in the Acknowledgments: without his "persistent encouragement and kindly interest," the book "would not have been put together."

> This book would not have been put together but for the persistent encouragement and kindly interest of Mr. Albert Jay Nock: and it was in *The Freeman* that the first five chapters, in somewhat briefer form, appeared. [P03-001]

## The Freeman and Its Significance

*The Freeman*, which Nock edited from 1920 to 1924, was a weekly journal of politics, economics, and the arts that became an unlikely home for serious architectural criticism. Under Nock's editorship, the journal published work by a remarkable constellation of American intellectuals, including Van Wyck Brooks, Walter Lippmann, and the young Lewis Mumford. The first five chapters of what would become *Sticks and Stones* — covering the Medieval Tradition through the Diaspora of the Pioneer — first reached readers through *The Freeman*'s pages.

Nock's willingness to publish extended architectural analysis in a general-interest journal reflects an editorial philosophy that saw culture and building as inseparable from political and economic life — a view Mumford shared. This publishing history is significant: *Sticks and Stones* began not as an academic monograph but as public intellectual journalism aimed at a broad, educated readership.

## Relationship with Mumford

The exact nature of Nock's editorial influence on the *Sticks and Stones* material is not detailed by Mumford beyond the Acknowledgments. However, the context is suggestive. Nock was a thoroughgoing individualist and critic of state power, whose own major works (*Our Enemy the State*, *Memoirs of a Superfluous Man*) articulated a philosophy of radical personal independence. Mumford's architectural argument — which consistently links the quality of building to the health of community institutions and the distribution of political power — may have found a sympathetic if intellectually distinct audience in Nock.

Mumford's inclusion of Nock alongside Patrick Geddes and Victor Branford in the Acknowledgments points to the eclectic intellectual network that fed into *Sticks and Stones*: libertarian journalism, Scottish civic sociology, regional planning, and architectural practice all contributed to the book's distinctive synthesis.
