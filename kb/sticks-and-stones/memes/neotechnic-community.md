## Overview

The neotechnic community is a concept coined by the Scottish biologist and sociologist [[patrick-geddes|Patrick Geddes]], and adopted by Lewis Mumford in *Sticks and Stones*, to describe the emerging technological and social order made possible by electricity — as opposed to the paleotechnic order of coal and steam. Where the paleotechnic era concentrated production in dark, congested industrial cities, the neotechnic era promised to decentralize it: electric power and motor transport could distribute manufacturing across the countryside, restoring the small workshop, the regional economy, and the human scale of community life.

## Core Ideas

Geddes's neotechnic thesis rests on a simple but far-reaching technological observation: steam power is centripetal — it demands concentration. Coal must be brought to the factory; workers must cluster around the factory; the factory must be near its market. Electricity, by contrast, is centrifugal: it can be transmitted over distance and tapped at any scale. A single electric motor can power a small workshop in a village as efficiently as a thousand motors can power a factory in a city.

Mumford elaborates the architectural and social implications:

> Professor Patrick Geddes has characterized the transition from steam to electricity as one from the paleotechnic to the neotechnic order; and intuitive technological geniuses, like Mr. Henry Ford, have been quick to see the possibilities of little factories set in the midst of the countryside. [(008-034)]

The neotechnic community, in this vision, is not anti-technological — it is *differently* technological. It replaces the dark Satanic mill with the sunlit workshop; the congested industrial district with the dispersed network of small producers; the anonymous mass market with direct, local relationships between maker and user.

## Technological Basis

Three interrelated technologies underpin the neotechnic vision:

1. **Electricity**: Unlike steam, electric power is clean, silent, divisible, and transmissible. It eliminates the need for the centralized factory and the industrial slum that grew around it.

2. **The internal combustion engine (and motor transport)**: The gasoline engine and later the automobile and truck reduced the advantages of urban concentration, making it possible to move goods and people efficiently between dispersed sites.

3. **The electric motor**: In industries such as wood-turning and furniture-making, the electric motor "placed the individual worker on a par with the multiple-machine factory, even as motor transportation is reducing the advantages of the big city over the small town or village" [(008-034)].

Together, these technologies created the material conditions for a re-scattering of production — and with it, a potential revival of the regionally-rooted [[medieval-building-tradition|craft tradition]] that industrialism had destroyed.

## Architectural and Urban Implications

Mumford saw the neotechnic order as having profound implications for architecture and [[town-planning|city planning]]. If production could be decentralized, so could population. The overcrowded metropolis — product of the paleotechnic era's centripetal logic — could give way to a network of smaller, more humane settlements, each integrated with its surrounding countryside.

This vision shaped Mumford's advocacy for:

- **Regional planning** (exemplified by Benton MacKaye's Appalachian Trail proposal, which linked recreation, reforestation, and electric power development into a single regional framework)
- **The garden city**, which combined the cultural advantages of urban life with direct access to nature
- **Decentralized building trades**, with local sawmills and workshops serving their immediate region

> With direct sale and service, from local sawmills and local furniture-making shops, the older handicrafts themselves might reenter once more through the back door — as indeed they have already begun to do in response to the demands of the wealthy. [(008-034)]

The neotechnic community thus promised to resolve the central conflict of *Sticks and Stones*: the tension between the industrialism that built the modern world and the craftsmanship that gave the medieval world its architectural integrity.

## In the Text

The neotechnic concept appears primarily in Chapter VIII ("Architecture and Civilization"), where Mumford moves from historical analysis to prescription. Having traced the degradation of architecture through industrialism and romanticism, he turns to the emerging technological possibilities that might support a renewal. The neotechnic community is his most hopeful vision: a future in which technology, rather than serving as an enemy of craft and community, becomes their ally.

The concept also connects Mumford to the broader regional planning movement of the 1920s — the [[regional-planning|Regional Planning]] Association of America, of which Mumford was a founding member, and which included MacKaye, Clarence Stein, and Henry [[frank-lloyd-wright|Wright]]. Their work on Sunnyside Gardens (Queens) and Radburn (New Jersey) was a direct attempt to realize neotechnic principles in built form.

## Legacy and Critique

The neotechnic vision has proven partially prescient and partially utopian. The internet, distributed renewable energy, and digital fabrication have revived many of its decentralizing hopes. But the actual trajectory of the twentieth century — suburban sprawl, automobile dominance, and the continued growth of megacities — followed a different path. The neotechnic community remains a compelling counterfactual: a road not taken, or perhaps a road still available.
