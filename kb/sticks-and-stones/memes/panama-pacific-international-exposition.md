## Overview

The Panama-Pacific International Exposition opened in San Francisco in 1915, celebrating the completion of the Panama Canal and showcasing American architectural achievement on the eve of the First World War. Coming two decades after the World's Columbian Exposition of 1893, the Panama-Pacific fair represented both the continuation and the twilight of the Beaux-Arts classical tradition that had dominated American civic architecture since the Chicago World's Fair.

## Historical Positioning

By 1915, the architectural momentum that had driven the Imperial Age was beginning to wane. Mumford notes that "since 1910 the momentum of the Imperial Age seems to have slackened a little: at any rate, in architecture it has lost much of the original energy which had been given to it by the success of the Chicago Exposition" (007-002). The Panama-Pacific Exposition, while still executed in the grand classical manner, opened at a moment when the assumptions underlying that style were increasingly questioned by advocates of a modern architectural idiom.

## Architectural Significance

The exposition's principal architects, including Bernard Maybeck, designed a temporary city of palaces around a central Court of the Universe. The fair's architecture drew on a eclectic mix of Spanish Colonial, Byzantine, and classical sources—a reflection of what Mumford described as the period's tendency to "add a new element" (007-003) to the architectural vocabulary without fully integrating it. The use of temporary staff construction, pioneered at the 1893 Chicago fair, reached its highest refinement at San Francisco.

## Legacy

The Panama-Pacific Exposition was the last of the great Beaux-Arts world's fairs in America. Within a decade, the architectural debates that had simmered since the 1890s would burst into open conflict between traditionalists and modernists. The fair's celebration of imperial classicism, achieved at enormous scale with temporary materials, exemplified both the ambitions and the contradictions of an architectural culture that Mumford characterized as favoring "fancy dress" over structural honesty (006-008).
