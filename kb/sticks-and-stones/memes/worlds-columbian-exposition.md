# World's Columbian Exposition

## Overview

The World's Columbian Exposition of 1893 was, in Mumford's account, the single most consequential architectural event in late 19th-century America. Under [[daniel-burnham|Daniel Burnham]]'s direction, the nation's leading architects created a temporary city of white classical buildings around a system of lagoons and canals — the "[[worlds-columbian-exposition-1893|White City]]" — that offered a compelling vision of urban order. "Messrs. [[charles-mckim|McKim]], White, Hunt, and [[daniel-burnham|Burnham]] divined that they were fated to serve [[renaissance-architecture|Renaissance]] despots and emperors with more than Roman power, and unerringly they chose the proper form for their activities" [(006-008)]. The Fair demonstrated American architectural capacity at its most ambitious — and, Mumford argues, its most misguided.

## History

The Exposition was organized to commemorate the 400th anniversary of Columbus's arrival in the Americas. [[chicago-school-city|Chicago]] won the competition to host it, and [[daniel-burnham|Burnham]] was appointed Director of Works. He assembled a team that included the country's leading architects — Richard Morris Hunt, [[charles-mckim|Charles McKim]], Stanford White, [[louis-sullivan|Louis Sullivan]] — as well as landscape architect Frederick Law Olmsted, who designed the site plan of lagoons and wooded islands. The Fair opened in May 1893 and attracted 27 million visitors before closing in October.

## Architecture

The Exposition's architecture was uniformly classical — a coordinated ensemble of white stucco buildings with colonnades, domes, and sculptural programs, arranged around the Court of Honor and its central basin. The effect was of a city designed as a single composition, with every building subordinated to the whole. [[louis-sullivan|Louis Sullivan]]'s Transportation Building was the notable exception — a polychromatic, richly ornamented design that stood out against the white classicism of its neighbors.

Mumford's analysis is pointed: "Behind the white staff façade of the World's Fair buildings was the steel and glass structure of the engineer: the building spoke one language and the construction another" [(006-009)]. The Fair perfected the art of architectural make-believe — a city of classical facades behind which the real processes of industrial America were hidden from view.

## Significance

Mumford treats the Exposition as a pivotal event, and his judgment is predominantly critical. The Fair demonstrated organizational genius and architectural skill, but it turned American architecture in the wrong direction. After 1893, the structural honesty of the [[chicago-school-city|Chicago]] School gave way to academic classicism; the skyscraper was dressed in Roman columns and [[renaissance-architecture|Renaissance]] domes; and the [[city-beautiful|City Beautiful]] movement substituted monumental planning for the social and economic reforms that Mumford believed were the real foundations of urban improvement.

The Exposition also marked [[henry-hobson-richardson|Richardson]]'s posthumous defeat. "[[henry-hobson-richardson|Richardson]]'s most intimate disciples reacted against the stamp of his personality" and embraced the academic correctness that the Fair represented [(006-003)]. The living tradition [[henry-hobson-richardson|Richardson]] had almost established was buried under a tide of white stucco.

## In the Text

Mumford discusses the Exposition most fully in Chapter VI, "The Imperial Facade." The title itself is his judgment: the Fair was the architectural expression of American imperial ambition, a facade behind which the realities of industrial exploitation, urban poverty, and environmental destruction continued unchecked.
