## Overview

The Stephen A. Schwarzman Building, the main branch of the New York Public Library, is a Beaux-Arts landmark on Fifth Avenue at 42nd Street in Manhattan. Designed by the firm Carrère and Hastings and opened in 1911, it is one of the most recognizable civic buildings in New York City, known for its grand marble façade, the iconic lion statues flanking its entrance, and its vast research collections.

## History

The library was formed through the consolidation of several existing libraries, including the Astor Library, the Lenox Library, and the Tilden Trust, in 1895. A design competition was held, and the Beaux-Arts firm Carrère and Hastings was selected. Notably, Charles Follen McKim had also been invited to compete but indignantly withdrew, objecting to the functional requirements set by the librarian. Construction proceeded from 1897 to 1911, with the building opening to the public on May 23, 1911.

## Architecture

The library is a quintessential example of the Beaux-Arts style in American civic architecture. Its Fifth Avenue façade features a triple-arched portico, Corinthian columns, and an attic story adorned with allegorical sculpture. The interior is organized around the principle of ease of circulation—a virtue Mumford concedes but considers misapplied to a library, where it increases noise and reduces reading-room space. The main reading room, with its coffered ceiling and monumental proportions, is one of the city's great interior spaces.

## In the Text

Mumford uses the New York Public Library as a case study in the failures of Beaux-Arts formalism. He writes:

> Indeed, so well does Roman architecture lend itself to the railroad station that one of the prime virtues of such a building, namely ease of circulation, was even communicated to the New York Public Library, where it is nothing but a nuisance, since it both increases the amount of noise and diminishes the amount of space for reading rooms that are already overcrowded. [(006-022)]

He further criticizes McKim's refusal to accommodate functional requirements, noting that "Mr. Charles McKim, for example, indignantly withdrew from the competition over the New York Public Library because the demands of the librarian for a convenient and expeditious administration of his business interfered with the full-blown conception which Mr. McKim had in mind" [(006-023)].
