# Romanesque Revival

The Romanesque Revival was a 19th-century architectural movement that drew inspiration from the round-arched, heavy-masonry church architecture of 11th- and 12th-century Europe. In its American incarnation, the style was so radically transformed by [[henry-hobson-richardson|Henry Hobson Richardson]] that it acquired a distinct name — Richardsonian Romanesque — and became, in Mumford's view, one of the few genuinely original contributions of 19th-century American architecture.

## Characteristics

Romanesque Revival architecture is defined by several distinctive features: **heavy, rock-faced stone masonry** laid in bold courses; **round arches** over windows and entryways, often deeply recessed; **short, stout columns** with richly carved capitals; **asymmetrical massing** with towers, turrets, and projecting bays; and a **polychromatic** use of contrasting stone colors. The style communicates solidity, permanence, and a connection to the earth — qualities that Mumford saw as a conscious antidote to the thinness of contemporary iron-front and cast-iron commercial architecture.

[[henry-hobson-richardson|Richardson]]'s version of the style departed from its European models in crucial ways. Where European Romanesque Revival often aimed at archaeological correctness, [[henry-hobson-richardson|Richardson]] used the Romanesque vocabulary as a point of departure for an architecture of broad, unified wall surfaces, powerful horizontal lines, and a sculptural handling of mass.

## Historical Context

The Romanesque Revival emerged in Germany and France in the 1840s as part of the broader historicist movement in 19th-century architecture. Its appeal lay in the perceived honesty and structural clarity of Romanesque construction, in contrast to the elaborate artifice of the [[gothic-revival|Gothic Revival]]. In America, the style arrived through immigrant architects and the influence of the German Rundbogenstil (round-arch style).

[[henry-hobson-richardson|Richardson]] encountered Romanesque architecture during his studies at the [[ecole-des-beaux-arts|École des Beaux-Arts]] in Paris, but it was not until his mature practice in the 1870s and 1880s that he transformed the style into a personal idiom. Mumford describes [[henry-hobson-richardson|Richardson]] as the pivotal figure who "began his career in America directly after the Civil War. Almost the first of the new generation of Americans to be trained by the [[ecole-des-beaux-arts|École]] des Beaux Arts, he brought back to America none of those atrocious adaptations of the French [[renaissance-architecture|Renaissance]]" [(005-006)].

## Key Figures

- **[[henry-hobson-richardson|H. H. Richardson]]** — The defining figure of American Romanesque Revival. His [[trinity-church-boston|Trinity Church]] in Boston (1877) and the Marshall Field Wholesale Store in [[chicago-school-city|Chicago]] (1887) are the canonical examples of Richardsonian Romanesque.
- **[[louis-sullivan|Louis Sullivan]]** — Briefly worked in [[henry-hobson-richardson|Richardson]]'s orbit; [[louis-sullivan|Sullivan]]'s early work shows [[henry-hobson-richardson|Richardson]]ian influence, though he would later move toward a more vertical, organic expression.
- **Richard Morris Hunt** — While primarily associated with [[beaux-arts|Beaux-Arts]] classicism, Hunt also produced Romanesque designs including the Lenox Library (1877).

## Key Examples

- **[[trinity-church-boston|Trinity Church]], Boston** (1877) — [[henry-hobson-richardson|Richardson]]'s masterpiece, with its bold central tower, polychromatic masonry, and richly decorated interior, established the Romanesque Revival as a major force in American architecture.
- **Marshall Field Wholesale Store, [[chicago-school-city|Chicago]]** (1887) — A commercial building of unprecedented power and simplicity. Mumford praises its "grandeur" achieved through plain wall surfaces and rhythmic window openings rather than applied ornament.

## In the Text

Mumford's discussion of the Romanesque Revival is embedded in his larger argument about the defeat of 19th-century [[romanticism|Romanticism]]. [[henry-hobson-richardson|Richardson]] represents, for Mumford, the moment when a revivalist impulse was transformed by an individual genius into something approaching an original style. The Romanesque Revival thus stands at a pivot point in Mumford's narrative — between the imitative historicism of the 19th century and the structural rationalism that would emerge in the [[chicago-school-city|Chicago]] School and ultimately in [[modern-movement|modern architecture]].

[[henry-hobson-richardson|Richardson]]'s Romanesque was distinguished by its "strength and vitality" [(005-008)] — qualities that Mumford saw as more than aesthetic. The bold massing and honest use of materials anticipated the functionalist emphasis of the coming Machine Age, even as the style's historical references rooted it in the Romantic tradition.

## Decline and Legacy

The Romanesque Revival declined rapidly after [[henry-hobson-richardson|Richardson]]'s death in 1886, displaced by the [[beaux-arts|Beaux-Arts]] classicism of the [[worlds-columbian-exposition|World's Columbian Exposition]] generation and the rise of the steel-frame skyscraper. Yet its influence persisted in subtler ways. The emphasis on unified mass, honest material expression, and the subordination of ornament to overall form — all [[henry-hobson-richardson|Richardson]]ian principles — passed into the DNA of American [[modern-movement|modernism]] through [[louis-sullivan|Louis Sullivan]] and, ultimately, [[frank-lloyd-wright|Frank Lloyd Wright]].
