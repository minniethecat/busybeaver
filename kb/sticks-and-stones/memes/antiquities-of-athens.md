## Overview

James Stuart and Nicholas Revett's *The Antiquities of Athens* was one of the most influential architectural publications of the 18th century. Published in multiple volumes between 1762 and 1816, its precisely measured drawings of Greek temples, monuments, and architectural details provided the empirical foundation for the [[greek-revival|Greek Revival]] throughout the English-speaking world. Lewis Mumford identifies it as a crucial link in the chain of classical transmission that shaped American architecture from the Revolution through the Civil War.

Mumford's treatment of the *Antiquities* is characteristically ambivalent. He acknowledges its scholarly importance — the measured drawings of Stuart and Revett were far more accurate than anything previously available — but he questions the architectural consequences of a tradition that increasingly derived its forms from printed sources rather than direct experience of building.

## The Book-Based Tradition

The *Antiquities of Athens* represents, in Mumford's genealogy, the moment when architectural classicism became irreversibly dependent on printed sources:

> First it is Palladio and Vignola; then it is Burlington and Chambers; then, after the middle of the eighteenth century, the brothers Adam and Stuart's *Antiquities of Athens*.

[002-013]

This chain of transmission is significant because each link moves further from the direct experience of building and closer to the authority of the printed page. Palladio had studied Roman ruins directly; Burlington had studied Palladio's books; the Adam brothers and Stuart had studied books and ruins. The American architect working from the *Antiquities of Athens* was three or four removes from the original source.

Mumford's point is not that the *Antiquities* was inaccurate — on the contrary, its measured drawings were the most precise of their time. His point is that an architecture based on books, however accurate the books, is fundamentally different from an architecture based on direct experience of materials, climate, and the act of building.

## Influence on American Architecture

The *Antiquities of Athens* had a direct and profound influence on the architecture of the early American Republic:

> The dominant designs of the early republican period proceeded directly or indirectly from such books as Stuart's *Antiquities of Athens*, and from such well-known examples of temple architecture in southern Europe as the Maison Carrée at Nîmes.

[003-005]

The Greek temple form — the portico, the colonnade, the pediment — was adopted as the architectural expression of republican virtue. Thomas Jefferson's design for the Virginia State Capitol, modeled on the Maison Carrée, was the most famous American example, but the influence of the *Antiquities* extended to countless banks, courthouses, churches, and private houses across the young Republic.

The irony, as Mumford notes, is that the Greek temple was not a democratic building type but a religious one — the dwelling of a god, not a place of assembly for citizens. The adaptation of temple forms to secular purposes required a considerable act of architectural translation, and the results, in his view, were mixed.

## The Limits of Antiquarianism

Mumford's larger critique of the *Antiquities of Athens* is part of his critique of architectural antiquarianism in general. The careful study of historical precedent, valuable as it was for scholarship, could not substitute for the living tradition of craftsmanship that the Renaissance had displaced. The architect who knew his Stuart and Revett but did not know his materials, his climate, and his community was, in Mumford's view, an incomplete architect.

The *Antiquities of Athens* was, in this sense, both a scholarly monument and a symptom of the condition it documented: the transformation of architecture from a living practice into a literate discipline, from the work of the hand into the work of the library.
