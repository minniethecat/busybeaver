# Baroque Architecture

## Overview

Baroque architecture emerged in late 16th-century Rome as an expression of the Counter-Reformation Church's desire to dramatize the power and glory of Catholicism through spatial and ornamental means. Characterized by curved forms, dynamic spatial sequences, and the integration of painting, sculpture, and architecture into a unified theatrical whole, the Baroque departed from the static harmony of the Renaissance in favor of movement, emotion, and grandeur. Lewis Mumford discusses the Baroque both as a stylistic system and as a reference point for understanding the classical vocabulary deployed at events like the 1893 World's Columbian Exposition.

## Characteristics

Baroque architecture is distinguished by several interrelated formal strategies:

- **Curvilinear forms**: Oval plans, undulating facades, and broken pediments replace the static circle and square of Renaissance composition.
- **Spatial drama**: Interlocking volumes, forced perspective, and light manipulated for theatrical effect create a sense of movement through space.
- **Synthesis of the arts**: Painting, sculpture, and architecture merge — frescoed ceilings dissolve into painted heavens, sculptural figures erupt from architectural frames.
- **The colossal order**: Giant columns or pilasters spanning multiple stories, unifying the facade into a single vertical sweep.
- **Rich ornamentation**: Abundant gilding, colored marbles, and sculptural detail contribute to an aesthetic of abundance and splendor.
- **Centralized planning**: Oval or complex centralized plans with radiating chapels replace the simple Latin cross.

The Baroque represented a radical break from the "grammatical accuracy" that had come to define late Renaissance classicism [002-012].

## Historical Context

The Baroque was born in Counter-Reformation Rome, where the Catholic Church commissioned ambitious building projects to reaffirm its spiritual authority in the wake of the Protestant Reformation. The completion of St. Peter's Basilica — with Gian Lorenzo Bernini's great piazza (1656–1667) and Baldacchino (1624–1633) — established the Baroque as the official style of triumphant Catholicism.

From Rome, the Baroque spread across Europe in distinct national variants: the austere French Baroque of Versailles under Louis XIV, the inventive Central European Baroque of the Habsburg domains, the restrained English Baroque of Christopher Wren and Nicholas Hawksmoor, and the colonial Baroque of Latin America, where indigenous craftsmen elaborated European models into the most ornate version of the style.

> Architecture, instead of striving to leave the imprint of a happy spirit on the superficies of a building, became a mere matter of grammatical accuracy and pronunciation. [002-012]

The Baroque's influence extended into the 20th century through the Beaux-Arts system, which transmitted Baroque planning principles — particularly the manipulation of axial vistas and monumental scale — to American architects trained at the École des Beaux-Arts in Paris.

## Key Figures

- **Gian Lorenzo Bernini** (1598–1680): The dominant figure of Roman Baroque, architect of St. Peter's piazza and Baldacchino, and the quintessential master of Baroque synthesis.
- **Francesco Borromini** (1599–1667): Bernini's rival, whose wildly inventive geometries at San Carlo alle Quattro Fontane pushed Baroque spatial manipulation to its extreme.
- **Christopher Wren** (1632–1723): English architect who brought a restrained, scientifically-informed Baroque to St. Paul's Cathedral and the London city churches after the Great Fire of 1666.

## Key Examples

- **St. Peter's Basilica and Piazza** (Bernini, 1656–1667) — the defining Baroque ensemble, where the embracing colonnades dramatize the church's universal embrace.
- **Palace of Versailles** (Louis Le Vau, Jules Hardouin-Mansart, 1661–1710) — the absolutist Baroque of France, where architecture, gardens, and ceremony combine into a total work of power.
- **San Carlo alle Quattro Fontane, Rome** (Borromini, 1638–1641) — a small church whose undulating facade and oval dome represent Baroque spatial ingenuity at its most intense.
- **St. Paul's Cathedral, London** (Wren, 1675–1710) — the restrained English Baroque, combining a classical temple front with a dome that pays homage to St. Peter's.

## In the Text

Mumford references the Baroque in two significant contexts. First, he uses Geoffrey Scott's *The Architecture of Humanism* (1914) — a famous defense of Baroque aesthetics — to characterize the classical architecture of the World's Columbian Exposition of 1893.

> Mr. Geoffrey Scott's defense of the Baroque, in *The Architecture of Humanism*, applies particularly to its essential vitality and the happy play of forms. [006-010]

In Scott's analysis, the Baroque is not decadent excess but the fulfillment of classical architecture's humanistic potential — architecture that engages the body and the emotions, not merely the intellect.

Second, Mumford notes the colonial Baroque as an enriching influence on American architectural eclecticism, alongside Byzantine and Syrian precedents.

> So the baroque architecture of Spain, which flourished so well in Mexico, and the ecclesiastical architecture of Byzantium and Syria, have added a new charm to our motley wardrobe: from the first came the mission churches of the Southwest, from the second the onion domes and polychrome brickwork of the Greek and Russian Orthodox churches. [007-003]

## Decline and Legacy

The Baroque declined in the mid-18th century as the rational spirit of the Enlightenment favored the more restrained and archaeologically correct Neoclassicism. The term "baroque" itself was originally pejorative — derived from the Portuguese *barroco* (an irregular pearl), suggesting excessive ornament and lack of discipline.

However, the Baroque's spatial principles — axial planning, dramatic sightlines, monumental scale — were absorbed into the Beaux-Arts tradition and re-emerged in the City Beautiful movement. At the 1893 Columbian Exposition, "these classic buildings were all that could be demanded" [006-010], their Baroque-derived grandeur providing precisely the spectacle of civic order that the White City promised.

The planning of Washington, D.C. under the McMillan Commission, the civic center ensembles of San Francisco and Denver, and the axial boulevards of Burnham's 1909 Plan of Chicago all bear the imprint of Baroque urbanism — the conviction that the city should be composed as a work of art.
