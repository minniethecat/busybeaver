# École des Beaux-Arts

The École des Beaux-Arts in Paris was, from the late seventeenth century through the early twentieth century, the most influential architectural school in the Western world. Its atelier system — in which students worked under practicing architects and competed for the prestigious Prix de Rome — set the standard for architectural education. For American architecture, the École was the primary conduit through which French classical discipline reached the generation that would define the Gilded Age and the imperial period. Mumford returns to the École throughout *Sticks and Stones* as both a source of professional competence and a vector of academic constraint: the institution that equipped [[henry-hobson-richardson|H. H. Richardson]] with discipline while arming [[daniel-burnham|Daniel Burnham]] and the architects of the [[worlds-columbian-exposition-1893|World's Columbian Exposition]] with a "Roman litany" of forms.

## History

The École was founded in 1671 as the Académie Royale d'Architecture under Jean-Baptiste Colbert, Louis XIV's minister. After suppression during the Revolution, it was reorganized as the École des Beaux-Arts in 1819, absorbing the Académie's traditions of classical instruction and competition. Its curriculum emphasized composition, the study of historical precedents, and the integration of painting and sculpture with architecture. Admission was fiercely competitive, governed by the *concours* (competition) system that culminated in the Grand Prix de Rome. The school's graduates dominated French and international architecture for two centuries.

For Americans, the École became accessible after the Civil War, when **Richard Morris Hunt** (1827–1895) became the first American admitted to its ateliers. Mumford acknowledges Hunt as the pioneer who "blazed" the trail for his countrymen [(006-003)]; his most visible work — the Administration Building of the [[worlds-columbian-exposition|World's Columbian Exposition]] — represented the full flowering of [[beaux-arts|Beaux-Arts]] ambition on American soil. A generation followed.

The most original of the École-trained Americans was **[[henry-hobson-richardson|Henry Hobson Richardson]]** (1838–1886). Mumford writes that "[[henry-hobson-richardson|Richardson]] began his career in America directly after the Civil War. Almost the first of the new generation of Americans to be trained by the École des Beaux Arts, he brought back to America none of those atrocious adaptations of the French [[renaissance-architecture|Renaissance]]" [(005-006)]. [[henry-hobson-richardson|Richardson]] absorbed the École's compositional discipline without adopting its formal vocabulary. His Romanesque architecture — [[trinity-church-boston|Trinity Church]] in Boston, the Marshall Field Building in [[chicago-school-city|Chicago]], the Pittsburgh Court House — represented a romanticism that the École's classicism would eventually displace. He "left scarcely a trace upon the period that followed" [(005-017)].

The most classically orthodox was **[[charles-mckim|Charles Follen McKim]]** (1847–1909), whose work — the Boston Public Library, the restoration of L'Enfant's plan for [[washington-dc|Washington, D.C.]], [[pennsylvania-station|Pennsylvania Station]] in [[new-york-city|New York]] — embodied the "imperial" mode that Mumford anatomizes in Chapter VI. [[charles-mckim|McKim]] was also instrumental in founding the [[american-academy-in-rome|American Academy in Rome]] in 1894, extending the [[beaux-arts|Beaux-Arts]] lineage across the Atlantic.

In dialectical contrast, **[[louis-sullivan|Louis Sullivan]]** (1856–1924) studied briefly at the École before developing an architectural language that rejected its academic premises. [[louis-sullivan|Sullivan]]'s Transportation Building at the [[worlds-columbian-exposition|World's Columbian Exposition]] — its polychromatic "Golden Door" — was the lone dissent amid [[worlds-columbian-exposition|the White City]]'s "Roman litany" [(006-012)]. [[louis-sullivan|Sullivan]] later complained, in *The Autobiography of an Idea*, that "imperialism stifled the more creative modes of architecture which might have derived from our fine achievements in science, from our tentative experiments in democracy" [(006-035)].

## The Beaux-Arts Method and Its American Legacy

The [[beaux-arts|Beaux-Arts]] pedagogical method shaped not only individual architects but the institutional structure of American practice. Its core elements — the atelier, the *esquisse*, the graded competition — were transplanted to American architecture schools and remain embedded in studio culture today.

The method was founded on the conviction that architecture was a learned discipline. Students progressed through a sequence of competitions at increasing scales, mastering classical composition before being permitted to design original work. The system produced architects of extraordinary technical competence. It also, Mumford argues, produced a tendency toward academic formalism ill-suited to the new conditions of the industrial age.

| [[beaux-arts|Beaux-Arts]] Element | Description | American Legacy |
|-------------------|-------------|-----------------|
| Atelier system | Students apprenticed to practicing architects | The architecture studio (design jury, desk crit) |
| Esquisse | Rapid sketch problem, fixed time limit | Design charrette, competition brief |
| Prix de Rome | Grand prize: residency at Villa Medici, Rome | [[american-academy-in-rome|American Academy in Rome]] (founded 1894) |
| Concours | Sequential competitive examinations | The [[architectural-competition]] system for civic commissions |
| Classical canon | Study of orders, proportion, historical precedent | Persistence of classical vocabulary in institutional architecture |

The limitation of this inheritance was its inability to grapple with genuinely new conditions. Mumford's verdict is precise:

> The Beaux-Arts method was founded on the conviction that architecture was a learned discipline, not an intuitive art. Students progressed through a sequence of competitions at increasing scales, mastering classical composition before being permitted to design original work. The method produced architects of extraordinary technical competence and compositional skill — but also, as Mumford argues, a tendency toward academic formalism that was ill-suited to the new materials, the new processes, the new functions and the new forms of community life that had been created everywhere outside the schools.

[(007-035)]

## The Critique: Formalism and the Imperial Façade

Mumford's treatment of the [[beaux-arts|Beaux-Arts]] legacy is dialectical. He credits the École with raising the technical standard of American practice. But he sees its influence as inseparable from the imperial culture it served.

The generation of students who studied at the École after the Civil War "was ready, at last, to follow the lone trail which Richard H. Hunt had blazed" [(006-003)]. They brought back a discipline of composition and a canon of taste. But they also brought back something Mumford calls more troubling: a readiness to treat architecture as a mask. The [[worlds-columbian-exposition|World's Columbian Exposition]], in which [[charles-mckim|McKim]], Hunt, and their colleagues achieved "the effects of unity by subordinating their work to an established precedent" — they "chanted a Roman litany above the Babel of individual styles" [(006-012)] — was the [[beaux-arts|Beaux-Arts]] method's apotheosis and its reductio ad absurdum.

> Behind the white staff façade of the World's Fair buildings was the steel and glass structure of the engineer: the building spoke one language and the "architecture" another. If the coming of the skyscraper had turned masonry into veneer, here was a mode of architecture which was little but veneer.

[(006-014)]

The École's formalism, in other words, met its limit in the [[skyscraper|steel-frame building]]. The classical orders that had been the substance of [[beaux-arts|Beaux-Arts]] training had become, under American conditions, an applied surface. The discipline of "the paper plan" [(005-017)] had triumphed over the craft of building.

Yet the [[beaux-arts|Beaux-Arts]] system's influence on architectural education persists — in the studio culture, the design jury, and the emphasis on drawing and composition that characterize most architecture schools today. Even the [[modern-movement|modern movement]], which defined itself in opposition to academic classicism, inherited the atelier structure and the competition mentality. The École's deepest legacy may be institutional rather than stylistic: it created the professional culture within which subsequent generations of architects, including its severest critics, would work.
