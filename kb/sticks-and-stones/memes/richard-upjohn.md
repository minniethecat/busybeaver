# Richard Upjohn

**Richard Upjohn** (January 22, 1802 – August 16, 1878) was an English-born American architect best known for his Gothic Revival churches. His most celebrated work, Trinity Church in New York City (1846), established him as the leading ecclesiastical architect of mid-nineteenth-century America.

## In the Text

Mumford names Upjohn among the few nineteenth-century architects who approached the Gothic style with genuine understanding rather than superficial imitation:

> There are a number of honorable exceptions to this rule, for architects like Renwick, who designed St. Patrick's Cathedral, and Upjohn, who built Trinity Church, had a more sincere understanding of the conventional task; and by any standard of esthetic decency the old Gothic building of New York University, on Washington Square, was a far finer structure than the bulky office building that has taken its place. (004-028)

## Career

Born in England, Upjohn trained as a cabinetmaker before emigrating to the United States in 1829. He established his architectural practice in Boston and later moved to New York. Trinity Church, completed in 1846 at the head of Wall Street, became the iconic image of American Gothic Revival and cemented Upjohn's reputation.

## Architectural Approach

Unlike many contemporaries who applied Gothic ornament superficially, Upjohn studied medieval English parish churches and adapted their principles to American conditions. He was a founding member and first president of the American Institute of Architects (1857). His book *Upjohn's Rural Architecture* (1852) disseminated Gothic designs for small wooden churches across the United States, extending his influence far beyond his own built works.

## Upjohn and the Gothic Revival

Mumford's praise of Upjohn's "sincere understanding" carries its full weight only when measured against the broader Gothic Revival movement that Upjohn worked within. The revival had begun with Horace Walpole's Strawberry Hill, but what attracted Walpole and his followers "was little more than the phosphorescence of decay: he summoned up the ghosts of the Middle Ages but not the guilds" (004-005). The prevailing Gothic manner, Mumford observes, substituted "wall paper that imitated stucco" for honest construction, and the picturesque became "the enemy of simple honesty and necessity" (004-006).

It is against this backdrop of superficial medievalism that Upjohn's achievement must be assessed. His study of actual English parish churches — not merely their ornamental details but their structural logic and liturgical function — gave his work an authenticity that distinguished it from the "heap of bric-a-brac and curios" and "Gothic touches about doors and the exterior of windows" that characterized so much nineteenth-century building (004-007). Upjohn thus represented one path the Gothic Revival might have taken — one rooted in genuine understanding of medieval precedent — even as the broader movement splintered into the discordant eclecticism that Mumford catalogues throughout his architectural history.
