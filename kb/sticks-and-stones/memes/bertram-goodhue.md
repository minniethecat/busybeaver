## Overview

Bertram Grosvenor Goodhue (1869–1924) was an American architect whose career traced an arc from [[gothic-revival|Gothic Revival]] romanticism through Spanish Baroque eclecticism to an increasingly abstract [[modern-movement|modernism]]. In Lewis Mumford's *Sticks and Stones*, Goodhue appears at multiple points in the narrative: as a skilled Gothic revivalist, as an exemplar of eclectic borrowing from non-classical sources, and finally as one of the "men of great ability" whose individual achievements outstripped the civilization that produced them.

## Biography

Born in Pomfret, Connecticut, Goodhue entered the Boston architectural office of [[ralph-adams-cram|Ralph Adams Cram]] as a young man and became a partner in the firm Cram, Goodhue & Ferguson. His early work was firmly within the [[gothic-revival|Gothic Revival]] tradition. After the partnership dissolved in 1914, Goodhue's independent practice moved in increasingly eclectic and then modernist directions, culminating in the Nebraska State Capitol, completed after his sudden death in 1924.

## Major Works

**St. Thomas Church** (with Cram, 1914, [[new-york-city|New York City]]). A masterpiece of American [[gothic-revival|Gothic Revival]], recognized by Mumford as among the finest achievements of the surviving romanticist tradition [007-017].

**Church of the Intercession** (1915, [[new-york-city|New York City]]). A notable example of Goodhue's use of local materials — Mumford cited it as one of the few [[new-york-city|New York]] buildings employing the region's native schist, gneiss, and limestone:

> so New York City, which has a solid foundation of schist, gneiss, and limestone, can point to only a handful of buildings, notably the College of the City of New York and Mr. Goodhue's Church of the Intercession, in which these excellent local materials were used. [007-022]

**Panama-Pacific Exposition** (1915, San Francisco). Goodhue's design for this world's fair introduced Spanish Baroque ornament to American architecture, drawing on Mexican colonial precedents:

> from the first came new lessons in ornament and color, applied with great success by Mr. Bertram Goodhue in the Panama-Pacific Exposition, and now budding lustily in southern villas and gardens [008-005]

**Nebraska State Capitol** (1922–1932, Lincoln). Goodhue's final and most original work, described by Mumford as possessing "the strength and originality" characteristic of the best contemporary public buildings [008-009].

## Architectural Philosophy

Goodhue's career embodied the eclectic breadth of early twentieth-century American architecture. His evolution from Gothic to Spanish Baroque to proto-modern forms demonstrated a restless refusal to be confined to a single idiom. Significantly, Mumford noted that "in his most recent efforts Mr. Goodhue, for one, had abandoned the molds of romanticism" [007-017] — a trajectory that distinguished him from Cram, his former partner, who remained committed to the medieval model.

This willingness to evolve placed Goodhue in an ambiguous position: neither fully a romanticist nor fully a modernist, but an architect whose stylistic range reflected the broader eclectic currents of the imperial age.

## Influence and Legacy

Mumford placed Goodhue in a lineage of American architectural excellence stretching from the founding era to the twentieth century:

> We have not lacked architects of boldness and originality, from Latrobe to Louis H. Sullivan: nor have we lacked men of great ability, from Thomas Jefferson to Bertram Goodhue [008-003]

Yet this praise carries an implicit critique: Goodhue's individual brilliance, like that of his contemporaries, operated within a civilization that failed to provide the conditions for a coherent architectural culture. His West Point additions (with Cram) were cited alongside other "isolated buildings of great esthetic interest" that remained "separate jewels" unable to transform "the matrix of our physical community" [008-010].
