# World's Columbian Exposition (1893)

The [[worlds-columbian-exposition|World's Columbian Exposition]], opened in [[chicago-school-city|Chicago]] in May 1893, was the decisive architectural event of its era. Staged in Jackson Park and covering over 600 acres, the fair assembled the most ambitious collaboration of American architects yet seen under the direction of [[daniel-burnham|Daniel Burnham]]. Its gleaming neoclassical Court of Honor — the "White City" — presented a vision of imperial order that would dominate American civic architecture for a generation. For [[henry-hobson-richardson|H. H. Richardson]]'s followers, the Exposition marked a turning point: romanticism was supplanted by academic classicism, and American architecture entered what Mumford calls "the complete rehabilitation of the Roman mode" [(006-034)]. The fair's influence radiated outward through the [[city-beautiful|City Beautiful]] movement, reshaping [[washington-dc|Washington, D.C.]], and embedding [[beaux-arts|Beaux-Arts]] conventions in the fabric of American public life.

## Historical Context

By 1890, the frontier had closed and American capital had consolidated. The generation of architects who had studied at the [[ecole-des-beaux-arts|École des Beaux-Arts]] after the Civil War was, at last, ready to assume leadership of the profession. Mumford describes the moment with precision:

> The decade between 1890 and 1900 saw the rise of a new period in American architecture. This period had, it is true, been dimly foreshadowed by the grandiose L'Enfant, but if the superficial forms resembled those of the early republic, and if the precedents of classic architecture again became a guide, the dawning age was neither a revival nor a continuation.

[(006-001)]

The fair arrived at precisely the moment when "the stage was set for a new act of the drama" [(006-001)]. [[chicago-school-city|Chicago]] had already pioneered the skyscraper; now its architects would demonstrate their "enterprise and capacity for organization" [(006-012)] in transforming an "unkempt wilderness" into what contemporaries hailed as a dream city.

## The Architecture of the White City

Under [[daniel-burnham|Burnham]]'s direction, the exposition's main buildings were arranged around a central lagoon — the Court of Honor — and unified by a common classical vocabulary. The façades were executed in white staff, a temporary material of plaster and hemp fiber that gave the ensemble its famous luminosity.

> Led by the New Yorkers, who had come more decisively under European influence, they brought to this exposition the combination of skill and taste in all the departments of the work that had, two centuries earlier, created the magnificent formalities of Versailles. There was unity of plan in the grouping of the main buildings about the lagoon; there was unity of tone and color in the gleaming white façades; there was unity of effect in the use of classic orders and classic forms of decoration.

[(006-012)]

The stylistic unity was achieved not through shared creative purpose but through subordination to precedent. Mumford notes that John Wellborn Root had "initially conceived of a variegated oriental setting" [(006-012)], but his death in 1891 cleared the way for the uniform classicism favored by the Eastern architects.

## The Defeat of Romanticism

The Exposition's triumph was also a reckoning. [[henry-hobson-richardson|Richardson]], who had died in 1886, represented an architecture of mass, material honesty, and romantic individualism. His imitators had continued for a time — Mumford notes that "up to the time of the Chicago World's Fair, [[henry-hobson-richardson|Richardson]] had imitators, and they were not always mean ones" [(005-015)] — but the fair's classical discipline rendered that tradition obsolete.

> Romanticism met its great defeat in the office-building. By the use of the passenger elevator, first designed for an exhibition-tower adjacent to the Crystal Palace in 1853, it had become possible to raise the height of buildings to seven stories: the desire for ground-rents presently increased the height to ten.

[(005-020)]

The underlying shift was structural as well as aesthetic. The [[steel-frame-construction|steel cage]], which emerged around 1888, removed the necessity for solid masonry and "placed a premium upon the mask" [(006-001)]. The White City's staff façades — "the building spoke one language and the 'architecture' another" [(006-014)] — embodied this divorce between structure and appearance. Mumford reflects on the fair as both achievement and simulacrum:

> Correct in proportion, elegant in detail, courteous in relation to each other, the buildings of the World's Fair were, nevertheless, only the simulacra of a living architecture: they were the concentrated expression of an age which sought to produce "values" rather than goods. In comparison with this new style, the romanticism of the Victorian Age, with its avid respect for the medieval building traditions, was honesty and dignity itself.

[(006-016)]

## The City Beautiful Movement

The fair's most consequential legacy was the [[city-beautiful|City Beautiful]] movement, which proposed to extend its principles to the permanent fabric of American cities. Mumford captures the force of this ambition:

> The Roman precedent, modified by the work of Louis XIV and Napoleon III, by Le Nôtre and Haussmann, formed the basis not merely for the World's Fair, but for the host of city plans that were produced in the two decades that followed.

[(006-017)]

The civic center and the parkway represented the constructive side of this effort. Groups of harmonious white buildings rose in Cleveland, Pittsburgh, and Springfield, Massachusetts. The restoration of L'Enfant's plan for [[washington-dc|Washington, D.C.]], which began in 1901, was its most enduring fulfillment. [[daniel-burnham|Burnham]] himself went on to co-author the 1909 Plan of [[chicago-school-city|Chicago]] with Edward Bennett.

Yet Mumford levels a sharp critique. The [[city-beautiful|City Beautiful]], he argues, "suggested to the civic enthusiast that every city might become a fair: it introduced the notion of the [[city-beautiful|City Beautiful]] as a sort of municipal cosmetic" [(006-017)]. It addressed surface order while neglecting "the billboard, the skysign, the subway, the [[skyscraper|tall building]], which undermined the effects of the plan even when it was achieved" [(006-020)]. The architect, "cut off from his true function to serve and beautify the community, made an accessory of business itself, like the merest salesman or advertising agent," speedily lost his leadership [(006-019)].

## The Imperial Mirror

The fair was, in Mumford's reading, an imperial phenomenon — a "precise and classic example" of the new order "in miniature" [(006-034)]. Behind the stately colonnades lay the sideshows of the Midway Plaisance; the same tension between monumentality and commerce that characterized American cities at scale. [[daniel-burnham|Burnham]] himself said, according to his biographers, "that it is what the Romans would have wished to create in permanent form" [(006-040)].

> In creating this fair, the enterprise and capacity for organization which the architects of Chicago had applied to the construction of the skyscraper transformed the unkempt wilderness of Jackson Park into the Great White City in the space of two short years.

[(006-012)]

The Exposition built a template for the decades that followed. Its influence radiated through civic centers, railroad stations, museums, and the [[american-academy-in-rome|American Academy in Rome]]. The [[architectural-competition|architectural competition]] system, already established as the mechanism for selecting monumental designs, was reinforced by the fair's example. And the [[modern-movement|modern movement]], when it emerged after 1910, defined itself in conscious opposition to everything [[worlds-columbian-exposition|the White City]] represented.
