# Rhode Island

Rhode Island appears in Lewis Mumford's *Sticks and Stones* primarily as an illustration of architectural regionalism -- the way that local conditions, materials, and traditions produce distinct building practices even within small geographic areas.

## Local Variation in Colonial Architecture

Mumford emphasizes how finely differentiated colonial building practices could be. "In every detail of house construction, from the setting of fireplaces to the slope of the roof, there were local peculiarities which distinguished not merely the Dutch settlements from the English, but which even characterized several settlements in Rhode Island that were scarcely a day's tramp apart" [002-003].

This observation supports one of Mumford's central themes: that pre-industrial architecture was organically rooted in place, climate, and community tradition -- a condition that modern, standardized building methods have largely erased. Rhode Island's architectural heritage thus serves as evidence of the regional diversity that characterized early American building before the homogenizing influence of pattern books and professional architects.

## The Carpenter-Builder and the First Architect

Rhode Island occupies a pivotal position in Mumford's narrative of architectural professionalization. Newport was the site where the medieval guild tradition of the [[carpenter-builder]] first encountered the professional architect in colonial America. Mumford identifies Peter Harrison, who designed the Redwood Library in Newport, as probably the first professional architect to work on American soil:

"For about a hundred years the carpenter-builder continued to remain on the scene, and work in his forthright and painstaking and honest manner; but in the middle of the eighteenth century he was joined, for the first time, by the professional architect, the first one being probably Peter Harrison, who designed the Redwood Library, which still stands in Newport" [002-006].

This transition, occurring in a Rhode Island port town, marks a watershed in American building culture. Under the influence of architects and "amateurs of taste," the carpenter-builder lost his status as an independent craftsman who built intelligently for his social equals. Mumford presents this shift from vernacular craftsmanship to professional design as a loss as much as a gain: the carpenter was forced to meet "the swift, corrosive influences brought in from foreign lands by men who had visited the ports of the world" [002-006]. Rhode Island, with its maritime commerce, was among the first regions to absorb these imported fashions -- a pattern that would eventually spread throughout the colonies.

The Redwood Library (completed 1750) thus stands as more than a colonial landmark; it represents the moment when American architecture began its long migration from a craft rooted in local materials and community traditions toward a profession governed by book-learning, pattern-book correctness, and the authority of the trained specialist. The [[craftsmanship]] that had characterized the [[medieval-building-tradition|medieval building tradition]] began its slow retreat in places like Newport even as it persisted in the rural districts of New England.

## Newport and the Gilded Age

If colonial Newport represented the arrival of architectural professionalism, Gilded Age Newport represented its decadence. By the 1870s, the town had become a showcase for the ostentatious country villas of the newly rich -- architecture that Mumford judged harshly as a symptom of the collapse of taste and proportion that marked the era. Surveying the domestic architecture of the period, he wrote:

"In the awkward country villas that began to fill the still-remote suburbs of the larger cities, all sense of style and proportion were lost: the plan was marked by meaningless irregularities; a dingy, muddy color spread over the wooden facades. There exists a huge and beautifully printed volume, of which, I believe, there are not more than a hundred copies, on the villas of Newport in 1876: the compiler thereof sought to satisfy the vanity of the original owners and the curiosity of a later generation; yet mid all these examples of the 'novel' and the 'unique,' there is not a single mansion that would satisfy any conceivable line of descendants" [005-016].

This passage establishes Newport as the negative mirror of its colonial past. Where the earlier Rhode Island settlements had produced architecture of honest materials, functional clarity, and organic regional character, the Gilded Age villas -- many built by the same architects trained in [[eclecticism|eclectic]] historicism -- offered only self-conscious novelty. Newport's trajectory from Redwood Library to the mansions of Bellevue Avenue traces, in miniature, the larger arc of architectural decline that Mumford charts from the [[colonial-architecture|colonial period]] through the [[gilded-age|Gilded Age]].

The contrast is instructive. The colonial carpenter-builders of Rhode Island, working within the limitations of local materials and free from the burden of stylistic self-consciousness, had produced buildings that remained fresh and vital across centuries. Their Gilded Age successors, armed with pattern books, architectural journals, and commissions from patrons determined to display their wealth, produced little of lasting value. Mumford's judgment on the Newport villas is characteristically blunt: however much they satisfied the vanity of their original owners, they satisfied no conceivable standard of architectural excellence.

## Scholarly Study

The early architecture of Rhode Island attracted serious scholarly attention in the early twentieth century. Mumford cites "Isham and Brown's work on the early architecture of Connecticut and Rhode Island" [011-002] as having "made a new departure" in the study of American colonial building. This reference, from the bibliographical notes of *Sticks and Stones*, places Rhode Island within the broader scholarly effort to document and understand America's vernacular architectural heritage.

## Context in New England

Rhode Island's architectural character belongs to the broader New England tradition that Mumford traced from medieval carpenter-builders through colonial adaptations to nineteenth-century transformations. The state's early buildings -- along with those of Connecticut and Massachusetts -- represent the first chapter of American architecture: a chapter in which local materials, craft skill, and community needs shaped building form more powerfully than imported styles or professional design.
