## Overview

The historic preservation movement occupies an ambiguous position in Lewis Mumford's architectural thought. On one hand, he acknowledges a profound human need: "There is something in man that compels him to respect the human imprint of art: he lives more nobly surrounded by his own reflections, as a god might live" [007-043]. On the other hand, he warns that preservation can become a form of cultural necrophilia — a retreat from the demands of the present into a sanitized version of the past.

The tension between these two imperatives — the imperative to preserve and the imperative to create — runs throughout *Sticks and Stones*. Mumford's treatment of preservation is therefore not a simple endorsement or rejection but a sustained meditation on the conditions under which preservation serves living culture rather than substituting for it.

## Preservation and the Colonial Revival

The colonial revival that began after the Civil War was, in Mumford's view, the first significant wave of architectural preservation in America. "Ever since colonial architecture was reappreciated after the Civil War, a large amount of material has appeared on the early architecture of the colonies" [006-047]. This reappreciation produced genuine scholarship — careful measured drawings, photographic surveys, and historical monographs — that enriched the understanding of early American building.

But it also produced a great deal of counterfeit. The colonial revival houses of the 1890s and 1900s, with their Palladian windows and dentil cornices, were not preservation but pastiche. Mumford's distinction is important: the scholarly recovery of the colonial period was legitimate and valuable; the attempt to build new houses in the colonial style was, in his view, a form of architectural falsehood.

## The Limits of Revival

Mumford's most penetrating criticism of the preservation impulse is captured in a single sentence:

> Once we have genuinely appreciated the influence that created early colonial architecture, we see that it is irrecoverable: what we call a revival is really a second burial.

[006-047]

This is a radical claim. Preservation, in Mumford's reading, can honor the past only by recognizing its irrecoverability. The colonial house was the product of specific conditions — a craft tradition, a climate-responsive building culture, a social order — that no longer exist. To reproduce its forms under contemporary conditions is not to preserve it but to embalm it.

A "second burial" is worse than a first because it substitutes the appearance of the past for the reality of the past. The colonial revival house does not teach us how to build honestly in our own time; it teaches us how to simulate the appearance of a lost authenticity.

## Preservation Against the Machine

In the final chapters of *Sticks and Stones*, Mumford's treatment of preservation becomes more urgent. The forces that are "inimical to architecture" — speculative development, standardized production, the destruction of regional character — are "seen at their worst in the business district of the metropolis; but more and more they tend to spread throughout the rest of the community" [007-006].

In this context, preservation takes on a new function: it becomes a form of resistance. The preservation of historic buildings, districts, and landscapes is, for Mumford, not an act of nostalgia but an act of cultural self-defense. It is a way of maintaining a connection to the pre-industrial past that can inform and discipline the architecture of the present.

## Mumford's Criterion

The criterion Mumford offers for distinguishing legitimate preservation from sterile revival is deceptively simple: does it serve the living? A preserved building that is used, inhabited, and adapted — that continues to do real work in the life of the community — is a living artifact. A preserved building that is cordoned off, restored to a hypothetical original condition, and treated as a museum piece is dead.

This distinction has profound implications for preservation practice. It suggests that adaptive reuse — the conversion of old buildings to new purposes — is more respectful of the past than the most scrupulous restoration. The life of the building, not the purity of its original form, is what matters.
