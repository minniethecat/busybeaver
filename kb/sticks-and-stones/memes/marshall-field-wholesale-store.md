## Overview

The Marshall Field Wholesale Store in Chicago, designed by Henry Hobson Richardson and built between 1885 and 1887, was a seven-story masonry commercial building that represented the practical apex of load-bearing stone and brick construction. Located in Chicago's commercial district, it was one of Richardson's most influential urban works and a defining monument of the Romanesque Revival in American architecture.

## A Structural Turning Point

In *Sticks and Stones*, Mumford positioned the Marshall Field Building at a critical juncture in architectural history — the moment when traditional masonry reached its physical limits against the commercial pressure for greater height. He wrote: (005-014)

> Richardson's Marshall Field Building in Chicago was seven stories high; and that was about as far as solid stone or brick could climb without becoming undignified and futile by its bulk.

The building thus marked both the culmination of masonry architecture and its impending obsolescence. Mumford noted that "the possibilities of masonry and the possibilities of commercial gain through ground-rents were at loggerheads, and by 1888 masonry was defeated," as the steel-cage frame displaced load-bearing walls as the structural basis for tall commercial buildings. (005-014)

## Richardson's Achievement

The Marshall Field Building exemplified Richardson's mature Romanesque style — massive stone walls, round arches, and a sense of gravity and permanence that resisted the emerging commercial aesthetic of lightness and verticality. Mumford observed that these buildings "held their own against the insurrections of traffic and realty speculation," their sheer physical substance making demolition economically daunting. (005-013)

Richardson's achievement as a builder-mason was all the more remarkable given the context of the Gilded Age. Mumford described him as a figure who "pitted his own single powers against the barbarism of the Gilded Age; but, unlike his contemporaries in England, he did not turn his back upon the excellences of industrialism." (005-008) Richardson reportedly told his biographer: "The things I want most to design are a grain-elevator and the interior of a great river-steamboat" — an aspiration that revealed his willingness to engage with the industrial world on its own terms, even as his masonry buildings embodied a craft tradition that industry was rapidly displacing. (005-008)

## The Steel Frame and Architecture's Transformation

The defeat of masonry by steel was swift and comprehensive. Mumford captured the epochal nature of this transition: "Within a decade of his death, however, only the empty forms of architecture remained, for the steel-cage of the engineer had become the new structural reality." (005-015) The introduction of steel-cage construction "removed the necessity for solid masonry, and placed a premium upon the mask" — the architectural surface became a veneer, detached from the structural logic that had governed building since antiquity. (006-003)

This transformation had profound consequences for the architectural profession. The architect, who had been a master-builder directing craftsmen, became a designer of surfaces — a coordinator of engineered systems. The Marshall Field Wholesale Store stood at the threshold of this transformation: still a building in which the structural and aesthetic logic were one, but already pushing against the limits that would render that unity impossible.

## Comparison with the Brooklyn Bridge

Mumford's discussion of the Marshall Field Building is framed by a broader meditation on the relationship between masonry and steel. The Brooklyn Bridge, completed in 1884 — roughly contemporary with the Marshall Field Store — represented a fusion of the two traditions. Mumford rued that "if the architectural elements of the massive piers have perhaps too much the bare quality of engineering, if the pointed arches meet esthetic betrayal in the flat solidity of the cornices, if, in short, the masonry does not sing as Richardson alone perhaps could have made it sing, the steel work itself makes up for this, by the architectural beauty of its pattern." (005-026) The implication was clear: Richardson's masonry could sing; the engineering structures that followed could only perform.

## Legacy

Richardson did not live to see the steel-frame revolution that followed within a decade of his death. As Mumford reflected: "within a decade of his death, however, only the empty forms of architecture remained, for the steel-cage of the engineer had become the new structural reality." (005-015) The Marshall Field Wholesale Store stood as a monument to the final moment when masonry alone defined the structural and aesthetic logic of the American commercial building.

The building's demolition in 1930 — less than fifty years after its construction — confirmed Mumford's observation about the economic forces arrayed against masonry. The very solidity that had made Richardson's buildings resistant to demolition could not, in the end, withstand the calculus of rising land values and the demand for ever-taller structures.
