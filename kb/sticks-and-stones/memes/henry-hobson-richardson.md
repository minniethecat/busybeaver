# Henry Hobson Richardson

## Overview

H. H. Richardson (1838–1886) was the most important American architect of the post-Civil War generation. Mumford calls him "the chief exponent of American romanticism" and presents him as the figure who, "for a while, might breast the tide of mechanical industry and create for a good part of the scene a sense of stability and harmony which it had all too plainly lacked" [(005-005)]. Trained at the [[ecole-des-beaux-arts|École des Beaux-Arts]], Richardson absorbed French discipline without succumbing to French formula, developing a personal Romanesque-inspired style that came closer than any of his contemporaries to establishing a distinctively American architecture.

> Inarticulate as H. H. Richardson, the chief exponent of American romanticism, was, it seemed for a while as if he might breast the tide of mechanical industry and create for a good part of the scene a sense of stability and harmony which it had all too plainly lacked. In relation to his age, however, Richardson was in the biological sense a "sport"; surrounded by jerry-builders, who had degraded the craft of building, and engineers who ignored it, he was perhaps the last of the great medieval line of master-masons. [(005-005)]

## Biography

Richardson was born in Louisiana and educated at Harvard College before becoming "almost the first of the new generation of Americans to be trained by the [[ecole-des-beaux-arts|École]] des Beaux Arts" [(005-006)]. Crucially, "he brought back to America none of those atrocious adaptations of the French [[renaissance-architecture|Renaissance]] like the [[new-york-city|New York]], Philadelphia, and Boston Post Offices" — instead, he fell under the influence of [[eugene-viollet-le-duc|Viollet-le-Duc]] and spent his early career working in a mode closer to the Gothic revival before developing his mature Romanesque manner [(005-006)].

His career began in earnest after the Civil War, and by the 1880s his reputation was immense. Mumford records that "in a symposium on the ten finest buildings in America, conducted by an architectural journal in the 'eighties, Richardson was given five" [(005-009)]. Yet this dominance was fragile. Richardson's health was failing — he died at 47, in 1886 — and "the echoes of Richardson's style resounded in the work of the Western architects" only briefly before his own pupils, [[charles-mckim|McKim]] and White, turned toward academic classicism [(005-028)].

## Architectural Philosophy

Richardson's architecture was an attempt to synthesize the discipline of [[beaux-arts|Beaux-Arts]] training with the vitality of medieval building. "Richardson's brand of romanticism was a genuine attempt to embrace the age" — he was not a narrow revivalist but an architect who sought forms adequate to the scale and energy of industrial America [(005-008)]. Though most people "vaguely associate him with ecclesiastical work," his long list of public works included only five churches [(005-008)].

> The proof of Richardson's genius as a builder lies in the difference between the accepted drawings for Trinity Church and the finished building. His ideas altered with the progress of the work, and in almost every case the building itself is a vast improvement over the paper design. Moreover, in his capacity as master-mason, Richardson trained an able corps of craftsmen; and so pervasive was his influence that one still finds on houses Richardson never saw, the touches of delicate, leafy stone-carving he had introduced. [(005-007)]

The quality Mumford most admires in Richardson is his plasticity — his capacity to develop and transform a design as it takes physical form. This is the mark of the builder, not merely the designer — an architect who understood materials and construction at the scale of the building itself. Richardson himself said to his biographer: "The things I want most to design are a grain-elevator and the interior of a great river-steamboat" [(005-008)] — a remarkable ambition that shows how far he stood from the narrow revivalism of some of his contemporaries.

## Influence and Legacy

Richardson's achievement, in Mumford's account, is bittersweet. "I have emphasized what was strong and fine in Richardson's work in order to show how free it was from the minor faults of romanticism; and yet it reckoned without its host, and Richardson, alas! left scarcely a trace upon the period that followed" [(005-013)].

His immediate influence was substantial: "Up to the time of the [[worlds-columbian-exposition-1893|Chicago World's Fair]], Richardson had imitators, and they were not always mean ones" — L. H. Buffington in Minneapolis among them [(005-012)]. But the steel frame and the elevator transformed the building industry within a decade of his death. "Richardson, fortunately, did not live to see the undermining of the tradition he had founded and almost established. Within a decade of his death, however, only the empty forms of architecture remained, for the steel-cage of the engineer had become the new structural reality" [(005-015)].

> Richardson's most intimate disciples reacted against the stamp of his personality and sought a more neutral mode of expression, consecrated by established canons of good taste. On top of this, the introduction of steel-cage construction removed the necessity for solid masonry, and placed a premium upon the mask. [(006-003)]

Mumford sees Richardson as the last figure who might have given American architecture a coherent tradition — and whose failure to do so was not personal but historical. The forces of mechanical industry, speculative building, and academic revivalism were too powerful for any single architect to overcome. "So much time had been spent in mismanaging the structure" — the building of the age as much as any specific building — that Richardson's vision could not hold [(005-010)].

Richardson's legacy is thus doubly poignant: he demonstrated what a living architecture could look like, precisely at the moment when the conditions that made such an architecture possible were dissolving. He remains the great "what if" of American architecture — the architect who almost established a tradition, and whose eclipse marked the victory of the machine process over the builder's art.

## In Mumford's Text

Mumford devotes the bulk of Chapter V ("The Defeat of [[romanticism|Romanticism]]") to Richardson and his context. The chapter title itself is Richardson's story: romanticism made a last stand, and Richardson was its greatest champion. Mumford likens him to a medieval master-mason transposed into the Gilded Age — an anachronism whose very strength was a "fatal weakness in the growing centers of commerce and industry" [(005-013)]. The difficulty of tearing down Richardson's buildings, Mumford notes with irony, "only increased the demand for a more frail and facile method of construction" [(005-013)].

Richardson also appears in Chapter VI, where Mumford describes how his pupils [[charles-mckim|McKim]] and White "reacted against the stamp of his personality" and turned toward academic classicism after his death [(006-003)], and how his "fine eye for color contrasts" had called into existence numerous small local quarries — quarries that were subsequently "allowed to lapse" under the imperial regime's preference for Vermont marble and Indiana limestone [(006-019)].
