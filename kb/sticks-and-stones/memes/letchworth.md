## Overview

Letchworth Garden City, founded in 1903 in Hertfordshire, England, was the world's first garden city, implementing Ebenezer Howard's principles of limited-size communities integrating residential, industrial, and agricultural land. Lewis Mumford cited Letchworth and its successor Welwyn as proof that comprehensive architectural design through community planning was not merely a theoretical ideal but a practical achievement: "That it can be done again the examples of Letchworth and Welwyn in England, and numerous smaller gardened cities created by municipal authorities in England and other parts of Europe, bear evidence" [008-703].

## In the Text

Mumford's single explicit reference to Letchworth appears in his concluding chapter, "Architecture and Civilization," where he argues for community planning as the necessary precondition for a genuine American architecture. Letchworth serves as a key piece of evidence in this argument — its existence demonstrates that the garden city is not "a strange importation from Europe" but rather "a sophisticated recovery of a form that we once enjoyed on our Atlantic seaboard" [008-707].

## Planning Principles

Letchworth was designed by Raymond Unwin and Barry Parker following Howard's vision of a self-contained community surrounded by a permanent agricultural greenbelt. The plan separated residential areas from industrial zones, incorporated generous open spaces and parkland, and limited the town's ultimate population. Raymon Unwin, who later contributed an analysis of skyscraper congestion to the *Journal of the American Institute of Architects* that Mumford also cited, brought to Letchworth the principles of picturesque town planning derived from Camillo Sitte [007-530].

## Legacy

Letchworth Garden City demonstrated the viability of Howard's ideas and inspired the global garden city movement. Welwyn Garden City, founded in 1920, extended the experiment. Mumford's citation of Letchworth in defense of community planning connected his American argument to this international movement, positioning the garden city as a proven alternative to the "flatulent and inorganic conception of city-development" that characterized machine-age urban growth [008-707].
