## Overview

The Smithsonian Institution Building, commonly known as the Smithsonian Castle, was designed by architect [[james-renwick|James Renwick Jr.]] and constructed between 1847 and 1855 on the National Mall in Washington, D.C. As the original home of the Smithsonian Institution, the building was among the earliest examples of the Gothic Revival style applied to institutional architecture in the United States. In Lewis Mumford's *Sticks and Stones*, it appears as an emblem of the mid-19th century's architectural confusion — a building whose style, however picturesque, bore no organic relationship to its purpose.

Mumford's judgment is characteristically sharp. He describes the Smithsonian as an example of "the brummagem gothic" [007-039] — a dismissive term that suggests both the building's imitation of medieval forms and its distance from the structural logic that made those forms meaningful. The Smithsonian, for Mumford, represents the moment when American architecture began to treat historical styles as costumes rather than as living traditions.

## Gothic Revival in America

The Smithsonian was built at a time when the [[gothic-revival|Gothic Revival]] had established itself as the dominant mode for educational and ecclesiastical architecture in both Britain and America. The style carried associations of learning, piety, and institutional permanence — qualities that the Smithsonian's founders sought to project. But the Gothic of the Smithsonian was not the structural Gothic of the medieval cathedral — the Gothic of flying buttresses and ribbed vaults working in precise counterpoint.

It was, instead, the Gothic of the pattern book: applied pointed arches, decorative tracery, and the general effect of medievalism without the structural discipline that made medieval building great. The nine towers of the Smithsonian Castle, its crenellated battlements, and its warm red sandstone conveyed an impression of Oxbridge collegiate architecture transplanted to the capital of the young republic. But this impression, Mumford argues, was essentially theatrical — a set piece rather than a building that grew from the needs of its program.

The growth of eclecticism had, by the middle of the 19th century, "given the American city the aspect of a museum and the American countryside a touch of the picture-book" [003-011]. Washington Irving's Sunnyside and "the first Smithsonian building were in the predominant Gothic mode" [003-011], part of a wave of romantic medievalism that swept through American architecture in the decades before the Civil War.

## The Question of Materials

Mumford's criticism of the Smithsonian is also tied to his analysis of the changing relationship between architecture and materials in the industrial age. At the time of the Smithsonian's construction, the use of iron in building was a subject of active debate. Robert Dale Owen, the social reformer and advocate for the Smithsonian, observed that "of late years a rival material, from the mine, seems encroaching on these [stone, clay, wood] and the next generation may see, arising on our continent, villages, or it may be cities, of iron" [004-008].

Mumford, quoting Owen, draws an ironic parallel. Owen's enthusiasm for iron construction — which he saw as heralding a new, rational architecture — was coupled with his advocacy of the Smithsonian's Gothic design. The Smithsonian thus stood at the intersection of two incompatible architectural principles: the medieval revival, which looked backward to a craft tradition that industrialization had already destroyed, and the nascent machine aesthetic, which would not find its architectural expression for another half century.

The result was what Mumford calls "brummagem" — showy but cheap, an imitation of quality rather than the thing itself. The Smithsonian's Gothic details, however carefully executed, could not conceal the fact that the building was a product of its age: an age that had lost the medieval craftsman's intimate knowledge of stone and had not yet acquired the modern engineer's command of steel.

## A Museum Culture

The Smithsonian also exemplifies what Mumford identifies as the "museum culture" of 19th-century American architecture. An architecture of eclectic quotation, he argues, transforms the city into a repository of the past rather than a field of living creation:

> The one thing the museum cannot attempt to do is to supply a soil for living art: all that it can present is a pattern for reproduction.

[006-039]

A building that functions as a museum of styles — and the Smithsonian, as a building that drew its forms from medieval precedents while housing a modern scientific institution, was precisely such a building — cannot itself become a source of living architectural tradition. It can only offer patterns for reproduction. And reproduction, Mumford insists, is not creation.

## Renwick's Design

[[james-renwick|James Renwick Jr.]] (1818–1895), the architect of the Smithsonian, was one of the most prominent American practitioners of the Gothic Revival. He would later design St. Patrick's Cathedral in New York — a building that shared the Smithsonian's medieval vocabulary but deployed it on a far grander scale. Renwick was not a mere copyist; his work shows a genuine engagement with the forms of medieval architecture. But like all architects of the Gothic Revival in America, he faced the fundamental problem that the Gothic was not a native tradition. The pointed arch and the ribbed vault had evolved to solve specific structural problems in 12th-century France; transplanted to 19th-century Washington, they became decorative motifs, carrying cultural associations but performing no structural work.
