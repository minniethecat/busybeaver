## Overview

William Morris Hunt was the preeminent American painter of the post-Civil War generation, a key figure in introducing French Barbizon and academic painting traditions to the United States. In *Sticks and Stones*, Hunt appears not for his easel paintings but for his ill-fated mural commission at the New York State Capitol in Albany — a story Mumford tells as an emblem of the institutional chaos that beset American art in the Gilded Age.

> William Morris Hunt, then at the height of his powers, was commissioned to paint two large mural compositions for the assembly chamber of this blessed building. [005-016]

## The Albany Capitol Murals

The New York State Capitol was already a monument to mismanagement by the time Richardson and Leopold Eidlitz took it over in 1878: five million dollars had been spent over ten years of misconstruction. [005-015] Richardson attempted to reorient the building from its original Renaissance design toward his own Romanesque manner, only to be "legislated back into Renaissance by the offended lawgivers." [005-015] It was into this chaos that Hunt was summoned for the assembly chamber murals.

> So much time had been spent in mismanaging the structure that Hunt was given only two months to transfer his cartouche to the panels; but he worked heroically, and, as one of his biographers says, the work was a great triumph. [005-016]

The triumph was brief. The political corruption that had plagued the building's construction continued after its completion:

> "The building had fallen into the hands of a political ring, and the poor construction was revealed in the leaking of the massive roof and the settling of the whole structure. Before ten years had passed, great portions of Hunt's paintings flaked off, and what remained was walled up behind the rebuilding necessary to avert utter ruin." [005-016]

## An Emblem of the Gilded Age

Mumford uses Hunt's story to make a larger point about Richardson's achievement. The fact that Richardson managed to produce lasting architecture at all — buildings like Trinity Church in Boston, the Marshall Field Wholesale Store in Chicago, and the Allegheny County Courthouse — is made remarkable by the context of institutional dysfunction in which he worked:

> In a period like this, Richardson's comparative success takes on heroic proportions. [005-016]

Hunt's fate — a major artistic commission destroyed by shoddy construction and political corruption within a single decade — represents the normal condition of American art in the Gilded Age. The architect, the painter, the sculptor all labored under conditions that made durable achievement the exception rather than the rule. The Albany Capitol became, in Mumford's telling, a synecdoche for the larger predicament of culture under industrial capitalism: immense resources, occasional genius, and systematic waste.

## Artistic Career Beyond Albany

Though *Sticks and Stones* focuses narrowly on the Albany episode, Hunt's broader significance is considerable. Trained in Düsseldorf and Paris, he studied under Thomas Couture and was influenced by Jean-François Millet and the Barbizon School. His return to Boston in 1855 established him as the leading artistic figure of that city's cultural renaissance. His mural *The Flight of Night* (1878), painted for the New York State Capitol, was among the most ambitious American mural projects of its era — and its loss remains one of the great material tragedies of 19th-century American art.
