## Overview

The White City was the architectural centerpiece of the World's Columbian Exposition of 1893, a unified ensemble of neoclassical exhibition buildings arrayed around a central lagoon in Chicago's Jackson Park. Mumford treats it as the definitive architectural expression of the American imperial age — a moment when the nation's architects, having mastered the Beaux-Arts vocabulary, achieved a formal triumph that would shape American civic architecture for a generation.

> Hardly had the process of concentration and consolidation begun before the proper form manifested itself. The occasion for its appearance was the World's Columbian Exposition, opened in 1893. In creating this fair, the enterprise and capacity for organization which the architects of Chicago had applied to the construction of the skyscraper transformed the unkempt wilderness of Jackson Park into the Great White City in the space of two short years. [006-008]

## Design and Unity

The White City's defining quality was its visual coherence. Under the direction of Daniel Burnham as chief of construction, the leading architectural firms of New York and Chicago collaborated to produce a coordinated ensemble: white staff (a temporary material of plaster and hemp fiber) façades, uniform cornice heights, and a consistent classical vocabulary. Frederick Law Olmsted laid out the grounds, including the lagoon, wooded island, and ceremonial courts.

> There was unity of plan in the grouping of the main buildings about the lagoon; there was unity of tone and color in the gleaming white façades; there was unity of effect in the use of classic orders and classic forms of decoration. [006-008]

The architectural firms included Richard Morris Hunt (Administration Building), McKim, Mead & White (Agricultural Building), George B. Post (Manufactures and Liberal Arts Building), and the Chicago partnership of Burnham and Root — though John Wellborn Root died before the fair's completion, and his vision of a variegated oriental setting was replaced by the unified classical scheme favored by the Eastern architects.

## The Imperial Veneer

Mumford's analysis is characteristically ambivalent. He acknowledges the White City's formal success — "a capital triumph of the academic imagination" — but identifies it as fundamentally superficial: a mask, not a body. The classical orders were applied to steel-and-glass exhibition halls, with the "architecture" functioning as a decorative skin.

> It would be foolish to quarrel with the style that was chosen for these exposition buildings, or to deny its propriety. Messrs. McKim, White, Hunt, and Burnham divined that they were fated to serve Renaissance despots and emperors with more than Roman power, and unerringly they chose the proper form for their activities. [006-009]

> Whereas Rome had cast its spell over the architects of the early Renaissance because they wished once more to enter into its life ... it attracted the architects of the White City because of its external features — because of its stereotyped canons and rules — because of the relatively small number of choices it offered for a lapse in taste — because of its skill in conspicuous waste, and because of that very noncommittal quality in its massive forms which permitted the basilica to become a church, or the temple to become a modern bank. [006-009]

Behind the white staff façade, the reality was modern engineering:

> Behind the white staff façade of the World's Fair buildings was the steel and glass structure of the engineer: the building spoke one language and the "architecture" another. If the coming of the skyscraper had turned masonry into veneer, here was a mode of architecture which was little but veneer. [006-010]

## The City Beautiful Legacy

The White City's most consequential legacy was the City Beautiful movement. The fair demonstrated that coordinated architectural planning could produce civic grandeur on an unprecedented scale, and it inspired a generation of municipal improvement efforts — from the McMillan Commission's restoration of the L'Enfant Plan for Washington, D.C., to civic centers in Cleveland, Pittsburgh, and Springfield.

> The evil of the World's Fair triumph was that it suggested to the civic enthusiast that every city might become a fair: it introduced the notion of the City Beautiful as a sort of municipal cosmetic, and reduced the work of the architect to that of putting a pleasing front upon the scrappy building, upon the monotonous streets and the mean houses, that characterized vast areas in the newer and larger cities. [006-011]

Mumford's critique is sharp: City Beautiful planning treated architecture as decoration rather than organic urban reform. Daniel Burnham, the fair's impresario, is singled out for justifying the Periclean improvements of Athens not as embodiments of citizenship and religion but as measures to attract visitors — "an accessory of business itself, like the merest salesman or advertising agent." [006-012]

## Material Consequences

The White City's aesthetic preferences reshaped the American building materials industry. The demand for uniform white classical façades led to the consolidation of stone quarrying around a few dominant sources — Vermont marble and Indiana limestone — at the expense of the local quarries that had flourished under Richardson's more color-sensitive approach.

> The result was that during this period numerous small local quarries, which had been called into existence by Richardson's fine eye for color contrasts, were allowed to lapse. Vermont marble and Indiana limestone served better the traditions that had been created in the White City. [006-027]

This consolidation was part of a broader pattern Mumford identifies with the imperial age: the exploitation of regional resources for the benefit of metropolitan centers, with all roads leading "literally to Rome."
