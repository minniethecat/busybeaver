# Chicago School & Midwestern Architecture

The Midwest — and Chicago in particular — was the laboratory for America's most original architectural contributions. In *Sticks and Stones*, Mumford traces how the Chicago School, born from the city's explosive growth after the Great Fire of 1871, produced the skyscraper, the steel frame, and a new kind of architectural thinking unburdened by European precedent. From William Le Baron Jenney to Louis Sullivan and Frank Lloyd Wright, the Midwestern current ran against the Beaux-Arts academicism of the East Coast, proposing instead an architecture rooted in democracy, nature, and the American landscape.

## Chicago: The Laboratory City

Chicago after the Great Fire was a city of radical opportunity — empty blocks, rising land values, and clients willing to experiment. Jenney, Burnham, Root, Adler, and Sullivan transformed the city into an architectural proving ground. The steel frame, the elevator, and fireproof construction converged to make the skyscraper possible, and Chicago architects pushed the type further than anyone.

## The Chicago School

The Chicago School — Sullivan's doctrine of form following function, the expressive treatment of the steel frame, the tripartite window — marked the first genuinely American architecture. Mumford sees in Sullivan's skyscrapers and warehouses a direct, functional integrity that the Eastern Beaux-Arts architects could not match. The Chicago School's influence radiated across the Midwest and beyond.

## Prairie School & Organic Architecture

Frank Lloyd Wright emerged from the Chicago School to create the Prairie houses — low, horizontal, integrated with the Midwestern landscape. These houses, Mumford argues, were the first modern architecture in America: open plans, continuous space, and a rejection of historical ornament. The Prairie School represented a brief but brilliant flowering before Wright's career took a more individualistic turn.
