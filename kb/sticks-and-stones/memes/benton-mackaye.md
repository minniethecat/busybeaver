# Benton MacKaye

Benton MacKaye (1879–1975) appears in Lewis Mumford's *Sticks and Stones* as a contributor to the emerging philosophy of regional planning. A forester, conservationist, and regional planner, MacKaye originated the concept of the Appalachian Trail and co-founded the Regional Planning Association of America with Mumford and others.

## Regional Planning Philosophy

MacKaye's approach to regional planning emphasized the integration of natural conservation with human settlement — principles that resonated with Mumford's own critique of metropolitan overgrowth. In the acknowledgments of *Sticks and Stones*, Mumford credits MacKaye among those whose "persistent encouragement and kindly interest" made the book possible.

> This book would not have been put together but for the persistent encouragement and kindly interest of Benton MacKaye. [P03-001]

MacKaye's philosophy grew from his training as a forester under [[gifford-pinchot|Gifford Pinchot]] and his exposure to the regional survey methods of [[patrick-geddes|Patrick Geddes]]. He conceived of planning not as the design of individual buildings or even cities, but as the management of entire watersheds and ecological regions — an approach Mumford would embrace in his later work on regional planning [(008-070)].

## The Appalachian Trail

MacKaye's most famous contribution — the Appalachian Trail — originated in a 1921 article proposing a continuous footpath along the Appalachian mountain chain. The trail was not merely recreational in MacKaye's vision; it was a project of regional reconstruction, linking wilderness preserves, community camps, and educational centers into a coherent spatial network. This integration of conservation, recreation, and planning exemplified the regionalist principles that Mumford and the [[regional-planning-association-of-america|RPAA]] championed.

## A Comprehensive Plan for the Appalachians

Mumford presents MacKaye's Appalachian vision as far more ambitious than a hiking trail. MacKaye had "sketched out a bold and fundamental plan for associating the development of a spinal recreational trail with an electric power development for the whole Appalachian region, along the ridgeway; both trail and power being used as a basis for the reafforestation and the repeopling of the whole upland area, with a corresponding decentralization and depopulation of the overcrowded, spotty coastal region" (008-026). The plan integrated conservation, energy infrastructure, recreation, and population redistribution into a single regional strategy — precisely the kind of multi-dimensional thinking that Mumford championed.

The urgency of MacKaye's proposal was underscored by the environmental degradation it sought to reverse. The Appalachian forest had been subjected to relentless extraction: "the stripping of the Appalachian forest was the first step in our campaign against nature" (008-016), and timber had been so thoroughly mined that "good housing in the east depends to no little extent upon our ability to recover continuous local supplies of timber throughout the Appalachian region" (008-022). MacKaye's plan addressed both ecological restoration and economic reconstruction, treating the ridgeway as a spine for a new pattern of decentralized settlement — a concrete alternative to the metropolitan congestion that Mumford diagnosed throughout *Sticks and Stones*.

## Influence on American Planning

MacKaye's work bridged the conservation movement of the early twentieth century and the regional planning efforts that Mumford championed. His concept of the Appalachian Trail — a continuous wilderness corridor along the eastern United States — embodied the principle that planning should operate at the scale of natural regions rather than political boundaries.

The beginnings of genuine regional planning, Mumford notes, took shape in places like Ontario, Canada, "where the social survey has been carried farthest" [(003-080)]. This survey-based approach to planning — understanding a region's natural and social resources before designing interventions — reflected MacKaye's influence on the movement.
