## Overview

The Woolworth Building, completed in 1913 to the designs of Cass Gilbert, was the tallest building in the world at the time of its completion. In Lewis Mumford's *Sticks and Stones*, the building appears as an exemplar of a transitional moment in skyscraper design — when architects still approached the [[skyscraper|tall building]] as a problem in traditional architectural composition, treating it as "a tower" with accented vertical lines.

## Architecture

The 792-foot, 57-story tower employed a [[steel-frame-construction|steel skeleton]] clad in terra-cotta Gothic ornament. Gilbert conceived the building as a column with base, shaft, and capital — an approach that the academic architects of the period favored for skyscraper design. Mumford placed the Woolworth within the evolution of skyscraper aesthetics:

> Then the skyscraper was treated as a tower, and its vertical lines were accented by piers which simulated the acrobatic leap of stone construction: the Woolworth Tower and the Bush Tower were both designed in this fashion, and, in spite of numerous defects in detail, they remain with the new Shelton Hotel in New York among the most satisfactory examples of the skyscraper. [007-035]

The Woolworth represented the "tower" approach to skyscraper composition — distinct from the earlier "column" model (base-shaft-capital, exemplified by the Flatiron Building) and the later "pure mechanical form" toward which Mumford saw the skyscraper evolving.

## In the Text

Mumford deployed the Woolworth Building as a rhetorical benchmark for the impossibility of true revival. Discussing the Colonial Revival movement's failure to capture the spirit of early American architecture, he wrote:

> What we have built in the colonial mode is all very well in its way: unfortunately, it bears the same relation to the work of the late seventeenth and early eighteenth centuries that the Woolworth Building bears to the cathedrals of the Middle Age, or the patriotism of the National Security League to the principles of Franklin and Jefferson. [002-024]

The comparison is pointed: just as the Woolworth's Gothic ornament had no organic connection to medieval cathedral-building (which was an expression of communal faith and [[medieval-building-tradition|craft tradition]]), so the Colonial Revival's "photographic accuracy" had no connection to the living culture that produced the original colonial architecture. Both are technically accomplished simulations that miss the essential spirit of their models.
