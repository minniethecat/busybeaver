# Manhattan Island

Manhattan Island serves as a central cautionary example in Lewis Mumford's *Sticks and Stones* — a case study in how speculative land development, rather than deliberate planning, shaped American urban form. Mumford contrasts Manhattan's pattern of private land monopoly with the communal settlement patterns of Puritan New England.

## Colonial Origins: The Trader's Paradise

Mumford draws a sharp distinction between the Puritan method of settlement and the commercial foundation of Manhattan. "The advantage of the Puritan method of settlement comes out plainly when one contrasts it with the trader's paradise of Manhattan; for by the middle of the seventeenth century all the land on Manhattan Island was privately owned, although only a small part of it was cultivated, and so eagerly had the teeth of monopoly bitten into this fine morsel that there was already a housing-shortage" [001-009].

This early pattern of land speculation set a precedent that would shape the island's development for centuries. Unlike the New England town, where land was distributed according to community needs, Manhattan's land was treated as a commodity from the start.

## The 1811 Grid Plan

The city plan laid down for Manhattan Island in 1811 represented, in Mumford's view, the triumph of commercial expediency over thoughtful urban design. "Beginning its life by bargaining in necessities, the trading post ends by making a necessity of bargaining; and it was the impetus from its original commercial habits which determined the characteristics of the abortive city plan that was laid down for Manhattan Island in 1811" [001-015].

This grid plan — which imposed a uniform rectangular pattern across the island's varied topography — prioritized ease of land subdivision and sale over any consideration of natural features, public spaces, or civic beauty.

## Central Park and the Reform Impulse

The creation of Central Park represented a partial corrective to Manhattan's planning failures. However, Mumford notes that even this achievement was contested. "One of the reports for Manhattan, for example, devoted pages and pages to showing the improvement that would follow the demolition of the wall around Central Park — and the importance of clipped trees in the design of grand avenues!" [006-012] — a proposal that reveals the persistent tension between commercial development and public amenity.

By 1911, the inadequacy of park provision remained acute. "In the matter of public parks, for example, the Committee on Congestion in New York, in 1911, calculated that the park space needed for the East Side alone, on the scale provided by the city of Hartford, would be greater than the entire area of Manhattan Island" [006-028].

## The Skyline as Spectacle

Despite its planning failures, Mumford acknowledged the visual power of Manhattan's skyline. "When one approaches Manhattan Island, for instance, from the Staten Island Ferry or the Brooklyn Bridge, the great towers on the tip of the island sometimes look like the fairy stalagmites of an opened grotto; and from an occasional vantage point on the twentieth floor of an office building one may now and again recapture this impression" [007-026].

This aesthetic appreciation was qualified, however, by criticism of how the island's waterfront was developed "without the slightest regard for its potential facilities for recreation" [008-021].
