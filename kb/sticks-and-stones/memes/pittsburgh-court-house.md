## Overview

The Allegheny County Court House in Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania, is a monumental public building designed by architect Henry Hobson Richardson and completed in 1888. It is widely regarded as one of Richardson's greatest works and a definitive expression of the Richardsonian Romanesque style. The complex includes both the courthouse and the adjoining county jail, connected by a bridge modeled after the Bridge of Sighs in Venice.

## History

Richardson received the commission for the Allegheny County buildings in 1883 following a design competition. The project occupied the final years of his career. Construction began in 1884, and the buildings were completed in 1888, two years after Richardson's death in 1886. The courthouse replaced an earlier structure destroyed by fire and was intended to serve as a dignified civic centerpiece for a rapidly industrializing city.

## Architecture

The courthouse is constructed of rusticated pink Milford granite, with massive round-arched entrances, a distinctive tower rising from the center of the complex, and deeply recessed windows characteristic of the Romanesque style. Richardson's design integrates the courthouse and jail into a unified composition while giving each building its own architectural identity. The interior features a grand central courtyard and richly detailed stone carving executed by the craftsmen Richardson trained. The building's weighty, asymmetrical massing and expressive stonework represent a departure from the classical formalism that would soon dominate American civic architecture.

## Mumford's Assessment

Mumford regarded the Pittsburgh Court House and [[trinity-church-boston|Trinity Church]] as Richardson's two most ambitious architectural conceptions, naming them together as "the hugest of his architectural conceptions." Yet he was careful to distinguish scale from quality, noting that Richardson's smaller buildings demonstrated equal mastery:

> If the Pittsburgh Court House and Trinity Church stand out as the hugest of his architectural conceptions, it is the smaller buildings that test the skill and imagination of the master, and the public libraries at North Easton, Malden, and Quincy, Mass., and some of the little railway stations in Massachusetts stand on an equally high level. [(005-008)]

Mumford also used the completion of the Court House as a chronological anchor in his narrative of late-nineteenth-century American architecture. He positioned Richardson's death in 1886 -- immediately after finishing the Pittsburgh building -- as a pivotal moment, pairing it with the opening of the [[brooklyn-bridge|Brooklyn Bridge]] in 1884 to mark the simultaneous climax and closing of the romantic tradition:

> The Brooklyn Bridge was opened in 1884; Richardson died, after finishing the Pittsburgh Court House, in 1886. There was a short period during which the echoes of Richardson's style resounded in the work of the Western architects; and then in New York two of Richardson's own pupils, Messrs. McKim and White, who had caught the spirit of the period that was to follow the passing of the frontier, prepared an appropriate mold for its activities. [(005-028)]

For Mumford, Richardson's death immediately after completing the Court House carried symbolic weight: "Richardson had not died too soon. The quality of mind and culture which shines through his work was opposed to nearly every manifestation of the period that succeeded him." [(005-028)] The [[worlds-columbian-exposition-1893|World's Columbian Exposition]] of 1893, with its academic classicism, would inaugurate the [[gilded-age|imperial architecture]] that swept aside the Romanesque tradition Richardson had almost single-handedly established.

## Romanesque as Masonry's Last Stand

Mumford situated Richardson's Romanesque within a broader argument about the fate of masonry construction in the machine age. Richardson was, in Mumford's view, "the last of the great medieval line of master-masons," a builder whose allegiance to stone placed him in direct conflict with the technological trajectory of his era:

> Richardson was a mason, and masonry was being driven out by steel; he was an original artist, and original art was being thrust into the background by connoisseurship and collection; he was a builder, and architecture was committing itself more and more to the paper plan; he insisted upon building foursquare, and building was doomed more and more to *façaderie*. [(005-012)]

The very solidity that made the Court House monumental also made it, in an ironic sense, commercially unviable. Mumford observed that Richardson's massive stone structures resisted the speculative cycle of demolition and redevelopment that governed American cities:

> The very strength of Richardson's buildings was a fatal weakness in the growing centers of commerce and industry. It takes more than a little audacity to tear down one of Richardson's monuments, and so, rather ironically, they have held their own against the insurrections of traffic and realty speculation; but the difficulty of getting rid of these Romanesque structures only increased the demand for a more frail and facile method of construction. [(005-012)]

This argument placed the Pittsburgh Court House at the center of a structural turning point. The same year construction finished on Richardson's courthouse, 1888, Mumford declared that "masonry was defeated" -- the passenger elevator and steel-cage construction had made the [[skyscraper]] economically inevitable, and the massive stone piers demanded by the Romanesque idiom could not compete:

> Richardson's Marshall Field Building in Chicago was seven stories high; and that was about as far as solid stone or brick could climb without becoming undignified and futile by its bulk. The possibilities of masonry and the possibilities of commercial gain through ground-rents were at loggerheads, and by 1888 masonry was defeated. [(005-013)]

The Pittsburgh Court House thus stands as both a masterpiece and an elegy -- the supreme achievement of a building tradition that, within Richardson's own lifetime, was being rendered obsolete by the steel frame. Mumford saw in this not a failure of design but a failure of civilization to sustain the craft traditions and human scale that masonry architecture embodied.
