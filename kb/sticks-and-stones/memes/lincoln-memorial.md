## Overview

The Lincoln Memorial is a neoclassical monument on the National Mall in Washington, D.C., dedicated to President Abraham Lincoln. Designed by architect Henry Bacon, a pupil of Charles Follen McKim, it was completed in 1922. The memorial takes the form of a Greek Doric temple and stands at the western end of the National Mall, aligned with the Washington Monument and the United States Capitol.

## History

The memorial's placement was determined during the restoration of L'Enfant's original plan for Washington, which began in 1901 under the [[mcmillan-commission|McMillan Commission]]. The axis of the Mall was extended westward, passing through the Washington Monument, and the site of the Lincoln Memorial was assigned at the new western terminus. As Mumford notes, this was "the first of a whole series of temples devoted to the national deities" (006-025). Bacon's design was selected from among several proposals, and construction proceeded from 1914 to 1922. The memorial was dedicated on May 30, 1922.

## Architecture

The Lincoln Memorial is modeled after a Greek Doric temple, with 36 fluted columns representing the number of states in the Union at the time of Lincoln's death. The building is constructed primarily of Colorado Yule marble and Indiana limestone. Inside, a large seated statue of Lincoln by Daniel Chester French occupies the central chamber, flanked by inscriptions of the Gettysburg Address and Lincoln's Second Inaugural Address. The memorial's elevated position on a raised platform and its axial alignment with the Capitol and Washington Monument give it a commanding presence on the Mall.

## Mumford's Critique

Mumford is sharply critical of the Lincoln Memorial, seeing it not as an authentic expression of Lincoln's democratic spirit but as a hollow imperial monument:

> In the Lincoln Memorial, in the McKinley Memorial at Niles, Ohio, in the Hall of Fame at New York University, and in their prototype, Grant's Tomb, one feels not the living beauty of our American past, but the mortuary air of archaeology. The America that Lincoln was bred in, the homespun and humane and humorous America that he wished to preserve, has nothing in common with the sedulously classic monument that was erected to his memory. (006-025)

He presses the question further, asking whether the memorial truly honors Lincoln or the generation that commissioned it:

> Who lives in that shrine, I wonder — Lincoln, or the men who conceived it: the leader who beheld the mournful victory of the Civil War, or the generation that took pleasure in the mean triumph of the Spanish-American exploit, and placed the imperial standard in the Philippines and the Caribbean? (006-025)

## Architecture of Compensation

Mumford located the Lincoln Memorial within a broader category of "imperial architecture" that he described as an architecture of compensation — buildings that "provide grandiloquent stones for people who have been deprived of bread and sunlight and all that keeps man from becoming vile" (006-032). Behind the monumental façades of the imperial age, he argued, stood "a landless proletariat, doomed to the servile routine of the factory system," and beyond the great cities, "a countryside whose goods are drained away, whose children are uprooted from the soil" (006-032). This was not, Mumford insisted, "a casual observation: it is the translation of the last three census reports into plain English" (006-032).

The Lincoln Memorial was the first of a series of national temples in Washington, and for Mumford it exemplified the disparity between the democratic ideals of American society and the monumental, archaeology-derived forms chosen to represent them. He asks rhetorically: "Can one take the pretensions of this architecture seriously; can one worry about its esthetics or take full delight in such finer forms as Mr. Pope's Temple of the Scottish Rite in Washington, or Mr. Bacon's Lincoln Memorial? Yes, perhaps — if one refuses to look beyond the mask" (006-032).

## The Classical Form and Its Contradictions

Mumford's critique of the Lincoln Memorial belongs to his broader analysis of the neoclassical revival in American architecture. He saw in the choice of the Greek temple form a revealing contradiction. The temple was the preeminent architectural symbol of the ancient polis — a building type that expressed civic unity and shared religious purpose. But transplanted to the National Mall in twentieth-century Washington, the same form served a different function: it monumentalized not collective life but national power. The gap between the democratic content (Lincoln's legacy) and the imperial form (the Greek Doric temple) was, for Mumford, the memorial's central failure.

This critique was not directed at Bacon's skill as a designer — Mumford acknowledged the "finer forms" of Bacon's work (006-032). Rather, it was aimed at the entire cultural apparatus that insisted on clothing American institutions in borrowed classical garments. The Lincoln Memorial expressed, in stone, what Mumford saw as the fundamental weakness of the imperial age: its inability to generate architectural forms adequate to its own democratic ideals.
