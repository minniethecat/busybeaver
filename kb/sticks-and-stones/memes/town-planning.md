# Town Planning

## Overview

Town planning, for Mumford, is not a specialized branch of architecture but its essential precondition. "If we are to have a fine architecture, we must begin at the other end," he insists — "not with the building itself, but with the whole complex out of which architect, builder, and patron spring, and into which the finished building, whether it be a cottage or a skyscraper, is set" [(008-013)]. Throughout Sticks and Stones, Mumford argues that architecture cannot be understood or practiced in isolation from the urban framework in which buildings stand.

## Core Ideas

Mumford's conception of town planning rests on several interconnected ideas. First, that the physical form of the city shapes and is shaped by the social and economic order. The medieval town, with its integrated mix of dwelling, workshop, and marketplace, expressed a communal way of life; the modern industrial city, with its segregated zones of business, industry, and residence, expresses a disintegrated one.

Second, town planning must operate at the scale of the region, not just the individual site. Mumford consistently argues for planning that integrates housing, transportation, industry, and open space into a coherent whole. The alternative is a patchwork of palliative measures: "where we eliminate sunlight we introduce electric light; where we congest business, we build skyscrapers; where we overcrowd the thoroughfares with traffic we burrow subways" [(007-015)].

Third, effective town planning requires collective land ownership or control. In discussing the garden city movement, Mumford notes that "the essential elements in a garden-city are the common holding of land by the community, and the cooperative ownership and direction of the economic life of the city" [(001-027)]. Without this, private [[speculative-building|land speculation]] constantly undermines planning efforts.

## Historical Context

The need for town planning as a conscious discipline arose from the disorder of the industrial city. In the medieval period, Mumford argues, town form emerged organically from the needs of community life; no separate discipline of "planning" was needed because the town was a direct expression of its social order. The [[renaissance-architecture|Renaissance]] began the process of separating civic design from everyday building, and the industrial revolution completed it.

By the late 19th century, the consequences of unplanned urban growth were impossible to ignore: overcrowded tenements, polluted air and water, inadequate light and ventilation, and the destruction of natural amenities. The town planning movement emerged as a response — an attempt to bring conscious collective intelligence to bear on the form of human settlement.

## Key Figures

- **Ebenezer Howard** (1850–1928): Author of Garden Cities of To-morrow (1898), whose vision of limited-size cities surrounded by agricultural greenbelts deeply influenced Mumford's own thinking.
- **Frederick Law Olmsted** (1822–1903): Landscape architect of Central Park and Riverside, Illinois, who demonstrated that large-scale planning could create public amenities of enduring value.
- **[[patrick-geddes|Patrick Geddes]]** (1854–1932): Scottish biologist and town planner whose concept of the "valley section" and [[regional-planning|regional survey]] method shaped Mumford's regional planning philosophy.
- **Henry [[frank-lloyd-wright|Wright]]** (1878–1936): American planner whose economic analyses Mumford cites on the shifting cost structure of building [(007-012)].

## Key Examples

- **Central Park**, [[new-york-city|New York]] (Olmsted & Vaux, 1858): The first major American work of landscape planning, demonstrating the public value of reserved open space in a growing city.
- **Riverside**, Illinois (Olmsted, 1869): A planned suburban community that attempted to reconcile urban convenience with natural landscape.
- **Letchworth [[garden-city|Garden City]]**, England (1903): The first built expression of Howard's garden city idea, combining planned industry, housing, and agriculture.

## In the Text

Mumford's treatment of town planning runs as a counterpoint to his architectural narrative. Each chapter traces not only the evolution of building forms but the transformation of the urban context in which they appeared. The medieval town, the [[renaissance-architecture|Renaissance]] city, the industrial mill town, the imperial metropolis, the machine-age business district — each represents a different relationship between architecture and community.

His critique of modern planning is that it has been reactive rather than formative. Planners have been called in to mitigate the damage done by unplanned growth, rather than to shape growth from the start. "The processes which are inimical to architecture are, perhaps, seen at their worst in the business district of the metropolis; but more and more they tend to spread throughout the rest of the community" [(007-006)].

Mumford's vision of positive town planning is encapsulated in the garden city ideal, but he is clear that the garden city is not merely a physical plan. It requires a transformation of land tenure, economic organization, and community governance. Planning, in his view, is ultimately a political and moral enterprise — the art of creating the physical conditions for a good life.

## Legacy

Town planning as a profession expanded enormously in the 20th century, with mixed results. Zoning, master plans, and building codes have prevented the worst excesses of unregulated development, but they have rarely achieved Mumford's vision of integrated [[regional-planning|regional design]]. The garden city movement influenced the British New Towns program and American greenbelt towns, though these often fell short of Howard's cooperative economic model.

Mumford's insistence that architecture and planning are inseparable, that individual buildings can only be as good as the urban framework they inhabit, remains a challenge to both professions. Contemporary movements such as New Urbanism, transit-oriented development, and ecological urbanism all draw, directly or indirectly, on the tradition of thought Mumford articulates.
