## Overview

Palladianism was, in Lewis Mumford's architectural genealogy, the single most influential classical tradition in the Anglo-American world. Originating in the designs and treatise of the 16th-century Italian architect [[andrea-palladio|Andrea Palladio]], the style was transmitted to England through Inigo Jones in the early 17th century, reached its apotheosis under Lord Burlington in the 18th, and crossed the Atlantic to shape the domestic and civic architecture of colonial America and the early Republic.

Mumford traces this transmission with precision: "First it is Palladio and Vignola; then it is Burlington and Chambers; then, after the middle of the eighteenth century, the brothers Adam and Stuart's *Antiquities of Athens*" [002-013]. Palladianism was, in this sense, the founding link in the chain of architectural classicism that dominated Western building until the rise of the [[modern-movement|modern movement]].

## The Palladian Villa

The Palladian villa was the building type that proved most influential in America, adapted to the conditions of the southern plantation and, later, the country estates of the [[gilded-age|Gilded Age]]. Mumford describes the scale on which Palladian mansions were built:

> Pliny the Younger might well have been proud of such an estate. On a substantial basis like this, a Palladian mansion was possible; and up and down the land, wherever the means justified the end, Palladian mansions were built.

[003-005]

The Virginia planter Robert Carter, who "at his death was described as the possessor of an estate of 300,000 acres of land, about 1,000 slaves, and ten thousand pounds," was typical of the class that patronized Palladian architecture in America. The parallel between Roman latifundia and Virginia plantations was, Mumford notes, not lost on the architects or their clients.

## Jefferson and the Republican Villa

[[thomas-jefferson|Thomas Jefferson]]'s adaptation of Palladian principles at [[monticello|Monticello]] and the [[university-of-virginia|University of Virginia]] represents, for Mumford, the most sophisticated American appropriation of the Palladian tradition. Jefferson, who owned a copy of Palladio's *Quattro Libri*, translated the Veneto villa type to the Virginia Piedmont, substituting the slave quarters of Mulberry Row for the farm buildings of the Venetian countryside.

Mumford's treatment of Jeffersonian Palladianism is characteristically double-edged. He admires the architectural literacy and formal sophistication of Monticello, but he notes the uncomfortable congruence between Palladio's villa economy — based on the agricultural surplus of the Veneto — and Jefferson's, based on enslaved labor.

## The Pattern Book Tradition

Palladianism was disseminated in America less through direct study of Palladio's treatise than through the intermediary of English pattern books. Builders and carpenters across the colonies reproduced Palladian motifs — the temple-front portico, the Venetian window, the symmetrical plan — from engraved plates that simplified and standardized the originals.

This pattern-book Palladianism was, in Mumford's analysis, both the strength and the weakness of the American classical tradition. It made architectural literacy widely available, enabling a carpenter in [[salem-massachusetts|Salem]] or a builder in [[charleston-architecture|Charleston]] to produce buildings of genuine dignity. But it also reduced Palladio's subtle proportioning system to a set of reproducible formulas — a "mere matter of grammatical accuracy and pronunciation" [002-012] rather than genuine architectural thought.

## Mumford's Assessment

Mumford's final judgment on Palladianism is measured. The style provided American architecture with a language of classical dignity that it would not otherwise have possessed. It enabled Jefferson to give architectural form to republican ideals, and it gave the colonial gentry a mode of building adequate to their ambitions. But it was, ultimately, a borrowed language — a set of forms imported from a very different climate, society, and building tradition. The architecture that Mumford most admired — the indigenous craft tradition of the colonial house, the tectonic power of [[richardsonian-romanesque|Richardsonian Romanesque]] — was precisely the architecture that stood outside the Palladian tradition.
