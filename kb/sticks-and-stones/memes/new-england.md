# New England

New England occupies a foundational position in Lewis Mumford's *Sticks and Stones* as the primary American embodiment of the medieval building tradition. Mumford traces the region's architectural character to its colonial village pattern, where community institutions — particularly the meetinghouse — shaped both physical form and social life.

## The Medieval Tradition in America

For Mumford, the New England village represents the purest survival of medieval communal building practices in the United States. Unlike the speculative development that later characterized American cities, the early New England settlement grew organically around shared institutions.

> The capital example of the medieval tradition lies in the New England village. [001-003]

The meetinghouse served as both religious and civic center, determining "the character and limits of the community" [001-005]. This integration of spiritual and practical functions echoed the medieval European pattern that Mumford admired throughout his career.

## Land and Community

Mumford contrasts the New England land system favorably with contemporary English practice, noting that "whereas in England the common lands were being confiscated for the benefit of an aristocracy," [001-004] the New England town preserved common holdings. All inhabitants "were copartners in the village" [001-006], sharing both responsibilities and benefits of communal land ownership.

## The Carpenter-Builder and Craftsmanship Tradition

The architecture of early New England was not the work of professional architects but of guild-trained [[carpenter-builder|carpenter-builders]] who brought the medieval craft tradition to American soil. Mumford emphasizes that the quality of colonial building derived from a social system in which the craftsman worked not merely for payment but as an expression of communal life.

> Schooled in the traditions of his guild, the medieval carpenter pours his all into the work. Since sale does not enter into the bargain, it is both to his patron's advantage to give him the best materials, and to his own advantage to make the most of them. [001-020]

The carpenter-builder adapted English building traditions to the harsher New England climate. Half-timbered houses of early settlers were covered with clapboards for greater warmth, and interiors were eventually lined with panelled pine or oak. The [[craftsmanship|craft tradition]] was both practical and inventive: the gambrel roof, "which gave additional room to the second story without raising the rooftree, is a product of this system; and the variation in its length and pitch in New England, New Jersey, and New York is a witness to the freedom of design that prevailed throughout the work" [001-020].

This system produced architecture of organic growth rather than formal design. The John Ward house in Salem exemplified the dynamic quality Mumford admired: "the house has developed as the family within it has prospered, and brought forth children; as sons and daughters have married, as children have become more numerous, there have been additions: by a lean-to at one end the kitchen has achieved a separate existence" [001-022]. Each step toward the building altered the relation of planes formed by the gable ends, producing an effect Mumford compared to walking around [[notre-dame|Notre Dame in Paris]] — "the building seems in motion, as well as the spectator; and this quality delights the eye quite as much as formal decoration" [001-023].

The Puritan suspicion of visual ornament was, in Mumford's view, the one significant limitation of this building culture. "Whereas medieval architecture had cherished the sculptor and the painter, even in the commonest vernacular work, the Puritans looked upon every diversion of the eye as a diversion from the Lord" [001-024]. By separating the artist from the useful arts, Puritanism inadvertently "paved the way for the finicking graces of the eighteenth" [001-024] — the very decorative excesses the Puritans had sought to avoid.

## The Village as Landscape: Nature and Community Planning

Mumford devotes some of his most lyrical prose to the physical arrangement of the New England village, which he regarded as an integrated whole in which buildings, trees, and land formed a single composition. The village was not merely a collection of houses but a designed environment in which natural and built elements reinforced each other.

> Consider the village itself. In the center is a common, a little to one side will be the meetinghouse, perhaps a square barnlike structure, with a hipped roof and a cupola, like that at Hingham; and adjacent or across the way will be the grammar school. Along the roads where the houses are set at regular intervals is a great columnar arcade of elm trees. [001-026]

The trees were not ornamental afterthoughts but essential architectural elements. Mumford writes that "the trees, above all, are an important part of New England architecture: in summer they absorb the moisture and cool the air, besides giving shade; in the winter their huge boles serve as a partial windbrake" [001-026]. The elm-lined village streets and the apple trees clinging to the warmer side of each house created what Mumford called "a more complete and intelligent partnership between the earth and man than existed, for a little while, in the old New England village" [001-026].

This integration of landscape and settlement led Mumford to an arresting comparison: the New England village was, in effect, an early [[garden-city|garden city]]. Long before [[ebenezer-howard|Ebenezer Howard]] formulated the garden city concept, the Puritan settlers had created communities that embodied its essential principles — common holding of land, cooperative ownership, and the integration of dwelling with cultivated nature. "Nowadays we have begun to talk about garden cities," Mumford observes, "but the fact of the matter is that the New England village up to the middle of the eighteenth century was a garden-city in every sense that we now apply to that term" [001-027].

The village form was also a product of genuine community planning, though it was not called by that name. The irregularity that superficial observers attributed to "the vagaries of the straying cow" [001-017] was in fact a rational response to practical needs: "the inequality in size and shape of plots shows always that attention was paid to the function the land was to perform, rather than to the mere possession of property" [001-017]. A 1635 decree of the Massachusetts General Court required that no dwelling be placed more than half a mile from the meetinghouse, ensuring compactness. Variations in house placement often reflected attention to wind, sun exposure, and views — principles of [[vernacular-architecture|vernacular design]] that professional architects would later systematize.

For Mumford, this tradition held lessons for modern [[regional-planning|regional planning]]. The medieval inheritance of New England was "a much more substantial matter than the building of perpendicular churches or Tudor country-houses in painfully archaeological adaptations" [001-028]. To recover what was valuable in the colonial tradition required more than copying architectural forms — it required recovering "the interests, the standards, the institutions that gave to the villages and buildings of early times their appropriate shapes" [001-028].

## Legacy and Influence

The New England village model influenced later American thinkers, including [[benton-mackaye|Benton MacKaye]], whose regional planning philosophy drew on the communal patterns Mumford identified. The [[garden-city|Garden City]] movement, as promoted by [[ebenezer-howard|Ebenezer Howard]], represented a twentieth-century attempt to revive similar principles of community-centered design.
