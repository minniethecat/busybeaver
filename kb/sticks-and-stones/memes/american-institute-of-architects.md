## Overview

The American Institute of Architects (AIA) is the primary professional organization for architects in the United States, founded in 1857.

In Lewis Mumford's *Sticks and Stones* (1924), the AIA appears not as a subject of architectural history but as a crucial institutional source: its *Journal* provided Mumford with economic data, urban analysis, and a platform for his own writing, while its committee reports articulated the tension between architectural ideals and the commercial realities of American building.

## Role in Sticks and Stones

Mumford's relationship to the AIA was practical and intellectual. The AIA's publications served him in three distinct ways:

**As a publishing venue.** Mumford acknowledged that portions of *Sticks and Stones* first appeared in the *Journal of the American Institute of Architects*, edited by Charles Harris Whitaker, whose "admirable public work" Mumford praised in his acknowledgments [P03-001].

Chapter Six of the book was among the material published there.

**As a source of economic data.** Mumford drew on AIA publications for quantitative evidence about the changing economics of building.

Henry [[frank-lloyd-wright|Wright]]'s estimate of housing costs — showing that by 1920, site and mechanical equipment consumed nearly half the total cost of a house, compared to less than 10 percent in 1800 — came from the *Journal*:

> According to an estimate by Mr. Henry Wright in the *Journal of the American Institute of Architects*, the structure of the dwelling house represented over ninety percent of the cost in 1800. [008-015]

Similarly, Raymond Unwin's calculation of street widths needed to accommodate skyscrapers (241 feet for the [[chicago-school-city|Chicago]] Loop at twenty stories) appeared in the same *Journal* [008-024].

**As a source of reformist thought.** The AIA's Committee on Community Planning report (1924) provided Mumford with a definition of community planning that encapsulated his own position — beginning not from the needs of the existing metropolis but from the question "how big must a city be to perform all of its social, educational, and industrial functions" [008-060].

## Critique of the Profession

Mumford's references to the AIA were not uniformly admiring.

When discussing the AIA's Educational Committee, which sought to "educate public taste in the arts," Mumford was dismissive, grouping the effort alongside [[louis-sullivan|Louis Sullivan]]'s democratic rhetoric and the modernists' embrace of the machine as inadequate responses to the systemic problems facing architecture:

> it is not sufficient, with Mr. Louis Sullivan, to say that we must accept and enthrone the virtues of democracy; still less is there any meaning in the attempt of the Educational Committee of the American Institute of Architects to educate public taste in the arts. [008-040]

For Mumford, the problem was not public ignorance but the economic and institutional framework within which architecture operated. Taste-education was a superficial remedy for a structural condition.
