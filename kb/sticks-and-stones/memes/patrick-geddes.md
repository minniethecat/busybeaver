# Patrick Geddes

## Overview

Patrick Geddes (1854–1932) was the Scottish thinker whose synthesis of biology, sociology, and town planning provided the intellectual framework for the regional planning movement. Mumford was his most influential American disciple, and Geddes's method — the [[regional-planning|regional survey]], the attention to the relation between occupation and settlement, the conviction that planning must proceed from understanding rather than from aesthetic preference — runs through the entire argument of Sticks and Stones. Geddes taught that cities could not be understood in isolation from their regions, and that architecture could not be renewed without renewing the civilization that produced it.

## Biography

Geddes trained as a biologist under T. H. Huxley in London before turning his attention to human communities. His early work on the evolution of sex prepared him to think about human settlement in ecological terms — as the product of interactions between organisms and their environment. He established the Outlook Tower in Edinburgh as a laboratory for urban observation, and his work on the planning of Edinburgh's Old Town demonstrated that the methods of biological survey could be applied to cities.

## Major Works

- **Cities in Evolution** (1915): Geddes's major theoretical work, arguing that cities must be understood as products of their regions — their geology, climate, vegetation, and economic life — rather than as isolated architectural compositions. Mumford listed this work as essential sociological background for *Sticks and Stones* (015-002).
- **The Valley Section**: Geddes's diagnostic diagram, tracing human occupations from the miner and woodman at the head of the valley through the shepherd, peasant, and fisherman to the merchant and financier at the port. The valley section showed that settlement patterns followed the logic of work and resources.
- **The Outlook Tower**, Edinburgh (1892): A museum of urban observation, where visitors ascended from exhibits on geology and biology at the base to a camera obscura and panoramic view of the city and region at the top — a physical embodiment of Geddes's method of proceeding from region to city to neighborhood to building.

## Architectural Philosophy

Geddes's contribution to architectural thought was not formal but methodological. He insisted that architecture must be understood in its regional and ecological context — that the form of a building was shaped by the climate, materials, and economic activities of its region before it was shaped by the intentions of its architect. Mumford's entire argument — that architecture reflects civilization, and that the condition of building is the condition of society — is a Geddesian argument.

## Influence and Legacy

Geddes's influence ran primarily through his disciples: Mumford in America, the regional planners of the RPAA, and the British town planning movement. His method — survey before plan, understand before act — became a fundamental principle of planning education. Mumford's own career as a critic of architecture and cities was a sustained application of Geddes's method to the American scene.

Mumford drew directly on Geddes's distinction between the paleotechnic order (steam-powered, centralized, extractive) and the neotechnic order (electric, dispersed, humane), seeing in the electric motor and the small countryside factory the possibility of restoring the craft-scale production and regional integration that steam-powered industrialism had destroyed (008-034). The objectives of regional planning, Mumford noted, had been anticipated by the conservation movement of the nineteenth century, whose scattered achievements represented the rudiments of the comprehensive regional approach that Geddes had articulated in theoretical form (008-027).
