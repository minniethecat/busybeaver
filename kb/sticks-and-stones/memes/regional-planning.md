# Regional Planning

## Overview

Regional planning extends the principles of town planning to the scale of the region — the natural geographic unit within which cities, towns, farms, forests, and watersheds form an interdependent system. For Mumford, regional planning is not merely an administrative technique but the necessary foundation for any genuine renewal of architecture and community life. It is the art of seeing "the interdependence of city and country" and acting on that understanding [(008-024)].

## Core Ideas

Mumford's conception of regional planning is built on the ecological insight that city and countryside are not opposites but complementary parts of a single system. "To see the interdependence of city and country, to realize that the growth and concentration of one is associated with the depletion and impoverishment of the other, to appreciate that the building of a city is at the same time the un-building of a countryside" — this is the foundational perception of regional planning [(008-024)].

From this follows a practical program: the rational distribution of population, industry, and agriculture across the region; the conservation of natural resources (watersheds, forests, agricultural land); the development of transportation and power networks that serve regional integration rather than metropolitan concentration; and the establishment of institutional frameworks for regional governance.

Mumford draws on the concrete example of Ontario, Canada, where "the social utilization of waterpower has directly benefited the rural communities" [(008-026)]. He also notes that the objectives of regional planning were anticipated by the conservation movement of the 19th century, though without the institutional mechanisms to implement them at regional scale [(008-027)].

## Historical Context

The regional planning movement emerged in the early 20th century as a response to the destructive effects of unplanned metropolitan growth. [[patrick-geddes|Patrick Geddes]], the Scottish biologist and town planner, was its intellectual founder; his "valley section" — a diagram tracing human settlement from mountain headwaters to coastal port — provided the conceptual tool for thinking about human communities in ecological terms.

In America, the Regional Planning Association of America (RPAA), founded in 1923 and including Mumford among its members, adapted Geddes's ideas to the American context. The RPAA advocated for [[garden-city|garden cities]], regional power authorities, and the conservation of natural resources within a framework of regional planning.

## Key Figures

- **[[patrick-geddes|Patrick Geddes]]** (1854–1932): Scottish biologist, sociologist, and town planner whose concept of the regional survey and the conurbation provided the intellectual foundations of the regional planning movement.
- **Benton MacKaye** (1879–1975): American forester and planner whose proposal for the Appalachian Trail was a work of regional planning — a continuous wild corridor along the Appalachian ridge, conceived as a counterbalance to metropolitan concentration.
- **Lewis Mumford** (1895–1990): Author of Sticks and Stones and, through his involvement with the RPAA and his writings, the most influential American advocate for regional planning as the framework for architecture and community life.

## Key Examples

- **Tennessee Valley Authority** (1933): The most ambitious American experiment in regional planning — a federal authority that integrated flood control, power generation, agricultural improvement, and community development across a seven-state region.
- **Ontario Hydro**: A Canadian example Mumford cites approvingly, where the "social utilization of waterpower has directly benefited the rural communities, and prevented the congestion of the great cities at their expense" [(008-026)].
- **The Appalachian Trail**: MacKaye's proposal for a wilderness corridor — a work of regional planning that treated open space and recreation as integral to the regional system.

## In the Text

Mumford's advocacy for regional planning appears most explicitly in Chapter VIII, "Architecture and Civilization," where he argues that "if we are to have a fine architecture, we must begin at the other end from that where our sumptuously illustrated magazines on home-building and architecture begin — not with the building itself, but with the whole complex out of which architect, builder, and patron spring" [(008-013)].

This is the culminating argument of the book. Architecture, Mumford insists, cannot be renewed from within — by better architects, better materials, or better styles. It can only be renewed as part of a larger transformation of civilization: "Home, meeting-place, and factory; polity, culture, and art have still to be united and wrought together, and this task is one of the fundamental tasks of our civilization" [(008-064)].

Regional planning, in this vision, is the practical mechanism for achieving that integration. It is the art of creating the physical and institutional framework within which a living architecture can once again flourish.

## Legacy

Regional planning as a formal discipline has had a mixed record. The RPAA's vision was only partially realized through the New Deal (TVA, greenbelt towns) and largely abandoned in the postwar period of highway-driven suburbanization. Yet the core ideas have proved enduring: environmental impact assessment, growth management, watershed planning, and the "smart growth" movement all draw on the regional planning tradition.

Mumford's insistence that architecture cannot be separated from its regional context — that the quality of buildings depends on the quality of the settlements they belong to — remains a challenge to both professions. In an era of climate change and global urbanization, the regional scale at which Mumford thought may be more relevant than ever.
