# Churches & Religious Architecture

Religious buildings — churches, cathedrals, and chapels — have been among the most architecturally ambitious projects in every era. In *Sticks and Stones*, Mumford traces ecclesiastical architecture from the transplanted medieval tradition through the Gothic Revival's moral crusade, to the monumental urban churches of the Gilded Age. This page catalogs the religious structures and movements discussed in the book.

## Gothic Revival Churches

The Gothic Revival was more than a stylistic preference — it was a moral argument. Led by Richard Upjohn and the Ecclesiological Society, the movement insisted that Gothic architecture was the only fitting expression of Christian worship. Mumford observes that this medievalist impulse was also a reaction against industrial society, a reaching backward toward organic community.

## Religious Architecture & the Modern Era

As the twentieth century advanced, church architecture moved away from historical revivalism. Modern religious buildings sought new forms — simplified, functional, and integrated with community life. The Ecclesiastical tradition gave way to experiments in form and material.
