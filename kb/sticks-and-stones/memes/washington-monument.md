## Overview

The Washington Monument appears in Lewis Mumford's *Sticks and Stones* at a critical juncture — the reorganization of the [[washington-dc|Washington, D.C.]] plan that began in 1901 under the [[mcmillan-commission|McMillan Commission]]. Mumford notes that "in the restoration of the original plan of Washington, which began in 1901, the axis of the plan was so altered as to make it pass through the Washington Monument; and at the same time the place of the [[lincoln-memorial|Lincoln Memorial]] was assigned" [(006-025)].

This brief reference belies the Monument's significance in the larger story Mumford tells. The Washington Monument — an unadorned Egyptian obelisk, the tallest stone structure in the world at its completion — was the spatial anchor around which the [[beaux-arts|Beaux-Arts]] transformation of Washington was organized. It was the fixed point in a classical composition that extended from the Capitol, along the newly created Mall, past the Monument, across the planned Memorial Bridge, to the site of the future Lincoln Memorial.

## L'Enfant's Vision and Its Restoration

The McMillan Commission's plan was, in Mumford's telling, a restoration as much as a creation. [[pierre-charles-lenfant|Pierre Charles L'Enfant]]'s original 1791 plan for Washington had envisioned a grand axial composition connecting the Capitol and the President's House with broad avenues, public squares, and monumental buildings. But L'Enfant's plan had been compromised by piecemeal development over the course of the 19th century.

The McMillan Commission, under the leadership of [[daniel-burnham|Daniel Burnham]], Charles McKim, and Frederick Law Olmsted Jr., restored and extended L'Enfant's vision. The axis was shifted to pass through the Washington Monument, which had been completed in 1884 after decades of intermittent construction. The Mall was cleared of the railroad tracks, market sheds, and informal plantings that had encroached upon it. And the sites for the Lincoln Memorial and the Memorial Bridge were fixed, establishing the monumental core of the capital as it exists today.

## The Obelisk as Symbol

The choice of the obelisk form for the Washington Monument was itself significant. The obelisk — an ancient Egyptian form, adopted by the Romans and revived by the Renaissance — was the purest expression of classical monumentality. It was abstract, geometric, and free of the stylistic associations that attached to columned temples and domed rotundas. It could be appropriated by any classical tradition without contradiction.

For Mumford, the Washington Monument's abstraction was both its strength and its limitation. It was a powerful urban marker — a point of orientation visible from miles away. But it was also, in a sense, empty — a form that signified monumentality without specifying what it commemorated. The content of the Monument was supplied by its location, its surroundings, and the civic rituals performed in its shadow.

## Mumford's Assessment

Mumford's discussion of the Washington Monument and the McMillan Plan is characteristically ambivalent. He acknowledges the formal achievement of the restored Mall — its dignity, its clarity, its power as a work of civic art. But he questions whether the classical language of the plan was adequate to the democratic purposes it was meant to serve.

The Washington Monument, in this reading, is a monument to classical form as much as to Washington — a demonstration that American architecture, at the highest levels of civic patronage, remained committed to the Roman precedent that Mumford had traced throughout the book.
