# Notre-Dame de Paris

Notre-Dame de Paris appears in Lewis Mumford's *Sticks and Stones* not as a work of architecture to be analyzed on its own terms, but through the lens of Victor Hugo's famous argument about architecture and the printed word. Mumford uses the cathedral to illustrate a broader thesis about the changing role of building in human civilization.

## Architecture as the Stone Record

Mumford invokes Hugo's declaration that "the printing-press destroyed architecture, which had hitherto been the stone record of mankind" [002-012]. This thesis — from Hugo's novel *Notre-Dame de Paris* (1831), specifically the chapter "This Will Kill That" — held that before the printing press, architecture was the primary medium through which humanity expressed and transmitted its collective knowledge, beliefs, and aspirations. The book, in Hugo's view, supplanted the building.

Mumford draws on this idea in his own narrative of architectural history, tracing how the shared symbolic language embodied in medieval cathedrals gave way to the fragmented communication of the printed page — a transformation that paralleled, and partly caused, the decline of a unified architectural culture.

## Spatial Experience

Beyond the literary reference, Mumford uses Notre-Dame to illustrate a principle of architectural experience: that a building's form cannot be grasped from a single viewpoint. "Every step that brings one nearer to the house alters the relation of the planes formed by the gable ends; and so one must have got the same effect in these old village streets as one gets today when one skirts around, let us say, Notre Dame in Paris, now overwhelmed by the towers at the front, and now seeing them reduced to nothing by the tall spire in the rear" [001-023].

This description — comparing the sequential spatial experience of a medieval village street to the shifting perspectives around the cathedral — underscores Mumford's conviction that architecture must be understood as an art of movement through space, not merely of static composition.

## The Cathedral and the Book

Mumford's use of Hugo's thesis points to a deeper transformation in the relationship between architecture and culture. The Renaissance, Mumford argues, did not merely substitute one style for another — it restructured the very process by which architectural knowledge was created and transmitted. The master mason who "knew his stone and his workmen and his tools and the tradition of his art gave way to the architect who knew his Palladio and his Vignola and his Vitruvius" [002-012].

The consequences extended beyond the architect's study into the status of Gothic forms themselves. Where the medieval cathedral had been the collective expression of a city's spiritual and civic life, the Renaissance imposed a classical grammar that treated the pointed arch as an error. Mumford captures this reversal with precision: "to build with a pointed arch was barbarous, to build with disregard for formal symmetry was barbarous, to permit the common workman to carry out his individual taste in carving was to risk vulgarity and pander to an obsolete sense of democracy" [002-012].

This revaluation of the Gothic had direct consequences for how the cathedral was understood by later centuries. The eighteenth-century Enlightenment and the early American republic dismissed medieval architecture wholesale. Mumford notes that "radicalism" associated itself with the worship of rule and reason, "as opposed to the caprice, the irrationality, the brute traditionalism of what the children of that age then characterized as 'Gothic superstition'" [003-011]. The Gothic cathedral — and Notre-Dame as its supreme example — stood on the wrong side of this intellectual divide for more than two centuries.

## The Cathedral as Craft

The cathedral also serves Mumford as an emblem of the medieval craft tradition from which modern architecture has become estranged. In his discussion of handicraft esthetics in the final chapter, Mumford reflects on how the overwhelming intricacy of a Gothic facade — its "myopic intricacy of the pattern" — can obscure for the modern viewer what is most vital about it. "Those of us who have become acclimated to industrialism sometimes find the effulgence and profusion of craftsmanship a little bewildering: but if our enjoyment of the portals of a medieval cathedral or the facade of an East Indian house is dulled by the myopic intricacy of the pattern, our appreciation of the craftsman's fun and interest should be heightened. Granting that art is an end in itself, is it not an end to the worker as well as the spectator? A great part of craftsmanship needs no other justification than that it bears the mark of a joyous spirit" [008-039].

This view of the cathedral — not as an iconographic puzzle to be decoded but as the deposit of countless individual acts of joyful labor — connects Notre-Dame to the central argument of *Sticks and Stones*: that architecture flourishes when the worker pours into the work a "vital superfluity" beyond bare function, and withers when labor becomes mere toil.

Mumford further contrasts the medieval cathedral with the buildings of classical revivalism to make a point about architectural differentiation. "In medieval building the town hall is a different sort of building from the cathedral: using the same elements, perhaps, it nevertheless contrives an altogether different effect" [003-017]. The Gothic builder could produce both civic and sacred architecture from a shared vocabulary of craft without collapsing their distinct identities — a capacity that the classical architects of the early American republic, in Mumford's judgment, largely lost.

The distance between the cathedral as a living craft tradition and its modern imitations is illustrated with particular sharpness in Mumford's comparison of the Woolworth Building to "the cathedrals of the Middle Age" [002-024]. The Gothic skyscraper, however accomplished as a commercial landmark, lacked everything organic about the medieval original — the communal faith, the guild organization, and the craftsman's personal investment that made the stone record possible.
