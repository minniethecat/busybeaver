## Overview

The original Pennsylvania Station in [[new-york-city|New York City]], completed in 1910 to the designs of [[charles-mckim|McKim]], Mead & White, was one of the defining architectural achievements of America's imperial age. In Lewis Mumford's *Sticks and Stones*, the station appears as the quintessential expression of the period's marriage of Roman monumentality with modern transportation — a building whose classical grandeur served both practical circulation and imperial symbolism.

## Architecture

The station was conceived on a Roman scale, its main waiting room modeled on the Baths of Caracalla. Pink Milford granite and travertine clad a steel-frame structure whose vast concourse was spanned by arched glass and steel vaults. The design represented the fullest American expression of the [[beaux-arts|Beaux-Arts]] principle that modern infrastructure deserved monumental treatment.

Mumford praised the station's most functionally honest element:

> Esthetically considered, it is true, perhaps, that the finest element in the Pennsylvania station is the train hall, where the architect has dealt sincerely with his steel elements and has not permitted himself to cast a fond, retrospective eye upon the Roman baths. [007-024]

This acknowledgment of structural honesty within a classical envelope captures the tension at the heart of [[beaux-arts|Beaux-Arts]] railroad architecture: the engineering was modern, but its architectural expression looked backward.

## Historical Context

Pennsylvania Station was built at the height of the imperial phase Mumford identifies in American architecture (roughly 1890–1910). The railroad station, he argued, was the period's characteristic building type — a monument to centralization, long-distance trade, and metropolitan dominance:

> It was no accident that the great triumphs of American architecture during the imperial period were the railroad stations; particularly the Pennsylvania and the Grand Central in New York, and the Union Station in Washington. [007-024]

The station was designed by [[charles-mckim|Charles McKim]], co-founder of the [[american-academy-in-rome|American Academy in Rome]] and one of the architects who "worshiped most wholeheartedly at the imperial shrine." McKim's commitment to the monumental conception was uncompromising: Mumford noted that "Mr. Charles McKim, for example, indignantly withdrew from the competition over the New York Public Library because the demands of the librarian for a convenient and expeditious administration of his business interfered with the full-blown conception which Mr. McKim had in mind" (006-023). The same imperious approach to architectural form — the priority of the monumental ensemble over functional accommodation — shaped Pennsylvania Station.

For Mumford, the station embodied the paradox of imperial architecture: technically accomplished, aesthetically impressive, yet fundamentally an instrument of metropolitan exploitation. The Beaux-Arts monuments of the period, magnificent in themselves, had become "wholly divorced from [their] social and economic foundations" (007-006).

> Under this rule, all roads lead literally to Rome. While, as the German historian, W. H. Riehl, points out, the provincial highroads served to bring the city out into the countryside, the railroads served to bring the major cities together and to drain the products of rural regions into the metropolis. [007-024]

## Demolition

The original station was demolished in 1963–1966 to make way for the current Madison Square Garden and Penn Station complex — an act widely regarded as one of the greatest architectural losses in American history and a catalyst for the historic preservation movement.
