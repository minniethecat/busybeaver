# Baron Georges-Eugène Haussmann

Georges-Eugène Haussmann (1809–1891) was the Prefect of the Seine under Emperor Napoleon III, responsible for the most dramatic urban transformation of the nineteenth century: the reconstruction of Paris. Between 1853 and 1870, Haussmann demolished medieval quarters, cut broad boulevards through the city's dense fabric, standardized building facades, and created the park system that defines modern Paris.

## The Haussmann Precedent

Mumford places Haussmann within a long lineage of imperial city-making that stretches back to ancient Rome:

> "The Roman precedent, modified by the work of Louis XIV and Napoleon III, by Le Nôtre and Haussmann, formed the basis not merely for the World's Fair, but for the host of city plans that were produced in the two decades that followed" [(005-070)].

This genealogy links military parade grounds, royal gardens, imperial boulevards, and finally the civic centers and parkways of American cities. Mumford notes how this sequence seemed to promise the architect a new role:

> "It seemed for a while as if the architect might take the place of the engineer as city planner, and that the mangled regularity of the engineer's gridiron plan, laid down without respect to topographic advantage or to use, might be definitely supplanted in the remodeled central districts and in the new extensions and suburbs of the American city" [(005-070)].

## Critique

Mumford's assessment of Haussmann's legacy is deeply ambivalent. While acknowledging the grandeur of the Parisian boulevards, he notes that the reconstruction came at a steep social cost:

> "The same process that creates an unearned increment for the landlords who possess favored sites, contributes a generous quota — which might be called the unearned excrement — of depression, overcrowding, and bad living, in the dormitory districts of the city" [(006-051)].

The boulevards served military as well as aesthetic purposes — wide, straight avenues were difficult to barricade — and the displacement of working-class populations to the city's periphery created the conditions for the social tensions that would later erupt in the Paris Commune. Mumford is explicit about the pattern:

> "This had happened in imperial Rome; it had happened again in Paris under Napoleon III, where Haussmann's sweeping reconstructions created new slums in the districts behind the grand avenues, quite as bad, if far less obvious, as those that had been cleared away; and it happened once again in our American cities" [(006-051)].

The historical logic is clear: "Historically, the imperial monument and the slum-tenement go hand in hand" [(006-051)].

## Influence on the City Beautiful Movement

The Haussmann model directly influenced the American [[city-beautiful|City Beautiful Movement]], whose advocates looked to Paris as the exemplar of civic order. Yet Mumford criticizes this influence as superficial:

> "The evil of the World's Fair triumph was that it suggested to the civic enthusiast that every city might become a fair: it introduced the notion of the City Beautiful as a sort of municipal cosmetic, and reduced the work of the architect to that of putting a pleasing front upon the scrappy building, upon the monotonous streets and the mean houses" [(005-070)].

In their rush to emulate Haussmann, American planners "took refuge in the paper symmetry of axial avenues and round-points, as one finds them in Haussmann's Paris, and neglected the deeper and more genuine beauties of, let us say, the High Street in Oxford or Chipping Camden" [(005-071)]. The critique is that Haussmann's approach — and its American imitators — confused the appearance of order with its substance, creating scenographic urbanism rather than genuinely livable communities.
