## Overview

Samuel McIntire (1757–1811) was a carver and architect of Salem, Massachusetts, whose work adapted the neoclassical style of Robert Adam to the merchant houses of Federal-period New England. Lewis Mumford characterized him as "the last great figure in a dying line of craftsmen-artists" and described how he "provided an interior setting in the fashion of Robert Adam, which enabled the merchant of Salem to live like a lord in Berkeley Square" [003-190]. McIntire's career marks the transition from the vernacular craft tradition to the professionalized architecture of the nineteenth century.

## Career

A carver of "figureheads for ships and moldings for cabins" by training, McIntire brought the decorative skills of maritime craftsmanship to domestic architecture [003-190]. Working alongside Charles Bulfinch, who represented the new professional-architect type — a merchant's son educated by grand tour — McIntire embodied the older tradition of the artisan who learned through practice rather than formal study. Mumford's contrast between the two figures illuminates the changing nature of architectural practice in early republican America [003-190].

## Architectural Style

McIntire's work transformed the "low-lying traditional farmhouse" into the "bulky square house with its hipped roof, its classical pilasters, its frequently ill-proportioned cupola, its 'captain's walk,' or 'widow's walk'" [003-190]. These features expressed the rising ambitions of the New England merchant class, for whom architecture became a vehicle of social display. Mumford observes that "the merchant with his eye for magnitude lords it over the farmer with his homely interest in the wind and the weather" — McIntire's architecture registered this shift in social values [003-190].

## In the Text

Mumford's discussion of McIntire appears in the context of his analysis of America's transition from classical culture to commercial society. He presents McIntire as caught between two worlds: the craft tradition he inherited and the merchant-driven demand for conspicuous display that defined his clientele. Mumford's assessment is characteristically double-edged — he admires McIntire's craft skill while lamenting that he was "compelled to make up by wealth of ornament a beauty which the earlier provincial houses had achieved by adaptation to the site without, and to subtlety of proportion within" [003-190].

## Legacy

McIntire's work survives in the historic houses of Salem, Massachusetts, where his carved interiors and architectural designs are preserved. His career illustrates a pivotal moment in American architectural history, when the figure of the craftsman-architect was giving way to the professional practitioner. Mumford's phrase "the last great figure in a dying line of craftsmen-artists" captures the elegiac quality of this transition [003-190].
