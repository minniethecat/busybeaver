## Overview

Burnham and Root was one of the most influential architectural firms in late nineteenth-century America. Founded in Chicago by Daniel Hudson Burnham (1846–1912) and John Wellborn Root (1850–1891), the firm played a central role in the development of the Chicago School of architecture, which adapted traditional masonry construction to the demands of the commercial skyscraper.

## Architectural Contribution

The firm's most celebrated work, the Monadnock Building in Chicago (1891), represented a culmination of the load-bearing masonry tradition pushed to its structural limit. Mumford identifies the Monadnock as a rare exception among early tall buildings, noting its lasting influence on European modernism:

> Our first tall buildings were designed for the most part by men who thought in terms of established architectural forms: Burnham and Root's Monadnock Building, in Chicago, which has exerted such a powerful influence over the new school of German architects, was an almost isolated exception; and, significantly enough, it did not employ the steel skeleton! [007-020]

The Monadnock's sixteen stories of unadorned brick marked both the apex and the terminus of masonry skyscraper construction; within a few years, steel-frame construction would render such massive bearing walls obsolete.

## The Root-Burnham Partnership

Root was the firm's principal designer, bringing a sensitive and inventive approach to form, while Burnham provided organizational genius that would later make him the impresario of the World's Columbian Exposition. Root's untimely death in 1891, just as the Exposition plans were taking shape, left Burnham to direct the fair without his partner's design sensibility — a loss that Mumford and other critics have cited as pivotal in the triumph of Beaux-Arts classicism over the indigenous Chicago School at the fair.

## The Imperial Turn

After Root's death, Burnham's career trajectory embodied the shift from Chicago School commercial modernism to Beaux-Arts imperial grandeur that Mumford traces across the turn of the century. At the World's Columbian Exposition, Root had envisioned a variegated oriental setting, but the architects of the fair instead achieved unity by conforming to classical precedent (006-007). Burnham found his métier in this monumental mode; Mumford groups him with McKim, White, and Hunt as architects who "divined that they were fated to serve Renaissance despots and emperors with more than Roman power, and unerringly they chose the proper form for their activities" (006-008).

Burnham's own words confirmed this imperial aspiration. He praised the improvements of Periclean Athens not as the embodiment of citizenship and religion at their height, but as a measure for increasing urban attractiveness to visitors (006-013). Of the World's Fair itself, Burnham declared that "it is what the Romans would have wished to create in permanent form" (006-031). Mumford counters this boast with sardonic restraint: the imperial cities of America are indeed what the Romans created, but whether the form proves permanent is a matter best left to history (006-031). The shift from the Monadnock's honest brick to the Exposition's plaster classicism thus encapsulated a broader transformation in American architecture — one that Mumford regarded as a defeat for the indigenous modernism Root had represented.

## Legacy

After Root's death and the success of the 1893 World's Fair, the firm evolved into D.H. Burnham and Company, shifting decisively toward the classical idiom that would dominate American civic architecture for a generation. The transition from Burnham and Root's commercial modernism to Burnham's imperial classicism encapsulates the broader architectural transformation that Mumford traces across the turn of the century.
