## Overview

Pierre Charles L'Enfant (1754–1825) was a French-born American military engineer whose 1791 plan for the new federal capital at Washington, D.C., represents the most ambitious application of formal Baroque city planning principles in American history. His design — with radiating avenues cut through the orthogonal street grid, key public buildings placed on commanding sites, and a grand central mall — gave what Lewis Mumford calls "great dignity to the new capital city" [005-004].

In Mumford's architectural history, L'Enfant occupies a paradoxical position. His Washington plan was "the last gasp of the classical order" in American urbanism [005-008] — a belated expression of the same Renaissance principles that had shaped the grand designs of Louis XIV's Paris. Yet L'Enfant's vision would not be fully realized for more than a century, and its eventual fulfillment in the early 20th century belonged to an entirely different architectural epoch: the rise of the Imperial Façade.

## The Washington Plan

L'Enfant's commission to design the federal capital came at a unique moment in American history. The Revolutionary War had forged a new political order, and the [[early-republic|Early Republic]] sought architectural forms adequate to its ambitions. The classical tradition, reinterpreted through the lens of the Renaissance, provided the vocabulary.

Mumford describes L'Enfant's approach: "Stirred by the memory of the grand design of Paris under Louis XIV, with its radiating avenues that cut through the city in the way that riding lanes cut through the hunting forest, L'Enfant sought to superimpose a dignified pattern upon the rectangular plan provided by the commissioners of Washington" [005-004]. By putting the major public buildings in key positions, by providing for a proper physical relation between the various departments of the government, and by planning spacious avenues of approach culminating in squares, triangles, and round-points, L'Enfant created a monumental framework for the administrative buildings of the American state.

The plan was a synthesis of Baroque grandeur and republican purpose. Unlike the purely ceremonial axis of Versailles, which terminated in the king's bedchamber, L'Enfant's Washington organized its vistas around the institutions of democratic government — the Capitol, the President's House, and the spaces between them. The architecture of power was made to serve the architecture of citizenship.

## A Vision Deferred

For much of the 19th century, L'Enfant's plan existed more as an aspiration than a reality. Mumford notes that "in the years that followed his plan was often ignored and overridden" [005-004]. The capital grew haphazardly, its grand avenues cut short by speculative development, its ceremonial spaces encroached upon by the utilitarian demands of a working city.

The full realization of L'Enfant's vision would not come until the McMillan Commission of 1901–1902, which undertook the systematic restoration and extension of the Washington plan. But by that time, the meaning of the classical vocabulary had changed. What had been, in L'Enfant's day, the natural architectural language of a republic founded on classical precedents had become, in the age of the [[imperial-facade|Imperial Façade]], the visual rhetoric of imperial power.

"The realities of the imperial regime at length caught up with the dreamer born out of his due time," Mumford writes of the early-20th-century restoration of L'Enfant's plan [006-021]. The civic center and the parkway, in cities like Cleveland, Pittsburgh, and Springfield, Massachusetts, represented "the better and more constructive side of this effort" [006-021]. But they also revealed the superficiality of an approach to city planning that "centered attention upon parkways alone, grand avenues alone, and squares like the Place de l'Etoile alone" [006-021].

## The Classical Order and the Grid

L'Enfant's Washington plan must be understood against the background of the dominant American urban form of the early 19th century: the rectangular grid. The grid plan, adopted by New York's commissioners in 1811, represented an entirely different conception of urban order — one based on the logic of real estate speculation rather than the geometry of ceremonial space.

Mumford draws a sharp contrast between the two models. Within a short while after the introduction of the New York grid, "the effects of the rectangular streets and rectangular lots became evident; whereas the prints of New York before 1825 show a constant variety in the elevation and layout of houses, those after this date resemble more and more standardized boxes" [005-005]. Long monotonous streets that terminated nowhere, filled by rows of monotonous houses — this was the net contribution of the formal grid plan.

Classical taste, Mumford insists, "was not responsible for these enormities" [005-005]. But on the whole it did nothing to check them. The thrifty merchants of New York, unable to understand L'Enfant's vision for Washington, "seized upon that part of it which was intelligible: its regularity, its appearance of order" [005-005]. They stripped the classical city plan of its ceremonial logic and retained only the geometry.

## L'Enfant's Legacy

Mumford's assessment of L'Enfant is ultimately generous. The Washington plan, he suggests, was a genuine achievement of the classical imagination — the last work of American architecture in which the forms of antiquity still carried their full weight of meaning. After L'Enfant, the classical tradition would persist for another half century, most notably in [[thomas-jefferson|Thomas Jefferson's]] University of Virginia. But it would gradually hollow out, becoming first a style, then a costume, and finally a mask.

The decade between 1890 and 1900 "saw the rise of a new period in American architecture" [006-032] — a period "dimly foreshadowed by the grandiose L'Enfant" [006-032]. But the architects of the Imperial Façade, for all their superficial resemblance to the forms of the early republic, were engaged in a fundamentally different enterprise. The classical precincts of the City Beautiful movement were not expressions of republican virtue but assertions of institutional power. The return to L'Enfant's forms, in the early 20th century, marked not a revival of his vision but its final transformation into something he would scarcely have recognized.
