# Robert Adam

Robert Adam (1728–1792), the most celebrated British architect of the eighteenth century, appears in Lewis Mumford's *Sticks and Stones* as part of the transatlantic transmission of architectural ideas. Adam's neoclassical style, developed after his Grand Tour studies of Roman and Renaissance architecture, influenced American building through pattern books and the broader Anglo-American architectural culture.

## The Adam Style

Adam's distinctive synthesis of Roman, Renaissance, and Palladian elements created what became known as the Adam style — characterized by delicate ornament, refined proportions, and integrated interior design. His work at Syon House, Kedleston Hall, and Osterley Park established a vocabulary that would be disseminated throughout the English-speaking world. Mumford notes the precision with which Adam's patrons pursued classical forms:

> Mr. Arthur T. Bolton, the biographer of the brothers Adam, has exhibited letters from Robert Adam's patrons in England which mark their avid and precise interest in classical forms; and without doubt a little digging would uncover similar examples in America. [003-007]

## Adam in New England

In New England, Adam's influence reached craftsmen like Samuel McIntire of Salem:

> Samuel McIntire, a carver of figureheads for ships and moldings for cabins, provided an interior setting in the fashion of Robert Adam, which enabled the merchant of Salem to live like a lord in Berkeley Square. [003-012]

This absorption of Adam's neoclassical language by a self-taught American artisan illustrated the democratic transmission of architectural ideas that Mumford traced throughout American building — from pattern books and Grand Tour souvenirs to the drawing rooms of merchant princes.

## Beyond Imitation

Mumford compared the Renaissance-influenced architects of nineteenth-century America to Adam himself:

> Of all the Renaissance architects, their impulses and interests were nearest, perhaps, to Robert Adam, whose church at West Wycombe could be turned into a ballroom by the simple act of removing the pews, and permitting the gay walls and decorations to speak for themselves. [006-009]

This flexibility — architecture serving social life rather than imposing its own rigid program — represented an ideal that Mumford found lacking in much of the imperial architecture that followed.

## Transatlantic Influence

In America, the Adam style reached architects like [[thomas-jefferson|Thomas Jefferson]], whose neoclassical designs for [[university-of-virginia|the University of Virginia]] and Monticello drew on the same classical sources that Adam had reinterpreted. The pattern books that carried Adam's designs across the Atlantic were part of the broader architectural culture that Mumford traces from Renaissance Italy through [[ecole-des-beaux-arts|École des Beaux-Arts]] France to America.
