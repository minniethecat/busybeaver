## Overview

Henry Bacon (1866–1924) was an American Beaux-Arts architect whose most famous work, the Lincoln Memorial (completed 1922) in Washington, D.C., stands as one of the most recognized monuments in the United States. A pupil of Charles Follen McKim and a product of the firm McKim, Mead & White, Bacon represented the academic classical tradition that dominated American institutional architecture in the early twentieth century.

## The Lincoln Memorial and Mumford's Critique

In *Sticks and Stones*, Mumford invoked Bacon's Lincoln Memorial as part of a broader critique of the academic classicism that followed the McMillan Commission's 1901 restoration of the Washington plan. He wrote:[006-025]

> In the restoration of the original plan of Washington, which began in 1901, the axis of the plan was so altered as to make it pass through the Washington Monument; and at the same time the place of the Lincoln Memorial, designed by the late Mr. Henry Bacon, a pupil of Mr. McKim's, was assigned.

Mumford's judgment was characteristically pointed. He grouped the Lincoln Memorial with the McKinley Memorial at Niles, Ohio, the Hall of Fame at New York University, and Grant's Tomb as examples of a dead classicism:[006-025]

> In the Lincoln Memorial, in the McKinley Memorial at Niles, Ohio, in the Hall of Fame at New York University, and in their prototype, Grant's Tomb, one feels not the living beauty of our American past, but the mortuary air of archaeology.

## The Esthetic Question

Mumford sharpened this critique in a later passage, questioning whether the pretensions of academic classicism merited serious esthetic engagement:[006-032]

> Can one take the pretensions of this architecture seriously; can one worry about its esthetics or take full delight in such finer forms as Mr. Pope's Temple of the Scottish Rite in Washington, or Mr. Bacon's Lincoln Memorial?

For Mumford, the issue was not Bacon's individual talent — he acknowledged the Lincoln Memorial as a "finer form" — but the larger problem of a classical language severed from the living conditions and democratic aspirations of twentieth-century America.

## Significance

Bacon's career exemplifies the contradictions of American Beaux-Arts practice: technically accomplished, institutionally dominant, yet increasingly estranged from the architectural vitality that Mumford located in figures like Richardson, Sullivan, and Wright. The Lincoln Memorial endures as a monument of civic architecture, even as Mumford's criticism reminds us that its classical forms belonged to a tradition Mumford considered exhausted.
