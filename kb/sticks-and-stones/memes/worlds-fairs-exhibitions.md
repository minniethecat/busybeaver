# World's Fairs & Architectural Exhibitions

World's fairs were the architectural spectacles of the nineteenth and early twentieth centuries — temporary cities built to showcase national ambition, technological progress, and architectural fashion. In *Sticks and Stones*, the 1893 World's Columbian Exposition in Chicago stands as a pivotal event: it crystallized the Beaux-Arts "White City" aesthetic that would dominate American institutional architecture for a generation, and it represented, in Mumford's telling, a defeat for the more honest, indigenous Chicago School.

## World's Fairs

International expositions were engines of architectural innovation and standardization. They tested new materials, launched careers, and broadcast styles across continents. From the Crystal Palace (1851) through the Columbian Exposition (1893) to the Century of Progress (1933), world's fairs condensed architectural trends into spectacular, ephemeral form. Each fair left a legacy — not just in its surviving buildings, but in the planning models and stylistic ambitions it set in motion.

## Exhibitions & Competitions

Architectural competitions and exhibitions were the proving grounds of the profession — venues where young architects secured reputations, where firms tested new ideas, and where the public encountered architecture as spectacle. Mumford notes how the exhibition format, inherited from the Beaux-Arts tradition, shaped the way American architects thought about design: as a problem to be solved, a drawing to be judged, a vision to be sold.
