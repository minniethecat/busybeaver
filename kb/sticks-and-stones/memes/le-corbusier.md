## Overview

Le Corbusier (Charles-Édouard Jeanneret, 1887–1965) appears in Lewis Mumford's *Sticks and Stones* only in a bibliographic note at the end of the book, but the reference is significant. In his Notes on Books, Mumford recommends *Vers Une Architecture* (1923) by "the architect whose pen-name is 'Le Corbusier-Saugnier'" as "an able exposition of the absolutist, mechanical point of view" [014-003] — a position with which Mumford had a complex, critical engagement.

The brief reference captures a larger intellectual relationship. Le Corbusier and Mumford were contemporaries grappling with the same problem: what should architecture become in the age of the machine? Their answers, however, were fundamentally different. Le Corbusier embraced the machine as a model for architecture — the house as a "machine for living in." Mumford, while acknowledging the transformative power of the machine, insisted that architecture must transcend it — that the machine was a means, not an end. As a counterpoint to Le Corbusier's mechanical absolutism, Mumford also recommended T. E. Hulme's *Speculations*, which he described as "an interesting philosophic apology for mechanism" [014-003].

## Vers Une Architecture

*Vers Une Architecture* (Toward an Architecture), published in 1923, was the manifesto of the [[modern-movement|modern movement]]. It juxtaposed photographs of grain elevators, airplanes, and automobiles with Greek temples, arguing that the engineer's aesthetic — the beauty of functional, unornamented form — was the true architecture of the modern age. Le Corbusier's famous dictum — "a house is a machine for living in" — encapsulated his program: architecture must learn from the functional logic of the machine.

Mumford's characterization of Le Corbusier's position as "absolutist" and "mechanical" is carefully chosen. He respected the intellectual clarity of Le Corbusier's argument, but he rejected its conclusions. For Mumford, the machine was not a model for architecture but a condition architecture had to master. The task was not to make buildings more like machines but to make machines serve human purposes — purposes that included beauty, community, and connection to nature.

## Two Visions of Modernity

The contrast between Le Corbusier's and Mumford's visions of modernity is instructive. Le Corbusier's ideal was the *Ville Radieuse* — the Radiant City, a symmetrical composition of cruciform towers set in a park, served by highways and airports, organized around the rational distribution of functions. Mumford's ideal was the [[garden-city|Garden City]] — a community of limited size, surrounded by agricultural land, integrating work, home, and civic life.

Both claimed to be the authentic architectural expression of modern civilization. Both appealed to the authority of the machine. But they drew opposite conclusions from the same premises. Le Corbusier saw the machine as a model of efficiency and standardization; Mumford saw it as a tool that must be subordinated to biological and social purposes.

## Mumford's Critical Engagement

Mumford's engagement with Le Corbusier's ideas continued long after *Sticks and Stones*. In his later work, particularly *The Culture of Cities* (1938) and *The City in History* (1961), he developed a sustained critique of the Radiant City model — arguing that it was the architectural expression of bureaucratic centralization, that it ignored the social complexity of real cities, and that its formal orderliness concealed a profound indifference to human experience.

Yet Mumford never dismissed Le Corbusier outright. He recognized the Swiss architect as a genuine creative force — an artist whose buildings, whatever their theoretical limitations, achieved a formal power that commanded attention. The difference between them was not about talent but about first principles.
