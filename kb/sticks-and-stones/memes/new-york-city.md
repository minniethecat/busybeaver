# New York City

New York City is the central stage of Mumford's architectural narrative — the city where every architectural force in American history has converged and found its most extreme expression. From its origins as the Dutch trading post of New Amsterdam, through its 19th-century transformation into an imperial metropolis, to its apotheosis in the skyscraper age, New York concentrates within its grid of streets and towers every phase of American building that *Sticks and Stones* examines. Mumford traces New York's architectural evolution through the [[the-machine-age|machine age]], from the medieval craft economy of its Dutch beginnings to the [[modern-movement|modern movement]]'s anxious confrontation with its steel-and-glass towers. The city serves as his primary laboratory for testing the relationship between architecture and civilization, and its buildings — the [[worlds-columbian-exposition-1893|World's Columbian Exposition]] of [[chicago-school-city|Chicago]] notwithstanding — are the proving ground where American architectural ideas have been most relentlessly tested.

## Dutch Origins and Colonial Foundation

New York began as New Amsterdam, a Dutch trading post at the tip of Manhattan Island. Mumford observes that while the Dutch settlement was "a replica of the Old World port, with its gabled brick houses, and its well-banked canals and fine gardens, it left no decided pattern on the American scene." The medieval guild and civic regulations that governed honest workmanship in the early settlement were gradually eroded by the commercial energies of the port. Unlike New England, which developed close village communities, New York from its earliest days was shaped by trade and exchange — a pattern that would define its architectural development for centuries.

The Dutch period left only scattered architectural traces. Mumford notes the **Van Cortlandt Mansion** (1748) — its "carved grotesque heads" one of the few surviving instances of medieval decoration in America, a relic of an artisan tradition that the [[renaissance-architecture|Renaissance]] and then industrialism would eventually overwhelm. The transition from Dutch to English rule in 1664 did not fundamentally alter this commercial orientation — New York was always a city of merchants, and its architecture would always serve commerce first.

> "These trading posts like Manhattan and Fort Orange were, as Messrs. Petersen and Edwards have shown in their study of New York as an Eighteenth Century Municipality, medieval in their economy: numerous guild and civic regulations which provided for honest weight and measure and workmanship continued in force within the town for a considerable period." [(001-015)]

## The Gridiron Plan of 1811

The single most consequential architectural decision in New York's history was the Commissioners' Plan of 1811, which imposed a rigid gridiron of streets and avenues across the entire island of Manhattan. Mumford treats this act as a decisive turning point — the moment when classical formalism, stripped of L'Enfant's spatial imagination, became an instrument of commercial speculation rather than civic order. Within a generation, the varied streetscapes of early New York gave way to "standardized boxes" arranged in "long monotonous streets that terminated nowhere."

The grid was perfectly suited to the rapid subdivision and sale of real estate, and perfectly indifferent to topography, views, or human scale. Its logic was the logic of the ledger book. Where the original plan of [[washington-dc|Washington, D.C.]] attempted to subordinate land to civic meaning, New York's grid subordinated everything to land value. This was, in Mumford's account, the American urban compromise: formal geometry without formal purpose.

Among the early buildings that rose on the grid, the **Subtreasury Building** (1842, Town & Davis) exemplified the late classical public design that the plan encouraged; while the **Tombs Prison** (1838, John Haviland) represented a grotesque outlier — an "Egyptian Revival" fantasy that Mumford singles out as symptomatic of the period's eclectic confusion [(003-044)]. As the century wore on, [[trinity-church-boston|Trinity Church]] (1846, Richard Upjohn) provided a rare instance of Gothic sincerity in an age of stylistic chaos.

> "Within a short while after its introduction in New York in 1811 the effects of the rectangular streets and rectangular lots became evident; whereas the prints of New York before 1825 show a constant variety in the elevation and layout of houses, those after this date resemble more and more standardized boxes. Long monotonous streets that terminated nowhere, filled by rows of monotonous houses — this was the net contribution of the formal plan." [(003-042)]

## The Brooklyn Bridge: Engineering as Architecture

The [[brooklyn-bridge|Brooklyn Bridge]], opened in 1884, represents for Mumford the high point of industrial achievement in New York — a structure in which the demands of engineering and the possibilities of architecture were reconciled. Designed by John Roebling and completed by his son [[washington-dc|Washington]] Roebling, the bridge demonstrated that industrial civilization could, when freed from the imperatives of "sloppy workmanship and quick turnover," produce structures of genuine beauty. The bridge's suspended cables, derived from the elastic curve of mathematical physics, created a form that was simultaneously functional and expressive.

Mumford singles out the bridge as "beyond any other aspect of New York, a source of joy and inspiration to the artist." In an age when most New York building was driven by speculation, the [[brooklyn-bridge|Brooklyn Bridge]] stood as proof that structural honesty and aesthetic achievement were not incompatible — a lesson that the skyscraper architects of the following decades would struggle to apply.

> "The strong lines of the bridge, and the beautiful curve described by its suspended cables, were derived from an elegant formula in mathematical physics — the elastic curve... beyond any other aspect of New York, I think, the Brooklyn Bridge has been a source of joy and inspiration to the artist." [(005-022)]

## The Skyscraper City

### Ambivalence at Ground Level

New York is the skyscraper city par excellence, and Mumford's treatment of its towers is characteristically ambivalent. He grants that from the harbor, the "great towers on the tip of the island sometimes look like the fairy stalagmites of an opened grotto" — but this is a view available only to the arriving traveler or the aviator, not to the millions who "fill the pavements and shuttle back and forth in tubes." At street level, the skyscraper's aesthetic qualities reduce to "the entrance, the elevator, and the window-pocked wall."

### From Ornament to Honest Engineering

Mumford traces the evolution from the heavily ornamented early towers to [[louis-sullivan|Louis Sullivan]]'s draped cube, and finally to the zoning-enforced setbacks of the 1920s. The **Flatiron Building** (1902, D. H. [[daniel-burnham|Burnham]] & Co.) marked the arrival of [[chicago-school-city|Chicago]] School structural logic on New York soil; the **[[woolworth-building|Woolworth Building]]** (1913, Cass Gilbert) took the skyscraper-as-Gothic-cathedral conceit to its literal extreme. The journey, he suggests, has been from false architecture toward honest engineering, but it has not yet reached genuine architecture: "A great architecture is something to be seen and felt and lived in."

> "All the current praise of the skyscraper boils down to the fact that the more recent buildings have ceased to be as bad as their prototypes. Granted. The uneasy hemming and hawing of ornament, which once agitated the whole façade, has now been reduced to a concentrated gesture... A great architecture, however, is something to be seen and felt and lived in." [(008-017)]

## The Imperial Metropolis

### Monuments of the Age: Railroad Stations

In Mumford's historical scheme, New York reaches its architectural zenith — and its deepest social contradiction — during the imperial period that follows the closing of the frontier. The great railroad stations of this era, particularly [[charles-mckim|McKim]], Mead & White's **[[pennsylvania-station|Pennsylvania Station]]** (1910) and **Grand Central Terminal** (1913, Reed & Stem; Warren & Wetmore), are "the great triumphs of American architecture during the imperial period." They are, Mumford argues, the authentic monuments of the age — more honest and more accomplished than the memorial temples and civic centers.

### Cultural Institutions and Local Materials

The imperial monument-building extended to cultural institutions. **New York Public Library** (1911, Carrere & Hastings), with its Roman circulation scheme applied to a function it did not suit, exemplified the [[beaux-arts|Beaux-Arts]] method's tendency to impose classical templates regardless of program. In contrast, the **College of the City of New York** (1908, George B. Post) and **Church of the Intercession** (1915, [[bertram-goodhue|Bertram Goodhue]]) showed that local materials — Manhattan schist and gneiss — could produce architecture of dignity without imported stone.

### The Shadow of the Imperial City

Yet the imperial metropolis carries a shadow. The same forces that produced [[pennsylvania-station|Pennsylvania Station]] also produced the tenement districts of the Lower East Side. "Historically, the imperial monument and the slum-tenement go hand in hand." The multiple-family tenement, introduced in New York as early as 1835, concentrated poverty, disease, and vice in conditions "infinitely lower than they had been in the most primitive farmhouse of the colonial period." The juxtaposition of monumental architecture and substandard housing is, for Mumford, the indelible signature of imperial urbanism.

Mumford further notes that the concentration of wealth and power in Manhattan drained the countryside and smaller cities, creating "an unearned increment for the landlords who possess favored sites" alongside "a generous quota — which might be called the unearned excrement — of depression, overcrowding, and bad living." The imperial metropolis was architecturally magnificent and socially unsustainable — a contradiction that would ultimately demand regional planning and decentralization.

> "It was no accident that the great triumphs of American architecture during the imperial period were the railroad stations; particularly the Pennsylvania and the Grand Central in New York, and the Union Station in Washington. Nor is it by mere chance that the Washington and the Pennsylvania stations are the monuments to two architects, McKim and Burnham, who worshiped most wholeheartedly at the imperial shrine." [(006-038)]
