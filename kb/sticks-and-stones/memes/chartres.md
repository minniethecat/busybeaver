# Chartres Cathedral

**Chartres Cathedral** (French: *Cathédrale Notre-Dame de Chartres*) is a medieval Catholic cathedral in Chartres, France, widely considered one of the finest examples of French Gothic architecture. Built mostly between 1194 and 1220, it is renowned for its architectural unity, sculptural decoration, and stained glass windows.

## In the Text

Lewis Mumford references Chartres in *Sticks and Stones* as an exemplar of architecture's transcendent spiritual value. Writing in Chapter IV ("The Diaspora of the Pioneer"), he observes that architecture can retain its cultural significance even after the beliefs that inspired it have faded:

> While in almost every age architecture has an independent value to the spirit, so that we can rejoice in Chartres or Winchester even though we have abandoned the Roman faith, in the early industrial period architecture is reduced to a symptom. (004-030)

## Architectural Significance

Chartres represents the high point of the French Gothic style. Its innovations include flying buttresses that allowed for unprecedented window height, the use of ribbed vaults, and an extensive program of sculptural decoration on its portals. The cathedral's stained glass — much of it original — covers approximately 2,500 square meters and depicts biblical narratives, saints' lives, and medieval trades.

## Authenticity and the Gothic Revival

Mumford's praise of Chartres gains its force from the contrast he draws between authentic medieval building and the nineteenth-century Gothic Revival. What attracted Horace Walpole and his followers to the Gothic was not the structural logic or the guild-based craftsmanship of buildings like Chartres, but "the phosphorescence of decay" — a taste for ruins rather than for sound masonry (004-005). The revivalists "summoned up the ghosts of the Middle Ages but not the guilds" (004-005), producing buildings that were "a little more than scenery, a little less than solids" (004-018).

For a time, the Gothic revival seemed poised to dominate nineteenth-century architecture, reinforced by the intellectual conviction that Gothic forms embodied organic growth and functional honesty (004-019). But Mumford argues that the underlying culture had already fragmented too far for any single stylistic current to prevail. Chartres thus stands in his argument not merely as a great building but as a measure of what had been lost — a unified architectural culture in which structure, symbolism, and craft were inseparable, and which the industrial era, for all its technical ingenuity, proved incapable of replicating.

## Legacy

Chartres has influenced generations of architects and remains a pilgrimage site for both religious devotion and architectural study. Its inclusion in Mumford's analysis underscores the cathedral's status as a benchmark against which later architectural achievements are measured.
