# James Renwick Jr.

**James Renwick Jr.** (November 11, 1818 – June 23, 1895) was an American architect whose most famous work, St. Patrick's Cathedral in New York City (1858–1879), stands as a landmark of the Gothic Revival in the United States. He was also responsible for the original Smithsonian Institution Building (the "Castle") in Washington, D.C.

## In the Text

Mumford places Renwick alongside Richard Upjohn as an architect who brought genuine understanding to the Gothic idiom, rather than the shallow historicism that characterized much nineteenth-century American building:

> There are a number of honorable exceptions to this rule, for architects like Renwick, who designed St. Patrick's Cathedral, and Upjohn, who built Trinity Church, had a more sincere understanding of the conventional task; and by any standard of esthetic decency the old Gothic building of New York University, on Washington Square, was a far finer structure than the bulky office building that has taken its place. (004-028)

This passage occurs at the conclusion of Mumford's survey of the antebellum decades — a period he characterized as one in which "architectural forms were brought together by a mere juxtaposition of materials, held in place by neither imagination nor logic." (004-028) Renwick and Upjohn, by contrast, worked within a coherent stylistic convention, and their buildings possessed an integrity that the eclecticism of the age rarely achieved.

## The Gothic Revival in America

Renwick's career unfolded during a period when American architecture was dominated by what Mumford called the "classical myth" — the assumption, inherited from the early republic, that classical forms provided the only legitimate language for public buildings. As Mumford observed of the early republic's classical mode: "When the purpose of the structure happens to conform to the style, the result may be admirable in every way. When it does not happen to conform the result is tedious and banal." (003-017) The Gothic Revival, by offering an alternative vocabulary, challenged this monopoly — particularly for ecclesiastical architecture, where the medieval associations of Gothic forms carried symbolic weight.

Renwick's work demonstrated that Gothic architecture was not merely an antiquarian exercise but could serve contemporary American needs. His success with St. Patrick's Cathedral and the Smithsonian Institution Building helped establish the Gothic Revival as a legitimate mode for American institutional architecture, paving the way for later architects like Ralph Adams Cram.

## Richardson, Trinity Church, and the Gothic Legacy

Renwick's generation — practitioners of what might be called "applied Gothic" — was succeeded by architects who sought a more structurally authentic revival of medieval building. Henry Hobson Richardson, whose Trinity Church in Boston (1872–1877) became the most influential American church of the nineteenth century, represented this transition. Mumford noted that "so far had the art of masonry disappeared that in Trinity Church Richardson sometimes introduced struts and girders without any attempt to assimilate them in the composition; but as far as any single man could absorb and live with a vanished tradition, Richardson did." (005-006)

Richardson himself moved beyond Gothic precedent toward the Romanesque — "only primitive sources supply the energy for a long career," as his French mentor Viollet-le-Duc had taught — but Renwick had demonstrated, a generation earlier, that American architects could work convincingly within the Gothic convention. (005-006)

## Career and Major Works

Renwick studied engineering at Columbia College and entered architecture without formal European training — an unusual path for a major American architect of his era. His breakthrough came in 1846 when he won the competition for the Smithsonian Institution Building. St. Patrick's Cathedral, his magnum opus, took over twenty years to complete and remains the largest Gothic Revival Catholic cathedral in the United States.

## Design Character

Renwick's work adapted French and English Gothic precedents to American urban settings. His buildings display a careful attention to proportion and detail that Mumford contrasts favorably with the "bulky office building" — a reference to the commercial architecture that was beginning to dominate American cities in the late nineteenth century. Renwick's ability to work convincingly within the Gothic convention placed him among the more disciplined architects of his generation.

Unlike many of his contemporaries who treated Gothic ornament as surface decoration, Renwick understood the structural logic of the style — the relationship between pointed arch, ribbed vault, and flying buttress that gave medieval cathedrals their coherence. This "sincere understanding of the conventional task" distinguished his work from the pattern-book eclecticism that Mumford condemned.
