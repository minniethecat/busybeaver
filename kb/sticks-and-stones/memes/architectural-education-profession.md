# Architectural Education & the Profession

The making of an architect — how one learned to design, to draw, to build — changed fundamentally across the period *Sticks and Stones* covers. The nineteenth century began with apprenticeship as the only path; it ended with university-trained professionals, licensing exams, and a national professional organization, the American Institute of Architects. The École des Beaux-Arts in Paris served as the model and the magnet for ambitious young Americans — Richard Morris Hunt, Henry Hobson Richardson, Charles McKim — who brought its atelier system and its classical rigor home with them.

## Architectural Education

From the Beaux-Arts atelier to the first American architecture schools at MIT, Cornell, and Columbia, architectural education professionalized rapidly after the Civil War. The atelier system — competitive, meritocratic, drawing-based — shaped how a generation of American architects thought about design. Mumford, critical of the Beaux-Arts monopoly on taste, nonetheless recognized the discipline and culture it imparted.

## Educational Institutions

The architecture schools themselves — the École des Beaux-Arts, MIT, the University of Pennsylvania, Columbia, Harvard — became centers of architectural culture. Their rivalries and pedagogical divisions (Beaux-Arts versus Modern, East Coast versus Midwest) shaped American building for decades. Each institution carried a distinct design philosophy, transmitted through its graduates to the cities they built.

## Architects & the Profession

The professionalization of architecture — the founding of the AIA in 1857, the establishment of licensing laws, the rise of the large corporate firm — transformed the architect from craftsman to professional. Sullivan and Wright resisted this bureaucratization; Burnham and McKim embraced it. The tension between individual artistic vision and organized professional practice runs through Mumford's entire narrative.
