## Overview

Salem, Massachusetts occupies a special place in Lewis Mumford's architectural imagination. It appears in *Sticks and Stones* not merely as a geographical location but as a type — the New England colonial town at its most concentrated and characteristic. Through the example of Salem, and specifically through the John Ward house, Mumford examines the conditions that made early American architecture what it was: its craftsmanship, its social underpinnings, and its eventual dissolution under the pressures of commerce and industrialization.

## The John Ward House

Mumford's description of the John Ward house in Salem is one of the most evocative passages in the book:

> Let us take a brief look at one of these seventeenth-century buildings; let us say, the John Ward house in Salem which still survives as a relic. As one approaches the village on some November day, when the salt odor of the sea and the marshes is sharp in the air, one sees the dark, weathered oak of its frame, the white plaster infill, the steep pitched roof, the massive central chimney.

[001-007]

The Ward house serves for Mumford as an emblem of what early American building achieved: a direct, unselfconscious response to climate, materials, and the needs of daily life. It was not architecture in the self-conscious Renaissance sense — it was building, the work of a carpenter-builder working within a craft tradition that preceded and exceeded him.

## The Craft Tradition

What distinguishes the Ward house and its contemporaries is their participation in a living craft tradition. The colonial builder "knew his stone and his workmen and his tools and the tradition of his art" [002-012] in a way that the 19th-century architect, educated through books and pattern drawings, did not.

This tradition was communal, oral, and transmitted through apprenticeship. It produced buildings that were not necessarily beautiful in any formal sense but that possessed the deeper beauty of fitness — the beauty of a thing well made for a clear purpose. The steep roof shed New England snow; the massive chimney heated the entire house; the small windows conserved warmth while admitting sufficient light. Every element served a function, and the whole was greater than its parts.

## Salem's Transformation

Salem did not remain a village of craftsmen and fishermen. By the late 18th century, it had become one of the wealthiest ports in America, its merchants trading with China, India, and the West Indies. This commercial prosperity transformed its architecture. The houses of the merchant princes — built by carvers like [[samuel-mcintire|Samuel McIntire]], who began his career as a carver of ship figureheads — replaced the severe dwellings of the Puritan settlers.

Mumford notes the irony: "In New England, not unnaturally, the influence of the merchant prevailed in architecture for a longer time, perhaps, than it did elsewhere" [003-013]. The very commercial success that made Salem's finest Federal houses possible was the force that would, over the course of the 19th century, erode the craft tradition that had produced the Ward house.

## The Irrecoverable Past

Mumford's fascination with Salem is tinged with a melancholy awareness of loss:

> Once we have genuinely appreciated the influence that created early colonial architecture, we see that it is irrecoverable: what we call a revival is really a second burial.

[006-047]

The Ward house can be preserved as a museum piece; it can be measured, photographed, and published in scholarly monographs. But the conditions that produced it — the craft tradition, the social order, the limited scale of the colonial town — cannot be recovered. The preservation of Salem's colonial architecture is, for Mumford, a valuable but necessarily limited undertaking. It can teach us what we have lost; it cannot teach us how to build again.
