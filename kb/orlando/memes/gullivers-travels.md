# Gulliver's Travels

*Gulliver's Travels* (1726) is Jonathan Swift's satirical masterpiece, a work that Woolf invokes in *Orlando* as an essential document of the Augustan literary temperament. It appears at a critical juncture in Chapter IV, both as a textual reference and as an implied character key for its author.

## Textual Presentation

Woolf's first reference to *Gulliver's Travels* occurs in a metafictional address to the reader, grouping it with two other key Augustan texts:

> "If the reader will here refer to the Rape of the Lock, to the Spectator, to Gulliver's Travels, he will understand precisely what these mysterious words may mean." (010-080)

The "mysterious words" refer to the narrator's preceding catalogue of the writers' mundane habits — that Addison scented his handkerchiefs, Pope suffered with his head, and Swift had a fine malacca cane (010-080). Woolf's suggestion is that the works themselves encode the full personalities of their authors, rendering biographical detail mere supplement.

Swift himself enters the narrative with theatrical abruptness, his arrival staged as a dramatic interruption:

> "And when Mr. Addison has said his say, there is a terrific rap at the door, and Mr. Swift, who had these arbitrary ways with him, walks in unannounced. One moment, where is Gulliver's Travels?" (010-085)

The narrator's interjected question — "where is Gulliver's Travels?" — reiterates the novel's central conceit: that reading the work is the truest way to know its author. Swift's "arbitrary" entrance, without the courtesies Addison observes, mirrors the savage independence of his satirical vision.

## The Houyhnhnm Passage

The narrator answers her own rhetorical question by quoting directly from *Gulliver's Travels*, selecting a passage from the voyage to the land of the Houyhnhnms — the rational horses whose society exposes the brutishness of humanity:

> "I enjoyed perfect Health of Body and Tranquility of Mind; I did not find the Treachery or Inconstancy of a Friend nor the Injuries of a secret or open Enemy. I had no occasion of bribing, flattering, or pimping, to procure the Favour of any great Man or of his Minion. I wanted no Fence against Fraud or Oppression; Here was neither Physician to destroy my Body, nor Lawyer to ruin my Fortune; No Informer to watch my Words, and Actions, or forge Accusations against me for Hire; Here were no Gibers, Censurers, Backbiters, Pickpockets, Highwaymen, Housebreakers, Attorneys, Bawds, Buffoons, Gamesters, Politicians, Wits, splenetick tedious Talkers." (010-086)

The choice of this particular passage is pointed. Swift's catalogue of absences — no treachery, no flattery, no lawyers, no politicians — is a negative inventory of Augustan London. By embedding it at the climax of the salon scene, Woolf lets Swift's own prose perform the satirical work of puncturing the pretensions of the literary world Orlando has just entered.

The passage also illuminates the characterisation of Swift himself earlier in the scene. The man who "had these arbitrary ways with him" (010-085) and who "walks in unannounced" is precisely the man who could imagine — and would prefer — a society without the rituals of deference and corruption catalogued in the Houyhnhnm passage. Woolf's method is characteristically oblique: she never states that Swift was a misanthrope; she lets his own fiction say it for her.

## Narrative Function

*Gulliver's Travels* serves Woolf's larger argument about the indivisibility of literary style and authorial character. The novel's narrator insists that "every secret of a writer's soul, every experience of his life, every quality of his mind is written large in his works" (010-081). By invoking *Gulliver's Travels* at the moment of Swift's entrance, Woolf collapses the distance between the man and the book — implying that Swift's famous misanthropy, his "savage indignation," is legible in every page of his masterpiece.

The grouping of Swift with Pope and Addison also constructs a composite portrait of Augustan literary culture: Addison the polished essayist, Pope the elegant wit, Swift the uncompromising satirist. Together, their works form a triptych through which Orlando — and the reader — apprehend the spirit of the eighteenth century.
