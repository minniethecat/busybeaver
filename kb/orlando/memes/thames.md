# The Thames

The River Thames is the central geographical artery of *Orlando*, linking the rural world of the Oak Tree estate to the urban world of London. Its frozen surface in Chapter I provides the setting for the novel's most celebrated set-piece: the Great Frost Fair, where the ordinary is transformed into the extraordinary and Orlando meets Sasha.

## The Frost Fair

When an extraordinary frost seizes England during the reign of James I, the Thames freezes solid. "The frost continued unbroken for three months," the narrator reports, and upon the ice a carnival city springs into existence. (007-049) The frozen river becomes a democratic space where "apprentices; tailors; fishwives; horse dealers; cony catchers; starving scholars; maidservants in their wimples; orange girls; ostlers; sober citizens; bawdy tapsters" mingle with the court — "a great carnival, a vast assembly, an orgy of pleasure." (007-049)

## The Royal Enclosure

The court claims its own territory on the ice, sectioned off by a "silken rope" that separates the Royal enclosure from the common crowd. Orlando and Sasha repeatedly "slip under the silken rope" to escape the court's scrutiny — gestures of transgression made possible by the frozen river's temporary suspension of normal social boundaries. (007-035)

## The Thaw

The Frost Fair ends in catastrophe. "Where, for three months and more, there had been solid ice of such thickness that it seemed permanent as stone, and a whole gay city had been erected upon it, was now a race of turbulent yellow waters." (007-058) The ice cracks, the carnival structures collapse, and the river reclaims its liquid nature. The thaw becomes a metaphor for the dissolution of Orlando's romantic hopes: Sasha sails away with the Russian ship as the ice breaks, and Orlando is left on the banks at Wapping, watching the flood.

## Later Appearances

The Thames reappears throughout the novel as a constant amid transformation. Orlando sails up it on her return from Constantinople, with Captain Bartolus pointing out the landmarks. (010-018) The river connects the novel's geography — from the great house deep in the country, past London and its landmarks, to the sea and the world beyond. Its perpetual flow is the novel's deepest geographical metaphor for time itself: always the same river, never the same water.
