# Fame and Reputation

Fame is both desired and mocked throughout *Orlando*. The novel traces the arc of Orlando's relationship to literary reputation — from youthful ambition through disillusionment to indifference — suggesting that the creative act itself, not its reception, is what matters.

## Concept Definition

The novel presents fame as a social construct, dependent on the whims of fashion and the accidents of history. Nick Greene, who mocked Orlando's poetry in the Elizabethan era, returns in the Victorian age as a celebrated critic — the same man, different century, reversed judgment. Fame, the novel suggests, tells us more about the age than about the artist.

## Textual Manifestation

Orlando's early ambition is satirised: the young nobleman dreams of literary immortality, but his verse is derivative. Nick Greene's dismissal — the poet has "a butcher's face and the butcher a poet's" (008-012) — punctures Orlando's pretensions.

After burning his works, Orlando begins again without concern for reputation. "He had no thought of publication" (008-039); he writes purely for the act itself.

## Key Scenes

The irony of Orlando's eventual literary prize: she wins recognition for "The Oak Tree" only when she has ceased to care about recognition. The prize arrives when "she had thought of literature all these years" (012-034) and found it both necessary and insufficient.

Greene's reappearance as the arbiter of Victorian taste satirises the cultural machinery that manufactures reputations.

## Literary Criticism

> Woolf's treatment of fame in *Orlando* is informed by her own experience as a writer navigating literary reputation. The novel's satire of Greene — and through him, of the professional critic — reflects Woolf's ambivalence about the institutions that confer literary value.
