# Constantinople

Constantinople is the site of Orlando's ambassadorship and the location of one of the novel's pivotal events: the seven-day sleep from which Orlando awakens as a woman. It appears primarily in Chapter III, where it serves as the threshold between Orlando's life as a man and her life as a woman.

## Geographic Location

Constantinople is introduced as the destination to which Orlando seeks appointment after the Great Frost and his disillusionment with Sasha. "He asked King Charles to send him as Ambassador Extraordinary to Constantinople. The King was walking in Whitehall. Nell Gwyn was on his arm. She was pelting him with hazel nuts. 'Twas a thousand pities, that amorous lady sighed, that such a pair of legs should leave the country." (008-073)

The city appears in the narrative in fragmentary, impressionistic glimpses: the procession of "purple Janissaries running on foot and waving great ostrich-feather fans," diplomats seated "on brocades of gold and silver stuffs," and the Galata Bridge with its "green-turbaned pilgrims without eyes or noses." (009-006) Woolf deliberately sketches Constantinople through exoticized fragments, acknowledging that these are "fragments such as these that we must do our best to make up a picture of Orlando's life and character at this time." (009-007)

## Textual Description

Orlando's arrival in Constantinople is marked by calculated opulence. "After luncheon, lackeys announced that his coach and six was at the door, and he went, preceded by purple Janissaries running on foot and waving great ostrich-feather fans above their heads, to call upon the other ambassadors and dignitaries of state." (009-006) The city is rendered as a place of ceremony and spectacle, where every encounter is staged and every gesture carries diplomatic weight.

## Narrative Function

### The Dukedom and Transformation

Constantinople serves a double narrative function. First, it is where Orlando receives his Dukedom — "the conferring of the Dukedom was the occasion of a very famous, and indeed, much disputed incident" — celebrated with a grand entertainment at the end of Ramadan. (009-008) Second, and more crucially, it is the location of the novel's central metamorphosis.

After the celebration, Orlando falls into a seven-day sleep from which no amount of "plasters, nettles, emetics" can rouse him. (009-013) When he wakes, the transformation is complete: Orlando is a woman. The secretaries discover the marriage deed to Rosina Pepita among his papers, and the legal machinery that will pursue Orlando for the rest of the novel is set in motion.

### The Gipsy Interlude

After the transformation, Orlando flees Constantinople to join a gipsy tribe encamped "on the high ground outside Broussa," where she lives for a time among people who "had no words for 'beautiful'... but only 'grass' and 'sheep.'" The contrast between Constantinople's diplomatic opulence and the gipsies' elemental simplicity frames the transformation as not merely a change of sex but a journey through entirely different modes of being in the world.

## Thematic Role

Constantinople represents the threshold between Orlando's gendered existences. It is fitting that the transformation occurs in a city that straddles two continents, Europe and Asia — a place of crossing and hybridity. Orlando arrives as a man and an ambassador; she leaves as a woman who must forge a new identity outside the structures of court and state.
