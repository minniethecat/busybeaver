**Orlando in the Twentieth Century** is the closing section of the novel, set on 11 October 1928 — the novel's "present." In this final chapter, Orlando is a modern woman driving a motor car, shopping at Marshall & Snelgrove's, and experiencing the fractured simultaneity of modern consciousness.

## In the Novel

The twentieth-century chapter marks a decisive shift in the novel's tempo. The leisurely, century-spanning narrative suddenly accelerates into the fragmented present: the ringing of telephones, the flash of electric signs, the jolt of the motor car. Orlando experiences what the novel calls "the present moment" — not as a point on a timeline but as a thick, saturated experience containing all of the past.

The chapter culminates with Shelmerdine's return from Cape Horn, Orlando's vision of the wild goose, and the striking of midnight. The oak tree — the poem and the physical tree — endures into the modern age, anchoring Orlando in a world of accelerating change.

## Significance

The final chapter is Woolf's meditation on modernity itself — on what it means to live in a time of technological and psychological transformation. Orlando's survival into the twentieth century, with three centuries of experience intact, figures the persistence of the self across the ruptures of historical change.
