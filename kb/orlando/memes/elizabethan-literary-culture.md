# Elizabethan Literary Culture

The Elizabethan literary world forms the backdrop of Orlando's early life in the novel. Woolf depicts the period not as a mere historical setting but as a distinct imaginative climate — one in which poetry, patronage, and literary ambition shaped the possibilities of a young nobleman's life. Through the figure of [[nick-greene|Nicholas Greene]], the novel simultaneously celebrates the Elizabethan golden age and subjects its myths to satirical scrutiny, revealing a literary culture as obsessed with belatedness and decline as any later period.

## Concept Definition

Woolf presents Elizabethan literary culture as both a historical reality and a constructed memory. The narrator's famous declaration establishes the period as fundamentally alien to modern sensibilities:

> "The age was the Elizabethan; their morals were not ours; nor their poets; nor their climate; nor their vegetables even." [007-017]

The progression — from morals to poets to climate to vegetables — is characteristic of Woolf's deflationary wit. The grand historical categories (morals, poets) are levelled with the mundane (climate, vegetables), suggesting that the difference between historical periods is at once profound and absurd. This is not nostalgia for a golden age but a recognition of radical difference: the Elizabethans inhabited a world where "the brilliant amorous day was divided as sheerly from the night as land from water" [007-017], a world whose imaginative boundaries were drawn differently from our own.

## Textual Manifestation

### The Culture of Patronage

Orlando's entry into literary life is mediated through the aristocratic system of patronage that structured Elizabethan literary production. After an extended period of solitude and reading, Orlando "commissioned Mr. Isham of Norfolk to deliver to Mr. Nicholas Greene of Clifford's Inn a document which set forth Orlando's admiration for his works" [008-015], inviting the famous writer to his country estate. This gesture — the wealthy nobleman seeking the company of the celebrated author — reproduces the Renaissance model of literary patronage in miniature: the writer depends on the patron's wealth, and the patron basks in the writer's reflected glory.

### The Great House as Literary Salon

The novel evokes the literary gathering as a scene of almost magical prestige. At Orlando's estate, "Many Kings, Queens, and Ambassadors had been received there; Judges had stood there in their ermine. The loveliest ladies of the land had come there; and the sternest warriors" [008-016]. The great house becomes a metaphor for the literary culture itself — grand, hierarchical, and already haunted by the memory of its own past glories. The architecture of the house, with its "three hundred and sixty-five bedrooms" [008-016], mirrors the capaciousness of Elizabethan literary ambition.

### The Literary Pantheon

Orlando carries within him a catalogue of Elizabethan literary heroes. The narrator records him "reeling off the names of his favourite heroes" — Shakespeare, Marlowe, Ben Jonson, Browne, Donne — "all now writing or just having written" [008-019]. The phrase "now writing or just having written" captures the peculiar immediacy of the Elizabethan moment: Orlando lives at a time when the works we now regard as monuments of English literature are still warm from the press, their authors still alive, their reputations still contested. This roll call of names functions as a compressed history of the period's literary achievement, each name freighted with cultural significance that the novel both invokes and interrogates.

## Key Scenes

### Nicholas Greene's Visit

The visit of Nicholas Greene to Orlando's estate is the novel's most sustained engagement with Elizabethan literary culture. Greene, a "very famous writer at that time" [008-015], arrives as both honoured guest and satirical figure, delivering extended monologues on the state of letters. His pronouncements are both comic and revealing, a window into the literary marketplace of the period:

> "The great age of literature is past; the great age of literature was the Greek; the Elizabethan age was inferior in every respect to the Greek." [008-021]

Greene's dismissal of his contemporaries is comprehensive — and startling in its vehemence. Shakespeare "had taken them chiefly from Marlowe"; Browne "was for writing poetry in prose, and people soon got tired of such conceits as that"; Donne was "a mountebank who had wrapped up his lack of meaning in hard words" [008-020]. These judgments, delivered by a figure who is himself a self-interested participant in the literary marketplace, create a layered satire: the Elizabethans, whom we now regard as foundational to English literature, were themselves obsessed with belatedness and decline. Every age, Woolf suggests, believes itself to be living in the shadow of a greater past.

### The Cock Tavern

Greene's reminiscence of an evening at the Cock Tavern in Fleet Street evokes the tavern culture that was central to the Elizabethan literary world. The scene is rendered with a vividness that transforms monumental figures into flesh-and-blood participants in a convivial, competitive culture:

> "Kit Marlowe was there and some others. Kit was in high feather, rather drunk, which he easily became, and in a mood to say silly things." [008-022]

The image of Marlowe "brandishing his glass at the company" [008-022] is both affectionate and deflationary — the author of *Doctor Faustus* reduced, in Greene's memory, to a drunk saying silly things. Woolf's treatment of literary celebrity operates by this double movement throughout the novel: the great are brought down to human scale, but their humanity is treated not as diminishment but as the necessary condition of their achievement.

### The Legacy of Patronage

Greene's visit also illuminates the economics of Elizabethan literary culture. Orlando pays Greene a pension — a direct transfer of aristocratic wealth to literary production — and Greene, for his part, is acutely conscious of the transaction. The visit is at once a social occasion, an intellectual exchange, and a commercial arrangement, the boundaries between these categories blurred in ways characteristic of the patronage system.

## Cross-Chapter Connections

The Elizabethan literary culture established in the early chapters echoes throughout the novel. [[the-oak-tree-poem|"The Oak Tree"]] — begun in Orlando's Elizabethan youth and completed centuries later — represents the continuity of literary ambition across the historical periods the novel traverses. The poem itself, initially conceived in "the Elizabethan manner" [008-002] before evolving through successive revisions, becomes a palimpsest of literary history, its layers recording the changing fashions and sensibilities that Orlando lives through.

The contrast between Elizabethan literary culture and the Victorian literary world of [[victorian-literature]] — with its mass production, moral earnestness, and commercial machinery — provides one of the novel's most pointed historical critiques. What began as an aristocratic culture of patronage and tavern sociability becomes, by the nineteenth century, an industry.
