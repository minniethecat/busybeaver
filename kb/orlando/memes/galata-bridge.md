# Galata Bridge

The Galata Bridge spans the Golden Horn in Constantinople (modern-day Istanbul), connecting the old city with the district of Galata. In Virginia Woolf's *Orlando*, the bridge serves as a site of observation and escape during Orlando's tenure as British Ambassador to the Ottoman court.

## Orlando's Observations

Each morning during his time in Constantinople, Orlando would rise at about seven, wrap himself in a Turkish cloak, light a cheroot, and lean on the parapet to gaze at the city below. From this vantage point, the Galata Bridge was a central feature of the urban panorama:

> At this hour the mist would lie so thick that the domes of Santa Sofia and the rest would seem to be afloat; gradually the mist would uncover them; the bubbles would be seen to be firmly fixed; there would be the river; there the Galata Bridge; there the green-turbaned pilgrims without eyes or noses, begging alms; there the pariah dogs picking up offal; there the shawled women; there the innumerable donkeys; there men on horses carrying long poles. (009-285)

The bridge functioned as a theatre of urban life, where the full diversity of Ottoman society — pilgrims, merchants, beggars, animals — passed in constant motion.

## Night Wanderings

Fatigued by the elaborate ceremonial duties of diplomatic life, Orlando would sometimes escape his residence at night in disguise:

> And sometimes, it is said, he would pass out of his own gates late at night so disguised that the sentries did not know him. Then he would mingle with the crowd on the Galata Bridge; or stroll through the bazaars; or throw aside his shoes and join the worshippers in the Mosques. (009-295)

These nocturnal excursions to the Galata Bridge and its surrounding districts represent Orlando's desire to shed the constraints of his official identity and immerse himself in the life of the city.

## Rosina Pepita

After Orlando's mysterious trance and transformation, his secretaries discovered among his papers a deed of marriage between Orlando and "Rosina Pepita, a dancer, father unknown, but reputed a gipsy, mother also unknown but reputed a seller of old iron in the marketplace over against the Galata Bridge" (009-309). This document, of uncertain authenticity, links the Galata Bridge to the most enigmatic episode of Orlando's life in Constantinople.
