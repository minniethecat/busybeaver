# A Visit to a Nobleman in the Country

"A Visit to a Nobleman in the Country" is a satirical pamphlet by the Elizabethan poet Nick Greene, dashed off immediately after his return from Orlando's country estate. The work transforms Greene's visit into a venomous literary caricature, exposing Orlando's private follies to public ridicule — and giving Woolf one of the novel's sharpest portraits of literary treachery.

## Textual Presentation

The pamphlet's genesis is mercilessly efficient. No sooner has Greene returned to his lodgings at Fetter Lane — where "Mrs. Greene, that is to say, was giving birth to a baby in one room; Tom Fletcher was drinking gin in another" (007-191) — than the visit crystallises into copy:

> The subject was made for him. A noble Lord at home. A visit to a Nobleman in the country — his new poem was to have some such title as that. Seizing the pen with which his little boy was tickling the cat's ears, and dipping it in the eggcup which served for inkpot, Greene dashed off a very spirited satire there and then. (007-191)

Woolf's domestic details — pen used as cat-toy, eggcup for inkpot — contrast savagely with the satire's claim to literary authority. Greene writes in squalor; his malice is indistinguishable from his poverty.

The result is precise in its cruelty. Nothing Orlando said or did escapes:

> It was so done to a turn that no one could doubt that the young Lord who was roasted was Orlando; his most private sayings and doings, his enthusiasms and follies, down to the very colour of his hair and the foreign way he had of rolling his *r*'s, were there to the life. (007-191)

Greene's technique is a literary strip-tease: character assassination masked as observation. The detail about Orlando's rolled *r*'s — a phonetic idiosyncrasy only noticeable in the intimacy of prolonged conversation — is the betrayer's signature. It proves Greene was watching closely, and that his watching was always instrumental.

Greene reinforces the ridicule by embedding excerpts from Orlando's own unpublished work:

> And if there had been any doubt about it, Greene clinched the matter by introducing, with scarcely any disguise, passages from that aristocratic tragedy, the Death of Hercules, which he found as he expected, wordy and bombastic in the extreme. (007-191)

This is the double betrayal: Greene not only mocks the host but plunders the unpublished manuscript entrusted to him for a private literary opinion.

## Narrative Function

The pamphlet's commercial success — "which ran at once into several editions, and paid the expenses of Mrs. Greene's tenth lying-in" (007-193) — is the novel's most cynical economic transaction. Art, here, is a commodity that converts friendship into coin. Greene's satire, unlike Orlando's earnest "Aethelbert" or "The Oak Tree," achieves immediate publication and profit precisely because it abandons every literary ideal that Orlando cherishes.

Orlando's response is visceral and definitive:

> When he had read it, which he did with deadly composure from start to finish, he rang for the footman; delivered the document to him at the end of a pair of tongs; bade him drop it in the filthiest heart of the foulest midden on the estate. (007-193)

The tongs — refusing even skin-contact with the pamphlet — and the command for the "swiftest horse" to fetch Norwegian elkhounds seal Orlando's retreat:

> "For," he murmured, scarcely above his breath as he turned to his books, "I have done with men." (007-193)

The pamphlet is the novel's hinge between Orlando's social life and his withdrawal into solitude. The night after reading it, Orlando burns fifty-seven poetical works, retaining only "The Oak Tree" (007-203). Greene's satire functions as a literary auto-da-fe: by mocking Orlando's ambition, it purifies it.

## Literary Criticism

The episode is a sustained parody of literary patronage in the Elizabethan and Jacobean periods. Greene is a composite of the historical Robert Greene (who attacked Shakespeare as "an upstart Crow") and the Grub Street hack of Pope's *Dunciad*. Woolf, who knew literary reviewing's capacity for casual cruelty from her own experience, makes Greene's satire a study in how literature can be weaponised by resentment.
