# Biography

*Orlando* is subtitled "A Biography," and the novel's most sustained joke is its parody of biographical conventions. Woolf simultaneously mocks and reinvents the genre, asking: can any single narrative truly capture a life?

## Concept Definition

The narrator repeatedly ironises the biographer's limitations. "We must here snatch time to point out, since this is a biography, the extraordinary discrepancy between time on the clock and time in the mind" (008-038). The conventions of biography — linear chronology, factual accuracy, decorum — are shown to be inadequate to the reality of a lived life.

Woolf's critique targets the Victorian biographical tradition most directly. The biographer's duty, as the novel's narrator wryly observes, is to be dull: "It is the duty of the biographer to plod, to follow the beaten track, to be dull" (012-015). This self-undermining commentary runs throughout the novel, creating a metafictional dimension in which the narrative constantly questions its own authority.

## Textual Manifestation

The narrator's self-conscious interventions parody biographical convention at every turn:

> But here, alas! we come to the verge of that dark abyss which the biographer must always skirt. For the biographer is a person with a passion for facts, tied down by the pegs of dates, and imprisoned in the net of chronology. (010-026)

When Orlando's inner life becomes intense, the biographer retreats, unable to follow. The novel's famous "apology" passages — where the narrator apologises for not describing this or that scene — expose the arbitrariness of biographical selection:

> But let other pens treat of sex and sexuality; we quit such odious subjects as soon as we can. (009-045)

## Literary Criticism

> *Orlando* is Woolf's most direct engagement with the genre her father, Leslie Stephen, helped to define through the *Dictionary of National Biography*. The novel is at once a loving tribute and a radical subversion of Victorian biographical practice.

The novel's subtitle — "A Biography" — is a declaration of both allegiance to and rebellion against the genre. By making her protagonist live for four centuries and change sex, Woolf exposes the biographical conventions of linear chronology and stable identity as fictions. The "biographer" narrator, with his fussy concern for propriety and facts, becomes a comic character in his own right.
