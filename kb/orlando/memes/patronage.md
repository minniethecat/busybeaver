**Patronage** — the support of artists and writers by wealthy individuals or institutions — is a recurring theme in *Orlando*, reflecting the changing economic conditions of literary production across the centuries the novel spans. From the Elizabethan court to the Victorian literary marketplace, the novel traces the shift from patronage to commercial publication.

## In the Novel

Orlando himself functions as a patron as well as an artist: his wealth allows him to entertain poets and writers (notably Nick Greene in his various incarnations), and his estate provides the material basis for his own literary work. The novel's treatment of patronage is characteristically double-edged — it enables art, but it also distorts it, creating relations of dependency and flattery that Woolf, who published through her own Hogarth Press, viewed with suspicion.

The transition from aristocratic patronage to commercial publishing that the novel traces mirrors the historical development of English literature itself.
