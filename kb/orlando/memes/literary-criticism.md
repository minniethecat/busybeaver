# Literary Criticism

Literary criticism is a significant thematic concern in Virginia Woolf's *Orlando*, explored through the evolving figure of [[nick-greene]] and through the protagonist's own struggles with writing and reception. The novel interrogates the relationship between the creative artist and the critical establishment across three centuries of English literary history.

## Nick Greene as Embodiment of the Critic

Nick Greene first appears in the Elizabethan section as a poet whom the young Orlando invites to his country estate. Greene's conversation reveals the critic's temperament: dismissive of his own age, nostalgic for a lost golden era, and personally bitter about his lack of recognition:

> At the first mention of the word, the poet's eyes flashed fire; he dropped the fine gentleman airs he had worn; thumped his glass on the table, and launched into one of the longest, most intricate, most passionate, and bitterest stories that Orlando had ever heard, save from the lips of a jilted woman, about a play of his; another poet; and a critic. (004-169)

Greene's diatribe is both comic and diagnostic. The critic's grievance — that his genius goes unrecognized while lesser talents prosper — reveals the psychology of literary judgement as an expression of personal resentment.

## The Victorian Reappearance

When Orlando encounters Greene again in the Victorian era, he has transformed into the most influential critic of the age — a Knight, a Litt.D., a Professor, and the author of a score of volumes. His critical method, however, remains unchanged: he valorizes the past at the expense of the present. His article on [[john-donne]] triggers one of Orlando's deepest crises of creative confidence.

Orlando reads Greene's criticism in Hyde Park and is overwhelmed by the impossibility of writing:

> For reading Sir Nicholas and his friends (as she did in the intervals of looking about her), she somehow got the impression — here she rose and walked — they made one feel — it was an extremely uncomfortable feeling — one must never, never say what one thought...They made one feel, she continued, that one must always, always write like somebody else. (012-859)

## The Critic as Obstacle to Creation

Woolf uses literary criticism in *Orlando* to explore a fundamental tension: the critic demands conformity to established standards, while the creative impulse demands originality. Orlando's long struggle to write "The Oak Tree" is continually disrupted not by lack of material but by internalized critical voices. The poem's completion coincides with Orlando's liberation from caring about critical judgement.

The novel itself performs a kind of critique of criticism — suggesting that the biographer, the critic, and the literary historian are all engaged in a reductive enterprise that misses the essential nature of both life and art.
