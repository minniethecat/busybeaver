# The Coronation Carnival

The Coronation Carnival is the great festival held on the frozen Thames to celebrate the coronation of James I, described in Chapter I of *Orlando*. It is the setting for Orlando's first encounter with Sasha, the Muscovite princess, and one of the novel's most spectacular transformations of the ordinary into the extraordinary.

## The Frozen City

While the countryside suffers "the extremity of want," London enjoys "a carnival of the utmost brilliancy." The new King, seizing the opportunity "that his coronation gave him to curry favour with the citizens," directs that the frozen Thames be transformed into a pleasure ground. The river, frozen "to a depth of twenty feet and more for six or seven miles on either side," becomes the site of a temporary city. (007-026)

The carnival is lit at night by "torches, bonfires, flaming cressets," transforming churches and palaces whose white stone fronts show "in streaks and patches of white and crimson where the booths and arbours illuminated the ice." The narrator describes an "astonishing white glare" that turns the familiar city into a dreamscape, where "the strangest transformation took place." (007-048)

## Orlando Meets Sasha

Among the foreign dignitaries attending the coronation is the Muscovite Ambassador, accompanied by the Princess Marousha Stanilovska Dagmar Natasha Iliana Romanovitch — Sasha. "It was through this accident that Orlando and the Princess became acquainted. They were seated opposite each other at the great table spread under a huge awning for the entertainment of the notables." (007-029)

The Princess is placed between two young lords, "Lord Francis Vere and the other the young Earl of Moray," and the narrator describes with amusement the predicament she soon has them in. Orlando, watching from across the table, is captivated — not yet by love, but by fascination with this exotic creature from a land of furs and beards and mysterious black liquids.

## The Carnival as Social Space

The frozen Thames becomes a democratic space in which the rigid hierarchies of the court are temporarily suspended. The Royal enclosure is marked off by a silken rope, but Orlando and Sasha repeatedly slip beneath it. "Apprentices; tailors; fishwives; horse dealers; cony catchers; starving scholars; maidservants in their wimples; orange girls; ostlers; sober citizens; bawdy tapsters" crowd the ice alongside the court — a "great carnival, a vast assembly, an orgy of pleasure." (007-049)

## Thematic Role

The Coronation Carnival is the novel's first great spectacle — a demonstration of Woolf's capacity to render the fantastic through accumulation of precise, sensory detail. It establishes the carnivalesque mode that recurs throughout *Orlando*: the temporary suspension of ordinary rules, the mingling of high and low, and the transformation of the familiar world into something strange and luminous. The carnival is also the scene of Orlando's first great passion, and the narrative traces with precision the movement from curiosity to fascination to obsession that will culminate in the catastrophe of the Great Thaw.
