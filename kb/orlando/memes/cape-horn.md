# Cape Horn

Cape Horn is the southernmost headland of the Tierra del Fuego archipelago in southern Chile, marking the northern boundary of the Drake Passage. In Virginia Woolf's *Orlando*, it functions less as a geographical location than as a recurring symbolic motif — a shorthand for adventure, masculine endeavour, and the peculiar bond between Orlando and her husband Marmaduke Bonthrop Shelmerdine.

## Shelmerdine and the Cape

Shelmerdine's identity is inseparable from Cape Horn. When Orlando first meets him, he reveals his life's occupation with characteristic brevity:

> "For the Horn," he said briefly, and blushed. (For a man had to blush as a woman had, only at rather different things.) It was only by dint of great pressure on her side and the use of much intuition that she gathered that his life was spent in the most desperate and splendid of adventures — which is to voyage round Cape Horn in the teeth of a gale. (011-724)

The blush is significant: Shelmerdine's masculine identity is tied to the Cape, yet he exhibits the modesty and reticence more conventionally associated with femininity. This gender inversion mirrors Orlando's own androgynous nature.

## Domestic Rituals

Orlando's conversations with Shelmerdine about Cape Horn become a form of domestic intimacy. She asks him to describe it, and he builds models on the ground:

> "Tell me, Mar," she would say, "about Cape Horn." Then Shelmerdine would make a little model on the ground of the Cape with twigs and dead leaves and an empty snail shell or two. (011-746)

These miniature reconstructions — with their twigs, leaves, and snail shells — transform the remote, dangerous Cape into something manageable and domestic. Through Shelmerdine's narration, Orlando experiences the adventure vicariously:

> She would listen to every word; interpreting them rightly, so as to see, that is to say, without his having to tell her, the phosphorescence on the waves; the icicles clanking in the shrouds; how he went to the top of the mast in a gale; there reflected on the destiny of man; came down again; had a whisky and soda; went on shore; was trapped by a black woman; repented; reasoned it out; read Pascal; determined to write philosophy; bought a monkey; debated the true end of life; decided in favour of Cape Horn, and so on. (011-748)

## Separation and Independence

Cape Horn also represents the physical separation that defines the Orlandos' marriage. Shelmerdine's perpetual voyaging allows both partners to maintain their independence:

> She was married, true; but if one's husband was always sailing round Cape Horn, was it marriage? If one liked him, was it marriage? If one liked other people, was it marriage? And finally, if one still wished, more than anything in the whole world, to write poetry, was it marriage? She had her doubts. (011-776)

This unconventional arrangement — a marriage sustained by distance and periodic reunion — reflects Woolf's exploration of the possibilities for combining marriage with artistic vocation and personal freedom.

## The Final Image

In the novel's closing pages, Cape Horn appears as a climactic image in Orlando's consciousness. Looking into the darkness, she sees Shelmerdine's brig battling the Horn:

> She looked into the darkness. There was her husband's brig, rising to the top of the wave! Up, it went, and up and up. The white arch of a thousand deaths rose before it. Oh rash, oh ridiculous man, always sailing, so uselessly, round Cape Horn in the teeth of a gale! But the brig was through the arch and out on the other side; it was safe at last! (012-963)

The passage captures the novel's central tension: the urge toward adventure, risk, and the "useless" pursuit of ecstasy, set against the desire for safety and domestic completion.
