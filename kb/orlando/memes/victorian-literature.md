Victorian Literature is a satirical target in Chapter VI of Virginia Woolf's *Orlando*, representing the literary culture of the nineteenth century — its moral earnestness, its massive volume of production, and its stifling effect on the individual creative spirit. Woolf's treatment of Victorian literary culture reflects her own ambivalent relationship to her father Leslie Stephen's generation of men of letters.

## Textual Manifestation

Orlando's encounter with Victorian literature is triggered by her impulsive order of "the whole of Victorian literature" to furnish her newly respectable home:

> The whole of Victorian literature done up in grey paper and neatly tied with string was delivered at her door. [012-049]

The overwhelming material presence of these books — filling rooms and spilling down staircases — satirizes the Victorian equation of quantity with quality:

> The house was crammed — there were parcels slipping down the staircase — with the whole of Victorian literature. [012-049]

Woolf's narrator then offers a mock-critical assessment of the period's literary production:

> For, of course, to the Victorians themselves Victorian literature meant not merely the works of Thackeray and Dickens and George Eliot, but a whole host of minor writers whose works, now forgotten, were once celebrated. [012-050]

## Satirical Dimensions

Woolf's treatment of Victorian literature operates on several levels:

- **Material excess**: The physical bulk of Victorian books — "sixty volumes octavo" — becomes a metaphor for the period's literary prolixity and moral didacticism.
- **Critical absurdity**: The narrator's suggestion that Victorian literature can only be assessed by reading all "sixty volumes" or by reading none of them parodies the challenges of literary canon formation.
- **Biographical subversion**: Orlando's status as a writer from the Elizabethan age who must now contend with Victorian literary conventions dramatizes the historical contingency of literary taste.

## Victorian Literary Figures Referenced

The novel explicitly references several Victorian literary figures, including Alfred, Lord Tennyson (whose laureateship Orlando observes with bemusement) and the critic "the most influential critic of the Victorian age" — a likely reference to Matthew Arnold or Leslie Stephen. Orlando's own poem "The Oak Tree" wins a Victorian-era literary prize, itself a comment on the period's institutionalization of literary value.
