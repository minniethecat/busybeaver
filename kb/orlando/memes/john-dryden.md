# John Dryden

**John Dryden** (1631–1700) was a poet, playwright, and literary critic who appears in the novel as part of the constellation of Restoration literary figures Orlando encountered. He is mentioned alongside Addison and Pope as one of the great literary men whom Orlando revered.

## In the Narrative

Dryden first appears as Orlando's ship sails up the Thames upon her return to England. Captain Bartolus points out the Cocoa Tree coffeehouse, identifying the notable literary figures within: "one might see Mr. Addison taking his coffee; the other two gentlemen... were Mr. Dryden and Mr. Pope" [049-062]. The Captain dismissively calls them "Sad dogs... but men of parts, none the less."

The names "Addison, Dryden, Pope" echo in Orlando's mind "like an incantation" [049-062]. Despite her earlier disillusioning experience with Nick Greene, Orlando remains susceptible to the mystique of celebrated writers, and these three names represent the pinnacle of literary achievement in her imagination.

## Later Appearances

Later in the novel, Dryden appears as one of the literary portraits that seem to nod approvingly at Orlando when she wins a literary prize: "her old friends Dryden, Pope, Swift, Addison regarded her demurely at first as who should say, Here's the prize winner!" [049-095]. This whimsical touch — portraits coming alive to acknowledge Orlando's success — underscores the continuing hold that literary reputation exercises over her.

## Historical Dryden vs. Novelistic Dryden

The historical John Dryden was England's first Poet Laureate and a dominant figure of Restoration literature. In the novel, he functions primarily as a symbol of literary greatness — one of the "great men" in whom Orlando "bestowed her credulity." The narrative suggests that Orlando's reverence for such figures is a form of belief: "Something, perhaps, we must believe in, and as Orlando... had no belief in the usual divinities she bestowed her credulity upon great men" [049-064].
