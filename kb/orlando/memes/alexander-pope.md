# Alexander Pope

Alexander Pope, the great poet of the Augustan age, appears in Chapter IV as one of the literary figures Orlando encounters while navigating eighteenth-century London society. His presence is both a tribute to and a gentle satire of literary greatness.

## Role Overview

Pope appears in the salons and coffee houses of Queen Anne's London, where Orlando — now a woman — moves among the leading literary figures of the age. He is one of the triumvirate of great Augustan writers (with Addison and Swift) whom Orlando meets, and their encounters provide occasion for Woolf's ironic commentary on literary reputation and the gap between the man and the work.

## Appearance and Identity

Pope is rendered as a small, physically deformed man whose appearance contrasts with the grandeur of his verse. The narrator's description treats his body with the same unflinching eye brought to Queen Elizabeth — physical reality as a check on literary idealisation.

## In the Text: The Wasp of Twickenham

Pope first appears as a geographical landmark. Sailing up the Thames, Orlando is told the Cocoa Tree coffee house is where "Mr. Dryden and Mr. Pope" might be seen (010-018). She repeats the names "as if the words were an incantation" — "Addison, Dryden, Pope" (010-019). The litany of Augustan names marks Orlando's re-entry into English literary culture after her years among the gipsies.

In the drawing rooms of Queen Anne's London, Pope emerges as a creature of fierce wit and sharp physical presence. The narrator renders him in quick, vivid strokes: "Mr. Pope's tongue flickered like a lizard's, how his eyes flashed, how his hand trembled" (010-081). But he is also capable of sudden illumination — "seven beams in quick succession (as Mr. Pope did that night) and then lapse into darkness for a year or two" (010-079). The image of Pope as a lighthouse, brilliant but intermittent, captures Woolf's ambivalent admiration.

The comedy of the drawing room revolves around social precedence. When Nell announces "Mr. Addison waited on her Ladyship," Pope rises "with a wry smile, made his congee, and limped off" (010-083). The detail of the limp — Pope's physical infirmity — is never sentimentalised; it is simply part of the man, as real as his wit. The furniture is arranged "all in a circle, so that Mr. Pope could not say that Mr. Addison came before him, or the other way about" (010-088) — literary greatness reduced to the geometry of seating.

In a striking passage, Orlando and Pope ride through dark streets between Mayfair and Blackfriars, their conversation illuminated in fragments by the flickering streetlamps: "for ten minutes Orlando and Mr. Pope would be in darkness; and then for about half a minute again in the light" (010-074). The intermittent illumination becomes a metaphor for Orlando's understanding of the man — glimpsed in flashes, never fully seen.

Orlando's ultimate judgement on Pope is characteristically Woolfian: Pope's genius is real, but his social pettiness is equally real. "The sneer of Mr. Pope, in the condescension of Mr. Addison, and in the secret of Lord Chesterfield" combine to sour Orlando on the company of wits (010-095). She recoils from the literary world not because the work is bad but because the people are small — and the novel insists that both things are true simultaneously.

## Story Arc

Orlando meets Pope, Addison, and Swift in the coffee houses and drawing rooms of eighteenth-century London. The encounters are brief, almost anecdotal, part of the rapid sketch of the century's literary culture.

## Character Relationships

- **With Orlando**: A figure of literary eminence whom Orlando observes with the double perspective of one who has known centuries of literary fashion.

## Thematic Function

Pope, like Addison and Swift, represents the reality behind literary reputation — the imperfect human beings who produce perfect works. The novel's willingness to show these figures as ordinary (even comical) people is part of its larger argument that art should be valued independently of its creator.

## Literary Criticism

> Woolf's Pope is one of several historical figures in *Orlando* who are treated with affectionate irreverence. His appearance connects the novel to the Augustan tradition while participating in the modernist demystification of literary authority.
