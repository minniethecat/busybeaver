# Muscovite Embassy

The Muscovite Embassy was a Russian diplomatic mission to the English court during the early years of King James I's reign. Its arrival in London coincided with the Great Frost of 1608, and it brought with it the Princess Marousha Stanilovska Dagmar Natasha Iliana Romanovitch — known as Sasha — who became the object of Orlando's first passionate love affair.

## The Embassy's Arrival

The Muscovite Ambassador and his retinue arrived in London during the extraordinary winter of the Great Frost, when the River Thames froze solid and a great carnival was held upon the ice. The Russians were figures of mystery and fascination to the English court:

> Very little was known of the Muscovites. In their great beards and furred hats they sat almost silent; drinking some black liquid which they spat out now and then upon the ice. None spoke English, and French with which some at least were familiar was then little spoken at the English Court. (004-065)

The Embassy established its pavilion on the frozen Thames, where the carnival atmosphere provided a backdrop for diplomatic and social encounters between the English and Russian courts.

## Sasha and Orlando

It was at the Embassy's pavilion that Orlando first saw Sasha. The encounter occurred on the evening of 7 January:

> He had indeed just brought his feet together about six in the evening of the seventh of January at the finish of some such quadrille or minuet when he beheld, coming from the pavilion of the Muscovite Embassy, a figure, which, whether boy's or woman's, for the loose tunic and trousers of the Russian fashion served to disguise the sex, filled him with the highest curiosity. (004-063)

Orlando was immediately captivated. The ambiguity of Sasha's appearance — dressed in oyster-coloured velvet, gliding across the ice with extraordinary speed — plunged Orlando into a state of romantic obsession. Images and metaphors crowded his mind: "He called her a melon, a pineapple, an olive tree, an emerald, and a fox in the snow all in the space of three seconds" (004-063).

## The Ship of the Embassy

The Muscovite ship, flying its double-headed black eagle from the mainmast, remained frozen in the Thames throughout the carnival. It became the site of Orlando and Sasha's intimate encounters. On one occasion, they climbed aboard, ostensibly to retrieve Sasha's clothing, and encountered a young Russian sailor who led Sasha below deck — an incident that foreshadowed the affair's eventual betrayal:

> Among them was the ship of the Muscovite Embassy flying its double-headed black eagle from the main mast, which was hung with many-coloured icicles several yards in length. (004-093)

## The Embassy's Departure

When the Great Frost broke, the thaw came suddenly and violently. Orlando, riding along the riverbank, searched desperately for the Russian ship:

> Hastily, he made count of them all; the French; the Spanish; the Austrian; the Turk. All still floated, though the French had broken loose from her moorings, and the Turkish vessel had taken a great rent in her side and was fast filling with water. But the Russian ship was nowhere to be seen. (004-127)

The Muscovite Embassy had departed, taking Sasha with it. The ship was last seen on the horizon, its black eagles flying from the masthead, standing out to sea. Orlando's first great love had ended in abandonment and heartbreak.

## Diplomatic and Narrative Significance

The Muscovite Embassy represents Woolf's use of historical diplomacy as a narrative device. The embassy provides a plausible mechanism for bringing the exotic, androgynous Sasha into Orlando's world. The cultural distance between the English and Russian courts amplifies Sasha's mystery and allure, while the temporary nature of diplomatic missions foreshadows the inevitable ending of the romance.
