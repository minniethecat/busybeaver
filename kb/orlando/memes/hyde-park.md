# Hyde Park

**Hyde Park** appears in the novel as the setting for one of Orlando's moments of literary reflection. She visits the park after acquiring a sheaf of critical papers from a bookshop, seeking to understand "the noble art of prose composition" by reading the work of established critics.

## Orlando's Reflection

Sitting beneath a tree in Hyde Park, Orlando attempts to learn from Sir Nicholas Greene's critical writings. The narrator describes her state of mind as she reads, surrounded by the park's sensory richness:

> She had carried off a sheaf of papers and critical journals from the bookshop, and at length, flinging herself on her elbow beneath a tree, she spread these pages round her and did her best to fathom the noble art of prose composition as these masters practised it. [049-085]

The scene becomes a meditation on the relationship between life and literature. As Orlando reads, the life of the park continues around her — dogs bark, carriage wheels rush, "a gigantic rubber ball bounced on the newspaper" — creating a tension between the vitality of lived experience and the formal constraints of literary convention [049-085].

## The Crisis of Voice

Orlando's reading of Sir Nicholas Greene's article provokes a crisis of confidence. She feels that critics like Greene "made one feel... that one must always, always write like somebody else" [049-085]. Standing by the Serpentine — "a bronze colour; spider-thin boats were skimming from side to side" — she weeps at the impossibility of finding her own authentic literary voice within a tradition dominated by male critics and established forms.

This scene in Hyde Park crystallises one of the novel's central preoccupations: the tension between individual expression and inherited literary tradition, and the particular challenges faced by a woman writer navigating a male-dominated literary culture.
