# Orlando

Orlando is the novel's protagonist — a young Elizabethan nobleman, a lover of poetry and solitude, who lives for over three hundred years and transforms from man to woman in Constantinople. Through Orlando's consciousness, Woolf explores [[gender-and-sexuality|gender]], [[time]], [[identity]], and the [[art-and-poetry|artistic vocation]].

## Role Overview

Orlando begins as a sixteen-year-old boy at the Elizabethan court, wins the favour of Queen Elizabeth, falls desperately in love with the Russian princess Sasha during the Great Frost, and retires to his Kentish estate to write. After serving as Ambassador to Constantinople, he undergoes a mysterious sex change and wakes as a woman. As Lady Orlando, she navigates eighteenth-century literary society, survives the Victorian era's oppressive gender norms, marries Marmaduke Bonthrop Shelmerdine, and finally integrates her multiplied selves in the "present moment" of 1928.

The narrator describes the young Orlando: "He — for there could be no doubt of his sex, though the fashion of the time did something to disguise it — was in the act of slicing at the head of a Moor" (007-001).

## Story Arc

### First Appearance and Identity

Orlando is introduced as a boy of sixteen, alone in the great house of his fathers, "slicing at the head of a Moor which hung from the rafters" (007-002) — an image that establishes his aristocratic lineage, his isolation, and his combination of violence and sensitivity.

As a young man, Orlando is described as having "eyes like drenched violets" and "the shapely legs of a young nobleman" (007-011). His beauty attracts the ageing Queen Elizabeth, who "made him her Treasurer and Steward" (007-015).

After the transformation, Orlando's appearance changes — she becomes a woman "of great beauty" — but her identity remains continuous: "Orlando had become a woman — there is no denying it. But in every other respect, Orlando remained precisely as he had been" (009-044).

### Key Events

- **The Great Frost (1608)**: Orlando meets and falls in love with Sasha, the Russian princess, during the frozen Thames carnival. Their affair and her betrayal define his emotional life for centuries.
- **Retreat to the Country**: After Sasha's departure, Orlando retreats to his estate, burns his poetry at Nick Greene's mockery, and devotes himself to solitary writing — the period in which "The Oak Tree" takes root.
- **Ambassador to Constantinople**: Orlando's political career culminates in his appointment as Ambassador, during which he is made a Duke. The transformation occurs during his sleep amid political unrest.
- **Return to England as a Woman**: Orlando sails home as a woman, navigating the new constraints of femininity. Captain Bartolus's gallantry teaches her what society now expects of her body and behaviour.
- **Marriage to Shelmerdine**: In the Victorian era, pressured by "the spirit of the age" (011-023), Orlando marries Marmaduke Bonthrop Shelmerdine, a sea captain with whom she shares an immediate androgynous recognition.
- **Integration (1928)**: In the final chapter, Orlando returns to the oak tree, integrates her multiple selves, and achieves a hard-won wholeness. The novel ends with Shelmerdine's return and the striking of midnight.

### Ending

The novel closes with Orlando standing beneath the oak tree as "the twelfth stroke of midnight sounded" (012-102). She has survived four centuries, loved and lost, written her poem, and found a self capacious enough to contain all she has been.

## Character Relationships

- **With Sasha**: Orlando's first and most devastating love. Sasha's betrayal teaches him the cruelty of passion and shapes his emotional reserve for centuries.
- **With Queen Elizabeth**: A relationship of patronage and ambiguous desire. The ageing Queen's favour sets Orlando's court career in motion.
- **With Shelmerdine**: The novel's resolution of the love plot. Their marriage transcends gender categories: "she was a woman and he was a man, but what either was in the spirit of the other, the other was as well" (012-046).
- **With Nick Greene**: A lifelong literary antagonist — first a mocker, later a knighted critic. Greene represents the literary establishment Orlando both needs and transcends.

## Thematic Function

Orlando embodies every major theme of the novel. The sex change literalises Woolf's argument about gender fluidity. The four-century lifespan dramatises the elasticity of time. The composition of "The Oak Tree" exemplifies the artistic vocation. And the closing integration of multiple selves offers the novel's resolution to the problem of identity.

## Literary Criticism

> Orlando has been read as a portrait of Vita Sackville-West (to whom the novel is dedicated), as a fantasy of transcending gender, and as Woolf's most personal exploration of the writing life. The character's freedom from ageing and fixed gender is the novel's utopian gesture.
