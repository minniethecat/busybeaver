# Greenwich

Greenwich appears in *Orlando* as the seat of the Court under James I during the Great Frost, serving as the command centre from which the monarch orchestrates the transformation of the frozen Thames into a pleasure ground. It is the administrative and symbolic origin of the carnival that brings Orlando and Sasha together.

## Geographic Location

Greenwich lies on the south bank of the Thames, east of central London. The Royal Palace at Greenwich (the Palace of Placentia) was a Tudor and Stuart residence, birthplace of Henry VIII and Elizabeth I, and a favoured seat of the early Stuart monarchy. In the novel, the Court's presence at Greenwich during the Frost makes the frozen river between Greenwich and central London the stage for the carnival.

## The Frost Fair Command Centre

The Great Frost that seizes England is described in apocalyptic terms: "Birds froze in midair and fell like stones to the ground. At Norwich a young country woman started to cross the road in her usual robust health and was seen by the onlookers to turn visibly to powder and be blown in a puff of dust over the roofs." (007-025) Yet while the country suffers, the Court at Greenwich converts disaster into spectacle.

"But while the country people suffered the extremity of want, and the trade of the country was at a standstill, London enjoyed a carnival of the utmost brilliancy. The Court was at Greenwich, and the new King seized the opportunity that his coronation gave him to curry favour with the citizens. He directed that the river, which was frozen to a depth of twenty feet and more for six or seven miles on either side, should be swept, decorated, and given all the semblance of a park or pleasure ground, with arbours, mazes, alleys, drinking booths, etc., at his expense." (007-026)

Greenwich is thus the seat of power that converts natural catastrophe into political theatre — the King using the frozen river as a stage for royal munificence and the display of favour toward the citizens of London.

## Nick Greene's Visit

Greenwich reappears indirectly in Chapter II, when the poet Nick Greene visits Orlando's country estate. Overwhelmed by the luxury of Orlando's hospitality — "the softest pillows between the smoothest sheets" — Greene experiences a peculiar form of artistic claustrophobia: "he thought that unless he could hear the drays roar upon the cobbles of Fleet Street, he would never write another line." (008-026) This episode is the inverse of the Greenwich carnival — instead of the Court imposing its spectacle on nature, nature (in the form of the great estate's silence) imposes itself upon the city poet, who longs for the noise of London.

## The Return Voyage

When Orlando returns to England as a woman in the eighteenth century, Captain Bartolus points out Greenwich from the ship: "'Greenwich Hospital, erected in memory of Queen Mary by her husband, his late Majesty, William the Third.'" (010-018) Greenwich Hospital, designed by Christopher Wren and built on the site of the old palace, had become a naval hospital — the palace of pleasure replaced by a monument to maritime charity. The transformation of Greenwich from courtly playground to naval hospital mirrors Orlando's own journey from courtier to returned traveller, from man to woman, from subject to sceptic.

## Narrative Function

Greenwich operates in the novel as the geographical origin of political spectacle: when the Court is at Greenwich, the world is rearranged for display. The frozen river becomes a park, the citizens become an audience, and the King's favour becomes the currency of the realm. In this sense Greenwich is the palace of artifice — the place that authorises the transformation of reality into theatre, a theme Woolf extends across the entire novel.
