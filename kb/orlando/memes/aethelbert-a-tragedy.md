# Aethelbert: A Tragedy in Five Acts

"Aethelbert: A Tragedy in Five Acts" is Orlando's first literary attempt — a fledgling Elizabethan verse drama begun at sixteen in the attic of his father's great house. The play, with its "Kings and Queens of impossible territories" and "horrid plots" (007-013), is the novel's emblem of youthful literary ambition: grand in design, derivative in execution, and ultimately abandoned for the quieter, more enduring work of "The Oak Tree."

## Textual Presentation

The play's first appearance is embedded in the novel's opening portrait of Orlando as a boy. After the famous image of Orlando "slicing at the head of a Moor" (007-001), Woolf offers a quieter counter-image:

> Orlando slowly drew in his head, sat down at the table, and, with the half-conscious air of one doing what they do every day of their lives at this hour, took out a writing book labelled "Aethelbert: A Tragedy in Five Acts," and dipped an old stained goose quill in the ink. (007-013)

The writing that follows is fluent but abstract: "Vice, Crime, Misery were the personages of his drama; there were Kings and Queens of impossible territories; horrid plots confounded them; noble sentiments suffused them" (007-013). Woolf's irony is gentle but precise — the boy writes what he thinks literature should be, not what he knows. The narrator notes that "there was never a word said as he himself would have said it, but all was turned with a fluency and sweetness which, considering his age — he was not yet seventeen — and that the sixteenth century had still some years of its course to run, were remarkable enough" (007-013).

The play's limitations are exposed through a single detail: Orlando attempts to describe the shade of green of a laurel bush outside his window and, "after that, of course, he could write no more. Green in nature is one thing, green in literature another. Nature and letters seem to have a natural antipathy; bring them together and they tear each other to pieces" (007-013).

By Chapter II, "Aethelbert" has been displaced. In the dead of night, Orlando "chose from this repository one thick document called 'Xenophila a Tragedy,' or some such title, and one thin one, called simply 'The Oak Tree' (this was the only monosyllabic title among the lot)" (008-155). The shift from the grandiloquent five-act tragedy to the monosyllabic "The Oak Tree" marks Orlando's growth from imitative Elizabethan to a writer finding her own voice.

## Narrative Function

"Aethelbert" serves three interconnected narrative purposes. First, it establishes Orlando's literary bent from the opening pages — this is a protagonist whose interior life is structured around writing. Second, it exemplifies the derivative, convention-bound literary culture of the Elizabethan age, against which Orlando's later development as a poet must be measured. Third, and most importantly, its abandonment and replacement by "The Oak Tree" enacts in miniature the novel's central aesthetic argument: that true art is not the grandiose public performance of tragedy but the quiet, private, persistent labour of the poem carried through centuries.

The play also foreshadows Nick Greene's later mockery of Orlando's writing. Greene, the professional poet, would find in "Aethelbert" everything he despises about amateur noblemen who dabble in verse — and Orlando's eventual burning of his juvenilia (retaining only "The Oak Tree") is a preemptive internalisation of Greene's criticism.

## Literary Criticism

> The discarded tragedy and the persistent poem form a parable of artistic maturation. "Aethelbert" represents literature as Orlando first understands it — a performance of noble sentiments in five acts, inherited from Renaissance convention. "The Oak Tree" represents literature as Orlando discovers it — a voice answering a voice, conducted in solitude, indifferent to generic expectations. The journey from one to the other is the journey of the novel itself.

## Critical Reception

Though a juvenilia, "Aethelbert" has attracted critical attention as Woolf's coded self-portrait of the young artist. The play's abandonment mirrors Woolf's own discarded early efforts, and the detail of the green laurel bush — nature resisting literary transcription — has been read as an early formulation of the aesthetic problem that would occupy Woolf throughout her career.
