# The Serpentine

The Serpentine is the artificial lake in London's Hyde Park that appears at two crucial points in *Orlando*, framing the novel's long arc from the Elizabethan past to the modernist present. In both instances, the lake becomes a site of epiphany — a place where the boundary between perception and imagination dissolves.

## Geographic Location

The Serpentine was created in 1730 by order of Queen Caroline, damming the Westbourne River to form the centrepiece of Hyde Park. It appears in the novel's opening and closing chapters, functioning as a geographical anchor that persists while everything else changes.

## The Elizabethan Serpentine

In the novel's first chapter, Orlando encounters the Serpentine (anachronistically, since it was not yet built in the Elizabethan period) during his flight from Sasha's betrayal. "Some blind instinct, for he was past reasoning, must have driven him to take the river bank in the direction of the sea." (007-058) On the banks of the Thames at Wapping, he watches the Frost dissolve into flood — a scene that prefigures the later Serpentine epiphanies.

## The 1928 Epiphany

In the novel's final chapter, set in 1928, Orlando turns into Hyde Park and encounters the Serpentine in a state of heightened consciousness. "She turned into Hyde Park, which she had known of old (beneath that cleft tree, she remembered, the Duke of Hamilton fell run through the body by Lord Mohun)." (012-042)

The lake becomes the vehicle for one of Woolf's most sustained descriptions of the mind's power to transfigure reality. Orlando's psychological state transforms the mundane scene:

> Now, the truth is that when one has been in a state of mind (as nurses call it)—and the tears still stood in Orlando's eyes—the thing one is looking at becomes, not itself, but another thing, which is bigger and much more important and yet remains the same thing. If one looks at the Serpentine in this state of mind, the waves soon become just as big as the waves on the Atlantic; the toy boats become indistinguishable from ocean liners. (012-043)

A child's toy boat on the water becomes Shelmerdine's brig; the small waves become ocean swells. Orlando cries "Ecstasy!" and "A toy boat on the Serpentine," alternating the two phrases "for the thoughts were interchangeable and meant exactly the same thing." (012-044) The scene distills the novel's central insight: that the quality of consciousness transforms the object perceived, and that the boundary between the mundane and the sublime is permeable.

## Thematic Role

The Serpentine embodies Woolf's method of revelation through the ordinary. A park lake, a toy boat, a spring afternoon — these unremarkable things become, through the alchemy of Orlando's consciousness, the materials of transcendence. The lake is both literally a lake and figuratively the Atlantic; the toy boat is both a toy and Shelmerdine's ship. This doubleness — the capacity to hold two realities simultaneously — is the novel's deepest formal principle, and the Serpentine is its geographical emblem.
