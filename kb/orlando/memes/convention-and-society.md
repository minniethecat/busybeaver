# Convention and Society

Social convention is the antagonist of individual freedom throughout *Orlando*. Each century imposes its own rigid codes of behaviour, dress, speech, and thought — and Orlando must navigate or resist them all.

## Concept Definition

The novel satirises social convention as both powerful and arbitrary. What is proper in one century is scandalous in the next. Orlando's cross-century perspective exposes the relativity of all social codes: the Elizabethan ruff, the Victorian crinoline, the twentieth-century motorcar — each is its era's "spirit of the age" made visible.

## Textual Manifestation

The Victorian era is the novel's most concentrated satire of social convention: "The sexes drew further and further apart. No open conversation was tolerated. Evasions and concealments were sedulously practised" (011-003). The "spirit of the age" becomes a literal force that Orlando must contend with.

Orlando's experience of gendered convention after the transformation is particularly acute. As a woman, she discovers constraints she never knew as a man: how to dress, how to speak, how to move through public space. "How difficult it is for a woman to express her thoughts" (010-007).

## Key Scenes

The "spirit of the age" passage dramatises social pressure as a quasi-supernatural force: "the spirit of the age, however, that it batters down anyone who tries to make stand against it" (011-023). Orlando must marry to appease it.

Orlando's navigation of eighteenth-century literary society reveals the conventions governing intellectual life — who may speak, what may be said, how reputations are made and unmade.

## Literary Criticism

> Woolf's critique of social convention in *Orlando* extends the feminist analysis of *A Room of One's Own* into narrative form. By showing Orlando navigating gender conventions from both sides, the novel exposes their constructedness and their costs.
