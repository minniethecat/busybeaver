# Art and Poetry

Orlando's vocation as a poet — embodied in the lifelong composition of "The Oak Tree" — is the thread that connects all selves across four centuries. Art is presented as the truest form of identity: more stable than gender, more enduring than love, more real than social position.

## Concept Definition

The novel treats artistic creation as both compulsion and salvation. Orlando writes not for fame but from inner necessity: "the disease" of poetry, as Nick Greene first calls it, persists through every transformation. The manuscript survives when love affairs fade and political careers collapse. It is the one thing Orlando never abandons.

## Textual Manifestation

### The Young Poet

In Chapter I, Orlando covers "ten pages and more with poetry" (007-003), though the narrator notes the verse was "fluent, evidently, but he was abstract" (007-003). The young Orlando writes in imitation, not yet in possession of an authentic voice.

### Disillusionment and Renewal

Nick Greene's mockery of Orlando's verse drives the young nobleman to burn "fifty-seven poetical works, only retaining 'The Oak Tree'" (008-035). This purgation becomes regenerative — stripping away literary affectation reveals a core creative impulse that has nothing to do with fashion or reputation.

### The Mature Artist

By the twentieth century, Orlando has won a prize for "The Oak Tree" — recognition that arrives, characteristically, only when she no longer needs it. She reflects that she "had thought of literature all these years" (012-034) and found it both glorious and disappointing, like everything else in her long life.

## Key Scenes

Orlando's solitary creative practice: "He would go out after dinner into the garden and there, walking up and down, he would think of nothing but the oak tree" (008-039). This patient, obsessive attention to a single work across centuries is the novel's image of artistic integrity.

The manuscript's survival through all wanderings: "she had out her pen and paper, and started afresh upon 'The Oak Tree'" (010-031) — the creative impulse is presented as irrepressible, independent of circumstance.

## Literary Criticism

> *Orlando* is Woolf's most extended and personal meditation on the artistic vocation. "The Oak Tree" functions as a wry self-portrait: a single work pursued through decades of changing fashion, indifferent to reputation yet central to the artist's sense of self.
