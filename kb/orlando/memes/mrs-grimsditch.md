# Mrs. Grimsditch

Mrs. Grimsditch is Orlando's housekeeper, one of the core household servants whose presence across the novel embodies the persistence of domestic life through historical upheaval and personal transformation.

## Role Overview

Mrs. Grimsditch belongs to the stable domestic infrastructure of Orlando's great house. Along with Mr. Dupper, Basket the butler, and Bartholomew, she forms a human chain of continuity — servants who accept Orlando's transformations with practical equanimity.

## Appearance and Identity

The narrator sketches Mrs. Grimsditch with affectionate economy. She is the type of the faithful retainer — competent, unflappable, and fundamentally uninterested in the metaphysical questions that preoccupy her employer.

## In the Corpus: The Voice of Domestic Continuity

Mrs. Grimsditch first appears in Chapter II as one of the regular voices of concern during Orlando's withdrawal from society: "Mrs. Grimsditch, the housekeeper, would say to Mr. Dupper, the chaplain, how she hoped his Lordship had not met with some bad accident" (008-004). She is paired with Dupper throughout — the two of them forming a domestic chorus whose practical solicitude contrasts with Orlando's romantic agonies.

When Orlando stays out late among the tombs, it is Mrs. Grimsditch who keeps the practical watch: "Mrs. Grimsditch, seeing the light in the window, put the tankard from her lips and said Praise be to God, his Lordship was safe in his room again" (008-006). The detail of the tankard — a tankard of ale at her lips — is typical of Woolf's rendering of servants: fully human, with appetites and habits of their own.

In Chapter IV, when Orlando returns from Constantinople as a woman, Mrs. Grimsditch is at the head of the welcoming party: "held above their heads, were Mrs. Grimsditch, Mr. Dupper, and a whole retinue of servants come to greet her" (010-022). Later, over her dish of china tea, she delivers the novel's most memorable verdict on Orlando's transformation: "if her Lord was a Lady now, she had never seen a lovelier one, nor one with such a pair of legs" (010-023). The observation is practical, appreciative, and entirely untroubled by metaphysical anxiety — the housekeeper's perspective on what the novel treats as its central mystery.

The narrator later notes her passing with characteristic brevity: "Grimsditch was dead" (012-063). The sentence is blunt, unsentimental — like the housekeeper herself. She has been succeeded by the Widow Bartholomew, and the household carries on.

## Story Arc

The servants' perspective on Orlando's transformations is one of the novel's running jokes. When Orlando returns as a woman, the household staff adjusts with minimal fuss — the practical matters of running a great house transcend questions of gender.

## Character Relationships

- **With Orlando**: A representative of the domestic world that grounds Orlando's more extravagant experiences.

## Thematic Function

Mrs. Grimsditch embodies the continuity of ordinary life. While Orlando lives through four centuries, changes sex, and writes poetry, the household staff continues to dust the furniture, prepare the meals, and maintain the rhythms of daily existence.

## Literary Criticism

> The servants in *Orlando* provide a comic counterpoint to the novel's grand themes. Their matter-of-fact acceptance of the impossible — a master who lives for centuries and changes sex — is both a joke and a philosophical point: ordinary life is the ground against which extraordinary events take their meaning.
