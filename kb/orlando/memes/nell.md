# Nell

Nell is one of the London street figures Orlando encounters — a representative of the urban demimonde that exists beneath the polite society of each century. Her brief appearance contributes to the novel's social breadth.

## Role Overview

Nell appears briefly in Orlando's London wanderings — a figure from the streets who provides a perspective outside the aristocratic and literary circles that dominate the narrative. Her presence is one of the novel's acknowledgements that Orlando's world, however expansive, is still bounded by class.

## Appearance and Identity

The narrator's rendering of street figures like Nell is brief but vivid, capturing a world of poverty and survival that runs parallel to Orlando's privileged existence.

## In the Text: The Encounter

Orlando meets Nell during the period when she experiments with moving between genders by cross-dressing. "Nell, as the girl called herself, unbuttoned her gloves; carefully concealed the left-hand thumb, which wanted a piece; then drew her arm through Orlando's" (010-093). Nell's damaged hand — a physical marker of her hard life — is presented with the same matter-of-fact attention that the novel gives to Orlando's aristocratic features.

Nell's response to Orlando's company is one of the novel's most genuine moments of cross-class encounter. When Orlando makes a remark about Mr. Pope, "Nell burst into such a roar of laughter as might have been heard across the way" (010-094). The narrator notes that "the hours sped faster or more merrily, though Mistress Nell had not a particle of wit about her, and when the name of Mr. Pope came up in the conversation, she took him to be a Papist" (010-095). Nell knows nothing of literature, but she knows life — and Orlando, weary of wits and poets, finds her company a relief.

## Story Arc

Nell's encounter with Orlando is fleeting — a moment of contact across class lines that the novel notes and passes over. It is one of several such moments that remind the reader of the social world Orlando's aristocratic perspective largely excludes.

## Character Relationships

- **With Orlando**: A brief encounter across the class divide; Nell's ignorance of literary society is paradoxically what makes her company valuable to Orlando.

## Thematic Function

Nell and similar street figures serve as reminders of the social boundaries that even Orlando's fluid identity cannot fully cross. Orlando can change sex and live for centuries, but the class structure of English society remains stubbornly in place.

## Literary Criticism

> The novel's treatment of working-class characters is limited by its aristocratic perspective, but figures like Nell gesture toward a broader social world that Woolf's fiction would explore more fully in later works.
