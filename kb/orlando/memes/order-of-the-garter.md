The **Order of the Garter** is the most senior order of knighthood in the British honours system, founded by King Edward III in 1348. In *Orlando*, the Order is referenced as part of the pageantry and hierarchy of the Elizabethan court, where Orlando receives honours befitting his status as the Queen's favourite.

## The Order in Orlando

Orlando is made a Knight of the Garter during his youth at the Elizabethan court — one of the many distinctions that mark his rapid rise under the Queen's patronage. The Garter ceremony, with its elaborate ritual and symbolic regalia, exemplifies the novel's fascination with the external trappings of rank and gender. Woolf treats these honours with characteristic irony: the garter itself, an intimate article of clothing, becomes a symbol of the highest public distinction.

## Historical Context

The Order of the Garter remains the pinnacle of the British honours system. Its motto — *Honi soit qui mal y pense* ("Shame on him who thinks evil of it") — and its legendary origin (Edward III picking up a lady's fallen garter) resonate with the novel's themes of chivalry, gender, and the transformation of private gesture into public ritual.
