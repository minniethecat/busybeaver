# Orlando's Lawsuits

The lawsuits against Orlando are the legal mechanism through which the novel explores the intersection of gender, property, and identity. After Orlando returns to England as a woman, she discovers that the law has constructed three incompatible narratives about who she is — all of which threaten to strip her of her titles and estates.

## The Three Charges

No sooner has Orlando arrived at her house in Blackfriars than "a succession of Bow Street runners and other grave emissaries from the Law Courts" inform her that she faces legal challenges. The three primary charges are "that she was dead, and therefore could not hold any property whatsoever; that she was a woman, which amounts to much the same thing; that she was an English Duke who had married one Rosina Pepita, a dancer; and had had by her three sons, which sons now declaring that their father was deceased, claimed that all his property descended to them." (010-021)

The narrator's parenthetical aside — "that she was a woman, which amounts to much the same thing" — delivers the novel's most concentrated legal satire. Under English law, the categories of "woman" and "dead" are functionally equivalent when it comes to property rights: both strip the subject of legal personhood.

## Legal Suspension

The consequences are drastic: "All her estates were put in Chancery and her titles pronounced in abeyance while the suits were under litigation. Thus it was in a highly ambiguous condition, uncertain whether she was alive or dead, man or woman, Duke or nonentity, that she posted down to her country seat." (010-021) Orlando is permitted to reside at the estate "in a state of incognito or incognita, as the case might turn out to be."

## Resolution

The lawsuits are finally settled in Chapter V. Orlando reads the verdict to Shelmerdine: "'The lawsuits are settled,' she read out … 'some in my favour, as for example … others not. Turkish marriage annulled. Children pronounced illegitimate. So they don't inherit, which is all to the good. … Sex? Ah! what about sex? My sex,' she read out with some solemnity, 'is pronounced indisputably, and beyond the shadow of a doubt, female.'" (011-050) The estates descend to her — though much reduced, "for the cost of the lawsuits had been prodigious."

## Thematic Role

The lawsuit sequence is Woolf's satire of the law's inability to accommodate identities that do not fit its categories. The law simultaneously declares Orlando dead, a woman, and a man who fathered children — three mutually contradictory propositions — because its logic is built on binary categories that cannot process transformation. The resolution ("indisputably female") is both a legal settlement and a narrative convenience, the novel's acknowledgment that identity, in the end, exceeds any legal declaration.
