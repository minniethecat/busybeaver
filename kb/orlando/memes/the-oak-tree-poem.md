# The Oak Tree (Poem)

"The Oak Tree" is the poem Orlando begins as a boy of sixteen and completes three hundred years later — a work carried through shipwrecks, sex changes, and centuries, "sea-stained, bloodstained, travel-stained" (012-032). It is the novel's emblem of the artistic vocation: solitary, continuous, indifferent to fame, and ultimately the only thing that endures.

## Textual Presentation

The poem first appears as a boy's manuscript: "an old writing book, stitched together with silk stolen from his mother's workbox, and labelled in a round schoolboy hand, 'The Oak Tree, a Poem'" (008-155). From these modest beginnings, it becomes Orlando's lifelong companion.

In Chapter II, after Nick Greene's mockery, Orlando burns fifty-seven poetical works in a great conflagration — jettisoning *[[aethelbert-a-tragedy]]* along with the rest — "only retaining 'The Oak Tree,' which was his boyish dream and very short" (008-080). The survival of this single poem through the fire establishes its privileged status among Orlando's writings.

The manuscript's physical deterioration mirrors Orlando's journey. During the gipsy years, "having no ink; and but little paper," Orlando "made ink from berries and wine; and finding a few margins and blank spaces in the manuscript of 'The Oak Tree,' managed, by writing a kind of shorthand, to describe the scenery" (012-015). The manuscript becomes palimpsest — new writing layered over old, the poem growing through the very scarcity that should have silenced it.

In the eighteenth century, Orlando struggles with style: "The letter *S*, she reflected, is the serpent in the poet's Eden... But *S* was nothing, in her opinion, compared with the termination 'ing.' The present participle is the Devil himself" (012-022). The poem becomes a vehicle for Woolf's own reflections on the craft of writing.

In the final chapter, Orlando holds the manuscript in a London street: "It was her manuscript, 'The Oak Tree'" (012-037). She has "been working at it for close on three hundred years now. It was time to make an end" (012-032). The poem is published, wins a prize, goes through seven editions — achievements that matter less to Orlando than the oak tree itself, to which she returns to bury a copy of the first edition "as a tribute... a return to the land of what the land has given me" (012-055).

## Narrative Function

"The Oak Tree" serves as the novel's connective tissue — a thread running from the boyish Orlando slicing at the Moor's head to the woman of thirty-six driving through London in 1928. It is the one constant across Orlando's transformations of sex, century, and circumstance.

The poem also enables Woolf's satire of the literary marketplace. When Orlando meets Nick Greene — now a knighted critic who compares her to Milton — and receives a cheque for two hundred guineas, she is baffled: "What has praise and fame to do with poetry? What has seven editions... got to do with the value of it? Was not writing poetry a secret transaction, a voice answering a voice?" (012-055). The poem's value is in the making, not the market.

## Literary Criticism

> "The Oak Tree" embodies Woolf's belief that the artist's true work is private and continuous, conducted over a lifetime in defiance of fashion and fame. The poem that is rewritten across centuries is a metaphor for the creative process itself — never finished, always revised, always carried in the breast pocket. Its connection to the physical oak tree on Orlando's estate links art to nature in a reciprocal relationship: the land gives, the poem returns.
