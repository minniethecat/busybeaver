# Orlando's Appointment to Constantinople

Orlando's appointment as Ambassador Extraordinary to Constantinople is the hinge event of Chapter III — the diplomatic mission that transports him from Restoration England to the Ottoman capital, where he receives his Dukedom and undergoes the gender transformation that divides the novel in two.

## The Appointment

After the disasters of the Frost, the Thaw, his exile, and his entanglement with Nick Greene, Orlando finds his home "uninhabitable." The narrator reports that "he did what any other young man would have done in his place, and asked King Charles to send him as Ambassador Extraordinary to Constantinople." (008-073) The scene of the request is rendered with characteristic Woolfian irreverence:

> The King was walking in Whitehall. Nell Gwyn was on his arm. She was pelting him with hazel nuts. 'Twas a thousand pities, that amorous lady sighed, that such a pair of legs should leave the country. (008-073)

The appointment is thus established as simultaneously momentous and comic — a diplomatic posting of imperial significance, arranged in a scene of hazelnut-throwing flirtation.

## Arrival in Constantinople

Orlando's arrival in Constantinople is presented in deliberately fragmentary, exoticised terms. "After luncheon, lackeys announced that his coach and six was at the door, and he went, preceded by purple Janissaries running on foot and waving great ostrich-feather fans above their heads, to call upon the other ambassadors and dignitaries of state." (009-006) The narrator openly acknowledges the limits of the account: "It is with fragments such as these that we must do our best to make up a picture of Orlando's life and character at this time." (009-007)

## The Dukedom and the Ramadan Entertainment

The climax of Orlando's diplomatic career in Constantinople is the conferring of the Dukedom. "It was at the end of the great fast of Ramadan that the Order of the Bath and the patent of nobility arrived in a frigate commanded by Sir Adrian Scrope; and Orlando made this the occasion for an entertainment of the utmost magnificence." (009-008) The narrator describes this event as "a very famous, and indeed, much disputed incident" — though the details, we are told, must be reconstructed "among burnt papers and little bits of tape as best we may."

## The Seven-Day Sleep

After the entertainment, Orlando falls into a sleep from which no amount of medical intervention can rouse him. "He applied remedies which had been used on the previous occasion, plasters, nettles, emetics, etc., but without success. Orlando slept on." (009-013) Among his papers, the secretaries discover the marriage deed to Rosina Pepita — a document of "far greater significance" than the poetry and state papers that surround it.

When Orlando wakes, seven days later, he is a woman. The Constantinople appointment, which began as an escape from an uninhabitable life in England, has delivered him to a transformation that renders all his previous identities — man, Duke, Ambassador — provisional and permeable.
