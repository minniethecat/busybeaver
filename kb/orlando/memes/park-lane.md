# Park Lane

Park Lane is a major road in the City of Westminster, London, running along the eastern edge of Hyde Park. In Virginia Woolf's *Orlando*, Park Lane serves as a recurring landmark that anchors the protagonist's London life across several centuries, from the gravel pits of the Elizabethan era to the motor traffic of the twentieth century.

## Historical Evolution

Woolf traces the transformation of Park Lane across the centuries of Orlando's life. In the Elizabethan period, the area was still semi-rural and hazardous:

> Then the benighted traveller had to trust to the stars or the red flame of some night watchman to save him from the gravel pits at Park Lane or the oak woods where swine rootled in the Tottenham Court Road. (010-557)

By the Victorian era, Park Lane had become the address of wealth and power. Orlando, now a woman, lived nearby in Curzon Street and observed the spectacle of London society:

> It was as if a golden river had coagulated and massed itself in golden blocks across Park Lane. The ladies held card-cases between their fingers; the gentlemen balanced gold-mounted canes between their knees. (012-867)

## Orlando's Relationship to Park Lane

Park Lane appears at several key moments in Orlando's modern consciousness. After receiving Nick Greene's devastating critical article, Orlando walks through London in a state of despair, then experiences a sudden epiphany:

> "Ecstasy!" she cried. "Ecstasy! Where's the post office?" she wondered. "For I must wire at once to Shel and tell him..." And repeating "A toy boat on the Serpentine," and "Ecstasy," alternately, for the thoughts were interchangeable and meant exactly the same thing, she hurried towards Park Lane. (012-863)

The juxtaposition of "ecstasy" with the mundane act of crossing Park Lane captures Woolf's method in the novel's final sections — the interpenetration of transcendent experience and ordinary urban life.

At Stanhope Gate, waiting for carriages to pass, Orlando reflects on her marriage to [[marmaduke-bonthrop-shelmerdine|shelmerdine]]:

> Thus she spoke aloud, waiting for the carriages to cross at Stanhope Gate, for the consequence of not living with one's husband, except when the wind is sunk, is that one talks nonsense aloud in Park Lane. (012-865)

## The Twentieth-Century Transformation

In the novel's final section, set in 1928, Orlando observes how Park Lane has changed. The horse-drawn carriages have been replaced by motor cars, and the social landscape has been transformed:

> Once more Orlando stood at the window, but let the reader take courage; nothing of the same sort is going to happen today, which is not, by any means, the same day. No — for if we look out of the window, as Orlando was doing at the moment, we shall see that Park Lane itself has considerably changed. Indeed one might stand there ten minutes or more, as Orlando stood now, without seeing a single barouche landau. (012-895)

Park Lane thus functions as a barometer of historical change, marking the passage from the Victorian era to modernity.

## The Coach Accident

During Orlando's carriage ride with [[alexander-pope]], the coach lurched violently in a rut on Park Lane, interrupting her romantic reverie:

> Here the coach gave a great lurch as it fell into a rut in Park Lane — "without genius we should be upset and undone. Most august, most lucid of beams" — thus she was apostrophizing the hump on the cushion when they drove beneath one of the street lamps in Berkeley Square and she realized her mistake. (010-557)

This comic moment — Orlando mistaking a cushion hump for Pope's noble forehead — illustrates the gap between literary idealization and physical reality, a recurring theme in the novel.
