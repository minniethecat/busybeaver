# Blackfriars

Blackfriars is the London district where Orlando's family maintains a residence — a grand house with "gardens running down to the river." It serves as a key narrative location in the early chapters of the novel, associated with both romantic escape and legal entrapment.

## Geographic Location

Blackfriars lies on the north bank of the Thames, near the Inns of Court and the centre of London's legal establishment. The district takes its name from the Dominican priory that once occupied the site. In *Orlando*, the family's Blackfriars house functions as a London base separate from the great country estate and the Court.

## The Planned Elopement

Blackfriars first appears as the crucial location in Orlando's doomed plan to flee with Sasha. On the night of the Frost Fair's dissolution, Orlando remembers the signal: "'Jour de ma vie!' It was their signal. At midnight they would meet at an inn near Blackfriars. Horses waited there. Everything was in readiness for their flight." (007-054) He waits through the night while the ice cracks and the rain falls, but Sasha never comes — she has sailed away with the Russian ship.

The Blackfriars inn thus becomes the novel's emblem of romantic betrayal: a place where everything was prepared for flight, but where no flight occurs.

## The Return and the Law

When Orlando returns to England as a woman in the eighteenth century, Blackfriars takes on a different character. "No sooner had she returned to her home in Blackfriars than she was made aware by a succession of Bow Street runners and other grave emissaries from the Law Courts that she was a party to three major suits." (010-021) The house that once promised romantic escape now becomes the site of legal siege.

The narrator notes the darkness of the streets between Mayfair and Blackfriars in this period: "the less we see the more we believe." (010-074) The physical darkness of the route becomes a metaphor for the legal obscurity that surrounds Orlando's identity — the courts cannot determine whether she is alive or dead, man or woman, Duke or nonentity.

## Narrative Function

Blackfriars represents the tension between private desire and public constraint that runs through the novel. In the first instance, it is the staging ground for a forbidden love that never materializes. In the second, it is the place where the law's impersonal machinery intrudes upon Orlando's private life, threatening to strip her of property, title, and identity.

The house with "gardens running down to the river" is a liminal space — between land and water, between the private world of feeling and the public world of law. In a novel obsessed with thresholds and transformations, Blackfriars is the architectural embodiment of the boundary between one life and another.
