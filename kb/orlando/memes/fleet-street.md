# Fleet Street

Fleet Street appears in *Orlando* primarily through the voice of Nick Greene, the Elizabethan poet and critic who visits Orlando's country estate and finds its silence suffocating. It is not a place Orlando visits but a place imagined and remembered — a symbol of the urban literary world from which the country nobleman is excluded.

## Geographic Location

Fleet Street runs east from the Strand toward St Paul's Cathedral in the City of London. In the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries it was the centre of London's printing and bookselling trades, and the taverns along it — most famously the Cock Tavern and the Mermaid Tavern — were the gathering places of playwrights and poets. By the eighteenth century it had become the home of London's newspaper press.

## Nick Greene's Tavern Memory

The most vivid image of Fleet Street in the novel comes not from direct narration but from Nick Greene's nostalgic memory. Dining at Orlando's table, Greene recalls a legendary night among the Elizabethan playwrights:

"He could remember, he said, a night at the Cock Tavern in Fleet Street when Kit Marlowe was there and some others. Kit was in high feather, rather drunk, which he easily became, and in a mood to say silly things. He could see him now, brandishing his glass at the company and hiccuping out, 'Stap my vitals, Bill' (this was to Shakespeare), 'there's a great wave coming and you're on top of it,' and Shakespeare replied, 'Yes, I'm on top of it, and I'm staying there.'" (008-022)

This tavern scene — Marlowe drunk, Shakespeare confident, the Cock Tavern on Fleet Street as the crucible of English drama — is the novel's most concentrated image of literary history as lived experience. Woolf conjures the scene through Greene's voice, giving it the texture of authentic memory while simultaneously marking it as the self-serving nostalgia of a minor poet who measures his own importance by the greatness of those he once drank with.

## The Silence of the Country

Fleet Street serves as the counterpoint to Orlando's country estate. When Nick Greene stays at the great house, the silence and luxury become unbearable:

"Lying in bed of a morning on the softest pillows between the smoothest sheets and looking out of his oriel window upon turf which for three centuries had known neither dandelion nor dock weed, he thought that unless he could somehow make his escape, he should be smothered alive. Getting up and hearing the pigeons coo, dressing and hearing the fountains fall, he thought that unless he could hear the drays roar upon the cobbles of Fleet Street, he would never write another line." (008-026)

The "drays roar upon the cobbles of Fleet Street" becomes the sound of literary vitality itself. The country estate, for all its beauty and comfort, is a place where art suffocates. Fleet Street, for all its noise and vulgarity, is where art lives.

## The Coach Journey Home

In the eighteenth century, Fleet Street appears in a coach journey that maps Orlando's return to her London life. Riding with Mr. Pope through the London night, "the coach went from light to darkness down the Haymarket, along the Strand, up Fleet Street, and reached, at length, her house in Blackfriars." (010-078) The journey — Haymarket, Strand, Fleet Street, Blackfriars — traces the geography of literary London, each landmark a node in the cultural map of the city.

## Narrative Function

Fleet Street functions in the novel as a synecdoche for the literary life itself — noisy, competitive, alcoholic, and irreplaceable. It is the place Orlando cannot inhabit, separated from it by rank, wealth, and the isolating luxury of the country estate. In this sense, Fleet Street is not just a street but a measure of Orlando's distance from the creative world he admires: the world of Marlowe, Shakespeare, Jonson, and the tavern wits.
