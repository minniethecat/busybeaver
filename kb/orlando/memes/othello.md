# Othello

*Othello* (c. 1603–1604) is Shakespeare's tragedy of jealousy, race, and military honour. In *Orlando*, the play appears in the least heroic context imaginable: as one item in a triptych of tragedies that Nick Greene claims were dashed off in conditions of domestic and professional chaos.

## Textual Presentation

*Othello* enters the novel at the climax of Nick Greene's deflationary portrait of Elizabethan literary production. Having described a boozy evening at the Cock Tavern with Kit Marlowe — "Kit was in high feather, rather drunk" (008-022) — Greene pivots to the scandalous ordinariness of Shakespeare's working methods:

> "Their poetry was scribbled down on the backs of washing bills held to the heads of printer's devils at the street door. Thus Hamlet went to press; thus Lear; thus Othello. No wonder, as Greene said, that these plays show the faults they do. The rest of the time was spent in carousings and junketings in taverns and in beer gardens, when things were said that passed belief for wit, and things were done that made the utmost frolic of the court appear dull." (008-024)

The catalogue — *Hamlet*, *Lear*, *Othello* — constitutes a roll call of Shakespeare's supreme tragic achievements, presented with deliberate bathos. The word "thus" does the heavy lifting: it implies that the process of printing *Othello* was as mundane, as haphazard, and as compromised as the process of printing any other play. The specific mention of "washing bills" — the Elizabethan equivalent of a laundry receipt — grounds literary greatness in the grubby materiality of everyday life.

The sentence's three-beat rhythm — "Thus Hamlet went to press; thus Lear; thus Othello" — is itself a small formal joke. The rhetorical figure of *tricolon* (the rule of three) is the orator's most polished tool, deployed here to describe the very opposite of polish: haste, improvisation, and economic necessity.

## Narrative Function

Woolf's treatment of *Othello* — and of Shakespeare more broadly — in the Greene episode is part of the novel's systematic campaign against literary hero-worship. Throughout *Orlando*, great writers are shown not as demigods but as flawed, embodied, circumstantial human beings. Shakespeare scribbles on washing bills; Pope has a head that aches and a forehead "no bigger than another man's" (010-074); Swift carries a cane and walks in unannounced. The cumulative effect is not to diminish these writers but to complicate our picture of literary greatness — to insist that masterpieces emerge from the same material world that produces laundry lists and hangovers.

For Orlando, the Greene anecdote about *Othello* and its companion tragedies is a decisive moment in his literary education. The young nobleman, who has been producing his own derivative plays and poems in secret, is both delighted and disoriented by the revelation that his literary idols were "half drunken and all amorous" (008-024). The knowledge liberates him — if Shakespeare could write *Othello* amid "carousings and junketings," perhaps Orlando need not wait for perfect conditions to begin his own work. But it also wounds him: the glamour of the literary vocation, which had sustained his private ambitions, is revealed to be partly a fiction.
