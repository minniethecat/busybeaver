# Rustum

Rustum is Orlando's guide and companion during the Constantinople period. He is peripheral to the main action but provides a perspective outside the English social world that dominates the novel.

## Role Overview

Rustum appears during Orlando's ambassadorship to Constantinople, accompanying the English nobleman through the Ottoman world. As a non-European character, he represents the novel's occasional movement beyond English social horizons.

## Appearance and Identity

The narrator's treatment of Rustum reflects the novel's period-bound perspective on non-European characters — he is rendered in orientalist terms that the novel neither fully endorses nor explicitly critiques.

## In the Corpus: The Gipsy Interlude

Rustum's most detailed appearances come during the gipsy interlude after Orlando's transformation. The narrator presents him in close physical detail: he is someone whose pipe Orlando shares, accepting "a puff from old Rustum's pipe, filled though it was with cow dung" (009-046). This detail captures both the intimacy of Orlando's life among the gipsies and the material simplicity of their world.

Rustum functions as the philosophical voice of the gipsy tribe. When the young gipsies laugh at Orlando's strange English ideas, it is Rustum who takes her seriously: "But Rustum el Sadi, the old man who had brought Orlando out of Constantinople on his donkey, sat silent" (009-051). Later, "Rustum followed her out of the tent alone and said that she need not mind if her father were a Duke" — offering a form of kindness rooted in the gipsies' indifference to the hierarchies Orlando was raised to value (009-054).

His influence persists long after Orlando leaves the gipsies. Back in England, "even the bones of her ancestors, Sir Miles, Sir Gervase, and the rest, had lost something of their sanctity since Rustum el Sadi had waved his hand that night in the Asian mountains" (010-028). Rustum's sceptical gesture — a wave of the hand — has permanently altered Orlando's relationship to English history and ancestry.

## Echoes in Later Chapters

Rustum's voice haunts Orlando across the centuries. Even in the novel's final pages, in 1928, "the raucous voice of old Rustum, the gipsy, croaked in her ears, 'What is your antiquity and your race, and your possessions?'" (012-092). The question — which the gipsies ask of all English claims to superiority — becomes one of the novel's enduring refrains.

## Story Arc

Rustum's most significant role is as witness to the events surrounding Orlando's transformation. The political unrest in Constantinople, in which Rustum is involved, provides the external crisis that accompanies Orlando's internal metamorphosis.

## Character Relationships

- **With Orlando**: Guide and companion in the foreign world of Constantinople; later a permanent presence in her consciousness.

## Thematic Function

Rustum represents the world beyond England that Orlando briefly inhabits. His presence — and Orlando's sojourn among the gipsies after the transformation — provides a defamiliarising perspective on English customs and assumptions.

## Literary Criticism

> The novel's treatment of non-European characters has been critiqued for its orientalism, though some readings see Woolf's deployment of these figures as knowingly ironic — a reflection of the period perspectives the novel simultaneously inhabits and satirises.
