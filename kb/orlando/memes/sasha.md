# Sasha

The Princess Marousha Stanilovska Dagmar Natasha Iliana Romanovitch — known simply as Sasha — is the Russian princess who arrives in London with the Muscovite Embassy during the Great Frost. She becomes Orlando's first great love and his first great heartbreak, her departure with the thaw marking the end of his youthful innocence.

## Role Overview

Sasha appears in the narrative as a figure of exotic androgyny. When Orlando first sees her skating on the frozen Thames, her gender is ambiguous: "a figure, which, whether boy's or woman's, for the loose tunic and trousers of the Russian fashion served to disguise the sex, filled him with the highest curiosity" (004-063). This ambiguity is central to her appeal — she embodies the fluidity of desire that the novel celebrates.

## Appearance and Identity

Sasha is dressed in "oyster-coloured velvet, trimmed with some unfamiliar greenish-coloured fur" (004-063). She is "about middle height, very slenderly fashioned," and possesses "the extraordinary seductiveness which issued from the whole person" (004-063). Orlando's response is a cascade of sensory metaphors:

> He called her a melon, a pineapple, an olive tree, an emerald, and a fox in the snow all in the space of three seconds; he did not know whether he had heard her, tasted her, seen her, or all three together. (004-063)

Her name, revealed to be "the Princess Marousha Stanilovska Dagmar Natasha Iliana Romanovitch," is itself a performance of exotic excess. She is "called" Sasha — a diminutive that domesticates the elaborate foreign title.

## Story Arc

### The Great Frost Romance

Sasha arrives with the Muscovite Embassy during the winter of 1608, when the Thames freezes solid. She and Orlando meet at the carnival on the ice and their romance unfolds against the backdrop of the frozen river. They skate, they whisper in French, and they slip away from the court to explore London together.

### The Betrayal

Orlando discovers Sasha alone with a Russian sailor on the Embassy's ship, frozen into the ice. The discovery prefigures the larger betrayal to come. When the thaw arrives, Sasha leaves with the departing Embassy without a word of farewell:

> Hastily, he made count of them all; the French; the Spanish; the Austrian; the Turk. All still floated... But the Russian ship was nowhere to be seen. (004-127)

### Orlando's Aftermath

Orlando's grief is seismic. He falls into a trance-like sleep that lasts seven days, and when he wakes, his innocence is gone. Sasha's betrayal marks the end of the Elizabethan chapter and the beginning of Orlando's emotional adulthood.

## Literary Criticism

> Sasha has been interpreted as a representation of Vita Sackville-West's own romantic elusiveness in Woolf's imagination. The Russian princess who captivates and then abandons the protagonist mirrors the pattern of Woolf's infatuation with Sackville-West — a love that was real but could never be fully possessed.

Woolf's treatment of Sasha also draws on the literary tradition of the exotic femme fatale, while complicating it through the character's androgyny and independence. Sasha is not a villain but a free agent whose freedom — to come, to love, to leave — is what makes her desirable in the first place.
