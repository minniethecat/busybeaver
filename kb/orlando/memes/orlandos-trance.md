# Orlando's Trance

Orlando's seven-day trance is the novel's most mythic event — the deep, impenetrable sleep during which Orlando changes from man to woman. Falling asleep as the Duke, "sunk in profound slumber amid bedclothes that were much tumbled" (009-013), Orlando wakes on the seventh day to the trumpet blast of "Truth!" and "stood upright in complete nakedness before us, and while the trumpets pealed Truth! Truth! Truth! we have no choice left but confess — he was a woman" (009-040)(009-041). The trance is framed by an elaborate allegorical masque in which Purity, Chastity, and Modesty are each summoned and then dismissed, clearing the way for the truth of Orlando's transformed body.

## Event Background

Orlando has been serving as British Ambassador to Constantinople, elevated to the rank of Duke after extraordinary success in negotiating a treaty with the Sultan. His life follows a pleasant routine: rising about seven, wrapping himself in a Turkish cloak, lighting a cheroot, and leaning on the parapet (009-002). He has become the Sultan's favourite, and the city holds him in high esteem.

However, Lieutenant Brigge has detected signs of an impending insurrection among the native population. The tension builds, and on the seventh day of Orlando's trance — "Thursday, May the 10th" — "the first shot was fired of that terrible and bloody insurrection" (009-014). The Turks rise against the Sultan, set fire to the town, and "put every foreigner they could find either to the sword or to the bastinado" (009-014). Orlando sleeps through it all.

## Event Course

### The Sleep

On the night before, the Embassy had been "empty of company, and shut up for the night in the usual way" (009-012). But "nobody has ever known exactly what took place later that night" (009-012). The sentries' testimony offers no clarity — only that by morning, "the Duke, as we must now call him, was found by his secretaries sunk in profound slumber amid bedclothes that were much tumbled" (009-013). His coronet has rolled to the floor; his cloak and garter are "flung all of a heap on a chair"; papers of state litter the table (009-013). He does not wake.

Orlando sleeps for seven days while the insurrection rages outside. The trance is total, absolute — a withdrawal from history and identity alike.

### The Masque

Woolf interrupts the violence with an allegorical interlude. Three female figures enter the sleeping chamber: Our Lady of Purity, Our Lady of Chastity, and Our Lady of Modesty. Each delivers a rhetorical self-description and is answered by a trumpet blast and the cry "Avaunt! Begone!"

Our Lady of Purity speaks first: "I am the guardian of the sleeping fawn; the snow is dear to me; and the moon rising; and the silver sea. With my robes I cover the speckled hen's eggs and the brindled sea shell; I cover vice and poverty" (009-017). The trumpets peal, and the cry goes up: "Purity Avaunt! Begone Purity!" (009-019).

Our Lady of Chastity follows: "I am she whose touch freezes and whose glance turns to stone. I have stayed the star in its dancing, and the wave as it falls... Rather than let Orlando wake, I will freeze him to the bone" (009-021). Again, trumpets: "Chastity Avaunt! Begone Chastity!" (009-023).

Our Lady of Modesty speaks last, so low one can hardly hear: "I am she that men call Modesty. Virgin I am and ever shall be. Not for me the fruitful fields and the fertile vineyard. Increase is odious to me" (009-025).

The three figures join hands, making "gestures of farewell and despair towards the bed where Orlando lies sleeping" (009-036), and flee — "waving their draperies over their heads, as if to shut out something that they dare not look upon" (009-037).

### The Awakening

With Purity, Chastity, and Modesty banished, the trumpeters — who have been in the room all along — "ranging themselves side by side in order, blow one terrific blast: 'The Truth!'" (009-038)(009-039).

>
> at which Orlando woke.
>
> He stretched himself. He rose. He stood upright in complete nakedness before us, and while the trumpets pealed Truth! Truth! Truth! we have no choice left but confess — he was a woman. (009-039)(009-040)(009-041)

The revelation is at once absolute and rendered in comic anti-climax. Woolf presents the transformation as fact, not as crisis. "Orlando had become a woman — there is no denying it. But in every other respect, Orlando remained precisely as he had been. The change of sex, though it altered their future, did nothing whatever to alter their identity" (009-044).

## Narrative Significance

The trance is the novel's central pivot — the moment that divides Orlando's life into "before" and "after." By presenting the sex change during impenetrable sleep, Woolf refuses to explain, justify, or psychologize it. The transformation is a mystery that the text can only surround with allegory and declare with trumpets.

The masque of Purity, Chastity, and Modesty is a virtuoso set piece that does multiple things at once: it satirizes the conventions of Renaissance and Restoration masque; it dramatizes the cultural forces that police gender and sexuality; and it clears symbolic space for a truth that those forces cannot countenance. The three allegorical ladies — the "coverers" and "hiders" — must be expelled before Orlando can stand revealed.

Most radical is Woolf's insistence on continuity: "The change of sex, though it altered their future, did nothing whatever to alter their identity" (009-044). Orlando's self persists unchanged across the gender binary — a claim so provocative that Woolf immediately retreats into irony: "But let other pens treat of sex and sexuality; we quit such odious subjects as soon as we can" (009-045).

## Theme Connections

- **Gender and transformation**: The trance is the novel's most literal engagement with the transformation theme — but Woolf insists transformation is bodily, not psychological. Orlando's identity remains continuous. "Their faces remained, as their portraits prove, practically the same" (009-044).
- **Truth and revelation**: The trumpet blast — "Truth! Truth! Truth!" — frames gender revelation as a moment of enlightenment, not catastrophe. The truth about Orlando's body is what the allegorical ladies (social convention) could not face.
- **Sleep and awakening**: The trance is one of several moments in the novel where consciousness is suspended — like the frozen Thames, like Orlando's visionary states. Sleep is the medium through which transformation becomes possible.
- **Allegory and satire**: The masque is Woolf at her most playful — parodying courtly entertainment while making serious claims about what society's guardians of purity cannot tolerate.

## Historical Authenticity

The trance is entirely fictional, set during the historical context of diplomatic service in Ottoman Constantinople. The insurrection is based loosely on the political instability of the Ottoman Empire in the seventeenth century, but the trance itself, the masque, and the miraculous transformation are Woolf's invention, drawing on traditions of allegorical masque in English Renaissance literature.
