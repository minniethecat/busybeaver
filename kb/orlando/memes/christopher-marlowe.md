# Christopher Marlowe

**Christopher Marlowe** (1564–1593) was the foremost Elizabethan playwright before Shakespeare, the author of *Doctor Faustus*, *Tamburlaine*, and *The Jew of Malta*. In *Orlando*, Marlowe appears not directly but through the recollections of Nicholas Greene, the poet-critic whose visit to Orlando's estate in Chapter II offers the novel's most sustained portrait of Elizabethan literary culture. He returns in the novel's imaginative geography decades later, when Orlando's mind summons his name among the great poets whose lines "began booming and reverberating" through her consciousness.

## The Cock Tavern Scene

Greene, reminiscing about an evening at the Cock Tavern in Fleet Street, conjures Marlowe with the affectionate irreverence of personal memory:

> "Kit Marlowe was there and some others. Kit was in high feather, rather drunk, which he easily became, and in a mood to say silly things" [008-022].

The image of Marlowe "brandishing his glass at the company" and hiccuping out "Stap my vitals, Bill" to Shakespeare transforms the monumental author of *Doctor Faustus* into a flesh-and-blood participant in a convivial, competitive tavern culture [008-022]. Marlowe's reported prophecy — "there's a great wave coming and you're on top of it" — is doubly ironic: delivered by a drunk man who would be dead within two nights, it is also, from the reader's vantage, perfectly accurate about Shakespeare's subsequent achievement.

Greene's account frames Marlowe in the peculiar light of literary rivalry. He claims that Shakespeare "had taken them chiefly from Marlowe" — a charge of borrowing that, whether fair or not, registers the younger playwright's acknowledged debt to his predecessor [008-020]. Marlowe's mighty line — the blank verse innovation that transformed English dramatic poetry — had paved the way for Shakespeare's own achievements, making him, in Greene's dismissive but illuminating phrase, a source to be plundered rather than a genius to be revered.

## Orlando's Literary Pantheon

Marlowe's name reverberates beyond the Cock Tavern scene. Returning to England after her transformation, Orlando's mind, stirred by the sight of St. Paul's dome, turns to poetry:

> "The distraction of sex, which hers was, and what it meant, subsided; she thought now only of the glory of poetry, and the great lines of Marlowe, Shakespeare, Ben Jonson, Milton began booming and reverberating, as if a golden clapper beat against a golden bell in the cathedral tower which was her mind" [049-048].

In this passage, Marlowe's name stands at the head of the English poetic tradition — the first name Orlando's mind reaches for in its catalogue of literary greatness. The architectural imagery (the dome, the bell, the cathedral tower) yokes Marlowe's blank verse to the physical fabric of London, suggesting that the city's monuments and the nation's poetry are constructed from the same imaginative material.

## Greene's Return and the Victorian Judgment

When Nicholas Greene reappears in the Victorian era — transformed from a shabby Elizabethan hack into a prosperous man of letters — he delivers a retrospective verdict on the literary giants of his youth:

> "Ah! my dear lady, the great days of literature are over. Marlowe, Shakespeare, Ben Jonson — those were the giants. Dryden, Pope, Addison — those were the heroes. All, all are dead now" [012-033].

The judgment is characteristically Greene — always declaring the present inferior to the past, always positioning himself as the keeper of vanished standards. But the arc of Marlowe's reputation across the three centuries of Orlando's life is instructive: the playwright who was, in Greene's Elizabethan account, merely a "likely boy" who "died before he was thirty" has become, by the Victorian era, one of the "giants" [008-020]. The passage of time has done for Marlowe what it does for all artists in *Orlando*: transformed the contingent judgments of contemporaries into the settled verdicts of tradition, even as the terms of those verdicts remain comically unstable.

## Literary Significance

Woolf's treatment of Marlowe exemplifies the novel's double movement in representing literary celebrity: the great are brought down to human scale through the textures of lived experience — drunkenness, boastfulness, the heat of a tavern room — but this humanization reads not as diminishment but as the necessary condition of literary achievement. The Marlowe who brandishes his glass is the same Marlowe who wrote *Tamburlaine*; the novel insists that these two figures are, and must be, one.
