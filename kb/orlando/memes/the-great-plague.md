# The Great Plague

The **Great Plague** of 1665 was the last major epidemic of bubonic plague in England, killing an estimated 100,000 people — nearly a quarter of London's population. In *Orlando*, the plague functions not as a dramatized event but as an invisible historical force, one of several catastrophes that reshape the world during the protagonist's absence. Its narrative treatment exemplifies Woolf's characteristic strategy of compressing events of immense human consequence into subordinate clauses, while expanding moments of private reverie across pages.

## Narrative Treatment

When Orlando returns to England after her transformation in Constantinople and sojourn with the gypsies, she finds London fundamentally altered. As the ship sails up the Thames, Captain Bartolus narrates the transformed cityscape:

> "That, then, was the dome of St. Paul's which Mr. Wren had built during her absence. Nearby, a shock of golden hair burst from a pillar — Captain Bartolus was at her side to inform her that that was the Monument; there had been a plague and a fire during her absence, he said" [049-058].

The plague enters the narrative, characteristically, in a subordinate clause within reported speech — a disaster filtered through a sea captain's casual tour-guide commentary, its horror already receding into the commemorative architecture of the Monument and the rebuilt cathedral. St. Paul's itself had burned in the Great Fire of 1666, which followed the plague; its new dome, designed by Sir Christopher Wren, dominated a skyline Orlando no longer recognized.

The passage continues to catalogue the visible transformations: "London itself had completely changed since she had last seen it. Then, she remembered, it had been a huddle of little black, beetle-browed houses. The heads of rebels had grinned on pikes at Temple Bar. The cobbled pavements had reeked of garbage and ordure" [049-058]. The London Orlando now sees — "broad and orderly thoroughfares," plate-glass windows, "ladies in flowered silk" walking on raised footpaths — is a city remade by catastrophe and reconstruction [049-058].

## Historical Time and Narrative Scale

Woolf's treatment of the plague is characteristically oblique. Unlike the Great Frost of 1608, which receives an extended set-piece description across the final pages of Chapter I, the plague is compressed into a single reported line — an event that happens offstage, its devastation measurable only in the transformed city Orlando confronts upon her return. This asymmetry reflects the novel's distinctive treatment of historical time: events of immense human consequence — plague, fire, the deaths of monarchs — pass in a paragraph, while a moment of private reverie may fill pages. By withholding the plague from direct representation, Woolf emphasizes the gap between historical magnitude and narrative attention, reinforcing *Orlando*'s implicit argument about the limitations of conventional historiography.

The Great Plague also belongs to a pattern of absences that structure the novel's treatment of history. Just as Orlando sleeps through the Restoration and travels through the eighteenth century in a haze, the plague occurs in the gap between her departure and return — a catastrophe that, like so much of history, happens elsewhere, to other people, while the protagonist is looking the other way.
