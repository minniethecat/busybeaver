# The Rape of the Lock

*The Rape of the Lock* (1712, expanded 1714) is Alexander Pope's mock-epic poem, widely regarded as the finest example of the genre in English. In *Orlando*, Woolf treats the poem not merely as a literary reference but as a hermeneutic device: reading a page or two of *The Rape of the Lock*, she suggests, tells us more about Pope's character — and about Orlando's response to him — than any narrator could.

## Textual Presentation

*The Rape of the Lock* appears in Woolf's metafictional aside as one of three texts that together unlock the Augustan age:

> "If the reader will here refer to the Rape of the Lock, to the Spectator, to Gulliver's Travels, he will understand precisely what these mysterious words may mean. Indeed, biographers and critics might save themselves all their labours if readers would only take this advice." (010-080)

The "mysterious words" in question are the narrator's catalogue of trivialities — Pope "suffered with his head," Addison "scented his handkerchiefs," Swift "had a fine malacca cane" (010-080). Woolf's claim is audacious: that these mundane details, filtered through the lens of each writer's style, become legible as expressions of character.

After this directive, Woolf returns to *The Rape of the Lock* as an emotional index for Orlando's state of mind:

> "So, now that we have read a page or two of the Rape of the Lock, we know exactly why Orlando was so much amused and so much frightened, and so very bright-cheeked and bright-eyed that afternoon." (010-082)

The poem thus functions as a kind of atmospheric tuning fork — its elegant, razor-sharp satire of social vanity provides the exact emotional register for Orlando's experience of Pope's company. Orlando's "amused and frightened" response mirrors the poem's own double effect: its glittering surface and its savage undercurrent.

The carriage scene with Pope (010-074) enacts this doubleness in miniature: in the alternating light and darkness, Orlando oscillates between adoration and disillusionment, mistaking the hump of a cushion for the poet's noble brow — a comic deflation worthy of Pope himself.

## Narrative Function

*The Rape of the Lock* performs a triple function in Woolf's narrative. First, it serves as historical synecdoche: the poem stands for the entire Augustan mode of wit, polish, and ironic distance. Second, it functions as a characterological shortcut: reading Pope's poetry, Woolf argues, is a more reliable route to Pope's personality than any biographical account. Third, it provides the emotional gamut — amusement, fright, brightness — that structures Orlando's encounter with literary genius.

The invocation of Pope's poem also participates in the novel's broader meditation on the relationship between literature and life. Throughout *Orlando*, literary works are not inert cultural artifacts but live presences that shape how characters perceive and are perceived. *The Rape of the Lock* epitomises this: its spirit infuses the Chapter IV salon scenes, giving them their particular tonal blend of elegance and acid.
