# Wapping

Wapping is the riverside district east of the Tower of London that serves as a recurrent marginal space in *Orlando* — a place of sailors, dockworkers, and the "low company" that the aristocratic Orlando is drawn to throughout the novel.

## Geographic Location

Wapping lies on the north bank of the Thames in East London, beyond the Tower. Historically a dockland district of wharves, taverns, and maritime commerce, it was the point of departure and arrival for ships sailing to and from London. The narrator notes Orlando's affinity for such places, attributing it to "a certain grandmother of his" who "had worn a smock and carried milk-pails" — "some grains of the Kentish or Sussex earth were mixed with the thin, fine fluid which came to him from Normandy." (007-018)

## The Thaw at Wapping

After the Frost Fair's dissolution and Sasha's betrayal, Orlando finds himself at Wapping's riverbank at dawn. "A sight of the most extraordinary nature met his eyes. Where, for three months and more, there had been solid ice of such thickness that it seemed permanent as stone, and a whole gay city had been built upon it, was now a race of turbulent yellow waters." (007-058) The Thames, released from its icy suspension, rages with the violence of thaw — "here, huge masses of ice had come into collision and had raised themselves up, creaking and groaning, to rest on end like so many pinnacles of marble." It is among these icebergs that Orlando watches a party of stranded wretches swept away, a spectacle of indifferent nature that mirrors his own emotional devastation.

## The Return Voyage

When Orlando returns to England as a woman, the ship sails past Wapping, and the sight triggers an overwhelming flood of memory. Captain Bartolus, guiding her through the London approach, cannot know that for Orlando the view of Wapping Old Stairs summons "the dark night, the monstrous downpour, the violent surges of the flood" of three centuries earlier. (010-015) The place is palimpsestic: the Elizabethan Frost, Sasha's betrayal, the frozen bumboat woman with her apples — all superimposed upon the orderly eighteenth-century waterfront.

## Narrative Function

Wapping embodies the novel's geography of class. Orlando's repeated visits to Wapping — to the beer gardens, the dockside taverns, the "low company" of lettered wits — represent an aristocratic compulsion toward the demimonde. The district is where the novel's social boundaries blur: courtiers and sailors, dukes and dockworkers, the frozen carnival that erases all distinctions of rank.
