# John Donne

John Donne (1572–1631) is the English metaphysical poet, preacher, and Dean of St. Paul's Cathedral. In *Orlando*, he appears not as a character but as a reference point — a name dropped by Nick Greene amidst the flood of literary culture that Orlando navigates in the modern age. His mention signals the weight of the English literary canon against which Orlando's more private, protean art stands.

## In *Orlando*

John Donne is mentioned by name once in the novel, in Chapter VI. Orlando, transformed into a woman and newly returned from Constantinople, has settled into eighteenth-century London literary society. She has been invited into the circle of Mr. Addison's coffee-house wit and is absorbing the literary currents of the age. But she finds that Addison, Pope, and the celebrated writers leave her cold:

> "A toy boat, a toy boat, a toy boat," she repeated, thus enforcing upon herself the fact that it is not articles by Nick Greene or John Donne nor eight-hour bills nor covenant nor factory acts that matter; it's something useless, sudden, violent; something that costs a life; red, purple, blue; a spurt of water like a water-jet.
>
> (012-045)

The pairing of Nick Greene with John Donne is pointed. Greene, the fictional critic who has somehow survived for two centuries, is placed alongside Donne, the canonical poet. Orlando's dismissal of both — grouping "articles by Nick Greene or John Donne" with parliamentary bills and factory acts — signals her discovery that literature, even at its most celebrated, is not the thing that matters most. The true subject of writing, she realises, is the "useless, sudden, violent" thing — the ineffable experience that costs "a life" and cannot be captured in the formal categories of literary history.

## Thematic Role

Donne represents the English literary canon as a system — a body of work so established, so thoroughly assimilated into the national literary consciousness, that to invoke his name is to invoke a standard. Orlando's casual grouping of Donne with Greene — the fictional and the real, the parochial critic and the immortal poet — expresses the novel's characteristic flattening of literary hierarchies. In Woolf's conception, the oak tree of Orlando's private poem is as real and as important as the canonised works of Donne.

## Literary-Historical Context

John Donne's poetry — his Songs and Sonnets, his Holy Sonnets, his Anniversaries — is characterised by the metaphysical conceit: the extended metaphor that yokes together disparate domains (love and astronomy, devotion and geography). Woolf admired the metaphysical poets, and Sir Thomas Browne — another of Orlando's literary models — writes in a prose style deeply influenced by the metaphysical tradition. Donne appears in *Orlando* not by accident but as part of a web of seventeenth-century English prose and poetry that Woolf valued for its density, its complexity, and its resistance to the smooth surfaces of Victorian realism.
