**The Gipsy** is an unnamed member — and implicit leader — of the gipsy tribe that Orlando joins after departing Constantinople following the transformation. The gipsy figures embody the novel's meditation on the relationship between nature and civilization, offering a way of life radically different from the English aristocratic world Orlando has known.

## Role in the Novel

After waking as a woman, Orlando leaves Constantinople with a gipsy tribe and lives among them in the hills near Brusa. The gipsies accept Orlando without the rigid gender expectations of English society, and their relationship to nature — direct, unmediated, unsentimental — provides a foil to the aesthetic and literary sensibilities Orlando has cultivated.

The gipsy's critique of Orlando's love of nature ("it was not nature but the love of nature that possessed Orlando") is one of the novel's sharpest philosophical moments: the gipsy sees Orlando's attachment to landscape as a form of ownership rather than true belonging. This encounter forces Orlando to reassess the values of the civilisation left behind.

## Significance

The gipsy episode serves as a structural hinge in the novel, bridging Orlando's male and female lives and providing an external vantage point from which English society can be seen afresh. The gipsy's worldview — practical, communal, anti-aesthetic — challenges the novel's own discursive mode.
