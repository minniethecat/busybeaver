# The Wild Goose

The **wild goose** is the central symbolic motif of *Orlando*, representing the elusive object of artistic and poetic pursuit — the perfect phrase, the definitive poem, the truth that perpetually recedes beyond the writer's grasp. It appears at key moments across the novel, haunting Orlando from childhood to the final page, and serves as the novel's most sustained figure for the artistic vocation itself.

## Early Appearances

The wild goose motif enters the novel obliquely, embedded in Woolf's characteristic attention to the natural world. Early in the narrative, Orlando walks "very quickly uphill through ferns and hawthorn bushes, startling deer and wild birds, to a place crowned by a single oak tree" — the wild birds flushed from their cover prefiguring the symbolic goose that will become the dominant image of Orlando's artistic longing [007-018].

Later, during Orlando's flight across the moor after Shelmerdine's departure, the wild bird returns in a different register. Descending into a state of rapturous communion with nature, Orlando watches rooks wheeling overhead and collects their fallen feathers:

> "She loved wild birds' feathers. She had used to collect them as a boy. She picked it up and stuck it in her hat" [012-074].

What begins as a childhood habit — the collection of wild birds' feathers — is here elevated into a figure for the consolations of solitude. Orlando's ecstatic union with the moor, culminating in her declaration "I am nature's bride," is mediated through the feathers she gathers: "These are wild birds' feathers — the owl's, the nightjar's. I shall dream wild dreams" [012-074]. The feathers function as tokens of an alternative life — one free of marriage, society, and the demands of biographical narrative itself.

## The Great Speech

The wild goose achieves its fullest expression in Chapter VI, as Orlando drives through the London suburbs toward her country estate. The image, accumulated over four centuries of experience, erupts into the novel's most celebrated passage:

> "Haunted! ever since I was a child. There flies the wild goose. It flies past the window out to sea. Up I jumped and stretched after it. But the goose flies too fast. I've seen it, here—there—there—England, Persia, Italy. Always it flies fast out to sea and always I fling after it words like nets which shrivel as I've seen nets shrivel drawn on deck with only seaweed in them; and sometimes there's an inch of silver—six words—in the bottom of the net. But never the great fish who lives in the coral groves" [012-081].

The wild goose chase — a proverbial phrase for a futile pursuit — is here transformed into a figure for the artistic vocation itself. The nets Orlando flings are words, and the "inch of silver" she sometimes retrieves represents the fragmentary successes that sustain a lifetime of writing. The image reconciles two competing attitudes toward artistic labour: the Romantic faith in inspired utterance (the goose as muse, the poet as inspired pursuer) and the Modernist acknowledgment of inevitable incompleteness (the great fish always eludes the net, the perfect poem is never written).

The geographical range — "England, Persia, Italy" — maps Orlando's four-hundred-year pursuit across the full span of the novel, making the wild goose the connective thread that binds the disparate episodes of the biography into a single artistic quest.

## The Closing Image

At the novel's close, as midnight strikes on "Thursday, the eleventh of October, Nineteen Hundred and Twenty-eight," the wild goose makes its final appearance. Shelmerdine returns in an aeroplane, the machinery of the modern world descending upon the ancient estate:

> "And as Shelmerdine, now grown a fine sea captain, hale, fresh-coloured, and alert, leapt to the ground, there sprang up over his head a single wild bird." "It is the goose!" Orlando cried. "The wild goose…" [012-102].

The novel ends on this image — the wild goose made momentarily present, the symbol briefly touching the earth it has circled for four hundred years. The ellipsis that follows Orlando's exclamation is the novel's final punctuation, suspending the narrative in the instant of arrival without resolution. Has the goose been caught, or has it merely passed closer than before? The ambiguity is deliberate, and it encodes the novel's deepest wisdom about the artistic life: the great fish may never be landed, but the inch of silver — the six words at the bottom of the net — may be enough.
