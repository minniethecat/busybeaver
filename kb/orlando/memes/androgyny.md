**Androgyny** is a central concept in *Orlando* and in Virginia Woolf's broader thinking about gender. Orlando's transformation from man to woman is not a simple switch but a revelation of the androgynous nature that was always present — "in every human being a vacillation from one sex to the other takes place," the novel's biographer observes.

## Manifestations in the Novel

Orlando's gender transformation literalises Woolf's belief in the androgyny of the creative mind. After the transformation, Orlando retains memories, habits, and preferences from life as a man, while acquiring new perspectives and experiences as a woman. The novel insists that this doubleness is not anomalous but universal — that the rigid separation of masculine and feminine is a social fiction.

The character of Archduke Harry / Archduchess Harriet provides a comic mirror of this theme, cross-dressing to pursue Orlando across gender boundaries. The novel's treatment of these shifts is consistently playful rather than polemical, yet the philosophical implications are serious.

## Literary-Critical Context

Woolf developed the concept of the androgynous mind most explicitly in *A Room of One's Own* (1929), published the year after *Orlando*. She argued that the fully creative mind is "resonant and porous," able to transmit emotion "without impediment," and that this requires the transcendence of gender — a thesis that *Orlando* dramatises in narrative form.
