The **Publication of The Oak Tree** is the event by which Orlando's long poem — composed and revised over more than three hundred years — is finally published and wins a literary prize. The event brings a major thematic arc of the novel to resolution: the relationship between the artist and the work, between persistence and recognition.

## In the Novel

Nick Greene, now an eminent Victorian critic, encourages Orlando to publish the poem. To Orlando's astonishment, "The Oak Tree" wins a prize — the very prize that Greene himself had once coveted. The irony is characteristic: the poem that Orlando wrote in obscurity, for no audience but the oak tree itself, achieves public acclaim, while the careerist Greene has settled into the role of literary gatekeeper.

The publication occurs in the Victorian period, an age that Woolf portrays as oppressively domestic and convention-bound — making Orlando's literary success all the more incongruous and satisfying.
