**History and Historiography** are twin concerns of *Orlando*: the novel is a work of imaginative history (tracing three centuries of English life) and simultaneously a critique of how history is written. The biographer's struggles to capture Orlando's life mirror the historian's struggle to capture the past.

## In the Novel

*Orlando* treats history not as a neutral backdrop but as a shaping force — each age has its "spirit" that determines what can be thought, felt, and written. Yet the novel also insists on the idiosyncratic, the personal, the resistant: Orlando survives across ages precisely by not fully conforming to any one of them.

The novel's historiographical method owes much to Lytton Strachey's *Eminent Victorians* (1918), which revolutionised biography by treating its subjects with ironic detachment. Woolf extends Strachey's method into fantasy, creating a historiography in which the impossible (a three-hundred-year lifespan, a change of sex) illuminates the actual.
