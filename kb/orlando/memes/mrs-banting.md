Mrs. Banting is the midwife who delivers Orlando's son during the Victorian era in Chapter VI of Virginia Woolf's *Orlando*. Though a minor character who appears in only a single sentence, her role marks a pivotal moment in Orlando's biography: the transition into motherhood.

## Textual Appearance

Mrs. Banting's sole appearance in the novel is brief but momentous:

> "It's a very fine boy, M'Lady," said Mrs. Banting, the midwife, putting her firstborn child into Orlando's arms. In other words, Orlando was safely delivered of a son. [012-058]

The clinical, even comic, brevity of this announcement — "In other words, Orlando was safely delivered of a son" — is characteristic of Woolf's treatment of biological events in the novel. Birth, like death, is acknowledged but not dwelt upon; the biographer-narrator moves briskly past the physical to the social and symbolic.

## Narrative Function

Mrs. Banting serves as a functional character — the necessary agent of Orlando's delivery — but her presence also signifies several thematic elements:

- **Victorian domesticity**: The midwife embodies the Victorian era's medicalization and professionalization of childbirth, contrasting with earlier periods' less formal arrangements.
- **Female community**: Mrs. Banting represents the quiet, practical network of women — midwives, servants, shop assistants — who enable the lives of the novel's more prominent figures.
- **The ordinary within the extraordinary**: Orlando's fantastical biography — spanning centuries and changing sex — is punctuated by moments of ordinary human existence, of which childbirth attended by a midwife is the most elemental.
