# The Great Frost

The Great Frost of 1608 is the novel's first major set piece — a winter so severe that the River Thames freezes "to a depth of twenty feet and more for six or seven miles on either side" (007-061), transforming London into a carnival on ice. It is during this frozen spectacle that Orlando meets the Russian princess Sasha, falls devastatingly in love, and suffers the betrayal that shapes his emotional life for centuries.

## Event Background

The Frost arrives in the Elizabethan winter of Orlando's sixteenth year, after a sequence of casual love affairs that leave him restless. Orlando has been betrothed to Euphrosyne, one of the Irish Desmonds, and "matters had gone so far that the lawyers on both sides were busy with covenants, jointures, settlements" (007-057) when the climate itself intervenes. Woolf frames the Frost as both meteorological event and narrative pivot — an interruption of the ordinary that enables the extraordinary.

The weather, Woolf notes, was of "another temper altogether" in the Elizabethan age: "Sunsets were redder and more intense; dawns were whiter and more auroral... The rain fell vehemently, or not at all" (007-043). This climatic extremity mirrors the emotional extremes of the era, and the Frost becomes the ultimate expression of a world in which "violence was all" (007-043).

## Event Course

### The Freeze

The Frost's severity is rendered through a catalogue of surreal images: "Birds froze in midair and fell like stones to the ground" (007-059). At Norwich, a country woman crossing the road is "seen by the onlookers to turn visibly to powder and be blown in a puff of dust over the roofs" (007-059). Corpses freeze in their beds; whole herds of swine stand "frozen immovable upon the road" (007-059); ploughmen, shepherds, and bird-scaring boys are "struck stark in the act of the moment" (007-059). The petrifaction is so complete that some of the frozen wayfarers are later mistaken for natural rock formations in Derbyshire.

### The Carnival

While the countryside suffers, London transforms the disaster into festivity. King James I "directed that the river... should be swept, decorated, and given all the semblance of a park or pleasure ground, with arbours, mazes, alleys, drinking booths" (007-061). A Royal enclosure, "railed off from the public only by a silken rope" (007-061), becomes the centre of courtly life:

> Great statesmen, in their beards and ruffs, dispatched affairs of state under the crimson awning of the Royal Pagoda. Soldiers planned the conquest of the Moor and the downfall of the Turk in striped arbours surmounted by plumes of ostrich feathers. Admirals strode up and down the narrow pathways, glass in hand, sweeping the horizon and telling stories of the northwest passage and the Spanish Armada. (007-061)

At night the carnival reaches its peak: "the moon and stars blazed with the hard fixity of diamonds, and to the fine music of flute and trumpet the courtiers danced" (007-061). Beneath the ice, frozen porpoises, flounders, and shoals of eels lie suspended in a state "which puzzled the philosophers" (007-061) — death or suspended animation.

### The Meeting

At a banquet under a vast awning, Orlando is seated opposite the Princess Marousha Stanilovska Dagmar Natasha Iliana Romanovitch — "Sasha," as he calls her — a member of the Muscovite Embassy. When the two English lords beside her prove incapable of speaking French, Sasha catches Orlando's eye across the table and laughs. "He laughed, but the laugh on his lips froze in wonder. Whom had he loved, what had he loved, he asked himself in a tumult of emotion, until now?" (007-067).

Orlando's transformation is immediate and total. "Love had meant to him nothing but sawdust and cinders" (007-067); now, "the thickness of his blood melted; the ice turned to wine in his veins; he heard the waters flowing and the birds singing; spring broke over the hard wintry landscape" (007-067).

### The Secret Days

Orlando and Sasha slip away from the court into the frozen reaches of the Thames where "save for seabirds and some old country woman hacking at the ice... not a living soul ever came their way" (007-081). They have the frozen river to themselves:

> Hot with skating and with love they would throw themselves down in some solitary reach, where the yellow osiers fringed the bank, and wrapped in a great fur cloak Orlando would take her in his arms, and know, for the first time, he murmured, the delights of love. (007-081)

Orlando ransacks language for images to capture Sasha: "Snow, cream, marble, cherries, alabaster, golden wire? None of these. She was like a fox, or an olive tree; like the waves of the sea... like nothing he had seen or known in England" (007-087). English, he finds, is "too frank, too candid, too honeyed a speech for Sasha" (007-087), whose every openness conceals something withheld.

### The Betrayal

The thaw comes with violence. On the night they plan to elope, a storm breaks. Orlando waits in the courtyard as "great guns seemed to boom. Huge noises as of the tearing and rending of oak trees could be heard" (007-123). St. Paul's strikes midnight, then one, then two. Sasha never comes. Orlando, "crying aloud with an awful irony, and all his teeth showing, '*Jour de ma vie!*'" (007-123), dashes his lantern to the ground and gallops away into the storm. The Frost that brought them together has ended in flood, flight, and the first great wound of Orlando's life.

## Narrative Significance

The Great Frost is the novel's originating trauma. Orlando's love for Sasha and her betrayal establish the emotional pattern — intense attachment followed by devastating loss — that recurs across his four-century life. The Frost also marks Orlando's first experience of time's elasticity: the weeks on the ice feel eternal while they last, yet in memory they contract to a single vivid image.

Structurally, the Frost is the novel's first demonstration of Woolf's method — using an extreme natural event to compress history, psychology, and comedy into a single sustained set piece. The juxtaposition of cosmic cold with human passion, of public carnival with private betrayal, establishes the tonal range the novel will sustain throughout.

## Theme Connections

- **Love and desire**: The Frost is Orlando's first experience of passionate love, and its ending teaches him that love is inseparable from loss. The pattern echoes across centuries, through Archduke Harry's pursuit and the eventual resolution with Shelmerdine.
- **Time and climate**: Woolf connects emotional extremity to climatic extremity — the Elizabethan age of "violence was all" produces both the Great Frost and the Great Passion. As centuries pass and the weather moderates, so too does the intensity of feeling.
- **Nature as agent**: The Frost is not mere backdrop but an active force that shapes plot, character, and theme. Nature freezes, melts, floods, and petrifies — it is as much a character as any person in the novel.

## Historical Authenticity

The Great Frost is based on the historical Frost Fair of 1608, one of several occasions when the Thames froze solid enough to host fairs and carnivals during the Little Ice Age. Woolf draws on contemporary accounts of frozen birds, petrified wayfarers, and carnival on the ice. The last Thames Frost Fair occurred in 1814; by Woolf's time, the embankments and faster-flowing river made freezing impossible, adding an elegiac dimension to her depiction of the Elizabethan climate.
