# Cock Tavern

The Cock Tavern was a tavern located in Fleet Street, London, during the Elizabethan era. It served as a gathering place for the leading playwrights and poets of the age, including Christopher Marlowe, William Shakespeare, and Ben Jonson.

## Location and Historical Context

The Cock Tavern stood on Fleet Street, a major thoroughfare in the City of London. During the late 16th and early 17th centuries, Fleet Street was home to numerous taverns that functioned as informal meeting places for writers, actors, and intellectuals. The tavern culture of Elizabethan London provided a social infrastructure that complemented the theatrical culture of the Bankside playhouses.

## Role in Orlando

The Cock Tavern appears in the novel through the recollections of [[nick-greene]], who reminisces about it during his dinner with Orlando at Orlando's country estate. Greene, now a critic of the Victorian age, looks back nostalgically to the Elizabethan period, which he describes as a lost golden age of English literature.

Greene recalls a particular night at the Cock Tavern:

> He could remember, he said, a night at the Cock Tavern in Fleet Street when Kit Marlowe was there and some others. Kit was in high feather, rather drunk, which he easily became, and in a mood to say silly things. He could see him now, brandishing his glass at the company and hiccuping out, "Stap my vitals, Bill" (this was to Shakespeare), "there's a great wave coming and you're on top of it," by which he meant, Greene explained, that they were trembling on the verge of a great age in English literature, and that Shakespeare was to be a poet of some importance. (004-177)

The scene captures a moment of mythologized literary history: Marlowe, drunk and prophetic, hailing Shakespeare as the poet of a coming age. Greene's recollection is colored by nostalgia and irony — Marlowe was killed shortly thereafter in a drunken brawl and never lived to see whether his prophecy came true.

## Literary Significance

The Cock Tavern represents the myth of Elizabethan literary camaraderie. The image of Marlowe, Shakespeare, and Jonson gathered in a Fleet Street tavern has become a enduring motif in English literary history. In *Orlando*, Woolf uses this image to explore the relationship between literature, memory, and nostalgia.

Nick Greene's invocation of the Cock Tavern serves a dual purpose. On one level, it represents his genuine reverence for the Elizabethan era. On another, it reveals the critic's tendency to valorize the past at the expense of the present — a theme that runs throughout the novel's treatment of literary culture.
