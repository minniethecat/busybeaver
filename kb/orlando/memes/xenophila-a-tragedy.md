# Xenophila: A Tragedy

"Xenophila a Tragedy" — the narrator hedges with "or some such title" — is one of the forty-seven plays, histories, romances, and poems Orlando produced before the age of twenty-five. It is the thick document Orlando retrieves alongside the thin "The Oak Tree" on the night his literary ambition crystallises into purpose, only to be later consigned to the bonfire after Nick Greene's mockery.

## Textual Presentation

"Xenophila" appears at a pivotal moment in Chapter II. Orlando, alone at midnight, unlocks a cabinet containing fifty cedar-wood drawers, each holding a manuscript — mythological tragedies with titles like "The Death of Ajax," "The Birth of Pyramus," and "The Return of Odysseus" (008-010). From this repository of secret juvenilia, "he chose from this repository one thick document called 'Xenophila a Tragedy,' or some such title, and one thin one, called simply 'The Oak Tree' (this was the only monosyllabic title among the lot)" (008-011).

The narrator's deliberately vague memory of the title ("or some such title") distinguishes "Xenophila" from the precisely recalled "The Oak Tree." The vagueness is the point: "Xenophila" represents the generic, interchangeable, classically-imitative literature that young Orlando produces in quantity; "The Oak Tree" represents the singular, personal voice that will sustain him through four centuries.

The title itself — "Xenophila," lover of the strange or foreign — suggests the young Orlando's attraction to the exotic and grandiose, the opposite of the rooted, English particularity of an oak tree.

## Narrative Function

"Xenophila" serves as the foil to "The Oak Tree" in the novel's central parable of artistic maturation. The contrast is physical (thick vs. thin), titular (polysyllabic Greek vs. monosyllabic English), and aesthetic (derivative classical tragedy vs. personal lyric). When Orlando later burns "fifty-seven poetical works, only retaining 'The Oak Tree'" (008-035) after Nick Greene's devastating visit, "Xenophila" is among the casualties — and its destruction, far from being a loss, is portrayed as a necessary purgation.
