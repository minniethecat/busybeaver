# Identity

Orlando's identity persists across four centuries and a sex change, yet it is constantly reshaped by experience, memory, and history. Woolf asks: what is the self that endures through change, and how many selves can one person contain?

## Concept Definition

The novel advances a layered model of identity. At its core is a continuous consciousness — "Orlando remained precisely as he had been" (009-044) after the transformation — surrounded by multiplying selves that accumulate with time. The narrator notes that "she had a great variety of selves to call upon, far more than we have been able to find room for" (012-019), since conventional biography can barely account for "six or seven selves, whereas a person may well have as many thousand" (012-019).

Identity in *Orlando* is not fixed but cumulative. Each self is a response to a particular era: "For she had a great variety of selves to call upon, far more than we have been able to find room for, since a biography is considered complete if it merely accounts for six or seven selves, whereas a person may well have as many thousand" (012-019). This multiplication of selves challenges the biographical convention that a life is a single coherent narrative.

## Textual Manifestation

The sex change "did nothing whatever to alter their identity" (009-044), separating identity from the body and from gender — the novel's most radical philosophical claim.

Each era adds a new layer to Orlando's self: Elizabethan page, Jacobean lover, ambassador to Constantinople, eighteenth-century lady of letters, Victorian wife, twentieth-century motorist. None replaces the others; all coexist and can be summoned as needed.

The narrator elaborates this theory explicitly:

> These selves of which we are built up, one on top of another, as plates are piled on a waiter's hand, have attachments elsewhere — sympathies, little constitutions and rights of their own, call them what you will (and for many of these things there is no name) so that one will only come if it is raining, another in a room with green curtains, another when Mrs. Jones is not there, another if you can promise it a glass of wine — and so on. (012-020)

This passage articulates the novel's most sophisticated philosophical position: identity is both real (the selves exist) and contingent (they depend on context). The metaphor of plates piled on a waiter's hand captures the precarious balance between multiplicity and integration.

## Key Scenes

The narrator's theory of the multiplied self emerges in the final chapter, where Orlando's accumulated identities are catalogued:

> She had a great variety of selves to call upon, far more than we have been able to find room for, since a biography is considered complete if it merely accounts for six or seven selves, whereas a person may well have as many thousand. (012-019)

Beneath the oak tree in the final chapter, Orlando feels the selves converge into an integrated whole — "the whole of her most carefully organized attiring of life" (012-093) collapses, leaving the bare fact of being.

The moment of convergence is also a moment of temporal collapse: all Orlando's past selves are present simultaneously:

> She was a feather blown on the gale, she was a straw; a ship, a waterfall, a bubble. She was all these things and nothing. (012-094)

## Literary Criticism

> *Orlando* engages the philosophical problem of personal identity through comic narrative, treating seriously what philosophy treats solemnly: that identity is simultaneously real and constructed, singular and multiple. The novel anticipates later phenomenological and narratological accounts of selfhood.

The novel's treatment of identity has been read as a proto-postmodern deconstruction of the unified subject. By narrating identity as a stack of contingent selves rather than an essential core, Woolf anticipates the work of later theorists who would challenge the Enlightenment model of the autonomous individual.
