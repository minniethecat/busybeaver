# Richmond

Richmond appears at two distinct moments in *Orlando*, serving as the setting for Queen Elizabeth's possessiveness over the young Orlando (007-015) and, two centuries later, as a picturesque destination for courtship and romance (010-099). The place functions as a narrative hinge between two eras, two Orlando identities, and two modes of love.

## Geographic Location

Richmond lies on the Thames southwest of London. Richmond Palace was a Tudor royal residence, and Richmond Park — enclosed by Charles I in the seventeenth century — remains one of London's great royal parks. In the novel's geography, Richmond sits at the boundary between the urban world of London and the rural world of Orlando's country estate.

## Queen Elizabeth at Richmond

Richmond first appears in Chapter I as the place where Queen Elizabeth's affection for Orlando hardens into a claim of possession. After summoning Orlando to Whitehall, the ageing Queen takes him to Richmond:

"For the old woman loved him. And the Queen, who knew a man when she saw one, though not, it is said, in the usual way, plotted for him a splendid ambitious career. Lands were given him, houses assigned him. He was to be the son of her old age; the limb of her infirmity; the oak tree on which she leant her degradation. She croaked out these promises and strange domineering tendernesses (they were at Richmond now) sitting bolt upright in her stiff brocades by the fire which, however high they piled it, never kept her warm." (007-015)

The parenthesis — "(they were at Richmond now)" — drops the location almost as an afterthought, yet it is loaded with significance (007-015). Richmond is where the Queen's love becomes territorial, where Orlando is claimed as "the limb of her infirmity; the oak tree on which she leant her degradation." The domestic interior at Richmond — the stiff brocades, the fire that never warms — contrasts with the public pageantry of Whitehall, revealing the private desperation behind the Queen's public power.

## Eighteenth-Century Courtship

Two centuries later, Richmond reappears in an entirely different register. Orlando, now a woman in the eighteenth century, lives a life of shifting identities and costumes. The narrator sketches her daily metamorphosis (010-099):

"So then one may sketch her spending her morning in a China robe of ambiguous gender among her books; then receiving a client or two in the same garment; then she would take a turn in the garden and clip the nut trees — for which knee-breeches were convenient; then she would change into a flowered taffeta which best suited a drive to Richmond and a proposal of marriage from some great nobleman." (010-099)

Richmond has been transformed from the scene of the Queen's desperate possessiveness to the destination for a light afternoon's romantic intrigue (010-099). The drive to Richmond, in "flowered taffeta," is a social ritual — the place where marriage proposals happen as a matter of form. The contrast could not be starker: what was once the site of an old woman's "strange domineering tendernesses" has become a stage for the polite comedy of eighteenth-century courtship.

## Narrative Function

Richmond embodies one of the novel's central ideas: that places persist while meanings change (007-015, 010-099). The same physical location serves entirely different narrative functions across centuries — from the theatre of royal power to the stage of romantic comedy. In this sense Richmond is a miniature of the novel itself, which tells the story of how the same self (Orlando) can mean entirely different things in different times and different genders.
