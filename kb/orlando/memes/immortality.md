**Immortality** in *Orlando* is neither straightforward nor supernatural. Orlando does not age in a conventional sense — living from the Elizabethan period to 1928 — yet the novel treats this extraordinary longevity with the same matter-of-fact tone it brings to biography and history. Immortality is less a magical gift than a metaphor for the persistence of the self across time.

## Manifestations in the Novel

Orlando's three-century lifespan is the novel's most conspicuous fantastical device, yet it functions not as escapism but as a structural necessity. To explore how gender shapes identity, Woolf needs Orlando to live through multiple historical periods, experiencing each as both man and woman. Immortality enables this comparative experiment.

The novel also reflects on other forms of immortality: the immortality of art (Orlando's poem survives for centuries), of nature (the oak tree endures while generations come and go), and of reputation (Orlando seeks fame, but fame proves as mutable as gender).

## Literary-Critical Perspectives

Critics have noted that Orlando's longevity mirrors the temporal reach of the English literary tradition that Woolf invokes throughout the novel. Orlando lives through the very literary periods — Elizabethan, Restoration, Augustan, Romantic, Victorian, Modernist — that constitute the canon. Immortality thus becomes a device for literary criticism as much as for characterisation.
