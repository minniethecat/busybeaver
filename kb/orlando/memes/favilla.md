# Favilla

Favilla is one of three noblewomen whose names are "freely coupled with his in marriage" during Orlando's Elizabethan youth — the others being Clorinda and Euphrosyne. Orlando names all three in his sonnets. (007-021)

## Character Description

Favilla is introduced as "of a different sort altogether" from the sweet-mannered Clorinda. "She was the daughter of a poor Somersetshire gentleman; who, by sheer assiduity and the use of her eyes, had worked her way up at court, where her address in horsemanship, her fine instep, and her grace in dancing won the admiration of all." (007-023)

Favilla is a social climber, a self-made woman at the Elizabethan court, and Woolf sketches her with an economic realism that cuts against the romantic conventions of the setting. The narrator notes — with characteristic ironic sympathy — that "it must be said in justice that Favilla had few stockings and those for the most part of drugget." (007-023)

## The Spaniel Incident

Favilla's engagement to Orlando ends in a single night over an act of animal cruelty. She was "so ill-advised as to whip a spaniel that had torn one of her silk stockings within an inch of its life beneath Orlando's window." (007-023) Orlando, whom the narrator describes as "a passionate lover of animals," now sees her with fresh eyes — and finds her teeth crooked, with "the two front turned inward, which, he said, is a sure sign of a perverse and cruel disposition in woman, and so broke the engagement that very night forever." (007-023)

The spaniel incident is a classically Woolfian moment: a trivial domestic event — a torn stocking, a whipped dog — becomes the hinge on which a major life decision turns, revealing character through the accumulation of small, vivid details.

## Thematic Role

Favilla's function in the narrative is twofold. She represents the type of the ambitious courtier, using her physical charms to rise above her modest origins. And her dismissal — like Clorinda's — establishes the pattern of Orlando's repeated withdrawals from conventional heterosexual romance. The cruelty to an animal, the newly-noticed crooked teeth, the overnight reversal of affection: all mark Orlando's nature as fundamentally unsuited to the marriage plot that the novel consistently subverts.

> Favilla, who comes next, was of a different sort altogether. She was the daughter of a poor Somersetshire gentleman; who, by sheer assiduity and the use of her eyes, had worked her way up at court, where her address in horsemanship, her fine instep, and her grace in dancing won the admiration of all.
>
> (007-023)
