# The Great Fire of London

The **Great Fire of London** (1666) is a historical event that occurs during Orlando's long absence from England — between her departure for Constantinople as Ambassador and her return as a woman. The fire, along with the Great Plague of 1665, serves as a temporal marker of how much the world has changed while Orlando underwent her transformation and sojourn with the gypsies.

## In the Novel

The fire appears obliquely in Chapter 4, when Orlando returns to England and observes a transformed London skyline. The narrator notes that during her absence there had been "a plague and a fire" — the Great Plague of 1665 and the Great Fire of 1666 — and that St. Paul's Cathedral, "which Mr. Wren had built during her absence," now dominated the skyline [049-058]. The rebuilt cathedral, designed by Sir Christopher Wren after the fire destroyed the old Gothic structure, becomes a symbol of the new London to which Orlando must adapt.

## Narrative Function

In Woolf's novel, the Great Fire functions not as a detailed historical set piece (unlike the Great Frost of 1608) but as a chronological marker — one of many events that occur during Orlando's narrative lacunae, demonstrating how history accelerates in the protagonist's absence. The fire, the plague, and the rebuilding of London compress decades into a single paragraph, illustrating the novel's treatment of time as elastic and uneven.

The London that Orlando knew as a young Elizabethan courtier has been largely erased and rebuilt. This material transformation of the city mirrors Orlando's own bodily transformation — both are irreversible changes that occur "offstage" and must be reckoned with upon return.

## Historical Background

The historical Great Fire began on 2 September 1666 in a bakery on Pudding Lane and burned for four days, destroying approximately 13,200 houses, 87 parish churches, and St. Paul's Cathedral. Sir Christopher Wren's reconstruction of St. Paul's and fifty-two city churches fundamentally reshaped the London skyline that Orlando observes from her ship.
