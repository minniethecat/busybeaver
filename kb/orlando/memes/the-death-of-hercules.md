# The Death of Hercules

"The Death of Hercules" is a youthful verse tragedy by Orlando, composed during his Elizabethan period. The play, though never directly quoted in the novel, becomes a key object in Orlando's literary education: offered in earnest to the poet Nick Greene for criticism, it is returned as ammunition for public ridicule.

## Textual Presentation

The play enters the narrative at the moment of Greene's departure from Orlando's estate. Orlando, "for he had never yet liked to mention the subject," finally takes the risk:

> He had the temerity to press his play upon the Death of Hercules upon the poet and ask his opinion of it. The poet took it; muttered something about Glawr and Cicero, which Orlando cut short by promising to pay the pension quarterly; whereupon Greene, with many protestations of affection, jumped into the coach and was gone. (007-187)

The scene is exquisitely painful. Orlando's "temerity" — the word suggests a shyness approaching fear — is met with Greene's deflecting mutter about "Glawr" (Glory) and Cicero (007-187). The pension promise — a quarterly payment in exchange for literary judgment — transforms the exchange into a transaction, corrupting it before Greene has even read the manuscript. Orlando, the wealthy patron, is buying what should be given freely: honest opinion (007-187).

Greene's verdict comes not in private letter but in public print. The satire "A Visit to a Nobleman in the Country" includes:

> passages from that aristocratic tragedy, the Death of Hercules, which he found as he expected, wordy and bombastic in the extreme. (007-191)

The judgment — "wordy and bombastic" — is filtered through Greene's hostility, but Woolf does not let us dismiss it (007-191). Orlando's earnestness, his aristocratic leisure, and his classical subject matter all mark the play as derivative juvenilia in the Elizabethan mode.

## Narrative Function

The play serves a dual role: it is simultaneously a genuine artistic attempt and a narrative device for exposing the economics of literary patronage (007-187). Greene's betrayal — taking the manuscript offered in trust and mining it for public mockery — is the novel's definitive statement on the relationship between wealth, art, and criticism (007-191).

The play's title is itself suggestive. Hercules, the classical hero of twelve labours, dies by fire — poisoned by a garment steeped in centaur's blood (007-187). The myth resonates: Orlando too will be "poisoned" by the literary garment Greene wraps around him, and the fifty-seven books he burns after reading the satire are a literary funeral pyre.

Greene's description — "wordy and bombastic in the extreme" — contains its own critique (007-191). "Wordy" indicts Orlando's prolixity (he would later scratch out twenty-six volumes of "The Oak Tree"); "bombastic" targets the inflated diction of Elizabethan tragedy that Orlando, at this stage, imitates rather than transcends. Both judgments are, Woolf implies, partially true: Orlando has not yet found the plain style that the monosyllabic "The Oak Tree" represents.

## Literary Context

The episode mirrors the historical Robert Greene's attack on Shakespeare in *Greene's Groats-Worth of Wit* (1592), which called Shakespeare "an upstart Crow, beautified with our feathers." Woolf reverses the dynamic: here the aristocrat is the aspirant, and the Grub Street hack holds the critical power. The irony cuts both ways — Greene is venal, but Orlando is naive; the literary world rewards neither virtue (007-191).
