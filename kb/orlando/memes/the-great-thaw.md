# The Great Thaw

The Great Thaw is the catastrophic end of the Great Frost in Chapter I of *Orlando* — a spectacular dissolution in which the frozen carnival city on the Thames breaks apart, Sasha departs with the Russian ship, and Orlando's first love ends amid the turbulent floodwaters at Wapping.

## The Breaking of the Ice

After three months of extraordinary frost, during which the Thames froze to a depth of twenty feet and a whole city of tents, booths, and amusements was erected upon its surface, the thaw comes with "unusual suddenness." At dawn, "the sky turning a pale yellow and the rain almost ceasing," the ice begins to crack:

> Where, for three months and more, there had been solid ice of such thickness that it seemed permanent as stone, and a whole gay city had been built upon it, was now a race of turbulent yellow waters. (007-058)

The narrative describes the ice breaking into "huge masses" that collide and rear up "creaking and groaning, to rest on end like so many pinnacles of marble." All the structures of the carnival — the tents, the arbours, the skating rinks — are swept away by the flood.

## The Human Cost

The narrator reports "the deaths of many thousands" in the catastrophe. A particularly vivid image captures a party of stranded wretches on an iceberg, confronting an impossible choice: "whether they jumped into the flood or stayed on the ice their doom was certain... sometimes a whole group would fling themselves into the water together, and sometimes solitary figures would hurl themselves from the bank into the flood rather than let a gold goblet escape them." (007-058)

## Sasha's Departure

For Orlando, the thaw is not merely a natural disaster but a personal catastrophe. He waits through the night at the appointed inn near Blackfriars, but Sasha never comes. At dawn he witnesses the Russian ship unfurling its sails and departing through the breaking ice. The thaw thus carries away Orlando's first great love — Sasha the Muscovite princess sails out of his life on the same flood that destroys the carnival.

## Orlando's Rage

Standing knee-deep in the floodwaters at Wapping, Orlando erupts in a torrent of misogynistic invective — a scene that marks the novel's first direct engagement with the language of gender. "Flinging himself from his horse, he made, in his rage, as if he would breast the flood. Standing knee-deep in water, he hurled at the faithless woman all the insults that have ever been the lot of her sex. Faithless, mutable, fickle, he called her; devil, adulteress, deceiver." (007-060) The swirling waters take his words and toss "at his feet a broken pot and a little straw" — nature's dismissive reply to human passion.

## Aftermath

The narrator sums up the consequences concisely: "that disastrous winter which saw the frost, the flood, the deaths of many thousands, and the complete downfall of Orlando's hopes — for he was exiled from Court; in deep disgrace with the most powerful nobles of his time." (008-002) Orlando retreats to his country estate, and the novel pivots from public spectacle to private introspection.

## Literary Significance

The Great Thaw is one of Woolf's most virtuosic set-pieces — a scene of apocalyptic violence rendered in prose of extraordinary kinetic energy. It functions as both a literal event and a psychological metaphor: the breaking of ice mirrors the breaking of Orlando's romantic illusions, and the flood that sweeps away the carnival city prefigures the flood of historical change that will sweep away every era the novel traverses.
