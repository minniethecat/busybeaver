# Clorinda

Clorinda is one of three noblewomen whose names are "freely coupled with his in marriage" during Orlando's youth at the Elizabethan court — the others being Favilla and Euphrosyne. Orlando names all three in his sonnets. (007-021)

## Character Description

Clorinda is described as "a sweet-mannered gentle lady enough," and Orlando "was greatly taken with her for six months and a half." However, her physical and temperamental shortcomings gradually emerge: "she had white eyelashes and could not bear the sight of blood. A hare brought up roasted at her father's table turned her faint." (007-022)

## Orlando's Rejection

Woolf maps Orlando's withdrawal from the engagement through a series of accumulating aversions. Clorinda "was much under the influence of the Priests too, and stinted her underlinen in order to give to the poor." Her piety, combined with her efforts to "reform Orlando of his sins," produces the opposite effect: "which sickened him, so that he drew back from the marriage, and did not much regret it when she died soon after of the smallpox." (007-022)

## Thematic Role

Clorinda, along with Favilla and Euphrosyne, represents the conventional heterosexual romance that Orlando repeatedly fails to consummate. Each woman is dismissed for a different flaw, and the cumulative effect is to establish a pattern of Orlando's romantic incompatibility with normative expectations — a pattern that the novel's central gender transformation will later recontextualize.
