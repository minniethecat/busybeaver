**Disguise and Performance** are recurring motifs in *Orlando*, from Archduke Harry's cross-dressing pursuit to Orlando's own negotiation of gender roles across centuries. The novel suggests that all social identity is, to some degree, performed — that gender, rank, and even historical period are roles we inhabit rather than essences we express.

## In the Novel

Archduke Harry's disguise as Archduchess Harriet is the novel's most overt instance of cross-dressing performance, but the theme pervades the entire work. Orlando's transformation from man to woman can be read as the ultimate costume change — one that reveals rather than conceals, exposing the performative nature of gender itself.

The novel also treats literary style as a form of performance: Orlando's writing changes with each historical period, reflecting the "spirit of the age" that demands conformity to its stylistic conventions. To write in one's own voice, the novel suggests, is itself a kind of performance — a negotiation between inner impulse and outer expectation.
