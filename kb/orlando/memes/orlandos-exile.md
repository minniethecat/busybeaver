# Orlando's Exile

Orlando's exile is the period of rural seclusion that follows the disasters of the Great Frost and Thaw. Disgraced at court, his romantic hopes destroyed, Orlando retreats to his ancestral country house and dedicates himself to writing — a withdrawal from the world that becomes the foundation of his identity as a writer.

## The Retreat

"In the summer of that disastrous winter which saw the frost, the flood, the deaths of many thousands, and the complete downfall of Orlando's hopes — for he was exiled from Court; in deep disgrace with the most powerful nobles of his time" — Orlando retires to his country estate. (008-002) The narrator lists the cumulative disasters in a single, breathless sentence that telescopes public catastrophe and private ruin into one continuous event.

The Irish house of Desmond is "justly enraged" by Orlando's conduct; the King has "trouble enough with the Irish" already. Court, which once seemed the natural centre of Orlando's world, is now closed to him.

## The Writing Life

In his seclusion, Orlando turns to literature with obsessive intensity. The narrator describes him writing "some forty-seven plays, histories, romances, poems; some in prose, some in verse; some in French, some in Italian; all of them so profound, so eloquent that it was universally agreed that he was the greatest writer of the age." (008-014) The irony, of course, is that no one has read them — they exist only in manuscript, piled in his great house, the secret labor of an exile.

This period establishes Orlando's lifelong relationship with writing as a private, almost clandestine activity. Throughout the centuries that follow, he (and later she) will repeatedly return to the manuscript of "The Oak Tree" — hiding it when interrupted, carrying it across continents, and adding to it across three hundred years.

## The Oak Tree

The country estate's great oak tree becomes the geographical and spiritual anchor of Orlando's retreat. The narrator describes it as "the earth's spine beneath him"— a place where the transitory surface of life gives way to something permanent and sustaining. The tree becomes the title and subject of Orlando's great poem, and the image recurs throughout the novel as a symbol of continuity amid transformation.

## Breaking the Solitude

Eventually, "after many months of such feverish labour," Orlando decides "to break the solitude of years and communicate with the outer world." He contacts a friend in London, Giles Isham of Norfolk, who can "put him in touch with some member of that blessed, indeed sacred, fraternity" of writers. (008-014) This decision, prompted by the desire for literary recognition, sets in motion the novel's next major episode: Orlando's entanglement with Nick Greene and the world of Elizabethan literary criticism.
