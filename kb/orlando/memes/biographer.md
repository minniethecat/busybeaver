## Definition and Narrative Role

The biographer in *Orlando* is not an invisible omniscient narrator but a character in their own right — a self-conscious, often flustered chronicler who openly wrestles with the conventions of life-writing. Woolf deploys the biographer as a satirical persona, parodying the Victorian "man of letters" who presumes to know a subject completely while repeatedly confessing the limits of his knowledge. The biographer frets about decorum, apologises for indelicacies, and addresses the reader directly — a metafictional device that foregrounds the artifice of biographical form.

Unlike a traditional narrator, the biographer-narrator calls attention to the very act of composition. "Happy the mother who bears, happier still the biographer who records the life of such a one!" he exclaims, positioning himself as the fortunate scribe privileged to follow Orlando's extraordinary trajectory. Yet this self-congratulation is undercut by the biographer's persistent anxiety: he cannot keep pace with his subject's transformations, cannot adequately explain Orlando's sex change, and must at times confess that "we have no information" about crucial episodes.

## Textual Manifestation

The biographer intrudes throughout the novel with metacommentary that blends arch humility, mock-pedantry, and genuine perplexity. Early in the narrative, he draws attention to a "curious trait" in Orlando's character, noting that "a certain grandmother of his had worn a smock and carried milk-pails" — a genealogical anecdote that simultaneously explains and undermines the aristocratic Orlando's earthiness para 22.

As the narrative accelerates, the biographer's voice becomes increasingly breathless. When Orlando's moods oscillate violently, "the biographer must not stop either, but must fly as fast as he can and so keep pace with the unthinking passionate foolish actions" para 42. This comic self-awareness positions the biographer as a character trapped by the very conventions he upholds: he must record everything faithfully, yet his subject defies the orderly, chronological expectations of Victorian biography.

The biographer's commentary on the limits of factual knowledge marks the novel's most sustained critique of biographical form. Woolf's biographer confesses that "these are not matters on which a biographer can profitably enlarge," gesturing toward the vast interior life — of desire, memory, and sensation — that conventional biography cannot accommodate para 73.

## Key Scenes

### The Biographer's Confession of Ignorance

Throughout the novel, the biographer repeatedly admits the limits of his knowledge. He cannot explain Orlando's transformation from man to woman, cannot penetrate the private recesses of Orlando's consciousness, and cannot reconcile the competing demands of chronology and psychological truth. These confessions are not failures of craft but demonstrations of the genre's inadequacy: the biographical form cannot contain Orlando's protean selfhood.

### The Biographer and the Oak Tree

The biographer's relationship to Orlando's poem "The Oak Tree" is particularly revealing. He treats the poem as a biographical artifact — a document that tracks Orlando's development across centuries — yet the poem itself resists such instrumental reading. The tension between the biographer's documentary impulse and the poem's aesthetic autonomy mirrors the larger conflict between fact and imagination that structures the novel.

### The Biographer at the Close

In the novel's final pages, the biographer's orderly chronology dissolves entirely: past and present, memory and sensation merge as Orlando stands beneath the oak tree. The biographer, who began by promising a linear account from "deed to deed, from glory to glory," must finally concede that a life cannot be captured in sequential sentences. The clock strikes; Orlando becomes a multiplicity of selves; the biographer falls silent.

## Critical Perspectives

Woolf's biographer-narrator is widely recognized as a parody of the Victorian biographical tradition epitomized by Leslie Stephen's *Dictionary of National Biography*. The device allows Woolf to critique what she called, in her essay "The Art of Biography," the "granite-like solidity" of factual biography while demonstrating the "rainbow-like intangibility" of lived experience. The biographer's voice — pompous yet vulnerable, omniscient yet ignorant — enacts the impossibility of capturing a life in prose.

Feminist critics have noted that the biographer's anxiety about Orlando's sex change mirrors the culture's broader discomfort with gender ambiguity. The biographer's stated inability to find "any explanation" for Orlando's transformation — despite narrating it in vivid detail — exposes the inadequacy of binary gender categories within biographical discourse. The biographer, for all his pretensions to authority, is unable to discipline Orlando's body or identity into coherent narrative form.
