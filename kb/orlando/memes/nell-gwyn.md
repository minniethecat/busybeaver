# Nell Gwyn

**Nell Gwyn** (1650–1687) was a celebrated Restoration actress and the long-time mistress of King Charles II. In *Orlando*, she appears as a fleeting figure in the fashionable London world that Orlando re-enters after returning from Constantinople.

## In the Novel

Nell Gwyn's appearances in the novel are brief but evocative. During Orlando's return to London society, the narrator observes that "Nell Gwyn was on his arm" [008-073] — one of the figures in the swirl of Restoration social life that Orlando must navigate as a woman.

Later, after Orlando's transformation, there is a passing reference to the envious remark that "this was Nell Gwyn's tribute to the memory of a leg" [009-007] — an allusion to Gwyn's famous wit and the physicality that defined her public persona. These glancing mentions position Gwyn as part of the texture of Restoration London: theatrical, irreverent, and sexually frank.

## Restoration Context

Nell Gwyn represents the theatrical and libertine culture of Charles II's court — a stark contrast to the Elizabethan and Jacobean courts of Orlando's youth. Her presence in the novel, however brief, underscores the transformation of English social life during Orlando's absence: the reopening of theatres, the rise of actresses on the public stage (women's roles having previously been played by boys), and the open acknowledgment of royal mistresses in public life.

## Wit and Reputation

Gwyn was known for her quick wit and her lack of pretension. According to popular legend, when her coach was surrounded by an angry mob who mistook her for the King's Catholic mistress, she leaned out the window and declared, "Pray, good people, be civil — I am the Protestant whore." This irreverence, blending comedy with sharp social awareness, aligns her with the novel's own tonal range.
