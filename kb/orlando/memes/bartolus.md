# Nicholas Benedict Bartolus

Captain Nicholas Benedict Bartolus is the sea captain who brings Orlando back from Constantinople to England in Chapter IV. He serves a dual narrative function: as a practical escort who guides Orlando through her return voyage, and as a comic foil whose matter-of-fact observations contrast with Orlando's tumultuous inner life.

## Role in the Narrative

Captain Bartolus first appears at dinner during Orlando's voyage home, introducing himself as he helps her to a slice of corned beef. "Captain Nicholas Benedict Bartolus, a sea captain of distinguished aspect," the narrator notes, establishing his character with economy and precision. (010-004) Throughout the voyage back to England, he remains at Orlando's side, unwittingly bearing witness to the chaos of her emotions.

## Arrival in London

As the ship approaches the English coast, Bartolus becomes Orlando's guide to a city she no longer recognizes. He identifies the landmarks one by one — St. Paul's, the Tower of London, Greenwich Hospital, Westminster Abbey, the Houses of Parliament — providing the factual counterpoint to Orlando's flood of memory and sensation. (010-018) His resolute right hand and matter-of-fact commentary serve as an anchor for Orlando's disorientation upon returning to her homeland after more than a century abroad.

## Clothing and Gender

Bartolus reappears briefly in a passage that explicitly connects Orlando's change of clothing to her change in social treatment. When "Captain Bartolus saw Orlando's skirt, he had an awning stretched for her immediately, pressed her to take another slice of beef, and invited her to go ashore with him in the longboat." (010-053) The narrator observes that "these compliments would certainly not have been paid her had her skirts, instead of flowing, been cut tight to her legs in the fashion of breeches," using Bartolus as the vehicle for one of the novel's most direct meditations on the power of dress to shape identity and social interaction.

## Literary Significance

Bartolus is also responsible for one of the novel's most important moments of literary catalysis. As the ship sailed past the Cocoa Tree coffeehouse, he identified to Orlando the figures of Mr. Addison, Mr. Dryden, and Mr. Pope — "Sad dogs," he added, by which he meant that they were Papists, "but men of parts, none the less." (010-018, 010-063) This offhand remark sets in motion Orlando's renewed fascination with literary greatness, which drives much of the novel's satirical treatment of eighteenth-century literary society. The names "Addison, Dryden, Pope" chime in Orlando's head "like an incantation ever since," ultimately compelling her back into society despite her determination to forswear it. (010-063)

## Critical Function

Bartolus serves as a classic Woolfian minor character — he appears briefly to fulfill a crucial structural role and then vanishes from the narrative. His function is to provide comic relief, geographical orientation, and an unintentional catalyst for Orlando's literary ambitions, all while remaining almost entirely unaware of the depth and complexity of his passenger's inner life.
