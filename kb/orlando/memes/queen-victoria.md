# Queen Victoria

**Queen Victoria** (1819–1901) reigns over the period that Orlando finds most oppressive in her four-century life. In Woolf's satire of nineteenth-century England, Victoria's name becomes metonymous with the era's great "battle between the respectability of the nineteenth century and the immorality of the ages that preceded it" — a battle that threatens to smother Orlando entirely.

## In the Novel

Victoria appears in the novel not as a character but as a pervasive force — the namesake of an age whose "damp" seeps into everything. As the nineteenth century settles over England, the narrator describes how "the great cloud which hung, not only over London, but over the whole of the British Isles on the first day of the nineteenth century stayed, or settled, or increased, so that not a single ray of sunlight could penetrate it" [011-025].

The Victorian era is figured as a suffocating domesticity: "The sexes grew farther and farther apart," and the respectable woman must cover every inch of furniture in fabric, marry, and produce. Orlando, who has lived freely as man and woman across centuries, finds this rigid categorisation particularly unbearable. The narrator remarks that "it would no doubt have been different had she lived all the year round with him as Queen Victoria recommended" [012-045] — the recommendation being marriage as the sole legitimate framework for cohabitation.

## The Victorian Marriage Plot

The Victorian era's chief demand of Orlando is marriage, and the novel treats this as both comic absurdity and existential threat. Orlando's resistance — her refusal to comply with the "spirit of the age" — nearly kills her, until she meets Marmaduke Bonthrop Shelmerdine and discovers that marriage to a kindred spirit can be a form of freedom rather than imprisonment.

When Orlando finally marries Shel, the ceremony's spiritual force is asserted against all worldly authority: "not the Archbishop nor the Pope nor Queen Victoria on her throne could force her" [011-021] to remove her wedding ring. The invocation of Victoria here is pointed — the ring represents a bond freely chosen, outside the coercive domesticity that Victoria's name represents.

## Transition to the Modern Age

The passing of Victoria's reign marks a transition in the novel as significant as Orlando's own transformation. The narrator observes the change in atmosphere after "King Edward — see, there he was, stepping out of his neat brougham... — had succeeded Queen Victoria" [012-060], and the "thick, watery, prismatic" quality of Victorian life gives way to the sharper, more fragmented sensibility of the Edwardian and modern periods.
