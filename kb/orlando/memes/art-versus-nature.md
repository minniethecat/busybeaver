**Art versus Nature** is a structuring dialectic in *Orlando*, embodied most vividly in the relationship between the poem "The Oak Tree" and the physical oak tree on Orlando's estate. The novel explores whether art imitates nature, competes with it, or offers something nature cannot.

## In the Novel

Orlando's devotion is split between the poem (art) and the tree (nature) that share a name. The tree endures across centuries in a way the poem, constantly revised, does not — yet the poem achieves publication and recognition in a way the tree, silent and rooted, cannot. Neither art nor nature is given primacy; instead, the novel suggests they are complementary modes of engaging with the world.

The gipsy episode brings this dialectic into sharp focus. The gipsy's critique of Orlando's love of nature — "it was not nature but the love of nature that possessed Orlando" — exposes the gap between nature itself and the artistic sensibility that seeks to capture it. Art, from the gipsy's perspective, is a form of possession rather than appreciation.
