**The Body** in *Orlando* is at once the most intimate and the most public aspect of identity. Orlando's body changes — from male to female — and this change is both momentous and strangely matter-of-fact. The novel treats the body as a surface on which gender, age, and historical period are inscribed, but also as something that exceeds these inscriptions.

## In the Novel

Orlando's transformation is the novel's most spectacular bodily event, but the body is a persistent concern throughout. The aging Queen Elizabeth's body, the frozen bodies at the Frost Fair, the body of the gipsy inured to the elements, Shelmerdine's seafaring body — all offer different models of what it means to be embodied.

The novel's treatment of the body is closely tied to its treatment of gender: Orlando's transformation reveals that gender is not a fixed property of the body but a performance that the body enables and constrains. Yet the body also resists pure constructionism — Orlando's new body brings new sensations and experiences that are not merely cultural.
