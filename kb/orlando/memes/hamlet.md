# Hamlet

*Hamlet* (c. 1599–1601) is Shakespeare's most celebrated tragedy. In *Orlando*, the play appears not on stage but in the backstage gossip of Nick Greene, the Elizabethan poet-critic whose anecdotes about Shakespeare's working habits form one of the novel's sharpest satires of literary reputation.

## Textual Presentation

*Hamlet* enters the novel through the voice of Nick Greene, the professional critic whom Orlando hosts at his country estate. Greene, having dismissed Orlando's own literary ambitions, pivots to gossip about the great Elizabethan playwrights he claims to have known intimately:

> "By this time Orlando had abandoned all hope of discussing his own work with the poet; but this mattered the less as the talk now got upon the lives and characters of Shakespeare, Ben Jonson, and the rest, all of whom Greene had known intimately and about whom he had a thousand anecdotes of the most amusing kind to tell." (008-024)

What follows is a deflating portrait of literary production in the Elizabethan age. Greene describes a world in which the greatest works of English drama were composed in conditions of haste, drunkenness, and domestic chaos:

> "Their poetry was scribbled down on the backs of washing bills held to the heads of printer's devils at the street door. Thus Hamlet went to press; thus Lear; thus Othello. No wonder, as Greene said, that these plays show the faults they do." (008-024)

The repeated "thus" — *thus Hamlet went to press; thus Lear; thus Othello* — is the punchline of Greene's deflationary anecdote. The syntax collapses three of the greatest tragedies in the language into a single dismissive gesture, reducing their differences to a repeated "thus." The effect is comic but also unsettling: the conditions of literary production, Greene insists, are inseparable from literary judgment.

## Narrative Function

The Greene episode, and the offhand mention of *Hamlet* within it, serves Woolf's career-long interrogation of literary greatness. By placing Shakespeare's tragedies in the context of "washing bills" and "printer's devils," Woolf strips away the marmoreal dignity that literary history confers on canonical works.

Orlando's immediate response — "Orlando had never laughed so much in his life. These, then, were his gods! Half were drunken and all were amorous" (008-024) — captures the ambivalence of the scene. The laughter is genuine, but so is the disillusionment. The discovery that his literary idols were imperfect is simultaneously liberating and wounding: it frees him from the paralyzing reverence that blocks his own writing, but it also strips away the idealism that had sustained his devotion. Greene's gossip leaves Orlando with "a plaguey spirit of unrest that would never suffer him to sleep sound again" (008-024).

Shakespeare's presence reverberates through the novel long after the Greene episode. In Chapter III, Orlando reflects that "Shakespeare must have written like that, anonymously, needing no thanking or naming" (008-045). In Chapter V, "the great lines of Marlowe, Shakespeare, Ben Jonson, Milton began booming and reverberating" in Orlando's mind (010-017). And in the final chapter, "she had seen Shakespeare's script and Milton's" (012-041), and "The whole works of Shakespeare cost half a crown and could be put in your pocket" (012-041). *Hamlet* is the most famous component of this lifelong Shakespearean presence — the play whose title Greene's "thus" reduces to a single syllable, and whose canonical prestige the novel simultaneously deflates and reaffirms.

## Critical Context

The Greene episode anticipates one of Woolf's most famous arguments — developed at length in *A Room of One's Own* (1929) — about the material conditions of literary production. Greene's image of Shakespeare's plays "scribbled down on the backs of washing bills" (008-024) offers, in comic miniature, the thesis that would later become a cornerstone of feminist literary criticism: that artistic achievement is inseparable from the economic and social circumstances of its creation.

The detail is pointedly anti-romantic. Where literary tradition imagines Shakespeare composing in solitary genius, Greene's anecdote places him in a world of "carousings and junketings in taverns and in beer gardens, when things were said that passed belief for wit" (008-024). The "washing bills" — a reminder that even Shakespeare had domestic debts — function as Woolf's materialist rejoinder to the cult of solitary genius.
