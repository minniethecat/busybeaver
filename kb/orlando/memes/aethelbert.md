# Aethelbert: A Tragedy in Five Acts

***Aethelbert: A Tragedy in Five Acts*** is a fictional play written by the young Orlando during his literary apprenticeship in the Elizabethan period. It appears in Chapter 2 as a symbol of Orlando's early and earnest — if not entirely distinguished — ambition to become a writer.

## In the Novel

The narrator introduces *Aethelbert* in a scene of characteristic domestic comedy. After observing the gardens from his window, Orlando "slowly drew in his head, sat down at the table, and, with the half-conscious air of one doing what they do every day of their lives at this hour, took out a writing book labelled 'Aethelbert: A Tragedy in Five Acts,' and dipped an old stained goose quill in the ink" [007-002].

The gesture captures the ritualised quality of Orlando's literary efforts — the work is part of a daily routine, as automatic as breathing, yet suffused with self-consciousness. The "old stained goose quill" and the half-conscious air suggest both dedication and a certain youthful pretension.

## The Oak Tree Poem

*Aethelbert* prefigures Orlando's lifelong relationship with writing, culminating in the composition of "The Oak Tree" poem that spans centuries. The tragedy-in-five-acts form echoes the Elizabethan and Jacobean theatrical conventions that Orlando absorbed as a young courtier, reflecting the literary culture of Shakespeare's age.

Where the later "Oak Tree" poem represents organic growth — a living document revised across lifetimes — *Aethelbert* represents the more conventional ambitions of youth: the desire to produce something grand, formally correct, and worthy of the age's highest literary forms.
