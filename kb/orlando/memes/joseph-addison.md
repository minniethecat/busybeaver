# Joseph Addison

Joseph Addison, the great Augustan essayist, appears in Chapter IV as part of the literary society through which Orlando moves in the eighteenth century. His presence contributes to Woolf's affectionate satire of literary fame.

## Role Overview

Addison is encountered alongside Pope and Swift in the drawing rooms and coffee houses of Queen Anne's London. The narrator treats the great figures of the age as both genuinely impressive and slightly absurd — men whose literary achievements are undeniable but whose human presence falls short of their reputation.

## Appearance and Identity

The narrator describes the literary lions Orlando encounters, noting their human peculiarities alongside their intellectual stature. "Addison, Dryden, Pope" (011-063) are named as figures whose work Orlando knows but whose persons are something else entirely.

## In the Text: The Augustan Circle

Addison first enters the narrative as a geographical marker. Sailing up the Thames on her return from Constantinople, Orlando is shown the Cocoa Tree coffee house from the deck: "there he was — one might see Mr. Addison taking his coffee" (010-018). Captain Bartolus names the trinity of the age: "Addison, Dryden, Pope" (010-019). The names are an incantation — the landmarks of literary London as real to Orlando as Wapping Old Stairs or the Tower.

When Orlando finally enters the social world of these figures, the reality is deflating in Woolf's characteristic manner. She discovers that "Addison, Pope, Swift, proved, she found, to be fond of tea. They liked arbours. They collected little bits of china" (010-080). The domesticity of titans — the great essayist who collects china — is the novel's most sustained joke about literary reputation.

The drawing-room dynamics of the circle are rendered with comic precision: when Nell announces "Mr. Addison waited on her Ladyship," Mr. Pope rises "with a wry smile" and takes his leave — the rivalry of wits played out in seating arrangements and social precedence (010-083). The narrator notes the care taken to arrange furniture "in a circle, so that Mr. Pope could not say that Mr. Addison came before him, or the other way about" (010-088). Literary greatness, seen from Orlando's drawing room, is a matter of chairs and place cards.

Orlando's eventual disillusionment with this world is registered through Addison's manner: "the sneer of Mr. Pope, in the condescension of Mr. Addison, and in the secret of Lord Chesterfield which took away her relish for the society of wits" (010-095). The "condescension" of Addison — a word carrying the weight of class and gender — is part of what drives Orlando away from literary London.

In the novel's final chapter, Addison appears among the ghosts of the past: "Dryden, Pope, Addison — those were the heroes. All, all are dead now" (012-032). The names have passed from living men to literary monuments — the process of canonisation that is itself one of the novel's subjects.

## Story Arc

Orlando's encounters with Addison are part of her education in the gap between literary reputation and literary reality. Having known Nick Greene across centuries, she is already acquainted with the figure of the professional writer; the great Augustans are a different order of literary being, yet still recognisably human.

## Character Relationships

- **With Orlando**: A figure of the literary establishment that Orlando both admires and sees through.

## Thematic Function

Addison, like Pope and Swift, represents the institution of literature — the social world in which reputations are made and unmade. His presence in the novel connects Orlando's story to the real history of English letters.

## Literary Criticism

> Woolf's inclusion of historical literary figures in *Orlando* has been read as her engagement with the English literary tradition, claiming a place for her own work within it while simultaneously subjecting it to comic scrutiny.
