# Cheapside

**Cheapside** was a principal commercial street in Elizabethan London, running eastward from St. Paul's Cathedral. In *Orlando*, Cheapside appears as a metonym for the bustling commercial and artisanal world that stands in contrast to Orlando's aristocratic isolation, and as a spatial marker of the boundary between noble amateurism and professional authorship — a boundary Orlando must learn to cross.

## Printing and Publication

The young Orlando, consumed by literary ambition, has his first work printed at Cheapside:

> "One he had had printed by John Ball of the Feathers and Coronet opposite St. Paul's Cross, Cheapside" [008-010].

The printer John Ball, a real historical figure who operated at this location, serves as Orlando's tentative entry into the world of letters — though Orlando, acutely aware that "to write, much more to publish, was, he knew, for a nobleman an inexpiable disgrace," never dares to show the printed play even to his mother [008-010]. The act of printing at Cheapside thus encodes a fundamental tension in the young Orlando's character: the collision between literary ambition and aristocratic shame, enacted geographically in the movement from the great house to the commercial street.

The sheer volume of Orlando's early literary output — "some forty-seven plays, histories, romances, poems; some in prose, some in verse; some in French, some in Italian; all romantic, and all long" — makes the single Cheapside printing all the more poignant [008-010]. Of forty-seven works, only one ventures beyond the locked cedar cabinet into the public world of print, and even that one remains hidden from everyone.

## Commerce and the Poet's Memory

Later, Orlando's memory conjures the image of a shabby poet glimpsed years earlier, accessing the recollection through the associative logic Woolf describes in a celebrated passage about Memory as seamstress:

> "Memory is the seamstress, and a capricious one at that. Memory runs her needle in and out, up and down, hither and thither. We know not what comes next, or what follows after" [008-012].

From this cascade of associations, Orlando assembles the face, then the clothing, and finally "a pair of thick boots such as citizens wear in Cheapside" [008-012]. This sartorial detail — the stout, practical boots of the merchant class — marks the social distance between the aristocrat Orlando and the Grub Street world of professional writers. Orlando's immediate reaction is telling: "Not a Nobleman; not one of us," he thinks, adding parenthetically that "it shows what an effect noble birth has upon the mind and incidentally how difficult it is for a nobleman to be a writer" [008-012].

The boots from Cheapside thus crystallize the class anxiety that shadows Orlando's entire literary vocation. To write is to risk descent into the commercial sphere — to wear, metaphorically, the citizen's thick boots rather than the courtier's silk stockings. Cheapside is the place where this risk becomes visible.

## Literary Significance

Cheapside functions in the novel as more than a geographical reference. It operates as a spatial metaphor for the threshold Orlando must cross between two incompatible identities: the aristocrat who writes in secret and the author who publishes openly. The journey from Cheapside — where a single printed play must be hidden even from one's mother — to the Hogarth Press — where the novel itself is published under the author's own imprint — measures the distance *Orlando* travels across three centuries of literary history.
