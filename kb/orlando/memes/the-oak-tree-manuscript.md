**The Oak Tree (Manuscript)** is the physical document — a poem written over more than three hundred years — that Orlando carries through the centuries, from the Elizabethan age to the twentieth century. More than just a literary work, the manuscript is a material object that embodies the novel's central themes: the persistence of artistic creation across time, the relationship between self and art, and the oak tree as the novel's master symbol.

## The Manuscript in the Novel

Orlando begins writing "The Oak Tree" as a young man in the Elizabethan period. The poem grows and changes as Orlando does — through the Jacobean and Restoration eras, through the transformation and the return to England, through the Victorian age and into the modernist present. The manuscript is buried, lost, recovered, revised, and ultimately published (winning a prize).

The physical manuscript — stained, crossed-out, amended — literalises the novel's vision of time as accretive rather than linear. The poem's completion and recognition in the twentieth century (the novel's "present") brings the work to a kind of closure, even as the novel itself resists the conventions of closure.

## Symbolism

The manuscript shares its title with the oak tree on Orlando's estate — the actual tree to which Orlando is devoted. The twinning of poem and tree suggests the inseparability of art and nature, of the made and the found, that runs through the novel.
