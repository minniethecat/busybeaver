The **Bloomsbury Group** was an informal circle of writers, artists, and intellectuals who lived and worked in the Bloomsbury district of London in the early twentieth century. Virginia Woolf was a central member, and the group's ethos — its rejection of Victorian convention, its commitment to intellectual and artistic freedom, its playful seriousness — pervades *Orlando*.

## Influence on Orlando

*Orlando* can be read as a Bloomsbury book: its mock-biography form, its treatment of gender as fluid and performative, its playful engagement with literary history, and its dedication to Vita Sackville-West all reflect Bloomsbury values. The novel's freedom from generic convention — it is simultaneously biography, fantasy, literary criticism, and love letter — is a Bloomsbury freedom.

The dedication to Sackville-West, the aristocratic writer and gardener who was Woolf's lover, embeds the novel in the intimate social world of Bloomsbury, where personal relationships and artistic collaboration were inseparable.
