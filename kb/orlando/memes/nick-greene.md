# Nick Greene

Nicholas Greene is the Elizabethan poet who visits Orlando in the country, savages his verse, and drives him to burn his collected works. He reappears in the Victorian era as Sir Nicholas Greene, the doyen of literary criticism — the same man, the same attitudes, but now an established authority rather than a hungry young satirist.

## Role Overview

Greene is the novel's satirical portrait of the professional literary world. In his youth, he is a witty but destructive critic whose mockery of Orlando's poetry nearly extinguishes the young nobleman's creative ambition:

> The poet's eyes flashed fire; he dropped the fine gentleman airs he had worn; thumped his glass on the table, and launched into one of the longest, most intricate, most passionate, and bitterest stories that Orlando had ever heard, save from the lips of a jilted woman, about a play of his; another poet; and a critic. (004-169)

In his old age, he has become the very establishment he once mocked, "risen in the world and become certainly a Knight and doubtless a very influential critic" (012-033).

## Appearance and Identity

In Chapter II, Greene arrives at Orlando's estate "plainly dressed in his slouched hat and black doublet" — the image of the starving artist. The narrator notes the ironic contrast: "the poet has a butcher's face and the butcher a poet's" (008-012). By the Victorian era, he has become a figure of institutional respectability, "not changed, for all his knighthood" (012-033) — suggesting that literary fashion changes but its gatekeepers remain the same.

## Story Arc

### Elizabethan Phase: The Hungry Satirist

Greene stays at Orlando's estate, eats and drinks copiously, and proceeds to demolish Orlando's poetic ambitions. He mocks the young nobleman's verse and tells scandalous stories of the great poets — Shakespeare, Marlowe, Jonson — stripping them of their glamour. His account of the Cock Tavern is a masterpiece of nostalgic myth-making:

> He could remember, he said, a night at the Cock Tavern in Fleet Street when Kit Marlowe was there and some others. Kit was in high feather, rather drunk, which he easily became, and in a mood to say silly things. He could see him now, brandishing his glass at the company and hiccuping out, "Stap my vitals, Bill" (this was to Shakespeare), "there's a great wave coming and you're on top of it." (004-177)

### Aftermath: Orlando's Creative Crisis

Orlando, devastated by Greene's mockery, burns his collected works:

> He had burnt his fifty-seven poetical works, only retaining "The Oak Tree," which was his boyish dream and very short. (008-035)

This purgation proves essential to his artistic development. Greene's cruelty, paradoxically, is a gift — it strips away the derivative juvenilia and leaves only the genuine poem.

### Victorian Reappearance: The Knighted Critic

Greene reappears centuries later as a knighted literary critic. Orlando, now a woman, meets him again and finds him essentially unchanged. His review of John Donne's collected works embodies the critic's limited vision — judging everything by the standards of a past golden age that never quite existed.

## Thematic Function

Greene embodies the literary establishment in all its fickleness and self-importance. His transformation from impoverished satirist to knighted critic satirises the institutional machinery of literary reputation. He also serves as a foil to Orlando's genuine, non-institutional relationship to art.

## Literary Criticism

> Greene has been read as Woolf's revenge portrait of the literary critic — a figure who has power over reputations but no creative gift of his own. His reappearance in the Victorian era is one of the novel's most pointed jokes about the persistence of critical mediocrity.

The character may be partly modelled on real figures from Woolf's literary world, including the critic Edmund Gosse, another knighted man of letters whose reputation outlasted his relevance. Greene's unchanging nature across three centuries embodies Woolf's argument that literary institutions — prizes, reviews, reputations — are more durable than the art they claim to serve.
