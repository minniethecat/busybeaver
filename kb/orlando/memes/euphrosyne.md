# Euphrosyne

Euphrosyne is the young noblewoman to whom Orlando becomes briefly engaged in the Elizabethan period. Her role is small but significant: she is the conventional romantic option that Orlando's unconventional nature must reject.

## Role Overview

Orlando's betrothal to Euphrosyne occurs during his time at the Elizabethan court. She is described as beautiful and appropriate — everything a young nobleman's bride should be. But the Queen's jealousy, and Orlando's own nature, ensure the match never comes to fruition.

## Appearance and Identity

The narrator describes a woman of "extraordinary beauty" — the conventional Elizabethan ideal of feminine loveliness. Her very conventionality is what makes her wrong for Orlando.

## In the Corpus

Euphrosyne appears as one of three early loves memorialised in Orlando's poetry: "The names of three at least were freely coupled with his in marriage — Clorinda, Favilla, Euphrosyne — so he called them in his sonnets" (007-021). Of the three, she was the most serious attachment.

The narrator provides her full lineage with characteristic irony: "The third, Euphrosyne, was by far the most serious of his flames. She was by birth one of the Irish Desmonds and had therefore a family tree of her own as old and deeply rooted as Orlando's itself" (007-024). This detail of ancestry — the Desmond family being among the oldest of the Anglo-Irish nobility — makes Euphrosyne Orlando's social equal, and therefore a plausible match.

During the Frost Fair, Euphrosyne's proprietary claim on Orlando is made visible: "The Lady Euphrosyne hung upon his arm" (007-027). But Orlando's attention is elsewhere — already drawn to the foreign princess on the ice.

The courtship's end is marked by a telling detail of material exchange: "Euphrosyne of the Sonnets wore Orlando's splendid sapphire on the second finger of her left hand" (007-034). The gift of a jewel — and its subsequent wearing — is the novel's shorthand for the conventions of Elizabethan courtship that Orlando will repeatedly evade.

## Story Arc

Orlando's engagement to Euphrosyne is noted in passing, one of the brief episodes that fill his early years at court. The Queen's displeasure at the match is one of the complications that drives the plot of the early chapters.

## Character Relationships

- **With Orlando**: A conventional romantic attachment that Orlando's unconventional nature must transcend.

## Thematic Function

Euphrosyne represents the normative romantic path that Orlando repeatedly approaches and then swerves away from. Her presence in the narrative establishes by contrast the unconventional nature of Orlando's true desires.

## Literary Criticism

> Euphrosyne is a minor figure who serves a structural purpose: she represents the marriage plot that *Orlando* consistently subverts. Her name (meaning "mirth" or "joy" in Greek) is one of the Three Graces — an ironic name for a character whose function is to be rejected.
