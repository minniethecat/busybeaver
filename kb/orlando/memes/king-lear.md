# King Lear

*King Lear* is a tragedy by William Shakespeare, referenced in Virginia Woolf's *Orlando* in the context of Nick Greene's anecdotes about Elizabethan playwrights and their haphazard methods of composition.

## In the Novel

During Nick Greene's visit to Orlando's estate, the talk turns to Shakespeare and his contemporaries. Greene, who claims to have known them all intimately, describes how the great plays of the age were produced under conditions far from the romantic ideal:

> "Their poetry was scribbled down on the backs of washing bills held to the heads of printer's devils at the street door. Thus Hamlet went to press; thus Lear; thus Othello." (007-181)

Greene uses this to explain what he sees as the faults in these plays, presenting the image of a literary culture where genius and carelessness coexisted.

The sentence's three-beat rhythm — "Thus Hamlet went to press; thus Lear; thus Othello" — is a small formal irony: the rhetorical figure of *tricolon* (the rule of three) deployed to describe haste, improvisation, and economic necessity rather than polished oratory.

## Narrative Function

The mention of *King Lear* alongside *Hamlet* and *Othello* serves to ground Greene's stories in actual literary history. By naming these three tragedies — widely regarded as Shakespeare's greatest works — Woolf both authenticates Greene's character (he was present at their creation) and ironically undercuts his dismissal of them. The passage participates in the novel's broader meditation on literary reputation, the passage of time, and the gap between the circumstances of creation and the judgment of posterity. (007-181)
