# Archduke Harry / Archduchess Harriet

The Archduke (later Archduchess) is the novel's most sustained comic set piece on gender — a figure who first pursues Orlando as a man, and later pursues Orlando as a woman, exposing the absurdity of fixed romantic and gender conventions.

## Role Overview

The Archduke Harry first appears in Chapter II as a persistent suitor who pursues Orlando even into retirement. When Orlando returns to England as a woman, the Archduke reappears — now as the Archduchess Harriet — and resumes pursuit, unaware that Orlando was once the man he desired.

## Appearance and Identity

The Archduke is described as "a very tall gentleman in black, who had come, he said, to offer his services to Orlando" (008-063). His height and solemnity make him comic, and his persistent pursuit despite Orlando's clear disinterest adds to the farce.

When Orlando encounters the Archduchess Harriet after the transformation, "she found herself looking into the eyes of the Archduke Harry" (010-035) — a recognition that reveals the absurdity of the gender categories that both have now crossed.

## Story Arc

### Key Events

- **First Pursuit**: The Archduke Harry pursues Orlando relentlessly, appearing at the estate despite Orlando's clear signals of disinterest.
- **Reappearance as a Woman**: After Orlando's transformation, the Archduchess Harriet appears in London society. Orlando recognises her — she is the Archduke Harry, now presenting as female.
- **Mutual Recognition**: When Orlando confronts the Archduchess, the recognition is mutual. Both understand that the other has crossed a gender boundary, and the comedy of their situation is shared.

## Character Relationships

- **With Orlando**: A comic mirror of Orlando's own gender fluidity. The Archduke/Archduchess's ability to change gender presentation at will mirrors and parodies Orlando's transformation.

## Thematic Function

The Archduke/Archduchess serves as comic relief that undermines any reading of the novel that takes gender too seriously. If Orlando's transformation is meaningful, the Archduke's is farcical — and the juxtaposition suggests that neither is entirely one or the other.

## Literary Criticism

> The Archduke/Archduchess plot is often read as Woolf's exploration of cross-dressing and gender performance in the eighteenth-century tradition, drawing on the comic possibilities of mistaken identity while also making a serious point about the performative nature of gender.
