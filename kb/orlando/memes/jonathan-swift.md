# Jonathan Swift

Jonathan Swift, the greatest satirist of the Augustan age, appears briefly in Chapter IV among the literary figures of eighteenth-century London. His presence is particularly apt for a novel that is itself a sustained work of satire.

## Role Overview

Swift appears alongside Pope and Addison in the literary society Orlando enters as a woman. The narrator treats these figures with characteristic irony — respectful of their achievements but unsparing about their human frailties.

## Appearance and Identity

The narrator's description of the Augustan literary greats emphasises the body beneath the reputation. Swift, like Pope, is seen as a physical being — not merely an authorial name but a breathing, eating, imperfect human presence.

## In the Text: The Augustan Circle

Swift belongs to the company of literary men whose ordinary habits Orlando discovers with amused surprise: "Addison, Pope, Swift, proved, she found, to be fond of tea. They liked arbours. They collected little bits of china" (010-080). The domesticity of these titans is one of the novel's most sustained jokes — the great satirist who "collected little bits of china".

His most vivid entrance comes in a scene of domestic interruption that captures his personality in a gesture: "there is a terrific rap at the door, and Mr. Swift, who had these arbitrary ways with him, walks in unannounced" (010-085). The suddenness of the entrance — Swift's refusal to observe the niceties of eighteenth-century calling conventions — marks him as a force apart from the polite literary world Orlando navigates.

Even in the novel's final chapter, set in 1928, Swift appears among the spectral company of the great: "the great men she had known; Dryden, Swift, and Pope; and statesmen in colloquy; and lovers dallying in the window-seats" (012-087). He has become part of the accumulated texture of Orlando's centuries-spanning consciousness — a figure who persists in memory long after the brief encounter has passed.

## Story Arc

Orlando's encounter with Swift is brief but meaningful — it places her at the centre of a literary tradition that her own poem "The Oak Tree" both inherits and transcends.

## Character Relationships

- **With Orlando**: Swift represents the power of satire — a mode that *Orlando* itself adopts in its treatment of literary society.

## Thematic Function

Swift's presence in the novel is appropriate given his own experiments with fantasy and satire. *Gulliver's Travels*, like *Orlando*, uses fantastic premises to satirise social convention. The two works stand in a line of English satirical fantasy that Woolf both acknowledges and extends.
