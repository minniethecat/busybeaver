# Literary Betrayal

The theme of literary betrayal runs through *Orlando* as a sharp critique of the relationship between artist and critic. Nick Greene's treatment of Orlando dramatises how the literary world can turn creative generosity into an instrument of wounding — the critic who is welcomed as a guest and colleague instead uses his access to destroy.

## Concept Definition

Literary betrayal in *Orlando* is not merely professional rivalry but a breach of personal trust. Orlando opens his home and his work to Greene, seeking honest fellowship. Greene repays this openness with mockery — savaging Orlando's verse in conversation, and later immortalising his ridicule in print. The betrayal cuts deeper because it exploits the artist's vulnerability: the need to be read, to be taken seriously, to belong to a community of letters.

## Textual Manifestation

The betrayal operates on two levels. First, the personal: Greene's visit to Orlando's estate, where "the poet has a butcher's face and the butcher a poet's" (008-012), establishes the dynamic of the predator welcomed as guest. Second, the published: Greene's satires — *A Visit to a Nobleman in the Country* and later commentary on *The Death of Hercules* — translate private cruelty into public record.

The narrator captures the wound precisely: Orlando burns "fifty-seven poetical works" (008-035) after Greene's visit, retaining only *The Oak Tree*. The bonfire is a direct consequence of betrayed trust.

## Key Scenes

The country visit (Chapter II) is the primal scene of literary betrayal. Greene eats Orlando's food, drinks his wine, disparages the great poets of the age, and then turns his wit on his host's own verse. The betrayal is complete when he later publishes *A Visit to a Nobleman in the Country*, a satire that makes Orlando's private literary aspirations a public joke.

In the Victorian era, Greene reappears — now knighted, now an institutional gatekeeper. The betrayal structure repeats: the critic who once wounded Orlando as a young man now controls access to literary recognition. That Orlando eventually wins the prize Greene's world confers is Woolf's final ironic comment on the relationship between genuine art and the institutions that judge it.

## Literary Criticism

> Woolf's portrait of Greene has been read biographically as her reckoning with the literary reviewing culture of her own time. The novel suggests that the critic's power to wound is structural, not personal — it inheres in the asymmetry between the artist who creates and the critic who judges. Greene reappears in the Victorian era unchanged because the dynamic itself is unchanging.
