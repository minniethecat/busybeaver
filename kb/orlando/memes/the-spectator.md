# The Spectator

*The Spectator* (1711–1712) is Joseph Addison and Richard Steele's landmark daily periodical, which fundamentally shaped the literary culture of early eighteenth-century England. In *Orlando*, Woolf invokes *The Spectator* as a shorthand for the urbane, witty, and socially observant literary style of the Augustan age, and she directs the reader to it as an interpretive key.

## Textual Presentation

Woolf embeds *The Spectator* within a narrative strategy that collapses the distance between fiction and literary history. In a metafictional aside, the narrator instructs:

> "If the reader will here refer to the Rape of the Lock, to the Spectator, to Gulliver's Travels, he will understand precisely what these mysterious words may mean. Indeed, biographers and critics might save themselves all their labours if readers would only take this advice." (010-080)

This directive positions *The Spectator* as one of three contemporary texts — alongside Pope's poem and Swift's satire — that together encode the "spirit of the age." The narrator implies that reading *The Spectator* conveys more about Addison's character than any biography could.

Later, when Mr. Addison arrives at Orlando's house for a visit, the narrator deploys an even more direct literary device:

> "In came Mr. Addison. Let us, as he takes his seat, read the following passage from the Spectator:" (010-083)

Woolf then quotes a passage from *The Spectator* (010-084) in which Addison describes woman as "a beautiful, romantic animal, that may be adorned with furs and feathers, pearls and diamonds, ores and silks." The embedded quotation itself provides a direct window into the polite, ornamental style of eighteenth-century prose.

## Woolf's Quotation Technique

The device by which *The Spectator* enters the novel — "Let us, as he takes his seat, read the following passage" (010-083) — is one of Woolf's most striking narrative innovations in *Orlando*. Rather than describing Addison or narrating his conversation, the narrator simply substitutes a passage from his own writing. The implication is audacious: Addison's periodical prose *is* his presence; the text stands in for the man.

The quoted passage itself — with its ornate catalogue of "furs and feathers, pearls and diamonds, ores and silks" (010-084) — exemplifies the polished, decorous style for which *The Spectator* was celebrated. But Woolf's framing complicates any simple admiration. By presenting the passage immediately after Pope's departure "with a wry smile" (010-081) and immediately before Swift's "terrific rap at the door" (010-085), she embeds Addison's polished urbanity within a spectrum of Augustan authorial temperaments — Pope's brittle elegance on one side, Swift's savage directness on the other.

The narrator's subsequent commentary reinforces the technique's conceptual foundation: "We hold that gentleman, cocked hat and all, in the hollow of our hands. Look once more into the crystal. Is he not clear to the very wrinkle in his stocking?" (010-085). The "crystal" is the quoted text itself — Woolf's assertion that literary style offers a transparency of character that biographical narration cannot match.

## Narrative Function

*The Spectator* serves a dual function in the novel. Structurally, it anchors the Chapter IV social scenes in a recognizable historical reality — the world of coffee houses, wits, and polite letters that Addison and Steele chronicled. Thematically, it illustrates Woolf's argument about the relationship between a writer's work and character: "every secret of a writer's soul, every experience of his life, every quality of his mind is written large in his works" (010-081). By directing the reader to *The Spectator* rather than narrating Addison's visit in conventional realist prose, Woolf performs her conviction that literary style *is* character.

The device also contributes to the novel's satire of literary criticism and biography. The narrator's claim that critics "might save themselves all their labours" (010-080) if readers simply went to the primary texts is a characteristically Woolfian jab at the secondary literature industry.
