# William Shakespeare

Shakespeare is never directly present in *Orlando* but is the novel's presiding literary spirit — the figure against whom all other literary reputations are measured and found wanting.

## Role Overview

Shakespeare is referenced repeatedly as the unreachable summit of English literature. Nick Greene tells scandalous stories about him. Orlando's own poem exists in his shadow. The novel's very act of writing in the English literary tradition takes Shakespeare as its unavoidable precursor.

## Appearance and Identity

The novel never describes Shakespeare directly; he remains an absence, a name, a reputation. This indirection is deliberate — Shakespeare is more powerful as an idea than he could be as a character.

## In the Text: The Name That Haunts

Shakespeare first enters the novel through the voice of Nick Greene, the scurrilous Elizabethan poet who provides Orlando's introduction to literary life. Greene's account is spiked with professional envy: "Shakespeare, he admitted, had written some scenes that were well enough" (008-020) — the grudging concession is recognisable to anyone who has encountered literary jealousy. But Greene cannot resist naming him as the chief offender in the commercialisation of letters: "Shakespeare was already paying his way" (008-021), a crime in the eyes of the gentleman-amateur Greene.

Greene also reports the authentic note of Shakespeare's speech — "'Stap my vitals, Bill' (this was to Shakespeare), 'there's a great wave coming and you're on top of it'" (008-022) — a fragment of reported conversation that brings the absent figure momentarily to life.

Orlando's own creative life is lived in Shakespeare's shadow. When she composes, the narrator suggests, "Shakespeare must have written like that" (008-045) — the ultimate comparison, the standard against which all writing is judged. Later, reflecting on the strange power of art, Orlando thinks that "a silly song of Shakespeare's has done more for the poor and the wicked than all the preachers and philanthropists in the world" (010-026). Shakespeare's power, in this view, is not merely aesthetic but moral — a force that actually changes human life.

In the novel's final chapter, the litany of the literary dead is recited: "Marlowe, Shakespeare, Ben Jonson — those were the giants. Dryden, Pope, Addison — those were the heroes. All, all are dead now" (012-032). But the very act of naming them keeps them present — and Shakespeare, first among the giants, presides over a tradition that the novel both inherits and renews.

Orlando has even seen "Shakespeare's script" (012-041) — a manuscript, a physical trace of the hand that wrote the words. The materiality of the script connects Orlando's own act of writing to Shakespeare's: both are ink on paper, both are efforts to capture something of life in words.

## Story Arc

Shakespeare is the figure whom Orlando can never equal and never escape. "The Oak Tree" is written in the knowledge that the greatest poetry in the language has already been written. Yet the novel suggests that this knowledge is liberating rather than paralysing: once you accept you cannot be Shakespeare, you are free to be yourself.

## Character Relationships

- **With Orlando**: The impossible standard, the literary father, the ghost that haunts all English writing.

## Thematic Function

Shakespeare functions as the limiting case of literary fame — so great that no living writer can approach it. His absence from the novel's cast of historical figures (all of whom appear directly) is significant: some things cannot be represented, only acknowledged.

## Literary Criticism

> Shakespeare's spectral presence in *Orlando* reflects Woolf's lifelong engagement with his work and reputation. In *A Room of One's Own*, she imagined "Shakespeare's sister"; in *Orlando*, she leaves the man himself in shadow, acknowledging the impossibility of adding anything to what already exists.
