# Temple Bar

**Temple Bar** was a historic gateway that marked the western boundary of the City of London, standing at the junction where Fleet Street becomes the Strand. In the novel, it serves as a grim landmark that Orlando remembers from the seventeenth century.

## In Orlando's Memory

When Orlando returns to London as a woman and observes the city's transformation, the narrator contrasts the elegant Restoration and eighteenth-century city with the more brutal London of her youth. She remembers that "the heads of rebels had grinned on pikes at Temple Bar" [049-058], a macabre detail that evokes the public executions and displays that characterised earlier periods of English history.

The city Orlando returns to is markedly different: "the cobbled pavements had reeked of garbage and ordure. Now, as the ship sailed past Wapping, she caught glimpses of broad and orderly thoroughfares" [049-058]. Temple Bar thus functions as a node in the novel's meditation on the passage of time and the transformation of urban space.

## Historical Context

The historical Temple Bar was designed by Christopher Wren and erected in 1672, replacing an earlier timber structure. It served as the ceremonial entrance to the City of London, where the monarch would traditionally pause to request the Lord Mayor's permission to enter. The gateway was removed in 1878 to ease traffic congestion and now stands near St. Paul's Cathedral. In the novel, it appears as part of the dense fabric of London landmarks — St. Paul's, the Tower, Westminster Abbey — that Captain Bartolus points out to Orlando as her ship approaches the city.
