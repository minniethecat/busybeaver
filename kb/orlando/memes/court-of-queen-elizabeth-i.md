The **Court of Queen Elizabeth I** is the royal court of the Tudor monarch, serving as the primary setting for the opening chapter of Virginia Woolf's *Orlando*. It is here that the young Orlando first gains favour with the Queen and begins his life at the center of English power.

## The Court in Orlando

Orlando is introduced to the Elizabethan court as a boy of sixteen. The Queen, taken with his beauty and innocence, grants him estates and positions of honour. The court is depicted as a world of intrigue, ceremony, and rigid hierarchy, where favour is precarious and ambition drives all.

At court, Orlando witnesses the aging Queen's final years and the transition to the Jacobean era under King James I. The court scenes establish the novel's concern with power, gender, and the passage of time — the ageing Queen's hand upon the young Orlando's head literalises the novel's meditation on mortality.

## Historical Context

The Elizabethan court was the centre of English political and cultural life during the reign of Elizabeth I (1558–1603). Woolf draws on historical accounts of the court's splendour — its progresses between Whitehall, Richmond, and Greenwich — and its literary culture, which flourished with figures such as Edmund Spenser and William Shakespeare.
