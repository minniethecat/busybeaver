# Queen Elizabeth I

Queen Elizabeth I appears in the novel's opening chapters as the ageing monarch who takes a fancy to the beautiful young Orlando. Her portrayal is both comic and poignant — a powerful woman confronting mortality, drawn to the youth and beauty she has lost.

## Role Overview

The Queen visits Orlando's father's estate and, struck by Orlando's beauty, offers him favour and position at court. She makes Orlando her Treasurer and Steward, and her affection for him borders on the romantic. Her decline and death mark the end of the novel's Elizabethan section and the passage of one era into another.

## Appearance and Identity

Woolf renders Elizabeth in her old age with unsparing physical detail: the Queen is "old, and worn, and bent" (007-015), leaning on Orlando's arm for support. Yet her majesty persists through physical decay. The contrast between her royal power and her failing body is one of the novel's first meditations on time and mortality.

## Story Arc

### Key Events

- **The Royal Visit**: Elizabeth visits the great house and is struck by Orlando's beauty. That night, "when Orlando was sound asleep," she "made over formally, putting her hand and seal to certain documents" (007-011), conferring lands and offices upon him.
- **Orlando at Court**: Under Elizabeth's patronage, Orlando flourishes at court, becoming the Queen's favoured young nobleman.

### Ending

Elizabeth's death is noted in passing, as one of the many losses that accumulate across Orlando's long life. She is later remembered as a presence in the great house: "in this very room, she remembered, that Queen Elizabeth had stood astride the fireplace with a flagon of beer in her hand" (011-011).

## Character Relationships

- **With Orlando**: A relationship of patronage, ambiguous desire, and the collision of youth with age. "The old woman loved him" (007-015), the narrator states plainly, and Orlando's innocence in receiving that love is part of the novel's comedy.

## Thematic Function

Elizabeth embodies the passage of time and the decay of even the most powerful bodies. She is the novel's first major meditation on mortality. She also establishes Orlando's pattern of attracting older, powerful figures whose favour shapes his worldly fortunes.

## Literary Criticism

> Woolf's Elizabeth is a comic creation that nevertheless carries genuine pathos. The historical queen is refracted through the novel's fantastical lens, becoming both a real figure from English history and a character in Orlando's personal mythology.
