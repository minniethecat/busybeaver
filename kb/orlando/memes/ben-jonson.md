## Character Overview

Ben Jonson (1572–1637) appears in *Orlando* not as a speaking character but as a figure discussed in the Cock Tavern scene. Nick Greene, holding court among the Elizabethan literati, regales Orlando with anecdotes about the great playwrights of the age — Shakespeare, Marlowe, Jonson, Browne, and Donne — "all now writing or just having written" para 84. Jonson's presence in this catalogue signals his status as one of the luminaries of Elizabethan literary culture, the context in which Orlando first attempts to establish himself as a writer.

## Appearance and Identity

Although Jonson never appears in person in the novel, his name conjures a specific image: the learned classicist, the author of *Volpone* and *The Alchemist*, the rival and admirer of Shakespeare. Greene's anecdotes present Jonson as part of a vanished golden age of English drama — an age that Orlando, arriving too late to participate in directly, can only glimpse through Greene's reminiscences.

## Role in the Narrative

### The Cock Tavern Scene

Jonson is named twice in the Cock Tavern scene. Greene first lists him among the constellation of writers "all now writing or just having written" — a roll call that leaves Orlando "reeling off the names of his favourite heroes" in astonished admiration para 84. Later, as the conversation deepens, Greene's talk turns to "the lives and characters of Shakespeare, Ben Jonson, and the rest, all of whom Greene had known intimately and about whom he had a thousand anecdotes" para 89.

The effect on Orlando is transformative: he abandons all hope of discussing his own work with Greene and instead surrenders to the pleasure of listening. Jonson and his contemporaries represent a literary community that Orlando, as an isolated nobleman scribbling in solitude, can only envy from afar.

### Later Echoes

Jonson's name resurfaces in Orlando's memory as a phantom of literary ambition. When Orlando, now a woman in the eighteenth century, reflects on the literary world she once aspired to enter, the names of Jonson and his fellow playwrights float through her consciousness as emblems of a path not taken — the life of the professional writer, embedded in a community of peers, that Orlando's aristocratic birth and changeful identity have always denied her.

## Literary-Historical Significance

Ben Jonson was a historical figure — playwright, poet, and literary critic — whose presence in the novel anchors its fictional narrative in the real world of Elizabethan and Jacobean letters. Jonson was known for his classical erudition, his city comedies, and his masques for the Jacobean court. His inclusion alongside Shakespeare and Marlowe in Greene's anecdotes reflects the actual literary landscape of early seventeenth-century London, where these three figures dominated the stage.

Woolf's choice to name Jonson rather than invent a fictional playwright signals her commitment to a specific kind of historical texture. *Orlando* is not a historical novel in the conventional sense, but its strategic deployment of real historical figures — Jonson, Shakespeare, Pope, Queen Elizabeth — creates a fabric of verisimilitude against which the fantastic elements (the three-hundred-year lifespan, the sex change) stand out in sharper relief.
