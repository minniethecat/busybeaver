## Definition

Melancholy in *Orlando* is not merely sadness but a structural emotional state through which the protagonist passes in each era of the novel. It is associated with winter, ice, solitude, and the contemplation of mortality. Woolf treats melancholy not as pathology but as a necessary dimension of Orlando's sensibility — the shadow that gives depth to the novel's comic brightness. In the tradition of Renaissance humoral psychology, melancholy is the temperament of the artist and philosopher, and Orlando's susceptibility to it marks him as a reflective consciousness.

## Textual Manifestation

### The Frozen Thames

Orlando's first recorded melancholy occurs during the Great Frost. Without warning, "suddenly Orlando would fall into one of his moods of melancholy; the sight of the old woman hobbling over the ice might be the cause of it, or nothing; and would fling himself face downwards on the ice and look into the frozen waters and think of death" para 41. The passage establishes the essential features of Orlando's melancholy: its sudden onset, its ambiguous causation (the old woman or "nothing"), and its association with ice and death. The frozen Thames becomes the objective correlative of an interior winter.

### The Biographer's Commentary on Melancholy

Woolf has the biographer remark on the difficulty of conveying melancholy through biographical form. The biographer concedes that "these are not matters on which a biographer can profitably enlarge," acknowledging the gap between external fact and interior feeling para 73. Melancholy, precisely because it resists biographical narration, becomes a site where the novel's metafictional concerns and its psychological depth converge.

### Marmaduke Bonthrop Shelmerdine

When Orlando first encounters Shelmerdine, she recognizes in him a kindred melancholy temperament: "there was something romantic and chivalrous, passionate, melancholy, yet determined about him which went with the wild, dark-plumed name" para 344. Their shared susceptibility to melancholy — a romantic darkness that coexists with vitality — becomes the basis of their immediate mutual recognition. Melancholy here functions not as isolation but as the ground of the deepest human connection in the novel.

## Key Scenes

### The Oak Tree: Solitude and Shadow

Orlando's relationship to the oak tree on his estate alternates between joyous identification and melancholic retreat. In periods of melancholy, the tree becomes not a symbol of rootedness but a site of isolation, where Orlando "flung himself on the ground and felt the bones of the tree running out to him like ribs." The oak tree, like melancholy itself, is a constant through Orlando's transformations — a fixed point in the flux of identity.

### The Victorian Cloud

The Victorian era brings a peculiarly oppressive melancholy. The damp, the moral earnestness, the weight of procreation and empire — all press upon Orlando with a heaviness unknown in earlier centuries. This period-specific melancholy mirrors the novel's larger argument that emotion is historically conditioned: what feels like personal sadness may be, in part, the Spirit of the Age settling upon the soul.

## Critical Perspectives

Orlando's melancholy has been read through the lens of Woolf's own mental health history. Woolf experienced recurrent depressive episodes throughout her life, and the novel's treatment of melancholy as a sudden, unmotivated descent mirrors clinical descriptions of depressive onset. Yet the novel refuses a purely pathological reading: Orlando's melancholy is also the wellspring of his art, the condition that drives him to write.

The association between melancholy and winter ice participates in a larger symbolic economy in the novel: cold preserves, heat transforms. Melancholy, like ice, arrests movement, halts the narrative's forward rush, and forces introspection. In a novel otherwise characterized by headlong chronological velocity, these frozen moments of melancholy provide structural counterpoint.
