# Rosina Pepita

Rosina Pepita is the dancer whom Orlando marries during his time as Ambassador in Constantinople. Though she never appears directly in the narrative, her existence — documented in a marriage deed — generates major legal consequences when Orlando returns to England as a woman.

## The Marriage Deed

Pepita enters the story through a legal document discovered by Orlando's secretaries after his prolonged sleep in Constantinople. Among papers "scribbled over with poetry, in which frequent mention was made of an oak tree," the secretaries find "a document of far greater significance" — "a deed of marriage, drawn up, signed, and witnessed between his Lordship, Orlando, Knight of the Garter, etc., etc., etc., and Rosina Pepita, a dancer, father unknown, but reputed a gipsy, mother also unknown but reputed a seller of old iron in the marketplace over against the Galata Bridge." (009-013) The secretaries look at each other in dismay.

## Legal Consequences

Upon Orlando's return to England, the marriage to Pepita becomes one of the three primary legal charges against her. The courts declare that "she was an English Duke who had married one Rosina Pepita, a dancer; and had had by her three sons, which sons now declaring that their father was deceased, claimed that all his property descended to them." (010-021) Thus, Pepita's existence becomes a crucial node in the legal machinery that threatens to dismantle Orlando's identity — the courts must determine not only whether she is alive or dead, but whether she is man or woman, and by extension whether her marriage and children are legally valid.

## Resolution

The lawsuits are eventually settled, with the Turkish marriage annulled and the "children pronounced illegitimate." Orlando dryly summarizes: "they said I had three sons by Pepita, a Spanish dancer." (011-050) The narrator's substitution of "Spanish" for the earlier "gipsy" identification is itself telling — Pepita's ethnic identity shifts depending on who is speaking, underscoring the narrative's treatment of identity as fluid and contingent.

## Thematic Significance

Rosina Pepita functions as an absent presence — she never speaks a word in the novel, yet her existence drives significant plot developments and legal entanglements. Her unknown parentage ("father unknown, but reputed a gipsy, mother also unknown") mirrors Orlando's own fluid identity, and her role as a dancer aligns her with the performative, transformative qualities that the novel celebrates. Orlando's passing reference to his marriage late in the novel — "if one has married a Rosina Pepita, and so on" (010-002) — suggests how thoroughly she has been absorbed into the texture of Orlando's complicated life story.
