# Cocoa Tree

The Cocoa Tree was a celebrated chocolate house in early 18th-century London, located in Pall Mall. It was one of the most prominent gathering places for Whig politicians and literary figures during the reign of Queen Anne. In Virginia Woolf's *Orlando*, it serves as a landmark of literary London observed by the protagonist upon her return from the East.

## Orlando's First Glimpse

As Orlando's ship sailed up the Thames toward London Bridge after her years in Constantinople, Captain Bartolus pointed out the landmarks of the city. Among them was the Cocoa Tree, where three of the greatest literary figures of the age could be seen taking their refreshment:

> Even now — if she turned her head a little to the left and looked along the line of his first finger — so — they were passing the Cocoa Tree, where — yes, there he was — one might see Mr. Addison taking his coffee; the other two gentlemen — "there, Ma'am, a little to the right of the lamppost, one of 'em humped, t'other much the same as you or me" — were Mr. Dryden and Mr. Pope. (010-445)

The sighting was described by the Captain with characteristic bluntness: "'Sad dogs,' said the Captain, by which he meant that they were Papists, 'but men of parts, none the less'" (010-445).

## Literary Significance

The Cocoa Tree represents the gravitational pull that literary fame exerted on Orlando. The names — Addison, Dryden, Pope — function for her as a kind of incantation:

> Addison, Dryden, Pope, he had said, pointing to the Cocoa Tree, and Addison, Dryden, Pope had chimed in her head like an incantation ever since. (010-535)

Despite her earlier disillusionment with Nick Greene, Orlando retained an almost religious reverence for great writers. The glimpse of these three figures from the deck of the ship took on the quality of a vision: "Her instinct was a sound one. One can only believe entirely, perhaps, in what one cannot see" (010-535).

This distant sighting at the Cocoa Tree propelled Orlando back into London literary society, leading to her eventual encounters with Pope, Addison, and Swift — encounters that would prove both exhilarating and disillusioning.

## Historical Context

The Cocoa Tree chocolate house was historically a Tory establishment — a rival to the Whig-frequented St. James's Coffee House. In the early 18th century, chocolate houses and coffee houses served as the social infrastructure of political and literary life, functioning as informal clubs where ideas were exchanged and alliances formed.
