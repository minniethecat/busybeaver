# Love and Desire

Love in *Orlando* is at once a sublime force, a source of absurdity, and the vehicle through which the novel's deepest thinking about [[gender-and-sexuality]] unfolds. Across three centuries and a change of sex, Orlando's loves trace a trajectory from the delusions of heterosexual romance to a union that transcends gender categories altogether. Each love affair teaches Orlando something the previous one could not — and the arc from [[sasha]] to [[marmaduke-bonthrop-shelmerdine]] maps a movement from possession toward recognition, from the conventions of [[englishness]] to something unnameable that the novel calls simply "sympathy."

## Concept Definition

The novel refuses to sentimentalise love while taking it utterly seriously. Orlando's first great passion for Sasha is intoxicating — "Images, metaphors of the most extreme and extravagant, twined and twisted in his mind" (007-027) — but ends in betrayal when she sails away on the Russian ship. His second encounter, with the Archduchess Harriet / Archduke Harry, turns romantic pursuit into gender farce: the same person pursues Orlando as both sexes, exposing the absurdity of fixed romantic conventions. Only with Shelmerdine, arriving in the final chapter, does Orlando find a love that accommodates rather than consumes the self — "each was surprised at the quickness of the other's sympathy" (012-046).

Woolf's treatment of love is inseparable from her treatment of gender. Each love affair crosses a different boundary — nationality (Sasha), biological sex (the Archduke/Archduchess), social convention (Shelmerdine's seafaring absence) — and each crossing expands Orlando's understanding of what love can be. By the end, love is neither possession nor performance but a recognition of shared solitude.

## Textual Manifestation

### Sasha: The First Love (Chapter I)

Orlando's passion for the Muscovite princess is the novel's portrait of romantic love at its most consuming and most deluded. In the frozen carnival on the Thames, Orlando experiences love as a transformation of reality itself: "Love had meant to him nothing but sawdust and cinders. The joys he had had of it tasted insipid in the extreme" (007-067). Everything prior is retrospectively annihilated; only Sasha exists.

The affair's intensity is inseparable from the frozen setting: "Hot with skating and with love they would throw themselves down in some solitary reach" (007-081). The ice that suspends ordinary temporality also suspends ordinary judgment — and when the thaw comes, the delusion collapses. Orlando, waiting for Sasha's ship, hurls "at the faithless woman all the insults that have ever been the lot of her sex" (007-060). The scene is both painful and ironic: Orlando's misogynistic rage will look very different once he himself becomes a woman.

Orlando's cry upon Sasha's betrayal — "Faithless, mutable, fickle" (007-060) — distils love's cruelty into three adjectives. The scene by the Thames where Orlando waits for the Russian ship, watches it depart, and "hurled at the faithless woman" every insult in the catalogue, is the novel's most direct depiction of heartbreak — and its most ironic, given the transformation to come.

### The Archduke/Archduchess: The Farce (Chapters II–IV)

The Archduke Harry's pursuit of Orlando — and later, the Archduchess Harriet's pursuit of Orlando-as-woman — turns romantic pursuit into structural comedy. The same person, in different gendered presentations, pursues Orlando across the sex change. The reversals are not merely comic: they expose the arbitrariness of the conventions that govern romantic pursuit. If the same soul can present as male suitor and female confidante, what does "heterosexual" mean?

Orlando's flight from the Archduchess — "she would have none of him" — is one of the novel's most farcical sequences, involving a toad, a leap from a window, and a hasty flight to London. But beneath the comedy is a serious point: Orlando, now a woman, experiences the claustrophobia of being pursued in a way she never felt as a man.

### Shelmerdine: The Resolution (Chapter VI)

Marmaduke Bonthrop Shelmerdine arrives in the final chapter as love's answer — a partner whose own androgyny mirrors Orlando's: "She was a woman — but Lord! what a woman! He was a man — but Lord! what a man!" (012-046). Their connection is instantaneous and wordless: "each was surprised at the quickness of the other's sympathy" (012-046).

The meeting with Shelmerdine on the moorland outside London crystallises the novel's vision: "Are you positive you aren't a man?" he asks. "Are you positive you aren't a woman?" she replies (012-046). This mutual recognition of androgyny — that each contains qualities the world has assigned to the other sex — is love at its most mature: neither possession nor performance but recognition of a shared condition.

Their marriage is unconventional — he returns to his ship, she to her oak tree — but it is precisely this freedom that makes their union work. Shelmerdine does not complete Orlando; he recognises her. The novel's final image of love is not fusion but parallel solitude: two people "thinking the same thoughts, saying the same things" across the distance of sea and land. Orlando at the oak tree, while Shelmerdine sails at sea, embodies love as Woolf ultimately conceives it: not fusion but fidelity across distance, sustained by the imagination rather than proximity.

## Literary Criticism

> Love in *Orlando* is inseparable from the novel's gender politics. Each love affair teaches Orlando something about the other sex, and ultimately about the artificiality of sexual categories. The trajectory from Sasha to Shelmerdine maps a movement from heterosexual romance toward a more fluid, androgynous conception of desire — a conception that anticipates later feminist and queer theory in its insistence that love's truest form transcends the gender binary.
>
> Woolf's comic treatment of the Archduke/Archduchess episode — the same person pursuing Orlando as both a man and a woman — is more than farce. It enacts the novel's central thesis: that gendered conventions of romantic pursuit are costumes, not essences. When Orlando-as-woman flees the Archduchess's advances, she experiences for the first time the vulnerability of being the pursued rather than the pursuer — a radical shift in consciousness that her transformation has made possible.
