# Marañón River

## Overview

The Marañón River is the name given to the [[Upper Amazon]] in its Peruvian section. In Jules Verne's *The [[Giant Raft]]*, the river still bears the name of the Marañón through Peru, and the distinction between the Marañón, the Solimões, and the [[Amazon]] is carefully observed by the characters. The Marañón rises in the Peruvian Andes and flows northwestward before turning east to cross the Peruvian selva, eventually reaching the Brazilian frontier at [[Tabatinga]], where the river's name changes.

## Geography

The Marañón is the westernmost and highest section of the [[Amazon]] system. Its headwaters lie in the high Peruvian Andes at the Nevado de Yerupajá, where glacial meltwater feeds a network of streams that coalesce into the upper Marañón. The river cuts a dramatic gorge through the eastern cordillera before emerging onto the Amazonian plain, where it meanders through the selva baja of the [[Loreto]] department.

In the novel's careful geographic taxonomy, the river "is called the Marañón in its journey through Colombia and Peru" until reaching the Brazilian frontier [005-023]. Below [[Tabatinga]] the river becomes the Solimões — named after the Solimões indigenous people — and further downstream, after the junction with the [[Rio Negro]] at [[Manaus]], it takes the name of the [[Amazon]] proper. This three-name structure reflects 19th-century geographical convention, which distinguished the Peruvian, central Brazilian, and lower sections of the great waterway.

## In the Novel

The novel distinguishes the Peruvian section of the [[Amazon]] by its local name, the Marañón. The narrative notes that indigenous populations have "their abode on the left bank of the Marañón" in the region near [[Iquitos]] [003-003], and the river "is called the Marañón in its journey through Colombia and Peru" [005-023].

The Marañón appears in the historical survey of [[Amazon]] exploration that Verne weaves into the opening chapters. The French geographer La Condamine, during his 1743 expedition, reached "the mouth at [[Napo]]" via its junction with the Marañón on the 31st of July of that year [005-037]. This was part of La Condamine's celebrated descent of the river, during which he made the first scientific measurements of the Amazon's course. More than half a century later, Alexander von Humboldt and Aimé Bonpland completed their own survey of the region, and the novel records that they mapped "the Marañón as far as Napo" [005-038].

At the Brazilian frontier, the river's identity shifts. The text explains that below [[Tabatinga]] the river changes its name to the Solimões, but upstream "it is Peruvian, and is called the Marañón" [012-012]. The [[Jangada]]'s departure point at [[Iquitos]] — the Peruvian frontier town where [[Joam Garral]]'s [[Fazenda]] is situated — lies squarely within the Marañón section of the river [003-002].

## The Three Names of the Amazon

The naming convention observed in the novel reflects a genuine 19th-century geographical understanding. From its Andean source to the Brazilian frontier, the river was universally known as the Marañón. From [[Tabatinga]] to the [[Rio Negro]] junction at [[Manaus]], it took the name Solimões (or Solimoens), "from the name of the native [[Tribe]] which a century ago lived on its banks" [005-024]. Only after receiving the dark waters of the Rio Negro did the river become the [[Amazon]] proper — "the Amazonas, or river of the Amazons" [005-024].

This tripartite nomenclature persisted in Brazilian and Peruvian usage well into the 20th century. Modern Brazilians still refer to the stretch between [[Tabatinga]] and [[Manaus]] as the Rio Solimões, reserving "Amazonas" for the river below the Negro confluence. The Marañón name survives today as the designation of the Peruvian section above [[Iquitos]], where the river's Andean character — swifter, narrower, and more turbulent — distinguishes it from the broad, island-dotted Solimões.

## Historical Context

The Marañón was the first section of the [[Amazon]] to be explored by Europeans. Francisco de [[Orellana]]'s epic descent of the river in 1541–1542 began not on the Marañón itself but on the [[Napo]], which he followed down to its junction with the Marañón before continuing downstream. The expedition of Pedro de Ursúa and Lope de Aguirre (1560–1561) also began on the upper Marañón, launching from the Peruvian highlands in search of El Dorado.

In the novel, Verne draws extensively on the 18th-century expeditions that mapped the Marañón with scientific precision. La Condamine's journey of 1743 was particularly important: he determined the geographical coordinates of the [[Napo]]–Marañón junction by observing an emersion of Jupiter's first satellite — a feat of Enlightenment science conducted in the depths of the Amazonian wilderness [005-037]. Humboldt and Bonpland's later survey, completed during their American expedition of 1799–1804, extended the cartographic knowledge of the Marañón and confirmed La Condamine's measurements.

## Journey Role

The Marañón is the stage on which the novel's first act unfolds. The [[Jangada]] is constructed at [[Iquitos]], on the left bank of the Marañón, at [[Joam Garral]]'s [[Fazenda]] [003-002]. The entire Peruvian section of the voyage — from Iquitos to the Brazilian frontier at [[Tabatinga]] — takes place on the Marañón, passing landmarks such as [[Pebas]], [[Loreto]], and the confluence of the [[Napo]]. The raft's departure on the Marañón marks the beginning of the great journey, and the transition from the Marañón to the Solimões at Tabatinga represents the first major geographical milestone of the voyage.
