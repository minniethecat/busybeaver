# Mission

## Definition

Missions were religious settlements established by Catholic missionary orders — predominantly the Jesuits, followed by Franciscans and Carmelites — throughout the [[Amazon Basin]] from the 17th century onward. These stations served multiple functions: centers of evangelization for indigenous peoples, nodes of colonial administration, and hubs of regional commerce. In *The [[Giant Raft]]*, missions appear as waypoints along the [[Jangada]]'s voyage, representing the intersection of European civilization and indigenous life on the [[Amazon]].

## In the Story

### The Mission at Iquitos

The most prominent mission in the novel is the one at [[Iquitos]], where [[Padre Passanha]] serves as chief. The narrative describes Iquitos as having been "founded by the missionaries" [009-027], established as a settlement "up to the seventeenth year of the century" [003-003]. The mission's bell called the faithful to "the dilapidated cottage which served them for a [[Church]]" [003-005] — a humble structure reflecting the frontier character of [[Upper Amazon]] Catholicism.

[[Padre Passanha]], "the accomplished type of those great missionaries who have done so much for civilization in the interior of South America" [009-026], presides over the [[Iquitos]] mission. When the [[Jangada]] departs, the priest is "preparing to return to Pará" [009-028], having been "placed at Iquitos by a younger missionary." The transition of mission leadership mirrors the broader passage of the colonial era into the more secular 19th century.

### The Mission of Cocha

During the journey downriver, the expedition encounters the mission of Cocha, "in charge of a Franciscan monk" [011-008]. The narrator recounts the mission's history: "It was in 1770 that this mission was founded by the Jesuit missionaries" [011-013], and the party is "received by the neophytes of the mission" [011-010] — indigenous converts who work the mission's lands and attend its services.

The Cocha mission becomes a waypoint in the story of [[Madame Valdez]] (Manoel's mother), whose escape through the [[Amazon]] involved being taken by "Indians, who took her to the missions, where the escort was waiting for her" [011-070]. The missions thus serve as sanctuaries — islands of European order in the indigenous wilderness.

### The Mission Economy

Verne's account notes the economic dimension of [[Amazon]] missions. Indigenous converts at the mission stations cultivated tobacco and other crops "which he bought at the mission stations or in the villages" [001-020]. The missions functioned as trading posts, with "the lay brothers of the mission, who are at the same time wholesale merchants" [010-054], facilitating the flow of Amazon products to markets downstream.

### The Hispano-Brazilian Missions

The narrative also references the geopolitical role of missions in the contested borderlands between Spanish and Portuguese spheres: "There was no better method for the profit of the Hispano-Brazilian missions" [012-010] than the river trade that connected the [[Upper Amazon]] to Pará. Missions served as anchors of territorial claims, their presence on the riverbank signifying the reach of colonial administration.

## Historical Context

The Jesuit missions of the [[Amazon]] reached their zenith in the 17th and 18th centuries, before the order's expulsion from Portuguese territories in 1759 and Spanish territories in 1767. At their peak, the missions constituted a network of self-sufficient communities, each centered on a [[Church]], school, and workshops, sustained by indigenous labor organized under the *reducción* system.

After the Jesuit expulsion, Franciscan and Carmelite orders assumed responsibility for many [[Amazon]] missions, though on a reduced scale. By the mid-19th century — the period of *The [[Giant Raft]]* — the missions had declined in political and economic importance, supplanted by secular settlements and the growing [[rubber]] trade. The "dilapidated cottage" that served as [[Iquitos]]'s [[Church]] [003-005] reflects this diminished state, as does the transfer of [[Padre Passanha]] to Pará.

## Significance

The missions in *The [[Giant Raft]]* embody the complex legacy of European colonization in the [[Amazon]]. They are simultaneously institutions of cultural imposition — the "neophytes" of Cocha, the mission bell summoning indigenous converts — and genuine centers of refuge, education, and commerce. Verne's treatment is characteristically ambivalent: he admires "those great missionaries who have done so much for civilization" [009-026] while acknowledging the missions' role in a colonial project that displaced and transformed indigenous societies.

The presence of missions throughout the [[Jangada]]'s journey also grounds the narrative in a specific historical geography. Each mission — [[Iquitos]], Cocha, Laguna — is a real place on the [[Amazon]], and Verne's inclusion of them lends the novel its documentary texture. The [[Giant Raft]] is not merely a fictional voyage; it is a journey through a landscape thick with the accumulated history of European presence in the Americas.
