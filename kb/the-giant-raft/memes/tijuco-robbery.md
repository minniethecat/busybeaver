# The Tijuco Robbery

## Summary

The Tijuco diamond robbery of 1826 was the notorious crime that set the entire plot of *The Giant Raft* in motion. A gang of armed bandits ambushed the annual diamond convoy departing Tijuco, massacring the military escort and stealing gems worth millions. A young clerk named Joam Dacosta was wrongly accused, convicted, and sentenced to death for orchestrating the crime — though he escaped and lived for twenty-three years as Joam Garral, the true perpetrators included the blackmailer Torres and his accomplice Ortega [019-032][020-006].

## Background

In the Brazilian diamond district centered on Tijuco, it was customary to send off in one delivery the diamonds collected during the year. The stones were divided into two lots according to size after being sorted in a dozen sieves with holes of different dimensions. These lots, worth many millions, were put into sacks and forwarded to Rio de Janeiro under heavy escort — a workman chosen by the superintendent, four cavalrymen from the district regiment, and ten men on foot. For the sake of precaution, the start was always kept secret [019-032].

> "At Tijuco sudden fortunes are realized enough to turn any man's head!" [019-020]

The young Joam Dacosta, about twenty-two or twenty-three years of age, had been employed in the offices of the governor-general at Tijuco — putting him in a position to know the date on which the convoy's departure was to take place. This proximity to sensitive information would prove fateful [019-032].

## Narrative

On the night of January 22, 1826, close to Vila Rica, a gang of armed smugglers ambushed the diamond convoy. The escort defended themselves bravely, but all were massacred, with the exception of one man who, seriously wounded, managed to escape and bring news of the horrible deed. The workman accompanying the convoy was not spared any more than the soldiers. He fell beneath the blows of the thieves, and his body was never found [019-032].

> "A terrible drama happened at Tijuco, which showed that criminals would recoil from nothing if they could gain a fortune by one bold stroke." [019-028]

Suspicion quickly fell on Joam Dacosta. Because he held a position that gave him access to the convoy's departure date, he was accused of having informed the gang. He protested his innocence in vain. He was charged, arrested, tried, and sentenced to death — a sentence requiring his execution within twenty-four hours [019-034]. Dacosta escaped from the prison at Vila Rica a few hours before his scheduled execution [020-008].

The stolen diamonds were never recovered. Years later, Torres revealed that he had been a member of the actual gang — and that his comrade Ortega had been the true author of the crime [020-012][038-028]. Torres used his knowledge to blackmail Joam Dacosta, whose innocence could only be definitively proven by deciphering the cryptogram that Ortega had composed and entrusted to Torres on his deathbed [039-040].

## Participants

- **[[joam-garral]]** (Joam Dacosta) — The young clerk wrongly accused, convicted, and sentenced to death for the crime
- **[[torres]]** — Member of the actual gang who committed the robbery; later became Joam's blackmailer
- **[[ortega]]** — The true perpetrator who authored the cryptogram; feigned death during the attack and escaped
- The governor-general of Tijuco — Employer of Dacosta at the time of the robbery
- The military escort (4 cavalrymen, 10 infantry) — All massacred except one survivor

## Consequences

The robbery's consequences rippled across more than two decades. Joam Dacosta, a condemned fugitive, rebuilt his life as Joam Garral — a wealthy fazendero at Iquitos with a wife and children. His desire to clear his name and secure his daughter Minha's marriage drove the entire Amazon journey [023-007]. The cryptogram, composed by Ortega and possessed by Torres, became the sole material proof of Dacosta's innocence — its decipherment by Judge Jarriquez using Ortega's name as the key would ultimately restore Dacosta's honor [039-042].

> "The material proof sought after for so long was the incontestable witness of the innocence of Joam Dacosta, returned to life, restored to honor." [039-042]

## Timeline

The robbery occurred in 1826, when the barber Fragoso was about eight years old and Joam Dacosta was twenty-two or twenty-three [019-028][019-032]. Twenty-three years later, as the Garral family journeyed down the Amazon aboard the jangada, the past caught up with Joam at Manaus, where Torres confronted him with the accusation and the cryptogram [020-006]. The resolution came only after Torres's death and Fragoso's discovery of the name Ortega — the single missing piece that unlocked the cipher and proved Joam's innocence [038-080].
