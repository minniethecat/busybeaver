# Torres

## Introduction

Torres is the primary antagonist of *The [[Giant Raft]]*. A former [[Capitão do Mato]] — a [[Bush-Captain]] employed to hunt escaped slaves in the Brazilian wilderness — Torres comes into possession of the [[Cryptogram]] that contains the confession proving [[Joam Dacosta]]'s innocence. Rather than deliver this document to the authorities, he attempts to use it for personal gain: first through blackmail, then through a demand for [[Minha]]'s hand in marriage. His sudden death — struck by lightning while confronting Joam aboard the [[Jangada]] — transforms him from antagonist to plot mechanism, as the encrypted document he carried becomes the sole key to Joam's salvation.

## Biography

Torres is introduced in the novel's opening pages as "a fitting comrade for his fellow [[Capitães do Mato]]" [001-008], immediately establishing his rough, marginal background in the Brazilian wilderness. Unlike most backwoodsmen, however, "he bore some special grudge" [002-009] — he is driven not by poverty or mere opportunism but by a calculating ambition that sets him apart from his peers.

When Torres handles the [[Cryptogram]], "it seemed as though the hands of Torres felt the enormous sum" [001-018] — a revealing gesture that exposes his primary motivation: greed. He "glanced over the yellow paper for the last time" [001-020] before concealing it, marking the transition from possessor to extortionist.

Torres's profession as a [[Capitão do Mato]] — a [[Bush-Captain]] who hunted escaped slaves — places him in the morally compromised space between civilization and wilderness. The narrative notes that he is not one "who go to sleep without making sure that no one is near them" [001-023], emphasizing the constant vigilance of a man who lives by his wits and trusts no one.

> Torres was not one of those people who drop off to sleep without certain preliminaries. He was in the habit of drinking a drop or two of strong liquor, and of then smoking a pipe; the spirits, he said, overexcited the brain, and the tobacco smoke agreeably mingled with the general haziness of his reverie. [001-023]

## Character Analysis

### The Calculating Opportunist

Torres is distinguished from Verne's more theatrical villains by his cold calculation. He does not act on impulse; he plans. His decision to shadow the Garral family down the [[Amazon]], waiting for the optimal moment to reveal his knowledge, demonstrates patience and strategic thinking. He understands that the [[Cryptogram]]'s value increases as Joam approaches Belém — closer to the authorities, closer to his past catching up with him.

When a [[guariba]] monkey steals the case containing his precious document, Torres reveals the relentless persistence beneath his calculation:

> Torres threw himself in pursuit of the [[guariba]]. He knew that to reach such an active animal was not easy. On the ground he could get away too fast, in the branches he could get away too far. A well-aimed gunshot could alone stop him as he ran or climbed, but Torres possessed no firearm. [002-027]

> An hour passed in this way without any result. Torres showed a persistency which was quite natural. How without this document could he get his money? [002-032]

### The Blackmail Scene

Torres's confrontation with Joam aboard the [[Jangada]] is the novel's dramatic centerpiece. He demands half of Joam's fortune in exchange for the document, then escalates his demand when refused: he will accept [[Minha]]'s hand in marriage instead [020-051]. This escalation reveals Torres's character flaw — he overreaches. Greed gives way to something more personal: the desire to insert himself into the family he is threatening.

The opening of the blackmail scene is captured in Torres's own words, cold and direct:

> "Joam," he said, "your name is not Garral. Your name is Dacosta!" At the guilty name which Torres thus gave him, [[Joam Garral]] could not repress a slight shudder. "You are [[Joam Dacosta]]," continued Torres, "who, twenty-five years ago, were a clerk in the governor-general's office at [[Tijuco]], and you are the man who was sentenced to death in this affair of the robbery and murder!" [020-004]

Joam's refusal — "I refuse the document! I refuse the fortune!" [020-069] — catches Torres off guard. He has misjudged his target: Joam values his honor over his life. Torres's shock at this refusal is the first sign that his strategy, however carefully planned, is built on a misunderstanding of his adversary.

> "You are a consummate scoundrel, Torres," quietly said Joam, whose coolness never forsook him. "A scoundrel and a murderer were made to understand each other." [020-033]

### The Menace Beneath the Surface

Torres's menace is not always overt. [[Benito]] perceives it in moments of quiet observation, noting the evil that flickers across the adventurer's face:

> [[Benito]] was not deceived when he said that the adventurer's eyes were never off [[Joam Garral]] as long as he fancied he was unobserved. No! he was not deceived when he said that Torres' face grew evil when he looked at his father! [017-028]

This quality of concealed threat makes Torres more unsettling than a purely brutish antagonist. He is a man who ingratiates himself, speaks "more willingly to [[Yaquita]] and her daughter" [016-095], while nursing a secret that could destroy the entire family.

### Death and Narrative Function

Torres's death is among the most melodramatic moments in Verne's fiction. Struck by lightning while standing on the [[Jangada]], his body is hurled into the [[Amazon]] [031-062]. The suddenness of his death serves a dual narrative purpose: it removes the antagonist without Joam having to commit violence, and it transforms the [[Cryptogram]] from an instrument of blackmail into a desperate puzzle — the only remaining key to Joam's exoneration.

The final duel with [[Benito]] on the cliffs above the [[Amazon]] reveals Torres's last words and the culmination of his villainy:

> "Torres!" exclaimed Benito, "defend yourself, or I will kill you like a mad dog!" "Mad! so be it!" answered Torres. "But I bite, [[Benito Dacosta]], and beware of the wounds!" [026-082]

[[Benito]]'s subsequent dive to recover Torres's body is one of the novel's most suspenseful sequences. Torres, dead, remains central to the plot: his corpse carries the document, and his past — revealed through [[Fragoso]]'s recognition — connects him to [[The Tijuco Robbery]] gang, providing the crucial link that eventually leads to the [[Cryptogram]]'s deciphering.

> Torres is dead, father; killed by my hand! "Dead!—Torres!—Dead!" gasped [[Joam Dacosta]]. "My son! You have ruined me!" [026-123]

## Relationships

- [[joam-garral]] — The object of Torres's blackmail. Torres recognizes Joam as Joam Dacosta, the fugitive from [[Tijuco]], and attempts to leverage this knowledge. Their confrontation aboard the [[Jangada]] [020-051] [020-069] is the novel's dramatic turning point.

- [[minha]] — Torres's escalation from demanding money to demanding Minha's hand in marriage [020-051] transforms his blackmail from financial crime to personal violation. Minha becomes an unwilling bargaining chip, and Torres's demand for her reveals the predatory nature beneath his calculating exterior.

> "You have a daughter!—I like her—and I want to marry her!" ... "I am the sole judge of what it suits me to do," said Torres. "I wish to be the son-in-law of [[Joam Garral]], and I will." [020-023]

- [[fragoso]] — The loquacious barber who recognizes Torres from his past. Fragoso reveals that Torres was connected to the gang that committed the [[Tijuco]] [[Diamond]] robbery — the very crime for which Joam was condemned. This revelation provides the crucial link between Torres's possession of the [[Cryptogram]] and the original crime.

- [[manoel]] — Manoel's antipathy toward Torres is immediate and instinctive. When Torres boards the [[Jangada]], Manoel is "quite disposed" to confront him [016-095], and during the blackmail scene, Manoel's outrage reflects the reader's own.

## In the Novel

### Part I: The Opening and the Shadowing

Torres appears in Chapter 1, alone in the forest, handling the [[Cryptogram]] — a document "written many years before" [001-005]. These opening pages establish his character entirely through gesture and interior monologue: his greed, his vigilance, his calculating nature. He is a man who has been waiting for his moment, and the moment is approaching.

Throughout the [[Jangada]]'s voyage, Torres shadows the family, appearing periodically. His presence is unsettling rather than overtly threatening — he speaks "more willingly to [[Yaquita]] and her daughter" [016-095], ingratiating himself while observing. The family's "evident coolness" toward him [016-095] registers the instinctive recognition of a predator.

### Part II: The Confrontation and Death

The blackmail scene in Chapter 20 is the novel's pivot. Everything that follows — the arrest, the trial, the imprisonment, the [[Cryptogram]] sessions — flows from this confrontation. Torres's demand, Joam's refusal, and the subsequent arrest at Manaos form a causal chain that drives the entire second half.

> "A reply," slowly answered Torres, "that will keep me from finding out the chief of the police at [[Manaus]], and saying to him, 'A man is there whose identity can easily be established, who can be recognized even after twenty-five years' absence, and this man was the instigator of the [[Diamond]] robbery at [[Tijuco]]. He was the accomplice of the murderers of the soldiers of the escort; he is the man who escaped from execution; he is [[Joam Garral]], whose true name is [[Joam Dacosta]].'" [020-012]

Torres's death by lightning in Chapter 31 is abrupt and, in retrospect, inevitable: a man who lived by exploiting secrets dies with the most important secret still locked in the document on his body. His death creates the novel's central puzzle — the [[Cryptogram]] must now be deciphered without its possessor's cooperation.

## Significance

Torres serves multiple functions in *The [[Giant Raft]]*. As antagonist, he personifies the threat of exposure that shadows Joam throughout the voyage. As plot mechanism, his possession of the [[Cryptogram]] and sudden death create the conditions for the novel's detective-story second half. As character, he represents the dark side of the [[Amazon]] frontier — a man shaped by the violence and moral ambiguity of the [[Capitão do Mato]] system, who sees the cryptogram not as an instrument of justice but as a commodity to be sold to the highest bidder.

His death is thematically significant as well. Lightning — an act of nature, of God, of chance — resolves what human justice cannot. Torres cannot be prosecuted for blackmail because Joam cannot reveal the blackmail without revealing his own identity. The deus ex machina of the lightning strike preserves Joam's moral purity (he never has to dirty his hands) while removing the obstacle that his honor would not allow him to remove himself.

> "The writing is in a safe place," replied Torres, "and you will not have it until your daughter has become my wife. Now will you still refuse me?" [020-049]
