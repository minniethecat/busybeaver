# Jangada Construction

## Summary

The construction of the giant [[Jangada]] at [[Iquitos]] was the preparatory event that enabled [[Joam Dacosta]]'s family to journey down the [[Amazon River]]. Over the course of a month, an immense floating raft measuring a thousand feet long and sixty feet wide was assembled from felled trees, complete with living quarters, a chapel, and supplies — forming what resembled a small village on the water [006-017] [008-013] [009-013].

## Narrative

[[Joam Garral]] had for many years sent rafts of wood from his [[Fazenda]] to [[Manaus]] and Belém along the [[Amazon]] [004-016]. When the time came for his daughter [[Minha]]'s wedding in Belém, he decided to construct an unusually large raft to carry his entire family and household downstream [006-014] [006-016].

The [[Jangada]] was to be built at a spot where the river formed a natural harbor, and work proceeded throughout the month [006-024] [006-033]. The plan was not yet complete at the outset, requiring adjustments as construction advanced [007-005]. The raft measured a thousand feet long and sixty feet wide — enormous proportions even by [[Amazon]] standards [008-013].

Trees felled from the [[Fazenda]] were arranged to form the floor of the [[Jangada]], rising about thirty inches above the waterline [008-013]. Long boathooks were prepared to keep the [[Giant Raft]] in midstream during navigation [008-023]. At the rear of the jangada, the master's house was built, serving as the family residence [008-024]. [[Benito]] suggested planting trees on the raft to beautify it [008-042].

A huge pile of supplies was loaded aboard, including a special wine cellar that did honor to [[Benito]]'s preparations [009-004] [009-009]. Fifty hundredweight of goods were taken on, and the [[Jangada]] took on the appearance of a small village, with houses, a chapel, and stores [009-013] [009-018]. The craft was secured from land-based threats by the river itself [009-020].

To keep the raft in the current, long oars were employed — though ordinary oars had no effect on a raft of such dimensions [009-023]. A little bell surmounted a small chapel built in the center of the [[Jangada]] [009-032]. When the river rose, there was concern the raft might flood, but the construction held [009-039].

At the critical moment of launch, the raft was moored to the bank with strong cables until the waters lifted it [009-044]. Six cries of joy arose as the [[Jangada]] floated at last, and the current began to carry it downstream [009-050].

## Participants

- **[[joam-dacosta|Joam Dacosta]]** — Commissioned the raft and provided the timber from his [[Fazenda]]
- **[[benito|Benito]]** — Oversaw much of the construction and provisioning
- **[[manoel|Manoel]]** — Assisted with the planning and preparation
- **[[fragoso|Fragoso]]** — Contributed to the work as part of the household

## Timeline

The construction occurred during the month preceding the family's departure, concluding with the launch of the [[Jangada]] on the eve of the journey downstream. The raft was ready to receive passengers on the morning of departure, marking the beginning of the [[Amazon]] voyage [010-002] [010-005].
