# Orellana

## Introduction

Francisco de Orellana is the 16th-century Spanish explorer who, as a lieutenant of Gonzalo Pizarro, became the first European to navigate the entire length of the [[Amazon River]] in 1541–1542. In "The [[Giant Raft]]," Jules Verne repeatedly invokes Orellana as a historical parallel to the Garral family's own [[Amazon]] voyage, using the conquistador's legendary journey to frame the modern expedition within centuries of Amazon exploration [005-024][005-035].

## In the Novel

Verne introduces Orellana early in the narrative, describing how the Garral party follows in the wake of "the adventurous Orellana, whose vague but enthusiastic narratives had from the commencement called attention to the marvelous regions of the [[Amazon]]" [005-024]. The author notes that in the 16th century, Orellana, as "the lieutenant of one of the brothers Pizarro," undertook the first recorded descent of the great river [005-035].

Throughout the journey, the characters are conscious of retracing Orellana's route. The narrator observes how they traveled "going down it, however, like Orellana and La Condamine did so many years before" — connecting the 19th-century family expedition to the 16th-century voyage of discovery [011-033]. References to Orellana's encounters with indigenous "warriors who so much astonished" him serve to contrast the perilous nature of the original exploration with the relative safety of the modern raft journey [015-048].

Near the novel's conclusion, the narrative returns to Orellana's legacy, mentioning the river [[Nhamundas]] upon which, in 1539, Orellana asserted he was attacked by female warriors — the legendary Amazons who gave the river its name [040-004]. This final reference ties the novel's end back to the beginning, framing the entire narrative as one episode in the long history of [[Amazon]] exploration.

## Significance

Orellana serves as a historical touchstone in Verne's narrative, connecting the fictional voyage of the Garral family to the real history of [[Amazon]] exploration. By repeatedly invoking Orellana's name, Verne situates his adventure story within a tradition of discovery that stretches back to the earliest European contact with the Americas. The contrast between Orellana's perilous 16th-century expedition and the Garral family's relatively comfortable journey aboard the [[Jangada]] highlights the technological and social changes of three centuries, while also underscoring the enduring mystery and danger of the Amazon.
