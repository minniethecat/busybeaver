# Judge Jarriquez

## Introduction

Judge Vicente Jarriquez is the magistrate of [[Manaus]] who conducts the preliminary investigation following the [[Arrest of Joam Dacosta]] -- revealed to be [[Joam Garral]] living under an assumed identity. Jarriquez is a complex judicial figure: sharp, skeptical, yet fundamentally just, whose interrogation of the accused forms a pivotal chapter in the novel's legal drama [024-001][024-003][024-004].

## Biography

Judge Jarriquez is described as "a surly little fellow, whom forty years' practice in criminal procedure had not rendered particularly friendly toward those who came before him" [024-003]. Despite his gruff exterior, he "was not a bad man. Nervous, fidgety, talkative, keen, crafty, he had a curious look about him, with his big head on his little body; his ruffled hair, which would not have disgraced the judge's wig of the past; his piercing gimlet-like eyes" [024-004]. He was an "original" whose private interests included chess, of which he was a past master, and "Chinese puzzles, enigmas, charades, rebuses, anagrams, riddles, and such things" [024-005][024-006]. He was a confirmed old bachelor, wholly dedicated to his professional duties [024-005]. His task was, in principle, straightforward: to examine the accused and determine whether the charges warranted a full trial [024-007].

## Relationships

- **[[joam-garral]]** (Joam Dacosta) -- The accused whom Jarriquez interrogates; their exchange forms the core of chapters 24--25
- **[[Judge Ribeiro]]** -- Jarriquez's predecessor in [[Tijuco]], who had defended Dacosta at the original trial and never doubted his innocence [024-068][024-069]

## In the Novel

Upon [[Joam Dacosta]]'s arrest, Judge Jarriquez made his way to the prison in God-the-Son Street to begin the interrogation [024-010]. Seated before his desk, perched on his high chair with his back turned toward the window so his face remained in shadow while the accused stood in full daylight, he prepared for the examination [024-011]. He studied the accused for some time before speaking, his small eyes hidden by their lids and seemingly trying to penetrate the prisoner's thoughts [024-013]. He opened with the formal question: "Your name?" [024-014].

As [[Joam Dacosta]] recounted his life story -- the escape from [[Tijuco]], the years of honest labor building the [[Fazenda]] at [[Iquitos]], his family -- Judge Jarriquez found himself "little accustomed to such a course" [024-024]. The judge's small eyes seemed to gleam with interest despite his effort to maintain an impassive judicial mask, and the narrator notes that Jarriquez seemed to say to himself: "Never came across anything like this before" [024-029][024-030].

When Dacosta appealed to the memory of [[Judge Ribeiro]] -- the magistrate who had defended him at the original [[Tijuco]] trial and who never doubted his innocence -- Jarriquez responded with marked incredulity: "Really! ... You wrote to Judge Ribeiro" [024-068][024-069][024-065]. Dacosta's plea that all would be lost if he did not find "another Judge Ribeiro" in Jarriquez was met with evident hardness of heart [024-069][024-072].

> "Eh! [[Joam Dacosta]]," quickly replied Judge Jarriquez. "You protest your innocence; but all prisoners do as much! After all, you only offer moral presumptions. Have you any material proof?" [024-077]

At these words, Judge Jarriquez left his chair -- "This was too much for him, and he had to take two or three circuits of the room to recover himself" [024-079]. He then declared the exchange "too strong," a reaction that revealed both his frustration and his reluctant engagement with the case [024-079].

When the examination resumed, Jarriquez could not restrain a movement of his shoulders when Dacosta insisted that moral proofs were "those most worthy of being brought forward in matters of justice" [025-006]. His brusque manner -- "To the point" -- pressed the accused toward the material proofs that would ultimately determine the case [025-008]. By the end of the examination, Jarriquez found himself unsettled:

> "Well, well! This is a much stranger affair than I ever thought it would be!" [025-051]

## Significance

Judge Jarriquez represents the machinery of imperial Brazilian justice -- bureaucratic, skeptical, and procedurally rigid, yet not fundamentally corrupt. His character serves to dramatize the precarious position of [[Joam Dacosta]], whose fate hangs not on the truth of his innocence but on the cold procedures of the law.

> He had either to inquire nor to rule; he had not even to regulate a discussion nor to obtain a verdict, neither to apply the articles of the penal code nor to pronounce a sentence. Unfortunately for the fazender, such formalities were no longer necessary [024-007].

Through Jarriquez, Verne critiques a legal system in which justice depends less on moral truth than on the production of documentary proof -- a tension that drives the novel's climactic race to decipher the [[Cryptogram]].
