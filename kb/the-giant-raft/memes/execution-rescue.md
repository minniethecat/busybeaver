# Near-Execution and Rescue of Joam Dacosta

## Introduction

The near-execution and last-minute rescue of [[Joam Dacosta]] constitutes the dramatic climax of "The [[Giant Raft]]." Condemned to death for the [[Tijuco]] [[Diamond]] robbery of 1826, [[Joam Garral]] (living under an assumed name) was arrested at [[Manaus]] and sentenced to execution. Moments before the sentence was to be carried out, a cannon shot rang out — the prearranged signal that the [[Cryptogram]] had been successfully deciphered, proving his innocence and halting the execution [031-018].

## Background

Following his arrest at [[Manaus]], [[Joam Dacosta]] was imprisoned and brought before [[Judge Jarriquez]] for a preliminary investigation [024-010]. Despite Joam's protestations of innocence, the judge demanded proof — proof that Joam could not immediately produce. The [[Cryptogram]] that held the key to his exoneration remained undeciphered, and [[Torres]], the blackmailer who possessed the document, held the only copy.

After [[Torres]]'s death, the [[Cryptogram]] was recovered, but the crucial key — the name of the document's author — remained unknown. It was [[Fragoso]], the barber, who discovered during his travels that the name "[[Ortega]]" was the key to the cipher [038-023][038-051]. Armed with this name, [[Judge Jarriquez]] was able to reconstitute the cryptogram's message, revealing that Ortega — not [[Joam Dacosta]] — was the true author of [[The Tijuco Robbery]] [038-065][038-066].

## The Rescue

As the hour of execution approached, the tension aboard the [[Jangada]] and in the prison became unbearable. A cannon had been positioned as a signaling device: if the [[Cryptogram]] was deciphered and Joam was proven innocent before the execution, the cannon would be fired to halt the proceedings [031-018].

At the critical moment — when the executioner's hand was raised — the cannon shot echoed across [[Manaus]]. The signal was recognized: [[Joam Dacosta]] was innocent. The execution was halted, and Joam was freed [031-018].

## Aftermath

[[Joam Dacosta]]'s exoneration transformed the final chapters of the novel. The family, which had been plunged into despair by the arrest and impending execution, was reunited in joy. The [[Wedding of Minha and Manoel]] Valdez, which had been postponed by the crisis, could now proceed. The [[Jangada]] completed its voyage to Pará, carrying not a condemned man but a vindicated one.

## Significance

The near-execution and rescue is the novel's most suspenseful sequence and a classic example of Verne's use of last-minute reprieve — a device he employed in several of his works. The cannon shot that halts the execution functions as a deus ex machina grounded in rational, documentary proof: Joam is saved not by supernatural intervention but by the successful application of cryptographic analysis. This resolution reinforces the novel's Enlightenment values — truth, reason, and persistence triumph over injustice and corruption.
