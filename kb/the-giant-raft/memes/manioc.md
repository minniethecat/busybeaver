# Manioc

**Manioc** (cassava) is the principal food crop of the [[Amazon Basin]] and a dietary staple for the characters in _The Giant Raft_. The root of the manioc plant — specifically the sweet manioc variety — provides flour, bread, and fermented beverages that sustain both native inhabitants and travelers along the river.

## Cultivation

Along the [[Amazon]], manioc is cultivated in small clearings and plantations. The landscape near [[Iquitos]] features "a few _sítios_, or manioc and coffee plantations" scattered among the forest [003-019]. Further downriver, the following year's harvest is planned: "fields of manioc, coffee-shrubs, sugar-canes, and pineapples" will be planted in the rich alluvial soil [006-032].

The manioc plant reaches a height of about six feet and "forms the principal food of the native population of the [[Amazon]] valley" [009-003]. Its cultivation requires minimal effort — the root is simply boiled to remove naturally occurring toxins in the bitter variety, or eaten directly in the case of the sweet manioc.

## Preparation and Consumption

Manioc is prepared in several distinct forms along the [[Amazon]]:

- **_Beiju_**: A Brazilian preparation — "a sort of national tart" made from manioc flour, thoroughly mixed with spices and baked [009-007]
- **Manioc flour (_farinha_)**: The boiled root of the sweet manioc is pounded into a fine flour that forms the basis of many meals. [[Fragoso]], the traveling barber, "refreshed himself with a little assai, some manioc flour, and turtle eggs" at a river settlement [012-061]
- **Manioc cakes**: Mixed with spices and formed into flat cakes, providing a portable food for river journeys

Manioc flour is often mixed with water and fermented into a mildly alcoholic beverage called _caxiri_, consumed at celebrations and gatherings along the river [011-010].

## Role as a Staple Food

The captain of the [[Jangada]] carried provisions that included "the root of the sweet manioc" as an essential part of the journey's food supply [001-024]. Manioc's importance to Amazonian life cannot be overstated — it is the caloric foundation upon which the region's cultures are built, providing reliable sustenance in an environment where other crops may fail.
