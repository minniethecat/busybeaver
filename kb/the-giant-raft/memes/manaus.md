# Manaus

## Overview

Capital city of Amazonas province where Judge Jarriquez presides — the destination where Joam's fate will be decided. Manaus is the pivotal location of the novel's second half: the jangada's voyage is interrupted here by Joam Dacosta's arrest, and the cryptogram deciphering effort unfolds within its walls. As Verne describes it, Manaus is "on the left bank of the Rio Negro, the most important and remarkable of all the tributaries of the great artery of Brazil" [021-003]. By 1852, the town -- formerly called Barra de Rio Negro -- had become the capital of the large province of Amazonas [021-007]. It is where the Rio Negro's dark waters meet the Amazon, and where the journey's most dramatic events take place: the arrest, the interrogation, the deciphering, and ultimately the vindication.

## Geography

Manaus is situated at 3 degrees 8 minutes 4 seconds south latitude, some four hundred and twenty leagues from Belem and about ten miles from the embouchure of the Rio Negro [021-002]. The town is not built on the Amazon itself but on the left bank of the Rio Negro [021-003]. The Rio Negro rises in the heart of the province of Popayan and, after a noble course of some seventeen hundred miles, "mingles its cloudy waters with those of the Amazon through a mouth eleven hundred feet wide" [021-004]. Such is its vigorous influx that many miles must pass before the dark waters fully blend with the Amazon's current.

The convergence of the two rivers creates a huge bay fifteen leagues across, extending to the islands of Anavilhanas, and in one of its indentations the port of Manaus is situated [021-005]. Vessels of all kinds gather there, some moored awaiting favorable winds, others under repair along the numerous *iguarapes* (canals) that intersect the town and give it what Verne describes as "its slightly Dutch appearance" [021-005]. The Amazon itself, from the frontier of Brazil to Manaus, takes the name of the Solimoes, and only from Manaus onward to the sea does it become the Amazonas proper [005-024].

The town's trade was already significant by the mid-19th century. With the introduction of steam vessels, Verne notes that "the trade of Manaus is destined to increase enormously" [021-006]. Woods, cocoa, caoutchouc, coffee, sarsaparilla, sugar-canes, indigo, muscado nuts, salt fish, and turtle butter were all brought here from across the Amazon basin, arriving via the Rio Negro from the west and north, the Madeira from the west and south, before being shipped eastward to the Atlantic coast [021-006].

Three principal thoroughfares run through the town, bearing names "highly characteristic of the tone of thought prevalent in these parts — God-the-Father Street, God-the-Son Street, and God-the-Holy Ghost Street" [021-009]. The prison where Joam Dacosta was held, an old missionary convent, was situated on God-the-Son Street, on the bank of one of the principal iguarapes of the town [025-010].

## In the Novel

Manaus serves as the narrative fulcrum of "The Giant Raft." The jangada's approach to the city marks the point where the Garral family's peaceful voyage transforms into a struggle for survival.

**The Approach.** As the raft nears its destination, the family anticipates relief — they believe in twenty days they shall be at Manaus, where Torres must leave them [017-021]. The approach is measured by the darkening waters of the Rio Negro: "Three days passed. They neared Manaus. Twenty-four hours more and the raft would be off the mouth of the Rio Negro, before the capital of the province of Amazonas" [018-040]. Joam Garral has already arranged his meeting with Judge Ribeiro, the first magistrate of the province — the one man who knows his true identity and believes in his innocence:

> "Tomorrow I shall be with Judge Ribeiro, the first magistrate of the province, whom I have advised of my arrival at Manaus. If you dare, meet me there!" [020-090]

**The Arrest.** On the morning of August 24th, a large pirogue comes off from the left bank and hails the jangada. A dozen paddlers have "swiftly brought it from Manaus, and with a few men it carried the chief of the police, who made himself known and came on board" [020-105]. Joam Garral is arrested under his true name, Joam Dacosta — the condemned man of the Tijuco diamond robbery. The warrant was issued not by Judge Ribeiro but by his substitute: Ribeiro had been "struck with apoplexy yesterday evening, and died during the night at two o'clock, without having recovered his consciousness" [020-112].

Torres's earlier threat had been precise in its menace:

> "A reply that will keep me from finding out the chief of the police at Manaus, and saying to him, 'A man is there whose identity can easily be established, who can be recognized even after twenty-five years' absence, and this man was the instigator of the diamond robbery at Tijuco.'" [020-012]

**The Interrogation.** On the morrow of the arrest, Judge Vicente Jarriquez — "a surly little fellow" with "piercing gimlet-like eyes" and a mind sharpened by decades of criminal procedure [025-003] — conducts the formal examination. The prison is an old missionary convent on God-the-Son Street. Jarriquez, though not a bad man, is predisposed to see guilt: "he had had so many cases of this sort, and tried and sentenced so many rascals, that a prisoner's innocence seemed to him a priori inadmissable" [025-003]. Joam Dacosta protests his innocence and speaks of a material proof — a document held by Torres — but without it in hand, the judge remains unmoved:

> "You protest your innocence; but all prisoners do as much! After all, you only offer moral presumptions. Have you any material proof?" [025-032]

**The Cryptogram.** Manaus becomes the setting for the frantic deciphering effort. Judge Jarriquez, who happens to be "a past master" at puzzles, enigmas, charades, and anagrams [025-005], receives the cryptogram document and attempts to crack its code. The document — a hundred lines of seemingly random letters — contains the confession of the true author of the Tijuco crime. Jarriquez works through the permutations but cannot find the key; he ultimately declares it undecipherable. Yet Fragoso, through a stroke of serendipitous memory, discovers that the true criminal signed his name "Ortega" — a name he had encountered during his wandering life — and this knowledge ultimately leads to the cryptogram's solution and Joam's vindication.

**Manaus as Destination and Threshold.** Before becoming the city of judgment, Manaus had long been part of the Garral family's commercial world. Joam's fazenda "every year sent rafts of wood to Manaus, to Belem, and the seacoast of Para" [004-016]. Yaquita, raised at Iquitos, might have been sent to "a convent at Manaus or Belem" for her education [003-031]. The arrival at Manaus transforms it from a trading destination into the stage where Joam's past and present collide. After the arrest, Yaquita resolves that "on the morrow she would go to the gate of the prison, and never leave it until it was opened" [021-013].

**Public Opinion.** The arrest stirs the population of Manaus. The Diario do Grao-Para publishes an article decidedly hostile to the prisoner [026-104], and "a mob of Indians and negroes hurried, in their blind folly, to surround the prison and roar forth tumultuous shouts of death" [026-105]. Only after the cryptogram is solved does public sentiment reverse completely: "To anger succeeded pity. The population no longer thronged to the prison of Manaus to roar out cries of death to the prisoner" [040-016].

## Historical Context

By 1881, the Amazon had been explored but remained a frontier of Western geographical knowledge. Verne's detailed descriptions draw on contemporary travel literature and scientific expeditions. Manaus itself was a city in transition — from a colonial outpost to a rubber-boom capital. Verne accurately notes the town's former name, Barra de Rio Negro, and its elevation to capital of Amazonas province in 1826 [021-007]. He describes its population of about five thousand, of which "at least three thousand are in government employ" [021-008], and catalogues its public buildings: the legislative chamber, the government house, the treasury, the post-office, the customhouse, a college founded in 1848, and a hospital erected in 1851 [021-008].

Verne also distinguishes Manaus from the legendary Manoa or El Dorado, the "city of romance" that explorers had sought near the legendary lake of Parima [021-008]. He wryly notes that the sixteenth-century fable of a monarch "every morning covered with powder of gold" was "disproved, and with extreme regret, for the auriferous deposits which had deceived the greedy scrutiny of the gold-seekers turned out to be only worthless flakes of mica" [021-008]. This distinction between myth and reality reflects Verne's characteristic blend of geographical precision and narrative color.

The town's trade in woods, cocoa, rubber (caoutchouc), coffee, and other commodities presages the Amazon rubber boom that would peak in the decades following the novel's publication, transforming Manaus into one of the wealthiest cities in Brazil.

## Journey Role

Manaus is the midpoint and crisis point of the 800-league journey from Iquitos to Belem aboard the jangada. The raft arrives here at the mouth of the Rio Negro and is detained while Joam Dacosta's fate hangs in the balance. As Fragoso's tale of the legendary trunk of Manaus illustrates, the city occupies a special place in local river-lore:

> "It appears that there is — or rather formerly was — a trunk of turuma, which every year at the same time descended the Rio Negro, stopping several days at Manaus, and going on into Para, halting at every port, where the natives ornamented it with little flags." [011-051]

The river from Manaus to the sea is the Amazonas proper — the final leg of the journey [005-024]. The city thus represents both a barrier and a gateway: the jangada cannot proceed until Joam's innocence is proved, but once vindication comes, the way to Belem lies open. Trombetas, the Guariba inn, and other landmarks along the lower Amazon await the raft's resumed voyage.

In the novel's architecture, Manaus divides the narrative. Part One — the voyage from Iquitos through the frontier and the Solimoes — concludes here. Part Two — imprisonment, the cryptogram puzzle, the execution countdown, and the eventual rescue — unfolds entirely within and around Manaus. The city is where the narrative pauses — as Verne writes:

> "Such is Manaus, which, for the benefit of the reader, it was necessary to sketch. Here the voyage of the giant raft, so tragically interrupted, had just come to a pause in the midst of its long journey, and here will be unfolded the further vicissitudes of the mysterious history of the fazender of Iquitos." [021-010]
