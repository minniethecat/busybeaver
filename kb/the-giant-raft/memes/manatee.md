# Manatee

The **manatee** (*peixe-boi* in Portuguese) is a large herbivorous aquatic mammal that inhabits the [[Amazon River]] and its tributaries. In *The [[Giant Raft]]*, the manatee appears in a hunting scene at the village of [[Coary]], and is referenced as one of the characteristic species of the [[Amazon]]'s black-water rivers.

## In the Story

### Black Water and Manatees

The manatee is first mentioned in connection with the phenomenon of the [[Amazon]]'s two-colored waters. When the travelers observe the striking contrast between the black waters of the [[Rio Negro]] and the yellowish waters of the Amazon, [[Fragoso]] remarks that one might "ask the opinions of the caymans, dolphins, and manatees, for they certainly prefer the black waters to the others to enjoy themselves in" (014-027). Manoel confirms that the black waters are "particularly attractive to those animals" (014-027).

### The Hunt at Coary

The principal manatee episode occurs during the [[Jangada]]'s stop at the village of [[Coary]]. The local inhabitants are described as "intrepid hunters, or, if they prefer it, intrepid fishers for the manatee" (015-036). On the morning of the party's arrival, [[Benito]], Manoel, and [[Fragoso]] witness a manatee-hunting expedition.

Two of "these herbivorous cetaceans" have been signaled in the black waters near the village (015-036). The hunters set out in ubas — small native canoes — each carrying three fishermen. One hunter stands in the bow with "a very primitive harpoon — a long nail at the end of a stick," while the other two noiselessly paddle (015-040).

The manatees, spouting to expel the air "which had become useless for their breathing purposes," initially flee when the boats approach (015-039). The hunters wait patiently for the animals to surface again — in "ten minutes or thereabouts" they must come up for air (015-040). When the black points emerge, the harpooner strikes.

### A Declining Species

Verne notes the manatee's declining population in the [[Amazon]], observing that the specimen harpooned at [[Coary]] "only measured about three feet long". He explains that "these poor cetaceans have been so hunted that they have become very rare in the Amazon and its affluents, and so little time is left them to grow that the giants of the species do not now exceed seven feet". This contrasts with manatees "twelve and fifteen feet long, which still abound in the rivers and lakes of Africa".

The reason for their persecution is clear: "the flesh of the manatee is excellent, superior even to that of pork, and the oil furnished by its lard, which is three inches thick, is a product of great value" (015-045). When smoke-dried, the meat "keeps for a long time" and becomes an important trade commodity.

## Natural History

The Amazonian manatee (*Trichechus inunguis*) is the smallest of the three manatee species and the only one restricted to fresh water. It is a herbivore, feeding on aquatic vegetation, and is entirely dependent on surfacing to breathe — the behavior that makes it vulnerable to harpoon hunters.

Verne accurately portrays the manatee as a peaceful creature: "this inoffensive animal, who rarely defends himself when touched by the iron of the harpoon". This gentleness, combined with its valuable meat and oil, has made it one of the [[Amazon]]'s most heavily exploited species.
