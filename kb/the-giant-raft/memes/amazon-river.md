# Amazon River

## Definition

The Amazon River is the principal waterway of South America, flowing eastward across the continent from the Peruvian Andes to the Atlantic Ocean. In Jules Verne's *The [[Giant Raft]]*, it serves as both the physical setting and narrative backbone — the characters travel its full navigable length aboard the [[Jangada]], an 800-league journey from the Peruvian frontier to the Brazilian coast (005-003).

## In the Novel

The Amazon is introduced as a vast and mysterious presence. [[Joam Garral]]'s [[Fazenda]] lies near [[Iquitos]], "on the left bank" of the river (003-002), in the forests of the Upper Amazon where the air is thick and "there is generally little wind" (001-019). The river's character shifts dramatically along the journey: from the narrow channels of the upper reaches, through the "windings of the river" that the [[Jangada]]'s crew must navigate (008-023), to the broad expanse of the Lower Amazon near the journey's end.

The narrative moves from [[Iquitos]] — described as a village "on the left bank of the Amazon" (003-002) — through a chain of settlements and landmarks: [[Pevas]], the frontier, [[Ega]], and finally [[Manaus]] and Belém. The voyage begins on June 6th, with the pilot Araujo at the bow and the crew armed with their long poles (010-003). Each reach of the river presents different challenges, from hidden sandbanks to the [[Tidal Bore]] known as the [[Pororoca]].

Verne emphasizes the river's dual nature: it is both a highway for civilization and a wild force. The [[Jangada]]'s journey exploits the Amazon's seasonal floods — between the mean and the higher level the Amazon's height could vary as much as forty feet, and between the mean and the lower level as much as thirty feet (008-010). The builders deliberately used this phenomenon to "launch the jangada, after having built it in comparative security" (008-010).

> "And along its whole extent, like the thousand tentacles of some gigantic polyp, two hundred tributaries, flowing from north or south, themselves fed by smaller affluents without number, by the side of which the large rivers of Europe are but petty streamlets." (005-009)

The river is not neutral — it is an active force shaping the characters' fortunes. The current furnishes the motive power for the [[Jangada]], while a crew of forty Indians and forty blacks work the long boathooks to keep the [[Giant Raft]] in midstream (008-023). The gradient is so slight that "there is no other river in the world whose inclination is so slight" (010-008), and the average speed cannot exceed two leagues in twenty-four hours except during the flood season (010-009).

> "A river which, fed by its myriad tributaries, discharges into the Atlantic over two hundred and fifty millions of cubic meters of water every hour." (005-012)

## Analysis

The Amazon drains a basin of approximately 7 million square kilometers. Verne's 1881 account accurately captures its key hydrological features: the enormous seasonal variation in water level (which the [[Jangada]]'s builders exploit), the network of tributaries that feed it, and the [[Pororoca]] — the [[Tidal Bore]] phenomenon where Atlantic tides force a wall of water upstream. The journey from [[Iquitos]] to Belém covers roughly 3,700 km (about 800 leagues), a distance that Verne's characters traverse over several months (005-003).

Verne devotes an entire chapter to the hydrological and geographical description of the Amazon (005-001 through 005-048), a characteristic didactic passage that positions the river as both scientific subject and narrative engine. The river's three names — Maranon in Peru, Solimoes from the Brazilian frontier to [[Manaus]], and Amazonas from Manaus to the sea — reflect its passage through different political and cultural territories (005-024).

> "The largest river in the whole world!" said [[Benito]] to [[Manoel Valdez]], on the morrow. They were sitting on the bank which formed the southern boundary of the [[Fazenda]], and looking at the liquid molecules passing slowly by, which, coming from the enormous range of the Andes, were on their road to lose themselves in the Atlantic Ocean eight hundred leagues away. (005-002—005-003)

The river's hydrological system is remarkable. Its basin is "constantly swept by westerly winds" (005-028), and unlike the Nile or Mississippi, the Amazon "crosses a most healthy part of South America" (005-028). Professor Agassiz is quoted by Verne as noting that "a soft and gentle breeze is constantly observable, and produces an evaporation, thanks to which the temperature is kept down, and the sun does not give out heat unchecked. The constancy of this refreshing breeze renders the climate of the river Amazon agreeable, and even delightful" (005-029).

> "But it is not so with the [[Pororoca]], that species of eddy which for three days in the height of the syzygies raises the waters of the Amazon, and turns them back at the rate of seventeen kilometers per hour." (012-023)

The [[Pororoca]]'s effects are said to be felt all the way to the Brazilian frontier, more than six hundred leagues from the Atlantic (012-023). Verne describes it as "a tide-race, or pororoca, to which the ebbs, the bores, and the eddies of other rivers are but tiny ripples fanned up by the breeze" (005-015).

> "And what a mouth! An arm of the sea in which one island, Marajo, has a circumference of more than five hundred leagues!" (005-014)

## Historical Context

By 1881, the Amazon had been extensively explored but remained a frontier of Western knowledge. Verne drew on contemporary accounts from naturalists like Henry Walter Bates and Alfred Russel Wallace, who had documented the region's extraordinary biodiversity and the river's hydrological features. The novel's detailed geographical descriptions reflect this scientific literature, making *The [[Giant Raft]]* one of Verne's most geographically grounded works.

Verne traces the river's exploration history in chapter 5: [[Orellana]] descended the [[Rio Negro]] in 1540 (005-035), Pedro Texeira ascended the Amazon in 1636-1637 (005-036), and La Condamine made the first scientific survey in 1743, "just in time to observe an emersion of the first satellite of Jupiter — which allowed this 'Humboldt of the eighteenth century' to accurately determine the latitude and longitude of the spot" (005-037). Humboldt and Bonpland completed La Condamine's work fifty-five years later (005-038).

By Verne's time, the river was being commercialized. In 1857, "the course of the Amazon was declared to be free and open to all flags" (005-041), and by 1881 lines of steamboats connected directly with Liverpool, plying from the mouth up to [[Manaus]], with others ascending to [[Iquitos]] (005-042). Verne notes both the promise and the cost: "No progress can be accomplished without detriment to the indigenous races" (005-044). On the Upper Amazon, many tribes had already disappeared at the time of the novel's writing (005-045).
