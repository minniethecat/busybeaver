# Óbidos

## Overview

Óbidos is a Brazilian settlement on the Lower [[Amazon River]], situated at the narrowest point of the great waterway approximately one hundred miles from the Atlantic Ocean [040-012]. It marks a significant geographical transition on the [[Amazon]]: below Óbidos, ocean tides become perceptible, transforming the character of the river from a purely fluvial system to one increasingly influenced by the sea [040-020]. The town is among the final settlements passed by the [[Jangada]] before it reaches the Atlantic.

## Geography

Óbidos occupies a strategic position at the narrowest constriction of the [[Amazon]]'s main channel. The river here is confined between higher banks than in the broad middle Amazon, accelerating the current and concentrating the flow. Settlers occupy both banks in the stretch from Óbidos up to the town of [[Monte Alegre]] [040-011], indicating a region of relatively dense habitation compared to the sparsely populated upper river. The black waters that pass the town's houses signal the influence of dark-water tributaries entering the main stream in this region [040-012].

> "The tide, which since leaving Obidos had begun to be felt, was now perceptible, and the current of the [[Amazon]] was beginning to be reversed." [040-020]

## In the Novel

The [[Jangada]] passes Óbidos in the final stages of its voyage, after the resolution of [[Joam Dacosta]]'s legal ordeal. At this point in the narrative, the raft is approaching the Atlantic, and the travelers can feel the growing influence of ocean tides on the river [040-020]. The passage of Óbidos marks the transition from the fluvial [[Amazon]] to the estuarine Amazon, where the river's character fundamentally changes. The town appears briefly in the text, a waypoint in the accelerating final movement toward the sea.

## Journey Role

Óbidos represents the gateway to the tidal [[Amazon]] on the [[Jangada]]'s itinerary. Above Óbidos, the river behaves as a purely freshwater system driven by gravity; below it, the twice-daily pulse of the Atlantic tide pushes upstream, reversing the current and creating the complex hydrodynamic environment of the lower river. In the novel's geography, Óbidos is the last significant waypoint before the ocean, marking the approaching end of the three-thousand-mile voyage from [[Iquitos]].
