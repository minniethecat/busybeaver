# Tribe

## Definition

The indigenous tribes of the [[Amazon Basin]] represent the river's original human inhabitants — peoples whose presence predates European contact by millennia and whose cultures, languages, and ways of life were fundamentally reshaped by colonization. By the mid-19th century, the time of *The [[Giant Raft]]*, many [[Amazon]] tribes had already experienced profound demographic decline, displacement, and cultural transformation. Verne's novel registers this reality through a series of observations about the tribes encountered — or conspicuously absent — along the [[Jangada]]'s route.

## In the Story

### The Vanished Tribes

The novel's opening chapters establish a theme of indigenous absence. The narrator notes that "on the [[Upper Amazon]] many Indian tribes have already disappeared" [005-045], their names surviving only as geographical markers: the Solimões River takes "its name from the Indian tribe Solimão, of which survivors are now difficult to discover" [005-024]. The [[Amazon]] itself bears a name that gestures toward indigenous legend — the Amazons of the [[Nhamundá]] — even as the people who originated that legend have vanished into myth.

### The Nomadic Tribes

Manoel offers a summary assessment of the indigenous presence on the river: "Its Indians, its nomadic tribes — and even its wild beasts — are no longer to be met with here" [008-049], at least in the more settled reaches. This characterization — pairing indigenous people with wildlife — reflects the 19th-century European perspective that Verne largely reproduces, framing tribes as part of the [[Amazon]]'s natural landscape rather than as sovereign peoples.

Yet the tribes have not entirely disappeared. The narrator observes that "the river belong to peaceable tribes, and the fiercest of them have retired farther into the interior" [009-020]. This is both an observation and a rationalization: the [[Amazon]] is safe for European travelers because its indigenous inhabitants have been pushed away from the main waterway.

### Specific Tribes

The novel names several specific indigenous groups:

- **Mayoruna and Orejone tribes**: "Nothing can be more picturesque than... the Mayoruna and Orejone tribes" [010-041]. The Orejones (from Spanish *orejones*, "big ears") were known for the practice of stretching their earlobes with large wooden plugs. These tribes inhabit the upper reaches of the [[Amazon]], near the Peruvian frontier.

- **Ticuna**: "The whole tribe of the Ticunas would come to drink" [012-037] from a fermented beverage prepared during their festivals. The Ticuna (also spelled Tikuna) remain one of the largest indigenous groups in the Brazilian [[Amazon]] today.

- **Yurumas**: The Xingu River is described as "frequented by Yurumas Indians, whose principal industry consists in manufacturing weapons" [040-020], suggesting the persistence of indigenous craft traditions into the modern period.

### The Tribe at the Fazenda

The most extended indigenous presence in the novel is the community of workers at [[The Garral Fazenda]]. "Quite a tribe from one hundred and fifty to two hundred workers" [009-045] labors on the estate — indigenous and mixed-race people employed in the [[rubber]] and agricultural operations. This economically integrated community represents one model of indigenous adaptation to the colonial economy, though at the cost of cultural autonomy.

### Indigenous Women and Families

During the [[manatee]] hunt, the party encounters indigenous women harvesting turtle eggs: "Representatives of different tribes of Indians are found in the neighborhood" [015-028]. The narrator notes that these women "do not form separate tribes; they are simply the wives who accompany their husbands" [015-048], a reflection on the gendered organization of indigenous labor and family life.

## Significance

Verne's treatment of [[Amazon]] tribes is simultaneously ethnographic and elegiac. He catalogs tribal names and customs with the curiosity of a 19th-century naturalist, while also recording — sometimes unwittingly — the signs of demographic catastrophe. The tribes that appear in *The [[Giant Raft]]* are already remnants: the Solimão, barely remembered; the Mayoruna and Orejone, withdrawn from the river; the Ticuna, consigned to a brief ethnographic mention.

This elegiac quality is not unique to Verne's treatment of indigenous peoples; it pervades the novel's larger attitude toward the [[Amazon]]. The river is a place where things disappear — the chapels, the fazendas, even the [[Giant Raft]] itself will eventually be dismantled or washed away. The tribes are part of this larger pattern of transience, their presence on the river already verging on memory.

Yet Verne also documents indigenous resilience. The workers at the [[Fazenda]], the turtle-egg harvesters on the beach, the weapon-makers of the Xingu — these are not vanished peoples but living communities, adapting to the new realities of the 19th-century [[Amazon]]. The novel captures both the disappearance and the persistence, without fully resolving the tension between them.
