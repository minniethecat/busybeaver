# Judge Ribeiro

## Introduction

Judge Ribeiro was the first magistrate of the province of [[Tijuco]] and the sole judicial official who believed in the innocence of [[Joam Dacosta]], the young clerk wrongly condemned for the [[Diamond]] robbery of 1826. His unexpected death by apoplexy on the eve of Joam's planned appeal constitutes one of the novel's most tragic turns — eliminating the one man whose authority and conviction might have prevented twenty-three years of injustice [020-090][020-114].

> "Judge Ribeiro alone knew that I was innocent, my dear ones. The death of the judge may be fatal to me, but that is no reason for me to despair." [020-116]

## Biography

Before he became judge at [[Manaus]] and chief magistrate in the province, Ribeiro had known the young clerk [[Joam Dacosta]] at the time he was being prosecuted for the murder in the [[Diamond]] arrayal. He was then an advocate at [[Vila Rica]], and he it was who defended the prisoner at the trial. He took the cause to heart and made it his own, and from an examination of the papers and detailed information he came to the conclusion that his client was wrongfully accused — that Joam Dacosta was innocent [023-003].

> "Notwithstanding his talent and zeal, Ribeiro was unable to persuade the jury to take the same view of the matter. How could he remove so strong a presumption? If it was not [[Joam Dacosta]], who had every facility for informing the scoundrels of the convoy's departure, who was it?" [023-004]

Ribeiro defended him with great warmth and with all his powers, but he could not succeed in saving him. The verdict of the jury was affirmative on all the questions. [[Joam Dacosta]], convicted of aggravated and premeditated murder, heard himself condemned to death [023-005].

Twenty years later, Ribeiro the advocate became the chief justice of [[Manaus]]. In the depths of his retreat the fazender of [[Iquitos]] heard of the change and saw a favorable opportunity for bringing forward the revision of the former proceedings against him [023-007]. Unlike every other official involved in the case, Judge Ribeiro had examined the evidence and reached the independent conclusion that [[Joam Dacosta]] was innocent of the crime for which he had been condemned to death [020-116].

## In the Novel

[[Joam Garral]]'s plan upon arriving at [[Manaus]] was to seek the protection of Judge Ribeiro, the first magistrate of the province, by means of correspondence he had maintained with the judge over the years [020-090][020-102]. The judge was to be the instrument of Joam's vindication — the one authority whose knowledge of the case could reopen the investigation and clear his name.

When [[Torres]] learned of this plan, he was "evidently disconcerted" — for if Judge Ribeiro were to champion Joam's cause, Torres's scheme of blackmail would collapse [020-091]. Joam's calm response — "At Judge Ribeiro's" — conveyed his absolute confidence in the magistrate's integrity [020-092].

That confidence was shattered when the family arrived at [[Manaus]] to learn devastating news: "Judge Ribeiro was struck with apoplexy yesterday evening, and died during the night at two o'clock, without having recovered his consciousness" [020-114]. His death had occurred just hours before Joam could present himself. The timing, as the narrator notes, was catastrophic — the substitute magistrate who took Ribeiro's place was none other than [[Judge Jarriquez]], who lacked Ribeiro's knowledge of the case and his predisposition toward the accused [020-114].

In the aftermath, Joam revealed to his family what he had kept secret: "Judge Ribeiro alone knew that I was innocent. I was to place myself in his hands. His death has struck me down" [020-116]. The loss of Ribeiro transformed a journey of hope into one of desperation, forcing Joam to place his fate in the hands of a skeptical and uninformed judiciary.

> "Judge Ribeiro was at first troubled about this communication. How could it be otherwise? [[Joam Dacosta]] had written to him, telling him his secret and asking for his help." [023-022]

## Relationships

- **[[joam-garral]]** (Joam Dacosta) — Ribeiro defended Dacosta at his original trial in [[Vila Rica]] and was the only magistrate who believed in his innocence
- **[[judge-jarriquez]]** — Ribeiro's substitute after his death; the contrast between the two judges defines the novel's legal drama
- **[[torres]]** — The blackmailer whose plans were threatened by Ribeiro's potential intervention

## Significance

Judge Ribeiro's role, though brief, is pivotal. He represents the possibility of justice within a flawed system — the one honest magistrate whose knowledge and conviction might have righted a wrongful conviction. His sudden death functions as the novel's peripeteia, transforming what had been a hopeful journey into a desperate race against the machinery of imperial Brazilian justice.

> "Had it not been for Ribeiro's nomination to the chief justiceship in the province of Amazonas, he might perhaps have hesitated, for he had no new material proof of his innocence to bring forward." [023-007]

In the structure of Verne's plot, Ribeiro is the absent savior — the man who could have solved everything but who dies at the critical moment, forcing the protagonists to find another path to exoneration through the deciphering of the [[Cryptogram]].
