# Yaquita

## Introduction

Yaquita Garral is the wife of Joam Garral and the matriarch of the family at the center of *The Giant Raft*. Daughter of the Portuguese farmer Magalhães, she marries Joam at twenty-two, builds a family with him at the fazenda near Iquitos, and accompanies him on the 800-league journey down the Amazon aboard the jangada. Her role transforms from supportive farm wife to the emotional anchor of a family facing ruin — and, ultimately, to the wife of an exoneree whose identity as Joam Dacosta she embraces without hesitation.

> "Yaquita was now forty-four. In that tropical country where women are already old at thirty she had learned the secret of resisting the climate's destructive influences, and her features, a little sharpened but still beautiful, retained the haughty outline of the Portuguese type, in which nobility of face unites so naturally with dignity of mind." [003-039]

## Biography

Yaquita was born to Magalhães, a Portuguese immigrant who had established himself as a farmer in the Peruvian Amazon. After her mother's death, she lived alone with her father until Joam Dacosta — then a young fugitive — arrived at the farm, wounded and despairing [003-011] [003-021]. She was twenty-two when her dying father placed her hand in Joam's, binding them in marriage [003-023] [003-027].

At the fazenda, Yaquita bore Joam a son, Benito, and a daughter, Minha [003-030]. She knew her husband's true identity as Joam Dacosta and kept his secret for over two decades, building a prosperous life while carrying the unspoken weight of his past [004-018] [023-008].

> "A year after her marriage Yaquita presented her husband with a son, and, two years after, a daughter. Benito and Minha, the grandchildren of the old Portuguese, became worthy of their grandfather, children worthy of Joam and Yaquita." [003-030]

When Joam finally agreed to journey to Belém, Yaquita felt both joy and apprehension — joy at returning to Brazil and seeing her daughter married, apprehension about what awaited them at the journey's end [004-042] [004-045].

> "'Ah! Joam! my Joam!' cried Yaquita, in her joy. 'Thank you for me! Thank you for them!' And tears of affection came to her eyes as her husband clasped her to his heart." [004-051] [004-052]

## Character Analysis

### Quiet Strength and Loyalty

Yaquita's defining trait is her unwavering loyalty. She had "divined the secret" of Joam's past long before he confessed it, yet her affection never wavered [023-016]. When Joam is arrested at Manaos, the narrative notes that "with Yaquita Dacosta it was as with Yaquita Garral" [022-014] — her identity shifts seamlessly because her devotion is to the man, not the name.

> "Yaquita had taken her husband's hand, and with that gentle voice which had been to him all the music of his life: 'Joam,' she said, 'I am going to talk to you about something which we ardently wish, and which will make you as happy as we are.'" [004-022]

### Maternal Authority

As the family matriarch, Yaquita presides over the domestic life of the jangada. She and Cybele manage the household preparations for the voyage [006-035], she receives visitors [012-044], and she provides the emotional center around which the family clusters in moments of crisis [026-114]. Her authority is quiet but absolute — when the family gathers, it is Yaquita who sets the tone.

> "Heaven knows what care Yaquita and her daughter took to make him comfortable! Assuredly the good old priest had never been so lodged in his modest parsonage!" [009-030]

### Emotional Resilience Under Crisis

The arrest of Joam Dacosta at the end of Part I shatters the family, and Yaquita is described as "almost helpless" but rising nevertheless [022-014]. She does not retreat into grief; instead she becomes a steadying presence for Benito, who is "a prey to despair which made her tremble for his reason" [035-016]. Her visits to Joam in prison are daily rituals that sustain both the prisoner and the family [037-002].

> "Between her husband, with whom she passed all the time that was allowed her, and her son, a prey to despair which made her tremble for his reason, the brave Yaquita lost none of her moral energy. In her they found the valiant daughter of Magalhães, the worthy wife of the fazender of Iquitos." [035-016]

## Relationships

- [[joam-garral]] (Joam Dacosta) — Her husband. Yaquita married him knowing his secret, kept it for decades, and stood by him through his arrest and trial. She is his emotional anchor throughout the crisis. "Yaquita! my wife! my children!" he cries when reunited with them in prison [026-119].

- [[benito]] — Her son. She worries deeply about his impulsive nature during the crisis, fearing for his reason as he blames himself for his father's arrest [027-002] [035-016].

- [[minha]] — Her daughter. Yaquita's concern for Minha's marriage to Manoel is one of the catalysts for the journey down the Amazon [004-009] [004-010].

- [[manoel]] — Her future son-in-law. When Manoel hesitates to marry Minha after Joam's arrest, fearing he brings disgrace upon the family, Yaquita confronts him directly, reminding him that "Joam Dacosta had not been arrested" when he made his promise [022-063] [022-067] [022-069].

> "Yaquita, looking him straight in the face, continued: 'Yesterday, after the interview you had with Joam Dacosta, my husband, you came to me and called me—mother! You took Minha's hand, and called her—your wife! You then knew everything, and the past life of Joam Dacosta had been disclosed to you.'" [022-067]

- [[cybele]] — The old negress, freed slave of Magalhães, who serves Yaquita as a domestic companion. Cybele has been with the family since before Yaquita's marriage and cannot understand why they would leave the fazenda [003-044] [006-035].

## In the Novel

### Part I: The Journey

Yaquita is introduced in Chapter 3 as the daughter of Magalhães, living a quiet life at the fazenda [003-011]. When Joam arrives wounded, she recognizes his "sterling qualities" even as the young man remains "so gentle to others, so stern to himself" [003-021].

The marriage question that opens Chapter 4 places Yaquita at the center of the family's decision-making. She has noticed the love between Minha and Manoel and gently raises the issue with her husband, knowing it will lead inevitably to the larger question of returning to Brazil [004-009] [004-010]. Her careful, loving persuasion — "that gentle voice which had been to him all the music of his life" — moves Joam to consent [004-022].

During the voyage, Yaquita enjoys the novelty of Brazilian towns [012-024], receives visitors graciously [012-044], and presides over the domestic rhythm of the jangada. She is described as "content" with the floating home her husband has built [008-041].

> "It was as charming as could be; and as Yaquita, her daughter, and Lina were content, we need say no more about it." [008-041]

### Part II: The Crisis

The turning point comes at the end of Chapter 20, when Joam announces that "peculiar circumstances" force him to alter the wedding plans [020-074]. Yaquita's instinctive response is protective — she "opened her arms to protect, to defend" her daughter [020-077].

When Joam is arrested, Yaquita is struck with "stupor" [020-110]. But she recovers quickly: by Chapter 22 she confronts Manoel about his wavering commitment to Minha, looking him "straight in the face" and demanding that he honor his promise [022-067]. This scene reveals Yaquita's steel — she will not allow the family to fragment under pressure.

> "At these words Yaquita and Minha, struck with stupor, stopped without any power to move." [020-110]

> "Yaquita slowly advanced toward Manoel. 'Manoel,' she said, 'listen to what I have to say, for my conscience commands me to speak as I am about to do.'" [022-064]

During the imprisonment, Yaquita visits Joam daily [037-002]. Her presence sustains him: "He again lived the happy days he had spent with Yaquita" in his prison cell [037-011]. When Judge Jarriquez finally deciphers the cryptogram and announces the name of the real culprit, Yaquita is beside Joam, clasped to his heart [039-005].

### Part III: Resolution

At the novel's close, Yaquita stands beside Joam at the double wedding of Minha and Manoel — a ceremony that echoes their own marriage years before, as "the same bands which had formerly blessed Joam and Yaquita were again stretched forth" [040-041]. The symmetry affirms that the family, tested and nearly destroyed, has been restored.

> "The same bands which had formerly blessed Joam and Yaquita were again stretched forth to give the nuptial benediction to their child." [040-041]

## Significance

Yaquita embodies the theme of steadfast loyalty in *The Giant Raft*. Her character arc is not one of transformation but of constancy — she is the same devoted wife and mother at the end that she was at the beginning, but the circumstances that test her reveal the depth of that devotion.

Her role also underscores the domestic stakes of the novel's central conflict. Joam's conviction would mean not merely one man's death but the destruction of an entire family — a point made visible through Yaquita's suffering and resilience. In the novel's moral economy, Joam's exoneration is a victory for the family unit that Yaquita holds together.
