# Iquitos

## Overview

Iquitos is a Peruvian village situated on the left bank of the [[Upper Amazon]] [003-002], and the location of the Garral family's [[Fazenda]] — the prosperous plantation that serves as the starting point for the entire novel. It is from Iquitos that [[Joam Garral]] launches the giant [[Jangada]] on its 800-league voyage down the [[Amazon]] to Belém [006-011]. The village and its surrounding estate represent the idyllic frontier life that Joam built during his twenty-three years in hiding, a world of order and prosperity carved from the Amazonian wilderness.

> "The village of Iquitos is situated on the left bank of the [[Amazon]], about sixty miles from the point where that river, known in its upper course as the [[Marañón]], receives the waters of the Ucayali." [003-002]

## Geography

The village of Iquitos lies on the left bank of the [[Amazon]], in Peruvian territory, at the point where the river is still known as the [[Marañón]] [003-002]. Like other hamlets of the [[Upper Amazon]], it was a modest collection of dwellings at the time of the novel [003-003]. The surrounding region consists of dense tropical forest, and the local indigenous population — the Indians of Iquitos — went about "in almost a state of nature" [003-005], reflecting the remoteness and frontier character of the settlement.

> "Iquitos, like every other collection of huts, hamlet, or village on the banks of the [[Upper Amazon]], has not improved very much since the first establishment of the Spaniards." [003-003]

The [[Fazenda]] itself occupied part of "those magnificent forests which are not far from the banks of the river" [006-012], with the great timber used for the [[Jangada]]'s construction cut from these very lands. Down the stream, the tributaries that feed the [[Amazon]] proper made Iquitos a natural embarkation point, and the river's course from here — past "the islands of Iquitos and [[Parianta]]" [007-006] — marked the beginning of the long descent toward the Atlantic.

> "The settlement of Iquitos included a part of those magnificent forests which are not far from the banks of the river." [006-012]

## In the Novel

Iquitos is the novel's point of departure, both geographically and narratively. [[Joam Garral]], known only as the respected fazendero of Iquitos [003-028], has transformed Magalhães's struggling establishment into "one of the richest of the province" [003-019]. His identity as [[Joam Dacosta]] — the fugitive from the [[Tijuco]] [[Diamond]] robbery — is unknown to everyone in the village except his wife [[Yaquita]].

The decision to leave Iquitos is the novel's inciting event. Joam had originally hoped to have [[Minha]]'s wedding celebrated at Iquitos, with "grand ceremonies and the attendance of the whole population of the river" [004-011], but his wife's invalid condition — "she cannot visit Iquitos" [004-012] — combined with Joam's secret desire to clear his name in Brazil, shifts the destination to Belém [004-046].

> "Joam had thought of the marriage to take place at Iquitos, with grand ceremonies and the attendance of the whole population of the river." [004-011]

The departure from Iquitos is both joyous and poignant. [[Minha]] is "quite unaffected at leaving Iquitos" [006-038], excited by the adventure ahead, while the old servant [[Cybele]] cannot understand why they would leave. [[Padre Passanha]], who had long served at the [[Mission]] of Iquitos [009-027], accompanies them on the voyage, carrying the "poverty-stricken [[Church]] of Iquitos" in his heart [009-033] as they set off. The [[Jangada]] itself is built at Iquitos from the timber of the [[Fazenda]]'s forests [006-011], and when the family finally boards, [[Yaquita]] remarks that the floating home is so comfortable "we might fancy that we had never left Iquitos" [008-027].

> "It seemed as if a part of the [[Fazenda]] of Iquitos had been cut away from the bank and turned into a floating island." [006-011]

## Historical Context

The historical Iquitos was transformed by the [[Amazon]] [[rubber]] boom of the late nineteenth century, growing from a small [[Mission]] settlement into a major commercial port. At the time of Verne's writing (1881), the rubber boom was in its early stages, and Iquitos was still a relatively modest settlement — consistent with Verne's depiction. Verne accurately portrays its remote, frontier character: a place of "wretched huts" where indigenous people lived at subsistence level, contrasting sharply with [[The Garral Fazenda]]'s cultivated prosperity.

Today, Iquitos is the largest city in the Peruvian [[Amazon]], a major port, and the most populous city in the world that cannot be reached by road — accessible only by river or air. Verne's choice of Iquitos as the novel's starting point reflects its status, even in 1881, as the effective head of navigation for large vessels on the Amazon.

## Journey Role

Iquitos is the Alpha point of the novel's [[Amazon]] itinerary — the beginning of an 800-league journey that ends at Belém (Pará). Every subsequent stop along the river — [[Pevas]], [[Tabatinga]], [[Ega]], [[Manaus]], [[Óbidos]], [[Monte Alegre]] — is measured in distance from this origin. The voyage from Iquitos to Belém traces the entire length of the navigable Brazilian Amazon, making Iquitos not just a setting but the launching point for the novel's geographical and narrative arc.

> "Down the stream the tributaries which form the [[Amazon]] collect the waters of an immense basin, and among them the Iça, Japurá, [[Rio Negro]], Madeira, Tapajós, Xingu, and Tocantins come in to swell the volume of the great river." [005-027]
