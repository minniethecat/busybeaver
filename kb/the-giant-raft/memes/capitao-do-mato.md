# Capitão do Mato

## Definition

The capitão do mato (Portuguese for "bush-captain") was a recognized profession in colonial and imperial Brazil — armed men employed to track and capture escaped slaves who had fled into the wilderness. In *The [[Giant Raft]]*, Jules Verne identifies the antagonist [[Torres]] as having been a capitão do mato, using this profession to signal his morally ambiguous character.

## In the Novel

[[Torres]] is identified in the novel's opening pages as "a fitting comrade for his fellow capitães do mato" (001-008). This identification is significant and immediately sets his character: [[The Bush-Captain]] profession placed Torres in a role that existed at the margins of Brazilian society — necessary to the plantation economy but morally tainted by its association with slavery.

[[Torres]] demonstrates the skills typical of a capitão do mato throughout the narrative: he is a man of the wilderness who "was not one of those people who go to sleep without making sure that no one is near them" (001-023), showing the vigilance and survival instincts that bush-captains developed. When he handles the [[Cryptogram]], "it seemed as though the hands of Torres felt the enormous sum" (001-018) — a gesture revealing the opportunism that the profession cultivated.

The reference to capitães do mato is brief but loaded. [[Torres]] "heard nothing of all these sounds" of the forest (001-013), suggesting an intimate familiarity with the wilderness — a hallmark of [[The Bush-Captain]]'s trade. His possession of the [[Cryptogram]] and his willingness to use it for leverage align with the morally flexible, survival-oriented nature that the profession demanded.

## Technical Detail

Capitães do mato operated primarily in the frontier regions of Brazil, where escaped slaves could form quilombos (maroon settlements) in the dense forest. They were typically armed with firearms and machetes, and their tracking skills were honed by years of navigating the forest. The profession existed from the early colonial period through the abolition of slavery in Brazil in 1888 — just seven years after *The [[Giant Raft]]* was published.

## Historical Context

When Verne wrote *The [[Giant Raft]]* in 1881, Brazil was still a slave-holding empire (abolition would come in 1888). The reference to a capitão do mato would have carried immediate connotations for French readers familiar with Brazilian society. Verne uses the term without extensive explanation, suggesting it was part of the contemporary understanding of Brazil as a frontier society where wilderness and civilization were in constant tension.
