# Ortega

## Introduction

Ortega is the critical figure whose identity unlocks the central mystery of the novel. A former member of the [[Diamond]] convoy guard who participated in [[The Tijuco Robbery]] of 1826, Ortega was the true author of the [[Cryptogram]] that [[Joam Dacosta]] carried for twenty-three years. His name, discovered by [[Fragoso]] and brought back to the [[Jangada]], provided [[Judge Jarriquez]] with the key needed to decipher the document and ultimately prove Joam Dacosta's innocence [038-023][038-051].

> "[[Joam Dacosta]] is innocent! It was not he who committed the crime of [[Tijuco]]; it was the comrade of [[Torres]], the author of that document! It was Ortega!" [038-051]

## Biography

What was known with certainty about Ortega was that he had been a member of the band that perpetrated the [[Tijuco]] [[Diamond]] robbery — the crime for which [[Joam Dacosta]] had been wrongly condemned to death. An intimate friendship existed between him and [[Torres]], they were always seen together, and Torres had watched at his bedside when he died [038-026]. [[Fragoso]]'s investigation brought back proof that Ortega was the true author of the crime [038-028].

During the attack of the scoundrels who awaited the convoy just beyond [[Vila Rica]], Ortega pretended to defend himself with the soldiers of the escort, and then, falling among the dead, he was carried away by his accomplices. Hence it was that the solitary soldier who survived the massacre reported that Ortega had perished in the struggle [039-035]. However, he had in fact survived. Unable to re-enter [[Tijuco]], he fled to the provinces in the north of Brazil, to those districts of the [[Upper Amazon]] where the [[Capitães do Mato]] are to be found, and for many years he followed the trade of a chaser of men [039-037].

## In the Novel

Ortega does not appear directly in the narrative — he is a posthumous figure whose story emerges through the investigations of [[Fragoso]] and the testimony of [[Torres]]. The critical revelation comes in chapter 38, when Fragoso announces the name "Ortega" — the missing key that [[Judge Jarriquez]] had been unable to discover [038-023][038-051].

The judge, presented with this name, immediately recognized its significance. He placed the name of Ortega over the six last letters of the [[Cryptogram]]'s coded paragraph and saw that the numerical values of the letters in "Ortega" corresponded exactly to the cipher — the discovery that enabled him to reconstitute the entire message [038-065][038-066].

> "An exclamation immediately escaped him. He saw, at first glance, that the six last letters were inferior in alphabetical order to those which composed Ortega's name, and that consequently they might yield the number." [038-066]

The name of Ortega had enabled the judge to discover in a single stroke what had eluded all for so long [038-080].

> "This number, 432513, was the number sought for so long! The name of Ortega had enabled him to discover it! At length he held the key of the document, which would incontestably prove the innocence of [[Joam Dacosta]]." [038-080]

[[Torres]], the antagonist who had been blackmailing [[Joam Dacosta]] with the [[Cryptogram]], confirmed Ortega's role in a confession. Torres had been Ortega's companion during his fugitive years in the northern provinces [039-038]. The two had become "most intimate," and it was from Ortega that Torres had obtained the coded document [039-042]. Ortega, feeling himself doomed and mortally wounded in a scuffle with some negroes on the Madeira, had entrusted the document to Torres before his death, making him swear to convey it to Joam Dacosta, whose name and address he gave him, and with his last breath he whispered the number 432513, without which the document would remain undecipherable [039-040].

> "Ortega dead, we know how the unworthy [[Torres]] acquitted himself of his [[Mission]], how he resolved to turn to his own profit the secret of which he was the possessor, and how he tried to make it the subject of an odious bargain." [039-041]

The irony of the situation is profound. [[Torres]], who had used the document to blackmail an innocent man, did not even know the value of Ortega's name — the very key to the cipher [039-048]. The [[Cryptogram]] could only be deciphered with the name of its author, and it was [[Fragoso]]'s chance discovery of Ortega's name that ultimately brought about [[Joam Dacosta]]'s exoneration [039-055].

## Relationships

- **[[torres]]** — Ortega's companion during his fugitive years; Torres inherited the [[Cryptogram]] from Ortega
- **[[joam-garral]]** (Joam Dacosta) — The innocent man condemned for Ortega's crime
- **[[fragoso]]** — The barber whose investigation uncovered Ortega's name, enabling the [[Cryptogram]]'s decipherment
- **[[judge-jarriquez]]** — The magistrate who used Ortega's name to crack the [[Cipher]]

## Significance

Ortega is the invisible fulcrum upon which the entire plot turns. Though dead before the narrative begins, his actions — [[The Tijuco Robbery]], the authorship of the [[Cryptogram]], and his deathbed transfer of the document to [[Torres]] — set in motion the chain of events that drive the novel.

> "The name of Ortega, brought back by [[Fragoso]], and which was the signature of the document, had afforded the means of unraveling the [[Cryptogram]], thanks to the sagacity of [[Judge Jarriquez]]. Yes, the material proof sought after for so long was the incontestable witness of the innocence of [[Joam Dacosta]], returned to life, restored to honor." [039-042]

Ortega represents the theme of hidden truth in "The [[Giant Raft]]": justice is obstructed not by malice alone but by the absence of a single piece of information. His name, the last key in the cryptographic puzzle, functions as a symbol of how the most crucial truths can remain concealed for decades, resting on a single word unknown to those who most need it.

> "If, in spite of all the shrewdness of his analytical talents, he had not been able to read the document, which was absolutely undecipherable to anyone who had not got the key, had he not at any rate discovered the system on which the [[Cryptogram]] was composed? Without him what could have been done with only the name of Ortega?" [039-055]
