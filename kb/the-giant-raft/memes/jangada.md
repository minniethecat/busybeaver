# Jangada

## Definition

A jangada is a massive river raft constructed from enormous tree trunks, traditionally used for transportation along the [[Amazon River]] and its tributaries. In Jules Verne's *The Giant Raft*, the jangada serves as the primary vessel carrying [[Joam Garral]]'s family and their entire household on an 800-league journey from [[Iquitos]] to Belém.

## In the Novel

The jangada forms the literal and narrative centerpiece of the story. It was conceived as

> "safer than any other vessel of the country, larger than a hundred egariteas, or vigilingas, coupled together" (006-017)

—a floating village large enough to transport the entire Garral household, including livestock, stores, and a crew of forty Indians and forty blacks (008-022). [[Joam Garral]] himself ordered its construction, declaring

> "the jangada must be built and ready to launch" (006-024)

in a single month.

The construction site was chosen at the junction of the Nanay River with the [[Amazon]], where the river current would assist in launching the completed vessel (008-004, 008-005). The jangada's immense scale was such that Verne describes it as having "a small village" appearance, with the master's house at the rear, workers' huts, and a huge pile of firewood for cooking (008-024, 009-004, 009-013).

At its core, the jangada's floor was formed of trunks

> "rising about thirty inches above the load waterline" (008-013),

designed to cope with the seasonal floods that would naturally lift and launch the raft—a phenomenon the builders deliberately exploited (008-010).

## Analysis

The jangada was constructed from the trunks of enormous [[Amazon]] trees, lashed together to form a floating platform. Verne provides specific structural detail: the floor rose about thirty inches above the water line (008-013), and the plan scaled to extraordinary dimensions—

> "the enormous mass, for such were the jangadas, or river rafts, which attained the dimensions of a small island" (006-016).

The raft measured a thousand feet long and sixty broad, with a surface of sixty thousand square feet (008-013). A large crew worked long boathooks to keep the giant raft in midstream along the winding river (008-023).

The vessel included a dedicated wine cellar, which

> "did honor to [[Benito]], who had been appointed its commander-in-chief" (009-009),

stocked with several hundred bottles of sherry, port, and letubal, alongside demijohns of excellent tafia. This attention to domestic detail underscores the jangada's function not merely as a vehicle but as a complete mobile household for the months-long journey.

The jangada's construction exploited the [[Amazon]]'s [[Seasonal Flood]] cycle:

> "This [[Joam Garral]] knew by experience, and he intended to profit by the phenomenon to launch the jangada, after having built it in comfort on the river bank" (008-010).

Between the mean and higher level, the [[Amazon]] could vary as much as forty feet, and between the mean and lower level, as much as thirty feet — a difference of seventy feet that the fazender used to his advantage (008-010).

## Historical Context

River rafts have been used on the [[Amazon]] for centuries as a means of transporting goods and people across vast distances. Verne's 1881 depiction draws on contemporary accounts of Amazon navigation while amplifying the scale for dramatic effect. The idea of building and launching a vessel timed to the Amazon's [[Seasonal Flood]] cycle reflects actual practices known to 19th-century explorers and settlers.
