# Palm

**Palm trees** are a defining feature of the Amazonian landscape in _The Giant Raft_, appearing throughout the journey from [[Iquitos]] to the Atlantic. Jules Verne describes multiple varieties — wax-palms, wine palms (_baccabas_), and _boiassu_ palms — each with distinct characteristics and uses for the inhabitants of the [[Amazon Basin]].

## Varieties of Amazon Palms

The [[Amazon]] contains an extraordinary diversity of palm species. Along the riverbanks, "gigantic palm-trees with their drooping leaves" cast deep shadows over the water [010-031]. The wax-palm (_Ceroxylon_) stands "one hundred and twenty feet high," its trunk "handsomely ringed with brown" [006-030]. Higher up the tributaries, the wine palms (_baccabas_) provide a fermented beverage, their branches described as "hanging gracefully down to the very ground" [007-083].

The **boiassu palm** is particularly valued: its large leaves are placed "round a central space" to create temporary shelters [012-020], and its "supple fibers are used" for weaving and construction [012-021].

## Uses and Products

Palm trees provide an extraordinary range of products to the Amazonian people:

- **Palm-leaf hats**: Lightweight hats woven from palm leaves, worn by travelers for sun protection [002-059]
- **Palm wine**: A fermented drink "got from the juice of the palm, and the aromatic flavor of the pineapple" [009-008]
- **Palm wood**: Used for crafting items such as "a cup and ball in palm wood" for games [007-129]
- **Palm fruit**: The fruit of certain palms provides food and drink — "a sort of thick liquid, made of the fruit of the palm-tree, and drunk from a cuia or gourd" [012-053]
- **Palm fiber**: The "ramicles of a certain palm-tree growing very abundantly" produce strong fiber for ropes and cordage [008-012]
- **Palm nuts**: The nuts produced by palm-trees give the river its name in parts of its course [015-034]

## Cultural Significance

Indigenous people of the [[Amazon]] incorporate palm materials into their daily lives and adornments. At one settlement, visitors observed inhabitants with "their noses pierced with a rod of palm" as a cultural practice [010-046]. Palm-leaf fans ("made of the spines of palm-trees, six inches long") keep insects at bay [011-007].

## In the Landscape

Palms dominate the Amazonian skyline alongside banana trees and other tropical vegetation. The [[Fazenda]] at [[Iquitos]] is framed by "the summits of the graceful palms and banana-trees" [003-004], while river villages are nestled "amid the palms" with their ubas drawn up on the shore [010-041].
