# Guariba

The **guariba** is a species of howling monkey native to the forests of the [[Upper Amazon]]. In Jules Verne's *The [[Giant Raft]]*, a guariba plays a memorable role in Chapter II, where the antagonist [[Torres]] awakens to find one stealing his precious tin case — the case containing the coded document that holds the secret of [[Joam Dacosta]]'s identity.

## Description

Of all the prehensile-tailed monkeys that inhabit the [[Upper Amazon]] forests — "graceful sahuis, horned sapajous, gray-coated monos, sagouins which seem to wear a mask on their grimacing faces" — the guariba is "without doubt the most eccentric" (002-004). Unlike the mucura, which is "as ferocious as he is foul," the guariba is of "sociable disposition, and not very savage" (002-004). It delights in company and generally travels in groups.

Also known in Brazil as the **barbado**, the guariba is of large size. The "suppleness and stoutness of his limbs proclaim him a formidable creature" (002-005). Nature has endowed the guariba and its relatives not merely with four hands but with a fifth — "the extremity of their caudal appendage possesses a perfect power of prehension" (002-006), making the prehensile tail a fully functional grasping limb.

## In the Story

### The Theft of the Tin Case

The guariba encounter occurs in Chapter II, set in the Peruvian forest near the Brazilian frontier. [[Torres]], the captain of the woods and antagonist of the novel, has fallen asleep at the foot of a tree, resting his head upon the tin case that contains the [[Cryptogram]] document — the only proof of [[Joam Dacosta]]'s innocence of the [[Tijuco]] [[Diamond]] robbery.

The guariba noiselessly approaches the sleeping [[Torres]], "brandishing a sturdy cudgel, which, wielded by his muscular arm, would have proved a formidable weapon" (002-007). The monkey's attitude is threatening: it appears ready to crush the motionless man with a single blow, and "assuredly at that moment the life of Torres hung by a thread" (002-013).

But instead of attacking, the guariba becomes fascinated by the tin case. It seizes the container, and the rattling sound of the metal within — like "a rattle in the hands of a child" — enchants the monkey (002-016). The guariba carries the case to its mouth, testing it with its teeth, perhaps believing it to be "some fruit of a new kind" (002-016).

[[Torres]] awakens instantly. "A guariba!" he cries, seizing his manchetta (002-019). The monkey, "not so brave before a waking man as a sleeping one," retreats — but it takes the tin case with it (002-021).

### The Pursuit

What follows is a lengthy and comical chase through the forest. [[Torres]] pursues the guariba desperately, for the case contains his entire scheme of blackmail. The guariba, with its prehensile tail and arboreal agility, easily evades him — "on the ground he could get away too fast, in the branches he could get away too far". The monkey seems to treat the pursuit as a game, patiently waiting whenever Torres hides, only to resume its flight the moment he reappears.

"Confound the guariba!" [[Torres]] shouts in frustration. "There will be no end to this, and he will lead me back to the Brazilian frontier" (002-030).

The chase ends only when [[Torres]], in desperation, shoots the guariba with a long-range pistol. The monkey falls, mortally wounded — but the shot has also struck a man who happened to be passing through the forest: [[Joam Garral]]'s employee [[Fragoso]], the wandering barber. This accident brings Fragoso into the story and sets in motion the events that will ultimately expose Torres's scheme.

## Significance

The guariba episode serves several narrative functions. It establishes [[Torres]]'s character — his desperation, his greed, and his violent nature. It creates a moment of dark comedy in an otherwise tense plot. And most importantly, it brings [[Fragoso]] into contact with Torres, creating the connection that will later prove crucial when Fragoso recognizes Torres in [[Manaus]] and testifies about his past.

The episode also showcases Verne's characteristic blend of natural history and adventure fiction. The detailed description of the guariba — its taxonomy, its behavior, its physical characteristics — is interwoven with the suspense of the chase and the farce of a monkey outwitting a human antagonist.
