# Death of Torres

## Introduction

The death of [[Torres]] — the blackmailer who had threatened to expose [[Joam Dacosta]]'s true identity — was a pivotal event in "The [[Giant Raft]]." Torres's body was discovered in the waters of the [[Amazon]], killed under mysterious circumstances. His death not only removed the immediate threat of blackmail but also led to the recovery of the [[Cryptogram]], the document that would ultimately prove Joam's innocence [027-032][028-005][029-003].

## Background

[[Torres]] had boarded the [[Jangada]] as a passenger, insinuating himself into the Garral family's company while secretly harboring the [[Cryptogram]] that contained the proof of [[Joam Dacosta]]'s innocence — and his own leverage for blackmail. He demanded Joam's daughter [[Minha]]'s hand in marriage as the price of his silence, a demand that was met with horror and refusal by the family [020-090][020-091].

After his blackmail attempt was rejected, [[Torres]] became increasingly desperate. The [[Cryptogram]] was his only asset, and the name of its author — the crucial key to deciphering it — was unknown to him. Alone, the document was worthless; in the hands of someone who could read it, it was Joam's salvation.

## The Discovery

[[Torres]]'s body was found in the river under circumstances that suggested foul play. The discovery sent shockwaves through the [[Jangada]] community. The immediate question was whether [[Joam Dacosta]] — who had the strongest motive to eliminate Torres — was responsible for the death [027-032][028-005].

The recovery of the [[Cryptogram]] from [[Torres]]'s possessions transformed the situation. The document was now in the hands of the Garral family, but the crucial key — [[Ortega]]'s name — remained unknown. It was [[Fragoso]], the barber, who later discovered during his travels that "Ortega" was the name that could unlock the cipher [038-023].

## Aftermath

[[Torres]]'s death, while removing the immediate threat of blackmail, complicated [[Joam Dacosta]]'s legal situation. The authorities at [[Manaus]] viewed the death with suspicion, and Joam — already under arrest — faced the additional shadow of a potential murder accusation. However, the recovery of the [[Cryptogram]] proved to be the decisive factor: once deciphered with [[Ortega]]'s name, it revealed the truth of [[The Tijuco Robbery]] and exonerated Joam Dacosta.

The mystery of who killed [[Torres]] was ultimately resolved. He had been killed by a blow to the head, and the circumstances pointed to a personal enemy — not Joam, but someone from Torres's own dark past in the Brazilian provinces.

## Significance

[[Torres]]'s death is the novel's pivotal peripeteia — the moment when the antagonist is eliminated but the crisis deepens before its resolution. Verne uses the death to explore themes of justice and irony: the blackmailer who held the key to an innocent man's freedom dies without ever understanding the value of what he possessed. The [[Cryptogram]], passing from Torres's hands to the Garral family, transforms from an instrument of extortion into an instrument of justice — a reversal that underscores the novel's moral framework.
