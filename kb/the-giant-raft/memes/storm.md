# Storm

**Storms** are a frequent and dramatic feature of the [[Amazon Basin]], encountered by the travelers in _The Giant Raft_ as they navigate the great river. Unlike the temperate storms of higher latitudes, [[Amazon]] storms arrive suddenly, unleash torrential rain and fierce winds, then pass with equal speed — a rhythm dictated by the immense humidity and heat of the equatorial climate.

## Amazon Storm Patterns

The [[Amazon]] experiences "continual rains, but in frequent storms" rather than steady drizzle [015-030]. The weather can shift rapidly: a calm morning gives way to an oppressive afternoon, with "large bats of ruddy color" streaking through the air as a harbinger of the approaching tempest [015-002]. Thunderclouds build into towering cumuli, "warning them of renewed storms" [015-046].

The storms that sweep the [[Amazon]] valley are intense but often localized. The travelers on the [[Jangada]] sometimes observe lightning from "a distant storm which did not reach the entrance to the river" where they were moored [015-054]. The great width of the Amazon — often several miles across — means that a storm can rage on one bank while the other remains untouched.

## Storms and the Jangada

The [[Giant Raft]] itself is built to withstand [[Amazon]] weather. Constructed of massive tree trunks pinned together with iron-shod piles, the [[Jangada]] rides out storms that would capsize smaller vessels. The crew's experience with river navigation allows them to read the signs of approaching weather and seek shelter in sheltered bays or behind islands when necessary.

During the [[Lower Amazon]] portion of the journey, the travelers "did not experience any of the storms which so frequently rage along the course of the river" [040-023] — a stroke of fortune that allows them to reach their destination unhindered by weather delays.

## A Force of Nature

The [[Amazon]] storm represents the raw power of the equatorial environment. "These could not hinder the progress of the enormous raft" [015-030], but they demanded vigilance and respect from all who traveled the great waterway. The [[Jangada]]'s crew understood that in the contest between human engineering and Amazon weather, prudent seamanship — not brute force — was the only reliable defense.
