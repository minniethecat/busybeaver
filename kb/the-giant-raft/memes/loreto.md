# Loreto

## Overview

Loreto is a small Peruvian settlement on the left bank of the Upper [[Amazon River]], occupying a strategic position as the final Peruvian town before the Brazilian frontier [011-012]. The [[Jangada]] makes a brief stop at Loreto during its downriver voyage from [[Iquitos]], and the town proves memorable primarily for one notorious feature: its mosquitoes, which attack the travelers with unusual ferocity [011-015].

## Geography

Loreto sits on the [[Upper Amazon]] in Peruvian territory, upstream of the Brazilian border. The settlement lies on the left bank of the river among the characteristic lowland rainforest of the region. Verne describes the environs as typical of the Peruvian [[Amazon]], with dense vegetation and abundant insect life [011-011]. After passing Loreto, the Amazon makes a slight turn toward the east as it approaches the frontier [011-024].

> "Loreto is the last Peruvian town situated on the left bank of the [[Amazon]] before the Brazilian frontier." [011-012]

## In the Novel

The [[Jangada]]'s brief stop at Loreto provides one of the more vivid comic episodes in the voyage narrative. The town's mosquitoes descend upon the party in punishing numbers, and Manoel warns the others of their reputation for ferocity [011-015] [011-020]. The experience is sufficiently memorable that it is later recalled by Madame des Odonais when recounting her own [[Amazon]] journey [011-070].

The stop also serves a geographical narrative function, marking the [[Jangada]]'s approach to the border between Peru and Brazil. At Loreto, a handful of Peruvian soldiers are stationed, representing the last outpost of Peruvian authority before the raft enters Brazilian territory [011-014].

## Journey Role

Loreto represents the final Peruvian waypoint on the [[Jangada]]'s [[Amazon]] voyage. After departing Loreto, the raft crosses into Brazilian territory at [[tabatinga|Tabatinga]], where the political and legal stakes of the journey intensify. In narrative terms, Loreto is the last stop in what might be called the "Peruvian phase" of the expedition — a relatively peaceful segment before the complications of Brazilian jurisdiction and [[Joam Dacosta]]'s legal situation come to the foreground.
