# Benito

## Introduction

Benito Garral is the son of [[Joam Garral]] and a central supporting character in *The [[Giant Raft]]*. Energetic, resourceful, and fiercely loyal, he combines practical engineering skill with intellectual tenacity. He takes charge of the [[Jangada]]'s construction and navigation, undertakes the novel's most physically daring feat — diving beneath the raft to retrieve [[Torres]]'s body — and works obsessively to decipher the [[Cryptogram]] that will prove his father's innocence. Among the younger generation aboard the jangada, Benito is the character who most actively drives the plot forward.

## Biography

Benito was born at the [[Fazenda]], the first child of Joam and [[Yaquita]], a year after their marriage [003-030]. Raised on the [[Amazon]] frontier, he grew into a capable young man comfortable with both the physical demands of life on the river and the intellectual challenges that his father's secret would eventually impose.

> Benito was now aged twenty-one, and finished his studies at Belém, where Manoel had made his acquaintance. [003-041]

When Joam finally decided to build the [[Jangada]], he entrusted the project to Benito: "the jangada must be built and ready to launch" within a month [006-024]. Benito rose to the challenge, overseeing everything from the raft's structural design to its provisions. The wine cellar "did honor to Benito, who had" stocked it [009-009], revealing his attention to the domestic comforts of the voyage alongside its engineering demands.

> "Benito Garral," replied Manoel. "It was I who shot the monkey, Benito," said Manoel, "it was you who brought him down!" [002-078]

## Character Analysis

### The Engineer-Navigator

Benito's primary role in Part I is practical. He navigates the [[Jangada]] through the [[Amazon]]'s winding channels, a responsibility that requires constant vigilance [008-023]. He plants trees on the raft at the suggestion of others, transforming it into a floating garden [008-042]. His competence is never in doubt — he is the son who can be relied upon to make things work.

### Physical Courage: The Underwater Dive

Benito's defining moment comes in Chapter 31, after [[Torres]]'s body has been thrown into the river. Desperate to recover the document that may contain evidence of his father's innocence, Benito declares his intention in stark terms:

> "Plunge down myself beneath the waters," replied Benito, "and search with my eyes — with my hands." [029-012]

The dive is one of the novel's most suspenseful sequences — Benito must find [[Torres]]'s corpse in the murky [[Amazon]] waters, retrieve the document, and surface before his breath gives out. His success is a turning point: though the document proves to be encrypted, its recovery makes the eventual deciphering possible.

### Intellectual Tenacity: The Cryptogram

Alongside Manoel and [[Judge Jarriquez]], Benito throws himself into the [[Cryptogram]]'s deciphering. He spends "long hours in endeavoring to decipher" the document [032-019], his frustration mounting as the code resists every attempt. When the efforts of the professional judge fail, Benito continues working — his persistence, combined with Manoel's insight, ultimately contributes to the breakthrough. His determination is captured in his cry to his imprisoned father:

> "We shall find it, father!" exclaimed Benito. "There never was a document of this sort yet which could stand examination. Have confidence — yes, confidence!" [032-009]

### The Weight of Guilt

After Joam's arrest, Benito is "quite overwhelmed, and accused himself of having destroyed his father" [027-002]. The depth of his anguish finds its most unguarded expression when he cries out:

> "My father!" exclaimed Benito. "I have killed him!" [027-010]

This self-reproach — irrational but psychologically acute — reveals the depth of his filial devotion. He is saved from despair only by the entreaties of his mother, sister, and [[Padre Passanha]] [027-002]. His mother fears "for his reason" during the crisis [035-016], a measure of how intensely Benito internalizes the family's suffering.

## Relationships

- [[joam-garral]] — His father. Benito's love for Joam is the engine of his actions throughout Part II. From the underwater dive to the [[Cryptogram]] sessions, everything he does after the arrest is directed toward saving his father.

- [[yaquita]] — His mother. After the arrest, Yaquita fears for Benito's mental state [035-016]. Their relationship underscores the emotional toll the crisis takes on the entire family.

- [[minha]] — His sister. Benito is protective of Minha, and their sibling bond is one of the novel's steady relationships.

- [[manoel]] — His closest friend and future brother-in-law. Manoel first confided his love for [[Minha]] to Benito [004-003], and the two young men work together throughout the crisis — from the underwater recovery of the document to the [[Cryptogram]] sessions [032-019]. Before the dive, Benito says nothing to his mother or his sister, adding "if this last hope fails it will kill them" [027-035], keeping the dangerous undertaking from [[Yaquita]] and Minha to spare them additional anxiety.

- [[fragoso]] — The loquacious barber becomes an unlikely ally. Fragoso's discovery that [[Torres]] was a member of the gang that committed [[The Tijuco Robbery]] provides a crucial piece of the puzzle.

## In the Novel

### Part I: The Journey

Benito is introduced as a "well-built" young man "of distinguished bearing, with a certain native pride which became him well" [003-035]. He accompanies his mother to visit towns along the river [012-015], hunts in the forest [007-003], and handles the [[Jangada]]'s navigation with growing expertise. His competence and good nature make him the ideal elder son — responsible, capable, and devoted to his family.

### Part II: The Crisis

The arrest transforms Benito from capable navigator to desperate son. The underwater dive (Chapter 31) is his physical trial; the [[Cryptogram]] sessions are his intellectual trial. In both, he demonstrates the qualities that Verne most admires: courage, persistence, and technical skill applied to a moral purpose.

Benito's emotional arc during the crisis is downward then upward. Initially crushed by guilt, he channels his despair into action — first the dive, then the deciphering. When the [[Cryptogram]] finally yields its secret, Benito's relief is proportionate to his earlier anguish.

### Part III: Resolution

At the novel's close, Benito stands with his family at the double wedding, his father exonerated and the family restored. His role as protector and problem-solver is complete, and he can return to being simply a son and brother.

## Significance

Benito represents the active, capable younger generation in *The [[Giant Raft]]*. Where Joam is weighed down by his past and Manoel by his professional duties, Benito acts — he builds, navigates, dives, and deciphers. He is the character who most directly embodies the Vernean ideal of the competent man: someone whose technical skill serves moral ends.

His arc also traces the novel's thematic concern with sonship. Benito's desperate efforts to save his father invert the expected parent-child dynamic — the son becomes the protector, the child becomes the rescuer. In this sense, Benito's story is a coming-of-age narrative embedded within the larger adventure plot: by the novel's end, he has proven himself worthy of the father whose name he will carry forward.
