# Pororoca

## Definition

The pororoca (from the Tupi word for "great destructive noise") is a tidal bore that occurs at the mouth of the [[Amazon River]]. When Atlantic Ocean tides meet the river's outflow, they create a surging wall of water that can travel hundreds of kilometers upstream with tremendous force. In *The [[Giant Raft]]*, it represents one of the most feared natural hazards of [[Amazon]] navigation.

## In the Novel

Verne uses the pororoca as a looming threat in the narrative. As the Garral family's [[Jangada]] approaches the [[Amazon]]'s mouth, the possibility of encountering "that species of eddy which for three days in the height of the syzygies raises the waters" (012-023) adds tension to the final stages of their journey. The phenomenon is described as "phenomenal, a tide-race, or pororoca, to which the ebbs, the bores" give rise (005-015) — a uniquely Amazonian hazard.

The [[Jangada]], despite its massive size, would not be immune to the pororoca's force, which Verne notes is "by far the most formidable of tide-races" (040-023). The crew's awareness of this danger influences their navigation decisions in the lower reaches, where the river widens and "the influence of ordinary tides is not perceptible" (012-023) — making the pororoca's sudden appearance all the more dangerous.

## Technical Detail

The pororoca is one of the world's strongest tidal bores, rivaled only by China's Qiantang River bore. It occurs when the incoming Atlantic tide — which can reach 4 meters at the river's mouth — compresses against the [[Amazon]]'s massive outflow, creating a breaking wave front that travels upstream at speeds of 15–25 km/h. The wave can be heard long before it is seen, earning its Tupi name for the "great roar" it produces.

The bore is strongest during spring tides (new and full moons) and is most dramatic between February and March, when the river is at its lowest level relative to the tidal inflow.

## Historical Context

By Verne's time, the pororoca was well known to navigators and documented in travel literature. It had claimed numerous small vessels and was a significant obstacle to [[Amazon]] commerce. Verne's inclusion of this phenomenon reflects his characteristic blend of geographical accuracy and dramatic storytelling — the pororoca serves both as a real navigational concern and as a narrative device for heightening tension.
