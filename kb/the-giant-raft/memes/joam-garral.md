# Joam Garral

## Introduction

Joam Garral is the central figure of *The Giant Raft* — a prosperous fazendero living near Iquitos, Peru, who organizes an extraordinary journey down the Amazon aboard a giant raft. But behind this respectable exterior lies a hidden past: Garral is actually Joam Dacosta, a man wrongly condemned for the Tijuco diamond robbery who has lived under an assumed identity for twenty-six years (003-009). As the adventurer Torres confronts him:

> "You are Joam Dacosta," continued Torres, "who, twenty-five years ago, were a clerk in the governor-general's office at Tijuco, and you are the man who was sentenced to death in this affair of the robbery and murder!" (020-006)

Garral's double identity — the respected fazendero and the fugitive convict — creates the central dramatic tension of the entire novel.

## Biography

### Arrival in Peru

Joam Garral arrived in Peru as a young man of twenty-two, exhausted and destitute. "Magalhães had found him half-dead with hunger and fatigue in the neighboring forest" (003-012). Brazilian by birth, Garral "was without family or fortune. Trouble, he said, had obliged him to quit his country and abandon all thoughts of return" (003-014). The Portuguese plantation owner Magalhães, impressed by Garral's character, offered him hospitality:

> The noble, high-spirited look which Joam Garral bore in spite of his exhaustion had touched him. He received him, restored him, and, for several days to begin with, offered him a hospitality which lasted for his life. (003-012)

Garral threw himself into the work of the fazenda with determination. "Joam Garral accepted the offer without hesitation" and "entered resolutely into the service of the fazenda, deciding to devote to it all his powers" (003-015, 003-016).

### Rise to Prosperity

Under Garral's management, the fazenda flourished. "His wood trade, which extended by means of the Amazon up to Pará, was soon considerably extended under the impulse of Joam Garral. The fazenda began to grow in proportion, and to spread out along the bank of the river up to its junction with the Nanay" (003-017). Within ten years of his arrival, the estate "had become one of the richest establishments on the Upper Amazon" (003-019).

In gratitude for Garral's transformative work, Magalhães first gave him an interest in the profits, then "four years after his arrival he had made him a partner on the same footing as himself, and with equal shares" (003-020). When Magalhães was mortally wounded by a falling tree, he took Yaquita's hand "and put it into that of Joam Garral, making him swear to take her for his wife" (003-023). In 1830, "Joam Garral became the new fazender of Iquitos, to the immense satisfaction of all those who composed the staff of the farm" (003-028).

### The Burden of the Past

Despite his prosperity, Garral carried a hidden weight. In 1852, at age forty-eight, "his calm temperament seemed to indicate an interior fire, kept well under control" (003-036). The narrator observes a "depth of sadness which even the tenderness of Yaquita had not been able to subdue" (003-037):

> Respected by all, placed in all the conditions that would seem necessary to happiness, why was not this just man more cheerful and less reserved? Why did he seem to be happy for others and not for himself? Was this disposition attributable to some secret grief? Herein was a constant source of anxiety to his wife. (003-038)

The source of this sadness was his hidden identity: for "twenty-five years Joam Garral had never crossed the Brazilian frontier, his wife and daughter had never set foot on Brazilian soil" (004-018). He could not risk returning to Brazil, where a death sentence still awaited him.

### Confrontation and Arrest

When the adventurer Torres arrives on the jangada, he reveals Garral's secret. Torres possesses a document written by one of the true criminals, containing a confession that proves Garral's innocence. Torres threatens: "He knew where Joam Dacosta had taken refuge, and under what name the innocent man had again begun a new life" (020-044). When Garral refuses to buy Torres's silence, the adventurer denounces him to the authorities at Manaus.

The chief of police confronts him: "Joam Garral, you have also been Joam Dacosta; both names have been borne by the same man — I arrest you!" (020-109). Garral's response to his family reveals his essential nobility:

> "There is no mistake here, my son," replied Joam Garral; "Joam Dacosta and Joam Garral are one. I am in truth Joam Dacosta! I am the honest man whom a legal error unjustly doomed to death twenty-five years ago in the place of the true culprit! That I am quite innocent I swear before Heaven, once for all, on your heads, my children, and on the head of your mother!" (020-120)

Garral had always maintained his innocence. When Torres first confronted him, he responded directly: "Torres, if you wish to become one of the family of Joam Dacosta, you ought to know that Joam Dacosta was innocent of the crime for which he was condemned" (020-035).

### The Path to Vindication

Garral had entrusted his case to Judge Ribeiro of Manaus — who, as a young advocate, had defended him at the original trial and "came to the conclusion that his client was wrongfully accused, and that he had taken not the slightest part in the murder of the escort or the theft of the diamonds — in a word, that Joam Dacosta was innocent" (023-003). A "secret correspondence took place between the magistrate and Joam Dacosta" (023-024), and through Ribeiro, Garral had arranged to present himself to Brazilian justice.

Ribeiro believed that Garral's life itself was proof of his innocence: "That proof, Benito, lies wholly in the twenty-three years of an honorable and honored life, lies entirely in the bearing of Joam Dacosta, who comes forward to say to justice, 'Here am I! I do not care for this false existence any more'" (022-008).

After the cryptogram is finally deciphered and his innocence proven, "Joam Dacosta reentered his home with his head erect, and it was indeed a family of happy hearts which he brought back with him from beyond the Brazilian frontier" (040-046).

## Relationships

- [[yaquita]] — His devoted wife, the daughter of Magalhães. Garral married her in 1830 after promising her dying father to take her as his wife (003-023). She never doubted his innocence even after the revelation of his past (022-014).
- [[benito]] — His son, a spirited young man of twenty-one who helps construct and navigate the jangada. "Benito was possessed of a cheerful disposition, an active mind, a lively intelligence, and qualities of heart equal to those of his head" (003-032).
- [[minha]] — His daughter, twenty years old and "the very image of Yaquita" (003-042). Her impending marriage to Manoel Valdez provides the pretext for the journey down the Amazon.
- [[manoel-valdez]] — Benito's closest friend, a young medical officer who loves Minha and whom Garral treats as "another son" (003-035).
- [[torres]] — The adventurer who recognizes Garral's true identity. Torres possesses the cryptogram that can prove Garral's innocence but attempts to extort wealth and marriage to Minha before revealing it.
- [[judge-jarriquez]] — The magistrate at Manaus who Garral's advocate Judge Ribeiro was succeeded by; holds jurisdiction over Garral's fate after Ribeiro's death.

## In the Novel

Garral's story spans the entire novel, from his fazenda near Iquitos to the final vindication. The fazenda is situated "on the left bank of the Amazon, near the seventy-fourth meridian" (003-002), bounded by the River Nanay to the north and the Amazon to the east (003-008). The journey downriver carries the entire Garral household aboard a jangada — a giant timber raft constructed specifically for the voyage, measuring a thousand feet long by sixty feet wide. The jangada included "the master's house" at the rear, "arranged to contain several bedrooms and a large dining-hall," with rooms designated for Joam, Yaquita, Benito, Manoel, Minha, and the household servants (008-024).

Garral's secret identity as Joam Dacosta creates the novel's central tension: having been convicted for the Tijuco diamond robbery, "Joam Dacosta, convicted of aggravated and premeditated murder, did not even obtain the benefit of extenuating circumstances, and heard himself condemned to death" (023-005). He escaped from prison the night before his scheduled execution: "during the night which preceded his execution, and when the gallows was already erected, Joam Dacosta managed to escape from the prison at Vila Rica" (023-006).

As Yaquita responds to Manoel after Garral's arrest, her absolute faith in her husband shines through:

> Yaquita, looking him straight in the face, continued: "Yesterday, after the interview you had with Joam Dacosta, my husband, you came to me and called me—mother! You took Minha's hand, and called her—your wife! You then knew everything, and the past life of Joam Dacosta had been disclosed to you." (022-067)

The second half of the novel centers on the desperate race to decipher the cryptogram before the jangada reaches Belém, where Brazilian authorities would be compelled to execute the death sentence. Garral's ultimate vindication and the full restoration of his honor form the novel's triumphant conclusion.

## Significance

Joam Garral embodies Verne's theme of the honest man wronged by circumstance — a man whose integrity survives twenty-five years of hiding, only to be affirmed at last. His transformation from Joam Dacosta — a condemned criminal — to Joam Garral — a respected fazendero — demonstrates the possibility of redemption through honest labor and family devotion. The journey down the Amazon is simultaneously a physical voyage and a moral one: a movement toward justice, truth, and the clearing of his name.

Garral's story also illuminates Verne's recurring interest in the triumph of scientific reason. The cryptogram — an encoded document that Garral must decipher — represents the Enlightenment faith that truth can be extracted from apparent chaos through methodical intelligence. Garral's eventual vindication affirms both his personal honor and the rationalist worldview that pervades Verne's fiction.
