# Arrest of Joam Dacosta

## Summary

The arrest of Joam Dacosta—the respected fazendero known for twenty-three years as Joam Garral—occurs at [[manaus|Manaus]] when [[torres|Torres]], having been refused in his blackmail demand for Minha's hand, reveals Joam's true identity to the Brazilian authorities [020-109]. The arrest transforms the jangada's voyage from a family celebration into a life-or-death legal crisis, forcing the Garral family to race against the execution clock to prove that Joam was innocent of the [[tijuco-robbery|Tijuco diamond robbery]].

## Background

Twenty-three years before the novel's main action, in 1826, a diamond convoy was attacked near [[tijuco|Tijuco]] in Minas Gerais. The escort was massacred, the diamonds stolen, and Joam Dacosta—a young clerk in the governor-general's office—was convicted of masterminding the crime and sentenced to death [024-026] [019-034]. He escaped and fled to the Peruvian Amazon, where he assumed the name Joam Garral and built the prosperous [[fazenda|fazenda]] near Iquitos.

For over two decades, Joam's new identity held. He married [[yaquita|Yaquita]], raised two children—[[benito|Benito]] and [[minha|Minha]]—and became one of the wealthiest landowners on the Upper Amazon [003-014] [003-016]. But the secret of his past was known to one man: Torres, a former colleague in the Brazilian army who had somehow obtained the coded document that could prove Joam's innocence [023-031].

When Torres appeared aboard the jangada, he came not to exonerate Joam but to blackmail him—demanding Minha's hand in marriage in exchange for the exculpatory document. Joam refused. Torres's retaliation was swift and devastating: he denounced Joam Dacosta to the authorities at Manaus [020-109].

## Narrative

The arrest is executed with brutal efficiency. As the jangada arrives at Manaus, the police chief boards the raft and confronts Joam directly:

> "The name of Joam Dacosta and not Joam Garral has been borne by the same man—I arrest you!" [020-109]

Joam's immediate response is not denial but a procedural challenge: "The warrant in virtue of which you arrest me—has it been issued against me by the Brazilian authorities?" [020-113]. This question, seemingly technical, reflects Joam's hope that the Peruvian-Brazilian jurisdictional boundary might offer protection. But the warrant is valid, and Joam is taken into custody.

The news spreads rapidly through Manaus and along the riverfront [022-050] [022-059]. The arrest of a wealthy fazendero traveling aboard a famous giant raft is a sensation, and public opinion divides. Some see Joam as a dangerous criminal finally brought to justice; others, who have come to know the Garral family, doubt the accusation.

For the family, the arrest is catastrophic. Benito, who had never known his father's secret, is shattered by the revelation [022-040]. Minha refuses to proceed with her wedding to [[manoel|Manoel Valdez]] unless her father is cleared [023-010]. Yaquita, who alone knew Joam's true identity, faces the collapse of the life they built together.

> "The news of the arrest of Joam Dacosta had soon spread throughout the town" [022-059]

Benito's fury is immediate and absolute. Confronting Manoel, he demands more than the mere expulsion of Torres from the raft:

> "Yes, Manoel, yes! The indignant reply of an honest man. He kicked Torres off the raft. But it is not enough to have kicked him out. No! That will not do for me. It was on Torres' information that they came here and arrested my father; is not that so?" [022-040]

For Yaquita, the arrest carries a deeper burden—the stigma that now attaches to the family name, reaching even to her daughter:

> "Perhaps so," replied Yaquita; "but then Joam Dacosta had not been arrested. The position is not now the same. However innocent he may be, my husband is in the hands of justice; his past life has been publicly proclaimed. Minha is a convict's daughter." [022-069]

The arrest sets in motion a desperate defense. Judge [[judge-jarriquez|Jarriquez]], the magistrate assigned to the case, reviews the original trial records and confirms that the conviction was legally sound [024-007]. Joam's only hope lies in the cryptogram that Torres possesses—a document encrypted with a cipher that proves the real culprit was [[ortega|Ortega]] [038-023] [038-029].

Torres, however, does not live to produce the document. He is killed before he can deliver it, and the cryptogram passes to the Garral family in circumstances that make its deciphering the novel's central intellectual puzzle. Benito, Manoel, and eventually Judge Jarriquez himself work frantically to crack the code before Joam's execution can be carried out [035-013] [037-007].

> "During the night before the arrival of the raft at Manaus Judge Ribeiro was seized with an attack of apoplexy. But the denunciation of Torres, whose scheme of extortion had collapsed in face of the noble anger of his victim, had produced its effect. Joam Dacosta was arrested in the bosom of his family, and his old advocate was no longer in this world to defend him!" [023-033]

## Consequences

The arrest transforms every aspect of the novel. The jangada, once a vehicle of celebration, becomes a floating prison of anxiety. The voyage's destination shifts from Belém (for the wedding) to Rio de Janeiro (for a potential imperial pardon, should all else fail) [036-022].

More profoundly, the arrest forces Joam's family to confront the question of identity: Is Joam Garral—the loving husband, father, and fazendero—the same person as Joam Dacosta, the convicted diamond thief? The novel's answer is layered. Joam is legally Joam Dacosta, the fugitive; but morally, he is Joam Garral, the innocent man who has lived a life of integrity. The tension between legal identity and moral truth drives the remainder of the narrative.

The arrest also activates the novel's themes of justice and redemption. The Brazilian legal system, correctly applied, would execute an innocent man—a critique Verne embeds without explicit editorializing. The salvation comes not from the law but from intellect (the cryptogram's deciphering), loyalty (Fragoso's detective work), and the confession of the dying [[ortega|Ortega]].

In the prison, as the hour of execution approaches, Joam reflects on the chain of events that brought him there—from the decision to seek rehabilitation to the fatal encounter with his denouncer:

> "And now he was thinking of the day when his daughter's marriage with Manoel had been decided. Could he allow that union to take place under a false name without acquainting the lad with the mystery of his life? No! And so at the advice of Judge Ribeiro he resolved to come and claim the revision of his sentence, to demand the rehabilitation which was his due! He was starting with his people, and then came the intervention of Torres, the detestable bargain proposed by the scoundrel, the indignant refusal of the father to hand over his daughter to save his honor and his life, and then the denunciation and the arrest!" [037-013]
