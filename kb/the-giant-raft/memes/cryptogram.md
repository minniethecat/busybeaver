# Cryptogram

## Definition

The cryptogram is an encrypted document that forms the central plot device of Jules Verne's *The [[Giant Raft]]*. It contains a confession that can prove [[Joam Dacosta]]'s innocence in the infamous [[Tijuco]] [[Diamond]] robbery, but its message is locked behind a substitution cipher that must be deciphered before Joam's execution.

> "Some particular 'word' has to be known before the lock of the safe will act, and some 'cipher' is necessary before that cryptogram can be read." [001-005]

## In the Novel

The novel opens with the cryptogram itself — a "strange assemblage of letters" held by a man who "remained for some moments lost in thought" [001-005]. The document consists of "about a hundred lines, with the letters at even distances, and undivided into words," written many years earlier on "good stout paper" [001-005].

> "The combinations which they lead to can be counted by millions, and no calculator's life would suffice to express them." [001-005]

The man holding the document, [[Torres]], is described as "a fitting comrade for his fellow [[Capitães do Mato]]" — a [[Bush-Captain]], a man of the wilderness whose possession of this document sets the entire plot in motion [001-008]. The nature of the document is confirmed late in the novel: "this document is a cryptogram" [031-062], as the deciphering climax approaches.

> "Before his very eyes, was a cryptogram! And so from that moment he thought of nothing but how to devote all the sagacity of his mind to the discovery of the system." [032-023]

## Analysis

Verne's cryptogram uses a substitution cipher — each letter in the encoded message represents a different letter in the plaintext. The key insight for breaking such ciphers involves recognizing patterns in the encoded text and identifying which letters correspond to common letters in the target language. The cipher's construction follows the principle that "some 'cipher' is necessary before that cryptogram can be read" [001-005] — an apt description of the symmetric-key cryptography that fascinated 19th-century readers.

> "[[Judge Jarriquez]] herein alluded to a story by the great American romancer, which is a masterpiece. Who has not read the 'Gold Bug?' In this novel a cryptogram, composed of ciphers, letters, algebraic signs, asterisks, full-stops, and commas, is submitted to a truly mathematical analysis." [032-077]

The critical breakthrough in the novel comes when the name "[[Ortega]]" is discovered. Placed over the six last letters of the cryptogram's coded paragraph, the numerical values of the letters in "Ortega" corresponded exactly to the cipher, yielding the number 432513 — the key that enabled [[Judge Jarriquez]] to reconstitute the entire message [038-065][038-066]. The cryptogram had been composed by Ortega, the true perpetrator of [[The Tijuco Robbery]], who used his own name as the cipher key — a cryptographic signature that, when finally uncovered, served as the incontrovertible material proof of [[Joam Dacosta]]'s innocence [039-042].

> "This number, 432513, was the number sought for so long! The name of [[Ortega]] had enabled him to discover it! At length he held the key of the document, which would incontestably prove the innocence of [[Joam Dacosta]]." [038-080]

## Historical Context

Cryptography in the 19th century was transitioning from an art to a science. Edgar Allan Poe's "The Gold-Bug" (1843) had popularized substitution ciphers in fiction, and Verne builds on this tradition — the novel explicitly references Poe's work [032-077]. The concept of a cryptogram as a plot-driving legal document — rather than merely a treasure map — was an innovative twist that Verne used to blend adventure with procedural detective work.

> "On the reading of the American document depended only a treasure, while on that of this one depended a man's life. Its solution was consequently all the more interesting." [032-077]
