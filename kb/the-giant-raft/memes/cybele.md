# Cybele

## Introduction

Cybele is an elderly black woman, about sixty years of age, who serves in the household of [[Joam Garral]] at the [[Fazenda]] of [[Iquitos]]. She is a distinctive figure: legally free through the will of her former master, yet bound to the family by profound affection, having served as the nurse of [[Yaquita Garral]]. Cybele accompanies the Garral family on their monumental voyage down the [[Amazon]] aboard the [[Giant Raft]] [003-044].

## Biography

Cybele was introduced as "an old negress, of some sixty years," who had gained her freedom through the will of her master but remained in service out of devotion to the family she had served her entire life [003-044]. She had been the nurse of [[Yaquita]], [[Joam Garral]]'s wife, and was considered "one of the family," so integrated into the household that she addressed both mother and daughter with the familiarity of thou and thee [003-044]. Her entire existence had been spent on the [[Fazenda]] — "in these fields, in the middle of these forests, on that bank of the river which bounded the horizon of the farm" [003-044].

According to [[Fragoso]], Cybele had been "on the farm for the last fifty years," making her one of the longest-serving members of the Garral household [013-058].

## In the Novel

Cybele appears throughout the narrative primarily in a domestic capacity. When preparations for the great [[Amazon]] voyage begin, she assists [[Yaquita]] with the practical arrangements, though she cannot comprehend the purpose of the expedition. "Will they be better than what I see now?" was her invariable reply when told of the sights they would encounter — a sentiment that captures her rooted contentment with the life she has always known [006-035][006-037].

Aboard the [[Jangada]], Cybele is listed among the party who gather for the departure ceremony: "[[Yaquita]], her daughter, [[Manoel Valdez]], [[Padre Passanha]], [[Benito]], [[Lina]], [[Fragoso]], Cybele, and some of the servants, Indian or negro, of the [[Fazenda]]" [009-041]. She assists Yaquita and Lina in maintaining order during the voyage [010-015] and is present at key domestic moments — standing with [[Joam Garral]], his wife, and Lina in front of the house [017-046].

Cybele remains with the family through the entire journey. In the final chapters, when the landscape transforms from dense forest to open hills as the raft approaches Belém, Verne notes that "neither [[Yaquita]], nor her daughter, nor [[Lina]], nor old Cybele, had ever seen anything like this" [040-014]. She is present among the servants who follow the family as they process to the chapel for [[Minha]]'s wedding in the novel's climactic finale [040-040].

## Relationships

- **[[yaquita]]** — Cybele served as Yaquita's nurse and remains her devoted servant; the two share a bond that transcends the usual mistress-servant relationship
- **[[lina]]** — Fellow servant aboard the [[Jangada]]; the two work together in assisting [[Yaquita]] with domestic duties
- **[[minha]]** — [[Yaquita]]'s daughter, whom Cybele has known since birth

## Significance

Cybele represents the traditional, pre-modern social structure of the Brazilian [[Fazenda]] that forms the backdrop of the novel. Her character — a freed slave who remains with her former master's family out of genuine affection — reflects the complex and often idealized portrayal of plantation servitude in 19th-century literature. Though minor in terms of narrative agency, Cybele is a constant domestic presence throughout the story, her role as [[Yaquita]]'s old nurse anchoring the Garral family's identity even as they embark on their extraordinary adventure.
