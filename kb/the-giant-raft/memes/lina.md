# Lina

## Introduction

Lina is a young woman who serves as the devoted maid and companion to [[Minha Garral]], the daughter of [[Joam Garral]]. She is characterized by her gentle nature, her complete devotion to her mistress, and — most notably — her irrepressible laughter and playful spirit [003-045]. Lina accompanies the Garral family on their journey down the [[Amazon]] aboard the [[Giant Raft]].

> "She was called Lina. One of those gentle creatures, completely devoted to her mistress, whose laughter was as infectious as her good humor was constant." [003-045]

Lina belongs to the household staff of the [[Fazenda]] of [[Iquitos]], where she grew up alongside [[Minha]] in the cultivated prosperity of the Garral estate. Though a servant by station, her relationship with Minha transcends the formal boundaries of mistress and maid — the two young women share a bond closer to that of sisters, and Lina's opinions, however playfully expressed, are heard and respected within the family circle.

## Biography

Lina was one of the household servants at the [[Fazenda]] of [[Iquitos]], completely devoted to [[Minha]] [003-045]. Unlike her mistress, who could sometimes be sorrowful, Lina remained joyous and unaffected by melancholy [006-038]. As Minha's inseparable companion, Lina could not leave her mistress and therefore joined the expedition aboard the [[Jangada]] [007-004].

> "Lina could not leave her mistress — and the girls — for Lina could not be separated from [[Minha]] — accompanied the expedition." [007-004]

At the [[Fazenda]], Lina had been a constant presence in [[Minha]]'s daily life, sharing in her studies, her leisure, and her quiet moments by the river. When the decision was made to journey down the [[Amazon]], there was never any question that Lina would remain behind — she was as much a part of the family's floating household as [[Yaquita]] or [[Benito]]. Her cheerful disposition made her a natural morale-booster during the long days on the raft, and her singing voice filled the evenings on board [008-016].

## In the Novel

Lina's most prominent role occurs during a forest excursion early in the voyage. She engages in playful banter with the group, her laughter so noisy that it becomes a subject of affectionate teasing among the family [007-014][007-015][007-023][007-041]. When [[Minha]] affectionately mocks her, [[Benito]] defends Lina by noting that her absurdity is such that it actually highlights Benito's own cleverness — a running joke within the family circle [007-073][007-074].

> "Who knows?" said Lina, laughing. [007-014]

> "Lina is right," answered [[Minha]], when Lina had spoken — the family's affectionate refrain whenever Lina ventured an opinion. [007-015]

During the same excursion, Lina demonstrates resourcefulness and keen observation. She points to a [[Liana]] (cipó) as a solution to a problem [007-069]. Throughout the scene, the group debates whether "Lina is right" or "Lina is wrong" — a recurring motif that underscores her role as the family's spirited foil [007-087][007-088].

> "And Lina pointed to a [[Liana]] of the cipó kind, hanging from the branches of a large tree." [007-069]

When [[Manoel Valdez]] briefly loses consciousness during the adventure, it is Lina who cries out in alarm and then announces his recovery: "He breathes again! His heart beats!" [007-126]. Her dramatic exclamation upon seeing a [[Liana]] — "to hang himself! and young still!" — reveals her theatrical, humorous personality that lightens even tense moments [007-130].

> "Mr. Manoel! Mr. Manoel!" cried Lina. "He breathes again! His heart beats!" [007-126]

After the crisis passes, Manoel warmly thanks Lina for the good idea she had contributed — the [[Liana]] that proved instrumental in the rescue — confirming that even the family's "absurd" jester can be the source of genuine insight and practical wisdom [007-146]. Throughout the rest of the voyage, Lina continues to be the raft's resident spirit of good cheer, always singing and always ready with a smile [008-016].

## Relationships

- **[[minha]]** — Lina's mistress; the two young women are inseparable companions
- **[[benito]]** — Teases Lina affectionately; the two share a playful dynamic
- **[[manoel]]** — Lina assists in the rescue when Manoel is injured

## Significance

Lina functions as the novel's comic relief — a stock character in Verne's fiction whose laughter and lightheartedness provide contrast to the dramatic tension of the main plot. Her devotion to [[Minha]] also illustrates the social structure of a Brazilian [[Fazenda]] household in the 19th century. Though a minor character appearing primarily in the early chapters, Lina's presence establishes the domestic warmth and familial bonds that motivate the central characters throughout their perilous journey.
