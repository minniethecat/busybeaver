# Diamond

## Definition

In *The Giant Raft*, diamonds function less as geological specimens than as plot catalysts—the precious stones whose theft from the Tijuco district sets in motion the entire chain of events that define Joam Dacosta's life. The "diamond affair" drives the novel's central mystery, linking the remote mining district of Minas Gerais to the Amazon voyage and the Garral family's fate.

## In the Novel

Diamonds enter the narrative through [[torres|Torres]], who reveals his knowledge of the Tijuco robbery to the passengers of the jangada. His conversation with Manoel and the others lays out the geography and economy of the diamond district in detail [019-003] [019-006]. Torres speaks with insider knowledge of "the capital of the diamond district, Tijuco" [018-066], alarming the family with his familiarity.

The Tijuco diamond robbery itself is described as a notorious crime. A convoy of diamonds, collected over the course of a year from the district's mines and guarded by soldiers, was attacked and plundered by a band of smugglers [019-032] [019-034]. The escort was massacred, the diamonds stolen, and Joam Dacosta—an employee of the governor-general's office at Tijuco—was convicted of being the instigator [020-006] [020-012].

> "In the capital of the diamond district, Tijuco" — Torres begins his dangerous revelations [018-066]

The diamond thus functions as a MacGuffin in Verne's plot. Its material value matters less than its narrative function: the theft creates the mystery (who really did it?), the wrongful conviction creates the stakes (Joam's life), and the cryptogram that proves Joam's innocence is directly connected to the real diamond thief, [[ortega|Ortega]].

The hold diamonds exert over the imagination is immediately evident when Fragoso, playing unwittingly into Torres's hands, voices the dream of every visitor to the diamond valley:

> "Ah! I should like to see that country," said Fragoso, who unconsciously played Torres' game. "It seems to me I should finish by picking up a diamond worth something considerable." [019-010]

## Analysis

Verne uses the diamond not merely as a plot device but as a lens through which to examine guilt, identity, and justice. The diamond's physical nature—hard, beautiful, permanent—contrasts with the fragility of human reputation. Joam Dacosta has spent over twenty years building an honorable life at the fazenda, yet the shadow of the diamond crime can undo everything in an instant [020-008].

The diamond also enables Verne's characteristic documentary impulse. Through Torres's account and Joam's recollections, readers learn about the diamond district's geography, the procedures for transporting precious stones, and the criminal economy that preyed on the trade [019-026] [019-028]. This blend of adventure and information is a hallmark of Verne's *Voyages extraordinaires*.

Verne further explores the moral economy of diamonds. Torres speaks cynically of fortunes made overnight at Tijuco [019-020] and of diamonds obtained "in scoundrel-fashion—that is to say, by theft" [019-028]. The contrast between diamonds honestly mined and those stolen in the Tijuco robbery mirrors the novel's deeper investigation of true versus false identity.

The scale of the diamond trade's criminal underside is underscored by Torres's blunt summary of what was at stake:

> "So be it," answered Torres. "Well, the story is about stealing diamonds, and a handful of those pretty stones is worth a million, sometimes two!" [019-030]

Torres's own account of the diamond underworld reveals how fortunes were made outside the law:

> "But what is still easy is to get the diamonds in scoundrel-fashion—that is, by theft; and—stop! in 1826, when I was about eight years old, a terrible drama happened at Tijuco, which showed that criminals would recoil from nothing if they could gain a fortune by one bold stroke. But perhaps you are not interested?" [019-028]

## Historical Context

The Tijuco (modern Diamantina) diamond district in Minas Gerais was one of the world's most significant diamond-producing regions in the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries. Discovered in the 1720s, the Brazilian diamond fields were placed under Portuguese crown monopoly, with the famous Diamond Contract system regulating production until Brazilian independence.

By Verne's time (1881), the Brazilian diamond rush had passed its peak, but the lore of sudden fortunes, daring robberies, and the district's isolation in the Serra do Espinhaço mountains provided rich material for adventure fiction. The Abaeté diamond, mentioned by Torres [019-020], was a real famous stone discovered in the region, grounding Verne's fiction in documented fact.

The novel draws on genuine diamond history for its most colorful anecdotes. Torres recounts the fate of the famous Abaete stone to illustrate both the wealth and the bureaucracy of the diamond trade:

> "No," replied Torres; "the diamond was handed over to the governor-general of the mines. The value of the stone was recognized, and King John VI, of Portugal, had it cut, and wore it on his neck on great occasions. As for the convicts, they got their pardon, but that was all, and the cleverest could not get much of an income out of that!" [019-022]

The consequence of the diamond robbery was lethal. When Judge Jarriquez confronts Joam with the bare fact of his sentence, the diamond's role in the tragedy is reduced to its starkest form:

> "Because I was sentenced to death in 1826 in the diamond affair at Tijuco." [024-026]
