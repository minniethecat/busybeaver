# Caoutchouc

**Caoutchouc** — natural rubber — is the most important economic product of the [[Amazon Basin]] in Jules Verne's *The [[Giant Raft]]*. It appears throughout the novel as a symbol of the region's natural wealth, a trade commodity that drives commerce along the great river, and a backdrop to the lives of the characters who inhabit the [[Amazon]].

## The Seringal and Seringueira

The Portuguese word *seringal* designates a caoutchouc plantation — "the caoutchouc being extracted from the *seringueira* tree, whose scientific name is *siphonia elastica*" (017-034). Verne notes with concern that "by negligence or bad management, the number of these trees is decreasing in the basin of the [[Amazon]]," though the forests of seringueira trees remain "very considerable on the banks of the Madeira, Purus, and other tributaries" (017-035).

The harvesting and processing of caoutchouc "principally takes place during the months of May, June, and July". The process begins by confirming that the trees, "well prepared by the river floods which have bathed their stems to a height of about four feet, are in good condition" for tapping. The latex is collected and the layers "formed in succession are detached from the scoop, exposed to the sun, hardened, and assume the brownish color with which we are familiar".

## Economic Importance

### As a Livelihood

When [[Joam Garral]] first arrives in Peru as a young fugitive, his original intention is to join a *seringal* — "a caoutchouc concern, in which in those days a good workman could earn from five to six piastres a day, and could hope to become a master if he had any luck" (003-015). This reveals caoutchouc as the default economic opportunity for a man seeking his fortune on the [[Amazon]] frontier in the 1820s.

Magalhães, however, wisely counsels Joam that work in the seringals is seasonal — "only during a few months of the year" — and does not "constitute the permanent position that a young man ought to wish for" (003-015). This advice leads Joam to join the [[Fazenda]] instead, changing the course of his life.

### As Cargo

When the Garral family launches their giant [[Jangada]] down the [[Amazon]], caoutchouc forms the centerpiece of their commercial cargo. "Seven thousand arrobas of caoutchouc, each of about thirty pounds, composed the most precious part of the cargo, for every pound of it was worth from three to four francs" (009-018). Alongside sarsaparilla, the caoutchouc represents the tangible wealth of the Amazon forest.

Along the journey, [[Benito]] trades for additional caoutchouc. At a seringal on the island of Cocos, he "found a capital opportunity" and "bought from the Indians all the caoutchouc stored in their cabins" at a "sufficiently remunerative" price (017-040).

### As a Regional Export

At [[Manaus]], the commercial hub of the [[Amazon]], caoutchouc is listed among the principal exports — alongside "woods used in building and furniture work, cocoa, coffee, sarsaparilla, sugar-canes, indigo, muscado nuts, salt fish, turtle butter, and other commodities" — all brought from every direction by "the innumerable streams" that feed the great river (021-006). The introduction of steam vessels, Verne observes, is destined to "increase enormously" Manaus's trade in these goods.

## Natural Context

The seringueira tree grows alongside other characteristic [[Amazon]] flora: "tucumas, or ficuses, capriciously twisted like centenarian olive-trees," "euphorbias, which produce caoutchouc," "gualtes, noble [[Palm]]-trees, with slender, graceful, and glossy stems," and cacao-trees that "shoot up of their own accord on the banks of the Amazon and its tributaries" (007-083).

## Historical Note

*The [[Giant Raft]]* was published in 1881, at the very beginning of the [[Amazon]] rubber boom that would transform the region's economy between 1879 and 1912. Verne's detailed attention to caoutchouc — its extraction process, its market value, its role in commerce — reflects contemporary European fascination with this Amazonian product. The rubber boom would eventually make [[Manaus]] one of the wealthiest cities in the world, before the collapse triggered by the smuggling of seeds to British plantations in Southeast Asia.
