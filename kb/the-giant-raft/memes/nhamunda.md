# Nhamundá

## Overview

The Rio Nhamunda (also spelled Nhamundá, Jamundá, or Yamundá) is a river in the [[Lower Amazon]] region of Brazil. It holds a singular place in the history of [[Amazon]] exploration as the reputed site of Francisco de [[Orellana]]'s 1539 encounter with female warriors — an account that gave the [[Amazon River]] its immortal name. The river flows into the Amazon from the north near the village of Faro, in what is today the Brazilian state of Pará.

## Geography

The Nhamundá is described in the novel as "one of the middle-sized affluents of the great river" [005-024]. It drains the region between the [[Amazon]] main stem and the Guiana highlands to the north, flowing southward through a landscape of lowland tropical forest before emptying into the Amazon. The village of Faro, situated at the river's mouth, marks the confluence — "the village of Faro and its celebrated river of the Nhamundas" [040-004].

Unlike the great southern tributaries such as the Madeira or the Purus, which descend from the Andes and carry enormous volumes of sediment, the Nhamundá is a shorter, clearer river characteristic of the Guiana Shield drainage. Its watershed lies entirely within the lowland [[Amazon Basin]], giving it a gentler current and a more stable flow regime than the Andean-fed affluents.

## In the Novel

The Nhamundá appears in the narrative primarily through its legendary association rather than as a physical waypoint on the [[Jangada]]'s voyage. In the description of the [[Amazon]]'s naming, the text explains that the old Spaniards, "descendants of the adventurous [[Orellana]]," spread "vague but enthusiastic stories" claiming that a [[Tribe]] of female warriors existed on the Rio Nhamunda — thus the great river became known as the "river of the Amazons," or "Amazonas" [005-024].

As the [[Jangada]] passes through the [[Lower Amazon]], nearing the end of its voyage, the raft leaves behind "the village of Faro and its celebrated river of the Nhamundas, on which, in 1539, [[Orellana]] asserted he was attacked by female warriors, who have never been seen again since" [040-004]. The narrative treats the legend with characteristic 19th-century skepticism, dryly noting that the story "justifies the immortal name of the river of the Amazons" while observing that the warriors were never found again.

The Nhamundá appears in the closing chapters of the novel, during the final stage of the [[Jangada]]'s descent toward the Atlantic. By this point in the journey, the raft has passed the great tributaries of the central [[Amazon]] and is moving through the narrower, more settled lower reaches. The reference to the Nhamundá serves as a cultural and historical anchor point — a reminder of the region's deep colonial history — rather than a navigational landmark.

## Legend of the Amazons

The story that gave the [[Amazon]] its name originated with Francisco de [[Orellana]]'s 1541–1542 expedition, the first European descent of the river's full length. Orellana's chronicler, the Dominican friar Gaspar de Carvajal, recorded that near the mouth of a river on the north bank — generally identified as the Nhamundá — the expedition was attacked by a group of fierce female warriors. These women, described as tall, fair-skinned, and armed with bows, reportedly fought at the head of a larger indigenous force.

Carvajal's account draws deliberate parallels with the Amazons of Greek mythology, a [[Tribe]] of warrior women said to live without men. The classical resonance was not lost on [[Orellana]]'s Spanish patrons, and the name "Rio de las Amazonas" quickly replaced the expedition's original designation for the river. Over time, the name expanded to encompass the entire region.

Whether [[Orellana]] and Carvajal actually encountered women warriors, or mistook long-haired Tapuya male warriors — a practice documented among several indigenous groups of the [[Lower Amazon]] — remains a subject of historical debate. What is certain is that no subsequent expedition, including those of Pedro Texeira in 1637 and La Condamine in 1743, reported any sighting of the Amazons of the Nhamundá. The novel reflects this enduring mystery with its wry observation that the warriors "have never been seen again since" [040-004].

## Historical Context

The [[Lower Amazon]], where the Nhamundá enters the main stream, was one of the earliest regions of European contact in the basin. [[Orellana]]'s descent in 1541–1542 passed through this stretch, as did Pedro Texeira's reverse journey up the river in 1637 — in the novel's historical survey, Texeira "ascended the [[Amazon]] to [[Napo]] with a fleet of forty-seven pirogues" [005-036] in the following century. The Nhamundá region lay within the sphere of Portuguese influence after the Iberian Union, though its indigenous populations remained largely autonomous well into the 18th century.

La Condamine's scientific expedition of 1743 also traversed the [[Lower Amazon]], and his measurements and observations formed much of the geographic knowledge that Verne drew upon for the novel. By the mid-19th century, the lower [[Amazon]] villages — Faro, [[Silves]], [[Vila Bela]] — had become small but established settlements in the province of Pará, connected to Belém and the Atlantic by the river highway that was the region's only practical route of travel.

Verne's treatment of the Nhamundá legend typifies his approach throughout *The [[Giant Raft]]*: a fascination with historical and geographical curiosities, presented with a rationalist's detachment. The Amazons are mentioned, their story is told, and the river that bears their name flows on — "justifying" the immortal name even as the warriors themselves fade into the realm of "vague but enthusiastic stories" [005-024].

## Journey Role

The Nhamundá marks one of the final geographic landmarks on the [[Jangada]]'s voyage. By the time the raft passes Faro and the Nhamundá's mouth, the journey is in its closing stages, with only [[Silves]], [[Vila Bela]], and the [[Lower Amazon]] settlements remaining before the Atlantic. Unlike the great tributaries of the upper and central [[Amazon]] — the [[Napo]], the [[Içá]], the Purus, the Madeira — the Nhamundá is not a navigational challenge but a cultural landmark, a place where the river's name and its history converge in a single legendary association.
