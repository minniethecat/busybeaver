**Bourgeois Socialism** (also called **Conservative or Bourgeois Socialism**) is the target of Section 2 of Chapter III of the Manifesto. Marx and Engels anatomise a form of reformism that presents itself as socialism while seeking to preserve the essential structure of bourgeois society. It is, in their analysis, not an alternative to capitalism but an ideological defence of it — socialism stripped of its revolutionary content and repurposed as a salve for the bourgeois conscience.

## Definition

The Manifesto defines bourgeois Socialism with devastating concision: "The Socialistic bourgeois want all the advantages of modern social conditions without the struggles and dangers necessarily resulting therefrom. They desire the existing state of society minus its revolutionary and disintegrating elements. They wish for a bourgeoisie without a proletariat" [(005-004)].

The logic is structural. The bourgeoisie benefits from capitalism — from private property, the market, wage-labour. But it also faces a threat: the proletariat that capitalism itself creates and concentrates. Bourgeois Socialism is the attempt to solve the second problem without touching the first — to ameliorate poverty, regulate working conditions, and calm social unrest, all while leaving the relations of production intact. "The bourgeoisie naturally conceives the world in which it is supreme to be the best; and bourgeois Socialism develops this comfortable conception into various more or less complete systems" [(005-004)].

The result, Marx and Engels argue, is not socialism at all but a demand that the proletariat "should remain within the bounds of existing society, but should cast away all its hateful ideas concerning the bourgeoisie" [(005-004)].

## In the Manifesto

### The Personnel of Bourgeois Socialism

The Manifesto gives a remarkably vivid catalogue of bourgeois Socialism's proponents: "economists, philanthropists, humanitarians, improvers of the condition of the working class, organisers of charity, members of societies for the prevention of cruelty to animals, temperance fanatics, hole-and-corner reformers of every imaginable kind" [(005-002)]. The list is satirical in intent — Marx and Engels lump together serious political economists with animal-welfare activists and temperance campaigners — but it makes a theoretical point: all these figures share a common structure. They address the symptoms of capitalist exploitation while leaving the cause untouched.

### Proudhon as Exemplar

Pierre-Joseph Proudhon's *Philosophie de la Misère* (1846) is cited as the most systematic expression of bourgeois Socialism: "We may cite Proudhon's *Philosophie de la Misère* as an example of this form" [(005-003)]. Marx had already demolished Proudhon's book in *The Poverty of Philosophy* (1847), and the Manifesto's treatment is brief because it assumes the reader is familiar with that critique. Proudhon rejected revolutionary politics, defended small-scale private property, and sought to reform credit and exchange rather than abolish the wage relation — precisely the combination that Marx and Engels diagnosed as bourgeois Socialism.

### The Practical Variant: Reform Without Revolution

A second, "more practical, but less systematic" form of bourgeois Socialism sought to convince workers that "no mere political reform, but only a change in the material conditions of existence, in economic relations, could be of any advantage to them" [(005-005)]. This sounds radical but is, in the Manifesto's analysis, a deception. By "change in the material conditions of existence," bourgeois Socialism does not mean the abolition of capitalist relations of production — "an abolition that can be effected only by a revolution" — but rather "administrative reforms, based on the continued existence of these relations; reforms, therefore, that in no respect affect the relations between capital and labour, but, at the best, lessen the cost, and simplify the administrative work, of bourgeois government" [(005-005)].

### Free Trade: For the Benefit of the Working Class

The section closes with a rhetorical flourish that reduces bourgeois Socialism to its skeletal logic. Every policy, no matter how contradictory, is justified by the same formula: "Free trade: for the benefit of the working class. Protective duties: for the benefit of the working class. Prison Reform: for the benefit of the working class. This is the last word and the only seriously meant word of bourgeois Socialism" [(005-007)]. The final sentence delivers the killing blow: "It is summed up in the phrase: the bourgeois is a bourgeois — for the benefit of the working class" [(005-008)].

## Distinction from Other Forms

Bourgeois Socialism must be distinguished from the two other reactionary socialisms critiqued in Chapter III. [[german-socialism|German or "True" Socialism]] is the ideology of the petty bourgeoisie in a semi-feudal context; Feudal Socialism (the lament of the aristocracy displaced by the bourgeoisie, critiqued in Ch. III §1c). Bourgeois Socialism, by contrast, is the ideology of the bourgeoisie itself — an attempt to absorb and neutralise socialist criticism without conceding anything of substance. It is, for Marx and Engels, the most insidious form of anti-revolutionary ideology because it wears the language of reform.
