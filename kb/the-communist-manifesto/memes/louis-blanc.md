> In France the Communists ally themselves with the Social-Democrats, against the conservative and radical bourgeoisie, reserving, however, the right to take up a critical position in regard to phrases and illusions traditionally handed down from the great Revolution. [(007-002)]

**Louis Jean Joseph Blanc** (1811–1882) was a French politician, historian, and socialist theorist whose doctrine of state-organized "social workshops" made him the intellectual spokesman of the French Social-Democratic current that the Manifesto identifies as a tactical ally of the Communists in the struggle against the conservative and radical bourgeoisie (007-002). As a member of the Provisional Government during the [[february-revolution-1848|February Revolution of 1848]], Blanc became the first self-declared socialist to hold executive power in a modern European state — an experiment whose defeat in the June Days would mark a decisive turning point in the history of the European labour movement.

## In the Manifesto

### Section IV: The Communists and the Social-Democrats

Louis Blanc is not named directly in the Manifesto, but he is unmistakably present as the leading representative of the French Social-Democratic tendency with which Marx and Engels prescribe tactical alliance. The Manifesto's Section IV — "Position of the Communists in Relation to the Various Existing Opposition Parties" — is the document's most concretely political passage, outlining a differentiated strategy toward different national movements. In France, "the Communists ally themselves with the Social-Democrats, against the conservative and radical bourgeoisie" (007-002).

This alliance, however, is neither unconditional nor uncritical. The Communists "reserve the right to take up a critical position in regard to phrases and illusions traditionally handed down from the great Revolution" (007-002). The "phrases and illusions" were precisely the ideological patrimony that Blanc represented: the belief, inherited from the Jacobin tradition of 1793, that the state could be captured from within and turned into an instrument of social transformation without a fundamental rupture in class power. For Marx and Engels, this was an illusion — but an illusion held by a movement that genuinely mobilized workers against the bourgeoisie, and therefore one whose forces the Communists could not afford to spurn.

The Manifesto's stance toward Blanc's current thus embodies the dual character of communist politics: the imperative of unity in immediate struggles, combined with the imperative of theoretical independence. The Social-Democrats were allies in the fight against bourgeois domination, but their programme — state-directed reform within capitalism — represented a dead end that the Communists' own politics had already transcended.

## Historical Context

### The Theory of Social Workshops

Blanc's signature contribution to socialist thought was his theory of *l'organisation du travail* (the organization of labour), developed in his 1839 book of the same name. His thesis was deceptively simple: the competitive market was the root cause of working-class misery, and the remedy was for the democratic state to establish "social workshops" — producers' cooperatives financed by public capital, managed democratically by the workers themselves, and operating in competition with private enterprises. Because the social workshops would eliminate the parasitic profit of the capitalist, they would consistently undersell private competitors, attract the best workers, and eventually drive private capital out of production entirely.

This was reformist socialism in its classic form: the revolutionary transformation of society was to be achieved not through the violent overthrow of the state but through its gradual economic displacement by cooperative production. The state — a democratic republic based on universal suffrage — would provide the initial capital; the cooperative principle would do the rest. Blanc's slogan captured this gradualism in a single phrase: "From each according to his abilities, to each according to his needs" — a formulation later adopted by Marx in his *Critique of the Gotha Programme* (1875), though Marx gave it a radically different theoretical content by tying it to the higher phase of communist society, not to state-sponsored reform under capitalism.

### The 1848 Revolution and the Luxembourg Commission

Blanc's moment of historical testing arrived in February 1848, when the [[february-revolution-1848|overthrow of Louis-Philippe]] brought a Provisional Government to power in Paris. Under pressure from armed workers who had made the revolution, the new government included Blanc as a minister and — critically — established the Luxembourg Commission (Commission du Gouvernement pour les Travailleurs), with Blanc as its president and the worker-representative Alexandre Martin (known as "Albert") as vice-president.

The Luxembourg Commission was an unprecedented institution: a quasi-ministry of labour that brought together delegates from the Parisian trades to deliberate on the organization of work. Its location — the Palais du Luxembourg, the former seat of the Chamber of Peers — was deliberately symbolic: the aristocracy's political chamber was now the seat of working-class representation. From its platform, Blanc proclaimed the "right to work" as a fundamental social right and called for the establishment of National Workshops (Ateliers Nationaux) that would provide employment for the Parisian unemployed.

But here the tragic irony of Blanc's 1848 revealed itself. The National Workshops that the Provisional Government actually established — under the direction of Blanc's political rival Marie, not Blanc himself — were a mockery of his theory. They were not organized as cooperative producers but as a system of poor-relief through make-work: digging ditches on the Champ de Mars. The workshops were deliberately designed to discredit Blanc's ideas and to isolate the Parisian workers politically. When the government abruptly closed them in June 1848, the result was the June insurrection — three days of ferocious street fighting in which thousands of workers were killed and thousands more deported to Algeria.

### Exile and Aftermath

After the June Days, Blanc fled to England, where he remained in exile until 1870. During his exile, he wrote extensively: his *Histoire de la Révolution française* (1847–1862, 12 volumes) was a major contribution to republican historiography, and his *Histoire de la révolution de 1848* (1870) provided a detailed account of the events in which he had been a central participant. He returned to France after the fall of Napoleon III and was elected to the National Assembly in 1871, where he sat on the extreme left and opposed the savage repression of the Paris Commune — though he did not support the Commune itself, holding to his lifelong conviction that socialism must come through the republic, not against it.

## Relationship to Marxism

Marx's relationship to Blanc was one of simultaneous tactical cooperation and theoretical opposition. In the Manifesto, Marx and Engels prescribe tactical alliance with Blanc's current — but this is an alliance in action, not in theory. The "phrases and illusions" against which the Communists reserve the right to criticize are, in significant part, the illusions of Blanc's state-socialism: the belief that the bourgeois state could be used as a neutral instrument of social transformation, and that cooperative production within capitalism could gradually displace capitalist enterprise.

Marx returned to this critique in *The Class Struggles in France, 1848–1850* (1850) and *The Eighteenth Brumaire of Louis Bonaparte* (1852). In these works, he subjected the Luxembourg Commission and Blanc's role in the Provisional Government to withering analysis. The Commission, Marx argued, was not a breakthrough for working-class power but a method of neutralizing it: by giving the workers a debating society while the real levers of power remained in bourgeois hands, Blanc inadvertently served as a safety valve for the very class he sought to emancipate. The Luxembourg Commission, in Marx's phrase, was a "socialist synagogue" whose priests preached the gospel of a new world while the old world's armies prepared for massacre.

Yet Marx never dismissed Blanc as a mere charlatan. Unlike [[pierre-joseph-proudhon|Proudhon]], whom Marx regarded as a petit-bourgeois ideologue whose theoretical confusion led directly to political impotence, Blanc was a genuine representative of the working-class movement — limited, confused, and reformist, but connected to real masses in struggle. That is why the Manifesto's Section IV prescribes alliance rather than opposition: Blanc's Social-Democrats, whatever their illusions, were fighters against the bourgeoisie, and in that fight, the Communists stood with them.

## Key Works

- *L'Organisation du travail* (1839) — The foundational text of Blanc's socialism, arguing for state-financed social workshops as the path to working-class emancipation
- *Histoire de dix ans, 1830–1840* (1841–1844, 5 volumes) — A searing indictment of the July Monarchy, enormously influential in radical circles
- *Histoire de la Révolution française* (1847–1862, 12 volumes) — Major contribution to republican historiography
- *Le Catéchisme des socialistes* (1849) — A popular exposition of socialist principles
- *Histoire de la révolution de 1848* (1870, 2 volumes) — Participant's account of the events of 1848
