**Industry**, and specifically **modern industry**, is the material basis of bourgeois society — the system of machine-based, factory-organised production that "has converted the little workshop of the patriarchal master into the great factory of the industrial capitalist" [(001-030)]. For Marx and Engels, modern industry is not merely a technology but a social relation: it concentrates workers, revolutionises the instruments of production, and generates the productive forces that bourgeois property relations can no longer contain.

## Definition

The Manifesto distinguishes three historical stages of industrial organisation:

1. **The feudal system of industry** — "industrial production was monopolised by closed guilds" [(001-007)]. This system was adequate to the restricted markets of feudal society but "no longer sufficed for the growing wants of the new markets" [(001-007)] opened up by the discovery of America and the rounding of the Cape.

2. **The manufacturing system** — an intermediate form that "took its place" [(001-008)] after the guild system became inadequate. Based on the division of labour within workshops, manufacture "supplanted" the guild-masters and the "division of labour between the different corporate guilds vanished in the face of division of labour in each single workshop" [(001-008)].

3. **Modern industry** — "the place of manufacture was taken by the giant, Modern Industry" [(001-009)], based on machinery, steam power, and the factory system. "Modern industry has established the world-market" [(001-009)], creating a genuinely global system of production and exchange.

This historical sequence is central to the Manifesto's theory of history. Each stage of industry corresponds to a stage in the development of class relations — from guild-master and journeyman, through manufacturing middle class, to the industrial capitalist and the modern proletarian.

## In the Manifesto

### Industry as Revolutionary Force

The Manifesto's most sustained analysis of industry appears in Section I, where it is presented as the motive force of bourgeois revolution. "The discovery of America, the rounding of the Cape, opened up fresh ground for the rising bourgeoisie. The East-Indian and Chinese markets, the colonisation of America, trade with the colonies, the increase in the means of exchange and in commodities generally, gave to commerce, to navigation, to industry, an impulse never before known" [(001-006)].

Industry is not static; it is a force of perpetual revolution. "The bourgeoisie cannot exist without constantly revolutionising the instruments of production, and thereby the relations of production, and with them the whole relations of society" [(001-014)]. This distinguishes bourgeois industry from all previous modes of production. Feudal industry was conservative, regulated, guild-bound. Modern industry is dynamic, competitive, boundary-dissolving.

### The Social Effects of Industry

The development of industry transforms the class structure. "Modern industry has converted the little workshop of the patriarchal master into the great factory of the industrial capitalist" [(001-030)]. It concentrates "masses of labourers, crowded in the factory," who are "organised like soldiers" [(001-030)] under the command of the capitalist. The worker becomes "an appendage of the machine" [(001-031)], and "the less the skill and exertion of strength implied in manual labour, in other words, the more modern industry becomes developed, the more is the labour of men superseded by that of women" [(001-031)].

Industry also creates the conditions for working-class solidarity. "The advance of industry, whose involuntary promoter is the bourgeoisie, replaces the isolation of the labourers, due to competition, by their revolutionary combination, due to association" [(001-050)]. The factory concentrates workers in large numbers, disciplines them through collective labour, and facilitates communication through "the improved means of communication that are created by modern industry" [(001-037)].

### Industry as the Bourgeoisie's Undoing

The Manifesto argues that modern industry has developed productive forces that exceed the capacity of bourgeois property relations to organise them. "For many a decade past the history of industry and commerce is but the history of the revolt of modern productive forces against modern conditions of production, against the property relations that are the conditions for the existence of the bourgeoisie and of its rule" [(001-025)].

The commercial crises that periodically shake bourgeois society are the clearest expression of this contradiction. "In these crises a great part not only of the existing products but also of the previously created productive forces are periodically destroyed" [(001-025)]. Industry, which the bourgeoisie has developed to an unprecedented degree, has become a force it can no longer control.

### Industry, Chemistry, and Technology

The Manifesto gives a materialist account of technological progress that anticipates later Marxist analyses. "Subjection of Nature's forces to man, machinery, application of chemistry to industry and agriculture, steam-navigation, railways, electric telegraphs, clearing of whole continents for cultivation, canalisation of rivers, whole populations conjured out of the ground — what earlier century had even a presentiment that such productive forces slumbered in the lap of social labour?" [(001-022)].

This catalogue of technological achievements is not mere celebration. It sets up the contradiction at the heart of bourgeois society: the same productive forces that demonstrate humanity's capacity to overcome scarcity are held back by the property relations that organise them for private profit.

## Historical Context

The Manifesto was written at a moment of intense industrial transformation. The 1840s saw the completion of the first phase of the Industrial Revolution in Britain and its rapid extension to the Continent. Railways were spreading across Europe; the telegraph was shrinking distances; the factory system was transforming the landscape and the class structure.

Marx and Engels wrote the Manifesto from the perspective of the most industrially advanced nation, England, but addressed it primarily to the German workers' movement. Germany in 1848 was still predominantly agricultural and artisanal — but it was "on the eve of a bourgeois revolution" [(007-007)] that would accelerate industrial development. The Manifesto's analysis of industry was thus simultaneously a description of England's present and a prediction of Germany's future.
