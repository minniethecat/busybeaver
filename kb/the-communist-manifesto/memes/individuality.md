## Definition

In the *Communist Manifesto*, **individuality** designates not an abstract human capacity, but a historically specific social relation. Marx and Engels distinguish between two irreconcilable concepts: **bourgeois individuality** — the freedom to own, buy, and sell private property — and **personal individuality** — the free development of each person's capacities and relations. The Manifesto argues that capitalism abolishes the latter while protecting the former, and that only the abolition of bourgeois property can restore genuine individuality.

## In the Manifesto

The critique of bourgeois individuality forms the conceptual core of Section II ("Proletarians and Communists"), where Marx and Engels directly address the bourgeois charge that communism would "abolish individuality and freedom":

> The abolition of bourgeois individuality, bourgeois independence, and bourgeois freedom is undoubtedly aimed at. [(002-024)]

For Marx and Engels, the "individual" celebrated by bourgeois society is not the person but the property owner. They challenge their opponents to acknowledge this identification:

> You must, therefore, confess that by "individual" you mean no other person than the bourgeois, than the middle-class owner of property. This person must, indeed, be swept out of the way, and made impossible. [(002-030)]

The Manifesto traces how capitalist production itself already destroys the individuality of the proletarian. Under the regime of machinery and the division of labour, work loses its character as a meaningful human activity:

> Owing to the extensive use of machinery and to division of labour, the work of the proletarians has lost all individual character, and consequently, all charm for the workman. [(001-029)]

Marx and Engels argue that what the bourgeois calls "individuality" is actually the social power to appropriate the labour of others. When this power is abolished through the conversion of capital into common property, individuality — in the degraded, bourgeois sense — indeed vanishes:

> From the moment when labour can no longer be converted into capital, money, or rent, into a social power capable of being monopolised, i.e., from the moment when individual property can no longer be transformed into bourgeois property, into capital, from that moment, you say individuality vanishes. [(002-029)]

## Historical Context

The concept of individuality in the Manifesto draws on two intellectual currents. The first is the Enlightenment ideal of the autonomous rational subject, as articulated by Kant and Rousseau. The second is the critique of this ideal developed by the Young Hegelians, particularly Ludwig Feuerbach's analysis of alienation and Max Stirner's radical individualism in *The Ego and Its Own* (1844). Marx and Engels' innovation was to ground individuality not in abstract consciousness but in material conditions of production — arguing that the form individuality takes in any epoch is determined by the prevailing mode of production.

## Significance

The Manifesto's treatment of individuality is one of its most rhetorically sophisticated passages. Rather than conceding the bourgeois claim to defend individual freedom, Marx and Engels reverse the charge: it is capitalism that annihilates individuality — reducing the worker to an appendage of the machine — while communism, by abolishing class exploitation, creates the material conditions for the free development of each. The famous formula from Section II — "an association, in which the free development of each is the condition for the free development of all" [(002-055)] — summarizes this vision.
