> A spectre is haunting Europe — the spectre of Communism. [(P01-001)]

The **Revolutions of 1848** — sometimes called the "Springtime of Nations" — were the most extensive revolutionary wave in European history. Within weeks of the *Manifesto of the Communist Party*'s publication in London in February 1848, revolutions erupted in Paris, Vienna, Berlin, Milan, Budapest, Prague, Venice, and across the German and Italian states. They were, in one sense, the Manifesto's immediate vindication: the "spectre of Communism" that the old powers had formed a "holy alliance to exorcise" [(P01-001)] had materialized as a continent-wide insurrection. In another sense, they were the Manifesto's first practical test — providing both confirmation and qualification of its analysis of bourgeois revolution, proletarian insurgency, and the relationship between the two.

## In the Manifesto

The Manifesto does not describe the 1848 revolutions — it was written *before* them, in December 1847–January 1848, and published in London in late February 1848, almost exactly as the barricades were going up in Paris. Yet the Manifesto anticipates the revolutions with remarkable precision. Its analysis of the political dynamics of bourgeois society — the tendency of the liberal [[bourgeoisie|bourgeoisie]] to ally with absolutism against the [[proletariat|proletariat]] the moment its own democratic demands are met — would be confirmed in every theatre of the 1848 revolutions.

### The Manifesto's Predictive Framework

The Manifesto's strategic line in Germany — "the Communists turn their attention chiefly to Germany, because that country is on the eve of a bourgeois revolution" [(007-006)] — was formulated months before the March 1848 revolutions in Vienna and Berlin. The prediction that the German bourgeoisie would prove incapable of carrying through its own revolution to completion, and that "the bourgeois revolution in Germany will be but the prelude to an immediately following proletarian revolution" [(007-006)], was substantially borne out: in the Frankfurt Parliament, the liberal bourgeoisie proved unwilling to break with the monarchies or to arm the people, and by 1849 the revolution had been crushed by the very absolutist powers the bourgeoisie had initially opposed.

The strategy for Germany was further elaborated:

> But they never cease, for a single instant, to instil into the working class the clearest possible recognition of the hostile antagonism between bourgeoisie and proletariat, in order that the German workers may straightaway use, as so many weapons against the bourgeoisie, the social and political conditions that the bourgeoisie must necessarily introduce along with its supremacy, and in order that, after the fall of the reactionary classes in Germany, the fight against the bourgeoisie itself may immediately begin. [(007-006)]

This two-stage strategy — support the bourgeois revolution against absolutism, then immediately pivot to the proletarian revolution — was the strategic framework through which Marx and Engels analysed every theatre of the 1848 upheavals.

The Manifesto's internationalist thesis — that revolution in one country is inseparable from revolution across the continent — was confirmed by the extraordinary simultaneity of the 1848 upheavals. Within a single month (February–March 1848), regimes fell or were shaken in Paris, Vienna, Berlin, Milan, Venice, Budapest, and Prague. The "world market" that the Manifesto analyzed as the material basis of bourgeois internationalism had produced a correspondingly international revolutionary crisis:

> The need of a constantly expanding market for its products chases the bourgeoisie over the whole surface of the globe. It must nestle everywhere, settle everywhere, establish connexions everywhere. [(001-018)]

The same world market that unified the bourgeoisie across national borders also unified its antagonist. "The working men have no country" [(002-052)] was not a sentimental slogan; it was a strategic deduction from the structure of [[capital|capitalist]] production itself. The 1848 revolutions demonstrated this truth empirically — revolution in Paris triggered revolution in Vienna, which triggered revolution in Berlin, which triggered revolution in Milan. The "spectre" common to all of them, as the Manifesto had declared, was [[communism|Communism]] itself [(P01-001)].

### Marx and Engels in 1848

Marx and Engels did not merely observe the revolutions from London — they participated. Returning to Germany, they established the *Neue Rheinische Zeitung* in Cologne (June 1848–May 1849), a daily newspaper that functioned as the organ of the [[communist-league|Communist League]]'s left wing within the broader democratic movement. Engels participated in the armed insurrection in Baden and the Palatinate (May–July 1849), serving as an aide-de-camp to August Willich's volunteer corps. Marx's experience of the revolutions — particularly the June Days in Paris (June 23–26, 1848), when the republican government massacred thousands of Parisian workers — would inform his subsequent analysis in *The Class Struggles in France* and *The Eighteenth Brumaire of Louis Bonaparte*, two of the most important applications of the Manifesto's theoretical framework to contemporary history.

The Manifesto's closing declaration — "The Communists disdain to conceal their views and aims. They openly declare that their ends can be attained only by the forcible overthrow of all existing social conditions" [(007-011)] — was no longer a theoretical proposition. It was being tested in the crucible of revolutionary practice, and Marx and Engels placed themselves inside that crucible.

## The Course of the Revolutions

### France: February Revolution to June Days

The revolution began in Paris on February 22–24, 1848, when street fighting toppled King Louis-Philippe and established the Second Republic. The Provisional Government included the Social-Democrat Alexandre Ledru-Rollin, the socialist [[louis-blanc|Louis Blanc]], and the worker Albert — the first time in European history that representatives of the organized working class held governmental power. The government proclaimed universal male suffrage and the "right to work," establishing National Workshops for the unemployed.

The June Days (June 23–26, 1848) shattered this alliance. When the government moved to close the National Workshops — which had become centres of working-class organization — the workers of eastern Paris rose in insurrection. General Louis-Eugene Cavaignac suppressed the uprising with extreme violence: approximately 3,000 insurgents were killed in the fighting, and another 15,000 were arrested or deported to Algeria. The "social republic" had revealed itself as a bourgeois republic willing to massacre workers to defend property.

In December 1848, Louis-Napoleon Bonaparte — the nephew of Napoleon I, running on a platform of order, property, and the Napoleonic legend — was elected President with 74% of the vote. In December 1851, he staged a coup d'etat, dissolving the National Assembly and establishing the Second Empire with himself as Emperor Napoleon III. Marx's *Eighteenth Brumaire* (1852) would analyze this trajectory — revolution to reaction, republic to empire — as the logical outcome of the class contradictions laid bare in June 1848.

### The German States: March Revolution and Frankfurt Parliament

The German revolutions began in the Grand Duchy of Baden in late February and spread to Vienna (March 13), Berlin (March 18), and across the smaller German states. Everywhere, the pattern was similar: popular uprisings forced concessions (constitutions, press freedom, the promise of national unification), the old regimes regrouped, the liberal bourgeoisie proved unwilling to mobilize the masses against reaction, and the counter-revolution triumphed. The Frankfurt Parliament (May 1848–May 1849) — the elected national assembly that was to draft a liberal constitution for a united Germany — debated while the counter-revolution gathered strength; when it finally offered a German crown to the Prussian king Friedrich Wilhelm IV, he contemptuously refused it as a "crown from the gutter."

This trajectory confirmed the Manifesto's analysis that the German bourgeoisie, facing a more developed [[proletariat|proletariat]] than its English or French predecessors had confronted, would prove incapable of carrying its own revolution through. The Manifesto had argued that "the bourgeois revolution in Germany will be but the prelude to an immediately following proletarian revolution" [(007-007)] — but the immediate outcome was defeat on both fronts. The bourgeoisie could not win without the proletariat, but it feared the proletariat more than it feared absolutism.

### The Austrian Empire and Italy

In the Habsburg domains, the revolutions combined liberal constitutional demands with national aspirations: Hungarians under Lajos Kossuth, Czechs under Frantisek Palacky, and Italians in Lombardy-Venetia all rose against Austrian rule. The Empire survived through the military intervention of its most reactionary elements — Field Marshal Radetzky in Italy, General Windischgratz in Prague and Vienna — and through the manipulation of national antagonisms (Croats against Hungarians, for example). The Hungarian revolution was finally crushed in August 1849 with the assistance of Russian troops, dispatched by Tsar Nicholas I at the Austrian emperor's request.

## Aftermath and Significance

By 1850, the revolutions had been defeated across the continent. The old regimes had survived, but the post-1815 conservative order had been irreparably damaged. Serfdom was abolished in the Austrian Empire (though the peasants remained bound by other obligations); constitutional government, however limited, had been introduced in Prussia and several German states; universal male suffrage, proclaimed in France in 1848, was not entirely extinguished even under Bonapartism.

For Marx and Engels, the 1848 revolutions provided the empirical material for the most important developments of their political theory in the 1850s and beyond. Several key conclusions emerged:

1. **The bourgeoisie ceases to be revolutionary.** Where the Manifesto had analyzed the bourgeoisie's historical trajectory from revolutionary to conservative, 1848 demonstrated it: the liberal bourgeoisie, once in power or even close to it, preferred alliance with the old regime to alliance with the proletariat.

2. **The peasantry as a political force.** The French peasantry's massive vote for Louis-Napoleon in December 1848 forced Marx to theorize the peasantry's political role — as a class "much as potatoes in a sack form a sack of potatoes" (*Eighteenth Brumaire*), incapable of representing itself but decisive in shifting the balance between bourgeoisie and proletariat.

3. **The state as an independent actor.** Bonapartism — a form of state that appears to stand above all classes while in reality serving the bourgeoisie — became, through Marx's analysis of 1848–1851, a permanent category of Marxist political theory.

4. **Permanent revolution.** The Manifesto's thesis that "the bourgeois revolution in Germany will be but the prelude to an immediately following proletarian revolution" [(007-006)] was the germ of the theory of *permanent revolution* — the idea that in countries where the bourgeoisie cannot carry its own democratic revolution to completion, the proletariat must take the lead in that revolution and, in the process, pass directly to socialist measures. This thesis, adumbrated by Marx in his 1850 "Address of the Central Committee to the Communist League," would be systematically developed by Trotsky in *Results and Prospects* (1906) and become one of the central strategic debates of 20th-century Marxism.

## The 1848 Revolutions and the Manifesto's Theory of History

### Verification of the Class Struggle Thesis

The Manifesto opens with the declaration that:

> The history of all hitherto existing societies is the history of class struggles. [(001-001)]

The 1848 revolutions were a spectacular empirical verification of this thesis across an entire continent, in compressed time. In France, the February Revolution pitted the republican bourgeoisie and the proletariat against the financial aristocracy of the July Monarchy; the June Days then pitted the republican bourgeoisie against the proletariat; the December election pitted the peasantry (as a decisive electoral bloc) against the republic that had massacred the workers. Each phase revealed a different configuration of the class struggle, and each configuration moved history forward — not linearly, but dialectically, through negation and counter-negation.

The Manifesto had described the bourgeoisie's revolutionary trajectory with precision:

> The bourgeoisie, historically, has played a most revolutionary part. [(001-012)]

But it had also identified the terminus of that trajectory. "The weapons with which the bourgeoisie felled feudalism to the ground are now turned against the bourgeoisie itself" [(001-026)], and "what the bourgeoisie, therefore, produces, above all, is its own grave-diggers. Its fall and the victory of the proletariat are equally inevitable" [(001-050)]. The 1848 revolutions demonstrated the first half of this dialectic — the bourgeoisie turning its weapons against feudalism — but they also demonstrated something the Manifesto had not fully theorized: the bourgeoisie's capacity to turn back, to abandon its own revolution rather than risk empowering the proletariat. This was not a refutation of the Manifesto's analysis so much as a complication of its timeline.

### The International Character of Revolution

The Manifesto's internationalism was confirmed not only by the simultaneity of the 1848 revolutions but also by their interconnectedness. The revolution that began in Paris in February reached Vienna in March, Berlin in March, Milan in March, Budapest in March, Prague in June. The counter-revolution followed the same transnational logic: the defeat of the Paris workers in June 1848 emboldened reaction everywhere; the Russian intervention that crushed the Hungarian revolution in 1849 was the final act of a continental drama. "In short," as the Manifesto's Section IV summarized, "the Communists everywhere support every revolutionary movement against the existing social and political order of things" [(007-008)]. The 1848 revolutions had demonstrated, on a continental scale, that every such movement was part of a single historical process.

### Lessons for Party Building

The failure of the 1848 revolutions taught Marx and Engels that the spontaneous uprising of the masses, however heroic, was insufficient without an organized political party capable of providing strategic direction. This lesson was already inscribed in the Manifesto's conception of the Communists as "the most advanced and resolute section of the working-class parties of every country" [(002-006)], but 1848 gave it the weight of experience. The [[communist-league|Communist League]]'s small membership — a few hundred at most — had been unable to exercise decisive influence over the mass movements that erupted in 1848. The lesson Marx and Engels drew was not that the party was unnecessary, but that it must be built to a scale adequate to the tasks history would set before it.

## Further Reading

- Karl Marx, *The Class Struggles in France, 1848–1850* (1850) — Contemporary analysis of the February Revolution, the June Days, and the rise of Bonapartism.
- Karl Marx, *The Eighteenth Brumaire of Louis Bonaparte* (1852) — The classic analysis of the 1848–1851 crisis in France, containing the theory of Bonapartism and the famous opening line: "Hegel remarks somewhere that all great world-historic facts and personages appear, so to speak, twice. He forgot to add: the first time as tragedy, the second time as farce."
- [[friedrich-engels|Friedrich Engels]], *Revolution and Counter-Revolution in Germany* (1851–1852) — Engels' narrative history of the German 1848 revolution, originally published under Marx's name in the *New York Daily Tribune*.
- Eric Hobsbawm, *The Age of Revolution, 1789–1848* (1962) and *The Age of Capital, 1848–1875* (1975) — Indispensable synthetic histories of the 1848 watershed.
- Mike Rapport, *1848: Year of Revolution* (2008) — The best modern single-volume history of the 1848 revolutions.
- Jonathan Sperber, *The European Revolutions, 1848–1851* (2nd ed., 2005) — Standard scholarly textbook on the revolutionary conjuncture.
