> Sismondi was the head of this school, not only in France but also in England. This school of Socialism dissected with great acuteness the contradictions in the conditions of modern production. It laid bare the hypocritical apologies of economists. [(004-016–004-017)]

**Jean Charles Léonard Simonde de Sismondi** (1773–1842) was a Swiss historian and political economist whose critique of industrial capitalism made him, in the assessment of [[karl-marx|Marx]] and [[friedrich-engels|Engels]], the intellectual leader of petty-bourgeois socialism — a school that "dissected with great acuteness the contradictions in the conditions of modern production" yet whose positive programme was both "reactionary and Utopian" (004-018).

## In the Manifesto

### Ch. III §1a: Petty-Bourgeois Socialism

Sismondi appears in the Manifesto as the pivotal figure in the section on "Petty-Bourgeois Socialism" — the second of the three reactionary socialist tendencies critiqued in Ch. III. Marx and Engels locate his intellectual significance not merely in his individual writings but in his representativeness: he is the spokesman of a class — the [[petty-bourgeoisie|petty bourgeoisie]] — that is being destroyed by capitalist development and that reacts by criticizing capitalism from the standpoint of the past rather than the future.

The Manifesto's assessment of Sismondi is dialectically precise. On the one hand, his school "dissected with great acuteness the contradictions in the conditions of modern production": the disastrous effects of machinery and the division of labour, the concentration of capital and land, overproduction and commercial crises, the ruin of the petty bourgeois and peasant, the misery of the proletariat, the anarchy of production, the inequality of wealth, the dissolution of old moral bonds and family relations (004-017). This is a formidable catalogue of indictment — one that in many respects anticipates Marx's own critique in *Capital*.

On the other hand, the school's positive programme is fatally limited. "In its positive aims, however, this form of Socialism aspires either to restoring the old means of production and of exchange, and with them the old property relations, and the old society, or to cramping the modern means of production and of exchange within the framework of the old property relations" (004-018). The Manifesto's verdict is final: "In either case, it is both reactionary and Utopian. Its last words are: corporate guilds for manufacture, patriarchal relations in agriculture" (004-018–004-019).

The class logic is clear. Sismondi represented a class — the small proprietors, peasants, and artisans — whose conditions of existence were being annihilated by capitalist development (004-014–004-015). From the standpoint of this class, he could see what the bourgeois economists could not: that capitalist production was inherently crisis-prone, that competition destroyed what it touched, that the market's "invisible hand" was in fact a fist. But because his standpoint was that of a dying class, his solutions could only look backward — to guilds, to patriarchal agriculture, to a world that capitalist development had already dissolved beyond recall.

## Historical Context

### From Adam Smith's Disciple to Capitalism's Critic

Sismondi began his intellectual career as an orthodox follower of Adam Smith. His *De la richesse commerciale* (1803) was a faithful exposition of Smithian free-trade doctrine. But the Napoleonic Wars, the post-war economic crises, and his own empirical research into the conditions of English and continental workers drove him to a radical break with classical political economy.

The turning point was his *Nouveaux principes d'économie politique* (1819), a work that directly challenged the optimistic assumptions of Smith, Ricardo, and Jean-Baptiste Say. Where classical economics saw equilibrium, Sismondi saw disequilibrium; where it saw harmony, he saw crisis; where it saw progress, he saw immiseration. His central theoretical innovation was a theory of underconsumption: capitalist production, he argued, tends to outrun the purchasing power of the masses, generating periodic crises of overproduction. The market does not automatically clear — it periodically chokes on its own abundance while workers starve for lack of wages to buy the goods they have produced.

This was a genuinely original insight. Marx would later develop a far more systematic crisis theory grounded in the law of the tendency of the rate of profit to fall, but he acknowledged Sismondi's priority in identifying the contradiction between production and consumption under capitalism. The Manifesto's own analysis of commercial crises — "epidemic of over-production" in which "society suddenly finds itself put back into a state of momentary barbarism" (001-025) — echoes Sismondi's language and diagnosis.

### The Historian of Liberty

Sismondi was also a major historian. His *Histoire des républiques italiennes du Moyen Âge* (1807–1818), in sixteen volumes, was a pioneering work of medieval history that traced the rise of the Italian city-republics and their decline into tyranny. His *Histoire des Français* (1821–1844), running to thirty-one volumes, was the most comprehensive narrative history of France produced in the early nineteenth century.

These historical works informed his economic critique. Sismondi's study of the Italian republics convinced him that civic liberty depended on the widespread distribution of property — a classical republican thesis that he transposed onto modern commercial society. The concentration of wealth that industrial capitalism was producing was not merely an economic problem, in his view, but a political catastrophe: it was destroying the material basis of citizenship itself.

### Sismondi and Marx: Appropriation and Transcendence

Marx's relationship to Sismondi was one of critical appropriation. He recognized Sismondi as a genuine critic of capitalism whose insights — on crises, on immiseration, on the contradiction between production and consumption — were far superior to the complacent apologetics of the "vulgar economists" who followed Ricardo. But he rejected Sismondi's solution as fundamentally reactionary. The answer to capitalist crisis was not to return to small-scale production — which capitalism was destroying by its own inner logic — but to push through capitalism to its revolutionary negation. Sismondi wanted to save the petty bourgeoisie from the bourgeoisie; Marx wanted the proletariat to abolish both.

## Related Figures

Sismondi stands at the head of a tradition of anti-capitalist criticism from a pre-capitalist standpoint. His intellectual descendants include the Russian Narodniks, who argued that Russia could bypass capitalism by building on the peasant commune — a position Marx engaged with seriously in his later years. Within the Manifesto's typology, Sismondi's petty-bourgeois socialism is flanked on the right by [[feudal-socialism|feudal socialism]] (the aristocrats who criticize the bourgeoisie from above) and on the left by the critical-utopian socialism of [[henri-de-saint-simon|Saint-Simon]], [[charles-fourier|Fourier]], and [[robert-owen|Owen]] — who, unlike Sismondi, at least envision a new social order rather than a restoration of the old. The [[petty-bourgeoisie|petty bourgeoisie]] as a class provides the social basis for Sismondi's politics: "fluctuating between proletariat and bourgeoisie," its members "are being constantly hurled down into the proletariat by the action of competition" (004-015).

## Key Works

- *De la richesse commerciale* (1803) — Early Smithian phase
- *Histoire des républiques italiennes du Moyen Âge* (1807–1818, 16 vols.) — Historical masterpiece
- *Nouveaux principes d'économie politique* (1819) — Break with classical economics; underconsumption theory
- *Études sur l'économie politique* (1837–1838) — Later synthesis of historical and economic analysis
- *Histoire des Français* (1821–1844, 31 vols.) — Comprehensive national history
