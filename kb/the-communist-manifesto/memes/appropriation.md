**Appropriation** is the act of taking possession of the products of labour — a concept that, in the Manifesto, marks the crucial distinction between two fundamentally different social relations. "Communism deprives no man of the power to appropriate the products of society; all that it does is to deprive him of the power to subjugate the labour of others by means of such appropriation" [(002-031)].

## Definition

In Marx and Engels' usage, appropriation has a double meaning. On one hand, it designates the universal human activity by which people transform nature and make it their own — the "personal appropriation of the products of labour, an appropriation that is made for the maintenance and reproduction of human life" [(002-021)]. This form of appropriation leaves no surplus beyond what is necessary for subsistence; it is the labourer's direct claim on what they have produced.

On the other hand, appropriation designates the specifically capitalist form by which one class seizes the surplus product of another. Under capitalism, the capitalist appropriates the surplus value created by the worker — a form of appropriation that "leaves a surplus" [(002-021)] and thereby enables accumulation, investment, and the reproduction of class relations on an expanded scale.

The distinction is politically decisive. The Manifesto's Communism aims to abolish only the second form of appropriation — the appropriation of others' labour — while preserving the first — the direct appropriation of what one has produced. It is the bourgeois critics who conflate the two, accusing Communists of wanting to abolish all property, all appropriation, all individuality.

## In the Manifesto

### Historical Modes of Appropriation

The Manifesto situates appropriation within a historical sequence. "All the preceding classes that got the upper hand, sought to fortify their already acquired status by subjecting society at large to their conditions of appropriation" [(001-045)]. Each ruling class establishes a mode of appropriation suited to its interests — feudal dues and tithes for the lords, wage-labour and surplus value for the bourgeoisie — and then represents this historically specific form as eternal and natural.

The proletariat, however, differs from all preceding revolutionary classes. It does not seek to install a new mode of appropriation for itself; it seeks to abolish class appropriation altogether. "The proletarians cannot become masters of the productive forces of society, except by abolishing their own previous mode of appropriation, and thereby also every other previous mode of appropriation" [(001-045)]. The proletariat has "nothing of their own to secure and to fortify; their mission is to destroy all previous securities for, and insurances of, individual property" [(001-045)].

### Personal vs. Capitalist Appropriation

In Section II, Marx and Engels address head-on the bourgeois charge that Communism would abolish all property — and therefore all appropriation. The response turns on a distinction that the bourgeoisie systematically obscures: "We by no means intend to abolish this personal appropriation of the products of labour, an appropriation that is made for the maintenance and reproduction of human life, and that leaves no surplus wherewith to command the labour of others" [(002-021)].

This "personal appropriation" is the direct, subsistence-oriented claim of the producer on their product. It is what every worker needs to survive. What Communism intends to abolish is something quite different: the appropriation of surplus labour, the power of one class "to subjugate the labour of others by means of such appropriation" [(002-031)].

The wage relation is, in this light, a specific historical form of appropriation. The worker receives the minimum necessary for subsistence — "the average price of wage-labour is the minimum wage" [(002-021)] — while the capitalist appropriates the remainder. This is not theft in the legal sense; it is the normal functioning of bourgeois property relations. But it is appropriation of others' labour nonetheless, and it is this that Communism proposes to end.

### Appropriation and the State

The Manifesto's political programme culminates in the appropriation of the means of production by the proletariat organised as the ruling class. The transitional measures include: "Centralisation of the means of communication and transport in the hands of the State" and "Extension of factories and instruments of production owned by the State" [(002-078)]. This is appropriation on a society-wide scale — the transfer of the means of production from private hands to collective control.

But this appropriation is not an end in itself. It is a transitional step towards the abolition of class society, after which "the public power will lose its political character" [(002-082)] and appropriation will cease to be a relation of domination and become simply the collective management of shared resources.

## Historical Context

The concept of appropriation has roots in the legal language of property — particularly Roman law and its distinction between *possessio* (possession) and *proprietas* (ownership). Marx and Engels draw on this legal vocabulary while giving it a materialist reinterpretation. Appropriation is not primarily a juridical act but a productive relation: the real process by which the products of labour are distributed, consumed, and accumulated.

The Manifesto's discussion of appropriation also engages with the socialist and communist literature of the 1840s, particularly Proudhon's famous slogan "Property is theft." The Manifesto's position is more nuanced: it is not property as such that is theft but the specific form of bourgeois property that enables the appropriation of others' labour. "The distinguishing feature of Communism is not the abolition of property generally, but the abolition of bourgeois property" [(002-011)].
