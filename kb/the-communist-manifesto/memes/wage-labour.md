**Wage-labour** is the axis around which Marx and Engels' critique of capitalism turns. If the [[bourgeoisie]] is defined by its ownership of the [[means-of-production|means of production]], and the [[proletariat]] by its propertylessness, then wage-labour is the mechanism that binds them together — the form in which living labour is purchased, consumed, and transformed into the [[capital]] that dominates it.

> The essential condition for the existence, and for the sway of the bourgeois class, is the formation and augmentation of capital; the condition for capital is wage-labour. Wage-labour rests exclusively on competition between the labourers. [(001-050)]

From the opening chapter, the Manifesto frames this dynamic: as capital develops, so does the modern working class, producing "a class of labourers, who live only so long as they find work, and who find work only so long as their labour increases capital" [(001-028)]. The wage therefore names not a fair bargain but a structural subjugation.

## Definition

In the Manifesto, wage-labour is defined as the commodity-form of labour-power under capitalism. The proletarian, owning no means of production, is compelled to sell their capacity to work to the capitalist for a wage. This wage is not the equivalent of the value the worker produces — it is "the minimum wage, i.e., that quantum of the means of subsistence which is absolutely requisite to keep the labourer in bare existence as a labourer" [(002-021)]. The difference between the value produced and the wage received is surplus value — the source of capitalist profit.

What distinguishes wage-labour from earlier forms of unfree labour (chattel slavery, [[serfdom]]) is its formal freedom. The wage-labourer is juridically free to choose which capitalist to work for, and the wage contract appears as an exchange of equivalents. But this freedom is, in substance, the freedom to starve if one refuses to sell one's labour-power.

> No sooner is the exploitation of the labourer by the manufacturer, so far at an end, that he receives his wages in cash, than he is set upon by the other portions of the bourgeoisie, the landlord, the shopkeeper, the pawnbroker, etc. [(001-032)]

The wage is not a reward for labour but its cost of reproduction. The Manifesto drives this home with one of its sharpest formulations: those who work acquire nothing, and those who acquire anything do not work [(002-033)]. The wage, far from being the worker's property, is the price of their own reproduction as a factor of production — and the product they create becomes [[private-property]] held by the [[bourgeoisie]].

### The Wage as a Commodity Price

The Manifesto treats labour-power as a commodity whose price obeys the same laws as any other commodity. "The price of a commodity, and therefore also of labour, is equal to its cost of production. In proportion, therefore, as the repulsiveness of the work increases, the wage decreases" [(001-029)]. This is the logic of the [[market]] generalised to human beings: the [[competition]] among workers to sell their labour-power drives wages down toward bare subsistence, while the introduction of [[machinery]] and the [[division-of-labour]] strips work of all individual character, erasing whatever bargaining power skilled artisans once possessed.

### The Illusion of the Free Contract

The wage contract presents itself as a free exchange between equals. The Manifesto dismantles this appearance by showing that the worker's "freedom" is the freedom of the propertyless: the proletarian must sell labour-power to survive, and the capitalist must purchase it to accumulate. This is not a symmetrical dependence.

> In bourgeois society, living labour is but a means to increase accumulated labour. In Communist society, accumulated labour is but a means to widen, to enrich, to promote the existence of the labourer. [(002-022)]

The wage relation thus effects a fundamental inversion — what should be a means (accumulated labour, i.e., [[capital]]) becomes the end, and what should be the end (living labour, i.e., human beings) becomes a means.

## In the Manifesto

Wage-labour is analysed in the Manifesto primarily in Section II, where Marx and Engels deploy it to answer the central objection against [[communism]]: that the abolition of private property would destroy the incentive to work and produce generalised "laziness." The entire Section II pivots on showing that wage-labour is not a natural or eternal form of work but a historically specific institution — one that benefits only the capitalist and can therefore be abolished without fear of universal idleness.

> It has been objected that upon the abolition of private property all work will cease, and universal laziness will overtake us. According to this, bourgeois society ought long ago to have gone to the dogs through sheer idleness; for those of its members who work, acquire nothing, and those who acquire anything, do not work. [(002-032)] [(002-033)]

### Wage-Labour Does Not Create Property for the Labourer

The pivotal argument unfolds in a series of rhetorical questions:

> But does wage-labour create any property for the labourer? Not a bit. It creates capital, i.e., that kind of property which exploits wage-labour, and which cannot increase except upon condition of begetting a new supply of wage-labour for fresh exploitation. [(002-016)]

This formulation reveals the dialectical character of wage-labour: the worker, through their own labour, produces the instrument of their own subjugation. [[capital|Capital]] is not an independent force that confronts labour from outside — it is "accumulated labour" [(002-020)], the congealed product of past wage-labour that now stands opposed to living labour. The consequence is radical: "In bourgeois society, living labour is but a means to increase accumulated labour. In Communist society, accumulated labour is but a means to widen, to enrich, to promote the existence of the labourer" [(002-022)]. The entire critique turns on the inversion that wage-labour effects.

### The Minimum Wage and the Immiseration Thesis

The Manifesto asserts a tendential law of wages under capitalism:

> The average price of wage-labour is the minimum wage, i.e., that quantum of the means of subsistence, which is absolutely requisite in bare existence as a labourer. [(002-021)]

This is not a claim that wages never rise above subsistence — historical evidence from the Factory Acts era shows they occasionally did — but rather a structural claim about the logic of the wage system. [[competition|Competition]] between workers, reinforced by the reserve army of the unemployed and the expansion of the labour market through [[machinery]], exerts a constant downward pressure on wages. "The growing competition among the bourgeois, and the resulting commercial crises, make the wages of the workers ever more fluctuating" [(001-036)].

This immiseration thesis — that capitalism tends to reduce the [[proletariat]] to subsistence, and that periodic [[crisis|crises]] throw sections of the working class back into deeper poverty — is one of the Manifesto's most debated claims. But its function in the text is clear: it demonstrates that the interests of wage-labour and [[capital]] are irreconcilably antagonistic. The improvement of the proletariat's condition within capitalism can never be more than temporary and partial. Even skilled and well-paid sections of the working class "sink gradually into the proletariat" [(001-049)] as [[modern-industry]] advances.

### The Abolition of Wage-Labour

[[communism|Communism]], in the Manifesto's programme, aims not to redistribute wages more fairly but to abolish wage-labour as such.

> When, in the course of development, class distinctions have disappeared, and all production has been concentrated in the hands of a vast association of the whole nation, the public power will lose its political character. [(002-064)]

The abolition of wage-labour is the abolition of the [[proletariat]]'s condition as a class — the end of the division of society into those who must sell their labour to live and those who buy it to accumulate. The Manifesto argues that this abolition is not a utopian demand but the logical outcome of capitalism's own development: the concentration of production in ever fewer hands, the growth of the working class, and the recurrent crises that demonstrate the irrationality of a system that produces too much while workers go without.

## Historical Context

The concept of wage-labour in the Manifesto synthesises the labour theory of value inherited from **classical political economy** with the socialist critique of wage-slavery. Adam Smith had argued that labour is the "real measure of the exchangeable value of all commodities"; David Ricardo had demonstrated that the price of labour, like any commodity, tends toward its natural price — the cost of subsistence. The Ricardian socialists (Thomas Hodgskin, John Bray, John Francis Bray) had drawn the socialist conclusion: if labour is the source of all value, then profit, interest, and rent are deductions from the worker's product.

Marx and Engels went further. For them, wage-labour was not merely an unjust distribution of the product but a specific historical form of labour itself — one that arose with the separation of the producer from the [[means-of-production]] ("primitive accumulation") and that would be superseded when the [[proletariat]] reappropriated those means collectively. The Manifesto is the first published text in which this theory, later developed at length in *Capital* and the *Grundrisse*, is presented in a politically programmatic form.

### Predecessors and Contrasts

Wage-labour in 1848 was neither universal nor uncontested. [[serfdom|Serfdom]] still existed in parts of Eastern Europe; chattel slavery persisted in the Americas; guild-based [[manufacture]] had only recently given way to industrial wage-work in much of Western Europe. The Manifesto's insistence that wage-labour was already becoming the dominant form of social labour was itself a political claim — one that identified the wage-relation as the specific site of capitalist [[exploitation]], distinct from the personal bonds of [[feudalism]] or the direct coercion of chattel slavery.

> The bourgeoisie has stripped of its halo every occupation hitherto honoured and looked up to with reverent awe. It has converted the physician, the lawyer, the priest, the poet, the man of science, into its paid wage labourers. [(001-014)]

By showing that even the "liberal professions" were being drawn into the wage nexus, Marx and Engels universalised wage-labour as the signature social relation of the epoch — not merely the fate of factory hands but the destiny of all labour under bourgeois society.

## Critique and Significance

The category of wage-labour in the Manifesto has been the subject of extensive debate within the Marxist tradition and beyond. Three lines of critique and development are especially notable.

### The Distinction Between Labour and Labour-Power

The Manifesto uses "wage-labour" to name both the activity of working and the commodity sold by the worker — a conflation that Marx himself would later correct. In *Capital* and the *Grundrisse*, Marx draws a sharp distinction between **labour** (the actual expenditure of human energy in production) and **labour-power** (the capacity to labour, which the worker sells as a commodity). The wage, strictly speaking, purchases labour-power, not labour — and the difference between the value of labour-power (the cost of its reproduction) and the value created by labour is the source of surplus value. The Manifesto's compressed formulation anticipates this distinction without yet making it explicit, relying instead on the vivid paradox that wage-labour "creates capital" [(002-016)] — a formulation whose full theoretical elaboration required the mature critique of political economy.

### The Immiseration Debate

The claim that "the average price of wage-labour is the minimum wage" [(002-021)] has been one of the most contested elements of the Manifesto's analysis. Critics from Eduard Bernstein onward have argued that the historical record demonstrates rising real wages under capitalism, not the progressive immiseration the Manifesto appears to predict. Defenders note that the thesis is tendential rather than absolute — it describes a structural pressure, not a monotonic decline — and that Marx and Engels themselves acknowledged in later writings that workers could win temporary improvements through trade-union organisation and political struggle. As the Manifesto itself observes, proletarians "compel legislative recognition of particular interests of the workers, by taking advantage of the divisions among the bourgeoisie itself" [(001-039)]. The immiseration thesis functions in the text less as an empirical forecast than as a demonstration that the logic of the wage-system is antagonistic to the interests of the wage-earner: any improvement is conditional, reversible, and won against the grain of the system.

### The Enduring Diagnostic Power

Despite its polemical brevity, the Manifesto's analysis of wage-labour retains diagnostic power. The concept identifies a structural feature of capitalism — the separation of the producer from the [[means-of-production]] and the consequent reduction of human labour to a commodity — that remains operative regardless of whether wages are at subsistence or above it. The wage-relation, in this reading, is not defined by the absolute level of remuneration but by the fact that the worker does not control the product of their labour, the conditions of their labour, or the purpose to which their labour is directed. The line from "physician, lawyer, priest, poet, man of science" being "converted into paid wage labourers" [(001-014)] points forward to contemporary analyses of the commodification of care work, creative labour, and knowledge production — domains that 1848 still associated with honoured professions but that capital has progressively subsumed under the logic of the wage.
