# Credit

## Definition

Credit, in the Manifesto, is not theorised as an abstract economic category but is treated as a strategic instrument of class power. Marx and Engels address credit only in the ten-point transitional programme of Ch. II, where they demand: "Centralisation of credit in the hands of the State, by means of a national bank with State capital and an exclusive monopoly" (002-076). The concept thus belongs to the revolutionary programme rather than to the historical analysis of bourgeois society. Credit appears as a circulatory mechanism of capitalism that must be seized and monopolised by the proletarian State — not merely regulated but wholly appropriated — to deprive the bourgeoisie of the financial leverage that sustains its economic dominance.

## In the Manifesto

The demand for the centralisation of credit stands at item five in the ten measures proposed for "the most advanced countries" (002-071). It is positioned between the confiscatory measures of items one through four — abolition of property in land, a heavy progressive income tax, abolition of inheritance, and confiscation of emigrant and rebel property (002-072–002-075) — and the infrastructural demands of items six through ten — centralisation of transport, extension of state-owned factories, equal liability to labour, combination of agriculture and industry, and free education (002-076–002-081).

This placement is deliberate. The first four measures attack the private ownership of land and capital at the level of individual property rights; the centralisation of credit attacks the *systemic* apparatus that enables capital to circulate, accumulate, and concentrate. A national bank with an "exclusive monopoly" means the abolition of private banking — the bourgeoisie can no longer allocate credit according to the logic of profit, and the State becomes the sole source of investment capital. Marx and Engels understood that whoever controls the credit system controls the direction of economic development.

The phrase "State capital" is significant: the national bank is not an empty shell but is capitalised with public resources, giving it the material capacity to direct investment. The "exclusive monopoly" clause forecloses any parallel private credit system that could compete with or undermine the State's financial authority.

The strategic logic behind this demand is articulated earlier in Ch. II: "The proletariat will use its political supremacy to wrest, by degrees, all capital from the bourgeoisie, to centralise all instruments of production in the hands of the State" (002-068). Credit centralisation is one means — a critical means — by which this gradual expropriation is to be accomplished. By controlling the flow of credit, the proletarian State can starve bourgeois enterprises of investment while directing capital toward socially necessary production, thereby "increasing the total of productive forces as rapidly as possible" (002-068).

## Historical Context

The demand for a State monopoly over credit was not invented by Marx and Engels. In the 1840s, debates over banking reform and credit policy were central to radical politics across Europe. In France, the socialist theorist Pierre-Joseph Proudhon had proposed a "People's Bank" that would issue interest-free credit to workers — a scheme Marx would later savage in *The Poverty of Philosophy* (1847) for its failure to grasp that credit reform alone, without the abolition of capitalist property relations, could not emancipate the working class. The Manifesto's programme, by contrast, treats credit centralisation not as an end in itself but as one instrument within a comprehensive programme of revolutionary transformation.

The model of a State bank with exclusive issuing privileges had historical precedents. The Bank of England, founded in 1694, was a private institution that functioned as the State's banker and gradually acquired a de facto monopoly on note issuance. Marx and Engels inverted this model: instead of a private bank serving the State on terms favourable to finance capital, they proposed a public bank that would subordinate finance to the needs of the associated producers. The "exclusive monopoly" clause was a direct rebuttal to the existing arrangement in which private banking houses — the Rothschilds, the Barings — exercised enormous influence over State policy through their control of public credit.

The broader intellectual context was the Saint-Simonian critique of the "idle" financier class. The Saint-Simonians had argued that the credit system should be reorganised so that capital flowed to productive industry rather than to speculative rentiers. Marx and Engels absorbed this critique but gave it a class content: the credit system, like all economic institutions under capitalism, served the bourgeoisie; only the revolutionary expropriation of the bourgeoisie could transform it into an instrument of social production.

## Related Concepts

Credit centralisation cannot be understood in isolation from [[centralisation]], which provides the overarching strategic framework. The Manifesto's programme calls for centralisation across multiple domains — political, economic, financial — and credit is the financial dimension of this broader process. The [[state]] is the institutional agent that carries out the centralisation: it is "the proletariat organised as the ruling class" (002-082) that monopolises credit. [[capital]] names what credit mobilises and circulates under capitalism, and what the proletariat must wrest from bourgeois control. [[political-power]] is the precondition — without the political supremacy won through [[proletarian-revolution]], the centralisation of credit is unachievable.
