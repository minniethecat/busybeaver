## Definition

The **petty bourgeoisie** (or *petite bourgeoisie*) designates the class of small-scale property owners who work with their own means of production — shopkeepers, self-employed artisans, small peasants, and independent tradespeople — and who occupy an intermediate position between the two great contending classes of capitalist society: the bourgeoisie and the proletariat. Unlike the full bourgeoisie, the petty bourgeois does not employ significant wage-labour and typically participates directly in production. Unlike the proletarian, the petty bourgeois retains nominal ownership of tools, stock, or land. Marx and Engels treat this class as historically transitional: its lower strata are continuously being proletarianised by the competitive pressure of large capital, while its upper strata may, in exceptional cases, rise into the bourgeoisie.

## In the Manifesto

The petty bourgeoisie appears in the Manifesto primarily as a class being destroyed by capitalism — its members are not a revolutionary force but the raw material from which the proletariat is replenished. Marx and Engels locate the petty bourgeoisie within the historical trajectory that began with the guild-master and now ends with the unpropertied wage-worker:

> "The lower strata of the middle class — the small tradespeople, shopkeepers, and retired tradesmen generally, the handicraftsmen and peasants — all these sink gradually into the proletariat, partly because their diminutive capital does not suffice for the scale on which modern industry is carried on, and is swamped in the competition with the large capitalists, partly because their specialised skill is rendered worthless by new methods of production." [(001-033)]

This passage makes two distinct arguments about proletarianisation: quantitative (small capital cannot match the scale of modern industry) and qualitative (craft skill, the petty bourgeois's core asset, is deskilled by mechanisation). Both forces operate simultaneously and reinforce each other.

The Manifesto returns to this theme in a later passage that describes the petty bourgeois as caught between two hostile forces:

> "The lower middle class, the small manufacturer, the shopkeeper, the artisan, the peasant, all these fight against the bourgeoisie, to save from extinction their existence as fractions of the middle class. They are therefore not revolutionary, but conservative. Nay more, they are reactionary, for they try to roll back the wheel of history." [(001-042)]

This is a crucial political judgment: the petty bourgeoisie is not merely passive in history but actively *reactionary* — it resists capitalist development in the name of a past order (the guild system, the independent peasantry) rather than struggling for a future one. The Manifesto distinguishes the "non-industrial bourgeois" from the industrial proletariat precisely on this political axis:

> "The non-industrial bourgeois, the petty bourgeoisie. Thus the whole historical movement is concentrated in the hands of the bourgeoisie; every victory so obtained is a victory for the bourgeoisie." [(001-035)]

In the section on German or "True" Socialism (Chapter III), the Manifesto critiques the petty-bourgeois standpoint that claims to transcend class conflict while in fact merely expressing the material interests of a declining class:

> "In Germany the petty bourgeois class, a relique of the sixteenth century, and since then constantly cropping up again under various forms, is the real social basis of the existing state of things." [(004-034)]

## Historical Context

The Manifesto summarises this dissolution with clinical precision: "The lower strata of the middle class — the small tradespeople, shopkeepers, and retired tradesmen generally, the handicraftsmen and peasants — all these sink gradually into the proletariat" [(001-033)].

The petty bourgeoisie was, in 1848, the numerically largest propertied class in Continental Europe. France, Germany, and Switzerland contained millions of peasant proprietors, independent artisans, and small shopkeepers. The revolutions of 1848 were in significant part driven by petty-bourgeois discontent — with taxation, with the removal of guild protections, with competition from factory production. Marx and Engels' harsh political judgment on this class must be understood in this context: the Manifesto was intervening against a powerful current of petty-bourgeois radicalism (represented by figures like Louis Blanc in France and the "True Socialists" in Germany) that sought to restore small property rather than abolish private property altogether. The Manifesto's argument that the petty bourgeoisie is "reactionary" is a direct response to these movements.

## Significance

The concept of the petty bourgeoisie illuminates one of the Manifesto's central theses: that capitalism simplifies class antagonisms by polarising society into two great hostile camps. The petty bourgeoisie is, in this schema, the class that *resists* simplification — it clings to an intermediate position that the movement of capital makes increasingly untenable. Marx and Engels' political hostility to this class stems not from moral disapproval but from a structural analysis: the petty bourgeoisie's material interest in preserving private property aligns it, in the decisive moments of class struggle, with the bourgeoisie against the proletariat, even as it is also crushed by the bourgeoisie. This analysis would prove influential in later Marxist debates about the political role of the peasantry and the middle class, from the Russian Revolution through to 20th-century discussions of the "new petty bourgeoisie" of salaried professionals.
