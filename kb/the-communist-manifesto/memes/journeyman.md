The **journeyman** is one of the earliest figures of wage-dependence to appear in the Manifesto's historical schema. "Freeman and slave, patrician and plebeian, lord and serf, guild-master and journeyman, in a word, oppressor and oppressed, stood in constant opposition to one another" [(001-002)]. The opposition of guild-master and journeyman is, for Marx and Engels, the characteristic class antagonism of feudal industrial production — the urban counterpart to the agrarian antagonism of lord and serf.

## Definition

In the feudal guild system, the journeyman occupied the middle rank of a three-tier hierarchy: apprentice, journeyman, guild-master. "In the Middle Ages, feudal lords, vassals, guild-masters, journeymen, apprentices, serfs; in almost all of these classes, again, subordinate gradations" [(001-003)]. Having completed an apprenticeship, the journeyman was a skilled worker who sold his labour to a guild-master for a daily wage — hence the term "journeyman," from the French *journée* (day). He possessed the technical knowledge to produce goods independently but lacked the capital and guild membership required to own a workshop or employ others.

The journeyman thus embodies a specific historical form of exploitation: not the chattel slavery of the ancient world, nor the serfdom tied to the land, nor yet the "free" wage-labourer of capitalism who can be hired and fired at will. The journeyman was bound by guild regulations, customary wages, and the personal authority of the master — an intermediate form of subordination that combined elements of personal dependence with elements of market exchange.

## In the Manifesto

### Journeyman and Guild-Master: The Pre-Modern Class Struggle

The Manifesto opens with an assertion: the history of all society is the history of class struggles, and the struggle between guild-master and journeyman is one of its canonical forms. The antagonism is structural: the master owns the workshop, the tools, the raw materials, and controls access to the market; the journeyman possesses only his skill and his labour-power. The master appropriates a portion of the value the journeyman creates — whether through direct wage-setting or through the control of market access.

But this antagonism operates within the confined space of the guild town. It is local, limited in scale, and constrained by customary relationships that the Manifesto sees as pre-modern. The feudal system bound master and journeyman together in a web of mutual obligation that simultaneously expressed and masked exploitation. It took the bourgeoisie, with its "icy water of egotistical calculation" [(001-014)], to strip away these veils and reveal the naked cash nexus beneath.

### The Displacement of the Journeyman

The rise of manufacture and modern industry destroyed the journeyman's world. "The guild-masters were pushed on one side by the manufacturing middle class; division of labour between the different corporate guilds vanished in the face of division of labour in each single workshop" [(001-007)]. The manufacturing system concentrated production, simplified tasks through the [[division-of-labour]], and reduced the skilled journeyman to a mere "appendage of the machine" [(001-021)].

This displacement was not merely economic but social. The journeyman had occupied a recognised place in a hierarchical order; the factory hand, by contrast, was an atom in a mass — stripped of status, tradition, and the possibility of rising to mastership. The dissolution of the guild system thus created the conditions for the emergence of the modern [[proletariat]]: a class of workers who owned nothing but their labour-power and had no prospect of escaping wage-dependence.
