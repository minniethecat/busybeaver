## Definition

**Serfdom** was the dominant mode of agricultural production under European feudalism, in which labourers (serfs) were legally bound to a specific plot of land owned by a feudal lord. Unlike slaves, serfs could not be bought and sold as chattel; unlike free wage-labourers, they could not leave the land or choose their employer. The serf was obligated to render labour services, payments in kind, or both to the lord, and in return received protection and a subsistence plot. In Marxist analysis, serfdom constitutes a distinct mode of production — a specific configuration of productive forces and relations of production — in which surplus extraction takes the form of direct, personalised compulsion rather than the impersonal market mechanisms of capitalism.

## In the Manifesto

The Manifesto treats serfdom as the exploited class position within feudalism, the historical antecedent to wage-labour under capitalism. The opening passage establishes the continuity of class antagonism across modes of production:

> "Freeman and slave, patrician and plebeian, lord and serf, guild-master and journeyman, in a word, oppressor and oppressed, stood in constant opposition to one another." [(001-002)]

The structure of this sentence is significant: "lord and serf" is paired as one of four antagonistic dyads, positioning serfdom as the rural counterpart to the urban "guild-master and journeyman." The parallel is not accidental — it demonstrates that every mode of production organises exploitation through a specific, historically concrete class relation.

Marx and Engels identify the dissolution of serfdom as the demographic wellspring of the early proletariat:

> "From the serfs of the Middle Ages sprang the chartered burghers of the earliest towns. Out of these burghers the first elements of the bourgeoisie were developed." [(001-005)]

This genealogy is crucial to the Manifesto's historical materialism: the bourgeoisie does not appear *ex nihilo* but emerges from the disintegration of feudal relations. Escaped serfs who fled to the towns formed the nucleus of the medieval burgher class, from which both the bourgeoisie and, eventually, the proletariat evolved. The transformation of serf into wage-labourer was not a single event but a centuries-long process.

The Manifesto returns to the figure of the serf in a passage that illustrates the ambiguous character of bourgeois freedom compared to feudal bondage:

> "The serf, in the period of serfdom, raised himself to membership in the commune, just as the petty bourgeois, under the yoke of feudal absolutism, managed to become a bourgeois." [(001-049)]

Here Marx and Engels make a subtle point: the serf was not absolutely immobile. Within the interstices of feudal society, serfs could achieve a measure of emancipation — rising to commune membership — just as the early modern petty bourgeois could accumulate enough to cross the threshold into the bourgeoisie. But these individual paths of escape did not abolish the class relation itself; they merely demonstrated that the system permitted exceptions that proved the rule of exploitation.

## Historical Context

Serfdom in Western Europe had largely dissolved by the time Marx and Engels wrote the Manifesto in 1848. The Manifesto traces the class structure that succeeded it: "In the Middle Ages, feudal lords, vassals, guild-masters, journeymen, apprentices, serfs; in almost all of these classes, again, subordinate gradations" [(001-003)] — in which the serf occupies the base of a graduated feudal hierarchy.

In England, serfdom had effectively ended by the late 14th century; in France, the Revolution of 1789 abolished remaining seigneurial rights. However, serfdom persisted in Eastern Europe — in Russia, serfdom would not be abolished until 1861, and in Prussia, emancipation occurred in stages between 1807 and 1850. Marx and Engels were writing at a moment when serfdom was still a living memory in the West and a contemporary reality in the East.

The Manifesto's treatment of serfdom draws on a broader tradition of French and British historiography (Thierry, Guizot, the Scottish Enlightenment) that understood European history as a sequence of class struggles rooted in changes in property relations. Marx and Engels explicitly connect this dissolution to the formation of modern classes: "From the serfs of the Middle Ages sprang the chartered burghers of the earliest towns" [(001-005)] — making serfdom not a static institution but the demographic seedbed of capitalist society.

## Related Concepts

- **[[bourgeoisie]]**: The class that emerged from the dissolution of serfdom and ultimately destroyed the feudal order.
- **[[proletariat]]**: The modern exploited class, whose historical predecessor was the serf.
- **[[class-struggle]]**: The continuity of class antagonism from lord–serf to bourgeois–proletarian is the Manifesto's framing thesis.
- **[[guild-master]]**: The urban counterpart to the feudal lord in the Manifesto's typology of pre-capitalist class relations.
- **[[means-of-production]]**: Under serfdom, the means of production (land) are owned by the lord; the serf retains limited use-rights to a subsistence plot — a configuration that contrasts with both slavery and wage-labour.

## Significance

The concept of serfdom performs essential theoretical work in the Manifesto. It establishes the *historicity* of class relations — the claim that exploitation is not a permanent feature of human society but takes different forms in different epochs. It provides a *genealogy* of the bourgeoisie and proletariat, showing that these classes are products of a specific historical development, not eternal categories. And it underpins the Manifesto's political argument: if the bourgeoisie overthrew feudal relations of production, establishing a new form of exploitation in place of the old, then the proletariat can in turn overthrow bourgeois relations and establish a classless society. The historical supersession of serfdom becomes a proof-of-concept for the supersession of capitalism.
