# Karl Marx

## Biography

Karl Marx (1818–1883) was a German philosopher, economist, historian, sociologist, political theorist, journalist, and revolutionary socialist. Born in Trier in the Kingdom of Prussia, Marx studied law and philosophy at the universities of Bonn and Berlin, where he was deeply influenced by the Young Hegelians. After a frustrated academic career and censorship of the *Rheinische Zeitung* he edited, Marx moved to Paris in 1843, where he met [[friedrich-engels|Friedrich Engels]] and began a lifelong intellectual partnership. Expelled from France and then Prussia, Marx settled in London in 1849, where he lived in poverty, supported by Engels, while producing his major works including *Capital* (Vol. I, 1867).

Marx was commissioned by the [[communist-league|Communist League]] alongside Engels to write a statement of principles, resulting in the Manifesto — a text that would become one of the most widely read and influential political documents in history. His intellectual project united German idealist philosophy, French socialist thought, and British political economy into a comprehensive critique of [[capital|capitalism]], grounded in the theory of [[mode-of-production|historical materialism]]: the view that the economic base of society fundamentally determines its political and ideological superstructure.

## In the Manifesto

Although Marx does not appear by name in the body of the Manifesto, the entire document is an expression of his analysis. The famous opening, "A spectre is haunting Europe — the spectre of Communism," frames the urgency that Marx and Engels intended to address [(P01-001)]. The text was drafted by both authors but bears Marx's characteristic method. The Manifesto was "sketched" by "Communists of various nationalities" assembled in London, Marx and Engels among them [(P01-006)], and the Communist League had specifically commissioned them for the task.

> A spectre is haunting Europe — the spectre of Communism. All the Powers of old Europe have entered into a holy alliance to exorcise this spectre: Pope and Czar, Metternich and Guizot, French Radicals and German police-spies. [(P01-001)]

The historical analysis of [[class-struggle|class struggle]] as the motor of all history — "The history of all hitherto existing societies is the history of class struggles" [(001-001)] — is Marx's fundamental thesis, developed in his earlier work *The German Ideology* and elaborated across his career.

> The history of all hitherto existing societies is the history of class struggles. [(001-001)]

The structural account of how the [[bourgeoisie]] arose from within [[feudalism|feudal society]] and came to dominate the modern [[state]] [(001-004)] through [(001-006)] applies Marx's materialist conception of history to contemporary economic development.

### The Materialist Conception of History

The Manifesto applies Marx's signature method — historical materialism — to the analysis of contemporary society. For Marx, the economic structure of society, the "relations of production" that correspond to a given stage of the "material forces of production," constitutes the real foundation on which legal and political superstructures arise. This method is not stated explicitly in the Manifesto but is everywhere operative: the rise of the bourgeoisie is not explained by ideas or ambition but by the transformation of the [[mode-of-production|mode of production]] itself, from feudal guilds through [[manufacture]] to [[modern-industry|Modern Industry]] and the [[world-market|world market]].

The Manifesto's account of how the bourgeoisie "has played a most revolutionary part in history" [(001-007)] is a direct application of this method. Marx does not moralise about the bourgeoisie's virtues or vices; he derives its character from its economic function. "The bourgeoisie, wherever it has got the upper hand, has put an end to all feudal, patriarchal, idyllic relations" and "has left remaining no other nexus between man and man than naked self-interest, than callous 'cash payment'" [(001-008)]. This transformation is not a conspiracy but the necessary expression of capitalist [[relations-of-production|relations of production]].

### The Role of the Communists

The Manifesto's positive account of the Communists — that they "do not form a separate party opposed to other working-class parties" and "point out and bring to the front the common interests of the entire proletariat" [(002-005)] — reflects Marx's conviction that the [[communism|Communist movement]] is not a sect but the practical expression of a real historical tendency.

> The Communists do not form a separate party opposed to other working-class parties. They have no interests separate and apart from those of the proletariat as a whole. [(002-005)]

The Communists are "the most advanced and resolute section of the working-class parties," distinguished by their "clearly understanding the line of march" of the proletarian movement [(002-006)]. Marx insisted that the theoretical conclusions of the Communists "merely express, in general terms, actual relations springing from an existing class struggle, from a historical movement going on under our very eyes" [(002-008)]. This claim — that communism is not an ideal invented by reformers but the theoretical expression of a real movement — is the hallmark of Marx's mature thought.

> The theoretical conclusions of the Communists are in no way based on ideas or principles that have been invented, or discovered, by this or that would-be universal reformer. They merely express, in general terms, actual relations springing from an existing class struggle, from a historical movement going on under our very eyes. [(002-008)]

## Key Ideas

Marx's key intellectual contributions center on [[mode-of-production|historical materialism]], the theory that material economic conditions — the mode of production and the class relations that flow from it — determine the broad character of social, political, and intellectual life. From this foundation he developed the labor theory of value, the analysis of surplus value and [[exploitation]] under capitalism, and the theory of [[class-struggle|class struggle]] as the dynamic force propelling historical change from one mode of production to the next. His analysis of capitalism as a system in permanent [[crisis]], driven by the contradiction between expanding [[productive-forces|productive forces]] and constraining [[relations-of-production|relations of production]], is evident throughout the Manifesto's account of commercial crises in Chapter I.

The doctrine of the dictatorship of the [[proletariat]] — the transitional political form by which the working class would reorganize society — was articulated in Marx's later writings but is implicit in the Manifesto's call for the proletariat to "raise itself up" by becoming the ruling class. Marx envisioned [[communism]] not as an ideal to which reality must conform, but as a real movement abolishing the present state of things, embodied in the actual class struggles of his time.

### Historical Materialism in Practice

The Manifesto is the most compressed application of Marx's materialist method to political analysis. Where the *German Ideology* (1845–46) elaborated historical materialism as a philosophical system, the Manifesto demonstrates it as a tool of political intervention. The argument proceeds on two levels simultaneously: a historical narrative of the bourgeoisie's rise and coming fall, and a systematic critique of the [[ideology|ideological]] forms — law, morality, religion — that naturalise bourgeois rule. "Law, morality, religion, are to him so many bourgeois prejudices, behind which lurk in ambush just as many bourgeois interests" [(001-044)].

> Law, morality, religion, are to him so many bourgeois prejudices, behind which lurk in ambush just as many bourgeois interests. [(001-044)]

Marx's analysis of the [[proletariat]] as a revolutionary class flows directly from this materialist method. The proletariat is not chosen as the agent of revolution on ethical grounds; it is designated by its structural position in capitalist production. "All previous historical movements were movements of minorities, or in the interests of minorities. The proletarian movement is the self-conscious, independent movement of the immense majority, in the interests of the immense majority" [(001-046)]. Because the proletariat is the class that produces all wealth and owns nothing, its emancipation requires the abolition of class society as such.

## Relationship to Marxism

As the co-author of the Manifesto and the principal theoretical architect of the doctrine the document launched, Marx occupies a unique position. The Manifesto is simultaneously a product of Marx's thought and a primary source for the tradition that bears his name. Marxism as a political, intellectual, and revolutionary movement developed through Engels's posthumous editing and systematisation of Marx's manuscripts, through the debates of the Second International, and through the transformations wrought by Lenin and later Marxists. While the Manifesto itself was a collaborative work, the unifying theoretical framework — the materialist conception of [[mode-of-production|history]] and the critique of political economy — was Marx's distinctive contribution, making him the foundational figure of the entire Marxist tradition.

## Legacy

Marx died in 1883, but the intellectual and political movement he co-founded continued to develop and ramify through the twentieth century. Engels devoted his remaining years to editing and publishing Marx's manuscripts — most notably Volumes II and III of *Capital* — and defending the materialist conception of history against its critics and vulgarisers. By the late nineteenth century, Marxism had become the dominant theoretical framework of the European labour movement, institutionalised in the mass parties of the Second International.

The Russian Revolution of 1917, led by Lenin and the Bolsheviks, transformed Marxism from a theoretical and political tendency into a state doctrine. Lenin's interpretation of Marx — particularly his theory of the vanguard party and his analysis of imperialism as the highest stage of capitalism — shaped the course of [[revolution]] in Russia, China, Cuba, Vietnam, and across the decolonising world. The Manifesto's declaration that "the proletariat alone is a really revolutionary class" [(001-042)] and its call for the "forcible overthrow of all existing social conditions" [(007-012)] were invoked by revolutionaries across continents.

The collapse of the Soviet Union in 1991 led many to declare the death of Marxism. Yet Marx's analysis of capitalism — its inherent tendency toward [[crisis]], the concentration of [[capital]], the global expansion of the [[world-market|world market]], and the immiseration of labour — has repeatedly returned to prominence during moments of economic upheaval, from the financial crisis of 2008 to the pandemic disruptions of the 2020s. The closing words of the Manifesto remain the most famous summation of Marx's political legacy:

> Working Men of All Countries, Unite! [(007-010)]
