# Crisis

## Definition

Crisis, in the *Communist Manifesto*, refers to the periodic commercial crises inherent in the capitalist mode of production — recurrent ruptures in which the over-production of commodities overwhelms the market's capacity to consume, leading to the destruction of productive forces on a massive scale and to the immiseration of the proletariat. Unlike scarcity crises in pre-capitalist societies, the crises of capitalism are crises of abundance: "society suddenly finds itself put back into a state of momentary barbarism" not because there is too little, but "because there is too much civilisation, too much means of subsistence, too much industry, too much commerce" (001-025).

## In the Manifesto

### The Epidemic of Over-Production

The Manifesto's most extended treatment of crisis appears in its analysis of the bourgeoisie's contradictions (Section I). Marx and Engels deploy the metaphor of epidemic to capture the paradoxical character of capitalist crisis:

> It is enough to mention the commercial crises that by their periodical return put on its trial, each time more threateningly, the existence of the entire bourgeois society. In these crises a great part not only of the existing products, but also of the previously created productive forces, are periodically destroyed. In these crises there breaks out an epidemic that, in all earlier epochs, would have seemed an absurdity — the epidemic of over-production. (001-025)

The metaphor of sorcery — the bourgeoisie as a "sorcerer, who is no longer able to control the powers of the nether world whom he has called up by his spells" (001-025) — conveys the structural character of crisis: it is not an accident or a failure of policy but an expression of the fundamental contradiction between the forces and relations of production. The productive forces that the bourgeoisie summoned up "have become too powerful for these conditions" and are "fettered" by bourgeois property relations (001-025).

### Crisis and the Immiseration of the Proletariat

Crisis is not an abstract economic phenomenon; it has immediate and devastating consequences for the working class. The Manifesto describes how "the collisions between individual workmen and individual bourgeois take more and more the character of collisions between two classes" (001-039), and how commercial crises drive down wages and intensify exploitation. The resulting "commercial crises" (001-036) make "the wages of the labourer... ever more fluctuating" and render his existence ever more precarious.

### Crisis as Self-Defeating

The Manifesto notes with bitter irony that the bourgeoisie can only overcome crises "by paving the way for more extensive and more destructive crises, and by diminishing the means whereby crises are prevented" (001-025). Each resolution of a crisis merely prepares the ground for the next, more severe one. The bourgeoisie's weapons against crisis — "the conquest of new markets, and... the more thorough exploitation of the old ones" (001-025) — are also the mechanisms that intensify the contradictions that produce crises in the first place. This cyclical character — crisis, recovery, more extensive crisis — is, for Marx and Engels, evidence that the bourgeois mode of production has exhausted its historical possibilities.

## Historical Context

The commercial crises of the 1830s and 1840s provided the immediate empirical evidence for the Manifesto's analysis. The panic of 1837 in the United States, followed by the depression of 1839–1843 in Britain, and the crisis of 1847 — which broke out in the very months the Manifesto was being drafted — demonstrated with brutal clarity the inherent instability of industrial capitalism. The crisis of 1847 was particularly significant: it began as a financial panic in London in October 1847, spread rapidly to the Continent, and produced widespread unemployment and misery. Marx and Engels saw in these events not accidents but the working out of a systemic logic.

The concept of periodic crisis drew on the observations of earlier political economists, notably Jean Charles Léonard de Sismondi, who had argued that capitalism was inherently prone to crises of over-production because the purchasing power of the working class could never keep pace with the expansion of production. The Manifesto radicalises this insight: what Sismondi saw as a flaw in capitalism, Marx and Engels see as its fatal contradiction — the very dynamic that drives capitalism forward (constant revolutionising of production) is also the dynamic that periodically throws it into crisis.

## Significance

The theory of crisis occupies a pivotal position in the Manifesto's argument. It is the mechanism by which the contradiction between the forces and relations of production becomes empirically visible and politically consequential. Crisis demonstrates that the bourgeoisie's rule has become "incompetent to assure an existence to its slave within his slavery" (001-043) — that is, the system can no longer guarantee even the minimum conditions of subsistence to the class on whose exploitation it depends. In this sense, crisis is the negation of bourgeois society from within: it is the system's own logic producing its own periodic breakdown, and each breakdown brings closer the moment when "the conditions of bourgeois society are too narrow to comprise the wealth created by them" (001-025). The Manifesto's theory of crisis thus bridges its analysis of the bourgeoisie's revolutionary achievements and its prediction of proletarian revolution.
