# Communist League

## Overview

The Communist League (Bund der Kommunisten) was an international revolutionary political organization founded in London in June 1847. It was the successor to the [[league-of-the-just|League of the Just]], a secret society of German emigre workers and artisans influenced by [[utopian-socialism|utopian socialist]] ideas. The League was reorganized by [[karl-marx|Karl Marx]] and [[friedrich-engels|Friedrich Engels]], who drafted its new programme — the *Manifesto of the Communist Party* — and its guiding slogan, "Working Men of All Countries, Unite!" The League operated transnationally across England, France, Germany, Belgium, and Switzerland, and was the first Marxist political party in history. It dissolved in 1852 following political repression and internal factional disputes.

## In the Manifesto

### The Founding Commission

The Communist League is the organization that commissioned the Manifesto itself. In the Preamble, Marx and Engels directly situate the League as the body behind the text's publication. The Manifesto opens by declaring that the time has come for Communists to openly publish their programme:

> It is high time that Communists should openly, in the face of the whole world, publish their views, their aims, their tendencies, and meet this nursery tale of the Spectre of Communism with a Manifesto of the party itself. [(P01-005)]

The League gathered delegates from multiple nations to produce a document in multiple languages, reflecting its distinctly international character:

> To this end, Communists of various nationalities have assembled in London, and sketched the following Manifesto, to be published in the English, French, German, Italian, Flemish and Danish languages. [(P01-006)]

### The Party of the Proletariat

The Manifesto's Section II explicitly articulates the League's political self-understanding. The Communists, as members of this organization, define themselves in relation to the working class as a whole, not as a faction apart from it:

> The Communists do not form a separate party opposed to other working-class parties. [(002-002)]

They declare that their role is fundamentally international in scope, representing the common interests of the [[proletariat]] across all nations, and that they embody the movement's historical direction in every phase:

> The Communists are distinguished from the other working-class parties by this only: (1) In the national struggles of the proletarians of the different countries, they point out and bring to the front the common interests of the entire proletariat, independently of all nationality. (2) In the various stages of development which the struggle of the working class against the bourgeoisie has to pass through, they always and everywhere represent the interests of the movement as a whole. [(002-005)]

They further declare their role as the most advanced and theoretically conscious section, combining practical militancy with an understanding of the movement's trajectory:

> The Communists, therefore, are on the one hand, practically, the most advanced and resolute section of the working-class parties of every country, that section which pushes forward all others; on the other hand, theoretically, they have over the great mass of the proletariat the advantage of clearly understanding the line of march, the conditions, and the ultimate general results of the proletarian movement. [(002-006)]

The immediate political goal unites the League with all proletarian parties — but it is the theoretical clarity about the historical process that distinguishes it. The Communist programme is not an arbitrary set of doctrines; it emerges from the analysis of existing social conditions unfolding in real time (002-008). The Manifesto distills this programme into a single demand: "Abolition of private property" (002-012), a slogan that distinguishes the League's communism from all forms of mere reformism.

### Strategic Posture Toward Existing Movements

In Section IV, the Manifesto summarizes the League's strategic posture toward existing working-class movements:

> The Communists fight for the attainment of the immediate aims, for the enforcement of the momentary interests of the working class; but in the movement of the present, they also represent and take care of the future of that movement. [(007-002)]

It then surveys the League's tactical alliances across Europe — with the [[social-democrats-france|Social-Democrats]] in France, Radicals in Switzerland, agrarian revolutionaries in Poland who fomented the [[cracow-insurrection-1846|Cracow insurrection of 1846]], and the [[bourgeoisie]] against absolutism in Germany — each time affirming a distinct Communist position (007-002).

In Germany, where the League concentrated its primary efforts, the Communists were to fight alongside the bourgeoisie in its revolutionary struggle against the [[absolute-monarchy|absolute monarchy]] and feudal order, while simultaneously preparing the [[proletariat]] to turn against its temporary bourgeois allies once reaction was defeated:

> But they never cease, for a single instant, to instil into the working class the clearest possible recognition of the hostile antagonism between bourgeoisie and proletariat, in order that the German workers may straightaway use, as so many weapons against the bourgeoisie, the social and political conditions that the bourgeoisie must necessarily introduce along with its supremacy, and in order that, after the fall of the reactionary classes in Germany, the fight against the bourgeoisie itself may immediately begin. [(007-006)]

### The Internationalist Call

The closing call — "Working Men of All Countries, Unite!" (007-012) — became the League's motto and remains the emblematic slogan of the international Communist movement. It crystallises the League's foundational principle: the emancipation of the [[proletariat]] is not a national but an international task, and the Communist party is the organisational vehicle for that task.

> The Communists disdain to conceal their views and aims. They openly declare that their ends can be attained only by the forcible overthrow of all existing social conditions. Let the ruling classes tremble at a Communistic revolution. The proletarians have nothing to lose but their chains. They have a world to win. [(007-011)]

## Historical Role

The Communist League's 1848 Congress, meeting at the height of Europe's revolutionary year, formally adopted the Manifesto as its programme. The League's membership reached only a few hundred members at its peak, but its historical significance far exceeded its numerical size. During the [[revolutions-of-1848|revolutions of 1848]], League members — including Marx and Engels themselves — actively participated in revolutionary organizing in Germany, publishing the *Neue Rheinische Zeitung* and agitating for democratic and proletarian demands.

### The 1848 Revolutions

The [[revolutions-of-1848]] that swept across Europe provided the League with its first real test as a revolutionary organization. Marx and Engels returned to Germany to participate directly in the struggle, using the League's network of contacts to distribute revolutionary literature and coordinate action among workers' associations. The League's strategy in Germany, as articulated in Section IV, was two-stage: support the bourgeoisie against the feudal-absolutist order in the first phase, then immediately pivot to the struggle for proletarian power as the revolution unfolded (007-006, 007-007).

### The Cologne Trial and Dissolution

After the defeat of the 1848–49 revolutions, the League was targeted by Prussian police. The Cologne Communist Trial of 1852 led to the imprisonment of several members and the League's formal dissolution. Marx documented this episode in *Revelations Concerning the Communist Trial in Cologne* (1853), a detailed exposure of the fabricated evidence and police provocations used to destroy the organization. Despite its short existence, the Communist League was the organizational seed from which the First International (International Workingmen's Association, 1864) and the entire tradition of Marxist political parties subsequently grew.

## Marxist Assessment

### A Model of Proletarian Organization

Marx and Engels regarded the Communist League as the model of a proletarian political party: international in scope, theoretically grounded in the materialist conception of history, and permanently committed to the revolutionary overthrow of bourgeois society. In Section II, the Manifesto's description of the Communists as representatives of the movement "as a whole" (002-005, 002-006) reflects the League's own self-conception — not a mere sect or faction, but the conscious political expression of the proletariat's historical interests.

The League's theoretical method was grounded not in abstract principles but in the analysis of actually existing social relations. The theoretical conclusions of the Communists, the Manifesto insists, "are in no way based on ideas or principles that have been invented, or discovered, by this or that would-be universal reformer" (002-008). They instead "merely express, in general terms, actual relations springing from an existing class struggle, from a historical movement going on under our very eyes" (002-008). This methodological commitment distinguishes the League from its [[utopian-socialism|utopian socialist]] predecessors, who appealed to universal reason and moral sentiment rather than to the dynamics of [[class-struggle|class struggle]] itself.

### Dissolution and Historical Lessons

The League's dissolution, while a tactical defeat, only confirmed Marx and Engels' analysis that revolutionary organization must adapt to changing political conditions and that the proletariat's emancipation is a long historical process. The practical experience of the League — its successes and failures — fed directly into Marx and Engels' later political work, particularly the founding of the International Workingmen's Association in 1864. The League demonstrated that a relatively small, politically conscious organization could exercise an influence far beyond its numerical strength, provided it was armed with a correct theoretical analysis and embedded within the broader working-class movement. This insight — that a revolutionary party is not a mass electoral machine but a vanguard that "pushes forward all others" (002-006) — became a foundational principle of subsequent Marxist political practice.

## Key Figures

- **[[karl-marx|Karl Marx]] (1818–1883)** — Co-author of the Manifesto, central theoretical and political leader of the League.
- **[[friedrich-engels|Friedrich Engels]] (1820–1895)** — Co-author of the Manifesto, League organizer and correspondent.
- **Karl Schapper (1812–1870)** — Veteran of the League of the Just, central committee member of the Communist League.
- **Joseph Moll (1813–1849)** — German watchmaker and League organizer, killed during the 1849 Baden uprising.
- **Wilhelm Wolff (1809–1864)** — Silesian-born communist, close collaborator of Marx and Engels, to whom Marx dedicated *Capital*.
