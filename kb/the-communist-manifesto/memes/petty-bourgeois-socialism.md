# Petty-Bourgeois Socialism

## Definition

Petty-Bourgeois Socialism is the form of socialist thought that arose from the standpoint of the intermediate classes — peasants, artisans, small traders — who were being destroyed by capitalist development. Its most distinguished representative was the Swiss economist Jean Charles Léonard Simonde de Sismondi. Marx and Engels acknowledge its analytical penetration — it "dissected with great acuteness the contradictions in the conditions of modern production" (004-017) — but condemn its positive programme as "both reactionary and Utopian" (004-018), because it sought not to transcend capitalism but to retreat from it into a pre-industrial past that was already irrevocably destroyed.

## In the Manifesto

The Manifesto analyses Petty-Bourgeois Socialism as the second form of "Reactionary Socialism" in Ch. III. The section opens by situating its class basis: "The feudal aristocracy was not the only class that was ruined by the bourgeoisie" (004-014). The medieval burghers and small peasant proprietors — the precursors of the modern bourgeoisie themselves — were being ground down by the same capitalist development their ancestors had set in motion. In "countries where modern civilisation has become fully developed, a new class of petty bourgeois has been formed, fluctuating between proletariat and bourgeoisie and ever renewing itself as a supplementary part of bourgeois society" (004-015). But this class is permanently threatened: "the individual members of this class, however, are being constantly hurled down into the proletariat by the action of competition" (004-015).

The intellectual expression of this class's predicament took shape in France, "where the peasants constitute far more than half of the population." It was "natural that writers who sided with the proletariat against the bourgeoisie, should use, in their criticism of the bourgeois regime, the standard of the peasant and petty bourgeois" (004-016). Sismondi was the head of this school.

The Manifesto's treatment of Petty-Bourgeois Socialism is notable for its qualified praise. The school "laid bare the hypocritical apologies of economists" and "proved, incontrovertibly, the disastrous effects of machinery and division of labour; the concentration of capital and land in a few hands; overproduction and crises; it pointed out the inevitable ruin of the petty bourgeois and peasant, the misery of the proletariat, the anarchy in production, the crying inequalities in the distribution of wealth" (004-017). This is a remarkable catalogue of credits — almost every major theme of Marxist critique is anticipated in it.

But the positive programme is fatally flawed. Petty-Bourgeois Socialism "aspires either to restoring the old means of production and of exchange, and with them the old property relations, and the old society, or to cramping the modern means of production and of exchange, within the framework of the old property relations" (004-018). Its last words are "corporate guilds for manufacture, patriarchal relations in agriculture" (004-019) — a programme that seeks not forward movement but backward retreat. The Manifesto's judgement is final: "Ultimately, when stubborn historical facts had dispersed all intoxicating effects of self-deception, this form of Socialism ended in a miserable fit of the blues" (004-020).

## Historical Context

Sismondi (1773–1842) was a genuine pioneer of economic criticism. His *New Principles of Political Economy* (1819) challenged the classical political economy of Adam Smith and David Ricardo by arguing that capitalism inevitably produced crises of under-consumption, that the concentration of wealth created a structural gap between production and purchasing power, and that the displacement of small producers was not a temporary adjustment but a permanent feature of capitalist development. These insights directly influenced Marx's own crisis theory.

But Sismondi's alternative was not socialism in the Marxist sense. He looked backward to a society of independent small producers, regulated by state intervention to protect the peasant and artisan from the ravages of competition. Marx and Engels saw this as a futile nostalgia — the very productive forces Sismondi criticised were also the material precondition for a society beyond scarcity and class antagonism. The Manifesto's criticism of Petty-Bourgeois Socialism is therefore not a rejection of its diagnosis but a rejection of its cure: there is no going back to the guild and the patriarchal farm.

## Related Concepts

Petty-Bourgeois Socialism is the second form of reactionary socialism, situated between [[feudal-socialism]] (the critique from the displaced aristocracy) and [[german-socialism]] (the philosophical translation of French socialist ideas into reactionary German idealism). Its class base is the [[petty-bourgeoisie]] — the "fluctuating" intermediate class "constantly hurled down into the proletariat by the action of competition" (004-015). The concept is tied to [[crisis]] and [[over-production]], two phenomena that Sismondi was among the first to identify as structural features of capitalism rather than accidental malfunctions. The [[bourgeoisie]] is the target of Petty-Bourgeois Socialism's criticism, and the [[proletariat]] is the class on whose behalf it claims to speak, even as its real sympathies lie with the doomed world of the independent small producer.
