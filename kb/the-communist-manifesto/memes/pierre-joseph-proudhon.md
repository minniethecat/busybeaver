# Pierre-Joseph Proudhon

## Biography

Pierre-Joseph Proudhon (1809–1865) was a French philosopher, economist, and political theorist, widely regarded as the first thinker to call himself an anarchist. Born in Besancon to a working-class family, he was largely self-educated and worked as a printer before turning to writing. His 1840 work *What Is Property?* gained instant notoriety for its famous answer — "Property is theft!" — and established him as a radical voice in French political thought. Proudhon's subsequent works developed a theory of mutualism: a decentralized, non-statist socialism based on free association among producers, cooperative credit, and the abolition of interest and rent.

Proudhon's intellectual relationship with [[karl-marx|Marx]] was complicated. The two men corresponded and debated in Paris during the mid-1840s; Marx initially admired Proudhon's early radicalism. The relationship soured irreparably after the publication of Proudhon's *System of Economic Contradictions, or The Philosophy of Poverty* (1846), to which Marx responded savagely with *The Poverty of Philosophy* (1847). By the time the Manifesto was written, Proudhon had become a prime target of Marx and [[friedrich-engels|Engels]]'s critique of reformist socialism.

## In the Manifesto

Proudhon is the only named individual explicitly cited in the Manifesto's section on [[bourgeois-socialism|"Conservative, or Bourgeois, Socialism."]] The section opens by identifying "a part of the bourgeoisie" that is "desirous of redressing social grievances, in order to secure the continued existence of bourgeois society" (005-001). To this category belong "economists, philanthropists, humanitarians, improvers of the condition of the working class" — and "this form of Socialism has, moreover, been worked out into complete systems" (005-002). The text then declares: "We may cite Proudhon's *Philosophie de la Misere* as an example of this form" (005-003).

The context of this citation is damning. Marx and Engels place Proudhon's thought squarely within a tendency that "want all the advantages of modern social conditions without the struggles and dangers necessarily resulting therefrom" and that "desire the existing state of society minus its revolutionary and disintegrating elements" (005-004). The critique is precise: Proudhon's mutualism, which sought to reform property relations through credit associations and free exchange while rejecting revolutionary politics, is classified not as genuine proletarian socialism but as a variant of [[bourgeoisie|bourgeois]] [[reform|reformism]].

The fundamental objection is political. Proudhon's system, in Marx and Engels's view, "but requires in reality, that the [[proletariat]] should remain within the bounds of existing society, but should cast away all its hateful ideas concerning the bourgeoisie" (005-004). By treating economic reform as a substitute for [[class-struggle]] and revolutionary transformation, Proudhonism becomes, in the Manifesto's taxonomy, a form of socialist [[ideology]] that serves bourgeois interests.

## Key Ideas

Proudhon's central ideas — mutualism, federalism, and the critique of authority — form a distinctive current within 19th-century socialism. His mutualism proposed that workers could emancipate themselves through cooperative production and exchange, bypassing both the state and capitalist markets. His theory of property distinguished between "property" as monopoly and exploitation (illegitimate) and "possession" as personal use (legitimate), a distinction that separated him from both liberal defenders of [[private-property]] and communists who sought its total abolition.

Proudhon's federalism envisioned a society organized from the bottom up through voluntary contractual associations, with political authority decentralized to the smallest possible units. This anti-statist orientation put him at odds not only with bourgeois republicans but also with the state-socialist and [[communism|communist]] currents of his time. His opposition to revolutionary violence and his preference for gradual, economic transformation through mutual credit banks and workers' associations placed him firmly in the reformist camp that the Manifesto excoriates.

## Relationship to Marxism

Proudhon represents a major point of demarcation for the emerging Marxist tradition. Marx's *The Poverty of Philosophy* (1847) was written as a direct refutation of Proudhon's *The Philosophy of Poverty*, and the critique carried over into the Manifesto's classification of Proudhon's socialism as "conservative, or bourgeois." In Marx's view, Proudhon's rejection of the state, revolutionary politics, and the category of [[class-struggle]] marked his doctrine as a [[petty-bourgeoisie|petty-bourgeois]] deviation — a socialism that sought to ameliorate capitalism rather than abolish it. Proudhon's influence nonetheless persisted, particularly in the French workers' movement and later in anarcho-syndicalism. The Marx-Proudhon split prefigured the enduring tension between Marxist and anarchist currents within the broader socialist movement, a division that would culminate in the First International.
