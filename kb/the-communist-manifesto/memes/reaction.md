**Reaction** and the **reactionary** are defined in the Manifesto not as mere political conservatism but as a structurally determined orientation — the attempt by classes threatened by the advance of capitalism to arrest or reverse historical movement. Marx and Engels deploy the term as a critical weapon, exposing the objective meaning of political positions that present themselves as progressive or even socialist while serving, in reality, the interests of a dying social order.

## In the Manifesto

### The Bourgeoisie as Reactionary

The Manifesto's use of "reactionary" is first directed at the bourgeoisie itself. Having described the bourgeoisie's historically revolutionary role in destroying feudal society, Marx and Engels immediately add a dialectical qualification: the bourgeoisie, once the agent of progress, becomes the obstacle to it.

> The bourgeoisie, historically, has played a most revolutionary part. (001-011)

But this revolutionary function is finite. The very productive forces the bourgeoisie has summoned forth — "more massive and more colossal productive forces than have all preceding generations together" (001-022) — now turn against it. The bourgeoisie becomes reactionary when it attempts to preserve the existing property relations against the further development of the productive forces. "The weapons with which the bourgeoisie felled feudalism are now turned against the bourgeoisie itself" (001-023).

The Manifesto thus treats "reactionary" as a **relational** rather than an absolute category. A class becomes reactionary not because of its ideas or intentions but because of its changing relationship to the development of the productive forces. The bourgeoisie, once revolutionary, becomes reactionary precisely at the point where its system of property becomes "too narrow to comprise the wealth created by it" (001-023).

### Reactionary Socialism

The most sustained treatment of reactionary politics appears in Section III, where Marx and Engels analyse the various schools of socialism that defined themselves in opposition to bourgeois society.

**Feudal Socialism** is reactionary in the most straightforward sense: the French and English aristocracies, "not without some truth," indict the bourgeoisie for having created a proletariat that will "sweep away the old order of society" (004-004). But their critique is not aimed at liberating the proletariat — it seeks to restore the feudal order that capitalism destroyed. "In its last words: corporate guilds for manufacture, patriarchal relations in agriculture" (004-019).

> So little do they conceal the reactionary character of their criticism that their chief indictment against the bourgeoisie is precisely that under its regime a class is being developed which is destined to cut up root and branch the old order of society. (004-008)

**Petty-Bourgeois Socialism** is simultaneously reactionary and utopian. It "aspires either to restoring the old means of production and of exchange, and with them the old property relations, and the old society, or to cramping the modern means of production and of exchange, within the framework of the old property relations" (004-018). In either case, "it is both reactionary and Utopian" (004-018). The petty bourgeois, caught between capital and labour, seeks not to transcend capitalism but to flee from it backward into an idealized pre-industrial past.

**German or "True" Socialism** represents yet another form of reactionary politics. Importing French socialist ideas into Germany without the material conditions that produced them, it "served as a welcome scarecrow against the threatening bourgeoisie" for the German absolutist governments (004-034). Posing as a critique of liberalism from the left, it functioned in practice as a tool of the reactionary German state.

> It directly represented a reactionary interest, the interest of the German Philistines. (004-034)

### Critical-Utopian Socialism as Reactionary

Even the Critical-Utopian Socialists — Saint-Simon, Fourier, Owen — whose critique of bourgeois society was in many respects radical, become reactionary in their practical orientation. Because they see no independent historical role for the proletariat, they address themselves "to society at large, without distinction of class" (006-004). Their refusal to participate in the actual class struggle — in strikes, in political organisation, in revolutionary action — means that their sects "formed mere reactionary sects" (006-012), clinging to outdated doctrines in the face of a developing working-class movement.

> Hence, in every case, formed mere reactionary sects. They hold fast by the original views of their masters, in opposition to the progressive historical development of the proletariat. (006-012)

### The Fall of the Reactionary Classes

The Manifesto concludes its strategic section (Section IV) with a concrete political forecast for Germany, where the bourgeoisie is still in the process of overthrowing feudalism. Marx and Engels predict that the German bourgeois revolution "will be but the prelude to an immediately following proletarian revolution" (007-006). After the fall of the reactionary classes — meaning the German aristocracy, the absolutist state, and the petty-bourgeois strata allied with them — the struggle will immediately shift to the confrontation between bourgeoisie and proletariat.

> After the fall of the reactionary classes in Germany, the fight against the bourgeoisie itself may immediately begin. (007-006)

## Historical Context

The Manifesto was written in the year of revolutions, 1848, when the European continent was convulsed by uprisings against absolutist monarchies. The "reactionary classes" were not abstractions — they were the Holy Alliance powers (Russia, Austria, Prussia), the French Legitimists, the English Tories, and the petty-bourgeois democrats who sought to arrest the revolution at its halfway point. Marx and Engels' insistence on distinguishing between genuinely progressive and secretly reactionary political programmes reflected the lived experience of the 1848 revolutions, in which the liberal bourgeoisie repeatedly allied with the forces of reaction against the threatening proletariat.

## Significance

The concept of reaction in the Manifesto serves as a powerful analytical tool. It reveals that political positions cannot be judged by their self-description — "feudal socialism," for instance, called itself socialist while serving reactionary ends — but only by their objective relation to the development of the productive forces and the class struggle. A doctrine is reactionary not because it is conservative in temperament but because it seeks to arrest or reverse the development of the productive forces, to preserve or restore relations of production that have been historically superseded.

This analysis also establishes a dialectical understanding of historical progress: the bourgeoisie is simultaneously the most revolutionary class in history (destroying feudalism) and, in the present, a reactionary force (defending the very property relations it once revolutionised). Reaction is thus not a fixed property of any class but a function of its historical position relative to the forward movement of the productive forces.
