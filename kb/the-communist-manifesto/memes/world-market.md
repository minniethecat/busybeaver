## Definition

The **world-market** in the *Communist Manifesto* is the global system of trade and exchange created by the bourgeoisie through modern industry, colonial expansion, and improvements in navigation and communication. It is not merely an economic fact — a larger market for goods — but a transformative social force: by drawing every nation into a single global economy, it universalises capitalist relations of production and, paradoxically, creates the material conditions for international working-class solidarity.

## In the Manifesto

The world-market is presented as the crowning achievement of the bourgeoisie — and the mechanism through which it revolutionises the entire planet. Its establishment marks the decisive break between the medieval and modern worlds:

> Modern industry has established the world-market, for which the discovery of America paved the way. [(001-009)]

Marx and Engels trace a causal chain: the discovery of America opened new territories for trade; colonial markets created demand that feudal guild production could not satisfy; this demand drove the development of modern industry; and modern industry, in turn, consolidated a genuinely global market. The world-market is thus both effect and cause of capitalist development.

Once established, the world-market becomes the bourgeoisie's instrument for reshaping all societies in its own image. The drive for profit compels perpetual geographic expansion:

> The need of a constantly expanding market for its products chases the bourgeoisie over the whole surface of the globe. It must nestle everywhere, settle everywhere, establish connections everywhere. [(001-018)]

Through this global reach, capitalism imposes uniformity. National differences — in production, in consumption, in culture — are eroded by the logic of the market:

> The bourgeoisie has through its exploitation of the world-market given a cosmopolitan character to production and consumption in every country. [(001-019)]

This homogenisation extends to the political sphere. The world-market dissolves national antagonisms not through goodwill but through the uniformity of economic conditions:

> National differences and antagonisms between peoples are daily more and more vanishing, owing to the development of the bourgeoisie, to freedom of commerce, to the world-market, to uniformity in the mode of production and in the conditions of life corresponding thereto. [(002-053)]

## Historical Context

The world-market described in the Manifesto was a reality of nineteenth-century capitalism. The British Empire — upon which, as Marx and Engels later wrote, "the sun never sets" — had created a trading network spanning India, China, the Caribbean, Africa, and the Americas. Steamships and railways compressed distances; the electric telegraph (mentioned in the Manifesto alongside steam-navigation and railways [(001-022)]) enabled near-instantaneous commercial communication. The repeal of the Corn Laws in 1846 had committed Britain to free trade, the policy the Manifesto identifies as the bourgeois programme for the world-market.

## Significance

The world-market is the concept through which the Manifesto transforms Marx's philosophy of history into a political programme. If capitalism is a global system, then the proletariat's struggle must be global too. The world-market that unifies the bourgeoisie across national borders also unifies the workers those borders divide. This is the materialist foundation for the Manifesto's concluding call — "Working Men of All Countries, Unite!" — which follows directly from the analysis of the world-market's homogenising force. The concept thus links the Manifesto's economic analysis to its political strategy: internationalism is not a moral choice but a practical necessity dictated by the structure of capitalist production.
