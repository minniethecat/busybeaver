# Bourgeoisie

## Definition

The bourgeoisie is the class of modern capitalists -- owners of the means of social production and employers of wage-labour. Marx and Engels define them as the product of a long historical development, arising from the ruins of feudal society through a series of revolutions in the modes of production and of exchange. In their analysis, bourgeois society simplified social antagonisms: "Society as a whole is more and more splitting up into two great hostile camps, into two great classes, directly facing each other: Bourgeoisie and Proletariat" (001-004).

## Historical Origins

The term *bourgeoisie* derives from the French *bourgeois*, originally referring to town-dwellers and merchants in medieval France. Marx and Engels trace the class's lineage back to the medieval burghers: "From the serfs of the Middle Ages sprang the chartered burghers of the earliest towns. From these burgesses the first elements of the bourgeoisie were developed" (001-005). The discovery of America and the opening of new trade routes provided the material foundation for its rise, as they "opened up fresh ground for the rising bourgeoisie" and "gave to commerce, to navigation, to industry, an impulse never before known" (001-006).

The development of the bourgeoisie was inseparable from the transformation of production itself. The feudal guild system "no longer sufficed for the growing wants of the new markets. The manufacturing system took its place" (001-007). When even manufacture proved insufficient, "steam and machinery revolutionised industrial production," and "the place of manufacture was taken by the giant, Modern Industry, the place of the industrial middle class, by industrial millionaires, the leaders of whole industrial armies, the modern bourgeois" (001-008). This was, as Marx and Engels summarised, "a long course of development, of a series of revolutions in the modes of production and of exchange" (001-010).

Politically, each step in the bourgeoisie's economic development was "accompanied by a corresponding political advance" (001-011). From an oppressed class under feudal nobility, through its role as a "taxable 'third estate' of the monarchy," the bourgeoisie eventually "conquered for itself, in the modern representative State, exclusive political sway" (001-011). In a formulation that would become one of the Manifesto's most cited lines, Marx and Engels declared that "the executive of the modern State is but a committee for managing the common affairs of the whole bourgeoisie" (001-011).

By the time Marx and Engels wrote in 1848, the term had come to designate the capitalist class as a whole -- the class that had displaced the feudal aristocracy as the dominant social force across Western Europe, particularly in England, France, and the German states.

## Revolutionary Role

The *Communist Manifesto* presents the bourgeoisie as a historically revolutionary class that has transformed every aspect of society. Marx and Engels open this section of their analysis with one of the Manifesto's most emphatic declarations: "The bourgeoisie, historically, has played a most revolutionary part" (001-012).

### Dissolution of Traditional Relations

Wherever it gained power, the bourgeoisie dismantled pre-modern social bonds:

> "The bourgeoisie, wherever it has got the upper hand, has put an end to all feudal, patriarchal, idyllic relations. It has pitilessly torn asunder the motley feudal ties that bound man to his 'natural superiors,' and has left remaining no other nexus between man and man than naked self-interest, than callous 'cash payment.'" (001-013)

This dissolution extended to every sacred institution of pre-bourgeois society. The bourgeoisie "has stripped of its halo every occupation hitherto honoured and looked up to with reverent awe. It has converted the physician, the lawyer, the priest, the poet, the man of science, into its paid wage labourers" (001-014). It "has torn away from the family its sentimental veil, and has reduced the family relation to a mere money relation" (001-015). Marx and Engels' verdict was stark: "In one word, for exploitation, veiled by religious and political illusions, it has substituted naked, shameless, direct, brutal exploitation" (001-013).

At the same time, the Manifesto acknowledged the bourgeoisie's unprecedented productive achievements. It "has been the first to show what man's activity can bring about. It has accomplished wonders far surpassing Egyptian pyramids, Roman aqueducts, and Gothic cathedrals; it has conducted expeditions that put in the shade all former Exoduses of nations and crusades" (001-016).

### Constant Revolution of Production

Central to Marx and Engels' analysis is the claim that the bourgeoisie cannot exist without constantly transforming production:

> "The bourgeoisie cannot exist without constantly revolutionising the instruments of production, and thereby the relations of production, and with them the whole relations of society." (001-017)

Unlike all earlier ruling classes, for whom "conservation of the old modes of production in unaltered form, was... the first condition of existence," the bourgeoisie's survival depends on perpetual disruption: "Constant revolutionising of production, uninterrupted disturbance of all social conditions, everlasting uncertainty and agitation distinguish the bourgeois epoch from all earlier ones" (001-017). This yields the Manifesto's most poetic passage:

> "All fixed, fast-frozen relations, with their train of ancient and venerable prejudices and opinions, are swept away, all new-formed ones become antiquated before they can ossify. All that is solid melts into air, all that is holy is profaned, and man is at last compelled to face with sober senses, his real conditions of life, and his relations with his kind." (001-017)

### Global Reach

The bourgeoisie's need for expanding markets drives it across the entire globe: "The need of a constantly expanding market for its products chases the bourgeoisie over the whole surface of the globe. It must nestle everywhere, settle everywhere, establish connexions everywhere" (001-018). Through this global expansion,

> "The bourgeoisie has through its exploitation of the world-market given a cosmopolitan character to production and consumption in every country." (001-019)

All old-established national industries are destroyed or daily being destroyed, replaced by new industries that "no longer work up indigenous raw material, but raw material drawn from the remotest zones; industries whose products are consumed, not only at home, but in every quarter of the globe" (001-019). The result is "universal inter-dependence of nations," applying as much to intellectual as to material production: "from the numerous national and local literatures, there arises a world literature" (001-019).

> "The cheap prices of its commodities are the heavy artillery with which it batters down all Chinese walls, with which it forces the barbarians' intensely obstinate hatred of foreigners to capitulate. It compels all nations, on pain of extinction, to adopt the bourgeois mode of production... In one word, it creates a world after its own image." (001-020)

The bourgeoisie also reshaped the spatial distribution of humanity, subjecting "the country to the rule of the towns" and creating "enormous cities" (001-021). In the process, "it has agglomerated production, and has concentrated property in a few hands," with "political centralisation" as "the necessary consequence" (001-022). During "its rule of scarce one hundred years," the bourgeoisie "has created more massive and more colossal productive forces than have all preceding generations together" (001-022).

## The Bourgeoisie and Its Contradictions

Despite its revolutionary achievements, bourgeois society is, in Marx and Engels' view, inherently unstable. The productive forces that the bourgeoisie summoned up have outgrown the social relations within which they were created.

> "Modern bourgeois society with its relations of production, of exchange and of property, a society that has conjured up such gigantic means of production and of exchange, is like the sorcerer, who is no longer able to control the powers of the nether world whom he has called up by his spells." (001-025)

The periodic commercial crises that rock bourgeois society are the most visible symptom of this contradiction. In these crises, "there breaks out an epidemic that, in all earlier epochs, would have seemed an absurdity -- the epidemic of over-production" (001-025). Society is thrown "back into a state of momentary barbarism," not because there is too little, but "because there is too much civilisation, too much means of subsistence, too much industry, too much commerce" (001-025). The bourgeoisie can only resolve these crises "by paving the way for more extensive and more destructive crises, and by diminishing the means whereby crises are prevented" (001-025).

The bourgeoisie has thus created the conditions of its own destruction:

> "Not only has the bourgeoisie forged the weapons that bring death to itself; it has also called into existence the men who are to wield those weapons -- the modern working class -- the proletarians." (001-027)

The argument culminates in one of the Manifesto's most quoted propositions: "What the bourgeoisie, therefore, produces, above all, is its own grave-diggers. Its fall and the victory of the proletariat are equally inevitable" (001-050). The essential condition of bourgeois existence -- the formation and augmentation of [[capital]] through [[wage-labour]] -- is undermined by the very advance of industry that the bourgeoisie promotes. Competition among workers is replaced "by their revolutionary combination, due to association" (001-050), and the development of [[modern-industry]] "cuts from under its feet the very foundation on which the bourgeoisie produces and appropriates products" (001-050).

## The Bourgeoisie in the Communist Programme

In their polemic against bourgeois objections to [[communism]], Marx and Engels systematically dismantled the ideological claims of their opponents. They characterised the distinguishing feature of communism as "not the abolition of property generally, but the abolition of bourgeois property" (002-011), which they called "the final and most complete expression of the system of producing and appropriating products, that is based on class antagonisms, on the exploitation of the many by the few" (002-011).

> "But don't wrangle with us so long as you apply, to our intended abolition of bourgeois property, the standard of your bourgeois notions of freedom, culture, law, etc. Your very ideas are but the outgrowth of the conditions of your bourgeois production and bourgeois property." (002-036)

The Communists' goal was nothing less than to use political power "to wrest, by degrees, all capital from the bourgeoisie" and to "centralise all instruments of production in the hands of the State" (002-068). The final vision held that "in place of the old bourgeois society, with its classes and class antagonisms, we shall have an association, in which the free development of each is the condition for the free development of all" (002-083).

## Related Concepts

The bourgeoisie is defined in dialectical opposition to the [[proletariat]]. Its rise is inseparable from the development of [[capital]] and the concentration of the [[means-of-production]] in private hands. The bourgeoisie's ideology -- [[free-trade]], [[competition]], individual rights -- is analysed as the political expression of its economic interests. The theory of [[class-struggle]] places the bourgeoisie as the antagonist class in the modern epoch, opposed to the working class it exploits.

The bourgeoisie's political power is exercised through the [[state]], which Marx and Engels describe as "but a committee for managing the common affairs of the whole bourgeoisie" (001-011). Its economic dominance rests on the [[mode-of-production]] specific to capitalism -- the combination of [[wage-labour]], [[private-property]], and the relentless drive to revolutionise the [[productive-forces]].

The related concept of [[petty-bourgeoisie]] describes the intermediate strata -- small tradespeople, shopkeepers, handicraftsmen, and peasants -- who "sink gradually into the proletariat" as they are "swamped in the competition with the large capitalists" (001-033). The [[bourgeois-socialism]] critiqued in the Manifesto represents an attempt by a section of the bourgeoisie "to secure the continued existence of bourgeois society" (005-001) while redressing social grievances.

The [[ruling-class]] page provides the broader theoretical framework for understanding the bourgeoisie's position. In the Manifesto's periodisation, the bourgeoisie is the ruling class of the epoch following [[feudalism]] and preceding the anticipated [[proletarian-revolution]]. The [[industrial-revolution]] was the economic transformation that brought the bourgeoisie to dominance, while the [[revolutions-of-1848]] -- breaking out in the same year the Manifesto was published -- demonstrated the political tensions inherent in bourgeois rule.

## Significance

The bourgeoisie occupies a central position in Marxist theory as the ruling class of capitalism. The Manifesto's analysis of this class -- its historical origins, its revolutionary transformation of production and social relations, and its inevitable crisis -- forms the foundation of [[marx-theory-of-history|historical materialism]]. The prediction that bourgeois society would generate [[crisis]] of [[over-production]] that it could not resolve remains influential in critiques of capitalism today. The class's globalising dynamic, captured in the image of "a world after its own image" (001-020), has proven to be one of the Manifesto's most prescient observations, anticipating the trajectory of [[world-market|global capitalism]] over the subsequent century and a half.
