> In France the Communists ally themselves with the Social-Democrats, against the conservative and radical bourgeoisie, reserving, however, the right to take up a critical position in regard to phrases and illusions traditionally handed down from the great Revolution. [(007-002)]

The **Social-Democrats** (*Démocrates-Socialistes*, or "Démoc-Socs") were the democratic-socialist current in French politics during the 1840s, representing the left wing of the republican movement that would play a central role in the [[revolutions-of-1848|Revolutions of 1848]]. In Section IV of the *Manifesto of the Communist Party*, [[karl-marx|Marx]] and [[friedrich-engels|Engels]] name the French Social-Democrats as the strategic ally of the Communists in France — a tactical alliance qualified by the critical independence that Communists maintain toward their allies' ideological limitations.

## In the Manifesto

Section IV of the Manifesto — "Position of the Communists in Relation to the Various Existing Opposition Parties" — is the text's most concretely political section, surveying the tactical situation in each major European country and specifying the Communists' posture toward other political forces. The passage on France is the most developed:

> The Communists fight for the attainment of the immediate aims, for the enforcement of the momentary interests of the working class; but in the movement of the present, they also represent and take care of the future of that movement. In France the Communists ally themselves with the Social-Democrats, against the conservative and radical bourgeoisie, reserving, however, the right to take up a critical position in regard to phrases and illusions traditionally handed down from the great Revolution. [(007-002)]

This formulation encapsulates the Manifesto's distinctive strategic logic: **united front with critical independence**. The Communists support the Social-Democrats against the common enemy (the conservative and radical bourgeoisie), but they do not subordinate themselves to the Social-Democrats' programme or ideology. The "phrases and illusions traditionally handed down from the great Revolution" — the Jacobin rhetoric of universal rights, the belief that the democratic republic alone can emancipate labour — are critiqued even as the alliance is maintained.

The general principle underlying this specific alliance is stated in the same section:

> In short, the Communists everywhere support every revolutionary movement against the existing social and political order of things. [(007-008)]

This universal commitment to revolutionary movements provides the strategic rationale for allying with the Social-Democrats in France, the [[chartists|Chartists]] in England, and the agrarian revolutionaries in Poland. But the Communists do not enter these alliances as passive followers [(007-003)]:

> In all these movements they bring to the front, as the leading question in each, the property question, no matter what its degree of development at the time. [(007-009)]

The "property question" — the abolition of bourgeois private property — is the distinctive Communist demand that the Social-Democrats, with their programme of democratic reform within the framework of private property, could not themselves raise. This is the substantive content of the "critical position" [(007-002)] the Communists reserve: not a mere difference of rhetorical emphasis, but an insistence on the revolutionary transformation of property relations that alone can emancipate the proletariat.

### The Réformistes

The Social-Democrats should be distinguished from the **Réformistes** — the group around the newspaper *La Réforme* (founded 1843), led by Alexandre Ledru-Rollin, with [[louis-blanc|Louis Blanc]] and the socialist economist Alexandre Martin (known as "Albert") among its contributors. The Réformistes advocated democratic republicanism combined with social reform: universal male suffrage, the "right to work," state-supported producers' cooperatives ("national workshops"), and progressive taxation. They differed from the more radical, insurrectionary currents (Blanqui, Barbès) in their commitment to legal, electoral methods, and from the utopian socialist sects (Fourierists, Cabetists) in their engagement with political struggle.

In the Manifesto's Section III.3, the Réformistes appear alongside the [[chartists|Chartists]] as the authentic political organizations of the working class that the utopian sects — Owenites and Fourierists — oppose:

> The Owenites in England, and the Fourierists in France, respectively, oppose the Chartists and the "Réformistes." [(006-014)]

This double mention — in Section III as a working-class political force, in Section IV as a tactical ally — positions the Réformistes/Social-Democrats as the French counterpart to English Chartism: a mass democratic movement that the Communists support while seeking to push further.

## Historical Context

### Origins

The Social-Democratic current emerged from the left wing of French republicanism in the years after the July Revolution of 1830. The July Monarchy of Louis-Philippe (1830–1848) was a regime of the *haute bourgeoisie* — bankers, financiers, large landowners — that systematically excluded both the working class and the petty bourgeoisie from political power. The opposition to this regime crystallized around two poles: the "dynastic left" of Odilon Barrot, which sought to extend the suffrage within the framework of constitutional monarchy, and the radical republicans of *La Réforme*, who demanded universal male suffrage and social legislation.

### The 1848 Revolution

The February Revolution of 1848 elevated the Social-Democrats to positions of governmental power for the first time. Ledru-Rollin became Minister of the Interior in the Provisional Government; Louis Blanc and Albert headed the Luxembourg Commission, charged with addressing "the labour question." The Provisional Government proclaimed the "right to work" and established National Workshops — though in practice these were poorly funded public works projects rather than Blanc's vision of worker-managed producers' cooperatives.

The June Days (June 23–26, 1848) — the bloody suppression of a working-class insurrection in Paris by the republican government — exposed the class contradictions within the republican alliance. General Cavaignac's forces killed several thousand workers; the "social republic" revealed itself as a bourgeois republic that would massacre the proletariat to defend property. The Social-Democrats, caught between their republican principles and their working-class base, were politically shattered by the June Days. Louis Blanc fled to England; Ledru-Rollin's candidacy in the December 1848 presidential election was crushed by Louis-Napoléon Bonaparte.

### Marx and Engels' Assessment

For Marx and Engels, the trajectory of the French Social-Democrats was paradigmatic. In *The Class Struggles in France, 1848–1850* and *The Eighteenth Brumaire of Louis Bonaparte*, Marx analyzed the June Days as the historical verification of the Manifesto's strategic line: the democratic republic is not the abolition of class rule but its "most perfected form," and the proletariat cannot rely on its bourgeois republican allies to carry through the social revolution. The Social-Democrats' failure — their inability to break with bourgeois republicanism when the class struggle sharpened — is precisely the danger against which the Manifesto's "critical position in regard to phrases and illusions" [(007-002)] was meant to guard.

The Manifesto is explicit about why such "phrases and illusions" persist: bourgeois ideas are not neutral truths but class-bound products of material conditions [(002-036)]:

> But don't wrangle with us so long as you apply, to our intended abolition of bourgeois property, the standard of your bourgeois notions of freedom, culture, law, etc. Your very ideas are but the outgrowth of the conditions of your bourgeois production and bourgeois property, just as your jurisprudence is but the will of your class made into a law for all, a will, whose essential character and direction are determined by the economical conditions of existence of your class. [(002-036)]

The Social-Democrats' "phrases and illusions traditionally handed down from the great Revolution" [(007-002)] — liberty, equality, fraternity as universal ideals — are precisely such outgrowths of bourgeois conditions of existence. The Communists' critical independence consists in recognising these ideals as class-bound, however progressive they may once have been [(002-008)], and in refusing to subordinate proletarian interests to them.

Furthermore, the Manifesto grounds the Communists' strategic orientation in a class analysis that identifies which social forces are genuinely revolutionary. The Social-Democrats drew support from both workers and the petty bourgeoisie; the Manifesto's class taxonomy illuminates why this coalition was inherently unstable:

> Of all the classes that stand face to face with the bourgeoisie today, the proletariat alone is a really revolutionary class. The other classes decay and finally disappear in the face of Modern Industry; the proletariat is its special and essential product. The lower middle class, the small manufacturer, the shopkeeper, the artisan, the peasant, all these fight against the bourgeoisie, to save from extinction their existence as fractions of the middle class. They are therefore not revolutionary, but conservative. Nay more, they are reactionary, for they try to roll back the wheel of history. If by chance they are revolutionary, they are so only in view of their impending transfer into the proletariat, they thus defend not their present, but their future interests, they desert their own standpoint to place themselves at that of the proletariat. [(001-042)]

This passage provides the theoretical architecture for understanding the Social-Democrats' tragic trajectory in 1848. The petty-bourgeois elements within the Social-Democratic coalition — the small shopkeepers, artisans, and peasants who rallied to the democratic republic — were, in the Manifesto's terms, "not revolutionary, but conservative" [(001-042)], fighting to preserve their threatened existence as fractions of the middle class. When the class struggle sharpened in the June Days, these elements sided with the bourgeois republic against the proletariat. The "critical position" [(007-002)] was not sectarian fastidiousness but a strategic imperative grounded in class analysis.

## Significance

The Manifesto's treatment of the French Social-Democrats is the locus classicus of communist united-front tactics. The formulation — alliance against the common enemy, combined with critical ideological independence — would be theorized and re-theorized by every subsequent Marxist tradition: the "united front" of the Comintern's Third and Fourth Congresses, the "popular front" of the 1930s, Mao's "united front" in the Chinese Revolution, the "strategy of tension" in post-1968 Western Marxism. Each of these descended, directly or indirectly, from the four sentences in Section IV of the Manifesto that defined the Communists' relationship to the French Social-Democrats.

The Social-Democrats' historical trajectory also served as a negative object lesson. Their inability to break with bourgeois republicanism, their faith that the democratic republic could be extended to include "the social," their fragmentation when class struggle intensified — these became, for Marxists, the classic pathologies of reformism, the political expression of the petty bourgeoisie's oscillation between the bourgeoisie and the proletariat. The Manifesto's insistence on raising "the property question" [(007-009)] in every alliance, and its recognition that "the proletariat alone is a really revolutionary class" [(001-042)], remain the theoretical foundation for distinguishing a communist united front from mere class-collaboration.
