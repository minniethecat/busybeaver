# Capital

## Definition

In the *Communist Manifesto*, capital is defined not as a mere quantity of money or a stock of physical assets but as a social relationship. Marx and Engels characterize it as *"that kind of property which exploits wage-labour, and which cannot increase except upon condition of begetting a new supply of wage-labour for fresh exploitation"* (002-016). Capital is therefore inseparable from wage-labour — it grows by extracting surplus value from workers, and the existence of a class of wage-labourers is its precondition. The Manifesto insists that capital *"is a collective product, and only by the united action of many members, nay, in the last resort, only by the united action of all members of society, can it be set in motion"* (002-017).

## In the Manifesto

The Manifesto traces the historical rise of capital alongside the development of the bourgeoisie. From the earliest chartered burghers of medieval towns, the bourgeoisie *"developed, increased its capital, and pushed into the background every class handed down from the Middle Ages"* (001-009). The accumulation of capital is the driving force behind the bourgeoisie's revolutionary transformation of the world — the creation of a world market, the subjugation of the countryside to the town, and the constant revolutionizing of the instruments of production.

Marx and Engels make a crucial distinction between capital as a social power and property as a personal possession. *"Capital is, therefore, not a personal, it is a social power"* (002-018). The Communists' call for the abolition of private property refers only to bourgeois property — that is, capital — not to the property of the petty artisan or peasant, which has already been destroyed by the development of industry. When capital is converted into common property, they argue, *"it is only the social character of the property that is changed. It loses its class-character"* (002-019).

The Manifesto describes capital's relentless expansion and its consequences. The bourgeoisie's existence depends on *"the formation and augmentation of capital; the condition for capital is wage-labour"* (001-050). The industrial capitalist *"lives merely to increase capital, and is allowed to live only in so far as the interest of the ruling class requires it"* (002-021). This drive to accumulate transforms all social relations. The bourgeois family is *"based on capital, on private gain"* (002-039), and in bourgeois society, *"capital is independent and has individuality, while the living person is dependent and has no individuality"* (002-023). The Manifesto's ten-point program concludes that the proletariat must use its political supremacy *"to wrest, by degrees, all capital from the bourgeoisie, to centralise all instruments of production in the hands of the State"* (002-068).

### Capital as a Social Relation

This subsection draws together the key passages in which Marx and Engels articulate their most original insight: capital is not a thing but a social relationship that dominates those caught within it.

> "But does wage-labour create any property for the labourer? Not a bit. It creates capital, i.e., that kind of property which exploits wage-labour, and which cannot increase except upon condition of begetting a new supply of wage-labour for fresh exploitation." (002-016)

> "To be a capitalist, is to have not only a purely personal, but a social status in production. Capital is a collective product, and only by the united action of many members, nay, in the last resort, only by the united action of all members of society, can it be set in motion." (002-017)

> "Capital is, therefore, not a personal, it is a social power." (002-018)

> "When, therefore, capital is converted into common property, into the property of all members of society, personal property is not thereby transformed into social property. It is only the social character of the property that is changed. It loses its class-character." (002-019)

> "In bourgeois society, therefore, the past dominates the present; in Communist society, the present dominates the past. In bourgeois society capital is independent and has individuality, while the living person is dependent and has no individuality." (002-023)

These passages establish a chain of reasoning: capital is created by wage-labour, exists as a collective social product, functions impersonally as a social power, and subordinates living persons to its own independent logic. The political conclusion — that capital can be converted into common property without abolishing personal property — follows directly from this analysis. This is a foundational distinction for the Manifesto's entire program, and it remains one of its most contested claims.

## Historical Context

The concept of capital as a social relation rather than a thing was Marx's decisive break from classical political economy. Adam Smith and David Ricardo had analyzed capital as a stock of goods used in production, but they treated it as a natural and transhistorical category. Marx recognized that capital is a historically specific form of property that presupposes the separation of workers from the means of production and the conversion of labour-power into a commodity. The volatile boom-and-bust cycles of the 1830s and 1840s — with financial panics in 1837 and 1847 — lent empirical force to the Manifesto's analysis of capital as an inherently crisis-prone system.

## Related Concepts

Capital cannot be understood in isolation from [[wage-labour]], which it exploits and on which it depends for its growth. It is accumulated through the private ownership of the [[means-of-production]] by the [[bourgeoisie]]. The [[proletariat]], deprived of any ownership stake in capital, is compelled to sell its labour-power to survive. The antagonism between capital and labour drives the [[class-struggle]] that structures modern history. The Manifesto argues that [[communism]]'s central program is the abolition of capital as private property.

The logic of capital accumulation also leads to recurring [[crisis]] of over-production — an "epidemic" that the Manifesto identifies as the most distinctive pathology of bourgeois society. This crisis tendency is inseparable from capital's dynamic as a social power that outgrows the [[private-property]] relations within which it first developed.

## Significance

Capital is the organizing concept of Marx's critique of political economy. The Manifesto's compact analysis of capital — as a collective social power, accumulated through exploitation, and driving toward its own supersession — prefigures the three volumes of *Das Kapital* that Marx would devote to the subject over the following decades. By reformulating capital as a social relation rather than a stock of things, Marx opened up a space for critique that classical economics had foreclosed: if capital is a historically specific social form, it can be historically transformed. This insight remains foundational for every tradition of anti-capitalist thought and practice.
