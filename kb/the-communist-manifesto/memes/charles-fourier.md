> The Socialist and Communist systems properly so called, those of Saint-Simon, Fourier, Owen and others, spring into existence in the early undeveloped period, described above, of the struggle between proletariat and bourgeoisie. [(006-003)]

**Charles Fourier** (1772–1837) was a French philosopher and social theorist whose elaborate vision of a harmoniously organized society — centred on cooperative communities called "phalanstères" (phalanxes) — made him, alongside [[robert-owen|Robert Owen]] and [[henri-de-saint-simon|Henri de Saint-Simon]], one of the three foundational figures of [[utopian-socialism|utopian socialism]]. In the *Manifesto of the Communist Party*, [[karl-marx|Marx]] and [[friedrich-engels|Engels]] credit Fourier with a penetrating critique of bourgeois society while repudiating his rejection of political action and [[class-struggle|class struggle]]. Fourier's followers, the Fourierists, became objects of the Manifesto's sharpest polemic: by opposing the [[chartists|Chartists]] and Réformistes in the name of apolitical community-building, they had "deadened the class struggle" [(006-012)] and become, in effect, allies of the existing order.

## In the Manifesto

### The Critique of Fourierism

Fourier appears in the Manifesto at two levels: as an individual thinker (Section III.3, "Critical-Utopian Socialism and Communism") and through the political practice of his followers, the Fourierist school (the same section's concluding polemic).

**Fourier's achievement.** Marx and Engels grant Fourier — along with [[henri-de-saint-simon|Saint-Simon]] and Owen — a historical significance that no other socialist tendency in the Manifesto receives. These thinkers "attack every principle of existing society" [(006-011)] and their writings contain "the most valuable materials for the enlightenment of the working class" [(006-011)]. Fourier's contribution was distinctive: he was the great satirist of commercial society, the first thinker to make the critique of *civilisation* itself — not just this or that abuse, but the entire organization of modern social life — the centre of a systematic doctrine. His dissection of the irrationalities of the [[market]] — where abundance coexists with poverty, where merchants profit from scarcity, where "in civilisation, poverty is born of abundance itself" — anticipates key themes of Marx's later critique of commodity fetishism.

**Fourier's limitation.** The utopians' common weakness, in the Manifesto's analysis, is their inability to see the [[proletariat]] as a historical agent. The founders of these systems "see, indeed, the class antagonisms, as well as the action of the decomposing elements, in the prevailing form of society" [(006-004)], but the working class "offers to them the spectacle of a class without any historical initiative or any independent political movement" [(006-005)]. Consequently, Fourier and his followers do not call on workers to organize and fight — they propose that society as a whole, including the ruling class, adopt their blueprints through the force of reason and example. Fourier famously waited at home every day at noon for a wealthy philanthropist who would finance the first phalanstère — no one ever came.

> They still dream of experimental realisation of their social Utopias — of founding isolated "phalanstères," of establishing "Home Colonies," of setting up a "Little Icaria" — and to realise all these castles in the air, they are compelled to appeal to the feelings and purses of the bourgeois. [(006-011)]

**The Fourierists' reactionary turn.** The Manifesto's verdict on the Fourierist *school* is harsher than its verdict on Fourier himself. By the 1840s, the utopians' original systems had become obstacles to proletarian self-emancipation. Marx and Engels make this historical logic explicit:

> The significance of Critical-Utopian Socialism and Communism bears an inverse relation to historical development. In proportion as the modern class struggle develops and takes definite shape, this fantastic standing apart from the contest, these fantastic attacks on it, lose all practical value and all theoretical justification. Therefore, although the originators of these systems were, in many respects, revolutionary, their disciples have, in every case, formed mere reactionary sects. [(006-012)]

Where Fourier's original doctrine was a genuine, if premature, response to the emergence of [[class-antagonism|class antagonism]], his epigones — clinging to the master's rejection of politics in a period when the proletariat had begun to organize independently — became an obstacle to the real movement:

> The Owenites in England, and the Fourierists in France, respectively, oppose the Chartists and the "Réformistes." [(006-014)]

This is the decisive political indictment: in opposing the Chartists — the first mass independent political movement of the British working class — the Owenites and Fourierists objectively aligned themselves with the [[bourgeoisie]], whatever their subjective intentions.

## Historical Context

### Fourier's System

Fourier was born in Besançon, France, in 1772, the son of a cloth merchant. His lifelong hatred of commerce is often traced to a childhood episode: as a young clerk, he was ordered to dump a cargo of rice into the sea to keep prices high — an experience that crystallized his perception of the market as a system of organized waste and fraud.

Fourier's system, first expounded in *Théorie des Quatre Mouvements* (1808) and developed in a series of increasingly elaborate works, rested on the concept of the **phalanstère** — a self-sufficient cooperative community of exactly 1,620 people, organized around "passional attraction" (Fourier's theory that human passions, properly harmonized, could replace coercion as the basis of social organization). Work in the phalanstère would be rotated frequently to avoid monotony; children, whom Fourier considered naturally industrious, would do the dirty work that adults disliked; and the entire community would be housed in a single grand building — the *phalanstère* — combining residence, workshop, dining hall, library, and theatre.

Fourier's critique of "civilisation" — his term for the existing commercial order — was often brilliant. He exposed the structural irrationalities of competitive capitalism: the contradiction between collective production and private appropriation, the wastefulness of middlemen and speculation, the degradation of labour into a torment to be avoided rather than a source of fulfilment. [[friedrich-engels|Friedrich Engels]], in *Anti-Dühring* (1878), would later write that Fourier "uses the dialectic method in the same masterly way as his contemporary Hegel" and that his critique "contains the elements of a profound economic analysis."

### Fourierism as a Movement

After Fourier's death in 1837, his disciples — led by Victor Considérant — transformed Fourierism into an organized movement. Fourierist colonies were established in France, Algeria, and most notably the United States (Brook Farm in Massachusetts, the North American Phalanx in New Jersey, and several others). All failed within a few years.

In France, the Fourierists clustered around the newspaper *La Démocratie Pacifique* and, during the 1848 Revolution, briefly entered electoral politics under Considérant's leadership. Their principled rejection of revolutionary methods, however — Fourier had always insisted that social transformation would come through peaceful example, not class conflict — placed them in increasing tension with the emerging workers' movement, exactly as the Manifesto diagnosed.

### Marx, Engels, and Fourier

Marx and Engels' relationship to Fourier was one of dialectical appropriation. Engels, who had encountered Fourier's writings during his Paris stay in the mid-1840s, consistently praised Fourier's wit, his critique of marriage and the family (Fourier wrote that the degree of women's emancipation was "the natural measure of general emancipation"), and his dialectical grasp of social contradictions. [[karl-marx|Marx]], in *The Holy Family* (1845), had defended Fourier against the "Critical Critics" of the Young Hegelian circle.

Yet the Manifesto marks a decisive break. The utopians' fundamental political limitation, as the Manifesto diagnoses it, is their rejection of the [[proletariat|proletariat's]] autonomous agency:

> Hence, they reject all political, and especially all revolutionary, action; they wish to attain their ends by peaceful means, and endeavour, by small experiments, necessarily doomed to failure, and by the force of example, to pave the way for the new social Gospel. [(006-009)]

By 1848, the question was no longer whether capitalism was irrational — Fourier had demonstrated that conclusively — but *how* it was to be overthrown. The Fourierists' answer — example, persuasion, the philanthropist's cheque — had been overtaken by events: the Chartist mass movement in England, the [[revolutions-of-1848|1848 revolutions]] on the continent, the emergence of the proletariat as an independent political force. Fourier's legacy was thus preserved within [[communism]] (his critique of commercial society, his dialectical method, his feminism avant la lettre) while his politics — the rejection of class struggle, the dream of transcending conflict through harmony — was definitively repudiated.

## Key Works

- *Théorie des Quatre Mouvements et des Destinées Générales* (1808) — Fourier's first and most accessible major work, outlining his cosmology, theory of the passions, and critique of civilisation.
- *Traité de l'Association Domestique-Agricole* (1822) — The most developed exposition of the phalanstère system, later republished as *Théorie de l'Unité Universelle*.
- *Le Nouveau Monde Industriel et Sociétaire* (1829) — Fourier's mature statement of his social system, aimed at a popular audience.
- *La Fausse Industrie* (1835–1836) — A concentrated critique of the irrationalities of commercial capitalism ("false industry"), containing some of Fourier's most incisive economic observations.

## Significance

Charles Fourier is, in many respects, the most *Marxist* of the utopian socialists — not because Marx agreed with him, but because Fourier anticipated, in fantastical form, problems that Marxism would later re-pose on a materialist basis. His critique of the fragmentation and degradation of labour under capitalism prefigures Marx's theory of alienation; his insistence that social transformation requires the transcendence of the [[division-of-labour|division of labour]] — that in a genuinely free society, "work" would become a form of play, a expression of human creativity rather than a means of survival — anticipates the Marx of the *Grundrisse* and *The German Ideology*; and his declaration that "the extension of women's rights is the general principle of all social progress" was taken up directly by Marx in *Capital* and by Engels in *The Origin of the Family, Private Property and the State*.

Yet the Manifesto's verdict stands. The utopians "endeavour, and that consistently, to deaden the class struggle and to reconcile the class antagonisms" [(006-011)]. Fourierism failed not because its critique of capitalism was wrong, but because it was politically sterile. The phalanstère was a beautiful blueprint that required no class struggle, no organization of the proletariat, no conquest of political power — only a benefactor who never arrived. The Manifesto's communism is Fourier's critique of civilisation *plus* the organized revolutionary movement of the working class — the insight that the phalanstère cannot be built within the old society by appealing to its rulers, but must be won by overthrowing them.
