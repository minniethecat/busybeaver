## Definition

Relations of production (*Produktionsverhältnisse*) are the social relations into which human beings enter — independently of their will — in the process of material production. At their core are property relations: who owns the means of production, who controls the labour process, and how the social product is distributed among classes. In class society, relations of production are necessarily relations of exploitation: the ruling class appropriates surplus labour from the direct producers by virtue of its ownership of the means of production. These relations constitute the "economic structure of society, the real foundation on which rises a legal and political superstructure" (Marx, 1859 Preface).

## In the Manifesto

The Manifesto is structured around a contradiction within the relations of production. Marx and Engels argue that the bourgeoisie is a revolutionary class because it "cannot exist without constantly revolutionising the instruments of production, and thereby the relations of production, and with them the whole relations of society" [(001-013)]. This constant revolutionising of the relations of production is the bourgeoisie's historical achievement and, simultaneously, the source of the perpetual crisis and "everlasting uncertainty" that distinguishes the bourgeois epoch from all earlier ones.

The feudal relations of property — "the feudal organisation of agriculture and manufacturing industry" — became fetters on the productive forces that had developed within them; they "had to be burst asunder; they were burst asunder" and replaced by bourgeois property relations [(001-019)]. The Manifesto argues that the same process is now repeating itself: "modern bourgeois society with its relations of production, of exchange and of property" has produced productive forces so colossal that they "revolt" against the very relations that called them into being [(001-023)].

In the polemic against bourgeois objections in Ch. II, Marx and Engels historicise relations of production against the bourgeoisie's own natural-law pretensions: "The selfish misconception that induces you to transform into eternal laws of nature and of reason, the social forms springing from your present mode of production and form of property — historical relations that rise and disappear in the progress of production — this misconception you share with every ruling class that has preceded you" [(002-015)].

The idea that capitalist relations of production are historically transient — and not eternal — is the theoretical pivot of the entire Manifesto. The Communist revolution will not merely redistribute property within existing relations; it will abolish the relations of production themselves, and thereby "the conditions for the existence of class antagonisms and of classes generally" [(002-052)].

## Historical Context (1848)

The concept of relations of production was a major theoretical innovation of Marx and Engels's break with the Young Hegelian idealism of the early 1840s. Where Hegel and his followers saw history as the unfolding of Spirit or ideas, Marx and Engels insisted that the real motor was material: the contradiction between developing productive forces and stagnant relations of production. The Manifesto of 1848 is the first political document to make this theoretical schema the foundation of a practical revolutionary programme.

The concept also reflected the concrete experience of the 1840s. The rapid expansion of factory production in Britain, France, and the German states was visibly transforming social relations — dissolving the ties of guild, estate, and locality and replacing them with the single cash-nexus relation between capital and wage-labour. The Manifesto's insistence that the relations of production are "revolutionised" by the bourgeoisie names this lived experience of social dislocation.

## Related Concepts

Relations of production form one pole of the dialectic that constitutes the [[mode-of-production]]; the other pole is the [[productive-forces]]. The productive forces develop within and against the relations of production; when the contradiction becomes explosive, social revolution transforms the relations to match the new level of the productive forces.

The concept is closely tied to [[private-property]], which is the juridical expression of bourgeois relations of production. The Manifesto's declaration that "the theory of the Communists may be summed up in the single sentence: Abolition of private property" [(002-003)] is, in materialist terms, the demand to abolish the specific relations of production that define capitalism.

The concept also underlies the Manifesto's treatment of [[class-struggle]]: the class structure of any given society is the personification of its relations of production. The antagonism between bourgeois and proletarian is not a moral conflict but the living expression of a contradiction in the relations of production.
