## Definition

Class antagonism designates the structural opposition between social classes rooted in their relationship to the means of production. In Marx and Engels's historical materialist framework, all hitherto existing societies have been organised around an antagonism between an exploiting (ruling) class and an exploited (producing) class — master and slave, lord and serf, guild-master and journeyman, and finally bourgeois and proletarian. This antagonism is not a moral failing but a structural feature of the mode of production, and it is the motor of historical change.

## In the Manifesto

The Manifesto opens by grounding its entire analysis in class antagonism. Marx and Engels declare that "the history of all hitherto existing society is the history of class struggles" and proceed to sketch the distinctive character of modern class antagonism: its simplification. Whereas earlier epochs presented "a complicated arrangement of society into various orders, a manifold gradation of social rank," the bourgeois epoch "has simplified the class antagonisms" into "two great hostile camps … Bourgeoisie and Proletariat" [(001-003)].

This simplification is itself a historical product. The bourgeoisie swept away the complex feudal hierarchy and concentrated social conflict along a single axis. The Manifesto's central theoretical claim is that bourgeois private property is "the final and most complete expression of the system of producing and appropriating products, that is based on class antagonisms, on the exploitation of the many by the few" [(002-003)].

In Ch. II, the authors address bourgeois objections to Communism by arguing that all past societies were organised around class antagonism. "The history of all past society," they write, "has consisted in the development of class antagonisms, antagonisms that assumed different forms at different epochs" [(002-027)]. The charge that Communism would abolish morality, religion, or the family is met with the reply that these institutions were always specific expressions of particular class antagonisms, and that their disappearance with the abolition of classes is neither surprising nor lamentable.

The concept reaches its climax in the Manifesto's revolutionary programme. When the proletariat "makes itself the ruling class, and, as such, sweeps away by force the old conditions of production, then it will, along with these conditions, have swept away the conditions for the existence of class antagonisms and of classes generally" [(002-052)]. The abolition of class antagonism is thus the ultimate telos of the Communist project.

## Historical Context (1848)

Marx and Engels developed the concept of class antagonism in opposition to both the utopian socialists, who dreamed of class reconciliation without revolution, and the bourgeois economists, who naturalised class relations as eternal laws. Drawing on the French historians of the Restoration period (Thierry, Guizot), who had already described history as a struggle between classes, Marx and Engels gave this insight a materialist grounding: each class antagonism corresponds to a specific stage in the development of productive forces, and each is resolved only when the subordinate class develops the capacity to inaugurate a new mode of production.

The simplification of class antagonism into the bourgeois-proletarian binary was not, for Marx and Engels, a permanent condition — it was a historical achievement of the bourgeoisie itself, which had destroyed all intermediate estates and thrown society into a single, transparent conflict. This simplification was, in their view, the precondition for socialist revolution: a complex class map obscured the true nature of exploitation; a binary one made it unmistakable.

## Related Concepts

Class antagonism is not identical with [[class-struggle]] — the former names the structural opposition inherent in a mode of production; the latter names the active, conscious political contest that emerges from that structural condition. The Manifesto treats class antagonism as objective and invariant under class society, while class struggle is the historical process by which that antagonism is worked out and ultimately overcome.

The concept is intimately connected to [[means-of-production]] and [[relations-of-production]]: class antagonism arises precisely from the contradiction between a class that owns the means of production and a class that must sell its labour-power to survive. The Manifesto's analysis of [[private-property]] is likewise organised around class antagonism, since private property is "based on class antagonisms, on the exploitation of the many by the few" [(002-003)].
