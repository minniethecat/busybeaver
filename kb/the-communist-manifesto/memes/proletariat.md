# Proletariat

## Definition

The proletariat is the class of modern wage-labourers — those who possess no means of production of their own and are compelled to sell their labour-power to the bourgeoisie in order to survive. Marx and Engels define them succinctly: *"a class of labourers, who live only so long as they find work, and who find work only so long as their labour increases capital"* (001-028). Unlike earlier oppressed classes in history, the proletariat is the product of Modern Industry itself, and its conditions of existence represent the complete negation of all previous forms of property and social relations.

## In the Manifesto

The *Communist Manifesto* traces the emergence of the proletariat as the necessary consequence of bourgeois development. Marx and Engels argue that the bourgeoisie not only creates the material forces that will destroy it, but also *"has called into existence the men who are to wield those weapons — the modern working class — the proletarians"* (001-027). In proportion as capital develops, the proletariat develops alongside it, becoming a commodity subject to all the fluctuations of the market.

### Stages of Development

The proletariat passes through distinct stages of development. At first, workers struggle as isolated individuals against individual bourgeois; later they form trade unions and combine as a class. Marx and Engels describe how the lower strata of the middle class continually sink into the proletariat, so that *"the proletariat is recruited from all classes of the population"* (001-033). As industry develops, the proletariat *"not only increases in number; it becomes concentrated in greater masses, its strength grows, and it feels that strength more"* (001-036). The collisions between individual workers and individual bourgeois increasingly take on the character of collisions between two whole classes.

> At this stage the labourers still form an incoherent mass scattered over the whole country, and broken up by their mutual competition. If anywhere they unite to form more compact bodies, this is not yet the consequence of their own active union, but of the union of the bourgeoisie, which class, in order to attain its own political ends, is compelled to set the whole proletariat in motion, and is moreover yet, for a time, able to do so. At this stage, therefore, the proletarians do not fight their enemies, but the enemies of their enemies, the remnants of absolute monarchy, the landowners, the non-industrial bourgeois, the petty bourgeoisie. Thus the whole historical movement is concentrated in the hands of the bourgeoisie; every victory so obtained is a victory for the bourgeoisie. [001-035]

The turning point comes when the workers begin to organise permanently. Marx and Engels describe this transformation in detail:

> Thereupon the workers begin to form combinations (Trades Unions) against the bourgeois; they club together in order to keep up the rate of wages; they found permanent associations in order to make provision beforehand for these occasional revolts. Here and there the contest breaks out into riots. [001-037]

This organisational phase leads to a qualitative shift in the struggle:

> Now and then the workers are victorious, but only for a time. The real fruit of their battles lies, not in the immediate result, but in the ever-expanding union of the workers. This union is helped on by the improved means of communication that are created by modern industry and that place the workers of different localities in contact with one another. It was just this contact that was needed to centralise the numerous local struggles, all of the same character, into one national struggle between classes. But every class struggle is a political struggle. [001-039]

### The Revolutionary Class

Marx and Engels make a striking claim about the proletariat's unique political position: *"Of all the classes that stand face to face with the bourgeoisie today, the proletariat alone is a really revolutionary class"* (001-042). Unlike the lower middle classes, who fight to preserve their own existence and are therefore conservative or reactionary, the proletariat has nothing to lose but its chains. The proletariat is *"the lowest stratum of our present society"* that *"cannot stir, cannot raise itself up, without the whole superincumbent strata of official society being sprung into the air"* (001-046).

The basis for this revolutionary character is the proletariat's total negation of all previous forms of property and social relations:

> All the preceding classes that got the upper hand, sought to fortify their already acquired status by subjecting society at large to their conditions of appropriation. The proletarians cannot become masters of the productive forces of society, except by abolishing their own previous mode of appropriation, and thereby also every other previous mode of appropriation. They have nothing of their own to secure and to fortify; their mission is to destroy all previous securities for, and insurances of, individual property. [001-045]

### Proletariat and Communists

The relationship between the proletariat and the [[communism|Communists]] is defined in Section II. The Communists *"have no interests separate and apart from those of the proletariat as a whole"* (002-003). Their immediate aim is identical to that of all other [[proletarian-revolution|proletarian parties]]: *"formation of the proletariat into a class, overthrow of the bourgeois supremacy, conquest of political power by the proletariat"* (002-007). The distinctive feature of the Communists is that *"they have over the great mass of the proletariat the advantage of clearly understanding the line of march, the conditions, and the ultimate general results of the proletarian movement"* (002-006).

The Manifesto's first chapter culminates in the declaration that *"the bourgeoisie, therefore, produces, above all, its own grave-diggers. Its fall and the victory of the proletariat are equally inevitable"* (001-050). This is the dialectical conclusion of the [[class-struggle|class analysis]]: the very development of [[capital]] and industry that enriches the [[bourgeoisie]] simultaneously creates the conditions for its overthrow — a concentrated, organized, and class-conscious proletariat.

## Historical Context

The modern proletariat emerged from the [[industrial-revolution|Industrial Revolution]] that swept through England in the late 18th century and spread across Europe in the first half of the 19th century. The enclosure of common lands, the mechanization of textile production, and the concentration of manufacturing in factories uprooted millions from agrarian life and converted them into urban wage-labourers. By the time Marx and Engels wrote in 1848, the working class was a visible and growing social force — particularly in England, where the [[chartists|Chartist movement]] had mobilized workers around demands for political rights, and in France, where the [[february-revolution-1848|1848 February Revolution]] would shortly erupt with strong working-class participation.

## Related Concepts

The proletariat is defined in direct opposition to the [[bourgeoisie]], the class that owns the [[means-of-production]] and employs wage-labour. Its existence is inseparable from the accumulation of [[capital]], which grows only through the [[exploitation]] of labour. The [[class-struggle]] between proletariat and bourgeoisie is the driving force of modern history. The economic mechanism binding the proletarian to the capitalist is [[wage-labour]]. The proletariat's political vehicle is the [[communist-league|Communist League]] and the broader Communist movement, whose immediate aim is the *"formation of the proletariat into a class"* and its conquest of [[political-power|political power]]. In a broader historical perspective, the proletariat is distinguished from the [[petty-bourgeoisie|petty bourgeoisie]] and the [[aristocracy]] — classes that either seek to preserve existing conditions or turn the clock backward. The [[proletarian-revolution]] is the political event through which the proletariat seizes power and begins the transformation of society, a process that culminates in the abolition of [[private-property|private property]] and [[class-antagonism|class antagonisms]] themselves.

## Significance

The concept of the proletariat is central to Marxist theory as the revolutionary subject of history — the first class in history whose emancipation necessarily entails the emancipation of all humanity. Unlike the bourgeoisie or the feudal aristocracy, the proletariat has no particular class interest to defend against the rest of society; its liberation is incompatible with class society itself.

> All previous historical movements were movements of minorities, or in the interests of minorities. The proletarian movement is the self-conscious, independent movement of the immense majority, in the interests of the immense majority. [001-046]

This idea of the proletariat as a "universal class" has been one of the most influential and contested concepts in modern political thought, shaping revolutions, social movements, and political parties across the globe for more than a century and a half.
