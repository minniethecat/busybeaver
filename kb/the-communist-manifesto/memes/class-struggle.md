# Class Struggle

## Definition

Class struggle is the conflict between social classes whose economic interests are fundamentally opposed. Marx and Engels famously open the Manifesto with the declaration: *"The history of all hitherto existing societies is the history of class struggles"* (001-001). On this view, every form of society is structured around the antagonism between an exploiting class and an exploited class — and this antagonism is the motor that propels historical change forward, each epoch's class struggles culminating either in *"a revolutionary re-constitution of society at large, or in the common ruin of the contending classes"* (001-002).

> The history of all hitherto existing societies is the history of class struggles. Freeman and slave, patrician and plebeian, lord and serf, guild-master and journeyman, in a word, oppressor and oppressed, stood in constant opposition to one another, carried on an uninterrupted, now hidden, now open fight, a fight that each time ended, either in a revolutionary re-constitution of society at large, or in the common ruin of the contending classes. [001-001--001-002]

The Manifesto thus locates class struggle at the very foundation of historical movement. The opening passage serves not merely as a rhetorical flourish but as a statement of methodological intent: to understand any society, one must first identify its fundamental class antagonism.

## In the Manifesto

The Manifesto presents class struggle not merely as a recurrent feature of history but as the explanatory key to all social, political, and ideological transformations. Its first section surveys the succession of class antagonisms — *"Freeman and slave, patrician and plebeian, lord and serf, guild-master and journeyman, in a word, oppressor and oppressed"* (001-002) — before turning to the distinctive character of the modern class struggle between bourgeoisie and proletariat.

### The Simplification of Class Antagonism

Marx and Engels argue that the modern epoch is distinguished by the radical simplification of class antagonisms. Whereas earlier historical periods exhibited *"a complicated arrangement of society into various orders, a manifold gradation of social rank"* (001-003) — patricians, knights, plebeians, slaves in Rome; feudal lords, vassals, guild-masters, journeymen, apprentices, serfs in the Middle Ages — the bourgeois era collapses this complexity into a single, defining opposition.

> The modern bourgeois society that has sprouted from the ruins of feudal society has not done away with class antagonisms. It has but established new classes, new conditions of oppression, new forms of struggle in place of the old ones. Our epoch, the epoch of the bourgeoisie, possesses, however, this distinctive feature: it has simplified the class antagonisms. Society as a whole is more and more splitting up into two great hostile camps, into two great classes, directly facing each other: Bourgeoisie and Proletariat. [001-004]

This simplification is itself a historical product. The intermediate classes that populated earlier societies — small tradespeople, shopkeepers, handicraftsmen, peasants — are progressively *"precipitated into the proletariat"* (001-040) by the advance of modern industry. The [[bourgeoisie]] grows ever smaller and richer, the [[proletariat]] ever larger and more concentrated, and the space between them progressively empties out.

### The Political Character of Class Struggle

The Manifesto insists that class struggle is inherently political. Describing the development of the proletariat's organization, Marx and Engels trace how scattered local conflicts, at first waged by individual workers against individual bourgeois, are gradually centralized through improved means of communication into a national struggle between classes (001-036--001-037). This process culminates in a definitive assertion:

> The lower strata of the middle class — the small tradespeople, shopkeepers, retired tradesmen generally, the handicraftsmen and peasants — all these sink gradually into the proletariat, partly because their diminutive capital does not suffice for the scale on which Modern Industry is carried on, and is swamped in the competition with the large capitalists, partly because their specialized skill is rendered worthless by new methods of production. Thus the proletariat is recruited from all classes of the population. [001-033]

> But every class struggle is a political struggle. [001-037]

The assertion that *"every class struggle is a political struggle"* (001-037) is one of the most frequently quoted lines in the Manifesto. It underscores the argument that economic conflicts — over wages, working conditions, the length of the working day — cannot be resolved within the framework of the existing order. Even when workers form trade unions to defend their wages, the logic of their struggle pushes toward political organization — the formation of the proletariat into a class and, ultimately, into a political party (001-038). The political dimension of class struggle is inseparable from its economic content: the state, far from being a neutral arbiter between classes, is *"but a committee for managing the common affairs of the whole bourgeoisie"* (001-014).

### The Proletariat as Revolutionary Class

Not all classes are revolutionary. The Manifesto draws a sharp distinction between the [[proletariat]] and the intermediate classes that also find themselves in conflict with the [[bourgeoisie]].

> Of all the classes that stand face to face with the bourgeoisie today, the proletariat alone is a really revolutionary class. The other classes decay and finally disappear in the face of Modern Industry; the proletariat is its special and essential product. [001-042]

The lower middle class — the small manufacturer, the shopkeeper, the artisan, the peasant — fights the bourgeoisie only *"to save from extinction their existence as fractions of the middle class"* (001-042). They are therefore *"not revolutionary, but conservative. Nay more, they are reactionary, for they try to roll back the wheel of history"* (001-042). The lumpenproletariat, the *"dangerous class"* of social scum, is even less reliable: it may *"be swept into the movement by a proletarian revolution"* but its conditions of life *"prepare it far more for the part of a bribed tool of reactionary intrigue"* (001-043).

### Communism as the Expression of Class Struggle

The theoretical conclusions of the Communists are not abstract principles invented by reformers but *"merely express, in general terms, actual relations springing from an existing class struggle, from a historical movement going on under our very eyes"* (002-008). Communism is thus presented not as an ideal to be imposed on society but as the theoretical expression of a real movement already underway.

The Communists distinguish themselves from other working-class parties by always representing *"the interests of the movement as a whole"* (002-005): in the national struggles of different countries they emphasize the common interests of the entire proletariat, and across the various stages of development they keep the ultimate goal — *"formation of the proletariat into a class, overthrow of the bourgeois supremacy, conquest of political power by the proletariat"* (002-007) — in view.

As the class struggle develops and takes definite shape, earlier forms of socialism that stand apart from the contest — seeking to reconcile classes rather than recognize their irreconcilable antagonism — *"lose all practical value and all theoretical justification"* (006-012). The rival socialist doctrines surveyed in Section III of the Manifesto are judged not by their abstract moral worth but by their relationship to the developing class struggle: those that stand above it are dismissed as utopian; those that try to deaden it are condemned as reactionary.

> In proportion as the modern class struggle develops and takes definite shape, this fantastic standing apart from the contest, these fantastic attacks on it, lose all practical value and all theoretical justification. They, therefore, endeavour, and that consistently, to deaden the class struggle and to reconcile the class antagonisms. [006-012]

## Historical Context

The concept of class struggle did not originate with Marx and Engels. French historians of the Restoration period, notably Augustin Thierry and Francois Guizot, had already interpreted the [[french-revolution]] in terms of class conflict between the Third Estate and the aristocracy. The British political economists had analyzed the tripartite division of society into landlords, capitalists, and laborers — though they treated this division as natural rather than historically transient.

What Marx and Engels added was the materialist grounding: class struggles are rooted in the changing conditions of production, and each mode of production generates its own characteristic class antagonisms. By 1848, the intensifying conflict between industrial capitalists and an increasingly organized working class gave the concept immediate political urgency, especially in the wake of the 1830 and [[revolutions-of-1848|1848 revolutions]] across Europe. The Manifesto was written in the heat of that moment — commissioned by the [[communist-league]] and published in February 1848, just as revolutionary uprisings were beginning to sweep the continent.

The period 1830–1848 had seen the first sustained wave of independent working-class organization: the Lyons silk-weavers' revolts (1831, 1834), the Chartist movement in Britain (1838–1848), and the Silesian weavers' uprising (1844). These struggles provided the practical ground from which Marx and Engels' theory abstracted its general conclusions.

## Related Concepts

Class struggle is the dynamic mechanism connecting the [[bourgeoisie]] and the [[proletariat]] in an antagonistic relationship. It is driven by the contradictions inherent in the [[means-of-production]] and the accumulation of [[capital]]. The Manifesto's analysis of class struggle is grounded in the broader framework of [[historical-materialism]], which holds that the economic base of society determines its political and ideological superstructure. The concepts of [[revolution]] and [[dictatorship-of-the-proletariat]] emerge as the practical conclusions drawn from the theory of class struggle.

Crucially, class struggle is not limited to the economic sphere. The Manifesto's analysis insists that legal institutions, moral codes, religious beliefs, and political ideologies are all shaped by — and in turn shape — the ongoing conflict between classes. The abolition of existing property relations (002-013), the transformation of the family (002-027), and the withering away of national antagonisms (002-033) are all understood as moments within the broader class struggle, not as reforms to be pursued in isolation from it.

## Significance

The theory of class struggle is arguably the most far-reaching and controversial element of the Marxist intellectual tradition. It provided a framework for understanding not only economic relations but also law, morality, religion, and political ideology as expressions of class interests. Whether accepted or rejected, the claim that class conflict drives historical development has shaped modern historiography, sociology, and political theory. In practical politics, the idea that the working class must organize as a class — and that this organization culminates in the conquest of political power — provided the strategic foundation for socialist and communist movements worldwide.

The Manifesto's opening claim about class struggle is simultaneously a descriptive historical thesis and a prescriptive political program. As description, it asserts that understanding any society requires identifying its fundamental class antagonism. As prescription, it calls on the proletariat to become conscious of its historical role and to organize for the overthrow of bourgeois rule — to transform the ongoing, unconscious class struggle into a conscious, organized, and ultimately revolutionary one (001-037--001-038).
