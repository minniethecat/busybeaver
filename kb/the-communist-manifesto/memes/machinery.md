In the Manifesto, **machinery** is not merely a technical instrument but a world-historical force. It is machinery — together with steam power, the division of labour, and the application of chemistry — that "revolutionised industrial production" [(001-008)] and, in doing so, created the material conditions for the modern class struggle. Machinery, in Marx and Engels' account, is the lever by which the bourgeoisie transforms the world and involuntarily produces its own gravediggers.

## Definition

Machinery, as deployed in the Manifesto, refers to the mechanised means of production that replaced hand labour and manufacture in the course of the Industrial Revolution. It is one of the central forces that "the bourgeoisie, during its rule of scarce one hundred years, has created" — forces "more massive and more colossal productive forces than have all preceding generations together" [(001-022)]. Machinery is both the emblem of bourgeois achievement and the mechanism through which labour is degraded, homogenised, and ultimately organised into a revolutionary class.

"[[modern-industry|Modern Industry]]" in the Manifesto denotes the factory system that arose when "steam and machinery revolutionised industrial production" [(001-008)]. This was not a gradual evolution but a qualitative rupture: the subordination of the worker to the machine, the deskilling of labour, and the concentration of formerly scattered workers into collective workplaces. "Owing to the extensive use of machinery and to division of labour, the work of the proletarians has lost all individual character, and, consequently, all charm for the workman" [(001-029)].

## In the Manifesto

### Machinery and the Bourgeois Revolution in Production

The Manifesto presents machinery as the culmination of a historical sequence. Feudal industry, "monopolised by closed guilds, now no longer sufficed for the growing wants of the new markets. The manufacturing system took its place" [(001-007)]. But even "manufacture no longer sufficed. Thereupon, steam and machinery revolutionised industrial production" [(001-008)]. Each stage — guild, manufacture, machinery — represents a quantum leap in productive capacity, and each leap destroys the social relations that preceded it.

The bourgeoisie's unique historical role is to be "the involuntary promoter" of this development. The Manifesto describes the vast increase in productive forces — "subjection of Nature's forces to man, machinery, application of chemistry to industry and agriculture, steam-navigation, railways, electric telegraphs" [(001-022)] — as achievements that earlier generations could not have imagined possible.

### Machinery and the Degradation of Labour

The introduction of machinery transforms the character of labour itself. "Owing to the extensive use of machinery and to division of labour, the work of the proletarians has lost all individual character, and, consequently, all charm for the workman. He becomes an appendage of the machine, and it is only the most simple, most monotonous, and most easily acquired knack, that is required of him" [(001-029)].

This degradation is not incidental to machinery but intrinsic to its capitalist deployment. The worker, instead of directing the tool, is directed by the machine; skill is transferred from the labourer to the mechanism. "In proportion as machinery obliterates all distinctions of labour," the wages of the worker are "nearly everywhere reduced to the same level" [(001-036)]. The result is a homogenised, interchangeable labour force — the raw material of the proletariat as a class.

### The Workers' Response: Machine-Breaking

The Manifesto records the earliest phase of proletarian resistance: "They smash to pieces machinery, they set factories ablaze" [(001-034)]. This Luddite response — attacking the physical instrument of their degradation rather than the social system that deploys it — represents, for Marx and Engels, an immature form of class consciousness. "At this stage the labourers still form an incoherent mass scattered over the whole country, and broken up by their mutual competition" [(001-034)]. The destruction of machinery gives way to organised trade unionism and, ultimately, to political organisation — the recognition that the enemy is not the machine but the class that owns it.

### Machinery in Feudal Socialism's Critique

The Manifesto also records how machinery featured in the reactionary critique of capitalism. The "disastrous effects of machinery and division of labour" [(004-017)] formed part of the feudal socialists' indictment of bourgeois society. Marx and Engels dismiss this critique not because it is factually wrong — they themselves describe those same "disastrous effects" — but because it is made in bad faith: the feudalists lament the effects while ignoring that their own expropriation occurred through the same logic of economic development they now deplore.

He becomes an appendage of the machine, and it is only the most simple, most monotonous, and most easily acquired knack, that is required of him.

## Historical Context

When the Manifesto was published in 1848, the mechanisation of industry was well underway in Britain and advancing rapidly in France and the German states. The power loom, the steam engine, and the spinning jenny had transformed textile production; the railway had revolutionised transport. The term "Industrial Revolution" — though not used by Marx and Engels themselves — captures this transformation of productive technique that generated the world of the Manifesto.

The Luddite disturbances of 1811–1816, in which British handloom weavers destroyed the machines that threatened their livelihoods, formed the historical backdrop to the Manifesto's account of machine-breaking. By 1848, however, machine-breaking had given way to Chartism and trade unionism — the transition from attacking things to attacking social relations that the Manifesto theorises.
