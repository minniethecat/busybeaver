**Freedom**, in the Manifesto's critique, is the central ideological claim of bourgeois society — and its central mystification.

> By freedom is meant, under the present bourgeois conditions of production, free trade, free selling and buying. [(002-025)]

The Manifesto does not deny that bourgeois society has abolished feudal unfreedom; it insists that what it has put in place is not genuine human freedom but "that single, unconscionable freedom — Free Trade" [(001-013)].

## Definition

In Marx and Engels' usage, freedom is not an abstract universal but a historically specific form of domination. The bourgeoisie proclaims freedom as a universal right, but the content of this right is the freedom of capital: the freedom to buy and sell, to own property, to employ wage-labour, to compete.

> The abolition of bourgeois individuality, bourgeois independence, and bourgeois freedom is undoubtedly aimed at. [(002-024)]

The Manifesto distinguishes three senses of freedom:

1. **Bourgeois freedom** — the freedom of the market, of contract, of [[private-property]]. This is the only freedom that bourgeois society recognises, and it is a freedom that presupposes the existence of individuals who lack all property [(002-025)].

2. **Personal freedom under capital** — the freedom of the worker to sell their labour-power, which is the freedom to choose which capitalist to be exploited by. The proletarian "is without property; his relation to his wife and children has no longer anything in common with the bourgeois family-relations; modern industrial labour, modern subjection to capital, the same in England as in France, in America as in Germany, has stripped him of every trace of national character" [(001-044)].

3. **Genuine freedom** — the freedom that will be realised when class distinctions have been abolished and "the free development of each is the condition for the free development of all" [(002-078)].

## In the Manifesto

### Freedom as Bourgeois Ideology

The Manifesto opens its critique of bourgeois freedom in Section I, where it describes the historical role of the [[bourgeoisie]].

> It has pitilessly torn asunder the motley feudal ties that bound man to his 'natural superiors,' and has left remaining no other nexus between man and man than naked self-interest, than callous 'cash payment.' [(001-013)]

The bourgeoisie represents itself as the liberator of humanity, the destroyer of feudal chains — and there is a real historical truth in this claim. But the freedom it installs is the freedom of the market, and the market reduces all human relations to "exchange value" [(001-013)].

The Manifesto's most concentrated discussion of freedom comes in Section II, where Marx and Engels respond to the bourgeois objection that [[communism]] "abolishes" freedom. "And the abolition of this state of things is called by the bourgeois, abolition of individuality and freedom! And rightly so. The abolition of bourgeois individuality, bourgeois independence, and bourgeois freedom is undoubtedly aimed at" [(002-024)].

### The Freedom of Capital

The Manifesto insists on the class character of freedom. "By freedom is meant, under the present bourgeois conditions of production, free trade, free selling and buying" [(002-025)]. This freedom depends on the existence of a class that has nothing to sell but its labour-power.

> But if selling and buying disappears, free selling and buying disappears also. [(002-026)]

The freedom of the market is the freedom of those who own [[capital]]; for those who own nothing, it is the freedom to be exploited.

This argument is extended in the Manifesto's discussion of "freedom of commerce" as a driving force of bourgeois globalisation.

> National differences and antagonisms between peoples are daily more and more vanishing, owing to the development of the [[bourgeoisie]], to freedom of commerce, to the [[world-market]], to uniformity in the mode of production and in the conditions of life corresponding thereto. [(002-053)]

[[free-trade|Free trade]] is the mechanism by which the bourgeoisie draws all nations "into civilisation" — but this civilisation is one of "naked, shameless, direct, brutal exploitation" [(001-013)].

### The Communist Conception of Freedom

Against bourgeois freedom, the Manifesto sets a different conception — one that can only be realised through the abolition of class society.

> In place of the old bourgeois society, with its classes and class antagonisms, we shall have an association, in which the free development of each is the condition for the free development of all. [(002-078)]

This is not the freedom of the isolated individual but the freedom of associated producers — a freedom that presupposes the abolition of [[private-property]] and the collective control of the conditions of production.

### Freedom and the Charge of Abolishing Property

The bourgeois charge that [[communism]] would abolish [[private-property|property]] is inseparable from the charge that it would abolish freedom. Marx and Engels meet this charge directly by exposing the class content of bourgeois notions of property and liberty.

> We Communists have been reproached with the desire of abolishing the right of personally acquiring property as the fruit of a man's own labour, which property is alleged to be the groundwork of all personal freedom, activity and independence. [(002-013)]

The reply is devastating in its simplicity: the property that the bourgeois claims as the fruit of his labour is in fact [[capital]] — the accumulated product of the [[exploitation]] of [[wage-labour]]. "Hard-won, self-acquired, self-earned property! Do you mean the property of the petty artisan and of the small peasant, a form of property that preceded the bourgeois form? There is no need to abolish that; the development of [[industry]] has abolished it and is daily abolishing it" [(002-014)].

The concept of bourgeois [[individuality]] is tied to this form of property. The Manifesto's critique of freedom is completed by its critique of "eternal truths":

> "There are, besides, eternal truths, such as Freedom, Justice, etc. that are common to all states of society. But Communism abolishes eternal truths, it abolishes all religion, and all morality, instead of constituting them on a new basis; it therefore acts in contradiction to all past historical experience." [(002-062)]

Marx and Engels respond that every form of society has had its own ideas of freedom, justice, and morality — ideas that correspond to the prevailing mode of production and class relations. The "eternal truth" of freedom under bourgeois society is simply the ideological reflex of the [[mode-of-production|capitalist mode of production]].

## Historical Context

### The French Revolution and Bourgeois Liberty

The Manifesto's critique of freedom is directed against the political economy of its time. The classical economists — Smith, Ricardo, Say — had celebrated [[free-trade]] as the natural expression of human liberty. The [[french-revolution|French Revolution]] had proclaimed the "Rights of Man," including liberty, as universal principles. The Manifesto does not reject these declarations as mere hypocrisy; it shows that they express, in idealised form, the practical requirements of capitalist production.

The concept of freedom was also at stake in the political struggles of the 1840s. The working-class movement demanded political liberties — freedom of the press, freedom of association, universal suffrage. The Manifesto supports these demands as steps towards the political organisation of the [[proletariat]], but insists that they do not constitute [[emancipation]] in themselves.

> Bourgeois freedom of the press, bourgeois legislation, bourgeois liberty and equality are the forms in which class rule is exercised, not the transcendence of class rule. [(004-031)]

### Freedom of Conscience and Religious Liberty

The Manifesto also addresses the concept of freedom in the domain of ideas and [[religion]]. In a passage that links intellectual freedom to the material conditions of [[competition]], Marx and Engels argue:

> The ideas of religious liberty and freedom of conscience merely gave expression to the sway of free competition within the domain of knowledge. [(002-060)]

Just as the bourgeoisie presents economic competition as the natural expression of freedom, so too it presents intellectual competition — the free market of ideas — as the natural expression of intellectual liberty. Both are expressions of the same underlying reality: the rule of [[capital]] over all spheres of human life.

## Freedom and the Bourgeois State

The Manifesto treats the [[state]] as the political form through which bourgeois freedom is guaranteed and enforced. Bourgeois freedom is not simply a spontaneous feature of market society — it depends on a coercive apparatus that protects [[private-property]], enforces contracts, and suppresses challenges to the existing order. [[political-power|Political power]] is "merely the organised power of one class for oppressing another" [(002-064)].

This paradox lies at the heart of the Marxian critique of bourgeois freedom. The worker is formally free — free to enter or refuse any particular wage contract. But the worker is not free from the necessity of entering a wage contract in the first place, because the means of subsistence are monopolised by the capitalist class. The [[state]], through its protection of property rights and its suppression of [[proletariat|workers']] collective action, ensures that this condition is maintained. The worker's formal legal freedom is entirely compatible with — indeed, it is the condition of — real economic compulsion.

The Manifesto also discusses the way that bourgeois freedom is extended and deepened through political struggles. The [[ten-hours-bill|Ten Hours' Bill]] (1847) in England demonstrated that the working class could, through political agitation, compel the state to limit the freedom of capital — limiting the working day was an act of interference with the "freedom" of the capitalist to exploit labour at will. Such reforms did not abolish [[exploitation]], but they showed that the [[state]] could be forced to act against the immediate interests of the [[bourgeoisie]], however temporarily [(001-057)].

The [[communist-league|Communist League]] and other workers' organisations of the 1840s understood that the struggle for political liberties — freedom of the press, freedom of association, universal suffrage — was a necessary stage in the development of working-class consciousness and organisation. The Manifesto does not oppose these demands; it insists only that they are means, not ends. The end is the conquest of [[political-power]] by the [[proletariat]], the abolition of class distinctions, and the establishment of an [[association]] in which "the free development of each is the condition for the free development of all" [(002-078)].
