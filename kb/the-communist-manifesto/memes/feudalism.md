Feudalism, in the *Communist Manifesto*, is the pre-capitalist [[mode-of-production]] from whose "ruins" modern bourgeois society "sprouted" (001-004). Marx and Engels treat feudalism not as a static traditional order but as a specific economic structure with its own [[class-struggle|class antagonisms]], productive relations, and internal contradictions that ultimately made it vulnerable to bourgeois revolution.

## The Feudal Social Order

The *Manifesto* characterises feudal society as a hierarchical system of personal dependence. In the celebrated passage on class history, Marx and Engels enumerate the feudal class structure: "feudal lords, vassals, guild-masters, journeymen, apprentices, serfs" — a complex gradation in which "subordinate gradations" existed within almost every class (001-003). Unlike the simplified class antagonism of bourgeois society ([[bourgeoisie]] versus [[proletariat]]), feudal society was marked by a multiplicity of status ranks and personal bonds.

### The Guild System and Closed Production

The feudal system of industry was defined by its closed, monopolistic character. Industrial production was "monopolised by closed guilds" (001-007), with strict regulation of who could produce, what could be made, and how goods could be exchanged. The [[guild-master]], [[journeyman]], and apprentice hierarchy within the [[guild]] system constituted a rigid economic order in which each person's place was fixed by custom and regulation rather than [[competition]] or contract.

This system, adequate for a limited local market, proved incapable of meeting the demands created by colonial expansion and the growth of [[trade]].

> The feudal system of industry, under which industrial production was monopolised by closed guilds, now no longer sufficed for the growing wants of the new markets. The manufacturing system took its place. (001-007)

### Class Structure and Personal Dependence

The feudal class hierarchy was distinctive for its "manifold gradation" (001-003). Marx and Engels name six distinct ranks — feudal lords, vassals, guild-masters, journeymen, apprentices, and serfs — each of which contained further "subordinate gradations." This stood in sharp contrast to the emerging capitalist order, which relentlessly simplified class relations into the binary opposition of [[bourgeoisie]] and [[proletariat]].

> In the earlier epochs of history, we find almost everywhere a complicated arrangement of society into various orders, a manifold gradation of social rank. In ancient Rome we have patricians, knights, plebeians, slaves; in the Middle Ages, feudal lords, vassals, guild-masters, journeymen, apprentices, serfs; in almost all of these classes, again, subordinate gradations. (001-003)

## The Bourgeoisie's Destruction of Feudalism

A central theme of the *Manifesto* is the bourgeoisie's revolutionary destruction of feudal relations. "The bourgeoisie, wherever it has got the upper hand, has put an end to all feudal, patriarchal, idyllic relations," Marx and Engels declare. "It has pitilessly torn asunder the motley feudal ties that bound man to his 'natural superiors,' and has left remaining no other nexus between man and man than naked self-interest, than callous 'cash payment'" (001-013). The emotional language — "pitilessly," "torn asunder," "drowned" — registers both the violence of the transition and its irreversibility.

### Economic Weapons and Internal Contradictions

The bourgeoisie's weapons against feudalism were economic rather than military. "The weapons with which the bourgeoisie felled feudalism to the ground," Marx and Engels observe with grim irony, "are now turned against the bourgeoisie itself" (001-026). These weapons — [[modern-industry]], the [[world-market]], the concentration of [[productive-forces]] — were generated *within* feudal society: "the means of production and of exchange, on whose foundation the bourgeoisie built itself up, were generated in feudal society" (001-023).

The destruction of feudalism was not an external assault but an internal decomposition. Feudal [[relations-of-production]] became "fetters" on the development of productive forces (001-023), and the bourgeoisie, which had grown up within the interstices of feudal society, burst those fetters asunder:

> The means of production and of exchange, on whose foundation the bourgeoisie built itself up, were generated in feudal society. At a certain stage in the development of these means of production and of exchange, the conditions under which feudal society produced and exchanged, the feudal organisation of agriculture and manufacturing industry, in one word, the feudal relations of property became no longer compatible with the already developed productive forces; they became so many fetters. They had to be burst asunder; they were burst asunder. (001-023)

### The Bourgeoisie's Political Ascent

The destruction of feudalism was also a political process. Marx and Engels trace the bourgeoisie's political trajectory from subordination to dominance: "An oppressed class under the sway of the feudal nobility" (001-011), the bourgeoisie gradually accumulated political power in urban communes, then served monarchies "as a counterpoise against the nobility," until finally conquering "exclusive political sway" in the modern representative [[state]]. The feudal aristocracy, once the unquestioned ruling class, was reduced to a political vestige.

## Feudal Property

The question of property is central to the *Manifesto*'s treatment of feudalism. Marx and Engels distinguish feudal property from both ancient and bourgeois property, noting that each [[mode-of-production]] has its own distinctive property form. The [[french-revolution]] "abolished feudal property in favour of bourgeois property" (002-010), a historical precedent they invoke to argue that the abolition of bourgeois property in favour of communal property is no more arbitrary a transformation.

> The French Revolution, for example, abolished feudal property in favour of bourgeois property. (002-010)

The polemic against bourgeois defenders of [[private-property]] turns on this point. Marx and Engels argue that what the bourgeoisie "see clearly in the case of ancient property, what you admit in the case of feudal property, you are of course forbidden to admit in the case of your own bourgeois form of property" (002-037). The historical dissolution of feudal property is treated by the bourgeoisie as a natural and progressive event; the proposed dissolution of bourgeois property is treated as an unnatural crime. The double standard reveals bourgeois property as historically contingent rather than eternal.

## Feudal Ideology and Its Afterlife

The *Manifesto* also attends to the ideological dimensions of feudalism's collapse. "When Christian ideas succumbed in the 18th century to rationalist ideas, feudal society fought its death battle with the then revolutionary bourgeoisie" (002-060). The ideological struggle was not a separate realm but an integral part of the material conflict.

### Feudal Socialism and Reactionary Nostalgia

Even after feudalism's defeat, remnants of feudal consciousness persisted. Marx and Engels devote an entire section of Chapter III to [[feudal-socialism]], the ideology of the dispossessed [[aristocracy]] that "waved the proletarian alms-bag in front for a banner" while displaying "on their hindquarters the old feudal coats of arms" (004-005). The feudal socialists, including the [[french-legitimists]] and [[young-england]], claimed to champion the working class against bourgeois exploitation, but their true grievance was their own displacement from power.

> In pointing out that their mode of exploitation was different to that of the bourgeoisie, the feudalists forget that they exploited under circumstances and conditions that were quite different, and that are now antiquated. In showing that, under their rule, the modern proletariat never existed, they forget that the modern bourgeoisie is the necessary offspring of their own form of society. (004-007)

The feudal socialist critique of bourgeois society is, in Marx and Engels's judgement, "half lamentation, half lampoon; half echo of the past, half menace of the future" — politically impotent because it cannot grasp that capitalism is the historical product of feudalism itself.

### Surviving Remnants in Semi-Feudal States

The *Manifesto* acknowledges that feudalism was not everywhere equally vanquished. In Germany, where capitalist development lagged behind England and France, the bourgeoisie "had just begun its contest with feudal absolutism" (004-022) at the time of writing. The persistence of semi-feudal conditions in Germany shaped the specific character of [[german-socialism]], which Marx and Engels deride as a derivative and reactionary doctrine that translated French socialist ideas into "the language of German speculative philosophy" while leaving the fight against the German "feudal squirearchy" (007-005) incomplete.

## The Historical Logic of Feudalism's Destruction

### Feudalism as a Model of Revolutionary Transition

Feudalism serves two crucial argumentative functions in the *Manifesto*. First, it historicises bourgeois society: by showing that capitalism emerged from feudalism through a revolutionary transformation, Marx and Engels undermine the claim that bourgeois property relations are eternal or natural. Second, it models the logic of revolutionary transition: if the bourgeoisie could overthrow feudalism when feudal property relations became "fetters" on the development of [[productive-forces]] (001-023), then the [[proletariat]] is equally entitled to overthrow bourgeois property relations when they become fetters on further development.

The weapons metaphor captures this dialectic precisely:

> The weapons with which the bourgeoisie felled feudalism to the ground are now turned against the bourgeoisie itself. (001-026)

The same productive forces that made feudalism obsolete — [[modern-industry]], the [[world-market]], the concentration of capital, the organisation of labour — now threaten to make capitalism itself obsolete. The history of feudalism's destruction is, in effect, a preview of capitalism's own future.

### The Serf as Historical Precedent

The *Manifesto* draws a pointed analogy between the feudal serf and the modern worker. "The serf, in the period of serfdom, raised himself to membership in the commune, just as the petty bourgeois, under the yoke of feudal absolutism, managed to develop into a bourgeois" (001-049). But whereas both serf and petty bourgeois could historically improve their condition within their respective systems, the modern proletarian "sinks deeper and deeper below the conditions of existence of his own class" (001-049). The structural immiseration of the proletariat makes bourgeois rule uniquely unsustainable — a historical novelty that feudalism's own internal mobility did not possess.

> The feudal aristocracy was not the only class that was ruined by the bourgeoisie, not the only class whose conditions of existence pined and perished in the atmosphere of modern bourgeois society. The mediaeval burgesses and the small peasant proprietors were the precursors of the modern bourgeoisie. (004-014)
