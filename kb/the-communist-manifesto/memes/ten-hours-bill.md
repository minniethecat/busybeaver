> Thus the ten-hours' bill in England was carried. [(001-038)]

The **Ten Hours' Bill** — formally the Factory Act of 1847 — was a landmark piece of British labour legislation that limited the working day for women and young persons (aged 13–18) in textile mills to ten hours. In the Manifesto, Marx and Engels cite it as the prime example of how the organized working class, by "taking advantage of the divisions among the bourgeoisie itself" (001-038), compels legislative recognition of its particular interests — a strategic insight that grounds the Communist tactic of exploiting contradictions within the ruling class to win reforms that strengthen the proletariat for the larger struggle.

## In the Manifesto

### Section I: The Ten Hours' Bill as Class-Struggle Victory

The Ten Hours' Bill appears in the Manifesto at a crucial point in the analysis of proletarian class formation. Marx and Engels describe how the organization of the proletariat into a class "is continually being upset again by the competition between the workers themselves. But it ever rises up again, stronger, firmer, mightier" (001-037–001-038). In this dialectic of defeat and resurgence, legislative victories wrung from the bourgeoisie play a specific role: they demonstrate to the working class that organization can win concrete gains, building confidence and cohesion for future struggles.

The Ten Hours' Bill is the concrete example. It "was carried" not because the bourgeoisie was persuaded of its justice but because divisions within the bourgeoisie — between the landed aristocracy (which supported factory regulation to weaken its industrial rivals) and the manufacturers — created an opening that organized working-class pressure could exploit. This is communist realpolitik in miniature: the working class does not wait for a unified "revolutionary situation" but fights wherever and whenever it can, using every division in the enemy camp to advance its own interests, even if only partially and temporarily.

## Historical Background

### The Factory Reform Movement

The struggle for the ten-hour day was the longest and most bitterly contested reform campaign in early-Victorian Britain. The first Factory Act (1802) was a dead letter; subsequent legislation in 1819, 1825, and 1831 made modest improvements but left the working day effectively unlimited. The key breakthrough came with the Factory Act of 1833, which banned the employment of children under nine in textile mills and limited children aged 9–13 to nine hours of work per day — but left adult workers unprotected.

The ten-hour movement was led by a coalition of Tory radicals (including Lord Ashley, later the Earl of Shaftesbury), who opposed industrial capitalism on paternalist grounds, and working-class organizations, particularly the Short Time Committees that mobilized factory workers in Lancashire and Yorkshire. The movement's tactics combined parliamentary lobbying with mass agitation: monster petitions, public meetings, and in some cases, strike action. The alliance was uneasy — the Tory reformers wanted to regulate capitalism, not abolish it — but it was effective.

### The 1847 Act and Its Circumvention

The Factory Act of 1847 (10 & 11 Vict. c. 29), commonly known as the Ten Hours' Bill, received Royal Assent on 8 June 1847 and came into force on 1 July 1847. It limited the working day for women and young persons to ten hours on the first five days of the week and eight hours on Saturday — an effective limit of 58 hours per week.

But the manufacturers almost immediately devised methods of evasion. The most notorious was the "relay system" — staggering the working hours of protected workers so that the machinery, operated by unprotected adult males, could run for fifteen or sixteen hours a day. This turned the Ten Hours' Act into a legal fiction and precipitated a further round of struggle that culminated in the Factory Act of 1850, which established a "normal working day" from 6 a.m. to 6 p.m. (with a 90-minute meal break), effectively limiting all workers' hours to ten and a half.

## Marxist Analysis

### Reform as a School of Class Struggle

For Marx, the Ten Hours' Bill was not merely a humanitarian measure — it was a demonstration of the strategic principle that reforms won through class struggle strengthen the proletariat's capacity for further struggle. Every legislative victory, however limited, educates the working class in the nature of political power: it learns that the state is not a neutral arbiter but an instrument of class rule that can be compelled, by organized pressure, to make concessions. These concessions are never permanent — the bourgeoisie will always seek to erode or circumvent them — but the struggle to defend them is itself a school of class consciousness.

Marx returned to the Ten Hours' Bill repeatedly in his later work. In *Capital*, Volume I, the struggle for the normal working day occupies a central chapter, in which the Ten Hours' Act figures as a turning point: "the first time that in broad daylight the political economy of the middle class succumbed to the political economy of the working class" (*Capital*, Ch. 10). The legislative limitation of the working day was, for Marx, a victory of principle as well as practice: it demonstrated that the length of the working day was not determined by natural law or economic necessity but by the balance of class forces — and that the working class, when organized, could shift that balance in its favour.

### The Limits of Reform

But Marx was also clear about the limits of factory legislation. The Ten Hours' Bill did not abolish exploitation; it merely limited its duration. It improved the conditions of wage slavery without abolishing wage slavery itself. The bourgeoisie, once it had accommodated itself to the new limits, found that shorter hours could be compensated for by intensified exploitation — speeding up machinery, tightening discipline, extracting more labour in less time. The struggle for the ten-hour day was thus, from a communist standpoint, a necessary but insufficient step: necessary because it built working-class organization and demonstrated that capitalism could be constrained, and insufficient because it left the fundamental relation of capital and wage labour intact. The ultimate aim remained the abolition of the wages system itself.
