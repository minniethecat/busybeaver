# Minimum Wage

## Definition

The minimum wage, in the Manifesto's political economy, is not a legally mandated floor on wages but an analytical concept: it is *"that quantum of the means of subsistence which is absolutely requisite to keep the labourer in bare existence as a labourer"* (002-021). Marx and Engels argue that the average price of wage-labour necessarily tends toward this minimum — the cost of keeping the worker alive and able to work — because competition among workers for employment drives wages down, while the reserve army of the unemployed exerts constant downward pressure. What the worker receives is not a share of the value he creates but merely enough to reproduce his labour-power for another day.

## In the Manifesto

The concept appears in the Manifesto's Section II, during the polemic against bourgeois objections to communism. The bourgeois charges that communism would abolish property; Marx and Engels reply that capital is not a personal possession but a social power, and that *"the average price of wage-labour is the minimum wage, i.e., that quantum of the means of subsistence which is absolutely requisite to keep the labourer in bare existence as a labourer"* (002-021). The implication is clear: under capitalism, the worker already possesses nothing beyond bare subsistence. The abolition of bourgeois property would not deprive the worker of anything he actually owns — because what he "owns" is nothing more than the means of his own survival.

This argument is grounded in the broader analysis of wage-labour developed in Section I. There, Marx and Engels describe the relentless downward pressure on wages generated by the capitalist mode of production: *"in proportion as the use of machinery and division of labour increases, in the same proportion the burden of toil also increases ... the more the work increases, the wage decreases"* (001-029). The introduction of machinery does not lighten the worker's burden but intensifies it while simultaneously cheapening the labour-power that must compete with it. The result is that *"the cost of production of a workman is restricted, almost entirely, to the means of subsistence that he requires for his maintenance, and for the propagation of his race"* (002-021).

The tendency of wages toward the subsistence minimum is not a contingent outcome but a structural feature of capitalism. The Manifesto connects it to the logic of competition: *"The growing competition among the bourgeois, and the resulting commercial crises, make the wages of the workers ever more fluctuating"* (001-036). Wages may rise temporarily during periods of expansion, but crisis, mechanization, and the competition among workers for scarce employment drive them back toward the minimum. The worker's position is thus one of structural precarity — his income is never secure, and his bargaining power is systematically undermined by the very development of the productive forces.

## Historical Context

The idea that wages tend toward a subsistence minimum has roots in classical political economy. David Ricardo's "iron law of wages" held that the natural price of labour is that which enables workers to subsist and reproduce. Ferdinand Lassalle would later popularize this as the "brazen law of wages." Marx and Engels adopted the core insight — that wages under capitalism are driven toward subsistence — but gave it a historical rather than a natural-law foundation. The minimum wage was not a law of nature but a product of specific social relations: the separation of the worker from the means of production, the compulsion to sell labour-power as a commodity, and the competitive dynamics of the capitalist market. In the 1840s, the condition of the English working class — documented by Engels in *The Condition of the Working Class in England* (1845) — provided grim empirical confirmation. Factory workers in Manchester routinely earned barely enough to keep body and soul together, and periods of unemployment reduced them to destitution.

## Related Concepts

The minimum wage is directly derived from the nature of [[wage-labour]] — the sale of labour-power as a commodity under capitalism. It is the mechanism through which [[exploitation]] operates, since the worker receives only the cost of subsistence while producing value far exceeding that cost. The concept is inseparable from [[capital]], which can only accumulate by keeping the price of labour-power at or near the minimum. The structural tendency of wages toward subsistence intensifies the antagonism between [[bourgeoisie]] and [[proletariat]], and is exacerbated by [[competition]] both among capitalists (driving down prices and wages) and among workers (competing for employment).

## Significance

The concept of the minimum wage — that the price of labour-power tends toward bare subsistence — is one of the most politically explosive claims in the Manifesto. If wages are structurally driven toward subsistence, then no amount of moral exhortation or individual effort can lift the working class out of poverty within the framework of capitalism. Trade unions may win temporary improvements, but the structural tendency reasserts itself. This analysis undergirds the Manifesto's political conclusion: the emancipation of the working class cannot be achieved within the wage system but requires the abolition of wage-labour itself. *"What, then, does the abolition of {bourgeois} property mean to the proletarian? The abolition of the wage system, the condition of his existence"* (002-033). The minimum wage is thus not merely an economic concept but a revolutionary one: it demonstrates that the interests of capital and labour are irreconcilable, and that the logic of capitalism cannot be reformed away.
