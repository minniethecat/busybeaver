**Trade** in the Manifesto is the dynamic force that propels the bourgeoisie onto the world stage. "The discovery of America, the rounding of the Cape, opened up fresh ground for the rising bourgeoisie. The East-Indian and Chinese markets, the colonisation of America, trade with the colonies, the increase in the means of exchange and in commodities generally, gave to commerce, to navigation, to industry, an impulse never before known" [(001-006)]. Trade is not merely the exchange of goods; it is the mechanism through which the bourgeoisie transforms the world after its own image, "compels all nations, on pain of extinction, to adopt the bourgeois mode of production" [(001-017)].

## Definition

Trade, in the Manifesto's usage, encompasses the full spectrum of commercial activity — from local exchange to colonial plunder to the "universal inter-dependence of nations" [(001-017)] that constitutes the world market. "The need of a constantly expanding market for its products chases the bourgeoisie over the whole surface of the globe" [(001-016)]. Trade is the bourgeoisie's compulsion, its distinctive mode of existence.

The Manifesto distinguishes between the concrete forms of trade (colonial trade, the East-Indian market, free trade) and the abstract system they constitute. But the thrust of the analysis is always material: trade is driven by the need to realise surplus value, and it reshapes every society it touches — dissolving old industries, creating new wants, and drawing previously isolated peoples into the orbit of capital.

## In the Manifesto

### Colonial Trade and Primitive Accumulation

The Manifesto links the expansion of trade directly to colonialism. "The discovery of America, the rounding of the Cape" — these are the geographic preconditions of the world market. Colonial trade was not a peripheral activity but the central mechanism through which the bourgeoisie accumulated the capital that would finance the Industrial Revolution. "The increase in the means of exchange and in commodities generally, gave to commerce, to navigation, to industry, an impulse never before known, and thereby, to the revolutionary element in the tottering feudal society, a rapid development" [(001-006)].

This is the Manifesto's compressed version of what Marx would later elaborate in *Capital* as "primitive accumulation": the violent creation of the preconditions for capitalism through colonial expropriation, the slave trade, and the enclosure of common lands. Trade in this account is never an innocent exchange of equivalents; it is an instrument of conquest.

### Free Trade as Bourgeois Ideology

The Manifesto registers the ideological force of free trade — "that single, unconscionable freedom — Free Trade" [(001-013)] — as the distilled expression of bourgeois social relations. Where the bourgeoisie presents free trade as a universal principle of liberty and prosperity, Marx and Engels expose it as a class-specific programme. "The [[bourgeoisie]]... has set up that single, unconscionable freedom — Free Trade" [(001-013)]: the freedom is real, but it is the freedom of capital to exploit labour without feudal restrictions.

"The weapons with which the bourgeoisie felled feudalism to the ground are now turned against the bourgeoisie itself" [(001-027)]. Free trade, which the bourgeoisie deployed against feudal monopolies and tariffs, would eventually — in the Manifesto's analysis — serve the proletariat by intensifying the contradictions of capitalism and creating a unified world proletariat.

### The Bourgeoisie's Trade Fetishism

The Manifesto observes that the bourgeoisie cannot conceive of any economic order other than one based on unrestricted trade. "The bourgeoisie... for exploitation, veiled by religious and political illusions, [feudal society] has substituted naked, shameless, direct, brutal exploitation" [(001-013)]. The "fettered traders of the Middle Ages" gave way to "free trade, free selling and buying" [(002-025–026)] — but the freedom is an illusion, as the proletarian who "freely" sells his labour-power has no real alternative but to do so.

### Trade and the Crisis of Overproduction

The Manifesto identifies the "commercial crises" [(001-020)] that periodically convulse bourgeois society as consequences of trade's own contradictions. "In these crises a great part not only of the existing products, but also of the previously created productive forces, are periodically destroyed" [(001-020)]. The world market that trade has created is too vast to be governed; the bourgeoisie's solution — "the conquest of new markets, and the more thorough exploitation of the old ones" [(001-021)] — only defers and deepens the next crisis.

## Historical Context

The Manifesto was written at the high tide of free-trade ideology. The Anti-Corn Law League, led by Richard Cobden and John Bright, had just achieved the repeal of the Corn Laws in 1846 — a victory celebrated by free traders across Europe. The Zollverein had created a single German market. The British Empire was consolidating its global commercial dominance. Marx and Engels, writing in this environment, absorb free trade's language while inverting its content: free trade is indeed revolutionary, but what it revolutionises is the conditions for proletarian revolution.
