> We do not here refer to that literature which, in every great modern revolution, has always given voice to the demands of the proletariat, such as the writings of Babeuf and others. [(006-001)]

**François-Noël "Gracchus" Babeuf** (1760–1797) was a French revolutionary and political writer who stands at the origin of modern communist thought. Organized around the *Conspiracy of the Equals* (Conjuration des Égaux) in 1796, Babeuf was the first to translate the abstract egalitarianism of the French Revolution into a practical programme for the revolutionary overthrow of private property and the establishment of a communist society. In the Manifesto, Marx and Engels acknowledge him as the earliest voice of proletarian demands in modern revolutionary literature (006-001), even as they locate his thought within the pre-scientific phase of socialist development.

## In the Manifesto

### Ch. III §3: Critical-Utopian Socialism and Communism

Babeuf appears in the Manifesto at the opening of the section on "Critical-Utopian Socialism and Communism" — the third category of socialist literature critiqued in Chapter III. Marx and Engels draw a sharp distinction between two kinds of revolutionary literature. The first kind, represented by Babeuf (referred to simply as "the writings of Babeuf and others"), arose directly from the early proletarian movements of the revolutionary epoch: "the first direct attempts of the proletariat to attain its own ends, made in times of universal excitement, when feudal society was being overthrown" (006-001–006-002).

These attempts, the Manifesto argues, "necessarily failed, owing to the then undeveloped state of the proletariat, as well as to the absence of the economic conditions for its emancipation, conditions that had yet to be produced, and could be produced by the impending bourgeois epoch alone" (006-002). As a result, this early revolutionary literature "had necessarily a reactionary character. It inculcated universal asceticism and social levelling in its crudest form" (006-002).

The Manifesto's classification is precise: Babeuf represents a phase of proletarian consciousness that is genuinely revolutionary in its rejection of existing society, yet necessarily crude and ascetic because the material conditions for communism — the full development of capitalist productive forces — had not yet emerged. The school of [[henri-de-saint-simon|Saint-Simon]], [[charles-fourier|Fourier]], and [[robert-owen|Owen]], by contrast, belongs to a later, more developed phase: "The Socialist and Communist systems properly so called... spring into existence in the early undeveloped period... of the struggle between proletariat and bourgeoisie" (006-003).

## Historical Context

### From Feudal Land Agent to Revolutionary Communist

Babeuf was born into a poor family in Saint-Quentin, Picardy. Before the Revolution, he worked as a *commissaire à terrier* (feudal land agent), a position that gave him intimate knowledge of the seigneurial dues and obligations that crushed the French peasantry. This experience — seeing from within how the feudal system extracted surplus from the rural poor — radicalized him permanently.

He greeted the Revolution of 1789 with enthusiasm and moved to Paris, where he became a political journalist. His newspaper, *Le Tribun du Peuple* (The Tribune of the People), disseminated increasingly radical ideas: not merely political equality (already proclaimed by the Revolution), but *real* equality — the abolition of private property, the equal distribution of goods, and the suppression of inheritance. He took the name "Gracchus" after the Roman tribunes Tiberius and Gaius Gracchus, who had pursued agrarian reform on behalf of the Roman poor.

### The Conspiracy of the Equals (1796)

Babeuf's decisive political act was the Conspiracy of the Equals — a clandestine organization that plotted to overthrow the Directory (the post-Thermidor government) and establish a revolutionary dictatorship that would abolish private property and institute a communist regime. The Conspiracy was unprecedented: it was the first organized attempt to seize state power with the explicit aim of abolishing class society itself.

The conspiracy was betrayed by an informer, and in May 1796 Babeuf and his co-conspirators were arrested. After a dramatic trial at Vendôme, Babeuf and Augustin Darthé were sentenced to death and guillotined on 27 May 1797. Before his execution, Babeuf wrote a final letter to his wife and children that became a foundational document of revolutionary martyrdom: "I am sacrificing myself for the people... I die innocent; I die for the best of causes."

### Babeuf's Programme: The Doctrine of Babeuf (Babouvism)

The conspirators' programme, later compiled by Babeuf's follower Philippe Buonarroti in *Conspiration pour l'Égalité dite de Babeuf* (1828), outlined a communist society organized around:

- Abolition of private property in the means of production
- Common ownership of land and industry
- Equal obligation to work for all able-bodied citizens
- Central administration of production and distribution
- Suppression of inheritance to prevent re-accumulation of wealth
- Universal education to extinguish class distinctions

This programme anticipated many themes that would recur in later socialist and communist movements: the revolutionary dictatorship of the oppressed, the planned economy, the link between political revolution and social transformation. However, as Marx and Engels noted, the level of productive forces in 1796 was insufficient to sustain such a transformation. The programme's "asceticism and social levelling in its crudest form" (006-002) reflected the undeveloped state both of the proletariat and of the economic conditions for emancipation.

## Relationship to Marxism

Marx and Engels' relationship to Babeuf was one of historical recognition combined with theoretical transcendence. They acknowledged Babeuf as a precursor — the first to understand that the political revolution of the bourgeoisie could be pushed further toward the social revolution of the proletariat. The Manifesto's very opening gesture — acknowledging that the spectre of communism haunted Europe from the revolutionary epoch onward — implicitly includes Babeuf in the genealogy.

But they also identified Babeuf's historical limits. The ascetic egalitarianism of the Conspiracy of the Equals was, in their analysis, a necessary product of its time: the proletariat was still embryonic, the capitalist mode of production had not yet developed its full productive powers, and therefore the only imaginable communism was one of universal deprivation and levelling-down rather than of abundance. This is the theoretical point of the Manifesto's distinction between Babeuf's "reactionary" revolutionary literature and the properly "critical-utopian" systems of Saint-Simon, Fourier, and Owen — the latter, while still utopian (i.e., unscientific), at least envisioned a new social order rather than a return to pre-capitalist simplicity.

Later Marxists continued to honour Babeuf. The French communist tradition in particular — from Auguste Blanqui through the Paris Commune to the French Communist Party — traced its lineage through Babeuf. The Russian Bolsheviks saw in Babeuf's Conspiracy a precursor of their own strategy of insurrectionary seizure of power by a disciplined revolutionary minority, though Babeuf's failure also served as a cautionary tale about the necessity of mass support and favourable objective conditions.

## Key Works

- *Cadastre perpétuel* (1789) — Proposal for a permanent land register and tax reform
- *Le Tribun du Peuple* (1794–1796) — Revolutionary newspaper, principal vehicle of Babeuf's ideas
- *Manifeste des Plébéiens* (1795) — Manifesto of the Plebeians, calling for real equality and the abolition of private property
- *Analyse de la doctrine de Babeuf* (1797, posthumous compilation by Buonarroti) — Systematic exposition of Babouvist communism
