**Reform** occupies an ambivalent position in the Manifesto. On the one hand, Marx and Engels acknowledge the real gains won through reform movements — the Factory Acts, the Ten Hours' Bill, the extension of the suffrage. On the other hand, they insist that reform, pursued as an end in itself, becomes a trap: an apparatus for managing discontent while leaving the fundamental structures of capitalist exploitation intact. "They therefore search after a new social science, after new social laws, that are to create these conditions. Such is the tendency of the 'reformers'" [(002-008)].

## Definition

In the Manifesto's polemical vocabulary, reform designates any programme that seeks to ameliorate the condition of the working class without abolishing the wage relation. Reform addresses the symptoms of capitalist crisis — poverty, unemployment, precarious working conditions — while leaving the cause untouched. It is, in this sense, the political expression of the contradiction between the manifest failures of capitalism and the bourgeoisie's interest in preserving it.

The Manifesto distinguishes between two kinds of reform. The first, which it associates with the bourgeois [[revolution|revolutionary]] tradition, genuinely advances the interests of the rising bourgeoisie against feudal remnants (the English Reform agitation of 1832, the July Revolution of 1830). The second, which it calls "bourgeois socialism," uses the language of reform to disarm the proletariat — "to the claim of the Communists, viz., that the proletariat must organise as a class, they reply: 'Reform! Reform!'" [(005-005)].

## In the Manifesto

### Reform as Bourgeois Strategy

Section III devotes sustained attention to "Conservative, or Bourgeois, Socialism," which the Manifesto identifies as the characteristic ideology of those who want "the grievances of the existing state of society to be redressed, but not its foundation" [(005-001)]. The bourgeois reformer promises to eliminate the "abuses" of capitalism — speculation, monopoly, extreme inequality — while preserving the system of private property and wage-labour that generates these abuses in the first place.

The Manifesto's critique is structural rather than moral. The problem with reform is not that the reformers are insincere (though some assuredly are) but that reform, within the framework of capitalism, can never be more than palliative. "The bourgeoisie... demands the reform of society, not indeed in the interests of the revolutionary proletariat, but in the interest of the 'reforming' bourgeoisie" [(005-003)]. Reform becomes a mechanism of class containment — a safety valve that releases enough pressure to prevent an explosion.

### The Historical Role of Reform Movements

The Manifesto does not dismiss reform outright. The English reform agitation that culminated in the Reform Act of 1832 is treated as a genuine bourgeois revolution — "the political victory of the manufacturing capitalist over the landed aristocracy" [(004-002)]. Similarly, the Ten Hours' Bill and other factory legislation are acknowledged as real victories for the working class, won through organised struggle.

But these victories are always partial and precarious. They demonstrate what can be achieved through collective action while simultaneously demonstrating the limits of action that does not challenge the wage system itself. Reform, in the Manifesto's dialectic, is at once a school of class struggle and a potential dead end — depending on whether it is subordinated to the revolutionary project or treated as a substitute for it.

### Against "Mere Political Reform"

The Manifesto's sharpest words are reserved for those who advocate "mere political reform" as an alternative to social revolution. "They wish for a real (wahre) change of the present state of society, but only within its framework. They desire the existing state of society, minus its revolutionary and disintegrating elements" [(005-005)]. This is the voice of the "hole-and-corner reformers of every imaginable kind" [(005-002)] — those who see in every social ill an opportunity for a new scheme, a new committee, a new petition, but never for the abolition of the conditions that produce the ill.

## Historical Context

The Manifesto's polemic against reformism was aimed at several contemporary targets. The most immediate was the "True Socialism" of Karl Grün and his circle, which combined French socialist slogans with German philosophical phraseology while opposing political action by the working class. The broader target was the philanthropic reformism of the liberal bourgeoisie — the notion that capitalism's problems could be solved through charity, education, and moral improvement without touching the sanctity of private property.

The Revolutions of 1848, which erupted within weeks of the Manifesto's publication, would provide a dramatic test of the reform-versus-revolution question. Across Europe, liberal reformers who had demanded constitutional change in March found themselves, by June, siding with the forces of order against the revolutionary workers who demanded social transformation. The Communist League, for which the Manifesto was written, participated actively in these revolutions — and was destroyed in their defeat.
