## Definition

**Modern industry** — also rendered as "large-scale industry" or "the factory system" — is the fully developed capitalist mode of production. It is distinguished from the earlier manufacturing system by the application of steam power and machinery, which replace the skilled artisan's hand tools with mechanised processes and transform the workshop into the factory. For Marx and Engels, modern industry represents the culmination of a historical sequence: guild production → manufacture → modern industry, each stage representing a more advanced — and more socially destructive — form of productive organisation.

## In the Manifesto

### The triumph of steam and machinery

The transition to modern industry is presented as the decisive moment in the triumph of the [[bourgeoisie]]. The feudal [[guild]] system and the manufacturing system that followed could not meet the demands of expanding world markets. Steam and machinery broke these constraints:

> Thereupon, steam and machinery revolutionised industrial production. The place of manufacture was taken by the giant, Modern Industry. [(001-008)]

The historical sequence that produced modern industry was described by the Manifesto as beginning with the breakdown of the feudal guild order. The feudal system of industry could no longer satisfy the growing wants of newly opened markets:

> The feudal system of industry, under which industrial production was monopolised by closed guilds, now no longer sufficed for the growing wants of the new markets. The manufacturing system took its place. The guild-masters were pushed on one side by the manufacturing middle class; division of labour between the different corporate guilds vanished in the face of division of labour in each single workshop. [(001-007)]

The connection between modern industry and the rise of the [[bourgeoisie]] to political power is direct and explicit. The Manifesto traces the bourgeoisie's political ascendancy to the establishment of modern industry:

> The bourgeoisie has at last, since the establishment of Modern Industry and of the world-market, conquered for itself, in the modern representative State, exclusive political sway. The executive of the modern State is but a committee for managing the common affairs of the whole bourgeoisie. [(001-011)]

### The factory and the degradation of labour

This revolution in production reorganised social relations at their foundation. The patriarchal master's workshop — a space where personal relations between master and worker persisted — was replaced by the impersonal factory:

> Modern industry has converted the little workshop of the patriarchal master into the great factory of the industrial capitalist. [(001-030)]

Within the factory, the logic of capitalist production reaches its extreme. The worker is stripped not only of the product of labour but of skill itself. The Manifesto identifies a direct inverse relationship between industrial development and the value of human labour:

> The less the skill and exertion of strength implied in manual labour, in other words, the more modern industry becomes developed, the more is the labour of men superseded by that of women. [(001-031)]

The degradation of labour under modern industry is further underscored by the observation that the worker's relation to the machine is one of subordination, not mastery. Owing to the extensive use of [[machinery]] and the [[division-of-labour]], the worker becomes "an appendage of the machine" and the work loses "all individual character, and consequently, all charm for the workman" [(001-029)]. The fewer skills required, the lower the cost of producing the worker — and thus the lower the wage.

### The scale of modern industry and its destructive force

Modern industry operates on a scale that destroys all competitors. The Manifesto describes how the petty bourgeoisie — artisans, handicraftsmen, small tradespeople — are crushed by the sheer scale of modern industry:

> The lower strata of the middle class — the small tradespeople, shopkeepers, retired tradesmen generally, the handicraftsmen and peasants — all these sink gradually into the proletariat, partly because their diminutive capital does not suffice for the scale on which Modern Industry is carried on, and is swamped in the competition with the large capitalists, partly because their specialized skill is rendered worthless by the new methods of production. [(001-033)]

Modern industry also destroys the family. In a passage from Chapter II that extends the analysis of the factory system beyond mere economics, the Manifesto describes how industrial production tears apart proletarian family bonds:

> The bourgeois clap-trap about the family and education, about the hallowed co-relation of parent and child, becomes all the more disgusting, the more, by the action of Modern Industry, all family ties among the proletarians are torn asunder, and their children transformed into simple articles of commerce and instruments of labour. [(002-044)]

### The productive role of modern industry in proletarian organisation

Modern industry also creates the material conditions for proletarian organisation. The concentration of workers in factories and industrial cities, and the new communications networks built to serve industry, transform isolated workers into a collective class:

> ...the means of communication that are created by modern industry and that place the workers of different localities in contact with one another. [(001-037)]

But the most decisive dialectical role of modern industry is revealed at the end of Chapter I. Modern industry does not merely produce commodities; by replacing the isolation of individual labourers with their revolutionary [[association]] in the factory, it destroys the very foundation of capitalist production:

> The advance of industry, whose involuntary promoter is the bourgeoisie, replaces the isolation of the labourers, due to competition, by their revolutionary combination, due to association. The development of Modern Industry, therefore, cuts from under its feet the very foundation on which the bourgeoisie produces and appropriates products. What the bourgeoisie, therefore, produces, above all, is its own grave-diggers. [(001-050)]

## Historical Context

### The Industrial Revolution in Britain

Modern industry emerged in Britain in the late eighteenth century with the textile mills of Lancashire and Yorkshire, powered first by water, then by James Watt's improved steam engine. By 1848, when the Manifesto was published, the factory system had spread across northwest Europe, transforming Manchester, Leeds, Birmingham, and parts of Belgium and France into industrial centres. The speed of this transformation — from workshop to factory in a single generation — is what Marx and Engels captured in the Manifesto's breathless prose. They saw in modern industry not merely a new technology but a new mode of social existence.

### The world-market and modern industry

The rise of modern industry cannot be separated from the expansion of the [[world-market]]. The Manifesto directly links the two: "Modern industry has established the world-market, for which the discovery of America paved the way" [(001-009)]. This world-market, in turn, gave "an immense development to [[commerce]], to navigation, to communication by land," which "reacted on the extension of industry" [(001-009)]. The relationship between modern industry and the [[world-market]] is thus a mutually reinforcing spiral: industry creates markets, and expanded markets demand more industry.

This expansion also transformed the political geography of the world. The Manifesto describes how the bourgeoisie, through modern industry, draws "all, even the most barbarian, nations into [[civilisation]]" and compels "all nations, on pain of extinction, to adopt the bourgeois mode of production" [(001-020)]. Modern industry, in this sense, is not merely an economic force but the vehicle of a global civilisational transformation.

### The productive forces unleashed

Marx and Engels were genuinely awed by what modern industry had achieved in less than a century. The Manifesto celebrates the "more massive and more colossal productive forces" created by the bourgeoisie's "rule of scarce one hundred years [that] have all preceding generations together" [(001-022)]. The list is worth quoting in full: "Subjection of Nature's forces to man, [[machinery]], application of chemistry to industry and agriculture, steam-navigation, railways, electric telegraphs, clearing of whole continents for cultivation, canalisation of rivers, whole populations conjured out of the ground" [(001-022)]. This is the most direct statement in the Manifesto of the productive miracle that modern industry represents — and it makes the subsequent argument that these forces have outgrown bourgeois property relations all the more powerful.

## Significance

Modern industry is the material proof, in Marx and Engels' argument, that the [[bourgeoisie]] is a revolutionary class — but also that it creates its own grave-diggers. The same factories that concentrate capital concentrate workers; the same machinery that deskills labour makes workers interchangeable and therefore organisable. The Manifesto's analysis of modern industry is thus double-edged: it is at once a celebration of productive power and an indictment of the social relations that harness that power for private profit.

The concept anchors the Manifesto's most characteristic rhetorical move — turning the bourgeoisie's boasts (productivity, world market, technological mastery) into evidence for its inevitable overthrow. The structural importance of this concept is reflected in its placement within the text: it appears at the pivotal moment in Chapter I where the historical narrative of bourgeois ascension pivots into the analysis of proletarian class formation. It is modern industry that converts the patriarchal workshop into the factory [(001-030)], modern industry that makes skill worthless and wages uniform [(001-031)], modern industry that dissolves all other classes into the proletariat [(001-033), (001-042)], and modern industry whose development finally cuts away the very basis of bourgeois property [(001-050)]. No other single concept in the Manifesto carries this much analytical weight across so many paragraphs of the text.
