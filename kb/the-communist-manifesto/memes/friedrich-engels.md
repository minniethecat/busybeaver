# Friedrich Engels

## Biography

Friedrich Engels (1820–1895) was a German philosopher, political theorist, historian, journalist, and revolutionary socialist. Born in Barmen (now Wuppertal), Prussia, to a wealthy textile-manufacturing family, Engels was sent to Manchester in 1842 to work in the family's cotton mill — an experience that gave him firsthand knowledge of industrial [[capital|capitalism]]'s effects on the working class. His 1845 book *The Condition of the Working Class in England* was a pioneering work of urban sociology and industrial reportage. In 1844, Engels met [[karl-marx|Karl Marx]] in Paris, beginning a partnership that would last nearly forty years until Marx's death.

Engels played multiple roles in the partnership: co-theorist, editor, translator, financial supporter, and posthumous custodian of Marx's intellectual legacy. He joined the [[communist-league|Communist League]] in 1847 and collaborated with Marx on the Manifesto; his own draft, the "Principles of Communism," served as a preparatory text. After Marx's death in 1883, Engels edited and published Volumes II and III of *Capital* from Marx's manuscripts, and continued to develop and defend Marxist theory in works such as *Socialism: Utopian and Scientific* (1880) and *The Origin of the Family, Private Property and the State* (1884).

## In the Manifesto

Like Marx, Engels does not appear by name in the text of the Manifesto, yet his contribution to the document was essential. The two authors were commissioned by the Communist League after "Communists of various nationalities had assembled in London and sketched the following Manifesto" [(P01-006)]. Engels's earlier draft, the "Principles of Communism" (1847), provided the catechetical structure that Marx reshaped into the Manifesto's rhetorical form. The description in the Preamble of "a spectre haunting Europe" and the specific naming of "Metternich and Guizot, French Radicals and German police-spies" reflects Engels's sharp observational eye, trained during his years in Manchester and his wide reading of the European press.

> A spectre is haunting Europe — the spectre of Communism. All the Powers of old Europe have entered into a holy alliance to exorcise this spectre: Pope and Czar, Metternich and Guizot, French Radicals and German police-spies. [(P01-001)]

Engels's practical experience of industrial capitalism informs the Manifesto's concrete economic descriptions. The analysis of how the [[bourgeoisie]] "has converted the physician, the lawyer, the priest, the poet, the man of science, into its paid wage labourers" [(001-014)] echoes observations Engels had made in his earlier writings on the commercialisation of professional life under capitalism.

> The bourgeoisie has stripped of its halo every occupation hitherto honoured and looked up to with reverent awe. It has converted the physician, the lawyer, the priest, the poet, the man of science, into its paid wage labourers. [(001-014)]

The Manifesto's argument that Communists "point out and bring to the front the common interests of the entire proletariat, independently of all nationality" [(002-005)] reflects Engels's lifelong internationalism and his effort to connect workers' movements across national boundaries.

> The Communists are distinguished from the other working-class parties by this only: (1) In the national struggles of the proletarians of the different countries, they point out and bring to the front the common interests of the entire proletariat, independently of all nationality. (2) In the various stages of development which the struggle of the working class against the bourgeoisie has to pass through, they always and everywhere represent the interests of the movement as a whole. [(002-005)]

Engels's distinctive contribution is also visible in the Manifesto's treatment of the family and gender. The rebuttal of the bourgeois charge that Communists wish to abolish the family — that "Differences of age and sex have no longer any distinctive social validity for the working class" [(001-031)] — draws on themes Engels would later develop systematically in *The Origin of the Family*.

> The less the skill and exertion of strength implied in manual labour, in other words, the more modern industry becomes developed, the more is the labour of men superseded by that of women. Differences of age and sex have no longer any distinctive social validity for the working class. All are instruments of labour, more or less expensive to use, according to their age and sex. [(001-031)]

His co-authorship of this foundational text places him at the origin point of the entire Marxist tradition.

### The Manchester Years and Industrial Observation

Engels's two years in Manchester (1842–1844) were formative for the empirical dimension of the Manifesto's argument. Sent by his father to manage the family's cotton-thread factory, Engels witnessed at close range the conditions of England's industrial [[proletariat]] — the overcrowded slums, child labour, epidemic disease, and the moral degradation that rapid industrialisation produced. The Manifesto's vivid description of how the [[bourgeoisie]] "has agglomerated population, centralised means of production, and has concentrated property in a few hands" [(001-017)] reflects Engels's direct observation of Manchester, the shock city of the [[industrial-revolution|Industrial Revolution]].

Engels's Manchester experience also exposed him to the Chartist movement, the first mass working-class political movement in history. The [[chartists|Chartists]]' demand for universal suffrage and their organisation of workers across trades demonstrated the practical possibility of working-class political mobilisation — a lesson Engels brought into the [[communist-league|Communist League]]'s strategy and into the Manifesto's declaration that the [[proletariat]] must "win the battle of democracy" [(002-067)].

## Key Ideas

Engels's intellectual contributions span political economy, anthropology, military history, and philosophy. His concept of "scientific socialism" — distinguishing the Marx-Engels project from the "utopian" socialism of [[henri-de-saint-simon|Saint-Simon]], [[charles-fourier|Fourier]], and [[robert-owen|Owen]] — is articulated in the Manifesto's critique of earlier socialist systems. However, Engels was more than a populariser of Marx; his independent works developed a dialectics of nature, a materialist analysis of the [[state]] and the [[family]], and a theory of how capitalism shapes urban space and class consciousness.

Engels argued that the condition of women under capitalism provided a key to understanding class society as a whole, developing the thesis that the monogamous family, [[private-property|private property]], and the state emerged together in a single historical process. His analysis of the military and technological dimensions of historical change also informed the Marxian understanding of how [[productive-forces|productive forces]] develop. Unlike Marx, Engels maintained a practical involvement with the business world, giving him an intimate understanding of capitalist enterprise that he fed into their joint theoretical work.

### Scientific versus Utopian Socialism

Engels's distinction between "scientific" and "utopian" socialism is one of his most enduring contributions. In the Manifesto, the critique of the Critical-Utopian Socialists and Communists — that "although the originators of these systems were, in many respects, revolutionary, their disciples have, in every case, formed mere reactionary sects" [(006-012)] — is developed by Engels at greater length in *Socialism: Utopian and Scientific* (1880). Where [[henri-de-saint-simon|Saint-Simon]], [[charles-fourier|Fourier]], and [[robert-owen|Owen]] envisioned ideal communities imposed from above by enlightened elites, Engels and Marx insisted that [[communism]] must be understood as "the real movement which abolishes the present state of things" — a movement rooted in the actual development of the [[proletariat]] and the contradictions of [[capital|capitalist]] production.

> The significance of Critical-Utopian Socialism and Communism bears an inverse relation to historical development. In proportion as the modern class struggle develops and takes definite shape, this fantastic standing apart from the contest, these fantastic attacks on it, lose all practical value and all theoretical justification. [(006-012)]

The Manifesto's closing call — "Working Men of All Countries, Unite!" [(007-012)] — encapsulates Engels's conviction that the emancipation of the working class must be the act of the working class itself, not a gift from above.

## Relationship to Marxism

Engels stands alongside Marx as co-founder of the Marxist tradition. Through his editing and publication of Marx's posthumous works, his own prolific writings, and his guidance of the growing socialist parties of the Second International, Engels was the single most important figure in shaping Marxism as a systematic doctrine and political movement after Marx's death. The Manifesto itself was a collaborative work; Engels acknowledged Marx as the primary theorist while Marx acknowledged Engels's economic and practical insights. The two names on the title page are inseparable, and the tradition they launched bears Marx's name but carries Engels's organisational and editorial stamp on every major text of the founding period.

## Selected Works

- *The Condition of the Working Class in England* (1845) — Pioneering empirical study of industrial capitalism's social effects, based on Engels's observations in Manchester
- *Principles of Communism* (1847) — Catechetical draft that served as the preparatory text for the Manifesto
- *Socialism: Utopian and Scientific* (1880) — Exposition of scientific socialism and critique of utopian predecessors
- *The Origin of the Family, Private Property and the State* (1884) — Materialist analysis of the family, the state, and the transition from primitive communism to class society
- *Ludwig Feuerbach and the End of Classical German Philosophy* (1886) — Clarification of the Marx-Engels relationship to Hegelian and Feuerbachian philosophy
