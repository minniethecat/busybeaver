> In Poland they support the party that insists on an agrarian revolution as the prime condition for national emancipation, that party which fomented the insurrection of Cracow in 1846. [(007-004)]

The **Cracow Insurrection of 1846** (Powstanie Krakowskie) was a Polish nationalist and democratic uprising in the Free City of Cracow, led by the Polish Democratic Society under Jan Tyssowski and Edward Dembowski. In the Manifesto's closing section, Marx and Engels invoke it as the exemplary action of the revolutionary party in Poland — the party that "insists on an agrarian revolution as the prime condition for national emancipation" (007-004) and with which the Communists ally in the struggle against the partitioning powers of Austria, Prussia, and Russia.

## In the Manifesto

### Section IV: Poland and the International Character of Revolution

The reference to the Cracow Insurrection appears in Section IV's survey of communist tactics across national contexts: France, Switzerland, Poland, Germany. Each nation receives a distinct tactical prescription based on the concrete state of its class struggle, but a common principle runs through all: Communists support the most advanced revolutionary forces in each country while maintaining theoretical and organizational independence.

Poland occupies a special place in this survey. Unlike France or Germany, where the class struggle between bourgeoisie and proletariat had already assumed a developed form, Poland's primary contradiction was national — the struggle against foreign partition — and within that national struggle, the decisive question was agrarian. The Polish revolutionary democrats understood that national emancipation could not be achieved without agrarian revolution: the Polish peasantry, still subject to semi-feudal obligations, had no material stake in a national independence that left the landlord's power intact. Only the abolition of feudal dues and the distribution of land to the peasantry could transform the national question from an aristocratic cause into a popular revolution.

This was the programme of the party that fomented the Cracow Insurrection — and this, for Marx and Engels, was precisely why Communists should support it. The agrarian revolution in Poland, though not socialist in content, was a necessary stage in the development of the conditions for socialism: by sweeping away feudal relations in the countryside, it would create a modern class structure — bourgeoisie and proletariat — within which the class struggle could develop to its full maturity.

## Historical Background

### The Polish Question and the Partitions

Poland had been partitioned out of existence in three stages (1772, 1793, 1795) by Russia, Prussia, and Austria. The Congress of Vienna (1815) confirmed the partitions, creating the Free City of Cracow as a nominally independent republic under the joint protection of the three partitioning powers. The Polish national movement, which had staged major insurrections in 1794 (Kościuszko), 1830–1831 (November Uprising), and would rise again in 1863 (January Uprising), was a permanent feature of European politics — and a permanent preoccupation of the European revolutionary left.

### The 1846 Uprising

The Cracow Insurrection began on the night of 20–21 February 1846, when a small force of Polish democrats and armed townspeople seized control of the city. Jan Tyssowski was proclaimed dictator, and a revolutionary manifesto was issued that abolished feudal dues (corvée), promised land to the peasantry, and proclaimed universal equality before the law. Edward Dembowski, the insurrection's radical ideologue, attempted to extend the revolution into the surrounding Galician countryside, organizing peasant assemblies and calling for a national rising of all classes against the Austrian occupiers.

The insurrection was crushed within two weeks. The Austrian authorities, with the support of the partitioning powers, marched on Cracow with overwhelming force. Dembowski was killed leading a religious procession — an ironic death for a radical democrat — and Tyssowski fled into exile. In the aftermath, Austria annexed the Free City of Cracow outright, extinguishing the last shred of Polish formal independence.

### The Galician Slaughter

The Cracow Insurrection was fatally undermined by an event that exposed the deep class divisions within Polish society: the Galician Slaughter (Rzeź galicyjska). In the countryside surrounding Cracow, the Austrian authorities had cynically encouraged the Galician peasantry to rise against their Polish landlords, spreading rumours that the nobles were planning a massacre of peasants while in fact it was the democrats who were seeking peasant support. The result was a wave of peasant attacks on manor houses across western Galicia — a jacquerie that killed an estimated 1,000 to 2,000 landlords and officials. The peasant rising, directed against the Polish nobility rather than the Austrian state, demonstrated that the national question in Poland could not be separated from the agrarian question. The nobility's nationalism, which offered independence without land reform, commanded no loyalty from the peasantry; only a revolutionary programme that made agrarian revolution the condition of national emancipation could bridge the class divide.

## Marxist Analysis

### The Agrarian Revolution as Democratic Precondition

The Cracow Insurrection's significance for Marx and Engels lay in its demonstration of a general principle: in countries where feudal relations persisted, the democratic revolution — the abolition of feudal dues, the distribution of land, the establishment of a republic — was the immediate strategic task, and Communists must support the party that carried this task forward most consistently. This was not a concession to reformism but a recognition that the working class cannot leap directly to socialism from a feudal base. The bourgeois-democratic revolution, however limited its ultimate horizon, clears the ground for the class struggle between proletariat and bourgeoisie by abolishing the pre-capitalist forms of exploitation that fragment the exploited classes and obscure the fundamental antagonism of modern society.

The Cracow revolutionaries understood this in practice: their manifesto abolished feudal obligations and called the peasantry into the national struggle. That the insurrection was defeated — in part by the very peasantry it sought to liberate — only confirmed the necessity of its programme. The Galician peasantry, deceived by Austrian propaganda and alienated by centuries of noble oppression, could not be won to the national cause overnight. But the Cracow programme pointed the way forward: only a revolution that gave the peasantry land could transform them from passive victims into active subjects of history.

### Poland and the International Revolution

The Manifesto's support for the Polish cause was not merely tactical — it was rooted in the analysis of the international character of the coming revolution. Poland, partitioned by the three great conservative powers of Europe (Russia, Prussia, Austria), was the keystone of the counter-revolutionary order established at Vienna in 1815. The reestablishment of an independent, democratic Poland would strike at all three powers simultaneously, breaking the Holy Alliance and opening the way for revolution in Germany and beyond. This is why Marx and Engels returned to the Polish question throughout their political careers: Polish independence was, for them, not a Polish question but a European question, and the revolutionary party in Poland — the party of the Cracow Insurrection — was the ally of every European revolutionary.
