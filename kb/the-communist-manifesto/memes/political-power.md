# Political Power

## Definition

Political power, in the Manifesto's rigorously functionalist definition, is "the organised power of one class for oppressing another" (002-082). Marx and Engels strip the concept of its juridical and constitutional veneer: political power is not a relation between citizens and the State but a relation between classes, mediated by the State apparatus. It is the means by which economic domination is translated into coercive legal authority, and its character is determined entirely by the class that wields it — feudal political power organises the oppression of serfs by lords; bourgeois political power organises the oppression of workers by capitalists.

## In the Manifesto

Political power appears in the Manifesto at three decisive moments: as an object of historical description, as a goal of revolutionary strategy, and as a transitional necessity destined for abolition.

In Ch. I, Marx and Engels describe the political trajectory of the bourgeoisie as a class that "after the establishment of large-scale industry and of the world market, it has conquered for itself, in the modern representative State, exclusive political sway" (001-011). Bourgeois political power is the crowning achievement of bourgeois economic dominance — the transformation of factual control over production into legal-political sovereignty.

In Ch. II, the Communists' immediate aim is stated with precision: "the conquest of political power by the proletariat" (002-034). This is not a demand for suffrage or parliamentary representation — it is a call for the revolutionary seizure of the State apparatus. The proletariat, having conquered political power, will use it to "centralise all instruments of production in the hands of the State" (007-010) and to "wrest, by degrees, all capital from the bourgeoisie" (002-068). Political power is thus simultaneously the target of revolution and the tool by which the revolution consolidates itself.

The Manifesto's most theoretically significant claim about political power comes in its closing pages. In the famous passage on the withering away of the State, Marx and Engels assert that with the abolition of class distinctions, "the public power will lose its political character. Political power, properly so called, is merely the organised power of one class for oppressing another" (002-082). When classes are abolished, power in society remains — administration, coordination, the management of things — but it ceases to be *political* power because it no longer serves the oppression of one class by another.

## Historical Context

The concept of political power as class rule did not originate with Marx and Engels, but they gave it its most uncompromising formulation. French historians of the Restoration period — Augustin Thierry, François Guizot, Adolphe Thiers — had already described the history of France as a struggle between classes for control of the State. What Marx and Engels added was the materialist grounding: political power is not a cause but an effect, the superstructural expression of relations of production. The bourgeoisie did not become the ruling class because it seized political power — it seized political power because it had already become the ruling class economically.

The 1848 context is crucial. Across Europe, liberal and democratic movements were challenging absolutist regimes, demanding constitutions, parliaments, and expanded suffrage. The Manifesto's insistence that political power is fundamentally class power was a direct intervention in these debates: it denied the liberal illusion that political reform could neutralise class domination. A parliament elected by universal suffrage under capitalism was still, in its essential function, a committee for managing bourgeois affairs.

## Related Concepts

Political power is inseparable from the concept of the [[state]], which is the institutional apparatus through which political power is exercised. The [[ruling-class]] is the class that holds political power — "the class which is the ruling *material* force of society is at the same time its ruling *intellectual* force" (001-040). The [[bourgeoisie]] is the class that presently holds political power in modern society, and the [[proletariat]] is the class that must conquer it. [[proletarian-revolution]] names the process by which political power is transferred from the bourgeoisie to the proletariat, and [[class-struggle]] is the ongoing contest over who holds and exercises that power.
