# Nationality

## Definition

Nationality, in the Manifesto's analysis, is a historically specific form of social organization that is being simultaneously universalized and undermined by the development of capitalism. On the one hand, the bourgeoisie creates the modern nation-state as the political form adequate to capitalist production — *"one code of laws, one national class-interest, one frontier and one customs-tariff"* (001-022). On the other hand, the same bourgeoisie, through the world market, dissolves national boundaries and creates an interdependent global economy in which *"national one-sidedness and narrow-mindedness become more and more impossible"* (001-019). For the proletariat, nationality is a form that must be transcended: the class struggle begins as a national struggle but is international in content, because the exploitation of labour by capital knows no national boundaries.

## In the Manifesto

The Manifesto's treatment of nationality is dialectical. The Communists are accused of wanting to abolish countries and nationality; Marx and Engels reply, *"The working men have no country. We cannot take from them what they have not got"* (002-051). This famous declaration does not mean that workers are unpatriotic by choice but that the proletarian, *"stripped ... of every trace of national character"* by the very logic of bourgeois society (001-044), has no country in the sense that he has no stake in the national property that the bourgeoisie defends. Law, morality, religion — all the ideological apparatuses of national belonging — are, for the proletarian, so many bourgeois prejudices behind which lurk bourgeois interests.

Yet the Manifesto does not call for the immediate abolition of the nation as a political form. The proletariat's struggle is *"in form"* national — *"the proletariat of each country must, of course, first of all settle matters with its own bourgeoisie"* (002-052) — but *"in substance"* it is international, since the bourgeoisie it fights is part of a global system. The Communist programme preserves this productive tension: the proletariat must organize itself as a national class, seize political power within the national framework, and use that power to centralize the means of production. But the end result is the withering away of national antagonisms: *"In proportion as the exploitation of one individual by another is put an end to, the exploitation of one nation by another will also be put an end to. In proportion as the antagonism between classes within the nation vanishes, the hostility of one nation to another will come to an end"* (002-052).

The Manifesto opens with an international declaration: *"Communists of various nationalities have assembled in London and sketched the following Manifesto"* (P01-006) — a performative assertion that the communist movement, from its inception, operates beyond national boundaries. The first point distinguishing Communists from other working-class parties reinforces this: *"In the national struggles of the proletarians of the different countries, the Communists point out and bring to the front the common interests of the entire proletariat, independently of all nationality"* (002-005). Communists are thus the internationalist conscience of the national workers' movements, reminding each national struggle that its ultimate horizon is the global emancipation of the working class.

## Historical Context

The Manifesto was published in February 1848, the same month that revolutions erupted across Europe — in Paris, Vienna, Berlin, Milan, and Budapest. The simultaneity of these uprisings, and the rapidity with which revolutionary fervour crossed national borders, seemed to confirm the Manifesto's thesis that the class struggle was international in substance if national in form. The Communist League, for which the Manifesto was written, was itself an international organization with members in London, Brussels, Paris, and several German cities. Yet the 1848 revolutions also revealed the centrifugal force of national sentiment: in Germany and Italy, the national question (unification) competed with and often displaced the social question. Marx and Engels' later writings on Ireland, Poland, and India would further develop the relationship between national liberation and class struggle, introducing the idea that the working class of an oppressor nation cannot be free while its own bourgeoisie oppresses another nation.

## Related Concepts

The concept of nationality is dialectically linked to the rise of the [[bourgeoisie]], which created the modern nation-state as the political framework for capitalist accumulation. It is also structurally connected to the [[proletariat]], whose class interest is international — the abolition of wage-labour cannot be accomplished within the confines of a single nation. The tension between the national form and the international substance of the class struggle is central to the Marxist theory of [[class-struggle]]. The abolition of nationality is, in the long run, a consequence of the abolition of classes — the project that defines [[communism]]. In the Manifesto's political programme, the national [[state]] serves as the instrument through which the proletariat centralizes the means of production before the state itself withers away.

## Significance

The Manifesto's treatment of nationality established the foundational framework for Marxist internationalism. The slogan "Workers of the world, unite!" is the practical conclusion drawn from the theoretical claim that the proletariat has no country. This internationalism has been both the greatest strength and the most persistent challenge of the Marxist tradition. In practice, working-class movements have repeatedly fractured along national lines — most catastrophically in 1914, when the parties of the Second International supported their respective national bourgeoisies in the First World War. The tension between national loyalty and class solidarity remains unresolved in the twenty-first century, as globalization deepens economic interdependence while nationalism resurges politically. The Manifesto's dialectical formula — national in form, international in substance — continues to define the strategic horizon of socialist politics.
