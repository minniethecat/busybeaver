## Definition

In the *Communist Manifesto*, the **aristocracy** — also termed the "feudal aristocracy" — is the hereditary landed ruling class of the medieval period, whose political and economic dominance was destroyed by the rise of the bourgeoisie. Marx and Engels treat the aristocracy as historically superseded: a class whose mode of production (feudal land tenure) and corresponding forms of social organisation (guilds, vassalage, monarchy) were dismantled by capitalist industry, trade, and the bourgeois revolutions.

## In the Manifesto

The aristocracy appears in the Manifesto primarily not as a living force but as a defeated class reacting to its overthrow. The opening of Section I establishes the historical sequence: the bourgeoisie emerged from within feudal society and, having developed its productive forces beyond what feudal relations could contain, broke those relations:

> The feudal system of industry, under which industrial production was monopolised by closed guilds, now no longer sufficed for the growing wants of the new markets. [(001-007)]

The aristocracy is thus the class that the bourgeoisie defeated — the "old society" against which the rising middle class waged its revolutionary struggle.

But the Manifesto's most striking treatment of the aristocracy comes in Section III, under the heading of "Feudal Socialism." Here Marx and Engels expose the aristocracy's cynical post-defeat strategy: dressing its reactionary agenda in socialist rhetoric to win the support of the very proletariat whose exploitation it once oversaw:

> In order to arouse sympathy, the aristocracy were obliged to lose sight, apparently, of their own interests, and to formulate their indictment against the bourgeoisie in the interest of the exploited working class alone. [(004-003)]

> The aristocracy, in order to rally the people to them, waved the proletarian alms-bag in front for a banner. [(004-005)]

Marx and Engels dismiss this "feudal socialism" as "half lamentation, half lampoon; half echo of the past, half menace of the future" — a performance of proletarian solidarity that conceals the aristocracy's real desire to restore its own lost privileges [(004-004)].

The Manifesto also notes that the aristocracy was not alone in being ruined by bourgeois society. The petty bourgeoisie, peasants, and artisan classes all faced dissolution under capitalism, creating a heterogeneous pool of potential reactionary allies:

> The feudal aristocracy was not the only class that was ruined by the bourgeoisie, not the only class whose conditions of existence pined and perished in the atmosphere of modern bourgeois society. [(004-014)]

## Historical Context

The Manifesto was written in 1848, the year of revolutions across Europe. In Germany, the struggle between the bourgeoisie and the feudal aristocracy was still ongoing — the bourgeois liberal movement had not yet secured the political dominance it had achieved in England and France. This German context, noted explicitly in Section IV [(004-030)], shapes the Manifesto's treatment: the aristocracy is not merely a historical memory but a still-active political force against which the bourgeoisie fights, even as the proletariat prepares to fight the bourgeoisie in turn.

## Significance

The Manifesto's treatment of the aristocracy serves a dual strategic purpose. Historically, it demonstrates the law of class succession: every ruling class is eventually overthrown by the class it called into being. Politically, it immunises the proletariat against aristocratic co-optation: by exposing the fraud of "feudal socialism," Marx and Engels warn workers not to be seduced by aristocratic rhetoric that mimics their grievances while serving reactionary ends. The aristocracy is the cautionary proof that no ruling class ever surrenders power voluntarily, and that the rhetoric of the defeated must be scrutinised for the interests it conceals.
