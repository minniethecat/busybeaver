Colonialism, in the *Communist Manifesto*, is understood as the overseas territorial conquest and economic exploitation that accompanied the rise of the bourgeoisie. Marx and Engels present colonialism not as an incidental feature of capitalist development but as one of its material preconditions — the violent opening of new markets and sources of raw materials that gave "an impulse never before known" to commerce, navigation, and industry (001-006).

## Colonial Expansion and the Rise of the Bourgeoisie

The *Manifesto* identifies the "discovery of America" and "the rounding of the Cape" as pivotal events that "opened up fresh ground for the rising bourgeoisie" (001-006). These geographical discoveries were not merely navigational achievements; they were the initiating shocks that transformed the economic structure of Europe. The East-Indian and Chinese markets, the colonisation of America, and trade with the colonies combined to produce what Marx and Engels describe as an unprecedented acceleration of commercial and industrial activity (001-006).

This colonial expansion directly undermined the feudal economic order. The feudal system of industry, characterised by "closed guilds" with limited production capacity, "now no longer sufficed for the growing wants of the new markets" (001-007). The manufacturing system that replaced it was itself superseded by modern industry as colonial markets continued to expand.

## The World Market

Colonialism made possible the creation of what Marx and Engels call the "world-market" — a global economic system in which production and consumption take on a "cosmopolitan character" (001-019). "Modern industry has established the world-market, for which the discovery of America paved the way," they write, emphasising the causal chain from colonial conquest to industrial capitalism (001-009). This market "has given an immense development to commerce, to navigation, to communication by land," transforming not only economic relations but the entire material basis of society (001-009).

## Colonialism as Primitive Accumulation

Although Marx and Engels do not use the term "primitive accumulation" in the *Manifesto*, their account of colonialism closely anticipates the analysis Marx would later develop in *Capital*. Colonial conquest, the plunder of overseas territories, and the forced integration of non-European peoples into the world market function as the violent prehistory of capital — the process by which the bourgeoisie accumulated the resources necessary to overturn feudal relations and establish its own mode of production. The "revolutionary element in the tottering feudal society" received its decisive impetus from colonial expansion (001-006).

> The discovery of America, the rounding of the Cape, opened up fresh ground for the rising bourgeoisie. (001-006)

## Significance in the Manifesto's Argument

Colonialism serves two argumentative functions in the *Manifesto*. First, it is a historical explanation — it shows *how* the bourgeoisie rose to power, grounding that rise in concrete material processes rather than abstract historical inevitability. Second, it is a demonstration of the bourgeoisie's revolutionary character — the colonial project exemplifies the restless, boundary-dissolving energy that Marx and Engels identify as the bourgeoisie's historically progressive role, even as they condemn its violent consequences. The same force that "drowned the most heavenly ecstasies of religious fervour, of chivalrous enthusiasm, of philistine sentimentalism, in the icy water of egotistical calculation" also tore down the walls that had separated the continents (001-015).
