# Chartists

## Overview

The Chartists were the participants in Chartism, the first mass working-class political movement in history, which emerged in Britain in the late 1830s. Named after the People's Charter of 1838, the movement demanded six parliamentary reforms: universal male suffrage, secret ballot, equal electoral districts, abolition of property qualifications for MPs, payment of MPs, and annual parliaments. Chartism organized massive petition campaigns, strikes, and demonstrations — including the Newport Rising of 1839 — drawing its base from industrial workers, artisans, and the labouring poor who had been radicalized by the harsh conditions of early industrial capitalism. While the movement declined after 1848 without achieving its immediate demands, all six points of the Charter (except annual parliaments) were eventually enacted into British law.

> The [[bourgeoisie|bourgeoisie]] itself, therefore, supplies the proletariat with its own instruments of political and general education, in other words, it furnishes the proletariat with weapons for fighting the bourgeoisie. [(001-039)]

Chartism represented precisely this dynamic: the [[bourgeoisie|bourgeoisie]], in pursuing the 1832 [[reform|Reform Act]] that enfranchised the middle class while excluding workers, unwittingly furnished the [[proletariat|proletariat]] with the political education and the grievance that gave birth to the first independent mass working-class movement. The Chartists thus embodied the dialectical process described in the opening chapter of the Manifesto.

## In the Manifesto

### The Chartists as Exemplary Working-Class Party

Chartism is the only named working-class movement that Marx and Engels cite approvingly as an existing ally of the Communists. In Section IV, the Manifesto states:

> Section II has made clear the relations of the Communists to the existing working-class parties, such as the Chartists in England and the Agrarian Reformers in America. [(007-001)]

This characterization of Chartists as a "working-class party" places them firmly within the camp of the [[proletariat|proletarian]] movement with which Communists seek alliance. The strategic logic is spelled out immediately: *"The Communists fight for the attainment of the immediate aims, for the enforcement of the momentary interests of the working class; but in the movement of the present, they also represent and take care of the future of that movement"* (007-002). The Chartists — fighting for suffrage and democratic rights — represented the "immediate aims" that Communists support, even as Communists bring to the front the longer-term revolutionary perspective.

The Manifesto also invokes the Chartists in its critique of [[utopian-socialism|utopian socialism]]. In Section III.3, Marx and Engels describe how the [[owenites|Owenites]] and Fourierists, by clinging to their rejection of political action, have become reactionary:

> The Owenites in England, and the Fourierists in France, respectively, oppose the Chartists and the "Réformistes." [(006-014)]

This passage draws a sharp line: on one side, the critical-utopian sects that refuse [[class-struggle|class struggle]] and political organizing; on the other, the Chartists, who represent the real, independent political movement of the working class. The Owenites and Fourierists had by the 1840s degenerated into sects that *"violently oppose all political action on the part of the working class; such action, according to them, can only result from blind unbelief in the new Gospel"* (006-013). In opposing the Chartists, they objectively aligned themselves with the [[bourgeoisie|bourgeoisie]].

### The Political Character of Class Struggle

The Manifesto's analysis of [[proletariat|proletarian]] development provides the theoretical framework for understanding what the Chartists achieved. Marx and Engels describe how workers, initially isolated and competing, gradually combine:

> Now and then the workers are victorious, but only for a time. The real fruit of their battles lies, not in the immediate result, but in the ever-expanding union of the workers. This union is helped on by the improved means of communication that are created by modern industry and that place the workers of different localities in contact with one another. It was just this contact that was needed to centralise the numerous local struggles, all of the same character, into one national struggle between classes. But every class struggle is a political struggle. [(001-037)]

Chartism was the historical realization of this theoretical claim — the first national political struggle of the working class. The Manifesto's central assertion that *"every [[class-struggle|class struggle]] is a political struggle"* (001-037) found its empirical vindication in the Chartists' three great national petitions and their demand for parliamentary reform as the condition of working-class emancipation.

Marx and Engels further describe how the [[proletariat|proletariat]], through its organization, *"compels legislative recognition of particular interests of the workers, by taking advantage of the divisions among the [[bourgeoisie|bourgeoisie]] itself. Thus the [[ten-hours-bill|ten-hours' bill]] in England was carried"* (001-038). The Chartist movement, though it did not itself achieve its six points in its own lifetime, demonstrated this same strategic logic: its mass agitation created the political pressure that eventually compelled the very reforms it demanded.

### The Communists' Relationship to the Chartists

The Manifesto's description of Communist strategy in Section II directly frames the relationship to movements like Chartism. The Communists *"do not form a separate party opposed to other working-class parties"* (002-002). They are rather *"the most advanced and resolute section of the working-class parties of every country, that section which pushes forward all others"* (002-006). The immediate aim is the same as that of all other proletarian parties: *"formation of the [[proletariat|proletariat]] into a class, overthrow of the bourgeois supremacy, conquest of [[political-power|political power]] by the proletariat"* (002-007).

> The theoretical conclusions of the Communists are in no way based on ideas or principles that have been invented, or discovered, by this or that would-be universal reformer. They merely express, in general terms, actual relations springing from an existing class struggle, from a historical movement going on under our very eyes. [(002-008)]

The Chartist movement was precisely such a "historical movement going on under our very eyes" — the living demonstration that the British working class was capable of independent political organization. By presenting Chartism as the exemplary working-class party in England, Marx and Engels implicitly credit the Chartists with demonstrating that proletarian political action — rather than utopian schemes or bourgeois philanthropy — is the path to working-class emancipation.

## Historical Role

### Origins and Social Base

Chartism emerged from the confluence of working-class disillusionment with the 1832 [[reform|Reform Act]] (which enfranchised the middle class but excluded workers) and the economic distress of the 1830s and 1840s. Its three great national petitions (1839, 1842, 1848) gathered millions of signatures and were presented to Parliament, only to be rejected each time. The movement split between "moral force" Chartists, led by William Lovett, who advocated peaceful agitation, and "physical force" Chartists, led by Feargus O'Connor, who threatened direct action. The Newport Rising of November 1839 — in which armed Chartists marched on the town of Newport, Monmouthshire — was violently suppressed, and its leaders were transported to Australia. Chartism declined after the defeat of the 1848 petition, but its legacy transformed British politics by proving the capacity of working people for independent mass political organization.

The social base of Chartism was the industrial working class created by the [[industrial-revolution|Industrial Revolution]] — the class that Marx and Engels identified as the product of *"the giant, [[modern-industry|Modern Industry]]"* (001-008). The Manifesto describes how *"the [[proletariat|proletariat]] not only increases in number; it becomes concentrated in greater masses, its strength grows, and it feels that strength more"* (001-036). This concentration in factory districts — Lancashire, Yorkshire, South Wales, the Scottish Lowlands — was the material precondition for the scale of Chartist mobilization.

### Engels and the Chartists

Friedrich Engels had closely observed Chartism during his Manchester years (1842–1844). The industrial city was both the epicentre of British capitalism and a stronghold of the Chartist movement. Engels's firsthand engagement with Chartist leaders — including his contributions to the *Northern Star* and his personal acquaintance with George Julian Harney and Ernest Jones — informed the Manifesto's strategic orientation toward existing working-class movements. In *The Condition of the Working Class in England* (1845), Engels provided the detailed empirical account of proletarian conditions that underpinned the Manifesto's theoretical claims. The Chartists were not an abstraction for Marx and Engels; they were living political collaborators in the international working-class movement.

> Of all the classes that stand face to face with the bourgeoisie today, the proletariat alone is a really revolutionary class. [(001-042)]

The Chartists, as the organized political expression of the English [[proletariat|proletariat]], embodied this revolutionary potential. Unlike the *"lower middle class, the small manufacturer, the shopkeeper, the artisan, the peasant"* who are *"not revolutionary, but conservative"* (001-042), the Chartists drew their strength from the class that had nothing to lose but its chains.

## Marxist Assessment

### Chartism as Proletarian Political Action

For Marx and Engels, the Chartists represented something indispensable: a genuinely independent working-class political movement, not a liberal reform campaign or a utopian experiment. The Manifesto's endorsement of the Chartists reflects Marx and Engels' strategic principle that Communists must support and participate in every real movement of the working class while simultaneously "bringing to the front" the long-term revolutionary perspective.

> In short, the Communists everywhere support every revolutionary movement against the existing social and political order of things. [(007-008)]

This was not uncritical support. Communists *"never cease, for a single instant, to instil into the working class the clearest possible recognition of the hostile antagonism between [[bourgeoisie|bourgeoisie]] and [[proletariat|proletariat]]"* (007-006). In the Chartist context, this meant supporting the demand for the People's Charter while criticizing the movement's limitation to political reform — for without the abolition of bourgeois property, even universal suffrage could be absorbed and neutralized by the capitalist state.

### The Limits of Chartism

The Chartists' ultimate failure also served as a negative lesson. The movement lacked three elements that the Communist Manifesto identifies as essential: revolutionary theory, an international orientation, and the demand for the abolition of bourgeois property as its central objective. The Manifesto insists that the Communists bring to the front *"as the leading question in each, the property question, no matter what its degree of development at the time"* (007-009).

The Chartists fought for the ballot — an instrument that could, in principle, be used either for or against working-class interests. Without the theoretical framework that identified the state as the executive committee of the [[bourgeoisie|bourgeoisie]], the Chartists could not formulate a strategy that went beyond parliamentary reform to the conquest of [[political-power|political power]]. The Manifesto's critique of the critical-utopian sects bears on this point: *"In proportion as the modern [[class-struggle|class struggle]] develops and takes definite shape, this fantastic standing apart from the contest, these fantastic attacks on it, lose all practical value and all theoretical justification"* (006-012). The lesson for Chartism was that without revolutionary theory — without the understanding that the emancipation of the working class must be the act of the working class itself — even a mass movement could be defeated.

### Chartism's Enduring Legacy

Despite its defeat, Chartism demonstrated a principle that became axiomatic in the Marxist tradition: the working class, when organized independently on its own political terrain, possesses a capacity for mass action that no other social force can match. The Manifesto's closing declaration — *"The proletarians have nothing to lose but their chains. They have a world to win"* (007-011) — was not a rhetorical flourish but a judgement confirmed by the Chartist experience. Millions of working people who had no property, no vote, and no formal education nevertheless built the most formidable political movement in nineteenth-century Britain.

The Chartist legacy also vindicated the Manifesto's internationalism. Communism is by its nature a transnational movement, not because the Communists invented internationalism but because capitalism itself is a world system:

> The Communists labour everywhere for the union and agreement of the democratic parties of all countries. [(007-010)]

Chartism demonstrated that the working-class struggle was national in form — shaped by the specific political conditions of Britain — but its content and its enemies were international. The British industrial capitalist and the French bourgeois were structurally identical, and the Chartists and the [[agrarian-reformers|Agrarian Reformers]] in America were, without necessarily knowing it, allies in the same global struggle.

## Key Figures

- **Feargus O'Connor (1796–1855)** — Irish-born Chartist leader, publisher of the *Northern Star*, the movement's most influential newspaper.
- **William Lovett (1800–1877)** — Cornish-born cabinetmaker, principal author of the People's Charter, leader of the "moral force" wing.
- **Ernest Jones (1819–1869)** — Chartist poet and orator, later a close friend and collaborator of Marx and Engels.
- **George Julian Harney (1817–1897)** — Radical Chartist journalist, editor of the *Red Republican*, maintained international correspondence with Marx.
