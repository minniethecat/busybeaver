In the *Communist Manifesto*, "private property" denotes specifically **bourgeois private property** — the ownership of the means of production by the capitalist class — and not personal possessions or the "property of the petty artisan and of the small peasant" (002-011). The abolition of this specific form of property is the central demand around which Marx and Engels organise the Communist programme, and the point around which most bourgeois objections to Communism revolve. The theory of the Communists "may be summed up in the single sentence: Abolition of private property" (002-012), a formulation that distills the practical programme to its economic essence while inviting precisely the misunderstandings that Chapter II systematically addresses.

## Bourgeois Property vs. Property in General

Marx and Engels are careful to distinguish their target. "The distinguishing feature of Communism is not the abolition of property generally, but the abolition of bourgeois property," they write in response to the charge that Communists seek to abolish all property (002-011). Bourgeois private property is "the final and most complete expression of the system of producing and appropriating products, that is based on class antagonisms, on the exploitation of the many by the few" (002-011). It is not a natural or eternal institution but a historically specific form of organising production and distribution.

The *Manifesto* further distinguishes bourgeois property from earlier historical forms. The French Revolution "abolished feudal property in favour of bourgeois property" (002-010), demonstrating that property forms are mutable and historically contingent. Feudal property, based on personal dependence and status hierarchy, gave way to bourgeois property based on capital ownership and wage labour. The Communist demand to abolish bourgeois property is thus presented as the next stage in a historical sequence, not an arbitrary assault on a timeless right.

### The Logic of the Distinction

The precision of this distinction is philosophically significant. "All property relations in the past have continually been subject to historical change consequent upon the change in historical conditions" (002-009), which means the bourgeois form cannot claim exemption from the historical process that produced it. Marx and Engels use the French Revolution — which the bourgeoisie itself celebrates — as their proof: if one form of property can legitimately replace another, there is no reason the process should stop with bourgeois property (002-010). The abolition they demand is thus framed as the completion of a historical logic the bourgeoisie itself set in motion, not a contradiction of it.

## The Charge of "Destroying Personal Property"

A substantial portion of Chapter II is devoted to rebutting the charge that Communists intend to abolish "personally acquired property" — the fruits of individual labour. Marx and Engels respond with two arguments. First, the property of the petty artisan and small peasant was already being destroyed by the bourgeoisie itself through the concentration of capital: "the bourgeoisie is not a ruling class because it owns capital, but it owns capital because it is a ruling class" — and its rule has already "abolished" the small property of the majority (002-015). Second, wage labour "creates no property for the labourer" — it creates only capital, "the property that exploits wage-labour" (002-016). The worker who owns nothing but their labour power has no property to lose.

> The distinguishing feature of Communism is not the abolition of property generally, but the abolition of bourgeois property. (002-011)

> Hard-won, self-acquired, self-earned property! Do you mean the property of the petty artisan and of the small peasant, a form of property that preceded the bourgeois form? There is no need to abolish that; the development of industry has to a great extent already destroyed it, and is still destroying it daily. (002-014)

### The Irony of the Bourgeois Defence

Marx and Engels deploy a sharp irony against the bourgeois defence of personal property. They note that "in your existing society, private property is already done away with for nine-tenths of the population; its existence for the few is solely due to its non-existence in the hands of those nine-tenths" (002-027). The bourgeoisie, having already expropriated the vast majority, now presents itself as the defender of property rights. The charge against Communism is thus revealed as a defence not of property in general but of the class monopoly that bourgeois property represents. As they put it with biting clarity: "In one word, you reproach us with intending to do away with your property. Precisely so; that is just what we intend" (002-028).

## Property and Class Antagonism

Private property is inseparable from class antagonism in the *Manifesto*'s analysis. The "conditions of bourgeois society are too narrow to comprise the wealth created by them" (001-025) — a contradiction expressed in commercial crises in which "there is too much civilisation, too much means of subsistence, too much industry, too much commerce" (001-025). These crises reveal that the private property system has become a fetter on the productive forces it once unleashed, just as feudal property became a fetter on the productive forces generated within feudal society (001-023). The abolition of private property is therefore not merely a moral demand but a historical necessity — the productive forces have outgrown the property relations that once served them.

> The conditions of bourgeois society are too narrow to comprise the wealth created by them. (001-025)

> The productive forces at the disposal of society no longer tend to further the development of the conditions of bourgeois property; on the contrary, they have become too powerful for these conditions, by which they are fettered. (001-025)

### Property as a Social Relation

The *Manifesto* reconceptualises property not as a thing but as a social relation. "Capital is a collective product, and only by the united action of many members, nay, in the last resort, only by the united action of all members of society, can it be set in motion" (002-017). Since capital is already social in its production, converting it to common property does not abolish personal property but merely changes the social character of property (002-019). The bourgeois conception of property as an individual right obscures its fundamentally social nature — a mystification that serves the ruling class's interest by presenting a historically contingent arrangement as natural law (002-037).

## Historical Property Forms

The *Manifesto* surveys property across historical epochs to relativise the bourgeois form. In ancient society, "the antagonism between patricians, knights, plebeians, slaves" structured a distinctive property system; in the Middle Ages, "feudal lords, vassals, guild-masters, journeymen, apprentices, serfs" organised production and appropriation through personal ties (001-003). Bourgeois property "sprouted from the ruins of feudal society" (001-004) and represents only the latest in a succession of exploitative property forms.

> What you see clearly in the case of ancient property, what you admit in the case of feudal property, you are of course forbidden to admit in the case of your own bourgeois form of property. (002-037)

### The Historical Sequence

This historical survey reveals a pattern: each property form emerges within the previous one, develops the productive forces to a certain point, then becomes a fetter. Feudal property relations "became no longer compatible with the already developed productive forces; they became so many fetters. They had to be burst asunder; they were burst asunder" (001-023). Bourgeois property now stands in the same relation to the productive forces it has called into being. The [[productive-forces|productive forces]] that the bourgeoisie developed have become "too powerful for these conditions, by which they are fettered" (001-025). The abolition of private property is thus the historical resolution of a contradiction, not a moral choice.

## The Communist Programme and Property

The abolition of private property is not merely a theoretical position but the organising principle of the Communist programme. The ten measures enumerated at the end of Chapter II (002-072 through 002-081) — from the "abolition of property in land" (002-072) to the "centralisation of credit in the hands of the State" (002-076) — are described as "despotic inroads on the rights of property, and on the conditions of bourgeois production" (002-069). These measures are transitional: they "appear economically insufficient and untenable, but which, in the course of the movement, outstrip themselves, necessitate further inroads upon the old social order" (002-069).

The ultimate horizon is not mere redistribution but the transformation of the character of property itself. "When, in the course of development, class distinctions have disappeared, and all production has been concentrated in the hands of a vast association of the whole nation, the public power will lose its political character" (002-082). The abolition of bourgeois private property is inseparable from the abolition of class rule itself, for "political power, properly so called, is merely the organised power of one class for oppressing another" (002-082).

> The Communist revolution is the most radical rupture with traditional property relations; no wonder that its development involves the most radical rupture with traditional ideas. (002-065)
