Of all the charges levelled against communism, none provoked greater indignation among the bourgeoisie than the alleged abolition of the family. Marx and Engels confront this charge directly in Section II of the Manifesto, not by denying it but by turning it back upon its accusers: the bourgeois family, they argue, is already an institution of exploitation and hypocrisy, and its "abolition" would mean not the destruction of human intimacy but the emancipation of familial relations from the logic of capital.

## Definition

In the Manifesto's analytical vocabulary, the **family** is not a transhistorical natural form but a historically specific institution determined by the prevailing mode of production. The bourgeois family — monogamous, patriarchal, property-transmitting — is "based on capital, on private gain" [(002-039)]. It serves two functions in capitalist society: the biological and social reproduction of labour-power, and the orderly transmission of private property across generations through inheritance. For the proletariat, by contrast, the family in this bourgeois sense is already practically non-existent, dissolved by the very conditions of industrial capitalism.

## In the Manifesto

The Manifesto's treatment of the family unfolds across three interconnected arguments in Section II, forming one of the text's most rhetorically charged passages.

### The Bourgeois Family Unmasked

"The bourgeoisie has torn away from the family its sentimental veil, and has reduced the family relation to a mere money relation" [(001-015)]. This sentence, from the Manifesto's opening panegyric to the revolutionary role of the bourgeoisie, already contains the core thesis: capitalism itself has already destroyed the family as an institution of genuine affection and mutual care. What remains is the family as a property relation — a vehicle for the accumulation and bequest of capital.

When Communists are accused of seeking to abolish the family, Marx and Engels reply with a counter-question: "On what foundation is the present family, the bourgeois family, based? On capital, on private gain. In its completely developed form, this family exists only for the bourgeoisie" [(002-039)]. The charge of abolition is thus exposed as a category error: Communists seek to abolish not the family as such, but the bourgeois form of the family — a form that has already hollowed out the family's human content and left only its property function intact.

### Proletarian Reality

The argument's most devastating move comes in its third paragraph. "But you Communists would introduce community of women, screams the whole bourgeoisie in chorus" [(002-040)]. To this, Marx and Engels reply that "the bourgeois sees in his wife a mere instrument of production" — and that the charge of "community of women" is thus a projection of the bourgeoisie's own treatment of women as property onto the Communists.

The reality of proletarian life gives the lie to bourgeois sanctimony about the family: "The bourgeois family will vanish as a matter of course with the vanishing of capital. Do you charge us with wanting to stop the exploitation of children by their parents? We would stop that exploitation" [(002-041)]. The industrial revolution has already separated proletarian children from their parents through factory labour; the bourgeois family "finds its complement in the practical absence of the family among the proletarians, and in public prostitution" [(002-040)]. What the bourgeoisie mourns is not the loss of the family — which for the proletariat has already occurred — but the loss of its own exclusive property rights in wives and children.

### Education and Family

The critique of the family is inseparable from the question of education. The Manifesto proposes "free education for all children in public schools" combined with the "abolition of children's factory labour" [(002-068)] — measures that would liberate children from both economic exploitation and the exclusive ideological control of bourgeois parents. When the bourgeoisie denounces this as "destructive of the most hallowed of relations," Marx and Engels dismiss it as "bourgeois clap-trap about the family and education, about the hallowed co-relation of parent and child" [(002-044)].

## Historical Context

The Manifesto's critique of the family draws on two bodies of thought that Marx and Engels radicalised. The first was the **utopian socialist tradition**, particularly the writings of [[charles-fourier|Charles Fourier]], who had argued that the degree of women's emancipation was "the natural measure of general emancipation" in any society. Fourier's critique of marriage as a form of property relation and his vision of communal living arrangements directly influenced Engels' later, more developed treatment of the family in *The Origin of the Family, Private Property and the State* (1884).

The second was the **empirical reality of industrial capitalism** in the 1830s and 1840s. The Factory Acts debates in England had exposed the appalling conditions of child labour; the Poor Law Commission reports documented the systematic destruction of working-class families by the workhouse system; and Engels' own *The Condition of the Working Class in England* (1845) provided a detailed ethnographic account of family dissolution under industrialism. The Manifesto's arguments about the family were not abstract philosophical speculation but were grounded in this lived experience of proletarian family dismemberment.
