> In the French revolution of July 1830, and in the English reform agitation, these aristocracies again succumbed to the hateful upstart. [(004-002)]

The **July Revolution of 1830** (*Trois Glorieuses* — the Three Glorious Days) was the revolution that definitively ended Bourbon rule in France and installed the "bourgeois monarchy" of Louis-Philippe d'Orléans. In the Manifesto, Marx and Engels cite it as the event that sealed the political fate of the French aristocracy: after 1830, the Legitimists, supporters of the deposed Bourbon line, were reduced from a serious political force to a merely literary opposition — "half lamentation, half lampoon" (004-003). This defeat of the feudalists by the bourgeoisie is the historical precondition for the emergence of Feudal Socialism as a distinct ideological tendency.

## In the Manifesto

### Ch. III §1a: The Political Defeat of the Aristocracy

The July Revolution is not the subject of extended analysis in the Manifesto, but it appears as a decisive turning point in the genealogy of Feudal Socialism. Marx and Engels trace the origins of this ideology to the historical situation of the French and English aristocracies after they had been definitively defeated by the bourgeoisie. "In the French revolution of July 1830, and in the English reform agitation, these aristocracies again succumbed to the hateful upstart. Thenceforth, a serious political contest was altogether out of the question. A literary battle alone remained possible" (004-002).

This is a thesis about the relation between political power and ideology. When the aristocracy still commanded real political force, its opposition to the bourgeoisie could take the form of practical political contest. After the July Revolution — which replaced the Bourbon Charles X with the Orléanist Louis-Philippe — the Legitimists were reduced to the margins of French politics. Their critique of bourgeois society could no longer aspire to govern; it could only "sing lampoons on their new master" and "whisper sinister prophecies" (004-003). Feudal Socialism, in its French variant, is the ideological expression of this political impotence: anti-bourgeois in form, aristocratic in content, and ultimately reactionary in its practical effects.

## Historical Background

### From Restoration to Revolution

The Bourbon Restoration (1814–1830) was a compromise between the ancien régime and the Revolution of 1789. Louis XVIII, restored to the throne after Napoleon's fall, ruled under a Constitutional Charter that preserved the essential gains of the Revolution — civil equality, the Napoleonic Code, the redistribution of Church lands — while restoring the monarchy and a restricted franchise. The Restoration was an unstable equilibrium: the ultra-royalists dreamed of a full return to the pre-1789 order, while liberals and Bonapartists pushed for an extension of political rights and a foreign policy that would restore French national grandeur.

When Charles X succeeded his brother Louis XVIII in 1824, the fragile compromise collapsed. Charles was an unrepentant ultra-royalist who believed in the divine right of kings and sought to roll back the Revolution's legacy. His government passed laws indemnifying émigrés for lands confiscated during the Revolution, restored the death penalty for sacrilege, and imposed press censorship. In July 1830, Charles issued the Four Ordinances of Saint-Cloud — dissolving the newly elected Chamber of Deputies, restricting the franchise to the wealthiest landowners, and imposing draconian press controls. The response was insurrection.

### The Three Glorious Days

On 27 July 1830, Paris rose. Over three days — 27, 28, and 29 July — barricades went up across the city's eastern working-class districts. The insurgents were a cross-class coalition: republican students and lawyers, Bonapartist veterans of the Grande Armée, and — crucially — the Parisian working class, the *ouvriers* and artisans who had been radicalized by economic crisis and political exclusion. The royal army, demoralized and poorly led, could not contain the uprising. On 29 July, the Tuileries Palace fell, and Charles X abdicated and fled to England.

The revolution's outcome, however, revealed its limits. The republican and working-class forces that had fought on the barricades were outmaneuvered by the liberal bourgeoisie, who installed Louis-Philippe d'Orléans — a cousin of Charles X but a man who had fought under the tricolour in 1792 — as "King of the French." The new regime was explicitly bourgeois: the tricolour replaced the Bourbon white flag, the Charter was revised to limit royal power, and the franchise was extended (though still restricted to about 170,000 voters in a population of 30 million). The revolution of 1830 was, in essence, the political coming-to-power of the financial bourgeoisie — the class of bankers, stock-jobbers, and large industrialists whom Marx would later excoriate in *The Class Struggles in France* as the real rulers behind the façade of constitutional monarchy.

## Marxist Analysis

### The Bourgeois Revolution's Second Act

The July Revolution occupies a specific place in Marx's periodization of French history. If the Revolution of 1789 was the first act of the bourgeois revolution — the destruction of feudalism and the establishment of bourgeois property relations — then the July Revolution of 1830 was its consolidation: the definitive exclusion of the landed aristocracy from political power and the establishment of a regime that openly served the interests of the financial bourgeoisie. The 1830 revolution solved the contradiction between an aristocratic monarchy and a bourgeois society by replacing the former with the latter.

But this consolidation was also a clarification. The July Monarchy, by governing openly in the interests of the bourgeoisie, stripped away the illusions that had surrounded earlier regimes. The working class could see, with increasing clarity, that the republic for which they had fought on the barricades had been stolen and replaced with a monarchy of the rich. This lesson — that the bourgeoisie could not be trusted as an ally, that working-class sacrifices on behalf of "the people" would always be betrayed — was a crucial element in the political education of the French proletariat and a precondition for the independent working-class politics that emerged in the 1840s.

The Manifesto's brief reference to July 1830 thus condenses a whole historical dialectic: the bourgeoisie defeats the aristocracy, consolidates its political power, and in doing so, clarifies the fundamental contradiction — the antagonism between bourgeoisie and proletariat — that will drive the next phase of the revolution.
