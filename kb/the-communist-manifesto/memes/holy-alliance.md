# Holy Alliance

## Overview

The Holy Alliance was a coalition of European monarchies — Russia, Austria, and Prussia — formed in September 1815 at the Congress of Vienna under the initiative of Tsar Alexander I of Russia. Ostensibly a Christian covenant among monarchs to govern according to "the principles of justice, charity, and peace," the Alliance in practice served as a counter-revolutionary instrument dedicated to suppressing liberalism, nationalism, and constitutional movements wherever they arose on the continent. Its most notorious interventions included the suppression of constitutional revolts in Naples and Piedmont (1821) and the authorization of French intervention against the Spanish liberal revolution (1823). While the Alliance as a formal bond weakened after Alexander's death in 1825, the reactionary international cooperation it embodied — the "Concert of Europe" — persisted as a repressive force well into the revolutionary year of 1848.

## In the Manifesto

Although Marx and Engels do not name the Holy Alliance directly in the Manifesto, its historical presence haunts their analysis of the reactionary forces arrayed against the [[bourgeoisie]] and the [[proletariat]] alike. In Section III.1, the Manifesto discusses how [[feudal-socialism|feudal and clerical reactionaries]] rallied against bourgeois society: *"In political practice, therefore, they join in all coercive measures against the working class"* (004-014). The Holy Alliance was the preeminent vehicle of such coercive [[aristocracy|aristocratic]] and absolutist coordination.

More pointedly, in the analysis of "German, or 'True,' Socialism," Marx and Engels describe how the absolutist governments of Germany used pseudo-socialist rhetoric as a weapon against the liberal bourgeoisie: *"To the absolute governments, with their following of parsons, professors, country squires and officials, it served as a welcome scarecrow against the threatening bourgeoisie"* (004-020). The political landscape described — in which [[absolute-monarchy|absolutist monarchies]], feudal aristocracies, and clerical [[reaction|reactionaries]] collaborated transnationally to contain both bourgeois liberalism and proletarian revolution — was the direct institutional legacy of the Holy Alliance system.

In Section IV, the Manifesto's declaration that *"In Germany they fight with the bourgeoisie whenever it acts in a revolutionary way, against the absolute monarchy, the feudal squirearchy, and the petty bourgeoisie"* (007-005) takes aim at the very constellation of reactionary power that the Holy Alliance had sustained across Europe for three decades.

## Historical Role

The Holy Alliance originated in the aftermath of the Napoleonic Wars, as Europe's old regimes sought to restore the pre-1789 monarchical order and prevent any recurrence of revolutionary upheaval. Its founding document, drafted by Alexander I and signed by Francis I of Austria and Frederick William III of Prussia, was a mystical-religious proclamation that the British foreign minister Castlereagh dismissed as "a piece of sublime mysticism and nonsense." Yet its practical instrument, the Quadruple Alliance (with Britain), provided the military-diplomatic architecture for intervention against revolutions. The Alliance suppressed liberal-nationalist uprisings in the Italian states and, through the French Bourbon intervention in Spain (1823), crushed the liberal Trienio Liberal. By 1848, the system the Holy Alliance represented was the primary target of revolutionary movements across the continent — a reactionary order that Marx and Engels argued the [[bourgeoisie]] itself had an interest in overthrowing, but whose fall would immediately expose the deeper [[class-struggle|class antagonism]] between bourgeoisie and proletariat.

## Marxist Assessment

Marx and Engels' analysis of the Holy Alliance is integral to their [[historical-materialism|materialist conception of history]]. They did not view the Alliance as a mere diplomatic arrangement but as the organized political expression of a specific stage in the [[class-struggle]]: the moment when the [[feudalism|feudal-absolutist]] order, having been shaken but not destroyed by the [[french-revolution|French Revolution]] and Napoleon, regrouped to defend itself against the dual threat of bourgeois constitutionalism and proletarian revolution. The Manifesto's strategic line in Germany — tactical support for the bourgeoisie against absolutism, while preparing for the immediately following proletarian revolution — is premised on the assumption that the Holy Alliance's legacy of reactionary governance was the immediate obstacle to be cleared before the decisive class struggle could unfold. The [[february-revolution-1848|revolutions of 1848]] would vindicate this analysis, briefly shattering the post-1815 order across the continent before reaction regrouped once more.
