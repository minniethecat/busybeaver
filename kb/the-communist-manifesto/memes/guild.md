The **guild** appears at the very opening of the Manifesto as one of the defining institutions of the feudal world that capitalism would sweep away. "Freeman and slave, patrician and plebeian, lord and serf, guild-master and journeyman, in a word, oppressor and oppressed, stood in constant opposition to one another" [(001-001)]. The guild-master stands to the journeyman as the lord stands to the serf — an exploiter, a representative of a class order that the Manifesto subjects to historical critique.

## Definition

The guild system was the dominant form of industrial organisation in medieval and early-modern European towns. Production was regulated by corporate bodies (guilds) that controlled entry into a trade, set standards of workmanship, fixed prices and wages, and governed the relationship between masters, journeymen, and apprentices.

> The feudal system of industry, under which industrial production was monopolised by closed guilds, now no longer sufficed for the growing wants of the new markets. [(001-007)]

This was a system of privilege and restriction — the antithesis of the free market that the [[bourgeoisie]] would later establish. The guild-master was simultaneously a producer, an employer, and a member of the urban patriciate. The relation was one of both economic exploitation and personal subordination, regulated not by the "cash nexus" but by customary obligation and hierarchical status. The guild's authority rested on its monopoly over a specific trade within a given town — no one could practice a craft without guild membership, and membership itself was tightly controlled through a hierarchy of apprenticeship, journeymanship, and mastership.

> In the earlier epochs of history, we find almost everywhere a complicated arrangement of society into various orders, a manifold gradation of social rank. In ancient Rome we have patricians, knights, plebeians, slaves; in the Middle Ages, feudal lords, vassals, guild-masters, journeymen, apprentices, serfs; in almost all of these classes, again, subordinate gradations. [(001-003)]

In this taxonomy, the guild-master occupies a specific rung in the medieval hierarchy — distinct from the feudal lord (who held land), yet also distinct from the apprentice (who held nothing but his labour-power). The guild-master owned his tools, his workshop, and his stock of raw materials, and he commanded the labour of his journeymen and apprentices through a combination of economic power, customary authority, and legal monopoly.

## In the Manifesto

### The Guild as a Fetter on Production

The Manifesto's historical narrative presents the guild as the first obstacle that the rising bourgeoisie had to overcome.

> The feudal system of industry, in which industrial production was monopolised by closed guilds, now no longer sufficed for the growing wants of the new markets. The manufacturing system took its place. The guild-masters were pushed on one side by the manufacturing middle class; division of labour between the different corporate guilds vanished in the face of division of labour in each single workshop. [(001-007)]

The logic here is straightforward and materialist: guild production was restricted in scale, regulated by tradition rather than profit, and closed to innovation by its own rules of apprenticeship and mastership. As [[colonialism|colonial]] expansion created vast new markets, the guild system could not meet the rising demand. [[manufacture|Manufacture]] — production organised by a capitalist employer in a workshop with wage-labourers — emerged to fill the gap. The guild-master "was pushed on one side by the manufacturing middle class" [(001-011)].

### The Guild in Feudal Socialism

The [[feudal-socialism|feudal socialists]] criticised in Section III of the Manifesto looked back to the guild system as a lost ideal. Their programme called for "corporate guilds for manufacture, patriarchal relations in agriculture" [(004-019)] — an attempt to restore the regulated, hierarchical, pre-capitalist order.

> Its last words are: corporate guilds for manufacture, patriarchal relations in agriculture. [(004-019)]

Marx and Engels dismiss this as a "half lamentation, half lampoon; half echo of the past, half menace of the future" [(004-016)]. The guild was not a benign institution of mutual aid but a form of class rule — the guild-master exploited the [[journeyman]] no less than the capitalist exploits the proletarian, even if the form of exploitation was different.

### The Transition from Guild to Manufacture

The Manifesto describes a structural transition in the organisation of labour. "Division of labour between the different corporate guilds vanished in the face of division of labour in each single workshop" [(001-011)].

> Division of labour between the different corporate guilds vanished in the face of division of labour in each single workshop. [(001-011)]

Under the guild system, each trade was a distinct corporation with its own monopoly; the [[division-of-labour|division of labour]] was social — between carpenters, weavers, smiths, and so on. Under manufacture, the division of labour became technical — a single commodity was produced through a sequence of specialised operations within a single workshop.

This transition dissolved the guild as a social form. The master became an employer; the journeyman became a wage-labourer; the apprentice became a child worker. The personal bonds that had characterised guild production were replaced by the cash nexus. The entire feudal-industrial order — in which "industrial production was monopolised by closed guilds" [(001-007)] — proved incapable of competing with the productive dynamism of the capitalist mode of production.

### The Guild in the History of Class Struggles

The guild-master appears in the very opening sentence of the Manifesto's historical survey as one half of a class antagonism:

> Freeman and slave, patrician and plebeian, lord and serf, guild-master and journeyman, in a word, oppressor and oppressed, stood in constant opposition to one another, carried on an uninterrupted, now hidden, now open fight, a fight that each time ended, either in a revolutionary re-constitution of society at large, or in the common ruin of the contending classes. [(001-001)]

This placement is significant. By listing the guild-master/journeyman relation alongside the freeman/slave, patrician/plebeian, and lord/serf relations, Marx and Engels establish the guild system as an instance of the same fundamental structure that recurs throughout history: the division of society into oppressing and oppressed classes. The guild was not an exception to the rule of class exploitation but a specific medieval expression of it.

### Material Conditions of Guild Decline

The expansion of commerce and the [[world-market|world market]] made the guild form untenable. The Manifesto traces the causal chain with precision: "The discovery of America, the rounding of the Cape, opened up fresh ground for the rising bourgeoisie" [(001-006)]. These geographical discoveries created markets whose scale far exceeded what guild-regulated production could supply. The guild's closed, local, monopolistic structure was structurally incapable of expanding output to match the "growing wants of the new markets" [(001-007)]. The dissolution of the guild system was therefore not a matter of political choice or ideological preference — it was a material necessity imposed by the development of the productive forces.

> The discovery of America, the rounding of the Cape, opened up fresh ground for the rising bourgeoisie. The East-Indian and Chinese markets, the colonisation of America, trade with the colonies, the increase in the means of exchange and in commodities generally, gave to commerce, to navigation, to industry, an impulse never before known, and thereby, to the revolutionary element in the tottering feudal society, a rapid development. [(001-006)]

## Historical Context

The guild system reached its peak in the thirteenth to fifteenth centuries and declined gradually over the following three hundred years. In England, the Statute of Artificers (1563) formalised the guild-based regulation of apprenticeship, but by the eighteenth century the system was effectively dead in most industries. The [[french-revolution|French Revolution]] (1789) abolished the guilds outright — a measure that the Manifesto applauds as part of the bourgeois revolution's destruction of feudal privilege. As the text notes in Section II: "The French Revolution, for example, abolished feudal property in favour of bourgeois property" [(002-010)].

By 1848, guilds survived only as vestigial institutions in the German states — and even there they were rapidly being dismantled. The Manifesto's treatment of guilds is therefore both historical (describing a past mode of production) and polemical (rejecting reactionary attempts to revive what had been historically superseded). The insistence on a return to guild regulation was a hallmark of [[petty-bourgeois-socialism|petty-bourgeois socialism]], which "aspires either to restoring the old means of production and of exchange, and with them the old property relations, and the old society, or to cramping the modern means of production and of exchange" [(004-018)].

## Significance

The guild plays a precise structural role in the Manifesto's theory of historical development. It is at once the dominant form of industrial organisation under [[feudalism]], the first obstacle that commercial capital had to overcome, and the nostalgic object of reactionary socialism's critique of bourgeois society. The guild thus appears at three distinct moments in the Manifesto's argument: as a term in the historical taxonomy of class antagonisms, as a fetter on the development of the [[productive-forces|productive forces]], and as a rallying cry for those who would reverse the course of history.

The systematic treatment of the guild — from its opening appearance as a class relation to its supersession by manufacture and finally to its invocation by [[feudal-socialism|feudal]] and [[petty-bourgeois-socialism|petty-bourgeois]] socialism — exemplifies the dialectical character of the Manifesto's historical method. The guild is not dismissed as simply "bad" or "backward"; it is shown to be a necessary form of industrial organisation at a particular stage of productive development, one that became a fetter only when the productive forces outgrew it. The Manifesto's critique of the guild is thus a critique of its historical limitations, not a moral condemnation.

> The feudal system of industry, under which industrial production was monopolised by closed guilds, now no longer sufficed for the growing wants of the new markets. [(001-007)]

The guild-master's supersession by the [[bourgeoisie|manufacturing middle class]] was therefore not an accident or an injustice but a historically necessary transition — one that the Manifesto registers without nostalgia. For Marx and Engels, the destruction of the guilds was a progressive act, however brutal its execution, because it cleared the ground for the higher development of the productive forces that would ultimately make communism possible.
