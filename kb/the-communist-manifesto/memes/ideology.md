Ideology, in the Marxist tradition inaugurated by the *Communist Manifesto*, designates the system of ideas through which the material relations of production are represented, justified, and naturalised. Although the *Manifesto* does not use the term "ideology" itself — that term was developed more fully in *The German Ideology* (1845–46) — its analysis of the relationship between ruling ideas and ruling classes is foundational for all subsequent Marxist theories of ideology.

## The Ruling Ideas Are the Ideas of the Ruling Class

The cornerstone of the *Manifesto*'s treatment of ideology is the proposition that "the ruling ideas of each age have ever been the ideas of its ruling class" (002-058). This is not merely an empirical observation but a structural claim about how intellectual production is organised. "The class which has the means of material production at its disposal, has control at the same time over the means of mental production, so that thereby, generally speaking, the ideas of those who lack the means of mental production are subject to it" (002-058). The control of material production — factories, land, capital — gives the bourgeoisie control over the production of ideas — universities, newspapers, pulpits, and the entire apparatus of cultural legitimation.

## Ideological Domains in the Manifesto

The *Manifesto* analyses several specific domains of bourgeois ideology. **Law** is described as "but the will of your class made into a law for all, a will, whose essential character and direction are determined by the economical conditions of existence of your class" (002-028). **Morality** and **religion** are dismissed as "so many bourgeois prejudices, behind which lurk in ambush just as many bourgeois interests" (001-044). **Education** is indicted for serving bourgeois class domination: the bourgeois protests that Communist proposals would destroy "the most hallowed of relations" by substituting "a social education" for "a home education" — to which Marx and Engels reply that bourgeois education itself "is determined by the social conditions under which you educate" and is "interference by society" no less than Communist education would be (002-027).

## The Historicisation of Ideas

A key ideological operation that the *Manifesto* both critiques and performs is the historicisation of ideas. Bourgeois ideologists present the ideas of their class as eternal truths, "eternal laws of Nature and of Reason" (002-032). Marx and Engels respond by showing that these ideas are historically specific products of a particular mode of production — "the intellectual creations of individual nations become common property" as "national one-sidedness and narrow-mindedness become more and more impossible" (001-019). The Communist demand to abolish the eternal truths of bourgeois society is no more radical than the bourgeoisie's own abolition of feudal truths: "When Christian ideas succumbed in the 18th century to rationalist ideas, feudal society fought its death battle with the then revolutionary bourgeoisie" (002-060). Ideas are weapons in class struggle, not neutral descriptions of reality.

> The class which has the means of material production at its disposal, has control at the same time over the means of mental production. (002-058)

## The Bourgeois Ideologists

The *Manifesto* also attends to the social position of intellectuals themselves. "A portion of the bourgeois ideologists, who have raised themselves to the level of comprehending theoretically the historical movement as a whole," Marx and Engels note, "go over to the proletariat" (001-041). This is a remarkable acknowledgment that intellectual defection from the ruling class is possible — that the comprehension of historical necessity can override class interest. It also implicitly distinguishes between two kinds of intellectuals: those who produce apologetics for the existing order, and those (like Marx and Engels themselves) who use theoretical understanding to side with the revolutionary class.
