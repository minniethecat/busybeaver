> The Socialist and Communist systems properly so called, those of Saint-Simon, Fourier, Owen and others, spring into existence in the early undeveloped period, described above, of the struggle between proletariat and bourgeoisie. [(006-003)]

**Robert Owen** (1771–1858) was a Welsh textile manufacturer, social reformer, and one of the founding figures of the cooperative and trade union movements in Britain. In the *Manifesto of the Communist Party*, [[karl-marx|Marx]] and [[friedrich-engels|Engels]] classified Owen — alongside [[charles-fourier|Charles Fourier]] and Henri de Saint-Simon — as a "critical-utopian socialist": a thinker whose system represented an authentic, if premature, response to the emergence of the [[proletariat]] as a suffering class, but whose rejection of political action and class struggle rendered it reactionary once the proletariat had begun to organize independently. Owen's New Lanark experiment and his advocacy of cooperative communities made him the most practically influential utopian socialist in the English-speaking world.

## In the Manifesto

### Section III.3: Critical-Utopian Socialism and Communism

The Manifesto devotes the final subsection of Section III to "Critical-Utopian Socialism and Communism," treating it as the most advanced form of pre-Marxist socialism — advanced precisely because it grasps the class antagonism between bourgeoisie and proletariat, even if it cannot yet conceive of the proletariat as a self-acting revolutionary force. Owen is named alongside Saint-Simon and Fourier as one of the three representative figures of this tradition [(006-003)].

Marx and Engels' assessment is dialectical — simultaneously appreciative and critical:

**Recognition.** The utopian socialists "attack every principle of existing society" and are "full of the most valuable materials for the enlightenment of the working class" [(006-008)]. Their systems contain genuine insights into the contradictions of capitalist society: the subordination of human needs to profit, the irrationality of competition, the wastefulness of anarchy in production.

**Limitation.** The utopians' fatal weakness is their inability to grasp the historical agency of the proletariat [(006-004)]. The founders of utopian systems could perceive [[class-antagonism|class antagonisms]] in the prevailing form of society, but they could not yet see the proletariat as an active historical subject:

> The proletariat, as yet in its infancy, offers to them the spectacle of a class without any historical initiative or any independent political movement. [(006-004)]

Consequently, the utopians do not call on the proletariat to organize and fight — they appeal to the whole of society, and especially to the ruling class, to adopt their schemes through reason and example. Their orientation is fundamentally philanthropic rather than political:

> Only from the point of view of being the most suffering class does the proletariat exist for them. [(006-007)]

> They habitually appeal to society at large, without distinction of class; nay, by preference, to the ruling class. For how can people, when once they understand their system, fail to see in it the best possible plan of the best possible state of society? [(006-008)]

This appeal to the ruling class is the structural expression of the utopians' inability to conceive of the [[proletariat]] as a self-emancipating class. They "still dream of experimental realisation of their social Utopias — of founding isolated 'phalanstères,' of establishing 'Home Colonies,' of setting up a 'Little Icaria'" [(006-011)].

**Reactionary trajectory.** The Manifesto's sharpest critique is reserved for the utopians' political trajectory once the class struggle develops beyond their theoretical horizon. The Owenites and Fourierists, by their principled rejection of political action, end up in de facto opposition to the working-class movement:

> The Owenites in England, and the Fourierists in France, respectively, oppose the Chartists and the "Réformistes." [(006-014)]

This is the decisive line: [[utopian-socialism|utopian socialism]], whatever its original merits, becomes reactionary the moment it positions itself against the real, independent political movement of the [[proletariat|working class]] — in England, the [[chartists|Chartists]]; in France, the democratic-socialist Réformistes around the newspaper *La Réforme*.

> In proportion as the modern class struggle develops and takes definite shape, this fantastic standing apart from the contest, these fantastic attacks on it, lose all practical value and all theoretical justification. Therefore, although the originators of these systems were, in many respects, revolutionary, their disciples have, in every case, formed mere reactionary sects. [(006-012)]

This passage encapsulates the dialectical logic of the Manifesto's entire treatment of utopian socialism: the founders were historically progressive in their time, but the historical development of the [[class-struggle|class struggle]] itself transforms their systems from a critical force into a conservative one. What was once an anticipation becomes an obstruction.

## Historical Context

### From New Lanark to the Cooperative Movement

Robert Owen was born in Newtown, Montgomeryshire, Wales, in 1771. After a precocious career in the cotton trade, he became manager and part-owner of the New Lanark mills in Scotland in 1799. At New Lanark, Owen implemented a series of reforms unprecedented in early industrial Britain: he reduced the working day from 14 to 10.5 hours, refused to employ children under ten, established the first infant school in Britain (the "Institute for the Formation of Character"), provided workers with decent housing, and created a company store selling goods at near-cost prices. Productivity and profits rose — demonstrating, Owen argued, that humane conditions and profitability were compatible.

New Lanark became a destination for reformers, politicians, and intellectuals from across Europe and America. Owen used the platform to advocate for factory legislation (contributing to the Factory Act of 1819), national education, and, increasingly, the establishment of self-sufficient cooperative communities that would bypass the competitive market entirely.

### Owenism as a Mass Movement

Owen's ideas, unlike those of Fourier or Saint-Simon, generated a mass following among British workers. After his break with New Lanark's investors in 1824, Owen turned to the cooperative and labour movements. [[owenites|Owenite]] "labour exchanges" — where goods were exchanged according to the labour time embodied in them — briefly flourished in the early 1830s. The Grand National Consolidated Trades Union, which Owen helped found in 1834, was the most ambitious attempt at a national working-class organization before Chartism. At its peak it claimed half a million members, though it collapsed within a year under employer counter-attacks and internal organizational weaknesses.

Owen's American experiment at New Harmony, Indiana (1825–1829), consumed much of his fortune and failed within four years. Yet his influence on the British labour movement was lasting: Owenism provided the intellectual bridge from 18th-century radicalism to 19th-century socialism and trade unionism.

### Owen and Marx

Marx and Engels' relationship to Owen was one of critical appropriation. Engels, in *Socialism: Utopian and Scientific* (1880), would later systematize the distinction between "utopian" and "scientific" socialism that the Manifesto had first sketched: Owen, like Fourier and Saint-Simon, had correctly identified many of capitalism's evils but had failed to grasp the historical laws of its development and the specific revolutionary role of the proletariat. Owen thought society could be transformed by appealing to reason, morality, and the enlightened self-interest of the ruling class — a position the Manifesto dismisses as "castles in the air" [(006-009)].

The Manifesto's critique of the utopian approach to social change is sharp:

> Hence, they reject all political, and especially all revolutionary, action; they wish to attain their ends by peaceful means, and endeavour, by small experiments, necessarily doomed to failure, and by the force of example, to pave the way for the new social Gospel. [(006-009)]

This rejection of political action is not incidental to utopian socialism — it is constitutive of its entire worldview. The utopians substitute the inventive activity of a single mind for the collective historical action of a class [(006-006)].

Nevertheless, Marx and Engels consistently acknowledged their debt to Owen's practical achievements. Owen had demonstrated, through New Lanark, that the capitalist organization of production was not a law of nature — that different social arrangements were possible, and that workers could manage production themselves. The cooperative factories that Owen inspired were, for Marx, "the first examples of the emergence of a new form of production within the old form" (*Capital*, Vol. III).

## Key Works

- *A New View of Society; or, Essays on the Principle of the Formation of the Human Character* (1813–1816) — Four essays arguing that human character is formed by environment, and that social reform must therefore transform the environment.
- *Report to the County of Lanark* (1820) — Owen's most developed economic argument for cooperative communities as the solution to post-Napoleonic distress.
- *The Book of the New Moral World* (1836–1844) — Owen's systematic exposition of his "rational system of society," in seven parts.
- *The Revolution in the Mind and Practice of the Human Race* (1849) — Late work restating Owen's conviction that social transformation requires a moral and intellectual revolution, not a political one.

## Significance

Robert Owen occupies a unique position in the prehistory of Marxist socialism. He was the only major utopian socialist who was also a successful capitalist — a man who had managed factories, employed thousands, and made a fortune, yet spent the second half of his life campaigning for a social system that would abolish capitalism, competition, and private profit. The Manifesto's critique of Owen is thus not a dismissal but a *supersession* (Aufhebung): what was progressive in Owen — his demonstration that production could be organized cooperatively, his refusal to treat poverty as natural or inevitable — is preserved within communism, while what was utopian — the appeal to reason over class struggle, the "Home Colonies" over the conquest of political power — is negated.

The Owenite trajectory — from a mass working-class movement to a sect that refused political action — served Marx and Engels as a cautionary example of what happens when socialism is separated from the real movement of the working class. The Manifesto's insistence that Communists "do not form a separate party opposed to other working-class parties" [(002-002)] and that they "fight for the attainment of the immediate aims" of the working class even while maintaining the long-term revolutionary perspective [(007-002)] is, in part, a strategic lesson drawn from Owenism's political failure [(006-009)] [(006-012)].
