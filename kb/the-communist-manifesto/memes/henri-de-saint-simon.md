> The Socialist and Communist systems properly so called, those of Saint-Simon, Fourier, Owen and others, spring into existence in the early undeveloped period, described above, of the struggle between proletariat and bourgeoisie. [(006-003)]

**Henri de Saint-Simon** (1760–1825) was a French social theorist, aristocrat, and the founder of Saint-Simonianism — a technocratic and industrialist doctrine that profoundly influenced early nineteenth-century socialist, positivist, and managerial thought. In the *Manifesto of the Communist Party*, [[karl-marx|Marx]] and [[friedrich-engels|Engels]] placed Saint-Simon first among the three "critical-utopian socialists" — alongside [[charles-fourier|Charles Fourier]] and [[robert-owen|Robert Owen]] — whose systems represented the most advanced form of pre-Marxist socialism [(006-003)].

## In the Manifesto

### Section III.3: Critical-Utopian Socialism and Communism

The Manifesto devotes the final subsection of Ch. III to the critical-utopian systems, assessing them as the highest development of pre-proletarian socialism precisely because they recognize the reality of class antagonism — even if they cannot yet grasp the proletariat as the agent of its own emancipation.

Saint-Simon appears in the Manifesto only through this single direct mention [(006-003)], but his influence pervades the entire section. The critique levelled against the utopians — that they "reject all political, and especially all revolutionary, action," that they "wish to attain their ends by peaceful means," that they "endeavour, by small experiments, necessarily doomed to failure, and by the force of example, to pave the way for the new social Gospel" [(006-009)] — applies directly to Saint-Simon's method. His appeals were addressed not to the working class but to the industriels (industrialists, bankers, engineers, and scientists) and ultimately to the King and the ruling powers.

Marx and Engels' criticism is simultaneously generous and devastating. They acknowledge that the utopian publications "contain also a critical element. They attack every principle of existing society. Hence they are full of the most valuable materials for the enlightenment of the working class" [(006-011)]. But the political trajectory of Saint-Simon's disciples — the Saint-Simonian sect that formed after his death under Prosper Enfantin and Saint-Amand Bazard — confirmed the Manifesto's thesis that utopian socialism, once it positions itself against the independent political movement of the working class, becomes reactionary [(006-012–006-013)].

## Historical Context

### Aristocrat Turned Social Prophet

Claude Henri de Rouvroy, comte de Saint-Simon, was born into an impoverished branch of the French aristocracy — he claimed descent from Charlemagne. He fought in the American War of Independence under Washington, was imprisoned during the Terror (narrowly escaping the guillotine), and emerged from the revolutionary period a speculator in nationalized Church lands — briefly a wealthy man before losing his fortune.

The experience of revolution and its aftermath shaped Saint-Simon's central question: how could post-revolutionary society be reorganized on a rational, productive basis without the chaos of class war? His answer was a technocratic vision in which scientists, engineers, and industrialists would replace the old feudal and clerical ruling classes, directing society according to the principles of industrial production rather than dynastic or theological authority.

### The Doctrine: From *industriels* to *Nouveau Christianisme*

Saint-Simon's thought evolved through three phases. In his early works, notably *L'Industrie* (1817–1818) and *L'Organisateur* (1819–1820), he championed the productive classes — the *industriels* — against the "idlers" (the old nobility and clergy). Society, he argued, should be organized like a vast workshop, directed by those who create wealth rather than those who merely consume it. The famous "Parabola of Saint-Simon" — the hypothetical removal of France's leading scientists, artists, and industrialists, contrasted with the removal of its nobles, bishops, and court officials — dramatized the argument that the nation's productive elite was its true ruling class in everything but political form.

In his middle period, Saint-Simon extended this technocratic vision to politics. *Du système industriel* (1821–1822) and *Catéchisme des industriels* (1823–1824) called for a reorganized State in which an elected chamber of industry would replace parliamentary politics, and the administration of things would replace the government of persons — a formulation that would echo through Engels' *Anti-Dühring* and later Marxist accounts of the "withering away of the State."

His final work, *Nouveau Christianisme* (1825), took a religious turn. Saint-Simon called for a new Christianity stripped of theology and focused on "the amelioration of the moral and physical condition of the poorest and most numerous class." This moral imperative — that society should be organized to benefit the most suffering class — profoundly influenced the young Auguste Comte (Saint-Simon's secretary from 1817 to 1824) and, through Comte, the positivist tradition. It also anticipated the Manifesto's own moral urgency, though Marx and Engels would locate the motor of history not in moral sentiment but in class struggle.

### The Saint-Simonian School and Its Fate

After Saint-Simon's death in 1825, his disciples — Enfantin, Bazard, Olinde Rodrigues, and others — transformed his doctrines into a quasi-religious movement, the "Saint-Simonian Church," complete with a hierarchy of "Fathers," communal living experiments at Ménilmontant, and increasingly eccentric doctrines about the "rehabilitation of the flesh." The movement was suppressed by the French authorities in 1832, and its leading figures dispersed into the very capitalist enterprises they had once criticized: the Péreire brothers, former Saint-Simonians, founded the Crédit Mobilier, a pioneering investment bank; Ferdinand de Lesseps, another former follower, built the Suez Canal.

This trajectory — from revolutionary criticism to sectarian isolation to absorption into the capitalist order — is precisely what Marx and Engels were describing when they wrote that the utopians' disciples "have, in every case, formed mere reactionary sects" and that "by degrees they sink into the category of the reactionary conservative Socialists" [(006-012)].

### Saint-Simon's Influence on Marx

Despite the Manifesto's critique, Marx's debt to Saint-Simon was significant. The Saint-Simonian concept of the "administration of things" replacing the "government of persons" entered the Marxist tradition as the theory of the State's withering away. Saint-Simon's emphasis on the primacy of industrial production, his class analysis (however rudimentary) of "productive" versus "idle" classes, and his vision of a rationally planned economy all prefigured elements of Marx's own system. The difference, as the Manifesto insists, was that Saint-Simon — and the utopians generally — imagined that reason and example could persuade the ruling class to adopt their schemes, whereas Marx located the transformative agency in the revolutionary self-activity of the proletariat itself.

## Related Figures

Saint-Simon stands at the head of the utopian socialist triad named in the Manifesto. [[charles-fourier|Fourier]] shared his critique of existing society but located the solution in the reorganization of labour into passionate associations (phalanxes) rather than in technocratic administration. [[robert-owen|Owen]] shared his practical orientation but grounded his experiments in factory management and cooperative production rather than in the appeals to enlightened elites that characterized Saint-Simon's method. [[karl-marx|Marx]] and [[friedrich-engels|Engels]] inherited from all three the critique of capitalist society, the vision of a classless future, and the conviction that existing social arrangements were neither natural nor eternal — while decisively breaking with their rejection of class struggle as the motor of historical change.

## Key Works

- *Lettres d'un habitant de Genève à ses contemporains* (1803) — Early scientific-messianic vision
- *L'Industrie* (1817–1818) — Defence of the productive classes against idlers
- *L'Organisateur* (1819–1820) — The famous "Parabola" and technocratic programme
- *Du système industriel* (1821–1822) — Blueprint for industrialist political order
- *Catéchisme des industriels* (1823–1824) — Popular exposition of Saint-Simonian doctrine
- *Nouveau Christianisme* (1825) — Religious-moral turn toward amelioration of the poorest class
