**Civilisation**, in the Manifesto's lexicon, designates the specifically bourgeois form of society — a mode of production that has created "more massive and more colossal productive forces than have all preceding generations together" [(001-022)] while simultaneously generating the crises that will destroy it. "Because there is too much civilisation, too much means of subsistence, too much industry, too much commerce" [(001-025)], bourgeois society periodically plunges into crisis — a "state of momentary barbarism" [(001-025)] that reveals civilisation as its own antithesis.

## Definition

Marx and Engels use "civilisation" in a historically specific sense. It is not a universal stage of human progress but the concrete social order produced by the bourgeoisie — a civilisation of world markets, industrial production, urbanisation, and "naked, shameless, direct, brutal exploitation" [(001-014)].

The Manifesto's analysis of civilisation is dialectical. Bourgeois civilisation is a real advance over feudal society. "The bourgeoisie, during its rule of scarce one hundred years, has created more massive and more colossal productive forces than have all preceding generations together" [(001-022)]. It has drawn "all, even the most barbarian, nations into civilisation" [(001-020)], breaking down national barriers and creating a genuinely world-historical system of production and exchange.

But this civilisation is built on a contradiction. The same productive forces that generate its wealth periodically overwhelm its capacity to organise them. "Modern bourgeois society... is like the sorcerer, who is no longer able to control the powers of the nether world whom he has called up by his spells" [(001-025)]. Civilisation produces barbarism — not as an external threat but as its own internal consequence.

## In the Manifesto

### The Civilising Mission of the Bourgeoisie

Section I of the Manifesto describes the bourgeoisie's "civilising mission" with a mixture of genuine admiration and savage irony. "The bourgeoisie, by the rapid improvement of all instruments of production, by the immensely facilitated means of communication, draws all, even the most barbarian, nations into civilisation" [(001-020)]. "The cheap prices of its commodities are the heavy artillery with which it batters down all Chinese walls, with which it forces the barbarians' intensely obstinate hatred of foreigners to capitulate" [(001-020)].

This is not a celebration of colonialism but a materialist analysis of its mechanisms. The bourgeoisie does not conquer through armies alone but through the economic logic of the market, which "compels all nations, on pain of extinction, to adopt the bourgeois mode of production" [(001-020)]. "It compels them to introduce what it calls civilisation into their midst, i.e., to become bourgeois themselves. In one word, it creates a world after its own image" [(001-020)].

The Manifesto's use of "what it calls civilisation" signals its critical distance. Bourgeois civilisation is not genuine civilisation in any normative sense; it is a historically specific form of domination that represents itself as universal.

### Civilisation in Crisis

The most radical element of the Manifesto's analysis of civilisation is its theory of crisis. "For many a decade past the history of industry and commerce is but the history of the revolt of modern productive forces against modern conditions of production, against the property relations that are the conditions for the existence of the bourgeoisie and of its rule" [(001-025)]. Each commercial crisis "puts back into the question the existence of the entire bourgeois society" [(001-025)].

The cause is not scarcity but abundance. "And how does the bourgeoisie get over these crises? On the one hand by enforced destruction of a mass of productive forces; on the other, by the conquest of new markets, and by the more thorough exploitation of the old ones" [(001-025)]. The civilisation that produces too much must periodically destroy its own products in order to resume production — an absurdity that reveals the limits of the bourgeois order.

### Uneven and Combined Development

The Manifesto's closing section introduces a refinement of the civilisation concept: "Germany... is on the eve of a bourgeois revolution that is bound to be carried out under more advanced conditions of European civilisation, and with a much more developed proletariat, than that of England was in the seventeenth, and of France in the eighteenth century" [(007-007)]. Civilisation does not develop uniformly; its contradictions are concentrated in the countries where it is most advanced, and the "bourgeois revolution in Germany will be but the prelude to an immediately following proletarian revolution" [(007-007)].

## Historical Context

The concept of civilisation in the Manifesto reflects the intellectual climate of the 1840s. The French historian François Guizot had popularised the notion of "the history of civilisation in Europe" as the progressive triumph of the middle classes and representative government. The Manifesto appropriates this narrative while inverting it: civilisation is indeed the work of the bourgeoisie, but its progressive phase is drawing to a close.

The Manifesto also draws on the socialist critique of civilisation developed by Fourier, who had argued that "civilisation" produced poverty in proportion to the wealth it created — that "in civilisation, poverty is born of superabundance itself." The Manifesto translates this moral critique into a materialist analysis of the forces and relations of production.
