**Association** is, in the Manifesto's dialectic of class formation, the counter-movement to [[competition]]. If competition fragments the [[proletariat]] into isolated individuals who confront each other as rivals in the labour market, association reunites them as a collective force. "Thereupon the workers begin to form combinations (Trades' Unions) against the bourgeois; they club together in order to keep up the rate of wages; they found permanent associations in order to make provision beforehand for these occasional revolts" [(001-036)]. The movement from competition to association is the pivot on which the Manifesto's entire political strategy turns.

## Definition

Association in the Manifesto has a dual meaning. In its narrower, organisational sense, it refers to trade unions, friendly societies, and workers' political organisations — the institutional forms through which the proletariat defends its immediate interests. In its broader, historical sense, it names the telos of communist society itself: "an association, in which the free development of each is the condition for the free development of all" [(002-083)].

Between these two poles — the trade union and the classless society — lies the entire spectrum of proletarian self-organisation. The Manifesto traces a trajectory from local, ephemeral combinations through national, permanent associations to the ultimate association of the whole people. Each stage of association corresponds to a stage in the development of the proletariat from a class "in itself" to a class "for itself."

## In the Manifesto

### The Genesis of Association

The Manifesto describes the formation of workers' associations as a response to the experience of competition. "The collisions between individual workmen and individual bourgeois take more and more the character of collisions between two classes" [(001-035)]. The initial motivations are defensive — to resist wage cuts, to maintain standards, to provide mutual aid — but the logic of association pushes beyond these limited aims.

The historical sequence is significant. Workers first form associations within individual workshops; then across workshops within a trade; then across trades within a locality; and finally, through improved communication and transport, across the entire country. "This organisation of the proletarians into a class, and consequently into a political party, is continually being upset again by the competition between the workers themselves. But it ever rises up again, stronger, firmer, mightier" [(001-038)].

### Association and the State

The Manifesto is careful to distinguish proletarian association from the bourgeois state. The state, in its analysis, is "but a committee for managing the common affairs of the whole bourgeoisie" [(001-011)]. Proletarian association is not a request for inclusion within the existing state but the construction of an alternative form of collective power.

This culminates in the programme for the conquest of political power. "The proletariat will use its political supremacy to wrest, by degrees, all capital from the bourgeoisie, to centralise all instruments of production in the hands of the State, i.e., of the proletariat organised as the ruling class" [(002-068)]. The state itself is to be transformed from an instrument of class domination into an instrument of associated production.

### Free Association as the Goal

The concluding vision of Section II is framed in terms of association: "In place of the old bourgeois society, with its classes and class antagonisms, we shall have an association, in which the free development of each is the condition for the free development of all" [(002-083)]. This is not an abandonment of the individual for the collective but a reconciliation of the two — a form of social organisation in which individual freedom and collective coordination cease to be antagonistic.

## Historical Context

The concept of association had a rich history in socialist thought before 1848. The utopian socialists — [[charles-fourier|Fourier]] with his phalansteries, [[robert-owen|Owen]] with his cooperative villages — had made association the centrepiece of their schemes for social reorganisation. The difference in the Manifesto is that association is no longer a utopian blueprint to be imposed from above but a historical tendency emergent from within capitalism itself.

The immediate historical referent is the wave of trade union organisation and working-class political mobilisation that swept Europe in the 1830s and 1840s: the Chartist movement in Britain, the Paris workers' uprisings, the Silesian weavers' revolt of 1844. The Manifesto's analysis of association is at once a description of these movements and a strategic orientation for them — an argument that they must move from economic defence to political offence.
