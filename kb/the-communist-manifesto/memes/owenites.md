> The Owenites in England, and the Fourierists in France, respectively, oppose the Chartists and the "Réformistes." [(006-014)]

The **Owenites** and **Fourierists** were the organized followers of [[robert-owen|Robert Owen]] in England and [[charles-fourier|Charles Fourier]] in France — the two most important utopian socialist movements of the early nineteenth century. In the Manifesto, Marx and Engels invoke them at a decisive moment in the critique of Critical-Utopian Socialism: not to dismiss their founders' contributions (which were "in many respects, revolutionary"), but to indict their disciples' degeneration into reactionary sects that, by the 1840s, actively impeded the development of independent working-class politics. "They, therefore, violently oppose all political action on the part of the working class; such action, according to them, can only result from blind unbelief in the new Gospel" (006-014).

## In the Manifesto

### Ch. III §3: The Fate of the Utopian Sects

The Manifesto's treatment of the Owenites and Fourierists is organized around a dialectical thesis: the historical significance of utopian socialism "bears an inverse relation to historical development" (006-010). The more the class struggle develops and takes definite shape — the more the proletariat becomes a self-conscious political force — the more the utopian sects lose their progressive content and become obstacles to emancipation.

This is a thesis about the relation between ideas and class struggle. When Owen and Fourier first developed their systems, in the "early undeveloped period" of the struggle between proletariat and bourgeoisie (006-003), their critiques of bourgeois society performed genuinely progressive functions: they exposed the contradictions of capitalism, denounced exploitation, and imagined a future society organized on cooperative rather than competitive principles. They were utopian — they believed that socialism could be realized through the power of example and moral persuasion rather than through class struggle — but their utopianism reflected the immaturity of the proletarian movement itself. The working class, not yet fully constituted as a political subject, could only appear in their systems as "a class without any historical initiative or any independent political movement" (006-005).

But as capitalism developed and the proletariat grew, the relation reversed. The utopian systems, which had once been ahead of their time, now fell behind it. The founders' disciples — the Owenites and Fourierists of the 1840s — clung to their masters' original doctrines as if they were eternal truths, refusing to recognize the qualitative change that had taken place in the class struggle. They "still dream of experimental realisation of their social Utopias, of founding isolated 'phalanstères,' of establishing 'Home Colonies,' of setting up a 'Little Icaria' — duodecimo editions of the New Jerusalem" (006-011). And because these schemes required the patronage of the wealthy, the utopian sects found themselves structurally dependent on the very class whose power they claimed to oppose: "they are compelled to appeal to the feelings and purses of the bourgeois" (006-011).

The Manifesto's final verdict on the Owenites and Fourierists is delivered in a single devastating sentence: "They, therefore, violently oppose all political action on the part of the working class" (006-014). This is the ultimate indictment: a socialism that opposes working-class political action is not socialism at all — it is an obstacle to socialism. The Owenites opposed the Chartists, the Fourierists opposed the Réformistes — and in doing so, they objectively served the interests of the bourgeoisie, however sincerely they believed in their own radicalism.

## Historical Role

### The Owenite Movement in Britain

Robert Owen's ideas gave rise to the most extensive working-class movement in Britain before Chartism. The Owenite movement of the 1820s and 1830s was not merely a set of cooperative experiments (New Lanark, New Harmony, Orbiston, Ralahine, Queenwood) but a vast network of cooperative societies, labour exchanges, trade unions, and educational institutions. The Grand National Consolidated Trades Union of 1834, which briefly united hundreds of thousands of workers, was substantially Owenite in inspiration. Owenite halls across the industrial districts served as centres of working-class self-education and organization.

But the movement's anti-political bias proved fatal. Owen himself was hostile to trade unionism (seeing it as a distraction from cooperation), to Chartism (seeing it as futile political reformism), and to the idea of class struggle as such. When the Chartist movement emerged in the late 1830s as the mass political expression of the British working class, the Owenite leadership opposed it — and in doing so, marginalized itself from the living movement of the class it claimed to represent. By 1848, when the Manifesto was published, Owenism had been reduced to a scattering of cooperative societies and communitarian experiments, its political influence spent.

### The Fourierist Movement in France

The Fourierist movement in France followed a similar trajectory. Fourier's ideas, propagated after his death by disciples such as Victor Considérant, attracted a substantial following among the French petty bourgeoisie and intellectual strata in the 1830s and 1840s. The Fourierist newspaper *La Phalange* (later *La Démocratie pacifique*) disseminated the master's doctrines: the reorganization of society into self-sufficient cooperative communities (phalansteries), the transformation of labour into "attractive work" through the harmonious arrangement of human passions, and the rejection of political revolution in favour of peaceful social transformation through example.

Like the Owenites, the Fourierists found themselves on the wrong side of historical development. The 1848 Revolution, in which the Parisian working class emerged as an independent political force, posed a choice that Fourierist doctrine could not accommodate: support the revolutionary action of the proletariat, or oppose it. The Fourierists chose opposition — and, in doing so, sealed their fate as a sect detached from the actual movement of history.

## Marxist Assessment

Marx and Engels' critique of the Owenite and Fourierist sects is not a critique of utopian socialism as such. They explicitly distinguish between the founders — who "were, in many respects, revolutionary" (006-010) — and their disciples, who "have, in every case, formed mere reactionary sects" (006-010). The distinction is crucial to understanding the Manifesto's method: ideas are not judged abstractly but in their relation to the concrete development of the class struggle. The same ideas that were progressive in 1820 — the critique of competition, the vision of cooperative production, the exposure of bourgeois hypocrisy — became reactionary in 1848, when they were deployed *against* the independent political mobilization of the working class.

The methodological lesson generalizes. The Manifesto does not reject the utopian socialist critique of capitalism; it *historicizes* it, showing that the truth-content of utopian socialism was inseparable from the historical moment of its emergence and that its continued assertion beyond that moment transformed it into its opposite. Socialism, to be genuine, must be the self-emancipation of the working class — not the charitable project of enlightened philanthropists, however sincere.
