> A spectre is haunting Europe — the spectre of Communism. All the Powers of old Europe have entered into a holy alliance to exorcise this spectre: Pope and Czar, Metternich and Guizot, French Radicals and German police-spies. [(P01-001)]

With this electrifying opening, the *Manifesto of the Communist Party* (1848) introduced **communism** into the political lexicon of the modern world. In the hands of [[karl-marx|Karl Marx]] and [[friedrich-engels|Friedrich Engels]], communism was no longer a diffuse utopian aspiration but a rigorous theoretical system grounded in the analysis of [[class-struggle|class struggle]], the dynamics of [[capital|capitalist]] production, and the historical mission of the [[proletariat]]. The Manifesto's communism was at once a diagnosis of the present, a theory of history, and a call to revolutionary action — a synthesis that would shape the course of the next century and a half of global politics.

## Definition

In the strict sense established by Marx and Engels in Section II of the Manifesto, communism is the theoretical and practical movement that seeks to abolish **bourgeois private property** — not property in general, but the specific form of property that enables the [[bourgeoisie]] to appropriate the surplus labour of the [[proletariat]]. The theoretical conclusions of the Communists "merely express, in general terms, actual relations springing from an existing class struggle, from a historical movement going on under our very eyes" [(002-008)]. Positively, communism aims at "the formation of the proletariat into a class, overthrow of the bourgeois supremacy, conquest of political power by the proletariat" [(002-007)].

The core theoretical claim distinguishing communism from all other working-class movements is encapsulated in a single sentence: **"Abolition of private property"** [(002-012)]. But this abolition is not an end in itself — it is the necessary precondition for the emancipation of labour from the wage-system and the dissolution of class antagonisms as such. "In bourgeois society, living labour is but a means to increase accumulated labour. In Communist society, accumulated labour is but a means to widen, to enrich, to promote the existence of the labourer" [(002-022)].

> The theory of the Communists may be summed up in the single sentence: Abolition of private property. [(002-012)]

> The distinguishing feature of Communism is not the abolition of property generally, but the abolition of bourgeois property. But modern bourgeois private property is the final and most complete expression of the system of producing and appropriating products, that is based on class antagonisms, on the exploitation of the many by the few. [(002-011)]

## In the Manifesto

The Manifesto is, from its title page onward, a document of communist self-clarification. "It is high time that Communists should openly, in the face of the whole world, publish their views, their aims, their tendencies, and meet this nursery tale of the Spectre of Communism with a Manifesto of the party itself" [(P01-003)].

### Section II: The Theoretical Core

The most concentrated exposition of communism in the text occupies the entirety of Section II, "Proletarians and Communists." Here Marx and Engels answer, point by point, the principal objections raised against communism by its bourgeois critics.

**The Communists' relationship to the proletariat.** "The Communists do not form a separate party opposed to other working-class parties. They have no interests separate and apart from those of the proletariat as a whole" [(002-002)]. What distinguishes Communists is not a sectarian programme but two characteristics: (1) in national struggles, "they point out and bring to the front the common interests of the entire proletariat, independently of all nationality"; and (2) in the successive stages of the class struggle, "they always and everywhere represent the interests of the movement as a whole" [(002-005)]. Communists are thus "the most advanced and resolute section of the working-class parties of every country" — practically, the vanguard that pushes forward all others; theoretically, the section that "clearly understand[s] the line of march, the conditions, and the ultimate general results of the proletarian movement" [(002-006)].

**The abolition of property.** The Manifesto's defence of property abolition proceeds dialectically. The existing property relations of bourgeois society are not eternal but historically specific — "the final and most complete expression of the system of producing and appropriating products, that is based on class antagonisms, on the exploitation of the many by the few" [(002-011)]. Wage-labour does not create property for the labourer; it creates [[capital]], "that kind of property which exploits wage-labour, and which cannot increase except upon condition of begetting a new supply of wage-labour for fresh exploitation" [(002-014)]. To abolish bourgeois property is therefore to abolish the exploitation of labour — not to abolish personal freedom but to establish its material basis.

**The charges rebutted.** Marx and Engels systematically dismantle the bourgeois objections to communism: the charge that communism would abolish individual freedom (it would abolish bourgeois individuality — "the freedom of the egoistic individual to trade freely" [(002-026)]); the charge that communism would abolish the family (the bourgeois family already "finds its complement in the practical absence of the family among the proletarians, and in public prostitution" [(002-040)]); the charge that communism would abolish nationality ("The working men have no country" [(002-051)], but the proletariat must first "constitute itself the nation" before class distinctions vanish); the charge that communism abolishes "eternal truths" — religion, morality, freedom, justice — which are not eternal at all, but the ideological reflexes of a specific mode of production.

> The Communists are on the one hand, practically, the most advanced and resolute section of the working-class parties of every country, that section which pushes forward all others; on the other hand, theoretically, they have over the great mass of the proletariat the advantage of clearly understanding the line of march, the conditions, and the ultimate general results of the proletarian movement. [(002-006)]

> The charges against Communism made from a religious, a philosophical, and, generally, from an ideological standpoint, are not deserving of serious examination. [(002-056)]

### Section III: Communism Distinguished from Other Socialisms

The Manifesto devotes its entire third section to demarcating communism from the various competing socialist and communist currents of the 1840s: reactionary socialism (feudal, petty-bourgeois, and German or "True" Socialism), conservative or bourgeois socialism, and critical-utopian socialism and communism. In each case, the critique turns on the same criterion: whether the variant in question grasps the historical movement of the class struggle, or instead seeks to "deadening" it, to turn it back, to transcend it without the revolutionary agency of the proletariat.

### The Ten-Point Programme

As an immediate practical programme "for the most advanced countries," the Manifesto proposes ten measures [(002-068)]:

| # | Measure | Domain |
|---|---------|--------|
| 1 | Abolition of property in land and application of all rents of land to public purposes | Land |
| 2 | A heavy progressive or graduated income tax | Taxation |
| 3 | Abolition of all rights of inheritance | Property |
| 4 | Confiscation of the property of all emigrants and rebels | Property |
| 5 | Centralisation of credit in the hands of the State | Finance |
| 6 | Centralisation of the means of communication and transport in the hands of the State | Infrastructure |
| 7 | Extension of factories and instruments of production owned by the State | Industry |
| 8 | Equal obligation of all to work; establishment of industrial armies, especially for agriculture | Labour |
| 9 | Combination of agriculture with manufacturing industries; gradual abolition of the distinction between town and country | Spatial |
| 10 | Free education for all children in public schools; abolition of children's factory labour | Education |

Marx and Engels are careful to qualify that these measures "will, of course, be different in different countries" [(002-067)] and that they are transitional — steps toward the eventual abolition of class distinctions and the "withering away" of the state's political character.

## Historical Context

Communism as Marx and Engels defined it in 1848 did not emerge from a vacuum. Its immediate organizational matrix was the **Communist League** (Bund der Kommunisten), an international revolutionary society of German émigré workers that commissioned the Manifesto at its November–December 1847 Congress in London. The League itself had evolved from the earlier **League of the Just** — a secret society influenced by utopian, conspiratorial traditions — under Marx and Engels' theoretical guidance.

The intellectual prehistory of communism stretched further back. The term *communist* had circulated in French radical circles since the 1830s, associated with figures like **Étienne Cabet** (whose *Voyage en Icarie* depicted a communist utopia) and the egalitarian conspirator **Gracchus Babeuf** (executed in 1797 for his "Conspiracy of the Equals"). These early communisms, however, were either utopian-blueprint or insurrectionary-conspiratorial in character — neither grounded in a materialist analysis of capitalism's laws of motion.

What Marx and Engels added was precisely this materialist foundation. By fusing German philosophy (Hegel's dialectic turned "right side up"), French political theory (the class analysis of the Restoration historians), and English political economy (Smith, Ricardo), they transformed communism from a moral protest against inequality into a science of revolutionary praxis. "The theoretical conclusions of the Communists are in no way based on ideas or principles that have been invented, or discovered, by this or that would-be universal reformer. They merely express, in general terms, actual relations springing from an existing class struggle" [(002-008)].

The Manifesto appeared in February 1848, weeks before the outbreak of the revolutions that swept across Europe. It was first published in German, with subsequent editions in French, English, Polish, Italian, Danish, Flemish, Swedish, and Russian — a testament to the international character of the communist movement from its inception.

> Working men have no country. We cannot take from them what they have not got. Since the proletariat must first of all acquire political supremacy, must rise to be the leading class of the nation, must constitute itself the nation, it is, so far, itself national, though not in the bourgeois sense of the word. [(002-052)]

> In place of the old bourgeois society, with its classes and class antagonisms, we shall have an association, in which the free development of each is the condition for the free development of all. [(002-083)]

## Related Concepts

Communism in the Manifesto is defined relationally, through its opposition to and entanglement with other core concepts:

| Concept | Relation to Communism |
|---------|----------------------|
| [[proletariat|Proletariat]] | The revolutionary subject — communism is the proletariat's theory and programme |
| [[bourgeoisie|Bourgeoisie]] | The class whose rule communism seeks to overthrow; communism is the negation of bourgeois property relations |
| [[class-struggle|Class Struggle]] | The motor of history that communism neither invents nor denies, but brings to consciousness and accelerates |
| [[capital|Capital]] | The social relation whose abolition defines communism's positive content |
| [[means-of-production|Means of Production]] | What communism socialises — the material-technical basis of production, concentrated and centralised by capitalism itself |
| [[revolution|Revolution]] | The political form of the transition to communism — the forcible overthrow of the bourgeois state and the conquest of political power by the proletariat |
| [[private-property|Private Property]] | Not property as such, but bourgeois private property — the specific form communism abolishes |

## Significance

The Manifesto's articulation of communism marks a rupture in the history of political thought. Before 1848, socialism and communism were minority currents within the European left — diverse, sectarian, often fantastical. After 1848, and increasingly after the 1870s, "communism" became the name of the most consequential political movement of the modern era.

**Three dimensions of the Manifesto's significance** stand out:

1. **Theory of the party.** The Manifesto invented the figure of the communist party as a distinct kind of political organization: not a parliamentary faction, not a conspiratorial cell, but the organized vanguard of a class that "always and everywhere represent[s] the interests of the movement as a whole" [(002-005)]. This conception of the party — as the bearer of a strategic perspective that transcends the immediate and the local — would shape every subsequent Leninist, Maoist, and Trotskyist organization.

2. **Internationalism.** "Working men have no country." The Manifesto grounds proletarian internationalism not in abstract humanitarian sentiment but in the structural reality of a world market that already unifies the bourgeoisie across national borders — and therefore unifies its antagonist as well. The slogan "Working Men of All Countries, Unite!" [(007-010)] is not a wish but a strategic deduction from the nature of capitalism.

3. **The dialectic of freedom and necessity.** The Manifesto's communism is voluntarist and determinist in the same breath. History moves toward communism with "the inexorability of a law of nature" — and yet this movement can only be realised through the conscious, organized, revolutionary action of the proletariat. This dialectic — freedom within necessity, strategy within structural determination — is the philosophical signature of Marxist communism, distinguishing it from both fatalistic economism and voluntarist putschism.

> The Communist revolution is the most radical rupture with traditional property relations; no wonder that its development involves the most radical rupture with traditional ideas. [(002-065)]

> What else does the history of ideas prove, than that intellectual production changes its character in proportion as material production is changed? The ruling ideas of each age have ever been the ideas of its ruling class. [(002-058)]

## Further Reading

- [[friedrich-engels|Friedrich Engels]], *Principles of Communism* (1847) — The draft that preceded the Manifesto, organised as a catechism of 25 questions and answers.
- Karl Marx, *Critique of the Gotha Programme* (1875) — Marx's most detailed discussion of the distinction between the "lower" and "higher" phases of communist society.
- Karl Marx and Friedrich Engels, *The German Ideology* (1846) — The first systematic exposition of the materialist conception of history that underpins the Manifesto's communism.
- [[karl-marx|Karl Marx]], *Capital*, Volume I (1867) — The full scientific elaboration of the theory of value and exploitation that the Manifesto presents in compressed political form.
- Hal Draper, *Karl Marx's Theory of Revolution*, Volumes I–V (1977–1990) — The most exhaustive scholarly treatment of Marx and Engels' political theory, especially Volume II on the theory of the party.
- Eric Hobsbawm, *The Age of Revolution, 1789–1848* (1962) — Historical context for the revolutionary conjuncture in which the Manifesto was born.
- David McLellan, *Karl Marx: A Biography* (1973) and *The Thought of Karl Marx* (1971) — Accessible introductions to Marx's intellectual development.
