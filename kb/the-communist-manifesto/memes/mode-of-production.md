## Definition

The mode of production (*Produktionsweise*) is the central concept of Marxian historical materialism. It designates the historically specific ensemble through which a society organises its material reproduction: the productive forces (technology, labour-power, science) together with the relations of production (the class structure of ownership and control over the means of production). Each major epoch — primitive communism, slavery, feudalism, capitalism, and (prospectively) communism — is defined by its distinctive mode of production. The mode of production is the material "base" upon which the legal, political, and ideological "superstructure" is erected, and transformations in the base ultimately drive transformations in the superstructure.

## In the Manifesto

Although the Manifesto does not use the term *mode of production* in the full theoretical sense Marx would later develop in the 1859 Preface, the concept is everywhere operative. The opening narrative of Ch. I is a history of modes of production: the feudal mode, with its guild-system and its "complicated arrangement of society into various orders" [(001-003)], is overthrown by the bourgeois mode, which "cannot exist without constantly revolutionising the instruments of production, and thereby the relations of production, and with them the whole relations of society" [(001-013)].

Marx and Engels explicitly frame the bourgeois mode as a historical — and therefore transient — form. "The modern bourgeoisie is itself the product of a long course of development, of a series of revolutions in the modes of production and of exchange" [(001-009)]. The bourgeoisie's historical mission was to sweep away the feudal mode of production and replace it with one based on wage-labour, competition, and the world market. But the very dynamism that makes the bourgeois mode revolutionary also dooms it: the productive forces it unleashes eventually outgrow "the conditions of bourgeois property" and "bring disorder into the whole of bourgeois society" [(001-023)].

The Manifesto also describes the bourgeoisie's global imposition of its mode of production. It "compels all nations, on pain of extinction, to adopt the bourgeois mode of production; it compels them to introduce what it calls civilisation into their midst, i.e., to become bourgeois themselves. In one word, it creates a world after its own image" [(001-016)].

The revolutionary transition to communism is conceptualised in terms of mode of production. The Communists' programme — abolition of private property, centralisation of credit and transport in the hands of the state, free public education — is a transition to a new mode of production in which "the free development of each is the condition for the free development of all" [(002-053)].

## Historical Context (1848)

The concept of modes of production had been worked out by Marx and Engels in *The German Ideology* (1845–46), where they distinguished tribal, ancient, feudal, and bourgeois modes as successive forms of the division of labour. By 1848, the immediate political relevance of the concept was its demolition of the bourgeois claim that private property and wage-labour were "eternal laws of nature and of reason." Marx and Engels's reply — "what you admit in the case of feudal property, you are of course forbidden to admit in the case of your own bourgeois form of property" — uses the mode-of-production framework to historicise capitalism and thereby deprive it of its natural-law legitimacy.

## Related Concepts

The mode of production is the unity of [[productive-forces]] and [[relations-of-production]]. The productive forces develop dynamically within a given mode; when they reach a point at which the existing relations fetter further development, the mode enters a revolutionary crisis. The Manifesto traces this dialectic through two transitions: feudalism → capitalism (the bourgeoisie's historical role) and capitalism → communism (the proletariat's historical mission).

The concept is distinct from [[means-of-production]], which refers only to the instruments and objects of labour, and from [[relations-of-production]], which refers to the class structure of ownership. The mode of production encompasses both and adds the specific way they are articulated.
