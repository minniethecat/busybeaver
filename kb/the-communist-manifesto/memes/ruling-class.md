The **ruling class**, in the framework of the Manifesto, is the class that holds the dominant position in a given mode of production — owning or controlling the means of material production and, through that control, shaping the ideas, laws, and institutions of the society it governs. "The ruling ideas of each age have ever been the ideas of its ruling class" [(002-058)].

## Definition

Marx and Engels define the ruling class through a chain of causal determinations. Ownership of the means of material production — land in feudalism, capital in bourgeois society — is the foundation. From this material base flows control over the means of mental production: "The class which has the means of material production at its disposal, has control at the same time over the means of mental production, so that thereby, generally speaking, the ideas of those who lack the means of mental production are subject to it" [(002-058)]. Law, morality, religion — each of these appears as a neutral or universal institution but, in reality, serves the interests of the ruling class.

This is not a conspiracy theory. The Manifesto does not suggest that the ruling class secretly orchestrates cultural production according to a master plan. The mechanism is structural: those who control the material conditions of life — who own the presses, fund the schools, endow the churches — determine, without needing to conspire, the broad contours of what can be thought and said.

The concept of the ruling class is thus inseparable from the concept of [[class-struggle]]. Every ruling class maintains its position through a combination of force and ideology. Political power, in the Manifesto's formulation, "is merely the organised power of one class for oppressing another" [(002-082)].

## In the Manifesto

### The Bourgeoisie as Ruling Class

The Manifesto's primary object of analysis is the [[bourgeoisie]] as the ruling class of modern society. "The bourgeoisie... has conquered for itself, in the modern representative State, exclusive political sway. The executive of the modern State is but a committee for managing the common affairs of the whole bourgeoisie" [(001-012)]. This formulation — deliberately provocative in its reduction of the state to an instrument of class rule — was one of the Manifesto's most scandalous claims in 1848 and remains one of its most debated.

The bourgeoisie as a ruling class differs from all its predecessors in two respects. First, it is a revolutionary class that cannot exist without constantly revolutionising the instruments of production and thereby the relations of production [(001-014)]. Earlier ruling classes were conservative by instinct, seeking to preserve the existing order. The bourgeoisie, by contrast, dissolves all fixed relations in the "icy water of egotistical calculation" [(001-014)].

Second, the bourgeoisie is the first ruling class whose rule generates the conditions of its own destruction. "What the bourgeoisie, therefore, produces, above all, is its own grave-diggers. Its fall and the victory of the proletariat are equally inevitable" [(001-050)].

### The Proletariat Organised as Ruling Class

The Manifesto's political programme culminates in the conquest of political power by the proletariat. "The first step in the revolution by the working class is to raise the proletariat to the position of ruling class" [(002-068)]. This formulation — the proletariat as ruling class — contains a paradox that Marx and Engels were fully aware of: the class that has been stripped of all property, that has "nothing to lose but its chains" (P01-005), becomes the ruler.

The resolution of this paradox lies in the transitional character of proletarian rule. The proletariat uses its political supremacy "to wrest, by degrees, all capital from the bourgeoisie, to centralise all instruments of production in the hands of the State, i.e., of the proletariat organised as the ruling class" [(002-068)]. But this rule is self-liquidating. When all production has been socialised and class distinctions have disappeared, "the public power will lose its political character" and the proletariat "will thereby have abolished its own supremacy as a class" [(002-082)].

### The Dissolution of a Ruling Class

The Manifesto provides a vivid phenomenology of a ruling class in crisis. As the class struggle approaches its decisive hour, "the process of dissolution going on within the ruling class, in fact within the whole range of society, assumes such a violent, glaring character, that a small section of the ruling class cuts itself adrift, and joins the revolutionary class" [(001-041)]. This defection of bourgeois ideologists — "who have raised themselves to the level of comprehending theoretically the historical movement as a whole" [(001-041)] — is one of the processes by which the ruling class supplies its antagonist with "fresh elements of enlightenment and progress" [(001-040)].

The Manifesto also tracks the economic dissolution of the ruling class: "entire sections of the ruling classes are, by the advance of industry, precipitated into the proletariat" [(001-040)]. The bourgeoisie's own economic dynamism produces the crisis in which it becomes "unfit any longer to be the ruling class in society" [(001-049)].

### Ruling Class and Education

The reach of the ruling class extends into the formation of consciousness. The Manifesto addresses education directly: "The Communists have not invented the intervention of society in education; they do but seek to alter the character of that intervention, and to rescue education from the influence of the ruling class" [(002-043)]. Bourgeois education is not neutral transmission of knowledge but a process through which the assumptions and interests of the ruling class are naturalised.

## Historical Specificity

A central argument of Section II of the Manifesto is that every epoch's ruling class mistakes its own historically specific forms for eternal truths. "The selfish misconception that induces you to transform into eternal laws of nature and of reason, the social forms springing from your present mode of production and form of property — historical relations that rise and disappear in the progress of production — this misconception you share with every ruling class that has preceded you" [(002-037)]. The bourgeoisie believes that private property, the family, and wage-labour are natural and permanent; just as the feudal lords believed that hereditary privilege was divinely ordained.

The Manifesto's critique of the ruling class is thus also a critique of ideology — of the way material power translates into intellectual domination. "What you see clearly in the case of ancient property, what you admit in the case of feudal property, you are of course forbidden to admit in the case of your own bourgeois form of property" [(002-037)].
