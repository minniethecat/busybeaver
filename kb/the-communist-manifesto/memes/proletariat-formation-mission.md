**The proletariat**, as presented in the *Communist Manifesto*, occupies a distinctive and paradoxical position in modern society: it is simultaneously the most degraded product of capitalism and the one class capable of transcending capitalism altogether. Marx and Engels construct an argument in which the very forces that create the proletariat as an exploited class also equip it with the capacity — and the historical necessity — to become its own negation. This synthesis traces that argument across three dimensions: the economic formation of the proletariat, its political development from scattered individuals to organized class, and the unique character of its historical mission.

## I. The Proletariat as a Product of Capitalism

The proletariat does not pre-exist capitalism; it is brought into being by the same process that creates modern industry. Marx and Engels establish this from the outset of their analysis: "In proportion as the bourgeoisie, i.e., capital, is developed, in the same proportion is the proletariat, the modern working class, developed" (001-028). The bourgeois class cannot accumulate capital without simultaneously generating a mass of propertyless wage-labourers whose survival depends on selling their labour-power. This is not a contingent correlation but a structural necessity: "The condition for capital is wage-labour" (001-050).

The degradation that accompanies this formation is not accidental. The Manifesto describes modern industrial labour as a process of systematic deskilling and dehumanization. The worker "becomes an appendage of the machine" (001-029), reduced to performing "the most simple, most monotonous, and most easily acquired knack" (001-029). The work is repulsive, the wage tends toward the minimum required for subsistence, and "the cost of production of a workman is restricted, almost entirely, to the means of subsistence that he requires for his maintenance" (001-032).

Marx and Engels describe the worker as reduced to a commodity like any other — "slaves of the bourgeois class, and of the bourgeois State" (001-031). Furthermore, "the lower strata of the middle class... sink gradually into the proletariat" (001-033), so that the proletariat "is recruited from all classes of the population" (001-033).

This formation process is, however, also a process of concentration. As capitalism develops, the proletariat is gathered into ever-larger industrial centres, working side by side in factories, sharing common conditions and common grievances. "The proletariat not only increases in number; it becomes concentrated in greater masses, its strength grows, and it feels that strength more" (001-036). The same [[machinery]] and [[modern-industry|modern industry]] that degrade the individual worker create the objective conditions for collective action. The proletariat is forged by capitalism, but its forging contains the seeds of capitalism's destruction.

## II. The Development of the Proletariat into a Class

The Manifesto traces a dynamic progression through which the proletariat moves from a mere aggregate of atomized individuals — a "class in itself" — to an organized, politically conscious force — a "class for itself." This development passes through distinct phases.

The struggle begins at the point of production. "At first the contest is carried on by individual labourers, then by the workpeople of a factory, then by the operatives of one trade, in one locality, against the individual bourgeois who directly exploits them" (001-034). These early struggles are defensive and fragmented. Workers direct their attacks "not against the bourgeois conditions of production, but against the instruments of production themselves" (001-034) — smashing machines, burning factories, failing to grasp that the enemy is not the machine but the social relation that deploys it.

The turning point comes with the formation of trade unions and the broadening of the struggle beyond the individual workplace. "Thereupon the workers begin to form combinations (Trades' Unions) against the bourgeois" (001-035). They club together to maintain wages, fund strikes, and sustain collective resistance. At this stage the struggle remains primarily economic, but its logic increasingly draws workers toward political organization.

As Marx and Engels argue in a celebrated formulation: "Every class struggle is a political struggle" (001-037). The distinction between economic and political action dissolves in practice: the defence of wages against the combined pressure of capital requires organization on a national scale, and this organization inevitably confronts the political power of the bourgeois state.

The peculiarity of the proletariat's development is that it does not remain class-bound. "Entire sections of the ruling class are, by the advance of industry, precipitated into the proletariat, or are at least threatened in their conditions of existence" (001-040). More significantly, "a portion of the bourgeoisie goes over to the proletariat, and in particular, a portion of the bourgeois ideologists, who have raised themselves to the level of comprehending theoretically the historical movement as a whole" (001-041). The [[communism|Communists]] themselves represent this crossover: they are "the most advanced and resolute section of the working-class parties of every country" who possess "the advantage of clearly understanding the line of march, the conditions, and the ultimate general results of the proletarian movement" (002-006).

The ultimate aim of this development is articulated with precision: "the immediate aim of the Communists is the same as that of all other proletarian parties: formation of the proletariat into a class, overthrow of the bourgeois supremacy, conquest of political power by the proletariat" (002-007). The formation into a class is not a natural or automatic process — it requires conscious organization, theoretical clarity, and political struggle.

## III. The Unique Character of the Proletariat's Historical Mission

What makes the proletariat's mission historically unprecedented is not merely that it is a revolutionary class — history has seen many of those — but that its revolution is of a fundamentally different kind. Marx and Engels drive this point home through a series of sharp contrasts with earlier revolutionary classes.

The most concentrated statement comes at the close of Section I: "All the preceding classes that got the upper hand sought to fortify their already acquired status by subjecting society at large to their conditions of appropriation" (001-045). The bourgeoisie, like the feudal aristocracy before it, revolutionized the mode of production only to install itself as a new ruling class. Its emancipation was the emancipation of a particular interest — the class that owned capital — and it consolidated this emancipation through a new set of property relations, a new state, a new ideology.

The proletariat cannot follow this path even if it wanted to. "The proletariat, the lowest stratum of our present society, cannot stir, cannot raise itself up, without the whole superincumbent strata of official society being sprung into the air" (001-046). Because the proletariat has no property to defend, no vested interest to preserve, no class beneath it to exploit — "the proletarians have nothing to lose but their chains. They have a world to win" (007-011) — its self-emancipation necessarily entails the abolition of class society as such.

"Of all the classes that stand face to face with the bourgeoisie today, the proletariat alone is a really revolutionary class" (001-042). The lower middle classes — small tradespeople, artisans, peasants — are "not revolutionary, but conservative. Nay more, they are reactionary, for they try to roll back the wheel of history" (001-042). They fight not to transcend the existing order but to recover a vanishing position within it.

This is the distinctive dialectic of proletarian revolution. The [[bourgeoisie]] "produces, above all, its own grave-diggers" (001-050). The very development of capitalism — the concentration of capital, the socialization of labour, the organization of workers, the crises of overproduction — creates the conditions for its supersession. The Communist programme — "abolition of private property" (002-012), and through it the abolition of [[wage-labour]] and [[exploitation]] — is not an abstract ideal imposed from outside but "merely express[es], in general terms, actual relations springing from an existing class struggle, from a historical movement going on under our very eyes" (002-008).

## Conclusion

The Manifesto's account of the proletariat is both an economic analysis and a political programme. The proletariat is formed by capital as an exploited class; it develops through struggle into a politically organized class; and its historical mission — unlike that of any preceding revolutionary class — is to abolish class society rather than to reconstitute it under new management.

The coherence of this argument rests on a single unifying proposition: the same logic of capitalist development that creates a mass of propertyless, concentrated, and disciplined wage-labourers also creates, objectively and irresistibly, the conditions in which those labourers can recognize their common interest, organize, and act to overthrow the system that produced them. Whether this proposition holds up as historical prediction is a separate question; what is undeniable is the analytical power of the framework that generated it.
