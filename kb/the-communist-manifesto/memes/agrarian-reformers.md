> Section II has made clear the relations of the Communists to the existing working-class parties, such as the Chartists in England and the Agrarian Reformers in America. [(007-001)]

The **Agrarian Reformers** — formally the National Reform Association — were a working-class land-reform movement in the United States during the 1840s. In Section IV of the Manifesto, Marx and Engels name them alongside the [[chartists|Chartists]] as an example of "the existing working-class parties" whose relations to the Communists have been clarified in the preceding analysis: parties with which Communists ally in the immediate struggle while maintaining theoretical independence and pushing toward the ultimate aim of proletarian revolution (007-001–007-002).

## In the Manifesto

### Section IV: A Working-Class Party in America

The Agrarian Reformers appear in the Manifesto at a strategic moment — the opening of Section IV, where Marx and Engels transition from theoretical analysis to the concrete question of communist political tactics. The reference to the Agrarian Reformers serves a double function. First, it demonstrates that the Manifesto's political horizon is international: the working-class movement exists not only in Britain and France but in the United States as well. Second, it shows that the Communists' strategy of tactical alliance with non-communist working-class parties applies across national contexts.

The relations between Communists and "existing working-class parties" are governed by a dialectic of unity and independence. "The Communists fight for the attainment of the immediate aims, for the enforcement of the momentary interests of the working class; but in the movement of the present, they also represent and take care of the future of that movement" (007-002). In the American context, this meant supporting the Agrarian Reformers' demand for free land while criticizing the limitations of a programme that sought to resolve the social question through land distribution rather than the abolition of private property in the means of production.

## Historical Role

### Land Reform as Working-Class Politics

The National Reform Association, founded in 1844 by the radical printer George Henry Evans and the labour leader John Windt, organized around a single demand: that the vast public lands of the United States be distributed in small parcels, free of charge, to actual settlers, with provisions to prevent speculation and land monopoly. This was not agrarian nostalgia — it was a specifically working-class response to the conditions of early American capitalism. In an economy where wage labour alternated with westward migration, where the frontier functioned as a "safety valve" for urban unemployment, access to land was a material question of working-class survival.

Evans articulated the movement's logic with the slogan "Vote Yourself a Farm." The argument was that if workers could obtain land freely, they would not be compelled to sell their labour power to capitalists at starvation wages — the existence of an alternative to wage labour would drive wages up for those who remained in the cities. This was, in effect, a strategy for raising the price of labour power by reducing the reserve army of the unemployed through land distribution.

### The Limits of Agrarian Reform

Marx and Engels' support for the Agrarian Reformers was tactical, not strategic. They recognized that land reform addressed a real working-class grievance and that the movement mobilized actual workers — but they also recognized its theoretical limitations. Distributing land to individual settlers did not abolish the capitalist mode of production; it merely extended it into new territories, creating a class of small proprietors whose eventual expropriation by large capital was, in historical terms, all but inevitable. The agrarian reform programme was, in Marxist terms, a petty-bourgeois solution to a proletarian problem: it sought to turn workers into small proprietors rather than to abolish the system that turned proprietors into workers.

Moreover, the movement's focus on free land as a panacea for working-class poverty obscured the specifically capitalist mechanisms of exploitation — the extraction of surplus value at the point of production — that operated regardless of whether workers had alternatives in the west. A worker who escaped the factory by becoming a homesteader had not overthrown capitalism; they had merely changed their class position within it.

## Marxist Assessment

The Manifesto's reference to the Agrarian Reformers exemplifies the communist strategy of the "united front": support for working-class movements on the basis of their immediate demands, combined with relentless criticism of their theoretical limitations and the patient work of winning their most advanced elements to the communist programme. The Agrarian Reformers were not socialists — their programme was land distribution, not the socialization of the means of production. But they were a genuine working-class movement, and as such, they were allies in the fight against bourgeois domination. The task of Communists was to fight alongside them without becoming subordinate to them, supporting their progressive demands while pointing beyond them to the revolutionary transformation of society as a whole.
