# Centralisation

## Definition

Centralisation, in the Manifesto, operates on two distinct but related planes: political centralisation — the historical process by which the bourgeoisie unified scattered jurisdictions, laws, and markets into a single nation-state — and economic centralisation — the concentration of the means of production in ever fewer hands, culminating in the revolutionary programme's demand that the proletariat centralise "all instruments of production in the hands of the State" (007-010). The concept thus bridges historical description and revolutionary prescription: what the bourgeoisie achieved politically, the proletariat must extend economically.

## In the Manifesto

Political centralisation appears in Ch. I as one of the bourgeoisie's most revolutionary historical achievements. Marx and Engels describe a process of radical simplification and unification: "Independent, or but loosely connected provinces, with separate interests, laws, governments, and systems of taxation, became lumped together into one nation, with one government, one code of laws, one national class-interest, one frontier, and one customs-tariff" (001-022). This was no accidental development — it was the "necessary consequence" of the concentration of economic power, the political form required by a class that needed uniform markets, predictable laws, and a State apparatus capable of enforcing property rights across an entire national territory.

The transition programme in Ch. II gives centralisation its revolutionary inflection. Among the ten measures proposed for "the most advanced countries," items five and six are directly concerned with centralisation: "Centralisation of credit in the hands of the State, by means of a national bank with State capital and an exclusive monopoly" (007-012) and "Centralisation of the means of communication and transport in the hands of the State" (007-013). Credit and transport — the circulatory systems of capitalist economy — are to be concentrated under State control, not to intensify exploitation but to deprive the bourgeoisie of the infrastructure of its power.

The strategic logic is spelled out at the conclusion of Ch. II: "The proletariat will use its political supremacy to wrest, by degrees, all capital from the bourgeoisie, to centralise all instruments of production in the hands of the State, i.e., of the proletariat organised as the ruling class" (002-068). Centralisation here is the mechanism of expropriation — the transitional State does not abolish the concentration of productive forces but redirects it from private to public hands, transforming the bourgeoisie's own weapon against it.

## Historical Context

The political centralisation described in the Manifesto was a lived reality for mid-nineteenth-century Europeans. Germany in 1848 was still a patchwork of thirty-nine states under the German Confederation, and the demand for national unification was a central plank of the liberal and democratic movements. France had achieved territorial centralisation under the Revolution and Napoleon, but the tension between Paris and the provinces remained acute. Marx and Engels saw this process as historically progressive — the bourgeoisie was doing the necessary work of creating a unified national framework that the proletariat would later inherit and transform.

In tracing centralisation to its material roots, the Manifesto was making a methodological point: political forms do not arise from ideas or constitutions but from the requirements of the prevailing mode of production. The bourgeoisie did not invent the nation-state because it read nationalist philosophers — it created the nation-state because capitalism required it.

## Related Concepts

Centralisation is inseparable from the concept of the [[state]], which is the institutional apparatus through which political centralisation is achieved and through which the proletariat will centralise the means of production. [[political-power]] is what is exercised through the centralised State — the "organised power of one class for oppressing another" (002-082). The [[bourgeoisie]] is the historical agent of political centralisation in the modern epoch, while the [[proletariat]] is the class that must carry centralisation to its revolutionary conclusion. [[proletarian-revolution]] names the process by which centralised State power is transferred from one class to another and redirected toward the abolition of class distinctions altogether.
