Commerce, in the *Communist Manifesto*, is not merely the exchange of goods but the material engine of bourgeois expansion. Marx and Engels trace how the growth of commerce — fuelled by colonial conquest, the discovery of new markets, and improvements in navigation — transformed feudal society and propelled the bourgeoisie to political dominance.

## Commerce and the Rise of the Bourgeoisie

The *Manifesto* identifies the expansion of commerce as the primary driver of the bourgeoisie's ascent. The discovery of America and the rounding of the Cape "opened up fresh ground for the rising bourgeoisie" (001-006). Trade with the East-Indian and Chinese markets, the colonisation of America, and "the increase in the means of exchange and in commodities generally, gave to commerce, to navigation, to industry, an impulse never before known" (001-006). This commercial revolution created a self-reinforcing cycle: new markets demanded more goods, which required expanded production, which generated more capital for further commercial ventures.

The feudal system of production, with its closed guilds and limited scale, proved incapable of meeting the demands generated by this commercial expansion. "The feudal system of industry, under which industrial production was monopolised by closed guilds, now no longer sufficed for the growing wants of the new markets" (001-007). Commerce thus acted as a solvent of feudal relations, creating the material conditions for the manufacturing system and eventually modern industry to replace the guild-based organisation of production.

## The World Market and the Cosmopolitan Character of Commerce

Commerce, in Marx and Engels' analysis, has a relentless cosmopolitan drive. The bourgeoisie "chases over the whole surface of the globe" in its need for constantly expanding markets, and "must nestle everywhere, settle everywhere, establish connexions everywhere" (001-010). The exploitation of the world market "has given a cosmopolitan character to production and consumption in every country" (001-019). This is simultaneously a description of commercial reality and a structural argument: commerce makes national boundaries and local cultures economically obsolete, replacing them with "universal inter-dependence of nations" (001-019).

> Modern industry has established the world-market, for which the discovery of America paved the way. This market has given an immense development to commerce, to navigation, to communication by land. (001-009)

## Commerce and Crisis

The *Manifesto* also presents commerce as a source of instability. The commercial crises that periodically convulse bourgeois society — crises in which "there is too much civilisation, too much means of subsistence, too much industry, too much commerce" (001-025) — reveal the irrationality at the heart of the commercial system. The bourgeoisie overcomes these crises "by the conquest of new markets, and by the more thorough exploitation of the old ones" (001-025), a response that only prepares "more extensive and more destructive crises" in the future. Commerce is thus both the source of the bourgeoisie's power and the mechanism of its eventual undoing.
