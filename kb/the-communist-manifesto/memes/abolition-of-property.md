# Abolition of Property

## Definition

The abolition of property, in the Manifesto's precise formulation, means the abolition of *bourgeois* private property — not property in general, but the specific historical form of property that rests on the exploitation of wage-labour and the accumulation of capital. Marx and Engels declare: *"the distinguishing feature of Communism is not the abolition of property generally, but the abolition of bourgeois property"* (002-011). Bourgeois property is *"the final and most complete expression of the system of producing and appropriating products that is based on class antagonisms, on the exploitation of the many by the few"* (002-011). To abolish this form of property is to abolish the class relation that enables the capitalist to appropriate the surplus value produced by the worker.

## In the Manifesto

The abolition of property is the theoretical and political centre of Section II of the Manifesto, which directly addresses bourgeois objections to communism. Marx and Engels systematically dismantle the charge that communists want to abolish property by showing that the bourgeoisie's own historical rise was predicated on the abolition of feudal property: *"the French Revolution, for example, abolished feudal property in favour of bourgeois property"* (002-010). What the bourgeoisie calls "property" is not a universal category but a historically specific form — and communists merely propose to do to bourgeois property what the bourgeoisie itself did to feudal property.

The argument proceeds through a series of rhetorical moves. The Manifesto first establishes that *"the abolition of existing property relations is not at all a distinctive feature of Communism"* (002-008), since all property relations have been subject to continual historical change. It then narrows the target: *"the theory of the Communists may be summed up in the single sentence: Abolition of private property"* (002-012). But this is immediately qualified: the private property to be abolished is not *"the property of the petty artisan and of the small peasant"* — *"that property ... the bourgeoisie has not to abolish; the development of industry has abolished it and is abolishing it daily"* (002-013). Rather, it is *"modern bourgeois private property"* (002-015) — the capital that employs wage-labour and accumulates through exploitation.

The decisive argument is that the worker, under capitalism, already has no property. *"But does wage-labour create any property for the labourer? Not a bit. It creates capital, i.e., that kind of property which exploits wage-labour"* (002-016). The abolition of bourgeois property therefore does not deprive the proletarian of anything he possesses — because he possesses nothing. What it abolishes is the social power that capital confers on the bourgeoisie: the power to command the labour of others. *"In this sense, the theory of the Communists may be summed up in the single sentence: Abolition of private property"* (002-012) — a sentence that the Manifesto defends point by point against each bourgeois objection.

## Historical Context

The demand for the abolition of private property was not unique to Marx and Engels. It had been advanced by earlier communist and socialist thinkers, including Gracchus Babeuf and his Conspiracy of the Equals (1796), as well as by the French utopian communists of the 1830s and 1840s such as Etienne Cabet and Theodore Dezamy. What distinguished Marx and Engels' formulation was its historical specificity. Earlier communists had called for the abolition of property as a moral imperative — property was theft, in Proudhon's phrase. Marx and Engels argued instead that bourgeois property was already being abolished by the development of capitalism itself (through the concentration and centralization of capital), and that the communist demand merely gave conscious expression to a process already underway. The abolition of property was thus not a utopian demand but a historical necessity grounded in the contradictions of the existing mode of production.

## Related Concepts

The abolition of property is the necessary consequence of the analysis of [[private-property]] as a historically specific form of class domination, not a natural right. It is inseparable from the concept of [[exploitation]], since bourgeois property is precisely the mechanism through which the surplus value produced by [[wage-labour]] is appropriated by [[capital]]. The abolition of property is the central demand that distinguishes [[communism]] from all other forms of socialism, and it entails the abolition of the [[bourgeoisie]] as a class — since the bourgeoisie exists only insofar as it owns capital. The concept is directly linked to [[revolution]] as the political process through which the proletariat seizes the means of production and transforms them into social property.

## Significance

The abolition of property has been the single most controversial and consequential demand in the entire Marxist programme. Critics have charged that it leads inexorably to totalitarianism, since the abolition of private property in the means of production eliminates the material basis for independent civil society. Defenders argue that the Manifesto distinguishes clearly between personal property (consumer goods, homes) and bourgeois property (capital, means of production), and that only the latter is to be abolished. The demand also raises the question of transition: how, concretely, is the abolition of bourgeois property to be accomplished? The Manifesto's answer, in its ten-point programme, includes the centralization of credit, transport, and the means of production in the hands of the state — measures that proved enormously influential in the twentieth century. The concept remains at the heart of debates about socialism, communism, and the relationship between property rights and human freedom.
