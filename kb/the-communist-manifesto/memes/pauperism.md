## Definition

**Pauperism** denotes the condition of extreme, structural poverty that Marx and Engels identify as an inherent outcome of capitalist development. It is not merely individual misfortune or temporary deprivation but a systemic phenomenon: the accumulation of capital on one side of society generates, as its necessary counterpart, the accumulation of misery, degradation, and destitution on the other. Pauperism is distinguished from the general condition of the proletariat in that the pauper is no longer even able to sell their labour-power — they have fallen out of the active industrial army into the "surplus population" or industrial reserve army. The pauper represents the *limit case* of proletarian existence, the point at which exploitation has exhausted the worker's capacity to reproduce themselves.

## In the Manifesto

The Manifesto introduces pauperism in a striking passage that links the worker's descent into destitution directly to the competitive dynamics of capitalist production:

> "He becomes a pauper, and pauperism develops more rapidly than population and wealth." [(001-049)]

This sentence — "pauperism develops more rapidly than population and wealth" — contains a compressed but explosive claim: economic growth under capitalism does not reduce poverty; it accelerates its production. The expansion of wealth and the expansion of destitution are two faces of a single process. Marx and Engels are directly rebutting the Malthusian argument that poverty is a function of population outstripping resources; on the contrary, poverty increases *faster than* population, and does so precisely as wealth grows.

The Manifesto embeds this claim within its broader account of the proletariat's formation. The paragraph leading to the sentence on pauperism traces the worker's trajectory. It begins with the deskilling of the artisan:

> "The workman becomes an appendage of the machine, and it is only the most simple, most monotonous, and most easily acquired knack, that is required of him." [(001-029)]

This degradation of labour — the transformation of skilled craft into repetitive machine-tending — is the first stage in the worker's descent. The process then extends beyond the individual worker to the family:

> "The modern working class, developed — a class of labourers, who live only so long as they find work, and who find work only so long as their labour increases capital." [(001-028)]

The wage-labour relation, which formally presupposes a free contract between equals, terminates in the systematic production of unfreedom and destitution. The paragraph culminates in the declaration that the worker "becomes a pauper" — a being who has been stripped of every human attribute that could be sold on the market. Crucially, the Manifesto presents this trajectory not as the failure of capitalism but as its normal operation: pauperism is the destination toward which every proletarian tends, even if individual workers may temporarily escape it.

## Historical Context

The term "pauperism" was widely used in 19th-century social commentary and political economy to describe what contemporaries saw as a new and alarming phenomenon: the emergence of a permanent, hereditary class of the destitute in the midst of industrial prosperity. The Manifesto highlights this paradox through the general law of capitalist accumulation: "The modern labourer [...] instead of rising with the progress of industry, sinks deeper and deeper below the conditions of existence of his own class" [(001-049)]. The English Poor Law of 1834 — which replaced outdoor relief with the workhouse system — was a direct response to the perceived crisis of pauperism. Friedrich Engels had investigated the conditions of the English working class firsthand in Manchester (1842–1844), and his *The Condition of the Working Class in England* (1845) documented the degradation — physical, moral, and social — that industrial capitalism inflicted on the labouring population. The Manifesto's treatment of pauperism condenses these observations into a theoretical claim: the pauper is not a deviation from capitalist normality but its ultimate product.

## Related Concepts

- **[[proletariat]]**: The class of wage-labourers, of which the pauper represents the most degraded stratum — the worker who can no longer find a buyer for their labour-power.
- **[[over-production]]**: The crisis mechanism that periodically throws masses of workers out of employment, swelling the ranks of pauperism.
- **[[bourgeoisie]]**: The class whose accumulation of capital drives the countervailing accumulation of misery.
- **[[capital]]**: The self-expanding value whose concentration at one pole produces pauperisation at the other.
- **[[class-struggle]]**: The pauper's condition exposes the exploitative logic of the wage relation, stripping away the ideological veil of free contract.

## Significance

Pauperism is one of the Manifesto's most rhetorically powerful and theoretically consequential concepts. It provides the empirical foundation for the claim that the bourgeoisie is unfit to rule: a class that produces mass destitution in the midst of growing wealth has, by its own actions, demonstrated that its mode of production is barbaric. As Marx and Engels write of the proletarian's condition, "the cost of production of a workman is restricted, almost entirely, to the means of subsistence that he requires for his maintenance, and for the propagation of his race" [(001-029)] — a reduction of the human being to the bare biological minimum, beyond which lies only pauperism. Pauperism also clarifies the Manifesto's theory of immiseration — not the claim that workers get absolutely poorer in every period (wages can rise), but the structural claim that capitalism generates a permanent surplus population whose existence is a condition of the system's functioning. This concept would be developed at length in *Capital*, where Marx distinguishes between the "active," "reserve," and "stagnant" components of the industrial population, with pauperism constituting the "lowest sediment."
