## Definition

Utopian socialism is the term Marx and Engels used (partly as a tribute, partly as a critique) for the doctrines of the early nineteenth-century socialist thinkers — principally Henri de Saint-Simon, Charles Fourier, and Robert Owen — and their followers. These thinkers produced searching critiques of capitalist society, exposed the misery of the working class, and sketched visionary blueprints for a cooperative social order. But they did so before the proletariat had developed into an independent political force and before the class struggle had assumed its modern form. Their socialism was therefore "utopian" in the precise sense that it sought to transcend class antagonism through moral appeal and social experiment rather than through the political self-emancipation of the working class.

## In the Manifesto

The Manifesto devotes the third and final subsection of Ch. III to "Critical-Utopian Socialism and Communism." Marx and Engels open by situating these doctrines historically: "The first direct attempts of the proletariat to attain its own ends, made in times of universal excitement, when feudal society was being overthrown, these attempts necessarily failed, owing to the then undeveloped state of the proIetariat, as well as to the absence of the economic conditions for its emancipation" [(006-001)].

The founders — Saint-Simon, Fourier, Owen — are treated with notable respect. Their writings "contain also a critical element. They attack every principle of existing society. Hence they are full of the most valuable materials for the enlightenment of the working class" [(006-005)]. Their practical proposals — abolition of the antithesis between town and country, transformation of the family, conversion of the state into a mere administration of production — point toward "the disappearance of class antagonisms" [(006-005)].

Yet the judgement is ultimately severe. The utopian socialists "reject all political, and especially all revolutionary, action; they wish to attain their ends by peaceful means, and endeavour, by small experiments, necessarily doomed to failure, and by the force of example, to pave the way for the new social Gospel" [(006-004)]. Their significance bears "an inverse relation to historical development" — the more the modern class struggle develops, the more their doctrines lose "all practical value and all theoretical justification" [(006-006)]. Their followers, Marx and Engels charge, have degenerated into "mere reactionary sects" who "endeavour, and that consistently, to deaden the class struggle and to reconcile the class antagonisms" [(006-006)].

The section closes with a final image of the utopians sinking "into the category of the reactionary conservative Socialists depicted above, differing from these only by more systematic pedantry, and by their fanatical and superstitious belief in the miraculous effects of their social science" [(006-006)].

## Historical Context (1848)

By 1848, utopian socialism was already a receding force. Saint-Simon died in 1825, Fourier in 1837, and Owen's New Harmony experiment had collapsed in 1829. Their followers had fragmented into quarrelling sects. The 1830s and 1840s had seen the rise of new working-class movements — Chartism in Britain, the League of the Just in Germany, Blanquism in France — that embraced political struggle and, in varying degrees, revolutionary methods. The Manifesto's critique of utopian socialism was thus also a settling of accounts with Marx and Engels's own intellectual past, and a declaration of allegiance to the new, class-conscious proletarian movement.

## Related Concepts

Utopian socialism is contrasted with [[communism]] (or "scientific socialism," though this later term does not appear in the Manifesto) not by its ends — which largely overlapped — but by its means. The utopians sought to persuade all classes of the rationality of socialism; Communists hold that socialism will be achieved only through the political class struggle of the proletariat. The tension is dramatised in the Manifesto's treatment of [[class-struggle]]: the utopians perceive antagonism but wish to reconcile it; the Communists seek to intensify it to the point of revolutionary resolution.
