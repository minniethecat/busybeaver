The **French Legitimists** (*Légitimistes*) were the political supporters of the senior Bourbon line, deposed by the July Revolution of 1830, which replaced Charles X with the "bourgeois monarch" Louis-Philippe of the House of Orléans. In the Manifesto, Marx and Engels cite them — alongside [[young-england|Young England]] in England — as the French exemplar of Feudal Socialism: aristocrats who denounced the bourgeoisie not from the standpoint of the proletariat but from wounded aristocratic pride, deploying the language of anti-capitalist critique while in practice allying with the bourgeoisie against the working class whenever the latter threatened the existing order.

## In the Manifesto

### Ch. III §1a: Feudal Socialism's French Variant

The Legitimists occupy a symmetrical position in the Manifesto's analysis of Feudal Socialism. Where Young England represented the English variant — Tory aristocrats denouncing the Manchester manufacturers in the name of a romanticized medieval hierarchy — the Legitimists represented the French variant: the party of the old landed nobility, displaced from political power in 1830, who clothed their resentment of the Orléanist bourgeoisie in the language of social criticism. "In pointing out that their mode of exploitation was different to that of the bourgeoisie," Marx and Engels observe, "the feudalists forget that they exploited under circumstances and conditions that were quite different, and that are now antiquated" (004-005).

The Legitimists' anti-bourgeois polemics had a certain apparent radicalism. They denounced the July Monarchy as a regime of bankers and stock-jobbers; they decried the moral corruption of a society organized around the cash nexus; they lamented the destruction of traditional communities and the atomization of social life. But this radicalism was entirely superficial. What the Legitimists objected to was not exploitation or class domination as such, but the *form* these took under bourgeois rule: anonymous, impersonal, mediated by the market rather than sanctified by tradition and religion. Their "socialism" was the socialism of the master who misses his faithful servant.

The Manifesto draws the practical conclusion: "In political practice, therefore, they join in all coercive measures against the working class" (004-011). When the proletariat rose, the Legitimists' anti-bourgeois rhetoric evaporated, and they stood shoulder to shoulder with the bourgeoisie in defence of property and order. Feudal Socialism was, in the end, a theatre of aristocratic resentment — politically impotent except as an auxiliary force for the repression of the only class capable of genuinely overthrowing capitalist society.

## Historical Role

### From Restoration to Reaction

The Legitimists traced their political lineage to the Restoration monarchy (1814–1830), when Louis XVIII and Charles X ruled under a constitutional charter that attempted to reconcile the Bourbon restoration with the irreversible social changes wrought by the Revolution of 1789. The Restoration was a compromise: the aristocracy recovered its titles and much of its land, but the essential gains of the Revolution — the abolition of feudalism, civil equality before the law, the redistribution of Church lands — were preserved. The Legitimists were the party that found this compromise intolerable: they wanted full restoration of the ancien régime, including the return of confiscated lands to the nobility and the Church.

The July Revolution of 1830 — three days of street fighting that sent Charles X into exile and brought Louis-Philippe to power — was the Legitimists' political death sentence. The new regime was explicitly bourgeois: Louis-Philippe styled himself "King of the French" (not "King of France"), ruled under the tricolour (not the Bourbon white flag), and anchored his power in the upper bourgeoisie of finance and industry. The Legitimists retreated to their country estates, refused to swear allegiance to the new monarch, and cultivated an opposition of sullen, powerless resentment.

### The Politics of Nostalgia

The Legitimist critique of the July Monarchy had a superficial affinity with socialist criticism. Both denounced the domination of finance capital, the reduction of all social relations to money relations, the destruction of traditional solidarities by market competition. But the affinity was deceptive. The socialists criticized capitalism in the name of the future — a society of associated producers in which the free development of each would be the condition for the free development of all. The Legitimists criticized capitalism in the name of the past — a hierarchical society of orders and estates in which everyone knew their place and deference was a virtue. The difference was not merely one of political programme but of class standpoint: socialism spoke for the proletariat, the class without property whose emancipation required the abolition of class society; Legitimism spoke for the landed aristocracy, the class whose property was threatened by capitalist development and whose "critique" of the bourgeoisie was a strategy for defending its own privileges under new conditions.

## Marxist Assessment

The Manifesto's treatment of the Legitimists illustrates a general principle of Marxist political analysis: the class standpoint from which a critique of capitalism is made is the decisive determinant of its political meaning. Anti-capitalism is not inherently progressive. It can be the anti-capitalism of the aristocrat who misses serfdom, of the petty bourgeois who wants to turn back the clock to a world of small proprietors, or of the proletarian who seeks to abolish class society entirely. The Legitimists exemplify the first type — and the Manifesto's anatomy of their position serves as a warning that the rhetoric of social criticism can be appropriated by forces whose practical politics are the opposite of emancipation.

As the nineteenth century progressed, Legitimism merged with other reactionary currents into a generalized French conservatism. Its specifically anti-bourgeois edge dulled as the aristocracy's economic integration into capitalism deepened. But the phenomenon the Manifesto identified — the appropriation of anti-capitalist language by forces of the right — would recur throughout the history of capitalist society, from the "conservative revolutionaries" of Weimar Germany to the "anti-globalist" nationalists of the twenty-first century. The Legitimists were the first, but they were not the last.
