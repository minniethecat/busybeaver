Free trade, in the *Communist Manifesto*, is both an economic policy and an ideological slogan. It is the concrete commercial arrangement through which the bourgeoisie organises the world market, and it is the rhetorical figure through which bourgeois freedom more generally is legitimised. Marx and Engels treat free trade as the paradigmatic expression of the "single, unconscionable freedom — Free Trade" (002-025) that characterises bourgeois society.

## Free Trade as Bourgeois Freedom

The *Manifesto* identifies free trade as the concrete meaning of bourgeois "freedom" in general. "By freedom is meant, under the present bourgeois conditions of production, free trade, free selling and buying" (002-025). This identification is polemical: it reduces the abstract language of liberty to a specific commercial arrangement. When the bourgeoisie speaks of "freedom," it means the freedom to buy and sell without restriction — the freedom of capital, not the freedom of persons. The abolition of this commercial freedom — the restriction of "free selling and buying" — "would be the annihilation of the bourgeoisie, of private property, and of the means of subsistence of the bourgeois" (002-026).

## The Historical Function of Free Trade

Free trade serves a historically progressive function in the *Manifesto*'s analysis, despite its exploitative character. By tearing down national barriers, abolishing protective tariffs, and forcing all nations to adopt the bourgeois mode of production "on pain of extinction" (001-018), free trade accelerates the global unification of the bourgeoisie and the creation of a truly world-wide proletariat. It "compels all nations, on pain of extinction, to adopt the bourgeois mode of production; it compels them to introduce what it calls civilisation into their midst" (001-018). This process is violent and exploitative, but it creates the material conditions for proletarian internationalism.

## Free Trade and the Crises of Overproduction

Free trade is implicated in the periodic commercial crises that Marx and Engels describe. The unrestricted expansion of markets, when combined with the anarchic character of capitalist production, generates crises of overproduction — moments when "there is too much civilisation, too much means of subsistence, too much industry, too much commerce" (001-025). The bourgeoisie's response to these crises is "the conquest of new markets, and by the more thorough exploitation of the old ones" (001-025), which only extends the geographic reach of free trade and prepares the ground for deeper future crises.

> By freedom is meant, under the present bourgeois conditions of production, free trade, free selling and buying. (002-025)

## The Communist Position on Free Trade

The *Manifesto* does not advocate a return to protectionism or feudal restrictions on commerce — such measures would be reactionary. Instead, Communists accept free trade as a necessary stage in the development of the productive forces while preparing for its supersession. The abolition of bourgeois property will abolish the conditions under which "free trade" means the freedom of capital to exploit labour, replacing it with a genuinely free association of producers.
