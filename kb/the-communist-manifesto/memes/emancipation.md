**Emancipation**, in the Manifesto's programme, is the liberation of the proletariat from the condition of wage-slavery and, through that liberation, the emancipation of humanity from all forms of class domination. Unlike the bourgeois revolutions that emancipated one class at the expense of another, proletarian emancipation "will cause them to vanish still faster" [(002-054)] — the abolition of the proletariat as a class through the abolition of class society itself.

## Definition

Marx and Engels distinguish between two levels of emancipation. **Political emancipation** is the achievement of formal legal equality, civil rights, and representative government — the programme of the bourgeois revolution. This is real progress, but it is incomplete: it leaves intact the material relations of exploitation that make a mockery of formal equality.

**Human emancipation** is the abolition of the conditions that make emancipation necessary in the first place. It requires not the reform of existing property relations but their revolutionary overthrow. "The first step in the revolution by the working class is to raise the proletariat to the position of ruling class" [(002-068)]. From that position, the proletariat uses its political supremacy "to wrest, by degrees, all capital from the bourgeoisie" [(002-068)], until class distinctions have disappeared and "the public power will lose its political character" [(002-082)].

The Manifesto's conception of emancipation is thus dialectical: the proletariat must first become the ruling class in order to abolish itself as a class. This is not a paradox but a recognition that the conditions of emancipation cannot be created by moral appeal or gradual reform — they must be won through the conquest of political power.

## In the Manifesto

### The Conditions of Emancipation

The Manifesto insists that emancipation is not a matter of will or moral conviction. It requires material conditions — the development of the productive forces, the concentration of the proletariat, the simplification of class antagonisms — that are themselves the product of bourgeois society. "The first direct attempts of the proletariat to attain its own ends... necessarily failed, owing to the then undeveloped state of the proletariat, as well as to the absence of the economic conditions for its emancipation, conditions that had yet to be produced, and could be produced by the impending bourgeois epoch alone" [(006-002)].

This is the Manifesto's critique of utopian socialism. The utopians — Fourier, Owen, Saint-Simon — correctly identified the misery of the working class and the contradictions of bourgeois society. But "the economic situation, as they find it, does not as yet offer to them the material conditions for the emancipation of the proletariat" [(006-005)]. They substitute "the historically created conditions of emancipation to fantastic ones" [(006-006)] — blueprints for ideal communities, appeals to the conscience of the rich — because they cannot yet see the proletariat as a revolutionary force capable of achieving its own emancipation.

### Emancipation and the Proletariat's Mission

The Manifesto assigns the proletariat a unique historical mission. Unlike all preceding classes, the proletariat cannot emancipate itself without emancipating humanity as a whole. "The proletarians have nothing to lose but their chains. They have a world to win" (P01-005). The specificity of the proletariat's condition — propertyless, concentrated in factories, disciplined by collective labour — makes it the first class whose self-emancipation coincides with the abolition of all class domination.

This "mission" is not a moral imperative but a structural necessity. The proletariat is "the special and essential product" of bourgeois society [(001-028)]; its emancipation is simultaneously the destruction of the conditions that produce it. "The proletariat alone is a really revolutionary class" [(001-028)] precisely because it has no stake in the existing order — no property to defend, no privilege to preserve.

### National Emancipation and Internationalism

The Manifesto addresses the question of national emancipation in its closing section. "In Poland the Communists support the party that insists on an agrarian revolution as the prime condition for national emancipation" [(007-004)]. This is a strategic support for national liberation movements — but always with the understanding that national emancipation is a stage on the road to international emancipation. "Working men have no country" [(002-053)]; the proletariat's struggle is global in scope.

## Historical Context

The concept of emancipation had deep roots in the Enlightenment and the French Revolution, where it was understood primarily as emancipation from feudal bonds, religious authority, and arbitrary government. The Manifesto radicalises this tradition by revealing its class content: the emancipation achieved by the bourgeois revolution was the emancipation of the bourgeoisie — the freedom to trade, to own property, to exploit labour.

The 1840s also saw the emergence of working-class movements that demanded emancipation in a more radical sense: the Chartists in England, the revolutionary workers of Paris, the communist leagues in Germany. The Manifesto was written for and addressed to these movements, providing them with a theory of why mere political reform could never achieve human emancipation and why the conquest of political power by the proletariat was necessary.
