# Industrial Revolution

## Overview

The Industrial Revolution was the epochal transformation of production from manual, artisanal methods to machine-based, factory-organized manufacturing, beginning in Great Britain in the late eighteenth century and spreading across the European continent and North America through the nineteenth century. Fueled by steam power, mechanization (particularly in textiles and metallurgy), and the factory system, the Industrial Revolution generated an unprecedented expansion of productive capacity. It simultaneously created the two great classes of modern capitalism: the industrial [[bourgeoisie]], who owned the new means of production, and the industrial [[proletariat]], who owned nothing but their labour-power and were compelled to sell it for wages. The Industrial Revolution thus forms the material foundation of the entire analysis presented in the *Communist Manifesto*.

## In the Manifesto

The Industrial Revolution is the unnamed but omnipresent historical force that structures the Manifesto's entire opening section, "Bourgeois and Proletarians." Marx and Engels describe how *"steam and machinery revolutionised industrial production. The place of manufacture was taken by the giant, Modern Industry, the place of the industrial middle class, by industrial millionaires, the leaders of whole industrial armies, the modern bourgeois"* (001-008). This passage condenses the core transformation: the technical revolution in production simultaneously produced a new ruling class and a new form of class power.

The Manifesto characterizes the bourgeois epoch as defined by constant productive transformation: *"The bourgeoisie cannot exist without constantly revolutionising the instruments of production, and thereby the relations of production, and with them the whole relations of society"* (001-017). This is a direct theorization of the Industrial Revolution's ongoing, disruptive logic — the permanent technological and social upheaval that distinguishes capitalism from all previous [[mode-of-production|modes of production]].

The industrial transformation also produces its own gravedigger: the modern proletariat. Marx and Engels describe how industrial labour degrades the worker: *"Owing to the extensive use of machinery and to division of labour, the work of the proletarians has lost all individual character, and consequently, all charm for the workman. He becomes an appendage of the machine"* (001-021). The factory system concentrates and disciplines workers, creating — unintentionally from the bourgeoisie's standpoint — the collective subject capable of revolutionary [[class-struggle|class action]].

The Manifesto's ten-point programme in Section II explicitly references industrial organization: point 8 demands *"Establishment of industrial armies, especially for agriculture"* (002-079), and point 10 calls for *"Combination of education with industrial production, &c., &c."* (002-081). These demands reflect the recognition that industrial [[productive-forces]] , once freed from bourgeois [[private-property|property relations]], could be reorganized for human benefit.

## Historical Background

The Industrial Revolution's technical innovations — the steam engine (James Watt, 1769), the spinning jenny (James Hargreaves, 1764), the power loom (Edmund Cartwright, 1785) — transformed textile production from a cottage industry into a factory-based system. Iron production, coal mining, and railway construction created the infrastructure of industrial capitalism. By the 1840s, when [[karl-marx|Marx]] and [[friedrich-engels|Engels]] wrote the Manifesto, Britain had been transformed: the population was increasingly urban, the factory system was entrenched, and the social catastrophes of industrialization — mass poverty, child labour, cyclical unemployment, urban squalor — were visible to all observers. Engels had documented these conditions firsthand in *The Condition of the Working Class in England* (1845).

## Marxist Analysis

Marx and Engels' theoretical contribution was to recognize the Industrial Revolution not as a merely technical event but as a revolution in social relations. The introduction of [[machinery]] did not simply increase productivity; it transformed the relationship between capital and labour, concentrating the [[means-of-production]] in fewer hands, deskilling the craft worker, making women and children into wage-labourers, and creating the material conditions for collective working-class organization. The Manifesto's claim that the bourgeoisie "creates a world after its own image" (001-019) captures the totalizing character of the Industrial Revolution: it is not only an economic transformation but a social, cultural, and political one that remakes every existing institution — family, nation, law, religion, art — on bourgeois foundations. At the same time, the very dynamism that gives the bourgeoisie its revolutionary power — the ceaseless expansion of productive forces — produces periodic [[crisis|crises of overproduction]] that expose the irrationality of private appropriation of social production.

## Aftermath and Legacy

The Industrial Revolution inaugurated the capitalist era and set the stage for all subsequent working-class movements. The concentration of workers in factories and industrial cities made possible the trade union movement, [[chartists|Chartism]], and the revolutionary organizations of 1848. The Manifesto itself is a product of this context: a theoretical synthesis that could only arise once industrial capitalism had developed sufficiently to reveal its essential dynamics. Marx and Engels' analysis of the Industrial Revolution — its creation of the [[proletariat]] as a class, its incessant revolutionizing of production, its generation of periodic crises — remains foundational to all subsequent Marxist political economy, from Marx's *Capital* through contemporary analyses of automation, globalization, and the digital economy.
