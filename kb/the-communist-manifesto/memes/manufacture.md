The Manifesto's account of economic history proceeds through three distinct stages of production. Feudal industry was "monopolised by closed [[guild|guilds]]" [(001-007)]. This system "no longer sufficed for the growing wants of the new markets. The manufacturing system took its place" [(001-007)]. But "even manufacture no longer sufficed. Thereupon, steam and machinery revolutionised industrial production" [(001-008)]. **Manufacture** thus occupies a pivotal transitional position — it is the form of production that breaks the guild monopoly, concentrates labour under a single roof, and refines the [[division-of-labour|division of labour]] to the point where [[machinery]] becomes possible and necessary.

## Definition

Manufacture, in the Manifesto's historical schema, designates the system of workshop production that prevailed in Europe from roughly the sixteenth to the late eighteenth centuries. Unlike the guild system, which was governed by customary rules, restricted entry, and maintained quality through apprenticeship, manufacture was organised around the capitalist who owned the raw materials and the workshop, and who employed wage-labourers performing specialised tasks. "The guild-master was pushed on one side by the manufacturing middle class; division of labour between the different corporate guilds vanished in the face of division of labour in each single workshop" [(001-011)].

The "manufacturer" in this sense is not the factory owner of the nineteenth century but the workshop master of the transitional period. The Manifesto distinguishes between the "individual bourgeois manufacturer" [(001-030)] of the manufacturing period and the industrial capitalist who deploys [[machinery|machinery]] on a large scale.

## In the Manifesto

### Manufacture as a Historical Stage

The Manifesto's Section I traces the dialectical progression from feudalism through manufacture to modern industry. The discovery of America and the opening of colonial markets "gave to commerce, to navigation, to industry, an impulse never before known, and thereby, to the revolutionary element in the tottering feudal society, a rapid development" [(001-006)]. This impulse first expressed itself in the expansion of manufacture, which could meet the rising demand in a way that guild production could not.

> The feudal system of industry, under which industrial production was monopolised by closed guilds, now no longer sufficed for the growing wants of the new markets. The manufacturing system took its place. [(001-007)]

"Meantime the markets kept ever growing, the demand ever rising. Even manufacture no longer sufficed" [(001-008)]. The limitations of manufacture — its dependence on skilled hand labour, its restricted scale, its inability to fully subordinate the worker to capital — drove the transition to machinery. The steam engine and the power loom accomplished what the workshop could not: the complete transformation of the labour process.

> Meantime the markets kept ever growing, the demand ever rising. Even manufacture no longer sufficed. Thereupon, steam and machinery revolutionised industrial production. The place of manufacture was taken by the giant, Modern Industry, the place of the industrial middle class, by industrial millionaires, the leaders of whole industrial armies, the modern bourgeois. [(001-008)]

### Manufacture and Division of Labour

Under manufacture, the division of labour was refined to an unprecedented degree. Where the guild system divided labour between trades (carpenters, weavers, smiths), manufacture divided labour within the production of a single commodity. Each worker performed a specialised operation; the commodity was the product not of an individual craftsman but of the collective worker.

> The guild-masters were pushed on one side by the manufacturing middle class; division of labour between the different corporate guilds vanished in the face of division of labour in each single workshop. [(001-011)]

This refinement of the division of labour prepared the ground for [[machinery|machinery]] by decomposing complex tasks into simple, repeatable motions — motions that could eventually be mechanised. "Division of labour... vanished in the face of division of labour in each single workshop" [(001-011)]: the transition was from social division of labour to technical division of labour, from the differentiation of trades to the decomposition of tasks.

### The Fate of the Manufacturing Middle Class

The Manifesto notes the progressive elimination of intermediate strata — including those associated with manufacture. "The lower middle class, the small manufacturer, the shopkeeper, the artisan, the peasant, all these fight against the bourgeoisie, to save from extinction their existence as fractions of the middle class" [(001-042)]. The "small manufacturer" — the workshop master who does not yet deploy machinery — is squeezed between the industrial capitalist above and the [[proletariat|proletariat]] below. He is destined, in the Manifesto's analysis, to be either proletarianised or destroyed.

The intermediate position of the manufacturer, neither feudal [[guild-master]] nor modern industrialist, made this class particularly vulnerable. While the guild-master owned his tools and controlled production from start to finish, and the industrial capitalist commanded armies of workers through [[machinery]], the manufacturer occupied a precarious middle ground — reliant on skilled hand labour yet already subordinated to the logic of capital accumulation.

> No sooner is the exploitation of the labourer by the manufacturer, so far at an end, that he receives his wages in cash, than he is set upon by the other portions of the bourgeoisie, the landlord, the shopkeeper, the pawnbroker, etc. [(001-030)]

### In Feudal Socialism's Programme

The reactionary feudal socialism criticised in Section III of the Manifesto looked back to pre-industrial forms of production, proposing "corporate guilds for manufacture, patriarchal relations in agriculture" [(004-019)] as an alternative to bourgeois society. Marx and Engels treat this programme as a nostalgic fantasy — an attempt to restore social forms that the development of productive forces has rendered obsolete.

> Its last words are: corporate guilds for manufacture, patriarchal relations in agriculture. [(004-019)]

This programme, the Manifesto argues, is not merely impractical but reactionary — it seeks to reverse the historical movement that has already swept away the guild system, manufacture, and every prior form of industrial organisation. The manufacturing system itself, which feudal socialism treats as a recovery target, was already obsolete by 1848.

## Manufacture and the Concentration of Labour

### The Workshop as Proto-Factory

The manufacturing workshop was a transitional form between the dispersed domestic industry of the guild era and the concentrated factory of the [[industrial-revolution|industrial era]]. For the first time in European economic history, large numbers of wage-labourers worked under a single roof, performing coordinated tasks under the direction of a capitalist employer.

This concentration had political as well as economic consequences. By bringing workers together in one workplace, manufacture created the material conditions for collective consciousness and organisation. The [[proletariat|proletariat]] began to form itself as a class not in the factory alone but in the manufacturing workshop, where workers first experienced their common condition as wage-labourers.

> The bourgeoisie, wherever it has got the upper hand, has put an end to all feudal, patriarchal, idyllic relations. It has pitilessly torn asunder the motley feudal ties that bound man to his "natural superiors," and has left remaining no other nexus between man and man than naked self-interest, than callous "cash payment." [(001-013)]

### Manufacture and the World Market

The expansion of manufacture was inseparable from the growth of global trade. The discovery of America and the sea route to the East Indies created markets that the feudal guild system could not satisfy. Manufacture emerged as the productive form capable of meeting this demand, and in doing so it tied local production to international markets.

This connection to the world market had transformative effects. Manufacture drew raw materials from distant colonies, sold finished goods across continents, and exposed European economies to competitive pressures previously unknown. The logic of expanding production to meet expanding markets — a logic that would later drive the [[industrial-revolution|industrial revolution]] itself — first manifested in the manufacturing period.

> The discovery of America, the rounding of the Cape, opened up fresh ground for the rising bourgeoisie. The East-Indian and Chinese markets, the colonisation of America, trade with the colonies, the increase in the means of exchange and in commodities generally, gave to commerce, to navigation, to industry, an impulse never before known, and thereby, to the revolutionary element in the tottering feudal society, a rapid development. [(001-006)]

### The Transition to Modern Industry

The very success of manufacture sowed the seeds of its own destruction. As markets continued to expand, the limitations of hand production became increasingly evident. Skilled labour was expensive and scarce; quality control across distributed workshops was difficult; and the pace of production remained bounded by human endurance.

> Modern industry has established the world-market, for which the discovery of America paved the way. This market has given an immense development to commerce, to navigation, to communication by land. [(001-009)]

The solution was technological: steam power and mechanisation. The [[machinery|machine]] replaced the skilled hand; the factory replaced the workshop; the industrial capitalist replaced the manufacturer. But this transition was not merely technological — it was a revolution in social relations, completing the subordination of labour to capital that manufacture had only begun.

## Historical Context

The manufacturing system reached its apogee in eighteenth-century England, in industries such as woollen textiles, metalwork, and pottery. The division of labour in manufacture was famously analysed by Adam Smith in *The Wealth of Nations* (1776), whose pin-factory example demonstrated the enormous productivity gains that specialisation could achieve. Marx and Engels absorbed Smith's account but historicised it: manufacture was not the natural form of industrial organisation but a historically specific stage that contained within itself the seeds of its own supersession.

The manufacturing period also coincided with, and was propelled by, the expansion of European [[colonialism]]. The Atlantic slave trade, the plantation economies of the Americas, and the extraction of wealth from Asia provided both the raw materials and the market demand that fuelled the growth of European manufacture. The [[commerce|commercial]] revolution of the early modern period created the conditions under which the manufacturing system could flourish, and the manufacturing system in turn created the conditions under which modern [[industry]] could emerge.
