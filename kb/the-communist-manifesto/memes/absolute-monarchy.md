# Absolute Monarchy

## Definition

Absolute monarchy, in the Manifesto's usage, designates the centralized monarchical state that emerged from the dissolution of medieval feudalism. It was the political form in which the feudal nobility exercised power during the transition from feudal to capitalist society — a state characterized by the concentration of political authority in the monarch, supported by a standing army, a centralized bureaucracy, and a system of taxation. Marx and Engels treat absolute monarchy as the last political form of feudal rule before the bourgeoisie seized state power for itself.

## In the Manifesto

The Manifesto describes the rise of the bourgeoisie as a political struggle against the institutions of feudal power, of which absolute monarchy was the apex. In the early phases of bourgeois development, the bourgeoisie existed as an *"armed and self-governing association in the medieval commune"* and later as the *"Third Estate"* in monarchies — *"a {class} an oppressed under the sway of the feudal nobility"* (001-011). The bourgeoisie *"knew that the {regime} ... used by the feudal nobility"* had to be dismantled for capitalist relations of production to flourish.

The Manifesto presents absolute monarchy as one pole in a political spectrum that the bourgeoisie systematically eliminated. Marx and Engels note that in Germany, the bourgeoisie *"fought ... against the feudal aristocracy and absolute monarchy, in other words, the liberal bourgeoisie"* (004-030) — positioning the German bourgeoisie's struggle against absolutism as part of a broader European pattern, even if the German variant arrived belatedly and therefore timidly. The German bourgeoisie, coming too late to play the revolutionary role its French counterpart had played, could only mount a half-hearted challenge to the absolutist state.

The final mention of absolute monarchy appears in the Manifesto's explicit political program. In Section IV, Marx and Engels note that in Germany the party *"fights ... against the absolute monarchy, the feudal squirearchy, and the petty-bourgeoisie"* (007-005). Here absolute monarchy is listed alongside other forces that the Communist party must oppose — but crucially, it is identified as a feudal remnant, distinct from the bourgeoisie itself. The Communists support the bourgeoisie where it still struggles against absolutism (as in Germany), while simultaneously preparing the proletariat for the struggle against the bourgeoisie itself.


> The fight of the German, and especially, of the Prussian bourgeoisie, against feudal aristocracy and absolute monarchy, in other words, the liberal movement, became more earnest.
>
> (004-030)

## Historical Context

Absolute monarchy emerged in Europe between the sixteenth and eighteenth centuries as feudal lordship gave way to centralized state power. In France, the model case, the Bourbon monarchy under Louis XIV represented the classic form of absolutism — *l'etat, c'est moi*. The French Revolution of 1789 overthrew this absolutist state, establishing the political conditions for capitalist development. In Germany, however, absolutism persisted well into the nineteenth century; the Prussian monarchy remained semi-feudal, and the German bourgeoisie of 1848 was too weak and late-arriving to accomplish what the French bourgeoisie had achieved in 1789. Marx and Engels' nuanced position — supporting the bourgeoisie against absolutism while opposing the bourgeoisie itself — reflects the specific political conjuncture of the 1848 revolutions in Germany.

## Related Concepts

Absolute monarchy is the political form corresponding to the later stages of [[feudalism]], and its overthrow by the [[bourgeoisie]] is a key moment in the transition from feudal to capitalist society. The [[aristocracy]] was the ruling class under absolutism, and the concept is closely tied to the broader Marxist theory of the [[state]] as an instrument of class rule. The struggle against absolute monarchy provides the historical context for understanding [[revolution]] as a political process through which one class displaces another from state power. In the Manifesto's political calculus, the attitude toward absolute monarchy distinguishes between countries where the bourgeois revolution has already occurred (France, England) and those where it has not (Germany).

## Significance

The Manifesto's treatment of absolute monarchy illustrates Marx and Engels' political realism. They do not treat all forms of the state as equally reactionary in every historical moment. Where the bourgeoisie is still struggling against feudal-absolutist forms, Communists support it — not because they share its aims, but because the defeat of absolutism clears the ground for the subsequent struggle between bourgeoisie and proletariat. This strategic flexibility, grounded in historical analysis rather than abstract principle, became a defining feature of Marxist political practice. The concept of absolute monarchy thus serves as a case study in how Marxist politics navigates the uneven development of capitalism across different national contexts.
