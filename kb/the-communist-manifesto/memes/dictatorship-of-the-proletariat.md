# Dictatorship of the Proletariat

## Definition

The dictatorship of the proletariat is the political form of the transition period between capitalist and communist society, in which the working class, having overthrown the [[bourgeoisie]], exercises political power to suppress the resistance of the former exploiting classes and to reorganise production on a social basis. The term, most fully elaborated in Marx's *Critique of the Gotha Programme* (1875), designates not the absence of democracy but its radical extension: for the first time in history, the vast majority exercises political power over a dispossessed minority. The Manifesto does not use the phrase itself, but it supplies every essential theoretical element from which the concept was subsequently developed.

## In the Manifesto

### The Conquest of Political Power

The Manifesto defines the immediate aim of the Communists in explicitly political terms: "formation of the [[proletariat|proletariat]] into a class, overthrow of the bourgeois supremacy, conquest of political power by the proletariat" (002-007). This conquest is not an end in itself but the precondition for the revolutionary transformation of society. The proletariat, organised as a class and then as a political party, must first seize the existing state apparatus before it can begin to dismantle it and erect new institutions of working-class rule. The Communists "always and everywhere represent the interests of the movement as a whole" (002-005) and possess "the advantage of clearly understanding the line of march, the conditions, and the ultimate general results of the proletarian movement" (002-006).

### The State as Class Power

The Manifesto's analysis of the [[state]] provides the theoretical foundation. In its most cited political formulation, Marx and Engels declare that "the executive of the modern State is but a committee for managing the common affairs of the whole bourgeoisie" (001-011). If the existing state is an instrument of bourgeois class rule, then the working class cannot simply take possession of it — it must transform the character of the state itself.

> When, in the course of development, class distinctions have disappeared, and all production has been concentrated in the hands of a vast association of the whole nation, the public power will lose its political character. Political power, properly so called, is merely the organised power of one class for oppressing another. If the proletariat during its contest with the bourgeoisie is compelled, by the force of circumstances, to organise itself as a class, if, by means of a revolution, it makes itself the ruling class, and, as such, sweeps away by force the old conditions of production, then it will, along with these conditions, have swept away the conditions for the existence of class antagonisms and of classes generally, and will thereby have abolished its own supremacy as a class. (002-082)

This passage contains the dialectical core of the concept: the dictatorship of the proletariat is the first form of state power in history that aims at its own dissolution. By abolishing the material conditions of class society, the proletariat simultaneously abolishes the conditions of its own existence as a class and, with them, the very need for political power.

### Every Class Struggle Is a Political Struggle

The assertion that "every class struggle is a political struggle" (001-037) anchors the Manifesto's political logic. The organisation of the proletariat "into a class, and consequently into a political party, is continually being upset again by the competition between the workers themselves. But it ever rises up again, stronger, firmer, mightier" (001-038). Economic conflicts over wages and working conditions inevitably develop into political conflicts, because they challenge the legal and institutional framework that guarantees bourgeois property. The dictatorship of the proletariat is the terminus of this process: the point at which class struggle, having become conscious and organised, takes political form as the rule of the revolutionary class.

### The Revolutionary Proletariat

The Manifesto insists that "of all the classes that stand face to face with the bourgeoisie today, the proletariat alone is a really revolutionary class" (001-042). The intermediate strata — the [[petty-bourgeoisie]], the peasantry — fight the bourgeoisie only to preserve their own existence as fractions of the middle class; they are "not revolutionary, but conservative. Nay more, they are reactionary, for they try to roll back the wheel of history" (001-042). Only the proletariat, as the "special and essential product" of modern industry (001-042), has no stake in the existing order and can carry the revolution to its conclusion. The dictatorship of the proletariat is the political expression of this unique historical position: the class whose particular interests coincide with the universal interest in the abolition of class society. In the Manifesto's closing vision, "in place of the old bourgeois society, with its classes and class antagonisms, we shall have an association, in which the free development of each is the condition for the free development of all" (002-083).

## Historical Context

### From Manifesto to Mature Formulation

The concept of the dictatorship of the proletariat underwent significant development after 1848. Marx first used the term in *The Class Struggles in France* (1850), counterposing the "dictatorship of the working class" to the "bourgeois dictatorship" of the provisional government. The fullest elaboration came in the *Critique of the Gotha Programme* (1875): "Between capitalist and communist society lies the period of the revolutionary transformation of the one into the other. Corresponding to this is also a political transition period in which the state can be nothing but *the revolutionary dictatorship of the proletariat*."

The historical experience that shaped this development included the Paris Commune of 1871, which Marx hailed as the political form at last discovered for the emancipation of labour. The Commune — with its elected and recallable officials, its abolition of the standing army, and its merger of legislative and executive functions — provided a concrete model of proletarian political power qualitatively different from bourgeois parliamentarism.

### Debates Within Marxism

The concept became one of the most contested terms in the Marxist tradition. In the Second International, Karl Kautsky interpreted it as a description of the class character of a democratic republic based on universal suffrage — a formulation Lenin attacked in *The State and Revolution* (1917) for evading the question of smashing the existing state apparatus. Lenin insisted the dictatorship of the proletariat meant a state of the Paris Commune type: the armed workers, direct democracy combined with the suppression of the exploiting classes. The 1917 [[french-revolution|revolutionary]] experience and subsequent Soviet state — whether it represented the dictatorship of the proletariat or the dictatorship of the party *over* the proletariat — became the central question dividing the Marxist left in the twentieth century.

## Related Concepts

The dictatorship of the proletariat is inseparable from the theory of [[class-struggle]], which provides its dynamic logic: the antagonism between [[bourgeoisie]] and [[proletariat]] drives toward a revolutionary confrontation in which the proletariat conquers political power. The concept presupposes the Marxist theory of the [[state]] as an instrument of class rule and the theory of [[revolution]] as the means by which one class displaces another from power. The immediate aim — "conquest of political power by the proletariat" (002-007) — connects the dictatorship of the proletariat to the broader programme of [[communism]], whose ultimate goal is the abolition of class distinctions and the withering away of the state itself (002-082). The [[communist-league]], which commissioned the Manifesto, was itself the organisational embodiment of the principle that the Communists form the most advanced and resolute section of the working-class movement.

## Significance

The dictatorship of the proletariat represents the specifically Marxist answer to the question of political transition: how does a revolutionary class exercise power between the overthrow of the old order and the establishment of the new? It distinguishes Marxism from anarchism, which rejects all forms of state power, and from reformism, which seeks to transform capitalism through the existing state apparatus. For Marx and Engels, the transitional character of proletarian rule — exercised by the vast majority, dismantling the conditions of its own existence from the moment of its establishment — is what distinguishes it from all previous forms of state power. Whether the historical experiments that claimed this name fulfilled this vision remains one of the most bitterly contested questions in the history of socialist politics.
