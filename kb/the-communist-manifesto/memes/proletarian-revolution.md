The **proletarian revolution** is the Manifesto's climactic political concept — the event toward which the entire historical analysis of Sections I and II is directed, and which Section IV situates as the immediate strategic horizon. "The Communists disdain to conceal their views and aims. They openly declare that their ends can be attained only by the forcible overthrow of all existing social conditions. Let the ruling classes tremble at a Communistic revolution" [(007-011)].

## Definition

A proletarian revolution differs from all previous revolutions — bourgeois, feudal, or otherwise — in two fundamental respects. First, all previous revolutions were "movements of minorities, or in the interests of minorities" [(001-046)]. The proletarian revolution is "the self-conscious, independent movement of the immense majority, in the interests of the immense majority" [(001-046)]. Second, whereas previous revolutions replaced one form of class rule with another, the proletarian revolution aims at the abolition of class rule altogether. "If the proletariat during its contest with the bourgeoisie is compelled, by the force of circumstances, to organise itself as a class, if, by means of a revolution, it makes itself the ruling class, and, as such, sweeps away by force the old conditions of production, then it will, along with these conditions, have swept away the conditions for the existence of class antagonisms and of classes generally" [(002-082)].

The revolution is thus a self-abolishing act. The proletariat seizes power not to perpetuate its own rule but to create the conditions in which rule — all rule — becomes unnecessary.

## In the Manifesto

### The Precondition: Class Consciousness

The Manifesto traces the formation of the proletariat as a revolutionary subject through a series of stages. Workers begin as a "scattered" mass, competing with one another [(001-028)]. They are united, at first, by the bourgeoisie itself — drawn into political struggles against the aristocracy and against foreign competitors. Through these struggles, the proletariat acquires "its own instruments of political and general education" — the bourgeoisie "furnishes the proletariat with weapons for fighting the bourgeoisie" [(001-039)].

The transition from scattered mass to revolutionary class is facilitated by two additional processes. First, sections of the ruling class are "precipitated into the proletariat" by the advance of industry, supplying "fresh elements of enlightenment and progress" [(001-040)]. Second, as the class struggle nears its decisive hour, "a small section of the ruling class cuts itself adrift, and joins the revolutionary class" — "a portion of the bourgeois ideologists, who have raised themselves to the level of comprehending theoretically the historical movement as a whole" [(001-041)].

### The Act: Proletariat as Ruling Class

The first step of the revolution is political: "to raise the proletariat to the position of ruling class, to win the battle of democracy" [(002-068)]. This formulation is deliberately ambiguous between parliamentary and extra-parliamentary paths — the Manifesto does not rule out democratic means but consistently treats the state as an instrument of class rule that will not surrender power voluntarily.

Once in power, the proletariat uses its political supremacy "to wrest, by degrees, all capital from the bourgeoisie, to centralise all instruments of production in the hands of the State, i.e., of the proletariat organised as the ruling class" [(002-068)]. The Manifesto then lists ten specific measures — from the abolition of property in land to free public education — as the transitional programme of the revolutionary government.

### The Strategy: Germany as the Decisive Theatre

Section IV of the Manifesto identifies Germany as the country "on the eve of a bourgeois revolution" that will, because of Germany's more advanced proletariat, be "but the prelude to an immediately following proletarian revolution" [(007-007)]. This "permanent revolution" thesis — that the bourgeois revolution must be pushed through to its proletarian conclusion without pause — would become a central strategic concept in subsequent Marxist politics, from 1848 through to the Russian Revolutions of 1917.

The Manifesto ends with a rhetorical crescendo that fuses the inevitability thesis with the call to action: "The proletarians have nothing to lose but their chains. They have a world to win" [(007-011)].

### The "Dangerous Class"

The Manifesto distinguishes carefully between the proletariat — the organised, productive working class — and the "dangerous class," "the social scum, that passively rotting mass thrown off by the lowest layers of old society" [(001-043)]. This lumpenproletariat — the "dangerous class" "may, here and there, be swept into the movement by a proletarian revolution; its conditions of life, however, prepare it far more for the part of a bribed tool of reactionary intrigue" [(001-043)]. The distinction is strategic: a successful revolution requires the organised [[proletariat]], not the desperate and easily manipulated underclass.
