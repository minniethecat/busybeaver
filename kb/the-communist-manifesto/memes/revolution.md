> All fixed, fast-frozen relations, with their train of ancient and venerable prejudices and opinions, are swept away, all new-formed ones become antiquated before they can ossify. All that is solid melts into air, all that is holy is profaned, and man is at last compelled to face with sober senses, his real conditions of life, and his relations with his kind. [(001-024)]

**Revolution** is the central dynamic principle of the *Manifesto of the Communist Party*. For [[karl-marx|Marx]] and [[friedrich-engels|Engels]], revolution is not a contingent political event but the necessary form through which the contradictions of each mode of production are resolved — and through which one mode of production gives way to another. The Manifesto theorises revolution at three distinct levels: as the universal mechanism of historical change (the "revolutionary re-constitution of society"), as the specific achievement of the [[bourgeoisie]] (the bourgeois revolution that swept away feudalism), and as the historical mission of the [[proletariat]] (the communist revolution that will abolish [[class-struggle|class]] society as such).

## Definition

In the Manifesto's materialist framework, revolution is the political expression of a structural contradiction: the conflict between the forces and relations of production. "At a certain stage in the development of these means of production and of exchange," the existing property relations become "so many fetters" on the further development of the productive forces — and "then begins an epoch of social revolution" (Marx, *Preface to A Contribution to the Critique of Political Economy*, 1859). The Manifesto presents this dynamic in narrative form: history is "an uninterrupted, now hidden, now open fight" between oppressor and oppressed, "a fight that each time ended, either in a revolutionary re-constitution of society at large, or in the common ruin of the contending classes" [(001-003)].

The specifically **communist revolution** is distinguished from all previous revolutions by its aim: not the transfer of political power from one exploiting class to another, but the abolition of class rule as such. "All previous historical movements were movements of minorities, or in the interest of minorities. The proletarian movement is the self-conscious, independent movement of the immense majority, in the interest of the immense majority" [(001-039)].

> The history of all hitherto existing societies is the history of class struggles. Freeman and slave, patrician and plebeian, lord and serf, guild-master and journeyman, in a word, oppressor and oppressed, stood in constant opposition to one another, carried on an uninterrupted, now hidden, now open fight, a fight that each time ended, either in a revolutionary re-constitution of society at large, or in the common ruin of the contending classes. [(001-002)]

> The immediate aim of the Communist is the same as that of all the other proletarian parties: formation of the proletariat into a class, overthrow of the bourgeois supremacy, conquest of political power by the proletariat. [(002-007)]

## In the Manifesto

### The Bourgeois Revolution

Before theorising proletarian revolution, the Manifesto offers a sustained analysis of the **bourgeois revolution** — the historical process by which the bourgeoisie overthrew feudalism and established capitalist society. "The bourgeoisie, historically, has played a most revolutionary part" [(001-018)]. This revolution operated on multiple fronts simultaneously:

**Economic revolution.** "The bourgeoisie cannot exist without constantly revolutionising the instruments of production, and thereby the relations of production, and with them the whole relations of society" [(001-024)]. Steam, machinery, and the factory system did not merely increase productivity — they dissolved the entire social world of feudalism, from guild regulations to patriarchal relations.

**Political revolution.** "Each step in the development of the bourgeoisie was accompanied by a corresponding political advance. An estate under the sway of the feudal lords, an armed and self-governing association in the medieval commune... the bourgeoisie has at last, since the establishment of Modern Industry and of the world market, conquered for itself, in the modern representative State, exclusive political sway" [(001-013)].

**Ideological revolution.** Bourgeois society dissolved every traditional bond — religious, familial, patriarchal — replacing them with "the single unconscionable freedom — Free Trade" [(001-027)] and reducing all human relations to "naked self-interest" and "callous 'cash payment'" [(001-020)].

The French Revolution of 1789 is the paradigmatic bourgeois revolution in the Manifesto's analysis: "The French Revolution, for example, abolished feudal property in favour of bourgeois property" [(002-010)]. It established the political forms — representative government, legal equality, the rights of man — that correspond to the economic requirements of capitalism, while disguising class rule beneath the language of universal emancipation.

> The bourgeoisie, historically, has played a most revolutionary part. [(001-012)]

> We see then: the means of production and of exchange, on whose foundation the bourgeoisie built itself up, were generated in feudal society. At a certain stage in the development of these means of production and of exchange, the conditions under which feudal society produced and exchanged, the feudal organisation of agriculture and manufacturing industry, in one word, the feudal relations of property became no longer compatible with the already developed productive forces; they became so many fetters. They had to be burst asunder; they were burst asunder. [(001-023)]

### The Proletarian Revolution

The Manifesto argues that the bourgeoisie, by its own relentless development, creates the conditions for its own overthrow. The same industrial development that concentrates capital also concentrates the proletariat, transforms it from "a mass of isolated individuals" into an organized class, and generates the periodic commercial crises that reveal capitalism's inability to govern the productive forces it has unleashed. "What the bourgeoisie, therefore, produces, above all, is its own grave-diggers. Its fall and the victory of the proletariat are equally inevitable" [(001-043)].

The proletarian revolution proceeds through distinct phases:

| Phase | Action | PN |
|-------|--------|-----|
| 1 | The proletariat conquers political power — "to raise the proletariat to the position of ruling class, to win the battle of democracy" | [(002-062)] |
| 2 | The proletariat uses political supremacy "to wrest, by degrees, all capital from the bourgeoisie, to centralise all instruments of production in the hands of the State" | [(002-063)] |
| 3 | Despotic inroads on property rights — measures that "appear economically insufficient and untenable" but "necessitate further inroads upon the old social order" | [(002-065)] |
| 4 | Class distinctions disappear, all production is concentrated "in the hands of a vast association of the whole nation" | [(002-068)] |
| 5 | The public power loses its political character — the State "withers away" as the organized power of one class for oppressing another | [(002-068)] |

> When, in the course of development, class distinctions have disappeared, and all production has been concentrated in the hands of a vast association of the whole nation, the public power will lose its political character. Political power, properly so called, is merely the organised power of one class for oppressing another. [(002-068)]

> The weapons with which the bourgeoisie felled feudalism to the ground are now turned against the bourgeoisie itself. But not only has the bourgeoisie forged the weapons that bring death to itself; it has also called into existence the men who are to wield those weapons — the modern working class — the proletarians. [(001-026)]

### Which Class is Revolutionary?

The Manifesto is analytically precise about which social classes can be revolutionary — and which cannot. "Of all the classes that stand face to face with the bourgeoisie today, the proletariat alone is a really revolutionary class" [(001-036)]. The other classes — the lower middle class, the peasantry, the *Lumpenproletariat* — are either "not revolutionary, but conservative" or, worse, "reactionary" [(001-036)]. Even the bourgeoisie, once revolutionary against feudalism, has become the conservative defender of the existing order, its revolutionary role exhausted with the consolidation of its class rule. Only the proletariat, "the special and essential product" of Modern Industry [(001-036)], has no interest in preserving any aspect of the existing mode of production — its emancipation requires the total abolition of class society.

## Historical Context

The Manifesto's theory of revolution synthesised three intellectual traditions. From **German philosophy**, Marx took Hegel's dialectic — the understanding that historical development proceeds through contradiction, negation, and qualitative transformation — but anchored it in material production rather than the movement of Spirit. From **French political experience**, the Manifesto absorbed the lessons of 1789, 1830, and the insurrectionary traditions of the sans-culottes and the Babouvist conspirators — the understanding that revolution is made by organized political forces, not by the automatic workings of economic laws. From **English political economy**, it derived the analysis of capitalism's internal dynamics — competition, concentration, crisis — that made proletarian revolution not a moral imperative but a structural tendency.

The immediate conjuncture was decisive. Written in late 1847 and published in February 1848, the Manifesto appeared on the eve of the most widespread revolutionary wave Europe had seen since 1789. Within weeks of its publication, revolutions broke out in Paris, Vienna, Berlin, Milan, Budapest, and across the German and Italian states. The Manifesto's analysis of revolution was thus not merely theoretical — it was an intervention in a revolutionary situation, a strategic document addressed to the Communist League's cadres who would participate in the barricade fighting of 1848–49.

> The Communists everywhere support every revolutionary movement against the existing social and political order of things. [(007-008)]

> The Communists turn their attention chiefly to Germany, because that country is on the eve of a bourgeois revolution that is bound to be carried out under more advanced conditions of European civilisation, and with a much more developed proletariat, than that of England was in the seventeenth, and of France in the eighteenth century, and because the bourgeois revolution in Germany will be but the prelude to an immediately following proletarian revolution. [(007-007)]

## Significance

The Manifesto's theory of revolution is arguably its most consequential legacy — and its most contested. Three dimensions deserve emphasis:

**1. Revolution as normality, not pathology.** Against the entire tradition of counter-revolutionary thought (from Burke to de Maistre to Tocqueville), the Manifesto refuses to treat revolution as a "breakdown" of social order. Revolution is the normal — indeed, the only — mechanism by which history advances from one mode of production to another. The real "disorder" is the permanent crisis, immiseration, and violence of class society itself.

**2. The dialectic of reform and revolution.** The Manifesto's 10-point programme for "the most advanced countries" [(002-067)] demonstrates that Marx and Engels did not counterpose revolution to reform in a mechanical way. Revolutionary strategy includes transitional demands — reforms that, in their implementation, "outstrip themselves" and "necessitate further inroads upon the old social order" [(002-065)]. This dialectic of reform and revolution would become the strategic core of every subsequent Marxist political tradition, from Kautsky to Lenin to Gramsci.

**3. The withering away of the state.** The Manifesto already contains, in compressed form, the theory of the state's eventual dissolution. Political power is "merely the organised power of one class for oppressing another" [(002-068)]. When classes disappear, so does the political character of public power. This anti-statist conclusion — that the proletarian revolution, uniquely among revolutions, does not consolidate a new form of class rule but creates the conditions for the abolition of class rule as such — is the radical democratic kernel of Marxist politics.

> The first step in the revolution by the working class, is to raise the proletariat to the position of ruling class, to win the battle of democracy. The proletariat will use its political supremacy to wrest, by degrees, all capital from the bourgeoisie, to centralise all instruments of production in the hands of the State, i.e., of the proletariat organised as the ruling class; and to increase the total of productive forces as rapidly as possible. [(002-067)]

> The Communists disdain to conceal their views and aims. They openly declare that their ends can be attained only by the forcible overthrow of all existing social conditions. Let the ruling classes tremble at a Communistic revolution. The proletarians have nothing to lose but their chains. They have a world to win. [(007-011)]

## Further Reading

- Karl Marx, *The Eighteenth Brumaire of Louis Bonaparte* (1852) — The most developed application of the Manifesto's theory of revolution to a concrete historical event (the 1848–51 French crisis), containing the famous formulation that proletarian revolutions "criticise themselves constantly" and "deride the insufficient breadth of their previous attempts."
- Karl Marx, *The Civil War in France* (1871) — Marx's analysis of the Paris Commune as the first historical example of the "dictatorship of the proletariat" — the political form of the transitional state after a workers' revolution.
- [[friedrich-engels|Friedrich Engels]], *Introduction to Marx's "The Class Struggles in France"* (1895) — Engels' late reflections on the changing conditions of revolutionary strategy, including the famous reassessment of barricade fighting.
- V. I. Lenin, *The State and Revolution* (1917) — The most systematic Marxist theorisation of the state and its revolutionary overthrow, drawing directly on the Manifesto and Marx's writings on the Paris Commune.
- Eric Hobsbawm, *The Age of Revolution, 1789–1848* (1962) — The indispensable historical synthesis of the revolutionary era that produced the Manifesto.
