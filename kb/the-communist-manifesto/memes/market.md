The **market** — specifically the world market — is the engine of bourgeois civilisation in the Manifesto's historical narrative. It is not merely a place where goods are bought and sold; it is the driving force that transforms every aspect of human society. "The need of a constantly expanding market for its products chases the bourgeoisie over the entire surface of the globe" [(001-018)]. The market is the medium through which the [[bourgeoisie]] conquers the world, and, paradoxically, the medium through which it creates the conditions for its own overthrow.

## Definition

In the Manifesto, the market appears in two registers. Historically, it is the expanding sphere of exchange that arose with the dissolution of feudalism, driving technological innovation, colonial expansion, and the formation of the modern nation-state. Structurally, it is the system of commodity exchange that mediates all social relations under capitalism — a system whose inherent crises (overproduction, commercial crashes) reveal the irrationality at the heart of bourgeois rationality.

The market is not neutral terrain. It is a field of power relations, structured by the ownership of the [[means-of-production|means of production]] and driven by the competitive logic of [[capital]]. "Modern industry has established the world-market, for which the discovery of America paved the way" [(001-011)]. The world market is both the precondition of modern industry and its product — a dialectical relationship in which the expansion of productive forces and the expansion of the market reinforce each other in an upward spiral that eventually reaches its limit.

## In the Manifesto

### The World Market as Historical Force

The opening pages of Section I narrate the emergence of the world market as the great historical rupture that separates the modern epoch from all previous ones. "The discovery of America, the rounding of the Cape, opened up fresh ground for the rising bourgeoisie. The East-Indian and Chinese markets, the colonisation of America, trade with the colonies, the increase in the means of exchange and in commodities generally, gave to commerce, to navigation, to industry, an impulse never before known" [(001-006)].

This is not a story of gradual evolution but of revolutionary transformation. The medieval guild system, with its limited local markets and regulated production, could not contain the forces unleashed by global trade. "The feudal system of industry, in which industrial production was monopolised by closed guilds, now no longer sufficed for the growing wants of the new markets. The manufacturing system took its place" [(001-007)].

### Market Expansion and Bourgeois Globalisation

Perhaps the most celebrated passage in the Manifesto is the description of the bourgeoisie's world-historical mission: the creation of a truly global market. "The bourgeoisie has through its exploitation of the world-market given a cosmopolitan character to production and consumption in every country... In place of the old wants, satisfied by the production of the country, we find new wants, requiring for their satisfaction the products of distant lands and climes" [(001-019)].

This globalisation is both destructive and creative. It "draws all, even the most barbarian, nations into civilisation... it compels all nations, on pain of extinction, to adopt the bourgeois mode of production" [(001-019)]. The cheapness of commodities becomes "the heavy artillery with which [the bourgeoisie] batters down all Chinese walls" [(001-020)]. The universal market is the instrument of a universal revolution — the subordination of all pre-capitalist societies to the logic of commodity exchange.

### Market Crises and the Limits of Capitalism

If the expanding market is the bourgeoisie's great achievement, its recurrent contraction is its great vulnerability. "In these crises, a great part not only of the existing products, but also of the previously created productive forces, are periodically destroyed... because there is too much civilisation, too much means of subsistence, too much industry, too much commerce" [(001-025)].

The Manifesto identifies a structural contradiction: the very development of productive forces that the market compels eventually outstrips the market's capacity to absorb what is produced. The bourgeoisie's response — "the conquest of new markets, and... the more thorough exploitation of the old ones" [(001-025)] — can only defer, not resolve, the crisis. Each expansion "paves the way for more extensive and more destructive crises" [(001-025)].

## Historical Context

Marx and Engels wrote the Manifesto at a moment when the world market was undergoing one of its periodic convulsions. The commercial crisis of 1847, which began in Britain and spread rapidly across Europe, provided the immediate context for their analysis. The crisis demonstrated, with brutal clarity, the vulnerability of an economic system organised around market exchange.

The theoretical framework drew on the classical economists' analysis of markets — particularly the insight that the division of labour is limited by the extent of the market (Adam Smith) — but radicalised it. Where Smith and Ricardo saw the expansion of markets as a benign process of growing wealth, Marx and Engels saw it as the expansion of a system of exploitation that would eventually reach its geographical and social limits.
