# Feudal Socialism

## Definition

Feudal Socialism is the term Marx and Engels give to the anti-bourgeois literature produced by the French and English aristocracies after their political defeat in the July Revolution of 1830 and the English Reform agitation. It was not socialism in any meaningful sense — it was the revenge of a displaced ruling class, using the grievances of the working class as a pretext to attack its bourgeois successor. The Manifesto's definition is characteristically sharp: Feudal Socialism is "half lamentation, half lampoon; half echo of the past, half menace of the future" (004-004) — a literary performance that struck the bourgeoisie to the heart with incisive criticism but was ultimately "ludicrous in its effect, through total incapacity to comprehend the march of modern history" (004-004).

## In the Manifesto

The Manifesto's critique of Feudal Socialism occupies the opening section of Ch. III ("Socialist and Communist Literature"), under the subheading "A. Feudal Socialism." It is the first of three forms of "Reactionary Socialism" analysed — the others being [[petty-bourgeois-socialism]] and [[german-socialism]].

### Historical Origins

Marx and Engels describe the historical origins of Feudal Socialism with biting irony. After the [[aristocracy]]'s political defeat in 1830, "a serious political contest was altogether out of the question. A literary battle alone remained possible" (004-002). Unable to wield power directly, the aristocracy turned to pamphleteering. The twist was that they could no longer defend their own interests openly — "the old cries of the restoration period had become impossible" (004-002) — so they disguised their reactionary agenda in the language of working-class advocacy: "the aristocracy were obliged to lose sight, apparently, of their own interests, and to formulate their indictment against the [[bourgeoisie]] in the interest of the exploited working class alone" (004-003).

> Owing to their historical position, it became the vocation of the aristocracies of France and England to write pamphlets against modern bourgeois society. In the French revolution of July 1830, and in the English reform agitation, these aristocracies again succumbed to the hateful upstart. Thenceforth, a serious political contest was altogether out of the question. A literary battle alone remained possible. But even in the domain of literature the old cries of the restoration period had become impossible. [004-002]

> In order to arouse sympathy, the aristocracy were obliged to lose sight, apparently, of their own interests, and to formulate their indictment against the bourgeoisie in the interest of the exploited working class alone. Thus the aristocracy took their revenge by singing lampoons on their new master, and whispering in his ears sinister prophecies of coming catastrophe. [004-003]

### The Central Critique

The Manifesto identifies the central contradiction of Feudal Socialism with devastating precision: "In pointing out that their mode of exploitation was different to that of the bourgeoisie, the feudalists forget that they exploited under circumstances and conditions that were quite different, and that are now antiquated" (004-007). The feudal lords criticise bourgeois exploitation, but they do so from the standpoint of a different — and historically superseded — form of exploitation. They do not oppose exploitation as such; they oppose the fact that someone else is doing the exploiting.

> In pointing out that their mode of exploitation was different to that of the bourgeoisie, the feudalists forget that they exploited under circumstances and conditions that were quite different, and that are now antiquated. In showing that, under their rule, the modern proletariat never existed, they forget that the modern bourgeoisie is the necessary offspring of their own form of society. [004-007]

The political character of this critique is unambiguously reactionary. Marx and Engels observe that "so little do they conceal the reactionary character of their criticism that their chief accusation against the bourgeoisie amounts to this, that under the bourgeois *regime* a class is being developed, which is destined to cut up root and branch the old order of society" (004-008). The real objection is not to the creation of a [[proletariat]] but to the creation of a *revolutionary* proletariat: "What they upbraid the bourgeoisie with is not so much that it creates a proletariat, as that it creates a *revolutionary* proletariat" (004-009).

> For the rest, so little do they conceal the reactionary character of their criticism that their chief accusation against the bourgeoisie amounts to this, that under the bourgeois regime a class is being developed, which is destined to cut up root and branch the old order of society. [004-008]

In practice, therefore, the feudal socialists "join in all coercive measures against the working class" (004-010), even as they posture as its defenders. The Manifesto goes further, exposing their hypocrisy in economic life: "despite their high falutin phrases, they stoop to pick up the golden apples dropped from the tree of industry, and to barter truth, love, and honour for traffic in wool, beetroot-sugar, and potato spirits" (004-010).

### The Clerical Connection

The Manifesto links Feudal Socialism to its clerical counterpart: "As the parson has ever gone hand in hand with the landlord, so has Clerical Socialism with Feudal Socialism" (004-011). Christian Socialism — the attempt to give "Christian asceticism a Socialist tinge" (004-012) — is dismissed as "the holy water with which the priest consecrates the heart-burnings of the aristocrat" (004-012). Marx and Engels locate Christian Socialism squarely within the feudal reaction, noting that the aristocracy's pamphleteering found a natural ally in the church: "Has not Christianity declaimed against private property, against marriage, against the State? Has it not preached in the place of these, charity and poverty, celibacy and mortification of the flesh, monastic life and Mother Church?" (004-012).

> Nothing is easier than to give Christian asceticism a Socialist tinge. Has not Christianity declaimed against private property, against marriage, against the State? Has it not preached in the place of these, charity and poverty, celibacy and mortification of the flesh, monastic life and Mother Church? Christian Socialism is but the holy water with which the priest consecrates the heart-burnings of the aristocrat. [004-012]

## Historical Context

Feudal Socialism was not a straw man. In France, the Legitimist party — supporters of the deposed Bourbon monarchy — published pamphlets attacking the July Monarchy's industrial capitalism and its social costs. "One section of the French Legitimists and 'Young England' exhibited this spectacle" (004-006). In England, the "Young England" movement, led by Benjamin Disraeli and other Tory aristocrats, romanticised the pre-industrial social order and criticised the factory system from a paternalistic, anti-capitalist standpoint. Disraeli's novel *Sybil, or The Two Nations* (1845) gave literary expression to this current, describing a Britain divided between rich and poor as if they were "two nations."

Marx and Engels were not blind to the literary merit of some of this criticism. Feudal Socialism was capable of "bitter, witty and incisive criticism, striking the bourgeoisie to the very heart's core" (004-004). But they saw through the performance: Feudal Socialism was not a genuine defence of the working class but the death rattle of a doomed aristocracy, trying to rally popular support by "waving the proletarian alms-bag in front for a banner" even as the people saw "on their hindquarters the old feudal coats of arms" (004-005), and "deserted with loud and irreverent laughter" (004-005).

> The aristocracy, in order to rally the people to them, waved the proletarian alms-bag in front for a banner. But the people, so often as it joined them, saw on their hindquarters the old feudal coats of arms, and deserted with loud and irreverent laughter. [004-005]

### The French Legitimists

The French Legitimists represented the aristocratic faction that refused to accept the [[july-revolution]] of 1830 and the bourgeois monarchy of Louis-Philippe. Writers associated with this current — such as the Vicomte d'Arlincourt — produced polemics against industrial capitalism that appealed to a romanticised vision of feudal hierarchy, where lords and peasants were bound by mutual obligation rather than the cash nexus. The Manifesto's critique of this tradition is devastating precisely because it acknowledges the literary power of the aristocratic counter-attack while exposing its political bankruptcy.

### Young England

"Young England" was a political-literary movement within the Tory Party of the 1840s, associated with Benjamin Disraeli, Lord John Manners, and George Smythe. Its members looked back to an idealised medieval past of paternalistic social relations and attacked the inhumanity of the factory system. Disraeli's *Sybil* gave voice to the "two nations" thesis — a Britain divided between the rich and poor who were "as ignorant of each other's habits, thoughts, and feelings, as if they were dwellers in different zones, or inhabitants of different planets." Yet the Manifesto's verdict is unambiguous: this paternalistic critique was a form of aristocratic nostalgia, not a genuine movement for working-class emancipation.

### The Defeat of 1830

The [[july-revolution]] of 1830 was the decisive historical watershed that transformed the aristocracy from a ruling class into a pamphleteering one. Charles X's attempt to restore absolute monarchy was defeated in three days of street fighting in Paris, and the "bourgeois monarch" Louis-Philippe ascended the throne. "In the French revolution of July 1830, and in the English reform agitation, these aristocracies again succumbed to the hateful upstart" (004-002). After this defeat, direct political contest was "altogether out of the question" (004-002); what remained was only literary warfare. The English Reform agitation — culminating in the Reform Act of 1832 — similarly confirmed the political ascendancy of the industrial bourgeoisie over the landed aristocracy, pushing the latter into pamphleteering opposition.

## Related Concepts

Feudal Socialism is the first of the three forms of reactionary socialism identified in Ch. III. It is followed by [[petty-bourgeois-socialism]], which criticises capitalism from the standpoint of the doomed intermediate classes, and [[german-socialism]], which translates French socialist ideas into German philosophical jargon while serving the interests of the reactionary state. The [[aristocracy]] is the class agent of Feudal Socialism — a class that once ruled by feudal right and now can only lament its displacement. The [[bourgeoisie]] is the target of Feudal Socialism's critique, but not because it exploits — because it has displaced the old exploiters.

The concept of [[reaction]] — the political attempt to restore a superseded social order — is central to understanding Feudal Socialism. The Manifesto's analysis of this phenomenon connects it to the broader theme of [[class-struggle]], in which the aristocracy is not the vanguard of the future but the rearguard of the past. The clerical dimension of Feudal Socialism also links the concept to [[religion]] and its role in sanctifying class interests — "the holy water with which the priest consecrates the heart-burnings of the aristocrat" (004-012).
