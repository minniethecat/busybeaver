Among the bourgeois objections that Marx and Engels confront in the Manifesto, the charge that communism abolishes religion and morality occupies a privileged rhetorical position. "It abolishes all religion, and all morality, instead of constituting them on a new basis," cry the bourgeoisie's ideological spokesmen [(002-062)]. Rather than defend communism against this charge, the Manifesto embraces it — and in doing so, articulates one of the foundational principles of historical materialism: that ideas, including religious ideas, are the reflexes of material conditions, and that the transformation of those conditions entails the transformation of the ideas themselves.

## Definition

In the Manifesto's theoretical framework, **religion** is an element of the ideological superstructure — a set of beliefs, values, and institutions that arise from and serve to legitimate a particular mode of production. Religion is not, as its adherents claim, an "eternal truth" that stands outside history. Rather, it is a historically produced form of consciousness that reflects the social relations of its time. "When the ancient world was in its last throes, the ancient religions were overcome by Christianity. When Christian ideas succumbed in the 18th century to rationalist ideas, feudal society fought its death battle with the then revolutionary bourgeoisie" [(002-060)]. The succession of religious forms — pagan, Christian, rationalist — corresponds to the succession of modes of production and the class struggles they generate.

## In the Manifesto

Religion appears explicitly in only a few passages of the Manifesto, but its treatment encapsulates the text's broader theory of ideology and its relationship to class struggle.

### The Charge Answered

The most concentrated discussion occurs in the rebuttal to bourgeois objections in Section II. The bourgeoisie accuses communism of abolishing "eternal truths" — "religion, morality, philosophy, politics, law, etc." [(002-061)]. Marx and Engels do not contest the premise that communism would transform these. Instead, they contest their claim to eternity. "The charges raised against the Communist mode of producing and appropriating are at the same time charges against the appropriation and production of the products of intellectual labour" [(002-061)]. The bourgeoisie's "eternal truths" are not universally valid propositions but the intellectual products of a specific class society — "so many bourgeois prejudices, behind which lurk in ambush just as many bourgeois interests" [(002-062)].

The dialectical point is that communism does not abolish religion and morality in the abstract — it abolishes their *bourgeois* form, the form in which they function as ideological justifications for exploitation. What replaces them is not a void but forms of consciousness appropriate to a classless society — forms that, because they no longer need to justify exploitation, need no longer present themselves as "eternal."

### Religion as Bourgeois Prejudice

Earlier in the text, Marx and Engels had already signalled religion's ideological function. The proletarian, deprived of all property and family ties, sees through the bourgeois mystifications: "Law, morality, religion, are to him so many bourgeois prejudices, behind which lurk in ambush just as many bourgeois interests" [(001-044)]. This is not the cynicism of the déclassé but the clear-sightedness of the propertyless: the proletarian has no interest in the preservation of "eternal truths" that sanctify their exploitation.

### The Historical Analogy

The Manifesto's single historical analogy for religious transformation is strategically chosen: "When the ancient world was in its last throes, the ancient religions were overcome by Christianity. When Christian ideas succumbed in the 18th century to rationalist ideas, feudal society fought its death battle with the then revolutionary bourgeoisie" [(002-060)]. The pattern is clear: a new ascending class brings with it new ideological forms that challenge the old order's religious justifications. The bourgeoisie, once the carrier of rationalist enlightenment against feudal Christianity, has now become the defender of a new set of "eternal truths" — the very truths that the proletarian revolution will supersede.

## Historical Context

The Manifesto's treatment of religion synthesises two currents of the European Enlightenment. The first was the **French materialist tradition** (Holbach, Helvétius, Diderot), which had subjected religion to philosophical critique as priestcraft and superstition. The second was the **German Left Hegelian critique**, particularly [[ludwig-feuerbach|Ludwig Feuerbach]]'s *The Essence of Christianity* (1841), which argued that God was a projection of human essence — that theology was, in reality, anthropology. Marx and Engels absorbed both traditions but went beyond them: for the materialist conception of history, religious ideas are not merely errors to be refuted by reason but ideological forms rooted in alienated social relations. To overcome religion, one must overcome the conditions that make it necessary.

The Manifesto also reflects the specific conjuncture of the 1840s, in which religious discourse was a primary register of political conflict. The Holy Alliance (Russia, Austria, Prussia) legitimated itself in explicitly Christian terms; the "feudal socialists" invoked religion as a bulwark against bourgeois individualism; and the "German or True Socialists" translated French socialist ideas into the language of German speculative philosophy and Christian ethics. In each case, religion functioned to sanitise reactionary politics — and the Manifesto's anti-religious polemic is, in the first instance, a polemic against these political uses of religion.
