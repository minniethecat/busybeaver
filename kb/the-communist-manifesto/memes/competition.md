**Competition** is, alongside [[capital|capital accumulation]], the great driving force of bourgeois society in Marx and Engels' analysis. It is the mechanism that shatters feudal guilds, compels constant technological revolution, and reduces social relations to the cash nexus. But competition is also the force that fragments the [[proletariat]], pitting worker against worker in a race to the bottom — until [[association]] and combination reverse the dynamic.

> Into their place stepped free competition, accompanied by a social and political constitution adapted to it, and by the economical and political sway of the bourgeois class. [(001-024)]

## Definition

In the Manifesto, competition operates at two distinct but interconnected levels. Between capitalists, it is the coercive law that forces every individual bourgeois to accumulate, innovate, and expand or else be destroyed. Between workers, it is the fragmentation mechanism that keeps wages at subsistence level and prevents the formation of a class-conscious proletariat. Competition is not a natural state but a historically specific product of the dissolution of feudal bonds — "free" only in the sense that it has swept away the personal dependencies of the old regime and replaced them with the impersonal domination of the [[market]].

The Manifesto insists that the competitive regime, for all its talk of freedom, is itself a form of despotism. Workers who must sell themselves piecemeal "are a commodity, like every other article of commerce, and are consequently exposed to all the vicissitudes of competition, to all the fluctuations of the market" [(001-028)].

> Masses of labourers, crowded into the factory, are organised like soldiers.... Not only are they slaves of the bourgeois class, and of the bourgeois State; they are daily and hourly enslaved by the machine, by the over-looker, and, above all, by the individual bourgeois manufacturer himself. [(001-030)]

## In the Manifesto

### Competition Between Capitalists

The Manifesto treats competition as a relentless process of concentration and centralisation. The small tradespeople, shopkeepers, handicraftsmen and peasants all sink gradually into the proletariat "partly because their diminutive capital does not suffice for the scale on which Modern Industry is carried on, and is swamped in the competition with the large capitalists" [(001-033)].

> The lower strata of the middle class — the small tradespeople, shopkeepers, and retired tradesmen generally, the handicraftsmen and peasants — all these sink gradually into the proletariat, partly because their diminutive capital does not suffice for the scale on which Modern Industry is carried on, and is swamped in the competition with the large capitalists, partly because their specialized skill is rendered worthless by the new methods of production. [(001-033)]

This process is cumulative: as competition eliminates smaller capitals, the survivors grow larger, only to face fiercer competition among themselves. "The growing competition among the bourgeois, and the resulting commercial crises, make the wages of the workers ever more fluctuating" [(001-036)]. The competitive pressure to reduce costs translates directly into downward pressure on wages and working conditions.

> The growing competition among the bourgeois, and the resulting commercial crises, make the wages of the workers ever more fluctuating. The unceasing improvement of machinery, ever more rapidly developing, makes their livelihood more and more precarious; the collisions between individual workmen and individual bourgeois take more and more the character of collisions between two classes. [(001-036)]

### Competition Between Workers

The second level of competition is between workers themselves. "Wage-labour rests exclusively on competition between the laborers" [(001-050)]. The reserve army of the unemployed, the introduction of [[machinery]] that deskills labour, and the expansion of the labour market through the destruction of rural economies all intensify this intra-class competition.

> At this stage the labourers still form an incoherent mass scattered over the whole country, and broken up by their mutual competition. [(001-035)]

But the Manifesto also describes the counter-movement. "Thereupon the workers begin to form combinations (Trades' Unions) against the bourgeois; they club together in order to keep up the rate of wages; they found permanent associations in order to make provision beforehand for these occasional revolts" [(001-036)]. The movement from competition to association is, in the Manifesto's historical narrative, the process by which a [[class-struggle|class]] "in itself" becomes a class "for itself."

The dialectic of competition and [[association]] reaches its culmination in the Manifesto's closing argument of Section I: "The advance of industry, whose involuntary promoter is the bourgeoisie, replaces the isolation of the labourers, due to competition, by their revolutionary combination, due to association" [(001-050)].

### Competition and Bourgeois Ideology

The Manifesto identifies the ideology of free competition as a distinctive feature of bourgeois thought. In Section III, it criticises the German "True" Socialists for failing to understand that "the abolition of bourgeois competition" by the state (in the form of reactionary socialism) is "only the reactionary trick of setting the guild-system against the modern representative state, of setting competition against competition" [(004-031)].

> By this, the long wished-for opportunity was offered to "True" Socialism of confronting the political movement with the Socialist demands, of hurling the traditional anathemas against liberalism, against representative government, against bourgeois competition, bourgeois freedom of the press, bourgeois legislation, bourgeois liberty and equality, and of preaching to the masses that they had nothing to gain, and everything to lose, by this bourgeois movement. [(004-031)]

Competition is not merely an economic arrangement but a legitimating ideology that presents bourgeois rule as the outcome of free, individual choice. The Manifesto's analysis extends even to the realm of ideas: "The ideas of religious liberty and freedom of conscience merely gave expression to the sway of free competition within the domain of knowledge" [(002-060)].

### Competition and Class Dissolution

A distinctive aspect of the Manifesto's analysis is the role of competition in dissolving intermediate classes. The [[petty-bourgeoisie]] — the small manufacturer, shopkeeper, artisan, and peasant — is depicted as a class condemned by the competitive logic of [[modern-industry|Modern Industry]]. The Manifesto describes how "the individual members of this class, however, are being constantly hurled down into the proletariat by the action of competition" [(004-015)]. This downward mobility is not accidental but structural: competition creates a polarisation of society into two great hostile camps, [[bourgeoisie]] and [[proletariat]], with the middle strata ground away between them.

The competitive dynamic thus serves a historical function: it accelerates the simplification of class antagonisms that, in the Manifesto's teleology, is the precondition for revolutionary consciousness and action.

## Beyond the Nation: Competition and the World Market

The Manifesto insists that competition is inherently globalising. The [[bourgeoisie]] "must nestle everywhere, settle everywhere, establish connexions everywhere" [(001-018)]; it cannot survive without constantly extending the sphere of its competitive operations. As the Manifesto declares, "the need of a constantly expanding market for its products chases the bourgeoisie over the whole surface of the globe" [(001-018)]. The [[world-market]] is not merely the arena in which competition plays out — it is actively produced by the competitive drive to find new outlets for commodities and new sources of cheap raw materials. Each [[crisis]] of [[over-production]] sends capital abroad in search of new markets, extending the competitive logic across the entire surface of the globe.

This global dimension of competition has a dialectical effect on the [[proletariat]]. On one hand, competition between workers is internationalised: workers in one country can be played off against those in another, as capital migrates to wherever labour is cheapest and least organised. On the other hand, the very process of global market integration that [[commerce]] compels creates the material conditions for international working-class solidarity. The Manifesto's famous closing appeal — "Working Men of All Countries, Unite!" — presupposes that competition has already created a world in which the interests of workers everywhere are materially linked.

This globalising logic is not a later addition to the Manifesto's argument but integral to it. Section I describes how the bourgeoisie has "through its exploitation of the world-market given a cosmopolitan character to production and consumption in every country" [(001-019)]. Competition is, from the Manifesto's opening analysis, the engine of [[colonialism]] and the driving force behind the integration of the entire world into a single system of [[relations-of-production]].

## Historical Context

The concept of competition that Marx and Engels deploy draws on classical political economy — particularly Adam Smith's notion of the "invisible hand" — but inverts its normative valence. Where Smith saw competition as the benign mechanism that harmonises private self-interest with public good, the Manifesto sees it as a coercive, destructive force that enriches the few at the expense of the many.

The historical referent is the rapid industrialisation of Britain in the first half of the 19th century. The repeal of the Corn Laws (1846), the expansion of factory production, and the repeated commercial crises of the 1830s and 1840s provided Marx and Engels with abundant evidence of competition's effects: boom-and-bust cycles, the immiseration of hand-loom weavers displaced by power looms, and the concentration of capital into ever-fewer hands. The Manifesto's analysis of competition is, in this sense, an empirically grounded description of early industrial capitalism rather than abstract theorising.

This period also saw the rise of [[free-trade|free trade]] ideology, embodied in the Anti-Corn Law League (founded 1838), which presented the abolition of tariffs as the path to universal prosperity. The Manifesto challenges this narrative directly: competition and [[crisis]] are not accidental byproducts but necessary features of the capitalist mode of production.
