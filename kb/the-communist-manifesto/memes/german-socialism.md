**German Socialism** (also called **"True" Socialism**) is the target of one of the most sustained polemics in the Manifesto. In Section C of Chapter III, Marx and Engels dissect a peculiarly German deformation of French socialist thought — a variant that translated class demands into philosophical abstractions, stripped socialism of its revolutionary content, and ended up serving as a weapon of the absolutist state against the liberal bourgeoisie.

## Definition

German Socialism is the product of a historical mismatch. French socialist and communist literature — "a literature that originated under the pressure of a bourgeoisie in power, and that was the expression of the struggle against this power" [(004-022)] — was imported into Germany at a moment when the German bourgeoisie had only just begun its contest with feudal absolutism. The German philosophical intelligentsia seized upon this literature but "only forgetting, that when these writings immigrated from France into Germany, French social conditions had not immigrated along with them" [(004-023)].

The result was a philosophical translation in the worst sense: German writers "wrote their philosophical nonsense beneath the French original. For instance, beneath the French criticism of the economic functions of money, they wrote 'Alienation of Humanity,' and beneath the French criticism of the bourgeois State they wrote 'dethronement of the Category of the General'" [(004-026)]. What in France had been a weapon of class struggle became in Germany a speculative exercise detached from material conditions.

Marx and Engels are merciless in their characterisation of this literature's intellectual quality: "The robe of speculative cobwebs, embroidered with flowers of rhetoric, steeped in the dew of sickly sentiment — this transcendental robe in which the German Socialists wrapped their sorry 'eternal truths'" [(004-036)].

## In the Manifesto

### The Mechanism of Emasculation

The Manifesto traces a precise chain of distortion. French socialist literature expressed the struggle of a specific class — the proletariat — against a specific oppressor — the bourgeoisie. The German philosophers, importing these ideas, replaced class content with abstract universals. "Since it ceased in the hands of the German to express the struggle of one class with the other, he felt conscious of having overcome 'French one-sidedness' and of representing, not true requirements, but the requirements of truth; not the interests of the proletariat, but the interests of Human Nature, of Man in general, who belongs to no class, has no reality, who exists only in the misty realm of philosophical fantasy" [(004-028)].

This is the definitive account of ideological distortion in the Manifesto: the substitution of a concrete historical agent (the proletariat) with an abstract philosophical category ("Man"), a move that simultaneously claims to be more universal than class politics while in fact being more helpless.

### The Political Function of "True" Socialism

The critique is not merely philosophical. Marx and Engels identify the concrete political role that German Socialism played in the 1840s. As the Prussian bourgeoisie's liberal movement grew more earnest, "True" Socialism found its opportunity: it directed its fire at liberalism, at representative government, at bourgeois freedoms — at everything the bourgeoisie was fighting for, not at the feudal-absolutist state that was the real enemy [(004-031)].

"German Socialism forgot, in the nick of time, that the French criticism, whose silly echo it was, presupposed the existence of modern bourgeois society, with its corresponding economic conditions of existence, and the political constitution adapted thereto, the very things whose attainment was the object of the pending struggle in Germany" [(004-031)]. In attacking the bourgeoisie while ignoring absolutism, German Socialism objectively aligned itself with the forces of reaction.

The Manifesto makes this alignment brutally explicit: "To the absolute governments, with their following of parsons, professors, country squires and officials, it served as a welcome scarecrow against the threatening bourgeoisie" [(004-032)]. And when these same governments put down working-class uprisings with "floggings and bullets," True Socialism provided "a sweet finish" to the repression [(004-033)].

### The Petty-Bourgeois Social Basis

Marx and Engels identify the class basis of German Socialism with precision: it "directly represented a reactionary interest, the interest of the German Philistines" — the [[petty-bourgeoisie]] of Germany, "a relique of the sixteenth century, and since then constantly cropping up again under various forms" [(004-034)]. The petty bourgeoisie was threatened from both sides: by the concentration of capital (the rising bourgeoisie) and by the revolutionary proletariat. "True Socialism appeared to kill these two birds with one stone. It spread like an epidemic" [(004-035)].

By the end of the section, the Manifesto has constructed a devastating chain of identification: German Socialism = the ideological expression of the German petty bourgeoisie = a tool of the absolutist state = an enemy of the working class.

## Historical Specificity

The Manifesto's critique of German Socialism must be understood in the context of 1840s German politics. Germany in this period was not yet a unified nation-state but a patchwork of kingdoms, principalities, and free cities, dominated by Prussia and Austria. The bourgeoisie was weak and dispersed; the proletariat was nascent. The 1848 revolutions would soon sweep across the German states, and Marx and Engels saw the ideological confusion spread by "True" Socialism as a direct obstacle to the development of an independent working-class politics.

The figures Marx and Engels had in mind included Karl Grün, Moses Hess, and Hermann Kriege — writers associated with the "True Socialist" current who had influenced the early German workers' movement. By 1847, when the Manifesto was written, Marx and Engels had decisively broken with this tendency, and the Manifesto's Chapter III served as the public announcement of that break.


> The French Socialist and Communist literature was thus completely emasculated. And, since it ceased in the hands of the German to express the struggle of one class with the other, he felt conscious of having overcome "French one-sidedness" and of representing, not true requirements, but the requirements of truth; not the interests of the proletariat, but the interests of Human Nature, of Man in general, who belongs to no class, has no reality, who exists only in the misty realm of philosophical fantasy.
>
> (004-028)
