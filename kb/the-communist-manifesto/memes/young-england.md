**Young England** was a political and literary group within the British Tory Party in the early 1840s, led by the young Benjamin Disraeli (later Prime Minister) and including aristocratic figures such as Lord John Manners, George Smythe, and Alexander Baillie-Cochrane. In the Manifesto, Marx and Engels cite Young England — alongside the French Legitimists — as the English exemplar of Feudal Socialism: a tendency that "went into the battle against the bourgeoisie" with "the weapons of the aristocracy" (004-001), denouncing bourgeois society in the name of an idealized pre-capitalist past while in practice collaborating in the repression of the working class.

## In the Manifesto

### Ch. III §1a: Feudal Socialism

Young England appears in the Manifesto at a precise point in the anatomy of Feudal Socialism — the first and most openly reactionary of the three varieties of "Reactionary Socialism" dissected in Chapter III. Marx and Engels identify two national variants of this tendency: the French Legitimists (supporters of the Bourbon monarchy overthrown in 1830) and Young England. Both, they argue, "exhibited this spectacle" — the spectacle of aristocrats denouncing the bourgeoisie for creating a revolutionary proletariat, while hypocritically participating in the industrial exploitation they profess to despise (004-006–004-011).

The Manifesto's characterization is devastatingly precise. The feudal socialists "forget that the modern bourgeoisie is the necessary offspring of their own form of society" (004-005). Their critique of capitalism is not made from the standpoint of the future but from the standpoint of the past. What genuinely disturbs them is not exploitation as such but the particular *form* exploitation takes under bourgeois rule: they are nostalgic for the open, patriarchal domination of the feudal lord over the serf, and resentful of the anonymous, cash-nexus domination of the capitalist over the wage-worker.

The political consequences follow logically: "In political practice, therefore, they join in all coercive measures against the working class" (004-011). The feudal socialist's romantic rhetoric about medieval community and organic social bonds serves, in the end, as cover for an alliance with the very bourgeoisie they claim to despise — an alliance that is sealed in the shared project of repressing proletarian revolution.

## Historical Role

### Tory Radicalism and the "Condition of England" Question

Young England emerged from a specific conjuncture in early-Victorian politics. The 1832 Reform Act had broken the political monopoly of the landed aristocracy without establishing full democracy; the 1834 Poor Law Amendment Act had created the hated workhouse system; and the Chartist movement was mobilizing the industrial working class around demands for universal male suffrage. In this context, a faction of young Tory aristocrats began to argue that the Conservative Party's salvation lay in breaking with the laissez-faire dogmas of the Manchester School and embracing a programme of social reform that would bind the working class to the aristocracy through ties of paternalist protection rather than market exploitation.

This was "Tory Radicalism" — a paradoxical blend of reactionary nostalgia and reformist paternalism. Disraeli's novels of the period, particularly *Coningsby* (1844) and *Sybil, or The Two Nations* (1845), dramatized the "Condition of England" question: the division of society into "two nations between whom there is no intercourse and no sympathy... the rich and the poor." Disraeli's solution was not democratic revolution but a revived aristocratic paternalism — a "new feudalism" in which the rich would care for the poor as a matter of honour and duty, binding the classes together in organic harmony rather than leaving them to the atomizing logic of the market.

### The Political Limits of Paternalism

Young England's programme — factory reform, protection for agricultural labourers, revival of the Church's social role — was not entirely empty. The Ten Hours' Bill, which Marx and Engels elsewhere in the Manifesto cite as a genuine working-class victory (001-038), drew support from Tory reformers whose motives were hardly those of proletarian solidarity. But the limits of paternalism were structural. Young England could criticize the effects of capitalism without touching its foundations; it could denounce the bourgeoisie without mobilizing the proletariat; it could romanticize the Middle Ages without proposing a feasible alternative to industrial society. It was, in the end, a literary and parliamentary tendency — not a mass movement, and certainly not a revolutionary one.

The movement dissipated rapidly after 1845, undermined by the contradiction between its medievalist rhetoric and its members' practical participation in agricultural and industrial capitalism. Disraeli himself went on to become leader of the Conservative Party and Prime Minister — a trajectory that confirms rather than refutes the Manifesto's analysis. The Young Englander who once denounced "the two nations" ended his career as the architect of an imperialist Conservatism that had thoroughly accommodated itself to bourgeois rule.

## Marxist Assessment

Marx and Engels' treatment of Young England exemplifies their dialectical method. They do not deny that the group's criticisms of bourgeois society contained elements of truth — the recognition of class division as the central fact of modern society was, after all, the starting point of their own analysis. But what matters, for Marxism, is not what a tendency says but what class interests its practice serves. Young England's practice — alliance with bourgeois repression, paternalist reformism that substituted charity for rights, nostalgia for a past that never existed — served the interests of the landed aristocracy, and through them, the interests of the existing order.

The theoretical point generalizes beyond Young England. Feudal Socialism, in all its national variants, demonstrates that anti-capitalist rhetoric is not necessarily progressive. The class standpoint from which capitalism is criticized determines whether the critique opens the door to socialist transformation or merely reinforces existing relations of domination under a different ideological banner. Young England was anti-capitalist — but it was anti-capitalist from the right, and its "socialism" was ultimately a defence of aristocratic privilege dressed in medieval costume.
