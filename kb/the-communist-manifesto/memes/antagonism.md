**Class antagonism** is the master concept of the Manifesto's theory of history. "The history of all hitherto existing society is the history of class struggles" [(001-001)] — and those struggles, in every epoch, are rooted in the antagonism between those who control the means of production and those who labour under that control. In bourgeois society, this antagonism has been simplified to its starkest form: "Society as a whole is more and more splitting up into two great hostile camps, into two great classes directly facing each other — [[bourgeoisie|Bourgeoisie]] and [[proletariat|Proletariat]]" [(001-004)].

## Definition

Antagonism, in the Manifesto's usage, is more than conflict. It designates a structural opposition of interests that is built into the relations of production themselves. The capitalist's interest lies in maximising the surplus value extracted from labour; the worker's interest lies in resisting that extraction. This is not a misunderstanding that could be resolved through dialogue, nor a contingent clash that might be smoothed over by reform. It is, in the Manifesto's analysis, constitutive of the wage relation itself.

"[[wage-labour|Wage-labour]] rests exclusively on competition between the labourers. The advances of industry, whose involuntary promoter is the bourgeoisie, replaces the isolation of the labourers, due to competition, by their revolutionary combination, due to association" [(001-050)]. Antagonism is the historical engine: it drives the formation of the proletariat as a class, compels the bourgeoisie to revolutionise the instruments of production, and ultimately generates the crisis in which the whole system is overthrown.

## In the Manifesto

### Antagonism as Historical Motor

Section I of the Manifesto is structured around the history of class antagonisms. "Freeman and slave, patrician and plebeian, lord and serf, guild-master and journeyman, in a word, oppressor and oppressed, stood in constant opposition to one another" [(001-001)]. Each mode of production has its characteristic form of class antagonism; each generates the forces that will eventually destroy it.

The Manifesto draws a direct line from feudal antagonism through bourgeois revolution to the emerging antagonism between bourgeoisie and proletariat. "The weapons with which the bourgeoisie felled feudalism to the ground are now turned against the bourgeoisie itself. But not only has the bourgeoisie forged the weapons that bring death to itself; it has also called into existence the men who are to wield those weapons — the modern working class — the proletarians" [(001-027)].

### The Simplification of Antagonism

A distinctive claim of the Manifesto is that bourgeois society simplifies class antagonisms rather than multiplying them. The middle strata — peasants, artisans, small traders — are progressively proletarianised. "The lower strata of the middle class... sink gradually into the proletariat" [(001-033)]. The result is that "the class struggle... approaches the decisive hour" [(001-050)] — a polarisation that makes revolutionary confrontation both possible and inevitable.

This "simplification thesis" has been one of the most contested claims in the Manifesto. The persistence and growth of middle-class strata in advanced capitalist societies has led many to argue that Marx and Engels underestimated the bourgeoisie's capacity to generate intermediate positions. But the Manifesto itself acknowledges that this simplification is a tendency, not an accomplished fact — a direction of movement that may be counteracted by other forces.

### The Abolition of Antagonism

The Manifesto's programme is not to manage or soften class antagonisms but to abolish the conditions that produce them. "When, in the course of development, class distinctions have disappeared, and all production has been concentrated in the hands of a vast association of the whole nation, the public power will lose its political character" [(002-064)]. The end of class antagonism is simultaneously the end of the political — the state as an instrument of class rule withers away when there are no classes left to rule over.

## Historical Context

The concept of class antagonism in the Manifesto stands in explicit opposition to two rival positions. Against the bourgeois political economists, who depicted capitalism as a harmonious system of voluntary exchange, Marx and Engels insisted on its fundamentally antagonistic character. Against the utopian socialists, who sought to reconcile the classes through moral appeal and model communities, they insisted that antagonism could only be abolished through revolutionary confrontation.

The year 1848 provided ample confirmation of the antagonism thesis. Within weeks of the Manifesto's publication, revolution broke out across Europe — in Paris, Vienna, Berlin, Milan, and dozens of smaller cities. The "spectre of Communism" that the Manifesto had announced at its opening had taken material form in barricades and insurrections.
