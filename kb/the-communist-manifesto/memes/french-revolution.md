# French Revolution

> The French Revolution, for example, abolished feudal property in favour of bourgeois property. [(002-010)]

The **French Revolution** (1789–1799) was a period of radical social, political, and legal transformation in France that overthrew the [[absolute-monarchy|Bourbon absolute monarchy]], abolished [[feudalism|feudal]] privileges, and established the political dominance of the [[bourgeoisie]]. Beginning with the convening of the Estates-General in May 1789 and the storming of the Bastille in July, the [[revolution]] passed through successive phases — constitutional monarchy, the Jacobin Republic, the Thermidorian Reaction, and the Directory — before culminating in Napoleon Bonaparte's coup of 18 Brumaire (1799). The Revolution eliminated the feudal-aristocratic legal order, replaced it with bourgeois property rights and legal equality, and proclaimed the universalist principles of liberty, equality, and fraternity. Its international impact was immense: the Revolutionary and Napoleonic Wars exported its principles across Europe, permanently disrupting the continent's old regime systems.

## Overview

The French Revolution (1789–1799) was a period of radical social, political, and legal transformation in France that overthrew the Bourbon absolute monarchy, abolished feudal privileges, and established the political dominance of the bourgeoisie. Beginning with the convening of the Estates-General in May 1789 and the storming of the Bastille in July, the Revolution passed through successive phases — constitutional monarchy, the Jacobin Republic, the Thermidorian Reaction, and the Directory — before culminating in Napoleon Bonaparte's coup of 18 Brumaire (1799). The Revolution eliminated the feudal-aristocratic legal order, replaced it with bourgeois property rights and legal equality, and proclaimed the universalist principles of liberty, equality, and fraternity. Its international impact was immense: the Revolutionary and Napoleonic Wars exported its principles across Europe, permanently disrupting the continent's old regime systems.

## In the Manifesto

Marx and Engels invoke the French Revolution as the paradigmatic example of a revolutionary transformation of property relations. In Section II, they write: *"The French Revolution, for example, abolished feudal property in favour of bourgeois property"* [(002-010)]. This single sentence distills the materialist interpretation of the Revolution: not as a contest of abstract ideas or political forms, but as a definitive reorganization of the social relations of production — the expropriation of the feudal aristocracy and the consolidation of bourgeois [[private-property]] as the dominant form.

> The bourgeoisie, historically, has played a most revolutionary part. [(001-018)]

This famous declaration grounds the Manifesto's reading of 1789 within a broader historical logic: the bourgeoisie did not merely seize power — it transformed the entire social order. "The bourgeoisie cannot exist without constantly revolutionising the instruments of production, and thereby the relations of production, and with them the whole relations of society" [(001-024)].

### The Ideological Reception in Germany

In Section III.1, the Manifesto turns to the German reception of the French Revolution's ideas, offering a sharp materialist critique of ideological transplantation. Marx and Engels note that when French socialist and communist literature reached Germany, *"German philosophers, would-be philosophers, and beaux esprits, eagerly seized on this literature, only forgetting, that when these writings immigrated from France into Germany, French social conditions had not immigrated along with them"* [(004-018)]. Thus *"to the German philosophers of the eighteenth century, the demands of the first French Revolution were nothing more than the demands of 'Practical Reason' in general, and the utterance of the will of the revolutionary French bourgeoisie signified in their eyes the law of pure Will, of Will as it was bound to be, of true human Will generally"* [(004-023)].

> This process the German Socialists extolled as an advance of the "German science" over the "superficial" critical writings of the French. They were solely concerned in bringing the new French ideas into harmony with their own traditional philosophical standpoint. [(004-024)]

This passage is theoretically significant: Marx and Engels argue that the French Revolution's ideas cannot be understood in abstraction from the class that produced them. The revolutionary slogans of liberty and equality were not timeless truths but the ideological expression of a rising bourgeoisie overthrowing feudalism. The German '"True" Socialists' — who translated French socialist ideas into the language of Hegelian philosophy without importing the French social conditions that had produced them — thus performed what Marx and Engels regarded as a double mystification: stripping the French ideas of their revolutionary content while draping them in metaphysical abstraction.

### The Revolution as Model and Benchmark

The French Revolution also serves as a historical benchmark for the coming [[proletarian-revolution|proletarian revolution]]. In Section IV, Marx and Engels note that Germany is "on the eve of a bourgeois revolution" comparable to those of England in the seventeenth century and France in the eighteenth [(007-005)]. But unlike its predecessors, the German bourgeois revolution would occur under more advanced conditions, with a more developed proletariat, and would therefore be "but the prelude to an immediately following proletarian revolution" [(007-005)].

> The Communists turn their attention chiefly to Germany, because that country is on the eve of a bourgeois revolution that is bound to be carried out under more advanced conditions of European civilisation, and with a much more developed proletariat, than that of England was in the seventeenth, and of France in the eighteenth century, and because the bourgeois revolution in Germany will be but the prelude to an immediately following proletarian revolution. [(007-005)]

## Historical Background

The Revolution's causes were structural: a fiscal crisis of the absolutist state, the rigidity of the estate system (clergy, nobility, Third Estate), the aspirations of an economically ascendant bourgeoisie excluded from political power, and the immiseration of the peasantry. The Revolution abolished the seigneurial system on the night of August 4, 1789; promulgated the Declaration of the Rights of Man and of the Citizen; secularized Church lands; and eventually, under the Jacobin Committee of Public Safety (1793–1794), introduced universal male suffrage, price controls, and the revolutionary terror. The Thermidorian Reaction (1794) rolled back Jacobin radicalism, and the Directory (1795–1799) consolidated bourgeois republicanism before giving way to Napoleon's authoritarian state.

### The Ancien Regime and Feudal Crisis

The French Revolution was the historical resolution of a structural contradiction that the Manifesto describes in universal terms: "At a certain stage in the development of these means of production and of exchange... the feudal relations of property became no longer compatible with the already developed productive forces; they became so many fetters" [(001-023)]. In France, the bourgeoisie had grown wealthy through commerce, manufacture, and finance, yet remained politically subordinate to the feudal nobility and the absolutist monarchy — a situation the Manifesto traces in its account of how the bourgeoisie emerged from "the chartered burghers of the earliest towns" [(001-005)] to become the dominant economic force whose "conditions of existence" no longer corresponded to the feudal political order.

> Each step in the development of the bourgeoisie was accompanied by a corresponding political advance of that class. An oppressed class under the sway of the feudal nobility... the bourgeoisie has at last, since the establishment of Modern Industry and of the world-market, conquered for itself, in the modern representative State, exclusive political sway. [(001-013)]

### Revolutionary Phases and the Bourgeois Settlement

The Manifesto does not narrate the internal phases of the French Revolution, but its account of the character of bourgeois [[ruling-class|class rule]] provides the analytical framework for understanding each phase: the constitutional monarchy (1789–1792) as the bourgeoisie's initial bid for political power within existing forms; the Jacobin Republic (1793–1794) as the moment when the revolutionary bourgeoisie was forced to mobilize the sans-culottes — the propertyless urban masses who briefly pushed the revolution beyond bourgeois limits; the Thermidorian Reaction (1794) as the reassertion of bourgeois order against the plebeian threat; and the Directory (1795–1799), followed by Napoleon's 18 Brumaire, as the consolidation of the bourgeois state in its most concentrated, bureaucratic form. This trajectory illustrates the Manifesto's thesis that every bourgeois revolution, once having mobilized the [[proletariat]] and the dispossessed to destroy feudalism, must then contain and suppress the forces it has aroused.

## Marxist Analysis

For Marx and Engels, the French Revolution occupies a dual theoretical place. Positively, it was the great bourgeois revolution — the historical event that cleared away the feudal obstacles to capitalist development and established the political forms (representative government, legal equality, national unification) corresponding to the bourgeois mode of production. Negatively, it was a revolution that merely replaced one form of [[class-struggle|class rule]] with another: the revolutionary bourgeoisie, having mobilized the sans-culottes and the peasantry to destroy feudalism, consolidated its own class dictatorship and confronted the emerging proletariat as a new ruling class. The Manifesto's references to the French Revolution thus serve to illustrate its central historical thesis: every revolution in property relations is a class revolution, and the proletarian revolution — unlike the bourgeois one — will not merely substitute one exploiting class for another but will abolish class antagonisms altogether.

> The history of all hitherto existing societies is the history of class struggles. Freeman and slave, patrician and plebeian, lord and serf, guild-master and journeyman, in a word, oppressor and oppressed, stood in constant opposition to one another, carried on an uninterrupted, now hidden, now open fight, a fight that each time ended, either in a revolutionary re-constitution of society at large, or in the common ruin of the contending classes. [(001-003)]

### The French Revolution in the Manifesto's Revolutionary Typology

The Manifesto sketches an implicit typology of revolutions, within which the French Revolution occupies a specific and consequential place. The **bourgeois revolution** (1789) establishes capitalist property relations and the political supremacy of the bourgeoisie, sweeping away the feudal order but reproducing class rule in a new form. The **proletarian revolution** is distinguished from all past revolutions by its aim: "All previous historical movements were movements of minorities, or in the interest of minorities. The proletarian movement is the self-conscious, independent movement of the immense majority, in the interest of the immense majority" [(001-039)]. Where the French Revolution ended in the dictatorship of the bourgeoisie, the communists openly declare "that their ends can be attained only by the forcible overthrow of all existing social conditions" — and unlike 1789, the resulting revolution will not merely redistribute property between classes but abolish [[private-property|property]] as a class relation [(004-031)].

In Section IV, Marx and Engels note that Germany is "on the eve of a bourgeois revolution" comparable to those of England in the seventeenth century and France in the eighteenth [(007-005)]. The French Revolution thus serves as both a model and a historical benchmark against which the coming German revolution is measured. Yet it also functions as a warning: the [[revolutions-of-1848|Revolutions of 1848]] and the [[february-revolution-1848|February Revolution of 1848]] demonstrated that the bourgeoisie, once possessed of political power, would betray its radical allies and align with the forces of reaction rather than tolerate a genuinely democratic outcome. This pattern, established in 1789 and repeated in [[july-revolution-1830|1830]] and 1848, formed a central element of Marx and Engels' strategic thinking.

## Aftermath and Legacy

The French Revolution's legacy is contested and immense. It established the political lexicon of modern democracy — left and right, rights, nation, revolution itself — and demonstrated that mass collective action could overthrow a social order. For the labour movement, the Revolution was the inspiration for subsequent insurrectionary traditions (1830, 1848, the [[revolutions-of-1848|Paris Commune of 1871]]) while also serving as a cautionary tale about the bourgeois appropriation of popular revolution. Marx and Engels' lifelong engagement with the Revolution — from the early *German Ideology* through *The Eighteenth Brumaire of Louis Bonaparte* (1852) — shaped their theory of the [[state]], of revolution, and of the relationship between class forces and political forms.

### The Revolution's Echo: 1789 in Later Revolutionary Movements

The French Revolution did not remain confined to France. The Manifesto opens with the image of "the spectre of Communism" haunting all the "Powers of old Europe" — "Pope and Czar, Metternich and Guizot, French Radicals and German police-spies" [(P01-001)] — and this opening inverts the earlier spectre that had haunted the European old regime after 1789: the spectre of revolution itself. The Holy Alliance of 1815 had been forged precisely to contain the revolutionary contagion that France had unleashed. By 1848, the bourgeoisie of Europe, having once wielded revolutionary slogans against the feudal nobility, now confronted a new spectre — the [[communism|communist]] movement — and found itself repeating the pattern of its French predecessor: first rousing the masses against the old order, then recoiling in fear from the forces it had set in motion.

> In the French revolution of July 1830, and in the English reform agitation, these aristocracies again succumbed to the hateful upstart. Henceforth, a serious political contest was altogether out of the question. A literary battle alone remained possible. [(004-002)]

The French Revolution thus stands at the origin of a recurring dynamic: the dialectic in which each revolutionary wave summons forth a counter-wave, and each bourgeois revolution contains within itself the seeds of the proletarian revolution that will succeed it. This dialectic, theorized in the Manifesto and illustrated by the arc from 1789 through 1830 and 1848 to the 1871 [[revolutions-of-1848|Commune]], is one of the constitutive structures of modern political history.
