# State

## Definition

The State, in the Manifesto's analysis, is not a neutral arbiter standing above class antagonisms but the organised political instrument through which the ruling class exercises its domination. Marx and Engels define the State functionally: it is the executive committee of the bourgeoisie, the concentrated expression of class power, and the apparatus that transforms economic domination into legal and political authority. The State is not an independent entity with interests of its own — it is, in the most famous formulation of the Manifesto, "but a committee for managing the common affairs of the whole bourgeoisie" (001-011).

> The executive of the modern State is but a committee for managing the common affairs of the whole bourgeoisie. (001-011)

## In the Manifesto

The Manifesto's treatment of the State unfolds across three registers: the State as product of bourgeois revolution, the State as instrument of class rule, and the State as target of proletarian conquest.

### The State as Product of Bourgeois Revolution

In Ch. I, Marx and Engels describe how the bourgeoisie's rise to economic dominance was "accompanied by a corresponding political advance" (001-011). The modern representative State emerged as the political form appropriate to capitalist production — the feudal patchwork of estates, chartered towns, and local jurisdictions giving way to a unified national administration.

> Independent, or but loosely connected provinces, with separate interests, laws, governments, and systems of taxation, became lumped together into one nation, with one government, one code of laws, one national class-interest, one frontier, and one customs-tariff. (001-022)

Political centralisation was the "necessary consequence" of economic concentration (001-022). The [[bourgeoisie]], "during its rule of scarce one hundred years, has created more massive and more colossal productive forces than have all preceding generations together" (001-022). This transformation of scattered feudal jurisdictions into a unified national State was not a matter of administrative convenience — it was the political expression of a new [[mode-of-production|mode of production]] requiring uniform markets, uniform laws, and uniform administration.

### The State as Instrument of Class Rule

But the State is not merely a neutral administrative apparatus. The Manifesto's most quoted line on the subject is unambiguous: "The executive of the modern State is but a committee for managing the common affairs of the whole bourgeoisie" (001-011). This does not mean every ministerial decision directly serves the interests of individual capitalists — it means the State as a whole is structurally oriented toward maintaining bourgeois [[private-property|property relations]] and suppressing challenges to them.

The class character of the State is made brutally concrete in the description of factory life: "Not only are they slaves of the bourgeois class, and of the bourgeois State; they are daily and hourly enslaved by the machine, by the over-looker, and, above all, by the individual bourgeois manufacturer himself" (001-030). The State here is not an abstraction — it is the armed enforcer of a regime in which workers are [[exploitation|exploited]] at the point of production and suppressed by law and police when they resist.

The State's class character is also evident in the Communist programme's transitional demands. The proletariat, having seized power, will use the State apparatus to:

> centralise all instruments of production in the hands of the State, *i.e*., of the proletariat organised as the ruling class; and to increase the total of productive forces as rapidly as possible. (002-068)

The specific measures — "centralisation of credit in the hands of the State" (002-076), "centralisation of the means of communication and transport in the hands of the State" (002-077), "extension of factories and instruments of production owned by the State" (002-078) — reveal the State as nothing more than the administrative arm of whichever class controls it. Under the bourgeoisie it administers the common affairs of [[capital|capital]]; under the [[proletariat]] it administers the expropriation of capital.

### The State as Target of Revolutionary Conquest

The immediate aim of the Communists is "the conquest of political power by the proletariat" (002-034) — the proletariat must "raise itself to the position of ruling class" and "centralise all instruments of production in the hands of the State" (002-068). This transitional workers' State — what Marx would later call the dictatorship of the proletariat — is the mechanism by which the expropriators are expropriated and the conditions for class rule are abolished.

> The proletariat will use its political supremacy to wrest, by degrees, all capital from the bourgeoisie, to centralise all instruments of production in the hands of the State, *i.e*., of the proletariat organised as the ruling class. (002-068)

The conquest of the State is not an end in itself. The Communist programme envisions measures — "abolition of property in land" (002-072), "a heavy progressive or graduated income tax" (002-073), "abolition of all right of inheritance" (002-074), "confiscation of the property of all emigrants and rebels" (002-075) — that systematically dismantle the economic base of class society. These "despotic inroads on the rights of property" (002-069) are necessary precisely because the bourgeois State has spent its entire existence protecting those same rights.

### The Withering of the State

Only when classes have been abolished does the State lose its political character:

> When, in the course of development, class distinctions have disappeared, and all production has been concentrated in the hands of a vast association of the whole nation, the public power will lose its political character. Political power, properly so called, is merely the organised power of one class for oppressing another. (002-082)

The State does not endure forever — it withers away when the class antagonism that generated it has been overcome. "If the proletariat during its contest with the bourgeoisie is compelled, by the force of circumstances, to organise itself as a class, if, by means of a revolution, it makes itself the ruling class, and, as such, sweeps away by force the old conditions of production, then it will, along with these conditions, have swept away the conditions for the existence of class antagonisms and of classes generally, and will thereby have abolished its own supremacy as a class" (002-082). The State is thus a historically specific institution — born with [[class-struggle|class antagonism]], destined to perish with it.

## Historical Context

The Manifesto's theory of the State was forged in polemical opposition to two dominant views: the Hegelian conception of the State as the embodiment of ethical life and universal reason, and the liberal view of the State as a neutral guarantor of individual rights. Marx, who had already broken with Hegel's philosophy of the State in his 1843 *Critique of Hegel's Philosophy of Right*, argued that the State was not the realisation of freedom but the political expression of civil society's contradictions. The Manifesto condensed this earlier critique into a weapon for political agitation — the State was not to be perfected or reformed but conquered and ultimately abolished.

The 1840s provided ample empirical evidence for this view. Across Europe, states repeatedly intervened to suppress workers' organisations, enforce [[private-property|property rights]] against popular demands, and protect the interests of industrial and financial [[capital|capital]]. The French July Monarchy (1830–1848) was a particularly egregious example — a state explicitly designed to serve the interests of the financial aristocracy. When the [[revolutions-of-1848|revolutions of 1848]] broke out, the question of state power moved from theory to immediate practical contestation.

### The State in the Communist Programme

The ten-point programme of Ch. II reveals the practical scope of State power as conceived in the Manifesto. Measures 5 through 7 explicitly place key economic functions in State hands. The transitional State envisaged here is not a minimal night-watchman but an active agent of economic transformation — nationalising credit (002-076), transport (002-077), and extending public ownership of factories and land (002-078). This programme anticipates the twentieth-century debates about nationalisation, planning, and the role of the State in economic development that would divide the socialist movement.

## Related Concepts

The State is the institutional form of [[political-power]] — "the organised power of one class for oppressing another" (002-082) — and the Manifesto treats the two concepts as inseparable. The State's class character is defined by its relationship to the [[ruling-class]]: the class that controls the means of material production thereby controls the means of political and intellectual production. [[centralisation]] is the historical process by which the modern State concentrates scattered jurisdictions and powers into a unified national apparatus, a process driven by the bourgeoisie's need for uniform markets and administration. The State stands in opposition to the [[proletariat]], whose revolutionary task is to seize State power, transform it into an instrument for abolishing class distinctions, and thereby create the conditions for the State's own dissolution.

The State is also intimately connected to [[private-property]] — it is the legal and coercive apparatus that guarantees bourgeois property relations. The [[bourgeoisie]] conquered the State precisely to secure the property order upon which its existence depends. The State's relationship to [[class-struggle]] is dialectical: it is both the product of class struggle (the political form won by the victorious bourgeoisie) and its object (the prize for which the proletariat contends). [[proletarian-revolution]] is the process by which the proletariat seizes State power, and [[emancipation]] — human, not merely political — is the goal toward which the conquered State is wielded and ultimately dissolved.

## Significance

The Manifesto's theory of the State has been one of the most contested elements of Marxist theory. Engels later elaborated the thesis in *The Origin of the Family, Private Property and the State* (1884), arguing that the State arose historically from the need to contain class antagonisms and would wither away when those antagonisms disappeared. Lenin, in *The State and Revolution* (1917), radicalised the Manifesto's argument into a theory of the smashability of the bourgeois State machine and its replacement by the Soviets as a transitional workers' State. The question of whether the State can be reformed from within or must be smashed from without remains a central dividing line between [[reform|social-democratic]] and [[revolution|revolutionary]] Marxist traditions — a debate that traces directly back to the Manifesto's uncompromising formulation.

Beyond the strategic debate, the Manifesto's functionalist definition of the State — as the executive committee of the ruling class — has proven remarkably durable as an analytical tool. It illuminates phenomena from the role of the State in colonial accumulation to the [[february-revolution-1848|February Revolution of 1848]], from the suppression of the [[chartists|Chartist]] movement in England to the police repression of [[association|trade unions]] across Europe. The insight that legal and political forms are inseparable from class content remains a cornerstone of critical political analysis.
