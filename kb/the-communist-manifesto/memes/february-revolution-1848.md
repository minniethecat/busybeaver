> In France the Communists ally themselves with the Social-Democrats, against the conservative and radical bourgeoisie. [(007-002)]

> The Communists fight for the attainment of the immediate aims, for the enforcement of the momentary interests of the working class; but in the movement of the present, they also represent and take care of the future of that movement. [(007-002)]

The **February Revolution of 1848** was the spark that ignited the European revolutionary conflagration of 1848. In three days — February 22–24 — the July Monarchy of King Louis-Philippe collapsed, the Second French Republic was proclaimed, and a Provisional Government that included representatives of the organized working class took power. The *Manifesto of the Communist Party* was published in London in the same month: its analysis of the dynamics of bourgeois revolution and proletarian insurgency was, in effect, submitted to the verdict of history almost immediately.

> The Communists everywhere support every revolutionary movement against the existing social and political order of things. [(007-008)]

## The Fall of the July Monarchy

### Causes

The July Monarchy (1830–1848) was a regime of the *haute bourgeoisie* — "not the French bourgeoisie," Marx wrote, "but a fraction of it: the bankers, the stock-exchange kings, the railway kings, the owners of coal and iron mines and forests, a section of the landed aristocracy allied to them — the so-called *financial aristocracy*" (*The Class Struggles in France*). Under the doctrinaire liberal François Guizot, premier from 1840 to 1848, the regime's policy was summed up in his notorious advice to the disenfranchised: "Enrichissez-vous" (Enrich yourselves).

The regime refused to extend the suffrage — only about 250,000 of the wealthiest men (less than 1% of the population) could vote. It repressed workers' organizations (the Le Chapelier Law of 1791, banning trade associations, remained in force). Its foreign policy was subservient to Britain (the *entente cordiale*), alienating nationalist sentiment. The economic crisis of 1846–1847 — poor harvests, the potato blight, a financial panic — radicalized both the urban working class and the peasantry.

> The bourgeoisie, historically, has played a most revolutionary part. [(001-012)]

### The Banquet Campaign

The immediate precipitant was the "banquet campaign" of 1847–1848. Barred from holding political meetings, the opposition organized a series of reform banquets across France — ostensibly private dinners where toasts were raised to electoral reform and universal suffrage. The campaign was led by the "dynastic left" (Odilon Barrot) and the republican left (Alexandre Ledru-Rollin, [[louis-blanc|Louis Blanc]]). When Guizot banned a major banquet scheduled for February 22, 1848 in Paris, it became the trigger for revolution.

### The Three Days

**February 22.** Students and workers gathered in the streets of Paris. Skirmishes with the Municipal Guard began. Barricades started to appear in the working-class eastern districts.

**February 23.** The situation escalated. The National Guard — the bourgeois citizens' militia — fraternized with the demonstrators rather than suppressing them, chanting "Vive la Réforme!" The king dismissed Guizot, but the gesture came too late. That evening, soldiers of the 14th Line Regiment fired on a crowd outside the Ministry of Foreign Affairs on the Boulevard des Capucines, killing between 20 and 50 people. The bodies were loaded onto a cart and paraded through the streets by torchlight — a scene that transformed a political protest into an insurrection.

**February 24.** Paris rose. Barricades blocked the streets. The insurgents advanced on the Tuileries Palace. Louis-Philippe, seeing that the army and National Guard would not fight for him, abdicated in favour of his grandson and fled to England. The Chamber of Deputies attempted to establish a regency, but a crowd invaded the chamber. A Provisional Government was proclaimed at the Hôtel de Ville (City Hall), composed of moderate republicans (Lamartine, Ledru-Rollin) and socialists (Louis Blanc, Albert).

> The executive of the modern State is but a committee for managing the common affairs of the whole bourgeoisie. [(001-011)]

## Proclamation of the Republic and the "Right to Work"

The Provisional Government proclaimed the Republic on February 25 and immediately enacted a series of measures unprecedented in modern European history:

- **Universal male suffrage** — the electorate expanded from 250,000 to over 9 million men.
- **Abolition of slavery** in all French colonies (April 27, 1848, through the efforts of Victor Schœlcher).
- **Freedom of the press and assembly** — political clubs proliferated; hundreds of newspapers appeared.
- **The "right to work"** (*droit au travail*) — the government guaranteed employment to all citizens and established **National Workshops** (*Ateliers Nationaux*) to provide it.
- **The Luxembourg Commission** — a body chaired by Louis Blanc and Albert to investigate "the labour question" and propose social reforms. It became a forum for workers' delegates to voice grievances and a symbol of the "social republic."

The National Workshops, however, became a point of bitter contention. The moderate republicans, who controlled the Ministry of Public Works, deliberately sabotaged them — assigning workers to pointless earth-moving projects and paying below-subsistence wages — to discredit Blanc's vision of state-supported producers' cooperatives. The Workshops enrolled over 100,000 workers by June 1848, making them the largest concentration of organized (and increasingly radicalized) workers in Europe.

> The proletarian movement is the self-conscious, independent movement of the immense majority, in the interest of the immense majority. [(001-039)]

## The June Days and the Counter-Revolution

The Provisional Government's April 1848 elections to the Constituent Assembly produced a massive conservative majority — the peasantry, voting for the first time, largely supported the notables and priests who had always dominated rural life. The new government moved to close the National Workshops. On June 21, it announced that workers aged 18–25 would be offered enlistment in the army and the rest would be sent to drain swamps in the provinces.

The response was the **June Days** (June 23–26, 1848): the first great independent insurrection of the modern industrial proletariat. Between 40,000 and 50,000 workers — unled, without a political organization, fighting for "the social republic" — built barricades across eastern Paris. The government declared a state of siege and handed full military authority to General Louis-Eugène Cavaignac. After four days of fighting — the bloodiest class confrontation in 19th-century European history before the Paris Commune of 1871 — the insurrection was crushed. Approximately 3,000 insurgents were summarily executed or killed in combat; another 15,000 were imprisoned, transported to Algeria, or exiled.

For Marx and Engels, the June Days were the empirical vindication of the Manifesto's central thesis. The bourgeoisie had proved willing to massacre the proletariat to defend property. The "democratic republic" had revealed itself as "the perfected form of bourgeois rule." The Manifesto's insistence that the proletariat must "conquer political power" itself [(002-007)] — not rely on bourgeois republican allies — was written in the blood of the June insurgents.

> The immediate aim of the Communist is the same as that of all the other proletarian parties: formation of the proletariat into a class, overthrow of the bourgeois supremacy, conquest of political power by the proletariat. [(002-007)]

> All previous historical movements were movements of minorities, or in the interest of minorities. The proletarian movement is the self-conscious, independent movement of the immense majority, in the interest of the immense majority. [(001-046)]

## The Road to Bonapartism

The repression of June cleared the path for Louis-Napoléon Bonaparte. In December 1848, capitalizing on the peasantry's attachment to the Napoleonic legend, the bourgeoisie's fear of "anarchy," and the workers' disenfranchisement (many were still imprisoned or in exile), Bonaparte won the presidential election with 5.5 million votes (74%). In December 1851, he staged a coup d'état, dissolving the Assembly and establishing a dictatorship that was ratified by plebiscite. In December 1852, the Second Republic became the Second Empire, with Louis-Napoléon as Emperor Napoleon III. The trajectory — February (proletarian-socialist republic) to June (bourgeois republic) to December 1851 (dictatorship) — became, for Marx, the classic pattern of bourgeois revolution in its decline, analyzed in *The Eighteenth Brumaire of Louis Bonaparte* (1852).

> The bourgeoisie finds itself involved in a constant battle. At first with the aristocracy; later on, with those portions of the bourgeoisie itself, whose interests have become antagonistic to the progress of industry; at all times, with the bourgeoisie of foreign countries. In all these battles it sees itself compelled to appeal to the proletariat, to ask for its help, and thus, to drag it into the political arena. [(001-039)]

## Significance

The February Revolution encapsulates the dialectic that the Manifesto theorizes. Its first phase — the overthrow of the monarchy, the proclamation of the republic, universal suffrage, the right to work — was the most radical political transformation in Europe since 1789. Its second phase — the June Days, the Bonapartist reaction — demonstrated that radical political transformation is not the same as social emancipation. The republic that abolished slavery and proclaimed the right to work was the same republic that massacred the workers who demanded that the right be made real.

This is the Manifesto's strategic lesson in concrete form: the Communists support every democratic revolution against absolutism, "reserving, however, the right to take up a critical position" [(007-002)] — because the democratic revolution, carried through by the bourgeoisie, stops at the threshold of property. The tasks that the February Revolution initiated — the overthrow of monarchy, the democratization of the state, the emancipation of labour — could not be completed within the framework of bourgeois republicanism. Only the proletarian revolution could complete them — but the proletariat, in February 1848, was not yet prepared for that task. By June, it had learned.

> The Communists disdain to conceal their views and aims. They openly declare that their ends can be attained only by the forcible overthrow of all existing social conditions. Let the ruling classes tremble at a Communistic revolution. The proletarians have nothing to lose but their chains. They have a world to win. [(007-011)]

> The development of Modern Industry, therefore, cuts from under its feet the very foundation on which the bourgeoisie produces and appropriates products. What the bourgeoisie, therefore, produces, above all, is its own grave-diggers. Its fall and the victory of the proletariat are equally inevitable. [(001-050)]

> The history of all hitherto existing societies is the history of class struggles. [(001-001)]

## Further Reading

- [[karl-marx|Karl Marx]], *The Class Struggles in France, 1848–1850* (1850) — Marx's contemporary analysis of the revolution, the June Days, and the rise of Bonapartism.
- [[karl-marx|Karl Marx]], *The Eighteenth Brumaire of Louis Bonaparte* (1852) — The definitive Marxist analysis of the 1848–1851 crisis.
- Alexis de Tocqueville, *Recollections* (1893, posthumous) — Tocqueville's first-hand account as a member of the Constituent Assembly, providing the liberal counter-perspective.
