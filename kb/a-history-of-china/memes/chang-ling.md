## Overview

[[chang-ling|Chang Ling]] (fl. second century A.D.), also known as Chang Tao-ling, was the founder of the "Way of the Five Pecks of Rice" (Wu-tou-mi Tao) — the earliest organised Taoist church in Chinese history. Operating in the mountainous region of modern Szechwan during the declining decades of the Later Han, he established a theocratic community that combined faith-healing, communal organisation, and millenarian expectation. His movement, which administered its own territories independently of the Han imperial administration, provided the template for subsequent Taoist religious institutions and directly influenced the ideology of the great peasant rebellions — including the [[yellow-turban-rebellion|Yellow Turban Rebellion]] — that would soon engulf the dynasty [020-109][027-181].

## Founding the Taoist Church

Chang Ling emerged in the context of mounting social crisis in the second-century Later Han. The imperial state was losing its grip on the provinces; banditry, famine, and epidemic disease were widespread. In eastern Szechwan, Chang Ling began preaching a message of spiritual healing and moral regeneration, claiming to have received a revelation from the deified [[lao-tzu|Lao Tzu]], who appointed him as his earthly representative — the "Celestial Master" (T'ien-shih) [020-109].

> The leaders of the movement, Chang Ling and others, were members of a secret society that claimed supernatural authority. [020-109]

His church was not merely a spiritual movement but a comprehensive social organisation. Adherents contributed five pecks of rice annually — hence the movement's name — which supported a priestly hierarchy and a rudimentary welfare system. The church established its own administrative districts, replacing imperial officials with "libationers" (chi-chiu) who combined priestly, judicial, and civil functions [027-181].

> The church-Taoism of Chang Ling developed alongside popular Taoism and remained distinct from the philosophical Taoism of the gentry. [027-181]

## Distinction from Philosophical Taoism

It is essential to distinguish the church-[[taoism|Taoism]] of Chang Ling from the philosophical [[taoism|Taoism]] of [[lao-tzu|Lao Tzu]] and [[chuang-tzu|Chuang Tzu]] that had developed among the Warring States gentry. While the gentry Taoists sought individual spiritual liberation through quietism and withdrawal from public life, Chang Ling's movement was collective, institutional, and politically engaged. Its followers expected the imminent end of the current world order and the establishment of a utopian "Great Peace" (T'ai-p'ing) — a vision that had obvious subversive implications in a crumbling empire [027-181][030-038].

> Some Buddhists remained faithful; others joined the Church of Chang Ling's Taoism. [030-038]

The millenarian strain in Chang Ling's teaching proved contagious. Within decades of his death, related Taoist movements had spread across northern and eastern China, culminating in the great [[yellow-turban-rebellion|Yellow Turban Rebellion]] of 184 — a mass uprising that, though suppressed, fatally weakened the Later Han state and ushered in the era of the [[three-kingdoms|Three Kingdoms]]. Chang Ling's own church in Szechwan survived the collapse of the Han, maintaining a semi-autonomous existence under the leadership of his son Chang Heng and grandson Chang Lu [020-109][027-181].

## Legacy

Chang Ling's lasting achievement was to give [[taoism|Taoism]] an institutional form that could survive the rise and fall of dynasties. The Celestial Masters (T'ien-shih) church that he founded continued as a recognised religious institution through the entire imperial period, and its priesthood — hereditary within the Chang family — maintained a continuous line of descent that, according to tradition, persists to the present day. The integration of communal ritual, moral teaching, and millenarian expectation that he pioneered became permanent features of Chinese popular religion [074-277].
