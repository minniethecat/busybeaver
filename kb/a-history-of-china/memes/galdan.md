## Overview

[[galdan|Galdan]] was a [[mongol|Mongol]] prince of the late seventeenth century who led an insurgency against the newly established [[qing-dynasty|Manchu (Qing) dynasty]]. As the Manchu court rapidly sinicized under Chinese cultural influence, Galdan attempted to establish an independent Mongol realm free from both Chinese and Manchu domination. His rebellion provoked a series of personal military campaigns by the [[kang-hsi|K'ang-hsi]] Emperor (1690–1696), which ended in Galdan's defeat and the consolidation of Qing control over Mongolia.

## Background: Mongol Alienation from the Manchus

The [[mongol|Mongols]] had initially been willing supporters of the Manchus during the Manchu conquest of China, when there was "plenty of booty" to be gained from raiding [056-003] [056-004]. But the relationship soured as the Manchu court underwent rapid sinification under the influence of the Chinese gentry.

> Now, however, the Manchus, under the influence of the Chinese gentry whom they brought, and could not but bring, to their court, were rapidly becoming Chinese in respect to culture. Even in the time of K'ang-hsi the Manchus began to forget Manchurian. [056-005] [056-006] [056-007] [056-008]

The alienation deepened as the Manchu elite lost their steppe identity. Eberhard reports that "later even the emperors did not understand Manchurian," and as a result, "the [[mongol|Mongols]] became alienated from the Manchurians, and the situation began once more to be the same as at the time of the Ming rulers" [056-009] [056-010] [056-011].

## Galdan's Rebellion

Galdan was "a quite different type of insurgent commander" — not a Chinese rebel but a Mongol prince who sought to reassert steppe independence [056-001]. His goal was clear: to found an independent Mongol realm entirely free from Chinese influence.

> Thus Galdan tried to found an independent Mongol realm, free from Chinese influence. [056-012] [056-013]

The Manchus could not tolerate this. An independent Mongol state would threaten the flank of their homeland, Manchuria, and would attract those Manchus who objected to the court's sinification. The strategic stakes were existential.

> The Manchus could not permit this, as such a realm would have threatened the flank of their homeland, Manchuria, and would have attracted those Manchus who objected to sinification. [056-014] [056-015] [056-016]

## The Kang-hsi Emperor's Campaigns

Between 1690 and 1696, the [[kang-hsi|K'ang-hsi]] Emperor waged a series of military campaigns against Galdan, in which the emperor himself took part in battle — a mark of the seriousness with which the Qing court regarded the Mongol threat.

> Between 1690 and 1696 there were battles, in which the emperor actually took part in person. Galdan was defeated. [056-016] [056-017] [056-018]

## Aftermath and the Dzungar Wars

Galdan's defeat did not end Mongol resistance. In 1715, new disturbances broke out in western Mongolia. [[tsewang-rabdan|Tsewang Rabdan]], whom the Chinese had made khan of the Ölöt (Dzungars), rose against them. The subsequent wars extended far into Turkestan, involving both the Turkish population and the Dzungars, and ultimately led to the Chinese conquest of all Mongolia and parts of eastern Turkestan.

> Tsewang Rabdan, whom the Chinese had made khan of the Ölöt, rose against the Chinese. The wars that followed, extending far into Turkestan and also involving its Turkish population together with the Dzungars, ended with the Chinese conquest of the whole of Mongolia and of parts of eastern Turkestan. [056-019] [056-020] [056-021] [056-022]

Galdan's rebellion thus proved to be the opening chapter of a longer struggle — the Qing conquest of Inner Asia — that would define China's northwestern frontier for the next century.
