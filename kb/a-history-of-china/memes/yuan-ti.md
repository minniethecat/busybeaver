## Overview

The imperial name [[yuan-ti|Yüan Ti]] was borne by multiple Chinese emperors, of whom two are historically significant. The Han dynasty Yüan Ti (r. 48–32 BCE) was the emperor whose widow initiated the chain of events leading to [[wang-mang|Wang Mang]]'s usurpation. The Eastern Chin Yüan Ti (r. 317–323 CE) was the founder of the Eastern Chin dynasty, who re-established Chinese imperial rule in the south after the [[hsiung-nu|Hu]]n conquest of the north.

## Han Yüan Ti and the Fall of the Former Han

The Han emperor Yüan Ti died leaving a power vacuum that his widow immediately filled. She placed her brothers — and critically, her nephew [[wang-mang|Wang Mang]] — in all the principal government posts.

> The beginning of its fall came with the activities of the widow of the emperor Yüan Ti. She virtually ruled in the name of her eighteen-year-old son, the emperor Ch'eng Ti (32–7 B.C.), and placed all her brothers, and also her nephew, Wang Mang, in the principal government posts. [018-133] [018-134] [018-135] [018-136]

Eberhard identifies this moment as the beginning of the end for the Former Han dynasty. The Wang family consolidated power so effectively that [[wang-mang|Wang Mang]] would eventually seize the throne outright in 9 CE, establishing the short-lived Hsin dynasty. The Yüan Ti succession crisis thus directly precipitated one of the most dramatic dynastic ruptures in Chinese history.

## Eastern Chin Yüan Ti: Founding a Southern Dynasty

In 317 CE, [[hsiung-nu|Huns]] destroyed the Western Chin dynasty in the north. A Chin prince who would not normally have inherited the throne seized the moment: he declared himself emperor under the name Yüan Ti and founded the Eastern Chin dynasty (317–419), with his capital near present-day Nanking.

> When in 317 the Huns had destroyed the Chinese Chin dynasty in the north, a Chin prince who normally would not have become heir to the throne declared himself, under the name Yüan Ti, the first emperor of the "Eastern Chin dynasty" (317–419). [031-041] [031-042] [031-043] [031-044]

Countless members of the Chinese gentry fled south from the [[hsiung-nu|Huns]] at this time. Eberhard notes they did so "not out of loyalty to the Chinese dynasty or out of national feeling, but because they saw" better opportunities for survival and status under a Chinese regime [031-047] [031-048].

## Challenges as Emperor

Unlike later Eastern Chin rulers, Yüan Ti possessed some personal and political qualities. He had arrived in the south earlier than most of the northern émigrés and had established some independent footing. Yet even he was soon caught in the web of gentry factionalism.

> With the exception of the founder of the state, Yüan Ti, who had come rather earlier, they belonged to the group of the new immigrants, and so had no firm footing and were therefore caught at once in the net of the newly re-grouping gentry class. [032-005] [032-006] [032-007] [032-008]

Yüan Ti lived to see the first great military challenge to his dynasty: the rebellion of [[wang-tun|Wang Tun]], which began in the region of present-day Hankow — already one of the most important regions in China.

> The emperor Yüan Ti lived to see the first great rising. This rising (under Wang Tun) started in the region of the present Hankow, a region that today is one of the most important in China. [032-010] [032-011]

## Legacy

The two Yüan Ti emperors bookend a crucial arc in Chinese history: one inadvertently set the stage for the fall of the first great unified empire, while the other founded a refugee dynasty that preserved Chinese imperial institutions through three centuries of division. The Eastern Chin Yüan Ti's decision to claim the throne in 317 ensured that a Chinese imperial state survived south of the [[yangtze-river|Yangtze]], providing the template for the Southern Dynasties that followed.
