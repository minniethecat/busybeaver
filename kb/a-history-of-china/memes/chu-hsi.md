## Overview

Chu Hsi (Chinese: 朱熹, 1130–1200) was the most influential Chinese philosopher of the second millennium. A scholar of the [[sung-dynasty|Sung dynasty]], he synthesized the diverse strands of [[confucianism|Confucian]] thought into a comprehensive philosophical system that became the official orthodoxy of the imperial state and the basis of the [[examination-system|civil service examinations]] from the fourteenth century until their abolition in 1905. Eberhard describes him as "the famous scholar who gave [[confucianism|Confucianism]] its final form" [044-029].

Chu Hsi's philosophy, known in the West as [[confucianism|Neo-Confucianism]], integrated metaphysical and cosmological elements drawn from [[taoism|Taoism]] and [[buddhism|Buddhism]] into the Confucian ethical framework. His central concept was _li_ (理, "principle" or "pattern"), the rational order underlying all phenomena, which he distinguished from _ch'i_ (氣, "material force"), the substance through which principle manifests. This dualistic framework provided a philosophical foundation for Confucian moral cultivation and social ethics.

His commentaries on the Confucian classics — particularly the _Four Books_ (_Analects_, _[[mencius|Mencius]]_, _Great Learning_, _Doctrine of the Mean_) — became the standard texts for the imperial civil service examinations, shaping the education of China's governing elite for over six hundred years [044-041].

## Life and Background

Chu Hsi was born in 1130 in what is now Fukien province. His father was a [[gentry|scholar-official]] who opposed the government's appeasement policy toward the Jurchen invaders from the north. After his father's death, Chu Hsi studied under several prominent scholars of the time, immersing himself in Confucian, Buddhist, and Taoist texts before ultimately committing himself to the Confucian tradition.

Despite passing the civil service examinations with distinction at a young age, Chu Hsi spent much of his career alternating between minor official posts and periods of scholarly retirement. His political career was marked by principled stands against corruption and advocacy for reform, which often put him at odds with powerful court factions. In his later years, his teachings were briefly proscribed as "false learning," but soon after his death they were rehabilitated and elevated to official status.

## Teachings and Philosophy

> "Chu Hsi (1130-1200), the famous scholar who gave Confucianism its final form, appears repeatedly in European writings. He gave to the 'laws' the interpretation that laws are the principles by which man has to live, principles given perfect expression by Chu Hsi, who also composed commentaries on all the chief historical works. Chu Hsi's commentaries became standard works for centuries." [044-041]

Chu Hsi's philosophical system rested on several foundational concepts:

- **Li and Ch'i** (principle and material force): Chu Hsi taught that every thing in the universe possesses both _li_ (its rational principle or pattern) and _ch'i_ (the material force through which it becomes actual). The _li_ of all things is ultimately one — the Supreme Ultimate (T'ai-chi) — but manifests differently in each particular entity.
- **The Investigation of Things** (kê-wu): Chu Hsi held that moral and intellectual cultivation required the systematic investigation of the principles inherent in things — from classic texts to natural phenomena. Through this "investigation," the mind could overcome the obscuring effects of impure _ch'i_ and attain clarity.
- **Self-cultivation**: The purpose of philosophical inquiry was not abstract knowledge but moral self-transformation. Chu Hsi's program emphasized "abiding in reverence" (chü-ching) — a state of focused attentiveness — as the practical method of cultivation.

His explanations of the classics became the standard interpretation across East Asia, influencing thought not only in China but also in Korea, Japan, and Vietnam [056-094]. The Chu Hsi school's emphasis on rational inquiry and textual study, however, was later challenged by the more intuitive approach of [[wang-yang-ming|Wang Yang-ming]]'s "School of Mind."

## Political Vision

Chu Hsi's philosophy carried direct political implications. He believed that the ruler, like every individual, must cultivate his moral nature and govern through principled understanding rather than mere expediency. This placed him in the reformist tradition of the Sung, though his approach differed substantially from the state-activist program of [[wang-an-shih|Wang An-shih]].

> "Eberhard notes that Chu Hsi gave a specific interpretation to the concept of law, treating laws as the rational principles by which both nature and human society are governed." [044-041]

Where [[wang-an-shih|Wang An-shih]] sought to reform institutions through state action, Chu Hsi emphasized the reform of the individual as the prerequisite for social and political improvement. His stress on moral self-cultivation as the foundation of good governance became a hallmark of Confucian political thought in later imperial China.

Chu Hsi also contributed to historical scholarship, composing commentaries on the chief historical works and establishing a framework for understanding Chinese history as a moral drama of dynastic rise and decline [044-042]. His historical interpretations reinforced the conservative tendency in later Confucian thought, emphasizing the normative patterns of the past as guides for the present.

## Influence and Legacy

Chu Hsi's influence on Chinese civilization can hardly be overstated. From 1313, when the Mongol [[yuan-dynasty|Yüan dynasty]] adopted his commentaries as the basis for the civil service examinations, until 1905, when the [[examination-system|examination system]] was abolished, his interpretation of the Confucian canon defined the intellectual formation of China's governing class.

In the [[ming-dynasty|Ming]] and [[ching-dynasty|Ch'ing]] periods, the Chu Hsi school represented the conservative, scholarly tendency within [[confucianism|Confucianism]] [053-057]. His thought also spread to Korea, where it became the dominant philosophical school (Sŏngnihak), and to Japan, where it influenced Tokugawa-era political philosophy.

Modern scholarship continues to engage with Chu Hsi's work. Eberhard notes that "a comprehensive study of Chu Hsi is a great desideratum" — a judgment that remains relevant despite significant advances in Neo-Confucian studies since his time [071-135]. His synthesis of metaphysical depth with ethical rigor represents one of the highest achievements of Chinese philosophy.
