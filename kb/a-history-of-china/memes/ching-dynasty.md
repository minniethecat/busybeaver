## Overview

The Ch'ing dynasty (1644-1911) was China's last imperial dynasty. Founded by the Manchu, it was called Tai Ch'ing (054-008). It ruled from Peking for 267 years after the [[ming-dynasty]] fell.

> The Manchus had gained the mastery over China owing rather to China's internal situation than to their military superiority. (054-129)

Under the Ch'ing, China reached its greatest territorial extent. The empire stretched across Mongolia, Tibet, Turkestan, and Taiwan. The dynasty combined Chinese administration with Manchu identity (056-005).

## Founding and Rise

The conquest began as the [[ming-dynasty]] collapsed. Nurhachu unified the Manchu tribes and controlled Manchuria by 1618. His successors built the Banner Organization, turning tribes into a military state (054-083).

In 1644, rebel Li Tzu-ch'eng seized Peking. The last Ming emperor killed himself. General Wu San-kui allied with the Manchus, entering Peking on June 6th, 1644 (054-121).

The Manchus subdued the Yangtze region by 1645. The last Ming resistance fell in 1681. The Chinese gentry ultimately collaborated under tolerable conditions (054-170).

## Government and Administration

The dynasty ran a dual government: Manchus and Chinese shared every office. Manchus held superior rank, garrisoned in cities on state pensions (054-143).

Chinese were forced to wear pigtails and Manchu dress as a mark of submission (054-139).

Manchus did not need to pass the exams required of Chinese (054-146).

The K'ang-hsi emperor (1663-1722) was the great consolidator of Manchu rule. He drew scholars to court and stamped out corruption (055-016).

## Economy and Trade

Early Ch'ing enjoyed commercial growth from American silver. Trade in silk and tea boomed (055-151). Internal peace brought a population surge from 100 million to over 400 million by 1850 (055-093).

But the economy had structural weaknesses. Cultivated land per person fell from 11.6 to 6.1 mou between 1578 and 1729 (055-109). From the late 18th century, opium imports drained silver.

By 1835, silver doubled in value against copper, devastating farmers (055-165). Unlike Europe and Japan, China failed to industrialize. Commercial profits went into land rather than factories (055-176).

## Culture and Society

The Ch'ing era saw major cultural achievements. Emperors sponsored vast encyclopaedias like the *T'u-shu chi-ch'eng* (056-088). The novel *Hung-lou-meng* became the finest in Chinese literature (056-114).

> The period of Ch'ien-lung is not only that of the greatest expansion of the Chinese empire, but also that of the greatest prosperity under the Manchu regime. (057-086)

Kiangsi kilns produced celebrated *famille verte* and monochrome porcelain (056-149).

The empirical "Han school" of scholarship arose, led by Tai Chen. European missionaries influenced court art through painters like Giuseppe Castiglione (056-161).

## Decline and Fall

Decline began in the late 18th century. Corruption and the White Lotus Rebellion (1793-1802) signaled crisis (057-091).

In the 19th century, defeat in the [[opium-war]] brought unequal treaties and open treaty ports (057-185).

The [[tai-ping-rebellion]] (1851-1864) nearly destroyed the dynasty. Led by Hung Hsiu-ch'uan, it killed millions and devastated central China (058-144). Muslim revolts and the Nien Fei rising deepened the catastrophe.

The Sino-Japanese War of 1894 cost China Taiwan. The Boxer Rising of 1900 brought foreign occupation of Peking. The Hundred Days' Reform of 1898 was crushed by Empress Dowager Tz'u Hsi (060-072).

In October 1911, the Wuch'ang Uprising sparked nationwide revolution. [[yuan-shih-kai]] mediated between court and rebels.

The Ch'ing abdicated on February 12, 1912 (061-025). [[sun-yat-sen]] became president of the new Republic of China.
