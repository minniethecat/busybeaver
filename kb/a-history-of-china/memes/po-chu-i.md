## Overview

[[po-chu-i|Po Chü-i]] (772–846) was, alongside his friend Yüan Chen, one of the most popular and widely read poets of the mid- to late-T'ang period. His work achieved a reach unmatched by most of his predecessors — in an age before printing, his poems circulated in manuscript and were recited across the empire, making him one of the first Chinese poets to attain genuine mass popularity during his own lifetime. Unlike the more hermetic [[li-po|Li T'ai-po]] and the austere [[tu-fu|Tu Fu]], Po Chü-i cultivated a deliberately plain, direct language intended to be understood even by the uneducated [037-070].

## Life and Career

Po Chü-i was born in 772, early in the reign of the T'ang emperor Te Tsung, and came of age during the long recovery from the [[an-lu-shan-rebellion|An Lu-shan Rebellion]]. He passed the imperial examinations and entered the civil service, holding a series of provincial and capital posts. His official career brought him into direct contact with the social conditions of the countryside and the lives of ordinary people, experiences that would shape the subject matter of his most celebrated poems [037-070].

> Po Chü-i (772-846) and Yüan Chen (779-831) were the most popular poets of their time. [037-070]

His close friendship with the poet and fellow official Yüan Chen (779–831) was a defining feature of his literary life. The two men exchanged poems throughout their careers, developing a style of verse-letter that combined personal intimacy with public commentary. Their collaborative poetic correspondence became a model for later generations of [[gentry|literati]] [037-070].

## Poetic Style and Influence

Po Chü-i consciously rejected the dense allusiveness and aristocratic sensibility that had characterised much of earlier T'ang poetry. Instead, he wrote in a vernacular-inflected classical idiom, addressing subjects — peasant suffering, the corruption of officials, the loneliness of old age — that the High T'ang masters had largely avoided. His "New Yüeh-fu" ballads retold popular songs in a form that aimed to be both morally instructive and immediately comprehensible [044-066].

> The efforts to bring poetry closer to the common people were supported by the rise of a new reading public in the growing cities of the middle and lower Yangtze. [044-066]

The poet's fame extended well beyond China's borders. In Japan, Po Chü-i became the most revered of all Chinese poets during the Heian period; his collected works were treated as a model of literary elegance and were studied by every educated member of the Japanese court. In Korea and Vietnam likewise, his poems were read, imitated, and used in civil-service examinations for centuries after his death [074-084].

> Poets: T'ao Ch'ien, Po Chü-i, Li T'ai-po, Tu Fu, etc. [074-084]

## Later Years

In his later years, after a period of political exile from the capital, Po Chü-i turned increasingly towards [[buddhism|Buddhist]] themes. Settling into semi-retirement at a villa outside Loyang, he wrote some of his most reflective poetry — meditations on impermanence, friendship, and the consolations of the natural world. By the time of his death in 846, he had outlived most of his contemporaries and had become, in the eyes of the reading public, the grand old man of T'ang letters [074-079].
