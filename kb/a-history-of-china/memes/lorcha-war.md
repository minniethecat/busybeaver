## Overview

The Lorcha War of 1856–1860 — also known as the Arrow War or the Second Opium War — was the conflict that completed the opening of China to European penetration begun by the First [[opium-war|Opium War]] (1839–1842). Named after the *lorcha* (a type of sailing vessel) whose seizure by Chinese authorities provided the immediate pretext for British military action, the war resulted in the Treaty of Tientsin (1860), which legalized the opium trade, opened the [[yangtze-river|Yangtze River]] to foreign navigation, and established permanent European legations in [[peking|Peking]].

The war unfolded against the backdrop of the devastating [[tai-ping-rebellion|T'ai P'ing Rebellion]], which was simultaneously raging across central China, severely constraining the Ch'ing government's ability to resist foreign pressure.

## Background

The [[opium-war|First Opium War]] had ended with the [[opium-war|Treaty of Nanking]] (1842), but its terms were not faithfully observed by either side. The British permitted Chinese junks to fly the British flag in order to facilitate smuggling — a practice that also enabled these vessels to receive protection from British warships against the pirates then infesting the southern coast [058-013]. The Chinese, for their part, "placed every possible obstacle in the way of the British" [058-017].

The immediate trigger came in 1856, when Chinese authorities "held up a ship sailing under the British flag, pulled down its flag, and arrested the crew on suspicion of smuggling" [058-019]. In connection with this and other incidents:

> "Britain decided to go to war. Thus began the 'Lorcha War' of 1857, in which France joined for the sake of the booty to be expected." [058-021]

## Course of the War

Britain was simultaneously engaged in the Crimean War and in suppressing the Indian Mutiny, so initially "only a small force of a few thousand men could be landed in China" [058-024]. Canton was bombarded, as were the Taku forts guarding the approach to Tientsin. When negotiations failed to produce the desired concessions, a larger expedition was fitted out in 1860:

> "This time some 20,000 strong. The troops landed at Tientsin and marched on Peking; the emperor fled to Jehol and did not return; he died in 1861." [058-028]

The European force captured [[peking|Peking]], and the emperor's flight — followed shortly by his death — left the Ch'ing court in disarray.

## The Treaty of Tientsin (1860)

The new treaty imposed far-reaching terms [058-030]:

| Provision | Significance |
|-----------|-------------|
| Opening of further treaty ports | Expanded European commercial access |
| Cession of Kowloon | Extended Britain's Hong Kong foothold |
| British legation in [[peking|Peking]] | Permanent diplomatic presence at the capital |
| Freedom of navigation on the [[yangtze-river|Yangtze]] | Opened China's interior to foreign commerce |
| British right to purchase land in China | Established property rights for foreigners |
| Extraterritoriality (consular courts) | British subjects exempt from Chinese law |
| Unrestricted missionary activity | Opened the entire country to Christian missions |
| Legalization of the opium trade | Formalized the narcotics commerce |
| War indemnity | Financial penalty imposed on China |

In European eyes, "Britain had now succeeded in turning China not actually into a colony" but into a state sufficiently opened to commerce [058-039]. The Most Favoured Nation clause in the original Nanking treaty meant that all these privileges were automatically extended to other European powers and the United States [058-002].

## The Wider Crisis

The Lorcha War was only one of several simultaneous catastrophes afflicting mid-nineteenth-century China. As the corpus notes:

> "While in the central provinces the T'ai P'ing rebellion was raging, China was suffering grave setbacks owing to the Lorcha War of 1856; and there were also great and serious risings in other parts of the country." [059-001]

In 1855, the Yellow River had changed its course, devastating the provinces of Honan and Anhui [059-004]. In these same provinces, the peasant rising of the [[nien-fei|Nien Fei]] had begun and "only became formidable after 1855, owing to the increasing misery of the peasants" [059-007]. This was followed by a series of Mohammedan revolts in Kansu, Shensi, Yünnan, and Turkestan, suppressed with great brutality at the cost of millions of lives [059-010].

## Historical Significance

The Lorcha War resolved the ambiguities left by the [[opium-war|First Opium War]]. Where the 1842 [[opium-war|Treaty of Nanking]] had created a tentative framework for Sino-European relations, the 1860 Treaty of Tientsin established the full apparatus of the "unequal treaty system" that would define China's relationship with the West for the next eight decades. The legalization of the opium trade, in particular, was a moral nadir: having fought a war partly over the right to import narcotics, Britain now secured formal Chinese consent to the commerce.

The war also demonstrated the Ch'ing state's incapacity to manage simultaneous internal and external crises. The combination of the Lorcha War, the [[tai-ping-rebellion|T'ai P'ing Rebellion]], the [[nien-fei|Nien Fei]] rising, the Yellow River disaster, and the Mohammedan revolts created a perfect storm that came close to destroying the dynasty in the 1850s and 1860s. That the Ch'ing survived at all owed much to the military talents of provincial officials like [[tseng-kuo-fan|Tseng Kuo-fan]] and [[li-hung-chang|Li Hung-chang]], who raised regional armies to suppress the domestic rebellions while the court negotiated with the foreigners.
