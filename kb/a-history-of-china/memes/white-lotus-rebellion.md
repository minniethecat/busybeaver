## Overview

The White Lotus Rebellion (1774–1802) was the first great popular rising of the Ch'ing dynasty's period of decline, erupting in the reign of the [[chien-lung|Ch'ien-lung]] emperor. Organised by the secret White Lotus Society (*Pai-lien-hui*), a religious-political organisation with roots extending back to the [[ming-dynasty|Ming]] epoch, the rebellion spread across six provinces and required an immense military operation to suppress. It inaugurated a century of internal upheaval that would culminate in the [[tai-ping-rebellion|T'ai P'ing Rebellion]] and the [[boxer-rebellion|Boxer Rising]].

Like the [[red-eyebrows|Red Eyebrows]] of the Han period and the [[yellow-turban-rebellion|Yellow Turbans]], the White Lotus rising demonstrated how religious secret societies could mobilize peasant discontent into large-scale insurrectionary movements that shook the foundations of imperial rule.

## Background

The rebellion emerged during what was ostensibly the peak of Ch'ing power. The [[chien-lung|Ch'ien-lung]] reign (1735–1796) saw the greatest territorial expansion of the Chinese empire and unprecedented prosperity — yet "there began at the same time to be signs of internal decay" [057-088]. The economic pressures that fuelled discontent included population growth, land scarcity, and the burden of taxation imposed by a gentry class and a Manchu ruling elite that lived on state pensions without productive labour [057-099].

The White Lotus Society had existed as a secret religious organisation for centuries and "had played a part in the Ming epoch" [057-093]. It was reorganized in the late eighteenth century by a man named Liu Sung, who was captured and condemned to penal servitude [057-094]. His followers regrouped, particularly in the province of Anhui, and the movement continued to spread.

## The Rising

The first major outbreak occurred in 1774 in Shantung province [057-090]. The following year, in 1775, a second rising erupted in Honan — explicitly identified as the work of the "Society of the White Lotus" [057-092]. These risings were produced, the corpus states, "as always, by excessive oppression of the people by the government or the governing class" [057-097].

The government, however, interpreted the movement through a political rather than economic lens:

> "As, however, the anger of the population was naturally directed also against the idle Manchus of the cities, who lived on their state pensions, did no work, and behaved as a ruling class, the government saw in these movements a nationalist spirit, and took drastic steps against them." [057-099]

The government's response was extraordinarily brutal. A kind of Inquisition swept through the affected regions. The corpus records that "six provinces were affected, and in and around the single city of Wuch'ang in four months more than 20,000 people were beheaded" [057-108]. The cost of the rising to the government "ran into millions" [057-109].

In reaction to this repression, the popular leaders tightened their organisation and "marched north-west from the western provinces of which they had gained control" [057-111]. The rising was suppressed only by "a very big military operation, and not until 1802" — with especially heavy fighting between 1793 and 1802, the same years that revolutionary France was convulsing Europe [057-113].

## The Leadership and Tactics

When the government targeted the movement's nationalist character, the White Lotus leaders adapted their programme. They "acclaimed a supposed descendant from the Ming dynasty as the future emperor," shifting from a purely social rebellion to one with dynastic pretensions [057-103]. Government troops captured the movement's leader, but "he succeeded in escaping" [057-105], prolonging the conflict.

## Aftermath

The [[chien-lung|Ch'ien-lung]] emperor abdicated on New Year's Day 1795 after sixty years on the throne, dying in 1799 [057-117]. His successor, Jen Tsung (reign name Chia-ch'ing, r. 1796–1821), inherited the task of suppression. The White Lotus rising was finally extinguished during his reign, but in 1813 a new rising erupted in North China — this time organised by another secret organisation, the "Society of Heaven's Law" [057-120]. One of its leaders even penetrated the imperial palace with a group of followers, throwing himself upon the emperor, who was saved only through his son's intervention [057-123].

The government failed to learn the fundamental lesson of these uprisings:

> "The government failed to realize that the actual cause of the risings was the general impoverishment, and saw in them a nationalist movement, thus actually arousing a national consciousness among the Chinese people." [057-128]

## Historical Significance

The White Lotus Rebellion marked the beginning of the Ch'ing dynasty's long nineteenth-century crisis. Where earlier risings had been isolated and quickly crushed, the White Lotus movement demonstrated that the Manchu state could be challenged on a multi-provincial scale by organisations rooted in the rural population. The memory of these risings "was kept alive among the Chinese people" [057-127], feeding into the chain of secret-society revolts that continued through the nineteenth century.

The corpus explicitly links the White Lotus to earlier and later movements, placing it in a genealogy of Chinese popular rebellion stretching from the [[red-eyebrows|Red Eyebrows]] through the [[yellow-turban-rebellion|Yellow Turbans]] to the modern era [074-160]. The rebellion exposed the structural weaknesses of late imperial governance — the disconnect between a Manchu ruling class and the Han population, the inability to address rural immiseration, and the state's reliance on brute force rather than reform to manage social conflict.
