## Overview

[[lu-hsiang-shan|Lu Hsiang-shan]] (1139–1192), also known as Lu Chiu-yüan, was a [[sung-dynasty|Sung dynasty]] philosopher who founded the "School of Mind" (_Hsin-hsüeh_) in [[confucianism|Neo-Confucian]] thought. In opposition to the rationalist school of [[chu-hsi|Chu Hsi]], which emphasized the systematic investigation of things (_ko-wu_), Lu argued that the mind itself is the source of all principle (_li_), and that true understanding comes through direct intuitive realization rather than cumulative study.

> The philosopher Lu Hsiang-shan (1139-1192) had first developed, while classical Neo-Confucianism was more an integration of monastic Buddhism into Confucianism. [053-065]

His ideas were later powerfully revived and extended by [[wang-yang-ming|Wang Yang-ming]] in the Ming dynasty, forming the Lu-Wang school that became the dominant rival to the [[chu-hsi|Chu Hsi]] orthodoxy in late imperial China.

## Philosophical System

Lu Hsiang-shan's central philosophical claim was that the mind (_hsin_) is identical with principle (_li_). In his famous formulation, "The universe is my mind, and my mind is the universe." This meant that moral knowledge was innate and accessible through introspection, not dependent on the exhaustive textual study prescribed by the [[chu-hsi|Chu Hsi]] school.

His approach drew on Mencian ideas of innate moral sense but incorporated elements of meditative practice influenced by Buddhism:

> This idea shows an element of meditative Buddhism along lines which the philosopher Lu Hsiang-shan (1139-1192) had first developed. [053-065]

The practical consequence was a more egalitarian approach to moral cultivation: since each person possesses the complete moral principle within their own mind, enlightenment is in principle available to all, not only to the highly educated scholar-elite.

## The Debate with Chu Hsi

Lu Hsiang-shan's most famous intellectual encounter was a debate with [[chu-hsi|Chu Hsi]] at the Goose Lake Monastery (O-lu Ssu) in 1175. [[chu-hsi|Chu Hsi]] argued for the primacy of "investigating things" and extensive reading; Lu countered that without first establishing the right orientation of the mind, such study was merely the accumulation of dead facts. The debate became legendary in Chinese intellectual history as the definitive confrontation between the rationalist and intuitionist wings of [[confucianism|Neo-Confucianism]].

## Connection to Wang An-shih

Lu identified intellectually with the reformist tradition of [[wang-an-shih|Wang An-shih]]:

> Lu had felt himself close to Wang An-shih (1021-1086), and this whole school, representing the small gentry of the Yangtze area, was called the Southern or the Lin-ch'uan school. [053-065]

This affiliation with Wang's reformist legacy — representing the interests of the smaller gentry of the [[yangtze-river|Yangtze]] region against the great landed families of the north — gave Lu's philosophy a distinct social and political dimension. The Southern school's emphasis on individual moral intuition over institutional orthodoxy resonated with the more fluid social structure of the commercially dynamic southeast.

## Scholarly Reception

Modern Western scholarship on Lu Hsiang-shan includes dedicated monographs:

> For Lu Hsiang-shan the book by Huang Siu-ch'i, _Lu Hsiang-shan, a Twelfth-century Chinese Idealist Philosopher_, New Haven 1944, is the most complete study. [072-067]

This study established Lu as a major figure in the global history of idealist philosophy, comparable in some respects to Western idealists such as Berkeley, though with distinctively Chinese metaphysical foundations.

## Legacy

Lu Hsiang-shan's influence was magnified when [[wang-yang-ming|Wang Yang-ming]] (1472–1529) revived and systematized his ideas three centuries later. Wang credited Lu with the original insight that principle and mind are one, and built an elaborate philosophical and practical system on this foundation. The Lu-Wang school became especially influential in Japan (as the Oyōmei school) and played a significant role in East Asian intellectual history well into the modern period.
