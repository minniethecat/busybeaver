## Overview

The Hsiung-nu were the first great nomadic confederation to confront a unified imperial China as a peer power. Emerging during the late [[ch'in-dynasty|Ch'in dynasty]] (221-206 BCE) as a relatively small tribal union north of the Chinese frontier, they expanded rapidly under their second ruler [[mao-tun|Mao Tun]] into a formidable empire that dominated the eastern Eurasian steppe for centuries [015-064]. Eberhard identifies them as the archetypal steppe empire -- the model for all later nomadic polities from the Toba and Turks to the [[mongol|Mongols]] -- and traces their influence across seven hundred years of Chinese frontier history, from the founding of the [[han-dynasty|Han dynasty]] through the period of division when Hun successor kingdoms ruled much of North China.

Unlike later conquest dynasties that entered China and were absorbed, the Hsiung-nu pursued what Eberhard calls a "national" policy: maintaining their steppe homeland, preserving their nomadic way of life, and treating China as a tributary source of goods rather than a territory to be ruled from within. This strategic choice -- fiercely defended by the conservative tribal aristocracy -- allowed the Hsiung-nu state to endure far longer than those of the Toba, [[mongol|Mongols]], or Manchus, each of whom relocated their centres of power into China proper and were rapidly sinicized [015-124].

## Origins and Rise of the Steppe Empire

> "In the time of the Ch'in dynasty there had already come into unpleasant prominence north of the Chinese frontier the tribal union, then relatively small, of the Hsiung-nu. Since then, the Hsiung-nu empire had destroyed the federation of the Yueh-chih tribes (some of which seem to have been of Indo-European language stock) and incorporated their people into their own federation." [015-064]

The early expansion of the Hsiung-nu was swift and decisive. Under [[mao-tun|Mao Tun]] (who ruled from approximately 209 BCE), they defeated the Yueh-chih -- driving them westward in a migration that would ultimately send shockwaves as far as Bactria and northern India -- and conquered the Tung-hu, the less well-organized eastern pastoral tribes [015-067]. By the time the Han founder [[kao-tsu|Kao Tsu]] ([[kao-tsu|Liu Pang]]) consolidated his rule in China, the Hsiung-nu controlled a population estimated at over two million and fielded a cavalry-based military that no Chinese army of the time could match in open battle on the steppe [015-109].

The Hsiung-nu state was a feudal nomad empire. Its social structure had three strata: a warrior nobility, commoner herdsmen, and a class of slaves [015-096]. This was not a simple tribal confederation but an organized state apparatus modelled in part on Chinese precedents. Chinese emigrants -- artisans, farmers, and above all literate administrators -- staffed the Hsiung-nu secretariat, wrote Chinese, and taught the arts of warfare against fortified towns [015-073]. The Hsiung-nu ruler adopted the Chinese imperial title and developed a court ceremonial comparable to that of the Chinese Son of Heaven [015-089].

> "Thus the basis of the organization of the Hsiung-nu state lay in its rivalry with the neighbouring China; but the details naturally corresponded to the special nature of the Hsiung-nu social system." [015-092]

## Relations with the Han Dynasty

The defining geopolitical relationship of the early imperial period was that between the Han and the Hsiung-nu -- two empires of roughly equal power, locked in a tense equilibrium of diplomacy, tribute, intermarriage, and intermittent warfare that lasted nearly two centuries.

In 200 BCE, [[kao-tsu|Kao Tsu]] narrowly escaped disaster in northern Shansi, where a Chinese opponent of the Han founder had already concluded an alliance with [[mao-tun|Mao Tun]]. Had the battle gone differently, China might have fallen under Hsiung-nu domination at the very outset of the Han dynasty [015-112]. The near-catastrophe persuaded [[kao-tsu|Kao Tsu]] to negotiate. A treaty was concluded, sealed by the marriage of a Chinese princess to [[mao-tun|Mao Tun]], in which China agreed to provide the Hsiung-nu with foodstuffs, clothing, and other goods in exchange for an end to raids [015-132].

This was significant beyond its immediate terms: it was "the first international treaty in the Far East between two independent powers mutually recognized as equals, and the forms of international diplomacy developed in this time remained the standard forms for the next thousand years" [015-136]. The treaty was renewed with each new ruler on either side but was never fully observed. The Hsiung-nu court's appetite for luxury goods grew with the expansion of the empire; the Chinese sought to give as little as possible and frequently cheated on the terms. Raids continued [015-140].

Under Wu Ti (r. 141-87 BCE), the dynamic shifted. The Han launched an aggressive policy of military expansion toward Central Asia, designed to outflank the Hsiung-nu by seizing the Tarim Basin and allying with the Yueh-chih and other peoples on the Hsiung-nu's western flank [017-117]. This strategy largely succeeded: the Hsiung-nu were pushed back, their empire fractured, and by the first century CE they had split into Northern and Southern branches. The Southern Hsiung-nu submitted to the Han and were settled within the empire's frontiers; the Northern Hsiung-nu were destroyed by Han forces in 89-91 CE and driven westward in a migration that some scholars associate with the Huns who later appeared on the frontiers of Europe.

## Social and Military Organization

The Hsiung-nu state was organized on a "cone" structure characteristic of pastoral societies, in which the ruling lineage sat at the apex and subordinate tribal groups -- both original and incorporated -- radiated outward in layers of decreasing privilege [024-017]. Eberhard identifies two recurring tendencies in Hsiung-nu governance:

- **Bureaucratic centralization**: The federation leader repeatedly attempted to build a bureaucratic system using personal bondsmen as a nucleus, transforming tribal warriors into state functionaries [024-002].
- **Dynastic replacement**: Tribal leaders were replaced by members of the ruling family, converting a confederation of independent chieftains into a patrimonial empire [024-005].

When "outer tribes" were incorporated, a further reorganization replaced tribal military units with decimal-based formations -- groups of hundred, groups of thousand -- dissolving original tribal identities into standardized regiments [024-008]. Over time, these military units acquired social cohesion and came to resemble tribes again, though they were "secondary" or artificial tribes bearing the names of their commanders rather than organic kinship communities [024-014].

The Hsiung-nu military relied on the mounted archer, a form of warfare at which the nomads excelled and against which Chinese infantry and chariotry were poorly suited. Hsiung-nu horsemen, with their technique of mobile archery, could strike at will and withdraw before Chinese forces could concentrate, a tactical asymmetry that persisted until the Han developed their own cavalry arm [015-106].

## Later Hun Kingdoms

The story of the Hsiung-nu does not end with the Han dynasty. During the period of division that followed the Han collapse (220-589 CE), Hun successor states ruled large portions of North China.

By the late third century CE, Eberhard notes, Chinese sources begin referring to the Hsiung-nu as "Hu" -- hence "Huns" -- a term with both a broad sense (all northern and western non-Chinese) and a narrower one (the Hsiung-nu specifically) [024-020]. Nineteen Hsiung-nu tribes lived south of the Toba in what is now Shansi, and in 287 CE their leader [[liu-yuan|Liu Yuan]] -- who bore a Chinese name but was descended from the Hun shan-yu, from the line of [[mao-tun|Mao Tun]] -- emerged as one of the principal figures of the age. His descent from the ancient ruling family of the Huns conferred enormous prestige, which he augmented with organizational brilliance [024-074].

The collapse of the Western Chin dynasty created a power vacuum that the Huns exploited. Chinese princes and generals, locked in struggles for the imperial throne, "entered into agreements with the frontier peoples to assure their aid in the struggle for power" [024-093]. The Huns were drawn into these civil wars as mercenaries and allies, and in the chaos they seized the initiative. [[liu-yuan|Liu Yuan]]'s descendants founded the first of the great alien dynasties of North China: the Hun Han dynasty (later renamed Former Chao, 304-329 CE), which claimed legitimacy as both heirs of the Han imperial tradition and successors to the Hsiung-nu shan-yu.

> "Why should not the Huns have the same right? Why should they not also found a dynasty, being an old civilized ruling family like any Chinese ruling family?" [024-159]

A second Hun kingdom, the Hsia (407-431 CE), was founded by Helian Bobo in the Ordos region, building on the legacy of Hunnic-Turkish aristocratic traditions that regarded imperial rule as the natural prerogative of a noble lineage regardless of ethnic origin [024-169]. Although these Hun states proved ephemeral compared to the seven-century endurance of the original steppe empire, they established a pattern -- the alien conquest dynasty claiming Chinese imperial legitimacy -- that would be repeated by the Toba, the Kitan, the Juchen, the [[mongol|Mongols]], and the Manchus.
