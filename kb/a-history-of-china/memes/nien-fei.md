## Overview

The Nien Fei rebellion was a peasant uprising that ravaged the central Chinese provinces of Honan and Anhui during the mid-nineteenth century. Originating around 1855, the revolt intensified as a result of growing rural misery, worsened by the Yellow River's change of course in the same year, which had diverted its waters toward Tientsin and caused devastating flooding in Honan and Anhui [059-070].

> In 1855 the Yellow River had changed its course, entering the sea once more at Tientsin, to the great loss of the regions of Honan and Anhui. In these two central provinces the peasant rising of the so-called "Nien Fei" had begun, but it only became formidable after 1855, owing to the increasing misery of the peasants. [059-070]

The Nien Fei operated contemporaneously with the far larger [[tai-ping-rebellion|T'ai P'ing Rebellion]] (1851–1864), which was raging in the central provinces at the same time. The Qing government, already struggling to suppress the T'ai P'ing, faced the Nien Fei as an additional threat that extended the period of civil conflict [059-001].

> This purely peasant revolt was not suppressed by the Manchu government until 1868, after many collisions. [059-070]

The suppression of the Nien Fei, together with the [[tai-ping-rebellion|T'ai P'ing Rebellion]] and the Mohammedan risings, inflicted enormous demographic losses on China during this turbulent period, though reliable statistics are unavailable. The ferocity of the suppressions contributed to a substantial decline in China's population in the affected regions [059-026].
