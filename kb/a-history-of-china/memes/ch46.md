[046-001] The influx of migrants and the increase of tenants and their poverty

[046-002] seriously threatened the state and cut down its defensive strength more

[046-003] and more.

[046-004] At this stage, Chia Ssu-tao drafted a reform law. Chia had come to the

[046-005] court through his sister becoming the emperor's concubine, but he

[046-006] himself belonged to the lesser gentry. His proposal was that state funds

[046-007] should be applied to the purchase of land in the possession of the

[046-008] greater gentry over and above a fixed maximum. Peasants were to be

[046-009] settled on this land, and its yield was to belong to the state, which

[046-010] would be able to use it to meet military expenditure. In this way the

[046-011] country's military strength was to be restored. Chia's influence lasted

[046-012] just ten years, until 1275. He began putting the law into effect in the

[046-013] region south of Nanking, where the principal estates of the greater

[046-014] gentry were then situated. He brought upon himself, of course, the

[046-015] mortal hatred of the greater gentry, and paid for his action with his

[046-016] life. The emperor, in entering upon this policy, no doubt had hoped to

[046-017] recover some of his power, but the greater gentry brought him down. The

[046-018] gentry now openly played into the hands of the approaching Mongols, so

[046-019] hastening the final collapse of the Sung. The peasants and the lesser

[046-020] gentry would have fought the Mongols if it had been possible; but the

[046-021] greater gentry enthusiastically went over to the Mongols, hoping to save

[046-022] their property and so their influence by quickly joining the enemy. On a

[046-023] long view they had not judged badly. The Mongols removed the members of

[046-024] the gentry from all political posts, but left them their estates; and

[046-025] before long the greater gentry reappeared in political life. And when,

[046-026] later, the Mongol empire in China was brought down by a popular rising,

[046-027] the greater gentry showed themselves to be the most faithful allies of

[046-028] the Mongols!

[046-029] (5) The empire of the Juchên in the north (1115-1234)

[046-030] 1 _Rapid expansion from northern Korea to the Yangtze_

[046-031] The Juchên in the past had been only a small league of Tungus tribes,

[046-032] whose name is preserved in that of the present Tungus tribe of the

[046-033] Jurchen, which came under the domination of the Kitan after the collapse

[046-034] of the state of Po-hai in northern Korea. We have already briefly

[046-035] mentioned the reasons for their rise. After their first successes

[046-036] against the Kitan (1114), their chieftain at once proclaimed himself

[046-037] emperor (1115), giving his dynasty the name "Chin" (The Golden). The

[046-038] Chin quickly continued their victorious progress. In 1125 the Kitan

[046-039] empire was destroyed. It will be remembered that the Sung were at once

[046-040] attacked, although they had recently been allied with the Chin against

[046-041] the Kitan. In 1126 the Sung capital was taken. The Chin invasions were

[046-042] pushed farther south, and in 1130 the Yangtze was crossed. But the Chin

[046-043] did not hold the whole of these conquests. Their empire was not yet

[046-044] consolidated. Their partial withdrawal closed the first phase of the

[046-045] Chin empire.

[046-046] 2 _United front of all Chinese_

[046-047] But a few years after this maximum expansion, a withdrawal began which

[046-048] went on much more quickly than usual in such cases. The reasons were to

[046-049] be found both in external and in internal politics. The Juchên had

[046-050] gained great agrarian regions in a rapid march of conquest. Once more

[046-051] great cities with a huge urban population and immense wealth had fallen

[046-052] to alien conquerors. Now the Juchên wanted to enjoy this wealth as the

[046-053] Kitan had done before them. All the Juchên people counted as citizens of

[046-054] the highest class; they were free from taxation and only liable to

[046-055] military service. They were entitled to take possession of as much

[046-056] cultivable land as they wanted; this they did, and they took not only

[046-057] the "state domains" actually granted to them but also peasant

[046-058] properties, so that Chinese free peasants had nothing left but the worst

[046-059] fields, unless they became tenants on Juchên estates. A united front was

[046-060] therefore formed between all Chinese, both peasants and landowning

[046-061] gentry, against the Chin, such as it had not been possible to form

[046-062] against the Kitan. This made an important contribution later to the

[046-063] rapid collapse of the Chin empire.

[046-064] The Chin who had thus come into possession of the cultivable land and

[046-065] at the same time of the wealth of the towns, began a sort of competition

[046-066] with each other for the best winnings, especially after the government

[046-067] had returned to the old Sung capital, Pien-liang (now K'ai-feng, in

[046-068] eastern Honan). Serious crises developed in their own ranks. In 1149 the

[046-069] ruler was assassinated by his chancellor (a member of the imperial

[046-070] family), who in turn was murdered in 1161. The Chin thus failed to

[046-071] attain what had been secured by all earlier conquerors, a reconciliation

[046-072] of the various elements of the population and the collaboration of at

[046-073] least one group of the defeated Chinese.

[046-074] 3 _Start of the Mongol empire_

[046-075] The cessation of fighting against the Sung brought no real advantage in

[046-076] external affairs, though the tribute payments appealed to the greed of

[046-077] the rulers and were therefore welcomed. There could be no question of

[046-078] further campaigns against the south, for the Hsia empire in the west had

[046-079] not been destroyed, though some of its territory had been annexed; and a

[046-080] new peril soon made its appearance in the rear of the Chin. When in the

[046-081] tenth century the Sha-t'o Turks had to withdraw from their dominating

[046-082] position in China, because of their great loss of numbers and

[046-083] consequently of strength, they went back into Mongolia and there united

[046-084] with the Ta-tan (Tatars), among whom a new small league of tribes had

[046-085] formed towards the end of the eleventh century, consisting mainly of

[046-086] Mongols and Turks. In 1139 one of the chieftains of the Juchên rebelled

[046-087] and entered into negotiations with the South Chinese. He was killed, but

[046-088] his sons and his whole tribe then rebelled and went into Mongolia, where

[046-089] they made common cause with the Mongols. The Chin pursued them, and

[046-090] fought against them and against the Mongols, but without success.

[046-091] Accordingly negotiations were begun, and a promise was given to deliver

[046-092] meat and grain every year and to cede twenty-seven military strongholds.

[046-093] A high title was conferred on the tribal leader of the Mongols, in the

[046-094] hope of gaining his favour. He declined it, however, and in 1147 assumed

[046-095] the title of emperor of the "greater Mongol empire". This was the

[046-096] beginning of the power of the Mongols, who remained thereafter a

[046-097] dangerous enemy of the Chin in the north, until in 1189 Genghiz Khan

[046-098] became their leader and made the Mongols the greatest power of central

[046-099] Asia. In any case, the Chin had reason to fear the Mongols from 1147

[046-100] onward, and therefore were the more inclined to leave the Sung in peace.

[046-101] In 1210 the Mongols began the first great assault against the Chin, the

[046-102] moment they had conquered the Hsia. In the years 1215-17 the Mongols

[046-103] took the military key-positions from the Chin. After that there could be

[046-104] no serious defence of the Chin empire. There came a respite only because

[046-105] the Mongols had turned against the West. But in 1234 the empire finally

[046-106] fell to the Mongols.

[046-107] Many of the Chin entered the service of the Mongols, and with their

[046-108] permission returned to Manchuria; there they fell back to the cultural

[046-109] level of a warlike nomad people. Not until the sixteenth century did

[046-110] these Tunguses recover, reorganize, and appear again in history this

[046-111] time under the name of Manchus.

[046-112] The North Chinese under Chin rule did not regard the Mongols as enemies

[046-113] of their country, but were ready at once to collaborate with them. The

[046-114] Mongols were even more friendly to them than to the South Chinese, and

[046-115] treated them rather better.

[046-116] Chapter Ten

[046-117] THE PERIOD OF ABSOLUTISM

[046-118] (A) The Mongol Epoch (1280-1368)

[046-119] 1 _Beginning of new foreign rules_

[046-120] During more than half of the third period of "Modern Times" which now

[046-121] began, China was under alien rule. Of the 631 years from 1280 to 1911,

[046-122] China was under national rulers for 276 years and under alien rule for

[046-123] 355\. The alien rulers were first the Mongols, and later the Tungus

[046-124] Manchus. It is interesting to note that the alien rulers in the earlier

[046-125] period came mainly from the north-west, and only in modern times did

[046-126] peoples from the north-east rule over China. This was due in part to the

[046-127] fact that only peoples who had attained a certain level of civilization

[046-128] were capable of dominance. In antiquity and the Middle Ages, eastern

[046-129] Mongolia and Manchuria were at a relatively low level of civilization,

[046-130] from which they emerged only gradually through permanent contact with

[046-131] other nomad peoples, especially Turks. We are dealing here, of course,

[046-132] only with the Mongol epoch in China and not with the great Mongol

[046-133] empire, so that we need not enter further into these questions.

[046-134] Yet another point is characteristic: the Mongols were the first alien

[046-135] people to rule the whole of China; the Manchus, who appeared in the

[046-136] seventeenth century, were the second and last. All alien peoples before

[046-137] these two ruled only parts of China. Why was it that the Mongols were

[046-138] able to be so much more successful than their predecessors? In the first

[046-139] place the Mongol political league was numerically stronger than those of

[046-140] the earlier alien peoples; secondly, the military organization and

[046-141] technical equipment of the Mongols were exceptionally advanced for their

[046-142] day. It must be borne in mind, for instance, that during their many

[046-143] years of war against the Sung dynasty in South China the Mongols already

[046-144] made use of small cannon in laying siege to towns. We have no exact

[046-145] knowledge of the number of Mongols who invaded and occupied China, but

[046-146] it is estimated that there were more than a million Mongols living in

[046-147] China. Not all of them, of course, were really Mongols! The name covered

[046-148] Turks, Tunguses, and others; among the auxiliaries of the Mongols were

[046-149] Uighurs, men from Central Asia and the Middle East, and even Europeans.

[046-150] When the Mongols attacked China they had the advantage of all the arts

[046-151] and crafts and all the new technical advances of western and central

[046-152] Asia and of Europe. Thus they had attained a high degree of technical

[046-153] progress, and at the same time their number was very great.

[046-154] 2 "_Nationality legislation_"

[046-155] It was only after the Hsia empire in North China, and then the empire of

[046-156] the Juchên, had been destroyed by the Mongols, and only after long and

[046-157] remarkably modern tactical preparation, that the Mongols conquered South

[046-158] China, the empire of the Sung dynasty. They were now faced with the

[046-159] problem of ruling their great new empire. The conqueror of that empire,

[046-160] Kublai, himself recognized that China could not be treated in quite the

[046-161] same way as the Mongols' previous conquests; he therefore separated the

[046-162] empire in China from the rest of the Mongol empire. Mongol China became

[046-163] an independent realm within the Mongol empire, a sort of Dominion. The

[046-164] Mongol rulers were well aware that in spite of their numerical strength

[046-165] they were still only a minority in China, and this implied certain

[046-166] dangers. They therefore elaborated a "nationality legislation", the

[046-167] first of its kind in the Far East. The purpose of this legislation was,

[046-168] of course, to be the protection of the Mongols. The population of

[046-169] conquered China was divided into four groups--(1) Mongols, themselves

[046-170] falling into four sub-groups (the oldest Mongol tribes, the White

[046-171] Tatars, the Black Tatars, the Wild Tatars); (2) Central Asian

[046-172] auxiliaries (Naimans, Uighurs, and various other Turkish people,

[046-173] Tanguts, and so on); (3) North Chinese; (4) South Chinese. The Mongols

[046-174] formed the privileged ruling class. They remained militarily organized,

[046-175] and were distributed in garrisons over all the big towns of China as

[046-176] soldiers, maintained by the state. All the higher government posts were

[046-177] reserved for them, so that they also formed the heads of the official

[046-178] staffs. The auxiliary peoples were also admitted into the government

[046-179] service; they, too, had privileges, but were not all soldiers but in

[046-180] many cases merchants, who used their privileged position to promote

[046-181] business. Not a few of these merchants were Uighurs and Mohammedans;

[046-182] many Uighurs were also employed as clerks, as the Mongols were very

[046-183] often unable to read and write Chinese, and the government offices were

[046-184] bilingual, working in Mongolian and Chinese. The clever Uighurs quickly

[046-185] learned enough of both languages for official purposes, and made

[046-186] themselves indispensable assistants to the Mongols. Persian, the main

[046-187] language of administration in the western parts of the Mongol empire

[046-188] besides Uighuric, also was a _lingua franca_ among the new rulers of

[046-189] China.
