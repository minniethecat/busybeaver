# Tou Ku

**Tou Ku** ({{zh|竇固}}, fl. 73–92 CE) was a Chinese general and statesman of the Eastern [[Han dynasty]], best known as the leader of the Tou clan (Tou family clique) and the sponsor of [[Pan Ch'ao]]'s famous expedition to the Western Regions.

## The Tou Clan

Tou Ku belonged to one of the most powerful consort families of the Eastern Han. The Tou clan had produced empresses and dominated court politics through strategic marriage alliances with the imperial Liu family.

By the mid-first century CE, the Tou family led the expansionist faction at court, advocating aggressive military policies against the Northern [[Hsiung-nu]] confederation on the northern and western frontiers.

> The clique of Tou Ku and Pan Ch'ao: the danger of the restoration of the Hsiung-nu. [020-022]

The Tou clique's power rested on a network of familial and political alliances within the Han aristocracy.

> She was a member of the family of Tou Ku. [020-035]

## Military Campaigns

Tou Ku's most significant military achievement was the campaign of 89 CE, in which Han forces, in alliance with the Southern [[hsiung-nu|Hsiung-nu]], decisively defeated the Northern [[hsiung-nu|Hsiung-nu]]. This victory broke the power of the northern confederation and opened the way for Han expansion into the Tarim Basin and beyond.

The campaign was enabled by a shift in court politics: the young Emperor Ho came of age, and the Tou faction — which had briefly been sidelined — returned to dominance at court.

## Sponsorship of Pan Ch'ao

Tou Ku's most lasting legacy was his sponsorship of [[pan-chao|Pan Ch'ao]]. Recognising [[pan-chao|Pan Ch'ao]]'s diplomatic and military talents, Tou Ku appointed him to lead the Han expedition into the Western Regions. This thirty-year mission extended Han influence deep into Central Asia, securing the trade routes that would later form the [[Silk Road]].

## Legacy

Tou Ku is remembered less for his own achievements than for the political network he led and the subordinates he sponsored.

His clan exemplified the double-edged nature of Eastern Han consort politics: capable of projecting imperial power abroad, but also a source of factional instability at court. The Tou family's fortunes declined rapidly after Tou Ku's death, as [[eunuch|eunuch faction]]s gained ascendancy at the imperial court.
