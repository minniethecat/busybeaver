## Overview

The Sung dynasty (960–1279) was an imperial dynasty of China founded by Chao K'uang-yin, who came from a Chinese military family in the service of the Later Chou dynasty (041-169). The dynasty is divided into two distinct periods: the Northern Sung (960–1127), with its capital at Kaifeng, and the Southern Sung (1127–1279), with its capital at Hangchow (then called Lin-an). The Sung period is widely recognized as a high point of Chinese civilization, marked by unprecedented economic growth, technological innovation, and cultural achievement (045-149). Despite its cultural brilliance, the dynasty faced persistent military pressure from northern steppe empires, culminating in the Mongol conquest that extinguished the Southern Sung in 1279.

The Sung dynasty succeeded the period of the Five Dynasties and was itself followed by the Mongol Yuan dynasty. To distinguish it from the earlier and unrelated Liu-Sung dynasty (420–478), historians refer to it simply as the Sung (032-146).

## Founding and Rise

The founder of the Sung dynasty, Chao K'uang-yin, came of a Chinese military family and rose through the ranks of the Later Chou dynasty (041-169). In 960, he seized power through a coup, naming his new dynasty the Sung (042-001). Unlike many founders of Chinese dynasties, Chao K'uang-yin did not come from the old aristocracy but from the gentry class, a social transformation that shaped the character of Sung governance.

> The founder of the Sung dynasty, Chao K'uang-yin, came of a Chinese military family. (041-169)

The new emperor consolidated power by persuading his generals to retire in exchange for wealth and privilege, a peaceful transition known as "releasing military power over a cup of wine." This set the tone for Sung civil-military relations: the dynasty would privilege civilian bureaucratic governance over military command, a policy that ensured internal stability but left the empire vulnerable to external threats.

## Government and Administration

Sung governance represented the maturation of the Chinese civil service system. The examination system was expanded and refined during this period, becoming the primary channel for entry into the imperial bureaucracy. The gentry class — educated landowners who staffed the civil service — became the dominant social and political elite.

The dynasty established a centralized administration with power concentrated in the imperial court. The Sung institution of the palace examination allowed the emperor to bypass the aristocratic networks that had dominated earlier dynasties and to recruit officials on the basis of merit. This system produced a remarkably stable bureaucratic apparatus that persisted even after the loss of the northern territories in 1127, when the social structure of the Southern Sung empire had not been fundamentally disrupted by the catastrophe (045-094).

## Economy and Trade

The Sung dynasty presided over what many historians describe as China's first commercial revolution. The period saw the widespread use of paper currency, the development of a national market, and the flourishing of long-distance trade. The Southern Sung period in particular was again one of flourishing economic activity (045-149).

Commercial agriculture expanded as farmers shifted from subsistence crops to cash crops like tea, sugar, and silk. Urban centers grew dramatically — Hangchow, the Southern Sung capital, became one of the world's largest cities. Maritime trade with Southeast Asia, India, and the Middle East brought unprecedented wealth to the southern ports. The government collected substantial revenues from commercial taxes and trade monopolies, reducing its dependence on the traditional land tax.

## Culture and Society

Sung culture reached extraordinary heights in philosophy, literature, and the arts. Neo-Confucianism, a philosophical revival and reinterpretation of classical Confucian thought, was systematized during the Southern Sung by the philosopher Chu Hsi. Landscape painting achieved new levels of sophistication, and the imperial painting academy fostered generations of master artists.

The Sung dynasty also witnessed significant technological advances, including the development of movable-type printing, gunpowder weapons, and the mariner's compass. Porcelain production reached new levels of refinement, with Sung celadon and other wares becoming prized trade goods. The period's intellectual culture was shaped by widespread literacy and the availability of printed books, which expanded access to education beyond the traditional elite.

The Southern Sung period is noted for a nascent nationalism, whose origins seem to lie in the identity forged during the long struggle against northern invaders (049-061).

## Decline and Fall

The military weakness inherent in the Sung system became apparent early. The dynasty faced continuous pressure from nomadic peoples to the north. In the early 12th century, the Jurchen (Chin) invaded, capturing the Sung capital Kaifeng and taking the reigning emperor and his father prisoner, ending the Northern Sung dynasty (044-143). One prince escaped south, reestablished the court in Nanking, and founded the "Southern Sung" dynasty (045-078).

The Southern Sung survived for another 150 years, but faced permanent war threats from the north (045-175). In the 13th century, the Mongols under Kublai Khan turned their attention to South China. After years of war against the Sung dynasty in South China, the Mongols also gained experience in naval warfare and siege techniques that ultimately proved decisive (046-143). The last Sung emperor, a child, perished at the battle of Yaishan in 1279, bringing the dynasty to its end.
