# Section 32

[032-001] We cannot enter into all the intrigues recorded at this time. The

[032-002] details are, indeed, historically unimportant. They were concerned only

[032-003] with the affairs of the court and its entourage. Not a single ruler of

[032-004] the Eastern Chin dynasty possessed personal or political qualities of

[032-005] any importance. The rulers' power was extremely limited because, with

[032-006] the exception of the founder of the state, Yüan Ti, who had come rather

[032-007] earlier, they belonged to the group of the new immigrants, and so had no

[032-008] firm footing and were therefore caught at once in the net of the newly

[032-009] re-grouping gentry class.

[032-010] The emperor Yüan Ti lived to see the first great rising. This rising

[032-011] (under Wang Tun) started in the region of the present Hankow, a region

[032-012] that today is one of the most important in China; it was already a

[032-013] centre of special activity. To it lead all the trade routes from the

[032-014] western provinces of Szechwan and Kweichow and from the central

[032-015] provinces of Hupei, Hunan, and Kiangsi. Normally the traffic from those

[032-016] provinces comes down the Yangtze, and thus in practice this region is

[032-017] united with that of the lower Yangtze, the environment of Nanking, so

[032-018] that Hankow might just as well have been the capital as Nanking. For

[032-019] this reason, in the period with which we are now concerned the region of

[032-020] the present Hankow was several times the place of origin of great

[032-021] risings whose aim was to gain control of the whole of the southern

[032-022] empire.

[032-023] Wang Tun had grown rich and powerful in this region; he also had near

[032-024] relatives at the imperial court; so he was able to march against the

[032-025] capital. The emperor in his weakness was ready to abdicate but died

[032-026] before that stage was reached. His son, however, defeated Wang Tun with

[032-027] the aid of General Yü Liang (A.D. 323). Yü Liang was the empress's

[032-028] brother; he, too, came from a northern family. Yüan Ti's successor also

[032-029] died early, and the young son of Yü Liang's sister came to the throne as

[032-030] Emperor Ch'eng (326-342); his mother ruled as regent, but Yü Liang

[032-031] carried on the actual business of government. Against this clique rose

[032-032] Su Chün, another member of the northern gentry, who had made himself

[032-033] leader of a bandit gang in A.D. 300 but had then been given a military

[032-034] command by the dynasty. In 328 he captured the capital and kidnapped the

[032-035] emperor, but then fell before the counterthrust of the Yü Liang party.

[032-036] The domination of Yü Liang's clique continued after the death of the

[032-037] twenty-one-years-old emperor. His twenty-year-old brother was set in

[032-038] his place; he, too, died two years later, and his two-year-old son

[032-039] became emperor (Mu Ti, 345-361).

[032-040] Meanwhile this clique was reinforced by the very important Huan family.

[032-041] This family came from the same city as the imperial house and was a very

[032-042] old gentry family of that city. One of the family attained a high post

[032-043] through personal friendship with Yü Liang: on his death his son Huan Wen

[032-044] came into special prominence as military commander.

[032-045] Huan Wen, like Wang Tun and others before him, tried to secure a firm

[032-046] foundation for his power, once more in the west. In 347 he reconquered

[032-047] Szechwan and deposed the local dynasty. Following this, Huan Wen and the

[032-048] Yü family undertook several joint campaigns against northern states--the

[032-049] first reaction of the south against the north, which in the past had

[032-050] always been the aggressor. The first fighting took place directly to the

[032-051] north, where the collapse of the "Later Chao" seemed to make

[032-052] intervention easy. The main objective was the regaining of the regions

[032-053] of eastern Honan, northern Anhui and Kiangsu, in which were the family

[032-054] seats of Huan's and the emperor's families, as well as that of the Hsieh

[032-055] family which also formed an important group in the court clique. The

[032-056] purpose of the northern campaigns was not, of course, merely to defend

[032-057] private interests of court cliques: the northern frontier was the weak

[032-058] spot of the southern empire, for its plains could easily be overrun. It

[032-059] was then observed that the new "Earlier Ch'in" state was trying to

[032-060] spread from the north-west eastwards into this plain, and Ch'in was

[032-061] attacked in an attempt to gain a more favourable frontier territory.

[032-062] These expeditions brought no important practical benefit to the south;

[032-063] and they were not embarked on with full force, because there was only

[032-064] the one court clique at the back of them, and that not whole-heartedly,

[032-065] since it was too much taken up with the politics of the court.

[032-066] Huan Wen's power steadily grew in the period that followed. He sent his

[032-067] brothers and relatives to administer the regions along the upper

[032-068] Yangtze; those fertile regions were the basis of his power. In 371 he

[032-069] deposed the reigning emperor and appointed in his place a frail old

[032-070] prince who died a year later, as required, and was replaced by a child.

[032-071] The time had now come when Huan Wen might have ascended the throne

[032-072] himself, but he died. None of his family could assemble as much power as

[032-073] Huan Wen had done. The equality of strength of the Huan and the Hsieh

[032-074] saved the dynasty for a time.

[032-075] In 383 came the great assault of the Tibetan Fu Chien against the

[032-076] south. As we know, the defence was carried out more by the methods of

[032-077] diplomacy and intrigue than by military means, and it led to the

[032-078] disaster in the north already described. The successes of the southern

[032-079] state especially strengthened the Hsieh family, whose generals had come

[032-080] to the fore. The emperor (Hsiao Wu Ti, 373-396), who had come to the

[032-081] throne as a child, played no part in events at any time during his

[032-082] reign. He occupied himself occasionally with Buddhism, and otherwise

[032-083] only with women and wine. He was followed by his five-year-old son. At

[032-084] this time there were some changes in the court clique. In the Huan

[032-085] family Huan Hsüan, a son of Huan Wen, came especially into prominence.

[032-086] He parted from the Hsieh family, which had been closest to the emperor,

[032-087] and united with the Wang (the empress's) and Yin families. The Wang, an

[032-088] old Shansi family, had already provided two empresses, and was therefore

[032-089] strongly represented at court. The Yin had worked at first with the

[032-090] Hsieh, especially as the two families came from the same region, but

[032-091] afterwards the Yin went over to Huan Hsüan. At first this new clique had

[032-092] success, but later one of its generals, Liu Lao-chih, went over to the

[032-093] Hsieh clique, and its power declined. Wang Kung was killed, and Yin

[032-094] Chung-k'an fell away from Huan Hsüan and was killed by him in 399. Huan

[032-095] Hsüan himself, however, held his own in the regions loyal to him. Liu

[032-096] Lao-chih had originally belonged to the Hsieh clique, and his family

[032-097] came from a region not far from that of the Hsieh. He was very

[032-098] ambitious, however, and always took the side which seemed most to his

[032-099] own interest. For a time he joined Huan Hsüan; then he went over to the

[032-100] Hsieh, and finally returned to Huan Hsüan in 402 when the latter reached

[032-101] the height of his power. At that moment Liu Lao-chih was responsible for

[032-102] the defence of the capital from Huan Hsüan, but instead he passed over

[032-103] to him. Thus Huan Hsüan conquered the capital, deposed the emperor, and

[032-104] began a dynasty of his own. Then came the reaction, led by an earlier

[032-105] subordinate of Liu Lao-chih, Liu Yü. It may be assumed that these two

[032-106] army commanders were in some way related, though the two branches of

[032-107] their family must have been long separated. Liu Yü had distinguished

[032-108] himself especially in the suppression of a great popular rising which,

[032-109] around the year 400, had brought wide stretches of Chinese territory

[032-110] under the rebels' power, beginning with the southern coast. This rising

[032-111] was the first in the south. It was led by members of a secret society

[032-112] which was a direct continuation of the "Yellow Turbans" of the latter

[032-113] part of the second century A.D. and of organized church-Taoism. The

[032-114] whole course of this rising of the exploited and ill-treated lower

[032-115] classes was very similar to that of the popular rising of the "Yellow

[032-116] Turbans". The movement spread as far as the neighbourhood of Canton,

[032-117] but in the end it was suppressed, mainly by Liu Yü.

[032-118] Through these achievements Liu Yü's military power and political

[032-119] influence steadily increased; he became the exponent of all the cliques

[032-120] working against the Huan clique. He arranged for his supporters to

[032-121] dispose of Huan Hsüan's chief collaborators; and then, in 404, he

[032-122] himself marched on the capital. Huan Hsüan had to flee, and in his

[032-123] flight he was killed in the upper Yangtze region. The emperor was

[032-124] restored to his throne, but he had as little to say as ever, for the

[032-125] real power was Liu Yü's.

[032-126] Before making himself emperor, Liu Yü began his great northern campaign,

[032-127] aimed at the conquest of the whole of western China. The Toba had

[032-128] promised to remain neutral, and in 415 he was able to conquer the "Later

[032-129] Ch'in" in Shensi. The first aim of this campaign was to make more

[032-130] accessible the trade routes to Central Asia, which up to now had led

[032-131] through the difficult mountain passes of Szechwan; to this end treaties

[032-132] of alliance had been concluded with the states in Kansu against the

[032-133] "Later Ch'in". In the second place, this war was intended to increase Liu

[032-134] Yü's military strength to such an extent that the imperial crown would

[032-135] be assured to him; and finally he hoped to cut the claws of pro-Huan

[032-136] Hsüan elements in the "Later Ch'in" kingdom who, for the sake of the

[032-137] link with Turkestan, had designs on Szechwan.

[032-138] 3 _The Liu-Sung dynasty_ (A.D. 420-478) _and the Southern Ch'i dynasty_

[032-139] (479-501)

[032-140] After his successes in 416-17 in Shensi, Liu Yü returned to the capital,

[032-141] and shortly after he lost the chief fruits of his victory to Ho-lien

[032-142] P'o-p'o, the Hun ruler in the north, while Liu Yü himself was occupied

[032-143] with the killing of the emperor (419) and the installation of a puppet.

[032-144] In 420 the puppet had to abdicate and Liu Yü became emperor. He called

[032-145] his dynasty the Sung dynasty, but to distinguish it from another and

[032-146] more famous Sung dynasty of later time his dynasty is also called the

[032-147] Liu-Sung dynasty.

[032-148] The struggles and intrigues of cliques against each other continued as

[032-149] before. We shall pass quickly over this period after a glance at the

[032-150] nature of these internal struggles.

[032-151] Part of the old imperial family and its following fled northward from

[032-152] Liu Yü and surrendered to the Toba. There they agitated for a campaign

[032-153] of vengeance against South China, and they were supported at the court

[032-154] of the Toba by many families of the gentry with landed interests in the

[032-155] south. Thus long-continued fighting started between Sung and Toba,

[032-156] concerned mainly with the domains of the deposed imperial family and

[032-157] its following. This fighting brought little success to south China, and

[032-158] about 450 it produced among the Toba an economic and social crisis that

[032-159] brought the wars to a temporary close. In this pause the Sung turned to

[032-160] the extreme south, and tried to gain influence there and in Annam. The

[032-161] merchant class and the gentry families of the capital who were allied

[032-162] with it were those chiefly interested in this expansion.

[032-163] About 450 began the Toba policy of shifting the central government to

[032-164] the region of the Yellow River, to Loyang; for this purpose the frontier

[032-165] had to be pushed farther south. Their great campaign brought the Toba in

[032-166] 450 down to the Yangtze. The Sung suffered a heavy defeat; they had to

[032-167] pay tribute, and the Toba annexed parts of their northern territory.

[032-168] The Sung emperors who followed were as impotent as their predecessors

[032-169] and personally much more repulsive. Nothing happened at court but

[032-170] drinking, licentiousness, and continual murders.
