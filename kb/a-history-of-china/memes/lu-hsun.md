## Overview

Lu Hsün (1881–1936), pen name of Chou Shu-jen, is widely regarded as the father of modern Chinese literature. Trained initially in medicine in Japan, he abandoned his medical studies to pursue a literary career, believing that curing China's spiritual ailments was more urgent than treating its physical ones. His short stories, essays, and polemics shaped the intellectual climate of Republican China and remain central to the Chinese literary canon.

> But another well-known writer, Lu Hsün (1881-1936), while following Hu Shih's literary revolution, went far beyond him in his political radicalism. [064-166]

## Literary Career and Influence

Lu Hsün's first and most famous short story, "A Madman's Diary" (1918), was published in the journal _New Youth_ and is considered the first modern Chinese short story. Written in the vernacular Chinese (_pai-hua_) that the [[may-fourth-movement|May Fourth Movement]] championed, it used the conceit of a man who sees "cannibalism" in Confucian morality to deliver a devastating critique of traditional Chinese society.

His story collections _Call to Arms_ (_Na-han_) and _Wandering_ (_P'ang-huang_) established the short story as a major form in modern Chinese literature. Works like "The True Story of Ah Q" created archetypal characters that entered the Chinese cultural vocabulary. Lu Hsün's protagonist Ah Q, with his self-deceiving "spiritual victory," became the defining portrait of what Lu Hsün saw as the Chinese national character's weaknesses.

Beyond his fiction, Lu Hsün was a prolific essayist and translator. His miscellaneous essays (_tsa-wen_), collected in numerous volumes, engaged the political and cultural controversies of his day with fierce satirical wit. He translated works from Russian, Japanese, and Eastern European literature, introducing Chinese readers to foreign literary traditions.

## Political Engagement

Lu Hsün was a consistent critic of the Kuomintang government and its suppression of leftist intellectuals. Though he sympathized with the Chinese Communist Party and was hailed by [[mao-tse-tung|Mao Tse-tung]] as a cultural hero after his death, Lu Hsün never formally joined the Party. His independence of mind and suspicion of all organized authority made him a complex figure who defied easy political categorization.

> Lu Hsün became the acknowledged leader of the left-wing literary movement in the 1930s. [073-641]

His death in 1936, on the eve of the Sino-Japanese War, was mourned across the political spectrum. Posthumously, both the Nationalists and Communists claimed his legacy, though it was the Communists who most successfully enshrined him as a cultural icon of the revolution.
