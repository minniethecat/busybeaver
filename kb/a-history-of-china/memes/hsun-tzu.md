## Overview

[[Hsün Tzu]] (c. 298–238 BC) was a [[Confucianism|Confucian]] philosopher of the late [[Warring States period]]. A native of the state of Chao, he belonged to the "scholars" (ju) class and lived in present-day Shantung in eastern China. Along with [[Mencius]], he is regarded as one of the two most important elaborators of [[Confucius]]'s thought, though his philosophical conclusions differed sharply from those of [[mencius|Mencius]].

Unlike [[mencius|Mencius]], who held that human nature is inherently good, Hsün Tzu argued that man's nature is fundamentally bad and that moral cultivation must be achieved through deliberate effort, education, and adherence to ritual (li).

## Biography

Hsün Tzu was a follower of [[confucianism|Confucianism]] who, like [[mencius|Mencius]], elaborated the ideas of [[Confucius]]. He lived during the late Warring States period in eastern China, in what is now Shantung province.

> "Mencius (_c_. 372-289 B.C.) and Hsün Tz[)u] (_c_. 298-238 B.C.) were both followers of Confucianism. Both belonged to the so-called 'scholars', and both lived in the present Shantung, that is to say, in eastern China. Both elaborated the ideas of Confucius, but neither of them achieved personal success." [012-007–012-011]

Hsün Tzu held official positions in the state of Ch'i and later in Ch'u, where he served as a magistrate. His students included [[Han Fei Tzu]] and Li Ssu, who would become leading figures of the [[Legalism|Legalist]] school — an irony given that Legalism stood in opposition to Confucian thought.

## Philosophy

Hsün Tzu's chief contribution to Chinese philosophy was his recognition of a dynamic relationship between nature and human agency:

> "Hsün Tz[)u]'s chief importance lies in the fact that he recognized that the 'laws' of nature are unchanging but that man's fate is determined not by nature alone but, in addition, by his own activities. Man's nature is basically bad, but by working on himself within the framework of society, he can change his nature and can develop. Thus, Hsün Tz[)u]'s philosophy contains a dynamic element, fit for a dynamic period of history." [012-019–012-025]

This view placed him in direct opposition to [[mencius|Mencius]]. Where [[mencius|Mencius]] saw moral self-cultivation as the unfolding of innate goodness, Hsün Tzu saw it as the deliberate reshaping of an originally flawed nature through education, social norms, and ritual practice.

His philosophy emphasized the importance of:
- **Ritual (li)**: As an externally imposed framework for moral behavior
- **Education**: As the primary means of transforming human nature
- **Law**: As a necessary supplement to ritual in governing society

## Legacy

Hsün Tzu occupies a pivotal position in the development of Chinese philosophy. His synthesis of Confucian ethics with a realistic assessment of human nature influenced both Confucian orthodoxy and its Legalist critics. The "Hsiin Tzu" text, a collection of 32 chapters largely written by Hsün Tzu himself, remains one of the most important philosophical works of the Warring States period.

His thought was transmitted through his students and continued to be studied throughout Chinese history. He is listed among the major philosophers in the historical index:

> "Huai-nan Tz[)u], Hsün Tz[)u], Mencius, Hui Tz[)u], Mo Ti" [074-065]
