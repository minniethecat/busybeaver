## Overview

The May Fourth Movement of 1919 was the intellectual watershed of modern Chinese history. Beginning with a student demonstration in [[peking|Peking]] on May 4, 1919, against the Versailles Treaty's transfer of German concessions in Shantung to Japan, it rapidly developed into a broad cultural and political movement that challenged Confucian tradition, promoted vernacular literature, and introduced Western ideas of science and democracy to a generation of Chinese intellectuals. The movement gave its name to an entire era of Chinese cultural transformation and laid the intellectual foundations for both the Nationalist and Communist revolutions that would reshape twentieth-century China.

## The Demonstration of May 4, 1919

The movement takes its name from a specific event:

> "On May 4th, 1919, students of the National University in Peking demonstrated against the government and their pro-Japanese adherents. When the police attacked the students and jailed some, more demonstrations and student strikes and finally a general boycott of Japanese imports were the consequence." [062-002]

Professors including [[tsai-yuan-pei|Ts'ai Yüan-p'ei]], later president of the Academia Sinica, actively supported the students [062-007]. The demonstration was a response to the decision of the Paris Peace Conference to award Germany's former concessions in Shantung province to Japan rather than returning them to Chinese sovereignty — a bitter betrayal of China's expectations as one of the victorious Allied powers.

## The New Youth Circle

The intellectual forces mobilized by the demonstrations coalesced around the journal *New Youth* (*Hsin Ch'ing-nien*), founded in 1915 by [[chen-tu-hsiu|Ch'en Tu-hsiu]]. The journal was "progressive, against the monarchy, [[confucius|Confucius]], and the old traditions" [062-010]. [[chen-tu-hsiu|Ch'en Tu-hsiu]] himself was more radical than many of his fellow contributors but initially "favoured Western democracy and Western science," influenced by the American philosopher John Dewey, who was a guest professor in [[peking|Peking]] in 1919–1920 [062-013].

[[hu-shih|Hu Shih]] (born 1892), another key figure, gained his greatest influence through his proposal for a "literary revolution," published in *New Youth* in 1917 [062-019]. This revolution called for the abandonment of classical literary Chinese (*wen-yen*) in favour of the vernacular language (*pai-hua*), democratizing access to written culture and enabling the mass literacy campaigns of subsequent decades.

## From Liberalism to Communism

In its early phase, the May Fourth Movement was not communist. As the corpus notes:

> "The 'Movement of May Fourth' of 1919 had Western ideological traits but was not communistic. This changed with the success of communism in Russia and with the theoretical writings of Lenin. Here it was shown that communist theories could be applied to a country similar to China in its level of development." [064-157]

From 1919 onward, some of the movement's leaders turned toward communism. The National University of [[peking|Peking]] became the movement's first centre, and [[chen-tu-hsiu|Ch'en Tu-hsiu]], dean of the College of Letters, emerged as one of its communist leaders from 1920 onward [064-163]. [[hu-shih|Hu Shih]], by contrast, "did not move to the left with this group; he remained a liberal" [064-165].

The writer [[lu-hsun|Lu Hsün]] (1881–1936), while initially following [[hu-shih|Hu Shih]] in the literary revolution, "identified politically with Ch'en" [064-167]. Another key figure was Li Ta-chao, the Director of the University Library, who also turned toward communism — and with whom we find "one of his employees in the Library, [[mao-tse-tung|Mao Tse-tung]]." The corpus states unequivocally: "the nucleus of the Chinese Communist Party was formed in this group" [064-170].

## Historical Significance

The May Fourth Movement was the crucible from which modern China emerged. In the span of a few years, the intellectual landscape of China was transformed: the classical literary language was replaced by the vernacular, [[confucianism|Confucianism]] was dethroned from its position as state orthodoxy, and both liberal democracy and Marxism-Leninism gained articulate Chinese adherents. The movement's leaders — [[chen-tu-hsiu|Ch'en Tu-hsiu]], [[hu-shih|Hu Shih]], [[lu-hsun|Lu Hsün]], Li Ta-chao, [[mao-tse-tung|Mao Tse-tung]] — would dominate Chinese intellectual and political life for the next half-century.

The movement also established a pattern that would recur throughout twentieth-century Chinese history: student-led protest against government policy, rapidly escalating into a broader challenge to the existing political order. The May Fourth generation's conviction that China needed fundamental transformation — not merely technical modernization on the model of the failed Self-Strengthening Movement — set the terms of political debate for decades.

Perhaps most consequentially, the movement incubated the nucleus of the Chinese Communist Party. What began as a cultural and intellectual awakening ended by creating the political instrument that would, thirty years later, reunify China under a revolutionary regime fundamentally hostile to the Western liberal ideas that had originally inspired the movement.
