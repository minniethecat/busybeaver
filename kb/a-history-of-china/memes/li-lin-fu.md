# Li Lin-fu

**Li Lin-fu** ({{zh|李林甫}}, d. 752) was a prominent chancellor of the [[Tang dynasty]] during the reign of Emperor [[Hsüan-tsung]] (r. 712–756). He dominated the Tang court for nearly two decades, combining administrative competence with ruthless political tactics that reshaped the imperial government and inadvertently set the stage for the [[An Lu-shan Rebellion]].

## Rise to the Chancellorship

Li Lin-fu belonged to the Tang imperial clan by distant descent, which facilitated his entry into official life. He rose through the bureaucracy by cultivating connections at court and demonstrating genuine administrative talent. By the 730s he had secured the position of chief minister.

> A very ambitious but capable and energetic Li Lin-fu, a member of the imperial family, became the real ruler of the empire. [037-134]

His ascendancy marked a shift away from the collegial aristocratic governance of the early Tang toward a more autocratic style in which a single chancellor accumulated unprecedented power.

## Administration and Policies

As chancellor, Li Lin-fu centralised authority in his own hands, marginalising rival officials and reducing the influence of the traditional aristocracy. He reformed the tax system and strengthened frontier defences by promoting the use of non-Chinese military governors (chieftains) — a policy intended to professionalise the army without burdening the central treasury.

However, this policy had an unintended consequence. By concentrating military command in the hands of frontier generals of non-Chinese origin, Li Lin-fu created a power base independent of the civilian bureaucracy.

> He had sponsored An Lu-shan, a general of Turkish origin. [037-157]

## Death and the Yang Clique

Li Lin-fu died in 752 while still in office. His death created a power vacuum at court that was quickly filled by the Yang family — the relatives of the emperor's favourite consort, [[Yang Kuei-fei]].

> Meanwhile Li Lin-fu died. The clique of Li Lin-fu was replaced by the Yang family. [037-159]

The Yang clique proved far less capable than Li Lin-fu in managing the empire's military affairs. Within three years of his death, [[an-lu-shan|An Lu-shan]] — the general Li Lin-fu had sponsored — launched the rebellion that would devastate the Tang dynasty.

## Legacy

Li Lin-fu is a controversial figure in Chinese historiography. Traditional Confucian histories portray him as a villainous minister whose machinations weakened the Tang.

Modern historians offer a more nuanced assessment: he was an able administrator who managed the empire competently for nearly twenty years.

His concentration of power and sponsorship of frontier generals, however, created structural weaknesses that his successors could not manage. His career illustrates the late Tang dilemma — the very policies that sustained the empire in the short term contributed to its long-term fragility.
