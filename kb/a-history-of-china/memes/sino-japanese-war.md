## Overview

The Sino-Japanese War of 1894–1895 was a pivotal conflict that exposed the military weakness of Qing China and announced Japan's emergence as an imperial power in East Asia. The war broke out over the contested status of Korea, where Japanese penetration since 1876 had steadily eroded China's traditional protectorate [059-166].

> In 1894 the Sino-Japanese War broke out over Korea, as an outcome of the undefined position that had existed since 1885 owing to the imperialistic policy of the Japanese. [059-166]

Japan had pursued an aggressive expansionist policy for decades. As early as 1869, Japan sent a delegation of amity to China, and a first Sino-Japanese treaty was signed in 1871. But Japan soon began to carry out imperialistic plans: in 1874 it attacked the Ryukyu Islands and Formosa; in 1876 it annexed the Ryukyu Islands in violation of treaty; and the same year began Japanese penetration into Korea. By 1885 Japan had forced a declaration that Korea was a joint sphere of interest, ending China's unchallenged protectorate [059-168].

China was poorly prepared for war. Its sole modern naval force was the North China squadron, but this represented the entirety of Chinese military preparation. The Governor General of Chihli, [[li-hung-chang|Li Hung-chang]], who had previously rendered good service, commanded Chinese forces but lost the war decisively [059-170].

> The Governor General of Chihli (now Hopei — the province in which Peking is situated), Li Hung-chang, was a general who had done good service, but he lost the war, and at Shimonoseki (1895) he had to sign a treaty on very harsh terms. [059-171]

The Treaty of Shimonoseki (1895) forced China to relinquish its protectorate over Korea and cede Formosa (Taiwan) to Japan. Japan's demand for South Manchuria was blocked only by the diplomatic intervention of France, Germany, and Russia [059-172].
