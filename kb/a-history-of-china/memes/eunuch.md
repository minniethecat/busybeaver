## Overview

Eunuchs (宦官, _huan-kuan_) were a distinctive and recurrent feature of Chinese imperial politics. Although their formal role was limited to service within the inner quarters of the palace -- tending to the emperor's many wives and concubines -- eunuchs repeatedly amassed political power that rivalled and sometimes eclipsed that of the regular bureaucracy. Because they "had the ear of the emperor and so could influence him," as Eberhard notes, eunuchs "formed an important political factor" across multiple dynasties [020-067].

The eunuch phenomenon was not a peripheral curiosity of court life but a structural element of the Chinese imperial system. The tension between the inner court dominated by eunuchs and the outer court of [[gentry|scholar-official]]s drawn from the [[gentry]] was a recurring dynamic that shaped the fate of dynasties. The [[examination-system|imperial examination system]] was one of the mechanisms through which the gentry sought to limit eunuch influence, but under the [[ming-dynasty|Ming dynasty]] in particular, eunuchs reached heights of power that transformed the nature of imperial government.

At their peak, eunuchs controlled appointments, operated a secret police apparatus, commanded armies, and accumulated personal fortunes exceeding the annual budget of the state. Famous eunuchs like [[cheng-ho|Cheng Ho]] ([[cheng-ho|Zheng He]]), the great maritime explorer, and [[liu-chin|Liu Chin]], the de facto ruler of the Ming empire, represent the extraordinary range of roles that palace eunuchs could come to occupy.

## Origins and the Han Dynasty

The institutional basis of eunuch power lay in the structure of the imperial palace itself. The emperor maintained hundreds of wives and concubines, and to ensure the legitimacy of the imperial bloodline, all male servants in the inner palace had to be castrated. Under the Later Han dynasty (25-220 CE), the scale of this operation grew dramatically:

> "The many hundred wives and concubines in the palace made necessary a great army of eunuchs. As they had the ear of the emperor and so could influence him, the eunuchs formed an important political factor." [020-066]

The Later Han witnessed the first great eunuch-[[gentry|literati]] conflict in Chinese history. For a time, "the main struggle was between the group of eunuchs and the group of scholars" -- that is, between the inner court and the regular bureaucracy staffed by the [[gentry]] [020-069]. The eunuchs served the interests of particular cliques associated with the emperor's wives, while the scholars served rival cliques. These struggles "grew more and more sanguinary in the middle of the second century" [020-073], and the resulting political paralysis contributed directly to the collapse of the Later Han and the rise of provincial warlords like [[tsao-tsao|Ts'ao Ts'ao]].

The eunuch-gentry conflict of the Later Han established a pattern that would recur throughout Chinese history: the emperor, isolated within the palace, turning to his personal servants as a counterweight against the Confucian-educated officials who dominated the outer bureaucracy.

## The Tang Dynasty and the Eunuch-Literati Struggle

Under the [[tang-dynasty|Tang dynasty]] (618-907 CE), the pattern repeated itself with renewed intensity. After the catastrophic [[an-lu-shan-rebellion|An Lu-shan rebellion]] (755-763 CE) weakened the central government and empowered regional military governors, the court fell increasingly under eunuch control.

> "In the capital, eunuchs ruled in the interests of various cliques. Several emperors fell victim to them or to the drinking of 'elixirs of long life'." [038-073-074]

Eberhard explicitly compares the Tang situation to the Later Han: "The struggle between cliques soon became a struggle between eunuchs and [[gentry|literati]], in much the same way as at the end of the second Han dynasty" [038-079]. The eunuchs' control over access to the emperor gave them decisive leverage. As trade diminished and tax revenues fell into the hands of provincial military governors, the impoverished central government became ever more dependent on the palace eunuch establishment.

The Tang eunuchs were not merely political operators; they also controlled the imperial guard and could make and unmake emperors. At least two Tang emperors were murdered by eunuchs, and several more were enthroned at their pleasure. This level of power anticipated the even more extensive eunuch dominance of the Ming.

## The Ming Dynasty: Eunuchs at the Apex of Power

The [[ming-dynasty|Ming dynasty]] (1368-1644) represents the high point of eunuch power in Chinese history. The founder of the dynasty, [[chu-yuan-chang|Chu Yuan-chang]], had initially tried to limit eunuch influence, but his successors -- particularly after the capital was moved to [[peking|Peking]] -- increasingly relied on eunuchs as a tool of personal rule.

### The Systematization of Eunuch Power

The Ming emperors deliberately cultivated the eunuchs as a counterweight to the gentry bureaucracy:

> "Originally eunuchs were forbidden to acquire education. But soon the Ming emperors used the eunuchs as a tool to counteract the power of gentry cliques and thus to strengthen their personal power. When, later, eunuchs controlled appointments to government posts, long established practices of bureaucratic administration were eliminated and the court, i.e. the emperor and his tools, the eunuchs, could create a rule by way of arbitrary decisions, a despotic rule." [051-014-020]

This transformation required the education of eunuchs -- a radical break with earlier practice. "Whole departments of eunuchs came into existence at court, and these were soon made use of for confidential business of the emperor's outside the palace" [051-026-027]. The eunuch establishment became a parallel bureaucracy, handling everything from tax collection to diplomacy.

The eunuchs accumulated enormous wealth by accepting bribes in exchange for placing gentry petitions before the emperor. They then shared this wealth with their relatives from the rising "small gentry" class, whose increased influence Eberhard directly connects to "the increased influence of the eunuchs at court" [051-034-035].

### The Secret Police and Despotic Rule

Under the Ming, the eunuchs established an institution unprecedented in Chinese history: a secret police with powers extending far beyond the palace walls. "This office was set up by the eunuchs and the clique at their back, and was the first dictatorial organ created in the course of a development towards despotism that made steady progress in these years" [052-135-138]. Many people were arrested and disappeared without public trial [052-134-135].

The reign of the young emperor Wu Tsung (r. 1505-1521) saw the apex of eunuch tyranny under [[liu-chin|Liu Chin]], who secured the emperor's formal permission to issue all commands himself while the emperor occupied himself entirely with his pleasures [052-145-147]. [[liu-chin|Liu Chin]]'s first major decree resulted in "the removal from office or the punishment or murder of over three hundred prominent persons, the leaders of the cliques opposed to him" [052-150-151].

### The Eunuch as Court Patron and the Small Gentry

A notable social development of the Ming period was the phenomenon of educated men voluntarily undergoing castration after passing the state examinations. "A good many members of the new class had themselves castrated after they had passed their state examination" [051-013-014]. This drastic step was a calculated career move: by entering the eunuch establishment, a man of the "small gentry" could gain direct access to the emperor and bypass the patronage networks of the established gentry families.

## Social Role and Legacy

> "Thus the eunuchs generally accumulated great wealth, which they shared with their small gentry relatives. The rise of the small gentry class was therefore connected with the increased influence of the eunuchs at court." [051-032-035]

The eunuch phenomenon reveals a structural tension at the heart of Chinese imperial government. The emperor, in theory an absolute monarch, was in practice isolated within the palace and dependent on information filtered through layers of bureaucratic intermediaries. The eunuchs -- physically incapable of founding a rival dynasty -- seemed to the emperors the only truly trustworthy servants. Yet this very trust made them dangerous: unaccountable to the outer bureaucracy and answerable only to the emperor, eunuchs could and did establish what Eberhard calls "rule by way of arbitrary decisions, a despotic rule."

The struggle between eunuchs and the [[confucianism|Confucian]] [[gentry|scholar-official]]s was, at root, a struggle over the nature of imperial rule itself: should the empire be governed through regular bureaucratic procedures and the [[examination-system|examination system]], or through the personal will of the emperor as executed by his intimate servants? This question was never definitively resolved, and the ebb and flow of eunuch power across the centuries reflects the shifting balance between these two competing visions of government.

After the Ming collapse, the Ch'ing dynasty (1644-1912) imposed severe restrictions on eunuch power, and the eunuch system was finally abolished following the fall of the imperial state. But the record they left in the historical sources testifies to their centrality -- for good and for ill -- in the political life of imperial China.
