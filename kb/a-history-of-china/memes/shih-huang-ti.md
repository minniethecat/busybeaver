## Overview

Shih Huang-ti (Chinese: 始皇帝, "First Emperor"), previously known as Cheng (政), was the founder of the [[chin-dynasty|Ch'in dynasty]] and the first ruler to unify China under a centralized imperial system in 221 BCE. His reign marked the transition from the feudal [[chou-dynasty|Chou]] multi-state order to a bureaucratic empire governed by appointed officials rather than hereditary nobles. Eberhard describes him as "evidently an average man with an almost pathological urge for personal fame and power" who built his system on the Legalist reforms already underway in Ch'in [014-006].

The First Emperor's achievements include standardizing the Chinese writing system, currency, weights and measures, and axle widths across the newly unified realm. He also initiated the construction of what would become the [[great-wall|Great Wall]] and his own monumental mausoleum guarded by the terracotta army. However, his harsh Legalist rule — marked by book-burning, forced labor, and suppression of dissent — led to the dynasty's rapid collapse shortly after his death in 210 BCE.

Shih Huang-ti came to the throne as a boy under the regency of [[lu-pu-wei|Lü Pu-wei]], a wealthy merchant who had risen to become chancellor of Ch'in [013-064]. After reaching majority, Shih Huang-ti asserted personal control and completed the conquest of the remaining independent states, a project that had been gradually advanced by his Ch'in predecessors over the preceding century.

## Rise to Power

> "In 221 B.C. Shih Huang-ti had become emperor of all China. He had before him so many urgent tasks that we can hardly imagine how he accomplished them." [014-001]

Shih Huang-ti inherited the throne of Ch'in as a young child, with his father's former chancellor [[lu-pu-wei|Lü Pu-wei]] acting as regent [013-064]. The state of Ch'in, located in the Wei River valley in the west, had been steadily growing in power for over a century through Legalist administrative reforms, agricultural development, and military expansion.

Soon after Shih Huang-ti's accession, [[lu-pu-wei|Lü Pu-wei]] was dismissed from his position, allowing the young king to assert direct authority [013-071]. The political struggle between the ruler and the powerful merchant-regent reflected the broader shift in Ch'in from a feudal state toward centralized bureaucratic rule. Under Shih Huang-ti's command, Ch'in systematically conquered and annexed the remaining independent states: Han, Chao, Wei, Ch'u, Yen, and Ch'i, completing the unification in 221 BCE [013-076].

## Reign and Reforms

The First Emperor undertook a sweeping program of standardization and centralization that fundamentally reshaped Chinese civilization. His reforms dismantled the old feudal system and replaced it with a unified administrative structure [016-004].

> "Shih Huang-ti undertook periodical journeys, inspecting his empire and especially the subjugated east and south. He showed an astonishing belief in the magic of his power and position." [014-017]

Key reforms and projects included:

- **Administrative centralization**: The empire was divided into commanderies (chün) and counties (hsien) governed by officials appointed by and accountable to the central government, replacing the hereditary feudal lordships of the Chou period [016-004].
- **Standardization**: Writing, weights and measures, currency, and even cart axle widths were standardized across the empire to facilitate administration, trade, and communication.
- **The Great Wall**: Existing northern border fortifications built by various states were connected and extended to defend against the [[hsiung-nu|Hsiung-nu]] nomads — an enormous project that consumed vast labor and lives.
- **Legalist ideology**: Shih Huang-ti adopted a notion of total state control derived from Legalist philosophy, suppressing rival schools of thought, most notoriously by ordering the burning of books and the execution of scholars who opposed his policies [014-020].

His periodical journeys across the empire served both to inspect his territories and to demonstrate imperial power, particularly in the recently subjugated eastern and southern regions [014-017].

## Historical Assessment

The First Emperor's legacy is deeply contested. On one hand, he created the institutional framework of imperial China — centralized bureaucracy, standardized writing, uniform administration — that would endure for over two millennia. On the other, his brutal methods and the rapid collapse of his dynasty after his death made him a cautionary figure in Chinese historiography.

> "The system of the Former Han dynasty was in many respects a copy of the Ch'in system." [016-004]

Eberhard notes that subsequent dynasties, especially the [[han-dynasty|Han]], inherited and preserved much of the Ch'in administrative structure while softening its harshest features. The Han historian Ssu-ma Ch'ien portrayed Shih Huang-ti as a tyrant, and traditional Chinese historiography largely followed this judgment. Modern assessments are more nuanced, recognizing the First Emperor's role as the architect of Chinese imperial unity while acknowledging the human cost of his megalomania.

The Ch'in dynasty collapsed within four years of Shih Huang-ti's death, brought down by popular revolts against heavy taxation, forced labor, and the ruthless application of Legalist law. Yet the imperial model he established proved durable: every subsequent Chinese dynasty until 1912 would govern through a centralized bureaucratic system that ultimately traced its design to the First Emperor's reign.
