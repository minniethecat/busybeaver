## Overview

[[chu-ko-liang|Chu-ko Liang]] (181–234 CE) was the premier and chief strategist of [[shu-han|Shu Han]], the south-western kingdom of the [[three-kingdoms|Three Kingdoms]] period. A man from Shantung, he served as the architect of Shu Han's political and military strategy and as regent after the death of the founding ruler. His death in 234 CE marked a decisive turning point, after which Shu Han rapidly declined and was conquered by Wei in 263.

## Premier and Strategist

Chu-ko Liang designed Shu Han's governing strategy from the state's earliest days:

> The political plans of Shu Han were well considered and far-seeing. They were evolved by the premier, a man from Shantung named Chu-ko Liang; for the ruler died in 226 and his successor was still a child. [022-112, 022-113, 022-114]

The corpus identifies him not only as premier but also as a general [073-218], indicating that his role fused civil and military authority — a common pattern in the Three Kingdoms era, when a state's survival often rested on a single figure's judgment in both realms.

## Regency

When [[liu-pei|Liu Pei]], the founding ruler of Shu Han, died in 226 CE, his heir was still a minor. Chu-ko Liang assumed control as regent, guiding the state through a fragile succession:

> But Chu-ko Liang lived only for a further eight years, and after his death in 234 the decline of Shu Han began. [022-114, 022-115, 022-116]

His regency lasted only eight years — too short to build institutions that could outlast him. During that window he held Shu Han together against the demographic and military superiority of the northern state of Wei under [[tsao-tsao|Ts'ao Ts'ao]].

## The Decline After 234

The immediate consequence of Chu-ko Liang's death was a collapse in strategic judgment:

> Its political leaders no longer had a sense of what was possible. Thus Wei inflicted several defeats on Shu Han, and finally subjugated it in 263. [022-116, 022-117, 022-118]

The contrast between the "well considered and far-seeing" plans of his era and the loss of any "sense of what was possible" afterward underscores how dependent Shu Han was on his intelligence. Without his guidance, the state was conquered just 29 years after his passing.

## Legacy

Chu-ko Liang's reputation as a paragon of strategic genius grew in the centuries after his death. The corpus itself makes the implicit case for his indispensability: Shu Han's coherence under his premiership, its precarious survival during his regency, and its rapid collapse once he was gone. Later Chinese tradition canonised him as the supreme strategist — a figure whose intellect alone could hold a kingdom together, celebrated in literature, folklore, and popular culture as the embodiment of loyal service and foresight.
