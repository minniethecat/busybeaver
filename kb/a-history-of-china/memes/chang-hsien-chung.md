## Overview

Chang Hsien-chung (1606–1647) was one of the two most prominent rebel leaders during the final years of the [[ming-dynasty|Ming dynasty]], alongside [[li-tzu-cheng|Li Tzu-ch'eng]]. Operating primarily in western China, particularly Szechwan, Chang led a massive peasant insurrection that weakened Ming authority at a moment when the dynasty faced simultaneous threats from internal rebellion and the rising Manchu power in the northeast. Eberhard notes that Chang's rebellion, together with Li's, decimated the gentry class in western China and helped create the conditions for the Manchu conquest.

Like other late Ming rebels, Chang struggled to transition from military leader to state-builder. While he established himself as ruler of Szechwan, he proved incapable of constructing a durable administration — a failure that Eberhard identifies as typical of the late Ming insurgents.

## Life and Background

Chang emerged from the social chaos of the 1630s and 1640s, when a combination of famine, fiscal collapse, and military pressure from the Manchus destabilized Ming rule. Eberhard records that "the years around 1640 brought several widespread droughts in North China, a natural phenomenon that was repeated in the nineteenth century, when unrest again ensued" [054-068].

> "The two most successful leaders of bands were Li Tzu-ch'êng and Chang Hsien-chung. Li came from the province of Shensi; he had come to the fore during a disastrous famine in his country." [054-064]

Chang's early career was marked by shifting allegiances. He "returned for a time to the support of the government, but later established himself in western China" [054-069]. This pattern — oscillating between rebellion and cooperation with Ming authorities — reflected the pragmatic survival strategies of late Ming warlords operating in a collapsing political order.

## Military Campaigns

Chang's primary theater of operations was western China, particularly Szechwan. While Li Tzu-ch'eng advanced on [[peking|Peking]] from the north, Chang consolidated power in the west. The Szechwan rebellion, which James B. Parsons studied in detail, was one of the bloodiest episodes of the Ming-Qing transition [072-087].

> "Chang Hsien-chung and Li Tzu-ch'êng were the first to fall; the pirate Coxinga lasted a little longer and was even able to plunder Nanking in 1659." [054-175]

Eberhard situates Chang's rebellion within the wider pattern of Ming collapse. The Manchu conquest succeeded partly because the Ming dynasty had already been fatally weakened by internal rebellions. Chang's insurrection in the west "decimated" the gentry, removing the social class that might otherwise have organized effective resistance to the Manchu invaders [054-188].

## Political Failure

Eberhard's analysis emphasizes the political limitations of the late Ming rebels. Despite their military successes, they failed to construct viable alternative governments:

> "It was typical, however, of all these insurgents that none of them had any great objective in view. They wanted to get enough to eat for themselves and their followers; they wanted to enrich themselves by conquest; but they were incapable of building up an ordered and new administration." [054-071]

This failure was not simply a matter of personal incapacity. The late Ming rebels operated in a context where the traditional gentry — the class that had historically provided administrative expertise to new dynasties — had been weakened or destroyed in the very regions where the rebels were strongest. [[wu-san-kui|Wu San-kui]], the Ming general who first allied with the Manchus and later rebelled against them, found that "in the west, where he was in power, the gentry counted for nothing; they had in any case been weaker in the west, and they had been decimated by the insurrection of Chang Hsien-chung" [054-188].

## Historical Assessment

Chang Hsien-chung occupies an ambiguous position in Chinese historiography. Traditional sources portray him as a brutal warlord; the Szechwan rebellion under his leadership is associated with massive population decline in the region. Modern historians, particularly Marxist-influenced scholarship, have attempted to rehabilitate the late Ming peasant rebels as proto-revolutionary figures responding to feudal oppression.

Eberhard's treatment is characteristically structural: Chang and his fellow rebels were products of a system in collapse, lacking the ideological resources or social base to construct a new order. Their historical significance lies less in what they achieved than in what their failure enabled — the Manchu conquest of China and the establishment of the [[ching-dynasty|Ch'ing dynasty]].
