# Section 43

[043-001] Large-scale landowning proved especially injurious in the Sung period,

[043-002] for two reasons. To begin with, the official salaries, which had always

[043-003] been small in China, were now totally inadequate, and so the officials

[043-004] were given a fixed quantity of land, the yield of which was regarded as

[043-005] an addition to salary. This land was free from part of the taxes. Before

[043-006] long the officials had secured the liberation of the whole of their land

[043-007] from the chief taxes. In the second place, the taxation system was

[043-008] simplified by making the amount of tax proportional to the amount of

[043-009] land owned. The lowest bracket, however, in this new system of taxation

[043-010] comprised more land than a poor peasant would actually own, and this was

[043-011] a heavy blow to the small peasant-owners, who in the past had paid a

[043-012] proportion of their produce. Most of them had so little land that they

[043-013] could barely live on its yield. Their liability to taxation was at all

[043-014] times a very heavy burden to them while the big landowners got off

[043-015] lightly. Thus this measure, though administratively a saving of

[043-016] expense, proved unsocial.

[043-017] All this made itself felt especially in the south with its great estates

[043-018] of tax-evading landowners. Here the remaining small peasant-owners had

[043-019] to pay the new taxes or to become tenants of the landowners and lose

[043-020] their property. The north was still suffering from the war-devastation

[043-021] of the tenth century. As the landlords were always the first sufferers

[043-022] from popular uprisings as well as from war, they had disappeared,

[043-023] leaving their former tenants as free peasants. From this period on, we

[043-024] have enough data to observe a social "law ": as the capital was the

[043-025] largest consumer, especially of high-priced products such as vegetables

[043-026] which could not be transported over long distances, the gentry always

[043-027] tried to control the land around the capital. Here, we find the highest

[043-028] concentration of landlords and tenants. Production in this circle

[043-029] shifted from rice and wheat to mulberry trees for silk, and vegetables

[043-030] grown under the trees. These urban demands resulted in the growth of an

[043-031] "industrial" quarter on the outskirts of the capital, in which

[043-032] especially silk for the upper classes was produced. The next circle also

[043-033] contained many landlords, but production was more in staple foods such

[043-034] as wheat and rice which could be transported. Exploitation in this

[043-035] second circle was not much less than in the first circle, because of

[043-036] less close supervision by the authorities. In the third circle we find

[043-037] independent subsistence farmers. Some provincial capitals, especially in

[043-038] Szechwan, exhibited a similar pattern of circles. With the shift of the

[043-039] capital, a complete reorganization appeared: landlords and officials

[043-040] gave up their properties, cultivation changed, and a new system of

[043-041] circles began to form around the new capital. We find, therefore, the

[043-042] grotesque result that the thinly populated province of Shensi in the

[043-043] north-west yielded about a quarter of the total revenues of the state:

[043-044] it had no large landowners, no wealthy gentry, with their evasion of

[043-045] taxation, only a mass of newly-settled small peasants' holdings. For

[043-046] this reason the government was particularly interested in that province,

[043-047] and closely watched the political changes in its neighbourhood. In 990 a

[043-048] man belonging to a sinified Toba family, living on the border of Shensi,

[043-049] had made himself king with the support of remnants of Toba tribes. In

[043-050] 1034 came severe fighting, and in 1038 the king proclaimed himself

[043-051] emperor, in the Hsia dynasty, and threatened the whole of north-western

[043-052] China. Tribute was now also paid to this state (250,000 strings), but

[043-053] the fight against it continued, to save that important province.

[043-054] These were the main events in internal and external affairs during the

[043-055] Sung period until 1068. It will be seen that foreign affairs were of

[043-056] much less importance than developments in the country.

[043-057] 3 _Reforms and Welfare schemes_

[043-058] The situation just described was bound to produce a reaction. In spite

[043-059] of the inflationary measures the revenue fell, partly in consequence of

[043-060] the tax evasions of the great landowners. It fell from 150,000,000 in

[043-061] 1021 to 116,000,000 in 1065. Expenditure did not fall, and there was a

[043-062] constant succession of budget deficits. The young emperor Shen Tsung

[043-063] (1068-1085) became convinced that the policy followed by the ruling

[043-064] clique of officials and gentry was bad, and he gave his adhesion to a

[043-065] small group led by Wang An-shih (1021-1086). The ruling gentry clique

[043-066] represented especially the interests of the large tea producers and

[043-067] merchants in Szechwan and Kiangsi. It advocated a policy of

[043-068] _laisser-faire_ in trade: it held that everything would adjust itself.

[043-069] Wang An-shih himself came from Kiangsi and was therefore supported at

[043-070] first by the government clique, within which the Kiangsi group was

[043-071] trying to gain predominance over the Szechwan group. But Wang An-shih

[043-072] came from a poor family, as did his supporters, for whom he quickly

[043-073] secured posts. They represented the interests of the small landholders

[043-074] and the small dealers. This group succeeded in gaining power, and in

[043-075] carrying out a number of reforms, all directed against the monopolist

[043-076] merchants. Credits for small peasants were introduced, and officials

[043-077] were given bigger salaries, in order to make them independent and to

[043-078] recruit officials who were not big landowners. The army was greatly

[043-079] reduced, and in addition to the paid soldiery a national militia was

[043-080] created. Special attention was paid to the province of Shensi, whose

[043-081] conditions were taken more or less as a model.

[043-082] It seems that one consequence of Wang's reforms was a strong fall in the

[043-083] prices, i.e. a deflation; therefore, as soon as the first decrees were

[043-084] issued, the large plantation owners and the merchants who were allied to

[043-085] them, offered furious opposition. A group of officials and landlords who

[043-086] still had large properties in the vicinity of Loyang--at that time a

[043-087] quiet cultural centre--also joined them. Even some of Wang An-shih's

[043-088] former adherents came out against him. After a few years the emperor was

[043-089] no longer able to retain Wang An-shih and had to abandon the new policy.

[043-090] How really economic interests were here at issue may be seen from the

[043-091] fact that for many of the new decrees which were not directly concerned

[043-092] with economic affairs, such, for instance, as the reform of the

[043-093] examination system, Wang An-shih was strongly attacked though his

[043-094] opponents had themselves advocated them in the past and had no practical

[043-095] objection to offer to them. The contest, however, between the two groups

[043-096] was not over. The monopolistic landowners and their merchants had the

[043-097] upper hand from 1086 to 1102, but then the advocates of the policy

[043-098] represented by Wang again came into power for a short time. They had but

[043-099] little success to show, as they did not remain in power long enough and,

[043-100] owing to the strong opposition, they were never able to make their

[043-101] control really effective.

[043-102] Basically, both groups were against allowing the developing middle class

[043-103] and especially the merchants to gain too much freedom, and whatever

[043-104] freedom they in fact gained, came through extra-legal or illegal

[043-105] practices. A proverb of the time said "People hate their ruler as

[043-106] animals hate the net (of the hunter)". The basic laws of medieval times

[043-107] which had attempted to create stable social classes remained: down to

[043-108] the nineteenth century there were slaves, different classes of serfs or

[043-109] "commoners", and free burghers. Craftsmen remained under work

[043-110] obligation. Merchants were second-class people. Each class had to wear

[043-111] dresses of special colour and material, so that the social status of a

[043-112] person, even if he was not an official and thus recognizable by his

[043-113] insignia, was immediately clear when one saw him. The houses of

[043-114] different classes differed from one another by the type of tiles, the

[043-115] decorations of the doors and gates; the size of the main reception room

[043-116] of the house was prescribed and was kept small for all non-officials;

[043-117] and even size and form of the tombs was prescribed in detail for each

[043-118] class. Once a person had a certain privilege, he and his descendants

[043-119] even if they had lost their position in the bureaucracy, retained these

[043-120] privileges over generations. All burghers were admitted to the

[043-121] examinations and, thus, there was a certain social mobility allowed

[043-122] within the leading class of the society, and a new "small gentry"

[043-123] developed by this system.

[043-124] Yet, the wars of the transition period had created a feeling of

[043-125] insecurity within the gentry. The eleventh and twelfth centuries were

[043-126] periods of extensive social legislation in order to give the lower

[043-127] classes some degree of security and thus prevent them from attempting to

[043-128] upset the status quo. In addition to the "ever-normal granaries" of the

[043-129] state, "social granaries" were revived, into which all farmers of a

[043-130] village had to deliver grain for periods of need. In 1098 a bureau for

[043-131] housing and care was created which created homes for the old and

[043-132] destitute; 1102 a bureau for medical care sent state doctors to homes

[043-133] and hospitals as well as to private homes to care for poor patients;

[043-134] from 1104 a bureau of burials took charge of the costs of burials of

[043-135] poor persons. Doctors as craftsmen were under corvée obligation and

[043-136] could easily be ordered by the state. Often, however, Buddhist priests

[043-137] took charge of medical care, burial costs and hospitalization. The state

[043-138] gave them premiums if they did good work. The Ministry of Civil Affairs

[043-139] made the surveys of cases and costs, while the Ministry of Finances paid

[043-140] the costs. We hear of state orphanages in 1247, a free pharmacy in 1248,

[043-141] state hospitals were reorganized in 1143. In 1167 the government gave

[043-142] low-interest loans to poor persons and (from 1159 on) sold cheap grain

[043-143] from state granaries. Fire protection services in large cities were

[043-144] organized. Finally, from 1141 on, the government opened up to

[043-145] twenty-three geisha houses for the entertainment of soldiers who were

[043-146] far from home in the capital and had no possibility for other

[043-147] amusements. Public baths had existed already some centuries ago; now

[043-148] Buddhist temples opened public baths as social service.

[043-149] Social services for the officials were also extended. Already from the

[043-150] eighth century on, offices were closed every tenth day and during

[043-151] holidays, a total of almost eighty days per year. Even criminals got

[043-152] some leave and exiles had the right of a home leave once every three

[043-153] years. The pensions for retired officials after the age of seventy which

[043-154] amounted to 50 per cent of the salary from the eighth century on, were

[043-155] again raised, though widows did not receive benefits.

[043-156] 4 _Cultural situation (philosophy, religion, literature, painting_)

[043-157] Culturally the eleventh century was the most active period China had so

[043-158] far experienced, apart from the fourth century B.C. As a consequence of

[043-159] the immensely increased number of educated people resulting from the

[043-160] invention of printing, circles of scholars and private schools set up by

[043-161] scholars were scattered all over the country. The various philosophical

[043-162] schools differed in their political attitude and in the choice of

[043-163] literary models with which they were politically in sympathy. Thus Wang

[043-164] An-shih and his followers preferred the rigid classic style of Han Yü

[043-165] (768-825) who lived in the T'ang period and had also been an opponent of

[043-166] the monopolistic tendencies of pre-capitalism. For the Wang An-shih

[043-167] group formed itself into a school with a philosophy of its own and with

[043-168] its own commentaries on the classics. As the representative of the small

[043-169] merchants and the small landholders, this school advocated policies of

[043-170] state control and specialized in the study and annotation of classical

[043-171] books which seemed to favour their ideas.

[043-172] But the Wang An-shih school was unable to hold its own against the

[043-173] school that stood for monopolist trade capitalism, the new philosophy

[043-174] described as Neo-Confucianism or the Sung school. Here Confucianism and

[043-175] Buddhism were for the first time united. In the last centuries,

[043-176] Buddhistic ideas had penetrated all of Chinese culture: the slaughtering

[043-177] of animals and the executions of criminals were allowed only on certain

[043-178] days, in accordance with Buddhist rules. Formerly, monks and nuns had to

[043-179] greet the emperor as all citizens had to do; now they were exempt from

[043-180] this rule. On the other hand, the first Sung emperor was willing to

[043-181] throw himself to the earth in front of the Buddha statues, but he was

[043-182] told he did not have to do it because he was the "Buddha of the present

[043-183] time" and thus equal to the God. Buddhist priests participated in the

[043-184] celebrations on the emperor's birthday, and emperors from time to time

[043-185] gave free meals to large crowds of monks. Buddhist thought entered the

[043-186] field of justice: in Sung time we hear complaints that judges did not

[043-187] apply the laws and showed laxity, because they hoped to gain religious

[043-188] merit by sparing the lives of criminals. We had seen how the main

[043-189] current of Buddhism had changed from a revolutionary to a reactionary

[043-190] doctrine. The new greater gentry of the eleventh century adopted a

[043-191] number of elements of this reactionary Buddhism and incorporated them in

[043-192] the Confucianist system. This brought into Confucianism a metaphysic

[043-193] which it had lacked in the past, greatly extending its influence on the

[043-194] people and at the same time taking the wind out of the sails of

[043-195] Buddhism. The greater gentry never again placed themselves on the side

[043-196] of the Buddhist Church as they had done in the T'ang period. When they

[043-197] got tired of Confucianism, they interested themselves in Taoism of the

[043-198] politically innocent, escapist, meditative Buddhism.
