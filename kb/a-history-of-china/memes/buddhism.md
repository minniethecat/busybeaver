## Overview

Buddhism was the most significant foreign cultural import in pre-modern Chinese history. Entering China from India via the Silk Road through Central Asia during the [[han-dynasty|Later Han dynasty]] (first century CE), it grew from a marginal foreign cult into one of the three great religious-philosophical traditions of China, alongside [[confucianism]] and [[taoism]]. Eberhard traces Buddhism's trajectory in China through three major phases: its introduction and gradual spread during the period of division, its triumph as a dominant cultural force under the [[tang-dynasty|T'ang]], and its eventual retreat in the face of Neo-Confucian revival.

Buddhism offered China something its indigenous traditions lacked: a developed metaphysics, a professional celibate clergy, and a sophisticated system of monastic institutions. It brought with it not only religious ideas but also Indian art, medicine, astronomy, and logic [022-024]. At its height, Buddhism shaped everything from imperial politics to popular piety, from painting and sculpture to the development of printing.

## Introduction and Early Spread

> "According to our present knowledge, Buddhism entered China from India through Central Asia. The first Buddhist missionaries arrived in China in the first century A.D., during the Later Han dynasty." [021-126]

Buddhism initially found acceptance among certain educated circles in the Han capital. Its ideas were foreign and its doctrines complex, yet it offered intellectual stimulation at a time when the established [[confucianism|Confucian]] orthodoxy was losing its creative energy [021-140]. During the centuries of division that followed the Han collapse (220–589 CE), Buddhism found fertile ground.

Several factors contributed to its spread:

- **Political fragmentation**: With no single imperial authority enforcing ideological conformity, diverse ideas flourished
- **Foreign dynasties**: Non-Chinese rulers of the northern kingdoms, themselves outsiders, were often well-disposed toward Buddhism [027-118]
- **Popular appeal**: Buddhism's promise of salvation, its rich ritual life, and its compassionate deities appealed to ordinary people in ways that the more austere Confucian tradition did not
- **Trade networks**: Buddhist monks traveled along the same Central Asian trade routes as merchants, creating a transnational religious network [025-097]

## The Triumph of Buddhism

> "The peoples of Central Asia, of the Turkish empire, were all captured by Buddhism, and especially by its Mahayana form." [029-148]

Buddhism reached its zenith of influence during the [[tang-dynasty|T'ang dynasty]] (618–907 CE). The T'ang was a cosmopolitan age in which foreign religions — Buddhism, Nestorian Christianity, Zoroastrianism, and Manichaeism — all found a place. Eberhard notes that a decisive element in the preference for Buddhism among non-Chinese rulers was "certainly the fact that Buddhism accepted all foreigners alike" [029-150].

Mahayana Buddhism, with its pantheon of bodhisattvas, its elaborate ritual, and its philosophical depth, became the dominant form in China. The "Khotan Buddhism" of Central Asia had a special influence on Chinese Buddhist development [027-146].

## Schools and Forms

Buddhism in China developed several distinctive schools:

- **T'ien-t'ai**: A comprehensive synthesis of Buddhist doctrines, named after the mountain where its founder lived
- **Ch'an** (Zen in Japanese): Emphasizing direct meditative experience over scriptural study, Ch'an became the most influential school among the educated elite
- **Pure Land**: A devotional form centered on faith in the Buddha Amitabha, enormously popular among ordinary people

> "In spite of Buddhism, the old religion of the peasants continued to exist. The peasant continued to worship his local gods, his ancestors, and the forces of nature." [027-175]

Eberhard emphasizes that even at Buddhism's height, the old folk religion persisted alongside it, creating a layered religious landscape rather than a simple replacement.

## Retreat and Transformation

By the late T'ang, Buddhism faced growing criticism from Confucian scholars who viewed it as a foreign, parasitic institution that drained state resources. The great persecution of 845 CE, in which thousands of monasteries were destroyed and monks and nuns forced to return to lay life, marked a turning point [029-142].

Yet Buddhism did not disappear. Instead, it was gradually absorbed and transformed. Elements of Buddhist philosophy were incorporated into [[confucianism|Neo-Confucian]] thought, Buddhist artistic motifs became part of the Chinese visual vocabulary, and Buddhist-inspired popular practices — from vegetarianism to funeral rites — became permanent features of Chinese life. The foreign religion had become Chinese.
