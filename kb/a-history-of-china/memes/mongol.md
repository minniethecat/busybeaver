## Overview

The Mongols were the first alien people to rule the whole of China, and their impact on Chinese history was profound and enduring. Eberhard identifies the Mongol epoch (1280–1368) as the opening chapter of China's "Period of Absolutism," noting that of the 631 years from 1280 to 1911, China was under alien rule for 355 — first the Mongols, and later the Tungus Manchus [046-121]. The Mongol conquest brought China into direct contact with the wider Eurasian world, from Persia to Europe, but also imposed a harsh system of ethnic stratification, drained the country of metallic currency, and impoverished the peasantry through heavy taxation, forced labour, and land expropriation [047-147].

The Mongol story in China spans far more than the Yuan dynasty itself. Proto-Mongol tribes had inhabited the northern frontier since antiquity; Mongol-led confederations challenged Chinese states from the [[tang-dynasty|T'ang]] period onward; and after their expulsion from China in 1368, the Mongols remained a formidable military threat on the northern border throughout the [[ming-dynasty|Ming]] epoch. Their legacy includes the elevation of [[peking|Peking]] to the status of a true imperial capital, the introduction of Lamaist [[buddhism|Buddhism]] as a state-patronized religion, and a lasting sense of Chinese national consciousness forged in reaction to Mongol discriminatory rule.

## Origins and Early History

> "In the north, there were the tribes later known as Mongols, the so-called proto-Mongols." [003-063]

The Mongols belong to the broader family of Altaic peoples of the eastern Asian steppe, alongside the Turks and Tunguses. Archaeological evidence traces a purely Mongoloid people with a neolithic culture in North China [003-005]. In the first millennium BCE, the pastoral nomads of the northern grasslands included tribal groups that would later coalesce into the Mongol confederation. Eberhard notes that these early peoples, like the Tunguses, belonged to the Mongol race [003-064], though not all northern nomads were Mongols — some were Turks, and others belonged to distinct ethnic groupings [003-066].

During the T'ang period, the Kitan — a league of tribes under the leadership of an apparently Mongol tribe — grew steadily stronger in north-eastern Mongolia and became the dominant power in the north by about A.D. 900 [044-152]. The Kitan claimed the Chinese imperial throne and ruled parts of North China as the Liao dynasty (937–1125), foreshadowing the more comprehensive Mongol conquest to come. Their tribal name "Kitan" is the origin of the medieval European name for China, "Cathay."

The Mongols proper emerged as a distinct political force in the twelfth century. After the Sha-t'o Turks withdrew from their dominating position in China in the tenth century, they returned to Mongolia and united with the Ta-tan (Tatars), forming a new league of tribes consisting mainly of Mongols and Turks [046-083]. By 1139, Mongol power was growing rapidly, and in 1147 the Mongol tribal leader declined a high title offered by the Chin (Juchên) emperor and instead "assumed the title of emperor of the 'greater Mongol empire'" [046-095]. This moment marks the formal beginning of Mongol imperial ambition.

## Rise of the Mongol Empire under Genghiz Khan

The foundation of the world-conquering Mongol empire is inseparable from the figure of Genghiz Khan (c. 1162–1227), who became the leader of the Mongols in 1189 and "made the Mongols the greatest power of central Asia" [046-097]. Eberhard's text mentions Genghiz Khan's great campaign against Turkestan, in which he was accompanied by the Kitan statesman Yeh-lü Ch'u-ts'ai, who left an account of the journey and many poems about Samarkand [048-120]. Genghiz Khan was also reportedly inclined toward [[taoism|Taoism]] and admitted a Taoist monk to his camp between 1221 and 1224, whose travel account has been preserved as an important historical source [048-126].

Under Genghiz Khan's leadership, the Mongols developed a military organization and technical equipment that were "exceptionally advanced for their day" [046-140]. They already made use of small cannon in laying siege to towns during their campaigns against the Sung [046-143]. The Mongol political league was also numerically stronger than those of earlier alien peoples, and their ranks included not only ethnic Mongols but also Turks, Tunguses, [[uighur|Uighurs]], men from Central Asia and the Middle East, and even Europeans [046-146].

The Mongol assault on northern China proceeded methodically. In 1210, the moment they had conquered the Hsia (the Tangut state in the Ordos region), the Mongols began their first great assault against the Chin empire of the Juchên [046-101]. By 1215–1217 they had taken the key military positions, and after a pause while they turned against the West, the Chin empire finally fell to the Mongols in 1234 [046-106]. Many of the Chin entered Mongol service and, with Mongol permission, returned to Manchuria — later to re-emerge in history as the Manchus [046-107].

## The Mongol Epoch in China (Yuan Dynasty, 1280–1368)

### Kublai Khan and the Conquest of the Sung

> "Kublai, himself recognized that China could not be treated in quite the same way as the Mongols' previous conquests; he therefore separated the empire in China from the rest of the Mongol empire. Mongol China became an independent realm within the Mongol empire, a sort of Dominion." [046-160]

The conquest of South China — the empire of the Sung dynasty — was undertaken only after long and remarkably modern tactical preparation [046-157]. Kublai Khan (r. 1260–1294), the conqueror of the Sung, understood that governing China required a different approach from the Mongols' earlier conquests. His separation of China from the greater Mongol empire established the Yuan dynasty as a distinct political entity.

Kublai's military campaigns extended beyond China. A concluding war against Japan in 1281 ended in complete failure when the invasion fleet was destroyed by a storm; Chinese ships and soldiers were furnished for this expedition [047-020]. Mongol wars followed in the south: Burma (1282), Annam and Cambodia (1284), and Java (1292). Java could not be held, but almost the whole of Indo-China came under Mongol rule, to the satisfaction of Chinese merchants for whom Indo-China had been a principal export market since the Sung period [047-025].

### Nationality Legislation and Social Structure

The most distinctive feature of Mongol rule in China was the "nationality legislation" — the first of its kind in the Far East — designed to protect the Mongol ruling minority [046-166]. The entire population was divided into four ranked categories:

| Rank | Group | Composition and Rights |
|------|-------|----------------------|
| 1 | Mongols | The privileged ruling class, organized militarily and distributed in garrisons over all major cities; all higher government posts reserved for them [046-169] |
| 2 | Central Asian Auxiliaries | Naimans, [[uighur|Uighurs]], Turks, Tanguts, and others; admitted to government service and enjoyed commercial privileges; many were merchants [046-171] |
| 3 | North Chinese | Inhabitants of the former Chin empire; treated better than the South Chinese and more willing to collaborate with the Mongols [046-113] |
| 4 | South Chinese | Former subjects of the Sung; the lowest status, virtually without rights; intermarriage with them was prohibited [047-001] |

South Chinese were forbidden to carry arms and, for a time, even to learn Mongol or other foreign languages [047-003]. Their ignorance of northern and western Asian languages prevented them from engaging in commerce like the foreign merchants, while foreigners were free to learn Chinese and gain a footing in internal trade [047-007].

The gentry's response to the Mongol conquest was pragmatic. As the Sung dynasty collapsed, "the greater gentry enthusiastically went over to the Mongols, hoping to save their property and so their influence by quickly joining the enemy" [046-021]. The Mongols removed the gentry from all political posts but left them their estates, and before long the greater gentry reappeared in political life [046-023].

### Peking as Imperial Capital

The Mongols made [[peking|Peking]] their capital — a natural choice, for it was near their homeland in Mongolia, and the emperor and his entourage could return there in summer when China became too hot or humid [047-043]. From [[peking|Peking]] they maintained contact with the rest of the Mongol empire. The city was transformed into a magnificent capital on a scale never before seen in China, with great palaces built by Chinese forced labour [047-051]. The vast population of the capital — the court, officials, foreign merchants, and servile labourers — required massive food shipments from the south, and the transport system inherited by the Mongols had not been designed for such long-distance traffic. A great fleet had to be built, canals regulated, and new canals excavated, all requiring vast quantities of forced labour brought from afar [047-069].

### Economic Impact

The Mongol epoch was "one of continual and rapid impoverishment in China, simultaneously with a great display of magnificence" [047-152]. Several factors converged to drain the economy:

- Approximately one million Mongols had to be maintained by the peasantry [047-082]
- Temples were granted great estates free from taxation — some 170,000 *ch'ing* of cultivated land [047-103]
- Some 200,000 *ch'ing* were turned into military settlements whose taxes went directly to the army [047-106]
- Mongol nobles and dignitaries received large land grants taken from free peasants [047-112]
- In North China, many peasants were entirely dispossessed, their land turned into pasturage for Mongol horses [047-116]
- Foreign merchants, free of taxation, accumulated wealth that largely flowed out of China [047-132]
- The government issued great quantities of paper money, which quickly depreciated [047-142]

> "The enthusiastic descriptions of the Mongol empire in China offered by travellers from the Near East or from Europe, such as Marco Polo, give an entirely false picture: as foreigners they had a privileged position, living in the cities and seeing nothing of the situation of the general population." [047-154]

Statistics from 1329 recorded some 7,600,000 persons in the empire officially listed as starving — a substantial percentage of a total population estimated at 45,000,000 [047-161].

## Culture under Mongol Rule

Despite the economic hardship, the Mongol period produced significant cultural achievements, particularly in theatre and the arts. "The greatest literary achievements of the Mongol period belong beyond question to the theatre (or, rather, opera)" [048-140]. The Mongol emperors were great theatre-goers, and wealthy families were also enthusiasts, so that educated people gradually devoted themselves to writing librettos — work that in the past had been left to others. Most authors used pseudonyms, partly because playwriting was not considered befitting for a scholar and partly because their works criticized contemporary conditions [048-146].

Notable dramatic works of the period include *P'i-p'a-chi* ("The Story of a Lute"), written about 1356, and *Chao-shih ku-erh-chi* ("The Story of the Orphan of Chao"), a work that so enthralled Voltaire that he made a paraphrase of it [048-154]. The southern drama *Hsi-hsiang-chi* ("The Romance of the Western Chamber") by Wang Shih-fu and Kuan Han-ch'ing became one of the most famous works of the Chinese theatrical tradition [048-158].

In the fine arts, foreign influence was more pronounced than in literature, largely because of the Mongol rulers' preference for Lamaism — the distinctive Tibetan form of Buddhism that fused Bon elements with Mahayana doctrine [048-164]. Religious sculpture came under Tibetan and Nepalese influence, particularly through the sculptor Aniko (born 1244 in Nepal), and this influence lasted until the Manchu epoch [048-172]. The Mongols also developed the art of carpet-knotting in China, found to this day only in North China [049-008], and cloisonne technique probably arrived through West Asian influence during this period [049-013].

Painting, however, remained largely free from alien influence. The most famous painters of the Mongol epoch were Chao Meng-fu (1254–1322), a relative of the deposed Sung imperial family, and Ni Tsan (1301–1374) [049-017]. Chinese scholars who withdrew from official life during the Mongol period devoted themselves to poetry, meticulously observing the rules laid down by the various Sung schools, though their work was more often "routine productions rather than the outcome of any true poetic inspiration" [048-113].

## Decline and Fall

> "The Mongol dynasty had been brought down, almost without resistance." [048-072]

The collapse of Mongol rule in China, when it came, was rapid. Eberhard identifies three principal reasons [048-081]:

1. **Military decay**: The Mongol soldiers, born in China and living on pay in their garrisons with nothing to do, had seen nothing of war and learned nothing of the soldiers' trade. By about 1320, an army nominally 1,000 strong could not defeat a group of barely fifty bandits [047-034].

2. **Administrative failure**: The attempt to rule the empire through Mongols or other aliens while excluding the Chinese gentry from administration failed through ignorance of revenue sources and abuses arising from the favoured treatment of aliens [048-085].

3. **Loss of interest**: By the mid-fourteenth century, it was obvious to the Mongols that their hold over China was increasingly precarious and that there was little to be gained from the impoverished country. They "seem in consequence to have lost interest in the troublesome task of maintaining their rule, preferring, in so far as they had not already entirely degenerated, to return to their old home in the north" [048-092].

The popular risings that brought down the dynasty began as social movements, not national ones: "They never attacked the Mongols as aliens, but always the rich in general, whether Chinese or foreign" [047-169]. But when the government tightened its nationality laws in response, the character of the risings changed: within a few years "the aim of the rebels became no longer merely the expulsion of the rich but also the expulsion of the Mongols: a social movement thus became a national one" [048-016].

The decisive figure was [[chu-yuan-chang|Chu Yuan-chang]], the son of a small peasant — one of only three men of peasant origin to become emperor and found a dynasty in all of Chinese history [049-043]. After joining the rebel leader Kuo Tzu-hsing in 1353 and eventually taking over his army, Chu captured Nanking, crossed the [[yangtze-river|Yangtze]], and conquered the wealthy south-eastern provinces. By winning gentry support through abandoning indiscriminate violence against the rich, he transformed himself from a peasant rebel into a contender for the throne [048-054]. In 1368 his generals captured [[peking|Peking]] almost without a blow; "The Mongol ruler fled on horseback with his immediate entourage into the north of China, and soon after into Mongolia" [048-070].

## Aftermath and Legacy

The Mongols were driven out of China but never truly beaten in their homeland. After their withdrawal, they reorganized and remained capable of counter-thrusts against the Ming frontier for two centuries [049-067]. The most serious Mongol threat came from Yen-ta (Altan Khan), who from 1549 onward conducted sustained campaigns against China, at one point reaching [[peking|Peking]] and laying siege to it [053-107]. A compromise was eventually reached through state-controlled frontier markets where Mongols could trade their goods on favourable terms, and in 1571 Yen-ta accepted a Chinese title [053-115]. This frontier trade contributed to the formation of a local merchant class in Shansi province, from which the first Chinese bankers later emerged [053-118].

The Mongol epoch left indelible marks. It elevated [[peking|Peking]] from a regional centre to a true imperial capital. It introduced a sharpened sense of Chinese national consciousness: the discriminatory laws of the Mongols "greatly fostered this feeling," so that from the Ming period onward, "it was regarded a shame to serve a foreigner as official, even if he was a ruler of China" [049-064]. The Mongol model of absolutist monarchy — the emperor as master over the life and death of his subjects — shaped Ming and Ch'ing political culture [049-161]. And the period's cultural achievements, particularly in opera, Lamaist art, and decorative crafts, permanently enriched Chinese civilization.

The Mongol epoch also demonstrated a recurring pattern in Chinese history: the conquest dynasty that enters China as a warrior elite, is gradually softened by the sedentary wealth it seizes, and eventually collapses — either absorbed, expelled, or both. This pattern would repeat under the Manchus three centuries later, though with important differences that Eberhard invites us to compare [048-098].
