## Overview

Li T'ai-po (701–762), commonly known as Li Po or Li Bai, stands as one of the two supreme poets of China's golden age of poetry during the T'ang dynasty. Alongside his contemporary [[tu-fu|Tu Fu]], Li Po represents the pinnacle of classical Chinese verse. While [[tu-fu|Tu Fu]] was known for his formal precision and moral seriousness, Li Po's poetry was celebrated for its spontaneous feeling, imaginative power, and Taoist-inspired freedom.

> Widespread fame came to Li T'ai-po (701-762) and Tu Fu (712-770); in China two poets almost equal to these two in fame are hardly to be found. [037-068]

## Poetry and Style

Li Po's verse broke from the formalistic traditions of the preceding Southern Dynasties period. Earlier poetry had been a learned pastime characterized by precious, formalistic compositions. The T'ang poets, and Li Po foremost among them, brought genuine emotion and personal expression into Chinese verse, transforming it from a courtly accomplishment into a living art form.

> The T'ang poets brought to it genuine feeling. [037-068]

Li Po's poetry ranged across diverse subjects: the joys of wine and friendship, the beauty of mountain landscapes, the melancholy of separation, and the fleeting nature of human existence. His most famous works include "Drinking Alone by Moonlight," "The Road to Shu is Hard," and numerous quatrains that capture moments of intense feeling in economical form. He was renowned for his ability to compose verse extemporaneously, often while drinking, giving rise to the legend that he drowned while trying to embrace the moon's reflection in a river.

## Life and Career

Though of uncertain origins — possibly born in Central Asia to a merchant family — Li Po spent most of his career in China proper. He briefly enjoyed imperial patronage at the T'ang court in Ch'ang-an under [[hsuan-tsung|Hsüan Tsung]], where his poetic gifts earned him recognition. However, his unconventional personality and political indiscretions led to his dismissal from court.

> On Li T'ai-po and Po Chü-i we have well-written books by Western scholars. [070-183]

Li Po spent his later years as an itinerant poet, wandering through the famous landscapes of central and eastern China. His attachment to [[taoism|Taoist philosophy]] and practice deepened during this period, and he was formally initiated into the Taoist priesthood. His poetry from these years reflects both a deepening spiritual engagement and a persistent attachment to the pleasures of the material world.

## Legacy

Li Po's influence on Chinese literature is incalculable. His poems are memorized by schoolchildren, quoted in daily conversation, and have inspired countless imitators across the centuries. In the West, his work has been widely translated, with notable versions by Arthur Waley, Ezra Pound, and many others. He remains, alongside [[tu-fu|Tu Fu]], the most universally recognized name in Chinese poetry.

> Poets include T'ao Ch'ien, Po Chü-i, Li T'ai-po, Tu Fu, and others of the first rank. [074-084]
