# Section 64

[064-001] Until the time of the "Manchurian incident" (1931), the Nanking

[064-002] government steadily grew in strength. It gained the confidence of the

[064-003] western powers, who proposed to make use of it in opposition to Japan's

[064-004] policy of expansion in the Pacific sphere. On the strength of this

[064-005] favourable situation in its foreign relations, the Nanking government

[064-006] succeeded in getting rid of one after another of the Capitulations.

[064-007] Above all, the administration of the "Maritime Customs", that is to say

[064-008] of the collection of duties on imports and exports, was brought under

[064-009] the control of the Chinese government: until then it had been under

[064-010] foreign control. Now that China could act with more freedom in the

[064-011] matter of tariffs, the government had greater financial resources, and

[064-012] through this and other measures it became financially more independent

[064-013] of the provinces. It succeeded in building up a small but modern army,

[064-014] loyal to the government and superior to the still existing provincial

[064-015] armies. This army gained its military experience in skirmishes with the

[064-016] Communists and the remaining generals.

[064-017] It is true that when in 1931 the Japanese occupied Manchuria, Nanking

[064-018] was helpless, since Manchuria was only loosely associated with Nanking,

[064-019] and its governor, Chang Hsüeh-liang, had tried to remain independent of

[064-020] it. Thus Manchuria was lost almost without a blow. On the other hand,

[064-021] the fighting with Japan that broke out soon afterwards in Shanghai

[064-022] brought credit to the young Nanking army, though owing to its numerical

[064-023] inferiority it was unsuccessful. China protested to the League of

[064-024] Nations against its loss of Manchuria. The League sent a commission (the

[064-025] Lytton Commission), which condemned Japan's action, but nothing further

[064-026] happened, and China indignantly broke away from her association with the

[064-027] Western powers (1932-1933). In view of the tense European situation

[064-028] (the beginning of the Hitler era in Germany, and the Italian plans of

[064-029] expansion), the Western powers did not want to fight Japan on China's

[064-030] behalf, and without that nothing more could be done. They pursued,

[064-031] indeed, a policy of playing off Japan against China, in order to keep

[064-032] those two powers occupied with each other, and so to divert Japan from

[064-033] Indo-China and the Pacific.

[064-034] China had thus to be prepared for being involved one day in a great war

[064-035] with Japan. Chiang Kai-shek wanted to postpone war as long as possible.

[064-036] He wanted time to establish his power more thoroughly within the

[064-037] country, and to strengthen his army. In regard to external relations,

[064-038] the great powers would have to decide their attitude sooner or later.

[064-039] America could not be expected to take up a clear attitude: she was for

[064-040] peace and commerce, and she made greater profits out of her relations

[064-041] with Japan than with China; she sent supplies to both (until 1941). On

[064-042] the other hand, Britain and France were more and more turning away from

[064-043] Japan, and Russo-Japanese relations were at all times tense. Japan tried

[064-044] to emerge from her isolation by joining the "axis powers", Germany and

[064-045] Italy (1936); but it was still doubtful whether the Western powers would

[064-046] proceed with Russia, and therefore against Japan, or with the Axis, and

[064-047] therefore in alliance with Japan.

[064-048] Japan for her part considered that if she was to raise the standard of

[064-049] living of her large population and to remain a world power, she must

[064-050] bring into being her "Greater East Asia", so as to have the needed raw

[064-051] material sources and export markets in the event of a collision with the

[064-052] Western powers; in addition to this, she needed a security girdle as

[064-053] extensive as possible in case of a conflict with Russia. In any case,

[064-054] "Greater East Asia" must be secured before the European conflict should

[064-055] break out.

[064-056] 4 _The Sino-Japanese war_ (1937-1945)

[064-057] Accordingly, from 1933 onward Japan followed up her conquest of

[064-058] Manchuria by bringing her influence to bear in Inner Mongolia and in

[064-059] North China. She succeeded first, by means of an immense system of

[064-060] smuggling, currency manipulation, and propaganda, in bringing a number

[064-061] of Mongol princes over to her side, and then (at the end of 1935) in

[064-062] establishing a semi-dependent government in North China. Chiang Kai-shek

[064-063] took no action.

[064-064] The signal for the outbreak of war was an "incident" by the Marco Polo

[064-065] Bridge, south of Peking (July 7th, 1937). The Japanese government

[064-066] profited by a quite unimportant incident, undoubtedly provoked by the

[064-067] Japanese, in order to extend its dominion a little further. China still

[064-068] hesitated; there were negotiations. Japan brought up reinforcements and

[064-069] put forward demands which China could not be expected to be ready to

[064-070] fulfil. Japan then occupied Peking and Tientsin and wide regions between

[064-071] them and south of them. The Chinese soldiers stationed there withdrew

[064-072] almost without striking a blow, but formed up again and began to offer

[064-073] resistance. In order to facilitate the planned occupation of North

[064-074] China, including the province of Shantung, Japan decided on a

[064-075] diversionary campaign against Shanghai. The Nanking government sent its

[064-076] best troops to the new front, and held it for nearly three months

[064-077] against superior forces; but meanwhile the Japanese steadily advanced in

[064-078] North China. On November 9th Nanking fell into their hands. By the

[064-079] beginning of January 1938, the province of Shantung had also been

[064-080] conquered.

[064-081] Chiang Kai-shek and his government fled to Ch'ung-k'ing (Chungking), the

[064-082] most important commercial and financial centre of the interior after

[064-083] Hankow, which was soon threatened by the Japanese fleet. By means of a

[064-084] number of landings the Japanese soon conquered the whole coast of China,

[064-085] so cutting off all supplies to the country; against hard fighting in

[064-086] some places they pushed inland along the railways and conquered the

[064-087] whole eastern half of China, the richest and most highly developed part

[064-088] of the country. Chiang Kai-shek had the support only of the

[064-089] agriculturally rich province of Szechwan, and of the scarcely developed

[064-090] provinces surrounding it. Here there was as yet no industry. Everything

[064-091] in the way of machinery and supplies that could be transported from the

[064-092] hastily dismantled factories was carried westward. Students and

[064-093] professors went west with all the contents of their universities, and

[064-094] worked on in small villages under very difficult conditions--one of the

[064-095] most memorable achievements of this war for China. But all this was by

[064-096] no means enough for waging a defensive war against Japan. Even the

[064-097] famous Burma Road could not save China.

[064-098] By 1940-1941 Japan had attained her war aim: China was no longer a

[064-099] dangerous adversary. She was still able to engage in small-scale

[064-100] fighting, but could no longer secure any decisive result. Puppet

[064-101] governments were set up in Peking, Canton, and Nanking, and the Japanese

[064-102] waited for these governments gradually to induce supporters of Chiang

[064-103] Kai-shek to come over to their side. Most was expected of Wang

[064-104] Ching-wei, who headed the new Nanking government. He was one of the

[064-105] oldest followers of Sun Yat-sen, and was regarded as a democrat. In

[064-106] 1925, after Sun Yat-sen's death, he had been for a time the head of the

[064-107] Nanking government, and for a short time in 1930 he had led a government

[064-108] in Peking that was opposed to Chiang Kai-shek's dictatorship. Beyond any

[064-109] question Wang still had many followers, including some in the highest

[064-110] circles at Chungking, men of eastern China who considered that

[064-111] collaboration with Japan, especially in the economic field, offered good

[064-112] prospects. Japan paid lip service to this policy: there was talk of

[064-113] sister peoples, which could help each other and supply each other's

[064-114] needs. There was propaganda for a new "Greater East Asian" philosophy,

[064-115] _Wang-tao_, in accordance with which all the peoples of the East could

[064-116] live together in peace under a thinly disguised dictatorship. What

[064-117] actually happened was that everywhere Japanese capitalists established

[064-118] themselves in the former Chinese industrial plants, bought up land and

[064-119] securities, and exploited the country for the conduct of their war.

[064-120] After the great initial successes of Hitlerite Germany in 1939-1941,

[064-121] Japan became convinced that the time had come for a decisive blow

[064-122] against the positions of the Western European powers and the United

[064-123] States in the Far East. Lightning blows were struck at Hong Kong and

[064-124] Singapore, at French Indo-China, and at the Netherlands East Indies. The

[064-125] American navy seemed to have been eliminated by the attack on Pearl

[064-126] Harbour, and one group of islands after another fell into the hands of

[064-127] the Japanese. Japan was at the gates of India and Australia. Russia was

[064-128] carrying on a desperate defensive struggle against the Axis, and there

[064-129] was no reason to expect any intervention from her in the Far East.

[064-130] Greater East Asia seemed assured against every danger.

[064-131] The situation of Chiang Kai-shek's Chungking government seemed hopeless.

[064-132] Even the Burma Road was cut, and supplies could only be sent by air;

[064-133] there was shortage of everything. With immense energy small industries

[064-134] were begun all over western China, often organized as co-operatives;

[064-135] roads and railways were built--but with such resources would it ever be

[064-136] possible to throw the Japanese into the sea? Everything depended on

[064-137] holding out until a new page was turned in Europe. Infinitely slow

[064-138] seemed the progress of the first gleams of hope--the steady front in

[064-139] Burma, the reconquest of the first groups of inlands; the first bomb

[064-140] attacks on Japan itself. Even in May, 1945, with the war ended in

[064-141] Europe, there seemed no sign of its ending in the Far East. Then came

[064-142] the atom bomb, bringing the collapse of Japan; the Japanese armies

[064-143] receded from China, and suddenly China was free, mistress once more in

[064-144] her own country as she had not been for decades.

[064-145] Chapter Twelve

[064-146] PRESENT-DAY CHINA

[064-147] 1 _The growth of communism_

[064-148] In order to understand today's China, we have to go back in time to

[064-149] report events which were cut short or left out of our earlier discussion

[064-150] in order to present them in the context of this chapter.

[064-151] Although socialism and communism had been known in China long ago, this

[064-152] line of development of Western philosophy had interested Chinese

[064-153] intellectuals much less than liberalistic, democratic Western ideas. It

[064-154] was widely believed that communism had no real prospects for China, as a

[064-155] dictatorship of the proletariat seemed to be relevant only in a highly

[064-156] industrialized and not in an agrarian society. Thus, in its beginning

[064-157] the "Movement of May Fourth" of 1919 had Western ideological traits but

[064-158] was not communistic. This changed with the success of communism in

[064-159] Russia and with the theoretical writings of Lenin. Here it was shown

[064-160] that communist theories could be applied to a country similar to China

[064-161] in its level of development. Already from 1919 on, some of the leaders

[064-162] of the Movement turned towards communism: the National University of

[064-163] Peking became the first centre of this movement, and Ch'en Tu-hsiu, then

[064-164] dean of the College of Letters, from 1920 on became one of its leaders.

[064-165] Hu Shih did not move to the left with this group; he remained a liberal.

[064-166] But another well-known writer, Lu Hsün (1881-1936), while following Hu

[064-167] Shih in the "Literary Revolution," identified politically with Ch'en.

[064-168] There was still another man, the Director of the University Library, Li

[064-169] Ta-chao, who turned towards communism. With him we find one of his

[064-170] employees in the Library, Mao Tse-tung. In fact, the nucleus of the

[064-171] Communist Party, which was officially created as late as 1921, was a

[064-172] student organization including some professors in Peking. On the other

[064-173] hand, a student group in Paris had also learned about communism and had

[064-174] organized; the leaders of this group were Chou En-lai and Li Li-san. A

[064-175] little later, a third group organized in Germany; Chu Tê belonged to

[064-176] this group. The leadership of Communist China since 1949 has been in the

[064-177] hands of men of these three former student groups.

[064-178] After 1920, Sun Yat-sen, too, became interested in the developments in

[064-179] Soviet Russia. Yet, he never actually became a communist; his belief

[064-180] that the soil should belong to the tiller cannot really be combined with

[064-181] communism, which advocates the abolition of individual land-holdings.

[064-182] Yet, Soviet Russia found it useful to help Sun Yat-sen and advised the

[064-183] Chinese Communist Party to collaborate with the KMT (Kuomintang). This

[064-184] collaboration, not always easy, continued until the fall of Shanghai in

[064-185] 1927.
