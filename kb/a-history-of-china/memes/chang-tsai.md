## Overview

[[chang-tsai|Chang Tsai]] (1020–1077) was a pivotal Neo-Confucian thinker of the Northern [[sung-dynasty|Sung dynasty]]. Working alongside [[chou-tun-i|Chou Tun-i]] (1017–1073), he helped construct a comprehensive cosmological system grounded in the concept of *ch'i* (vital energy or material force) — a theory designed to counter the metaphysical dominance of Buddhism in Chinese intellectual life.

> Men like Chou Tun-i (1017-1073) and Chang Tsai (1020-1077) developed a cosmological theory which could measure up with Buddhistic cosmology. [044-001] [044-002]

## The Philosophy of Ch'i

Chang Tsai's major work, the *Cheng-meng* (Correcting the Unenlightened), presents *ch'i* as the fundamental substance of the universe. In his system, the Supreme Ultimate (*t'ai-chi*) is not a separate metaphysical entity but rather the primordial, undifferentiated state of *ch'i* itself. Through a process of condensation and dispersion, *ch'i* produces the visible world of forms and, upon dissolution, returns to its original, invisible state.

This monistic philosophy provided a Confucian alternative to the Buddhist doctrine of emptiness (*śūnyatā*) and the Taoist concept of non-being (*wu*). By grounding ethics and cosmology alike in the dynamics of *ch'i*, Chang Tsai offered a this-worldly metaphysics that affirmed the reality and value of human experience.

His ideas were part of a broader Neo-Confucian engagement with the problem of evil and human nature. While [[confucius|Confucius]] had assumed the perfectibility of all persons, later thinkers recognized that a more sophisticated account of human difference was needed — one that Chang Tsai's *ch'i*-based theory helped to supply.

> But perhaps more important was the attempt of the Neo-Confucianists to explain the problem of evil. [044-003] [044-004]

## Influence

Chang Tsai's thought deeply influenced the later development of [[confucianism|Neo-Confucianism]], particularly through the synthesizing work of [[chu-hsi|Chu Hsi]] (1130–1200). Together with [[chou-tun-i|Chou Tun-i]] and the [[cheng-hao|Ch'eng]] [[cheng-i|brothers]], he is counted among the "Five Masters" of the Northern Sung whose ideas formed the foundation of the orthodox Neo-Confucian tradition.
