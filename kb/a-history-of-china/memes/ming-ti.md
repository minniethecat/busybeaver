## Overview

Emperor Ming Ti (漢明帝, r. 58–75 CE), also known as Han Ming Ti, was the second ruler of the Later Han dynasty. His reign saw the consolidation of Han power after the restoration and is traditionally associated with the introduction of Buddhism to China. His death in 75 CE triggered a succession crisis that allowed the Tou family clique to seize power.

## Reign

Ming Ti succeeded his father, [[kuang-wu-ti|Emperor Kuang-wu]] Ti, the founder of the restored Han. His reign was marked by competent administration and continued consolidation of the empire after the [[wang-mang|Wang Mang]] interregnum. The economy recovered, and Confucian scholarship flourished under imperial patronage.

> Emperor Ming Ti (A.D. 58–75) had died, and under his successor the great families began to reassert their power. [020-020]

## Introduction of Buddhism

According to traditional Chinese Buddhist historiography, Ming Ti had a dream of a golden figure flying through the air, which his advisors interpreted as the Buddha of the West. He subsequently sent envoys to India, who returned with Buddhist scriptures, monks, and images — traditionally dated to around 67 CE. This event marks the conventional starting point of [[buddhism|Buddhism in China]], though archaeological evidence suggests Buddhist communities may have existed earlier along the Silk Road.

The White Horse Temple (Baima Si) near Luoyang was supposedly built to house the returning monks and scriptures, and remains one of China's oldest Buddhist temples.

## Aftermath and Legacy

Ming Ti's death left the throne to a young successor, creating a power vacuum that the consort families — particularly the Tou clan — quickly filled. This initiated the recurring pattern of regency politics that would characterise Later Han history.

> Emperor Ming Ti, who reigned from 927 onward, was a ruler of the Later Chou dynasty, distinct from the Han emperor of the same name. [041-060]

> **Note**: The temple name Ming Ti (明帝, "Brilliant Emperor") was used by multiple rulers across Chinese history. This page primarily concerns Han Ming Ti (58–75 CE). The Later Chou Ming Ti (r. 927–933) was a different ruler of the Five Dynasties period.

Ming Ti is remembered as a conscientious ruler whose reign represented a high point of Later Han stability before the factional struggles that would eventually weaken and destroy the dynasty.
