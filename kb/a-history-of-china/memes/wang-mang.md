## Overview

Wang Mang was a nephew of the Empress Dowager Wang of the [[han-dynasty|Former Han dynasty]] who rose through the ranks of the Wang consort family to become the most powerful figure at court. In 9 CE he deposed the infant Han emperor and proclaimed his own Hsin (New) dynasty. Eberhard describes Wang Mang's usurpation as "unusual" in Chinese history, noting that while many dynastic founders came from the nobility, Wang Mang was the first to attempt a thoroughgoing program of ideological reform from above [018-157].

Wang Mang's reign was marked by a sweeping series of reforms — including the nationalization of land, the abolition of slavery, state control of commerce, and currency changes — that have led some modern historians to describe him as an early socialist or reformer ahead of his time. However, his policies created administrative chaos, alienated the powerful landowning [[gentry]] class, and combined with natural disasters and foreign wars to bring about his rapid downfall. His dynasty lasted only from 9 to 23 CE.

## Rise to Power

> "Among the rivals of the eunuchs were the families of the empresses. At the beginning of the Christian era the Wang family succeeded in gaining actual power… the nephew Wang Mang steadily advanced, securing during the whole of his rule the support of the secret societies." [018-136]

Wang Mang came from the Wang family, one of the great consort clans that dominated Han court politics. His aunt was the Empress Dowager Wang, giving him access to the highest levels of power. As the Han emperors grew weaker and the court became divided between [[eunuch]] factions and consort families, Wang Mang positioned himself as a capable and virtuous administrator [018-139].

When Emperor Ai Ti died, Wang Mang placed a child on the throne and served as regent. After the young emperor died — "probably with Wang Mang's aid," Eberhard notes — Wang Mang chose a one-year-old baby as the new emperor [018-148]. With a compliant secret society network supporting him, he finally took the throne himself in 9 CE [018-155].

## Reforms and Policies

> "Wang Mang's great series of certain laws might appear today as an extremely modern socialist program. He forbade the sale of land, so that the land 'belonged to the state and the farmers to the state'." [019-001]

Wang Mang's reform program was unprecedented in its scope:

| Reform Area | Policy | Outcome |
|-------------|--------|---------|
| Land | Nationalization, prohibition of private land sales | Widespread resistance from landowners; rescinded after 3 years |
| Slavery | Abolition of private slavery | Difficult to enforce; contributed to gentry opposition |
| Economy | State monopolies, currency reforms, price controls | Created confusion and black markets |
| Administration | Restoration of idealized Chou dynasty institutions | Bureaucratic chaos; officials couldn't implement changes |

The reforms reflected a utopian vision drawn from [[confucianism|Confucian]] classical texts. Wang Mang sought to recreate an idealized version of the early [[chou-dynasty|Chou dynasty]] social order [019-001]. However, his policies ignored practical realities and the interests of the powerful landowning families who formed the backbone of the state.

## Foreign Policy and Military Crisis

Wang Mang's foreign policy proved equally disastrous. He provoked the powerful [[hsiung-nu]] confederation on the northern frontier through a series of diplomatic insults and aggressive military posturing [019-048].

> "Wang Mang concentrated a vast army on the northern frontier. But before Wang Mang's campaign against the Hsiung-nu could get properly under way, conditions in the country grew steadily worse." [019-060]

The military buildup drained the treasury at a time when Wang Mang desperately needed money for his domestic programs [019-042]. Rather than gaining prestige through victory, the costly and unsuccessful frontier policy further undermined his already fragile legitimacy.

## Downfall

Internal rebellions proved to be Wang Mang's undoing. The most famous of these was the [[red-eyebrows|Red Eyebrows]] uprising, a peasant movement that arose in response to famine, flood, and the breakdown of Wang Mang's administrative system [019-079].

> "The armies sent against them by Wang Mang either went over to the Red Eyebrows or were defeated. The rebels broke into the capital, and Wang Mang was killed." [019-079]

Eberhard notes that many of the "bandits" who fought against Wang Mang were later romanticized as popular heroes fighting against a usurper [019-090]. In 23 CE, rebel forces entered the capital, and Wang Mang was killed. The Han dynasty was restored shortly afterward by a member of the Liu clan.

## Historical Assessment

Wang Mang's reputation has been contested throughout Chinese history. Traditional Confucian historiography condemned him as a usurper, deliberately omitting his dynasty from the legitimate succession. Later historians, both Chinese and Western, have offered more nuanced assessments, noting the idealism behind his reforms even while acknowledging their practical failure [018-171].

His reign represents a pivotal moment in Chinese history — the interruption of the great [[han-dynasty|Han dynasty]] and a cautionary tale about the dangers of radical reform imposed from above without sufficient social and administrative foundation [020-089].
