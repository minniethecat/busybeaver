# Section 69

[069-001] p. 54: The work done by Kat[=o] Shigeru and Niida Noboru on property and

[069-002] family has been used here. For the later period, work done by Makino

[069-003] Tatsumi has also been incorporated.--Literature on the plough and on

[069-004] iron for implements has been mentioned above. Concerning the fallow

[069-005] system, I have incorporated the ideas of Kat[=o] Shigeru, [=O]shima

[069-006] Toshikaza, Hsü Ti-shan and Wan Kuo-ting. Hsü Ti-shan believes that a

[069-007] kind of 3-field system had developed by this time. Traces of such a

[069-008] system have been observed in modern China (H.D. Scholz). For these

[069-009] questions, the translation by N. Lee Swann, _Food and Money in Ancient

[069-010] China_, 1959 is very important.

[069-011] p. 55: For all questions of money and credit from this period down to

[069-012] modern times, the best brief introduction is by Lien-sheng Yang, _Money

[069-013] and Credit in China_, Cambridge 1952. The _Introduction to the Economic

[069-014] History of China_, London 1954, by E. Stuart Kirby is certainly still

[069-015] the best brief introduction into all problems of Chinese Economic

[069-016] history and contains a bibliography in Western and Chinese-Japanese

[069-017] languages. Articles by Chinese authors on economic problems have been

[069-018] translated in E-tu Zen Sun and J. de Francis, _Chinese Social History_,

[069-019] Washington 1956.--Data on the size of early cities have been collected

[069-020] by T. Sekino and Kat[=o] Shigeru.

[069-021] p. 56: T. Sekino studied the forms of cities. C. Hentze believes that

[069-022] the city even in the Shang period normally had a square plan.--T. Sekino

[069-023] has also made the first research on city coins. Such a privilege and

[069-024] such independence of cities disappear later, but occasionally the

[069-025] privilege of minting was given to persons of high rank.--K.A. Wittfogel,

[069-026] _Oriental Despotism_, New Haven 1957 regards irrigation as a key

[069-027] economic and social factor and has built up his theory around this

[069-028] concept. I do not accept his theory here or later. Evidence seems to

[069-029] point towards the importance of transportation systems rather than of

[069-030] government-sponsored or operated irrigation systems.--Concerning steel,

[069-031] we follow Yang K'uan; a special study by J. Needham is under

[069-032] preparation. Centre of steel production at this time was Wan (later

[069-033] Nanyang in Honan).--For early Chinese law, the study by A.F.P. Hulsewé,

[069-034] _Remnants of Han Law_, Leiden 1955 is the best work in English. He does

[069-035] not, however, regard Li K'ui as the main creator of Chinese law, though

[069-036] Kuo Mo-jo and others do. It is obvious, however, that Han law was not a

[069-037] creation of the Han Chinese alone and that some type of code must have

[069-038] existed before Han, even if such a code was not written by the man Li

[069-039] K'ui. A special study on Li was made by O. Franke.

[069-040] p. 57: In the description of border conditions, research by O. Lattimore

[069-041] has been taken into consideration.

[069-042] p. 59: For Shang Yang and this whole period, the classical work in

[069-043] English is still J.J.L. Duyvendak, _The Book of Lord Shang_, London

[069-044] 1928; the translation by Ma Perleberg of _The Works of Kung-sun

[069-045] Lung-tzu_, Hongkong 1952 as well as the translation of the _Economic

[069-046] Dialogues in Ancient China: The Kuan-tzu_, edited by L. Maverick, New

[069-047] Haven 1954 have not found general approval, but may serve as

[069-048] introductions to the way philosophers of our period worked. Han Fei

[069-049] Tz[)u]; has been translated by W.K. Liao, _The Complete Works of Han Fei

[069-050] Tz[)u]_, London 1939 (only part 1).

[069-051] p. 60: Needham does not have such a positive attitude towards Tsou Yen,

[069-052] and regards Western influences upon Tsou Yen as not too likely. The

[069-053] discussion on pp. 60-1 follows mainly my own researches.

[069-054] p. 61: The interpretation of secret societies is influenced by general

[069-055] sociological theory and detailed reports on later secret societies. S.

[069-056] Murayama and most modern Chinese scholars stress almost solely the

[069-057] social element in the so-called "peasant rebellions".

[069-058] _Chapter Five_

[069-059] p. 63: The analysis of the emergence of Ch'in bureaucracy has profited

[069-060] from general sociological theory, especially M. Weber (see the new

[069-061] analysis by R. Bendix, _Max Weber, an Intellectual Portrait_, Garden

[069-062] City 1960, p. 117-157). Early administration systems of this type in

[069-063] China have been studied in several articles in the journal _Yü-kung_

[069-064] (vol. 6 and 7).

[069-065] p. 65: In the discussion of language, I use arguments which have been

[069-066] brought forth by P. Serruys against the previously generally accepted

[069-067] theories of B. Karlgren.--For weights and measures I have referred to T.

[069-068] Sekino, Liu Fu and Wu Ch'eng-lo.

[069-069] p. 66: For this period, D. Bodde's _China's First Unifier_, Leiden 1938

[069-070] and his _Statesman, Patriot, and General in Ancient China_, New Haven

[069-071] 1940 remain valuable studies.

[069-072] _Chapter Six_

[069-073] p. 71: The basic historical text for this whole period, the _Dynastic

[069-074] History of the Han Dynasty_, is now in part available in English

[069-075] translation (H.H. Dubs, _The History of the Former Han Dynasty_,

[069-076] Baltimore 1938, 3 volumes).

[069-077] p. 72: The description of the gentry is based upon my own research.

[069-078] Other scholars define the word "gentry", if applied to China,

[069-079] differently (some of the relevant studies are discussed in my note in

[069-080] the _Bull. School of Orient. & African Studies_, 1955, p. 373 f.).

[069-081] p. 73: The theory of the cycle of mobility has been brought forth by Fr.

[069-082] L.K. Hsu and others. I have based my criticism upon a forthcoming study

[069-083] of _Social Mobility in Traditional Chinese Society_. The basic point is

[069-084] not the momentary economic or political power of such a family, but the

[069-085] social status of the family (_Li-shih yen-chiu_, Peking 1955, No. 4, p.

[069-086] 122). The social status was, increasingly, defined and fixed by law

[069-087] (Ch'ü T'ung-tsu).--The difference in the size of gentry and other

[069-088] families has been pointed out by a number of scholars such as Fr. L.K.

[069-089] Hsu, H.T. Fei, O. Lang. My own research seems to indicate that gentry

[069-090] families, on the average, married earlier than other families.

[069-091] p. 74: The Han system of examinations or rather of selection has been

[069-092] studied by Yang Lien-sheng; and analysis of the social origin of

[069-093] candidates has been made in the _Bull. Chinese Studies_, vol. 2, 1941,

[069-094] and 3, 1942.--The meaning of the term "Hundred Families" has been

[069-095] discussed by W. Eichhorn, Kuo Mo-jo, Ch'en Meng-chia and especially by

[069-096] Hsü T'ung-hsin. It was later also a fiscal term.

[069-097] p. 75: The analysis of Hsiung-nu society is based mainly upon my own

[069-098] research. There is no satisfactory history of these northern federations

[069-099] available in English. The compilation of W.M. MacGovern, _The Early

[069-100] Empires of Central Asia_, Chapel Hill 1939, is now quite antiquated.--An

[069-101] attempt to construct a model of Central Asian nomadic social structure

[069-102] has been made by E.E. Bacon, _Obok, a Study of Social Structure in

[069-103] Eurasia_, New York 1958, but the model constructed by B. Vladimirtsov

[069-104] and modified by O. Lattimore remains valuable.--For origin and

[069-105] early-development of Hsiung-nu society see O. Maenchen, K. Jettmar, B.

[069-106] Bernstam, Uchida Gimpu and many others.

[069-107] p. 79: Material on the "classes" (_sz[)u] min_) will be found in a

[069-108] forthcoming book. Studies by Ch'ü T'ung-tsu and Tamai Korehiro are

[069-109] important here. An up-to-date history of Chinese education is still a

[069-110] desideratum.

[069-111] p. 80: For Tung Chung-shu, I rely mainly upon O. Franke.--Some scholars

[069-112] do not accept this "double standard", although we have clear texts which

[069-113] show that cases were evaluated on the basis of Confucian texts and not

[069-114] on the basis of laws. In fact, local judges probably only in exceptional

[069-115] cases knew the text of the law or had the code. They judged on the basis

[069-116] of "customary law".

[069-117] p. 81: Based mainly upon my own research. K.A. Wittfogel, _Oriental

[069-118] Despotism_, New Haven 1957, has a different interpretation.

[069-119] p. 82: Cases in which the Han emperors disregarded the law code were

[069-120] studied by Y. Hisamura.--I have used here studies published in the

[069-121] _Bull, of Chinese Studies_, vol. 2 and 3 and in _Tôyô gakuho_,

[069-122] vol. 8 and 9, in addition to my own research.

[069-123] p. 85: On local administration see Kat[=o] Shigeru and Yen Keng-wang's

[069-124] studies.

[069-125] p. 86: The problem of the Chinese gold, which will be touched upon later

[069-126] again, has gained theoretical interest, because it could be used as a

[069-127] test of M. Lombard's theories concerning the importance of gold in the

[069-128] West (_Annales, Economies, Sociétés, Civilisations_, vol. 12, Paris

[069-129] 1957, No. 1, p. 7-28). It was used in China from c. 600 B.C. on in form

[069-130] of coins or bars, but disappeared almost completely from A.D. 200 on,

[069-131] i.e. the period of economic decline (see L.S. Yang, Kat[=o]

[069-132] Shigeru).--The payment to border tribes occurs many times again in

[069-133] Chinese history down to recent times; it has its parallel in British

[069-134] payments to tribes in the North-West Frontier Province in India which

[069-135] continued even after the Independence.

[069-136] p. 88: According to later sources, one third of the tributary gifts was

[069-137] used in the Imperial ancestor temples, one third in the Imperial

[069-138] mausolea, but one third was used as gifts to guests of the Emperor.--The

[069-139] trade aspect of the tributes was first pointed but by E. Parker, later

[069-140] by O. Lattimore, recently by J.K. Fairbank.--The importance of Chang

[069-141] Ch'ien for East-West contacts was systematically studied by B. Laufer;

[069-142] his _Sino-Iranica_, Chicago 1919 is still a classic.

[069-143] p. 89: The most important trait which points to foreign trade, is the

[069-144] occurrence of glass in Chinese tombs in Indo-China and of glass in China

[069-145] proper from the fifth century B.C. on; it is assumed that this glass was

[069-146] imported from the Near East, possibly from Egypt (O. Janse, N. Egami,

[069-147] Seligman).

[069-148] p. 91: Large parts of the "Discussions" have been translated by Esson M.

[069-149] Gale, _Discourses on Salt and Iron_, Leiden 1931; the continuation of

[069-150] this translation is in _Jour. Royal As. Society, North-China Branch_

[069-151] 1934.--The history of eunuchs in China remains to be written. They were

[069-152] known since at least the seventh century B.C. The hypothesis has been

[069-153] made that this custom had its origin in Asia Minor and spread from there

[069-154] (R.F. Spencer in _Ciba Symposia_, vol. 8, No. 7, 1946 with references).

[069-155] p. 92: The main source on Wang Mang is translated by C.B. Sargent, _Wang

[069-156] Mang, a translation_, Shanghai 1950 and H.H. Dubs, _History of the

[069-157] Former Han Dynasty_, vol, 3, Baltimore 1955.

[069-158] p. 93: This evaluation of the "Old character school" is not generally

[069-159] accepted. A quite different view is represented by Tjan Tjoe Som and

[069-160] R.P. Kramers and others who regard the differences between the schools

[069-161] as of a philological and not a political kind. I follow here most

[069-162] strongly the Chinese school as represented by Ku Chieh-kang and his

[069-163] friends, and my own studies.

[069-164] p. 93: Falsification of texts refers to changes in the Tso-chuan. My

[069-165] interpretation relies again upon Ku Chieh-kang, and Japanese

[069-166] astronomical studies (Ijima Tadao), but others, too, admit

[069-167] falsifications (H.H. Dubs); B. Karlgren and others regard the book as in

[069-168] its main body genuine. The other text mentioned here is the _Chou-li_

[069-169] which is certainly not written by Wang Mang (_Jung-chai Hsü-pi_ 16), but

[069-170] heavily mis-used by him (in general see S. Uno).

[069-171] p. 94: I am influenced here by some of H.H. Dubs's studies. For this and

[069-172] the following period, the work by H. Bielenstein, _The Restoration of

[069-173] the Han Dynasty_, Stockholm 1953 and 1959 is the best monograph.--The

[069-174] "equalization offices" and their influence upon modern United States has

[069-175] been studied by B. Bodde in the _Far Eastern Quarterly_, vol. 5, 1946.

[069-176] p. 95: H. Bielenstein regards a great flood as one of the main reasons

[069-177] for the breakdown of Wang Mang's rule.

[069-178] p. 98: For the understanding of Chinese military colonies in Central

[069-179] Asia as well as for the understanding of military organization, civil

[069-180] administration and business, the studies of Lao Kan on texts excavated

[069-181] in Central Asia and Kansu are of greatest importance.

[069-182] p. 101: Mazdaistic elements in this rebellion have been mentioned mainly

[069-183] by H.H. Dubs. Zoroastrism (Zoroaster born 569 B.C.) and Mazdaism were

[069-184] eminently "political" religions from their very beginning on. Most

[069-185] scholars admit the presence of Mazdaism in China only from 519 on

[069-186] (Ishida Mikinosuke, O. Franke). Dubs's theory can be strengthened by

[069-187] astronomical material.--The basic religious text of this group, the

[069-188] "Book of the Great Peace" has been studied by W. Eichhorn Maspero

[069-189] and Ho Ch'ang-ch'ün.
