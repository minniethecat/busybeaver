## Overview

[[liang-chi-chao|Liang Ch'i-ch'ao]] (1873–1929) was one of the most influential Chinese intellectuals of the transition from imperial to Republican China. A disciple of [[kang-yu-wei|K'ang Yu-wei]] and a leading figure in the [[hsiung-nu|Hu]]ndred Days' Reform of 1898, he spent fourteen years in exile in [[japan|Japan]] where he edited influential journals that introduced a generation of Chinese readers to Western political philosophy, constitutionalism, and the concept of national citizenship.

> The reform program was soon elaborated by Liang Ch'i-ch'ao, a collaborator of Sun Yat-sen in the early revolutionary period, who became the most widely read political essayist of his generation. [056-142]

## The Reform Movement

Liang emerged as a leading voice of the reform faction at the Qing court in the 1890s. Together with his teacher [[kang-yu-wei|K'ang Yu-wei]], he convinced the [[peking|Kuang-hsü Emperor]] to launch the [[hsiung-nu|Hu]]ndred Days' Reform in the summer of 1898 — an ambitious program of educational, administrative, and military modernization. When the [[peking|Empress Dowager Tz'u-hsi]] staged a coup and reversed the reforms, Liang narrowly escaped execution and fled to [[japan|Japan]].

## Exile and Journalism

Liang's most enduring contribution to Chinese political thought came during his years in exile. Through journals such as *Qingyi Bao* (The China Discussion) and *Xinmin Congbao* (New Citizen Journal), he became the preeminent public intellectual of his era:

> For Liang, see J.R. Levenson, _Liang Ch'i-ch'ao and the Mind of Modern China_, which remains the definitive intellectual biography. [072-123]

He coined the concept of the "new citizen" (xinmin) — a politically conscious, patriotic individual who would serve as the foundation for a modern Chinese nation-state. His lucid prose style, combining classical elegance with vernacular accessibility, influenced an entire generation of Chinese readers, including a young [[mao-tse-tung|Mao Tse-tung]].

## Later Career and Legacy

After the [[peking|1911 Revolution]], Liang returned to China and served in several Republican governments, including as Minister of Justice and Minister of Finance. He broke with [[sun-yat-sen|Sun Yat-sen]] over the question of constitutional monarchy versus republicanism, and later opposed [[yuan-shih-kai|Yuan Shih-kai's]] monarchical ambitions.

> Liang Ch'i-ch'ao, journalist and scholar, bridged the classical scholarly tradition and the new public sphere of newspapers, magazines, and political pamphlets that emerged in early twentieth-century China. [073-597]

Liang's later years were devoted to scholarship, producing important works on Chinese intellectual history and historiography. His intellectual trajectory — from Confucian reformer to Western-influenced liberal to culturally conservative scholar — mirrored the broader ideological struggles of China's traumatic transition to modernity.
