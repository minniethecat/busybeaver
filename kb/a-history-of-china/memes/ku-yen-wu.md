## Overview

**Ku Yen-wu** (1613–1682) was a seminal scholar of the late [[Ming Dynasty]] and early [[Ch'ing Dynasty]] transition period. A polymath whose work spanned geography, philology, history, and statecraft, he is best known for founding the tradition of "evidential research" (k'ao-cheng hsüeh) and for his massive geographical compendium, the _T'ien-hsia chün-kuo li-ping shu_ (Book of the Strengths and Weaknesses of All the Provinces and States Under Heaven).

## The Geographical Encyclopaedia

Ku Yen-wu's great geographical work marked the beginning of a new era in Chinese scholarship:

> "The compilations began with the great geographical encyclopaedia of Ku Yen-wu (1613-1682), and attained their climax in the gigantic eighteenth-century encyclopaedia _T'u-shu chi-ch'eng_, scientifically impeccable in the accuracy of its references to sources." [056-087–056-089]

This project was unprecedented in its scope and methodology. Rather than relying on received tradition, Ku Yen-wu traveled extensively throughout China, consulting local gazetteers, inscriptions, and oral histories to compile empirical data on the geography, economy, and administration of each region. His approach emphasized direct observation and primary-source verification — principles that would later characterize the k'ao-cheng movement.

## The "Archaeological School"

Ku Yen-wu is recognized as a foundational figure in the emergence of what became known as the "Archaeological School" (k'ao-chü hsüeh) of Chinese scholarship:

> "Here were already the beginnings of the 'Archaeological School', built up in the course of the eighteenth century." [056-090–056-091]

This school rejected the abstract moralizing of Sung-Ming [[confucianism|Neo-Confucianism]] in favor of rigorous philological and historical research. Its practitioners sought to recover the original meanings of classical texts through careful collation, phonology, and epigraphy. This turn toward empirical methodology in the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries paralleled, in some respects, the rise of modern scientific scholarship in Europe during the same period.

## Scholarship and Methodology

Ku Yen-wu is identified in the scholarly index as:

> "Ku Yen-wu, geographer" [073-539]

Though "geographer" captures one dimension of his work, his intellectual range was far broader. His major works include:

- **_Jih-chih lu_ (Record of Daily Knowledge)**: A collection of scholarly notes on classics, history, government, and economics, compiled over three decades
- **_Yin-hsüeh wu-shu_ (Five Books on Phonology)**: Foundational works in the historical phonology of ancient Chinese
- **_T'ien-hsia chün-kuo li-ping shu_**: His magnum opus in historical geography

His research methods — emphasizing the verification of textual claims against material evidence, the systematic comparison of variant sources, and the rejection of unsupported tradition — profoundly influenced later scholars of the Ch'ing dynasty.

## Legacy

Western scholarship has recognized Ku Yen-wu's importance:

> "For Ku Yen-wu, I used the work by H. Wilhelm." [072-117]

Ku Yen-wu, along with his near-contemporary [[Huang Tsung-hsi]], is regarded as one of the founding figures of early modern Chinese scholarship. The evidential research tradition he helped establish dominated Chinese intellectual life through the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries and provided the scholarly foundation upon which twentieth-century Chinese historians would build.

His insistence that scholarship must be grounded in demonstrable evidence rather than philosophical speculation represented a fundamental reorientation of Chinese intellectual priorities — one that would prove essential to China's engagement with modern scientific methods.
