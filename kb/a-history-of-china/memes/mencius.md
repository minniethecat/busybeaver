## Overview

[[Mencius]] (Meng Tzu, c. 372–289 BC) was the most influential [[confucianism|Confucian]] philosopher after [[confucius|Confucius]] himself. Living in the Warring States period in present-day Shantung, he elaborated the ideas of [[confucius|Confucius]] and formulated the enduring political theory that the legitimacy of a ruler is determined by the voice of the "people" — a doctrine that persisted throughout the history of China [012-018].

## Biography

Mencius belonged to the class of "scholars" who carried forward the Confucian tradition during the turbulent Warring States period. Like his near-contemporary Hsün Tzu (c. 298–238 BC), he lived in what is now Shantung province — the heartland of classical Chinese philosophy — and devoted himself to elaborating the ideas of [[confucius|Confucius]]. Neither Mencius nor Hsün Tzu achieved personal success in their own lifetimes [012-011].

> Mencius (Meng Tzu) recognized that the removal of the ruling house of the Chou no longer presented any problem. The difficult question for him was when a change of ruler would be justified. And how could it be ascertained whom Heaven had destined as successor if the existing dynasty was brought down? Mencius replied that the voice of the "people", that is to say of the upper class and its following, would declare the right man, and that this man would then be Heaven's nominee. This theory persisted throughout the history of China. [012-011–012-019]

## Philosophy

Mencius's central contribution to Confucian political thought was his resolution of the succession problem. By his time, the ruling house of the Chou dynasty had lost all effective authority, and the question was no longer whether a change of ruler could occur, but how it could be justified [012-012]. Mencius answered that Heaven's choice was revealed through the voice of the "people" — by which he meant the upper class and its following — who would declare the rightful successor [012-016].

This theory of popular acclamation as the expression of Heaven's mandate became a permanent feature of Chinese political thought, invoked by dynastic founders and rebels alike for over two thousand years [012-018].

Mencius stood in the mainstream of the Confucian school, which held fast to the old feudal order of society and was prepared to agree only to superficial changes [012-028]. This conservatism placed him in direct opposition to the school of [[mo-ti|Mo Ti]], which proposed to alter the fundamental principles of society — extending family love to the entire upper class, rejecting lavish ritual expenditure, and organising disciplined private armies of supporters [012-029].

## Legacy

Alongside [[confucius|Confucius]], Mencius became one of the twin pillars of orthodox [[confucianism|Confucianism]]. His name was Latinised by early Jesuit missionaries as "Mencius," and his collected works — the _Mencius_ — became one of the Four Books that formed the core of the Chinese civil service examination curriculum from the Sung dynasty onward. His theory of the people's voice as Heaven's mandate provided the ideological framework for legitimising dynastic change throughout imperial Chinese history.
