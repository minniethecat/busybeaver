## Overview

[[huan-hsuan|Huan Hsüan]] (369–404) was the son of the powerful Eastern Chin general [[huan-wen|Huan Wen]], who inherited his father's military network and regional base, then used it to seize the imperial capital at Chien-k'ang, depose the reigning emperor, and briefly establish his own short-lived dynasty. His rapid rise and equally rapid fall — defeated within months by the general Liu Yü, the future founder of the [[liu-sung-dynasty]] — marked the final collapse of effective central authority under the Eastern Chin and the beginning of the transition to the Southern Dynasties period [032-085][032-121].

## Inheritance and Ascent

Huan Hsüan was born into the most powerful military family of the late fourth century. His father, [[huan-wen|Huan Wen]], had dominated the Eastern Chin court in the 360s and 370s, leading campaigns into the north and reducing the emperor to a figurehead. On his death, [[huan-wen|Huan Wen]]'s command passed to his brother [[hsiung-nu|Hu]]an Ch'ung, and then, after a period of eclipse, to Huan Hsüan himself. By the late 390s, Huan Hsüan commanded a formidable military apparatus in the middle [[yangtze-river|Yangtze]] region, centred on his father's old base at Chiang-ling [032-085].

> A son of Huan Wen, Huan Hsüan, came especially to the fore. [032-085]

Huan Hsüan's ascent was aided by the self-destruction of his rivals. The Yin family, who had initially allied with him, broke away and were crushed. The general Ssu-ma Yüan-hsien, who attempted to suppress him, was outmanoeuvred. [[hsiung-nu|Hu]]an built a coalition of disaffected aristocrats and provincial commanders, skillfully playing on resentment against the central court's demands for taxes and military service [032-091][032-094].

> At first this new clique had a considerable following. Then the Yin went over to Huan Hsüan. [032-091]

## The Usurpation

In 402, Huan Hsüan marched on the capital at Chien-k'ang. The general [[liu-lao-chih|Liu Lao-chih]], sent to defend the city, instead surrendered to him — a defection that handed [[hsiung-nu|Hu]]an the capital without a battle. Once in control, Huan Hsüan deposed the emperor, purged his leading opponents in the Hsieh and Wang clans, and in the winter of 403 proclaimed himself emperor of a new "Ch'u" dynasty [032-099][032-103].

> Thus Huan Hsüan conquered the capital, deposed the emperor, and made himself emperor. [032-103]

The usurpation proved fragile. Huan Hsüan lacked the broad gentry support that had sustained earlier strongmen like his father. His rule rested almost entirely on the personal loyalty of a narrow clique of military commanders from the middle [[yangtze-river|Yangtze]], and he alienated the great émigré families of the capital — the very families whose endorsement was essential to any stable government in the south [032-121].

## Defeat and Death

Within months of his enthronement, the general Liu Yü — a man of humble origin who had risen through the ranks of the northern garrison — raised the standard of rebellion. Liu Yü gathered a coalition of disaffected officers and marched on the capital. Huan Hsüan was forced to flee Chien-k'ang and attempted to retreat westward to his [[yangtze-river|Yangtze]] power base, but Liu Yü's forces pursued him relentlessly. He was killed in 404, and his "Ch'u" dynasty expired with him [032-121][032-122].

> Huan Hsüan had to flee, and in his flight he was killed. [032-122]

Liu Yü would go on to become the effective ruler of the Eastern Chin and then, in 420, the founder of the Liu-Sung dynasty — the first of the Southern Dynasties. Huan Hsüan's brief usurpation thus served as the catalyst for the final dissolution of the Chin political order and the opening of a new chapter in Chinese history [032-122].
