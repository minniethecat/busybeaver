# Section 52

[052-001] This transfer of the capital to the north, as the result of the victory

[052-002] of the military party and Buddhists allied to them, produced a new

[052-003] element of instability: the north was of military importance, but the

[052-004] Yangtze region remained the economic centre of the country. The

[052-005] interests of the gentry of the Yangtze region were injured by the

[052-006] transfer. The first Ming emperor had taken care to make his court

[052-007] resemble the court of the Mongol rulers, but on the whole had exercised

[052-008] relative economy. Yung-lo (1403-1424), however, lived in the actual

[052-009] palaces of the Mongol rulers, and all the luxury of the Mongol epoch was

[052-010] revived. This made the reign of Yung-lo the most magnificent period of

[052-011] the Ming epoch, but beneath the surface decay had begun. Typical of the

[052-012] unmitigated absolutism which developed now, was the word of one of the

[052-013] emperor's political and military advisors, significantly a Buddhist

[052-014] monk: "I know the way of heaven. Why discuss the hearts of the people?"

[052-015] 9 _Navy. Southward expansion_

[052-016] After the collapse of Mongol rule in Indo-China, partly through the

[052-017] simple withdrawal of the Mongols, and partly through attacks from

[052-018] various Chinese generals, there were independence movements in

[052-019] south-west China and Indo-China. In 1393 wars broke out in Annam.

[052-020] Yung-lo considered that the time had come to annex these regions to

[052-021] China and so to open a new field for Chinese trade, which was suffering

[052-022] continual disturbance from the Japanese. He sent armies to Yünnan and

[052-023] Indo-China; at the same time he had a fleet built by one of his eunuchs,

[052-024] Cheng Ho. The fleet was successfully protected from attack by the

[052-025] Japanese. Cheng Ho, who had promoted the plan and also carried it out,

[052-026] began in 1405 his famous mission to Indo-China, which had been envisaged

[052-027] as giving at least moral support to the land operations, but was also

[052-028] intended to renew trade connections with Indo-China, where they had been

[052-029] interrupted by the collapse of Mongol rule. Cheng Ho sailed past

[052-030] Indo-China and ultimately reached the coast of Arabia. His account of

[052-031] his voyage is an important source of information about conditions in

[052-032] southern Asia early in the fifteenth century. Cheng Ho and his fleet

[052-033] made some further cruises, but they were discontinued. There may have

[052-034] been several reasons, (1) As state enterprises, the expeditions were

[052-035] very costly. Foreign goods could be obtained more cheaply and with less

[052-036] trouble if foreign merchants came themselves to China or Chinese

[052-037] merchants travelled at their own risk. (2) The moral success of the

[052-038] naval enterprises was assured. China was recognized as a power

[052-039] throughout southern Asia, and Annam had been reconquered. (3) After the

[052-040] collapse of the Mongol emperor Timur, who died in 1406, there no longer

[052-041] existed any great power in Central Asia, so that trade missions from the

[052-042] kingdom of the Shahruk in North Persia were able to make their way to

[052-043] China, including the famous mission of 1409-1411. (4) Finally, the fleet

[052-044] would have had to be permanently guarded against the Japanese, as it had

[052-045] been stationed not in South China but in the Yangtze region. As early as

[052-046] 1411 the canals had been repaired, and from 1415 onward all the traffic

[052-047] of the country went by the canals, so evading the Japanese peril. This

[052-048] ended the short chapter of Chinese naval history.

[052-049] These travels of Cheng Ho seem to have had one more cultural result: a

[052-050] large number of fairy-tales from the Middle East were brought to China,

[052-051] or at all events reached China at that time. The Chinese, being a

[052-052] realistically-minded people, have produced few fairy-tales of their own.

[052-053] The bulk of their finest fairy-tales were brought by Buddhist monks, in

[052-054] the course of the first millennium A.D., from India by way of Central

[052-055] Asia. The Buddhists made use of them to render their sermons more

[052-056] interesting and impressive. As time went on, these stories spread all

[052-057] over China, modified in harmony with the spirit of the people and

[052-058] adapted to the Chinese environment. Only the fables failed to strike

[052-059] root in China: the matter-of-fact Chinese was not interested in animals

[052-060] that talked and behaved to each other like human beings. In addition,

[052-061] however, to these early fairy-tales, there was another group of stories

[052-062] that did not spread throughout China, but were found only in the

[052-063] south-eastern coastal provinces. These came from the Middle East,

[052-064] especially from Persia. The fairy-tales of Indian origin spread not only

[052-065] to Central Asia but at the same time to Persia, where they found a very

[052-066] congenial soil. The Persians made radical changes in the stories and

[052-067] gave them the form in which they came to Europe by various

[052-068] routes--through North Africa to Spain and France; through

[052-069] Constantinople, Venice, or Genoa to France; through Russian Turkestan to

[052-070] Russia, Finland, and Sweden; through Turkey and the Balkans to Hungary

[052-071] and Germany. Thus the stories found a European home. And this same

[052-072] Persian form was carried by sea in Cheng Ho's time to South China. Thus

[052-073] we have the strange experience of finding some of our own finest

[052-074] fairy-tales in almost the same form in South China.

[052-075] 10 _Struggles between cliques_

[052-076] Yung-lo's successor died early. Under the latter's son, the emperor

[052-077] Hsüan Tsung (1426-1435; reign name Hsüan-tê), fixed numbers of

[052-078] candidates were assigned for the state examinations. It had been found

[052-079] that almost the whole of the gentry in the Yangtze region sat at the

[052-080] examinations; and that at these examinations their representatives made

[052-081] sure, through their mutual relations, that only their members should

[052-082] pass, so that the candidates from the north were virtually excluded. The

[052-083] important military clique in the north protested against this, and a

[052-084] compromise was arrived at: at every examination one-third of the

[052-085] candidates must come from the north and two-thirds from the south. This

[052-086] system lasted for a long time, and led to many disputes.

[052-087] At his death Hsüan Tsung left the empire to his eight-year-old son Ying

[052-088] Tsung (1436-49 and 1459-64), who was entirely in the hands of the Yang

[052-089] clique, which was associated with his grandmother. Soon, however,

[052-090] another clique, led by the eunuch Wang Chen, gained the upper hand at

[052-091] court. The Mongols were very active at this time, and made several raids

[052-092] on the province of Shansi; Wang Chen proposed a great campaign against

[052-093] them, and in this campaign he took with him the young emperor, who had

[052-094] reached his twenty-first birthday in 1449. The emperor had grown up in

[052-095] the palace and knew nothing of the world outside; he was therefore glad

[052-096] to go with Wang Chen; but that eunuch had also lived in the palace and

[052-097] also knew nothing of the world, and in particular of war. Consequently

[052-098] he failed in the organization of reinforcements for his army, some

[052-099] 100,000 strong; after a few brief engagements the Oirat-Mongol prince

[052-100] Esen had the imperial army surrounded and the emperor a prisoner. The

[052-101] eunuch Wang Chen came to his end, and his clique, of course, no longer

[052-102] counted. The Mongols had no intention of killing the emperor; they

[052-103] proposed to hold him to ransom, at a high price. The various cliques at

[052-104] court cared little, however, about their ruler. After the fall of the

[052-105] Wang clique there were two others, of which one, that of General Yü,

[052-106] became particularly powerful, as he had been able to repel a Mongol

[052-107] attack on Peking. Yü proclaimed a new emperor--not the captive emperor's

[052-108] son, a baby, but his brother, who became the emperor Ching Tsung. The

[052-109] Yang clique insisted on the rights of the imperial baby. From all this

[052-110] the Mongols saw that the Chinese were not inclined to spend a lot of

[052-111] money on their imperial captive. Accordingly they made an enormous

[052-112] reduction in the ransom demanded, and more or less forced the Chinese to

[052-113] take back their former emperor. The Mongols hoped that this would at

[052-114] least produce political disturbances by which they might profit, once

[052-115] the old emperor was back in Peking. And this did soon happen. At first

[052-116] the ransomed emperor was pushed out of sight into a palace, and Ching

[052-117] Tsung continued to reign. But in 1456 Ching Tsung fell ill, and a

[052-118] successor to him had to be chosen. The Yü clique wanted to have the son

[052-119] of Ching Tsung; the Yang clique wanted the son of the deposed emperor

[052-120] Ying Tsung. No agreement was reached, so that in the end a third clique,

[052-121] led by the soldier Shih Hêng, who had helped to defend Peking against

[052-122] the Mongols, found its opportunity, and by a _coup d'état_ reinstated

[052-123] the deposed emperor Ying Tsung.

[052-124] This was not done out of love for the emperor, but because Shih Hêng

[052-125] hoped that under the rule of the completely incompetent Ying Tsung he

[052-126] could best carry out a plan of his own, to set up his own dynasty. It is

[052-127] not so easy, however, to carry a conspiracy to success when there are

[052-128] several rival parties, each of which is ready to betray any of the

[052-129] others. Shih Hêng's plan became known before long, and he himself was

[052-130] beheaded (1460).

[052-131] The next forty years were filled with struggles between cliques, which

[052-132] steadily grew in ferocity, particularly since a special office, a sort

[052-133] of secret police headquarters, was set up in the palace, with functions

[052-134] which it extended beyond the palace, with the result that many people

[052-135] were arrested and disappeared. This office was set up by the eunuchs and

[052-136] the clique at their back, and was the first dictatorial organ created in

[052-137] the course of a development towards despotism that made steady progress

[052-138] in these years.

[052-139] In 1505 Wu Tsung came to the throne, an inexperienced youth of fifteen

[052-140] who was entirely controlled by the eunuchs who had brought him up. The

[052-141] leader of the eunuchs was Liu Chin, who had the support of a group of

[052-142] people of the gentry and the middle class. Liu Chin succeeded within a

[052-143] year in getting rid of the eunuchs at court who belonged to other

[052-144] cliques and were working against him. After that he proceeded to

[052-145] establish his power. He secured in entirely official form the emperor's

[052-146] permission for him to issue all commands himself; the emperor devoted

[052-147] himself only to his pleasures, and care was taken that they should keep

[052-148] him sufficiently occupied to have no chance to notice what was going on

[052-149] in the country. The first important decree issued by Liu Chin resulted

[052-150] in the removal from office or the punishment or murder of over three

[052-151] hundred prominent persons, the leaders of the cliques opposed to him. He

[052-152] filled their posts with his own supporters, until all the higher posts

[052-153] in every department were in the hands of members of his group. He

[052-154] collected large sums of money which he quite openly extracted from the

[052-155] provinces as a special tax for his own benefit. When later his house was

[052-156] searched there were found 240,000 bars and 57,800 pieces of gold (a bar

[052-157] was equivalent of ten pieces), 791,800 ounces and 5,000,000 bars of

[052-158] silver (a bar was five ounces), three bushels of precious stones, two

[052-159] gold cuirasses, 3,000 gold rings, and much else--of a total value

[052-160] exceeding the annual budget of the state! The treasure was to have been

[052-161] used to finance a revolt planned by Liu Chin and his supporters.

[052-162] Among the people whom Liu Chin had punished were several members of the

[052-163] former clique of the Yang, and also the philosopher Wang Yang-ming, who

[052-164] later became so famous, a member of the Wang family which was allied to

[052-165] the Yang. In 1510 the Yang won over one of the eunuchs in the palace and

[052-166] so became acquainted with Liu Chin's plans. When a revolt broke out in

[052-167] western China, this eunuch (whose political allegiance was, of course,

[052-168] unknown to Liu Chin) secured appointment as army commander. With the

[052-169] army intended for the crushing of the revolt, Liu Chin's palace was

[052-170] attacked when he was asleep, and he and all his supporters were

[052-171] arrested. Thus the other group came into power in the palace, including

[052-172] the philosopher Wang Yang-ming (1473-1529). Liu Chin's rule had done

[052-173] great harm to the country, as enormous taxation had been expended for

[052-174] the private benefit of his clique. On top of this had been the young

[052-175] emperor's extravagance: his latest pleasures had been the building of

[052-176] palaces and the carrying out of military games; he constantly assumed

[052-177] new military titles and was burning to go to war.
