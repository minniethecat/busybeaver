## Overview

Yakub Beg (c. 1820–1877), known in Chinese sources as An Chi-yeh, was the most successful of the Mohammedan rebels who challenged [[ching-dynasty|Ch'ing dynasty]] authority in Central Asia during the nineteenth century. Rising from Chinese governorship to independent ruler of Kashgar, he conquered all of Chinese Turkestan (modern Sinkiang) by 1874 and established a state that secured diplomatic recognition from the Ottoman Empire, Russia, and Great Britain.

Eberhard distinguishes Yakub Beg's rebellion from contemporaneous Mohammedan uprisings in Kansu, Shensi, and Yünnan by its geopolitical character: unlike the domestic ethnic-religious revolts, Yakub Beg's state operated as a de facto independent country caught between the expanding Russian and British empires. His death in 1877 — and the subsequent Chinese reconquest — marked the beginning of the incorporation of Turkestan into modern China.

## Rise to Power

Yakub Beg's origins were modest. Eberhard records that he "had risen to the Chinese governorship when he made himself ruler of Kashgar" [059-052]. In 1866, amid the wider context of Mohammedan uprisings across western China, he "began to try to make himself independent of Chinese control" [059-054].

> "Yakub Beg (his Chinese name was An Chi-yeh) had risen to the Chinese governorship when he made himself ruler of Kashgar. In 1866 he began to try to make himself independent of Chinese control. He conquered Ili, and then in a rapid campaign made himself master of all Turkestan." [059-052]

His rapid conquest of Ili and the rest of Turkestan demonstrated both military capability and the vacuum of Chinese authority in the region. The Ch'ing dynasty, simultaneously grappling with the [[tai-ping-rebellion|Taiping Rebellion]], the Nien Rebellion, and other Mohammedan revolts, could not project effective power into distant Turkestan.

## Political Vision

Yakub Beg's state-building was more sophisticated than that of the other Mohammedan rebels. Eberhard observes that his state "had a much better prospect of endurance than the other Mohammedan states" because of its geopolitical position [059-057].

> "Turkestan was connected with China only by the few routes that led between the desert and the Tibetan mountains. The state was supported against China by Russia, which was continually pressing eastward, and in the south by Great Britain, which was pressing towards Tibet." [059-059]

Yakub Beg skillfully maneuvered between the great powers. Ottoman Turkey sent military missions: "Missions went to and fro, and Turkish officers came to Yakub Beg and organized his army" [059-065]. He "recognized the Turkish sultan as Khalif" — a symbolic act that gave his rule Islamic legitimacy while creating a formal connection to the Ottoman Empire [059-066]. He also "concluded treaties with Russia and Great Britain" [059-067], securing de facto recognition from the two empires whose rivalry in Central Asia defined the era's geopolitics.

## Downfall

Despite his diplomatic successes, Yakub Beg's state proved fragile. Eberhard notes that "in spite of all this he was unable to maintain his position indefinitely" [059-068]. The decisive factor was the recovery of Ch'ing military power. After the suppression of the Taiping and other eastern rebellions, the Ch'ing court turned its attention westward.

The Chinese commander [[tso-tsung-tang|Tso Tsung-t'ang]] led the reconquest. China then "ended Yakub Beg's rule" in 1877, though the exact circumstances of his death remain disputed — Chinese sources claimed he committed suicide, while others suggest he was assassinated or died of natural causes [059-071].

> "After the removal of Yakub Beg, China sent a mission to Russia with the aim of recovering the Ili region." [059-087]

The Chinese reconquest was followed by delicate negotiations with Russia, which had occupied the Ili region in 1881 during Yakub Beg's final years [059-082]. The eventual Sino-Russian treaty returned most of Ili to China, though Russia retained significant territorial concessions.

## Historical Assessment

Eberhard places Yakub Beg's rebellion within the broader context of late Ch'ing decline and recovery. The Mohammedan revolts — of which Yakub Beg's was the most consequential — revealed both the weakness of the dynasty (its inability to maintain control over peripheral territories) and its residual strength (its capacity, once eastern rebellions were suppressed, to reconquer lost territory).

Yakub Beg's state also illuminates the transformation of Central Asian politics under pressure from Russian and British imperialism. His attempt to create an independent Turkestani state, balanced between China, Russia, Britain, and the Ottoman Empire, anticipated the "Great Game" dynamics that would define the region into the twentieth century. The Chinese reconquest under [[tso-tsung-tang|Tso Tsung-t'ang]] established the modern Chinese claim to Sinkiang, making Yakub Beg's rebellion not merely a late imperial episode but a formative moment in the definition of China's modern borders.
