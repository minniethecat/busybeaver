## Overview

[[Ch'en Tu-hsiu]] (1879–1942) was the founding editor of _Hsin Ch'ing-nien_ ("New Youth"), the journal that became the rallying point for China's New Culture Movement, and subsequently a founding leader of the Chinese Communist Party. More radical than most of his intellectual contemporaries, Ch'en bridged the transition from Western-style liberal reformism to revolutionary communism in the tumultuous years following the First World War [062-010].

## New Youth and the New Culture Movement

In 1915, Ch'en Tu-hsiu created the journal _Hsin Ch'ing-nien_ ("New Youth"), which rapidly became the flagship publication of China's emerging progressive intelligentsia. The journal was resolutely progressive, taking a firm stand against the monarchy, against [[confucius|Confucius]], and against the old traditions that progressive intellectuals regarded as shackles on Chinese modernisation [062-010].

> The journal was progressive, against the monarchy, Confucius, and the old traditions. [062-010–062-011]

Ch'en himself was more radical than the other contributors to his journal. At first, however, his radicalism took a liberal rather than a socialist form: he favoured Western democracy and Western science, and was influenced principally by the American philosopher John Dewey, who served as a guest professor at [[peking|Peking]] University in 1919–1920 [062-012].

> Ch'en Tu-hsiu who put himself strongly behind the students, was more radical than other contributors but at first favoured Western democracy and Western science; he was influenced mainly by John Dewey who was guest professor in Peking in 1919-20. [062-012–062-015]

Alongside Ch'en, the New Culture Movement attracted a spectrum of reformist intellectuals. [[hu-shih|Hu Shih]], a student of Dewey's, tended towards liberalism in politics and pragmatism in philosophy [062-016]. Others criticised conservatism on the basis of traditional Chinese scholarship. When the student demonstrations of the [[may-fourth-movement|May Fourth Movement]] erupted in 1919, Ch'en threw himself strongly behind the students [062-012].

## Turn to Communism

The intellectual ferment of the May Fourth period pushed segments of the movement towards more radical solutions. The National University of [[peking|Peking]] became the first centre of the communist turn, and Ch'en Tu-hsiu — then dean of the College of Letters — from 1920 onward became one of the communist faction's leaders [064-163].

> Peking became the first centre of this movement, and Ch'en Tu-hsiu, then dean of the College of Letters, from 1920 on became one of its leaders. [064-163–064-164]

Not all of Ch'en's associates followed him leftward. [[hu-shih|Hu Shih]] remained a liberal and did not join the communist group [064-165]. The celebrated writer [[lu-hsun|Lu Hsün]] (1881–1936), though he followed [[hu-shih|Hu Shih]] in the "Literary Revolution" — the drive to replace classical Chinese with the vernacular in written communication — identified politically with Ch'en's communist faction [064-166]. Meanwhile, the university librarian Li Ta-chao, who had also turned towards communism, counted among his library employees the young [[mao-tse-tung|Mao Tse-tung]] [064-169].

## Legacy

Ch'en Tu-hsiu served as the first General Secretary of the Chinese Communist Party from its formal founding in 1921 until 1927, when he was removed from leadership following the party's catastrophic split with [[chiang-kai-shek|Chiang Kai-shek]]'s KMT. Expelled from the party in 1929 for alleged "Trotskyism," he spent his final years in relative obscurity. Yet his role as the intellectual catalyst of the New Culture Movement — and as the figure who first provided Chinese progressivism with an institutional platform — remained foundational to the transformation of twentieth-century Chinese thought.
