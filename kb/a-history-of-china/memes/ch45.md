# Section 45

[045-001] Then came the rising of the tribes of the north. They had remained

[045-002] military tribes; of all the wealth nothing reached them, and they were

[045-003] given no military employment, so that they had no hope of improving

[045-004] their position. The leadership was assumed by the tribe of the Juchên

[045-005] (1114). In a campaign of unprecedented rapidity they captured Peking,

[045-006] and the Liao dynasty was ended (1125), a year earlier, as we know, than

[045-007] the end of the Sung.

[045-008] 2 _The State of the Kara-Kitai_

[045-009] A small troop of Liao, under the command of a member of the ruling

[045-010] family, fled into the west. They were pursued without cessation, but

[045-011] they succeeded in fighting their way through. After a few years of

[045-012] nomad life in the mountains of northern Turkestan, they were able to

[045-013] gain the collaboration of a few more tribes, and with them they then

[045-014] invaded western Turkestan. There they founded the "Western Liao" state,

[045-015] or, as the western sources call it, the "Kara-Kitai" state, with its

[045-016] capital at Balasagun. This state must not be regarded as a purely Kitan

[045-017] state. The Kitan formed only a very thin stratum, and the real power was

[045-018] in the hands of autochthonous Turkish tribes, to whom the Kitan soon

[045-019] became entirely assimilated in culture. Thus the history of this state

[045-020] belongs to that of western Asia, especially as the relations of the

[045-021] Kara-Kitai with the Far East were entirely broken off. In 1211 the state

[045-022] was finally destroyed.

[045-023] (3) The Hsi-Hsia State in the north (1038-1227)

[045-024] 1 _Continuation of Turkish traditions_

[045-025] After the end of the Toba state in North China in 550, some tribes of

[045-026] the Toba, including members of the ruling tribe with the tribal name

[045-027] Toba, withdrew to the borderland between Tibet and China, where they

[045-028] ruled over Tibetan and Tangut tribes. At the beginning of the T'ang

[045-029] dynasty this tribe of Toba joined the T'ang. The tribal leader received

[045-030] in return, as a distinction, the family name of the T'ang dynasty, Li.

[045-031] His dependence on China was, however, only nominal and soon came

[045-032] entirely to an end. In the tenth century the tribe gained in strength.

[045-033] It is typical of the long continuance of old tribal traditions that a

[045-034] leader of the tribe in the tenth century married a woman belonging to

[045-035] the family to which the khans of the Hsiung-nu and all Turkish ruling

[045-036] houses had belonged since 200 B.C. With the rise of the Kitan in the

[045-037] north and of the Tibetan state in the south, the tribe decided to seek

[045-038] the friendship of China. Its first mission, in 982, was well received.

[045-039] Presents were sent to the chieftain of the tribe, he was helped against

[045-040] his enemies, and he was given the status of a feudatory of the Sung; in

[045-041] 988 the family name of the Sung, Chao, was conferred on him. Then the

[045-042] Kitan took a hand. They over-trumped the Sung by proclaiming the tribal

[045-043] chieftain king of Hsia (990). Now the small state became interesting. It

[045-044] was pampered by Liao and Sung in the effort to win it over or to keep

[045-045] its friendship. The state grew; in 1031 its ruler resumed the old family

[045-046] name of the Toba, thus proclaiming his intention to continue the Toba

[045-047] empire; in 1034 he definitely parted from the Sung, and in 1038 he

[045-048] proclaimed himself emperor in the Hsia dynasty, or, as the Chinese

[045-049] generally called it, the "Hsi-Hsia", which means the Western Hsia. This

[045-050] name, too, had associations with the old Hun tradition; it recalled the

[045-051] state of Ho-lien P'o-p'o in the early fifth century. The state soon

[045-052] covered the present province of Kansu, small parts of the adjoining

[045-053] Tibetan territory, and parts of the Ordos region. It attacked the

[045-054] province of Shensi, but the Chinese and the Liao attached the greatest

[045-055] importance to that territory. Thus that was the scene of most of the

[045-056] fighting.

[045-057] [Illustration: 12 Ancient tiled pagoda at Chengting (Hopei). _Photo H.

[045-058] Hammer-Morrisson_.]

[045-059] [Illustration: 13 Horse-training. Painting by Li Lung-mien. Late Sung

[045-060] period. _Manchu Royal House Collection_.] The Hsia state had a ruling

[045-061] group of Toba, but these Toba had become entirely tibetanized. The

[045-062] language of the country was Tibetan; the customs were those of the

[045-063] Tanguts. A script was devised, in imitation of the Chinese script. Only

[045-064] in recent years has it begun to be studied.

[045-065] In 1125, when the Tungusic Juchên destroyed the Liao, the Hsia also lost

[045-066] large territories in the east of their country, especially the province

[045-067] of Shensi, which they had conquered; but they were still able to hold

[045-068] their own. Their political importance to China, however, vanished, since

[045-069] they were now divided from southern China and as partners were no longer

[045-070] of the same value to it. Not until the Mongols became a power did the

[045-071] Hsia recover some of their importance; but they were among the first

[045-072] victims of the Mongols: in 1209 they had to submit to them, and in 1227,

[045-073] the year of the death of Genghiz Khan, they were annihilated.

[045-074] (4) The empire of the Southern Sung dynasty (1127-1279)

[045-075] 1 _Foundation_

[045-076] In the disaster of 1126, when the Juchên captured the Sung capital and

[045-077] destroyed the Sung empire, a brother of the captive emperor escaped. He

[045-078] made himself emperor in Nanking and founded the "Southern Sung" dynasty,

[045-079] whose capital was soon shifted to the present Hangchow. The foundation

[045-080] of the new dynasty was a relatively easy matter, and the new state was

[045-081] much more solid than the southern kingdoms of 800 years earlier, for the

[045-082] south had already been economically supreme, and the great families that

[045-083] had ruled the state were virtually all from the south. The loss of the

[045-084] north, i.e. the area north of the Yellow River and of parts of Kiangsu,

[045-085] was of no importance to this governing group and meant no loss of

[045-086] estates to it. Thus the transition from the Northern to the Southern

[045-087] Sung was not of fundamental importance. Consequently the Juchên had no

[045-088] chance of success when they arranged for Liu Yü, who came of a northern

[045-089] Chinese family of small peasants and had become an official, to be

[045-090] proclaimed emperor in the "Ch'i" dynasty in 1130. They hoped that this

[045-091] puppet might attract the southern Chinese, but seven years later they

[045-092] dropped him.

[045-093] 2 _Internal situation_

[045-094] As the social structure of the Southern Sung empire had not been

[045-095] changed, the country was not affected by the dynastic development. Only

[045-096] the policy of diplomacy could not be pursued at once, as the Juchên were

[045-097] bellicose at first and would not negotiate. There were therefore several

[045-098] battles at the outset (in 1131 and 1134), in which the Chinese were

[045-099] actually the more successful, but not decisively. The Sung military

[045-100] group was faced as early as in 1131 with furious opposition from the

[045-101] greater gentry, led by Ch'in K'ui, one of the largest landowners of all.

[045-102] His estates were around Nanking, and so in the deployment region and the

[045-103] region from which most of the soldiers had to be drawn for the defensive

[045-104] struggle. Ch'in K'ui secured the assassination of the leader of the

[045-105] military party, General Yo Fei, in 1141, and was able to conclude peace

[045-106] with the Juchên. The Sung had to accept the status of vassals and to pay

[045-107] annual tribute to the Juchên. This was the situation that best pleased

[045-108] the greater gentry. They paid hardly any taxes (in many districts the

[045-109] greater gentry directly owned more than 30 per cent of the land, in

[045-110] addition to which they had indirect interests in the soil), and they

[045-111] were now free from the war peril that ate into their revenues. The

[045-112] tribute amounted only to 500,000 strings of cash. Popular literature,

[045-113] however, to this day represents Ch'in K'ui as a traitor and Yo Fei as a

[045-114] national hero.

[045-115] In 1165 it was agreed between the Sung and the Juchên to regard each

[045-116] other as states with equal rights. It is interesting to note here that

[045-117] in the treaties during the Han time with the Hsiung-nu, the two

[045-118] countries called one another brothers--with the Chinese ruler as the

[045-119] older and thus privileged brother; but the treaties since the T'ang time

[045-120] with northern powers and with Tibetans used the terms father-in-law and

[045-121] son-in-law. The foreign power was the "father-in-law", i.e. the older

[045-122] and, therefore, in a certain way the more privileged; the Chinese were

[045-123] the "son-in-law", the representative of the paternal lineage and,

[045-124] therefore, in another respect also the more privileged! In spite of such

[045-125] agreements with the Juchên, fighting continued, but it was mainly of the

[045-126] character of frontier engagements. Not until 1204 did the military

[045-127] party, led by Han T'o-wei, regain power; it resolved upon an active

[045-128] policy against the north. In preparation for this a military reform was

[045-129] carried out. The campaign proved a disastrous failure, as a result of

[045-130] which large territories in the north were lost. The Sung sued for

[045-131] peace; Han T'o-wei's head was cut off and sent to the Juchên. In this

[045-132] way peace was restored in 1208. The old treaty relationship was now

[045-133] resumed, but the relations between the two states remained tense.

[045-134] Meanwhile the Sung observed with malicious pleasure how the Mongols were

[045-135] growing steadily stronger, first destroying the Hsia state and then

[045-136] aiming the first heavy blows against the Juchên. In the end the Sung

[045-137] entered into alliance with the Mongols (1233) and joined them in

[045-138] attacking the Juchên, thus hastening the end of the Juchên state.

[045-139] The Sung now faced the Mongols, and were defenceless against them. All

[045-140] the buffer states had gone. The Sung were quite without adequate

[045-141] military defence. They hoped to stave off the Mongols in the same way as

[045-142] they had met the Kitan and the Juchên. This time, however, they

[045-143] misjudged the situation. In the great operations begun by the Mongols in

[045-144] 1273 the Sung were defeated over and over again. In 1276 their capital

[045-145] was taken by the Mongols and the emperor was made prisoner. For three

[045-146] years longer there was a Sung emperor, in flight from the Mongols, until

[045-147] the last emperor perished near Macao in South China.

[045-148] 3 _Cultural situation; reasons for the collapse_

[045-149] The Southern Sung period was again one of flourishing culture. The

[045-150] imperial court was entirely in the power of the greater gentry; several

[045-151] times the emperors, who personally do not deserve individual mention,

[045-152] were compelled to abdicate. They then lived on with a court of their

[045-153] own, devoting themselves to pleasure in much the same way as the

[045-154] "reigning" emperor. Round them was a countless swarm of poets and

[045-155] artists. Never was there a time so rich in poets, though hardly one of

[045-156] them was in any way outstanding. The poets, unlike those of earlier

[045-157] times, belonged to the lesser gentry who were suffering from the

[045-158] prevailing inflation. Salaries bore no relation to prices. Food was not

[045-159] dear, but the things which a man of the upper class ought to have were

[045-160] far out of reach: a big house cost 2,000 strings of cash, a concubine

[045-161] 800 strings. Thus the lesser gentry and the intelligentsia all lived on

[045-162] their patrons among the greater gentry--with the result that they were

[045-163] entirely shut out of politics. This explains why the literature of the

[045-164] time is so unpolitical, and also why scarcely any philosophical works

[045-165] appeared. The writers took refuge more and more in romanticism and

[045-166] flight from realities.

[045-167] The greater gentry, on the other hand, led a very elegant life, building

[045-168] themselves magnificent palaces in the capital. They also speculated in

[045-169] every direction. They speculated in land, in money, and above all in

[045-170] the paper money that was coming more and more into use. In 1166 the

[045-171] paper circulation exceeded the value of 10,000,000 strings!

[045-172] It seems that after 1127 a good number of farmers had left Honan and the

[045-173] Yellow River plains when the Juchên conquered these places and showed

[045-174] little interest in fostering agriculture; more left the border areas of

[045-175] Southern Sung because of permanent war threat. Many of these lived

[045-176] miserably as tenants on the farms of the gentry between Nanking and

[045-177] Hangchow. Others migrated farther to the south, across Kiangsi into

[045-178] southern Fukien. These migrants seem to have been the ancestors of the

[045-179] Hakka which in the following centuries continued their migration towards

[045-180] the south and who from the nineteenth century on were most strongly

[045-181] concentrated in Kwangtung and Kwangsi provinces as free farmers on hill

[045-182] slopes or as tenants of local landowners in the plains.
