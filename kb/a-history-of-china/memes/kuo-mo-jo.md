## Overview

**Kuo Mo-jo** (1892–1978) was one of the most influential Chinese historians and intellectuals of the twentieth century. A prolific scholar, writer, and public figure, he made foundational contributions to the study of ancient Chinese society, including the [[Shang Dynasty]], slavery in early China, the well-field (ching-t'ien) system, and the origins of Chinese law. His work is frequently cited in [[Eberhard]]'s scholarly notes as a key reference point for debates on early Chinese social and economic history.

## Scholarly Contributions

### Early Agriculture and Technology

Kuo Mo-jo advanced the thesis that the Shang dynasty had already developed relatively sophisticated agricultural technology:

> "Kuo Mo-jo believes, that the Shang already used a real plough drawn by animals." [068-055]

This position placed him in ongoing debates about the technological level of Bronze Age China and the transition from Neolithic agriculture to more intensive forms of cultivation.

### Slavery and Social Structure

His research on slavery in ancient China was part of a broader scholarly conversation about the nature of Shang and Chou society. His work was consulted alongside that of other major scholars:

> "The work on slaves by T. Pippon, E. Erkes, M. Wilbur, Wan Kuo-ting, Kuo Mo-jo, Niida Noboru, Kao Nien-chih and others has been consulted." [068-116]

### The Well-Field System

Kuo Mo-jo took a skeptical position on the historicity of the well-field (ching-t'ien) system, the idealized land-distribution scheme described in the [[Mencius]] and the Chou-li:

> "Some authors, such as Kuo Mo-jo, regard the whole system as an utopia, but believe in an original 'village community'." [068-125]

This represented a middle ground between those who accepted the well-field system as historical fact and those who dismissed it entirely as a later fabrication.

### The Introduction of Iron

His work also contributed to the dating and significance of iron metallurgy in China:

> "For the introduction of iron and the importance of Ch'i, see Chu Hsi-tsu, Kuo Mo-jo, Yang K'uan, Sekino, Takeshi." [068-145]

### Legal History

In the debate over whether the Legalist reformer Li K'ui was the principal creator of Chinese codified law, Kuo Mo-jo was among those who supported the traditional attribution:

> "Kuo Mo-jo and others do [regard Li K'ui as the main creator of Chinese law]. It is obvious, however, that Han law was not a creation of the Han Chinese alone." [069-036]

Kuo Mo-jo's work on oracle-bone inscriptions and bronze vessel inscriptions was also foundational. His studies of Shang oracle texts helped establish the chronology and social structure of the Shang dynasty, and his analysis of Western Chou bronze inscriptions illuminated the political and ritual systems of the early Chou period.

## Career and Legacy

Kuo Mo-jo was not only a scholar but also a prominent literary figure and a public intellectual. He served in various official positions after 1949, including president of the Chinese Academy of Sciences. His Marxist approach to Chinese historiography — interpreting ancient Chinese history through the lens of historical materialism and stages of social development — became highly influential, though also controversial among scholars who questioned the applicability of Western-derived periodization to Chinese history.

His numerous publications on oracle bones, bronze inscriptions, and ancient society remain standard references in the field of early Chinese history.
