## Overview

[[K'ang-hsi]] (Sheng Tsu, 1654–1722) was the second emperor of the Manchu [[ching-dynasty|Ch'ing dynasty]] and one of the greatest rulers in Chinese history. His reign, which lasted from 1662 to 1722, consolidated the Manchu conquest, expanded the empire's frontiers, and oversaw a revival of Chinese scholarship and culture that won the loyalty of the gentry class [055-013].

## Biography

The young emperor Sheng Tsu — K'ang-hsi being the name of his reign period, not his personal name — ascended the throne as a child and quickly demonstrated a keen interest in Chinese culture. He gave privileged treatment to the scholars of the gentry who came forward, initiating a deliberate policy of cultural reconciliation between the Manchu ruling house and the Chinese educated elite [055-013].

> The young emperor Sheng Tsu (1663-1722; K'ang-hsi is the name by which his rule was known, not his name) was keenly interested in Chinese culture and gave privileged treatment to the scholars of the gentry who came forward. [055-013–055-015]

A rapid recovery took place in the early decades of his rule. The disturbances of the transitional years had eliminated the worst enemies of the people — the formidable rival cliques and power-hungry individuals — and the gentry had become more cautious in their behaviour toward the peasants [055-016].

## Consolidation and Expansion

The rise of the Manchu dynasty actually began under K'ang-hsi's rule. The emperor faced three principal tasks: the elimination of the last supporters of the Ming dynasty and of ambitious generals such as [[wu-san-kui|Wu San-kui]] who had tried to make themselves independent; the occupation of Formosa (Taiwan), accomplished in 1683; and the assertion of control over the [[mongol|Mongols]] of Central Asia [055-187].

> The rise of the Manchu dynasty actually began under the K'ang-hsi rule (1663-1722). The emperor had three tasks. The first was the removal of the last supporters of the Ming dynasty and of the generals, such as Wu San-kui, who had tried to make themselves independent. [055-187–055-191]

These campaigns, most of them in the south-west or south of China, scarcely affected the population of China proper. In 1683 Formosa was occupied and the last Ming loyalist resistance was extinguished [055-192].

## Cultural Policy and Manchu Sinicisation

Even in K'ang-hsi's own time, the Manchus began to forget their native language. Tutors were brought to court to teach the young Manchus Chinese, and before long even the emperors themselves did not understand Manchurian [056-008]. This rapid assimilation had strategic consequences: the [[mongol|Mongols]] became alienated from the Manchus, and the situation began to resemble that of the Ming period, with restive steppe peoples pressing against the imperial frontier [056-010].

> Even in the time of K'ang-hsi the Manchus began to forget Manchurian; they brought tutors to court to teach the young Manchus Chinese. Later even the emperors did not understand Manchurian! [056-008–056-010]

## Patronage of Learning

Under K'ang-hsi, Chinese culture began to flourish again. The emperor had attracted the gentry — and with them the intelligentsia — to his court, knowing that his uneducated Manchus could not alone administer the enormous empire [056-070]. He showed great personal interest in Chinese culture, delved deeply into it himself, and commissioned many works of compilation, especially encyclopaedias that enabled information to be rapidly gained on all sorts of subjects [056-072].

> In this K'ang-hsi period culture began to flourish again. The emperor had attracted the gentry, and so the intelligentsia, to his court because his uneducated Manchus could not alone have administered the enormous empire; and he showed great interest in Chinese culture, himself delved deeply into it, and had many works compiled, especially works of an encyclopaedic character. [056-070–056-076]

The most famous of these enterprises — the _K'ang-hsi Tzu-tien_ (K'ang-hsi Dictionary) and the _Ku-chin T'u-shu Chi-ch'eng_ ("Complete Collection of Illustrations and Writings from the Earliest to Current Times") — remained standard reference works for centuries.

## Legacy

K'ang-hsi set the template for the great Manchu emperors. His combination of military vigilance, administrative competence, and sincere cultural engagement secured the dynasty's legitimacy among the Chinese [[gentry|literati]]. His grandson, the [[chien-lung|Ch'ien-lung]] emperor, consciously modelled his own sixty-year reign on K'ang-hsi's example — though the empire [[chien-lung|Ch'ien-lung]] inherited was already far larger and more secure than the one K'ang-hsi had been called upon to consolidate.
