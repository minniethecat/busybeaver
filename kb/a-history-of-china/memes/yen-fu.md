## Overview

[[yen-fu|Yen Fu]] (1853–1921) was the first major Chinese translator of Western philosophical, scientific, and sociological works. His translations, beginning in the latter half of the nineteenth century, marked the start of a new literary style that broke from classical conventions and paved the way for the "literary revolution" of 1917.

> Translation began with Yen Fu (1853-1921), who translated the first philosophical and scientific books and books on social questions and made his compatriots acquainted with Western thought. [056-137] [056-138] [056-139]

## Translation and the New Style

Yen Fu's work was part of a broader transformation in Chinese intellectual life that began around the middle of the nineteenth century, as the influence of Europe became increasingly pronounced. He focused on works of philosophy, science, and social thought — subjects that had no existing vocabulary in classical Chinese, forcing him to develop new modes of expression.

> About the middle of the nineteenth century the influence of Europe became more and more marked. [056-136]

Together with Lin Shu (1852–1924), who translated Western short stories and novels, Yen Fu inaugurated what contemporaries recognized as a "new style" of Chinese prose. This style was soon elaborated by [[liang-chi-chao|Liang Ch'i-ch'ao]] and other reformist intellectuals, ultimately contributing to the literary revolution of 1917.

> With these two began the new style, which was soon elaborated by Liang Ch'i-ch'ao, a collaborator of Sun Yat-sen's, and by others, and which ultimately produced the "literary revolution" of 1917. [056-141] [056-142] [056-143] [056-144]

## Legacy

Yen Fu's pioneering translations opened a window onto Western thought at a critical moment in China's modern transformation. The translation movement he initiated continued through the twentieth century, with almost every book of outstanding importance in world literature eventually rendered into Chinese.

> Translation has continued to this day; almost every book of outstanding importance in world literature is translated within a short time of its appearance. [056-144] [056-145]
