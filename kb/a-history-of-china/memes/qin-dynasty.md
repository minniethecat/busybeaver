## Overview

The Ch'in dynasty (221–206 B.C.) was the first imperial dynasty of a unified China. Founded by Shih Huang Ti, it conquered all rival feudal states. Though brief, its fifteen-year reign transformed Chinese civilization.

The Ch'in abolished the old [[chou-dynasty]] feudal order and replaced it with a centralized bureaucratic administration. It standardized writing, weights, measures, and even the gauge of cart axles across the realm.

> The territories of the state of Ch'in, the present Shensi and eastern Kansu, were from a geographical point of view transit regions, closed off in the north by steppes and deserts and in the south by almost impassable mountains. (013-009)

The dynasty was built on [[legalism]], the state philosophy that demanded absolute obedience to the ruler and strict laws. Its most famous advocate was [[shang-yang]], who reorganized Ch'in society on militarized lines.

The Ch'in represents the transition from antiquity to the Middle Ages (014-078). Its administrative system provided the template for the [[han-dynasty]] and all later Chinese empires, despite its rapid collapse.

## Origins and Rise

The Ch'in began as a feudal state under the [[chou-dynasty]] in north-west China. In the 8th century B.C. a Ch'in prince saved the Chou ruler and reclaimed lost land (007-143).

Five centuries later this family ruled all China.

> The state of Ch'in had never been so closely associated with the feudal communities of the rest of China as the other feudal states. A great part of its population, including the ruling class, was not purely Chinese but contained an admixture of Turks and Tibetans. The other Chinese even called Ch'in a "barbarian state." (013-024)

Ch'in's peripheral position proved an advantage. Feudal traditions were least developed there, and alien peoples strongly represented (012-098). It mobilized peasants for war and adopted cavalry early (011-035).

In the 4th century B.C., [[shang-yang]] (died 338 B.C.) reorganized the state on Legalist principles. The population was militarized, land redistributed, and mutual responsibility enforced.

By 250 B.C., Ch'in had "made an end of its own feudal system" (013-035). Between 230 and 222 B.C. it conquered all rivals. In 256 B.C. the last Chou emperor abdicated in Ch'in's favor (012-111).

## Government and Standardization

After unification, the realm was split into provinces and prefectures. Each province had civil and military governors, plus a controller reporting to the emperor (013-100). No single official could build feudal power.

The Ch'in standardized the written language, adopting a uniform script throughout the empire. Local characters and borrowed words were eliminated from official use (013-127).

Weights and measures were standardized for central tax collection (013-143). Even the gauge of wagon tracks was fixed, ensuring all carts could use the same roads (013-154).

In 213 B.C., the great burning of books destroyed Confucianist writings. They "kept alive the memory of the old feudal conditions" that the new order had abolished (013-170). Only practical works were spared.

## The Reign of Shih Huang Ti

Shih Huang Ti was born the son of a Ch'in prince and a merchant's concubine. He ascended as a child under the regency of [[lu-pu-wei]], a horse-dealer turned prime minister (013-062).

After dismissing his regent, he surrounded himself with Legalist advisers and launched the wars of unification. Between 230 and 222 B.C., all rival feudal states fell.

The First Emperor deported nobles to the capital, stripping their local power (013-081). He wanted "industrious, uneducated, and tax-paying peasants" (013-163). Scholars were unwanted unless serving the state.

His reign saw massive construction. Old walls were joined against the [[hsiung-nu]]. Completed in 214 B.C., the [[great-wall-of-china]] was built "out of the blood and sweat of countless pressed labourers" (014-054).

## Collapse

Shih Huang Ti's death on a journey "was the signal for the rising of many rebellious elements" (014-056). Within months, six rebellions erupted across the empire.

Nobles sought to regain lost power. Generals rebelled against central control. Common people rose against the crushing burden of forced labor far from home (014-057).

The succession collapsed. The young heir was assassinated. By 206 B.C., the rebel Liu Pang entered the capital and dethroned the nominal emperor (014-065).

After defeating his rivals, Liu Pang founded the [[han-dynasty]], which ruled China for four centuries. The Ch'in's centralized structures endured, forming the basis of all later imperial governance.
