## Overview

[[Liu Shao-ch'i]] was a senior leader of the Chinese Communist Party who rose to serve as President (Chairman) of the People's Republic of China. He represented a more pragmatic, economically-oriented line within the Party, favouring practical development over [[mao-tse-tung|Mao Tse-tung]]'s doctrine of permanent revolutionary struggle. In 1966, his rivalry with [[mao-tse-tung|Mao Tse-tung]] erupted into open conflict, culminating in Liu's purge during the Cultural Revolution [067-102–067-105]. He was publicly condemned as the "number one capitalist-roader," stripped of all positions, and died under harsh conditions in 1969. He was posthumously rehabilitated by the Party in 1980.

## Role in the CCP

Liu Shao-ch'i was a key political leader within the Chinese Communist Party during its consolidation of power after 1949 [073-621]. As Chairman of the PRC, he occupied the highest state office, while Mao retained control of the Party apparatus. Over time, their diverging visions for China's development hardened into incompatible positions: Liu's pragmatism emphasised economic recovery and improving living standards for the generation that had endured decades of war and revolution, while Mao insisted on continued ideological struggle and sacrifice [067-105–067-110].

## The 1966 Power Struggle

By the 1960s, Mao and his old revolutionary comrades had aged, and a new generation had appeared for whom the pre-revolutionary "old regime" was dim history rather than lived experience [067-099–067-101]. Against this backdrop, the struggle between Mao and Liu Shao-ch'i became increasingly apparent in 1966 [067-102].

> In the struggle between Mao and Liu Shao-ch'i, which became increasingly apparent in 1966, Mao tried to retain his power by mobilizing young people as "Red Guards" and by inciting them to make the "Great Proletarian Revolution." [067-102–067-105]

Mao's mobilisation of youth as Red Guards was a tactical innovation: facing a Party apparatus in which Liu had substantial institutional support, Mao bypassed the formal structure and appealed directly to the young, urging them to attack "persons in authority taking the capitalist road" — a formulation aimed squarely at Liu and his allies.

## Theoretical Disagreements

The motives behind the Mao-Liu struggle were complex, blending personal rivalry with substantive theoretical disagreements [067-105–067-107]. Among the key questions dividing them was whether China's present generation should toil only to build a better life for the next, or whether it should itself enjoy the fruits of its labour after decades of suffering [067-107–067-109].

> The motives behind the struggle are diverse. It is on the one hand a conflict of persons contending for power, but there are also disagreements over theory: for example, should China's present generation toil to make possible a better life only for the next generation, or should it enjoy the fruits of its labor, after its many years of suffering? Mao opposes such "weakening" and favours a new generation willing to endure hardships, as he did in his youth. [067-105–067-111]

A further dimension of the conflict concerned the international orientation of communism: whether the Chinese Party under Mao's banner should supplant the Soviet Union as leader of world communism, or collaborate with the Russians to secure material support for China's development [067-112–067-116]. These strategic questions intersected with the domestic power struggle, making the conflict between Mao and Liu not merely personal but ideological in scope.

## Aftermath and Legacy

When Chinese youth were summoned to fight for Mao, forces were loosed that could not be controlled. Independent action by Red Guard factions, comparable in nature to youth revolts then occurring in Western countries, eroded the power and prestige of the entire older leadership [067-117–067-121]. Even by 1969, it remained impossible to re-establish unity and order; the Mao and Liu factions still opposed each other, and local conflicts persisted [067-121–067-122].

Liu Shao-ch'i himself did not survive the turmoil he had been engulfed by. Expelled from the Party in 1968, he died in 1969 — by some accounts denied medical care while under detention. His posthumous rehabilitation in 1980, under the post-Mao leadership of Teng Hsiao-p'ing, acknowledged that his persecution had been unjust, and his contributions to the Party and state were officially restored.
