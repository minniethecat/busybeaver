## Overview

Wang Tun was a military governor who launched the first major rebellion against the [[tsin-dynasty|Eastern Tsin]] court in the early fourth century CE. Based in the region of present-day Hankow — strategically positioned at the confluence of trade routes from Szechwan, Kweichow, [[hsiung-nu|Hu]]nan, and the [[yangtze-river|Yangtze]] — Wang amassed wealth and military power that enabled him to march on the imperial capital at Nanking. Eberhard presents Wang Tun's rebellion as the inaugural instance of a recurring pattern in the Eastern Tsin period: powerful regional commanders leveraging economic and military resources to challenge a weak central court.

His uprising, though ultimately defeated by General [[yu-liang|Yü Liang]], established the template for later challengers including [[huan-wen|Huan Wen]], who Eberhard explicitly compares to Wang Tun [032-045].

## Rise to Power

Wang Tun's power base was the middle [[yangtze-river|Yangtze]] region. Eberhard explains the strategic logic of this location:

> "To it lead all the trade routes from the western provinces of Szechwan and Kweichow and from the central provinces of Hupei, Hunan, and Kiangsi. Normally the traffic from those provinces comes down the Yangtze, and thus in practice this region is united with that of the lower Yangtze, the environment of Nanking, so that Hankow might just as well have been the capital as Nanking." [032-014]

This economic geography meant that whoever controlled the Hankow region controlled the commercial arteries of the southern empire. Wang Tun exploited this position to "grow rich and powerful" [032-023], accumulating resources that rivaled those of the imperial court.

## Rebellion

Wang Tun's uprising began during the reign of Emperor [[yuan-ti|Yüan Ti]], the founder of the Eastern Tsin. The rebellion exploited the weakness of a court that had only recently re-established itself in the south after the collapse of the Western Tsin.

> "Wang Tun had grown rich and powerful in this region; he also had near relatives at the imperial court; so he was able to march against the capital. The emperor in his weakness was ready to abdicate but died before that stage was reached." [032-023]

The rebellion thus came close to succeeding through a combination of military force and court connections. The emperor's death — whether from natural causes or the stress of the crisis — left the dynasty in the hands of his son, who proved more capable of organizing resistance.

The new emperor, with the support of General [[yu-liang|Yü Liang]] (the empress's brother), defeated Wang Tun in 323 CE [032-026]. [[yu-liang|Yü Liang]]'s role in suppressing the rebellion elevated him to a dominant position at court, where he would become a central figure in Eastern Tsin politics.

## Influence and Legacy

Wang Tun's rebellion established a recurring pattern in the Eastern Tsin and subsequent southern dynasties. Eberhard explicitly links Wang Tun to later challengers:

> "Huan Wen, like Wang Tun and others before him, tried to secure a firm position of power first of all in this region, from which the whole of the lower Yangtze valley could be controlled." [032-045]

This pattern — a military governor building power in the middle [[yangtze-river|Yangtze]], then marching downstream to threaten or seize the capital — repeated throughout the Southern Dynasties period. The geographic logic that Eberhard identifies — the unity of the [[yangtze-river|Yangtze]] trade system, the equivalence of Hankow and Nanking as potential capitals — meant that any strongman in the middle [[yangtze-river|Yangtze]] was inherently a threat to the lower [[yangtze-river|Yangtze]] court.

The structural weakness that Wang Tun exploited was itself a product of the Tsin migration southward. The Eastern Tsin court, transplanted from the north and dependent on the support of powerful émigré families, never achieved the centralized control that would have been necessary to prevent regional commanders from accumulating independent power.
