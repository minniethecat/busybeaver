## Overview

[[kao-tsu|Kao Tsu]] (personal name Liu Pang, r. 202–195 B.C.) was the founder of the [[han-dynasty|Han dynasty]], one of the most consequential reigns in Chinese imperial history. Born into a farming family in eastern China, he came to power during the civil wars that followed the collapse of the Ch'in empire, defeating his aristocratic rival Hsiang Yü through a combination of strategic pragmatism and an uncanny ability to attract talented followers. As emperor, he forged a compromise between the centralising legacy of Ch'in Legalism and the need to co-opt the emerging gentry class — a settlement that would define Chinese governance until the twentieth century [014-077][014-087].

## Rise from Obscurity

Liu Pang began his career as a minor village official — a "t'ing-chang" or patrol officer — in the region of modern Kiangsu. When the rebellion against the Ch'in dynasty erupted in 209 B.C., he joined the insurgent forces and gradually built a following among peasants, local strongmen, and disaffected Ch'in officials. His great rival Hsiang Yü, an aristocrat of the old Ch'u ruling house, commanded superior forces and possessed the prestige of lineage; Liu Pang possessed neither, but compensated with adaptability and a willingness to delegate authority to able subordinates [014-077].

> The period of the Han dynasty began with the accession of the first commoner to mount the Chinese throne, the farmer Liu Pang, who ruled under the name of Kao Tsu. [014-077]

After Hsiang Yü's defeat and suicide in 202 B.C., Liu Pang took the imperial title and established his capital at Ch'ang-an. He chose the dynastic name "Han," after the river valley in which he had first been enfeoffed — a name that would come to designate the Chinese people themselves [014-087].

## The Gentry Compromise

As emperor, Kao Tsu faced the fundamental problem that would occupy every subsequent Han ruler: how to govern a vast agrarian empire without the resources of a modern bureaucracy. His solution was to create a symbiotic relationship between the throne and the gentry — the class of landowning families who controlled local society. Kao Tsu allowed many of the harsh Ch'in laws to lapse, reducing the tax burden and granting the gentry a substantial degree of local autonomy in return for their recognition of imperial authority [014-094][014-102].

> Emperor Kao Tsu came from eastern China, and his old friends and fellow-countrymen formed the core of the new ruling group. [014-087][014-094]

This arrangement was not the product of any grand design but of practical necessity. Kao Tsu and his original followers were themselves farmers and minor gentry; they governed as they understood, relying on personal networks rather than impersonal institutions. The system of "gentry society" that emerged — in which local elites supplied the officials who staffed the imperial administration — proved remarkably durable, persisting in its essentials until the twentieth century [014-110][015-043].

> Gentry society continued from Kao Tsu's time to 1948, but it went through many changes. [015-043]

## Confrontation with the Hsiung-nu

Kao Tsu's reign also saw the first major confrontation between a unified China and the nomadic confederation of the [[hsiung-nu]], led by the formidable shan-yü [[mao-tun|Mao Tun]]. In 200 B.C., the emperor personally led a campaign against the [[hsiung-nu|Hsiung-nu]] and was trapped at P'ai-teng, near modern Ta-t'ung, narrowly escaping capture or death. The debacle forced a strategic recalibration: Kao Tsu adopted a policy of appeasement, sending Chinese princesses as brides and annual gifts of silk and grain to the [[hsiung-nu|Hsiung-nu]] court — the origins of the "ho-ch'in" (peace-and-kinship) system that would govern Sino-nomadic relations for centuries [015-102][015-103][015-112].

> Kao Tsu was faced in Mao Tun not with any ordinary barbarian but with the most dangerous of enemies. In 200 B.C. Kao Tsu was very near suffering disaster. [015-102][015-112]

Kao Tsu died in 195 B.C., leaving the empire to his widow, the Empress Lü, who would dominate the court for the next fifteen years. His dynasty, however, would endure for four centuries — the longest continuous imperial house in Chinese history — and the ethnic majority of China would forever after call themselves "the men of Han" [015-160].
