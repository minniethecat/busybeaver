Republican China was the political entity that succeeded the [[ching-dynasty|Ch'ing dynasty]] after the revolution of 1911 and represented China's first attempt at constitutional, non-monarchical government. The Republic's history falls into two broad periods: the era of the warlords from 1912 to 1927, when central authority collapsed and regional military commanders held effective power, and the period of Nationalist China from 1927 to 1949, when the [[kuomintang|Kuomintang]] (KMT) under [[chiang-kai-shek|Chiang Kai-shek]] established a fragile national government that ultimately succumbed to Japanese invasion and civil war with the Communists.

> The Republic of China, proclaimed in 1912, was the first attempt in Chinese history to create a modern nation-state on the ruins of the imperial system.
>
> — [061-026]

## The 1911 Revolution and Founding

The revolution that overthrew the Ch'ing dynasty began with a military uprising at Wuchang in October 1911 and spread rapidly through the provinces. The revolutionary movement found its symbolic leader in [[sun-yat-sen|Sun Yat-sen]], a Western-educated physician who had spent years organizing opposition to Manchu rule from exile.

> Sun Yat-sen (1866–1925), who had been living abroad and turned the scattered revolutionary groups into a coherent political movement, was elected provisional president of the Republic in Nanking on December 29, 1911.
>
> — [060-112]

Sun's government in Nanking, however, lacked the military power to control the entire country. To avoid prolonged civil war, Sun resigned the presidency in favor of [[yuan-shih-kai|Yüan Shih-k'ai]], the commander of the Ch'ing imperial army, who had negotiated the abdication of the Manchu emperor and commanded the loyalty of the most powerful military forces in the north.

> Following the abdication of the imperial house, Sun Yat-sen resigned in Nanking, and Yüan Shih-k'ai was recognized as president of the Republic.
>
> — [061-032]

## The Warlord Era (1912–1927)

The Republic's early years were marked by the collapse of central authority and the fragmentation of the country into domains controlled by regional military commanders—the warlords. Republican sentiment was confined largely to a small, Western-educated urban elite, while the vast majority of the population remained oriented toward traditional forms of local authority.

> Republican feeling existed only among a thin stratum of intellectuals and the new professional classes in the treaty ports. The great mass of the population had little confidence in the republican government and remained under the sway of traditional elites.
>
> — [062-088/061-150]

Yüan Shih-k'ai's death in 1916 removed the last figure capable of holding the center together, and the country descended into nearly a decade of armed competition among rival military factions. Various warlord cliques—the Chihli, the Anhwei, and the Fengtien—contended for control of Peking and the revenues of the central government, while the provinces were governed as personal fiefdoms.

> The first period of the Republic, until 1927, was marked by the rule of the warlords, who treated the country as a collection of military satrapies and reduced the central government to a prize to be fought over.
>
> — [062-111]

## Nationalist China (1927–1949)

A new phase opened with the Northern Expedition of 1926–1928, in which the Kuomintang army under Chiang Kai-shek broke the power of the northern warlords and established a national government at Nanking.

> Chiang Kai-shek soon became the dominant figure in the Kuomintang, and his Northern Expedition succeeded in bringing much of eastern and central China under a single national government for the first time since 1916.
>
> — [063-104]

The Nanking government pursued an ambitious program of modernization—building roads, railways, and factories, expanding education, and drafting new legal codes—but its authority remained shallow. The regime depended heavily on the support of urban commercial interests and the rural gentry; it never achieved the deep penetration of village society that would be required to mobilize the country against the twin threats of Japanese aggression and Communist insurgency.

> The situation of Chiang Kai-shek's Chungking government during the war with Japan seemed hopeless, yet it survived through a combination of diplomatic skill, geographic remoteness, and the inability of the Japanese to administer the vast territories they had overrun.
>
> — [064-131]

The Second Sino-Japanese War (1937–1945) devastated the Nationalist government. Forced to retreat to the interior city of Chungking in Szechwan, the KMT regime lost control of the wealthy coastal provinces and suffered enormous military and civilian casualties. Although China emerged on the victorious side in 1945, the Nationalist government was exhausted, its administration riddled with corruption, and its armies demoralized.

> After the Second World War Chiang Kai-shek had to face the Communists in a civil war that would determine the future of China.
>
> — [062-170]

## The Civil War and Retreat

The resumption of civil war between the Nationalists and the Chinese Communist Party in 1946 ended in disaster for the KMT. Despite American material support, Nationalist forces suffered a series of catastrophic defeats in Manchuria and North China. In 1949, the Communists seized Peking and proclaimed the People's Republic of China.

> Chiang Kai-shek and his government fled to Chungking and then to Taiwan, where the Republic of China continued to exist as a separate state under Nationalist rule.
>
> — [064-081]

The Republic of China continued to exist on Taiwan, where it maintained its claim to be the legitimate government of all China under the framework of the 1947 constitution. On Taiwan, the KMT regime presided over land reform, economic development, and eventually a transition to multi-party democracy.

## Sun Yat-sen's Ideological Legacy

Sun Yat-sen articulated the ideological foundations of the Republic in his Three Principles of the People: nationalism, democracy, and the people's livelihood. These principles, blending Western political ideas with Chinese conditions, provided the Kuomintang with its doctrinal core and continued to be invoked by both the Nationalist government on Taiwan and—selectively—by the Communist regime on the mainland.

> Sun Yat-sen, the "Father of the Republic," accordingly laid down three principles that were to guide the new China: national independence from foreign domination, democratic constitutional government, and a program of economic reconstruction that he called the "people's livelihood."
>
> — [061-126/127]

The tension between Sun's democratic ideals and the reality of one-party rule under the KMT—and later under the Communists—reflected the deeper challenge of republican governance in a society that had known only imperial rule for two millennia.

## Legacy

The Republican period transformed the terms of Chinese political life. The vocabulary of citizenship, rights, constitutions, and popular sovereignty, introduced during these decades, permanently altered the language in which Chinese people debated their collective future. The Republic's institutional experiments—parliamentary assemblies, political parties, a written constitution—provided models that, however imperfectly realized, shaped the political imagination of subsequent generations.

The Republic also demonstrated, through its failures, the formidable obstacles to nation-building in twentieth-century China: the weakness of the central state after the collapse of the monarchy, the centrifugal pull of regionalism, the predatory presence of foreign powers, and the difficulty of reconciling modern forms of government with a society still organized around kinship and locality.
