## Overview

**Shao Yung** (1011–1077) was a philosopher and poet of the Northern [[Sung Dynasty|Sung]] period, recognised as one of the principal figures of [[confucianism|Neo-Confucianism]]. Along with [[Ssu-ma Kuang]], he led the so-called Loyang School, a circle of scholars centred on the secondary capital that stood midway between the radical reformism of [[Wang An-shih]] and the conservative opposition.

## The Loyang School

During the eleventh century, Sung intellectual life was divided among competing schools. The Loyang School occupied a centrist position:

> "Midway between the two stood the so-called Loyang-School, whose greatest leaders were the historian and poet Ssu-ma Kuang (1019–1086) and the philosopher-poet Shao Yung (1011–1077)." [044-068] [044-069] [044-070] [044-071]

This school focused on philosophical inquiry, historical compilation, and literary production rather than direct factional engagement.

## Philosophical Contributions

Shao Yung's work explored the relationship between cosmic patterns (li) and the material world (ch'i). He developed a numerological and diagrammatic approach to cosmology that influenced later thinkers including [[Chu Hsi]].

He was also noted for his contributions to Sung literary culture, particularly the informal genre of _pi-chi_ (miscellaneous notes):

> "In addition to its poems, the Sung literature was famous for the so-called _pi-chi_ or miscellaneous notes." [044-072] [044-073]

## Legacy

Shao Yung's integration of Taoist cosmological concepts with Confucian ethics helped shape the mature Neo-Confucian synthesis. His residence at Loyang, known as the "Nest of Peace" (An-lo wo), became a symbol of the cultivated withdrawal from factional politics in favour of scholarly contemplation — an ideal that resonated through later Chinese [[gentry|literati]] culture.
