## Overview

Sun Yat-sen (Chinese: 孫中山, 1866–1925) was the central figure in the Chinese republican revolution that overthrew the [[ching-dynasty|Ch'ing dynasty]] in 1911–1912, ending over two thousand years of imperial rule. Revered as the "Father of the Chinese Republic" (Kuo-fu), Sun's political philosophy — encapsulated in his _Three Principles of the People_ (San-min Chu-i: nationalism, democracy, and people's livelihood) — provided the ideological foundation for the Kuomintang (Nationalist Party) and shaped Chinese political discourse throughout the twentieth century. Eberhard notes that Sun "turned the secret society tradition into a political instrument" by transforming the Triad network into a modern revolutionary organization [060-136].

Unlike most Chinese revolutionaries of his generation, Sun spent much of his early life abroad. Born to a peasant family in Kwangtung province near Canton, he received his education in Hawaii and Hong Kong, where he trained as a medical doctor. This overseas experience shaped his political vision, which combined elements of Western democratic thought with Chinese nationalist aspirations [060-112].

Sun's career was marked by repeated failures and exiles, yet he retained an extraordinary ability to inspire loyalty and attract support. His political doctrines continue to be claimed — in different and often contradictory interpretations — by both the Kuomintang in Taiwan and the Chinese Communist Party on the mainland.

## Life and Background

> "Sun Yat-sen (1866-1925), who had been living abroad, turned the secret society tradition into a political instrument." [060-112]

Sun Yat-sen was born in 1866 in the village of Ts'ui-heng in Kwangtung province, the son of a peasant family. At age thirteen, he joined his elder brother in Hawaii, where he received a Western-style education at a missionary school. He later studied medicine in Hong Kong, qualifying as a doctor in 1892. However, his political interests soon overtook his medical career.

Deeply troubled by China's weakness in the face of foreign encroachment and by the corruption of the Ch'ing regime, Sun began organizing revolutionary cells among overseas Chinese communities in the 1890s. His first serious uprising, in Canton in 1895, failed, forcing him into exile. He spent the following sixteen years traveling among Chinese diaspora communities in Japan, Southeast Asia, Europe, and the Americas, raising funds and building a revolutionary network [060-112].

Eberhard draws attention to Sun's innovative use of the Triad secret society tradition — transforming clandestine organizations that had long existed on the margins of Chinese society into disciplined revolutionary cells [060-136]. This organizational innovation proved crucial: when the Wuchang Uprising of October 1911 unexpectedly succeeded, Sun's network provided the political infrastructure for the new republic.

## Political Vision: The Three Principles

Sun Yat-sen's political thought was formally articulated in the _Three Principles of the People_, which he developed over several decades and published in definitive form in 1924:

- **Nationalism** (Min-tsu): China must free itself from foreign domination and unequal treaties, while also integrating its diverse ethnic groups — Han, Manchu, Mongol, Tibetan, and Muslim — into a unified nation. Sun's nationalism was anti-imperialist but not xenophobic; he envisioned a China strong enough to stand as an equal among the world's powers.
- **Democracy** (Min-ch'üan): China needed a constitutional government based on popular sovereignty. However, Sun believed that China was not yet ready for full democracy. He proposed a period of "political tutelage" under KMT one-party rule, during which the people would be gradually trained in self-government — a concept that later proved convenient for authoritarian successors.
- **People's Livelihood** (Min-shêng): Often equated by Sun with socialism, this principle addressed economic inequality. Its centerpiece was the "equalization of land rights" — a Georgist-inspired policy that would tax increases in land value resulting from social development rather than individual effort. Later, under the influence of Soviet advisors, Sun added more radical agrarian elements to this principle.

> "After 1920, Sun Yat-sen, too, became interested in the Russian Revolution and the Soviet model of party organization." [064-178]

In his final years, Sun reorganized the Kuomintang along Leninist lines — creating a disciplined party structure with a centralized leadership that could function as a "revolutionary vanguard." This reorganization, carried out with Soviet advisors' help, also opened the party to Communist members [064-178]. Sun saw no contradiction between his Three Principles and the admission of Communists; the alliance was pragmatic, designed to secure Soviet support and to harness the organizational energy of the Communist movement for the nationalist cause.

## Revolutionary Leadership

> "At the end of 1924 Sun Yat-sen with some of his followers went north to Peking to negotiate with the northern militarists for the peaceful reunification of China. But the death of Sun Yat-sen intervened." [063-092]

Sun Yat-sen's life as a revolutionary was marked more by failure than success. Despite organizing numerous uprisings, none succeeded while he was personally directing them. When the Wuchang Uprising unexpectedly succeeded on October 10, 1911, Sun was in the United States, reading about the events in a newspaper. He hurried back to China, where he was elected provisional president of the new republic on December 29, 1911 [061-021].

His presidency lasted barely six weeks. To avoid a civil war with the powerful northern general [[yuan-shih-kai|Yüan Shih-k'ai]] — who commanded the loyalty of the modernized Peiyang Army — Sun resigned the presidency in February 1912, allowing Yüan to become president in exchange for his nominal acceptance of the republic [061-032]. Yüan soon proved to have imperial ambitions of his own, attempting to proclaim himself emperor in 1915–1916.

The years that followed saw China fragment into warlord domains, with Sun maintaining a precarious base in Canton. He served as "generalissimo" of a southern military government but struggled to assert real authority over the provincial militarists whose support he depended on [063-078]. It was only in 1924, the year before his death, that Sun finally succeeded in establishing a secure revolutionary base in Canton with a party army trained at the Whampoa Military Academy, where [[chiang-kai-shek|Chiang Kai-shek]] served as commandant [063-104].

## Influence and Legacy

Sun Yat-sen's posthumous influence vastly exceeded his actual political achievements during his lifetime. After his death in [[peking|Peking]] in March 1925, Sun was elevated to the status of a secular saint by the Kuomintang. His _Three Principles_ became the party's official doctrine, and his portrait was enshrined in public buildings across Nationalist China.

> "Sun Yat-sen, too, became interested in the Russian model after 1920. Russia found it useful to help Sun Yat-sen and advised the Communist Party to cooperate with the Kuomintang." [064-182]

Sun's decision to admit Communists into the KMT and to accept Soviet assistance would prove fateful [064-182]. His successor [[chiang-kai-shek|Chiang Kai-shek]] would later violently purge the Communists from the party, and the struggle between the KMT and the Communists would define Chinese politics for decades. Yet both sides claimed Sun's legacy: the Nationalists as his organizational heirs, and the Communists as the true fulfillment of his revolutionary principles.

Historians have produced numerous studies of Sun's life and thought. Paul Linebarger's _The Political Doctrines of Sun Yat-sen_ (Baltimore, 1937) and his _Sun Yat-sen and the Chinese Republic_ (1925) are among the foundational English-language treatments [072-187]. Sun's political doctrines continue to be studied and debated, their ambiguity — Sun was more a visionary than a systematic thinker — allowing each generation to reinterpret them for its own purposes.
