## Overview

**T'ai Tsung** (born Li Shih-min; r. 627–649) was the second emperor of the [[tang-dynasty|T'ang dynasty]] and one of the most celebrated rulers in Chinese history. Though not the dynasty's formal founder, he was the driving military and political force behind its establishment. His reign is widely regarded as marking the zenith of T'ang power, expanding Chinese influence deep into Central Asia while consolidating domestic institutions that would prove durable for centuries.

## Biography

### Early Military Exploits

Li Shih-min first distinguished himself during the Turkish siege of the Sui capital, where his tactical cunning saved the day:

> The young Chinese commander, Li Shih-min, succeeded in giving the Turks the impression that large reinforcements were on the way; a Chinese princess who was with the Turks spread the rumour that the Turks were to be attacked by another army. [034-157]

His ability to work with the steppe peoples stemmed partly from his family background, which was itself likely of partially Turkic origin:

> It is doubtful whether that family was purely Chinese, and in any case Li Shih-min's descent from it is a matter of conjecture. It is possible that his family was a sinified Toba family. [035-023] [035-024]

### The Founding of the T'ang

Li Shih-min's father, [[li-yuan|Li Yüan]], was the nominal leader of the rebellion against the Sui, but the real driving force was Li Shih-min himself:

> The nominal leadership in the rising that now began lay in the hands of Li Shih-min's father, Li Yüan; in practice Li Shih-min saw to everything. [035-031]

His alliance with Turkish forces was critical to the T'ang's initial military success. By the end of 617, Turkish auxiliaries had helped him reach the walls of Ch'ang-an.

### The Coup and Accession

Despite his pivotal role, Li Shih-min was not initially named heir. This created fatal tensions within the imperial family:

> The active spirit at the beginning of the T'ang rule had not been the emperor but his son Li Shih-min, who was not, however, named as heir to the throne. The result of this was tension between Li Shih-min and his father and brothers. [036-068] [036-070]

When his brothers learned he was claiming the succession, they conspired against him. The conflict culminated in a violent coup in 626:

> There came an armed collision between the brothers, in which Li Shih-min was the victor. The brothers and their families were exterminated, the father compelled to abdicate, and Li Shih-min became emperor, assuming the name T'ai Tsung (627–649). [036-075] [036-077]

### The Zenith of T'ang Power

T'ai Tsung's reign represented the apex of Chinese power in the pre-modern era:

> His reign marked the zenith of the power of China and of the T'ang dynasty. [036-078]

Under his rule, Chinese forces penetrated deep into Turkestan, establishing protectorates that secured the Silk Road trade routes. Caravans streamed into China from western and central Asia, and the capital Ch'ang-an became one of the world's great cosmopolitan centres.

### The Korean Wars and Later Years

The final years of T'ai Tsung's reign were consumed by a massive military campaign against Korea, continuing the expansionist programme begun under the Sui:

> The last years of T'ai Tsung's reign were filled with a great war against Korea, which represented a continuation of the plans of the Sui dynasty. This time Korea came firmly into Chinese possession. [036-094] [036-096]

The Korean campaign would continue after his death in 649, under his son [[kao-tsung|Kao Tsung]]:

> T'ai Tsung's son, Kao Tsung (650–683), merely carried to a conclusion what T'ai Tsung had begun. [036-104]

### Posthumous Influence

T'ai Tsung's reign became the benchmark against which all later emperors were measured. His concubine Wu — who would later become the notorious [[empress-wu|Empress Wu]] — began her ascent in his court:

> She had been a concubine of T'ai Tsung, and after his death had become a Buddhist nun — a frequent custom of the time — until Kao Tsung fell in love with her and made her a concubine of his own. [036-136]
