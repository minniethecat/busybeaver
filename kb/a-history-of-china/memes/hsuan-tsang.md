## Overview

**Hsüan-tsang** was a Chinese Buddhist monk of the early [[tang-dynasty|T'ang dynasty]] whose seventeen-year pilgrimage to India (c. 629–645) and subsequent translation of Buddhist scriptures made him one of the most influential figures in the transmission of [[buddhism|Buddhism]] to East Asia. His journey along the Silk Road and his detailed accounts of the lands he visited provided invaluable geographical and cultural knowledge, while his translations helped establish the mature Chinese Buddhist canon.

## Biography

### Journey to India

Hsüan-tsang travelled along the caravan routes that connected T'ang China with Central Asia and India, following in the footsteps of generations of Buddhist pilgrims:

> The cultural influence of Buddhism found expression in new and improved translations of countless texts, and in the passage of pilgrims along the caravan routes, helped by the merchants, as far as western Asia and India, like the famous Hsüan-tsang. [037-016] [037-018]

The merchant networks of the Silk Road provided the infrastructure that made such long-distance pilgrimage possible. [[buddhism|Buddhist monks]] had been travelling between India and China for centuries, accompanying traders and establishing monastic communities along the way:

> With the traders came Buddhist monks; trade and Buddhism thus always went together. [025-097]

### Translation Work

Upon returning to China, Hsüan-tsang devoted the remainder of his life to translating the Sanskrit texts he had collected. The T'ang capital Ch'ang-an had become a major centre of Buddhist scholarship, and translations were being produced not only into Chinese but into numerous other Asian languages:

> Translations were made not only from Indian or other languages into Chinese, but also, for instance, from Chinese into the Uighur and other Turkish tongues, and into Tibetan, Korean, and Japanese. [037-019] [037-020]

### Legacy

Hsüan-tsang's work was part of a broader wave of Buddhist transmission that saw the establishment of Buddhism as a permanent element of Chinese civilisation. The translation movement he helped lead was so extensive that by the T'ang period, Buddhist monks had become influential figures at court:

> The emperor appointed a Buddhist monk as head of the Buddhist state church, making him a kind of Buddhist pope. [029-178]

His journey to India and his detailed records — the _[[tang-dynasty|Great T'ang]] Records on the Western Regions_ — remained for centuries the most important Chinese source of knowledge about Central Asia and India.
