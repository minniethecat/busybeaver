## Overview

[[chang-hsueh-liang|Chang Hsüeh-liang]] (1901–2001) was a Chinese warlord and military commander who inherited control of Manchuria from his father, the powerful marshal Chang Tso-lin.

Known as the "Young Marshal," his most consequential act was the Sian Incident of 1936, in which he detained [[chiang-kai-shek|Chiang Kai-shek]] to compel the Nationalist leader to form a united front with the Communists against Japanese aggression.

## Inheritance of Manchuria

Chang Hsüeh-liang inherited the governance of Manchuria after his father was assassinated by Japanese agents in 1928.

He initially aligned with [[chiang-kai-shek|Chiang Kai-shek]]'s Nationalist government, accepting nominal subordination to Nanking in exchange for autonomy in the northeast.

> And its governor, Chang Hsüeh-liang, had tried to remain independent [064-019]

## The Sian Incident

The defining event of Chang's career occurred in December 1936. With Japan occupying Manchuria and pressing into northern China, Chang grew frustrated with [[chiang-kai-shek|Chiang Kai-shek]]'s policy of prioritizing the campaign against the Communists over resistance to Japan.

During a visit by Chiang to Sian (modern Xi'an), Chang and his co-conspirator General Yang [[hsiung-nu|Hu]]-ch'eng placed the Generalissimo under house arrest.

With mediation from [[chou-en-lai|Chou En-lai]], the crisis was resolved when Chiang agreed to suspend the anti-Communist campaign in favor of a united Chinese front against Japan.

## Aftermath and Captivity

Following the Sian Incident, Chang voluntarily accompanied [[chiang-kai-shek|Chiang Kai-shek]] back to Nanking to demonstrate his good faith. He was immediately placed under house arrest.

Chang remained in captivity — first in mainland China, then in Taiwan — for over fifty years, from 1936 until Chiang Ching-kuo's death in 1988.

He spent his final years in Hawaii, where he died in 2001 at the age of 100.

## Historical Assessment

Chang Hsüeh-liang remains a controversial figure. His actions at Sian are credited by many historians with enabling the united Chinese resistance that ultimately contributed to Japan's defeat.

Critics note that his earlier failure to resist Japanese encroachment in Manchuria, and his personal weaknesses, diminished his effectiveness as a military commander.

Regardless of these judgments, his pivotal role in reshaping the course of modern Chinese history at the Sian Incident is undisputed.
