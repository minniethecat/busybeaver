## Overview

**Shang Yang** (died 338 BC), also known as Shang Tz[)u] or Lord Shang, was the most famous representative of the [[Legalism|Legalist]] school of Chinese philosophy and the principal architect of the state of [[Ch'in Dynasty|Ch'in]] during the [[Warring States period]]. His radical reforms transformed Ch'in from a relatively backward frontier state into a centralized, militarized power capable of unifying China.

Unlike the Confucian "scholars" who came from the educated class, Shang Yang and the Legalists came from old princely families that had lost their feudal possessions. They were members of the upper class with practical political experience, offering their expertise to ruling princes.

## Background and Rise

> "The supporters of this school came principally from old princely families that had lost their feudal possessions, and not from among the so-called scholars. They were people belonging to the upper class who possessed political experience and now offered their knowledge to other princes who still reigned." [012-074–012-078]

Shang Yang was born in the state of Wei but found his opportunity in Ch'in, a western state where the old feudal order was weakest and alien influences were strongest:

> "Shang Yang became the actual organizer of the state of Ch'in." [012-099–012-100]

## Reforms in Ch'in

Shang Yang's reforms in Ch'in, implemented from approximately 356 BC, were comprehensive and transformative:

- **Land reform**: Abolished the old well-field (ching-t'ien) system, allowing private ownership and sale of land
- **Military reorganization**: Introduced a merit-based rank system, rewarding soldiers for enemy kills rather than noble birth
- **Centralized administration**: Divided the state into counties (hsien) directly administered by the central government
- **Standardized law**: Codified laws applied uniformly to all, with severe punishments for violations
- **Agriculture promoted**: Encouraged farming and settlement while restricting commerce

These reforms broke the power of the hereditary aristocracy and created a direct relationship between the state and its subjects.

## The Book of Lord Shang

The "Shang Chün Shu" (Book of Lord Shang), a collection of Legalist writings attributed to Shang Yang and his followers, articulates the core Legalist principles:
- Law (fa) should be supreme, applied equally to all regardless of rank
- The state's strength derives from agriculture and war
- Traditional morality and ritual are obstacles to effective governance

## Legacy

Shang Yang's ideas were further developed by his successor [[Han Fei Tzu]] (died 233 BC), who synthesized Legalist thought into its most comprehensive form:

> "His ideas were further developed by Han Fei Tz[)u] (died 233 B.C.). The mentality which speaks out of his writings has closest similarity to the famous Indian Arthashastra which originated slightly earlier; both books exhibit a 'Machiavellian' spirit." [012-100–012-103]

The "Machiavellian" comparison highlights the pragmatic, amoral character of Legalist statecraft — a radical departure from the moralistic Confucian approach to governance.

Shang Yang's reforms in Ch'in laid the institutional foundation for the eventual unification of China under [[Shih Huang Ti]] in 221 BC. Although Shang Yang himself was executed by being torn apart by chariots after the death of his patron Duke Hsiao, his policies endured and shaped the Ch'in state that would conquer all of China.
