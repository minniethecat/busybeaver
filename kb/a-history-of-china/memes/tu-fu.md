## Overview

[[Tu Fu]] (712–770) was, together with [[li-po|Li T'ai-po]] ([[li-po|Li Po]], 701–762), one of the two greatest poets of the [[tang-dynasty|T'ang dynasty]] — an era that brought existing Chinese poetic forms to their highest perfection [037-072]. His work, marked by profound emotional depth and technical mastery, has earned him an enduring place in the Chinese literary canon.

## Biography

Tu Fu lived during the height and subsequent decline of the T'ang dynasty. He was a younger contemporary of [[li-po|Li T'ai-po]], and the two are perennially paired in Chinese literary history as the twin pinnacles of classical verse [037-069]. While [[li-po|Li Po]] was celebrated for his romantic, free-spirited verse, Tu Fu's poetry was characterised by moral seriousness, historical consciousness, and a deep empathy for the suffering of ordinary people during the upheavals of his time — particularly the devastation wrought by the [[an-lu-shan-rebellion|An Lu-shan Rebellion]] (755–763).

> Widespread fame came to Li T'ai-po (701-762) and Tu Fu (712-770); in China two poets almost equal to these two in popularity were Po Chü-i (772-846) and Yüan Chen (779-831), who in their works kept as close as possible to the vernacular. [037-068–037-071]

## Literary Achievement

Tu Fu's poetry exemplified the T'ang achievement of perfecting inherited forms. The T'ang poets adopted the rules of form laid down by the poetic art of the south in the fifth century, but whereas earlier poetry had been a learned pastime — precious and formalistic — Tu Fu and his contemporaries brought genuine feeling to the craft [037-064]. New forms of poetry rarely appeared during the T'ang period, but existing forms were brought to their highest perfection [037-072].

Alongside Tu Fu and [[li-po|Li Po]], the poets [[po-chu-i|Po Chü-i]] (772–846) and Yüan Chen (779–831) achieved widespread popularity by writing as close to the vernacular as possible — a different but complementary approach to the classical tradition that Tu Fu represented [037-070].

## Legacy

Tu Fu's influence on Chinese poetry has been profound and lasting. His works, collected in the standard canon of T'ang literature, have been studied, memorised, and imitated by generations of Chinese scholars and poets. His integration of personal experience with historical awareness — what later critics called the "poet-historian" quality — established a model of the engaged literary intellectual that resonated throughout East Asian literary culture.
