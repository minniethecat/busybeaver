## Overview

[[liu-pei|Liu Pei]] was a prominent warlord during the collapse of the [[han-dynasty|Later Han dynasty]] and the founder of the Shu Han kingdom.

A distant member of the Han imperial clan, he claimed legitimacy as the rightful successor to the Han throne. His struggle against [[tsao-tsao|Ts'ao Ts'ao]] and other rivals defined the opening chapter of the [[three-kingdoms|Three Kingdoms]] era.

> First Ts'ao Ts'ao, Lü Pu, Yüan Shao, and Sun Ts'ê; later came Liu Pei. [020-080]

## Rise to Power

The collapse of centralized Han authority after 180 CE created opportunities for ambitious regional commanders.

Liu Pei was among the contenders who emerged from the chaos:

> All were striving to gain control of the government, and all were engaged in mutual hostilities from about 180 onwards. [020-081]

Each warlord sought to control the person of the last Han emperor, Hsien Ti, whose abdication was required to legitimize a new dynasty according to the precedent established by [[wang-mang|Wang Mang]].

## The Kingdom of Shu Han

By approximately 200 CE, the struggle had consolidated around three major powers:

> In the west, in the province of Szechwan, Liu Pei had established himself, and in the south-east Sun Ts'ê's brother. [020-097]

Liu Pei's base in the western province of Szechwan provided natural defenses.

The fertile basin supported a strong agricultural economy, enabling his Shu Han kingdom to sustain prolonged military campaigns.

## Military Campaigns and Alliances

Liu Pei's career was marked by shifting alliances and military campaigns against both [[tsao-tsao|Ts'ao Ts'ao]] in the north and the Sun family in the south-east.

> Liu Pei, general and ruler [073-620]

His alliance with Sun Ch'üan proved decisive at the Battle of the Red Cliff, where their combined forces halted [[tsao-tsao|Ts'ao Ts'ao]]'s southern advance.

However, the alliance broke down after Liu Pei seized territories that Sun Ch'üan considered within his sphere of influence.

## Historical Assessment

Liu Pei's claim to Han legitimacy distinguished him from his rivals. While [[tsao-tsao|Ts'ao Ts'ao]] controlled the north through military power and the Wei kingdom's formal succession, Liu Pei positioned himself as the defender of Han orthodoxy.

His portrayal in the later literary tradition, particularly the novel _Romance of the Three Kingdoms_, transformed him into a paragon of Confucian virtue — loyal, benevolent, and righteous. The historical record is more complex, showing a pragmatic commander capable of both generosity and ruthlessness.
