# Section 37

[037-001] In this epoch Buddhism helped to create the first beginnings of

[037-002] large-scale capitalism. In connection with the growing foreign trade,

[037-003] the monasteries grew in importance as repositories of capital; the

[037-004] temples bought more and more land, became more and more wealthy, and so

[037-005] gained increasing influence over economic affairs. They accumulated

[037-006] large quantities of metal, which they stored in the form of bronze

[037-007] figures of Buddha, and with these stocks they exercised controlling

[037-008] influence over the money market. There is a constant succession of

[037-009] records of the total weight of the bronze figures, as an indication of

[037-010] the money value they represented. It is interesting to observe that

[037-011] temples and monasteries acquired also shops and had rental income from

[037-012] them. They further operated many mills, as did the owners of private

[037-013] estates (now called "_chuang_") and thus controlled the price of flour,

[037-014] and polished rice.

[037-015] The cultural influence of Buddhism found expression in new and improved

[037-016] translations of countless texts, and in the passage of pilgrims along

[037-017] the caravan routes, helped by the merchants, as far as western Asia and

[037-018] India, like the famous Hsüan-tsang. Translations were made not only from

[037-019] Indian or other languages into Chinese, but also, for instance, from

[037-020] Chinese into the Uighur and other Turkish tongues, and into Tibetan,

[037-021] Korean, and Japanese.

[037-022] The attitude of the Turks can only be understood when we realize that

[037-023] the background of events during the time of empress Wu was formed by the

[037-024] activities of groups of the eastern Chinese gentry. The northern Turks,

[037-025] who since 630 had been under Chinese overlordship, had fought many wars

[037-026] of liberation against the Chinese; and through the conquest of

[037-027] neighbouring Turks they had gradually become once more, in the

[037-028] decade-and-a-half after the death of Kao Tsung, a great Turkish realm.

[037-029] In 698 the Turkish khan, at the height of his power, demanded a Chinese

[037-030] prince for his daughter--not, as had been usual in the past, a princess

[037-031] for his son. His intention, no doubt, was to conquer China with the

[037-032] prince's aid, to remove the empress Wu, and to restore the T'ang

[037-033] dynasty--but under Turkish overlordship! Thus, when the empress Wu sent

[037-034] a member of her own family, the khan rejected him and demanded the

[037-035] restoration of the deposed T'ang emperor. To enforce this demand, he

[037-036] embarked on a great campaign against China. In this the Turks must have

[037-037] been able to rely on the support of a strong group inside China, for

[037-038] before the Turkish attack became dangerous the empress Wu recalled the

[037-039] deposed emperor, at first as "heir to the throne"; thus she yielded to

[037-040] the khan's principal demand.

[037-041] In spite of this, the Turkish attacks did not cease. After a series of

[037-042] imbroglios within the country in which a group under the leadership of

[037-043] the powerful Ts'ui gentry family had liquidated the supporters of the

[037-044] empress Wu shortly before her death, a T'ang prince finally succeeded in

[037-045] killing empress Wei and her clique. At first, his father ascended the

[037-046] throne, but was soon persuaded to abdicate in favour of his son, now

[037-047] called emperor Hsüang Tsung (713-755), just as the first ruler of the

[037-048] T'ang dynasty had done. The practice of abdicating--in contradiction

[037-049] with the Chinese concept of the ruler as son of Heaven and the duties of

[037-050] a son towards his father--seems to have impressed Japan where similar

[037-051] steps later became quite common. With Hsüan Tsung there began now a

[037-052] period of forty-five years, which the Chinese describe as the second

[037-053] blossoming of T'ang culture, a period that became famous especially for

[037-054] its painting and literature.

[037-055] 5 _Second blossoming of T'ang culture_

[037-056] The T'ang literature shows the co-operation of many favourable factors.

[037-057] The ancient Chinese classical style of official reports and decrees

[037-058] which the Toba had already revived, now led to the clear prose style of

[037-059] the essayists, of whom Han Yü (768-825) and Liu Tsung-yüan (747-796)

[037-060] call for special mention. But entirely new forms of sentences make their

[037-061] appearance in prose writing, with new pictures and similes brought from

[037-062] India through the medium of the Buddhist translations. Poetry was also

[037-063] enriched by the simple songs that spread in the north under Turkish

[037-064] influence, and by southern influences. The great poets of the T'ang

[037-065] period adopted the rules of form laid down by the poetic art of the

[037-066] south in the fifth century; but while at that time the writing of poetry

[037-067] was a learned pastime, precious and formalistic, the T'ang poets brought

[037-068] to it genuine feeling. Widespread fame came to Li T'ai-po (701-762) and

[037-069] Tu Fu (712-770); in China two poets almost equal to these two in

[037-070] popularity were Po Chü-i (772-846) and Yüan Chen (779-831), who in their

[037-071] works kept as close as possible to the vernacular.

[037-072] New forms of poetry rarely made their appearance in the T'ang period,

[037-073] but the existing forms were brought to the highest perfection. Not until

[037-074] the very end of the T'ang period did there appear the form of a "free"

[037-075] versification, with lines of no fixed length. This form came from the

[037-076] indigenous folk-songs of south-western China, and was spread through the

[037-077] agency of the _filles de joie_ in the tea-houses. Before long it became

[037-078] the custom to string such songs together in a continuous series--the

[037-079] first step towards opera. For these song sequences were sung by way of

[037-080] accompaniment to the theatrical productions. The Chinese theatre had

[037-081] developed from two sources--from religious games, bullfights and

[037-082] wrestling, among Turkish and Mongol peoples, which developed into

[037-083] dancing displays; and from sacrificial games of South Chinese origin.

[037-084] Thus the Chinese theatre, with its union with music, should rather be

[037-085] called opera, although it offers a sort of pantomimic show. What

[037-086] amounted to a court conservatoire trained actors and musicians as early

[037-087] as in the T'ang period for this court opera. These actors and musicians

[037-088] were selected from the best-looking "commoners", but they soon tended to

[037-089] become a special caste with a legal status just below that of

[037-090] "burghers".

[037-091] In plastic art there are fine sculptures in stone and bronze, and we

[037-092] have also technically excellent fabrics, the finest of lacquer, and

[037-093] remains of artistic buildings; but the principal achievement of the

[037-094] T'ang period lies undoubtedly in the field of painting. As in poetry, in

[037-095] painting there are strong traces of alien influences; even before the

[037-096] T'ang period, the painter Hsieh Ho laid down the six fundamental laws of

[037-097] painting, in all probability drawn from Indian practice. Foreigners were

[037-098] continually brought into China as decorators of Buddhist temples, since

[037-099] the Chinese could not know at first how the new gods had to be

[037-100] presented. The Chinese regarded these painters as craftsmen, but admired

[037-101] their skill and their technique and learned from them.

[037-102] The most famous Chinese painter of the T'ang period is Wu Tao-tz[)u],

[037-103] who was also the painter most strongly influenced by Central Asian

[037-104] works. As a pious Buddhist he painted pictures for temples among others.

[037-105] Among the landscape painters, Wang Wei (721-759) ranks first; he was

[037-106] also a famous poet and aimed at uniting poem and painting into an

[037-107] integral whole. With him begins the great tradition of Chinese landscape

[037-108] painting, which attained its zenith later, in the Sung epoch.

[037-109] Porcelain had been invented in China long ago. There was as yet none of

[037-110] the white porcelain that is preferred today; the inside was a

[037-111] brownish-yellow; but on the whole it was already technically and

[037-112] artistically of a very high quality. Since porcelain was at first

[037-113] produced only for the requirements of the court and of high

[037-114] dignitaries--mostly in state factories--a few centuries later the T'ang

[037-115] porcelain had become a great rarity. But in the centuries that followed,

[037-116] porcelain became an important new article of Chinese export. The Chinese

[037-117] prisoners taken by the Arabs in the great battle of Samarkand (751), the

[037-118] first clash between the world of Islam and China, brought to the West

[037-119] the knowledge of Chinese culture, of several Chinese crafts, of the art

[037-120] of papermaking, and also of porcelain.

[037-121] The emperor Hsüan Tsung gave active encouragement to all things

[037-122] artistic. Poets and painters contributed to the elegance of his

[037-123] magnificent court ceremonial. As time went on he showed less and less

[037-124] interest in public affairs, and grew increasingly inclined to Taoism and

[037-125] mysticism in general--an outcome of the fact that the conduct of matters

[037-126] of state was gradually taken out of his hands. On the whole, however,

[037-127] Buddhism was pushed into the background in favour of Confucianism, as a

[037-128] reaction from the unusual privileges that had been accorded to the

[037-129] Buddhists in the past fifteen years under the empress Wu.

[037-130] 6 _Revolt of a military governor_

[037-131] At the beginning of Hsüan Tsung's reign the capital had been in the east

[037-132] at Loyang; then it was transferred once more to Ch'ang-an in the west

[037-133] due to pressure of the western gentry. The emperor soon came under the

[037-134] influence of the unscrupulous but capable and energetic Li Lin-fu, a

[037-135] distant relative of the ruler. Li was a virtual dictator at the court

[037-136] from 736 to 752, who had first advanced in power by helping the

[037-137] concubine Wu, a relative of the famous empress Wu, and by continually

[037-138] playing the eastern against the western gentry. After the death of the

[037-139] concubine Wu, he procured for the emperor a new concubine named Yang, of

[037-140] a western family. This woman, usually called "Concubine Yang" (Yang

[037-141] Kui-fei), became the heroine of countless stage-plays and stories and

[037-142] even films; all the misfortunes that marked the end of Hsüan Tsung's

[037-143] reign were attributed solely to her. This is incorrect, as she was but a

[037-144] link in the chain of influences that played upon the emperor. Naturally

[037-145] she found important official posts for her brothers and all her

[037-146] relatives; but more important than these was a military governor named

[037-147] An Lu-shan (703-757). His mother was a Turkish shamaness, his father, a

[037-148] foreigner probably of Sogdian origin. An Lu-shan succeeded in gaining

[037-149] favour with the Li clique, which hoped to make use of him for its own

[037-150] ends. Chinese sources describe him as a prodigy of evil, and it will be

[037-151] very difficult today to gain a true picture of his personality. In any

[037-152] case, he was certainly a very capable officer. His rise started from a

[037-153] victory over the Kitan in 744. He spent some time establishing relations

[037-154] with the court and then went back to resume operations against the

[037-155] Kitan. He made so much of the Kitan peril that he was permitted a larger

[037-156] army than usual, and he had command of 150,000 troops in the

[037-157] neighbourhood of Peking. Meanwhile Li Lin-fu died. He had sponsored An

[037-158] as a counterbalance against the western gentry. When now, within the

[037-159] clique of Li Lin-fu, the Yang family tried to seize power, they turned

[037-160] against An Lu-shan. But he marched against the capital, Ch'ang-an, with

[037-161] 200,000 men; on his way he conquered Loyang and made himself emperor

[037-162] (756: Yen dynasty). T'ang troops were sent against him under the

[037-163] leadership of the Chinese Kuo Tz[)u]-i, a Kitan commander, and a Turk,

[037-164] Ko-shu Han.

[037-165] The first two generals had considerable success, but Ko-shu Han, whose

[037-166] task was to prevent access to the western capital, was quickly defeated

[037-167] and taken prisoner. The emperor fled betimes, and An Lu-shan captured

[037-168] Ch'ang-an. The emperor now abdicated; his son, emperor Su Tsung

[037-169] (756-762), also fled, though not with him into Szechwan, but into

[037-170] north-western Shensi. There he defended himself against An Lu-shan and

[037-171] his capable general Shih Ss[)u]-ming (himself a Turk), and sought aid in

[037-172] Central Asia. A small Arab troop came from the caliph Abu-Jafar, and

[037-173] also small bands from Turkestan; of more importance was the arrival of

[037-174] Uighur cavalry in substantial strength. At the end of 757 there was a

[037-175] great battle in the neighbourhood of the capital, in which An Lu-shan

[037-176] was defeated by the Uighurs; shortly afterwards he was murdered by one

[037-177] of his eunuchs. His followers fled; Loyang was captured and looted by

[037-178] the Uighurs. The victors further received in payment from the T'ang

[037-179] government 10,000 rolls of silk with a promise of 20,000 rolls a year;

[037-180] the Uighur khan was given a daughter of the emperor as his wife. An

[037-181] Lu-shan's general, the Turk Shih Ss[)u]-ming, entered into An Lu-shan's

[037-182] heritage, and dominated so large a part of eastern China that the

[037-183] Chinese once more made use of the Uighurs to bring him down. The

[037-184] commanders in the fighting against Shih Ss[)u]-ming this time were once

[037-185] more Kuo Tz[)u]-i and the Kitan general, together with P'u-ku Huai-en, a

[037-186] member of a Tölös family that had long been living in China. At first

[037-187] Shih Ss[)u]-ming was victorious, and he won back Loyang, but then he was

[037-188] murdered by his own son, and only by taking advantage of the

[037-189] disturbances that now arose were the government troops able to quell the

[037-190] dangerous rising.
