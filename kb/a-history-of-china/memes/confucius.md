## Overview

Confucius (Chinese: K'ung Tzŭ), who lived approximately 551–479 BCE during the later [[chou-dynasty]] Spring and Autumn period, is China's best-known philosopher and the foundational figure of [[confucianism]]. His teachings on ethics, proper social conduct, and the ideal of the "chün-tzŭ" (gentleman or superior man) shaped Chinese political thought, education, and social organization for over two thousand years. Eberhard notes that Confucius's importance lies in his role as a systematizer and transmitter of older [[chou-dynasty|Chou]] cultural traditions rather than as an original religious prophet.

Confucius came from a family of the lower nobility in the state of Lu (in modern Shantung province). His father died early, and he grew up in modest circumstances. After holding minor offices, he spent years traveling from court to court seeking a ruler who would implement his reform program. He ultimately returned to Lu and devoted himself to teaching and editing the classical texts, including the _Spring and Autumn Annals_, which he is traditionally credited with compiling.

## Life and Background

> "Confucius (Chinese: K'ung Tzŭ) was a member of the lower nobility, the class that had to live from the knowledge of ceremonies and rites… His father died early; his mother seems to have been a concubine. This would explain why Confucius repeatedly lauded the introduction of a special right for the son of a wife proper." [008-095]

Confucius's own family background was modest. Eberhard describes how his family seems to have belonged to a class of lower social status who earned their living through knowledge of ceremonies and the conduct of rites [008-109]. This background helps explain his lifelong concern with proper ritual conduct (li) and the importance of social hierarchy based on moral worth rather than birth.

As a young man, Confucius held minor governmental positions in Lu. Later in life, he traveled widely, seeking a feudal prince who would put his political and ethical program into practice. None would, and he eventually abandoned his wanderings to concentrate on teaching [008-112]. His students, drawn from various social classes, became the transmitters of his ideas to later generations.

## Teachings and Philosophy

Confucius's teaching was fundamentally a social and ethical philosophy rather than a religion. Eberhard emphasizes that his doctrine centered on proper human relationships and the cultivation of moral character [008-169].

> "Confucius was fully conscious of his membership of a social group, the lower nobility, the class that had to live from the knowledge of ceremonies and rites… This social group had not yet previously produced a man of wider significance." [008-124]

The core of Confucius's teaching rested on several key concepts:

- **Jên** (humaneness or benevolence): The fundamental virtue — treating others with humanity and compassion.
- **Li** (ritual propriety): Proper conduct in social relationships, ceremonies, and daily life, rooted in the traditions of the early [[chou-dynasty|Chou]].
- **Chün-tzŭ** (the superior man): From Confucius onward, this term came to mean an ideal moral gentleman rather than merely a noble by birth [009-012]. The chün-tzŭ cultivates virtue through study and self-discipline.
- **Rectification of names** (chêng-ming): The doctrine that words and titles must correspond to reality — a ruler must rule, a father must act as a father.

Confucius made small but significant changes to the traditions he inherited. Eberhard notes that he was not a revolutionary but a reformer who worked through reinterpretation of existing cultural materials [008-184]. He systematized and transmitted the older Chou cultural heritage, giving it a moral depth it had previously lacked.

## Political Vision

> "Thus Confucius undoubtedly had ideas of reform. He tried to realize them. He was not successful. But after his death, his pupils slowly infiltrated the feudal courts and gained influence." [009-022]

Confucius's political philosophy rested on the idea of rule by moral example rather than by force. He believed that if the ruler cultivated virtue, the people would naturally follow. This was a radical departure from the hereditary feudal system of his time, where birth determined political power.

He criticized the inadequate feudal system and expressed ideas for reform [009-007]. However, the feudal princes of his time could not fulfill what Confucius required of them — they were unwilling to subordinate their personal interests to moral principles [008-173].

Despite his political failure in life, Confucius's ideas proved extraordinarily influential after his death. His pupils and their successors gradually gained positions of influence in various feudal courts, spreading his teachings throughout China [009-022].

## Influence and Legacy

Confucius's influence extended far beyond his own time and place. In eighteenth-century Europe, he was admired as the preeminent Chinese philosopher, and his ideas had some influence on Enlightenment thinkers, including possibly on the French Revolution [056-177].

> "The Spring and Autumn Annals, attributed to Confucius himself, were essentially books of law. They contained 'cases' and Confucius's decisions of those cases." [016-097]

His compilation and editing of classical texts — especially the _Spring and Autumn Annals_ — established a canonical tradition that shaped Chinese education and civil service examinations until the early twentieth century. The [[confucianism|Confucian]] classics became the core curriculum for the [[examination-system|imperial examination system]], which selected officials for the imperial bureaucracy.

Later philosophers including [[mencius|Mencius]] and [[chu-hsi|Chu Hsi]] further elaborated on Confucius's ideas. In the Sung dynasty, [[confucianism|Neo-Confucianism]] emerged as a major philosophical synthesis that dominated Chinese intellectual life until modern times [053-057]. Throughout Chinese history, reformers and revolutionaries alike had to grapple with Confucius's legacy — some attacking him as a symbol of the old order, others finding in his texts justification for change [062-011].
