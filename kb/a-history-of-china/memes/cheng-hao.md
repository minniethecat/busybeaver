## Overview

Ch'eng Hao (Chinese: 程顥, 1032–1085) was one of the founding figures of [[confucianism|Neo-Confucianism]] during the [[sung-dynasty|Sung dynasty]]. Together with his younger brother [[ch'eng-i|Ch'eng I]] (1033–1107), he sought to provide Confucian ethics with a metaphysical foundation capable of rivaling the sophisticated cosmologies of [[buddhism|Buddhism]] and [[taoism|Taoism]]. The two brothers, known collectively as "the two Ch'engs," were among the most important philosophers of the Northern Sung, and their ideas — especially their theory of Material Force (_ch'i_) and human nature — became central to the Neo-Confucian synthesis later systematized by [[chu-hsi|Chu Hsi]].

The Sung dynasty saw the great Neo-Confucian revival, in which "philosophers like [[chu-hsi|Chu Hsi]] and the brothers Ch'eng synthesized Confucian ethics with elements borrowed from Buddhist metaphysics and Taoist cosmology" [034-115]. Ch'eng Hao's contribution to this synthesis was foundational: he and his brother were the first to articulate a systematic explanation for human inequality within a Confucian framework that maintained the essential neutrality of human nature.

## The Problem of Human Nature

Ch'eng Hao's most significant philosophical contribution was his theory of human nature and its relationship to Material Force. The problem he addressed was a pressing one within the Confucian tradition. [[confucius|Confucius]] and his early followers had held that every person could perfect themselves by overcoming the evil within them, implying a fundamental equality of human potential [044-004–044-008]. As the gentry society consolidated during the Han period, however, this optimistic view appeared increasingly unrealistic, and the idea of different grades of human nature gained currency [044-009–044-013].

> "The Neo-Confucianists, especially Ch'eng Hao (1032-1085) and Ch'eng I (1033-1107), tried to find the reasons for this inequality. According to them, nature is neutral; but physical form originates with the combination of nature with Material Force (_ch'i_). This combination produces individuals in which there is a lack of balance or harmony." [044-017–044-021]

This formulation was a crucial innovation. By distinguishing between an originally neutral nature and the distorting influence of Material Force, Ch'eng Hao could simultaneously affirm the Confucian conviction that all human beings share a common moral endowment and acknowledge the observable reality of differing moral capacities. The task of self-cultivation, in this view, was to transform physical form and recover one's original nature [044-021–044-022].

## The Role of _Jen_ (Love)

For Ch'eng Hao, the force by which such transformation was possible was _jen_ — love or humaneness, conceived not merely as an emotion but as the creative, life-giving quality of nature itself [044-022–044-024]. Through the cultivation of _jen_, the individual could overcome the imbalances introduced by Material Force and return to a state of harmony with the cosmos.

This emphasis on _jen_ as a creative, transformative power distinguished Ch'eng Hao's thought from the more analytical approach of his brother [[cheng-i|Ch'eng I]]. Where [[cheng-i|Ch'eng I]] stressed the systematic investigation of principles (_li_), Ch'eng Hao placed greater weight on the intuitive experience of unity with all things — an orientation that later influenced the idealist wing of [[confucianism|Neo-Confucianism]] represented by [[wang-yang-ming|Wang Yang-ming]].

> "Man should try to transform physical form and recover original nature. The creative force by which such a transformation is possible is _jen_, love, the creative, life-giving quality of nature itself." [044-021–044-024]

## Political and Intellectual Context

Ch'eng Hao lived during one of the most intellectually vibrant and politically contentious periods in Chinese history. The Northern Sung court was polarized by the reform program of [[wang-an-shih|Wang An-shih]] (1021–1086), whose New Policies sought to restructure state finances, agriculture, and the [[examination-system|examination system]] through aggressive governmental intervention. Ch'eng Hao, like many Neo-Confucian thinkers, was aligned with the conservative opposition centred on the historian Ssu-ma Kuang, which viewed Wang's state-activism as a threat to the moral foundations of governance [044-054][044-067–044-068].

This political alignment reflected a deeper philosophical difference. While [[wang-an-shih|Wang An-shih]] believed that institutional reform was the key to social improvement, the Neo-Confucians maintained that moral self-cultivation — the transformation of the individual — was the indispensable prerequisite for any meaningful change. Ch'eng Hao's entire philosophical enterprise, with its focus on recovering original nature through _jen_, was thus embedded in a broader argument about how society should be ordered and governed.

His contemporaries in the Neo-Confucian movement included [[chou-tun-i|Chou Tun-i]] (1017–1073), who pioneered the cosmological diagram of the Supreme Ultimate (T'ai-chi), and [[chang-tsai|Chang Tsai]] (1020–1077), who developed an alternative theory of Material Force as the fundamental substance of the universe [044-001–044-002]. Together, these thinkers constituted the first generation of the Neo-Confucian revival, generating the concepts that [[chu-hsi|Chu Hsi]] would later synthesize into a comprehensive system.

## Legacy

Ch'eng Hao's influence was transmitted principally through the work of [[chu-hsi|Chu Hsi]] (1130–1200), who drew on the ideas of both Ch'eng brothers — along with those of [[chou-tun-i|Chou Tun-i]], [[chang-tsai|Chang Tsai]], and [[shao-yung|Shao Yung]] — to construct the philosophical edifice that would become Chinese state orthodoxy for over six centuries. [[chu-hsi|Chu Hsi]] adopted and refined the Ch'eng brothers' distinction between principle (_li_) and Material Force (_ch'i_), making it the conceptual backbone of his system.

The Ch'eng brothers are listed alongside [[chu-hsi|Chu Hsi]] and other major philosophers in the corpus index, indicating their recognized standing in the Chinese philosophical tradition [074-067]. Ch'eng Hao is separately indexed as "Ch'eng Hao, philosopher," confirming his status as a thinker of independent significance distinct from his brother [073-166].

The Neo-Confucian synthesis that Ch'eng Hao helped to found accepted, as early [[confucianism|Confucianism]] had, an inequality of men, while channeling the practical application of _jen_ through _li_ — the system of rules of behaviour grounded in a stratified class society [044-025–044-028]. This conservatism ensured that Ch'eng Hao's thought, as interpreted through [[chu-hsi|Chu Hsi]], would serve the interests of the gentry state for centuries. Yet the spiritual depth of his vision — his insistence on the transformative power of love and the possibility of recovering an original harmony with nature — continued to inspire philosophical reflection and remains a vital element of the Confucian heritage.
