## Overview

The Opium War (1839–1842), also known as the First Opium War, was China's first armed conflict with a Western power and a watershed moment that exposed the military and technological inferiority of the Qing empire. The war was triggered by the Chinese government's attempt to suppress the lucrative British opium trade, which had been draining silver from China and causing widespread social harm [074-032].

> Accordingly, from 1800 onwards opium became more and more the chief article of trade, especially for the English, who were able to bring it conveniently from India. Opium is harmful to the people; the opium trade resulted in certain groups of merchants being inordinately enriched; a great deal of Chinese money went abroad. [074-000]

In 1839, the Qing government appointed [[lin-tse-hsu|Lin Tsê-hsü]] as imperial commissioner to Canton, where he prohibited the opium trade and publicly burned chests of opium found in British possession. Britain viewed this as a threat to its Far Eastern trade and decided on military intervention — both to protect commerce and to compel China to open additional ports [074-000].

> In 1840 British ships-of-war appeared off the south-eastern coast of China and bombarded it. In 1841 the Chinese opened negotiations and dismissed Lin Tsê-hsü. As the Chinese concessions were regarded as inadequate, hostilities continued; the British entered the Yangtze estuary and threatened Nanking. [074-000]

China proved defenceless. The Qing possessed no modern navy, and European weapons were decisively superior. In 1842, China capitulated and signed the Treaty of Nanking — the first of the "unequal treaties." Under its terms, Hong Kong was ceded to Great Britain, a war indemnity was paid, five ports were opened to European trade, and the Canton merchants' monopoly was abolished. Despite the treaty, opium smuggling continued unabated [074-000].

The Opium War marked the beginning of a century of foreign encroachment on Chinese sovereignty. It was followed by the [[lorcha-war|Lorcha War]] (1856–1860) and successive conflicts that progressively weakened the Qing state [074-032].
