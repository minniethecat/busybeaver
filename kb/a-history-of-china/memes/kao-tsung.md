## Overview

Kao Tsung (r. 650–683) was the third emperor of the T'ang dynasty, succeeding his father [[tai-tsung|T'ai Tsung]]. His reign marked both the territorial apex and the beginning of internal decline for the early T'ang. While Kao Tsung completed the conquest of Korea that his father had begun, his weakness allowed his consort Empress Wu to accumulate unprecedented power, setting the stage for her eventual usurpation of the throne.

> T'ai Tsung's son, Kao Tsung (650-683), merely carried to a conclusion the successful foreign policy of his father. [036-104]

Contemporary scholarship views Kao Tsung as a competent but unexceptional ruler who benefited from the institutional momentum left by [[tai-tsung|T'ai Tsung]], yet whose personal limitations permitted the rise of a formidable empress who would fundamentally alter the T'ang political order.

## Reign and Foreign Policy

The early years of Kao Tsung's reign saw the continuation of [[tai-tsung|T'ai Tsung]]'s expansionist foreign policy. The most significant military achievement was the final conquest of the Korean kingdom of Koguryŏ, a campaign that had eluded both Sui and early T'ang armies. Under Kao Tsung, Chinese forces finally subdued the Korean peninsula, though the occupation proved short-lived.

> Kao Tsung's reign, 683, came the first of the wars of liberation of the Turkish peoples. [036-132]

The Turkish frontier, which had been pacified under [[tai-tsung|T'ai Tsung]], began to stir again during Kao Tsung's later years. The Western Turkish khaganate reasserted its independence, and within a decade and a half of Kao Tsung's death, a great Turkish realm had re-emerged along China's northern border.

> And with the end of Kao Tsung's reign began the decline of the T'ang empire. [036-134]

## Empress Wu and the Succession

Kao Tsung's historical legacy is inseparable from his consort, the Empress Wu. Originally a concubine of [[tai-tsung|T'ai Tsung]], Wu Chao entered Kao Tsung's palace and rapidly ascended from minor consort to empress. She demonstrated exceptional political acumen and progressively sidelined the emperor from active governance.

> After Kao Tsung's death in 683 she became regent for her son. [036-143]

Following Kao Tsung's death, Empress Wu became regent for her son Chung Tsung, whom she soon deposed in favor of another son, Jui Tsung. She would eventually proclaim herself emperor of a new Chou dynasty in 690, the only woman in Chinese history to rule as emperor in her own name.

> A decade-and-a-half after the death of Kao Tsung, a great Turkish realm had re-established itself. [037-028]

## Assessment

Historians have traditionally judged Kao Tsung harshly, viewing him as a weak ruler dominated by his empress. Modern scholarship offers a more nuanced assessment: Kao Tsung maintained the administrative systems inherited from [[tai-tsung|T'ai Tsung]] and presided over a period of continued prosperity. The T'ang economy expanded, the [[examination-system|examination system]] matured, and Chinese cultural influence reached new heights during his reign. However, his failure to check Empress Wu's ambition fundamentally altered the dynastic succession and paved the way for the only interruption in T'ang rule.
