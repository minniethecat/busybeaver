## Overview

[[wu-ti|Wu Ti]] (Emperor Wu of Han, r. 141–86 BC) presided over one of the most expansionist reigns in Chinese history. His fifty-four years on the throne transformed the [[han-dynasty|Han dynasty]] from a consolidation-minded state into an aggressively expansionist empire. Through sustained military campaigns against the [[hsiung-nu|Hsiung-nu]] confederation, diplomatic missions into Central Asia, and the conquest of southern territories, Wu Ti extended Chinese power far beyond the boundaries inherited from his predecessors.

## Military Expansion

Wu Ti's reign was defined by an unrelenting military posture against the steppe nomads:

> Hostilities against the Hsiung-nu continued incessantly, after the death of Wu Ti, under his successor, so that the Hsiung-nu were further weakened. [018-017]

The strategic logic was comprehensive — direct military pressure combined with diplomatic encirclement. The Chinese concluded alliances with the Wu-sun in the north of the Tarim basin, securing that vital region and threatening the [[hsiung-nu|Hsiung-nu]] from their rear. Chinese agents also employed "intrigue, espionage, and sabotage to promote disunity and disorder within the [[hsiung-nu|Hsiung-nu]]" [018-012].

In 108 BC, Chinese armies conquered the western part of Korea, which was already an important transit region for trade with Japan. This brought that commerce under direct Chinese government influence [018-001].

## Southern Campaigns and Trade

Wu Ti's expansion was driven substantially by commercial interests:

> Wu Ti's active policy had not been directed only against the Hsiung-nu. After heavy fighting he brought southern China, with the region round Canton, and the south-eastern coast, firmly under Chinese dominion — in this case again on account of trade interests. [018-022]

Foreign merchant colonies — often Sogdian traders — were already established in Canton and other coastal towns, dealing in Indian and Middle Eastern goods. The southern wars gave Wu Ti control of the revenues from this commerce [018-028]. He also attempted several times to advance through Yünnan to secure a better land route to India, though these attempts failed [018-030].

## Economic Strains and State Response

The cost of empire proved enormous:

> The wars carried on by Wu Ti and his successors had been expensive. The maintenance of large armies of occupation in the new regions, especially in Turkestan, also meant a permanent drain on the national funds. [018-060]

The armies required vast numbers of horses — the steppe peoples could only be fought with cavalry — and when the [[hsiung-nu|Hsiung-nu]] stopped supplying them as tribute and the campaigns failed to produce enough as booty, peasants were compelled to rear horses for the government [018-064].

Wu Ti was aware of the fiscal strains and responded with state intervention in the economy. Already under his reign, there were signs of developments that would culminate in state monopolies over salt, iron, and other key commodities — measures designed to fund the military without further burdening the peasantry [018-105].

## Succession Crisis

Despite his long and consequential reign, Wu Ti's final years were marred by political turmoil:

> In spite of his long rule, Wu Ti did not leave an adult heir, as the crown prince was executed, with many other persons, shortly before Wu Ti's death. The crown prince had been implicated in an alleged attempt by a large group of people to remove the emperor by various sorts of magic. [018-033]

A regency council had to be established for the young heir, effectively placing government in the hands of a general and his clique until the heir's death and into the beginning of his successor's reign [018-039].

## Legacy

The military expansion under Wu Ti permanently altered the geopolitical landscape of East Asia. The collapse of the [[hsiung-nu|Hsiung-nu]] empire — driven by "continual disastrous wars against the Chinese, in which not only many men but, especially, large quantities of cattle fell into Chinese hands" [018-044] — freed China from the steppe threat for a generation and allowed Chinese authority to penetrate deep into Central Asia. Wu Ti's reign established the precedent of an activist, interventionist state that would shape Chinese imperial ideology for centuries to come.
