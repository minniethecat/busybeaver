## Overview

The Chou dynasty (c. 1028–256 B.C.) was the longest-ruling dynasty in
Chinese history, spanning nearly eight centuries.

It was founded by [[wu-wang|Wu Wang]] through the conquest of the
[[shang-dynasty|Shang]] and marks the beginning of China's documented
history (006-068).

The dynasty divides into the Western Chou (c. 1028–771 B.C.), with
its capital near Sian, and the Eastern Chou (770–256 B.C.), with the
capital at Loyang.

The Eastern Chou encompasses the Spring and Autumn period and the
Warring States period, ending with unification under the [[qin-dynasty|
Ch'in]].

With the Chou, "everything which was later regarded as typically
'Chinese' began to emerge" (006-035). Its cultural legacy includes
[[confucius|Confucius]], [[lao-tzu|Lao Tzu]], and the Hundred Schools
of Thought.

## Founding and Rise

> The Chou ruler, named Wu Wang ("the martial king"), crossed his eastern
> frontier and pushed into central Honan. Wu Wang forced a passage across
> the Yellow River and annihilated the Shang army. Thus was the Chou
> dynasty founded, and with it we begin the actual history of China.
> [006-061]

The Chou originated as a small realm in western China during the Shang
period. Their ruling house was related to the Turkish group, and the
population consisted mainly of Turks and Tibetans (006-046).

Through intermarriage with the Shang, the Chou absorbed Shang culture
while building military strength. Their state grew as Shang power
diminished through disloyalty and eastern wars (006-058).

After the conquest, the Chou justified their rule through the Mandate
of Heaven (*t'ien-ming*). Heaven withdraws its mandate from corrupt
rulers and confers it upon the virtuous.

This concept became the cornerstone of Chinese political theory,
invoked by every subsequent dynasty to legitimize its rule.

## Government and Feudal System

> We find in the early Chou time the typical signs of true feudalism:
> fiefs were given in a ceremony in which symbolically a piece of earth
> was handed over to the new fiefholder, and his rights and obligations
> were inscribed in a "charter." [006-090]

The Chou established a feudal system to govern their vast empire.
As an alien minority, the conquerors spread across the country,
establishing garrison-settlements under their chieftains.

Most fiefholders were members of the Chou ruling family or the Chi
clan; others were heads of allied tribes (006-094). Peasants and serfs
were bound to fiefholders and attached to the land.

The capital at Sian was a twin city: one part for the Chou elite,
the other for the subjugated Shang. A second capital at Loyang served
as the administrative centre (007-011).

At the apex stood the emperor as the "Son of Heaven" (007-034).
The ruler himself offered the supreme sacrifices, fusing political
authority with family and religious ritual.

## Culture and Philosophy

The Chou presided over a cultural synthesis fusing steppe traditions
with Shang civilization. They abolished human sacrifice, systematized
ancestor worship, and formalized beliefs about the soul (006-154).

With the decline of central authority, "scholars" — literate ritual
specialists descended from the Shang priesthood — gained influence
at feudal courts. They became the carriers of Chinese literary culture.

The Eastern Chou produced the Hundred Schools of Thought.
[[confucius|Confucius]] (551–479 B.C.) systematized Chou ritual and
moral traditions into [[confucianism|Confucianism]], the dominant
tradition of imperial China.

Alongside Confucianism, [[taoism|Taoism]] — associated with [[lao-tzu|
Lao Tzu]] — emphasized harmony with the Tao. The Legalists shaped the
[[qin-dynasty|Ch'in dynasty]]. The Mohists and Yin-Yang school also
flourished.

## Economy and Society

The Chou economy rested on agriculture organized through the feudal
system. Iron technology, introduced during the Eastern Chou,
revolutionized both farming and warfare.

Trade expanded and metal coinage became widespread. Cities grew as
commercial centres, and a merchant class emerged, gradually complicating
the old social hierarchy of nobles, peasants, and slaves.

## Decline and Fall

The Chou central government weakened steadily during the Eastern Chou.
Feudal lords increasingly acted as independent rulers, paying little
attention to the distant court at Loyang (007-066).

In 771 B.C., an alliance of northern feudal states attacked and killed
the Chou ruler. The western territories were lost, and a Chou prince
was conducted eastward to Loyang (007-141).

The new ruler in Loyang was "foredoomed to impotence" (007-149). His
actual authority extended little beyond the city and its immediate
surroundings.

By the fourth century B.C., only seven major states remained. The
state of [[qin-dynasty|Ch'in]], reformed under [[shang-yang|Shang
Yang]], emerged as the dominant military power.

In 256 B.C., the last Chou emperor was compelled to abdicate in
favour of Ch'in. The dynasty that had ruled for nearly eight centuries
came to its end.

The Chou legacy outlasted the dynasty itself. The feudal model, the
patriarchal family, and the Son of Heaven concept shaped Chinese
civilization through every subsequent dynasty.

The philosophical traditions of the Hundred Schools — Confucianism,
Taoism, Legalism — remained foundational to Chinese thought for over
two thousand years.
