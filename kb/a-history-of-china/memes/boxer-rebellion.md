## Overview

The Boxer Rising of 1900 was a popular movement that erupted in the final years of the Ch'ing dynasty, aimed originally at the ruling gentry and the Manchu government but redirected by the [[peking|Peking]] court against the foreign powers then carving China into "spheres of interest." The rising provoked an international military expedition that captured [[peking|Peking]], forcing the dowager empress [[tzu-hsi|Tz'u Hsi]] and the emperor to flee the capital. It represented both the failure of conservative resistance to modernization and the desperate reaction of a population suffering under foreign encroachment.

The Boxer Rising came at the end of a turbulent decade. China had been defeated by Japan in 1895, the reform movement of [[kang-yu-wei|K'ang Yo-wei]] had been crushed in the [[hsiung-nu|Hu]]ndred Days' Reform of 1898, and the European powers were openly dividing Chinese territory among themselves.

## Background

The root cause of the rising was China's inability to modernize in the face of Western imperialism. While Japan had rapidly industrialized after the Meiji Restoration, "the ruling class in China had shown a marked repugnance to any modernization; and the centre of this conservatism was the dowager empress Tz'u Hsi" [060-005]. The empress was a woman of strong personality but lacked the education to grasp that "modernization was an absolute necessity for China if it was to remain an independent state" [060-008].

> "She felt instinctively that Europeanization would wreck the foundations of the power of the Manchus and the gentry, and would bring another class, the middle class and the merchants, into power." [060-013]

The defeat in the Sino-Japanese War of 1894–1895 shattered Chinese complacency. Japan, long despised as a barbarian state, had triumphed. The Europeans, meanwhile, "were all cutting bits out of China for themselves, extracting from the government one privilege after another, and quite openly dividing China into 'spheres of interest', obviously as the prelude to annexation of the whole country" [060-028].

A critical difference between China and Japan lay in their social structures. As the corpus explains:

> "The basis of the modern capitalist states of the West is the middle class. Japan had for centuries had a middle class (the merchants) that had entered into a symbiosis with the feudal lords. For the middle class the transition to modern capitalism, and for the feudal lords the way to Western imperialism, was easy. In China there was only a weak middle class, vegetating under the dominance of the gentry." [060-038]

## The Hundred Days' Reform and Its Suppression

In 1898, the scholar K'ang Yo-wei, a follower of the empiricist Han school of philosophy, gained the emperor's ear and persuaded him to issue edicts ordering "the fundamental reorganization of education, law, trade, communications, and the army" [060-054]. These reforms "would have paved the way for a liberalization of Chinese society" [060-057], but they aroused the fierce opposition of the conservative gentry.

The dowager empress Tz'u Hsi acted with "lightning speed" [060-073]. The reformers were arrested and executed or forced to flee; the emperor himself was made a prisoner in a palace near [[peking|Peking]] and remained captive until his death. The empress resumed her regency, and General [[yuan-shih-kai|Yüan Shih-k'ai]], commanding the only modern-equipped troops in China, chose not to intervene on the reformers' behalf — "he was already pursuing a personal policy, and thought it safer to give the reformers no help" [060-083].

## The Rising of 1900

From 1898, the dowager empress imposed "a thoroughly reactionary rule" [060-085]. But China's situation permitted no pause. In 1900:

> "came the so-called Boxer Rising, a new popular movement against the gentry and the Manchus similar to the many that had preceded it. The Peking government succeeded, however, in negotiations that brought the movement into the service of the government and directed it against the Europeans." [060-087]

This was a masterstroke of political manipulation: the government neutralized a domestic threat by redirecting popular anger outward. The Boxers, originally anti-Manchu and anti-gentry, became instruments of the court's anti-foreign policy.

However, "incidents resulted which the [[peking|Peking]] government had not anticipated" [060-092]. An international army was assembled and marched from Tientsin against [[peking|Peking]] to relieve the besieged European legations and to punish the Chinese government.

## The International Intervention

The European powers captured [[peking|Peking]] in 1900. The dowager empress and her prisoner, the emperor, fled the capital. Some of the imperial palaces were looted [060-096]. The peace treaty that followed imposed further concessions on China and "enormous war indemnities, the payment of which continued into the 1940's" — though most of the powers later placed the money at China's disposal for educational and cultural purposes [060-099].

## Aftermath and Significance

The Boxer Rising was simultaneously the last gasp of conservative Manchu resistance to modernization and a harbinger of the revolutionary nationalism that would transform China in the following decades. The empress's strategy of channelling popular discontent against foreigners provided only temporary relief. The humiliating foreign occupation of [[peking|Peking]] and the crushing indemnities deepened the dynasty's illegitimacy.

The failure of the Boxer policy demonstrated conclusively that neither conservative reaction nor xenophobic populism could restore China's sovereignty. The generation that came of age after 1900 — the students and intellectuals who would lead the [[may-fourth-movement|May Fourth Movement]] of 1919 — drew the opposite lesson: only thoroughgoing modernization and the creation of a strong, unified national state could end the era of foreign domination.
