# Section 70

[070-001] p. 102: For the "church" I rely mainly upon H. Maspero and W. Eichhorn.

[070-002] p. 103: I use here concepts developed by Cheng Chen-to and especially by

[070-003] Jung Chao-tsu.

[070-004] p. 104: Wang Ch'ung's importance has recently been mentioned again by J.

[070-005] Needham.

[070-006] p. 105: These "court poets" have their direct parallel in Western Asia.

[070-007] This trend, however, did not become typical in China.--On the general

[070-008] history of paper read A. Kroeber, _Anthropology_, New York 1948, p.

[070-009] 490f., and Dard Hunter, _Paper Making_, New York 1947 (2nd ed.).

[070-010] _Chapter Seven_

[070-011] p. 109: The main historical sources for this period have been translated

[070-012] by Achilles Fang, _The Chronicle of the Three Kingdoms_, Cambridge,

[070-013] Mass. 1952; the epic which describes this time is C.H. Brewitt-Taylor,

[070-014] _San Kuo, or Romance of the Three Kingdoms_, Shanghai 1925.

[070-015] p. 112: For problems of migration and settlement in the South, we relied

[070-016] in part upon research by Ch'en Yüan and Wang Yi-t'ung.

[070-017] p. 114: For the history of the Hsiung-nu I am relying mainly upon my own

[070-018] studies.

[070-019] p. 117: This analysis of tribal structure is based mainly upon my own

[070-020] research; it differs in detail from the studies by E. Bacon, _Obok, a

[070-021] Study of Social Structure in Eurasia_, New York 1958, B. Vladimirtsov,

[070-022] O. Lattimore's _Inner Asian Frontiers of China_, New York 1951 (2nd

[070-023] edit.) and the studies by L.M.J. Schram, _The Monguors of the

[070-024] Kansu-Tibetan Frontier_, Philadelphia 1954 and 1957.

[070-025] p. 118: The use of the word "Huns" does not imply that we identify the

[070-026] early or the late Hsiung-nu with the European Huns. This question is

[070-027] still very much under discussion (O. Maenchen, W. Haussig, W. Henning,

[070-028] and others).

[070-029] p. 119: For the history of the early Hsien-pi states see the monograph

[070-030] by G. Schreiber, "The History of the Former Yen Dynasty", in _Monomenta

[070-031] Serica_, vol. 14 and 15 (1949-56). For all translations from Chinese

[070-032] Dynastic Histories of the period between 220 and 960 the _Catalogue of

[070-033] Translations from the Chinese Dynastic Histories for the Period

[070-034] 220-960_, by Hans H. Frankel, Berkeley 1957, is a reliable guide.

[070-035] p. 125: For the description of conditions in Turkestan, especially in

[070-036] Tunhuang, I rely upon my own studies, but studies by A. von Gabein, L.

[070-037] Ligeti, J.R. Ware, O. Franke and Tsukamoto Zenryû have been used, too.

[070-038] p. 133: These songs have first been studied by Hu Shih, later by Chinese

[070-039] folklorists.

[070-040] p. 134: For problems of Chinese Buddhism see Arthur F. Wright, _Buddhism

[070-041] in Chinese History_, Stanford 1959, with further bibliography. I have

[070-042] used for this and later periods, in addition to my own sociological

[070-043] studies, R. Michihata, J. Gernet, and Tamai Korehiro.--It is interesting

[070-044] that the rise of landowning temples in India occurred at exactly the

[070-045] same time (R.S. Sharma in _Journ. Econ. and Soc. Hist. Orient_, vol. 1,

[070-046] 1958, p. 316). Perhaps even more interesting, but still unstudied, is

[070-047] the existence of Buddhist temples in India which owned land and villages

[070-048] which were donated by contributions from China.--For the use of foreign

[070-049] monks in Chinese bureaucracies, I have used M. Weber's theory as an

[070-050] interpretative tool.

[070-051] p. 135: The important deities of Khotan Buddhism are Vai['s]ramana and

[070-052] Kubera, (research by P. Demiéville, R. Stein and others).--Where, how,

[070-053] and why Hinayana and Mahayana developed as separate sects, is not yet

[070-054] studied. Also, a sociological analysis of the different Buddhist sects

[070-055] in China has not even been attempted yet.

[070-056] p. 136: Such public religious disputations were known also in India.

[070-057] p. 137: Analysis of the tribal names has been made by L. Bazin.

[070-058] pp. 138-9: The personality type which was the ideal of the Toba

[070-059] corresponded closely to the type described by G. Geesemann, _Heroische

[070-060] Lebensform_, Berlin 1943.

[070-061] p. 142: The Toba occur in contemporary Western sources as Tabar, Tabgaç,

[070-062] Tafkaç and similar names. The ethnic name also occurs as a title (O.

[070-063] Pritsak, P. Pelliot, W. Haussig and others).--On the _chün-t'ien_ system

[070-064] cf. the article by Wan Kuo-ting in E-tu Zen Sun, _Chinese Social

[070-065] History_, Washington 1956, p. 157-184. I also used Yoshimi Matsumoto and

[070-066] T'ang Ch'ang-ju.--Census fragments from Tunhuang have been published by

[070-067] L. Giles, Niida Noboru and other Japanese scholars.

[070-068] p. 143: On slaves for the earlier time see M. Wilbur, _Slavery in China

[070-069] during the Former Han Dynasty_, Chicago 1943. For our period Wang

[070-070] Yi-t'ung and especially Niida Noboru and Ch'ü T'ung-tsu. I used for this

[070-071] discussion Niida, Ch'ü and Tamai Korehiro.--For the _pu-ch'ü_ I used in

[070-072] addition Yang Chung-i, H. Maspero, E. Balazs, W. Eichhorn. Yang's

[070-073] article is translated in E-tu Zen Sun's book, _Chinese Social History_,

[070-074] pp. 142-56.--The question of slaves and their importance in Chinese

[070-075] society has always been given much attention by Chinese Communist

[070-076] authors. I believe that a clear distinction between slaves and serfs is

[070-077] very important.

[070-078] p. 145: The political use of Buddhism has been asserted for Japan as

[070-079] well as for Korea and Tibet (H. Hoffmann, _Quellen zur Geschichte der

[070-080] tibetischen Bon-Religion_, Mainz 1950, p. 220 f.). A case could be made

[070-081] for Burma. In China, Buddhism was later again used as a tool by rulers

[070-082] (see below).

[070-083] p. 146: The first text in which such problems of state versus church are

[070-084] mentioned is Mou Tz[)u] (P. Pelliot transl.). More recently, some of the

[070-085] problems have been studied by R. Michihata and E. Zürcher. Michihata

[070-086] also studied the temple slaves. Temple families were slightly different.

[070-087] They have been studied mainly by R. Michihata, J. Gernet and Wang

[070-088] Yi-t'ung. The information on T'an-yao is mainly in _Wei-shu_ 114

[070-089] (transl. J. Ware).--The best work on Yün-kang is now Seiichi Mizuno and

[070-090] Toshio Nagahiro, _Yün-kang. The Buddhist Cave-Temples of the Fifth

[070-091] Century A.D. in North China_, Kyoto 1951-6, thus far 16 volumes. For

[070-092] Chinese Buddhist art, the work by Tokiwa Daijô and Sekino Tadashi,

[070-093] _Chinese Buddhist Monuments_, Tokyo 1926-38, 5 volumes, is most

[070-094] profusely illustrated.--As a general reader for the whole of Chinese

[070-095] art, Alexander Soper and L. Sickman's _The Art and Architecture of

[070-096] China_, Baltimore 1956 may be consulted.

[070-097] p, 147: Zenryû Tsukamoto has analysed one such popular, revolutionary

[070-098] Buddhist text from the fifth century A.D. I rely here for the whole

[070-099] chapter mainly upon my own research.

[070-100] p. 150: On the Ephtalites (or Hephtalites) see R. Ghirshman and

[070-101] Enoki.--The carpet ceremony has been studied by P. Boodberg, and in a

[070-102] comparative way by L. Olschki, _The Myth of Felt_, Berkeley 1949.

[070-103] p. 151: For Yang Chien and his time see now A.F. Wright, "The Formation

[070-104] of Sui Ideology" in John K. Fairbank, _Chinese Thought and

[070-105] Institutions_, Chicago 1957, pp. 71-104.

[070-106] p. 153: The processes described here, have not yet been thoroughly

[070-107] analysed. A preliminary review of literature is given by H. Wiens,

[070-108] _China's March towards the Tropics_, Hamden 1954. I used Ch'en Yüan,

[070-109] Wang Yi-t'ung and my own research.

[070-110] p. 154: It is interesting to compare such hunting parks with the

[070-111] "_paradeisos"_ (Paradise) of the Near East and with the "Garden of

[070-112] Eden".--Most of the data on gardens and manors have been brought

[070-113] together and studied by Japanese scholars, especially by Kat[=o]

[070-114] Shigeru, some also by Ho Tzû-ch'üan.--The disappearance of "village

[070-115] commons" in China should be compared with the same process in Europe;

[070-116] both processes, however, developed quite differently. The origin of

[070-117] manors and their importance for the social structure of the Far East

[070-118] (China as well as Japan) is the subject of many studies in Japan and in

[070-119] modern China. This problem is connected with the general problem of

[070-120] feudalism East and West. The manor (_chuang_: Japanese _shô_) in later

[070-121] periods has been studied by Y. Sudô. H. Maspero also devotes attention

[070-122] to this problem. Much more research remains to be done.

[070-123] p. 158: This popular rebellion by Sun En has been studied by W.

[070-124] Eichhorn.

[070-125] p. 163: On foreign music in China see L.C. Goodrich and Ch'ü T'ung-tsu,

[070-126] H.G. Farmer, S. Kishibe and others.--Niida Noboru pointed out that

[070-127] musicians belonged to one of the lower social classes, but had special

[070-128] privileges because of their close relations to the rulers.

[070-129] p. 164: Meditative or _Ch'an_ (Japanese: _Zen_) Buddhism in this period

[070-130] has been studied by Hu Shih, but further analysis is necessary.--The

[070-131] philosophical trends of this period have been analysed by E.

[070-132] Balazs.--Mention should also be made of the aesthetic-philosophical

[070-133] conversation which was fashionable in the third century, but in other

[070-134] form still occurred in our period, the so-called "pure talk"

[070-135] (_ch'ing-t'an_) (E. Balazs, H. Wilhelm and others).

[070-136] _Chapter Eight_

[070-137] p. 167: For genealogies and rules of giving names, I use my own research

[070-138] and the study by W. Bauer.

[070-139] p. 168: For Emperor Wen Ti, I rely mainly upon A.F. Wright's

[070-140] above-mentioned article, but also upon O. Franke.

[070-141] p. 169: The relevant texts concerning the T'u-chüeh are available in

[070-142] French (E. Chavannes) and recently also in German translation (Liu

[070-143] Mau-tsai, _Die chinesischen Nachrichten zur Geschichte der Ost-T[vu]rken_,

[070-144] Wiesbaden 1958, 2 vol.).--The Tölös are called T'e-lo in Chinese

[070-145] sources; the T'u-yü-hun are called Aza in Central Asian sources (P.

[070-146] Pelliot, A. Minorsky, F.W. Thomas, L. Hambis, _et al_.). The most

[070-147] important text concerning the T'u-yü-hun had been translated by Th. D.

[070-148] Caroll, _Account of the T'u-yü-hun in the History of the Chin Dynasty_,

[070-149] Berkeley 1953.

[070-150] p. 171: The transcription of names on this and on the other maps could

[070-151] not be adjusted to the transcription of the text for technical reasons.

[070-152] p. 172: It is possible that I have underestimated the role of Li Yüan. I

[070-153] relied here mainly upon O. Franke and upon W. Bingham's _The Founding of

[070-154] the T'ang Dynasty_, Baltimore 1941.

[070-155] p. 173: The best comprehensive study of T'ang economy in a Western

[070-156] language is still E. Balazs's work. I relied, however, strongly upon Wan

[070-157] Kuo-ting, Yang Chung-i, Kat[=o] Shigeru, J. Gernet, T. Naba, Niida

[070-158] Noboru, Yoshimi Matsumoto.

[070-159] pp. 173-4: For the description of the administration I used my own

[070-160] studies and the work of R. des Rotours; for the military organization I

[070-161] used Kikuchi Hideo. A real study of Chinese army organization and

[070-162] strategy does not yet exist. The best detailed study, but for the Han

[070-163] period, is written by H. Maspero.

[070-164] p. 174: For the first occurrence of the title _tu-tu_ we used W.

[070-165] Eichhorn; in the form _tutuq_ the title occurs since 646 in Central Asia

[070-166] (J. Hamilton).

[070-167] p. 177: The name T'u-fan seems to be a transcription of Tüpöt which,

[070-168] in turn, became our Tibet. (J. Hamilton).--The Uighurs are the Hui-ho or

[070-169] Hui-hu of Chinese sources.

[070-170] p. 179: On relations with Central Asia and the West see Ho Chien-min and

[070-171] Hsiang Ta, whose classical studies on Ch'ang-an city life have recently

[070-172] been strongly criticized by Chinese scholars.--Some authors (J.K.

[070-173] Rideout) point to the growing influence of eunuchs in this period.--The

[070-174] sources paint the pictures of the Empress Wu in very dark colours. A

[070-175] more detailed study of this period seems to be necessary.

[070-176] p. 180: The best study of "family privileges" (_yin_) in general is by

[070-177] E.A. Kracke, _Civil Service in Early Sung China_, Cambridge, Mass. 1953.

[070-178] p. 180-1: The economic importance of organized Buddhism has been studied

[070-179] by many authors, especially J. Gernet, Yang Lien-sheng, Ch'üan

[070-180] Han-sheng, K. Tamai and R. Michihata.

[070-181] p. 182: The best comprehensive study on T'ang prose in English is still

[070-182] E.D. Edwards, _Chinese Prose Literature of the T'ang Period_, London

[070-183] 1937-8, 2 vol. On Li T'ai-po and Po Chü-i we have well-written books by

[070-184] A. Waley, _The Poetry and Career of Li Po_, London 1951 and _The Life

[070-185] and Times of Po Chü-i_, London 1950.--On the "free poem" (_tz[)u]_),

[070-186] which technically is not a free poem, see A. Hoffmann and Hu Shih. For

[070-187] the early Chinese theatre, the classical study is still Wang Kuo-wei's

[070-188] analysis, but there is an almost unbelievable number of studies

[070-189] constantly written in China and Japan, especially on the later theatre

[070-190] and drama.

[070-191] p. 184: Conditions at the court of Hsüan Tsung and the life of Yang

[070-192] Kui-fei have been studied by Howard Levy and others, An Lu-shan's

[070-193] importance mainly by E.G. Pulleyblank, _The Background of the Rebellion

[070-194] of An Lu-shan_, London 1955.

[070-195] p. 187: The tax reform of Yang Yen has been studied by K. Hino; the most

[070-196] important figures in T'ang economic history are Liu Yen (studied by Chü

[070-197] Ch'ing-yüan) and Lu Chih (754-805; studied by E. Balazs and others).
