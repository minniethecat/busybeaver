# Section 68

[068-001] p. 9: Comparison with Ainu has been made by Weidenreich. The theory of

[068-002] desiccation of Asia is not the Huntington theory, but I rely here upon

[068-003] arguments by J.G. Andersson and Sven Hedin.

[068-004] p. 10. The earlier theories of R. Heine-Geldern have been used here.

[068-005] p. 11: This is a summary of my own theories. Concerning the Tungus

[068-006] tribes, K. Jettmar (_Wiener Beiträge zur Kulturgeschichte_, vol. 9,

[068-007] 1952, p. 484f and later studies) has proposed a more refined theory;

[068-008] other parts of the theory, as far as it is concerned with conditions in

[068-009] Central Asia, have been modified by F. Kussmaul (in: _Tribus_, vol.

[068-010] 1952-3, pp. 305-60). Archaeological data from Central Asia have been

[068-011] analysed again by K. Jettmar (in: _The Museum of Far Eastern

[068-012] Antiquities, Bulletin_ No. 23, 1951). The discussion on domestication of

[068-013] large animals relies on the studies by C.O. Sauer, H. von Wissmann,

[068-014] Menghin, Amschler, Flohr and, most recently, F. Han[vc]ar (in:

[068-015] _Saeculum_, vol. 10, 1959, pp. 21-37 with further literature), and also

[068-016] on my own research.

[068-017] p. 12: An analysis of the situation in the South according to Western

[068-018] and Chinese studies is found in H.J. Wiens, _China's March toward the

[068-019] Tropics_, Hamden 1954. Much further work is now published by Ling

[068-020] Shun-sheng, Rui Yi-fu and other anthropologists in Taipei. The best

[068-021] analysis of denshiring in the Far East is still the book by K.J. Pelzer,

[068-022] _Population and Land Utilization_, New York 1941. The anthropological

[068-023] theories on this page are my own, influenced by ideas of R.

[068-024] Heine-Geldern and Gordon Luce.

[068-025] p. 14: Sociological theory, as developed by R. Thurnwald and others, has

[068-026] been used as a theoretical tool here, together with observations by A.

[068-027] Credner and H. Bernatzik. Concerning rice in Yang-shao see R.

[068-028] Heine-Geldern in _Anthropos_, vol. 27, p. 595.

[068-029] p. 15: Wu Chin-ting defended the local origin of Yang-shao; T.J. Arne,

[068-030] J.G. Andersson and many others suggested Western influences. Most

[068-031] recently R. Heine-Geldern elaborated this theory. The allusion to

[068-032] Indo-Europeans refers to the studies by G. Haloun and others concerning

[068-033] the Ta-Hsia, the later Yüeh-chih, and the Tocharian problem.

[068-034] p. 16: R. Heine-Geldern proposed a "Pontic migration". Yin Huan-chang

[068-035] discussed most recently Lung-shan culture and the mound-dwellers.

[068-036] p. 17: The original _Chu-shu chi-nien_ version of the stories about Yao

[068-037] has been accepted here, together with my own research and the studies by

[068-038] B. Karlgren, M. Loehr, G. Haloun, E.H. Minns and others concerning the

[068-039] origin and early distribution of bronze and the animal style. Smith

[068-040] families or tribes are well known from Central Asia, but also from India

[068-041] and Africa (see W. Ruben, _Eisenschmiede und Dämonen in Indien_, Leiden

[068-042] 1939, for general discussion).--For a discussion of the Hsia see E.

[068-043] Erkes.

[068-044] _Chapter Two_

[068-045] p. 19: The discussion in this chapter relies mainly upon the Anyang

[068-046] excavation reports and the studies by Tung Tso-pin and, most strongly,

[068-047] Ch'en Meng-chia. In English, the best work is still H.G. Creel, _The

[068-048] Birth of China_, London 1936 and his more specialized _Studies in Early

[068-049] Chinese Culture_, Baltimore 1937.

[068-050] p. 20: The possibility of a "megalithic" culture in the Far East has

[068-051] often been discussed, by O. Menghin, R. Heine-Geldern, Cheng Tê-k'un,

[068-052] Ling Shun-sheng and others. Megaliths occur mainly in South-East Asia,

[068-053] southern China, Korea and Japan.--Teng Ch'u-min and others believe that

[068-054] silk existed already in the time of Yang-shao.

[068-055] p. 21: Kuo Mo-jo believes, that the Shang already used a real plough

[068-056] drawn by animals. The main discussion on ploughs in China is by Hsü

[068-057] Chung-shu; for general anthropological discussion see E. Werth and H.

[068-058] Kothe.

[068-059] p. 22: For the discussion of the T'ao-t'ieh see the research by B.

[068-060] Karlgren and C. Hentze.

[068-061] p. 23: I follow here mainly Ch'en Meng-chia, but work by B. Schindler,

[068-062] C. Hentze, H. Maspero and also my own research has been considered.

[068-063] p. 24: I am accepting here a narrow definition of feudalism (see my

[068-064] _Conquerors and Rulers_, Leiden 1952).--The division of armies into

[068-065] "right" and "left" is interesting in the light of the theories

[068-066] concerning the importance of systems of orientation (Fr. Rock and

[068-067] others).

[068-068] p. 25: Here, the work by W. Koppers, O. Spengler, F. Han[vc]ar, V.G.

[068-069] Childe and many others, concerning the domestication of the horse and

[068-070] the introduction of the war-chariot in general, and work by Shih

[068-071] Chang-ju, Ch'en Meng-chia, O. Maenchen, Uchida Gimpu and others

[068-072] concerning horses, riding and chariots in China has been used, in

[068-073] addition to my own research.

[068-074] p. 26: Concerning the wild animals, I have relied upon Ch'en Meng-chia,

[068-075] Hsü Chung-shu and Tung Tso-pin.--The discussion as to whether there was

[068-076] a period of "slave society" (as postulated by Marxist theory) in China,

[068-077] and when it flourished, is still going on under the leadership of Kuo

[068-078] Mo-jo and his group. I prefer to differentiate between slaves and serfs,

[068-079] and relied for factual data upon texts from oracle bones, not upon

[068-080] historical texts.--The problem of Shang chronology is still not solved,

[068-081] in spite of extensive work by Liu Ch'ao-yang, Tung Tso-pin and many

[068-082] Japanese and Western scholars. The old chronology, however, seems to be

[068-083] rejected by most scholars now.

[068-084] _Chapter Three_

[068-085] p. 29: Discussing the early script and language, I refer to the great

[068-086] number of unidentified Shang characters and, especially, to the

[068-087] composite characters which have been mentioned often by C. Hentze in his

[068-088] research; on the other hand, the original language of the Chou may have

[068-089] been different from classical Chinese, if we can judge from the form of

[068-090] the names of the earliest Chou ancestors. Problems of substrata

[068-091] languages enter at this stage. Our first understanding of Chou language

[068-092] and dialects seems to come through the method applied by P. Serruys,

[068-093] rather than through the more generally accepted theories and methods of

[068-094] B. Karlgren and his school.

[068-095] p. 30: I reject here the statement of classical texts that the last

[068-096] Shang ruler was unworthy, and accept the new interpretation of Ch'en

[068-097] Meng-chia which is based upon oracle bone texts,--The most recent

[068-098] general study on feudalism, and on feudalism in China, is in R.

[068-099] Coulborn, _Feudalism in History_, Princeton 1956. Stimulating, but in

[068-100] parts antiquated, is M. Granet, _La Féodalité Chinoise_, Oslo 1952. I

[068-101] rely here on my own research. The instalment procedure has been

[068-102] described by H. Maspero and Ch'i Sz[)u]-ho.

[068-103] p. 31: The interpretation of land-holding and clans follows my own

[068-104] research which is influenced by Niida Noboru, Kat[=o] Shigeru and other

[068-105] Japanese scholars, as well as by G. Haloun.--Concerning the origin of

[068-106] family names see preliminarily Yang Hsi-mei; much further research is

[068-107] still necessary. The general development of Chinese names is now studied

[068-108] by Wolfgang Bauer.--The spread of cities in this period has been studied

[068-109] by Li Chi, _The Formation of the Chinese People_, Cambridge 1928. My

[068-110] interpretation relies mainly upon a study of the distribution of

[068-111] non-Chinese tribes and data on early cities coming from excavation

[068-112] reports (see my "Data on the Structure of the Chinese City" in _Economic

[068-113] Development and Cultural Change_, 1956, pp. 253-68, and "The Formation

[068-114] of Chinese Civilization" in _Sociologus_ 7, 1959, pp. 97-112).

[068-115] p. 32: The work on slaves by T. Pippon, E. Erkes, M. Wilbur, Wan

[068-116] Kuo-ting, Kuo Mo-jo, Niida Noboru, Kao Nien-chih and others has been

[068-117] consulted; the interpretation by E.G. Pulleyblank, however, was not

[068-118] accepted.

[068-119] p. 33: This interpretation of the "well-field" system relies in part

[068-120] upon the work done by Hsü Ti-shan, in part upon M. Granet and H.

[068-121] Maspero, and attempts to utilize insight from general anthropological

[068-122] theory and field-work mainly in South-East Asia. Other interpretations

[068-123] have been proposed by Yang Lien-sheng, Wan Kuo-ting, Ch'i Sz[)u]-ho P.

[068-124] Demiéville, Hu Shih, Chi Ch'ao-ting, K.A. Wittfogel, and others Some

[068-125] authors, such as Kuo Mo-jo, regard the whole system as an utopia, but

[068-126] believe in an original "village community".--The characterization of the

[068-127] _Chou-li_ relies in part upon the work done by Hsü Chung-shu and Ku

[068-128] Chieh-kang on the titles of nobility, research by Yang K'uan and textual

[068-129] criticism by B. Karlgren, O. Franke, and again Ku Chieh-kang and his

[068-130] school.--The discussion on twin cities is intended to draw attention to

[068-131] its West Asian parallels, the "acropolis" or "ark" city, as well as to

[068-132] the theories on the difference between Western and Asian cities (M.

[068-133] Weber) and the specific type of cities in "dual societies" (H. Boeke).

[068-134] p. 34: This is a modified form of the Hu Shih theory.--The problem of

[068-135] nomadic agrarian inter-action and conflict has been studied for a later

[068-136] period mainly by O. Lattimore. Here, general anthropological research as

[068-137] well as my own have been applied.

[068-138] p. 36: The supra-stratification theory as developed by R. Thurnwald has

[068-139] been used as analytic tool here.

[068-140] p. 38: For this period, a novel interpretation is presented by R.L.

[068-141] Walker, _The Multi-State System of China_, Hamden 1953. For the concepts

[068-142] of sovereignty, I have used here the _Chou-li_ text and interpretations

[068-143] based upon this text.

[068-144] p. 40: For the introduction of iron and the importance of Ch'i, see Chu

[068-145] Hsi-tsu, Kuo Mo-jo, Yang K'uan, Sekino, Takeshi.--Some scholars (G.

[068-146] Haloun) tend to interpret attacks such as the one of 660 B.C. as attacks

[068-147] from outside the borders of China.

[068-148] p. 41: For Confucius see H.G. Creel, _Confucius_, New York 1949. I do

[068-149] not, however, follow his interpretation, but rather the ideas of Hu

[068-150] Shih, O. Franke and others.

[068-151] p. 42: For "chün-tz[)u]" and its counterpart "hsiao-jen" see D. Bodde

[068-152] and Ch'en Meng-chia.

[068-153] p 43: I rely strongly here upon O. Franke and Ku Chieh-kang and upon my

[068-154] own work on eclipses.

[068-155] p. 44: I regard the Confucian traditions concerning the model emperors

[068-156] of early time as such a falsification. The whole concept of "abdication"

[068-157] has been analysed by M. Granet. The later ceremony of abdication was

[068-158] developed upon the basis of the interpretations of Confucius and has

[068-159] been studied by Ku Chieh-kang and Miyakawa Hisayuki. Already Confucius'

[068-160] disciple Meng Tz[)u], and later Chuang Tz[)u] and Han Fei Tz[)u] were

[068-161] against this theory.--As a general introduction to the philosophy of

[068-162] this period, Y.L. Feng's _History of Chinese Philosophy_, London 1937

[068-163] has still to be recommended, although further research has made many

[068-164] advances.--My analysis of the role of Confucianism in society is

[068-165] influenced by theories in the field of Sociology of religion.

[068-166] p. 45: The temple in Turkestan was in Khotan and is already mentioned in

[068-167] the _Wei-shu_ chapter 102. The analysis of the famous "Book on the

[068-168] transfiguration of Lao Tz[)u] into a Western Barbarian" by Wang

[068-169] Wei-cheng is penetrating and has been used here. The evaluation of Lao

[068-170] Tz[)u] and his pupils as against Confucius by J. Needham, in his

[068-171] _Science and Civilization in China_, Cambridge 1954 _et seq_. (in volume

[068-172] 2) is very stimulating, though necessarily limited to some aspects only.

[068-173] p. 47: The concept of _wu-wei_ has often been discussed; some, such as

[068-174] Masaaki Matsumoto, interpreted the concept purely in social terms as

[068-175] "refusal of actions carrying worldly estimation".

[068-176] p. 49 Further literature concerning alchemy and breathing exercises is

[068-177] found in J. Needham's book.

[068-178] _Chapter Four_

[068-179] p. 51: I have used here the general framework of R.L. Walker, but more

[068-180] upon Yang K'uan's studies.

[068-181] p. 52: The interpretation of the change of myths in this period is based

[068-182] in part upon the work done by H. Maspero, G. Haloun, and Ku Chieh-kang.

[068-183] The analysis of legends made by B. Karlgren from a philological point of

[068-184] view ("Legends and Cults in Ancient China", _The Museum of Far Eastern

[068-185] Antiquities, Bulletin_ No. 18, 1946, pp. 199-365) follows another

[068-186] direction.

[068-187] p. 53: The discussion on riding involves the theories concerning

[068-188] horse-nomadic tribes and the period of this way of life. It also

[068-189] involves the problem of the invention of stirrup and saddle. The saddle

[068-190] seems to have been used in China already at the beginning of our period;

[068-191] the stirrup seems to be as late as the fifth century A.D. The article by

[068-192] A. Kroeber, _The Ancient Oikumene as an Historic Culture Aggregate_,

[068-193] Huxley Memorial Lecture for 1945, is very instructive for our problems

[068-194] and also for its theoretical approach.--The custom of attracting

[068-195] settlers from other areas in order to have more production as well as

[068-196] more manpower seems to have been known in India at the same time.
