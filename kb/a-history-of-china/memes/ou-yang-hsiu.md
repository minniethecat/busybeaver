## Overview

[[ou-yang-hsiu|Ou-yang Hsiu]] (1007–1072) was a prominent historian, statesman, and literary figure of the Northern [[sung-dynasty|Sung dynasty]]. A leading conservative voice of his era, he is remembered both for his scholarly achievements and for his role in developing the first systematic censorship rules for printed books in Chinese history.

> To the famous historian, anti-Buddhist and conservative Ou-yang Hsiu (1007-1072), the enemy of Wang An-shih, belongs the sad glory of having developed the first censorship rules. [051-078] [051-079] [051-080]

## Political Stance and Opposition to Reform

Ou-yang Hsiu was a staunch opponent of [[wang-an-shih|Wang An-shih]] (1021–1086), the radical reformer whose New Policies convulsed the Sung court in the 1070s. Where [[wang-an-shih|Wang An-shih]] sought to expand state intervention in the economy and overhaul the [[examination-system|examination system]], Ou-yang Hsiu represented the conservative gentry interest that viewed such changes as destabilizing to the established social order. The political struggle between these two factions — the reformers led by [[wang-an-shih|Wang An-shih]] and the conservatives led by figures like Ou-yang Hsiu and Ssu-ma Kuang — defined Sung political life for decades.

## Censorship and Print Culture

Ou-yang Hsiu's most consequential institutional legacy was the formalization of book censorship. Print technology had begun as a private enterprise in the Sung period, and with it came anxieties about the circulation of heterodox or politically dangerous ideas. The censorship apparatus Ou-yang Hsiu helped establish became a permanent feature of Chinese governance from the Ming dynasty onward.

> Censorship of printed books had started almost with the beginning of book printing as a private enterprise. Since Ming time, it became a permanent feature of Chinese governments. [051-076] [051-077] [051-080]

## As a Writer

Ou-yang Hsiu was also recognized as a master of classical prose, counted among the great literary figures of the Sung. His historical writings set a standard for subsequent dynastic historiography.

> Ou-yang Hsiu, writer [074-039]
