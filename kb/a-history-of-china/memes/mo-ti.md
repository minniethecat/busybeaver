## Overview

[[mo-ti|Mo Ti]] (also known as Mozi or Mo Tzu) was one of the most influential philosophers of the [[warring-states-period|Warring States period]] and the founder of Mohism, the first major rival to [[confucianism|Confucianism]]. Active in the fifth century BCE, he challenged the Confucian emphasis on ritual and hierarchical relationships, proposing instead a philosophy grounded in universal love, meritocracy, and practical utility.

## Philosophy of Universal Love

Mo Ti's most distinctive doctrine was "universal love" (chien ai) — the idea that one should care equally for all people, not just family members or social superiors:

> This school of thinkers was the school of Mo Ti, which at the time of its emergence represented a radical challenge to Confucian particularism... [012-026]

Where [[confucianism|Confucianism]] distinguished between obligations to family, community, and strangers, Mo Ti argued that partiality was the root of social conflict. If everyone treated others' families, states, and interests as their own, warfare and exploitation would cease.

## Social Reform Program

Mo Ti did not merely theorize — he proposed concrete institutional changes:

> The school of Mo Ti proposed to alter the existing social structure by replacing hereditary privilege with a system of promotion based on merit and ability... [012-029]

His vision of social mobility — "exalting the worthy" (shang hsien) — directly challenged the aristocratic assumptions of his era. He argued that rulers should appoint officials based on competence rather than birth, a position that anticipated later Chinese meritocratic traditions.

## The "People" and Utilitarianism

Mo Ti developed a distinctive form of utilitarian ethics:

> The upper class, which Mo Ti called the "people" — one must consider what benefits the majority rather than what pleases the elite... [012-032]

He evaluated policies and practices by their concrete outcomes for the common people, not by their conformity to tradition or ritual propriety. This pragmatic, outcome-oriented thinking set Mohism apart from other classical schools and gave it particular appeal among artisans and lower-ranking members of society.

## Anti-Warfare Stance

Mo Ti and his followers were famous for their opposition to offensive warfare. According to traditional accounts, they developed defensive siege technologies and would travel to intervene diplomatically when one state planned to attack another. Their commitment to "universal love" was not merely theoretical — it translated into active peacemaking:

> We see clearly in Mo Ti's and his followers' ideas the first systematic articulation of anti-militarism in Chinese thought, grounded in a philosophical commitment to the equal worth of all human lives. [012-051]

## Decline and Legacy

Mohism flourished for several centuries after Mo Ti's death, rivaling [[confucianism|Confucianism]] in popularity during the Warring States period. However, it declined sharply after the Han dynasty's unification, possibly because its organizational structure — a quasi-military order with strict discipline — proved unsustainable under imperial rule. Mo Ti's ideas were largely neglected until modern scholars rediscovered them:

> Mo Ti, the philosopher whose radical egalitarianism and utilitarian ethics anticipate themes in modern Western philosophy... [073-709]

Today, Mo Ti is recognized as one of the most original thinkers of classical China, and his concepts of impartial care and merit-based governance continue to resonate in contemporary discussions of ethics and political philosophy.
