The Yüan dynasty was the period of Mongol rule over China, lasting from the final conquest of the Southern [[sung-dynasty|Sung]] in 1280 to the expulsion of the Mongols and the founding of the [[ming-dynasty|Ming dynasty]] in 1368. It was the first time in Chinese history that the entire country was ruled by a non-Chinese conquering power, and it integrated China into a transcontinental empire stretching from the Pacific to Eastern Europe. The Mongol epoch brought intensified contact with the wider world, transformed China's economy and society, and left what later historians would describe as "indelible traces" on Chinese civilization.

## Origins and Conquest

The Yüan dynasty had its origins in the rise of the Mongol confederation under Chinggis Khan in the early thirteenth century. After unifying the Mongol tribes, Chinggis launched a series of campaigns that created the largest contiguous land empire in history. Northern China fell to the Mongols in the 1230s, but the conquest of the Southern Sung—the richest and most populous region—took decades longer.

The final subjugation of the Sung was completed under Khubilai Khan, a grandson of Chinggis, who established his capital at what is now Peking and proclaimed the Yüan dynasty in 1271, adopting the Chinese dynastic name before the conquest was even complete. The last Sung loyalists were defeated in 1279, and for the first time China was ruled as part of a world empire.

> Start of the Mongol empire and the Mongol epoch (1280–1368) in China marked a fundamental break in the pattern of Chinese dynastic history.
>
> — [001-165/167]

## Government and Administration

The Yüan rulers faced the challenge of governing China's vast sedentary population while maintaining their identity as a steppe conquering elite. Khubilai Khan adopted many trappings of Chinese imperial rule—the dynastic name, the capital city, the ritual forms—but the substance of Yüan government differed markedly from its Chinese predecessors.

The Mongols established a four-tiered social hierarchy that placed Mongols at the top, followed by non-Chinese allies from Central Asia and the Middle East (the _se-mu_ or "colored eyes"), then the former subjects of the Chin dynasty in the north (the _han-jen_), and finally the former Sung subjects of the south (the _nan-jen_). This system institutionalized ethnic discrimination as a principle of governance and ensured that key military and fiscal posts remained in Mongol and Central Asian hands.

> In foreign affairs the Mongol epoch was for China something of a watershed. For the first time, China was part of a world empire that extended far beyond the traditional Chinese sphere of influence.
>
> — [047-016]

## Economy and Society

The Mongol conquest inflicted severe demographic and economic damage, particularly in the north where resistance had been most stubborn. Yet the _Pax Mongolica_—the peace imposed across the Mongol domains—also created conditions for intensified long-distance trade. China's integration into a transcontinental commercial network brought new crops, technologies, and ideas from Persia, India, and the Islamic world.

> We have in the Mongol epoch in China the imposing picture of a realm opened to the world as never before, with merchants, missionaries, and envoys traveling freely across Asia along routes protected by Mongol power.
>
> — [047-147]

The Yüan government promoted commerce and relied heavily on tax farming by Central Asian financiers rather than the traditional Confucian agrarian tax base. The result was a more monetized economy, but also one in which peasants bore heavy burdens and suffered from the inflationary effects of paper currency issued to finance imperial expenditures.

> The denudation of the country—the exhaustion of its agricultural base through excessive taxation and corvée demands—was one of the most serious legacies of the Mongol epoch.
>
> — [047-151]

Chinese society under the Yüan saw significant internal migration as northern populations moved to escape Mongol military conscription and labor requisitions. At the same time, a large number of Central Asians, Persians, and even Europeans settled in China, creating cosmopolitan urban centers such as Ch'üan-chou (Zayton) and the capital at Khanbaliq (Peking).

> During the Mongol epoch a large number of the Chinese population migrated from the war-devastated northern regions to the relative safety and prosperity of the south.
>
> — [048-107]

## Culture and the Arts

The Yüan period witnessed an important shift in Chinese cultural life. With many Confucian scholars excluded from government service by Mongol preferences for non-Chinese administrators, the educated elite turned to private pursuits: painting, poetry, and drama. The Yüan is celebrated as the golden age of Chinese drama, producing masterpieces such as _The Story of the Western Wing_ and _The Injustice to Tou O_.

> The painters of the Mongol epoch were Chao Mêng-fu (also called Chao Tzu-ang), who revived the T'ang style of landscape painting, and the so-called Four Great Masters of the Yüan, who established the literati painting tradition that would dominate later Chinese art.
>
> — [049-017]

In painting, the Yüan masters moved away from the professional, decorative style favored at the Sung court toward a more personal, expressive mode of landscape painting—the beginnings of the literati (_wen-jen_) tradition that would influence Chinese art for the next six centuries. Chao Mêng-fu, a descendant of the Sung imperial family who served the Mongols, was the pivotal figure in this transition.

> In the Mongol epoch, most of the Chinese painters retired from public life and devoted themselves to art as a form of private cultivation and veiled protest against foreign rule.
>
> — [051-119]

Chinese scholars of later periods generally took a dim view of the Yüan, regarding it as an era of national humiliation and cultural decline. Yet the same sources acknowledge that the period was fertile in unexpected ways.

> Chinese historians have no good word to say of the Mongol epoch and complain bitterly of the oppression, corruption, and misrule that characterized it. Nevertheless, the Mongol epoch in China left indelible traces on the country's institutions, economy, and culture.
>
> — [049-028/032]

## Decline and Fall

The Yüan dynasty unraveled rapidly in the mid-fourteenth century. A combination of factors—factional infighting among the Mongol elite, fiscal collapse driven by paper currency inflation, natural disasters including the flooding of the Yellow River, and a series of popular uprisings—eroded Mongol authority. The Red Turban movement, a millenarian sect with roots in Buddhist and Manichaean belief, mobilized peasant discontent into armed rebellion.

> The popular movement at the end of the Mongol epoch drew on deep reservoirs of social and economic grievance, as well as anti-Mongol sentiment that had been building throughout the period of foreign rule.
>
> — [058-143]

From among the rebel leaders emerged Chu Yüan-chang, a former monk from an impoverished peasant family, who defeated both his Chinese rivals and the Mongol forces. In 1368, Chu proclaimed the [[ming-dynasty|Ming dynasty]] and expelled the last Yüan emperor from Peking. The Mongols retreated to the steppe, where they maintained a rump state known as the Northern Yüan, continuing to claim legitimacy even as their former Chinese domains moved into a new era.

> The luxury of the Mongol epoch was swept away by the austere and nativist reaction that characterized the early Ming.
>
> — [052-009]

## Legacy

The Yüan period's most important legacy was the integration of China into a world system. Although the Ming dynasty repudiated much of the Mongol inheritance, the connections established under Yüan rule—commercial, technological, and intellectual—endured. The Ming founder's effort to turn inward, to restore a purely Chinese form of governance, was itself shaped by the experience of having been ruled by foreigners.

The Yüan also left institutional legacies. The province (_sheng_) as an administrative unit, the use of Peking as the imperial capital, and aspects of military organization all passed from the Yüan to the Ming and Ch'ing. Even the Chinese distaste for foreign rule, so prominent in Ming and later nationalist historiography, was in part a reaction to the Yüan experience and thus, paradoxically, a testament to its impact.
