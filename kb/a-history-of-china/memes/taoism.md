## Overview

Taoism is one of the three great religious-philosophical traditions of China, alongside [[confucianism]] and [[buddhism]]. It originated in the late Zhou period with the teachings of [[lao-tzu|Lao Tzu]] (Lao Tz[)u]) and [[chuang-tzu|Chuang Tzu]] (Chuang Tz[)u]), and over two millennia developed into two distinct currents: **philosophical Taoism** (Tao-chia), an individualistic and mystical worldview practiced by the educated gentry as a private complement to their public Confucian lives; and **religious Taoism** (Tao-chiao), an organized church with a pantheon of deities, a professional priesthood, and a popular following among the peasantry.

Throughout Chinese history, Taoism served as the counterweight to the dominant [[confucianism|Confucian]] state ideology. Eberhard observes that "throughout history, Taoism remained the philosophic attitude of individuals of the highest circle of society" [010-086], while its popular form became what he describes as a systematization of indigenous folk religion [027-178]. Where [[confucianism|Confucianism]] emphasized social order, ritual propriety, and public duty, Taoism championed individual freedom, spontaneous nature, and withdrawal from the artificial constraints of society.

## Philosophical Foundations: Lao Tzu and the Tao Te Ching

The foundational text of Taoism is the _Tao Te Ching_ (Tao Te Ching), traditionally attributed to [[lao-tzu|Lao Tzu]]. Eberhard describes it as "the 'Book of the World Law and its Power'" [009-094], noting that "the book is written in quite simple language, at times in rhyme, but the sense is so vague that countless versions, differing radically from each other, can be based on it" [009-096]. This vagueness, he emphasizes, "is deliberate" [009-099] -- [[lao-tzu|Lao Tzu]] was attempting to express emotional and intuitive truths for which the Chinese language of the fourth century BCE was fundamentally inadequate [009-109].

> "Lao Tz[)u]'s teaching is essentially an effort to bring man's life on earth into harmony with the life and law of the universe (Tao). This was also Confucius's purpose. But while Confucius set out to attain that purpose in a sort of primitive scientific way, by laying down a number of rules of human conduct, Lao Tz[)u] tries to attain his ideal by an intuitive, emotional method." [009-100]

The central concept of [[lao-tzu|Lao Tzu]]'s teaching is the **Tao** (literally "the Way"), the underlying principle and law of the universe that governs all natural processes. The task of the individual, according to the Taoist view, "consists in the most complete adherence to the Tao that is conceivable, as far as possible performing no act that runs counter to the Tao" [009-134]. This doctrine of non-interference, or **_wu-wei_** ("passive achievement" or "non-action") [009-136], holds that the ideal life is one lived in accordance with one's own nature, free from the artificial impositions of society and government.

> "The more the people are forbidden to do this and that, the poorer will they be. The more sharp weapons the people possess, the more will darkness and bewilderment spread through the land. The more craft and cunning men have, the more useless and pernicious contraptions will they invent. The more laws and edicts are imposed, the more thieves and bandits there will be." [009-141]

## Chuang Tzu and the Taoist Way of Life

If [[lao-tzu|Lao Tzu]] was the foundational thinker of Taoism, [[chuang-tzu|Chuang Tzu]] was its greatest literary artist. Eberhard calls him "probably the most gifted poet among the Chinese philosophers and Taoists" [010-096]. Where [[lao-tzu|Lao Tzu]]'s _Tao Te Ching_ is terse and enigmatic, [[chuang-tzu|Chuang Tzu]]'s work is filled with parables, humor, and vivid imagery that bring Taoist ideas to life.

A defining characteristic of the Taoist, as described by Eberhard, is the withdrawal from social life: "A Taoist, as a follower of Lao Tz[)u] is called, withdraws from all social life, and carries out none of the rites and ceremonies which a man of the upper class should observe throughout the day" [009-125]. The Taoist lives "far from the city, surrounded by Nature ... entirely according to his nature" [009-130], seeing his own nature as a part of the Tao itself [009-133].

The famous story of [[chuang-tzu|Chuang Tzu]]'s refusal of high office illustrates the Taoist attitude:

> "I have heard," he replied, "that in Ch'u there is a tortoise sacred to the gods. It has now been dead for 3,000 years, and the king keeps it in a shrine with silken cloths, and gives it shelter in the halls of a temple. Which do you think that tortoise would prefer--to be dead and have its bones thus honoured, or to be alive and dragging its tail after it in the mud?" ... "Begone! I, too, would rather drag my tail after me in the mud!" [009-171] [009-179]

A distinctive feature of Chinese intellectual life was that [[confucianism|Confucianism]] and Taoism were not mutually exclusive. Eberhard notes that "it was quite possible, and later it became the rule, for one and the same person to be both Confucian and Taoist" [010-015]. As an official and head of a family, a man would think and act as a Confucian; in private, in retirement, or after dismissal from office, he would feel and think as a Taoist [010-016]. This complementary relationship between the two traditions -- public duty and private freedom -- became a defining pattern of Chinese gentry culture.

### Major Taoist Philosophers

A thread of Taoist thought can be traced across several centuries. Eberhard identifies the following figures as central to the philosophical tradition:

| Figure | Period | Significance |
|--------|--------|-------------|
| [[lao-tzu|Lao Tzu]] | 4th c. BCE (likely) | Author of the _Tao Te Ching_; founder of Taoist philosophy |
| [[chuang-tzu|Chuang Tzu]] | 4th c. BCE | Most gifted poet among the Taoists; author of the _[[chuang-tzu|Chuang Tzu]]_ |
| [[hsiung-nu|Hu]]ai-nan Tzu | 2nd c. BCE | Compiled Taoist and cosmological teachings |
| Chung-ch'ang T'ung | 2nd c. CE | Later Han Taoist thinker |
| Yuan Chi | 210--263 CE | Member of the Seven Sages of the Bamboo Grove |
| Liu Ling | 221--300 CE | Eccentric Taoist poet and wine-lover |
| T'ao Ch'ien | 365--427 CE | Great Taoist nature poet |

After the fourth century CE, "the stream of original thought dried up, and we rarely find a new idea among the late Taoists" [010-100]. Instead, the late Taoists expressed their philosophy through poetry and painting, creating "work which is among the finest achievements of mankind" [010-107].

## Religious Taoism: Chang Ling and the Organized Church

While philosophical Taoism remained a pursuit of the educated elite, a second current -- organized, popular religious Taoism -- emerged during the Later Han dynasty. Eberhard refers to this as "church-Taoism" [027-181], distinguishing it sharply from the original philosophical tradition.

The pivotal figure was **[[chang-ling|Chang Ling]]**, who founded a Taoist church that combined elements of Taoist philosophy with popular religion, faith healing, and communal organization. [[chang-ling|Chang Ling]] and his associates were originally leaders of a popular movement [020-109], and their church became the model for organized religious Taoism. The **[[yellow-turban-rebellion|Yellow Turban Rebellion]]** (184 CE), one of the great peasant uprisings that shook the Later Han, was directly connected to this popular Taoist movement [020-141].

Over time, the popular religion of the peasants -- local diviners, Chinese shamans (_wu_), and sorcerers -- adopted Taoist terminology. Eberhard notes that "often, this popular religion is called 'Taoism', because a systematization of the popular pantheon was attempted, and Lao Tz[)u] and other Taoists played a role in this pantheon" [027-178]. Thus three distinct currents coexisted: philosophic Taoism, church-Taoism (of [[chang-ling|Chang Ling]] and his successors), and the popular folk religion that increasingly used Taoist vocabulary [027-180].

### Key Developments in Religious Taoism

- **[[chang-ling|Chang Ling]]'s Church** (2nd c. CE): The first organized Taoist religious institution, combining philosophical Taoist elements with popular cults and messianic beliefs
- **K'ou Ch'ien-chih** (5th c. CE): A Taoist leader who allied with the gentry faction of the Ts'ui family against Buddhists, leading to the persecution of Buddhism in 446 CE [030-058]
- **Cheng-i-chiao** (Correct Unity Sect): A Taoist group that emerged in the Southern Sung period, through which [[chang-ling|Chang Ling]]'s church Taoism was perpetuated [053-071] [053-074]
- **Mongol period**: The [[mongol|Mongols]] showed interest in Taoism; Genghis Khan "admitted a Taoist monk to his camp (1221-1224)" [048-127]

## Relationships with Buddhism and the State

Taoism's relationship with [[buddhism]] was complex and often competitive. Eberhard observes that genuine (philosophical) Taoism and Hinayana Buddhism shared a similar individualistic character -- both were religions of "salvation without a God" [027-159]. Yet organized Taoism and Buddhism often clashed. The conflicts, however, reflected "more the power struggle between cliques than between religious groups" [030-053].

The most dramatic instance of Taoist-Buddhist conflict occurred in 446 CE, when the Taoist leader K'ou Ch'ien-chih united with a powerful Chinese gentry faction against the Buddhists, resulting in the destruction of temples and monasteries and the death of many monks [030-055]. Later, during the T'ang dynasty, the ruling gentry were characteristically "Confucianist and at the same time Taoist" [038-114], with genuine Taoism experiencing periodic revivals alongside renewed interest in popular Taoist cults [033-162].

Under the [[tang-dynasty|T'ang]], intellectual debates between "Confucians, Buddhists, and Taoists" became regular and elaborate affairs [033-166], reflecting the cosmopolitan character of the age. But Taoism never became a state ideology in the way [[confucianism|Confucianism]] did. As Eberhard notes, "Taoism was out of the question as a state doctrine" for the upper classes, who continued to rely on [[confucianism|Confucianism]] for the organization of government and society [061-164].

## Legacy

Taoism's most enduring influence came not through its organized church but through its shaping of Chinese aesthetics and sensibility. The strong Chinese feeling for nature, the reluctance to interfere with the sanctified order of nature by technical means, and the ideal of the scholar-recluse were all reinforced, if not created, by Taoist thought [010-088]. [[lao-tzu|Lao Tzu]]'s teaching "has had the strongest influence to this day" in inspiring creative work in poetry and painting [010-106].

In the realm of politics, the Taoist principle of non-interference ironically found application in a direction [[lao-tzu|Lao Tzu]] never intended. Eberhard points out that "the 'Legalist theory' which provided the philosophic basis for dictatorship in the third century B.C. was attributable to Lao Tz[)u]" [009-162], since the doctrine of _wu-wei_ could be interpreted as a justification for a ruler to govern by doing nothing while his ministers administered an all-encompassing system of laws and punishments.

Across Chinese history, Taoism -- in both its philosophical and religious forms -- provided the essential complement to the Confucian order. If [[confucianism|Confucianism]] was the architecture of public life, Taoism was the landscape of the private soul.
