## Overview

The Han dynasty (206 B.C.–A.D. 220) was founded by Liu Chi, also called Liu Pang. He assumed the title of Emperor in 206 B.C. and gave his dynasty the name Han (014-075). He was posthumously named Kao Tsu (014-076).

The dynasty divides into two periods: Western Han (206 B.C.–A.D. 9), capital at Sian, and Later Han (A.D. 25–220), capital at Loyang (019-109). The interregnum of [[wang-mang|Wang Mang]] (A.D. 9–23) separated the two.

> In 206 B.C. Liu Chi assumed the title of Emperor and gave his dynasty the name of the Han Dynasty. (014-075)

The Han marks the beginning of the Chinese Middle Ages (014-077). The preceding [[qin-dynasty|Ch'in dynasty]] was the transition from antiquity. Han institutions became the model for all later dynasties (016-137).

> Under the Han dynasty we meet in China with a new form of state, the "gentry state." The feudalism of ancient times has come definitely to its end. (014-080)

China's majority ethnic group calls itself the "Han people" today (016-059). The dynasty's institutions shaped Chinese civilization for two millennia.

## Founding and Rise

Liu Chi came from a peasant family, not the old nobility (014-087). After the Ch'in First Emperor died, he entered the capital and dethroned the emperor in 206 B.C. (014-065).

He defeated a rival and eighteen other reborn feudal states (014-068).

[[qin-dynasty|Kao Tsu]] then established a mixed administration of feudal fiefs and direct imperial rule (014-106). He recruited officials from the emerging gentry class (014-120).

The empress Lu ruled from 195 to 179 B.C., trying to install her own clan in power (015-162). After her death, her family was exterminated.

Kao Tsu's son Wen Ti then came to the throne (015-168). His frugal reign brought peace and economic growth (016-001).

## Government and Administration

The Han established the framework for Chinese government over two millennia. The emperor was absolute in theory but bound by the law code. He answered to Heaven, which conferred his mandate (016-138).

A court secretariat supervised the ministries as a central statistical authority (017-009). The Han kept separate treasuries for emperor and state — a distinction Europe achieved only in the late Middle Ages (017-089).

Provincial administration was de facto independent of the capital. Governors appointed and discharged their own subordinates (017-111).

This checked centralization. Frontier towns functioned normally even when contact with the capital was lost (017-118).

Around 100 B.C., the Han introduced the examination system for selecting officials (016-026). It operated in principle until 1904.

The exams tested [[confucianism|Confucian]] ideals, not job skills (016-029). Schools for officials' sons spread Confucian thought (016-022).

The philosopher Tung Chung-shu (179–104 B.C.) declared the Confucian classics were books of legal decisions for gentry (016-094).

This created a two-tier system: gentry judged by different rules from commoners (016-105). The [[confucianism|Confucian]] classics functioned as a parallel legal code for the elite, enduring into modern times.

## Economy and Trade

Under Wen Ti, peace and unified territory brought economic growth (016-004). Population and production increased. Coin-minting restrictions were lifted to prevent deflation (016-010).

The gentry moved into cities and living standards rose (016-014).

Emperor Wu Ti (141–86 B.C.) expanded both the military and commerce. The envoy Chang Ch'ien returned from Central Asia in 125 B.C. with reports on western trade routes (017-196).

Chinese campaigns brought Tarim basin states under tribute. The [[silk-road|Silk Road]] came under Chinese protection (017-210).

New crops — grape, peach, pomegranate — entered China from the west (017-214). Korea was conquered in 108 B.C., bringing Japan trade under Chinese influence (018-001).

Wu Ti also brought the Canton region under Chinese dominion (018-023).

Wu Ti's wars proved ruinously expensive. Occupation armies in Turkestan drained the treasury (018-060). Peasants were heavily taxed and conscripted (018-068).

Tribute missions demanded costly silk return gifts, draining national wealth without corresponding economic return (018-083).

The "Discussions on Salt and Iron" debated state economic policy. Sang Hung-yang proposed monopolies and controls. [[confucianism|Confucian]] opponents argued the crisis was a moral failing (018-097).

These debates shaped Chinese economic thought for centuries.

## Decline and Fall

[[wang-mang|Wang Mang]], nephew of the empress dowager, removed rival cliques and placed puppet rulers on the throne (018-136).

In A.D. 8 he declared himself emperor of the Hsin dynasty, using mass petitions and fabricated omens to claim legitimacy (018-151).

His reforms redistributed land, banned private slavery, and imposed state monopolies on wine, salt, iron, and coinage (019-001).

In practice, the measures impoverished the peasants. Officials turned the laws to private gain (019-038).

> Scarcely had the popular rising begun when descendants of the deposed Han dynasty appeared and tried to secure the support of the upper class. (019-088)

The Red Eyebrows rebellion erupted in A.D. 18, a massive peasant insurrection. Vast swarms marched on the capital, killing officials and gentry (019-077).

Wang Mang was killed in A.D. 22, his skull kept in the treasury for two centuries (019-100).

Liu Hsiu, a Han prince, rallied big landowners in Nanyang. In A.D. 24 he became the first emperor of the Later Han, ruling as Kuang-wu Ti (019-109).

The capital moved to Loyang, nearer the wheat regions (019-117). The early Later Han enjoyed economic recovery (019-126).

Internal struggles progressively weakened the Later Han. Eunuchs at court fought against scholar-officials of the gentry (020-066). Provincial generals grew powerful as the central court lost control (020-077).

> Among the new gentry, families entered into alliances with each other... various dynasties were brought to their end by the cliques; this was the fate of the Han dynasty. (018-106)

In 184 the Yellow Turban rebellion broke out. Religious leaders blended Mazdaism and Daoism to mobilize peasants against the gentry order (020-106). The rising fatally weakened central authority (020-144).

By A.D. 200, three rival warlords divided the empire (020-095).

[[three-kingdoms|Ts'ao Ts'ao]] dominated the north, [[chu-ko-liang|Liu Pei]] held Szechwan, and Sun Ts'e controlled the south-east.

In A.D. 220, Ts'ao Ts'ao's son forced the last Han emperor to abdicate, founding the Wei dynasty and starting the [[three-kingdoms|Three Kingdoms]] era (020-161).

Ts'ao Ts'ao settled nineteen tribes of Hsiung-nu within Shansi for military aid. This created a foreign-inhabited realm inside China that shaped the next four centuries (020-156).
