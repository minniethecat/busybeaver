# Section 53

[053-001] 11 _Risings_

[053-002] The emperor might have had a good opportunity for fighting, for his

[053-003] misrule had resulted in a great popular rising which began in the west,

[053-004] in Szechwan, and then spread to the east. As always, the rising was

[053-005] joined by some ruined scholars, and the movement, which had at first

[053-006] been directed against the gentry as such, was turned into a movement

[053-007] against the government of the moment. No longer were all the wealthy and

[053-008] all officials murdered, but only those who did not join the movement. In

[053-009] 1512 the rebels were finally overcome, not so much by any military

[053-010] capacity of the government armies as through the loss of the rebels'

[053-011] fleet of boats in a typhoon.

[053-012] In 1517 a new favourite of the emperor's induced him to make a great

[053-013] tour in the north, to which the favourite belonged. The tour and the

[053-014] hunting greatly pleased the emperor, so that he continued his

[053-015] journeying. This was the year in which the Portuguese Fernão Pires de

[053-016] Andrade landed in Canton--the first modern European to enter China.

[053-017] In 1518 Wang Yang-ming, the philosopher general, crushed a rising in

[053-018] Kiangsi. The rising had been the outcome of years of unrest, which had

[053-019] two causes: native risings of the sort we described above, and loss for

[053-020] the gentry due to the transfer of the capital. The province of Kiangsi

[053-021] was a part of the Yangtze region, and the great landowners there had

[053-022] lived on the profit from their supplies to Nanking. When the capital was

[053-023] moved to Peking, their takings fell. They placed themselves under a

[053-024] prince who lived in Nanking. This prince regarded Wang Yang-ming's move

[053-025] into Kiangsi as a threat to him, and so rose openly against the

[053-026] government and supported the Kiangsi gentry. Wang Yang-ming defeated

[053-027] him, and so came into the highest favour with the incompetent emperor.

[053-028] When peace had been restored in Nanking, the emperor dressed himself up

[053-029] as an army commander, marched south, and made a triumphal entry into

[053-030] Nanking.

[053-031] One other aspect of Wang Yang-ming's expeditions has not yet been

[053-032] studied: he crushed also the so-called salt-merchant rebels in the

[053-033] southernmost part of Kiangsi and adjoining Kwangtung. These

[053-034] merchants-turned-rebels had dominated a small area, off and on since

[053-035] the eleventh century. At this moment, they seem to have had connections

[053-036] with the rich inland merchants of Hsin-an and perhaps also with

[053-037] foreigners. Information is still too scanty to give more details, but a

[053-038] local movement as persistent as this one deserves attention.

[053-039] Wang Yang-ming became acquainted as early as 1519 with the first

[053-040] European rifles, imported by the Portuguese who had landed in 1517. (The

[053-041] Chinese then called them Fu-lan-chi, meaning Franks. Wang was the first

[053-042] Chinese who spoke of the "Franks".) The Chinese had already had mortars

[053-043] which hurled stones, as early as the second century A.D. In the seventh

[053-044] or eighth century their mortars had sent stones of a couple of

[053-045] hundredweights some four hundred yards. There is mention in the eleventh

[053-046] century of cannon which apparently shot with a charge of a sort of

[053-047] gunpowder. The Mongols were already using true cannon in their sieges.

[053-048] In 1519, the first Portuguese were presented to the Chinese emperor in

[053-049] Nanking, where they were entertained for about a year in a hostel, a

[053-050] certain Lin Hsün learned about their rifles and copied them for Wang

[053-051] Yang-ming. In general, however, the Chinese had no respect for the

[053-052] Europeans, whom they described as "bandits" who had expelled the lawful

[053-053] king of Malacca and had now come to China as its representatives. Later

[053-054] they were regarded as a sort of Japanese, because they, too, practiced

[053-055] piracy.

[053-056] 12 _Machiavellism_

[053-057] All main schools of Chinese philosophy were still based on Confucius.

[053-058] Wang Yang-ming's philosophy also followed Confucius, but he liberated

[053-059] himself from the Neo-Confucian tendency as represented by Chu Hsi, which

[053-060] started in the Sung epoch and continued to rule in China in his time and

[053-061] after him; he introduced into Confucian philosophy the conception of

[053-062] "intuition". He regarded intuition as the decisive philosophic

[053-063] experience; only through intuition could man come to true knowledge.

[053-064] This idea shows an element of meditative Buddhism along lines which the

[053-065] philosopher Lu Hsiang-shan (1139-1192) had first developed, while

[053-066] classical Neo-Confucianism was more an integration of monastic Buddhism

[053-067] into Confucianism. Lu had felt himself close to Wang An-shih

[053-068] (1021-1086), and this whole school, representing the small gentry of the

[053-069] Yangtze area, was called the Southern or the Lin-ch'uan school,

[053-070] Lin-ch'uan in Kiangsi being Wang An-shih's home. During the Mongol

[053-071] period, a Taoist group, the _Cheng-i-chiao_ (Correct Unity Sect) had

[053-072] developed in Lin-ch'uan and had accepted some of the Lin-ch'uan

[053-073] school's ideas. Originally, this group was a continuation of Chang

[053-074] Ling's church Taoism. Through the _Cheng-i_ adherents, the Southern

[053-075] school had gained political influence on the despotic Mongol rulers. The

[053-076] despotic Yung-lo emperor had favoured the monk Tao-yen (_c_. 1338-1418)

[053-077] who had also Taoist training and proposed a philosophy which also

[053-078] stressed intuition. He was, incidentally, in charge of the compilation

[053-079] of the largest encyclopaedia ever written, the _Yung-lo ta-tien_

[053-080] commissioned by the Yung-lo emperor.

[053-081] Wang Yang-ming followed the Lin-ch'uan tradition. The introduction of

[053-082] the conception of intuition, a highly subjective conception, into the

[053-083] system of a practical state philosophy like Confucianism could not but

[053-084] lead in the practice of the statesman to Machiavellism. The statesman

[053-085] who followed the teaching of Wang Yang-ming had the opportunity of

[053-086] justifying whatever he did by his intuition.

[053-087] Wang Yang-ming failed to gain acceptance for his philosophy. His

[053-088] disciples also failed to establish his doctrine in China, because it

[053-089] served the interests of an individual despot against those of the gentry

[053-090] as a class, and the middle class, which might have formed a

[053-091] counterweight against them, was not yet politically ripe for the seizure

[053-092] of the opportunity here offered to it. In Japan, however, Wang's

[053-093] doctrine gained many followers, because it admirably served the

[053-094] dictatorial state system which had developed in that country.

[053-095] Incidentally, Chiang Kai-shek in those years in which he showed Fascist

[053-096] tendencies, also got interested in Wang Yang-ming.

[053-097] 13 _Foreign relations in the sixteenth century_

[053-098] The feeble emperor Wu Tsung died in 1521, after an ineffective reign,

[053-099] without leaving an heir. The clique then in power at court looked among

[053-100] the possible pretenders for the one who seemed least likely to do

[053-101] anything, and their choice fell on the fifteen-year-old Shih Tsung, who

[053-102] was made emperor. The forty-five years of his reign were filled in home

[053-103] affairs with intrigues between the cliques at court, with growing

[053-104] distress in the country, and with revolts on a larger and larger scale.

[053-105] Abroad there were wars with Annam, increasing raids by the Japanese,

[053-106] and, above all, long-continued fighting against the famous Mongol ruler

[053-107] Yen-ta, from 1549 onward. At one time Yen-ta reached Peking and laid

[053-108] siege to it. The emperor, who had no knowledge of affairs, and to whom

[053-109] Yen-ta had been represented as a petty bandit, was utterly dismayed and

[053-110] ready to do whatever Yen-ta asked; in the end he was dissuaded from

[053-111] this, and an agreement was arrived at with Yen-ta for state-controlled

[053-112] markets to be set up along the frontier, where the Mongols could

[053-113] dispose of their goods against Chinese goods on very favourable terms.

[053-114] After further difficulties lasting many years, a compromise was arrived

[053-115] at: the Mongols were earning good profits from the markets, and in 1571

[053-116] Yen-ta accepted a Chinese title. On the Chinese side, this Mongol trade,

[053-117] which continued in rather different form in the Manchu epoch, led to the

[053-118] formation of a local merchant class in the frontier province of Shansi,

[053-119] with great experience in credit business; later the first Chinese

[053-120] bankers came almost entirely from this quarter.

[053-121] After a brief interregnum there came once more to the throne a

[053-122] ten-year-old boy, the emperor Shen Tsung (reign name Wan-li; 1573-1619).

[053-123] He, too, was entirely under the influence of various cliques, at first

[053-124] that of his tutor, the scholar Chang Chü-chan. About the time of the

[053-125] death, in 1582, of Yen-ta we hear for the first time of a new people. In

[053-126] 1581 there had been unrest in southern Manchuria. The Mongolian tribal

[053-127] federation of the Tümet attacked China, and there resulted collisions

[053-128] not only with the Chinese but between the different tribes living there.

[053-129] In southern and central Manchuria were remnants of the Tungus Juchên.

[053-130] The Mongols had subjugated the Juchên, but the latter had virtually

[053-131] become independent after the collapse of Mongol rule over China. They

[053-132] had formed several tribal alliances, but in 1581-83 these fought each

[053-133] other, so that one of the alliances to all intents was destroyed. The

[053-134] Chinese intervened as mediators in these struggles, and drew a

[053-135] demarcation line between the territories of the various Tungus tribes.

[053-136] All this is only worth mention because it was from these tribes that

[053-137] there developed the tribal league of the Manchus, who were then to rule

[053-138] China for some three hundred years.

[053-139] In 1592 the Japanese invaded Korea. This was their first real effort to

[053-140] set foot on the continent, a purely imperialistic move. Korea, as a

[053-141] Chinese vassal, appealed for Chinese aid. At first the Chinese army had

[053-142] no success, but in 1598 the Japanese were forced to abandon Korea. They

[053-143] revenged themselves by intensifying their raids on the coast of central

[053-144] China; they often massacred whole towns, and burned down the looted

[053-145] houses. The fighting in Korea had its influence on the Tungus tribes: as

[053-146] they were not directly involved, it contributed to their further

[053-147] strengthening.

[053-148] The East India Company was founded in 1600. At this time, while the

[053-149] English were trying to establish themselves in India, the Chinese tried

[053-150] to gain increased influence in the south by wars in Annam, Burma, and

[053-151] Thailand (1594-1604). These wars were for China colonial wars, similar

[053-152] to the colonial fighting by the British in India. But there began to be

[053-153] defined already at that time in the south of Asia the outlines of the

[053-154] states as they exist at the present time.

[053-155] In 1601 the first European, the Jesuit Matteo Ricci, succeeded in

[053-156] gaining access to the Chinese court, through the agency of a eunuch. He

[053-157] made some presents, and the Chinese regarded his visit as a mission from

[053-158] Europe bringing tribute. Ricci was therefore permitted to remain in

[053-159] Peking. He was an astronomer and was able to demonstrate to his Chinese

[053-160] colleagues the latest achievements of European astronomy. In 1613, after

[053-161] Ricci's death, the Jesuits and some Chinese whom they had converted were

[053-162] commissioned to reform the Chinese calendar. In the time of the Mongols,

[053-163] Arabs had been at work in Peking as astronomers, and their influence had

[053-164] continued under the Ming until the Europeans came. By his astronomical

[053-165] labours Ricci won a place of honour in Chinese literature; he is the

[053-166] European most often mentioned.

[053-167] The missionary work was less effective. The missionaries penetrated by

[053-168] the old trade routes from Canton and Macao into the province of Kiangsi

[053-169] and then into Nanking. Kiangsi and Nanking were their chief centres.

[053-170] They soon realized that missionary activity that began in the lower

[053-171] strata would have no success; it was necessary to work from above,

[053-172] beginning with the emperor, and then, they hoped, the whole country

[053-173] could be converted to Christianity. When later the emperors of the Ming

[053-174] dynasty were expelled and fugitives in South China, one of the

[053-175] pretenders to the throne was actually converted--but it was politically

[053-176] too late. The missionaries had, moreover, mistaken ideas as to the

[053-177] nature of Chinese religion; we know today that a universal adoption of

[053-178] Christianity in China would have been impossible even if an emperor had

[053-179] personally adopted that foreign faith: there were emperors who had been

[053-180] interested in Buddhism or in Taoism, but that had been their private

[053-181] affair and had never prevented them, as heads of the state, from

[053-182] promoting the religious system which politically was the most

[053-183] expedient--that is to say, usually Confucianism. What we have said here

[053-184] in regard to the Christian mission at the Ming court is applicable also

[053-185] to the missionaries at the court of the first Manchu emperors, in the

[053-186] seventeenth century. Early in the eighteenth century missionary activity

[053-187] was prohibited--not for religious but for political reasons, and only

[053-188] under the pressure of the Capitulations in the nineteenth century were

[053-189] the missionaries enabled to resume their labours.
