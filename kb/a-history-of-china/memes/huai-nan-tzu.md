## Overview

[[huai-nan-tzu|Huai-nan Tzu]] (literally "the Master of [[hsiung-nu|Hu]]ai-nan") was the honorific title of Liu An, a Han-dynasty prince who lived in the second century BCE.

He was the grandson of the founding Han emperor [[kao-tsu|Liu Pang]] and ruled the kingdom of [[hsiung-nu|Hu]]ai-nan.

Among the intellectual figures of the [[han-dynasty|Han dynasty]], his work stands as one of only three books of major importance, alongside the writings of [[tung-chung-shu|Tung Chung-shu]] and [[wang-chung|Wang Ch'ung]].

> Liu An called _Huai-nan Tz[)u]_. Prince Liu An occupied himself with Taoism and allied problems, gathered around him scholars of different schools, and carried on discussions with them. [021-053]

## The Huai-nan Tzu Text

The book attributed to him, also called the _Huai-nan Tzu_, is a compendium of knowledge covering cosmology, philosophy, governance, and natural science.

It synthesizes Taoist thought with elements from other schools including [[confucianism|Confucianism]] and Legalism:

> Many of his writings are lost, but enough is extant to show that he was one of the earliest Chinese alchemists. [021-056]

The surviving text, compiled with the assistance of the scholars he patronized, reflects the eclectic intellectual culture of the early Han court.

## Alchemy and the Search for Immortality

Huai-nan Tzu's significance in the history of science lies in his pioneering role in Chinese alchemy:

> The question has not yet been settled, but it is probable that alchemy first appeared in China, together with the cult of the "art" of prolonging life, and was later carried to the West, where it flourished among the Arabs and in medieval Europe. [021-058]

His investigations into alchemy were closely tied to Taoist conceptions of immortality.

This fusion of philosophical [[taoism|Taoism]] with practical alchemy would prove enormously influential on later Chinese religious and scientific traditions.

## Place in the Taoist Tradition

Huai-nan Tzu belongs to a lineage of Taoist thinkers that spans several centuries, extending from the classical period of [[lao-tzu|Lao Tzu]] and [[chuang-tzu|Chuang Tzu]] through the fourth century CE:

> A thin thread extends from them as far as the fourth century A.D.: Huai-nan Tz[)u], Chung-ch'ang T'ung, Yüan Chi, Liu Ling, and T'ao Ch'ien, are some of the most eminent names of Taoist philosophers. [010-097]

His position in this tradition places him as a bridge figure who took the philosophical insights of the early Taoist masters into new domains of practical inquiry.

He is remembered alongside philosophers like [[hsun-tzu|Hsün Tzu]], [[mencius|Mencius]], and [[mo-ti|Mo Ti]] as one of the great minds of classical China.

## Legacy

Although many of his original writings are lost, Huai-nan Tzu's influence persisted through the transmitted portions of his work.

His synthesis of [[taoism|Taoist philosophy]] with systematic inquiry into nature anticipated later developments in Chinese thought and science.

The _Huai-nan Tzu_ remains an essential text for scholars studying the intellectual history of the Han dynasty.
