## Overview

[[yuan-shih-kai|Yüan Shih-k'ai]] (1859–1916) was the most powerful military-political figure of China's transition from empire to republic. As a general of the [[ching-dynasty|Ch'ing dynasty]], he commanded the modernised Peiyang Army; as provisional president of the new Republic, he outmanoeuvred both the revolutionary faction and the Manchu court; and in the final year of his life, he attempted to crown himself emperor — a move that destroyed his authority and plunged China into a decade of warlord fragmentation. No single figure better illustrates the tension between reform, authoritarianism, and revolution that defined China's early twentieth century [060-080].

## The Ch'ing General

Yüan Shih-k'ai rose to prominence during the closing decades of the Ch'ing dynasty as one of the empire's most effective military modernisers. Stationed at Tientsin, he trained and commanded the Peiyang Army — a German-drilled, Western-equipped force that became the strongest military formation in north China and the personal power base on which his entire career would rest [060-080].

> General Yüan Shih-k'ai, who was then stationed at Tientsin, was the commander of the modern-trained Peiyang Army. [060-080]

When the Empress Dowager Tz'u-hsi purged the reform movement in 1898, Yüan Shih-k'ai's loyalty to the throne — and his betrayal of the reformers — secured him her favour. But his very success made him a threat. After the death of Tz'u-hsi in 1908, the new regent, Prince Ch'un, dismissed Yüan Shih-k'ai from all his posts, fearing his independent power [060-131][060-133].

> The regent felt that the dynasty could not maintain itself if it allowed Yüan Shih-k'ai, the man who had the only effective army in north China, to remain in a position of command. [060-131–060-133]

## The 1911 Revolution and the Presidency

The dismissal proved temporary. When the [[wu-han-rebellion]] broke out in October 1911, the dynasty discovered that the Peiyang Army would obey no commander but Yüan Shih-k'ai. In desperation, the court recalled him and appointed him premier. Yüan now held a hand of unmatched strength: he could deliver the abdication of the Manchu house to the revolutionaries — or crush the revolution for the dynasty — and he negotiated with both sides simultaneously [060-187].

> Yüan Shih-k'ai was recalled on November 10th, 1911, and appointed premier. [060-187]

The outcome was a compromise that made Yüan Shih-k'ai the first president of the Republic of China in February 1912, replacing [[sun-yat-sen|Sun Yat-sen]], who had held the provisional presidency at Nanking. The Manchu abdication followed within days. Yüan now commanded the strongest army, occupied the capital at [[peking|Peking]], and held the legal title of head of state [061-022][061-033].

> The National Assembly in Nanking elected Yüan Shih-k'ai as president. [061-033]

## The Monarchical Attempt and Fall

Yüan Shih-k'ai's presidency grew steadily more authoritarian. He dissolved parliament, revised the constitution to concentrate power in his own hands, and suppressed the [[kuomintang]] opposition led by [[sun-yat-sen|Sun Yat-sen]]. By 1915, he had concluded that only a restoration of monarchy — with himself as emperor of a new "[[hsiung-nu|Hu]]ng-hsien" dynasty — could provide the centralisation China required [062-127][062-129].

> There then came, in the middle of 1915, an open monarchical move centred upon Yüan Shih-k'ai. [062-127]

The monarchical gambit proved catastrophic. Provincial governors whom Yüan believed he controlled declared independence one by one. Foreign governments, whose loans he needed, refused to recognise the new empire. Within months, Yüan was forced to cancel the enthronement plans. He died in June 1916, a broken man, leaving behind a fragmented military command that would devolve into the warlord era [063-001][063-013].

> Meanwhile Yüan Shih-k'ai had made all preparations for his enthronement, but in the spring of 1916 province after province declared its independence. [063-001]
