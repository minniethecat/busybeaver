## Overview

[[Cheng Ho]] was a [[ming-dynasty|Ming dynasty]] [[eunuch]] admiral whose seven naval expeditions (1405–1433) represented the high-water mark of pre-modern Chinese maritime power. Commissioned by the [[yung-lo|Yung-lo Emperor]], he commanded a vast fleet that sailed past Indo-China to the coast of Arabia, re-establishing trade connections disrupted by the collapse of Mongol rule and bringing China recognition as a naval power throughout southern Asia [052-029].

## Biography

Cheng Ho served as a eunuch in the court of the [[yung-lo|Yung-lo Emperor]], who entrusted him with building a fleet for southward expansion. Cheng Ho not only promoted the plan for naval expeditions but also personally carried them out [052-025]. His first and most famous mission began in 1405, directed towards Indo-China. The expedition was envisaged as giving moral support to concurrent land operations in Yünnan and Annam, but was also intended to renew trade connections that had been interrupted by the collapse of Mongol rule [052-026].

> Cheng Ho, who had promoted the plan and also carried it out, began in 1405 his famous mission to Indo-China, which had been envisaged as giving at least moral support to the land operations, but was also intended to renew trade connections with Indo-China, where they had been interrupted by the collapse of Mongol rule. [052-025–052-029]

Cheng Ho sailed well beyond his original destination, past Indo-China and ultimately to the coast of Arabia. His own account of the voyage became an important source of information about conditions in southern Asia in the early fifteenth century [052-029].

## The Expeditions

Cheng Ho and his fleet made several further cruises after the initial 1405 mission, though they were eventually discontinued. Several factors contributed to this decision [052-033]:

- **Economic cost**: As state enterprises, the expeditions were expensive. Foreign goods could be obtained more cheaply if foreign merchants came to China or Chinese merchants travelled at their own risk [052-035].
- **Moral success achieved**: China had been recognised as a power throughout southern Asia, and Annam had been reconquered [052-038].
- **Changed geopolitical conditions**: After the death of the Mongol emperor Timur in 1406, no great power remained in Central Asia, and overland trade missions from Persia were able to reach China directly [052-040].
- **Japanese piracy**: The fleet, stationed in the [[yangtze-river|Yangtze]] region rather than South China, would have required permanent protection against [[japan|Japanese]] attacks. By 1411 the Grand Canal had been repaired, and from 1415 all traffic moved by canal, evading the Japanese peril and ending this short chapter of Chinese naval history [052-045].

## Cultural Legacy

Cheng Ho's voyages produced one lasting cultural result: a large number of fairy-tales from the Middle East — especially Persia — were brought to China by sea during his time [052-049]. These stories, already transformed by Persian storytellers from their Indian Buddhist origins, found a home in the south-eastern coastal provinces of China in forms that closely resembled the versions that had travelled westward to Europe [052-072].

> These travels of Cheng Ho seem to have had one more cultural result: a large number of fairy-tales from the Middle East were brought to China, or at all events reached China at that time. [052-049–052-051]

## Legacy

Cheng Ho's expeditions demonstrated China's capacity for long-range naval power projection in the early fifteenth century, but their discontinuation marked a turning point. The shift to canal-based transport and the inward turn of Ming policy after Yung-lo's reign meant that China would not again mount comparable maritime ventures. Cheng Ho remains a singular figure: the eunuch admiral who briefly made the Ming dynasty a recognised naval power across the Indian Ocean.
