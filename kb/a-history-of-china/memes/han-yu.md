## Overview

[[han-yu|Han Yü]] (768–825) was the most celebrated prose writer of the [[tang-dynasty|T'ang dynasty]] and a prominent Confucian thinker.

Along with his contemporary Liu Tsung-yüan, he led the Classical Prose Movement (_ku-wen yün-tung_), which sought to replace the ornate parallel prose of the Six Dynasties period with a direct, plain style modeled on pre-Han classics.

> The essayists, of whom Han Yü (768–825) and Liu Tsung-yüan (747–796) call for special mention. [037-059]

## The Classical Prose Movement

Han Yü championed a return to what he called "ancient prose" (_ku-wen_) — the straightforward, unadorned style of classical Confucian texts.

This was both a literary reform and a moral program: he believed that clear prose was essential for transmitting Confucian ethical principles effectively.

> An-shih and his followers preferred the rigid classic style of Han Yü [043-164]

His influence on prose style extended well beyond his own era, shaping the literary standards of later dynasties including the [[sung-dynasty|Sung]].

## Defense of Confucianism

Han Yü is perhaps most famous for his polemical defense of [[confucianism|Confucianism]] against Buddhism and [[taoism|Taoism]].

> A group of men was formed which found its clearest formulation by Han Yü: some [044-014]

He argued that Buddhism was a foreign religion incompatible with Chinese culture and governance.

His "Memorial on the Bone of Buddha" — a scathing criticism of imperial patronage of Buddhism — nearly cost him his life when it offended the emperor.

## Philosophical Significance

Han Yü played a key role in the Confucian revival that anticipated the rise of [[confucianism|Neo-Confucianism]] in the Sung dynasty.

> Han Yü, philosopher [073-399]

He reasserted the centrality of the Confucian classics, emphasized the transmission of orthodox teachings (_tao-t'ung_), and provided a foundation upon which later thinkers like [[chu-hsi|Chu Hsi]] would build.

His conception of an unbroken lineage of Confucian sages, from the ancient kings through [[mencius|Mencius]], became a core element of Neo-Confucian identity.

## Literary Style and Influence

Han Yü's prose was characterized by directness, vigor, and moral seriousness.

He rejected the elaborate parallelism and allusive ornamentation that had dominated Chinese prose for centuries, arguing that form should serve content rather than display the author's erudition.

His essays, letters, and memorials became models studied by examination candidates for generations after his death.
