## Overview

Chu Tê (1886–1976) was the principal military commander of the Chinese Communist Party from the founding of the Red Army through the establishment of the People's Republic. While [[mao-tse-tung|Mao Tse-tung]] provided the political and strategic direction, Chu Tê built and led the military forces that fought the Kuomintang, the Japanese, and ultimately prevailed in the Chinese Civil War. His combination of military professionalism and political loyalty made him an indispensable figure in the Communist revolution.

> Chu Tê belonged to a communist group organized in Germany. [064-175]

## Early Career and Military Training

Chu Tê came from a peasant family in Szechwan province. He began his military career as a warlord officer in Yunnan, rising to the rank of brigadier general. Disillusioned with warlord politics, he traveled to Europe in 1922, where he studied in Germany and joined the Chinese Communist Party through contacts with [[chou-en-lai|Chou En-lai]] and other Chinese communist students in Berlin.

His European experience exposed him to Marxist theory and modern military organization. Upon returning to China in 1926, Chu Tê participated in the Northern Expedition before joining the Communist uprising in Nan-ch'ang on August 1, 1927 — an event later commemorated as the founding date of the People's Liberation Army.

## Red Army Commander

Following the failure of the early urban uprisings, Chu Tê led his remaining forces to the Ching-kang Mountains in Kiangsi, where he joined forces with [[mao-tse-tung|Mao Tse-tung]] in 1928. The partnership between Chu Tê (military command) and [[mao-tse-tung|Mao Tse-tung]] (political direction) became the foundation of the Red Army's success. Their combined forces developed the guerrilla warfare tactics — "the enemy advances, we retreat; the enemy camps, we harass; the enemy tires, we attack; the enemy retreats, we pursue" — that sustained the Communist movement through its darkest periods.

> Chu Tê, as commander-in-chief, led the Red Army through the Long March and the anti-Japanese war. [073-219]

Chu Tê commanded the Red Army during the Long March (1934–1935) and served as commander-in-chief of the Eighth Route Army during the Sino-Japanese War (1937–1945). During the Civil War (1946–1949), he remained the titular commander-in-chief, though operational command increasingly fell to younger generals like Lin Piao and P'eng Tê-huai.

## Later Years

After 1949, Chu Tê held the position of Commander-in-Chief of the People's Liberation Army and served as Vice Chairman of the People's Republic. He was one of the Ten Marshals appointed in 1955. During the Cultural Revolution, he was targeted by Red Guards but survived politically, unlike many of his comrades. He died in 1976, the same year as [[mao-tse-tung|Mao Tse-tung]] and [[chou-en-lai|Chou En-lai]], marking the end of the revolutionary generation's leadership.
