## Overview

[[han-fei-tzu|Han Fei Tzu]] was the foremost theorist of the [[legalism|Legalist]] school of Chinese philosophy during the late [[warring-states-period|Warring States period]]. A prince of the Han state and a student of the Confucian philosopher [[hsun-tzu|Hsün Tzu]], Han Fei Tzu broke with his teacher's tradition to develop a rigorous philosophy of law, state power, and administrative technique that would profoundly influence the [[ch'in-dynasty|Ch'in dynasty]] unification under [[shih-huang-ti|Shih Huang-ti]].

## Philosophical Development

Han Fei Tzu built upon earlier Legalist thinkers, particularly Shang Tzu and Shen Pu-hai, while incorporating elements from the Daoist tradition:

> These ideas were further developed by Han Fei Tzu (died 233 BCE), who systematized Legalist thought into its most complete and influential form... [012-100]

His innovation was to synthesize three strands of statecraft — law (fa), administrative method (shu), and political power (shih) — into a unified system. Unlike earlier Legalists who emphasized one element at the expense of others, Han Fei Tzu argued that all three were necessary for effective governance.

## Core Ideas

Han Fei Tzu's philosophy centered on the conviction that human nature was fundamentally self-interested and that only clear laws with predictable rewards and punishments could produce social order:

> Philosophers of our period worked within the framework established by earlier thinkers. Han Fei Tzu, in particular, developed a comprehensive theory of how the ruler should govern through law rather than personal virtue. [069-048]

He rejected the Confucian ideal of rule by moral example, arguing that even a mediocre ruler could govern effectively if the institutional framework was sound. This pragmatic, institutional approach to political power distinguished Legalism from all other schools of classical Chinese thought.

## Influence and Death

Han Fei Tzu's ideas attracted the attention of the Ch'in king who would become the First Emperor:

> Kung-sun Lung, Shang Tzu, Han Fei Tzu, Tsou Yen — the Legalists provided the ideological foundation for Ch'in unification... [074-066]

Despite — or because of — his influence, Han Fei Tzu died under ambiguous circumstances at the Ch'in court in 233 BCE, reportedly poisoned by his former classmate Li Ssu, who saw him as a rival for the king's favor. His writings survived, however, preserved in the text that bears his name, the *Han Fei Tzu*:

> Liao, *The Complete Works of Han Fei Tzu*, remains the authoritative translation and study of his surviving corpus. [069-049]

## Legacy

Han Fei Tzu's influence extended far beyond his own time. The Ch'in dynasty implemented many of his principles with brutal consistency, and while the Han dynasty officially adopted [[confucianism|Confucianism]], it retained Legalist administrative techniques in practice:

> Later Chuang Tzu and Han Fei Tzu were studied alongside Confucian texts, reflecting the enduring relevance of Legalist statecraft even under Confucian orthodoxy. [068-160]

The tension he identified — between rule by law and rule by virtue, between institutional design and personal morality — has persisted throughout Chinese political thought and remains central to understanding Chinese governance traditions.
