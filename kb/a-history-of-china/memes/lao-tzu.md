## Overview

Lao Tzu (Chinese: 老子, literally "Old Master") is the legendary founder of [[taoism|Taoism]] and one of the most influential figures in Chinese intellectual history. Traditionally placed in the sixth century BC as an older contemporary of [[confucius|Confucius]], modern scholarship dates him to the latter part of the fourth century BC, during the Warring States period [009-078]. His teachings are preserved in the _Tao Tê Ching_ (道德经, "Book of the World Law and its Power"), a short but profoundly ambiguous text that has become one of the most translated works in world literature [009-075].

Eberhard notes that while [[confucius|Confucius]] enjoyed preeminence in eighteenth-century Europe, the twentieth century — marked by its own "internal crisis" — saw Lao Tzu steadily advance in Western esteem, with his book translated "almost a hundred times into various European languages" [009-073]. Unlike [[confucius|Confucius]]'s rationalistic program of social rules, Lao Tzu offered an intuitive, emotional path to harmony with the universe, one that resonated with those disillusioned by the failures of organized society [009-100].

The central concept of Lao Tzu's thought is the _Tao_ (道), the universal law or way that underlies all existence. From this flows his most distinctive doctrine: _wu-wei_ (无为), often translated as "non-action" or "passive achievement" — the idea that the highest form of action is that which accords so completely with the natural order that it requires no forced effort [009-136].

## Life and Background

Virtually nothing reliable is known about Lao Tzu's life. The oldest biography, written around 100 BC by the historian Ssu-ma Ch'ien, relates that he served as an official at the Chou court and, growing weary of political life, withdrew to his country estate where he died in old age [009-080]. Eberhard cautions that even this sparse account "may be legendary, but it fits well into the picture given to us by Lao Tzŭ's teaching and by the life of his later followers" [009-084].

From the second century AD onward — at least four hundred years after his purported death — legends multiplied concerning his westward journey. Later narratives placed him in Turkestan, where a temple was actually constructed in his honor during the Medieval period; other traditions carried him as far as India or Sogdiana (Samarkand and Bokhara), where he was sometimes identified as the teacher or forerunner of the Buddha, and at other times of Mani, the founder of Manichaeism [009-087]. Eberhard dismisses these accounts bluntly: "For all this there is not a vestige of documentary evidence" [009-092].

> "According to the general view among the Chinese, Lao Tzŭ was an older contemporary of Confucius; recent Chinese and Western research (A. Waley; H.H. Dubs) has contested this view and places Lao Tzŭ in the latter part of the fourth century B.C., or even later." [009-075]

The story of Lao Tzu's final departure, preserved in Ssu-ma Ch'ien's biography, is itself a literary crystallization of Taoist ideals rather than a historical record: sensing the Chou dynasty's decline, he mounted a water buffalo and rode toward the western frontier. At the pass, the gatekeeper Yin Hsi, recognizing the departing sage, begged him to leave his teachings in writing before vanishing. The resulting five thousand characters became the _Tao Tê Ching_ — after which Lao Tzu passed through the gate and was never seen again.

## Teachings and Philosophy

### The Tao and the Tao Tê Ching

Lao Tzu's entire known teaching is contained in a single small book, the _Tao Tê Ching_. Eberhard describes it as written "in quite simple language, at times in rhyme, but the sense is so vague that countless versions, differing radically from each other, can be based on it, and just as many translations are possible, all philologically defensible" [009-094]. This vagueness, he argues, is no accident: "Lao Tzŭ is trying to express something for which the language of his day was inadequate; and what he wanted to express belonged to the emotional, not the intellectual, side of the human character, so that any perfectly clear expression of it in words was entirely impossible" [009-108].

The book's title couples two key concepts: _Tao_ (道), the cosmic way or principle, and _Tê_ (德), its power or virtue as manifested in the world. The core project of the text is "an effort to bring man's life on earth into harmony with the life and law of the universe" [009-100] — a goal shared with [[confucius|Confucius]], but pursued through radically different means.

### Wu-wei: The Doctrine of Non-Action

> "The true Taoist withdraws also from his family. Typical of this is another story, surely apocryphal, from Chuang Tzŭ (Ch. 3, 3). At the death of Lao Tzŭ a disciple went to the family and expressed his sympathy quite briefly and formally. The other disciples were astonished, and asked his reason. He said: 'Yes, at first I thought that he was our man, but he is not. When I went to grieve, the old men were bewailing him as though they were bewailing a son, and the young wept as though they were mourning a mother. To bind them so closely to himself, he must have spoken words which he should not have spoken, and wept tears which he should not have wept. That, however, is a falling away from the heavenly nature.'" [010-002]

While [[confucius|Confucius]] sought harmony through the meticulous observance of social rules and rituals — "a sort of primitive scientific way, by laying down a number of rules of human conduct" — Lao Tzu "tries to attain his ideal by an intuitive, emotional method" [009-104]. The Taoist does not struggle to reform society; he withdraws from it.

The follower of Lao Tzu "withdraws from all social life, and carries out none of the rites and ceremonies which a man of the upper class should observe throughout the day. He lives in self-imposed seclusion, in an elaborate primitivity" [009-125]. This withdrawal is not mere escapism but a positive spiritual discipline: by stripping away the artificial accretions of civilization, the Taoist aligns his own nature with the Tao itself. "His own nature, like everything else, represents for him a part of the Tao, and the task of the individual consists in the most complete adherence to the Tao that is conceivable, as far as possible performing no act that runs counter to the Tao. This is the main element of Lao Tzŭ's doctrine, the doctrine of _wu-wei_, 'passive achievement'" [009-132].

### Political Philosophy

Lao Tzu extended his principle of non-action to the art of government. He assumed "that an ideal life in society was possible if everyone followed his own nature entirely and no artificial restrictions were imposed" [009-139]. His political vision was radically minimalist, skeptical of law, technology, and the apparatus of state power:

> "The more the people are forbidden to do this and that, the poorer will they be. The more sharp weapons the people possess, the more will darkness and bewilderment spread through the land. The more craft and cunning men have, the more useless and pernicious contraptions will they invent." [009-141]

Eberhard characterizes this as an "individualist and anarchist doctrine" that "was not suited to become a state philosophy" — in contrast to [[confucianism|Confucianism]], which proved an ideal instrument for imperial administration [010-083]. Yet Lao Tzu's political thought has exerted a subterranean influence throughout Chinese history, always available as a resource for those seeking an alternative to the official Confucian orthodoxy.

## Influence and Legacy

Lao Tzu's influence unfolded along two divergent tracks. On one hand, [[taoism|Taoism]] developed into an organized religion with a priesthood, pantheon, temples, and liturgical practices — a transformation that "was certainly not in the spirit of Lao Tzŭ" [010-085]. The deification of Lao Tzu himself, who came to be worshipped as a divine being (T'ai-shang Lao-chün, "The Most High Lord Lao"), represents perhaps the most dramatic departure from the anti-institutional thrust of his original teaching [010-085].

On the other hand, Lao Tzu's philosophy continued to shape the inner lives of the Chinese educated elite. Eberhard describes how "it became the rule for one and the same person to be both Confucian and Taoist" — as an official and head of a family, a man would think and act as a Confucian; "as a private individual, when he had retired far from the city to live in his country mansion, or when he had been dismissed from his post or suffered some other trouble, he would feel and think as a Taoist" [010-015]. This dual orientation — public [[confucianism|Confucianism]] and private [[taoism|Taoism]] — became one of the most durable patterns in Chinese cultural history.

The appeal of Lao Tzu's thought extended far beyond China. In the modern West, his rejection of instrumental rationality, his ecological sensibility avant la lettre, and his therapeutic quietism have found receptive audiences across multiple cultural moments — from the counterculture of the 1960s to contemporary mindfulness movements. Each generation has discovered its own Lao Tzu in the "deliberate vagueness" of the _Tao Tê Ching_ [009-099].
