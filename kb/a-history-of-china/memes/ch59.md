# Section 59

[059-001] While in the central provinces the T'ai P'ing rebellion was raging,

[059-002] China was suffering grave setbacks owing to the Lorcha War of 1856; and

[059-003] there were also great and serious risings in other parts of the country.

[059-004] In 1855 the Yellow River had changed its course, entering the sea once

[059-005] more at Tientsin, to the great loss of the regions of Honan and Anhui.

[059-006] In these two central provinces the peasant rising of the so-called "Nien

[059-007] Fei" had begun, but it only became formidable after 1855, owing to the

[059-008] increasing misery of the peasants. This purely peasant revolt was not

[059-009] suppressed by the Manchu government until 1868, after many collisions.

[059-010] Then, however, there began the so-called "Mohammedan risings". Here

[059-011] there are, in all, five movements to distinguish: (1) the Mohammedan

[059-012] rising in Kansu (1864-5); (2) the Salar movement in Shensi; (3) the

[059-013] Mohammedan revolt in Yünnan (1855-1873); (4) the rising in Kansu (1895);

[059-014] (5) the rebellion of Yakub Beg in Turkestan (from 1866 onward).

[059-015] While we are fairly well informed about the other popular risings of

[059-016] this period, the Mohammedan revolts have not yet been well studied. We

[059-017] know from unofficial accounts that these risings were suppressed with

[059-018] great brutality. To this day there are many Mohammedans in, for

[059-019] instance, Yünnan, but the revolt there is said to have cost a million

[059-020] lives. The figures all rest on very rough estimates: in Kansu the

[059-021] population is said to have fallen from fifteen millions to one million;

[059-022] the Turkestan revolt is said to have cost ten million lives. There are

[059-023] no reliable statistics; but it is understandable that at that time the

[059-024] population of China must have fallen considerably, especially if we bear

[059-025] in mind the equally ferocious suppression of the risings of the T'ai

[059-026] P'ing and the Nien Fei within China, and smaller risings of which we

[059-027] have made no mention.

[059-028] The Mohammedan risings were not elements of a general Mohammedan revolt,

[059-029] but separate events only incidentally connected with each other. The

[059-030] risings had different causes. An important factor was the general

[059-031] distress in China. This was partly due to the fact that the officials

[059-032] were exploiting the peasant population more ruthlessly than ever. In

[059-033] addition to this, owing to the national feeling which had been aroused

[059-034] in so unfortunate a way, the Chinese felt a revulsion against

[059-035] non-Chinese, such as the Salars, who were of Turkish race. Here there

[059-036] were always possibilities of friction, which might have been removed

[059-037] with a little consideration but which swelled to importance through the

[059-038] tactless behaviour of Chinese officials. Finally there came divisions

[059-039] among the Mohammedans of China which led to fighting between themselves.

[059-040] All these risings were marked by two characteristics. They had no

[059-041] general political aim such as the founding of a great and universal

[059-042] Islamic state. Separate states were founded, but they were too small to

[059-043] endure; they would have needed the protection of great states. But they

[059-044] were not moved by any pan-Islamic idea. Secondly, they all took place on

[059-045] Chinese soil, and all the Mohammedans involved, except in the rising of

[059-046] the Salars, were Chinese. These Chinese who became Mohammedans are

[059-047] called Dungans. The Dungans are, of course, no longer pure Chinese,

[059-048] because Chinese who have gone over to Islam readily form mixed

[059-049] marriages with Islamic non-Chinese, that is to say with Turks and

[059-050] Mongols.

[059-051] The revolt, however, of Yakub Beg in Turkestan had a quite different

[059-052] character. Yakub Beg (his Chinese name was An Chi-yeh) had risen to the

[059-053] Chinese governorship when he made himself ruler of Kashgar. In 1866 he

[059-054] began to try to make himself independent of Chinese control. He

[059-055] conquered Ili, and then in a rapid campaign made himself master of all

[059-056] Turkestan.

[059-057] His state had a much better prospect of endurance than the other

[059-058] Mohammedan states. He had full control of it from 1874. Turkestan was

[059-059] connected with China only by the few routes that led between the desert

[059-060] and the Tibetan mountains. The state was supported against China by

[059-061] Russia, which was continually pressing eastward, and in the south by

[059-062] Great Britain, which was pressing towards Tibet. Farther west was the

[059-063] great Ottoman empire; the attempt to gain direct contact with it was not

[059-064] hopeless in itself, and this was recognized at Istanbul. Missions went

[059-065] to and fro, and Turkish officers came to Yakub Beg and organized his

[059-066] army; Yakub Beg recognized the Turkish sultan as Khalif. He also

[059-067] concluded treaties with Russia and Great Britain. But in spite of all

[059-068] this he was unable to maintain his hold of Turkestan. In 1877 the famous

[059-069] Chinese general Tso Tsung-t'ang (1812-1885), who had fought against the

[059-070] T'ai P'ing and also against the Mohammedans in Kansu, marched into

[059-071] Turkestan and ended Yakub Beg's rule.

[059-072] Yakub was defeated, however, not so much by Chinese superiority as by a

[059-073] combination of circumstances. In order to build up his kingdom he was

[059-074] compelled to impose heavy taxation, and this made him unpopular with his

[059-075] own followers: they had to pay taxes under the Chinese, but the Chinese

[059-076] collection had been much less rigorous than that of Yakub Beg. It was

[059-077] technically impossible for the Ottoman empire to give him any aid, even

[059-078] had its internal situation permitted it. Britain and Russia would

[059-079] probably have been glad to see a weakening of the Chinese hold over

[059-080] Turkestan, but they did not want a strong new state there, once they had

[059-081] found that neither of them could control the country while it was in

[059-082] Yakub Beg's hands. In 1881 Russia occupied the Ili region, Yakub's first

[059-083] conquest. In the end the two great powers considered it better for

[059-084] Turkestan to return officially into the hands of the weakened China,

[059-085] hoping that in practice they would be able to bring Turkestan more and

[059-086] more under their control. Consequently, when in 1880, three years after

[059-087] the removal of Yakub Beg, China sent a mission to Russia with the

[059-088] request for the return of the Ili region to her, Russia gave way, and

[059-089] the Treaty of Ili was concluded, ending for the time the Russian

[059-090] penetration of Turkestan. In 1882 the Manchu government raised

[059-091] Turkestan to a "new frontier" (Sinkiang) with a special administration.

[059-092] This process of colonial penetration of Turkestan continued. Until the

[059-093] end of the first world war there was no fundamental change in the

[059-094] situation in the country, owing to the rivalry between Great Britain and

[059-095] Russia. But after 1920 a period began in which Turkestan became almost

[059-096] independent, under a number of rulers of parts of the country. Then,

[059-097] from 1928 onward, a more and more thorough penetration by Russia began,

[059-098] so that by 1940 Turkestan could almost be called a Soviet Republic. The

[059-099] second world war diverted Russian attention to the West, and at the same

[059-100] time compelled the Chinese to retreat into the interior from the

[059-101] Japanese, so that by 1943 the country was more firmly held by the

[059-102] Chinese government than it had been for seventy years. After the

[059-103] creation of the People's Democracy mass immigration into Sinkiang began,

[059-104] in connection with the development of oil fields and of many new

[059-105] industries in the border area between Sinkiang and China proper. Roads

[059-106] and air communications opened Sinkiang. Yet, the differences between

[059-107] immigrant Chinese and local, Muslim Turks, continue to play a role.

[059-108] 9 _Collision with Japan; further Capitulations_

[059-109] The reign of Wen Tsung (reign name Hsien-feng 1851-1861) was marked

[059-110] throughout by the T'ai P'ing and other rebellions and by wars with the

[059-111] Europeans, and that of Mu Tsung (reign name T'ung-chih: 1862-1874) by

[059-112] the great Mohammedan disturbances. There began also a conflict with

[059-113] Japan which lasted until 1945. Mu Tsung came to the throne as a child of

[059-114] five, and never played a part of his own. It had been the general rule

[059-115] for princes to serve as regents for minors on the imperial throne, but

[059-116] this time the princes concerned won such notoriety through their

[059-117] intrigues that the Peking court circles decided to entrust the regency

[059-118] to two concubines of the late emperor. One of these, called Tz[)u] Hsi

[059-119] (born 1835), of the Manchu tribe of the Yehe-Nara, quickly gained the

[059-120] upper hand. The empress Tz[)u] Hsi was one of the strongest

[059-121] personalities of the later nineteenth century who played an active part

[059-122] in Chinese political life. She played a more active part than any

[059-123] emperor had played for many decades.

[059-124] Meanwhile great changes had taken place in Japan. The restoration of the

[059-125] Meiji had ended the age of feudalism, at least on the surface. Japan

[059-126] rapidly became Westernized, and at the same time entered on an

[059-127] imperialist policy. Her aims from 1868 onward were clear, and remained

[059-128] unaltered until the end of the second World War: she was to be

[059-129] surrounded by a wide girdle of territories under Japanese domination, in

[059-130] order to prevent the approach of any enemy to the Japanese homeland.

[059-131] This girdle was divided into several zones--(1) the inner zone with the

[059-132] Kurile Islands, Sakhalin, Korea, the Ryukyu archipelago, and Formosa;

[059-133] (2) the outer zone with the Marianne, Philippine, and Caroline Islands,

[059-134] eastern China, Manchuria, and eastern Siberia; (3) the third zone, not

[059-135] clearly defined, including especially the Netherlands Indies,

[059-136] Indo-China, and the whole of China, a zone of undefined extent. The

[059-137] outward form of this subjugated region was to be that of the Greater

[059-138] Japanese Empire, described as the Imperium of the Yellow Race (the main

[059-139] ideas were contained in the Tanaka Memorandum 1927 and in the Tada

[059-140] Interview of 1936). Round Japan, moreover, a girdle was to be created of

[059-141] producers of raw materials and purchasers of manufactures, to provide

[059-142] Japanese industry with a market. Japan had sent a delegation of amity to

[059-143] China as early as 1869, and a first Sino-Japanese treaty was signed in

[059-144] 1871; from then on, Japan began to carry out her imperialistic plans. In

[059-145] 1874 she attacked the Ryukyu islands and Formosa on the pretext that

[059-146] some Japanese had been murdered there. Under the treaty of 1874 Japan

[059-147] withdrew once more, only demanding a substantial indemnity; but in 1876,

[059-148] in violation of the treaty and without a declaration of war, she annexed

[059-149] the Ryukyu Islands. In 1876 began the Japanese penetration into Korea;

[059-150] by 1885 she had reached the stage of a declaration that Korea was a

[059-151] joint sphere of interest of China and Japan; until then China's

[059-152] protectorate over Korea had been unchallenged. At the same time (1876)

[059-153] Great Britain had secured further Capitulations in the Chefoo

[059-154] Convention; in 1862 France had acquired Cochin China, in 1864 Cambodia,

[059-155] in 1874 Tongking, and in 1883 Annam. This led in 1884 to war between

[059-156] France and China, in which the French did not by any means gain an

[059-157] indubitable victory; but the Treaty of Tientsin left them with their

[059-158] acquisitions.

[059-159] Meanwhile, at the beginning of 1875, the young Chinese emperor died of

[059-160] smallpox, without issue. Under the influence of the two empresses, who

[059-161] still remained regents, a cousin of the dead emperor, the three-year-old

[059-162] prince Tsai T'ien was chosen as emperor Tê Tsung (reign name Kuang-hsü:

[059-163] 1875-1909). He came of age in 1889 and took over the government of the

[059-164] country. The empress Tz[)u] Hsi retired, but did not really relinquish

[059-165] the reins.

[059-166] In 1894 the Sino-Japanese War broke out over Korea, as an outcome of the

[059-167] undefined position that had existed since 1885 owing to the

[059-168] imperialistic policy of the Japanese. China had created a North China

[059-169] squadron, but this was all that can be regarded as Chinese preparation

[059-170] for the long-expected war. The Governor General of Chihli (now

[059-171] Hopei--the province in which Peking is situated), Li Hung-chang, was a

[059-172] general who had done good service, but he lost the war, and at

[059-173] Shimonoseki (1895) he had to sign a treaty on very harsh terms, in which

[059-174] China relinquished her protectorate over Korea and lost Formosa. The

[059-175] intervention of France, Germany, and Russia compelled Japan to content

[059-176] herself with these acquisitions, abandoning her demand for South

[059-177] Manchuria.

[059-178] 10 _Russia in Manchuria_

[059-179] After the Crimean War, Russia had turned her attention once more to the

[059-180] East. There had been hostilities with China over eastern Siberia, which

[059-181] were brought to an end in 1858 by the Treaty of Aigun, under which China

[059-182] ceded certain territories in northern Manchuria. This made possible the

[059-183] founding of Vladivostok in 1860. Russia received Sakhalin from Japan in

[059-184] 1875 in exchange for the Kurile Islands. She received from China the

[059-185] important Port Arthur as a leased territory, and then tried to secure

[059-186] the whole of South Manchuria. This brought Japan's policy of expansion

[059-187] into conflict with Russia's plans in the Far East. Russia wanted

[059-188] Manchuria in order to be able to pursue a policy in the Pacific; but

[059-189] Japan herself planned to march into Manchuria from Korea, of which she

[059-190] already had possession. This imperialist rivalry made war inevitable:

[059-191] Russia lost the war; under the Treaty of Portsmouth in 1905 Russia gave

[059-192] Japan the main railway through Manchuria, with adjoining territory. Thus

[059-193] Manchuria became Japan's sphere of influence and was lost to the Manchus

[059-194] without their being consulted in any way. The Japanese penetration of

[059-195] Manchuria then proceeded stage by stage, not without occasional

[059-196] setbacks, until she had occupied the whole of Manchuria from 1932 to

[059-197] 1945\. After the end of the second world war, Manchuria was returned to

[059-198] China, with certain reservations in favour of the Soviet Union, which

[059-199] were later revoked.
