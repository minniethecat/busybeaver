## Overview

[[hsiao-tao-cheng|Hsiao Tao-ch'eng]] was a general of the [[liu-sung-dynasty|Liu Sung dynasty]] who rose through the factional struggles of the late fifth century to usurp the throne. In 479 CE, after murdering the reigning emperor and the entire imperial family, he founded the Southern Ch'i dynasty (479–501). His rise exemplifies the pattern of military usurpation that characterized the Southern Dynasties period.

## Rise Through Factional War

From 460 CE onward, the Liu Sung empire in the south was wracked by a series of princely rebellions, in which the northern Toba (Northern Wei) frequently intervened — hoping to gain overlordship over the entire southern realm by supporting one pretender or another.

> In these struggles in the south the Hsiao family, thanks mainly to General Hsiao Tao-ch'eng, steadily gained in power, especially as the family was united by marriage with the imperial house. [033-004] [033-005] [033-006]

The Hsiao family's marriage connection to the Liu Sung imperial house gave them both legitimacy and access, while Hsiao Tao-ch'eng's military skill allowed them to navigate the treacherous factional waters and emerge as the dominant power.

## Usurpation and Foundation of Southern Ch'i

By 477 CE, Hsiao Tao-ch'eng was ready to move against the throne directly. He arranged the assassination of the reigning emperor through an accomplice — "the son of a shamaness" — and installed a puppet boy emperor, making himself regent.

> In 477 Hsiao Tao-ch'eng finally had the emperor killed by an accomplice, the son of a shamaness; he set a boy on the throne and made himself regent. [033-007] [033-008] [033-009]

The puppet arrangement did not last long. Shortly afterward, Hsiao Tao-ch'eng ordered the murder of the boy emperor and all remaining members of the Liu Sung imperial family. With the old dynasty extinguished, he proclaimed the Southern Ch'i dynasty in 479.

> Very soon after this the boy emperor and all the members of the imperial family were murdered, and Hsiao Tao-ch'eng created the "Southern Ch'i" dynasty (479–501). [033-010] [033-011]

## Aftermath

The establishment of Southern Ch'i triggered immediate consequences. The remaining followers of the deposed Liu Sung dynasty fled northward to the Toba (Northern Wei), and fighting between the Toba and the south resumed. This fighting ended with a Northern Wei victory and the Toba's final establishment in their new capital of Loyang.

> Once more the remaining followers of the deposed dynasty fled northward to the Toba, and at once fighting between Toba and the south began again. This fighting ended with a victory for the Toba. [033-011] [033-012] [033-013] [033-014]

## Legacy

Hsiao Tao-ch'eng's usurpation followed a pattern established earlier in the Eastern Chin and Liu Sung periods: military strongmen, often from gentry families with marriage ties to the throne, would destroy one dynasty and found another. Southern Ch'i itself would prove short-lived — in 501, a general named Hsiao Yen (not of Hsiao Tao-ch'eng's line) would repeat the pattern and found the Liang dynasty. The Southern Dynasties thus became a succession of military usurpations, each legitimized by the same gentry class that had enabled the previous dynasty's rise.
