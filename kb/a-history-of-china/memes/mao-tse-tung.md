## Overview

[[Mao Tse-tung]] (1893–1976) was the paramount leader of the Chinese Communist Party and the founder of the People's Republic of China. From his beginnings as a library employee at [[peking|Peking]] University to his dominance over one-fifth of humanity, Mao's career traced a revolutionary arc that fundamentally reshaped Chinese society — imposing, in the assessment of the source history, an ideology created by Europeans upon a civilisation with three thousand years of independent political tradition [066-096].

## Early Career

Mao Tse-tung first appears in the historical record as one of the employees of Li Ta-chao, the director of the [[peking|Peking]] University Library, who had turned towards communism. The nucleus of what would become the Chinese Communist Party — officially created in 1921 — was a student organisation that included professors at [[peking|Peking]] University [064-170]. At the same time, parallel communist groups were organising among Chinese students abroad: [[chou-en-lai|Chou En-lai]] and Li Li-san led a group in Paris, while [[chu-te|Chu Tê]] belonged to a third group that organised in Germany [064-174].

> With him we find one of his employees in the Library, Mao Tse-tung. In fact, the nucleus of the Communist Party, which was officially created as late as 1921, was a student organization including some professors in Peking. [064-170–064-172]

After his time in [[peking|Peking]], Mao returned to his home province of [[hsiung-nu|Hu]]nan, where he organised the farmers. By the time of [[chiang-kai-shek|Chiang Kai-shek]]'s Northern Expedition, Mao's adherents in [[hsiung-nu|Hu]]nan already numbered in the millions — a mass base that made the rapid advance of the communist-advised KMT armies possible [065-004].

> In the meantime, Mao Tse-tung had given up his studies in Peking and had returned to his home in Hunan. Here, he organized his countrymen, the farmers of Hunan. It is said that at the verge of the northern expedition of Chiang Kai-shek, Mao's adherents in Hunan already numbered in the millions. [065-001–065-005]

## Peasant-Based Communism

In 1927, Mao articulated his most distinctive theoretical contribution: that communism in China could succeed only if it was based upon farmers rather than the urban proletariat. This unorthodox position drew years of severe attacks branding him a deviationist from orthodox Marxist-Leninist doctrine [065-007]. When [[chiang-kai-shek|Chiang Kai-shek]] broke with the KMT left in 1927 and began purging communists, Mao's rural strategy proved prescient: the communists retreated to the [[hsiung-nu|Hu]]nan-Kiangsi border area, the centre of Mao's activities, and even the orthodox communist wing that had condemned him was forced to seek his protection [065-017].

> Mao developed his ideas in written form in 1927; he showed that communism in China could be successful only if it was based upon farmers. Because of this unorthodox attitude, he was for years severely attacked as a deviationist. [065-006–065-010]

A small communist state began to develop in Kiangsi under constant KMT pressure. By 1934, this pressure became so intense that Kiangsi had to be abandoned, and the communists embarked on the epic "Long March" — fighting their way through western and north-western China into the sparsely inhabited northern province of Shensi, where they established a new base at Yenan [065-021].

## Imposing an Alien Ideology

The source text offers a stark evaluation of Mao's ultimate achievement: he accomplished what Christian missionaries had dreamed of for centuries and knew they could never achieve — he imposed an ideology created by Europeans, understandable only in the context of nineteenth-century Central Europe, upon the Chinese people [066-096].

> What the missionaries had dreamed of for centuries and knew they would never accomplish, Mao Tse-tung achieved; he imposed an ideology created by Europeans and understandable only in the context of Central Europe in the nineteenth century. [066-096–066-098]

The durability of this imposition remained uncertain. One school of analysts has argued that the friction between Soviet Russia and Communist China indicates that China's communism had in fact become Chinese, pointing out that Chinese communist practices often represented direct continuations of earlier Chinese social patterns [066-099].

## The Cultural Revolution

By the 1960s, Mao and his followers had grown old, and a new generation had appeared for whom the "old regime" was dim history rather than lived reality [067-099]. In the struggle between Mao and [[liu-shao-chi|Liu Shao-ch'i]] that became increasingly apparent in 1966, Mao sought to retain his power by mobilising young people as "Red Guards" and inciting them to launch the "Great Proletarian Cultural Revolution" [067-102].

> In the struggle between Mao and Liu Shao-ch'i, which became increasingly apparent in 1966, Mao tried to retain his power by mobilizing young people as "Red Guards" and by inciting them to make the "Great Proletarian Revolution." [067-102–067-105]

The motives behind the struggle were complex: personal rivalry for power intertwined with theoretical disagreements over China's direction — whether the present generation should sacrifice for the future, or enjoy the fruits of its labour after decades of suffering [067-107]. Mao opposed such "weakening" and favoured a new generation willing to endure hardships as he had in his youth [067-110]. There was also the question of whether Maoism should supplant the Russian party as the leader of world communism [067-112].

When Chinese youth was summoned to take up the fight for Mao, however, forces were loosed that could not be controlled. Independent action by youth groups — similar in nature to youth revolts in Western countries — eroded the power and prestige of older leaders, and by 1969 it remained impossible to re-establish unity and order [067-117].
