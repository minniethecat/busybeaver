## Overview

Wang An-shih (Chinese: 王安石, 1021–1086) was the most ambitious reformer of the [[sung-dynasty|Sung dynasty]] and one of the most controversial figures in Chinese political history. As chief minister under Emperor Shên-tsung (r. 1067–1085), he launched a sweeping program of institutional reforms that touched nearly every aspect of state and society — from agricultural loans and price stabilization to military reorganization and the [[examination-system|civil service examinations]]. Eberhard characterizes his policies as approaching "pre-capitalism" in their economic sophistication [043-166].

Wang's "New Policies" (hsin-fa) aimed to strengthen the state by increasing revenue without raising direct taxes, curbing the power of large landowners and merchant monopolies, and rationalizing government administration. His reforms provoked fierce opposition from conservative [[gentry|scholar-official]]s, who saw them as a dangerous departure from Confucian traditions of limited government. The ensuing political struggle polarized the Sung court for decades and has continued to divide historians — some seeing Wang as a proto-modern economic reformer, others as an authoritarian centralizer.

Wang An-shih himself came from Kiangsi province in the south and represented a rising class of gentry families whose interests often conflicted with the established northern bureaucratic elite [043-069].

## Rise to Power

> "A small group led by Wang An-shih (1021-1086). The ruling gentry class, finding itself in a serious crisis, split on the question of the way to a solution." [043-065]

Wang An-shih entered the imperial bureaucracy through the civil service examinations and spent his early career in provincial posts, where he gained firsthand experience with the economic problems facing rural communities. His administrative competence and his detailed knowledge of local conditions attracted the attention of Emperor Shên-tsung, who ascended the throne in 1067 determined to strengthen the Sung state against external threats from the Khitan Liao and Tangut Hsi-hsia empires.

Wang came from Kiangsi, a southern province that was rising in economic and cultural importance but whose gentry families were often excluded from the highest levels of the northern-dominated bureaucracy [043-069]. The political struggle over Wang's reforms thus had a regional dimension, pitting southern newcomers against the established Szechwan-based faction at court [043-071].

In 1069, Emperor Shên-tsung appointed Wang as chief minister with a mandate to reform. Wang moved quickly, implementing a series of policies that would collectively become known as the New Policies.

## Political Vision and Reforms

Wang An-shih's reform program rested on the conviction that the state could and should play an active role in managing the economy to benefit the common people while strengthening the dynasty. His policies represented a radical departure from the traditional Confucian laissez-faire approach to economic governance.

> "The Wang An-shih school of political philosophy represented a different approach to the crisis of the Sung state, emphasizing active governmental intervention in the economy." [044-054]

Key reforms included:

- **The Green Sprouts Loan** (ch'ing-miao fa): The state provided low-interest agricultural loans to farmers at planting time, repayable at harvest. This aimed to break the power of private moneylenders who charged usurious rates. Critics argued it forced loans on unwilling peasants.
- **The Pao-chia system**: A community-based mutual responsibility organization for local defense and tax collection, grouping households into units of ten, fifty, and five hundred. This was intended to reduce reliance on expensive professional armies.
- **The Hired Service system** (mu-i fa): Instead of requiring households to provide corvée labor service to the state, they could pay a tax, and the government would hire professionals. This monetized and rationalized a burdensome traditional obligation.
- **Price stabilization** (shih-i fa): The state bought goods when prices were low and sold when prices were high, aiming to stabilize markets and prevent merchant speculation.
- **Examination reform**: Wang replaced the traditional emphasis on poetry and literary composition in the civil service examinations with practical subjects including law, economics, and administration [043-093].

Eberhard describes the economic thrust of Wang's program as a form of "pre-capitalism" — an early attempt at systematic state intervention in a market economy that anticipated elements of modern economic policy [043-166].

## Downfall

Wang's reforms provoked intense opposition from conservative officials led by Ssu-ma Kuang, the great historian, and [[su-tung-po|Su Tung-p'o]], the renowned poet [044-054]. The conservatives, who drew support from the old northern bureaucratic families, argued that Wang's policies disrupted the natural social order, placed excessive power in the hands of the state, and threatened the moral foundations of governance.

> "The emperor was no longer able to retain Wang An-shih and had to abandon the new policies." [043-089]

Political attacks on Wang were both ideological and personal. His reform of the [[examination-system|examination system]] drew particular fire from scholars whose careers had been built on mastery of the traditional curriculum [043-093]. His opponents accused him of arrogance, stubbornness, and a disregard for established procedures.

Wang was dismissed in 1074, briefly recalled, and then permanently removed from power in 1076. After Emperor Shên-tsung's death in 1085, the conservatives under Ssu-ma Kuang returned to power and dismantled most of the New Policies. Wang died in retirement the following year, his life's work apparently in ruins.

## Influence and Legacy

Despite the reversal of his policies, Wang An-shih's ideas continued to resonate. The Wang An-shih school remained an identifiable political faction within the Sung bureaucracy, though it was unable to maintain power against the conservative opposition [043-172]. Later generations of reformers in Chinese history, from the Ming to the late Ch'ing, would invoke Wang's example — for better or worse — when arguing for state-led reform.

> "Eberhard emphasizes that Wang An-shih's reforms represented an early system that approached the ideas of pre-capitalism." [043-166]

Historical assessments of Wang have varied dramatically. Traditional Confucian historiography, following Ssu-ma Kuang, condemned him as a disruptor of harmony. Twentieth-century scholars, influenced by modern economic ideas, re-evaluated him as a visionary reformer. In the West, the most comprehensive study remains H.R. Williamson's _Wang An-shih_ (London, 1935), though Eberhard notes the field deserves fuller treatment [071-120].

The fundamental question Wang's career poses — how far the state should intervene in economic life and at what cost to individual liberty — remains as relevant to modern governance as it was to eleventh-century China.
