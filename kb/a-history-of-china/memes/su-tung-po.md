## Overview

[[Su Tung-p'o]] (Su Shih, 1036–1101) was the greatest of the "Three Sus" — the celebrated literary family of the Northern [[sung-dynasty|Sung dynasty]] from present-day Szechwan province. His revolutionary use of vernacular language in classical poetry infused the form with a warmth and emotional directness that set him apart from his contemporaries and earned him an enduring place in the Chinese literary tradition [044-057].

## Biography

Su Tung-p'o was the son of Su Shih (the father, whose name is romanised identically to Su Tung-p'o's own formal name) and the elder brother of Su Che. Together, the "Three Sus" led a poetic school associated with the Shu region (modern Szechwan) and with the Kiangsi school of poetry [044-056]. Their work represented a deliberate departure from the ornate, allusive style that had dominated Chinese verse.

> The greatest of the three was Su Tung-p'o (1036-1101); the others were his father, Su Shih, and his brother, Su Che. [044-057–044-058]

Su Tung-p'o's career as a [[gentry|scholar-official]] was marked by the political turbulence of the Northern Sung. He was caught in the factional struggles between the reformist [[wang-an-shih|Wang An-shih]] and the conservative Ssu-ma Kuang, suffering banishment and political persecution — experiences that deepened the emotional range of his poetry and prose.

## Poetic Innovation

The most distinctive feature of Su Tung-p'o's poetry was his bold use of the vernacular. It had not been usual to introduce the phrases of everyday life into classical poetry, but Su Tung-p'o made use of the most everyday expressions — and far from diminishing his artistic effectiveness, the result was to give his poems much more genuine feeling than those of other poets [044-061].

> It had not been usual to introduce the phrases of everyday life into poetry, but Su Tung-p'o made use of the most everyday expressions, without diminishing his artistic effectiveness by so doing; on the contrary, the result was to give his poems much more genuine feeling than those of other poets. [044-061–044-064]

This naturalism placed him in harmony with the T'ang period poet [[po-chu-i|Po Chü-i]] (772–846), who had similarly sought to bring poetry closer to ordinary speech. Like the Neo-Confucian philosophers of his era, Su Tung-p'o was supported by representatives of the burgeoning commercial economy [044-065]. Politically, however, he and his fellow Shu poets were sharply conservative [044-067].

## Broader Accomplishments

Beyond poetry, Su Tung-p'o was a calligrapher, painter, essayist, and gastronome of distinction. His informal essays — collected under the title _Tung-p'o Chih-lin_ — pioneered a genre of casual, conversational prose that influenced later Chinese literary culture. The standard introduction to his life and work remains Lin Yutang's _The Gay Genius: The Life and Times of Su Tung-p'o_ (New York, 1947) [071-140].

## Legacy

Su Tung-p'o's integration of vernacular speech into classical poetry permanently expanded the expressive range of Chinese verse. His poems, memorised by schoolchildren and quoted by scholars, have remained among the most widely loved works in the Chinese language for nearly a millennium. His ability to transmute everyday experience — a walk in the rain, a meal with friends, the view from a boat — into art of the highest order made him the quintessential Sung dynasty literary figure.
