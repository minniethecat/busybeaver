# Section 56

[056-001] A quite different type of insurgent commander was the Mongol prince

[056-002] Galdan. He, too, planned to make himself independent of Manchu

[056-003] overlordship. At first the Mongols had readily supported the Manchus,

[056-004] when the latter were making raids into China and there was plenty of

[056-005] booty. Now, however, the Manchus, under the influence of the Chinese

[056-006] gentry whom they brought, and could not but bring, to their court, were

[056-007] rapidly becoming Chinese in respect to culture. Even in the time of

[056-008] K'ang-hsi the Manchus began to forget Manchurian; they brought tutors to

[056-009] court to teach the young Manchus Chinese. Later even the emperors did

[056-010] not understand Manchurian! As a result of this process, the Mongols

[056-011] became alienated from the Manchurians, and the situation began once more

[056-012] to be the same as at the time of the Ming rulers. Thus Galdan tried to

[056-013] found an independent Mongol realm, free from Chinese influence.

[056-014] The Manchus could not permit this, as such a realm would have threatened

[056-015] the flank of their homeland, Manchuria, and would have attracted those

[056-016] Manchus who objected to sinification. Between 1690 and 1696 there were

[056-017] battles, in which the emperor actually took part in person. Galdan was

[056-018] defeated. In 1715, however, there were new disturbances, this time in

[056-019] western Mongolia. Tsewang Rabdan, whom the Chinese had made khan of the

[056-020] Ölöt, rose against the Chinese. The wars that followed, extending far

[056-021] into Turkestan and also involving its Turkish population together with

[056-022] the Dzungars, ended with the Chinese conquest of the whole of Mongolia

[056-023] and of parts of eastern Turkestan. As Tsewang Rabdan had tried to extend

[056-024] his power as far as Tibet, a campaign was undertaken also into Tibet,

[056-025] Lhasa was occupied, a new Dalai Lama was installed there as supreme

[056-026] ruler, and Tibet was made into a protectorate. Since then Tibet has

[056-027] remained to this day under some form of Chinese colonial rule.

[056-028] This penetration of the Chinese into Turkestan took place just at the

[056-029] time when the Russians were enormously expanding their empire in Asia,

[056-030] and this formed the third problem for the Manchus. In 1650 the Russians

[056-031] had established a fort by the river Amur. The Manchus regarded the Amur

[056-032] (which they called the "River of the Black Dragon") as part of their own

[056-033] territory, and in 1685 they destroyed the Russian settlement. After this

[056-034] there were negotiations, which culminated in 1689 in the Treaty of

[056-035] Nerchinsk. This treaty was the first concluded by the Chinese state with

[056-036] a European power. Jesuit missionaries played a part in the negotiations

[056-037] as interpreters. Owing to the difficulties of translation the text of

[056-038] the treaty, in Chinese, Russian, and Manchurian, contained some

[056-039] obscurities, particularly in regard to the frontier line. Accordingly,

[056-040] in 1727 the Russians asked for a revision of the old treaty. The Chinese

[056-041] emperor, whose rule name was Yung-cheng, arranged for the negotiations

[056-042] to be carried on at the frontier, in the town of Kyakhta, in Mongolia,

[056-043] where after long discussions a new treaty was concluded. Under this

[056-044] treaty the Russians received permission to set up a legation and a

[056-045] commercial agency in Peking, and also to maintain a church. This was the

[056-046] beginning of the foreign Capitulations. From the Chinese point of view

[056-047] there was nothing special in a facility of this sort. For some fifteen

[056-048] centuries all the "barbarians" who had to bring tribute had been given

[056-049] houses in the capital, where their envoys could wait until the emperor

[056-050] would receive them--usually on New Year's Day. The custom had sprung up

[056-051] at the reception of the Huns. Moreover, permission had always been given

[056-052] for envoys to be accompanied by a few merchants, who during the envoy's

[056-053] stay did a certain amount of business. Furthermore the time had been

[056-054] when the Uighurs were permitted to set up a temple of their own. At the

[056-055] time of the permission given to the Russians to set up a "legation", a

[056-056] similar office was set up (in 1729) for "Uighur" peoples (meaning

[056-057] Mohammedans), again under the control of an office, called the Office

[056-058] for Regulation of Barbarians. The Mohammedan office was placed under two

[056-059] Mohammedan leaders who lived in Peking. The Europeans, however, had

[056-060] quite different ideas about a "legation", and about the significance of

[056-061] permission to trade. They regarded this as the opening of diplomatic

[056-062] relations between states on terms of equality, and the carrying on of

[056-063] trade as a special privilege, a sort of Capitulation. This reciprocal

[056-064] misunderstanding produced in the nineteenth century a number of serious

[056-065] political conflicts. The Europeans charged the Chinese with breach of

[056-066] treaties, failure to meet their obligations, and other such things,

[056-067] while the Chinese considered that they had acted with perfect

[056-068] correctness.

[056-069] 4 _Culture_

[056-070] In this K'ang-hsi period culture began to flourish again. The emperor

[056-071] had attracted the gentry, and so the intelligentsia, to his court

[056-072] because his uneducated Manchus could not alone have administered the

[056-073] enormous empire; and he showed great interest in Chinese culture,

[056-074] himself delved deeply into it, and had many works compiled, especially

[056-075] works of an encyclopaedic character. The encyclopaedias enabled

[056-076] information to be rapidly gained on all sorts of subjects, and thus were

[056-077] just what an interested ruler needed, especially when, as a foreigner,

[056-078] he was not in a position to gain really thorough instruction in things

[056-079] Chinese. The Chinese encyclopaedias of the seventeenth and especially of

[056-080] the eighteenth century were thus the outcome of the initiative of the

[056-081] Manchurian emperor, and were compiled for his information; they were not

[056-082] due, like the French encyclopaedias of the eighteenth century, to a

[056-083] movement for the spread of knowledge among the people. For this latter

[056-084] purpose the gigantic encyclopaedias of the Manchus, each of which fills

[056-085] several bookcases, were much too expensive and were printed in much too

[056-086] limited editions. The compilations began with the great geographical

[056-087] encyclopaedia of Ku Yen-wu (1613-1682), and attained their climax in the

[056-088] gigantic eighteenth-century encyclopaedia _T'u-shu chi-ch'eng_,

[056-089] scientifically impeccable in the accuracy of its references to sources.

[056-090] Here were already the beginnings of the "Archaeological School", built

[056-091] up in the course of the eighteenth century. This school was usually

[056-092] called "Han school" because the adherents went back to the commentaries

[056-093] of the classical texts written in Han time and discarded the orthodox

[056-094] explanations of Chu Hsi's school of Sung time. Later, its most prominent

[056-095] leader was Tai Chen (1723-1777). Tai was greatly interested in

[056-096] technology and science; he can be regarded as the first philosopher who

[056-097] exhibited an empirical, scientific way of thinking. Late nineteenth and

[056-098] early twentieth century Chinese scholarship is greatly obliged to him.

[056-099] The most famous literary works of the Manchu epoch belong once more to

[056-100] the field which Chinese do not regard as that of true literature--the

[056-101] novel, the short story, and the drama. Poetry did exist, but it kept to

[056-102] the old paths and had few fresh ideas. All the various forms of the Sung

[056-103] period were made use of. The essayists, too, offered nothing new, though

[056-104] their number was legion. One of the best known is Yüan Mei (1716-1797),

[056-105] who was also the author of the collection of short stories _Tse-pu-yü_

[056-106] ("The Master did not tell"), which is regarded very highly by the

[056-107] Chinese. The volume of short stories entitled _Liao-chai chich-i_, by

[056-108] P'u Sung-lin (1640-1715?), is world-famous and has been translated into

[056-109] every civilized language. Both collections are distinguished by their

[056-110] simple but elegant style. The short story was popular among the greater

[056-111] gentry; it abandoned the popular style it had in the Ming epoch, and

[056-112] adopted the polished language of scholars.

[056-113] The Manchu epoch has left to us what is by general consent the finest

[056-114] novel in Chinese literature, _Hung-lou-meng_ ("The Dream of the Red

[056-115] Chamber"), by Ts'ao Hsüeh-ch'in, who died in 1763. It describes the

[056-116] downfall of a rich and powerful family from the highest rank of the

[056-117] gentry, and the decadent son's love of a young and emotional lady of the

[056-118] highest circles. The story is clothed in a mystical garb that does

[056-119] something to soften its tragic ending. The interesting novel _Ju-lin

[056-120] wai-shih_ ("Private Reports from the Life of Scholars"), by Wu

[056-121] Ching-tz[)u] (1701-1754), is a mordant criticism of Confucianism with

[056-122] its rigid formalism, of the social system, and of the examination

[056-123] system. Social criticism is the theme of many novels. The most modern in

[056-124] spirit of the works of this period is perhaps the treatment of feminism

[056-125] in the novel _Ching-hua-yüan_, by Li Yu-chên (d. 1830), which demanded

[056-126] equal rights for men and women.

[056-127] The drama developed quickly in the Manchu epoch, particularly in

[056-128] quantity, especially since the emperors greatly appreciated the theatre.

[056-129] A catalogue of plays compiled in 1781 contains 1,013 titles! Some of

[056-130] these dramas were of unprecedented length. One of them was played in 26

[056-131] parts containing 240 acts; a performance took two years to complete!

[056-132] Probably the finest dramas of the Manchu epoch are those of Li Yü (born

[056-133] 1611), who also became the first of the Chinese dramatic critics. What

[056-134] he had to say about the art of the theatre, and about aesthetics in

[056-135] general, is still worth reading.

[056-136] About the middle of the nineteenth century the influence of Europe

[056-137] became more and more marked. Translation began with Yen Fu (1853-1921),

[056-138] who translated the first philosophical and scientific books and books on

[056-139] social questions and made his compatriots acquainted with Western

[056-140] thought. At the same time Lin Shu (1852-1924) translated the first

[056-141] Western short stories and novels. With these two began the new style,

[056-142] which was soon elaborated by Liang Ch'i-ch'ao, a collaborator of Sun

[056-143] Yat-sen's, and by others, and which ultimately produced the "literary

[056-144] revolution" of 1917. Translation has continued to this day; almost every

[056-145] book of outstanding importance in world literature is translated within

[056-146] a few months of its appearance, and on the average these translations

[056-147] are of a fairly high level.

[056-148] Particularly fine work was produced in the field of porcelain in the

[056-149] Manchu epoch. In 1680 the famous kilns in the province of Kiangsi were

[056-150] reopened, and porcelain that is among the most artistically perfect in

[056-151] the world was fired in them. Among the new colours were especially green

[056-152] shades (one group is known as _famille verte_) and also black and yellow

[056-153] compositions. Monochrome porcelain also developed further, including

[056-154] very fine dark blue, brilliant red (called "ox-blood"), and white. In

[056-155] the eighteenth century, however, there began an unmistakable decline,

[056-156] which has continued to this day, although there are still a few

[056-157] craftsmen and a few kilns that produce outstanding work (usually

[056-158] attempts to imitate old models), often in small factories.

[056-159] In painting, European influence soon shows itself. The best-known

[056-160] example of this is Lang Shih-ning, an Italian missionary whose original

[056-161] name was Giuseppe Castiglione (1688-1766); he began to work in China in

[056-162] 1715\. He learned the Chinese method of painting, but introduced a number

[056-163] of technical tricks of European painters, which were adopted in general

[056-164] practice in China, especially by the official court painters: the

[056-165] painting of the scholars who lived in seclusion remained uninfluenced.

[056-166] Dutch flower-painting also had some influence in China as early as the

[056-167] eighteenth century.

[056-168] The missionaries played an important part at court. The first Manchu

[056-169] emperors were as generous in this matter as the Mongols had been, and

[056-170] allowed the foreigners to work in peace. They showed special interest in

[056-171] the European science introduced by the missionaries; they had less

[056-172] sympathy for their religious message. The missionaries, for their part,

[056-173] sent to Europe enthusiastic accounts of the wonderful conditions in

[056-174] China, and so helped to popularize the idea that was being formed in

[056-175] Europe of an "enlightened", a constitutional, monarchy. The leaders of

[056-176] the Enlightenment read these reports with enthusiasm, with the result

[056-177] that they had an influence on the French Revolution. Confucius was found

[056-178] particularly attractive, and was regarded as a forerunner of the

[056-179] Enlightenment. The "Monadism" of the philosopher Leibniz was influenced

[056-180] by these reports.

[056-181] The missionaries gained a reputation at court as "scientists", and in

[056-182] this they were of service both to China and to Europe. The behaviour of

[056-183] the European merchants who followed the missions, spreading gradually in

[056-184] growing numbers along the coasts of China, was not by any means so

[056-185] irreproachable. The Chinese were certainly justified when they declared

[056-186] that European ships often made landings on the coast and simply looted,

[056-187] just as the Japanese had done before them. Reports of this came to the

[056-188] court, and as captured foreigners described themselves as "Christians"

[056-189] and also seemed to have some connection with the missionaries living at

[056-190] court, and as disputes had broken out among the missionaries themselves

[056-191] in connection with papal ecclesiastical policy, in the Yung-cheng period

[056-192] (1723-1736; the name of the emperor was Shih Tsung) Christianity was

[056-193] placed under a general ban, being regarded as a secret political

[056-194] organization.
