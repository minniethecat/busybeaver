## Overview

**Tso Tsung-t'ang** (1812–1885) was one of the most prominent Chinese military commanders of the late [[Qing dynasty|Ch'ing]] period. His career spanned the suppression of the [[Taiping Rebellion|T'ai P'ing Rebellion]], the pacification of Muslim uprisings in Kansu, and the reconquest of Turkestan. His campaigns restored Chinese control over the territory that later became Xinjiang.

## Military Career

Tso Tsung-t'ang rose to prominence during the mid-nineteenth century, a period of widespread rebellion against Qing rule. He saw action against the T'ai P'ing in central China:

> "The famous Chinese general Tso Tsung-t'ang (1812–1885), who had fought against the T'ai P'ing and also against the Mohammedans in Kansu." [059-068] [059-069]

He subsequently campaigned against Mohammedan ([[hsiung-nu|Hu]]i) forces in the northwestern province of Kansu, gaining valuable experience in frontier warfare.

## Reconquest of Turkestan

In 1877, Tso Tsung-t'ang marched westward into Turkestan to confront the regime of [[Yakub Beg]], a Turkic adventurer who had established an independent Islamic state in the Tarim Basin with tacit British support:

> "In 1877 the famous Chinese general Tso Tsung-t'ang marched into Turkestan and ended Yakub Beg's rule." [059-068] [059-069] [059-070] [059-071]

[[yakub-beg|Yakub Beg]] was defeated not solely by Chinese military superiority but by internal weaknesses within his own regime. The Qing reconquest of Turkestan marked one of the dynasty's last major military successes.

## Legacy

Tso Tsung-t'ang's campaigns ensured that the northwest remained under Chinese sovereignty. The province of Xinjiang ("New Dominion") was formally established in 1884, largely as a result of his reconquest.

He is remembered alongside [[tseng-kuo-fan|Tseng Kuo-fan]] and [[li-hung-chang|Li Hung-chang]] as one of the three great Han Chinese officials who sustained the late Qing dynasty through its final decades.
