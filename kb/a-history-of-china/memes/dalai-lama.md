## Overview

The [[Dalai Lama]] is the supreme religious ruler of [[Tibet]] and the highest spiritual authority in [[Tibetan Buddhism|Lamaism]], a distinctive form of Buddhism practised across Tibet, Mongolia, and parts of the Himalayan region. Believed by followers to be the reincarnation of the Bodhisattva Avalokiteshvara, the Dalai Lama has served as both the spiritual and temporal head of the Tibetan state since the seventeenth century. The institution combines elements of Mahayana Buddhist doctrine with the unique theocratic traditions of the Tibetan plateau.

## Religious Background

The religious tradition over which the Dalai Lama presides — Lamaism — represents a distinctive branch of Buddhism that developed in Tibet through the fusion of Indian Buddhist teachings with indigenous Bon practices:

> "Lamaism is a special form of Buddhism" (048-165)

Lamaism developed a complex institutional structure centred on monastic communities, scholastic debate, and elaborate ritual. The office of the Dalai Lama emerged in the sixteenth century within the Gelugpa (Yellow Hat) school, which gradually achieved political dominance over rival sects with the backing of Mongol military power. The title "Dalai" itself derives from a [[mongol|Mongolian]] word meaning "ocean," signifying the depth of the Lama's wisdom.

## Political Role and Qing Relations

The Dalai Lama's position as temporal ruler of Tibet was consolidated through the intervention of the [[Qing Dynasty|Qing empire]]. During the early eighteenth century, Qing forces entered Tibet and established a protectorate:

> "Lhasa was occupied, a new Dalai Lama was installed there as s" (056-025)

The Qing installation of a new Dalai Lama at [[Lhasa]] marked the beginning of a complex relationship between the Manchu court and the Tibetan theocracy. The Dalai Lama's authority was maintained through a combination of religious prestige, Qing military backing, and the administrative apparatus of the Gelugpa monasteries. This arrangement persisted until the fall of the Qing dynasty in 1911.

The institution's broader significance extended beyond Tibet's borders. Lamaism spread among the [[mongol|Mongols]] and other Inner Asian peoples:

> "rulers' predilection for the Lamaism that was widespread in their" (048-164)

Mongol princes, in particular, became devout patrons of the Gelugpa order, strengthening the Dalai Lama's influence across the steppe world.

## Later Developments

In the twentieth century, the Dalai Lama's position was challenged by the rise of Chinese nationalism and the incorporation of Tibet into the People's Republic of China:

> "of the Dalai Lama to India. The constructi" (062-149)

The flight of the fourteenth Dalai Lama to India in 1959 marked a turning point in the institution's history, creating a Tibetan government-in-exile and transforming the Dalai Lama into an international figure whose influence extended far beyond the traditional Tibetan cultural sphere.
