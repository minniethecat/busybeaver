## Overview

**Huang Ch'ao** was a Chinese rebel leader whose massive uprising from 874 to 884 dealt the decisive blow to the [[tang-dynasty|T'ang dynasty]], marking one of the few times in Chinese history that a peasant-led rebellion captured the imperial capital. His movement mobilised vast numbers of disaffected commoners, and although ultimately defeated, the wars that followed his rebellion fundamentally reshaped the social order of late imperial China.

## Biography

### Rise of the Rebellion

Huang Ch'ao emerged as a rebel leader in the aftermath of the [[an-lu-shan-rebellion|An Lu-shan Rebellion]], during a period of intensifying agrarian crisis and weakening central authority. After his fellow rebel Wang Hsien-chih was defeated and beheaded by government forces with the aid of [[uighur|Sha-t'o]] Turkish troops in 878, [[hsiung-nu|Hu]]ang assumed sole leadership of the movement:

> Huang Ch'ao now moved into the south-east and the south, where in 879 he captured and burned down Canton; according to an Arab source, over 120,000 foreign merchants lost their lives in addition to the Chinese. [038-180] [038-181]

### The March on the Capitals

After consolidating his forces in the south, [[hsiung-nu|Hu]]ang turned northward:

> From Canton Huang Ch'ao returned to the north, laden with loot from that rich commercial harbour. [038-183]

His advance was temporarily checked by Sha-t'o troops, but he outflanked them by moving through the lower [[yangtze-river|Yangtze]] region. At the end of 880 he captured the eastern capital (Loyang), and shortly thereafter the western capital, Ch'ang-an — the imperial seat:

> Huang Ch'ao now captured with ease the western capital as well, and removed every member of the ruling family on whom he could lay hands. He did not, however, remain permanently in the capital. [038-188]

The T'ang emperor fled into Szechwan, and for a brief moment Huang Ch'ao controlled the heart of the empire:

> It was the first time that a peasant rising had succeeded against the gentry. [038-191]

### Defeat and Death

[[hsiung-nu|Hu]]ang's hold on the capital proved tenuous. The T'ang court enlisted the Sha-t'o Turks under Li K'o-yung to retake the city:

> At the end of 881 Li K'o-yung fell upon the capital; Huang Ch'ao was able to hold out, but a further attack was made in 883 and he was defeated and forced to flee; in 884 he was killed by the Sha-t'o. [039-012] [039-015]

### Legacy and Aftermath

The Huang Ch'ao rebellion did not merely disrupt the T'ang — it shattered the established social hierarchy:

> The rebellion of Huang Ch'ao in fact meant the end of the T'ang dynasty and the division of China into a number of independent states. [039-051]

> The wars which followed Huang Ch'ao's rebellion greatly affected the gentry. A number of families were so strongly affected that they disappeared entirely from the scene. Commoners from the followers of Huang Ch'ao or other armies succeeded to get into power, to acquire land, and to climb the social ladder. [039-074] [039-077]

By approximately A.D. 1000, nearly half of the [[gentry]] families were new families of low origin — a direct consequence of the social upheaval Huang Ch'ao set in motion.

One of [[hsiung-nu|Hu]]ang's former followers, [[chu-chuan-chung|Chu Ch'üan-chung]], would go on to found the Later Liang dynasty (906), the first of the Five Dynasties:

> The founder of this Later Liang dynasty, Chu Ch'üan-chung (906), was himself an eastern Chinese and, as will be remembered, a past supporter of the revolutionary Huang Ch'ao, but he had then gone over to the T'ang and had gained high military rank. [040-183]
