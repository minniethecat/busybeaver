# Section 72

[072-001] p. 246: The necessity of transports of grain and salt was one of the

[072-002] reasons for the emergence of the Hsin-an and Hui-chou merchants. The

[072-003] importance of these developments is only partially known (studies mainly

[072-004] by H. Fujii and in _Li-shih-yen-chiu_ 1955, No. 3). Data are also in an

[072-005] unpublished thesis by Ch. Mac Sherry, _The Impairment of the Ming

[072-006] Tributary System_, and in an article by Wang Ch'ung-wu.

[072-007] p. 247: The tax system of the Ming has been studied among others by

[072-008] Liang Fang-chung. Yoshiyuki Suto analysed the methods of tax evasion in

[072-009] the periods before the reform. For the land grants, I used Wan

[072-010] Kuo-ting's data.

[072-011] p. 248: Based mainly upon my own research. On the progress of

[072-012] agriculture wrote Li Chien-nung and also Kat[=o] Shigeru and others.

[072-013] p. 250: I believe that further research would discover that the

[072-014] "agrarian revolution" was a key factor in the economic and social

[072-015] development of China. It probably led to another change in dietary

[072-016] habits; it certainly led to a greater labour input per person, i.e. a

[072-017] higher number of full working days per year than before. It may be--but

[072-018] only further research can try to show this--that the "agrarian

[072-019] revolution" turned China away from technology and industry.--On cotton

[072-020] and its importance see the studies by M. Amano, and some preliminary

[072-021] remarks by P. Pelliot.

[072-022] pp. 250-1: Detailed study of Central Chinese urban centres in this time

[072-023] is a great desideratum. My remarks here have to be taken as very

[072-024] preliminary. Notice the special character of the industries

[072-025] mentioned!--The porcelain centre of Ching-tê-chen was inhabited by

[072-026] workers and merchants (70-80 per cent of population); there were more

[072-027] than 200 private kilns.--On indented labour see Li Chien-nung, H. Iwami

[072-028] and Y. Yamane.

[072-029] p. 253: On _pien-wen_ I used R. Michihata, and for this general

[072-030] discussion R. Irvin, _The Evolution of a Chinese Novel_, Cambridge,

[072-031] Mass., 1953, and studies by J. Jaworski and J. Pru[vs]ek. Many texts of

[072-032] _pien-wen_ and related styles have been found in Tunhuang and have been

[072-033] recently republished by Chinese scholars.

[072-034] p. 254: _Shui-hu-chuan_ has been translated by Pearl Buck, _All Men are

[072-035] Brothers_. Parts of _Hsi-yu-chi_ have been translated by A. Waley,

[072-036] _Monkey_, London 1946. _San-kuo yen-i_ is translated by C.H.

[072-037] Brewitt-Taylor, _San Kuo, or Romance of the Three Kingdoms_, Shanghai

[072-038] 1925 (a new edition just published). A purged translation of

[072-039] Chin-p'ing-mei is published by Fr. Kuhn _Chin P'ing Mei_, New York 1940.

[072-040] p. 255: Even the "murder story" was already known in Ming time. An

[072-041] example is R.H. van Gulik, _Dee Gong An. Three Murder Cases solved by

[072-042] Judge Dee_, Tokyo 1949.

[072-043] p. 256: For a special group of block-prints see R.H. van Gulik, _Erotic

[072-044] Colour Prints of the Ming Dynasty_, Tokyo 1951. This book is also an

[072-045] excellent introduction into Chinese psychology.

[072-046] p. 257: Here I use work done by David Chan.

[072-047] p. 258: I use here the research of J.J.L. Duyvendak; the reasons for the

[072-048] end of such enterprises, as given here, may not exhaust the problem. It

[072-049] may not be without relevance that Cheng came from a Muslim family. His

[072-050] father was a pilgrim (_Bull. Chin. Studies_, vol. 3, pp. 131-70).

[072-051] Further research is desirable.--Concerning folk-tales, I use my own

[072-052] research. The main Buddhist tales are the _Jataka_ stories. They are

[072-053] still used by Burmese Buddhists in the same context.

[072-054] p. 260: The Oirat (Uyrat, Ojrot, Ölöt) were a confederation of four

[072-055] tribal groups: Khosud, Dzungar, Dörbet and Turgut.

[072-056] p. 261: I regard this analysis of Ming political history as

[072-057] unsatisfactory, but to my knowledge no large-scale analysis has been

[072-058] made.--For Wang Yang-ming I use mainly my own research.

[072-059] p. 262: For the coastal salt-merchants I used Lo Hsiang-lin's work.

[072-060] p. 263: On the rifles I used P. Pelliot. There is a large literature on

[072-061] the use of explosives and the invention of cannons, especially L.C.

[072-062] Goodrich and Feng Chia-sheng in _Isis_, vol. 36, 1946 and 39, 1948; also

[072-063] G. Sarton, Li Ch'iao-p'ing, J. Pru[vs]ek, J. Needham, and M. Ishida; a

[072-064] comparative, general study is by K. Huuri, _Studia Orientalia_ vol. 9,

[072-065] 1941.--For the earliest contacts of Wang with Portuguese, I used Chang

[072-066] Wei-hua's monograph.--While there is no satisfactory, comprehensive

[072-067] study in English on Wang, for Lu Hsiang-shan the book by Huang Siu-ch'i,

[072-068] _Lu Hsiang-shan, a Twelfth-century Chinese Idealist Philosopher_, New

[072-069] Haven 1944, can be used.

[072-070] p. 264: For Tao-yen, I used work done by David Chan.--Large parts of the

[072-071] _Yung-lo ta-tien_ are now lost (Kuo Po-kung, Yüan T'ung-li studied this

[072-072] problem).

[072-073] p. 265: Yen-ta's Mongol name is Altan Qan (died 1582), leader of the

[072-074] Tümet. He is also responsible for the re-introduction of Lamaism into

[072-075] Mongolia (1574).--For the border trade I used Hou Jen-chih; for the

[072-076] Shansi bankers Ch'en Ch'i-t'ien and P. Maybon. For the beginnings of the

[072-077] Manchu see Fr. Michael, _The Origins of Manchu Rule in China_, Baltimore

[072-078] 1942.

[072-079] p. 266: M. Ricci's diary (Matthew Ricci, _China in the Sixteenth

[072-080] Century_. The Journals of M. Ricci, transl. by L.J. Gallagher, New York

[072-081] 1953) gives much insight into the life of Chinese officials in this

[072-082] period. Recently, J. Needham has tried to show that Ricci and his

[072-083] followers did not bring much which was not already known in China, but

[072-084] that they actually attempted to prevent the Chinese from learning about

[072-085] the Copernican theory.

[072-086] p. 267: For Coxinga I used M. Eder's study.--The Szechwan rebellion was

[072-087] led by Chang Hsien-chung (1606-1647); I used work done by James B.

[072-088] Parsons. Cheng T'ien-t'ing, Sun Yueh and others have recently published

[072-089] the important documents concerning all late Ming peasant

[072-090] rebellions.--For the Tung-lin academy see Ch. O. Hucker in J.K.

[072-091] Fairbank, _Chinese Thought and Institutions_, Chicago 1957. A different

[072-092] interpretation is indicated by Shang Yüeh in _Li-shih yen-chiu_ 1955,

[072-093] No. 3.

[072-094] p. 268: Work on the "academies" (shu-yüan) in the earlier time is done

[072-095] by Ho Yu-shen.

[072-096] pp. 273-4: Based upon my own, as yet unfinished research.

[072-097] p. 274: The population of 1953 as given here, includes Chinese outside

[072-098] of mainland China. The population of mainland China was 582.6 millions.

[072-099] If the rate of increase of about 2 per cent per year has remained the

[072-100] same, the population of mainland China in 1960 may be close to 680

[072-101] million. In general see P.T. Ho. _Studies on the Population of China,

[072-102] 1368-1953_, Cambridge, Mass., 1960.

[072-103] p. 276: Based upon my own research.--A different view of the development

[072-104] of Chinese industry is found in Norman Jacobs, _Modern Capitalism and

[072-105] Eastern Asia_, Hong Kong 1958. Jacobs attempted a comparison of China

[072-106] with Japan and with Europe. Different again is Marion Levy and Shih

[072-107] Kuo-heng, _The Rise of the Modern Chinese Business Class_, New York

[072-108] 1949\. Both books are influenced by the sociological theories of T.

[072-109] Parsons.

[072-110] p. 277: The Dzungars (Dsunghar; Chun-ko-erh) are one of the four Ölöt

[072-111] (Oirat) groups. I am here using studies by E. Haenisch and W. Fuchs.

[072-112] p. 278: Tibetan-Chinese relations have been studied by L. Petech, _China

[072-113] and Tibet in the Early 18th Century_, Leiden 1950. A collection of data

[072-114] is found in M.W. Fisher and L.E. Rose, _England, India, Nepal, Tibet,

[072-115] China, 1765-1958_, Berkeley 1959. For diplomatic relations and tributary

[072-116] systems of this period, I referred to J.K. Fairbank and Teng Ssu-yü.

[072-117] p. 279: For Ku Yen-wu, I used the work by H. Wilhelm.--A man who

[072-118] deserves special mention in this period is the scholar Huang Tsung-hsi

[072-119] (1610-1695) as the first Chinese who discussed the possibility of a

[072-120] non-monarchic form of government in his treatise of 1662. For him see

[072-121] Lin Mou-sheng, _Men and Ideas_, New York 1942, and especially W.T. de

[072-122] Bary in J.K. Fairbank, _Chinese Thought and Institutions_, Chicago 1957.

[072-123] pp. 280-1: On Liang see now J.R. Levenson, _Liang Ch'i-ch'ao and the Mind

[072-124] of Modern China_, London 1959.

[072-125] p. 282: It should also be pointed out that the Yung-cheng emperor was

[072-126] personally more inclined towards Lamaism.--The Kalmuks are largely

[072-127] identical with the above-mentioned Ölöt.

[072-128] p. 286: The existence of _hong_ is known since 1686, see P'eng Tse-i and

[072-129] Wang Chu-an's recent studies. For details on foreign trade see H.B.

[072-130] Morse, _The Chronicles of the East India Company Trading to China

[072-131] 1635-1834_, Oxford 1926, 4 vols., and J.K. Fairbank, _Trade and

[072-132] Diplomacy on the China Coast. The Opening of the Treaty Ports,

[072-133] 1842-1854_, Cambridge, Mass., 1953, 2 vols.--For Lin I used G.W.

[072-134] Overdijkink's study.

[072-135] p. 287: On customs read St. F. Wright, _Hart and the Chinese Customs_,

[072-136] Belfast 1950.

[072-137] p. 288: For early industry see A. Feuerwerker, _China's Early

[072-138] Industrialization: Sheng Hsuan-huai (1844-1916_), Cambridge, Mass.,

[072-139] 1958.

[072-140] p. 289: The Chinese source materials for the Mohammedan revolts have

[072-141] recently been published, but an analysis of the importance of the

[072-142] revolts still remains to be done.--On T'ai-p'ing much has been

[072-143] published, especially in the last years in China, so that all documents

[072-144] are now available. I used among other studies, details brought out by Lo

[072-145] Hsiang-lin and Jen Yu-wen.

[072-146] p. 291: For Tsêng Kuo-fan see W.J. Hail, _Tsêng Kuo-fan and the

[072-147] T'ai-p'ing Rebellion_, New Haven 1927, but new research on him is about

[072-148] to be published.--The Nien-fei had some connection with the White Lotus,

[072-149] and were known since 1814, see Chiang Siang-tseh, _The Nien Rebellion_,

[072-150] Seattle 1954.

[072-151] p. 292: Little is known about Salars, Dungans and Yakub Beg's rebellion,

[072-152] mainly because relevant Turkish sources have not yet been studied. On

[072-153] Salars see L. Schram, _The Monguors of Kansu_, Philadelphia 1954, p. 23

[072-154] and P. Pelliot; on Dungans see I. Grebe.

[072-155] p. 293: On Tso Tsung-t'ang see G. Ch'en, _Tso Tung T'ang, Pioneer

[072-156] Promotor of the Modern Dockyard and Woollen Mill in China_, Peking 1938,

[072-157] and _Yenching Journal of Soc. Studies_, vol. I.

[072-158] p. 294: For the T'ung-chih period, see now Mary C. Wright, _The Last

[072-159] Stand of Chinese Conservativism. The T'ung-chih Restoration, 1862-1874_,

[072-160] Stanford 1957.

[072-161] p. 295: Ryukyu is Chinese: Liu-ch'iu; Okinawa is one of the islands of

[072-162] this group.--Formosa is Chinese: T'ai-wan (Taiwan). Korea is Chinese:

[072-163] Chao-hsien, Japanese: Chôsen.

[072-164] p. 297: M.C. Wright has shown the advisers around the ruler before the

[072-165] Empress Dowager realized the severity of the situation.--Much research

[072-166] is under way to study the beginning of industrialization of Japan, and

[072-167] my opinions have changed greatly, due to the research done by Japanese

[072-168] scholars and such Western scholars as H. Rosovsky and Th. Smith. The

[072-169] eminent role of the lower aristocracy has been established. Similar

[072-170] research for China has not even seriously started. My remarks are

[072-171] entirely preliminary.

[072-172] p. 298: For K'ang Yo-wei, I use work done by O. Franke and others. See

[072-173] M.E. Cameron, _The Reform Movement in China, 1898-1921_, Stanford 1921.

[072-174] The best bibliography for this period is J.K. Fairbank and Liu

[072-175] Kwang-ching, _Modern China: A Bibliographical Guide to Chinese Works,

[072-176] 1898-1937_, Cambridge, Mass., 1950. The political history of the time,

[072-177] as seen by a Chinese scholar, is found in Li Chien-nung, _The Political

[072-178] History of China 1840-1928_, Princeton 1956.--For the social history of

[072-179] this period see Chang Chung-li, _The Chinese Gentry_, Seattle 1955.--For

[072-180] the history of Tz[)u] Hsi Bland-Backhouse, _China under the Empress

[072-181] Dowager_, Peking 1939 (Third ed.) is antiquated, but still used. For

[072-182] some of K'ang Yo-wei's ideas, see now K'ang Yo-wei: _Ta T'ung Shu. The

[072-183] One World Philosophy of K'ang Yu Wei_, London 1957.

[072-184] _Chapter Eleven_

[072-185] p. 305: I rely here partly upon W. Franke's recent studies. For Sun

[072-186] Yat-sen (Sun I-hsien; also called Sun Chung-shan) see P. Linebarger,

[072-187] _Sun Yat-sen and the Chinese Republic_, Cambridge, Mass., 1925 and his

[072-188] later _The Political Doctrines of Sun Yat-sen_, Baltimore

[072-189] 1937.--Independently, Atatürk in Turkey developed a similar theory of

[072-190] the growth of democracy.

[072-191] p. 306: On student activities see Kiang Wen-han, _The Ideological

[072-192] Background of the Chinese Student Movement_, New York 1948.

[072-193] p. 307: On Hu Shih see his own _The Chinese Renaissance_, Chicago 1934

[072-194] and J. de Francis, _Nationalism and Language Reform in China_, Princeton

[072-195] 1950.

[072-196] p. 310: The declaration of Independence of Mongolia had its basis in the

[072-197] early treaty of the Mongols with the Manchus (1636): "In case the Tai

[072-198] Ch'ing Dynasty falls, you will exist according to previous basic laws"

[072-199] (R.J. Miller, _Monasteries and Culture Change in Inner Mongolia_,

[072-200] Wiesbaden 1959, p. 4).
