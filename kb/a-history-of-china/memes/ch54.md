# Section 54

[054-001] 14 _External and internal perils_

[054-002] Towards the end of the reign of Wan-li, about 1620, the danger that

[054-003] threatened the empire became more and more evident. The Manchus

[054-004] complained, no doubt with justice, of excesses on the part of Chinese

[054-005] officials; the friction constantly increased, and the Manchus began to

[054-006] attack the Chinese cities in Manchuria. In 1616, after his first

[054-007] considerable successes, their leader Nurhachu assumed the imperial

[054-008] title; the name of the dynasty was Tai Ch'ing (interpreted as "The great

[054-009] clarity", but probably a transliteration of a Manchurian word meaning

[054-010] "hero"). In 1618, the year in which the Thirty Years War started in

[054-011] Europe, the Manchus conquered the greater part of Manchuria, and in 1621

[054-012] their capital was Liaoyang, then the largest town in Manchuria.

[054-013] But the Manchu menace was far from being the only one. On the south-east

[054-014] coast a pirate made himself independent; later, with his family, he

[054-015] dominated Formosa and fought many battles with the Europeans there

[054-016] (European sources call him Coxinga). In western China there came a great

[054-017] popular rising, in which some of the natives joined, and which spread

[054-018] through a large part of the southern provinces. This rising was

[054-019] particularly sanguinary, and when it was ultimately crushed by the

[054-020] Manchus the province of Szechwan, formerly so populous, was almost

[054-021] depopulated, so that it had later to be resettled. And in the province

[054-022] of Shantung in the east there came another great rising, also very

[054-023] sanguinary, that of the secret society of the "White Lotus". We have

[054-024] already pointed out that these risings of secret societies were always a

[054-025] sign of intolerable conditions among the peasantry. This was now the

[054-026] case once more. All the elements of danger which we mentioned at the

[054-027] outset of this chapter began during this period, between 1610 and 1640,

[054-028] to develop to the full.

[054-029] Then there were the conditions in the capital itself. The struggles

[054-030] between cliques came to a climax. On the death of Shen Tsung (or Wan-li;

[054-031] 1573-1619), he was succeeded by his son, who died scarcely a month

[054-032] later, and then by his sixteen-year-old grandson. The grandson had been

[054-033] from his earliest youth under the influence of a eunuch, Wei

[054-034] Chung-hsien, who had castrated himself. With the emperor's wet-nurse and

[054-035] other people, mostly of the middle class, this man formed a powerful

[054-036] group. The moment the new emperor ascended the throne, Wei was

[054-037] all-powerful. He began by murdering every eunuch who did not belong to

[054-038] his clique, and then murdered the rest of his opponents. Meanwhile the

[054-039] gentry had concluded among themselves a defensive alliance that was a

[054-040] sort of party; this party was called the Tung-lin Academy. It was

[054-041] confined to literati among the gentry, and included in particular the

[054-042] literati who had failed to make their way at court, and who lived on

[054-043] their estates in Central China and were trying to gain power themselves.

[054-044] This group was opposed to Wei Chung-hsien, who ruthlessly had every

[054-045] discoverable member murdered. The remainder went into hiding and

[054-046] organized themselves secretly under another name. As the new emperor had

[054-047] no son, the attempt was made to foist a son upon him; at his death in

[054-048] 1627, eight women of the harem were suddenly found to be pregnant! He

[054-049] was succeeded by his brother, who was one of the opponents of Wei

[054-050] Chung-hsien and, with the aid of the opposing clique, was able to bring

[054-051] him to his end. The new emperor tried to restore order at court and in

[054-052] the capital by means of political and economic decrees, but in spite of

[054-053] his good intentions and his unquestionable capacity he was unable to

[054-054] cope with the universal confusion. There was insurrection in every part

[054-055] of the country. The gentry, organized in their "Academies", and secretly

[054-056] at work in the provinces, no longer supported the government; the

[054-057] central power no longer had adequate revenues, so that it was unable to

[054-058] pay the armies that should have marched against all the rebels and also

[054-059] against external enemies. It was clear that the dynasty was approaching

[054-060] its end, and the only uncertainty was as to its successor. The various

[054-061] insurgents negotiated or fought with each other; generals loyal to the

[054-062] government won occasional successes against the rebels; other generals

[054-063] went over to the rebels or to the Manchus. The two most successful

[054-064] leaders of bands were Li Tz[)u]-ch'êng and Chang Hsien-chung. Li came

[054-065] from the province of Shensi; he had come to the fore during a disastrous

[054-066] famine in his country. The years around 1640 brought several widespread

[054-067] droughts in North China, a natural phenomenon that was repeated in the

[054-068] nineteenth century, when unrest again ensued. Chang Hsien-chung returned

[054-069] for a time to the support of the government, but later established

[054-070] himself in western China. It was typical, however, of all these

[054-071] insurgents that none of them had any great objective in view. They

[054-072] wanted to get enough to eat for themselves and their followers; they

[054-073] wanted to enrich themselves by conquest; but they were incapable of

[054-074] building up an ordered and new administration. Li ultimately made

[054-075] himself "king" in the province of Shensi and called his dynasty "Shun",

[054-076] but this made no difference: there was no distribution of land among the

[054-077] peasants serving in Li's army; no plan was set into operation for the

[054-078] collection of taxes; not one of the pressing problems was faced.

[054-079] Meanwhile the Manchus were gaining support. Almost all the Mongol

[054-080] princes voluntarily joined them and took part in the raids into North

[054-081] China. In 1637 the united Manchus and Mongols conquered Korea. Their

[054-082] power steadily grew. What the insurgents in China failed to achieve, the

[054-083] Manchus achieved with the aid of their Chinese advisers: they created a

[054-084] new military organization, the "Banner Organization". The men fit for

[054-085] service were distributed among eight "banners", and these banners became

[054-086] the basis of the Manchu state administration. By this device the

[054-087] Manchus emerged from the stage of tribal union, just as before them

[054-088] Turks and other northern peoples had several times abandoned the

[054-089] traditional authority of a hierarchy of tribal leaders, a system of

[054-090] ruling families, in favour of the authority, based on efficiency, of

[054-091] military leaders. At the same time the Manchus set up a central

[054-092] government with special ministries on the Chinese model. In 1638 the

[054-093] Manchus appeared before Peking, but they retired once more. Manchu

[054-094] armies even reached the province of Shantung. They were hampered by the

[054-095] death at the critical moment of the Manchu ruler Abahai (1626-1643). His

[054-096] son Fu Lin was not entirely normal and was barely six years old; there

[054-097] was a regency of princes, the most prominent among them being Prince

[054-098] Dorgon.

[054-099] Meanwhile Li Tz[)u]-ch'êng broke through to Peking. The city had a

[054-100] strong garrison, but owing to the disorganization of the government the

[054-101] different commanders were working against each other; and the soldiers

[054-102] had no fighting spirit because they had no pay for a long time. Thus the

[054-103] city fell, on April 24th, 1644, and the last Ming emperor killed

[054-104] himself. A prince was proclaimed emperor; he fled through western and

[054-105] southern China, continually trying to make a stand, but it was too late;

[054-106] without the support of the gentry he had no resource, and ultimately, in

[054-107] 1659, he was compelled to flee into Burma.

[054-108] Thus Li Tz[)u]-ch'êng was now emperor. It should have been his task

[054-109] rapidly to build up a government, and to take up arms against the other

[054-110] rebels and against the Manchus. Instead of this he behaved in such a way

[054-111] that he was unable to gain any support from the existing officials in

[054-112] the capital; and as there was no one among his former supporters who had

[054-113] any positive, constructive ideas, just nothing was done.

[054-114] This, however, improved the chances of all the other aspirants to the

[054-115] imperial throne. The first to realize this clearly, and also to possess

[054-116] enough political sagacity to avoid alienating the gentry, was General Wu

[054-117] San-kui, who was commanding on the Manchu front. He saw that in the

[054-118] existing conditions in the capital he could easily secure the imperial

[054-119] throne for himself if only he had enough soldiers. Accordingly he

[054-120] negotiated with the Manchu Prince Dorgon, formed an alliance with the

[054-121] Manchus, and with them entered Peking on June 6th, 1644. Li

[054-122] Tz[)u]-ch'eng quickly looted the city, burned down whatever he could,

[054-123] and fled into the west, continually pursued by Wu San-kui. In the end he

[054-124] was abandoned by all his supporters and killed by peasants. The Manchus,

[054-125] however, had no intention of leaving Wu San-kui in power: they

[054-126] established themselves in Peking, and Wu became their general.

[054-127] (C) The Manchu Dynasty (1644-1911)

[054-128] 1 _Installation of Manchus_

[054-129] The Manchus had gained the mastery over China owing rather to China's

[054-130] internal situation than to their military superiority. How was it that

[054-131] the dynasty could endure for so long, although the Manchus were not

[054-132] numerous, although the first Manchu ruler (Fu Lin, known under the rule

[054-133] name Shun-chih; 1644-1662) was a psychopathic youth, although there were

[054-134] princes of the Ming dynasty ruling in South China, and although there

[054-135] were strong groups of rebels all over the country? The Manchus were

[054-136] aliens; at that time the national feeling of the Chinese had already

[054-137] been awakened; aliens were despised. In addition to this, the Manchus

[054-138] demanded that as a sign of their subjection the Chinese should wear

[054-139] pigtails and assume Manchurian clothing (law of 1645). Such laws could

[054-140] not but offend national pride. Moreover, marriages between Manchus and

[054-141] Chinese were prohibited, and a dual government was set up, with Manchus

[054-142] always alongside Chinese in every office, the Manchus being of course in

[054-143] the superior position. The Manchu soldiers were distributed in military

[054-144] garrisons among the great cities, and were paid state pensions, which

[054-145] had to be provided by taxation. They were the master race, and had no

[054-146] need to work. Manchus did not have to attend the difficult state

[054-147] examinations which the Chinese had to pass in order to gain an

[054-148] appointment. How was it that in spite of all this the Manchus were able

[054-149] to establish themselves?

[054-150] The conquering Manchu generals first went south from eastern China, and

[054-151] in 1645 captured Nanking, where a Ming prince had ruled. The region

[054-152] round Nanking was the economic centre of China. Soon the Manchus were in

[054-153] the adjoining southern provinces, and thus they conquered the whole of

[054-154] the territory of the landowning gentry, who after the events of the

[054-155] beginning of the seventeenth century had no longer trusted the Ming

[054-156] rulers. The Ming prince in Nanking was just as incapable, and surrounded

[054-157] by just as evil a clique, as the Ming emperors of the past. The gentry

[054-158] were not inclined to defend him. A considerable section of the gentry

[054-159] were reduced to utter despair; they had no desire to support the Ming

[054-160] any longer; in their own interest they could not support the rebel

[054-161] leaders; and they regarded the Manchus as just a particular sort of

[054-162] "rebels". Interpreting the refusal of some Sung ministers to serve the

[054-163] foreign Mongols as an act of loyalty, it was now regarded as shameful to

[054-164] desert a dynasty when it came to an end and to serve the new ruler, even

[054-165] if the new regime promised to be better. Many thousands of officials,

[054-166] scholars, and great landowners committed suicide. Many books, often

[054-167] really moving and tragic, are filled with the story of their lives. Some

[054-168] of them tried to form insurgent bands with their peasants and went into

[054-169] the mountains, but they were unable to maintain themselves there. The

[054-170] great bulk of the élite soon brought themselves to collaborate with the

[054-171] conquerors when they were offered tolerable conditions. In the end the

[054-172] Manchus did not interfere in the ownership of land in central China.

[054-173] At the time when in Europe Louis XIV was reigning, the Thirty Years War

[054-174] was coming to an end, and Cromwell was carrying out his reforms in

[054-175] England, the Manchus conquered the whole of China. Chang Hsien-chung and

[054-176] Li Tz[)u]-ch'êng were the first to fall; the pirate Coxinga lasted a

[054-177] little longer and was even able to plunder Nanking in 1659, but in 1661

[054-178] he had to retire to Formosa. Wu San-kui, who meanwhile had conquered

[054-179] western China, saw that the situation was becoming difficult for him.

[054-180] His task was to drive out the last Ming pretenders for the Manchus. As

[054-181] he had already been opposed to the Ming in 1644, and as the Ming no

[054-182] longer had any following among the gentry, he could not suddenly work

[054-183] with them against the Manchus. He therefore handed over to the Manchus

[054-184] the last Ming prince, whom the Burmese had delivered up to him in 1661.

[054-185] Wu San-kui's only possible allies against the Manchus were the gentry.

[054-186] But in the west, where he was in power, the gentry counted for nothing;

[054-187] they had in any case been weaker in the west, and they had been

[054-188] decimated by the insurrection of Chang Hsien-chung. Thus Wu San-kui was

[054-189] compelled to try to push eastwards, in order to unite with the gentry of

[054-190] the Yangtze region against the Manchus. The Manchus guessed Wu San-kui's

[054-191] plan, and in 1673, after every effort at accommodation had failed, open

[054-192] war came. Wu San-kui made himself emperor, and the Manchus marched

[054-193] against him. Meanwhile, the Chinese gentry of the Yangtze region had

[054-194] come to terms with the Manchus, and they gave Wu San-kui no help. He

[054-195] vegetated in the south-west, a region too poor to maintain an army that

[054-196] could conquer all China, and too small to enable him to last

[054-197] indefinitely as an independent power. He was able to hold his own until

[054-198] his death, although, with the loss of the support of the gentry, he had

[054-199] no prospect of final success. Not until 1681 was his successor, his

[054-200] grandson Wu Shih-fan, defeated. The end of the rule of Wu San-kui and

[054-201] his successor marked the end of the national governments of China; the

[054-202] whole country was now under alien domination, for the simple reason that

[054-203] all the opponents of the Manchus had failed. Only the Manchus were

[054-204] accredited with the ability to bring order out of the universal

[054-205] confusion, so that there was clearly no alternative but to put up with

[054-206] the many insults and humiliations they inflicted--with the result that

[054-207] the national feeling that had just been aroused died away, except where

[054-208] it was kept alive in a few secret societies. There will be more to say

[054-209] about this, once the works which were suppressed by the Manchus are

[054-210] published.
