## Overview

The An Lu-shan Rebellion (755–763 CE) was one of the most catastrophic civil wars in Chinese history and the defining crisis of the [[tang-dynasty|T'ang dynasty]]. Led by [[an-lu-shan|An Lu-shan]], a military governor of Sogdian-Turkish origin stationed near modern [[peking|Peking]], the rebellion swept across northern China, captured both imperial capitals, and forced the abdication of Emperor [[tang-dynasty|Hsüan Tsung]]. Though ultimately suppressed with the aid of [[uighur]] mercenary cavalry, the conflict shattered T'ang central authority and permanently altered China's political, economic, and social trajectory. The historian Eberhard identifies the end of [[an-lu-shan|An Lu-shan]]'s rebellion as the watershed moment that inaugurated China's "Modern Times" [039-061].

The rebellion was the first of many military-governor revolts that would plague the later T'ang period, and it exposed the fatal weakness of delegating military power to regional commanders. In its aftermath, the dynasty survived for another century and a half but never regained the unity and power that had made early T'ang China the pre-eminent empire of East Asia.

## Background

The roots of the rebellion lie in the structural transformation of the T'ang military system during the first half of the eighth century. To defend the empire's vast northern frontiers, the T'ang court created the office of military governor (*chieh-tu-shih*), granting regional commanders control over troops, supplies, and revenue. By the 740s, these governors commanded armies of tens of thousands and exercised near-absolute authority within their jurisdictions.

Emperor [[hsuan-tsung|Hsüan Tsung]] (r. 713–756) had presided over the cultural and political zenith of the T'ang, but in his later years he withdrew from active governance. The capital at [[yangtze-river|Ch'ang-an]] fell under the domination of the capable but unscrupulous minister [[li-lin-fu|Li Lin-fu]], who ruled as a virtual dictator from 736 to 752 and "continually played the eastern against the western gentry" [037-134]. After [[li-lin-fu|Li Lin-fu]] procured the concubine Yang Kuei-fei for the emperor, her family rose to prominence and formed a rival power bloc at court.

[[an-lu-shan|An Lu-shan]] himself was a figure of remarkable background. As the corpus records:

> "An Lu-shan (703-757). His mother was a Turkish shamaness, his father, a foreigner probably of Sogdian origin. An Lu-shan succeeded in gaining favour with the Li clique, which hoped to make use of him for its own ends." [037-147]

He rose to prominence after a victory over the Kitan in 744 and was permitted to raise an unusually large army. By 755 he commanded some 150,000 troops in the region of modern [[peking|Peking]] [037-149]. When [[li-lin-fu|Li Lin-fu]] died and the Yang family moved against him, [[an-lu-shan|An Lu-shan]] chose rebellion rather than political destruction.

## Course of the Rebellion

> "He marched against the capital, Ch'ang-an, with 200,000 men; on his way he conquered Loyang and made himself emperor (756: Yen dynasty). T'ang troops were sent against him under the leadership of the Chinese Kuo Tzu-i, a Kitan commander, and a Turk, Ko-shu Han." [037-160]

The rebellion unfolded in several distinct phases:

1. **Initial Triumph (755–756)**: [[an-lu-shan|An Lu-shan]]'s forces swept south from their base near [[peking|Peking]], overran the eastern capital Loyang, and drove toward Ch'ang-an. The T'ang general Ko-shu Han, tasked with blocking the rebels' approach to the western capital, was "quickly defeated and taken prisoner" [037-167]. Emperor [[hsuan-tsung|Hsüan Tsung]] fled toward Szechwan and abdicated; [[an-lu-shan|An Lu-shan]] captured Ch'ang-an and proclaimed the short-lived Yen dynasty.

2. **Imperial Recovery (756–757)**: The new emperor [[su-tsung|Su Tsung]] (r. 756–762) established a resistance base in north-western Shensi and desperately sought foreign aid. A small Arab contingent arrived from the Caliph Abu-Jafar alongside Uighur cavalry "in substantial strength" [037-174]. At the end of 757, a great battle near the capital saw [[an-lu-shan|An Lu-shan]] defeated by the [[uighur|Uighurs]], and shortly afterward he was murdered by one of his own eunuchs [037-175].

3. **The Second Phase under Shih Ssu-ming (758–763)**: [[an-lu-shan|An Lu-shan]]'s capable general, the Turk Shih Ssu-ming, "entered into [[an-lu-shan|An Lu-shan]]'s heritage and dominated so large a part of eastern China" that the T'ang once again needed Uighur assistance [037-181]. Though Shih Ssu-ming initially recaptured Loyang, he too was murdered, this time by his own son. The resulting factional chaos among the rebels allowed government forces to finally suppress the rising.

## Aftermath and Consequences

The cost of suppressing the rebellion fundamentally transformed the T'ang state. The [[uighur|Uighurs]], whose cavalry had proven decisive, extracted enormous payment: 10,000 rolls of silk immediately, an annual tribute of 20,000 rolls, and the hand of an imperial princess for the Uighur khan [037-178]. The dynasty's dependence on foreign troops "brought the end of the T'ang dynasty" in the longer term, as military governors across the empire followed [[an-lu-shan|An Lu-shan]]'s precedent and built autonomous power bases [039-061].

In the devastated north, the old aristocracy never recovered its former wealth and influence. The empire's economic centre of gravity shifted permanently southward toward the [[yangtze-river|Yangtze River]] valley, where trade and commerce flourished relatively undisturbed by the war. This geographic rebalancing contributed to the rise of a commercial middle class that would characterize Chinese society in subsequent centuries.

The rebellion also triggered a broader crisis of confidence in the T'ang model of governance. The equal-land system, the *pao-chia* mutual-responsibility network, and the entire T'ang fiscal apparatus — already under strain before the rebellion — unraveled as the court lost the ability to conduct accurate population registers in rebel-controlled territories.

## Historical Significance

Eberhard identifies the [[an-lu-shan|An Lu-shan]] rebellion as nothing less than the inception of a new historical epoch:

> "The first beginnings of the changes which lead to the 'Modern Times' actually can be seen from the end of An Lu-shan's rebellion on, from c. A.D. 780 on, and the transformation was more or less completed only in the middle of the eleventh century." [039-061]

The rebellion's significance extends across multiple dimensions:

- **Military**: It was "the first of many similar ones in the later T'ang period," demonstrating that the military-governor system was inherently destabilising whenever central authority weakened [038-006]. The rebels [[an-lu-shan|An Lu-shan]] and Shih Ssu-ming were both non-Chinese, as were many of the loyalist commanders, illustrating the multi-ethnic character of late T'ang military power [038-012].

- **Political**: The rebellion ended the era of strong unified imperial rule that had characterised the early T'ang. After 763, the central government never again exercised uncontested control over the whole empire. Regional autonomy grew inexorably until the dynasty's final collapse in 906.

- **Economic**: The destruction of the northern heartland accelerated the long-term southward shift of population and economic activity, a trend that would continue through the [[ming-dynasty|Ming dynasty]] and beyond. [[an-lu-shan|An Lu-shan]] himself is noted in the corpus as having "been a businessman for a while" — a detail that reflects the growing importance of commerce in T'ang society [039-055].

- **Cultural**: The rebellion shattered the cosmopolitan self-confidence of High T'ang culture. The age of poets like [[li-po|Li Po]] and [[tu-fu|Tu Fu]], of the magnificent court ceremonial of [[hsuan-tsung|Hsüan Tsung]], gave way to an era of retrenchment and introspection. The [[buddhism|Buddhist]] and [[confucianism|Confucian]] institutions that had flourished under imperial patronage now faced questioning and, in the case of Buddhism, eventual persecution in the ninth century.

The [[an-lu-shan|An Lu-shan]] rebellion thus stands as one of the decisive turning points of Chinese history — comparable in its transformative impact to the [[yellow-turban-rebellion|Yellow Turban Rebellion]] that had brought down the Han dynasty seven centuries earlier, and presaging the military fragmentation that would characterise the Five Dynasties period after the T'ang's fall.
