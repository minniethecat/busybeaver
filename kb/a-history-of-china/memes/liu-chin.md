## Overview

[[liu-chin|Liu Chin]] (d. 1510) was the most powerful eunuch of the Ming dynasty during the early reign of the [[cheng-te-emperor|Cheng-te Emperor]] (r. 1505–1521). Rising from the palace staff to effective control of the imperial bureaucracy, he briefly held a concentration of personal power that exceeded any Ming eunuch before him. His spectacular downfall — arrested, executed, and his vast fortune confiscated after a revolt triggered investigations into his misrule — provided a cautionary tale that Ming historians would retell for generations [052-141][052-149].

## Rise to Power

Liu Chin came to prominence following the death of the [[hsiung-nu|Hu]]ng-chih Emperor in 1505, when the fourteen-year-old Cheng-te Emperor ascended the throne. The young monarch showed little interest in the routines of government, preferring hunting, music, and the company of his palace attendants. Liu Chin, as head of the eunuch staff, moved into the vacuum, building a coalition of eunuchs and opportunistic officials who controlled access to the emperor and directed government business on his behalf [052-141].

> The leader of the eunuchs was Liu Chin, who had the support of a group of officials drawn from both the upper gentry and the middle class. [052-141]

Within a remarkably short time, Liu Chin succeeded in placing his own men in key positions throughout the administration. His most important decree — a comprehensive land-tax reassessment — was officially framed as an anti-corruption measure but in practice served to extort payments from landowners, enriching the eunuch establishment while punishing its gentry opponents [052-142][052-149].

> Liu Chin succeeded within a year in placing his followers in the most important positions of the state. The most important decree issued by Liu Chin resulted in a large-scale land-tax reassessment. [052-142][052-149]

## Opposition and Downfall

Liu Chin's power provoked resistance from the civil bureaucracy and the military establishment. He punished numerous members of the gentry who opposed him, creating a reservoir of resentment that waited only for an opportunity to strike. That opportunity came in 1510, when a revolt broke out among the imperial princes — a rebellion Liu Chin had partly financed through his own exactions and which now turned against its patron [052-161][052-162][052-166].

> Among the people whom Liu Chin had punished were several members of the upper gentry who had been banished to distant frontier posts. [052-162]

The general sent to crush the revolt — a man who had secured his appointment without Liu Chin's knowledge — used his newly acquired intelligence about Liu Chin's financial network to expose the eunuch's treachery. When the army returned victorious, Liu Chin's palace was searched, revealing stores of weapons, precious metals, and imperial regalia that strongly suggested plans for usurpation [052-166][052-168][052-169].

> After the crushing of the revolt, Liu Chin's palace was searched and arms, treasures, and seals were found; he was executed and his property was confiscated. [052-169]

Liu Chin's downfall was characteristically swift — arrested, tried, and executed in the same campaign season. The episode reinforced a narrative deeply embedded in Ming political culture: the eunuch who overreaches inevitably falls, and the fall restores the moral equilibrium of the Confucian state [052-172].

## Legacy

Liu Chin's rule had done lasting damage to Ming governance. The talented official and philosopher [[wang-yang-ming|Wang Yang-ming]] (1473–1529), who had protested against Liu Chin's abuses and suffered banishment as a result, would later reflect that the eunuch's regime had exposed the fragility of institutional checks on arbitrary power. The episode fed directly into the reformist energies of the mid-Ming, as [[gentry|scholar-official]]s sought constitutional and moral restraints on the imperial household [052-172].

> Liu Chin's rule had done much to discredit the eunuch system among the gentry. [052-172]
