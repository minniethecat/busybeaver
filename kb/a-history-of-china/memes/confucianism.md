## Overview

Confucianism began as the teachings of [[confucius|Confucius]] (551–479 BCE) but evolved into a comprehensive ethical, political, and social system that dominated Chinese civilization for over two millennia. Eberhard describes Confucianism not as a religion in the Western sense but as a social philosophy that "became binding on every member of the gentry class" and provided the ideological foundation of the Chinese imperial state [009-039].

Unlike [[buddhism|Buddhism]] or Christianity, Confucianism did not develop a professional priesthood, a developed theology of the afterlife, or an ecclesiastical organization separate from the state. Instead, it functioned as what Eberhard calls the "philosophic basis of the gentry state" [018-182] — a shared ethical language that united the [[gentry|scholar-official]] class and legitimated the imperial order.

## Core Doctrines

> "Confucianism did not become a religion. It became the ethical code of the gentry class. Only then did Confucianism begin to exercise its enormous influence on Chinese society." [009-042]

The essential tenets of Confucianism include:

- **Filial piety** (hsiao): Reverence for parents and ancestors, extended to loyalty toward the ruler
- **[[hsiung-nu|Hu]]maneness** (jên): Benevolence and compassion in human relationships
- **Ritual propriety** (li): Proper conduct in ceremonies and daily life
- **The rectification of names** (chêng-ming): Social roles must correspond to their titles
- **Cultivation of virtue**: Self-discipline and moral education as the path to a well-ordered society

Confucianism solved the problem of how family ethics could be extended to the political realm. As Eberhard explains, the relationship of son to father became the model for the relationship of subject to ruler — the state was conceived as a family writ large [008-158].

## Rise as State Ideology

During the [[han-dynasty|Han dynasty]], Confucianism was elevated from one school among many to the official state doctrine. The decisive step came when it was decreed that every official should be versed in the Confucian classics, and schools were established for the purpose of Confucian education [016-024].

> "A powerful part was played by Confucianism in the formation of the Chinese state and its administration." [009-049]

The [[examination-system|imperial examination system]], which selected officials based on their knowledge of the Confucian canon, was the institutional mechanism through which Confucianism maintained its dominance. By tying social advancement to mastery of the Confucian classics, the system ensured that the gentry class — and through them, the entire society — remained oriented toward Confucian values.

## Evolution and Neo-Confucianism

Confucianism was not static. Over the centuries, it absorbed elements from competing traditions:

| Period | Development |
|--------|------------|
| 6th–5th c. BCE | [[confucius|Confucius]] teaches; core ideas established |
| 4th–3rd c. BCE | [[mencius|Mencius]] and Hsün Tzŭ elaborate rival interpretations |
| 2nd c. BCE | Han dynasty adopts Confucianism as state doctrine |
| 3rd–6th c. CE | Buddhism and [[taoism|Taoism]] challenge Confucian dominance |
| 10th–13th c. CE | Neo-Confucian synthesis: [[chu-hsi|Chu Hsi]] and the Sung revival |

The Sung dynasty saw the great Neo-Confucian revival. Philosophers like [[chu-hsi|Chu Hsi]] and the brothers Ch'êng synthesized Confucian ethics with elements borrowed from Buddhist metaphysics and Taoist cosmology. This Neo-Confucianism, with its emphasis on the investigation of things (ko-wu) and the principle of Heaven (t'ien-li), became the orthodox interpretation that dominated Chinese intellectual life until the twentieth century [034-115].

## Social Role and Legacy

> "Confucianism had been restored in accordance with the needs of the time, and the gentry went strong support for Confucianism, and a reorganization of the state on Confucian lines was carried through." [034-115]

Confucianism was more than a philosophy — it was the cultural glue that held Chinese society together. It provided the script for family life, the curriculum for education, the ethics of government, and the shared vocabulary of public discourse. Even when dynasties fell and foreign conquerors ruled, the Confucian system proved remarkably resilient [027-076].

In the twentieth century, Confucianism was attacked by reformers and revolutionaries as an obstacle to modernization. Yet its values — respect for education, family solidarity, moral self-cultivation, and social harmony — have persisted in Chinese culture well beyond the formal abolition of the imperial system.
