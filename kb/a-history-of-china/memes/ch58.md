# Section 58

[058-001] This treaty introduced the period of the Capitulations. It contained

[058-002] the dangerous clause which added most to China's misfortunes--the Most

[058-003] Favoured Nation clause, providing that if China granted any privilege to

[058-004] any other state, that privilege should also automatically be granted to

[058-005] Great Britain. In connection with this treaty it was agreed that the

[058-006] Chinese customs should be supervised by European consuls; and a trade

[058-007] treaty was granted. Similar treaties followed in 1844 with France and

[058-008] the United States. The missionaries returned; until 1860, however, they

[058-009] were only permitted to work in the treaty ports. Shanghai was thrown

[058-010] open in 1843, and developed with extraordinary rapidity from a town to a

[058-011] city of a million and a centre of world-wide importance.

[058-012] The terms of the Nanking Treaty were not observed by either side; both

[058-013] evaded them. In order to facilitate the smuggling, the British had

[058-014] permitted certain Chinese junks to fly the British flag. This also

[058-015] enabled these vessels to be protected by British ships-of-war from

[058-016] pirates, which at that time were very numerous off the southern coast

[058-017] owing to the economic depression. The Chinese, for their part, placed

[058-018] every possible obstacle in the way of the British. In 1856 the Chinese

[058-019] held up a ship sailing under the British flag, pulled down its flag, and

[058-020] arrested the crew on suspicion of smuggling. In connection with this and

[058-021] other events, Britain decided to go to war. Thus began the "Lorcha War"

[058-022] of 1857, in which France joined for the sake of the booty to be

[058-023] expected. Britain had just ended the Crimean War, and was engaged in

[058-024] heavy fighting against the Moguls in India. Consequently only a small

[058-025] force of a few thousand men could be landed in China; Canton, however,

[058-026] was bombarded, and also the forts of Tientsin. There still seemed no

[058-027] prospect of gaining the desired objectives by negotiation, and in 1860 a

[058-028] new expedition was fitted out, this time some 20,000 strong. The troops

[058-029] landed at Tientsin and marched on Peking; the emperor fled to Jehol and

[058-030] did not return; he died in 1861. The new Treaty of Tientsin (1860)

[058-031] provided for (a) the opening of further ports to European traders; (b)

[058-032] the session of Kowloon, the strip of land lying opposite Hong Kong; (c)

[058-033] the establishment of a British legation in Peking; (d) freedom of

[058-034] navigation along the Yangtze; (e) permission for British subjects to

[058-035] purchase land in China; (f) the British to be subject to their own

[058-036] consular courts and not to the Chinese courts; (g) missionary activity

[058-037] to be permitted throughout the country. In addition to this, the

[058-038] commercial treaty was revised, the opium trade was permitted once more,

[058-039] and a war indemnity was to be paid by China. In the eyes of Europe,

[058-040] Britain had now succeeded in turning China not actually into a colony,

[058-041] but at all events into a semi-colony; China must be expected soon to

[058-042] share the fate of India. China, however, with her very different

[058-043] conceptions of intercourse between states, did not realize the full

[058-044] import of these terms; some of them were regarded as concessions on

[058-045] unimportant points, which there was no harm in granting to the trading

[058-046] "barbarians", as had been done in the past; some were regarded as simple

[058-047] injustices, which at a given moment could be swept away by

[058-048] administrative action.

[058-049] But the result of this European penetration was that China's balance of

[058-050] trade was adverse, and became more and more so, as under the commercial

[058-051] treaties she could neither stop the importation of European goods nor

[058-052] set a duty on them; and on the other hand she could not compel

[058-053] foreigners to buy Chinese goods. The efflux of silver brought general

[058-054] impoverishment to China, widespread financial stringency to the state,

[058-055] and continuous financial crises and inflation. China had never had much

[058-056] liquid capital, and she was soon compelled to take up foreign loans in

[058-057] order to pay her debts. At that time internal loans were out of the

[058-058] question (the first internal loan was floated in 1894): the population

[058-059] did not even know what a state loan meant; consequently the loans had to

[058-060] be issued abroad. This, however, entailed the giving of securities,

[058-061] generally in the form of economic privileges. Under the Most Favoured

[058-062] Nation clause, however, these privileges had then to be granted to other

[058-063] states which had made no loans to China. Clearly a vicious spiral, which

[058-064] in the end could only bring disaster.

[058-065] The only exception to the general impoverishment, in which not only the

[058-066] peasants but the old upper classes were involved, was a certain section

[058-067] of the trading community and the middle class, which had grown rich

[058-068] through its dealings with the Europeans. These people now accumulated

[058-069] capital, became Europeanized with their staffs, acquired land from the

[058-070] impoverished gentry, and sent their sons abroad to foreign universities.

[058-071] They founded the first industrial undertakings, and learned European

[058-072] capitalist methods. This class was, of course, to be found mainly in the

[058-073] treaty ports in the south and in their environs. The south, as far north

[058-074] as Shanghai, became more modern and more advanced; the north made no

[058-075] advance. In the south, European ways of thought were learnt, and Chinese

[058-076] and European theories were compared. Criticism began. The first

[058-077] revolutionary societies were formed in this atmosphere in the south.

[058-078] 8 _Risings in Turkestan and within China: the T'ai P'ing Rebellion_

[058-079] But the emperor Hsüan Tsung (reign name Tao-kuang), a man in poor health

[058-080] though not without ability, had much graver anxieties than those caused

[058-081] by the Europeans. He did not yet fully realize the seriousness of the

[058-082] European peril.

[058-086] Hammer-Morrisson_.]

[058-087] In Turkestan, where Turkish Mohammedans lived under

[058-088] Chinese rule, conditions were far from being as the Chinese desired. The

[058-089] Chinese, a fundamentally rationalistic people, regarded religion as a

[058-090] purely political matter, and accordingly required every citizen to take

[058-091] part in the official form of worship. Subject to that, he might

[058-092] privately belong to any other religion. To a Mohammedan, this was

[058-093] impossible and intolerable. The Mohammedans were only ready to practice

[058-094] their own religion, and absolutely refused to take part in any other.

[058-095] The Chinese also tried to apply to Turkestan in other matters the same

[058-096] legislation that applied to all China, but this proved irreconcilable

[058-097] with the demands made by Islam on its followers. All this produced

[058-098] continual unrest.

[058-099] Turkestan had a feudal system of government with a number of feudal

[058-100] lords (_beg_), who tried to maintain their influence and who had the

[058-101] support of the Mohammedan population. The Chinese had come to Turkestan

[058-102] as soldiers and officials, to administer the country. They regarded

[058-103] themselves as the lords of the land and occupied themselves with the

[058-104] extraction of taxes. Most of the officials were also associated with the

[058-105] Chinese merchants who travelled throughout Turkestan and as far as

[058-106] Siberia. The conflicts implicit in this situation produced great

[058-107] Mohammedan risings in the nineteenth century. The first came in

[058-108] 1825-1827; in 1845 a second rising flamed up, and thirty years later

[058-109] these revolts led to the temporary loss of the whole of Turkestan.

[058-110] In 1848, native unrest began in the province of Hunan, as a result of

[058-111] the constantly growing pressure of the Chinese settlers on the native

[058-112] population; in the same year there was unrest farther south, in the

[058-113] province of Kwangsi, this time in connection with the influence of the

[058-114] Europeans. The leader was a quite simple man of Hakka blood, Hung

[058-115] Hsiu-ch'üan (born 1814), who gathered impoverished Hakka peasants round

[058-116] him as every peasant leader had done in the past. Very often the nucleus

[058-117] of these peasant movements had been a secret society with a particular

[058-118] religious tinge; this time the peasant revolutionaries came forward as

[058-119] at the same time the preachers of a new religion of their own. Hung had

[058-120] heard of Christianity from missionaries (1837), and he mixed up

[058-121] Christian ideas with those of ancient China and proclaimed to his

[058-122] followers a doctrine that promised the Kingdom of God on earth. He

[058-123] called himself "Christ's younger brother", and his kingdom was to be

[058-124] called _T'ai P'ing_ ("Supreme Peace"). He made his first comrades,

[058-125] charcoal makers, local doctors, peddlers and farmers, into kings, and

[058-126] made himself emperor. At bottom the movement, like all similar ones

[058-127] before it, was not religious but social; and it produced a great

[058-128] response from the peasants. The program of the T'ai P'ing, in some

[058-129] points influenced by Christian ideas but more so by traditional Chinese

[058-130] thought, was in many points revolutionary: (a) all property was communal

[058-131] property; (b) land was classified into categories according to its

[058-132] fertility and equally distributed among men and women. Every producer

[058-133] kept of the produce as much as he and his family needed and delivered

[058-134] the rest into the communal granary; (c) administration and tax systems

[058-135] were revised; (d) women were given equal rights: they fought together

[058-136] with men in the army and had access to official position. They had to

[058-137] marry, but monogamy was requested; (e) the use of opium, tobacco and

[058-138] alcohol was prohibited, prostitution was illegal; (f) foreigners were

[058-139] regarded as equals, capitulations as the Manchus had accepted were not

[058-140] recognized. A large part of the officials, and particularly of the

[058-141] soldiers sent against the revolutionaries, were Manchus, and

[058-142] consequently the movement very soon became a nationalist movement, much

[058-143] as the popular movement at the end of the Mongol epoch had done. Hung

[058-144] made rapid progress; in 1852 he captured Hankow, and in 1853 Nanking,

[058-145] the important centre in the east. With clear political insight he made

[058-146] Nanking his capital. In this he returned to the old traditions of the

[058-147] beginning of the Ming epoch, no doubt expecting in this way to attract

[058-148] support from the eastern Chinese gentry, who had no liking for a capital

[058-149] far away in the north. He made a parade of adhesion to the ancient

[058-150] Chinese tradition: his followers cut off their pigtails and allowed

[058-151] their hair to grow as in the past.

[058-152] He did not succeed, however, in carrying his reforms from the stage of

[058-153] sporadic action to a systematic reorganization of the country, and he

[058-154] also failed to enlist the elements needed for this as for all other

[058-155] administrative work, so that the good start soon degenerated into a

[058-156] terrorist regime.

[058-157] Hung's followers pressed on from Nanking, and in 1853-1855 they advanced

[058-158] nearly to Tientsin; but they failed to capture Peking itself.

[058-159] The new T'ai P'ing state faced the Europeans with big problems. Should

[058-160] they work with it or against it? The T'ai P'ing always insisted that

[058-161] they were Christians; the missionaries hoped now to have the opportunity

[058-162] of converting all China to Christianity. The T'ai P'ing treated the

[058-163] missionaries well but did not let them operate. After long hesitation

[058-164] and much vacillation, however, the Europeans placed themselves on the

[058-165] side of the Manchus. Not out of any belief that the T'ai P'ing movement

[058-166] was without justification, but because they had concluded treaties with

[058-167] the Manchu government and given loans to it, of which nothing would

[058-168] have remained if the Manchus had fallen; because they preferred the weak

[058-169] Manchu government to a strong T'ai P'ing government; and because they

[058-170] disliked the socialistic element in many of the measured adopted by the

[058-171] T'ai P'ing.

[058-172] At first it seemed as if the Manchus would be able to cope unaided with

[058-173] the T'ai P'ing, but the same thing happened as at the end of the Mongol

[058-174] rule: the imperial armies, consisting of the "banners" of the Manchus,

[058-175] the Mongols, and some Chinese, had lost their military skill in the long

[058-176] years of peace; they had lost their old fighting spirit and were glad to

[058-177] be able to live in peace on their state pensions. Now three men came to

[058-178] the fore--a Mongol named Seng-ko-lin-ch'in, a man of great personal

[058-179] bravery, who defended the interests of the Manchu rulers; and two

[058-180] Chinese, Tsêng Kuo-fan (1811-1892) and Li Hung-chang (1823-1901), who

[058-181] were in the service of the Manchus but used their position simply to

[058-182] further the interests of the gentry. The Mongol saved Peking from

[058-183] capture by the T'ai P'ing. The two Chinese were living in central China,

[058-184] and there they recruited, Li at his own expense and Tsêng out of the

[058-185] resources at his disposal as a provincial governor, a sort of militia,

[058-186] consisting of peasants out to protect their homes from destruction by

[058-187] the peasants of the T'ai P'ing. Thus the peasants of central China, all

[058-188] suffering from impoverishment, were divided into two groups, one

[058-189] following the T'ai P'ing, the other following Tsêng Kuo-fan. Tsêng's

[058-190] army, too, might be described as a "national" army, because Tsêng was

[058-191] not fighting for the interests of the Manchus. Thus the peasants, all

[058-192] anti-Manchu, could choose between two sides, between the T'ai P'ing and

[058-193] Tsêng Kuo-fan. Although Tsêng represented the gentry and was thus

[058-194] against the simple common people, peasants fought in masses on his side,

[058-195] for he paid better, and especially more regularly. Tsêng, being a good

[058-196] strategist, won successes and gained adherents. Thus by 1856 the T'ai

[058-197] P'ing were pressed back on Nanking and some of the towns round it; in

[058-198] 1864 Nanking was captured.
