## Overview

[[wen-ti|Wen Ti]] (Emperor Wen of Han, r. 180–157 BC) was a son of [[kao-tsu|Kao Tsu]], the founder of the [[han-dynasty|Han dynasty]]. His reign marked a decisive turning point in early imperial history — a period of consolidation, economic recovery, and the quiet penetration of Confucian ideals into the machinery of the state. Where his successor [[wu-ti|Wu Ti]] would become famous for military expansion, Wen Ti's legacy was the creation of the stable, prosperous foundation that made such expansion possible.

## A Reign of Peace and Frugality

The contrast with the turbulent decades that preceded Wen Ti's accession could hardly have been sharper:

> On the whole Wen Ti's period of rule passed in comparative peace. For the first time since the beginning of Chinese history, great areas of continuous territory were under unified rule, without unending internal warfare such as had existed under Shih Huang-ti and Kao Tsu. The creation of so extensive a region of peace produced great economic advantages. [016-001]

The burdens on the peasantry were substantially reduced. Wen Ti's court was notably frugal, avoiding the expensive projects and military adventures that had drained the treasury under previous reigns [016-007]. This restraint allowed the population to grow, fresh land to be brought under cultivation, and production to increase — generating a virtuous cycle of rising output and expanding trade [016-008].

## Economic Liberalization

One of the most striking policies of Wen Ti's reign was monetary liberalization:

> The most outstanding sign of this was the abandonment of restrictions on the minting of copper coin, in order to prevent deflation through insufficiency of payment media. As a consequence more taxes were brought in, partly in kind, partly in coin, and this increased the power of the central government. [016-010]

The economic advance enriched not only the state but also the emerging gentry class. These landowners and officials "streamed into the towns, their standard of living rose, and they made themselves more and more into a class apart from the general population" [016-014]. Freed from material cares, they turned to scholarship — with consequences that would reshape Chinese civilization.

## The Confucian Turn

The gentry's turn to classical learning under Wen Ti's prosperous peace had profound long-term effects:

> They went back to the old writings and studied them once more. They even began to identify themselves with the nobles of feudal times, to adopt the rules of good behaviour and the ceremonial described in the Confucianist books, and very gradually, as time went on, to make these their textbooks of good form. From this point the Confucianist ideals first began to penetrate the official class recruited from the gentry, and then the state organization itself. [016-017]

It was expected that an official should be versed in [[confucianism|Confucianism]], and schools were established for Confucian education. Around 100 BC, this led to the introduction of the [[examination-system|examination system]] — which would remain in operation, in principle, until 1904 [016-025].

## The Social Order Consolidated

The Wen Ti era also saw the legal formalization of China's traditional four-class social structure. Officials (*shih*) and farmers (*nung*) were recognized as the two "respectable" classes (*liang-min*), while artisans (*kung*) and merchants (*shang*) labored under numerous restrictions. Below them were "lowly people" (*ch'ien-min*) and, below even these, slaves who were not considered part of society proper [016-034].

## Legacy

Wen Ti's reign demonstrated that imperial strength did not depend solely on military power. By restraining expenditure, liberalizing the economy, and presiding over an extended peace, he created conditions in which population, production, and culture could flourish. The capital grew rich through his policies [017-158], and the Confucian character of the Chinese state — which would endure for two millennia — first took root in the prosperous soil his reign provided.
