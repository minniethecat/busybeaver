## Overview

**Liu Lao-chih** was a military commander of the Eastern [[Jin Dynasty|Jin]] period, active during the late fourth and early fifth centuries. His shifting allegiances between the great families of the south — the Hsieh, the Wang, and the upstart [[huan-hsuan|Huan Hsüan]] — directly shaped the political transition from the Eastern Jin to the brief [[hsiung-nu|Hu]]an Ch'u interregnum and ultimately to the Liu Sung dynasty.

## Role in the Jin Power Struggles

Liu Lao-chih operated in a period of intense factional conflict among the southern aristocracy. The Hsieh and Wang families dominated the court, while [[huan-hsuan|Huan Hsüan]] — a military governor from the upcountry — sought to carve out his own domain:

> "At first this new clique had success, but later one of its generals, Liu Lao-chih, went over to the Hsieh clique, and its power declined." [032-091] [032-092] [032-093]

His defection from the Yin–[[hsiung-nu|Hu]]an coalition to the Hsieh camp was decisive in checking [[huan-hsuan|Huan Hsüan]]'s first bid for power.

## Betrayal of the Capital

By 402, [[huan-hsuan|Huan Hsüan]] had rebuilt his strength and marched on the Jin capital at Chien-k'ang (modern [[Nanking]]). Liu Lao-chih, who had been entrusted with the defence of the capital, instead chose to switch sides again:

> "At that moment Liu Lao-chih was responsible for the defence of the capital from Huan Hsüan, but instead he passed over." [032-101] [032-102]

His defection allowed [[huan-hsuan|Huan Hsüan]] to seize the capital, depose the reigning emperor, and proclaim a new dynasty in 403. The [[hsiung-nu|Hu]]an Ch'u regime, however, lasted only a few months.

## Legacy and the Rise of Liu Yü

The reaction against [[huan-hsuan|Huan Hsüan]] was led by one of Liu Lao-chih's own former subordinates:

> "Then came the reaction, led by an earlier subordinate of Liu Lao-chih, Liu Yü. It may be assumed that these two army commanders were in some way related, though the two branches of their family must have been long separated." [032-104] [032-105] [032-106] [032-107]

Liu Yü went on to defeat [[huan-hsuan|Huan Hsüan]], restore the Jin (briefly), and then in 420 depose the last Jin emperor to found the Liu Sung dynasty — the first of the Southern Dynasties. Liu Lao-chih's career thus bookended the final collapse of the Eastern Jin.
