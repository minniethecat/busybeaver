[001-264] INTRODUCTION

[001-265] There are indeed enough Histories of China already: why yet another one?

[001-266] Because the time has come for new departures; because we need to clear

[001-267] away the false notions with which the general public is constantly being

[001-268] fed by one author after another; because from time to time syntheses

[001-269] become necessary for the presentation of the stage reached by research.

[001-270] Histories of China fall, with few exceptions, into one or the other of

[001-271] two groups, pro-Chinese and anti-Chinese: the latter used to

[001-272] predominate, but today the former type is much more frequently found. We

[001-273] have no desire to show that China's history is the most glorious or her

[001-274] civilization the oldest in the world. A claim to the longest history

[001-275] does not establish the greatness of a civilization; the importance of a

[001-276] civilization becomes apparent in its achievements. A thousand years ago

[001-277] China's civilization towered over those of the peoples of Europe. Today

[001-278] the West is leading; tomorrow China may lead again. We need to realize

[001-279] how China became what she is, and to note the paths pursued by the

[001-280] Chinese in human thought and action. The lives of emperors, the great

[001-281] battles, this or the other famous deed, matter less to us than the

[001-282] discovery of the great forces that underlie these features and govern

[001-283] the human element. Only when we have knowledge of those forces and

[001-284] counter-forces can we realize the significance of the great

[001-285] personalities who have emerged in China; and only then will the history

[001-286] of China become intelligible even to those who have little knowledge of

[001-287] the Far East and can make nothing of a mere enumeration of dynasties and

[001-288] campaigns.

[001-289] Views on China's history have radically changed in recent years. Until

[001-290] about thirty years ago our knowledge of the earliest times in China

[001-291] depended entirely on Chinese documents of much later date; now we are

[001-292] able to rely on many excavations which enable us to check the written

[001-293] sources. Ethnological, anthropological, and sociological research has

[001-294] begun for China and her neighbours; thus we are in a position to write

[001-295] with some confidence about the making of China, and about her ethnical

[001-296] development, where formerly we could only grope in the dark. The claim

[001-297] that "the Chinese race" produced the high Chinese civilization entirely

[001-298] by its own efforts, thanks to its special gifts, has become just as

[001-299] untenable as the other theory that immigrants from the West, some

[001-300] conceivably from Europe, carried civilization to the Far East. We know

[001-301] now that in early times there was no "Chinese race", there were not even

[001-302] "Chinese", just as there were no "French" and no "Swiss" two thousand

[001-303] years ago. The "Chinese" resulted from the amalgamation of many separate

[001-304] peoples of different races in an enormously complicated and

[001-305] long-drawn-out process, as with all the other high civilizations of the

[001-306] world.
