# Section 17

[017-001] In all this there gradually developed a division into civil and military

[017-002] administration. A number of regions would make up a province with a

[017-003] military governor, who was in a sense the representative of the imperial

[017-004] army, and who was supposed to come into activity only in the event of

[017-005] war.

[017-006] This administration of the Han period lacked the tight organization that

[017-007] would make precise functioning possible. On the other hand, an

[017-008] extremely important institution had already come into existence in a

[017-009] primitive form. As central statistical authority, the court secretariat

[017-010] had a special position within the ministries and supervised the

[017-011] administration of the other offices. Thus there existed alongside the

[017-012] executive a means of independent supervision of it, and the resulting

[017-013] rivalry enabled the emperor or the chancellor to detect and eliminate

[017-014] irregularities. Later, in the system of the T'ang period (A.D. 618-906),

[017-015] this institution developed into an independent censorship, and the

[017-016] system was given a new form as a "State and Court Secretariat", in which

[017-017] the whole executive was comprised and unified. Towards the end of the

[017-018] T'ang period the permanent state of war necessitated the permanent

[017-019] commissioning of the imperial generals-in-chief and of the military

[017-020] governors, and as a result there came into existence a "Privy Council of

[017-021] State", which gradually took over functions of the executive. The system

[017-081] There is no denying that according to our standard this whole system was

[017-082] still elementary and "personal", that is to say, attached to the

[017-083] emperor's person--though it should not be overlooked that we ourselves

[017-084] are not yet far from a similar phase of development. To this day the

[017-085] titles of not a few of the highest officers of state--the Lord Privy

[017-086] Seal, for instance--recall that in the past their offices were conceived

[017-087] as concerned purely with the personal service of the monarch. In one

[017-088] point, however, the Han administrative set-up was quite modern: it

[017-089] already had a clear separation between the emperor's private treasury

[017-090] and the state treasury; laws determined which of the two received

[017-091] certain taxes and which had to make certain payments. This separation,

[017-092] which in Europe occurred not until the late Middle Ages, in China was

[017-093] abolished at the end of the Han Dynasty.

[017-094] The picture changes considerably to the advantage of the Chinese as

[017-095] soon as we consider the provincial administration. The governor of a

[017-096] province, and each of his district officers or prefects, had a staff

[017-097] often of more than a hundred officials. These officials were drawn from

[017-098] the province or prefecture and from the personal friends of the

[017-099] administrator, and they were appointed by the governor or the prefect.

[017-100] The staff was made up of officials responsible for communications with

[017-101] the central or provincial administration (private secretary, controller,

[017-102] finance officer), and a group of officials who carried on the actual

[017-103] local administration. There were departments for transport, finance,

[017-104] education, justice, medicine (hygiene), economic and military affairs,

[017-105] market control, and presents (which had to be made to the higher

[017-106] officials at the New Year and on other occasions). In addition to these

[017-107] offices, organized in a quite modern style, there was an office for

[017-108] advising the governor and another for drafting official documents and

[017-109] letters.

[017-110] The interesting feature of this system is that the provincial

[017-111] administration was _de facto_ independent of the central administration,

[017-112] and that the governor and even his prefects could rule like kings in

[017-113] their regions, appointing and discharging as they chose. This was a

[017-114] vestige of feudalism, but on the other hand it was a healthy check

[017-115] against excessive centralization. It is thanks to this system that even

[017-116] the collapse of the central power or the cutting off of a part of the

[017-117] empire did not bring the collapse of the country. In a remote frontier

[017-118] town like Tunhuang, on the border of Turkestan, the life of the local

[017-119] Chinese went on undisturbed whether communication with the capital was

[017-120] maintained or was broken through invasions by foreigners. The official

[017-121] sent from the centre would be liable at any time to be transferred

[017-122] elsewhere; and he had to depend on the practical knowledge of his

[017-123] subordinates, the members of the local families of the gentry. These

[017-124] officials had the local government in their hands, and carried on the

[017-125] administration of places like Tunhuang through a thousand years and

[017-126] more. The Hsin family, for instance, was living there in 50 B.C. and was

[017-127] still there in A.D. 950; and so were the Yin, Ling-hu, Li, and K'ang

[017-128] families.

[017-129] All the officials of the various offices or Ministries were appointed

[017-130] under the state examination system, but they had no special professional

[017-131] training; only for the more important subordinate posts were there

[017-132] specialists, such as jurists, physicians, and so on. A change came

[017-133] towards the end of the T'ang period, when a Department of Commerce and

[017-134] Monopolies was set up; only specialists were appointed to it, and it was

[017-135] placed directly under the emperor. Except for this, any official could

[017-136] be transferred from any ministry to any other without regard to his

[017-137] experience.

[017-138] 4 _Turkestan policy. End of the Hsiung-nu empire_

[017-139] In the two decades between 160 and 140 B.C. there had been further

[017-140] trouble with the Hsiung-nu, though there was no large-scale fighting.

[017-141] There was a fundamental change of policy under the next emperor, Wu (or

[017-142] Wu Ti, 141-86 B.C.). The Chinese entered for the first time upon an

[017-143] active policy against the Hsiung-nu. There seem to have been several

[017-144] reasons for this policy, and several objectives. The raids of the

[017-145] Hsiung-nu from the Ordos region and from northern Shansi had shown

[017-146] themselves to be a direct menace to the capital and to its extremely

[017-147] important hinterland. Northern Shansi is mountainous, with deep ravines.

[017-148] A considerable army on horseback could penetrate some distance to the

[017-149] south before attracting attention. Northern Shensi and the Ordos region

[017-150] are steppe country, in which there were very few Chinese settlements and

[017-151] through which an army of horsemen could advance very quickly. It was

[017-152] therefore determined to push back the Hsiung-nu far enough to remove

[017-153] this threat. It was also of importance to break the power of the

[017-154] Hsiung-nu in the province of Kansu, and to separate them as far as

[017-155] possible from the Tibetans living in that region, to prevent any union

[017-156] between those two dangerous adversaries. A third point of importance was

[017-157] the safeguarding of caravan routes. The state, and especially the

[017-158] capital, had grown rich through Wen Ti's policy. Goods streamed into the

[017-159] capital from all quarters. Commerce with central Asia had particularly

[017-160] increased, bringing the products of the Middle East to China. The

[017-161] caravan routes passed through western Shensi and Kansu to eastern

[017-162] Turkestan, but at that time the Hsiung-nu dominated the approaches to

[017-163] Turkestan and were in a position to divert the trade to themselves or

[017-164] cut it off. The commerce brought profit not only to the caravan traders,

[017-165] most of whom were probably foreigners, but to the officials in the

[017-166] provinces and prefectures through which the routes passed. Thus the

[017-167] officials in western China were interested in the trade routes being

[017-168] brought under direct control, so that the caravans could arrive

[017-169] regularly and be immune from robbery. Finally, the Chinese government

[017-170] may well have regarded it as little to its honour to be still paying

[017-171] dues to the Hsiung-nu and sending princesses to their rulers, now that

[017-172] China was incomparably wealthier and stronger than at the time when that

[017-173] policy of appeasement had begun.

[017-174] [Illustration: Map 3. China in the struggle with the Huns or Hsiung Nu

[017-175] (_roughly 128-100 B.C._)]

[017-176] The first active step taken was to try, in 133 B.C., to capture the

[017-177] head of the Hsiung-nu state, who was called a _shan-yü_ but the

[017-178] _shan-yü_ saw through the plan and escaped. There followed a period of

[017-179] continuous fighting until 119 B.C. The Chinese made countless attacks,

[017-180] without lasting success. But the Hsiung-nu were weakened, one sign of

[017-181] this being that there were dissensions after the death of the _shan-yü_

[017-182] Chün-ch'en, and in 127 B.C. his son went over to the Chinese. Finally

[017-183] the Chinese altered their tactics, advancing in 119 B.C. with a strong

[017-184] army of cavalry, which suffered enormous losses but inflicted serious

[017-185] loss on the Hsiung-nu. After that the Hsiung-nu withdrew farther to the

[017-186] north, and the Chinese settled peasants in the important region of

[017-187] Kansu.

[017-188] Meanwhile, in 125 B.C., the famous Chang Ch'ien had returned. He had

[017-189] been sent in 138 to conclude an alliance with the Yüeh-chih against the

[017-190] Hsiung-nu. The Yüeh-chih had formerly been neighbours of the Hsiung-nu

[017-191] as far as the Ala Shan region, but owing to defeat by the Hsiung-nu

[017-192] their remnants had migrated to western Turkestan. Chang Ch'ien had

[017-193] followed them. Politically he had no success, but he brought back

[017-194] accurate information about the countries in the far west, concerning

[017-195] which nothing had been known beyond the vague reports of merchants. Now

[017-196] it was learnt whence the foreign goods came and whither the Chinese

[017-197] goods went. Chang Ch'ien's reports (which are one of the principal

[017-198] sources for the history of central Asia at that remote time)

[017-199] strengthened the desire to enter into direct and assured commercial

[017-200] relations with those distant countries. The government evidently thought

[017-201] of getting this commerce into its own hands. The way to do this was to

[017-202] impose "tribute" on the countries concerned. The idea was that the

[017-203] missions bringing the annual "tribute" would be a sort of state

[017-204] bartering commissions. The state laid under tribute must supply

[017-205] specified goods at its own cost, and received in return Chinese produce,

[017-206] the value of which was to be roughly equal to the "tribute". Thus Chang

[017-207] Ch'ien's reports had the result that, after the first successes against

[017-208] the Hsiung-nu, there was increased interest in a central Asian policy.

[017-209] The greatest military success were the campaigns of General Li Kuang-li

[017-210] to Ferghana in 104 and 102 B.C. The result of the campaigns was to bring

[017-211] under tribute all the small states in the Tarim basin and some of the

[017-212] states of western Turkestan. From now on not only foreign consumer goods

[017-213] came freely into China, but with them a great number of other things,

[017-214] notably plants such as grape, peach, pomegranate.
