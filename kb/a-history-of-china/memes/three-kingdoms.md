## Overview

The Three Kingdoms period (AD 220–280) marked the beginning of China's first great division following the collapse of the Han dynasty. For sixty years, three rival states — Wei in the north, Wu in the southeast, and Shu Han in the southwest — contended for supremacy, each claiming to be the legitimate ruler of all China [031-057].

> At the outset (220-280) there were three kingdoms (Wei, Wu, Shu Han); then came an unstable reunion during twenty-seven years (280-307) under the rule of the Western Chin. [007-000]

The period was far more than a military struggle. It represented a fundamental transformation of Chinese society: the gentry class, which had developed during the Han, was now forced to defend itself against the feudal traditions of the Turkic and Mongol peoples who dominated the north. In the south, the gentry continued in the traditional pattern of clique struggle [007-000].

Economically, the Three Kingdoms period saw a partial reversal from money economy back to natural economy after some four centuries of developed monetary system. City populations declined, especially in the old capitals, though overall population may not have decreased as severely as in the contemporaneous Roman Empire [007-000].

The period's cultural legacy proved immense. The historical novel _San-kuo yen-i_ ("The Tale of the Three Kingdoms"), attributed to Lo Kuan-chung (14th century), became one of China's most beloved literary works and the model for countless later novels [051-065].

> "The Tale of the Three Kingdoms" became the model for countless later novels and had a great influence on the development of historical novels in the Far East. [051-069]
