## Overview

Peking, the capital of the present Hopei province, occupies a unique place in Chinese history as one of the world's great imperial cities. Located on the north-eastern edge of the North China Plain, it lies within the territory that Eberhard identifies as the "north-east culture" area, centred in Hopei, Shantung, and southern Manchuria [003-051]. From its prehistoric origins as the home of the [[peking-man|Peking Man]] to its modern role as the seat of the Communist government, Peking has been shaped by its position at the frontier between the sedentary Chinese world and the nomadic peoples of the steppe.

The city rose to lasting prominence during the Kitan (Liao) period in the tenth century, when northern trade routes were diverted away from the [[yellow-turban-rebellion|Huang-ho]] basin toward Peking, making it "the main centre of the power of the Kitan" [041-133]. Successive dynasties -- the Kitan, Juchen, [[mongol|Mongols]], Ming, and Ch'ing -- each made Peking their capital, attracted by its strategic location near the steppe and the vast wealth accumulated there. Eberhard notes that the [[mongol|Mongols']] choice of Peking as capital "was entirely natural, for Peking was near their homeland Mongolia" [047-044].

Throughout its history, Peking served as both a bridge and a bulwark between China and the Inner Asian world. It was the stage for dynastic rises and falls, the centre of [[confucianism|Confucian]] statecraft, and, in its modern incarnation, the birthplace of movements that would fundamentally reshape Chinese society, including the [[may-fourth-movement|May Fourth Movement]] of 1919.

## Geography and Location

Peking lies in the northern province of Hopei, situated on the edge of the great North China Plain where it meets the foothills leading toward the [[mongol|Mongolian]] steppe. Eberhard places it within the "north-east culture" zone of early China, alongside Shantung and southern Manchuria [003-051]. This location at the nexus of agricultural China and pastoral Inner Asia fundamentally defined the city's strategic and political character.

The city's position on the plain, south of the Yen Mountains and north of the [[yangtze-river|Yangtze]] heartland, gave it command over the northern approaches to the Chinese core. The Sui emperor [[yang-ti|Yang Ti]] recognized the strategic value of the Peking region as early as the early seventh century, when he constructed a canal that "went north almost to the present Peking" [034-113], linking the northern frontier to the economic centres of the south.

Key geographic features of the Peking region include:

- **The North China Plain**: Peking sits at the northern edge of this fertile alluvial plain, which supported the dense agricultural populations that sustained imperial capitals
- **Proximity to Mongolia**: The city lies less than 300 miles from the [[mongol|Mongolian]] border, making it the closest major Chinese city to the steppe
- **The Yen Mountains**: Rising to the north and west, these mountains formed a natural defensive barrier against nomadic incursions
- **The Grand Canal extension**: [[yang-ti|Yang Ti]]'s seventh-century canal connected Peking to the economic resources of the [[yangtze-river|Yangtze]] basin, a link that would prove vital when the city later became the imperial capital

## Historical Development

### Prehistoric and Early Periods

The Peking region has been inhabited since remote antiquity. The most famous evidence is the so-called [[peking-man|Peking Man]] (_Sinanthropus pekinensis_), whose fossil remains were discovered at Chou-k'ou-tien, a cave site south of the city [002-127]. "The Peking Man lived in caves; no doubt he was a hunter," Eberhard writes [002-133], and his presence testifies to the region's suitability for human habitation from the earliest times. After the period of the Peking Man, however, there comes a great gap in the archaeological record [002-144], and the connection to later inhabitants remains uncertain.

In the early historical period, Peking lay at the northern fringe of the Chinese world. During the third century CE, as the Han empire fragmented, non-Chinese tribal confederations moved into the region. In 281 CE, the Mu-jung section of the Hsien-pi tribes "marched south into China, and occupied the region round Peking" [024-063]. After fierce fighting, a treaty was concluded in 289 under which the Mu-jung recognized Chinese suzerainty -- an early instance of the pattern of alternating conflict and accommodation that would characterize Peking's frontier history.

### Rise under the Kitan (Liao Dynasty)

Peking's emergence as a major political centre dates to the tenth and eleventh centuries, when the Kitan people established the Liao dynasty over northern China. The Kitan conquest fundamentally reoriented the economic geography of the north: trade that had previously flowed toward the [[hsiung-nu|Hu]]ang-ho basin "was now perforce diverted to Peking, which soon became the main centre of the power of the Kitan" [041-133].

> "Thus great wealth accumulated in Peking, the capital of the Liao; in this wealth the whole Kitan ruling class participated, but the tribes in the north, owing to their remoteness, did not." [044-197]

This accumulation of wealth in Peking created a powerful economic and political centre whose gravitational pull would endure even after the Liao dynasty itself fell. The city walls and fortifications that rose during this period marked the beginning of Peking's physical transformation into an imperial capital.

### The Chin (Juchen) Dynasty

The Juchen people, a Tungusic group from Manchuria, overthrew the Kitan in the early twelfth century. In 1114, "in a campaign of unprecedented rapidity they captured Peking, and the Liao dynasty was ended (1125)" [045-005]. The Juchen then established their own Chin dynasty, retaining Peking as a key centre of their realm.

The Chin period deepened Peking's integration into the political geography of conquest dynasties. The Sung founder, Chao K'uang-yin, himself "came of a Chinese military family living to the south of Peking" [041-170], illustrating how the city and its surrounding region had become a breeding ground for the military elites who contested for imperial power.

### The Mongol Yuan Dynasty

The [[mongol|Mongol]] conquest brought Peking to the pinnacle of its medieval importance. The [[mongol|Mongols]] made Peking their principal capital -- a choice that "was entirely natural, for Peking was near their homeland Mongolia" [047-044]. From Peking, the Mongol emperors could return to Mongolia in summer when the Chinese climate proved uncomfortable, and they were "able to maintain contact with the rest of the Mongol empire" [047-046].

> "The immense increase in the population of Peking (the huge court with its enormous expenditure, the mass of officials, the great merchant population, and the great number of servants, workmen, carters and so on) demanded a supply of food that could no longer be provided by the country round Peking itself." [047-059]

Under the [[mongol|Mongols]], Peking -- known as Khanbaliq ("City of the Khan") or Ta-tu ("Great Capital") -- became one of the great cities of the world, visited by Marco Polo and described in terms of wonder by travellers from across Eurasia. The Mongol period established the basic spatial layout of the imperial city that subsequent dynasties would inherit and embellish.

### The Ming Dynasty Transfer

When [[chu-yuan-chang|Chu Yuan-chang]] founded the [[ming-dynasty]] in 1368, his generals "captured Peking almost without a blow" and the Mongol ruler fled northward [048-070]. The Ming initially established their capital at Nanking, but Peking retained its strategic importance. [[chu-yuan-chang|Chu Yuan-chang]] stationed one of his sons as "king" in Peking, recognizing it as "a post of exceptional importance" for the defence of the northern frontier [051-178].

The decisive moment came when [[chu-yuan-chang|Chu Yuan-chang]]'s son, the prince enfeoffed at Peking, rebelled against his nephew. Marching south with his strong army, he seized the throne and ruled as the Yung-lo emperor. As Eberhard relates: "he transferred the capital to Peking, where it remained throughout the Ming epoch. Nanking became a sort of second capital" [051-191-192].

The Ming emperors rebuilt Peking on a grand scale. Eberhard notes that among the great building projects of the early Ming were "the great city walls of Peking; and large parts of the palaces of Peking, begun in the Mongol epoch" [051-138]. The monumental Peking we know -- with its massive walls, the Forbidden City, the Temple of Heaven, and the grid of streets and avenues -- is substantially a Ming creation.

The Ming dynasty's end was itself enacted on Peking's stage. The last Ming emperor hanged himself from a pavilion on the "Coal Hill" (Mei Shan) overlooking the Forbidden City as rebel forces entered the capital [001-249].

### The Ch'ing and Modern Eras

Under the Manchu Ch'ing dynasty, Peking remained the imperial capital. The city grew into a vast commercial and craft centre. Eberhard records that in the nineteenth century "there existed over 420 different guilds" in Peking, each trade having its own organization [039-149] -- a measure of the city's economic complexity.

During the Opium Wars, Peking became a target of Western military power. Anglo-French troops landed at Tientsin and marched on Peking in 1860; the emperor fled to Jehol and died there [058-029]. The resulting treaty established a British legation in Peking, ending the city's diplomatic isolation [058-033]. Later, the T'ai P'ing rebels advanced nearly to Tientsin but "failed to capture Peking itself" [058-158].

In the twentieth century, Peking became the crucible of modern Chinese political movements. On May 4, 1919, "students of the National University in Peking demonstrated against the government and their pro-Japanese adherents" [062-003], launching the [[may-fourth-movement|May Fourth Movement]] that transformed Chinese intellectual life. The city was also the seat of the warlord government under Yuan Shih-k'ai and the early republican administration.

In 1949, the communists under [[mao-tse-tung|Mao Tse-tung]] "established their new capital in Peking" [065-085], restoring the city to its historic role as the political centre of China.

## Political Capital

Peking's unique status as an imperial capital spanning multiple dynasties stems from its strategic geography at the intersection of the Chinese agricultural world and the Inner Asian steppe.

The city served as the primary or secondary capital for six successive regimes over nearly a thousand years:

| Dynasty | Period | Role of Peking |
|---------|--------|---------------|
| Liao (Kitan) | 10th--12th c. | Southern capital; main economic centre |
| Chin (Juchen) | 12th--13th c. | Central capital (Chung-tu) |
| Yuan (Mongol) | 13th--14th c. | Principal capital (Khanbaliq/Ta-tu) |
| Ming | 15th--17th c. | Primary capital from 1421 |
| Ch'ing (Manchu) | 17th--20th c. | Imperial capital |
| People's Republic | 1949--present | National capital |

Peking's political primacy was never inevitable. Its rival [[nanking|Nanking]] ("Southern Capital") repeatedly challenged it for pre-eminence, reflecting a deeper tension in Chinese statecraft between the economic and cultural centre of gravity in the [[yangtze-river|Yangtze]] basin and the strategic necessities of the northern frontier. The Ming founder [[chu-yuan-chang|Chu Yuan-chang]] made his capital at Nanking, and it was only the usurpation of the Yung-lo emperor -- a prince whose power base lay in the north -- that restored Peking to primacy.

## Cultural Significance

Peking has long been more than a political centre; it has been a cultural standard-bearer. Eberhard observes that "we distinguish to this day between a Nanking and Peking 'High Chinese,' to say nothing of dialects" [034-047], indicating that Peking's speech became the prestige standard for the Mandarin language.

The city's cultural institutions reflected its imperial status:

- **The Imperial Academy** (Kuo-tzu-chien): The highest educational institution of the empire, where scholars prepared for the [[confucianism|Confucian]] civil service examinations
- **The National University of Peking**: Founded in 1898, it became the intellectual engine of modern Chinese thought, hosting John Dewey as a guest professor in 1919-20 [062-015] and incubating the [[may-fourth-movement|May Fourth Movement]]
- **The Forbidden City**: The walled palace complex built by the Ming and inherited by the Ch'ing, housing the imperial court and its vast artistic collections
- **Guild culture**: The over 420 trade guilds of nineteenth-century Peking created a dense fabric of craft traditions, commercial networks, and communal religious life [039-149]

Peking's cultural influence radiated outward through the [[examination-system|examination system]], which drew aspirants from every province to the capital for the highest-level tests. Success in the metropolitan examinations conferred the _chin-shih_ degree, the crowning achievement of the [[confucius|Confucian]] scholar's career.

The city also served as a gateway for foreign cultural influence. [[buddhism|Buddhist]] temples, mosques serving the Muslim communities of the north-west, and, in the modern period, Christian missions and Western educational institutions all found their place in Peking's cosmopolitan fabric. Yet Eberhard emphasizes that the old religion of the peasants persisted alongside these imported traditions, creating a layered cultural landscape.

## Modern Transformation

Peking's twentieth-century transformation reflects the broader drama of China's passage from empire to republic to communist state. The city was the stage for events that would reshape the nation:

- **The [[may-fourth-movement|May Fourth Movement]] (1919)**: Student demonstrations at the National University of Peking against the Versailles Treaty's transfer of German concessions in Shantung to Japan ignited a nationwide cultural and political awakening [062-003]
- **The Republican interlude**: Under Yuan Shih-k'ai, Peking hosted a fragile republican government whose authority was undermined by warlordism and foreign encroachment [062-121]
- **The Sino-Japanese War**: Peking fell to Japanese forces, becoming part of the occupied territories
- **The Communist victory (1949)**: [[mao-tse-tung|Mao Tse-tung]]'s forces "established their new capital in Peking," restoring the ancient city to its role as the seat of Chinese power [065-085]

The modern transformation brought both continuity and rupture. The great city walls that had defined Peking's physical identity since the Ming period were largely demolished in the 1950s and 1960s. The guilds that had organized the city's economic life for centuries were dissolved. Yet Peking retained its symbolic centrality: T'ien-an-men Square, enlarged under communist rule, became the ceremonial heart of the People's Republic, facing the Forbidden City across Chang-an Avenue -- an architectural dialogue between the imperial past and the revolutionary present.
