## Overview

[[Wang Yang-ming]] (1473–1529) was a [[ming-dynasty|Ming dynasty]] philosopher, general, and statesman whose doctrine of intuitive knowledge challenged the Neo-Confucian orthodoxy established by [[chu-hsi|Chu Hsi]]. A member of the prominent Wang family allied to the Yang clique, he rose to power after the fall of the eunuch [[eunuch|Liu Chin]] in 1510 and went on to suppress major rebellions in Kiangsi and Nanking. His philosophy, rooted in the Lin-ch'uan tradition of [[wang-an-shih|Wang An-shih]], introduced the primacy of intuition as the path to true knowledge — a radical departure that shaped political thought in both China and Japan.

## Biography

Wang Yang-ming was born in 1473 into a family of [[gentry|scholar-official]]s. His early career was marred by conflict with the powerful eunuch [[liu-chin|Liu Chin]], who had him punished alongside other members of the Yang faction [052-163]. After [[liu-chin|Liu Chin]]'s downfall in 1510, Wang entered palace circles and began his rise as both a military commander and a philosopher [052-172].

In 1518, Wang crushed a major uprising in Kiangsi province — the culmination of years of unrest fueled by the transfer of the capital from Nanking to [[peking|Peking]], which had devastated the local gentry's economic interests [053-017]. A prince residing in Nanking viewed Wang's military move into Kiangsi as a direct threat and rose openly against the government, rallying the Kiangsi gentry to his side. Wang defeated him, earning the highest favour of the incompetent emperor [053-026].

> Wang Yang-ming, the philosopher general, crushed a rising in Kiangsi. The rising had been the outcome of years of unrest, which had two causes: native risings and loss for the gentry due to the transfer of the capital. [053-017–053-020]

Wang also suppressed the so-called salt-merchant rebels in the southernmost part of Kiangsi and adjoining Kwangtung — a persistent local movement that had dominated a small area intermittently since the eleventh century [053-031]. His expeditions brought him into contact with the Portuguese, who had landed in Canton in 1517. As early as 1519, Wang became the first Chinese to speak of the "Franks" (Fu-lan-chi) after encountering European firearms imported by the Portuguese [053-039].

## Philosophy

Wang Yang-ming's philosophy followed [[confucius|Confucius]] in its foundations but represented a decisive break from the Neo-Confucian system of [[chu-hsi|Chu Hsi]] that had dominated Chinese thought since the Sung period [053-058]. His central innovation was the concept of intuition as the decisive philosophic experience: only through intuition, he argued, could one attain true knowledge [053-062].

> Wang Yang-ming's philosophy also followed Confucius, but he liberated himself from the Neo-Confucian tendency as represented by Chu Hsi, which started in the Sung epoch and continued to rule in China in his time and after him; he introduced into Confucian philosophy the conception of intuition. He regarded intuition as the decisive philosophic experience; only through intuition could man come to true knowledge. [053-058–053-063]

Wang followed the Lin-ch'uan tradition, named after the Kiangsi hometown of [[wang-an-shih|Wang An-shih]] (1021–1086). This Southern school represented the interests of the small gentry of the [[yangtze-river|Yangtze]] area and had absorbed elements from both the philosopher [[lu-hsiang-shan|Lu Hsiang-shan]] (1139–1192) and Taoist groups such as the Cheng-i-chiao (Correct Unity Sect) [053-081].

However, the introduction of a highly subjective concept like intuition into the practical framework of Confucian statecraft had problematic consequences. In the hands of statesmen, Wang's doctrine could justify virtually any action by appeal to personal intuition — a tendency the historical text characterises as "Machiavellism" [053-084]. Wang himself failed to gain acceptance for his philosophy in China: it served the interests of individual despots against the gentry as a class, and the middle class was not yet politically mature enough to seize the opportunity it offered [053-087].

## Legacy

Wang Yang-ming's doctrine found a far more receptive audience in Japan, where it admirably served the dictatorial state system that had developed in that country [053-092]. In the twentieth century, [[chiang-kai-shek|Chiang Kai-shek]] — during the years in which he showed Fascist tendencies — also took an interest in Wang's thought [053-096].

Wang remains a paradoxical figure: a brilliant general who stabilised the Ming state in the volatile Cheng-te reign, and a radical philosopher whose subjective turn in [[confucianism|Confucianism]] proved more influential abroad than at home.
