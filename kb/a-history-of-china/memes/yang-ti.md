## Overview

[[yang-ti|Yang Ti]] (r. 604–618) was the second and final emperor of the short-lived [[sui-dynasty|Sui dynasty]]. He came to power after the murder of his father, Emperor Wen Ti, and pursued an ambitious program of infrastructure, territorial expansion, and cultural patronage. However, his extravagant spending, massive forced-labour projects, and disastrous military campaigns exhausted the empire's resources, leading to widespread rebellion and the dynasty's collapse.

> The prince and his followers murdered the emperor and set aside the heir-apparent; and Kuang came to the throne, assuming the name of Yang Ti. [034-100]

His reign represents one of the most dramatic arcs in Chinese imperial history: a period of extraordinary achievement in engineering and state-building followed by rapid disintegration. The [[tang-dynasty|T'ang dynasty]] that succeeded the Sui inherited and completed many of Yang Ti's projects, particularly the canal system.

## Accession and Early Rule

Yang Ti's first acts as emperor focused on reorienting the empire's political and economic geography:

> His first act was to transfer the capital back to the east, to Loyang, close to the grain-producing regions. [034-100]

This relocation of the capital from Ch'ang-an to [[peking|Lo-yang]] was strategically motivated — it placed the court closer to the grain-rich northeast and the canal network that would become the economic backbone of the empire. The move also reflected a shift in political power toward the northeastern and southern gentry, who had supported Yang Ti's rise.

## Canal Construction

Yang Ti's most enduring legacy was the construction of a vast canal network linking north and south China:

> In addition to a serviceable canal to the south, Yang Ti made another that went north almost to the present Peking. [034-112]

> There are records of lighters of 500 and even 800 tons capacity! These are dimensions unheard of in the West in those times. [034-112]

These canals — precursors to the famous Imperial Canal — connected the [[yangtze-river|Yangtze River]] with the Yellow River and extended north toward modern [[peking|Peking]]. The engineering achievement was immense, but the human cost was severe:

> Still more expensive were the great canal works. In addition to this, the emperor Yang Ti, unlike his father, was very extravagant. He built enormous palaces and undertook long journeys throughout the empire with an immense following. [034-179]

## Foreign Policy and the Turks

Yang Ti pursued an active and complex foreign policy, particularly toward the Turkic peoples on the northern frontier:

> Under the emperor Yang Ti, P'ei Chü carried this policy further. He induced the Tölös tribes to attack the T'u-yü-hun, and then himself attacked the latter, so destroying their power. [034-142]

The strategy of "using barbarians against barbarians" — sowing division among the Turks while extending Chinese influence into Central Asia — achieved tactical successes. However, these intrigues were expensive and ultimately provoked a decisive Turkic counter-attack in 615 that nearly captured the emperor himself.

## The Korean Campaigns

Yang Ti inherited and intensified his father's expansionist policy toward Korea:

> The last years of T'ai Tsung's reign were filled with a great war against Korea, which represented a continuation of the plans of the Sui emperor Yang Ti. [036-096]

The Korean campaigns were massive military undertakings involving combined land and sea operations. They proved extremely costly in both treasure and lives, contributing significantly to the fiscal exhaustion of the Sui state and popular discontent.

## Downfall

The combination of overtaxation, forced labour on the canals, and disastrous foreign wars proved fatal:

> All this wrecked the prosperity which his father had built up and had tried to safeguard. The only productive expenditure was that on the canals, and they could not begin to pay in so short a period. [034-179]

By 618, rebellions had spread across the empire. [[li-yuan|Li Yüan]], supported by his son [[li-yuan|Li Shih-min]], rose in revolt and captured Ch'ang-an:

> After capturing Ch'ang-an he installed a puppet emperor there, a grandson of Yang Ti. In 618 the puppet was dethroned and Li Yüan, the father, was made emperor, in the T'ang dynasty. [035-035]

Yang Ti was assassinated by his own entourage, and the Sui dynasty — which had reunified China after centuries of division — came to an abrupt end after only two reigns.

## Legacy

Yang Ti's reign illustrates the paradox of ambitious state-building in imperial China. The Grand Canal system, completed and extended by later dynasties, became one of the most important infrastructure projects in world history, facilitating economic integration between north and south for over a millennium. Yet the speed and brutality with which Yang Ti pursued his projects alienated every constituency that might have sustained his rule — the peasantry, the gentry, the military, and the frontier peoples. The [[tang-dynasty|T'ang]] learned from his example: they completed the canals, but did so more gradually, while maintaining the tax base and military loyalty that the Sui had lost.
