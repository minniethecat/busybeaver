The Sui dynasty was the first ruling house to reunify China after the long period of division that followed the collapse of the [[han-dynasty|Han dynasty]] in the early third century. Although its direct rule lasted less than four decades, the Sui's achievements—political reunification, the construction of the Grand Canal, the restoration of centralized administration, and the consolidation of the northern frontiers—laid the institutional and physical infrastructure upon which the [[tang-dynasty|T'ang dynasty]] would build one of the most brilliant eras in Chinese history.

> Thus began the Sui dynasty, the first dynasty that was once more to rule over the whole of China.
>
> — [031-022]

## Origins and Founding

The Sui dynasty emerged from the power struggles of the Northern Chou dynasty, one of the successor states that had ruled northern China during the period of division. Yang Chien, a general of mixed Chinese and non-Chinese descent serving the Northern Chou, seized power through a palace coup and proclaimed himself emperor in AD 580.

> The founder of the Sui dynasty, known by his reign name of Wen Ti, was a member of the Northern Chou aristocracy who, through a combination of military command and political maneuvering, succeeded in making himself master first of the north and then of all China.
>
> — [034-062]

Wen Ti consolidated his hold on the north by liquidating rival claimants and then turned his attention southward. By 589, Sui armies had conquered the last of the southern dynasties, the Ch'ên, and for the first time since the fall of the Han, a single imperial government ruled the entirety of China proper.

> The new Sui empire found itself faced with the task of consolidating control over a vast territory that had been divided for centuries, each region having developed its own administrative traditions and local elites.
>
> — [034-044]

## Reunification and Centralization

The Sui undertook an ambitious program of administrative recentralization. The dynasty abolished the hereditary privileges that had accumulated during the centuries of division and asserted direct imperial control over provincial appointments. It began by liquidating the petty states and local power structures that had proliferated during the period of division.

> The Sui dynasty began by liquidating the petty states that had emerged during the preceding centuries of division, establishing in their place a unified administrative hierarchy responsible directly to the throne.
>
> — [033-110]

This program of centralization, though necessary for reunification, created powerful enemies among the old aristocratic families who had prospered under the looser arrangements of the divided period. The Sui also revived and reformed the examination system for selecting officials, aiming to replace hereditary privilege with merit-based recruitment—an innovation that would become central to Chinese governance under the T'ang.

## The Grand Canal

The Sui dynasty's most enduring physical achievement was the construction of the Grand Canal, a massive waterway linking the Yellow River valley in the north with the Yangtze River valley in the south. The canal served multiple strategic purposes: it facilitated the transport of grain and tax revenues from the productive southern regions to the political capital in the north, enabled the rapid movement of troops to suppress regional unrest, and symbolized the economic integration of the reunified empire.

> The canals constructed under the Sui, and the useful waterways created by uniting existing rivers and lakes, created a vast internal transportation network that would serve as the economic backbone of the empire for centuries to come.
>
> — [036-082]

The canal's construction, however, came at enormous human cost. Hundreds of thousands of laborers were conscripted for the work, and the heavy demands of corvée labor—combined with military campaigns on the frontiers—generated widespread resentment that contributed to the dynasty's rapid collapse.

## Frontier Policy and the Turks

The Sui faced a complex strategic situation on the northern frontier, where the Turkish Khaganate had become the dominant power in the steppe. The dynasty pursued a sophisticated policy of divide and rule, exploiting divisions within the Turkish confederation to prevent the emergence of a unified threat.

> In the first year of the Sui period the Turks split into two sections, so that the Sui had hopes of gaining influence over one faction against the other. This division proved a fortunate situation for the Sui, allowing them to manage the frontier without committing to a full-scale war.
>
> — [034-127/122]

The Sui maintained diplomatic relations with both the Eastern and Western Turks, playing one against the other while strengthening border defenses. Sui rulers also adopted traditional Chinese frontier strategies, including the construction of defensive walls and the settlement of allied nomadic groups within the empire's borders as a buffer against hostile tribes.

## The Second Emperor and Collapse

Wen Ti was succeeded by his son, known as Yang Ti, whose reign marked a drastic shift from consolidation to overreach. Yang Ti pursued an aggressive program of military expansion, launching costly campaigns against the Korean kingdom of Koguryŏ and undertaking massive construction projects including further extensions of the canal system.

> The effort to remove the Sui came from within. Yang Ti's extravagant military campaigns and the crushing burden of forced labor for his construction projects exhausted the population and alienated the old aristocratic families who had been the dynasty's original supporters.
>
> — [035-029]

The Korean campaigns, in particular, proved disastrous. Repeated Sui expeditions against Koguryŏ met with stiff resistance and heavy casualties, draining the treasury and destroying the dynasty's military prestige. As peasant rebellions multiplied and provincial commanders asserted their independence, Yang Ti lost control of the empire. He retreated to the south and was murdered by members of his own entourage in 618.

> He was murdered, however, by members of the deposed northern aristocracy who had transferred their allegiance to the rising power of the T'ang.
>
> — [036-007]

## Legacy

The Sui dynasty's brevity belies its historical importance. Viewed in retrospect, the Sui appears as a compressed rehearsal for the T'ang: it achieved political unification, recentralized the administration, reformed official recruitment, built the Grand Canal, and managed the Turkish frontier—all within a single generation. The T'ang dynasty, founded by Li Yüan in 618, inherited these achievements and, by avoiding Yang Ti's excesses, made them the foundation of three centuries of stable rule.

> The short Sui period can only be described as a transitional epoch of the utmost importance—a bridge between the age of division and the golden age of the T'ang.
>
> — [034-011]

The Sui also demonstrated a recurring pattern in Chinese dynastic history: the first ruler of a new dynasty typically consolidates power and establishes institutions, while the second ruler either stabilizes and expands those foundations or—as in Yang Ti's case—overreaches catastrophically and brings the entire edifice crashing down.
