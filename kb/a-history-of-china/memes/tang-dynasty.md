## Overview

The T'ang dynasty (A.D. 618–906) stands as one of the most celebrated periods in Chinese history, a high-water mark of imperial power, cosmopolitan culture, and administrative sophistication. Founded by the Li family in the wake of the Sui collapse, the dynasty presided over a vast realm stretching from the Tarim Basin in the west to Korea in the east, from the [[mongol|Mongolian]] steppe in the north to Annam in the south. Eberhard opens his treatment of the period by noting: "_The T'ang dynasty_ (A.D. 618-906)" [001-128], and the dynasty's reach is illustrated by "Map 5: The T'ang realm (_about A.D. 750_)" [035-001].

The T'ang witnessed the full maturation of a bureaucratic state governed through the [[confucianism|Confucian]] [[examination-system|examination system]], the rise of [[buddhism|Buddhism]] as a mass religion with vast economic power, the second great blossoming of Chinese poetry and painting, and a cosmopolitanism unmatched in Chinese history until the modern era. Its capital Ch'ang-an was the largest and most sophisticated city in the world, and the Silk Road brought merchants, pilgrims, and envoys from across Asia into its streets [036-110]. Yet the dynasty's very strengths — its reliance on powerful military governors, its exploitation of foreign trade and foreign auxiliaries, and the unchecked growth of Buddhist economic power — carried within them the seeds of its eventual fragmentation and collapse [039-035].

## Founding and Rise

The T'ang dynasty emerged from the chaos that consumed the Sui. [[tai-tsung|Li Shih-min]], later the emperor [[tai-tsung|T'ai Tsung]], was the "hero of the Turkish siege" [035-016] who drove the founding of the dynasty after his father [[li-yuan|Li Yuan]] was proclaimed emperor in 618. Internal fighting continued until 623 when "the whole empire brought under the rule of the T'ang" [035-037].

[[tai-tsung|Li Shih-min]]'s path to power was not peaceful. In 626, at the very moment when the western Turks threatened the capital, an armed collision between [[tai-tsung|Li Shih-min]] and his brothers resulted in his victory: "The brothers and their families were exterminated, the father compelled to abdicate, and [[tai-tsung|Li Shih-min]] became emperor, assuming the name [[tai-tsung|T'ai Tsung]] (627-649)" [036-075]. Eberhard judges his reign to mark "the zenith of the power of China and of the T'ang dynasty" [036-078].

Under [[tai-tsung|T'ai Tsung]], China projected power on an unprecedented scale. The eastern Turks were overthrown in 629-30, and the khan was taken to Ch'ang-an, where the Chinese emperor made himself "Heavenly Khan" of the Turks [036-025]. By 640, the Tarim Basin came under Chinese dominance [036-057]. Korean campaigns continued the ambitious plans of the Sui, and for a time Korea came "firmly into Chinese possession" [036-096]. The dynasty's foreign policy was "mainly concerned with the Turks and Turkestan" [036-001], and its Turkestan strategy was driven by the need to prevent a union between Turks and Tibetans — "this, together with commercial interests, seems to have been the political motive of the Chinese Turkestan policy under the T'ang" [036-049].

## Government and Administration

The T'ang inherited and refined administrative structures that traced back through the Toba and Han. A system of dual civil and military administration was maintained, and "at first, remained unchanged in the beginning of the T'ang" [035-081]. The backbone of military power in the seventh century was a militia of some six hundred units, averaging a thousand men each, recruited from the farming population for short-term service [035-082].

Great reforms began immediately after unification. A new land law, the _chun-t'ien_ (equal-field) system, aimed at equalizing ownership "so that as far as possible all peasants should own the same amount of land and the formation of large estates be prevented" [035-040]. To enforce tax collection and public order, "the T'ang renewed the old '_pao-chia_' system, as a part of a general reform of the administration in 624" [035-065], in which groups of five families were collectively responsible for taxes, corvee obligations, and crimes.

The central administration grew increasingly sophisticated. Towards the end of the T'ang period, the state secretariat operated in two parts, possessing "all information about the economic and political affairs of the empire, and it made the actual decisions" [035-116]. A section for economic affairs was added, "working quite independently of it and directly under the emperor" [035-121], along with a Privy Council — a mainly military body "probably intended to control the generals" [035-127]. Eberhard observes that this administrative apparatus "might compare favourably with European systems of the eighteenth century" [035-118].

The [[examination-system|examination system]] under the T'ang became the principal route to office for the eastern gentry, whose sons "had to pass through the examinations" [036-166], in contrast with the western gentry who claimed family privileges. This created lasting differences in "education and outlook between both groups" [036-167] and shaped the composition of the T'ang ruling elite.

## Culture and Society

The T'ang presided over what the Chinese describe as "the second blossoming of T'ang culture" [037-053], a period of extraordinary creativity in literature, painting, music, and religious thought. The crowding of people into the capital — at times containing two million inhabitants — and the accumulation of resources there "promoted a rich cultural life" [035-149]. Eberhard notes: "We know of many poets of that period whose poems were real masterpieces; and artists whose works were admired centuries later" [035-151].

Among the literary giants, [[li-po|Li T'ai-po]] (701–762) and [[tu-fu|Tu Fu]] (712–770) achieved "widespread fame" [037-068], while Po Chu-i (772–846) and Yuan Chen (779–831) kept their works "as close as possible to the vernacular" [037-071]. The T'ang poets "brought to it genuine feeling" [037-068], in contrast with the formalistic poetry of earlier centuries.

In the visual arts, the principal achievement "lies undoubtedly in the field of painting" [037-094]. Wu Tao-tzu, the most famous painter of the period, was strongly influenced by Central Asian works and painted Buddhist temple murals [037-102]. Wang Wei (721–759), also a celebrated poet, "aimed at uniting poem and painting into an integral whole" [037-106], beginning the great tradition of Chinese landscape painting that would reach its zenith in the Sung epoch.

> "Everyone, of course, was Confucian, as was fitting for a member of the gentry, but Confucianism was so taken for granted that it was not discussed. It was the basis of morality for the gentry, but held no problems. It no longer contained anything of interest." [035-157]

Religious life was dominated by Buddhism, which flourished under official patronage, especially during the reign of the empress Wu, who "endowed them with enormous sums of money, and tried to make Buddhism a sort of state religion" [036-171]. At the same time, [[taoism|Taoism]] and meditative Buddhism attracted those who retired from the bustle of the capital "to work on their estates and to enjoy the society of their friends" [035-154]. Three new Buddhist sects emerged in the T'ang period, including the Pure Land school (_Ching-t'u tsung_, since 641), which required of its mainly lower-class adherents "only the permanent invocation of the Buddha Amithabha" [038-132].

The T'ang court cultivated an opera tradition — "what amounted to a court conservatoire trained actors and musicians as early as in the T'ang period for this court opera" [037-086] — and new verse forms developed from southern folk songs, which "was the first step towards opera" [037-079].

## Economy and Trade

The T'ang economy was vast and monetized. In the middle of the eighth century, the annual state budget included over a million tons of grain, twenty-seven million pieces of textiles, and two million strings of money — "much more than the state budget of the Han period" [035-142]. The population of the empire reached approximately fifty million [035-143].

Trade flourished along the Silk Road and by sea. Foreign merchants established permanent communities across China: "The whole country was covered by a commercial network; foreign merchants who had come overland to China met others who had come by sea" [036-110]. The foreigners "set up their own counting-houses and warehouses; whole quarters of the capital were inhabited entirely by foreigners who lived as if they were in their own country" [036-112]. Foreign religions, including Manichaeism, Mazdaism, Nestorian Christianity, and Islam, arrived along with the merchants [036-114].

Buddhist temples became major economic institutions. They "grew in importance as repositories of capital; the temples bought more and more land, became more and more wealthy, and so gained increasing influence over economic affairs" [037-002]. Monasteries accumulated bronze Buddha statues that represented enormous stored wealth, and with "these stocks they exercised controlling influence over the money market" [037-007]. They also acquired shops, operated mills, and collected rental income [037-011].

## Decline and Fall

> "The T'ang regime. Most of the historians attribute it to a woman, the later empress Wu." [036-135]

The dynasty's trajectory toward decline began in the late seventh century with the reign of the empress Wu. She had been a concubine of [[tai-tsung|T'ai Tsung]] and, after his death, became a Buddhist nun until the next emperor fell in love with her [036-136]. By 690 she deposed the T'ang heir and made herself empress in the "Chou dynasty" (690–701), which "officially ended the T'ang dynasty" [036-146]. Although the T'ang were later restored, the deep factional divides she exploited between the eastern and western gentry persisted [036-166].

The military governorship system, created to meet frontier threats, proved the dynasty's undoing. Regional commanders "soon became more important than the civil administrators" and could command entire provinces, enabling them "to pursue a policy of their own, even against the central government" [035-109]. The absence of a Privy Council "in the early T'ang period gave the military governors much too great freedom, ultimately with baneful results" [035-131].

The cataclysmic turning point was the rebellion of [[an-lu-shan|An Lu-shan]] (755–763). A military governor of mixed Turkish-Sogdian origin [037-147], [[an-lu-shan|An Lu-shan]] marched against the capital with 200,000 men, captured both Ch'ang-an and Loyang, and declared himself emperor [037-161]. Eberhard observes that "[[an-lu-shan|An Lu-shan]]'s rising was the first of many similar ones in the later T'ang period" [038-006], and notes that the gentry of eastern China "entirely ready to support [[an-lu-shan|An Lu-shan]] against the government" [038-008].

Though the rebellion was suppressed with Uighur cavalry support, the cost was catastrophic. From 750 onward, "the empire consisted of an impotent central government and powerful military governors, who handed on their positions to their sons" [038-056]. The equal-field land system was abandoned in 780 [038-064]. The government's response to its financial crisis — the wholesale confiscation of Buddhist property in 843 — saw "four thousand, six hundred Buddhist temples, 40,000 shrines and monasteries" secularized, and "two hundred and sixty thousand, five hundred monks" forced into lay life [038-102].

The final blow came from below. In 874, a great peasant rising began under Wang Hsien-chih and [[hsiung-nu|Hu]]ang Ch'ao, a salt merchant who had become impoverished [038-148]. [[hsiung-nu|Hu]]ang Ch'ao, who was "well educated" and may have failed the state examination [038-151], captured Canton — where according to an Arab source over 120,000 foreign merchants were killed [038-182] — and then marched north to seize both capitals, declaring himself emperor in 881 [038-188]. Eberhard calls this "the first time that a peasant rising had succeeded against the gentry" [038-190].

The T'ang briefly regained their capitals with the aid of the Turkish Sha-t'o, but the dynasty was a shell: "This popular rising, which had only been overcome with the aid of foreign troops, brought the end of the T'ang dynasty" [039-016]. In 907, the warlord Chu Ch'uan-chung deposed the last T'ang puppet and established the Later Liang dynasty, formally ending nearly three centuries of T'ang rule [039-033].

Eberhard's epitaph on the dynasty is sober and comprehensive: the empire had "risen to unprecedented power" but its "downfall had been brought about by the military governors, who had built up their power and had become independent hereditary satraps, exploiting the people for their own purposes" [039-035]. Added to this, "the empire had been weakened first by its foreign trade and then by the dependence on foreigners, especially Turks, into which it had fallen owing to internal conditions. A large part of the national income had gone abroad" [039-041].
