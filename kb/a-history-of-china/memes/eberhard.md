## Overview

Wolfram Eberhard (1909–1989) was a German-born sociologist and sinologist who authored _A History of China_, the source text for this wiki.

A prolific scholar of Chinese folklore, society, and history, Eberhard taught at the University of California, Berkeley from 1952 to 1976. His historical survey, first published in 1950 and subsequently revised, became a standard introductory text for English-language readers.

## Scholarly Career

Eberhard studied at the University of Berlin and developed expertise in Chinese sociology, folklore, and history.

His early career in Germany was interrupted by the political situation of the 1930s, leading him to teach in Turkey before ultimately settling in the United States.

At Berkeley, he built a strong program in Chinese studies and trained a generation of American sinologists.

## A History of China

Eberhard's most widely read work, _A History of China_, provides a comprehensive survey from prehistory to the mid-twentieth century.

The book is noted for its attention to social and economic history, its treatment of minority peoples and border regions, and its accessible narrative style.

It has been translated into multiple languages and remains in print decades after its first publication.

## Scholarly Contributions

Beyond his historical survey, Eberhard made significant contributions to the study of Chinese folklore and popular culture.

He collected and analyzed traditional tales, examined the social structure of pre-modern China, and explored cultural contacts between China and neighboring regions.

His sociological approach to Chinese history, emphasizing class structures and economic forces, distinguished his work from more traditional political histories.

## Legacy

Eberhard's influence extends through his students, his publications, and the continued use of his historical survey in classrooms worldwide.

His interdisciplinary approach — combining sociology, folklore studies, and historical analysis — helped broaden the scope of Western sinology in the post-war period.
