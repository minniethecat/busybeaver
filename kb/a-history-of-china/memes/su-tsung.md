## Overview

[[su-tsung|Su Tsung]] (also known as Li Heng) was a T'ang dynasty emperor who reigned from 756 to 762, ascending the throne in the midst of the [[an-lu-shan-rebellion|An Lu-shan Rebellion]] after his father [[hsuan-tsung|Hsuan Tsung]] abdicated. His reign was entirely consumed by the civil war, during which he established an imperial base in north-western Shensi and secured critical foreign military aid — most notably Uighur cavalry — to recover the capital Ch'ang-an and suppress the rebellion.

> "The emperor now abdicated; his son, emperor Su Tsung (756-762), also fled, though not with him into Szechwan, but into north-western Shensi." [037-168]

## Ascension During Crisis

Su Tsung came to power under extraordinary circumstances. In 756, the rebel general [[an-lu-shan|An Lu-shan]] captured the eastern capital Loyang, proclaimed himself emperor of a new Yen dynasty, and then marched on the western capital Ch'ang-an. The T'ang general Ko-shu Han, tasked with blocking the rebel advance, was defeated and taken prisoner [037-168]. Hsuan Tsung fled westward into Szechwan, but en route his bodyguard mutinied, forcing the execution of his consort Yang Kuei-fei. Broken by these events, Hsuan Tsung abdicated in favour of his son Su Tsung [037-168].

Rather than accompany his father to Szechwan, Su Tsung retreated separately to north-western Shensi, where he established a resistance headquarters and began rallying loyalist forces to continue the fight against the rebellion [037-169].

## The Struggle to Recover the Empire

### Foreign Alliances

With the empire's regular armies shattered and the capital in rebel hands, Su Tsung was forced to seek military assistance from abroad. He dispatched envoys to Central Asia and successfully recruited several auxiliary forces: a small Arab contingent sent by the Caliph Abu-Jafar, minor bands from Turkestan, and — most decisively — Uighur cavalry in substantial strength [037-172] [037-174].

### The Battle for Ch'ang-an (757)

The foreign intervention proved decisive. At the end of 757, a major battle was fought near Ch'ang-an:

> "At the end of 757 there was a great battle in the neighbourhood of the capital, in which An Lu-shan was defeated by the Uighurs; shortly afterwards he was murdered by one of his eunuchs." [037-175]

The rebel forces were routed, and the imperial forces recaptured both Loyang and the capital. However, the Uighur auxiliaries looted Loyang after its capture [037-178]. Su Tsung's government paid a heavy price for this assistance: the [[uighur|Uighurs]] received 10,000 rolls of silk as an immediate reward, an annual tribute of 20,000 rolls of silk, and the Uighur khan was given a daughter of the emperor in marriage, sealing a political alliance between the two courts [037-179] [037-180].

### The Second Phase of the Rebellion

The death of [[an-lu-shan|An Lu-shan]] did not end the conflict. His general, the Turk Shih Ssu-ming, inherited the rebel cause and controlled a vast territory in eastern China. Su Tsung's regime was compelled to summon the [[uighur|Uighurs]] once again, this time under the joint command of Kuo Tzu-i, a Kitan general, and P'u-ku [[hsiung-nu|Hu]]ai-en [037-183]. After suffering initial defeats — including the loss of Loyang a second time — the government forces ultimately prevailed when Shih Ssu-ming was murdered by his own son, and the ensuing disorder allowed the imperial armies to crush the rebellion.

## Death and Succession

Su Tsung died in 762, after six years of unremitting civil war that had devastated the empire's finances, depopulated its heartlands, and permanently weakened the authority of the T'ang dynasty. During his reign, tax revenues from large parts of the empire had ceased entirely, yet enormous sums continued to flow to foreign allies [038-028].

> "When the emperor Su Tsung died, in 762, Tengri, the khan of the Uighurs, decided to make himself ruler over China. The events of the preceding years had shown him that China alone was entirely defenceless." [038-028]

The Uighur khan's bid for power was thwarted only by the intervention of P'u-ku [[hsiung-nu|Hu]]ai-en, who was related to Tengri by marriage [038-032]. This episode underscored how profoundly the rebellion had weakened the dynasty: at the moment of Su Tsung's death, a foreign khan seriously contemplated seizing control of China itself.

## Legacy

Su Tsung's reign represents a turning point in T'ang history. He succeeded in preserving the dynasty against an existential threat, but the cost was immense. The reliance on Uighur mercenaries and the massive payments to foreign allies drained the imperial treasury and set a precedent for military dependency on steppe powers. The rebellion he suppressed shattered the empire's fiscal foundations and inaugurated the long decline of the T'ang dynasty. Su Tsung is recorded in the corpus index as simply "Su Tsung, T'ang ruler" — a laconic entry that belies the tumult of his reign [074-244].
