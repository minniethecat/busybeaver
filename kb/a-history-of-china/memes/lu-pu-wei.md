## Overview

[[lü-pu-wei|Lü Pu-wei]] was one of the most remarkable figures of the Warring States period — a merchant who rose from commoner status to become Prime Minister of the [[qin-dynasty|Ch'in]] state and regent for the future First Emperor. His career marks a pivotal moment in Chinese political history: the first time a man outside the traditional aristocracy attained supreme power, and the last time a policy of peaceful consolidation guided the Ch'in state before the Legalist juggernaut of conquest consumed all of China.

## The Merchant in Chao

Lü Pu-wei's ascent began with a chance encounter in the state of Chao, where a son of the Ch'in ruler was living as a political hostage. Lü recognized the opportunity:

> There he made the acquaintance of an unusual man, the merchant Lü Pu-wei, a man of education and of great political influence. Lü Pu-wei persuaded the feudal ruler of Ch'in to declare this son his successor. [013-057]

To cement the arrangement, Lü Pu-wei "also sold a girl to the prince to be his wife, and the son of this marriage was to be the famous and notorious [[shih-huang-ti|Shih Huang-ti]]" [013-060] — the future [[shih-huang-ti|First Emperor of China]].

## Prime Minister and Regent

Lü Pu-wei accompanied his protégé to Ch'in, where he became the prince's Prime Minister. When the prince died in 247 BC, Lü Pu-wei assumed the regency for the young [[shih-huang-ti|Shih Huang-ti]] (then called Cheng):

> For the first time in Chinese history a merchant, a commoner, had reached one of the highest positions in the state. [013-065]

The exact nature of his commercial activities is uncertain, but contemporary evidence suggests he "probably dealt in horses, the principal export of the state of Chao. As horses were an absolute necessity for the armies of that time, it is easy to imagine that a horse-dealer might gain great political influence" [013-067]. The horse trade, essential to the chariot and cavalry warfare of the era, provided a natural bridge between commerce and state power.

## Dismissal and the Legalist Turn

Lü Pu-wei's regency pursued a policy of peaceful consolidation. This approach was abruptly reversed after [[shih-huang-ti|Shih Huang-ti]] came of age:

> Soon after Shih Huang-ti's accession Lü Pu-wei was dismissed, and a new group of advisers, strong supporters of the Legalist school, came into power. These new men began an active policy of conquest instead of the peaceful course which Lü Pu-wei had pursued. [013-071]

The contrast could not have been sharper. Under Lü Pu-wei, the Qin state had maintained its strength without aggressive expansion. His Legalist successors — drawing on the tradition of [[shang-yang|Shang Yang]] — launched the series of campaigns that, between 230 and 222 BC, conquered and annexed all the remaining feudal states, unifying China under the Qin banner [013-074].

## Assessment

Lü Pu-wei's career illuminates a path not taken in Chinese history. His commercial background, his preference for peaceful statecraft, and his role as a commoner who attained regency all distinguish him from the aristocratic and Legalist figures who dominated the Qin unification. His dismissal cleared the way for the militarized, centralized Qin empire — the very state that would later be remembered both for its monumental achievements and for the severity of its Legalist governance. The merchant-regent who might have guided China toward a different model of imperial rule was instead relegated to a footnote, his peaceful course overtaken by the relentless logic of conquest.
