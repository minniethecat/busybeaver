## Overview

Karl August Wittfogel (1896–1988) was a German-American sinologist, historian, and social theorist whose work on Chinese society — particularly his theory of "hydraulic civilization" and "Oriental despotism" — had a profound impact on the study of Chinese political economy in the mid-twentieth century. Eberhard engages with Wittfogel's scholarship at multiple points in _A History of China_, both drawing on his empirical research and contesting his broader theoretical conclusions.

Wittfogel's most significant contribution to Chinese historical studies was his collaborative work with Feng Chia-sheng, _History of Chinese Society: Liao_ (Philadelphia, 1949), which Eberhard describes as "the standard work" for that period [071-143]. His theoretical magnum opus, _Oriental Despotism: A Comparative Study of Total Power_ (New Haven, 1957), advanced the controversial thesis that large-scale irrigation agriculture in "hydraulic societies" — including China — necessitated centralized bureaucratic control, creating a distinctive form of autocracy fundamentally different from Western feudalism.

Eberhard's relationship with Wittfogel's work is one of critical engagement. He acknowledges _Oriental Despotism_ as a significant contribution while registering his reservations: the book, he notes, "has a different interpretation" from his own [069-117] and, crucially, "does not go into the necessary detailed work" to substantiate its sweeping claims [071-035].

## Scholarly Contributions

### History of Chinese Society: Liao

Wittfogel's most enduring empirical contribution is the monumental _History of Chinese Society: Liao (907–1125)_, co-authored with the Chinese scholar Feng Chia-sheng and published by the American Philosophical Society in 1949. Eberhard repeatedly cites this work as authoritative: "For this period the standard work is K.A. Wittfogel and Feng Chia-sheng, _History of Chinese Society, Liao_" [071-143]. The volume is also referenced as the definitive treatment of the Kara-Kitai (Western Liao) [071-150].

The Liao study was methodologically innovative. It applied sociological categories — class structure, economic organization, administrative institutions — to the analysis of a conquest dynasty, treating the Khitan state not as a peripheral curiosity but as a sophisticated dual-administration system that balanced nomadic and sedentary populations. This approach influenced a generation of scholarship on China's Inner Asian frontier.

### Oriental Despotism and the Hydraulic Thesis

Wittfogel's _Oriental Despotism_ (1957) was one of the most influential — and controversial — works of twentieth-century sinology. Its central argument held that societies dependent on large-scale irrigation (hydraulic agriculture) developed distinctive political structures: centralized bureaucracy, state control of the economy, and an autocratic ruler whose power was unchecked by independent aristocracies, churches, or cities.

Eberhard summarizes the thesis succinctly: Wittfogel "regards irrigation as a key economic and social factor and has built up his theory around this element" [069-025]. The implications for Chinese history were far-reaching: if China's political authoritarianism was rooted in the material requirements of rice agriculture, then Chinese despotism was not a cultural pathology but a structural product of environmental conditions.

> "K.A. Wittfogel's _Oriental Despotism_, New Haven 1957 does not go into the necessary detailed work. The problem of social mobility is now under study, after preliminary research by K.A. Wittfogel, E. Kracke, myself and others." [071-035]

Eberhard's critique is measured but significant. While he acknowledges the theory's generative power — Wittfogel's work had stimulated further research on social mobility by Eberhard himself, E. Kracke, Ho Ping-ti, and R.M. Marsh [071-038] — he is unwilling to accept its totalizing claims without more granular empirical support.

### The Crisis Cycle Theory

A subsidiary but important Wittfogel thesis, developed as early as 1938, proposed that Chinese history exhibited a cyclical pattern of dynastic crisis driven by tax evasion. As Eberhard describes it: "This process of tax-evasion has been used by K.A. Wittfogel (1938) to construct a theory of a crisis cycle in China." Eberhard's verdict is unambiguous: "I do not think that such far-reaching conclusions are warranted" [071-106].

## Influence and Legacy

Wittfogel occupies an ambiguous position in the historiography of China. His empirical work — especially the Liao volume — remains a standard reference, its detailed sociological analysis of a conquest dynasty undiminished by the passage of time. Eberhard lists Wittfogel alongside such major figures as [[hu-shih|Hu Shih]], Yang Lien-sheng, and Paul Demiéville as scholars who have contributed to the interpretation of key problems in Chinese social history [068-124].

His theoretical work has proven more contentious. The hydraulic despotism thesis, while enormously influential in its day — shaping debates in comparative politics, development economics, and Cold War Sovietology as well as sinology — has been widely criticized for its environmental determinism, its inattention to counterexamples, and its political instrumentalization during the McCarthy era (Wittfogel himself testified against former Communist associates, including Owen Lattimore).

Eberhard's own position is characteristic of the broader scholarly reception: respectful acknowledgment of Wittfogel's empirical contributions, coupled with resistance to his theoretical ambitions. The hydraulic thesis raised important questions about the relationship between ecology and politics; whether it provided satisfactory answers remained, in Eberhard's view, an open question — one requiring the "necessary detailed work" that Wittfogel had not himself performed [071-035].
