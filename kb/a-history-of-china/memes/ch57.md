# Section 57

[057-001] 5 _Relations with the outer world_

[057-002] During the Yung-cheng period there was long-continued guerrilla fighting

[057-003] with natives in south-west China. The pressure of population in China

[057-004] sought an outlet in emigration. More and more Chinese moved into the

[057-005] south-west, and took the land from the natives, and the fighting was the

[057-006] consequence of this.

[057-007] At the beginning of the Ch'ien-lung period (1736-1796), fighting started

[057-008] again in Turkestan. Mongols, now called Kalmuks, defeated by the

[057-009] Chinese, had migrated to the Ili region, where after heavy fighting they

[057-010] gained supremacy over some of the Kazaks and other Turkish peoples

[057-011] living there and in western Turkestan. Some Kazak tribes went over to

[057-012] the Russians, and in 1735 the Russian colonialists founded the town of

[057-013] Orenburg in the western Kazak region. The Kalmuks fought the Chinese

[057-014] without cessation until, in 1739, they entered into an agreement under

[057-015] which they ceded half their territory to Manchu China, retaining only

[057-016] the Ili region. The Kalmuks subsequently reunited with other sections of

[057-017] the Kazaks against the Chinese. In 1754 peace was again concluded with

[057-018] China, but it was followed by raids on both sides, so that the Manchus

[057-019] determined to enter on a great campaign against the Ili region. This

[057-020] ended with a decisive victory for the Chinese (1755). In the years that

[057-021] followed, however, the Chinese began to be afraid that the various Kazak

[057-022] tribes might unite in order to occupy the territory of the Kalmuks,

[057-023] which was almost unpopulated owing to the mass slaughter of Kalmuks by

[057-024] the Chinese. Unrest began among the Mohammedans throughout the

[057-025] neighbouring western Turkestan, and the same Chinese generals who had

[057-026] fought the Kalmuks marched into Turkestan and captured the Mohammedan

[057-027] city states of Uch, Kashgar, and Yarkand.

[057-028] The reinforcements for these campaigns, and for the garrisons which in

[057-029] the following decades were stationed in the Ili region and in the west

[057-030] of eastern Turkestan, marched along the road from Peking that leads

[057-031] northward through Mongolia to the far distant Uliassutai and Kobdo. The

[057-032] cost of transport for one _shih_ (about 66 lb.) amounted to 120 pieces

[057-033] of silver. In 1781 certain economies were introduced, but between 1781

[057-034] and 1791 over 30,000 tons, making some 8 tons a day, was transported to

[057-035] that region. The cost of transport for supplies alone amounted in the

[057-036] course of time to the not inconsiderable sum of 120,000,000 pieces of

[057-037] silver. In addition to this there was the cost of the transported goods

[057-038] and of the pay of soldiers and of the administration. These figures

[057-039] apply to the period of occupation, of relative peace: during the actual

[057-040] wars of conquest the expenditure was naturally far higher. Thus these

[057-041] campaigns, though I do not think they brought actual economic ruin to

[057-042] China, were nevertheless a costly enterprise, and one which produced

[057-043] little positive advantage.

[057-044] In addition to this, these wars brought China into conflict with the

[057-045] European colonial powers. In the years during which the Chinese armies

[057-046] were fighting in the Ili region, the Russians were putting out their

[057-047] feelers in that direction, and the Chinese annals show plainly how the

[057-048] Russians intervened in the fighting with the Kalmuks and Kazaks. The Hi

[057-049] region remained thereafter a bone of contention between China and

[057-050] Russia, until it finally went to Russia, bit by bit, between 1847 and

[057-051] 1881\. The Kalmuks and Kazaks played a special part in Russo-Chinese

[057-052] relations. The Chinese had sent a mission to the Kalmuks farthest west,

[057-053] by the lower Volga, and had entered into relations with them, as early

[057-054] as 1714. As Russian pressure on the Volga region continually grew, these

[057-055] Kalmuks (mainly the Turgut tribe), who had lived there since 1630,

[057-056] decided to return into Chinese territory (1771). During this enormously

[057-057] difficult migration, almost entirely through hostile territory, a large

[057-058] number of the Turgut perished; 85,000, however, reached the Hi region,

[057-059] where they were settled by the Chinese on the lands of the eastern

[057-060] Kalmuks, who had been largely exterminated.

[057-061] In the south, too, the Chinese came into direct touch with the European

[057-062] powers. In 1757 the English occupied Calcutta, and in 1766 the province

[057-063] of Bengal. In 1767 a Manchu general, Ming Jui, who had been victorious

[057-064] in the fighting for eastern Turkestan, marched against Burma, which was

[057-065] made a dependency once more in 1769. And in 1790-1791 the Chinese

[057-066] conquered Nepal, south of Tibet, because Nepalese had made two attacks

[057-067] on Tibet. Thus English and Chinese political interests came here into

[057-068] contact.

[057-069] For the Ch'ien-lung period's many wars of conquest there seem to have

[057-070] been two main reasons. The first was the need for security. The Mongols

[057-071] had to be overthrown because otherwise the homeland of the Manchus was

[057-072] menaced; in order to make sure of the suppression of the eastern

[057-073] Mongols, the western Mongols (Kalmuks) had to be overthrown; to make

[057-074] them harmless, Turkestan and the Ili region had to be conquered; Tibet

[057-075] was needed for the security of Turkestan and Mongolia--and so on. Vast

[057-076] territories, however, were conquered in this process which were of no

[057-077] economic value, and most of which actually cost a great deal of money

[057-078] and brought nothing in. They were conquered simply for security. That

[057-079] advantage had been gained: an aggressor would have to cross great areas

[057-080] of unproductive territory, with difficult conditions for reinforcements,

[057-081] before he could actually reach China. In the second place, the Chinese

[057-082] may actually have noticed the efforts that were being made by the

[057-083] European powers, especially Russia and England, to divide Asia among

[057-084] themselves, and accordingly they made sure of their own good share.

[057-085] 6 _Decline; revolts_

[057-086] The period of Ch'ien-lung is not only that of the greatest expansion of

[057-087] the Chinese empire, but also that of the greatest prosperity under the

[057-088] Manchu regime. But there began at the same time to be signs of internal

[057-089] decline. If we are to fix a particular year for this, perhaps it should

[057-090] be the year 1774, in which came the first great popular rising, in the

[057-091] province of Shantung. In 1775 there came another popular rising, in

[057-092] Honan--that of the "Society of the White Lotus". This society, which had

[057-093] long existed as a secret organization and had played a part in the Ming

[057-094] epoch, had been reorganized by a man named Liu Sung. Liu Sung was

[057-095] captured and was condemned to penal servitude. His followers, however,

[057-096] regrouped themselves, particularly in the province of Anhui. These

[057-097] risings had been produced, as always, by excessive oppression of the

[057-098] people by the government or the governing class. As, however, the anger

[057-099] of the population was naturally directed also against the idle Manchus

[057-100] of the cities, who lived on their state pensions, did no work, and

[057-101] behaved as a ruling class, the government saw in these movements a

[057-102] nationalist spirit, and took drastic steps against them. The popular

[057-103] leaders now altered their program, and acclaimed a supposed descendant

[057-104] from the Ming dynasty as the future emperor. Government troops caught

[057-105] the leader of the "White Lotus" agitation, but he succeeded in escaping.

[057-106] In the regions through which the society had spread, there then began a

[057-107] sort of Inquisition, of exceptional ferocity. Six provinces were

[057-108] affected, and in and around the single city of Wuch'ang in four months

[057-109] more than 20,000 people were beheaded. The cost of the rising to the

[057-110] government ran into millions. In answer to this oppression, the popular

[057-111] leaders tightened their organization and marched north-west from the

[057-112] western provinces of which they had gained control. The rising was

[057-113] suppressed only by a very big military operation, and not until 1802.

[057-114] There had been very heavy fighting between 1793 and 1802--just when in

[057-115] Europe, in the French Revolution, another oppressed population won its

[057-116] freedom.

[057-117] The Ch'ien-lung emperor abdicated on New Year's Day, 1795, after ruling

[057-118] for sixty years. He died in 1799. His successor was Jen Tsung

[057-119] (1796-1821; reign name: Chia-ch'ing). In the course of his reign the

[057-120] rising of the "White Lotus" was suppressed, but in 1813 there began a

[057-121] new rising, this time in North China--again that of a secret

[057-122] organization, the "Society of Heaven's Law". One of its leaders bribed

[057-123] some eunuchs, and penetrated with a group of followers into the palace;

[057-124] he threw himself upon the emperor, who was only saved through the

[057-125] intervention of his son. At the same time the rising spread in the

[057-126] provinces. Once more the government succeeded in suppressing it and

[057-127] capturing the leaders. But the memory of these risings was kept alive

[057-128] among the Chinese people. For the government failed to realize that the

[057-129] actual cause of the risings was the general impoverishment, and saw in

[057-130] them a nationalist movement, thus actually arousing a national

[057-131] consciousness, stronger than in the Ming epoch, among the middle and

[057-132] lower classes of the people, together with hatred of the Manchus. They

[057-133] were held responsible for every evil suffered, regardless of the fact

[057-134] that similar evils had existed earlier.

[057-135] 7 _European Imperialism in the Far East_

[057-136] With the Tao-kuang period (1821-1850) began a new period in Chinese

[057-137] history, which came to an end only in 1911.

[057-138] In foreign affairs these ninety years were marked by the steadily

[057-139] growing influence of the Western powers, aimed at turning China into a

[057-140] colony. Culturally this period was that of the gradual infiltration of

[057-141] Western civilization into the Far East; it was recognized in China that

[057-142] it was necessary to learn from the West. In home affairs we see the

[057-143] collapse of the dynasty and the destruction of the unity of the empire;

[057-144] of four great civil wars, one almost brought the dynasty to its end.

[057-145] North and South China, the coastal area and the interior, developed in

[057-146] different ways.

[057-147] Great Britain had made several attempts to improve her trade relations

[057-148] with China, but the mission of 1793 had no success, and that of 1816

[057-149] also failed. English merchants, like all foreign merchants, were only

[057-150] permitted to settle in a small area adjoining Canton and at Macao, and

[057-151] were only permitted to trade with a particular group of monopolists,

[057-152] known as the "Hong". The Hong had to pay taxes to the state, but they

[057-153] had a wonderful opportunity of enriching themselves. The Europeans were

[057-154] entirely at their mercy, for they were not allowed to travel inland, and

[057-155] they were not allowed to try to negotiate with other merchants, to

[057-156] secure lower prices by competition.

[057-157] The Europeans concentrated especially on the purchase of silk and tea;

[057-158] but what could they import into China? The higher the price of the goods

[057-159] and the smaller the cargo space involved, the better were the chances of

[057-160] profit for the merchants. It proved, however, that European woollens or

[057-161] luxury goods could not be sold; the Chinese would probably have been

[057-162] glad to buy food, but transport was too expensive to permit profitable

[057-163] business. Thus a new article was soon discovered--opium, carried from

[057-164] India to China: the price was high and the cargo space involved was very

[057-165] small. The Chinese were familiar with opium, and bought it readily.

[057-166] Accordingly, from 1800 onwards opium became more and more the chief

[057-167] article of trade, especially for the English, who were able to bring it

[057-168] conveniently from India. Opium is harmful to the people; the opium trade

[057-169] resulted in certain groups of merchants being inordinately enriched; a

[057-170] great deal of Chinese money went abroad. The government became

[057-171] apprehensive and sent Lin Tsê-hsü as its commissioner to Canton. In 1839

[057-172] he prohibited the opium trade and burned the chests of opium found in

[057-173] British possession. The British view was that to tolerate the Chinese

[057-174] action might mean the destruction of British trade in the Far East and

[057-175] that, on the other hand, it might be possible by active intervention to

[057-176] compel the Chinese to open other ports to European trade and to shake

[057-177] off the monopoly of the Canton merchants. In 1840 British ships-of-war

[057-178] appeared off the south-eastern coast of China and bombarded it. In 1841

[057-179] the Chinese opened negotiations and dismissed Lin Tsê-hsü. As the

[057-180] Chinese concessions were regarded as inadequate, hostilities continued;

[057-181] the British entered the Yangtze estuary and threatened Nanking. In this

[057-182] first armed conflict with the West, China found herself defenceless

[057-183] owing to her lack of a navy, and it was also found that the European

[057-184] weapons were far superior to those of the Chinese. In 1842 China was

[057-185] compelled to capitulate: under the Treaty of Nanking Hong Kong was ceded

[057-186] to Great Britain, a war indemnity was paid, certain ports were thrown

[057-187] open to European trade, and the monopoly was brought to an end. A great

[057-188] deal of opium came, however, into China through smuggling--regrettably,

[057-189] for the state lost the customs revenue!
