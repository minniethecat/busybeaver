## Overview

[[lin-tse-hsu|Lin Tsê-hsü]] (1785–1850) was a [[qing-dynasty|Qing dynasty]] official whose principled stand against the British opium trade made him a national hero in China and a symbol of resistance to foreign encroachment. His uncompromising actions as Imperial Commissioner at Canton in 1839 triggered the chain of events that led to the [[opium-war|First Opium War]], yet his moral clarity and personal integrity earned him enduring respect even as the military outcome proved disastrous for the dynasty.

> The emperor, increasingly apprehensive about the social and economic effects of the opium trade, sent Lin Tsê-hsü as its commissioner to Canton to suppress the traffic once and for all. [057-171]

## The Opium Crisis at Canton

Arriving at [[peking|Canton]] in March 1839, Lin moved decisively against the foreign opium merchants. He surrounded the foreign factories, demanded the surrender of all opium stocks, and when the British superintendent of trade refused to cooperate, Lin confiscated and publicly destroyed some 20,000 chests of opium — an act that directly precipitated military confrontation with Britain.

Lin combined moral suasion with coercive pressure. He wrote an open letter to [[peking|Queen Victoria]], appealing to her conscience and arguing that Britain should not profit from a trade it had banned within its own borders. The letter — never delivered — became one of the most famous documents of nineteenth-century Chinese diplomacy.

## Dismissal and Aftermath

Lin's uncompromising approach alarmed elements at the Qing court who feared the consequences of war:

> The court, under pressure from pro-peace elements and alarmed by British naval mobilization, abandoned negotiations and dismissed Lin Tsê-hsü from his post. [057-179]

He was blamed for provoking the war and exiled to [[peking|Chinese Turkestan]], though he was later rehabilitated and served in other provincial posts. The [[opium-war|Treaty of Nanking]] (1842), which ended the war, granted Britain the concessions Lin had fought to prevent — including the opening of [[peking|treaty ports]] and the cession of [[peking|Hong Kong]].

## Legacy

Lin Tsê-hsü's reputation survived the military defeat he helped precipitate. He was remembered as a model Confucian official — incorruptible, principled, and patriotically devoted to the nation's welfare. In the twentieth century, both the [[kuomintang|Nationalists]] and [[mao-tse-tung|Communists]] claimed his legacy, and June 3 — the day he began destroying the opium at [[peking|Humen]] — is observed as an anti-drug day in [[china|China]] and [[peking|Taiwan]]. His career encapsulated the dilemmas faced by Qing officials confronting Western power: moral rightness was no substitute for military and technological superiority.
