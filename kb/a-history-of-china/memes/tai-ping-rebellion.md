## Overview

The T'ai P'ing Rebellion (1851–1864) was one of the largest civil wars in Chinese history, originating among impoverished Hakka peasants in the southern province of Kwangsi. Its leader, [[hsiung-nu|Hu]]ng Hsiu-ch'üan (born 1814), fused Christian ideas learned from missionaries with traditional Chinese thought to proclaim a new doctrine promising the Kingdom of God on earth. He called himself "Christ's younger brother" and named his kingdom _T'ai P'ing_ ("Supreme Peace") [058-124].

> The leader was a quite simple man of Hakka blood, Hung Hsiu-ch'üan (born 1814), who gathered impoverished Hakka peasants round him as every peasant leader had done in the past. [058-114]

The rebellion rapidly evolved from a religious-social movement into an anti-Manchu nationalist uprising. [[hsiung-nu|Hu]]ng's forces captured Hankow in 1852 and Nanking in 1853, establishing the latter as their capital — a deliberate echo of the Ming dynasty's founding [058-143]. At its height, the T'ai P'ing controlled much of central China and advanced nearly to Tientsin (1853–1855), threatening [[peking|Peking]] itself [058-157].

The movement's program was revolutionary: communal ownership of property, equal land distribution between men and women, equal rights for women including military service and official positions, prohibition of opium, tobacco, and alcohol, and the recognition of foreigners as equals [058-128]. However, [[hsiung-nu|Hu]]ng failed to systematize these reforms, and the regime degenerated into terrorism [058-159].

The Europeans, after long hesitation, sided with the Manchus — preferring a weak Qing government bound by treaties to a strong, unpredictable T'ai P'ing state with socialistic elements [058-169]. The rebellion was ultimately suppressed by regional Chinese forces led by [[tseng-kuo-fan|Tsêng Kuo-fan]] and [[li-hung-chang|Li Hung-chang]], who recruited peasant militias to defend their homes. Nanking fell in 1864, ending the T'ai P'ing Heavenly Kingdom [058-183].

> While in the central provinces the T'ai P'ing rebellion was raging, China was suffering grave setbacks owing to the Lorcha War of 1856; and there were also great and serious risings in other parts of the country. [059-001]

The suppression of the rebellion, together with contemporary uprisings including the [[nien-fei|Nien Fei]] and Mohammedan revolts, caused enormous demographic loss — the population of some provinces fell dramatically, though reliable statistics are absent [059-026].
