## Overview

[[an-lu-shan|An Lu-shan]] (703–757) was a military governor ([[an-lu-shan-rebellion|jiedushi]]) of the [[tang-dynasty|T'ang dynasty]] whose massive rebellion, launched in 755, nearly toppled the dynasty and permanently weakened its central authority. Of mixed Sogdian and Turkic origin, his career exemplified both the cosmopolitan nature of T'ang frontier administration and the dangers of concentrating military power in the hands of provincial governors.

> An Lu-shan (703-757). His mother was a Turkic shamaness, his father probably of Sogdian origin. An Lu-shan succeeded in gaining the favour of the emperor, who loaded him with honours. [037-147][037-148]

His rebellion — known as the [[an-lu-shan-rebellion|An Lu-shan Rebellion]] — was the first of a series of provincial uprisings that transformed T'ang China from a centralized empire into a fragmented collection of semi-autonomous military regions. The economic and demographic cost of the uprising was staggering, and the dynasty never fully recovered its former power.

## Rise to Power

An Lu-shan's ascent was built on his command of the frontier armies in the northeast:

> An Lu-shan had been a military governor. He commanded a number of armies on the northern frontier and possessed the confidence of the emperor. [038-002]

> His rising was the first of many military revolts that would plague the late T'ang. [038-006]

He cultivated a close relationship with [[hsuan-tsung|Emperor Hsüan Tsung]] and [[yang-kuei-fei|Yang Kuei-fei]], accumulating multiple military commands that gave him control over the vital northeast frontier. By 755, he commanded some 160,000 troops — nearly half the empire's total military strength.

## The Rebellion (755–757)

In late 755, An Lu-shan marched south from his base in modern Hebei toward the T'ang capitals:

> Against An Lu-shan, loyalist forces marched against the capital, but he defended himself against them and captured the eastern capital, Lo-yang. [037-160][037-170]

> The emperor fled betimes, and An Lu-shan captured the western capital, Ch'ang-an. [037-167]

The fall of both capitals — [[peking|Lo-yang]] and [[peking|Ch'ang-an]] — was a psychological shock that permanently damaged the dynasty's prestige. The emperor fled to Szechwan while his son organized the resistance from the northwest.

## Base of Support

An Lu-shan's rebellion drew on deep regional and ethnic grievances:

> Entirely ready to support An Lu-shan against the government, because they felt they were being exploited by the central administration. [038-008]

> The rebels An Lu-shan and [[an-lu-shan-rebellion|Shih Ssŭ-ming]] were non-Chinese, and many of their followers were frontier peoples dissatisfied with T'ang rule. [038-012]

His coalition included disaffected frontier tribes, provincial elites resentful of central control, and military commanders who saw greater opportunity in rebellion than in loyalty to the distant T'ang court.

## Death and Aftermath

An Lu-shan was assassinated in 757 by his own son, but the rebellion continued under [[an-lu-shan-rebellion|Shih Ssŭ-ming]] and his successors until 763:

> Shih Ssŭ-ming entered into An Lu-shan's conspiracy and continued the revolt after An's death. [037-181]

> The effects of the rebellion can be seen from its end onward: the T'ang state never recovered its fiscal or military integrity. [039-061]

The rebellion permanently altered China's political geography. The central government, forced to grant extensive autonomy to provincial military governors in exchange for their nominal loyalty, gradually lost control over tax revenue and military recruitment, setting the stage for the regional fragmentation that characterized the late T'ang and the Five Dynasties period that followed.
