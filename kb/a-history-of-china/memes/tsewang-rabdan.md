## Overview

**Tsewang Rabdan** was a leader of the Ölöt ([[Dzungar]]) [[mongol|Mongols]] in the late seventeenth and early eighteenth centuries. Installed as khan with Chinese backing, he subsequently turned against the [[Qing dynasty|Ch'ing]] empire during the reign of the [[Kangxi Emperor|K'ang-hsi Emperor]], triggering a series of frontier wars that expanded Qing control deep into Central Asia.

## Rise to Power

Tsewang Rabdan emerged in the aftermath of the defeat of [[galdan|Galdan]], the previous Dzungar khan who had been crushed by the [[kang-hsi|K'ang-hsi]] Emperor in personal campaigns between 1690 and 1696. The Qing, seeking a pacified western frontier, elevated Tsewang Rabdan as a client ruler:

> "Tsewang Rabdan, whom the Chinese had made khan of the Ölöt, rose against the Chinese." [056-019] [056-020]

## Rebellion Against the Qing

In 1715, new disturbances broke out in western Mongolia, and Tsewang Rabdan led an uprising against his former patrons. The wars that followed extended far beyond Mongolia:

> "The wars that followed, extending far into Turkestan and also involving its Turkish population together with the Dzungars, ended with the Chinese conquest of the whole of Mongolia and of parts of eastern Turkestan." [056-020] [056-021] [056-022] [056-023]

## Tibetan Campaign

Tsewang Rabdan's ambitions extended southward as well. He attempted to project Dzungar power into [[Tibet]]:

> "As Tsewang Rabdan had tried to extend his power as far as Tibet, a campaign was undertaken also into Tibet, Lhasa was occupied, a new Dalai Lama was installed there as supreme." [056-023] [056-024] [056-025]

The Qing response was decisive: Chinese forces occupied Lhasa and reorganised Tibetan governance, further entrenching Qing suzerainty over the Tibetan plateau.

## Legacy

Tsewang Rabdan's rebellion, though ultimately defeated, reshaped the political geography of Inner Asia. The Qing conquest of Mongolia, Turkestan, and the consolidation of control over Tibet were all accelerated by his challenge. The Dzungar people themselves were largely annihilated in the subsequent Qing campaigns of the 1750s under the [[Ch'ien-lung Emperor]].
