# Section 71

[071-001] pp. 187-8: The conditions at the time of this persecution are well

[071-002] described by E.O. Reischauer, _Ennin's Travels in T'ang China_, New York

[071-003] 1955, on the basis of his _Ennin's Diary. The Record of a Pilgrimage to

[071-004] China_, New York 1955. The persecution of Buddhism has been analysed in

[071-005] its economic character by Niida Noboru and other Japanese

[071-006] scholars.--Metal statues had to be delivered to the Salt and Iron Office

[071-007] in order to be converted into cash; iron statues were collected by local

[071-008] offices for the production of agricultural implements; figures in gold,

[071-009] silver or other rare materials were to be handed over to the Finance

[071-010] Office. Figures made of stone, clay or wood were not affected

[071-011] (Michihata).

[071-012] p. 189: It seems important to note that popular movements are often not

[071-013] led by simple farmers of members of the lower classes. There are other

[071-014] salt merchants and persons of similar status known as leaders.

[071-015] p. 190: For the Sha-t'o, I am relying upon my own research. Tatars are

[071-016] the Ta-tan of the Chinese sources. The term is here used in a narrow

[071-017] sense.

[071-018] _Chapter Nine_

[071-019] p. 195: Many Chinese and Japanese authors have a new period begin with

[071-020] the early (Ch'ien Mu) or the late tenth century (T'ao Hsi-sheng, Li

[071-021] Chien-nung), while others prefer a cut already in the Middle of the

[071-022] T'ang Dynasty (Teng Ch'u-min, Naito Torajiro). For many Marxists, the

[071-023] period which we called "Modern Times" is at best a sub-period within a

[071-024] larger period which really started with what we called "Medieval China".

[071-025] p. 196: For the change in the composition of the gentry, I am using my

[071-026] own research.--For clan rules, clan foundations, etc., I used D.C.

[071-027] Twitchett, J. Fischer, Hu Hsien-chin, Ch'ü T'ung-tsu, Niida Noboru and

[071-028] T. Makino. The best analysis of the clan rules is by Wang Hui-chen in

[071-029] D.S. Nivison, _Confucianism in Action_, Stanford 1959, p. 63-96.--I do

[071-030] not regard such marriage systems as "survivals" of ancient systems which

[071-031] have been studied by M. Granet and systematically analysed by C.

[071-032] Lévy-Strauss in his _Les structures élémentaires de la parenté_, Paris

[071-033] 1949, pp. 381-443. In some cases, the reasons for the establishment of

[071-034] such rules can still be recognized.--A detailed study of despotism in

[071-035] China still has to be written. K.A. Wittfogel's _Oriental Despotism_,

[071-036] New Haven 1957 does not go into the necessary detailed work.

[071-037] p. 197: The problem of social mobility is now under study, after

[071-038] preliminary research by K.A. Wittfogel, E. Kracke, myself and others. E.

[071-039] Kracke, Ho Ping-ti, R.M. Marsh and I are now working on this topic.--For

[071-040] the craftsmen and artisans, much material has recently been collected by

[071-041] Chinese scholars. I have used mainly Li Chien-nung and articles in

[071-042] _Li-shih yen-chiu_ 1955, No. 3 and in _Mem. Inst. Orient. Cult_.

[071-043] 1956.--On the origin of guilds see Kat[=o] Shigeru; a general study of

[071-044] guilds and their function has not yet been made (preliminary work by P.

[071-045] Maybon, H.B. Morse, J. St. Burgess, K.A. Wittfogel and others).

[071-046] Comparisons with Near-Eastern guilds on the one hand and with Japanese

[071-047] guilds on the other, are quite interesting but parallels should not be

[071-048] over-estimated. The _tong_ of U.S. Chinatowns (_tang_ in Mandarin) are

[071-049] late and organizations of businessmen only (S. Yokoyama and Laai

[071-050] Yi-faai). They are not the same as the _hui-kuan_.

[071-051] p. 198: For the merchants I used Ch'ü T'ung-tsu, Sung Hsi and Wada

[071-052] Kiyoshi.--For trade, I used extensively Ch'üan Han-sheng and J.

[071-053] Kuwabara.--On labour legislation in early modern times I used Ko

[071-054] Ch'ang-chi and especially Li Chien-nung, also my own studies.--On

[071-055] strikes I used Kat[=o] Shigeru and modern Chinese authors.--The problem

[071-056] of "vagrants" has been taken up by Li Chien-nung who always refers to

[071-057] the original sources and to modern Chinese research.--The growth of

[071-058] cities, perhaps the most striking event in this period, has been studied

[071-059] for the earlier part of our period by Kat[=o] Shigeru. Li Chien-nung

[071-060] also deals extensively with investments in industry and agriculture. The

[071-061] problem as to whether China would have developed into an industrial

[071-062] society without outside stimulus is much discussed by Marxist authors in

[071-063] China.

[071-064] p. 199: On money policy see Yang Lien-sheng, Kat[=o] Shigeru and others.

[071-065] p. 200: The history of one of the Southern Dynasties has been translated

[071-066] by Ed. H. Schafer, _The Empire of Min_, Tokyo 1954; Schafer's

[071-067] annotations provide much detail for the cultural and economic conditions

[071-068] of the coastal area.--For tea and its history, I use my own research;

[071-069] for tea trade a study by K. Kawakami and an article in the _Frontier

[071-070] Studies_, vol. 3, 1943.--Salt consumption according to H.T. Fei,

[071-071] _Earthbound China, 1945, p_. 163.

[071-072] p. 201: For salt I used largely my own research. For porcelain

[071-073] production Li Chien-nung and other modern articles.--On paper, the

[071-074] classical study is Th. F. Carter, _The Invention of Printing in China_,

[071-075] New York 1925 (a revised edition now published by L.C. Goodrich).

[071-076] p. 202: For paper money in the early period, see Yang Lien-sheng, _Money

[071-077] and Credit in China_, Cambridge, Mass., 1952. Although the origin of

[071-078] paper money seems to be well established, it is interesting to note that

[071-079] already in the third century A.D. money made of paper was produced and

[071-080] was burned during funeral ceremonies to serve as financial help for the

[071-081] dead. This money was, however, in the form of coins.--On iron money see

[071-082] Yang Lien-sheng; I also used an article in _Tung-fang tsa-chih_, vol.

[071-083] 35, No. 10.

[071-084] p. 203: For the Kitan (Chines: Ch'i-tan) and their history see K.A.

[071-085] Wittfogel and Feng Chia-sheng, _History of Chinese Society. Liao_,

[071-086] Philadelphia 1949.

[071-087] p. 204: For these dynasties, I rely upon my own research.--Niida Noboru

[071-088] and Kat[=o] Shigeru have studied adoption laws; our specific case has in

[071-089] addition been studied by M. Kurihara. This system of adoptions is

[071-090] non-Chinese and has its parallels among Turkish tribes (A. Kollantz,

[071-091] Abdulkadir Inan, Osman Turan).

[071-092] p. 207: For the persecution I used K. Tamai and my own research.

[071-093] p. 211: This is based mainly upon my own research.--The remark on tax

[071-094] income is from Ch'üan Han-sheng.

[071-095] p. 212: Fan Chung-yen has been studied recently by J. Fischer and D.

[071-096] Twitchett, but these notes on price policies are based upon my own

[071-097] work.--I regard the statement, that it was the gentry which prevented

[071-098] the growth of an industrial society--a statement which has often been

[071-099] made before--as preliminary, and believe that further research,

[071-100] especially in the growth of cities and urban institutions may lead to

[071-101] quite different explanations.--On estate management I relied on Y.

[071-102] Sudô's work.

[071-103] p. 213: Research on place names such as mentioned here, has not yet been

[071-104] systematically done.--On _i-chuang_ I relied upon the work by T. Makino

[071-105] and D. Twitchett.--This process of tax-evasion has been used by K.A.

[071-106] Wittfogel (1938) to construct a theory of a crisis cycle in China. I do

[071-107] not think that such far-reaching conclusions are warranted.

[071-108] p. 214: This "law" was developed on the basis of Chinese materials from

[071-109] different periods as well as on materials from other parts of Asia.--In

[071-110] the study of tenancy, cases should be studied in which wealthier farmers

[071-111] rent additional land which gets cultivated by farm labourers. Such cases

[071-112] are well known from recent periods, but have not yet been studied in

[071-113] earlier periods. At the same time, the problem of farm labourers should

[071-114] be investigated. Such people were common in the Sung time. Research

[071-115] along these lines could further clarify the importance of the so-called

[071-116] "guest families" (_k'o-hu_) which were alluded to in these pages. They

[071-117] constituted often one third of the total population in the Sung period.

[071-118] The problem of migration and mobility might also be clarified by

[071-119] studying the _k'o-hu_.

[071-120] p. 215: For Wang An-shih, the most comprehensive work is still H.

[071-121] Williamson's _Wang An-shih_, London 1935, 3 vol., but this work in no

[071-122] way exhausts the problems. We have so much personal data on Wang that a

[071-123] psychological study could be attempted; and we have since Williamson's

[071-124] time much deeper insight into the reforms and theories of Wang. I used,

[071-125] in addition to Williamson, O. Franke, and my own research.

[071-126] p. 216: Based mainly upon Ch'ü T'ung-tsu.--For the social legislation

[071-127] see Hsü I-t'ang; for economic problems I used Ch'üan Han-sheng, Ts'en

[071-128] Chung-mien and Liu Ming-shu.--Most of these relief measures had their

[071-129] precursors in the T'ang period.

[071-130] p. 217: It is interesting to note that later Buddhism gave up its

[071-131] "social gospel" in China. Buddhist circles in Asian countries at the

[071-132] present time attempt to revive this attitude.

[071-133] p. 218: For slaughtering I used A. Hulsewé; for greeting R. Michihata;

[071-134] on law Ch'ü T'ung-tsu; on philosophy I adapted ideas from Chan Wing-sit.

[071-135] p. 219: A comprehensive study of Chu Hsi is a great desideratum. Thus

[071-136] far, we have in English mainly the essays by Feng Yu-lan (transl. and

[071-137] annotated by D. Bodde) in the _Harvard Journal of Asiat. Stud_., vol. 7,

[071-138] 1942\. T. Makino emphasized Chu's influence upon the Far East, J. Needham

[071-139] his interest in science.

[071-140] p. 220: For Su Tung-p'o as general introduction see Lin Yutang, _The Gay

[071-141] Genius. The Life and Times of Su Tung-p'o_, New York 1947.--For

[071-142] painting, I am using concepts of A. Soper here.

[071-143] p. 222: For this period the standard work is K.A. Wittfogel and Feng

[071-144] Chia-sheng, _History of Chinese Society, Liao_, Philadelphia

[071-145] 1949.--Po-hai had been in tributary relations with the dynasties of

[071-146] North China before its defeat, and resumed these from 932 on; there were

[071-147] even relations with one of the South Chinese states; in the same way,

[071-148] Kao-li continuously played one state against the other (M. Rogers _et

[071-149] al_.).

[071-150] p. 223: On the Kara-Kitai see Appendix to Wittfogel-Feng.

[071-151] p. 228: For the Hakka, I relied mainly upon Lo Hsiang-lin; for Chia

[071-152] Ssu-tao upon H. Franke.

[071-153] p. 229: The Juchên (Jurchen) are also called Nü-chih and Nü-chen, but

[071-154] Juchên seems to be correct (_Studia Serica_, vol. 3, No. 2).

[071-155] _Chapter Ten_

[071-156] p. 233: I use here mainly Meng Ssu-liang, but also others, such as Chü

[071-157] Ch'ing-yüan and Li Chien-nung.--The early political developments are

[071-158] described by H.D. Martin, _The Rise of Chingis Khan and his Conquest of

[071-159] North China_, Baltimore 1950.

[071-160] p. 236: I am alluding here to such Taoist sects as the Cheng-i-chiao

[071-161] (Sun K'o-k'uan and especially the study in _Kita Aziya gakuh[=o]_, vol. 2).

[071-162] pp. 236-7: For taxation and all other economic questions I have relied

[071-163] upon Wan Kuo-ting and especially upon H. Franke. The first part of the

[071-164] main economic text is translated and annotated by H.F. Schurmann,

[071-165] _Economic Structure of the Yüan Dynasty_, Cambridge, Mass., 1956.

[071-166] p. 237: On migrations see T. Makino and others.--For the system of

[071-167] communications during the Mongol time and the privileges of merchants, I

[071-168] used P. Olbricht.

[071-169] p. 238: For the popular rebellions of this time, I used a study in the

[071-170] _Bull. Acad. Sinica_, vol. 10, 1948, but also Meng Ssu-liang and others.

[071-171] p. 239: On the White Lotus Society (Pai-lien-hui) see note to previous

[071-172] page and an article by Hagiwara Jumpei.

[071-173] p. 240: H. Serruys, _The Mongols in China during the Hung-wu Period_,

[071-174] Bruges 1959, has studied in this book and in an article the fate of

[071-175] isolated Mongol groups in China after the breakdown of the dynasty.

[071-176] pp. 241-2: The travel report of Ch'ang-ch'un has been translated by A.

[071-177] Waley, _The Travels of an Alchemist_, London 1931.

[071-178] p. 242: _Hsi-hsiang-chi_ has been translated by S.I. Hsiung. _The

[071-179] Romance of the Western Chamber_, London 1935. All important analytic

[071-180] literature on drama and theatre is written by Chinese and Japanese

[071-181] authors, especially by Yoshikawa Kôjirô.--For Bon and early Lamaism, I

[071-182] used H. Hoffmann.

[071-183] p. 243: Lamaism in Mongolia disappeared later, however, and was

[071-184] reintroduced in the reformed form (Tsong-kha-pa, 1358-1419) in the

[071-185] sixteenth century. See R.J. Miller, _Monasteries and Culture Change in

[071-186] Inner Mongolia_, Wiesbaden 1959.

[071-187] p. 245: Much more research is necessary to clarify Japanese-Chinese

[071-188] relations in this period, especially to determine the size of trade.

[071-189] Good material is in the article by S. Iwao. Important is also S. Sakuma

[071-190] and an article in _Li-shih yen-chiu_ 1955, No. 3. For the loss of coins,

[071-191] I relied upon D. Brown.
