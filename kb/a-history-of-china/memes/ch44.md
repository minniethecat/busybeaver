# Section 44

[044-001] Men like Chou Tun-i (1017-1073) and Chang Tsai (1020-1077) developed a

[044-002] cosmological theory which could measure up with Buddhistic cosmology and

[044-003] metaphysics. But perhaps more important was the attempt of the

[044-004] Neo-Confucianists to explain the problem of evil. Confucius and his

[044-005] followers had believed that every person could perfect himself by

[044-006] overcoming the evil in him. As the good persons should be the _élite_

[044-007] and rule the others, theoretically everybody who was a member of human

[044-008] society, could move up and become a leader. It was commonly assumed that

[044-009] human nature is good or indifferent, and that human feelings are evil

[044-010] and have to be tamed and educated. When in Han time with the

[044-011] establishment of the gentry society and its social classes, the idea

[044-012] that any person could move up to become a leader if he only perfected

[044-013] himself, appeared to be too unrealistic, the theory of different grades

[044-014] of men was formed which found its clearest formulation by Han Yü: some

[044-015] people have a good, others a neutral, and still others a bad nature;

[044-016] therefore, not everybody can become a leader. The Neo-Confucianists,

[044-017] especially Ch'eng Hao (1032-1085) and Ch'eng I (1033-1107), tried to

[044-018] find the reasons for this inequality. According to them, nature is

[044-019] neutral; but physical form originates with the combination of nature

[044-020] with Material Force (_ch'i_). This combination produces individuals in

[044-021] which there is a lack of balance or harmony. Man should try to transform

[044-022] physical form and recover original nature. The creative force by which

[044-023] such a transformation is possible is _jen_, love, the creative,

[044-024] life-giving quality of nature itself.

[044-025] It should be remarked that Neo-Confucianism accepts an inequality of

[044-026] men, as early Confucianism did; and that _jen_, love, in its practical

[044-027] application has to be channelled by _li_, the system of rules of

[044-028] behaviour. The _li_, however, always started from the idea of a

[044-029] stratified class society. Chu Hsi (1130-1200), the famous scholar and

[044-030] systematizer of Neo-Confucian thoughts, brought out rules of behaviour

[044-031] for those burghers who did not belong to the gentry and could not,

[044-032] therefore, be expected to perform all _li_; his "simplified _li_"

[044-033] exercised a great influence not only upon contemporary China, but also

[044-034] upon Korea and Annam and there strengthened a hitherto looser

[044-035] patriarchal, patrilinear family system.

[044-036] The Neo-Confucianists also compiled great analytical works of history

[044-037] and encyclopaedias whose authority continued for many centuries. They

[044-038] interpreted in these works all history in accordance with their outlook;

[044-039] they issued new commentaries on all the classics in order to spread

[044-040] interpretations that served their purposes. In the field of commentary

[044-041] this school of thought was given perfect expression by Chu Hsi, who also

[044-042] wrote one of the chief historical works. Chu Hsi's commentaries became

[044-043] standard works for centuries, until the beginning of the twentieth

[044-044] century. Yet, although Chu became the symbol of conservatism, he was

[044-045] quite interested in science, and in this field he had an open eye for

[044-046] changes.

[044-047] The Sung period is so important, because it is also the time of the

[044-048] greatest development of Chinese science and technology. Many new

[044-049] theories, but also many practical, new inventions were made. Medicine

[044-050] made substantial progress. About 1145 the first autopsy was made, on the

[044-051] body of a South Chinese captive. In the field of agriculture, new

[044-052] varieties of rice were developed, new techniques applied, new plants

[044-053] introduced.

[044-054] The Wang An-shih school of political philosophy had opponents also in

[044-055] the field of literary style, the so-called Shu Group (Shu means the

[044-056] present province of Szechwan), whose leaders were the famous Three Sus.

[044-057] The greatest of the three was Su Tung-p'o (1036-1101); the others were

[044-058] his father, Su Shih, and his brother, Su Che. It is characteristic of

[044-059] these Shu poets, and also of the Kiangsi school associated with them,

[044-060] that they made as much use as they could of the vernacular. It had not

[044-061] been usual to introduce the phrases of everyday life into poetry, but Su

[044-062] Tung-p'o made use of the most everyday expressions, without diminishing

[044-063] his artistic effectiveness by so doing; on the contrary, the result was

[044-064] to give his poems much more genuine feeling than those of other poets.

[044-065] These poets were in harmony with the writings of the T'ang period poet

[044-066] Po Chü-i (772-846) and were supported, like Neo-Confucianism, by

[044-067] representatives of trade capitalism. Politically, in their conservatism

[044-068] they were sharply opposed to the Wang An-shih group. Midway between the

[044-069] two stood the so-called Loyang-School, whose greatest leaders were the

[044-070] historian and poet Ss[)u]-ma Kuang (1019-1086) and the philosopher-poet

[044-071] Shao Yung (1011-1077).

[044-072] In addition to its poems, the Sung literature was famous for the

[044-073] so-called _pi-chi_ or miscellaneous notes. These consist of short notes

[044-074] of the most various sort, notes on literature, art, politics,

[044-075] archaeology, all mixed together. The _pi-chi_ are a treasure-house for

[044-076] the history of the culture of the time; they contain many details, often

[044-077] of importance, about China's neighbouring peoples. They were intended to

[044-078] serve as suggestions for learned conversation when scholars came

[044-079] together; they aimed at showing how wide was a scholar's knowledge. To

[044-080] this group we must add the accounts of travel, of which some of great

[044-081] value dating from the Sung period are still extant; they contain

[044-082] information of the greatest importance about the early Mongols and also

[044-083] about Turkestan and South China.

[044-084] While the Sung period was one of perfection in all fields of art,

[044-085] painting undoubtedly gained its highest development in this time. We

[044-086] find now two main streams in painting: some painters preferred the

[044-087] decorative, pompous, but realistic approach, with great attention to the

[044-088] detail. Later theoreticians brought this school in connection with one

[044-089] school of meditative Buddhism, the so-called northern school. Men who

[044-090] belonged to this school of painting often were active court officials or

[044-091] painted for the court and for other representative purposes. One of the

[044-092] most famous among them, Li Lung-mien (ca. 1040-1106), for instance

[044-093] painted the different breeds of horses in the imperial stables. He was

[044-094] also famous for his Buddhistic figures. Another school, later called the

[044-095] southern school, regarded painting as an intimate, personal expression.

[044-096] They tried to paint inner realities and not outer forms. They, too, were

[044-097] educated, but they did not paint for anybody. They painted in their

[044-098] country houses when they felt in the mood for expression. Their

[044-099] paintings did not stress details, but tried to give the spirit of a

[044-100] landscape, for in this field they excelled most. Best known of them is

[044-101] Mi Fei (ca. 1051-1107), a painter as well as a calligrapher, art

[044-102] collector, and art critic. Typically, his paintings were not much liked

[044-103] by the emperor Hui Tsung (ruled 1101-1125) who was one of the greatest

[044-104] art collectors and whose catalogue of his collection became very famous.

[044-105] He created the Painting Academy, an institution which mainly gave

[044-106] official recognition to painters in form of titles which gave the

[044-107] painter access to and status at court. Ma Yüan (_c_. 1190-1224), member

[044-108] of a whole painter's family, and Hsia Kui (_c_. 1180-1230) continued the

[044-109] more "impressionistic" tradition. Already in Sung time, however, many

[044-110] painters could and did paint in different styles, "copying", i.e.

[044-111] painting in the way of T'ang painters, in order to express their

[044-112] changing emotions by changed styles, a fact which often makes the dating

[044-113] of Chinese paintings very difficult.

[044-114] Finally, art craft has left us famous porcelains of the Sung period. The

[044-115] most characteristic production of that time is the green porcelain known

[044-116] as "Celadon". It consists usually of a rather solid paste, less like

[044-117] porcelain than stoneware, covered with a green glaze; decoration is

[044-118] incised, not painted, under the glaze. In the Sung period, however, came

[044-119] the first pure white porcelain with incised ornamentation under the

[044-120] glaze, and also with painting on the glaze. Not until near the end of

[044-121] the Sung period did the blue and white porcelain begin (blue painting on

[044-122] a white ground). The cobalt needed for this came from Asia Minor. In

[044-123] exchange for the cobalt, Chinese porcelain went to Asia Minor. This

[044-124] trade did not, however, grow greatly until the Mongol epoch; later

[044-125] really substantial orders were placed in China, the Chinese executing

[044-126] the patterns wanted in the West.

[044-127] 5 _Military collapse_

[044-128] In foreign affairs the whole eleventh century was a period of diplomatic

[044-129] manoeuvring, with every possible effort to avoid war. There was

[044-130] long-continued fighting with the Kitan, and at times also with the

[044-131] Turco-Tibetan Hsia, but diplomacy carried the day: tribute was paid to

[044-132] both enemies, and the effort was made to stir up the Kitan against the

[044-133] Hsia and vice versa; the other parties also intrigued in like fashion.

[044-134] In 1110 the situation seemed to improve for the Sung in this game, as a

[044-135] new enemy appeared in the rear of the Liao (Kitan), the Tungusic Juchên

[044-136] (Jurchen), who in the past had been more or less subject to the Kitan.

[044-137] In 1114 the Juchên made themselves independent and became a political

[044-138] factor. The Kitan were crippled, and it became an easy matter to attack

[044-139] them. But this pleasant situation did not last long. The Juchên

[044-140] conquered Peking, and in 1125 the Kitan empire was destroyed; but in the

[044-141] same year the Juchên marched against the Sung. In 1126 they captured

[044-142] the Sung capital; the emperor and his art-loving father, who had retired

[044-143] a little earlier, were taken prisoner, and the Northern Sung dynasty was

[044-144] at an end.

[044-145] The collapse came so quickly because the whole edifice of security

[044-146] between the Kitan and the Sung was based on a policy of balance and of

[044-147] diplomacy. Neither state was armed in any way, and so both collapsed at

[044-148] the first assault from a military power.

[044-149] (2) The Liao (Kitan) dynasty in the north (937-1125)

[044-150] 1 _Social structure. Claim to the Chinese imperial throne_

[044-151] The Kitan, a league of tribes under the leadership of an apparently

[044-152] Mongol tribe, had grown steadily stronger in north-eastern Mongolia

[044-153] during the T'ang epoch. They had gained the allegiance of many tribes in

[044-154] the west and also in Korea and Manchuria, and in the end, about A.D.

[044-155] 900, had become the dominant power in the north. The process of growth

[044-156] of this nomad power was the same as that of other nomad states, such as

[044-157] the Toba state, and therefore need not be described again in any detail

[044-158] here. When the T'ang dynasty was deposed, the Kitan were among the

[044-159] claimants to the Chinese throne, feeling fully justified in their claim

[044-160] as the strongest power in the Far East. Owing to the strength of the

[044-161] Sha-t'o Turks, who themselves claimed leadership in China, the expansion

[044-162] of the Kitan empire slowed down. In the many battles the Kitan suffered

[044-163] several setbacks. They also had enemies in the rear, a state named

[044-164] Po-hai, ruled by Tunguses, in northern Korea, and the new Korean state

[044-165] of Kao-li, which liberated itself from Chinese overlordship in 919.

[044-166] In 927 the Kitan finally destroyed Po-hai. This brought many Tungus

[044-167] tribes, including the Jurchen (Juchên), under Kitan dominance. Then, in

[044-168] 936, the Kitan gained the allegiance of the Turkish general Shih

[044-169] Ching-t'ang, and he was set on the Chinese throne as a feudatory of the

[044-170] Kitan. It was hoped now to secure dominance over China, and accordingly

[044-171] the Mongol name of the dynasty was altered to "Liao dynasty" in 937,

[044-172] indicating the claim to the Chinese throne. Considerable regions of

[044-173] North China came at once under the direct rule of the Liao. As a whole,

[044-174] however, the plan failed: the feudatory Shih Ching-t'ang tried to make

[044-175] himself independent; Chinese fought the Liao; and the Chinese sceptre

[044-176] soon came back into the hands of a Sha-t'o dynasty (947). This ended the

[044-177] plans of the Liao to conquer the whole of China.

[044-178] For this there were several reasons. A nomad people was again ruling

[044-179] the agrarian regions of North China. This time the representatives of

[044-180] the ruling class remained military commanders, and at the same time

[044-181] retained their herds of horses. As early as 1100 they had well over

[044-182] 10,000 herds, each of more than a thousand animals. The army commanders

[044-183] had been awarded large regions which they themselves had conquered. They

[044-184] collected the taxes in these regions, and passed on to the state only

[044-185] the yield of the wine tax. On the other hand, in order to feed the

[044-186] armies, in which there were now many Chinese soldiers, the frontier

[044-187] regions were settled, the soldiers working as peasants in times of

[044-188] peace, and peasants being required to contribute to the support of the

[044-189] army. Both processes increased the interest of the Kitan ruling class in

[044-190] the maintenance of peace. That class was growing rich, and preferred

[044-191] living on the income from its properties or settlements to going to war,

[044-192] which had become a more and more serious matter after the founding of

[044-193] the great Sung empire, and was bound to be less remunerative. The herds

[044-194] of horses were a further excellent source of income, for they could be

[044-195] sold to the Sung, who had no horses. Then, from 1004 onward, came the

[044-196] tribute payments from China, strengthening the interest in the

[044-197] maintenance of peace. Thus great wealth accumulated in Peking, the

[044-198] capital of the Liao; in this wealth the whole Kitan ruling class

[044-199] participated, but the tribes in the north, owing to their remoteness,

[044-200] had no share in it. In 988 the Chinese began negotiations, as a move in

[044-201] their diplomacy, with the ruler of the later realm of the Hsia; in 990

[044-202] the Kitan also negotiated with him, and they soon became a third partner

[044-203] in the diplomatic game. Delegations were continually going from one to

[044-204] another of the three realms, and they were joined by trade missions.

[044-205] Agreement was soon reached on frontier questions, on armament, on

[044-206] questions of demobilization, on the demilitarization of particular

[044-207] regions, and so on, for the last thing anyone wanted was to fight.
