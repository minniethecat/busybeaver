## Overview

[[cheng-i|Ch'eng I]] (1033–1107) was, together with his elder brother [[cheng-hao|Ch'eng Hao]] (1032–1085), one of the most influential philosophers of the Northern [[sung-dynasty|Sung dynasty]]. The two brothers refined and systematized the cosmological insights of their predecessors [[chou-tun-i|Chou Tun-i]] and [[chang-tsai|Chang Tsai]], establishing the concept of *li* (principle or pattern) as the central category of Neo-Confucian thought.

> Especially Ch'eng Hao (1032-1085) and Ch'eng I (1033-1107), tried to explain human nature in a much more elaborate and metaphysical way than the earlier Confucianists had done. [044-017]

## The Concept of Li

Where [[chang-tsai|Chang Tsai]] had emphasized *ch'i* (vital energy) as the fundamental substance of reality, the Ch'eng brothers shifted the emphasis to *li* — the rational principle or pattern that structures both the natural world and human morality. For Ch'eng I, *li* is universal and unchanging: there is a *li* for being a ruler, a *li* for being a parent, a *li* for every relationship and every thing. To understand *li* is to understand the normative order of the cosmos.

This dual emphasis on *li* (principle) and *ch'i* (material force) became the defining framework of Neo-Confucian metaphysics. The Ch'eng brothers' articulation of *li* was later adopted and expanded by [[chu-hsi|Chu Hsi]] (1130–1200), who made it the cornerstone of the orthodox Neo-Confucian synthesis that would dominate Chinese intellectual life for the next seven centuries.

Ch'eng I is particularly associated with the school of *li* (the "School of Principle," *li-hsüeh*), in contrast to his brother [[cheng-hao|Ch'eng Hao]]'s more idealistic emphasis on *hsin* (mind). This divergence between the two brothers foreshadowed the later split between the rationalist tradition of [[chu-hsi|Chu Hsi]] and the idealist tradition of [[wang-yang-ming|Wang Yang-ming]].

## Historical Context

The Ch'eng brothers lived in an era of intense political factionalism. The reformist policies of [[wang-an-shih|Wang An-shih]] had divided the Sung elite, and the brothers' philosophical circle was closely associated with the conservative opposition led by Ssu-ma Kuang and [[ou-yang-hsiu|Ou-yang Hsiu]]. Ch'eng I himself served briefly as a tutor to the young Emperor Che-tsung but was dismissed when the reform faction returned to power.
