## Overview

The Yangtze River (Ch'ang Chiang, the "Long River") is the central artery of Chinese civilization south of the [[yellow-river|Yellow River]]. Flowing from the mountains of Tibet eastward to the East China Sea, the Yangtze and its vast fertile basin have been, for more than a millennium, the demographic and economic powerhouse of China.

Eberhard identifies the Yangtze region as the pivot around which successive dynasties turned. Without its grain, its trade networks, and its dense populations, the imperial capitals of the north, from [[peking]] to [[nanking]], could not have been sustained.

The river's historical importance crystallized during the third century CE, when the Chin dynasty's flight southward transformed the lower Yangtze from a sparsely settled frontier into the cultivated heartland of the southern state.

By the Sung period the Yangtze valley had become the industrial and commercial centre of China, a position it retained through the Ming, Ch'ing, and into the modern era.

The crossing of the Yangtze has repeatedly marked inflection points in Chinese history: by the Chin (Juchen) in 1130, by Ming loyalists against the Manchus, by British warships in 1842, and by the Northern Expedition in 1926.

## Geography

The Yangtze rises in the highlands of Tibet and traverses the full breadth of central China, passing through the provinces of Szechwan, [[hsiung-nu|Hu]]pei, [[hsiung-nu|Hu]]nan, Kiangsi, Anhwei, and Kiangsu before emptying into the East China Sea at the great Yangtze delta. Its length, approximately 6,300 kilometres, makes it the longest river in Asia.

Eberhard describes the Yangtze lowlands as a region of extraordinary natural abundance. The landscape is "cut up by hundreds of water-courses and canals" [033-048], creating a natural transport network long before the construction of engineered canals.

This geography of interconnected streams and lakes made the Yangtze basin both highly productive for rice agriculture and easily traversable for trade.

The river naturally divides into three geographical zones, each with distinct historical roles:

- **The Upper Yangtze (Szechwan basin)**: A fertile, mountainous region surrounded by highlands. Its remoteness made it difficult for imperial governments to control; it repeatedly served as a base for regional warlords and as a haven for dynasties in retreat. The upper Yangtze was "the basis of [[hsiung-nu|Hu]]an Wen's power" [032-068].

- **The Middle Yangtze ([[hsiung-nu|Hu]]pei-[[hsiung-nu|Hu]]nan plain)**: Centred on the triple-city of Wuhan, this region sits at the crossroads of trade routes from Szechwan, Kweichow, [[hsiung-nu|Hu]]nan, and Kiangsi. Eberhard notes that "normally the traffic from those provinces comes down the Yangtze" [032-016], making it the launching point for the Wuchang Uprising of 1911.

- **The Lower Yangtze (Kiangsu-Chekiang delta)**: Drained by the Yangtze delta and centred on [[nanking]] and Shanghai, this was the most densely populated and economically advanced region. By the Ming period it had become the economic centre of the empire, a position it has never relinquished.

## Historical Significance

The Yangtze's place in Chinese history is defined by its role as both a frontier and a core -- the line dividing north from south in times of division, and the economic engine that sustained imperial unity in times of consolidation.

### The Southward Shift

Until the third century CE, the Yangtze basin had been of secondary importance compared with the Yellow River heartland of the north. As Eberhard explains:

> "Until the third century, the south had been of no great economic importance, in spite of the good climate and the extraordinary fertility of the Yangtze valley. The country had been too thinly settled, and the indigenous population had not become adapted to organized trade. After the move southward of the Chin dynasty the many immigrants had made the country of the lower Yangtze more thickly populated, but not over-populated." [031-106, 031-109]

This mass migration transformed the Yangtze region. The Chin court's presence created demand; prices rose, stimulating local rice production. The estates of the southern gentry yielded more than the small northern properties ever had. From this point forward, the Yangtze basin was never again marginal to Chinese political and economic calculations.

### Dynasty and Division

The Yangtze served repeatedly as the boundary between rival northern and southern states. During the period of division following the Han, the regions along the upper Yangtze were political battlegrounds where native unrest and Toba expansionism weakened the southern regimes [033-048, 033-054].

The Ch'en dynasty of the sixth century saw its "territory confined to the lower Yangtze valley" [033-105], a prelude to the Sui reunification of 589.

In the twelfth century the Yangtze was crossed by the invading Chin (Juchen) forces:

> "In 1130 the Yangtze was crossed. But the Chin rulers were not yet strong enough to administer the whole of the vast territory they had conquered; and the hot, humid climate of central and southern China was unbearable to them. A Chinese puppet government was therefore established south of the Yangtze." [046-042-043]

This pattern -- northern military power stalled at the Yangtze, southern regimes persisting behind its watery barrier -- recurred through Chinese history. The river was a moat that protected the south, but also a line that, once crossed, signalled the collapse of southern resistance.

### Imperial Canal and Integration

A decisive moment in the Yangtze's integration into a unified imperial economy came with the Sui dynasty's construction of the "Imperial Canal" (the Grand Canal):

> "It was at this time that the first forerunner of the famous 'Imperial Canal' was constructed, the canal that connects the Yangtze with the Yellow River." [034-106]

This engineering feat permanently bound the economic output of the Yangtze basin to the political centres of the north. Henceforth, [[peking|Peking]] would be fed by Yangtze grain; northern armies would be provisioned by southern harvests.

The canal rendered China's political geography legible: political power lay in the north, economic power lay in the Yangtze basin, and the canal was the ligament between them.

### Modern Conflicts

In the nineteenth and twentieth centuries, the Yangtze estuary became a theatre of conflict with Western powers. During the Opium War, "the British entered the Yangtze estuary and threatened Nanking" [057-181], forcing the humiliating [[opium-war|Treaty of Nanking]] in 1842, which opened the treaty ports and inaugurated the era of the Unequal Treaties.

The treaty terms included British rights of "navigation along the Yangtze" [058-034], a privilege that symbolized the collapse of Chinese sovereignty over its own waterways.

In the revolutionary era, the Yangtze was the axis of the Northern Expedition: "The Yangtze was reached, and in 1926 the southern government established itself at Hankow" [063-124]. By the early twentieth century, "the great bulk of China's young industries lay in the Yangtze" region [063-158], making control of the river essential to any claimant to national power.

## Economic Role

The Yangtze basin has been China's economic centre of gravity since at least the Sung dynasty. Eberhard identifies it as the driver of the profound economic transformations that reshaped Chinese society between the tenth and nineteenth centuries.

Population density in the Yangtze plains "had further increased since the Sung time" [050-050]. The agricultural revolution of the Ming period -- intensification of rice cultivation, expansion of cotton production, and new techniques of mountain farming -- was concentrated in the Yangtze region.

The raw cotton of the north was transported "into the industrial centre of the time, the Yangtze valley" [050-107], where it was processed and re-exported as cloth.

Urbanization proceeded rapidly. Nanking, the Ming capital, "grew tremendously because of the presence of the court and administration," and even after the capital was moved to [[peking|Peking]], Nanking "continued to remain the cultural capital of China" [050-113n]. Shanghai emerged as a centre of cotton cloth production.

A system of middlemen, factory owners, and seasonal labourers -- "small farmers from Chekiang who came to the Yangtze area after they had finished their own harvest" [050-131] -- constituted an increasingly sophisticated commercial economy.

> "Consumption in the Yangtze cities raised the value of the land around the cities. The small farmers who were squeezed out, migrated to the south. Absentee landlords in cities relied partly on migratory, seasonal labour supplied by small farmers from Chekiang who came to the Yangtze area after they had finished their own harvest." [050-128, 050-131]

Even after political power shifted northward with the Ming transfer of the capital to [[peking|Peking]], the Yangtze region's economic primacy was unchallenged. "The Yangtze region remained the economic centre of the country" [052-004]. The gentry of the Yangtze region, enriched by commerce and land rents, formed a political constituency whose interests any government ignored at its peril [052-079].

## Key Cities and Regions

The Yangtze corridor is defined by a constellation of cities, each shaped by its position along the river:

| City / Region | Position on the Yangtze | Historical Role |
|--------------|------------------------|-----------------|
| **Nanking** (Nanjing) | Lower Yangtze, Kiangsu | Southern capital; Ming founder's seat; site of the 1842 treaty that ended the Opium War |
| **Wuhan** (Hankow-Hanyang-Wuchang) | Middle Yangtze, [[hsiung-nu|Hu]]pei | Commercial crossroads; site of the 1911 Wuchang Uprising that launched the [[republican-china|Republican Revolution]] [060-176] |
| **Shanghai** | Yangtze delta, Kiangsu | Treaty port turned industrial metropolis; centre of cotton and silk production |
| **Ch'ung-k'ing** (Chongqing) | Upper Yangtze, Szechwan | Mountain fortress; wartime capital during the Sino-Japanese War |
| **Yangchow** | Lower Yangtze, Kiangsu | Strategic city controlling the Grand Canal junction; devastated during the [[an-lu-shan-rebellion]] |
| **Hangchow** (Hangzhou) | Yangtze delta, Chekiang | Southern Sung capital; commercial and cultural centre of the delta region |

Nanking holds a special position in the Yangtze system. As Eberhard explains, the economic unity of the middle and lower Yangtze meant "Hankow might just as well have been the capital as Nanking" [032-016-017] -- both cities draw their strength from the same river-borne trade.

The political rivalry between Nanking and [[peking]] is, at bottom, a contest between the economic logic of the Yangtze basin and the strategic logic of the northern frontier.

## Cultural Importance

The Yangtze region has been a crucible of Chinese cultural innovation for centuries. Its commercial wealth patronized literature, art, and religion on a scale unmatched elsewhere in China.

### Commerce and Character

The economic vitality of the Yangtze region shaped its cultural identity. Eberhard records a contemporary observation about the lower Yangtze people: "they were so much interested in business that they paid no attention to agriculture" [038-155].

This commercial ethos, lamented by Confucian moralists, powered the urban culture that distinguished Yangtze cities from the agrarian conservatism of the north.

### Printing and the Arts

The Yangtze valley was the birthplace of one of China's most significant technological and cultural achievements -- colour block printing:

> "Block printing in colours, developed in the Yangtze valley, was adopted by Japan and found its highest refinement there." [051-133]

This innovation democratized access to visual art, allowing the urban middle classes of Ming cities to own reproductions of master paintings. The Yangtze's printing workshops produced not only coloured prints but also books, pamphlets, and religious texts that circulated throughout the empire.

### Schools and Scholarship

The intellectual life of the Yangtze region was sustained by its gentry class, whose wealth from land and commerce supported a network of academies and literary societies. The Southern School (Lin-ch'uan school), based in the "Yangtze area" [053-069], was one of the major schools of Ch'ing scholarship.

The density of educated gentry in the Yangtze region was unmatched. Eberhard notes that "almost the whole of the gentry in the Yangtze region" participated in the [[examination-system|examination system]] [052-079], making it the largest recruiting ground for the imperial bureaucracy.

### Resistance and Identity

The Yangtze region's cultural identity was also expressed in resistance to foreign rule. During the Ming-Ch'ing transition in the seventeenth century, the Chinese gentry of the Yangtze region mounted organized resistance against the Manchu invaders [054-190].

The Manchu occupation of "the rich Yangtze" territories [055-195] was the final blow to Ming loyalist hopes. Yet the region's tradition of political autonomy persisted into the modern era, feeding the revolutionary currents that would sweep China in the twentieth century.
