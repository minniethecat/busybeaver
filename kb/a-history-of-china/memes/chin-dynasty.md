## Overview

The Chin dynasty (265–419) was an imperial
dynasty founded by Ssu-ma Yen (Emperor Wu
Ti), who seized power by usurping the Wei
dynasty (023-048).
The dynasty comprised two periods: the
Western Chin (265–317) with its capital at
Loyang, and the Eastern Chin (317–419)
whose capital near Nanking became a centre
of refined civilization (031-044).

The Western Chin briefly reunified China
by conquering the southern state of Wu in
280, ending the [[three-kingdoms]] era and
restoring imperial unity (023-109).
But internal weakness — driven by court
intrigue, a failed disarmament, and the
growing power of the [[hsiung-nu]] on the
frontier — proved fatal (023-140).

> The change of dynasty in the state of Wei
did not bring any turn in China's internal
history. Ssu-ma Yen, who as emperor was
called Wu Ti (265–289), had come to the
throne with the aid of his clique and his
extraordinarily large family. (023-079)

In 311 the Hsiung-nu under [[liu-yuan]]'s
successor captured Loyang, taking Emperor
Huai Ti prisoner (025-015).
A Chin prince, [[yuan-ti]], fled south and
in 317 proclaimed himself the first emperor
of the Eastern Chin, from a capital beside
present-day Nanking (031-042).

## Founding and Rise

The Ssu-ma family rose within the Wei
dynasty. Ssu-ma I controlled the government
by the end of Wen Ti's reign, and the
family's power grew steadily (023-038).
In 264 a Ssu-ma declared himself king;
in 265 his son Ssu-ma Yen staged a formal
abdication of the Wei throne and became the
first Chin ruler, taking the name Wu Ti
[023-047, 023-050].

Despite constant intrigue among the new
imperial princes, the Chin army conquered
Wu in 280 and reunited the empire
[023-086, 023-109].
Each prince received a large territory to
administer and maintained his own army,
independent of the central government
(023-094).

> After the destruction of Wu there remained
no external enemy that represented a
potential danger, so that a general
disarmament was decreed (280) in order to
restore a healthy economic situation.
(023-112)

## Government and Administration

The Western Chin government was shaped by
the Ssu-ma family's need to reward allies.
Wu Ti appointed family members and allied
gentry to high office, continuing the
patterns of intrigue of the Wei court
(023-082).

A distinctive feature was the empowerment
of imperial princes:

- Princes received large regions to
administer as their fiscal domains
- Each prince maintained an independent
army beyond central control
- Control officials in princely courts
proved ineffective at enforcement
- Princes made other governors dependent
upon them, undermining the centre
[023-088–023-096]

The abortive disarmament of 280 was the
most consequential Western Chin policy.
Troops were disbanded and weapons ordered
surrendered, so the metal could be minted
into scarce coin [023-113, 023-120].
But discharged soldiers sold their weapons
to the Hsiung-nu and Hsien-pi instead,
further arming the frontier peoples while
the central government lost military power
[023-127, 023-146].

## Decline and Fall

The Western Chin unravelled after the death
of Wu Ti. Imperial princes fought each
other continually, and the dynasty offered
no effective resistance to external threats
(025-022).
In 311, Huns under Liu Ts'ung captured
Loyang. Emperor Huai Ti was taken prisoner
and executed two years later
[025-017–025-019].

A desperate court clique installed a new
emperor, Min Ti, at Ch'ang-an. He received
no reinforcements and surrendered in 316,
bringing the Western Chin to its end
[025-021, 025-028].

> Its princes and generals and many of its
gentry became landless and homeless and had
to flee into the south. (025-028)

## Eastern Chin (317–419)

The Eastern Chin was founded by Yüan Ti,
a Chin prince who established a new court
south of the Yangtze (031-044).
Northern gentry fled south with him — not
out of loyalty but because they had little
prospect of influence under alien rulers
and feared the nomads would turn farmland
into pasturage [031-046–031-052].

The southern court was a scene of luxury,
poetry, music, and endless intrigue.
The highest refinement of Far Eastern
civilization between the fourth and sixth
centuries was found in South China, but
the accompaniments of this over-refinement
were terrible [031-189–031-192].

No Eastern Chin ruler after Yüan Ti had
political qualities of any importance
(032-004).
The dynasty suffered repeated rebellions:
Wang Tun rose from the Hankow region in the
320s, and later the Huan family dominated
the court through military power
[032-023, 032-043].
Huan Wen led northern campaigns against the
Earlier Ch'in state but secured no lasting
gains [032-048, 032-062].

The dynasty endured just over a century
before giving way to the Liu-Sung in 420.
Immigration from the north spurred
economic development of the Yangtze valley,
bringing more land into cultivation and
stimulating rice production and trade
[031-078, 031-113].
