## Overview

**Kung-sun Lung** (fl. late 4th–early 3rd century BC) was a prominent philosopher of the School of Names (Ming-chia), also known as the Dialecticians or Logicians, during the [[Warring States period]]. His work focused on the relationship between names (ming) and actualities (shih), and he is best known for paradoxes such as "a white horse is not a horse" (pai-ma fei ma).

> "Here are a number of names to mention: the most important are Kung-sun Lung and Hui Tz[)u], who are comparable with the ancient Greek dialecticians and Sophists." [012-064–012-065]

## Historical Context

The School of Names emerged during the fifth century BC alongside the "scholars" (ju) who journeyed from one princely court to another, each promoting their own methods of governance and philosophy. In this competitive intellectual environment, the ability to win debates became a practical skill, and logicians developed increasingly sophisticated argumentation techniques.

> "They saw their main task in the development of logic. Since, as we have mentioned, many 'scholars' journeyed from one princely court to another, and other people came forward, each recommending his own method to the princes." [012-066–012-068]

The comparison to Greek dialecticians and Sophists is apt: both traditions emerged in periods of political fragmentation, both questioned conventional understandings of language and reality, and both were sometimes criticized for privileging verbal cleverness over substantive truth.

## Philosophy

Kung-sun Lung's most famous argument — that "a white horse is not a horse" — is not mere sophistry. It rests on a distinction between:
- The concept "horse" (ma), which refers to the form or essence of horseness
- The concept "white horse" (pai-ma), which combines the form of horseness with the form of whiteness

By this reasoning, "white horse" and "horse" are different objects of reference, since one includes a color determination that the other does not. This anticipated later philosophical discussions about universals versus particulars and the relationship between language and reality.

The "Kung-sun Lung-tzu" (Book of Kung-sun Lung), a short text of six chapters attributed to him, survives as one of the few primary sources for the School of Names.

## Legacy

Kung-sun Lung appears in historical indices of major Chinese philosophers:

> "Kung-sun Lung, Shang Tz[)u], Han Fei Tzu" [074-066]

> "Kung-sun Lung, philosopher" [073-544]

His work on the philosophy of language and logic had limited direct influence during the Han dynasty, when [[confucianism|Confucianism]] became dominant and speculative logic was discouraged. However, modern scholars have re-evaluated the School of Names as representing an important analytical tradition in Chinese thought that paralleled developments in Greek and Indian philosophy.

A modern translation and study of Kung-sun Lung's work exists:

> "Ma Perleberg of _The Works of Kung-sun Lung_" [069-044]
