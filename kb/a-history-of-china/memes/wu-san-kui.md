## Overview

[[wu-san-kui|Wu San-kui]] occupies one of the most contested places in Chinese history — a Ming general whose fateful decision to ally with the invading Manchus in 1644 opened the gates of China to alien rule, and who later attempted to reverse that outcome by declaring himself emperor in a doomed rebellion. His career traces the entire arc of the Ming-Qing transition: from collaborator, to conqueror on behalf of the new dynasty, to rebel against the power he had helped install.

## The Alliance of 1644

In the chaotic spring of 1644, the rebel leader Li Tz[)u]-ch'eng had captured [[peking|Peking]] and brought down the Ming dynasty. Wu San-kui, commanding Ming forces on the northern frontier, made a consequential choice:

> Wu San-kui negotiated with the Manchu Prince Dorgon, formed an alliance with the Manchus, and with them entered Peking on June 6th, 1644. Li Tz[)u]-ch'eng quickly looted the city, burned down whatever he could, and fled into the west, continually pursued by Wu San-kui. In the end he was abandoned by all his supporters and killed by peasants. [054-120]

The Manchus, however, had no intention of leaving Wu San-kui as an independent power. They "established themselves in [[peking|Peking]], and Wu became their general" [054-125] — an instrument of the new Manchu dynasty rather than its partner.

## Conquest and Service

For the next two decades, Wu San-kui served the Manchus as their most effective military commander in the south and west. He conquered western China for the new regime [054-178], and his task was to eliminate the remaining Ming loyalists — the pretenders who still claimed the imperial title:

> He therefore handed over to the Manchus the last Ming prince, whom the Burmese had delivered up to him in 1661. [054-184]

This act — delivering the final Ming heir to his Manchu overlords — foreclosed any possibility of reconciliation with Ming loyalists and sealed Wu's reputation as a traitor to the dynasty he had once served.

## The Rebellion of 1673

By the early 1670s, Wu San-kui's position had become untenable. Stationed in the remote south-west, he saw that the Manchus intended to centralize power and eliminate the semi-autonomous feudatories of which he was the most powerful:

> Wu San-kui's only possible allies against the Manchus were the gentry. But in the west, where he was in power, the gentry counted for nothing; they had in any case been weaker in the west, and they had been decimated by the insurrection of Chang Hsien-chung. Thus Wu San-kui was compelled to try to push eastwards, in order to unite with the gentry of the Yangtze region against the Manchus. [054-185]

The Manchus anticipated his strategy. In 1673, after every effort at accommodation had failed, open war broke out. Wu San-kui declared himself emperor [054-192], and the Manchus marched against him.

## Isolation and Defeat

Wu's rebellion foundered on the unwillingness of the [[yangtze-river|Yangtze]] gentry to support him. They had already "come to terms with the Manchus, and they gave Wu San-kui no help" [054-194]. Without gentry backing, Wu was trapped in the impoverished south-west:

> He vegetated in the south-west, a region too poor to maintain an army that could conquer all China, and too small to enable him to last indefinitely as an independent power. He was able to hold his own until his death, although, with the loss of the support of the gentry, he had no prospect of final success. [054-195]

Wu San-kui died in 1678, still holding out. His grandson Wu Shih-fan was defeated in 1681, marking the final extinction of the revolt.

## Legacy

The end of Wu San-kui's rebellion carried a significance that transcended the fate of one general:

> The end of the rule of Wu San-kui and his successor marked the end of the national governments of China; the whole country was now under alien domination, for the simple reason that all the opponents of the Manchus had failed. [054-200]

Wu San-kui's career — from collaborator to rebel — illustrates the impossible position of Chinese military commanders in the Ming-Qing transition. His alliance with the Manchus had been a tactical decision born of the crisis of 1644; his rebellion was a belated attempt to undo the consequences of that decision. Both failed, and in failing, they ensured that the Manchu conquest would be complete.
