# Section 55

[055-001] In the first phase of the Manchu conquest the gentry had refused to

[055-002] support either the Ming princes or Wu San-kui, or any of the rebels, or

[055-003] the Manchus themselves. A second phase began about twenty years after

[055-004] the capture of Peking, when the Manchus won over the gentry by desisting

[055-005] from any interference with the ownership of land, and by the use of

[055-006] Manchu troops to clear away the "rebels" who were hostile to the gentry.

[055-007] A reputable government was then set up in Peking, free from eunuchs and

[055-008] from all the old cliques; in their place the government looked for

[055-009] Chinese scholars for its administrative posts. Literati and scholars

[055-010] streamed into Peking, especially members of the "Academies" that still

[055-011] existed in secret, men who had been the chief sufferers from the

[055-012] conditions at the end of the Ming epoch. The young emperor Sheng Tsu

[055-013] (1663-1722; K'ang-hsi is the name by which his rule was known, not his

[055-014] name) was keenly interested in Chinese culture and gave privileged

[055-015] treatment to the scholars of the gentry who came forward. A rapid

[055-016] recovery quite clearly took place. The disturbances of the years that

[055-017] had passed had got rid of the worst enemies of the people, the

[055-018] formidable rival cliques and the individuals lusting for power; the

[055-019] gentry had become more cautious in their behaviour to the peasants; and

[055-020] bribery had been largely stamped out. Finally, the empire had been

[055-021] greatly expanded. All these things helped to stabilize the regime of the

[055-022] Manchus.

[055-023] 2 _Decline in the eighteenth century_

[055-024] The improvement continued until the middle of the eighteenth century.

[055-025] About the time of the French Revolution there began a continuous

[055-026] decline, slow at first and then gathering speed. The European works on

[055-027] China offer various reasons for this: the many foreign wars (to which we

[055-028] shall refer later) of the emperor, known by the name of his ruling

[055-029] period, Ch'ien-lung, his craze for building, and the irruption of the

[055-030] Europeans into Chinese trade. In the eighteenth century the court

[055-031] surrounded itself with great splendour, and countless palaces and other

[055-032] luxurious buildings were erected, but it must be borne in mind that so

[055-033] great an empire as the China of that day possessed very considerable

[055-034] financial strength, and could support this luxury. The wars were

[055-035] certainly not inexpensive, as they took place along the Russian

[055-036] frontier and entailed expenditure on the transport of reinforcements and

[055-037] supplies; the wars against Turkestan and Tibet were carried on with

[055-038] relatively small forces. This expenditure should not have been beyond

[055-039] the resources of an ordered budget. Interestingly enough, the period

[055-040] between 1640 and 1840 belongs to those periods for which almost no

[055-041] significant work in the field of internal social and economic

[055-042] developments has been made; Western scholars have been too much

[055-043] interested in the impact of Western economy and culture or in the

[055-044] military events. Chinese scholars thus far have shown a prejudice

[055-045] against the Manchu dynasty and were mainly interested in the study of

[055-046] anti-Manchu movements and the downfall of the dynasty. On the other

[055-047] hand, the documentary material for this period is extremely extensive,

[055-048] and many years of work are necessary to reach any general conclusions

[055-049] even in one single field. The following remarks should, therefore, be

[055-050] taken as very tentative and preliminary, and they are, naturally,

[055-051] fragmentary.

[055-058] The decline of the Manchu dynasty began at a time when the European

[055-059] trade was still insignificant, and not as late as after 1842, when China

[055-060] had to submit to the foreign Capitulations. These cannot have been the

[055-061] true cause of the decline. Above all, the decline was not so noticeable

[055-062] in the state of the Exchequer as in a general impoverishment of China.

[055-063] The number of really wealthy persons among the gentry diminished, but

[055-064] the middle class, that is to say the people who had education but little

[055-065] or no money and property, grew steadily in number.

[055-066] One of the deeper reasons for the decline of the Manchu dynasty seems to

[055-067] lie in the enormous increase in the population. Here are a few Chinese

[055-068] statistics:

[055-082] It may be objected that these figures are incorrect and exaggerated.

[055-083] Undoubtedly they contain errors. But the first figure (for 1578) of some

[055-084] sixty millions is in close agreement with all other figures of early

[055-085] times; the figure for 1850 seems high, but cannot be far wrong, for even

[055-086] after the great T'ai P'ing Rebellion of 1851, which, together with its

[055-087] after-effects, costs the lives of countless millions, all statisticians

[055-088] of today estimate the population of China at more than four hundred

[055-089] millions. If we enter these data together with the census of 1953 into a

[055-090] chart (see p. 273), a fairly smooth curve emerges; the special features

[055-091] are that already under the Ming the population was increasing and,

[055-092] secondly, that the high rate of increase in the population began with

[055-093] the long period of internal peace since about 1700. From that time

[055-094] onwards, all China's wars were fought at so great a distance from China

[055-095] proper that the population was not directly affected. Moreover, in the

[055-096] seventeenth and eighteenth centuries the Manchus saw to the maintenance

[055-097] of the river dykes, so that the worst inundations were prevented. Thus

[055-098] there were not so many of the floods which had often cost the lives of

[055-099] many million people in China; and there were no internal wars, with

[055-100] their heavy cost in lives.

[055-102] the needed proportion. I have, unfortunately, no statistics for all

[055-103] periods; but the general tendency is shown by the following table:

[055-111] Six _mou_ are about one acre. In 1578, there were 66 _mou_ land per

[055-112] family of the total population. This was close to the figures regarded

[055-113] as ideal by Chinese early economists for the producing family (100

[055-114] _mou_) considering the fact that about 80 per cent of all families at

[055-115] that time were producers. By 1729 it was only 35 _mou_ per family, i.e.

[055-116] the land had to produce almost twice as much as before. We have shown

[055-117] that the agricultural developments in the Ming time greatly increased

[055-118] the productivity of the land. This then, obviously resulted in an

[055-119] increase of population. But by the middle of the eighteenth century,

[055-120] assuming that production doubled since the sixteenth century, population

[055-121] pressure was again as heavy as it had been then. And after _c_. 1750,

[055-122] population pressure continued to build up to the present time.

[055-123] Internal colonization continued during the Manchu time; there was a

[055-124] continuous, but slow flow of people into Kwangsi, Kweichow, Yünnan. In

[055-125] spite of laws which prohibited emigration, Chinese also moved into

[055-126] South-East Asia. Chinese settlement in Manchuria was allowed only in the

[055-127] last years of the Manchus. But such internal colonization or emigration

[055-128] could alleviated the pressure only in some areas, while it continued to

[055-129] build up in others.

[055-130] In Europe as well as in Japan, we find a strong population increase; in

[055-131] Europe at almost the same time as in China. But before population

[055-132] pressure became too serious in Europe or Japan, industry developed and

[055-133] absorbed the excess population. Thus, farms did not decrease too much in

[055-134] size. Too small farms are always and in many ways uneconomical. With the

[055-135] development of industries, the percentage of farm population decreased.

[055-136] In China, however, the farm population was still as high as 73.3 per

[055-137] cent of the total population in 1932 and the percentage rose to 81 per

[055-138] cent in 1950.

[055-139] From the middle of the seventeenth century on, commercial activities,

[055-140] especially along the coast, continued to increase and we find gentry

[055-141] families who equip sons who were unwilling or not capable to study and

[055-142] to enter the ranks of the officials, but who were too unruly to sit in

[055-143] villages and collect the rent from the tenants of the family, with money

[055-144] to enter business. The newly settled areas of Kwangtung and Kwangsi were

[055-145] ideal places for them: here they could sell Chinese products to the

[055-146] native tribes or to the new settlers at high prices. Some of these men

[055-147] introduced new techniques from the old provinces of China into the

[055-148] "colonial" areas and set up dye factories, textile factories, etc., in

[055-149] the new towns of the south. But the greatest stimulus for these

[055-150] commercial activities was foreign, European trade. American silver which

[055-151] had flooded Europe in the sixteenth century, began to flow into China

[055-152] from the beginning of the seventeenth century on. The influx was stopped

[055-153] not until between 1661 and 1684 when the government again prohibited

[055-154] coastal shipping and removed coastal settlements into the interior in

[055-155] order to stop piracy along the coasts of Fukien and independence

[055-156] movements on Formosa. But even during these twenty-three years, the

[055-157] price of silver was so low that home production was given up because it

[055-158] did not pay off. In the eighteenth century, silver again continued to

[055-159] enter China, while silk and tea were exported. This demand led to a

[055-160] strong rise in the prices of silk and tea, and benefited the merchants.

[055-161] When, from the late eighteenth century on, opium began to be imported,

[055-162] the silver left China again. The merchants profited this time from the

[055-163] opium trade, but farmers had to suffer: the price of silver went up, and

[055-164] taxes had to be paid in silver, while farm products were sold for

[055-165] copper. By 1835, the ounce of silver had a value of 2,000 copper coins

[055-166] instead of one thousand before 1800. High gains in commerce prevented

[055-167] investment in industries, because they would give lower and later

[055-168] profits than commerce. From the nineteenth century on, more and more

[055-169] industrial goods were offered by importers which also prevented

[055-170] industrialization. Finally, the gentry basically remained

[055-171] anti-industrial and anti-business. They tried to operate necessary

[055-172] enterprises such as mining, melting, porcelain production as far as

[055-173] possible as government establishments; but as the operators were

[055-174] officials, they were not too business-minded and these enterprises did

[055-175] not develop well. The businessmen certainly had enough capital, but they

[055-176] invested it in land instead of investing it in industries which could at

[055-177] any moment be taken away by the government, controlled by the officials

[055-178] or forced to sell at set prices, and which were always subject to

[055-179] exploitation by dishonest officials. A businessman felt secure only when

[055-180] he had invested in land, when he had received an official title upon the

[055-181] payment of large sums of money, or when he succeeded to push at least

[055-182] one of his sons into the government bureaucracy. No doubt, in spite of

[055-183] all this, Chinese business and industry kept on developing in the Manchu

[055-184] time, but they did not develop at such a speed as to transform the

[055-185] country from an agrarian into a modern industrial nation.

[055-186] 3 _Expansion in Central Asia; the first State treaty_

[055-187] The rise of the Manchu dynasty actually began under the K'ang-hsi rule

[055-188] (1663-1722). The emperor had three tasks. The first was the removal of

[055-189] the last supporters of the Ming dynasty and of the generals, such as Wu

[055-190] San-kui, who had tried to make themselves independent. This necessitated

[055-191] a long series of campaigns, most of them in the south-west or south of

[055-192] China; these scarcely affected the population of China proper. In 1683

[055-193] Formosa was occupied and the last of the insurgent army commanders was

[055-194] defeated. It was shown above that the situation of all these leaders

[055-195] became hopeless as soon as the Manchus had occupied the rich Yangtze

[055-196] region and the intelligentsia and the gentry of that region had gone

[055-197] over to them.
