# Section 33

[033-001] From 460 onward there were a number of important risings of princes; in

[033-002] some of them the Toba had a hand. They hoped by supporting one or

[033-003] another of the pretenders to gain overlordship over the whole of the

[033-004] southern empire. In these struggles in the south the Hsiao family,

[033-005] thanks mainly to General Hsiao Tao-ch'eng, steadily gained in power,

[033-006] especially as the family was united by marriage with the imperial house.

[033-007] In 477 Hsiao Tao-ch'eng finally had the emperor killed by an accomplice,

[033-008] the son of a shamaness; he set a boy on the throne and made himself

[033-009] regent. Very soon after this the boy emperor and all the members of the

[033-010] imperial family were murdered, and Hsiao Tao-ch'eng created the

[033-011] "Southern Ch'i" dynasty (479-501). Once more the remaining followers of

[033-012] the deposed dynasty fled northward to the Toba, and at once fighting

[033-013] between Toba and the south began again.

[033-014] This fighting ended with a victory for the Toba and with the final

[033-015] establishment of the Toba in the new capital of Loyang. South China was

[033-016] heavily defeated again and again, but never finally conquered. There

[033-017] were intervals of peace. In the years between 480 and 490 there was less

[033-018] disorder in the south, at all events in internal affairs. Princes were

[033-019] more often appointed to governorships, and the influence of the cliques

[033-020] was thus weakened. In spite of this, a stable regime was not built up,

[033-021] and in 494 a prince rose against the youthful emperor. This prince, with

[033-022] the help of his clique including the Ch'en family, which later attained

[033-023] importance, won the day, murdered the emperor, and became emperor

[033-024] himself. All that is recorded about him is that he fought unsuccessfully

[033-025] against the Toba, and that he had the whole of his own family killed out

[033-026] of fear that one of its members might act exactly as he had done. After

[033-027] his death there were conflicts between the emperor's few remaining

[033-028] relatives; in these the Toba again had a hand. The victor was a person

[033-029] named Hsiao Yen; he removed the reigning emperor in the usual way and

[033-030] made himself emperor. Although he belonged to the imperial family, he

[033-031] altered the name of the dynasty, and reigned from 502 as the first

[033-032] emperor of the "Liang dynasty".

[033-038] 4 _The Liang dynasty_ (A.D. 502-556)

[033-039] The fighting with the Toba continued until 515. As a rule the Toba were

[033-040] the more successful, not at least through the aid of princes of the

[033-041] deposed "Southern Ch'i dynasty" and their followers. Wars began also in

[033-042] the west, where the Toba tried to cut off the access of the Liang to the

[033-043] caravan routes to Turkestan. In 507, however, the Toba suffered an

[033-044] important defeat. The southern states had tried at all times to work

[033-045] with the Kansu states against the northern states; the Toba now followed

[033-046] suit and allied themselves with a large group of native chieftains of

[033-047] the south, whom they incited to move against the Liang. This produced

[033-048] great native unrest, especially in the provinces by the upper Yangtze.

[033-049] The natives, who were steadily pushed back by the Chinese peasants, were

[033-050] reduced to migrating into the mountain country or to working for the

[033-051] Chinese in semi-servile conditions; and they were ready for revolt and

[033-052] very glad to work with the Toba. The result of this unrest was not

[033-053] decisive, but it greatly reduced the strength of the regions along the

[033-054] upper Yangtze. Thus the main strength of the southern state was more

[033-055] than ever confined to the Nanking region.

[033-056] The first emperor of the Liang dynasty, who assumed the name Wu Ti

[033-057] (502-549), became well known in the Western world owing to his love of

[033-058] literature and of Buddhism. After he had come to the throne with the aid

[033-059] of his followers, he took no further interest in politics; he left that

[033-060] to his court clique. From now on, however, the political initiative

[033-061] really belonged to the north. At this time there began in the Toba

[033-062] empire the risings of tribal leaders against the government which we

[033-063] have fully described above. One of these leaders, Hou Ching, who had

[033-064] become powerful as a military leader in the north, tried in 547 to

[033-065] conclude a private alliance with the Liang to strengthen his own

[033-066] position. At the same time the ruler of the northern state of the

[033-067] "Northern Ch'i", then in process of formation, himself wanted to

[033-068] negotiate an alliance with the Liang, in order to be able to get rid of

[033-069] Hou Ching. There was indecision in Liang. Hou Ching, who had been

[033-070] getting into difficulties, now negotiated with a dissatisfied prince in

[033-071] Liang, invaded the country in 548 with the prince's aid, captured the

[033-072] capital in 549, and killed Emperor Wu. Hou Ching now staged the usual

[033-073] spectacle: he put a puppet on the imperial throne, deposed him eighteen

[033-074] months later and made himself emperor.

[033-075] This man of the Toba on the throne of South China was unable, however,

[033-076] to maintain his position; he had not sufficient backing. He was at war

[033-077] with the new rulers in the northern empire, and his own army, which was

[033-078] not very large, melted away; above all, he proceeded with excessive

[033-079] harshness against the helpers who had gained access for him to the

[033-080] Liang, and thereafter he failed to secure a following from among the

[033-081] leading cliques at court. In 552 he was driven out by a Chinese army led

[033-082] by one of the princes and was killed.

[033-083] The new emperor had been a prince in the upper Yangtze region, and his

[033-084] closest associates were engaged there. They did not want to move to the

[033-085] distant capital, Nanking, because their private financial interests

[033-086] would have suffered. The emperor therefore remained in the city now

[033-087] called Hankow. He left the eastern territory in the hands of two

[033-088] powerful generals, one of whom belonged to the Ch'en family, which he no

[033-089] longer had the strength to remove. In this situation the generals in the

[033-090] east made themselves independent, and this naturally produced tension at

[033-091] once between the east and the west of the Liang empire; this tension was

[033-092] now exploited by the leaders of the Chou state then in the making in the

[033-093] north. On the invitation of a clique in the south and with its support,

[033-094] the Chou invaded the present province of Hupei and in 555 captured the

[033-095] Liang emperor's capital. They were now able to achieve their old

[033-096] ambition: a prince of the Chou dynasty was installed as a feudatory of

[033-097] the north, reigning until 587 in the present Hankow. He was permitted to

[033-098] call his quasi-feudal territory a kingdom and his dynasty, as we know

[033-099] already, the "Later Liang dynasty".

[033-100] 5 _The Ch'en dynasty (A.D. 557-588) and its ending by the Sui_

[033-101] The more important of the independent generals in the east, Ch'en

[033-102] Pa-hsien, installed a shadow emperor, forced him to abdicate, and made

[033-103] himself emperor. The Ch'en dynasty which thus began was even feebler

[033-104] than the preceding dynasties. Its territory was confined to the lower

[033-105] Yangtze valley. Once more cliques and rival pretenders were at work and

[033-106] prevented any sort of constructive home policy. Abroad, certain

[033-107] advantages were gained in north China over the Northern Ch'i dynasty,

[033-108] but none of any great importance.

[033-109] Meanwhile in the north Yang Chien had brought into power the Chinese

[033-110] Sui dynasty. It began by liquidating the quasi-feudal state of the

[033-111] "Later Liang". Then followed, in 588-9, the conquest of the Ch'en

[033-112] empire, almost without any serious resistance. This brought all China

[033-113] once more under united rule, and a period of 360 years of division was

[033-114] ended.

[033-115] 6 _Cultural achievements of the south_

[033-116] For nearly three hundred years the southern empire had witnessed

[033-117] unceasing struggles between important cliques, making impossible any

[033-118] peaceful development within the country. Culturally, however, the period

[033-119] was rich in achievement. The court and the palaces of wealthy members of

[033-120] the gentry attracted scholars and poets, and the gentry themselves had

[033-121] time for artistic occupations. A large number of the best-known Chinese

[033-122] poets appeared in this period, and their works plainly reflect the

[033-123] conditions of that time: they are poems for the small circle of scholars

[033-124] among the gentry and for cultured patrons, spiced with quotations and

[033-125] allusions, elaborate in metre and construction, masterpieces of

[033-126] aesthetic sensitivity--but unintelligible except to highly educated

[033-127] members of the aristocracy. The works were of the most artificial type,

[033-128] far removed from all natural feeling.

[033-129] Music, too, was never so assiduously cultivated as at this time. But the

[033-130] old Chinese music disappeared in the south as in the north, where

[033-131] dancing troupes and women musicians in the Sogdian commercial colonies

[033-132] of the province of Kansu established the music of western Turkestan.

[033-133] Here in the south, native courtesans brought the aboriginal, non-Chinese

[033-134] music to the court; Chinese poets wrote songs in Chinese for this music,

[033-135] and so the old Chinese music became unfashionable and was forgotten. The

[033-136] upper class, the gentry, bought these girls, often in large numbers, and

[033-137] organized them in troupes of singers and dancers, who had to appear on

[033-138] festal occasions and even at the court. For merchants and other people

[033-139] who lacked full social recognition there were brothels, a quite natural

[033-140] feature wherever there were considerable commercial colonies or

[033-141] collections of merchants, including the capital of the southern empire.

[033-142] In their ideology, as will be remembered, the Chinese gentry were always

[033-143] in favour of Confucianism. Here in the south, however, the association

[033-144] with Confucianism was less serious, the southern gentry, with their

[033-145] relations with the merchant class, having acquired the character of

[033-146] "colonial" gentry. They were brought up as Confucians, but were

[033-147] interested in all sorts of different religious movements, and

[033-148] especially in Buddhism. A different type of Buddhism from that in the

[033-149] north had spread over most of the south, a meditative Buddhism that was

[033-150] very close ideologically to the original Taoism, and so fulfilled the

[033-151] same social functions as Taoism. Those who found the official life with

[033-152] its intrigues repulsive, occupied themselves with meditative Buddhism.

[033-153] The monks told of the sad fate of the wicked in the life to come, and

[033-154] industriously filled the gentry with apprehension, so that they tried to

[033-155] make up for their evil deeds by rich gifts to the monasteries. Many

[033-156] emperors in this period, especially Wu Ti of the Liang dynasty, inclined

[033-157] to Buddhism. Wu Ti turned to it especially in his old age, when he was

[033-158] shut out entirely from the tasks of a ruler and was no longer satisfied

[033-159] with the usual pleasures of the court. Several times he instituted

[033-160] Buddhist ceremonies of purification on a large scale in the hope of so

[033-161] securing forgiveness for the many murders he had committed.

[033-162] Genuine Taoism also came to the fore again, and with it the popular

[033-163] religion with its magic, now amplified with the many local deities that

[033-164] had been taken over from the indigenous population of the south. For a

[033-165] time it became the fashion at court to pass the time in learned

[033-166] discussions between Confucians, Buddhists, and Taoists, which were quite

[033-167] similar to the debates between learned men centuries earlier at the

[033-168] wealthy little Indian courts. For the court clique this was more a

[033-169] matter of pastime than of religious controversy. It seems thoroughly in

[033-170] harmony with the political events that here, for the first time in the

[033-171] history of Chinese philosophy, materialist currents made their

[033-172] appearance, running parallel with Machiavellian theories of power for

[033-173] the benefit of the wealthiest of the gentry.
