## Overview

Liu Yao was the final ruler of the Former [[Chao|Zhao]] state, a [[hsiung-nu|Xiongnu]]-led kingdom that dominated northern China in the early fourth century AD. Succeeding Liu Ts'ung, Liu Yao inherited an empire already weakened by internal division and the growing power of his erstwhile ally [[Shih Lo]]. His reign ended in military defeat and the absorption of his territory by [[shih-lo|Shih Lo]]'s Later Chao dynasty. Liu Yao's fall marked the end of the first phase of [[hsiung-nu|Xiongnu]] state-building in China and the consolidation of [[shih-lo|Shih Lo]]'s power over the north.

## Life and Background

Liu Yao came from the ruling house of the [[hsiung-nu|Xiongnu]] state founded by [[liu-yuan|Liu Yuan]], who had declared a Han-style dynasty in AD 304. The [[hsiung-nu|Xiongnu]] leadership had adopted the surname Liu, claiming descent from a Han princess, and positioned themselves as restorers of Chinese imperial tradition even as they ruled a multi-ethnic steppe empire. Liu Yao grew up in this hybrid cultural environment, trained in both Chinese statecraft and [[hsiung-nu|Xiongnu]] military traditions.

## Rise to Power

Liu Yao succeeded Liu Ts'ung, who had expanded [[hsiung-nu|Xiongnu]] power but also presided over growing tensions within the ruling coalition. By the time of Liu Yao's accession, the military commander [[shih-lo|Shih Lo]] had built a largely autonomous power base in the eastern territories:

> "nor had his successor Liu Yao" (025-060)

Liu Yao faced the same structural challenge that had undermined his predecessor: the inability to control [[shih-lo|Shih Lo]], whose military prowess and independent following made him a rival rather than a subordinate. Liu Yao attempted to assert central authority but lacked the resources to compel [[shih-lo|Shih Lo]]'s obedience.

## Downfall and Later Years

The conflict between Liu Yao and [[shih-lo|Shih Lo]] escalated into open warfare. [[shih-lo|Shih Lo]], a former slave who had risen through the ranks of the [[hsiung-nu|Xiongnu]] military, proved the more effective commander:

> "Shih Lo, and the remainder of Liu Yao's empire" (025-063)

Tribes and officials who had previously aligned with the Liu house began to defect to [[shih-lo|Shih Lo]], judging his prospects superior. The coalition that [[liu-yuan|Liu Yuan]] had assembled unravelled as its constituent groups calculated their interests independently.

[[shih-lo|Shih Lo]]'s victory was decisive:

> "after his destruction of Liu Yao, no longer hesitated: ex-slav" (025-074)

With Liu Yao eliminated, [[shih-lo|Shih Lo]] formally established his own dynasty — the Later Chao — and made no pretence of loyalty to the Liu house. The [[hsiung-nu|Xiongnu]] experiment in Chinese-style statehood came to an end, replaced by a more nakedly militaristic regime.

## Historical Assessment

Liu Yao's historical significance lies less in his personal achievements than in what his failure reveals about the political dynamics of the Sixteen Kingdoms period. The [[hsiung-nu|Xiongnu]] state founded by [[liu-yuan|Liu Yuan]] depended on a coalition of steppe and Chinese elements held together by military success and the personal authority of the founder. By the third generation, centrifugal forces overwhelmed central control. Liu Yao was not without ability, but he faced a rival of exceptional talent in [[shih-lo|Shih Lo]], and the structural weaknesses of the Former Zhao state gave him few resources with which to resist.
