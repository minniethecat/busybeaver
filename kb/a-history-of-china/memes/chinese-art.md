## Overview

Chinese art is one of the world's oldest continuous artistic traditions, stretching from the [[yangtze-river|Yang-shao]] painted pottery of the third millennium BC through the bronze ritual vessels of the [[confucianism|Shang]], the Buddhist cave-temples of the Toba period, the landscape painting of the [[tang-dynasty|T'ang]] and Sung, and the blue-and-white porcelain of the [[ming-dynasty|Ming]]. Across these eras, Chinese art was never a single, unified tradition but rather a complex interplay of court patronage, gentry connoisseurship, foreign stimulus, and folk craft. Eberhard sketches this trajectory through the lens of social and political history, emphasizing that art was always embedded in the structures of power, religion, and commerce.

The distinction between "artist" and "craftsman" was fundamental to the Chinese conception: the former was an educated member of the gentry who painted as a cultivated pastime, while the latter produced objects for use, ritual, or sale. This social divide shaped every field, from painting to porcelain.

## Painting

> "The principal achievement of the T'ang period lies undoubtedly in the field of painting. As in poetry, in painting there are strong traces of alien influences; even before the T'ang period, the painter Hsieh Ho laid down the six fundamental laws of painting, in all probability drawn from Indian practice." [037-094-095]

Painting occupied the highest rung among the Chinese arts because it was the medium of the literate gentleman. In the Han period, painting was done on silk, of which plenty came into the market through the control of silk-producing southern China [021-092]. The gentry painted as a social pastime, just as they assembled for poetry, discussion, or song and dance; they painted for aesthetic pleasure and rarely as a means of earning [021-098-100]. A man paid for his work, such as a portrait for the ancestral cult, was regarded as a craftsman, not as an artist [021-109].

During the T'ang period, the practice reached its first great flowering. The most famous painter was Wu Tao-tzu, strongly influenced by Central Asian works and active as a pious Buddhist painting pictures for temples [037-102-103]. Among landscape painters, Wang Wei (721--759) ranked first; a famous poet as well, he aimed at uniting poem and painting into an integral whole, inaugurating the great tradition of Chinese landscape painting [037-105-108].

| Period | Key Painters | Characteristics |
|--------|-------------|-----------------|
| Han (206 BC--220 CE) | Anonymous craftsmen | Silk painting, tomb reliefs, idealized scenes of gentry life |
| T'ang (618--907) | Wu Tao-tzu, Wang Wei | Buddhist temple painting, landscape origins, Central Asian influence |
| Sung (960--1279) | Mi Fei, [[li-lung-mien|Li Lung-mien]], Ma Yuan | Northern/southern schools, intimate personal expression, academic style |
| Ming (1368--1644) | Ch'in Ying, T'ang Yin, Tung Ch'i-ch'ang | Academic style, elegant women, art theory, wood-cut prints |
| Ch'ing (1644--1912) | Giuseppe Castiglione (Lang Shih-ning) | European influence via Jesuit missionaries at court |

By the Sung period, painting had reached its highest development [044-085]. Two main streams emerged: a northern school, whose painters were often court officials producing decorative, realistic works with great attention to detail; and a southern school, which regarded painting as an intimate, personal expression, painting inner realities rather than outer forms [044-086-099]. Mi Fei (ca. 1051--1107) was a painter, calligrapher, art collector, and critic, whose works were not much liked by Emperor [[hsiung-nu|Hu]]i Tsung, himself one of the greatest art collectors [044-102-103]. The emperor created the Painting Academy, an institution that gave official recognition to painters in the form of titles granting access to and status at court [044-105-107].

In the Manchu (Ch'ing) period, European influence entered through the Italian missionary Giuseppe Castiglione (Lang Shih-ning, 1688--1766), who learned the Chinese method of painting but introduced a number of European techniques that were adopted especially by official court painters; the painting of scholars in seclusion remained uninfluenced [056-159-165].

## Bronze and Sculpture

> "The Shang had sculptures in stone, an art which later more or less completely disappeared and which was resuscitated only in post-Christian times under the influence of Indian Buddhism." [004-132]

The earliest great art form of Chinese civilization was bronze-casting. Bronze vessels appeared around 1450 BC and were entirely different from anything produced in other parts of Asia. Their ornamentation combined elements of the "animal style" typical of steppe peoples with southern decorative patterns, probably first applied to wooden and gourd vessels and then transferred to bronze [004-080-087]. The famous bronze vessels of the Shang are of a southern type and reveal an advanced technique that has scarcely been excelled since [004-141-142].

These vessels were used for religious service, not for everyday life -- for daily use there were earthenware vessels. Even in the middle of the first millennium BC, bronze was exceedingly dear, and China's chronic scarcity of metal meant that metal implements were never universally adopted [004-143-161].

Sculpture revived dramatically with the arrival of [[buddhism]]. The Toba Turks constructed the vast cave-temple complex of Yun-kang in northern Shansi, where hundreds of caves were cut into steep river cliffs and thousands of busts and reliefs sculptured in stone [030-005-009]. Elements from the Greco-Bactrian culture (in present Afghanistan) were reworked by Toba artists -- "old forms were filled with a new content" -- in a synthesis of Western inspiration and Turkish initiative [030-026-030]. All steppe rulers showed special interest in sculpture, and after the Toba period, sculpture flourished further in the T'ang period, the period of the Five Dynasties, and the Mongol period [030-031-034].

## Porcelain and Ceramics

Porcelain was invented early in China, a consequence of the persistent scarcity of metal. Porcelain vessels had many of the qualities of metal ones but were cheaper [004-162]. The earthenware of the Shang period was already very near to porcelain -- a brilliant white pottery lacking only the glaze [004-165-166].

The great age of porcelain began in the T'ang period. T'ang porcelain was technically and artistically of very high quality, though the white porcelain preferred today was not yet developed [037-110-112]. Since it was produced only for the court and high dignitaries, T'ang porcelain later became a great rarity [037-113-115]. Captured Chinese craftsmen brought the knowledge of porcelain-making to the Islamic world after the battle of Samarkand (751) [037-117-119]. By around 800, the Abbasid caliphate used imported Chinese porcelain, and by the end of the fourteenth century porcelain was known in Eastern Africa [040-101-102].

The Sung period produced the famous celadon (green porcelain), consisting of a solid paste covered with green glaze and incised decoration. The first pure white porcelain with incised ornamentation under the glaze appeared, and near the end of the period, blue-and-white porcelain began, using cobalt from Asia Minor [044-114-123].

| Period | Porcelain Development |
|--------|----------------------|
| Shang (c. 1600--1046 BC) | Brilliant white earthenware, near-porcelain, lacking glaze |
| T'ang (618--907) | High-quality court porcelain, brownish-yellow inside; export to Islamic world |
| Sung (960--1279) | Celadon (green porcelain); first pure white porcelain; blue-and-white begins |
| Ming (1368--1644) | Blue-and-white perfected; three-colour and five-colour (enamel); export to Europe |
| Ch'ing (1644--1912) | *Famille verte*, *famille rose*, ox-blood red; eventual industrial decline |

In the Ming epoch, blue decoration on a white ground became general, produced at the famous kilns in Ching-te-chen, Kiangsi. The finest specimens are in the palace at Istanbul. Three-colour (lead glaze) and five-colour (enamel) techniques emerged, and the exported porcelains first influenced European ceramics at Delft, then were imitated by Bottger in Europe [051-142-154].

## Ceremonial Pottery in Prehistory

Chinese art's roots lie deep in the Neolithic. The Yang-shao culture (c. 2200--1600 BC) produced wonderfully fine painted pottery used as gifts to the dead, painted in white, red, and black with stylized patterns [003-151-154]. This painted pottery tradition disappeared with the beginning of urban civilization and the invention of writing [003-157-158].

A separate tradition, the Lung-shan black pottery, emerged in the plains of eastern China and interacted with the painted-pottery culture. Its exceptionally fine quality and polished surfaces, together with an absence of metal, distinguished it from the Yang-shao tradition [004-015]. These early ceramics established basic forms -- tripod vessels, stemmed cups, and storage jars -- that would persist through later Chinese pottery.

## Lacquer, Textiles, and Applied Arts

Beyond painting, bronze, and porcelain, Chinese applied arts produced technically excellent fabrics, fine lacquerware, and artistic buildings throughout the T'ang period [037-091-092]. Han tombs have yielded artistic representations of human figures on lacquer caskets [021-122].

The Ming epoch was especially famous for its lacquers -- carved lacquer, lacquer painting, and gold lacquer -- and for cloisonne work, closely associated with contemporary work in Japan [051-157-159]. Wood-cut and block-printing developed as a cheap substitute for real paintings: new urbanites who wanted paintings of the masters found in the multi-colour wood-cut print a cheap mass medium, a technique adopted by Japan and brought there to its highest refinement [051-128-134].
