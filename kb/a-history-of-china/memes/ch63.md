# Section 63

[063-001] Meanwhile Yüan Shih-k'ai had made all preparations for turning the

[063-002] Republic once more into an empire, in which he would be emperor; the

[063-003] empire was to be based once more on the gentry group. In 1914 he secured

[063-004] an amendment of the Constitution under which the governing power was to

[063-005] be entirely in the hands of the president; at the end of 1914 he secured

[063-006] his appointment as president for life, and at the end of 1915 he induced

[063-007] the parliament to resolve that he should become emperor.

[063-008] This naturally aroused the resentment of the republicans, but it also

[063-009] annoyed the generals belonging to the gentry, who had the same ambition.

[063-010] Thus there were disturbances, especially in the south, where Sun Yat-sen

[063-011] with his followers agitated for a democratic republic. The foreign

[063-012] powers recognized that a divided China would be much easier to penetrate

[063-013] and annex than a united China, and accordingly opposed Yüan Shih-k'ai.

[063-014] Before he could ascend the throne, he died suddenly--and this

[063-015] terminated the first attempt to re-establish monarchy.

[063-016] Yüan was succeeded as president by Li Yüan-hung. Meanwhile five

[063-017] provinces had declared themselves independent. Foreign pressure on China

[063-018] steadily grew. She was forced to declare war on Germany, and though this

[063-019] made no practical difference to the war, it enabled the European powers

[063-020] to penetrate further into China. Difficulties grew to such an extent in

[063-021] 1917 that a dictatorship was set up and soon after came an interlude,

[063-022] the recall of the Manchus and the reinstatement of the deposed emperor

[063-023] (July 1st-8th, 1917).

[063-024] This led to various risings of generals, each aiming simply at the

[063-025] satisfaction of his thirst for personal power. Ultimately the victorious

[063-026] group of generals, headed by Tuan Ch'i-jui, secured the election of Fêng

[063-027] Kuo-chang in place of the retiring president. Fêng was succeeded at the

[063-028] end of 1918 by Hsü Shih-ch'ang, who held office until 1922. Hsü, as a

[063-029] former ward of the emperor, was a typical representative of the gentry,

[063-030] and was opposed to all republican reforms.

[063-031] The south held aloof from these northern governments. In Canton an

[063-032] opposition government was set up, formed mainly of followers of Sun

[063-033] Yat-sen; the Peking government was unable to remove the Canton

[063-034] government. But the Peking government and its president scarcely counted

[063-035] any longer even in the north. All that counted were the generals, the

[063-036] most prominent of whom were: (1) Chang Tso-lin, who had control of

[063-037] Manchuria and had made certain terms with Japan, but who was ultimately

[063-038] murdered by the Japanese (1928); (2) Wu P'ei-fu, who held North China;

[063-039] (3) the so-called "Christian general", Fêng Yü-hsiang, and (4) Ts'ao

[063-040] K'un, who became president in 1923.

[063-041] At the end of the first world war Japan had a hold over China amounting

[063-042] almost to military control of the country. China did not sign the Treaty

[063-043] of Versailles, because she considered that she had been duped by Japan,

[063-044] since Japan had driven the Germans out of China but had not returned the

[063-045] liberated territory to the Chinese. In 1921 peace was concluded with

[063-046] Germany, the German privileges being abolished. The same applied to

[063-047] Austria. Russia, immediately after the setting up of the Soviet

[063-048] government, had renounced all her rights under the Capitulations. This

[063-049] was the first step in the gradual rescinding of the Capitulations; the

[063-050] last of them went only in 1943, as a consequence of the difficult

[063-051] situation of the Europeans and Americans in the Pacific produced by the

[063-052] Second World War.

[063-053] At the end of the first world war the foreign powers revised their

[063-054] attitude towards China. The idea of territorial partitioning of the

[063-055] country was replaced by an attempt at financial exploitation; military

[063-056] friction between the Western powers and Japan was in this way to be

[063-057] minimized. Financial control was to be exercised by an international

[063-058] banking consortium (1920). It was necessary for political reasons that

[063-059] this committee should be joined by Japan. After her Twenty-one Demands,

[063-060] however, Japan was hated throughout China. During the world war she had

[063-061] given loans to the various governments and rebels, and in this way had

[063-062] secured one privilege after another. Consequently China declined the

[063-063] banking consortium. She tried to secure capital from her own resources;

[063-064] but in the existing political situation and the acute economic

[063-065] depression internal loans had no success.

[063-066] In an agreement between the United States and Japan in 1917, the United

[063-067] States, in consequence of the war, had to give their assent to special

[063-068] rights for Japan in China. After the war the international conference at

[063-069] Washington (November 1921-February 1922) tried to set narrower limits to

[063-070] Japan's influence over China, and also to re-determine the relative

[063-071] strength in the Pacific of the four great powers (America, Britain,

[063-072] France, Japan). After the failure of the banking plan this was the last

[063-073] means of preventing military conflicts between the powers in the Far

[063-074] East. This brought some relief to China, as Japan had to yield for the

[063-075] time to the pressure of the western powers.

[063-076] The years that followed until 1927 were those of the complete collapse

[063-077] of the political power of the Peking government--years of entire

[063-078] dissolution. In the south Sun Yat-sen had been elected generalissimo in

[063-079] 1921\. In 1924 he was re-elected with a mandate for a campaign against

[063-080] the north. In 1924 there also met in Canton the first general congress

[063-081] of the Kuomintang ("People's Party"). The Kuomintang (in 1929 it had

[063-082] 653,000 members, or roughly 0.15 per cent of the population) is the

[063-083] continuation of the Komingtang ("Revolutionary Party") founded by Sun

[063-084] Yat-sen, which as a middle-class party had worked for the removal of the

[063-085] dynasty. The new Kuomintang was more socialistic, as is shown by its

[063-086] admission of Communists and the stress laid upon land reform.

[063-087] At the end of 1924 Sun Yat-sen with some of his followers went to

[063-088] Peking, to discuss the possibility of a reunion between north and south

[063-089] on the basis of the program of the People's Party. There, however, he

[063-090] died at the beginning of 1925, before any definite results had been

[063-091] attained; there was no prospect of achieving anything by the

[063-092] negotiations, and the south broke them off. But the death of Sun Yat-sen

[063-093] had been followed after a time by tension within the party between its

[063-094] right and left wings. The southern government had invited a number of

[063-095] Russian advisers in 1923 to assist in building up the administration,

[063-096] civil and military, and on their advice the system of government had

[063-097] been reorganized on lines similar to those of the soviet and commissar

[063-098] system. This change had been advocated by an old friend of Sun Yat-sen,

[063-099] Chiang Kai-shek, who later married Sun's sister-in-law. Chiang Kai-shek,

[063-100] who was born in 1886, was the head of the military academy at Whampoa,

[063-101] near Canton, where Russian instructors were at work. The new system was

[063-102] approved by Sun Yat-sen's successor, Hu Han-min (who died in 1936), in

[063-103] his capacity of party leader. It was opposed by the elements of the

[063-104] right, who at first had little influence. Chiang Kai-shek soon became

[063-105] one of the principal leaders of the south, as he had command of the

[063-106] efficient troops of Canton, who had been organized by the Russians.

[063-107] The People's Party of the south and its governments, at that time fairly

[063-108] radical in politics, were disliked by the foreign powers; only Japan

[063-109] supported them for a time, owing to the anti-British feeling of the

[063-110] South Chinese and in order to further her purpose of maintaining

[063-111] disunion in China. The first serious collision with the outer world came

[063-112] on May 30th, 1925, when British soldiers shot at a crowd demonstrating

[063-113] in Shanghai. This produced a widespread boycott of British goods in

[063-114] Canton and in British Hong Kong, inflicting a great loss on British

[063-115] trade with China and bringing considerable advantages in consequence to

[063-116] Japanese trade and shipping: from the time of this boycott began the

[063-117] Japanese grip on Chinese coastwise shipping.

[063-118] The second party congress was held in Canton in 1926. Chiang Kai-shek

[063-119] already played a prominent part. The People's Party, under Chiang

[063-120] Kai-shek and with the support of the communists, began the great

[063-121] campaign against the north. At first it had good success: the various

[063-122] provincial governors and generals and the Peking government were played

[063-123] off against each other, and in a short time one leader after another was

[063-124] defeated. The Yangtze was reached, and in 1926 the southern government

[063-125] moved to Hankow. All over the southern provinces there now came a

[063-126] genuine rising of the masses of the people, mainly the result of

[063-127] communist propaganda and of the government's promise to give land to the

[063-128] peasants, to set limits to the big estates, and to bring order into the

[063-129] taxation. In spite of its communist element, at the beginning of 1927

[063-130] the southern government was essentially one of the middle class and the

[063-131] peasantry, with a socialistic tendency.

[063-132] 3 _Second period of the Republic: Nationalist China_

[063-133] With the continued success of the northern campaign, and with Chiang

[063-134] Kai-shek's southern army at the gates of Shanghai (March 21st, 1927), a

[063-135] decision had to be taken. Should the left wing be allowed to gain the

[063-136] upper hand, and the great capitalists of Shanghai be expropriated as it

[063-137] was proposed to expropriate the gentry? Or should the right wing

[063-138] prevail, an alliance be concluded with the capitalists, and limits be

[063-139] set to the expropriation of landed estates? Chiang Kai-shek, through his

[063-140] marriage with Sun Yat-sen's wife's sister, had become allied with one of

[063-141] the greatest banking families. In the days of the siege of Shanghai

[063-142] Chiang, together with his closest colleagues (with the exception of Hu

[063-143] Han-min and Wang Chying-wei, a leader who will be mentioned later),

[063-144] decided on the second alternative. Shanghai came into his hands without

[063-145] a struggle, and the capital of the Shanghai financiers, and soon foreign

[063-146] capital as well, was placed at his disposal, so that he was able to pay

[063-147] his troops and finance his administration. At the same time the Russian

[063-148] advisers were dismissed or executed.

[063-149] The decision arrived at by Chiang Kai-shek and his friends did not

[063-150] remain unopposed, and he parted from the "left group" (1927) which

[063-151] formed a rival government in Hankow, while Chiang Kai-shek made Nanking

[063-152] the seat of his government (April 1927). In that year Chiang not only

[063-153] concluded peace with the financiers and industrialists, but also a sort

[063-154] of "armistice" with the landowning gentry. "Land reform" still stood on

[063-155] the party program, but nothing was done, and in this way the confidence

[063-156] and co-operation of large sections of the gentry was secured. The choice

[063-157] of Nanking as the new capital pleased both the industrialists and the

[063-158] agrarians: the great bulk of China's young industries lay in the Yangtze

[063-159] region, and that region was still the principal one for agricultural

[063-160] produce; the landowners of the region were also in a better position

[063-161] with the great market of the capital in their neighbourhood.

[063-162] Meanwhile the Nanking government had succeeded in carrying its dealings

[063-163] with the northern generals to a point at which they were largely

[063-164] out-manoeuvred and became ready for some sort of collaboration (1928).

[063-165] There were now four supreme commanders--Chiang Kai-shek, Fêng Yü-hsiang

[063-166] (the "Christian general"), Yen Hsi-shan, the governor of Shansi, and the

[063-167] Muslim Li Chung-yen. Naturally this was not a permanent solution; not

[063-168] only did Chiang Kai-shek's three rivals try to free themselves from his

[063-169] ever-growing influence and to gain full power themselves, but various

[063-170] groups under military leadership rose again and again, even in the home

[063-171] of the Republic, Canton itself. These struggles, which were carried on

[063-172] more by means of diplomacy and bribery than at arms, lasted until 1936.

[063-173] Chiang Kai-shek, as by far the most skilful player in this game, and at

[063-174] the same time the man who had the support of the foreign governments

[063-175] and of the financiers of Shanghai, gained the victory. China became

[063-176] unified under his dictatorship.

[063-177] As early as 1928, when there seemed a possibility of uniting China, with

[063-178] the exception of Manchuria, which was dominated by Japan, and when the

[063-179] European powers began more and more to support Chiang Kai-shek, Japan

[063-180] felt that her interests in North China were threatened, and landed

[063-181] troops in Shantung. There was hard fighting on May 3rd, 1928. General

[063-182] Chang Tso-lin, in Manchuria, who was allied to Japan, endeavoured to

[063-183] secure a cessation of hostilities, but he fell victim to a Japanese

[063-184] assassin; his place was taken by his son, Chang Hsüeh-liang, who pursued

[063-185] an anti-Japanese policy. The Japanese recognized, however, that in view

[063-186] of the international situation the time had not yet come for

[063-187] intervention in North China. In 1929 they withdrew their troops and

[063-188] concentrated instead on their plans for Manchuria.
