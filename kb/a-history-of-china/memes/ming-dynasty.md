## Overview

The **Ming dynasty** (1368–1644 CE), also called the Ming Epoch, was the last great native Chinese imperial dynasty. Founded by [[chu-yuan-chang|Chu Yuan-chang]] -- a peasant monk turned rebel leader -- after the expulsion of the Mongol Yuan dynasty, the Ming represented a resurgence of Chinese national feeling and a return to Confucian principles of governance [049-024]. Yet the Ming was also an age of profound transformation: an agricultural revolution doubled rice production [050-058], the [[peking|Peking]]-based court oversaw an empire stretching from Manchuria to the borders of Burma [050-019], and Admiral [[cheng-ho|Cheng Ho]]'s treasure fleets sailed as far as Arabia decades before the European age of exploration [052-090].

The Ming witnessed the rise of a commercialized economy centered on the [[yangtze-river|Yangtze valley]], the flourishing of the vernacular novel, and the perfection of blue-and-white porcelain that remains synonymous with Chinese art. At its height under the [[yung-lo|Yung-lo Emperor]] (r. 1403-1424), the Ming court was the most magnificent in Asia [052-055]. But the dynasty also incubated the forces of its own destruction: factional warfare between gentry cliques, the corrosive power of [[eunuch|palace eunuchs]], fiscal crisis, and finally the massive peasant rebellions that opened the gates of [[peking|Peking]] to the Manchus in 1644 [053-120].

Only three times in Chinese history had a man of the peasantry become emperor and founder of a dynasty: the founder of the [[han-dynasty|Han]], the founder of one of the Five Dynasties in the tenth century, and [[chu-yuan-chang|Chu Yuan-chang]], founder of the Ming [049-052]. This fact alone captures the remarkable social mobility -- and revolutionary potential -- of the Ming founding moment.

## Founding

> "Chu was of the humblest origin; he became a monk and a peasant leader at one and the same time. Only three times in Chinese history has a man of the peasantry become emperor and founder of a dynasty." [049-052]

The Ming dynasty was born from the collapse of Mongol rule in China. The popular risings of the mid-fourteenth century were initially economic and social in character, not directed against the [[mongol|Mongols]] as an alien people [049-037]. [[chu-yuan-chang|Chu Yuan-chang]]'s rising was at first a purely social movement -- Eberhard calls it "revolutionary" [049-045]. Only when the [[mongol|Mongols]] responded with tightened nationality laws did the movement become a national struggle against foreign rule [049-060].

Chu captured [[peking|Peking]] in 1368 and was recognized as emperor, taking the reign title [[hsiung-nu|Hu]]ng-wu. The [[mongol|Mongols]] were driven out quickly, and the ease of success gave the Chinese a new sense of national superiority and self-confidence [049-080]. This nascent nationalism -- distinct from the older cultural sense of Chinese superiority -- had its roots in the Southern Sung period and was greatly fostered by the discriminatory laws of the [[mongol|Mongols]] [049-090]. From this time onward, it was regarded as shameful to serve a foreigner as an official [049-095].

Chu established his capital at [[nanking|Nanking]] in the wealthy [[yangtze-river|Yangtze]] delta. His early laws were directed against the rich: many wealthy families were compelled to migrate to Nanking, losing their land; land was redistributed among poor peasants; and new land registers were compiled to prevent the rich from evading taxation [049-142]. The number of monks was cut down -- an ironic measure from a former monk [049-156]. Yet these reforms were only half carried out, especially in the Shanghai hinterland where the emperor depended on wealthy landowners to finance his armies [049-168].

## Government and Bureaucracy

The Ming governmental system was an uneasy hybrid of revolutionary ideals and Mongol-era absolutism. [[chu-yuan-chang|Chu Yuan-chang]]'s conception of an emperor was that of an absolute monarch, master over life and death of his subjects -- a conception formed by the Mongol emperors with their magnificence in [[peking|Peking]] [049-184]. He conferred enormous land grants on relatives and supporters; a single individual might receive land sufficient for 20,000 peasant families [049-210]. Pension payments to the imperial family alone consumed over eight million _shih_ of grain annually -- more than 160,000 tons [049-220].

A critical turning point came through a dynastic succession crisis. When [[chu-yuan-chang|Chu Yuan-chang]] ([[chu-yuan-chang|T'ai Tsu]]) died in 1398, his grandson [[hsiung-nu|Hu]]i Ti succeeded him with the support of southern Confucian gentry families [052-018]. But Chu's son, the prince stationed at [[peking|Peking]] with a strong army, marched south, captured Nanking, and made himself emperor as Ch'eng Tsu -- better known by his reign name **Yung-lo** [052-026]. He transferred the capital to [[peking|Peking]], where it remained throughout the Ming epoch, while Nanking became a subsidiary capital [052-034].

This transfer created a structural tension: the north was of military importance, but the [[yangtze-river|Yangtze]] region remained the economic centre of the country [052-048]. The interests of the [[yangtze-river|Yangtze]] gentry were injured by the transfer.

> "I know the way of heaven. Why discuss the hearts of the people?" [052-070]

This statement, attributed to one of Yung-lo's political and military advisors -- significantly a Buddhist monk -- captures the unmitigated absolutism that developed under his reign. Yung-lo revived all the luxury of the Mongol epoch, making his reign the most magnificent period of the Ming, but beneath the surface decay had already begun [052-064].

The later Ming saw the rise of eunuch power as a defining feature of court politics. [[eunuch|Eunuchs]], originally forbidden education, were increasingly used by Ming emperors to counteract the power of gentry cliques and strengthen personal rule [051-082]. Whole departments of eunuchs came into existence, handling confidential imperial business [051-100]. The eunuch [[liu-chin|Liu Chin]], who controlled the young emperor Wu Tsung (r. 1505-1521), provides the most extreme example: he issued all commands himself, removed or murdered over three hundred opponents, and amassed a treasure exceeding the annual state budget [052-138].

The state [[examination-system|examination system]], meanwhile, produced a new "small gentry" class. Fixed numbers of candidates were assigned per province after protests from the northern military clique -- one-third from the north, two-thirds from the south [052-110]. But the democratization of access to office brought no benefit to the general population; it resulted only in further exploitation of the peasants, as newly appointed officials sought to recoup their examination debts [051-058].

## Economy and Maritime Expeditions

The Ming epoch witnessed what Eberhard describes as an "agricultural revolution" [050-058]. New irrigation machines, especially the Persian wheel, were introduced. More importantly, the adoption of quick-ripening Champa rice from Indo-China enabled double-cropping: the fastest varieties took only sixty days from transplantation to harvest [050-080]. This more than doubled rice production. Fish were kept in the rice fields, providing food, fertilization, and -- though the Chinese did not know it -- malaria control through mosquito predation [050-096]. The discovery of crop rotation ended the old fallow system, and continuous cultivation with multiple harvests per year became standard [050-108]. The population revolution that began around 1550 was a direct result of this earlier agrarian revolution [050-121].

Cotton transformed both agriculture and manufacturing. Grown in the north in summer alongside winter wheat, raw cotton was transported to the industrial centre of the [[yangtze-river|Yangtze]] valley and re-exported as cotton cloth [050-148]. Shanghai began to emerge as a centre of cotton textile production, with factories operating forty or more looms [050-088]. A system of middlemen bought raw materials from producers and sold to factories [050-094].

The most dramatic chapter in Ming economic history was the maritime expeditions of the eunuch admiral [[cheng-ho|Cheng Ho]]:

| Expedition | Years | Scope |
|-----------|-------|-------|
| First voyage | 1405-1407 | Indo-China to India |
| Subsequent voyages | 1407-1433 | India, Arabia, East Africa |
| Final extent | — | Coast of Arabia and East Africa |

Yung-lo ordered the fleet built to support land operations in Annam and to renew trade connections interrupted by the Mongol collapse [052-082]. [[cheng-ho|Cheng Ho]]'s ships sailed past Indo-China and ultimately reached the coast of Arabia [052-100]. His account provides an important source on fifteenth-century southern Asia. However, the expeditions were discontinued for several reasons: they were costly state enterprises; foreign goods could be obtained more cheaply if foreign merchants came to China; and by 1415 canal repairs allowed all internal traffic to proceed safely, avoiding Japanese pirates [052-106].

Commerce flourished domestically as well. The Hsin-an merchants, originally grain transporters to the frontier, diversified into silver, salt, rice, cotton, silk, and tea, eventually establishing contacts with smugglers and monopolizing key trades across the empire [050-134]. Ming cities shed their Sung-era ward divisions; business spread throughout urban areas, shops no longer closed at sunset, and guilds developed to exercise local influence on officials [050-188].

## Culture and Society

The Ming was the great age of the Chinese vernacular novel. While the [[mongol|Mongols]] had developed the theatre, the novel may be regarded as the "typical Ming creation" [051-032]. Its roots lay in the oral traditions of story-tellers, and it preserved the structural features of that art: division into chapters corresponding to pauses for collecting money, interspersed poems, and -- most importantly -- composition in everyday language rather than the literary Chinese of the gentry [051-050].

Three masterpieces define Ming fiction: _Shui-hu-chuan_ ("The Story of the River Bank"), c. 1550, which contrasts decadent rulers with idealized middle-class brigands [051-058]; _Hsi-yu-chi_ ("The Westward Journey") by Feng Meng-lung (1574-1645), a satirical novel treating all religions with ironic freedom [051-068]; and _San-kuo yen-i_ ("The Tale of the Three Kingdoms") by Lo Kuan-chung, a romanticized historical novel that became the model for countless imitations [051-080]. The sixteenth century also saw the rise of the sensational and erotic novel, most famously the _Chin-p'ing-mei_, which was mercilessly suppressed by the conservative gentry [051-092].

Drama continued to thrive. Many of the finest Chinese dramas were written under the Ming, and they are still performed today [051-152]. The most famous dramatists were Wang Shih-chen (1526-1590) and T'ang Hsien-tsu (1556-1617), whose _Mu-tan-ting_ ("The Peony Pavilion") remains one of the great love stories of Chinese literature [051-158].

> "The novel may be regarded as the typical Ming creation. Its precursors were the stories of story-tellers centuries ago… It was written in everyday language, not in the language of the gentry." [051-032]

Painting flourished with masters such as Ch'in Ying (c. 1525), known for the Academic Style with turquoise-green ground; T'ang Yin (1470-1523), the painter of elegant women; and Tung Ch'i-ch'ang (1555-1636), famous as a calligraphist and art theorist [051-192]. The Ming is also celebrated for monumental architecture, including the imperial palaces of [[peking|Peking]] and the grand porcelain production -- especially the blue-decorated white-ground porcelain that reached its peak in this period [051-218]. Book printing, now a profitable private enterprise, spread literacy and enabled the rise of the small gentry, but it also brought with it the first systematic censorship rules [051-110].

The philosopher [[wang-yang-ming|Wang Yang-ming]] (1473-1529), member of a prominent gentry family allied to the Yang clique, emerged as the most influential Ming thinker. His political engagement -- he was a figure in the factional struggles that brought down [[liu-chin|Liu Chin]] -- illustrated the deepening entanglement of scholarship, politics, and patronage in the late Ming court [052-182].

## Decline and Fall

Beneath the surface of Ming magnificence, the dynasty was rotting. The transfer of the capital to [[peking|Peking]] created a permanent fiscal burden: the north produced little agricultural surplus, yet enormous quantities of grain had to be transported from the [[yangtze-river|Yangtze]] to feed the court, the bureaucracy, and the frontier armies [049-120]. The pension system for the imperial clan grew unsupportable. The military colony system (_ying_), though effective at colonizing the south, proved inadequate to supply the northern armies, and the merchant grain-transport scheme collapsed when the government substituted cash for salt certificates [050-042].

Factional warfare at court accelerated the decay. The forty years after the Yung-lo emperor's death were filled with increasingly ferocious clique struggles [052-098]. A secret-police headquarters was established in the palace by the eunuchs -- the first dictatorial organ on the road to despotism [052-112]. Under [[liu-chin|Liu Chin]] (c. 1505-1510), over three hundred prominent persons were removed, punished, or murdered, and the revenue of entire provinces was extracted as personal tribute [052-142]. When his house was searched, it contained treasure exceeding China's annual budget: 240,000 bars of gold, 5,000,000 bars of silver, and bushels of precious stones [052-156].

Meanwhile, external pressures mounted. Japanese piracy plagued the eastern and southern coasts from the late fourteenth century onward [049-130]. The coastal defenses proved ineffective; some settlements were moved inland to prevent Chinese collusion with the pirates [049-174]. The construction of a Chinese navy was prevented by continual pirate attacks on shipyard cities [049-180]. Along the northern frontier, the [[mongol|Mongols]] remained a persistent threat. In 1449, the Oirat-Mongol prince Esen captured the young emperor Ying Tsung in battle -- a disaster blamed on the incompetent eunuch [[wang-chen|Wang Chen]] [052-040]. The emperor was eventually ransomed, but the episode revealed the court's dysfunction: the various cliques cared little about their captive ruler [052-050].

| Crisis | Date | Outcome |
|--------|------|---------|
| Ying Tsung captured by Oirat [[mongol|Mongols]] | 1449 | Emperor ransomed; political chaos ensues |
| [[liu-chin|Liu Chin]] eunuch dictatorship | 1505-1510 | Eunuch executed; clique warfare continues |
| Japanese pirate raids | 1387-1467 | Coastal fortifications built; navy abandoned |
| Peasant rebellions (Li Tzu-ch'eng) | 1630s-1644 | [[peking|Peking]] falls; last emperor commits suicide |
| Manchu conquest | 1644 | Ming replaced by Ch'ing dynasty |

The end came with shocking speed. Li Tzu-ch'eng's rebel army broke through to [[peking|Peking]]. The city had a strong garrison, but disorganized commanders working against each other and unpaid soldiers without fighting spirit could not hold it [053-086]. On April 24, 1644, the city fell, and the last Ming emperor hanged himself on the "Coal Hill" at [[peking|Peking]] [001-249].

Li Tzu-ch'eng became emperor but proved incapable of building a government [053-102]. General [[wu-san-kui|Wu San-kui]], commanding the Manchu frontier, negotiated an alliance with the Manchu prince Dorgon, entered [[peking|Peking]] on June 6, 1644, and the Manchus established themselves as the new ruling dynasty -- the Ch'ing [053-126]. Ming princes continued to rule in parts of South China for some years, but the gentry, disillusioned by decades of Ming misrule, offered little support [053-150]. Nanking, where a Ming prince held out, fell to the Manchus in 1645. The last Ming pretender fled into Burma, where he was delivered to the Manchu general [[wu-san-kui|Wu San-kui]] and executed in 1661 [055-054].
