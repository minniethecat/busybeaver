## Overview

[[feng-yu-hsiang|Fêng Yü-hsiang]] (1882–1948) was one of the most colorful and unpredictable military leaders of China's [[republican-china|Republican era]]. Nicknamed the "Christian General" for his unusual practice of baptizing his troops with a fire hose, he commanded the Kuominchün (National People's Army) and controlled a power base stretching across northwestern China during the turbulent warlord period of the 1920s and 1930s.

> The so-called "Christian general", Fêng Yü-hsiang, was among the major commanders contending for power in north China after the death of Yuan Shih-kai. [063-039]

## The Warlord Era

Fêng rose through the ranks of the [[peking|Peiyang]] Army under [[yuan-shih-kai|Yuan Shih-kai]] and emerged as an independent power after Yuan's death in 1916. His forces, based in Shensi and later expanded into Honan and the northwest, were known for their discipline and ideological training — unusual among the often predatory warlord armies of the period.

His baptizing of entire units, though often mocked by foreign observers, reflected a genuine personal commitment to Protestant Christianity that distinguished him from the more secular or traditionally-minded warlords of his generation.

## Alignment with the Kuomintang

In the mid-1920s, Fêng aligned with the [[kuomintang|Kuomintang]] (KMT) and the Northern Expedition:

> Among the commanders — Chiang Kai-shek, Fêng Yü-hsiang, and others — shifting alliances and mutual suspicion characterized the political landscape of the Nationalist era. [063-165]

He participated in the second phase of the Northern Expedition (1928), helping to defeat the northern warlords and nominally unify China under the [[kuomintang|Nationalist]] government. However, his relationship with [[chiang-kai-shek|Chiang Kai-shek]] was never stable: Fêng rebelled against Chiang in the Central Plains War of 1930, was defeated, and spent much of the 1930s in eclipse, though he returned to nominal KMT service during the [[sino-japanese-war|Second Sino-Japanese War]].

## Legacy

Fêng Yü-hsiang's career illustrated the fluidity of warlord politics in Republican China — where ideology, personal ambition, and shifting alliances created a constantly evolving political landscape. His "Christian general" persona, while eccentric, reflected the broader penetration of Western ideas and institutions into Chinese military and political life during the early twentieth century. After 1949, he was posthumously honored by the [[china|People's Republic]] for his later cooperation with left-wing forces, a rare distinction for a former warlord.
