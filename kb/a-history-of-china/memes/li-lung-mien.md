## Overview

Li Lung-mien (ca. 1040–1106), also known by his formal name Li Gonglin, was one of the most celebrated painters of the Northern [[Sung Dynasty|Sung dynasty]]. A [[gentry|scholar-official]] who excelled in both calligraphy and painting, he was particularly renowned for his depictions of horses, figure subjects, and Buddhist themes. Li Lung-mien exemplified the [[Literati Painting|literati painting]] ideal that emerged during the Sung period, valuing expressive brushwork and scholarly cultivation over mere technical precision.

## Life and Background

Li Lung-mien came from a scholarly family in the [[yangtze-river|Yangtze]] region. He pursued the conventional career path of a Sung gentleman, passing the civil service examinations and holding various official posts. His administrative career, however, was secondary to his artistic pursuits. Like many Sung [[gentry|literati]], he regarded painting not as a professional trade but as a cultivated accomplishment — a means of personal expression and social communication among educated men.

> "most famous among them, Li Lung-mien (ca. 1040-1106), for instance" (044-092)

His position within the Sung elite gave him access to the imperial collections and the circles of connoisseurship that shaped Sung artistic taste. He was able to study ancient masterpieces firsthand and to exchange ideas with the leading intellectuals of his day.

## Artistic Achievement

Li Lung-mien's most celebrated works were his horse paintings. The horse held a particular significance in Chinese art — a symbol of power, speed, and the relationship between the cultivated man and the natural world:

> "Horse-training. Painting by Li Lung-mien. Late Sung period" (001-244)
> "Horse-training. Painting by Li Lung-mien. Late Sung" (045-059)

His horse paintings were distinguished by their combination of anatomical precision and expressive line. Unlike the detailed, coloured depictions favoured by professional court painters, Li Lung-mien worked primarily in monochrome ink, using the calligraphic line — the *pai-miao* (plain outline) technique — to capture form and movement with seeming effortlessness.

Beyond equine subjects, Li Lung-mien produced figure paintings, landscapes, and Buddhist subjects. His figure style drew on the precedent of the T'ang master Wu Tao-tzu but refined it with a distinctly Sung sensibility: more restrained, more introspective, and more attuned to the rhythms of the brush as an extension of the painter's moral and intellectual character.

## Influence and Legacy

Li Lung-mien's approach to painting — scholarly, calligraphic, and emotionally restrained — became a foundational model for the [[gentry|literati]] tradition that would dominate Chinese painting theory for the next nine centuries. Later critics placed him alongside the calligrapher Mi Fu and the landscapist Li Ch'eng as defining figures of Northern Sung art.

His influence extended beyond his own works. The [[gentry|literati]] ideal that he embodied — that painting should express the inner character of the painter, not merely reproduce external appearances — reshaped Chinese aesthetic philosophy. Successive generations of scholar-painters, from the Yüan masters to the Ming and Ch'ing individualists, acknowledged their debt to the Sung [[gentry|literati]] tradition that Li Lung-mien helped establish.
