## Overview

**Chu Yüan-chang** (T'ai Tsu; r. 1368–1398) was the founder of the [[ming-dynasty|Ming dynasty]], one of the most remarkable figures in Chinese history for his rise from extreme poverty to imperial power. Born the son of a tenant peasant and orphaned by plague, he became a Buddhist monk, then a rebel leader, and ultimately the emperor who drove out the [[mongol|Mongol]] Yuan regime and reunified China under native rule.

## Biography

### Early Life and Rise to Power

Chu Yüan-chang was born into destitution. After losing his entire family to plague, he entered a Buddhist monastery — a common path of survival for the dispossessed:

> In 1353 Kuo was joined by a man named Chu Yüan-chang, the son of a small peasant, probably a tenant farmer. Chu's parents and all his relatives had died from a plague, after which he had first entered a monastery and become a monk. This was a favourite resource — and has been almost to the present day — for men without means. [048-031] [048-032] [048-033]

### The Peasant Rising

Chu's rebellion began as a social movement rooted in economic grievances rather than anti-Mongol nationalism:

> The rising under Chu Yüan-chang, which steadily gained impetus, was at first a purely social movement; Chu was of the humblest origin; he became a monk and a peasant leader at one and the same time. [049-038] [049-040]

His movement drew strength from the widespread popular risings during the latter half of Mongol rule:

> The many popular risings during the latter half of the period of Mongol rule in China were all of a purely economic and social character, and at first they were not directed at all against the Mongols as representatives of an alien people. [049-035]

### Founding the Ming Dynasty

In 1368, Chu captured [[peking|Peking]] and proclaimed the Ming dynasty:

> At the time when Chu Yüan-chang conquered Peking, in 1368, becoming the recognized emperor of China (Ming dynasty), it seemed as though he would inaugurate a policy of social reform. [049-137]

His early legislation targeted the wealthy, but the fiscal demands of maintaining his military tempered his reformist instincts:

> The emperor depended on this wealthy class for the financing of his great armies, and so could not be too hard on it. [049-155]

### Social and Political Constraints

Despite his peasant origins, Chu and his advisors could not escape the gravitational pull of the existing social order:

> Chu Yüan-chang and his entourage were also unable to free themselves entirely from the existing social system. Neither Chu, nor anybody else before and long after him discussed the possibility of a form of land reform. [049-156] [049-157]

### Court Politics and Succession

After consolidating power, Chu faced the perennial challenge of dynastic succession:

> After the founding of the dynasty by Chu Yüan-chang, important questions had to be dealt with apart from the social legislation. What was to be the attitude towards the gentry? Chu, like many revolutionaries before and after him, recognized that these people had been serviceable in the past and would be indispensable in the future. [051-161] [051-163]

In his final years, rivalry among his sons shaped the succession:

> In Chu Yüan-chang's last years (he was named T'ai Tsu as emperor) there were tensions at court. The heir to the throne died in 1391; and when the emperor himself died in 1398, the son of the late heir-apparent was installed as emperor (Hui Ti, 1399–1402). [051-180] [051-182]

This arrangement was soon contested by Chu's son, the prince of [[peking|Peking]], who marched south with his army, overthrew [[hsiung-nu|Hu]]i Ti, and became the [[yung-lo|Yung-lo Emperor]]. The Ming dynasty Chu founded would rule China for nearly three centuries.
