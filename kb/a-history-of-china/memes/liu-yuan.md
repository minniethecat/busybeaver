## Overview

[[liu-yuan|Liu Yüan]] (d. 310) was the [[hsiung-nu|Hsiung-nu]] leader who, in A.D. 304, became the first non-Chinese ruler to establish an independent dynastic state on the soil of northern China after the collapse of the Western [[chin-dynasty|Chin dynasty]]. A grandson of the southern [[hsiung-nu|Hsiung-nu]] shan-yü, Liu Yüan had been raised at the Chinese court as a hostage-prince and received a thorough classical education. When the Chin empire disintegrated in the "Rebellion of the Eight Princes," he seized the opportunity to rally the [[hsiung-nu|Hsiung-nu]] and proclaim himself king — and later emperor — of a restored Han dynasty [024-072].

## Rise to Power

The [[hsiung-nu|Hsiung-nu]] had been settled within the frontiers of the Chinese empire since the first century A.D., and by the late third century their leaders had become increasingly sinicised. Liu Yüan's own grandfather, the shan-yü Yü-fu-lo, had fought as an ally of the Later Han; his father, Liu Pao, held the Chinese title of "Duke of the [[hsiung-nu|Hsiung-nu]]." Liu Yüan himself was sent as a youth to the Chin capital at Loyang, where he studied the Confucian classics, mastered Chinese calligraphy, and earned the respect of Chin courtiers — all while remaining, in the eyes of his own people, their natural hereditary leader [024-166].

> Their leader in A.D. 287, Liu Yüan, was one of those Hsiung-nu nobles who had grown up at the Chinese court. [024-072]

When civil war erupted among the Chin princes in the 290s, the [[hsiung-nu|Hsiung-nu]] nobles gathered in secret council and elected Liu Yüan as their supreme chieftain, reviving the ancient title of shan-yü. In 304, Liu went further: he proclaimed himself King of Han, deliberately adopting the dynastic name of the great Chinese empire that had first opened relations with the [[hsiung-nu|Hsiung-nu]] five centuries earlier [024-172].

## The "Han Dynasty" of the Huns

Liu Yüan's choice of the dynastic name "Han" was a calculated act of political theatre. By claiming descent through a Han princess given in marriage to a [[hsiung-nu|Hsiung-nu]] shan-yü, he argued that he was not a foreign conqueror but a legitimate heir restoring the Han imperial line — a line the Chin had usurped. He went so far as to perform the imperial sacrifices to Heaven and to the ancestors of the Han ruling house [024-173].

> They called Liu Yüan's dynasty the "Han dynasty," arguing that the Hsiung-nu had entered into a brother relationship with the first Han emperor and that the Chinese name Liu was therefore their legitimate family name. [024-173]

The symbolic programme worked. Liu Yüan attracted not only [[hsiung-nu|Hsiung-nu]] warriors but also significant numbers of Chinese frontier commanders, disaffected gentry, and bandit leaders who saw in his multi-ethnic state a more promising vehicle than the crumbling Chin. He surrounded himself with a court that mixed [[hsiung-nu|Hsiung-nu]] nobles and Chinese [[gentry|literati]], consciously replicating the institutional forms of a Confucian monarchy [025-001].

> Thus the Hun Liu Yüan had no intention of restoring the old shan-yü system or of pressing southward as a nomadic raider; he planned instead to establish a regular Chinese-style administration in the territories he controlled. [025-001]

## Military Campaigns and Death

From his base in southern Shansi, Liu Yüan launched a series of campaigns against the Chin, seizing the fortified city of P'ing-yang and advancing to the Yellow River. His forces twice besieged the Chin capital at Loyang, though the city held out. Liu Yüan died in 310, shortly after these campaigns, leaving his state — now styled the "Earlier Chao" — to his son Liu Ts'ung [025-015].

> Meanwhile Liu Yüan provided himself with court ceremonials and a bureaucracy; he died in the following year, and his son Liu Ts'ung succeeded him. [025-010][025-015]

His most formidable lieutenant, the former slave [[shih-lo|Shih Lo]], would go on to eclipse Liu Yüan's heirs and found his own dynasty. Yet it was Liu Yüan who had first demonstrated that a non-Chinese ruler could command the allegiances necessary to sustain a Chinese-style imperial state — a precedent that would shape the politics of the following three centuries [025-033].
