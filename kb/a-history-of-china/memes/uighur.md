## Overview

The Uighurs (also rendered as Uyghurs or Hui-hu) were a [[tang-dynasty|Turkish]] people who played a pivotal role in Chinese history from the seventh through the fourteenth centuries. Originally a tribal confederation on the [[mongol|Mongolian]] steppe, they rose to prominence as indispensable military allies of the [[tang-dynasty|T'ang dynasty]], helping to suppress the catastrophic [[an-lu-shan-rebellion|An Lu-shan rebellion]] and thereby preserving the dynasty for another century and a half. In return for their services, the Uighurs extracted enormous payments in silk, gained preferential trading rights, and secured marriage alliances with the imperial house. Eberhard identifies the Uighurs as one of the key foreign peoples whose interactions with China shaped the course of the T'ang period and beyond [001-135].

The Uighurs were distinctive among the steppe peoples for adopting [[buddhism|Manichaeism]] as their state religion, a choice that set them apart from the Buddhist, Nestorian Christian, and shamanist traditions of their neighbours. Following the collapse of their steppe empire in the mid-ninth century, Uighur diaspora communities dispersed across Central Asia and northern China, where their literacy and administrative skills made them valued clerks and merchants under the [[mongol|Mongol Empire]]. The modern Uyghur people of Xinjiang are their cultural and linguistic descendants.

## Origins and Early History

The Uighurs emerged as one of the major Turkish tribal confederations on the eastern Eurasian steppe. They were part of the broader Turkish-speaking world that stretched from the borders of China to Central Asia, and the title of "khan" was widely accepted among the Uighurs as it was among the Tibetans and Sogdians [035-101]. The Uighurs formed part of a Turkish federation that Eberhard lists alongside other Turkish peoples who interacted with China across many centuries [074-371].

The Uighurs are identified in Chinese sources as the Hui-ho or Hui-hu, terms used interchangeably in historical records to denote the same people [070-168]. Their early political organization followed the typical steppe pattern of loose tribal confederation under a supreme khan, with military prowess in mounted archery forming the basis of their power.

In the early T'ang period, the Uighurs were one among several Turkish groups contending for dominance on the steppe. The Chinese court maintained a complex diplomatic balancing act among these peoples, seeking to prevent any single confederation from becoming powerful enough to threaten the empire's northern frontiers. Eberhard notes that the T'ang dynasty's foreign policy was "mainly concerned with the Turks and Turkestan" [036-001], and the Uighurs were central to this strategic calculus.

## Role in the An Lu-shan Rebellion

The Uighurs' most consequential intervention in Chinese history came during the [[an-lu-shan|An Lu-shan]] rebellion (755--763 CE). When the rebellion of the Sogdian-Turkish general [[an-lu-shan|An Lu-shan]] threatened to overthrow the T'ang dynasty, the imperial court turned to the Uighurs for military assistance. As Eberhard records, the T'ang emperor was "able to rely on the Uighurs; above all, they were furnished with Uighur cavalry in substantial strength" [036-064].

> "An Lu-shan was defeated by the Uighurs; shortly afterwards he was murdered by one of his own eunuchs." [037-176]

The Uighur intervention proved decisive. In a great battle near the capital Ch'ang-an at the end of 757, Uighur cavalry routed the rebel forces [037-174]. The T'ang court paid handsomely for this service: the Uighurs received 10,000 rolls of silk as an immediate reward, an annual tribute of 20,000 rolls of silk, and most significantly, "the Uighur khan was given a daughter of the emperor" in marriage, cementing a political alliance between the two courts [037-180].

The Uighurs were called upon a second time when [[an-lu-shan|An Lu-shan]]'s successor, the Turk Shih Ssu-ming, revived the rebellion and recaptured the eastern capital of Loyang. The T'ang "once more made use of the Uighurs to bring him down" [037-183]. Under the leadership of P'u-ku [[hsiung-nu|Hu]]ai-en, a general of Tolos origin, the Uighur forces advanced from the north in coordination with T'ang armies [038-037].

However, the alliance came at a steep price. Eberhard observes that "friendship with the Uighurs had to be paid for this time" [038-044], and the dynasty's dependence on foreign auxiliaries set a dangerous precedent. The cost of Uighur military support drained the imperial treasury and contributed to the fiscal crises that plagued the later T'ang period.

> "In the battle near the capital, An Lu-shan was defeated by the Uighurs; shortly afterwards he was murdered by one of his own eunuchs." [037-175]

## Religion and Culture

The Uighurs were notable among steppe peoples for their adoption of Manichaeism as a state religion. Manichaeism, founded by the Persian prophet Mani in the third century CE, was a dualistic faith that combined elements of Zoroastrianism, Christianity, and Buddhism [009-092]. It reached the Uighurs through Sogdian merchants and missionaries who travelled the Silk Road trade networks.

In 762, Tengri, the khan of the Uighurs, formally embraced Manichaeism, and the religion became closely identified with Uighur political identity thereafter [038-028]. This religious choice had diplomatic consequences: when the Uighurs' power waned in the ninth century, the T'ang government "attacked the religion of the Uighurs, Manichaeism" as a means of diminishing their influence [038-094].

The Uighurs also developed a sophisticated literate culture. Chinese texts were translated "into the Uighur and other Turkish tongues," indicating a high level of bilingualism and cultural exchange [037-020]. The Uighur script, derived from Sogdian writing, later became the basis for the [[mongol|Mongolian]] script and, through it, the Manchu script, making the Uighurs one of the most important transmitters of literacy across Inner Asia.

## Decline and Fragmentation

The Uighur steppe empire began to disintegrate in the ninth century. Eberhard notes that among the factors that "belittled" Uighur power was "the break-up of the Uighurs (from 832 onward)" [038-084]. This fragmentation was driven by a combination of internal rivalries, pressure from the rising Kirghiz confederation to the north, and the erosion of their privileged economic relationship with the T'ang court.

As their steppe power collapsed, Uighur diaspora communities scattered across Central Asia and northern China. Some "placed themselves under the Uighurs living in China, in order to enjoy the political protection of the Uighur embassy" [038-087]. But this arrangement could not last: by the end of the ninth century, Eberhard records simply that "the Uighurs no longer counted" as a significant political force [038-089].

The Uighur collapse coincided with and contributed to the broader decline of T'ang central authority. The disappearance of a strong Uighur buffer state on the northern frontier exposed China to new threats from other steppe peoples, even as the T'ang dynasty's own military and fiscal capacity continued to erode.

## Uighurs under the Mongol Empire

Despite their political eclipse, the Uighurs retained a significant cultural and administrative role in Inner Asia. When the [[mongol|Mongols]] built their vast empire in the thirteenth century, they found the literate and commercially sophisticated Uighurs to be indispensable auxiliaries.

Eberhard records that Uighurs were prominent among the "auxiliaries (Naimans, Uighurs, and various other Turkish peoples)" who served the Mongol khans [046-172]. Uighur merchants conducted trade across the empire, and "many Uighurs were also employed as clerks" in the Mongol administration [046-182]. Their literacy in multiple languages -- Uighuric, [[mongol|Mongolian]], and Chinese -- made them ideal intermediaries. Eberhard adds that "the clever Uighurs quickly" adapted to the Mongol system and became essential to its functioning [046-184].

The Uighur language, moreover, served as a lingua franca across the Mongol domains, alongside [[mongol|Mongolian]] and Chinese [046-188]. Uighur merchants were among the many foreign traders who operated in Mongol China, and "a few of these merchants were Uighurs and Mohammedans" [046-181]. Under Mongol protection, Uighur communities were "permitted to set up a temple" in China, reflecting the religious tolerance that characterized Mongol rule [056-054].

## Legacy

The Uighur legacy spans military, cultural, and administrative domains. As cavalry allies, they preserved the T'ang dynasty at its moment of greatest peril. As cultural transmitters, their script became the foundation of literacy for the Mongol and Manchu empires. As administrators and merchants, they bridged the Chinese, Turkish, and [[mongol|Mongolian]] worlds across centuries of Inner Asian history.

The Uighur relationship with China established a recurring pattern in Chinese foreign relations: the use of "barbarian against barbarian" to maintain security on the northern frontier. This strategy, though effective in the short term, created dangerous dependencies -- the Uighurs, like later alien auxiliaries, could not be controlled once their services had been secured. The enormous cost of their alliance contributed to the fiscal exhaustion of the T'ang state, even as their military assistance proved decisive in crushing the [[an-lu-shan|An Lu-shan]] rebellion.

The modern Uyghur people of Xinjiang, though separated from the medieval Uighurs by centuries of ethnic mixing and cultural change, trace their identity to this historic people. The Uighur name endures as a marker of one of the most durable Turkish-speaking cultures of Inner Asia, a people who moved from the steppe to the scribe's desk without losing their distinctive identity.
