## Overview

The [[Yung-lo]] Emperor (r. 1403–1424), born Chu Ti, was the third ruler of the [[ming-dynasty|Ming dynasty]] and one of its most ambitious and controversial sovereigns. After seizing the throne through a violent coup against his nephew, he transferred the imperial capital from Nanking to [[peking|Peking]], where it remained for the rest of the Ming epoch [051-190]. His reign, characterised by unprecedented luxury, absolutism, and outward expansion, also produced the celebrated naval expeditions of [[cheng-ho|Cheng Ho]] and the monumental _Yung-lo ta-tien_ encyclopaedia [053-079].

## Biography

Chu Ti was a son of the Ming founder [[chu-yuan-chang|Chu Yüan-chang]], serving as king in [[peking|Peking]] with a strong army at his command [051-186]. When his father died in 1398 and the throne passed to his young nephew [[hsiung-nu|Hu]]i Ti, Chu Ti marched south, captured Nanking, and burned the imperial palaces. After a great massacre of the young emperor's supporters, he declared himself emperor, taking the reign name Yung-lo [051-189].

> This prince, Ch'eng Tsu, marched south and captured Nanking, where the palaces were burnt down. There was a great massacre of supporters of the young emperor, and the victor made himself emperor (better known under his reign name, Yung-lo). As he had established himself in Peking, he transferred the capital to Peking, where it remained throughout the Ming epoch. [051-188–051-192]

The transfer of the capital to the north — the result of the victory of the military party and their Buddhist allies — created a lasting structural tension. The north was of military importance, but the [[yangtze-river|Yangtze]] region remained the economic centre of the country, and the interests of the southern gentry were permanently injured by the move [052-001].

## Reign and Governance

Yung-lo lived in the actual palaces of the Mongol rulers and revived all the luxury of the Mongol epoch, making his reign the most magnificent period of the Ming dynasty. Beneath the surface splendour, however, decay had already begun [052-008]. His style of governance represented unmitigated absolutism: a political and military advisor — significantly, a Buddhist monk — famously declared, "I know the way of heaven. Why discuss the hearts of the people?" [052-012]

Yung-lo pursued an aggressive policy of southward expansion. He sent armies to Yünnan and Indo-China and, recognising that Chinese trade was suffering continual disturbance from Japanese piracy, commissioned the eunuch admiral [[cheng-ho|Cheng Ho]] to build a fleet and undertake missions to southern Asia [052-020]. These expeditions, beginning in 1405, reached the coast of Arabia and briefly established China as a recognised naval power throughout the region [052-029].

> Yung-lo considered that the time had come to annex these regions to China and so to open a new field for Chinese trade, which was suffering continual disturbance from the Japanese. He sent armies to Yünnan and Indo-China; at the same time he had a fleet built by one of his eunuchs, Cheng Ho. [052-020–052-024]

## Cultural Patronage

The emperor favoured the monk Tao-yen (c. 1338–1418), who combined Buddhist and Taoist training and proposed a philosophy stressing intuition [053-076]. Tao-yen oversaw the compilation of the largest encyclopaedia ever written — the _Yung-lo ta-tien_ — commissioned by Yung-lo himself [053-078]. Much of this vast work was later lost, with only fragments surviving into modern times [072-071].

## Legacy

Yung-lo's reign set the pattern for Ming absolutism and permanently shifted China's political geography northward to [[peking|Peking]]. His naval programme, though abandoned by his successors after the canals were repaired in 1411–1415, left a cultural imprint through the fairy tales and stories brought to China from the Middle East and Persia by [[cheng-ho|Cheng Ho]]'s fleet [052-061]. His successor died early, and the dynasty soon fell into the power struggles between cliques that would define its later history [052-076].
