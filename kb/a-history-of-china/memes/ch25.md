# Section 25

[025-001] Thus the Hun Liu Yüan had no intention of restoring the old empire of

[025-002] Mao Tun, the empire of the nomads; he intended to become emperor of

[025-003] China, emperor of a country of farmers. In this lay the fundamental

[025-004] difference between the earlier Hun empire and this new one. The question

[025-005] whether the Huns should join in the struggle for the Chinese imperial

[025-006] throne was therefore decided among the Huns themselves in 304 in the

[025-007] affirmative, by the founding of the "Hun Han dynasty". All that remained

[025-008] was the practical question of how to hold out with their small army of

[025-009] 50,000 men if serious opposition should be offered to the "barbarians".

[025-010] Meanwhile Liu Yüan provided himself with court ceremonial on the Chinese

[025-011] model, in a capital which, after several changes, was established at

[025-012] P'ing-ch'êng in southern Shansi. He attracted more and more of the

[025-013] Chinese gentry, who were glad to come to this still rather barbaric but

[025-014] well-organized court. In 309 the first attack was made on the Chinese

[025-015] capital, Loyang. Liu Yüan died in the following year, and in 311, under

[025-016] his successor Liu Ts'ung (310-318), the attack was renewed and Loyang

[025-017] fell. The Chin emperor, Huai Ti, was captured and kept a prisoner in

[025-018] P'ing-ch'êng until in 313 a conspiracy in his favour was brought to

[025-019] light in the Hun empire, and he and all his supporters were killed.

[025-020] Meanwhile the Chinese clique of the Chin dynasty had hastened to make a

[025-021] prince emperor in the second capital, Ch'ang-an (Min Ti, 313-316) while

[025-022] the princes' struggles for the throne continued. Nobody troubled about

[025-023] the fate of the unfortunate emperor in his capital. He received no

[025-024] reinforcements, so that he was helpless in face of the next attack of

[025-025] the Huns, and in 316 he was compelled to surrender like his predecessor.

[025-026] Now the Hun Han dynasty held both capitals, which meant virtually the

[025-027] whole of the western part of North China, and the so-called "Western

[025-028] Chin dynasty" thus came to its end. Its princes and generals and many of

[025-029] its gentry became landless and homeless and had to flee into the south.

[025-030] (C) The alien empires in North China, down to the Toba (A.D. 317-385)

[025-031] 1 _The Later Chao dynasty in eastern North China (Hun_; 329-352)

[025-032] At this time the eastern part of North China was entirely in the hands

[025-033] of Shih Lo, a former follower of Liu Yüan. Shih Lo had escaped from

[025-034] slavery in China and had risen to be a military leader among

[025-035] detribalized Huns. In 310 he had not only undertaken a great campaign

[025-036] right across China to the south, but had slaughtered more than 100,000

[025-037] Chinese, including forty-eight princes of the Chin dynasty, who had

[025-038] formed a vast burial procession for a prince. This achievement added

[025-039] considerably to Shih Lo's power, and his relations with Liu Ts'ung,

[025-040] already tense, became still more so. Liu Yüan had tried to organize the

[025-041] Hun state on the Chinese model, intending in this way to gain efficient

[025-042] control of China; Shih Lo rejected Chinese methods, and held to the old

[025-043] warrior-nomad tradition, making raids with the aid of nomad fighters. He

[025-044] did not contemplate holding the territories of central and southern

[025-045] China which he had conquered; he withdrew, and in the two years 314-315

[025-046] he contented himself with bringing considerable expanses in

[025-047] north-eastern China, especially territories of the Hsien-pi, under his

[025-048] direct rule, as a base for further raids. Many Huns in Liu Ts'ung's

[025-049] dominion found Shih Lo's method of rule more to their taste than living

[025-050] in a state ruled by officials, and they went over to Shih Lo and joined

[025-051] him in breaking entirely with Liu Ts'ung. There was a further motive for

[025-052] this: in states founded by nomads, with a federation of tribes as their

[025-053] basis, the personal qualities of the ruler played an important part. The

[025-054] chiefs of the various tribes would not give unqualified allegiance to

[025-055] the son of a dead ruler unless the son was a strong personality or gave

[025-056] promise of becoming one. Failing that, there would be independence

[025-057] movements. Liu Ts'ung did not possess the indisputable charisma of his

[025-058] predecessor Liu Yüan; and the Huns looked with contempt on his court

[025-059] splendour, which could only have been justified if he had conquered all

[025-060] China. Liu Ts'ung had no such ambition; nor had his successor Liu Yao

[025-061] (319-329), who gave the Hun Han dynasty retroactively, from its start

[025-062] with Liu Yüan, the new name of "Earlier Chao dynasty" (304-329). Many

[025-063] tribes then went over to Shih Lo, and the remainder of Liu Yao's empire

[025-064] was reduced to a precarious existence. In 329 the whole of it was

[025-065] annexed by Shih Lo.

[025-066] Although Shih Lo had long been much more powerful than the emperors of

[025-067] the "Earlier Chao dynasty", until their removal he had not ventured to

[025-068] assume the title of emperor. The reason for this seems to have lain in

[025-069] the conceptions of nobility held by the Turkish peoples in general and

[025-070] the Huns in particular, according to which only those could become

[025-071] _shan-yü_ (or, later, emperor) who could show descent from the Tu-ku

[025-072] tribe the rightful _shan-yü_ stock. In accordance with this conception,

[025-073] all later Hun dynasties deliberately disowned Shih Lo. For Shih Lo,

[025-074] after his destruction of Liu Yao, no longer hesitated: ex-slave as he

[025-075] was, and descended from one of the non-noble stocks of the Huns, he made

[025-076] himself emperor of the "Later Chao dynasty" (329-352).

[025-077] Shih Lo was a forceful army commander, but he was a man without

[025-078] statesmanship, and without the culture of his day. He had no Chinese

[025-079] education; he hated the Chinese and would have been glad to make north

[025-080] China a grazing ground for his nomad tribes of Huns. Accordingly he had

[025-081] no desire to rule all China. The part already subjugated, embracing the

[025-082] whole of north China with the exception of the present province of

[025-083] Kansu, sufficed for his purpose.

[025-084] The governor of that province was a loyal subject of the Chinese Chin

[025-085] dynasty, a man famous for his good administration, and himself a

[025-086] Chinese. After the execution of the Chin emperor Huai Ti by the Huns in

[025-087] 313, he regarded himself as no longer bound to the central government;

[025-088] he made himself independent and founded the "Earlier Liang dynasty",

[025-089] which was to last until 376. This mainly Chinese realm was not very

[025-090] large, although it had admitted a broad stream of Chinese emigrants from

[025-091] the dissolving Chin empire; but economically the Liang realm was very

[025-092] prosperous, so that it was able to extend its influence as far as

[025-093] Turkestan. During the earlier struggles Turkestan had been virtually in

[025-094] isolation, but now new contacts began to be established. Many traders

[025-095] from Turkestan set up branches in Liang. In the capital there were whole

[025-096] quarters inhabited only by aliens from western and eastern Turkestan and

[025-097] from India. With the traders came Buddhist monks; trade and Buddhism

[025-098] seemed to be closely associated everywhere. In the trading centres

[025-099] monasteries were installed in the form of blocks of houses within strong

[025-100] walls that successfully resisted many an attack. Consequently the

[025-101] Buddhists were able to serve as bankers for the merchants, who deposited

[025-102] their money in the monasteries, which made a charge for its custody; the

[025-103] merchants also warehoused their goods in the monasteries. Sometimes the

[025-104] process was reversed, a trade centre being formed around an existing

[025-105] monastery. In this case the monastery also served as a hostel for the

[025-106] merchants. Economically this Chinese state in Kansu was much more like a

[025-107] Turkestan city state that lived by commerce than the agrarian states of

[025-108] the Far East, although agriculture was also pursued under the Earlier

[025-109] Liang.

[025-110] From this trip to the remote west we will return first to the Hun

[025-111] capital. From 329 onward Shih Lo possessed a wide empire, but an

[025-112] unstable one. He himself felt at all times insecure, because the Huns

[025-113] regarded him, on account of his humble origin, as a "revolutionary". He

[025-114] exterminated every member of the Liu family, that is to say the old

[025-115] _shan-yü_ family, of whom he could get hold, in order to remove any

[025-116] possible pretender to the throne; but he could not count on the loyalty

[025-117] of the Hun and other Turkish tribes under his rule. During this period

[025-118] not a few Huns went over to the small realm of the Toba; other Hun

[025-119] tribes withdrew entirely from the political scene and lived with their

[025-120] herds as nomad tribes in Shansi and in the Ordos region. The general

[025-121] insecurity undermined the strength of Shih Lo's empire. He died in 333,

[025-122] and there came to the throne, after a short interregnum, another

[025-123] personality of a certain greatness, Shih Hu (334-349). He transferred

[025-124] the capital to the city of Yeh, in northern Honan, where the rulers of

[025-125] the Wei dynasty had reigned. There are many accounts of the magnificence

[025-126] of the court of Yeh. Foreigners, especially Buddhist monks, played a

[025-127] greater part there than Chinese. On the one hand, it was not easy for

[025-128] Shih Hu to gain the active support of the educated Chinese gentry after

[025-129] the murders of Shih Lo and, on the other hand, Shih Hu seems to have

[025-130] understood that foreigners without family and without other relations to

[025-131] the native population, but with special skills, are the most reliable

[025-132] and loyal servants of a ruler. Indeed, his administration seems to have

[025-133] been good, but the regime remained completely parasitic, with no

[025-134] support of the masses or the gentry. After Shih Hu's death there were

[025-135] fearful combats between his sons; ultimately a member of an entirely

[025-136] different family of Hun origin seized power, but was destroyed in 352 by

[025-137] the Hsien-pi, bringing to an end the Later Chao dynasty.

[025-138] 2 _Earlier Yen dynasty in the north-east (proto-Mongol; 352-370), and

[025-139] the Earlier Ch'in dynasty in all north China (Tibetan; 351-394_)

[025-140] In the north, proto-Mongol Hsien-pi tribes had again made themselves

[025-141] independent; in the past they had been subjects of Liu Yüan and then of

[025-142] Shih Lo. A man belonging to one of these tribes, the tribe of the

[025-143] Mu-jung, became the leader of a league of tribes, and in 337 founded the

[025-144] state of Yen. This proto-Mongol state of the Mu-jung, which the

[025-145] historians call the "Earlier Yen" state, conquered parts of southern

[025-146] Manchuria and also the state of Kao-li in Korea, and there began then an

[025-147] immigration of Hsien-pi into Korea, which became noticeable at a later

[025-148] date. The conquest of Korea, which was still, as in the past, a Japanese

[025-149] market and was very wealthy, enormously strengthened the state of Yen.

[025-150] Not until a little later, when Japan's trade relations were diverted to

[025-151] central China, did Korea's importance begin to diminish. Although this

[025-152] "Earlier Yen dynasty" of the Mu-jung officially entered on the heritage

[025-153] of the Huns, and its regime was therefore dated only from 352 (until

[025-154] 370), it failed either to subjugate the whole realm of the "Later Chao"

[025-155] or effectively to strengthen the state it had acquired. This old Hun

[025-156] territory had suffered economically from the anti-agrarian nomad

[025-157] tendency of the last of the Hun emperors; and unremunerative wars

[025-158] against the Chinese in the south had done nothing to improve its

[025-159] position. In addition to this, the realm of the Toba was dangerously

[025-160] gaining strength on the flank of the new empire. But the most dangerous

[025-161] enemy was in the west, on former Hun soil, in the province of

[025-162] Shensi--Tibetans, who finally came forward once more with claims to

[025-163] dominance. These were Tibetans of the P'u family, which later changed

[025-164] its name to Fu. The head of the family had worked his way up as a leader

[025-165] of Tibetan auxiliaries under the "Later Chao", gaining more and more

[025-166] power and following. When under that dynasty the death of Shih Hu marked

[025-167] the beginning of general dissolution, he gathered his Tibetans around

[025-168] him in the west, declared himself independent of the Huns, and made

[025-169] himself emperor of the "Earlier Ch'in dynasty" (351-394). He died in

[025-170] 355, and was followed after a short interregnum by Fu Chien (357-385),

[025-171] who was unquestionably one of the most important figures of the fourth

[025-172] century. This Tibetan empire ultimately defeated the "Earlier Yen

[025-173] dynasty" and annexed the realm of the Mu-jung. Thus the Mu-jung Hsien-pi

[025-174] came under the dominion of the Tibetans; they were distributed among a

[025-175] number of places as garrisons of mounted troops.
