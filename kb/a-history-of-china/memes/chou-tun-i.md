## Overview

[[chou-tun-i|Chou Tun-i]] (1017–1073) was one of the founding figures of [[confucianism|Neo-Confucianism]], the philosophical movement that reshaped Chinese thought during the [[sung-dynasty|Sung dynasty]]. Together with his contemporary [[chang-tsai|Chang Tsai]] (1020–1077), he developed a cosmological framework that could compete with the sophisticated metaphysical systems of Buddhism, which had dominated Chinese intellectual life for centuries.

> Men like Chou Tun-i (1017-1073) and Chang Tsai (1020-1077) developed a cosmological theory which could measure up with Buddhistic cosmology. [044-001] [044-002]

## Philosophical Contributions

Chou Tun-i's most famous work is the *T'ai-chi T'u Shuo* (Explanation of the Diagram of the Supreme Ultimate), a concise metaphysical treatise that synthesized Taoist cosmology with Confucian ethical concerns. His diagram of the Supreme Ultimate (*t'ai-chi*) provided a visual and conceptual model of how the undifferentiated primordial unity gives rise to the duality of *yin* and *yang*, and from there to the myriad things of the world.

This cosmological vision was part of a broader Neo-Confucian attempt to address questions that classical [[confucianism|Confucianism]] had left unanswered — particularly the problem of evil and the relationship between human nature and the cosmos. Where [[confucius|Confucius]] and his early followers had assumed that every person could perfect themselves by overcoming innate evil, the Neo-Confucianists sought to provide a metaphysical grounding for this moral optimism.

> Confucius and his followers had believed that every person could perfect himself by overcoming the evil in him. It was commonly assumed that human nature is good or indifferent, and that human feelings are evil and have to be tamed and educated. [044-005] [044-006] [044-008] [044-009] [044-010]

Chou Tun-i's ideas were later systematized and expanded by [[chu-hsi|Chu Hsi]] (1130–1200), who made them the foundation of the orthodox Neo-Confucian curriculum that dominated Chinese education until the twentieth century.

## Legacy

Chou Tun-i is recognized as one of the "Five Masters" of early [[confucianism|Neo-Confucianism]], alongside [[chang-tsai|Chang Tsai]], [[cheng-hao|Ch'eng Hao]], [[cheng-i|Ch'eng I]], and [[shao-yung|Shao Yung]]. His integration of metaphysical speculation with Confucian moral philosophy set the pattern that all subsequent Neo-Confucian thinkers would follow.
