## Overview

[[hu-shih|Hu Shih]] (1891–1962) was one of the most influential Chinese intellectuals of the twentieth century. A student of John Dewey at Columbia University, he brought American pragmatism to China and applied it to the reform of Chinese language and thought. As a leader of the "Literary Revolution" — the campaign to replace classical literary Chinese with the vernacular in all forms of written communication — he helped to transform the intellectual landscape of modern China. Unlike many of his May Fourth contemporaries, however, Hu Shih remained a committed liberal who refused the turn to Marxism, a choice that would define his complex legacy in both Nationalist and Communist China [062-016][062-018].

## The Literary Revolution

Hu Shih returned to China in 1917 to take up a professorship at the National University of [[peking|Peking]], where he quickly became one of the most visible figures in the burgeoning New Culture Movement. His core argument, set out in a series of articles in [[chen-tu-hsiu|Ch'en Tu-hsiu's]] journal _New Youth_, was deceptively simple: that the classical written language (wen-yen) was a dead medium, inaccessible to the vast majority of Chinese, and that a living national culture required a living national language — the vernacular (pai-hua) [062-016].

> Hu Shih (born 1891), a student of Dewey, tended towards liberalism in politics and pragmatism in philosophy. [062-016]

The campaign succeeded beyond all expectation. Within a few years, the Ministry of Education had mandated the use of vernacular Chinese in primary school textbooks, and a generation of young writers began producing the first modern Chinese literature. Hu Shih himself published a volume of vernacular poems in 1920 — the first such collection by a major scholar — and oversaw the creation of a standardised national grammar [062-018].

> Hu Shih, who was then regarded by the reformers as the man who had put the reform of Chinese thought on the basis of Chinese thought. [062-018]

## The Liberal Alternative

As the New Culture Movement fragmented in the early 1920s, Hu Shih emerged as the leading spokesman for a distinctive liberal-pragmatist position. Against the Marxists, he argued that China's problems were not fundamentally economic but cultural and intellectual — the legacy of centuries of authoritarian Confucian orthodoxy. The solution was not class revolution but the gradual reconstruction of Chinese civilisation through critical inquiry, scientific method, and piecemeal reform [064-165].

> Hu Shih did not move to the left with the others; he remained a liberal. [064-165]

This position brought him into conflict with both the rising communist movement and, eventually, the Nationalist regime of [[chiang-kai-shek|Chiang Kai-shek]]. Though he served briefly as Nationalist China's ambassador to the United States during the Second World War, he remained an independent voice, criticising government censorship and defending academic freedom. After 1949, he lived in exile, first in the United States and then in Taiwan, where he continued to write and teach until his death [068-124][072-193].

## Scholarly Contributions

Beyond his political interventions, Hu Shih was a pioneering scholar of Chinese intellectual history. His doctoral dissertation on the history of Chinese logic, later expanded into _The Development of Logical Method in Ancient China_, applied Deweyan methodology to the analysis of classical texts. He was among the first to subject the Confucian canon to systematic textual criticism, challenging the traditional attributions of authorship and dating — work that influenced a generation of younger scholars including [[chi-chao-ting|Chi Ch'ao-ting]] and the economic historian [[wittfogel|K. A. Wittfogel]] [068-134][070-038].
