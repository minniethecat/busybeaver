# Section 42

[042-001] This Sung dynasty, as Chao K'uang-yin named it, no longer turned against

[042-002] the northern peoples, particularly the Kitan, but against the south.

[042-003] This was not exactly an heroic policy: the north of China remained in

[042-004] the hands of the Kitan. There were frequent clashes, but no real effort

[042-005] was made to destroy the Kitan, whose dynasty was now called "Liao". The

[042-006] second emperor of the Sung was actually heavily defeated several times

[042-007] by the Kitan. But they, for their part, made no attempt to conquer the

[042-008] whole of China, especially since the task would have become more and

[042-009] more burdensome the farther south the Sung expanded. And very soon there

[042-010] were other reasons why the Kitan should refrain from turning their whole

[042-011] strength against the Chinese.

[042-012] [Illustration: 10 Ladies of the Court: clay models which accompanied

[042-013] the dead person to the grave. T'ang period. _In the collection of the

[042-014] Museum für Völkerkunde, Berlin_.]

[042-015] [Illustration: 11 Distinguished founder: a temple banner found at

[042-018] As we said, the Sung turned at once against the states in the south.

[042-019] Some of the many small southern states had made substantial economic and

[042-020] cultural advance, but militarily they were not strong. Chao K'uang-yin

[042-021] (named as emperor T'ai Tsu) attacked them in succession. Most of them

[042-022] fell very quickly and without any heavy fighting, especially since the

[042-023] Sung dealt mildly with the defeated rulers and their following. The

[042-024] gentry and the merchants in these small states could not but realize the

[042-025] advantages of a widened and well-ordered economic field, and they were

[042-026] therefore entirely in favour of the annexation of their country so soon

[042-027] as it proved to be tolerable. And the Sung empire could only endure and

[042-028] gain strength if it had control of the regions along the Yangtze and

[042-029] around Canton, with their great economic resources. The process of

[042-030] absorbing the small states in the south continued until 980. Before it

[042-031] was ended, the Sung tried to extend their influence in the south beyond

[042-032] the Chinese border, and secured a sort of protectorate over parts of

[042-033] Annam (973). This sphere of influence was politically insignificant and

[042-034] not directly of any economic importance; but it fulfilled for the Sung

[042-035] the same functions which colonial territories fulfilled for Europeans,

[042-036] serving as a field of operation for the commercial class, who imported

[042-037] raw materials from it--mainly, it is true, luxury articles such as

[042-038] special sorts of wood, perfumes, ivory, and so on--and exported Chinese

[042-039] manufactures. As the power of the empire grew, this zone of influence

[042-040] extended as far as Indonesia: the process had begun in the T'ang period.

[042-041] The trade with the south had not the deleterious effects of the trade

[042-042] with Central Asia. There was no sale of refined metals, and none of

[042-043] fabrics, as the natives produced their own textiles which sufficed for

[042-044] their needs. And the export of porcelain brought no economic injury to

[042-045] China, but the reverse.

[042-046] This Sung policy was entirely in the interest of the gentry and of the

[042-047] trading community which was now closely connected with them. Undoubtedly

[042-048] it strengthened China. The policy of nonintervention in the north was

[042-049] endurable even when peace with the Kitan had to be bought by the payment

[042-050] of an annual tribute. From 1004 onwards, 100,000 ounces of silver and

[042-051] 200,000 bales of silk were paid annually to the Kitan, amounting in

[042-052] value to about 270,000 strings of cash, each of 1,000 coins. The state

[042-053] budget amounted to some 20,000,000 strings of cash. In 1038 the payments

[042-054] amounted to 500,000 strings, but the budget was by then much larger. One

[042-055] is liable to get a false impression when reading of these big payments

[042-056] if one does not take into account what percentage they formed of the

[042-057] total revenues of the state. The tribute to the Kitan amounted to less

[042-058] than 2 per cent of the revenue, while the expenditure on the army

[042-059] accounted for 25 per cent of the budget. It cost much less to pay

[042-060] tribute than to maintain large armies and go to war. Financial

[042-061] considerations played a great part during the Sung epoch. The taxation

[042-062] revenue of the empire rose rapidly after the pacification of the south;

[042-063] soon after the beginning of the dynasty the state budget was double that

[042-064] of the T'ang. If the state expenditure in the eleventh century had not

[042-065] continually grown through the increase in military expenditure--in spite

[042-066] of everything!--there would have come a period of great prosperity in

[042-067] the empire.

[042-068] 2 _Administration and army. Inflation_

[042-069] The Sung emperor, like the rulers of the transition period, had gained

[042-070] the throne by his personal abilities as military leader; in fact, he had

[042-071] been made emperor by his soldiers as had happened to so many emperors in

[042-072] later Imperial Rome. For the next 300 years we observe a change in the

[042-073] position of the emperor. On the one hand, if he was active and

[042-074] intelligent enough, he exercised much more personal influence than the

[042-075] rulers of the Middle Ages. On the other hand, at the same time, the

[042-076] emperors were much closer to their ministers as before. We hear of

[042-077] ministers who patted the ruler on the shoulders when they retired from

[042-078] an audience; another one fell asleep on the emperor's knee and was not

[042-079] punished for this familiarity. The emperor was called "_kuan-chia_"

[042-080] (Administrator) and even called himself so. And in the early twelfth

[042-081] century an emperor stated "I do not regard the empire as my personal

[042-082] property; my job is to guide the people". Financially-minded as the Sung

[042-083] dynasty was, the cost of the operation of the palace was calculated, so

[042-084] that the emperor had a budget: in 1068 the salaries of all officials in

[042-085] the capital amounted to 40,000 strings of money per month, the armies

[042-086] 100,000, and the emperor's ordinary monthly budget was 70,000 strings.

[042-087] For festivals, imperial birthdays, weddings and burials extra allowances

[042-088] were made. Thus, the Sung rulers may be called "moderate absolutists"

[042-089] and not despots.

[042-090] One of the first acts of the new Sung emperor, in 963, was a fundamental

[042-091] reorganization of the administration of the country. The old system of a

[042-092] civil administration and a military administration independent of it was

[042-093] brought to an end and the whole administration of the country placed in

[042-094] the hands of civil officials. The gentry welcomed this measure and gave

[042-095] it full support, because it enabled the influence of the gentry to grow

[042-096] and removed the fear of competition from the military, some of whom did

[042-097] not belong by birth to the gentry. The generals by whose aid the empire

[042-098] had been created were put on pension, or transferred to civil

[042-099] employment, as quickly as possible. The army was demobilized, and this

[042-100] measure was bound up with the settlement of peasants in the regions

[042-101] which war had depopulated, or on new land. Soon after this the revenue

[042-102] noticeably increased. Above all, the army was placed directly under the

[042-103] central administration, and the system of military governors was thus

[042-104] brought to an end. The soldiers became mercenaries of the state, whereas

[042-105] in the past there had been conscription. In 975 the army had numbered

[042-106] only 378,000, and its cost had not been insupportable. Although the

[042-107] numbers increased greatly, reaching 912,000 in 1017 and 1,259,000 in

[042-108] 1045, this implied no increase in military strength; for men who had

[042-109] once been soldiers remained with the army even when they were too old

[042-110] for service. Moreover, the soldiers grew more and more exacting; when

[042-111] detachments were transferred to another region, for instance, the

[042-112] soldiers would not carry their baggage; an army of porters had to be

[042-113] assembled. The soldiers also refused to go to regions remote from their

[042-114] homes until they were given extra pay. Such allowances gradually became

[042-115] customary, and so the military expenditure grew by leaps and bounds

[042-116] without any corresponding increase in the striking power of the army.

[042-117] The government was unable to meet the whole cost of the army out of

[042-118] taxation revenue. The attempt was made to cover the expenditure by

[042-119] coining fresh money. In connection with the increase in commercial

[042-120] capital described above, and the consequent beginning of an industry,

[042-121] China's metal production had greatly increased. In 1050 thirteen times

[042-122] as much silver, eight times as much copper, and fourteen times as much

[042-123] iron was produced as in 800. Thus the circulation of the copper currency

[042-124] was increased. The cost of minting, however, amounted in China to about

[042-125] 75 per cent and often over 100 per cent of the value of the money

[042-126] coined. In addition to this, the metal was produced in the south, while

[042-127] the capital was in the north. The coin had therefore to be carried a

[042-128] long distance to reach the capital and to be sent on to the soldiers in

[042-129] the north.

[042-130] To meet the increasing expenditure, an unexampled quantity of new money

[042-131] was put into circulation. The state budget increased from 22,200,000 in

[042-132] A.D. 1000 to 150,800,000 in 1021. The Kitan state coined a great deal of

[042-133] silver, and some of the tribute was paid to it in silver. The greatly

[042-134] increased production of silver led to its being put into circulation in

[042-135] China itself. And this provided a new field of speculation, through the

[042-136] variations in the rates for silver and for copper. Speculation was also

[042-137] possible with the deposit certificates, which were issued in quantities

[042-138] by the state from the beginning of the eleventh century, and to which

[042-139] the first true paper money was soon added. The paper money and the

[042-140] certificates were redeemable at a definite date, but at a reduction of

[042-141] at least 3 per cent of their value; this, too, yielded a certain revenue

[042-142] to the state.

[042-143] The inflation that resulted from all these measures brought profit to

[042-144] the big merchants in spite of the fact that they had to supply directly

[042-145] or indirectly all non-agricultural taxes (in 1160 some 40,000,000

[042-146] strings annually), especially the salt tax (50 per cent), wine tax (36

[042-147] per cent), tea tax (7 per cent) and customs (7 per cent). Although the

[042-148] official economic thinking remained Confucian, i.e. anti-business and

[042-149] pro-agrarian, we find in this time insight in price laws, for instance,

[042-150] that peace times and/or decrease of population induce deflation. The

[042-151] government had always attempted to manipulate the prices by

[042-152] interference. Already in much earlier times, again and again, attempts

[042-153] had been made to lower the prices by the so-called "ever-normal

[042-154] granaries" of the government which threw grain on the market when prices

[042-155] were too high and bought grain when prices were low. But now, in

[042-156] addition to such measures, we also find others which exhibit a deeper

[042-157] insight: in a period of starvation, the scholar and official Fan

[042-158] Chung-yen instead of officially reducing grain prices, raised the prices

[042-159] in his district considerably. Although the population got angry,

[042-160] merchants started to import large amounts of grain; as soon as this

[042-161] happened, Fan (himself a big landowner) reduced the price again. Similar

[042-162] results were achieved by others by just stimulating merchants to import

[042-163] grain into deficit areas.

[042-164] With the social structure of medieval Europe, similar financial and

[042-165] fiscal developments which gave new chances to merchants, eventually led

[042-166] to industrial capitalism and industrial society. In China, however, the

[042-167] gentry in their capacity of officials hindered the growth of independent

[042-168] trade, and permitted its existence only in association with themselves.

[042-169] As they also represented landed property, it was in land that the

[042-170] newly-formed capital was invested. Thus we see in the Sung period, and

[042-171] especially in the eleventh century, the greatest accumulation of estates

[042-172] that there had ever been up to then in China.

[042-173] Many of these estates came into origin as gifts of the emperor to

[042-174] individuals or to temples, others were created on hillsides on land

[042-175] which belonged to the villages. From this time on, the rest of the

[042-176] village commons in China proper disappeared. Villagers could no longer

[042-177] use the top-soil of the hills as fertilizer, or the trees as firewood

[042-178] and building material. In addition, the hillside estates diverted the

[042-179] water of springs and creeks, thus damaging severely the irrigation works

[042-180] of the villagers in the plains. The estates (_chuang_) were controlled

[042-181] by appointed managers who often became hereditary managers. The tenants

[042-182] on the estates were quite often non-registered migrants, of whom we

[042-183] spoke previously as "vagrants", and as such they depended upon the

[042-184] managers who could always denounce them to the authorities which would

[042-185] lead to punishment because nobody was allowed to leave his home without

[042-186] officially changing his registration. Many estates operated mills and

[042-187] even textile factories with non-registered weavers. Others seem to have

[042-188] specialized in sheep breeding. Present-day village names ending with

[042-189] -_chuang_ indicate such former estates. A new development in this period

[042-190] were the "clan estates" (_i-chuang_), created by Fan Chung-yen

[042-191] (989-1052) in 1048. The income of these clan estates were used for the

[042-192] benefit of the whole clan, were controlled by clan-appointed managers

[042-193] and had tax-free status, guaranteed by the government which regarded

[042-194] them as welfare institutions. Technically, they might better be called

[042-195] corporations because they were similar in structure to some of our

[042-196] industrial corporations. Under the Chinese economic system, large-scale

[042-197] landowning always proved socially and politically injurious. Up to very

[042-198] recent times the peasant who rented his land paid 40-50 per cent of the

[042-199] produce to the landowner, who was responsible for payment of the normal

[042-200] land tax. The landlord, however, had always found means of evading

[042-201] payment. As each district had to yield a definite amount of taxation,

[042-202] the more the big landowners succeeded in evading payment the more had to

[042-203] be paid by the independent small farmers. These independent peasants

[042-204] could then either "give" their land to the big landowner and pay rent to

[042-205] him, thus escaping from the attentions of the tax-officer, or simply

[042-206] leave the district and secretly enter another one where they were not

[042-207] registered. In either case the government lost taxes.
