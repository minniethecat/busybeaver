# Section 51

[051-001] The Chinese empire was greater than it had been before the Mongol epoch,

[051-002] and the population was also greater, so that more officials were needed.

[051-003] Thus in the Ming epoch there began a certain democratization, larger

[051-004] sections of the population having the opportunity of gaining government

[051-005] positions; but this democratization brought no benefit to the general

[051-006] population but resulted in further exploitation of the peasants.

[051-007] The new "small gentry" did not consist of great families like the

[051-008] original gentry. When, therefore, people of that class wanted to play a

[051-009] political part in the central government, or to gain a position there,

[051-010] they had either to get into close touch with one of the families of the

[051-011] gentry, or to try to approach the emperor directly. In the immediate

[051-012] entourage of the emperor, however, were the eunuchs. A good many members

[051-013] of the new class had themselves castrated after they had passed their

[051-014] state examination. Originally eunuchs were forbidden to acquire

[051-015] education. But soon the Ming emperors used the eunuchs as a tool to

[051-016] counteract the power of gentry cliques and thus to strengthen their

[051-017] personal power. When, later, eunuchs controlled appointments to

[051-018] government posts, long established practices of bureaucratic

[051-019] administration were eliminated and the court, i.e. the emperor and his

[051-020] tools, the eunuchs, could create a rule by way of arbitrary decisions, a

[051-021] despotic rule. For such purposes, eunuchs had to have education, and

[051-022] these new educated eunuchs, when they had once secured a position, were

[051-023] able to gain great influence in the immediate entourage of the emperor;

[051-024] later such educated eunuchs were preferred, especially as many offices

[051-025] were created which were only filled by eunuchs and for which educated

[051-026] eunuchs were needed. Whole departments of eunuchs came into existence at

[051-027] court, and these were soon made use of for confidential business of the

[051-028] emperor's outside the palace.

[051-029] These eunuchs worked, of course, in the interest of their families. On

[051-030] the other hand, they were very ready to accept large bribes from the

[051-031] gentry for placing the desires of people of the gentry before the

[051-032] emperor and gaining his consent. Thus the eunuchs generally accumulated

[051-033] great wealth, which they shared with their small gentry relatives. The

[051-034] rise of the small gentry class was therefore connected with the

[051-035] increased influence of the eunuchs at court.

[051-036] 7 _Literature, art, crafts_

[051-037] The growth of the small gentry which had its stronghold in the

[051-038] provincial towns and cities, as well as the rise of the merchant class

[051-039] and the liberation of the artisans, are reflected in the new literature

[051-040] of Ming time. While the Mongols had developed the theatre, the novel may

[051-041] be regarded as the typical Ming creation. Its precursors were the

[051-042] stories of story-tellers centuries ago. They had developed many styles,

[051-043] one of which, for instance, consisted of prose with intercalated poetic

[051-044] parts (_pien-wen_). Buddhists monks had used these forms of popular

[051-045] literature and spread their teachings in similar forms; due to them,

[051-046] many Indian stories and tales found their way into the Chinese

[051-047] folklore. Soon, these stories of story-tellers or monks were written

[051-048] down, and out of them developed the Chinese classical novel. It

[051-049] preserved many traits of the stories: it was cut into chapters

[051-050] corresponding with the interruptions which the story-teller made in

[051-051] order to collect money; it was interspersed with poems. But most of all,

[051-052] it was written in everyday language, not in the language of the gentry.

[051-053] To this day every Chinese knows and reads with enthusiasm

[051-054] _Shui-hu-chuan_ ("The Story of the River Bank"), probably written about

[051-055] 1550 by Wang Tao-k'un, in which the ruling class was first described in

[051-056] its decay. Against it are held up as ideals representatives of the

[051-057] middle class in the guise of the gentleman brigand. Every Chinese also

[051-058] knows the great satirical novel _Hsi-yu-chi_ ("The Westward Journey"),

[051-059] by Feng Meng-lung (1574-1645), in which ironical treatment is meted out

[051-060] to all religions and sects against a mythological background, with a

[051-061] freedom that would not have been possible earlier. The characters are

[051-062] not presented as individuals but as representatives of human types: the

[051-063] intellectual, the hedonist, the pious man, and the simpleton, are drawn

[051-064] with incomparable skill, with their merits and defects. A third famous

[051-065] novel is _San-kuo yen-i_ ("The Tale of the Three Kingdoms"), by Lo

[051-066] Kuan-chung. Just as the European middle class read with avidity the

[051-067] romances of chivalry, so the comfortable class in China was enthusiastic

[051-068] over romanticized pictures of the struggle of the gentry in the third

[051-069] century. "The Tale of the Three Kingdoms" became the model for countless

[051-070] historical novels of its own and subsequent periods. Later, mainly in

[051-071] the sixteenth century, the sensational and erotic novel developed, most

[051-072] of all in Nanking. It has deeply influenced Japanese writers, but was

[051-073] mercilessly suppressed by the Chinese gentry which resented the

[051-074] frivolity of this wealthy and luxurious urban class of middle or small

[051-075] gentry families who associated with rich merchants, actors, artists and

[051-076] musicians. Censorship of printed books had started almost with the

[051-077] beginning of book printing as a private enterprise: to the famous

[051-078] historian, anti-Buddhist and conservative Ou-yang Hsiu (1007-1072), the

[051-079] enemy of Wang An-shih, belongs the sad glory of having developed the

[051-080] first censorship rules. Since Ming time, it became a permanent feature

[051-081] of Chinese governments.

[051-082] The best known of the erotic novels is the _Chin-p'ing-mei_ which, for

[051-083] reasons of our own censors can be published only in expurgated

[051-084] translations. It was written probably towards the end of the sixteenth

[051-085] century. This novel, as all others, has been written and re-written by

[051-086] many authors, so that many different versions exist. It might be pointed

[051-087] out that many novels were printed in Hui-chou, the commercial centre of

[051-088] the time.

[051-089] The short story which formerly served the entertainment of the educated

[051-090] only and which was, therefore, written in classical Chinese, now also

[051-091] became a literary form appreciated by the middle classes. The collection

[051-092] _Chin-ku ch'i-kuan_ ("Strange Stories of New Times and Old"), compiled

[051-093] by Feng Meng-lung, is the best-known of these collections in vernacular

[051-094] Chinese.

[051-095] Little original work was done in the Ming epoch in the fields generally

[051-096] regarded as "literature" by educated Chinese, those of poetry and the

[051-097] essay. There are some admirable essays, but these are only isolated

[051-098] examples out of thousands. So also with poetry: the poets of the gentry,

[051-099] united in "clubs", chose the poets of the Sung epoch as their models to

[051-100] emulate.

[051-101] The Chinese drama made further progress in the Ming epoch. Many of the

[051-102] finest Chinese dramas were written under the Ming; they are still

[051-103] produced again and again to this day. The most famous dramatists of the

[051-104] Ming epoch are Wang Shih-chen (1526-1590) and T'ang Hsien-tsu

[051-105] (1556-1617). T'ang wrote the well-known drama _Mu-tan-ting_ ("The Peony

[051-106] Pavilion"), one of the finest love-stories of Chinese literature, full

[051-107] of romance and remote from all reality. This is true also of the other

[051-108] dramas by T'ang, especially his "Four Dreams", a series of four plays.

[051-109] In them a man lives in dream through many years of his future life, with

[051-110] the result that he realizes the worthlessness of life and decides to

[051-111] become a monk.

[051-112] Together with the development of the drama (or, rather, the opera) in

[051-113] the Ming epoch went an important endeavour in the modernization of

[051-114] music, the attempt to create a "well-tempered scale" made in 1584 by Chu

[051-115] Tsai-yü. This solved in China a problem which was not tackled till later

[051-116] in Europe. The first Chinese theorists of music who occupied themselves

[051-117] with this problem were Ching Fang (77-37 B.C.) and Ho Ch'êng-t'ien (A.D.

[051-118] 370-447).

[051-119] In the Mongol epoch, most of the Chinese painters had lived in central

[051-120] China; this remained so in the Ming epoch. Of the many painters of the

[051-121] Ming epoch, all held in high esteem in China, mention must be made

[051-122] especially of Ch'in Ying (_c_. 1525), T'ang Yin (1470-1523), and Tung

[051-123] Ch'i-ch'ang (1555-1636). Ch'in Ying painted in the Academic Style,

[051-124] indicating every detail, however small, and showing preference for a

[051-125] turquoise-green ground. T'ang Yin was the painter of elegant women; Tung

[051-126] became famous especially as a calligraphist and a theoretician of the

[051-127] art of painting; a textbook of the art was written by him.

[051-128] Just as puppet plays and shadow theatre are the "opera of the common

[051-129] man" and took a new development in Ming time, the wood-cut and

[051-130] block-printing developed largely as a cheap substitute of real

[051-131] paintings. The new urbanites wanted to have paintings of the masters and

[051-132] found in the wood-cut which soon became a multi-colour print a cheap

[051-133] mass medium. Block printing in colours, developed in the Yangtze valley,

[051-134] was adopted by Japan and found its highest refinement there. But the

[051-135] Ming are also famous for their monumental architecture which largely

[051-136] followed Mongol patterns. Among the most famous examples is the famous

[051-137] Great Wall which had been in dilapidation and was rebuilt; the great

[051-138] city walls of Peking; and large parts of the palaces of Peking, begun in

[051-139] the Mongol epoch. It was at this time that the official style which we

[051-140] may observe to this day in North China was developed, the style employed

[051-141] everywhere, until in the age of concrete it lost its justification.

[051-142] In the Ming epoch the porcelain with blue decoration on a white ground

[051-143] became general; the first examples, from the famous kilns in

[051-144] Ching-te-chen, in the province of Kiangsi, were relatively coarse, but

[051-145] in the fifteenth century the production was much finer. In the sixteenth

[051-146] century the quality deteriorated, owing to the disuse of the cobalt from

[051-147] the Middle East (perhaps from Persia) in favour of Sumatra cobalt, which

[051-148] did not yield the same brilliant colour. In the Ming epoch there also

[051-149] appeared the first brilliant red colour, a product of iron, and a start

[051-150] was then made with three-colour porcelain (with lead glaze) or

[051-151] five-colour (enamel). The many porcelains exported to western Asia and

[051-152] Europe first influenced European ceramics (Delft), and then were

[051-153] imitated in Europe (Böttger); the early European porcelains long showed

[051-154] Chinese influence (the so-called onion pattern, blue on a white ground).

[051-155] In addition to the porcelain of the Ming epoch, of which the finest

[051-156] specimens are in the palace at Istanbul, especially famous are the

[051-157] lacquers (carved lacquer, lacquer painting, gold lacquer) of the Ming

[051-158] epoch and the cloisonné work of the same period. These are closely

[051-159] associated with the contemporary work in Japan.

[051-160] 8 _Politics at court_

[051-161] After the founding of the dynasty by Chu Yüan-chang, important questions

[051-162] had to be dealt with apart from the social legislation. What was to be

[051-163] done, for instance, with Chu's helpers? Chu, like many revolutionaries

[051-164] before and after him, recognized that these people had been serviceable

[051-165] in the years of struggle but could no longer remain useful. He got rid

[051-166] of them by the simple device of setting one against another so that they

[051-167] murdered one another. In the first decades of his rule the dangerous

[051-168] cliques of gentry had formed again, and were engaged in mutual

[051-169] struggles. The most formidable clique was led by Hu Wei-yung. Hu was a

[051-170] man of the gentry of Chu's old homeland, and one of his oldest

[051-171] supporters. Hu and his relations controlled the country after 1370,

[051-172] until in 1380 Chu succeeded in beheading Hu and exterminating his

[051-173] clique. New cliques formed before long and were exterminated in turn.

[051-174] Chu had founded Nanking in the years of revolution, and he made it his

[051-175] capital. In so doing he met the wishes of the rich grain producers of

[051-176] the Yangtze delta. But the north was the most threatened part of his

[051-177] empire, so that troops had to be permanently stationed there in

[051-178] considerable strength. Thus Peking, where Chu placed one of his sons as

[051-179] "king", was a post of exceptional importance.

[051-180] In Chu Yüan-chang's last years (he was named T'ai Tsu as emperor)

[051-181] difficulties arose in regard to the dynasty. The heir to the throne died

[051-182] in 1391; and when the emperor himself died in 1398, the son of the late

[051-183] heir-apparent was installed as emperor (Hui Ti, 1399-1402). This choice

[051-184] had the support of some of the influential Confucian gentry families of

[051-185] the south. But a protest against his enthronement came from the other

[051-186] son of Chu Yüan-chang, who as king in Peking had hoped to become

[051-187] emperor. With his strong army this prince, Ch'eng Tsu, marched south and

[051-188] captured Nanking, where the palaces were burnt down. There was a great

[051-189] massacre of supporters of the young emperor, and the victor made himself

[051-190] emperor (better known under his reign name, Yung-lo). As he had

[051-191] established himself in Peking, he transferred the capital to Peking,

[051-192] where it remained throughout the Ming epoch. Nanking became a sort of

[051-193] subsidiary capital.
