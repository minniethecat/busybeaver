## Overview

[[wang-chen|Wang Chen]] was a eunuch of the [[ming-dynasty|Ming dynasty]] who rose to dominate the imperial court during the reign of the young Ying Tsung emperor (r. 1436–1449). His ill-conceived military campaign against the Oirat [[mongol|Mongols]] in 1449 resulted in one of the most humiliating disasters in Ming history: the capture of the emperor by the Mongol prince Esen at the Tumu Crisis.

## Rise to Power

At the death of the [[hsuan-tsung|Hsüan Tsung]] emperor, the throne passed to his eight-year-old son Ying Tsung (1436–1449). The boy emperor was initially under the control of the Yang clique, which was associated with his grandmother. But court politics shifted rapidly.

> Soon, however, another clique, led by the eunuch Wang Chen, gained the upper hand at court. [052-089] [052-090]

Wang Chen's ascendancy represented a recurring pattern in Ming politics: eunuchs, positioned at the innermost core of palace life, could outmaneuver the regular bureaucracy and gentry cliques by controlling access to the emperor. By the time Ying Tsung reached adulthood, Wang Chen had consolidated his position as the dominant figure at court.

## The Disastrous Mongol Campaign

| Period | Event |
|--------|-------|
| 1436 | Ying Tsung enthroned at age 8 under Yang clique regency |
| c. 1440–1445 | Wang Chen's clique displaced the Yang faction |
| 1449 | Wang Chen proposed great campaign against Oirat [[mongol|Mongols]] |
| 1449 | Tumu Crisis: Ming army of ~100,000 surrounded; emperor captured |
| 1449 | Wang Chen killed; his clique collapsed |

The [[mongol|Mongols]] under the Oirat prince Esen had been conducting raids into the province of Shansi, and Wang Chen saw an opportunity for military glory. He proposed a great campaign — and insisted on taking the young emperor, who had just turned twenty-one, along with the army.

> Wang Chen proposed a great campaign against them, and in this campaign he took with him the young emperor, who had reached his twenty-first birthday in 1449. [052-092] [052-093] [052-094]

The decision proved catastrophic. Wang Chen, like the emperor, had spent his entire life within the palace walls and knew nothing of warfare.

> The emperor had grown up in the palace and knew nothing of the world outside; he was therefore glad to go with Wang Chen; but that eunuch had also lived in the palace and also knew nothing of the world, and in particular of war. [052-095] [052-096] [052-097]

Wang Chen failed to organize reinforcements for his army of approximately 100,000 men. After a few brief engagements, the Oirat-Mongol prince Esen surrounded the imperial army and took the emperor prisoner.

> He failed in the organization of reinforcements for his army, some 100,000 strong; after a few brief engagements the Oirat-Mongol prince Esen had the imperial army surrounded and the emperor a prisoner. [052-097] [052-098] [052-099] [052-100]

## Aftermath

Wang Chen himself perished in the disaster: "the eunuch Wang Chen came to his end, and his clique, of course, no longer held power" [052-101]. The [[mongol|Mongols]], who had no intention of killing the emperor, proposed to hold him for a high ransom. But the various cliques at court, Eberhard notes, "cared little about their ruler" — they simply installed a new emperor and negotiated for Ying Tsung's eventual return [052-103] [052-104].

The Tumu Crisis exposed the deep dysfunction of Ming court politics: a system in which palace-raised eunuchs and emperors, isolated from the realities of the world, could lead the empire into military catastrophe.
