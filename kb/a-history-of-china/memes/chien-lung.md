## Overview

[[Ch'ien-lung]] (1711–1799) was the fifth emperor of the Manchu [[ching-dynasty|Ch'ing dynasty]], ruling from 1736 to 1796. His sixty-year reign represented the apex of Manchu imperial power — an era of unprecedented territorial expansion, economic prosperity, and cultural patronage — yet it also witnessed the first unmistakable signs of internal decay that would eventually consume the dynasty [057-086].

## Biography

Ch'ien-lung was the grandson of the [[kang-hsi|K'ang-hsi]] emperor, and consciously modelled his rule on his grandfather's pattern of vigorous personal governance and cultural patronage. He ascended the throne in 1736 at the age of twenty-five and immediately embarked on an ambitious programme of military expansion and domestic consolidation.

> The period of Ch'ien-lung is not only that of the greatest expansion of the Chinese empire, but also that of the greatest prosperity under the Manchu regime. But there began at the same time to be signs of internal decline. [057-086–057-089]

At the beginning of his reign, fighting resumed in Turkestan. The Kalmuks — western [[mongol|Mongols]] who had migrated to the Ili region — were defeated in a series of arduous campaigns that extended Ch'ing control deep into Central Asia [057-007]. Some Kazak tribes, pressed between the expanding Ch'ing and Russian empires, went over to the Russians, and in 1735 the Russian colonialists founded the town of Orenburg in the western Kazak region [057-012].

## Military Expansion

The wars of conquest under Ch'ien-lung appear to have been driven by two main reasons. The first was the need for security: the [[mongol|Mongols]] had to be overthrown because otherwise the Manchu homeland was menaced; to suppress the eastern [[mongol|Mongols]], the western [[mongol|Mongols]] (Kalmuks) had to be overthrown; to render them harmless, Turkestan and the Ili region had to be conquered; Tibet was needed for the security of Turkestan and Mongolia — and so on, in an ever-widening arc of strategic necessity [057-069].

> For the Ch'ien-lung period's many wars of conquest there seem to have been two main reasons. The first was the need for security. The Mongols had to be overthrown because otherwise the homeland of the Manchus was menaced; in order to make sure of the suppression of the eastern Mongols, the western Mongols (Kalmuks) had to be overthrown; to make them harmless, Turkestan and the Ili region had to be conquered; Tibet was needed for the security of Turkestan and Mongolia — and so on. [057-069–057-076]

These campaigns, though costly, brought the Ch'ing empire to its greatest territorial extent, surpassing even the Han and T'ang dynasties at their peaks.

## Court and Culture

The eighteenth-century Ch'ing court surrounded itself with great splendour. Countless palaces and other luxurious buildings were erected during Ch'ien-lung's reign, reflecting his well-known craze for building [055-029]. It must be borne in mind, however, that so great an empire as the China of that day possessed very considerable financial strength and could support this luxury [055-031].

The emperor also oversaw the compilation of the _Ssu-k'u Ch'üan-shu_ ("Complete Library of the Four Treasuries"), one of the largest literary projects in Chinese history — a fourfold effort that combined cultural patronage with systematic censorship of anti-Manchu writings.

## Decline and Abdication

If a particular year must be fixed for the onset of dynastic decline under Ch'ien-lung, perhaps it should be 1774, the year of the first great popular rising in the province of Shantung [057-090]. In 1775 came another rising in Honan — that of the "Society of the White Lotus," a secret organisation that would bedevil the dynasty for decades [057-091].

> If we are to fix a particular year for this, perhaps it should be the year 1774, in which came the first great popular rising, in the province of Shantung. In 1775 there came another popular rising, in Honan — that of the "Society of the White Lotus". [057-090–057-092]

Ch'ien-lung abdicated on New Year's Day, 1795, after ruling for sixty years — a filial gesture to avoid out-reigning his grandfather [[kang-hsi|K'ang-hsi]]. He died in 1799 [057-117]. His successor, known by the reign name Chia-ch'ing, inherited an empire that appeared majestic on the surface but was structurally weakened by corruption, fiscal strain, and widespread discontent [057-118].

## Legacy

Ch'ien-lung's reign is remembered as both the zenith and the turning-point of Manchu rule. The territorial gains of his campaigns defined the borders of modern China, while his cultural projects preserved vast swathes of the Chinese literary heritage. Yet the rebellions that erupted in his later years — particularly the White Lotus rising — revealed fissures that successive emperors could not seal, setting the stage for the long nineteenth-century crisis of the Ch'ing dynasty.
