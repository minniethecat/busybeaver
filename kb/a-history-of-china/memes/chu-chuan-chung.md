# Chu Ch'üan-chung

**Chu Ch'üan-chung** ({{zh|朱全忠}}, 852–912) was a Chinese military commander and warlord who founded the [[Later Liang dynasty]], becoming its first emperor and ending nearly three centuries of [[Tang dynasty]] rule. His brief reign inaugurated the turbulent Five Dynasties and Ten Kingdoms period (907–960).

## Rise to Power

Chu Ch'üan-chung began his career in the waning years of the Tang dynasty. He rose through the military ranks as a capable field commander amid the widespread rebellions and provincial fragmentation that characterised the late ninth century.

> In a short time Chu Ch'üan-chung, a man who had begun his career as a bandit, became, as a military commander, one of the most powerful figures in the empire. [039-020]

By 903 he had established a power base in the eastern plains and manoeuvred to seize the person of the Tang emperor.

> Chu Ch'üan-chung had control of the plains in the east. [039-023]

> The emperor fell into the hands of Chu Ch'üan-chung, who killed him. [039-030]

## Founding of Later Liang

With the Tang emperor eliminated, Chu Ch'üan-chung proclaimed himself emperor in 906 (or 907 by conventional dating), establishing the Later Liang dynasty with its capital at Kaifeng. This act formally ended the Tang dynasty, though several rival states in the south and west refused to recognise his authority.

> Of this Later Liang dynasty, Chu Ch'üan-chung (906), was the first ruler. [040-181]

His dynasty proved fragile. The empire remained divided, with rival polities controlling Shansi and the Sichuan basin. Chu was unable to unify the former Tang territories by force.

> Chu Ch'üan-chung did not succeed in destroying all the other claimants. [041-002]

## Reign and Death

Chu Ch'üan-chung's Later Liang was one of the shortest-lived of the Five Dynasties, beset by internal family conflict and military pressure from the Shatuo Turks in Shansi.

> Chu Ch'üan-chung's dynasty was one of the three short-lived regional dynasties of this period. [041-013]

In 912, after only six years on the throne, he was assassinated in a palace coup led by one of his own sons — a pattern of intra-family violence that characterised the politics of the Five Dynasties era.

> He died in 912 after the murder of Chu Ch'üan-chung by one of his sons. [041-033]

## Legacy

Chu Ch'üan-chung is remembered primarily as the man who terminated the Tang dynasty, one of China's most celebrated imperial ages.

His career — bandit to general to emperor — exemplifies the social mobility and political instability of the late Tang and early Five Dynasties period. The Later Liang dynasty he founded lasted only sixteen years (907–923) before being overthrown by the Shatuo-led Later Tang.
