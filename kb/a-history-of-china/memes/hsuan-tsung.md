## Overview

[[hsuan-tsung|Hsüan Tsung]] (685–762, r. 712–756) presided over the longest and most culturally brilliant reign of the [[tang-dynasty|T'ang dynasty]]. The first decades of his rule, known under the reign title K'ai-yüan (713–741), are remembered as a golden age of administrative reform, economic prosperity, and literary patronage. The closing phase — dominated by his infatuation with the celebrated consort [[yang-kuei-fei|Yang Kuei-fei]] and his delegation of power to her favourites — ended with the catastrophic [[an-lu-shan-rebellion|An Lu-shan Rebellion]] of 755, his flight from the capital, and his abdication in favour of his son. No T'ang reign better illustrates the arc from enlightened governance to imperial tragedy that became a central theme of Chinese historiography [037-051][037-142].

## The K'ai-yüan Golden Age

Hsüan Tsung came to the throne in 712 after a period of palace coups and female dominance at court that had characterised the early T'ang. His predecessors had included the Empress Wu, who briefly ruled in her own name, and the Empress Wei, whose murder cleared the way for Hsüan Tsung's accession. Determined to restore the prestige of the imperial house, the young emperor surrounded himself with capable ministers and launched a comprehensive programme of administrative centralisation [037-051].

> With Hsüan Tsung there began now a period of active state intervention in economic life. [037-051]

Under his chief minister Yao Ch'ung, and later Chang Chiu-ling, the government rationalised the tax system, re-registered the peasantry, and strengthened the system of grain transport from the [[yangtze-river|Yangtze]] delta to the capital at Ch'ang-an. The capital itself, with a population approaching one million, became the largest and most cosmopolitan city in the world — a magnet for merchants, monks, and envoys from Central Asia, Persia, India, and beyond [037-121][037-131].

> The emperor Hsüan Tsung gave active encouragement to all forms of trade, and the capital became a centre of international commerce. [037-121]

## Cultural Patronage

Hsüan Tsung's court was the epicentre of High T'ang culture. The emperor himself was an accomplished musician and established the "Pear Garden" academy for the training of performers — an institution that later generations of actors regarded as their professional ancestor. Poets such as [[li-po|Li Po]] and [[tu-fu|Tu Fu]] passed through the capital during his reign, and the emperor's patronage of the arts set a standard that later dynasties would seek to emulate [037-131].

> At the beginning of Hsüan Tsung's reign the capital had been the centre of a brilliant court life, with poets, painters, and musicians enjoying imperial patronage. [037-131]

The reign also saw the compilation of major administrative and ritual encyclopaedias, including the _T'ang Liu-tien_ (Institutes of the Six Ministries), which codified the structure of the T'ang state and became a model for subsequent dynasties. [[buddhism]] flourished, but so too did a revived [[confucianism]] and the nascent religious [[taoism|Taoism]] that the imperial Li family claimed as its ancestral faith [037-121].

## The Fall

The turning-point came in the 740s, when the ageing emperor became increasingly reliant on the guidance of his consort Yang Kuei-fei and her cousin, the military governor Yang Kuo-chung. The concentration of power in the Yang family alienated the frontier generals, above all the Sogdian-Turkic commander [[an-lu-shan|An Lu-shan]], who commanded three key military provinces in the northeast. In 755, [[an-lu-shan|An Lu-shan]] launched a rebellion that swept down from his base near modern [[peking|Peking]] and captured the eastern capital of Loyang within weeks [037-142][074-191].

> The misfortunes that marked the end of Hsüan Tsung's reign began with the rebellion of the frontier general An Lu-shan. [037-142]

Hsüan Tsung fled westward to Szechwan, but on the road his own bodyguard mutinied, murdered Yang Kuo-chung, and forced the emperor to order the execution of Yang Kuei-fei. Broken by the loss, he abdicated in favour of his son [[su-tsung]], who would spend the next seven years fighting to suppress the rebellion. Hsüan Tsung returned to Ch'ang-an only in the last months of his life, to die in a palace that was a shadow of the one he had left [037-142].
