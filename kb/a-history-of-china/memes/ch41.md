# Section 41

[041-001] His northern frontier remained still more insecure than the southern,

[041-002] for Chu Ch'üan-chung did not succeed in destroying the Turkish general

[041-003] Li K'o-yung; on the contrary, the latter continually widened the range

[041-004] of his power. Fortunately he, too, had an enemy at his back--the Kitan

[041-005] (or Khitan), whose ruler had made himself emperor in 916, and so staked

[041-006] a claim to reign over all China. The first Kitan emperor held a middle

[041-007] course between Chu and Li, and so was able to establish and expand his

[041-008] empire in peace. The striking power of his empire, which from 937 onward

[041-009] was officially called the Liao empire, grew steadily, because the old

[041-010] tribal league of the Kitan was transformed into a centrally commanded

[041-011] military organization.

[041-012] To these dangers from abroad threatening the Later Liang state internal

[041-013] troubles were added. Chu Ch'üan-chung's dynasty was one of the three

[041-014] Chinese dynasties that have ever come to power through a popular rising.

[041-015] He himself was of peasant origin, and so were a large part of his

[041-016] subordinates and helpers. Many of them had originally been independent

[041-017] peasant leaders; others had been under Huang Ch'ao. All of them were

[041-018] opposed to the gentry, and the great slaughter of the gentry of the

[041-019] capital, shortly before the beginning of Chu's rule, had been welcomed

[041-020] by Chu and his followers. The gentry therefore would not co-operate with

[041-021] Chu and preferred to join the Turk Li K'o-yung. But Chu could not

[041-022] confidently rely on his old comrades. They were jealous of his success

[041-023] in gaining the place they all coveted, and were ready to join in any

[041-024] independent enterprise as opportunity offered. All of them, moreover, as

[041-025] soon as they were given any administrative post, busied themselves with

[041-026] the acquisition of money and wealth as quickly as possible. These abuses

[041-027] not only ate into the revenues of the state but actually produced a

[041-028] common front between the peasantry and the remnants of the gentry

[041-029] against the upstarts.

[041-030] In 917, after Li K'o-yung's death, the Sha-t'o Turks beat off an attack

[041-031] from the Kitan, and so were safe for a time from the northern menace.

[041-032] They then marched against the Liang state, where a crisis had been

[041-033] produced in 912 after the murder of Chu Ch'üan-chung by one of his sons.

[041-034] The Liang generals saw no reason why they should fight for the dynasty,

[041-035] and all of them went over to the enemy. Thus the "Later T'ang dynasty"

[041-036] (923-936) came into power in North China, under the son of Li K'o-yung.

[041-037] The dominant element at this time was quite clearly the Chinese gentry,

[041-038] especially in western and central China. The Sha-t'o themselves must

[041-039] have been extraordinarily few in number, probably little more than

[041-040] 100,000 men. Most of them, moreover, were politically passive, being

[041-041] simple soldiers. Only the ruling family and its following played any

[041-042] active part, together with a few families related to it by marriage. The

[041-043] whole state was regarded by the Sha-t'o rulers as a sort of family

[041-044] enterprise, members of the family being placed in the most important

[041-045] positions. As there were not enough of them, they adopted into the

[041-046] family large numbers of aliens of all nationalities. Military posts were

[041-047] given to faithful members of Li K'o-yung's or his successor's bodyguard,

[041-048] and also to domestic servants and other clients of the family. Thus,

[041-049] while in the Later Liang state elements from the peasantry had risen in

[041-050] the world, some of these neo-gentry reaching the top of the social

[041-051] pyramid in the centuries that followed, in the Sha-t'o state some of its

[041-052] warriors, drawn from the most various peoples, entered the gentry class

[041-053] through their personal relations with the ruler. But in spite of all

[041-054] this the bulk of the officials came once more from the Chinese. These

[041-055] educated Chinese not only succeeded in winning over the rulers

[041-056] themselves to the Chinese cultural ideal, but persuaded them to adopt

[041-057] laws that substantially restricted the privileges of the Sha-t'o and

[041-058] brought advantages only to the Chinese gentry. Consequently all the

[041-059] Chinese historians are enthusiastic about the "Later T'ang", and

[041-060] especially about the emperor Ming Ti, who reigned from 927 onward, after

[041-061] the assassination of his predecessor. They also abused the Liang because

[041-062] they were against the gentry.

[041-063] In 936 the Later T'ang dynasty gave place to the Later Chin dynasty

[041-064] (936-946), but this involved no change in the structure of the empire.

[041-065] The change of dynasty meant no more than that instead of the son

[041-066] following the father the son-in-law had ascended the throne. It was of

[041-067] more importance that the son-in-law, the Sha-t'o Turk Shih Ching-t'ang,

[041-068] succeeded in doing this by allying himself with the Kitan and ceding to

[041-069] them some of the northern provinces. The youthful successor, however, of

[041-070] the first ruler of this dynasty was soon made to realize that the Kitan

[041-071] regarded the founding of his dynasty as no more than a transition stage

[041-072] on the way to their annexation of the whole of North China. The old

[041-073] Sha-t'o nobles, who had not been sinified in the slightest, suggested a

[041-074] preventive war; the actual court group, strongly sinified, hesitated,

[041-075] but ultimately were unable to avoid war. The war was very quickly

[041-076] decided by several governors in eastern China going over to the Kitan,

[041-077] who had promised them the imperial title. In the course of 946-7 the

[041-078] Kitan occupied the capital and almost the whole of the country. In 947

[041-079] the Kitan ruler proclaimed himself emperor of the Kitan and the Chinese.

[041-080] [Illustration: Map 6: The State of the later T'ang dynasty]

[041-081] The Chinese gentry seem to have accepted this situation because a Kitan

[041-082] emperor was just as acceptable to them as a Sha-t'o emperor; but the

[041-083] Sha-t'o were not prepared to submit to the Kitan regime, because under

[041-084] it they would have lost their position of privilege. At the head of this

[041-085] opposition group stood the Sha-t'o general Liu Chih-yuan, who founded

[041-086] the "Later Han dynasty" (947-950). He was able to hold out against the

[041-087] Kitan only because in 947 the Kitan emperor died and his son had to

[041-088] leave China and retreat to the north; fighting had broken out between

[041-089] the empress dowager, who had some Chinese support, and the young heir to

[041-090] the throne. The new Turkish dynasty, however, was unable to withstand

[041-091] the internal Chinese resistance. Its founder died in 948, and his son,

[041-092] owing to his youth, was entirely in the hands of a court clique. In his

[041-093] effort to free himself from the tutelage of this group he made a

[041-094] miscalculation, for the men on whom he thought he could depend were

[041-095] largely supporters of the clique. So he lost his throne and his life,

[041-096] and a Chinese general, Kuo Wei, took his place, founding the "Later Chou

[041-097] dynasty" (951-959).

[041-098] A feature of importance was that in the years of the short-lived "Later

[041-099] Han dynasty" a tendency showed itself among the Chinese military leaders

[041-100] to work with the states in the south. The increase in the political

[041-101] influence of the south was due to its economic advance while the north

[041-102] was reduced to economic chaos by the continual heavy fighting, and by

[041-103] the complete irresponsibility of the Sha-t'o ruler in financial matters:

[041-104] several times in this period the whole of the money in the state

[041-105] treasury was handed out to soldiers to prevent them from going over to

[041-106] some enemy or other. On the other hand, there was a tendency in the

[041-107] south for the many neighbouring states to amalgamate, and as this

[041-108] process took place close to the frontier of North China the northern

[041-109] states could not passively look on. During the "Later Han" period there

[041-110] were wars and risings, which continued in the time of the "Later Chou".

[041-111] On the whole, the few years of the rule of the second emperor of the

[041-112] "Later Chou" (954-958) form a bright spot in those dismal fifty-five

[041-113] years. Sociologically regarded, that dynasty formed merely a transition

[041-114] stage on the way to the Sung dynasty that now followed: the Chinese

[041-115] gentry ruled under the leadership of an upstart who had risen from the

[041-116] ranks, and they ruled in accordance with the old principles of gentry

[041-117] rule. The Sha-t'o, who had formed the three preceding dynasties, had

[041-118] been so reduced that they were now a tiny minority and no longer

[041-119] counted. This minority had only been able to maintain its position

[041-120] through the special social conditions created by the "Later Liang"

[041-121] dynasty: the Liang, who had come from the lower classes of the

[041-122] population, had driven the gentry into the arms of the Sha-t'o Turks. As

[041-123] soon as the upstarts, in so far as they had not fallen again or been

[041-124] exterminated, had more or less assimilated themselves to the old gentry,

[041-125] and on the other hand the leaders of the Sha-t'o had become numerically

[041-126] too weak, there was a possibility of resuming the old form of rule.

[041-127] There had been certain changes in this period. The north-west of China,

[041-128] the region of the old capital Ch'ang-an, had been so ruined by the

[041-129] fighting that had gone on mainly there and farther north, that it was

[041-130] eliminated as a centre of power for a hundred years to come; it had been

[041-131] largely depopulated. The north was under the rule of the Kitan: its

[041-132] trade, which in the past had been with the Huang-ho basin, was now

[041-133] perforce diverted to Peking, which soon became the main centre of the

[041-134] power of the Kitan. The south, particularly the lower Yangtze region and

[041-135] the province of Szechwan, had made economic progress, at least in

[041-136] comparison with the north; consequently it had gained in political

[041-137] importance.

[041-138] One other event of this time has to be mentioned: the great persecution

[041-139] of Buddhism in 955, but not only because 30,336 temples and monasteries

[041-140] were secularized and only some 2,700 with 61,200 monks were left.

[041-141] Although the immediate reason for this action seems to have been that

[041-142] too many men entered the monasteries in order to avoid being taken as

[041-143] soldiers, the effect of the law of 955 was that from now on the

[041-144] Buddhists were put under regulations which clarified once and for ever

[041-145] their position within the framework of a society which had as its aim to

[041-146] define clearly the status of each individual within each social class.

[041-147] Private persons were no more allowed to erect temples and monasteries.

[041-148] The number of temples per district was legally fixed. A person could

[041-149] become monk only if the head of the family gave its permission. He had

[041-150] to be over fifteen years of age and had to know by heart at least one

[041-151] hundred pages of texts. The state took over the control of the

[041-152] ordinations which could be performed only after a successful

[041-153] examination. Each year a list of all monks had to be submitted to the

[041-154] government in two copies. Monks had to carry six identification cards

[041-155] with them, one of which was the ordination diploma for which a fee had

[041-156] to be paid to the government (already since 755). The diploma was, in

[041-157] the eleventh century, issued by the Bureau of Sacrifices, but the money

[041-158] was collected by the Ministry of Agriculture. It can be regarded as a

[041-159] payment _in lieu_ of land tax. The price was in the eleventh century 130

[041-160] strings, which represented the value of a small farm or the value of

[041-161] some 17,000 litres of grain. The price of the diploma went up to 220

[041-162] strings in 1101, and the then government sold 30,000 diplomas per year

[041-163] in order to get still more cash. But as diplomas could be traded, a

[041-164] black market developed, on which they were sold for as little as twenty

[041-165] strings.

[041-166] (B) Period of Moderate Absolutism

[041-167] (1) The Northern Sung dynasty

[041-168] 1 _Southward expansion_

[041-169] The founder of the Sung dynasty, Chao K'uang-yin, came of a Chinese

[041-170] military family living to the south of Peking. He advanced from general

[041-171] to emperor, and so differed in no way from the emperors who had preceded

[041-172] him. But his dynasty did not disappear as quickly as the others; for

[041-173] this there were several reasons. To begin with, there was the simple

[041-174] fact that he remained alive longer than the other founders of dynasties,

[041-175] and so was able to place his rule on a firmer foundation. But in

[041-176] addition to this he followed a new course, which in certain ways

[041-177] smoothed matters for him and for his successors, in foreign policy.
