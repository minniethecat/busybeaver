# Section 60

[060-001] 11 _Reform and reaction: the Boxer Rising_

[060-002] China had lost the war with Japan because she was entirely without

[060-003] modern armament. While Japan went to work at once with all her energy to

[060-004] emulate Western industrialization, the ruling class in China had shown a

[060-005] marked repugnance to any modernization; and the centre of this

[060-006] conservatism was the dowager empress Tz[)u] Hsi. She was a woman of

[060-007] strong personality, but too uneducated--in the modern sense--to be able

[060-008] to realize that modernization was an absolute necessity for China if it

[060-009] was to remain an independent state. The empress failed to realize that

[060-010] the Europeans were fundamentally different from the neighbouring tribes

[060-011] or the pirates of the past; she had not the capacity to acquire a

[060-012] general grasp of the realities of world politics. She felt instinctively

[060-013] that Europeanization would wreck the foundations of the power of the

[060-014] Manchus and the gentry, and would bring another class, the middle class

[060-015] and the merchants, into power.

[060-016] There were reasonable men, however, who had seen the necessity of

[060-017] reform--especially Li Hung-chang, who has already been mentioned. In

[060-018] 1896 he went on a mission to Moscow, and then toured Europe. The

[060-019] reformers were, however, divided into two groups. One group advocated

[060-020] the acquisition of a certain amount of technical knowledge from abroad

[060-021] and its introduction by slow reforms, without altering the social

[060-022] structure of the state or the composition of the government. The others

[060-023] held that the state needed fundamental changes, and that superficial

[060-024] loans from Europe were not enough. The failure in the war with Japan

[060-025] made the general desire for reform more and more insistent not only in

[060-026] the country but in Peking. Until now Japan had been despised as a

[060-027] barbarian state; now Japan had won! The Europeans had been despised; now

[060-028] they were all cutting bits out of China for themselves, extracting from

[060-029] the government one privilege after another, and quite openly dividing

[060-030] China into "spheres of interest", obviously as the prelude to annexation

[060-031] of the whole country.

[060-032] In Europe at that time the question was being discussed over and over

[060-033] again, why Japan had so quickly succeeded in making herself a modern

[060-034] power, and why China was not succeeding in doing so; the Japanese were

[060-035] praised for their capacity and the Chinese blamed for their lassitude.

[060-036] Both in Europe and in Chinese circles it was overlooked that there were

[060-037] fundamental differences in the social structures of the two countries.

[060-038] The basis of the modern capitalist states of the West is the middle

[060-039] class. Japan had for centuries had a middle class (the merchants) that

[060-040] had entered into a symbiosis with the feudal lords. For the middle class

[060-041] the transition to modern capitalism, and for the feudal lords the way to

[060-042] Western imperialism, was easy. In China there was only a weak middle

[060-043] class, vegetating under the dominance of the gentry; the middle class

[060-044] had still to gain the strength to liberate itself before it could become

[060-045] the support for a capitalistic state. And the gentry were still strong

[060-046] enough to maintain their dominance and so to prevent a radical

[060-047] reconstruction; all they would agree to were a few reforms from which

[060-048] they might hope to secure an increase of power for their own ends.

[060-049] In 1895 and in 1698 a scholar, K'ang Yo-wei, who was admitted into the

[060-050] presence of the emperor, submitted to him memoranda in which he called

[060-051] for radical reform. K'ang was a scholar who belonged to the empiricist

[060-052] school of philosophy of the early Manchu period, the so-called Han

[060-053] school. He was a man of strong and persuasive personality, and had such

[060-054] an influence on the emperor that in 1898 the emperor issued several

[060-055] edicts ordering the fundamental reorganization of education, law, trade,

[060-056] communications, and the army. These laws were not at all bad in

[060-057] themselves; they would have paved the way for a liberalization of

[060-058] Chinese society. But they aroused the utmost hatred in the conservative

[060-059] gentry and also in the moderate reformers among the gentry. K'ang Yo-wei

[060-060] and his followers, to whom a number of well-known modern scholars

[060-061] belonged, had strong support in South China. We have already mentioned

[060-062] that owing to the increased penetration of European goods and ideas,

[060-063] South China had become more progressive than the north; this had added

[060-064] to the tension already existing for other reasons between north and

[060-065] south. In foreign policy the north was more favourable to Russia and

[060-066] radically opposed to Japan and Great Britain; the south was in favour of

[060-067] co-operation with Britain and Japan, in order to learn from those two

[060-068] states how reform could be carried through. In the north the men of the

[060-069] south were suspected of being anti-Manchu and revolutionary in feeling.

[060-070] This was to some extent true, though K'ang Yo-wei and his friends were

[060-071] as yet largely unconscious of it.

[060-072] When the empress Tz[)u] Hsi saw that the emperor was actually thinking

[060-073] about reforms, she went to work with lightning speed. Very soon the

[060-074] reformers had to flee; those who failed to make good their escape were

[060-075] arrested and executed. The emperor was made a prisoner in a palace near

[060-076] Peking, and remained a captive until his death; the empress resumed her

[060-077] regency on his behalf. The period of reforms lasted only for a few

[060-078] months of 1898. A leading part in the extermination of the reformers was

[060-079] played by troops from Kansu under the command of a Mohammedan, Tung

[060-080] Fu-hsiang. General Yüan Shih-k'ai, who was then stationed at Tientsin in

[060-081] command of 7,000 troops with modern equipment, the only ones in China,

[060-082] could have removed the empress and protected the reformers; but he was

[060-083] already pursuing a personal policy, and thought it safer to give the

[060-084] reformers no help.

[060-085] There now began, from 1898, a thoroughly reactionary rule of the dowager

[060-086] empress. But China's general situation permitted no breathing-space. In

[060-087] 1900 came the so-called Boxer Rising, a new popular movement against the

[060-088] gentry and the Manchus similar to the many that had preceded it. The

[060-089] Peking government succeeded, however, in negotiations that brought the

[060-090] movement into the service of the government and directed it against the

[060-091] foreigners. This removed the danger to the government and at the same

[060-092] time helped it against the hated foreigners. But incidents resulted

[060-093] which the Peking government had not anticipated. An international army

[060-094] was sent to China, and marched from Tientsin against Peking, to liberate

[060-095] the besieged European legations and to punish the government. The

[060-096] Europeans captured Peking (1900); the dowager empress and her prisoner,

[060-097] the emperor, had to flee; some of the palaces were looted. The peace

[060-098] treaty that followed exacted further concessions from China to the

[060-099] Europeans and enormous war indemnities, the payment of which continued

[060-100] into the 1940's, though most of the states placed the money at China's

[060-101] disposal for educational purposes. When in 1902 the dowager empress

[060-102] returned to Peking and put the emperor back into his palace-prison, she

[060-103] was forced by what had happened to realize that at all events a certain

[060-104] measure of reform was necessary. The reforms, however, which she

[060-105] decreed, mainly in 1904, were very modest and were never fully carried

[060-106] out. They were only intended to make an impression on the outer world

[060-107] and to appease the continually growing body of supporters of the reform

[060-108] party, especially numerous in South China. The south remained,

[060-109] nevertheless, a focus of hostility to the Manchus. After his failure in

[060-110] 1898, K'ang Yo-wei went to Europe, and no longer played any important

[060-111] political part. His place was soon taken by a young Chinese physician

[060-112] who had been living abroad, Sun Yat-sen (1866-1925), who turned the

[060-113] reform party into a middle-class revolutionary party.

[060-114] 12 _End of the dynasty_

[060-115] Meanwhile the dowager empress held her own. General Yüan Shih-k'ai, who

[060-116] had played so dubious a part in 1898, was not impeccably loyal to her,

[060-117] and remained unreliable. He was beyond challenge the strongest man in

[060-118] the country, for he possessed the only modern army; but he was still

[060-119] biding his time.

[060-120] In 1908 the dowager empress fell ill; she was seventy-four years old.

[060-121] When she felt that her end was near, she seems to have had the captive

[060-122] emperor Tê Tsung assassinated (at 5 p.m. on November 14th); she herself

[060-123] died next day (November 15th, 2 p.m.): she was evidently determined that

[060-124] this man, whom she had ill-treated and oppressed all his life, should

[060-125] not regain independence. As Tê Tsung had no children, she nominated on

[060-126] the day of her death the two-year-old prince P'u Yi as emperor (reign

[060-127] name Hsüan-t'ung, 1909-1911).

[060-128] The fact that another child was to reign and a new regency to act for

[060-129] him, together with all the failures in home and foreign policy, brought

[060-130] further strength to the revolutionary party. The government believed

[060-131] that it could only maintain itself if it allowed Yüan Shih-k'ai, the

[060-132] commander of the modern troops, to come to power. The chief regent,

[060-133] however, worked against Yüan Shih-k'ai and dismissed him at the

[060-134] beginning of 1909; Yüan's supporters remained at their posts. Yüan

[060-135] himself now entered into relations with the revolutionaries, whose

[060-136] centre was Canton, and whose undisputed leader was now Sun Yat-sen. At

[060-137] this time Sun and his supporters had already made attempts at

[060-138] revolution, but without success, as his following was as yet too small.

[060-139] It consisted mainly of young intellectuals who had been educated in

[060-140] Europe and America; the great mass of the Chinese people remained

[060-141] unconvinced: the common people could not understand the new ideals, and

[060-142] the middle class did not entirely trust the young intellectuals.

[060-143] The state of China in 1911 was as lamentable as could be: the European

[060-144] states, Russia, America, and Japan regarded China as a field for their

[060-145] own plans, and in their calculations paid scarcely any attention to the

[060-146] Chinese government. Foreign capital was penetrating everywhere in the

[060-147] form of loans or railway and other enterprises. If it had not been for

[060-148] the mutual rivalries of the powers, China would long ago have been

[060-149] annexed by one of them. The government needed a great deal of money for

[060-150] the payment of the war indemnities, and for carrying out the few reforms

[060-151] at last decided on. In order to get money from the provinces, it had to

[060-152] permit the viceroys even more freedom than they already possessed. The

[060-153] result was a spectacle altogether resembling that of the end of the

[060-154] T'ang dynasty, about A.D. 900: the various governors were trying to make

[060-155] themselves independent. In addition to this there was the revolutionary

[060-156] movement in the south.

[060-157] The government made some concession to the progressives, by providing

[060-158] the first beginnings of parliamentary rule. In 1910 a national assembly

[060-159] was convoked. It had a Lower House with representatives of the provinces

[060-160] (provincial diets were also set up), and an Upper House, in which sat

[060-161] representatives of the imperial house, the nobility, the gentry, and

[060-162] also the protectorates. The members of the Upper House were all

[060-163] nominated by the regent. It very soon proved that the members of the

[060-164] Lower House, mainly representatives of the provincial gentry, had a much

[060-165] more practical outlook than the routineers of Peking. Thus the Lower

[060-166] House grew in importance, a fact which, of course, brought grist to the

[060-167] mills of the revolutionary movement.

[060-168] In 1910 the first risings directed actually against the regency took

[060-169] place, in the province of Hunan. In 1911 the "railway disturbances"

[060-170] broke out in western China as a reply of the railway shareholders in the

[060-171] province of Szechwan to the government decree of nationalization of all

[060-172] the railways. The modernist students, most of whom were sons of

[060-173] merchants who owned railway shares, supported the movement, and the

[060-174] government was unable to control them. At the same time a great

[060-175] anti-Manchu revolution began in Wuch'ang, one of the cities of which

[060-176] Wuhan, on the Yangtze, now consists. The revolution was the result of

[060-177] government action against a group of terrorists. Its leader was an

[060-178] officer named Li Yüan-hung. The Manchus soon had some success in this

[060-179] quarter, but the other provincial governors now rose in rapid

[060-180] succession, repudiated the Manchus, and declared themselves independent.

[060-181] Most of the Manchu garrisons in the provinces were murdered. The

[060-182] governors remained at the head of their troops in their provinces, and

[060-183] for the moment made common cause with the revolutionaries, from whom

[060-184] they meant to break free at the first opportunity. The Manchus

[060-185] themselves failed at first to realize the gravity of the revolutionary

[060-186] movement; they then fell into panic-stricken desperation. As a last

[060-187] resource, Yüan Shih-k'ai was recalled (November 10th, 1911) and made

[060-188] prime minister.
