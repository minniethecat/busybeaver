## Overview

[[yu-liang|Yü Liang]] was a general of the [[eastern-chin-dynasty|Eastern Chin dynasty]] who became the effective ruler of the southern Chinese empire during the early fourth century CE. As the brother of an empress and leader of a powerful northern gentry clique, Yü Liang dominated court politics, survived a major rebellion, and established a political dynasty that passed to his protégé [[huan-wen|Huan Wen]].

## Rise to Power

Yü Liang came from a northern family that had fled south after the [[hsiung-nu|Hu]]n conquest of North China. His sister married an Eastern Chin emperor, giving him access to the inner circle of power. When the emperor died early, Yü Liang's young nephew — the son of his sister — came to the throne as Emperor Ch'eng (326–342).

> The young son of Yü Liang's sister came to the throne as Emperor Ch'eng (326–342); his mother ruled as regent, but Yü Liang carried on the actual business of government. [032-029] [032-030] [032-031]

As de facto regent, Yü Liang controlled the administration while the empress dowager held nominal authority. He was part of the broader pattern of northern émigré families who dominated the Eastern Chin court — families that, unlike the local southern gentry, had no established base and relied on court positions for their power.

## The Su Chün Rebellion

Yü Liang's dominance was challenged in 328 CE by Su Chün, another member of the northern gentry who had risen from bandit leader to military commander under the dynasty. Su Chün captured the capital and kidnapped the young emperor, threatening to overthrow Yü Liang's clique entirely.

> In 328 he captured the capital and kidnapped the emperor, but then fell before the counterthrust of the Yü Liang party. [032-034] [032-035]

The crushing of Su Chün's rebellion solidified Yü Liang's position and demonstrated the military capability of his faction. The victory ensured that his clique would continue to dominate the Eastern Chin court for years to come.

## The Yü Liang Clique

After the rebellion, Yü Liang's clique continued to rule even as emperors died in quick succession. The twenty-one-year-old Emperor Ch'eng was succeeded by his twenty-year-old brother, who died two years later, leaving a two-year-old son as emperor (Mu Ti, 345–361).

> The domination of Yü Liang's clique continued after the death of the twenty-one-year-old emperor. [032-036] [032-037]

Yü Liang strengthened his faction by incorporating the [[hsiung-nu|Hu]]an family, an old gentry family from the same city as the imperial house. One member of the [[hsiung-nu|Hu]]an family attained high office through personal friendship with Yü Liang, and upon Yü Liang's death, his son [[huan-wen|Huan Wen]] emerged as the dominant military commander of the Eastern Chin.

> One of the family attained a high post through personal friendship with Yü Liang: on his death his son Huan Wen came into special prominence as military commander. [032-042] [032-043]

## Legacy

Yü Liang represented the archetype of the northern émigré power-broker in the Eastern Chin period. His career illustrates the pattern described by Eberhard: northern families, lacking local roots in the south, relied on court positions and clique politics to maintain their status. The clique he built outlasted him, passing to [[huan-wen|Huan Wen]], who would follow the same trajectory as [[wang-tun|Wang Tun]] before him — attempting to translate military power into dynastic ambition.
