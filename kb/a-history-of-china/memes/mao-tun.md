## Overview

Mao Tun (also known as Modu Chanyu, r. c. 209–174 BCE) was the second ruler of the [[hsiung-nu|Hsiung-nu]] empire and the architect of the first great steppe confederation to confront imperial China as a near-equal. Coming to power shortly after the founding of the [[han-dynasty|Han dynasty]], Mao Tun transformed the [[hsiung-nu|Hsiung-nu]] from a collection of nomadic tribes into a centralized feudal state capable of challenging China militarily and diplomatically. Eberhard emphasizes that [[kao-tsu|Kao Tsu]], the Han founder, faced in Mao Tun "not a mere nomad chieftain but with the most dangerous of enemies" [015-102].

Mao Tun established the template for steppe empires that later rulers — Turks, [[mongol|Mongols]], and Manchus — would follow. His treaty with the Han dynasty, involving a Chinese princess marriage and recognition of equality between the two states, set a precedent invoked by his descendants for five centuries.

## Life and Background

Mao Tun became ruler of the [[hsiung-nu|Hsiung-nu]] through a dramatic coup against his father T'ou-man. According to Chinese sources, he trained his personal guard to absolute obedience by ordering them to shoot his favorite horse, then his favorite wife, and finally his father — executing any who hesitated. This ruthless discipline forged the core of his military power.

> "But what was the purpose of all this? Mao Tun, the second ruler of the Hsiung-nu, and his first successors undoubtedly intended ultimately to conquer China, exactly as many other northern peoples after them planned to do." [015-078]

Under Mao Tun, the [[hsiung-nu|Hsiung-nu]] state developed a sophisticated organization modeled partly on Chinese example. Eberhard describes how the [[hsiung-nu|Hsiung-nu]] ruler aspired to "come forward as Son of Heaven and develop a court ceremonial similar to that of a Chinese emperor" [015-089]. The state contained a class of nobility, commoners, and slaves, supplemented by a nascent court bureaucracy [015-098].

## Rise to Power

> "Thus Kao Tsu was faced in Mao Tun not with a mere nomad chieftain but with the most dangerous of enemies, and Kao Tsu's policy had to be directed to preventing any interference of the Hsiung-nu in North Chinese affairs." [015-102]

Mao Tun's rise coincided with the chaotic transition from Ch'in to Han rule in China. In 200 BCE, he nearly achieved what later steppe conquerors would accomplish: the subjugation of China. A Chinese ally of Mao Tun had already agreed to cooperate, and [[kao-tsu|Kao Tsu]] "was very near suffering disaster in northern Shansi, as a result of which China would have come under the rule of the [[hsiung-nu|Hsiung-nu]]" [015-111].

The [[hsiung-nu|Hsiung-nu]], with their "technique of horsemen's warfare," controlled a population that Eberhard estimates may have exceeded two million people [015-109]. Yet Mao Tun ultimately refrained from pressing his advantage. Eberhard attributes this to the influence of tribal chieftains who upheld a national policy of maintaining traditional nomadic life: "Mao Tun fell in with their view, and the [[hsiung-nu|Hsiung-nu]] maintained their state as long as they kept to the old ways of life" [015-123].

## Political Vision

Mao Tun's genius lay in recognizing that nomad conquest of China did not necessarily mean abandoning the steppe. He extracted tribute and recognition from the Han without relocating his power center southward. The treaty he concluded — in which a Han princess was given in marriage and the two states were declared equals — became the model for Sino-nomadic diplomacy.

> "This was the first international treaty in the history of the Far East to be recorded in detail." [015-135]

The treaty established a relationship that later [[hsiung-nu|Hu]]n rulers would invoke as precedent. Five hundred years later, [[liu-yuan|Liu Yüan]] "called his dynasty the 'Han dynasty', and so linked it with the most famous of all the Chinese dynasties, pointing to the pact which their ancestor Mao Tun had concluded five hundred years earlier with the first emperor of the Han dynasty" [024-175]. [[liu-yuan|Liu Yüan]] further recalled that "Mao Tun and his successors had married Chinese princesses," making the [[hsiung-nu|Hu]]n ruling family, in their own view, legitimate heirs to the Chinese imperial throne [024-179].

## Influence and Legacy

Mao Tun's empire-building established patterns that persisted across Inner Asian history. His descendants continued to influence Chinese politics for centuries. The [[hsiung-nu|Hu]]n "Hsia" kingdom (407–431 CE), founded by Ho-lien P'o-p'o, explicitly traced its lineage to Mao Tun: its ruler "belonged to the family of Mao Tun" [028-112]. Unlike [[liu-yuan|Liu Yüan]]'s sinicized [[hsiung-nu|Hu]]n Han dynasty, the Hsia consciously rejected Chinese culture — demonstrating the enduring prestige of Mao Tun's purely nomadic legacy.

Eberhard draws a direct parallel between Mao Tun's confrontation with Han and later steppe threats. In 615 CE, the Turk khan Shih-pi surrounded the Sui emperor, placing him "in just the same desperate situation as when, eight centuries earlier, the Chinese emperor had been beleaguered by Mao Tun" [034-155]. The comparison underscores Mao Tun's status as the archetype of the steppe conqueror in Chinese historical consciousness.

The [[hsiung-nu|Hsiung-nu]] state that Mao Tun built proved durable precisely because of his political choices. [[liu-yuan|Liu Yüan]], a later [[hsiung-nu|Hu]]n ruler, "had no intention of restoring the old empire of Mao Tun, the empire of the nomads; he intended to become emperor of China, emperor of a country of farmers" [025-002]. This distinction — between Mao Tun's nomadic empire and the sinicized [[hsiung-nu|Hu]]n states that followed — defines the fundamental tension in Inner Asian relations with China throughout imperial history.
