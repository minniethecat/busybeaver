## Overview

The Red Eyebrows Rebellion (c. 18–24 CE) was one of the most consequential popular uprisings in early Chinese history. Emerging from the desperate peasantry during the reign of the usurper [[wang-mang|Wang Mang]], the Red Eyebrows (*Ch'ih-mei*) derived their name from the practice of painting their eyebrows red as a binding badge of membership [019-067]. The rebellion swept across the empire, overwhelming [[wang-mang|Wang Mang]]'s forces and contributing directly to the collapse of the Hsin dynasty and the restoration of the Han imperial house under [[kuang-wu-ti|Kuang-wu Ti]], founder of the Later Han dynasty.

The rebellion exemplifies a recurring pattern in Chinese history: peasant distress escalating through secret-society organization into a revolutionary movement that overthrows the existing order — a pattern repeated centuries later by the [[yellow-turban-rebellion|Yellow Turbans]] and [[white-lotus-rebellion|White Lotus]] risings.

## Background

The rebellion's roots lay in the failed reforms of [[wang-mang|Wang Mang]], who had usurped the Han throne in 9 CE and proclaimed the Hsin dynasty. [[wang-mang|Wang Mang]]'s ambitious programme of land nationalization, currency reform, and economic regulation proved impossible to implement and generated widespread misery. By 12 CE, [[wang-mang|Wang Mang]] "felt obliged to abrogate all his reform legislation because it could not be carried into effect; and the economic situation proved more lamentable than ever" [019-062]. His impending campaign against the [[hsiung-nu]] further strained the empire's resources.

The accumulated distress of the peasantry reached a breaking point. As the corpus records:

> "There were continual risings, which culminated in A.D. 18 in a great popular insurrection, a genuine revolutionary rising of the peasants, whose distress had grown beyond bearing through Wang Mang's ill-judged measures." [019-064]

## The Rebellion

The Red Eyebrows organized themselves as a secret society, a form of rural association that under normal conditions was harmless but "may, in emergency situations, become an immensely effective instrument in the hands of the rural population" [019-071]. By painting their eyebrows red, the rebels created an indelible mark of commitment that bound members to the movement [019-068].

The rising rapidly outgrew its original objectives:

> "Occasionally, however, the movement grows far beyond its leaders' original objective and becomes a popular revolutionary movement, directed against the whole ruling class. That is what happened on this occasion. Vast swarms of peasants marched to the capital, killing all officials and people of position on their way." [019-075]

The movement proved unstoppable. Troops sent by [[wang-mang|Wang Mang]] either defected to the rebels or turned to plundering themselves. The frontier forces stationed against the [[hsiung-nu|Hsiung-nu]], receiving no reinforcements, "themselves went over to plundering, so that ultimately the army simply disintegrated" [019-083]. Fortunately for China, the *shan-yü* of the [[hsiung-nu|Hsiung-nu]] did not exploit this moment of weakness [019-085].

## The Fall of Wang Mang

As the rebellion gathered force, descendants of the deposed Han dynasty emerged to claim leadership. They positioned themselves as fighters against the usurper and attracted gentry support. [[kuang-wu-ti|Liu Hsiu]], a prince of the Han line, assembled a following, and his party conquered the capital. [[wang-mang|Wang Mang]], placing absolute faith in his own sanctity, refused to flee:

> "He sat in his robes in the throne-room and recited the ancient writings, convinced that he would overcome his adversaries by the power of his words. But a soldier cut off his head (A.D. 22)." [019-102]

[[wang-mang|Wang Mang]]'s skull was preserved in the imperial treasury for two hundred years as a trophy [019-104].

## Aftermath

The fighting did not end with [[wang-mang|Wang Mang]]'s death. Various factions of the Han restoration party fought among themselves, and all of them "fought the Red Eyebrows. In those years millions of men came to their deaths" [019-107]. Ultimately, [[kuang-wu-ti|Liu Hsiu]] prevailed, becoming the first emperor of the Later Han dynasty with the reign name Kuang-wu Ti (r. 25–57 CE) [019-109].

The Red Eyebrows thus achieved the paradoxical outcome common to many peasant risings: they destroyed the hated regime but did not themselves take power. Instead, a member of the old imperial house restored the dynasty the rebels had, in effect, fought to avenge.

## Historical Significance

The Red Eyebrows Rebellion established a model of secret-society-organized peasant revolt that would recur throughout Chinese history. The corpus explicitly links the Red Eyebrows to later movements including the [[yellow-turban-rebellion|Yellow Turbans]] and the [[white-lotus-rebellion|White Lotus Society]] [074-160]. The rebellion demonstrated both the explosive potential of rural distress and the characteristic limitation of peasant movements — their inability to produce an alternative governing structure, leaving power to be claimed by members of the existing elite.

The rebellion also marked the definitive end of [[wang-mang|Wang Mang]]'s experiment in radical reform. The Later Han dynasty that emerged from the chaos would abandon [[wang-mang|Wang Mang]]'s interventionist policies and return to the more conservative governance model of the Former Han, with lasting consequences for the development of the Chinese imperial state.
