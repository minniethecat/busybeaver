## Overview

[[tseng-kuo-fan|Tsêng Kuo-fan]] (1811–1892) was a [[qing-dynasty|Qing dynasty]] official, Confucian scholar, and military commander best known for organizing and leading the [[hsiung-nu|Hu]]nan Army (Xiang Army) that ultimately crushed the [[tai-ping-rebellion|Taiping Rebellion]] (1850–1864). His success marked a pivotal shift in Qing military organization — from the hereditary Eight Banners to regionally-recruited, personally-loyal provincial forces.

> Tsêng Kuo-fan (1811-1892) and Li Hung-chang (1823-1901), who organized and led regional armies that saved the dynasty from the Taiping rebels. [058-180]

His legacy extends beyond military achievement: he became the leading figure of the Tongzhi Restoration and the Self-Strengthening Movement, which sought to modernize China's military and industrial capacity while preserving Confucian social and political values.

## Military Career

When the [[tai-ping-rebellion|Taiping Rebellion]] overwhelmed regular Qing forces in the 1850s, Tsêng Kuo-fan was tasked with raising a new type of army from his home province of [[hsiung-nu|Hu]]nan:

> Tsêng Kuo-fan organized a regional force based on personal loyalty, rigorous training, and Confucian indoctrination, paid for by local taxes and contributions rather than the imperial treasury. [058-189]

His [[hsiung-nu|Hu]]nan Army proved far more effective than the demoralized Banner forces, combining traditional infantry tactics with Western-supplied firearms. The campaign against the Taiping lasted over a decade, culminating in the recapture of Nanking in 1864.

## The Self-Strengthening Movement

In the aftermath of the Taiping war and the [[lorcha-war|Second Opium War]], Tsêng became a leading voice for selective modernization:

> Tsêng Kuo-fan represented the conservative wing of the reform movement, advocating the adoption of Western military technology while preserving the essential structure of Confucian society. [058-193]

This philosophy — summarized as "Chinese learning as the base, Western learning for practical use" (Zhongxue wei ti, Xixue wei yong) — shaped the Self-Strengthening Movement of the 1860s–1890s, which established arsenals, shipyards, and technical schools but stopped short of political reform.

## Legacy

> For Tsêng Kuo-fan, see W.J. Hail, _Tsêng Kuo-fan and the Taiping Rebellion_. [072-146]

Tsêng's dual identity — Confucian moralist and pragmatic modernizer — made him a complex figure in Chinese history. He was revered by conservatives for saving the dynasty and reviving Confucian governance, yet later reformers and revolutionaries criticized him for defending a decaying imperial order. His protégé [[li-hung-chang|Li Hung-chang]] carried forward his modernization program into the 1890s, while his model of provincial militarization inadvertently laid the groundwork for the warlordism that would fragment China after the dynasty's fall.
