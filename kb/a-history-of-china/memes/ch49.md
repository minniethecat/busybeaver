# Section 49

[049-001] In architecture, too, Indian and Tibetan influence was felt in this

[049-002] period. The Tibetan pagodas came into special prominence alongside the

[049-003] previously known form of pagoda, which has many storeys, growing smaller

[049-004] as they go upward; these towers originally contained relics of Buddha

[049-005] and his disciples. The Tibetan pagoda has not this division into

[049-006] storeys, and its lower part is much larger in circumference, and often

[049-007] round. To this day Peking is rich in pagodas in the Tibetan style.

[049-008] The Mongols also developed in China the art of carpet-knotting, which to

[049-009] this day is found only in North China in the zone of northern influence.

[049-010] There were carpets before these, but they were mainly of felt. The

[049-011] knotted carpets were produced in imperial workshops--only, of course,

[049-012] for the Mongols, who were used to carpets. A further development

[049-013] probably also due to West Asian influence was that of cloisonné

[049-014] technique in China in this period.

[049-015] Painting, on the other hand, remained free from alien influence, with

[049-016] the exception of the craft painting for the temples. The most famous

[049-017] painters of the Mongol epoch were Chao Mêng-fu (also called Chao

[049-018] Chung-mu, 1254-1322), a relative of the deposed imperial family of the

[049-019] Sung dynasty, and Ni Tsan (1301-1374).

[049-020] (B) The Ming Epoch (1368-1644)

[049-021] 1 _Start. National feeling_

[049-022] It was necessary to give special attention to the reasons for the

[049-023] downfall of Mongol rule in China, in order to make clear the cause and

[049-024] the character of the Ming epoch that followed it. It is possible that

[049-025] the erroneous impression might be gained that the Mongol epoch in China

[049-026] was entirely without merits, and that the Mongol rule over China

[049-027] differed entirely from the Mongol rule over other countries of Asia.

[049-028] Chinese historians have no good word to say of the Mongol epoch and

[049-029] avoid the subject as far as they can. It is true that the union of the

[049-030] national Mongol culture with Chinese culture, as envisaged by the Mongol

[049-031] rulers, was not a sound conception, and consequently did not endure for

[049-032] long. Nevertheless, the Mongol epoch in China left indelible traces, and

[049-033] without it China's further development would certainly have taken a

[049-034] different course.

[049-035] The many popular risings during the latter half of the period of Mongol

[049-036] rule in China were all of a purely economic and social character, and at

[049-037] first they were not directed at all against the Mongols as

[049-038] representatives of an alien people. The rising under Chu Yüan-chang,

[049-039] which steadily gained impetus, was at first a purely social movement;

[049-040] indeed, it may fairly be called revolutionary. Chu was of the humblest

[049-041] origin; he became a monk and a peasant leader at one and the same time.

[049-042] Only three times in Chinese history has a man of the peasantry become

[049-043] emperor and founder of a dynasty. The first of these three men founded

[049-044] the Han dynasty; the second founded the first of the so-called "Five

[049-045] Dynasties" in the tenth century; Chu was the third.

[049-046] Not until the Mongols had answered Chu's rising with a tightening of the

[049-047] nationality laws did the revolutionary movement become a national

[049-048] movement, directed against the foreigners as such. And only when Chu

[049-049] came under the influence of the first people of the gentry who joined

[049-050] him, whether voluntarily or perforce, did what had been a revolutionary

[049-051] movement become a struggle for the substitution of one dynasty for

[049-052] another without interfering with the existing social system. Both these

[049-053] points were of the utmost importance to the whole development of the

[049-054] Ming epoch.

[049-055] The Mongols were driven out fairly quickly and without great difficulty.

[049-056] The Chinese drew from the ease of their success a sense of superiority

[049-057] and a clear feeling of nationalism. This feeling should not be

[049-058] confounded with the very old feeling of Chinese as a culturally superior

[049-059] group according to which, at least in theory though rarely in practice,

[049-060] every person who assimilated Chinese cultural values and traits was a

[049-061] "Chinese". The roots of nationalism seem to lie in the Southern Sung

[049-062] period, growing up in the course of contacts with the Juchên and

[049-063] Mongols; but the discriminatory laws of the Mongols greatly fostered

[049-064] this feeling. From now on, it was regarded a shame to serve a foreigner

[049-065] as official, even if he was a ruler of China.

[049-066] 2 _Wars against Mongols and Japanese_

[049-067] It had been easy to drive the Mongols out of China, but they were never

[049-068] really beaten in their own country. On the contrary, they seem to have

[049-069] regained strength after their withdrawal from China: they reorganized

[049-070] themselves and were soon capable of counter-thrusts, while Chinese

[049-071] offensives had as a rule very little success, and at all events no

[049-072] decisive success. In the course of time, however, the Chinese gained a

[049-073] certain influence over Turkestan, but it was never absolute, always

[049-074] challenged. After the Mongol empire had fallen to pieces, small states

[049-075] came into existence in Turkestan, for a long time with varying fortunes;

[049-076] the most important one during the Ming epoch was that of Hami, until in

[049-077] 1473 it was occupied by the city-state of Turfan. At this time China

[049-078] actively intervened in the policy of Turkestan in a number of combats

[049-079] with the Mongols. As the situation changed from time to time, these

[049-080] city-states united more or less closely with China or fell away from her

[049-081] altogether. In this period, however, Turkestan was of no military or

[049-082] economic importance to China.

[049-083] In the time of the Ming there also began in the east and south the

[049-084] plague of Japanese piracy. Japanese contacts with the coastal provinces

[049-085] of China (Kiangsu, Chekiang and Fukien) had a very long history:

[049-086] pilgrims from Japan often went to these places in order to study

[049-087] Buddhism in the famous monasteries of Central China; businessmen sold at

[049-088] high prices Japanese swords and other Japanese products here and bought

[049-089] Chinese products; they also tried to get Chinese copper coins which had

[049-090] a higher value in Japan. Chinese merchants co-operated with Japanese

[049-091] merchants and also with pirates in the guise of merchants. Some Chinese

[049-092] who were or felt persecuted by the government, became pirates

[049-093] themselves. This trade-piracy had started already at the end of the Sung

[049-094] dynasty, when Japanese navigation had become superior to Korean shipping

[049-095] which had in earlier times dominated the eastern seaboard. These

[049-096] conditions may even have been one of the reasons why the Mongols tried

[049-097] to subdue Japan. As early as 1387 the Chinese had to begin the building

[049-098] of fortifications along the eastern and southern coasts of the country;

[049-099] The Japanese attacks now often took the character of organized raids: a

[049-100] small, fast-sailing flotilla would land in a bay, as far as possible

[049-101] without attracting notice; the soldiers would march against the nearest

[049-102] town, generally overcoming it, looting, and withdrawing. The defensive

[049-103] measures adopted from time to time during the Ming epoch were of little

[049-104] avail, as it was impossible effectively to garrison the whole coast.

[049-105] Some of the coastal settlements were transferred inland, to prevent the

[049-106] Chinese from co-operating with the Japanese, and to give the Japanese so

[049-107] long a march inland as to allow time for defensive measures. The

[049-108] Japanese pirates prevented the creation of a Chinese navy in this period

[049-109] by their continual threats to the coastal cities in which the shipyards

[049-110] lay. Not until much later, at a time of unrest in Japan in 1467, was

[049-111] there any peace from the Japanese pirates.

[049-112] The Japanese attacks were especially embarrassing for the Chinese

[049-113] government for one other reason. Large armies had to be kept all along

[049-114] China's northern border, from Manchuria to Central Asia. Food supplies

[049-115] could not be collected in north China which did not have enough

[049-116] surplusses. Canal transportation from Central China was not reliable, as

[049-117] the canals did not always have enough water and were often clogged by

[049-118] hundreds of ships. And even if canals were used, grain still had to be

[049-119] transported by land from the end of the canals to the frontier. The Ming

[049-120] government therefore, had organized an overseas flotilla of grain ships

[049-121] which brought grain from Central China directly to the front in

[049-122] Liao-tung and Manchuria. And these ships, vitally important, were so

[049-123] often attacked by the pirates, that this plan later had to be given up

[049-124] again.

[049-125] These activities along the coast led the Chinese to the belief that

[049-126] basically all foreigners who came by ships were "barbarians"; when

[049-127] towards the end of the Ming epoch the Japanese were replaced by

[049-128] Europeans who did not behave much differently and were also

[049-129] pirate-merchants, the nations of Western Europe, too, were regarded as

[049-130] "barbarians" and were looked upon with great suspicion. On the other

[049-131] side, continental powers, even if they were enemies, had long been

[049-132] regarded as "states", sometimes even as equals. Therefore, when at a

[049-133] much later time the Chinese came into contact with Russians, their

[049-134] attitude towards them was similar to that which they had taken towards

[049-135] other Asian continental powers.

[049-136] 3 _Social legislation within the existing order_

[049-137] At the time when Chu Yüan-chang conquered Peking, in 1368, becoming the

[049-138] recognized emperor of China (Ming dynasty), it seemed as though he would

[049-139] remain a revolutionary in spite of everything. His first laws were

[049-140] directed against the rich. Many of the rich were compelled to migrate to

[049-141] the capital, Nanking, thus losing their land and the power based on it.

[049-142] Land was redistributed among poor peasants; new land registers were also

[049-143] compiled, in order to prevent the rich from evading taxation. The number

[049-144] of monks living in idleness was cut down and precisely determined; the

[049-145] possessions of the temples were reduced, land exempted from taxation

[049-146] being thus made taxable--all this, incidentally, although Chu had

[049-147] himself been a monk! These laws might have paved the way to social

[049-148] harmony and removed the worst of the poverty of the Mongol epoch. But

[049-149] all this was frustrated in the very first years of Chu's reign. The laws

[049-150] were only half carried into effect or not at all, especially in the

[049-151] hinterland of the present Shanghai. That region had been conquered by

[049-152] Chu at the very beginning of the Ming epoch; in it lived the wealthy

[049-153] landowners who had already been paying the bulk of the taxes under the

[049-154] Mongols. The emperor depended on this wealthy class for the financing of

[049-155] his great armies, and so could not be too hard on it.

[049-156] Chu Yüan-chang and his entourage were also unable to free themselves

[049-157] from some of the ideas of the Mongol epoch. Neither Chu, nor anybody

[049-158] else before and long after him discussed the possibility of a form of

[049-159] government other than that of a monarchy. The first ever to discuss this

[049-160] question, although very timidly, was Huang Tsung-hsi (1610-1695), at the

[049-161] end of the Ming dynasty. Chu's conception of an emperor was that of an

[049-162] absolute monarch, master over life and death of his subjects; it was

[049-163] formed by the Mongol emperors with their magnificence and the huge

[049-164] expenditure of their life in Peking; Chu was oblivious of the fact that

[049-165] Peking had been the capital of a vast empire embracing almost the whole

[049-166] of Asia, and expenses could well be higher than for a capital only of

[049-167] China. It did not occur to Chu and his supporters that they could have

[049-168] done without imperial state and splendour; on the contrary, they felt

[049-169] compelled to display it. At first Chu personally showed no excessive

[049-170] signs of this tendency, though they emerged later; but he conferred

[049-171] great land grants on all his relatives, friends, and supporters; he

[049-172] would give to a single person land sufficient for 20,000 peasant

[049-173] families; he ordered the payment of state pensions to members of the

[049-174] imperial family, just as the Mongols had done, and the total of these

[049-175] pension payments was often higher than the revenue of the region

[049-176] involved. For the capital alone over eight million _shih_ of grain had

[049-177] to be provided in payment of pensions--that is to say, more than 160,000

[049-178] tons! These pension payments were in themselves a heavy burden on the

[049-179] state; not only that, but they formed a difficult transport problem! We

[049-180] have no close figure of the total population at the beginning of the

[049-181] Ming epoch; about 1500 it is estimated to have been 53,280,000, and this

[049-182] population had to provide some 266,000,000 _shih_ in taxes. At the

[049-183] beginning of the Ming epoch the population and revenue must, however,

[049-184] have been smaller.
