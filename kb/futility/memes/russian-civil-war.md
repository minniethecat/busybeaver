The Russian Civil War (1917–1922) is the shadow conflict that shapes the second half of William Gerhardie's *Futility*. While the war itself rarely occupies the narrative foreground, its effects — displacement, violence, the collapse of economic life, the convergence of refugees and soldiers in [[Siberia]] and the Far East — determine the world the characters inhabit. Gerhardie's narrator adds "the knowledge of a civil war fumbling in the snow" (026-001) to the novel's catalogue of miseries, capturing the war's dual character: sprawling and lethal, yet somehow incompetent, "fumbling" rather than conquering.

### Distant Thunder: War as Background

The war remains offstage throughout the novel. Characters hear of battles, encounter refugees, navigate checkpoints — but no battle is described, no combat witnessed. This narrative choice is deliberate: the war affects the characters not as combatants but as civilians caught in its gravitational field. The family flees Petrograd not because fighting has reached the city but because the Bolshevik revolution has made their former life untenable. The war is the "prevailing Russian atmosphere" (028-023) — something breathed rather than observed.

## The War in the Novel

The Civil War enters *Futility* through indirection. It is the reason the [[Nikolai Vasilievich]] family flees eastward; the reason [[Vladivostok]] fills with Allied officers and currency speculators; the reason [[kolchaks-army|Kolchak's Army]] rises and falls in [[omsk|Omsk]]. But Gerhardie's characters, consistent with their treatment of the [[Russian Revolution 1917]], register the war primarily through its inconveniences. "The prevailing Russian atmosphere of the time was conducive to talking, among other things, to the civil war" (028-023) — the syntactic demotion of the war to "among other things" is pure Gerhardie, refusing heroic or tragic framing.

The novel's most sustained engagement with the Civil War comes through the figure of Admiral Kolchak. The narrator encounters him in a scene of almost surreal intimacy:

> When I turned into my bedroom I beheld the Admiral and a little dark-haired man, aquiline featured, sitting on my bed and talking like two conspirators. The dark-haired little man then rose with the precision common to Russian officers, and shook hands. He was, I learnt afterwards, Admiral Kolchak. (019-072)

The Supreme Ruler of All Russia is introduced as "a little dark-haired man" sitting on the narrator's bed. The scene is domestic, almost comic — the most powerful man in White Russia reduced to a bedroom conspirator. This deflation of historical figures into ordinary mortals is characteristic of Gerhardie's method. Kolchak is never made heroic; he is merely another character in the crowded novel, another man with schemes that will fail.

### Kolchak's Omsk: Dictatorship as Farce

The narrator's arrival in Omsk under the Kolchak regime produces one of the novel's most politically acute passages:

> When the train arrived at Omsk, the new regime of Kolchak had been established. The Admiral was distinctly pleased with the change; for he no longer believed in granting the Russian people a Constituent Assembly because he had grounds for thinking that the Russian people, if given this opportunity, would take advantage of it and elect a government other than that of Kolchak. And the Admiral was rather fond of little Kolchak, whose interpretation of democracy was that of denying the people the chance of expressing it. (023-001)

The irony cuts in multiple directions. The British Admiral's fondness for Kolchak is based on Kolchak's contempt for democracy — a fine position for a representative of a constitutional monarchy to take. The phrase "little Kolchak" is devastating: the Supreme Ruler, diminished by an adjective, becomes a pet rather than a leader. The novel's political vision is here at its sharpest: the Civil War is not a clash of ideologies but a competition among authoritarians, each denying the people a voice in their own name.

## Gerhardie's Perspective

Gerhardie's treatment of the Civil War is unique in English literature. He served with the British Military Mission in Siberia and witnessed the conflict firsthand, but his novel refuses every available generic framework. The Civil War is neither tragedy (the characters are too absurd) nor epic (the scale is too fragmented) nor polemic (Gerhardie has no political program). Instead, it is rendered as a condition — like the weather, like the family, like [[futility-theme|futility]] itself — something to be endured rather than understood.

The novel's most pointed political observation comes through a diplomat who fears "the danger of being massacred by Kolchak's officers" (029-010), while simultaneously being "condemned for the 'betrayal' of Kolchak" (029-009). The contradiction — simultaneously threatened by and accused of betraying the same force — captures the impossible position of civilians and minor officials caught between collapsing armies.

### The Symmetry of Bloodshed

Gerhardie's most concise political judgment reduces the entire Civil War to a single sentence:

> "The proposition would appear self-evident. But it seems as if the one idea of the Kolchakites is bloodshed to suppress bloodshed; and that this also happens to be the idea of the Bolsheviks; and that the Kolchakites are shocked at it." (025-031)

The symmetry is the point. Both sides practice "bloodshed to suppress bloodshed" — the same violence, justified by opposing ideologies, distinguished only by the Kolchakites' hypocritical shock at their own methods. The narrator refuses to take sides not from neutrality but from an exhausted recognition that all sides have converged on the same practice. This is futility as political analysis: the war changes nothing because both victor and vanquished are morally indistinguishable.

## Connection to the Novel's Geography

The Civil War is the engine of the novel's eastward movement. The family flees [[Petrograd]] because of the [[Russian Revolution 1917]]; they cross [[siberia|Siberia]] because the Civil War has made the Trans-Siberian Railway a corridor of conflict and escape; they end in [[vladivostok|Vladivostok]] because it is the farthest point from the fighting, a city held by Allied forces where the war is felt as rumor and refugee influx rather than combat. The war shapes the novel's space without ever fully occupying its narrative.

### The Day of the Coup

Even when political violence erupts directly into the narrative, Gerhardie filters it through incongruous cheerfulness:

> It was the day of the Social Revolutionary coup d'etat. Early in the morning troops of revolutionary partisans had occupied the city peacefully and taken possession of the public buildings, to wild cheering from the local crowds. The Russian national flag had been hauled down and a red one hoisted in its stead. Processions had appeared with revolutionary banners, and the town was decorated in red. (027-003)

The scene is described with the same tone Gerhardie applies to dinner parties and gramophone records. "Wild cheering," "processions," "decorated in red" — the vocabulary is that of a festival, not a coup. The narrator's detachment is complete: one flag replaces another, crowds cheer, and the novel continues as if nothing of consequence has occurred. Because, in Gerhardie's scheme, nothing of consequence has occurred.

## Aftermath and Futility

The narrator's summary — "Kolchak has tried it. Denikin has tried it. Yudenich has tried it. I should give it a rest now" (033-004) — captures the war's outcome from the White perspective: complete failure, enumerated like a list of failed experiments. The rhythm of the sentence — three names, three attempts, one weary conclusion — is an epitaph for an entire movement:

> "Well," I said quietly, "Kolchak has tried it. Denikin has tried it. Yudenich has tried it. I should give it a rest now." (033-004)

The dismissiveness is not casual but philosophical. The narrator does not argue that the White cause was wrong; he argues that it was pointless, and that pointlessness is the more damning judgment. The [[bolsheviks|Bolsheviks]] won; the Whites were defeated, exiled, or killed; the Allied intervention withdrew. For the novel's characters, this means permanent displacement. There is no Petrograd to return to, no family fortune to recover. The war's futility mirrors the novel's larger theme: effort and outcome are uncorrelated, and the world goes on regardless.
