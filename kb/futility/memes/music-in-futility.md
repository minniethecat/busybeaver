# Music in Futility

Music runs through *Futility* as both a cultural marker and an emotional register. Characters play the piano, attend the opera, sing sentimental songs, reminisce about musical comedy careers, and drop the names of composers — Turgenev, Goethe, Dostoevski, Chekhov, and then "Wagner, Scriabin, Debussy and Richard Strauss" (036-005). Music is at once a mark of cultivation and a refuge from the chaos of revolution and romantic disaster.

## Overview

In a novel about people who cannot act effectively — in love, in politics, in art — music offers a mode of expression that bypasses the futility of words. When Nikolai Vasilievich's affairs collapse and the family disintegrates, the response is often musical: someone sits at the piano, someone sings, someone recalls an aria. Music fills the spaces that language cannot reach.

## The Piano as Emotional Centre

The piano appears repeatedly as a site of domestic intimacy and emotional display. Fanny Ivanovna plays; Vera plays; characters gather around the instrument not for performance but for the particular quality of attention that music demands and permits. The piano is where emotions that cannot be spoken are instead played — a kind of sanctioned vulnerability in a world otherwise governed by irony and misdirection.

## Opera and Aspiration

Gerhardie's characters repeatedly reference opera, particularly Gounod's *Faust* — a work about the desire to transcend mortal limits, to grasp more than life can offer. The narrator's intellectual name-dropping — from Wagner to Scriabin to Debussy — fails to impress, but the attempt itself is revealing: music is the vocabulary of those who wish to be taken seriously, to be seen as cultured, to rise above their circumstances.

## Musical Comedy and Social Ascent

Fanny Ivanovna reveals a past in musical comedy — "I went on the stage, into musical comedy" (006-022) — a detail that situates the family between artistic aspiration and social respectability. Musical comedy, in the novel's universe, is slightly disreputable, the poor relation of opera and serious music, just as the Bursanovs themselves hover between aristocracy and impoverishment.

## Key Passage

> And feeling that my affairs were in a bad way I made a bold coup to regain my tottering prestige. I appeared furiously, almost indecently intellectual, talked in quick succession of Turgenev, Goethe, Dostoevski, Chekhov, Flaubert, Shakespeare and Tolstoy. It impressed nobody. She hardly listened to me. So I tried Wagner, Scriabin, Debussy and Richard Strauss. Nothing doing. (036-005)
