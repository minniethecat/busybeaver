## Overview

Moscow appears in William Gerhardie's *Futility* primarily as a place of origin — the city the family has left behind for [[petrograd|Petrograd]] and, ultimately, for [[siberia|Siberia]]. It is mentioned less as a setting than as a memory, a gravitational center of Russian identity that exerts its pull even from a distance. When characters speak of Moscow, they speak of home, of the life that was, and of the world the revolution has swept away.

## The Mama in Moscow

Moscow enters the novel most vividly through a single, repeated phrase: "To see Mama" [004-066]. "I didn't know you had a Mama in Moscow" [004-067], the narrator responds, and the exchange captures Moscow's function in the novel's emotional geography. Moscow is where mothers are, where obligations originate, where the claims of the past reside. "Mama wants to keep her" [004-072] — the city is a locus of familial authority that persists even as the family disperses.

## Moscow as Counterpoint

Throughout the novel, Moscow functions as the unvisited alternative to [[petrograd|Petrograd]] and [[siberia|Siberia]]. The characters move from Petrograd eastward into Siberia; Moscow, to the west, represents the road not taken — the capital of old Russia, of established order, of the life that might have been lived if history had unfolded differently. Its absence from the novel's primary settings is itself significant.

## Historical Context

As the ancient capital of Russia, Moscow carries historical weight that [[petrograd|Petrograd]], for all its imperial grandeur, cannot match. The revolution's relocation of the capital back to Moscow in 1918 occurs during the novel's timeframe, though Gerhardie does not emphasize this. Moscow in *Futility* is pre-revolutionary Moscow — the Moscow of mothers and memories, not yet the Moscow of Bolshevik power.
