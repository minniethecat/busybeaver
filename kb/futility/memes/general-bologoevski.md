## Overview

General Bologoevski is the supreme comic creation among the military figures in William Gerhardie's *Futility*. A "quiet silver-haired" [020-002] Russian general with British sympathies, he combines the gravity of rank with the absurdity of endless monologue, the dignity of command with explosive profanity. He is everywhere in the novel's Siberian sections — at dinners, at dances, at conferences — always talking, always "continuing about the situation" [019-012], and always providing the novel with its most reliable comic relief.

## The Endless Monologue

Bologoevski's signature is his capacity for sustained discourse. "General Bologoevski then continued about the situation" [019-012] — and continued, and continued. After "three-quarters of an hour, General Bologoevski then continued about the situation" [019-015], Gerhardie's repetition of the phrase capturing the general's inexhaustible capacity for saying nothing at length. His talk is "loose" [019-007], his subject always "the situation" — a word capacious enough to contain everything and commit to nothing.

## The Explosive Temper

Beneath the general's silver-haired composure lies volcanic rage. "Damrotten pigs!" roared General Bologoevski [023-005] — the mangled curse ("damn rotten" filtered through a Russian accent) capturing both his fury and its essential comedy. "But, as usual" [023-005], Gerhardie adds, deflating the outburst before it can register as genuinely threatening. The general's rage, like everything else about him, is part of a performance.

## The British Connection

Bologoevski is described as "a quiet silver-haired British" sympathizer [020-002], his Russian identity overlaid with British affectations. "He understood the Bolsheviks" [020-004] he claims, though what exactly he understands is never specified. The general's British leanings place him in the novel's international circle — he dines with the narrator, dances at British functions, and sits between the narrator and "a British flag-lieutenant" [028-002].

## Social Ubiquity

Like [[kniaz|Kniaz]], Bologoevski is everywhere. "I sat between General Bologoevski and a British flag-lieutenant" [028-002]; "General Bologoevski at my side was telling me" [028-006]; "I would find myself asking General Bologoevski, whom I always went to see" [032-005]. He is the narrator's default companion, a figure so reliably present that his absence would be more notable than his presence.
