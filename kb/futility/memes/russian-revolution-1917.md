The Russian Revolution of 1917 — encompassing the February Revolution that overthrew the Tsarist autocracy and the October Revolution that brought the [[Bolsheviks]] to power — is the founding political event of William Gerhardie's *Futility*. It shatters the world of the [[Nikolai Vasilievich]] family, transforms [[Petrograd]] from imperial capital to revolutionary battleground, and sets in motion the chain of displacements — through [[Siberia]] to [[Vladivostok]] — that constitutes the novel's geography. Yet Gerhardie's treatment of the Revolution is characteristically oblique: it happens mostly offstage, registered through domestic disruption, absurdist detail, and the characters' persistent refusal to grasp its significance.

## The Revolution in the Novel

The Revolution enters *Futility* in Part II as a sudden intrusion into the narrator's personal chronology. "But just before the revolution, Admiral Butt, who had gone on a visit to England..." (014-001) — the sentence is vintage Gerhardie, placing world-historical rupture in syntactic subordination to a minor character's travel plans. The narrator's return to Russia is itself framed by revolution: "When I returned the revolution had already broken out" (014-005).

The novel's most sustained treatment of the Revolution comes through the reactions of the characters, none of whom understand what is happening. Nikolai Vasilievich "failed to see how the revolution could affect his own position" (014-008). The narrator describes the February Revolution as a "great bloodless revolution" (014-010), a characterization that captures both the initial euphoria and the Gerhardian irony — bloodlessness is temporary.

The domestic sphere absorbs and neutralizes the political. "All seemed drunk with the revolution. Shots were heard every now and then" (015-002), but the family continues its dinner rituals. Fanny Ivanovna's characteristic response is to deny reality altogether: "All this — the war, the revolution — she had overlooked: and it did not in the least disturb her" (016-025). The novel's final judgment on revolutionary consciousness comes from an unnamed character who "would not admit that any revolution had ever taken place in Russia" (035-019) — denial as a survival strategy.

## Gerhardie's Treatment

Gerhardie's Revolution is not the heroic event of Soviet mythology but a disruption registered through domestic chaos. His narrative technique — fragmentary, ironic, committed to the trivial detail — is perfectly suited to capturing how historical catastrophes are actually experienced: not as grand narratives but as a series of inconveniences, rumors, and incomprehensible changes to daily life.

The narrator's own attitude is characteristically ambiguous. He "scented revolutionary blood in the air" (015-009) with something between excitement and irony. The "student-revolutionaries, the heroes of our younger days" (017-001) are recalled with knowing nostalgia. Gerhardie, who served with the British Military Mission during the Allied Intervention, writes from the position of someone who has seen revolutionary aftermath up close and found it resistant to both heroic and tragic treatment.

## Impact on the Novel's Characters

The Revolution affects each character differently, revealing their essential natures through their responses to it:

- **[[Nikolai Vasilievich]]** — fails to see how it affects him (014-008); continues his goldmine schemes regardless
- **[[Fanny Ivanovna]]** — simply overlooks it (016-025); domestic reality overrides political reality
- **The narrator** — observes with ironic detachment; his British affiliation gives him an outsider's perspective
- **The Admiral** — continues his financial speculations (024-005); the Revolution is a market disruption, not a political event
- **eisenstein** — associated with revolutionary intelligentsia (014-007); his exact role remains ambiguous

## Connection to the Russian Civil War

The Revolution leads directly to the [[Russian Civil War]], which forms the backdrop for the novel's later chapters. The Bolshevik consolidation of power prompted Allied intervention — including the British Military Mission the narrator serves with — and the eastward flight of anti-Bolshevik forces. The [[Czech Legion]]'s presence in Siberia, [[kolchaks-army|kolchak's army]]'s campaign, and the eventual convergence of refugees in [[Vladivostok]] all trace back to the revolutionary rupture of 1917.
