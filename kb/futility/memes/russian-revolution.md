# Russian Revolution in Futility

The Russian Revolution of 1917 is the historical ground beneath Gerhardie's novel — the event that scatters the [[british-embassy-petrograd|Bursanov family]], launches the [[allied-intervention|Allied Intervention]], and transforms [[petrograd|Petrograd]] from imperial capital to [[bolsheviks|Bolshevik]] stronghold. But in *[[futility-theme|Futility]]*, the Revolution is never treated as grand history. It is experienced sideways, through personal chaos, bureaucratic farce, and the narrator's ironic detachment.

## Overview

### Arrival in Revolutionary Petrograd

The narrator arrives in [[petrograd|Petrograd]] shortly after the Revolution, ascending "the broad staircase with its worn red carpet to the Chancery" of the [[british-embassy-petrograd|British Embassy]], where "very perfect young men, very perfectly dressed, were conversing in very perfect intonations about love among monkeys" (014-002). The contrast between revolutionary upheaval and diplomatic nonchalance is Gerhardie's signature technique: history happens, but people remain preoccupied with their own small dramas.

At the Embassy, even Yuri the hall-porter is caught up in the atmosphere of confusion. When the Ambassador's car pulls up, the narrator himself is momentarily disoriented:

> Helping me on with my coat, Yuri was suddenly seized by a strange panic. He dropped the coat on the floor and dashed to the door. I followed him, thinking it was the revolution. (014-003)

The joke is on the narrator: it is only the Ambassador arriving. The word "revolution" has become so ambient that any sudden movement triggers the reflex — revolution as a state of constant, comic misapprehension.

### The Day of the Outbreak

The narrator's personal experience of the February Revolution is marked by inconvenience rather than ideology. Gerhardie renders the day with cinematic immediacy:

> Then one morning, as I was about to cross the Troitski Bridge to meet the Admiral, I was stopped by the police and was compelled to go home and change into uniform. When I returned the revolution had already broken out. The Admiral had just witnessed the sacking of the Arsenal by a disorderly crowd. Regiment after regiment was going over to the revolution. Solitary shots, and now and then machine-gun fire, were heard from various quarters of the city. (014-005)

The narrator and [[admiral-butt|Admiral Butt]] stand at the window and watch. They are spectators to history, not participants — a posture that defines the novel's entire engagement with political events. The revolution breaks out while the narrator is changing clothes; the absurdity is the point.

### The Revolutionary Intelligentsia

Later that same day, the narrator dines with [[zina|Zina's]] household on the Petrograd side. The scene is a miniature of revolutionary sociology:

> The presence of a score of students, male and female, an engineer, a lawyer, and a journalist or two, all of that revolutionary intelligentsia, probably accounted for the Liberal atmosphere that prevailed. Yesterday they had been revolutionaries; today they were contented Liberals, hailing Lvov and Miliukov as the heroes of the day. The engineer drank to the future: "The old world is dead: long live the new world!" (014-007)

The speed of ideological adaptation — revolutionaries yesterday, complacent Liberals today — is rendered with gentle mockery. Political conviction is indistinguishable from social type. The [[february-revolution-1917|February Revolution]] has already become an occasion for dinner-party toasts.

### Revolution in the Streets

Gerhardie conveys the atmosphere of revolutionary Petrograd through sensory fragments — the Neva, the crowds, the gunfire:

> I passed the sombre silhouettes of the snow-covered barges frozen on the Neva. It was dark now and the crowds in the streets were more tumultuous. Soldiers and civilians alike walked aimlessly, rifle slung over the shoulder. Several wine-cellars had been broken into; there were drunkards in the streets; but anyhow, all seemed drunk with the revolution. Shots were heard every now and then, mostly fired in the air, while the law courts had gone up in flames. The revolution, it was felt, had been established. (015-002)

The phrase "all seemed drunk with the revolution" captures the novel's sense of intoxication and unreality. The burning law courts are mentioned in passing, as one more detail in the urban landscape — not as a moral outrage, but as atmospheric evidence that something has changed.

## As Comic Opera

### The Intervention as Farce

Gerhardie's most striking treatment of the Revolution comes through the [[allied-intervention|Allied Intervention]], which the narrator describes as "a series of comic opera attempts to wipe out the Russian revolution" (019-002). The phrase is carefully chosen — comic opera, not tragedy — and it sets the tone for the entire novel's engagement with politics. The Intervention is not condemned as evil but dismissed as absurd:

> The Admiral and I, and a few others — interesting types, I can assure you — travelled to Siberia, where we engaged in a series of comic opera attempts to wipe out the Russian revolution. By now, "Intervention" has been relegated to the shelf of history. (019-002)

Yet the narrator immediately complicates this dismissal: he cannot remember the Intervention merely as "an adventure in futility, as admittedly it was," but also as "an ever-shifting, changing sense of being alive" (019-002). The Revolution is futile, but futility — as the novel's title insists — is not nothing. It is the background against which love, memory, and sensation become possible.

### Vladivostok and the Symbolic Weather

The material conditions of the counter-revolutionary effort are rendered with bleak precision. Arriving in Vladivostok, the narrator sees the port as a metaphor for the entire Russian situation:

> It was raining heavily when we arrived in Vladivostok, and the port, as we surveyed it from the boat, looked grey and hopeless, like the Russian situation. A flat had been allotted us, a bare, unfurnished flat in a deserted house standing in a grim and desolate by-street; and there the Admiral made his temporary headquarters. It poured all day long, and it seemed, indeed, as though the rain, playing havoc with the town, would never cease, even as the misery and blundering in Russia would never cease. (019-003)

The passage uses weather as a symbol — rain that "would never cease" mirrors the "misery and blundering" — but the symbol is so direct, so unadorned, that it borders on the comic. This is Gerhardie's method: take a conventional literary device (pathetic fallacy) and push it to the point of self-awareness.

## Through Personal Lens

### Domestic Chaos and Historical Event

The novel filters the Revolution through domestic chaos. When "the revolution had already broken out" (014-005), the narrator's primary concern is his uniform, his position, and his romantic prospects. The [[nikolai-vasilievich|Nikolai Vasilievich]] household absorbs the revolution as it absorbs everything — into the family's permanent state of crisis:

> And then a horrible groan was heard from the adjoining room. I cast a swift interrogative glance at Nikolai Vasilievich. I had an idea that they had hidden some wretched half-mutilated policeman, the victim of a revolutionary mob. But Nikolai Vasilievich and Fanny Ivanovna looked awkward and ashamed. (015-006)

The groan turns out to be domestic, not political. The revolution provides a dramatic explanation for any strange noise; the family's private miseries are mistaken for public ones.

### Nina's Refusal of History

Most radically, [[nina|Nina]] simply refuses to acknowledge the revolution at all. For her, Gerhardie writes, the war and the revolution are events to be "overlooked" — they do not exist if she does not attend to them:

> All this — the war, the revolution — she had overlooked: and it did not exist. Scriabin — she had overlooked him. And he did not exist. But she was there, watchful. (016-025)

This is perhaps the novel's deepest comment on revolution and history: for some people, a world-historical event can be willed out of existence. Nina's capacity to "overlook" is not ignorance but a form of existential resistance.

### The Bolshevik Revolution Arrives at Home

By the time of the [[bolshevism|Bolshevik]] takeover, the revolution has become a domestic housing problem:

> Their house, which, strictly speaking, belonged to them no longer, had, since the Bolshevik revolution, been invaded by a host of undesirable people and there was hardly a room left in the house that they could call their own. (019-059)

The grand political transformation reduces to the indignity of unwanted roommates. [[nikolai-vasilievich|Nikolai Vasilievich's]] complaint is not about ideology but about real estate — the revolution as a zoning dispute.

### Denial and the Old Order

By the novel's end, the revolution is being denied outright. One of the elderly grandfathers refuses to accept that any revolution has occurred:

> I found that he would not admit that any revolution had ever taken place in Russia. "Nonsense," he kept saying. "Nonsense. In France there has been a revolution. But this is Russia. This is not France." (035-019)

The old man's logic — revolutions happen in France, therefore they cannot happen in Russia — is a perfect comic distillation of the Russian emigre sensibility. History is what happens elsewhere, to other people. Russia, in the emigre imagination, remains unchanged and unchanging.

### The Revolution Drags On

Between the initial outbreak and the later Bolshevik consolidation, the revolution settles into a wearisome duration:

> The revolution dragged on through the winter and "deepened" as the months advanced. The forerunners of confusion became visible: food and commodities were being procured in an irregular manner. All were waiting. (016-001)

"All were waiting" — the passive, suspended temporality of revolution as everyday life. The quotation marks around "deepened" suggest that Gerhardie is quoting revolutionary rhetoric with a touch of irony; the revolution's "deepening" is indistinguishable from its stagnation.

## Key Passage

> The Admiral and I, and a few others — interesting types, I can assure you — travelled to Siberia, where we engaged in a series of comic opera attempts to wipe out the Russian revolution. By now, "Intervention" has been relegated to the shelf of history. But I cannot but remember it, not merely as an adventure in futility, as admittedly it was, but as an ever-shifting, changing sense of being alive. For the experience of love is inseparable from its background. Alone it does not exist. It is a modulation of an environment. (019-002)

This passage is the novel's thesis statement on the Revolution. "Comic opera" establishes the genre; "adventure in futility" names the theme; "ever-shifting, changing sense of being alive" reclaims value from futility itself. The Revolution is the "background" that modulates love, the environment that makes experience possible.
