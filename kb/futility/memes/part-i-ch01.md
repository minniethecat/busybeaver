# I

[002-001]And then it struck me that the only thing to do was to fit all this into a book. It is the classic way of treating life. For my ineffectual return to [[vladivostok|Vladivostok]] is the effectual conclusion of my theme. And the harbour has been strangely, knowingly responsive. It has sounded the note of departure, and the tall stone houses of the port seem to brood as I walk below, and “set the tone.” And because of this and the sense that I am marking time till the big steamer comes and bears me home to [[england|England]] I am eagerly retrospective. …

[002-002]When the *Simbirsk*, of the Russian Volunteer Fleet, had at last completely vanished, carrying away the three sisters to [[shanghai|Shanghai]], I came back to my room at the hotel. I had just moved in there. It was a bare and dingy room in a small and shabby hostel. A bed was eventually provided, but in lieu of bedsheets I was to lie on a dirty tablecloth which was to serve again as tablecloth next morning when I had my breakfast.

[002-003]“Is this sheet clean?” I asked.

[002-004]“Yes,” said the boy-attendant.

[002-005]“Quite clean?”

[002-006]“Quite.”

[002-007]“Sure nobody slept on it?”

[002-008]“Nobody. Only the boss.”

[002-009]Big drops like tears fell on the windowpane and instantly made room for others. A ruined writing-table stood in the corner. I sat down. I fingered a typically Russian pen with a no less typically Russian nib, such as one is likely to encounter in almost any Russian government department, and dipping it repeatedly into ink that was like syrup, I made a bold beginning.

[002-010]When night came I lay there on the tablecloth, hungry and worried by enormous hungry bugs that bit like dogs, and thought of [[nina|Nina]], [[sonia|Sonia]], [[vera|Vera]], [[nikolai-vasilievich|Nikolai Vasilievich]] and his unconventional [[family-in-futility|family]]. In the morning the rain ceased.

[002-011]I paced the country, now in the embrace of autumn. I wandered in remote [[place-index|places]] by the sea, in the abandoned park that used to be a park essentially for [[love-in-futility|lovers]], and thought of them. Here the foliage was more dense, the corners more secluded, the disorder more magnificent. I sat on an old bench that had names and initials cut out with a penknife, under the trees turning gold and auburn, and shivered in the sharp autumn wind that sent the fallen yellow leaves whirling down the alley. And the vast sea of Russian life seemed to be closing over me. …
