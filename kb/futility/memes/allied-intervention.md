# Allied Intervention in Siberia

The Allied Intervention in the Russian Civil War (1918–1922) was a multinational military effort to support anti-Bolshevik forces and secure Allied matériel. In Gerhardie's *Futility*, it becomes the novel's great comic subject — a historical episode rendered as personal farce, as bureaucratic absurdity, and as an unexpected source of tenderness.

## In the Novel

### The Comic Opera

The narrator's account of the Intervention is Gerhardie's most sustained treatment of the relationship between history and experience. "By now, 'Intervention' has been relegated to the shelf of history," the narrator writes, but he insists on remembering it as more than "an adventure in futility" — rather as "an ever-shifting, changing sense of being alive. For the experience of love is inseparable from its background of war and revolution, love's relation to the background being that of the mirage to the desert" (019-002). The Intervention is simultaneously a historical event, a personal memory, and a metaphor for the novel's larger concerns.

### The Train Compartment Campaign

The comic centre of the Intervention narrative is the Admiral's campaign for a train compartment. For a week, Nikolai Vasilievich "kept on applying to the Admiral for a coupé in his train to Omsk" (022-001), deploying every form of emotional pressure. "Every day that week our waiting-room was crowded with petitioners" (022-001). The Admiral, embodying British rationality, insists that "'No was No, and would they please understand it?'" — but the Russians reply "that No was not the point, the point being: What were they to do without Nikolai Vasilievich?" (022-004). The bureaucratic battle for a coupé to Omsk becomes the novel's emblem of political action: immense effort expended on a trivial objective, by people who are at once comic and strangely dignified.

> The Admiral still said No. He held that it was not human nature but just Russian nature, and as an illustration of his point he meant to show that when an Englishman says No he does mean No. But none of them would understand the Admiral's interpretation of No. They had all grown up with the idea that No meant Yes after an adequate amount of pressure and insistence. (022-003)

This passage crystallizes the novel's cultural comedy: the collision between English empiricism ("when an Englishman says No he does mean No") and Russian persistence ("No meant Yes after an adequate amount of pressure"). The Admiral's "sterling value of British character" (022-004) is, in the end, no match for the Bursanovs.

### The Journey Itself

> That night we started on our trip along the great, now pitiably disorganized Siberian track. It was a lovely night in late autumn. The Admiral's special train had been brought over on the main line; and the General and I, both somewhat under the influence of liquor, walked arm in arm up and down the platform; and the General, in an overflow of feeling, spoke piteously of his ruined soul, his wasted life, his blighted career. (022-005)

The journey through Siberia is a pilgrimage through absurdity. The "pitiably disorganized" track, the intoxicated General confessing his soul, the painted train bearing the instruments of a collapsing military operation — all render the Intervention as something between tragedy and farce. Gerhardie's narrator refuses to choose between these registers, insisting instead on their simultaneity.

### The Government of Governments

The political dimension of the Intervention is rendered through the narrator's bewildered encounter with Siberian governance. General Bologoevski explains "that there was a General Horvat who had formed an All-Russia Government, and that there was also a Siberian Government, defying General Horvat on the one hand and the Bolsheviks on the other, and that there were various officer organizations grouped about this or the other government, and some rather inclined to be on their own" (019-012). The multiplication of governments — each defying the others, none effectively governing — is the political equivalent of the Bursanov family: a system of overlapping claims, contradictory loyalties, and institutionalized futility.

### The Admiral at Night

The novel's treatment of the Admiral oscillates between satire and something stranger — a kind of affection for the man's impossible rectitude. At night, "the thought of the gallant Admiral in the bedroom opposite, tucked away between his heavy blankets, his teeth in a glass of water on the table at his side — no presentable sight!" (019-073) humanizes the figure of authority. The Admiral, stripped of his uniform and his "No," is just a man with false teeth — and the narrator's affection for him, like the novel's affection for all its characters, survives the comedy intact.

## The Narrator's Agency

The narrator's role in the Intervention is characteristically ambiguous. He is attached to the Admiral's mission, present at all the key scenes, yet he exercises almost no agency. He watches the Bursanovs besiege the waiting-room; he rides the Admiral's train across Siberia; he encounters Nina again in Vladivostok. But he does not shape events — he records them. This passivity is central to the novel's vision of historical experience: the individual is carried along by forces he cannot control, and the only meaningful choice is how to remember what happened.

## The End of the Intervention

The narrator's final assessment of the Intervention arrives not as a political judgment but as an aesthetic one. The episode has been "relegated to the shelf of history" (019-002), but the feeling of being alive during it — the "ever-shifting, changing sense" — persists. This is the novel's deepest insight about historical experience: the meaning of an event is not the same as its outcome. The Intervention was a failure; the memory of it is a kind of triumph.

## Key Passages

> The Admiral and I, and a few others — interesting types, I can assure you — travelled to Siberia, where we engaged in a series of comic opera attempts to wipe out the Russian revolution. By now, "Intervention" has been relegated to the shelf of history. But I cannot but remember it, not merely as an adventure in futility, as admittedly it was, but as an ever-shifting, changing sense of being alive. (019-002)

This is the novel's thesis statement on the Intervention: history may classify it as a failure, but the experience itself — the "sense of being alive" — resists that retrospective judgment. The irony is that the most futile episode in the novel is also the one the narrator most vividly remembers.
