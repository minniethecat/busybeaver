## Overview

Death appears in William Gerhardie's *Futility* in multiple registers: as personal grief, as philosophical speculation, and as the ultimate expression of the novel's governing theme. Characters confront the death of parents, the deaths of children, and the ever-present possibility of their own deaths against a backdrop of [[war-in-futility|war]] and [[russian-revolution|revolution]]. The novel treats death with characteristic irony — it is both the most serious of subjects and, in the disordered world Gerhardie depicts, strangely resistant to resolution. The revolutionary era's casual violence makes death so ubiquitous that it becomes atmospheric rather than climactic: bodies pile up on train station steps, prisoners are killed "noiselessly" in prison, and mass graves receive the snow's indifferent cover. Gerhardie refuses to let death settle into tragedy, instead presenting it as the ultimate expression of futility — an event so final and so meaningless that it resists the narrative closure that traditional fiction demands.

## Death as Personal Loss

The novel's treatment of death is grounded in specific losses. Nina recalls how "soon after my father's death we lost all our money" [006-022], linking mortality to the family's financial ruin and social displacement. The death of a parent becomes the pivot on which the family's fortunes turn — a personal catastrophe that reshapes all subsequent events. Nina continues: "I belong (I hope you will forgive me for saying it, but it is a salient point in my tragedy) to a very proud family indeed. My father and all my brothers were officers in the German Guards." The father's death is not merely a biographical fact but the origin of "tragedy" — the moment when inherited status dissolves and the daughters must fend for themselves.

Elsewhere, the novel treats death with a casualness that borders on the comic. Visiting Zina's relatives, the narrator encounters "two ancient grandfathers — the oldest thing in veterans — who had outlived their welcome, whose deaths, in fact, were looked forward to with undisguised impatience and freely discussed at meals" (010-017). The grandfathers' deaths are not tragedies but inconveniences — anticipated with the same practicality one might reserve for a long-delayed renovation. This tonal register — death as something simultaneously momentous and banal — is characteristic of Gerhardie's technique.

### Death and the Family Romance

The death of the father, in particular, structures the novel's emotional economy. Nina's loss initiates the chain of events that brings the family under Nikolai Vasilievich's ambiguous protection. The absent father — dead, unreachable — creates a void that Nikolai Vasilievich fills inadequately, his own promised wealth always deferred. In this sense, death is not merely background but the enabling condition of the novel's central relationship: the family's dependence on a man who can never deliver what they need. The question "the grandfathers dead, I suppose?" (019-050) is posed casually, as though death were simply another item on a checklist of family news, indistinguishable from births, marriages, or financial setbacks.

## The Unmourned and Unmournable

Gerhardie's irony extends to the emotional economy of grief. After the street fighting in Vladivostok, the narrator encounters a scene of theatrical mourning:

> "Returning, we perceived the two mothers lamenting bitterly the death of the same children (which they had been quick to take for granted)" (025-025)

The parenthetical aside — "which they had been quick to take for granted" — is devastating in its precision. The mothers have not waited for confirmation of death; they have leapt to the most dramatic possible conclusion. Grief, in Gerhardie's rendering, is not merely felt but performed, and performed with an eagerness that undermines its authenticity.

The episode of feigned death pushes this logic to absurdist extremes. A man lies in a pile of corpses, pretending to be dead:

> "One man three hours afterwards was still breathing heavily. He lay on the steps, bleeding, and covered by other bleeding bodies. Another man in the pile was but slightly hit. He lay alone in the pile of dead" (025-029)

The survivor's strategy — to play dead among the dead — literalizes the novel's governing idea: in a world where death is everywhere, the boundary between the living and the dead becomes porous, uncertain. The man who "shams death" for three hours is a figure for the novel's characters generally, who drift through a landscape of political violence in a state of suspended animation, neither fully alive nor fully dead.

### The Landscape of Death

The morning after the street fighting reveals death's scale and the novel's response to it:

> "The morning unveiled a gruesome picture. The snow that had fallen in the night, and was still falling, now covered the ground and its dead bodies some inches deep. The square, the streets, the yards, the rails, and sundry ditches betrayed them lying in horrid postures, dead or dying." (025-027)

The snow that "covered the ground and its dead bodies some inches deep" acts as nature's own shroud — an indifferent blanket that erases the distinction between the dead and the merely fallen. The narrator continues:

> "Those that were not dead, when discovered were finished with the bayonet by the 'loyal' troops, amid unspeakable yells. Then they lay still and stiff in horrible attitudes."

The quotation marks around "loyal" are Gerhardie's signature irony: the troops who bayonet the wounded are loyal to nobody, loyalty itself having become a meaningless term in the chaos of civil war. The scene reaches its philosophical climax with the narrator's reflection: "Perhaps there is nothing that brings home so clearly the conviction of the temporary nature of all things as a battlefield after the battle" (025-027). Death teaches not transcendence but impermanence — the lesson of futility.

Fanny Ivanovna provides a harrowing account of the executions:

> "'I shall not forget that night,' said Fanny Ivanovna, 'nor what I saw this morning. The faces of the prisoners, some almost green from fright, as they stood with their hands up in the cold grey light of the morning, and the babyish face of that Cossack subaltern — a veritable mother's darling — as he detailed them into two parties.'" (025-028)

The "babyish face" of the executioner — "a veritable mother's darling" — collapses the distance between innocence and cruelty, childhood and death. Gerhardie refuses the comforts of moral clarity: the killers themselves are children.

Even the aftermath is managed with bureaucratic discretion: "prisoners, it was said, were being killed in prison — noiselessly — out of consideration for the Allies in the city" (025-038). The word "noiselessly" is a masterpiece of understatement, transforming mass murder into an act of diplomatic courtesy. Death, in *Futility*, is not merely violent; it is polite.

### The Funeral That Isn't

The novel's most darkly comic treatment of death comes when the narrator inquires about a funeral procession he has seen:

> "'Fanny Ivanovna,' I cried, 'I had meant to ask you what was that funeral procession you all followed yesterday?'" (026-036)

The answer reveals that the corpse was not a victim of political violence but Fanny Ivanovna's inconvenient husband, whose death opens the way for her to become a "merry widow":

> "'Yes,' smiled Nikolai Vasilievich; 'she is a widow now. A merry widow!' And Fanny Ivanovna laughed in a loud and jarring manner. ... We all felt that it was the best thing for the man, and nothing more was said on the subject." (026-039)

Death here is not tragedy but convenience — the removal of an obstacle, greeted with laughter and a collective shrug. "It was the best thing for the man" is the novel's epitaph for conventional mourning.

## Philosophical Confrontation

Baron Wunderhausen, a minor but pointed character, offers the novel's most direct philosophical confrontation with death:

> "'There is no proof,' said Baron Wunderhausen, 'that death is the end. But there is no proof, as yet, that death is not the end.'" (017-039)

The statement is perfectly balanced — a seesaw of negation that leaves the question exactly where it began. This is Gerhardie's philosophical method: not to answer but to frame, to hold open a question that traditional fiction would close with meaning.

The narrator himself speculates about what it might mean to "awaken on the other side of death and ask to be told the reason":

> "'Perhaps,' I said, 'when we awaken on the other side of death and ask to be told the reason, they will shrug their shoulders and will say, 'We don't know. It is beyond us. Do you not know?' ... And we shall never know. Never. ...'" (017-036)

The repetition of "Never" echoes the desperate refrain of the Russian song — "Never, never, never, never ... never" — linking metaphysical speculation to the novel's larger pattern of irresolution. Even in the afterlife, the narrator imagines, there will be no answer, only a celestial shrug.

## Death and Atmosphere

Death suffuses the novel's atmosphere rather than its plot. "The imminence of death" (016-026) is felt in the very landscape — a Siberian house where "other men had met their death" in earlier political violence:

> "In this building, not so long ago, other men had met their death. At each coup d'état this house had been besieged. Fugitives had taken shelter in these rooms. Even on this sofa a body had been stabbed to death. And now we revelled noisily." (028-002)

The juxtaposition is characteristically Gerhardian: a body stabbed to death on this very sofa, and now a drunken dinner party on the same furniture. "And now we revelled noisily" — the sentence pivots on that "and now," as if to say: what else is there to do? The "dark, dark night of early spring" watches this revelry "a breathing, watching presence," a reminder that death is the silent guest at every party.

The revolutionary era's casual violence means that death is never far from consciousness. On the day of the revolution, the narrator hears speeches "of a menacingly proletarian character, threatening death to capital and revolution to the world at large" (014-006). Death becomes political rhetoric, a slogan shouted from platforms. The refrain "The old world is dead: long live the new world!" (014-007) transforms death into a condition of renewal — though the novel itself is skeptical of any such transformation.

## Unresolved Endings

The novel frames death's central philosophical problem as one of resolution denied:

> "The problem, like so many problems, died its death without solution." (024-027)

This meta-commentary could describe the novel itself — a narrative that repeatedly raises questions of meaning, love, and mortality, only to let them expire without resolution. The phrase "died its death" is deliberately redundant: death, in Gerhardie's world, is not a passage to something else but a terminal event, a closure that offers no transcendence.

Uncle Kostia reflects that everything depends "surely on the sadness of one's death to others" (035-025), but the narrator immediately punctures this sentiment:

> "'Yes. But to dwell prematurely on the sadness of one's death to others, Uncle Kostia, is like asking for money in advance. It's commercially unsound.'" (035-025)

The narrator's deflation — reducing death's pathos to a financial transaction — is characteristic of the novel's refusal to let any sentiment stand unchallenged. Death's meaning, if it has any, lies not in metaphysics but in the grief it leaves behind; and even that grief, Gerhardie insists, can be commodified.

### Death's Tonal Range

What distinguishes Gerhardie's handling of death is its tonal instability. In the space of a few pages, the novel moves from the horror of mass execution to the comedy of a convenient funeral, from philosophical speculation about the afterlife to the grim pragmatism of bayoneting the wounded. This tonal range is not carelessness but method. Death in *Futility* is too large and too various to be contained within a single emotional register. It is at once the novel's most serious subject and, in the disordered world it depicts, the one most susceptible to irony — because what could be more futile than the certainty that everyone you love, everyone you hate, and everyone you have never met will, in the end, "die its death without solution"?
