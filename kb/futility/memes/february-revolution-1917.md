## Overview

The February Revolution of 1917 — the first of Russia's two revolutions that year — overthrew the Romanov dynasty and established the Provisional Government. In William Gerhardie's *Futility*, the February Revolution is the pivot on which the narrative turns: it transforms [[petrograd|Petrograd]] from imperial capital to revolutionary city, upends the social order on which the characters depend, and sets in motion the chain of events that will drive them across [[siberia|Siberia]] toward an uncertain future.

## The Revolution in Petrograd

The revolution is experienced in the novel not as grand political theater but as street-level disruption. The narrator, walking "the Troitski Bridge to meet the Admiral," is "stopped by the police" [014-005] — the machinery of the old regime still functioning even as it collapses. The revolution is present in the novel as atmosphere: the sense that everything solid is melting, that the rules that governed life yesterday may not govern it tomorrow.

## From Tsar to Provisional Government

Gerhardie does not narrate the abdication of Nicholas II directly. Instead, the revolution is felt through its effects: the disappearance of old certainties, the emergence of new authorities whose legitimacy is uncertain. The Provisional Government that replaced the Tsar appears in the novel only obliquely, its authority already contested by the Bolshevik alternative that will overthrow it in October.

## Personal Consequences

For the novel's characters, the revolution means not political liberation but practical complication. Visas, travel permits, the legal status of foreigners — all become uncertain. [[fanny-ivanovna|Fanny Ivanovna]]'s German nationality, manageable under the Tsar, becomes a liability under the new dispensation. The revolution is not an idea but a condition: the new fact that must be navigated.
