# Marriage in Futility

Marriage in Gerhardie's novel functions less as a sacred bond than as a negotiable currency — something to be deferred, bartered, dissolved, or simply ignored. The institution is everywhere present and everywhere hollowed out, a formal shell around relationships defined by anything but formal commitment.

## Overview

The novel presents marriage as a paradox: it is the object of deep yearning and the source of constant disappointment. Fanny Ivanovna, who has never been legally married to Nikolai Vasilievich, is the character most invested in "the sanctity of marriage, and the very things, in fact, that had always been denied her: so much so that in her unstable, questionable position she had yet been stringently insistent on this aspect of their life, and always in her heart was reminded that she had no title to enforce that law, no claim, beyond a doleful craving for the decencies of usage and convention" (026-024). The irony is acute: the woman without a marriage is the one who most fervently believes in it.

Zina, by contrast, dismisses marriage as meaningless formalism: "What is marriage? A piece of paper. It's absurd. It means nothing" (010-008). Her declaration of willingness to live with Nikolai without marriage is both modern and calculated — it positions her as liberated while also serving her strategic interests. The novel refuses to take sides between Fanny Ivanovna's wounded traditionalism and Zina's breezy iconoclasm, suggesting instead that both positions are shaped by the same underlying reality: marriage in their world has become purely instrumental.

## In the Novel

The novel's marriage plot is essentially economic. Nikolai Vasilievich's marriage to Magda Nikolaevna depends on Cecedek recovering his confiscated property from the Russian authorities, a bureaucratic process that has been stalled since July 1914. "On his ultimate success in getting some of it refunded his marriage with Magda Nikolaevna must henceforth depend" (015-036). Marriage here is indistinguishable from a financial settlement — the romance is a footnote to the accounting.

Sonia's marriage to Baron Wunderhausen is similarly entangled with money, and Fanny Ivanovna regards it as "a drag on Nikolai Vasilievich's resources" (015-013). The baron, an elderly invalid, consented to the marriage "on condition that we took him home and looked after him" (015-013), turning the wedding into what amounts to a care arrangement.

Fanny Ivanovna's own position is the novel's most sustained meditation on marriage as absence. Her "instincts so discordant with her life" reach toward a respectability that is perpetually out of reach, and the presence of Nikolai's "two other wives" serves "to remind her of the painful irony of her life" (026-024). The novel treats her longing with genuine sympathy, even as it makes clear that the institution she longs for has already been hollowed out by the very people who claim to honor it.

## Broader Context

Gerhardie's treatment of marriage reflects the broader dissolution of social forms that accompanied the Russian Revolution. When all institutions — state, church, law — are in collapse, marriage becomes merely another contested territory. The novel documents this dissolution without nostalgia: the old order of marriage was no more just than the chaos that replaced it, only more orderly.

The Chekhovian influence is visible in the treatment of marriage as a site of quiet desperation, but Gerhardie adds a layer of absurdist comedy. Where Chekhov's characters suffer their unhappy marriages in melancholy, Gerhardie's characters turn marriage into farce — a transaction conducted in hospitals, refugee trains, and Vladivostok hotels, always contingent on mines that never materialize.
