Vladivostok is the terminal city of *Futility*'s geography — the Far Eastern port where [[Nikolai Vasilievich]]'s family, the narrator, and a floating population of refugees, Allied officers, and displaced Russians converge during the [[Russian Civil War]]. The narrator's return to Vladivostok frames the entire novel: "For my ineffectual return to Vladivostok is the effectual conclusion of my narrative" (002-001). A real city transformed by war into a surreal enclave of exile, Vladivostok functions in Gerhardie's novel as both physical setting and metaphysical condition — a place at the edge of a collapsing empire where the characters wait, scheme, dance, and grieve.

### First Impression: Rain and Hopelessness

The family's arrival in Vladivostok establishes the city's emotional register immediately:

> It was raining heavily when we arrived in Vladivostok, and the port, as we surveyed it from the boat, looked grey and hopeless, like the Russian situation. A flat had been allotted us, a bare, unfurnished flat in a deserted house standing in a grim and desolate by-street; and there the Admiral made his temporary headquarters. It poured all day long, and it seemed, indeed, as though the rain, playing havoc with the town, would never cease, even as the misery and blundering in Russia would never cease. (019-003)

The rain is both literal and metaphorical — "playing havoc with the town" and figuring the "misery and blundering" of Russia itself. The correspondence between weather and national condition ("grey and hopeless, like the Russian situation") is Gerhardie's characteristic move: making the external world an extension of psychological and political states.

## The City in the Novel

Vladivostok enters the narrative in Part III, as the family arrives after their trans-Siberian journey: "It was raining heavily when we arrived in Vladivostok, and the port, as we surveyed it..." (019-003). The description is characteristically withheld — Gerhardie's narrator tends to deflect from the physical world toward the human comedy — but the city's atmosphere accumulates through fragmentary impressions. The family crowds into cramped quarters, with Fanny Ivanovna describing the domestic arrangement with characteristic precision:

> "Still, it is more comfortable than living in an hotel. Sonia, Nina and Vera sleep here on the sofa and the bed we drag out from the other room. The adjoining room is Nikolai Vasilievich's and mine. The third is Pav'l Pavlch's, the Baron's. The others have remained at the hotel — I mean Kniaz and Eberheim. I don't care what Magda Nikolaevna does, but I think she has now found a house. And Uncle Kostia and the rest of them will probably settle at his sister's, the Olenins." (020-029)

This catalogue of sleeping arrangements — who sleeps where, with whom, in which room — is a miniature of the exile experience. The family has been compressed from a Petersburg household into a few rooms, distributed across hotels and borrowed flats, their domestic geography mapping their social fragmentation.

The city's strangeness is most sharply rendered through its effect on the characters' interior lives. The narrator observes: "These nights in early spring in Vladivostok are so intolerably sad that one feels one must scream or go mad and rush out into the street and sob" (029-007). This eruption of raw emotion, unusual in Gerhardie's ironic register, marks Vladivostok as a place where the novel's governing tone of comic detachment breaks down.

In Part IV, the city becomes the setting for the novel's final movements. The narrator arrives to find the family unchanged (034-001), the social rituals continue in the hotel rooms, and the narrative closes with him "pacing the streets of Vladivostok, seething with emotion" (037-019). The city that promised escape delivers only more waiting.

### The City of Speculators

Vladivostok during the intervention was a speculator's paradise, and Gerhardie captures this with comic precision. Eisenstein the dentist, a figure of grotesque opportunism, sets up practice there. The Admiral, "though he loathed all Jews," is drawn to Eisenstein because "on his first visit to him he heard Eisenstein engage in a vigorous cursing of his Chinese servant" (024-001). The city's moral economy is reduced to the question of who curses whom and in what language.

Currency speculation flourishes: "the exchange at Vladivostok... how easy it was to make money" (024-005). Nikolai Vasilievich's latest scheme — a "punitive expedition to the goldmines" organized through "two influential generals" requiring "a trip to Tokyo" (019-061) — captures the city's atmosphere of desperate entrepreneurial fantasy. Vladivostok is where hope and fraud become indistinguishable.

## Historical Context

During the [[Russian Civil War]] (1917-1922), Vladivostok was a city of extraordinary strategic and demographic complexity. As the eastern terminus of the Trans-Siberian Railway, it became a gathering point for multiple forces: the [[Czech Legion]], Allied intervention troops (British, American, Japanese, French), White Russian refugees, and speculators of every nationality. The city was under Allied control from 1918 to 1922, creating a pocket of relative stability at the far edge of a disintegrating state.

Gerhardie himself served with the British Military Mission in [[siberia|Siberia]] and spent time in Vladivostok. The novel's depiction of the city — with its British officers, American sailors, currency speculators, and displaced Russian gentry — draws directly on this experience. The "three U.S. naval uniforms" that appear in the hotel (027-039) and the Admiral's dealings (024-001) are not inventions but transcriptions of the historical Vladivostok of 1918-1920.

### The Aleutskaya and Svetlanskaya: Streets of Exile

Gerhardie names Vladivostok's streets with a specificity that grounds the novel's abstraction in real geography. The family is encountered "in the Aleutskaya as they returned from the restaurant Zolotoy Rog" (037-004). The narrator's final walk takes him past the Aleutskaya, down the Svetlanskaya, into a park overlooking the sea:

> We went by the Aleutskaya, bathed in sunshine, switched off down the Svetlanskaya and turned into a park overhanging the sea. Autumn stood at the door with its sombre moods of hopes frustrated, of joys gone, and aims blown to the wind, like leaves of autumn. (034-043)

The street names — Aleutskaya, Svetlanskaya — are real and specific, anchoring the novel's emotional abstractions in a mappable city. The "park overhanging the sea" looks out across the Pacific, toward America, toward Japan — all possible escapes, none of them taken.

## Thematic Significance

Vladivostok is the geographical embodiment of **[[futility-theme|futility (theme)]]** in Gerhardie's novel. The characters have traveled across a continent to reach it — from [[Petrograd]] through [[siberia|Siberia]] — yet nothing is resolved by their arrival. The family's problems are the same; the goldmines remain elusive; the waiting continues. The city is "the effectual conclusion" (002-001) precisely because it is no conclusion at all. As [[fanny-ivanovna|Fanny Ivanovna]] confesses, "She hated Vladivostok. And yet, she told me privately..." (037-004) — hatred and attachment fused in the characteristic Gerhardian paradox.

### Prison in Mitigated Form

The novel's most devastating assessment of Vladivostok comes through [[nikolai-vasilievich|Nikolai Vasilievich's]] silence at the Easter table:

> There was no kulich, no paskha. But Fanny Ivanovna had done up the table as well as she could for the occasion; and there was something pathetic about the poor results she had attained compared with the lustre of prewar Easter-week in Petersburg. We sat at table, and no one spoke. Nikolai Vasilievich was sad. Was he sad because he had returned from prison to something that was only prison in a mitigated form? (029-007)

The question is rhetorical — the narrator knows the answer. Vladivostok, for all the bustle of its streets and the scheming of its speculators, is "only prison in a mitigated form." The family has exchanged the chaos of revolutionary Petrograd for the "relative stability" of a city under foreign occupation, but the condition of exile is unchanged. The missing kulich and paskha — traditional Easter foods — measure the distance between prewar Petersburg and wartime Vladivostok more precisely than any political analysis.

The city also anchors the novel's treatment of **exile**. Vladivostok is technically still Russia, but its distance from the centers of Russian life — [[Moscow]], Petrograd — makes it a kind of internal exile. The characters are displaced not just from their former social position but from the very idea of home. Vladivostok's location on the Pacific, facing away from Europe, makes this displacement geographical as well as psychological.

### The Final Streets

The novel's last image of Vladivostok is the narrator in the Zolotoy Rog restaurant after Nina's steamer has departed:

> I walked briskly to the Zolotoy Rog and lunched lavishly and drank much wine — a luxury in these times! — as if to celebrate the occasion of my soul's release. I felt as if I was being freed from prison. I sat in the crowded, heated restaurant and watched the life bubbling about me — watched it in excitement, in exultation. But after lunch I thought I wanted to make certain — for real freedom could not come till I was certain — that the steamer had finally departed. (037-019)

The liberation is undercut by the compulsion to verify. Even in exultation, the narrator needs proof that Nina has really gone — as if the act of hoping might reverse the departure. The celebration is not quite freedom; it is freedom's performance, staged over lunch in a city that remains.

And amid the final pages, Fanny Ivanovna's relationship to Vladivostok is crystallised in a single, ambiguous sentence:

> Yesterday I had met them in the Aleutskaya as they returned from the restaurant Zolotoy Rog, where they now always went to lunch: the cooking arrangements in the rooms were thoroughly inadequate. Nikolai Vasilievich in his mackintosh and bowler hat looked markedly older and more worn than he had looked two years ago. Perhaps it was the shrewd light of the afternoon that scrutinized his features. There was a curious, mysterious, Monna Lisa look about the face of Fanny Ivanovna: as if she knew a thing or two — and kept it to herself. (037-004)

Fanny Ivanovna's "Monna Lisa look" is the face of exile: knowing, withholding, guarding secrets. She has been in Vladivostok long enough to develop a daily routine — lunch at the Zolotoy Rog, the inadequate cooking arrangements accepted as permanent — and to acquire the enigmatic expression of someone who has stopped explaining herself. The city remains, with its restaurants and its speculators and its rain. The narrator will leave eventually, on the "big steamer" bound for England (002-001), but the family will stay, waiting for goldmines that will never materialise, in a city that is Russia and not-Russia, home and exile, the end of the line and nowhere at all.
