Sonia is the eldest of the three sisters in [[Nikolai Vasilievich]]'s family, sister to [[Nina]] and [[Vera]], and wife to Baron Wunderhausen. Throughout *Futility*, she navigates the collapse of her family's fortunes against the backdrop of the [[Russian Revolution 1917]] and the subsequent [[Russian Civil War]], her marriage to a gold-digging baron serving as one of the novel's central illustrations of futility. She appears across all four parts of the novel, from the family's life in [[Petrograd]] to their displaced existence in [[Vladivostok]].

## Character Overview

Sonia is introduced alongside her sisters in the early chapters of Part I, where the three daughters of Nikolai Vasilievich are described as "beautiful girls" — "Sonia and Nina, so loving, so good, so kind, so gentle and so divinely fair" (006-027). She is positioned as the responsible eldest daughter, though her relationship with her stepmother [[Fanny Ivanovna]] is frequently strained. Sonia serves as a foil to Nina: where Nina is dreamy and romantic, Sonia is pragmatic, sharp-tongued, and increasingly disillusioned with the family's chaos.

Her defining trait is her outspoken frustration. Early in the novel, she declares: "I am sick to death of their faces" (004-016), referring to the suffocating presence of her family's dependents. She threatens to run away — "I'm sick of home. I shall run away" (004-018) — but ultimately stays, and her fate is sealed through marriage rather than escape. By Part III, she has married Baron Wunderhausen, a man whose chief interest, the narrator implies, lies in the family's elusive goldmines (011-003, 012-026).

In Vladivostok, Sonia's married life does not bring liberation. She remains embedded in the family's chaotic domestic arrangements, dodging furniture in the cramped hotel quarters (027-042), and enduring the continued presence of her baronial husband who is "not really disposed of" despite the marriage (011-009). Her story arc embodies the Chekhovian pattern of longed-for change that, when it arrives, changes nothing.

## Relationships

- [[Nikolai Vasilievich]] — father; Sonia's sharp tongue toward him reflects the family's dissolution of patriarchal authority (004-040, 004-043)
- [[Fanny Ivanovna]] — stepmother; their relationship is marked by Sonia's stern reproaches about the family's impractical domestic rituals (004-034)
- [[Nina]] — younger sister; the narrator explicitly compares them, noting Nina is the "compensation" for the family's chaos alongside Sonia (006-040)
- [[Vera]] — youngest sister; the three sisters frequently appear as a triad (009-001, 020-029, 034-031)
- Baron Wunderhausen — husband; a marriage of convenience driven by the family's financial desperation (011-003, 012-026)
- Kniaz — family dependant; involved in the baronial marriage scheme (011-003)

## Key Passages

> "I can't stand this any longer. I am sick to death of their faces."
> (004-016)

Sonia's outburst captures the claustrophobia of the family circle — a household overflowing with dependants, step-relatives, and hangers-on — and her role as the one sister willing to name the dysfunction aloud.

> "I'm sick of home. I shall run away."
> (004-018)

Her threat of escape, delivered in the same early chapter, establishes the novel's central rhythm: the desire to flee a collapsing world, and the impossibility of doing so. Sonia does "run" — into marriage — but the marriage leaves her exactly where she was, a pattern Gerhardie repeats across the novel's relationships.

> Sonia was really angry. "I would rather..."
> (004-043)

Her unfinished sentence, directed at her father Nikolai Vasilievich, hints at the depth of filial resentment that the novel's comic tone keeps just beneath the surface. Gerhardie consistently cuts away from Sonia's anger before it fully resolves, leaving the reader to infer the emotional cost of the family's dissolution.

## Thematic Significance

Sonia embodies the theme of **futility** in its most domestic register. Her marriage to Baron Wunderhausen — engineered by the family, motivated by the phantom goldmines — is a strategic move that achieves nothing. The baron remains in the household; the goldmines never materialize; Sonia's position is unchanged. [[futility-theme|futility (theme)]] is not just a historical condition in Gerhardie's novel but a domestic one, and Sonia is its primary vehicle.

She also illustrates the intersection of **[[Money in Futility]]** and **[[Family in Futility]]**. The family's financial desperation drives Sonia into a marriage that cannot solve it; the marriage, in turn, adds yet another dependent to an already overburdened household. Gerhardie's tragicomic mode — "[[comedy-and-tragedy|comedy and tragedy in futility]]" — is nowhere more evident than in Sonia's story, where the genuinely painful is rendered through farcical domestic scenes.

Sonia's relationship with **[[Music in Futility]]** is also notable: the narrator observes that she "played the piano with an agreeable crispness of touch" (016-021), a detail that distinguishes her from the family's prevailing atmosphere of sentimental excess. Her piano playing suggests an interior life that the novel's comic surface only gestures toward.

## Historical Context

Sonia represents a recognizable historical type: the daughters of the Russian gentry class whose marriage prospects were thrown into chaos by the Revolution. Her union with Baron Wunderhausen — a Baltic German aristocrat pursuing the family's rumored wealth — mirrors the desperate alliances formed by the displaced Russian upper class during the Civil War period. The baron, "after he had married Sonia, was not really disposed of" (011-009), embodying the half-comic, half-tragic fate of women whose marriages solved nothing while foreclosing all other possibilities.
