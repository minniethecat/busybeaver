# Family in Futility

The Bursanov family is the gravitational centre of Gerhardie's *Futility* — not a family in the conventional sense, but a constellation of dependencies, resentments, and shared history held together by Nikolai Vasilievich's money and Fanny Ivanovna's indomitable will. The narrator is "initiated into the complicated affairs of the Bursanov family" (003-001) as if entering an Ibsen drama, with each revelation exposing another layer of entanglement. The family expands and contracts across the novel's geography, absorbing members by financial need, affection, or sheer proximity, until it becomes difficult to say where the Bursanovs end and the rest of the world begins.

## An Ibsen Drama

The narrator describes his introduction to the family as "somewhat in the manner of an Ibsen drama with retrospective revelations" (003-001). The reference is precise: Ibsen's plays excavate buried family secrets, and the Bursanovs have secrets in abundance — unofficial marriages, children from previous unions, money flowing in irregular streams across borders and households.

> "It was somewhat in the manner of an Ibsen drama with retrospective revelations that I was initiated into the complicated affairs of the Bursanov family. I had been asked to call by the three sisters, all speaking simultaneously — a charming bouquet, the queen among whom I recognized only too well, and I called on them one evening in midsummer at their dacha, at a seaside place ten versts from Petersburg." (003-001)

The "three sisters, all speaking simultaneously" present themselves as "a charming bouquet," but the charm is strategic, a performance of domestic harmony that conceals chaos. The narrator arrives "a little bashful perhaps for I had not been invited by their elders" — a social trespass that mimics the reader's own intrusion into a family whose affairs are none of our business but impossible to look away from.

### The Theatrical Frame

The theatrical framing is deepened by the narrator's first inkling that "all was not well with the Bursanov family" (003-021), which arrives during a performance of Chekhov's *Three Sisters* — a play about another family of three sisters trapped in provincial longing. The coincidence is too perfect to be accidental. The Bursanovs are always performing family, and the audience (the narrator, the reader) is always watching them perform. The play-within-the-novel collapses the distance between art and life: the Bursanovs are a Chekhov play that has escaped the stage and taken up residence in a dacha by the sea.

## The Unconventional Household

Nikolai Vasilievich's "unconventional family" (002-010) defies legal and social categories. Fanny Ivanovna lives as his wife but is not legally married to him; Nikolai sends money to his legal wife "regularly every month, so as to keep the children" (006-027); Fanny Ivanovna's family in Germany believes she is married. The domestic arrangement is held together by financial obligation and emotional habit rather than legal structure.

> "Nikolai sent money to his wife regularly every month, so as to keep the children; and so I lived with him just as if I were his wife, and indeed few people knew that I was not. We lived very happily. He sent money to my large family in Germany, regularly every month, and naturally they thought that I was married to him." (006-027)

The nested dependencies are dizzying: Nikolai supports a wife he is separated from, a mistress who is treated as a wife, children from multiple relationships, and a network of in-laws in Germany who must be kept in ignorance. Fanny Ivanovna's justification — "How could I tell them that I was not? What did it matter after all, provided that they didn't know?" — converts deception into a form of care.

### The Failure of Divorce

The family's legal limbo is rooted in the Russian divorce system, which Fanny Ivanovna describes in harrowing terms:

> "I did not at that time know what a hopeless, cruel and heartbreaking thing a Russian divorce really is. Nikolai's wife did all she possibly could to prevent his getting a divorce." (006-025)

Nikolai's legal wife, who has herself run away with a "Jew dentist, with no practice" named Eisenstein, nevertheless refuses to grant a divorce. The result is a permanent state of legal suspension: Fanny Ivanovna is neither wife nor mistress, the children are legitimate in some contexts and illegitimate in others, and the entire family operates in a grey zone that mirrors the novel's larger world of political and social disintegration.

## The Family Economy

The Bursanov family is, above all, an economic entity. The narrator observes with disarming clarity his own position within this economy:

> "And the 'family,' Fanny Ivanovna," I cried, "I mean his wife and her family, his fiancée and her family, you and your family, his sisters and cousins, Kniaz and the others and their families — do they believe in the mines?" (007-052)

The narrator's question — repeated "family" four times in a single sentence — reveals the family's true structure: not a bloodline but a web of financial claimants, each with a stake in Nikolai Vasilievich's mythical Siberian mines. The family expands to include anyone who can plausibly attach themselves to the flow of money: "his families, dependents and hangers-on naturally all followed him" (019-060). When Nikolai leaves Petrograd for Siberia, the train contains "Fanny Ivanovna, Sonia, Nina, Baron Wunderhausen, Kniaz, Eberheim and the bookkeeper Stanitski" in one carriage, while "his wife was in the same train, but in a different carriage" — a mobile domestic arrangement that literalizes the family's spatial and emotional dispersion.

### Internal Factions

The family is riven by factional politics. Zina, a peripheral family member from the Petersburg side, confides to the narrator that Nikolai "is being made to appear a blackguard by people who misunderstand his complex personality" (010-015), blaming his troubles on "those petty worries, those mean jealousies, those paltry domestic squabbles." The three sisters, by contrast, operate as a political bloc in their own right:

> "The three sisters had a way of speaking simultaneously and almost word for word in matters of domestic politics. They were a party in themselves, stubbornly opposed to all the other camps of Nikolai Vasilievich's family." (016-018)

The phrase "domestic politics" is brilliantly chosen: the family is a miniature state, with factions, alliances, and competing claims on the central resource (Nikolai's money). The three sisters' choral speaking — "Helped us indeed!" they cried out simultaneously — suggests a collective identity forged in opposition, the siblings united against the world.

### The Extended Household

Gerhardie renders the family's porous boundaries through a series of set pieces. The visit to Zina's relatives reveals "a very large family in a small flat" filled with "innumerable aunts and uncles, sisters-in-law, second cousins, and suchlike relatives" (010-017). Among them is Uncle Kostia, a writer who has "never published a line" and lives "on the resources of his younger brother, Zina's father" (010-018). Uncle Kostia's parasitic relationship to the family economy — he "would wake up extremely late and would then sit for hours on his bed, thinking" — is treated with affectionate mockery. The family absorbs and sustains even its most unproductive members, because membership is defined not by contribution but by need.

Zina's mother offers the novel's most direct moral judgment of the family's dysfunction:

> "'He is such a really good man,' confided Zina's mother, a grey-haired, God-fearing old lady. 'And to be pursued by those two wicked women both bent on making his life miserable, these cold and heartless daughters who laugh at them ... their own father!'" (010-020)

The old lady's indignation — "for his own daughters to go spying on their father, God forgive me, is the very limit" — provides a moral counterpoint to the novel's prevailing irony. Someone, at least, takes the family's betrayals seriously, even if the novel itself refuses to.

## The Intuition of Disorder

The narrator's first inkling that "all was not well with the Bursanov family" (003-021) comes during a performance of Chekhov's *Three Sisters* — a play about another family of three sisters trapped in provincial longing. The theatrical frame is not incidental: the Bursanovs are always performing family, and the audience (the narrator, the reader) is always watching them perform.

### Domestic Revolution

The family's internal chaos is so consuming that it dwarfs the political revolution outside. The narrator observes:

> "I found the household in a state of wild excitement. However, the event had no connection with the Revolution. In fact, with continual domestic revolutions in their own home, the much ado about the political revolution appeared, particularly to the three sisters, a foolish affectation." (017-007)

The joke is precise: why bother with the Bolshevik Revolution when the Bursanov household produces a new revolution every day? "Continual domestic revolutions" is both comic hyperbole and accurate description — the family's internal upheavals are as frequent, as dramatic, and as inconclusive as the political ones outside.

## Family as Performance

The novel's closing movements show the family performing its unity with increasing desperation. At the Vladivostok parties, "Eisenstein was gleaming with silent satisfaction as he surveyed 'the family.' He felt, I think, that he was one of it at last, and now he was all right" (026-005). The quotation marks around "the family" signal the narrator's skepticism: this is family as wish, not reality. Eisenstein, the man who ran away with Nikolai's wife, is now — improbably, absurdly — absorbed into the family circle, his satisfaction the novel's verdict on the family's limitless capacity for accommodation.

Uncle Kostia, drunk on wine, attempts to articulate the family's bond:

> "We are all one family and Nikolai Vasilievich, our dear Nikolai Vasilievich, here stands at the head of it." (026-033)

The declaration is simultaneously heartfelt and hollow. The family is "one" only in Uncle Kostia's wine-warmed imagination; in reality it is a patchwork of rival claims, secret resentments, and financial dependencies. And yet Uncle Kostia's drunken toast captures something true: the Bursanovs, for all their dysfunction, are a family — not because they love each other but because they cannot escape each other.

The novel's final domestic image is of the three sisters and the "three brothers" walking arm in arm:

> "Such pitiful, heartrending scenes as this became a frequent occurrence. Each of the three sisters walked arm in arm with each of the 'three brothers,' and I trailed alone behind them, a kind of tutor, with a heinous sense of my three months' voyage ripening into a grievance." (036-001)

The narrator, the odd number out, trails behind the coupled pairs — excluded from the family tableau even as he narrates it. The "three brothers" in quotation marks are not brothers at all but suitors, their designation a fiction that the family collectively maintains. The scene is Gerhardie's final word on the Bursanovs: a family that has become interchangeable with its own performance, walking arm in arm toward a future that, like Nikolai's mines, will never materialize.
