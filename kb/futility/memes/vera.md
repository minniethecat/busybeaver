Vera is the youngest of the three daughters of [[Nikolai Vasilievich]], sister to [[Sonia]] and [[Nina]], and the most emotionally unguarded member of the family in William Gerhardie's *Futility*. Fiercely independent, often "sulky" (004-002) and "malicious" (004-026), she oscillates between open hostility and vulnerable affection throughout the novel's four parts. Her return from Moscow to rejoin the family in [[Petrograd]], and her subsequent journey across Siberia to [[Vladivostok]], trace the gravitational pull of the family that none of the sisters can escape.

## Character Overview

Vera is introduced in the early Petrograd chapters as the youngest sister, positioned at a distance from [[Sonia]] and [[Nina]]. The narrator notes bluntly: "there was no love wasted between Vera and her two elder sisters" (004-015). She occupies a "perch in the far corner" of the family apartment (004-021), from which she delivers sharp, unsolicited commentary on the family's affairs. When Nina threatens to reveal a secret, Vera cries "Aha! Aha! maliciously" (004-026), a moment that captures her role as the family's needling conscience.

Her separation from the family — she is sent to Moscow — is a defining early event. The family says goodbye to her "and no excess of emotion was displayed" (005-030), a characteristically Gerhardian understatement that hints at deeper feeling. Vera's return to Petrograd, however, is freighted with unspoken significance: "I understood now why Vera had come back to Petersburg" (009-011). The narrator's cryptic observation suggests that Vera, like all the characters, is pulled back into the family's orbit regardless of her own intentions.

In the Vladivostok chapters, Vera emerges from her "mute and hostile" (008-044) shell. She dances with the British officer Holdcroft (021-067), plays with a gramophone (027-036), and is described as "radiant and marvellously pretty" (034-017). Yet her vulnerability remains: she bursts into tears, her "tear-stained eyes" (008-050) betraying the emotional cost of the family's chaos. In the novel's final pages, "Vera's eyes were red" (037-017), a quiet image of grief as the narrative closes.

## Relationships

- [[Sonia]] — eldest sister; "no love wasted" between them (004-015); Vera needles Sonia about her marriage prospects (004-021)
- [[Nina]] — middle sister; Vera's relationship with Nina is competitive and teasing, but they sleep together on the sofa in Vladivostok (020-029)
- [[Nikolai Vasilievich]] — father; Vera shares the general filial exasperation but with less venom than Sonia
- [[Fanny Ivanovna]] — stepmother; Vera is more direct with Fanny Ivanovna than her sisters, complaining about the late dinners (004-042)
- Eisenstein — family acquaintance; his cry of "Vera! Vèrochka!" (008-045) reveals a tenderness toward her that the narrator notes as either "a revelation" or "only a confirmation" (008-048)
- Holdcroft — British officer; dances with Vera in Vladivostok (021-067), a hint of romantic possibility in the novel's closing chapters

## Key Passages

> Vera cried maliciously. "Aha!"
> (004-026)

Vera's untranslated exclamation — childlike in its cruelty, entirely sincere in its delight — encapsulates her role as the family's unfiltered id. Where Sonia expresses frustration through articulate complaint, Vera simply pounces.

> Eisenstein looked round and saw Vera in the twilight, mute and hostile.
> (008-044)

The image of Vera standing silently in the gathering darkness is one of the novel's most painterly character descriptions. Her hostility is not explained — Gerhardie rarely explains — but the word "mute" is crucial: Vera's rebellions are, finally, as ineffectual as everyone else's designs.

> "He's like Uncle Kostia!" Vera cried. "Has a bath once a year."
> (020-011)

Vera's comic register — blunt, physical, unconcerned with decorum — distinguishes her from both Sonia's sharpness and Nina's dreaminess. She is the sister most willing to puncture the family's pretensions with bathroom humor.

## Thematic Significance

Vera embodies the novel's treatment of **family** as both prison and only refuge. Her trip to Moscow represents the possibility of escape; her return to Petrograd confirms the impossibility. She is the sister most visibly wounded by the family's dynamics — her tears bookend the narrative — yet also the one who most instinctively defends its members. In this, she illustrates the central paradox of **[[Family in Futility]]**: the family that wounds you is also the only family you have.

Vera's trajectory also illuminates **[[Happiness in Futility]]**. In the Vladivostok chapters, she is capable of genuine joy — dancing, playing with the gramophone, appearing "radiant" — but these moments are framed by the novel's larger architecture of futility. Her happiness, like everyone's, is real but temporary, and the novel's final image of her "red eyes" (037-017) restores the dominant key.

## Historical Context

As the youngest daughter of a declining gentry family during the [[Russian Revolution 1917]], Vera occupies a precarious historical position. Too young to be married off like [[Sonia]], too old to be sheltered from the family's collapse, she inhabits a generational gap that the Revolution widened into a chasm. Her return from Moscow to Petrograd, undertaken against the backdrop of political disintegration, mirrors the larger pattern of displacement and return that defined the Russian upper class during the revolutionary period. Vera's occasional radiance in Vladivostok — a city teeming with refugees, Allied soldiers, and displaced Russians — captures the anomalous vitality that flourished in the Far Eastern extremity of the collapsing Empire.
