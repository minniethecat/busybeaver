# Oxford

Oxford appears in *Futility* as more than a geographical location — it is the pole of English identity against which the narrator's Russian experience is measured, a place he leaves for war and revolution and returns to diminished. Its libraries, clubs, and rituals form the counterpoint to the chaos of Petrograd and the absurdity of Vladivostok, yet the novel ultimately questions whether Oxford's order is anything more than a comforting fiction.

## Overview

The narrator's trajectory passes through Oxford twice: first before the war, when he was still a student; and again afterward, when he resumes his interrupted studies. "Then I went to Oxford, and when the war broke out I joined the Navy" (014-001) — this casual juxtaposition of academic life and global catastrophe is characteristic of the novel's tonal range. The second Oxford is diminished: "by contrast, Oxford with its sham clubs and sham societies appeared a doll's house" (032-014), its rituals hollowed by the narrator's experience of war, revolution, and love. After the Bursanovs, after Nina, "Oxford seemed a place of doing things in a small way, of small achievements and small ambitions" (037-017).

## The Two Oxfords

### Before the War

The narrator's first Oxford is barely described — it exists as a taken-for-granted background, the natural habitat of a young Anglo-Russian gentleman. He is there before the war, before Russia, before Nina. "Then I went to Oxford, and when the war broke out I joined the Navy. But just before the revolution, Admiral Butt, who had gone on a special mission to Russia, applied for my 'Anglo-Russian' services" (014-001). The war and the Admiral's mission intervene, and Oxford recedes into memory. This is the Oxford of tradition, of unquestioned assumptions, of a world that seems permanent.

### After the Revolution

The narrator returns to Oxford after his Siberian adventures, having "relinquished my commission" (032-001), but the institution no longer fits. The "sham clubs and sham societies" (032-014) echo the "sham" quality the narrator has come to recognize in all institutions — the British Embassy, Kolchak's government, the Bursanov family itself. Oxford's rituals of belonging now seem as theatrical as the Admiral's comic-opera intervention.

The return is not a homecoming but an estrangement. The narrator has seen a world in which institutions collapse, families disintegrate, and love remains unconsummated. Against that knowledge, Oxford's quadrangles and common rooms cannot compete.

### Love and the Abandoned Oxford

> "There!" I went on protestingly, "I chuck Oxford, come all the way to Vladivostok, spend three months on the journey … because … because I love you, and you—" (034-051)

This protest — the narrator's declaration that he abandoned Oxford for love — reveals the novel's central calculus. Oxford represents the life the narrator might have lived: orderly, English, detached from the Russian chaos that defines him. That he "chucks" it for Nina, for Vladivostok, for the futility of love itself (034-051), is the novel's verdict on the relative power of institutions and passions. The sentence trails off into an ellipsis, the love itself never fully articulated — as if even the declaration cannot escape the novel's governing principle of incompletion.

## Oxford as Symbol

In the novel's cultural geography, Oxford stands for the stability and tradition of English life — everything that the displaced Russian characters have lost. It serves as a point of reference for the "western mind" that the narrator, with his Anglo-Russian background, navigates between. Yet the novel is clear-eyed about the limits of this symbolism: Oxford cannot contain what the narrator has experienced, cannot make sense of the Bursanovs, cannot answer the questions that Siberia and Nina have raised.

The novel's final sections return the narrator to a diminished English life. After Petrograd, Siberia, and Vladivostok — after the Bursanovs, Nina, and the Admiral — the "small achievements and small ambitions" (037-017) of English academic life seem both comforting and inadequate, a doll's house in which the narrator can no longer quite fit.

## Key Passages

> No more novels! Life, I thought, was worth all the novels in the world. And life was Nina. And Nina was life. And, by contrast, the people I encountered seemed pretentious and insincere. The women in particular were unreal. They talked of things that did not interest them with an affected geniality. And, by contrast, Oxford with its sham clubs and sham societies appeared a doll's house. (032-014)

This is the narrator's verdict on his return to English life. The chiasmus — "Life was Nina. And Nina was life" — is the novel's most compressed statement of the narrator's romantic obsession, and the contrast with Oxford's artificiality is devastating. The "doll's house" metaphor echoes Ibsen: Oxford is a miniature world, perfectly ordered and perfectly unreal.
