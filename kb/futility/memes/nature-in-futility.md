## Overview

Nature in William Gerhardie's *Futility* is never merely backdrop. The Siberian landscape — its vast plains, relentless winters, and twilit summers — functions as an active presence, shaping character and mirroring emotion. More abstractly, "human nature" and "Russian nature" are debated by the novel's characters as explanations for the chaos of their lives, making nature a contested philosophical category as well as a physical reality.

## The Siberian Landscape

The novel's Siberian setting provides its most powerful natural imagery. The "dull and melancholy" plains stretching endlessly under rain [022-027] create an atmosphere of existential exposure — characters are dwarfed by the landscape, their personal dramas rendered insignificant by its scale. The "crushing, curbing sense" of nature [028-023] suggests not romantic sublimity but oppression: nature as a force that limits and diminishes human agency.

The Siberian winter is not merely cold but annihilating. The narrator describes a "white immensity" [025-033] that erases distinctions — between town and wilderness, between self and world. This is nature as the novel's governing metaphor: a vast, indifferent expanse against which human projects appear, in the novel's characteristic term, futile.

The journey across Siberia — by train, then by sledge — becomes a passage through a landscape that seems to belong to geological rather than human time. Characters are "swallowed up by the immense distances" [028-012], their identities thinning out as the landscape expands. This dissolution of self in space is the novel's most radical image of futility: the human reduced to a speck on a surface that extends without limit.

## Nature as Temporal Metaphor

The narrator reflects on "the conviction of the temporary nature of human things" [025-027], using the natural world's permanence as a foil to human transience. The Siberian landscape — ancient, indifferent — embodies the novel's central theme of futility: human endeavors are brief perturbations on a surface that will outlast them. The "temporary nature" of love affairs, political regimes, and even nations is set against the geological permanence of the plain.

## Seasons and Mood

The novel's emotional register shifts with its seasons. The Petrograd sections, set in a transitional period between war and revolution, are dominated by interiors — drawing rooms, railway compartments, hotel lobbies — where nature is held at bay. The Siberian sections throw characters into direct contact with the elements, and the novel's tone darkens accordingly. "The spring thaw had turned the roads to rivers of mud" [029-015] — nature is not beautiful but inconvenient, another obstacle in a world already crowded with them.

## Human Nature vs. Russian Nature

The novel's characters are amateur anthropologists of their own condition. Nikolai Vasilievich argues that certain behaviors derive not from "human nature but just Russian nature" [022-003], as if Russianness were a distinct natural force. This debate — whether the chaos surrounding them is universal or specifically national — runs through the novel, with nature serving as the contested ground for theories of character and destiny.

The distinction between "human nature" and "Russian nature" is central to the novel's meditation on national character. The [[russian-soul|Russian soul]] is, in this formulation, a force of nature — not something chosen or cultivated but something given, like the climate. To be Russian is to be subject to a particular kind of weather, internal as well as external.

## The Farce of Experience

In one of the novel's most self-aware reflections, the narrator acknowledges that "experiences must now appear in the nature of a farce" [022-001]. Here "nature" shifts from landscape to essence — the fundamental character of lived experience is farcical. "Indeed nothing less than human nature" [022-002] is at stake in this recognition: the novel suggests that to understand futility is to understand something essential about what it means to be human.

The farcical nature of experience is Gerhardie's answer to tragedy. In a tragic universe, suffering has meaning; in a farcical universe, suffering is merely absurd. Nature — both the Siberian wilderness and human nature — is the ground of this farce, the stage on which the comedy of futility is endlessly performed.

## Nature and Melancholy

The landscape is the physical correlative of the novel's dominant emotion: [[melancholy-in-futility|melancholy]]. The plains are "dull and melancholy in the rain" [022-027]; the "melancholy of the Siberian plain" [022-030] is indistinguishable from the melancholy of the characters who gaze upon it. Nature and feeling interpenetrate so completely that it becomes impossible to say whether the landscape is sad or the characters project their sadness onto it. This ambiguity — is melancholy in the world or in the mind? — is precisely Gerhardie's point.
