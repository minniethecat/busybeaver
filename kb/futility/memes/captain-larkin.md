# Captain Larkin

## Overview

Captain Larkin of the United States Navy appears in a single scene in _Futility_, where he participates in a dinner-table conversation about military discipline. His brief presence contributes to the novel's depiction of the Allied intervention in Siberia and the international milieu of Vladivostok during the Russian Civil War.

## The Discipline Debate

Larkin's sole significant moment comes during a dinner conversation where he is heard "talking of the decline of discipline" (028-007). Sir Hugo challenges him: "Well, Captain Larkin, I don't think I can agree with you, and I should be inclined, if you'll allow me, to suggest to you that your people are not as disciplined as our men, or, should I say, they have not had the same experience of discipline" (028-007). Sir Hugo then praises him with characteristic condescension: "Splendid fellow, Captain Larkin! Good. Very good. Splendid! Ha, ha, ha, ha! You're a diplomatist!" (028-009). Larkin's response is understated: "Captain Larkin ate his fish in silence" (028-010), a quiet reaction that speaks volumes about the dynamics of Allied military social relations.

## Narrative Function

Captain Larkin represents the American presence in the multinational military milieu of post-revolutionary Siberia. His brief, functional appearance—existing primarily to be contradicted by Sir Hugo—exemplifies how Gerhardie uses minor characters to populate the social landscape without developing them beyond their thematic utility. The silent eating of fish is a characteristically Gerhardian detail: the novel's most significant moments often occur in what is not said.
