# Identity in Futility

## Overview

Identity in _Futility_ is portrayed as unstable, constructed, and often imposed from without. Characters struggle to maintain coherent selves amid the dislocations of war, revolution, and exile. Gerhardie extends this concern from personal identity to national character.

## National Character

The novel opens with a reflection on national identity, acknowledging "the light which the Russian master has shed on the national character—a recurring sense of bewilderment" (P02-002). This framing establishes identity as a collective as well as personal concern, and bewilderment as the characteristic response to its instability.

## Assigned Roles

Characters in the novel are assigned roles they must play: "to assign successfully to each character the part he was to play in his life" (011-003). Identity is not chosen but allocated, and the gap between assigned role and authentic self generates the novel's persistent sense of dislocation. [[nikolai-vasilievich|Nikolai Vasilievich]] is assigned the role of family patriarch, [[nina|Nina]] the role of romantic interest, and the narrator the role of observer—but none feels fully at home in their designated part.

## The Self Under Pressure

The Admiral's compulsive negation—"He found himself saying No innumerable times each day" (022-002)—represents identity reduced to a single, futile gesture. When circumstances strip away the social supports of identity, what remains is not an authentic self but a mechanical reflex. This diminishment of personality under the pressure of history is one of the novel's most pointed observations.

## Identity and Exile

The condition of exile strips characters of the contexts that define them—home, profession, language, social rank. [[cecedek|Cecedek]], once a wealthy man whose property was confiscated by the revolutionary authorities (015-036), wanders through the novel as a diminished figure, his former identity erased by historical forces. The exile's identity crisis is both personal and collective: what does it mean to be Russian once Russia itself has been destroyed?
