# Russian Soul in Futility

The "Russian soul" is both a subject of Gerhardie's novel and a problem it deliberately refuses to solve. Through the dual perspective of an Anglo-Russian narrator, the novel navigates between the Western compulsion to explain the Russian character and the Russian experience of that character as simply the texture of lived existence. The term encompasses a distinctive set of national traits — emotional expansiveness, resistance to Western logic, a capacity for both grandeur and absurdity, and a spiritual depth that resists rational dissection — all of which Gerhardie renders with a comic precision that neither sentimentalizes nor debunks.

## Overview

In his preface to *Futility*, the English critic observes that the Western novelist has "undertaken to translate the Russian soul in terms of our vernacular" (P02-004), but this project of translation inevitably fails. The critic describes a recurring experience with the great Russian novels: "just as I feel the directing pressure of the novelist most strongly on my shoulder, when somehow I stumble, the path fades to a trail, the trail to a sand-heap, and hopelessly I perceive that the clue is gone" (P02-002). The Russian soul, in this formulation, is defined precisely by its resistance to Western comprehension — it is what escapes translation.

The preface identifies Gerhardie's distinctive achievement as bridging this gap without falsifying either side. The novelist possesses "enough of the true novelist's 'objectivity' to focus the two so utterly alien races to which he belongs almost equally by birth and bringing-up — the English and Russian; to sympathize with both, and to depict them for us as they see each other" (P02-007). The Russian soul in *Futility* thus emerges not as a fixed essence but as the product of mutual incomprehension and occasional, fleeting understanding. Gerhardie's characters, in the critic's words, are "Sons-and-daughters-of-Somebody, as Russian, I vow, as those of Dostoevsky or Goncharoff, and yet conceivable by me because presented to me by a mind open at once to their skies and to mine" (P02-006).

### The Critic's Frame

The preface to the novel establishes a framework for reading the Russian soul as a problem of literary representation. The critic notes a fundamental asymmetry: "there are few novelists nowadays, I suppose, who will not readily acknowledge that, in certain most intrinsic qualities of the art, the great Russians are what Henry James once called Balzac, the masters of us all" (P02-001). Yet this mastery is precisely what makes the Russians elusive to the Western reader. The same qualities that produce greatness in the Russian novel — emotional intensity, moral seriousness, a tolerance for digression and apparent formlessness — are also what cause the Western reader to lose the narrative thread.

The critic confesses his own prior failures of understanding. He had "vainly hunted, through the familiar scenery of vodka, muzhik, eikon, izba and all the rest, for the souls of the wooden puppets who seemed to me differentiated only from similar wooden puppets by being called Alexander Son-of-Somebody instead of Mr. Jones or M. Dupont" (P02-005). The catalogue of exotic props — vodka, peasant, icon, hut — stands in for a superficial reading of Russianness that Gerhardie's novel, the critic argues, decisively transcends.

## In the Novel

Within the novel itself, the Russian soul is less a philosophical abstraction than a performance. Uncle Kostia, drunk on wine, delivers the most explicit statement:

> "Uncle Kostia stood on the hearthrug, dazed and very red in the face, and held forth at great length: his Russian soul a reservoir of overflowing feeling. 'I feel positively strange,' he said. 'I swear I never felt like this before. I nodded, do you know, to some point in an argument with which at the time I happened to agree, and to my great embarrassment I somehow kept on nodding quite in spite of myself, and keep on nodding — do you see me, Fanny Ivanovna? — though the portion of the argument with which I agreed had long passed, and we were already on a different theme.'" (026-033)

His speech transforms national character into something simultaneously sincere and self-parodic — he means every word, and yet the reader cannot help noticing how thoroughly he performs "Russianness." The "reservoir of overflowing feeling" is both a genuine description and a self-conscious pose, a paradox that Gerhardie leaves unresolved.

Chopin playing from a neighboring apartment provides a counterpoint:

> "We listened to the music and grew still, and our souls were all music as though he had touched their strings. And the house seemed charmed, and the gruff Siberian night looked in through the window and listened in silence." (026-034)

The Russian soul here is not a rhetorical posture but a genuine receptivity to beauty that transcends national categories even as it expresses them. The "gruff Siberian night" that "looked in through the window and listened in silence" becomes an audience for this collective emotional experience, as if the Russian landscape itself participates in the soul's awakening.

The narrator's own relationship to Russia is marked by ambivalence. After the three sisters depart for Shanghai, he wanders through the abandoned park in Vladivostok:

> "I sat on an old bench that had names and initials cut out with a penknife, under the trees turning gold and auburn, and shivered in the sharp autumn wind that sent the fallen yellow leaves whirling down the alley. And the vast sea of Russian life seemed to be closing over me." (002-011)

The image is not entirely welcoming — the sea threatens to engulf as much as it promises to embrace. The Russian soul, for the Anglo-Russian narrator, is something he can observe, describe, and occasionally feel, but never fully inhabit.

### The Russian "No"

A more comic register of the Russian soul appears in the novel's treatment of negation. When the Admiral repeatedly refuses the family's petitions on behalf of Nikolai Vasilievich, the family simply ignores his refusals:

> "Now it is an intrinsic part of the Russian character that it does not accept No for No. It is constitutionally incapable of doing so. Its institutions are all a negation of that principle. And what is more, it refuses to confine that fact to within the Russian border. It regards it in the light of an export." (022-002)

The narrator presents this trait with mock-sociological detachment, as though analyzing a curious tribal custom. The British Admiral, by contrast, insists "that when he said a thing he meant it, this being the sterling value of British character" (022-004). The collision of the Russian soul with British empiricism produces a comedy of mutual incomprehension that is the novel's characteristic mode.

### The Performance of National Character

Nikolai Vasilievich's own performance of Russianness takes a more intellectual form. Quoting Krilov's fables, he displays "that curious pride that Russians usually take in Krilov's un-Russian (I think British) common sense" (010-023). The narrator's parenthetical aside — "I think British" — reveals the Anglo-Russian eye at work, perceiving in the Russian embrace of practical wisdom a paradoxical pride in qualities that Russians themselves recognize as foreign. The Russian soul, in this reading, takes pride in its own exceptions.

Even the revolutionary moment cannot escape Gerhardie's comic framing of national character. The narrator observes "a tendency to flamboyant extravagance and exaggeration" at revolutionary rallies, with speakers shouting slogans like "Down with Armies and Navies!" and "Through red terror to peace, freedom and brotherhood!" (014-006). The Russian soul in its political mode is as susceptible to grandiloquence as Uncle Kostia in his cups.

The drunken dinner scene on the icebreaker brings all registers of the Russian soul together in a chaotic synthesis:

> "And we sang the Russian robber song as best we could, and the Italianos both joined in as soon as they had got the hang of it. Dinner over, we sat about anyhow, and another soloist, a Hungarian prisoner of war, half-wailed, half-sobbed a Russian song that ended with the desperate refrain of 'Never, never, never, never ... never. ...' The Russian General's eyes blinked in the cigar smoke. 'What's that play, you remember — Those are not tears: it's the juice of my soul. The juice of my soul. ...'" (028-020)

The scene descends into chaos: "Cocktails, wine, liqueurs, whisky ... the icebreaker, the juice of my soul, never, never, les disques. ... Like dregs, they had been stirred from the bottom, swam up and began to flow hither and thither" (028-023). In this climactic passage, the Russian soul — that "reservoir of overflowing feeling" — dissolves into multilingual chaos, song, alcohol, and gramophone records. The irony is that this disintegration may be the novel's most authentic portrait of Russianness: a soul that can hold within itself the robber song and the gramophone, Chopin and the "gruff Siberian night," sublime feeling and absurd self-parody, all at once.

## Broader Context

Gerhardie's handling of the Russian soul marks a departure from both Dostoevskian mysticism and Western ethnographic curiosity. Where Dostoevsky treated the Russian soul as a vehicle for spiritual truth and Western observers treated it as an exotic puzzle, Gerhardie treats it as a lived contradiction — something his characters feel, perform, and sometimes weaponize, but never resolve.

The novel's title itself can be read as a commentary on the Russian soul discourse: the grandiloquent claims made for the national character ("reservoir of overflowing feeling") produce, in the end, futility. The Russian soul is real, and it changes nothing. Sir Hugo's declaration — "One national Russia! Excuse me, but — but — but — but if there is any national Russia today it is all on the other side" (029-034) — suggests that the very category of a unified national soul is itself a political fantasy, shattered by the realities of civil war and exile.

### Gerhardie's Anglo-Russian Optic

What distinguishes Gerhardie's treatment of the Russian soul from that of his predecessors is the narrator's dual identity. As the critic notes in the preface, Gerhardie "belongs almost equally by birth and bringing-up" to both the English and Russian races (P02-007). This double belonging produces a narrator who is neither insider nor outsider to the Russian soul. He can describe with ethnographic precision the Russian refusal to accept "No for No," yet he also experiences the emotional pull that the Russian soul exerts — the "vast sea of Russian life" that threatens to close over him.

The result is a Russian soul rendered in stereo: heard both through the Western ear that strains to comprehend and through the Russian heart that simply feels. This stereo vision is Gerhardie's unique formal achievement, and it is what rescues the concept of the Russian soul from the twin dangers of mystification on one side and caricature on the other.
