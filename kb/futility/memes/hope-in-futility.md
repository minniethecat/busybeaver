Hope in *Futility* is not the opposite of futility but its most intimate companion. Throughout William Gerhardie's novel, characters continue to hope — for goldmines, for marriages, for political outcomes, for the simple arrival of dinner — in the face of accumulating evidence that hope is unjustified. This persistence of hope against reason is not treated as heroic or redemptive; it is, rather, the psychological mechanism that makes futility bearable, and therefore the mechanism that perpetuates it. [[Nikolai Vasilievich]]'s quiet aphorism — "It is easier to hope" (017-025) — is the novel's most concise statement of its emotional economy.

### The Paradox of Easier Hoping

Nikolai Vasilievich's full statement is more nuanced than a simple proverb, and it deserves close attention:

> "It is easier to hope," said Nikolai Vasilievich, "and be disappointed, it is easier to hope knowing that one will be disappointed, than not hope at all." (017-025)

The grammar is carefully broken — the sentence stumbles over itself, repeating "easier to hope" twice, as if trying to convince itself. Hope is not a belief that things will improve; it is a psychological necessity that persists even when disappointment is certain. Nikolai's insight is that hopelessness is effortful — it demands a kind of existential vigilance that most people cannot sustain. Hope, even hope that knows it will fail, is the path of least resistance. This inverts the conventional morality of hope: hoping is not courageous but lazy.

## The Vocabulary of Hopelessness

Gerhardie deploys the word "hopeless" and its variants with strategic frequency across the novel. The cumulative effect is the construction of hopelessness as an atmosphere rather than a judgment — a condition of the world that characters inhabit like weather:

- The narrator's early confession: "hopelessly I perceive that the clue..." (P02-002) — hopelessness introduced in the preface as the narrative's default mode
- The judgment on characters: "You're... hopeless" (008-039) — applied to Fanny Ivanovna's domestic chaos
- The landscape itself: "that sense of utter hopelessness that clings to a Siberian town" (026-001) — hopelessness as geography
- The final domestic tableau: "hopes frustrated, of joys gone" (034-043) — hopelessness as elegy

The word appears in both comic and serious registers, often within the same paragraph. When [[Fanny Ivanovna]] says "We shall pull through; we shall pull through" (017-030), the narrator records it as "hopefully" — and the adverb, in Gerhardie's hands, carries both its ordinary meaning and a residue of authorial irony.

### Hopelessness as Atmosphere

Gerhardie's most sustained evocation of hopelessness is not a character's emotion but the Siberian landscape itself:

> Who can convey at all adequately that sense of utter hopelessness that clings to a Siberian winter night? Wherever else is there to be found that brooding, thrilling sense of frozen space, of snow and ice lost in inky darkness, that gruesome sense of never-ending night, and black despair and loneliness untold, immeasurable? (026-001)

The hopelessness is not inside the characters — it "clings" to the landscape, external and enveloping. Gerhardie's technique is to make hopelessness a property of the world rather than a personal failing. When the narrator says "I remember there was something hopeless about that night, a sense of dread about the political and economic chaos" (017-044), the hopelessness belongs to the night itself, to the atmosphere, not to any single observer.

## Nikolai Vasilievich's Philosophy of Hope

Nikolai Vasilievich articulates the novel's central insight about hope with uncharacteristic clarity: "It is easier to hope" (017-025). The statement is neither optimistic nor cynical. It describes a psychological fact: hoping requires less effort than despairing, and it is this very economy of effort — hope as the path of least resistance — that keeps the characters suspended in their futile pursuits.

The goldmines represent hope in its purest form: a fantasy of wealth that persists across decades, across the [[Russian Revolution 1917]], across the [[Russian Civil War]], across the trans-Siberian journey to [[Vladivostok]]. The goldmines never materialize, but the hope of them structures the family's entire existence.

### Enchanted Castles on Flimsy Foundations

The goldmine hope reaches its most exquisite absurdity in the Vladivostok chapters, where Nikolai continues to build elaborate dreams on nothing:

> What Nikolai Vasilievich actually waited for was best known to himself. His hopes had been built up on the assumption of a sudden recovery of his goldmines, a possibility he connected somehow with political developments in the Far East. It would not be fair to examine critically the grounds he had for this ambitious expectation, from any rational standpoint. Nikolai Vasilievich had built up enchanted castles of a rare magnitude and beauty upon this somewhat flimsy and elusive foundation. (028-004)

The narrator's tone is exquisitely balanced between affection and mockery. The "enchanted castles of a rare magnitude and beauty" are acknowledged as aesthetically impressive even while their foundation is exposed as "flimsy and elusive." This is Gerhardie's treatment of hope in miniature: the hope is beautiful, the hope is baseless, and the narrator refuses to choose between those two judgments.

Nikolai Vasilievich has "great hopes" of various schemes (020-065); characters "hope" for outcomes they know will not arrive. Hope is not a strategy but a habit, and Gerhardie treats it with the same amused, melancholic detachment he brings to all human irrationality.

### Hope's Collapse into Rage

Hope, when it finally fails, does not lead to serene acceptance. It leads to fury:

> Suddenly Nikolai Vasilievich went wild. I had never seen him in that state before. He abused the General in immoderate terms. He accused him of having deceived him, of having played with his hopes. (023-017)

The "brewing restlessness" accumulated through "years of intermittent waiting" does not dissipate into wisdom — it explodes into a tantrum. The "last puff of ineffectual remonstrance before his final sinking into hopeless resignation" (023-017) is a precise description of hope's final stage: not acceptance but exhausted rage.

## The Gleam of Hope

The novel's most striking image of hope comes late: "perhaps there was more hope now than there had been hitherto" (029-008), followed shortly by "There may be a gleam of hope there at least" (029-018). The "gleam" is characteristic Gerhardie — hope reduced to a flicker, a quality of light rather than a substance, visible at a distance but likely to disappear on approach:

> "I wasn't, Admiral," he said. "I might be one now. There may be a gleam of hope there at least. There's none here." (029-018)

That these lines appear in the Vladivostok chapters, when the family's situation is objectively most desperate, is essential to the novel's method: hope and hopelessness are not sequential (first you hope, then you despair) but simultaneous, each intensifying the other.

## Hope and the Novel's Structure

*Futility* is structured as a circle — the narrator's "ineffectual return" (002-001) — and hope is the engine of circular motion. Characters hope, act on hope, fail, and hope again. The narrative does not progress; it accumulates. Each hope, when disappointed, generates not despair but the next hope, and the novel's length is a function of this cycle's repetition. The narrator himself participates in this structure: he returns to Vladivostok hoping for resolution, finds none, and ends "seething with emotion" (037-019) — hoping still, the reader infers, though the hope has no object.

### Hope Beyond the Novel's End

The novel's final passage extends hope beyond its own boundaries. The narrator watches the steamer depart, lunching in the Zolotoy Rog restaurant, "watched it in excitement, in exultation" (037-019). But "real freedom could not come till I was certain — that the steamer had finally departed" (037-019). Even after Nina has sailed, he needs to verify her departure — as if hope itself might summon her back, as if the act of hoping keeps possibility alive. The novel does not conclude hope; it merely stops writing about it.

> Autumn stood at the door with its sombre moods of hopes frustrated, of joys gone, and aims blown to the wind, like leaves of autumn. (034-043)

The image is elegiac but not final. Autumn stands "at the door" — it has not yet entered. Frustrated hopes, like autumn leaves, will return. The cycle of hope and disappointment is not broken by the novel's ending; it simply continues off the page.

## Comparison to Related Themes

Hope in *Futility* is distinct from the novel's other emotional registers:

- Unlike **[[love-in-futility|Love in Futility]]**, which is directed at specific objects (Nina, the family), hope is diffuse — it attaches to whatever prospect is nearest, from goldmines to dinner
- Unlike **[[happiness-in-futility|Happiness in Futility]]**, which arrives in transient bursts (dancing, gramophones), hope is chronic — a low-grade fever rather than a spike
- Unlike the **[[comedy-and-tragedy|comedy and tragedy in futility]]** binary, hope occupies a middle register — neither funny nor sad, but simply human
