## Overview

In November 1918, Admiral Alexander Kolchak seized power in [[omsk|Omsk]], overthrowing the Directory that had briefly governed anti-Bolshevik Russia and proclaiming himself Supreme Ruler. This coup established the political order within which the later sections of William Gerhardie's *Futility* take place — the White Russian regime whose British allies include [[admiral-butt|Admiral Butt]] and [[sir-hugo|Sir Hugo]], and in whose army [[baron-wunderhausen|Baron Wunderhausen]] serves as a second lieutenant.

## The Coup

Kolchak's coup was swift and bloodless — a ministerial crisis resolved by military force. The novel does not narrate the coup directly; instead, it registers the coup's effects through the changed status of its characters. Kolchak's Omsk is a city of uniforms and conferences, of British officials and Russian generals, of a war being prosecuted with uncertain means against an increasingly certain enemy.

## The Kolchak Regime

The novel's treatment of Kolchak's government is characteristically indirect. "A second lieutenant in Kolchak's army" [024-006] is how [[baron-wunderhausen|Baron Wunderhausen]] appears in the later chapters — his military rank a sign of how the coup has reorganized social life. The regime exists as context: the reason British officers are in Siberia, the framework within which the novel's characters pursue their private concerns.

## Futility of the White Cause

Gerhardie's irony extends to the White cause itself. The novel never explicitly judges Kolchak's regime, but its treatment of official endeavor — the endless conferences, the vague missions, the "arrant nonsense" [020-001] spoken by generals — suggests the futility of the entire enterprise. Kolchak's coup, like everything in the novel, is a grand gesture whose consequences fail to match its ambitions.
