# Pride in Futility

## Overview

Pride functions as both a virtue and a vice in _Futility_, driving characters to maintain dignity in adversity while also preventing the compromises that might improve their circumstances. Gerhardie portrays pride as a quintessentially futile emotion — it preserves the self while ensuring its isolation. Every character in the novel is motivated by pride in some form, and every expression of pride carries within it the seed of its own undoing.

## Fanny Ivanovna and the Pride of Sacrifice

Fanny Ivanovna's narrative is structured around pride. Her self-introduction is a declaration of lineage: "I belong ... to a very proud family indeed. My father and all my brothers were officers in the German Guards" (006-022). This pride in ancestry is immediately complicated by the shame of her profession — she became a music-hall performer to support her family, and "they were ashamed of my profession, as I was myself" (006-022). The paradox is precise: her pride in family drives her to a sacrifice that injures the very pride it serves.

The narrator frames personal sacrifice explicitly in terms of pride: "I thought it would be a matter of duty to sacrifice my personal pride for Nikolai's children" (006-027). This formulation reveals how pride can be transmuted into nobility, but the transaction is never complete. Fanny Ivanovna "didn't want to add further injury to their pride" (006-026) by telling her German family the truth about her unmarried status — and thus pride generates deception, which generates further pride-wounds, in a recursive cycle of self-protection and self-harm.

## Pride as Consolation

In a world of diminished circumstances, pride becomes a form of consolation: "the children, as I say, were such a pride and consolation to me" (006-040). Gerhardie shows how pride attaches itself to whatever remains after loss, making it both a survival mechanism and a delusion. The children become a substitute for the status Fanny Ivanovna has lost, and her pride in them — "they were such a pride and consolation" — is genuine but also instrumental. Pride fills the void left by respectability.

## Nikolai's Curious Pride

Nikolai Vasilievich displays a different kind of pride — the intellectual vanity of the Russian who quotes Krilov's fables "with that curious pride that Russians usually take in Krilov's un-Russian (I think British) common sense" (010-023). The narrator's parenthetical comment undercuts Nikolai's pride even as he reports it: the national vanity of quoting a fabulist for his Britishness is itself an instance of the futility the fable describes. Nikolai also declares himself "proud — don't laugh — I am proud that at my age I should be capable of such love" (006-065), anticipating mockery and thus protecting his pride preemptively through self-deprecation.

## The Narrator's Creative Pride

The narrator himself is not immune. Describing his scheme for reorganizing the Bursanov household, he admits: "I could barely suppress the look of pride that every now and then would steal over my face. I explained and propounded with something of the insolence of a creator, an artist and a prophet" (011-016). This self-awareness — the recognition that his own pride in his rational plan is itself ridiculous — is characteristic of the novel's recursive irony. No one, not even the ironist, escapes the gravitational pull of pride.

## Pride and Place

Even the landscape participates: Lake Baikal is "that proudest of lakes" (022-022), momentarily serene before a gale "fearful and furious" shatters its composure. Nature itself, in Gerhardie's rendering, is subject to the same dynamics of pride and humiliation that govern human affairs.

## Key Passage

> "I belong ... to a very proud family indeed. My father and all my brothers were officers in the German Guards. ... I had a good voice and ... I went on the stage, into musical comedy. And, Andrei Andreiech, curious, is it not, that in spite of the fact that I and I alone kept the whole of our family ... they were ashamed of me." (006-022)
