## Overview

Stanitski is [[nikolai-vasilievich|Nikolai Vasilievich]]'s bookkeeper and one of the most endearing minor characters in William Gerhardie's *Futility*. Present at family gatherings, business discussions, and moments of crisis, Stanitski occupies the ambiguous position of employee-turned-confidant, his emotional transparency serving as a counterpoint to the more guarded emotional performances of the novel's principal characters.

## The Bookkeeper's Role

Stanitski is introduced as Nikolai's employee: "his bookkeeper Stanitski, at the instigation of his" [017-008] superiors, becomes involved in the family's financial machinations. But his role quickly exceeds the merely professional — he is present at dinners, at discussions of marriage and money, at the moments of crisis that constitute the novel's plot. The bookkeeper who knows too much is a familiar comic type, and Gerhardie deploys Stanitski with characteristic lightness.

## Emotional Transparency

Unlike the novel's principals, who perform their emotions with varying degrees of self-consciousness, Stanitski simply feels. "And Stanitski became emotional" [017-010] — the sentence captures both his vulnerability and his lack of emotional armor. He "gently, like a cat, felt his" way around Nikolai's moods [017-012], suggesting a sensitivity born of long service to a volatile master. His emotions are genuine in a world where genuineness is rare and often punished.

## The Optimist

The narrator calls him "an optimistic gentleman — Stanitski" [017-014], and the label sticks. In a novel defined by futility, Stanitski's optimism is both comic and strangely admirable — a refusal to be defeated by circumstances that would justify despair. "What a belief in" human nature [017-014], the narrator remarks, half-mocking and half-respectful.

## Social Integration

Stanitski's presence at family gatherings — "the bookkeeper Stanitski, all met in their frequent" conferences [028-005] — illustrates the porous boundary between business and family in the novel's world. He is listed alongside family members in the novel's roll-calls of characters [019-060], his status as employee not preventing his inclusion in the intimate circle. This blurring of professional and personal is characteristic of the novel's social world.
