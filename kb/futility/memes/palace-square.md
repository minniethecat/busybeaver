# Palace Square

## Overview

Palace Square (*Dvortsovaya Ploshchad*) is the central square of Petrograd (now Saint Petersburg), dominated by the Winter Palace and the Alexander Column. It serves as a brief but evocative backdrop in William Gerhardie's _Futility_, anchoring the narrator's arrival in revolutionary Petrograd in a specific, historically resonant location. As the symbolic heart of imperial Russia — the stage for coronations, military parades, and the Bloody Sunday massacre of 1905 — the square carries an immense weight of historical association that Gerhardie evokes with characteristic understatement.

## In the Novel

### Arrival at the Embassy

The narrator first recalls Palace Square on the morning he arrived in Petrograd to meet [[admiral-butt|Admiral Butt]] at the [[british-embassy-petrograd|British Embassy]]: "I ascended the broad staircase with its worn red carpet to the Chancery" (014-002), looking out toward the square. The Embassy's location on the square places the narrator — a British officer — at the precise intersection of imperial Russian power and the diplomatic apparatus of the Allied intervention. The "worn red carpet" of the staircase subtly echoes the fading grandeur of the regime headquartered across the square.

### The Night Walk

The narrator returns to the square later that night with [[nikolai-vasilievich|Nikolai Vasilievich]], walking through it with "a sense of dreary enchantment" (017-044) as the February Revolution unfolds around them. This phrase — "dreary enchantment" — captures the narrator's characteristic tonal register: the enchantment of being at the center of world-historical events, tempered by the dreariness of cold, confusion, and the nagging sense that history is an inconvenience rather than a drama.

The walk through the square is both political and personal: the Revolution is happening, but the narrator's mind is partly on Nina, partly on Nikolai Vasilievich's goldmines, partly on the absurdity of being a British officer in a collapsing empire. Palace Square thus becomes a stage on which the novel's central technique — the collision of historical gravity with personal triviality — is performed in miniature.

## Symbolic Significance

### Stage of Imperial Power

Palace Square was the historic stage of Russian imperial power: from the Winter Palace, the Tsars ruled an empire stretching from Poland to the Pacific. The square witnessed the Decembrist uprising of 1825, the emancipation celebrations of 1861, and most famously, the Bloody Sunday massacre of January 1905, when Imperial Guards fired on unarmed protesters, shattering the myth of the benevolent Tsar.

### Ironic Setting for Revolution

The square's imperial grandeur provides an ironic setting for the novel's opening revolutionary scenes. A space designed to project the permanence and majesty of the Romanov dynasty now hosts the confusion of its dissolution. Gerhardie does not dwell on this irony explicitly — it is not his method — but the juxtaposition is present in the physical details: the broad cobblestones, the columns of the Winter Palace, the darkness of a city in its final days as the imperial capital.

### Contrast with Later Settings

Palace Square marks the beginning of the novel's geographical trajectory: from the imperial center (Petrograd) eastward through Siberia to the Far Eastern port of [[vladivostok|Vladivostok]]. The square's ceremonial architecture gives way to the chaos of the trans-Siberian journey, the wooden houses of [[omsk|Omsk]], and finally the cosmopolitan bustle of Vladivostok. The novel's geography is a story of dispersal, and Palace Square is its point of origin — the center from which everything radiates outward and never returns.
