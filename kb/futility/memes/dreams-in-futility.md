## Overview

Dreams and the act of dreaming recur throughout William Gerhardie's *Futility* as a counterpoint to the novel's waking absurdities. Characters dream of past loves, lost cities, and alternate lives; the narrative itself drifts between waking and dreaming, blurring the boundary between actual events and the inner life. Dreams in the novel serve as escape, as premonition, and as a more honest register of emotional truth than the characters' waking words.

## Dreams as Memory and Longing

The narrator's memory of Petrograd is itself dreamlike: "How often then I dreamed of those white nights of Petrograd" [012-001], transforming the city into a luminous, irretrievable past. This nostalgizing dream-memory stands in contrast to the messy, compromised present. The city of Petrograd becomes not a place but a state of mind, accessible only through dreaming: "in this half light, this half dream, this unreal half existence" [012-028].

## The Shared Dream

Midway through the novel, the narrator and his companions experience a collective dreamlike state: "A horrible dream crept over us" [012-003], suggesting that the Siberian exile itself has a nightmarish, surreal quality. The shared nature of this dream — it affects all the characters simultaneously — reinforces the novel's sense that individual experience is subsumed into a larger, shared futility.

## Dreams of Nina

Nina becomes inextricably associated with the narrator's dream-life. "She would come to me in dreams" [032-004], he confesses, and even in "waking hours, that were like dreams, involuntarily I would find" himself thinking of her [032-005]. The blurring of dream and waking reality around Nina suggests that his love for her operates on a different plane from ordinary experience — more vivid, more obsessive, and ultimately more futile.

## Music as Dream

The novel explicitly links music and dreaming: "it has all been to him a dream in music" [026-034]. This connection — Nikolai Vasilievich experiences life as aesthetic reverie — positions dreaming as a mode of perception that is truer than rational analysis. "When we hear it" [026-034], the narrator continues, suggesting that art and dreams share the capacity to transport.

## Premonition and the Uncanny

In the novel's final section, the sudden return of Nina's image comes as "the dream of Nina, like a wave from nowhere" [033-048], an involuntary resurgence that carries the force of premonition. The narrator's sense that "somewhere I must have dreamt this, or perhaps had known it" [033-050] introduces an uncanny dimension — dreams collapse past, present, and future into a single overwhelming sensation, much as the novel itself collapses linear time.
