# Professor Metchnikoff

## Overview

"Professor Metchnikoff" is a horse belonging to the narrator's household in _Futility_. The name—pompously academic yet applied to an animal—is characteristic of Gerhardie's comic method. The horse serves as a vehicle for slapstick humor while also embodying the novel's theme of things not behaving as expected.

## Comic Role

Professor Metchnikoff is introduced as "that beautifully proportioned nag" waiting at the door for the narrator (011-058). The horse's dignified appearance belies its behavior: it has a habit of retreating backward, described as "composed and dignified," toward tramlines at crossroads. The Tartar coachman Alexei had warned that the animal was "unreliable" (011-061), a judgment the narrator had dismissed. The narrator's initial encounter is one of aesthetic appreciation: "My heart leapt within my breast at the agreeable sight of my beautifully proportioned nag 'Professor Metchnikoff'" (011-058).

## The Retreat Scene

The horse's most memorable scene involves a slapstick retreat while the entire household—Sonia, Nina, Vera, Kniaz, Fanny Ivanovna, Nikolai Vasilievich, and Baron Wunderhausen—watches from the balcony, laughing. The narrator, "seated in the open carriage," retreats steadily backward "to the accompaniment of laughter from the balcony," only for "the fiendish nag" to suddenly jerk forward and trot home "as though nothing had ever been the matter." The narrator's helpless question — "But where in heaven was 'Professor Metchnikoff'?" (011-059) — captures the comic desperation of the scene.

## Symbolic Function

Professor Metchnikoff's contrary behavior mirrors the novel's broader themes: the gap between expectation and reality, the futility of human will against perverse circumstance, and the comic indignity that undercuts pretension. The horse's academic name is itself a joke about the pretensions of intellect in a world governed by absurdity.
