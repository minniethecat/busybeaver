## Overview

William Gerhardie's *Futility* opens with an explicit acknowledgement of its theatrical model: "It was somewhat in the manner of an Ibsen drama" [003-001]. This reference to the Norwegian playwright Henrik Ibsen (1828–1906) establishes the novel's self-conscious relationship to dramatic form. Ibsen's method — the use of domestic settings to explore social and psychological tensions, the gradual revelation of past secrets, the ironic counterpoint of the trivial and the catastrophic — provides a template for Gerhardie's own narrative strategy.

## The Ibsenite Opening

The novel's opening comparison to Ibsen is both homage and misdirection. Ibsen's dramas typically unfold within a single household over a compressed timeframe, with past events — a forgery, an affair, a secret — erupting into the present with destructive force. Gerhardie adopts this structure of domestic revelation while extending it across the vast geography of revolutionary Russia. The "Ibsen drama with Russian" characteristics [003-001] that the narrator describes is Gerhardie's formula for his own novel: Scandinavian structure animated by Russian chaos.

## Theatrical Form and Narrative Voice

Gerhardie's use of Ibsenite conventions is characteristically ironic. Ibsen's plays build toward moments of revelation and moral reckoning; *Futility* builds toward moments that refuse to resolve. The narrator's detached, essayistic voice — so different from Ibsen's intense naturalism — transforms the dramatic inheritance into something new. The novel is an Ibsen play narrated by an observer who refuses to take the drama seriously.

## Beyond Ibsen

Gerhardie's relationship to Ibsen is more complex than simple influence. By naming his model in the first sentence, he simultaneously invokes and exceeds it. The novel's episodic structure, its geographical range, its tonal mixture of comedy and melancholy — all push beyond the confines of Ibsenite naturalism toward something that is distinctly Gerhardie's own. The Ibsen reference is a point of departure, not a destination.
