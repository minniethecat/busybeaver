## Overview

War permeates William Gerhardie's *Futility* as both historical backdrop and existential force. The novel spans the period of [[world-war-i|World War I]], the [[russian-revolution-1917|Russian Revolution]], and the ensuing [[russian-civil-war|Russian Civil War]], using military conflict less as a subject in itself than as a vast, impersonal mechanism that disrupts personal lives, derails plans, and underscores the novel's central theme of futility. The narrator, a British officer attached to the Military District Staff in Petrograd, navigates a world where war is omnipresent yet strangely detached from the characters' immediate emotional concerns.

### The Narrator's Entry into War

The narrator's trajectory is shaped directly by the war's outbreak: "when the war broke out I joined the Navy" (014-001). This decision places him in Petrograd and sets the entire narrative in motion. His arrival at the British Embassy reveals the surreal detachment of wartime diplomacy from the actual fighting:

> I still remember very vividly the morning following on my arrival in Petrograd, when I had to meet the Admiral for the first time at the British Embassy. I ascended the broad staircase with its worn red carpet to the Chancery. Very perfect young men, very perfectly dressed, were conversing in very perfect intonations about love among monkeys. It struck me as delightfully human for diplomats. (014-002)

The juxtaposition of global war with embassy chatter about "love among monkeys" establishes the novel's tonal approach to conflict: war is real but distant, filtered through layers of bureaucracy, irony, and personal preoccupation.

## War as Historical Backdrop

The novel opens in a Russia that "especially since the war, has undertaken to translate" English novels (P02-004), immediately establishing the conflict as a cultural watershed. The narrator's own trajectory is shaped by war: "when the war broke out I joined the Navy" (014-001), an act that places him in Petrograd and sets the entire narrative in motion.

The Great War serves as an invisible clock throughout Part I of the novel. Characters measure time by its progress: "the war was in its third year" (019-030), their personal dramas unfolding against artillery barrages that reach Petrograd only as distant rumor. Gerhardie's technique is to keep the war at arm's length — heard, discussed, feared — but never witnessed directly on the battlefield, reinforcing the novel's focus on the civilian and bureaucratic experience of conflict.

### The Solipsism of War

One of the novel's sharpest comic insights is the way characters interpret global events through purely personal lenses. [[nikolai-vasilievich|Nikolai Vasilievich]] "had regarded the war almost as a deliberate attempt" of providence to ruin his life (015-003). This is not merely comic egotism — it is Gerhardie's diagnosis of how ordinary people process historical catastrophe: by making it about themselves. The war exists not as a geopolitical event but as an inconvenience to Nikolai's mining investments, an obstacle to the narrator's romance, a pretext for [[fanny-ivanovna|Fanny Ivanovna's]] marital machinations.

> They were just the same. Kniaz sat in the corner of the little drawing-room in his usual chair, and it seemed that the revolution had impressed him. But how it had impressed him no one could have divined. Need I say that the three sisters sat in much the same positions, waiting — waiting for developments? Nikolai Vasilievich was very bitter. He had regarded the war almost as a deliberate attempt of providence to ruin him. (015-003)

## Personal Disruption

War functions primarily as a force that shatters personal plans. Nikolai Vasilievich's financial schemes are interrupted when war broke out and temporarily affected his prospects. The [[family-in-futility|Bursanov family]] sits unchanged by revolution, "waiting — waiting for developments" (015-003), their stasis and self-absorption forming a comic counterpoint to the historical convulsions around them.

Fanny Ivanovna, as "a German subject," faces displacement "when war broke out she was to go back" to Germany or be interned (015-008), illustrating how geopolitical conflict intrudes into intimate family arrangements. Her ambiguous national identity — a German in wartime Russia — mirrors the broader theme of displacement that defines the novel's emigre characters.

The narrator's romantic pursuit of [[nina|Nina]] is repeatedly thwarted by military obligations. His love story cannot proceed on its own terms because the machinery of war — orders, postings, the chain of command — intervenes at every critical moment, embodying the novel's thesis that individual desire is helpless against historical forces.

### War's Collateral Damage: The Marriage of Convenience

The war's most absurd intrusion into personal life is the marriage of Fanny Ivanovna to Eberheim — a purely bureaucratic arrangement necessitated by her status as an enemy alien:

> As Fanny Ivanovna was not married to him, she was a German subject, and when war broke out she was to go back to Germany or be interned in Vologda. She refused to go to Germany till Nikolai Vasilievich had provided for her for life, but as the war had further crippled his finances he was not in a position to give her the money; so they married her to Eberheim, an elderly gentleman of German extraction but a Russian subject. (015-008)

This is the war as bureaucratic absurdity: a woman marries a stranger to avoid internment, while remaining with the man she considers her husband. The Great War becomes, in Gerhardie's hands, a generator of farcical complications rather than heroic narratives.

## War and Revolution

The novel collapses the distinction between external war and internal revolution. For [[nina|Nina]], both are equally unreal — phenomena she simply overlooks:

> All this — the war, the revolution — she had overlooked: and it did not exist. Scriabin — she had overlooked him. And he did not exist. But she was there, watchful. (016-025)

This willful detachment from history is at once survival mechanism and moral blindness. Nina's ability to erase world events by ignoring them is both comic and chilling — a personal version of the collective Russian talent for absorbing catastrophe.

The narrator reflects on the absurdity of the Eastern Front with weary sarcasm: "muddleheaded politicians! The war was to be won on the Eastern" Front through incompetence (019-065). Gerhardie's treatment of Russia's exit from the war via the [[treaty-of-brest-litovsk|Treaty of Brest-Litovsk]] is characteristically oblique — the narrator registers the event through its effect on the family's dinner-table conversation rather than through political analysis (029-009). This narrative strategy insists that history's meaning is found in its impact on ordinary lives, not in diplomatic archives.

The collapse of the Eastern Front creates the power vacuum into which the [[bolsheviks|Bolsheviks]] step, linking the world war causally to the revolution. The British Military Mission, including the narrator, finds itself stranded in a country that has technically left the war but whose internal conflict is just beginning.

## Civil War and Aftermath

As the narrative moves into [[siberia|Siberia]], the [[russian-civil-war|Russian Civil War]] becomes the dominant military context:

> Who can convey at all adequately that sense of utter hopelessness that clings to a Siberian winter night? Wherever else is there to be found that brooding, thrilling sense of frozen space, of snow and ice lost in inky darkness, that gruesome sense of never-ending night, and black despair and loneliness untold, immeasurable? Add to this the knowledge of a civil war fumbling in the snow, of people ill-fed, ill-clothed and apathetic, lying on the frozen ground, cold and wretched and diseased. (026-001)

The word "fumbling" is carefully chosen — it suggests a conflict without clear fronts, without heroic narrative, a war of exhaustion and confusion rather than decisive battles.

The aftermath of conflict pervades the Siberian sections — a "Hungarian prisoner of war, half-wailed, half-sobbed" (028-020), a figure emblematic of the war's human detritus scattered across the Russian interior.

### The Banquet on the Site of Death

One of the novel's most striking passages juxtaposes revelry with recent violence:

> In this building, not so long ago, other men had met their death. At each coup d'etat this house had been besieged. Fugitives had taken shelter in these rooms. Even on this sofa a body had been stabbed to death. And now we revelled noisily. (028-002)

The dinner party continues over the literal furniture of murder. This is Gerhardie's vision of war's aftermath: not solemn remembrance but heedless continuation, life asserting itself over death with almost offensive vigour. The house in [[vladivostok|Vladivostok]] becomes a palimpsest of violence, each layer of death covered by another layer of feasting.

The [[allied-intervention|Allied Intervention in Siberia]] — the narrator's ostensible purpose — is portrayed as a venture of uncertain objectives and diminishing returns. The British presence in Siberia, meant to reverse the Bolshevik victory, becomes instead a backdrop for personal entanglements, reinforcing the novel's pattern of subordinating political action to private drama.

## War and the Russian Soul

War in *Futility* is frequently filtered through the lens of [[russian-soul|the Russian soul]], a concept the narrator invokes to explain both Russian military endurance and Russian political chaos. Nikolai Vasilievich's mysticism about "the soul of Russia" coexists uneasily with the practical reality of military collapse: the army "had melted away" (022-040), leaving behind a population that seemed to absorb catastrophe as a permanent condition.

## Existential Dimension

The narrator ultimately frames war as part of a broader pattern of [[futility-theme|futility]], a meditation triggered by [[uncle-kostia|Uncle Kostia's]] philosophical provocations during the train journey east:

> I returned to my coupe. It was evening now and the lights were lit. Dismal forests stretched over hundreds of versts. I lay back and the ideas let loose by Uncle Kostia set to work in my mind. And I thought: Where are we heading? Why? What is it all for? And then I thought of the war with its hysterical activity; I pictured soldiers boarding trains, to return to the front; the loading of ships with war materiel; the rush in the Ministry of Munitions. I thought of the Germans seething with energy in just the same way; and I contrasted in my mind this hustling activity, this strained efficiency. (022-079)

The word "hysterical" is significant — it connotes not just chaos but a kind of medical pathology, a collective nervous breakdown expressed as organized violence. The narrator's train-journey meditation, set against "dismal forests" stretching over "hundreds of versts," places war alongside other human endeavors that seem frantic yet purposeless. The symmetry between British and German activity — both nations "seething with energy in just the same way" — undercuts any notion of moral superiority, reducing the conflict to a vast, symmetrical waste.

War serves as the novel's ultimate metaphor for the human condition: vast forces beyond individual control, enormous expenditures of energy yielding trivial or inexplicable results, and the persistence of private feeling — love, greed, jealousy — in the midst of public catastrophe. The narrator's final position, adrift between England and Russia, between war and peacetime, between love and its disappointment, is the war's true legacy: a permanent state of in-betweenness.
