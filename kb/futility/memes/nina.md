# Nina

The middle of the three Bursanov sisters and the narrator [[andrei-andreiech|Andrei Andreiech's]] romantic obsession throughout Gerhardie's *Futility*. Nina is the "queen" of the bouquet (003-001) — distinguished from [[sonia|Sonia]] and [[vera|Vera]] by her father's steel-grey eyes, a disquieting sidelong glance, and an obstinacy inherited directly from [[nikolai-vasilievich|Nikolai Vasilievich]].

## Character Overview

### First Impressions

Nina appears with her sisters in all four parts of the novel, but she occupies a distinctive position within the trio from the very first meeting. The narrator is invited to the family dacha by "the three sisters, all speaking simultaneously — a charming bouquet, the queen among whom I recognized only too well" (003-001). The recognition is immediate and physical:

> But Nina called out "Papa!" and he turned round, and then I saw that she had his eyes, those steel-grey eyes softened by a charming, disquieting, sidelong look that was hers to give; and every now and then she would look straight into your eyes — anybody's eyes — down into your very soul, bathing her soul in your soul. (003-009)

The eyes are both inherited and transformed. They are [[nikolai-vasilievich|Nikolai Vasilievich's]] steel-grey eyes, but Nina's version is "softened" — the hardness of the father gentled into something that "bathes" the viewer's soul. The "disquieting" quality signals the destabilizing effect she will have on the narrator throughout the novel.

### Directness and Truth-Telling

Nina is the family member most willing to speak uncomfortable truths. When the narrator assumes [[fanny-ivanovna|Fanny Ivanovna]] is the sisters' mother, Nina corrects him bluntly:

> "She is not our mother," Nina said. "She is … Fanny Ivanovna." (003-008)

The ellipsis is telling — there is no word for what Fanny Ivanovna actually is ([[nikolai-vasilievich|Nikolai Vasilievich's]] unmarried partner, his financial dependent, his tormentor), so Nina supplies only the name. This directness recurs throughout the novel. When Fanny Ivanovna harangues [[nikolai-vasilievich|Nikolai Vasilievich]], it is Nina who defends him: "You leave him alone, Fanny Ivanovna" (008-030).

### Temperament: Between Comedy and Tragedy

Nina's temperament is a blend of seriousness and irony. During a family crisis, she sits "mute, wearing her natural expression, half serious, half ironic" (004-022). This "natural expression" is her default — not participating in the family drama but not detached from it either. At other moments she is petulant and childlike, "lying back on the sofa and swinging her pretty legs" (004-041) while complaining "I'm tired of waiting for Papa." The oscillation between childish impatience and adult knowingness is central to her characterization.

The narrator's first description of Nina's demeanor is framed through the Chekhov play the family watches together. At the *[[three-sisters|Three Sisters]]* performance, the narrator sits "behind Nina" (003-022) and reflects that Chekhov's characters seem "born on the line of demarcation between comedy and tragedy — in a kind of No Man's Land" (003-022). Nina herself occupies exactly this territory. She is funny without being a clown, tragic without being a victim.

### The Chekhovian Mode

The parallel between Nina and Chekhov's characters is deliberate and sustained. Watching *Three Sisters*, [[fanny-ivanovna|Fanny Ivanovna]] and the sisters watch "as if the Three Sisters were indeed their own particular tragedy" (003-022). Nina, positioned between the narrator's gaze and the stage, becomes a living point of contact between art and life. The narrator's "stupid scepticism that comes from too much happiness" (003-022) is a defense against the play's emotional demands — and, by extension, against Nina's.

### Silence and Wisdom

In repose, Nina projects an intelligence that may exceed her actual self:

> When she was silent Nina looked so wise. Perhaps she seemed wiser than she actually was. All this — the war, the revolution — she had overlooked: and it did not exist. Scriabin — she had overlooked him. And he did not exist. But she was there, watchful. (016-025)

The repetition of "overlooked" is striking. Nina does not ignore history; she wills it out of existence. The war, the revolution, even [[scriabin-in-futility|Scriabin]] — none of them exist if she does not attend to them. "But she was there, watchful" — her presence is the only thing she cannot overlook. The narrator's observation "Perhaps she seemed wiser than she actually was" introduces a note of skepticism even into his admiration.

### Physical Presence in Siberia

By the Siberian chapters, Nina has matured. Her reappearance in [[vladivostok|Vladivostok]] is dramatic:

> Then the door opened and Nina slipped into the room. I was staggered by her looks. To my mind she was irresistible. When she saw me she stopped dead. (020-013)

The narrator is "staggered" — a word that registers genuine physical impact. Nina "slipped into the room" with the same "sylphine litheness" the narrator later describes (020-080). She has grown into a more self-possessed presence, one who moves through the crowded social world of [[vladivostok|Vladivostok]] with ease. [[general-bologoevski|General Bologoevski]] stands beside the narrator and admires her openly:

> "What eyes! What calves! What ankles!" he was saying. "Look here, why in heaven don't you marry her? …" (020-091)

The General's appreciation is characteristically crude, but it registers something real: Nina has become a woman who attracts attention without seeking it.

## Relationships

### Andrei Andreiech (The Narrator)

The narrator falls in love with Nina from the start and remains in love with her throughout the novel, though the relationship is never consummated or even properly declared. He walks home from the theatre with "Nina clinging to my arm" (003-028), sits behind her during *[[three-sisters|Three Sisters]]* (003-022), and is tormented by her absence at the departure pier.

His love expresses itself through proximity rather than declaration. On the train to [[omsk|Omsk]], his thoughts drift into fantasy:

> When I closed my eyes I could see Nina, and my drowsy thoughts would linger: "She is a moi. ... Tucked away in that compartment with her sisters. ... A moi. ... Now they were undressing for the night. ... A moi. ... At a handstretch. Always there. But there was no hurry. O life ... leisurely life ... !" (022-006)

The repeated "a moi" (French for "mine") is partly possessive fantasy and partly self-mockery. The narrator knows she is not his; the fantasy is the point. "But there was no hurry" — the novel's governing temporality, the postponement of action into an indefinite future that never arrives.

The narrator even attempts to reorganize the family's lives — producing a "chart and diagram" which he shows to Nina: "I proceeded to lay before her the principles on which I said I was going to reshape their lives" (011-016). He and Nina bend over the chart together — "our heads nearly touched" (011-016) — in a scene of intimate absurdity. The narrator's love expresses itself as a bureaucratic impulse, a desire to systematize the chaos of the household. Nina's response is characteristically laconic: "'Why?' said Nina, with a look at me" (011-038).

By the novel's final section, the question of marriage has become public. [[madame-olenin|Madame Olenin]] announces that the narrator "has lost his heart to Nina" (035-007) and that "Andrei Andreiech wants to marry her" (035-009). The narrator protests: "But she doesn't want to" (035-011). [[madame-olenin|Madame Olenin's]] reply is devastatingly simple: "Ask her" (035-012). He never does.

The relationship's final note is absence. The family comes to see the narrator off "in practically its full strength. But she did not come. That settled it" (030-004). The repetition is merciless: "She had not come. That settled it" (030-004). The narrator attempts to celebrate his "soul's release" by lunching lavishly and drinking much wine (037-019), but he returns to the wharf anyway — and finds the steamer gone: "My heart sick with palpitation, I looked over the godowns of the Volunteer Fleet. The steamer had departed" (037-020). Love in *Futility* is always a missed boat.

### Nikolai Vasilievich

Nina shares her father's eyes and his obstinacy. She is the one who calls out "Papa!" when her sisters treat him with contempt (003-009), and her relationship with him is protective rather than hostile. [[fanny-ivanovna|Fanny Ivanovna]] diagnoses the family trait with frustration:

> "Obstinacy runs in the family. His grandmother was like that. Nina has it from her father. Obstinacy! What a terrible vice! There is no reason, no meaning in obstinacy beyond further obstinacy. It's a disease. There is no strength, no character about it. The weakest thing on earth is so often obstinate." (006-061)

[[fanny-ivanovna|Fanny Ivanovna's]] analysis — that obstinacy is a weakness, not a strength — applies to both father and daughter. But where [[nikolai-vasilievich|Nikolai Vasilievich's]] obstinacy drives the plot (the campaign for the train compartment, the refusal to divorce), Nina's obstinacy is quieter: a withholding, a silence, an absence.

### Fanny Ivanovna

Nina's relationship with her stepmother (not legally married to [[nikolai-vasilievich|Nikolai]]) is characterized by wary honesty. Nina corrects the narrator about [[fanny-ivanovna|Fanny Ivanovna's]] status — "She is not our mother. She is ... Fanny Ivanovna" (003-008) — and defends her father against [[fanny-ivanovna|Fanny Ivanovna's]] importunities: "You leave him alone, Fanny Ivanovna" (008-030). There is no open hostility between them, but no warmth either. Nina navigates the stepmother relationship with the same ironic detachment she brings to everything else.

### Sonia and Vera

Nina is part of an indivisible trio, always appearing alongside her sisters. The narrator first encounters them as "three sisters, all speaking simultaneously" (003-001). At home, the three are "purring round [Fanny Ivanovna] like three pretty kittens" (009-002). Yet within the trio, Nina is "the queen" (003-001) — the one the narrator singles out from the beginning, the one whose absence at the pier matters most.

By the novel's end, the trio dissolves. [[fanny-ivanovna|Fanny Ivanovna]] surveys them with a doleful look:

> "Sonia, Nina and Vera, the kittens. The real Sonia, Nina and Vera have lost patience with us." (037-008)

The "real" sisters have detached themselves from the family fiction. The kittens have become adults, and their patience — with [[nikolai-vasilievich|Nikolai Vasilievich's]] schemes, with [[fanny-ivanovna|Fanny Ivanovna's]] dramas — has run out.

### Suitors

In the Siberian chapters, Nina attracts the attention of multiple suitors. A British flag-lieutenant "had fallen in love with Nina at first sight and now drank in greedily everything I had to say about her" (028-002). A U.S. naval officer dances with her "the next moment" after arriving at a party (021-062). The narrator watches these attentions with a mixture of jealousy and passivity — he never competes, only observes.

## Thematic Significance

### The Line Between Comedy and Tragedy

Nina embodies the Chekhovian mode that Gerhardie sustains throughout the novel. She exists on "the line of demarcation between comedy and tragedy — in a kind of No Man's Land" (003-022). She is at once a love object in a farce and a figure of genuine pathos. The narrator's love for her is never consummated, never even properly declared; it is a feeling suspended in the novel's atmosphere of waiting, delay, and incompletion.

### Obstinacy as Family Inheritance

Like her father, Nina carries obstinacy as a family inheritance (006-061). But where [[nikolai-vasilievich|Nikolai Vasilievich's]] obstinacy is active and public, Nina's is passive and private. Her refusal to appear at the pier is the novel's purest expression of what [[fanny-ivanovna|Fanny Ivanovna]] calls "obstinacy beyond further obstinacy." Nina does not explain, does not apologize, does not even acknowledge her absence. She simply does not come.

### The Overlooked World

Nina's capacity to "overlook" — to will events out of existence by withdrawing attention from them — is perhaps her most radical quality. "All this — the war, the revolution — she had overlooked: and it did not exist" (016-025). In a novel saturated with historical catastrophe, Nina represents a kind of existential refusal. History cannot touch you if you refuse to look at it. The narrator finds this quality both admirable and unnerving, and he spends the entire novel trying to get her to look at him.

### Absence as Presence

The novel's treatment of Nina increasingly relies on her absence. The narrator calls on the family "in Nina's absence" (006-001). She does not come to the pier. The steamer departs while he is drinking wine. By the novel's end, Nina has become less a character than a condition — a state of yearning for something that is always elsewhere. "She did not come. That settled it" (030-004) is the novel's emotional epitaph.

## Key Passages

> I should not have thought that that youngish-looking, rather short but handsome man ... was their father, by the negligent, almost contemptuous manner in which his daughters treated him. But Nina called out "Papa!" and he turned round, and then I saw that she had his eyes, those steel-grey eyes softened by a charming, disquieting, sidelong look that was hers to give; and every now and then she would look straight into your eyes — anybody's eyes — down into your very soul, bathing her soul in your soul. (003-009)

This early description establishes Nina's physical and emotional magnetism. The eyes are both her father's and uniquely hers — inherited yet transformed. The "disquieting" quality of her gaze signals the destabilizing effect she will have on the narrator throughout the novel.

> When she was silent Nina looked so wise. Perhaps she seemed wiser than she actually was. All this — the war, the revolution — she had overlooked: and it did not exist. Scriabin — she had overlooked him. And he did not exist. But she was there, watchful. (016-025)

The most philosophically dense passage about Nina. Her wisdom may be an illusion, but her watchfulness is real. The capacity to "overlook" history is both a limitation and a power.

> Then the door opened and Nina slipped into the room. I was staggered by her looks. To my mind she was irresistible. When she saw me she stopped dead. (020-013)

The reunion in Vladivostok. The verb "staggered" registers genuine impact; Nina "stops dead" at the sight of the narrator. Both reactions suggest a mutual recognition that neither party will act upon.

> The family came to see us off in practically its full strength. But she did not come. That settled it. (030-004)

The devastating simplicity of Nina's non-appearance. The repetition — "She had not come. That settled it" — is one of the novel's most economical passages.

> And even now that they have gone and the steamer is about to reach the docks of that far eastern Paris, I can see them very vividly before me as they stood on the deck of the Simbirsk: three pretty kittens, each lovelier than the other and quite irresistible together. (037-018)

The final image of the three sisters — "three pretty kittens" on the deck of a departing ship. The narrator watches from the quay, unable to follow.

## Appearances

Nina appears throughout all four parts of the novel. In Part I ([[petrograd|Petrograd]]), she is introduced alongside her sisters at the dacha and the Mohovaya flat, attends the [[three-sisters|Three Sisters]] performance, and reveals the truth about [[fanny-ivanovna|Fanny Ivanovna's]] status. In Part II (the Revolution), she endures the family's dislocation. In Part III ([[siberia|Siberia]]), she attends [[admiral-butt|the Admiral's]] dances and concerts in [[vladivostok|Vladivostok]], attracting suitors. In Part IV, the chapter titled after her (Part IV, Chapter on Nina) culminates in her departure by ship and her pointed absence at the farewell pier (030-004, 037-008).
