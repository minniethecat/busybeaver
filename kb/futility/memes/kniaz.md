## Overview

Kniaz — whose name is the Russian word for "prince" — is one of the most quietly persistent presences in William Gerhardie's *Futility*. Always "sitting neatly in his usual" corner [015-015], he is a fixture of the family circle, a participant in all gatherings, and a key enabler of [[nikolai-vasilievich|Nikolai Vasilievich]]'s financial schemes. His presence is so constant and so unobtrusive that he becomes almost architectural — part of the furniture of the novel's world.

## The Name

"Kniaz" is at once a title and, in the novel's usage, a personal name. "His name was" Kniaz [007-030], the narrator notes, as if the title had absorbed the man. This ambiguity — is he a prince called Kniaz or a man called Prince? — is characteristic of Gerhardie's treatment of identity. In the displaced world of revolutionary Russia, titles become names, and names become uncertain.

## The Business Partner

Kniaz's practical function is to sustain Nikolai's belief in the Siberian goldmines. "Kniaz has some shares," Fanny Ivanovna explains [007-031], and "Kniaz helps him to sustain that belief" [007-049] — the verb "helps" suggesting collaboration in a shared illusion. The old man's presence legitimizes Nikolai's financial fantasies, providing the appearance of substance to schemes that the novel consistently undermines.

## The Silent Presence

Kniaz's defining characteristic is his stillness. "Kniaz sat in the corner of the little" room [015-003], always present, rarely speaking. When Fanny Ivanovna snaps at him — "Kniaz! Don't sit there like that" [015-016] — the rebuke only emphasizes his immobility. He is the audience before whom the family drama is performed, the constant witness to chaos he does nothing to prevent.

## Place in the Family

Kniaz is listed alongside family members in the novel's recurring roll-calls: "Sonia, Nina, Vera, Kniaz, and Baron Wunderhausen" [010-022]; "Sonia, Nina, Vera, Kniaz, Fanny Ivanovna, Nikolai Vasilievich" [011-063]. His inclusion in these lists marks him as family-adjacent — not blood but belonging, one of the extended household whose boundaries are defined by proximity rather than kinship.
