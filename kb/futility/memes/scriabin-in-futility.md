# Scriabin in Futility

## Overview

Alexander Scriabin (1872–1915), the Russian composer and pianist, is referenced in _Futility_ as a cultural figure whom the narrator notes that Nina has "overlooked." This glancing reference is characteristic of Gerhardie's method of cultural name-dropping, where artistic figures serve as shorthand for entire worlds of sensibility.

## Nina's Overlooking

The narrator describes his ecstatic response to Scriabin's music: "Scriabin, I burst out as the music stopped, is a persistent knocking at the doors of heaven" (016-019). But his attempt to share this enthusiasm fails: "She had overlooked: and it did not exist. Scriabin — she had overlooked him. And he did not exist. But she was there" (016-025). The narrator then tries other composers — "Wagner, Scriabin, Debussy and Richard Strauss. Nothing doing. I tackled Ibsen" (036-005) — all in vain. The parallel is revealing: Scriabin's ecstatic, mystical modernism represents a strain of Russian culture that Nina's indifference renders irrelevant—beautiful but futile. As the novel's preface suggests, she had "overlooked" the war, the revolution, and Scriabin alike.
