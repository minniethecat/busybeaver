## Overview

The Red Army was the military arm of the Bolshevik state, created in 1918 to defend the revolution against its enemies — foreign and domestic. In William Gerhardie's *Futility*, the Red Army is the force that will eventually defeat the [[white-army|White movement]] and consolidate Bolshevik power across Russia. It appears in the novel less as a presence than as a pressure — the reason the characters are fleeing eastward, the threat that makes [[omsk|Omsk]] a temporary refuge rather than a permanent home.

## Trotsky's Creation

Leon Trotsky, as Commissar of War, built the Red Army from scratch — recruiting former Tsarist officers, instituting conscription, and imposing the discipline that transformed revolutionary militias into a professional fighting force. Gerhardie does not narrate this process, but its effects are felt throughout the novel's later sections. The Red Army's advance across [[siberia|Siberia]] is the clock ticking beneath the novel's leisurely social scenes.

## The Bolshevik Military

The Red Army represented a new kind of military organization — politically indoctrinated, commissar-supervised, and ideologically committed to the defense of the revolution. For the novel's characters, who belong to the old world the Bolsheviks are dismantling, the Red Army is the embodiment of everything they fear. "General Bologoevski's" claim that he "understood the Bolsheviks" [020-004] is comic precisely because understanding the Bolsheviks is irrelevant — what matters is that the Bolsheviks are winning.

## The Victor

The novel is set during the period when the outcome of the Civil War remained uncertain, but Gerhardie's irony anticipates the Red Army's victory. Every White conference, every British dispatch, every Kolchak proclamation is shadowed by the knowledge — available to the reader if not the characters — that the Reds will win. The Red Army in *Futility* is thus the force of history itself: impersonal, inexorable, and indifferent to the personal dramas it displaces.
