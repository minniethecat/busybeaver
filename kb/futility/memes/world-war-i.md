# World War I in Futility

World War I is the great historical event that frames William Gerhardie's *[[futility-theme|Futility]]*, though it appears in the novel less as a subject than as a condition — the inescapable fact that determines where characters may live, whom they may marry, and whether their financial schemes will succeed. The war "broke out" and everything changed; the novel traces the consequences of this rupture across personal lives that seem, in their intimate chaos, both dwarfed by and strangely indifferent to the global catastrophe.

## Overview

The war functions in *Futility* as both origin story and framing device. It sends the narrator to [[oxford|Oxford]], to the Navy, and ultimately to [[petrograd|Petrograd]]; it displaces [[fanny-ivanovna|Fanny Ivanovna]] from her German nationality; it creates the chaos that enables [[nikolai-vasilievich|Nikolai Vasilievich's]] goldmine dreams and the [[allied-intervention|Allied Intervention]] that follows. Yet the war itself is almost never described directly. It is a negative space, defined by its effects rather than its events. As the narrator reflects at the novel's close, the war's "claim" on him persists long after its formal conclusion.

## The War's Arrival

### Personal Disruption

The novel registers the war's onset through personal disruption rather than public declaration. For the narrator, it is simply the moment a career begins:

> Then I went to Oxford, and when the war broke out I joined the Navy. (014-001)

The sentence is characteristically understated — no patriotic flourish, no moral justification. War is a bureaucratic fact: it sends the narrator to a specific institution, which leads to a specific assignment. Shortly before the [[russian-revolution|Russian Revolution]], [[admiral-butt|Admiral Butt]], "who had gone on a special mission to Russia, applied for my 'Anglo-Russian' services" (014-001). The war sets every subsequent movement in motion.

For [[fanny-ivanovna|Fanny Ivanovna]], the war creates an identity crisis of the most practical kind. Unmarried to [[nikolai-vasilievich|Nikolai Vasilievich]], she is classified as a German subject — an enemy alien in wartime Russia:

> As Fanny Ivanovna was not married to him, she was a German subject, and when war broke out she was to go back to Germany or be interned in Vologda. (015-008)

The solution is a marriage of convenience to [[eberheim|Eberheim]], "an elderly gentleman of German extraction but a Russian subject" (015-008). Nationality, in wartime, becomes a legal category that overrides every personal reality. Fanny Ivanovna's German origins — "My father and all my brothers were officers in the German Guards" (006-022), she proudly tells the narrator — are simultaneously her greatest distinction and her greatest liability.

### War as Bureaucratic Atmosphere

When the narrator arrives at the [[british-embassy-petrograd|British Embassy]] in Petrograd, the war is present not as conflict but as a kind of atmospheric pressure. The diplomats are "very perfect young men, very perfectly dressed" who converse "in very perfect intonations about love among monkeys" (014-002). The war — the whole reason for the Embassy's heightened importance — recedes behind social performance. This is Gerhardie's key insight about the Great War in his novel: it is simultaneously the most important thing in the world and the thing no one seems to be paying attention to.

### The Military Mission

The narrator's own role is distinctly unheroic. He serves on the "Military District Staff" (014-002), not at the front. The war is experienced through paperwork, rumor, and the gradual deterioration of the world the characters inhabit. [[admiral-butt|Admiral Butt]] and his party travel to [[siberia|Siberia]] to conduct what amounts to a bureaucratic war:

> We engaged in a series of comic opera attempts to wipe out the Russian revolution. (019-002)

The phrase "comic opera" — applied to [[allied-intervention|intervention]] rather than direct combat — captures the novel's sense that the war's continuation into the Russian theater is already a kind of farce.

## The Eastern Front

### Strategic Absurdity

Russia's war is fought on the Eastern Front, a theater Gerhardie evokes through absence rather than description. The most extended discussion of military strategy occurs as pure comic dialogue. [[nikolai-vasilievich|Nikolai Vasilievich]] insists that the war will be won on the Eastern Front — not for strategic reasons but because of mineral resources:

> "The war was to be won on the Eastern Front," said Nikolai Vasilievich. "It ought to, anyhow."
>
> "Why?"
>
> "Well, because the Eastern Front has unquestionably the greater resources in mineral wealth. The goldmines ought to be cleared of the enemy before anything else if you want to win the war." (019-065–068)

The logic is impeccable from a goldmine-owner's perspective and absurd as military strategy. [[nikolai-vasilievich|Nikolai Vasilievich]] cannot separate his private financial interests from his geopolitical analysis. The Eastern Front is not where armies clash but where his mines happen to be located.

### The Disintegrating Army

The novel captures the disintegration of the Russian military through the figure of a divisional commander whose troops have simply vanished:

> It transpired that four regiments composing the division had gone over to the enemy that very morning. Of the division there remained just fourteen men, the Commander and his divisional headquarters, comprising about three hundred officers. (024-016)

Three hundred officers commanding fourteen men: the ratio is grotesque. When asked what he will do, the Commander replies that "he would wait; he thought the men might return" (024-016). The war has become a state of suspended animation — an army of officers waiting for soldiers who will never come back.

### The Russian Officer in Exile

The war's displacement of the Russian officer class is rendered through [[general-bologoevski|General Bologoevski]], who finds himself unable even to dine in a restaurant while wearing his uniform:

> The head waiter came up to us and respectfully informed the General that by order of the Commander-in-Chief Russian officers were not admitted into restaurants. The General flushed. "What! Remove my epaulets! I, a Russian officer? Never!" he protested. (019-004–005)

The scene is both pathetic and comic. The General's pride in his epaulets — the symbols of a military order that no longer exists — is absurd, but Gerhardie does not mock him cruelly. The war has stripped these men of function but not identity.

## Aftermath and Consequences

### The War's Economic Wake

The war's economic devastation permeates the [[nikolai-vasilievich|Bursanov family's]] circumstances. [[nikolai-vasilievich|Nikolai Vasilievich]]'s goldmines — the family's supposed fortune — were "about to begin to pay, when war broke out" (019-059). The conditional is everything: the war intervenes at precisely the moment of promised prosperity, transforming the family's future from wealth to exile.

The war also enables peculiar career trajectories. [[baron-wunderhausen|Baron Wunderhausen]], now a second lieutenant in [[kolchaks-army|Kolchak's Army]], attaches himself to the Admiral's party; a dentist follows the Admiral to [[omsk|Omsk]] "in order to evade military service at the Base" (024-006). The war produces both heroes and draft-dodgers, and Gerhardie records both with the same equanimity.

### The Prewar World as Lost Eden

The General's nostalgia for the prewar world is a recurring note. After singing a Russian gypsy song, he speaks of "the good old prewar days" and "sighed, sighed deeply" (022-014). The prewar world figures in the novel as a lost Eden — remembered but irretrievable, and perhaps never quite as golden as it is recalled to have been.

### The War's Conclusion and Its Persistence

The war's formal end brings no resolution. The narrator resumes his interrupted course at [[oxford|Oxford]], "having 'relinquished my commission,' thanks to the conclusion of the war" (032-001). But the quotation marks around "relinquished my commission" suggest the gesture is provisional. The war's psychological claim endures:

> Since childhood I had more or less earmarked my future, and the circumscription did not really take in Nina. ... Pending that achievement, there was, of course, that other life, essentially out of focus with the novel, not really life at all, a transitory, irritating phase. ... But, as it happened, the war claimed me. (032-003)

"The war claimed me" — the past tense is deceptive. The war claimed him, and continues to claim him, through the memories and displacements it set in motion. The novel itself is a delayed product of the war; without the war, the narrator never goes to Russia, never meets [[nina|Nina]], never has a story to tell.

### Vestiges of War

Even in peacetime, the war's material traces persist. The narrator's voyage to the Far East is conducted on "that ancient liner (captured from the Germans in the war)" which "broke down every now and then at Godforsaken places like Perim" (033-044). The captured German liner, now a decrepit transport, is a material emblem of the war's afterlife: still moving, barely, through waters that no longer belong to any combatant.

The novel's final pages show the [[nikolai-vasilievich|Bursanovs]] planning yet another displacement — "Next month ... for Shanghai. Mama is going to start a business there. Hats. We have to do something" (036-016) — a reminder that the war's consequences continue to unfold indefinitely.
