# Suffering in Futility

## Overview

Suffering permeates _Futility_ as both a personal experience and a collective condition. Gerhardie treats suffering not as a path to redemption—departing from the Russian literary tradition—but as one more manifestation of futility. Characters suffer, change, and find that change itself has been futile.

## Suffering and Transformation

A character declares: "I have changed through suffering and age"（006-022）. This claim of transformation through suffering is a recurring motif, but Gerhardie's irony undercuts any suggestion that suffering leads to wisdom. The change it produces is as meaningless as the suffering itself.

## Physical and Emotional Pain

The novel registers suffering in both physical and emotional registers. A character "would writhe with pain"（004-017）, while others endure the quieter agonies of disappointment and loss. Gerhardie refuses to hierarchize these forms of suffering—all are equally real, and all are equally futile.

## Collective Suffering

Set against the backdrop of the Russian Revolution and its aftermath, the novel situates personal suffering within historical catastrophe. The "luxurious train, freshly painted, beautifully furnished"（022-021） contrasts with the suffering outside, creating a dissonance that characterizes the exile experience.
