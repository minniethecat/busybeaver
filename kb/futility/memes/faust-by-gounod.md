## Overview

Charles Gounod's opera *Faust* (1859), based on Goethe's tragic play, is one of the cultural touchstones referenced in William Gerhardie's *Futility*. The opera — one of the most popular of the nineteenth century — appears in the novel as part of the characters' shared cultural vocabulary, a work that educated Russians and Europeans alike would have known and whose themes of desire, damnation, and redemption resonate with the novel's own concerns.

## Faust in the Novel

The opera is referenced in the context of the novel's musical scenes. [[nikolai-vasilievich|Nikolai Vasilievich]]'s aesthetic sensibility — his experience of life "as a dream in music" [026-034] — encompasses the operatic repertoire of which *Faust* was a cornerstone. The opera's themes of a man who sells his soul for renewed youth and love find ironic echoes in Nikolai's own pursuit of [[zina|Zina]] and in the novel's broader meditation on desire and its costs.

## Gounod's Achievement

Gounod's *Faust* transformed Goethe's philosophical drama into lyric opera, emphasizing the love story of Faust and Marguerite over the cosmic machinery of the original. Its popularity in Russia — where it was performed regularly in [[petrograd|Petrograd]] and [[moscow|Moscow]] — made it a natural point of reference for Gerhardie's cosmopolitan characters. The opera's mixture of the sublime and the sentimental aligns with the novel's own tonal range.

## Cultural Context

The reference to *Faust* situates the novel's characters within a European cultural tradition that transcends national boundaries. That a British narrator, writing about Russians, can casually reference a French opera based on a German play is itself a statement about the cosmopolitan world that the [[russian-revolution-1917|revolution]] was in the process of destroying.
