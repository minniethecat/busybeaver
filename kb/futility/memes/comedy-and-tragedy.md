# Comedy and Tragedy in Futility

Gerhardie's *Futility* operates in what the narrator calls "a kind of No Man's Land" between comedy and tragedy (003-022). This is the novel's deepest formal principle: events that should be tragic — the collapse of a family, the failure of love, the chaos of revolution — are rendered comic by the sheer absurdity of human behaviour, while moments that should be comic are tinged with genuine pathos. The result is a work that refuses the consolations of either genre.

## Overview

The Chekhovian inheritance is explicit. Watching Chekhov's *Three Sisters*, the narrator observes that Chekhov's characters "seem as though they had all been born on the line of demarcation between comedy and tragedy — in a kind of No Man's Land" (003-022). This is not just commentary on Chekhov — it is Gerhardie's aesthetic manifesto. The three Bursanov sisters watch the play "as if the *Three Sisters* were indeed their own particular tragedy" (003-022), while the narrator, "buoyant and impatient," finds the Chekhov characters merely "preposterous." The gap between these two responses — tragic identification versus comic detachment — is the space the novel inhabits.

## Fanny Ivanovna's Tragedy

Fanny Ivanovna, the family matriarch, narrates her life as tragedy: the loss of family fortune, the descent into poverty, the dependence on Nikolai Vasilievich's irregular support. "I belong," she says with ironic self-awareness, "to a very proud family indeed. My father and all my brothers were officers in the German Guards" (006-022). Yet Gerhardie undercuts this self-dramatization: "In her great tragedy she was still a sound business woman" (006-078). The tragic self-image and the comic reality coexist, neither canceling the other.

## Farce as Truth

The novel's most radical genre claim comes when Fanny Ivanovna declares: "Our life can hardly be called a comedy, for if it were produced on the stage no one would believe it was real. No real people could be so silly. It is a farce" (012-006). This is the novel's hermeneutic key: life exceeds comedy because it is too improbable for fiction. Farce — not tragedy, not comedy, not even tragicomedy — is the only adequate genre for the Bursanovs' existence.

## Key Passage

> You know the manner of Chekhov's writing. You know the people in his plays. It seems as though they had all been born on the line of demarcation between comedy and tragedy — in a kind of No Man's Land. Fanny Ivanovna and the three sisters watched the play with intense interest, as if the Three Sisters were indeed their own particular tragedy. (003-022)
