## Overview

Time in William Gerhardie's *Futility* is less a neutral medium in which events occur than an active force — sometimes dragging, sometimes accelerating, always contributing to the novel's governing sense of futility. Characters wait: for letters, for trains, for revolutions to end, for love to be reciprocated, for something — anything — to happen. This condition of perpetual anticipation, in which the present is always a mere interval between a remembered past and a deferred future, is the novel's temporal signature. The waiting is not circumstantial but existential: it is the condition of life itself for the dispossessed and the displaced, who inhabit a temporality that belongs to neither the country they have left nor the country they are traveling toward.

## The Time of Waiting

The narrator's fundamental temporal experience is suspension:

> "And because of this and the sense that I am marking time till the big steamer comes and bears me home to England I am eagerly retrospective." (002-001)

He inhabits a present that has no content of its own, a mere interval. This is the temporality of the expatriate — stranded in a country whose calendar is governed by events (war, revolution) that he observes but does not control. The phrase "eagerly retrospective" captures the novel's temporal paradox: the narrator looks backward because the present offers nothing to look at and the future offers only departure.

Throughout the Petrograd sections, time passes in a state of suspension. The narrator describes evenings where "they had been sitting silently for a time. Nina seemed sad; Sonia and Vera sulky. It was twilight, but no one had thought of switching on the light" (004-002). The failure to switch on the light is a failure to enter time — the twilight prolongs itself indefinitely, a temporal no-man's-land between day and night. The family gives up on Nikolai Vasilievich "regularly every evening after waiting for him for two hours" (004-057), the "two hours" a recurring measure of wasted time that accumulates across the novel like a debt.

### The Political Economy of Waiting

Waiting is not merely an emotional state but a political condition. At the Admiral's headquarters in Omsk:

> "We entered a large dirty waiting-room where crowds of petitioners awaited their turn with a patience that bordered on spiritual resignation: after the Russian manner they all desired to see the head man personally, whose life was consequently spent in interviews." (023-007)

The "patience that bordered on spiritual resignation" is the temporal signature of a population accustomed to bureaucratic obstruction. The dirty waiting-room, the dirty woman, the dirty child — the repetition of "dirty" emphasizes the degradation that waiting produces. The "head man" whose "life was consequently spent in interviews" is himself a victim of this temporal regime, his time consumed by the petitioners whose time he consumes in turn.

Nikolai Vasilievich's own experience of bureaucratic time follows the same pattern. The General tells him to "Wait till the Supreme Ruler returns from Perm. I can do nothing without the Supreme Ruler" (023-011). The indefinite deferral — "one of these days" — is the state's preferred temporality, a promise of future action that guarantees present inaction. The narrator's reflection on Nikolai's explosion of rage makes explicit the temporal dimension:

> "Was it the explosion of that brewing restlessness that he had gathered in the years of intermittent waiting: the last puff of ineffectual remonstrance before his final sinking into hopeless resignation?" (023-017)

The "years of intermittent waiting" accumulate not wisdom but "brewing restlessness" — a temporal pressure that can only be released in "ineffectual remonstrance." Waiting, in *Futility*, produces not patience but desperation.

## Historical Time and Personal Time

The novel juxtaposes two temporal scales: the vast, impersonal time of history (the Great War in its third year, the revolutionary calendar) and the intimate, urgent time of personal desire. These scales do not align — historical events move too slowly or too quickly for the characters' emotional needs. The narrator finds himself "marking time" while empires collapse, his personal impatience set against historical catastrophe.

This disjunction is the source of much of the novel's comedy. Nikolai Vasilievich treats the war as a personal inconvenience, as if history ought to accommodate itself to his financial timetable. His inability to distinguish between the time of global conflict and the time of his own schemes is at once absurd and, in Gerhardie's treatment, oddly sympathetic — we are all, the novel suggests, trapped in the mismatch between our own temporal horizons and those of the world.

Gerhardie renders this temporal mismatch through a set piece of stasis:

> "The revolution dragged on through the winter and 'deepened' as the months advanced. The forerunners of confusion became visible: food and commodities were being procured in an irregular manner. All were waiting." (016-001)

The quotation marks around "deepened" are characteristic: the revolution's deepening is a cliche, a newspaper phrase that disguises the reality of mere duration. The revolution does not advance or resolve; it "drags on," its only significant action being to continue. "All were waiting" — the sentence that ends the passage — is the novel's temporal motto, applicable to every character in every situation.

### The Still Centre

Against the backdrop of historical movement, the characters maintain a startling immobility:

> "Pictures of them recur continually to my mind, as I write. I can see Fanny Ivanovna, and particularly I can see the three sisters, always sitting in the same positions, perched on sundry chairs and sofas, Fanny Ivanovna engaged in silent contemplation over needlework, and Kniaz sitting in his usual chair, reading, or more often sitting idly, thinking into space." (016-002)

The narrator then draws the contrast explicitly:

> "The seasons would be changing rapidly from one to the other — but their position never! Rain would drum against the windowpane, snow would be falling on the street below; then the ice on the Neva would begin to break and slowly move toward the sea; and still they sat on, unchanging, static, immutable."

The exclamation mark after "but their position never!" is one of the novel's rare eruptions of emphasis. The paradox is clear: historical time accelerates — seasons change, the ice breaks, the revolution deepens — while personal time stalls. The characters are fixed in their postures, "static, immutable," as though photography rather than narrative were the appropriate medium for their lives.

## Fanny Ivanovna's Complaint

Fanny Ivanovna gives voice to the novel's temporal despair in a passage that is at once comic and devastating:

> "'How tiring this is, Andrei Andreiech,' Fanny Ivanovna complained. 'To be always waiting to begin to live. When is that upward movement in happiness, that splendid life that we are always waiting for, to begin at last? Somehow you wait for the spring. But spring has come ... alone, and only emphasizes our misery, by the contrast. ... Spring makes me mad. I begin to want impossible things. ...'" (016-003)

The complaint is structured around a temporal paradox: they wait for spring, but spring arrives "alone" — without the happiness that was supposed to accompany it. The seasons change, but the characters do not. "Spring makes me mad" because it promises renewal and delivers only "contrast," a sharper awareness of what has not changed. "I begin to want impossible things" is the logical endpoint of temporal frustration: desire itself becomes impossible, oriented toward a future that will never arrive.

## The Seasons

Gerhardie uses seasonal imagery with precision, marking the passage of time while simultaneously revealing its emptiness. The novel opens on a day of "peculiar warmth and fragrance, when you could feel that winter has become spring" (010-001), a moment of transition that promises change but delivers only more of the same. The narrator strolls down the Nevski Prospekt and runs into Nikolai Vasilievich with "a pretty flapper on his arm" — the spring encounter leads nowhere, a false start that the novel's larger pattern of futility will absorb.

The Siberian winter provides the novel's most intense temporal imagery:

> "We were jolly, exuberant, self-centred and sentimental. I felt distinctly pleased with myself. I knew not why; that is the secret of good wine. Some people laughed, others after the manner of the Slav were fain to weep; and outside there raged the snowstorm of a Siberian winter night." (026-031)

The "snowstorm of a Siberian winter night" that "raged" outside the warm room is time itself, indifferent to human jollity and Slav weeping alike. The contrast between the indoors — wine, laughter, music — and the outdoors — snow, darkness, storm — is the contrast between the human attempt to escape time and time's refusal to be escaped.

The novel's closing image is autumnal:

> "Then they came out. Fanny Ivanovna, Nikolai Vasilievich and Kniaz stood on the steps and looked at us, as we walked away. ... The sharp autumn wind ruffled Nikolai Vasilievich's scanty hair, and the three of them, as they stood there hatless in the open, looked frail and weak and helplessly exposed to the storms of life." (037-017)

The "sharp autumn wind" that ruffles Nikolai's "scanty hair" is the wind of time itself, which has thinned his hair along with his prospects. The three figures standing "hatless in the open" are exposed to "the storms of life" — a phrase that fuses meteorological and existential time, as if the weather were simply life made visible.

Earlier, an autumn morning provides a different temporal register:

> "It was one of those glorious autumn days that are like the unfolding days of spring." (034-005)

The simile — autumn that resembles spring — collapses the distinction between beginning and ending, birth and decay. In Gerhardie's temporal scheme, autumn and spring are interchangeable because both are merely markers of a cycle that leads nowhere. The canary in its cage goes on with "his usual 'Chic! ... cherric! ...'" — the bird's unchanging song is the novel's most concise image of temporal futility: the same sounds, the same cage, day after day, season after season.

## Repetition and Return

Gerhardie structures time through repetition. The phrase "several times" recurs — Sonia says something "several times" (006-034), events are described as having happened "many times" (006-010). This iterative mode suggests a world in which nothing happens once and for all; everything recurs, and recurrence itself becomes a form of futility. To repeat is to fail to progress, and the novel's characters seem caught in temporal loops from which no forward movement is possible.

Easter, the most "festal day in the year," offers no release from the temporal cycle:

> "The occasion coincided with the release of Nikolai Vasilievich from prison, following on the seizure of the fortress by the Japanese. Already through the windows there gazed the evening of early spring. The church bells on that Easter Sunday, the most festal day in the year, rang dolefully through the Christian city seized by a heathen yellow race, and spoke of better days." (029-001)

Easter — the feast of resurrection — ought to be a triumph over time, a celebration of renewal. But the bells "rang dolefully" and "spoke of better days" — that is, of the past, not the future. The Christian calendar, like the revolutionary calendar, has become a mockery, its promises of transformation belied by the unchanging reality of waiting and loss.

### The Accumulated Years

The novel measures time in years of accumulated disappointment. Fanny Ivanovna has "lived with Nikolai Vasilievich now for over eleven years" (007-013) without seeing his mines produce a single copeck. Nikolai's enterprises have been promising wealth "for years and years" (019-059) without delivery. Kniaz, the silent prince, is the novel's most extreme temporal figure:

> "For twenty years or more he had said nothing. We felt that he had had ample time to think deep thoughts: and there at last he was pouring them out: giving us the benefit of all these years of silent contemplation." (029-019)

The "twenty years or more" of silence are presented as a reservoir of compressed time that, when finally released, produces nothing more than an outburst of wounded patriotism. The "ample time to think deep thoughts" has produced no wisdom — only the accumulation of time itself, which, like interest on a worthless bond, compounds without ever yielding value.

## Time and Memory

The narrator's relationship to time is mediated by [[memory-in-futility|memory]]. He reflects that "it had seemed to me for some time past that there was no love" (004-015), using temporal markers to situate emotional states. The past is not simply behind him but "for some time past" — a duration that extends into the present, coloring it. The novel's retrospective narration — the entire story told from a later vantage point — makes time itself the fundamental narrative device: everything we read has already happened, and the gap between the happening and the telling is the space in which meaning (or its absence) is created.

The Vladivostok chapters deepen this temporal meditation. The narrator observes the family in its terminal phase:

> "How melancholy, but strangely fascinating, were these evenings: this gathering of souls dissatisfied with life, yet always waiting patiently for betterment: enduring this unsatisfactory present because they believed that this present was not really life at all: that life was somewhere in the future: that this was but a temporary and transitory stage to be spent in patient waiting." (028-003)

The passage is the novel's most direct statement of its temporal philosophy. The characters "endure this unsatisfactory present" because they believe "that this present was not really life at all." Life is always deferred — "somewhere in the future" — and the present is merely "a temporary and transitory stage." But the novel's irony is that the "transitory stage" is all there is. The "patient waiting" will never end because the future that would end it will never arrive. This is the temporal dimension of futility: not that hopes are disappointed but that they are sustained, endlessly, in a present that is always only transitional, never the destination.

Nikolai Vasilievich's release from prison offers the novel's final temporal image:

> "Was he sad because he had returned from prison to something that was only prison in a mitigated form? Was it that the suspense had been too long, that he had succumbed in the waiting? Was it that he had suddenly, secretly, for no particular reason, on the eve of great changes, lost faith?" (029-007)

The repetition of "was it" — three questions, each starting the same way — enacts the circular temporality of waiting. Each question returns to the same starting point, no answer possible. "He had succumbed in the waiting" — the temporal condition has become more real than any event that might end it. The waiting is not for something; the waiting is the thing.
