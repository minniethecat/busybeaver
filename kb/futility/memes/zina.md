## Overview

Zina (Zinaida) is a central figure in the romantic entanglements of William Gerhardie's *Futility*. The young woman whom [[nikolai-vasilievich|Nikolai Vasilievich]] is expected — by his family and hers — to marry, Zina embodies the tension between passionate desire and familial obligation that drives much of the novel's plot. She is intelligent, emotionally direct, and capable of surprising literary and philosophical observation. Unlike many of the women in the Bursanov orbit, Zina speaks her own mind — and what she says often complicates the expectations others have placed upon her.

## Family Context

### The Dependent Wing

Zina belongs to the extended Russian family circle that revolves around Nikolai Vasilievich. She is the daughter of Andrei Andreiech's younger brother (010-018), making her part of the financially dependent network of relatives who look to Nikolai for support. Her father is described as "underpaid and ill" (014-008), a man who "had lost forever the hope of seeing better days, and failed to see how the revolution could affect his own position" (014-008). The family's liberalism — exhausted with the liberation of the serfs in 1861 — is a kind of historical fatigue, a belief that nothing much can change and nothing much can be done.

Her mother is "a grey-haired, God-fearing old lady" (010-020) who confides in the narrator about the family's tangled affairs:

> "Andrei Andreiech, our lives are muddled up enough, God forgive us, and none of us knows where he is or how he stands or what he is about; but there are people, Andrei Andreiech, who are in a much worse mess than we." (010-020)

The mother's piety and confusion — "none of us knows where he is or how he stands or what he is about" — is a diagnosis of the entire family system, and Zina grows up inside it.

### The Goldmine Myth

"Zina's people keep telling her" about the goldmines in Siberia (007-005), feeding her expectations of wealth and stability that the novel systematically undermines:

> "Andrei Andreiech, it is all his money. It is really comic, but they all believe him to be preposterously rich. A house-owner in Petersburg! Goldmines in Siberia! A millionaire! Zina's people keep telling her, 'Stick to him, stick to him, don't let him go. These goldmines in Siberia, these millions, this house in the Mohovaya!'" (007-005)

[[fanny-ivanovna|Fanny Ivanovna's]] account of the family's financial fantasies reveals Zina's position: she is being pushed toward Nikolai by relatives who believe he is rich, in a comedy of expectations that has nothing to do with love and everything to do with [[money-in-futility|money]].

### Uncle Kostia's Household

Zina shares her living quarters with [[uncle-kostia|Uncle Kostia]], who lives on the resources of her father, forming the "Zina-Uncle Kostia wing" of the family (021-009). This domestic arrangement — the dependent father, the God-fearing mother, the philosophical uncle who never publishes — forms the background against which Zina's character develops. She is the one practical person in a household of impractical men.

## The Reluctant Engagement

### An Obligation, Not a Choice

The expectation that Nikolai will marry Zina is treated by the novel as both inevitable and absurd. The revelation comes from Fanny Ivanovna:

> "'Fanny,' he said, '— don't be frightened — I've got to marry Zina.'" (006-056)

The verb "got to" captures the sense of obligation rather than desire — Nikolai presents the marriage as something happening to him rather than something he is choosing. Fanny Ivanovna's response is characteristically pointed:

> "Zina, Andrei Andreiech, was a girl in Sonia's school and Sonia's class, of Sonia's age. Seventeen, Andrei Andreiech." (006-057)

The emphasis on Zina's youth — "Seventeen, Andrei Andreiech" — registers Fanny Ivanovna's moral objection beneath the factual recitation. The marriage plan is a transaction dressed as a romance, and Fanny Ivanovna wants the narrator to see it.

### Zina's Own Voice

Zina herself is ambivalent about the marriage — an ambivalence she expresses with surprising directness:

> "I keep telling Nikolai," said Zina, " 'don't marry me, don't. It is superfluous. I love you so much that I am perfectly prepared to live with you ... just to show you how I really love you.' What is marriage? A piece of paper. It's absurd. It means nothing. What do we care? What do I care? I have been reading Verbitskaya's Springs of Happiness. She seems to agree with me." (010-008)

The speech is remarkable on multiple levels. First, it is one of the novel's most explicit declarations of desire — Zina is "perfectly prepared to live with" Nikolai, stating her love without indirection. Second, it dismisses the institution of marriage — "a piece of paper" — in terms that would have been radical in the novel's conservative milieu. Third, it reveals Zina's literary sensibility: she has been reading Verbitskaya, a popular Russian novelist, and has absorbed her views on love and convention. The gap between the radicalism of her views and the traditionalism of her family situation is the gap in which her character lives.

### Oscar Wilde and Literary Sensibility

Zina's intellectual range extends beyond Verbitskaya:

> "Your Oscar Wilde," Zina turned to me, "said that hearts were made to be broken." (010-012)

The possessive "Your Oscar Wilde" — addressed to the Anglo-Russian narrator — positions Zina as a reader who can reach across cultures. She quotes Wilde not to display learning but to articulate a feeling: that hearts are made to be broken, that romantic disappointment is not accidental but structural. The line is aphoristic, slightly theatrical, and entirely in character — Zina is the novel's most self-aware romantic, and her self-awareness is what makes her situation poignant.

## The Romantic Triangle

### Zina, Nikolai, and Fanny Ivanovna

The engagement creates a romantic triangle that is also an economic one. Fanny Ivanovna depends on Nikolai financially; Zina is expected to marry him for financial security; Nikolai himself is caught between obligation and desire. The triangle is resolved not through choice but through the novel's general dissolution — the departure to Siberia, the scattering of the family, the novel's characteristic retreat from resolution into atmosphere.

> "Oh, you haven't married Zina then?" (019-048)

The question, asked in Siberia, registers the engagement's collapse without dramatizing it. The novel does not show us the breakup; it shows us the aftermath, and the aftermath is simply the absence of the marriage that was supposed to happen.

### Zina and Sonia

Zina's relationship with her sister [[sonia|Sonia]] provides one of the novel's emotional counterpoints. Sonia cries:

> "But Zina doesn't want to live with them." (011-035)

The defense is direct and sisterly — Sonia speaks for Zina when Zina cannot speak for herself. This sisterly solidarity exists alongside the family's general dysfunction: the sisters are allies in a household where everyone is dependent on Nikolai's uncertain fortunes and [[fanny-ivanovna|Fanny Ivanovna's]] domestic management.

## Zina's Character

### Physical Description

Gerhardie's physical descriptions of Zina are sparse but vivid. At the Vladivostok ball, Vera observes her entrance:

> Stepping like a duck, Zina would not turn unless warned. (021-010)

The image is unflattering and affectionate — "stepping like a duck" suggests a physical awkwardness that contrasts with her intellectual confidence. A moment later: "Zina looked as though she regretted having come" (021-011). The regret is characteristic — Zina is someone who second-guesses her own presence, who is always aware of the gap between what she feels and what the social situation demands.

Earlier, at the theatre:

> Zina put her white-gloved hand on my arm. (010-015)

The white glove signals formality, occasion, a young woman dressing for a role she is not entirely sure she wants to play. The gesture — hand on the narrator's arm — is intimate and formal at once, the gesture of a date rather than a friend, and it registers the ambiguity of Zina's position in the narrator's romantic landscape.

### Directness and Self-Knowledge

Zina's most distinctive quality is her directness. Unlike [[nina|Nina]], who is the object of the narrator's obsessive but passive gaze, Zina speaks. She tells Nikolai not to marry her. She quotes Wilde. She states her desire to live with Nikolai. She is, of all the novel's young women, the one who most clearly knows what she wants and is willing to say so — even if the novel's structure of obligations prevents her from achieving it.

Her "passionate desire to live with Nicholas" (011-004) is described by the narrator himself — the phrase is his, but it registers a force that the novel's other romantic plot (Andrei's love for Nina) conspicuously lacks. Nina is desired; Zina desires. The asymmetry is central to the novel's emotional architecture.

## Role in the Narrative

### Structural Function

Zina functions as both love interest and structural device. Her presence keeps the romantic plot in motion — will Nikolai marry her or won't he? — while also serving as a measure of other characters' emotional capacities. The narrator's own feelings about Zina are characteristically ambivalent: he observes her with sympathy but never with the obsessive intensity he reserves for [[nina|Nina]].

### The Zina-Uncle Kostia Wing

At the Vladivostok ball, Gerhardie marks a social division:

> Almost immediately, the family was followed by the Zina-Uncle Kostia wing, and by Magda Nikolaevna and Čečedek. But they would not speak to one another. (021-009)

The "Zina-Uncle Kostia wing" is a faction — the dependent relatives arriving in a group, united by financial need and family connection, divided from the main Bursanov party by the novel's proliferating resentments. "But they would not speak to one another" — the social comedy of people who are financially bound but personally estranged.

### The Engagement's Dissolution

The engagement dissolves not through drama but through the novel's characteristic method: drift. Nikolai leaves Petrograd. Zina does not marry him. The question — "Oh, you haven't married Zina then?" (019-048) — is answered by silence. Zina's fate after the dissolution is not recorded; she fades from the narrative, one of the novel's many loose ends, her "passionate desire" unresolved.
