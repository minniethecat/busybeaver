# Disillusionment in Futility

Disillusionment is the emotional keynote of Gerhardie's novel. Every character lives in a state of chronic letdown — their hopes are perpetually deferred, their expectations perpetually betrayed. But the novel's treatment of disillusionment is more complex than simple pessimism: it asks whether the loss of illusions is a catastrophe to be mourned or a liberation to be embraced.

## Overview

The narrator articulates the central paradox with characteristic detachment: "An optimist is a fool, since he can't see what awaits him — disillusionment. But he is wise without knowing it, since, however bad the present, he remains an optimist as to the future, and so his present seems never quite so bad to him as it really is" (017-017). The optimist is both right and wrong: disillusionment is inevitable, but the anticipation of it destroys whatever comfort the present might offer. The pessimist sees clearly; the optimist lives better.

This paradox runs through the novel's entire moral economy. Fanny Ivanovna, who has the clearest view of her own situation, is also the most miserable. Nikolai Vasilievich, who sustains "the continued sustaining of illusions by identifying in his mind certain definite promises of a financial nature" with his dreams (028-004), is capable of moments of genuine happiness — even though, or precisely because, those promises are illusory.

## In the Novel

The question of whether to disillusion someone is posed directly: "Have you ever tried to disillusion him?" (007-044). The novel's answer is ambivalent. To disillusion someone is to do them a kind of violence — to take away the fictions that make their life bearable. Yet to leave them in illusion is to participate in a deception.

Nikolai Vasilievich is the novel's most sustained study in the management of illusion. He "had built up enchanted castles of a rare magnitude and beauty upon this somewhat flimsy and elusive foundation; and he could not have now examined this foundation with an open mind without ruining his dreams" (028-004). His elaborate psychological machinery for sustaining illusion is at once ridiculous and admirable — he is the architect of his own delusion, and his architecture is genuinely impressive.

The novel's theatrical episode literalizes the theme. Watching a play "holding forth on the disillusionment of life" (010-022), the characters become so uncomfortable that they leave before the curtain falls — not because the play is bad, but because it holds up a mirror they cannot bear to look into. The theater becomes a space where disillusionment is both performed and fled from.

The novel's final insight is that disillusionment, like fate, is never quite complete. Fanny Ivanovna, at the novel's end, still harbors "that suspicion in her heart and consequently that look of reassurance in her face" (037-004) — she knows better, and yet she hopes. Gerhardie suggests that this is the fundamental human condition: we are never fully disillusioned, because we cannot afford to be.

## Broader Context

Disillusionment in *Futility* belongs to a broader modernist preoccupation with the loss of metaphysical and social certainties. Where the Victorian novel could end with marriage and the restoration of order, the modernist novel — and Gerhardie's in particular — ends with the recognition that order was never there to begin with.

The novel's Anglo-Russian perspective is again crucial. Russian literature has its own rich tradition of disillusionment (Chekhov's characters are perpetually disappointed by their lives), but Gerhardie adds an English note of understated irony. The English narrator observes Russian disillusionment with a detachment that is itself a form of emotional management — he is, in his own way, sustaining an illusion of objectivity that the novel quietly undermines.
