# Fate in Futility

The concept of fate in Gerhardie's novel extends beyond mere fatalism. It becomes a moral alibi, a rhetorical device, and a source of dark comedy. Characters invoke fate not only to explain their misfortunes but to justify their moral failures, transforming what might be tragic into something almost absurd.

## Overview

Fate in *Futility* operates on multiple levels simultaneously. On one level, it is the genuinely crushing force of historical circumstance — the Russian Revolution, civil war, exile — that none of the characters can control. On another, it is a self-serving narrative that characters deploy to absolve themselves of responsibility. The novel's distinctive achievement is to hold both meanings in tension: fate is real, and fate is also a convenient fiction.

The narrator, Andrei Andreiech, observes this dynamic most acutely in Nikolai Vasilievich, whose confession of sin paradoxically becomes a form of purification. As Fanny Ivanovna bitterly explains, Nikolai "had told himself that so good and wise and indeed well versed was he in his own wickedness, that there could be no crime, no sin, of which we others could accuse him of which he had not already in his goodness and wisdom accused himself, and so forgiven himself and started clear, afresh, with our lives all wrecked and ruined" (006-074). Fate here functions as a kind of moral laundering: the admission of guilt preempts genuine accountability.

## In the Novel

Gerhardie treats fate as something his characters negotiate with rather than simply endure. Fanny Ivanovna observes that Nikolai Vasilievich seems almost to have "bribed" fate (006-074), capturing the novel's ironic sensibility: fate in *Futility* is less a cosmic decree than a corruptible bureaucracy, much like the collapsing Russian state itself.

This transactional relationship with destiny is clearest in the novel's treatment of the family's fortunes. The repeated refrain "their destiny hung on the mines" (037-004) — Nikolai Vasilievich's elusive Siberian gold mines — makes fate contingent on a business proposition that never materializes. The novel's title itself suggests that all this maneuvering amounts to nothing: the characters expend enormous energy bargaining with fate, yet "nothing ever happens" (037-004).

Nikolai Vasilievich's former father-in-law embodies a simpler, more traditional fatalism. When "fate had dealt him a cruel blow, unjustifiably cruel, in return for his moderation" (017-011), he responds not with negotiation but with tears — an uncomplicated grief that the novel treats with sympathy but also with the implicit suggestion that such straightforward suffering belongs to a vanishing world.

## Key Passages

> "I tried to shame him, but I had to realize that indeed nothing could shame him. I mean, he was already ashamed to his full capacity, conscious of unpardonable sin, conscious of being a bad man, the very worst man — had admitted it all to himself … and was satisfied, as though this confession to himself had cleansed him of his wickedness and he had come out of it, clean, sanctified." (006-074)

> "Why, it would be far better if he had not realized how he had sinned than to plume himself on being a sinner unavoidably and bowing to his fate so readily that you almost suspected that, after the manner of his race, he had bribed it heavily to please him." (006-074)

> "Henceforth, as in the past, their destiny hung on the mines, and Fanny Ivanovna's ideas as to their recovery were somewhat mixed." (037-004)

## Broader Context

Gerhardie's treatment of fate belongs to a distinctly modern, post-Chekhovian sensibility. Unlike the tragic fatalism of classical Russian literature — where fate descends from heaven or the state — fate in *Futility* is mediated through bureaucratic incompetence, financial speculation, and self-deception. This is fate after the death of God: still powerful, but rendered slightly ridiculous by its entanglement with the mundane machinery of modern life.

The novel's Anglo-Russian dual perspective is essential here. The English narrator observes the Russian characters' fatalism with a mixture of fascination and gentle irony, while the Russian characters experience it as the natural texture of existence. This double vision produces the novel's characteristic tone: neither fully tragic nor fully comic, but hovering in the space between.
