# British Embassy in Petrograd

The British Embassy in Petrograd is the narrator's professional home and one of the novel's key settings — a space where English order confronts Russian chaos, and where "very perfect young men, very perfectly dressed" maintain the forms of diplomacy while the world outside collapses. The Embassy is where the narrator meets the Admiral, where the Bursanov family stages its week-long campaign for a train compartment, and where the novel's comedy of Anglo-Russian collision reaches its fullest expression.

## In the Novel

### First Impressions

The Embassy is introduced through the narrator's first visit, a morning he remembers "very vividly" (014-002). The physical approach — "the broad staircase with its worn red carpet" — establishes the Embassy as a place of faded grandeur, an institution whose forms persist even as the substance of British power recedes. Inside, "very perfect young men, very perfectly dressed, were conversing in very perfect intonations about love among monkeys" (014-002). The repetition of "very perfect" is Gerhardie's deadpan satire: these diplomats are immaculate in manner and utterly trivial in substance. The narrator's reaction — "It struck me as delightfully human for diplomats" (014-002) — is characteristically double-edged: an appreciation of absurdity that is also a form of affection.

### The Admiral's Waiting Room

The Embassy serves as the staging ground for the Intervention — it is from here that the Admiral and narrator depart for Siberia. But its most extended comic set-piece is the Bursanov family's campaign for a train compartment. "Every day that week our waiting-room was crowded with petitioners" (022-001), and the Admiral finds himself "saying No innumerable times each day" (022-002). Each member of the family deploys a different strategy.

> The Admiral still said No. He held that it was not human nature but just Russian nature, and as an illustration of his point he meant to show that when an Englishman says No he does mean No. But none of them would understand the Admiral's interpretation of No. They had all grown up with the idea that No meant Yes after an adequate amount of pressure and insistence. (022-003)

The episode dramatizes the novel's central cultural conflict. The Admiral's "No" is an assertion of English rationality — a word that means what it means. The Bursanovs treat "No" as the opening move in a negotiation, and their persistence ("treating him as if he were just human like the rest of them," 022-004) is both comic and, in its way, dignified. The Admiral's faith in "the sterling value of British character" (022-004) proves spectacularly ineffective against Russian persistence — not because the Admiral is wrong, but because the categories he relies on ("English," "Russian," "character") have no purchase on the Bursanovs.

### The Embassy as English Island

Throughout the novel, the Embassy functions as a pocket of Englishness in a Russian world. It is where the narrator's dual identity — Anglo-Russian, insider and outsider — is most pronounced. He works there, belongs there, yet never quite fits: his Russian experience constantly exceeds the Embassy's categories. The "love among monkeys" conversation captures this perfectly — the Embassy's discourse is perfectly formed and perfectly irrelevant, a closed system of manners that cannot register the chaos outside its walls.

## Key Passages

> I ascended the broad staircase with its worn red carpet to the Chancery. Very perfect young men, very perfectly dressed, were conversing in very perfect intonations about love among monkeys. It struck me as delightfully human for diplomats. (014-002)

This is one of the novel's most quoted passages, and for good reason. In three sentences, Gerhardie establishes the Embassy's atmosphere (faded grandeur), its personnel (immaculate and absurd), and the narrator's perspective (affectionate irony). The "worn red carpet" and "love among monkeys" bracket the passage between pathos and comedy — the Embassy is at once a serious institution and a joke the narrator is in on.

> The Admiral still said that No was No, and would they please understand it? They all replied that No was not the point, the point being: What were they to do without Nikolai Vasilievich? Whereon the Admiral replied that when he said a thing he meant it, this being the sterling value of British character. But they persisted all the same, treating him as if he were just human like the rest of them. (022-004)

The Bursanovs' refusal to accept "No" is not merely stubbornness — it is a different epistemology, a different theory of what words mean and how decisions are made. The Admiral's appeal to "British character" is the appeal of a man who believes in institutions; the Bursanovs' persistence is the practice of people who believe only in relationships.

> I forget which wing of the family was the next to apply. I remember that every day that week our waiting-room was crowded with petitioners. The Admiral said No. He found himself saying No innumerable times each day. Now it is an intrinsic part of the Russian character that it does not accept No for No. It is constitutionally incapable of doing so. (022-001, 022-002)

The narrator's mock-sociological generalization — "it is an intrinsic part of the Russian character" — is Gerhardie's way of acknowledging the stereotype while also enacting it. The claim is absurd, and the narrator knows it; but it is also, in the context of the Bursanovs' siege, demonstrably true.

## The Embassy and the Novel's Form

The Embassy scenes frame the novel's larger movement from order to chaos. The first half of the novel, set largely in Petrograd, is anchored by the Embassy's routines — receptions, dispatches, the Admiral's pronouncements. The second half, set in Siberia, abandons the Embassy entirely, and with it the last pretence of institutional order. The novel's arc is from the "very perfect" Chancery to the "pitiably disorganized Siberian track" (022-005) — from a world where forms still hold to one where only human persistence remains.

## The Departure

The Embassy is the novel's point of departure, in every sense. It is from the Chancery staircase that the narrator leaves for Siberia; it is the Embassy's logic of "No" that the Bursanovs eventually overcome. When the train finally leaves Petrograd, the Embassy remains behind — a monument to a kind of order the novel has left forever. The narrator's final return to England, to Oxford, is a return to the Embassy's world without the Embassy's sustaining illusions.
