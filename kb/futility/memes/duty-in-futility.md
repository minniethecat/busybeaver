# Duty in Futility

## Overview

Duty in William Gerhardie's *Futility* is a word that characters deploy to explain actions they cannot otherwise justify. It appears most explicitly in the narrator's reflection: "I felt that it was my duty to sacrifice my personal pride for Nikolai" (006-027). But the novel's treatment of duty is characteristically ironic — what is called duty often looks like self-interest, and what is called sacrifice often looks like manipulation. Duty provides the moral vocabulary for a world that has lost any stable moral framework.

The Russian context intensifies the irony. In a society where duty (*dolg*) has traditionally meant service to Tsar, faith, and fatherland — the triadic formula of Official Nationality — the novel's characters are left without a sovereign to serve, without a faith to believe in, and without a fatherland that any longer exists in recognizable form. Duty becomes a word in search of a referent: a linguistic habit surviving the collapse of the world that gave it meaning.

## Military Duty

The narrator's ostensible purpose in Russia is military duty — he is a British officer attached to the Military District Staff in Petrograd. Yet this duty is treated with remarkable casualness. The Military Mission's objectives are vague, its effectiveness questionable, and its members more concerned with personal entanglements than with strategic outcomes. The war that gives military duty its meaning is distant, and the narrator's real obligations are to the Bursanov family and to his pursuit of [[nina|Nina]].

This displacement of military duty by personal obligation is not presented as a moral failure but as a simple fact of the narrator's experience. The novel refuses to hierarchize duties — is the narrator's obligation to His Majesty's Government more real than his obligation to the woman he loves? Gerhardie leaves the question open. But the very asking of it, in a novel set against the backdrop of the [[world-war-i|Great War]] and the [[russian-civil-war|Russian Civil War]], is a provocation.

## Familial Duty

The [[family-in-futility|Bursanov family]] is bound together by a web of asserted duties. Nina invokes her sacrifices as evidence of duty fulfilled; [[fanny-ivanovna|Fanny Ivanovna]] insists "it's your duty to take your children out" (020-068); [[nikolai-vasilievich|Nikolai Vasilievich]] demands loyalty on grounds never quite specified. Duty in the family context is a form of emotional currency, minted by whoever needs leverage at a given moment.

The narrator observes: "I didn't want to add further" to Nikolai's burdens (006-026), framing his restraint as a form of duty — the duty of the outsider not to complicate an already complicated family dynamic. But this self-restraint is also self-protection, and the novel does not disguise the self-interest beneath the language of obligation.

The family's eastward flight from Petrograd to Vladivostok, undertaken in the name of duty — to the children, to the goldmines, to the dependents — becomes a physical enactment of the novel's thesis: that duty is a journey with no destination, a movement that produces only more movement, more expenditure, more suffering for reasons that no one can quite articulate.

## Duty and Sacrifice

Duty is the language in which [[sacrifice-in-futility|sacrifice]] is justified. The narrator's "duty to sacrifice" personal pride (006-027) reveals the conceptual structure: duty provides the imperative, sacrifice provides the content. But the novel asks whether duty that demands sacrifice is really duty at all, or merely a rationalization for self-harm. When no one notices the sacrifice, when no one acknowledges the duty, the moral framework collapses into the void that is the novel's central theme.

The asymmetry is crucial: characters invoke duty to demand sacrifice *from others* far more often than they invoke it to justify their own. Fanny Ivanovna's insistence that Nikolai must "keep the children" — a duty she urges upon him — conveniently serves her own interest in maintaining the family structure in which she has invested her life. The novel is too subtle to call this hypocrisy; it is simply how duty works in a world where no one can claim moral authority.

## The Russian Context: *Dolg* After Empire

The Russian word *dolg* carries a weight that the English "duty" only partly conveys. In pre-revolutionary Russian culture, *dolg* was embedded in a religious and imperial framework: duty to God, duty to the Tsar, duty to the *rodina* (motherland). The Revolution did not abolish the concept of duty — it transferred it to new objects (the Party, the proletariat, the world revolution) — but for the characters of *Futility*, who belong to neither the old nor the new order, duty has lost its anchorage. They speak the language of *dolg* in a world that no longer recognizes what *dolg* means.

This is most visible in the figure of [[kniaz|Kniaz]], who has "not any definite duties" (009-030) despite his title and position. The aristocrat without duties is the novel's emblem of a class that has outlived its function — and of a moral vocabulary that has outlived its referents.

## Duty and Futility

The novel's deepest insight about duty is that it is futile — not because characters fail in their duties, but because the very concept of duty has no stable foundation. What one owes to family, to country, to oneself is never clear, and the language of duty serves mainly to obscure this uncertainty. In a world without transcendent moral authority, duty is what we say it is, and what we say it is changes with our moods and interests.

The narrator's final position — having left Russia, having left Nina, having discharged whatever duties he ever had — is not triumph but a peculiar emptiness. He has done his duty, or what passed for it, and the result is neither satisfaction nor guilt but the "sense of dreary enchantment" (017-044) that is the novel's characteristic tone. Duty fulfilled is indistinguishable from duty abandoned. The novel's final vision is of a world in which the most pressing duty is to recognize the futility of duty itself.
