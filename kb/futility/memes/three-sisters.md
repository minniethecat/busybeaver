# Three Sisters (Chekhov)

Anton Chekhov's *Three Sisters* (1901) is the most important literary reference within Gerhardie's *Futility*. The Bursanov sisters attend a performance of the play, and the experience becomes a moment of self-recognition that structures the novel's engagement with its own Chekhovian inheritance.

## In the Novel

The local theatre performance of *Three Sisters* is the novel's pivotal early scene. "Fanny Ivanovna and the three sisters watched the play with intense interest, as if the *Three Sisters* were indeed their own particular tragedy" (003-022). The identification is so complete that it threatens to collapse the distance between art and life — the Bursanovs are not merely watching Chekhov; they are watching themselves.

The narrator, sitting behind Nina, watches with "that stupid scepticism that comes from too much happiness" (003-022). His failure to be moved — "To me, buoyant and impatient, the people in the play appeared preposterous" — marks him as an outsider to the Chekhovian sensibility that the Bursanovs inhabit. The gap between his ironic detachment and their tragic identification is the novel's central dramatic tension.

Gerhardie makes the debt explicit through self-aware commentary. A character threatens to "write up such a *Three Sisters* as will knock old Chekhov into a cocked hat" (011-053) — a metafictional joke that acknowledges the novel's own status as a Chekhovian work while asserting its comic distinctiveness.

## Key Passage

> You know the manner of Chekhov's writing. You know the people in his plays. It seems as though they had all been born on the line of demarcation between comedy and tragedy — in a kind of No Man's Land. (003-022)
