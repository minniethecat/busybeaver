# Eisenstein

Eisenstein is a minor character in William Gerhardie's novel *Futility*: a Jewish dentist
who ran away with Nikolai Vasilievich's wife, became financially dependent on her, and
followed the sprawling family entourage across revolutionary Russia.

## Character Overview

Eisenstein occupies a peculiar position in the novel's domestic economy: he cuckolded
Nikolai Vasilievich, yet remains tethered to the same financial ecosystem as the man he
wronged — because Nikolai's wife, whom Eisenstein lives with, continues to receive money
from Nikolai to keep the children. He is thus both an antagonist and a dependent; a rival
who cannot afford to be a rival, and a lover who has long since ceased to be loved.

### As Nikolai's Wife's Lover

Fanny Ivanovna relates the backstory: Eisenstein was "a Jew dentist, with no practice"
(006-025), whom Nikolai's wife ran away with before Nikolai and Fanny met. He had "no
money" and "wasted her money in speculation on the Stock Exchange" (006-028). The wife,
having grown "tired of Eisenstein, who had spent all her money and proved a dismal
failure in dentistry," eventually tried to return to Nikolai — finding Fanny Ivanovna
in the way (006-028).

The wife's tactics against Fanny included spreading the story that Fanny was "a German
governess in her household" who "beguiled Nikolai into running away with me." These legal
entanglements, centered on Eisenstein's presence as a complicating factor, prevented
Nikolai from obtaining a divorce — which would have required him to plead guilty and
lose the children.

### As Vera's Father Figure

Eisenstein's most tender moment in the novel concerns Vera. When Eisenstein "looked round
and saw Vera in the twilight, mute and hostile," his cry of "Vera! Vèrochka!" (008-045) is
described by the narrator as either "a revelation" or "only a confirmation" (008-048) —
a moment of unexpected paternal tenderness toward a girl who is not his daughter.

### The Train Journey

During the family's flight eastward from Petrograd, Eisenstein follows Nikolai's wife,
who travels in a separate carriage from Nikolai's party. The narrator notes that
Eisenstein "invariably turned up by the next train in every town they halted"
(019-060). Despite his parasitic habits, he proves "helpful" during the journey,
assisting Zina's father with the ailing Eberheim at wayside stations, and contributing
to the collective effort of survival.

## Narrative Function

Eisenstein serves several narrative purposes:

1. **Comic obstruction**: The divorce that cannot happen because of him is one of the
   bureaucratic-legal absurdities that define the novel's comic vision of futility.
2. **Financial parasite**: He embodies the pattern of dependency that radiates outward
   from Nikolai Vasilievich — a man who supports not only his own dependents but the
   lover of his former wife.
3. **Tender counterpoint**: His moment of recognition for Vera complicates the reader's
   judgment, suggesting that even the most marginal figures in this sprawling family
   have moments of genuine feeling.
