## Overview

Admiral Butt is the ranking British military figure in William Gerhardie's *Futility*, sent to Russia "on a special" mission [014-001] just before the revolution. He serves as the narrator's superior officer and as a comic embodiment of British military authority — tall, imperious, and unexpectedly vulnerable to the indignities of daily life in revolutionary Russia, including nocturnal pursuits of cats and an inability to sleep.

## Arrival in Russia

The Admiral's arrival sets the stage for his characterization. Sent "just before the revolution" [014-001], he represents British official interest in Russian affairs at the moment of their collapse. "Finally the Admiral arrived. He was a tall, imperious" figure [014-004] — the adjective "imperious" capturing both his military bearing and his fundamental unsuitability for the chaotic world he has entered. The narrator meets him at the British Embassy [014-002], establishing the institutional framework within which the Admiral operates.

## Military Authority and Its Limits

The Admiral commands respect by virtue of his rank, but Gerhardie systematically undermines this authority through domestic comedy. "The Admiral made his temporary headquarters" [019-003] in a late by-street — the language of military command applied to a makeshift arrangement. His "serious, deliberate" manner [020-001] and "emphatic nod" [020-002] suggest a man accustomed to obedience, yet the novel places him in situations where obedience is irrelevant.

## The Cat Episode

The Admiral's most extended comic set-piece involves a nocturnal pursuit. "I beheld the Admiral and a little dark-haired man" [019-072] engaged in what turns out to be the pursuit of a cat. "Can you see the dog?" came the Admiral's sturdy voice from under a" chair [019-075]. "I believe there was a rat," said the Admiral [019-082]. The episode reduces military dignity to farce — the "gallant Admiral" [019-073] becomes a figure of pure comedy, unable to sleep and reduced to chasing vermin.

## The Admiral and the Narrator

The Admiral functions as the narrator's official superior, providing an institutional framework for the narrator's presence in Russia. Their relationship is characterized by the narrator's ironic observation: "I watched the Admiral who in his serious, deliberate" way [020-001] proceeded through situations that defied serious, deliberate response. The narrator's detachment from his own military role is mediated through his observation of the Admiral's absurd dignity.
