# Nikolai Vasilievich

The central male figure of the Bursanov household — not a patriarch in the traditional sense of authority and order, but a patriarch of dysfunction: financially indispensable, emotionally absent, and so obstinate that his obstinacy becomes a family trait passed down through generations. Nikolai Vasilievich is the man everyone depends on and no one can rely on.

## Character Overview

Nikolai Vasilievich is introduced through indirection — the narrator is "initiated into the complicated affairs of the Bursanov family" (003-001) as if entering an Ibsen drama, and Nikolai Vasilievich is the secret at the centre. He maintains an unconventional household: legally married to one woman, living with [[fanny-ivanovna]] as his de facto wife, supporting both families financially while refusing to resolve his situation through divorce.

He appears in all four parts of the novel, his presence felt even when he is not on the page. His defining trait is obstinacy — "a weak and sloppy man, and such a mule!" as Fanny Ivanovna describes him (006-061). This obstinacy is both his power and his limitation: it allows him to resist the Admiral's "No" through sheer persistence (022-001), but it also prevents him from making any decisive change to his circumstances.

### Financial Dependence and the Family Economy

The entire Bursanov household revolves around Nikolai Vasilievich's money. [[fanny-ivanovna]] is not his legal wife, yet he supports her and her mother in Germany; his legal wife receives regular payments to prevent scandal; Kniaz, the family hanger-on, borrows money against imaginary mining dividends; and his three daughters depend on him entirely. The narrator observes this parasitic economy with detached fascination.

> Nikolai was still always sending money to his wife to keep her quiet. She always threatened to make a nuisance of herself. She wanted the money, too—badly indeed, because that man Eisenstein she lived with was a lazy good-for-nothing. (006-028)

The money flows outward from Nikolai Vasilievich in all directions, yet his financial position deteriorates steadily — "the war had further crippled his finances" (015-008). The family continues to consume regardless, a microcosm of the broader Russian economic collapse unfolding around them.

> "Nikolai Vasilievich believes in the mines. Kniaz helps him to sustain that belief in return for Nikolai's faith in the shares. The money Kniaz borrows from Nikolai Vasilievich he regards merely as an advance on his future dividends." (007-049)

### The Two Nikolai Vasilieviches

One of the novel's most revealing passages comes when the narrator encounters Nikolai Vasilievich in the company of his mistress, transformed utterly from the morose figure of the Mohovaya flat:

> I could hardly recognize him. His careworn look seemed to have deserted him in his dissipation, as if ashamed to accompany him thither. He seemed ten years younger in her presence. He was smarter, bore himself better, seemed actually taller, bigger. … Oh, was it at all the same Nikolai Vasilievich who wrangled so furiously with Fanny Ivanovna? This Nikolai Vasilievich was as happy as a schoolboy. (010-007)

This doubleness is essential to his character. The man who sits silent and furious at the family dinner table is not the whole man. He possesses a capacity for joy that his domestic circumstances systematically suppress. The family misses this side of him entirely — "his absence annoyed everybody, for they suspected where he was" (004-057).

## Relationships

Nikolai Vasilievich's relationships define the novel's romantic geometry:

- **[[fanny-ivanovna]]**: His de facto wife, not legally married. She "pestered the life out of Nikolai Vasilievich" (003-010), demanding money, attention, and resolution. Their relationship is the novel's longest-running drama — a mixture of genuine affection, financial dependence, and mutual exasperation. Fanny Ivanovna met him in Basel, fell unexpectedly in love, and has spent over eleven years waiting for a divorce that never materialised (006-019, 007-013).

- **[[nina]]**, **[[sonia]]**, **[[vera]]**: His three daughters, "a charming bouquet, the queen among whom I recognized only too well" (003-001). Nina inherits her father's obstinacy; Vera is the estranged daughter living with his legal wife, whom "Nikolai Vasilievich hates" (006-043). The narrator's love for Nina complicates his position in the household.

- **[[andrei-andreiech]] (the narrator)**: A young Anglo-Russian who becomes entangled with the family, falls in love with Nina, and observes Nikolai Vasilievich with a mixture of bemusement and sympathy. Nikolai calls him "svoy chelovek" — one of the family (004-017).

- **The Admiral**: Nikolai's bureaucratic adversary. His week-long campaign for a train compartment to Omsk — "in the teeth of implacable refusals" (022-001) — is a comic masterpiece of Russian persistence against British intransigence.

- **[[kniaz]]**: The family parasite who sustains Nikolai's belief in mining shares in return for loans, a relationship of mutual delusion.

### The Waiting Household

The household operates on a rhythm of waiting. Every evening, the family waits two hours for Nikolai to appear for supper, then gives up:

> We danced, and then had supper, having given Nikolai Vasilievich up as we gave him up regularly every evening after waiting for him for two hours. His absence annoyed everybody, for they suspected where he was. (004-057)

This ritual of disappointed expectation — waiting, giving up, suspecting — encapsulates the family's entire dynamic. Nikolai Vasilievich is the absent centre, the one whose absence defines the household as much as his presence.

## Key Passages

> But Nikolai is obstinate as a mule. Obstinacy runs in the family. His grandmother was like that. Nina has it from her father. Obstinacy! What a terrible vice! There is no reason, no meaning in obstinacy beyond further obstinacy. It's a disease. (006-061)

Fanny Ivanovna's diagnosis of Nikolai's character is at once an accusation and a declaration of love. The obstinacy she condemns is the same force that has kept him in her life for decades, sending money to her family in Germany, refusing to abandon her despite endless complications.

> "Good God!" I cried and grasped Nikolai Vasilievich by the arm as the curtain fell upon the second act. "How can there be such people, Nikolai Vasilievich? Think of it! They can't do what they want. They can't get where they want. They don't even know what they want. They talk, talk, talk, and then go off and commit suicide or something. It is a hysterical cry for greater efforts, for higher aims—" (003-023)

The narrator's outburst at the performance of [[chekhovs-three-sisters]] is directed at Nikolai Vasilievich because he recognises the same futility in the man beside him. Nikolai's response is telling:

> Nikolai Vasilievich sat still and silent and very sad. He shook his head gravely and his face darkened. (003-024)

He recognises himself in the Prozorovs. He is one of those who "can't do what they want."

## Thematic Significance

Nikolai Vasilievich embodies the novel's central paradox: he is essential and irrelevant simultaneously. His money holds the family together; his presence is required for every decision; yet he cannot decide, cannot act, cannot resolve. He is [[futility-theme|futility]] in human form — not the philosophical futility of [[uncle-kostia]], but the practical, domestic futility of a man who keeps the machinery running while preventing it from ever reaching its destination.

### The Admiral's Door: Futility as Farce

His campaign for the Admiral's train compartment deserves special attention as the novel's comic set-piece:

> For a week or so Nikolai Vasilievich kept on applying to the Admiral for a coupe in his train to Omsk, in the teeth of implacable refusals. (022-001)

This week of "No" met with infinite variation of appeal is the novel in miniature: immense effort expended to achieve a small, temporary, and ultimately meaningless victory. The train to Omsk will leave; the family will board it; and nothing will be different. The Admiral's door becomes a symbol of bureaucratic [[futility-theme|futility]] — not the grand futility of war or revolution, but the quotidian futility of petition, persistence, and the hollow triumph of wearing down resistance.

> Nikolai Vasilievich seemed unusually morose as we drove home that night through the deserted streets of Petersburg. "The most perplexing thing about it all, Andrei Andreiech," said he, "is … well, it's like that fable of Krilov." And he quoted the fable with that curious pride that Russians usually take in Krilov's un-Russian (I think British) common sense. (010-023)

### The Silent Patriarch

Unlike the voluble Fanny Ivanovna or the philosophising Uncle Kostia, Nikolai Vasilievich communicates through silence, frowns, and grimaces. When confronted with emotional demands, he "would look bored and sullen and would wave his hand" (003-010). At the theatre, he "sat still and silent and very sad" (003-024). At the final departure, he "walked up and down the room, obviously to hide his emotion" (037-005). His emotional repertoire is minimal but eloquent — a man who has learned that speech only invites further demands.

## Appearances

Nikolai Vasilievich appears throughout the novel (Parts I–IV). He is present at the Mohovaya flat (004-001), at the theatre for [[chekhovs-three-sisters|Three Sisters]] (003-016), in the Admiral's waiting room (022-001), and at the final departure scene where "Nikolai Vasilievich walked up and down the room, obviously to hide his emotion" (037-005). His presence is felt most strongly in Part I, where the family's Petersburg life is established, and in Part II, where the comic-tragic disintegration accelerates under the pressures of [[war-in-futility|war]] and [[russian-revolution|revolution]].
