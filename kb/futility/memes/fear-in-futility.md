## Overview

Fear in William Gerhardie's *Futility* operates across scales: from the intimate anxieties of characters afraid of losing love, status, or money, to the existential dread produced by [[war-in-futility|war]] and [[russian-revolution|revolution]]. It is a quiet fear, rarely melodramatic — more often manifesting as hesitation, as a "vague fear" [033-050], or as the frantic energy of characters who sense their world dissolving. The novel treats fear not as cowardice but as the rational response to radical uncertainty.

## Domestic Fear

The novel's fear is grounded in the domestic sphere. Zina's mother is described as "a grey-haired, God-fearing old lady" [010-020] — fear of God serving as the traditional framework for understanding misfortune. But this religious fear is juxtaposed with more immediate anxieties: fear of poverty, fear of displacement, fear of being "pursued" by creditors or revolutionaries. The domestic world is no shelter; it is where fear lives.

## Political Fear

The revolutionary period transforms fear from private emotion to public condition. The "fearful lest it be cast to the wind" [026-001] of the tin roof in a Siberian storm becomes a metaphor for the precariousness of all shelter — physical, social, existential. Characters who "feared God, and knew no French" [026-002] represent an old order confronting incomprehensible change, their traditional fears inadequate to the new world.

## Fear and Hope

Fear is structurally paired with hope throughout the novel. Zina's mother "feared, against her hope, that Nikolai" would fail [029-062] — the two emotions intertwined, each giving the other its particular anguish. This pairing suggests that fear in *Futility* is not the opposite of hope but its shadow: to hope for anything in an uncertain world is necessarily to fear its loss.

## The Vague Fears

The novel's most characteristic fear is the one that resists naming. The narrator experiences "vague fears of being 'late'" [033-050] — the object of fear is trivial (missing an appointment), but the emotional register is existential. This disproportion between cause and feeling is Gerhardie's signature: the characters are haunted by anxieties they cannot articulate, fears that have outgrown their ostensible objects.

## The Great Men Diminished

In a characteristic Gerhardian reversal, even those who should inspire fear are revealed as inadequate. "The great men, I fear, looked small beneath her scorn" [036-005] — the phrase "I fear" is a polite locution that simultaneously acknowledges and dismisses fear's object. Authority figures, political leaders, "great men" — all are reduced by the novel's leveling irony.
