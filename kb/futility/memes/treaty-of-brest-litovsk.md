## Overview

The Treaty of Brest-Litovsk, signed in March 1918 between the Bolshevik government and the Central Powers, ended Russia's participation in [[world-war-i|World War I]] at enormous territorial cost. Its consequences — German occupation of vast Russian territories, Allied resentment at Russia's "betrayal," and the freeing of German troops for the Western Front — form part of the political backdrop against which William Gerhardie's *Futility* unfolds.

## Russia's Exit from the War

The treaty formalized Russia's exit from a conflict it could no longer sustain. "Russia's early exit from the war" [029-009] is referenced in the novel with the characteristic Gerhardian tone — not as tragedy or triumph but as one more event in a world where events succeed each other without necessarily making sense. The Allied characters, particularly the British, view the treaty as abandonment, but the novel's perspective remains detached.

## Consequences for the Novel's World

The treaty's consequences are felt indirectly. The Allied Intervention in [[siberia|Siberia]], which brings [[admiral-butt|Admiral Butt]] and [[sir-hugo|Sir Hugo]] to Russia, is in part a response to Brest-Litovsk — an attempt to prevent German exploitation of Russian resources and to reopen the Eastern Front. The novel's characters move through a landscape shaped by a treaty they rarely discuss but whose effects they cannot escape.

## Historical Significance

Brest-Litovsk was one of the most consequential treaties of the twentieth century. It demonstrated the Bolsheviks' willingness to pay any price for peace, established the pattern of German expansion into Russian territory, and set the stage for the complex Allied-Russian relations that form the novel's geopolitical subtext. Gerhardie's light touch with these matters is itself significant: the treaty matters, but what matters more is how people continue to live in its aftermath.
