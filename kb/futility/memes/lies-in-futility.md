# Lies in Futility

## Overview

Lies and deception permeate the social world of _Futility_, where characters engage in elaborate self-deception and mutual deceit. Gerhardie treats lying not merely as moral failure but as a structural necessity in a world where truth would dissolve the fragile bonds holding relationships together. The novel suggests that lies are not aberrations in social life — they are its foundation.

## The Discovery of Lies

The theme crystallizes in a key revelation: "I learnt that Nikolai Vasilievich had just discovered that his wife had been lying to him" (017-008). This discovery, however, does not lead to clarity or resolution but to further entanglement, illustrating how lies compound rather than resolve. The exposure of one lie typically reveals several more beneath it, creating a regress in which the characters become ever more deeply enmeshed.

## Half-Truths and Strategic Omission

"Half-truths are more dangerous than lies" (009-056) — the aphorism, delivered by a character whose own life is built on strategic omissions, captures the novel's practical ethics. A pure lie can be detected and confronted; a half-truth contains enough verifiable reality to resist exposure while still serving the purposes of deception. The Bursanov household runs on half-truths: about parentage, about money, about the nature of Nikolai Vasilievich's relationship with Nina.

## Self-Deception

The novel suggests that the most consequential lies are those characters tell themselves. The three sisters' belief that their father's return will solve everything amounts to a collective self-deception that the narrative systematically undermines. Gerhardie portrays self-deception not as weakness but as a survival mechanism in a world devoid of meaning. Fanny Ivanovna's insistence that she sacrificed the divorce "for Nikolai's children" (006-027) is partly true and partly a story she tells herself to make her situation bearable — and the novel refuses to adjudicate between these possibilities.

## The Narrator's Complicity

The narrator is deeply implicated in the economy of lies. "I was in the awkward predicament of having to dissemble. ... I was a liar" (011-003), he admits, positioning himself not as a detached observer of others' deceptions but as a participant. His retrospective narration is itself an act of reconstruction that may be another form of lying — the lie of narrative coherence imposed on chaotic experience.

## Lies and Social Order

Lying serves a social function in the novel's world. Characters withhold truths, make false promises, and maintain appearances not out of malice but because the social order depends on these fictions. When the Admiral finally yields to the Russians' importuning, he does so "by way of humouring them, as one is inclined to humour quaint, unreasonable children who know no better" (027-033). The decision is presented as a kind of benevolent lie — the Admiral maintains the fiction of British firmness while capitulating in practice. The novel suggests that such mutually sustained fictions are what allow society to function at all.

## Key Passage

> "Half-truths are more dangerous than lies," said she. (009-056)
