Siberia in William Gerhardie's *Futility* is simultaneously a geographical reality, a political theatre, and a psychological condition. It is the territory the [[Nikolai Vasilievich]] family crosses in their flight from revolutionary [[Petrograd]] to Far Eastern [[Vladivostok]]; it is the arena of the [[allied-intervention|Allied Intervention]] and [[kolchaks-army|Kolchak's]] doomed White regime; and it is the landscape that most fully embodies the novel's central emotion — "that sense of utter hopelessness that clings to a Siberian winter night" (026-001). Across the novel's third part, Siberia transforms from backdrop into subject, its vast plains and frozen railways becoming the objective correlative of futility itself.

### The Dual Entrance

Siberia enters *Futility* through two portals — the goldmines and the Intervention — and the novel never fully separates them. The goldmines are Nikolai's private Siberia, a fantasy of wealth whose geographical remoteness makes it permanently deferred. The Intervention is the public Siberia, a "comic opera" of great-power meddling staged on an implausibly vast set. The two Sibiras intersect in Nikolai's mind, where "the Czechs, as obviously they ought, would compensate him for his loss on the goldmines" (020-065) — the logic of fantasy attaching itself to the machinery of geopolitics.

## In the Novel

Siberia enters *Futility* through dual portals: the family's goldmines and the Allied Intervention. The goldmines — Nikolai Vasilievich's eternal, never-materializing source of wealth — are located in Siberia, and their geographical remoteness is essential to their function in the novel. No one has seen them; no one can verify their existence; their being-in-Siberia makes them permanently deferred. "He had gold-mining concessions in Siberia" (006-024) is a statement that hovers between fact and fantasy, and the distance between Petrograd and Siberia is the measure of that ambiguity.

The Allied Intervention provides Siberia's other narrative entrance:

> The Admiral and I, and a few others — interesting types, I can assure you — travelled to Siberia, where we engaged in a series of comic opera attempts to wipe out the Russian revolution. (019-002)

The juxtaposition — "comic opera attempts" against the immensity of Siberia — is characteristically Gerhardian. The Intervention, a real historical event involving tens of thousands of troops, is rendered as farce staged on an implausibly vast set.

### The Departure: A Hostage Train

The family's departure from Petrograd for Siberia is one of the novel's grand comic set-pieces — a train carrying not just Nikolai and his immediate circle but the entire extended dependency network:

> He travelled with Fanny Ivanovna, Sonia, Nina, Baron Wunderhausen, Kniaz, Eberheim and the bookkeeper Stanitski. His wife was in the same train, but in a different carriage, and she insisted on having Vera with her, for she was not well, and Cecedek was merely a man. Eisenstein followed her. At times it seemed as if he had lost sight of them; but he invariably turned up again — at one station or another. (019-060)

The train is a microcosm of the family economy: legal wife in one carriage, de facto wife in another, daughters distributed between them, hangers-on and parasites scattered throughout. Siberia, before it is even reached, is already an organizing principle — the eastward vector that concentrates and exposes the family's impossible geometry.

The train journey through Siberia occupies much of Part III and becomes the novel's dominant structural metaphor. Characters board the Trans-Siberian Express "as if in order to do something at the end of the journey, while actually the journey is in excess of anything we are likely to achieve" (022-056). The train moves, but arrival changes nothing:

> "Motives!" he cried. "That is the very point. There are no motives. The motives are naught. It is the consequences. Where are we going? Why are we going? Look: we are moving. Going somewhere. Doing something. The train rushes through Siberia. The wheels are moving. The engine-drivers are adding fuel to the engines. Why? Why are we here? What are we doing in Siberia? Where are we heading for? Something. Somewhere. But what? Where? Why?" (022-053)

[[uncle-kostia|Uncle Kostia's]] outburst on the train is the novel's philosophy in concentrated form. The motion is real — "the wheels are moving" — but the purpose is absent. Siberia, crossed at speed, becomes the landscape of purposeless motion: vast, indifferent, and entirely unresponsive to the human demand for meaning.

## The Siberian Landscape

Gerhardie's Siberia is rendered through a handful of recurring images that accumulate power through repetition:

| Image | Passage | Significance |
|-------|---------|-------------|
| The plains | "immense, monotonous Siberian plains, dull and melancholy" (022-027) | Landscape as emotional register |
| The winter night | "there raged the snowstorm of a Siberian winter night" (026-031) | Nature as overwhelming force |
| The railway track | "pitiably disorganized Siberian track" (022-005) | Civilization as fragile and failing |
| The melancholy | "The melancholy of the Siberian plain" (022-030) | Emotion projected onto geography |

The landscape is never merely descriptive. When the narrator notes "the melancholy of the Siberian plain must have communicated itself..." (022-030), the sentence deliberately trails off — the melancholy is too diffuse to be attributed to any single cause. Siberia, in Gerhardie's handling, is less a place than an atmosphere, and its most important quality is its indifference to the human comedy played out upon it.

### The View from the Train Window

The Siberian landscape is seen almost entirely from the moving train — a mobile frame that reinforces the novel's sense of passing through without arriving:

> On we went. Two days before we had left Irkutsk. The train rushed and roared and rattled. It was a weather that breeds pessimists. I stood looking out upon the steppes, these immense, monotonous Siberian plains, dull and melancholy in the rain. (022-027)

The landscape is "immense, monotonous," adjectives that describe both Siberia and futility itself. The narrator stands at the window, a spectator of emptiness. "It was a weather that breeds pessimists" — the sentence collapses climate into temperament, making the external world responsible for internal states.

And against this vastness, the human intimacy of the railway carriage persists:

> We were jolly, exuberant, self-centred and sentimental. I felt distinctly pleased with myself. I knew not why; that is the secret of good wine. Some people laughed, others after the manner of the Slav were fain to weep; and outside there raged the snowstorm of a Siberian winter night. (026-031)

The contrast is the point: inside the carriage, wine and sentiment; outside, the snowstorm. Siberia is what rages beyond the window, indifferent to the human warmth within and perfectly capable of extinguishing it.

## Key Passages

> The Admiral and I, and a few others — interesting types, I can assure you — travelled to Siberia, where we engaged in a series of comic opera attempts to wipe out the Russian revolution. By now, "Intervention" has been relegated to the shelf of history. But I cannot but remember it, not merely as an adventure in futility, as admittedly it was, but as an ever-shifting, changing sense of being alive.
> (019-002)

This passage, the novel's most direct statement of the Siberian adventure's meaning, performs a characteristic Gerhardian reversal. The Intervention is admitted to be futile, yet the memory of it pulses with vitality — "an ever-shifting, changing sense of being alive." Futility and aliveness are not opposed but intertwined, and Siberia is the place where this paradox becomes experiential rather than abstract.

> Add to this the knowledge of a civil war fumbling in the snow, of people starving in corners, and that sense of utter hopelessness that clings to a Siberian winter night.
> (026-001)

The sentence accumulates miseries — civil war, starvation, hopelessness — and attaches them all to the Siberian night, which "clings" like damp or cold. The verb is crucial: hopelessness is not an internal state but an external condition, something the landscape imposes on those who move through it.

## Political Geography

Siberia during the [[Russian Civil War]] was a patchwork of competing authorities. The novel registers this fragmentation through the characters' navigation of multiple, overlapping powers:

- **[[kolchaks-army|Kolchak's Army]]** controlled [[omsk|Omsk]] and claimed authority over White-held Siberia (023-001)
- **The [[Czech Legion]]** controlled the Trans-Siberian Railway (019-060, 020-065)
- **The [[allied-intervention|Allied Intervention]]** maintained a presence in major cities and along the railway (019-002)
- **Local authorities** and **[[bolsheviks|Bolshevik partisans]]** contested control of territory (027-003)

This jurisdictional chaos is the political equivalent of the novel's domestic chaos — multiple claimants to authority, none fully legitimate, all partially impotent. Nikolai Vasilievich's attempt to navigate this landscape — seeking permission to travel from one authority after another — mirrors his navigation of the family's domestic politics: the same combination of persistence, improvisation, and guaranteed failure.

### Omsk and the Kolchak Regime

The train's arrival at Omsk marks one of the novel's sharpest political passages:

> When the train arrived at Omsk, the new regime of Kolchak had been established. The Admiral was distinctly pleased with the change; for he no longer believed in granting the Russian people a Constituent Assembly because he had grounds for thinking that the Russian people, if given this opportunity, would take advantage of it and elect a government other than that of Kolchak. And the Admiral was rather fond of little Kolchak, whose interpretation of democracy was that of denying the people the chance of expressing it. (023-001)

The irony is layered: the Admiral's fondness for Kolchak is based on Kolchak's anti-democratic credentials; the British intervention, ostensibly supporting Russian self-determination, backs a dictator who denies the people any voice. Siberia is where the gap between Allied rhetoric and Allied practice becomes visible — and where Gerhardie's narrator permits himself his sharpest political satire.

## The Trans-Siberian Journey

The family's journey from Petrograd to Vladivostok is one of the novel's structuring events. The decision to leave is driven by the [[Russian Revolution 1917]]: "He decided to leave Petrograd for Siberia, and his families, dependents and hangers-on naturally all followed him" (019-059). The journey transforms the family from residents into refugees, and the railway carriage — a confined space in which the family's dynamics play out without respite — becomes a microcosm of their larger predicament.

The departure itself is described with a grim grandeur: "That night we started on our trip along the great, now pitiably disorganized Siberian track. It was a lovely night in late autumn" (022-005). The adjective "pitiably" does the work of an entire political analysis — the track is not just disorganized but pitiable, a piece of imperial infrastructure reduced by war and revolution to something that elicits not anger or frustration but pity.

The journey also provides the novel's most concentrated images of motion-without-progress. The train "rushes through Siberia" (022-053) but the destination, when reached, proves indistinguishable from the departure point. The narrator's "ineffectual return" to Vladivostok (002-001) completes the circle: the journey through Siberia, for all its immensity, changes nothing.

## Thematic Significance

Siberia is the geographical form of **[[futility-theme|futility (theme)]]**. Its vastness dwarfs human effort; its winter overwhelms human intention; its railway promises movement but delivers only displacement. The novel's political content — the Intervention, the Civil War, the Kolchak regime — is filtered through the Siberian landscape and emerges as farce. Gerhardie's Siberia is not the romantic wilderness of nineteenth-century Russian literature but a space of bureaucratic obstruction, frozen tracks, and "comic opera" military adventures.

The relationship between Siberia and **[[hope-in-futility|Hope in Futility]]** is particularly intimate. The goldmines are in Siberia; hope is therefore in Siberia; but Siberia is unreachable, or reachable only to reveal that the goldmines are not there. The geographical distance between the characters and their hopes is the novel's most precise metaphor for the psychological distance between hope and fulfillment.
