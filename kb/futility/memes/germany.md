## Overview

Germany in William Gerhardie's *Futility* is simultaneously a homeland and an enemy nation. For characters like [[fanny-ivanovna|Fanny Ivanovna]], who is German by birth or descent, Germany represents origin and identity; for the novel's Russian and British characters, it is the nation against which they are fighting World War I. This duality — Germany as both intimate and adversarial — creates the novel's characteristic tension between personal identity and political categorization.

## Fanny Ivanovna's Germany

Fanny Ivanovna's German identity is a recurring motif. "She was a German subject, and when war broke out she was to go back" [015-008] — her nationality making her an enemy alien in the country where she lives. The accusation that she was "a German governess" [006-028] in the household carries the sting of wartime suspicion: to be German in wartime Russia is to be permanently suspect. Her "people in Germany" [011-004] represent a connection she cannot sever and cannot safely maintain.

## The German-Russian Community

The novel features several characters of German origin in Russia: [[eberheim|Eberheim]] is "an elderly gentleman of German" extraction [015-008], and [[baron-wunderhausen|Baron Wunderhausen]] is a German-Russian baron. This community of Germans in Russia — long-established, often aristocratic — finds itself caught between identities as war makes nationality a matter of life and death. Germany in the novel is thus less a place than a condition: the condition of being categorized, of having one's identity determined by forces beyond one's control.

## Wartime Germany

World War I frames Germany as the enemy, but Gerhardie's treatment of this enmity is characteristically indirect. The war is mentioned, its effects are felt, but Germany as a geopolitical antagonist barely registers. What matters is not Germany the nation but "German" as a label applied to individuals — a label that can destroy lives without reference to any action or belief on the part of the labeled.
