# Happiness in Futility

In *Futility*, happiness is not celebrated but suspected. The narrator watches Chekhov's *Three Sisters* "with that stupid scepticism that comes from too much happiness" (003-022) — happiness is a kind of blindness, an incapacity to recognize tragedy. Gerhardie treats happiness as a temporary condition, always about to be interrupted, and perhaps most fully experienced only in retrospect, when it has already faded.

## Happiness as Obstacle

The narrator's "stupid scepticism" during the theatre performance is the most explicit treatment: his happiness prevents him from engaging with the Chekhovian suffering on stage. "To me, buoyant and impatient, the people in the play appeared preposterous. They annoyed me" (003-022). Happiness is the wrong register for Chekhov — and perhaps for life as Gerhardie depicts it. The novel suggests that to be happy is to be inattentive, to miss the real texture of existence.

## The Long Happy Evenings

Yet the novel is not without its idylls. "It was on one of those long, happy evenings which it had now become my custom to spend regularly at their large, luxurious flat" (004-001) — the evenings in the Mohovaya flat are genuinely happy, and the narrator's retrospective narration preserves their quality. But the past tense is crucial: these are remembered happinesses, happinesses that the narrator knows, as he writes, are already lost.

## Key Passage

> I sat behind Nina, and watched with that stupid scepticism that comes from too much happiness. To me, buoyant and impatient, the people in the play appeared preposterous. They annoyed me. They distressed me intensely. (003-022)
