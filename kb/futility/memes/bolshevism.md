# Bolshevism in Futility

In Gerhardie's *Futility*, Bolshevism appears not as an ideology to be debated but as a background condition — part of the political weather that shapes the novel's events without ever becoming its subject. The word "Bolshevism" functions primarily as a marker of the chaos that the novel's characters navigate with varying degrees of comic incompetence.

## Overview

The novel's treatment of Bolshevism is characteristically oblique. The Admiral's opposition to Nikolai Vasilievich's travel requests is couched in political terms — he must balance "that of international Bolshevism" against personal loyalties (022-001) — but the real drama is bureaucratic, not ideological. The Admiral's repeated "No" and the Russians' refusal to accept it becomes a comic set-piece about national character, with Bolshevism fading into the background as mere context for a farce about human obstinacy.

## Political Context as Comedy

Gerhardie's narrator recounts a week in which "our waiting-room was crowded with petitioners" — family members seeking passage on the Admiral's train to Omsk (022-001). The political mission against Bolshevism provides the pretext for this bureaucratic comedy, but the narrator's attention is firmly on the absurdity of the interactions: the Admiral insisting that "when an Englishman says No he does mean No," while the Russians treat No as merely the opening position in a negotiation (022-003).

## Bolshevism as Threat and Excuse

"There was altogether too much Bolshevism abroad" (024-001) — the phrase captures the novel's attitude: Bolshevism is a diffuse menace, invoked more often as rhetorical justification than analyzed as political reality. When a character shouts "None of your Bolshevism here, please!" (028-023), the word has become a catch-all for any unwelcome disruption, drained of specific ideological content.

## Key Passage

> For a week or so Nikolai Vasilievich kept on applying to the Admiral for a coupé in his train to Omsk, in the teeth of implacable refusals. Then, after much opposition from the Admiral, and a passionate, though indirect, appeal by Fanny Ivanovna, who brought to bear on him the combined moral force of her conjugal rights and her maternal affections, strengthened by an innuendo, subtly conveyed, that she might, if refused, throw in her lot, and with it that of her daughters, with that of international Bolshevism, this was conceded. (022-001)
