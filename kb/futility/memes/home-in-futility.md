# Home in Futility

## Overview

Home in *Futility* is less a place than an absence — a lost condition that characters remember, yearn for, and fail to reconstruct. Set in the world of Russian exiles after the Revolution, the novel explores what it means to be homeless in both a literal and existential sense. The characters move through a succession of provisional spaces — dachas, flats, hotels, waiting rooms, restaurants — none of which can sustain the illusion of permanence. Home is what has been left behind, and what has been left behind may never have existed in the first place.

## The Lost Home

The narrator's journey is framed by departure and return:

> "And because of this and the sense that I am marking time till the big steamer comes and bears me home to England I am eagerly retrospective." (002-001)

Yet this "home" — England — is not the home of memory. It is a destination, not a place of origin; a departure from Russia rather than a return to belonging. The narrator describes his arrival in Vladivostok as an "ineffectual return," a phrase that captures the paradox of the novel's geography: he returns to a place that was never home, and he waits to depart for a home that may not recognize him.

The Russian home that characters describe — "they were in their own happy home" (003-022) — exists only in recollection and perhaps never existed at all. This line occurs during the performance of Chekhov's *Three Sisters*, as the Bursanovs watch a play about another family that has lost its home. The doubling is precise: the Bursanovs recognize themselves in the Prozorov sisters because both families are defined by the home they no longer have:

> "Fanny Ivanovna and the three sisters watched the play with intense interest, as if the Three Sisters were indeed their own particular tragedy. I sat behind Nina, and watched with that stupid scepticism that comes from too much happiness. To me, buoyant and impatient, the people in the play appeared preposterous. They annoyed me. They distressed me intensely. Their black melancholy, their incredible listlessness seemed merely silly." (003-022)

The narrator, "buoyant and impatient" with "the stupid scepticism that comes from too much happiness," cannot yet understand what the Bursanovs immediately recognize: that the Chekhovian longing for home is not silly but structural, the defining condition of their lives. His impatience with the Prozorovs will, by the novel's end, be replaced by his own experience of displacement — a journey from scepticism to recognition that the novel traces across its geography.

### The Chekhovian Home

The Chekhovian frame is essential to understanding Gerhardie's treatment of home. In Chekhov's play, the Prozorov sisters dream endlessly of returning to Moscow — a home that is more idea than place, a fantasy of restoration that the play systematically denies. The Bursanovs, watching this play, are watching their own condition rendered as art. The narrator, riding home across the frozen Neva, makes the connection explicit:

> "As I drove home across the frozen river, on which the moon spread its yellow light, I thought of the Bursanovs' muddled life, and then Chekhov's Three Sisters dawned upon my memory." (007-056)

Home, for both the Prozorovs and the Bursanovs, is a place that exists only in the optative mood — "to Moscow, to Moscow" — a destination that recedes as one approaches it.

### The Dacha: Home as Season

The novel's first domestic space is the dacha at "a seaside place ten versts from Petersburg" (003-001), a "little wooden structure that hung out above the sea." The dacha is a seasonal home, occupied only in summer, and this temporariness makes it the novel's most characteristic domestic space:

> "I walked home with them to their dacha along the dark and muddy road — it had been raining while we were in the theatre — Nina clinging to my arm." (003-028)

The muddy road and the rain — the texture of a home that is already dissolving into weather — establish the novel's domestic register. The Bursanovs' home is always provisional, always borrowed, always on the verge of being left.

## The Disputed Home

The novel's most intense domestic drama occurs not in exile but in Petrograd, where the question of who belongs in the family home becomes a matter of violent confrontation. Fanny Ivanovna, the mistress of the house without legal standing, asserts her claim with desperate force:

> "'After all,' said Fanny Ivanovna, putting a terrible meaning of her own into these simple words, 'after all I am the mistress of this house. True, I have been thrown into the mud and trampled on, told I am not wanted, done away with, about to be thrown into the street like a dog, but while I am here I am the mistress of this flat. After all, I am!'" (009-019)

The repetition of "after all" — three times in a single speech — is the rhetoric of someone who knows her claim is legally groundless. The "terrible meaning of her own" that Fanny Ivanovna puts into these words is the meaning of someone who has no legal title to the home she occupies. She is the "mistress of this flat" only "while I am here" — a temporal qualification that acknowledges the fragility of her position.

Magda Nikolaevna, Nikolai's legal wife, makes the counter-claim with equal desperation:

> "'I came, and I was not admitted. ... Into my own house, my own flat. It was my flat, Andrei Andreiech. I chose it. I bought the things and arranged them. There isn't a single thing in here that wasn't mine. When all is said and done, they are my children, Andrei Andreiech. And I have to wait outside like some low hawker — a tatarin — on the landing ... not admitted. ...'" (009-034)

The legal wife, who "chose" the flat and "bought the things and arranged them," is excluded from her own home while the mistress presides within. The image of Magda waiting on the landing "like some low hawker — a tatarin" is the novel's most concentrated image of domestic dispossession: the rightful owner reduced to a peddler, barred from the home she furnished. The narrator discovers her weeping "between the large glass double-doors leading out into the dim street" (009-023) — she is trapped in a literal threshold, neither inside the home she claims nor outside the building that contains it.

## Exile in Vladivostok

In Vladivostok, the family occupies cramped rooms with inadequate cooking arrangements, forced to eat at the [[zolotoy-rog|Zolotoy Rog]] restaurant because "the cooking arrangements in the rooms were thoroughly inadequate" (037-004). [[sonia|Sonia]], [[nina|Nina]], and [[vera|Vera]] "sleep here on the sofa and the bed we drag out from the other room" (020-029). This improvised domesticity — a family of eleven crowded into temporary quarters — is the novel's most concrete image of homelessness: not destitution but diminishment, the inability to perform the rituals of home even as the need for them persists.

The family's arrival in Vladivostok is described in language that fuses domestic and national despair:

> "It was raining heavily when we arrived in Vladivostok, and the port, as we surveyed it from the boat, looked grey and hopeless, like the Russian situation. A flat had been allotted us, a bare, unfurnished flat in a deserted house standing in a grim and desolate by-street; and there the Admiral made his temporary headquarters. It poured all day long, and it seemed, indeed, as though the rain, playing havoc with the town, would never cease, even as the misery and blundering in Russia would never cease." (019-003)

The "bare, unfurnished flat in a deserted house" is the opposite of home — a space that has been assigned rather than chosen, in a house that has been abandoned, in a street that is "grim and desolate." The rain that "would never cease" becomes a figure for the permanence of displacement.

### The Spatial Economy of Displacement

Fanny Ivanovna's description of the Vladivostok arrangements reveals the family's spatial economy:

> "Sonia, Nina and Vera sleep here on the sofa and the bed we drag out from the other room. The adjoining room is Nikolai Vasilievich's and mine. The third is Pavl Pavlch's, the Baron's. The others have remained at the hotel — I mean Kniaz and Eberheim. I don't care what Magda Nikolaevna does, but I think she has now found a house. And Uncle Kostia and the rest of them will probably settle at his sister's, the Olenins. Kniaz comes here for his meals and spends the day with us ... though lately — she smiled — he has been going out hunting." (020-029)

The description is a map of domestic dissolution. The family is scattered across multiple locations — the flat, the hotel, Magda's house, the Olenins' — with Kniaz shuttling between them for meals. The "bed we drag out from the other room" is a gesture of domesticity so improvised that it underscores rather than conceals the absence of a real home. This is not a household but a diaspora in miniature.

The restaurant Zolotoy Rog becomes a substitute kitchen, a public space pressed into domestic service:

> "Yesterday I had met them in the Aleutskaya as they returned from the restaurant Zolotoy Rog, where they now always went to lunch: the cooking arrangements in the rooms were thoroughly inadequate." (037-004)

The "thoroughly inadequate" cooking arrangements are a synecdoche for the family's entire domestic situation.

### The Dacha by the Sea

Even in Vladivostok, the characters seek out dachas — seasonal homes that promise a temporary restoration of domestic normalcy but deliver only more displacement:

> "We did not go home. She said, 'I'm tired of seeing Papa, Fanny Ivanovna and Kniaz. They always quarrel, always quarrel. ... Kniaz is the best of the lot.' Instead we went to the Olenins who lived in a remote dacha by the sea. It was a place scarcely accessible by night, for there was not a light and the roads were pools of mud. The environment concentrated all the angry dogs and robbers in the town." (035-001)

The dacha — normally a place of summer leisure — becomes a refuge from family quarrels. But the refuge is scarcely accessible, surrounded by mud and angry dogs, "concentrating all the angry dogs and robbers in the town." Home, even in its dacha form, is a place you reach only by traversing danger.

## Home and National Identity

Gerhardie connects the loss of home to questions of national identity. The preface notes that "the western mind is at home" (P02-002) with certain subjects, but the Russian experience of homelessness — political, cultural, existential — resists such comfortable placement:

> "In Balzac, at all times, the western mind is at home: even when the presentment is obviously a caricature, one knows what is being caricatured. But there are moments — to me at least — in the greatest of Russian novels, and just as I feel the directing pressure of the novelist most strongly on my shoulder, when somehow I stumble, the path fades to a trail, the trail to a sand-heap, and hopelessly I perceive that the clue is gone." (P02-002)

The critic's language — "at home," "stumble," "the path fades" — transforms the act of reading into a spatial experience of disorientation. To read the Russians is to lose one's bearings, to be unhoused from the familiar.

The novel's exiles are doubly unhoused: they have lost their domestic homes and their national home. The train journey from Petrograd to Siberia — with Nikolai's wife in one carriage, his mistress in another, "and his families, dependents and hangers-on naturally all followed him" (019-060) — is an image of a nation in flight, a domestic order reduced to its mobile components. The family's movement eastward is not a journey toward a new home but a retreat from the loss of the old one.

### England as Anti-Home

For the narrator, England represents the fantasy of escape from Russian homelessness. But the novel undermines this fantasy. England is mentioned only as a destination, never as a place with its own textures and routines:

> "And then I only wished that they would go, and that I could return at once to England. The date of sailing was put off from week to week because of passport difficulties and dearth of accommodation in the steamers of the Russian Volunteer Fleet. I was frightened lest they should not be able to get away. For if they stayed, my soul was ruined." (037-001)

The narrator's desire for England is driven less by patriotism than by the fear that the family might "stay" — that Russian homelessness might become permanent, that his "soul was ruined" if he cannot escape. England is not a home to which he returns but an exit from the novel itself.

The narrator reflects on his departure from Petrograd amid the Bolshevik Revolution:

> "Petrograd looked thoroughly nasty on that cold November morning. There was the drizzling snow, and it was still dark as I walked home with Uncle Kostia. We had been at the Finland Station to see off two of Uncle Kostia's nieces who were going abroad." (017-001)

The walk "home" with Uncle Kostia is really a walk away from the Finland Station, the traditional point of departure from Russia. The nieces who are "going abroad" have already escaped; the narrator and Uncle Kostia, walking through the dark and snow, are still trying to reach a home that the revolution is in the process of abolishing.

## Domesticity as Illusion

Fanny Ivanovna's tragedy is that she has always wanted what she can never have: a legitimate home:

> "Perhaps she thought of her position, insecure and unconventional, disused, no longer wanted; and of her instincts so discordant with her life, her instincts that had always been on the side of respectability, the purity of home life, the sanctity of marriage, and the very things, in fact, that had always been denied her: so much so that in her unstable, questionable position she had yet been stringently insistent on this aspect of their life, and always in her heart was reminded that she had no title to enforce that law, no claim, beyond a doleful cry." (026-024)

The pathos is in the gap between instinct and reality: Fanny Ivanovna, who has lived for eleven years as an unofficial wife, who has no legal claim to the home she occupies, whose position depends entirely on Nikolai's continued affection and solvency, "had always been on the side of respectability, the purity of home life, the sanctity of marriage." She wants the very things that her own life, by its structure, denies.

### The Threshold Ritual

The novel's closing domestic image is of the three sisters being "escorted safely home":

> "The three sisters, escorted safely home, would cry out from the house steps, 'Good night, Brothers!' The 'three brothers' then would answer, 'Good night, Sisters!' I alone said nothing. I felt that an additional 'brother' might spoil the symmetry of the arrangement." (036-001)

The ritual is perfectly choreographed — the symmetrical call and response, the doorstep as threshold, the "brothers" and "sisters" in quotation marks — but the symmetry is purchased at the cost of exclusion. The narrator, the odd number, trails behind, excluded from the family tableau. "Home" here is a performance staged for the benefit of the departing brothers, a domestic pageant that conceals the reality of displacement beneath it.

The Admiral's departure sets off a cascade of exits: "Very soon the bulk of them was gone. And then the incredible happened. The three American boys arrived and took the three sisters to a dance. And on the eve of my departure! ... As for me, I would have to go home and pack. ... That settled it" (029-042). The narrator's conclusion is the novel's final judgment on home: it is the place you go to pack, the place you leave. Home, in *Futility*, is never a destination; it is always a departure.
