## Overview

The Russian Provisional Government was the liberal-democratic authority that replaced the Tsarist regime after the [[february-revolution-1917|February Revolution]] and was itself overthrown by the [[bolsheviks|Bolsheviks]] in the October Revolution. In William Gerhardie's *Futility*, the Provisional Government represents the brief, doomed interval between the old order and the new — a period of liberal hope that the novel's characteristic irony renders as merely one more stage in the general collapse.

## Between Two Revolutions

The Provisional Government governed Russia for approximately eight months — from March to November 1917 — during which it attempted to continue [[world-war-i|the war]], convene a Constituent Assembly, and maintain order in a country disintegrating around it. Gerhardie's novel passes through this period without dwelling on its politics; the Provisional Government is felt as the absence of the Tsar and the not-yet-arrival of Bolshevik power. The political chaos is reflected in General Bologoevski's description of competing authorities — "a General Horvat who had formed an All-Russia Government, and there was also a Siberian Government" (019-012) — a proliferation of governments that renders the very concept of legitimate rule absurd.

## The Failure to Govern

The Provisional Government's central failure was its inability to end the war or address land reform — the two demands that mattered most to the Russian population. This failure created the vacuum that the Bolsheviks filled. In the novel's terms, the Provisional Government represents the futility of moderate reform in revolutionary times: the attempt to steer a middle course when only extremes have purchase. The narrator's bureaucratic encounters in government offices — "an official such as you are likely to encounter in almost any Russian government department, dipping it repeatedly into ink" (002-009) — suggest the continuity of administrative dysfunction across regimes.

## Legacy in the Novel

For the novel's characters, the Provisional Government is not a subject of debate but a condition of existence — the brief window during which they might have escaped, the moment of indecision before the trap closed. Its fall is registered not as political event but as the further narrowing of possibilities, one more door closing on a world that has already lost most of its exits. When the narrator arrives in Omsk, the new Kolchak regime has already been established (023-001), rendering the Provisional Government's brief interlude a fading memory.
