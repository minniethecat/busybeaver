The [[bolshevism|Bolsheviks]] in [[russia|Russia]] are the gravitational center around which *Futility*'s entire cast orbits — always present, rarely seen, and understood principally through the displacement they have caused. Gerhardie never takes the reader inside Bolshevik councils or ideology; instead, the Bolsheviks exist as a condition of disruption: they have seized the mines (020-047), invaded the houses (019-059), and rendered an entire social order suddenly obsolete. The [[futility-theme|futility]] that pervades the novel finds its objective correlative in this political upheaval. To the novel's stranded Russian officers, dispossessed bourgeoisie, and bewildered Allied observers, "Bolshevik" functions less as a political designation than as a term for everything that has gone inexplicably wrong with the world.

### The Morning the World Changed

The Bolshevik Revolution enters the novel at street level, through a single character's morning walk:

> Petrograd looked thoroughly nasty on that cold November morning. There was the drizzling snow, and it was still dark as I walked home with Uncle Kostia. We had been at the Finland Station to see off two of Uncle Kostia's nieces who were going abroad. It was the morning of the Bolshevik Revolution, and Uncle Kostia looked pessimistic. "Do you remember all those student-revolutionaries, the heroes of our young intelligentsia, who had been persecuted by the old regime? Well," — he pointed from the bridge — (017-001)

The revolution arrives not as a cannonade but as a "nasty" morning, with "drizzling snow" and a pessimistic uncle. The Finland Station — site of Lenin's return — is the destination not for a revolutionary arrival but for a family farewell. Gerhardie's technique is to frame the world-historical within the domestic, and the revolution is no exception: it is something that happens while you are seeing off your nieces.

## The Revolution as Domestic Inconvenience

The Bolshevik Revolution enters *Futility* not as a world-historical event but as a series of domestic catastrophes. It is "the morning of the Bolshevik Revolution" and [[uncle-kostia|Uncle Kostia]] — a liberal lawyer — finds himself suddenly adrift in a city he no longer recognizes (017-001). The revolution impresses itself upon characters primarily through the disruption of personal arrangements: houses are requisitioned, families are forced eastward (019-059, 019-060), and the machinery of divorce — already Byzantine under the old regime — becomes impossible under the new.

The narrator, arriving in [[petrograd|Petrograd]] on the eve of these events, observes the revolution with the same ironic detachment he brings to everything else — as a spectacle that happens to other people, even when it is happening to him. The "great bloodless revolution" (014-010) becomes, in Gerhardie's rendering, a comedy of misrecognition in which nobody quite understands what they are participating in.

### The House Invaded

Nikolai Vasilievich's complaint about his house captures the revolution's material reality:

> His family, he said, had decided to leave Petrograd and go east because their house, which, strictly speaking, belonged to them no longer, had, since the Bolshevik revolution, been invaded by a host of undesirable people and there was hardly a room left in the house that they could call their own. (019-059)

The phrase "strictly speaking, belonged to them no longer" is exquisitely precise. The house still stands; the family still occupies it; but the legal framework that made it "theirs" has vanished. The "undesirable people" who have invaded are the revolution made flesh — not ideological abstractions but bodies occupying rooms, sleeping in beds, making the family's former life physically uninhabitable.

## A Rhetorical Catch-All

One of the novel's sharpest satirical devices is the promiscuity with which the epithet "Bolshevik" is deployed. It becomes a universal term of abuse, emptied of specific political content and applied to anyone inconvenient:

> "The Czechs are such awful swine. They're all Bolsheviks." (020-086)

> And then added, "And the Americans, too, are Bolsheviks. President Wilson. Nothing will come of it all." (020-087)

The escalation is comic: Czechs, Americans, Wilson himself — all Bolsheviks. The word has ceased to mean anything except "people I am angry with." [[fanny-ivanovna|Fanny Ivanovna]] accuses a prince of being a Bolshevik (029-035); [[nikolai-vasilievich|Nikolai Vasilievich]] wonders whether the Bolsheviks will return his house (020-065); the Admiral declares that "they are just a pack of damned Bolsheviks, the lot of them" (029-023). The term's semantic drift — from a specific revolutionary faction to a generalized expression of impotent rage — mirrors the broader collapse of meaning that the novel documents. When everything is falling apart, "Bolshevik" becomes the name for the force of disintegration itself, whatever its actual political form.

### The Commissaries Never Wash

The novel's treatment of anti-Bolshevik propaganda is both satirical and diagnostic:

> I remember how a remark of General Bologoevski's, that he understood the Bolshevik commissaries never washed, lit up the Admiral's face with ominous glee, and one could guess at sight that he condemned the Bolshevik commissaries. (020-004)

The Admiral's reaction is the reaction of a man who has found a moral justification for his hatred that requires no intellectual effort whatsoever. If the commissaries never wash, nothing else needs to be said about them. The hygiene slander is the laziest possible propaganda — and, Gerhardie implies, precisely the level of argument that sustained the White cause. The "ominous glee" on the Admiral's face is the pleasure of moral certainty purchased at no cost.

## Military Reality

Beneath the satire, the Bolsheviks represent a genuine military force — the only one that matters:

> "One national Russia! Excuse me, but — but — but — but if there is any national Russia today it is all on the other side. As for the Russian Army, the only Russian Army now is the Bolshevik Army. The others have all melted away." (029-034)

The stammer — "but — but — but — but" — is not a character tic but a rhetorical device. The speaker is trying to find a polite way to state an impolite truth: the White cause is finished. "Melted away" is the perfect verb for a military collapse that was not a defeat but a dissolution. The other armies did not lose; they simply ceased to exist.

The anti-Bolshevik Administration to which various characters appeal for redress (023-006) is revealed as a paper government, propped up by Allied support but incapable of projecting real power. Nikolai Vasilievich arrives at Omsk "suspicious of the punitive expedition" and seeking "redress and compensation in regard to his goldmines" (023-006), treating the seat of White power as a complaints department for dispossessed capitalists.

The Allied intervention — British, French, American, Japanese — is portrayed as a half-hearted enterprise, undermined by mutual suspicion and the dawning recognition that the Bolsheviks cannot be dislodged. Characters shuttle between [[vladivostok|Vladivostok]] and [[omsk|Omsk]], attending conferences and drafting memoranda, while Bolshevik forces consolidate their hold on the Russian interior. The "offensive against the Bolsheviks" that briefly recaptures the mines (019-060) is a temporary reversal, not a turning point.

### Nikolai's Hope Against Evidence

Even amid military collapse, Nikolai clings to his fantasy of restitution:

> "Well," said Nikolai Vasilievich, "I say this: it's no use Mr. Thomson coming out here at present, while the mines are still in Bolshevik hands. And I don't want to hold out false hopes to Mr. Thomson, for one can never quite be sure what may happen in Siberia yet. But between ourselves, I may tell you that now that the English have arrived and — well, that this punitive expedition to the mines has been arranged, we have good reason to feel optimistic." (020-047)

The optimism is qualified, hedged, directed at "between ourselves" — but it persists. The Bolsheviks hold the mines; the English have arrived; a punitive expedition has been "arranged" — these facts, strung together in Nikolai's hopeful syntax, constitute an argument that no rational observer would accept. The Bolsheviks appear in this passage as the obstacle to Nikolai's schemes, but the real obstacle is the gap between his fantasy and reality — a gap the Bolsheviks merely happen to occupy.

## Perception and Propaganda

The novel's characters absorb their understanding of the Bolsheviks through rumour, anecdote, and the lurid imaginings of exile. Bolshevik commissaries "never washed" (020-004); Bolsheviks are turning "honest people into Bolsheviks" (023-017); they are "in the pay of the Germans" (023-017). These allegations — some grounded in fragmentary fact, others pure fabulation — constitute the epistemology of civil war: when reliable information is impossible, prejudice expands to fill the void.

Gerhardie's treatment of this dynamic is characteristically double-edged. The absurdity of the rumours is evident, yet the fear that generates them is real. The Bolsheviks, by upturning the world his characters inhabited, have created a landscape in which no received truth can be taken for granted — a condition that *Futility* itself, as a novel written from the wreckage, both documents and reproduces.
