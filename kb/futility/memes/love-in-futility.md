# Love in Futility

Romantic love in William Gerhardie's *Futility* is never straightforward. It is obsessive, misdirected, entangled with money, politics, and family dysfunction — and almost never reciprocated. The narrator Andrei Andreiech's longing for [[nina|Nina]], the Admiral's infatuations, the tangled courtships among the Bursanov sisters: all are variations on the same theme of love that cannot find its object, or that finds it and destroys it. For the novel's characters, the experience of love "is inseparable from its background of war and revolution, love's relation to the background being that of the mirage to the desert" (019-002).

## Overview

Love in *Futility* operates under a peculiar law: it intensifies in proportion to its impossibility. The narrator wanders through "a park essentially for lovers" (002-011), thinking of them in the abstract while his own love for Nina remains unconsummated. The novel does not depict happy couples; it depicts people yearning, scheming, and suffering — and finding in that suffering a perverse vitality. The abandoned park, with its penknife-carved initials and whirling yellow leaves, becomes an emblem of love itself: once lived, now only remembered, but beautiful in its decay.

Nikolai Vasilievich, the patriarch himself a late-life romantic, confesses the transformative power of love with unexpected gravity: "It has come late, but still it has come at last, and I am proud — don't laugh — I am proud that at my age I should be capable of such love" (006-065). Even this confession is framed by the novel's irony — Fanny Ivanovna's response is to call it "obstinacy" rather than love (006-061), and the reader cannot be sure she is wrong.

## Forms of Love

### Narratorial Obsession

Andrei Andreiech's love for Nina is the novel's central romantic thread — a love that the narrator himself admits may be indistinguishable from obsession. He watches Nina from behind at the theatre during the *Three Sisters* performance, "with that stupid scepticism that comes from too much happiness" (003-022), already aware that his happiness is precarious. His love never finds its object; Nina's absence at the pier becomes the final, devastating note: "She did not come. That settled it" (030-004).

### The Obstacle of Obstinacy

Fanny Ivanovna reflects on the family's romantic entanglements with characteristic Russian fatalism: "Perhaps I like to think that it is obstinacy and not love. But if it is love... what can you do?" (006-061). Love in this world is indistinguishable from stubbornness — or perhaps love simply *is* stubbornness, a refusal to see clearly. Nikolai Vasilievich himself insists:

> "It is love," he said very seriously. "It has come late, but still it has come at last, and I am proud — don't laugh — I am proud that at my age I should be capable of such love. I thought that I had loved, I had loved, you, Fanny; but this is the love that comes once only, to which you yield gloriously, magnificently, or you are crushed and broken and thrust aside. …" (006-065)

This is the novel's most direct statement on romantic love — and it is spoken by a man whose romantic judgment has been consistently terrible, to a woman he has refused to marry for twenty years. The sincerity is genuine; the comedy is unmistakable.

### The Noble Lover's Refusal

When a character declares he "wouldn't think of taking advantage of your innocence" (010-009), the refusal is itself a kind of seduction — or an evasion dressed as honour. The narrator's sardonic reply — "that 'We all kill the thing we love,' and, in fact, a few other expensive things of that sort" (010-013) — collapses the distinction between romantic nobility and self-serving cowardice. The novel is full of such moments where love is declared precisely by being denied.

### Love Across Class and Nation

Nikolai Vasilievich's romantic entanglements cross boundaries of class, nationality, and propriety. "Does she love him? Cannot live without him?" the question is asked, and the answer — "'Don't you believe it'" — is cynicism worn as wisdom (007-002). Love in the Bursanov household is never separable from the question of money: Fanny Ivanovna's love for Nikolai began when "love was thrown into the bargain. It was unexpected, irrelevant. And then love became salient, supreme, altogether dominating" (006-024). The economic entanglement underlies every romantic attachment in the novel.

### Women Who Could Have Loved

Madame Olenin appears "with that look of hers as though she could have loved a lot" (035-003) — a single sentence that captures the novel's entire romantic philosophy. Love is a capacity, a potential that life systematically frustrates. The tragedy is not that love fails, but that it was never allowed to begin. This theme echoes through the novel's gallery of unmarried women, disappointed wives, and girls whose futures are foreclosed by revolution and exile.

## Love and the Landscape

The novel repeatedly associates love with place — specifically with the places where love is absent or impossible. The Admiral and the narrator travel across Siberia engaged in what the narrator calls "a series of comic opera attempts to wipe out the Russian revolution," but the memory persists "not merely as an adventure in futility, as admittedly it was, but as an ever-shifting, changing sense of being alive. For the experience of love is inseparable from its background of war and revolution, love's relation to the background being that of the mirage to the desert" (019-002). This is the novel's most explicit theorization of love's entanglement with place: love is not diminished by its absurd surroundings but somehow made more real by them.

In a Siberian moment of unexpected tenderness, the narrator watches the dissolving landscape and feels "a kind of tenderness, a warm, transfiguring love" (023-033) — a feeling that belongs neither to Nina nor to any specific person, but to the atmosphere itself. Love in *Futility* is often like this: a diffuse quality of experience rather than a relationship between two people.

## Key Passage

> I paced the country, now in the embrace of autumn. I wandered in remote places by the sea, in the abandoned park that used to be a park essentially for lovers, and thought of them. Here the foliage was more dense, the corners more secluded, the disorder more magnificent. I sat on an old bench that had names and initials cut out with a penknife, under the trees turning gold and auburn, and shivered in the sharp autumn wind that sent the fallen yellow leaves whirling down the alley. (002-011)

This passage establishes the novel's elegiac register for love — love as something already past, something that happened to other people in a park that is now abandoned. The "magnificent disorder" of the foliage mirrors the romantic chaos of the Bursanov household: beautiful, overgrown, decaying.

> "I loved him. Love was thrown into the bargain. It was unexpected, irrelevant. And then love became salient, supreme, altogether dominating; and as I realized how I loved him, so I realized that my family, my sacrifice and all that this had once meant to me, were but of secondary importance in the face of my love." (006-024)

Fanny Ivanovna's account of falling in love with Nikolai Vasilievich reveals the transactional origins of the novel's central romantic relationship — and its transformation into something genuinely felt. The progression from "irrelevant" to "supreme" is the emotional arc the novel distrusts but cannot fully dismiss.
