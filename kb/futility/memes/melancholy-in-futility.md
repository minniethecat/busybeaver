## Overview

Melancholy is the emotional key in which William Gerhardie's *Futility* is composed. It pervades the novel not as a single character's mood but as an atmospheric condition — as inescapable as the Siberian weather, as fundamental to the Russian character as the samovar. Characters are happy in their melancholy, tearfully emotional in it, and even the landscape participates: plains are "dull and melancholy in the rain" [022-027], and the evening air carries "that yearning melancholy call of life" [016-026].

## Russian Melancholy as National Trait

From the novel's opening sections, melancholy is identified as a distinctly Russian quality. The narrator observes "their black melancholy, their incredible inefficiency" [003-022] as defining traits of the Russian household he enters. This double attribution — melancholy paired with inefficiency — establishes the novel's characteristic irony: melancholy is both a spiritual depth and a practical liability, a sign of soul and an obstacle to action.

Gerhardie's narrator, as an English observer, positions melancholy as something foreign — a Russian condition he can describe but never fully inhabit. His outsider status allows him to anatomize Russian sadness with clinical precision while remaining somewhat immune to it, though by the novel's end the Siberian landscape has infected even his English sensibilities.

## The Pleasure of Sadness

Gerhardie's treatment of melancholy subverts the conventional opposition between happiness and sadness. Nikolai Vasilievich is described as "happy in his melancholy" [022-011], finding in his own sadness a kind of emotional richness. This is not clinical depression but a cultivated aesthetic stance — a way of experiencing the world more intensely. The tears he sheds are not signs of misery but of emotional fullness: "tearfully emotional, he" [022-011] inhabits his sadness as one might inhabit a warm room.

This paradox — that melancholy can be pleasurable — is central to the novel's emotional logic. The characters do not want to be cured of their sadness; their sadness is their depth. To be cheerful in Gerhardie's world is to be shallow; to be melancholy is to be alive to the true condition of things. In this sense, melancholy is the affective form of [[futility-theme|futility]] itself — the feeling that corresponds to the philosophical recognition that nothing ultimately matters.

## Landscape as Emotion

The Siberian setting makes melancholy geographical. The narrator gazes at "the melancholy of the Siberian plain" [022-030] as if the landscape itself were a feeling. "The samovar produced melancholy notes" [029-067] — even domestic objects participate in the general mood. The "fall of evening, and the melancholy of the ages" [033-050] collapses personal sadness into historical time, making the narrator's private feeling part of a vast, impersonal inheritance.

This technique — the projection of emotion onto landscape and object — is characteristic of Gerhardie's method. Nothing in the novel is merely material; everything is saturated with feeling. The [[nature-in-futility|Siberian landscape]] is not a neutral setting but an active participant in the novel's emotional life.

## Melancholy and the Russian Soul

Melancholy is the emotional signature of the [[russian-soul|Russian soul]] as Gerhardie constructs it. Where the English narrator represents order, irony, and detachment, the Russian characters represent depth, chaos, and feeling. Melancholy is what the narrator both envies and fears — the emotional intensity that his Englishness denies him, but that also makes the Russians incapable of practical action. Nikolai's melancholy is "vast and formless" [022-032], like Russia itself, and like Russia it resists all attempts at organization or reform.

## Melancholy's Flip Side

The novel's melancholy is unstable — it can "vanish" as suddenly as it descends. Nikolai, having been tearfully emotional moments before, is soon "spitting" — "his melancholy had vanished" [022-026]. This volatility is characteristic of Gerhardie's emotional register: feelings are intense but impermanent, sincere in the moment but subject to comic reversal. The speed with which melancholy gives way to irritation or appetite suggests that even the novel's deepest emotions are, at some level, performances — real while they last, but abandoned without ceremony when something else demands attention.
