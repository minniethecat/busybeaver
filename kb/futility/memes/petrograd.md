# Petrograd

St. Petersburg, renamed Petrograd during the First World War to sound less German, is the primary setting of *Futility* — the city where the Bursanov family maintains its "large, luxurious flat in the Mohovaya" (004-001), where the narrator works at the British Embassy, and where the *Three Sisters* is performed at the local theatre. The city appears in the novel not as a panoramic tourist destination but as an intimate geography of interiors, staircases, and frozen river crossings — the enclosed world of a doomed aristocracy on the eve of revolution.

## In the Novel

### The Embassy and the Mohovaya

The narrator's Petrograd is defined by two poles: the British Embassy and the Bursanov flat. His first morning in the city is spent ascending "the broad staircase with its worn red carpet to the Chancery," where "very perfect young men, very perfectly dressed, were conversing" (014-002) — a world of diplomatic propriety that the novel will systematically dismantle. The Mohovaya flat, by contrast, is the novel's domestic centre — the site of "long, happy evenings" (004-001) where the narrator is initiated into the family's secrets and the city's larger dramas are refracted through the lens of domestic comedy.

The flat itself is both a real presence and a phantom asset. Fanny Ivanovna rages that it is Nikolai Vasilievich's money that sustains it: "A house-owner in Petersburg! Goldmines in Siberia! A millionaire! ... this house in the Mohovaya! That's all, in fact, they are after" (007-005). The house, like so much in *Futility*, is simultaneously solid and spectral — a real address and a fiction that sustains an entire family economy.

### The City in Wartime and Revolution

By early 1917, Petrograd has changed. Walking from Zina's house to the Mohovaya, the narrator passes "the sombre silhouettes of the snow-covered barges frozen on the Neva" while "soldiers and civilians alike walked aimlessly, rifle slung over the shoulder" (015-002). The city's beauty — the frozen river, the yellow moonlight — persists alongside the gathering violence. The revolution enters the novel not as a dramatic event but as a change in the texture of the streets: more soldiers, more aimlessness, a growing sense that the old order is disintegrating.

### The Mohovaya as Battleground

The flat becomes the arena for the novel's domestic wars. Fanny Ivanovna stakes her claim — "after all I am the mistress of this house" (009-019) — in a declaration that is simultaneously an assertion of power and an admission of vulnerability. The broad winding staircase of the Mohovaya building witnesses departures, confessions, and flights: "I dashed down the staircase. I stood beneath the porch" (011-059). The staircase is a threshold between the interior world of the family and the exterior world of revolution — a liminal space where characters make decisions that cannot be unmade.

## Key Passages

> I still remember very vividly the morning following on my arrival in Petrograd, when I had to meet the Admiral for the first time at the British Embassy. I ascended the broad staircase with its worn red carpet to the Chancery. Very perfect young men, very perfectly dressed, were conversing in very perfect intonations about love among monkeys. (014-002)

The narrator's first impression of diplomatic Petrograd — "very perfect young men" discussing absurdities — sets the tone for the novel's treatment of official life. The "worn red carpet" signals an institution already in decline, its grandeur fraying.

> I hailed a driver who slumbered in his sleigh on the corner of the Mohovaya and the Pantilemenskaya. As I drove home across the frozen river, on which the moon spread its yellow light, I thought of the Bursanovs' muddled life, and then Chekhov's Three Sisters dawned upon my memory. (007-056)

The frozen Neva under moonlight is the novel's most lyrical image of Petrograd — a moment of stillness and beauty that frames the narrator's dawning recognition that he is living inside a Chekhov play.

> From Zina's house I remember walking to the Bursanovs in the Mohovaya. I passed the sombre silhouettes of the snow-covered barges frozen on the Neva. It was dark now and the crowds in the streets were more tumultuous. Soldiers and civilians alike walked aimlessly, rifle slung over the shoulder. (015-002)

This passage marks the novel's transition from domestic comedy to revolutionary backdrop. The frozen barges — immobile, dark, monumental — mirror the paralysis of the old regime. The aimless soldiers and civilians prefigure the collapse to come.

> When at last the three sisters returned from the landing, such depressing silence descended upon the room that I thought I would do well to follow the example of the two Pavel Pavlovichi and go home. There was no one to see me out this time. As I reached the lower steps of the broad winding staircase I heard the faint sound of a door opening. (009-023)

The staircase — the novel's recurring threshold — here becomes the site of an unspoken departure. The narrator leaves without being seen, and the "faint sound of a door opening" suggests something happening just beyond his perception. In a novel about partial knowledge, the staircase is where the narrator repeatedly reaches the edge of understanding without quite crossing it.

## The Theatre and Chekhov

The performance of Chekhov's *Three Sisters* at a Petrograd theatre (003-022) is the novel's most explicit intertextual moment. The narrator watches Nina from behind during the performance, the play's themes of provincial longing and deferred hope mirroring the Bursanovs' own situation. The theatre is another Petrograd interior — another enclosed space where art and life reflect each other, and where the narrator's love for Nina finds its aesthetic equivalent in Chekhov's doomed characters.

## After the Revolution

Petrograd is abandoned in the novel's later sections as the characters are displaced to Siberia, Vladivostok, and ultimately exile. The city remains as a memory — the place where everything began, where the family was intact, where love was still possible. In the novel's geography of loss, Petrograd is the Eden from which all subsequent locations are a fall.
