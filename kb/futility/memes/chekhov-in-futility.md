# Chekhov in Futility

## Overview

Anton Chekhov's presence in _Futility_ is both explicit—through direct reference to _Three Sisters_—and implicit, in the novel's tonal blend of comedy and pathos, its focus on provincial stasis, and its portrayal of characters who dream of futures that never arrive.

## Three Sisters Connection

The novel's structure echoes Chekhov's _Three Sisters_: both works center on three sisters waiting for deliverance that never comes. The family's excursion to see Chekhov's play at the local theatre (003-015) becomes a pivotal scene, as Uncle Kostia reflects: "Chekhov is a great artist" (003-027). Gerhardie transposes Chekhov's provincial Russian setting to the Far Eastern exile community, preserving the essential dynamic of longing and futility while adding a layer of historical catastrophe. The narrator explicitly likens the characters to Chekhovian figures: "You know the manner of Chekhov's writing. You know the people in his plays. It seems as though they had all been born on the line of demarcation between comedy and tragedy" (003-022).

## Tonal Inheritance

Gerhardie inherits from Chekhov the ability to treat tragic material with comic lightness. The Chekhovian tone—sympathetic but unsentimental, amused but not cruel—permeates _Futility_, making it a recognizably post-Chekhovian work.
