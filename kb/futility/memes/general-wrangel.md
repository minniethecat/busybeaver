# General Wrangel

## Overview

General Pyotr Nikolayevich Wrangel (1878–1928), also known as the "Black Baron," was one of the principal commanders of the White Army during the final phase of the Russian Civil War. In William Gerhardie's _Futility_, he appears not as a character but as a name in a news dispatch that marks the definitive collapse of the anti-Bolshevik cause. His symbolic function — the last White general to fall — echoes the novel's treatment of historical forces as distant yet decisive, felt primarily through their secondary effects on personal lives.

## In the Novel

While travelling aboard ship with [[sir-hugo|Sir Hugo]], the narrator shows the General a Reuter message reporting that "the French Government had ordered the disbandment of General Wrangel's Army, offering to transport the men to Brazil" (033-002). The General, who is himself a professional soldier of the old Russian army, receives this news with characteristic melancholy: his response is not outrage or despair but a kind of emotional acceptance, a recognition that the world he served has definitively ended.

The news item signals the end of organized White resistance from the Crimea — Wrangel's last stronghold — and underscores the novel's pervasive sense of futility. The collapse of Wrangel's army parallels the broader dissolution of the old Russian world that the characters have been fleeing since the Revolution. The detail that the men are to be transported "to Brazil" adds a note of geographical absurdity: White Russian soldiers, who fought to preserve the Tsarist order in their homeland, are now to become farmers in the tropics.

### Structure of the Scene

The scene is characteristic of Gerhardie's technique: a world-historical event arrives not as a dramatic crisis but as a piece of news read aloud on a ship, its impact registered indirectly through the reactions of men who are themselves already displaced. The General — himself a figure of the old order — receives the news of Wrangel's fall with the same emotional complexion he brings to everything: "the General's eyes were moist with tears: he was happy in his melancholy" (022-011).

## Historical Context

Wrangel assumed command of the White forces in South Russia in April 1920 after the resignation of General Denikin. A career cavalry officer who had served with distinction in the Russo-Japanese War and World War I, Wrangel brought organizational skill and personal charisma to a cause that was already failing. He held out in the Crimea until November 1920, when a Red Army offensive under Mikhail Frunze forced the evacuation of approximately 150,000 soldiers and civilians to Constantinople. The French decision to disband his forces, mentioned in the novel, occurred shortly thereafter, in early 1921 — contemporary with the novel's final chapters.

Unlike [[kolchaks-army|Kolchak's forces]] in Siberia, which the novel's characters encounter directly during their eastward journey, Wrangel's army exists in the novel only as a distant marker of finality. The geographical separation — the Crimea is thousands of miles from Vladivostok — mirrors the temporal separation between the characters' world and the world that is collapsing.

## Thematic Significance

### The Last White General

Wrangel's fall represents the terminal point of the White movement. By the time his army is disbanded, [[kolchaks-army|Admiral Kolchak]] has already been executed, Denikin has resigned, and Yudenich's northwestern army has dissolved. Wrangel is the last — and the news of his dissolution in the novel's final Part serves as a structural marker that the old Russia is not merely transformed but extinguished.

### Futility as Historical Process

The treatment of Wrangel in the novel exemplifies Gerhardie's approach to history: major events are registered not through direct portrayal but through their reverberations in private life. The narrator does not witness the fall of the Crimea; he reads about it in a dispatch, while aboard a ship en route to yet another displacement. History in _Futility_ is something that happens elsewhere, to other people, communicated through telegrams and rumors, and processed through the filter of personal preoccupation.
