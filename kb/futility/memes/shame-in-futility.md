# Shame in Futility

## Overview

Shame functions as a powerful social regulator in _Futility_, shaping character behaviour and revealing the gap between public appearance and private feeling. Gerhardie portrays shame not as a moral compass but as an instrument of social control that generates its own futility — it disciplines behaviour without improving character, and its primary effect is to drive the shamed into deeper concealment.

## Family Shame

Fanny Ivanovna's confession establishes shame as the central emotional currency of the Bursanov household. Her family "were ashamed of me" (006-022) — not for moral failure but for her profession as a music-hall performer, despite the fact that she alone supported them all. The paradox is devastating: "I and I alone kept the whole of our family — my sisters, my brothers, my aged mother, my grandfather, my grandmother and two of my aunts — they were ashamed of me" (006-022). Shame attaches not to wrongdoing but to the loss of status, making it a mechanism of class enforcement rather than ethical guidance.

The narrator himself experiences the acute physical symptoms of shame: "I had to realize, to my deep shame and anguish, that they were all at home, as they entered the room one by one. My face grew crimson" (011-029). The bodily reaction — the crimson face — registers shame as something prior to and more powerful than reflection. It is an autonomic response to exposure, not a considered moral judgment.

## Shame and Identity

Shame in the novel is intimately connected to identity and self-perception. Characters experience shame not for what they do but for what they are — or what circumstances have made them. Fanny Ivanovna's shame about her unmarried status coexists with her genuine pride in the children she raised. This simultaneity of pride and shame, of self-assertion and self-abasement, captures the novel's refusal to reduce its characters to single emotional states.

## Shame as Spectacle

The novel repeatedly stages scenes of public humiliation. At the dance, Nikolai Vasilievich's family "stood by the wall, in everybody's way, shamefaced and pitiful; and Zina looked as though she regretted her insistence on coming to this dance" (021-011). Shame here is not internal but theatrical — it is performed for an audience, and the performance itself becomes another layer of humiliation.

## Social Function of Shame

Gerhardie portrays shame as a mechanism of social discipline that ultimately fails. Characters who feel shame are not reformed by it; they simply learn to hide better. The emotion that should guide moral behaviour instead drives further deception and withdrawal. The Admiral, refusing to feel shame at his own inconsistency, simply redefines his capitulation as benevolence — "humouring" the Russians "as one is inclined to humour quaint, unreasonable children" (027-033). Those who are capable of shame suffer; those who are not continue untroubled. This asymmetry is the final futility of shame as a social regulator.
