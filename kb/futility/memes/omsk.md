# Omsk

Omsk is the central setting of the novel's Siberian sections — the city where [[nikolai-vasilievich|the Bursanov family]] gathers, where British officials conduct their ambiguous business, and where the [[russian-civil-war|Russian Civil War]] unfolds in the background of personal drama. As the capital of Admiral [[kolchak-coup|Kolchak's]] [[white-army|White government]], Omsk in William Gerhardie's *[[futility-theme|Futility]]* is both a real historical place and a symbolic landscape — a city at the edge of a collapsing empire, where the residues of old Russia mingle with the representatives of [[allied-intervention|Allied intervention]].

## Overview

The train arrives in Omsk and the political world has already shifted. The narrator's arrival coincides with Kolchak's consolidation of power, and the city that greets him is defined by the tension between its domestic interiors — warm, lit, filled with music and quarrel — and the Siberian winter outside, where "a civil war fumbles in the snow" (026-001). Gerhardie's Omsk is never one thing: it is a capital and a backwater, a family home and a military headquarters, a place of reunion and a prelude to departure.

## The Kolchak Capital

### Arrival and Regime Change

Historically, Omsk served as the seat of Admiral Kolchak's anti-Bolshevik government from 1918 to 1919. Gerhardie captures this period with characteristic indirection — politics is always filtered through personal perception. The narrator's arrival is narrated with deadpan irony:

> When the train arrived at Omsk, the new regime of Kolchak had been established. The Admiral was distinctly pleased with the change; for he no longer believed in granting the Russian people a Constituent Assembly because he had grounds for thinking that the Russian people, if given this opportunity, would take advantage of it and elect a government other than that of Kolchak. (023-001)

The logic is perfectly circular: democracy is denied to the Russian people because they might use it to choose the wrong government. [[admiral-butt|Admiral Butt]] is "rather fond of little Kolchak, whose interpretation of democracy was that of denying the people the chance to vote" (023-001). The British mission in Omsk is revealed as a commitment to anti-democratic force — and the novel refuses to be shocked by this.

### The Fragile Capital

Omsk's status as a capital is precarious. Power changes hands repeatedly, governments rise and fall, and [[general-bologoevski|General Bologoevski]] wakes one morning to discover "he had lost yet another government overnight" (029-002). The flags change — red to Japanese Rising Sun, back to Russian — and the city absorbs each transformation with the same weary indifference.

The fragility of Kolchak's regime is captured in [[eisenstein|Eisenstein's]] retrospective judgment:

> "Kolchak was impulsive and well-meaning," said Eisenstein, "but unfortunate in his selection of a task. He dismissed General Ditrich, who wanted to give up Omsk to save the Army, and replaced him by General Saharov, who undertook to keep Omsk; whereon General Saharov lost both Omsk and the Army." (029-016)

Omsk is the prize that cannot be kept. Kolchak's strategic error — holding the city when he should have retreated — destroys both the city and the army. The capital becomes a trap.

### The Coup d'Etat as Spectacle

Political violence in Omsk is rendered as a kind of civic theater. The narrator describes the Social Revolutionary coup with the detachment of a spectator:

> It was the day of the Social Revolutionary coup d'etat. Early in the morning troops of revolutionary partisans had occupied the city peacefully and taken possession of the public buildings, to wild cheering from the local crowds. The Russian national flag had been hauled down and a red one hoisted in its stead. Processions had appeared with revolutionary banners, and the town was decorated in red. (027-003)

The coup is "peaceful," the crowds are "wildly cheering," and the narrator's attention is elsewhere — on [[nina|Nina]], who "turned round and stopped, smiling. And the bright white winter day seemed to be smiling with her" (027-003). Revolution and romance coexist in the same sentence, neither diminishing the other.

But the violence is real. During the Gaida rising, the city becomes a battlefield:

> There was a continuous rattle of machine-gun fire. The station building and the square before it were being attacked by Gaida's men and defended by British-trained cadets from Russian Island School. A fearless cadet in British khaki lay on the bridge that traversed the rails, fully exposed to view, and rattled off his machine-gun; then he lay still. Several bodies were already lying on the square, some dead, others wriggling with pain. (025-024)

The "fearless cadet in British khaki" fires his machine-gun and dies. The British-trained cadets defend the station; the "British khaki" on a Russian body is a detail that captures the entanglement of the Allied intervention in the civil war. The bodies on the square — "some dead, others wriggling with pain" — are recorded without commentary.

## The Family Gathering Place

### Convergence in Exile

Omsk functions in the novel as the gravitational center toward which the dispersed family converges. Characters who began in [[petrograd|Petrograd]] reappear in Omsk — the Siberian city becoming an improbable reunion point for the entire extended household. The Bursanovs have fled east, driven by the [[bolshevism|Bolshevik]] seizure of their [[petrograd|Petrograd]] house, and Omsk is where they land.

The convergence is practical as well as thematic. [[eisenstein|Eisenstein]] the dentist follows [[admiral-butt|the Admiral]] from [[vladivostok|Vladivostok]] to Omsk "in order to evade military service at the Base" (024-006). [[baron-wunderhausen|Baron Wunderhausen]], "now a second lieutenant in Kolchak's Army," also arrives in Omsk "and asked the Admiral to take him on as his interpreter" (024-006). The Admiral's party accumulates followers the way a ship accumulates barnacles — Omsk is where they all attach themselves.

### The Admiral's House

The domestic center of Omsk in the novel is the Admiral's house — a space of warmth and comfort against the Siberian night. When [[admiral-butt|the Admiral]] is away, the narrator commandeers it for a dinner party:

> The house was lit and warm and comfortable. It was the Admiral's house. But the Admiral was away, and in his absence I had conceived it possible to give a dinner-party. The arrangement of the guests at table had been a delicate but delicious business. I had placed Fanny Ivanovna at the side of Magda Nikolaevna. I had seated Nikolai Vasilievich side by side with Eisenstein. I had sprinkled some of Zina's sisters amongst the three sisters. (026-002)

The seating arrangement is a miniature of the novel's social world — a comedy of placement and misplacement. The narrator's pleasure in the "delicate but delicious business" of assigning seats is both absurd and entirely human. In Omsk, as everywhere else, people are preoccupied with the small dramas of social positioning.

### The Siberian Winter Night

But the domestic warmth of Omsk is always framed by the Siberian winter outside. Gerhardie gives the landscape a rhetorical grandeur that contrasts sharply with the novel's usual ironic tone:

> Who can convey at all adequately that sense of utter hopelessness that clings to a Siberian winter night? Wherever else is there to be found that brooding, thrilling sense of frozen space, of snow and ice lost in inky darkness, that gruesome sense of never-ending night, and black despair and loneliness untold, immeasurable? Add to this the knowledge of a civil war fumbling in the snow, of people ill-fed, ill-clothed and apathetic, lying on the frozen ground, cold and wretched and diseased. (026-001)

The rhetoric is almost Dostoevskian — "black despair and loneliness untold, immeasurable" — but Gerhardie pulls back from earnestness through juxtaposition. The very next sentence returns us to the house: "The house was lit and warm and comfortable" (026-002). The Siberian sublime is acknowledged and then set aside in favor of dinner-party intrigue.

## Domestic Omsk

### The Dinner Party

The dinner party at the Admiral's house is one of the novel's great set-pieces, and Omsk provides its stage. Wine flows from [[admiral-butt|the Admiral's]] private cellar; guests become "more impulsive and less amenable to the control of the will" (026-003); the evening builds toward chaos:

> I sat still among my guests, strangely flushed, and the vast sea of Russian life seemed to be closing over me. (026-005)

The party culminates in a raid on the narrator's private stash of paskha (Russian Easter cheese), stored in the safe:

> I had a pyramid of considerable dimensions locked up in the safe. ... And now, at the close of dinner, the secret was betrayed. A dash was made for it. The guests armed themselves with knives, forks, and spoons. (026-030)

The paskha is a small absurdity — a food fight in the middle of a civil war — but it is precisely these absurdities that define the novel's sense of what matters. While governments fall outside, inside the house people are fighting over Easter cheese.

### Music and the Siberian Night

The evening's climax is musical. From the floor above, a Russian family begins to play Chopin:

> We listened to the music and grew still, and our souls were all music as though he had touched their strings. And the house seemed charmed, and the gruff Siberian night looked in through the window and listened in silence. (026-034)

The "gruff Siberian night" becomes a listener — the winter outside transformed from threat to audience. The passage captures something essential about Omsk in the novel: it is a place where art and domesticity create a fragile enchantment against the darkness. The music does not cancel the civil war but makes it, for a moment, irrelevant.

### The Social Texture

The social life of Omsk in the novel is rendered through a series of interiors. [[sir-hugo|Sir Hugo]] sits in "the little office" writing to a Czech Colonel "to apologize for misspelling the Colonel's name in a recent letter" (025-023). Dinners drag on interminably, filled with political argument and personal confession. [[general-bologoevski|General Bologoevski]] declares himself "at heart a democrat" while putting his hands "upon his heart, big and full of democratic feeling" (028-006). [[sir-hugo|Sir Hugo]] and [[captain-larkin|Captain Larkin]] spar over military discipline, ending in Sir Hugo's convulsive laughter: "Splendid fellow, Captain Larkin! Good. Very good. Splendid!" (028-009).

The evenings dissolve into chaos:

> As we passed into the anteroom, the company was getting rowdy. A French Colonel, cigar in mouth, was throwing gramophone records on the floor, as though they were quoits, adding, with a blissful sidelong smile at me, "Les disques!" Somebody had released the gramophone, and a rowdy one-step was the result. Cocktails, wine, liqueurs, whisky ... (028-023)

The French Colonel throwing gramophone records as quoits is a perfect image of Allied intervention in microcosm: the representatives of foreign powers, in the capital of a collapsing government, amusing themselves by destroying the furniture of civilization.

### Spring in Omsk

The novel does not linger in Omsk forever. As the narrative moves toward its conclusion, spring arrives:

> Six weeks elapsed, and the snow was melting in the valley. When the sun appeared behind the trees the birches, steeped in water, had that silvery appearance which is beautiful beyond measure. Spring was in the air. (028-001)

The beauty of the Siberian spring is "beyond measure" — one of the few moments in the novel where Gerhardie allows himself an un-ironized aesthetic response. But spring also means departure. The narrator's farewell visit to the family coincides with [[nikolai-vasilievich|Nikolai Vasilievich's]] release from prison and with Easter: "The church bells on that Easter Sunday, the most festal day in the year, rang dolefully through the Christian city stricken by the war" (029-001). Omsk is "the Christian city stricken by the war" — a phrase that gathers the city's identity into a single image of wounded faith.

### Prison and Its Uses

Omsk is also where [[nikolai-vasilievich|Nikolai Vasilievich]] is imprisoned — one of the novel's characteristic reversals, in which a victim of events is treated as their perpetrator. The imprisonment "had yet served the good purpose of further cementing his multitudinous friendships" (028-005). Even prison, in Gerhardie's world, is an opportunity for networking.
