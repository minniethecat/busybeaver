## Overview

Eberheim is one of the more shadowy figures in William Gerhardie's *Futility* — an "elderly gentleman of German" origin [015-008] who enters the family circle through a marriage of financial convenience. He is defined primarily by his illness and its symptoms: his "condition was so bad" [015-014], and his groans punctuate social gatherings with reminders of mortality. Eberheim is a minor character who registers disproportionately — his suffering is real, but the novel treats it with the same ironic detachment it applies to all human experience.

## Marriage for Money

Eberheim's entry into the family is explained with brutal economy: "so they married her to Eberheim, an elderly gentleman of German" origin [015-008]. The passive construction — "they married her" — denies agency to both bride and groom. Eberheim, whatever his own desires, is a piece in the family's financial arrangements. His nationality and age are specified; his character is not. He is a function before he is a person.

## The Groan

Eberheim's most distinctive feature is his groan. "We waited for Eberheim to groan again; but he too was" silent [015-028] — the groan anticipated, almost expected, as if it were his contribution to the conversation. The groan is both symptom and communication, a sound that conveys suffering without specifying its nature. In a novel where characters talk endlessly and say little, Eberheim's inarticulate groan may be the most honest utterance of all.

## Condition and Bribery

Eberheim's illness makes him vulnerable to manipulation. "Eberheim's condition was so bad" [015-014] that another character considers leading him aside to "bribe him" [015-014]. The juxtaposition of serious illness with casual bribery is characteristic of Gerhardie's moral universe — suffering does not exempt anyone from the novel's economy of manipulation.

## Place in the Circle

Eberheim appears in the novel's social roll-calls: "Baron Wunderhausen, Kniaz, Eberheim and the bookkeeper Stanitski" [019-060]. His inclusion alongside these other figures confirms his membership in the extended family circle, even as his illness sets him apart. He is present but not participatory, part of the group but already half-absent, his groans a memento mori at gatherings devoted to the denial of consequences.
