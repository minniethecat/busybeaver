# Andrei Andreiech

The narrator of *Futility* is a young Anglo-Russian, Oxford-educated, employed at the British Embassy in Petrograd, and hopelessly in love with [[nina|Nina Bursanov]]. His voice — ironic, self-deprecating, prone to digression — is the novel's governing consciousness, and his gradual entanglement with the Bursanov family provides the narrative's structure. He is both participant and observer, a man whose education has equipped him brilliantly for commentary and rather poorly for action.

## Character Overview

### The Observer's Stance

Andrei Andreiech is both participant and observer, a man who cannot decide whether to act or to watch. He wanders through "the abandoned park that used to be a park essentially for lovers" (002-011), thinking about love in the abstract while his own romantic life remains unconsummated. At the theatre, watching Chekhov's [[three-sisters|*Three Sisters*]], he sits behind Nina, observing her watching the play — a triple remove that captures his characteristic distance from his own experience:

> I sat behind Nina, and watched with that stupid scepticism that comes from too much happiness. To me, buoyant and impatient, the people in the play appeared ... (003-022)

The ellipsis is Gerhardie's — and it is telling. The narrator's concentration breaks mid-observation, as if even his analysis of his own scepticism cannot sustain itself. This self-interruption is the formal signature of Andrei's narrative voice.

His Anglo-Russian identity — British by education and employment, Russian by birth and cultural affiliation — places him between worlds, a position he shares with Gerhardie himself. This dual perspective enables the novel's distinctive tone: English irony applied to Russian material, producing something that is neither fully one nor the other. He moves through [[petrograd|Petrograd]] as both native and foreigner, belonging to both cultures and fully to neither.

### The Narrator as Character

Andrei Andreiech is, uniquely among the novel's characters, the medium through which all others are perceived. Yet he is not a transparent medium — his perceptions are colored by his own preoccupations, his own romantic frustrations, and his own ironic temperament. When he describes [[fanny-ivanovna|Fanny Ivanovna]], he is describing a performance for which he is the designated audience: "Sit down, Andrei Andreiech," she said. "I am always glad when you come" (006-002). He is cast in the role of listener, and he plays it well — it is, after all, the role he has chosen for himself in life.

### Intellectual Posture

His intellectual name-dropping — from Turgenev to H. G. Wells (036-005) — reveals a man who navigates the world through cultural references, who would rather quote than act, and whose education has equipped him for commentary rather than participation. He is, in this sense, the archetype of the modern narrator: sophisticated, paralyzed, endlessly observant. The [[oxford|Oxford]] education that sets him apart from the Russian characters is also what keeps him at a remove from direct experience — he has been trained to analyze, and analysis is a form of distance.

## Relationships

### With Nina

The object of his romantic obsession. He watches her, thinks about her, and rarely acts. Their relationship is defined by what does not happen — a non-consummation that is the novel's central romantic fact. He sits behind her at the theatre, watching her face rather than the stage; he paces an abandoned lovers' park, thinking of her absence rather than her presence. [[nina|Nina]] is in this sense the perfect object for his kind of love: distant, idealized, and in love with someone else.

### With Nikolai Vasilievich

The patriarch [[nikolai-vasilievich|Nikolai Vasilievich]] whom Andrei observes with a mixture of bemusement and reluctant sympathy. He is drawn into the family's orbit without ever fully belonging to it. When Nikolai applies for a train compartment to [[omsk|Omsk]], Andrei is drawn into the absurd negotiations — a "svoy chelovek" (one of the family, 004-017) who remains, nevertheless, an outsider with a diplomatic passport.

### With Fanny Ivanovna

[[fanny-ivanovna|Fanny Ivanovna]] adopts him as her primary audience. She confides in him at length (006), treating him as the ideal listener for her life story — "Ach! Andrei Andreiech! Andrei Andreiech! If you knew what I have suffered!" (006-006). He listens — it is what he does best. Their relationship is one of the novel's most sustained comic duets: the voluble, self-dramatizing woman and the silent, self-effacing young man, each giving the other exactly what they need (audience and material, respectively).

### With the Admiral

His superior at the Embassy and companion on the Siberian adventure. The "comic opera attempts to wipe out the Russian revolution" (019-002) are narrated with the detachment of a man who knows the enterprise is absurd but cannot quite bring himself to dismiss it:

> By now, "Intervention" has been relegated to the shelf of history. But I cannot but remember it, not merely as an adventure in futility, as admittedly it was, but as an ever-shifting, changing sense of being alive. (019-002)

This is the double vision that defines Andrei: he can name the futility and still value the experience.

### With Uncle Kostia

[[uncle-kostia|Uncle Kostia]] treats Andrei as his philosophical interlocutor — the baffled student who forces the master to clarify (and thereby to expose) his own position. "Can't you understand that it is the very fact of this physical futility that inflates me with a sense of spiritual importance?" (022-061). Andrei's "stupidity" — Uncle Kostia's word — is in fact the narrator's structural function: he asks the naive question so that the other characters can reveal themselves in answering it.

## Key Passages

> I paced the country, now in the embrace of autumn. I wandered in remote places by the sea, in the abandoned park that used to be a park essentially for lovers, and thought of them. Here the foliage was more dense, the corners more secluded, the disorder more magnificent. (002-011)

This passage, early in the novel, establishes Andrei's characteristic mode: lyrical, slightly self-mocking, turning his own solitude into a landscape. The abandoned park is a metaphor for his own romantic situation — once meant for love, now overgrown and beautiful in its decay. "The vast sea of Russian life seemed to be closing over me" (002-011) — the image is of submersion, of a consciousness overwhelmed by a context too large to be shaped.

> The Admiral and I, and a few others — interesting types, I can assure you — travelled to Siberia, where we engaged in a series of comic opera attempts to wipe out the Russian revolution. (019-002)

The parenthetical "interesting types, I can assure you" is pure Andrei — the narrator addressing his reader directly, acknowledging the artificiality of his own narrative, and winking at the absurdity of the enterprise he is describing. The phrase "comic opera" is a verdict, and the verdict is delivered with the lightness of a man who has earned the right to laugh at his own history.

> I sat behind Nina, and watched with that stupid scepticism that comes from too much happiness. (003-022)

"Stupid scepticism" — the self-diagnosis is lethal. He knows that his detachment is a function of his privilege, that his ironic stance is purchased by his distance from the suffering he observes. Yet the self-awareness does not lead to change; it merely becomes another layer of detachment.

> By now, "Intervention" has been relegated to the shelf of history. But I cannot but remember it, not merely as an adventure in futility, as admittedly it was, but as an ever-shifting, changing sense of being alive. (019-002)

The retrospective voice — "by now" — places the narrated events in the past, and the narrator in a present from which he looks back. The concession ("as admittedly it was") and the counter-claim ("but as an ever-shifting, changing sense of being alive") enact the novel's central dialectic: futility acknowledged, experience valued.

> For the experience of love is inseparable from its background. Alone it does not exist. It is a modulation of impressions, an interplay of "atmospheres," a quickening of the fibres of that background. (019-002)

This is Andrei's aesthetic credo — and Gerhardie's. Love, for the narrator, is never merely an emotion; it is an atmosphere, a texture, a quality of light. The scare quotes around "atmospheres" signal his characteristic self-consciousness — he knows he is being literary, and he will not let you forget it.

> Much of my experiences must now appear in the nature of a farce. This is not my fault. A good deal of life is a hilarious farce. (022-001)

The disclaimer — "This is not my fault" — is both disingenuous and true. The narrator did not invent the farcical quality of the Bursanovs' lives; he merely records it. But the recording transforms it, gives it shape, makes it available for laughter. The narrator's "fault" is not in the material but in the artistry with which he renders it.

## Thematic Significance

### The Consciousness of Futility

Andrei Andreiech is the novel's consciousness of [[futility-theme|futility]]. He recognizes — before any other character — that the Intervention is "an adventure in futility" (019-002), yet he also insists on its experiential value. This double vision — futility acknowledged but not surrendered to — is the novel's deepest insight, and Andrei is its vehicle.

### Anglo-Russian Identity

His split identity embodies the novel's larger theme of cultural displacement. He belongs to the world of the British Embassy and the world of the Mohovaya flat, and the distance between them is the space in which the novel's irony operates. He translates Russian experience into English prose, and something is inevitably lost — and gained — in the translation.

### The Narrator's Complicity

Andrei is not innocent. His detachment is a form of complicity in the family's chaos: by refusing to intervene, by choosing observation over action, he becomes part of the pattern he describes. His love for Nina is real but passive; his sympathy for Nikolai is real but ineffectual. The narrator who exposes futility is himself futile — and knows it.

### Love and Aesthetics

For Andrei, love is primarily an aesthetic experience. He loves Nina the way he loves a landscape or a Chekhov play — as a source of impressions, atmospheres, and "a quickening of the fibres" (019-002). The question the novel leaves open is whether this aestheticized love counts as love at all, or whether it is merely a sophisticated form of avoidance.

## Appearances

As first-person narrator, Andrei Andreiech is present throughout the novel (Parts I–IV). His narrative voice is the medium through which all events are filtered. Major episodes told from his perspective include: the Mohovaya evenings (003–005), Fanny Ivanovna's autobiographical monologue (006), the [[siberia|Siberian]] adventure with the Admiral (019), the train journey to [[vladivostok|Vladivostok]] (022–037), and the novel's elegiac conclusion.
