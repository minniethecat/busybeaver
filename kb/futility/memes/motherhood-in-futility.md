# Motherhood in Futility

Motherhood in *Futility* is at once sacred and instrumentalized. Fanny Ivanovna's maternal identity is the one source of moral authority she can claim unequivocally, yet it is also the ground on which Nikolai Vasilievich and the legal system can deny her autonomy. The novel presents motherhood as a relationship of profound power and profound vulnerability, often simultaneously.

## Overview

Fanny Ivanovna's most unassailable moral position is also her most vulnerable legal one. Her assertion "A mother is a mother to her children, always, whatever she has been" (006-030) is both a declaration of unconditional love and a plea for recognition — she has no legal standing, only maternal devotion.

The children in the novel — Nina, Vera, Sonia — are largely absent as characters but constantly present as objects of concern and negotiation. They are "such a pride" (006-040) to Fanny Ivanovna, but they are also hostages in the adult conflicts she cannot control. Nikolai Vasilievich, for all his failings, is still their father, and the threat of losing them hangs over all Fanny Ivanovna's calculations.

## In the Novel

The novel's most concentrated treatment of motherhood comes in Fanny Ivanovna's long monologue to Andrei Andreiech. She recounts Nikolai Vasilievich's threat: "if there was a legal case he would of course plead guilty and so lose the children; and Nikolai Vasilievich was again the one to blame" (006-025). The logic is perverse: Nikolai's infidelity would, if legally exposed, result not in his punishment but in *her* losing the children. The law protects the institution of marriage, not the reality of motherhood.

The children serve as a temporal marker throughout the narrative. Fanny Ivanovna repeatedly defers her own happiness with the refrain "When the children grow up we will get a divorce" (006-035), "When the children grow up we will, Fanny. I will be restored to you" (006-039). But the children's growing up is perpetually deferred — like the goldmines, like the political settlement, like everything else in the novel. Motherhood becomes another suspended promise.

The novel treats Fanny Ivanovna's maternal devotion with genuine respect while also suggesting its costs. Her "doleful craving for the decencies of usage and convention" (026-024) is partly a mother's desire to give her children a respectable life, and the impossibility of fulfilling that desire is one of the novel's quiet tragedies.

## Key Passages

> "'A mother is a mother to her children, always, whatever she has been.'" (006-030)

> "'We had the children — and what sweet girls they were!'" (006-027)

> "'When the children grow up we will get a divorce,' she echoed him. 'Yes, Fanny. When the children grow up I will be restored to you.'" (006-035)

> "'When the children grow up we will, Fanny. I will marry you. I am your real wife. Let the children grow up first. We must think of the children first.'" (006-036)

## Broader Context

Gerhardie's treatment of motherhood participates in a broader early twentieth-century reexamination of family structures. The Russian Revolution had promised to liberate women from traditional domestic roles, but the reality — as the novel documents — was far more complicated. Fanny Ivanovna is neither a traditional matriarch nor a liberated new woman; she is stranded between worlds, claiming maternal authority in a society that has stripped motherhood of its legal protections.

The repeated invocation of "the children" as a reason to defer action also echoes Chekhov's treatment of deferred hopes. In Chekhov, characters constantly postpone happiness to some future date; in Gerhardie, they postpone it until the children grow up — a date that, like Chekhov's Moscow, never quite arrives.
