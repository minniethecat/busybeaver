# Futility (Theme)

The sense that human effort is ultimately pointless — that actions lead nowhere, that passion dissolves into absurdity. In William Gerhardie's 1922 novel *Futility*, this theme is not merely stated but embodied in every character, every plot thread, and every narrative digression, creating a work that is at once a comedy of inaction and a serious meditation on the human condition.

## Overview

"Futility" is Gerhardie's central theme and the novel's title — a word that appears explicitly at key moments and implicitly saturates the entire narrative. The novel treats futility not as despair but as a kind of structural principle: characters fall in love with people who cannot love them back, embark on political missions that achieve nothing, and write books that go nowhere. The narrator, [[andrei-andreiech]], moves through the [[russian-revolution|Russian Revolution]], the [[allied-intervention|Allied Intervention]] in [[siberia|Siberia]], and the tangled love affairs of the [[family-in-futility|Bursanov family]], always aware that his actions are, as he puts it, "not merely an adventure in futility" but something more complex (019-002).

Gerhardie draws explicitly on Chekhov's *Three Sisters* as a model for this futility — the Bursanov sisters watch the play and recognize themselves in it, living on what the narrator calls "the line of demarcation between comedy and tragedy" (003-022). The novel's futility is thus a Chekhovian futility: not nihilistic, but painfully, comically human.

### The Title as Structural Principle

The novel's title is not incidental. "Futility" names both the theme and the method. Gerhardie structures his narrative as a series of futile gestures — the Admiral's Intervention, [[nikolai-vasilievich|Nikolai's]] divorce negotiations, the narrator's courtship of [[nina]] — each one advancing nothing, resolving nothing, yet each one generating the comic energy that drives the novel forward. The narrator himself frames the entire book as an act of retrospective ordering, imposing shape on chaos:

> And then it struck me that the only thing to do was to fit all this into a book. It is the classic way of treating life. For my ineffectual return to Vladivostok is the effectual conclusion of my theme. (002-001)

The paradox is deliberate: an "ineffectual return" that becomes "effectual" through art. Writing transforms futility into significance — or at least into comedy.

## In the Novel

Futility manifests in three interlocking registers throughout the novel:

**Political futility**: The Allied Intervention in Siberia — a historical episode that Gerhardie experienced first-hand — is depicted as "a series of comic opera attempts to wipe out the Russian revolution" (019-002). The narrator acknowledges the enterprise as genuinely futile, yet refuses to reduce it to mere waste, insisting that it was also "an ever-shifting, changing sense of being alive."

**Artistic futility**: [[uncle-kostia|Uncle Kostia]], the novel's most self-aware character, arrives at "the conclusion that it was futility to get up at all" (037-005) and declares that his reflections have taught him "the futility of writing" (022-037). Yet even this recognition becomes paradoxical: the very act of declaring writing futile is itself a piece of writing, and Kostia's exasperated cry reveals the novel's deepest irony.

**Romantic futility**: The narrator's love for [[nina]], like the tangled affections among the Bursanov sisters, the Admiral, and their various suitors, is a love that cannot be consummated — and perhaps gains its intensity from that very impossibility. Sir Hugo's question — "Is it not rather an adventure in futility?" (033-007) — could stand as a summary of every romantic arc in the book.

### The Chekhovian Model

The novel's treatment of futility is explicitly modelled on Anton Chekhov. The pivotal theatre scene in Part I establishes the framework:

> You know the manner of Chekhov's writing. You know the people in his plays. It seems as though they had all been born on the line of demarcation between comedy and tragedy — in a kind of No Man's Land. Fanny Ivanovna and the three sisters watched the play with intense interest, as if the Three Sisters were indeed their own particular tragedy. (003-022)

The "No Man's Land" between comedy and tragedy is precisely the territory Gerhardie claims for his own novel. His characters inhabit this space without resolving it. The threat to "write up such a Three Sisters as will knock old Chekhov into a cocked hat" (011-053) is a metafictional joke — Gerhardie is already doing exactly that, transforming Chekhovian stasis into something faster, more absurd, and distinctively Anglo-Russian.

### Hope Within Futility

Crucially, Gerhardie's futility is not despair. The novel repeatedly undercuts its own nihilistic gestures with affirmations of life, love, and the sheer pleasure of observation. Sir Hugo's dismissive phrase — "an adventure in futility" — is pointedly answered by the narrator:

> But I cannot but remember it, not merely as an adventure in futility, as admittedly it was, but as an ever-shifting, changing sense of being alive. For the experience of love is inseparable from its background. Alone it does not exist. (019-002)

This is the novel's deepest insight: futility and vitality are not opposites but companions. The fact that an enterprise is futile does not mean it is worthless. The narrator's Siberian adventure, like his love for Nina, gains its meaning not from its outcome but from the texture of experience itself.

## Key Passages

> The Admiral and I, and a few others — interesting types, I can assure you — travelled to Siberia, where we engaged in a series of comic opera attempts to wipe out the Russian revolution. By now, "Intervention" has been relegated to the shelf of history. But I cannot but remember it, not merely as an adventure in futility, as admittedly it was, but as an ever-shifting, changing sense of being alive. (019-002)

> "I have been thinking of this and that and the other, in fact, of one thing and another — precious but elusive thoughts, Andrei Andreiech. Beautiful emotions. A kaleidoscope of the most subtle colours, if I may so express myself. And, Andrei Andreiech, it has taught me a great truth. It has taught me the futility of writing." (022-037)

> "Oh!" groaned Uncle Kostia at my stupidity. "Can't you understand that it is the very fact of this physical futility that inflates me with a sense of spiritual importance?" (022-061)

The luggage had already been removed to the boat, and the three sisters, dressed for the voyage, had sat down before the final parting. ## Broader Context

Gerhardie's treatment of futility places him at the intersection of several literary traditions. The most obvious debt is to Anton Chekhov, whose *Three Sisters* functions within the novel as both explicit reference point and structural model (003-015). Gerhardie even has a character threaten to write "such a Three Sisters as will knock old Chekhov into a cocked hat" (011-053) — a metafictional joke that acknowledges the borrowing while asserting originality.

Beyond Chekhov, the novel belongs to the tradition of Anglo-Russian modernism that emerged from the peculiar circumstances of the Russian diaspora after 1917. Gerhardie, born in St. Petersburg to British parents, wrote in English about Russian life, creating a voice that [[katherine-mansfield|Katherine Mansfield]] — in the novel's dedication (P01-002) — recognized as genuinely new. The futility he depicts is not the existential despair of Beckett or the nihilism of Celine, but something closer to the comic pessimism of his near-contemporary Evelyn Waugh — a sense that life is absurd, and that absurdity is precisely what makes it worth writing about.

### Critical Reception

The novel's treatment of futility was immediately recognised as distinctive. The preface to the American edition notes that Gerhardie achieves something rare: an "objectivity" that allows him to "focus the two so utterly alien races to which he belongs almost equally by birth and bringing-up — the English and Russian; to sympathize with both" (P02-007). This dual perspective — insider and outsider simultaneously — is itself a source of the novel's comic vision of futility. Neither fully Russian nor fully English, the narrator occupies the same "No Man's Land" as Chekhov's characters: suspended between identities, observing futility from within and without.
