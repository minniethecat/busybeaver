# White Army

The White Army was the loose coalition of anti-Bolshevik military forces that fought the [[red-army|Red Army]] during the [[russian-civil-war|Russian Civil War]] (1917–1922). In William Gerhardie's *[[futility-theme|Futility]]*, the White cause is the political framework within which the novel's [[siberia|Siberian]] sections unfold — the British military mission supports it, [[baron-wunderhausen|Baron Wunderhausen]] serves in it, and Admiral [[kolchak-coup|Kolchak's coup]] attempts to unify it under a single command. But the novel systematically undermines the White cause, treating it less as a military endeavor than as a social event, a bureaucratic labyrinth, and a pretext for the comic absurdities that define Gerhardie's vision.

## Overview

The White movement was never a single organization but a coalition of monarchists, republicans, Cossacks, Social Revolutionaries, and former Tsarist officers united primarily by their opposition to [[bolshevism|Bolshevism]]. *Futility* captures this heterogeneity through minor characters — a dentist who follows [[admiral-butt|the Admiral]] to [[omsk|Omsk]], a general who threatens railway stationmasters with a pistol, a divisional commander with three hundred officers and fourteen men — and through the novel's governing irony: the Whites believe they are fighting for Russia, but Russia is already lost.

## Composition and Character

### A Coalition of Incompatibles

The White Army's internal contradictions are laid bare in a single exchange. [[uncle-kostia|Uncle Kostia]], the novel's resident cynic, identifies exactly who will form the new army:

> "I can guess the elements which will form the backbone of this new White Russian Army — monarchists altogether too brainless to realize that theirs is a lost cause." (021-035)

The diagnosis is brutal and accurate. The monarchists are "too brainless" not because they are stupid but because they refuse to accept that the world they seek to restore no longer exists. The White cause is, from its inception, an exercise in denial — and the novel treats it accordingly.

### Officers Without Soldiers

The most devastating image of the White Army's collapse comes during the Admiral's inspection of a division at the front. The narrator reports the situation with deadpan precision:

> It transpired that four regiments composing the division had gone over to the enemy that very morning. Of the division there remained just fourteen men, the Commander and his divisional headquarters, comprising about three hundred officers. (024-016)

Three hundred officers commanding fourteen men. The ratio is so grotesque it nearly reads as satire, but Gerhardie does not exaggerate — he simply records. When the Admiral asks the Commander what he plans to do, the reply is hollow: "He said that he would wait; he thought the men might return" (024-016). The Admiral, with characteristic bluntness, asks if the Commander is counting on God. The reply is the novel's epitaph for the White Army:

> "Yes, Your Excellency," sighed the Commander, "we have no one else to count upon." (024-018)

The White Army has become an army of officers waiting for soldiers who have already chosen the other side. The only remaining ally is God — and God, the novel implies, is not particularly interested.

### The Social Milieu of White Officers

The novel's White officers are drawn less as soldiers than as social types. [[baron-wunderhausen|Baron Wunderhausen]] appears as "a second lieutenant in Kolchak's Army, who arrived in Omsk and asked the Admiral to take him on as his interpreter" (024-006). The role of "interpreter" for a British admiral who already speaks Russian is a transparent pretext — the Baron, like so many White officers, is simply looking for a position.

[[general-bologoevski|General Bologoevski]], who has "travelled with us all the way from England, seemingly under vague instructions from some Allied War Office" (019-004), cannot even dine in a restaurant while wearing his uniform: "By order of the Commander-in-Chief Russian officers were not admitted into restaurants" (019-004). A White general, in the city his side nominally controls, cannot eat dinner. His protest — "What! Remove my epaulets! I, a Russian officer? Never!" (019-005) — is both dignified and absurd. The epaulets are all he has left.

The [[admiral-butt|Admiral]] treats his White allies with a mixture of condescension and camaraderie. He divides the world into "good fellows" and "rotters" (020-002), and his classification of Russian officers follows this binary. When a Russian general "was talking arrant nonsense," the Admiral simply "nodded with approval" (020-001). Diplomacy, for the Admiral, is less about policy than about personal chemistry.

## Kolchak's Command

### The Admiral's Vision

Admiral Kolchak's assumption of supreme command in November 1918 gives the White movement a nominal leader. The novel's narrator describes the situation with gentle mockery:

> When the train arrived at Omsk, the new regime of Kolchak had been established. The Admiral was distinctly pleased with the change; for he no longer believed in granting the Russian people a Constituent Assembly because he had grounds for thinking that the Russian people, if given this opportunity, would take advantage of it and elect a government other than that of Kolchak. (023-001)

The circularity is exquisite: [[kolchak-coup|Kolchak]] opposes democracy because the people might vote against him. [[admiral-butt|The Admiral]] — the British one, not Kolchak — is "distinctly pleased" with this logic. British support for the White cause is revealed as fundamentally anti-democratic.

### The Fragmented Command

White authority is undermined by its own fragmentation. [[general-bologoevski|General Bologoevski]]'s briefing on "the situation" reveals a landscape of competing authorities:

> I gathered that there was a General Horvat who had formed an All-Russia Government, and that there was also a Siberian Government, defying General Horvat on the one hand and the Bolsheviks on the other, and that there were various officer organizations grouped about this or the other government, and some rather inclined to be on their own, all looking forward to a possible intervention by the Allies. (019-012)

The White movement is not one army but many — competing governments, competing commanders, competing visions. The only thing they share is their dependence on Allied support and their hatred of the [[bolsheviks|Bolsheviks]].

### Kolchak's Failures

The novel's retrospective assessment of Kolchak is delivered by [[eisenstein|Eisenstein]], the Jewish dentist turned political commentator:

> "Kolchak was impulsive and well-meaning," said Eisenstein, "but unfortunate in his selection of a task. He dismissed General Ditrich, who wanted to give up Omsk to save the Army, and replaced him by General Saharov, who undertook to keep Omsk; whereon General Saharov lost both Omsk and the Army." (029-016)

"Impulsive and well-meaning" — the epitaph of a man who meant well but accomplished nothing. Kolchak's strategic errors are reduced to a single sentence: he chose the wrong general, and lost both the city and the army.

### Allied Fantasies

Even the [[admiral-butt|Admiral]] himself is not immune to delusions of grandeur. At night, in his hotel room, he dreams of personal glory:

> I was thinking of my meeting on the morrow with the family, with Nina. ... And, interlacing with these thoughts, there was the thought of the gallant Admiral in the bedroom opposite, tucked away between his heavy blankets, his teeth in a glass of water on the table at his side — no presentable sight! — seeing visions of a Napoleonic ride athwart the great Siberian plain, at the head of his vast new army. (019-073)

The "Napoleonic ride" is a fantasy, and the detail of the false teeth in a glass of water punctures it before it can gather momentum. The Admiral dreams of conquest; the narrator dreams of [[nina|Nina]]. Both dreams are equally unreal.

## The Futility of the White Cause

### Nina's Verdict

The novel's most succinct judgment on the White cause comes not from a soldier or a politician but from [[nina|Nina]], who speaks with the moral clarity that the novel's intellectuals lack:

> "Horrible!" she said. "It's shameful! The Whites kill the Reds, the Reds kill the Whites ... and nobody is any the farther. If people would only realize that killing is the first thing they shouldn't do." (025-030)

Nina's objection is not political but moral. She does not take sides; she refuses the logic of the conflict entirely. "Nobody is any the farther" — the civil war is not just futile but pointlessly destructive. The White cause, like the Red cause, produces only corpses.

### The General's Pistol

The White Army's authority, when it exercises any at all, is reduced to farce. When a train stops at a wayside station and will not move, [[general-bologoevski|General Bologoevski]] takes action:

> The General said he thought the stationmaster was a most "damrotten fellow," in the case of which type he usually relied on "elemental" measures. Accordingly he drew out his pistol and threatened to shoot him. (022-015)

A White general, reduced to threatening a stationmaster with a pistol to get a train moving. This is the effective limit of White military power in *Futility*: the ability to intimidate railway workers.

### The Army That Vanishes

The White Army's disappearance is rendered as a process of administrative dissolution. Government after government falls; [[general-bologoevski|General Bologoevski]] wakes one morning to find "he had lost yet another government overnight" (029-002). The White cause evaporates not in a dramatic battle but in a series of bureaucratic non-events. The headquarters of the Russian Zemstvo is "fiercely bombarded in the night" — but when Japanese troops finally storm the building, "they found, to their amazement, that there was no one there" (029-002). The enemy is never where you aim.

### An Army Without Purpose

The novel's governing theme finds its military expression in the White Army's ultimate failure. Despite British support, despite Kolchak's authority, despite the courage of individual soldiers, the White cause collapses. [[uncle-kostia|Uncle Kostia]], watching the intervention from his window, delivers the novel's verdict:

> "It amuses me to see how you are fussing over us and always in the wrong direction, running about like clowns in a circus." (029-014)

The Allies are clowns; the White Army is a circus. Gerhardie does not moralize this outcome — he simply records it, with the same ironic detachment he brings to all human endeavor. The White Army is not evil; it is, like everything else in the novel, futile.

### The Admiral's Defense

When challenged about the political legitimacy of the White cause, [[admiral-butt|the Admiral]] refuses to engage with specifics:

> "I will not argue about details, Prince Borisov. I am not a biologist and I don't dissect. And I don't propose to be dragged into pedantic microscopic analysis as to which is the particular political party to which the army, for the moment, swears allegiance. I am satisfied that it is a strong Russian Army, which it has been our object to create." (029-037)

The Admiral's refusal to "dissect" is a refusal to think. He is "satisfied" with a "strong Russian Army" regardless of what political party it serves — an admission that British policy has no ideological content beyond the creation of force. The White Army is an instrument without a purpose.
