## Overview

Passion in William Gerhardie's *Futility* is both life force and comic liability. Characters are driven by passionate desires — for love, for money, for recognition, for cards — that animate the novel's plot while simultaneously exposing its characters to ridicule. Gerhardie treats passion with characteristic ambivalence: it is what makes the characters vivid and what makes them absurd, the engine of narrative and the source of futility.

## Romantic Passion

The novel's central romantic triangle — Nikolai Vasilievich, Zina, and the narrator — is fueled by "passionate desire" [011-004]. Zina's desire "to live with Nicholas" [011-004] propels much of the plot, yet Gerhardie maintains ironic distance from this passion. The narrator's own "passionate explanations of my aloofness" [036-003] reveal the self-deception that passion enables — he protests too much, and his denials themselves become a form of passionate intensity.

## The Passions Aroused

In a characteristic Gerhardian moment, "the passions were aroused" [020-060] — the passive construction disclaiming agency even as it records emotional intensity. Passions in the novel are things that happen to people, not things they choose. "Eyes and nostrils flaming with passion" [029-019] describes a character whose passion has become entirely physical, almost animal — Gerhardie reduces romantic intensity to its bodily manifestations.

## Passion as Comic Obsession

The novel extends passion beyond romance into the realm of the absurd. The narrator confesses "I had a passion for that white and pasty substance" [026-030] — a minor culinary enthusiasm elevated to the language of grand desire. This deflation of passionate rhetoric is characteristic: Gerhardie democratizes passion, making it available for trivial objects, and in doing so undermines the claims of romantic passion to special significance.

## Passion and Self-Destruction

Nikolai is "passionately fond of cards" [029-055] — a passion that contributes to his financial ruin. The novel's treatment of passion thus includes its destructive dimension: passions are not merely comic but genuinely damaging. Yet even this destruction is treated without moralizing; it is simply another manifestation of the futility that governs all human endeavor.
