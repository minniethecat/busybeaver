Admiral Kolchak's regime — the "Kolchak State" established at [[omsk|Omsk]] (023-001) — represents the last, best hope of the anti-[[bolsheviks|Bolshevik]] forces in Siberia, and its failure is the novel's final verdict on the futility of resisting historical currents with inadequate means. In *Futility*, Kolchak appears only indirectly — glimpsed through the narrator's retrospective aside "I learnt afterwards, Admiral Kolchak" (019-072), debated by officers, and invoked as a symbol of the White cause's ambiguous legitimacy. His regime is the bureaucratic shadow behind which the novel's displaced characters attempt to reconstruct their shattered lives.

## The Omsk Regime

The Kolchak government, headquartered at Omsk, styled itself as the legitimate Russian authority — the "anti-Bolshevik Administration" to which characters appeal for redress of grievances (023-006). Its pretensions to statehood are undercut by Gerhardie's characteristic irony: the regime's authority is contingent entirely on Allied support, its territorial control is provisional, and its functionaries are haunted by the knowledge that the [[bolsheviks|Bolsheviks]] are consolidating power in the Russian heartland while they shuffle papers in Siberian exile.

> I arrived at Omsk, the new regime of Kolchak had been established. (023-001)

The narrator's arrival at Omsk coincides with the regime's brief moment of apparent viability — a window between its establishment and the slow, grinding realization that it cannot prevail. The Admiral himself is described, in one of the novel's rare moments of direct characterization, as "impulsive and well-meaning" (029-016) — a verdict that is also an epitaph for the White cause as a whole.

## The Army That Melted Away

Kolchak's Army is the institutional embodiment of the old order's military pretensions. A "second lieutenant in Kolchak's Army" arrives in Omsk (024-006), still wearing the uniform of a world that has ceased to exist. Kolchak's officers threaten massacre (029-010); the Kolchakites are obsessed with "bloodshed to suppress" (025-031). But the army's ferocity is inversely proportional to its effectiveness. As the narrator observes elsewhere: "The only Russian Army now is the Bolshevik Army. The others have all melted away" (029-034).

The army's collapse is not merely military but existential. Its officers — stripped of epaulets when they venture into Allied premises — inhabit a liminal zone between the authority they once commanded and the irrelevance to which history has consigned them. They are men for whom the uniform has become costume, the command a memory.

## Obscurity and Oblivion

Gerhardie treats Kolchak with the same deflationary irony he applies to all pretensions of authority. A character asks the "elementary question: 'Who is Kolchak? Is he a Bolshevik?'" (029-014) — a query that is both a joke at the expense of popular ignorance and a profound comment on the Admiral's actual historical significance. If the man who would be Russia's ruler is unknown to those he would rule, what does that say about the enterprise?

> "Well," I said quietly, "Kolchak has tried it. Denikin has tried it." (033-004)

This epitaph — delivered in the novel's closing movement — collapses the entire White resistance into a past-tense footnote. "Kolchak has tried it" — the perfect tense doing the work of dismissal, the attempt already concluded and judged. The Admiral's "impulsive and well-meaning" nature, his army, his state, his pretensions — all reduced to a conversational aside in a Vladivostok drawing room.
