# Memory in Futility

## Overview

Memory serves as both a structural device and a thematic concern in *Futility*. The novel is narrated retrospectively, with the narrator filtering events through the lens of recollection. Gerhardie uses memory to explore the gap between experience and its later reconstruction. The narrator's memory is selective, vivid, and self-consciously unreliable — he foregrounds the act of remembering as part of the story's meaning, not merely its mechanism. The past in *Futility* is neither a refuge nor a resource; it is a burden that characters carry, revisit, and fail to escape, and the act of remembering is itself an exercise in the futility that gives the novel its name.

## Narrative Memory

The narrator explicitly foregrounds the role of memory in his most sustained reflection on the act of writing:

> "Certain fragments of scene and speech come back to me with a peculiar insistence, as I write this third portion of my book. I have no hesitation in setting them down as I do, I think accurately enough, if not word for word. I remember them well because they had impressed me. That is the secret of memory. I have forgotten much, but there are scenes I cannot forget, fragments of speech that still ring in my ear, and I shall remember them always; at least, till I have finally pinned them to paper." (019-001)

The passage is a mini-manifesto for the novel's theory of memory. The narrator claims accuracy ("I think accurately enough, if not word for word") while simultaneously acknowledging subjectivity ("they had impressed me — that is the secret of memory"). The final clause — "at least, till I have finally pinned them to paper" — suggests that writing itself is a form of forgetting: once the memory is transcribed, it can be released. But the novel's own existence contradicts this. The memories, once "pinned to paper," do not disappear; they become the novel itself.

### The Unreliability of Recollection

This claim to accurate recollection is itself suspect — a hallmark of Gerhardie's ironic method. What the narrator "remembers" is as much construction as recovery. The confession scene with Nina illustrates this self-consciousness:

> "I could not help feeling misused and mishandled. I almost begrudged her the gallantry of my dash for water — two separate dashes, to be exact — when I remembered that they must have been carried out by other men before me, the confession tonight being, of course, an exact replica of the confessions that had preceded it, Lord knows how many times, like a melodrama with its laughter and hysterics occurring always at the proper interval as it is produced each night." (007-027)

The narrator's chivalric gesture — dashing for water — is retrospectively undermined by the realization that it is merely his turn in a repeating performance. Memory here reveals not uniqueness but repetition; the "exact replica" of previous confessions strips the moment of its romantic particularity. The narrator remembers, and in remembering understands that his own remembered self was merely playing a part.

## Vivid Recollection

Despite the retrospective frame, the narrator renders certain memories with extraordinary vividness. He recalls "very vividly the morning following on my arrival in [[petrograd|Petrograd]], when I had to meet the Admiral for the first time at the [[british-embassy-petrograd|British Embassy]]" (014-002), describing the worn red carpet of the Chancery staircase and the "very perfect young men" conversing in "very perfect intonations":

> "I still remember very vividly the morning following on my arrival in Petrograd, when I had to meet the Admiral for the first time at the British Embassy. I ascended the broad staircase with its worn red carpet to the Chancery. Very perfect young men, very perfectly dressed, were conversing in very perfect intonations about love among monkeys. It struck me as delightfully human for diplomats." (014-002)

This photographic specificity — the particular quality of light, the texture of fabric, the exact phrasing of remarks — creates the illusion of unmediated recall, even as the ironic distance of the narration reminds the reader that all memory is mediated. The "worn red carpet" carries its own history — it has been trodden by countless petitioners before the narrator, its wear a physical record of the embassy's accumulated past.

### The Texture of Sensory Memory

Gerhardie's rendered memories are distinguished by their sensory precision. Of an evening early in his acquaintance with the family, the narrator writes:

> "That evening I remember as an ever-deepening initiation into the very complicated affairs of the Bursanov family. It had been raining again, and the washed cobbles on either side of the street looked clean and shining as if newly polished." (008-001)

The "washed cobbles" gleaming in the aftermath of rain are a detail that no plot requires — they are pure atmosphere, the sensory residue of a moment that memory has preserved in its material texture. Similarly, the memory of a lunch stands out not for its substance but for a facial expression:

> "I forget the substance of the conversation of that lunch, which stands out in my memory merely on account of its coincidence with the day on which I met the family; but I remember how a remark of General Bologoevski's, that he understood the Bolshevik commissaries never washed, lit up the Admiral's face with ominous glee." (020-004)

Memory, here, is wholly arbitrary: the substance of conversation is forgotten, but the Admiral's facial expression — a momentary "ominous glee" — is preserved. Gerhardie's narrator remembers the wrong things, or rather, he remembers things that only a novelist would notice: not what was said but how a face looked when someone else said it.

## Memory and Forgetting

The novel is as concerned with forgetting as with remembering. The most harrowing instance comes from Fanny Ivanovna's account of the Vladivostok massacre:

> "And then that other boy of about the subaltern's own age, awfully good looking, who had been hiding in the chimney all night and was forgotten and only remembered as the prisoners had been marched off to the station to be killed. Then came that terrible rattle of machine-gun fire." (025-028)

The boy is "forgotten and only remembered" — the sequence is terrible in its precision. He is remembered only at the moment when remembering can do nothing for him, when the machine-gun fire that follows his recollection is the sound of his death. Memory here is not salvation but its opposite: the act of remembering serves only to register what has already been lost.

### Self-Serving Forgetting

Nina's mother, Fanny Ivanovna, experiences a more self-serving forgetting:

> "The children, Andrei Andreiech — I had forgotten them! I who had sacrificed everything for them, divorce and everything else, I had never given them a thought in my disaster. I took it up, Andrei Andreiech, promptly — I even admit somewhat dishonestly — for I was thinking more of myself, of me. Me! me! me! I had lived with him for eleven years!" (006-072)

The confession is remarkable for its self-awareness: Fanny Ivanovna catches herself in the act of forgetting, and then in the act of disguising that forgetting with a retrospective claim of maternal devotion. The repeated "Me! me! me!" is a cry of self-recognition that memory cannot erase.

### The Unmemorable

At the other extreme is Kniaz, the old Prince, whose entire mode of existence is defined by being unmemorable:

> "The old Prince was one of those quiet nonentities who enter unasked and leave unhindered almost any Russian home; and no one is likely to object to their coming because no one is likely to notice them. They have a face, a name, a manner so ordinary that you cannot remember them, ever. They are so colourless, so blank that they seem scarcely to exist at all." (007-030)

Kniaz is a figure for the opposite of memory: a man so blank that he leaves no trace on consciousness. His shares are "worthless — always have been — and never paid any dividends. Never so long as anybody can remember" (007-031). The double "never" — never paid, never remembered — makes Kniaz the novel's embodiment of the unmemorable, a walking erasure.

## Memory and the Burden of the Past

For the novel's characters, the past is not a resource but a burden. Zina, in a moment of melodramatic intensity, articulates this directly:

> "Why can't he break with the past? Why should the past always hold him? Why should he always bear the burden of these families? Andrei Andreiech! he hasn't lived yet. For was that life? I want to help him, make him happy, rid him of these petty worries, these mean intrigues. I want to help, to help, to help. But how can I help? ... I thought, all night I thought out solutions, and then I came to what seemed to me the one reasonable, the only just solution. I proposed that we should commit suicide together. But, Andrei Andreiech, he doesn't seem to be very keen on it." (010-016)

Zina's proposed solution — "we should commit suicide together" — is the logical endpoint of the desire to escape the past: if you cannot break with it, you can end it. But Nikolai "doesn't seem to be very keen on it." The past, however burdensome, is preferable to the void.

The narrator himself, lying awake at night, is invaded by memory:

> "It was very late that night when I fell asleep. I was thinking of my meeting on the morrow with the family, with Nina. I pictured to myself her image as I last remembered it. And, interlacing with these thoughts, there was the thought of the gallant Admiral in the bedroom opposite, tucked away between his heavy blankets, his teeth in a glass of water on the table at his side — no presentable sight! — seeing visions of a Napoleonic ride athwart the great Siberian plain." (019-073)

The image of Nina, "as I last remembered it," is a fixed portrait, frozen in memory — but the narrator is about to encounter the real Nina, who will have changed in ways memory cannot anticipate. The Admiral, meanwhile, is also caught in memory's grip, dreaming a Napoleonic future that will never come — a future-memory that is already a fantasy.

## Memory and Literature

The novel's treatment of memory is itself literary — it draws on a tradition of memory-writing that includes Chekhov and the great Russian novelists. After an evening of confessions, the narrator rides home across the frozen Neva:

> "I hailed a driver who slumbered in his sleigh on the corner of the Mohovaya and the Pantilemenskaya. As I drove home across the frozen river, on which the moon spread its yellow light, I thought of the Bursanovs' muddled life, and then Chekhov's Three Sisters dawned upon my memory." (007-056)

The phrase "dawned upon my memory" is significant: Chekhov's play is not consciously recalled but arises unbidden, a literary memory that illuminates the present. The "yellow light" of the moon on the frozen river is itself a Chekhovian image — a landscape of frozen longing that memory transforms into art. The narrator's memory is saturated with literature; he cannot remember without remembering what he has read.

### Memory as Farce

Fanny Ivanovna, reflecting on the absurdity of her life, makes a connection between memory and genre:

> "'I am now at the laughing stage. You see, our life can hardly be called a comedy, for if it were produced on the stage no one would believe it was real. No real people could be so silly. It is a farce, Andrei Andreiech. You were right when you made a farce of it then with your chart and diagram and things, do you remember?'" (012-006)

The narrator's response — "I remember that at these words I sat up in my chair" (012-012) — is a physical jolt of recognition. Fanny Ivanovna remembers his farcical chart; he remembers her remembering; and the novel itself is the product of these layered acts of recollection, a farce remembered and transformed into literature.

Even the critic's preface participates in the theme of memory:

> "And I remember nothing of that railway-journey, of its dust, discomfort, heat and length, because, on the second or third page, I had met living intelligible people." (P02-006)

The critic's forgetting of the railway journey — its "dust, discomfort, heat and length" — is the obverse of the narrator's vivid recollection. One person's memory of external reality is erased by another person's fictional world. Reading becomes a form of forgetting, just as writing, for the narrator, is a form of remembering.
