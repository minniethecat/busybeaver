# Freedom in Futility

## Overview

Freedom in _Futility_ is characterized by its absence. Characters yearn for liberation—from circumstances, from relationships, from themselves—yet find that "escaping towards light and freedom" leads only to the discovery "that he could not" (010-015). Gerhardie portrays freedom as a horizon that recedes as one approaches it.

## Constraint and Desire

The novel's characters are trapped by history, class, family obligation, and their own natures. The desire for freedom is universal but its attainment is impossible. This gap between desire and possibility becomes another expression of the novel's central theme.

## The Narrator's Failed Escapes

The narrator himself embodies the paradox of freedom. Having come to Russia "escaping towards light and freedom," he is soon entangled in the [[family-in-futility|family]]'s affairs upon learning "that he could not" (010-015) extricate himself. His eventual departure from Vladivostok at the novel's end is framed as a liberation—he lunches "lavishly and drank much wine" at the [[zolotoy-rog|Zolotoy Rog]] "as if to celebrate the occasion of my soul's release" (037-019)—yet the celebration feels forced. The narrator escapes geographically, but the emotional entanglement with [[nina|Nina]] and her family follows him across continents, suggesting that true freedom from the past is an impossibility.

## Freedom and Futility

The pursuit of freedom in the novel is itself an exercise in futility. Characters who seek to escape one set of constraints find themselves enmeshed in another. [[nikolai-vasilievich|Nikolai Vasilievich]] dreams of his claim being recognized, which would free the family from financial distress, but the claim is never realized. The novel suggests that the human condition is one of permanent constraint, and that the dream of freedom is the most persistent and most futile of illusions.
