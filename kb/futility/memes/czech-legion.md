The Czechoslovak Legion — referred to in Gerhardie's *Futility* as "the Czechs," "the Czecho-Slovak troops," and "the Czech Legion" — was a military force of Czech and Slovak volunteers that fought alongside the Allies during World War I and subsequently became a key actor in the [[Russian Civil War]]. In the novel, the Legion controls the Trans-Siberian Railway, shapes the family's passage through [[Siberia]], and serves as a recurring object of the characters' exasperated commentary. Their presence in the narrative reflects the historical reality of the Allied Intervention in Siberia, which Gerhardie witnessed firsthand as a member of the British Military Mission.

## The Legion in the Novel

The Czech Legion enters *Futility* through practical necessity. The [[Nikolai Vasilievich]] family's eastward journey is shaped by the Legion's control of the railway: "he learnt that the Czecho-Slovak troops in their recent..." (019-060). The Legion's strategic role is presented matter-of-factly — they are part of the landscape of the Civil War, as unavoidable as the snow.

But the characters' attitude toward the Czechs is far from neutral. The narrator reports that "The Czechs are such awful swine. They're..." (020-086) — a blunt outburst that captures the tension between refugees and the armed forces that controlled their movement. At the same time, the Legion is a source of cultural fascination: a "Czech concert" is proposed (021-048), and "the familiar Czecho-Slovak marching song" (021-078) provides the soundtrack for a Vladivostok evening.

The Legion's political complexity is acknowledged in the narrator's reference to "the dastardly action of the Czechs — that nation just then being..." (023-011), a phrase that simultaneously condemns and contextualizes. Gerhardie's irony allows the Legion to be both oppressor and oppressed, military force and nation-in-formation, obstacle and spectacle.

## Historical Background

| Phase | Date | Events |
|-------|------|--------|
| Formation | 1914–1917 | Czech and Slovak volunteers join the Tsarist army to fight Austria-Hungary |
| Expansion | 1917–1918 | Legion grows to ~60,000 men; aligned with Allied cause |
| Trans-Siberian Control | 1918–1919 | Legion seizes control of the Trans-Siberian Railway after the Bolshevik Revolution |
| Allied Intervention | 1918–1920 | Legion cooperates with Allied forces in Siberia, including the British Military Mission |
| Evacuation | 1919–1920 | Legion evacuated from Vladivostok; many members become founding figures of Czechoslovakia |

The Legion's predicament was unique: Czech and Slovak nationalists fighting for a state that did not yet exist, stranded in Russia by the [[Russian Revolution 1917]] and the Bolshevik peace with Germany, controlling a railway that stretched across a continent at war with itself. Their eventual evacuation through [[Vladivostok]] was one of the epic journeys of the twentieth century — a journey that Gerhardie's characters, traveling the same route in the opposite direction (toward exile rather than toward nationhood), mirror in ironic miniature.

## Role in the Novel's World

The Czech Legion serves three functions in *Futility*:

1. **Strategic reality**: They control the railway; the family's movement depends on their dispositions and decisions (019-060, 020-065).

2. **Social presence**: Czech officers appear at concerts and social gatherings in Vladivostok (021-048), part of the international mélange — British officers, American sailors, Russian refugees — that populates the novel's later chapters.

3. **Object of resentment**: The characters' frustration with the Czechs (020-086, 023-011) reflects the broader experience of civilians caught between armed forces they neither control nor fully understand. The Legion is simultaneously necessary (for order on the railway) and resented (for the power it wields over civilian life).

## Connection to Other Forces

The Czech Legion's relationship with [[kolchaks-army|kolchak's army]] was historically complex — the Legion controlled the railway that Kolchak's forces depended on for supply and movement. In the novel, this tension is registered indirectly through the characters' navigation of competing authorities in [[Omsk]] and along the railway. The Legion also intersected with the [[allied-intervention|allied intervention in siberia]], of which the narrator's British Military Mission was a part.
