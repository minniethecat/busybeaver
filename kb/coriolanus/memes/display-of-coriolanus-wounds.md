# Display of Coriolanus's Wounds

The **Display of Coriolanus's Wounds** is the ceremonial event in Act II, Scene II of *Coriolanus* in which the newly named Caius Marcius Coriolanus is required to exhibit his battle scars to the Roman Senate and people as part of the consular election ritual. Coriolanus's profound discomfort with this custom exposes the central tension between martial honor and political performance.

## The Ceremonial Context

In the Roman Republic, a candidate for consul was expected to stand before the people in the *toga candida* (the white toga of candidacy), and to display the wounds received in military service as visible proof of *virtus* (martial virtue) and sacrifice for the state. This ritual functioned as both an honor and a humbling obligation — the candidate must ask, not demand, the people's approval.

For Coriolanus, the ritual's dual nature as both proud display and humiliating petition proves intolerable. His objection is not to recognition of his wounds — he is, after all, proud of them — but to the transactional logic the ceremony imposes: the wounds, earned in authentic combat, are reduced to currency for purchasing votes. In Coriolanus's value system, a deed that must be performed for an audience is a deed degraded.

The wounds ceremony is Shakespeare's invention; Plutarch's Coriolanus displays his wounds without protest. Shakespeare's addition of Coriolanus's resistance transforms a biographical detail into a character-defining dramatic crisis.

## Cominius's Laudatory Speech

The Senate session opens with Menenius calling upon Cominius, the consul, to "report / A little of that worthy work perform'd / By Caius Marcius Coriolanus" (045-002). Cominius delivers an extended encomium, tracing Coriolanus's military career from his first battle at age sixteen — fighting against Tarquin — through seventeen subsequent battles, culminating in the extraordinary feats at Corioli:

> "His sword, death's stamp, / Where it did mark, it took; from face to foot / He was a thing of blood, whose every motion / Was timed with dying cries: alone he enter'd / The mortal gate of the city, which he painted / With shunless destiny; aidless came off, / And with a sudden re-inforcement struck / Corioli like a planet." (047-002)

Cominius's rhetoric transforms Coriolanus from a man into a force of nature. The metaphor "a thing of blood" erases the boundary between the warrior and his weapon; the simile "like a planet" elevates him to cosmic scale. The phrase "death's stamp" — death's official seal, death's currency — prefigures the play's obsession with wounds as tokens of value.

Throughout this speech, Coriolanus cannot bear to listen. He attempts to leave the chamber, protesting:

> "I had rather have my wounds to heal again / Than hear say how I got them." (046-002)

This is a remarkable statement. Coriolanus would rather re-experience the physical pain of his wounds than endure the psychological pain of hearing them described. The line reveals a vulnerability that his public persona ordinarily conceals: praise, for Coriolanus, is a form of violation.

And later:

> "I had rather have one scratch my head i' the sun / When the alarum were struck than idly sit / To hear my nothings monster'd." (047-002)

The word "monster'd" — made monstrous — is crucial. Cominius's praise, however well-intentioned, transforms Coriolanus's deeds into something unnatural, excessive, outside the human scale. Coriolanus resists not the recognition but the distortion: his actions, when narrated, become "nothings monster'd."

## The Wounds Display Refused

When the Senate confirms him as consul and Menenius reminds him that he must now "speak to the people," Coriolanus balks. He requests that he be allowed to "o'erleap that custom" (048-002), refusing to perform the ritual humiliation:

> "I cannot / Put on the gown, stand naked and entreat them, / For my wounds' sake, to give their suffrage: please you / That I may pass this doing." (048-002)

The phrase "stand naked" is metaphorical — the candidate wears the *toga candida*, not literal nakedness — but it captures Coriolanus's psychological reality. For him, the political performance is an exposure more intimate and more shameful than any physical nakedness. The word "entreat" is the heart of his objection: a Coriolanus does not entreat; a Coriolanus commands.

The tribune Sicinius insists that the people "Must have their voices; neither will they bate / One jot of ceremony" (048-002). This is the tribunes at their most procedurally correct — and their most politically astute. By insisting on the letter of the law, they force Coriolanus into a situation where his nature will destroy him. Menenius urges compliance, framing it as a formality — but Coriolanus cannot treat it as a formality because his entire identity is at stake.

Coriolanus's response reveals the core of his character:

> "To brag unto them, thus I did, and thus; / Show them the unaching scars which I should hide, / As if I had received them for the hire / Of their breath only!" (048-002)

The key word is "hire" — wages. For Coriolanus, displaying his wounds to the people transforms them from gifts freely given to the state into commodities purchased by popular approval. The wounds themselves are "unaching" — no longer painful — but the act of showing them creates a new kind of pain, a wound to his honor that will not heal.

## The Body Politic and the Body Wounded

Coriolanus's resistance to displaying his wounds engages one of Shakespeare's most persistent concerns: the relationship between the physical body and the body politic. In the Roman political imagination, the candidate's wounded body is a text that the people read — each scar tells a story of service and sacrifice. The ceremony makes the candidate's body legible to the citizenry.

Coriolanus refuses this legibility. His body, in his understanding, is not a text for public interpretation; it is a weapon, an instrument, a private possession. By refusing to display his wounds, he is refusing the fundamental premise of republican politics: that the leader's body belongs, in some sense, to the people he leads.

This refusal is simultaneously noble and catastrophic. It is noble because it refuses to commodify authentic sacrifice; it is catastrophic because it refuses the only available mechanism for translating martial virtue into political authority.

## Political Consequences

Coriolanus's resistance to this ritual is politically catastrophic. The tribunes immediately seize on it as the lever to destroy him:

> Brutus: "You see how he intends to use the people." (048-002)

Sicinius predicts that Coriolanus "will require them, / As if he did contemn what he requested / Should be in them to give" (048-002). This is an accurate prediction — when Coriolanus does appear before the people in the marketplace, his contempt is palpable, and the tribunes' manipulation transforms that contempt into popular rejection.

The tragedy of the wounds display is that Coriolanus is right — the ritual IS degrading, the display of wounds IS a form of commodification — but being right is politically useless. In the republican order, the appearance of humility matters more than the reality of virtue. Coriolanus's refusal to perform is, in one sense, a gesture of integrity; in another, it is a catastrophic failure of political imagination.
