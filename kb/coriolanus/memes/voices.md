# Voices

## Definition

In *Coriolanus*, **"voices"** is the play's most concentrated political pun. The word refers primarily to the votes of the Roman citizens — the electoral approval that [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] must solicit to become consul. But "voices" also means *speech* (the citizens' right to express political will), *noise* (the inchoate roar of the multitude), and *agency* (the capacity to act politically). When Coriolanus mockingly repeats "Your voices!" (002-003) after each citizen's grudging promise of support, Shakespeare condenses an entire theory of democratic legitimacy into two syllables.

> Your voices: for your voices I have fought;
> Watch'd for your voices; for your voices bear
> Of wounds two dozen odd; battles thrice six
> I have seen and heard of; for your voices have
> Done many things, some less, some more: your voices:
> Indeed, I would be consul. (002-003)

The repetition of "voices" — six times in a single speech — is not merely rhetorical. It enacts the logic of the electoral exchange: martial service is retroactively reframed as having been performed "for your voices," reducing battlefield valour to a transaction in which wounds are tendered as payment for votes.

## Historical Background

The Roman electoral system that Shakespeare loosely dramatises was the *comitia centuriata* — an assembly organised by centuries (military units) in which voting was weighted by wealth. The wealthiest centuries voted first and their decisions carried disproportionate influence. The ritual of *ambitus* (canvassing) required candidates to move among the people, wearing the whitened *toga candida* (from which "candidate" derives), personally asking for support.

Shakespeare strips away these institutional complexities and focuses on the raw interpersonal dynamic: a proud aristocrat forced to beg from those he despises. The historical consul was elected by a complex constitutional machinery; Shakespeare's consul is elected by the raw exchange of deference for approval. This simplification is dramatically powerful — it makes the electoral ritual a direct confrontation between character and class — but it also sharpens the play's anti-democratic edge.

## In the Play

### The Gown of Humility (Act 2, Scene 2–3)

The electoral ritual requires Coriolanus to stand in the marketplace wearing the "gown of humility" (002-003) and ask each citizen individually for his "voice." This is the custom that Coriolanus calls "a needless vouche" (002-003) — an empty form that debases the honour it is supposed to confirm.

The citizen encounters reveal the deep strangeness of the ritual:

| Citizen | Interaction | Coriolanus's Response |
|---------|------------|----------------------|
| First Citizen | "if he do require our voices, we ought not to deny him" | "Your good voice, sir; what say you?" |
| Third Citizen | "Are you all resolved to give your voices?" | "Worthy voices!" |
| Fourth Citizen | "You have received many wounds for your country" | "I will make much of your voices" |
| Fifth Citizen | "we give you our voices heartily" | "Most sweet voices!" |

The irony is scalding. Each "voice" Coriolanus receives is a humiliation he stores up. To the citizens, the exchange is the normal ritual of republican politics. To Coriolanus, it is a violation of the very honour the ritual is meant to certify.

> Kindly! Sir, I pray, let me ha't:
> I have wounds to show you, which shall be yours in private.
> Your good voice, sir; what say you? (002-003)

### The Revocation (Act 2, Scene 3 — Act 3, Scene 1)

The tribunes [[sicinius-velutus|Sicinius]] and [[junius-brutus|Brutus]] persuade the citizens to revoke their voices — a constitutional action that reveals the fragility of the electoral mandate:

> He said he had wounds, which he could show in private;
> And with his hat, thus waving it in scorn,
> "I would be consul," says he... (002-003)

The tribunes' argument is procedurally sound: Coriolanus mocked the ritual and therefore failed to earn the voices legitimately. But their manipulation of popular sentiment exposes the vulnerability of democratic process to demagogic interference. The voices that were given can be taken back — and it is this reversal that drives Coriolanus to his fatal rage in the Senate.

### The Beast with Many Heads

Coriolanus's contempt for popular "voices" culminates in his image of the "beast with many heads" (004-001) — the multitude as a monstrous organism whose many voices can never cohere into a single rational will. This Platonic anti-democratic trope (from *Republic* 493a–c) is Coriolanus's ultimate rejection of the electoral principle: if the people are a beast, their voices are mere noise, and the consul who solicits that noise debases the office he seeks.

## Relationship to Other Concepts

- [[wounds|Wounds]]: "Voices" and "wounds" are the two terms of the electoral exchange — wounds are the currency, voices the payment; the play's tragedy is that Coriolanus values the former absolutely and the latter not at all
- [[consulship|Consulship]]: The consulship is the prize for which voices are solicited; the electoral ritual is the constitutional bridge between popular will and executive power
- [[tribunate|Tribunate]]: The tribunes are the institutional embodiment of the people's "voice" — they speak for the plebeians, and their power to revoke voices is the mechanism of Coriolanus's political destruction
- [[plebeian-rights|Plebeian Rights]]: The right to give or withhold "voices" is the fundamental plebeian political right; Coriolanus's contempt for voices is a contempt for plebeian political agency itself
