# Roman Patricians

## Overview

The Roman Patricians are the hereditary aristocratic class whose political dominance forms the structural backdrop of *[[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]*. As a collective character, they appear throughout the play — in the [[roman-senate|Senate]], in the Forum, and in crowd scenes — representing the governing order that [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] embodies and defends. Their conflict with the plebeians, institutionalised in the historical [[class-conflict|Conflict of the Orders]], is the political engine of the tragedy.

## Role in the Play

### The Class Conflict

The patricians are defined in opposition to the plebeians from the opening scene. The [[first-citizen|First Citizen]] describes "the patricians good" as those whose "abundance" is measured by plebeian "sufferance" (000-016). [[menenius-agrippa|Menenius Agrippa]], himself a self-described "humorous patrician" (372), defends his class with the fable of the belly, arguing that the patricians are the nourishing centre of the body politic: "most charitable care Have the patricians of you" (000-032).

### Military and Political Support

After [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s victory at [[corioli|Corioli]], [[cominius|Cominius]] describes how "great patricians shall attend and shrug" at the recounting of his deeds (307). This image of patricians gathering to honour their military champion establishes the reciprocal relationship between the hero and his class. When [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] returns to [[rome|Rome]], he declares "The good patricians must be visited" (422) — acknowledging his obligations to his noble supporters before attending to his own household.

### The Crisis of Authority

In the Forum scene, [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] turns on his own class with characteristic fury: "O good but most unwise patricians! why, You grave but reckless senators, have you thus Given Hydra here to choose an officer?" (654). His rebuke is directed at the patricians' political miscalculation in granting the plebeians tribunician power. During the tumult that follows, the stage direction records the chaos: voices cry "[[tribunate|Tribunes]]!" "[[patricians|Patricians]]!" "[[fifth-citizen|Citizen]]s!" (683) — the three factions colliding in the Forum.

A single "[[a-patrician|Patrician]]" speaks one line as [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] is banished: "This man has marr'd his fortune" (724), to which a "[[second-patrician|Second Patrician]]" replies simply: "I would they were a-bed!" (726). These brief utterances capture the patricians' helplessness in the face of the political forces their champion has unleashed.

## Dramatic Significance

The Roman Patricians are more than a background element — they are the class that [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] embodies and, ultimately, is destroyed by. His tragedy is inseparable from theirs: he is their sword, and when the sword proves politically untenable, the patricians — like [[menenius-agrippa|Menenius]], like [[cominius|Cominius]] — stand by as he is cast out.
