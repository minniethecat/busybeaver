# Wounds

## Definition

In *Coriolanus*, **wounds** are far more than bodily injuries. They are a currency of political legitimacy, a language of masculine honour, and — in the play's central dramatic irony — the very things that Coriolanus cannot and will not display to secure the consulship. His twenty-seven wounds (counted with obsessive precision by [[volumnia|Volumnia]] and [[menenius-agrippa|Menenius]]) are the objective record of his service to Rome; his refusal to show them in the marketplace is the subjective act of pride that destroys him.

> I have some wounds upon me, and they smart
> To hear themselves remember'd. (001-009)

The wound is Shakespeare's master metaphor for the relationship between private virtue and public recognition. Coriolanus's body carries the text of his valour; the electoral ritual demands that text be read aloud. His tragedy is that he cannot bear to be the narrator of his own worth.

## Historical Background

In the Roman Republic, the display of wounds (*ostentatio vulnerum*) was a recognised element of political candidacy. A man seeking high office would remove his toga in the Forum to reveal the scars on his chest — each scar a visible affidavit of service to the *res publica*. Plutarch records this custom in his *Life of Coriolanus*, and Shakespeare follows him closely.

The custom rested on a particular logic of political legitimacy: the people's "voices" (votes) were given in exchange for demonstrated *virtus* (martial excellence). The wound was the proof of *virtus*. This exchange — scars for suffrage — is what Coriolanus finds intolerable:

> To brag unto them, thus I did, and thus;
> Show them the unaching scars which I should hide,
> As if I had received them for the hire
> Of their breath only! (002-003)

His objection is not to the wounds themselves — he is fiercely proud of them — but to the *transactional logic* of the display. For Coriolanus, wounds are intrinsic badges of honour; the electoral ritual turns them into extrinsic bargaining chips, and this commodification of valour is, to him, a form of degradation.

## In the Play

Wounds appear at four critical junctures, each marking a turn in Coriolanus's political fortunes:

### 1. Corioli: Wounds Earned (Act 1, Scene 9)

Coriolanus returns from the battle at Corioli bleeding and exhausted. [[cominius|Cominius]] praises him in an extended encomium, and the army acclaims him with his new *cognomen*. Here wounds function straightforwardly as honour: they are the physical evidence of the deed that earns him his name.

> Worthy sir, thou bleed'st;
> Thy exercise hath been too violent
> For a second course of fight. (001-009)

### 2. The Count: Wounds as Currency (Act 2)

As Coriolanus returns to Rome, his mother and Menenius count his wounds with almost fetishistic pleasure. Volumnia boasts "he had, before this last expedition, twenty-five wounds upon him" (002-002). Menenius writes to Volumnia: "Is he not wounded? he was wont to come home wounded" (002-002). The counting transforms wounds from evidence of specific acts into a cumulative political asset — a wound-count as election résumé.

### 3. The Refusal: Wounds Withheld (Act 2, Scene 3)

The consular election requires Coriolanus to stand in the marketplace in the "gown of humility" and display his wounds to the citizens whose votes he solicits. This he cannot do. He offers to show them "in private" (002-003) — a compromise that satisfies no one. His refusal is the pivot on which the play turns: the people feel cheated of the wound-spectacle they are owed, and the tribunes exploit this disappointment to engineer his downfall.

> I had rather have my wounds to heal again
> Than hear say how I got them. (002-003)

### 4. The Banishment: Wounds Mocked (Act 3)

After the banishment, the wounds that were Coriolanus's political capital become the subject of mockery. He dismisses them as "scratches with briers, / Scars to move laughter only" (003-003). This self-devaluation of his own wounds signals his psychological break from Rome. The marks that tied him to the state now mean nothing — or rather, they mean only that he gave his body to a people unworthy of the gift.

## Relationship to Other Concepts

- [[roman-honour|Roman Honour]]: Wounds are the physical embodiment of *virtus*, the Roman concept of martial excellence; the wound-display ritual is where private honour meets public validation
- [[consulship|Consulship]]: The electoral custom of wound display is a requirement of the consular candidacy; Coriolanus's refusal to comply is the proximate cause of his failed election
- [[voices|Voices]]: "Voices" (votes) are what the people give in exchange for the display of wounds; the play's language equates the two — "for your voices have / Done many things" — as if the wounds were suffered for votes rather than for Rome
- [[class-conflict|Class Conflict]]: The wound-display ritual exposes the dependency of patrician ambition on plebeian approval, a dependency Coriolanus finds humiliating
