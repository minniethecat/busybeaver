# Second Servingman

The **Second Servingman** is a servant in the household of [[tullus-aufidius|Tullus Aufidius]] in Shakespeare's *[[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]*. He appears in Act IV Scene V, where he encounters the disguised [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] and later participates in the comic post-meeting commentary with his fellow servants.

## Role in the Play

The Second Servingman first appears calling for "Cotus," another servant, and tells him his master wants him [021-002]. When [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] enters Aufidius's house, the Second Servingman confronts him, asking "Whence are you, sir? Has the porter his eyes in his head, that he gives entrance to such companions? Pray, get you out" [021-002]. He grows increasingly frustrated with [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s refusal to leave, threatening "Are you so brave? I'll have you talked with anon" [021-002].

After [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] and Aufidius depart, the Second Servingman rejoins his fellows. He admits he had intended to strike [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] with a cudgel but sensed something unusual about him [021-002]. He joins the debate about whether [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] or Aufidius is the greater soldier, initially defending his master but conceding [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s superiority [021-008].

## Key Dialogue

The Second Servingman contributes to the servants' comic debate on war and peace, agreeing with the [[first-servingman|First Servingman]] that "as war, in some sort, may be said to be a ravisher, so it cannot be denied but peace is a great maker of cuckolds" [021-008].

On [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s character, he observes: "Nay, I knew by his face that there was something in him: he had, sir, a kind of face, methought — I cannot tell how to term it" [021-002].

## Dramatic Function

The Second Servingman provides comic relief through his blustering attempt to eject [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] and his subsequent admiration for the disguised general. His initial hostility dissolving into awed respect mirrors the play's exploration of identity and reputation — even in disguise, [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s martial bearing commands recognition from common men.

## Relationships

- **[[tullus-aufidius|Tullus Aufidius]]**: The Second Servingman's master. He is loyal and initially defensive of Aufidius's military reputation [021-008].
- **[[first-servingman|First Servingman]]**: His frequent conversational partner in the comic scene that follows Aufidius and [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s meeting.
- **[[third-servingman|Third Servingman]]**: The third servant, who brings news from inside the house about the proceedings.
- **[[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]**: Initially hostile towards the disguised Roman, he is later deeply impressed by [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s physical presence.
