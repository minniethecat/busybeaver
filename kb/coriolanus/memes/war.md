## Definition

**War** in *Coriolanus* is the play's structural foundation. Every major plot event — Coriolanus's rise at Corioli, his political candidacy, his banishment, and his death — unfolds against a backdrop of military conflict between Rome and the Volscians. War is not merely setting but argument: the play asks whether martial excellence is sufficient qualification for civil authority.

Shakespeare presents war as both the forge of character and the solvent of peacetime order. The warrior's virtues — courage, decisiveness, contempt for comfort — that save Rome at Corioli are precisely the qualities that destroy the constitutional settlement at home.

## Historical Background

In Plutarch's *Life of Coriolanus*, the Volscian wars were a historical reality of early Republican Rome. The Romans and Volscians contested the Latin plain for generations, and the capture of Corioli by Caius Marcius was a recorded military achievement that earned him his agnomen. Shakespeare follows Plutarch closely in the battle sequences, but compresses and dramatises the military episodes to heighten the contrast between the warrior's clarity of purpose in the field and his political incoherence in the Forum.

## In the Play

War enters the play almost immediately. Menenius's fable of the belly temporarily placates the hungry citizens, but news that "the Volsces are in arms" (001-002) redirects the political crisis into a military one. This pattern — war as a release valve for domestic tension — recurs throughout the play.

The siege of Corioli is the play's martial centrepiece. Coriolanus's single-handed entry into the city gates — "Alone I fought in your Corioli walls" (008-002) — is the deed that earns him his name and defines his identity. The battle scenes (004-002 through 009-002) present war as a meritocracy: rank dissolves, and worth is measured in wounds and courage. Titus Lartius honours Marcius as "thou worthiest Marcius" (005-002), and Cominius declares his deeds beyond report (009-002).

But war is also a unifying force: patrician and plebeian fight side by side against a common enemy. The military camp at Corioli is perhaps the only space in the play where class antagonism briefly dissolves. Cominius's praise after the battle — "well fought; we are come off Like Romans" (006-002) — speaks to a martial identity that temporarily transcends political division.

The tragedy is that this martial unity cannot survive peacetime. The qualities that make Coriolanus a great soldier make him a catastrophic politician, and the play's closing acts trace the bloody consequences of that contradiction.
