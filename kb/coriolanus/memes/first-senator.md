# First Senator

**First Senator** is the designation for senatorial figures appearing in both [[rome|Rome]] and [[corioli|Corioli]] in Shakespeare's *[[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]*. In Roman scenes, the First Senator represents patrician authority and supports [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s elevation to [[consulship|consul]]; in [[volscians|Volsci]]an scenes, the First Senator serves as a military strategist advising Aufidius.

## Roman First Senator

### Military Council (Act I)

In Act I, the First Senator enters alongside [[cominius|Cominius]] and [[titus-lartius|Titus Lartius]] as part of the Roman senatorial delegation. He brings news of the [[volscians|Volsci]]an threat: "[[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Marcius]], 'tis true that you have lately told us; The [[volscians|Volsces]] are in arms" (006-001). The phrasing is noteworthy — he acknowledges [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s prior intelligence ("you have lately told us"), establishing the reciprocity between senatorial authority (which commands) and martial knowledge (which informs). He then directs [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] to join [[cominius|Cominius]] in the coming campaign: "Then, worthy [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Marcius]], Attend upon [[cominius|Cominius]] to these wars" (006-001). The epithet "worthy" is both honorific and functional — it marks the transition from intelligence-giver to soldier. He further commands: "Your company to the Capitol; where, I know, Our greatest friends attend us" (006-001).

### The Consular Elevation (Act II)

During the senate session in Act II, the First Senator plays a ceremonial role in [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s elevation to the [[consulship|consul]]ship. He calls upon [[cominius|Cominius]] to deliver the general's report of [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s battlefield deeds:

> Speak, good Cominius: Leave nothing out for length, and make us think Rather our state's defective for requital Than we to stretch it out. (018-002)

This speech reveals the First Senator's political calculus. The instruction to "leave nothing out" is qualified by an awareness that the recitation of [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s deeds will shame the state into recognizing its debt. The senator's language — "defective for requital" — frames gratitude as an accounting problem: [[rome|Rome]] owes [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] more than it can pay.

He later commands: "Call [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]" (018-002), formally summoning the hero before the senate. The imperative is simple but freighted: the First Senator speaks for the institutional body, and his "Call" is the act that ushers the private warrior into public office.

## Volscian First Senator

### The War Council (Act I, Scene II)

In the [[volscians|Volsci]]an camp, a different First Senator appears as a military counsellor to Aufidius. He interrogates the Roman intelligence: "So, your opinion is, Aufidius, That they of [[rome|Rome]] are entered in our counsels And know how we proceed" (007-002). The formulation — "your opinion is" rather than "we know" — maintains the diplomatic register of a senator addressing a general: he defers to Aufidius's military judgment while raising a strategic concern. He asserts [[volscians|Volsci]]an readiness with a note of defiance: "Our army's in the field: We never yet made doubt but [[rome|Rome]] was ready To answer us" (007-002).

### On the Walls of Corioli (Act I, Scene IV)

During the siege of [[corioli|Corioli]] (Act I, Scene IV), the First Senator appears on [[rome|the city]] walls, defiantly responding to the Roman demand to know Aufidius's whereabouts: "No, nor a man that fears you less than he, That's lesser than a little" (009-004). The double negative — "no … nor" — and the paradoxical "lesser than a little" compress the [[volscians|Volsci]]an defiance into a rhetorical gesture of extravagant contempt. It is [[rome|the city]]'s voice, speaking from its walls, refusing Roman interrogation.

## Dramatic Function

The First Senator functions as a structural device — a placeholder for institutional authority in both [[rome|Rome]] and [[corioli|Corioli]]. The title "First Senator" is not a proper name but a theatrical designation indicating the leading voice among the collective body of senators in any given scene. The same designation serving two opposed polities is itself a dramatic point: the institutional structures of [[rome|Rome]] and [[corioli|Corioli]] are mirror images, and the senator who commands in one city is indistinguishable from the senator who defies in the other.
