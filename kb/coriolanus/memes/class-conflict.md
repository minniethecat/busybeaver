# Class Conflict

## 概念定义

**Class conflict** in *Coriolanus* refers to the irreducible political antagonism between the patrician aristocracy and the plebeian commoners of Republican Rome. This is not a background condition but the engine of the plot: the play opens with starving citizens in armed revolt, proceeds through the political mobilization of the tribunate, and culminates in Coriolanus's banishment — each event driven by the collision of aristocratic contempt and plebeian assertion.

Shakespeare's Rome is divided into two political orders (*ordines*): the patricians, who monopolize the Senate, magistracies, and priesthoods, and the plebeians, who constitute the mass of citizens but possess only the tribunate as a political counterweight. The class conflict in the play is structural, not incidental — it is inscribed in the Roman constitution itself.

## 历史背景

The historical Conflict of the Orders lasted roughly from 494 BCE (the First Secession of the Plebs) to 287 BCE (the *Lex Hortensia* making plebiscites binding on all citizens). Key milestones included:

- **494 BCE**: Creation of the tribunate, elected by the plebeian assembly
- **451–450 BCE**: Publication of the Twelve Tables, Rome's first written law code
- **367 BCE**: *Lex Licinia Sextia* opened the consulship to plebeians
- **287 BCE**: *Lex Hortensia* gave plebiscites the force of law without senatorial ratification

Shakespeare compresses this centuries-long evolution into a single dramatic moment. His patricians behave as though plebeian political rights are a recent and reversible innovation, while his tribunes operate with the confidence of established constitutional officers. This temporal compression heightens the dramatic stakes: the class settlement is presented as fragile, contested, and perpetually on the brink of collapse.

## 剧中的体现

### The Corn Riot (Act 1, Scene 1)

The play opens in the midst of class warfare. Armed citizens gather with "staves, clubs, and other weapons" demanding grain at affordable prices (001-002). The First Citizen articulates the class analysis directly: "Caius Marcius is chief enemy to the people" (001-002). The patricians, in the citizens' view, hoard grain while the plebeians starve — a material grievance that the play never fully resolves, only displaces into the foreign war against the Volscians.

Menenius Agrippa attempts to defuse the revolt with the fable of the belly — an organic metaphor of the body politic in which the Senate is the belly that distributes nourishment to all members. The citizens are not persuaded: "It was an answer" (001-002). The class antagonism is too visceral for metaphor to dissolve.

### Coriolanus's Contempt (Acts 2–3)

Coriolanus embodies the patrician worldview in its most uncompromising form. He refers to the plebeians as "the mutable, rank-scented many" (015-002), "woollen vassals, things created / To buy and sell with groats" (015-002), and "the beast with many heads" (017-002). His contempt is not merely personal prejudice — it is a coherent aristocratic ideology that denies the plebeians any legitimate political standing.

The consular election of Act 2 forces this ideology into open conflict with the plebeian constitution. Coriolanus must perform deference to the very class he despises — and he cannot sustain the performance. His refusal to display his wounds publicly is not pride alone but a principled rejection of the plebeian right to judge him.

### The Tribunes' Counter-Mobilization (Act 3)

Sicinius and Brutus weaponize Coriolanus's contempt. They marshal the citizens' votes, orchestrate the revocation of the consular approval (013-002), and frame Coriolanus's patrician pride as tyrannical ambition: "charge him home, that he affects / Tyrannical power" (016-002). The class conflict here becomes a procedural battle fought through the institutions of the mixed constitution — the tribunes using their sacrosanct authority, the patricians relying on senatorial prerogative, and the citizens serving as the swing constituency whose allegiance determines the outcome.

### Banishment and Aftermath

Coriolanus's banishment is both a victory for the plebeian party and a demonstration of class conflict's destructive potential. "The nobility are vex'd," Sicinius observes, "whom we see have sided / In his behalf" (018-002). The banishment does not resolve the class conflict; it merely removes its most polarizing figure. The play's final acts demonstrate that class solidarity — even the threat of Coriolanus at the gates of Rome — cannot bridge the fundamental divide between patrician and plebeian, between aristocratic honour and popular sovereignty.

## 与其他概念的关系

- **Tribunate**: The institutional expression of class conflict. The tribunate exists because the plebeians are a distinct political order, and every tribunician action in the play is simultaneously an act of class struggle.

- **Plebeian Rights** (*ius plebis*): The substantive demands that drive plebeian political mobilization — grain at fair prices, political representation, protection from patrician abuse. The tribunate is the mechanism; plebeian rights are the goal.

- **Patrician Power** (*auctoritas patrum*): The aristocratic order's institutional advantage — control of the Senate, the magistracies, and the priesthoods. Class conflict in Coriolanus is fundamentally about whether patrician power can coexist with plebeian political rights.

- **Populism**: The political technique of mobilizing popular sentiment. Both Menenius (with the belly fable) and the tribunes (with their procedural maneuvering) practice populism, though to opposite ends. The class conflict in Coriolanus is refracted through competing populist appeals.

- **Roman Constitution**: The mixed constitution provides the institutional framework within which class conflict is fought. The distribution of powers between consuls, Senate, tribunes, and assemblies determines the strategic options available to each class.

## 文学批评视角

### 18th–19th Century Reception

The class politics of *Coriolanus* have generated sharply divergent critical interpretations. Samuel Johnson (1765) saw the play as a cautionary tale about the "licentiousness of the populace" and the dangers of democratic excess — a reading that aligns Coriolanus with contemporary anxieties about mob rule. William Hazlitt (1817), by contrast, detected in the play "the spirit of a republican" — an implicit critique of aristocratic arrogance that, Hazlitt argued, made the tribunes' case stronger than Shakespeare perhaps intended.

### 20th–21st Century Reinterpretations

Marxist critics of the 20th century, notably Bertolt Brecht in his adaptation *Coriolan* (1952–53), read the class conflict as the play's central subject rather than its backdrop. Brecht's version shifts the focus from Coriolanus's tragic flaw to the structural dynamics of class warfare, making the plebeians' decisions collective, strategic, and politically legible rather than merely fickle.

More recent historicist criticism (James Holstun 2000, Andrew Hadfield 2003) has situated *Coriolanus* within the context of the 1607 Midlands Rising — an anti-enclosure protest in Shakespeare's own Warwickshire that was suppressed with considerable violence just before the play was likely written. The opening scene's depiction of armed citizens demanding grain resonates with the real anxieties of Jacobean England, where food riots were a recurrent feature of dearth years.

## The Patrician-Plebeian Dynamic

The class conflict in *Coriolanus* is structured around the constitutional innovations of the early Roman Republic. The plebeians have recently won the right to elect tribunes — officers who can veto patrician actions — and this new political reality is the ground on which the tragedy unfolds. The patricians, represented by Coriolanus and his circle, regard the tribunician office as an affront to natural hierarchy: "What would you have, you curs," Coriolanus demands, "that like nor peace nor war?" (001-002). The plebeians, for their part, are driven by material grievances — corn prices, hunger, and the arrogance of their rulers. Shakespeare presents neither side uncritically: the tribunes are manipulative, the people fickle, the patricians arrogant. The tragedy is that all parties are trapped within a political structure that makes genuine accommodation impossible.
