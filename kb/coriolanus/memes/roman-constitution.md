# Roman Constitution

## Definition

The **Roman constitution** in *Coriolanus* is the unwritten framework of institutions, customs, and powers that structures political life in Republican Rome. It is a **mixed constitution** — combining monarchical (consuls), aristocratic (Senate), and democratic (tribunes, assemblies) elements — and the play's political drama consists precisely in the collision of these elements.

Shakespeare presents the Roman constitution not as a stable settlement but as a field of permanent contestation. The consulship claims monarchical authority, the Senate asserts aristocratic prerogative, and the tribunes invoke popular sovereignty — each element can plausibly claim constitutional legitimacy, and no meta-principle exists to adjudicate between them. The crisis of *Coriolanus* is, at root, a constitutional crisis.

## Historical Background

The Roman constitution evolved over centuries without ever being codified in a single document. Its key institutions included:

- **Consuls**: Two annually elected chief magistrates with *imperium* (military command and executive authority). The consulship represented the monarchical element — concentrated power, temporally limited.
- **Senate**: An aristocratic council of former magistrates that controlled finance, foreign policy, and — through *auctoritas patrum* — the ratification of legislation. The Senate represented the aristocratic element — collective wisdom, institutional continuity.
- **Tribunes**: Officers elected by the plebeian assembly with veto power (*intercessio*) and sacrosanct person. The tribunate represented the democratic element — popular sovereignty, protection against magisterial abuse.
- **Assemblies**: The *comitia centuriata* (elected senior magistrates), *comitia tributa* (elected junior magistrates), and *concilium plebis* (elected tribunes, passed plebiscites). Each had its own voting structure and jurisdiction.

Polybius (c. 200–118 BCE), in Book VI of his *Histories*, famously attributed Roman success to this mixed constitution, arguing that each element checked the others, preventing the degenerative cycle that afflicted pure constitutions (monarchy → tyranny → aristocracy → oligarchy → democracy → ochlocracy).

## In the Play

### The Constitution in Action (Act 1)

The opening scene immediately activates the constitution's democratic element. The citizens are armed and demanding grain — an extra-constitutional assertion of popular power. Menenius's belly fable is a constitutional argument dressed as organic metaphor: the Senate is the center that nourishes all members, and popular revolt is self-mutilation. Coriolanus's arrival with armed force represents the monarchical element — executive power asserting itself against popular disorder.

### The Consular Election (Act 2)

The consular election in Act 2 is the constitution's most elaborate procedural sequence. Coriolanus must secure the Senate's approval, then solicit the people's voices, then receive formal investiture. This three-stage process — senatorial nomination, popular ratification, magisterial inauguration — embodies the mixed constitution's logic of mutual dependence.

Coriolanus's refusal to perform the popular stage properly — "I cannot / Put on the gown, stand naked, and entreat them" (013-002) — is a constitutional violation in spirit if not in letter. He accepts the Senate's role but rejects the people's, treating popular consent as a degrading formality rather than a legitimate constitutional requirement.

### Constitutional Crisis (Act 3)

Act 3 stages a full constitutional crisis. The tribunes revoke the people's already-granted voices — an action of dubious constitutional precedent but effective political power. Coriolanus responds not by appealing to constitutional procedure but by attacking the constitution itself: the tribunate should be abolished, the plebeians disenfranchised, and the mixed constitution replaced with pure aristocratic rule.

The banishment that follows is the constitution's most extreme sanction — the people exercising their ultimate power against a citizen who would destroy their constitutional standing. It is constitutionally legal but politically catastrophic, demonstrating that the constitution's procedures can produce outcomes that threaten the constitution's survival.

### The Constitution's Limits (Acts 4–5)

Coriolanus's Volscian defection exposes the constitution's territorial limit. The Roman constitution governs within the *pomerium* — the sacred boundary of the city — but has no authority over what happens outside it. Coriolanus's exile transforms him from a constitutional actor into an external threat, and the constitution's mechanisms — debate, election, veto — are useless against an army at the gates.

## Relationship to Other Concepts

- **Tribunate**: The democratic element of the mixed constitution. Without the tribunate, the constitution would be a pure aristocracy.

- **Patrician Power**: The aristocratic element. The Senate's authority depends on the constitution's continued functioning; when the constitution breaks down, senatorial authority becomes merely the power of the wealthy.

- **Plebeian Rights**: The substantive content the democratic element exists to protect. The constitution is a framework; plebeian rights are what the framework secures.

- **Class Conflict**: The social dynamic the constitution is designed to manage. The mixed constitution channels class conflict into institutional procedures — elections, vetoes, assemblies — rather than allowing it to become civil war.

- **Consulship**: The monarchical element. The consul's *imperium* is the constitution's concentrated executive power; Coriolanus's pursuit of it and the tribunes' obstruction of it dramatize the tension between monarchical and democratic principles.

## Critical Perspectives

The Roman constitution in *Coriolanus* has been read as an allegory of Jacobean English constitutional conflicts. James I's struggles with Parliament — particularly the 1610 "Great Contract" negotiations and the 1614 "Addled Parliament" — provide a contemporary subtext for the play's dramatization of executive-popular tension. The tribunes' procedural obstruction of the consular election mirrors parliamentary tactics of delaying supply and attaching conditions to revenue bills.

Republican interpretations — from Machiavelli's *Discourses on Livy* (which Shakespeare likely knew through contemporary translations) through Pocock's *Machiavellian Moment* (1975) — have seen the mixed constitution as the play's normative frame. On this reading, the tragedy is not Coriolanus's alone but Rome's: the failure of the mixed constitution to accommodate a warrior of Coriolanus's greatness reveals a structural flaw in republican governance itself.
