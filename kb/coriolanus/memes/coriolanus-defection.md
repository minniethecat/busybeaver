# Defection to the Volscians

## Event Summary


### Analysis

Following his banishment from Rome, Coriolanus journeys to Antium — the capital of his sworn enemies, the Volscians — and, in disguise, presents himself at the house of Tullus Aufidius (Act IV, Scene V). He declares his identity and offers his military service to the man he has defeated five times in single combat. Aufidius, after an initial shock, embraces him with genuine passion: "Know thou first, / I loved the maid I married; never man / Sighed truer breath; but that I see thee here, / Thou noble thing, more dances my rapt heart / Than when I first my wedded mistress saw / Bestride my threshold" (022-002). The defection transforms the political geometry of the play, converting Rome's greatest warrior into its most dangerous enemy.

## The Journey to Antium

Shakespeare provides no transition scene for Coriolanus's journey. One moment he exits the gates of Rome, declaring "I turn my back: / There is a world elsewhere" (020-002); the next, he appears in Antium "in mean apparel, disguised and muffled" (022-001). The ellipsis is deliberate: the man who has defined himself through Roman identity ceases to exist between the city's walls and Aufidius's threshold.

His arrival at Aufidius's house is structured as a test of identity. The Servingmen refuse to admit him, treating him as a vagrant. Coriolanus, who once commanded armies, must now endure the contempt of servants — a humiliation that echoes his earlier refusal to display his wounds to the people. The scene's comedy of mistaken identity — "A marvellous poor one," a Servingman says of his guest (022-002) — underscores the radical stripping of status that banishment has effected.

## The Embrace of Enemies

Aufidius's reception of Coriolanus is the play's most intense moment of homosocial bonding. His speech — comparing his joy at Coriolanus's arrival to that of his wedding night — exceeds the rhetorical conventions of military alliance. The homoerotic charge is unmistakable and deliberate: Shakespeare emphasizes that the bond between these warriors is more passionate than any marriage in the play.

Aufidius offers Coriolanus half his army and a plan: "Let me twine / Mine arms about that body, where against / My grained ash an hundred times hath broke / And scarred the moon with splinters" (022-002). The image of the spear-shattered ash against Coriolanus's body, now replaced by an embrace, captures the transformation of enmity into alliance with visceral precision.

## The Political Calculus

Coriolanus's defection is both personal and strategic. He does not simply seek refuge; he offers to serve as Aufidius's instrument of revenge against Rome. His motivation, characteristically, is stated without diplomacy: "I do beseech you, / ... / That you ... / Shall see me ... / ... to do / An ample satisfaction to the state" (022-002). Aufidius, ever the strategist, recognizes the military value of Coriolanus's knowledge of Roman defenses and his demoralizing effect on Roman troops.

Yet Aufidius's embrace is not pure calculation. The speech reveals genuine admiration, even love, for the warrior who has bested him. The tragedy's subsequent action — Aufidius's growing resentment as Coriolanus's popularity eclipses his own among the Volscian soldiers (023-002) — is the inevitable consequence of an alliance founded on such volatile passion.

## Dramatic Significance

The defection is the structural hinge of the play. It converts Coriolanus from victim to aggressor, from the banished hero of Act IV Scene I to the besieging enemy of Act V. It also introduces the fatal irony that will drive the conclusion: in seeking revenge against Rome, Coriolanus places himself in the power of a man whose admiration is indistinguishable from envy, and whose embrace is merely the prelude to the knife.

## Participants

- **Caius Marcius Coriolanus** — the defector, stripped of name and status, offering his sword to his enemy. His disguise and the servants' mockery dramatize his loss of identity.

- **Tullus Aufidius** — the host whose passionate welcome masks the strategic calculation that will later justify Coriolanus's murder.

- **Servingmen** — the household servants whose comic banter provides the scene's ironic counterpoint. Their incomprehension of the great warrior in beggar's clothes mirrors Rome's incomprehension of what it has discarded.
