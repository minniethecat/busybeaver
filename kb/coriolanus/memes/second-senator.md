# Second Senator

The **Second Senator** is a supporting senatorial figure appearing in both the Roman and [[volscians|Volsci]]an political worlds of Shakespeare's *[[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]*. In [[volscians|Volsci]]an scenes, the Second Senator plays a practical military-administrative role; in the play's final act, he serves as a guard at the [[volscians|Volsci]]an gates, intercepting [[menenius-agrippa|Menenius]]'s plea to [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]].

## Volscian War Council

In the [[volscians|Volsci]]an war council (Act I, Scene II), the Second Senator delivers orders to Aufidius with brisk efficiency: "Noble Aufidius, Take your commission; hie you to your bands: Let us alone to guard [[corioli|Corioli]]" (007-002). This brief speech establishes the division of [[volscians|Volsci]]an military command — Aufidius leads the field army while the senators hold [[rome|the city]]. His terse farewell — "Farewell" (007-002) — completes the transaction: the senators are administrators, not warriors, and their speech is correspondingly direct.

## The Gate Scene (Act V)

The Second Senator's most dramatic and extended appearance occurs in Act V, Scene II, where he guards the gates of [[corioli|Corioli]] as [[menenius-agrippa|Menenius]] attempts to reach [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] and plead for [[rome|Rome]]. This scene is one of the play's most darkly comic episodes, and the Second Senator's deadpan dialogue with the Watch provides its sardonic frame.

### The Confrontation

When [[menenius-agrippa|Menenius]] approaches the gates, the Second Senator's command is immediate and absolute:

> Stand, and go back. (032-002)

[[menenius-agrippa|Menenius]], with characteristic self-importance, insists that [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] will know him. The Second Senator's reply is withering in its matter-of-fact brutality:

> You'll see your Rome embraced with fire before You'll speak with Coriolanus. (032-002)

The image of [[rome|Rome]] "embraced with fire" — domestic destruction rendered in the language of a lover's clasp — is the play's compressed vision of what is at stake. The Senator speaks not as an enemy gloating but as a functionary stating a fact.

### The Gatekeeper's Irony

Despite [[menenius-agrippa|Menenius]]'s protests and his claim to be [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s "liar" (a term meaning intimate friend, but with a sardonic edge the Senator does not miss), the Second Senator remains unmoved: "Howsoever you have been his liar, as you say you have, I am one that, telling true under him, must say, you cannot pass. Therefore, go back" (032-002).

After [[menenius-agrippa|Menenius]] departs in despair, the Second Senator's commentary to the Watch reveals a dry, almost theatrical irony:

> 'Tis a spell, you see, of much power: you know the way home again. (032-002)

"Spell" is sardonic — [[menenius-agrippa|Menenius]]'s words have no magical power over the Senator, whatever power they may once have held over [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]. When the Watch asks if the Senator is moved to sympathy, he replies with the play's most crystalline description of [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s character:

> The worthy fellow is our general: he's the rock, the oak not to be wind-shaken. (032-002)

The simile is double-edged. "Rock" and "oak" bespeak strength, but "not to be wind-shaken" implies an inflexibility indistinguishable from inhumanity. The Second Senator — a minor functionary — delivers one of the play's definitive judgments on its protagonist, and does so without apparent awareness of its profundity.

## Structural Parallelism

The Second Senator's gatekeeping function in Act V echoes the gate scenes at [[corioli|Corioli]] in Act I. Where the gates of [[corioli|Corioli]] were once breached by [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Marcius]]'s heroic solo assault, they are now guarded by a [[volscians|Volsci]]an senator against a Roman embassy. The reversal is complete: the Roman hero who took the gates now stands behind them, and the Roman patrician who once commanded [[rome|the city]] now cannot pass its threshold. The Second Senator is the instrument of this bitter symmetry.

## In Rome

In the Roman forum (Act III, Scene I), a Second Senator briefly appears during the confrontation between [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] and the tribunes, uttering only the command: "Get you gone" (022-003). This fleeting appearance — three words, no more — reinforces the Second Senator's function as a voice of institutional authority, whether Roman or [[volscians|Volsci]]an.
