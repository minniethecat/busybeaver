# Pride

## Definition

**Pride** in *Coriolanus* is the protagonist's defining characteristic — the quality that makes him Rome's greatest soldier and its most politically catastrophic citizen. Coriolanus's pride is not vanity or narcissism but a principled conviction of his own superiority, grounded in demonstrated martial excellence and reinforced by his mother's lifelong cultivation of the warrior identity.

Shakespeare presents pride as a genuinely tragic flaw (*hamartia*): it is inseparable from Coriolanus's greatness, and to cure it would be to destroy the hero. The play does not invite us to condescend to Coriolanus's pride but to recognize its structural necessity — this is who he is, and the story can end no other way.

## Historical Background

Classical ethics distinguished between legitimate and illegitimate pride. Aristotle's *megalopsychos* (great-souled man) properly recognizes his own worth and claims the honour due to him — this is virtue, not vice. Christian ethics, by contrast, identified *superbia* as the root of all sin: the creature's refusal to acknowledge its dependence on the Creator. Coriolanus inhabits both frameworks — his pride is Aristotelian in its grounding (he *has* done what he claims, he *is* who he says he is) and Christian in its consequences (it destroys him).

## In the Play

Coriolanus's pride manifests in his refusal to perform the rituals of political humility required by the Roman constitution. He will not display his wounds to the people (013-002), will not speak softly to those he considers his inferiors, and will not pretend that the citizens' voices matter to him. This is not stubbornness but integrity — the logical extension of a value system that measures worth by deeds, not words.

The tragedy is that this integrity, admirable on its own terms, is politically suicidal. The republican constitution requires its magistrates to treat plebeian consent as meaningful, even if they privately despise it. Coriolanus cannot sustain the performance, and his collapse — "I banish you!" (016-002) — is the moment when personal pride overwhelms constitutional order.

## Relationship to Other Concepts

- **Military Honor**: Pride is the subjective experience of military honour — the warrior's inner conviction of his own worth, confirmed by wounds and victory.
- **Patrician Power**: Aristocratic pride naturalizes patrician dominance. Coriolanus despises the plebeians not because he dislikes them but because his entire worldview defines them as politically negligible.
- **Exile**: Pride makes exile survivable ("There is a world elsewhere") and fatal (the exile cannot accept obscurity and must return as a conqueror).

## Critical Perspectives

A. C. Bradley (1912) identified pride as Coriolanus's tragic flaw but emphasized its positive dimension: "His pride is not a petty self-conceit; it is the pride of a man who knows his own worth." Later critics have probed the play's ambivalence about pride more deeply: does Shakespeare endorse the aristocratic value system that makes pride a virtue, or does he anatomize its destructive consequences?
