# Volscian Declaration of War

The **Volscian Declaration of War** is the political-military event in Act I, Scene I of *Coriolanus* in which the Roman Senate receives news that the Volsces have taken up arms against Rome. This announcement transforms the play's domestic class conflict into a military crisis and launches the campaign that will make Caius Marcius a hero.

## The Announcement

As Marcius finishes his contemptuous dismissal of the plebeians and their newly granted tribunes, a Messenger enters "hastily" (012-002) with urgent news:

> "The news is, sir, the Volsces are in arms." (012-002)

Marcius's response is characteristically combative — and revealing. Rather than alarm, he expresses satisfaction:

> "I am glad on't: then we shall ha' means to vent / Our musty superfluity." (012-002)

This line encapsulates Marcius's worldview: war is not a calamity but an opportunity — a way to channel the plebeians' dangerous energies outward against a foreign enemy rather than inward against the patrician order. The phrase "musty superfluity" reduces human beings to excess inventory, a metaphor drawn from grain storage that echoes the corn riot that has just concluded. The plebeians are not citizens but surplus — a problem to be managed, not a population to be represented.

The timing of the war declaration is dramaturgically precise. It arrives at the exact moment when the domestic political settlement — the creation of the tribunate — has been reached, as if the external world intrudes to deny the satisfaction of resolution. The play moves without pause from internal strife to external conflict, suggesting that for Rome, war is not an interruption of normal politics but its continuation by other means.

## The Senate's Response

The Senate convenes immediately. Senators arrive alongside Cominius (the consul), Titus Lartius, and — significantly — the newly elected tribunes Sicinius Velutus and Junius Brutus. The integration of the tribunes into this war council demonstrates how swiftly the tribunate has become part of Rome's political machinery.

First Senator confirms the intelligence:

> "Marcius, 'tis true that you have lately told us; / The Volsces are in arms." (012-002)

The First Senator's address to "Marcius" — not "Caius Marcius" or "general" — indicates Marcius's informal preeminence even before his formal elevation. The Senate looks to him for military judgment, a pattern that will culminate in the consular election of Act II.

## Tullus Aufidius: The Worthy Enemy

Marcius reveals his peculiar relationship with the Volscian general Tullus Aufidius — a mixture of envy, admiration, and competitive obsession:

> "They have a leader, / Tullus Aufidius, that will put you to't. / I sin in envying his nobility, / And were I any thing but what I am, / I would wish me only he." (012-002)

The verb "sin" is startling. Marcius, who rarely acknowledges any moral framework beyond martial honor, frames his envy of Aufidius as a transgression. This admission of an emotional bond across enemy lines introduces a homoerotic subtext to the military rivalry — a dynamic that Aufidius will later reciprocate when he welcomes Coriolanus to Antium.

When Cominius observes "You have fought together," Marcius replies with a remarkable confession:

> "Were half to half the world by the ears and he / Upon my party, I'ld revolt to make / Only my wars with him: he is a lion / That I am proud to hunt." (013-002)

This is one of the most extraordinary declarations in the play. Marcius claims he would betray his own side — "revolt" — not for any political or material gain but for the sheer pleasure of fighting Aufidius. The hunting metaphor ("a lion / That I am proud to hunt") transforms warfare from a matter of state policy into a personal sport. It also foreshadows, with grim irony, Marcius's actual defection: when he later "revolts" to join the Volscians, it is not to fight Aufidius but to fight beside him — a reversal that Aufidius will ultimately exploit.

## Military Dispositions

The Senate appoints Cominius as the commanding general and Marcius as his principal lieutenant:

> "Then, worthy Marcius, / Attend upon Cominius to these wars." (013-002)

The hierarchy is significant. Marcius, for all his martial prowess, serves under Cominius — a subordination that the consular election will formalize politically. The tension between Marcius's extraordinary military gifts and his subordinate institutional position is a constant source of friction throughout the play.

Titus Lartius, despite being physically infirm ("I'll lean upon one crutch and fight with t' other"), insists on joining the campaign, demonstrating the Roman aristocratic commitment to military service. His determination provides a comic note that also serves as a measure of Roman martial values: even the disabled nobleman insists on fighting.

Marcius, with characteristic contempt for the plebeians, suggests they be brought along:

> "Nay, let them follow: / The Volsces have much corn; take these rats thither / To gnaw their garners." (014-002)

The "rats" metaphor extends the dehumanisation of the plebeians that Marcius began with "musty superfluity." The image of rats gnawing at grain stores is particularly pointed: the plebeians who rioted over corn shortages are now imagined as vermin consuming Volscian grain. For Marcius, the plebeians are not soldiers but scavengers — and their hunger is not a legitimate need but an animal appetite.

## Political Undercurrents

As the scene closes and all others exit, Brutus and Sicinius remain to discuss their new enemy. Their exchange is the first instance of the conspiratorial conversations that will become their signature mode:

> Sicinius: "Was ever man so proud as is this Marcius?"
> Brutus: "He has no equal." (015-002)

They recognize that the war presents both danger and opportunity. If Marcius falls in battle, their political problem is solved. If he triumphs, his prestige — already formidable — will make him politically unassailable. Brutus observes:

> "Such a nature, / Tickled with good success, disdains the shadow / Which he treads on at noon." (015-002)

This psychological analysis is perceptive. The tribunes understand that Marcius's pride, combined with military success, will make him contemptuous of the very political rituals — canvassing, humility, the display of wounds — that Roman custom requires. They are, in effect, predicting the consular election crisis of Act II before Marcius has even departed for war.

## Strategic and Structural Significance

The Volscian declaration of war serves multiple structural functions in the play:

1. **Pivots from domestic to military action**: The shift from the corn riot and tribunate creation to military mobilization demonstrates the play's characteristic alternation between political and martial registers.
2. **Introduces the Aufidius rivalry**: The Marcius-Aufidius dynamic, established here through report rather than action, will dominate the play's second half.
3. **Demonstrates Marcius's dual nature**: His eagerness for war reveals both his martial virtue (the quality Rome most values) and his political limitation (the quality that will destroy him).
4. **Establishes the tribunes as observers and plotters**: Their first private conversation sets the pattern for their role throughout the play — watching, calculating, and waiting for Marcius to destroy himself.
