# Sacrifice

## Definition

**Sacrifice** — the surrender of self for a cause greater than self — is a recurring motif in *Coriolanus*, taking military, political, and familial forms. Coriolanus offers his body to Rome at Corioli; Rome sacrifices its greatest soldier to political expediency; Volumnia sacrifices her life's project to save the city. Each sacrifice transforms its participants, and none restores the order it was meant to preserve.

## Historical Background

Roman *devotio* was the ritualized self-sacrifice of a commander who, seeing his army failing, would vow his life to the gods of the underworld (*Di Manes*) and charge into the enemy ranks. His death purchased divine favour and military victory. This fusion of religious ritual and martial sacrifice was among the most admired acts in Roman culture.

## In the Play

Coriolanus's entry into Corioli alone is a form of secular *devotio* — the warrior offering himself to death for victory. Volumnia's final intervention before the walls of Rome in Act 5 is a different kind of sacrifice: she surrenders her son's martial honour — the quality she spent her life cultivating — to save the city that banished him. Both sacrifices succeed and fail simultaneously.

## Relationship to Other Concepts

- **Military Honor**: Military sacrifice is the ultimate proof of honour. Coriolanus's wounds are the visible record of his willingness to sacrifice his body for Rome.
- **Loyalty**: Sacrifice is loyalty's extreme expression — the surrender of self for the object of allegiance.
- **Pride**: Pride resists sacrifice; Coriolanus's inability to sacrifice his pride for political accommodation is his fatal flaw.
- **Virtus**: The Roman concept of *virtus* includes the willingness to sacrifice — to place the common good above personal survival.

## Critical Perspectives

The motif of sacrifice in *Coriolanus* has been read psychoanalytically (Coriolanus's self-sacrifice as a form of self-destruction driven by maternal demands) and politically (sacrifice as the ultimate claim to authority, appropriated by both Coriolanus and the tribunes in competing bids for legitimacy).
