# Rome

## Overview

**Rome** in Shakespeare's *Coriolanus* is far more than a setting — it is a political organism whose institutions, spaces, and social dynamics constitute the play's central antagonist. Unlike the Rome of *Julius Caesar* (imposing imperial grandeur) or *Titus Andronicus* (decadent corruption), Coriolanus's Rome is a *republican* city-state, defined by the [[tribunate|constitutional order]] that both enables and destroys its greatest soldier.

The city's political geography is precisely mapped in the play's dramaturgy: the **[[the-senate-house|Senate House]]** represents patrician authority, the **[[the-forum|Forum]]** is the arena of plebeian power, and the **[[the-capitol|Capitol]]** symbolizes the sacred centre of Roman identity. [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] moves through these spaces with increasing friction, his martial body — marked by twenty-seven wounds — unable to accommodate itself to the political body of the city.

> What's the matter, you dissentious rogues,
> That, rubbing the poor itch of your opinion,
> Make yourselves scabs? (001-001)

## Senate vs. Forum: The Two Romes

Coriolanus's Rome is a city divided against itself. The **Senate** embodies the patrician values of [[military-honor|honour]], lineage, and martial [[virtus|virtue]]; the **Forum** embodies the plebeian values of [[plebeian-rights|representation]], material welfare, and the [[tribunate|tribunician constitution]]. These are not merely different interests — they are different *languages*, and the tragedy of the play is in part the impossibility of translation between them.

The [[tribunate|tribunes]], created after the [[first-secession|First Secession]], are the constitutional embodiment of this division: elected by the plebeians, immune from patrician prosecution, holding the power of veto over senatorial actions. [[sicinius-velutus|Sicinius]] and [[junius-brutus|Brutus]] exploit these powers with considerable political skill, transforming Coriolanus's personal arrogance into a constitutional crisis.

## The Body Politic

Shakespeare deploys the classical metaphor of the **body politic** throughout the play — most famously in [[menenius-agrippa|Menenius's]] belly fable (001-002). Rome is imagined as a body whose health depends on the cooperation of all members, but this organic metaphor is repeatedly undercut by the reality of class antagonism:

- The plebeians are "the mutinous members" — necessary for the body's survival but treated with contempt
- The Senate is "the belly" — the distributing centre that is accused of hoarding
- [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] is simultaneously Rome's sword-arm (its military defender) and its disease (a "gangren'd foot" that must be amputated)

This imagery culminates in the play's central paradox: Rome must expel Coriolanus to survive politically, but cannot survive militarily without him. The city's constitution is its strength and its vulnerability.

## Historical Rome in the Play

Shakespeare's Rome is anachronistic in historically productive ways. The historical Caius Marcius Coriolanus lived in the early 5th century BCE, shortly after the foundation of the Republic. The institutions Shakespeare depicts — the tribunate, the consular elections, the complex voting procedures — reflect a developed republican constitution that would not have existed in so elaborate a form at the time.

But Shakespeare's anachronism serves a dramatic purpose: it creates a Rome where the tension between [[patrician-power|aristocratic military excellence]] and [[plebeian-rights|democratic accountability]] is fully institutionalized. This is not historical reconstruction but political philosophy in dramatic form.

## Rome as Tragic Architect

Rome does not simply witness Coriolanus's tragedy — it produces it. The same republican institutions that recognize martial excellence (the triumph, the consulship) also demand political performances he cannot give (the display of wounds, the solicitation of voices). The same city that needs his sword cannot tolerate his contempt.

When Coriolanus declares "I banish you! There is a world elsewhere" (016-002), he speaks a profound untruth. For a Roman of his class and formation, there is no "elsewhere" — only Rome and its negation. His defection to the Volscians confirms rather than escapes this logic: he remains defined by Rome even as he moves against it.
