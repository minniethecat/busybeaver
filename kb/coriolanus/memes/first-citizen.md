# First Citizen

## Overview

The First Citizen is the unnamed leader of the mutinous Roman populace who open *[[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]* with an armed rebellion against the patrician class. He serves as the dramatic voice of plebeian grievance, articulating the famine-driven anger that drives the play's central class conflict. His opening speech sets the political stakes: the people are starving while the patricians hoard grain, and [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Caius Marcius]] is identified as their chief enemy. Though he disappears after the first scene, his grievances echo throughout the play's subsequent political confrontations.

## Role in the Play

### The Opening Rebellion (000-110)

The First Citizen leads the opening scene with authority, establishing both the dramatic tension and the economic logic of the class conflict. He commands the crowd: "Before we proceed any further, hear me speak" (000-006) and polls them on their resolve: "You are all resolved rather to die than to famish?" (000-008). His rhetoric is direct and forceful — he names [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] as "chief enemy to the people" (000-011) and proposes assassination: "Let us kill him, and we'll have corn at our own price" (000-013).

When the [[second-citizen|Second Citizen]] urges caution — questioning whether [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s actions are driven by obedience to his mother or personal pride — the First Citizen delivers the play's most concentrated critique of patrician governance. He argues that the patricians' surplus is built on plebeian suffering: "the leanness that afflicts us, the object of our misery, is as an inventory to particularize their abundance; our sufferance is a gain to them" (000-016). This speech establishes the zero-sum economic logic that underlies the entire tragedy: patrician wealth and plebeian suffering are indissolubly linked, one the direct measure of the other.

The First Citizen's rhetorical skill is notable. He deploys a series of rhetorical questions, parallel structures, and vivid contrasts to marshal his argument. His language is concrete — "corn," "leanness," "misery" — rather than abstract, grounding the political dispute in bodily need. This makes him a more sympathetic figure than the tribunes who later manipulate the people for political advantage.

### The Belly Fable

The First Citizen's interaction with [[menenius-agrippa|Menenius Agrippa]] is his most substantial scene and one of the play's key ideological confrontations. When [[menenius-agrippa|Menenius]] enters, the [[second-citizen|Second Citizen]] identifies him as "one that hath always loved the people" (000-028), but the First Citizen responds with measured skepticism: "He's one honest enough: would all the rest were so!" (000-029).

[[menenius-agrippa|Menenius]] then delivers his famous fable of the belly and members — a parable in which the body's other members rebel against the belly, accusing it of idly receiving all the nourishment while they labor. The belly responds that it distributes the food back to all the members through the blood, just as the patrician [[roman-senate|Senate]] distributes the commonwealth's benefits to the people. The fable is [[menenius-agrippa|Menenius]]'s defense of patrician rule and a refutation of the First Citizen's zero-sum economics.

The First Citizen's response to the fable is revealing. He engages with the analogy on its own terms, asking: "Your belly's answer? What! / The kingly-crowned head, the vigilant eye, / The counsellor heart, the arm our soldier, / Our steed the leg, the tongue our trumpeter" (000-033). His counter-metaphor — attributing military and civic functions to different body parts — implicitly challenges [[menenius-agrippa|Menenius]]'s simplifying analogy. Yet the play does not resolve whether the First Citizen is convinced by the fable; the arrival of [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] interrupts the debate, and the First Citizen's political argument is displaced by martial confrontation.

## Dramatic Significance

The First Citizen represents the collective voice of the Roman *plebs* in a way that the tribunes Sicinius and Brutus do not. Where the tribunes are political operatives who calculate and scheme, the First Citizen speaks from genuine [[famine|hunger]] and grievance. His disappearance after Act I, Scene 1 reflects the play's shift from plebeian collective action to the political machinations of the tribunes — a shift that marks the tragedy's movement from social drama to personal conflict.

Critics have noted that Shakespeare gives the First Citizen some of the most persuasive language in the opening scene. His arguments carry weight precisely because they are grounded in material need rather than political ambition. This generosity toward the plebeian perspective distinguishes *[[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]* from a simple anti-populist polemic, even though the play's overall sympathies are complex and ambiguous.

## Historical Background

In Shakespeare's source (Plutarch's *Lives*), the First Citizen is a composite figure representing the plebeian secession — a historical event in which the Roman commoners withdrew their labor and threatened to found a rival city unless granted political representation. The historical grain shortage of 492–488 BCE underpins the opening scene's famine rhetoric, though Shakespeare compresses and dramatizes these events for theatrical effect.

The First Citizen's argument — that the patricians hoard grain at the people's expense — reflects real tensions in the early [[rome|Roman Republic]], where control of the grain supply was a central instrument of patrician power. The creation of the tribunate (represented in the play by Sicinius and Brutus) was one outcome of these [[class-conflict|class struggle]]s, though Shakespeare's play is deeply skeptical about whether the tribunes represent a genuine solution to plebeian grievances.
