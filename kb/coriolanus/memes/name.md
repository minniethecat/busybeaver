## Definition

The **name** "Coriolanus" is the play's central symbolic transaction. Caius Marcius enters the Volscian city of Corioli as a nameless soldier and emerges as "Coriolanus" — a man whose identity is fused with the place of his greatest deed. The name is both reward and burden: it confers immortal fame while binding the hero to a public identity he can never shed.

Naming in *Coriolanus* is never innocent. It is a political act, a conferral of honour, and a reduction of the self to a single, legible meaning. The play traces the arc from naming to unnaming — from "Welcome to Rome, renowned Coriolanus!" (011-005) to "kill, kill, kill, kill, kill him" (016-002), where the name is replaced by the deed of its destruction.

## Historical Background

Roman naming practice was deeply significant. The *tria nomina* system (praenomen, nomen, cognomen) encoded family, clan, and personal identity. A *cognomen ex virtute* — a name earned through military achievement — was the highest honour a Roman could receive. Caius Marcius's transformation into "Coriolanus" follows this pattern exactly, and Shakespeare's audience would have understood the weight of the moment when the Herald declares "With fame, a name to Caius Marcius Coriolanus" (011-005).

## In the Play

The name is first spoken before it is official. Lartius, in the aftermath of Corioli, calls him "thou worthiest Marcius" (005-002), and Cominius prepares to formalise the honour. But the most revealing moment comes from Lartius's question — "Marcius, his name?" (009-002) — which treats the name as something to be discovered rather than given. The name is already present in the deed; official recognition merely confirms what battle has revealed.

The Herald's declaration makes the naming public and political: "Know, Rome, that all alone Marcius did fight / Within Corioli gates: where he hath won, / With fame, a name to Caius Marcius Coriolanus" (011-005). The response — "Welcome to Rome, renowned Coriolanus!" (011-005) — is a collective act of naming, the polity's ratification of martial achievement.

But the name also becomes a weapon against its bearer. The tribunes use "Coriolanus" as a target; the people's gratitude turns to resentment without the name changing. The hero who was named for destroying a city is destroyed in turn by the city that named him.

Aufidius's obsession with the name is particularly revealing: "Not Afric owns a serpent I abhor / More than thy fame and envy" (008-002). He covets the name, the fame, the renown — not the man. When he orchestrates Coriolanus's death, he is attempting to transfer the name's power to himself. It fails: the name "Coriolanus" survives its bearer.
