# Coriolanus Denounces the Tribunes

## Event Summary

In Act III, Scene I, the political conflict between Coriolanus and the tribunes reaches its flashpoint. When Sicinius Velutus and Junius Brutus attempt to block Coriolanus's path through the Forum, he erupts into a blistering denunciation of the tribunate as an institution. He calls the tribunes "the tongues o' the common mouth" (003-001) and argues that giving the people political power has made Rome ungovernable. The tribunes, who have been waiting for exactly this moment, declare his words treason and call for his arrest. The confrontation escalates into a physical scuffle in which Coriolanus draws his sword, and by the scene's end, the tribunes have won the political battle: Coriolanus has damned himself in public.

## The Outburst

Coriolanus's denunciation is not a calculated political move but a volcanic release of long-suppressed contempt. He begins by attacking the tribunes personally — "What do you prate of service? ... You are the railing, cowardly curs that hate the nobles" (003-001) — and rapidly escalates to a structural critique of the tribunate:

> "You are the tongues o' the common mouth. I do despise them. / For they do prank them in authority / Against all noble sufferance" (003-001)

The "tongues o' the common mouth" metaphor is characteristically bodily and dismissive: the tribunes are not legitimate representatives but mere organs of a single, undifferentiated mass. Coriolanus argues that the creation of the tribunate has inverted the natural order: "In soothing them, we nourish 'gainst our senate / The cockle of rebellion, insolence, sedition" (003-001).

## The Tribunes' Counterstroke

Brutus and Sicinius have been waiting for Coriolanus to incriminate himself, and they seize the moment with calculated precision. Brutus declares: "We have a power, and here it is our own: / In the name o' the people, and in the power of us / The tribunes, we arrest you as a traitor" (003-001). The word "traitor" transforms Coriolanus's political outburst into a legal charge. Sicinius calls for the aediles to seize him, and when Coriolanus resists with force — drawing his sword and driving back the officers — he confirms the tribunes' narrative: the man who would be consul is a man of violence who cannot be trusted with power.

## The Senate's Intervention

Menenius and the patricians attempt to de-escalate, but the damage is done. Menenius urges Coriolanus to "put your shield before your heart" (003-001), arguing that the tribunes have legal authority that cannot be defied with a sword. Coriolanus's response — "Let them pull all about my ears" (003-001) — is the purest expression of his tragic flaw: he would rather see Rome destroyed than compromise his principles. The senators extract Coriolanus from the Forum, but the tribunes have the material they need. The accusation of treason, once made, cannot be unmade.

## Dramatic Significance

This confrontation is the hinge on which the entire tragedy turns. Before it, Coriolanus is Rome's war hero, poised to become consul. After it, he is a traitor facing banishment or death. The scene demonstrates the incompatibility of Coriolanus's martial ethic with democratic politics: his inability to flatter, equivocate, or dissemble — virtues on the battlefield — are fatal defects in the Forum. The tribunes, by contrast, understand that in politics, timing is everything: they did not need to defeat Coriolanus in argument, only to provoke him into destroying himself.
