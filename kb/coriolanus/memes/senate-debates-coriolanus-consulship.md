# Senate Debates Coriolanus's Consulship

## Event Summary

The Senate debate over Coriolanus's consulship takes place in the Capitol (Act II, Scene II), marking the formal transition from military triumph to political candidacy. Two Officers prepare the chamber and discuss the candidates, noting Coriolanus's pride and his strained relationship with the common people. Menenius opens the session by calling upon Cominius to recount Coriolanus's battlefield deeds. Coriolanus, uncomfortable with public praise, leaves the chamber rather than hear his own exploits recited. Cominius delivers an extended eulogy of Coriolanus's military career, from his first battle at sixteen against Tarquin through the recent victory at Corioli. The Senate unanimously approves the consulship, but the tribunes Sicinius and Brutus remind all present that Coriolanus must still seek the people's voices in the Forum — a custom he deeply resents.

> The Senate, Coriolanus, are well pleased / To make thee consul.
> — Menenius, Act II, Scene II

## Prelude: The Officers' Discussion

Before the Senate convenes, two Officers laying cushions in the Capitol discuss the consulship candidates. They observe that three men stand for the office, but "’tis thought of every one Coriolanus will carry it." The First Officer acknowledges Coriolanus as "a brave fellow" but notes his fatal flaw — "he's vengeance proud, and loves not the common people."

The Second Officer offers a more nuanced view: "He hath deserved worthily of his country: and his ascent is not by such easy degrees as those who, having been supple and courteous to the people, bonneted, without any further deed to have them at all into their estimation and report." This exchange establishes the central tension of the consulship: Coriolanus has earned the office through merit, but his contempt for popular approval may undo him.

> Come, come, they are almost here. How many stand for consulships? / Three, they say: but 'tis thought of every one Coriolanus will carry it.
> — First and Second Officers, Act II, Scene II

## Menenius Opens the Session

Menenius formally opens the Senate session by declaring the purpose of the after-meeting: "To gratify his noble service that Hath thus stood for his country." He calls upon Cominius to speak to Coriolanus's deeds, framing the consulship as a reward for military valor. The First Senator reinforces this, instructing Cominius to "Leave nothing out for length, and make us think Rather our state's defective for requital Than we to stretch it out."

The tribunes Sicinius and Brutus, though present, offer guarded support. Sicinius acknowledges "hearts Inclinable to honour and advance The theme of our assembly," but Brutus adds the caveat: "Which the rather We shall be blest to do, if he remember A kinder value of the people than He hath hereto prized them at." Menenius cuts him off — "That's off, that's off" — foreshadowing the conflict between senatorial approval and popular ratification.

> Having determined of the Volsces and / To send for Titus Lartius, it remains, / As the main point of this our after-meeting, / To gratify his noble service that / Hath thus stood for his country.
> — Menenius, Act II, Scene II

## Coriolanus Departs

When called upon to hear his own praises, Coriolanus attempts to leave the chamber. The First Senator urges him to stay: "never shame to hear What you have nobly done." Coriolanus's reply reveals his paradoxical relationship with honor — he would rather suffer physical wounds again than receive verbal recognition:

> Your honours' pardon: / I had rather have my wounds to heal again / Than hear say how I got them.
> — Coriolanus, Act II, Scene II

When Brutus asks if his words "disbench'd" Coriolanus, the candidate responds with contempt for words themselves: "oft, When blows have made me stay, I fled from words." He exits, declaring he "had rather have one scratch my head i' the sun When the alarum were struck than idly sit To hear my nothings monster'd." This departure is both an act of modesty and a display of the pride that will later destroy him — he cannot tolerate even the forms of political life.

## Cominius's Eulogy

In Coriolanus's absence, Cominius delivers an extended account of his military career. He begins with Coriolanus's first battle at sixteen against Tarquin, "When with his Amazonian chin he drove The bristled lips before him," and recounts how the young Martius "be strid An o'er-press'd Roman and i' the consul's view Slew three opposers: Tarquin's self he met, And struck him on his knee." For this early valor, Martius "was brow-bound with the oak."

Cominius traces Coriolanus's career through "the brunt of seventeen battles since," culminating in the Corioli campaign. His description of Coriolanus at Corioli emphasizes the supernatural quality of his fighting: "his sword, death's stamp, Where it did mark, it took; from face to foot He was a thing of blood." Cominius describes how Coriolanus "alone he enter'd The mortal gate of the city," and after aidless came off, "with a sudden re-inforcement struck Corioli like a planet."

> I shall lack voice: the deeds of Coriolanus / Should not be utter'd feebly.
> — Cominius, Act II, Scene II

## The Senate's Decision and the People's Voice

Following Cominius's eulogy, Menenius declares: "He's right noble: Let him be call'd for." When Coriolanus returns, Menenius delivers the formal decision: "The senate, Coriolanus, are well pleased To make thee consul." The Senate's approval is unanimous — but incomplete.

Menenius then addresses the remaining requirement: "It then remains That you do speak to the people." Coriolanus immediately resists, asking to be excused from this custom:

> I do beseech you, / Let me o'erleap that custom, for I cannot / Put on the gown, stand naked and entreat them, / For my wounds' sake, to give their suffrage.
> — Coriolanus, Act II, Scene II

The tribunes insist the custom must be observed. Menenius urges him: "Put them not to't: Pray you, go fit you to the custom and Take to you, as your predecessors have, Your honour with your form." The Senate concludes with Menenius commending the tribunes "to recommend... Our purpose to them: and to our noble consul Wish we all joy and honour." But the tension between senatorial honor and popular ratification has been established, setting the stage for the Forum confrontation that follows.

## Dramatic Function

This scene serves as the hinge between Coriolanus's military triumph (Act I) and his political catastrophe (Act II, Scene III). The Senate's unanimous approval demonstrates that Coriolanus has earned the highest office through merit, yet his visceral rejection of the "custom" of seeking popular approval reveals the character flaw that will lead to his banishment. The scene also establishes the structural conflict between patrician and plebeian political authority — the Senate can name a consul, but only the people can confirm him.
