# First Plebeian Secession

## Event Summary

The First Secession of the Plebs (historically 494 BCE) is the foundational constitutional event of *Coriolanus*. Though it occurs before the play's action begins, it haunts every scene: the tribunate it created is the institution through which Sicinius and Brutus challenge patrician authority. The play opens with a near-repetition of the secession — a corn riot by starving plebeians — and Menenius Agrippa's famous "belly fable" speech, which retells the founding parable that resolved the original crisis. The first secession is thus both the play's political backstory and its recurring dramatic pattern: the plebeians' withdrawal of consent as the ultimate weapon against patrician rule.

## Historical Context

In 494 BCE, according to Livy's account, the plebeian class of early Republican Rome, burdened by debt and excluded from political power, withdrew en masse from the city to the Mons Sacer (the Sacred Mount), a hill outside Rome's boundaries. This "secession" — a collective strike of the common people — paralyzed the city's economy and left it defenseless against external enemies. The patricians, recognizing that the Roman state could not function without plebeian labor and military service, capitulated.

The settlement created the office of *tribunus plebis* — tribune of the people — an official with the power to veto patrician magistrates and whose person was declared *sacrosanctus* (inviolable). The tribunate thus embedded a plebeian veto inside the patrician constitution, a structural tension that Shakespeare dramatizes throughout the play.

## The Belly Fable (Act I, Scene I)

### The Speech

Facing an angry mob in the play's opening scene, Menenius Agrippa deploys the parable that, according to tradition, resolved the original secession. He tells the citizens that "the body's members / Rebell'd against the belly" (001-002), accusing it of idleness while the other organs labored. The belly's reply is the conservative constitutional argument in miniature:

> "True is it, my incorporate friends," quoth he,
> "That I receive the general food at first,
> Which you do live upon; and fit it is,
> Because I am the storehouse and the shop
> Of the whole body: but, if you do remember,
> I send it through the rivers of your blood,
> Even to the court, the heart, to the seat o' the brain" (001-002)

Menenius's application is direct: "The senators of Rome are this good belly, / And you the mutinous members" (001-002). The fable argues that the patriciate, far from a parasitic class, is the distributive center through which nourishment reaches every part of the body politic.

### Dramatic Irony

Shakespeare immediately undermines Menenius's parable. Coriolanus enters to deliver a tirade of unrestrained contempt for the very citizens Menenius has been attempting to pacify: "What would you have, you curs, / That like nor peace nor war?" (001-002). The contrast between Menenius's diplomatic persuasion and Coriolanus's naked hostility exposes the structural gap between patrician ideology (the belly as generous provider) and patrician practice (contempt for the "mutinous members"). The First Citizen registers this irony when he observes that Coriolanus "spoke in a kind of mockery" when soliciting voices before the election (013-002).

## The Tribunate as Constitutional Legacy

The tribunes Sicinius and Brutus are the living products of the first secession. Their office was "chosen tribunes for the people" (001-002) — a phrase that simultaneously acknowledges popular mandate and constitutional office. Throughout the play, they invoke the prerogatives that the secession won:

- **The Veto**: Their power to "revoke / Your ignorant election" (013-002) derives from the tribunician veto, the original constitutional guarantee
- **Sacrosanctity**: Their persons are inviolable — an attack on a tribune is an attack on the people themselves
- **Popular Assembly**: They can "assemble presently the people hither" (014-002) and direct the plebeian vote

The tribunes' weaponization of these constitutional tools against Coriolanus — a hero whose identity is entirely martial — exposes the paradox of the secession settlement: it created an office designed to check the very aristocratic excellence that Coriolanus embodies.

## Coriolanus's Counter-Vision

Coriolanus's response to the secession's legacy is total rejection. Where Menenius deploys the belly fable, Coriolanus advocates extermination: "Would the nobility lay aside their ruth, / And let me use my sword, I'ld make a quarry / With thousands of these quarter'd slaves" (001-002). His fury at the tribunician office — "Behold, these are the tribunes of the people, / The tongues o' the common mouth: I do despise them" (013-002) — is a rejection not merely of Sicinius and Brutus as individuals, but of the constitutional settlement that created their office. In Coriolanus's political theology, popular consent is a wound more grievous than any he received at Corioli.

The announcement that the plebeians' other demand — corn at reasonable prices — has been granted provokes Coriolanus to fresh contempt: "They said they were an-hungry; sigh'd forth proverbs... / and a petition granted them, a strange one — / To break the heart of generosity, / And make bold power look pale" (001-002). Granting plebeian petitions, in his view, "breaks the heart of generosity" — it transforms aristocratic benevolence into a right, and rights are the death of aristocratic identity.

## Dramatic Significance

The first secession functions in *Coriolanus* not as a plot event but as a structural principle. The entire play can be read as a series of secessions — withdrawals, exiles, and defections that mirror the original plebeian withdrawal:

1. **The Corn Riot** (Act I, Scene I): A near-repetition of the original secession, quelled by Menenius's fable and the concession of tribunician demands
2. **Coriolanus's Banishment** (Act III): The plebeians' enforcement of their secession-won power — exile of a patrician who refuses the constitutional order
3. **Coriolanus's Defection** (Act IV): An ironic inversion — the aristocratic hero himself becomes a secessionist, withdrawing from Rome to join its enemies
4. **Volumnia's Intervention** (Act V): The resolution — a feminine intercession that succeeds where both the belly fable and martial force failed

Each of these events replays the secession pattern: withdrawal → crisis → resolution (or catastrophe). Shakespeare thus constructs the first secession not as historical reference but as dramatic DNA — the pattern that generates the play's entire action.

## Aftermath and Consequences

The first secession's most significant consequence is the tribunate itself — an institution that transforms Roman politics from aristocratic consensus into class competition conducted through constitutional forms. In *Coriolanus*, this transformation reaches its crisis point: the tribunician veto, designed to protect plebeian interests, becomes a weapon of political destruction, used to engineer the banishment of Rome's greatest soldier.

The deeper consequence is cultural. The secession established the principle that popular consent could be withdrawn — that the plebeian body could refuse to cooperate with the patrician regime. Coriolanus, who cannot imagine a world in which martial merit requires popular ratification, is destroyed by this principle. His tragedy is that he is a warrior born into a constitutional order that has made warrior values insufficient for political legitimacy.
