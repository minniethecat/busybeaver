# Death of Coriolanus

## Event Summary


### Analysis

Coriolanus is assassinated in the marketplace of Antium (Act V, Scene VI) by conspirators loyal to Tullus Aufidius. After sparing Rome at his mother's plea, he returns to the Volscian capital to defend his treaty. Aufidius, who has already prepared "three or four Conspirators of Aufidius' faction" (029-002), publicly provokes him by calling him "boy" — a calculated insult that strips Coriolanus of his adult martial identity. Coriolanus, unable to bear the diminution, erupts in a final rage that gives the conspirators their pretext to strike. His death is the direct consequence of his defining character trait: the inability to compromise pride for survival.

## The Provocation: "Boy"

Aufidius's strategy is psychological demolition before physical assassination. He accuses Coriolanus of betraying the Volscian cause — "he whined and roared away your victory" (029-002) — and then deploys the word calculated to undo him:

> "Name not the god, thou boy of tears" (029-002)

Coriolanus's response is instantaneous and catastrophic. He cannot let the insult stand; he must reassert his identity through martial boast:

> "Cut me to pieces, Volsces; men and lads, / Stain all your edges on me. Boy! False hound! / If you have writ your annals true, 'tis there, / That, like an eagle in a dove-cote, I / Fluttered your Volscians in Corioli: / Alone I did it. Boy!" (029-002)

The repetition of "Boy!" — now his own word, thrown back in fury — reveals the depth of the wound. The boast of Corioli, the crowning achievement of his life, becomes in this moment not a defense but a provocation to the conspirators who have already resolved to kill him.

## The Assassination

The conspirators strike during Coriolanus's tirade. Aufidius's preparation has been meticulous — the conspirators were recruited before Coriolanus's return, and the public setting ensures the killing can be framed as a response to treason. The Lord of Antium's pronouncement — "Bear from hence his body; / And mourn you for him: let him be regarded / As the most noble corse that ever herald / Did follow to his urn" (029-002) — attempts to restore dignity to the dead man, but the irony is bitter: the same Volscian lords who applauded the treaty now sanction his murder.

Aufidius's final speech enacts a rapid emotional shift — from rage ("My rage is gone / And I am struck with sorrow," 029-002) to a plea for shared mourning to a defiant insistence that "he shall have a noble memory" (029-002). This oscillation between triumph and grief captures the ambivalence at the heart of the tragedy: Aufidius has won, but in destroying his greatest rival he has destroyed the only man whose worth could measure his own.

## Dramatic Structure

The death of Coriolanus is structured as a mirror of the play's opening. Where Act I Scene I showed Coriolanus facing down a mutinous crowd with contemptuous invective, Act V Scene VI shows him facing down a hostile crowd with the same weapon — words of martial pride — only to find that words, however magnificent, cannot substitute for political instinct. The symmetry is exact: the play opens with citizens calling for his death ("Let us kill him, and we'll have corn at our own price," 001-002); it closes with conspirators acting on that same logic, killing the man who could not bend.

## Participants

- **Caius Marcius Coriolanus** — the victim, undone by his own refusal to accept insult. His final speech is both a magnificent assertion of martial identity and the fatal error that triggers the assassination.

- **Tullus Aufidius** — the architect of the murder. His tactical genius, demonstrated at Corioli, is here applied to a political assassination. The personal hatred that has animated him since his defeat in single combat finds its consummation.

- **Conspirators with Aufidius** — the "three or four" men (029-002) who carry out the assassination. They are Aufidius's instruments, recruited in advance, waiting for their cue.

- **Lords of Antium / Volscians** — the civic audience before whom the assassination is staged. Their presence legitimizes the killing as a public execution rather than a private murder.

## Critical Significance

Coriolanus's death is unusual in Shakespearean tragedy. Unlike Hamlet, who achieves a kind of resolution before dying, or Macbeth, who faces his end with desperate courage, Coriolanus dies in the grip of the same rage that has defined him from his first entrance. There is no anagnorisis, no moment of self-knowledge — only the fatal reflex of a warrior who has never learned any response to insult except escalation.

The "boy" provocation carries particular weight given the play's obsession with masculine identity. Volumnia has raised her son to equate manhood with violence; Aufidius's "boy" attacks that identity at its root. Coriolanus's death is thus a consequence not only of political miscalculation but of a psychological formation that has left him no other way to respond.
