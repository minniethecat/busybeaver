# Roman Herald

The Roman Herald is a ceremonial officer who appears in the triumph scene of Act II, Scene 1. Though a minor role with only a handful of lines, the Herald performs a crucial symbolic function: his official pronouncement transforms [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Caius Marcius]] into [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]], conferring the *agnomen* that marks the warrior's new identity.

## Dramatic Function

The Herald is the institutional voice of [[rome|Rome]]'s military ceremonial. As [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] returns in triumph, it is the Herald — not [[cominius|Cominius]] the general, not [[menenius-agrippa|Menenius]] the friend, not [[volumnia|Volumnia]] the mother — who formally announces what [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Marcius]] has achieved and what he shall now be called. This delegation of the naming act to a functionary underscores that [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s new identity is not personal but political: it is [[rome|Rome]], through its official herald, that renames him.

## The Proclamation

The Herald appears in a single scene, Act II Scene 1, speaking only twice. His first speech is the formal proclamation of [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s triumph:

> Know, Rome, that all alone Marcius did fight
> Within Corioli gates: where he hath won,
> With fame, a name to Caius Marcius; these
> In honour follows Coriolanus.
> Welcome to Rome, renowned Coriolanus!
>
> (011-005)

Several features of this proclamation are worth close attention. The address "Know, [[rome|Rome]]" is not directed to the senators or the generals — it is an address to the polity itself, in the voice of the polity's ceremonial apparatus. The phrase "all alone" compresses the solitary heroism that defines [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s battlefield identity into two words. And the chiastic movement — from "[[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Marcius]]" to "[[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]" via "[[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Caius Marcius]]" and back to "[[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]" — enacts in language the transformation the speech proclaims: the name is woven into the verse itself.

The crowd's response — "Welcome to [[rome|Rome]], renowned [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]!" (011-005) — is an echo, the people taking the Herald's words into their own mouths. For this moment, at least, the institutional voice and the popular voice are one.

The Herald's second and final line is practical rather than ceremonial: "Give way there, and go on!" (011-005) — clearing a path through the throng for the triumphal procession. The shift from ceremonial pronouncement to crowd-control practicality marks the Herald's descent from institutional mouthpiece back to ordinary functionary.

## The Herald as Bookend

Although the Herald does not appear again, the office is invoked in the play's final scene. After [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] is killed, the [[first-lord|First Lord]] declares: "let him be regarded / As the most noble corse that ever herald / Did follow to his urn" (029-002). The herald's funereal association — following the dead to the grave — bookends the ceremonial function established in the triumph. The herald who gave [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] his name in Act II is now, in figure, the escort to his burial in Act V. The symmetry is exact: the name was given by [[rome|Rome]]'s institutional voice, and the body is commended to the grave by the same institutional figure.

## The Name as Political Act

The Herald's role illuminates a central theme of the play: the relationship between individual identity and political recognition. [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] earns his name through solitary violence — fighting "all alone" within the gates — but the name itself can only be conferred by the polity. The Herald is the mechanism of that conferral, the instrument through which private heroism becomes public identity. That this mechanism is a minor functionary, not a general or a senator, suggests something about the impersonality of Roman political ceremony: the name is given not by a person but by an office.
