# Caius Marcius Coriolanus

## Character Overview

**Caius Marcius Coriolanus** is the protagonist of Shakespeare's tragedy — [[rome|Rome]]'s greatest warrior, whose inability to accommodate the political demands of the [[rome|Roman Republic]] leads to his destruction. He earns the *agnomen* "Coriolanus" for single-handedly capturing [[corioli|the Volscian city]] of [[corioli|Corioli]], but this martial triumph is the high point of a trajectory that descends through political failure, [[coriolanus-banishment|banishment]], [[coriolanus-defection|defection]] to the enemy, and [[death-of-coriolanus|death]] at the hands of his [[tullus-aufidius|Volscian rival]].

Coriolanus embodies the contradictions of the [[military-honor|aristocratic warrior]] in a [[tribunate|republican polity]]: his [[virtus|*virtus*]] (martial excellence) qualifies him for supreme office, but his [[class-conflict|contempt for the plebeian constitutional order]] makes him politically impossible. He is simultaneously the man [[rome|Rome]] most needs and the man [[rome|Rome]] cannot tolerate.

> I'll fight with none but thee; for I do hate thee
> Worse than a promise-breaker. (004-002)

## Birth and Family

Coriolanus was born Caius Marcius into the patrician class of Republican [[rome|Rome]]. His father died when he was young, and he was raised by his mother **[[volumnia|Volumnia]]**, who cultivated his martial identity with relentless intensity:

> Thy valiantness was mine, thou suck'dst it from me,
> But owe thy pride thyself. (017-002)

This maternal formation — the mother who prizes wounds above safety and honour above survival — is the psychological key to Coriolanus's character. He is married to **[[virgilia|Virgilia]]**, who represents the domestic tenderness he cannot publicly acknowledge, and has a young son (**[[young-marcius|Young Marcius]]**) who shows early signs of his father's martial temperament. His closest male relationship is with **[[menenius-agrippa|Menenius Agrippa]]**, the patrician elder who serves as surrogate father and political mentor — yet proves powerless to prevent Coriolanus's destruction.

> The gods bless you, sir! I have not deserv'd it.
> (002-002) — Coriolanus's characteristic refusal of praise, which masks a profound need for recognition.

## Military Career and the Battle of Corioli

### The Battle

Coriolanus's military career is the foundation of his identity and his claim to political authority. At the [[battle-of-corioli|Battle of Corioli]], when Roman forces waver, he enters the enemy city alone — "I'll fight with none but thee; for I do hate thee" (004-002) — and emerges victorious, covered in blood but triumphant. [[cominius|Cominius]], the [[consulship|consul]], proclaims:

> Caius Marcius Coriolanus.
> Bear th' addition nobly ever! (009-002)

His twenty-seven wounds are the physical ledger of his service — a body mapped by battle, each scar a material record of [[sacrifice|devotion]] to [[rome|Rome]]. When he stands for the [[consular-election|consulship]], the custom requires him to display these wounds to the people as proof of his worth — a ritual of political humility he cannot bring himself to perform:

> I cannot
> Put on the gown, stand naked, and entreat them. (013-002)

## Political Conflict and Banishment

### The Conflict with the Tribunes

Coriolanus's tragedy is fundamentally political. The [[tribunate|Roman constitution]] requires [[consulship|consul]]ar candidates to solicit the people's voices in the [[the-forum|Forum]], displaying their wounds and wearing the *toga candida*. Coriolanus's refusal to perform this ritual sincerely is exploited by the tribunes [[sicinius-velutus|Sicinius Velutus]] and [[junius-brutus|Junius Brutus]], who represent the [[plebeian-rights|plebeian constitutional order]] he despises.

The [[patrician-assembly|patricians]], for their part, are caught between their admiration for Coriolanus's [[virtus|martial virtue]] and the political necessity of accommodating the [[roman-citizens|plebeians]]. [[menenius-agrippa|Menenius]] attempts to mediate but cannot bridge the chasm between Coriolanus's absolutist honor-code and the transactional nature of republican politics.

The tribunes orchestrate the revocation of the [[consulship|consul]]ar approval and engineer Coriolanus's [[coriolanus-banishment|banishment]]. His response is magnificent self-assertion and catastrophic self-deception:

> You common cry of curs! whose breath I hate
> As reek o' th' rotten fens … I banish you! (016-002)

There is no "world elsewhere" for a man whose entire identity is Roman — and this recognition drives his subsequent trajectory.

## Defection to the Volscians

In exile, Coriolanus presents himself to his former enemy **[[tullus-aufidius|Tullus Aufidius]]** in [[antium|Antium]], [[sacrifice|offering]] his services against [[rome|Rome]]. This defection — [[rome|Rome]]'s greatest soldier leading [[rome|Rome]]'s enemies — is the ultimate breach of loyalty, and it sets in motion the play's final catastrophe.

> My name is Caius Marcius, who hath done
> To thee particularly, and to all the Volsces,
> Great hurt and mischief. (020-002)

The [[volscians|Volsci]]an soldiers embrace him with fervour, and even Aufidius is initially moved: "I have nightly since / Dreamt of encounters 'twixt thyself and me" (021-002). But Aufidius's jealousy grows as Coriolanus's martial reputation eclipses his own among the [[volscians|Volscians]].

## The Mother's Intervention

At the gates of [[rome|Rome]], with the [[volscians|Volsci]]an army poised to destroy [[rome|the city]], Coriolanus's mother **[[volumnia|Volumnia]]** — accompanied by [[virgilia|Virgilia]], [[young-marcius|Young Marcius]], and [[valeria|Valeria]] — confronts him. This scene (V.iii) is the emotional centre of the play. [[volumnia|Volumnia]]'s argument operates on multiple registers: she appeals to his *pietas* (familial duty), his Roman identity, and his fear of historical judgment:

> Thou art my warrior;
> I holp to frame thee. (026-002)

Coriolanus yields — "O mother, mother! What have you done?" (026-002) — a victory of familial love over martial vengeance that nevertheless seals his fate. He has spared [[rome|Rome]] but betrayed his [[volscians|Volsci]]an allies.

## Death and Legacy

Aufidius, exploiting Coriolanus's decision to withdraw from [[rome|Rome]], denounces him as a traitor to the [[volscians|Volscians]] at the [[volscian-senate-council|Volscian war council]]. When Aufidius calls him "boy," Coriolanus's characteristic fury erupts:

> Boy! … Cut me to pieces, Volsces; men and lads,
> Stain all your edges on me. (029-002)

The [[volscians|Volsci]]an lords and conspirators kill him on the spot. Aufidius, standing over the body, orders an honourable burial — a gesture that reveals the depth of his ambivalence. Coriolanus dies as he lived: defiant, proud, and unable to compromise.
