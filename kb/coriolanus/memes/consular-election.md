# Consular Election

## Event Summary

The Consular Election of Act II is the procedural heart of the play, spanning the Senate's nomination and the popular ratification in the marketplace. Coriolanus, having returned victorious from Corioli, is acclaimed by the Senate as the natural candidate for consul (011-002–012-002). Cominius delivers a stirring report of Coriolanus's battlefield deeds, and the Senate formally commends him (012-002). Menenius announces to the tribunes that "The senate, Coriolanus, are well pleased / To make thee consul" (012-002).

However, the Roman constitution demands that the candidate present himself in the marketplace wearing the "napless vesture of humility" — the gown of the suppliant — and display his battle wounds while soliciting the voices of the citizens (012-002–013-002). Coriolanus, who "cannot / Put on the gown, stand naked and entreat them" (012-002), performs the ritual with visible contempt, addressing the citizens in a tone of barely concealed scorn (013-002). His grudging performance and the tribunes Sicinius and Brutus's subsequent agitation lead the citizens to revoke their consent — an "ignorant election" that the tribunes skillfully manipulate into overturning (013-002). This event crystallizes the play's central conflict: the meritocratic warrior ethos cannot survive the procedural requirements of popular sovereignty.

## Key Moments

### Phase 1: Senate Endorsement (Act II, Scene II)

The scene opens with the Senate assembled. Cominius, as consul and former general, delivers an extended account of Coriolanus's heroism at Corioli — "His sword, death's stamp, / Where it did mark, it took; from face to foot / He was a thing of blood" (011-002). This battle narrative, witnessed by dignitaries and rewarded by acclamation, establishes the martial foundation of Coriolanus's candidacy. The First Officer observes the crowd's enthusiasm: "I have seen the dumb men throng to see him and / The blind to bear him speak: matrons flung gloves, / Ladies and maids their scarfs and handkerchers, / Upon him as he pass'd" (012-002).

Menenius formally conveys the Senate's decision: "The senate, Coriolanus, are well pleased / To make thee consul" (012-002). Coriolanus's immediate response is a request to bypass the customary popular ratification — "Let me o'erleap that custom, for I cannot / Put on the gown" (012-002). Sicinius rebuffs this: "Sir, the people / Must have their voices; neither will they bate / One jot of ceremony" (012-002).

### Phase 2: The Marketplace Ritual (Act II, Scene III)

Coriolanus enters the marketplace "in a gown of humility" (013-002), and the citizens, organized by the Third Citizen, approach "by ones, by twos, and by threes" to grant their individual voices (013-002). The ritual requires each citizen to offer his voice directly, and Coriolanus must "make his requests by particulars" (013-002).

Coriolanus's performance is technically compliant but tonally contemptuous. His dialogue with individual citizens reveals his disdain for the proceeding:
- To the First Citizen: "Well then, I pray, your price o' the consulship?" (013-002)
- To the Second: "A match, sir. There's in all two worthy voices begged. I have your alms: adieu" (013-002)
- To the Third: "Pray you now, if it may stand with the tune of your voices that I may be consul, I have here the customary gown" (013-002)

His final speech to the assembled citizens is a recitation of his wounds purchased in their service — "for your voices have / Done many things, some less, some more: your voices" (013-002) — ending with the flat declaration "Indeed, I would be consul" (013-002). The citizens, moved by his military record, grant their voices despite the third citizen's later observation: "He mock'd us when he begg'd our voices" (013-002).

### Phase 3: The Revocation (Act II, Scene III)

Immediately after Coriolanus's departure, the tribunes Sicinius and Brutus work the crowd. Sicinius presses the citizens to recognize what they witnessed: "Why either were you ignorant to see't, / Or, seeing it, of such childish friendliness / To yield your voices?" (013-002). The Second Citizen resolves to "have five hundred voices of that sound" (013-002) in opposition.

Brutus frames the election as a threat to liberty — "They have chose a consul that will from them take / Their liberties" (013-002) — while Sicinius orders a new assembly to "revoke / Your ignorant election" (013-002). Both tribunes deflect responsibility onto themselves ("Lay / A fault on us, your tribunes") while simultaneously claiming the citizens chose Coriolanus "More after our commandment than as guided / By your own true affections" (013-002) — a masterful rhetorical maneuver that preserves the fiction of popular sovereignty while engineering its result. The citizens exit chanting "almost all / Repent in their election" (013-002).

## Dramatic Significance

The Consular Election is structurally pivotal. It follows Coriolanus's military triumph (the Battle of Corioli) and precedes his banishment. The sequence of acclamation → ritual degradation → popular revocation traces the arc from heroic identity to political failure. Shakespeare constructs a precise dramatic irony: the very qualities that make Coriolanus an effective soldier — directness, contempt for performance, refusal to dissemble — are the qualities that disqualify him from constitutional office.

The election also establishes the tribunes as the play's principal antagonists. Sicinius and Brutus appear initially as constitutional officers enforcing procedure ("neither will they bate / One jot of ceremony"), but their manipulation of the citizens' remorse reveals their instrumental use of the forms of popular consent. The "ignorant election" that they revoke is, in their telling, both the people's sovereign choice and a mistake to be corrected — a paradox central to Shakespeare's representation of populist politics.

## Aftermath and Consequences

The immediate consequence of the revoked election is the crisis that leads to Coriolanus's trial and banishment (014-002). The tribunes, having demonstrated their power to overturn a consular election, press their advantage: they demand Coriolanus answer charges before the people, transforming the procedural defeat into a juridical one. The election's failure thus catalyzes the play's second half — Coriolanus's defection to the Volscians and eventual march on Rome.

More broadly, the election exposes the structural flaw in the Roman mixed constitution. Military merit (the Senate's criterion) and popular consent (the tribunician criterion) are incommensurable values, and the constitution provides no mechanism for reconciling them when they conflict. The gown of humility — a garment designed to symbolize the candidate's equality with the people — becomes, on Coriolanus, a costume of degradation that he cannot wear without contempt, and that the people cannot accept without suspicion.
