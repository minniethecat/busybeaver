# Second Citizen

## Overview

The Second Citizen is the more moderate voice in the opening rebellion of *[[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]*. Where the [[first-citizen|First Citizen]] calls for [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s death, the Second Citizen urges caution and reminds the crowd of [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s military service to [[rome|Rome]]. His role is to dramatise the internal divisions within the plebeian movement — not all citizens are equally radical, and his cautious interventions create the dramatic space for [[menenius-agrippa|Menenius Agrippa]]'s diplomatic intervention.

## Role in the Play

### The Voice of Moderation (000-050)

The Second Citizen speaks his first line as a restraining force: "One word, good citizens" (000-014). While the [[first-citizen|First Citizen]] has already proposed killing [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]], the Second Citizen redirects the question: "Would you proceed especially against [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Caius Marcius]]?" (000-018). When the crowd shouts for [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s death, he appeals to their memory: "Consider you what services he has done for his country?" (000-020).

His most significant intervention is a moral correction of the [[first-citizen|First Citizen]]'s ad hominem attack: "Nay, but speak not maliciously" (000-023). When the [[first-citizen|First Citizen]] claims [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s pride negates his achievements, the Second Citizen delivers a pointed rebuttal: "What he cannot help in his nature, you account a vice in him. You must in no way say he is covetous" (000-025). This defence — that [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] is proud but not greedy — is a nuanced moral judgment that the play does not refute.

### Alliance with Menenius

The Second Citizen is the first to recognise [[menenius-agrippa|Menenius Agrippa]]: "Worthy [[menenius-agrippa|Menenius Agrippa]]; one that hath always loved the people" (000-028). His respect for the patrician mediator distinguishes him from the [[first-citizen|First Citizen]], who responds with only grudging acknowledgement: "He's one honest enough: would all the rest were so!" (000-029). This exchange establishes the Second Citizen as a potential bridge between classes — a role that the tribunes, in their manipulation of the people, will later foreclose.

## Dramatic Significance

The Second Citizen demonstrates that the "people" in *[[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]* are not a monolithic mob. His presence complicates the play's apparent anti-populism by showing a plebeian who reasons, remembers, and distinguishes between virtues and vices. His disappearance after the opening scene, like the [[first-citizen|First Citizen]]'s, reflects the play's structural shift from the plebeian collective to the tribunes who claim to represent them.
