# Coriolanus's Triumph

## Event Summary


### Analysis

Following the Roman victory at Corioli, Caius Marcius returns to Rome in triumph. Cominius, his commanding general, publicly acclaims his heroic deeds and bestows upon him the agnomen "Coriolanus" in recognition of his single-handed capture of the Volscian city (009-002). The triumph marks the apex of Coriolanus's martial career — and the beginning of his political failure. The same population that cheers his battlefield valor will, within two acts, banish him from the city.

## The Battlefield Acclamation

The triumph scene is structured as public theater. Cominius, having witnessed Coriolanus's extraordinary feat at Corioli — entering the city gates alone and emerging bloodied but victorious — stages a formal recognition before the assembled army:

> "From this time, / For what he did before Corioli, call him, / With all the applause and clamour of the host, / CAIUS MARCIUS CORIOLANUS" (009-002)

The stage direction specifies "Flourish. Alarum. A retreat is sounded," marking the moment with martial music. Coriolanus's response is characteristically terse: "I will go wash" (009-002). He accepts the honor but cannot perform gratitude; he is more comfortable cleaning his wounds than receiving praise. This refusal of ceremonial reciprocity — the same refusal that will doom his consular candidacy — is already visible at his moment of greatest triumph.

## The Politics of Wounds

A crucial exchange between Coriolanus and Cominius reveals the tensions that will define the political action to come. Cominius offers Coriolanus a tenth of the spoils — "Of all the horses, / ... / Of all the treasure" — and Coriolanus refuses all but one special gift:

> "I sometimes lay here in Corioli / At a poor man's house; he used me kindly: / He cried to me; I saw him prisoner; / But then Aufidius was within my view, / And wrath o'erwhelmed my pity. I request you / To give my poor host freedom" (009-002)

This moment of private mercy — remembering a poor man's kindness even amid the carnage of battle — reveals a Coriolanus the political arena never sees. He cannot remember the man's name, but he remembers the debt. The irony is that the same man who cannot stoop to ask citizens for their votes can stoop to plead for a single prisoner's freedom.

## Volumnia's Triumph

The triumph reaches its emotional peak in Volumnia's welcome (011-005). Mother and son are reunited in a scene of competitive wound-counting: Volumnia exults that "there will be large cicatrices to show the people" (011-005), counting Coriolanus's twenty-seven wounds as evidence of his fitness for the consulship. Her maternal pride is indistinguishable from political calculation:

> "I have lived / To see inherited my very wishes / And the buildings of my fancy" (011-005)

The wounds that should mark Coriolanus's sacrifice become, in Volumnia's rhetoric, the campaign props of his consular bid. The scene's tone — triumphant but ominous — captures the play's central anxiety: the incompatibility of martial honor with democratic performance.

## The Consular Candidacy

The triumph scene segues directly into the consular election machinery. Menenius reports that "it remains the people do appoint the senators / To make Coriolanus consul" (011-005). But Brutus and Sicinius, observing the celebration, recognize an opportunity: Coriolanus's pride — his refusal to perform the humility expected of candidates — can be weaponized against him:

> "Doubt not / The commoners, for whom we stand, but they / Upon their ancient malice will forget ... / ... / Which he will give them make them thence decline" (011-005)

The triumph thus ends on a note of dramatic irony. The crowd cheers, the honors are granted, the consulship seems assured — and the tribunes are already planning its destruction.

## Dramatic Significance

The triumph is the hinge between Coriolanus's military and political careers. Every honor he receives makes his subsequent fall more precipitous. The scene dramatizes the paradox at the play's heart: the virtues that make Coriolanus a hero — courage, authenticity, refusal to dissemble — are the vices that make him unelectable. He cannot perform humility because he is genuinely proud; he cannot ask for votes because he has already earned them on the battlefield.

## Participants

- **Caius Marcius Coriolanus** — the triumphant hero, newly named, already uncomfortable with the ceremonial demands of his new status.

- **Cominius** — the consul who acclaims Coriolanus's deeds before the army, serving as the public validator of martial merit.

- **Menenius Agrippa** — the delighted patrician ally who sees in the triumph a vindication of his class. His enthusiasm blinds him to the political danger.

- **Volumnia** — the mother who counts her son's wounds like campaign medals, already planning the consular candidacy.

- **Titus Lartius** — the fellow general who shares in the triumph and provides a witness to Coriolanus's deeds at Corioli.
