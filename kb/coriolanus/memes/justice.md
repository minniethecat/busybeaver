# Justice

Justice in *Coriolanus* is a contested concept, pulled between the aristocratic honour code that Coriolanus embodies and the popular tribunate that claims to speak for the people. The play explores justice as legal process, as personal retribution, and — most troublingly — as the thin line separating civic order from the violence that threatens to consume it.

## "And Curse That Justice Did It"

Coriolanus's first extended speech in the play offers a cynical vision of popular justice. The plebeians' "virtue is / To make him worthy whose offence subdues him / And curse that justice did it" (076-080). In this view, the people's sense of justice is merely resentment dressed in principle — they honour the criminal and curse the law that punishes him. This contempt for popular justice runs through the play, informing Coriolanus's refusal to submit to the tribunes' authority and his insistence that true justice belongs to the patrician order alone.

## "In the Presence of Dreaded Justice"

When Sicinius pronounces Coriolanus's banishment, he invokes the language of justice carefully. Coriolanus has acted "not in the presence / Of dreaded justice, but on the ministers / That do distribute it" (520-530). The phrasing is significant: justice is "dreaded" — an instrument of awe and fear — and the tribunes are merely its "ministers" (distributors). Sicinius frames the banishment not as a political act but as the execution of a higher principle, attempting to clothe tribunician power in the language of legal authority.

## "He Does Fair Justice"

In a moment of remarkable self-awareness, Coriolanus acknowledges that if Aufidius kills him, "He does fair justice" (530-535). Standing outside Aufidius's house in Antium, Coriolanus confronts the possibility that his death would be just — a fair return for the Volscian lives he has taken. This recognition of justice as retribution is the closest Coriolanus comes to moral humility, yet it is framed in purely personal terms: justice between two warriors, not between a citizen and his community.

## Justice and the Failure of Institutions

The play's deeper interest lies in the failure of institutional justice. The tribunate, created to protect plebeian rights, becomes a vehicle for political vengeance. The Senate, guardian of patrician justice, cannot prevent Coriolanus's banishment. And the people, ostensibly the source of law's authority, are swayed by whatever voice speaks last. In the absence of reliable institutions, justice collapses into force — Coriolanus at the gates of Rome is justice's final, violent form.
