## Definition

**Fear** in *Coriolanus* operates on multiple registers: as a political tool wielded by the tribunes against the patricians, as the plebeians' rational response to hunger and war, and — most tellingly — as the emotion Coriolanus himself systematically denies. "Not fearing outward force" (014-002), he cannot recognise fear in others or understand its political legitimacy.

The play's treatment of fear is sophisticated: it is not simply condemned (as cowardice) or valorised (as prudent caution). Instead, Shakespeare shows that a political order that cannot accommodate fear — that treats all apprehension as weakness — is doomed to violent oscillation.

## Historical Background

Classical political thought treated fear as both necessary and dangerous. Machiavelli's *The Prince* (1513) argued that fear was a more reliable foundation for political authority than love, provided the ruler avoided being hated. Hobbes's *Leviathan* (1651) would later make fear of violent death the rational foundation of political obligation. *Coriolanus* (c. 1608) anticipates both analyses: the play examines what happens when a ruling class cannot decide whether it wants to be feared or loved, and gets the worst of both.

## In the Play

Fear is immediately present. The citizens arm themselves because they "fear to famish" (001-002) — hunger is a form of political fear. Menenius dismisses this as "cowardice" but cannot answer its material basis.

In battle, fear is the test of character. Coriolanus scorns the soldiers who flee at Corioli: "They fear us not, but issue forth their city" (004-002) — the Volscians are brave because Romans are cowardly. His own fearlessness is both his military genius and his political blindness. A man who does not fear death cannot understand those who do.

The tribunes understand fear better than anyone. They recognise that Coriolanus's "surly nature" can be provoked into self-destruction, and they use the people's fear of patrician domination to consolidate their own power. Fear, in their hands, is a precision instrument.

Aufidius's lieutenant says what the hero cannot: "Fear not our care, sir" (007-002). Loyalty, here, is the management of fear — a concept alien to Coriolanus, who expects loyalty to arise from admiration alone.

The most revealing moment comes from Cominius, who frames valour as the suppression of fear: "It is held / That valour is the chiefest virtue" (012-002) — the word "held" concedes that the primacy of courage is a social convention, not a natural fact. Fear, by implication, is the natural state; valour is what we build against it.
