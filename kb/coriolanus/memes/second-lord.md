## Overview

The Second Lord is a minor character in *[[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]* who appears in the play's final scene, Act V, Scene VI, in [[antium|Antium]]. Along with the other [[volscians|Volsci]]an lords, he witnesses [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s return from the Roman campaign and the confrontation between [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] and Aufidius that leads to the hero's death. The Second Lord stands out among the [[volscians|Volsci]]an leadership for his measured response — he attempts to calm the crowd and, after the assassination, offers a balanced judgment that partly absolves Aufidius.

## In the Play

The Second Lord enters with the other [[volscians|Volsci]]an lords to hear [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s accounting of the Roman campaign. When Aufidius accuses [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] of treachery and the conspirators begin shouting for [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s death (1372), the Second Lord is the first to call for restraint:

> Peace, ho! no outrage: peace! The man is noble and his fame folds-in This orb o' the earth. His last offences to us Shall have judicious hearing. Stand, Aufidius, And trouble not the peace. (1372)

His intervention — insisting on due process and acknowledging [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s nobility — is the voice of institutional order attempting to prevent mob violence. But the conspirators ignore him, and [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] is killed moments later.

After the assassination, as the [[first-lord|First Lord]] delivers his epitaph and Aufidius is overcome with remorse, the Second Lord offers the play's final political judgment (1376):

> His own impatience Takes from Aufidius a great part of blame. Let's make the best of it. (1376)

This line distributes responsibility: [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s own character — his "impatience" — shares the blame with Aufidius. The Second Lord's pragmatism ("Let's make the best of it") contrasts with the [[first-lord|First Lord]]'s ceremonial grief, reflecting the practical needs of the [[volscians|Volsci]]an state in the aftermath of violence.

## Dramatic Function

The Second Lord serves as the voice of legal process and pragmatic governance in the play's chaotic final moments. His call for "judicious hearing" (1372) represents what [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] never received in [[rome|Rome]] — a fair hearing — and his subsequent distribution of blame between [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] and Aufidius provides the audience with a framework for understanding the tragedy as the product of both external conspiracy and internal character flaw.
