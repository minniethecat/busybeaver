# Ingratitude

## Definition

**Ingratitude** in *Coriolanus* is the moral charge that animates much of the play's political rhetoric. Rome's failure to adequately reward Coriolanus for his martial service is framed not as a political disagreement but as a moral failing — the ungrateful city that devours its own defenders. The accusation flows in both directions: the patricians charge the plebeians with ingratitude for rejecting Coriolanus's consulship, while from the plebeian perspective, the Senate's hoarding of grain during famine is itself an act of ingratitude toward the people whose labour sustains the commonwealth.

## In the Play

The theme surfaces in multiple registers across the play's five acts. In the immediate aftermath of the Corioli victory, Cominius praises Coriolanus and warns that to conceal his deeds would let wounds "fester 'gainst ingratitude" (009-002), establishing the term as the play's keyword for the breach between martial merit and political recognition. The term reappears during the consular election sequence, where the Third Citizen delivers the play's most explicit moral pronouncement on the subject: "Ingratitude is monstrous, and for the multitude to be ingrateful, were to make a monster of the multitude" (013-002). This sentence is carefully equivocal — it condemns ingratitude while simultaneously acknowledging that the multitude possess the capacity for it, thereby implicating the very people whose voices the tribunes claim to represent.

Coriolanus himself absorbs and re-deploys the charge. When his mother Volumnia urges him to perform humility before the people, he retorts that such performance would make him "a beggar's shadow" — revealing his conviction that gratitude is owed to him, not merely offered. After his banishment, the language of ingratitude becomes explicitly militarized. Aufidius vows to pour war "into the bowels of ungrateful Rome" (021-002), and Coriolanus describes himself as having served a "thankless country" (021-002), making ingratitude the causal link between his expulsion and his armed return. By Act IV, Rome's ingratitude has become Coriolanus's casus belli.

Menenius Agrippa, the play's most articulate spokesman for patrician interests, also invokes the concept when he attempts to reason with the banished Coriolanus at the Volscian camp (025-001). His appeal to the obligations of gratitude between father-figure and son-figure fails — Coriolanus has moved beyond the moral economy of gratitude and injury into the existential territory of revenge.

## Dramatic Significance

The charge of ingratitude functions as the play's central mechanism for converting political disagreements into moral absolutes. By framing the conflict between Coriolanus and the people as a question of gratitude rather than of competing class interests, Shakespeare exposes how moral language can be weaponized to foreclose political debate. To call a political opponent "ungrateful" is to declare them not merely wrong but morally defective — and it is precisely this rhetoric that makes Coriolanus's reconciliation with Rome impossible. The tragedy of ingratitude, in the end, is that it demands a response disproportionate to the original injury: Coriolanus's revenge far exceeds Rome's slight, and in that excess lies the play's tragic logic.
