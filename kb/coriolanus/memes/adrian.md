# Adrian

Adrian is a minor character in *Coriolanus* — a Volscian who, in a brief scene on the highway between Rome and Antium, encounters a Roman spy and exchanges intelligence about the political situation in Rome following Coriolanus's banishment.

## The Spy Scene (Act IV Scene III)

Adrian appears in a single short scene (530-535) that serves as a narrative bridge between Coriolanus's banishment from Rome and his arrival in Antium. On the road between the two cities, Adrian meets a Roman who recognizes him: "I know you well, sir, and you know me: your name, I think, is Adrian" (530-535). The Roman reveals himself to be Nicanor, a Roman spy working against his own country. The two exchange news: Coriolanus has been banished, and the Volscian leadership is preparing for war.

## Dramatic Function

Adrian's scene serves an important expository function. Through this brief exchange between spies, Shakespeare conveys to the audience the news of Coriolanus's banishment reaching the Volscians — news that will shape their strategic calculations and set the stage for Coriolanus's defection. The scene demonstrates that the consequences of Rome's political decisions extend beyond its walls, rippling through the intelligence networks of its enemies. Adrian, though a mere messenger within the play's world, embodies the permeability of borders and the speed with which political information travels in times of conflict.

## The Volscian Spy Network

Adrian's presence, together with Nicanor, reveals the existence of an intelligence apparatus operating between Rome and the Volscian territories. The easy familiarity between the two spies — they recognize each other, exchange information without ceremony — suggests an established network of mutual surveillance. In a play preoccupied with public performance and hidden motives, this brief glimpse of the world of espionage reinforces the theme of deceptive appearances: even on an open highway, men are not what they seem.
