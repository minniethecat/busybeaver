## Overview

A Patrician is an unnamed minor character in *[[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]* who appears in Act III, Scene I. He is one of the Roman nobles who observe and react to [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s explosive confrontation with the tribunes Sicinius and Brutus. His brief lines capture the patrician class's mixture of dismay at [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s political recklessness and solidarity with him against the plebeian tribunes.

## In the Play

The Patrician first speaks after [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] has refused to moderate his language before the people and stormed off with [[cominius|Cominius]]. Alone on stage with [[menenius-agrippa|Menenius]], the Patrician remarks grimly: "This man has marr'd his fortune" (724). This line succinctly diagnoses [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s tragic flaw — his inability to compromise politically, which will cost him the [[consulship|consul]]ship and lead to his [[exile|banishment]].

[[menenius-agrippa|Menenius]] responds with a defense of [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s nature, and a [[second-patrician|Second Patrician]] adds a brief exclamation of frustration: "I would they were a-bed!" (726), referring to the troublesome common people.

Later in the same scene, when [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] returns with other patricians and defiantly declares that he will face death rather than change his ways toward the people (768), the same Patrician voices his approval: "You do the nobler" (769). This line reveals the patrician class's entrenched contempt for the plebeians and their instinctive support for [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s aristocratic defiance.

When [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] continues to boast of his mother's similar disdain for the common people, and [[menenius-agrippa|Menenius]] and senators attempt to calm him, the Patrician adds a dark final word: "Ay, and burn too" (777), endorsing [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s wish that the people be hanged.

## Dramatic Function

The Patrician serves as a representative voice of the Roman aristocracy, demonstrating how the patrician class enables [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s self-destructive pride rather than restraining it. His transformation from initial concern ("This man has marr'd his fortune") to unconditional endorsement ("You do the nobler") mirrors the political dynamics that drive the tragedy: the patricians' class solidarity prevents them from [[sacrifice|offering]] [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] the honest counsel that might have saved him.
