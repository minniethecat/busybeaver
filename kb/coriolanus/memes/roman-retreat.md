# Roman Retreat from Corioli

## Event Summary

The Roman retreat from the walls of Corioli is the crisis that makes Coriolanus's heroism possible. As the Romans advance on the Volscian stronghold (003-001), the defenders sally forth and drive the Roman soldiers back from the walls. Coriolanus, enraged at their cowardice, denounces his own men and charges alone through the city gates — which "Clapp'd to their gates: he is himself alone, To answer all the city" (003-002). This moment of spectacular individual valour transforms a rout into a triumph and earns Marcius the *agnomen* "Coriolanus."

## Dramatic Significance

The retreat and Coriolanus's counter-charge (003-002) establish the defining pattern of his character: martial prowess inseparable from contempt for collective action. His willingness to fight alone — "O me alone! Make you a sword of me" (003-002) — is both his greatest strength and the seed of his political destruction. The same refusal to stand with others that wins Corioli will later cost him Rome.

## Participants

- **Caius Marcius Coriolanus**: Rallies the retreating Romans with a furious denunciation, then enters Corioli's gates alone (003-002).
- **Titus Lartius**: Commands the Roman forces alongside Coriolanus during the assault (003-002).
- **Roman Soldiers**: Break and retreat from the walls, provoking Coriolanus's rage; later regroup to follow him into the city (003-002).

## Aftermath and Consequences

Coriolanus emerges from Corioli covered in blood but victorious — an image that cements his martial reputation and directly leads to his nomination for the consulship. The retreat thus serves as the crucial turning point from military to political identity, with all the tragic consequences that follow.
