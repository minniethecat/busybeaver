# Lieutenant to Aufidius

The Lieutenant to Aufidius is a [[volscians|Volsci]]an officer serving under [[tullus-aufidius]], listed among the "Dramatis Personae" as "Lieutenant to Aufidius" (P01-010). Though appearing only briefly in a single scene, the Lieutenant plays an important dramatic role as the voice of strategic caution — articulating the threat that [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s popularity poses to Aufidius's authority within the [[volscians|Volsci]]an camp.

## In the Play

The Lieutenant appears in Act IV, Scene 7, where he serves as Aufidius's sole confidant in a private military council. The scene opens with Aufidius asking whether the [[volscians|Volsci]]an soldiers "still fly to the Roman" (023-002). The Lieutenant's answer is direct and ominous:

> I do not know what witchcraft's in him, but
> Your soldiers use him as the grace 'fore meat,
> Their talk at table, and their thanks at end;
> And you are darken'd in this action, sir,
> Even by your own.
>
> (023-002)

The Lieutenant's frankness — he tells his commander that he is being eclipsed by a foreign ally — establishes him as a loyal subordinate willing to speak unwelcome truths. He goes further, advising that Aufidius should never have shared command:

> Yet I wish, sir—
> I mean for your particular—you had not
> Join'd in commission with him; but either
> Had borne the action of yourself, or else
> To him had left it solely.
>
> (023-002)

This counsel, though Aufidius deflects it with professions of political patience, plants the seed of [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s destruction. The Lieutenant's final question — "Sir, I beseech you, think you he'll carry [[rome|Rome]]?" (023-002) — prompts Aufidius's extended soliloquy on the volatility of power, in which Aufidius privately resolves that [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] "shalt be mine" once [[rome|Rome]] falls.

## Dramatic Function

The Lieutenant serves a choric function: his warnings articulate the threat that dramatic irony makes visible to the audience but that Aufidius refuses to acknowledge openly. By giving voice to the [[volscians|Volsci]]an soldiers' admiration for [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] and to strategic common sense ("don't share power with your rival"), the Lieutenant externalizes the internal conflict that Aufidius suppresses until the play's final scene, where he orchestrates [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s murder.

Structurally, the Lieutenant is to Aufidius what the loyal officer is to many of Shakespeare's tragic figures: a truth-teller whose warnings, if heeded, might avert catastrophe. That Aufidius listens but does not act — preferring to wait until "he shall come to his account" — makes the final assassination both inevitable and, in retrospect, coldly premeditated.
