# Plebeian Assembly Vote

## Event Summary

The Plebeian Assembly Vote on Coriolanus's consulship is the procedural center of the play's political action, spanning two acts and three crucial scenes. In Act II, Scene III, the citizens gather in the Forum to grant or withhold their "voices" (votes) — the ritual of popular consent required for Coriolanus to assume the consulship. Each citizen approaches individually to deliver his voice in a ceremony organized "by ones, by twos, and by threes" (013-002). The citizens initially grant their voices — "God save thee, noble consul!" (013-002) — but the tribunes immediately work to reverse the decision, persuading the citizens that Coriolanus "mock'd us when he begg'd our voices" (013-002).

In Act III, Scene III, the tribunes orchestrate the formal revocation. What was an election becomes a trial; the assembly that granted the consulship becomes the assembly that pronounces banishment. The vote thus traces a complete arc: consent freely given → consent withdrawn at instigation → consent weaponized as juridical destruction. Shakespeare presents democratic procedure not as a stable institution but as a volatile instrument, as easily turned against its own elected officers as deployed in their defense.

## Phase 1: The Forum Ritual (Act II, Scene III)

### The Citizens' Self-Consciousness

Before Coriolanus enters, the citizens debate among themselves. The Third Citizen articulates a sophisticated theory of their own political psychology:

> "We have power in ourselves to do it, but it is a power that we have no power to do; for if he show us his wounds and tell us his deeds, we are to put our tongues into those wounds and speak for them" (013-002)

This paradoxical formulation — "a power that we have no power to do" — captures the citizen's recognition that their formal sovereignty is constrained by the emotional force of Coriolanus's military record. They are free to vote, but not free to disregard his wounds. The image of "putting our tongues into those wounds" is strikingly intimate, suggesting that the vote is less a rational judgment than an embodied, almost erotic response to the spectacle of suffering.

The First Citizen recalls Coriolanus's earlier contempt: "once we stood up about the corn, he himself stuck not to call us the many-headed multitude" (013-002). The Hydra epithet, now internalized by the citizens themselves, marks the asymmetry of political knowledge in this scene: the citizens know exactly what Coriolanus thinks of them, yet they will still vote for him.

### Coriolanus's Performance

Coriolanus enters "in a gown of humility" (013-002), and the Third Citizen directs the proceedings: "come by him where he stands, by ones, by twos, and by threes. He's to make his requests by particulars; wherein every one of us has a single honour, in giving him our own voices with our own tongues" (013-002). The ritual constructs each citizen as an individual sovereign — "a single honour" — while simultaneously channeling that sovereignty through a choreographed sequence.

Coriolanus's performance oscillates between compliance and contempt. To the first pair of citizens, he negotiates his voice as a transaction:
- "Well then, I pray, your price o' the consulship?" (013-002)
- "A match, sir. There's in all two worthy voices begged. I have your alms: adieu" (013-002)

The word "alms" — charity given to beggars — transforms the constitutional ritual into degradation. Coriolanus frames the vote not as a right the citizens exercise but as a charity they bestow, and his "adieu" dismisses them with the brevity of contempt.

When the Fourth Citizen observes that Coriolanus has "not indeed loved the common people," Coriolanus replies with devastating frankness:

> "You should account me the more virtuous that I have not been common in my love. I will, sir, flatter my sworn brother, the people, to earn a dearer estimation of them... I will practise the insinuating nod and be off to them most counterfeitly" (013-002)

His admission that he will "counterfeit" popular affection — in the very act of soliciting popular consent — is a performative contradiction that undoes the ritual even as it fulfills it. He gives the citizens the form of what they require while telling them it is counterfeit.

### The Soliloquy of Contempt

After the citizens depart, Coriolanus delivers a soliloquy that reveals his true feelings:

> "Better it is to die, better to starve, / Than crave the hire which first we do deserve. / Why in this woolvish toge should I stand here, / To beg of Hob and Dick, that do appear, / Their needless vouches?" (013-002)

The names "Hob and Dick" — generic, diminutive English names — reduce the Roman citizenry to comic rustics. His final words before the last citizens arrive — "Your voices... for your voices have / Done many things, some less, some more: your voices: / Indeed, I would be consul" (013-002) — accumulate the word "voices" seven times in seven lines, a rhetorical excess that performs exactly the insincerity he has confessed to. The flat "Indeed" carries the weight of a sarcasm so compressed it barely registers as language.

### The Voices Granted

Despite everything, the citizens grant their voices. The Seventh Citizen declares: "Therefore let him be consul: the gods give him joy, and make him good friend to the people!" (013-002). The unanimous acclamation — "Amen, amen. God save thee, noble consul!" — completes the ritual. Menenius pronounces the formal conclusion: "You have stood your limitation; and the tribunes / Endue you with the people's voice" (013-002).

## Phase 2: The Tribunes' Reversal (Act II, Scene III)

Immediately after Coriolanus departs, the tribunes begin their work. The Second Citizen, reflecting, says: "to my poor unworthy notice, / He mock'd us when he begg'd our voices" (013-002). The Third Citizen elaborates a detailed recollection of Coriolanus's sarcastic tone:

> "'I would be consul,' says he: 'aged custom, / But by your voices, will not so permit me; / Your voices therefore.' When we granted that, / Here was 'I thank you for your voices: thank you: / Your most sweet voices: now you have left your voices, / I have no further with you.' Was not this mockery?" (013-002)

The Third Citizen's memory is accurate — Coriolanus did say these things, and the mockery was real. But the recognition of mockery arrives after the fact, retroactively transforming a compliant performance into a scandal. The tribunes skillfully convert post-hoc awareness into political momentum.

Sicinius's manipulation is masterful. First, he rebukes the citizens for their naivety: "Why either were you ignorant to see't, / Or, seeing it, of such childish friendliness / To yield your voices?" (013-002). Then Brutus supplies the script they should have followed — a pre-written set of demands that the tribunes had "lesson'd" the citizens to deliver. Finally, Sicinius provides the instruction for action:

> "Let them assemble, / And on a safer judgment all revoke / Your ignorant election" (013-002)

The word "ignorant" serves a double function: it describes the election as uninformed, and it insults the citizens who made it. The tribunes insinuate themselves as the citizens' protectors while simultaneously treating them as children who need instruction.

Brutus closes with the most revealing lines of the scene:

> "Lay / A fault on us, your tribunes; that we laboured, / No impediment between, but that you must / Cast your election on him" (013-002)

This is the paradox of tribunician authority in miniature. The tribunes claim they forced the citizens to vote for Coriolanus ("but that you must") in order to claim now that the citizens should revoke that forced vote — making the revocation, not the original vote, the authentic expression of popular will. The citizens, fully compliant, exit chanting: "almost all / Repent in their election" (013-002).

The tribunes' private conclusion — "this shall seem, as partly 'tis, their own, / Which we have goaded onward" (013-002) — admits the engineered nature of the revocation while preserving the fiction of popular sovereignty. The vote, in Shakespeare's anatomy, is both real ("partly 'tis, their own") and manufactured ("we have goaded onward"). The assembly's decision is neither wholly autonomous nor wholly coerced — it is the product of a complex interplay between genuine grievance and elite orchestration.

## Phase 3: The Banishment Assembly (Act III, Scene III)

The final phase transforms the assembly from an electoral body into a juridical one. The tribunes have by now full control of the proceedings. Sicinius issues the sentence:

> "Even from this instant, banish him our city" (017-002)

The assembly that granted Coriolanus the consulship now banishes him. The citizens echo in chorus: "It shall be so, it shall be so!" (017-002). The procedural arc is complete: voices given → voices questioned → voices revoked → voices weaponized.

## The Banishment Vote (Act III, Scene III — Detail)

When Sicinius demands, "What is the city but the people?" the citizens respond in affirmation: "True, the people are the city" (016-002). This is the constitutional theory of the tribunate — that the people are the sovereign, and the tribunes are their voice. Against this claim, the patricians have no effective response. Cominius warns that this logic will "lay the city flat; / To bring the roof to the foundation" (016-002), but his warning is powerless.

Sicinius demands Coriolanus be thrown from the Tarpeian Rock — summary execution. Menenius negotiates for a trial, and the tribunes grant it, confident in their control of the outcome. In the marketplace, Sicinius dictates the charges: Coriolanus is "a traitorous innovator, / A foe to the public weal" (016-002). When Coriolanus erupts, the tribunes pronounce banishment, and the citizens confirm.

Coriolanus's response — "I banish you!" — inverts the sentence but cannot reverse it. He leaves Rome, and the assembly's vote becomes the pivot on which the entire tragic action turns.

## Dramatic Significance

The Plebeian Assembly Vote is Shakespeare's most sustained dramatic treatment of democratic procedure. Its genius lies in its refusal to resolve the ambiguity of popular sovereignty. The citizens are neither noble republicans exercising mature judgment nor manipulated puppets with no agency. They are both — and neither description fully captures their condition.

The vote also exposes the temporal structure of democratic regret. The citizens grant their voices in the presence of Coriolanus's wounded body; they revoke them in the presence of the tribunes' persuasive speech. Each decision is, in its moment, sincere. The gap between them — the space in which "almost all / Repent in their election" — is the space of democratic politics itself: a permanent oscillation between consent and its withdrawal, neither ever final, both always available for reactivation.

## Aftermath and Consequences

The assembly's vote — first to grant, then to revoke — triggers the chain of events that constitutes the play's second half: Coriolanus's banishment (017-002), his defection to the Volscians (Act IV), his march on Rome (Act V), Volumnia's embassy, the treaty of peace, and Coriolanus's death. What begins as a procedural sequence in the Forum becomes the generative event of the entire tragic action.

The deeper consequence is constitutional. The assembly's vote reveals that popular sovereignty in Rome is both real and capturable. The citizens have genuine power — they can make and unmake consuls — but that power is exercised through procedures that can be manipulated by those who control the agenda. Coriolanus's contempt and the tribunes' cunning are different pathologies that produce the same result: an assembly whose formal power exceeds its practical autonomy.
