# Nicanor

## Overview

Nicanor is a minor but dramatically pointed character — a Roman who has defected to the [[volscians|Volscians]] and serves as a spy. In a single brief scene (Act IV, Scene 3), he meets a [[volscians|Volsci]]an contact and delivers the intelligence that confirms [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s [[exile|banishment]] from [[rome|Rome]] and his approach to [[antium|Antium]]. His betrayal mirrors and foreshadows [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s own defection: one Roman traitor's report sets the stage for another, far greater, act of treason. In the play's economy of loyalty and betrayal, Nicanor stands as a cautionary miniature of the trajectory [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] himself will follow.

## Role in the Play

### The Spy's Report (948-956)

Nicanor appears in a short scene consisting of a dialogue between a Roman (himself) and a [[volsce|Volsce]]. The [[volsce|Volsce]]'s cautious opening — "Nicanor? no." (948) — serves as both an identity check and an acknowledgment of the conspiratorial nature of their meeting. The scene is set in a "highway" between [[rome|Rome]] and [[antium|Antium]], emphasizing the liminal, borderless space that traitors inhabit.

Nicanor then delivers his intelligence: [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] has been banished from [[rome|Rome]], and his whereabouts are uncertain. The [[volsce|Volsce]] responds: "You will be welcome with this intelligence, Nicanor" (956). The scene is remarkably brief — approximately nine lines in the Folio — yet it accomplishes several crucial dramatic functions simultaneously.

### Structural Function

The scene bridges [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s [[exile|banishment]] in [[rome|Rome]] (Act IV, Scene 2) and his arrival in [[antium|Antium]] (Act IV, Scene 4). It demonstrates that news of [[rome|Rome]]'s internal turmoil has reached the [[volscians|Volscians]] before [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] himself does, heightening the dramatic irony of his approach. The audience knows, as [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] does not, that the [[volscians|Volscians]] are already aware of his circumstances and may be preparing to exploit them.

This intelligence scene also grounds the play's military dimension in practical political reality. Ancient warfare relied heavily on informants and defectors, and Nicanor's brief appearance reminds the audience that [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s actions have consequences in a wider political landscape that extends beyond [[rome|Rome]]'s walls.

## Dramatic Significance

### Mirror and Foreshadowing

Nicanor is a mirror-figure for [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]: a Roman who serves the [[volscians|Volscians]], just as [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] will soon do. His name — Greek in origin (Νικάνωρ, meaning "victorious man" or "conqueror") — marks him as an outsider in both worlds, a man without a country. In this, he prefigures [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s own tragic condition: "a lonely dragon" who belongs nowhere.

The difference in scale is deliberate and pointed. Nicanor is a petty traitor, motivated (presumably) by payment or self-interest. [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s defection, by contrast, is born of wounded honor and existential rage — it is an act of world-historical consequence that threatens to destroy [[rome|Rome]] itself. By placing the minor traitor immediately before the major one, Shakespeare invites the audience to consider what distinguishes the two. Is [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s betrayal nobler because its motives are grander? Or does the Nicanor scene expose treason as treason, regardless of its rhetoric of justification?

### Intelligence Networks

Nicanor's brief scene also illustrates the intelligence networks that underpinned ancient warfare. The mention of "Adrian" (950) — a [[volscians|Volsci]]an intermediary — suggests an organized espionage apparatus, with multiple handlers and cutouts. This detail, likely drawn from Shakespeare's reading of Roman military history, adds a layer of political realism to the play's representation of the Roman-[[volscians|Volsci]]an conflict.

## Historical Context

The historical Nicanor does not appear in Plutarch's *Life of [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]*. The character is either Shakespeare's invention or was drawn from another source now lost. The name Nicanor appears elsewhere in classical history — most notably as the name of several Macedonian officers and a Syrian general — but Shakespeare's use of a Greek name for a Roman traitor serving the [[volscians|Volscians]] may be deliberate: it marks Nicanor as ethnically ambiguous, a man without clear national [[loyalty|allegiance]].

The historical [[volscians|Volscians]] did employ spies and informants in their conflicts with [[rome|Rome]] during the fifth century BCE. The strategic importance of [[antium|Antium]] as a [[volscians|Volsci]]an stronghold made intelligence about Roman political developments particularly valuable.
