# Body Politic

## Definition

The **body politic** is a metaphor that represents the political community as a single organism, with different social groups corresponding to different bodily organs. In *Coriolanus*, Menenius Agrippa deploys the metaphor to argue that the patrician Senate, like the belly, may appear idle but in fact distributes nourishment to all the body's members — an argument designed to quell plebeian unrest over grain shortages. The metaphor's rhetorical power lies in its apparent naturalness: if the state is a body, then hierarchy is not a political arrangement but a biological necessity, and rebellion against the ruling class is as self-defeating as the hand refusing to feed the mouth.

## In the Play

The body politic metaphor dominates the play's opening scene. Menenius tells the mutinous citizens a fable in which "all the body's members / Rebell'd against the belly" (001-002), accusing it of idleness while they labour. The belly replies that although it receives food first, it "send[s] it through the rivers of your blood" to every part of the body — the Senate, analogously, "digest[s] things rightly / Touching the weal o' the common" for the benefit of all.

Shakespeare's treatment of the metaphor is characteristically ambivalent. Menenius's fable is meant to justify aristocratic rule, but the play's subsequent action — famine, riot, the creation of the tribunate — suggests the body politic is chronically diseased. The First Citizen's sharp rejoinder to Menenius — that the belly is not the Senate but "the cormorant belly" that devours all — exposes the metaphor's vulnerability to counter-interpretation. The same image that naturalizes patrician rule can be inverted to condemn it as parasitism.

Coriolanus himself rejects the metaphor's implication of interdependence, treating the plebeians not as fellow members of a body but as a foreign contagion. When the tribunes challenge his consular ambitions, he erupts with the language of amputation: the people are a "common cry of curs" whose breath he hates (016-002), a "multitudinous tongue" that should be cut from the body politic rather than represented within it. His mother Volumnia later invokes an inverted body metaphor when pleading with him to spare Rome (026-001), urging him to recognize that the body he would destroy is the one that bore him.

## Dramatic Significance

The body politic metaphor is the play's master trope for the problem of political unity. Rome in *Coriolanus* is a body at war with itself — the patrician head cannot command the plebeian limbs, and the people's tribunes have become, from the aristocratic perspective, a cancerous growth in the state. Coriolanus's tragedy is, in part, the tragedy of a man who cannot think organically: his inability to imagine himself as part of a larger social body makes him simultaneously a supreme warrior (the body's sword-arm) and a civic failure (an organ that rejects the organism). When he returns at the head of a Volscian army, the metaphor becomes literal — the body politic's own right hand has turned against it.
