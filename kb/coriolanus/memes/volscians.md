# Volscians

## Definition


### Analysis

The **Volscians** (*Volsci*) were an Italic people who inhabited the region south of [[rome|Rome]] in the hills and plains of Latium during the early Republic. In Shakespeare's *Coriolanus*, they are Rome's principal external enemy — the martial rival against whom Roman *virtus* is tested and proven, and the state that ultimately serves as both refuge and executioner for Rome's greatest soldier.

The Volscians are defined primarily through the figure of their general, **[[tullus-aufidius|Tullus Aufidius]]**, and through the dramatic geography of their cities — [[corioli|Corioli]], [[antium|Antium]], and the [[volscian-territory|Volscian territories]]. They are less fully individuated than the [[patricians|Romans]], functioning in part as a foil: a state that values martial excellence without the complicating presence of [[tribunate|republican institutions]].

> My name is Caius Marcius, who hath done
> To thee particularly, and to all the Volsces,
> Great hurt and mischief. (020-002)

## Key Characteristics

| Trait | Expression in the Play | Dramatic Function |
|-------|----------------------|-------------------|
| **Martial prowess** | Rome's equal in war; Aufidius swears mutual destruction with Coriolanus (002-002) | Provides a credible external threat |
| **Honour-based hierarchy** | Aufidius rules by martial reputation; soldiers adore Coriolanus (023-002) | Mirrors patrician values without republican complications |
| **Hospitality to defectors** | Acceptance of Coriolanus in Antium (021-002) | Demonstrates the principle of martial merit over national identity |
| **Internal rivalry** | Aufidius's jealousy of Coriolanus; conspirators urge assassination (029-002) | Replicates Roman political conflict in military form |
| **Capacity for violence** | The killing of Coriolanus: "Kill, kill, kill, kill, kill him!" (029-002) | The ultimate expression of honour-based justice |

## The Volscian State

Shakespeare's Volscian state is organized around military command rather than [[tribunate|republican institutions]]. The **[[volscian-senate-council|Volscian Senate]]** appears briefly (I.ii), but the real power lies with the general — [[tullus-aufidius|Aufidius]] — whose martial reputation is the foundation of his authority.

This political simplicity is dramatically functional. The Volscians represent a world where [[military-honor|martial honour]] is the sole currency of value — the world Coriolanus wishes Rome could be. His defection to the Volscians is thus not merely a strategic choice but a values choice: he moves from a republic where honour must negotiate with politics to a state where honour is politics.

But this simplicity is also the Volscian state's limitation. When Aufidius's jealousy overrides his martial respect for Coriolanus, there are no institutions to check him — no tribunes to mediate, no Senate to deliberate. The Volscian solution to political conflict is not deliberation but assassination.

## The Volscian Court at Antium

The domestic scene at Aufidius's house in [[antium|Antium]] (IV.v) offers the play's most intimate glimpse of Volscian society. The three servingmen — [[first-servingman]], [[second-servingman]], and [[third-servingman]] — provide a comic counterpoint to the grand political drama unfolding around them. Their banter about Coriolanus's identity, their debate over whether he or Aufidius is the greater soldier, and their remarks on war and peace constitute a miniature plebeian perspective within the Volscian world [021-002].

This scene is structurally significant because it demonstrates that class consciousness is not uniquely Roman. The Volscian servants, like the [[roman-citizens|Roman citizens]], have opinions about their masters, resent being pushed around, and form judgments about the great men who command them. The comic interlude universalizes the play's class dynamics: even in a state organized around martial honour, the common people have their own perspective on power.

The servingmen's closing dialogue — in which the First Servingman contrasts war and peace in one of Shakespeare's most vivid rhetorical set-pieces — transforms comic relief into political commentary:

> Let me have war, say I; it exceeds peace as far as day does night; it's spritely, waking, audible, and full of vent. Peace is a very apoplexy, lethargy; mulled, deaf, sleepy, insensible. [021-008]

The speech is both comic (a servant philosophizing about war) and deeply unsettling — it articulates the martial values that drive the entire Volscian state, distilled through the voice of its lowest member.

## The Conspirators and Volscian Political Culture

The [[conspirators-with-aufidius|conspirators]] who assassinate Coriolanus in the final scene represent the dark side of Volscian political culture. Where Rome resolves political conflicts through institutions (however imperfectly), the Volscian state resolves them through personal violence. The conspirators — [[first-conspirator]], [[second-conspirator]], and [[third-conspirator]] — function as a shadow Senate, deliberating Coriolanus's fate in private and executing their judgment without due process [029-002].

Their conspiracy mirrors the Roman political process that banished Coriolanus, but with a crucial difference: Rome's banishment, though manipulated by the tribunes, was at least nominally a public act with constitutional form. The Volscian conspiracy is entirely private — a handful of disgruntled nobles acting on their general's unspoken wishes. The Volscian state thus reveals itself, in its final action, as what Rome would become without republican institutions: rule by assassination.

## Coriolanus and the Volscians

Coriolanus's relationship to the Volscians follows a chiastic trajectory:

| Phase | Relationship | Outcome |
|-------|-------------|---------|
| **Conquest** (I.iv–ix) | Enemy — captures Corioli | Earns *agnomen* "Coriolanus" |
| **Defection** (IV.v) | Ally — offers services against Rome | Accepted by Aufidius with ambivalent welcome |
| **Command** (V.i–iii) | General — leads Volscian army against Rome | Achieves military dominance, but yields to Volumnia |
| **Death** (V.vi) | Victim — killed by Volscian conspirators | Denounced as traitor, stabbed in Corioli |

The Volscians thus serve as the arc of Coriolanus's entire tragic trajectory: they are the enemy he conquers, the ally he becomes, the instrument of his greatest power, and the agents of his death. The city that gave him his name — Corioli — is also the city where he dies.

## Volscians as Mirror

The Volscian state functions as a dramatic mirror of Rome:

| Aspect | Rome | Volsci |
|--------|------|--------|
| **Political structure** | Republican (Senate + Tribunes) | Military-aristocratic |
| **Core value** | *Virtus* + *eloquentia* (martial + rhetorical) | *Virtus* alone (martial) |
| **Conflict mode** | Internal (class conflict) | External (war) |
| **Treatment of Coriolanus** | Banishes him for political incompatibility | Welcomes him for martial excellence |
| **Ultimate judgment** | Political (banishment) | Physical (death) |

Both states destroy Coriolanus — Rome through political process, the Volscians through physical violence. The mirroring suggests that Coriolanus is impossible in any polity: the republic cannot accommodate his contempt, and the honour-state cannot tolerate his divided loyalties.

## Historical Background

The historical Volsci were an Oscan-speaking Italic people who occupied the Liris River valley and the Pomptine region south of Rome. They were among Rome's most persistent enemies during the early Republic, with warfare continuing from the late 6th through the 4th centuries BCE.

The historical Caius Marcius Coriolanus's capture of Corioli and subsequent defection to the Volscians are recorded in Livy (II.33–40) and Plutarch's *Life of Coriolanus*, Shakespeare's primary source. The Volscians were eventually conquered by Rome and absorbed into the Roman state through the Latin colonies and the extension of Roman citizenship.
