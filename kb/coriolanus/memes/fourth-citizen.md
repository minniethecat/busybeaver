# Fourth Citizen

The **Fourth Citizen** is a plebeian character in Shakespeare's *[[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]*, appearing alongside the other citizens in the Forum scene (Act II, Scene III). Where the [[third-citizen|Third Citizen]] is witty and philosophical, the Fourth Citizen is direct and plain-spoken, delivering three blunt assessments of [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s paradoxical relationship with the Roman people.

## In the Forum: The Three Speeches

When [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] presents himself in the "gown of humility" to solicit the people's voices for the [[consulship|consul]]ship, the Fourth Citizen refuses to flatter or equivocate. His three remarks form a terse, almost choral summary of the central tension in [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s political identity.

### First Speech: The Paradox of Merit

> You have deserved nobly of your country, and you have not deserved nobly. (015-003)

This opening paradox captures the duality of [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] — a military hero whose contempt for the commons renders him politically unacceptable. The Fourth Citizen speaks with a directness absent from his fellows: the [[first-citizen|First Citizen]] calculates politically, the Second wavers, and the Third deploys elaborate metaphor. Only the Fourth cuts straight to the contradiction.

### Second Speech: The Rod and the Scourge

> You have been a scourge to her enemies, you have been a rod to her friends; you have not indeed loved the common people. (015-003)

The distinction between "scourge" (a whip used against enemies) and "rod" (an instrument of discipline wielded against one's own) is precise and devastating. The Fourth Citizen's vocabulary implies that [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] treats Roman citizens — his putative "friends" — with the same punitive instrument he uses against enemies, merely in different measure. The closing clause — "you have not indeed loved the common people" — is the plainest possible statement of the truth [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] himself cannot dissemble.

### Third Speech: The Concession of Wounds

> You have received many wounds for your country. (015-003)

This final remark is not a retraction but a concession — the grudging acknowledgment of physical sacrifice that even [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s harshest plebeian critic cannot deny. The Fourth Citizen's three speeches, read in sequence, form a miniature argument: acknowledgment of merit, exposure of the flaw that undoes it, and a closing concession that renders the judgment not condemnation but honest appraisal.

## Dramatic Function

The Fourth Citizen serves as the plain-speaking conscience of the citizen body. Unlike the [[third-citizen|Third Citizen]]'s elaborate wordplay or the [[first-citizen|First Citizen]]'s political calculation, the Fourth Citizen speaks directly from a plebeian perspective — giving voice to the commons' genuine ambivalence about a patrician hero who despises them. His bluntness has an almost choral quality, distilling the political conflict of the play into a handful of terse, memorable lines.
