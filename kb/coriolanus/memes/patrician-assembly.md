# Patrician Assembly

## Event Summary

The Patrician Assembly of Act III (Scenes I–III) is the most explosive political confrontation in *Coriolanus*. It begins as the patricians attempt to escort Coriolanus to the marketplace to complete his consular ratification, and ends with his death sentence (commuted to banishment). The scene stages the collision between two constitutional authorities — the Senate and the tribunate — each claiming supremacy, neither willing to yield. Coriolanus's refusal to moderate his contempt for the plebeian order, combined with the tribunes' procedural cunning, transforms a political dispute into a constitutional crisis that the "double worship" of Rome's mixed constitution cannot resolve.

## The Confrontation (Act III, Scene I)

### The Tribunes' Blockade

As Coriolanus, escorted by senators and Cominius, prepares to enter the marketplace, Sicinius and Brutus intercept him: "Pass no further... It will be dangerous to go on" (014-002). Their declaration that the plebeians have revoked their consent transforms a ceremonial ratification into a constitutional standoff. The First Senator attempts to overrule them — "Tribunes, give way; he shall to the market-place" (014-002) — but Brutus replies: "The people are incensed against him" (014-002).

Coriolanus immediately identifies the tribunes' maneuver as a plot: "It is a purposed thing, and grows by plot, / To curb the will of the nobility" (014-002). His analysis is correct but his response is catastrophic: rather than submit to the procedural delay that Menenius and Cominius counsel, he launches into the most politically destructive speech of the play.

### Coriolanus's Constitutional Attack

Brutus accuses Coriolanus of having mocked the people during the election ritual and of having opposed the free corn distribution — charges that are factually true. Coriolanus's response is not to deny the charges but to amplify them:

> "In soothing them, we nourish 'gainst our senate / The cockle of rebellion, insolence, sedition, / Which we ourselves have plough'd for, sow'd, and scatter'd, / By mingling them with us, the honour'd number" (014-002)

The word "mingling" is the ideological core of the speech. The constitutional sin, in Coriolanus's view, is not the tribunes' existence but the very idea that patricians and plebeians should share political power. The "honour'd number" — the patriciate — has contaminated itself by admitting the plebeians into the constitution.

He then delivers his most explicit attack on the tribunate as an institution:

> "O good but most unwise patricians! why, / You grave but reckless senators, have you thus / Given Hydra here to choose an officer, / That with his peremptory 'shall'... wants not spirit / To say he'll turn your current in a ditch, / And make your channel his?" (014-002)

The Hydra metaphor — the many-headed monster of Greek myth — reduces the plebeian body to a shapeless, multiplying horror. The tribune is merely "the horn and noise o' the monster's" (014-002) — a sounding tube through which the Hydra's many voices pass. The constitutional crisis is captured in a single line: "when two authorities are up, / Neither supreme, how soon confusion / May enter 'twixt the gap of both and take / The one by the other" (014-002). Coriolanus identifies the structural flaw in the mixed constitution — competing sovereignties — and his solution is not reform but abolition: "pluck out / The multitudinous tongue" (014-002).

### The Tribunes' Counter-Offensive

Sicinius seizes the opening Coriolanus has given him: "Has spoken like a traitor, and shall answer / As traitors do" (014-002). The charge of treason, previously rhetorical, now becomes juridical. When Brutus declares Coriolanus must be thrown from the Tarpeian Rock — the traditional Roman execution for traitors — the confrontation becomes physical.

Coriolanus draws his sword: "Hence, rotten thing! or I shall shake thy bones / Out of thy garments" (014-002). The tribunes call in the plebeian mob, the senators call for weapons, and the scene erupts into chaos:

> "Tribunes!" "Patricians!" "Citizens!" "What, ho!" "Sicinius!" "Brutus!" "Coriolanus!" "Citizens!" "Peace, peace, peace!" "Stay, hold, peace!" (014-002)

Shakespeare's stage direction has all parties shouting simultaneously — a rare moment of pure theatrical cacophony that registers the collapse of all constitutional order into noise.

### The Senate's Failed Defense

Menenius and Cominius attempt a dual strategy: calm Coriolanus while placating the tribunes. Menenius negotiates for a trial by due process rather than summary execution — "Where he shall answer, by a lawful form, / In peace, to his utmost peril" (014-002). This is a tactical retreat that preserves the forms of law while conceding the tribunes' jurisdiction.

The senators' physical defense of Coriolanus — "We'll surety him" (014-002) — demonstrates aristocratic solidarity but also institutional weakness. They cannot command; they can only guarantee. Their "surety" requires Coriolanus to submit to a process he despises, and it is Menenius, not any senator, who must persuade Coriolanus to leave the forum.

## Volumnia's Political Intervention (Act III, Scene II)

At home, Volumnia attempts what the senators could not: persuade Coriolanus to perform political submission. Her argument is frank and pragmatic:

> "I have heard you say, / Honour and policy, like unsever'd friends, / I' the war do grow together: grant that, and tell me, / In peace what each of them by the other lose, / That they combine not there" (015-002)

Volumnia argues that deception in war (stratagems, feints, ambushes) is honored as "policy," but deception in politics is condemned as dishonor — yet they are the same skill. She urges Coriolanus to return to the marketplace and "speak to the people... not by your own instruction, / Nor by the matter which your heart prompts you, / But with such words that are but roted in / Your tongue" (015-002). The verb "roted" — learned by rote, mechanically — captures the purely performative submission she requires: say the words without meaning them.

Coriolanus's resistance is existential: "Would you have me / False to my nature? Rather say I play / The man I am" (015-002). The pun on "play" — to perform, to act — reveals the core of his tragedy. To perform anything other than what he is would be to "play" a part, to become an actor. And for Coriolanus, the theater of politics is indistinguishable from self-betrayal.

He relents, but his concession is ominous: "I will do't... Away, my disposition, and possess me / Some harlot's spirit!" (015-002). To perform political submission is, in his vocabulary, to become a whore — to sell his body (his voice, his wounds, his honor) for the price of the consulship.

## The Trial (Act III, Scene III)

The trial in the marketplace immediately collapses. Sicinius, in control of the proceedings, dictates the charges: "Marcius would have all from you" (016-002). When Coriolanus attempts to respond, Sicinius provokes him with the word "traitor," and Coriolanus erupts:

> "The common file — a plague! — tribunes for them! / The mouse ne'er shunn'd the cat as they do budge / From rascals worse than they" (016-002)

The tribunes pronounce sentence: banishment. Coriolanus's response is the play's most famous line: "I banish you!" (017-002). He reverses the sentence, exiling Rome from himself, the city from its greatest defender:

> "You common cry of curs! whose breath I hate / As reek o' the rotten fens, whose loves I prize / As the dead carcases of unburied men / That do corrupt my air, I banish you" (017-002)

The patrician assembly's failure is complete. They could neither control their champion nor resist his destruction. The "double worship" — the mixed constitution of Senate and tribunate — has produced not balance but paralysis, and from paralysis, catastrophe.

## Dramatic Significance

The Patrician Assembly represents the structural failure of the Roman mixed constitution. Shakespeare presents a political order with two competing claims to sovereignty — the patrician claim based on merit and tradition, the tribunician claim based on popular mandate — and no mechanism for resolving their conflict. The Senate's authority cannot command the tribunes; the tribunes' authority can destroy but cannot govern.

Coriolanus's speech on the "two authorities" (014-002) is Shakespeare's most explicit piece of constitutional analysis, diagnosing the fatal gap between competing sovereignties. The tragedy is that Coriolanus is right about the problem — "how soon confusion / May enter 'twixt the gap of both" — but his solution (abolish the tribunate, silence the multitude) is politically impossible and morally monstrous.

## Aftermath and Consequences

The immediate consequence is Coriolanus's banishment — the sentence that sets the play's second half in motion. The deeper consequence is the exposure of the Senate's institutional weakness: patrician solidarity, the senators' "surety," and Cominius's advocacy all prove insufficient against the tribunes' ability to mobilize the plebeian mob. The patricians have power without popular legitimacy; the tribunes have legitimacy without administrative capacity. The result is constitutional deadlock resolved only by Coriolanus's self-destruction.

Menenius's desperate attempt to preserve the forms of law — the trial, the "lawful form" — is the noblest gesture of the scene and the most futile. Due process, in a system where the two authorities are "neither supreme," becomes a weapon rather than a safeguard. The tribunes use the forms of law to destroy their enemy; the patricians cannot use those same forms to save their champion. The Patrician Assembly thus marks not merely Coriolanus's personal downfall but the collapse of the constitutional settlement that the first secession had created.
