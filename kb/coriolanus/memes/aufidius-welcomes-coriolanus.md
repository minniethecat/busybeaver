# Aufidius Welcomes Coriolanus

## Event Summary

After his banishment from Rome, Coriolanus travels alone to Antium, the capital of the Volscian state, and presents himself at the house of his mortal enemy, Tullus Aufidius. Admitted by servingmen who fail to recognize him, Coriolanus reveals his identity to Aufidius and offers either his life or his service. Aufidius, after an initial moment of stunned recognition, embraces his former enemy with extraordinary warmth, offering him joint command of the Volscian forces and hospitality among the Volscian senators. This scene (Act IV, Scene V) marks the dramatic turning point from Coriolanus's personal tragedy to a geopolitical crisis — the alliance of Rome's greatest general with its greatest enemy.

> My name is Caius Marcius, who hath done / To thee particularly and to all the Volsces / Great hurt and mischief.
> — Coriolanus revealing himself to Aufidius, Act IV, Scene V

## The Arrival at Antium

Coriolanus arrives at Antium in "mean apparel, disguised and muffled" (Act IV, Scene IV), having traveled alone from Rome. He pauses before the city gates to reflect on its significance — Antium is where Aufidius lives, the man he has fought in single combat and whose city he helped conquer. His soliloquy reveals a man who has abandoned all identity except his name: "only that name remains."

Entering Aufidius's house during a feast, Coriolanus is met by servingmen who fail to recognize the shabby stranger. They mock and dismiss him — "Where dwellest thou?" — but Coriolanus's commanding presence eventually compels one to fetch Aufidius.

## The Revelation

When Aufidius enters and demands the stranger's name, Coriolanus prepares him for the shock:

> Prepare thy brow to frown: know'st thou me yet?
> — Coriolanus, Act IV, Scene V

Aufidius does not recognize him, having last seen Coriolanus in armor on the battlefield. Coriolanus then reveals himself in one of the play's most potent speeches:

> My name is Caius Marcius, who hath done / To thee particularly and to all the Volsces / Great hurt and mischief; thereto witness may / My surname, Coriolanus: the painful service, / The extreme dangers and the drops of blood / Shed for my thankless country are requited / But with that surname ... only that name remains; / The cruelty and envy of the people, / Permitted by our dastard nobles, who / Have all forsook me, hath devour'd the rest.
> — Coriolanus, Act IV, Scene V

He offers Aufidius a stark choice: take his life in revenge, or accept his service against Rome.

## Aufidius's Response

Aufidius's response is extraordinary. Rather than seizing the opportunity to kill his enemy, he embraces Coriolanus with an intensity that borders on the erotic:

> Let me twine / Mine arms about that body, where against / My grained ash an hundred times hath broke / And scarr'd the moon with splinters.
> — Aufidius, Act IV, Scene V

Aufidius confesses that he has dreamed of Coriolanus nightly — "We have been down together in my sleep, Unbuckling helms, fisting each other's throat, And waked half dead with nothing." His welcome goes beyond pragmatic military alliance into something more personal: he declares that even if the Volscians had no other quarrel with Rome, Coriolanus's banishment alone would justify mustering an army "From twelve to seventy, and pouring war Into the bowels of ungrateful Rome."

Aufidius immediately brings Coriolanus to meet the Volscian senators, who are "taking their leaves of me, Who am prepared against your territories, Though not for Rome itself." He offers Coriolanus joint command: "Take the one half of my commission."

## The Servingmen's Commentary

The scene includes an extended comic interlude from the servingmen, who reflect on the encounter in prose. Their commentary provides a ground-level perspective on the extraordinary event they have witnessed. One observes: "I did not think he had been such a man: his face did bear a command in it." Another notes the irony: "Why, he is so made on here within, as if he were son and heir to Mars."

Their discussion also foreshadows the eventual conflict between Coriolanus and Aufidius, as one servingman observes: "I think our general is one o' the fairest men o' the world, and yet methinks he looks not so fair as Coriolanus." The rivalry, even in welcome, is already visible to the humblest observers.

## Dramatic Function

This scene is the fulcrum of the play's action. Before it, Coriolanus is a Roman general destroyed by Roman politics; after it, he is the leader of a foreign army marching on his own city. The scene's power lies in the contrast between Coriolanus's expectation and Aufidius's response — instead of the expected execution, he receives an embrace. Shakespeare suggests that true hatred and true love are closer than we imagine: Aufidius's welcome is born of the same intensity as his enmity. The scene also plants the seeds of the final tragedy, as Aufidius's warmth will later curdle into jealousy and conspiracy.
