# Volscian Soldiers

## Character Overview

The Volscian Soldiers play a crucial structural role — their adulation of [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] during his exile shifts the military balance and provokes Aufidius's jealousy. Their transfer of [[loyalty|allegiance]] from Aufidius to [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] demonstrates the volatility of martial loyalty and the power of demonstrated *virtus* to transcend national boundaries.

## Dramatic Role

The [[volscians|Volsci]]an soldiers are the martial instrument of [[rome|Rome]]'s enemy state, appearing in the battle sequences and later in the [[volscians|Volsci]]an camp (P01-017). After [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s defection, they embrace him with remarkable fervour: "Your soldiers use him as the grace 'fore meat, / Their talk at table" (023-002). This enthusiasm for the former Roman hero reveals a tension within the [[volscians|Volsci]]an camp — the soldiers' admiration for [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] displaces Aufidius from his position as the natural centre of [[volscians|Volsci]]an martial identity, fueling the jealousy that leads to the play's fatal conclusion. In the final scene, they are the instruments of [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s death, urged by Aufidius to "Cut me to pieces" (029-003).
