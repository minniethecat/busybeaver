# Young Marcius

Young Marcius is the son of [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Caius Marcius Coriolanus]] and [[virgilia]], and grandson of [[volumnia]]. Listed in the Dramatis Personae as "son to [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]" [P01-007], he appears in only three scenes but serves a crucial dramatic function: he is the living embodiment of [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s domestic ties — the one bond that ultimately overcomes the warrior's implacable pride.

## Character Portrait

Young Marcius is a mirror in miniature of his father. [[valeria|Valeria]]'s report in Act I, Scene 3 provides the most vivid portrait of the boy's character:

> "I saw him run after a gilded butterfly; and when he caught it, he let it go again; and after it again; and over and over he comes, and up again; catched it again; or whether his fall enraged him, or how 'twas, he did so set his teeth and tear it; O, I warrant, how he mammocked it!" [001-164]

This anecdote — the boy chasing, releasing, recapturing, and finally tearing apart a butterfly — encapsulates the Coriolanian temperament in childhood form: relentless, destructive, incapable of letting go. The verb "mammocked" (tore to shreds) is particularly violent, suggesting a native aggression that [[volumnia|Volumnia]]'s upbringing has channeled rather than created.

[[volumnia|Volumnia]]'s delighted response confirms that she sees in her grandson the same martial spirit she cultivated in her son: "One on's father's moods" (001-165). The butterfly-hunting child is not an innocent; he is already a small [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]], shaped by a culture that equates masculinity with destruction. [[valeria|Valeria]]'s anecdote is one of Shakespeare's most economical pieces of character establishment: in a single reported action, it sketches an entire genealogy of violence.

## Dramatic Function

In the play's structural economy, Young Marcius functions as a dramatic symbol rather than a developed character. His three appearances trace the arc of [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s relationship with [[rome|Rome]]:

1. **Act I, Scene 3** (001-164): Present as [[volumnia|Volumnia]] and [[valeria|Valeria]] discuss [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s campaign. Young Marcius does not speak, but the butterfly anecdote establishes him as his father's heir in temperament. His silent presence in this domestic scene anchors [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] in a world of family ties that the warrior himself seems to transcend.

2. **Act IV, Scene 1** (004-001ff): Bids farewell to his father as [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] departs into exile. The stage direction reads: "Enter [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]], [[volumnia|Volumnia]], [[virgilia|Virgilia]], and young [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Marcius]]." His inclusion in this leave-taking tableau underscores the domestic cost of political expulsion — [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] is not only banished from [[rome|Rome]] but exiled from his own son.

3. **Act V, Scene 3** (005-1254): The climactic family embassy. [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] sees "my young boy" kneeling before him alongside his mother and wife. He confesses:

> "My young boy / Hath an aspect of intercession, which / Great nature cries 'Deny not.'" [005-1255]

This moment — the sight of his son in supplication — is the final emotional lever that breaks [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s resolve. His decision to spare [[rome|Rome]], sealed by this family tableau, leads directly to his death at the hands of the [[volscians|Volscians]].

## Character Relationships

- **[[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Caius Marcius Coriolanus]] (father)** — Young Marcius mirrors his father's martial temperament from earliest childhood. [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s love for his son is the most unguarded of his emotional attachments, and it is exploited — whether intentionally or not — by [[volumnia|Volumnia]] in the final plea for [[rome|Rome]]. The father who refuses to show vulnerability to the Roman populace cannot refuse his own child.

- **[[virgilia|Virgilia]] (mother)** — The boy appears alongside his mother in all three of his scenes, reinforcing the play's association of the domestic sphere with the feminine and the martial sphere with the masculine. [[virgilia|Virgilia]]'s famous silence (she speaks far less than [[volumnia|Volumnia]]) is echoed in Young Marcius's own wordlessness: both are ciphers of domesticity in a play dominated by martial rhetoric.

- **[[volumnia|Volumnia]] (grandmother)** — [[volumnia|Volumnia]] delights in Young Marcius's precocious aggression, seeing in him the continuation of the martial lineage she shaped in [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]. Her pride in the boy's butterfly-killing reveals the self-perpetuating nature of [[rome|Rome]]'s warrior culture: violence is not innate but taught, generation after generation.

## Literary Interpretation

Young Marcius represents a recurring motif in Shakespeare's Roman plays: the child as political symbol and emotional weapon. Critics have noted parallels with Lucius in *Titus Andronicus* and the young son of Marc Antony in *Antony and Cleopatra* — all are children whose presence on stage serves to humanize a warrior protagonist at the moment of crisis.

Young Marcius's near-silence (he has no speaking lines in the Folio text) amplifies his symbolic function: he is less a character than a living argument for mercy, a silent reproach to his father's destructive pride. The wordless child kneeling before the wordless warrior — neither can articulate what passes between them, yet the bond is strong enough to move [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] where all the arguments of [[cominius|Cominius]], [[menenius-agrippa|Menenius]], and the Roman senators could not.

This silence is thematically significant. In a play dominated by persuasive speech — the tribunes' manipulation, [[menenius-agrippa|Menenius]]'s fables, [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s curses, [[volumnia|Volumnia]]'s rhetoric — it is the wordless presence of a child that proves most persuasive. Shakespeare suggests that the deepest human bonds transcend language, even as the play demonstrates language's power to destroy.

## Historical Context

In Plutarch's *Life of [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]*, the family embassy is described with particular attention to the visual tableau: the women and children kneeling before the conquering general. Shakespeare follows his source closely in this respect, but he reduces Plutarch's two sons to a single Young Marcius, concentrating the emotional force of fatherhood into one figure.

The Roman concept of *pietas* — duty to family, gods, and state — is embodied in Young Marcius. By kneeling to his son (who kneels to him), [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] at last fulfills the demands of *pietas*, but at the cost of his loyalty to the [[volscians|Volscians]]. The tragedy lies in the irreconcilability of these duties: to be a good father, [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] must become a traitor.
