# Banishment of Coriolanus

## Overview

The **Banishment of Coriolanus** is the tragic pivot of Shakespeare's play — the political act that transforms Rome's greatest soldier into its deadliest enemy. Engineered by the tribunes [[sicinius-velutus|Sicinius Velutus]] and [[junius-brutus|Junius Brutus]], exploiting both [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus's]] aristocratic pride and the constitutional mechanisms of the [[tribunate|Roman Republic]], the banishment is at once a political triumph for the [[plebeian-rights|plebeian cause]] and a strategic catastrophe for the state.

The banishment scene (III.iii) is one of Shakespeare's most concentrated studies in political theatre: a public proceeding that is nominally a trial but structurally a ritual of expulsion, where each participant — tribunes, plebeians, patricians, and Coriolanus himself — plays a predetermined role in a drama whose outcome is foreordained.

> You common cry of curs! whose breath I hate
> As reek o' th' rotten fens … I banish you! (016-002)

## The Tribunes' Strategy

[[sicinius-velutus|Sicinius]] and [[junius-brutus|Brutus]] execute a masterful piece of political manipulation. Their strategy operates on multiple levels:

1. **Provocation**: They know Coriolanus's temper and deliberately provoke his rage. Every calm assertion of tribunician authority is calculated to trigger his contempt for popular institutions.
2. **Framing**: They reframe Coriolanus's military service — his twenty-seven wounds — not as sacrifice but as evidence of a violent nature unsuited to civil society.
3. **Procedural Legitimacy**: They use every mechanism of the [[tribunate|tribunician constitution]]: the right to summon the people, the power of accusation, the authority to set the terms of judgment.
4. **Mob Management**: The plebeians are not autonomous actors but a chorus orchestrated by the tribunes. Their "voices" — the same voices that approved Coriolanus's consulship — are now marshalled against him.

> Sicinius: "We charge you that you have contriv'd to take / From Rome all season'd office, and to wind / Yourself into a power tyrannical." (015-001)

## Coriolanus's Complicity

The banishment is not simply done *to* Coriolanus — it is enabled by his own character. At every point where political prudence might have saved him, his [[pride|aristocratic pride]] drives him toward self-destruction:

- When [[menenius-agrippa|Menenius]] urges him to return to the Forum and speak mildly, he cannot sustain the performance for more than a few lines
- When the tribunes call him "traitor," he immediately escalates — "The fires i' th' lowest hell fold in the people!" (015-002)
- When the sentence is pronounced, rather than accept it with dignity, he reverses it rhetorically — "I banish you!" — a magnificent retort that reveals his inability to accept any authority beyond his own

Coriolanus's banishment is thus a collaboration between his enemies and his own character. The tribunes provide the mechanism; Coriolanus provides the justification.

## Constitutional Crisis

The banishment exposes a fundamental flaw in the Roman republican constitution. A system designed to balance [[patrician-power|patrician military excellence]] against [[plebeian-rights|plebeian popular sovereignty]] has no mechanism for handling a figure like Coriolanus — a man whose military virtue is too great to be ignored and whose political contempt is too profound to be accommodated.

The patricians, caught between their admiration for Coriolanus and their commitment to constitutional norms, are paralyzed. [[cominius|Cominius]] protests the sentence but cannot prevent it. The Senate watches as its greatest soldier is expelled by a popular vote — the ultimate demonstration that the [[tribunate|tribunician order]] has become more powerful than the patrician order it was designed to balance.

## Consequences

The banishment's consequences unfold with the inexorability of Greek tragedy:

| Consequence | Scene | Dramatic Logic |
|------------|-------|----------------|
| [[coriolanus-defection|Defection to Aufidius]] | IV.v | Rome's soldier becomes Rome's enemy |
| Volscian invasion of Rome | V.i–iii | The military consequence |
| [[volumnia-intervention|Volumnia's intervention]] | V.iii | The one force that can stop Coriolanus |
| [[death-of-coriolanus|Death at Corioli]] | V.vi | Aufidius exploits Coriolanus's mercy as weakness |

The banishment is the play's point of no return. Once Coriolanus passes through the [[the-gates-of-rome|Gates of Rome]], there is no path back — only the long arc toward the Volscian swords that await him at [[corioli|Corioli]].

## Dramatic Structure

Shakespeare places the banishment at the exact centre of the play's architecture (Act III of V), making it the axis around which the entire tragedy rotates. Everything before it tends toward it; everything after it follows from it. This structural centrality reflects the event's thematic weight: the banishment is the moment when the tension between [[military-honor|martial excellence]] and [[class-conflict|political order]] becomes irreconcilable.
