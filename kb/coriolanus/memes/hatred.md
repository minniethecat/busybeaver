## Definition

Hatred — rendered as "hate," "malice," "enmity," and "spite" — is the emotional infrastructure of *Coriolanus*. It operates at every level: personal (Coriolanus and Aufidius), class-based (patrician and plebeian), and national (Rome and the Volscians). Unlike anger, which is transient and explosive, hatred is presented as enduring and structural — a settled disposition rather than a passing passion.

## Historical Background

In classical and Renaissance thought, hatred (*odium*) was distinguished from anger (*ira*) by its durability. Thomas Aquinas categorised hatred as a capital vice directed at a person's very being, not merely their actions. This distinction illuminates Coriolanus: he does not merely resent the plebeians' actions; he hates what they *are*.

## In the Play

Coriolanus's hatred of the plebeians is established in his first speech: "He that will give good words to thee will flatter / Beneath abhorring... Who deserves greatness / Deserves your hate" (001-080). The paradox is revealing: greatness and plebeian hatred are mutually constitutive in his worldview — if the people do not hate you, you are not great.

The mutual hatred between Coriolanus and Aufidius is the play's central personal animus. Their confrontation at Corioli distills it: "I'll fight with none but thee; for I do hate thee / Worse than a promise-breaker," declares Coriolanus; "We hate alike: / Not Afric owns a serpent I abhor / More than thy fame and envy," replies Aufidius (002-295, 002-296). This is hatred as recognition — each defines himself against the other.

Aufidius's soliloquy after the battle reveals hatred's corrosive power: "My valour's poisoned / With only suffering stain by him... Embarquements all of fury, shall lift up / Their rotten privilege and custom 'gainst / My hate to Marcius" (003-338). Hatred here is not merely an emotion but a contamination that spreads from valour to the soul itself.

The tribunes weaponise class hatred against Coriolanus. Brutus urges: "We must suggest the people in what hatred / He still hath held them" (004-438). Sicinius follows: "enforce his pride, / And his old hate unto you... After the inveterate hate he bears you" (003-585). The strategy is to transform the people's wavering loyalty into settled hatred by demonstrating that their hatred is reciprocated.

The serving officers provide a meta-commentary on this dynamic. The First Officer notes that Coriolanus "seeks their hate with greater devotion than they can render it him" (004-455). The Second Officer agrees: "if they love they know not why, they hate upon no better a ground" (004-454). Hatred, in this analysis, is less a response to injury than a performative stance — the fickle people hate as capriciously as they love.

In exile, Coriolanus's hatred undergoes a geographical inversion: "My birth-place hate I, and my love's upon / This enemy town" (009-978). Hatred of Rome now drives him toward Antium — but this hatred is unstable, as the climactic scene proves.

## Relationships

Hatred is the enduring form of [[anger]] and the emotional expression of [[pride]]. It structures the [[class-conflict]] between patricians and plebeians and fuels the rivalry with [[Aufidius]]. The concept is dialectically linked to [[loyalty]]: Coriolanus's hatred of Rome drives his temporary loyalty to the Volscians.

## Critical Perspectives

William Hazlitt noted that *Coriolanus* is "a play about the poetry of power and the prose of hatred." More recently, psychoanalytic critics have read Coriolanus's hatred of the plebeians as a form of narcissistic abjection — he hates what reminds him of his own dependency. Political critics see the play's hatred economy as a study in how class animus can be mobilised by political actors (the tribunes) for instrumental ends.
