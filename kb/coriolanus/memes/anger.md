## Definition

Anger — rendered as "wrath," "rage," "choler," and "fury" as well as the plain term — is the emotional keynote of *Coriolanus*. It is the hero's defining passion, the political weapon his enemies wield against him, and the force that drives every major turning point in the plot. Unlike the cold, calculating anger of a Richard III, Coriolanus's anger is hot, visible, and self-destructive — "his heart's his mouth" (004-725).

## Historical Background

In Renaissance physiology, anger was associated with choler, one of the four humours, produced by an excess of yellow bile. The choleric temperament was linked to the element of fire and considered characteristic of soldiers and rulers. Shakespeare's audience would have recognised Coriolanus as a textbook choleric: quick to ignite, incapable of dissimulation, ultimately consumed by his own heat.

## In the Play

Anger manifests in Coriolanus from his first appearance. Before Corioli, he is already "sweat[ing] with wrath" at the Volscians' defiance (002-213). His martial fury is celebrated as valour — "wrath o'erwhelmed my pity" (003-323) is offered as explanation, not apology, for his ferocity in battle.

The turning point comes when the tribunes weaponise his choler. Sicinius explicitly schemes to provoke "his surly nature, / Which easily endures not article / Tying him to aught; so putting him to rage" (003-578). Brutus concurs: "If, as his nature is, he fall in rage / With their refusal, both observe and answer / The vantage of his anger" (003-592). Anger is no longer a character trait but a political instrument — predictable, exploitable, and ultimately fatal.

Menenius recognises the danger but cannot prevent it: "Put not your worthy rage into your tongue; / One time will owe another" (004-718). His subsequent description — "This tiger-footed rage, when it shall find / The harm of unscanned swiftness, will too late / Tie leaden pounds to's heels" (004-752) — frames anger as a form of reckless speed, an emotional momentum that cannot be braked.

Cominius extends the metaphor: the people's "rage doth rend / Like interrupted waters and o'erbear / What they are used to bear" (004-721). Anger is not unique to the hero; it is the emotional currency of the entire polity.

The most striking treatment of anger comes from Aufidius, whose hatred of Coriolanus is itself a kind of rage: "My valour's poisoned / With only suffering stain by him; for him / Shall fly out of itself: nor sleep nor sanctuary... Embarquements all of fury, shall lift up / Their rotten privilege and custom 'gainst / My hate to Marcius" (003-338). Aufidius's fury mirrors Coriolanus's — two warriors whose honour can only be satisfied by the other's destruction.

Volumnia provides the only model of anger management in the play: "I have a heart as little apt as yours, / But yet a brain that leads my use of anger / To better vantage" (004-781). She, too, is choleric — but her anger is strategic, not impulsive. This is the lesson Coriolanus cannot learn.

## Relationships

Anger is the emotional expression of [[pride]] and the behavioural manifestation of his [[nature]]. The tribunes' exploitation of his anger connects directly to [[class-conflict]] and [[populism]].

## Critical Perspectives

Seneca's *De Ira* (On Anger) was a key intertext for Renaissance treatments of wrath. Coriolanus can be read as a Senecan case study: anger as temporary madness that destroys the angry man before it harms his enemies. More recent criticism has focused on anger as a class marker — patrician rage is "noble," plebeian rage is "mutiny" — and on the gendering of anger (Volumnia's controlled anger versus Coriolanus's explosive rage).
