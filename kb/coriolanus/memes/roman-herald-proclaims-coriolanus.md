# Roman Herald Proclaims Coriolanus

## Event Summary

The Herald's proclamation (Act II, Scene I) is the public ceremony that transforms Marcius's military victory at Corioli into a formal civic honor. As Coriolanus enters Rome crowned with an oaken garland, flanked by Cominius and Titus Lartius, the Herald announces to the assembled people the deeds that have earned him his new name. The proclamation serves as the ritual bridge between the private battlefield honor of Act I and the political office of consul that Act II will confer. The ceremony also marks the only moment in the play when the Roman state formally and publicly celebrates Coriolanus without reservation — a moment of unity that the tribunes immediately begin to undermine.

> Know, Rome, that all alone Marcius did fight / Within Corioli gates: where he hath won, / With fame, a name to Caius Marcius; these / In honour follows Coriolanus. / Welcome to Rome, renowned Coriolanus!
> — Herald, Act II, Scene I

## The Triumphal Entry

The scene opens with Menenius and the tribunes in a public place. A flourish of trumpets signals the approach of the returning army, and Volumnia identifies the sound: "These are the ushers of Marcius: before him he carries noise, and behind him he leaves tears." A messenger confirms that Marcius is approaching "crowned with an oaken garland" — the corona civica, Rome's highest military decoration for saving a fellow citizen's life in battle.

The triumphal procession enters with full ceremonial pomp: a sennet sounds, trumpets blare, and between Cominius and Lartius walks Coriolanus, visibly marked by battle. Volumnia observes her son's wounds with a mother's pride: "every gash was an enemy's grave."

## The Herald's Announcement

The Herald steps forward and delivers the formal proclamation. His words are concise — a single sentence — yet they perform a monumental act of political creation:

> Know, Rome, that all alone Marcius did fight / Within Corioli gates: where he hath won, / With fame, a name to Caius Marcius; these / In honour follows Coriolanus. / Welcome to Rome, renowned Coriolanus!
> — Herald, Act II, Scene I

Each element of the proclamation carries legal and ritual weight. "Know, Rome" — the imperative formula of a public herald, addressing the entire citizen body. "All alone Marcius did fight Within Corioli gates" — the singular deed that justifies the honor. "With fame, a name to Caius Marcius" — the conversion of battlefield action into permanent identity, the private name "Marcius" augmented by the public name "Coriolanus." Finally, "Welcome to Rome, renowned Coriolanus!" — the civic embrace that completes the ritual.

The crowd responds in unison: "Welcome to Rome, renowned Coriolanus!" The collective acclamation is not mere applause — it constitutes the people's formal ratification of the honor the Senate has bestowed. Without this public echo, the name "Coriolanus" would remain a military nickname rather than a civic title.

## Coriolanus's Discomfort

As with every moment of public honor in the play, Coriolanus immediately attempts to shut it down. His first words upon hearing the people's acclamation are: "No more of this; it does offend my heart." He begs to be allowed to greet his family rather than endure further ceremony.

Menenius, ever the mediator between Coriolanus and the political world, steps in: "The gods of Rome, for your stay! ... A hundred thousand welcomes." He moves the focus from public ceremony to personal greeting, directing Coriolanus toward his mother and wife. The Herald, the professional manager of public ritual, resumes his function — "Give way there, and go on!" — clearing a path through the crowd.

Coriolanus greets Volumnia and Virgilia with uncharacteristic gentleness: "Your hand, and yours: Ere in our own house I do shade my head, The good patricians must be visited." But even in this moment of private reunion, the political demands are pressing — he must visit the patricians before he can rest.

## The Herald's Role

The Herald in *Coriolanus* appears only in this scene and has only a handful of lines. Yet his function is structurally essential. In Shakespeare's Roman plays, the herald (from Latin *praeco*) is the official voice of the state — neither a political actor like the tribunes nor a military figure like Cominius. His proclamation has no personal content; it is pure ritual speech, the state speaking through its designated instrument.

The Herald's final appearance in the play is at the opposite extreme of his function: after Coriolanus's death, the First Lord declares he shall be "regarded As the most noble corse that ever herald Did follow to his urn." The Herald who proclaimed Coriolanus's entrance into civic life will also accompany his exit from it — a symmetry that frames the entire tragedy between two acts of public ceremony.

## Immediate Aftermath

The proclamation scene transitions directly to political conflict. As the procession moves toward the Capitol with a flourish of cornets, Brutus and Sicinius remain behind. Brutus observes: "All tongues speak of him, and the bleared sights Are spectacled to see him." Sicinius notes the political implications: "On the sudden, I warrant him consul." The tribunes' private conference, which follows immediately, sets in motion the plot to undo everything the Herald has proclaimed.

## Dramatic Function

The Herald's proclamation represents the high-water mark of Coriolanus's public acceptance. For a brief moment, the entire Roman polity — Senate, army, and people — speaks with one voice in celebration of martial valor. The scene's placement at the beginning of Act II, immediately before the political machinations that will destroy Coriolanus, makes it structurally significant as the last moment of unity before the play descends into faction and banishment. The Herald's formal language, with its Roman gravity and ritual precision, sounds a note that the subsequent scenes of manipulation and rage will systematically dismantle.
