# Famine

## Definition

**Famine** — the scarcity of grain and consequent hunger of the plebeian population — is the material condition that opens *Coriolanus* and drives its initial action. The play begins not with rhetoric or political theory but with armed citizens demanding "corn at our own price" (001-002). The patrician hoarding of grain during a time of dearth is the immediate grievance that sets the class conflict in motion.

## Historical Background

Rome's grain supply was chronically vulnerable. The city's population outgrew local agricultural capacity, making it dependent on imported grain. Patrician landowners who controlled agricultural production could profit from scarcity by withholding supply — a practice the plebeians experienced as deliberate starvation. The *cura annonae* (care of the grain supply) became a defining obligation of Roman magistrates in the late Republic.

## In the Play

The famine grievance is never resolved — only displaced. The Volscian war provides an external enemy that temporarily unites the classes, and Coriolanus's military heroism provides a narrative that temporarily overshadows the grain question. But the play's opening demand — "we'll have corn at our own price" — hangs over the political action like an unanswered question.

## Relationship to Other Concepts

- **Class Conflict**: Famine is the material base of the class conflict — the visceral deprivation that transforms social tension into armed revolt.
- **Plebeian Rights**: The right to affordable grain is an implicit plebeian right, though never constitutionalized like the tribunate.
- **Tribunate**: The tribunes rise to power partly by representing plebeian economic grievances against patrician grain hoarding.
- **Patrician Power**: Control of grain supply is an instrument of patrician power — economic dominance reinforcing political dominance.

## Critical Perspectives

Materialist critics have emphasized the opening scene's grain riot as evidence that *Coriolanus* takes economic grievance seriously. The play may ultimately be about honour, pride, and constitutional crisis, but it begins with hunger — a reminder that political theory is grounded in bodily need.
