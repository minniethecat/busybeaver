# Roman Honour

## Definition

**Roman Honour** in *Coriolanus* is not a single stable value but a **contested field** on which the play's central conflicts are fought. For [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]], honour is absolute, intrinsic, and rooted in deeds — it cannot be given or taken away by popular acclaim. For the patrician order, honour is a negotiated status requiring public recognition. For the plebeians, honour-talk is a mask for aristocratic arrogance. And for [[tullus-aufidius|Aufidius]], honour is a zero-sum competition in which another's glory is one's own diminishment.

> I had rather have my wounds to heal again
> Than hear say how I got them. (002-003)

Coriolanus's statement of honour encapsulates his tragedy: he values the deed so absolutely that he cannot bear its narration. But in a political community, honour *is* its narration — the telling, the recognition, the public acknowledgment. Coriolanus's refusal to narrate his own worth makes him, in the play's political economy, worthless.

## Historical Background

The Roman ethical vocabulary distinguished several related concepts:

| Latin Term | English Equivalent | Core Meaning | Dramatic Role in the Play |
|-----------|-------------------|-------------|--------------------------|
| ***Honos*** | Honour | Public office + the esteem it confers | The consulship is *honos* in its institutional form |
| ***Virtus*** | Valour / Manliness | Martial courage; from *vir* (man) | Coriolanus's defining quality; the thing he cannot perform for crowds |
| ***Gloria*** | Glory | Fame won through great deeds | What Cominius confers in his encomium; what Coriolanus both craves and despises |
| ***Dignitas*** | Dignity / Standing | Political weight; influence proportional to merit | What Coriolanus forfeits by refusing the electoral ritual |
| ***Fama*** | Reputation | What is said of one; from *fari* (to speak) | The plebeian "voices" that Coriolanus scorns |

The play's ethical drama arises from the instability of these terms in practice. Coriolanus insists that *virtus* is self-sufficient — that deeds speak for themselves. But the political community of Rome requires that *virtus* be translated into *fama* — that deeds be spoken of, and that the speaking be ratified by popular "voices." The contradiction destroys him.

## In the Play

### Honour as Self-Sufficiency (Act 1)

Coriolanus's honour code is established in his first scenes. He fights alone within the walls of Corioli — a literally solitary act of *virtus* that earns him his *cognomen* and the accolades of the army. [[cominius|Cominius]] attempts to narrate his deeds in public, but Coriolanus interrupts:

> Pray now, no more: my mother,
> Who has a charter to extol her blood,
> When she does praise me grieves me. (001-009)

Even his mother's praise is unwelcome. The pattern is set: Coriolanus experiences honour as something degraded by utterance. The deed is sufficient; the telling is a kind of theft.

### Honour Versus Recognition (Act 2)

The consular election forces the contradiction into the open. Coriolanus must solicit "voices" — he must exchange his *virtus* for popular *fama*. The ritual of the gown of humility (002-003) is the institutional mechanism for this translation. Coriolanus's refusal to perform it is a refusal to accept that honour requires recognition:

> To brag unto them, thus I did, and thus;
> Show them the unaching scars which I should hide,
> As if I had received them for the hire
> Of their breath only! (002-003)

The irony is that Coriolanus *does* care about recognition — he accepts the *cognomen* "Coriolanus," he glories in Cominius's praise, he expects the consulship as his due. But he wants recognition on his own terms: from his equals (the patricians, his fellow soldiers), not from his inferiors (the citizens). Honour, for Coriolanus, is an aristocratic peer-relationship; the democratic electoral ritual corrupts it by making it a mass transaction.

### Honour's Reversal: Defection (Act 4)

When Rome banishes him, Coriolanus performs a radical revaluation:

> I banish you!...
> Thus I turn my back:
> There is a world elsewhere. (003-003)

His honour, no longer tethered to Rome, finds a new object: revenge. He defects to the Volscians and offers his military genius to [[tullus-aufidius|Aufidius]], his lifelong enemy. This is honour logic taken to its extreme: if Rome does not recognise his worth, Rome is worthless. The self that was forged in Roman service now exists only to destroy Rome.

### Honour's Collapse (Act 5)

In the final act, honour's contradictions collapse inward. Volumnia's plea forces Coriolanus to choose between two honour-claims: his oath to the Volscians (martial honour) versus his duty to his mother and Rome (filial and civic honour). He chooses filial duty — and Aufidius, exploiting the moment, denounces him as a traitor:

> He whined and roar'd away your victory...
> At a few drops of women's rheum, which are
> As cheap as lies, he sold the blood and labour
> Of our great action... (005-006)

Aufidius's accusation is, in honour terms, correct: Coriolanus broke faith with his Volscian allies. But the accusation is also self-serving: Aufidius has been looking for a pretext to destroy his rival. Honour-talk, in the play's final movement, is revealed as a weapon — a rhetorical resource that can be deployed by anyone against anyone.

## The Ethics of Honour: A Critical View

| Position | Advocate in the Play | Core Claim | Dramatic Outcome |
|----------|---------------------|-----------|-----------------|
| **Honour Absolutism** | Coriolanus | Honour is intrinsic to deeds; recognition is irrelevant | Political destruction + death |
| **Honour as Recognition** | Cominius, Menenius | Honour requires public acknowledgment | Complicit in Coriolanus's destruction |
| **Honour as Competition** | Aufidius | Honour is zero-sum; another's glory is my loss | Temporary victory; ultimately hollow |
| **Honour as Civic Duty** | Volumnia | Honour is service to Rome above all | Saves Rome; kills her son |

No position survives the play intact. Coriolanus's absolutism is nobly consistent but politically suicidal. The patricians' recognition-model collapses when the people withdraw their recognition. Aufidius's competitive model reduces honour to mere envy. And Volumnia's civic-duty model produces a mother who sends her son to his death. The play exposes honour not as a coherent ethical system but as a set of incompatible demands that no character can simultaneously satisfy.

## Relationship to Other Concepts

- [[wounds|Wounds]]: Wounds are the physical inscription of honour on the body; the wound-display ritual is where bodily *virtus* meets popular *fama*
- [[voices|Voices]]: "Voices" are the democratic mechanism by which honour is conferred or withheld; Coriolanus's contempt for voices is a contempt for the recognition-dependence of honour
- [[motherhood|Motherhood]]: Volumnia's maternal philosophy is honour-production — she raises her son to be a vessel of *virtus* and measures her own worth by his wounds
- [[class-conflict|Class Conflict]]: The play's class struggle is fought on the terrain of honour — who has the right to confer it, who can withhold it, and whether it is an aristocratic monopoly or a popular currency
