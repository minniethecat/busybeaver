# Exile

## 概念定义

**Exile** (*exilium*) is the punishment that fundamentally restructures the world of *Coriolanus*. When the tribunes Sicinius and Brutus engineer Coriolanus's banishment from Rome, they do more than remove a political opponent — they sever the bond between Rome's greatest warrior and the city he defines himself through defending. The exiled Coriolanus becomes a contradiction: a Roman without Rome, a general without an army, a patrician without a patria.

Exile in *Coriolanus* is not merely a plot event but a conceptual transformation. It converts Coriolanus from defender to destroyer, from patriot to traitor, from hero to enemy. The punishment that was intended to end the class conflict instead escalates it into existential crisis — both for Coriolanus, who must construct a new identity outside Rome, and for Rome, which must face its greatest soldier at the head of an enemy army.

## 历史背景

Roman exile had several distinct legal forms. *Exilium* proper was voluntary departure before condemnation — the accused citizen chose to leave Roman territory rather than face trial and possible execution. This was how Coriolanus's banishment is presented: the tribunes pronounce sentence and Coriolanus departs, rather than being physically expelled.

*Aquae et ignis interdictio* ("interdiction of fire and water") was a formal sentence barring the condemned from Roman territory and forbidding anyone from offering shelter or sustenance. This had the effect of stripping the exile of citizenship rights and property. The historical Coriolanus, according to Plutarch, went into exile among the Volsci and led them against Rome — the same trajectory Shakespeare dramatizes.

Exile occupied a paradoxical place in Roman culture: it was the most severe penalty short of death, yet it preserved the physical life of the condemned. For a man like Coriolanus, whose entire identity was bound up in service to Rome, exile was arguably worse than death — it was a living death, an existence stripped of meaning.

## 剧中的体现

### The Sentence (Act 3, Scene 3)

The banishment of Coriolanus is the climax of the tribunes' political campaign. Brutus frames the charge: "In this point charge him home, that he affects / Tyrannical power" (016-002). The tribunes marshal their orchestrated voices, and Sicinius pronounces the sentence: "It is a mind / That shall remain a poison where it is; / I say, banish him" (016-002).

Coriolanus's response is breathtaking: "I banish you!" (016-002). He inverts the sentence, claiming the power to exile the city that exiles him. This rhetorical reversal — "There is a world elsewhere" (016-002) — is both heroic defiance and profound self-deception. There is no world elsewhere for Coriolanus; his identity exists only in relation to Rome.

### The Departure (Act 4, Scene 1)

The farewell scene between Coriolanus and his family reveals the human cost of exile. Volumnia, the mother who shaped him for martial glory, now weeps at the destruction of her life's project. Coriolanus's stoic counsel — "Come, leave your tears" — is undermined by his own admission that Rome has become "the beast with many heads" that "butts me away" (017-002).

His parting words to his wife Virgilia — "My former fortune and my present state, / Could I but once more see them face to face" — gesture toward an impossible reconciliation. The exile must leave everything that defines him, and the audience knows, even if Coriolanus does not, that he will never return except as an enemy at the gates.

### The Volscian Defection (Acts 4–5)

Exile transforms Coriolanus into what he most despises: a traitor. He presents himself to Tullus Aufidius in Antium, offering his services against his own city. The Volscian soldiers adopt him with fervour: "Your soldiers use him as the grace 'fore meat, / Their talk at table, and their thanks at end" (023-002). Coriolanus has exchanged one martial identity for another, but the exchange is unstable — he remains a Roman leading Rome's enemies, a contradiction that cannot indefinitely sustain itself.

The final act of the tragedy demonstrates that exile cannot be undone by military force. When Coriolanus stands before the walls of Rome, his mother Volumnia persuades him to withdraw — not by appealing to his Volscian loyalty, which is a fiction, but by exposing the impossibility of his position. The exile who conquers Rome would destroy the only identity he has left.

## 与其他概念的关系

- **Class Conflict**: Exile is the ultimate weapon of the class war. The tribunes banish Coriolanus because they cannot coexist with him politically, and the patricians are too weak to prevent it. The banishment represents the plebeian victory in the class struggle — a victory that proves pyrrhic when the exiled general returns at the head of an invading army.

- **Military Honor**: Exile negates military honour. Coriolanus's deeds at Corioli were performed for Rome; the same deeds performed against Rome are not honour but treason. The exile's martial prowess becomes a threat rather than a shield, and the honour code that defined Coriolanus collapses into mere violence.

- **Loyalty**: Exile severs the bond of loyalty that ties citizen to state. Coriolanus's defection to the Volscians is the logical consequence of his banishment — if Rome has repudiated him, his obligations to Rome are dissolved. The Volscians understand this and accept him on the basis of his martial reputation, but the audience is reminded that loyalty built on resentment of one's own city is inherently unstable.

- **Pride**: Coriolanus's pride makes exile survivable — "There is a world elsewhere" — but also ensures that he cannot accept exile's finality. A humbler man might accept banishment and fade into obscurity; Coriolanus's pride demands that he return as a conqueror, a demand that can only end in death.

- **Tribunate**: The tribunes who banish Coriolanus wield exile as a political instrument. Their power to pronounce banishment is the ultimate expression of tribunician authority — and, paradoxically, the act that most endangers the plebeian order they represent.

## 文学批评视角

Exile in *Coriolanus* has been read through several critical lenses. The psychoanalytic tradition — notably Janet Adelman (1978) — interpreted Coriolanus's banishment as a traumatic separation from the maternal body, with Volumnia's later intervention before the walls of Rome representing a return of the repressed. In this reading, exile is the condition of being severed from the mother-city, and Coriolanus's identity crisis is fundamentally pre-oedipal.

Political critics have seen exile as Shakespeare's meditation on the fragility of citizenship in the early modern state. The ease with which a citizen can become an enemy — and the ease with which the people can revoke what the people have granted — reflects anxieties about political belonging in Jacobean England, where the monarch's favour could transform a subject into an exile overnight (as with Sir Walter Raleigh's banishment).

James Kuzner (2007) read *Coriolanus* as a tragedy of republican exclusion: the Roman polity can only constitute itself by expelling what it cannot assimilate. Exile, in this reading, is not a punishment for Coriolanus's pride but a structural necessity of the republican order, which depends on the existence of an outside — an enemy, a traitor, an exile — against which civic identity can define itself.
