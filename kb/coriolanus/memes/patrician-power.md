# Patrician Power

## Definition

**Patrician power** (*auctoritas patrum*) is the institutional, social, and ideological dominance of Rome's hereditary aristocracy. In *Coriolanus*, patrician power is not a static background condition but an active force — defended, asserted, and ultimately destabilized by the hero's refusal to accommodate the plebeian constitutional order.

Shakespeare presents patrician power through multiple lenses. Through Coriolanus, we see its most uncompromising form: martial *virtus* as the sole legitimate basis of authority, and popular consent as an impertinence. Through Menenius, we see its diplomatic face: paternalist accommodation that preserves aristocratic privilege by conceding symbolic recognition. Through the patricians who flank Coriolanus in Act 3, we see its fragility — an aristocracy that cannot control its own champion, watching helplessly as he provokes the plebeian assembly into banishing him.

## Historical Background

Roman patricians traced their lineage to the *patres* — the hundred senators appointed by Romulus at the founding of the city. In the early Republic (5th–3rd centuries BCE), patricians monopolized:

- **The Senate**: The deliberative body that controlled foreign policy, finance, and — through *auctoritas patrum* — the ratification of popular legislation.
- **Magistracies**: Consuls, praetors, censors, and aediles were initially patrician-exclusive offices.
- **Priesthoods**: The *pontifices*, *augures*, and *flamines* controlled religious law and ritual, giving patricians a sacred dimension of authority.
- **Legal knowledge**: Before the Twelve Tables, legal procedure was a patrician mystery, giving aristocrats effective control over justice.

The Conflict of the Orders progressively eroded patrician monopolies, but patrician social prestige — the weight of ancestry, the assumption of natural superiority, the identification of patrician interests with the common good — proved more durable than formal political control.

## In the Play

### Coriolanus's Aristocratic Ideology

Coriolanus is the most articulate exponent of patrician ideology in the play. His political philosophy can be summarized in a single principle: those who fight for Rome should govern Rome; those who do not fight should have no political standing. This principle is genuinely aristocratic — it ties political authority to demonstrated martial virtue — but it is also anti-republican, denying the constitutional settlement that gives plebeians political representation.

His contempt for the plebeians is not personal prejudice but structural analysis: "the mutable, rank-scented many" (015-002) are incapable of political judgment because they lack the *virtus* that alone confers political legitimacy. The patrician class, in Coriolanus's view, is not merely *entitled* to rule — it is *the only possible* ruling class, because only patricians possess the martial and moral qualities governance requires.

### Menenius's Paternalist Diplomacy

Menenius represents patrician power in its diplomatic mode. His fable of the belly (001-002) is a sophisticated defence of aristocratic privilege dressed as organic metaphor. The Senate is the belly — it receives the food first, but only to distribute it to all members. The citizens' arms, legs, and mouths are subordinate but essential; they receive their nourishment from the center.

The fable is a political argument disguised as a nature analogy. It naturalizes hierarchy — the belly *must* be central, just as the Senate *must* be sovereign — and delegitimizes resistance as self-destructive. The citizens, to their credit, see through the fable: "It was an answer" (001-002). But the fable works not as persuasion but as containment, buying time for military force to arrive.

### The Senate in Crisis (Acts 3–4)

The patrician class fractures under pressure. When the tribunes orchestrate Coriolanus's banishment, the patricians are divided — some counsel accommodation, others defiance, none can control their champion. The aristocracy's dependence on Coriolanus's military prestige reveals a structural weakness: patrician power requires plebeian consent to function, and Coriolanus makes plebeian consent impossible.

The senate scene in Act 3 shows patrician power at its most vulnerable — not from external assault but from internal incoherence. The tribunes can banish Coriolanus because the patricians cannot agree on how to defend him. The aristocracy that Coriolanus embodies proves incapable of the collective action his defence requires.

## Relationship to Other Concepts

- **Class Conflict**: Patrician power is the structure that class conflict challenges. Every institutional gain by the plebeians — the tribunate, written law, eligibility for office — is a limitation of patrician prerogative.

- **Tribunate**: The tribunate was created precisely to check patrician power. The tribunes' veto, their sacrosanctity, and their right to convene the plebeian assembly are all institutional counterweights to patrician dominance.

- **Plebeian Rights**: The mirror image of patrician power. Patrician rights are ancestral, customary, and assumed; plebeian rights are asserted, contested, and only as durable as the political coalition that defends them.

- **Military Honor**: The patrician claim to rule rests on martial *virtus*. Coriolanus's wounds are both proof of individual excellence and entitlement of his class — the class that fights for Rome deserves to govern Rome.

- **Roman Constitution**: The mixed constitution is the arena in which patrician power and plebeian rights negotiate their coexistence. The tragedy of *Coriolanus* is in part the failure of this negotiation.

The patrician identity is defined in opposition to the plebeian masses — Coriolanus embodies this aristocratic contempt.

## Critical Perspectives

Scholars have debated whether *Coriolanus* critiques or endorses patrician ideology. The conservative tradition — from Samuel Johnson through T. S. Eliot — read the play as celebrating Coriolanus's aristocratic authenticity against the tribunes' demagogic populism. On this reading, patrician power is natural, legitimate, and tragically undermined by constitutional concessions to the unworthy.

More recent critics have detected a more subversive Shakespeare. Anne Barton (1994) noted that Shakespeare stripped the tribunate of its historical legitimacy, but also refused to make Coriolanus sympathetic — the play denies the audience any comfortable political alignment. Paul Cantor (1976) argued that *Coriolanus* dramatizes the transition from a heroic-aristocratic value system to a republican-democratic one, and that the play registers the loss of aristocratic authenticity without endorsing aristocratic politics.

The materialist tradition — from Brecht onward — reads patrician power as ideology in the Marxist sense: a system of beliefs that naturalizes class domination and delegitimizes resistance. Coriolanus's tragedy, in this reading, is not that patrician *virtus* is incompatible with republican politics but that patrician ideology prevents him from recognizing plebeian interests as legitimate — a failure of political imagination that destroys both the hero and the polity he claims to defend.
