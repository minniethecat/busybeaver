# The Senate House

## Location Overview

The Senate House is the institutional seat of patrician authority, where the Senate endorses Coriolanus for the consulship and later attempts to defend him against tribunician assault. Its closed doors mark the boundary between aristocratic deliberation and popular politics. When Menenius tells Coriolanus that "The senate, Coriolanus, are well pleased To make thee consul" (012-002), the Senate House becomes the site where elite endorsement is bestowed — but Coriolanus must still face the people in the market-place.

## Dramatic Function

The Senate House and the market-place form the play's spatial dyad: patrician power versus popular sovereignty. Coriolanus's question — "Where? at the senate-house?" (013-002) — and his promise to "Repair to the senate-house" (013-002) mark his passage between these two spheres. In Act 4, as the Volscian threat grows, the nobles are reported "in great earnestness... going All to the senate-house" (023-002), and a messenger summons the tribunes "to the senate" to face the news of Coriolanus's invasion (023-002). The Senate House thus remains the gravitational center of Roman political authority throughout the play.

## Scenes Set Here

- **act-2-scene-2**: The Senate convenes to confirm Coriolanus's consulship (012-002).
- **act-3-scene-1**: Senators debate the tribunes' challenge to Coriolanus's authority (014-002).
