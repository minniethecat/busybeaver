## Definition

In *Coriolanus*, "nature" denotes an innate, unchangeable disposition — the fixed character that defines each person and, in the hero's case, drives the tragic arc. The word appears at crucial moments to justify, explain, or condemn behaviour, functioning as the play's key psychological concept. Coriolanus's nature is repeatedly described as "too noble for the world" (004-725) and fundamentally incompatible with political accommodation.

## Historical Background

Shakespeare inherited the Renaissance debate about the relationship between nature and nurture, *ingegno* versus *arte*. The Roman setting intensifies this theme: the patrician ideology held that virtue (*virtus*) was innate to certain bloodlines. Coriolanus embodies this belief to its tragic extreme — he cannot "dissemble with my nature" (004-793) even when his survival depends on it.

## In the Play

The concept of nature is introduced early, when the Second Citizen cautions: "What he cannot help in his nature, you account a vice in him" (001-044). This pre-emptively frames all of Coriolanus's later excesses as expressions of an immutable essence rather than choices.

Sicinius identifies the political danger of such a nature: "Such a nature, / Tickled with good success, disdains the shadow / Which he treads on at noon" (003-117). The tribune recognises that Coriolanus's character makes him incapable of subordination — a fatal flaw in a republic.

When the tribunes scheme to provoke Coriolanus's rage, Brutus explicitly relies on his nature: "If, as his nature is, he fall in rage / With their refusal, both observe and answer / The vantage of his anger" (003-592). Nature here becomes a political weapon — predictable enough to be exploited.

Menenius's famous defence — "His nature is too noble for the world: / He would not flatter Neptune for his trident, / Or Jove for's power to thunder. His heart's his mouth" (004-725) — is a double-edged compliment. It praises authenticity while admitting its destructive consequences.

Coriolanus himself embraces this framing: "Would you have me / False to my nature? Rather say I play / The man I am" (004-772). He presents inflexibility as integrity, refusal to compromise as self-consistency.

The most complex treatment comes from Aufidius's soliloquy, which attempts a clinical diagnosis: "whether nature, / Not to be other than one thing, not moving / From the casque to the cushion, but commanding peace / Even with the same austerity and garb / As he controlled the war" (010-1174). Aufidius identifies nature as the root cause — a singleness of being that cannot transition from helmet to senate cushion.

In the play's climactic scene, Coriolanus's nature finally yields — but only to an even greater natural force: "Great nature cries 'Deny not'" (012-1255). Family bonds, the most primal aspect of nature, overcome even his obstinate resolve.

## Relationships

Nature is the conceptual foundation for [[pride]], [[anger]], and [[military-honor]] — each of these traits is understood as an expression of Coriolanus's innate character. It stands in opposition to [[rhetoric]], the art of dissembling that his nature rejects. The concept is also linked to [[virtus]]: Roman virtue is innate, not learned.

## Critical Perspectives

The nature debate in *Coriolanus* has been read through multiple lenses: psychoanalytic critics see the fixed nature as a form of narcissistic rigidity; historicist critics connect it to early modern debates about monarchy versus republicanism (a king can afford to be "natural"; a consul cannot); and gender critics note that Volumnia, who shaped this nature, is the only force that can overcome it.
