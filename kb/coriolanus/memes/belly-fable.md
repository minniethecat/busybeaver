# Belly Fable

## Definition

The **Belly Fable** — also known as the Fable of the Belly and the Members — is a political allegory delivered by [[menenius-agrippa|Menenius Agrippa]] in the opening scene of Shakespeare's *Coriolanus*. It compares the Roman state to a human body, with the **senate as the belly** and the **plebeians as the mutinous members**. The fable argues that the patrician class, though it appears to consume without producing, is in fact the storehouse that nourishes the whole body politic.

> There was a time when all the body's members
> Rebell'd against the belly, thus accused it:
> That only like a gulf it did remain
> I' the midst o' the body, idle and unactive,
> Still cupboarding the viand, never bearing
> Like labour with the rest... (001-002)

The allegory is drawn from classical sources — both Livy and Plutarch recount it — but Shakespeare dramatises it as a living argument rather than a set piece, embedding it in a tense street confrontation that immediately establishes the play's central political theme: the interdependence of rich and poor in a functioning state, and the fragility of that mutual obligation.

## Historical Background

In Livy's *Ab Urbe Condita* (2.32), Menenius Agrippa uses the belly fable to persuade the plebeians to return from the Sacred Mount during the First Secession of 494 BCE. In Livy's account, the fable succeeds: the plebeians return to Rome, and the tribunate is established as part of the compromise.

Plutarch's *Life of Coriolanus* (section 6) gives a similar account, and this is Shakespeare's immediate source. But Shakespeare makes a crucial dramatic alteration: in the play, the fable does **not** resolve the conflict. Marcius enters immediately after, and his contempt for the citizens — his first words call them "dissentious rogues" and "scabs" (001-001) — re-ignites the hostility Menenius has only just calmed.

| Source | Outcome of the Fable |
|--------|---------------------|
| Livy (2.32) | Plebeians persuaded; Secession ends; Tribunate created |
| Plutarch (*Coriolanus* 6) | Plebeians persuaded; compromise follows |
| Shakespeare (Act 1, Scene 1) | Fable calms temporarily; Marcius's entry re-ignites conflict |

This structural choice is characteristic of Shakespeare's political method throughout the play: every gesture toward resolution is immediately undermined, and the patrician-plebeian conflict is presented as a cycle of provocation and reaction rather than a problem with a constitutional solution.

## In the Play

The belly fable occupies the opening movement of Act 1, Scene 1 (001-002). The scene begins with armed citizens in the street, resolving to kill Caius Marcius as "a very dog to the commonality" (001-001). Menenius enters and, through a combination of affable rhetoric and the fable's persuasive logic, attempts to defuse the rebellion.

### The Fable's Structure

Menenius does not deliver the fable as a monologue. Instead, Shakespeare breaks it into a dialogue between Menenius and the First Citizen, creating dramatic irony and allowing the plebeian perspective to push back:

1. **The Accusation**: Menenius presents the members' complaint — the belly sits idle while the limbs labour (001-002)
2. **The Citizen's Interruption**: The First Citizen demands to know "what answer made the belly?" — a moment of dramatic participation that gives the plebeian voice standing within the fable itself
3. **The Belly's Reply**: Menenius, speaking as the belly, explains that it receives food first only to distribute it "through the rivers of your blood, / Even to the court, the heart, to the seat o' the brain" (001-002)
4. **The Application**: Menenius makes the analogy explicit: "The senators of Rome are this good belly, / And you the mutinous members" (001-002)

> Note me this, good friend;
> Your most grave belly was deliberate,
> Not rash like his accusers, and thus answer'd:
> "True is it, my incorporate friends," quoth he,
> "That I receive the general food at first,
> Which you do live upon..." (001-002)

### Dramatic Function

The fable serves three dramatic purposes at once:

- **Exposition**: It establishes the nature of the class conflict — not a simple rich-vs-poor struggle but a dispute about the *terms of interdependence*
- **Character revelation**: Menenius is established as the witty, conciliatory patrician who can speak to both sides; his rhetorical skill is his political currency
- **Thematic overture**: The body politic metaphor recurs throughout the play — Coriolanus calls the people "the beast with many heads" (004-005), and the metaphor of the state as body becomes the play's dominant political image

The fable's irony deepens as the play progresses. Menenius argues that the belly/body relationship is natural and beneficial; but Coriolanus's later actions — his refusal to show his wounds, his contempt for the "voices" of the people, his defection to the Volscians — demonstrate precisely the *failure* of that organic interdependence that the fable celebrates.

## Relationship to Other Concepts

- [[class-conflict|Class Conflict]]: The belly fable is the play's foundational statement of class relations, framing patrician-plebeian tension as a problem of mutual recognition rather than material grievance alone
- [[patrician-power|Patrician Power]]: The fable is patrician ideology in narrative form — it justifies patrician dominance as natural and necessary, revealing how the ruling class legitimates its position
- [[plebeian-rights|Plebeian Rights]]: The fable's dramatic context (a mutiny over grain prices) highlights the material basis of plebeian grievance that the allegory itself elides — the citizens are hungry, not merely "mutinous"
- [[tribunate|Tribunate]]: In the historical account, the fable leads to the creation of the tribunate; in Shakespeare's version, the tribunate already exists and the fable's conciliatory force is short-lived
