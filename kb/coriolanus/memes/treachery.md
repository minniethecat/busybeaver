## Definition

"Treachery" and its cognates — "traitor," "treason," "traitorous" — form the play's vocabulary of ultimate political condemnation. To be declared a traitor in *Coriolanus* is to be placed beyond the protection of law: the tribunes decree that Coriolanus "dies to-night" (004-744) precisely because they have redefined his pride as treason. The accusation recurs at the play's end, when Aufidius uses the same charge to destroy him.

## Historical Background

In Roman law, *perduellio* (high treason) was a capital offence that could be prosecuted before the popular assembly. The tribunes' power to bring capital charges against a patrician was a cornerstone of the republican constitution Shakespeare inherited from Plutarch. The English context added further resonance: the Gunpowder Plot (1605) and subsequent treason trials had made "traitor" the most charged word in the Jacobean political lexicon.

## In the Play

The word "traitor" first appears at the turning point of the domestic plot. After Coriolanus denounces the tribunate, Sicinius declares: "Has spoken like a traitor, and shall answer / As traitors do" (004-665). Brutus seconds: "Manifest treason!" (004-667). Sicinius then formalises the charge: "in whose name myself / Attach thee as a traitorous innovator, / A foe to the public weal" (004-672). The sequence is a masterclass in political escalation — an angry speech is reframed as a capital crime.

The charge accelerates. Sicinius tells the patricians: "We are peremptory to dispatch / This viperous traitor: to eject him hence / Were but one danger, and to keep him here / Our certain death" (004-744). The logic is purely political: Coriolanus must die not for what he has done but for what he represents.

When the formal charges are read — "you have contrived to take / From Rome all seasoned office and to wind / Yourself into a power tyrannical; / For which you are a traitor to the people" (005-857) — Coriolanus's response is incredulity: "How! traitor!" (005-858). Then fury: "The fires i' the lowest hell fold-in the people! / Call me their traitor! Thou injurious tribune!" (005-860). The accusation of treachery, coming from those he considers base, is the ultimate insult — worse, in his value system, than the sentence of death that follows.

The play's final scene returns to the same charge, now spoken by Aufidius: "tell the traitor, in the high'st degree / He hath abused your powers" (013-1351). Coriolanus again echoes: "Traitor! how now!" (013-1352). Aufidius's triple iteration — "Ay, traitor, Marcius!" (013-1353) — mirrors the tribunes' earlier rhetorical strategy. The charge is the same; only the accuser has changed.

Aufidius's elaboration is devastatingly specific: "perfidiously / He has betrayed your business, and given up, / For certain drops of salt, your city Rome, / I say 'your city,' to his wife and mother; / Breaking his oath and resolution like / A twist of rotten silk" (013-1355). Coriolanus's mercy — his yielding to his mother's plea — is reframed as treason against the Volscians. The structural irony is complete: the same inflexibility that made him a traitor in Rome makes him a traitor in Antium.

## Relationships

Treachery is the political weaponisation of [[anger]] and [[hatred]]. It is the accusation that bridges Coriolanus's conflict with the [[tribunate]] and his final confrontation with [[Aufidius]]. The concept is closely tied to [[loyalty]] (treachery is loyalty's violation) and [[exile]] (banishment is the legal consequence of treason). It also connects to [[rhetoric]]: the charge of treachery is an act of political speech, not a description of objective fact.

## Critical Perspectives

The repeated use of "traitor" has been analysed as a study in political speech acts — how calling someone a traitor can *make* them one, regardless of their actions. Stanley Cavell's influential reading sees Coriolanus's response to the charge as a crisis of language itself: he cannot accept that words (mere "breath") can reconstitute his identity. Other critics have connected the treason motif to Jacobean debates about the limits of sovereign power and the right of resistance.
