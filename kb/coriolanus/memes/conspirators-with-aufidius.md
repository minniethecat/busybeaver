# Conspirators with Aufidius

The Conspirators with Aufidius are a faction of Volscian nobles and soldiers loyal to [[tullus-aufidius]], who conspire to assassinate [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] in the final scene of the play. Listed among the "Dramatis Personae" as "Conspirators with Aufidius" (P01-011), they appear as a collective force — "three or four Conspirators of Aufidius' faction" (029-002) — whose coordinated support transforms Aufidius's private resentment into public execution.

## Organizational Structure

The Conspirators operate as a compact military faction rather than a formal institution. Unlike the [[roman-senate]] or the [[tribunate]], they have no constitutional mandate or public standing. Their authority derives entirely from personal loyalty to [[tullus-aufidius|Aufidius]] and shared resentment against Coriolanus. Shakespeare presents them as a trio of named individuals — the [[first-conspirator]], [[second-conspirator]], and [[third-conspirator]] — each with a distinct rhetorical role in the conspiracy.

The structure of the conspiracy mirrors the Roman political machinations seen earlier in the play: a wronged leader (Aufidius, like [[sicinius-velutus]] and [[junius-brutus|Brutus]] among the [[tribunate]]) mobilizes allies to eliminate a common enemy. But where the Roman tribunes used legal process and public manipulation, the Volscian conspirators use direct violence — a contrast that reflects the play's distinction between Roman political culture and Volscian militarism.

Each conspirator's speech reveals a division of persuasive labor. The First Conspirator appeals to wounded pride and lost glory; the Second Conspirator emphasizes the personal insult to Aufidius; the Third Conspirator provides tactical counsel, urging immediate action before Coriolanus can rally support [029-002].

## Political Motivations

The Conspirators' grievances against Coriolanus are both personal and political. On the personal level, they resent the favor shown to a Roman defector over loyal Volscians. The First Conspirator's question — "Was he not / The worthiest of our blood?" — expresses the resentment of those who fought faithfully only to see a foreigner elevated above them [029-002].

Politically, the conspiracy is fueled by Coriolanus's peace treaty with Rome. Coriolanus, having marched to the very gates of Rome with a Volscian army, abruptly negotiates peace after his mother's intercession. From the Volscian perspective, this is a betrayal: a campaign that promised conquest and plunder ends in a negotiated settlement that leaves Rome intact. The conspirators articulate the unspoken anger of the Volscian military establishment at this lost opportunity.

The peace treaty creates a paradoxical situation: Coriolanus has both won the war (by bringing Rome to its knees) and lost it (by sparing the city). The conspirators exploit this ambiguity, framing Coriolanus as a traitor to both nations — "He has betray'd your business, and given up, / For certain drops of salt, your city Rome ... to his wife and mother" [029-002]. The phrase "drops of salt" — tears — reduces the emotional scene of Volumnia's supplication to a contemptible weakness, recasting Coriolanus's filial piety as military incompetence.

## In the Play

The Conspirators enter in Act V, Scene 6, immediately after Aufidius has dispatched his attendants. They greet him with deference: "Most welcome!" (029-002). Each conspirator speaks in turn, building the case for immediate action:

The First Conspirator establishes the grievance: Coriolanus has stolen the glory of the Volscian campaign — "When he had carried Rome and that we look'd / For no less spoil than glory" (029-002). The Second Conspirator notes the insult of Coriolanus's triumphal return to Antium while Aufidius "enter'd like a post, / And had no welcomes home" (029-002). The Third Conspirator, the most strategically minded, urges Aufidius to strike before Coriolanus can address the people: "at your vantage, / Ere he express himself, or move the people / With what he would say, let him feel your sword, / Which we will second" (029-002).

When Coriolanus enters and the confrontation escalates, the Conspirators ultimately seal his fate. Coriolanus boasts of his single-handed conquest of Corioli — "Alone I did it. Boy!" — and the Conspirators respond with a collective death sentence: "Let him die for't" (029-002). Moments later they strike: "Kill, kill, kill, kill, kill him! The Conspirators draw, and kill Coriolanus: Aufidius stands on his body" (029-002).

The Conspirators' final action is a staged execution dressed as spontaneous fury. The fivefold "kill" suggests not uncontrolled rage but the ceremonial rhythm of a political assassination — each cry a blow, each blow a ratification of the collective will. After the killing, the Conspirators vanish from the stage, their work complete; the remaining dialogue belongs to Aufidius and the Volscian lords who must manage the political aftermath.

## Theatrical and Dramatic Significance

The Conspirators' scene represents one of Shakespeare's most economical constructions of political violence. In fewer than a hundred lines, a conspiracy is proposed, justified, and executed. The rapid escalation — from whispered grievance to public assassination — reflects the play's broader concern with the speed at which political opinion can transform into violent action.

The tableau of the killing — Conspirators stabbing while Aufidius stands on Coriolanus's body — is a visual inversion of Coriolanus's victory at [[corioli]]. Where Coriolanus once fought alone and emerged covered in blood to the cheers of soldiers, he now dies at the hands of many, with his enemy literally standing over him. The conspirators thus function as a dramatic mirror: they do to Coriolanus what Coriolanus once did to the Volscians — destroy a singular threat through concentrated collective force.

Shakespeare's decision to use conspirators rather than having Aufidius kill Coriolanus alone also serves a thematic purpose. Throughout the play, Coriolanus has insisted on his radical independence — "I banish you" (016-002), "Alone I did it" (029-002). His death at the hands of a group, acting in concert, represents the final triumph of the collective over the individual that the play has traced from its opening scene.
