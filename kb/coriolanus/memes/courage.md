# Courage

Courage — rendered throughout the play as "valour" — stands as the preeminent martial virtue in *Coriolanus*. Cominius declares it "the chiefest virtue, and / Most dignifies the haver" (448-470), establishing a hierarchy of values in which martial bravery sits at the apex. Yet the play systematically tests whether courage alone can sustain a person through the demands of civic and political life.

## Valour as the Chiefest Virtue

Cominius's encomium of Coriolanus before the Senate is the play's most extended meditation on courage. Recounting Coriolanus's deeds from his first battle at sixteen — where "his Amazonian chin he drove / The bristled lips before him" — to the gates of Corioli, Cominius argues that valour is the virtue that "most dignifies the haver" (448-470). The speech establishes Coriolanus as courage incarnate, a man whose "rare example made the coward / Turn terror into sport" (448-470). This identification of martial courage with moral worth is the foundation of Coriolanus's claim to the consulship.

## The Limits of Martial Courage

Despite its primacy, courage proves insufficient for the challenges Coriolanus faces. His refusal to "check my courage for what they can give, / To have't with saying 'Good morrow'" (520-530) — that is, to moderate his warrior's directness for the sake of political decorum — leads directly to his banishment. Courage, which serves him absolutely on the battlefield, becomes a liability in the forum, where words, not swords, are the weapons.

## Courage in Exile

The theme of courage undergoes a transformation in exile. Coriolanus appeals to his mother's "ancient courage" as he bids farewell, invoking the stoic fortitude she taught him: "you were used / To say extremity was the trier of spirits" (582-587). Here courage shifts meaning from martial bravery to emotional resilience — the strength to endure Fortune's blows. Volumnia's "ancient courage" is a different species from the battlefield valour Coriolanus embodies, suggesting a more capacious understanding of the virtue than the play's martial culture typically allows.

## Aufidius and Poisoned Valour

For Aufidius, courage is a source of torment. His valour is "poison'd / With only suffering stain by him" (094-099) — diminished by Coriolanus's repeated victories over him. This metaphor of poisoned valour captures the dark psychology of martial competition: courage, in a zero-sum world of honour, is relative. One man's glory is another's shame. Aufidius's eventual conspiracy against Coriolanus is driven by this poisoned valour — a courage that, unable to triumph in fair combat, turns to treachery.

## Courage and Its Counterfeits

The play distinguishes genuine courage from its counterfeits. The plebeians' "mutinies and revolts, wherein they show'd / Most valour" (493-497) are dismissed by Coriolanus as self-serving — courage deployed for the wrong ends. And at the play's end, the Second Lord's lament that Aufidius "hast done a deed whereat valour will weep" (1373) judges the assassination as an act that even courage itself must mourn. The weeping of valour personified suggests a world where courage has been debased — reduced from open combat to covert murder.
