# Aufidius's Public Accusation

## Event Summary

In the final scene of the play, Tullus Aufidius enters the marketplace of Antium before the assembled Volscian lords and publicly accuses Coriolanus of treachery. Aufidius charges that Coriolanus "sold the state of Rome for a few drops of women's tears" (005-006) — a deliberate provocation designed to trigger the proud Roman's fatal outburst. Coriolanus's enraged response — boasting of his conquest of Corioli and calling the Volscians "old men, boys, and women" — seals his fate. The conspirators, positioned in the crowd, draw their swords and cut him down. The scene is both a political execution and a personal revenge: Aufidius's long-nursed jealousy finds its release in calculated public theater.

## Aufidius's Charge

Aufidius's accusation is rhetorically precise, exploiting the Volscian lords' existing resentment of Coriolanus's military dominance. He begins by stripping Coriolanus of his honorific name: "I am alone the villain of the world, / Coriolanus. Know, thou first, / It was I who raised thee from a Roman traitor / To lead our Volscian armies" (005-006). The accusation's central claim is that Coriolanus betrayed the Volscians by making peace with Rome — "he sold the blood / Of our great action for a few drops / Of a woman's tears" (005-006). The phrase "sold" recasts mercy as treason, and the reduction of Volumnia's eloquent appeal to "a few drops of a woman's tears" trivializes the dramatic power of the women's embassy scene that preceded it.

## Coriolanus's Fatal Rage

The accusation achieves its purpose instantly. Coriolanus erupts: "Measureless liar! ... Cut me to pieces, Volsces; men and lads, / Stain all your edges on me" (005-006). In his fury, he invokes his conquest of Corioli — "I fluttered your Volscians in Corioli: / Alone I did it" (005-006) — the very boast that confirms Aufidius's charge of arrogance. The Volscian lords, who moments earlier had greeted Coriolanus with honor, now cry "Tear him to pieces!" (005-006). Coriolanus's character, which has been his tragedy throughout, achieves its final expression: he cannot dissemble even to save his own life.

## The Assassination

Aufidius's conspirators, positioned among the crowd, rush forward with swords drawn. The stage direction is stark: "The Conspirators draw, and kill Coriolanus" (005-006). The killing is swift and collective — no single assassin claims credit. Aufidius himself participates, and the final image is of Aufidius standing on Coriolanus's body: "My rage is gone, / And I am struck with sorrow" (005-006). This posthumous respect — Aufidius's admission that Coriolanus was "a noble man" (005-006) — completes the tragedy's pattern: Coriolanus is honored only after he is dead.

## Dramatic Significance

Aufidius's public accusation is the play's final and most devastating political performance. Where the tribunes Brutus and Sicinius manipulated Roman public opinion to destroy Coriolanus politically, Aufidius manipulates Volscian public opinion to destroy him physically. The scene mirrors the forum confrontation of Act III: in both, Coriolanus is goaded into self-destructive rage by calculated insult. The difference is that in Antium there is no appeal, no banishment — only the sword.
