# Corioli

## Overview

**Corioli** is the Volscian city that gives [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] his name. Captured in a spectacular solo assault during the **[[battle-of-corioli|Battle of Corioli]]**, it is the site of Rome's greatest military triumph in the play (004-002) — and, in a bitter dramatic irony, the site of Rome's greatest soldier's [[death-of-coriolanus|death]] at the hands of the very Volscians he once conquered (029-002).

The city is more than a physical location: it is the symbolic ground where [[military-honor|martial identity]] is forged and destroyed. The walls of Corioli witness both the making of a hero (Act I) and the unmasking of a traitor (Act V), framing the entire arc of the tragedy.

> O me alone! Make you a sword of me.
> (003-002) — Coriolanus at the gates of Corioli

| Scene | Location | Action | Significance |
|-------|----------|--------|-------------|
| I.iv | Outside the walls | Roman advance and rout | Establishes Corioli as fortified stronghold |
| I.iv–v | Inside the walls | Marcius's solo assault | The defining act of martial *virtus* |
| I.v–vi | Within the city | Marcius emerges victorious | The city is taken |
| V.vi | Corioli (Volscian-held) | Death of Coriolanus | The city that gave him his name now witnesses his end |

## The Two Visits: I.iv and V.vi

Shakespeare structures Corioli's dramatic function through two visits by Coriolanus that mirror and invert each other:

| Aspect | First Visit (I.iv–vi) | Second Visit (V.vi) |
|--------|----------------------|---------------------|
| **Coriolanus's allegiance** | Roman | Volscian (defector) |
| **Coriolanus's role** | Conqueror | Betrayer |
| **Outcome** | Victory, naming, honour | Defeat, denunciation, death |
| **Relationship to city** | Entering as enemy warrior | Entering as false ally |
| **City's function** | Forge of identity | Site of annihilation |

This chiastic structure — the city that makes him unmakes him — is one of the play's most powerful architectural devices. Corioli is simultaneously Coriolanus's cradle and his grave.

## Corioli and Rome: The Symmetrical Cities

[[rome|Rome]] and Corioli function as symmetrical opposites across the play's moral geography:

| Aspect | Rome | Corioli |
|--------|------|---------|
| **Allegiance** | Roman/patrician | Volscian/enemy |
| **Political system** | Republican (Senate + Tribunes) | Military-aristocratic |
| **Coriolanus's standing** | Politically impossible | Militarily preeminent |
| **Danger to Coriolanus** | Political destruction (banishment) | Physical destruction (death) |
| **Gate imagery** | Gates of exile (IV.i) | Gates of battle (I.iv) |

Coriolanus moves between these two cities, unable to find a home in either. Rome cannot accommodate his contempt; Corioli cannot trust his loyalties. He is, in the deepest sense, a man without a city — which is to say, in the ancient world, a man without an identity.

## Historical Corioli

The historical Corioli was a Volscian stronghold in Latium, whose capture by Caius Marcius is recorded in Livy (II.33) and Plutarch's *Life of Coriolanus* (Shakespeare's primary source). The Volsci were an Italic people who contested Roman expansion in the 5th century BCE, and Corioli was one of their principal fortified settlements.

Shakespeare follows Plutarch closely in the battle narrative but compresses the historical timeline for dramatic effect. The historical capture of Corioli was part of a broader Roman campaign against the Volsci; Shakespeare makes it the singular event that defines his protagonist.

## Symbolic Resonance: The Walled City

The image of the **walled city** recurs throughout the play with symbolic density. Walls separate insiders from outsiders, citizens from enemies, belonging from exile. When Marcius enters Corioli's gates alone, he voluntarily becomes an outsider in enemy territory — a preview of his later exile (003-002). When the gates close behind him, he is symbolically already banished from Rome.

This gate imagery reaches its climax at the [[the-gates-of-rome|Gates of Rome]] in IV.i, when Coriolanus is formally expelled from his own city. Corioli's walls and Rome's walls define the same boundary: the line between belonging and exclusion that Coriolanus cannot help but cross.
