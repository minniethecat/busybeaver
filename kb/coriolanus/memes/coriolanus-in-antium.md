# Coriolanus in Antium

**Coriolanus in Antium** is the dramatic event in Act IV, Scenes IV–V of *Coriolanus* in which the banished Roman general arrives disguised in the enemy city of Antium and presents himself at the house of his mortal rival, Tullus Aufidius, offering his services to the Volscian state.

## Arrival in Disguise

Coriolanus enters Antium alone, wearing "mean apparel, disguised and muffled" (110-002). The city he once conquered now surrounds him, and he is acutely aware of the danger:

> "A goodly city is this Antium. City, / 'Tis I that made thy widows: many an heir / Of these fair edifices 'fore my wars / Have I heard groan and drop: then know me not, / Lest that thy wives with spits and boys with stones / In puny battle slay me." (110-002)

These lines convey the radical reversal of Coriolanus's fortunes. The conqueror has become the fugitive; the city he destroyed is now his only refuge. The phrase "made thy widows" is a stark acknowledgment of his past violence — an admission that would have been unthinkable for the Coriolanus of Acts I–III. His appeal to the city not to recognize him — "know me not" — is both a practical plea for safety and a deeper expression of his fractured identity. No longer "Coriolanus," the name Rome gave him, and no longer "Caius Marcius," the name his mother gave him, he enters Antium as a man without a name.

The costume change from general's armour to "mean apparel" is more than practical disguise; it is a visual emblem of his social death. The man who refused to display his wounds to the Roman people now hides his entire body from the Volscian people.

## The Soliloquy on Human Inconstancy

When a citizen directs him to Aufidius's house, Coriolanus delivers a bitter soliloquy on the mutability of human relationships:

> "O world, thy slippery turns! Friends now fast sworn, / Whose double bosoms seem to wear one heart, / Whose house, whose bed, whose meal, and exercise, / Are still together, who twin, as 'twere, in love / Unseparable, shall within this hour, / On a dissension of a doit, break out / To bitterest enmity: so, fellest foes, / Whose passions and whose plots have broke their sleep / To take the one the other, by some chance, / Some trick not worth an egg, shall grow dear friends / And interjoin their issues." (111-002)

This is one of the play's most philosophically ambitious passages. Coriolanus, who has spent the entire play despising rhetoric and verbal manipulation, suddenly becomes a moral philosopher. The soliloquy universalises his personal catastrophe: friendship and enmity, he argues, are equally fragile, equally products of chance rather than character. The domestic imagery — "house," "bed," "meal" — grounds the metaphysical speculation in the texture of ordinary life, making the argument's implications all the more unsettling.

The soliloquy's rhetorical architecture is a chiasmus: friends become enemies ("break out / To bitterest enmity"), and enemies become friends ("grow dear friends / And interjoin their issues"). This mirror structure enacts the very reversibility it describes, as if language itself cannot maintain stable oppositions.

His conclusion is personal and absolute:

> "My birth-place hate I, and my love's upon / This enemy town." (111-002)

The stark binary — hate my home, love the enemy — is characteristic of Coriolanus's all-or-nothing psychology. There is no middle ground, no ambivalence, no acknowledgment that exile might be a condition of complexity rather than simple inversion. His emotional world, like his political world, admits no gradations.

## Aufidius's House

The scene shifts to Aufidius's hall, where a feast is in progress. The servingmen's busy preparations — "Wine, wine, wine! What service is here!" (112-002) — provide a mundane counterpoint to the momentous meeting about to occur. Shakespeare frequently uses servants' scenes to provide ironic commentary on the main action, and the Antium servingmen are among his finest creations in this mode.

When Coriolanus enters the hall, the servingmen treat him as a vagrant, demanding to know "whence are you?" and telling him "Here's no place for you" (112-002). His anonymity among the revelers underscores his complete loss of status: in Rome, he was Coriolanus; in Antium, he is nobody.

The servingmen's dialogue is rich with dramatic irony:

> First Servingman: "Let me have war, say I; it exceeds peace as far as day does night; it's spritely, waking, audible, and full of vent." (112-002)

This comic celebration of war by the common soldier, delivered in prose, contrasts sharply with the aristocratic martial code that Coriolanus embodies — and that has just destroyed his life.

## The Offering and Its Reception

The crucial confrontation between Coriolanus and Aufidius occurs off-stage — a remarkable narrative choice for such a pivotal moment. Aufidius later describes the encounter:

> "I lov'd the maid I married; never man / Sigh'd truer breath; but that I see thee here, / Thou noble thing, more dances my rapt heart / Than when I first my wedded mistress saw / Bestride my threshold." (113-002)

Aufidius's language is explicitly romantic, even nuptial. The comparison between Coriolanus's arrival and his wedding night — "more dances my rapt heart / Than when I first my wedded mistress saw / Bestride my threshold" — frames the military alliance in the vocabulary of marriage. The word "rapt" carries both its modern sense (enthralled) and its Latin root *rapere* (to seize, to ravish). Aufidius is ravished by Coriolanus's presence.

This homoerotic charge — already present in Aufidius's earlier description of their rivalry as a form of intimacy — reaches its peak in this moment of reunion. But Shakespeare immediately undercuts it with dramatic irony. Aufidius's servants have already revealed that "our general himself makes a mistress of him" (113-002), and the audience knows that this "marriage" of convenience will end in the "divorce" of Aufidius's conspiracy.

## The Servingmen's Commentary

The servingmen's conversation after Coriolanus's reception provides the scene's choral commentary. They are at once awed, cynical, and prescient:

> "This peace is nothing, but to rust iron, increase tailors, and breed ballad-makers." (113-002)

The line distills a soldier's contempt for peacetime — peace as rust, peace as the proliferation of tailors and ballad-makers — but it also performs the scene's key thematic work. The peace that seemed so precious in Rome (the women's embassy, the treaty) is here dismissed as wasted time. The Volscian perspective reframes everything the Roman scenes have established.

> "Let me have war, say I."

The servingman's cry, repeated throughout the scene, echoes Marcius's own "I am glad on't" at the war declaration in Act I. The appetite for war is not limited to the aristocratic warrior; it permeates the entire social order. In Coriolanus's Rome — and in Aufidius's Antium — war is the default state; peace is the anomaly.

## Thematic Significance

Coriolanus's arrival in Antium crystallises several of the play's central themes:

- **Identity and naming**: Stripped of his Roman name, Coriolanus enters Antium unnamed — and leaves it with a new name (traitor) when Aufidius's conspiracy succeeds.
- **The reversibility of loyalty**: The scene dramatises the terrifying speed with which allegiance can be transferred. Coriolanus's hatred of Rome and love for Antium is presented not as a considered political position but as an emotional reflex — a reaction against his rejection.
- **The warrior's rootlessness**: Coriolanus belongs nowhere. Rome cannot contain him; Antium will not keep him. His tragedy is not that he is exiled from his city but that he is constitutionally incapable of belonging to any city.
- **The fragility of the political**: The scene's alternation between high rhetoric (Coriolanus's soliloquy) and low comedy (the servingmen) suggests that heroic action is always shadowed by mundane reality.
