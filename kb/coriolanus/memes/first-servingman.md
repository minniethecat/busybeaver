# First Servingman

The **First Servingman** is a servant in the household of [[tullus-aufidius|Tullus Aufidius]] in Shakespeare's *[[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]*. He appears in Act IV Scene V, set in [[antium|Antium]], where he interacts with [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] — who has come to Aufidius's house in disguise — and later provides witty commentary on [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s character and the nature of war and peace.

## Role in the Play

The First Servingman first appears calling for wine and complaining about his fellows being asleep [021-002]. When [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] arrives at Aufidius's house in humble attire, the First Servingman mistakes him for a vagrant and asks him to leave, failing to recognize the Roman general [021-002].

After Aufidius and [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] have met and left together, the First Servingman joins the Second and Third [[servingmen|Servingmen]] in a comic scene. He marvels at [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s physical strength — "he turned me about with his finger and his thumb, as one would set up a top" [021-002] — and discusses [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s military prowess against Aufidius at [[corioli|Corioli]] [021-008].

## Key Dialogue

The First Servingman delivers a famous speech praising war over peace:

> "Let me have war, say I; it exceeds peace as far as day does night; it's spritely, waking, audible, and full of vent. Peace is a very apoplexy, lethargy; mulled, deaf, sleepy, insensible; a getter of more bastard children than war's a destroyer of men." [021-008]

This passage is notable as one of Shakespeare's most vivid poetic contrasts between war and peace.

## Dramatic Function

The First Servingman and his fellow servants provide comic relief after the intense confrontation between [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] and Aufidius. Their scene functions as choral commentary on the main action, [[sacrifice|offering]] a "common man's" perspective on the great figures they serve. The servants' debate over whether [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] or Aufidius is the greater soldier mirrors the play's larger themes of rivalry, honor, and reputation.

## Relationships

- **[[tullus-aufidius|Tullus Aufidius]]**: The First Servingman's master. He expresses admiration for his general but is realistic about his military capabilities compared to [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] [021-008].
- **[[second-servingman|Second Servingman]]**: His fellow servant and conversational partner throughout the comic scene.
- **[[third-servingman|Third Servingman]]**: The third servant who brings news of the proceedings inside the house.
- **[[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]**: The First Servingman initially dismisses him as a tramp but is later deeply impressed by his physical presence and military reputation [021-002].
