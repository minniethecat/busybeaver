# Fame

Fame is one of the central motivating forces in *Coriolanus*, inextricably linked to the protagonist's identity, name, and ultimate tragedy. The play presents fame not as vanity but as the essential currency of Roman civic life — the public recognition through which martial deeds are converted into lasting honour.

## Fame as Maternal Ambition

The pursuit of fame is instilled in Coriolanus from his earliest years by his mother, Volumnia. In a striking passage, she describes how she "was pleased to let him seek danger where he was like to find fame" (048-052), revealing that she valued military glory over her son's safety. For Volumnia, honour without renown is "no better than picture-like to hang by the wall" — invisible and worthless without public acknowledgment (048-052). This maternal conditioning shapes Coriolanus's entire value system, making him incapable of the performative humility the political sphere demands.

## The Naming of Coriolanus

The play's central act of naming explicitly connects fame to identity. After the Battle of Corioli, the Herald proclaims that Marcius "hath won, / With fame, a name to Caius Marcius; these / In honour follows Coriolanus" (405-410). The surname "Coriolanus" is fame made concrete — a name earned through singular martial achievement. Yet this very fame, acquired in the private violence of war, becomes a liability in the public theatre of politics, where Coriolanus must display his wounds to earn the people's "voices."

## Fame and Envy

The dark side of fame is envy, expressed most powerfully by Aufidius. His hatred of Coriolanus is explicitly framed as a response to his rival's fame: "Not Afric owns a serpent I abhor / More than thy fame and envy" (094-099). Aufidius later laments how he "holp to reap the fame / Which he did end all his" (448-470), revealing how Coriolanus's fame paradoxically grew even as Aufidius served him. This envy, rooted in the zero-sum nature of martial renown, drives the conspiracy that ultimately kills Coriolanus.

## Fame in Death

Even after Coriolanus's death, the language of fame persists. The Second Lord declares that "The man is noble and his fame folds-in / This orb o' the earth" (460-470), suggesting that Coriolanus's renown transcends his controversial actions. The play thus ends with an ambivalent picture of fame: it is the engine of Coriolanus's greatness and the source of his destruction — a force that elevates him above common men while isolating him from the community whose recognition gives fame its meaning.
