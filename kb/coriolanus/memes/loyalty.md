# Loyalty

## Definition

**Loyalty** (*fides*) in *Coriolanus* is the bond of obligation that ties individuals to the communities — family, class, city — that define their identity. The play systematically tests every loyalty bond it establishes: citizen to state (Coriolanus's defection to the Volscians), soldier to commander (Aufidius's betrayal of his Volscian ally), and son to mother (the final confrontation at the gates of Rome).

Shakespeare presents loyalty not as a simple binary — loyal or disloyal — but as a field of competing obligations. Coriolanus is simultaneously loyal (to his martial identity, to his mother's demands) and disloyal (to Rome, to his class). The tragedy lies in the impossibility of honouring all the loyalty bonds that define him.

## Historical Background

Roman *fides* was a complex concept encompassing religious, moral, legal, and political dimensions. The temple of *Fides* on the Capitoline Hill housed the treaties and international agreements of the Roman people, and the goddess *Fides* personified the reliability Rome offered its allies. Breach of *fides* was not merely immoral but impious — an offence against the gods as well as against the injured party.

In the patron-client relationship (*clientela*), *fides* was reciprocal: the patron owed protection and assistance, the client owed loyalty and political support. This network of vertical obligation — later theorized by Machiavelli as a source of republican corruption — was fundamental to Roman social organization.

## In the Play

Coriolanus's loyalty is structured around martial *virtus* rather than civic obligation. He is loyal to his comrades in arms, to his general Cominius, to his mother Volumnia — but not to the Roman people or the Roman constitution. This hierarchy of loyalties makes him a great soldier but a political catastrophe.

The crisis of loyalty intensifies dramatically in Acts 4 and 5. Coriolanus's defection to the Volscians is the ultimate breach of loyalty — a Roman general leading Rome's enemies against his own city. Yet even this breach is motivated by a twisted form of loyalty: loyalty to his own wounded honour, which demands vengeance against the city that rejected him.

Aufidius's final betrayal — denouncing Coriolanus as a traitor to the Volscians and inciting the mob to kill him — mirrors Coriolanus's own trajectory. The Volscian general who welcomed the exiled Roman cannot tolerate being eclipsed by him, and loyalty between warriors yields to rivalry.

## Relationship to Other Concepts

- **Military Honor**: Martial *fides* — loyalty to comrades and commander — is the core of Coriolanus's identity. When this loyalty conflicts with civic loyalty, martial honour wins.
- **Exile**: Exile dissolves civic loyalty. Coriolanus owes Rome nothing once Rome has banished him — or so he believes. The play questions whether civic obligation can ever be fully dissolved.
- **Pride**: Pride distorts loyalty by making the self the ultimate object of allegiance. Coriolanus is most loyal to his own *virtus*, and all other loyalties are subordinate.
- **Patrician Power**: Class loyalty binds patricians together against plebeian assertion. The patricians' inability to defend Coriolanus effectively reflects a failure of class solidarity.

## Critical Perspectives

The loyalty crisis in *Coriolanus* resonated with Jacobean anxieties about treason and allegiance. The Gunpowder Plot (1605) — a conspiracy by Catholic recusants to blow up Parliament, within living memory when the play was written — raised the question of whether loyalty to one's faith could override loyalty to one's sovereign. Coriolanus's defection poses the same question in a classical register: can loyalty to honour justify loyalty's breach?
