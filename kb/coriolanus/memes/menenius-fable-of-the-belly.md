# Menenius's Fable of the Belly

## Event Summary

In the opening scene of *Coriolanus*, armed citizens march on Rome demanding grain. Menenius Agrippa intercepts them and, rather than deploying soldiers, deploys a story. His fable of the belly — in which the body's members rebel against the belly for hoarding food, only to learn that the belly distributes nourishment to every part — is Shakespeare's most explicit political allegory. The story temporarily calms the crowd but also reveals the deep ideological rifts that no story can permanently bridge.

## The Fable

When the First Citizen accuses the patricians of hoarding grain while the people starve — "They ne'er cared for us yet: suffer us to famish, and their storehouses crammed with grain" (001-001) — Menenius responds not with argument but with narrative:

> "There was a time when all the body's members / Rebell'd against the belly, thus accused it: / That only like a gulf it did remain / I' the midst o' the body, idle and unactive, / Still cupboarding the viand, never bearing / Like labour with the rest" (001-001)

The belly's answer — delivered with a smile "which ne'er came from the lungs" (001-001) — is that it receives food first only to redistribute it: "I send it through the rivers of your blood, / Even to the court, the heart, to the seat o' the brain" (001-001). The belly claims that it keeps only "the bran" while the members receive "the flour of all" (001-001).

## The Political Application

Menenius makes the allegory's meaning explicit: "The senators of Rome are this good belly, / And you the mutinous members" (001-001). The fable is a defense of patrician privilege disguised as an explanation of organic interdependence. It naturalizes hierarchy — the belly is "grave" and "deliberate," the members are rash and ignorant — while obscuring the fact that in Rome, the belly keeps rather more than bran. The First Citizen's earlier complaint about "storehouses crammed with grain" stands unrebutted; it is simply displaced by the aesthetic satisfaction of a well-told tale.

## The Fable's Limitations

The fable's effects are temporary and contingent. The citizens are not persuaded so much as interrupted: Caius Marcius's entrance immediately afterward, with his contemptuous address — "What's the matter, you dissentious rogues, / That, rubbing the poor itch of your opinion, / Make yourselves scabs?" (001-001) — shatters any goodwill Menenius created. The fable also fails structurally: the concession extracted from the scene — the creation of the tribunate — is precisely the institutional mechanism that will later destroy Coriolanus. The story that was meant to preserve patrician power instead helps create the political office that challenges it.

## Dramatic Significance

Menenius's fable is the play's first and most elaborate set-piece speech, establishing the rhetorical character of Roman politics. In a world where physical force is never far from the surface, language — stories, allegories, insults — serves as the primary weapon of class warfare. The fable also introduces Menenius as the play's great mediator, a role he will play until his failed embassy in Act V. His tragedy, in miniature, is the fable's tragedy: stories can delay conflict but cannot resolve it.
