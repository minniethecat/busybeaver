# Consulship

## Definition

The **consulship** was the supreme executive magistracy of Republican Rome — the office toward which every Roman political career aimed and which Coriolanus's military achievements should have secured. The contested consular election of Act 2 is the procedural center of *Coriolanus*, and its failure precipitates the crisis that ends in banishment, defection, and death.

## Historical Background

Two consuls were elected annually, sharing *imperium* — the supreme authority to command armies and administer justice. The consulship combined the monarchical element of the mixed constitution with the republican principles of collegiality and annuality. The tribunes' veto power (*intercessio*) extended to consular acts, creating the institutional tension Shakespeare dramatizes.

## In the Play

Coriolanus's pursuit of the consulship follows constitutional form — senatorial endorsement, then popular ratification. His refusal to perform the popular stage sincerely is the crux: the constitution requires a display of humility he cannot provide. The tribunes exploit this, first granting the voices, then revoking them — a procedural maneuver that uses constitutional mechanisms to produce constitutional crisis.

## Relationship to Other Concepts

- **Tribunate**: The tribunes' veto over consular action is the democratic check on executive power. The play dramatizes this check at its maximum intensity.
- **Military Honor**: Consular candidacy requires demonstrated *virtus*, which Coriolanus possesses absolutely. The irony is that the office designed to reward military excellence is denied to Rome's greatest soldier.
- **Roman Constitution**: The consulship is the monarchical element of the mixed constitution. The crisis demonstrates that the monarchical and democratic elements cannot coexist when both are asserted absolutely.

## Critical Perspectives

Political readings of the play have seen the contested consulship as a mirror of Jacobean conflicts between royal prerogative and parliamentary privilege. Coriolanus's claim that martial *virtus* entitles him to the consulship without popular ratification echoes James I's assertion of divine right against parliamentary consent.
