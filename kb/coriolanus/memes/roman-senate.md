# Roman Senate

## Definition

The **Roman Senate** in Shakespeare's *Coriolanus* is the supreme political council of Republican Rome — an assembly of patrician elders whose collective authority governs matters of war, peace, and the election of consuls. The Senate functions as both the institutional expression of [[patricians|patrician]] rule and the arena in which the play's central political conflicts are transacted: the nomination of [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] to the consulship, the management of the [[plebeians|plebeian]] corn crisis, and the escalating confrontation between aristocratic authority and [[tribunate|tribunician]] power.

Shakespeare's Senate is not a debating chamber but a symbol of institutional continuity — its members speak sparingly and collectively, their authority assumed rather than argued. The First Senator, unnamed, voices the corporate will of the body; individual senators ([[cominius|Cominius]], [[menenius-agrippa|Menenius]]) act as its instruments in military and political spheres respectively.

## Key Members

The Senate in *Coriolanus* is represented as a corporate body rather than a collection of individuated characters, but several named patricians function as its executive agents:

| Senator | Role in the Senate | Dramatic Function |
|---------|-------------------|------------------|
| **[[cominius|Cominius]]** | Consul and military commander | Delivers the formal nomination speech for Coriolanus in the Senate (002-001) — the institutional voice of martial meritocracy |
| **[[menenius-agrippa|Menenius]]** | Elder statesman and mediator | The Senate's rhetorical arm — his belly fable (001-055) is the Senate's self-justification made flesh |
| **First Senator** | Presiding officer (unnamed) | Opens and closes Senate proceedings; the impersonal voice of institutional procedure |

The Tribune [[sicinius-velutus|Sicinius Velutus]] observes that the Senate's authority depends on popular acquiescence — "the senators of Rome are this good belly" (001-073) — a metaphor that the tribunes weaponize against Coriolanus during his trial.

## Dramatic Role

The Senate serves three interconnected dramatic functions:

**1. The Institution of Legitimacy.** The Senate is the source of lawful authority in the play's Rome. Consuls are elected by the Senate, wars are declared by the Senate, and treaties are ratified by its members. When Cominius reports Coriolanus's victory at [[corioli|Corioli]], it is before the assembled Senate that the honours are decreed.

> Be it known, as to us, to all the world,
> That Caius Marcius wears this war's garland. (002-001)

**2. The Arena of Class Conflict.** The Senate chamber is the physical space where patrician and plebeian interests collide. The tribunes attend Senate sessions as observers, and the tension between senatorial authority and popular representation is acted out in the spatial arrangement of the stage — "[[the-senate-house|the Senate-house]]" (003-001), where the senators take their elevated seats while the tribunes sit apart.

**3. The Mirror of Coriolanus.** The Senate's values — martial excellence, deference to established authority, contempt for popular opinion — are Coriolanus's values pushed to their institutional extreme. His tragedy unfolds when the Senate, as an institution, cannot protect him from the popular forces it has failed to accommodate.

## The Senate and the Consular Election

The consular election sequence (Act II) represents the Senate at the height of its institutional power — and the moment when its limitations become most visible. Cominius's nomination speech in the Senate, recounting Coriolanus's sixteen years of military service and his single-handed capture of [[corioli]], is a masterpiece of patrician rhetoric: martial achievement translated into political currency through institutional ritual [002-001].

But the Senate's authority over the election is constitutionally incomplete. Under the Roman republican system, the Senate *nominates* but the people *confirm*. The Senate can declare Coriolanus worthy of the consulship, but it cannot make him consul without the people's voice — a procedural dependency that the patricians find galling and that the tribunes exploit. Menenius's exasperated question to the tribunes — "We have the election; / What needs the people's voice?" — reveals the patrician assumption that popular consent is a formality, not a constitutional requirement [013-001].

The Senate's failure in the election sequence is institutional rather than personal. It correctly identifies Coriolanus as Rome's most deserving candidate by its own criteria (martial excellence), but it cannot — or will not — manage the political process required to translate that judgment into popular ratification. The Senate's institutional dignity prevents it from engaging seriously with the ritual of popular confirmation, treating it as a degrading concession rather than a legitimate element of republican governance.

This institutional rigidness sets the stage for Coriolanus's destruction: the Senate that elevates him cannot protect him; the institution that defines his worth cannot defend its own judgment when challenged by the tribunes.

## Historical Background

The historical Roman Senate of the early Republic (5th century BCE) consisted of approximately 300 patrician members serving for life. It controlled state finances, foreign policy, and the nomination of magistrates — powers reflected with reasonable fidelity in Shakespeare's depiction, though the playwright compresses the chronology and simplifies the constitutional machinery (the historical Senate did not directly elect consuls; the *comitia centuriata* did).

Shakespeare's source, [[plutarch|Plutarch's]] *Life of Coriolanus* (via North's translation), emphasizes the Senate's dignity and its vulnerability to popular agitation — a tension that the play dramatizes more sharply than its source material.
