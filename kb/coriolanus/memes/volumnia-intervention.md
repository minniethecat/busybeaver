# Volumnia's Intervention

## Event Summary


### Analysis

Outside the walls of Rome, with the Volscian army poised for destruction, Volumnia leads an embassy of women — Virgilia (Coriolanus's wife), Valeria, and the young Marcius (his son) — to plead for the city's salvation (Act V, Scene III). After rejecting the appeals of Cominius and Menenius, Coriolanus is unmoved by political arguments; but his mother's supplication, culminating in an extended silent rebuke, breaks his resolve. He agrees to negotiate a peace, declaring, "O mother, mother! / What have you done?" (026-002). The intervention saves Rome but condemns Coriolanus to Aufidius's vengeance.

## The Embassy of Women

The scene is structured as a series of escalating appeals. Coriolanus has already refused Cominius, his former general, and Menenius, whom he once called father. His resolve seems absolute: "I'll never / Be such a gosling to obey instinct, but stand / As if a man were author of himself / And knew no other kin" (026-002). The arrival of the women tests this declaration of autonomous manhood.

Virgilia speaks first — "my young boy / Hath an aspect of intercession" (026-002) — but it is Volumnia who orchestrates the assault. Her rhetoric alternates between supplication and threat. She kneels, the gesture of ultimate submission, but her words carry the force of moral indictment:

> "Thou shalt no sooner / March to assault thy country than to tread — / Trust to't, thou shalt not — on thy mother's womb / That brought thee to this world" (026-002)

The image of Coriolanus trampling his mother's womb collapses the distinction between martial conquest and matricide. It is the one argument he cannot counter with warrior logic.

## The Silent Rebuke

Volumnia's most devastating weapon is silence. When Coriolanus wavers, she withdraws: "Come, let us go" (026-002). She tells Virgilia, "This fellow had a Volscian to his mother" (026-002), disowning him with the gentlest of words. The young Marcius is instructed not to plead — "Speak thou for him? / Tell him I had rather had eleven die nobly / For their country than one voluptuously surfeit out of action" (026-002). This invocation of patriotic martyrdom — the mother who would rather have her son die for Rome than live for himself — weaponizes the very values Volumnia instilled in Coriolanus as a child.

The turning point comes without words. The stage direction — the only extended passage of silence in a play built on verbal combat — marks Coriolanus's collapse: "holds her by the hand, silent" (026-002). Volumnia has broken him not with argument but with presence, with the inescapable reality of the maternal bond he has tried to disavow.

## The Cost of Victory

Coriolanus's surrender is absolute and self-aware:

> "O mother, mother! / What have you done? Behold, the heavens do ope, / The gods look down, and this unnatural scene / They laugh at. O my mother, mother! O! / You have won a happy victory to Rome; / But, for your son — believe it, O, believe it — / Most dangerously you have with him prevail'd, / If not most mortal to him" (026-002)

He knows, in the moment of capitulation, that he is signing his own death warrant. The Volscians will not forgive a second betrayal. Aufidius, who has watched the scene unfold, closes Act V Scene III with a soliloquy that confirms the fatal calculation: "I am glad thou hast set thy mercy and thy honour / At difference in thee: out of that I'll work / Myself a former fortune" (026-002).

## Dramatic Significance

Volumnia's intervention is the peripeteia of *Coriolanus* — the reversal that transforms the trajectory of the action. It represents the play's deepest engagement with the competing claims of family, state, and self. Coriolanus, who has spent the entire play asserting his independence from the Roman polity, discovers that he cannot escape the maternal bond. The scene inverts the play's opening dynamic: where Act I showed a mother sending her son to war as the price of honor, Act V shows a mother calling her son back from war as the price of love — and both demands prove fatal.

## Participants

- **Volumnia** — the architect of the intervention. Her rhetorical mastery — alternating supplication, moral indictment, and silence — achieves what no male petitioner could.

- **Caius Marcius Coriolanus** — the target, who capitulates knowing the cost. His surrender is simultaneously his most human and most fatal act.

- **Virgilia** — Coriolanus's wife, whose silent presence and tearful submission provide the emotional counterpoint to Volumnia's aggressive rhetoric.

- **Valeria** — the noblewoman whose presence adds civic weight to the family embassy.

- **Young Marcius** — Coriolanus's son, whose presence embodies the generational stakes of the decision.

- **Tullus Aufidius** — the silent observer whose closing soliloquy converts the scene's mercy into strategic opportunity.
