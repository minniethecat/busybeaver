# Menenius's Rejected Embassy

## Event Summary

In Act V, Scene II, Menenius Agrippa — the aging patrician who once called Coriolanus his "son" — arrives alone at the Volscian camp before Rome, seeking to negotiate where the official Roman embassy had failed. Two Volscian sentries bar his way, mock his age and his claim to be Coriolanus's friend. When Coriolanus finally appears, Menenius delivers an impassioned plea, invoking their past intimacy: "The glorious gods sit in hourly synod about thy particular prosperity, and love thee no worse than thy old father Menenius does!" (005-002). Coriolanus responds with chilling finality: "Away!" He gives Menenius a letter already written — evidence that the appeal was anticipated and preemptively rejected — and declares "Wife, mother, child, I know not. My affairs / Are servanted to others" (005-002). The embassy is the last personal bridge between Coriolanus and Rome, and it is burned.

## The Sentry Confrontation

Before Menenius can reach Coriolanus, he must pass through Volscian sentries who treat his claims of intimacy with contempt. When Menenius announces himself — "The general is my lover: I have been / The book of his good acts" (005-002) — the First Sentinel retorts that if Menenius "had told as many lies in his behalf as you have uttered words in your own, you should not pass here" (005-002). The sentries mock the idea that a "decayed dotant" (005-002) could influence the Volscian general, and they warn him: "you are condemned, our general has sworn you out of reprieve and pardon" (005-002). The guards' cynicism establishes the atmosphere of military siege — legal, moral, and personal niceties are suspended before the walls of a doomed city.

## Coriolanus's Rejection

When Coriolanus enters with Aufidius, Menenius's tone shifts from self-assurance to desperate supplication. He presents himself as the last hope: "I was hardly moved to come to thee; but being assured none but myself could move thee" (005-002). Coriolanus's reply — "Away!" — is the shortest, most brutal rejection in the play. Menenius cannot process it: "How! away!" (005-002). The exchange is structured as a systematic dismantling of every claim Menenius makes: their shared Roman identity, their paternal bond, the claims of pity. Coriolanus's response is not anger but something colder — neutrality governed by a new political reality in which "my affairs / Are servanted to others" and "my remission lies / In Volscian breasts" (005-002).

The pre-written letter Coriolanus hands Menenius — "I writ it for thy sake, / And would have sent it" (005-002) — is the cruelest touch. It reveals that Coriolanus anticipated this encounter and foreclosed it before Menenius spoke a word. The embassy was doomed before it began.

## Dramatic Significance

Menenius's failed embassy is structurally the second of three appeals to Coriolanus — preceded by the formal Roman embassy of Cominius, and followed by the women's procession led by Volumnia. Where Cominius represents the state and Volumnia represents family, Menenius represents the claim of personal friendship, the "old love" (005-002) that made him Coriolanus's surrogate father. Its failure demonstrates that no human claim — not Rome, not friendship, and (the audience now wonders) perhaps not even mother — can penetrate Coriolanus's armor of vengeance. The embassy also marks Menenius's final appearance in the play, ending his arc from confident mediator to broken old man.
