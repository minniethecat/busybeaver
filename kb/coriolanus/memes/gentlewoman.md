# Gentlewoman

## Overview

The Gentlewoman is a minor domestic attendant in the household of [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]], serving [[virgilia|Virgilia]]. Her single speaking moment — announcing the arrival of [[valeria|Valeria]] in Act I, Scene 3 — is one of the few glimpses of domestic service life in the play. Though her role is brief, she frames one of the play's most intimate scenes, where [[virgilia|Virgilia]] and [[volumnia|Volumnia]] discuss [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s absence at war.

## Her Single Scene: Announcing Valeria (003-002)

### The Interruption

The Gentlewoman enters in the midst of a charged conversation between [[volumnia|Volumnia]] and [[virgilia|Virgilia]]. [[volumnia|Volumnia]] has been delivering an extraordinary speech — declaring that she would rather have "eleven die nobly for their country than one voluptuously surfeit out of action" (003-002) — and [[virgilia|Virgilia]], distressed by the talk of blood and honour, has just demurred. The Gentlewoman's entrance is perfectly timed: she interrupts this torrent of martial rhetoric with the simple domestic announcement:

> Madam, the Lady Valeria is come to visit you. (003-002)

The contrast is structural. [[volumnia|Volumnia]] speaks of Hecuba, Hector, Grecian swords, and spitting blood; the Gentlewoman speaks of a social call. Her interruption pulls the scene back from the battlefield to the household — a shift that the rest of the episode (the conversation with [[valeria|Valeria]] about sewing, butterflies, and young [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Marcius]]'s exploits) will sustain.

### Volumnia's Dismissal

[[volumnia|Volumnia]] does not treat the Gentlewoman as a person but as an instrument. Her response is immediate and cutting:

> Away, you fool! … Tell Valeria, We are fit to bid her welcome. (003-002)

The epithet "fool" — addressed to a serving woman who has done nothing but perform her duty — reveals [[volumnia|Volumnia]]'s imperiousness in its most casual and unguarded form. She is not performing for an audience; she is simply speaking to a servant, and in that unguarded moment her character stands fully revealed. The stage direction then notes the Gentlewoman's exit and re-entry: she returns with [[valeria|Valeria]] and an Usher (003-002), her function complete.

## Dramatic Significance

The Gentlewoman's presence — and [[volumnia|Volumnia]]'s brusque treatment of her — reinforces the play's attention to social hierarchy even within the domestic sphere. She appears in the *Dramatis Personae* as "Gentlewoman, attending on [[virgilia|Virgilia]]" (P01-016), a designation that defines her entirely through service to another. Her brief appearance establishes the household as a setting before the key conversation between [[volumnia|Volumnia]], [[virgilia|Virgilia]], and [[valeria|Valeria]] unfolds. She is a functional character — a door through which [[valeria|Valeria]] enters — but her presence reminds the audience that the private grief of [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s wife is witnessed by servants who remain silent and unnamed.

The Gentlewoman's role also illuminates the play's structural interest in thresholds. She stands at the literal threshold of [[virgilia|Virgilia]]'s chamber, and her announcement marks the passage from the intimate mother-daughter conversation to the wider social world represented by [[valeria|Valeria]]. In a play obsessed with gates — the gates of [[corioli|Corioli]], the gates of [[rome|Rome]], the gates of [[antium|Antium]] — the Gentlewoman performs a miniature version of the gatekeeper's function in the domestic register.
