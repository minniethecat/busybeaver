## Overview

The Second Officer is a minor character in *[[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]* who appears in Act II, Scene II at the Capitol. Together with the [[first-officer|First Officer]], he prepares the senatorial chamber for [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s [[consulship|consul]]ar confirmation (450). Where the [[first-officer|First Officer]] criticizes [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s pride, the Second Officer mounts a spirited defense, arguing that [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s achievements justify his indifference to popular opinion.

## In the Play

The Second Officer enters with the [[first-officer|First Officer]] to lay cushions in the Capitol before the senatorial session. When the [[first-officer|First Officer]] asks how many candidates stand for the [[consulship|consul]]ship, the Second Officer replies: "Three, they say: but 'tis thought of every one [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] will carry it" (452).

After the [[first-officer|First Officer]] criticizes [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] as proud and contemptuous of the common people (453), the Second Officer offers a nuanced rebuttal (454):

> Faith, there had been many great men that have flattered the people, who ne'er loved them; and there be many that they have loved, they know not wherefore: so that, if they love they know not why, they hate upon no better a ground: therefore, for Coriolanus neither to care whether they love or hate him manifests the true knowledge he has in their disposition; and out of his noble carelessness lets them plainly see't. (454)

His argument — that the people's affections are arbitrary and therefore [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s indifference is a form of honesty — provides a counterpoint to the [[first-officer|First Officer]]'s suspicion.

When the [[first-officer|First Officer]] continues to insist that [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] actively seeks the people's hatred (455), the Second Officer responds with his most sustained defense, praising [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s military record and arguing that his deeds speak louder than any need for popular courtship (456):

> He hath deserved worthily of his country: and his ascent is not by such easy degrees as those who, having been supple and courteous to the people, bonneted, without any further deed to have them at all into their estimation and report: but he hath so planted his honours in their eyes, and his actions in their hearts, that for their tongues to be silent, and not confess so much, were a kind of ingrateful injury; to report otherwise, were a malice, that, giving itself the lie, would pluck reproof and rebuke from every ear that heard it. (456)

## Dramatic Function

The Second Officer and his colleague function as a miniature debate, encapsulating the play's central conflict between military merit and political popularity within a brief, realistic exchange. The Second Officer articulates the meritocratic ideal — that deeds, not flattery, should determine leadership — which is the standard by which [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] measures himself but which proves politically fatal in [[rome|Rome]]'s democratic institutions.
