# Rhetoric

## Definition

**Rhetoric** — the art of political persuasion — is a central but asymmetrically distributed skill in *Coriolanus*. The tribunes wield it expertly, Menenius practices a paternalist version, and Coriolanus refuses it on principle — a refusal that is itself a powerful rhetorical gesture.

## Historical Background

Roman republican politics was conducted through speech. The contio (public meeting), the Senate debate, and the law courts were all arenas of rhetorical competition. The ability to persuade — to move an audience through argument, emotion, and character — was the essential political skill, and Roman education was built around rhetorical training.

## In the Play

Menenius's belly fable is the play's most famous rhetorical performance — a political argument disguised as a children's story (001-002). The tribunes' orchestration of the consular election is a sustained exercise in political communication, framing Coriolanus's pride as tyranny. Coriolanus's anti-rhetoric — his insistence on plain speech, his refusal to flatter — is paradoxically the most distinctive rhetorical posture in the play.

The tragedy is partly a tragedy of rhetoric: Coriolanus cannot speak the language the republican constitution requires, and the constitution cannot accommodate a citizen who refuses its discursive norms.

## Relationship to Other Concepts

- **Populism**: Rhetoric is the instrument of populist politics. The tribunes' populism is effective because they are skilled rhetoricians.
- **Tribunate**: The tribunate functions through rhetoric — the tribunes' power is the power to persuade the plebeian assembly.
- **Class Conflict**: Rhetoric is the weapon of class warfare when physical force is not in play. The class conflict in Coriolanus is conducted through competing rhetorical appeals.
- **Roman Constitution**: The mixed constitution requires rhetoric to function; deliberation, debate, and persuasion are the mechanisms that translate social conflict into political decision.

## Critical Perspectives

Wayne Rebhorn (1990) and other scholars of Renaissance rhetoric have analyzed *Coriolanus* as a sustained meditation on the politics of eloquence. Coriolanus's anti-rhetoric is not apolitical but hyper-political — a refusal to participate in discursive exchange that is also a claim of aristocratic superiority over those who must persuade rather than command.
