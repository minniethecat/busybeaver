# Corn Riot

## Event Summary


### Analysis

The Corn Riot opens *Coriolanus* as a company of mutinous citizens armed with "staves, clubs, and other weapons" demand grain at fair prices (001-002). Menenius Agrippa attempts to pacify them with the celebrated fable of the belly — an allegory in which the Senate is the belly that distributes nourishment to the body's rebellious members. Caius Marcius enters and immediately insults the rioters as "dissentious rogues" and "curs," revealing the aristocratic contempt that will define his tragic trajectory. The riot is redirected rather than resolved: news of the Volscian army's approach diverts attention to war, and the concession extracted — the creation of five tribunes including Sicinius Velutus and Junius Brutus — plants the seed for Coriolanus's eventual downfall.

## The Belly Fable

Menenius Agrippa's fable of the belly (001-002) is the scene's rhetorical centerpiece and one of Shakespeare's most explicit political allegories. When the citizens protest that the patricians "suffer us to famish, and their storehouses crammed with grain" (001-002), Menenius counters with a tale in which the body's members rebel against the belly, accusing it of idleness:

> "There was a time when all the body's members / Rebell'd against the belly, thus accused it: / That only like a gulf it did remain / I' the midst o' the body, idle and unactive, / Still cupboarding the viand, never bearing / Like labour with the rest" (001-002)

The belly's reply — that it receives food only to "send it through the rivers of your blood / Even to the court, the heart, to the seat o' the brain" (001-002) — recasts the Senate not as parasitic but as distributive. Menenius concludes bluntly: "The senators of Rome are this good belly, / And you the mutinous members" (001-002). The allegory characterizes Menenius as the play's most persuasive — and patronizing — patrician voice.

## Coriolanus's Entrance

Caius Marcius's first appearance in the play is an explosion of contempt. Before any greeting, he addresses the citizens:

> "What's the matter, you dissentious rogues, / That, rubbing the poor itch of your opinion, / Make yourselves scabs?" (001-002)

When Menenius explains they demand "corn at their own rates" (001-002), Marcius escalates: "Hang 'em! They say! / They'll sit by the fire, and presume to know / What's done i' the Capitol" (001-002). His metaphor of the body — the citizens as diseases on the skin of the state — directly inverts Menenius's organic metaphor, replacing integrative anatomy with pathology.

The scene establishes Coriolanus's defining flaw: he cannot dissemble. When news arrives of the Volscian threat, his eagerness for battle is genuine — "I am glad on't: then we shall ha' means to vent / Our musty superfluity" (001-002) — but his dismissal of the citizens as "rats" to be led to "gnaw their garners" (001-002) ensures that even military victory cannot repair the political damage.

## The Tribunate as Political Concession

The riot's immediate outcome is the grant of "five tribunes to defend their vulgar wisdoms, / Of their own choice" (001-002). Marcius names the two who will become his nemeses: "one's Junius Brutus, / Sicinius Velutus" (001-002). His fury at this concession — "The rabble should have first unroof'd the city, / Ere so prevail'd with me" (001-002) — positions the tribunate as the structural obstacle that will later engineer his banishment.

The tribunes themselves close Act I Scene 1 by plotting to ensure Marcius receives no credit for the coming battle. Brutus observes that "Half all Cominius' honours are to Marcius, / Though Marcius earned them not" (001-002), while Sicinius notes that "if things go well, / Opinion that so sticks on Marcius shall / Of his demerits rob Cominius" (001-002). The political machination that begins here will culminate in Act III's impeachment proceedings.

## Dramatic Significance

The Corn Riot is the dramatic seed from which the entire tragedy grows. It introduces every major political tension: the plebeian–patrician conflict, the incompatibility of martial valor with democratic politics, and the tragic irony that the very qualities that make Coriolanus a hero on the battlefield render him unelectable in the forum. The concession of the tribunate — extracted under armed threat — gives the plebeians institutional power they will wield with devastating effect against the man who most opposed their enfranchisement.

## Political Philosophy

Shakespeare draws the Corn Riot from Plutarch's *Life of Coriolanus*, where the grain shortage is attributed to speculative hoarding by the wealthy. Shakespeare eliminates this detail, making the patricians' guilt ambiguous and the citizens' grievance more a matter of perception than proven exploitation. The belly fable itself derives from a classical tradition traceable to Aesop and Livy, but Shakespeare's deployment is ironic: Menenius, for all his rhetorical skill, fails to persuade the citizens, who remain "almost thoroughly persuaded" only of his condescension (001-002).

## Participants

- **Roman Citizens** — the mutinous crowd, armed with staves and clubs, whose demand for affordable grain opens the play. The First and Second Citizens give voice to plebeian grievances with surprising articulateness, countering Menenius's fable point for point.

- **Caius Marcius Coriolanus** — enters mid-scene and immediately alienates the populace with speeches of extraordinary contempt. His first words in the play are an insult, establishing the character's defining failure: an inability to perform the humility that Roman politics requires.

- **Menenius Agrippa** — the patrician mediator who deploys the belly fable. His paternalistic rhetoric reveals the limits of patrician persuasion: he believes the citizens can be managed with stories, not concessions.

- **Sicinius Velutus** — newly created tribune; closes the scene by plotting to deny Marcius credit for the coming Volscian campaign. His first appearance is already adversarial.

- **Junius Brutus** — co-tribune; partners with Sicinius in the scene-closing conspiracy against Marcius's reputation.

## Aftermath and Consequences

The riot's consequences reverberate through every subsequent act. The tribunate created here becomes the institutional weapon that impeaches Coriolanus (Act III), engineers his banishment (Act III–IV), and celebrates his departure. The class hatred that the riot crystallizes ensures that Coriolanus's battlefield heroism at Corioli cannot translate into political capital. When the citizens turn against him in the consular election (Act II–III), they are answering his opening insult — "you curs" (001-002) — with the institutional power the riot extracted.

## Historical Context

Shakespeare's Jacobean audience would have recognized the corn riot as a contemporary anxiety. The 1607 Midlands Rising — a protest against grain enclosure — occurred during the play's likely composition period. The belly fable had circulated in early modern political discourse, appearing in William Camden's *Remains* (1605) and earlier in Livy's account of the first plebeian secession (494 BCE). Shakespeare's innovation was to place the fable in the mouth of a character who is simultaneously the most rhetorically gifted and politically ineffective mediator in the play.
