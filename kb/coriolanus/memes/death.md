## Definition

**Death** pervades *Coriolanus* from its opening lines. The citizens are "resolved rather to die than to famish" (001-002); Coriolanus's martial identity is built on dealing death and risking it; and the play's climax is the hero's slaughter at the hands of Aufidius's conspirators. Death is the play's horizon — the condition against which all value, honour, and political negotiation is measured.

Unlike Shakespeare's other tragedies, *Coriolanus* treats death with a peculiar flatness. There is no ghost, no metaphysical reckoning, no dying poetry. Death is physical, political, and final: a body in the marketplace. The play's materialism extends even to its treatment of mortality.

## Historical Background

Roman culture invested death with meanings Coriolanus inherits. Death in battle (*mors pro patria*) was the supreme expression of *virtus*, and the Roman funeral procession celebrated ancestral achievement as a model for the living. Volumnia's declaration that she would rather have "eleven sons" die nobly than one live without honour (003-002) echoes the Roman matron's ideal. But *Coriolanus* also draws on the Senecan tradition of stoic death — the hero who meets his end with contempt for his killers — and the Machiavellian understanding of political murder as a tool of state.

## In the Play

Death appears first as a threat the citizens wield against the patricians: "Let us kill him" (001-002). The reversal is immediate — the patricians deploy death more effectively, directing the plebeian hunger for violence toward the Volscians. War converts domestic murderousness into patriotic sacrifice.

Coriolanus's relationship to death defines his character. At Corioli, he enters the city alone and is presumed "slain, sir, doubtless" (004-002) — only to emerge alive and bloodied. This near-death and survival is the pattern of his life: he repeatedly transcends mortality in battle, only to be destroyed by the political world that cannot contain his vitality.

Volumnia's maternal ideology is built on death. "Had he died in the business, madam; how then?" Virgilia asks. Volumnia's reply — "Then his good report should have been my son" (003-002) — subordinates bodily life to honour and fame. This is the value system that produces Coriolanus, and the play neither endorses nor condemns it, but shows its consequences.

The play's final death — Coriolanus cut down in the Volscian marketplace — is deliberately anti-climactic. There is no soliloquy, no reconciliation, no tragic recognition. The conspirators "kill, kill, kill, kill, kill him" (016-002) and it is done. The flatness of the death is the point: a political murder, not a tragic apotheosis.
