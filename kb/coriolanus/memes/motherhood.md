# Motherhood

## Definition

In *Coriolanus*, **motherhood** is not a private domestic relation but a **political force**. The relationship between [[volumnia|Volumnia]] and her son [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] is the play's most powerful — and most disturbing — emotional bond. Volumnia has shaped her son into a weapon of Roman military dominance; when that weapon turns against Rome, she is the only person who can disarm it. The play asks whether maternal influence is the foundation of civic virtue or a form of psychological violence that ultimately destroys both mother and child.

> If my son were my husband, I should freelier rejoice in that absence wherein he won honour
> than in the embracements of his bed where he would show most love. (001-003)

Volumnia's opening speech establishes the play's radical inversion of conventional maternal feeling. She loves her son not for himself but for the honour he wins. Blood is less important than *virtus*; the maternal body is a factory for martial glory.

## Historical Background

The Roman *matrona* (matron) occupied a social position with no exact equivalent in Greek or early modern English culture. She could own property, conduct business, appear in public without stigma, and — crucially for *Coriolanus* — exert considerable informal political influence through her male relatives.

The exemplary Roman mother was **Cornelia**, mother of Tiberius and Gaius Gracchus. When a wealthy visitor asked to see her jewels, Cornelia pointed to her sons and said: "These are my jewels." This ideal — the mother who values civic contribution above personal adornment, who measures her worth by her sons' public deeds — is the template for Volumnia. But Shakespeare pushes the ideal to the point of horror: Volumnia's maternal pride is indistinguishable from a death wish.

### The Maternal Ideal in Coriolanus

| Aspect | Cornelian Ideal | Volumnia's Extreme |
|--------|----------------|-------------------|
| Son-rearing | Raise sons for civic service | Raise son as killing machine |
| Wounds | Evidence of service | Counted with erotic pleasure |
| Death | Honourable if for Rome | "I had rather had eleven die nobly... than one voluptuously surfeit" |
| Intervention | Advise for civic good | Coerce through emotional destruction |

## In the Play

### Act 1, Scene 3: The Maternal Philosophy

The play's first domestic scene establishes Volumnia's philosophy of motherhood. While [[virgilia|Virgilia]] — Coriolanus's wife — fears for her husband's safety, Volumnia exults in the thought of him shedding blood:

> The breasts of Hecuba,
> When she did suckle Hector, look'd not lovelier
> Than Hector's forehead when it spit forth blood
> At Grecian sword, contemning. (001-003)

The image is grotesque: a mother comparing breastfeeding — the archetypal act of maternal nurture — to a wound spouting blood. Volumnia's mother-love is indistinguishable from a death cult. She has nursed her son on valour and now expects him to repay that nurture with wounds and renown.

### Act 2, Scene 1: The Wound-Count

When Coriolanus returns from Corioli, Volumnia's first instinct is to count his wounds — and to be disappointed if they are too few:

> O, he is wounded; I thank the gods for't. (002-001)

She later boasts: "He had, before this last expedition, twenty-five wounds upon him" (002-001). The wound-count is her maternal balance sheet — each scar a dividend on her investment. This is the logical endpoint of the Cornelian ideal: if sons are jewels, wounds are their facet-count.

### Act 3, Scene 2: The Political Coercion

When Coriolanus resolves not to humble himself before the people, it is Volumnia — not the tribunes, not the senators — who forces him to comply:

> At thy choice, then:
> To beg of thee, it is my more dishonour
> Than thou of them. Come all to ruin; let
> Thy mother rather feel thy pride than fear
> Thy dangerous stoutness... (003-002)

She threatens to withdraw her maternal approval — the ultimate emotional lever. Coriolanus capitulates: "Mother, I am going to the market-place" (003-002). But his compliance is hollow; he goes through the motions of humility with such transparent contempt that his candidacy collapses. Volumnia's maternal coercion produces not political success but catastrophe.

### Act 5, Scene 3: The Maternal Triumph

The play's climactic scene reverses the power dynamic. Coriolanus, now leading the Volscian army against Rome, is invulnerable to all appeals — until his mother arrives. Volumnia, [[virgilia|Virgilia]], [[young-marcius|Young Marcius]], and [[valeria|Valeria]] form a tableau of Roman womanhood that dissolves Coriolanus's resolve:

> My mother bows;
> As if Olympus to a molehill should
> In supplication nod... (005-003)

Volumnia's long speech (the longest in the play by a female character) deploys the full arsenal of maternal rhetoric: she reminds him that she is "the honour'd mould / Wherein this trunk was framed" (005-003), threatens to "tread... on thy mother's womb, / That brought thee to this world" (005-003), and finally shames him into making peace. She succeeds — but her success kills him. The Volscians, enraged at the aborted conquest, murder Coriolanus.

| Stage | Volumnia's Role | Outcome |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Act 1 | Instils martial ambition | Coriolanus becomes Rome's greatest soldier |
| Act 2 | Celebrates wounds; pushes for consulship | Coriolanus stands for election |
| Act 3 | Coerces humility | Coriolanus's hollow compliance backfires |
| Act 5 | Pleads for Rome | Coriolanus relents — and dies |
