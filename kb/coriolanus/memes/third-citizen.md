# Third Citizen

The **Third Citizen** is a plebeian character in Shakespeare's *[[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]*, appearing in the crucial Forum scene (Act II, Scene III) where [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] must solicit the people's "voices" to become [[consulship|consul]]. Among the citizen group, the Third Citizen is the most intellectually self-conscious — a figure who reflects on the paradoxes of popular sovereignty with sardonic wit.

## The Paradox of Popular Power

When the citizens gather to bestow their votes on [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]], the Third Citizen articulates the central tension of [[rome|Rome]]'s political system with remarkable philosophical clarity:

> We have power in ourselves to do it, but it is a power that we have no power to do; for if he show us his wounds and tell us his deeds, we are to put our tongues into those wounds and speak for them; so, if he tell us his noble deeds, we must also tell him our noble acceptance of them. Ingratitude is monstrous, and for the multitude to be ingrateful, were to make a monster of the multitude; of the which we being members, should bring ourselves to be monstrous members. (015-003)

This speech — arguably the most sophisticated political analysis spoken by any citizen in the play — acknowledges that the people's formal power is constrained by their emotional susceptibility to aristocratic performance.

## Wit and Self-Knowledge

The Third Citizen is distinguished by his capacity for self-mockery. When another citizen suggests their wits are "diversely coloured," he replies that "if all our wits were to issue out of one skull, they would fly east, west, north, south, and their consent of one direct way should be at once to all the points o' the compass" (015-003). He mocks the intellectual pretensions of his fellow citizens while including himself in the critique.

## Encounter with Coriolanus

When [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] enters in the "gown of humility" to request their voices, the Third Citizen observes the absurdity of the ritual with detached irony. When told that the greater part carries the decision, he concedes pragmatically: "I say, if he would incline to the people, there was never a worthier man" (015-003). His final line — "But this is something odd" (015-003) — captures the unease of a thoughtful observer watching an aristocratic warrior perform humility.
