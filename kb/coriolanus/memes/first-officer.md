## Overview

The First Officer is a minor character in *[[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]* who appears briefly in Act II, Scene II alongside the [[second-officer|Second Officer]]. The two officers are tasked with laying cushions in the Capitol ahead of [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s confirmation as [[consulship|consul]] (455). Through their brief exchange, Shakespeare provides a window into how [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] is perceived by ordinary Romans — respected for his military prowess but distrusted for his open contempt of the common people.

## In the Play

The First Officer and [[second-officer|Second Officer]] enter at the beginning of Act II, Scene II to prepare the Capitol for the senatorial session. Their conversation turns immediately to the [[consulship|consul]]ar election (451-452):

> Come, come, they are almost here. How many stand for consulships? (451)

Upon learning that [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] is the expected winner, the First Officer offers a candid assessment: "That's a brave fellow; but he's vengeance proud, and loves not the common people" (453). He then delivers a lengthy critique of [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s character, observing that far from being indifferent to the people's opinion, [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] actively seeks their hatred (455):

> If he did not care whether he had their love or no, he waved indifferently 'twixt doing them neither good nor harm: but he seeks their hate with greater devotion than they can render it him; and leaves nothing undone that may fully discover him their opposite. Now, to seem to affect the malice and displeasure of the people is as bad as that which he dislikes, to flatter them for their love. (455)

After the [[second-officer|Second Officer]] defends [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s achievements, the First Officer cuts the discussion short: "No more of him; he is a worthy man: make way, they are coming" (457). The senators then enter and the scene proceeds.

## Dramatic Function

The First Officer serves as a choric figure whose dialogue with the [[second-officer|Second Officer]] dramatizes the central tension of the play: [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s military greatness versus his political inadequacy. The officers' exchange provides the audience with a ground-level perspective on the aristocratic hero, bridging the gap between the senatorial elite and the common citizens. The First Officer's nuanced critique — acknowledging [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s worth while condemning his pride — mirrors the divided opinion that ultimately leads to [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s downfall.
