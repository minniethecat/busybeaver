# Treaty of Peace

## Event Summary

The Treaty of Peace is the climactic resolution of *Coriolanus*, occupying Acts V Scenes III–VI. Coriolanus, having marched on Rome at the head of a Volscian army, has refused all Roman embassies — Cominius, Menenius, and the senators. His mother Volumnia, his wife Virgilia, his son Young Marcius, and the noblewoman Valeria enter the Volscian camp in mourning habits (026-002). After a prolonged and impassioned appeal, Volumnia prevails. Coriolanus consents to withdraw the Volscian army and negotiate "convenient peace" (027-002). The ladies return to Rome as saviors, greeted by "trumpets, sackbuts, psalteries and fifes" (028-002). However, in Antium, Aufidius denounces Coriolanus's clemency as betrayal — "At a few drops of women's rheum, which are / As cheap as lies, he sold the blood and labour / Of our great action" (029-002) — and orchestrates his murder, ensuring the treaty becomes both Rome's salvation and Coriolanus's death warrant.

## The Embassy (Act V, Scene III)

### The Setting

Coriolanus has just reaffirmed his intransigence. To Aufidius he declares: "This last old man, / Whom with a crack'd heart I have sent to Rome, / Loved me above the measure of a father... for whose old love I have... once more offer'd / The first conditions, which they did refuse / And cannot now accept" (026-002). Menenius has been dismissed, and Coriolanus vows to entertain "fresh embassies and suits, / Nor from the state nor private friends, hereafter / Will I lend ear to" (026-002).

The entry of Volumnia, Virgilia, Young Marcius, and Valeria "in mourning habits" (026-002) immediately destabilizes this resolution. Coriolanus's reaction is visceral: he feels himself dissolving — "I melt, and am not / Of stronger earth than others" (026-002) — and his instinct is to preemptively refuse: "Let it be virtuous to be obstinate" (026-002).

### Volumnia's Speech

Volumnia's appeal is the longest and most rhetorically sophisticated speech by a female character in the play. She deploys multiple registers:

**1. The Logic of Mutual Destruction (027-002)**:
> "How can we for our country pray,
> Whereto we are bound, together with thy victory,
> Whereto we are bound? alack, or we must lose
> The country, our dear nurse, or else thy person,
> Our comfort in the country."

She frames the embassy not as a choice between competing loyalties but as a trap: whichever side wins, she loses. If Rome falls, Coriolanus is a traitor; if Coriolanus falls, Rome loses its greatest son.

**2. The Physical Appeal (027-002)**:
> "If I cannot persuade thee / Rather to show a noble grace to both parts / Than seek the end of one, thou shalt no sooner / March to assault thy country than to tread — / Trust to't, thou shalt not — on thy mother's womb, / That brought thee to this world."

She threatens to place her own body between her son and Rome — a gesture that literalizes the maternal claim as a physical barrier to martial action.

**3. The Honor Argument (027-002)**:
> "Thou know'st, great son, / The end of war's uncertain, but this certain, / That, if thou conquer Rome, the benefit / Which thou shalt thereby reap is such a name, / Whose repetition will be dogg'd with curses."

She offers a counter-narrative of honor: where Coriolanus sees martial glory in destroying Rome, Volumnia sees infamy. The conqueror of Rome will be remembered as "The man was noble, / But with his last attempt he wiped it out" — a warning that his Coriolanus name, won at Corioli, will be consumed by the curse of his country's destruction.

**4. The Negotiated Settlement (027-002)**:
> "Our suit / Is, that you reconcile them: while the Volsces / May say 'This mercy we have show'd;' the Romans, / 'This we received;' and each in either side / Give the all-hail to thee and cry 'Be blest / For making up this peace!'"

Volumnia's diplomatic genius is to frame the peace as a triple victory: Rome is saved, the Volscians appear magnanimous, and Coriolanus earns a new honor — peacemaker — that transcends his martial identity.

### Coriolanus's Surrender

After a long silence — the stage direction notes "He holds her by the hand, silent" (027-002) — Coriolanus breaks:

> "O mother, mother! / What have you done? Behold, the heavens do ope, / The gods look down, and this unnatural scene / They laugh at. O my mother, mother! O! / You have won a happy victory to Rome; / But, for your son — believe it, O, believe it, / Most dangerously you have with him prevail'd, / If not most mortal to him." (027-002)

The repetition of "mother" — four times in five lines — signals the collapse of Coriolanus's self-constructed identity. The warrior who refused to bend to consuls, tribunes, or the Roman people bends to the maternal voice. His concession is freighted with fatalistic clarity: "Most dangerously you have with him prevail'd" — the peace will kill him, and he knows it.

To Aufidius he says: "though I cannot make true wars, / I'll frame convenient peace" (027-002). The word "convenient" — adequate, fitting, but not glorious — captures the diminished register into which his martial identity has collapsed.

## The Aftermath in Rome (Act V, Scenes IV–V)

The news of the peace transforms Rome. The Second Messenger reports: "The ladies have prevail'd, / The Volscians are dislodged, and Marcius gone: / A merrier day did never yet greet Rome, / No, not the expulsion of the Tarquins" (028-002). The comparison to the founding of the Republic — the expulsion of the last kings — elevates Volumnia's diplomatic achievement to constitutional significance.

Menenius's tribute is pointed: "This Volumnia / Is worth of consuls, senators, patricians, / A city full; of tribunes, such as you, / A sea and land full" (028-002). The contrast between Volumnia's successful intercession and the failure of the tribunes (who engineered the banishment that created this crisis) constitutes Shakespeare's final judgment on the Roman political order: maternal persuasion succeeds where political institutions fail.

The entry into Rome (Act V, Scene V) is staged as a triumphal procession: "Behold our patroness, the life of Rome! / Call all your tribes together, praise the gods, / And make triumphant fires; strew flowers before them" (029-002). Volumnia is hailed as "patroness" — a feminized *pater patriae*, the savior of the fatherland — an honor that the Roman constitution had no formal office to bestow.

## The Volscian Reckoning (Act V, Scene VI)

In Antium, Aufidius denounces Coriolanus to the conspirators:

> "At a few drops of women's rheum, which are / As cheap as lies, he sold the blood and labour / Of our great action: therefore shall he die, / And I'll renew me in his fall." (029-002)

Aufidius's language works through radical devaluation: women's tears are "cheap as lies," the Volscian campaign is "sold" for nothing, and Aufidius's own rise requires Coriolanus's death. The peace that saved Rome is, in the Volscian interpretation, an act of treason that must be punished.

When Coriolanus enters, Aufidius publicly accuses him of having "water'd his new plants with dews of flattery" and "bow'd his nature" (029-002). The accusation is that Coriolanus, the man who could not stoop to solicit the plebeian voices, has now compromised his integrity entirely — not for Rome but for his mother. Aufidius calls him "thou boy of tears" — an insult that reduces the warrior to a child weeping at his mother's command. Coriolanus's furious response — "Cut me to pieces, Volsces; men and lads, / Stain all your edges on me" (029-002) — is his final assertion of the martial identity that the peace has destroyed. The Volscian lords and Aufidius kill him, and the treaty dies with its architect.

## Dramatic Significance

The Treaty of Peace is the play's structural pivot from triumph to tragedy. The very act that makes Coriolanus Rome's savior — for the second time — is the act that kills him. Shakespeare constructs an impossible situation: Coriolanus must either destroy his country (becoming a monster) or spare his country through maternal intercession (becoming, in Volscian eyes, a traitor). There is no third option that preserves both his honor and his life.

The gender politics of the scene are radical. The entire Roman political apparatus — consuls, senators, tribunes, citizens — has failed to stop Coriolanus. Three women and a boy succeed where armies could not. Volumnia's speech is not merely persuasive; it is constitutionally consequential, altering the fate of two nations through personal appeal. Shakespeare suggests that the affective bonds of family possess a political power that the formal institutions of the republic cannot match — and that this power, while redemptive, is also fatal.

## Aftermath and Consequences

The immediate consequence is the saving of Rome — "A merrier day did never yet greet Rome" — and the celebratory reception of Volumnia as *patroness*. The delayed consequence, arriving in the very next scene, is Coriolanus's assassination. Aufidius's conspirators, prepared before the peace was known, carry out the murder that the peace enables.

The deeper consequence is political: the peace treaty reveals that Rome's constitutional order is incapable of resolving existential crises through its normal mechanisms. The tribunes who banished Coriolanus cannot recall him; the senators who honored him cannot command him; the citizens who repented their election cannot reverse their choice. Only the extra-constitutional intervention of a mother's love — a force that operates outside the framework of Roman law — can save the state. This is simultaneously the play's most hopeful and most pessimistic insight: love can succeed where politics fails, but its success is inherently tragic.
