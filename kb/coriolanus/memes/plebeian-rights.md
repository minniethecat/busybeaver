# Plebeian Rights

## 概念定义

**Plebeian rights** (*ius plebis*) are the legal and political entitlements of the Roman commoner class. In *Coriolanus*, these rights are not a settled constitutional framework but a contested frontier — asserted by the tribunes, grudgingly acknowledged by the patricians, and invoked by the citizens when their material interests demand it. The play dramatizes the fragility of plebeian rights: every exercise of tribunician authority provokes a patrician backlash, and the rights that the tribunes defend are only as durable as the political coalition that sustains them.

The plebeian rights at issue in the play include the right to elect tribunes, the right to be consulted (however formally) on the appointment of consuls, the right to assembly, and — most viscerally — the right to affordable grain. The First Citizen's opening demand — "we'll have corn at our own price" (001-002) — establishes that plebeian rights are grounded in material survival, not abstract constitutional theory.

## 历史背景

In the early Roman Republic, plebeians were excluded from the Senate, the major priesthoods, and the higher magistracies. They were also subject to *nexum* — debt bondage — which could reduce a free citizen to effective servitude. The Struggle of the Orders was a centuries-long campaign to secure and extend plebeian rights against patrician resistance.

Key plebeian rights won during this struggle:

- **Tribunician power** (*potestas tribunicia*): The right to elect tribunes with veto power and sacrosanct person. This was the plebeians' most important constitutional weapon.

- **Right of appeal** (*provocatio ad populum*): The right of a citizen condemned by a magistrate to appeal to the popular assembly — a fundamental check on patrician magisterial power.

- **Access to law**: The Twelve Tables (451–450 BCE) were published in response to plebeian demands for transparent, written law rather than secret patrician legal knowledge.

- **Access to office**: The *Lex Licinia Sextia* (367 BCE) reserved one consulship for plebeians, breaking the patrician monopoly on executive power.

- **Legislative sovereignty**: The *Lex Hortensia* (287 BCE) made resolutions of the plebeian assembly (*plebiscita*) binding on all Romans without senatorial ratification.

Shakespeare's Rome exists at an indeterminate point in this evolution — the tribunate is well-established, but the patricians behave as though it were a recent and reversible innovation.

## 剧中的体现

### The Demand for Grain (Act 1, Scene 1)

The play opens with a direct assertion of plebeian rights in their most material form. Citizens take up arms because the patricians are hoarding grain while the people starve: "the gods sent not / Corn for the rich men only" (001-002). The First Citizen articulates the plebeian analysis with precision: "Caius Marcius is chief enemy to the people" (001-002). This is not mob irrationality but a coherent political diagnosis — the patrician class, personified by Coriolanus, is withholding the means of subsistence.

Menenius's fable of the belly is the patrician counter-argument: the Senate is not hoarding but stewarding resources, and the body politic functions only when all members accept their subordinate roles. The citizens are not persuaded, and their skepticism is justified — the belly fable is a defence of hierarchy dressed as an organic metaphor.

### The People's Voice (Act 2)

The consular election in Act 2 dramatizes the tension between the form and substance of plebeian political rights. Coriolanus must solicit the citizens' "voices" — a right the plebeians take seriously. The Third Citizen articulates the paradox of their position: "We have power in ourselves to do it, but it is a power that we have no power to do" (013-002). The plebeians possess the formal right to determine who becomes consul, but they lack the social standing to exercise it as genuine political judgment — they are expected to ratify the patrician candidate, not to choose.

When Coriolanus performs his reluctant solicitation and the citizens grant their voices, the tribunes move swiftly to revoke the approval. This revocation — arguably without constitutional precedent — asserts plebeian rights in their strongest form: the right not merely to consent but to withdraw consent upon reflection.

### Rights Under Siege (Acts 3–4)

Coriolanus's response to the tribunes' maneuvers is an open assault on plebeian rights themselves. He argues that the tribunate should be abolished and the plebeians returned to political non-existence: "What would you have, you curs, / That like nor peace nor war?" (014-002). His banishment represents the plebeian assembly exercising its ultimate sanction — a triumphant assertion of popular sovereignty that nonetheless leaves the city vulnerable to Coriolanus's Volscian revenge.

After the banishment, Brutus counsels strategic restraint: "Now we have shown our power, / Let us seem humbler after it is done" (018-002). This acknowledgment that plebeian power is safest when exercised sparingly reveals the structural asymmetry of the Roman constitution — the plebeians can banish a patrician, but they cannot govern without patrician cooperation.

## 与其他概念的关系

- **Tribunate**: The institutional mechanism through which plebeian rights are exercised and defended. Without the tribunate, plebeian rights would have no enforcement mechanism.

- **Class Conflict**: Plebeian rights are the substantive stakes of the class conflict. The patricians resist plebeian rights because they constrain aristocratic prerogative; the plebeians demand them as protection against patrician abuse.

- **Patrician Power**: The structure against which plebeian rights are defined. Every plebeian right is a limitation on what patricians can do unilaterally.

- **Populism**: The political technique by which plebeian rights are either advanced (by the tribunes) or managed and contained (by Menenius and the patricians). Populism is the style; plebeian rights are the substance.

- **Roman Constitution**: The framework that defines, distributes, and balances plebeian and patrician rights. The mixed constitution is the arena in which rights are contested.

## 文学批评视角

The question of how seriously Shakespeare took plebeian rights has divided critics. The conservative tradition — Johnson (1765), Coleridge (1818), T. S. Eliot (1919) — read the play as a warning against popular power, seeing the plebeians as inconstant, manipulable, and unfit for political responsibility. This reading emphasizes the tribunes' cynical manipulation of the citizens and the citizens' rapid changes of allegiance.

More recent criticism has recovered the political intelligence of Shakespeare's plebeians. Annabel Patterson (1989) argued that the citizens' speeches display genuine reasoning about obligation, representation, and the limits of deference — that Shakespeare gave the plebeians a political voice more articulate than the conservative critical tradition has acknowledged. The Third Citizen's meditation on power and ingratitude (013-002) is, in Patterson's reading, a serious contribution to republican political theory, not comic relief.

The Brechtian tradition — from Brecht's own adaptation (1952–53) through Günter Grass's *The Plebeians Rehearse the Uprising* (1966) — has treated plebeian rights as the play's normative centre, arguing that the tragedy lies not in Coriolanus's fall but in the failure of the plebeian movement to develop into genuine democratic self-governance. In this reading, the tribunes are not demagogues but imperfect representatives of a legitimate political movement, and Shakespeare's ambivalence reflects the contradictions of early modern republicanism itself.

## The Tribunician Constitution

The plebeian rights at issue in *Coriolanus* are the constitutional privileges of the Roman *plebs*: the right to elect tribunes, the right of tribunes to veto senatorial and consular actions, and the right to have popular assemblies (*concilia plebis*) whose resolutions carry the force of law. These rights are recent and contested — the patricians regard them as usurpations, while the tribunes Sicinius and Brutus defend them with a mix of procedural rigor and political cunning. The play dramatises the collision between these popular constitutional mechanisms and the aristocratic honour-code represented by Coriolanus, who refuses to recognise any authority that does not flow from martial virtue. The resulting impasse — neither side willing or able to accommodate the other — is the structural engine of the tragedy.
