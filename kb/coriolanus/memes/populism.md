# Populism

## 概念定义

**Populism** in *Coriolanus* is the political technique of appealing to, mobilizing, and manipulating the common people (*populus*) as a political force. Unlike the modern pejorative sense of the term, populism in Shakespeare's Rome is not inherently illegitimate — it is a structural feature of the mixed constitution, which gives the plebeian assembly genuine political powers and therefore makes popular support a rational object of political competition.

Shakespeare dramatizes populism in several distinct registers. The tribunes Sicinius and Brutus practice a strategic populism, mobilizing the citizens against Coriolanus with procedural skill and rhetorical finesse. Coriolanus himself practices an anti-populist populism — his refusal to flatter the people is itself a form of political performance, authenticating his aristocratic identity precisely by repudiating popular appeal. Menenius occupies a middle ground, practicing a paternalist populism that acknowledges the people's material grievances while defending patrician dominance. Each mode of populism succeeds and fails on its own terms, and the play refuses to endorse any single relationship between elite and popular politics.

## 历史背景

The Roman Republic was structurally populist in the sense that popular assemblies (the *comitia centuriata*, *comitia tributa*, and *concilium plebis*) held real legislative, electoral, and judicial powers. No magistrate could be elected without popular votes; no law could be enacted without popular approval. The tension between the aristocratic Senate (*auctoritas patrum*) and the popular assemblies defined Roman political culture.

The *populares* were politicians who advanced their careers by championing popular causes — land reform, grain distribution, debt relief — against the *optimates*, who defended senatorial prerogative and the traditional privileges of the elite. The Gracchi brothers (Tiberius and Gaius, 2nd century BCE) were the archetypal *populares*, and their violent deaths demonstrated the life-or-death stakes of Roman populist politics.

Shakespeare's Coriolanus is set in an earlier period (5th century BCE), but the play's political dynamics reflect the late Republic's populist-optimate conflicts, filtered through Shakespeare's early modern English context. The tribunes of *Coriolanus* are recognizable *populares* — politicians whose power base is the plebeian assembly and whose political style is direct appeal to popular grievances.

## 剧中的体现

### Menenius and the Belly Fable (Act 1, Scene 1)

The play's first populist performance comes from a patrician. Menenius Agrippa defuses a food riot with the fable of the belly — an organic metaphor in which the Senate is the belly that distributes nourishment to all bodily members (001-002). The fable is populist in form (a story the people can understand, delivered with apparent goodwill) and anti-populist in content (a defence of patrician supremacy dressed as natural order).

The citizens are not fooled: "It was an answer" (001-002). But the fable works — not because it persuades but because it defuses tension long enough for Coriolanus and the military to arrive. Menenius's paternalist populism is effective as containment, not as persuasion.

### The Tribunes' Mobilization (Acts 2–3)

Sicinius and Brutus practice a more sophisticated — and more cynical — mode of populism. They do not address the citizens with patrician condescension but with strategic empathy. When Coriolanus seeks the consulship, the tribunes orchestrate the citizens' refusal in terms that respect their political agency: "Have you a catalogue / Of all the voices that we have procured / Set down by the poll?" (016-002). This is not mob manipulation but electoral organization — the tribunes are doing politics in a republic where votes matter.

The tribunes' populism is effective because it treats the citizens as political actors with legitimate interests. Their cynicism — "Now we have shown our power, / Let us seem humbler after it is done" (018-002) — does not distinguish them from modern political operatives; it is the professional realism of anyone who works with electoral majorities.

### Coriolanus's Anti-Populism (Acts 2–3)

Coriolanus's refusal to perform populism is the most spectacular populist gesture in the play. His insistence on authenticity — "I will not do't" (013-002) — is itself a political performance, demonstrating his aristocratic integrity through the explicit repudiation of popular appeal. The irony is that this anti-populism succeeds with the very people it despises: the citizens initially grant their voices because Coriolanus's wounds speak more eloquently than any flattery could.

But Coriolanus cannot sustain even this negative populism. His public explosion against the tribunes — "You common cry of curs!" (014-002) — shatters the compact. Anti-populist authenticity becomes self-destructive when it crosses into active contempt for the people whose consent is constitutionally required.

### The Volscian Popularity (Acts 4–5)

Coriolanus's exile reveals that populism is not merely a Roman pathology. Among the Volscians, his martial reputation generates spontaneous popular adulation: the soldiers "use him as the grace 'fore meat" (023-002). This popularity among Rome's enemies is both a tribute to Coriolanus's *virtus* and a warning — popularity is transferable, volatile, and ultimately dangerous. Aufidius's jealousy of Coriolanus's Volscian popularity becomes the proximate cause of his death.

## 与其他概念的关系

- **Tribunate**: The institutional platform for populist politics. The tribunes are populists by constitutional design — their office exists to represent popular interests against patrician power.

- **Class Conflict**: Populism is the rhetorical mode through which class conflict is conducted. When the tribunes mobilize the citizens, they speak the language of class grievance; when Menenius tells the belly fable, he speaks the language of class reconciliation.

- **Plebeian Rights**: The substantive content of populist appeals. Populism that advances plebeian rights is democratically constructive; populism that merely manipulates plebeian sentiment is demagogic.

- **Patrician Power**: The institutional reality that populism challenges or reinforces. Menenius's populism reinforces patrician power; the tribunes' populism challenges it.

- **Rhetoric**: Populism is fundamentally a rhetorical practice — it is about *how* one speaks to the people, not merely what one says. Coriolanus's tragedy is in part a failure of rhetoric: he cannot speak the language the constitution requires.

## 文学批评视角

Shakespeare's treatment of populism has been the subject of intense critical scrutiny. The conservative reading — most influentially articulated by T. S. Eliot (1919) in "Hamlet and His Problems" but extended to *Coriolanus* by later critics — sees the play as an anti-democratic warning about the dangers of popular politics. The tribunes are demagogues, the citizens are fickle, and Coriolanus's aristocratic authenticity, however politically disastrous, is morally superior to the tribunes' strategic insincerity.

The historicist counter-reading — associated with the work of Annabel Patterson (1989), Andrew Hadfield (2003), and others — situates the play within early modern English debates about Parliament, popular representation, and the limits of monarchical authority. On this reading, Shakespeare does not endorse anti-populism but anatomizes it, showing how Coriolanus's contempt for popular politics makes him politically unintelligible and personally tragic.

Bertolt Brecht's adaptation *Coriolan* (1952–53) radicalized the play's populist potential by making the plebeians' political decisions collective, deliberative, and strategically rational. Brecht's tribunes are less cynical operatives than flawed representatives of a legitimate popular movement — a reinterpretation that brings to the surface tensions latent in Shakespeare's text. The Brechtian reading suggests that populism in *Coriolanus* is not a pathology to be cured but a political reality to be managed, and that the tragedy lies in the failure of all parties to manage it successfully.
