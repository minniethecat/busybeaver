## Definition

**Power** in *Coriolanus* is the central political question: by what right does any person or class govern? The play presents two irreconcilable models — aristocratic power grounded in martial service and natural superiority, and popular power grounded in numbers and constitutional representation. Neither model is presented as wholly legitimate; the tragedy lies in their mutual incomprehension.

Shakespeare does not take sides cleanly. The patrician claim to power through service is real (Coriolanus has saved Rome), but its contempt for the people is politically unsustainable. The popular claim through the tribunate is constitutionally valid but manipulable (Sicinius and Brutus are skilled demagogues). Power, in this play, is never innocent.

## Historical Background

The Roman Republic's "Struggle of the Orders" — the centuries-long conflict between patricians and plebeians — culminated in 494 BCE with the creation of the tribunate, a plebeian office with the power to veto patrician magistrates. Shakespeare's play draws on Plutarch's account of this period, when the tribunate was newly established and its constitutional limits were still contested. The tribunes' power to "speak against the consuls" was both a democratic innovation and, from the patrician perspective, a destabilising intrusion.

## In the Play

The conflict over power opens the play. The First Citizen's complaint — "What authority surfeits on would relieve us" (001-002) — frames the patrician monopoly on grain as an abuse of power. Menenius's belly fable is an argument for patrician power as organic and benevolent, but the citizens remain unconvinced.

Power shifts decisively with the creation of the tribunate. Sicinius and Brutus wield their office with tactical brilliance, using Coriolanus's pride as a weapon. Brutus acknowledges the reality: power can "go sleep" during Coriolanus's ascendancy (011-005), but "our authorities" (011-005) will reassert themselves when the hero stumbles.

Coriolanus's own understanding of power is martial and absolute. He cannot grasp a world in which "voices" have political weight equal to wounds. His refusal to perform the rituals of power — displaying his scars, speaking softly — is both principled and suicidal. "I banish you!" (016-002) is the cry of a man who believes power inheres in the person, not the office.

Aufidius offers a third model: power as pragmatic alliance. He welcomes the exiled Coriolanus not out of admiration but because "some parcels of their power are forth already" (002-002) — Rome is weakened, and Coriolanus is a weapon. When that weapon outshines him, Aufidius destroys it.
