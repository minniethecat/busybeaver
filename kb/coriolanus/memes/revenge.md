# Revenge

## Definition

**Revenge** in *Coriolanus* is the force that converts political defeat into existential threat. After his banishment, Coriolanus does not retire into obscurity nor accept defeat — he seeks revenge, offering his military services to Rome's enemies and leading a Volscian army to the city's gates. The revenge plot is both psychologically credible and structurally necessary: only the threat of total destruction can produce the conditions for the play's culminating scene of familial intervention. Unlike the revenge protagonists of Elizabethan and Jacobean tragedy — Hamlet, Hieronimo, Vindice — Coriolanus pursues his revenge without hesitation, doubt, or metaphysical anxiety, making his eventual capitulation to Volumnia all the more devastating.

## In the Play

The language of revenge intensifies progressively across the play's five acts. Early references are primarily associated with the plebeians — the First Citizen calls for revenge against the patricians with pikes (001-002), and the mutinous tone of the opening scene presages a cycle of retributive violence. After Coriolanus's banishment, however, revenge becomes the hero's defining motivation. In his soliloquy upon leaving Rome (020-001), he inverts the city's sentence: "I banish you," he declares, collapsing the distance between exile and revenge into a single performative utterance.

When Coriolanus reaches Antium, he presents himself to Aufidius with startling directness: he comes "in mere spite, / To be full quit of those my banishers" (021-002). The word "spite" strips revenge of any political justification — this is not a grievance to be adjudicated but a wound to be repaid. Aufidius, recognizing the military asset Coriolanus represents, grants him "the leading of thine own revenges" (021-002), and the personal vendetta of a single exiled aristocrat becomes, through this compact, a matter of state.

The revenge reaches its peak intensity in the first scenes of Act V. Coriolanus describes his planned assault on Rome as "revenge as spacious as between / The young'st and oldest thing" (022-001) — a cosmic scale that suggests revenge has expanded beyond the political into the existential. Menenius's embassy fails precisely because he attempts to reason with a man who has become pure instrument of retribution: "I'll not to Rome," Coriolanus tells him, "I'll back with you" — a refusal disguised as a dismissal (025-001).

The turning point arrives in the great scene of Volumnia's intercession (026-001). Even as he yields, Coriolanus frames his capitulation in the language of revenge foregone: "sweet as my revenge" is the kiss he imagines from his wife. The simile is devastating — the sweetness of reconciliation is measured against, and found no sweeter than, the sweetness of vengeance. He has chosen not to destroy Rome, but the choice costs him everything: Aufidius, who had granted him his revenge, now claims it back as his own.

## Dramatic Significance

Revenge is what makes the play's resolution possible and tragic. Without Coriolanus's thirst for vengeance, there would be no Volscian invasion; without the invasion, no embassy of the women; without the embassy, no tragic recognition. But revenge is also what makes Coriolanus's death inevitable — Aufidius cannot forgive being denied the revenge he had himself enabled. The cycle of revenge that begins with Rome's "ingratitude" ends with Coriolanus's murder, and the play closes in the grim irony that Aufidius, having killed his rival, immediately acknowledges his nobility. Revenge achieved is revenge hollowed out.
