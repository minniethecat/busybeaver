# The Market Place

## Location Overview

The Market Place is the space of plebeian Rome — where citizens gather, grain is distributed (or withheld), and the material grievances that drive the class conflict are most visible. It is the counterpart to the Senate House: popular commerce versus aristocratic deliberation. Throughout the play, characters move to and from the market-place as the arena where popular consent must be performed: after the battle at Corioli, Lartius commands "Go sound thy trumpet in the market-place; Call thither all the officers o' the town" (005-002).

## Dramatic Function

The market-place is the stage of popular politics — the space where Coriolanus's contempt for the people is most visibly tested. Brutus recalls how Coriolanus swore "never would he Appear i' the market-place nor on him put The napless vesture of humility" (011-002), marking the market-place as the site where aristocratic identity must submit to popular ritual. When the tribunes demand Coriolanus present himself there, the cry "he shall to the market-place" (014-002) and "Meet on the market-place" (014-002) transforms the location into a political battleground. Cominius warns Coriolanus after witnessing the mood there: "I have been i' the market-place; and, sir, 'tis fit You make strong party" (015-002), while Coriolanus himself, in anguished resignation, repeatedly announces "I am going to the market-place" (015-002). Even in the final act, the market-place remains the space of public judgment, as Aufidius summons the Volscian lords: "Bid them repair to the market-place; where I... Will vouch the truth of it" (029-002).

## Scenes Set Here

- **act-1-scene-1**: Citizens gather in the market-place to protest the corn shortage.
- **act-2-scene-1**: Brutus reports Coriolanus's refusal to appear in the market-place to seek the people's voices (011-002).
- **act-2-scene-3**: The popular assembly gathers; Coriolanus is directed to "Repair to the senate-house" after receiving the people's endorsement (013-002).
