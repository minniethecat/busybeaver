# Patricians

## Definition


### Analysis

The **Patricians** (*patricii*) were the hereditary aristocratic order of [[rome|Republican Rome]] — the landowning families who monopolized political office, religious authority, and military command in the early Republic. In Shakespeare's *Coriolanus*, the patricians are the governing class whose [[military-honor|martial values]] and political dominance are challenged by the [[plebeian-rights|plebeian movement]] and its [[tribunate|tribunician]] representatives.

The patrician order provides the play's normative framework: its members — [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]], [[menenius-agrippa|Menenius]], [[cominius|Cominius]], [[titus-lartius|Titus Lartius]] — share a common language of honour, service, and class solidarity. But Shakespeare exposes the fault lines within this order: the wisdom of Menenius versus the absolutism of Coriolanus, the political pragmatism of Cominius versus the martial purity of his greatest soldier.

## Key Characteristics

| Trait | Expression in the Play | Exemplary Figure |
|-------|----------------------|-----------------|
| **Martial nobility** | Honour measured in wounds and victories | [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] — twenty-seven wounds |
| **Political authority** | Control of the [[the-senate-house|Senate]] and consulship | [[cominius|Cominius]] — consul and commander |
| **Rhetorical skill** | Persuasion as mode of governance | [[menenius-agrippa|Menenius]] — the belly fable (001-002) |
| **Class solidarity** | Mutual defence against plebeian challenges | Patrician support of Coriolanus during his trial |
| **Contempt for the people** | Open disdain for plebeian political participation | Coriolanus — "You common cry of curs!" (016-002) |

## Dramatic Function

The patricians serve multiple dramatic functions in the play:

1. **Normative centre**: They represent the established order against which all political action is measured. Even when the patricians are wrong, the play takes their values seriously.

2. **Internal spectrum**: The patrician order is not monolithic. [[menenius-agrippa|Menenius]] represents patrician *wisdom* — the capacity for political accommodation. [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] represents patrician *purity* — the refusal to compromise. The tension between these two modes structures the play's political argument.

3. **Tragic enablers**: The patricians' unwillingness to check Coriolanus's arrogance contributes to his destruction. Their class solidarity, admirable in itself, becomes a form of enabling.

> The patricians of Coriolanus are not villains — they are guardians of a value system that has become politically unsustainable.

## Patrician Rhetoric: The Art of Governing Without Conceding

The patricians govern through a distinctive rhetorical mode that combines paternalistic authority with strategic evasion. Menenius's belly fable is the paradigmatic example. When the [[corn-riot|hungry citizens]] demand grain, Menenius responds not with concessions but with an allegory that recasts political hierarchy as bodily necessity: the Senate is the belly, distributing nourishment to the limbs that resent its apparent idleness [001-002]. The fable's rhetorical genius is that it acknowledges the plebeians' bitterness while foreclosing the possibility of change — rebellion against the belly is rebellion against one's own survival.

This pattern recurs throughout the play. When Coriolanus must display his wounds to the people to secure the consulship, the patricians frame the ritual as a necessary concession to irrational custom rather than a legitimate element of republican procedure. "It is a part / That I shall blush in acting," Coriolanus protests, and the patricians' response is not to defend the custom but to commiserate with his embarrassment [013-001]. They treat popular participation as a distasteful formality — a posture that fatally undermines their ability to maintain even the appearance of republican legitimacy.

The patrician rhetorical toolkit includes three characteristic moves:
- **Allegorization**: Translating specific economic grievances (hunger) into abstract political principles (order, hierarchy), as in Menenius's fable [001-002]
- **Heroic exceptionalism**: Citing individual martial achievements (Coriolanus at [[corioli]]) as evidence that the patrician system as a whole is just
- **Catastrophizing**: Framing any concession to the plebeians as the end of social order — "This double worship, / Where one part does disdain with cause, the other / Insult without all reason" [012-001]

The tragedy of patrician rhetoric is that it works to *delay* conflict without ever *resolving* it. The belly fable persuades the citizens to disperse — but they return in the next scene with the same grievances, now institutionalized through the [[tribunate]].

## Relationship to the Plebeians

The patrician-plebeian conflict is the engine of the play's political drama. The [[patrician-power|patrician order's]] monopoly on political and military authority is challenged by the [[plebeian-rights|plebeian movement]], institutionalized in the [[tribunate|tribunate]] and embodied by the tribunes [[sicinius-velutus|Sicinius]] and [[junius-brutus|Brutus]].

| Aspect | Patrician Position | Plebeian Position |
|--------|-------------------|-------------------|
| **Political legitimacy** | Birth, lineage, martial service | Popular election, numbers |
| **Economic power** | Land ownership, control of grain supply | Labour, military service in the ranks |
| **Religious authority** | Control of priesthoods and auspices | Excluded from sacred offices |
| **Constitutional form** | Senatorial oligarchy | Mixed republic with tribunician veto |

This conflict is not merely political — it is existential. The patricians believe their rule is natural and necessary; the plebeians believe their subjection is artificial and unjust. The play offers no resolution to this contradiction.

## Historical Background

The historical patrician order emerged in the earliest period of the Roman Republic (traditionally founded 509 BCE). The *patres* ("fathers") were the heads of Rome's founding families, and their descendants — the *patricii* — formed a closed hereditary caste that controlled the Senate, the magistracies, and the major priesthoods.

The Conflict of the Orders (*Secessio Plebis*) spanned the 5th to 3rd centuries BCE and resulted in the gradual erosion of patrician exclusivity. The creation of the [[tribunate|Tribunate of the Plebs]] (494 BCE) was the first major concession, followed by the publication of the Twelve Tables (c. 450 BCE), the legalization of patrician-plebeian intermarriage (445 BCE), and eventually plebeian access to the consulship (367 BCE).

Shakespeare compresses this centuries-long conflict into the dramatic frame of a single crisis — the career and destruction of Coriolanus.
