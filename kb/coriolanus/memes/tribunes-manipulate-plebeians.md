# Tribunes Manipulate the Plebeians

## Event Summary

The manipulation of the plebeians by the tribunes Sicinius Velutus and Junius Brutus is a sustained political operation spanning Acts II and III of *Coriolanus*. Recognizing that Coriolanus's pride makes him vulnerable to popular wrath, the tribunes orchestrate a campaign to turn the citizens against the newly proclaimed consul. Their methods include strategic rumor-mongering, procedural exploitation of the "voice" ritual, and the engineering of a public confrontation designed to provoke Coriolanus into self-destructive rage. The campaign culminates in the Forum trial where the tribunes successfully secure Coriolanus's banishment.

> In this point charge him home, that he affects / Tyrannical power: if he evade us there, / Enforce him with his envy to the people.
> — Brutus, Act III, Scene III

## The Tribunes' Motive

The tribunes' hostility toward Coriolanus has both personal and political roots. From their first appearance in Act II, Scene I, they express resentment at Coriolanus's contempt for their office and for the people they represent. When news arrives of Coriolanus's triumph, the tribunes are notably silent — their private conversation reveals their fear that Coriolanus's popularity will diminish their own power.

Menenius recognizes their scheming nature immediately. When they offer guarded support for Coriolanus's consulship (Act II, Scene II), Menenius cuts Brutus off with "That's off, that's off" — an instinctive recognition that the tribunes' caution is veiled hostility.

## Phase One: Undermining the Voice Ritual

The tribunes' first move is to exploit the constitutional requirement that a consul must seek the people's voices in the Forum. In Act II, Scene III, after Coriolanus has reluctantly performed the ritual and secured the voices, Sicinius and Brutus immediately work to reverse the decision.

> He has it now; and by his looks, methinks, / 'Tis warm at his heart.
> — Brutus, observing Coriolanus's resentment after the gown-of-humility, Act II, Scene III

The tribunes intercept the citizens after Coriolanus departs and interrogate them about how Coriolanus sought their voices. They plant doubt: "Did you perceive He did solicit you in free contempt When he did need your loves?" When the citizens admit Coriolanus "mocked" them, the tribunes seize on this, characterizing Coriolanus's reluctance as treasonous contempt for the people's authority.

They then instruct the citizens to reassemble and revoke their votes:

> Thus we debase / The nature of our seats and make the rabble / Call our cares trifles ... Bid them repair to the market-place; where I, / Even in theirs and in the commons' ears, / Will vouch the truth of it.
> — Sicinius, Act II, Scene III

## Phase Two: Engineering Confrontation

In Act III, Scene I, the tribunes escalate. They spread word among the citizens that Coriolanus intends to abolish the tribunate — a claim that plays on plebeian fears of patrician tyranny. When Coriolanus, returning from the Senate, encounters the tribunes in the street, Brutus directly accuses him: "you are like to do such business" — implying Coriolanus will dismantle the people's institutions.

> I say unto you, what he hath done famously, he did it to that end: though soft-conscienced men can be content to say it was for his country, he did it to please his mother and to be partly proud.
> — Brutus inciting the citizens, Act II, Scene III

The provocation works. Coriolanus explodes in rage, denouncing the tribunes and the people in a tirade that includes the infamous line "You common cry of curs! whose breath I hate As reek o' the rotten fens." The tribunes have achieved their goal: Coriolanus has publicly displayed the very contempt for the people that they accused him of harboring.

## Phase Three: The Forum Trial

In Act III, Scene III, the tribunes stage-manage Coriolanus's public trial. Brutus outlines their strategy:

> In this point charge him home, that he affects / Tyrannical power: if he evade us there, / Enforce him with his envy to the people, / And that the spoil got on the Antiates / Was ne'er distributed.
> — Brutus, Act III, Scene III

The charges are layered: first, the accusation of tyrannical ambition; if that fails, the charge of personal malice toward the people; and as a last resort, the false accusation that Coriolanus withheld war spoils. Sicinius instructs the Aedile to assemble the people and orchestrate the crowd's response:

> Assemble presently the people hither; / And when they hear me say 'It shall be so / I' the right and strength o' the commons,' be it either / For death, for fine, or banishment, then let them / If I say fine, cry 'Fine;' if death, cry 'Death.'
> — Sicinius, Act III, Scene III

The trial proceeds precisely as scripted. Each accusation provokes Coriolanus to further rage, and the tribunes use his outbursts as evidence of the very charges they have leveled. When Sicinius pronounces the sentence — banishment — the people cry "It shall be so," having been coached to echo the tribune's words.

## Aftermath

The tribunes' manipulation succeeds completely: Coriolanus is banished from Rome. In Act IV, Scene II, Sicinius and Brutus exult privately. They instruct the Aedile to dismiss the people — "Bid them all home; he's gone, and we'll no further" — confident that their enemy is eliminated. Yet their victory is hollow: within scenes, a Roman and a Volsce meet on the highway and exchange the intelligence that Coriolanus has joined the Volscian army, and is now marching on Rome.

## Dramatic Function

The tribunes' manipulation of the plebeians represents Shakespeare's critique of populist politics. Sicinius and Brutus are not genuinely advocates for the people — they weaponize popular institutions for personal political ends. Their methods — strategic disinformation, procedural exploitation, engineered confrontation — resonate as a timeless study in political manipulation. The tragedy lies in the fact that the tribunes, through their cynicism, destroy the very bridge between patricians and plebeians that the Roman constitution was designed to maintain.
