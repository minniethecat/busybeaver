# The Gates of Rome

## Location Overview

The Gates of Rome in Act 5 are the liminal space where Coriolanus must decide between martial vengeance and familial love. The walls that once defined his identity as Rome's defender now mark him as Rome's enemy. Menenius, rejected by the sentinels, laments being "blown out of your gates with sighs" (025-002), while Coriolanus himself declares that his ears "against your suits are stronger than Your gates against my force" (025-002), making the gates a metaphor for his own hardened resolve.

## Dramatic Function

The gates function as the ultimate spatial boundary of the play's political conflict. Coriolanus announces to Aufidius that "We will before the walls of Rome to-morrow Set down our host" (026-002), positioning the army at the very threshold of the city. After the women's intervention succeeds, the triumphant return through the gates signals the play's climactic reversal, and Coriolanus later reports to the Volscian lords that he "With bloody passage led your wars even to The gates of Rome" (029-002), claiming the spatial penetration of Rome's boundary as his crowning martial achievement — even as it proves to be the prelude to his death.

## Scenes Set Here

- **act-5-scene-3**: Coriolanus and the Volscian army encamp before the walls of Rome; Volumnia, Virgilia, and Young Marcius come to plead (026-002).
- **act-5-scene-5**: The triumphant return through the gates after the women's successful intervention.
