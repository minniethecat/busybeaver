# The Tarpeian Rock

## Location Overview

The Tarpeian Rock is Rome's place of execution for traitors, invoked by Coriolanus as a rhetorical image of defiance: he would sooner be thrown from the rock than yield to the tribunes' demands (015-002). Sicinius first orders Coriolanus to be seized and "Bear him to the rock Tarpeian, and from thence Into destruction cast him" (014-002), and later formally decrees the sentence: "He shall be thrown down the Tarpeian rock With rigorous hands" (014-002). After banishment, Coriolanus himself dares the tribunes to "pronounce the steep Tarpeian death" — declaring that he would not buy their mercy "at the price of one fair word" (016-002).

## Dramatic Function

The Tarpeian Rock functions as more than a threatened punishment — it is the symbolic hinge between Coriolanus's defiance and his banishment. The tribunes wield it as the ultimate sanction of popular authority (014-002), while Coriolanus appropriates it as a measure of his own intransigence, claiming he would endure "ten hills on the Tarpeian rock" rather than flatter the people (015-002). His banishment decree is framed in parallel terms: "In peril of precipitation From off the rock Tarpeian never more To enter our Rome gates" (016-002), so that the rock becomes both the threatened punishment and the rhetorical threshold he must cross into exile.

## Scenes Set Here

- **act-3-scene-1**: Sicinius orders Coriolanus seized and taken to the Tarpeian Rock (014-002).
- **act-3-scene-2**: Coriolanus declares he would endure the Tarpeian Rock rather than humble himself before the people (015-002).
