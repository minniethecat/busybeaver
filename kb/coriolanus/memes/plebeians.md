# Plebeians

## Definition

The **Plebeians** (*plebs*) were the common people of [[rome|Republican Rome]] — the labourers, artisans, small farmers, and urban poor who constituted the vast majority of the population but were excluded from political power, religious office, and the highest magistracies. In Shakespeare's *Coriolanus*, the plebeians are both the victims of [[patricians|patrician]] contempt and the wielders of a new kind of political power — the collective voice that can make or unmake consuls.

Shakespeare's representation of the plebeians has been among the most contested aspects of the play. Critics have seen them variously as a dangerous mob, a legitimate political constituency, and — in [[class-conflict|Marxist readings]] — as the true protagonists of a class tragedy whose aristocratic hero is merely the most visible symptom of structural antagonism.

> What's the matter, you dissentious rogues,
> That, rubbing the poor itch of your opinion,
> Make yourselves scabs? (001-001) — Coriolanus's first words, addressed to the plebeian citizens

## Key Characteristics

| Trait | Expression in the Play | Significance |
|-------|----------------------|-------------|
| **Economic vulnerability** | The [[corn-riot|corn riot]] (I.i) — hunger as political motivation | Material conditions drive political action |
| **Collective identity** | "The people" as a mass character | Plebeian power is numerical, not individual |
| **Political volatility** | Approve Coriolanus's consulship, then revoke it | The people are persuadable in both directions |
| **Tribunician representation** | [[sicinius-velutus|Sicinius]] and [[junius-brutus|Brutus]] as advocates | Institutional voice compensates for individual powerlessness |
| **Malleability** | Easily swayed by rhetoric | Both Menenius's fable and the tribunes' provocations work |

## The Corn Riot: Plebeian Agency

### The Grievance

The play opens with the [[corn-riot|plebeian corn riot]] — a direct action against patrician hoarding of grain during famine. This opening establishes the plebeians not as passive sufferers but as political actors capable of collective mobilization:

> First Citizen: "We are accounted poor citizens, the patricians good. What authority surfeits on would relieve us." (001-001)

The riot frames the entire political drama. The plebeians' grievances are materially real — they are hungry — and constitutionally legitimate — they seek representation through the [[tribunate|tribunate]]. But their methods (armed uprising) and their volatility (easily swayed by rhetoric) make them a politically unstable force.

## Plebeians as Chorus

Shakespeare gives the plebeians a choral function unusual in his Roman plays. Unlike the nameless crowds of *Julius Caesar*, the plebeians in *Coriolanus* speak, argue among themselves, change their minds, and reflect on their own actions:

> Third Citizen: "We have power in ourselves to do it, but it is a power that we have no power to do." (013-001)

This self-awareness — the recognition that their power is real but constrained — gives the plebeians a tragic dimension. They are not simply a mob; they are a political body struggling toward self-consciousness.

## The Tribunes and Plebeian Representation

### The Tribunes as Strategists

The relationship between the plebeians and their tribunes — [[sicinius-velutus|Sicinius]] and [[junius-brutus|Brutus]] — is the play's most complex political dynamic. The tribunes are the institutional embodiment of plebeian power, created by the [[first-secession]] and constitutionally empowered to veto patrician actions. But Shakespeare consistently problematizes the question of whether the tribunes truly *represent* the plebeians or *manipulate* them.

The tribunes' strategy throughout the play depends on managing plebeian opinion. When Coriolanus stands for the consulship, the tribunes do not openly oppose him — they coach the citizens to withdraw their voices: "Say you ne'er had done't — / Hang ye! — but that you put your voices to 's, / And that you gave it him against your will" [013-001]. The tribunes thus exploit the very plebeian malleability that the patricians despise, turning it from a weakness into a political weapon.

This dynamic creates an uncomfortable ambiguity: the tribunes' manipulation of the people is both cynical (they use the plebeians as instruments) and necessary (without such management, the plebeians would be politically helpless against patrician power). The tribunes operate in the space between authentic representation and strategic manipulation — a space that defines much of modern democratic politics.

### The Paradox of Representation

The plebeians themselves are not entirely passive in this relationship. The Third Citizen's famous line — "We have power in ourselves to do it, but it is a power that we have no power to do" [013-001] — expresses a sophisticated understanding of their own political situation. They know they have power (numbers, votes), but they also know that power is only effective when organized and directed. This acknowledgment of dependency on representation, combined with awareness of its dangers, is perhaps the play's most prescient political insight.

After Coriolanus's banishment, the plebeians celebrate in the streets, but their triumph is tinged with dramatic irony: the man they have expelled will return at the head of a Volscian army. The tribunes, having achieved their political objective, prove incapable of managing the military consequences — a failure that exposes the limits of purely political power in a world where ultimate authority still rests on martial force.

## The Plebeian Voice: Individual Citizens

Shakespeare individuates the plebeians through the First, Second, and Third Citizens, who serve as representative voices of the common people:

| Citizen | Perspective | Key Line |
|---------|------------|---------|
| **First Citizen** | The activist — "resolv'd rather to die than to famish" | Leads the corn riot, voices the case against Coriolanus |
| **Second Citizen** | The moderate — swayed by Menenius's fable | "I will lean to the commonwealth" (accepts mediation) |
| **Third Citizen** | The skeptic — aware of his own powerlessness | "A power that we have no power to do" — political self-awareness |

These three voices create a miniature political community within the play, capable of deliberation, persuasion, and collective decision — however imperfect.

## Historical Background

The historical plebeian order emerged alongside the [[patricians|patrician order]] in early Rome. The *plebs* were free citizens who served in the army and contributed to the economy but were excluded from the patrician monopoly on political and religious authority.

The Conflict of the Orders produced a series of plebeian victories:
- **494 BCE**: [[first-secession|First Secession]] — creation of the [[tribunate|Tribunate]]
- **c. 450 BCE**: Twelve Tables — written law accessible to all
- **445 BCE**: *Lex Canuleia* — legalization of intermarriage
- **367 BCE**: *Leges Liciniae Sextiae* — plebeian access to the consulship
- **287 BCE**: *Lex Hortensia* — plebiscites binding on all Romans

Shakespeare's play is set early in this trajectory, when the tribunate was still new and its powers still contested.

## Critical Perspectives

| School | Reading |
|--------|---------|
| **Traditional (Bradley)** | The plebeians as a mob — fickle, easily manipulated, undeserving of Coriolanus's respect |
| **Marxist (Brecht)** | The plebeians as the authentic political subject — their grievances are real, their methods rational |
| **New Historicist** | The plebeians as a figure for the early modern English commons — Shakespeare's Rome as a screen for contemporary class tensions |
| **Democratic** | The plebeians as proto-democratic actors — the play as a meditation on the possibilities and dangers of popular sovereignty |
