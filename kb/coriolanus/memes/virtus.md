# Virtus

## Definition

**Virtus** — from Latin *vir* (man) — is the Roman concept of manly excellence: the fusion of courage in battle, moral worth, and devotion to the *res publica*. Coriolanus embodies *virtus* absolutely, and the play's tragedy lies in the discovery that absolute virtue is incompatible with republican politics.

## Historical Background

*Virtus* was the supreme Roman value, demonstrated through military achievement and rewarded with political office. The display of wounds (*vulnera*) was the public proof of *virtus*, and the *corona civica* — the crown of oak leaves awarded for saving a citizen's life in battle — was its highest visible emblem.

## In the Play

Coriolanus performs *virtus* at Corioli with superhuman intensity — entering the enemy city alone, emerging victorious, earning his *agnomen*. His mother Volumnia confirms that she cultivated this *virtus* deliberately: "Thy valiantness was mine, thou suck'dst it from me" (017-002). Yet this same *virtus* makes him contemptuous of the plebeian constitution, unwilling to perform political humility, and ultimately incapable of surviving in the republic his martial excellence was supposed to serve.

## Relationship to Other Concepts

- **Military Honor**: *Virtus* is the inner quality; military honour is its public recognition. Coriolanus's wounds are the physical proof of his *virtus*.
- **Pride**: *Virtus* properly recognized is Aristotelian megalopsychia; *virtus* asserted against constitutional norms is *superbia*.
- **Consulship**: The office for which *virtus* is the primary qualification. Coriolanus's *virtus* should make him consul; his inability to translate *virtus* into political consent prevents it.
- **Patrician Power**: *Virtus* is the patrician's claim to rule — those who demonstrate martial excellence deserve political authority.

## Critical Perspectives

Critics have debated whether *Coriolanus* endorses or critiques the *virtus* ideal. The classical reading sees Coriolanus as a tragic hero whose virtue is incompatible with a degenerate age; the political reading sees him as a warning about the limits of martial values in a republican polity.
