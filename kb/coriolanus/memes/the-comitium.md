# The Comitium

## Location Overview

The Comitium was the designated space for popular assemblies in the Roman Forum, where the comitia tributa met and voted. Though not explicitly named in the play's stage directions — Shakespeare uses the anachronistic "Forum" and "market-place" settings — the popular assembly scenes evoke this institutional space of democratic procedure. The Forum scene where citizens gather to confirm Coriolanus's consulship (013-002) most closely corresponds to the Comitium's historical function as the arena of popular sovereignty.

## Dramatic Function

The assembly scene in the Forum (013-002) dramatizes the Comitium's core tension: the ritual of popular consent. Here Coriolanus must, however reluctantly, present himself before the citizens and seek their "voices" — the very institutional process that the historical Comitium was designed to facilitate. The tribunes' orchestration of the people's rejection of Coriolanus in this space (013-002) demonstrates how the institutions of popular sovereignty can be wielded as political weapons, transforming the Comitium from a space of consensus into one of confrontation.

## Scenes Set Here

- **act-2-scene-3**: The popular assembly gathers in the Forum to confirm or reject Coriolanus's consulship — the scene that most evokes the Comitium's institutional function (013-002).
