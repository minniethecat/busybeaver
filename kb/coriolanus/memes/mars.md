# Mars

## Definition

**Mars** — the Roman god of war — appears in *Coriolanus* not as a character but as an archetypal presence, invoked at key moments to define Coriolanus's identity. The hero is repeatedly compared to, addressed as, and associated with Mars, creating a mythological frame that elevates his martial prowess while marking his fundamental unsuitability for the civil sphere. The Mars association is not merely decorative; it functions as the play's primary symbolic register for the tragic gap between the warrior's absolute values and the politician's necessary compromises.

## In the Play

Coriolanus first invokes Mars in battle, praying "Now, Mars, I prithee, make us quick in work" (004-001) before leading the assault on Corioli. The prayer is answered — he emerges from the gates alone, covered in blood, a figure of superhuman martial efficacy. Cominius later reports that Coriolanus "struck Corioli like a planet" (009-002), and the cosmic language reinforces the Mars association: Coriolanus fights not like a mortal but like a force of nature, or a god.

The association intensifies when Aufidius greets the exiled Coriolanus with the exclamation "Why, thou Mars!" (021-002) — a recognition that transcends their personal enmity. The rivalry between the two warriors, which structures the play's martial plot, is implicitly a contest over who better embodies the Mars archetype. The Third Servingman's remark that Coriolanus is treated "as if he were son and heir to Mars" (021-002) captures the quasi-divine status the warrior achieves among the Volscians, for whom martial prowess is the supreme value.

Yet the Mars identification also isolates Coriolanus. Gods do not negotiate; they command. When Coriolanus is forced to seek the people's voices in the Forum, the Mars persona becomes a liability — a god cannot perform humility, cannot dissemble, cannot stoop. Volumnia, who "dedicated" her son to war, has created a Mars where Rome needed a politician.

The final Mars reference carries tragic weight. Facing assassination in the Volscian senate, Coriolanus cries "Hear'st thou, Mars?" (029-001) — a desperate appeal to the god who has defined his identity. No answer comes. The archetype that elevated him cannot save him; the god of war has no jurisdiction over the treacheries of peace.

## Dramatic Significance

The Mars motif crystallizes the play's central tension between martial and civil values. Mars is the god Coriolanus was raised to embody — his mother Volumnia having "dedicated" him to war — but a god cannot function in a republic. The tragedy of Coriolanus is, in part, the tragedy of Mars forced into the Forum. Shakespeare draws on the classical tradition in which Mars, though honoured, is ultimately subordinate to Jupiter's cosmic order — and Coriolanus, the mortal Mars, is ultimately subordinate to the maternal authority of Volumnia, who alone can command him to lay down his arms.
