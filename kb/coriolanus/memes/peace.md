## Definition

In *Coriolanus*, "peace" operates as both a political ideal and an ironic absence. Rome is nominally at peace during much of the play's domestic action, yet the city is riven by class conflict, and its greatest soldier proves incapable of the civic virtues peace demands. The servingmen's dialogue in Act IV distills the play's cynical view: peace is "a very apoplexy, lethargy; mulled, deaf, sleepy, insensible" (1070–1072).

## Historical Background

In Shakespeare's Plutarchan source, the transition from military to civil life was a central problem of Roman statecraft. The ideal of *pax Romana* — peace secured through military dominance — was complemented by the expectation that victorious generals would transition to political leadership. Coriolanus's inability to make this transition — to exchange the sword for the gown — is the play's structural engine.

## In the Play

Peace appears in *Coriolanus* primarily as a foil to war, the element in which the hero thrives. Early in the play, Coriolanus (then Caius Marcius) scornfully tells the citizens that they "like nor peace nor war" — the one frightens them, the other makes them proud (001-080). This binary structures his worldview: war is honest, peace is debased.

Volumnia articulates the peace-war dichotomy most explicitly when she counsels her son to dissemble before the people. She recalls his own maxim that "honour and policy, like unsevered friends, i' the war do grow together," then challenges him: "in peace what each of them by the other lose, that they combine not there" (004-788, 004-791). Her argument is that the same tactical dishonesty practised in war — feints, stratagems — should be acceptable in peacetime politics. Coriolanus cannot accept this equivalence.

Menenius attempts to defend Coriolanus by explaining that he "has been bred i' the wars / Since he could draw a sword, and is ill schooled / In bolted language" (004-755). The implication is that peace requires a different kind of eloquence — diplomatic, measured — that martial education does not provide.

The most extended meditation on peace comes, paradoxically, from the Volscian servingmen. The Second Servingman declares: "This peace is nothing, but to rust iron, increase tailors, and breed ballad-makers" (008-1070). The First Servingman elaborates: "Let me have war, say I; it exceeds peace as far as day does night; it's spritely, waking, audible, and full of vent. Peace is a very apoplexy, lethargy" (008-1071). This comic interlude encodes a serious critique: peace, in the play's value system, is stagnant and emasculating.

Coriolanus's final prayer before his banishment — that the gods "keep Rome in safety... Throng our large temples with the shows of peace, / And not our streets with war" (004-839) — is deeply ironic. The man who cannot abide peace invokes it as a blessing upon the city he is about to abandon.

## Relationships

Peace is the antithesis of [[military-honor]] and the condition Coriolanus's [[pride]] cannot accommodate. It is closely tied to [[class-conflict]], since the patrician-plebeian struggle intensifies in peacetime, when soldiers become politicians. The concept of [[rhetoric]] — the peacetime art of persuasion — is precisely what Coriolanus rejects. Peace also connects to [[exile]]: it is Coriolanus's failure at peace that leads to his banishment.

## Critical Perspectives

Critics have read the peace-war binary in *Coriolanus* as a reflection of Jacobean anxieties about militarism and civic order. The play, written around 1608, coincides with a period of relative peace under James I, during which the role of the martial aristocracy was being renegotiated. Coriolanus's tragedy can be understood as the tragedy of a warrior class rendered obsolete by peace.
