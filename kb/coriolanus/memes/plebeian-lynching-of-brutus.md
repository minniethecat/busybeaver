# Plebeian Attack on Brutus

The **Plebeian Attack on Brutus** is the political event in Act V, Scene IV of *Coriolanus* in which the Roman plebeians, having learned that the women's embassy has succeeded in turning back Coriolanus's army, turn violently upon their tribune Junius Brutus — the very leader they had previously supported in banishing Coriolanus.

## Context: Rome Under Siege

As the scene opens, Rome is in a state of despair. Coriolanus's Volscian army is encamped outside the city, and both the Senate's embassy and Menenius's personal appeal have been rejected. The tribunes, who engineered Coriolanus's banishment, now face the consequences of their political victory: the man they drove from Rome has returned at the head of an enemy army.

Menenius, now a broken man, confronts the tribune Sicinius with grim fatalism:

> "If it be possible for you to displace it with your little finger, there is some hope the ladies of Rome, especially his mother, may prevail with him. But I say there is no hope in't: our throats are sentenced and stay upon execution." (149-002)

The phrase "your little finger" is devastatingly sarcastic. Menenius reminds Sicinius that the political power the tribune wielded so effectively in peacetime is useless against an invading army. The tribunes, so fluent in the language of the forum, have no vocabulary for the siege.

Sicinius struggles to comprehend the transformation: "Is't possible that so short a time can alter the condition of a man?" (149-002). Menenius's reply — contrasting the "grub" and the "butterfly" — conveys the magnitude of Coriolanus's change from Roman general to vengeful dragon. The natural metaphor suggests that Coriolanus's transformation is not a perversion but a maturation: the grub was always destined to become what it becomes.

## The Mob Turns

A Messenger arrives with terrifying news:

> "Sir, if you'ld save your life, fly to your house: / The plebeians have got your fellow-tribune / And hale him up and down, all swearing, if / The Roman ladies bring not comfort home, / They'll give him death by inches." (150-002)

The plebeians — the same "common cry of curs" that Coriolanus had cursed at his banishment — have seized Brutus and are dragging him through the streets. The phrase "hale him up and down" conveys the mob's ferocity: Brutus is not merely attacked but paraded, his humiliation made public spectacle. The threat of "death by inches" — a slow, measured killing — suggests the mob's desire for prolonged vengeance rather than swift justice.

The mob that the tribunes had cultivated and manipulated as their political weapon has now turned against them. This is the play's most savage dramatic irony: the political instrument the tribunes perfected — the manipulation of popular feeling — has become the instrument of their own destruction.

## The Mob's Inconstancy

The attack on Brutus dramatises a theme that runs throughout the play: the terrifying volatility of the crowd. The same people who:

- Rioted for grain and were granted tribunes (Act I)
- Were persuaded by the tribunes to withdraw their support for Coriolanus (Act II)  
- Voted to banish Coriolanus (Act III)
- Now attack the tribunes who led them to that decision (Act V)

The plebeians' political consistency is non-existent — but their emotional logic is perfectly coherent. They respond to immediate stimuli (hunger, fear, hope) rather than abstract principles. When Coriolanus threatens Rome, he is the enemy; when the women's embassy removes that threat, the tribunes who engineered his banishment become the enemy. The mob's inconstancy is not irrationality but a different kind of rationality — one governed by survival rather than principle.

## Salvation Arrives

Immediately after this grim report, a Second Messenger enters with news that reverses everything:

> "Good news, good news; the ladies have prevail'd, / The Volscians are dislodged, and Marcius gone: / A merrier day did never yet greet Rome, / No, not the expulsion of the Tarquins." (150-002)

The women's embassy — led by Volumnia — has succeeded. Coriolanus has withdrawn his army. Rome is saved. The comparison to "the expulsion of the Tarquins" — Rome's foundational liberation from monarchy — is both hyperbolic and significant. The women's achievement is framed as a foundational act, a new liberation that rivals Rome's origin story.

The Second Messenger describes the jubilant chaos:

> "Ne'er through an arch so hurried the blown tide, / As the recomforted through the gates." (150-002)

The simile of the "blown tide" — the sea rushing through a narrow channel — captures the overwhelming force of collective emotion. The people are not individuals making choices but a natural force, as irresistible and as mindless as water finding its level.

The sounds of celebration — "Trumpets; hautboys; drums beat" — fill the stage, a stark contrast to the near-execution of Brutus moments before. The rapid alternation between despair and joy, violence and celebration, is characteristic of late Shakespearean dramaturgy: the emotional volatility that Coriolanus himself embodies is now performed by the entire city.

## Menenius's Verdict

Menenius's closing words encapsulate the scene's complex ironies. Praising Volumnia as "worth of consuls, senators, patricians, / A city full; of tribunes, such as you, / A sea and land full" (150-002), he implicitly contrasts the authentic heroism of Coriolanus's mother with the political parasitism of the tribunes. The arithmetic is brutal: one woman is worth more than all the tribunes the sea and land could hold.

Yet Menenius's celebration is shadowed by dramatic irony. The audience knows what Menenius does not: that Coriolanus's withdrawal seals his death in Antium. Volumnia's triumph is also her son's death sentence. The salvation of Rome is, for the Volumnia who will return to a sonless house, indistinguishable from catastrophe.

## Political Significance

This scene dramatises a profound political irony that runs through the entire play:

1. **The mob's inconstancy**: The same people who chanted for Coriolanus's banishment now attack his political enemies. The crowd's loyalty is revealed as entirely instrumental — they support whoever serves their immediate interests.
2. **The tribunes' vulnerability**: Brutus and Sicinius, who wielded the people as a weapon, discover that weapon can turn in their hands. Political power derived from popular manipulation is inherently unstable.
3. **The limits of demagoguery**: Political manipulation of the crowd is effective only so long as the crowd's interests are served. Once those interests shift — as they do when survival is at stake — the demagogue is expendable.
4. **The displacement of violence**: The play ends not with Coriolanus's death (which occurs off-stage, in Antium) but with this parodic echo of it — the near-lynching of Brutus, a surrogate sacrifice that substitutes for the tragedy the Roman audience cannot witness.

## Dramatic Function

The attack on Brutus serves a crucial dramatic function: it provides a moment of what might be called poetic justice — the architects of Coriolanus's banishment facing the consequences of their machinations. Simultaneously, it underscores the play's relentless skepticism about popular politics. The mob's loyalty is shown to be as fickle as Coriolanus himself had always claimed — a validation, in retrospect, of his darkest judgments about the people, delivered at the very moment when his absence has made those judgments irrelevant.
