# Volsce

**Volsce** is a [[volscians|Volsci]]an intelligence agent in Shakespeare's *[[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]*, appearing in a single scene (Act IV, Scene III) that proves pivotal to the play's military plot. Meeting the Roman turncoat [[nicanor|Nicanor]] on the road between [[rome|Rome]] and [[antium|Antium]], the Volsce receives intelligence that transforms [[volscians|Volsci]]an strategy.

## The Meeting with Nicanor

The scene opens with the Volsce encountering [[nicanor|Nicanor]], whom he initially fails to recognize: "It is so, sir: truly, I have forgot you" (020-003). When [[nicanor|Nicanor]] identifies himself, the Volsce is sceptical: "[[nicanor|Nicanor]]? no" (020-003), before accepting his identity with a soldier's practicality: "You had more beard when I last saw you; but your favour is well approved by your tongue."

Once recognition is established, the Volsce immediately turns to business: "What's the news in [[rome|Rome]]? I have a note from the [[volscians|Volsci]]an state, to find you out there: you have well saved me a day's journey" (020-003). He is an efficient operative — the encounter that might have been a coincidence becomes a productive intelligence exchange.

## The Critical Intelligence

The Volsce's questioning reveals [[rome|Rome]]'s vulnerability. When he learns that the Romans "are in a most warlike preparation," he presses for confirmation: "Hath been! is it ended, then? Our state thinks not so: they are in a most warlike preparation, and hope to come upon them in the heat of their division" (020-003).

The climactic revelation — "[[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] banished!" (020-003) — transforms the strategic calculus. The [[volscians|Volscians]] had been preparing for war against a [[rome|Rome]] led by its greatest general; the [[exile|banishment]] of [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] removes that obstacle. The Volsce's response is immediate and professional: "You will be welcome with this intelligence, [[nicanor|Nicanor]]" (020-003).

## Dramatic Significance

Though the Volsce appears in only one scene, his function is crucial: he is the conduit through which news of [[rome|Rome]]'s internal fracture reaches the [[volscians|Volsci]]an state. His report sets in motion the events of Act V — the [[volscians|Volsci]]an invasion and [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s tragic return at the head of an enemy army. The scene also establishes that intelligence-gathering is a routine, professionalized aspect of the Roman-[[volscians|Volsci]]an conflict, not merely the work of heroic individuals.
