# Volumnia

## Character Overview

Volumnia is [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s formidable mother, who raised him to prize martial honour above life itself. She takes pride in his wounds, exults in his victories, and ultimately persuades him to spare [[rome|Rome]] — an act of maternal [[rhetoric|persuasion]] that paradoxically seals his death at Aufidius's hands.

## The Matriarch of Martial Rome

Volumnia is [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s mother — perhaps the most formidable matriarch in Shakespeare. Named in the Dramatis Personae as "mother to [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]" (P01-013), she has shaped her son's identity from childhood: "Thy valiantness was mine, thou suck'dst it from me" (017-002). When her son returns from [[corioli|Corioli]] covered in wounds, she counts them with pride rather than concern — each wound a material record of honour. Her political instincts are sharper than her son's: she counsels him to perform the ritual humility required for the [[consulship|consul]]ship, recognising that his refusal to compromise is self-destructive. Yet in the play's climactic scene, she uses the same rhetorical power that forged his martial identity to persuade him to spare [[rome|Rome]] — a victory of maternal authority over martial vengeance that seals his fate.
