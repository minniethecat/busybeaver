## Overview

The First Lord is a minor character in *[[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]* who appears in the final scene of the play, Act V, Scene VI, set in [[antium|Antium]]. As one of the [[volscians|Volsci]]an lords who welcome [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] back from his campaign against [[rome|Rome]], the First Lord is among the first to condemn [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] for making peace with [[rome|Rome]] instead of pressing the attack. He witnesses the assassination of [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] and delivers the play's final tribute to the fallen hero.

## In the Play

The First Lord enters with the other [[volscians|Volsci]]an lords after Aufidius has instructed the conspirators on their plan (1361). He greets [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] with apparent courtesy but immediately voices displeasure at the news that [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] has made peace with [[rome|Rome]] (1347):

> And grieve to hear't. What faults he made before the last, I think Might have found easy fines: but there to end Where he was to begin and give away The benefit of our levies, answering us With our own charge, making a treaty where There was a yielding—this admits no excuse. (1347)

His criticism — that [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] squandered the [[volscians|Volsci]]an military advantage by making a treaty when [[rome|Rome]] was yielding — echoes Aufidius's accusation of treachery and sets the tone for the confrontation that follows.

When the argument between [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] and Aufidius escalates into mutual insults, the First Lord attempts to restore order: "Peace, both, and hear me speak" (1361). But his intervention comes too late; [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s defiant boasting triggers the conspirators, who draw their swords and kill him.

After [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] is slain and Aufidius stands on his body, the First Lord's reaction is one of sorrow rather than triumph: "O Tullus—" (1372), an incomplete exclamation that suggests shock at what has occurred. He then delivers the closing words on [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] (1376):

> Bear from hence his body; And mourn you for him: let him be regarded As the most noble corse that ever herald Did follow to his urn. (1376)

## Dramatic Function

The First Lord's final speech serves as an epitaph for [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]], restoring dignity to the fallen warrior after the chaotic violence of his death. His command to mourn [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] as "the most noble corse that ever herald did follow to his urn" (1376) reframes the assassination as tragedy rather than justice, echoing the play's ambivalent treatment of its protagonist. The First Lord thus functions as a voice of institutional authority that, in death, grants [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] the honor he was denied in life.
