# Coriolanus Refuses Spoils

## Event Summary

In the immediate aftermath of the victory at Corioli (Act I, Scene IX), the consul Cominius offers the soldier Marcius — not yet named Coriolanus — a tenth of all captured treasure, to be taken at his sole choice before the common distribution. Marcius refuses the reward in a speech that defines his character: he cannot "make my heart consent to take A bribe to pay my sword." He insists on taking only an equal share with his fellow soldiers. The army then acclaims him with the new name "Coriolanus." Immediately after refusing princely gifts, Coriolanus makes his sole request: the freedom of a poor Volscian host who had sheltered him in Corioli — a man whose name he has forgotten.

> I thank you, general; / But cannot make my heart consent to take / A bribe to pay my sword: I do refuse it; / And stand upon my common part with those / That have beheld the doing.
> — Marcius, Act I, Scene IX

## Cominius's Offer

The scene opens with Cominius attempting to eulogize Marcius's battlefield deeds. Marcius deflects the praise with characteristic discomfort — "Pray now, no more: my mother, Who has a charter to extol her blood, When she does praise me grieves me" — asserting that he has done only what any soldier would have done. Cominius insists that Rome must know the value of her own, calling silence about such deeds "Worse than a theft, no less than a traducement."

Marcius's response — "I have some wounds upon me, and they smart To hear themselves remember'd" — captures his peculiar relationship with honor: he will endure any physical wound, but verbal recognition causes him genuine pain.

Cominius then makes the formal offer:

> Of all the horses, / Whereof we have ta'en good and good store, of all / The treasure in this field achieved and city, / We render you the tenth, to be ta'en forth, / Before the common distribution, at / Your only choice.
> — Cominius, Act I, Scene IX

The offer is extraordinary — a tenth of all spoils, with first choice, before any distribution to the army. For a soldier, this represents the highest material reward Rome can bestow for valor.

## The Refusal

Marcius's refusal is immediate and categorical. He characterizes the reward as a "bribe to pay my sword" — a phrase that reveals his conception of military service as an end in itself, not a transaction. To accept payment for what he would have done anyway would corrupt the act:

> I thank you, general; / But cannot make my heart consent to take / A bribe to pay my sword: I do refuse it; / And stand upon my common part with those / That have beheld the doing.
> — Marcius, Act I, Scene IX

The army responds with a "long flourish" — trumpets, drums, and a chorus of voices crying "Marcius! Marcius!" as the soldiers cast their caps and lances into the air. But Marcius rebukes even this acclamation:

> May these same instruments, which you profane, / Never sound more! when drums and trumpets shall / I' the field prove flatterers, let courts and cities be / Made all of false-faced soothing!
> — Marcius, Act I, Scene IX

This speech reveals the depth of Marcius's anti-political philosophy: he believes that the instruments of war should remain pure of flattery, that martial honor is corrupted by public celebration. The very praise that would launch a political career is, to him, a form of degradation.

## The Naming

Cominius, undeterred by the refusal, declares that Marcius shall instead receive a reward that cannot be refused: a name. He proclaims that from this day forward, for what he has done before and in Corioli, Marcius shall be called "Caius Marcius Coriolanus." The army roars its approval:

> With all the applause and clamour of the host, / Caius Marcius Coriolanus! / Bear the addition nobly ever!
> — Cominius and All, Act I, Scene IX

The naming ceremony converts military achievement into identity. Unlike the refused spoils, the name cannot be rejected — it becomes part of who he is. The name "Coriolanus" is both honor and burden, a permanent reminder of the deeds he preferred not to hear spoken.

## The One Request

In one of the play's most revealing transitions, Coriolanus immediately pivots from refusing "most princely gifts" to asking a favor. He is "bound to beg" for the freedom of a poor man in Corioli who had sheltered him — a man whose name he has, characteristically, forgotten:

> I sometime lay here in Corioli / At a poor man's house; he used me kindly: / He cried to me; I saw him prisoner; / But then Aufidius was within my view, / And wrath o'erwhelm'd my pity: I request you / To give my poor host freedom.
> — Coriolanus, Act I, Scene IX

Cominius grants the request instantly: "Were he the butcher of my son, he should Be free as is the wind." When Lartius asks the man's name, Coriolanus confesses he has forgotten — "By Jupiter! forgot. I am weary; yea, my memory is tired. Have we no wine here?" — and exits to tend his wounds.

The juxtaposition is telling: Coriolanus refuses vast wealth for himself but asks mercy for a nameless enemy who showed him kindness. He remembers the kindness but not the name; he remembers every wound but wishes to hear none of them spoken. This selective memory defines his tragic character — a man who lives entirely in the physical world of deeds, unable or unwilling to navigate the verbal world of politics.

## Dramatic Function

This scene establishes the paradox at the heart of Coriolanus's character. His refusal of spoils is genuinely noble — it demonstrates an integrity that sets him apart from every other military figure in the play. Yet this same integrity contains the seeds of his destruction: a man who cannot accept the normal rewards of his society cannot function within its political institutions. The naming ceremony, which should be the triumphant climax of his military career, becomes instead the beginning of his political undoing.
