# Third Servingman

The **Third Servingman** is a servant in the household of [[tullus-aufidius|Tullus Aufidius]] in Shakespeare's *[[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]*. He appears in Act IV Scene V, where he is the most confrontational of the three servants who encounter the disguised [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]], and later serves as the news-bringer about the proceedings inside the house.

## Role in the Play

The Third Servingman enters to find the First and Second [[servingmen|Servingmen]] struggling to expel [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]. He takes the lead in confronting the stranger, asking "What have you to do here, fellow? Pray you, avoid the house" [021-002]. When [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] refuses to leave, the Third Servingman calls for his master to be informed of the "strange guest" [021-002].

The Third Servingman engages in a short verbal duel with [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]:

> "Where dwellest thou?" / "Under the canopy!" / "Where's that?" / "I' the city of kites and crows!" [021-002]

[[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] eventually beats him away, telling him to "serve with thy trencher" [021-002].

After the main action, the Third Servingman returns with news from inside the house. He reports that [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] "is so made on here within, as if he were son and heir to Mars" — seated at the head of the table, treated with reverence even by Aufidius [021-008]. He reveals that Aufidius "is cut i' the middle" — his authority halved — as [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] has been granted joint command of the [[volscians|Volsci]]an forces [021-008].

## Key Dialogue

The Third Servingman delivers one of the play's most vivid narrative passages, describing [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s reception:

> "Why, he is so made on here within, as if he were son and heir to Mars; set at upper end o' the table; no question asked him by any of the senators, but they stand bald before him: our general himself makes a mistress of him; sanctifies himself with's hand and turns up the white o' the eye to his discourse." [021-008]

His rustic [[rhetoric|eloquence]], using words like "directitude" [021-008] (a malapropism for "discredit" or similar), gives him a distinctive comic voice.

## Dramatic Function

The Third Servingman serves two important dramatic purposes. His confrontation with [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] provides both comic relief and a demonstration of [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s impatience with common people — a trait that defines his tragic arc. His later news-bearing scene functions as exposition, informing the audience (through the servants' dialogue) of the political shift that has occurred: [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] now shares command of the [[volscians|Volsci]]an army and [[rome|Rome]] faces imminent invasion.

## Relationships

- **[[tullus-aufidius|Tullus Aufidius]]**: The Third Servingman's master. He reports on Aufidius's diminished status since [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s arrival.
- **[[first-servingman|First Servingman]] & [[second-servingman|Second Servingman]]**: His fellow servants and conversational partners.
- **[[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]**: The Third Servingman's most direct antagonist among the servants — he is physically beaten by [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]] but later speaks admiringly of his martial reputation.
