# Coriolanus's Vow of Vengeance

## Event Summary

At the gates of Rome, immediately following his banishment, Coriolanus takes leave of his family and friends. What begins as a farewell rapidly transforms into something darker — a vow to return as an enemy. Surrounded by Volumnia, Virgilia, Menenius, and Cominius, Coriolanus delivers a speech that moves from consoling his mother to cursing his city. The scene marks the definitive emotional pivot of the tragedy: the soldier who fought for Rome now declares war upon it.

## The Farewell

Coriolanus's opening words to his weeping mother and wife attempt stoic reassurance: "Come, leave your tears: a brief farewell" (004-001). He urges Volumnia to endure with the same fortitude she instilled in him: "My mother, you wot well / My hazards still have been your solace" (004-001). There is a tender, almost domestic quality to the exchange that makes the turn to vengeance all the more shocking. He tells Virgilia "my sweet, come" (004-001) and instructs her to remarry, imagining a future husband who will say "that but for this, / Our good old friend, my wife's first husband, lived" (004-001).

## The Vow

The speech pivots when Coriolanus turns from personal farewell to political declaration. He assures his family that he will be safe "where I shall think upon the honour of my blood / And rot me inward for a lingering death" (004-001). The metaphor of inward rot captures the psychological transformation: the Roman identity that sustained him is now a poison. Coriolanus asserts that Rome "loved me not" and declares: "I shall be loved when I lack" (004-001). This paradoxical formulation — he will be valued only when he is absent, feared rather than honored — encapsulates the tragedy's inversion of martial and political worth.

The final exchange with Cominius crystallizes the vow. When Cominius urges patience — "the people will relent" — Coriolanus's response is absolute: "I do not ask you to relent: let them / Hang me on Rome's walls" (004-001). The image of his own body hanging from the city he saved is the vow's logical endpoint: Coriolanus would rather see himself destroyed than see Rome triumph over his will.

## Dramatic Significance

The vow of vengeance transforms the play's dramatic logic. Until this moment, Coriolanus has been the victim of political machination — proud, intemperate, but fundamentally a defender of Rome. The vow changes him from defender to destroyer. It also generates the central dramatic question of the final two acts: will anything — Roman embassy, personal friendship, filial duty — break this vow? The answer, provided by Volumnia in the women's embassy scene, is the tragedy's deepest irony: the only force that can break Coriolanus's oath is the mother who taught him to keep oaths.
