# Creation of the Tribunate

The **Creation of the Tribunate** is the political event in Act I, Scene I of *Coriolanus* in which the Roman plebeians, having risen in armed revolt over grain shortages, are granted five tribunes — officials elected to represent and defend their interests against the patrician class.

## Context

The event unfolds in the immediate aftermath of the corn riot. The plebeians, bearing "staves, clubs, and other weapons" (001-002), have taken to the streets demanding grain at affordable prices. Their particular grievance is directed at Caius Marcius, whom they identify as "chief enemy to the people" (001-002).

The plebeians' revolt draws on the historical memory of the **First Plebeian Secession** (494 BC), when the Roman commons withdrew from the city to the Sacred Mount, forcing the patricians to grant them tribunes. Shakespeare's compression of this historical event into a single scene accelerates the play's political tempo: what took years in Roman history occurs within minutes on stage.

## Menenius's Mediation

The patrician Menenius Agrippa attempts to calm the mutinous citizens through the famous **Belly Speech** — an allegory in which the Senate is the belly that stores and redistributes nourishment to the body's other members (the people):

> "The senators of Rome are this good belly, / And you the mutinous members; for examine / Their counsels and their cares, digest things rightly / Touching the weal o' the common, you shall find / No public benefit which you receive / But it proceeds or comes from them to you / And no way from yourselves." (004-002–005-002)

The Belly Speech adapts a well-known classical fable. Livy reports that Menenius Agrippa used this exact allegory during the historical First Secession, and Plutarch includes it in his *Life of Coriolanus*. Shakespeare's Menenius deploys the metaphor with characteristic rhetorical ease — but the speech's political implications are darker than its surface geniality suggests. By figuring the Senate as a digestive organ, Menenius naturalises hierarchy: the people are "members" who must trust the belly's wisdom without questioning its operations.

The speech partially succeeds in pacifying the crowd, but the underlying tensions remain unresolved. The plebeians' acceptance of the allegory reveals their political vulnerability — they can be persuaded by rhetoric that, on examination, justifies their own subordination.

## Marcius's Role

Caius Marcius enters and delivers a blistering denunciation of the plebeians, calling them "dissentious rogues" (003-002) and "curs" (004-002). Where Menenius speaks in the diplomatic language of the body politic, Marcius speaks in the language of contempt. He reveals that the other troop of mutineers has already been dissolved after being granted their petition:

> "They are dissolved: hang 'em! / They said they were an-hungry; sigh'd forth proverbs, / That hunger broke stone walls, that dogs must eat, / That meat was made for mouths, that the gods sent not / Corn for the rich men only: with these shreds / They vented their complainings; which being answer'd, / And a petition granted them, a strange one— / To break the heart of generosity, / And make bold power look pale—they threw their caps / As they would hang them on the horns o' the moon, / Shouting their emulation." (012-002)

Marcius's speech is remarkable not only for its venom but for what it reveals about his political philosophy. The plebeians' complaint — that "the gods sent not / Corn for the rich men only" — is, by any reasonable standard, legitimate. But Marcius treats it as a sign of dangerous presumption. In his universe, the assertion of need is an act of rebellion; the plebeians' hunger is not a problem to be solved but a "musty superfluity" to be vented.

Marcius reluctantly reveals what was granted — five tribunes:

> "Five tribunes to defend their vulgar wisdoms, / Of their own choice: one's Junius Brutus, / Sicinius Velutus, and I know not—" (012-002)

The phrase "vulgar wisdoms" condenses Marcius's entire political worldview. Wisdom, properly speaking, cannot be vulgar; the tribunes will "defend" something that, in Marcius's lexicon, is a contradiction in terms. His trailing off — "and I know not" — performs a calculated insult: the remaining tribunes are beneath his notice.

Marcius views this concession with deep contempt, believing "the rabble should have first unroof'd the city" before receiving such power. He predicts it "will in time / Win upon power and throw forth greater themes / For insurrection's arguing" (012-002). This prediction is, in dramatic terms, self-fulfilling — the tribunes will indeed use their power to destroy him.

## Dramatic Structure and Irony

The creation of the tribunate is doubly ironic in the play's architecture. First, the concession that Marcius so bitterly opposes — plebeian political representation — is granted precisely because of a revolt he himself helped provoke through his contempt. Second, the tribunes created in this scene will become the instruments of his destruction, proving his own prediction correct but for reasons he cannot perceive: the tribunes do not destroy him through institutional power alone but through their mastery of the very popular manipulation he despises.

The scene establishes a pattern that runs through the entire play: political concessions, once made, cannot be contained. The tribunes, created as a safety valve for plebeian discontent, become a permanent check on patrician power — and, ultimately, a weapon against the patrician who most openly despises them.

## Political Significance

The creation of the tribunate represents a watershed moment in the play's political landscape. It:

- **Institutionalizes plebeian power**: For the first time, the common people have official representatives with legal standing. The tribunes' authority — including the power of veto and the sacrosanctity of their persons — transforms the informal class struggle into a constitutional contest.
- **Creates the play's central antagonists**: The newly elected tribunes, Sicinius Velutus and Junius Brutus, become Marcius's primary political enemies. Their rise to power is the direct result of the patrician concession Marcius opposed.
- **Establishes the class conflict dynamic** that drives much of the subsequent action. From this point forward, the play operates on two fronts simultaneously: the external war with the Volscians and the internal war between patricians and plebeians.
- **Inverts the tragic trajectory**: In a typical revenge tragedy, the hero's enemies are external forces. In *Coriolanus*, the hero's enemies are created by the very political system he refuses to accommodate. The tribunes are not villains in the conventional sense — they are the predictable product of patrician arrogance.

## Historical and Critical Perspectives

Shakespeare's depiction of the tribunate's creation draws on Plutarch's account but compresses and dramatises it for theatrical effect. In Plutarch, the tribunate emerges gradually through sustained plebeian pressure; in Shakespeare, it is granted almost off-handedly, as a side-note to Marcius's military dispatch. This compression serves the play's thematic interests: by treating the tribunate as a hastily granted concession rather than a hard-won institutional reform, Shakespeare underscores the patrician class's failure to take plebeian grievances seriously — a failure that will prove catastrophic.

Critics have noted the play's ambivalent treatment of the tribunate. For some, the tribunes represent a legitimate democratic check on aristocratic power; for others, they embody the dangers of popular manipulation and demagoguery. The play itself refuses to resolve this ambiguity, presenting both the tribunes' legitimate grievances and their manipulative methods without endorsing either perspective entirely.

## Aftermath

In the scene's closing lines, Sicinius and Brutus — now tribunes — reveal their hostility toward Marcius. They conspire to use the coming war against the Volscians to their advantage, calculating that Marcius's pride will ultimately destroy him (012-002). This private plotting foreshadows the tribunes' later manipulation of the people to secure Marcius's banishment. The tribunate, created as an institution of plebeian protection, becomes an instrument of personal vendetta — a transformation that implicates both the tribunes' ambition and Marcius's intransigence.
