# Envy

## Definition

**Envy** in *Coriolanus* operates on two distinct but interlocking levels: the personal martial envy between Coriolanus and Aufidius, and the political envy the tribunes exploit to turn the people against the hero. Both forms of envy are destructive, but Shakespeare invests the martial variant with a strange nobility — Aufidius's "ancient envy" is almost indistinguishable from admiration, while the tribunes' political envy is presented as merely cynical manipulation. This distinction is itself political: the play invites us to regard aristocratic rivalry as grander, more meaningful, than democratic grievance.

## In the Play

Aufidius declares his envy openly in his first substantive scene: "Not Afric owns a serpent I abhor / More than thy fame and envy" (008-002). The syntax is telling — Aufidius hates not merely Coriolanus's fame but the "envy" it provokes in him, as though the emotion itself were an external affliction. Having been defeated by Coriolanus five times, Aufidius's hatred is fueled by wounded pride; his "emulation / Hath not that honour in't it had" (010-002) because he can no longer face Coriolanus as an equal. The word "emulation" here bridges the play's two envy registers — emulation can be noble (striving to equal excellence) or base (resenting superiority), and the Aufidius-Coriolanus relationship oscillates between these poles.

The tribunes weaponize envy politically. Brutus instructs Sicinius to "enforce him with his envy to the people" (016-002) — reframing Coriolanus's aristocratic pride as a form of contempt that the plebeians should resent. The tribunes understand that political envy is a more reliable mobilizing force than political principle: the people may not grasp constitutional arguments about the consulship, but they instinctively understand the injury of being looked down upon. When Coriolanus himself speaks of "the cruelty and envy of the people" (021-002), he reveals his own blindness — he cannot see that the people's "envy" is a rational response to aristocratic domination, not an irrational moral failing.

The resolution of the envy plot is characteristically Shakespearean. Aufidius's "root of ancient envy" (021-002) is briefly "weeded" from his heart by Coriolanus's nobility — only to return fatally in Act V. When Coriolanus achieves greater popularity among the Volscians than Aufidius himself, the old envy resurfaces with redoubled force. The final scene's assassination is, at its emotional core, the triumph of envy over admiration — Aufidius cannot bear to share glory, and so destroys the rival he could not equal.

## Dramatic Significance

Envy is the play's emotional first cause — the passion that converts political opposition into personal hatred and personal hatred into lethal action. Without envy, the tribunes could not mobilize the people; without envy, Aufidius would welcome Coriolanus as a comrade rather than ultimately destroy him as a rival. The play's tragic structure depends on envy as the force that prevents accommodation, compromise, and peace.
