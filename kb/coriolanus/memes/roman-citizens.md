# Roman Citizens

## Character Overview

The Roman Citizens are not an undifferentiated mob but a collective character with distinct voices. The First, Second, and [[third-citizen|Third Citizen]]s articulate genuine political reasoning about obligation, representation, and power. Their susceptibility to [[rhetoric|persuasion]] by tribunes and patricians alike reflects the structural position of the plebeian class in the Roman constitution.

## Dramatic Function

The character serves a supporting function in the dramatic structure of *[[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]*, advancing the play's central conflicts through action and speech.

## Dramatic Presence

The Roman citizens open the play in mutiny, entering "with staves, clubs, and other weapons" to protest the price of grain (001-002). Their collective voice — "Speak, speak" — establishes the volatile political energy that drives the drama. Though they are manipulated by the tribunes, they also demonstrate authentic grievances: [[famine|hunger]], disenfranchisement, and resentment of patrician privilege. In battle, they prove their worth as soldiers — "we are come off / Like Romans, neither foolish in our stands, / Nor cowardly in retreat" (006-002) — complicating [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]]'s contemptuous view of them as merely "dissentious rogues." Their presence as a collective character, speaking in unison and moving as a body, makes them a dramatic force unique in Shakespeare's Roman plays.
