# Tribunate

## Overview

The **Tribunate** (*Tribunus Plebis*) is the constitutional office that structures the political conflict of Shakespeare's *Coriolanus*. Created after the [[first-secession|First Plebeian Secession]] as a safeguard of [[plebeian-rights|plebeian interests]] against [[patrician-power|patrician domination]], the tribunate provides the institutional framework through which [[sicinius-velutus|Sicinius Velutus]] and [[junius-brutus|Junius Brutus]] oppose and ultimately destroy [[caius-marcius-coriolanus|Coriolanus]].

Shakespeare presents the tribunate with characteristic ambivalence. It is simultaneously a legitimate constitutional institution — the voice of the people in a mixed republic — and a vehicle for demagogic manipulation. The tribunes are not wrong to fear Coriolanus's contempt for popular government; they are wrong — or at least recklessly indifferent — in the methods they use to destroy him.

> What's the matter, you dissentious rogues,
> That, rubbing the poor itch of your opinion,
> Make yourselves scabs? (001-001) — Coriolanus's first words, addressed to the citizens whose tribunes will destroy him

## Constitutional Powers

| Power | Latin Term | Dramatic Use in the Play |
|-------|-----------|------------------------|
| **Sacrosanctity** | *sacrosanctitas* | Tribunes cannot be physically harmed; Coriolanus's threat to do so is treason |
| **Veto** | *intercessio* | Implied in the tribunes' ability to block senatorial actions |
| **Summoning the Assembly** | *ius agendi cum plebe* | Tribunes call the [[roman-citizens|people]] to the [[the-forum|Forum]] for Coriolanus's trial |
| **Prosecution** | *accusatio* | Tribunes bring charges against Coriolanus |
| **Plebeian Election** | — | Tribunes are elected by the people, not the Senate; this electoral base is their political power |

These powers, historically accurate in broad outline, give the tribunes an institutional standing that Coriolanus refuses to recognize. His central political error is to treat the tribunate as an aberration rather than a constitutional reality — to see [[sicinius-velutus|Sicinius]] and [[junius-brutus|Brutus]] as mere rabble-rousers rather than officers of the Roman state.

## Historical Origins: The First Secession

The tribunate was born from crisis. The historical [[first-secession|First Secession]] (traditionally 494 BCE) occurred when the [[roman-citizens|plebeians]], burdened by debt and excluded from political power, withdrew en masse from Rome to the Sacred Mount (*Mons Sacer*). The patricians, deprived of the plebeians' military and economic labour, were forced to negotiate. The result was the creation of the tribunate — an office designed to protect plebeians from patrician abuse.

Shakespeare alludes to this history through [[menenius-agrippa|Menenius's]] belly fable (001-002), which is the classical justification for patrician governance against plebeian secession. But the play treats the tribunate not as a historic compromise but as an ongoing crisis: the office that was created to balance the constitution has, in the tribunes' hands, become an engine of populist destruction.

> The play asks whether republican institutions can survive the very conflicts they were designed to manage.

## The Tribunes as Dramatic Characters

Shakespeare individualizes the tribunate through two sharply drawn characters:

### Sicinius Velutus
The senior tribune and strategic mastermind. [[sicinius-velutus|Sicinius]] is calculating, patient, and rhetorically skilled — a politician who understands that institutions are weapons and patience is power. He waits for Coriolanus to destroy himself, providing only the provocations that make self-destruction inevitable.

### Junius Brutus
The junior tribune, more reactive and less subtle than Sicinius. [[junius-brutus|Brutus]] shares Sicinius's political instincts but lacks his restraint — he is quicker to anger, more visibly pleased by Coriolanus's downfall, and less aware of the strategic consequences of their victory.

Together, they form a political partnership that is both efficient and shortsighted. They win the immediate battle — Coriolanus's banishment — but they cannot govern the consequences. When Coriolanus returns at the head of a Volscian army, the tribunes are revealed as tacticians who won a skirmish and lost a war.

## The Tribunate as Constitutional Paradox

The tribunate embodies a fundamental paradox of republican government:

| Aspect | Positive Function | Negative Potential |
|--------|------------------|-------------------|
| **Popular representation** | Gives voice to the excluded | Enables mob rule |
| **Checks on aristocracy** | Prevents oligarchic abuse | Paralyzes necessary governance |
| **Sacrosanct immunity** | Protects plebeian advocates | Shields demagogues from accountability |
| **Veto power** | Ensures consensual governance | Becomes obstructionist |

Shakespeare does not resolve this paradox — he dramatizes it. The tribunes are right that Coriolanus is a threat to popular government; Coriolanus is right that the tribunes are destroying the state. Both claims are true, and their simultaneous truth is the play's tragic essence.
