# Confession

## Overview

Confession is the culminating moment of revelation in *The Mystery at Lilac Inn*. When the true culprit behind the Crandall jewels theft finally admits to the crime, the confession provides the resolution that Nancy Drew's investigation has been building toward.

## The Path to Confession

The confession does not come easily. Mrs. Willoughby admits of an earlier incident: "I was so terribly excited" (008-038)—confessions emerge from pressure and careful questioning. Nancy must first gather sufficient evidence to confront the guilty party, building a case that leaves no room for denial.

## The Moment of Truth

When the confession finally comes, it validates Nancy's detective work and brings relief to those who have been wrongly suspected. The authorities are "trying to wring a confession from her" (014-029), demonstrating that confessions are extracted through persistent and skillful interrogation rather than voluntary admission.

## Aftermath

The confession sets in motion the resolution of the novel's conflicts. Even Nancy herself, analyzing the evidence, must "confess that seems unlikely" (015-078) when certain theories are proposed—confession is not only the criminal's admission but part of the honest investigator's methodology. With the truth established, the innocent are cleared and the Crandall jewels are recovered.
