# Viola Granger

## Role in the Story

Viola Granger is a suspect in the theft of the Crandall jewels. Her name first surfaces during Nancy's investigation when a witness mentions her in connection with the crime (011-049). Nancy dedicates significant effort to investigating Viola's background — searching court records and public documents to determine whether she had any involvement with the stolen jewelry. Viola Granger appears to have "come into considerable money suddenly" (014-012), a suspicious circumstance that deepens the investigation. She also "attempted to take some jewelry which did not belong to her" at a hotel (025-021), establishing a pattern of behavior that makes her a plausible suspect.

Ultimately, the investigation into Viola Granger serves as one of the novel's red herrings — a plausible alternative suspect whose potential guilt must be ruled out before the real culprits can be identified.

## Appearances

- **Chapter XI (011-049, 011-062)**: Viola Granger's name is first raised as a potential suspect. Nancy promises to investigate her background.
- **Chapter XII (012-003)**: Nancy's father, Carson Drew, agrees to find out what he can concerning Viola Granger.
- **Chapter XIV (014-008–041)**: Nancy researches Viola Granger at the court house and discovers she came into money suddenly. The question is posed: if Viola were guilty, would she have remained in town? Nancy remains uncertain, keeping Viola as an active suspect.
- **Chapter XV (015-078)**: Nancy reports that she still has not located Viola Granger's current whereabouts, maintaining her as one of two possible perpetrators alongside Mary Mason.
- **Chapter XXV (025-021)**: In the novel's resolution, Mr. Drew mentions Viola Granger's prior attempt to steal jewelry at a hotel, confirming the pattern of suspicion but also implying the real culprits were others.

## Key Actions

- **Named as a suspect** (011-049): Her name emerges early in the investigation as someone possibly connected to the Crandall jewels theft.
- **Sudden financial gain** (014-012): Court records reveal Viola Granger came into considerable money suddenly, providing circumstantial evidence of guilt.
- **Prior jewelry theft attempt** (025-021): It is revealed that Viola previously attempted to take jewelry that did not belong to her at a hotel, establishing a pattern.
- **Remains at large during the investigation** (015-078): Nancy is unable to locate Viola, keeping her as an active suspect throughout much of the novel.
