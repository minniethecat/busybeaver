# River Heights Police Department

The **River Heights Police Department** is the official law enforcement body involved in the investigation of the Crandall jewel theft. Throughout *The Mystery at Lilac Inn*, the police serve as both an institutional backdrop and a source of dramatic tension — their procedural methods and timelines sometimes align with, and sometimes conflict with, Nancy Drew's more intuitive investigative approach.

## Role in the Narrative

### The Official Investigation

The police are the designated authority for criminal investigation in River Heights. When the Crandall jewels are stolen, jurisdiction falls to the department, and they pursue the case through standard procedures: interviewing witnesses, gathering physical evidence, and following established lines of inquiry.

Their involvement is referenced throughout the novel:
- "The police would like to know" (007-041) — indicating their active interest in the case
- "Dad thinks they will arrest her in a few days" (010-025) — showing their role in determining legal outcomes

### Relationship with the Drews

The police department's relationship with Carson Drew and Nancy Drew is complex. As a respected criminal lawyer, Mr. Drew has professional standing with the department — he can engage with them on equal terms, advocate for clients, and influence the timing of police actions. This access proves crucial when the police prepare to arrest Mrs. Willoughby.

Nancy's relationship with the police is more informal. She shares information when it serves her purposes, but she operates outside departmental constraints — pursuing leads that the police might overlook and maintaining investigative independence. This dual-track approach — official and unofficial investigation proceeding in parallel — is characteristic of the Nancy Drew series.

### The Arrest Threat

The most significant narrative function of the police is the threatened arrest of Mrs. Willoughby. The department, following its evidence, reaches the logical conclusion that Mrs. Willoughby is the prime suspect. The tension this creates — an innocent woman facing arrest while the real criminals remain free — drives the novel's urgency.

"The police intend to arrest Mrs. Willoughby tomorrow morning" (017-024) — this statement crystallizes the conflict between procedural justice (the police following the evidence) and substantive justice (the real criminals should be caught).

## Organizational Character

The police department is portrayed as competent but limited — they follow evidence competently, but the evidence itself has been manipulated by the criminals. Their proceduralism, normally a strength, becomes a vulnerability when confronted with a sophisticated deception.

This portrayal reflects a recurring theme in the novel: the tension between institutional authority and individual initiative. The police represent the former — necessary, respected, but constrained by process. Nancy represents the latter — flexible, intuitive, and willing to pursue unconventional leads.

## Resolution

The police department's investigation is ultimately vindicated — once Nancy uncovers the true criminals and presents her evidence, the department acts decisively to make arrests and close the case. The dual-track approach succeeds: Nancy's initiative supplies what the police's procedure could not, and the police's authority gives legal force to Nancy's discoveries.
