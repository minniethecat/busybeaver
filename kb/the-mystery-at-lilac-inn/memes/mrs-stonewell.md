# Mrs. Howard Stonewell

## Role in the Story

Mrs. Howard Stonewell is a woman of comfortable means who employed Mary Mason as a maid for more than a year. When Mary falls under suspicion for the theft of the Crandall jewels, Nancy Drew visits Mrs. Stonewell to gather character testimony. Mrs. Stonewell provides one of the investigation's key exculpatory facts: Mary Mason never wore jewelry or showed any interest in fine things while in her employ, making it unlikely that she would steal jewels (012-036). Her testimony helps shift Nancy's suspicion away from Mary and toward other suspects, including Viola Granger.

## Appearances

- **Chapter XII (012-017–043)**: Nancy visits Mrs. Stonewell at her home to inquire about Mary Mason. Mrs. Stonewell receives Nancy politely, answers all her questions, and provides a detailed character reference. She is initially cautious but becomes cooperative when she realizes Nancy is acting as a detective. Her testimony covers Mary's employment history, habits, clothing, and demeanor.
- **Chapter XXV (025-040–042)**: After the case is resolved, Mr. Drew remarks that "that girl didn't steal Mrs. Stonewell blind when she worked there" and notes that "Mrs. Stonewell was lucky to escape without having her silver stolen" (025-040, 025-042), referring to the real culprits who might have targeted her home.

## Key Actions

- **Employs Mary Mason for over a year** (012-027): Provides Mary with steady work and observes her character closely over an extended period.
- **Provides critical exculpatory testimony** (012-036): When asked whether Mary ever showed interest in jewelry, Mrs. Stonewell admits that from Mary's plain clothing and lack of adornment, there was no evidence she cared for fine things — undermining the theory that Mary stole the jewels.
- **Cooperates fully with the investigation** (012-018–043): Answers Nancy's questions openly and without evasion, calling her "a detective" with an "awed tone" (012-026).
