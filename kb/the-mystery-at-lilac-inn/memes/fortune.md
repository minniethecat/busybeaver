# Fortune

**Fortune** — in the sense of wealth and inheritance — is the material and psychological driver of the central conflict in *The Mystery at Lilac Inn*. The Crandall family fortune, embodied in the famous jewels, motivates every major character's actions: the legitimate heirs seek to claim it, the criminals scheme to steal it, and Nancy Drew works to restore it to its rightful owners.

## The Crandall Fortune

The fortune is quantified dramatically: "I had a fortune in jewels! Forty thousand dollars' worth!" (006-014). This declaration establishes the stakes in concrete terms — the jewels represent not merely sentimental value but life-changing wealth.

The fortune's significance extends beyond its monetary value. For Emily Crandall, it represents:
- **Family legacy**: The jewels are her inheritance, connecting her to her family history
- **Economic independence**: The fortune would "set Dick up in business" (004-051), providing her brother with a livelihood
- **Justice**: Recovery of the fortune means the criminals' scheme has failed and the rightful order has been restored

## Fortune as Motivation

### The Heirs

Emily and Dick Crandall's relationship to the fortune is legitimate but complicated. They are the rightful heirs, yet they cannot access their inheritance until the jewels are recovered. The fortune exists for them as a promise deferred — something they "came into" (001-048) legally but cannot yet possess physically.

### The Criminals

The criminals are motivated by fortune in its purest form: greed. They seek to claim wealth that is not theirs, using imposture and theft to redirect the Crandall inheritance into their own hands. Their scheme is elaborate precisely because the fortune is so large — "forty thousand dollars' worth" justifies significant criminal investment.

### Nancy Drew

Nancy's relationship to the fortune is professional rather than personal. She has no financial stake in the outcome. Her motivation is justice — the restoration of fortune to its rightful owners. This disinterestedness distinguishes her from both the heirs (who need the fortune) and the criminals (who covet it).

## Fortune and Fate

The novel occasionally plays on the double meaning of "fortune" as both wealth and luck. The phrase "fortune favored her" (004-003) uses the term in its sense of fate or luck, suggesting that Nancy's success depends not only on skill but on being in the right place at the right time. This dual meaning enriches the theme: the characters pursue fortune (wealth), but fortune (luck) determines who succeeds.

## The Recovery

The climax of the novel is the recovery of the fortune: "help Emily Crandall regain her fortune" (014-069). This recovery represents more than the return of stolen property — it is the restoration of justice, the validation of the rightful heirs, and the defeat of the criminal scheme. The fortune's recovery is the novel's moral resolution.

## Key Passages

> "I had a fortune in jewels! Forty thousand dollars' worth!" (006-014)

The exclamation marks convey the emotional weight of the loss — this is not a minor theft but a catastrophic deprivation.

> "Risk losing my fortune now that I've come into it" (001-048)

The paradox of having legally inherited something one cannot physically possess creates the vulnerability that the criminals exploit.

> "Help Emily Crandall regain her fortune" (014-069)

This states Nancy's mission in its simplest form: the fortune is the object of the quest, and its recovery is the measure of success.
