**Hidelberg's** is the most exclusive dress shop in [[River Heights]], positioned at the top of the local retail hierarchy. It becomes a key clue nexus in the [[the-investigation|investigation]] when [[Mary Mason]], a woman of apparently modest means, is discovered to have purchased expensive gowns there.

## Role in the Narrative

### The Shopping Trip

[[Helen Corning]] insists on splurging at Hidelberg's for a party dress, introducing [[nancy-drew|Nancy Drew]] to the shop:

> "To Hidelberg's for a party dress." (010-045)

> "I know Hidelberg's is the most expensive place in town," Helen admitted; "but I told you that for this once I'm splurging. It will never happen again, probably." (010-048)

The trip establishes Hidelberg's as a marker of River Heights social stratification — a destination for the comfortably-off, not those of ordinary means.

### The Mary Mason Anomaly

The most significant narrative function of Hidelberg's is the contradiction it reveals. [[Helen Corning|Helen]], aware that Mary Mason works as a maid, immediately recognizes the incongruity:

> "Doesn't it strike you as odd that a girl in Mary Mason's position can afford to buy gowns at Hidelberg's?" (010-091)

This single observation becomes a persistent suspicion:

> "Of course, it's possible she found work," she reasoned; "but even if she did, it's not likely her wages would be enough to permit her to buy dresses at Hidelberg's. I can't understand it at all." (011-007)

### Verification and Confirmation

Later in the investigation, [[nancy-drew|Nancy]] uses Hidelberg's purchase records as a fact-checking node:

> "Yes, at the River Heights Department Store and at Hidelberg's. They had never heard of her at the River Heights Department Store, but at Hidelberg's I found that she had bought a dress." (015-061)

This confirms that Mary Mason did shop at Hidelberg's — making her expensive purchases a verified fact rather than speculation. No employment at the [[department-store|department store]]; no legitimate explainable income; yet luxury shopping at the most expensive boutique in town.

### Aftermath

> After leaving the Hidelberg shop, Helen Corning drove Nancy Drew home in her runabout. (011-002)

## Narrative Functions

1. **Clue generator** — transforms a shopping trip into an investigative lead through Helen's sharp observation
2. **Class hierarchy expositor** — pins River Heights social stratification through retail pricing tiers
3. **Mystery deepener** — the Hidelberg's purchases resist easy explanation, sustaining narrative tension
4. **Factual anchor** — verified purchase records provide hard evidence in a case built partly on suspicion
