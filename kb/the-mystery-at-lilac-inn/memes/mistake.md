# Mistake

**Mistake** — an error of fact, judgment, or perception — threads through *The Mystery at Lilac Inn* as both an obstacle to investigation and a revelation of character. Characters make mistakes, deny mistakes, correct mistakes, and exploit mistakes; their responses to error illuminate who they are.

## In the Novel

The most direct confrontation with mistake occurs early in the investigation when Mr. Drew's case files are opened. The manager's instinctive response is denial: "Oh, there must be a mistake" (006-004). His insistence that an error has occurred — rather than that a crime has been committed — reveals an unwillingness to confront the situation directly. But the response is immediate and firm: "There's no mistake. I left it on the table" (006-005). The certainty of the victim contrasts with the manager's evasion, and Nancy must navigate between these competing accounts to establish what actually happened.

Mistakes in the novel are not always innocent. The possibility of being "mistaken" about a person's identity or motives is a recurring anxiety (016-043). Nancy must constantly assess whether she has misjudged someone — whether a friendly face conceals hostile intent, or whether suspicion is unfairly directed. The novel treats premature certainty as the most dangerous kind of mistake, and Nancy's habit of reserving judgment until evidence is clear is presented as professional discipline.

## Mistake and Suspicion

The error of false accusation is a persistent threat in the investigation. Characters warn that it would be a mistake to leap to conclusions about who is responsible for the theft. The novel shows how mistakes compound: one erroneous assumption leads to another, creating chains of misdirection that obscure the truth. Nancy's approach — verifying each fact before building it into a conclusion — is the corrective.

The idea that "there can be no mistake about her buying all of those" items (014-044) appears as a seemingly solid fact in the investigation, yet the very need to assert that there is no mistake signals the fragility of evidence. Every claim must be tested; every assertion of certainty must be questioned.

## Mistake as Character Revelation

How characters respond to the possibility of mistake reveals their nature. Those who refuse to admit error — who insist on their version of events even when evidence contradicts it — are often concealing something. Those who can revise their understanding in light of new facts earn Nancy's trust. The novel draws a moral line not between those who make mistakes and those who do not, but between those who can recognize and correct their errors and those who cannot.

## Key Passages

> "Oh, there must be a mistake," the manager assured her. (006-004)

The manager's reflexive denial of error — his attempt to explain away evidence rather than confront it — marks him as unreliable in Nancy's assessment.

> "There's no mistake. I left it on the table" (006-005)

The victim's firmness in asserting facts provides the counterpoint: some things are not mistakes but deliberate acts.

> She decided that she had been mistaken (016-043)

Nancy's willingness to revise her judgment when evidence warrants it demonstrates the intellectual honesty that defines her investigative character.
