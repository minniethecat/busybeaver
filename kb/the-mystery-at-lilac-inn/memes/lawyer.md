# Lawyer

The **lawyer** — embodied by Carson Drew — serves as the institutional anchor of the investigation in *The Mystery at Lilac Inn*. As "a criminal and mystery-case lawyer" (002-021), Nancy's father provides the legal knowledge and professional connections that complement his daughter's fieldwork.

## Carson Drew, Criminal Lawyer

Carson Drew is introduced as a specialist: not a general practitioner but a lawyer who handles criminal cases and mysteries. This specialization matters — he has the specific expertise needed for the Crandall jewel theft. His work involves:
- Discussing "important cases with detectives and police officials" (002-022)
- Analyzing evidence from a legal perspective
- Advising Nancy on the boundaries of lawful investigation

His professional status opens doors. When Nancy is identified as "your father is a lawyer?" (003-045), it changes how people respond to her questions. The lawyer's daughter inherits some of the lawyer's authority.

## Lawyer and Detective

The relationship between lawyer and detective is one of the novel's key partnerships. Carson Drew and Nancy each have strengths the other lacks:
- **Carson**: Legal knowledge, professional standing, experience with criminal cases
- **Nancy**: Field investigation, social access, persistence in following leads

They work in parallel rather than in sequence — Carson does not wait for Nancy to finish before acting, nor does Nancy wait for her father's permission before pursuing leads. Their collaboration models an ideal working relationship between legal and investigative functions.

## The Law Beyond the Lawyer

The lawyer represents something larger than an individual character: the rule of law itself. In a story about theft, frame-ups, and imposture, the lawyer stands for the proposition that crimes will be prosecuted through legal process rather than vigilante justice. Nancy's investigation serves the law, and the law — through Carson Drew — serves justice.

## Key Passages

> "A criminal and mystery-case lawyer, Carson Drew" (002-021)

The introduction establishes Carson Drew's professional identity — he is not merely Nancy's father but a specialist whose expertise is directly relevant to the case.

> "Your father is a lawyer?" (003-045)

The question — and the reaction it produces — shows how professional identity shapes social interaction. Being a lawyer's daughter opens doors.

> "Discussed important cases with detectives and police officials" (002-022)

The lawyer's network spans the institutions of justice — he connects Nancy's amateur investigation to the official apparatus of law enforcement.
