## Overview

The motorboat is a powerful motorized watercraft that serves as a critical plot vehicle in the river-based action of *The Mystery at Lilac Inn*. Owned and operated by the criminals, it functions as their primary means of transporting stolen goods and escaping along the river near Lilac Inn. It also becomes the vessel through which Nancy Drew and her allies pursue the conspirators, culminating in a high-stakes river chase during a storm.

## Role in the Crime

The motorboat first enters the narrative as an auditory signal of approaching danger. The "faint chug-chug of a motorboat" alerts Nancy to an oncoming vessel heading directly toward the dock [017-052]. She observes that the "motorboat is coming toward this very dock" [017-053], establishing the river as a conduit for clandestine movement. The boat's operator is Tom Tozzle, a confederate in the jewel theft conspiracy, who uses the craft to navigate the waterway with the ease of a seasoned riverman [019-044].

The boat's condition offers clues to the criminals' activities. It is described as a "luxurious motorboat" [018-003], raising the question of how suspects of modest means could afford such a vessel — a detail that deepens Nancy's suspicion. The boat is kept "loaded with gas" and ready for immediate departure [020-050], indicating premeditation and the criminals' expectation of needing a quick escape route.

## The River Chase

When Nancy is taken captive by the conspirators, they move her "unceremoniously into the motorboat" [020-061] and depart down the river. The boat becomes a mobile prison, carrying Nancy away from Lilac Inn toward the remote cabin where she is held.

The motorboat later serves as the vehicle of rescue and pursuit. Once Nancy's allies mobilize, they use the boat to search the river. The chase sequence reaches its climax when "the motorboat rushed downstream" through "a blinding wall of rain" [021-004], with Tom Tozzle at the helm attempting to outrun both the storm and the pursuing authorities.

The river pursuit demonstrates the dual nature of the motorboat in the narrative: it is simultaneously the criminals' tool of escape and the means by which justice closes in on them. The same speed and mobility that enables the theft also enables the pursuit — a symmetry that underscores the novel's theme of crime containing the seeds of its own undoing.

## Strategic Significance

The motorboat represents a critical infrastructural element of the crime at Lilac Inn. The inn's riverside location and dock provide direct access to the waterway, making river transport a natural choice for moving stolen goods discreetly. The boat's presence at the dock is a physical manifestation of the connection between the inn's respectable facade and the criminal operation beneath it.

The craft also serves as a test of character. Tom Tozzle's skill as a riverman is his one area of competence, yet it is ultimately insufficient to save him — the storm and the pursuit converge to overwhelm even his expertise. The motorboat, like the criminals who operate it, is powerful but not invincible.
