## Overview

**Fanaticism** — religious zeal divorced from reason and humanity — is the great antagonist of Voltaire's history. More than any political ambition or economic interest, it is fanaticism that drives the atrocities he chronicles: the massacres, the autos-da-fé, the civil wars fought in the name of rival theologies. Voltaire distinguishes fanaticism from sincere religious belief; the former is not faith but its corruption, a "madness and enthusiasm" that transforms piety into a license for cruelty. (Ch. CXLII)

The concept runs through every major episode of the 16th century in Voltaire's account. The [[french-wars-of-religion|French Wars of Religion]] are in his telling not primarily political conflicts but eruptions of fanaticism — Catholic and Protestant alike — that consumed France for four decades. The [[st-bartholomews-day-massacre|St. Bartholomew's Day massacre]] (1572) stands as the supreme example: "So unhappy were those times, and so strongly did fanaticism or terror possess the minds of the people, that the parliament of Paris decreed an annual procession on St. Bartholomew's day, in thanksgiving to God." (Ch. CXLII)

## Origins and Context

Voltaire locates the sources of fanaticism not in religion itself but in the fusion of religious authority with political power. When the state enforces theological orthodoxy by violence, religion becomes an instrument of persecution. The [[the-inquisition|Inquisition]] institutionalized this fusion, making heresy a capital crime and transforming theological error into a matter for the executioner. (Ch. CXVIII)

The Reformation, while challenging Catholic authority, did not in Voltaire's view extinguish fanaticism — it transferred it. Calvin's Geneva and Catholic Spain both burned heretics; both claimed divine warrant for their violence. What made the 16th century uniquely fanatical was the conviction, shared by all parties, that religious error deserved death. The emergence of voices advocating toleration — the chancellor [[michel-de-lhopital|Michel de l'Hopital]], whom Voltaire calls "that resolute philosopher at a period of madness and enthusiasm" — marks the beginning of the turn against fanaticism. (Ch. CXLII)

## Historical Development

The [[french-wars-of-religion|French Wars of Religion]] (1562–1598) provide Voltaire with his most sustained case study of fanaticism in action. The conflict was not, he insists, a simple struggle between tolerance and intolerance; both sides were capable of fanaticism. The Catholic [[catholic-league|League]] and the Protestant [[huguenot|Huguenots]] each claimed divine sanction for violence. What distinguished the wars was not the justice of either cause but the intensity of the conviction that animated both.

Amid the fanaticism, one figure stands out in Voltaire's narrative: the chancellor [[michel-de-lhopital|Michel de l'Hopital]] — "that great legislator at a time when laws were wanting, that resolute philosopher at a period of madness and enthusiasm." When the prince of Condé was condemned to death, de l'Hopital refused to sign the sentence. He was among "the wisest heads" who believed the Protestants could be kept within bounds through moderation rather than war. His reforms separating the professions of sword and robe reshaped the French judiciary. (Ch. CXLII)

The [[auto-da-fe|auto-da-fé]] represented fanaticism in its most theatrical form — a public ceremony in which the state and church collaborated to destroy those who would not conform. Voltaire's description emphasizes the ceremony's deliberate confusion of categories: piety and murder, justice and revenge, festival and execution, all fused into a single spectacle designed to terrify and to edify simultaneously. (Ch. CXVIII)

The [[martyrdom|martyrs]] produced by persecution were the mirror image of fanaticism. Voltaire treats martyrdom with characteristic double vision: he recognizes the sincerity of many who died, but he cannot refrain from observing how often the concept was exploited to sanctify political violence or to transform fanaticism into a form of heroism. Both Catholics and Protestants produced martyrs; both sides used martyrdom narratives to mobilize support and demonize opponents. (Ch. CXVI, CXLII)

## Impact and Legacy

The critique of fanaticism is the moral center of Voltaire's historical project. His ''Essay on Universal History'' is, in significant measure, an argument against the fanaticism that he saw as the chief obstacle to human progress. The 16th century, in his rendering, is a cautionary tale: it shows what happens when religious conviction is armed with state power and freed from the restraints of reason and humanity.

The legacy of this critique extended far beyond Voltaire. The Enlightenment's case for toleration — for the separation of church and state, for freedom of conscience, for the subordination of religious enthusiasm to civil peace — was built on the historical evidence he assembled. When Voltaire has de l'Hopital write ''excidat ille dies'' — let that day perish — he is not merely reporting a historical sentiment; he is issuing a moral verdict on fanaticism itself. (Ch. CXLII)
