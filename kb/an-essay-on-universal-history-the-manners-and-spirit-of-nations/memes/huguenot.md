## Overview

The **Huguenots** were French Protestants who adhered to the Reformed confession associated with [[john-calvin|John Calvin]]. Emerging in the 1550s, they grew rapidly among the nobility, the urban bourgeoisie, and the literate classes, becoming a political as well as religious force that challenged the Catholic monarchy of the Valois dynasty. The resulting French Wars of Religion (1562–1598) were among the bloodiest confessional conflicts of the [[the-reformation|Reformation]] era, ending only with the [[edict-of-nantes|Edict of Nantes]], which granted the Huguenots limited toleration. (Ch. CXVI)

## Origins

Calvinism entered France through Geneva. [[john-calvin|Calvin]], himself a Frenchman exiled to Geneva, maintained close ties with his homeland, training pastors and dispatching them to establish Reformed congregations in France. By 1561, there were over 2,000 Reformed congregations across the kingdom, concentrated in the south and west (Languedoc, Guyenne, Poitou) and among the urban populations of Paris, Lyon, and Orléans.

The movement attracted converts across the social spectrum: nobles who resented the centralizing policies of the crown, merchants and artisans drawn to Calvin's theology of vocation, and literate laypeople who embraced the principle of vernacular scripture. The Huguenot nobility, led by the House of Bourbon (princes of the blood) and Admiral [[coligny|Gaspard de Coligny]], provided the movement with political and military leadership. (Ch. CXVI)

## The Wars of Religion

| Phase | Years | Key Events |
|-------|-------|------------|
| First War | 1562–63 | Massacre of Vassy; Edict of Amboise (limited toleration) |
| Second–Third Wars | 1567–70 | Peace of Saint-Germain (wider toleration, four security towns) |
| St. Bartholomew's Day | 1572 | Massacre of thousands of Huguenots in Paris and the provinces |
| Fourth–Eighth Wars | 1572–98 | Continued conflict; succession crisis; Henry of Navarre's conversion |

The conflict was ignited by the Massacre of Vassy (1562), when the Duke of Guise's men attacked a Huguenot congregation worshipping in a barn. The wars that followed were characterized by their peculiar ferocity — sieges, assassinations, and atrocities on both sides — and by the intervention of foreign powers: [[elizabeth-i|Elizabeth I]] of England supporting the Huguenots, [[philip-ii|Philip II]] of Spain backing the Catholic League. (Ch. CXVI)

> The state of religion in France under Francis I and his successors was one of the most violent and bloody scenes that ever desolated any part of the world. (Ch. CXVI)

## Voltaire's Analysis

Voltaire treats the persecution of the Huguenots as one of the great tragedies of French history — a catastrophe born of religious fanaticism that needlessly weakened the kingdom, drove out industrious citizens, and stained the monarchy with acts of barbarism. The St. Bartholomew's Day Massacre (1572) receives particular condemnation as an act of state-sanctioned murder that violated every principle of civilized governance.

He contrasts the intransigence of the Catholic League — which sought the total extirpation of heresy — with the politique position, exemplified by Henry of Navarre (later [[henry-iv-of-france|Henry IV]]), which prioritized civil peace over confessional uniformity. Henry IV's conversion to Catholicism ("Paris is well worth a mass") and his promulgation of the Edict of Nantes (1598) represented, in Voltaire's view, the triumph of political reason over religious fanaticism. (Ch. CXVI–CXVII)

## Impact and Legacy

The Edict of Nantes granted the Huguenots freedom of conscience, the right to worship in specified locations, access to public office, and control of several security towns. It was the first major edict of religious toleration in modern Europe — though Voltaire notes that it was a pragmatic political settlement, not a declaration of principle.

The Revocation of the Edict of Nantes by [[louis-xiv|Louis XIV]] in 1685 — which Voltaire discusses elsewhere with indignation — drove hundreds of thousands of Huguenots into exile, enriching the Protestant powers (Prussia, the Netherlands, England) at France's expense. The Huguenot diaspora became a powerful symbol of the costs of intolerance. (Ch. CXVI–CXVII)
