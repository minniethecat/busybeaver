## Overview

**Confession** — more precisely, the Sacrament of Penance, of which confession forms the central act — was one of the seven sacraments of the medieval [[catholicism|Catholic Church]]. The penitent was required to confess all mortal sins to a priest, express contrition, and perform the assigned satisfaction (penance), after which the priest pronounced [[absolution|absolution]]. This sacrament became a focal point of [[the-reformation|Reformation]] critique, attacked both for its theological foundations and for the abuses — particularly the sale of [[indulgences|indulgences]] — that had grown up around it. (Ch. CVI, CIX)

## Medieval Practice

By the later Middle Ages, auricular (private) confession to a priest had been mandatory for all Latin Christians since the Fourth Lateran Council (1215), which required annual confession as a minimum obligation. The theology underpinning the practice drew on Christ's words to the apostles ("Whose sins you shall forgive, they are forgiven") and on the developing doctrine of sacerdotal power.

The practice generated an extensive pastoral literature — manuals for confessors, catalogues of sins with corresponding penances, and treatises on casuistry (the resolution of cases of conscience). The confessional became a site of moral discipline and social control as much as spiritual consolation. (Ch. CIX)

| Confession | Oral enumeration of all mortal sins to a priest |

## Reformation Critique

The Reformers attacked the sacrament of penance on multiple grounds:

**Scriptural**: [[martin-luther|Luther]] argued that auricular confession lacked explicit biblical warrant. While he retained a form of private confession as a pastoral practice, he denied its status as a sacrament instituted by Christ, reducing the number of sacraments from seven to two ([[baptism|baptism]] and the [[eucharist|eucharist]]). (Ch. CVI)

**Theological**: The Reformers rejected the doctrine that priestly absolution effected forgiveness _ex opere operato_ (by the performance of the act itself). They insisted that forgiveness was granted directly by God through faith, not mediated through priestly authority.

**Practical**: The system of [[indulgences|indulgences]] — the remission of temporal punishment for sin, often obtained through financial contribution — had become inextricably linked with confession. Luther's Ninety-Five Theses (1517) were originally a protest against the abuse of indulgences, and his critique quickly extended to the sacrament of which they were a part. (Ch. CVI)

> Of Leo X and of the church — it was the sale of indulgences that first provoked Luther's opposition. (Ch. CVI)

## Voltaire's Treatment

Voltaire's treatment of confession is marked by his characteristic skepticism toward priestly power. He presents the sacrament as a mechanism by which the clergy exercised intimate control over the laity — knowing their secrets, directing their conduct, and extracting material contributions. The confessional, in his ironic narration, was as much an instrument of ecclesiastical dominion as of spiritual consolation.

The Reformation's abolition of mandatory auricular confession is presented as a liberation of conscience — one of the unintended benefits of a movement Voltaire otherwise regards as having replaced one form of theological tyranny with another. The Council of Trent (1545–63) reaffirmed the Catholic doctrine of penance, ensuring that confession would remain a defining difference between the confessions. (Ch. CVI, CIX)

## Impact and Legacy

The confessional divide over penance hardened into one of the lasting distinctions between Catholic and Protestant practice. In Catholic Europe, the confessional remained a central feature of religious life, its discipline reinforced by the Counter-Reformation. In Protestant territories, private confession largely disappeared or was transformed into a general confession recited by the congregation during worship.

The broader cultural legacy of the confessional — the practice of intimate self-examination and verbal disclosure — extended beyond religion into literature, philosophy, and eventually secular psychotherapy. (Ch. CIX)
