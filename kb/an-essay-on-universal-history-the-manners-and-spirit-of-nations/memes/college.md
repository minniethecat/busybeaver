## Overview

College — from the Latin *collegium*, a body of colleagues — referred in the early modern period both to the ecclesiastical body of cardinals that governed the Roman Church and elected its pope, and to the educational institutions that trained clergy and laity alike. The Sacred College of Cardinals was among the most powerful institutions in Europe, a senatorial body whose twenty-four members during the pontificate of Leo X (Ch. CXII) collectively controlled the papacy's spiritual and temporal authority. Voltaire observes that "the four and twenty cardinals who at that time composed the whole college" (Ch. CXII) were the arbiters of papal succession and, through it, of much of Italian and European politics.

## Origins and Context

The College of Cardinals emerged from the clergy of the Roman diocese in the early Middle Ages, gradually acquiring the exclusive right to elect the pope. By the sixteenth century, the college had become a body of immense political significance. Its members were drawn from the leading families of Italy and, increasingly, from the service of the great Catholic monarchs — France, Spain, and the Holy Roman Empire. The cardinals served simultaneously as princes of the church, papal ministers, and representatives of secular powers.

The educational college — the seminary or university college — followed a different trajectory. The Council of [[trent]] mandated the establishment of seminaries in every diocese for the training of clergy, responding to the Protestant critique that Catholic priests were ignorant and ill-prepared. Jesuit colleges, founded across Europe and as far afield as Goa and Macau, became the standard-bearers of Catholic education.

## Key Features

The institution of "college" in Voltaire's world encompassed several distinct forms:

| College Type | Function | Key Example |
|-------------|---------|-------------|
| **Sacred College of Cardinals** | Papal election and governance | The 24 cardinals under Leo X |
| **University College** | Higher education, theological training | The [[Sorbonne]] in Paris |
| **Jesuit College** | Secondary education, missionary training | Colleges in Rome, Goa, Macau |
| **Seminary (Tridentine)** | Clergy formation and discipline | Diocesan seminaries post-Trent |

> Henry VIII, having dissolved the monasteries, "founded six new bishoprics, and a college" from the spoils of the dissolved religious houses (Ch. CXIII). The royal college served a double purpose: to provide for the education of a reformed English clergy, and to demonstrate that the king, not the pope, was the proper patron of learning.

## Historical Development

The College of Cardinals reached the height of its political influence during the Renaissance papacy. Under Alexander VI and Julius II, the college was as much a military and diplomatic body as an ecclesiastical one. The election of Leo X in 1513 brought to the papal throne a Medici cardinal who "made his court the center of polite learning" (Ch. CVI) while the German Reformation erupted beyond the Alps.

Voltaire notes the paradoxical status of cardinals as churchmen who wielded secular power: "in Spain I behold a Ximenes under Isabella, who after the death of the queen is regent of the kingdom; who struts in a cordelier's habit... who raises an army at his own expense" (Ch. C). Cardinal Wolsey in England, Cardinal Granvelle in Flanders, and Cardinal Duprat in France all served as chief ministers to their respective monarchs.

The educational college underwent a dramatic expansion during the Catholic Reformation. The Jesuits, founded in 1540, made the college their primary instrument of influence, establishing a network of schools that educated the sons of Catholic elites across Europe. These colleges inculcated humanistic learning alongside Counter-Reformation orthodoxy.

## Impact and Legacy

The Sacred College of Cardinals survives as the electoral body of the papacy, though its political power has diminished. The educational college, meanwhile, became one of the most enduring legacies of the Catholic Reformation. The seminary system mandated by Trent professionalized the Catholic clergy, raising educational standards and creating a more disciplined ecclesiastical hierarchy.

Voltaire's treatment of the college reflects his ambivalence toward ecclesiastical institutions. He admires the learning that colleges fostered while deploring the political ambition that corrupted them. The cardinals who "disputed the point of precedency with the electors of Germany" (Ch. CXII) represented, in his view, the confusion of spiritual and temporal authority that the Reformation sought to remedy.
