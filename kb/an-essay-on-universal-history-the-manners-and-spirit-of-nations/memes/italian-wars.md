## Overview

The Italian Wars (1494–1559) were the great crucible of early modern European statecraft — a sixty-five-year contest that drew in every major power, revolutionized military technology, and produced the modern system of diplomatic equilibrium. Voltaire treats these wars as the narrative backbone of his account of the 16th century, structuring entire chapters around the campaigns of [[charles-v|Charles V]] and Francis I. The wars transformed Italy from the cultural center of Europe into a contested prize, while simultaneously giving birth to the concept of the balance of power.

## Origins: The French Invasion of 1494

| Phase | Years | Key Events | Outcome |
|-------|-------|------------|---------|
| First Italian War | 1494–1498 | Charles VIII invades Naples | French expelled; League of Venice formed |
| Second Italian War | 1499–1504 | Louis XII seizes Milan; contest for Naples | French retain Milan; Spain gains Naples |
| War of the League of Cambrai | 1508–1516 | Anti-Venetian coalition; French victory at Marignano | Francis I recovers Milan; Concordat of Bologna |
| Habsburg-Valois Wars | 1521–1559 | Four wars between Charles V and Francis I | Habsburg dominance in Italy confirmed |

The wars began when Charles VIII of France invaded Italy in 1494 to press a dynastic claim to the kingdom of Naples. His army, equipped with the first mobile artillery train seen in Italy, swept through the peninsula with shocking speed — inaugurating an era in which gunpowder weapons rendered medieval fortifications obsolete. Though Charles was eventually expelled, the French crown returned repeatedly under his successors Louis XII and Francis I.

## Francis I, Charles V, and the Struggle for Milan

The central drama of the Italian Wars was the personal rivalry between Francis I of France and Charles V of Habsburg, who competed for both the imperial crown and control of Italy. Voltaire frames their contest as the defining event of the age:

> Francis I, king of France, and Charles of Austria, king of Spain... stood candidates for the Imperial dignity, at a time when Germany, menaced by the Turks, had need of such a chief as Francis I, or Charles of Austria. There had been no instance before of such potent kings making interest for the Imperial diadem. (Ch. CI)

The duchy of Milan was the great prize. "His business was to conquer the dutchy of Milan, which had been lost by Lewis XII, and to wrest it once more from the unhappy family of Sforza." (Ch. CI) Francis I's victory at Marignano (1515) established him as a warrior-king, but his defeat and capture at the [[battle-of-pavia|Battle of Pavia]] (1525) shattered French power in Italy and inaugurated Habsburg preponderance.

## The Emergence of the Balance of Power

Voltaire identifies the Italian Wars as the crucible in which the modern European states-system was forged. After Pavia, the other powers — England, the Papacy, [[venice]] — coalesced against Charles V to prevent any single power from dominating the continent:

> The system of an equilibrium of power was at this very period established in Europe; for immediately after the taking of Francis I, England and the Italian states entered into a league with France to balance the power of the emperor. (Ch. CIII)

This recognition — that the balance of power emerged as a conscious policy during the Italian Wars — is one of Voltaire's most important analytical contributions. The wars taught European statesmen that no single power could be permitted to absorb Italy, and the resulting system of alliances and counter-alliances became the operating logic of European diplomacy for the next three centuries.

## Military Revolution and the Trace Italienne

The Italian Wars revolutionized warfare. The vulnerability of tall medieval walls to cannon fire produced the *trace italienne* — a low, thick, angled [[fortification]] system with bastions that deflected shot and provided interlocking fields of fire. The new fortifications made sieges protracted and expensive, shifting the advantage from the attacker to the defender. The sack of Rome (1527) by Charles V's unpaid troops demonstrated the brutal reality of early modern warfare — armies that were not paid lived off the land and punished cities that resisted.

## Aftermath: The Peace of Cateau-Cambrésis

The wars ended with the Peace of Cateau-Cambrésis (1559), which confirmed Spanish dominance in Italy. France renounced its claims to Milan, Naples, and Sicily, retaining only a few footholds. The settlement endured for over a century, and Italy entered a long period of Spanish hegemony that would not be broken until the War of the Spanish Succession (1701–1714). Yet the deeper legacy was diplomatic: the Italian Wars taught Europe that no power could achieve universal monarchy, and that the equilibrium Voltaire described would henceforth be the organizing principle of international politics.
