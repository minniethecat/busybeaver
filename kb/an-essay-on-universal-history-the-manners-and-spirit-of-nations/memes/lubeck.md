## Overview

Lübeck was the foremost city of the Hanseatic League and one of the most consequential urban centers of late medieval and early modern northern Europe. Its commercial wealth, naval power, and political independence allowed it to act as a quasi-sovereign entity — waging war, financing princes, and shaping the balance of power in Scandinavia and the Baltic. Voltaire devotes sustained attention to Lübeck's role, observing that "the Hanse towns alone, Hamburg, Lubeck, Dantzick, Rostock, Luneburg, Wismar, were able to resist those three kingdoms, because they had more wealth." (Ch. XCVIII)

## The Hanseatic Powerhouse

Founded in 1143 by Henry the Lion and granted imperial free city status in 1226, Lübeck became the de facto capital of the Hanseatic League — a commercial and defensive confederation of merchant towns that dominated Baltic and North Sea trade. The city's strategic position on the Trave River, connecting the Baltic to the inland trade routes of Germany, made it the natural hub between eastern raw materials and western manufactured goods.

> The Hanse towns alone, Hamburg, Lubeck, Dantzick, Rostock, Luneburg, Wismar, were able to resist those three kingdoms, because they had more wealth. Even the city of Lubeck waged war by itself against the successors of Margaret of Waldemar. (Ch. XCVIII)

## Lübeck and the Swedish War of Liberation

Lübeck's most decisive intervention in northern politics came during the Swedish War of Liberation (1521–1523). When [[gustavus-vasa|Gustavus Vasa]] rose against Christian II of Denmark, he was a fugitive peasant leader with no resources. Lübeck transformed his fortunes:

> Gustavus having purchased some muskets at Lubeck upon his own credit, his people learned to fight upon equal terms. Lubeck not only furnished him with arms, but likewise with troops, without which he would have found it very difficult to succeed. Thus the fate of Sweden depended on a little trading town. (Ch. XCVIII)

The city's investment paid dividends: Gustavus Vasa's victory established the independent Vasa dynasty in Sweden, and Lübeck secured favorable trading privileges. Voltaire's observation that "thus the fate of Sweden depended on a little trading town" captures the paradox of Hanseatic power — a merchant republic determining the destiny of a kingdom through commercial credit and arms supply.

## Reformation and Ecclesiastical Peculiarity

Lübeck adopted Lutheranism early in the Reformation, but unlike most Protestant territories it preserved the wealth and structure of its bishopric. Voltaire notes this as an exceptional case:

> Lubeck and Osnabrug are the only rich protestant bishoprics in Germany, whose revenues have not been secularized. (Ch. CXII)

While Protestant princes across Germany seized church lands, Lübeck's bishopric retained its endowments — a reflection of the city's political autonomy and the complexity of Reformation-era property settlements. The Treaty of Westphalia (1648) later formalized this arrangement.

## Decline of Hanseatic Power

The rise of territorial states and the discovery of transatlantic trade routes gradually eroded Hanseatic dominance. The Dutch Republic's commercial revolution and Sweden's emergence as a Baltic empire marginalized the league. Lübeck retained its status as a free imperial city until 1937, but its era as "the little trading town" that decided the fate of kingdoms had passed by the mid-17th century. The last Hanseatic Diet met in 1669, and the league effectively dissolved.
