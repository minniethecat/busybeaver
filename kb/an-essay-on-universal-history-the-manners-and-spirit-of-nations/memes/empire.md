## Overview

The concept of empire in sixteenth-century Europe encompassed both a specific institution — the Holy Roman Empire, whose elected emperor claimed precedence over all Christian princes — and a broader claim to universal authority. Voltaire's *Essay* examines empire as a political form through the reign of [[charles-v]], whose vast and scattered dominions — Spain, the Netherlands, Naples, the Austrian lands, and the imperial title — raised the question of whether any single ruler could or should dominate Europe.

## Origins and Context

The Holy Roman Empire traced its origins to Charlemagne's coronation in 800, but by the sixteenth century it had evolved into a complex federation of hundreds of semi-sovereign territories — prince-bishoprics, duchies, counties, and free imperial cities — under the nominal authority of an elected emperor. The emperor's power depended less on imperial institutions than on his hereditary lands.

As Voltaire notes, "The emperor has nothing at all; he is at his own expence, the head of the Christian world, *caput orbis Christiani*" (Ch. XCVIII) — a striking contrast with the king of England, who received "about twenty three millions of livres" for his civil list. The empire's weakness as an institution was paradoxically the source of its durability.

## Key Features

The imperial concept in Voltaire's account has several dimensions:

| Dimension | Description | Chapter Source |
|-----------|-------------|----------------|
| **Holy Roman Empire** | Elective monarchy over German and Italian territories | Ch. XCVIII–XCIX |
| **Universal Jurisdiction** | The emperor's claim to authority over all Christian rulers | Ch. XCVIII |
| **Ottoman Empire** | The rival Muslim empire under Suleiman | Ch. XCVIII, CXXX |
| **Russian Empire** | The emerging power of Muscovy under the Tsars | Ch. XCVIII |
| **Charles V's Composite Monarchy** | Personal union of Spain, Burgundy, Austria, and the Empire | Ch. CI–CIII |

> Voltaire observes that "the German emperors, as well as the popes, have always pretended to universal jurisdiction. It appears also that the king of Denmark wanted to flatter Maximilian, whose daughter he obtained for his son Christian II. This is the way that rights are established" (Ch. XCVIII).

## Historical Development

Charles V's reign (1519–1556) represented the apogee of imperial ambition in early modern Europe. His possessions spanned four continents, and his election as emperor in 1519 — defeating Francis I of France — made him the titular head of Christendom. Voltaire devotes an entire chapter to the question "whether Charles V aimed at universal monarchy" (Ch. CIII).

The imperial project faced structural limitations. The German princes guarded their liberties; the Protestant Reformation fractured religious unity; France, though Catholic, consistently opposed Habsburg hegemony; and the Ottoman Empire pressed from the east. Voltaire's narrative makes clear that the empire was never as powerful as it appeared — the very diversity that gave it theoretical universality made it practically ungovernable.

The Ottoman Empire provided a mirror image: a non-Christian empire that rivaled and often surpassed the Christian one in military effectiveness. Voltaire describes Suleiman's conquests and the Ottoman administrative system with a comparative eye, noting that empires took different institutional forms while sharing certain structural features — notably the concentration of military and fiscal power in the sovereign's hands.

In the east, the emerging Russian Empire under Ivan IV (John Basilowitz) represented a different imperial model: an autocratic monarchy built on conquest, where the Tsar "engrossed almost the whole trade of the empire to themselves" (Ch. XCVIII).

## Impact and Legacy

Voltaire's treatment of empire is characteristically skeptical of claims to universal authority. He notes wryly that the imperial chancery "wrote to the Swedes, just as that of Charlemain would have wrote to the people of Benevento or Guienne. But they wanted the armies and the power of Charlemain" (Ch. XCVIII). The gap between imperial pretension and actual power is a recurring theme.

The question of whether Charles V sought universal monarchy is left deliberately open — Voltaire presents the arguments on both sides without resolving them. What is clear is that the imperial idea, though weakened, survived Charles's abdication. The Habsburg dynasty continued to hold the imperial title, and the concept of empire as a form of composite monarchy remained relevant through the early modern period.

## Key Figures

- **[[charles-v]]** — Holy Roman Emperor (1519–1556), whose reign defined imperial politics in the Reformation era
- **Maximilian I** — Charles's grandfather, who "formed a scheme to make himself pope" (Ch. CI)
- **Francis I** — King of France, Charles's great rival for the imperial title
- **Suleiman the Magnificent** — Ottoman sultan whose empire pressed against Christendom
- **Ivan IV (John Basilowitz)** — Tsar who expanded the Russian Empire
