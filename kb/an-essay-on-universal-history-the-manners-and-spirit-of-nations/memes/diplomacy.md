## Overview

**Diplomacy** in the 16th century was the principal means by which the emerging sovereign states of Europe managed their rivalries, negotiated alliances, and concluded treaties without resorting to war. Voltaire's narrative of the age of [[charles-v|Charles V]] and [[francis-i|Francis I]] is saturated with diplomatic maneuvering — interviews between monarchs, missions by ambassadors, treaties of alliance, and the intricate negotiations that shaped the balance of power. The period witnessed the transition from medieval forms of ad hoc representation to the system of permanent resident ambassadors that would define modern European statecraft. (Ch. XCVIII, CI–CIV)

## Origins and Development

The practice of sending envoys to foreign courts is ancient, but permanent diplomatic representation emerged in the Italian city-states of the 15th century — notably Venice, Milan, and Florence — before spreading to the great monarchies of the north. By the time of the [[the-reformation|Reformation]], the major European powers maintained resident ambassadors at each other's courts as a matter of course.

Voltaire describes the Russian grand duke's ceremonial submission to the Tartar ambassador — prostrating himself and offering milk — as an example of the older, more ritualized form of diplomatic encounter that still persisted on Europe's eastern margins even as the western powers adopted more sophisticated practices. The contrast illuminates the uneven development of diplomatic norms across the continent. (Ch. XCVIII)

| Period | Diplomatic Form | Characteristics |
|--------|----------------|-----------------|
| Medieval | Ad hoc envoys | Sent for specific missions; no permanent presence |
| 15th c. Italian | Resident ambassadors | Permanent missions; written dispatches; balance-of-power logic |
| 16th c. European | Professional diplomacy | Accredited ambassadors; immunity; ceremonial precedence |

## Key Features

The diplomacy of the Reformation era was shaped by several distinctive features:

**Dynastic character**: Foreign policy remained the personal prerogative of the sovereign. The famous interview between [[francis-i|Francis I]] and [[charles-v|Charles V]] — and their subsequent quarrels — exemplified how personal relations between monarchs directly determined war and peace.

**Religious dimension**: The religious schism introduced by [[lutheranism|Lutheranism]] and [[calvinism|Calvinism]] complicated traditional alliance patterns. Catholic powers might ally with Protestant states when political interest demanded it, as Francis I did with the German Protestant princes and even the Ottoman sultan [[suleiman-the-magnificent|Suleiman]].

**Commercial interests**: Treaties increasingly addressed trade and navigation alongside dynastic and territorial questions, reflecting the growing importance of [[commerce|commerce]] in state policy. (Ch. CIV)

> The alliance between the king of France and sultan Solyman produced another war. (Ch. CIV)

## Voltaire's Perspective

Voltaire treats diplomacy with characteristic irony, frequently exposing the gap between its elevated language and the baser motives of vanity, greed, and personal pique that drove it. The alliance between the Most Christian King and the Ottoman sultan scandalized Christendom but demonstrated, in Voltaire's telling, that raison d'état trumped religious solidarity. This was, in his view, a sign of advancing civilization — the subordination of religious fanaticism to political calculation.

The congresses and treaties that punctuate the narrative serve Voltaire as evidence that Europe was slowly learning to resolve disputes through negotiation rather than endless religious warfare. (Ch. CIV, CXIX)

## Impact and Legacy

The diplomatic system that crystallized in the 16th century — resident ambassadors, written instructions, regular dispatches, and the beginnings of international law — provided the institutional framework within which the modern European states system operated. The principle that ambassadors enjoyed immunity and that treaties bound their signatories regardless of religious allegiance represented a crucial step toward a secular international order.

The [[concordat|concordats]] negotiated between the papacy and secular rulers exemplified the diplomatic approach to resolving church-state conflicts that Voltaire admired — pragmatic compromise rather than doctrinal absolutism. (Ch. CXVI)
