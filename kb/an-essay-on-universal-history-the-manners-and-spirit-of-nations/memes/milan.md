## Overview

Milan in Voltaire's Essay on Universal History is less a city than a chess piece — the duchy whose possession determined the balance of power between [[charles-v|Charles V]] and [[francis-i|Francis I]] for a generation. Its strategic location in Lombardy, its wealth, and its contested succession made it the focal point of the Italian Wars. Voltaire treats the struggle for Milan as the paradigmatic instance of early modern dynastic politics: a contest waged for territory but driven by honor, and conducted at a cost wildly disproportionate to any material gain.

The investiture of the Milanese became a symbol of imperial authority itself. When Charles V "grants to Francis Sforza the investiture of the Milanese... but all this he does for money" (Ch. CIII), Voltaire's dry observation captures the transactional character of Habsburg hegemony. Milan was not won by right or conquest but purchased — the ultimate commodity in the imperial patronage system.

## Origins and Context

Milan's importance derived from its geographical position commanding the Alpine passes between France and Italy, and from its economic wealth as one of Europe's principal manufacturing and trading centers. Under the Visconti dynasty (1277–1447) and then the Sforza (1450–1535), Milan developed into a Renaissance state of the first rank, its court rivaling those of [[florence|Florence]] and [[venice|Venice]] in cultural magnificence.

The death of the last Visconti duke in 1447 without a direct heir created a succession crisis that drew in the French crown — the House of Orléans claimed descent from the Visconti through a female line — and set the stage for the Italian Wars that would dominate the first half of the sixteenth century. Francis I's victory at Marignano in 1515 briefly restored French control, only to be decisively reversed at the [[battle-of-pavia|Battle of Pavia]] in 1525.

## Key Features

Voltaire identifies several factors that made the Milanese such a contested prize:

| Factor | Description | Consequence |
|--------|-------------|-------------|
| **Strategic location** | Commanded the Alpine passes between France and Italy | Essential for any power seeking Italian hegemony |
| **Economic wealth** | Major center of manufacturing, trade, and agriculture | Made the duchy financially self-sustaining |
| **Contested succession** | Visconti-Sforza line disputed by French and Imperial claimants | Provided legal pretext for military intervention |
| **Imperial fief** | Formally part of the Holy Roman Empire | Gave the emperor the right to grant investiture |
| **Papal interest** | Popes sought to prevent any single power from dominating northern Italy | Drew the papacy into shifting anti-French and anti-Imperial alliances |

> "He grants to Francis Sforza the investiture of the Milanese, and to Alexander of Medicis that of Tuscany; he names a duke of Mantua; he makes the pope restore Modena and Reggio to the duke of Ferrara: but all this he does for money." (Ch. CIII)

## Historical Development

The French claim to Milan was the original spark of the Italian Wars. Charles VIII's invasion of 1494 was aimed at Naples, but Louis XII's campaign of 1499–1500 targeted Milan directly, and Francis I's victory at Marignano in 1515 seemed to have secured the duchy for France. Voltaire presents Francis as the archetype of Renaissance kingship at this moment — young, victorious, celebrated throughout Europe — and Milan as the symbol of his Italian ambitions.

The [[battle-of-pavia|Battle of Pavia]] (24 February 1525) shattered those ambitions. The imperial army defeated the French forces, took Francis I prisoner, and demonstrated "the ascendancy of the Habsburg monarchy under Charles V." Fought near Pavia "in the Duchy of Milan," the battle marked the collapse of French power in Italy. "Francis I penetrated into the Milanese with a numerous army... Pope Clement VII, fearing the emperor's growing power, openly espoused the French cause. Yet the French were defeated" (Ch. CII–CIII).

The aftermath saw Milan pass definitively into the Habsburg sphere. Francis Sforza, the last Sforza duke, ruled as a Habsburg client until his death in 1535, after which the duchy was absorbed directly into Charles V's patrimony and eventually passed to the Spanish branch of the Habsburgs. The city that had been the prize of kings became a provincial capital of a global empire.

## Impact and Legacy

Milan's fate in Voltaire's narrative illustrates a broader pattern: the absorption of the independent Italian states into the great monarchies of the north. What the Visconti and Sforza had built — a prosperous, culturally brilliant Renaissance duchy — proved unable to defend itself against the fiscal-military power of the emerging nation-states. The same pattern played out in varying degrees across the peninsula.

Yet Milan was not simply a victim. Its economic vitality survived political subordination, and its strategic importance ensured that it remained a prize worth fighting over well into the eighteenth century. Voltaire's treatment accords Milan a certain dignity even in defeat: it was not weakness that lost the duchy but the overwhelming concentration of power in the hands of the Habsburg-Valois rivals.
