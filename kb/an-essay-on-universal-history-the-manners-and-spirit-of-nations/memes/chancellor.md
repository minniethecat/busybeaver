## Overview

The **Chancellor** was one of the most powerful officers of state in medieval and early modern European monarchies. As keeper of the royal seal, the chancellor authenticated all official acts of the crown; as the monarch's principal legal advisor, he shaped legislation and oversaw the judiciary. Voltaire's narrative draws attention to the chancellor's role as a civilizing force — a legal and administrative counterweight to the military nobility and the ecclesiastical hierarchy — particularly in the context of the religious and political crises of the sixteenth century. (Ch. C, CXVI)

## Functions and Authority

The chancellor's office combined executive, judicial, and legislative functions. In France, the chancellor presided over the king's council when the monarch was absent, oversaw the parlements (sovereign courts), and was in charge of the royal chancery that drafted and sealed all ordinances, edicts, and letters patent. In England, the lord chancellor was the keeper of the Great Seal, the presiding officer of the House of Lords, and the head of the judiciary. (Ch. C)

The office was typically held for life, which gave chancellors a degree of independence from the political winds that buffeted other ministers. This security of tenure could produce figures of extraordinary integrity — as with the celebrated French chancellor Michel de l'Hopital — or of extraordinary ambition, as with Cardinal Wolsey in England. (Ch. CXIII)

## Voltaire's Chancellors

Voltaire pays particular attention to the French chancellors of the Reformation era. Cardinal Duprat, chancellor under Francis I, concluded the Concordat of Bologna with Pope Leo X — an agreement Voltaire describes as one in which "the king and the pope gave to each other what did not belong to them." The king obtained nomination to benefices; the pope received the first fruits (annates), perpetuating a flow of French money to Rome that would fuel anti-papal sentiment. (Ch. CXVI)

The most admirable figure in Voltaire's account is Michel de l'Hopital, chancellor under Charles IX during the most violent phase of the [[french-wars-of-religion|French Wars of Religion]]. When the prince of Conde was condemned to death by a special commission in 1560, de l'Hopital "refused to sign the sentence" — an act of moral courage in a period when refusal could be fatal. Voltaire calls him "that great legislator at a time when laws were wanting, that resolute philosopher at a period of madness and enthusiasm." (Ch. CXLII)

De l'Hopital was also the architect of the reform that separated the nobility of the sword from the judiciary, settling the administration of justice "entirely on their deputies of the long robe." Voltaire sees this as a pivotal constitutional development — one that "contributed to the strength of the sovereign, by dividing two professions, which in conjunction might have balanced the power of the ministry." (Ch. CXLII)

## Precedence and Symbolic Status

Voltaire notes a curious detail about the social standing of the chancellor: "in France and England they yielded the pre-eminence to the chancellors" over cardinals, even though cardinals "disputed the point of precedency with the electors of Germany." This was, Voltaire observes, "one of the many contradictions that human pride had introduced into the republic of Christendom" — a reminder that the chancellor's authority rested not on ecclesiastical rank but on the secular power of the crown and the law. (Ch. C)
