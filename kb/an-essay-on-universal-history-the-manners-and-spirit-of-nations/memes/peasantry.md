## Overview

The peasantry formed the demographic foundation of early modern Europe — the vast majority of the population whose agricultural labor sustained the entire social and political order. Voltaire's narrative, though focused on kings, popes, and battles, repeatedly returns to the condition of the common people as a measure of the justice or injustice of political systems. His treatment of the peasantry is marked by a characteristic indignation at the extremes of noble privilege, particularly in Eastern Europe.

The Hungarian peasantry receives Voltaire's most sustained attention. The nobility "were petty tyrants, who would not be tyrannized themselves. Their liberty was a fatal independency, and they had reduced the rest of the nation to so miserable a servitude, that the peasants rose up in a body against their masters." (Ch. XCVIII)

In Western Europe — particularly in France under [[louis-xii|Louis XII]] and [[francis-i|Francis I]] — royal authority had progressively curtailed the most extreme noble privileges over peasants. In Eastern Europe, by contrast, the absence of strong central monarchy permitted noble domination to expand unchecked. This divergence in peasant status became one of the defining differences between Western and Eastern European social development.

## The Hungarian Peasant Rising

Voltaire's account of the Hungarian peasant uprising serves as his most detailed case study of rural oppression and its consequences. Hungary, though blessed by nature with "mines of gold, and with the real treasures of corn and wine," had been weakened by the elective monarchy and the tyranny of the nobles. The Hungarian nobility had reduced the common people to so miserable a servitude that the peasants rose up in a four-year civil war — a conflict that concluded, Voltaire observes, with "rivetting the chains of the common people."

The rising demonstrated the paradox of extreme noble privilege: it generated the very instability it was designed to prevent. "Second, privilege generated social instability. The Hungarian peasant rising and the repeated civil wars in Sweden demonstrated that extreme inequality of legal status was inherently unstable."

## Peasantry and Noble Privilege

Voltaire's central thesis is that privilege, when unchecked by royal power or representative institutions, degenerates into a form of licensed impunity. The power of nobles over peasants represented the most egregious form of this corruption:

> Voltaire describes Polish noble privilege in stark terms: "The nobles had also another privilege, in common with the palatines, that of not being confined for any crime, till they were juridically convicted. This indeed was the privilege of impunity. They had also the power of life and death over their peasants" (Ch. XCVIII).

"Third, privilege corrupted the administration of justice. The noble right to kill peasants with impunity — requiring only a payment of ten crowns on the grave — represented for Voltaire the ultimate degradation of legal equality."

## The Peasantry and the Reformation

The Reformation had a complex relationship with the peasantry. The early evangelical message of spiritual equality resonated powerfully among the rural population. The movement "spread rapidly through Germany and the Low Countries, attracting peasants, artisans, and disaffected clergy." The German Peasants' War (1524–1525), though predating Voltaire's main narrative, cast a long shadow over the Reformation's social implications.

In Sweden, the Dalecarlian peasants played a decisive role in the national liberation under [[gustavus-vasa|Gustavus Vasa]], who "was obliged to hide himself for some time in the mountains of Dalecarlia, disguised in a peasant's habit: he even worked at the mines, either for his subsistence, or in order to be concealed." (Ch. XCVIII) Yet Voltaire notes the irony that the Dalecarlians — the very peasants who had helped Gustavus Vasa to the throne — were the first to rebel against the religious changes he introduced.

## Economic Burdens

The peasantry bore the weight of multiple economic impositions: the [[tithe]], seigneurial dues, royal taxes, and the corvée (forced labor). The tithe system was particularly resented, as it diverted one-tenth of agricultural produce to the Church. In many regions, the Church owned between one-quarter and one-third of all arable land — property held in "dead hands" (*mortmain*) that could never be sold or inherited. "This concentration of wealth provoked resentment from both peasants burdened by tithe payments and princes who saw Church lands as a source of untapped revenue." (Ch. CVI)
