## Overview

Anabaptism (from the Greek _ana-_ "again" + _baptizein_ "to baptize") was the most radical wing of the sixteenth-century Reformation. Its adherents, called Anabaptists or "rebaptizers," rejected infant baptism and insisted that baptism was valid only when administered to adult believers capable of personal confession of faith. This seemingly narrow theological point had profound social implications: it implied that the existing Church was not truly Christian, that civil authorities had no jurisdiction over conscience, and that the union of church and state — the foundation of Christendom — was illegitimate. Voltaire devotes two chapters (Ch. CIX and CX) to the Anabaptists and their suppression.

## Origins and Context

Anabaptism emerged in the 1520s in Zurich, Switzerland, among followers of Zwingli who believed the reformer had not gone far enough. Conrad Grebel, Felix Manz, and George Blaurock performed the first adult baptisms in 1525, openly defying the Zurich city council's mandate that all infants be baptized. The council responded by making rebaptism a capital offense; Manz was executed by drowning in 1527 — a grim mockery of his belief in adult baptism.

The movement spread rapidly through Germany and the Low Countries, attracting peasants, artisans, and disaffected clergy. In Münster (1534–1535), Anabaptists under Jan van Leiden established a short-lived theocratic kingdom, practicing communal ownership of goods and polygamy. The Münster rebellion, violently crushed by Catholic and Protestant forces, permanently stained Anabaptism's reputation and was used to justify persecution of all Anabaptists regardless of their actual beliefs.

## Key Features

The core Anabaptist convictions included: (1) **believer's baptism** — baptism administered only to adults who professed personal faith; (2) **separation of church and state** — Christians should not hold civil office, swear oaths, or bear arms; (3) **the gathered church** — the true Church consists only of converted believers, not the entire population of a territory; (4) **discipleship** — the Christian life requires visible moral transformation, not mere doctrinal assent.

These principles, particularly the rejection of oaths and military service, brought Anabaptists into direct conflict with civil authorities everywhere. Voltaire notes that Luther, Zwingli, and Calvin all endorsed the persecution of Anabaptists, making them "the only sect that all the others united to condemn." (Ch. CIX)

## Historical Significance

The Anabaptist movement's historical significance lies not in its numbers but in its ideas. The principle of voluntary church membership — that religious affiliation cannot be coerced by the state — was a radical departure from the Constantinian model of Christendom that had prevailed for over a millennium. This principle, though suppressed in the sixteenth century, would later become foundational to modern conceptions of [[liberty]] and religious freedom.

The peaceful descendants of Anabaptism — Mennonites, Amish, and Hutterites — survive to the present day, preserving the original vision of a church separate from the world. The broader Anabaptist impulse toward religious liberty influenced the English Baptists and, through them, the American tradition of separation of church and state.
