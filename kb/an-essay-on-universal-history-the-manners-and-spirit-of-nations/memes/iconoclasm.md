## Overview

Iconoclasm — the deliberate destruction of religious images, icons, and statues — was a defining feature of the Protestant Reformation in the 16th century. Rooted in the Reformation's rejection of what it viewed as idolatrous Catholic practices, iconoclastic acts swept across Germany, Switzerland, the Low Countries, England, and Scotland as reformers sought to purify worship by removing material objects of devotion.

## Origins and Context

The theological foundation for Reformation iconoclasm lay in the biblical prohibition against graven images (Exodus 20:4–5). While the medieval Church had long distinguished between veneration (dulia) and worship (latria), Protestant reformers — particularly Luther's followers, Calvinists, and Zwinglians — argued that all religious images constituted idolatry. Voltaire notes that Luther himself, after being concealed in a Saxon fortress, declared that the devil had appeared to him and "proved to him... that it was idolatry" to consecrate the host, after which "the images were broke to pieces; the monks and nuns quitted their cloisters" throughout Saxony. (Ch. CVII)

## Spread Across Europe

Iconoclastic violence accompanied the spread of Reformation ideas across multiple regions:

- **Germany and Switzerland**: Following Luther's lead, images were destroyed in Wittenberg and across Saxony. Zwingli's Zurich and Calvin's Geneva similarly removed all religious imagery from churches, replacing ornate altars with plain communion tables.
- **England**: Under Henry VIII, the Dissolution of the Monasteries (1536–1541) involved not only the seizure of monastic wealth but the destruction of religious images. Voltaire records that "in the market places in London several wooden images were burnt, which the monks used to put into motion by springs," and that the revered shrine of St. Thomas of Canterbury — "immensely rich with precious stones" — was seized and its relics thrown into the flames. (Ch. CXIII)
- **Low Countries**: The *Beeldenstorm* (image storm) of 1566 saw Calvinist mobs ransack Catholic churches across the Spanish Netherlands, shattering stained glass, statues, and paintings.

## Historical Development

Iconoclasm was not a single coordinated movement but a series of locally-driven actions. In some regions, magistrates ordered the orderly removal of images; in others, popular riots preempted official action. The Zurich senate, after hearing both parties for two months, "condemned the Romish religion" and the people "readily assented." (Ch. CVII)

The Catholic Church responded through the Council of Trent (1563), which reaffirmed the legitimacy of religious images while calling for the elimination of superstitious abuses. This hardened the confessional divide: Protestant churches became visually austere spaces centered on the pulpit and the Word, while Catholic churches retained and even elaborated their visual splendor.

## Key Features

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Theological basis | Second Commandment prohibition of graven images |
| Primary targets | Statues, paintings, crucifixes, stained glass, relics, ornate altars |
| Geographic spread | Germany, Switzerland, England, Scotland, Low Countries, parts of France |
| Catholic response | Council of Trent reaffirmed image veneration; continued Baroque artistic patronage |

## Impact and Legacy

Iconoclasm permanently reshaped the visual landscape of European Christianity. Protestant church interiors became markedly different from their Catholic counterparts — a distinction still visible today. The destruction also represented an enormous loss of medieval art; countless works of painting, sculpture, and stained glass were irrevocably destroyed.

Voltaire observes that "every nation that has converted the religious houses to the public use, hath gained thereby, humanly speaking, and nobody has been injured" — though he adds the qualification that the ejected clergy "left no descendants that can complain," and the act was "but the injustice of a day, which has been productive of the blessings of many ages." (Ch. CXII)
