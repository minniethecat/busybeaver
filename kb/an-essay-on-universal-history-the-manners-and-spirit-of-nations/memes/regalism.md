## Overview

Regalism was the doctrine that the monarch, by virtue of his sovereign authority, possessed supreme jurisdiction over the Church within his realm. Rooted in medieval traditions of royal sacrality and refined by Renaissance theories of absolute sovereignty, regalism asserted the Crown's right to appoint bishops, tax ecclesiastical revenues, and regulate the Church's temporal affairs — claims that brought monarchs into direct conflict with the [[papacy]] throughout the Reformation era.

Voltaire encountered regalism in multiple national contexts — the French [[gallican-church|Gallican]] tradition, the Spanish *regalismo*, the English royal supremacy, and the competing claims of the Holy Roman Emperors. In each case, the monarch asserted authority over ecclesiastical matters that the papacy claimed as its exclusive preserve. For Voltaire, regalism represented the triumph of secular sovereignty over papal universalism — a development he generally regarded as progressive, though he was not blind to the abuses that royal control of the Church could produce.

## Regalism in France: Gallicanism

The French variant of regalism — Gallicanism — had deep medieval roots. The Pragmatic Sanction of Bourges (1438) asserted the authority of a general council over the pope and restricted papal revenues from French benefices, including [[annates]]. Though Louis XI abrogated the Pragmatic Sanction in 1461 under political pressure, the principles of Gallican liberty were revived by the [[parliament]] of Paris and the University of Paris throughout the sixteenth century. (Ch. CXVI)

The Concordat of Bologna (1516), negotiated between [[francis-i|Francis I]] and Pope [[leo-x|Leo X]], transformed but did not extinguish Gallicanism. The Concordat gave the king the right to nominate bishops — a power greater even than the Pragmatic Sanction's electoral provisions — while restoring papal revenues including annates. The arrangement was a royal-papal bargain that bypassed the traditional guardians of Gallican liberties: the cathedral chapters, which lost their electoral rights; the University of Paris, which protested bitterly; and the [[parliament]] of Paris, which registered the Concordat only under royal pressure. (Ch. CXVI)

## Regalism in England: Royal Supremacy

The English variant of regalism reached its logical extreme in the Act of Supremacy (1534), which declared [[henry-viii|Henry VIII]] "the only Supreme Head in earth of the Church of England." The Act suppressed the pope's authority, [[annates]], St. Peter's Pence, and the provision of benefices. As Voltaire records, "the king prevailed on the parliament to grant him the annates or first fruits, which had been paid to the pope." The revenue that had once flowed to Rome now enriched the royal treasury. (Ch. CXIII)

The English royal supremacy went further than Gallicanism in two respects: it denied the papacy any jurisdiction whatsoever within England, and it asserted that the king's authority extended to doctrinal as well as administrative matters. The resulting Church of England was a national church under royal governance — an arrangement that persisted, in modified form, to Voltaire's own day and which he regarded as a model of reasonable ecclesiastical governance.

## Regalism in Spain

Spanish regalism (*regalismo*) developed along lines parallel to Gallicanism but with distinctive features. The Catholic Monarchs, Ferdinand and Isabella, had obtained from the papacy the right of presentation (*patronato real*) to all major benefices in the kingdom of Granada and later in the Spanish Americas. This concession gave the Spanish Crown control over ecclesiastical appointments that exceeded even that of the French monarchy.

The Spanish *regalistas* of the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries argued that the Crown possessed inherent jurisdiction over the temporal affairs of the Church, derived not from papal concession but from the nature of sovereignty itself. This argument brought them into conflict with the [[jesuits|Jesuits]], who defended papal supremacy, and contributed to the eventual expulsion of the Jesuits from Spain in 1767.

## Impact and Legacy

Regalism, in its various national forms, reshaped the relationship between church and state throughout early modern Europe. By asserting the Crown's authority over ecclesiastical appointments and revenues, regalist monarchs transformed the Church from a transnational institution under papal governance into a collection of national churches under royal supervision. This transformation reduced the political power of the papacy while enhancing the authority of the emerging nation-state.

Voltaire's assessment of regalism was characteristically ambivalent. He approved of the limitation of papal power and the reduction of ecclesiastical revenues flowing to Rome. But he was also aware that royal control of the Church could serve tyranny as readily as liberty: a monarch who appointed bishops could use them as instruments of political control, and a church that served the Crown first might neglect its pastoral duties. The legacy of regalism, in Voltaire's view, was thus the creation of national churches that were less subject to Roman abuses but more subject to royal ones.
