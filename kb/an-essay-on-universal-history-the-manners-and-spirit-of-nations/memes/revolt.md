## Overview

Revolt in Voltaire's Essay on Universal History is never celebrated and never simply condemned. It is analyzed as a fact of political life — the predictable consequence of [[tyranny]] and the equally predictable cause of further repression. "Some sovereigns that changed the religion of their country, have been tyrants, from opposition and revolt, which will occasion cruelty" (Ch. CXIII). The observation is characteristic: revolt and tyranny form a closed loop, each justifying the other in a cycle that Voltaire records without endorsing either side.

The sixteenth century was an age of revolts: the German Peasants' War (1524–1525), the revolt of the Netherlands against Spanish rule, the religious wars in France, the uprising of the Swedish estates against Christian II of Denmark. Each presented the same moral question — when is resistance to authority justified? — and each, in Voltaire's telling, produced the same ambiguous answer: resistance sometimes succeeded, but it always cost more than anyone anticipated.

## Origins and Context

Voltaire locates the causes of revolt in two related phenomena: the concentration of power and the fusion of religious with political authority. The Reformation shattered the medieval consensus that obedience to the prince was obedience to God. When [[lutheranism|Luther]] taught that salvation came through faith alone, he also taught — however unintentionally — that papal and imperial authority were not absolute. The [[doctrine]] of liberty of conscience contained within it the seeds of political resistance.

The revolts Voltaire describes were not abstract philosophical exercises. The German Peasants' War was a response to feudal exactions; the Dutch Revolt was a response to Spanish taxation and religious persecution; the Swedish uprising was a response to the tyranny of Christian II. In each case, Voltaire is careful to specify the material grievances that gave theological dissent its explosive force.

## Key Features

Voltaire identifies a recurring pattern in the revolts he describes:

| Element | Description | Example |
|---------|-------------|---------|
| **Material grievance** | Taxation, feudal exaction, or economic oppression | Peasants' War, Dutch Revolt |
| **Theological justification** | Religious doctrine deployed to legitimize resistance | Luther's "liberty of a Christian" |
| **Outside intervention** | Foreign powers supporting rebels for strategic reasons | Elizabeth I's support for Dutch rebels |
| **Escalatory cruelty** | Revolt provokes repression, repression provokes further revolt | French Wars of Religion |
| **Ambiguous outcome** | Revolt rarely achieves its original aims | Peasants crushed; Dutch eventually independent |

> "Some sovereigns that changed the religion of their country, have been tyrants, from opposition and revolt, which will occasion cruelty. Henry VIII was cruel in his nature, a tyrant in government, in religion, and in his family. Yet he died in his bed." (Ch. CXIII)

## Historical Development

The most consequential revolt in Voltaire's narrative is the Dutch Revolt — the uprising of the Netherlands against Spanish rule that produced the Dutch Republic. "Before we shew what influence the republic of the United Provinces had upon Europe," Voltaire writes, the revolt itself must be understood as both a religious and a political phenomenon. The Seven United Provinces did not set out to create a republic; they were driven to it by Spanish intolerance and fiscal exaction.

The revolt of the Swedish estates under [[gustavus-vasa|Gustavus Vasa]] against Christian II of Denmark offers a contrasting pattern. Christian II had murdered Gustavus's mother and sister — "he ordered those two princesses to be sown into a sack, and thrown into the sea" (Ch. XCVIII) — and his tyranny made resistance morally unambiguous. Gustavus's successful revolt established the Vasa dynasty and made Sweden a Lutheran power, demonstrating that revolt could, in exceptional circumstances, produce a stable and legitimate political order.

The German Peasants' War represents the darkest face of revolt in Voltaire's account. Luther, who had inspired the peasants with his rhetoric of Christian liberty, "thundered against them from the pulpit" when they took up arms, urging the princes to "smite, slay, and stab" the rebels. The suppression was brutal, and Voltaire's silence on the peasants' fate is itself a judgment: the common people who revolted against oppression were abandoned by the very reformers whose doctrines had given them hope.

## Impact and Legacy

Voltaire draws no simple lesson from the revolts he describes. The Dutch succeeded where the German peasants failed; Gustavus Vasa became a legitimate king where rebel leaders elsewhere were executed as traitors. The difference lay not in the justice of the cause — all could claim grievances — but in the constellation of forces that happened to align behind them.

The ambiguous legacy of revolt shaped early modern political thought. The question of when resistance becomes legitimate — the question that animated the Reformation and the political philosophy that emerged from it — remained unresolved. Voltaire, writing two centuries later, still found it unresolved. His narrative records the revolts without resolving their meaning, leaving the reader to draw conclusions that Voltaire himself, perhaps, preferred not to state explicitly.

## Key Figures

- **Gustavus Vasa** — Leader of the Swedish revolt against Christian II of Denmark, later king of Sweden
- **William of Orange (William the Silent)** — Leader of the Dutch Revolt against Spanish rule
- **Archbishop Troll of Upsal** — Swedish primate who led an armed revolt against the Swedish estates
- **Christian II of Denmark** — King whose tyranny provoked the Swedish revolt, "the tyrant could indulge his revenge, but did not know how to fight"
