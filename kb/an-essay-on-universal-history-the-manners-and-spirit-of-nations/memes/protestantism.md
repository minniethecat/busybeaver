## Overview

Protestantism emerged in the early sixteenth century as a religious movement that challenged the authority of the Roman Catholic Church, leading to a permanent schism in Western Christianity. Beginning with [[luther-martin|Martin Luther]]'s objections to the sale of [[indulgences]] in 1517, the movement spread rapidly through Germany and beyond.

Voltaire chronicles this transformation in detail, noting how religious disputes became intertwined with political power struggles among Europe's monarchs.

The movement proceeded by a logic of escalation that Voltaire traces with clarity. As he records: "After railing against the indulgences, he proceeded to examine into the power of the person who granted them. Part of the veil was removed: the people being once set on, would fain judge of what they had hitherto adored." (Ch. CVII)

What began as a critique of a specific abuse expanded into a comprehensive challenge to papal authority and the entire sacramental system of the medieval Church.

## Origins and Context

The Protestant movement arose during the pontificate of [[leo-x|Leo X]], a period when the Catholic Church was at the height of its temporal power but facing growing criticism over clerical abuses.

The immediate catalyst was the public sale of indulgences — releases from purgatorial punishment — which the pope authorized to fund St. Peter's Basilica. As Voltaire observes: "Under the pretence of a war against the Turks, he set up a sale in all parts of Christendom, of what they call Indulgences." (Ch. CVI)

The indulgence trade was farmed out commercially, often operated from alehouses, with preachers making extravagant claims about their efficacy. When the [[dominicans|Dominican]] order was granted the indulgence franchise in Germany — traditionally held by the Augustinians — Luther, an Augustinian friar, was commissioned by his superiors to preach against the practice.

The broader context included long-standing grievances against papal fiscal exactions: [[annates]], provisions, [[dispensation|dispensations]], and the sale of offices. These abuses had been condemned by councils across Europe for generations before Luther.

What distinguished the Protestant moment was the convergence of theological critique, popular resentment, and princely self-interest.

## Core Theological Principles

| Principle | Meaning | Contrast with Catholicism |
|-----------|---------|--------------------------|
| *Sola Scriptura* | Scripture alone as the authority for doctrine | Rejects papal decrees and unwritten tradition |
| *Sola Fide* | Justification by faith alone | Rejects the necessity of works and indulgences for salvation |
| Universal Priesthood | All believers are priests; no separate sacerdotal class | Rejects the distinction between clergy and laity |
| Vernacular Scripture | The Bible in languages accessible to the laity | Rejects the Latin Vulgate as the exclusive authorized text |
| Two Sacraments | Only Baptism and Eucharist as dominical sacraments | Reduces the seven Catholic sacraments to two |

## Historical Development

### The German Phase

The movement progressed rapidly through the German lands. [[frederick-the-wise|Frederick the Wise]], Elector of Saxony, took Luther under his protection after the papal excommunication. German princes saw in Lutheranism an opportunity to assert independence from both the Emperor and the Pope.

Voltaire observes with characteristic bluntness: "The revenues of the archbishopric... and of such a number of rich abbies, were a bait to the secular princes." (Ch. CVII)

The Emperor [[charles-v|Charles V]] found himself caught between the Protestant princes and Catholic powers. The Lutheran religion proved instrumental in checking imperial power. When Charles V advanced into France in 1544, Protestant princes in Germany "had entered into a confederacy against him." (Ch. CIV)

The Peace of Augsburg (1555) formalized the confessional division of the Empire, establishing *cuius regio, eius religio* — the ruler's religion determined that of his subjects.

### Divergence and Multiplication

Protestantism did not remain a single movement. Luther's interpretation was challenged from the left by the [[anabaptism|Anabaptists]], who rejected infant baptism and the alliance of church and state, and from the Reformed tradition of [[zwingli-huldrych|Zwingli]] and [[calvin-john|Calvin]], which developed distinct theologies of the Eucharist and predestination.

The [[calvinism|Calvinist]] Reformation proved particularly influential in France (where its adherents were known as [[huguenot|Huguenots]]), the Netherlands, Scotland, and eventually England and the American colonies.

The multiplication of Protestant confessions generated a new problem: the fragmentation of Christendom into competing orthodoxies. Voltaire regarded this as both the strength and weakness of the movement — it had broken Rome's monopoly, but the principle of private judgment could not be confined within any single confessional boundary.

### The English Variant

The English Reformation followed a distinctive path, driven less by theology than by the dynastic and fiscal interests of [[henry-viii|Henry VIII]]. The Act of Supremacy (1534) suppressed the pope's authority and papal revenues but left most Catholic doctrine intact.

It was only under Edward VI and Elizabeth I that England developed a distinctively Protestant settlement — a middle way between Rome and Geneva that Voltaire admired for its moderation and subordination of ecclesiastical to civil authority. (Ch. CXIII)

## Impact and Legacy

Protestantism's political consequences were as significant as its theological ones. The movement contributed to the equilibrium of power in Europe by providing German princes with a cause around which to resist imperial centralization.

The intra-Christian competition also had a civilizing effect on the Catholic Church. As Voltaire concluded: "It must be in general allowed that the ecclesiastics of the church of Rome have been reformed by the protestants, just as two rivals become more circumspect by their jealousy of each other." (Ch. CVII)

The [[council-of-trent|Council of Trent]] (1545–1563), which addressed many of the abuses that had fueled the Reformation, was itself a response to the Protestant challenge.

The religious division had contrasting effects in different realms. [[francis-i|Francis I]] burned Lutherans at Paris while simultaneously allying with Lutheran princes in Germany — a contradiction that Voltaire highlights as characteristic of the political exploitation of religious conflict.

The legacy of Protestantism extended beyond theology into literacy, political theory, and economic culture. The vernacular Bible promoted reading; resistance to papal authority could become resistance to royal absolutism.

For Voltaire, the Reformation's greatest achievement was negative — it broke the monopoly of a single ecclesiastical authority over the conscience of Europe — but this created the space in which toleration and intellectual liberty could eventually flourish.
