## Overview

Siege warfare was the dominant form of military conflict in early modern Europe, far more common than pitched battles. A [[siege]] involved surrounding a fortified town, city, or castle, cutting off its supply lines, and employing artillery, mining, and starvation to force capitulation. The prevalence of siege warfare reflected the defensive advantage conferred by the bastioned [[fortification]] systems developed in response to gunpowder artillery.

Voltaire's narrative of 16th-century European conflicts repeatedly centers on sieges as the decisive military events: "He had just taken Toulon, and was now laying siege to Marseilles." (Ch. CIII) In the same period, the siege of Paris became one of the defining episodes of the [[french-wars-of-religion|French Wars of Religion]].

## Origins and Context

The shift from medieval castle warfare to early modern siege operations was driven by the development of artillery in the 15th century. Traditional high stone walls proved vulnerable to cannon fire, leading to the development of the *trace italienne* — low, thick, angled bastions designed to deflect shot and provide interlocking fields of fire. By the 16th century, the siege had become a highly formalized military science, governed by established rules of approach, bombardment, and negotiation. (Ch. CIII)

The cost of maintaining standing armies capable of conducting prolonged sieges placed enormous financial strain on early modern states. A single siege could consume a year's royal revenue in artillery, powder, wages, and provisions.

## Key Features

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Circumvallation | A line of fortifications built by the besiegers facing outward to prevent relief |
| Countervallation | A line facing inward to prevent sorties by the garrison |
| Artillery bombardment | Prolonged cannon fire to breach walls and demoralize defenders |
| Mining (sapping) | Digging tunnels under fortifications to collapse them |
| Starvation blockade | Cutting off food and water to force surrender |
| Honorable surrender | Negotiated capitulation with terms (the honors of war) |

## Historical Development

The siege of Constantinople in 1453 demonstrated the decisive power of gunpowder artillery against medieval walls, heralding a new era. During the Italian Wars, sieges multiplied as fortified cities changed hands repeatedly. The [[battle-of-pavia|Battle of Pavia]] (1525) was exceptional — most campaigns were decided not by field battles but by the outcome of sieges.

In the [[french-wars-of-religion|French Wars of Religion]], the siege of Paris (1590) exemplified siege warfare's political dimension. [[henry-iv-of-france|Henry IV]] invested the city, but "had it not been for the duke of Parma, or his own indulgence to the besieged, he would have placed the protestant religion on the throne." (Ch. CXXXIX) The city's Catholic population endured severe famine before the siege was lifted by a Spanish relief army under the [[duke-of-parma|Duke of Parma]].

Henry IV also conducted the siege of Rouen, which "the duke of Parma from obliging Henry IV to raise the siege of Rouen, as before he had obliged him to raise that of Paris." (Ch. CXXXVII) These twin sieges illustrate how control of the capital and major cities hinged on the ability to sustain or relieve a siege.

## Impact and Legacy

Siege warfare shaped the geography of early modern Europe, as cities invested heavily in bastioned fortifications. The enormous cost of siege warfare was a driving force behind the growth of the early modern fiscal-military state, as monarchs sought reliable tax revenues to fund artillery parks and professional armies. (Ch. CXLII)

The fear of a sack following a successful storm — when a besieging army broke into a city and massacred its inhabitants — was a constant terror for civilian populations, shaping the diplomatic calculations that often led cities to negotiate surrender rather than resist to the end. The formalized conventions of siege warfare, including the honors of war granted to garrisons that surrendered before a breach was stormed, represented an early form of the laws of war.
