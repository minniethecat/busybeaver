## Overview

The **Ottoman Empire** in the sixteenth century was the foremost Muslim power in the world, extending from Algiers to the Euphrates and from the Black Sea to the extremities of Greece. Under sultans Selim I, Solyman (Suleyman the Magnificent), and Selim II, the empire achieved its greatest territorial reach, threatening the heart of Christendom while simultaneously becoming an indispensable trading partner of the very European states it menaced. Voltaire devotes an entire chapter to its customs, government, and revenue, situating it within the wider balance of power that defined sixteenth-century Europe. (Ch. CXXX)

## Origins and Expansion

The grandeur of Ottoman arms outlasted that of the Persian Sophis; after Amurat II, it was "only one continued series of victories." Mahomet II (Mehmed the Conqueror) had subdued an extent of dominions sufficient to content any dynasty. But Selim I added new conquests: he made himself master of Syria and Mesopotamia in 1515, and undertook the conquest of Egypt — defended at that time by the Mamalukes, a formidable militia of Circassian slave-soldiers who had governed Egypt since the last [[crusade|crusades]]. (Ch. CXXX)

> The figurative manner of speaking peculiar to the oriental nations, hath ever introduced the most ridiculous pomp of titles among princes, together with the meanest appellations among their servants. The bashaws belonging to the Grand Signor call themselves his slaves. (Ch. CXXX)

Selim's conquest of Egypt was a turning point. He defeated Tumanbai, the last Mamaluke king, and — in an extraordinary gesture — entrusted the conquered ruler with the government of Egypt as his bashaw. Tumanbai, descending from royal dignity to the post of bashaw, "met with a bashaw's fate, which was to be strangled after an administration of a few months." (Ch. CXXX)

## The Reign of Solyman

Solyman, the son of Selim, was a formidable enemy to both Christians and Persians. His military achievements were extraordinary:

| Campaign | Target | Result |
|----------|--------|--------|
| Rhodes (1522) | Knights Hospitaller | Island taken |
| Hungary | Kingdom of Hungary | Greater part conquered |
| Moldavia and Walachia | Danubian principalities | Became fiefs of the empire |
| Vienna (1529) | Habsburg capital | Siege failed |
| Persia | Safavid Empire | Bagdat retaken, Georgia subdued |
| Yemen | Arabian peninsula | Subdued via Red Sea fleet |

Voltaire observes that Solyman "was a greater general than [[charles-v|Charles V]], but resembled him in his numerous voyages." He was the first Ottoman emperor to enter into an alliance with the French — "an alliance that has subsisted ever since" — marking the Ottoman entry into the European diplomatic system. He died besieging the town of Zigeth in Hungary, "and victory attended him to the grave; for no sooner had he expired, than the place was taken by storm." (Ch. CXXX)

## Government and Revenue

The administration of the Turkish empire differed fundamentally from European governments. Part of the Grand Signor's revenue consisted not in specie, as in Christian states, but in the product of the several countries subject to his dominion. The canal of Constantinople was covered year-round with vessels bringing provisions from Egypt, Greece, Natolia, and the coasts of the Black Sea for the seraglio, the Janizaries, and the fleet. (Ch. CXXX)

By the canon namt (registers of the empire), total government expenditure was supplied from a revenue of less than sixty million livres — "not a third part of what the French and English pay for the public debts." The Turks waged war not "with money and negotiations" like European princes, but with "the united strength and the impetuosity of the Janizaries." Confiscations formed a considerable part of the sultan's privy purse, a form of tyranny Voltaire condemns as "one of the oldest acts of tyranny, that the property of the whole family should devolve to the sovereign upon the condemnation of a criminal." (Ch. CXXX)

## Trade and Relations with Christendom

Despite constant warfare, a vigorous commerce flourished between the Ottoman Empire and Christian Europe. The Venetians, Genoese, Florentines, and Marseillais were rivals in the Turkey trade, paying for silks and other commodities of Asia in ready money. Christian merchants were enriched by this commerce, "but at the expense of Christendom." (Ch. CXXX)

Voltaire notes a revealing asymmetry: the Christian nations sent ambassadors and consuls to the Ottoman Port, while "none are sent from thence to reside with us. The Turks look upon these perpetual embassies as an homage which the necessities of Christian states offer up to their power." [[william-iii|William III]] of England observed that "there is no honour in regard to Turks" — an expression Voltaire finds fitting for a merchant but "does not become a king, jealous of what we call glory." (Ch. CXXX)

The empire reached its naval limits at the [[battle-of-lepanto|Battle of Lepanto]] (1571), where Pope Pius V organized a league with Venice and [[philip-ii|Philip II]] of Spain — the first time "the standard of the Cross Keys was displayed against the crescent." (Ch. CXXXI)
