## Overview

The papal bull in Voltaire's sixteenth century was the most solemn form of papal utterance — a legal and doctrinal instrument that bound the consciences of all Christendom. Voltaire notes that "the decrees of the popes" included "wise laws, and even laws useful to Christendom" (Ch. XCVIII), particularly when the papacy legislated on matters beyond its own immediate interests. Yet the bull was also the weapon with which Rome fought the Reformation, and the public burning of papal bulls by Martin Luther and his followers became the defining symbolic act of the Protestant break with Rome.

The bull's authority derived from the pope's claim to be the successor of Saint Peter and the vicar of Christ on earth. In canon law, a bull was binding on all the faithful unless it contradicted divine law or a decree of an ecumenical council — a loophole that reformers would exploit to the fullest.

## Origins and Context

The term "bull" derives from the Latin *bulla*, the leaden seal attached to the document that authenticated its origin from the papal chancery. By the late Middle Ages, the papal bull had become the standard instrument for papal legislation, canonizations, dispensations, excommunications, and the granting of indulgences.

The bull's form was highly standardized: it began with the pope's name followed by *servus servorum Dei* (servant of the servants of God), stated its subject, and concluded with dating and sealing clauses. The language was Latin, the style legalistic and peremptory. Yet the bull was not merely a legal document — it was a performative act that, by its very issuance, changed the juridical reality it described.

## Key Features

| Bull Type | Purpose | Example |
|-----------|---------|---------|
| **Doctrinal** | Define or condemn theological propositions | *Exsurge Domine* (1520) against Luther |
| **Disciplinary** | Regulate clerical conduct or Church governance | Reform bulls of the Fifth Lateran Council |
| **Crusade** | Proclaim and organize crusades | Bulls against the Ottoman Turks |
| **Foundation** | Establish universities, religious orders, dioceses | Approval of the [[jesuits]] (1540) |
| **Political** | Adjudicate territorial disputes, depose rulers | *Regnans in Excelsis* (1570) against Elizabeth I |

> The popes "have been observed to enact wise laws, and even laws useful to Christendom, where their own personal interests were not concerned." (Ch. XCVIII)

## Historical Development

The sixteenth century was the bull's great crisis. For centuries, the papal bull had been the ultimate authority in Western Christendom, capable of deposing emperors and dissolving oaths of allegiance. The Reformation shattered this monopoly of legitimate authority.

When [[luther-martin]] received the bull *Exsurge Domine* (1520), which condemned forty-one propositions drawn from his writings, he responded not with submission but with a counter-act of symbolic destruction: he burned the bull publicly at Wittenberg, together with books of canon law. This was not merely defiance but a revolutionary claim that the pope's bulls had no binding force over conscience.

Yet the bull remained a vital instrument for the Catholic Reformation. The bull *Regimini militantis Ecclesiae* (1540) approved the Society of Jesus (the [[jesuits]]), creating the order that would become the vanguard of Catholic reform and Counter-Reformation polemics. The Council of Trent's decrees were promulgated through papal bulls, giving them binding force throughout the Catholic world.

## Impact and Legacy

Voltaire is characteristically ambivalent about the papal bull's authority. He recognizes that papal legislation could be wise and useful, particularly when it addressed abuses like dueling: "These combats were anathematized by the decrees of the popes" — decrees issued as bulls (Ch. XCVIII). Yet he is equally aware that papal authority was a political weapon, and that the bull's claim to bind conscience was precisely what the Reformation rejected.

The bull's decline as an instrument of universal authority paralleled the rise of national churches and sovereign states. By the end of the sixteenth century, a papal bull had no legal effect in Protestant territories, and even in Catholic countries its reception was filtered through royal placets and parliamentary registration. The age when a pope could divide the non-Christian world between Portugal and Spain by a stroke of the pen (as Alexander VI had done in 1493) was passing.

## Key Figures

- **Leo X** — Medici pope who issued *Exsurge Domine* against Luther, triggering the definitive break
- **Paul III** — Farnese pope whose bulls approved the Jesuits and convoked the Council of Trent
- **Martin Luther** — Reformer whose burning of the papal bull became the symbolic founding act of the Reformation
- **Clement VII** — Pope whose refusal to annul Henry VIII's marriage led to the English break with Rome
