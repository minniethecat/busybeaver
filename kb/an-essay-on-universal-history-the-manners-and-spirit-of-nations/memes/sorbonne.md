## Overview

The Sorbonne — originally the College de Sorbonne, founded by Robert de Sorbon in 1257, and by the sixteenth century synonymous with the Faculty of Theology of the University of Paris — was the most authoritative theological institution in France during the Reformation era. Its doctors pronounced on matters ranging from the validity of royal marriages to the orthodoxy of Protestant doctrines, wielding an influence that extended far beyond the lecture hall into the highest councils of state. Voltaire depicted the Sorbonne as a body whose theological judgments were inseparable from political calculation, at times serving the interests of the French crown and at others aligning with the [[catholic-league|Catholic League]] against the monarchy itself.

The Sorbonne's interventions in the crises of sixteenth-century France illustrate the entanglement of theological authority with political power that characterized the French [[french-wars-of-religion|Wars of Religion]]. From its ruling on Henry VIII's marriage to its declaration deposing Henry III, the Sorbonne acted as a decisive force in shaping the political destiny of both France and Europe. Voltaire, always attentive to the gap between theological pretension and worldly motive, treated the faculty's pronouncements with the irony they invited. (Ch. CXIII)

## The Sorbonne and Henry VIII's Divorce

One of the Sorbonne's most consequential interventions came in the late 1520s, when Henry VIII of England sought theological opinions to support the annulment of his marriage to Catherine of Aragon. Henry's agents canvassed universities across Europe, and "the universities of France, and especially the Sorbonne, declared that Henry's marriage with Catharine of Arragon was illegal, and that the pope had no right to dispense with the law of Leviticus." Voltaire noted with amusement that the price of theological opinions varied "according to the weight of the doctor," with one Italian theologian receiving twenty crowns for his favorable opinion.

The Sorbonne's ruling provided Henry with precisely the intellectual cover he needed to proceed with the annulment and, ultimately, with the break from Rome. The irony was rich: the same theological faculty that would later condemn Protestantism as heretical had furnished the theological justification for England's schism. Voltaire, with characteristic understatement, observed that the opinions "had not cost him a great deal" — a remark that applied equally to the money paid and to the intellectual integrity sacrificed. (Ch. CXIII)

## The Sorbonne and the Catholic League

During the French Wars of Religion, the Sorbonne aligned itself decisively with the [[catholic-league|Catholic League]] and against the Protestant Henry of Navarre (the future Henry IV). Voltaire recorded that "the Sorbonne, and all the religious orders, were of Philip [II of Spain]'s side," supporting the Spanish king's ambition to see a Catholic claimant on the French throne. The faculty's partisanship was not merely academic; it provided religious legitimation for armed resistance to the legitimate heir.

After the assassination of Henry III by the Dominican friar Jacques Clement in 1589, the Sorbonne escalated its defiance to unprecedented heights. "Seventy doctors assembled in Sorbonne declare him cast from the throne, and his subjects released from the oath of allegiance," Voltaire wrote. "The priests refuse absolution to their penitents that recognize him as king." A theological faculty had presumed to depose a monarch and absolve his subjects of their loyalty — an act that demonstrated how thoroughly the Sorbonne had become an instrument of the League's political program. (Ch. CXXXV)

## Theological Orthodoxy and Its Limits

The Sorbonne's theological authority was exercised most consistently in the condemnation of Protestant doctrines. When [[luther-martin|Martin Luther]] "affirmed that Aristotle's doctrine was of no manner of use towards understanding the scripture, the sacred faculty of Paris treated this assertion, as erroneous and absurd." The faculty defended Aristotle — the "sovereign of the schools" — as vigorously as it defended transubstantiation, reflecting the fusion of scholastic philosophy with Catholic theology that the Reformation challenged at its foundations.

Yet Voltaire was careful to note the limits of the Sorbonne's intellectual authority. Its theological solemnity could not disguise the triviality of some of its preoccupations. The faculty "suspended for a while her old dispute, whether there were three Mary Magdalens or only one, to condemn the opinions of Luther" — a juxtaposition that Voltaire deployed to devastating effect. The same doctors who could not resolve the identity of a biblical figure presumed to adjudicate the most fundamental questions of Christian doctrine. (Ch. CVII, CXIII)

## Recalibrating Authority

The Sorbonne's political alignment with the League proved, in the end, self-defeating. When Henry IV converted to Catholicism ("Paris is well worth a mass") and entered the capital in 1594, the theological faculty that had declared him unfit to reign was compelled to accommodate itself to his rule. The Sorbonne's authority survived, but its pretension to sit in judgment on kings was permanently diminished. The [[gallican-church|Gallican Church]], with its insistence on the relative autonomy of the French Church from Rome and its subordination to the French crown, would increasingly define the Sorbonne's role in the centuries that followed.

Voltaire's treatment of the Sorbonne reflects his broader argument about the relationship between theology and politics: theological authority, when it exceeds its proper sphere and attempts to direct political outcomes, inevitably corrupts both itself and the state. The Sorbonne of the sixteenth century — learned, powerful, and deeply compromised — was exemplary of this thesis.
