## Overview

Lutheranism was the original Protestant tradition, emerging from Martin Luther's challenge to the Roman Catholic Church in 1517 and rapidly becoming the established religion across much of Northern Europe. Unlike later Reformed traditions, Lutheranism retained many elements of Catholic liturgy and church structure while fundamentally redefining the doctrines of salvation, authority, and the priesthood. As Voltaire chronicles, the movement spread from its Saxon origins to become a political force that reshaped the balance of power in the Holy Roman Empire.

## Origins and Context

The Lutheran movement began within the Augustinian order. Martin Luther, an Augustinian friar and professor at Wittenberg, was commissioned by his superiors to preach against the indulgence trade when the Dominican order obtained the franchise that had traditionally belonged to the Augustinians in Germany. This institutional rivalry provided the immediate occasion for Luther's protest.

The theological context was crucial. Luther's study of Scripture, particularly the Pauline epistles, led him to the doctrine of justification by faith alone—a direct challenge to the sacramental and penitential system of the medieval Church. Voltaire notes the rapid escalation: "After railing against the indulgences, he proceeded to examine into the power of the person who granted them. Part of the veil was removed: the people being once set on, would fain judge of what they had hitherto adored." (Ch. CVII)

## Key Features

Lutheranism is distinguished by several core doctrinal positions:

- **Sola Fide (Faith Alone)**: Salvation comes through faith in Christ, not through works, indulgences, or priestly mediation
- **Sola Scriptura (Scripture Alone)**: The Bible is the sole authority for Christian doctrine, superseding papal decrees and Church tradition
- **The Two Kingdoms**: A doctrine distinguishing spiritual authority (the Church) from temporal authority (the state), with both ordained by God for different purposes
- **Consubstantiation**: Christ is truly present "in, with, and under" the bread and wine of the Eucharist, distinct from both Catholic transubstantiation and Reformed symbolism
- **Universal Priesthood**: All baptized believers have direct access to God; no separate priestly caste mediates salvation

## Historical Development

Frederick the Wise, Elector of Saxony, became Luther's most important protector. This prince—who had earlier declined the imperial crown—shielded Luther after his excommunication and the Diet of Worms (1521). German princes quickly recognized that Lutheranism offered political as well as theological advantages.

Voltaire observes the material incentives at work: "The revenues of the archbishopric... and of such a number of rich abbies, were a bait to the secular princes." (Ch. CVII) Princes who embraced the Reformation could secularize church lands and assert independence from both the Emperor and the Pope.

The spread of Lutheranism was documented in Scandinavia as well. Voltaire records the progress of Lutheranism in Sweden, Denmark and Germany (Ch. CVII). Under Gustavus Vasa, Sweden became a Lutheran kingdom, while Denmark followed a similar path under Christian III.

## Impact and Legacy

Lutheranism's political consequences were far-reaching. The movement gave German princes a religious cause around which to resist imperial centralization under Charles V. As Voltaire describes, "the Lutheran religion preserved France, and did her more service than the Turks... The princes of that persuasion in Germany, jealous of the overgrown power of Charles, had entered into a confederacy against him." (Ch. CIV)

The Peace of Augsburg (1555) formally recognized the principle of *cuius regio, eius religio*—the ruler determines the religion of his territory—making Lutheranism a legally established confession within the Empire. This settlement, though imperfect, established the precedent that religious pluralism could be managed through political negotiation rather than only through force.

## Key Figures

- **Martin Luther** — Augustinian friar and theologian who initiated the Reformation
- **Frederick the Wise** — Elector of Saxony who protected Luther
- **Philip Melanchthon** — Luther's colleague who systematized Lutheran theology
- **Gustavus Vasa** — King of Sweden who established Lutheranism in his realm
