## Overview

The tithe (from Old English *teogoþa*, "tenth") was a compulsory ecclesiastical tax requiring the faithful to contribute one-tenth of their agricultural produce, livestock, and later other forms of income to the Church. It formed the economic foundation of the medieval and early modern Church, providing the income that sustained the [[clergy]], maintained church buildings, funded charitable works, and supported the ecclesiastical hierarchy from parish priests to the [[papacy]].

By the 16th century, the tithe system had become deeply intertwined with the [[benefice]] system and was a frequent source of conflict between clergy and laity. The [[the-reformation|Reformation]] brought fundamental changes to tithing in Protestant territories, though the institution itself persisted in modified forms.

## Origins and Context

Tithing has biblical precedents in the Old Testament (Leviticus 27:30–33) and was institutionalized in Western Europe during the Carolingian period (8th–10th centuries), when secular rulers made tithe payment legally compulsory. By the High Middle Ages, tithes were a universal obligation throughout Latin Christendom, typically divided into four portions: for the bishop, the parish clergy, the fabric of the church, and the poor.

## Key Features

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Great tithes | Levied on major crops: wheat, barley, oats, and wool |
| Small tithes | Levied on minor produce: poultry, eggs, vegetables, and fruit |
| Personal tithes | Levied on income from trade, labor, or professions |
| Mixed tithes | Levied on animal products: milk, cheese, and offspring |
| Appropriation | The practice by which monasteries and other institutions collected tithes in place of the local parish priest |

## Historical Development

By the late Middle Ages, the tithe system had generated substantial wealth for the Church. Monasteries controlled extensive agricultural lands and received tithes alongside other revenues. In many regions, the Church owned between one-quarter and one-third of all arable land — property held in "dead hands" (*mortmain*) that could never be sold or inherited. This concentration of wealth provoked resentment from both peasants burdened by tithe payments and princes who saw Church lands as a source of untapped revenue. (Ch. CVI)

The [[the-reformation|Reformation]] brought significant changes to tithing. In Protestant territories, tithes were often redirected from the Church to secular authorities or to support Protestant ministries and schools. The dissolution of the [[monasteries|monasticism]] in England under [[henry-viii|Henry VIII]] transferred vast monastic estates and their associated tithe rights to the Crown and lay purchasers, fundamentally reshaping the economic landscape.

## Impact and Legacy

The tithe system contributed to anti-clerical sentiment that fueled support for the Reformation. The perception that the Church extracted wealth from the laity while providing inadequate spiritual service was a recurring grievance. The [[the-reformation|Reformers]] did not abolish tithing — Luther and Calvin both affirmed the principle — but they redirected its revenues and challenged the theology that underpinned compulsory payment as a means of earning [[salvation]].

Tithes remained a feature of European life into the modern era. In France, they were abolished during the Revolution (1789); in England, they were commuted to money payments in 1836 and finally abolished in 1936. The controversy over tithes illustrates the persistent tension between the material foundations of religious institutions and their spiritual claims.
