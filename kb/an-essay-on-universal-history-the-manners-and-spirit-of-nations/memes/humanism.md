## Overview

Renaissance humanism was the intellectual movement that placed the study of classical antiquity — its languages, literature, philosophy, and history — at the center of education and cultural life. Originating in 14th-century Italy and reaching its zenith in the 15th and 16th centuries, humanism transformed European learning by shifting focus from medieval scholastic theology to the *studia humanitatis*: grammar, rhetoric, poetry, history, and moral philosophy, as embodied in the rediscovered texts of ancient Greece and Rome.

## Origins and Context

Humanism emerged from the confluence of several historical developments: the influx of Greek scholars and manuscripts into Italy following the fall of Constantinople (1453); the patronage of wealthy Italian city-states, particularly Florence under the Medici; and the invention of printing, which massively expanded access to classical texts. The movement's intellectual roots lay in the work of Petrarch (1304–1374), who championed the recovery of classical Latin style and the moral wisdom of ancient authors.

## The Medicean Age and Papal Patronage

Voltaire provides a vivid account of humanism at its peak under Pope Leo X (r. 1513–1521), a member of the Medici family. Leo and his brothers "were disciples of Angelus Politianus, and Chalcondylas, and showed themselves deserving of such masters. They expressed a high emulation in the encouraging of letters and the polite arts; so as to deserve that this should be stiled the Medicean age." (Ch. CVI)

Under Leo X, the papal court became a center of classical scholarship and literary refinement: "the barbarous stile of the datary was abolished, to make room for the eloquence of the cardinals Bembo and Sadoletus, at that time the pope's secretaries, men who knew how to imitate Cicero's latinity." (Ch. CVI) This transformation of papal chancery Latin from medieval ecclesiastical style to Ciceronian elegance was itself a humanist revolution at the heart of the Church.

## The Flourishing of Arts and Letters

Voltaire devotes an entire chapter (Ch. C, "Customs of the Fifteenth and Sixteenth Centuries, and the State of the Polite Arts") to chronicling the cultural achievements of Renaissance Italy. He compares the period to classical Athens: "Nothing can give us a stronger idea of ancient Greece: for as the arts flourished in Greece in the midst of foreign and civil wars, so they had the same fate in Italy."

Key achievements he catalogues include:

| Field | Achievement | Notable Figures |
|-------|-------------|-----------------|
| History | Guicciardin's history of the Italian wars | Guicciardin, Machiavelli |
| Poetry | Ariosto's *Orlando Furioso*, Tasso's *Jerusalem Delivered* | Ariosto, Tasso |
| Drama | Machiavelli's *Mandragora* — "preferable perhaps to all Aristophanes's comedies" | Machiavelli |
| Painting | "Carried to a pitch of perfection unknown to the ancient Greeks" | — |
| Architecture | St. Peter's Basilica — "the finest, the largest, and the boldest temple the world ever beheld" | — |
| Sculpture | "Not falling far short of those models" (the Greeks) | — |
| Music | "Greatly superior to that of the Greeks" | — |

## Irony of Papal Patronage

Voltaire notes a profound historical irony: Leo X, by patronizing learning and the arts, inadvertently contributed to the Reformation that would shatter papal authority. "It may be said that Leo X, by the encouragement he gave to learning, furnished arms against himself. I have heard an English nobleman affirm, that he had seen a letter from cardinal Pool to that pope, wherein he congratulated him upon his success in the propagation of literature, but at the same time reminded him that it was of dangerous consequence to make mankind too learned." (Ch. CVI)

This tension — between the cultivation of critical thought and the maintenance of ecclesiastical authority — would define the relationship between humanism and the Reformation. Many early reformers, including Luther and Calvin, were trained in humanist methods of textual criticism, which they applied to Scripture with revolutionary consequences.

## Impact and Legacy

While religious wars "were productive of barbarism and ruins" in Germany, France, and England, "Italy completely at rest... applied herself more than ever to the improvement of the liberal arts." Ten popes "successively and almost without interruption, contributed to finish the church of St. Peter, and at the same time encouraged the other arts. Nothing of the like kind was to be seen in the rest of Europe. In a word Italy alone had then the glory of being the seat of genius and taste, a glory heretofore confined to Greece." (Ch. C)

Humanism's legacy extended far beyond Italy. Its methods of textual scholarship, its educational ideals, its rediscovery of classical political thought, and its emphasis on human dignity and potential shaped the intellectual foundations of early modern Europe — from the Protestant and Catholic Reformations to the Scientific Revolution.
