## Overview

A religious [[sect]] in early modern Europe was a group of believers who separated from the established church to follow a particular interpretation of Christian doctrine, discipline, or practice. The 16th-century [[the-reformation|Reformation]] shattered the medieval unity of Western Christendom, producing a proliferation of sects — Lutheran, Calvinist, Zwinglian, Anabaptist, and many smaller groups — each claiming to have recovered the authentic teaching of Scripture.

Voltaire observed the centrifugal logic of sectarian fragmentation: "It is certain that unless there was some authority in the Christian world to determine the sense of scripture and the dogmas of religion, there would be as many sects as there are men that know how to read." (Ch. CVIII) His history of the Reformation chronicles this multiplication of sects across Germany, Switzerland, England, and France, treating it both as a theological catastrophe and a historical inevitability.

## Origins and Context

The concept of sect (*secta* in Latin, meaning a school of thought or way of life) predates Christianity. In antiquity, philosophical schools such as the Stoics and Epicureans were termed sects. The early church itself was initially regarded as a Jewish sect. In the medieval period, groups that deviated from Catholic orthodoxy — the [[albigensian-crusade|Albigensians]], [[waldensian|Waldensians]], and followers of [[wycliffe|John Wycliffe]] and [[jan-hus|Jan Hus]] — were condemned as heretical sects rather than legitimate alternatives. (Ch. CXVI)

The Reformation transformed the meaning of sect. When Luther, Zwingli, and Calvin each established distinct ecclesiastical structures with their own confessions of faith, catechisms, and liturgies, the term "sect" came to designate organized alternatives to the Roman Church rather than small heretical conventicles. Yet the pejorative connotation persisted: Catholics used "sect" to deny the legitimacy of Protestant churches, while Protestants applied it to groups they considered beyond the pale of orthodoxy.

## Key Features

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Doctrinal distinctiveness | Each sect defined itself by specific theological positions on [[sacrament|sacraments]], [[predestination]], or church governance |
| Separate ecclesial structure | Sects established their own ministers, synods, consistories, and disciplinary procedures |
| Confessional identity | Written confessions of faith (Augsburg Confession, Helvetic Confession) formalized sectarian boundaries |
| Mutual anathema | Sects commonly condemned each other as well as Rome; Lutherans and Calvinists persecuted Anabaptists |
| Political alliance | Sects aligned with princes and city magistrates who adopted their confession (*cuius regio, eius religio*) |
| Persecution dynamic | Sects were frequently persecuted by established churches, creating martyrologies that reinforced group identity |

## Historical Development

The first major sect of the Reformation was the Lutheran, which "was soon established without opposition in Sweden, and in Denmark, immediately after the tyrant had been expelled." (Ch. CVIII) In Germany, the [[peace-of-augsburg|Peace of Augsburg]] (1555) legally recognized the Lutheran confession alongside Catholicism, formally ending the medieval ideal of a single Christian commonwealth.

Zwingli's reformation in Zurich produced a distinct sect. "He was called in France a sacramentist, a name afterwards given to his whole sect." (Ch. CVII) Zwingli's doctrine of the Eucharist as a purely symbolic memorial distinguished his followers from both Lutherans (who affirmed Christ's real presence) and Catholics (who maintained transubstantiation). After Zwingli's death in battle, "his religion was afterwards called Calvinism, a name it took from Calvin, as the new world discovered by Columbus was denominated from Americus Vespucius." (Ch. CVII)

[[calvinism|Calvinism]] became the most dynamic and politically charged sect. "It was he that established synods, consistories, and deacons; and that regulated the form of prayer and public preaching: he even instituted a consistorial jurisdiction, with the right of excommunication." (Ch. CXII) Calvin's Geneva became the model for Reformed churches in France (the [[huguenot|Huguenots]]), the Netherlands (the Dutch Reformed Church), Scotland (the Kirk), and England (the Puritans).

The [[anabaptism|Anabaptists]] represented the radical wing of the Reformation. Voltaire distinguishes between the violent German Anabaptists — "a collectitious multitude of savage boors, who carried their fanatic fury to every excess" — and the peaceful English Anabaptists who were "in reality mere deists" and "the forerunners of those peaceful quakers." (Ch. CXIV) The English sect rejected infant baptism, the Trinity, and the doctrine of the Fall, foreshadowing later Unitarian and Quaker theology.

The Church of England constituted yet another sect, though Voltaire describes its origins in distinctly political terms: under Henry VIII and his successors, England oscillated between Catholicism and Protestantism depending on the monarch's preference, producing a national church that was "a medley of Sacramentarians and Lutherans." (Ch. CXIV)

In France, the sectarian dynamic produced civil war. "This was the sect that divided France for such a number of years." (Ch. CXII) The [[french-wars-of-religion|French Wars of Religion]] (1562–1598) pitted the Catholic majority against the Calvinist [[huguenot|Huguenot]] minority in a series of conflicts marked by massacres, assassinations, and foreign intervention.

## Impact and Legacy

The proliferation of sects in the 16th century permanently altered the religious and political landscape of Europe. Voltaire notes a crucial long-term consequence: the very multiplicity of sects created space for [[deism|deistic]] skepticism. "From these theological divisions baneful atheism arose in most parts of Europe." (Ch. CXIV) The exhaustion produced by sectarian violence contributed to the growth of [[toleration|religious toleration]] as a political necessity.

Voltaire's own perspective, shaped by Enlightenment [[deism]], regarded the multiplication of sects with ironic detachment: the disputes that generated civil wars and executions turned on theological subtleties that "neither themselves, nor the magistrates, nor Zwinglius could thoroughly understand." (Ch. CVII) This critical distance from sectarian enthusiasm became a hallmark of Enlightenment historiography.
