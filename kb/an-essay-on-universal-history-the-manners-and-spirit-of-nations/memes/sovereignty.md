## Overview

Sovereignty in the 16th century was the defining political concept around which European states organized themselves. It denoted supreme and indivisible authority within a territory—the power to make law, levy taxes, wage war, and dispense justice without external interference. Voltaire's narrative of the era is fundamentally a chronicle of sovereigns: Charles V, Francis I, Henry VIII, and Solyman each asserting and defending their sovereign prerogatives against rivals, subjects, and the Church.

## Origins and Context

The modern concept of sovereignty emerged gradually from the medieval system of overlapping jurisdictions, where feudal lords, bishops, the Holy Roman Emperor, and the Pope all claimed various forms of authority over the same territories. By the 16th century, monarchs were consolidating power, asserting that their authority within their realms was absolute and answerable to no external power—a doctrine later formalized by Jean Bodin.

Voltaire describes the practical exercise of sovereignty through Charles V's actions: "He disposes of all Lombardy like an absolute sovereign. He grants to Francis Sforza the investiture of the Milanese, and to Alexander of Medicis that of Tuscany; he names a duke of Mantua; he makes the pope restore Modena and Reggio to the duke of Ferrara: but all this he does for money." (Ch. CIII)

## Key Features

The exercise of sovereignty in the 16th century involved:

- **Territorial Jurisdiction**: Supreme authority over a defined geographical domain
- **Right of War and Peace**: The sovereign's exclusive prerogative to declare war and conclude treaties
- **Law-Making Power**: The authority to issue edicts and administer justice within the realm
- **Taxation**: The power to raise revenue, increasingly centralized under royal control
- **Church Relations**: The contested boundary between royal sovereignty and papal authority over ecclesiastical matters

The practical limits of sovereignty were constantly tested. Charles V, despite being the most powerful sovereign in Europe, could not achieve universal monarchy. Voltaire explains: "The system of an equilibrium of power was at this very period established in Europe; for immediately after the taking of Francis I, England and the Italian states entered into a league with France to balance the power of the emperor." (Ch. CIII)

## Historical Development

The 16th century saw sovereignty both consolidated and constrained. The Reformation introduced a new dimension: princes who embraced Protestantism asserted sovereignty over churches within their territories, rejecting papal jurisdiction. The Peace of Augsburg (1555) formalized the principle of *cuius regio, eius religio*—the ruler determines the religion of his realm—making religious authority an attribute of sovereignty.

Yet sovereignty was never truly absolute in practice. Even Charles V, at the height of his power, found his will checked by the German princes, the French king, the Pope, and the Ottoman sultan. His eventual abdication was in part a recognition that sovereignty over such vast and dispersed dominions was unsustainable.

## Impact and Legacy

The concept of sovereignty that crystallized in the 16th century formed the foundation of the modern state system. The equilibrium of power that Voltaire identifies—where "the several great states being a check to each other" (Ch. CIII)—became the organizing principle of European international relations for centuries. Sovereignty evolved from personal rule by a monarch to the abstract authority of the state, but its core claim—supreme authority within borders—remains the bedrock of international law.
