## Overview

Canon law constituted the legal system of the medieval and early modern Catholic Church, a comprehensive body of legislation governing ecclesiastical offices, sacramental discipline, clerical conduct, and church-state relations. Unlike secular legal systems rooted in royal or imperial authority, canon law derived its force from papal decretals, conciliar canons, and the accumulated jurisprudence of church courts. During the [[the-reformation|Reformation]], canon law became a contested terrain, as reformers challenged both specific provisions—particularly those concerning papal fiscal prerogatives—and the broader principle of papal legislative supremacy.

Voltaire's narrative of the sixteenth century reveals canon law as a pervasive yet increasingly contested framework. References to "the cannon law" appear in connection with the fiscal machinery of the [[papacy]]—the system of reservations, provisions, expectatives, and [[annates]] that reformers across Europe condemned as exploitative innovations rather than legitimate ecclesiastical legislation. (Ch. CVI, CXVI)

## Origins and Context

Canon law developed through the accumulation of papal decretals, conciliar legislation, and the work of medieval canonists. The *Decretum Gratiani* (c. 1140) provided the foundational synthesis, organizing disparate canons into a systematic legal corpus. Subsequent papal legislation—particularly the decretals of Gregory IX (1234) and Boniface VIII (1298)—expanded the scope of canon law into virtually every domain of ecclesiastical life.

By the late medieval period, canon law governed an extraordinarily wide range of matters: the conferral and tenure of [[benefice|benefices]], episcopal elections, [[monasticism|monastic]] discipline, matrimonial causes, testamentary dispositions, and the prosecution of [[heresy]]. The church courts constituted a parallel judicial system, often competing with secular tribunals for jurisdiction over clergy and ecclesiastical property.

## The Fiscal Apparatus

Voltaire's account highlights canon law's role in sustaining the papal fiscal system, particularly through the legal mechanisms governing benefices. The chancery of [[leo-x|Leo X]] operated what amounted to a comprehensive tariff of ecclesiastical dispensations. Voltaire records the fixed prices established for various offenses (Ch. CVI):

| Offense | Approximate Cost |
|---------|-----------------|
| Murder by a subdeacon or deacon | ~20 crowns (with permission to hold three benefices) |
| Assassination by a bishop or abbot | ~300 livres |
| Bestiality | 250 livres |

"All manner of uncleanness, even the most shocking to nature, had its settled price," Voltaire observes, noting that "dispensations were granted, not only for past but future sins." While acknowledging that "never did any council rank the taxation of sins among the articles of faith," Voltaire presents this system as evidence of the corruption that had overtaken the church's legal machinery on the eve of the Reformation. (Ch. CVI)

## The Concordat and Legal Terminology

The Concordat of 1516 between [[francis-i|Francis I]] and [[leo-x|Leo X]] represents a significant moment in the application of canon law to church-state relations. The concordat's terminology reveals the sophistication of the legal concepts at work—mandates, reservations, expectatives, and preventions were all technical terms of canon law governing appointments to ecclesiastical offices. Voltaire's text provides definitions of these terms (Ch. CXVI):

- **Mandate**: Where the pope, by virtue of his prerogatives, nominated to a benefice
- **Reservation**: A clause whereby the pope retained an appointment right to himself
- **Expectative**: A bull granting a future benefice before it became vacant
- **Prevention**: The right of a superior to claim jurisdiction before an inferior

These legal mechanisms constituted the infrastructure through which the [[papacy]] exercised patronage and extracted revenues from the national churches of Europe.

## Reformation Challenges

The Reformation fundamentally challenged the authority of canon law. When [[henry-viii|Henry VIII]] declared himself supreme head of the English church, he simultaneously asserted that English ecclesiastical law derived from royal rather than papal authority. The study of canon law at English universities was suppressed and replaced by civil law. Continental reformers went further, arguing that the very concept of a distinct ecclesiastical legal system lacked scriptural foundation—the church, in Luther's view, should be governed by the Word of God rather than by papal decretals. (Ch. CXIII)

## Impact and Legacy

The Reformation's assault on canon law contributed to the secularization of European legal systems. In Protestant territories, church courts were either abolished or subordinated to state authority. Even in Catholic countries, the post-Tridentine papacy exercised greater caution in its legislative claims, aware that the fiscal exploitation of canon law had been among the Reformation's most effective recruiting tools. The canon law that survived the Reformation—reformed and codified—continued to govern the internal life of the [[catholicism|Catholic]] Church, though its jurisdiction over the laity and its claims against secular authority were substantially reduced.
