## Overview

The figure of the prince in Voltaire's Essay on Universal History embodies the fundamental tension between centralized authority and territorial independence that shaped early modern Europe. Princes were not merely noblemen but rulers — often sovereign within their own domains — who could make war, levy taxes, and determine the religion of their subjects. "Luther succeeded in exciting the princes, nobility and magistrates against the pope and the bishops" (Ch. CIX), and this alignment of princely authority with the Reformation was, in Voltaire's analysis, the decisive political fact of the sixteenth century.

The [[the-reformation|Reformation]] provided German princes with a cause around which to resist imperial centralization, transforming what might have remained a theological dispute into a permanent restructuring of European political geography. Voltaire's treatment distinguishes between the constructive role of princes in Germany — where they sustained constitutional [[liberty]] against Habsburg overreach — and the destructive potential of princely independence elsewhere, particularly in Poland and Hungary.

## Origins and Context

The concept of princely sovereignty within the Holy Roman Empire evolved from the medieval fragmentation of Carolingian authority. By the Golden Bull of 1356, seven electors — three ecclesiastical and four secular princes — held the exclusive right to elect the emperor, formalizing what had long been practice. This constitutional recognition of princely power made the [[empire]] unique among European monarchies: the emperor presided over a federation of sovereign territories rather than a unified state.

The Peace of Augsburg (1555) marked the culmination of princely authority by establishing the principle of *cuius regio, eius religio* — the ruler determines the religion of his territory. Voltaire notes the consequences with characteristic irony: princes who had once submitted their disputes to the pope now asserted the right to determine what faith their subjects would profess, a power that even medieval emperors had not claimed so absolutely.

## Key Features

Voltaire identifies several defining characteristics of princely authority in its early modern form:

| Feature | Expression | Voltaire's Judgment |
|---------|-----------|---------------------|
| **Territorial sovereignty** | Princes ruled independent domains within the empire | Source of German liberty but also of political fragmentation |
| **Religious determination** | *Cuius regio, eius religio* after 1555 | A power that "excited the princes against the pope" |
| **Electoral function** | Seven electors chose the Holy Roman Emperor | Constitutional check on imperial power |
| **Military capacity** | Princes maintained armies and waged war | Could destabilize Europe or defend liberty |
| **Ecclesiastical principality** | Bishops and abbots ruled as territorial princes | Fusion of spiritual and temporal power unique to the empire |

> "The imperial chamber, paid by the princes and by the cities, and did not reside in the emperor's private demesnes, this was also a support of the public liberty." (Ch. XCIX)

## Historical Development

The evolution of princely power in Voltaire's period followed two divergent paths. In the Holy Roman Empire, the imperial diet (Reichstag) and the imperial chamber (Reichskammergericht) formed a conciliar constitution that balanced the emperor's authority against princely independence. The diet included both secular and ecclesiastical princes — [[bishop|bishops]] and abbots who held territorial sovereignty alongside their spiritual authority — a fusion that was "a distinctive feature of the imperial constitution" (Ch. CVI).

In France, the trajectory was opposite. "France alone, since the reign of Lewis XI, was become an absolute monarchy" (Ch. XCVIII), having subordinated its great princes to the crown. The difference, Voltaire suggests, was decisive: France became Europe's most powerful state precisely because its princes no longer functioned as independent rulers, while the German princes' successful defense of their liberties came at the cost of imperial weakness.

The Protestant Reformation accelerated these divergent paths. German princes who adopted Lutheranism or Calvinism gained not only theological justification for resisting the emperor but also material benefit — the secularization of church property and the elimination of papal taxation. The model of the cardinal-prince, embodied by figures like Cardinal Wolsey in England, represented the older fusion of ecclesiastical and secular authority that the Reformation would ultimately dissolve.

## Impact and Legacy

The princely order that Voltaire describes was the seedbed of modern federalism and religious pluralism. By preventing any single power from dominating Germany, the princes preserved a constitutional space in which multiple confessions and political forms could coexist. Yet this achievement came at a cost: the Thirty Years' War, fought largely over the scope of princely religious authority, devastated Central Europe.

Voltaire's comparative analysis reveals a deeper pattern. Countries that broke decisively with princely independence — France, England — became strong states. Those where princely liberty survived as noble privilege — Poland, Hungary — suffered military and political weakness. The German case was the exception: princely independence was balanced by imperial institutions in a constitutional equilibrium that Voltaire clearly admires, even as he records its costs.

## Key Figures

- **Frederick the Wise of Saxony** — Elector who protected Luther after the Diet of Worms, enabling the Reformation's survival
- **Philip of Hesse** — Leading Protestant prince whose military and political skill shaped the Schmalkaldic League
- **Maurice of Saxony** — Protestant prince whose shifting alliances demonstrated both the opportunities and dangers of princely sovereignty
- **Charles V** — Emperor whose reign was defined by the attempt to subordinate princely independence to imperial authority
