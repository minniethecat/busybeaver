## Overview

Commerce in sixteenth-century Europe was both an engine of urban wealth and a force transforming the balance of power among nations. Voltaire's *Essay* consistently treats commerce as a critical dimension of historical development, noting how the commercial prosperity of the Hanseatic towns, the trade networks of Venice and England, and the emerging Atlantic economy reshaped European politics. His observation that "commerce has gone thro' almost as many revolutions, as the government of empires" (Ch. XCVIII) reflects the centrality of trade to his understanding of civilization.

## Origins and Context

The commercial landscape of early modern Europe was shaped by several long-term developments: the decline of Mediterranean dominance after the Ottoman conquest of Constantinople, the rise of Atlantic trade following the Portuguese and Spanish voyages of discovery, and the emergence of northern European commercial networks centered on the Hanseatic League and the Low Countries.

Voltaire emphasizes that commercial wealth and military power were closely linked. The Hanseatic towns — Hamburg, Lubeck, Dantzick — "were able to resist those three kingdoms [Sweden, Denmark, Norway], because they had more wealth" (Ch. XCVIII). Lubeck, a single city, "waged war by itself against the successors of Margaret of Waldemar" — a striking demonstration that commercial capital could rival territorial power.

## Key Features

Commerce in Voltaire's account exhibits several distinctive characteristics:

> Voltaire notes that the English fur trade with Russia "have greatly contributed to enrich England. This is likewise a branch of trade, of which the Venetians were stripped" (Ch. XCVIII) — a zero-sum view of commercial competition.

## Historical Development

The sixteenth century saw dramatic shifts in commercial geography. The discovery of the sea route to India around the Cape of Good Hope and the Spanish conquests in the Americas redirected trade flows from the Mediterranean to the Atlantic. Portugal and Spain became the first Atlantic empires, but Voltaire notes that the ultimate beneficiaries were often the northern European maritime powers.

The English discovery of the port of Archangel in 1553, by Richard Chancellor, opened direct trade with Muscovy: "From that time the English have been almost the masters of the trade of Russia, whose valuable furs have greatly contributed to enrich England" (Ch. XCVIII). This was simultaneously a gain for England and a loss for Venice, whose fur trade "by the way of Constantinople" was undercut.

Commerce also played a crucial role in political events. Gustavus Vasa's liberation of Sweden depended on Lubeck's commercial credit: "Lubeck not only furnished him with arms, but likewise with troops, without which he would have found it very difficult to succeed. Thus the fate of Sweden depended on a little trading town" (Ch. XCVIII). Similarly, Francis I's ability to pay his enormous ransom after the [[battle-of-pavia]] depended on French commercial revenues.

## Impact and Legacy

Voltaire draws a pointed contrast between regions that embraced commerce and those that neglected it. Sweden and Denmark had "no regular commerce, no manufactures" until Gustavus Vasa "first drew the Swedes out of obscurity, and who likewise encouraged the Danes by his example" (Ch. XCVIII). Poland, by contrast, neglected commerce entirely: "The trade of corn and salt was left to Jews and to foreigners, who soon grew rich thro' the proud indolence of the nobles, and the slavery of the people" (Ch. XCVIII).

The relationship between commerce and religious institutions is a recurring theme. Voltaire observes bluntly that "the Portuguese sail to the East Indies merely for the sake of trade. Now trade and the inquisition are incompatible. Were it to be established at London, or at Amsterdam, those cities would neither be so powerful nor so opulent" (Ch. CIX). The [[the-inquisition]] and [[jesuits]] represented obstacles to the commercial spirit that was reshaping Europe.
