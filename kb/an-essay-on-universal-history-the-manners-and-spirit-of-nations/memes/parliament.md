## Overview

Parliament in the 16th century was the English institution through which the monarch consulted with representatives of the nobility, clergy, and commoners—primarily to secure consent for taxation. While still subordinate to the royal will under the Tudors, Parliament was steadily accumulating the institutional weight and precedent that would, in the following century, enable it to challenge royal absolutism. Voltaire, writing from a French perspective, observes the English parliamentary tradition as a distinctive feature of English governance.

## Origins and Context

The English Parliament originated in the medieval royal councils, evolving into a bicameral body with the House of Lords (nobility and bishops) and the House of Commons (elected representatives of counties and boroughs). By the reign of Henry VIII (1509–1547), Parliament had become an instrument for legitimizing royal policy—most dramatically in the break with Rome, which was effected through parliamentary statutes rather than royal decree alone.

The contrast with continental practice was significant. French monarchs convoked the Estates-General only in emergencies; the English Parliament met regularly. This institutional continuity gave Parliament a constitutional weight that no continental assembly possessed.

## Key Features

The 16th-century Parliament was characterized by:

- **Bicameral Structure**: Lords (hereditary peers and bishops) and Commons (elected members)
- **Fiscal Authority**: The principle that taxation required parliamentary consent
- **Statutory Legislation**: Laws enacted by "King-in-Parliament" carried supreme legal authority
- **Privilege**: Members enjoyed freedom of speech and freedom from arrest during sessions
- **Petition and Redress**: Parliament as a channel for presenting grievances to the Crown

## Historical Development

Under Henry VIII, Parliament was deployed as an instrument of the Reformation. The Act of Supremacy (1534), the dissolution of the monasteries, and the regulation of doctrine were all accomplished through parliamentary statute, establishing the principle that even matters of religion fell within Parliament's competence.

Voltaire notes the shifting alliances that characterized the period: "Henry VIII, king of England, concludes an alliance against France with that very emperor whose aunt he had so ignominiously repudiated, and whose cousin he had declared a bastard." (Ch. CIV) Such reversals underscore how parliamentary authority was exercised in service of royal policy.

The development of Parliament was part of a broader European pattern of representative institutions—from the Dutch States-General to the Swedish Riksdag—that mediated between sovereign power and the governed.

## Impact and Legacy

The 16th-century Parliament laid the groundwork for the constitutional conflicts of the 17th century—the English Civil War and the Glorious Revolution—that would definitively establish parliamentary supremacy. The English model of limited monarchy, where sovereignty was shared between Crown and Parliament, became an influential alternative to continental absolutism and was admired by Enlightenment thinkers including Voltaire himself.
