## Overview

Annates, also known as first fruits (*primitiæ*), were a ecclesiastical tax levied by the papacy on newly appointed [[bishop|bishops]], [[cardinal|abbots]], and other benefice-holders. The tax amounted to the first year's income of the [[benefice]], payable to the papal treasury in Rome. During the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries, annates became one of the most resented financial exactions imposed by the [[papacy]], and a central grievance cited by reformers in England, France, and Germany.

According to Voltaire's account, the annates constituted "the first fruits of ecclesiastical benefices, being the value of one year's profit, formerly paid to the pope." The system was perceived across much of Europe as a mechanism for draining national wealth to Rome without corresponding spiritual benefit. (Ch. CXIII)

## Origins and Context

The practice of annates emerged during the medieval consolidation of papal fiscal authority. By the late Middle Ages, the [[papacy]] had established a comprehensive system of ecclesiastical taxation, of which annates were among the most lucrative. Together with St. Peter's Pence, reservations, and provisions, annates formed part of the fiscal machinery that financed the Roman curia and papal patronage networks.

The legal basis for annates rested on canon law rather than scripture. The papacy claimed the right to tax benefices as the ultimate authority over church appointments, a claim increasingly contested by secular rulers who sought to retain these revenues within their own kingdoms. The tension between papal fiscal prerogatives and royal sovereignty became acute during the [[the-reformation|Reformation]] period. (Ch. CXIII, CXVI)

## Historical Development

### England under Henry VIII

The annates played a decisive role in the English [[the-reformation|Reformation]]. As Voltaire records, neither "St. Peter's pence, nor the reservations and provisos, nor the annates or first fruits, nor the levying of taxes, nor the sale of [[indulgences]], nor five hundred other exactions, constantly condemned by acts of parliament" could by themselves break England from Rome. The final rupture came with [[henry-viii|Henry VIII]]'s matrimonial crisis, but the annates had long prepared the ground of public resentment. (Ch. CXIII)

In 1534, Henry's [[parliament]] confirmed the Act of Supremacy, which "suppressed the pope's authority, and his annates, with St. Peter's pence, and the provision of benefices." Crucially, the Crown did not abolish the tax but redirected it: "The king prevailed on the parliament to grant him the annates or first fruits, which had been paid to the pope." The revenue now enriched the royal treasury rather than the papal court, and Henry used the proceeds to fund the establishment of six new bishoprics. (Ch. CXIII)

### France and the Concordat of 1516

In France, the annates persisted even after the Pragmatic Sanction of Bourges (1438) had nominally abolished them. Voltaire notes that "the popes continued to receive the annates for the most part, in spite of the pragmatic sanction." During the seventeen-year reign of [[louis-xii|Louis XII]], the papacy extracted "the exorbitant sum of three millions three hundred thousand livres" from the diocese of Paris alone. (Ch. CXVI)

The Concordat of 1516, negotiated between [[francis-i|Francis I]] and Pope [[leo-x|Leo X]] through Chancellor Duprat, established a controversial compromise. The king gained the right to nominate bishops, while the pope retained the first fruits by secret article. Voltaire observes that "it was said that the king and the pope gave to each other what did not belong to them." The University of Paris and the [[parliament]] vigorously opposed the concordat, but Francis I argued he had "rendered the nation" a service by reducing the annates to a fixed moderate sum—approximately four hundred thousand livres annually. (Ch. CXVI)

## Impact and Legacy

The annates controversy illustrates a broader pattern of the Reformation era: fiscal grievances against the [[papacy]] proved as potent as theological disputes in driving institutional separation from Rome. In England, the redirection of annates to the Crown provided a substantial financial incentive for the break with Rome. In France, the Concordat's retention of annates remained a source of Gallican resentment, contributing to the long-term tension between the French church and the papacy.

The annates system also reveals the structural weakness of papal authority: as Voltaire observes, "a power not founded on force, is really no power at all." When national sentiment turned against the papacy, the fiscal apparatus that had sustained it for centuries collapsed with remarkable speed. (Ch. CXIII)
