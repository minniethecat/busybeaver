## Overview

Scripture — the canonical texts of the Old and New Testaments — became the central contested authority of the sixteenth-century Reformation. The Protestant principle of *sola scriptura* (scripture alone) asserted that the Bible, interpreted by individual conscience, was the sole rule of Christian faith and practice, directly challenging the Catholic Church's claim to be the authoritative interpreter of revealed truth alongside written tradition. This elevation of scripture above ecclesiastical authority proved to be the most consequential intellectual move of the Reformation, fracturing the unity of Western Christendom and generating an endless proliferation of competing interpretations.

Voltaire observed that the appeal to scripture was wielded by all parties in the religious conflicts of the era. [[luther-martin|Martin Luther]] "offered to prove that the scripture no where makes express mention of purgatory," undermining the sacramental and penitential system of the late medieval Church. Yet the same scripture was invoked by the [[anabaptist|Anabaptists]] to justify radical social revolution, by the Catholic faculty of Paris to condemn Luther's rejection of Aristotle, and by kings and princes to legitimize their break from Roman authority. As Voltaire noted with characteristic irony, the most learned disputants "rummaged into the dark archives of antiquity, to find out pretences for separating from the church of Rome, in regard to mysteries which weak mortals can never fathom." (Ch. CVII)

## Scripture and the Reformation

The Reformation's turn to scripture was, in its origins, a move of simplification. Luther's project began not with a new theological system but with a conviction that the Bible, translated into vernacular languages and made accessible to the laity, would resolve the abuses and corruptions that had accumulated over centuries of ecclesiastical development. The [[doctrine]] of transubstantiation, the sale of [[indulgences]], the existence of [[purgatory]], and the sacrificial character of the [[mass|Mass]] were all challenged on the grounds that they lacked explicit scriptural warrant.

Luther's German translation of the Bible (1522–1534) was a watershed in European cultural history, providing not only a theological instrument but also a foundational text for the modern German language. The printing press, which Voltaire credited with transforming the intellectual landscape of Europe, multiplied the circulation of vernacular Bibles at an unprecedented rate. By the mid-sixteenth century, scripture was no longer the exclusive possession of a Latinate clerical elite but a book that could be read, debated, and interpreted by literate laypeople across Protestant Europe. (Ch. CVII)

## The Problem of Interpretation

The appeal to scripture alone quickly proved to be an unstable foundation for doctrinal unity. As Voltaire documented, the Reformation fragmented almost immediately into competing confessions, each claiming scriptural warrant for its positions. Luther, [[zwingli-huldrych|Zwingli]], and [[calvin-john|Calvin]] all appealed to the same Bible yet arrived at irreconcilable conclusions on the Eucharist, predestination, and the nature of the Church. "The question about [[free-will]], that other rock on which human reason has so often split, proved an endless source of nonsensical quarrels, to supply the torrent of theological hatred." (Ch. CVII)

The Anabaptists carried the logic of scriptural literalism to its most radical conclusion. Rejecting infant baptism on the grounds that the New Testament only records adult baptisms, they claimed direct inspiration and sought to reconstruct the primitive church from scriptural models alone. Voltaire recorded how these enthusiasts, "who knew no more of scripture, than that the enemies of the Lord ought to be put to the sword without mercy," established a theocratic kingdom at Munster, demonstrating the destructive potential of unmediated scriptural interpretation when separated from institutional and rational constraints. (Ch. CIX)

## Scripture and Political Authority

The Reformation appeal to scripture had profound political consequences. When Henry VIII of England sought to annul his marriage to Catherine of Aragon, he turned to scripture — specifically Leviticus — as the justification. The [[sorbonne|Sorbonne]] and other universities were enlisted to provide theological opinions on the scriptural validity of the marriage, transforming what was essentially a dynastic matter into a hermeneutical contest. Voltaire noted dryly that "the universities of France, and especially the Sorbonne, declared that Henry's marriage with Catharine of Arragon was illegal, and that the pope had no right to dispense with the law of Leviticus." The Bible had become an instrument of statecraft. (Ch. CXIII)

The [[council-of-trent|Council of Trent]] (1545–1563) responded to the Protestant challenge by defining the Catholic doctrine of scripture and tradition. Against *sola scriptura*, Trent affirmed that divine revelation is transmitted through both written scripture and unwritten apostolic tradition, with the Church alone possessing the authority to interpret both authentically. This definition hardened the confessional boundary between Catholic and Protestant Europe, making the status of scripture a marker of political as well as religious allegiance. (Ch. CXVII)
