## Overview

**Absolution** is the priestly declaration that a penitent's sins are forgiven, pronounced at the conclusion of the [[confession|sacrament of penance]]. In Catholic theology, the priest acts _in persona Christi_ (in the person of Christ), and the words of absolution — "I absolve you from your sins in the name of the Father, and of the Son, and of the Holy Spirit" — effect what they signify. The [[the-reformation|Reformation]]'s challenge to this doctrine struck at the heart of the medieval understanding of priesthood, sacramental power, and the economy of salvation. (Ch. CVI)

## Theological Foundations

The Catholic doctrine of absolution rests on several scriptural passages, chief among them Christ's words to the apostles after the resurrection: "Whose sins you shall forgive, they are forgiven them; and whose sins you shall retain, they are retained" (John 20:23). The Church interpreted this as Christ's institution of the sacrament of penance and the grant of the power of the keys — the authority to bind and loose — to the apostolic ministry and its successors.

By the high Middle Ages, scholastic theology had elaborated a precise account of how absolution worked: the priest's words were the instrumental cause of forgiveness, while God was the principal cause. The _ex opere operato_ principle held that the sacrament was efficacious by its very performance, provided the priest had valid orders and the penitent placed no obstacle (such as lack of contrition) in the way. (Ch. CVI, CIX)

| Aspect | Catholic Position | Protestant Critique |
|--------|------------------|-------------------|
| Authority | Priest acts as Christ's instrument | Only God forgives sins; no human mediation |
| Efficacy | _Ex opere operato_ | Faith alone receives forgiveness |
| Necessity | Required for mortal sins post-baptism | Not a sacrament; pastoral counsel optional |
| Formula | "I absolve you..." (indicative) | Declaration of gospel promise (declaratory) |

## The Reformation Challenge

[[martin-luther|Luther's]] attack on absolution was radical. He denied that any priest had the power to forgive sins by virtue of ordination. The "power of the keys," he argued, was the authority to preach the gospel — to declare God's forgiveness to the penitent believer — not to effect it sacramentally. The words of absolution were declaratory (announcing what God had already done) rather than efficacious (making it happen).

This redefinition struck at the ontological distinction between clergy and laity. If absolution was merely the declaration of the gospel promise, then any Christian could pronounce it — the pastor's words carried no special power that a layperson's did not. The [[priesthood-of-all-believers|priesthood of all believers]] dissolved the sacramental basis of clerical authority. (Ch. CVI)

> Luther succeeded in exciting the princes, nobility and magistrates against the pope and the bishops. (Ch. CIX)

The Council of Trent (1551) responded by reaffirming the Catholic doctrine in precise terms: absolution was a judicial act, not merely a declaratory one; the priest acted as judge in the tribunal of penance; and the words "I absolve you" were the necessary form of the sacrament. The confessional divide over absolution thus mirrored and reinforced the deeper division over the nature of the Church and its ministry. (Ch. CIX)

## Voltaire's Commentary

Voltaire treats the controversy over absolution with the same ironic detachment he brings to all disputes over sacerdotal power. He notes that the power to forgive sins — to open and shut the gates of heaven — was an enormous source of clerical authority, one that the Reformers were understandably reluctant to abandon entirely. Luther retained a modified form of private absolution, and even [[calvinism|Calvinist]] consistories exercised a kind of moral jurisdiction that echoed the confessional.

What Voltaire finds noteworthy is not the theological subtlety of the dispute but its social consequences: the power of absolution, when combined with the doctrine of [[purgatory|purgatory]] and the sale of [[indulgences|indulgences]], had created a vast system of spiritual taxation that enriched Rome at the expense of the faithful. The Reformation, whatever its own faults, broke this monopoly. (Ch. CVI, CIX)
