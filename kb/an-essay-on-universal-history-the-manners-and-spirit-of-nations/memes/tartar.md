## Overview

The Tartars appear in Voltaire's Essay on Universal History primarily as a counterpoint to European political development — a people whose conquests and customs illuminate, by contrast, the distinctive character of Western state formation. Voltaire's treatment of the Tartars is neither ethnographic nor systematic; it is strategic, deploying the Tartar example to make arguments about [[despotism]], [[diplomacy]], and the relationship between nomadic conquest and settled civilization.

The most striking Tartar episode in Voltaire's narrative concerns the ceremonial submission of the Russian grand duke to a Tartar ambassador, in which the grand duke "prostrated himself and offered milk" as a gesture of ritualized submission (Ch. XCVIII). Voltaire presents this as evidence of "the older, more ritualized form of diplomatic encounter that still persisted on Europe's eastern margins even as the western powers adopted more sophisticated practices."

## Origins and Context

The Tartar peoples — a term Voltaire uses broadly to encompass the various Turco-Mongol nomadic confederations of the Eurasian steppe — had dominated Russia from the thirteenth century through the Mongol invasion and the establishment of the Golden Horde. By the sixteenth century, the period of Voltaire's primary focus, Tartar power was in decline relative to the rising Russian state, but the legacy of Tartar domination continued to shape Russian political culture.

Voltaire situates the Tartars within a broader comparative framework of Asian empires. Like the [[mogul|Moguls]] of India and the [[ottoman-empire|Ottoman Turks]], the Tartars represented a form of conquest-based rule that stood in contrast to the legal and institutional development of Western Europe. Yet Voltaire is careful not to reduce these societies to simple Oriental despotism; his analysis acknowledges the complexity and durability of steppe political organization.

The Tartar presence on Europe's eastern frontier was one of the factors that, in Voltaire's view, retarded the development of Russian political institutions. Russia's position as a buffer between European civilization and steppe nomadism gave its political development a distinctive character — more autocratic than the West, more centralized than the East.

## Historical Development

Voltaire traces the Tartar impact on European history through several phases. The initial Mongol conquests of the thirteenth century established Tartar hegemony over Russia, a domination that lasted nearly two centuries. The gradual emancipation of Moscow from Tartar overlordship — culminating in the reign of Ivan III (r. 1462–1505) — was, in Voltaire's framework, the precondition for Russia's emergence as a European power.

By the sixteenth century, the period of Voltaire's detailed narrative, residual Tartar powers — the Khanate of Crimea, the Nogai Horde, and smaller confederations — continued to exert military pressure on Russia's southern and eastern frontiers. The survival of Tartar diplomatic rituals, such as the grand duke's ceremonial prostration, testified to the enduring prestige of steppe political forms even as the military balance shifted decisively in Russia's favour.

Voltaire's chapter on the Tartars (Ch. CXXVII) extends his analysis beyond Europe to the broader history of the steppe peoples, drawing comparisons with the conquests of Genghis Khan and Timur. The Tartar conquests serve, in Voltaire's narrative, as a limit case for his theory of civilization: they demonstrate that military power and territorial extent are not, in themselves, measures of civilizational achievement.

## Impact and Legacy

The Tartar legacy in Voltaire's analysis is primarily negative — a case study in what European civilization was not. The steppe empires, for all their military prowess, produced no lasting institutions, no [[commerce]], no [[liberty]] of the kind Voltaire admired in the German imperial constitution or the Dutch Republic. Their conquests were, in his phrase, "extensive rather than durable."

Yet Voltaire is not simply dismissive. The Tartar example serves a critical function in his argument: it demonstrates that the development of European-style political institutions was not inevitable or universal. The contrast between Western legal development and steppe despotism reinforces Voltaire's case that Europe's distinctive path — toward [[monarchy]] tempered by law, toward [[commerce]], toward the "general policy of Europe" (Ch. XCVIII) — was a contingent historical achievement, not a natural state of affairs.
