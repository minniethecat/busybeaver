## Overview

**Rebellion** — organized armed resistance against established authority — is a recurring phenomenon in Voltaire's ''Essay on Universal History'', treated not as simple criminality but as a complex political act whose legitimacy depended on the justice of the authority being resisted. Voltaire repeatedly distinguishes between rebellion against legitimate sovereigns and resistance against tyranny, and he is notably skeptical of the term when wielded by the powerful to delegitimize political opponents. "It is great injustice in father Daniel to assert, that Geneva at that time acted in open rebellion against the duke of Savoy," he writes, noting that the duke "was not the sovereign of that city." (098-001)

The concept of rebellion in Voltaire's narrative spans religious uprisings, national liberation movements, dynastic revolts, and colonial resistance — each demanding careful judgment about the legitimacy of both the rebels' grievances and the authority they challenged.

## Origins and Context

In early modern political thought, rebellion was the ultimate crime against the divinely ordained order. The doctrine of [[monarchy|divine right]] held that resistance to the sovereign was resistance to God. Yet the 16th century witnessed an unprecedented series of rebellions — religious, political, and social — that challenged this doctrine in practice. The [[the-reformation|Reformation]] provided a powerful new justification for resistance: if a ruler commanded what God forbade, was rebellion not a duty?

Voltaire navigates this contested terrain with characteristic irony. He is sympathetic to rebels who resisted tyranny (the [[united-provinces|Dutch revolt]] against Spain) and dismissive of rebellions he considered merely destructive (theological factions massacring each other). His criterion is consistently secular: does the rebellion advance or retard "the progress of the human mind"?

## Historical Development

Voltaire examines rebellion through several case studies. The Swedish liberation under [[gustavus-vasa|Gustavus Vasa]] followed the Stockholm Bloodbath and the deposition of Christian II, whom "the four states of the kingdom" decreed to depose. (098-001) This was not, in Voltaire's view, a rebellion but a just act of national self-defense.

The case of [[geneva|Geneva]] is treated with particular care. Father Daniel, a Jesuit historian, asserted that Geneva "acted in open rebellion against the duke of Savoy." (098-001) Voltaire corrects this: Geneva "was a free Imperial town" that "shared the government with the bishop," and the bishop had improperly "resigned his rights to the duke of Savoy." (098-001) What the Catholic historian called rebellion was, in Voltaire's rendering, the defense of established municipal liberties.

The [[united-provinces|Dutch revolt]] against [[philip-ii|Philip II of Spain]] receives Voltaire's qualified admiration. "The Flemings are naturally good subjects, and bad slaves," he observes, adding pointedly: "The very dread of the inquisition made more protestants than all Calvin's books, among a people who surely have no natural disposition to novelty or rebellion." (098-001) The revolt was, in his analysis, provoked by Philip's tyranny rather than innate rebelliousness.

By contrast, Voltaire is withering about rebellions he considers merely destructive. The theological "rebellions, and the rage of theological frenzy" that produced "so many murders" in the [[french-wars-of-religion|French Wars of Religion]] represent, for him, the perversion of religious sentiment into political violence. (098-001) Similarly, attempts by [[pope|Pope Pius V]] to "raise a rebellion in Ireland against queen Elizabeth" exemplify the use of religion as a mask for political subversion. (098-001)

## Impact and Legacy

Rebellion, as Voltaire presents it, was a structural feature of early modern Europe — not an aberration but a predictable consequence of contested sovereignty, religious division, and the incomplete consolidation of the state. The rebellions that succeeded (the Dutch Republic, the Swiss cantons, Sweden under the Vasas) created new polities; those that failed were retrospectively branded as criminal.

Voltaire's nuanced treatment — distinguishing Geneva's legitimate defense of liberty from the destructive fury of religious civil war — reflects the Enlightenment's effort to develop secular criteria for judging political violence. Authority was not self-justifying; rebellion was not automatically illegitimate. Each case demanded examination of the facts, the claims, and the consequences.
