## Overview

Gustavus Vasa (1496–1560), known as Gustav I, was the king who liberated Sweden from the Kalmar Union under Danish domination and founded the Vasa dynasty. His reign (1523–1560) established Sweden as an independent power, introduced Lutheranism as the state religion during the [[the-reformation]], and laid the foundations of the modern Swedish state.

Voltaire presents him as a hero shaped by adversity—an escaped prisoner who rallied the Dalecarlian peasants and, with arms purchased on credit from Lübeck, defeated [[christian-ii-of-denmark]]. His consolidation of Lutheranism made him a key figure alongside [[charles-v]] in the religious politics of sixteenth-century Europe.

## Rise from Obscurity

Gustavus was the nephew of King Charles Canutson (Karl Knutsson). When [[christian-ii-of-denmark]] sought to reassert control over Sweden, he demanded hostages—among them the young Gustavus. Taken aboard Christian's fleet and transported to Denmark in chains, Gustavus escaped his imprisonment and returned to Sweden in disguise.

> Scarce were they arrived when he ordered them to be put in irons, and set sail for Denmark with his prey. (Ch. XCVIII)

Hiding among the peasants of Dalecarlia, he worked at the mines for subsistence and concealment. When he revealed his identity, the Dalecarlians—"those savage men, who detested tyranny"—rallied to his cause.

> He was obliged to hide himself for some time in the mountains of Dalecarlia, disguised in a peasant's habit: he even worked at the mines, either for his subsistence, or in order to be concealed. (Ch. XCVIII)

## The War of Liberation

Gustavus's army initially lacked firearms, which had given the Danes their superiority. He purchased muskets at Lübeck on his own credit, and the Hanseatic city also furnished him with troops. Voltaire observes that "the fate of Sweden depended on a little trading town." After defeating Christian II's generals, Gustavus took Stockholm and was elected king by the Swedes in 1523.

> Thus the fate of Sweden depended on a little trading town. (Ch. XCVIII)

## The Reformation in Sweden

Gustavus established Lutheranism as the state religion, advancing the cause of the [[the-reformation]] in northern Europe. He preserved the episcopal hierarchy while retrenching the revenues and power of the bishops.

The Dalecarlians who had helped him to the throne became the first to resist the religious changes, petitioning their king "not to wear short clothes after the French fashion, and that whosoever eat flesh on Fridays should be burnt." Voltaire uses this to illustrate the rustic simplicity with which the common people understood the religious divide.

## Legacy

Voltaire credits Gustavus with drawing Sweden out of obscurity and making foreign nations aware of the weight Sweden might have in European affairs. [[francis-i]] of France concluded an alliance with the Lutheran king, sending him the collar of his order despite the statutes—an irony Voltaire notes with satisfaction.

Gustavus secured the crown for his family, obtaining from the estates that succession would be hereditary—though with the right of election reserved should the line become extinct.
