## Overview

The Mass in Voltaire's sixteenth-century world was not merely a religious service but the central act of Western Christendom — the "Holy Sacrifice" in which, according to Catholic [[doctrine]], Christ's body and blood were made present on the altar through the words of consecration spoken by the priest. Voltaire notes with characteristic irony that even the most violent men of the age attended Mass: "the good chevalier Bayard always took care to have a mass celebrated, when he went to fight a duel" (Ch. XCVIII), illustrating how the sacred and the brutal coexisted without apparent contradiction.

The Mass was also the Reformation's most explosive point of contention. Luther's rejection of the sacrificial character of the Mass, Zwingli's reduction of the Eucharist to a memorial, and the Catholic reaffirmation of transubstantiation at Trent defined the theological fault lines that would divide Europe for centuries.

## Origins and Context

The Mass as a liturgical form developed gradually from the Last Supper narratives of the Gospels through the elaboration of Roman, Galilean, and other regional rites. By the sixteenth century, the Roman Rite — in Latin, with the priest facing the altar rather than the congregation, and with the canon recited in a low voice — was the universal norm of the Western Church.

The Mass was understood theologically as both a sacrament (conferring grace on the recipient) and a sacrifice (offered to God for the living and the dead). This double character was the foundation of the vast system of endowed Masses, chantry chapels, and purgatorial piety that structured late medieval religious life. It was also the foundation of the abuse that most enraged the reformers: the sale of Masses for money.

## Key Features

Voltaire's text illuminates several aspects of the Mass as practiced in the sixteenth century:

| Aspect | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Language** | Latin throughout; congregation largely passive |
| **Orientation** | Priest faced east (liturgical east), back to the people |
| **Frequency** | Daily in cathedrals and monasteries; Sunday obligation for laity |
| **Endowments** | Wealthy patrons funded perpetual Masses for their souls |
| **Music** | Polyphonic settings by composers like Palestrina |
| **Pre-Reformation critique** | Erasmus and others mocked mechanical attendance without understanding |

> "The people in several countries began to be startled at the Roman Catholic religion: for, after having restored the fine arts, and embellished Rome by the hands of the great masters, they could not help laughing at the rest of Europe, which was too gross and ignorant to imitate them." (Ch. CVI)

## Historical Development

The Mass became the visible marker of religious division in the sixteenth century. When Luther retained much of the Mass structure while altering its theology (the *Deutsche Messe* of 1526), he signaled that reform could be moderate. When Zwingli abolished the Mass entirely in Zurich and replaced it with a simple communion service four times a year, he signaled that reform could be radical.

The Council of Trent (1545–1563) responded by codifying the Roman Missal. The Tridentine Mass, promulgated by Pius V in 1570, standardized the liturgy throughout the Latin Church and suppressed all rites less than two hundred years old. It would remain essentially unchanged until the Second Vatican Council (1962–1965).

Voltaire is attentive to the political dimensions of liturgical conflict. In France, the "concordat" between Francis I and Leo X gave the king control over episcopal appointments, which in turn shaped how the Mass was celebrated and who controlled its revenues. The Mass was never merely a matter of theology; it was a matter of power, patronage, and money.

## Impact and Legacy

Voltaire's Enlightenment perspective leads him to treat the Mass with a mixture of historical respect and ironic distance. He records without comment the ubiquity of Mass attendance among even the most vicious characters of the age — the duelist who hears Mass before killing, the prince who endows Masses with wealth extracted from peasants. This is not hypocrisy in Voltaire's telling but the genuine coexistence of piety and violence in a society that saw no contradiction between them.

The Mass also figures in Voltaire's broader argument about the evolution of religion. The Reformation's attack on the Mass was, in his view, part of a wider movement of the human mind toward a simpler, more rational religion — a movement that would culminate in the Enlightenment itself.

## Key Figures

- **Martin Luther** — Reformer who retained much of the Mass structure while transforming its theology
- **Ulrich Zwingli** — Swiss reformer who abolished the Mass entirely in favor of a simple memorial service
- **Pope Pius V** — Pope who standardized the Roman Missal after Trent (1570)
- **Giovanni Pierluigi da Palestrina** — Composer whose polyphonic Mass settings became the model of Tridentine liturgical music
