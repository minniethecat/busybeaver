## Overview

Privilege, in the early modern European context, denoted the legal immunities and exclusive rights held by the [[nobility]] as a hereditary estate. These privileges ranged from exemption from taxation and ordinary justice to the power of life and death over [[peasantry|peasants]]. Voltaire's analysis of privilege focuses particularly on Eastern Europe—Poland, Hungary, and Sweden—where the weakness of royal authority permitted the development of what he condemns as extreme noble privilege bordering on anarchy.

Voltaire's central thesis is that privilege, when unchecked by royal power or representative institutions, degenerates into a form of licensed impunity. His account of Polish noble privileges provides the most detailed case study: "The nobles had also another privilege, in common with the palatines, that of not being confined for any crime, till they were juridically convicted. This indeed was the privilege of impunity." The nobles possessed the power of life and death over their peasants, and "what is most mortifying to human nature, is, that such a privilege should subsist to this very day." (Ch. XCVIII)

## Origins and Context

The system of noble privilege originated in the medieval [[feudalism|feudal]] order, where military service in exchange for land tenure gradually hardened into hereditary status with attached legal immunities. By the sixteenth century, these privileges had become codified in the constitutions of most European kingdoms, though their extent varied dramatically by region.

In Western Europe—particularly in France under [[louis-xii|Louis XII]] and [[francis-i|Francis I]]—royal authority had progressively curtailed the most extreme noble privileges. In Eastern Europe, by contrast, the absence of strong central monarchy permitted privileges to expand unchecked. Voltaire notes that the Hungarian nobility "were petty tyrants, who would not be tyrannized themselves. Their liberty was a fatal independency, and they had reduced the rest of the nation to so miserable a servitude, that the peasants rose up in a body against their masters." (Ch. XCVIII)

## Regional Variations

### Poland: The Privilege of Anarchy

Poland represented, in Voltaire's analysis, the most extreme case of privilege undermining state capacity. The Polish diet operated under the *liberum veto*, whereby any single noble deputy could annul the collective decision of the entire assembly. Voltaire's verdict is unambiguous: "This power being extended to every gentleman, and carried so far as to annul by a single vote the collective suffrages of the republic, is become the privilege of anarchy." (Ch. XCVIII)

The palatines—provincial governors drawn from the highest nobility—originally held exclusive voting rights in the diet. King Casimir IV extended representation to the lesser nobility around 1460, but this democratization only multiplied the number of actors capable of obstructing royal policy. The noble right to resist confinement before trial effectively rendered criminal prosecution of nobles impossible, creating what Voltaire describes as legalized impunity.

### Hungary: The Fatal Independency

The Hungarian nobility's privileges produced what Voltaire terms a "fatal independency." The nobles reduced the peasantry to bondage while resisting any limitation on their own powers. After a four-year civil war between nobles and peasants, "the war concluded with rivetting the chains of the common people, who are still in a state of bondage under their lords." (Ch. XCVIII)

This internal division fatally weakened Hungary's capacity to resist Ottoman expansion. When Sultan [[suleiman-the-magnificent|Suleiman]] invaded in 1526, the kingdom could muster only 30,000 men—a force utterly inadequate to the threat. The [[battle-of-mohacs|Battle of Mohács]] demonstrated the strategic consequences of a social order built on extreme privilege. (Ch. XCVIII)

### Sweden: Clerical and Noble Privilege

In Sweden, the estates struggled against both noble and ecclesiastical privilege. The archbishop of Uppsala functioned as "the second person in the kingdom," and the clergy's temporal power provoked repeated crises. In 1448, the Swedish estates ordered an inquiry into "the estates, which the church had usurped in the times of confusion," triggering a seven-year civil war in which "the clergy one time in arms for their country, and another time against her, continued to excommunicate, to fight, and to plunder." (Ch. XCVIII)

### Western Europe: The Contrast

In France, the centralizing monarchy had largely suppressed the private wars of feudal lords by the reign of [[louis-xii|Louis XII]]. Voltaire contrasts Eastern European anarchy with the French model: "France alone, since the reign of Lewis XI, was become an absolute monarchy; happy government when such a king as Lewis XII repaired the mistakes which he committed in regard to foreign states, by his affection for his people." (Ch. XCVIII)

However, even in Western Europe, privilege persisted in attenuated forms. The Ottoman Empire offered a parallel case: the sultan's practice of confiscating the estates of deceased officials—though Voltaire condemns it as "nothing can be more detestable than a privilege of giving such rewards to cruelty"—functioned to prevent the accumulation of hereditary privilege. (Ch. CXXVIII)

## The Privilege of the Clergy

Ecclesiastical privilege constituted a distinct category that Voltaire analyzes separately from noble privilege. The clergy claimed exemption from secular taxation, separate legal jurisdiction, and immunity from prosecution in civil courts. These privileges were justified by reference to canon law and papal authority, but they generated persistent conflict with secular rulers throughout the Reformation era.

The [[annates]] and [[st-peters-pence|St. Peter's Pence]] were financial privileges of the [[papacy]] that drained national wealth to Rome. In England, [[henry-viii|Henry VIII]]'s break with Rome was precipitated in part by the determination to redirect these revenues to the Crown. The English parliament "suppressed the pope's authority, and his annates, with St. Peter's pence, and the provision of benefices." (Ch. CXIII)

In France, the [[pragmatic-sanction|Pragmatic Sanction]] of Bourges (1438) had nominally curtailed papal fiscal privileges, but successive popes continued to extract revenues. Voltaire notes that during the reign of [[louis-xii|Louis XII]], the papacy extracted "the exorbitant sum of three millions three hundred thousand livres" from the diocese of Paris alone. (Ch. CXVI)

## Impact and Legacy

Voltaire's analysis of privilege forms part of his broader critique of the *ancien régime* social order. He identifies three structural consequences of extreme privilege:

First, privilege undermined state capacity. The Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth, despite its size and wealth, was progressively dismembered by its neighbors precisely because the nobility's "golden liberty" prevented effective central government.

Second, privilege generated social instability. The Hungarian peasant rising and the repeated civil wars in Sweden demonstrated that extreme inequality of legal status was inherently unstable.

Third, privilege corrupted the administration of justice. The noble right to kill peasants with impunity—requiring only a payment of ten crowns on the grave—represented for Voltaire the ultimate degradation of legal equality.

The critique of privilege was not unique to Voltaire; it formed part of the broader Enlightenment attack on inherited status. But Voltaire's contribution was distinctive in its empirical grounding: rather than arguing from abstract principles of natural right, he demonstrated through historical narrative the concrete consequences of privilege for national strength and human welfare.
