## Overview

The Church of England was established when Henry VIII separated the English church from the papacy in the 1530s. Voltaire's account is characteristically sardonic: "England separated from Rome, because king Henry VIII was of an amorous disposition" (Ch. CXIII). The weight of centuries of papal exactions -- St. Peter's pence, reservations and provisos, annates, the sale of [[indulgences]], taxes, and "five hundred other exactions, constantly condemned by acts of parliament and by the complaints of the people" -- had failed to break the connection. A single transient passion accomplished what popular hatred could not.

The break was not a theological reformation in its origins. Henry VIII considered himself a Catholic king who rejected papal jurisdiction. The transformation of the English church into a Protestant body, with a vernacular liturgy and Calvinist-influenced doctrine, occurred under his successors Edward VI and Elizabeth I, with a violent Catholic interlude under Mary I.

## Origins: Henry VIII and the Divorce

Henry VIII had been married for eighteen years to [[charles-v|Catharine of Spain]], daughter of Ferdinand and Isabella and aunt to Charles V. She had borne him three children, of whom only the princess Mary survived. Henry's desire for a male heir intersected with his infatuation for Anne Boleyn, a private gentleman's daughter of "so gay a disposition" that the king might expect her to grant him favours. Instead, Anne "had the artifice not to yield to him, but to feed his flame to such a degree, that he resolved to make her his wife" (Ch. CXIII).

The king's pursuit of a divorce from Catharine set in motion a constitutional revolution. When [[papacy|Pope Clement VII]], who was effectively a prisoner of Charles V after the Sack of Rome, refused to annul the marriage, Henry took the radical step of asserting royal supremacy over the church in England. Parliament passed the Act of Supremacy in 1534, declaring the king "the only supreme head on earth of the Church of England."

> The first stone that was flung threw down this colossus, which had been shaken long before by the public hatred. (Ch. CXIII)

## The Henrician Settlement

Henry's reformation was administrative rather than doctrinal. He dissolved the monasteries, confiscated their wealth, and redirected ecclesiastical revenues to the crown. He authorized an English Bible but maintained Catholic doctrine on the [[eucharist]], [[transubstantiation]], and [[celibacy]]. Those who denied the king's supremacy or papal authority faced execution, but so did those who embraced [[lutheranism|Lutheran]] doctrines the king disapproved.

Voltaire presents Henry as a tyrant who happened to serve the cause of reformation: "Some sovereigns that changed the religion of their country, have been tyrants, from opposition and revolt, which will occasion cruelty. Henry VIII was cruel in his nature, a tyrant in government, in religion, and in his family" (Ch. CXIII). He married six wives, executed two of them, and died in his bed -- a contrast Voltaire notes with the fate of the humane but ineffective Henry VI, who was "dethroned, imprisoned, and murdered."

## Evolution Under Edward VI, Mary, and Elizabeth

Under Edward VI (1547-1553), the English church moved decisively toward Protestantism. The Book of Common Prayer, composed by Archbishop Thomas Cranmer, established an English liturgy. Images were removed from churches, and the doctrine of the real presence was abandoned in favor of a Reformed understanding.

Mary I (1553-1558), the daughter of Catharine of Spain, attempted to restore Catholicism. Her marriage to Philip II of Spain and her persecution of Protestants earned her the name "Bloody Mary." Her reign, however, was brief and ineffective: the Marian persecution made Protestantism more deeply rooted in English identity.

Elizabeth I (1558-1603) established the lasting [[doctrine|settlement]] of the Church of England. The Elizabethan Settlement of 1559 reinstated the royal supremacy and the Book of Common Prayer while adopting a deliberately ambiguous doctrinal position that allowed both Reformed and more traditional interpretations. This settlement, combined with the defeat of the Spanish Armada in 1588, secured the Church of England as a permanent feature of the European religious landscape. The English church became, as Voltaire's contemporary observers noted, a middle way between Rome and Geneva.

## Impact and Legacy

The creation of the Church of England transformed the political theology of monarchy. The English crown absorbed powers that elsewhere remained with the papacy, creating a model of royal ecclesiastical supremacy that would influence Protestant states across northern Europe. The precedent established by Henry VIII -- that a Christian prince could repudiate papal authority and establish a national church -- was followed by [[gustavus-vasa]] in Sweden and by other Protestant princes.

Voltaire also notes how the English experience with religious change demonstrated the power of state authority in matters of conscience. What had once seemed impossible -- the separation of a European kingdom from Rome -- was accomplished by the will of a single determined monarch backed by [[parliament]].

## Key Figures

- **Henry VIII** -- King who broke with Rome and established the royal supremacy
- **Anne Boleyn** -- The woman whose refusal to become Henry's mistress drove him to seek divorce
- **Catharine of Aragon** -- Henry's first wife, daughter of Ferdinand and Isabella, whose marriage the pope refused to annul
- **Thomas Cranmer** -- Archbishop of Canterbury who authored the Book of Common Prayer
- **Edward VI** -- Boy king under whom the English church became doctrinally Protestant
- **Mary I** -- Catholic queen whose persecution of Protestants proved counterproductive
- **Elizabeth I** -- Queen who established the lasting Elizabethan Settlement
