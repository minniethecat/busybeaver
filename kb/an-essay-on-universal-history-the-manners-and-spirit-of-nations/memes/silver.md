## Overview

Silver — the precious metal that served as the primary medium of exchange across Eurasia — experienced a revolutionary transformation in the sixteenth century with the discovery and exploitation of vast deposits in Spanish America. The influx of American silver into Europe and subsequently into Asian markets reshaped global commerce, altered the balance of economic power, and provided the material foundation for the Spanish Habsburg empire. Voltaire notes that while "the gold and silver mines were of no use at first but to the kings of Spain, and the merchants... by degrees that vast plenty of gold and silver which flowed in from America to Europe, has passed through a greater number of hands, and been more equally distributed" (Ch. CXXII).

## Origins and Context

Before the American discoveries, Europe's silver supply came primarily from mines in central Europe — Bohemia, Saxony, and the Tyrol. The discovery of the Potosi silver mountain in Upper Peru (1545) and the development of the mercury amalgamation process for extracting silver from ore transformed the scale of production. American mines, worked by native labor under the *mita* system, produced silver on a scale that dwarfed all previous sources.

Voltaire observes that the conquistadors' motive was straightforward: "out of the ransom already paid by Atabalipa, each Spanish horseman had two hundred and forty marks in pure gold; each foot soldier had one hundred and sixty: they divided about ten times as much silver in the same proportion" (Ch. CXXIV). The personal fortunes made by the conquistadors were only a foretaste of the systematic extraction that followed.

## Key Features

The flow of American silver had several distinct channels and effects:

| Channel | Mechanism | Primary Beneficiaries |
|---------|----------|----------------------|
| **Royal fifth (quinto)** | 20% tax on all American treasure | Spanish Crown |
| **Private remittances** | Merchant shipments to Seville | Spanish and foreign merchants |
| **Smuggling and evasion** | Unregistered shipments | Foreign traders, especially Dutch and English |
| **Asian trade** | Silver exchanged for Chinese goods via Manila | Chinese merchants, Portuguese intermediaries |
| **Military expenditure** | Spanish tercios paid in silver coin | Soldiers, suppliers, bankers (Fuggers, Genoese) |

> Despite fierce Spanish laws prohibiting foreign participation in American trade, Voltaire observes that "Spain subsists merely by a perpetual violation of it" — European merchants supplied the majority of goods shipped to America, carried in Spanish names (Ch. CXXII). The silver that legally belonged to the Spanish crown passed rapidly into the hands of Genoese bankers, German mercenaries, and Dutch carriers.

## Historical Development

The price revolution that followed the American silver influx was one of the most significant economic phenomena of the century. "The price of provisions has risen all over Europe very near in the same proportion" as silver spread through the continent (Ch. CXXII). This inflation eroded the value of fixed rents and feudal dues, benefiting merchants and commercial farmers while impoverishing traditional landholders.

The Spanish crown's dependence on American silver created a peculiar fiscal dynamic. Charles V used American treasure to finance his wars in Germany and Italy. Philip II, visiting London to marry Queen Mary, "lodged seven and twenty large chests of bullion in the Tower, together with a hundred horse load of gold and silver specie" (Ch. CXXII). But the constant warfare of the Spanish empire consumed even these vast sums: "the troubles of Flanders, and the intrigues of the league in France, cost this same Philip, by his own confession, above three thousand millions of livres" (Ch. CXXII).

Ethiopia, by contrast, was "poor, though abounding with silver mines" because "the inhabitants, less industrious than the Americans, knew not how to work them" (Ch. CXXI). Productivity, not mineral endowment alone, determined whether silver enriched a nation.

## Impact and Legacy

American silver integrated the world economy in ways that had no precedent. Silver crossed the Pacific from Acapulco to Manila, where it purchased Chinese silks, porcelain, and tea. It crossed the Atlantic to Seville, from where it financed the Spanish armies in Flanders and the Mediterranean fleets. It paid for the spices that Portuguese ships brought from the East Indies. Voltaire observes that the silver trade at Cadiz operated through a remarkable system of trust: armed men called "meteors" carried bars of silver from the custom house to waiting boats, "and the foreign merchant was never cheated" (Ch. CXXII).

The influx of silver also transformed European monetary systems, enabling the transition from a predominantly barter-and-kind economy to one based on coined money. The silver peso (piece of eight) became the first truly global currency, accepted from China to the Caribbean.

> The long-term consequence, as Voltaire shrewdly notes, was not the enrichment of Spain but the enrichment of Spain's rivals: the silver passed through Spanish hands into the coffers of Dutch, English, and French merchants who supplied the goods that Spain could not produce itself.
