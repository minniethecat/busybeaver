## Overview

The **Crusades** were a series of religiously sanctioned military campaigns initiated, supported, and sometimes directed by the [[papal-bull|papacy]] between the 11th and 16th centuries. The term is most commonly applied to the campaigns in the Eastern Mediterranean aimed at recovering the Holy Land from Muslim rule, but the crusading ideal was also applied to campaigns in Europe against heretics, pagans, and political opponents of the Church. By the era covered in Voltaire's narrative, the crusading movement had largely waned, though its legacy profoundly shaped European relations with the Islamic world. (Ch. CVI, CX)

## Origins and Context

The First Crusade was proclaimed by Pope Urban II in 1095 at the Council of Clermont. Responding to a Byzantine appeal for military assistance against the Seljuk Turks, Urban transformed the request into a call for the liberation of Jerusalem and the Holy Sepulchre. The response was overwhelming — tens of thousands of Europeans, from knights to peasants, took the cross and marched eastward.

The crusading movement was sustained by a powerful blend of religious zeal, the promise of [[indulgences|plenary indulgence]] (remission of all temporal punishment for sin), and the lure of land and wealth in the East. Military orders — the Templars, Hospitallers, and Teutonic Knights — emerged as permanent institutions to defend the crusader states. (Ch. CVI)

## Key Features

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Papal Authorization | Only the pope could proclaim a crusade and grant its spiritual privileges |
| The Cross | Participants took a vow and wore a cloth cross as a public pledge |
| Indulgence | Crusaders received a plenary indulgence, remitting all temporal punishment |
| Temporal Protection | Crusaders' property and families were placed under Church protection |
| Military Orders | Permanent religious-military institutions sustained the crusading enterprise |

The crusading ideal evolved over time. By the 13th century, crusades were proclaimed against pagan peoples in the Baltic, heretics in southern France (the Albigensian Crusade), and political enemies of the papacy. This expansion of the concept diluted its original purpose and contributed to its eventual decline. (Ch. CX)

## Historical Development

By the 16th century, the crusading movement had lost much of its vigor, though the ideal persisted in papal rhetoric. The Ottoman advance into Europe — including the [[battle-of-mohacs|Battle of Mohács]] (1526) — prompted calls for new crusades, but the political fragmentation of Christendom and the [[the-reformation|Protestant Reformation]] made unified action impossible.

> Under the pretence of a war against the Turks, [the pope] set up a sale in all parts of Christendom, of what they call Indulgences. (Ch. CVI)

The papal practice of invoking the crusade as a pretext for raising funds — as Leo X did for the construction of St. Peter's — further undermined the credibility of the crusading ideal in the eyes of reformers. (Ch. CVI)

## Impact and Legacy

The Crusades left a complex and contested legacy. They intensified cultural and economic exchange between Europe and the Islamic world, bringing Greek and Arabic learning to the Latin West and stimulating trade through the Italian maritime republics. The [[commerce|commercial]] networks established by the crusader states outlasted the states themselves.

At the same time, the Crusades hardened religious divisions and established a precedent for the use of violence in the service of religion — a precedent that the [[the-reformation|Reformation]] era extended in new and destructive directions. The military orders became anachronisms, their wealth eventually absorbed by secular rulers. (Ch. CX)
