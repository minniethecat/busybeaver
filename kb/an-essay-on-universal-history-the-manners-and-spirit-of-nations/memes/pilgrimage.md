## Overview

Pilgrimage, the practice of undertaking a journey to a sacred site for spiritual benefit, was among the most visible expressions of late medieval Catholic piety. Pilgrims traveled to Rome, Jerusalem, Santiago de Compostela, and countless local shrines, seeking [[indulgences]], miraculous cures, and spiritual merit. During the [[the-reformation|Reformation]], pilgrimage became a target of Protestant criticism, with reformers condemning the practice as superstitious, commercially exploitative, and without scriptural warrant.

Voltaire's *Essay on Universal History* provides several instances of pilgrimage as a transformative religious experience. Notably, Ignatius of Loyola, the founder of the [[jesuits]], was inspired to religious life after his convalescence: reading the lives of [[saints]], together with a visionary experience, "determined him to perform a pilgrimage to Jerusalem." The pilgrimage to the Holy Land represented the highest aspiration of late medieval devotion, a journey that could radically reorient a believer's life. (Ch. CXVII)

## Origins and Context

The practice of pilgrimage has deep roots in Christian tradition, extending back to the early church's veneration of martyr tombs and the Holy Land sites associated with Christ's life. By the medieval period, an elaborate infrastructure of pilgrimage routes, hospices, and shrines had developed across Europe. Major pilgrimage centers—Rome with the tombs of Saints Peter and Paul, Santiago de Compostela with the shrine of Saint James, and Jerusalem with the Holy Sepulchre—attracted thousands of pilgrims annually.

Pilgrimage was closely linked to the theology of [[indulgences]] and the cult of [[saints]]. Pilgrims believed that visiting sacred sites and venerating relics could reduce temporal punishment for sin, secure saintly intercession, and in some cases effect miraculous healings. The practice generated substantial revenues for the shrines, their host communities, and the ecclesiastical authorities who controlled access to the sacred.

## Reformation Critique

The Protestant reformers, led by [[luther-martin|Martin Luther]], subjected pilgrimage to sustained criticism. Luther argued that pilgrimage had no foundation in scripture and that the notion of earning spiritual merit through physical journeys contradicted the doctrine of justification by faith alone. The reformers also condemned the commercialization of pilgrimage, pointing to the sale of relics, indulgences, and votive offerings as evidence of systematic corruption.

The English Reformation under [[henry-viii|Henry VIII]] saw the systematic suppression of pilgrimage shrines. The dissolution of the [[monasteries|monasticism]] (1536–1541) dismantled the institutional infrastructure that had sustained pilgrimage for centuries. Thomas Cromwell's commissioners stripped shrines of their relics and precious metals, transforming sites of popular devotion into sources of Crown revenue. (Ch. CXIII)

## Pilgrimage Beyond Christianity

Voltaire's comparative perspective reveals that pilgrimage was not unique to Christianity. Writing of Japan, he observes that "they have pilgrimages, and the ordeals or proofs by fire, which heretofore constituted part of our jurisprudence." Similarly, his account of Persia notes the Shiite practice of pilgrimage to the tombs of Ali and his descendants, which served both religious and political functions. The Safavid dynasty exploited pilgrimage's political potential, redirecting devotion away from Mecca and toward Persian shrines to consolidate a distinct religious identity in opposition to the Ottoman Turks. (Ch. CXX, CXXIX)

This comparative dimension underscores pilgrimage's character as a near-universal religious phenomenon, transcending specific doctrinal traditions while taking distinct forms shaped by local political and cultural contexts.

## Impact and Legacy

The Reformation's assault on pilgrimage contributed to a profound transformation of European popular religion. Where medieval Christianity had been a religion of sacred places, Protestant Christianity increasingly became a religion of the Word—centered on scripture, preaching, and interior faith rather than physical journeys and material relics. The [[catholicism|Catholic]] Reformation responded by reaffirming the legitimacy of pilgrimage while instituting reforms to curb the most egregious commercial abuses.
