## Overview

**Baptism** is the Christian rite of initiation, symbolizing purification, rebirth, and admission into the community of believers. During the [[the-reformation|Reformation]], the question of baptism — whether it should be administered to infants or reserved for professing adults — became a source of violent controversy that went beyond the disputes over [[eucharist|the Eucharist]] and [[doctrine|doctrine]] that divided Catholics and mainstream Protestants. The radical reformers who rejected infant baptism and insisted on rebaptizing adult converts were called Anabaptists (from the Greek _anabaptizein_, "to baptize again"), and they were persecuted by Catholics, Lutherans, and Calvinists alike. (Ch. CIX)

## The Reformation Controversy

The mainstream Reformers — [[martin-luther|Luther]], [[zwingli|Zwingli]], and [[john-calvin|Calvin]] — retained infant baptism, grounding it in the analogy between circumcision (administered to infants under the old covenant) and baptism under the new. They regarded baptism as a sign of God's prevenient grace, not dependent on the recipient's conscious faith.

The radical reformers rejected this reasoning. Reading the New Testament, they found no instance of infant baptism; they saw the practice as a post-apostolic corruption that turned a voluntary profession of faith into an automatic rite of social incorporation. They insisted on rebaptizing those who had received only infant baptism and now made a conscious profession of faith. (Ch. CIX)

| Position | Proponents | Rationale |
|----------|-----------|-----------|
| Infant baptism | Catholics, Lutherans, Calvinists | Sign of grace; covenant continuity with circumcision |
| Believer's baptism | Anabaptists | No biblical warrant for infant baptism; faith must be conscious |
| Rebaptism | Anabaptists | Previous infant baptism invalid without personal faith |

## The Anabaptist Movement

Voltaire devotes a full chapter to the Anabaptists, treating them as a phenomenon that began in scriptural literalism and escalated into social revolution. The movement's founders, [[storck-and-muncer|Storck and Muncer]] of Saxony, claimed divine inspiration and condemned infant baptism as unbiblical. They rebaptized adult converts and preached a gospel of radical equality that threatened both ecclesiastical and secular authority.

> They condemned infant baptism, and rebaptized those that entered into their communion, because Christ was baptised at full age; and this is what gave them the name of Anabaptists. (Ch. CIX)

The movement soon splintered. While some Anabaptist communities — particularly the Mennonites in the Netherlands — evolved toward pacifism and withdrawal from worldly affairs, others, most notoriously at Münster (1534–35), attempted to establish a theocratic kingdom, instituting communal property and polygamy. The Münster rebellion was crushed with extreme brutality, and the term "Anabaptist" became synonymous with sedition across Europe. (Ch. CIX–CX)

## Voltaire's Assessment

Voltaire's treatment of the Anabaptist controversy is characteristic of his broader approach to the Reformation. He presents the dispute over [[baptism|baptism]] as an example of how theological minutiae, when combined with popular enthusiasm and the ambition of charismatic leaders, could produce catastrophic social upheaval. He notes the irony that those who took scripture most literally — reading the Bible without the mediation of clerical authority — produced doctrines most disruptive to the social order.

Yet Voltaire also acknowledges that the persecution of Anabaptists was often disproportionate and cruel, reflecting the intolerance common to all parties in the religious conflicts of the 16th century. The drowning of Anabaptists (a grim parody of their baptismal practice) exemplified, in his view, the barbarism that religious zeal could justify. (Ch. CIX–CX)

## Impact and Legacy

The Anabaptist movement, though crushed as a political force, left a lasting legacy. The Mennonites and later the Baptists preserved the principle of believer's baptism as a distinctive theological position. More broadly, the Anabaptist emphasis on voluntary religious profession and separation of church from state anticipated elements of modern religious liberty — though Voltaire would have noted that this legacy emerged despite, not because of, the intentions of its founders. (Ch. CX)
