## Overview

St. Peter's Pence (*denarius Sancti Petri*), also known as Peter's Pence or Rome-Scot, was an annual tax of one penny levied on every household in England and several other northern European kingdoms, payable to the [[papacy|papal]] treasury in Rome. Together with [[annates]], reservations, provisions, and [[indulgences]], St. Peter's Pence formed part of the comprehensive fiscal machinery through which the papacy extracted revenues from the national churches of Europe. The tax became one of the principal fiscal grievances cited during the English [[the-reformation|Reformation]].

As Voltaire documents, St. Peter's Pence was a levy "of a penny on every house wherein there were thirty pence, *vivæ pecuniæ*, to be collected and sent to Rome; one half of it went for alms to the English school at Rome, and the other half to the pope's use." What made this tax particularly galling to English sensibilities was not its monetary value — a penny per household was modest — but its symbolism: an annual tribute from every English household to a foreign ecclesiastical sovereign. (Ch. CXIII)

## Origins and Medieval Development

The origins of St. Peter's Pence are traditionally traced to the eighth century, when King Offa of Mercia is said to have instituted an annual payment to Rome as a form of pious tribute. By the Anglo-Saxon period, the tax was well established and was enforced through ecclesiastical penalties — those who failed to pay could be denied Christian burial. The Norman Conquest did not abolish the tax; rather, William the Conqueror confirmed it as a customary obligation of the English church to the Holy See.

Over the centuries, St. Peter's Pence became one of the most reliable sources of papal revenue from northern Europe. The tax was collected by local bishops and archdeacons, who were required to remit the proceeds to Rome. The administrative machinery of collection was itself a source of grievance: the papal collectors (*collectores*) were widely resented as foreign agents extracting English wealth for a distant and, in the eyes of many, corrupt court. By the late Middle Ages, alongside [[annates]] and other papal exactions, St. Peter's Pence had become emblematic of the financial burden that the papacy imposed on the English church.

## The Tudor Suppression

St. Peter's Pence was swept away in the legislative revolution of the English Reformation. In 1534, [[henry-viii|Henry VIII]]'s [[parliament]] passed the Act of Supremacy, which declared the king "the only supreme head in earth of the Church of England." The act explicitly "suppressed the pope's authority, and his annates, with St. Peter's pence, and the provision of benefices." The statutory language grouped these papal fiscal instruments together, treating them as a single system of foreign exaction that the Crown was now abolishing.

Crucially, the Crown did not simply abolish the tax — it redirected the revenue stream. As Voltaire records, "the king prevailed on the parliament to grant him the annates or first fruits, which had been paid to the pope." While Voltaire specifies annates, the same principle applied to other papal revenues: the money that had once flowed to Rome now flowed to the royal treasury. The financial dimension of the English Reformation was thus not merely a matter of ending payments to Rome; it was also a significant augmentation of Crown revenue. Henry used the proceeds to fund, among other projects, the establishment of six new bishoprics. (Ch. CXIII)

## Broader Significance

St. Peter's Pence exemplifies a broader pattern of the Reformation era: fiscal grievances against the [[papacy]] proved as potent a driver of institutional separation as theological disputes. As Voltaire observes, neither "St. Peter's pence, nor the reservations and provisos, nor the annates or first fruits, nor the levying of taxes, nor the sale of indulgences, nor five hundred other exactions, constantly condemned by acts of parliament and by the complaints of the people, could ever bring to pass" what Henry VIII's matrimonial crisis finally achieved. The fiscal apparatus had prepared the ground of public resentment; the king's marital ambitions provided the spark.

The suppression of St. Peter's Pence also illustrates a structural reality of papal authority that Voltaire repeatedly emphasizes: "a power not founded on force, is really no power at all." When the English Crown resolved to break with Rome, the entire edifice of papal fiscal jurisdiction — taxes, annates, provisions, dispensations — collapsed almost instantaneously. The papacy could issue bulls of excommunication against Henry, but without the cooperation of the secular power, these spiritual weapons had no effect on the Crown's ability to legislate and tax. (Ch. CXIII)
