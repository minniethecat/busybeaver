## Overview

Tyranny—the arbitrary, lawless, and violent exercise of political power—is one of the master concepts of Voltaire's *Essay on Universal History*. Unlike [[privilege]] or [[superstition]], which Voltaire analyzes with a certain anthropological detachment, tyranny elicits his most impassioned moral condemnation. The *Essay* documents tyranny across the full range of early modern polities: in hereditary monarchies, ecclesiastical principalities, and popular revolutions alike.

Voltaire's concept of tyranny is not identical with monarchy or even absolutism. A legitimate monarch who respects the laws and the [[estates]] is not a tyrant; conversely, popular assemblies that violate natural justice can be tyrannical. Tyranny is defined by its methods—arbitrary imprisonment, judicial murder, confiscation without cause—rather than by its institutional form.

## Christian II: The Archetypal Tyrant

[[christian-ii-of-denmark|Christian II]] of Denmark, whom Voltaire calls "the Nero of the North," serves as the *Essay*'s archetypal case study in tyranny. His crimes are narrated in detail across multiple chapters because they illustrate the full spectrum of tyrannical behavior: treachery, mass murder, and the corruption of religion into an instrument of state violence.

The central episode is the Stockholm Bloodbath of 1520. Christian and his accomplice, Archbishop Troll of Uppsala, invited the Swedish senate and ninety-four nobles to a feast after having sworn a sacramental oath to forget past grievances. "The tables were covered, and mirth and jollity were diffused all round, when Christian and the archbishop went out. They returned a moment after, followed by guards and executioners: the archbishop with the pope's bull in hand, caused all the company to be butchered. They ripped open the belly of the grand prior of St. John of Jerusalem, and plucked out his heart." (Ch. CVIII)

The massacre was not limited to the senate: "This feast of the two tyrants concluded with a general massacre of all ranks of people without distinction of age or sex. These monsters, who deserved to suffer at least the same punishment as the grand prior of St. John, died in their beds." (Ch. CVIII)

Christian's subsequent crimes included the murder of [[gustavus-vasa|Gustavus Vasa]]'s mother and sister: "he ordered those two princesses to be sown into a sack, and thrown into the sea. The tyrant could indulge his revenge, but did not know how to fight: he could murder innocent women, but he durst not go over to Sweden to oppose Gustavus in person." (Ch. XCVIII)

## The Mechanism of Tyrannical Rule

Voltaire identifies several structural features that enable tyranny:

**The corruption of religion**: Archbishop Troll's use of the papal bull to sanctify mass murder demonstrated how ecclesiastical authority could be weaponized. Gustavus Vasa, having delivered Sweden, "exterminated a religion, of which such a handle had been made to perpetrate the most horrid crimes." (Ch. CVIII)

**The complicity of institutions**: The Danish senate condemned their Swedish counterparts to lose "their nobility and their estates," and Emperor Maximilian confirmed this decree—an example of how "the German emperors, as well as the popes, have always pretended to universal jurisdiction." (Ch. XCVIII)

**The weakness of resistance**: Tyranny flourished where the people lacked arms, organization, or allies. The Dalecarlians—"those savage men, who detested tyranny so much the more, as their simple manners estranged them from all kind of policy"—rallied to Gustavus Vasa partly because their isolation from court culture had preserved their independence. (Ch. XCVIII)

## Henry VIII: Tyranny Within the Law

[[henry-viii|Henry VIII]] represents a different model of tyranny: one exercised through legal forms rather than in defiance of them. Voltaire's judgment is explicit: "The tyrant (for we can call him by no other name) was also divorced from this wife, before he put her to death." (Ch. CXIII)

Henry's tyranny was distinctive in operating through parliament rather than against it. The execution of Anne Boleyn was conducted "upon such slight foundations, that were a private citizen to quarrel with his wife for so trivial a matter, it would be considered as an injustice." The parliament's complicity made the tyranny more insidious because it preserved the appearance of legality. (Ch. CXIII)

"Some sovereigns that changed the religion of their country, have been tyrants, from opposition and revolt, which will occasion cruelty. Henry VIII was cruel in his nature, a tyrant in government, in religion, and in his family. Yet he died in his bed; and Henry VI, the humanest of princes, had been dethroned, imprisoned, and murdered." This observation—that tyranny often prospers while virtue suffers—is one of the *Essay*'s darkest conclusions. (Ch. CXIII)

## Hungary: Petty Tyranny

The Hungarian nobility represented a decentralized form of tyranny: "petty tyrants, who would not be tyrannized themselves." Their "liberty was a fatal independency" that reduced the peasantry to bondage while preventing effective central government. When the peasants rose in rebellion, "the nobles being better armed than the peasants, and having likewise all the money in their possession, gained at length the upper hand; and the war concluded with rivetting the chains of the common people." (Ch. XCVIII)

This passage reveals Voltaire's nuanced understanding of tyranny: it is not only the act of a single despot but can be distributed across a ruling class. The Hungarian nobles' "fatal independency" was simultaneously a form of liberty for themselves and tyranny for everyone else.

## The Right of Resistance

Against tyranny, Voltaire affirms—implicitly but unmistakably—a right of resistance. The deposition of Christian II by the Danish estates provides the *Essay*'s most dramatic example of legitimate tyrannicide through legal process:

"The Danes were then possessed of the right of electing their kings, and had also that of punishing a tyrant. The first who threw off their allegiance were the inhabitants of Jutland... all the inhabitants of that tract of land… transmitted to the tyrant the act of his deposition by the first magistrate of Jutland." (Ch. XCVIII)

The magistrate Mogens Munk presented the decree of deposition to Christian in person, within the tyrant's own palace: "The tyrant perceiving the rest of the state in commotion, being likewise hated by his officers, and grown distrustful of every body, received like a criminal in his own palace the decree presented to him by a single man unarmed." Voltaire adds: "It is fit posterity should know the name of this magistrate, which was Mons. My name, said he, ought to be written over the palace gates of all wicked princes." (Ch. XCVIII)

The speed and orderliness of the Danish revolution impressed Voltaire: "Denmark obeyed the decree; and never was there an instance of so just, so sudden, and quiet a revolution." (Ch. XCVIII)

## Tyranny and Empire

Voltaire extends his analysis of tyranny to non-European contexts. The Ottoman sultan was "absolute over the subjects belonging to his own domain," but his power was constrained by the viceroys who could "disobey him." The Mughal emperor Aurangzeb, despite vast wealth, ruled over a society where "there are none but great lords and poor wretches"—an observation Voltaire uses to critique European assumptions about Oriental despotism. (Ch. CXXVIII)

The Spanish conquest of the Americas provides a further case: "What would [Cortés] have said had he beheld an Auto da Fe?" The Inquisition's procedures, transplanting European tyranny to the New World, demonstrated that Christian powers could outdo the alleged cruelties of the Aztecs. (Ch. CXVIII)

## Voltaire's Anti-Tyranny

From the *Essay*'s historical evidence, Voltaire's anti-tyranny doctrine can be summarized as follows:

1. Tyranny is defined not by the identity of the ruler but by the lawlessness of the rule
2. Legitimate resistance requires legal forms—the decree, the deposition, the public judgment
3. Religion, when yoked to state power, is the most reliable enabler of tyranny
4. The remedy for tyranny is constitutional limitation: estates, parliaments, and the rule of law
5. Tyranny that operates through legal forms (Henry VIII) is more insidious than tyranny that openly violates them (Christian II)
