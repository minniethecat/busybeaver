## Overview

A dispensation (*dispensatio*) was a relaxation of canon law granted by papal authority for a specific case, exempting the recipient from a particular ecclesiastical obligation or prohibition. In theory, dispensations were acts of pastoral mercy, accommodating the rigor of the law to the circumstances of individual cases. In practice, they became one of the most lucrative and scandalous sources of papal revenue — and a principal grievance of the [[the-reformation|Reformation]].

Voltaire devoted sustained attention to the abuse of dispensations, which he regarded as emblematic of the corruption of the late medieval Church. The papal court had, in his account, systematized the sale of dispensations into a comprehensive tariff that covered every conceivable transgression of canon law — from the routine (dispensation to marry within prohibited degrees of kinship) to the shocking (absolution for murder or bestiality at fixed prices). The scandal of dispensations was among the grievances that fueled the Protestant break with Rome. (Ch. CVI)

## The System of Dispensations

The papal power of dispensation rested on the doctrine of the pope's plenitude of power (*plenitudo potestatis*), which held that the pope, as the vicar of Christ, could dispense from any merely ecclesiastical law. This power was theoretically limited to cases where dispensation served the good of the Church, but by the late Middle Ages it had become a major source of revenue for the [[curia|Roman Curia]].

The fiscal apparatus of dispensations was codified in the pontificate of John XXII (1316–1334), who systematized what had previously been *ad hoc* into a comprehensive code of the canon law known as the *Taxae Cancellariae Apostolicae*. This code established fixed prices for dispensations of every kind. As Voltaire records in vivid detail, the tariff covered "all manner of uncleanness, even the most shocking to nature":

> A subdeacon, or a deacon, guilty of murder, was absolved with permission to hold three benefices, for about twenty crowns. A bishop, or an abbot might assassinate a person for about three hundred livres. All manner of uncleanness, even the most shocking to nature, had its settled price. Bestiality was valued at two hundred and fifty livres. (Ch. CVI)

## Anticipated Dispensations

The abuse reached its logical extreme in the form of anticipated dispensations — indulgences that granted remission not only for past sins but for future ones. Voltaire records that in the archives of Joinville there was preserved an indulgence granted to the Cardinal of Lorraine and twelve of his retinue, "whereby the remission of three sins, whichever they chose to nominate, should be anticipated to each." The Duchess of Burgundy and Auvergne, sister to Charles VIII, and ten of her attendants obtained "the privilege of obtaining general absolution from all their sins, during their whole lives, upon forty-seven festivals, without reckoning Sundays." (Ch. CVI)

Voltaire acknowledged that "this strange abuse seems to have been derived from the ancient laws of the several European nations, those of the Franks, the Saxons, and the Burgundians" — the old Germanic systems of wergild, which assigned monetary values to every injury, had been adapted by the papal court into a system of spiritual accounting. But he insisted that the papal taxation of sins was a corruption without scriptural warrant: "Never did any council rank the taxation of sins among the articles of faith."

## The Henrician Dispensation

One of the most consequential dispensations in European history was that granted by Pope [[julius-ii|Julius II]] to [[henry-viii|Henry VIII]], permitting him to marry Catherine of Aragon, the widow of his elder brother Arthur. The marriage required a dispensation because canon law prohibited a man from marrying his brother's widow, on the basis of Levitical prohibition.

When Henry later sought to have this marriage annulled so that he could marry Anne Boleyn, he argued that the original dispensation had been invalid — that no pope could dispense from a divine law. Pope Clement VII, who was effectively the prisoner of Catherine's nephew, the Emperor [[charles-v|Charles V]], could not grant the annulment without undermining the legitimacy of his own predecessor's act. Voltaire observes that "a pope would hardly acknowledge that his predecessor had no right to grant such a dispensation: it would have been undermining the very foundation of papal authority." The resulting impasse precipitated the English break with Rome. (Ch. CXIII)

## Impact and Legacy

The abuse of dispensations was among the most powerful engines of anti-papal sentiment in Reformation Europe. The spectacle of sins — even the most serious — being commuted for money undermined the moral authority of the Church and gave credibility to the Reformers' charge that the papacy had substituted a religion of finance for a religion of grace.

The Council of Trent (1545–1563) addressed the abuse of dispensations indirectly, through its general reform of ecclesiastical discipline, but it did not abolish the papal power to dispense. The post-Tridentine papacy exercised the dispensing power with greater restraint, but the system itself survived into the modern era. The legacy of the medieval dispensation system was thus mixed: it generated the revenues that financed the Renaissance papacy and the construction of St. Peter's, but it also generated the scandal that helped to break the unity of Western Christendom.
