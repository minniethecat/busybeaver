## Overview

The Pragmatic Sanction of Bourges (1438) was a royal decree issued by King [[charles-vii|Charles VII]] of France that codified the liberties of the French church against [[papacy|papal]] authority. Based on the reform decrees of the Council of Basel (1431–1449), the Sanction affirmed the supremacy of general councils over the pope, restored the right of cathedral chapters and monasteries to elect their bishops and abbots, and abolished or restricted papal fiscal exactions including [[annates]], reservations, and expectatives. French Catholics came to be considered at Rome as schismatics because of the Pragmatic Sanction, yet the decree established the constitutional framework of Gallican liberties that endured into the eighteenth century.

Voltaire records that "the French, ever since the reign of Charles VII, were considered at Rome as schismatics, because of the pragmatic sanction, which had been made at Bourges, in conformity to the decrees of the fathers at the council of Basil, enemies to the papal power." The Pragmatic Sanction thus placed France in a distinctive position within Catholic Christendom: obedient to Rome in doctrine but fiercely independent in matters of ecclesiastical governance, taxation, and appointments. (Ch. CXVI)

## Historical Context

The Pragmatic Sanction was promulgated against the background of the Conciliar Movement, the great late medieval attempt to reform the church by subordinating papal authority to general councils. The Council of Constance (1414–1418) had healed the Great Western Schism and declared that a general council "holds power immediately from Christ, and all men of whatever rank and dignity, even the pope, are bound to obey it in matters pertaining to faith, the extirpation of the said schism, and the general reform of the church."

The Council of Basel (1431–1449) pursued this conciliar program more aggressively, enacting decrees that abolished annates, restricted papal reservations, and restored ecclesiastical elections. Pope Eugenius IV condemned the council and attempted to dissolve it, but the fathers at Basel continued to legislate. The Pragmatic Sanction of Bourges represented the French Crown's decision to adopt the Basel reform decrees as the law of the Kingdom of France, selective about which decrees to enforce, but firm in asserting royal control over the French church. (Ch. CXVI)

## Key Provisions

The Pragmatic Sanction's principal article was the restoration of ecclesiastical elections, an ancient usage that the papacy had progressively overridden through reservations and provisions. Under the Sanction, cathedral chapters were to elect their bishops, and monasteries were to elect their abbots, without papal interference. Voltaire describes this as "an usage tending to the encouragement of virtue and learning in better times, but a source of faction and discord." The elections were to be free and canonical, though the king retained the right to recommend candidates — a "recommendation" that frequently functioned as an effective nomination.

Other provisions limited papal fiscal authority in France. Annates — the first year's income from benefices — were formally abolished, though as Voltaire notes, "the popes continued to receive the annates for the most part, in spite of the pragmatic sanction." The papal reservation of benefices (the right to nominate to vacant offices) and expectatives (bills granting future rights to benefices) were likewise prohibited. The Pragmatic Sanction thus sought to keep French ecclesiastical revenues in France, a goal that aligned the interests of the French Crown with those of the French clergy and the University of Paris. (Ch. CXVI)

## The Concordat of 1516

The Pragmatic Sanction remained in force for nearly eighty years before being superseded by the Concordat of Bologna (1516), negotiated between King [[francis-i|Francis I]] and Pope [[leo-x|Leo X]] through Chancellor Duprat. The Concordat represented a compromise: the king gained the right to nominate bishops (a power even greater than the Sanction's recommendation right), while the pope retained the annates by secret article and reasserted his authority over provisions to benefices.

Voltaire's judgment on the Concordat is characteristically sharp: "it was said that the king and the pope gave to each other what did not belong to them." The University of Paris, deprived of its electoral privileges, published a prohibition against printing or obeying the Concordat. The [[parliament]] of Paris, as guardian of the Gallican liberties, reluctantly registered the Concordat only after repeated royal commands, protesting all the while that it acted "in obedience only to the repeated commands of his majesty."

Yet Voltaire also notes that Francis I had rendered France a genuine service by reducing the annates to a fixed moderate sum — approximately four hundred thousand livres annually, compared to the three million three hundred thousand livres that the papacy had extracted from the diocese of Paris alone during the reign of [[louis-xii|Louis XII]]. (Ch. CXVI)

## Legacy and Gallicanism

The Pragmatic Sanction, though superseded by the Concordat, established principles that shaped French ecclesiastical politics for centuries. The liberties of the [[gallican-church|Gallican Church]] — the French church's claim to administrative autonomy from Rome — drew on the precedent of the Sanction even after its formal abrogation. The parliament of Paris continued to regard itself as the guardian of these liberties, resisting what it perceived as papal encroachments on the rights of the French Crown and church.

The Pragmatic Sanction also illustrates a pattern that would repeat throughout the Reformation era: secular rulers asserting control over ecclesiastical appointments and revenues as an assertion of national sovereignty against Rome. The specific mechanisms differed — Henry VIII in England suppressed papal authority entirely, while the French kings negotiated a Concordat that preserved communion with Rome while maximizing royal control — but the underlying dynamic was similar. (Ch. CXVI)
