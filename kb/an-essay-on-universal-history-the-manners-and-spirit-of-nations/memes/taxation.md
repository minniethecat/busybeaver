## Overview

Taxation — the systematic extraction of revenue by governing authorities from their subjects — was among the most politically charged issues of the early modern period. The fiscal demands of the papacy, the ambitions of secular princes, and the rising cost of warfare collided with popular resentment against what many saw as unjust exactions. Voltaire observes that the reformers found their most receptive audience not in theological disputation but in the material grievance that "it signified greatly whether or not they were to pay exorbitant taxes to Rome" (Ch. CVI). This fiscal dimension of the Reformation transformed the abstract question of ecclesiastical authority into a concrete matter of who could levy taxes and for what purpose.

## Origins and Context

The medieval church had developed an elaborate system of taxation that included tithes (the tenth of agricultural produce), annates (the first year's revenue of a newly appointed benefice), Peter's Pence, and the sale of [[indulgences]]. These levies flowed from every corner of Latin Christendom to Rome, enriching the papal curia while draining local economies. Voltaire describes the papal taxation system as an "apostolic revenue, unlimited and uncertain before the time of pope John XXII, but by him digested into a code of the canon law" (Ch. CVI).

Secular rulers developed their own fiscal instruments in parallel. The French kings raised the *taille* (land tax), the *gabelle* (salt tax), and various aides. Spanish monarchs relied on the *alcabala* (sales tax) and American treasure. The Holy Roman Emperor collected imperial taxes through the diets, though often with great difficulty.

## Key Features

The taxation disputes of the Reformation era can be understood through several overlapping categories:

| Tax Type | Authority | Object of Taxation | Controversy |
|---------|-----------|-------------------|-------------|
| **Tithes** | Church | Agricultural produce (10%) | Diverted wealth from local communities to Rome |
| **Annates** | Papacy | First year's revenue of benefices | Seen as foreign extraction by national churches |
| **Indulgences** | Papacy | Voluntary payments for remission of sins | Catalyst for Luther's Ninety-Five Theses |
| **Royal taxes** | Secular monarchs | Land, salt, trade, persons | Required consent of estates/diets/parliaments |
| **Imperial taxes** | Emperor (via diet) | Principalities and free cities | Resisted by territorial princes seeking autonomy |

> The sale of indulgences and dispensations had reached a level of systematic commercialization that scandalized even loyal Catholics. Voltaire records specific prices from the canon law: absolution for murder by a deacon with permission to hold three benefices cost "about twenty crowns," while bestiality was valued at "two hundred and fifty livres" (Ch. CVI).

## Historical Development

The Reformation transformed taxation by transferring ecclesiastical revenues to secular authorities. In Sweden, Gustavus Vasa "suppressed all those commotions, and artfully established the new religion by preserving the hierarchy, only retrenching the revenues and power of the bishops" (Ch. XCVIII). In England, Henry VIII dissolved the monasteries and seized their estates, with annual revenues that "amounted to sixteen hundred thousand pounds sterling" according to some calculations (Ch. CXIII). In the German principalities, Lutheran rulers appropriated church lands to fund their territorial administrations.

The fiscal capacity of states varied enormously. Voltaire compares the Ottoman Empire, where "the revenue of the exchequer amounted only to near thirty two thousand purses, that is, to about forty six millions of livres" (Ch. CXXX), with the wealth flowing from American mines to Spain. The French monarchy, under Francis I, resorted to alienating royal domains and raising land taxes to fund its Italian wars. Voltaire notes this was "a great advantage which the kings of France had over their neighbours" — the ability to tax without consent — but adds that "this unhappy facility of exhausting the subject was productive of several calamities to France" (Ch. CII).

## Impact and Legacy

The fiscal struggles of the Reformation era established principles that would shape modern public finance: that taxation requires some form of consent, that ecclesiastical revenues are subject to secular jurisdiction, and that the power to tax is inseparable from [[sovereignty]]. The revolt of the [[united-provinces]] against Spanish rule was fueled in part by resistance to Philip II's taxes. The Polish constitution, by contrast, limited royal taxation through the diets, leaving the monarchy perpetually underfunded.

Voltaire's observation that Polish nobles controlled taxation while peasants remained serfs — "the nobles being all upon an equality, acted according to the resolutions of their diets" (Ch. XCVIII) — illustrates how taxation structures reflected and reinforced social hierarchies across early modern Europe.
