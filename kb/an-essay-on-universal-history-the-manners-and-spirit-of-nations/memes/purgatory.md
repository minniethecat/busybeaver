## Overview

Purgatory — the doctrine that souls of the faithful undergo purification after death through temporal punishment for venial sins before entering heaven — was one of the most contested theological concepts of the Reformation era. The doctrine held that indulgences could be applied by suffrage to souls in purgatory, reducing their time of suffering, a feature that made indulgences particularly attractive to the laity and enormously lucrative for the Church.

Voltaire treats purgatory not as a theological abstraction but as a social and economic reality: "a release from the pains of purgatory, either for yourself, or for your deceased friends." (Ch. XCVIII) The commodification of purgatorial relief through the sale of [[indulgences|indulgences]] was, in Voltaire's analysis, among the principal provocations of the [[the-reformation|Reformation]].

## Origins and Context

The doctrine of purgatory developed gradually in Western Christianity. Though the term "purgatorium" does not appear in Scripture, Catholic theologians drew on biblical passages such as 2 Maccabees 12:46 ("It is a holy and wholesome thought to pray for the dead") and 1 Corinthians 3:15 ("he himself shall be saved, yet so as by fire") to establish the concept. The doctrine was formally articulated at the Second Council of Lyon (1274) and reaffirmed at the Council of Florence (1439).

By the late Middle Ages, purgatory had become deeply embedded in popular piety. Indulgences were grants by the Catholic Church that remitted the temporal punishment due for sins that had already been forgiven through [[confession|confession]] and [[absolution|absolution]]. Based on the doctrine of the Treasury of Merit — the superabundant merits of Christ and the saints, which the [[papacy]] could dispense — indulgences could be applied to oneself or to the souls of the deceased in purgatory.

## Key Features

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Temporal punishment | Satisfaction for sin after forgiveness — distinct from eternal damnation of mortal sin |
| Purgatorial fire | The cleansing pain through which the soul is purified |
| Suffrage for the dead | Prayers, Masses, and indulgences offered by the living for souls in purgatory |
| Treasury of Merit | The theological basis for papal authority to grant indulgences |
| Plenary indulgence | Full remission of all temporal punishment due for sin |

## Historical Development

The medieval Church taught that the power of absolution, when combined with the doctrine of purgatory and the sale of [[indulgences|indulgences]], created a comprehensive spiritual economy. Pope [[leo-x|Leo X]] (r. 1513–1521) authorized a major indulgence campaign to fund the rebuilding of St. Peter's Basilica in Rome, promoted by preachers who made increasingly extravagant claims about the efficacy of indulgences, including the famous slogan attributed to Johann Tetzel: "As soon as a coin in the coffer rings, a soul from purgatory springs."

The Reformation directly challenged this system. Luther's Ninety-Five Theses (1517) questioned the pope's power to remit punishment in purgatory, arguing that the living could assist the dead only through prayer and that the sale of indulgences was a perversion of Christian charity. Voltaire captures the atmosphere in which this doctrine took hold: "There had been no separation as yet in laughing at indulgences, in desiring the eucharist to be administered in both kinds, in asserting some unintelligible nonsense about justification."

## Impact and Legacy

The Protestant rejection of purgatory was among the most consequential theological breaks of the Reformation. The doctrine of [[justification-by-faith|justification by faith alone]] rendered purgatory theologically unnecessary: if salvation is a free gift of God received through faith, there is no temporal punishment requiring satisfaction after death. The financial apparatus built around purgatorial relief — indulgences, [[annates]], Masses for the dead — collapsed in Protestant territories.

The [[council-of-trent|Council of Trent]] (1545–1563) reaffirmed the doctrine of purgatory but sought to curb the fiscal abuses associated with it. The Tridentine decree on purgatory mandated that "the more difficult and subtle questions, which do not make for edification, and from which there is very often no increase in piety, shall be excluded from popular discourse." The [[counter-reformation|Counter-Reformation]] thus retained the doctrine while purifying its practice — a pattern Voltaire regarded as characteristic of the Catholic Church's capacity for institutional self-preservation.
