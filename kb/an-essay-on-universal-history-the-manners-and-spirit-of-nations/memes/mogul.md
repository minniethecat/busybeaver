## Overview

The Mogul (Mughal) Empire enters Voltaire's Essay on Universal History as a comparative case — a non-European empire whose political and religious dynamics illuminate, by parallel and contrast, the distinctive features of European development. Voltaire's treatment of the Mogul Empire is concentrated in his chapter on India (Ch. CXXVIII), where he deploys the Mughal example to make two interconnected arguments: first, that conquest and political subjugation intensify rather than diminish popular superstition; and second, that the European stereotype of "Oriental despotism" is both partially accurate and importantly incomplete.

"The Mogul Empire: Under conquest and servitude, Indian superstition grew much stronger; as that of the Egyptians grew much stronger after they were subdued by the Romans" (Ch. CXXVIII). This observation — that foreign domination deepens rather than erodes popular religious enthusiasm — is characteristic of Voltaire's comparative method. The Mughal conquest did not rationalize Indian society; it drove the population deeper into the ritual observances that Voltaire, in his Enlightenment idiom, understood as "superstition."

## Origins and Context

The Mughal Empire was founded by Babur, a Central Asian conqueror of mixed Mongol and Turkic descent, who invaded India in 1526 and established a dynasty that would rule most of the subcontinent for over two centuries. By Voltaire's period of primary focus (the sixteenth century), the Mughal Empire was consolidating under Akbar the Great (r. 1556–1605), whose reign represented the zenith of Mughal power and cultural synthesis.

Voltaire situates the Mughal case within a broader argument about the relationship between conquest and civilization. Like the [[tartar|Tartar]] khanates of the Eurasian steppe and the [[ottoman-empire|Ottoman Empire]], the Mughal state represented a form of conquest-based rule that stood in contrast to the legal and institutional development of Western Europe. Yet Voltaire is careful to note that the Mughal emperor Aurangzeb, "despite vast wealth, ruled over a society where there are none but great lords and poor wretches" — an observation he uses "to critique European assumptions about Oriental despotism" (Ch. CXXVIII).

The Mughal Empire's relationship to European [[commerce]] was increasingly important by the seventeenth century. The Portuguese, Dutch, English, and French established trading posts on the Indian coast, and the competition between European commercial empires increasingly intersected with Mughal politics.

## Historical Development

Voltaire's narrative of the Mughal Empire focuses on its mature phase rather than its origins. The reign of Aurangzeb (r. 1658–1707), the last of the great Mughals, receives particular attention. Aurangzeb's combination of vast personal wealth, absolute political authority, and inability to transform Indian society provides Voltaire with the material for one of his characteristic ironies: the most powerful man in India could do nothing to alter the deep structures of Indian religious and social life.

The Mughal example also serves Voltaire's argument about the limits of military conquest as a vehicle of civilization. The Tartar and Mughal conquests "were extensive rather than durable" — they created empires of astonishing territorial reach but produced no lasting institutional transformation comparable to the development of European [[monarchy]], law, and [[commerce]].

Voltaire draws a further parallel between Mughal India and Roman Egypt: in both cases, foreign conquest intensified rather than diminished the indigenous population's attachment to traditional religious practices. "The people still plunged into rivers during eclipses, persuaded that the sun was falling into the claws of a dragon" (Ch. CXXVIII). This is not mockery — or not only mockery — but evidence for Voltaire's thesis that political servitude and intellectual credulity are two faces of the same condition.

## Impact and Legacy

The Mughal Empire's significance in Voltaire's work is primarily analytical rather than narrative. The Mughal case provides him with evidence for several of his core arguments: that European political development was contingent rather than inevitable; that the relationship between conquest and civilization is complex and often negative; and that the European stereotype of "Oriental despotism" obscures as much as it reveals.

The Mughal example also illuminates Voltaire's understanding of the relationship between religion and politics. Religious enthusiasm, in his analysis, flourishes under conditions of political powerlessness — a thesis he develops through the Indian case and applies, implicitly, to the European experience of the [[the-reformation|Reformation]] and the [[the-inquisition|Inquisition]].

For Voltaire's eighteenth-century readers, the Mughal Empire was also a contemporary reality — a declining but still formidable power whose relationship with the European trading companies (particularly the English East India Company) was reshaping the political economy of Asia. Voltaire's analysis, while rooted in the sixteenth century, gestures toward these contemporary developments.
