## Overview

The Renaissance, in Voltaire's treatment, is above all the flourishing of the polite arts—painting, sculpture, architecture, and literature—that reached its summit in Italy during the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries. It unfolded in parallel with the [[the-reformation]], as Italy, spared the theological convulsions consuming Germany and France, devoted herself to the cultivation of genius.

Writing from the perspective of the Enlightenment, Voltaire celebrates this period as an age of genius comparable to that of Periclean Athens and Augustan Rome. The [[the-renaissance]] produced works that rivalled antiquity even as the armies of [[charles-v]] were plundering Rome and the [[battle-of-pavia]] was reshaping the political map of Europe.

> This glory, during the whole sixteenth century, fell to the share of Italy only. Nothing can give us a stronger idea of ancient Greece: for as the arts flourished in Greece in the midst of foreign and civil wars, so they had the same fate in Italy; and almost every thing in that happy climate was carried to its summit of perfection, while the armies of Charles V. were plundering Rome. (Ch. C)

## Italy as the Seat of Genius

Voltaire insists that Italy alone, among the nations of Europe, was the true heir of ancient Greece in the arts. While Germany, France, and England were consumed by the religious wars of the [[the-reformation]], Italy—largely spared the theological convulsions—devoted herself to the cultivation of genius.

The sack of Rome in 1527, far from extinguishing the arts, was followed by an even greater burst of creative energy, as ten successive popes contributed to the completion of St. Peter's Basilica.

> The arts continued to flourish in Italy, because the pest of religious controversy had not penetrated into that country: While they were cutting one another's throats in Germany, France, and England for things they did not understand, Italy—at rest since the plundering of Rome by Charles V's army—applied herself more than ever to the liberal arts. (Ch. C)

## Painting and Sculpture

Voltaire's judgment on the visual arts is unequivocal: sixteenth-century Italian painting surpassed that of the ancient Greeks, who had neither the variety of colours used by the Italians nor any knowledge of perspective or chiaroscuro.

> It seems indubitable that in this sixteenth century, painting was carried to a pitch of perfection unknown to the ancient Greeks; for neither had they that variety of colours used by the Italians, nor were they acquainted with the art of perspective or of chiaro-oscuro. (Ch. C)

In architecture, Voltaire asserts the Italians surpassed the Greeks entirely. The great fabric of St. Peter's—built by Bramante, Michelangelo, and their successors—was "the finest, the largest, and the boldest temple the world ever beheld."

> All nations acknowledge that nothing can be compared to the principal of modern Rome. (Ch. C)

## Poetry: Ariosto and Tasso

Voltaire's most extended literary comparison pits the Italian Renaissance poets against Homer. He proposes a thought experiment: imagine a reader encountering both the Iliad and Tasso's Jerusalem Delivered for the first time, without knowing the names of the authors.

> Could he possibly avoid giving the preference to Tasso in every respect? Would not he find that the Italian was more interesting, had more economy, order, variety, exactness, and elegance; in a word, more of that softness which adds a lustre to the sublime. (Ch. C)

Ariosto's Orlando Furioso, despite a shared defect with Homer—"a luxuriant fancy, and an excessive fondness of the marvellous"—redeems itself by "the exactness of his allegories, by the keenness of his satyr, by his profound knowledge of human nature."

## History and Philosophy

Voltaire claims for Italy its own Thucydides in Francesco Guicciardini. Niccolo Machiavelli—historian, political thinker, and playwright—was a man of "fine genius" whose comedy La Mandragora is "preferable perhaps to all Aristophanes's comedies."

## The Medici Patronage

The Medici family, particularly [[leo-x]] (r. 1513–1521), embodied the Renaissance synthesis of temporal magnificence and artistic patronage. Leo, the great-grandson of Cosimo de Medici, had been made a cardinal at fourteen and pope at thirty-six.

> The bright days of the Roman empire seemed to revive. Religion had nothing austere in its appearance; but attracted respect by pompous ceremonies: the barbarous stile of the datary was abolished, to make room for the eloquence of the cardinals Bembo and Sadoletus, men who knew how to imitate Cicero's latinity. (Ch. CVI)

## The Invention of Printing

Voltaire singles out the invention of printing as the German contribution to the Renaissance—a discovery through which the Germans "have multiplied human knowledge." He corrects the false report that Faustus (Fust), Gutenberg's partner, was burned as a conjurer by the parliament of Paris.
