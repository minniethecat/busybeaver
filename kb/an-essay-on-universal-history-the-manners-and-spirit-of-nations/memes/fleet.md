## Overview

A fleet — an organized body of warships operating under unified command — was one of the most transformative military and economic institutions of the early modern period. The control of sea lanes by fleets determined the fate of empires, the flow of commerce, and the outcome of wars from the Mediterranean to the Indian Ocean. Voltaire observes that "the fleets of European nations have determined the fate of every coast they visit" (Ch. CXXIX), underscoring how naval power became the decisive factor in global competition among Portugal, Spain, the Ottoman Empire, [[venice]], and later England and the Dutch Republic.

## Origins and Context

The concept of a permanent fighting fleet emerged gradually from the medieval practice of assembling merchant vessels for wartime purposes. By the fifteenth century, the Italian maritime republics — particularly Venice and Genoa — had developed state-owned galley fleets to protect their commercial interests in the eastern Mediterranean. The Ottoman Empire built a formidable galley fleet to challenge Christian naval dominance, while Portugal pioneered the ocean-going sailing fleet that would open the route to India around the Cape of Good Hope.

> As Voltaire notes of Portugal's early expeditions: "a nation, no way powerful, extensive, or populous... fits out a small fleet of adventurers from the port of Lisbon, who discover these vast tracts of land, and make such noble settlements" (Ch. CXX).

## Key Features

The fleets of the sixteenth century fell into three distinct types, each with different strategic purposes:

| Fleet Type | Primary Vessel | Operating Range | Key Powers |
|-----------|---------------|-----------------|------------|
| **Mediterranean galley fleet** | Galley (oar-powered) | Coastal waters | Ottoman, Venice, Spain, Genoa |
| **Ocean-going sailing fleet** | Carrack, galleon | Trans-oceanic | Portugal, Spain, later England |
| **Combined expeditionary fleet** | Galleass, man-of-war | Mixed operations | Spain (Armada), Holy League |

The galley fleet dominated the Mediterranean, where winds were unpredictable and coastal bases plentiful. The sailing fleet ruled the Atlantic and Indian Oceans, where deep water and steady trade winds favored the broadside-armed galleon. The transition from galley to sailing ship as the primary fighting vessel marked a fundamental shift in naval architecture and tactics.

## Historical Development

The Portuguese established the model of the ocean-going trading fleet in the late fifteenth century. Vasco da Gama's voyage of 1497-1498, with a small fleet that "changed the commerce of the old world" (Ch. CXIX), demonstrated that a handful of well-armed ships could project power across oceans and challenge established trade networks. Alfonso de Albuquerque's fleets subsequently "defeated the sultan of Egypt's fleet" and captured key ports from Ormus to Malacca (Ch. CXIX).

In the Mediterranean, the rivalry between Ottoman and Christian fleets culminated at the [[battle-of-lepanto]] (1571), the largest galley engagement since Actium. Voltaire describes how "two hundred and six galleys, and five and twenty men of war, with fifty ships of burden" formed the Christian fleet under Don John of Austria, confronting an Ottoman fleet of some "two hundred and fifty galleys" (Ch. CXXXI). The Christian victory at Lepanto, though celebrated throughout Europe, did not permanently shift the balance of power — the Ottomans rebuilt their fleet within a year.

In the New World, fleets served both conquest and extraction. Cortez, lacking a fleet at Tenochtitlan, "made his soldiers... build nine large brigantines, in order to return to Mexico over that very lake" (Ch. CXXIII). The Spanish treasure fleets, carrying American silver and gold to Seville, became the economic lifeline of the Habsburg empire.

## Impact and Legacy

The fleet transformed European warfare from a land-centric affair to a global competition for maritime dominance. Voltaire specifically credits Andrew Doria, the Genoese admiral who "defeated the Turkish fleets several times" and chose to restore the liberty of his native Genoa rather than rule it as sovereign (Ch. CIV). Doria's career illustrates how fleet command produced a new type of military figure — the naval commander whose expertise and loyalty could determine the fortunes of states.

The Ottoman Empire's reliance on fleets extended to the very structure of its administration. As Voltaire records, "the captain bashaw did not raise from the fiefs... above eight hundred thousand livres for the fleet" (Ch. CXXX), a sum that reveals both the scale of naval expenditure and the fiscal limitations of even the greatest empires.
