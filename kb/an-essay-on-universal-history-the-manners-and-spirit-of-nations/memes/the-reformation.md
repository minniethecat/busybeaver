## Overview

The Reformation was the sixteenth-century religious revolution that shattered the unity of Western Christendom.

In Voltaire's "Essay on Universal History," it is treated not as a single event but as a chain of interconnected movements—Luther's revolt in Germany, Zwingli's in Switzerland, Calvin's in Geneva, and Henry VIII's schism in England—each shaped as much by political circumstance as by theological conviction.

The Reformation intersected with every major figure of the age: [[charles-v]] who fought to suppress it, [[gustavus-vasa]] who used it to consolidate Swedish independence, [[francis-i]] who alternately tolerated and persecuted its adherents, and [[henry-viii]] who turned a marital dispute into a national church.

The [[battle-of-mohacs]] and the [[battle-of-pavia]] unfolded against its backdrop, while the [[the-renaissance]] provided the intellectual tools—printing, humanist scholarship, critical readings of ancient texts—that made Luther's challenge possible.

## Origins and Context

The Reformation emerged from long-standing grievances against the papacy: the sale of indulgences, the accumulation of benefices by absentee clerics, and the temporal power of Rome. When [[leo-x]] authorized the sale of indulgences to fund the rebuilding of St. Peter's Basilica, the friar Martin Luther posted his Ninety-Five Theses at Wittenberg in 1517.

Voltaire notes that the controversy might have remained a minor theological dispute had it not intersected with the political ambitions of German princes eager to curb imperial and papal authority.

> Of Leo X, and of the church — Voltaire devotes a chapter to the state of the papacy on the eve of the Reformation, depicting a court more concerned with patronage and politics than with spiritual authority. (Ch. CVI)

## Luther and the German Reformation

Martin Luther's doctrine of justification by faith alone and his translation of the Bible into German provided both theological and linguistic foundations for the movement. Protected by Frederick the Wise of Saxony after the Diet of Worms (1521)—where [[charles-v]] declared him an outlaw—Luther translated the New Testament at the Wartburg while his ideas spread rapidly through the printing press.

> Of Luther and Zwinglius — Voltaire treats Luther not as a solitary prophet but alongside the Swiss reformer, comparing their temperaments and the different political environments that shaped their movements. (Ch. CVII)

## Spread Through Northern Europe

Lutheranism advanced rapidly in Sweden under [[gustavus-vasa]], who used the new religion to consolidate his rule after liberating Sweden from Danish domination. In Denmark, Frederick I permitted the change of religion.

Voltaire notes the irony that the Dalecarlians—the very peasants who had helped Gustavus Vasa to the throne—were the first to rebel against the religious changes.

> Progress of Lutheranism in Sweden, Denmark, and Germany — Voltaire traces the political geography of the Reformation, showing how it followed the fault lines of dynastic rivalry and national interest. (Ch. CVIII)

## Calvin, Geneva, and the Radical Reformation

The Anabaptists represented the radical wing, repudiated by Luther and persecuted by Catholic and Protestant authorities alike. In Geneva, John Calvin established a theocratic republic whose influence extended across Europe.

> Of Geneva and Calvin — and — Of Calvin and Servetus — two chapters in which Voltaire, himself a fierce critic of religious persecution, holds up Calvin's Geneva as proof that the Reformation did not end the burning of heretics. (Ch. CXI–CXII)

## The English Schism

[[henry-viii]]'s break with Rome was driven not by theology but by the king's desire to annul his marriage to Catherine of Aragon. The resulting Act of Supremacy (1534) made the English monarch head of the church. Voltaire treats the English Reformation as a case study in how personal passions can produce epochal historical change.

> Of King Henry VIII. Of the revolution of religion in England — Voltaire examines how the Levitical prohibition on marrying a brother's widow became the pretext for a national schism. (Ch. CXIII)
