## Overview

Monasticism — the withdrawal from worldly life to pursue spiritual perfection through prayer, asceticism, and communal discipline — constituted one of the most powerful institutions of medieval and early modern Europe. By the sixteenth century, religious orders had accumulated vast wealth and political influence, becoming targets of both reformist critique and internal reform. Voltaire devotes an entire chapter (Ch. CXVII, "Of the Religious Orders") to their history, noting the paradox that institutions founded on renunciation of the world had become among the world's greatest landowners.

## Origins and Context

The monastic tradition in Western Christianity traces its origins to the desert fathers of Egypt and the Rule of St. Benedict in the sixth century. Over the centuries, waves of reform produced new orders: the Cluniacs, the Cistercians, and in the thirteenth century, the mendicant orders — Dominicans and Franciscans (Cordeliers) — who renounced not only personal but corporate property and lived by begging.

These mendicant orders became particularly significant in the history of the Church. The Inquisition itself was "entrusted to the care of the Dominicans and the Cordeliers," giving monastic orders judicial power over life and death. The guardian of the Cordeliers at Paris and the provincial of the Dominicans served as grand inquisitors under the bull of Pope Alexander III in 1255. (Ch. CXVIII)

## Monastic Wealth and Temporal Power

By the sixteenth century, the wealth of religious houses had become immense. Monasteries controlled extensive agricultural lands, received tithes and benefices, and accumulated treasures through donations and bequests. In many regions, the Church owned between one-quarter and one-third of all arable land. This concentration of wealth in "dead hands" (property that could never be sold or inherited) provoked resentment from both peasants and princes.

The sale of indulgences — a practice Voltaire identifies with the papal agent Arcemboldi, "who had sold indulgences in Sweden and Denmark" — was in part a mechanism for extracting money from the faithful to fund the opulent lifestyle of the papal court and monastic establishments. The contrast between monastic wealth and the simple origins of Christian community became a central theme of Reformation critique. (Ch. XCVIII)

## Reformation and Dissolution

The Protestant Reformation fundamentally challenged the theological basis of monasticism. Luther, himself an Augustinian friar, came to reject the doctrine of salvation through works — of which monastic vows were the supreme example. His doctrine of justification by faith alone rendered the entire monastic enterprise spiritually superfluous. In England, Henry VIII dissolved the monasteries between 1536 and 1541, seizing their wealth and redistributing their lands to the gentry — a policy that simultaneously enriched the crown and created a class of landowners with a vested interest in the Protestant settlement. (Ch. CXIII)

## Impact and Legacy

Despite the Reformation's assault, monasticism survived and adapted. The Council of Trent (1545–1563) reformed religious orders, and new orders — most notably the [[jesuits]] — embraced active ministry in education, missionary work, and the combat of heresy. These new orders abandoned the cloister for the world, representing a fundamental reorientation of the monastic ideal from withdrawal to engagement.
