## Overview

The clergy constituted the First Estate in the tripartite social order of medieval and early modern Europe, standing above the nobility and commoners in formal rank. In the sixteenth century, as [[the-reformation]] unfolded, the clerical order was simultaneously at the height of its temporal power and the target of the most severe criticism it had ever faced. Voltaire's *Essay on Universal History* devotes sustained attention to the political, economic, and moral condition of the clergy across Europe, showing how their accumulated wealth and privileges became a primary catalyst for religious revolution.

## Origins and Context

The clergy's temporal power grew over centuries through a combination of land donations, legal immunities, and the Church's monopoly over education and administration. By the time of [[charles-v]], bishops and abbots often functioned as territorial princes. Voltaire notes that in Sweden, "the archbishop of Upsal, and six bishops, who were possessed of very near the same authority as most of the clergy had acquired in Germany and in other countries" held power second only to the crown (Ch. XCVIII).

The clerical estate was internally stratified: the higher clergy (bishops, abbots, cardinals) lived as feudal magnates, while the lower clergy (parish priests, mendicant friars) often shared the poverty of the common people. This disparity within the Church itself became a source of tension, exploited by reformers like [[lutheranism|Martin Luther]].

## Key Features

The power of the clergy in early modern Europe rested on several pillars:

- **Land Ownership**: The Church held approximately one-third of all arable land in many European kingdoms, making it the single largest landowner
- **Legal Privileges**: Clergy were subject to canon law rather than civil courts (benefit of clergy), and could not be taxed without their consent
- **Sacramental Authority**: Control over baptism, marriage, confession, and burial gave the clergy direct authority over every life stage
- **Education Monopoly**: Universities and schools were Church institutions; literacy was largely confined to the clerical class
- **Political Office**: Cardinals and bishops routinely served as royal ministers, as in the case of Cardinal Granvelle under Charles V and Cardinal Wolsey under Henry VIII

> The members of the sacred college disputed the point of precedency with the electors of Germany, and yet in France and England they yielded the pre-eminence to the chancellors; which is one of the many contradictions that human pride had introduced into the republic of Christendom. (Ch. XCVIII)

## Historical Development

Voltaire chronicles the clergy's evolution from spiritual shepherds to temporal princes. He notes that Pope Pius II had observed "that the clergy were forbid to marry for very strong reasons; but that there were much stronger reasons to allow them to marry" (Ch. CVI). The prohibition on marriage, combined with vast wealth, created the conditions for the scandalous behavior that reformers denounced.

The sale of indulgences — "that apostolic revenue, unlimited and uncertain before the time of pope John XXII. but by him digested into a code" (Ch. CVI) — represented the commercialization of spiritual authority at its most extreme. In Sweden, a papal legate named Arcemboldi "had squeezed near two millions of florins out of the poorest country in Europe" (Ch. XCVIII).

Protestant reformers argued that the clergy had become a parasitic class. Voltaire reports that Protestant writers "carefully collected proofs, to shew that in several parts of Germany, the people always obliged the secular clergy to have concubines, to the end that married women might be less exposed to danger" (Ch. CVI).

## Impact and Legacy

The clergy's resistance to reform proved costly. In Sweden, Archbishop Troll of Upsal led armed resistance against [[gustavus-vasa]], excommunicating the king and senate "at high mass; after which they laid their vestments on the altar, and putting on a cuirass and a sword, they went out of the church and commenced a civil war" which "lasted seven years" (Ch. XCVIII). This fusion of spiritual and military authority discredited the clerical estate in the eyes of many.

However, Voltaire also notes exceptions. Spain under Cardinal Ximenes was "remarkable for the exemplary lives of the clergy," demonstrating that reform could come from within. The Reformation ultimately transformed rather than eliminated the clerical order: Lutheran territories preserved the hierarchy while "retrenching the revenues and power of the bishops" (Ch. XCVIII).

## Key Figures

- **Archbishop Troll of Upsal** — Militant Swedish primate who fought against Gustavus Vasa
- **Cardinal Ximenes** — Spanish reformer who enforced strict clerical discipline
- **Cardinal Wolsey** — English chancellor whose wealth exemplified clerical accumulation
- **Cardinal Granvelle** — Imperial minister under [[charles-v]]
- **Pope Leo X** — Medici pope whose court epitomized both Renaissance culture and clerical worldliness
