## Overview

The Battle of Pavia (24 February 1525) was the decisive engagement of the Italian Wars in which the imperial army of [[charles-v]] defeated the French forces of [[francis-i]], taking the French king prisoner. Fought near the city of Pavia in the Duchy of Milan, the battle marked the collapse of French power in Italy and demonstrated the ascendancy of the Habsburg monarchy.

Voltaire treats Pavia as the dramatic turning point of the rivalry between [[charles-v]] and [[francis-i]]—a catastrophe for France that followed the glory of Marignano (1515).

The battle occurred in the same decade as the [[battle-of-mohacs]] (1526), together transforming the European balance. While the [[the-reformation]] divided Christendom and the [[the-renaissance]] reached its summit in Italy, Pavia demonstrated the supremacy of Habsburg arms under [[charles-v]] and the fragility of Valois ambitions.

## Background

The rivalry between [[charles-v]] and [[francis-i]] had been simmering since Charles's election as Holy Roman Emperor in 1519. When the electors chose Charles instead—financed by the Fugger banking house—the two monarchs became locked in a struggle for control of Italy and the European balance.

> Charles as sovereign of the Netherlands laid claim to Artois, and to a great many towns; as king of Naples and Sicily, he saw Francis I ready to make good his pretensions to those dominions. What a number of pretences for laying Europe waste! (Ch. CII)

## The Defection of the Constable of Bourbon

Voltaire identifies the personal injustice done to Charles of Bourbon as a primary cause of the French disaster. The Constable of Bourbon—who had contributed decisively to Francis's victory at Marignano—was targeted by the king's mother, Louisa of Savoy, who pursued a lawsuit against him for his entire estate.

> The mortifications the Constable had suffered upon all occasions, were his least complaint. Louisa of Savoy, duchess of Angouleme, the king's mother, had made overtures of marriage to the Constable upon the death of his wife: meeting with a repulse she resolved to ruin him. (Ch. CII–CIII)

Bourbon became the principal instrument of Charles's victory at Pavia—a defection that Voltaire presents as the bitter fruit of royal ingratitude.

## The Battle

[[francis-i]] penetrated into the Milanese with a numerous army. Pope Clement VII, fearing the emperor's growing power, openly espoused the French cause. Yet the French were defeated.

> Pope Clement VII having reason to be afraid of the emperor, openly espouses the cause of France. The best general of those days, John de Medicis, fights for him at the head of a chosen band. Yet he is defeated before Pavia. (Ch. CIII)

The French army was routed, the principal nobility taken prisoner alongside their king. Francis wrote the famous letter: "Madam, all is lost but my honour."

> Never was there a truer letter than that which he wrote to his mother: Madam, all is lost but my honour. The frontiers left naked, the exchequer empty, a consternation spread through the different orders of the kingdom. (Ch. CIII)

## Aftermath

The battle left France prostrate. [[francis-i]] was transported to Madrid as a prisoner, obtaining his freedom only by renouncing claims to Italy and surrendering Burgundy—promises he had no intention of keeping.

Voltaire notes that the victory did not make [[charles-v]] master of Europe. The system of counterbalancing power was established precisely at this moment.

> Immediately after the taking of Francis I, England and the Italian states entered into a league with France to balance the power of the emperor. (Ch. CIII)

## Historical Significance

The Battle of Pavia was one of the most memorable examples of the caprice of fortune. It shattered the aura of French invincibility that had surrounded Francis since Marignano, demonstrated the vulnerability of Valois power, and inaugurated two decades of Habsburg preponderance in Italian affairs.

Yet within a few years [[francis-i]] was again campaigning across the Alps, and the [[the-reformation]] continued to erode the religious unity that [[charles-v]] sought to defend.
