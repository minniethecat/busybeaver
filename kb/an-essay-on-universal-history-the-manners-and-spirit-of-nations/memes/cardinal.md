## Overview

The cardinalate in Voltaire's narrative of the sixteenth century represents the intersection of spiritual authority and temporal power at its most concentrated. Cardinals were not merely churchmen but princes of the Church in the most literal sense — rulers of territories, brokers of alliances, and sometimes commanders of armies. When Voltaire notes that "the feudal government had made sovereigns of several bishops and monks" (Ch. XCIX), the cardinals stood at the apex of this ecclesiastical-political complex.

The [[clergy]]'s highest rank epitomized both the achievements and the corruptions of the Renaissance Church. Cardinals were patrons of art and learning, but they were also products of a system in which ecclesiastical office was inseparable from family ambition, political calculation, and financial interest.

## Origins and Context

The office of cardinal originated in the early medieval Church as the senior clergy of Rome and its suburban dioceses, who assisted the pope in liturgical and administrative functions. By the sixteenth century, the College of Cardinals had evolved into something far greater: a body of international scope that elected the pope, governed the Church between pontificates, and supplied the personnel for the Roman [[curia]].

Voltaire is attentive to the gap between the cardinal's spiritual office and temporal reality. The concordat between [[francis-i]] and [[leo-x]] made the French king the effective master of episcopal and abbatial appointments, but cardinals, as members of the pope's immediate circle, remained instruments of papal policy — and sometimes of secular dynasties that placed their younger sons in the cardinalate.

## Key Features

The cardinalate as Voltaire describes it had several defining characteristics:

| Aspect | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Electoral function** | Exclusive right to elect the pope in conclave |
| **Curial administration** | Staffed the Roman congregations that governed the universal Church |
| **Territorial power** | Many cardinals ruled as temporal lords over substantial territories |
| **Diplomatic role** | Served as papal legates, negotiated treaties, mediated between princes |
| **Patronage** | Commissioned art, architecture, and scholarship on a princely scale |
| **Wealth** | Benefices, tithes, and family fortunes made cardinals among the richest men in Europe |

## Historical Development

The cardinalate was dramatically transformed by the crises of the sixteenth century. The Reformation challenged the very legitimacy of the papal monarchy that cardinals served. The sack of Rome (1527) exposed the vulnerability of the Curia to secular violence. And the Council of Trent (1545–1563), while reaffirming papal authority, also inaugurated reforms that would gradually reshape the cardinalate from a Renaissance court into a more recognizably ecclesiastical body.

Voltaire observes that the papacy, despite its spiritual claims, had long been entangled in the same feudal and dynastic logic that governed secular principalities. Cardinals were appointed not only for their theological learning but for their family connections — the Medici, Borgia, and Farnese cardinals were princes first and priests second. Yet Voltaire also notes that the popes "have been observed to enact wise laws, and even laws useful to Christendom, where their own personal interests were not concerned" (Ch. XCVIII).

## Impact and Legacy

The cardinalate was both a cause and a symptom of the Church's entanglement with temporal power that the Reformation attacked. When Luther burned the [[papal-bull]] *Exsurge Domine* in 1520, he was rejecting not just a theological document but the entire system of ecclesiastical authority that the cardinalate represented.

Yet the institution survived and adapted. The post-Tridentine cardinalate became more disciplined, more pastoral, and less flagrantly political — though it never fully shed its character as a court. The model of the cardinal-prince, embodied by figures like Cardinal Wolsey in England and Cardinal Richelieu in France (slightly after Voltaire's period), remained a central figure in European politics for another two centuries.

## Key Figures

- **Cardinal Wolsey** — Henry VIII's chief minister, whose failure to secure the king's annulment led to his downfall and the English break with Rome
- **Giulio de' Medici (Clement VII)** — Cardinal who became pope, presiding over the Church during the sack of Rome and the English schism
- **Cardinal Cisneros (Jiménez de Cisneros)** — Franciscan reformer who served as regent of Spain and exemplified the cardinal as both churchman and statesman
- **Alessandro Farnese (Paul III)** — Cardinal-patron who convened the Council of Trent and began Catholic reform
