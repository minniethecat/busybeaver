## Overview

The Battle of Mohács (1526) was one of the most consequential battles of the sixteenth century. Fought on the plains of southern Hungary, it pitted the army of [[suleiman-the-magnificent]] against the forces of King Louis II of Hungary. The Hungarian army was annihilated, the king was killed, and the defeat opened the door to over 150 years of Ottoman domination in central Hungary.

The battle occurred in the same decade as the [[battle-of-pavia]] (1525), together redrawing the map of Europe. While [[charles-v]] celebrated his victory over [[francis-i]] in the west, the Ottoman triumph at Mohács revealed the vulnerability of Christendom's eastern frontier.

This vulnerability was deepened by the religious divisions of the [[the-reformation]] and the political preoccupations of the [[the-renaissance]] courts.

## Background

Hungary had suffered decades of internal strife before Mohács. The kingdom, though blessed by nature with "mines of gold, and with the real treasures of corn and wine," had been weakened by the elective monarchy, the tyranny of the nobles over the peasantry, and the constant threat of Ottoman incursions.

The Hungarian nobility had reduced the common people to so miserable a servitude that the peasants rose up in a four-year civil war—a conflict that concluded, Voltaire observes, with "rivetting the chains of the common people."

## The Battle

King Louis II, brother-in-law to the Emperor [[charles-v]], could muster no more than thirty thousand fighting men against Suleiman's forces. The army was commanded by a Franciscan friar named Tomori, who—with five bishops among the commanders—promised the king divine victory.

> A cordelier, named Tomori, was general of this army, in which there were five bishops: this man promised king Lewis that he should obtain the victory; but the king was killed, and the army destroyed at the famous battle of Mohats, in 1526. (Ch. XCVIII)

## Aftermath

After his victory, Suleiman swept through Hungary with his armies, carrying off above two hundred thousand captives.

> In vain has nature blessed this kingdom with mines of gold, and with the real treasures of corn and wine; in vain has she favoured the inhabitants with strength of constitution, and with quick understanding: the country was a vast desert—ruinous towns, fields tilled with the sword, villages dug under ground where inhabitants buried themselves with corn and cattle. (Ch. XCVIII)

## Historical Significance

Mohács ended the independent Kingdom of Hungary as it had existed since the time of St. Stephen. The death of Louis II without an heir triggered a succession crisis that brought the Habsburgs to the Hungarian throne.

Ferdinand I, [[charles-v]]'s brother, claimed the crown. Hungary was partitioned between the Ottoman Empire, the Habsburg domains, and the semi-independent Principality of Transylvania, a division that would shape Central European politics until the Treaty of Karlowitz in 1699.
