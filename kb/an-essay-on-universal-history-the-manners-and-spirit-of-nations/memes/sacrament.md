## Overview

A **sacrament** is a Christian rite recognized as being of particular importance and significance, understood as a visible sign of invisible divine grace. In the [[catholicism|Catholic Church]], seven sacraments were formally defined: Baptism, Confirmation, the Eucharist ([[mass|Mass]]), Penance, Anointing of the Sick, Holy Orders, and Matrimony. The theological understanding and number of sacraments became one of the most sharply contested issues of the [[the-reformation|Protestant Reformation]], with reformers reducing the number and reinterpreting their nature. (Ch. CVI)

## Origins and Context

The concept of sacrament derives from the Latin _sacramentum_ — originally a military oath of allegiance, adapted by early Christian writers to describe the sacred mysteries of the faith. The medieval Church developed an elaborate sacramental theology, systematized by scholastic theologians who defined a sacrament as "a sign of a sacred thing inasmuch as it sanctifies men." The sevenfold enumeration was formally defined at the [[council|Council of Florence]] (1439) and reaffirmed at the [[council|Council of Trent]] (1547). (Ch. CVI)

## Key Features

| Sacrament | Description | Protestant View |
|-----------|-------------|-----------------|
| Baptism | Entry into the Christian community through water | Retained by most Protestants |
| Confirmation | Strengthening of baptismal grace by a bishop | Rejected as a sacrament by most |
| Eucharist (Mass) | The body and blood of Christ under bread and wine | Retained but reinterpreted |
| Penance | Confession and absolution of sins | Generally rejected as a sacrament |
| Extreme Unction | Anointing of the sick or dying | Retained by some, rejected by others |
| Holy Orders | Ordination of clergy | Rejected as a sacrament |
| Matrimony | Sanctification of marriage | Rejected as a sacrament by most |

The [[mass|Catholic Mass]] — the sacrament of the Eucharist — was the central act of worship in the medieval Church. The doctrine of transubstantiation, defined at the Fourth Lateran Council (1215), held that the bread and wine were literally transformed into the body and blood of Christ. This doctrine became a particular target of [[lutheranism|Lutheran]] criticism. (Ch. CVI)

## Historical Development

The [[the-reformation|Reformation]] shattered the medieval consensus on sacraments. [[martin-luther|Martin Luther]] reduced the number to two or three (Baptism, Eucharist, and in some formulations Penance), arguing that only those rites directly instituted by Christ in Scripture qualified as sacraments. Luther retained a modified form of the real presence in the Eucharist, rejecting transubstantiation as an Aristotelian distortion. (Ch. CVI)

The [[calvinism|Calvinist]] tradition went further, treating the sacraments as signs and seals of God's covenant rather than vehicles of grace in themselves. The [[council|Council of Trent]], in response, reaffirmed all seven sacraments and the doctrine of transubstantiation in the strongest terms, drawing a sharp confessional boundary between [[catholicism|Catholicism]] and [[protestantism|Protestantism]] that persists to the present day. (Ch. CVI)

## Impact and Legacy

The sacramental controversy was not merely theological — it had profound social and political consequences. The reduction of sacraments undermined the institutional power of the Church, since sacraments such as Holy Orders and Penance were the basis of clerical authority. The rejection of the sacrificial character of the [[mass|Mass]] transformed the role of the priest from mediator between God and humanity to preacher and pastor. These divergent sacramental theologies created fundamentally different religious cultures that underpinned the conflicts convulsing Europe from the 16th through the 17th centuries. (Ch. CVI)
