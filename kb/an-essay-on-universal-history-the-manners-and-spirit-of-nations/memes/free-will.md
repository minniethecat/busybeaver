## Overview

Free will — the doctrine that human beings possess the capacity to choose between good and evil without divine predetermination — was, in Voltaire's telling, one of the most bitterly contested propositions of the Reformation era. The question divided not only Catholics from Protestants but Protestants from one another, with [[luther-martin|Martin Luther]] denying free will in his polemic against Erasmus, [[calvin-john|John Calvin]] building an entire theological system on predestination, and the Catholic Church reaffirming the cooperation of human freedom with divine grace at the [[council-of-trent|Council of Trent]].

Voltaire approached the dispute with his signature blend of historical precision and philosophical irony. "The question about free-will," he wrote, "that other rock on which human reason has so often split, proved an endless source of nonsensical quarrels, to supply the torrent of theological hatred." For Voltaire, the free-will controversy was the paradigmatic case of a theological dispute that generated immense human suffering while remaining fundamentally irresolvable by human reason. (Ch. CVII)

## Luther's Denial of Free Will

Luther's position on free will was forged in his 1525 treatise *De Servo Arbitrio* (On the Bondage of the Will), written in response to Erasmus's *De Libero Arbitrio*. Luther argued that since the Fall, the human will is enslaved to sin and incapable of turning toward God without divine grace. Salvation was entirely the work of God; human free will, if it existed at all, extended only to matters below the level of salvation — what one ate, whom one married, what trade one practiced — but not to the decisive question of one's eternal destiny.

Voltaire recorded that "Luther denied free-will, which his followers have since granted," noting the irony that Lutheranism itself moderated its founder's most extreme position. The universities of Louvain and Paris both wrote against Luther on this point, with the Parisian theological faculty suspending "for a while her old dispute, whether there were three Mary Magdalens or only one, to condemn the opinions of Luther." Voltaire's juxtaposition of the two debates — free will and the number of Mary Magdalens — was deliberate: both represented, in his view, the tendency of scholastic theology to invest infinite passion in questions that reason could never definitively settle. (Ch. CVII)

## Calvin and Predestination

[[calvin-john|John Calvin]] developed the denial of free will into a comprehensive theological system centered on the doctrine of double predestination: God, from all eternity, had predestined some to salvation (the elect) and others to damnation (the reprobate), without regard to any foreseen merit or demerit. Human beings could do nothing to alter their eternal fate; the function of the law, the sacraments, and the visible Church was to guide the elect toward the assurance of their salvation and to maintain order among the reprobate.

Voltaire observed that this apparently terrifying doctrine had the paradoxical effect of attracting followers. "They deprived man of free-will, and the multitude ran after them." He attributed this success not to the intrinsic appeal of predestination but to the rigorism of the Reformers' moral demands: "the method to have many followers, now-a-days, is not by proposing a plain easy way to heaven; he who lays down the most rigid rules, will have the most numerous audience." Calvin's Geneva, where "plays and public shows were forbid" and instrumental music was suppressed for two centuries, exemplified the paradox of a theology that denied human freedom while demanding the most exacting moral discipline. (Ch. CX)

## The Council of Trent and the Catholic Response

The Catholic Church addressed the free-will controversy at the sixth session of the Council of Trent (1547), issuing a decree on justification that affirmed both the necessity of divine grace and the reality of human free cooperation. Against Luther, Trent insisted that free will, though weakened by sin, was not extinguished; against Calvin, it denied that predestination to damnation could be reconciled with divine justice. The Tridentine formulation was a characteristically Catholic attempt to hold together what the Reformers had separated: divine sovereignty and human responsibility, grace and free will, faith and works.

In practice, however, the Catholic position proved no more capable of ending the dispute than the Protestant positions had been. The debates continued within Catholicism between Dominicans and Jesuits, between Jansenists and Molinists, generating fresh controversies that would convulse the French Church in the seventeenth century. Voltaire's implication was clear: the free-will question was a theological quagmire from which no confession could emerge unscathed. (Ch. CXVII)

## A Philosophical Dead End

For Voltaire, the free-will controversy served as a cautionary tale about the limits of theological speculation. The question itself, he implied, was ill-posed: "nonsensical quarrels" that "human reason has so often split" upon were not signs of intellectual vitality but of a misdirection of intellectual energy. The same passion and erudition that theologians invested in reconciling free will with divine foreknowledge could have been directed toward practical morality, legal reform, or the cultivation of the arts and sciences.

The legacy of the free-will controversy extended beyond theology into the emerging distinction between religious and civil authority. By demonstrating that the most fundamental questions of human existence could not be resolved by theological argument, the Reformation debates inadvertently strengthened the case for [[liberty-of-conscience|liberty of conscience]]: if the learned could not agree on free will after decades of disputation, what justification could there be for compelling the unlearned to profess one position or another on pain of death?
