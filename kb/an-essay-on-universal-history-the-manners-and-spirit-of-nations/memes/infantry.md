## Overview

Infantry — soldiers who fought on foot rather than on horseback — underwent a revolutionary transformation during the early modern period, becoming the decisive arm of European warfare. The rise of pike-and-shot formations, the introduction of portable firearms, and the development of professional standing regiments turned infantry from an auxiliary force into the central instrument of military power. Voltaire observes that in the sixteenth century "the German and Spanish infantry were reputed the best" (Ch. C), while also noting the persistence of older patterns: in Hungary, "the infantry was a crowd of undisciplined peasants, who used to fight only between sowing time and harvest" (Ch. XCVIII).

## Origins and Context

Medieval warfare had been dominated by heavy cavalry — the mounted knight in armor, supported by his feudal retinue. Infantry played a secondary role, often as poorly armed levies. The Swiss cantons changed this in the fourteenth and fifteenth centuries, developing dense pike formations (*Gewalthaufen*) that could withstand and defeat cavalry charges. Their victories over the Burgundian knights in the 1470s demonstrated that disciplined foot soldiers with long pikes could humble the flower of European chivalry.

The introduction of gunpowder weapons added a new dimension. The arquebus, and later the musket, gave infantry the ability to penetrate armor at range. The combination of pikemen (to hold off cavalry) and shot (to deliver firepower) in the Spanish *tercio* created a formation that dominated European battlefields for over a century.

## Key Features

The infantry revolution of the sixteenth century can be analyzed through several components:

| Element | Description | Primary Innovators |
|---------|------------|-------------------|
| **Pike** | 18-foot wooden shaft with steel head | Swiss, Landsknechts |
| **Arquebus/Musket** | Matchlock firearm, armor-piercing at range | Spanish, German |
| **Tercio formation** | Combined pike-and-shot square | Spanish |
| **Professional regiments** | Standing units with permanent officers | Spanish, French, Imperial |
| **Drill and discipline** | Standardized training in formation movement | Dutch (Maurice of Nassau) |

> At the battle of Marignano (1515), Voltaire describes the Swiss infantry as nearly invincible: "the black German bands in the king's army were as good infantry as theirs, and they had no gendarmes... they were able to withstand almost two days the weight of those heavy horse, which were continually pressing upon their broken battalions" (Ch. CI). Marignano demonstrated both the formidable power of disciplined infantry and their vulnerability when isolated from cavalry and artillery support.

## Historical Development

The Swiss and German mercenary infantry — the famous Landsknechts — dominated the Italian Wars. Emperor Maximilian I had "introduced the arms of the Macedonian phalanx, which were pikes eighteen feet long" (Ch. C), deliberately reviving ancient infantry tactics. The Swiss, initially armed with pikes, "changed them afterwards for the two-handed sword" (Ch. C), illustrating the ongoing tactical experimentation of the period.

The Spanish *tercio*, developed by Gonzalo Fernandez de Cordoba (the "Great Captain"), integrated pikemen, arquebusiers, and swordsmen into a single flexible formation. At the battle of St. Quentin (1557), the Spanish infantry so thoroughly defeated the French that "the infantry was quite demolished, being all killed or taken prisoners: the conquerors lost no more than fourscore men" (Ch. CXXXIV). This lopsided result showed what professional infantry could achieve against a less disciplined opponent.

The Ottoman Empire fielded a different kind of infantry: the Janissaries, an elite corps of slave-soldiers raised through the *devshirme* system. Voltaire compares them to the Egyptian Mamluks — "a formidable militia of foreigners" (Ch. CXXX). The Janissaries combined archery with firearms earlier than most European infantry and were, until the late sixteenth century, the most feared infantry in the eastern Mediterranean.

## Impact and Legacy

The infantry revolution transformed the social basis of warfare. Medieval armies had been led by a hereditary warrior aristocracy whose military role was inseparable from their social status. The new infantry required neither noble birth nor expensive equipment: a pike cost little, and an arquebusier could be trained in weeks. This democratization of military effectiveness contributed, over the long term, to the erosion of aristocratic military monopoly.

The cost of maintaining professional infantry regiments also transformed state finance. Unlike feudal levies, who served for a fixed term, professional infantry had to be paid, fed, and equipped year-round. This created a permanent fiscal demand that drove the development of modern [[taxation]] systems and state bureaucracies.

Voltaire's account of infantry warfare in the New World provides a grim coda. At Tenochtitlan, Cortez's infantry fought battles in which "the killing of twenty Spaniards... was an event of greater importance to this part of the world, than the multitudes slain in our European engagements" (Ch. CXXIII). The technological superiority of European infantry over American warriors was as decisive as their numerical inferiority.
