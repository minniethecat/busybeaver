## Overview

Indulgences were grants by the Catholic Church that remitted the temporal punishment due for sins that had already been forgiven through confession and absolution. Based on the doctrine of the Treasury of Merit—the superabundant merits of Christ and the saints that the Church could dispense—indulgences could be applied to the living or, through suffrage, to souls in Purgatory. In the early 16th century, the aggressive marketing of indulgences to finance the construction of St. Peter's Basilica provoked Martin Luther's protest and ignited the Reformation.

## Origins and Context

The practice of granting indulgences evolved from the early Church's system of canonical penance. By the late Middle Ages, the theology of indulgences had become elaborate: the Church claimed the power to draw on a treasury of surplus merit accumulated by Christ's sacrifice and the supererogatory works of the saints.

Pope Leo X (r. 1513–1521) authorized a major indulgence campaign to fund the rebuilding of St. Peter's Basilica in Rome. The campaign was promoted by preachers who made increasingly extravagant claims about the efficacy of indulgences, including the notorious slogan attributed to Johann Tetzel: "As soon as a coin in the coffer rings, a soul from Purgatory springs."

Voltaire describes the commercial character of the operation: "Under the pretence of a war against the Turks, he set up a sale in all parts of Christendom, of what they call Indulgences." (Ch. CVI) The trade was farmed out commercially, with indulgence sellers operating from alehouses and making claims that exceeded even the Church's generous official theology.

## Key Features

The indulgence system involved several distinctive elements:

- **Treasury of Merit**: The theological basis for indulgences—the Church's claimed authority to dispense the surplus merits of Christ and saints
- **Plenary vs. Partial Indulgences**: A plenary indulgence remitted all temporal punishment; a partial indulgence remitted a portion
- **Conditions**: Indulgences typically required confession, communion, and the performance of specified works (prayer, pilgrimage, almsgiving)
- **Financial Commutation**: By the 15th century, many prescribed works could be commuted to monetary payments, effectively making indulgences purchasable
- **Application to the Dead**: Indulgences could be applied by suffrage to souls in Purgatory, a feature that made them particularly attractive to the laity

## Historical Significance

The indulgence controversy of 1517 was the proximate cause of the Reformation. When the Dominican order obtained the indulgence franchise in Saxony—territory traditionally served by Augustinians—Martin Luther was instructed by his Augustinian superiors to preach against the practice.

Luther's 95 Theses, posted (according to tradition) on the door of the Wittenberg Castle Church on October 31, 1517, focused specifically on indulgences. His central challenge was not initially to papal authority itself but to the claim that the Pope could remit the guilt or eternal punishment of sin through indulgences. The theological dispute rapidly expanded, however, into a comprehensive challenge to the sacramental system.

The Council of Trent (1545–1563) reformed the indulgence system in response to Protestant criticism, abolishing the most notorious financial abuses while reaffirming the underlying doctrine. The sale of indulgences was prohibited, though the granting of indulgences for pious works (without payment) continued.
