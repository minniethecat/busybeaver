## Overview

**Grace** (Latin: _gratia_; Greek: _charis_) is the unmerited and freely given favor of God toward humanity. In the context of Voltaire's history, the doctrine of grace became the theological fault line of the sixteenth century: the Protestant [[the-reformation|Reformation]] asserted that salvation came through grace alone (sola gratia), received by faith alone, while the Catholic Church maintained that grace operated through the sacraments and required human cooperation. What appeared to be an arcane theological dispute had enormous political and social consequences, shattering the unity of Western Christendom. (Ch. CVI, CIX)

## The Augustinian Background

The Western doctrine of grace was shaped decisively by St. Augustine's controversy with Pelagius in the early fifth century. Pelagius had taught that human beings could, by their natural powers and free will, live morally and merit salvation — a position Augustine denounced as denying the necessity of divine grace. Augustine's victorious position held that original sin had so corrupted human nature that no one could turn to God without a special, prevenient grace that preceded and enabled any good act.

This Augustinian framework — grace as necessary, unmerited, and efficacious — became the shared inheritance of medieval theology. But it contained an unresolved tension: if grace was necessary for salvation, and God alone decided to whom to grant it, did that not imply predestination? And if grace could be resisted, did that not make salvation partly dependent on human effort? (Ch. CVI)

## The Reformation Divide

The Reformation brought these latent tensions to the surface. [[martin-luther|Martin Luther]], an Augustinian friar, radicalized the doctrine of grace: human beings contributed nothing to their salvation; even faith was a gift of God; good works, while they might flow from a justified life, played no role in obtaining justification. This was the doctrine of _sola gratia_ — grace alone.

[[calvinism|John Calvin]] developed the most rigorous formulation. In his _Institutes_, Calvin argued for double predestination: God had, from eternity, elected some to salvation (the elect) and passed over others (the reprobate). Grace was irresistible — the elect could not finally reject it — and perseverance was guaranteed: those truly elect would never fall away. (Ch. CX–CXII)

| Doctrine | Catholic Position | Lutheran Position | Calvinist Position |
|----------|------------------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Source of grace | God, through sacraments | God, through Word and faith | God, by eternal decree |
| Human role | Cooperation (synergism) | Passive reception | None (monergism) |
| Sacramental mediation | Necessary (ex opere operato) | Baptism and Eucharist only | Symbolic only |
| Predestination | Conditional on foreknowledge | Single (election only) | Double (election and reprobation) |

## Voltaire's Perspective

Voltaire treats the grace controversy with his characteristic irony. He observes that the finer points of the dispute — sufficient versus efficacious grace, congruous versus condign merit — were unintelligible to most of those who fought and killed over them. Yet he does not dismiss the question as meaningless: he recognizes that at stake was the nature of human freedom, the character of God, and the institutional power of the Church.

If grace could be obtained through the sacraments administered by priests, then the clergy held the keys to salvation — a vast source of authority and revenue. If grace came directly from God to the individual believer through faith, then the entire sacerdotal system, from [[indulgences|indulgences]] to [[purgatory|purgatory]], collapsed. The doctrine of grace was thus inseparable from the question of ecclesiastical power. (Ch. CVI, CIX)

> Luther succeeded in exciting the princes, nobility and magistrates against the pope and the bishops. (Ch. CIX)
