## Overview

The bishop in early modern Europe was far more than a spiritual overseer. As Voltaire repeatedly demonstrates, bishops functioned as territorial princes, military commanders, royal ministers, and major economic actors. The episcopal office stood at the intersection of sacred and secular power, making bishops central figures in the political struggles of the Reformation era. By the time of [[the-reformation]], the wealth and worldly ambition of bishops had become a primary target of reformist criticism.

## Origins and Context

The episcopal office traces its origins to the early Church, but its temporal power expanded dramatically after the Roman Empire's collapse, when bishops often became the de facto governors of cities. By the late Middle Ages, many bishoprics carried the rights and revenues of feudal lordships. In the Holy Roman Empire, the prince-bishops (Furstbischofe) were sovereign rulers of territories that included cities like Mainz, Cologne, and Trier.

Voltaire emphasizes that bishops were deeply embedded in the systems of hereditary monarchy and imperial governance. In the Swedish crisis of the early sixteenth century, the Archbishop of Upsal functioned as "the second person in the kingdom" — and "whosoever is the second wants generally to be the first" (Ch. XCVIII).

## Key Features

The office of bishop in early modern Europe was characterized by several distinctive features:

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Diocesan Jurisdiction** | Spiritual authority over a defined territory with its parishes, monasteries, and clergy |
| **Temporal Lordship** | Ownership of land, collection of feudal dues, and civil jurisdiction over episcopal territories |
| **Political Office** | Seats in diets and parliaments; service as royal chancellors, ministers, and regents |
| **Military Command** | Raising and leading troops, as when five bishops fought in the Hungarian army at Mohacs |
| **Plural Benefices** | Holding multiple bishoprics simultaneously, accumulating their revenues |
| **Electoral Dignity** | In the Empire, three archbishops (Mainz, Trier, Cologne) were prince-electors |

> Voltaire reports that some prelates "possessed eight or nine bishopricks. We are astonished when we hear of the vast number of benefices enjoyed, for instance, by the cardinal of Lorraine, cardinal Woolsey, and so many others" (Ch. CVI).

## Historical Development

The crisis of episcopal authority in the sixteenth century unfolded on multiple fronts. In Sweden, Archbishop Troll of Upsal led an armed revolt against the Swedish estates after they deposed him, "assisted by the six Swedish bishops and the rest of the clergy," excommunicating the king and commencing a civil war that "lasted seven years: during the whole time there was nothing but a bloody anarchy" (Ch. XCVIII).

In Hungary, the bishop's traditional role as political leader proved catastrophic at the [[battle-of-mohacs]], where "a cordelier, named Tomori, was general of this army, in which there were five bishops" (Ch. XCVIII). The presence of bishops as military commanders reflected the degree to which the spiritual office had become indistinguishable from feudal lordship.

Yet the episcopal office also provided capable administrators. Voltaire notes that Charles V "entrusted the administration of Spain to his preceptor cardinal Adrian, whom he afterwards made pope" and that Cardinal Martinusius served as minister in Hungary — explaining that "those churchmen are frequently better instructed, and better qualified for conducting political affairs, than generals or courtiers" (Ch. XCVIII).

## Impact and Legacy

The Reformation transformed the episcopal office differently across Europe. In Lutheran territories, bishops were replaced by superintendents with purely spiritual functions. In Sweden, [[gustavus-vasa]] "artfully established the new religion by preserving the hierarchy, only retrenching the revenues and power of the bishops" (Ch. XCVIII). In England, the Henrician settlement retained bishops but subjected them to royal supremacy.

The Catholic Reformation, by contrast, reformed the episcopate from within. The Council of Trent mandated episcopal residence (bishops must actually live in their dioceses), prohibited plural benefices, and emphasized the bishop's pastoral role. The [[jesuits]] provided a new model of disciplined, educated clergy that stood in stark contrast to the worldly prelates of the early century.

## Key Figures

- **Archbishop Troll of Upsal** — Militant Swedish primate who died in battle "more gloriously than Christian, of the wounds he received in an engagement" (Ch. XCVIII)
- **Cardinal Wolsey** — English lord chancellor whose accumulation of benefices symbolized episcopal worldliness
- **Cardinal Ximenes** — Franciscan who governed Spain as regent "in a cordelier's habit, saying that with his cord he knew how to bind the grandees to their duty" (Ch. CVI)
- **Cardinal Granvelle** — Imperial minister and diplomat under [[charles-v]]
- **Cardinal Adrian of Utrecht** — Tutor to Charles V, later Pope Adrian VI
