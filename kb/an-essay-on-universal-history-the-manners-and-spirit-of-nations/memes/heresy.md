## Overview

Heresy, from the Greek _hairesis_ (choice or sect), designates the willful and persistent adherence to religious opinions condemned by the established Church. In the sixteenth century — the age of the Reformation — heresy became the central fault line of European society. The religious disputes that engaged Germany, the North, France, and England transformed what had been a theological category into a political and military one. As Voltaire observes, the very word "heretic" became a weapon wielded by each side against the other: Zwinglius was "considered as a holy martyr" by the Protestants and "as a detestable heretic" by the Catholics. (Ch. CVI)

## Origins and Context

The legal framework for prosecuting heresy developed gradually from the thirteenth century onward. Pope Alexander III established the Inquisition in France in 1255 under St. Lewis, entrusting it to the Dominicans and Cordeliers. These first inquisitors possessed the power to summon and excommunicate heretics, to grant indulgences to princes who exterminated the condemned, and to tax the sins of penitents. (Ch. CXVIII)

The irony of the Inquisition's origins is that its most strenuous defender was Frederick II — the same emperor repeatedly accused by the pope of being a Mahometan and an atheist. Frederick published four edicts at Pavia in 1244, commanding magistrates to burn obstinate heretics and imprison the repentant for life. The papacy later turned these very arms against the empire. (Ch. CXVIII)

## The Inquisition as Instrument

The Inquisition operated through a system of denunciation, secret trial, and public execution known as the auto da fé. In Spain, it reached its fullest expression under Ferdinand and Isabella, later becoming an instrument of state power under Philip II. Voltaire notes that Philip "solemnly vowed before a crucifix to destroy all Protestants; and he fulfilled his vow." Suspected heretics at Valladolid were burnt by a slow fire while Philip watched from his palace windows. (Ch. CXXXIV)

Yet the Inquisition was not uniformly powerful everywhere. In Venice, the Senate required three senators to assist the inquisitors, effectively neutralizing the tribunal. In Naples and Sicily, disputes between the pope and the king over who should nominate inquisitors meant that none were appointed at all. Voltaire notes that there were fewer heretics in Naples and Sicily than in other countries, demonstrating that the Inquisition "was not so much the bulwark of religion, as a scourge designed for the disturbance of the human species." (Ch. CXVIII)

## Heresy and the State

Heresy prosecutions in the sixteenth century were as much political as religious. In England, Parliament alternately condemned Catholics and Protestants depending on the monarch: "Under Henry VIII they persecuted the Protestants; they encouraged them under Edward VI; and burnt them under Mary." Bishop Burnet explained the peculiar cruelty of burning heretics by noting that since the Church believed the souls of heretics to be eternally burning in hell, they might imagine they were inflicting only a temporal anticipation of divine justice. (Ch. CXIV)

In France, the persecution of the Vaudois (Waldensians) in Provence demonstrated how heresy charges could escalate into massacre. The parliament of Provence sentenced nineteen inhabitants of Merindol to death; when the sentence was executed by military force under President Jean Meynier d'Oppede, twenty-two towns were reduced to ashes, and thousands of men, women, and children were slaughtered. Francis I, on his deathbed, recommended to his son Henry II that this barbarity be punished. (Ch. CXVI)

## Galileo and the Conflict with Science

The condemnation of Galileo Galilei by seven inquisitors in 1616 represents a defining moment in the history of heresy. The inquisitors declared the Copernican system "not only heretical in faith, but absurd in philosophy." Galileo was later condemned to do penance in prison and obliged to retract on his knees. Voltaire reflects: "There is a sort of fatality in human nature, that truth is to be persecuted when first it begins to make its appearance." This sentence against an opinion since "so variously confirmed" stands as a permanent testimony to the force of prejudice. (Ch. C)
