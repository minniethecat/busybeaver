## Overview

The house of Bourbon was a cadet branch of the Capetian dynasty that became central to the political history of sixteenth-century France. Voltaire's narrative features the Bourbons in two dramatically opposed roles: first, as the tragic figure of the Constable Charles of Bourbon, whose defection to Charles V brought France to the brink of ruin; and second, as Henry of Navarre, the Protestant Bourbon prince who survived the [[french-wars-of-religion]] to become Henry IV, founding a new royal dynasty that would rule France until the Revolution.

The Bourbon name appears throughout Voltaire's account as a constant presence in the political and military affairs of the age. John duke of Bourbonnois issued a chivalric challenge in 1414; Henry de Bourbon Montpensier, a prince of the blood, was killed at a tournament in 1560, an event that "intirely ceased" all tournaments thereafter (Ch. C); the Constable of Bourbon was the architect of the French victory at Marignano and then the instrument of France's deepest humiliation.

## The Constable of Bourbon

Charles of Bourbon, Constable of France, is one of the most compelling figures in Voltaire's narrative of the reign of Francis I. He was the hero of the [[battle-of-pavia|battle of Marignano]], where "all the world allowed that the honour of this victory was chiefly owing to the famous constable Charles of Bourbon" (Ch. CI). Yet he was driven from France by a personal and legal persecution orchestrated by Louise of Savoy, the king's mother, who had made overtures of marriage to the constable upon the death of his wife and, meeting with a repulse, "resolved to ruin him, since she could not have him for her husband" (Ch. CI).

The lawsuit that followed stripped Bourbon of his entire estate. The constable appealed to the king for a demurrer; Francis I "would not so much as see the bishop" sent as his envoy (Ch. CI). Bourbon then made the fateful decision to enter the service of [[charles-v|Charles V]], becoming generalissimo of the emperor's armies. His defection brought France to the edge of catastrophe. He had claims to Provence, and Charles V had promised him the ancient kingdom of Arles. Henry VIII supplied a hundred thousand crowns per month toward the war. Bourbon took Toulon and laid siege to Marseilles.

Voltaire frames Bourbon's choice as a moral crux: "It would have been great in him to have served with fidelity, and suffered with patience. But there is another kind of greatness, that of revenge: Charles of Bourbon unfortunately embraced the latter" (Ch. CI). He was killed in 1527 while scaling the walls of Rome -- the same sack that made [[charles-v|Clement VII]] a prisoner.

## Henry of Navarre and the Bourbon Succession

The other great Bourbon of Voltaire's narrative is Henry of Navarre, the future Henry IV. As the [[french-wars-of-religion]] intensified under the last Valois kings, the Bourbon prince emerged as the leader of the Protestant [[huguenot]] party. Pope Sixtus V issued a bull against him, calling the Bourbons "a spurious and execrable generation" and declaring that Henry and the prince of Conde had "forfeited all right of succession" (Ch. CXLII).

Yet Henry survived assassination attempts, religious persecution, and the [[french-wars-of-religion|St. Bartholomew's Day massacre]] to eventually convert to Catholicism ("Paris is worth a mass") and ascend the throne as Henry IV in 1589. The Bourbon dynasty he founded would rule France for over two centuries.

> What were the causes of all those misfortunes? Religion, ambition, the want of good laws, and mal-administration. (Ch. CXLI)

## Key Figures

- **Charles of Bourbon** -- Constable of France, victor of Marignano, who defected to Charles V after being despoiled by Francis I's mother
- **Henry of Navarre (Henry IV)** -- Bourbon prince who became king of France, ending the Wars of Religion
- **Louise of Savoy** -- Mother of Francis I, whose personal vendetta against the Constable triggered his defection
- **John duke of Bourbonnois** -- 15th-century Bourbon who issued a chivalric declaration to fight in England
- **Henry de Bourbon Montpensier** -- Prince of the blood killed in a tournament, ending the tournament tradition
