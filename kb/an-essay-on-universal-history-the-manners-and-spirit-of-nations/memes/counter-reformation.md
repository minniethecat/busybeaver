## Overview

The Counter-Reformation, also known as the Catholic Reformation, was the period of Catholic revival and institutional reform that began as a response to the [[the-reformation|Protestant Reformation]] in the 16th century. It encompassed doctrinal clarification at the [[council-of-trent|Council of Trent]], the founding of new religious orders such as the [[jesuits]], and a broad effort to correct clerical abuses while reaffirming the authority of the [[papacy]].

Voltaire observes that the competition between Catholicism and Protestantism had a paradoxical benefit: "It must be in general allowed that the ecclesiastics of the church of Rome have been reformed by the protestants, just as two rivals become more circumspect by their jealousy of each other." (Ch. CVII)

## Origins and Context

By the early 16th century, widespread clerical corruption had created an urgent need for reform within the Catholic Church. The practices of pluralism, [[simony]], and absenteeism among the [[clergy]] had eroded the Church's moral authority. The [[the-reformation|Reformation]] led by [[luther-martin|Martin Luther]] and [[calvin-john|John Calvin]] had fractured Western Christendom, and Catholic leaders recognized that the Church could no longer defer addressing systemic abuses. (Ch. CVI)

## Historical Development

The Council of [[council-of-trent|Trent]], convened in three sessions between 1545 and 1563, formed the doctrinal core of the Counter-Reformation. The council addressed the theological challenges raised by [[protestantism]] while also enacting practical reforms. Its decrees were promulgated through [[papal-bull|papal bulls]], giving them binding force throughout the Catholic world.

The Society of Jesus, founded by Ignatius of Loyola in 1540, became the intellectual vanguard of the Counter-Reformation. The [[jesuits]] established schools and universities across Catholic Europe, served as confessors to princes, and conducted missionary work in Asia and the Americas. The order's special vow of loyalty to the Pope made it a uniquely effective instrument of papal policy during this period. (Ch. CVI)

The Capuchin reform, begun by Matteo da Bascio in 1525, represented a return to the primitive [[franciscan|Franciscan]] ideal. Despite early setbacks — the founder briefly joined the Protestants — the Capuchins were recognized as an independent order in 1528 and became one of the most dynamic forces in the Catholic Counter-Reformation.

## Impact and Legacy

The Counter-Reformation transformed Catholic religious life across Europe. The confessional remained a central feature of Catholic piety, its discipline reinforced by Tridentine decrees. In Protestant territories, private [[confession]] largely disappeared or was transformed into a general confession recited by the congregation during worship.

The competition between Catholic and Protestant Europe shaped the political landscape of the continent for the next two centuries. The Counter-Reformation's emphasis on education produced a network of Catholic colleges that trained generations of clergy and laity, while its missionary impulse extended the reach of [[catholicism]] into the Americas, Africa, and Asia. The bull *Regimini militantis Ecclesiae* (1540) approving the Society of Jesus created the order that would become the vanguard of Catholic reform and Counter-Reformation polemics.

- **Charles Borromeo** — Archbishop of Milan, model of Tridentine episcopal reform
- **Teresa of Ávila** — Spanish mystic and reformer of the Carmelite order
