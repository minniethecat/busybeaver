## Overview

The estates (*états*, *Stände*, *stany*) were the hierarchically ordered social classes of early modern Europe, conventionally divided into three orders: the clergy (first estate), the nobility (second estate), and the commoners (third estate). In their representative form—as Estates-General, parliaments, diets, and cortes—these bodies constituted the primary institutional check on royal absolutism, wielding powers of taxation, legislation, and in some cases royal election.

Voltaire's *Essay on Universal History* provides a comparative survey of the estates system across Europe, demonstrating how the varying strength of representative institutions shaped national destinies. His central argument is that the estates, despite their structural inequities, served as a barrier against [[tyranny]]: where they remained powerful, liberty survived; where they were suppressed, despotism followed.

## The Functioning of Estates

The estates operated according to widely varying constitutional rules across Europe. In Poland, the diet (*Sejm*) was composed of the king, the senate (bishops and high officials), and the chamber of deputies (representing the *szlachta* or nobility). The Polish diet's unanimity requirement—the *liberum veto*—meant that "the power of the Polish noble being extended to every gentleman, and carried so far as to annul by a single vote the collective suffrages of the republic, is become the privilege of anarchy." (Ch. XCVIII)

In Sweden, "the four estates of the kingdom" elected kings and could depose tyrants. When Gustavus Vasa delivered Sweden from [[christian-ii-of-denmark|Christian II]], it was the estates who "decreed to give him the crown." The same estates later ordered inquiries into ecclesiastical land usurpation and authorized the reformation of religion. (Ch. XCVIII, CVIII)

In Castile, "Isabella respected the Cortes, which are the states of the kingdom." In Aragon, Ferdinand the Catholic "was not able to destroy the authority of the justiciary, who thought he had a right of trying crowned heads." The Spanish monarchy, despite its later reputation for absolutism, remained constitutionally constrained through the sixteenth century. (Ch. XCVIII)

## The Estates and Royal Power

The relationship between the estates and royal authority defined the constitutional character of each European monarchy. Voltaire identifies a spectrum:

**Strong estates, weak crown**: Poland and Hungary represented the extreme case, where the estates' power effectively paralyzed central government. The Hungarian nobility "were petty tyrants, who would not be tyrannized themselves." This produced a "fatal independency" that left both kingdoms unable to resist external threats. (Ch. XCVIII)

**Balanced constitution**: England after the Glorious Revolution represented the model Voltaire most admired, though his treatment in the *Essay* focuses on the Tudor period when the balance was still contested. Henry VIII used parliament to effect the break with Rome, demonstrating that "the parliament, ever subservient to the monarch's passions," could also be the instrument of royal absolutism. (Ch. CXIII)

**Absorbed estates**: France under Louis XI and his successors progressively neutralized the Estates-General. "France alone, since the reign of Lewis XI, was become an absolute monarchy." The French kings' ability to tax without consent—"this unhappy facility of exhausting the subject"—gave them military advantages but "was productive of several calamities to France." (Ch. XCVIII)

## Ecclesiastical Estates

The temporal possessions of the church constituted a distinct category in Voltaire's analysis. The clergy "were jealous of their temporal estates, as well as of their authority," and these material interests frequently determined ecclesiastical politics more than doctrinal considerations. (Ch. CXXXV)

The dissolution of the monasteries under [[henry-viii|Henry VIII]] represented the largest transfer of ecclesiastical estates to the Crown in European history. According to Burnet's calculation—which Voltaire notes "perhaps is exaggerated"—the annual revenue of the English monasteries amounted to £1,600,000 sterling. Henry "seized upon the shrine which was immensely rich with precious stones" of St. Thomas of Canterbury, and "the king took their estates into his own hands." Out of the spoils he "founded six new bishoprics, and a college, rewarded a few servants, and converted the remainder to his own use." (Ch. CXIII)

In Sweden, the estates' inquiry into "the estates, which the church had usurped in the times of confusion" precipitated civil war and ultimately the reformation of the Swedish church. The archbishop of Uppsala, armed with the pope's bull, excommunicated the king and senate, then "laying their vestments on the altar, and putting on a cuirass and a sword, they went out of the church and commenced a civil war." (Ch. XCVIII)

## Confiscation as a Political Instrument

The confiscation of estates was a recurring instrument of political coercion across early modern Europe. The Ottoman sultan claimed the personal estates of deceased officials "by ancient usurpation, which was but too long the practice of Christian powers." (Ch. CXXVIII)

In Spain, the Inquisition's power was substantially enhanced by its right to confiscate the estates of condemned heretics. When John of Braganza liberated Portugal from Spain, he "could do no more than deprive the inquisitors of the confiscated estates." (Ch. CXVIII)

The French League's demand for "the confiscation of the estates of the Huguenots to the advantage of the chiefs of the league" demonstrated how religious conflict and material expropriation reinforced each other. (Ch. CXLII)

## Comparative Perspective

Voltaire extends the concept of estates beyond Christian Europe. In Japan, the distinction between clergy and laity paralleled the European estates: "The clergy are in every respect distinct from the laity; and these two orders of men have a thorough contempt for each other." (Ch. CXX)

In the Ottoman Empire, the absence of hereditary estates—the sultan being "absolute over the subjects belonging to his own domain"—appeared to Voltaire as both a source of military strength and a form of institutionalized insecurity that prevented the development of stable property rights. (Ch. CXXVIII)

## Impact and Legacy

The estates system, for all its inequality, represented a genuine limitation on arbitrary power. Voltaire's comparative analysis suggests that the health of the estates was a reliable predictor of national liberty:

> "The public administration in all parts of the globe hath frequently been little better than plundering by authority; except in a few republican states, where the liberty and property of the subject are more sacred, and where the smallness of revenue is the cause of its being better administered." (Ch. CXXVIII)

This principle—that limited government produces better governance—is one of the most consistent themes of the *Essay*.
