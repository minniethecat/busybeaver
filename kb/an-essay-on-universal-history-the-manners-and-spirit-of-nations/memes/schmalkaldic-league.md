## Overview

The Schmalkaldic League was the military and political instrument through which the Protestant Reformation defended itself against imperial suppression. Formed in 1531 at the town of Schmalkalden in Thuringia, the league united Lutheran princes and free cities in a common front against Emperor [[charles-v|Charles V]], who sought to restore Catholic uniformity within the Holy Roman Empire. Voltaire treats the league not as a theological movement but as a struggle for political liberty: "It was rather the cause of liberty than that of Lutheranism." (Ch. CV)

## Formation and Organization

The league was formed in response to the Diet of Augsburg (1530), at which Charles V rejected the Lutheran Augsburg Confession and demanded that the Protestant estates return to Catholic obedience by April 1531. The Protestant princes — led by John Frederick I, Elector of Saxony, and Philip I, Landgrave of Hesse — concluded that only collective military deterrence could protect their religious settlement.

| Leader | Territory | Role |
|--------|-----------|------|
| John Frederick I | Electoral Saxony | Co-leader; the league's political anchor |
| Philip of Hesse | Landgraviate of Hesse | Co-leader; the league's most energetic military organizer |
| Free imperial cities | Strasbourg, Ulm, etc. | Financial contributors; urban Reformation strongholds |

The league's constitution provided for a common treasury, a unified military command, and a rotating presidency. At its peak, it encompassed most of northern and central Germany, representing a substantial challenge to imperial authority. The league's existence fundamentally constrained Charles V's freedom of action — whenever he sought to concentrate force against France or the Ottomans, the Protestant princes could exploit his distraction.

## The Political Logic of Protestant Arms

Voltaire's analysis emphasizes that confessional identity was inseparable from constitutional politics. The German princes who adopted Lutheranism did so partly from conviction and partly because the Reformation provided ideological cover for resisting Habsburg centralization:

> The princes of this persuasion were already in league against the authority of Charles V. as well as against Rome; but there had been no blood as yet spilt in the empire on the account of Luther. (Ch. CX)

The league's diplomatic value extended beyond Germany. Francis I of France — a Catholic king who burned Protestants at home — formed an alliance with the league to weaken his Habsburg rival. Voltaire notes the irony: "the Lutheran religion preserved France, and did her more service than the Turks, on whom the king had so greatly relied." (Ch. CIV) When Charles V threatened Paris in 1544, it was the league's mobilization in Germany that forced him to negotiate.

## The Schmalkaldic War and the Battle of Mühlberg

The league's military fortunes collapsed in the Schmalkaldic War (1546–1547). Charles V, having secured a truce with France and the Ottomans, turned his full attention to Germany:

> Nothing could be more complete than the victory of Mulberg. The elector of Saxony, and the landgrave of Hesse, were both taken prisoners; the Lutheran party was in the utmost consternation; and prodigious contributions were imposed on the vanquished provinces: all this together seemed to render him despotic in Germany. (Ch. CV)

Yet Voltaire immediately notes the reversal: "But the same thing happened to him now as after he had taken Francis I. prisoner; he lost the whole fruit of his victory." (Ch. CV) Maurice of Saxony, whom Charles had rewarded with the electoral dignity, switched sides and led a renewed Protestant coalition. The result was the Peace of Passau (1552) and ultimately the Peace of Augsburg (1555), which established the principle of *cuius regio, eius religio* — each prince could determine his territory's religion.

## Legacy

The Schmalkaldic League's most enduring achievement was negative: it prevented Charles V from crushing the Reformation by force, thereby ensuring that religious pluralism would be a permanent feature of German political life. The league's failure to achieve a lasting military victory demonstrated the limits of confessional alliance, but its political logic — defensive coalition against a hegemonic center — prefigured the confessional alliances of the Thirty Years' War and the [[peace-of-westphalia|Westphalian]] settlement that ended it.
