## Overview

The friar occupies a uniquely consequential position in Voltaire's Essay on Universal History: it was an Augustinian friar, Martin Luther, whose protest against the sale of [[indulgences]] launched the [[the-reformation|Reformation]] that shattered the unity of Western Christendom. Voltaire notes that "the Augustinian friar Martin Luther was commissioned to preach against the indulgence trade when the Dominican order obtained the franchise that had formerly belonged to the Augustinians" (Ch. CIX), and that this seemingly minor rivalry between mendicant orders "proved the source of fury, discord, and all manner of misfortunes that have afflicted most parts of Europe upwards of two hundred years."

The friar represented a distinctive form of religious life: unlike the cloistered [[monasticism|monk]], the friar moved through the world, preaching, teaching, and begging. This mobility gave the mendicant orders — [[franciscan|Franciscans]], [[dominicans|Dominicans]], Augustinians, and later [[jesuits|Jesuits]] — an influence that far exceeded their numbers, making them simultaneously the most effective agents of papal authority and, in Luther's case, its most devastating critic.

## Origins and Context

The mendicant orders emerged in the thirteenth century as a response to the growing urban population of medieval Europe. The Franciscan order was founded by Francis of Assisi (1181/82–1226), the son of a wealthy cloth merchant who renounced his inheritance to embrace "Lady Poverty." The Dominican Order (Order of Preachers) was founded by Saint Dominic in 1216, known as the "Black Friars" for their distinctive black mantle worn over a white habit. Both orders were fundamentally different from older monastic forms: they were urban, mobile, and oriented toward preaching rather than liturgical prayer.

By the sixteenth century, the rivalry between mendicant orders had become a significant force in its own right. The indulgence trade was franchised by the papacy to specific orders — a lucrative arrangement that pitted Dominicans against Augustinians, Franciscans against both. When Leo X authorized the sale of indulgences to fund the rebuilding of St. Peter's Basilica, the franchise went to the Dominicans. "The Augustinian friar Martin Luther was commissioned by his superiors to preach against the indulgence trade" (Ch. CIX) — a reminder that Luther's initial protest was less a theological revolution than an inter-order dispute that spiraled far beyond anyone's expectations.

## Historical Development

The friar's role in Voltaire's narrative centers on the Reformation crisis. Martin Luther, an Augustinian friar and professor of Scripture at Wittenberg, posted his Ninety-Five Theses in 1517 — an act that, in Voltaire's telling, might have remained a minor academic dispute had it not intersected with the political ambitions of German [[prince|princes]] eager to curb imperial and papal authority.

The Franciscan and Dominican orders responded to the Protestant challenge in markedly different ways. The Dominicans, long the guardians of orthodoxy and the staff of the Inquisition, became the intellectual shock troops of the Counter-Reformation. The Franciscans, with their more popular and emotional spirituality, adapted less easily to the new doctrinal rigor of the Council of Trent but remained immensely influential among the common people.

The internal stratification of the clerical estate meant that "the lower clergy (parish priests, mendicant friars) often shared the poverty of the common people," while bishops and abbots lived as feudal magnates. This class division within the church made the friar a potentially subversive figure — close enough to the people to channel their grievances, educated enough to articulate them in theological terms.

## Impact and Legacy

The Lutheran movement began within the Augustinian order, and the friar's role in launching the Reformation is Voltaire's central illustration of how institutional rivalries within the church could produce consequences no one intended. "This seemingly minor dispute over a farm proved the source of fury, discord, and all manner of misfortunes" — the "farm" being the indulgence trade franchised to the Dominicans rather than the Augustinians.

After Trent, the character of the mendicant orders changed. The [[jesuits|Jesuit]] order, though not formally mendicant, adopted the friar's mobility and preaching mission while adding a new element: systematic education and political influence. The older orders adapted or declined. Yet the figure of the friar — mobile, intellectual, connected to both elite and popular culture — remained central to Catholic life even as the church absorbed the lessons of the Reformation.
