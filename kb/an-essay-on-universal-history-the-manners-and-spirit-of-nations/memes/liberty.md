## Overview

Liberty in Voltaire's sixteenth-century Europe is a paradoxical concept. In Poland and Hungary, "liberty" meant the privileges of the noble estate — including immunity from prosecution and power of life and death over peasants — rather than any universal right. Voltaire is unsparing in his analysis: the Hungarian nobility's "liberty was a fatal independency" (Ch. XCVIII) that reduced their country to helplessness before the Ottoman Empire. Yet he also admires the constitutional arrangements that preserved freedom in Germany and the equilibrium of power that prevented universal monarchy under [[charles-v]].

## Origins and Context

The concept of liberty in early modern Europe was shaped by three distinct traditions: the classical republican liberty of Greece and Rome, the Germanic tradition of tribal freedom and elective kingship, and the Christian idea of spiritual liberty. By the sixteenth century, "liberty" in most European contexts referred primarily to corporate privileges — the rights of estates, cities, and ecclesiastical bodies against royal power — rather than to individual rights.

Voltaire is acutely aware of the gap between the language of liberty and the reality of oppression. In Poland, noble liberty coexisted with peasant servitude; in Hungary, "the Hungarian nobility were petty tyrants, who would not be tyrannized themselves" (Ch. XCVIII).

## Key Features

Voltaire's comparative analysis reveals several distinct patterns of liberty:

| Country | Form of Liberty | Beneficiaries | Cost |
|---------|----------------|---------------|------|
| **Poland** | Elective monarchy, liberum veto | All nobles (szlachta) | State paralysis, peasant servitude |
| **Hungary** | Elective kingship, noble immunities | Magnates and gentry | Military weakness, Ottoman conquest |
| **Germany** | Imperial constitution, territorial liberties | Princes, free cities, imperial knights | Imperial fragmentation |
| **Sweden** | Peasant representation in diet | All estates including peasants | Gradual erosion under strong kings |
| **France** | Minimal — absolutism since Louis XI | King and royal administration | Fewest institutional checks |

> Voltaire's sharpest verdict falls on Polish liberty: "This power being extended to every gentleman, and carried so far as to annul by a single vote the collective suffrages of the republic, is become the privilege of anarchy" (Ch. XCVIII).

## Historical Development

The tension between liberty and authority structures Voltaire's narrative of the Reformation era. The German imperial constitution, with its "supreme court of judicature, the Imperial chamber" paid by the princes and cities rather than the emperor, was "a support of the public liberty" (Ch. XCIX). Even the chamber's inability to enforce its decrees against great princes "shewed its existence" — the very "abuse of liberty" demonstrated that liberty was real.

Charles V's reign represented the great test of whether liberty could survive concentrated power. Voltaire argues that the "system of an equilibrium of power was at this very period established in Europe" (Ch. CIII) — a balance-of-power mechanism that prevented any single ruler from extinguishing the liberties of other states.

The Reformation added a religious dimension to political liberty. In Sweden, Gustavus Vasa combined religious reform with the consolidation of royal authority, while in Germany, Lutheran princes asserted their liberty against both emperor and pope. Zwinglius "seemed more zealous for liberty than religion" (Ch. CVII), connecting the Reformed faith to republican political principles.

## Impact and Legacy

Voltaire's treatment of liberty reveals a fundamental ambivalence. He recognizes that noble liberty in Eastern Europe was built on peasant oppression, yet he also sees constitutional checks on royal power as essential to good government. His ideal seems to be the German model: liberty secured by institutions rather than by individual veto, balanced between imperial authority and princely independence.

The English experience, barely touched on in Voltaire's sixteenth-century chapters, would later provide the model of liberty under law that Enlightenment thinkers celebrated. But Voltaire's own analysis here focuses on the continental pattern: liberty as privilege, and the slow, violent process by which privilege was either transformed into right or crushed by absolutism.

## Key Figures

- **Charles V** — Emperor whose power tested the limits of European liberty
- **Sigismund of Poland** — King under whom Polish noble liberty reached its classical form
- **Gustavus Vasa** — Swedish liberator who established a new balance of liberty and authority
- **Zwingli** — Reformer who linked religious reform to republican liberty
- **Albert of Austria** — First Habsburg king of Hungary, whose short reign triggered a crisis of elective liberty
