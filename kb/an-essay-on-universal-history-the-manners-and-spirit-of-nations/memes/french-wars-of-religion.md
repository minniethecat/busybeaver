## Overview

The **French Wars of Religion** were a series of eight civil wars that tore France apart between 1562 and 1598, pitting the Catholic majority against the Protestant [[huguenot|Huguenot]] minority. Voltaire treats these conflicts not as isolated events but as the inevitable consequence of the collision between the [[the-reformation|Reformation]]'s spread and the political ambitions of the great noble houses — the Catholic [[guise|House of Guise]] and the Protestant House of Bourbon. The wars produced some of the most shocking episodes of religious violence in European history, including the St. Bartholomew's Day massacre, and ended only with the conversion of Henry of Navarre to Catholicism. (Ch. CXVI, CXLII)

## Origins of the Conflict

The religious division in France took root under Francis I and hardened under Henry II. The chancellor Duprat concluded the Concordat of Bologna with Pope Leo X, which gave the king the nomination of benefices while the pope received the first fruits — an arrangement that tied the French church more closely to the crown but did nothing to suppress the spread of Protestant ideas. (Ch. CXVI)

By the death of Henry II in 1559, the [[calvinism|Calvinist]] movement had won substantial numbers of converts among the nobility, townspeople, and even segments of the clergy. The young king Francis II fell under the influence of the Guise family, whose militant Catholicism set them on a collision course with the Protestant Bourbons, led by the prince of Conde and the admiral de Coligni. (Ch. CXLII)

Voltaire notes the critical moment when the conflict transformed from a religious dispute into a political war: "Those very Huguenots, who, under Francis I and Henry II, understood nothing but praying and suffering, what they called martyrdom, were grown most furious enthusiasts." (Ch. CXLII)

## Key Phases and Atrocities

| Phase | Period | Key Events |
|-------|--------|------------|
| First War | 1562–1563 | Massacre of Vassy; assassination of Francis, Duke of Guise |
| Second–Third Wars | 1567–1570 | Battles of St. Denis and Jarnac; death of Conde |
| St. Bartholomew's Day | August 24, 1572 | Massacre of thousands of Huguenots in Paris and the provinces |
| War of the Three Henrys | 1585–1589 | Henry III, Henry of Guise, and Henry of Navarre |
| Henry IV's Victory | 1589–1598 | Conversion of Henry IV; Edict of Nantes |

The St. Bartholomew's Day massacre stands as the darkest chapter. Voltaire's account is unsparing: the court gave orders for the same massacres throughout all the provinces, but several governors refused to obey — St. Herem in Auvergne, la Guiche at Macon, and viscount d'Orte at Bayonne wrote to Charles IX that "they were ready to die for his service, but they would assassinate no man." (Ch. CXLII)

> The parliament of Paris decreed an annual procession on St. Bartholomew's day, in thanksgiving to God. The chancellor de l'Hopital was quite of a different opinion, when he wrote, excidat ille dies. The procession did not take place; they were shocked at length at the idea of consecrating the memory of what ought to have been consigned to eternal oblivion. (Ch. CXLII)

## The Role of Chancellor de l'Hopital

Amid the fanaticism, one figure stands out in Voltaire's narrative: the chancellor Michel de l'Hopital — "that great legislator at a time when laws were wanting, that resolute philosopher at a period of madness and enthusiasm." When the prince of Conde was condemned to death, de l'Hopital refused to sign the sentence. He was among "the wisest heads" who believed the Protestants could be kept within bounds through moderation rather than war. His reforms separating the professions of sword and robe reshaped the French judiciary. (Ch. CXLII)

## The Resolution

The wars ended only when [[henry-iv|Henry of Navarre]], the Protestant leader and legitimate heir, converted to Catholicism — reportedly quipping that "Paris is worth a mass." The Edict of Nantes (1598) granted the Huguenots limited toleration, establishing a fragile peace. Voltaire's treatment underscores the irony: France exhausted itself in religious slaughter, only to arrive at a settlement that wise statesmanship could have achieved decades earlier without the bloodshed. (Ch. CXLII)
