## Overview

The nobility constituted the Second Estate in the tripartite social order of early modern Europe, but in many regions they were the true ruling class. Voltaire's comparative survey of European societies reveals striking variations in noble privilege: from the powerful palatines of Poland and Hungary, to the relatively restrained nobility of France under [[charles-v]], to the simplicity of the northern kingdoms where titles of count and marquis had "not yet reached the Swedes, no more than the Danes" (Ch. XCVIII).

## Origins and Context

The nobility originated in the warrior class of the early Middle Ages — the mounted knights who provided military service in exchange for land tenure (the fief). Over centuries, this service nobility transformed into a hereditary caste defined by birth, titles, and legal privilege. By the sixteenth century, the nobility had lost its monopoly on warfare (thanks to gunpowder and professional armies) but retained its social preeminence and political power.

Voltaire's account pays particular attention to the nobility of Eastern Europe, where the absence of strong royal authority permitted the development of what he describes as extreme noble privilege bordering on anarchy.

## Key Features

The noble condition in early modern Europe varied dramatically by region:

| Region | Characteristic | Political Power |
|--------|---------------|-----------------|
| **Poland** | All nobles equal; no titles except "nobleman" | Each noble had veto power (liberum veto) over diet |
| **Hungary** | "Petty tyrants" who elected their kings | Limited royal authority at every election |
| **Sweden/Denmark** | Simple manners; no titles of count/marquis | Kings ruled with diet of nobles |
| **France** | Hierarchical titles; court nobility vs. provincial | Weakened by Francis I after feudal era |
| **Holy Roman Empire** | Princes, counts, knights; imperial immediacy | Sovereign within their territories |

> Voltaire describes Polish noble privilege in stark terms: "The nobles had also another privilege, in common with the palatines, that of not being confined for any crime, till they were juridically convicted. This indeed was the privilege of impunity. They had also the power of life and death over their peasants" (Ch. XCVIII).

## Historical Development

The evolution of noble power in the early modern period followed divergent paths. In Poland, the nobility's grip on the state deepened: "The palatines after depriving the common people of their liberty, were employed in defending their own against the encroachments of the regal power" (Ch. XCVIII). Polish kings "have no fortresses, nor the disposing of the public monies, nor the command of the armies" — a situation that preserved noble "liberty" at the cost of state effectiveness.

In Hungary, the nobility's independence proved fatal. Voltaire notes that "the Hungarian nobility were petty tyrants, who would not be tyrannized themselves. Their liberty was a fatal independency, and they had reduced the rest of the nation to so miserable a servitude, that the peasants rose up in a body against their masters" (Ch. XCVIII). This civil war lasted four years and contributed directly to Hungary's weakness against the Ottoman Empire — when Sultan Suleiman invaded, "the whole kingdom of Hungary could not in that urgent necessity furnish him with an army of thirty thousand fighting men" (Ch. XCVIII).

In the West, the nobility adapted. French nobles retained their prestige but lost their independence as Francis I and his successors built a centralized monarchy. The tournament culture described in Ch. C — where "above a hundred knights had been killed at these sports" — represented a nobility transitioning from military function to courtly display.

## Impact and Legacy

Voltaire's analysis reveals a paradox: the strongest noble privileges correlated with the weakest states. Poland's noble republic, with its liberum veto, became "the privilege of anarchy" so "flattering" that "no one ever would propose the abrogation of this custom" (Ch. XCVIII). Hungary's noble liberties left it defenseless against Ottoman conquest.

In the north, Gustavus Vasa "was the first who made foreign nations sensible of the weight which Sweden might have in the affairs of Europe" (Ch. XCVIII) — implying that effective monarchy, not noble privilege, was the foundation of national power. The northern nobility's very simplicity — lacking even titles — made them less dangerous to royal authority than their titled counterparts elsewhere.

## Key Figures

- **Charles Canutson** — Swedish nobleman elected king in 1448 during the revolt against Danish-episcopal rule
- **John of Salstad** — Archbishop of Upsal who led the nobility and clergy in armed revolt
- **Sigismund of the Jagellon race** — Polish king who died in 1548, contemporary of [[charles-v]]
- **John duke of Bourbonnois** — French noble who crossed to England with sixteen knights seeking duels "in order to avoid idleness, and to merit the good graces of the fair lady whose humble servant he was" (Ch. C)
