## Overview

Excommunication — the formal exclusion of an individual or community from the sacraments and the communion of the Church — was the most powerful spiritual weapon in the papal arsenal. But by the sixteenth century, as Voltaire repeatedly demonstrates, its effectiveness depended entirely on the political force backing it. When the Swedish estates were "excommunicated by the pope according to the usual stile," Voltaire notes dryly that "this excommunication, which of itself signified nothing, was rendered very considerable by Christian's arms" (Ch. XCVIII). The gap between the spiritual claim and the political reality of excommunication is a recurring theme in his history.

## Origins and Context

Excommunication has biblical roots (Matthew 18:17, 1 Corinthians 5:5) and was a standard feature of church discipline from the earliest Christian centuries. By the High Middle Ages, popes had elevated excommunication into an instrument of papal monarchy: emperors, kings, and entire cities could be placed under interdict, releasing subjects from their oaths of allegiance and forbidding the administration of sacraments.

The medieval papacy's most famous excommunications — of Emperor Henry IV at Canossa (1077), of King John of England (1209), of Emperor Frederick II (1245) — demonstrated both the power and the limits of the sanction. By the sixteenth century, repeated use had dulled its edge, and the rise of national monarchies made secular rulers less vulnerable to spiritual penalties.

## Key Features

Excommunication in Voltaire's account functioned at several levels:

| Level | Target | Effect | Example |
|-------|--------|--------|---------|
| **Papal** | Kings, states, heretics | Release of subjects from allegiance | Swedish estates, 1517 |
| **Episcopal** | Political opponents within diocese | Exclusion from sacraments in local church | Archbishop Troll vs. Charles Canutson |
| **Consistorial** | Members of Reformed congregation | Exclusion from Lord's Supper | Calvin's Geneva |
| **Ritual** | Annual promulgation (*In Coena Domini*) | Symbolic condemnation of all dissenters | Pius V's bull |

> Voltaire describes the Swedish case in detail: "the archbishop of Upsal, assisted by the six Swedish bishops and the rest of the clergy, excommunicated the king and the senate at high mass; after which they laid their vestments on the altar, and putting on a cuirass and a sword, they went out of the church and commenced a civil war" (Ch. XCVIII).

## Historical Development

The Swedish crisis of the early sixteenth century provides Voltaire with his most vivid case study of excommunication as political weapon. Archbishop Troll of Upsal, deposed by the Swedish estates in 1517, secured papal excommunication of the entire kingdom. The penalty "of itself signified nothing" — but Christian II of Denmark used it as justification for military intervention, culminating in the Stockholm Bloodbath (1520). The result was not submission but revolution: [[gustavus-vasa]] led a national uprising that expelled the Danes and broke Sweden's ties with Rome.

Luther's excommunication in 1521 (*Decet Romanum Pontificem*) was intended to silence a troublesome friar. Instead, it became a defining moment of the Reformation. Luther publicly burned the papal bull at Wittenberg, demonstrating that excommunication had lost its terror when the target no longer recognized the authority of the excommunicator. Voltaire reports that "the Dominicans supported by the pope's nuncios in Germany, caused his books to be burnt. The pope thundered out a new bull against him. Luther ordered the pope's bull and the decretals to be burnt in the public market place at Wittemberg" (Ch. CVII).

In Calvin's Geneva, excommunication took on a new form. Calvin "instituted a consistorial jurisdiction, with the right of excommunication" (Ch. CXII) — transferring the power from bishop to consistory, from hierarchical decree to communal discipline. The Reformed model of excommunication was arguably more effective than the papal one, precisely because it operated within a community that accepted its legitimacy.

## Impact and Legacy

Voltaire's treatment of excommunication is characteristically ironic. He notes that Henry VIII received the title "Defender of the Faith" from Leo X for writing against Luther — and then "a few years after proved the most inveterate enemy of Rome" (Ch. CVII). The pope who bestowed the title could not have imagined that the same king would later be excommunicated by a later pope. The spiritual weapon that had once brought emperors to their knees had become, in Voltaire's telling, a rhetorical gesture — powerful only when backed by armies.

The Peace of Westphalia (1648) effectively ended the political use of papal excommunication against sovereign states. The principle *cuius regio, eius religio* meant that rulers, not popes, determined the religion of their territories — and no external spiritual authority could release subjects from their allegiance.

## Key Figures

- **Archbishop Troll of Upsal** — Swedish primate who excommunicated the king and led armed revolt
- **Pope Leo X** — Excommunicated Luther in 1521, triggering the definitive break
- **Martin Luther** — Burned the papal bull of excommunication publicly at Wittenberg
- **John Calvin** — Established consistorial excommunication in Geneva
- **Pope Pius V** — Promulgated the annual bull *In Coena Domini* excommunicating all dissenters
