## Overview

The council was a fundamental institution of governance in early modern Europe, operating at every level from the ecumenical councils that defined Christian doctrine to the royal councils that advised kings, from the imperial diets that bound together the Holy Roman Empire to the consistories that governed Reformed churches. Voltaire's *Essay* traces the council as a mechanism for collective decision-making in an age when monarchical power was increasingly claiming absolute authority.

## Origins and Context

The conciliar tradition had ancient roots in both the Church (the Council of Nicaea, 325) and the Germanic assemblies that elected kings and decided on war and peace. The great Councils of Constance (1414–1418) and Basel (1431–1449) had asserted the superiority of councils over popes — a principle known as conciliarism — before the papacy reasserted its primacy in the fifteenth century.

In the Holy Roman Empire, the imperial diet (Reichstag) and the imperial chamber (Reichskammergericht) formed a conciliar constitution that balanced the emperor's authority. Voltaire notes that the imperial chamber, "paid by the princes and by the cities, and did not reside in the emperor's private demesnes, this was also a support of the public liberty" (Ch. XCIX).

## Key Features

The council as an institution took several distinct forms:

| Type | Function | Membership | Authority |
|------|----------|-----------|-----------|
| **Ecumenical Council** | Define doctrine, reform church | Bishops, theologians, princes | Binding on all Christians (in Catholic theory) |
| **Imperial Diet** | Imperial legislation, taxation, war | Electors, princes, free cities | Binding on empire; emperor presides |
| **Aulic Council** | Imperial judicial and advisory body | Emperor's appointees | Dependent on emperor alone |
| **Royal Council** | Advise monarch, execute policy | Nobles, clergy, lawyers appointed by king | Advisory to king |
| **Consistory** | Church discipline (Reformed) | Pastors and lay elders | Binding on congregation members |

> Voltaire compares the Aulic council, which "took its present form in 1512, and was dependent only on the emperors," with the Imperial chamber that represented the estates — noting that the former "soon became the principal support of their authority" while the latter "maintained its freedom" (Ch. XCIX).

## Historical Development

The Council of Constance (1414–1418) represented the high-water mark of conciliarism, ending the Great Western Schism by deposing three rival popes and electing Martin V. But the papacy soon reasserted its authority. By the time of [[the-reformation]], the popes resisted calls for a general council, fearing a resurgence of conciliarist challenges to papal supremacy.

The Fifth Lateran Council (1512–1517) was the last council before the Reformation. It addressed some reform issues but failed to grapple with the abuses that would trigger Luther's revolt. When Luther appealed from the pope to a general council, he revived the conciliarist tradition — but the papacy's long resistance to councils meant that by the time the Council of Trent finally convened (1545), half of Europe had already broken with Rome.

In the imperial sphere, the tension between the Aulic council (emperor's instrument) and the Imperial chamber (estates' institution) paralleled the broader struggle between imperial authority and princely liberty. Charles V's "council consisting of men of greater judgment than that of Francis I, acted with such policy as to prevail on Henry VIII to declare against France" (Ch. CII) — councils were also instruments of diplomacy and war.

## Impact and Legacy

The council as an institution was transformed by the Reformation. Protestant churches replaced episcopal hierarchy with conciliar governance — synods, consistories, and presbyteries. Calvin, as Voltaire notes, "established synods, consistories, and deacons; and that regulated the form of prayer and public preaching: he even instituted a consistorial jurisdiction, with the right of excommunication" (Ch. CXII). The conciliar principle migrated from church to state in the form of representative assemblies.

The Council of Trent (1545–1563) represented both the culmination of the conciliar tradition and its transformation. It defined Catholic doctrine with unprecedented precision, mandated episcopal residence, and created seminaries — but it operated under tight papal control, marking the end of genuine conciliarism within the Catholic Church.

## Key Figures

- **Emperor Sigismund** — Convened the Council of Constance, where "he appeared in all his glory" (Ch. XCIX)
- **Charles V** — Pressed for a general council to settle the religious question while advancing imperial interests
- **Pope Leo X** — Resisted conciliar reform during the critical years before the Reformation
- **John Calvin** — Established conciliar church governance in Geneva
- **Cardinal Granvelle** — Key figure in Charles V's imperial council
