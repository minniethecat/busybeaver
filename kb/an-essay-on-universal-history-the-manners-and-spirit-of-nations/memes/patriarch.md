## Overview

A **patriarch** is the highest ecclesiastical title in several Christian traditions, designating a bishop who presides over a patriarchate — a major jurisdiction of the Church. In the Western tradition, the title was also applied to [[saint|St. Benedict]], the "patriarch of the western monks," whose monastic rule in the sixth century shaped the organizational foundation of [[monasticism]] in Europe. (098-001)

In Voltaire's narrative, patriarchs appear in several contexts: the ancient patriarchates of the Eastern churches (Antioch, Jerusalem, Alexandria), the Latin patriarch established in Ethiopia under Portuguese influence, and the symbolic use of patriarchs as a justification for Portuguese voyages of exploration. Voltaire characteristically notes that "the patriarchs were only a pretence" — a cover for commercial and colonial ambition. (098-001)

## Origins and Context

The institution of patriarchates emerged in the early Church, when the bishops of the five major sees — Rome, Constantinople, Alexandria, Antioch, and Jerusalem — claimed primacy over surrounding regions. By the medieval period, the Eastern patriarchates had largely fallen under Muslim rule. The Syrian patriarch of Mosul, whom Voltaire describes as "hid himself at Mosul, which is said to be the ancient Nineveh," exercised a fragile authority over the St. Thomas Christians of India, who "did not so much as know that there was a church of Rome." (098-001)

In the Latin West, the title of patriarch was used more sparingly. The Patriarch of Venice and the Patriarch of Lisbon were among the few Western patriarchs outside Rome. However, the term acquired a broader cultural meaning through St. Benedict of Nursia, whom Voltaire invokes as "patriarch of the western monks" — the spiritual father of Western [[monasticism]], whose Rule written in the sixth century provided "a comfort to mankind, to see those asylums open to such as desired to retire from the oppression of the Goths and Vandals." (098-001)

## Key Features

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Ecclesiastical rank | Highest episcopal title below the pope in Eastern and some Western churches |
| Jurisdiction | A patriarchate — a major territorial or ritual jurisdiction |
| Eastern patriarchates | Alexandria, Antioch, Jerusalem, Constantinople (and later Moscow) |
| Monastic patriarch | St. Benedict, "patriarch of the western monks" (6th century) |
| Latin patriarchs | Honorific or missionary titles (Ethiopia, Venice, Lisbon, Jerusalem) |
| Political role | Patriarchs often served as mediators between Christian communities and Muslim rulers |

## Historical Development

Voltaire devotes particular attention to the Latin patriarchate established in Ethiopia (Abyssinia) under Portuguese influence in the 16th century. The Latin patriarch Oviedo, "with a few Portuguese soldiers, protected the young Negus of Abyssinia against the Moorish king." (098-001) However, the patriarch overreached: having served as the Negus's godfather, Oviedo "looked upon himself as his master" and ordered him "to pay obedience to the pope, threatening to excommunicate him in case of a refusal." (098-001) This episode illustrates the entanglement of missionary zeal with colonial ambition that Voltaire repeatedly critiques.

The Eastern patriarchates under Muslim rule fared poorly. The Syrian patriarch at Mosul presided over a "weak Syriac church" that was "buried, as it were, under its ruins, by Mahometan oppression." (098-001) The Portuguese later introduced Roman Catholicism into these communities, replacing the ancient Syriac rite with the Latin.

## Impact and Legacy

The patriarchal system shaped the governance of Christianity for over a millennium, providing a hierarchical framework that balanced local autonomy with universal communion. The Eastern patriarchates preserved Christian communities under Muslim rule, while the Western patriarchates contributed to the development of [[canon-law|canon law]] and ecclesiastical diplomacy.

Voltaire's critical perspective — that patriarchs served as "only a pretence" for Portuguese voyages — reflects his broader thesis that religious institutions were often instruments of secular power. (098-001) The title endures in both Eastern Orthodoxy and Eastern Catholicism, where patriarchs continue to govern their respective churches with a degree of independence from Rome.
