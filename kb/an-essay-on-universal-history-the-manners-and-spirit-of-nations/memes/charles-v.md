## Overview

Charles V (1500–1558) was Holy Roman Emperor from 1519 to 1556, King of Spain, and ruler of the Habsburg dominions across Europe and the Americas. His reign coincided with [[the-reformation]], the rise of the Ottoman Empire under [[suleiman-the-magnificent]], and the opening of the New World.

Voltaire depicts him as a monarch of immense ambition whose power was constantly checked by [[francis-i]] of France, the German princes, and the Turks. The [[battle-of-pavia]] (1525) and the [[battle-of-mohacs]] (1526) defined the military landscape of his reign, while his rivalry with [[gustavus-vasa]] and the Lutheran princes shaped the religious map of northern Europe.

## Rise to Power

Charles inherited a vast composite monarchy: Spain and its American colonies from his mother Joanna of Castile, the Burgundian Low Countries from his father Philip the Handsome, and the Austrian hereditary lands from his grandfather Maximilian I. His election as Holy Roman Emperor in 1519, financed by the Fugger banking family, made him the most powerful European ruler since Charlemagne.

> "The state of Europe at the time of Charles V" — Voltaire opens his account of the sixteenth century by surveying the condition of every European kingdom before examining Charles's reign. (Ch. XCVIII)

## Rivalry with Francis I

The defining conflict of Charles's reign was his rivalry with [[francis-i]] of France. Their wars spanned decades, from Charles's election in 1519 through the [[battle-of-pavia]] (1525), where Francis was taken prisoner, to the eventual Treaty of Crépy (1544).

Voltaire examines the question whether Charles aimed at universal monarchy—a charge leveled by his enemies—and concludes that his power, though vast, was perpetually counterbalanced.

> Francis I, taken prisoner at Pavia, was brought to Madrid. Rome was plundered by Charles's troops in 1527. Suleiman was repulsed from Vienna. Principalities were bestowed, Tunis was conquered. (Ch. CII)

## The Religious Question

Charles V presided over the empire at the moment when Luther's challenge to the papacy fractured Western Christendom. The Diet of Worms (1521), where Charles declared Luther an outlaw, set the stage for decades of religious conflict in Germany. The [[the-reformation]] became the central political as well as theological challenge of his reign.

The Troubles of Germany culminated in the battle of Mühlberg (1547), where Charles defeated the Protestant Schmalkaldic League—a temporary triumph that could not arrest the spread of Lutheranism. [[gustavus-vasa]] had already established Lutheranism in Sweden, demonstrating that the Reformation had become an irreversible political reality beyond Charles's control.

> The Troubles of Germany, the Battle of Mühlberg, the greatness and disgrace of Charles V, and his abdication are recounted by Voltaire in a single sweeping narrative. (Ch. CV)

## Abdication and Legacy

In 1556, worn by gout and decades of war, Charles V abdicated—dividing his empire between his son Philip II (Spain and the Netherlands) and his brother Ferdinand (the Holy Roman Empire). He retired to the monastery of Yuste in Spain. Voltaire's portrait emphasizes the contrast between Charles's immense power and his ultimate inability to impose unity on either Europe or the church.
