## Overview

A concordat is a formal agreement between the [[papacy]] and a secular sovereign regulating the relationship between church and state within a given territory. Concordats typically addressed three contentious areas: the appointment of bishops and abbots, the collection and distribution of ecclesiastical revenues (particularly [[annates]]), and the extent of papal jurisdiction over national churches.

Voltaire regarded concordats as instruments of pragmatic diplomacy rather than expressions of doctrinal principle: "it was said that the king and the pope gave to each other what did not belong to them." (Ch. XCVIII) The Concordat of Bologna (1516), negotiated between King [[francis-i|Francis I]] and Pope [[leo-x|Leo X]] through Chancellor Duprat, was the most consequential such agreement of the Reformation era.

## Origins and Context

The Concordat of Bologna emerged from the long history of conflict between the French crown and the papacy over control of ecclesiastical appointments. The Pragmatic Sanction of Bourges (1438), promulgated by King [[charles-vii|Charles VII]], had restored ecclesiastical elections, abolished [[annates]], and limited papal reservations and provisions. Though the Sanction established principles that shaped French ecclesiastical politics for centuries, it remained a source of tension between France and Rome.

The meeting between [[francis-i|Francis I]] and Pope [[leo-x|Leo X]] at Bologna in December 1515 provided the occasion for negotiating a permanent settlement. The Concordat, concluded the following year, replaced the Pragmatic Sanction with a new arrangement that gave the king the right to nominate bishops while restoring certain papal revenues.

## Key Features

| Provision | Effect |
|-----------|--------|
| Royal nomination of bishops | The king nominated candidates to vacant episcopal sees; the pope confirmed or rejected |
| Abolition of elective chapters | Cathedral chapters lost their right to elect bishops (Pragmatic Sanction provisions) |
| Restoration of annates | The pope regained the first year's income from newly conferred benefices |
| Papal right of refusal | The pope could reject a royal nominee, though this was rarely exercised |
| University privileges | The University of Paris lost its role in ecclesiastical appointments |

## Historical Development

The Concordat transformed the French ecclesiastical landscape. The king became the effective master of episcopal and abbatial appointments, wielding a power greater even than what the Pragmatic Sanction had granted the elective chapters. The University of Paris, deprived of its electoral privileges, published a prohibition against the Concordat's execution — a protest that proved futile against the combined will of king and pope.

Voltaire's judgment on the arrangement is characteristically sharp. He notes that the parliament and university "seemed to lose sight of an essential service which Francis I had rendered the nation by granting the annates; they had been paid before that time upon an exorbitant footing." The Concordat, in Voltaire's analysis, was less a victory for either church or state than a cynical redistribution of ecclesiastical spoils between two sovereigns.

The broader pattern of concordats as a mode of church-state diplomacy extended well beyond France. The Investiture Controversy of the 11th–12th centuries, culminating in the Concordat of Worms (1122), had established a theoretical distinction between the spiritual and temporal aspects of ecclesiastical appointments. This precedent shaped all subsequent concordats, including those of the early modern period.

## Impact and Legacy

The Concordat of Bologna gave the French monarchy control over the national church for the next three centuries — a power that effectively insulated the [[gallican-church|Gallican Church]] from direct Roman administration while simultaneously binding it to the crown. This arrangement paradoxically strengthened both royal authority and the autonomy of the French church, a pattern that would be replicated in other Catholic monarchies through similar concordats.

The Concordat also illustrates a pattern that would repeat throughout the Reformation era: secular rulers asserting control over ecclesiastical appointments and revenues as an expression of national sovereignty against Rome. Voltaire admired this pragmatic dimension of concordats — diplomatic compromise rather than doctrinal absolutism — as evidence that the interests of states ultimately trumped the claims of theology.
