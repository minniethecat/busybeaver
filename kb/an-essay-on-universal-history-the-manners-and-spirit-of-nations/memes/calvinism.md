## Overview

Calvinism represented the second great stream of the Protestant Reformation, distinct from Lutheranism in both theology and church organization. Originating in Geneva under John Calvin, the Reformed tradition spread to France (where its adherents were called Huguenots), the Netherlands, Scotland, and parts of Germany and Hungary. Voltaire devotes two chapters to Calvin and Geneva, treating the movement as a significant force in both religious and political history.

## Origins and Context

John Calvin was a French theologian trained in law who broke with the Catholic Church and settled in Geneva in the 1530s. Unlike Luther, whose Reformation emerged from monastic and academic contexts, Calvin brought a legal mind to theological systematization. His *Institutes of the Christian Religion* became the definitive statement of Reformed doctrine.

Geneva provided a unique setting for Calvin's experiment. The city had recently expelled its Catholic bishop and declared for the Reformation. Calvin, together with William Farel and later Theodore Beza, transformed Geneva into what became known as a model of Reformed discipline and moral governance.

## Key Features

Calvinism is distinguished by several distinctive doctrines:

- **Predestination**: God has eternally decreed who will be saved (the elect) and who will be damned (the reprobate), apart from any human merit or foreseen faith
- **Divine Sovereignty**: God's will is the ultimate cause of all events; nothing occurs outside His decree
- **Covenant Theology**: God relates to humanity through a series of covenants, with the church as the new Israel
- **Presbyterian Polity**: Church governance by elders (presbyters) rather than bishops, with representative assemblies (consistories, synods)
- **Iconoclasm**: The rejection of religious images, statues, and elaborate church decoration as violations of the Second Commandment

## Historical Development

Voltaire describes Calvin's system of church discipline in Geneva: the consistory, composed of pastors and lay elders, supervised public morals with remarkable rigor. Dancing, gambling, and elaborate dress were prohibited; attendance at sermons was mandatory. This created what Voltaire describes as an austere but orderly Protestant commonwealth.

The most controversial episode in Calvin's career was the execution of Michael Servetus, a Spanish physician who denied the Trinity. Servetus was burned at the stake in Geneva in 1553. Voltaire treats this event at length, noting the irony that the Reformed church employed the same methods of persecution it had condemned in the Catholic Church.

In France, Calvinism spread rapidly among the nobility and urban middle classes, leading to the Wars of Religion (1562–1598). In Scotland, John Knox brought the Reformed tradition, establishing the Presbyterian Church. In the Netherlands, Calvinism became the ideological foundation of the Dutch Revolt against Spain.

## Impact and Legacy

Calvinism's influence extended well beyond theology. The doctrine of vocation—that all honest work is a divine calling—contributed to what later scholars would call the Protestant work ethic. The emphasis on literacy (to read Scripture) promoted education, while presbyterian polity introduced representative governance into church life.

Voltaire's treatment of Calvin is characteristically ambivalent: he acknowledges Calvin's intellectual rigor and the moral discipline of Geneva, while condemning the execution of Servetus as an act of theological tyranny that betrayed the Reformation's own principles of individual conscience.

## Key Figures

- **John Calvin** — French theologian and reformer of Geneva
- **William Farel** — Pioneer of the Genevan Reformation
- **Theodore Beza** — Calvin's successor and systematizer
- **John Knox** — Scottish reformer who established Presbyterianism
- **Michael Servetus** — Unitarian physician executed for heresy in Geneva
