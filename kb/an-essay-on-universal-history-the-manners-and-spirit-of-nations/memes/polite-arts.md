## Overview

Voltaire devotes the second half of Chapter C to the "polite arts" -- the fine arts of painting, sculpture, architecture, and literature -- and their extraordinary flourishing in sixteenth-century Italy. He presents this cultural achievement as the counterpoint to the political and religious chaos of the age: "the never fading glory of the polite arts" persisted while the armies of [[charles-v|Charles V]] were plundering Rome and while "they were cutting one another's throats in Germany, France, and England, for things they did not understand" (Ch. C).

Italy's achievement was, in Voltaire's view, unparalleled since ancient Greece. "Nothing can give us a stronger idea of ancient Greece: for as the arts flourished in Greece in the midst of foreign and civil wars, so they had the same fate in Italy; and almost every thing in that happy climate was carried to its summit of perfection" (Ch. C). He systematically compares Italian achievements with their Greek antecedents and finds the Italians superior in painting, architecture, and -- with qualifications -- epic poetry.

## Painting and Sculpture

Voltaire makes a bold comparative claim: in painting, the modern Italians surpassed the ancients entirely. The Greeks "neither had that variety of colours used by the Italians, nor were they acquainted with the art of perspective or of chiaro-oscuro" (Ch. C). The discovery of perspective and the mastery of light and shadow (chiaroscuro) constituted a genuine advance beyond anything known to antiquity.

In sculpture, the contest was closer -- "sculpture, the easiest and the most confined of the two, was the art in which the Greeks excelled; and the Italians glory in not falling far short of those models" (Ch. C). Michelangelo and his contemporaries consciously competed with the classical statuary being unearthed throughout Italy, and Voltaire judges they came admirably close.

## Architecture

Architecture was the domain where Voltaire saw the clearest Italian superiority. He singles out St. Peter's Basilica in Rome as "the finest, the largest, and the boldest temple the world ever beheld" (Ch. C) -- a structure without parallel in antiquity. The completion of St. Peter's was the labor of ten successive popes, who "almost without interruption, contributed to finish the church of St. Peter, and at the same time encouraged the other arts" (Ch. C).

The contrast Voltaire draws is pointed: while religious wars produced "barbarism and ruins" in northern Europe, the popes of Rome were displaying "the wonders of architecture." The papacy, for all its political and theological failings, had become the greatest patron of the arts the world had known.

## Literature and Poetry

Voltaire extends his comparisons to literature. Machiavelli's comedy *Mandragora* is judged "preferable perhaps to all Aristophanes's comedies" (Ch. C), and Machiavelli himself -- historian, political theorist, and man of genius -- is rated "incomparably" above the Greek philosopher Plato as a writer.

On epic poetry, Voltaire makes a comparison that would have been provocative to his contemporaries: if one were to weigh Homer's *Odyssey* and Ariosto's *Orlando Furioso* "in the same scales, the Italian in many respects has greatly the advantage" (Ch. C). He then raises the ante by comparing Homer's *Iliad* with Tasso's *Jerusalem Delivered*, inviting the impartial reader to judge purely by pleasure received, without knowing the names of the authors or the time when their works were composed. His verdict is unequivocal: Tasso is "more interesting, had more economy, order, variety, exactness, and elegance; in a word, more of that softness which adds a lustre to the sublime" (Ch. C).

> In a word Italy alone had then the glory of being the seat of genius and taste, a glory heretofore confined to Greece. (Ch. C)

## The Arts and the Reformation

Voltaire offers a suggestive geopolitical explanation for Italy's cultural preeminence. The arts continued to flourish in Italy "because the pest of religious controversy had not penetrated into that country" (Ch. C). While Germany, France, and England were consumed by theological civil war, Italy -- following the trauma of the Sack of Rome in 1527 -- turned inward to the cultivation of beauty. The paradox is that the very papacy which persecuted [[galileo-galilei|Galileo]] and maintained the [[the-inquisition]] was also the institution that made possible the artistic achievements of Michelangelo, Raphael, and their successors.

## Key Figures

- **Michelangelo** -- Sculptor, painter, and architect whose work defined the High Renaissance
- **Raphael** -- Painter whose work rivaled the ancients in grace and composition
- **Ariosto** -- Author of *Orlando Furioso*, which Voltaire ranks above Homer's *Odyssey*
- **Tasso** -- Author of *Jerusalem Delivered*, judged superior to the *Iliad*
- **Machiavelli** -- Historian, political thinker, and comic playwright
- **Guicciardini** -- Italy's Thucydides, historian of the Italian Wars
