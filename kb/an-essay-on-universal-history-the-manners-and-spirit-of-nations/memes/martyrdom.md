## Overview

**Martyrdom** — the willing acceptance of death for one's religious convictions — was one of the most potent forces in the religious conflicts of the sixteenth century. Both Catholics and Protestants produced martyrs; both sides used martyrdom narratives to mobilize support, demonize opponents, and construct communal identity. Voltaire treats martyrdom with his characteristic double vision: he recognizes the sincerity of many who died, but he cannot refrain from observing how often the concept was exploited to sanctify political violence or to transform fanaticism into a form of heroism. (Ch. CXVI, CXLII)

## The Transformation of Martyrdom

For the early Church, martyrdom was the supreme witness to faith — the word itself derives from the Greek _martys_, meaning "witness." The blood of the martyrs, Tertullian wrote, was the seed of the Church. But in the sixteenth century, the concept underwent a profound transformation. Both sides of the confessional divide claimed the mantle of the early martyrs; each accused the other of being not a legitimate authority punishing heresy, but a tyrannical power persecuting truth.

The [[huguenot|Huguenots]] of France exemplify this transformation. Voltaire observes that under Francis I and Henry II, the French Protestants "understood nothing but praying and suffering, what they called martyrdom." Their willingness to die for their faith won them sympathy and respect. But over time, the passive endurance of persecution was supplemented — and in some cases supplanted — by active resistance. "Those very Huguenots," Voltaire writes, "were grown most furious enthusiasts; so that now they read the bible to find authority for assassinations." (Ch. CXLII)

## Poltrot de Mere: Martyr or Murderer?

The case of Poltrot de Mere illustrates the ambiguity Voltaire finds in Reformation-era martyrdom. Poltrot assassinated Francis, Duke of Guise — the leader of the Catholic faction — during the siege of Orleans in 1563. He "looked upon himself as another Aod sent by the Deity to kill a Philistine chief." Voltaire notes that "the party made verses to his honour; and I have seen a print of him myself, with an inscription extolling the deed to the sky." (Ch. CXLII)

Yet it was, Voltaire insists, "a cowardly deed: for he pretended to be a deserter, and struck the duke behind his back." Poltrot had the impudence to charge the admiral de Coligni and Theodore Beza with having connived at the murder. Coligni, "notwithstanding his being head of the party, never did the least thing that could render him suspected of so base an action." (Ch. CXLII)

The Poltrot case exposes the moral hazard at the heart of sixteenth-century martyrdom: once killing for religion was sanctified as martyrdom-in-reverse, the boundary between witness and assasssin dissolved. Anyone who died for the cause — however they had lived — could be claimed as a martyr.

## Voltaire's Skepticism

Voltaire's attitude toward martyrdom is characteristically complex. He does not mock the concept itself — the courage to die for one's convictions commands a certain respect in his narrative. But he is relentlessly skeptical of the use to which martyrdom narratives are put:

> So unhappy were those times, and so strongly did fanaticism or terror possess the minds of the people, that the parliament of Paris decreed an annual procession on St. Bartholomew's day, in thanksgiving to God. The chancellor de l'Hopital was quite of a different opinion, when he wrote, _excidat ille dies_ — let that day perish. (Ch. CXLII)

| Martyrdom Claim | Voltaire's Assessment |
|-----------------|----------------------|
| Protestant executions under Henry II | Genuine religious persecution |
| Poltrot de Mere's assassination of Guise | Cowardly murder dressed as holy war |
| St. Bartholomew's Day victims | Massacre, not martyrdom — political extermination |
| Catholic martyrs under Elizabeth I | Mirror image of Protestant persecution |

## The Social Function of Martyrdom

Beneath his irony, Voltaire perceives the social function of martyrdom. A persecuted minority that can claim martyrs gains a powerful tool: the narrative of innocent suffering wrongs the balance of moral legitimacy. The early Huguenots, by praying and suffering, won sympathy their theology alone could not command. But the transformation from suffering to fighting — from martyrdom to militancy — reveals the instability of the concept: martyrdom is a posture of weakness that, if successful, produces strength. Once strong enough to fight back, the martyrs become warriors; once warriors, they can no longer claim the moral high ground of innocent suffering. (Ch. CXVI, CXLII)
