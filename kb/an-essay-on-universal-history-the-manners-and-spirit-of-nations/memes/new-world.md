## Overview

The discovery and conquest of the New World stands as one of the transformative events in Voltaire's Essay on Universal History. The opening of the Americas redirected the flow of global [[commerce]], enriched the Spanish crown, extended the reach of European [[catholicism|Catholicism]], and provided Voltaire with some of his most searing material for the critique of religious violence and colonial exploitation. "What would Cortés have said," Voltaire asks, "had he beheld an Auto da Fe?" (Ch. CXVIII). The question is characteristically layered: it compares Spanish cruelty to Aztec cruelty, European religious persecution to the alleged barbarism of the people being conquered.

Voltaire's treatment of the New World spans multiple dimensions — economic, religious, political, and moral — and refuses to settle into a single judgment. The Americas were simultaneously a source of European wealth and power, a theatre of appalling violence, a field for missionary [[franciscan|Franciscan]] and [[jesuits|Jesuit]] evangelism, and an intellectual challenge to inherited European assumptions about human society and natural law.

## Origins and Context

The geographical discoveries of the late fifteenth and early sixteenth centuries — Columbus's voyages (from 1492), the Portuguese circumnavigation of Africa, and the Spanish conquests in the Americas — fundamentally altered the structure of the world economy. "The discovery of the sea route to India around the Cape of Good Hope and the Spanish conquests in the Americas redirected trade flows from the Mediterranean to the Atlantic."

Portugal and Spain became the first Atlantic empires, but Voltaire notes a characteristic irony: "the ultimate beneficiaries were often the northern European maritime powers" — the Dutch and English who would later challenge Iberian monopoly. The wealth of the Americas, channelled through Spain, financed the Habsburg bid for European hegemony under [[charles-v|Charles V]], whose empire "spanned Europe and the Americas."

The New World also entered the European imagination as a place of political and social possibility. The "republican tradition that Voltaire observed in the 16th century," nourished by classical and Italian models, "provided the institutional precedents and intellectual arguments that would inspire later republican movements in England, America, and France."

## Historical Development

Voltaire traces the New World's impact on Europe through several phases. The initial Spanish conquests — Cortés in Mexico, Pizarro in Peru — established a vast colonial empire from which silver and gold flowed into the Spanish treasury. This wealth, in Voltaire's analysis, was a mixed blessing: it made Spain temporarily the most powerful state in Europe, but it also distorted the Spanish economy and encouraged a policy of imperial overreach that would ultimately prove unsustainable.

The missionary orders followed the conquistadors. [[franciscan|Franciscans]] "followed conquistadors to the New World," establishing missions in Mexico, Peru, and the Philippines. The [[jesuits|Jesuits]] conducted "missionary work in Asia and the Americas," adapting their methods to local cultures with a pragmatism that Voltaire, despite his anticlericalism, found worthy of note. Yet he insists that the missions were "instruments of both evangelization and empire" — the spiritual and the coercive were inseparable in the colonial project.

The chapter "Of Columbus and America" (Ch. CXXII) provides Voltaire's most concentrated reflection on the New World discoveries. He treats Columbus as a figure of world-historical importance whose achievement transformed the material and intellectual horizons of Europe, even as the consequences of that transformation — for the indigenous peoples of the Americas — were catastrophic.

## Impact and Legacy

The New World's most enduring impact, in Voltaire's framework, was economic. The redirection of global trade from the Mediterranean to the Atlantic empowered the northern European maritime powers — England, the Dutch Republic — at the expense of Venice, Genoa, and the Ottoman intermediaries. The "sixteenth century saw dramatic shifts in commercial geography" whose consequences would unfold over the next three hundred years.

Politically, the American colonies became a site of European rivalry that extended the dynastic conflicts of the Old World across the Atlantic. Religiously, the New World extended the reach of [[catholicism|Catholicism]] even as the [[the-reformation|Reformation]] divided Europe — the "bull *Regimini militantis Ecclesiae* (1540) approving the Society of Jesus created the order that would become the vanguard of Catholic reform and Counter-Reformation polemics" in missions from China to Peru.

Intellectually, the discovery of the New World challenged European certainties. The existence of complex societies unknown to the ancients and unmentioned in Scripture raised questions about the universality of Christian history and the adequacy of classical geography. These questions, inchoate in the sixteenth century, would flower in the Enlightenment — not least in Voltaire's own work.
