## Overview

Simony — named after Simon Magus, who attempted to purchase the power of the Holy Spirit from the apostles (Acts 8:9–24) — was the practice of buying and selling ecclesiastical offices, [[benefice|benefices]], and spiritual privileges within the Catholic Church. Though repeatedly condemned by church councils and canon law as a mortal sin, simony was endemic in the late medieval Church and represented one of the most visible forms of clerical corruption.

Voltaire identifies simony among the clerical abuses that provoked widespread demands for reform: "The whole torrent of protestant and catholic writers make loud complaints against the loose morals of those times." (Ch. CVI) The practice became a central grievance of the [[the-reformation|Reformation]] and one of the principal targets of the [[counter-reformation|Catholic Reformation]].

## Origins and Context

The term derives from Simon Magus, a Samaritan sorcerer described in the Acts of the Apostles who offered money to the apostles Peter and John in exchange for the power to confer the Holy Spirit through the laying on of hands. The apostle Peter rebuked him: "May your money perish with you, because you thought you could buy the gift of God with money."

In the medieval Church, simony took many forms: the outright purchase of episcopal sees or abbacies, the payment of fees for ordination or absolution, and the system of papal provisions by which the [[papacy]] appointed candidates to benefices in exchange for payments. The practice was closely linked to the [[benefice]] system, which made ecclesiastical offices economically valuable.

## Key Features

| Form | Description |
|------|-------------|
| Simony of office | Purchase of episcopal sees, abbacies, or parish benefices |
| Simony of ordination | Payment for priestly ordination or consecration as bishop |
| Simony of jurisdiction | Payment for the exercise of ecclesiastical judicial authority |
| Papal provisions | Papal appointment to benefices, often involving payments to intermediaries |
| Pluralism (related) | Holding multiple benefices simultaneously, often acquired through simoniacal means |

## Historical Development

Simony was a recurring problem throughout the Middle Ages. The Gregorian Reform of the 11th century, led by Pope Gregory VII, made the elimination of simony a central objective. The Investiture Controversy (1075–1122) between the papacy and the Holy Roman Empire was in part a struggle over the control of ecclesiastical appointments and the revenues attached to them.

Despite centuries of reform efforts, simony remained widespread into the 16th century. The Renaissance papacy was particularly notorious: Pope [[leo-x|Leo X]] (r. 1513–1521) engaged in large-scale sales of offices and [[indulgences]] to finance the construction of St. Peter's Basilica. The perception that everything in the Church was for sale — from parish benefices to papal favors — fueled anti-clerical sentiment across Europe.

The [[the-reformation|Reformation]] directly attacked simony as evidence that the Catholic Church had abandoned its spiritual mission for worldly gain. The [[counter-reformation|Council of Trent]] (1545–1563) responded with strict decrees against simoniacal practices, mandating that ecclesiastical offices be conferred solely on the basis of merit and prohibiting all payments for ordination or appointment.

## Impact and Legacy

The campaign against simony illustrates the complex dynamics of church reform. While Protestant reformers used simony as evidence that the Catholic Church was irredeemably corrupt, Catholic reformers used the same evidence to argue for internal renewal. The Tridentine decrees against simony, combined with the creation of seminaries for the proper formation of the [[clergy]], gradually reduced the practice — though it never disappeared entirely.

The broader legacy of the simony controversy was the establishment of the principle that spiritual office should not be treated as a commodity. This principle influenced the development of professional standards for the clergy and contributed to the long-term separation between ecclesiastical and secular career paths in Catholic Europe.
