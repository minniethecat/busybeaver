## Overview

Monarchy was the dominant form of government in sixteenth-century Europe, but the term concealed enormous variation. Voltaire's comparative survey reveals a spectrum from the absolute monarchy of France — "since the reign of Lewis XI. was become an absolute monarchy; happy government when such a king as Lewis XII. repaired the mistakes" (Ch. C) — to the elective, fiscally constrained monarchies of Poland and Hungary, to the peculiar case of the Holy Roman Emperor who "has nothing at all; he is at his own expence, the head of the Christian world" (Ch. XCVIII). The reign of [[charles-v]] brought the question of monarchy to its sharpest point: could any single ruler achieve universal monarchy over Christendom?

## Origins and Context

Monarchy in Europe evolved from the fusion of Roman imperial tradition, Germanic kingship, and Christian sacral kingship. By the sixteenth century, most European kingdoms had become hereditary in practice if not always in law. The key variable was the degree to which representative institutions — diets, parliaments, cortes, estates — constrained royal power.

Voltaire carefully distinguishes between the theory and practice of monarchy. Even in France, the paradigm of absolutism, Francis I was forced to melt down the silver shrine of St. Martin at Tours to finance his wars, and "disposed of twenty new employments in the law, for a sum of money. Thus the public sale of justice, and the stripping of the shrines, shewed very plainly the bad state of his finances" (Ch. CII).

## Key Features

The varieties of monarchy in Voltaire's account can be classified:

| Type | Example | Revenue | Constraint |
|------|---------|---------|------------|
| **Absolute Monarchy** | France (post-Louis XI) | Sale of offices, taxes | Minimal institutional check |
| **Elective Monarchy** | Poland, Hungary, Bohemia | ~1.2M livres/year (Poland) | Diet controls army, treasury, fortresses |
| **Composite Monarchy** | Charles V's dominions | Scattered, irregular | Multiple constitutions, local privileges |
| **Imperial Monarchy** | Holy Roman Empire | None from empire | Princes, electors, imperial chamber |
| **Pontifical Monarchy** | Papal States, Rome | Spiritual revenues | College of Cardinals |

> Voltaire notes that in Poland "their kings have no fortresses, nor the disposing of the public monies, nor the command of the armies, their liberty has been hitherto preserved. The nation allowed the king only about twelve hundred thousand livres annually to support his dignity" (Ch. XCVIII).

## Historical Development

The sixteenth century saw monarchy ascendant in the West but embattled in the East. Francis I, despite catastrophic defeat at the [[battle-of-pavia]] and captivity in Madrid, emerged with his authority intact — if financially strained. The French monarchy's ability to survive royal captivity demonstrated how far royal institutions had penetrated the kingdom.

In Eastern Europe, the picture was very different. Hungarian kings were elected at diets that "always limited their authority after the example of the nobles in Poland" (Ch. XCVIII). The monarchy's weakness contributed directly to military catastrophe: when Suleiman invaded, "the whole kingdom of Hungary could not in that urgent necessity furnish him with an army of thirty thousand fighting men" (Ch. XCVIII).

Charles V's reign posed the question of universal monarchy directly. Voltaire devotes a chapter section to "whether Charles V aimed at universal monarchy" and concludes the notion was "as false and chimerical as that which was afterwards imputed to Lewis XIV" (Ch. CIII). The "system of an equilibrium of power was at this very period established in Europe" (Ch. CIII) — the balance of power principle that would define European statecraft for centuries.

## Impact and Legacy

Voltaire's treatment of monarchy is characteristically nuanced. He approves of monarchy when it serves the public good (Louis XII's "affection for his people") but condemns it when it becomes tyranny. The Polish system, for all its anarchic features, preserved liberty — but at the cost of state effectiveness. The French model delivered power but risked despotism.

The concept of monarchy was further transformed by the Reformation. [[gustavus-vasa]] established hereditary monarchy in Sweden while also breaking with Rome, demonstrating that royal supremacy and religious reform could reinforce each other. In England, Henry VIII's assertion of royal supremacy over the church created a new model of sacred monarchy without papal mediation.

## Key Figures

- **[[charles-v]]** — Emperor whose vast dominions raised the question of universal monarchy
- **Francis I** — French king who embodied Renaissance absolutism despite military disasters
- **Sigismund of the Jagellon race** — Polish king who died in 1548, exemplifying constrained monarchy
- **Louis XI** — French king who established the absolutist model
- **[[gustavus-vasa]]** — Swedish king who combined hereditary monarchy with religious reform
