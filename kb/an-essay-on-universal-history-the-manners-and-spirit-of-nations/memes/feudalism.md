## Overview

Feudalism in Voltaire's Essay on Universal History is treated less as a formal system to be defined than as a condition from which Europe was slowly emerging. The "private wars of the feudal lords were no longer suffered any where" by the early sixteenth century (Ch. XCVIII), marking the triumph of royal authority over a fragmented and violent past. Yet feudal structures persisted in modified forms — in the corporate privileges of the [[nobility]], in ecclesiastical temporal power, and in the patchwork of jurisdictions that continued to define the Holy Roman [[empire]].

Voltaire's analysis is characteristically comparative: the countries that most successfully broke from feudal decentralization — France under Louis XI, England under the Tudors — became the strongest states, while those where feudal liberty survived in the form of noble privilege, such as Poland and Hungary, suffered military and political weakness.

## Origins and Context

The feudal system emerged from the fusion of Roman and Germanic institutions after the collapse of the Carolingian Empire. "The feudal government had made sovereigns of several bishops and monks" (Ch. XCIX), creating a double authority — ecclesiastical and secular — that produced what Voltaire calls "eternal broils." The mixture of these "two jarring powers" had "depopulated Italy, Germany, and almost every other country in Europe."

By Voltaire's period of focus (the sixteenth century), feudalism was in retreat everywhere, but at different speeds and with different consequences. The "feudal laws in the West did not permit a bondman to be admitted into a monastery without his lord's consent" (Ch. C), illustrating how thoroughly the system penetrated every social institution, including the church.

## Key Features

Voltaire identifies several defining characteristics of feudal society in its late phase:

| Feature | Expression | Voltaire's Judgment |
|---------|-----------|---------------------|
| **Private warfare** | Lords waged war independently | "No longer suffered" — a mark of progress |
| **Noble privilege** | Immunity from royal justice, power over peasants | Source of "fatal independency" (Hungary) |
| **Ecclesiastical feudalism** | Bishops and abbots as temporal lords | Produced "eternal broils" with secular authority |
| **Customary law** | Trial by combat, dueling | "Anathematized by popes" yet persisted |
| **Land tenure** | Conditional grants for military service | Basis of all social and political relations |

> "The northern conquerors extended the customs of feudal tenures from Lombardy as far as India." (Ch. C)

## Historical Development

The feudal system underwent a long transformation from the ninth to the sixteenth century. Initially a response to the breakdown of centralized authority after Charlemagne, it became the default political order of medieval Europe. Voltaire traces its decline through several stages:

The consolidation of royal authority in France, beginning with Louis XI (r. 1461–1483), was the decisive break. "France alone, since the reign of Lewis XI, was become an absolute monarchy" (Ch. XCVIII). This was, in Voltaire's view, a "happy government" — at least when a king like Louis XII tempered absolutism with affection for his people.

In Germany, the feudal structure survived longer through the imperial constitution. The Holy Roman [[empire]] preserved the independence of princes, free cities, and imperial knights, creating a constitutional balance that Voltaire admires even as he acknowledges its costs in political fragmentation.

By the time of [[charles-v]] (r. 1519–1556), the question was no longer whether feudalism would survive but what would replace it: absolute monarchy, constitutional equilibrium, or noble anarchy. The different answers given by France, Germany, Poland, and Hungary form the comparative core of Voltaire's political analysis.

## Impact and Legacy

Feudalism's legacy in Voltaire's narrative is profoundly ambivalent. On one hand, its abolition was necessary for peace, order, and the rule of law — "the general policy of Europe was perfected to such a degree" that private wars ceased (Ch. XCVIII). On the other hand, the constitutional checks that feudalism had embedded in political life, particularly in Germany, provided a foundation for [[liberty]] that absolutism could not offer.

The feudal past also left deep marks on ecclesiastical institutions. Disputes over episcopal appointments "would have been productive of a civil war in the times of feudal government" (Ch. CII); that they no longer did so was a measure of how far Europe had moved. Yet the temporal power of the [[clergy]] and the [[bishop]] remained a feudal survival that the Reformation would decisively challenge.

## Key Figures

- **Louis XI of France** — The king who most decisively broke feudal power and established absolute monarchy
- **Charles V** — Emperor whose reign tested whether feudal decentralization could coexist with modern state power
- **Francis I** — French king who removed ecclesiastical disputes from parliamentary (feudal) jurisdiction
- **Ferdinand of Aragon** — Catholic king who could not "destroy the authority of the justiciary," a feudal survival
