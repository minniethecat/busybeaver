## Overview

The Franciscan order in Voltaire's narrative represents the best and the worst of medieval monasticism. On one hand, the Franciscans were among the most effective missionaries and reformers in the Church — men like Cardinal Cisneros, the Franciscan who governed Spain as regent and forcibly converted the Moors of Granada. On the other hand, the Franciscan ideal of radical poverty had, by the sixteenth century, spawned bitter internal conflicts between the Observant and Conventual branches, conflicts that Voltaire sees as symptomatic of a wider ecclesiastical decay.

The order's presence permeated every level of society: Franciscan confessors advised kings, Franciscan missionaries followed conquistadors to the New World, and Franciscan preachers filled the pulpits of Europe. When Luther attacked the Church's wealth and corruption, he was echoing criticisms that Franciscan reformers had been making for centuries.

## Origins and Context

The Franciscan order was founded by Francis of Assisi (1181/82–1226), the son of a wealthy cloth merchant who renounced his inheritance to embrace "Lady Poverty." The order's rule, approved orally by Innocent III in 1209 and in writing by Honorius III in 1223, committed the friars to live without property, to preach the Gospel, and to support themselves by begging — hence the term "mendicant" (from Latin *mendicare*, to beg).

Alongside the [[dominicans]], the Franciscans became one of the two great mendicant orders that transformed medieval Christianity. They staffed the universities (Bonaventure, Duns Scotus, William of Ockham were all Franciscans), served as inquisitors, and provided the papacy with a corps of preachers independent of local bishops.

## Historical Development

The sixteenth century was a period of crisis and renewal for the Franciscan order. The Protestant Reformation attacked the very idea of monasticism, and the Franciscans, as the most visible of the mendicant orders, bore the brunt of the attack. Luther, himself a former Augustinian friar, argued that monastic vows were unscriptural and that the monastic life had no basis in the Gospel.

Yet the Franciscans also produced some of the most effective Catholic reformers. Cardinal Francisco Jiménez de Cisneros (1436–1517), an Observant Franciscan, served as regent of Spain, founded the University of Alcalá, and sponsored the Complutensian Polyglot Bible — one of the great scholarly achievements of the age.

The Capuchin reform, begun by Matteo da Bascio in 1525, represented a return to the primitive Franciscan ideal. Despite early setbacks — the founder briefly joined the Protestants — the Capuchins were recognized as an independent order in 1528 and became one of the most dynamic forces in the Catholic Counter-Reformation.

## Impact and Legacy

Voltaire's assessment of the Franciscans is characteristically double-edged. He acknowledges their role in "embellishing Rome" and restoring "the fine arts" (Ch. CVI), but he also notes their implication in the wider corruptions of the Renaissance Church. The Franciscan ideal of poverty could not prevent the accumulation of wealth and power that came with royal patronage and popular devotion.

The order's missionary expansion to the Americas and Asia was, in Voltaire's secular perspective, a mixed blessing: it brought European religion to indigenous peoples but also facilitated their subjugation. The Franciscan missions in Mexico, Peru, and the Philippines were instruments of both evangelization and empire.

## Key Figures

- **Francis of Assisi** (1181/82–1226) — Founder; his ideal of radical poverty remained the order's spiritual lodestar
- **Cardinal Cisneros (Jiménez de Cisneros)** (1436–1517) — Observant Franciscan, regent of Spain, sponsor of the Complutensian Polyglot Bible
- **Matteo da Bascio** (1495–1552) — Founder of the Capuchin reform
- **John Duns Scotus** (c. 1266–1308) — Franciscan scholastic theologian, defender of the Immaculate Conception
