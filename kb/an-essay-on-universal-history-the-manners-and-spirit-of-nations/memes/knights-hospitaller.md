## Overview

The Knights Hospitaller, formally the Order of Knights of the Hospital of Saint John of Jerusalem, was one of the most enduring institutions of the medieval world. Founded during the Crusades as a hospitaller brotherhood caring for sick pilgrims, the order rapidly militarized and became a sovereign naval power that contested Ottoman expansion in the Mediterranean for centuries. Voltaire references the order repeatedly as a fixture of early modern European politics — from its role in the contest for the Portuguese succession to its governance of Tripoli and its participation in the battle of Lepanto. "Tripoli, which presents itself to us next as we travel westward, having been taken by Peter of Navarre in the reign of Ferdinand the Catholic in 1510, was given by Charles V to the knights of Malta." (Ch. CXXXII)

## Origins and Structure

The order originated around 1099 when Blessed Gerard established a hospital in Jerusalem to care for pilgrims. After the First Crusade, Pope Paschal II recognized it as a religious order in 1113. Under Raymond du Puy, the order adopted a military function, becoming one of the principal fighting forces of the Crusader states alongside the [[knights-templar|Knights Templar]].

| Period | Headquarters | Significance |
|--------|-------------|--------------|
| c. 1099–1187 | Jerusalem | Foundation and hospital mission |
| 1191–1291 | Acre | Final Crusader mainland stronghold |
| 1310–1522 | Rhodes | Sovereign military state; naval power |
| 1530–1798 | Malta | Bulwark against Ottoman expansion |

After the fall of Acre in 1291, the order relocated to Cyprus and then conquered Rhodes in 1310, where it established a sovereign principality. Expelled from Rhodes by Suleiman the Magnificent in 1522, the knights were granted Malta by Charles V in 1530, where they became known as the Knights of Malta.

## Military and Naval Role

The order maintained one of the most effective naval forces in the Mediterranean. Its galleys participated in the Battle of Lepanto (1571), which broke Ottoman naval supremacy. The order's organization by "langues" (national-linguistic divisions) reflected the pan-European character of its recruitment — each langue maintained its own auberge (hostel) and contributed galleys to the fleet.

Voltaire notes the peculiar dignity system within the order: the title of "grand commander" designated "the governor of a body of knights of Malta, belonging to a particular nation or kingdom, as here to Castile." (Ch. CXXXV) The prior of the order also featured in the secret accounts of Henry VIII's agent Crook, who recorded payments "to the prior of S. John fifteen crowns" in the campaign to secure university opinions favoring the king's divorce. (Ch. CXIII)

## Political Entanglements in Early Modern Europe

The order's sovereignty and prestige made it a player in dynastic politics. During the Portuguese succession crisis of 1580, "Antonio, prior of Crato, and knights of Malta, wanted to succeed the priestly king" — contesting Philip II of Spain's claim to the Portuguese throne. (Ch. CXLI) Though Antonio's bid failed, the episode illustrates the order's integration into the fabric of European aristocratic politics.

After the Great Siege of Malta (1565), the order's capital Valletta became one of the most heavily fortified cities in Europe, a testament to the military engineering of the [[italian-wars|Italian Wars]] era. The order survived the Reformation largely intact because its multinational character insulated it from the dynastic-religious conflicts that fractured other medieval institutions.

## Decline and Transformation

The order's military relevance declined after the 17th century as the Ottoman naval threat receded and European states built professional navies. Napoleon expelled the knights from Malta in 1798, effectively ending their territorial sovereignty. Yet the order persisted as a humanitarian and hospitaller organization — returning to its original mission — and continues to exist today as the Sovereign Military Order of Malta, a subject of international law.
