## Overview

Religious doctrine — the formally defined body of Christian teaching — was the neuralgic point of the Reformation. Disputes over transubstantiation, free will, predestination, and the sacraments were not mere academic exercises; they shattered the unity of Western Christendom and triggered wars, persecutions, and political realignments. Voltaire treats these doctrinal controversies with his characteristic blend of intellectual seriousness and ironic distance, noting that "the most silly propositions were intermixed with those of most importance; and on both sides the animosity was kept up by false accusations, furious invectives, and anathemas" (Ch. CVII).

## Origins and Context

Doctrine in the Western Church developed through councils, papal decrees, and the writings of Church Fathers. By the late Middle Ages, scholastic theology had elaborated an immense doctrinal edifice — but much of it rested on foundations that humanist scholarship began to question. Erasmus's edition of the Greek New Testament (1516) exposed discrepancies between the Vulgate Bible and the original text, undermining key doctrinal proof-texts.

Voltaire emphasizes that the Christian republic was "almost generally united in doctrine, but divided in every thing else" (Ch. CVI) before the Reformation. The doctrinal consensus masked deep political and institutional conflicts that would surface once the consensus itself was challenged.

## Key Features

The major doctrinal disputes of the Reformation era included:

| Doctrine | Catholic Position | Protestant Challenge |
|----------|------------------|---------------------|
| **Transubstantiation** | Bread and wine become Christ's body and blood | Luther: consubstantiation; Zwingli: symbolic |
| **Free Will** | Human will cooperates with divine grace | Luther: bondage of the will after the Fall |
| **Justification** | Faith plus works (penance, sacraments) | Faith alone (*sola fide*) |
| **Scripture and Tradition** | Both are authoritative | Scripture alone (*sola scriptura*) |
| **Sacraments** | Seven sacraments | Luther: 3 (later 2); generally 2 |
| **Papal Authority** | Pope as supreme pontiff | No biblical basis for papacy |

> Voltaire notes Luther's novel physical analogy: "He acknowledges that the body of Christ is in the consecrated elements: but it is there, he says, as fire is in a red hot iron; the fire and the iron subsist together" — a doctrine Osiander termed "impanation, invination, and consubstantiation" (Ch. CVII).

## Historical Development

The Reformation did not begin as a doctrinal revolution. Luther's Ninety-Five Theses (1517) focused on the abuse of [[indulgences]], a matter of practice rather than doctrine. But the logic of his position quickly pushed toward fundamental questions. At the Diet of Worms (1521), Luther "boldly exposed himself to the same fate as that of John Huss" and "spoke before the emperor and the diet, and defended his doctrine with great resolution" (Ch. CVII).

The multiplication of doctrinal positions proved impossible to contain. Zwingli in Zurich, Calvin in Geneva, and the English church under Henry VIII each developed distinctive doctrinal settlements. Voltaire describes how "there started up in a few years three new churches; that of Luther, that of Zwinglius, and that of England" (Ch. CVII) — each with its own doctrinal confession.

The Catholic response, crystallized at the Council of Trent (1545–1563), reaffirmed traditional doctrine while addressing some of the abuses reformers had denounced. The [[jesuits]] became the intellectual shock troops of Catholic doctrinal orthodoxy. The [[the-inquisition]] enforced doctrinal conformity through judicial means.

## Impact and Legacy

Voltaire's account of doctrinal controversy is marked by Enlightenment irony. He reports Luther's claim "that the devil had appeared to him, and reproached him with saying mass and consecrating the host" — and that Luther concluded "the devil was in the right, and therefore ought to be believed" (Ch. CVII). The point is not to mock Luther but to underline the epistemological fragility of doctrinal certainty.

The doctrinal fragmentation of the Reformation had political consequences that extended far beyond theology. The principle *cuius regio, eius religio* (whose realm, his religion) made doctrine a matter of state policy. Religious refugees — Huguenots from France, Catholics from England, Jews and Muslims from Spain — redistributed skills and capital across Europe.

## Key Figures

- **Martin Luther** — German reformer who challenged Catholic doctrine on justification and authority
- **John Calvin** — Systematic theologian of Reformed doctrine, predestination, and church discipline
- **Huldrych Zwingli** — Swiss reformer whose symbolic view of the Eucharist differed from Luther's
- **Pope Leo X** — Medici pope whose court embodied the worldliness reformers condemned
- **Henry VIII** — English king who received the title "Defender of the Faith" from Leo X before breaking with Rome
