## Overview

The Dutch Republic, formally the Republic of the Seven United Provinces, was founded through a long war of revolt against [[sovereignty|Philip II of Spain]]. Voltaire presents its origins as a case study in unintended consequences: "it was formed without design, and against all probability" (Ch. CXXXV). The wealthiest provinces -- Brabant, Flanders, and Hainault -- remained under Spanish rule, while "a little corner of the earth, almost submerged in water, which subsisted entirely by its herring fishery, rose to be a formidable power" (Ch. CXXXV).

The revolt began as a defense of ancient constitutional privileges against Philip's absolutism, but it became inseparable from the religious conflict between [[calvinism]] and the Catholic monarchy. The Dutch Republic emerged as Europe's most successful experiment in republican government, a Protestant [[commerce|commercial]] power that would challenge the Spanish and Portuguese empires across the globe.

## Origins and Context

The Netherlands were an assemblage of seigniories subject to Philip II under various titles. Each province had its particular laws and usages. In Friesland and Groningen, all the sovereign was owed was a subsidy of six thousand crowns. No taxes could be laid on the towns, nor foreign troops maintained, without the consent of the three orders of the state. As Voltaire notes, the ancient constitution of Brabant even provided that if the sovereign violated their privileges, "the states shall be discharged from their oath of allegiance" (Ch. CXXXV).

Philip II, who ruled from Madrid and never visited the Netherlands in person, resolved to abolish these liberties. He moved to impose arbitrary taxes and introduce [[the-inquisition]], which he could not establish in either Naples or Milan. The result was the opposite of what he intended: "his cruel despotism proved the cause of their grandeur" (Ch. CXXXV).

## Key Features

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Federal structure** | Seven sovereign provinces united under the States-General |
| **Stadtholder** | Provincial governor; the prince of Orange held this office for multiple provinces |
| **Calvinist identity** | The Reformed religion was established in opposition to Spanish Catholicism |
| **Maritime power** | The herring fishery was the original foundation of Dutch commerce |
| **Religious toleration** | Liberty of conscience was proclaimed during the revolt |

The republic's naval power grew rapidly during the long war against Spain. The Dutch fleet contributed decisively to the destruction of the Spanish Armada and, over forty years, the Dutch proved themselves formidable at sea: "the republic was every day growing so formidable at sea, that she contributed greatly to destroy that fleet of Philip's, intitled the Invincible" (Ch. CXXXV).

## Historical Development

The revolt began in 1566 with the confederate lords presenting grievances at Brussels. Philip responded by sending the duke of Alva with Spanish and Italian troops and orders to "employ the cord as much as the sword" (Ch. CXXXV). The execution of the counts of Egmont and Horn, together with eighteen gentlemen, became the first cement of the republic.

William of Nassau, prince of Orange -- known as William the Silent -- emerged as the leader of the revolt. Though he had neither troops nor money, persecution supplied his resources. The siege and defense of Leyden in 1574 demonstrated the extraordinary measures the Dutch would take: they pierced the dykes and flooded the fields with the waters of the Issel, the Maese, and the Ocean, while a fleet of two hundred boats carried supplies over the Spanish entrenchments (Ch. CXXXV).

Philip's 1580 proscription of William of Orange placed a bounty of 25,000 crowns on his head. William was assassinated in 1584 by Balthazar Gerard, a religious enthusiast who claimed divine impulse. Yet the republic survived its founder. Under William's son, Prince Maurice, the seven provinces continued to resist Spain, eventually achieving de facto independence.

> Never was there a people less acquainted with delicacy. When Louisa de Coligni was upon her journey to the Hague, in order to be married to prince William, they were at a loss to find a coach for her. (Ch. CXXXV)

## Impact and Legacy

The Dutch Republic became the model of a commercial, tolerant Protestant state. Its rise demonstrated that a small nation, united by liberty and necessity, could resist the most powerful monarchy in Europe. As Voltaire concludes, "for the space of forty years these people resembled the Lacedemonians, who constantly repulsed the great king" (Ch. CXXXV). The same simplicity and equality prevailed in Amsterdam as in Sparta -- with greater sobriety. The republic's success reshaped the European balance of power and foreshadowed the decline of Spanish hegemony.

## Key Figures

- **William the Silent** -- Prince of Orange, leader of the revolt and Stadtholder; assassinated in 1584
- **Philip II** -- King of Spain whose despotism provoked the revolt
- **Duke of Alva** -- Spanish governor whose executions and the Tenth Penny tax drove the provinces to rebellion
- **Prince Maurice** -- Son of William the Silent, who continued the war as Stadtholder
