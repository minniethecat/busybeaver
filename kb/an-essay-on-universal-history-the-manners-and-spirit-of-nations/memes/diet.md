## Overview

The **Diet** (from Latin _dieta_, German _Reichstag_) was the general assembly of the estates of the [[empire|Holy Roman Empire]]. It brought together the electors, princes, counts, prelates, and representatives of the imperial cities to deliberate on matters of common concern — legislation, taxation, war, and, critically during the Reformation era, the religious settlement of the Empire. The Diet was the principal forum in which the tensions between imperial authority, territorial [[sovereignty|sovereignty]], and religious division were negotiated throughout the 16th century. (Ch. CVI, CX)

## Origins and Context

The Diet evolved from the informal assemblies of the early medieval period, at which the emperor consulted with leading nobles and bishops. Over time, these gatherings became increasingly formalized, with defined membership, procedures, and competences. By the late 15th century, the Diet had assumed its mature form as a tripartite assembly of electors, princes, and cities.

The Diet's authority derived from custom and the mutual recognition of the estates rather than from any written constitution. It was not a [[parliament|parliament]] in the modern sense: it had no power to initiate legislation independently of the emperor, and its decisions (recesses) required imperial ratification. Nevertheless, no emperor could govern effectively without the Diet's consent on major matters. (Ch. CVI)

## Key Features

| Aspect | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| Composition | Three colleges: Electors, Princes (spiritual and temporal), and Imperial Cities |
| Convocation | Summoned by the emperor at his discretion; no fixed schedule or location |
| Deliberation | Colleges deliberated separately; decisions required agreement of all three |
| Recess | The formal record of the Diet's decisions, promulgated at its conclusion |
| Religious Role | After 1521, the Diet became the primary venue for resolving the religious schism |

The Diet of the [[empire|Holy Roman Empire]] was unique among European representative institutions in its inclusion of ecclesiastical princes — bishops and abbots who held territorial sovereignty alongside their spiritual authority. This fusion of spiritual and temporal power was a distinctive feature of the imperial constitution. (Ch. CVI)

## Historical Development

The most consequential diets of the Reformation era were those at Worms (1521), Speyer (1526, 1529), and Augsburg (1530, 1555). The Diet of Worms summoned [[martin-luther|Martin Luther]] to answer for his teachings before Emperor [[charles-v|Charles V]], resulting in the Edict of Worms that declared Luther an outlaw. The term "Protestant" itself originated at the Diet of Speyer (1529), when Lutheran princes issued a formal protest against the Diet's decision to enforce the Edict of Worms.

The Diet of Augsburg (1530) saw the presentation of the Augsburg Confession, the foundational doctrinal statement of [[lutheranism|Lutheranism]]. The Peace of Augsburg (1555), concluded at the same venue, established the principle of _cuius regio, eius religio_ (whose realm, his religion), granting territorial rulers the right to determine the religion of their subjects. (Ch. CX)

> The reformation, or rather the change of religion, which was brought about in one half of Europe, was a work that had been long preparing. (Ch. CVI)

## Impact and Legacy

The Diet represented a distinctive path of political development, balancing imperial authority with territorial [[liberty|liberties]] and corporate representation. Its role in managing the religious schism — however imperfectly — prevented the Empire from descending into the kind of centralized religious warfare that afflicted France and the Netherlands.

The principle of religious settlement through political negotiation at the Diet established a precedent for the Peace of Westphalia (1648), which concluded the Thirty Years' War and is often regarded as the foundation of the modern international order. The Diet continued to meet until the dissolution of the Empire in 1806. (Ch. CX)
