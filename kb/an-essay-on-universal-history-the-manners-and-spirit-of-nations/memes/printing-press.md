## Overview

The printing press was the technological precondition of the Reformation. Without it, Luther's theses and the flood of Protestant pamphlets could not have reached the breadth of audience they did. Voltaire identifies printing as the German invention that "by this discovery alone they have multiplied human knowledge" (Ch. C). The technology arrived at a moment when Europe was ready for it: literacy was spreading, vernacular translations of [[scripture]] were in demand, and the infrastructure of booksellers and fairs was already in place.

Yet the early reception of printing was marked by superstition and fear. When the first printed books reached Paris, the agents of Johann Fust were accused of magic -- a reflection of how poorly understood the technology was even among the educated. Voltaire treats this episode as emblematic of the "gross ignorance which overwhelmed Europe, and which even the art of printing could not for a long time dispel" (Ch. C).

## Origins and Context

Printing with movable type was developed in the German Rhineland in the mid-15th century, associated above all with Johannes Gutenberg of Mainz and his financial backer Johann Fust. The first major product was the Gutenberg Bible. From Mainz the technology spread rapidly to Strasbourg, Cologne, Basel, and then across the Alps into Italy.

> The Germans had the honour of being the inventers of printing, much about the time that engraving was discovered, and by this discovery alone they have multiplied human knowledge. (Ch. C)

The timing was critical. The printing press emerged just as the Renaissance [[humanism]] had created a hunger for classical texts, and just before [[the-reformation]] would generate an unprecedented demand for vernacular Bibles and theological pamphlets. Voltaire notes that printing and engraving were discovered nearly simultaneously, and both contributed to the diffusion of learning.

## Impact and Legacy

Printing transformed the religious landscape of Europe. Every Reformer -- [[lutheranism|Luther]], [[calvinism|Calvin]], Zwingli -- depended on printed books and pamphlets to disseminate their ideas. The Catholic Church, which had initially welcomed printing for producing [[indulgences]], soon found it to be a vehicle for [[heresy]]. The [[counter-reformation]] responded with its own printed propaganda, but the damage was already done: the printed word had shattered the Church's near-monopoly on the transmission of knowledge.

As Voltaire observes, the first printed books arriving in Paris provoked such suspicion that "the parliament in 1474 ordered all the books to be seized" (Ch. C). It took the direct intervention of Louis XI to protect the book trade, ordering that the proprietors should be reimbursed -- an early instance of state protection for the diffusion of knowledge. Voltaire considers this a turning point: even as the magistrates acted from superstition, the crown recognized printing's value.

## Key Figures

- **Johannes Gutenberg** -- The inventor of European movable type printing in Mainz
- **Johann Fust** -- Gutenberg's financier; his agents carried the first printed editions to Paris
- **Louis XI** -- French king who protected the Paris book trade from parliamentary seizure in 1474
