## Overview

The Council of Trent (1545–1563) was the nineteenth ecumenical council of the Catholic Church and the defining institutional response to the [[the-reformation|Protestant Reformation]]. Convened in the episcopal city of Trent (Trento) in northern Italy, the council met in three distinct periods — 1545–1547, 1551–1552, and 1562–1563 — under the pontificates of Paul III, Julius III, and Pius IV. Its decrees shaped Catholic doctrine, discipline, and governance for the next four centuries.

Voltaire regarded the Council of Trent as a pivotal moment in European history: it defined Catholic orthodoxy with unprecedented precision, mandated episcopal residence, and created seminaries for the training of clergy. Yet he also observed that the council operated under tight papal control, marking the end of genuine conciliarism within the Catholic Church. By the time the council finally convened, half of Europe had already broken with Rome — a delay that Voltaire considered characteristic of papal resistance to reform. (Ch. CVI–CXVI)

## Origins and Context

The demand for a general council had been a recurring theme of the late medieval Church. The conciliarist tradition, which held that a general council possessed authority superior to that of the pope, reached its peak at the Council of Constance (1414–1418) but was effectively suppressed by the papacy in the fifteenth century. When [[luther-martin|Martin Luther]] appealed from the pope to a general council in 1518, he revived the conciliarist tradition — but the papacy's long resistance to councils meant that decades passed before one was convened.

The Fifth Lateran Council (1512–1517) was the last council before the Reformation. It addressed some reform issues but failed to grapple with the abuses that would trigger Luther's revolt. Pope [[leo-x|Leo X]] and his successor Clement VII both resisted calls for a council, fearing a revival of conciliarism and scrutiny of papal finances. It was only under Paul III, and after extensive diplomatic negotiations with the Emperor [[charles-v|Charles V]] and other sovereigns, that the council was finally convoked.

## Key Doctrinal Decrees

| Decree | Date | Content |
|--------|------|---------|
| Decree on Scripture and Tradition | 1546 | Affirmed both Scripture and unwritten traditions as sources of revelation |
| Decree on Original Sin | 1546 | Defined the transmission and remission of original sin through baptism |
| Decree on Justification | 1547 | Rejected Lutheran *sola fide*; affirmed faith formed by charity and cooperation with grace |
| Decree on the Sacraments | 1547 | Affirmed seven sacraments instituted by Christ |
| Decree on the Eucharist | 1551 | Defined transubstantiation as the conversion of the whole substance of bread and wine |
| Decree on Penance and Extreme Unction | 1551 | Affirmed auricular confession and the sacrament of the dying |
| Decree on Purgatory | 1563 | Reaffirmed purgatory but prohibited "difficult and subtle questions" from popular discourse |
| Decree on the Invocation of Saints | 1563 | Affirmed veneration of saints, relics, and images while curbing abuses |

## Institutional Reforms

The council's reform decrees addressed the administrative and pastoral abuses that had fueled the Reformation. The most significant included the obligation of bishops to reside in their dioceses, the prohibition of pluralism (holding multiple benefices simultaneously), and the creation of diocesan seminaries for the education of clergy. These reforms aimed to create a more disciplined and pastorally effective episcopate.

The decree *Tametsi* (1563) reformed marriage law, requiring the presence of a parish priest and two witnesses for validity — a measure directed against clandestine marriages. The council also mandated provincial and diocesan synods to ensure the implementation of its decrees at the local level.

## Impact and Legacy

The Council of Trent represented both the culmination of the conciliar tradition and its transformation. While the council produced the most comprehensive doctrinal and disciplinary reform in Catholic history, it operated under papal supervision in a manner that the fifteenth-century conciliarists would not have recognized. The post-Tridentine Church was more centralized, more doctrinally uniform, and more effectively governed than its medieval predecessor.

In France, the council's decrees were never formally received by the Crown — a legacy of [[gallican-church|Gallican]] resistance to papal authority. The Catholic League, led by the House of Guise, demanded the publication of the Council of Trent as part of its program against the [[huguenot|Huguenots]], but the French monarchy resisted, viewing Tridentine reception as an encroachment on the liberties of the Gallican Church. (Ch. CXV–CXVI)

The [[counter-reformation|Counter-Reformation]] that followed Trent reshaped the Catholic Church into a more disciplined instrument of pastoral governance. The creation of the Congregation of the Council (1564) to interpret and enforce Tridentine decrees exemplified the new institutional machinery. The post-Tridentine [[curia]] was reorganized by Pope Sixtus V into fifteen standing congregations, each with defined competence over a specific area of church governance.
