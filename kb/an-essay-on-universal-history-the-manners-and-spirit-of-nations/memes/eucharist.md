## Overview

The Eucharist — also called Holy Communion, the Lord's Supper, or the Mass — is the central act of Christian worship, instituted by Christ at the Last Supper with the words "This is my body... this is my blood." In the sixteenth century, the theological question of how Christ is present in the consecrated elements — or whether he is present at all — became one of the most divisive issues of the Reformation, splitting not only Catholics from Protestants but also Lutherans from the Reformed.

## Origins and Context

The medieval Church taught the doctrine of transubstantiation, formally defined at the Fourth Lateran Council (1215): that the substance of the bread and wine is changed into the body and blood of Christ, while the accidents (appearance, taste, texture) remain unchanged. The Mass was understood as a true sacrifice — the same sacrifice as Calvary, offered anew by the priest for the living and the dead. This sacrificial understanding supported the entire edifice of endowed Masses, chantry chapels, and the buying and selling of Masses for the souls in Purgatory. (Ch. CV)

## The Reformation Debate

The Reformation debate over the Eucharist unfolded along three positions. **Luther** maintained the real presence of Christ ("consubstantiation" — Christ's body is present "in, with, and under" the bread), rejecting transubstantiation as an Aristotelian philosophical distortion but insisting on the literal meaning of "This is my body." **Zwingli** interpreted the words symbolically: the Eucharist is a memorial, a sign of spiritual communion with Christ, not a physical consumption of his body. **Calvin** occupied a middle position: Christ is spiritually present, truly received by faith, but not physically located in the elements.

At the Colloquy of Marburg (1529), Luther and Zwingli debated the Eucharist face to face. Luther reportedly wrote "This is my body" on the table in chalk and refused all symbolic interpretations. The failure to reach agreement at Marburg permanently divided the Lutheran and Reformed branches of Protestantism. (Ch. CVI)

## Historical Development

The Mass, in Voltaire's description of the Church under Leo X, had become a spectacular ritual embedded in the opulence of Renaissance Rome. The reformist critique was twofold: theological (the Mass as sacrifice was unbiblical) and practical (the commercialization of Masses — buying endowed Masses for the dead — was corrupt). The Council of Trent (1545–1563) reaffirmed transubstantiation and the sacrificial character of the Mass, establishing the Tridentine Mass as the normative Catholic liturgy for the next four centuries.

## Impact and Legacy

The Eucharistic division had consequences far beyond theology. Legal penalties for "sacrilege" against the consecrated host, the requirement of annual Communion, and the use of Communion as a test of religious conformity made the Eucharist a matter of life and death. In England, the "Test Act" required Communion in the Church of England as a condition for holding public office, effectively excluding Catholics and Dissenters from political life until 1828–1829.
