## Overview

Penance, one of the seven sacraments of the Catholic Church, encompasses the confession of sins to a priest, the expression of contrition, the granting of absolution, and the performance of satisfaction (prescribed prayers or good works). In the sixteenth century, the sacrament of penance became a flashpoint of the Reformation, as reformers challenged both its theological foundation and its practical abuse — particularly the sale of [[indulgences]], which were marketed as substitutes for the satisfaction component of penance.

## Origins and Context

The sacrament of penance evolved over the early centuries of the Church. In the primitive Church, public confession and prolonged public penance were the norm for grave sins. By the medieval period, private auricular confession to a priest had become the standard practice, codified by the Fourth Lateran Council (1215), which required annual confession as a minimum obligation for all Christians.

The theology of penance rested on the priest's power to bind and loose — the authority conferred by Christ to forgive sins. This power, however, became entangled with the Church's temporal authority. The Inquisition, when it reconciled penitents, claimed the power of "taxing their sins, and receiving sums of money by way of surety for their repentance." (Ch. CXVIII)

## Indulgences and the Reformation Crisis

The indulgence system represented the most visible corruption of the penitential economy. Indulgences were remissions of temporal punishment for sin — the "satisfaction" component of penance — granted by the Church in exchange for pious works or monetary contributions. By the early sixteenth century, the sale of indulgences had become a lucrative fundraising mechanism. The papal agent Arcemboldi sold indulgences in Sweden and Denmark, while the campaign to fund St. Peter's Basilica through indulgence sales in Germany provoked Martin Luther's protest. (Ch. XCVIII)

Luther's 95 Theses (1517) directly attacked the abuse of indulgences and, by implication, the entire sacramental system of penance. The reformers argued that no priest could forgive sins by his own authority, that true contrition required no satisfaction beyond divine grace, and that the sale of indulgences was a fraud against the faithful.

## Historical Development

The Council of Trent (1545–1563) reaffirmed the sacrament of penance against Protestant critiques, maintaining that auricular confession and priestly absolution were instituted by Christ. The Council distinguished between the "matter" of the sacrament (the acts of the penitent: contrition, confession, satisfaction) and the "form" (the words of absolution spoken by the priest), insisting that both were necessary for the sacrament's validity.

## Impact and Legacy

The dispute over penance contributed to the permanent division of Western Christianity. In Protestant churches, confession became either a general communal act or a voluntary spiritual counsel, stripped of its sacramental status. The Catholic Church retained the sacrament but reformed its administration, emphasizing the seal of the confessional and the pastoral role of the priest as confessor rather than judge.
