## Overview

The Catholic League (French: *La Ligue* or *Sainte Ligue*) was a confederation of Catholic nobles, clergy, and urban factions formed in France during the [[french-wars-of-religion|French Wars of Religion]]. Initially conceived as a defense of the Catholic faith against the Protestant [[huguenot|Huguenots]], the League under the leadership of [[henry-i-duke-of-guise|Henry I, Duke of Guise]] evolved into a revolutionary movement that challenged the authority of the French crown itself.

Voltaire describes the League as a force that "tended to subvert the throne, and dismember the state." (Ch. CXXXVII) Its most radical element, the faction of the Sixteen (*les Seize*) in Paris, went so far as to propose transferring the French crown to the Spanish Infanta, making [[philip-ii|Philip II of Spain]] the effective sovereign of France. The League's rise and fall marked the most dangerous crisis of the French monarchy between the Hundred Years' War and the Revolution of 1789.

## Origins and Context

The League originated in the convergence of religious zeal and aristocratic ambition. The Peace of Monsieur (1576), which granted substantial concessions to the Huguenots, provoked a Catholic backlash. The first League was formed at Péronne in 1576 under the leadership of Henry de Guise, but it was the second League, proclaimed in 1584–1585, that became a decisive force in French politics. The trigger was the death of the Duke of Anjou, the king's younger brother, which made the Protestant [[henry-iv-of-france|Henry of Navarre]] heir presumptive to the French throne. (Ch. CXLII)

The League's religious pretext masked dynastic ambition. Henry de Guise, immensely popular in Paris, cultivated the image of a Catholic champion while positioning himself as an alternative to the Valois monarchy. "His admirers extol his magnanimity," Voltaire writes, though he notes the contrast with Guise's behavior at the body of Admiral [[coligny|Coligny]] after the St. Bartholomew's Day massacre. (Ch. CXLII)

## Historical Development

The League's formation was a direct response to the perceived weakness of [[henry-iii-of-france|Henry III]]. Voltaire paints a devastating portrait of the last Valois king: he "built monkish cells at Vincennes for himself, and for the companions of his pleasures," went about "habited in a white sack, he wore a disciplining whip, and a pair of beads at his girdle, and called himself brother Henry." (Ch. CXLII) This spectacle of royal piety without royal authority encouraged the League to act.

The **Day of the Barricades** (May 1588) demonstrated the League's control of Paris. Henry III was forced to flee his own capital. Later that year, in desperation, the king summoned Henry de Guise and his brother the Cardinal of Lorraine to the Château de Blois and had them assassinated. "The murder of a hero, and a priest rendered him odious, though not more formidable, in the eye of all the catholics." (Ch. CXLII)

The assassination backfired catastrophically. "Seventy doctors assembled in Sorbonne declare him cast from the throne, and his subjects released from the oath of allegiance. The priests refuse absolution to their penitents that recognize him as king." (Ch. CXLII) The faction of Sixteen imprisoned royalist members of the [[parliament|Parlement]] in the Bastille. The widowed Duchess of Guise publicly demanded justice for her murdered husband. Henry III, isolated and excommunicated, was forced to ally with Henry of Navarre — the Protestant heir he had been persecuting.

The two Henrys jointly besieged Paris in 1589. Before the city could fall, a Dominican friar named Jacques Clément assassinated Henry III. On his deathbed, the last Valois recognized Henry of Navarre as his successor, but the League refused to accept a Protestant king. The ensuing struggle lasted until 1594, when Henry IV's conversion to Catholicism — "Paris is well worth a Mass" — deprived the League of its religious rationale. "At the same time that he was conquering Portugal, maintaining the war in Flanders, and invading England, he encouraged the Holy League in France." (Ch. CXXXVII)

Philip II's ambitions reached their peak in 1593 when the Spanish ambassador proposed to the Estates-General in Paris that the Infanta Isabella Clara Eugenia be made queen of France. The Salic law barring female succession was to be abolished. "There were long debates about the means of abolishing the Salic law; and at length the infanta was proposed as queen to the states." (Ch. CXXXVII) The proposal ultimately failed due to French resistance to Spanish domination and the Duke of Mayenne's rival ambitions.

## Impact and Legacy

The Catholic League's defeat established the principle that religious conformity could not override the laws of royal succession. Henry IV's conversion and the [[edict-of-nantes|Edict of Nantes]] (1598) ended the League as a military force, though its ideology survived in the *dévot* party of the 17th century. The League's challenge to the monarchy contributed to the development of absolutist theory: the experience of near-state-collapse under the League strengthened the arguments of those who insisted that undivided sovereignty must reside in the king alone. (Ch. CXLII)

The League also demonstrated the revolutionary potential of combining religious ideology with urban popular mobilization — a pattern that would recur in the [[fronde|Fronde]] (1648–1653) and again in the [[french-revolution|French Revolution]]. The Sorbonne's declaration releasing subjects from allegiance to a heretical king established a precedent for resistance theory that both Catholic and Protestant polemicists would develop throughout the early modern period.
