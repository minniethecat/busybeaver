## Overview

The Inquisition was the institutional mechanism through which the medieval and early modern Catholic Church enforced doctrinal orthodoxy. While inquisitorial procedures dated to the 13th century, the Inquisition took on new significance in the 16th century as a weapon against the Protestant Reformation. Voltaire treats the Inquisition as emblematic of the intersection of religious authority and political power, chronicling its operations with characteristic irony and moral condemnation.

## Origins and Context

The medieval Inquisition was established to combat the Cathar and Waldensian heresies in southern France and northern Italy. In 1478, Pope Sixtus IV authorized Ferdinand and Isabella to establish the Spanish Inquisition, which operated with unusual independence from Rome. The Spanish tribunal focused initially on *conversos* (Jewish converts to Christianity suspected of secret Judaizing) before expanding to Protestants, Moriscos, and other groups.

Voltaire notes that the Inquisition's procedures inverted ordinary standards of justice: the accused did not know their accusers, testimony from criminals and excommunicates was admissible, and confiscation of property created powerful financial incentives for conviction.

## Key Features

The inquisitorial system was characterized by:

- **Secret Proceedings**: Accusations, testimony, and deliberations were conducted in secrecy; the accused had no right to confront accusers
- **Judicial Torture**: Applied to extract confessions, though under regulated conditions
- **Auto-da-Fé**: The public ceremony in which sentences were pronounced and those condemned were handed over to secular authorities for execution, typically by burning
- **Confiscation of Property**: The goods of convicted heretics were seized, often dividing between the Inquisition, the state, and the accuser
- **Limpieza de Sangre (Purity of Blood)**: In Spain, statutes requiring proof of "old Christian" ancestry for public office and certain professions

## Historical Development

The Spanish Inquisition reached its peak of activity under Tomás de Torquemada (Grand Inquisitor, 1483–1498). Thousands were executed in its first decades, and tens of thousands more were subjected to lesser penalties including imprisonment, penance, and confiscation. Voltaire describes its procedures with undisguised revulsion.

The Roman Inquisition, reorganized by Pope Paul III in 1542, was directed primarily against Protestant ideas in Italy. It succeeded in suppressing the Reformation in the Italian peninsula but operated with less severity than its Spanish counterpart.

Voltaire describes the Inquisition's persistence into his own era, noting how ancient institutions maintain themselves through habit long after their original justifications have been forgotten. His critique is not merely that the Inquisition was cruel, but that it represented a fundamental confusion of spiritual authority with coercive force.

## Impact and Legacy

The Inquisition's effects extended well beyond its immediate victims. It created a climate of intellectual caution in Catholic countries, where the expression of unorthodox ideas could bring ruinous consequences. The Index of Prohibited Books, administered by the Inquisition, restricted the circulation of Protestant and scientific works across much of Europe.

Yet Voltaire also observes the limits of inquisitorial power. The Inquisition could not prevent the spread of Protestantism in Northern Europe, nor the eventual triumph of Enlightenment values. By his own time, its authority had been significantly curtailed by secular rulers who resented its encroachments on state power.
