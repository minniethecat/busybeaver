## Overview

Superstition—the complex of irrational beliefs, ritual practices, and credulous attachments that Voltaire distinguished from rational religion—appears throughout the *Essay on Universal History* as a universal human failing that transcends cultural and confessional boundaries. Voltaire's treatment of superstition is characteristically double-edged: he condemns its cruelties while analyzing its social functions with anthropological detachment.

The Reformation itself was partly a campaign against superstition, particularly the cult of [[relics]] and the commercial exploitation of popular credulity. The council of Geneva proclaimed its abolition of "the yoke of antichrist, to abolish superstition and to recover our liberty." (Ch. CIX) Yet Voltaire notes that superstition persisted even among the most cultivated classes of Renaissance Europe, and that the reformers' own zeal could generate new forms of irrationality.

## Superstition in Catholic Europe

The late medieval church had, in Voltaire's view, systematically cultivated popular superstition as a source of revenue and authority. Forged [[relics]] were "made use of in several places to impose upon the credulity of the people." (Ch. XCVIII) The sale of [[indulgences]], the veneration of images, and the attribution of miraculous powers to shrines and saints all constituted what Voltaire regarded as a debased form of religion.

The [[the-reformation|Reformation]] did not immediately dispel these practices. In England, when [[henry-viii|Henry VIII]]'s commissioners began dissolving the monasteries, "in the market places in London several wooden images were burnt, which the monks used to put into motion by springs." The exposure of these mechanical frauds was accompanied by the more traumatic destruction of the shrine of St. Thomas of Canterbury, whose "revered ashes" were "thrown into the flames." (Ch. CXIII)

In France, the superstition of judicial astrology and witchcraft reached its zenith under [[catherine-de-medici|Catherine de Medici]]. "Never was the absurd notion of witchcraft in greater credit. It was a common thing to make wax figures, and prick them to the heart, uttering at the same time unintelligible words. They imagined thus to destroy their enemies; nor did the want of success undeceive them." (Ch. CXLII) The Florentine astrologer Luke Gauric, who "in our days would have been no better than a mountebank," was "a person of great consequence" at the French court. (Ch. CXLII)

## Superstition and the Reformers

The Protestant reformers were ambivalent toward popular superstition. They subjected relic veneration and the cult of saints to sustained criticism, arguing that such practices "lacked scriptural warrant and had degenerated into superstition and commercial exploitation." Yet the same zeal that drove iconoclasm could generate its own forms of fanaticism.

The [[anabaptism|Anabaptist]] kingdom of Münster provided Voltaire with a case study in how anti-superstitious reform could itself degenerate into a new superstition. Jan van Leiden, the journeyman tailor turned prophet-king, "assured them that God had appeared to him, and appointed him king: he said it, and they believed him." The medals he struck, the seventeen wives he married, and the execution of one wife for questioning his authority—all demonstrated "a virtue not uncommon among banditti and tyrants, which was valour," but also a credulity that mirrored the Catholic superstitions the Anabaptists had supposedly rejected. (Ch. CVIII)

## Comparative Superstition

One of the most distinctive features of Voltaire's treatment of superstition is its comparative scope. He finds equivalent phenomena across cultures, using the cross-cultural evidence to argue that superstition is a universal human tendency rather than the peculiar failing of any one religion.

**India**: "Amidst such a multitude of extravagant opinions, and whimsical superstitions, who could imagine that all those Pagans in India do acknowledge, as well as ourselves, a Being infinitely perfect?" The ancient Brahmins possessed sublime theological conceptions, yet "the meanest and most absurd superstitions prevail still in that country: a contrast which too often appeareth in human nature." (Ch. CXX)

Devotees threw themselves under the wheels of the idol Jaganath's chariot, and young widows were conducted to the funeral pyre where "after dancing and singing, they flung themselves into the flames upon the bodies of their deceased husbands." Yet "in all countries superstition inspires the people with a supernatural strength." (Ch. CXXIX)

**Japan**: The Japanese believed in witchcraft—"a superstition in which we believed a long time"—and practiced ordeals by fire and pilgrimages, while maintaining "liberty of conscience… as well as in all other parts of the East." The doctrine of [[confucius|Confucius]], "as the whole of it consists in plain morality," charmed the most intelligent part of the nation. (Ch. CXX)

**The Mogul Empire**: Under conquest and servitude, Indian superstition "grew much stronger; as that of the Egyptians grew much stronger after they were subdued by the Romans." The people still plunged into rivers during eclipses, persuaded that "the sun was falling into the claws of a dragon." (Ch. CXXVIII)

## Voltaire's Taxonomy of Superstition

From the historical evidence, Voltaire implicitly develops a taxonomy of superstition:

1. **Clerical fraud**: Mechanized images, forged relics, and staged miracles designed to extract money from the credulous
2. **Judicial superstition**: Witchcraft trials, ordeals, and astrological jurisprudence
3. **Popular ritualism**: Pilgrimages, relic veneration, and propitiatory practices divorced from moral content
4. **Philosophical superstition**: The belief systems of the learned—astrology, alchemy, talismanic magic—that persisted alongside genuine scientific achievement

Against all these, Voltaire poses not atheism but what he calls "sound philosophy"—a rational religion stripped of supernatural accretions, focused on morality rather than ritual, and tolerant of doctrinal diversity. The "being of beings, the supreme, the invisible, the incomprehensible" acknowledged by the ancient Brahmins represented for Voltaire the universal rational core that superstition everywhere obscured. (Ch. CXX)
