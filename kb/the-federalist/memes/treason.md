# Treason

**Treason** is the most serious offense against the state — defined in Article III, Section 3 of the United States Constitution as consisting "only in levying War against them, or in adhering to their Enemies, giving them Aid and Comfort." It is the only crime explicitly defined in the Constitution, reflecting the Framers' determination to prevent the expansive and abusive use of treason charges that had characterized English law.

## Constitutional Definition

The Constitution's treason clause appears in the same article that establishes the federal judiciary. Madison, in Federalist No. 43, explained the provision as part of the broader scheme of defining federal judicial authority:

> "To declare the punishment of treason, but no attainder of treason shall work corruption of blood, or forfeiture except during the life of the person attainted." [043-009]

The clause contains three critical protections:

1. **Narrow definition**: Treason is limited to two specific acts — levying war and adhering to enemies
2. **Two-witness requirement**: Conviction requires the testimony of two witnesses to the same overt act, or confession in open court
3. **No corruption of blood**: Punishment cannot extend to the offender's heirs — a repudiation of the English practice of attainder

> As treason may be committed against the United States, the power of punishing it is necessarily vested in the national tribunals. [043-010]

Madison's discussion situated the treason clause within the enumeration of federal powers, treating it as an essential component of national sovereignty rather than a mere criminal provision.

## The Two-Witness Rule

Hamilton, writing in Federalist No. 84, emphasized the importance of the two-witness requirement as a protection against politically motivated prosecutions:

> No person shall be convicted of treason, unless on the testimony of two witnesses to the same overt act, or on confession in open court. [084-006]

This evidentiary requirement — more demanding than the standard for any other federal crime — was designed to prevent the kind of constructive treason prosecutions that had been used to suppress political opposition in England, where mere words or writings could constitute treason. By requiring two witnesses to the same overt act, the Constitution ensured that treason convictions would rest on unambiguous evidence of actual conduct rather than on the inferences and insinuations of political adversaries.

## Executive Clemency and Treason

In Federalist No. 74, Hamilton discussed the president's pardon power in relation to treason, arguing that the executive should possess the authority to grant reprieves and pardons for this offense:

> The expediency of vesting the power of pardoning in the President is, in relation to the crime of treason, that in seasons of insurrection or rebellion, there are often critical moments when a well-timed offer of pardon may restore the tranquility of the commonwealth. [074-006]

Hamilton noted that while some critics argued treason should be excepted from the pardon power — requiring legislative concurrence — the need for swift executive action in times of crisis outweighed this concern. A legislature could not convene and deliberate with the speed necessary to exploit a fleeting opportunity for reconciliation.

## Impeachment and Treason

Treason appears in the Constitution not only as a crime but also as a ground for impeachment. Article II, Section 4 provides that the President, Vice President, and all civil officers shall be removed upon impeachment for and conviction of "Treason, Bribery, or other high Crimes and Misdemeanors."

Hamilton addressed this in Federalist No. 69:

> The President of the United States would be liable to be impeached, tried, and, upon conviction of treason, bribery, or other high crimes or misdemeanors, removed from office. [069-006]

The juxtaposition of treason, bribery, and "other high crimes and misdemeanors" reinforced the gravity of the offense — it was listed first among the impeachable offenses, reflecting its status as the most serious breach of public trust.

## Protection Against Constructive Treason

The Federalist argument for the treason clause was part of a broader concern with preventing the manipulation of criminal law for political ends. The English experience — in which "constructive treason" had been expanded by judicial interpretation to encompass a wide range of conduct never contemplated by statute — served as a cautionary example. By inscribing the definition of treason in the constitutional text itself, and by limiting it to two specific overt acts, the Framers sought to place the offense beyond the reach of legislative or judicial expansion.

## Historical Context

The constitutional treatment of treason reflects the Framers' familiarity with English legal history, in which treason charges had been used as instruments of royal and parliamentary tyranny. By defining treason narrowly and imposing procedural safeguards, the Constitution sought to prevent the American republic from replicating these abuses while still preserving the government's capacity to punish genuine threats to national security.
