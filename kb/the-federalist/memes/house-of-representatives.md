The House of Representatives is the lower chamber of the United States Congress, established by Article I of the Constitution and defended in the Federalist Papers as the branch of government most directly accountable to the people. With its two-year terms and apportionment by population, the House embodies the principle of popular sovereignty at the national level.

## Constitutional Design

### Direct Popular Election

The House is the only branch of the original constitutional government directly elected by the people. Publius emphasizes this popular foundation, noting that the House "will derive its powers from the people of America" (039-014). This direct electoral connection was designed to ensure that the federal government remained responsive to the citizenry — a sharp departure from the Confederation Congress, whose members were appointed by state legislatures and thus insulated from direct popular accountability.

### Short Terms and Frequent Elections

Two-year terms, with the entire House elected at each cycle, ensure that representatives remain closely tied to their constituents. Frequent elections serve as a check on representatives: if they fail to represent their districts adequately, they can be promptly replaced. In Federalist No. 52, Madison explains that the two-year term balanced the need for representatives to acquire sufficient knowledge of federal affairs against the republican principle that "the House of Representatives should have an immediate dependence on, and an intimate sympathy with, the people" (052-005). He noted that the two-year term was actually longer than the annual elections common in most state legislatures, but shorter than the terms of the President and Senate — a middle ground appropriate for the chamber most sensitive to public opinion.

### Apportionment by Population

Unlike the Senate, where each state has equal representation, the House apportions seats based on population. This ensures that more populous states have proportionally greater influence in the legislative process. The decennial census — mandated by the Constitution — provides the mechanism for periodic reapportionment as population shifts. Madison addresses the apportionment scheme in Federalist No. 55, defending the initial ratio of one representative per thirty thousand inhabitants and predicting that the House would grow with the population — as it has, from 65 members in the First Congress to 435 today.

### Qualifications

The Constitution requires that representatives be at least 25 years old, have been citizens for at least seven years, and reside in the state they represent. Publius argues that these qualifications are "reasonable and proper," neither unduly restrictive nor dangerously lax — they ensure a minimum level of maturity and attachment to the country while leaving the choice of representatives largely to the voters' discretion (052-003).

## Relationship to the Senate

The House and Senate were designed as complementary chambers with distinct characters. Where the Senate provides stability, experience, and deliberation, the House provides democratic energy and responsiveness. Publius argues that the Senate serves as "an additional impediment" against "improper acts of legislation" (062-008), while the House ensures that the people's will is directly represented.

This bicameral structure means that legislation must pass through two differently constituted bodies before becoming law — a structural safeguard against hasty or faction-driven lawmaking. The House, with its frequent elections and larger membership, is more susceptible to popular passions; the Senate, with its longer terms and smaller size, can "suspend the blow" until reason and deliberation prevail.

## Exclusive Powers

The Constitution grants the House several exclusive powers that collectively establish it as the branch closest to the people and the guardian of the public purse. The most fundamental is the origination of revenue bills: all tax and spending legislation must originate in the House, ensuring that the chamber most immediately accountable to the electorate controls the power of taxation. This provision, derived from British parliamentary practice, reflects the principle that "the immediate representatives of the people" should control the raising and spending of public money — a principle that gives the House its distinctive constitutional character and its leverage over the other branches.

The House also holds the sole power to impeach federal officers, serving as the grand jury of the nation. In Federalist No. 65, Hamilton explains that the House — as "the accusers of the people" — is the appropriate body to initiate impeachment proceedings, while the Senate — with its greater "coolness" and "deliberation" — is the appropriate body to try them. This division of the impeachment power between the two chambers embodies the bicameral logic of the Constitution: the House supplies the democratic energy to accuse, the Senate supplies the judicial temperament to judge. Finally, the House serves as the tiebreaker in presidential elections: if no candidate receives a majority of electoral votes, the House chooses the President, with each state delegation casting one vote. This contingency provision ensures that a President will always be chosen even in a fragmented electoral field, while preserving a role for the most democratic branch in the selection of the chief executive.

## The Extended Republic and Representation

In Federalist No. 10, Madison argues that the extended republic — made possible in part by a House that represents diverse districts across a large territory — helps control the effects of faction. A larger republic encompasses a greater variety of interests, making it more difficult for any single faction to form a majority and oppress others (010-023). The House, by representing these diverse interests in a single deliberative assembly, becomes the institutional mechanism through which the extended republic governs itself.

## Debate over the Size of the House

Anti-Federalists objected that the House was too small to adequately represent a diverse and growing nation. Madison responded in Federalist No. 55 that the initial size of 65 members was adequate for the first Congress and that the Constitution provided for the House to grow with the population. He also warned that "had every Athenian citizen been a Socrates, every Athenian assembly would still have been a mob" — suggesting that a larger assembly is not necessarily a wiser one (055-009). The House must be large enough to represent diverse interests but small enough to deliberate effectively.
