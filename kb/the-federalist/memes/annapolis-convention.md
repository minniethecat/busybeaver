# Annapolis Convention

The **Annapolis Convention** (formally the Meeting of Commissioners to Remedy Defects of the Federal Government) was a gathering of delegates from five states held in Annapolis, Maryland, in September 1786. Though sparsely attended and brief, the Convention's report — drafted by [[Alexander Hamilton]] — called for a broader convention in Philadelphia the following year, directly precipitating the [[Constitutional Convention]] of 1787. The Federalist Papers, written to defend the [[Constitution]] produced by that Philadelphia Convention, are thus traceable in lineage to Annapolis.

## Overview

The Annapolis Convention convened on September 11, 1786, with delegates from only five states present: New York, New Jersey, Pennsylvania, Delaware, and Virginia. Four other states had appointed commissioners who failed to arrive in time, and four states appointed no delegates at all. Faced with this inadequate representation, the delegates declined to proceed with their original mandate — negotiating commercial agreements among the states — and instead adopted an address calling for a general convention "to render the constitution of the Federal Government adequate to the exigencies of the Union."

## In The Federalist Papers

[[James Madison]] references the Annapolis Convention in Federalist No. 40, where he defends the Constitutional Convention's authority to propose a new frame of government rather than merely amending the [[Articles of Confederation]]. The Annapolis recommendation, Madison argues, called for "such further provisions as should appear to them necessary to render the constitution of the federal government adequate to the exigencies of the Union" — language broad enough to authorize fundamental change.[^1]

> The act from Annapolis recommends the "appointment of commissioners to take into consideration the situation of the United States; to devise SUCH FURTHER PROVISIONS as shall appear to them necessary to render the Constitution of the federal government ADEQUATE TO THE EXIGENCIES OF THE UNION; and to report such an act for that purpose, to the United States in Congress assembled, as when agreed to by them, and afterwards confirmed by the legislature of every State, will effectually provide for the same."
>
> (Federalist No. 40, quoting the Annapolis report)

Madison uses the Annapolis precedent to rebut the charge that the Philadelphia Convention exceeded its mandate: if a convention of only five states could legitimately call for a general constitutional convention, surely twelve states acting on that call could propose the reforms the crisis demanded.

## Background

The [[Articles of Confederation]], adopted in 1781, had proven inadequate for governing the new nation. Congress lacked the power to tax, regulate interstate commerce, or compel state compliance with treaties. By 1786, the Confederation was in crisis: states erected trade barriers against each other, the national debt went unpaid, and Shays' Rebellion in Massachusetts exposed the government's inability to maintain order.

The immediate catalyst for Annapolis was a dispute between Virginia and Maryland over navigation rights on the Potomac River. The Mount Vernon Conference of 1785 resolved this dispute and led Virginia to propose a broader meeting of states to discuss commercial regulation — the meeting that became the Annapolis Convention.

## The Annapolis Report

The Convention's report, drafted principally by [[Alexander Hamilton]], was adopted on September 14, 1786. It identified "important defects in the system of the Federal Government" and recommended that the states appoint commissioners to meet in Philadelphia on the second Monday in May 1787 "to take into consideration the situation of the United States" and "devise such further provisions as shall appear to them necessary." The report was transmitted to Congress and the state legislatures. Congress endorsed the call on February 21, 1787, formally summoning the [[Constitutional Convention]].

## The Road from Annapolis to Philadelphia

The Annapolis Convention sits at the midpoint of a three-stage process that transformed American governance:

1. **Mount Vernon Conference (1785)**: A bilateral agreement between Virginia and Maryland on Potomac River navigation rights demonstrated that interstate cooperation was possible and desirable — creating momentum for the broader Annapolis meeting.

2. **Annapolis Convention (1786)**: Recognizing that five states could not negotiate a comprehensive commercial settlement, the delegates pivoted from their original mandate and proposed a general constitutional convention. This was a calculated escalation: the Confederation's defects were too deep for piecemeal commercial reform.

3. **Constitutional Convention (1787)**: Congress endorsed the Annapolis recommendation, and twelve states (all but Rhode Island) sent delegates to Philadelphia. The result was not an amended Articles of Confederation but an entirely new frame of government — the [[Constitution]] that The Federalist Papers defended.

This three-stage sequence illustrates a recurring pattern in the founding era: institutional failure at one level was met not by despair but by citizens convening outside formal structures to propose structural solutions. The [[the-federalist-papers|The Federalist Papers]] themselves would later embody the same pattern — a coordinated public argument designed to secure ratification when the Constitution's fate hung in the balance.

## Legacy

The Annapolis Convention succeeded not in its stated purpose — commercial reform — but in its unintended consequence: it launched the process that produced the [[Constitution]]. The logic of Annapolis — that informal, unauthorized assemblies of citizens could legitimately propose fundamental constitutional change — became a key justification for the Philadelphia Convention's work and, by extension, the system of government The Federalist Papers defend. The Annapolis-to-Philadelphia-to-ratification chain illustrates the founding generation's practical approach to constitutional reform: when existing forms proved inadequate, they created new ones.
