# Ex Post Facto Law

An **ex post facto law** is a law that retroactively criminalizes conduct that was legal when performed, increases the punishment for a crime after its commission, or alters the rules of evidence to make conviction easier. The United States Constitution prohibits both Congress (Article I, Section 9) and the states (Article I, Section 10) from passing ex post facto laws, establishing one of the few absolute prohibitions in the original Constitution.

## In the Federalist Papers

The Federalist Papers treat the prohibition against ex post facto laws as a fundamental bulwark of liberty. Madison, writing in Federalist No. 44, grouped ex post facto laws with bills of attainder and laws impairing the obligation of contracts as measures "contrary to the first principles of the social compact":

> Bills of attainder, ex post facto laws, and laws impairing the obligation of contracts, are contrary to the first principles of the social compact, and to every principle of sound legislation. [044-008]

Madison argued that the sober people of America had grown weary of "fluctuating policy" and "sudden changes and legislative interferences" in matters affecting personal rights. The prohibition was a "constitutional bulwark in favor of personal security and private rights" [044-008].

## The Prohibition's Constitutional Scope

The Constitution contains two distinct prohibitions on ex post facto laws. Article I, Section 9, Clause 3 prohibits Congress from passing such laws: "No Bill of Attainder or ex post facto Law shall be passed." Article I, Section 10, Clause 1 extends the same prohibition to the states: "No State shall ... pass any Bill of Attainder, ex post facto Law, or Law impairing the Obligation of Contracts."

The prohibition was among the specific textual limitations on legislative power that, Hamilton argued in Federalist No. 78, demonstrated the necessity of an independent judiciary. He described a limited Constitution as one that "contains certain specified exceptions to the legislative authority; such, for instance, as that it shall pass no bills of attainder, no ex post facto laws, and the like" [078-011]. Judicial review was the mechanism by which such prohibitions could be enforced.

## Habeas Corpus and Ex Post Facto: Twin Protections

Hamilton, in Federalist No. 84, identified the prohibition of ex post facto laws alongside the writ of habeas corpus as among the most important securities for individual liberty:

> The establishment of the writ of habeas corpus, the prohibition of ex post facto laws, and of TITLES OF NOBILITY, to which we have no corresponding provision in our Constitution, are perhaps greater securities to liberty and republicanism than any it contains. [084-007]

Hamilton further observed that "the creation of crimes after the commission of the fact, or, in other words, the subjecting of men to punishment for things which, when they were done, were breaches of no law, and the practice of arbitrary imprisonments, have been, in all ages, the favorite and most formidable instruments of tyranny" [084-007]. This passage links the ex post facto prohibition to the broader theme of preventing arbitrary government.

## The State Prohibition and the Contracts Clause

Madison's defense of the state-level prohibition in Federalist No. 44 emphasized that the ban on ex post facto laws was part of a trio of restrictions — along with the prohibition on bills of attainder and laws impairing the obligation of contracts — designed to protect "personal security and private rights" from legislative abuse [044-008]. The inclusion of the Contracts Clause alongside the ex post facto prohibition reflected the Framers' understanding that retroactive civil legislation could be as destructive to liberty as retroactive criminal legislation.

## The Social Compact Foundation

Madison grounded the prohibition in the theory of the social compact, arguing that retroactive legislation violates the fundamental premise of lawful government — that citizens are governed by known, pre-existing rules. A law that punishes conduct after the fact is indistinguishable from arbitrary decree, because the citizen could not have known, at the time of acting, that the conduct was prohibited. This reasoning places the ex post facto clause in the tradition of Enlightenment legal thought, which insisted that law must be prospective, general, and knowable.

## Anti-Federalist Concerns

Anti-Federalist writers worried that the Constitution's list of prohibitions, including the ex post facto clause, was insufficient to protect individual liberty. They argued that a bill of rights was necessary precisely because the specific textual prohibitions in Article I, Section 9 might not cover all varieties of arbitrary legislative action. The Federalist response — that an enumerated bill of rights was unnecessary and potentially dangerous because it might imply that unenumerated rights did not exist — was ultimately rejected by the ratifying conventions, leading to the adoption of the Bill of Rights.

## Legacy and Significance

The ex post facto clause was among the first provisions of the Constitution to receive judicial interpretation. In *Calder v. Bull* (1798), the Supreme Court held that the clause applies only to criminal laws, not civil legislation — a distinction that remains foundational. The clause has been invoked in contexts ranging from sex offender registration laws to changes in sentencing guidelines, with the Court consistently holding that the prohibition applies to laws that impose punishment, not to laws that impose civil regulatory burdens.
