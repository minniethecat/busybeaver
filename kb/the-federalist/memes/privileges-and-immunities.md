# Privileges and Immunities

The **Privileges and Immunities Clause** — Article IV, Section 2 of the Constitution — provides that "the Citizens of each State shall be entitled to all Privileges and Immunities of Citizens in the several States." This clause, inherited from the Articles of Confederation but substantially refined in the Constitution, is a fundamental guarantee of interstate citizenship equality. In the Federalist Papers, Madison and Hamilton each addressed the clause, emphasizing its role in binding the states into a single national community.

## From the Articles of Confederation to the Constitution

The Articles of Confederation contained a similar provision, but Madison, in Federalist No. 42, identified its defects. The Confederation's language was vague to the point of incoherence:

> The dissimilarity in the rules of naturalization has long been remarked as a fault in our system... In the fourth article of the Confederation, it is declared "that the FREE INHABITANTS of each of these States, paupers, vagabonds, and fugitives from justice, excepted, shall be entitled to all privileges and immunities of FREE CITIZENS in the several States; and THE PEOPLE of each State shall, in every other, enjoy all the privileges of trade and commerce," etc. There is a confusion of language here, which is remarkable. [042-019]

Madison noted the problem: by using "free inhabitants" in one part and "free citizens" in another, the Confederation's text created uncertainty about who was entitled to what. Worse, it gave each state the power to effectively naturalize aliens for all other states — an "improper power" that the Constitution corrected by granting Congress uniform authority over naturalization. [042-019]

The constitutional clause, by contrast, was drafted with precision: it spoke of "citizens" — a term whose meaning would be fixed by a uniform federal rule of naturalization — and it applied to "the several States," ensuring that no state could discriminate against citizens of another in fundamental matters of rights or livelihood.

## The National Judiciary as Enforcer

Hamilton, in Federalist No. 80, argued that the privileges and immunities clause required a national judiciary to enforce it. Without federal courts, the equal treatment of citizens across state lines would depend on the goodwill of state tribunals — which could not be counted upon to remain impartial in disputes involving their own citizens against outsiders. Hamilton placed the clause among the "provisions expressly contained in the articles of Union" that demanded a federal forum:

> It may be esteemed the basis of the Union, that "the citizens of each State shall be entitled to all the privileges and immunities of citizens of the several States." And if it be a just principle that every government ought to possess the means of executing its own provisions by its own authority, it will follow, that in order to the inviolable maintenance of that equality of privileges and immunities to which the citizens of the Union will be entitled, the national judiciary ought to preside. [080-011]

Hamilton reasoned that only a tribunal "having no local attachments" and "owing its official existence to the Union" could impartially adjudicate disputes between citizens of different states. He identified this as one of the cases in which "the State tribunals cannot be supposed to be impartial and unbiased" [080-003] — a class of disputes that, by its nature, demanded a federal forum. The privileges and immunities clause was thus both a substantive guarantee and a jurisdictional grant: it defined the right, and it implied the institutional means for its enforcement.

## The Federalist Vision of National Citizenship

Together, Madison and Hamilton's analysis reveals the clause as more than a technical provision. It was a structural element of the compound republic: the privileges and immunities clause, enforced by the federal judiciary, created a baseline of citizenship rights that no state could deny to citizens of another state. This was the foundation on which the Fourteenth Amendment would later build, extending the principle from interstate comity to federal protection of fundamental rights against state abridgment.

Hamilton's argument that the clause was "the basis of the Union" [080-011] was not rhetorical excess. Without a guarantee of equal treatment across state lines, the Union would be a hollow shell — a league of separate jurisdictions rather than a single national community. The clause, in conjunction with the federal courts, transformed American citizenship from a collection of state-specific statuses into a truly national identity.
