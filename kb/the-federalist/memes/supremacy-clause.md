# Supremacy Clause

The **Supremacy Clause** (Article VI, Clause 2 of the United States Constitution) establishes that the Constitution, federal laws made pursuant to it, and treaties are "the supreme Law of the Land," binding on state judges notwithstanding anything in state constitutions or laws to the contrary. Together with [[federalism]]'s enumeration of federal powers and the [[separation-of-powers|separation of powers]], the Supremacy Clause defines the hierarchical relationship between national and state law — a relationship [[alexander-hamilton|Hamilton]] defended as essential to effective government in [[the-federalist-papers|The Federalist]].

## Definition

The Supremacy Clause resolves conflicts between federal and state law by declaring federal law supreme within its constitutional sphere. It does not grant the federal government unlimited power — the federal government may act only within its enumerated powers — but where it acts constitutionally, its laws preempt conflicting state laws. The Clause is the textual foundation of the doctrine of **preemption**: when federal and state law conflict, federal law prevails.

## In The Federalist Papers

Hamilton addresses the Supremacy Clause most directly in Federalist No. 33, where he defends the "necessary and proper" clause and the Supremacy Clause against Anti-Federalist charges that they would create an unlimited national government. He argues that supremacy is inherent in the very concept of law:

> But it is said that the laws of the Union are to be the supreme law of the land. But what inference can be drawn from this, or what would they amount to, if they were not to be supreme? It is evident they would amount to nothing. A LAW, by the very meaning of the term, includes supremacy. It is a rule which those to whom it is prescribed are bound to observe.
>
> (033-009)

Hamilton's argument is structural: if Congress may constitutionally legislate on a subject, then its laws must be supreme over conflicting state laws; otherwise, "every state government may at its pleasure annul the laws of the Union" (033-009). Without supremacy, the federal government "would be a mere treaty, dependent upon the good faith of the parties" — precisely the fatal defect of the Articles of Confederation.

Madison addresses supremacy in Federalist No. 44, arguing that the clause is "a necessary consequence of the institution of a federal government." He dismisses fears that it imperils the states: federal supremacy operates only within the "limited portion" of power granted to the national government; outside that sphere, the states retain their own supremacy.

## Constitutional Text

Article VI, Clause 2:

> This Constitution, and the Laws of the United States which shall be made in Pursuance thereof; and all Treaties made, or which shall be made, under the Authority of the United States, shall be the supreme Law of the Land; and the Judges in every State shall be bound thereby, any Thing in the Constitution or Laws of any State to the Contrary notwithstanding.

Key phrases:
- **"made in Pursuance thereof"** — only constitutional federal laws enjoy supremacy; unconstitutional ones are void.
- **"the Judges in every State shall be bound thereby"** — the clause is directed at state courts, making them enforcers of federal supremacy.
- **"any Thing... to the Contrary notwithstanding"** — the strongest possible language of preemption.

## Anti-Federalist Objections

Anti-Federalists saw the Supremacy Clause as a weapon of consolidation. Brutus warned that combined with the "necessary and proper" clause, it would allow Congress to "pass any law which they may think proper" and render state constitutions "a mere dead letter." The Federal Farmer argued that the clause, together with federal judicial power, would "in the end" annihilate the states. Publius responded that these fears confused *constitutional* federal supremacy (within enumerated limits) with *unlimited* supremacy — the former was essential to any real union; the latter was precluded by the enumeration of federal powers.

## Legacy

The Supremacy Clause is the constitutional foundation of American federalism's hierarchical dimension. It was central to Chief Justice John Marshall's reasoning in *McCulloch v. Maryland* (1819), where the Court held that Maryland could not tax the Bank of the United States because "the power to tax involves the power to destroy." It underpins modern preemption doctrine, determining when federal statutes impliedly displace state tort law, environmental regulations, and consumer protections. The Supremacy Clause converts the Constitution from a treaty among sovereigns into a body of law enforceable by courts — the defining innovation of American constitutionalism.
