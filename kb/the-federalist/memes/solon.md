# Solon (c. 638–558 BCE)

## Role in The Federalist

Solon appears in The Federalist Papers as the Athenian counterpart to Lycurgus — the lawgiver who established a constitution balancing popular participation with institutional checks. Madison invokes Solon in Federalist No. 38 to illustrate a broader point about constitutional founding: the most celebrated lawgivers of antiquity "had no historical models to guide them" and yet produced constitutions that endured for centuries (038-005).

In Federalist No. 63, Madison reflects on the Athenian constitution attributed to Solon, noting that the Areopagus — Athens's council of elders — served as a check on "the tyranny of their own passions" in the popular assembly (063-009). This example supports Madison's argument that the Senate, with its longer terms and smaller size, would provide a similar stabilizing function in the American constitutional system.

## Biography

Solon was an Athenian statesman, poet, and lawgiver active around 594 BCE. Athens at the time was riven by conflict between wealthy aristocrats and impoverished debtors. Solon was elected archon (chief magistrate) with a mandate to reform the constitution. He canceled debts, abolished debt slavery, reorganized the citizen body into property classes, and established new political institutions including the Council of Four Hundred and a popular law court (the Heliaea).

His reforms were deliberately moderate — "I gave the people as much privilege as was sufficient, neither taking away nor adding to their rights" — and he reportedly left Athens for ten years after enacting his constitution to prevent anyone from pressuring him to change it. His constitution was not fully democratic by modern standards, but it broke the aristocracy's monopoly on political power and laid the foundation for the later Athenian democracy.

## Legacy

Solon, along with Lycurgus, became one of the twin pillars of classical constitutionalism in the Framers' education. His example supported the argument that a constitution should balance democratic participation with institutional checks — precisely the balance the American Constitution sought to achieve. His name continues to be invoked in debates about constitutional reform and the wisdom of moderation in political change.
