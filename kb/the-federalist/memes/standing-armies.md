# Standing Armies

## Definition

A **standing army** is a permanent, professional military force maintained by the government during peacetime. In the founding era, standing armies were widely regarded as instruments of tyranny — the means by which despots suppressed liberty and imposed their will. The English Civil War, Cromwell's Protectorate, and the Stuart monarchy had all demonstrated the danger. The Federalist Papers address this fear directly, arguing that the Constitution's combination of congressional funding control, biennial military appropriations, and the militia provides adequate safeguards.

## In The Federalist Papers

Hamilton devotes Federalist Nos. 24–29 to the subject of national defense and the standing army question, acknowledging the widespread "jealousy" of standing armies while arguing that the Constitution provides unprecedented protections against military tyranny.

Hamilton opens by confronting the fear head-on: the Constitution does not prohibit standing armies in peacetime; instead, it prohibits Congress from appropriating money for the army "for a longer term than two years" (024-013). This compromise — permitting a standing army while requiring biennial legislative reauthorization — reflects the Framers' judgment that a total prohibition on peacetime armies would leave the nation defenseless while unlimited military appropriations would invite tyranny.

> To render an army unnecessary, will be a more certain method of preventing its existence than a thousand prohibitions upon paper.
>
> (029-005)

The two-year appropriation limit is the key constitutional safeguard. Hamilton explains that "the whole power of raising armies is lodged in the legislature, not in the executive" — unlike the British system, where the Crown held the prerogative of raising forces. Congress, representing the people, must periodically reauthorize the army's funding; if the army threatens liberty, the people's representatives can simply refuse to fund it. This "legislative check" transforms the standing army from a monarchical instrument into a republican one.

Hamilton further argues that a standing army is practically necessary for American security. The United States shares borders with British Canada and Spanish Florida; its coastline is vulnerable to naval attack; and the frontier requires protection against raids. A militia alone cannot provide "the necessary degree of military science and discipline" required for effective defense (029-009). The "best possible security against standing armies" is not their prohibition but their combination with "a well-regulated militia" composed of the citizenry (029-009).

## Historical Context

Eighteenth-century British political thought was deeply hostile to standing armies. The English Bill of Rights of 1689 declared that "the raising or keeping a standing army within the kingdom in time of peace, unless it be with consent of Parliament, is against law." The American Revolution had been fought in part against the stationing of British troops in the colonies without colonial consent (a grievance listed in the Declaration of Independence). State constitutions typically limited or prohibited standing armies.

Yet the Articles of Confederation's reliance on state militias had proved disastrous during the Revolution and Shays' Rebellion. The Framers concluded that a professional military, subject to civilian control through the funding power, was safer than a prohibition that would leave the nation dependent on ad hoc forces.

## Key Arguments

Hamilton's case for the constitutional approach to standing armies rests on three pillars:

1. **Legislative funding control is the ultimate safeguard**: Congress must reauthorize military appropriations every two years. If the army becomes threatening, the people's representatives can defund it. This is "a great and real security" unknown to European monarchies (026-006).

2. **Geographic necessity demands military readiness**: American borders are exposed; the Atlantic coast is vulnerable; the western frontier requires defense. A militia, however virtuous, cannot provide the continuous training and rapid response that a professional army can (029-009).

3. **A well-regulated militia complements the standing army**: The militia — armed citizens organized by the states — serves as both a supplement to the professional army and a check on it. An armed populace makes military tyranny "impracticable" in a way that no constitutional provision alone could ensure (029-009).

## Constitutional Implementation

The military powers are distributed across Articles I and II:
- **Article I, Section 8**: Congress has power to "raise and support Armies" but "no Appropriation of Money to that Use shall be for a longer Term than two Years"; to "provide and maintain a Navy"; and to provide for "organizing, arming, and disciplining the Militia."
- **Article II, Section 2**: The President is Commander in Chief, but Congress alone declares war and funds the military.
- **Third Amendment**: No soldier shall be quartered in any house without the owner's consent — directly addressing a grievance from the colonial era.

## Legacy and Significance

The Framers' compromise has proved durable. The United States maintains the world's most powerful standing military, but it is funded through annual and biennial congressional appropriations, commanded by a civilian president, and subject to legislative oversight. The militia concept evolved into the National Guard — state-organized forces that can be federalized when needed. The anti-standing-army tradition survives in the deep American skepticism toward martial law, military involvement in domestic policing (the Posse Comitatus Act of 1878), and the constitutional requirement of civilian control of the military.
