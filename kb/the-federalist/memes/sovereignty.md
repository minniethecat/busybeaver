# Sovereignty

**Sovereignty** — the supreme political authority within a defined territory — was one of the most contested concepts in the ratification debates of 1787–1788. Traditional political theory, from Bodin to Blackstone, held that sovereignty was indivisible: there could be only one sovereign in a state. The [[The Federalist Papers|Federalist Papers]], written primarily by [[Alexander Hamilton]] and [[James Madison]], advanced a revolutionary argument that sovereignty could be divided between state and national governments, each supreme within its own sphere. This innovation was essential to the constitutional design and forms the theoretical foundation of American [[federalism]].

## The Traditional Doctrine of Indivisible Sovereignty

Under the orthodox theory inherited from European political philosophy, sovereignty was unitary. A government was either a [[confederation]] of sovereign states (in which the member states retained their full sovereignty) or a consolidated national government (in which the states were extinguished). Opponents of the [[Constitution]] — the [[Anti-Federalists]] — relied heavily on this binary framework, arguing that the proposed government would necessarily destroy the sovereignty of the states.

This traditional view drew on Jean Bodin's sixteenth-century formulation that sovereignty was the "absolute and perpetual power" of a commonwealth — indivisible by definition. Sir William Blackstone, the leading English legal authority familiar to American lawyers, similarly held that "there is and must be in all [governments] a supreme, irresistible, absolute, uncontrolled authority, in which the rights of sovereignty reside."

## Madison's Innovation: Divided Sovereignty

The Federalist Papers offered a third option. [[James Madison|Madison]], writing in Federalist No. 39, argued that the proposed government was neither wholly national nor wholly federal but a "composition" of both. He grounded this argument in the principle of popular sovereignty — the idea that all legitimate authority derives from the people:

> It is ESSENTIAL to such a government that it be derived from the great body of the society, not from an inconsiderable proportion, or a favored class of it. (039-006)

Because the people, not the states, were the ultimate source of authority, they could delegate different portions of that authority to different levels of government without contradiction. The states did not need to be abolished for a national government to exist; the people could grant specific powers to each.

Madison expanded on this in Federalist No. 46:

> The federal and State governments are in fact but different agents and trustees of the people, constituted with different powers, and designed for different purposes. (046-005)

## The State-Federal Balance

Under the proposed Constitution, the national government would be sovereign in matters of national concern — war, foreign relations, interstate commerce — while the states would remain sovereign in matters of local concern — criminal law, property, domestic relations. Neither would be wholly subordinate to the other; each would be supreme within its assigned sphere.

Hamilton explained the practical mechanism in Federalist No. 45: "The State governments may be regarded as constituent and essential parts of the federal government; whilst the latter is nowise essential to the operation or organization of the former" (045-010). The states, in other words, were structurally embedded in the federal system through their role in the [[Senate]] and the Electoral College.

## Constitutional Safeguards of State Sovereignty

The [[Constitution]] protected state sovereignty through multiple mechanisms:

- **Equal Senate representation**: Each state, regardless of population, received two senators — a structural guarantee that the smaller states would not be overwhelmed
- **Electoral College**: Presidential elections were mediated through state-chosen electors, not direct national popular vote
- **Reserved powers**: The Tenth Amendment would later codify what the Federalist Papers argued implicitly — that powers not delegated to the federal government were reserved to the states
- **Amendment process**: Constitutional amendments required approval by three-quarters of the states, ensuring that fundamental changes could not be imposed by a simple national majority

## The Supremacy Clause and Its Limits

Critics charged that the [[Supremacy Clause]] (Article VI) would obliterate state sovereignty by making federal law "the supreme law of the land." Hamilton responded in Federalist No. 27 that the supremacy of federal law applied only within the national government's enumerated sphere — it did not grant the federal government general legislative authority. The Constitution's "few and defined" federal powers (as Madison described them in Federalist No. 45) left the states with "numerous and indefinite" powers over ordinary life:

> The powers delegated by the proposed Constitution to the federal government are few and defined. Those which are to remain in the State governments are numerous and indefinite. (045-014)

## Legacy and Interpretation

Madison's theory of divided sovereignty was not merely a rhetorical device for securing ratification; it became the operating logic of American constitutionalism. The [[ratification]] debates themselves demonstrated the theory in action — the people of each state, acting through specially elected ratifying conventions, exercised their sovereign authority to decide whether to join the [[Union]].

The Civil War would later test the limits of divided sovereignty, forcing a reckoning with the question Madison had largely avoided: what happens when the two levels of sovereignty come into irreconcilable conflict? The Union's victory established that the national government's sovereignty was ultimately supreme in matters of national existence — but the states' residual sovereignty over local affairs remained and remains a defining feature of American federalism.
