# Original Jurisdiction

**Original Jurisdiction** refers to the constitutional grant in Article III, Section 2 that authorizes the Supreme Court to hear certain cases in the first instance — rather than on appeal from lower courts. In Federalist No. 81, Hamilton analyzed this provision at length, defending the limited scope of the Court's original jurisdiction while explaining the rationale for each category of cases it covers.

## The Scope of Original Jurisdiction

Hamilton described the original jurisdiction of the Supreme Court as deliberately narrow. Under the Constitution, it was confined to two classes of cases, "and those of a nature rarely to occur":

> The Supreme Court is to be invested with original jurisdiction, only "in cases affecting ambassadors, other public ministers, and consuls, and those in which A STATE shall be a party." [081-017]

This limited grant served an important structural purpose. By reserving only a narrow slice of original jurisdiction to the highest court, the Constitution ensured that most federal cases would begin in the subordinate tribunals, with the Supreme Court exercising appellate review. Hamilton emphasized this division:

> We have seen that the original jurisdiction of the Supreme Court would be confined to two classes of causes, and those of a nature rarely to occur. In all other cases of federal cognizance, the original jurisdiction would appertain to the inferior tribunals; and the Supreme Court would have nothing more than an appellate jurisdiction. [081-020]

## The Rationale for Each Category

Hamilton offered distinct justifications for each category of original jurisdiction. Cases involving ambassadors and public ministers touched directly on foreign relations and the public peace. As the "immediate representatives of their sovereigns," such officials deserved to have their cases heard by "the highest judicatory of the nation" [081-017]. Similarly, when a state was a party, "it would ill suit its dignity to be turned over to an inferior tribunal."

Consuls, though lacking a full diplomatic character, were still "public agents of the nations to which they belong," and therefore merited similar treatment [081-017].

## The Appellate-Original Division

Hamilton's larger purpose in Federalist No. 81 was to defend the Constitution's court structure. Opponents had argued that the Supreme Court's appellate jurisdiction over questions of fact would abolish the trial by jury. Hamilton countered that the division between original and appellate jurisdiction — with only "a very small portion of original jurisdiction preserved to the Supreme Court, and the rest consigned to the subordinate tribunals" — was a prudent distribution of authority [081-025].

The distinction between original and appellate jurisdiction, Hamilton argued, was a familiar feature of every legal system. What mattered was not whether the Supreme Court could review facts, but how the legislature would structure that review — including whether it would involve a jury. The phrase "appellate jurisdiction, both as to law and fact" did not abolish juries; it simply gave Congress the flexibility to design appropriate procedures.

## Marbury v. Madison and the Textual Limit

In Marbury v. Madison (1803), Chief Justice John Marshall held that Congress could not expand the Supreme Court's original jurisdiction beyond the categories specified in Article III. The constitutional enumeration was exclusive, not merely a floor. This reading vindicated Hamilton's characterization of the original jurisdiction as a narrow, carefully bounded grant — and established the principle that the Constitution's text, not legislative will, defines the Court's jurisdiction.
