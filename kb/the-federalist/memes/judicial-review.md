# Judicial Review

## Definition

**Judicial review** is the power of courts to examine the constitutionality of legislative and executive actions and to declare void those that conflict with the Constitution. Though not explicitly stated in the Constitution's text, Hamilton argues in Federalist No. 78 that judicial review is inherent in the concept of a written constitution that limits government power — without it, "all the reservations of particular rights or privileges would amount to nothing."

## In The Federalist Papers

Hamilton's Federalist No. 78 is the foundational American statement on judicial review. He begins by establishing the judiciary's structural position: it is "the least dangerous" branch because it has "neither FORCE nor WILL, but merely judgment" and must depend on the executive to enforce its decisions. The courts "may truly be said to have neither FORCE nor WILL, but merely judgment" (078-005).

The argument for judicial review flows from the nature of a limited constitution. A constitution that limits legislative authority requires that courts be empowered to enforce those limits: "No legislative act... contrary to the Constitution, can be valid." To deny this "would be to affirm that the deputy is greater than his principal; that the servant is above his master; that the representatives of the people are superior to the people themselves" (078-008). Courts do not claim superiority over the legislature — they merely declare that the will of the people, as expressed in the Constitution, prevails over the will of their agents, as expressed in statutes.

> The complete independence of the courts of justice is peculiarly essential in a limited Constitution. By a limited Constitution, I understand one which contains certain specified exceptions to the legislative authority... Limitations of this kind can be preserved in practice no other way than through the medium of courts of justice, whose duty it must be to declare all acts contrary to the manifest tenor of the Constitution void.
>
> (078-008)

The independence of the judiciary is secured by life tenure during good behavior and by protection against salary diminution: "Next to permanency in office, nothing can contribute more to the independence of the judges than a fixed provision for their support" (079-003). These provisions ensure that judges need not fear political retaliation for their constitutional rulings.

Hamilton further argues in Federalist No. 81 that the judicial power is properly bounded. The "authority of the judicial department" in matters of constitutional interpretation is a "complete security" against legislative encroachment, but it does not give courts superiority over the other branches — each branch interprets the Constitution within its own sphere (081-011).

## Historical Context

The concept of judicial review had antecedents in English common law (Coke's *Dr. Bonham's Case*, 1610, which suggested courts could void acts of Parliament contrary to "common right and reason") and in several state court decisions before 1787 that had struck down state laws as violating state constitutions. But the idea that a federal court could void an act of Congress was novel and controversial.

The Constitutional Convention debated and rejected a "Council of Revision" that would have combined judges with the executive to review legislation — the judges' role in constitutional review was instead left implicit, to be exercised by courts in the ordinary course of deciding cases. The Anti-Federalist Brutus warned that the federal judiciary would use its power to "mould the government into almost any shape it pleases," predicting exactly the expansive role courts would later assume.

## Key Arguments

1. **A limited constitution requires judicial enforcement**: Without courts to void unconstitutional laws, constitutional limits on legislative power are meaningless paper barriers.

2. **Judicial review is not judicial supremacy**: Courts do not make policy — they merely apply the superior law (the Constitution) over the inferior law (the statute) when the two conflict.

3. **Life tenure and salary protection ensure independence**: Judges must be free from political pressure to enforce the Constitution against popular but unconstitutional legislation.

4. **The judiciary is the least dangerous branch**: Lacking both purse and sword, courts pose no threat to liberty — their power is the power of reasoned judgment alone.

## Constitutional Implementation

- **Article III, Section 1**: Federal judges hold office during "good Behavior" (life tenure) and their compensation "shall not be diminished during their Continuance in Office."
- **Article III, Section 2**: The judicial power extends to "all Cases, in Law and Equity, arising under this Constitution, the Laws of the United States, and Treaties" — the textual basis for constitutional review.
- **Marbury v. Madison (1803)**: Chief Justice John Marshall formalized judicial review, declaring that "it is emphatically the province and duty of the judicial department to say what the law is."
- **Judicial hierarchy**: The Supreme Court's appellate jurisdiction over state court decisions involving federal questions ensures uniform constitutional interpretation.

## Anti-Federalist Counterarguments

The Anti-Federalist Brutus (likely Robert Yates) devoted several essays to attacking the proposed judiciary, arguing that:
- Life-tenured judges would become an independent aristocracy, answerable to no one.
- The power to interpret the Constitution "according to the letter and spirit" would enable courts to expand federal power indefinitely.
- Judicial review combined with the Supremacy Clause would allow federal courts to annihilate state laws and state courts.
- There was no mechanism to correct erroneous constitutional interpretations — the Court's word would be final and unreviewable.

## Legacy and Significance

Judicial review has become the most consequential American contribution to constitutional governance. The U.S. Supreme Court has exercised the power to strike down federal laws in hundreds of cases, shaping American life on issues from segregation (*Brown v. Board of Education*, 1954) to abortion (*Roe v. Wade*, 1973; *Dobbs*, 2022) to campaign finance (*Citizens United*, 2010). The practice has been adopted by constitutional courts worldwide — Germany, India, South Africa, Canada, and the European Court of Justice all exercise forms of constitutional review inspired by the American model.

The "counter-majoritarian difficulty" — the tension between judicial review and democratic self-government — remains the central debate in constitutional theory. Hamilton's answer, that the people's will in the Constitution prevails over the legislature's will in a statute, continues to frame the defense of the practice.
