# Impeachment

## Definition

**Impeachment** is the constitutional mechanism by which the House of Representatives brings charges against a federal officer and the Senate sits as a court to try those charges, with conviction resulting in removal from office and disqualification from future office. It is one of the most powerful checks the legislature holds over the executive and judiciary, designed to address abuses of power that fall short of criminal liability but nonetheless threaten the constitutional order.

## In The Federalist Papers

The Federalist Papers provide the most extensive founding-era analysis of impeachment in Federalist Nos. 65 and 66, where Hamilton defends the Senate as the tribunal for trying impeachments against critics who preferred the Supreme Court or a hybrid body.

Hamilton opens Federalist No. 65 by acknowledging that impeachment is inherently political: the offenses that trigger it "are of a nature which may with peculiar propriety be denominated POLITICAL, as they relate chiefly to injuries done immediately to the society itself" (065-003). The subjects of impeachment are not ordinary crimes but abuses of public trust — "the misconduct of public men, or, in other words, from the abuse or violation of some public trust" (065-004).

The question of which body should try impeachments occupied considerable attention. Hamilton rejects the Supreme Court as a trial venue, arguing that the Senate is "the most fit depositary of this important trust" (065-009). The Supreme Court's members would be too few in number and might later have to try the same defendant in a criminal proceeding — a double burden that would compromise impartiality. The Senate, by contrast, provides the "awful discretion" required for such politically charged judgments (065-010).

> A well-constituted court for the trial of impeachments is an object not more to be desired than difficult to be obtained in a government wholly elective.
>
> (065-004)

In Federalist No. 66, Hamilton addresses specific objections to the Senate's impeachment role, including the concern that combining impeachment trial power with appointment confirmation power would make the Senate "too powerful." Hamilton dismisses this, noting that the division of impeachment between House (prosecution) and Senate (trial) provides "an essential check in the hands of that body upon the encroachments of the executive" (066-003).

Madison also touches on impeachment in Federalist No. 47, observing that the Constitution separates impeachment from ordinary judicial power: the Supreme Court is "invested with the supreme appellate jurisdiction" while impeachment trials are assigned to the legislature (047-008). In Federalist No. 39, Madison notes that the President is subject to "impeachment" as a critical restraint distinguishing the American presidency from a monarchy (039-007).

## Historical Context

The Founders drew impeachment from English parliamentary practice, where the House of Commons prosecuted and the House of Lords judged. Impeachment had been used against royal ministers since the 14th century, though its use had declined by the 18th century. The most famous English impeachment — of Warren Hastings, Governor-General of Bengal — was underway during the Constitutional Convention of 1787, providing a live example of the mechanism's scope and limitations.

At the Convention, the delegates debated whether impeachment was necessary at all. Some argued that periodic elections were sufficient to remove bad executives; others countered that an executive who conspired with foreign powers or corrupted the legislature could do immense damage before the next election. The impeachment clause was included as a vital safety valve between elections.

## Key Arguments

Hamilton advances three principal arguments for the impeachment power. First, impeachment addresses political offenses that lie beyond the reach of ordinary criminal law: while courts prosecute crimes against individuals, impeachment reaches "injuries done immediately to the society itself" — abuses of power that harm the body politic without necessarily violating any statute (065-003). Second, the Senate is the optimal tribunal: the Supreme Court lacks the "fortitude" and public standing to confront a corrupt president, whereas the Senate, with its longer terms and greater membership, provides both independence and accountability; its members are "sufficiently dignified" and "sufficiently independent" to render impartial judgment (065-009). Third, the bicameral division of the impeachment power — the House prosecutes, the Senate judges — prevents abuse by ensuring that two bodies differently constituted and accountable to different constituencies share the power, and neither can wield it unilaterally for partisan purposes (066-003).

## Constitutional Implementation

The Constitution distributes the impeachment power across four provisions. Article I, Section 2 vests the House of Representatives with "the sole Power of Impeachment." Article I, Section 3 assigns the Senate "the sole Power to try all Impeachments," requiring a two-thirds majority for conviction and limiting judgment to removal and disqualification — the convicted party remains subject to criminal prosecution in ordinary courts. Article II, Section 4 specifies the grounds: the President, Vice President, and all civil officers "shall be removed from Office on Impeachment for, and Conviction of, Treason, Bribery, or other high Crimes and Misdemeanors." Article III, Section 2 excludes impeachment from the ordinary criminal process, providing that the trial of all crimes "except in Cases of Impeachment" shall be by jury.

## Anti-Federalist Counterarguments

Anti-Federalists, particularly Brutus, argued that the impeachment power was dangerously incomplete. The Senate, already powerful through its role in appointments and treaties, would be reluctant to convict executive officers whose appointment it had confirmed. Moreover, the two-thirds conviction threshold was too high — a president could retain office despite widespread belief in his corruption if he commanded the loyalty of just over one-third of the Senate.

Some Anti-Federalists also objected that the Constitution did not define "high Crimes and Misdemeanors" with sufficient precision, leaving the impeachment standard vulnerable to partisan manipulation. Hamilton's response — that impeachment is inherently political and cannot be reduced to a statutory checklist — satisfied the Constitution's supporters but continues to generate debate.

## Legacy and Significance

Impeachment has been initiated against three presidents: Andrew Johnson (1868), Bill Clinton (1998), and Donald Trump (2019 and 2021). All three were acquitted by the Senate. Richard Nixon resigned in 1974 before the full House could vote on articles of impeachment approved by the Judiciary Committee. Numerous federal judges have been impeached and removed, establishing that impeachment is primarily a tool for addressing judicial corruption while remaining an extraordinary remedy for executive malfeasance.

The debate Hamilton launched in Federalist Nos. 65–66 continues to shape impeachment proceedings: Is a criminal offense required, or is "abuse of public trust" sufficient? What standard of proof should the Senate apply? These unresolved questions make the Federalist Papers essential reading whenever impeachment returns to the national stage.
