# National Government

The **national government** is the central governing authority established by the Constitution of the United States, as advocated in the Federalist Papers. It is characterized by its power to act directly upon individual citizens — a critical departure from the Confederation model, under which Congress could only make requisitions upon the states. Publius devoted the early numbers of the Federalist to establishing the necessity of this national authority, arguing from considerations of safety, prosperity, and liberty alike.

## The Case for a National Government

In the opening essays of the Federalist Papers, Publius advances the argument that the safety and prosperity of the American people require an "efficient national government." This thesis appears as early as Federalist No. 1 and receives sustained development through the early numbers.

John Jay, writing in the earliest essays, emphasized the importance of unified national authority for both external security and internal tranquility:

> A national government, not being affected by those local circumstances which might lead to partiality, is less likely to become a party to the passions which inflame particular states. [003-013]

Hamilton and Madison expanded on this foundation, arguing that a properly constituted national government would attract superior talent to public office:

> When once an efficient national government is established, the best men in the country will not only consent to serve, but also will generally be appointed to manage it. [003-010]

## Direct Authority over Citizens

The single most consequential feature of the proposed national government was its capacity to legislate for and act upon individuals, not merely upon states in their corporate capacity. Under the Articles of Confederation, Congress could declare policy but could not enforce it — the national government depended entirely on state compliance.

Hamilton identified this as the fundamental error of the Confederation:

> The great and radical vice in the construction of the existing Confederation is in the principle of LEGISLATION for STATES or GOVERNMENTS, in their CORPORATE or COLLECTIVE CAPACITIES, and as contra-distinguished from the INDIVIDUALS of which they consist. [015-008]

The new national government would possess its own executive, legislative, and judicial organs, each capable of operating independently of the states within their constitutionally assigned spheres. This direct authority over individuals — the power to tax, to regulate conduct, to adjudicate disputes — distinguished the proposed Constitution from every previous experiment in republican confederation.

## The Necessity of Energy in Government

A government that cannot act is no government at all. Hamilton developed this theme extensively, arguing that energy in the executive and efficiency in the legislature were essential to the national government's capacity to fulfill its purposes. A feeble national government would invite foreign contempt, internal disorder, and the eventual dissolution of the union:

> A government ill executed, whatever it may be in theory, must be in practice a bad government. [003-006]

Energy and stability, properly understood, were not threats to liberty but its essential safeguards — without them, no government could protect the rights it was instituted to secure.

## National vs. State Authority

The Federalist Papers do not argue for the elimination of state governments. Rather, they contend that certain functions — defense, foreign relations, regulation of interstate commerce, and the protection of rights against state encroachment — require a national authority.

> They are advised to place in one national government the power of regulating commerce, of making treaties, and of providing for the common defense. [002-004]

The state governments would retain authority over local matters — what Madison would later describe as "the numerous and indefinite" objects that remain within state jurisdiction. The Constitution thus created not a consolidated unitary state but a compound republic in which both levels of government operated directly on the citizen within their respective spheres.

## Benefits Enumerated

Publius identified multiple advantages of a properly constituted national government:

1. **External security**: A unified government could coordinate defense against foreign threats more effectively than thirteen separate states [003-006]
2. **Internal peace**: National authority could suppress insurrections and prevent armed conflict between states [004-003]
3. **Commercial prosperity**: A national government could regulate trade, prevent destructive commercial rivalries between states, and negotiate favorable treaties [004-016]
4. **Qualified leadership**: National office would attract individuals of superior talent and character [003-010]

> Whether firmly united under one national government, or split into a number of confederacies, certain it is that foreign nations will know and view our situation exactly as it is. [004-019]

These benefits were not merely speculative. The experience under the Confederation had already demonstrated, in Hamilton's judgment, that disunion would reproduce on the American continent the very conditions of rivalry and war that had plagued Europe for centuries.
