# Bill of Attainder

A **bill of attainder** is a legislative act that imposes punishment — typically death, imprisonment, or forfeiture of property — on a specific person or group without the benefit of a judicial trial. Article I, Sections 9 and 10 of the United States Constitution prohibit both Congress and the states from passing bills of attainder.

## Constitutional Prohibition

The Constitution's prohibition of bills of attainder reflects the separation-of-powers principle: the legislature may make general laws, but only the judiciary may determine guilt and impose punishment in individual cases. By forbidding legislative trials, the Constitution ensures that the judicial power remains with the courts and that no branch may exercise the functions properly belonging to another.

Madison discussed this prohibition in Federalist No. 44, identifying it as one of the "restrictions on the authority of the several States" necessary to protect individual liberty. He grouped the ban on bills of attainder together with the prohibitions on ex post facto laws and laws impairing the obligation of contracts, treating all three as essential safeguards against arbitrary legislative power. In Federalist No. 84, Hamilton likewise enumerated the prohibition on bills of attainder — along with the ban on ex post facto laws and the guarantee of habeas corpus — as provisions that "create, in a formal manner, a system of securities for the rights of individuals" even before the addition of a bill of rights.

## Historical Background

The bill of attainder was a familiar instrument of parliamentary power in English legal history. Under the Tudor and Stuart monarchs, Parliament frequently passed bills of attainder against political enemies, bypassing the ordinary judicial process to secure convictions and confiscate property. The most notorious examples included the attainder of Thomas Wentworth, Earl of Strafford (1641), and the posthumous attainder of Oliver Cromwell and other regicides after the Restoration. The American Founders regarded this practice as a dangerous abuse of legislative authority — transforming the law-making body into a court, prosecutor, and jury all at once, without the procedural safeguards of a criminal trial.

## Separation of Powers Rationale

The prohibition on bills of attainder serves three interconnected constitutional values:

- **Judicial exclusivity**: Only courts, exercising the judicial power of the United States, may determine individual guilt. A legislature that convicts by statute usurps the judicial function.
- **Procedural fairness**: Criminal trials provide the accused with notice of charges, the right to confront witnesses, the assistance of counsel, and the judgment of an impartial jury — protections that bills of attainder entirely bypass.
- **Rule of law**: General laws, applied prospectively to classes of persons, are the proper business of the legislature. Laws targeting specific individuals for punishment violate the principle that government should operate by general rules, not by selective decrees.

## Relationship to Other Constitutional Protections

The attainder prohibition works in concert with related constitutional safeguards:

- **Ex post facto clause** (Article I, §9–10): Prohibits retroactive criminal laws, complementing the attainder ban's protection against legislative targeting
- **Habeas corpus** (Article I, §9): The right to challenge one's detention before a judge — rendered meaningless if the legislature can simply declare guilt by statute
- **Due process** (Fifth Amendment): The guarantee that no person shall be deprived of life, liberty, or property without due process of law reinforces the principle that punishment requires a judicial proceeding
- **Trial by jury** (Article III, §2; Sixth Amendment): The right to a jury trial in criminal cases, which bills of attainder circumvent

## In The Federalist Papers

Publius referenced bills of attainder in the broader context of constitutional limitations on legislative power. In Federalist No. 44, Madison argued that the prohibitions on bills of attainder, ex post facto laws, and laws impairing contracts were "proper and necessary" restraints on state legislatures — "speculative" objections to these restrictions, he wrote, could not survive scrutiny, given the "mischiefs" that attainted persons had suffered under legislative condemnation. Hamilton returned to the subject in Federalist No. 84, citing the attainder ban as evidence that the proposed Constitution already contained meaningful protections for individual rights, making a separate bill of rights unnecessary.

## Significance in American Constitutional Law

The Supreme Court has interpreted the Bill of Attainder Clause to prohibit any law that legislatively determines guilt and inflicts punishment on identifiable individuals without judicial trial. In *United States v. Lovett* (1946) and *United States v. Brown* (1965), the Court struck down congressional enactments that effectively punished specific individuals by barring them from government employment — holding that the constitutional prohibition applies regardless of whether the punishment takes the traditional form of death or imprisonment. The attainder clause thus remains a vital, if rarely invoked, safeguard against the concentration of prosecutorial and judicial power in the legislative branch.
