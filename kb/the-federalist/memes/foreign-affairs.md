# Foreign Affairs

**Foreign affairs** encompass the diplomatic, military, and commercial relations between the United States and other nations. Under the Constitution, the conduct of foreign affairs is primarily vested in the executive branch — the President serves as commander-in-chief, negotiates treaties (subject to Senate ratification), and receives ambassadors — while Congress retains powers over declarations of war, regulation of foreign commerce, and appropriations. This distribution of foreign affairs authority reflects the Framers' judgment that the nation must speak with one voice in diplomacy while preventing the concentration of war-making power in a single branch.

## In the Federalist Papers

### The Defects of the Confederation

The inadequacy of the Articles of Confederation in matters of foreign affairs was a recurring theme in the Federalist Papers. The Confederation's inability to enforce treaties, compel state compliance with diplomatic obligations, or present a unified front to the European powers was presented by Publius as one of the most urgent reasons for adopting a stronger national government.

Jay, in Federalist No. 2, argued that a unified national government was essential for effective diplomacy, contending that foreign nations would respect and negotiate with "one national government" more seriously than with a fragmented confederation of thirteen states [002-009]. Drawing on his experience as Secretary of Foreign Affairs under the Confederation, Jay emphasized that the interests and character of the American people were fundamentally uniform, making a single national government the natural vehicle for conducting foreign relations.

Hamilton, in Federalist No. 5, warned of the dangers that foreign powers would pose to a disunited America, predicting that separate confederacies would become "the instruments of foreign influence" and that European powers would play American states against one another [005-013, 005-014]. This warning drew on historical examples of European statecraft — particularly the balance-of-power politics that had produced centuries of war on the European continent — to argue that disunion would invite precisely the kind of foreign manipulation the Revolution had been fought to escape.

### The Treaty Power

Hamilton devoted Federalist No. 75 to defending the Constitution's allocation of the treaty power between the President and the Senate. He argued that the President's capacity for "decision, activity, secrecy, and dispatch" made the executive best suited to conduct negotiations, while the Senate's role in providing "advice and consent" ensured that treaties would benefit from the deliberation and collective judgment of a representative body. The requirement of a two-thirds Senate majority for treaty ratification served as a safeguard against both executive rashness and foreign corruption of a smaller body.

### The Danger of Foreign Influence

A persistent anxiety of the founding generation — reflected throughout The Federalist — was the danger that foreign powers would seek to manipulate American politics. Hamilton, in Federalist No. 68, argued that the Electoral College would serve as a barrier against "the desire in foreign powers to gain an improper ascendant in our councils" by making it difficult for foreign governments to influence the selection of the President [068-011]. This concern about foreign influence was not abstract: during the Confederation period, several European powers had cultivated relationships with individual states and political factions, and the Framers sought to design institutions that would be resistant to such external manipulation.

### Commerce and Foreign Relations

In Federalist No. 53, Hamilton — writing on the House of Representatives — highlighted the particular importance of knowledge of foreign affairs for legislators regulating commerce:

> The regulation of commerce, it is true, is a new source of legislation, and of information in this particular, the want of a familiarity with foreign affairs has not been mentioned as a defect. In regulating our own commerce, however, a thorough knowledge of the commercial laws and policy of foreign nations cannot be without its uses. [053-009]

This passage reflects the Framers' understanding that even domestic legislative competence required familiarity with foreign commercial systems, particularly given the new constitutional power to regulate interstate and international commerce. The connection between foreign affairs and commercial regulation was especially salient because one of the principal defects of the Articles of Confederation was the inability of Congress to retaliate against foreign trade restrictions or to negotiate commercial treaties that bound the states.

## Constitutional Distribution of Foreign Affairs Powers

The Constitution divides foreign affairs authority between the executive and legislative branches:

- **President**: Commander-in-chief of the armed forces; power to make treaties (with the advice and consent of two-thirds of the Senate); power to appoint ambassadors; power to receive foreign ambassadors
- **Senate**: Advice and consent on treaties and ambassador appointments
- **Congress**: Power to declare war; power to regulate foreign commerce; power to define and punish offenses against the law of nations; appropriations power

This division was designed to prevent the concentration of foreign affairs power in a single branch while ensuring that the nation could speak with one voice in its dealings with foreign powers. The Framers rejected both the British model — in which the Crown held near-exclusive control over foreign affairs and war-making — and the Confederation model — in which Congress possessed theoretical authority but lacked the means to compel state compliance.

## Evolution

The constitutional framework for foreign affairs has evolved significantly since the founding. The growth of American power, the emergence of executive agreements as an alternative to treaties, the creation of a permanent military and intelligence establishment, and the expanded role of international law and institutions have all shaped the modern conduct of American foreign policy. The Supreme Court has recognized the President's primacy in foreign affairs, particularly in *United States v. Curtiss-Wright Export Corp.* (1936), while also affirming Congress's substantial constitutional role in regulating foreign commerce and funding foreign engagements.

The tension between executive initiative and legislative prerogative in foreign affairs — anticipated by the Framers' careful division of authority — remains a central feature of American constitutional practice. The War Powers Resolution of 1973, congressional authorization requirements for the use of military force, and Senate advice-and-consent proceedings for major treaties all reflect the enduring relevance of the founding-era debate about how a republic should conduct its relations with the wider world.
