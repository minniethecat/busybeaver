# John Jay (1745–1829)

## Role in The Federalist

John Jay wrote only five of the 85 Federalist Papers — Nos. 2 through 5 and No. 64 — before illness forced him to withdraw from the project. Despite this small number, his contributions were strategically important: they established the foreign-policy and international-law framework for the argument that a unified union was essential to American security and standing in the world.

Jay opens Federalist No. 2 with a sweeping vision of American identity: "Providence has been pleased to give this one connected country to one united people — a people descended from the same ancestors, speaking the same language, professing the same religion, attached to the same principles of government" (002-007). This vision of national unity — grounded in shared ancestry, language, religion, and principles — provided the emotional and moral foundation for the more technical arguments Hamilton and Madison would develop in later essays.

In Federalist No. 64, Jay defends the treaty power — the subject he knew best, having served as Secretary of Foreign Affairs under the Confederation and as a diplomat in Spain and France. He argues that the Senate's role in treaty-making ensures both expertise and accountability. Treaties negotiated by the president with senatorial consent "will have the advantage of talents, the confidence of the people, and the sanction of the States" — a combination that would produce "wise and well-grounded" foreign policy.

## Biography

John Jay was born in New York City to a wealthy merchant family of French Huguenot descent. He studied at King's College (now Columbia), was admitted to the bar, and quickly rose to prominence in revolutionary politics. He served in the First and Second Continental Congresses, was President of the Continental Congress in 1778, and was one of the American commissioners who negotiated the Treaty of Paris (1783) that ended the Revolutionary War.

Under the Articles of Confederation, Jay served as Secretary of Foreign Affairs — a role that convinced him the Confederation's weakness in foreign policy was a grave national danger. He was not a delegate to the Constitutional Convention but became an energetic advocate for ratification, contributing to The Federalist and writing his own pamphlet, *An Address to the People of New York*.

George Washington appointed Jay as the first Chief Justice of the United States (1789–1795). Jay left the Court to negotiate the controversial Jay Treaty (1794) with Great Britain, which averted war but inflamed domestic politics. He later served as Governor of New York (1795–1801), where he signed the state's gradual emancipation law. He declined John Adams's offer to return to the Supreme Court and retired from public life.

## Key Ideas / Contributions

Jay's political thought focuses on two interconnected themes:

1. **American unity is providential**: Jay's opening essays in The Federalist ground the case for union not merely in practical necessity but in a vision of divine providence and shared national identity. "This country and this people seem to have been made for each other" (002-008).

2. **Foreign policy requires unity and expertise**: Drawing on his diplomatic experience, Jay argues that a fragmented America will be exploited by foreign powers while a unified America can negotiate from strength. The treaty power, shared between president and Senate, combines the "secrecy and dispatch" of executive negotiation with the deliberative wisdom and federal character of the Senate (064-017).

## Relationship to the Constitution

Though not a delegate at Philadelphia, Jay was a leading Federalist during New York's ratification struggle. His *Address to the People of New York* (1788) was second only to The Federalist in articulating the case for ratification in that crucial state. As Chief Justice, he established important precedents for the new federal judiciary, including the principle that states could be sued by citizens of other states (*Chisholm v. Georgia*, 1793) — a decision later overturned by the Eleventh Amendment. His diplomatic work, particularly the Jay Treaty, established the precedent that treaties are the supreme law of the land and may address matters beyond Congress's enumerated legislative powers.

## Legacy

Jay is often the least remembered of the three Federalist authors, overshadowed by Hamilton's energy and Madison's theoretical depth. Yet his contributions — the vision of providential national unity and the defense of the treaty power grounded in practical diplomatic experience — fill a lacuna in the Federalist argument. As the first Chief Justice, he established the institutional dignity of the Supreme Court. As a diplomat, he kept the young republic at peace. As Governor of New York, he advanced the cause of emancipation. His life exemplified the Federalist ideal of the statesman: principled, pragmatic, and devoted to the public good.
