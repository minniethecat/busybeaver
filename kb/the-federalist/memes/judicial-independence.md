# Judicial Independence

**Judicial independence** is the constitutional principle that judges must be insulated from political pressure — through life tenure during good behavior and protection against salary reduction — to decide cases impartially and to enforce constitutional limits against the other branches of government. [[Alexander Hamilton]]'s defense of judicial independence in Federalist Nos. 78 and 79 is the foundational American statement on why courts must stand apart from the political branches. The principle is closely tied to both [[separation-of-powers]] and [[judicial-review]], forming the structural foundation of the [[judiciary]]'s role in the constitutional order.

## In The Federalist Papers

Hamilton opens Federalist No. 78 by addressing the judiciary's structural position: it is "beyond comparison the weakest of the three departments of power" and "the least dangerous to the political rights of the Constitution," possessing "neither FORCE nor WILL, but merely judgment" (078-003). This very weakness, Hamilton argues, makes judicial independence essential — courts must be protected from the branches that command sword and purse.

The argument proceeds through three linked claims. First, an independent judiciary is necessary to enforce a limited [[constitution]]: "limitations of this kind can be preserved in practice no other way than through the medium of courts of justice, whose duty it must be to declare all acts contrary to the manifest tenor of the Constitution void" (078-008). Without independent courts, constitutional limits are mere aspirational statements.[^1]

Second, judicial independence protects individual rights against temporary popular passions. "This independence of the judges is equally requisite to guard the Constitution and the rights of individuals from the effects of those ill humors, which the arts of designing men, or the influence of particular conjunctures, sometimes disseminate among the people themselves" (078-014). Even when the majority supports an unconstitutional measure, independent judges must stand firm: "it would require an uncommon portion of fortitude in the judges to do their duty as faithful guardians of the Constitution, where legislative invasions of it had been instigated by the major voice of the community" (078-014).

Third, structural guarantees — not good intentions — secure independence. Federalist No. 79 establishes two institutional safeguards. Life tenure during good behavior means judges hold office until they resign, die, or are impeached for misconduct — not until the next election or a new political majority takes power; this is the "best expedient which can be devised in any government, to secure a steady, upright, and impartial administration of the laws" (078-012). Undiminished compensation reinforces tenure: "next to permanency in office, nothing can contribute more to the independence of the judges than a fixed provision for their support" (079-002). If Congress could reduce judicial salaries, it could "reduce [judges] by famine, or tempt them by largesses" to surrender their judgment, for "in the general course of human nature, a power over a man's subsistence amounts to a power over his will" (079-002).

## Historical Context

English common law provided a cautionary example. Before the Act of Settlement of 1701, English judges served at the pleasure of the Crown and could be removed by royal prerogative. The Stuart kings — particularly James I and Charles II — routinely dismissed judges who ruled against the Crown's interests. By contrast, the Act of Settlement established that judges would hold office "quamdiu se bene gesserint" (during good behavior), a formula the framers adopted into Article III.

Colonial experience reinforced the lesson. The Declaration of Independence accused George III of having "made Judges dependent on his Will alone, for the tenure of their offices, and the amount and payment of their salaries." The framers were determined that federal judges would not suffer the same dependency.

## Key Arguments

Judicial independence rests on three core principles. The first is separation from the political branches: courts must be institutionally autonomous to check the legislature and executive — a special case of the broader separation of powers. The second is that tenure and salary are the twin structural safeguards: independence is not an abstraction but a function of concrete institutional design, with life tenure preventing removal for unpopular decisions and salary protection blocking financial coercion. The third is that accountability need not entail dependence: Hamilton reconciles independence with republican government by noting that judges remain accountable through [[impeachment]] for misconduct and through the requirement of written, reasoned opinions that the public can scrutinize.

## Constitutional Implementation

Article III, Section 1 provides that federal judges "shall hold their Offices during good Behaviour, and shall, at stated Times, receive for their Services, a Compensation, which shall not be diminished during their Continuance in Office." The impeachment mechanism — through which judges may be removed by the House of Representatives and convicted by the Senate for "Treason, Bribery, or other high Crimes and Misdemeanors" — provides a political check on judicial misconduct that stops short of allowing removal for disagreement with judicial decisions.

## Judicial Independence in Practice

The principle of judicial independence was dramatically tested in [[marbury-v-madison|Marbury v. Madison]] (1803), where Chief Justice John Marshall asserted the power of judicial review while simultaneously avoiding a direct confrontation with the Jefferson administration — demonstrating that judicial independence requires not only structural guarantees but also strategic judgment in their exercise.

The [[Constitutional Convention]] of 1787 debated judicial tenure and salary provisions at length. While some delegates proposed a council of revision that would include judges in the veto process, the Convention ultimately chose to protect judicial independence through tenure and salary rather than through direct participation in legislation.

## Anti-Federalist Counterarguments

The Anti-Federalist [[Brutus]] (likely Robert Yates) devoted several essays to attacking the proposed judiciary. He warned that life tenure and salary immunity, combined with the power of judicial review and the Supremacy Clause, would create an unaccountable judicial aristocracy. Brutus predicted that federal judges would "not confine themselves to any fixed or established rules, but will determine, according to what appears to them, the reason and spirit of the Constitution," expanding federal power "until the states and their governments are totally subverted."

Hamilton's response in Federalist No. 81 acknowledged the concern but argued that impeachment provided accountability, that the absence of term limits did not mean the absence of all constraint, and that the alternative — a dependent judiciary — was far more dangerous to liberty.

## Legacy

Judicial independence has become a hallmark of constitutional democracy worldwide. The institutional design Hamilton defended — life tenure with undiminished salary — has been adopted, with variations, in constitutional courts from Germany to India to South Africa. The tension Hamilton and the [[Anti-Federalists]] identified — between judicial independence and democratic accountability — remains the central debate in every system of constitutional review. Hamilton's core insight endures: judges who fear for their jobs cannot be trusted to enforce the law against the powerful.
