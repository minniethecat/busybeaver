# Admission of New States

The **Admission of New States** is the constitutional power granted to Congress under Article IV, Section 3, authorizing the federal legislature to admit new states into the Union on an equal footing with the original thirteen. The provision corrected a significant gap in the Articles of Confederation, under which Congress had already assumed extra-constitutional authority over the western territories.

## A Missing Provision in the Articles of Confederation

The Articles of Confederation made no provision for the admission of new states beyond Canada and the "other colonies" — meaning the remaining British colonies. As Madison observed in Federalist No. 43, the framers of the Confederation had simply overlooked the question of how territories west of the Appalachians would eventually join the Union:

> In the articles of Confederation, no provision is found on this important subject. Canada was to be admitted of right, on her joining in the measures of the United States; and the other COLONIES, by which were evidently meant the other British colonies, at the discretion of nine States. The eventual establishment of NEW STATES seems to have been overlooked by the compilers of that instrument. We have seen the inconvenience of this omission, and the assumption of power into which Congress have been led by it. [043-011]

This omission forced the Confederation Congress into extra-constitutional action. As Hamilton documented in Federalist No. 38, Congress had already proceeded to "form new States, to erect temporary governments, to appoint officers for them, and to prescribe the conditions on which such States shall be admitted into the Confederacy" — all without constitutional authority. Madison noted that this "assumption of power" was understandable given the practical necessity, but it underscored the need for a Constitution that explicitly provided for what the Confederation had been forced to improvise. [043-011]

## The Constitutional Solution

The new Constitution addressed this gap explicitly, granting Congress the power "to admit new States into the Union" while adding guard-rails: no new state could be formed within the jurisdiction of an existing state, nor could states be joined without the consent of the legislatures involved. Madison praised this design:

> The general precaution, that no new States shall be formed, without the concurrence of the federal authority, and that of the States concerned, is consonant to the principles which ought to govern such transactions. The particular precaution against the erection of new States, by the partition of a State without its consent, quiets the jealousy of the larger States; as that of the smaller is quieted by a like precaution, against a junction of States without their consent. [043-012]

This dual-consent requirement — federal authority plus the affected states — was a balanced solution to a contentious problem. Larger states feared that their territory might be carved away to create new states that would vote against their interests in the Senate; smaller states feared that they might be absorbed into larger neighbors. The requirement that both the federal government and the states concerned must concur protected both interests simultaneously.

## The Northwest Territory Precedent

Even before the Constitution was ratified, the Confederation Congress had adopted the Northwest Ordinance of 1787, establishing the principle that new states carved from the western territory would enter the Union on an equal footing with the original states — not as colonies or subordinate jurisdictions. This was a radical departure from European imperial practice, where colonies remained perpetually subordinate to the mother country. Madison placed the admission of new states within the "fourth class" of constitutional powers — miscellaneous authorities that were necessary for the practical operation of the federal system. [043-003]

The constitutional admission clause provided the permanent legal framework for this principle. Over the following century, it enabled the peaceful expansion of the Union from thirteen seaboard states to a continental republic — a feat unmatched by any other federal system of the era.
