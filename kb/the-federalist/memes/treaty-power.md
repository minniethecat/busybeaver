# Treaty Power

The treaty power — the constitutional authority to negotiate, conclude, and ratify international agreements — is a shared power between the President and the Senate, established by Article II of the Constitution and defended in the Federalist Papers, particularly Federalist Nos. 64 and 75. The President makes treaties "by and with the advice and consent of the Senate, provided two thirds of the senators present concur" (075-003). Publius argues that this division of authority combines the energy and decisiveness of a single executive with the deliberation and collective judgment of the Senate, while ensuring that state interests are protected through equal state representation in the upper chamber.

## Constitutional Structure

Article II, Section 2, Clause 2 of the Constitution provides that the President "shall have Power, by and with the Advice and Consent of the Senate, to make Treaties, provided two thirds of the Senators present concur." This clause creates a shared power: the President negotiates and proposes, the Senate deliberates and consents (or withholds consent). No treaty becomes law without the concurrence of a supermajority of senators — a threshold significantly higher than the simple majority required for ordinary legislation.

The two-thirds requirement serves several purposes: it protects state interests by ensuring that a treaty cannot be ratified over the objection of a substantial minority of states; it encourages deliberation and compromise in the treaty-making process; and it prevents the President from using treaties as instruments of executive aggrandizement.

## Hamilton's Defense in Federalist No. 75

In Federalist No. 75, Hamilton presented the most comprehensive defense of the treaty power's design. He argued that the power to make treaties is neither purely executive nor purely legislative in character, but partakes of both — and therefore properly belongs to a shared institutional arrangement. "The qualities elsewhere detailed as indispensable in the management of foreign negotiations," Hamilton wrote, "point out the Executive as the most fit agent in those transactions; while the vast importance of the trust, and the operation of treaties as laws, plead strongly for the participation of the whole or a portion of the legislative body in the office of making them" (075-004).

Hamilton rejected the proposal that the House of Representatives should participate in treaty-making. The House's frequent elections and large size made it unsuitable for the secrecy, dispatch, and continuity that treaty negotiations require. By contrast, the Senate — with its longer terms, smaller size, and "greater extent of information and stability of character" — was the appropriate legislative partner for the treaty power (075-007).

## Jay's Defense in Federalist No. 64

In Federalist No. 64, John Jay offered a complementary defense, emphasizing the practical advantages of the treaty-making arrangement. He argued that the President, as the officer "most easily distinguished" among the nation's leaders, was best positioned to conduct delicate negotiations — selecting envoys, managing communications, and maintaining the secrecy often essential to successful diplomacy (064-005).

Jay also stressed the importance of the Senate's role as a check on executive discretion. A President acting alone might be tempted to sacrifice national interests for personal advantage — "ambition, avarice, personal animosity, party opposition, and many other motives" could corrupt treaty-making without legislative oversight (064-006). The requirement of senatorial consent ensured that treaties would serve the public good rather than private interest.

## Separation of Powers and the Treaty Power

The treaty power occupies a unique position in the separation of powers framework. Unlike ordinary legislation — which requires the concurrence of both houses of Congress — treaties are made by the President with the Senate alone. This departure from the standard bicameral legislative process reflects the distinct character of treaties: they are instruments of foreign policy requiring executive initiative and senatorial deliberation, but they operate domestically as "the supreme Law of the Land" under the Supremacy Clause of Article VI.

The exclusion of the House from treaty-making was controversial during ratification. Anti-Federalists argued that the House, as the chamber most directly accountable to the people, should participate in the creation of binding international obligations. Hamilton responded that the House's role was adequately protected through its control over appropriations — without House-approved funding, no treaty commitment could be implemented — and that the House's frequent elections and popular character made it ill-suited for the "secrecy and dispatch" required in treaty negotiations (075-008).

## Treaty Supremacy and Domestic Law

Under the Supremacy Clause, ratified treaties are "the supreme Law of the Land ... any Thing in the Constitution or Laws of any State to the Contrary notwithstanding." This means that valid treaties override conflicting state laws and can, in certain circumstances, have the force of federal legislation. The Federalist Papers anticipated this consequence: Publius treated treaties as binding national commitments that states could not unilaterally impair, consistent with the broader project of establishing a national government capable of speaking with one voice in foreign affairs.

## Comparison with Other Constitutional Powers

The treaty power exemplifies the Constitution's preference for shared rather than unilateral authority in matters of great consequence. Other shared powers — including appointments (President nominates, Senate confirms), war (Congress declares, President commands), and legislation (bicameral passage plus presentment) — follow the same structural logic: divide authority to prevent its abuse, while ensuring that government retains the energy to act. The treaty power's two-thirds supermajority requirement represents the highest threshold for any shared power under the Constitution, reflecting the Framers' recognition that treaties, once ratified, bind the nation indefinitely and may alter the balance of power between the United States and foreign nations.
