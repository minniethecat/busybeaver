# Presidential Veto

## Definition

The presidential veto, referred to in the Federalist Papers as the "qualified negative," is the power granted to the President under Article I, Section 7 of the Constitution to refuse to sign a bill passed by Congress, returning it with objections. Congress may override the veto by a two-thirds vote in both houses. The veto is a core element of the separation of powers, giving the executive a defensive check against legislative encroachment.

## In The Federalist Papers

Hamilton provided the most thorough defense of the veto power in Federalist No. 73. He described "the qualified negative of the President upon the acts or resolutions of the two houses of the legislature" as a necessary shield for the executive branch [073-006]. Without it, he warned, the President "might gradually be stripped of his authorities by successive resolutions, or annihilated by a single vote" [073-015].

In Federalist No. 69, Hamilton carefully distinguished the presidential veto from the absolute veto possessed by the British monarch. "The qualified negative of the President differs widely from the absolute negative of the British sovereign," he explained, noting that the monarch's veto was absolute and final, whereas the President's could be overridden by a congressional supermajority [069-007].

Hamilton identified two purposes for the veto in Federalist No. 73: first, to enable the President "to defend himself" against legislative encroachments on executive power; and second, "to increase the chances in favor of the community against the passing of bad laws, through haste, inadvertence, or design" [073-017]. The latter purpose, often overlooked, reveals that the veto was designed not merely as a separation-of-powers mechanism but as a general safeguard against ill-considered legislation.

## Historical Context

The concept of an executive veto was well known to the Framers from British constitutional practice, where the monarch possessed an absolute veto (though it had not been exercised since 1708). The Framers deliberately rejected the absolute veto in favor of a qualified one, reflecting their commitment to legislative supremacy tempered by executive checks.

The negative example of state constitutions under the Articles of Confederation also informed the Framers' thinking. Several states had experimented with executive councils or weak governors lacking veto power, and the resulting legislative dominance was seen as a failure of balanced government. The qualified negative was designed to remedy this constitutional defect.

## Key Arguments

**A defensive, not offensive, power.** Hamilton insisted that "the primary inducement to conferring the power in question upon the Executive is, to enable him to defend himself" [073-015]. The veto was not a tool for the President to shape legislation proactively, but a shield against legislative overreach.

**Qualified, not absolute.** The Framers' choice of a qualified rather than absolute negative reflected their understanding that final authority should rest with the people's representatives. The two-thirds override threshold was "a salutary check" on both executive obstruction and legislative haste [073-006].

**Protection against faction.** Hamilton argued that the veto would deter the passage of factional legislation. "The injury which may possibly be done by defeating a few good laws," he wrote, "will be amply compensated by the advantage of preventing a number of bad ones" [073-017].

**Comparison to New York's council of revision.** Hamilton noted that the proposed veto more closely resembled the New York model, where the governor shared veto power with judges in a council of revision, than the Massachusetts model of a pure executive veto [069-013]. This comparison was strategic: New York was the ratification debate's key battleground.

## Constitutional Implementation

The veto power is established in Article I, Section 7, Clause 2 of the Constitution. The President has ten days (excluding Sundays) to sign or veto a bill; failure to act results in the bill becoming law unless Congress adjourns during the ten-day period, in which case the bill dies — the so-called "pocket veto."

Historically, presidential vetoes have been relatively rare. Andrew Jackson was the first president to use the veto extensively, expanding its scope beyond constitutional objections to include policy disagreements. The override threshold has proven a formidable barrier: fewer than ten percent of all vetoes have been overridden.

## Anti-Federalist Counterarguments

Anti-Federalists argued that the qualified negative gave the President too much power, particularly when combined with the four-year term and eligibility for re-election. They warned that a president might use the veto to extort concessions from Congress or to block popular legislation. Some, like George Mason, argued that the veto should be shared with a council or with the judiciary, as in New York's system.

## Legacy and Significance

The presidential veto has evolved into one of the most powerful tools in American governance. While Hamilton envisioned it primarily as a defensive mechanism, modern presidents use the veto — and the threat of a veto — to shape legislation proactively. The rise of the "veto threat" as a bargaining tool represents a departure from Hamilton's original conception, though it remains consistent with the broader purpose of executive participation in the legislative process.

The line-item veto, briefly adopted in 1996 and struck down by the Supreme Court in *Clinton v. City of New York* (1998), represented a modern attempt to expand the veto power. The Court's decision reaffirmed the constitutional text, which requires the President to accept or reject an entire bill, not selected provisions.
