# Treaty Power

## Definition

The treaty power is the constitutional authority to negotiate, ratify, and enforce binding agreements with foreign nations. Under the Constitution, the President possesses the power to make treaties "by and with the advice and consent of the Senate," provided two-thirds of the senators present concur. Treaties, along with the Constitution and federal laws, constitute the supreme law of the land.

## In The Federalist Papers

The treaty power received sustained attention from Publius, particularly from John Jay in the early essays. Jay, who had served as Secretary of Foreign Affairs under the Confederation and had negotiated the Treaty of Paris, spoke with particular authority on the subject. In Federalist No. 3, Jay pointed to the case of "the treaty of peace with Britain" as evidence that a unified national government was better positioned than individual states to ensure compliance with international obligations [003-012].

Hamilton, in Federalist No. 15, returned to the treaty of peace as a cautionary example, observing that under the Confederation certain states had frustrated the treaty's execution despite its binding character under international law [015-005]. He argued that a government "clothed with the full authority of the Union" was necessary to prevent such violations by individual states [015-010].

In Federalist No. 64, Jay provided the most detailed defense of the treaty-making process established by the Constitution. He argued that the requirement of Senate consent — by a two-thirds supermajority — ensured both deliberation and broad consensus in treaty-making. He contrasted this with monarchical systems where treaties were the prerogative of a single individual, often exercised with little regard for the public interest.

## Historical Context

The treaty power was one of the most glaring deficiencies of the Articles of Confederation. Under the Articles, Congress could negotiate treaties but had no means to enforce them against recalcitrant states. This weakness was exposed by the Treaty of Paris (1783), which ended the Revolutionary War. Britain cited American violations of the treaty — including state laws impeding the collection of pre-war debts owed to British creditors — as justification for maintaining military posts in the Northwest Territory. The Confederation Congress was powerless to compel compliance.

The Constitutional Convention addressed this deficiency by making treaties part of the supreme law of the land (Article VI) and by establishing a clear — though carefully divided — treaty-making process. The division of the treaty power between the President and the Senate reflected both the influence of Montesquieu's separation-of-powers theory and the Founders' pragmatic concern that treaties required both energy (executive negotiation) and wisdom (senatorial deliberation).

## Key Arguments

**Federal supremacy in foreign affairs.** Jay and Hamilton both insisted that treaty compliance was impossible under a confederation of sovereign states. A federal government with direct authority over individuals — not merely states — was the only structure capable of honoring international commitments [015-010].

**Deliberative treaty-making.** In Federalist No. 64, Jay argued that the Senate's role in treaty-making was essential to prevent rash or corrupt agreements. The two-thirds threshold ensured that treaties would not be made "without the fullest information, and the most mature deliberation."

**The treaty power and the separation of powers.** Hamilton, in Federalist No. 75, addressed the hybrid character of treaty-making — executive in negotiation but legislative in effect. He defended the joint role of the President and Senate as a necessary accommodation between the need for "secrecy and dispatch" in negotiation and the need for "the exercise of the legislative power" in ratification.

## Constitutional Implementation

The treaty power is established through:

- **Article II, Section 2, Clause 2**: The President "shall have Power, by and with the Advice and Consent of the Senate, to make Treaties, provided two thirds of the Senators present concur."
- **Article VI, Clause 2 (Supremacy Clause)**: "All treaties made, or which shall be made, under the authority of the United States, shall be the supreme law of the land."
- **Article I, Section 10**: States are absolutely prohibited from entering into "any treaty, alliance, or confederation," reserving foreign relations exclusively to the federal government.

The treaty power has been shaped by subsequent practice. The rise of executive agreements — international agreements made by the President without Senate consent — has significantly expanded presidential authority in foreign affairs beyond what the Federalist essays contemplated. The Supreme Court has generally upheld these arrangements, though the precise boundaries between treaties and executive agreements remain contested.

## Anti-Federalist Counterarguments

Anti-Federalists raised two principal objections to the treaty power. First, they argued that the combination of the treaty power and the Supremacy Clause would allow treaties to override state laws and even constitutional protections — a concern that later resurfaced in debates over the proposed Bricker Amendment (1950s). Second, they objected to the two-thirds Senate threshold as insufficient, arguing that treaties — which could commit the nation to war or entangling alliances — should require broader consensus. Some Anti-Federalists proposed requiring House concurrence as well.

## Legacy and Significance

The treaty power established by the Constitution has enabled the United States to function as a unified actor in international affairs. From the Louisiana Purchase (1803) — accomplished by treaty — to the NATO alliance and arms control agreements of the modern era, the treaty-making process has been a central instrument of American foreign policy. The tension between the original constitutional design (requiring Senate consent) and the modern practice of executive agreements reflects a continuing debate over the proper balance of power in foreign affairs — a debate rooted in the arguments of the Federalist Papers.
