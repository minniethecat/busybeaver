# Suffrage

## Definition

**Suffrage** is the right to vote in elections for public office. In the founding era, suffrage was not a universal right but a privilege determined by state law, typically restricted to white male property owners. The Federalist Papers address suffrage primarily in the context of the House of Representatives, where the Constitution ties the franchise for congressional elections to each state's qualifications for its own lower house — a compromise that preserved state control over voting rights while establishing a direct popular foundation for the federal government.

## In The Federalist Papers

Madison addresses suffrage most directly in Federalist Nos. 52–54, which defend the structure and election of the House of Representatives. The Constitution provides that "the electors in each State shall have the qualifications requisite for electors of the most numerous branch of the State legislature" — meaning that states, not Congress, determine who may vote in federal elections. Madison defends this arrangement as both practical and principled: it avoids the "inconvenience" of a uniform federal suffrage standard while ensuring that those who choose state legislators also choose their federal representatives.

Hamilton, in Federalist No. 60, addresses concerns that Congress might manipulate elections to exclude certain classes of voters. He dismisses these fears, arguing that "the several States would never permit such an abuse" and that the diverse interests represented in Congress — "landed, mercantile, and manufacturing — men of different professions and occupations" — would prevent any single faction from restricting the franchise to its own advantage (035-012).

The broader Federalist theory of suffrage is grounded in the concept of popular sovereignty. Madison argues in Federalist No. 39 that the House of Representatives is "directly elected by the people" — making it, along with the presidency (indirectly elected), the foundation of the government's republican character. The people's "suffrages" — their votes — are the mechanism by which they consent to be governed and hold their representatives accountable.

## Historical Context

In 1787, suffrage laws varied widely among the states. Most required either property ownership or tax payment; some allowed free African Americans to vote; New Jersey briefly allowed propertied women to vote. The Framers did not attempt to impose a uniform suffrage standard — both because there was no consensus on what qualifications were appropriate and because state control of suffrage was a concession to the states' traditional authority over elections.

The omission of a federal suffrage standard became a point of Anti-Federalist criticism. Some argued that without explicit protections, Congress might restrict the franchise; others contended that the absence of a property requirement for federal electors would allow the "unpropertied multitude" to elect demagogues. The Federalist response was that the diversity of state suffrage laws served as a natural check: Congress, representing states with different qualifications, could never agree on a uniform restriction.

## Key Arguments

1. **State control preserves diversity**: Tying federal suffrage to state qualifications avoids the "extreme difficulty" of establishing a uniform national standard while respecting state sovereignty over elections.

2. **Popular election ensures accountability**: The House of Representatives, "dependent on the people alone" for its election, ensures that the government remains "strictly republican" — deriving its authority from "the great body of the people" rather than from the states as political entities.

3. **Diverse interests prevent manipulation**: The variety of economic interests represented in Congress — landowners, merchants, manufacturers, professionals — makes it impossible for any single class to capture the electoral process for its exclusive benefit.

## Constitutional Implementation

Suffrage is primarily addressed in **Article I, Section 2**: the House of Representatives shall be "chosen every second Year by the People of the several States," and "the Electors in each State shall have the Qualifications requisite for Electors of the most numerous Branch of the State Legislature." The Seventeenth Amendment (1913) extended this same principle to the Senate. Subsequent amendments expanded the franchise: the Fifteenth (race, 1870), Nineteenth (sex, 1920), Twenty-Fourth (poll tax abolition, 1964), and Twenty-Sixth (age 18, 1971).

## Legacy and Significance

The original constitutional compromise on suffrage — leaving qualifications to the states — proved remarkably durable. It was not until the Reconstruction Amendments that the federal government began to impose limits on state suffrage laws, and not until the Voting Rights Act of 1965 that Congress actively enforced those limits. The Federalist vision of suffrage as a state-regulated privilege gave way, over two centuries, to the modern understanding of voting as a fundamental constitutional right.
