# Faction

## Definition

A **faction** is "a number of citizens, whether amounting to a majority or a minority of the whole, who are united and actuated by some common impulse of passion, or of interest, adversed to the rights of other citizens, or to the permanent and aggregate interests of the community" (010-004). In The Federalist Papers, faction is treated as the central problem of popular government — an inevitable byproduct of liberty that must be controlled through constitutional design rather than suppressed through tyranny.

## In The Federalist Papers

James Madison provides the most thorough analysis of faction in Federalist No. 10, widely regarded as the single most important essay in the collection. Madison frames faction as the disease for which the proposed Constitution provides the cure (010-003), identifying two possible methods of addressing the problem: "the one, by removing its causes; the other, by controlling its effects" (010-005).

Madison argues that removing the causes of faction is both impossible and undesirable. The causes can be removed either by "destroying the liberty which is essential to its existence" or by "giving to every citizen the same opinions, the same passions, and the same interests" (010-006). The first remedy would be "worse than the disease," for "liberty is to faction what air is to fire, an aliment without which it instantly expires" (010-007). The second remedy is "impracticable" because the diversity of human faculties — from which differing property rights originate — naturally produces divergent opinions and interests (010-009).

Having rejected the removal of causes, Madison turns to controlling the effects. He distinguishes between minority and majority factions: a minority faction may be "relieved" by the "republican principle" of majority rule through regular votes (010-013). But a majority faction poses a graver threat, for "no cure for the mischiefs of faction" exists when "a majority is included in a faction" under pure democracy (010-015). Madison's celebrated solution is the **extended republic**: a large, diverse republic encompassing many interests makes it "more difficult for all who feel it to discover their own strength, and to act in unison with each other" (010-023). Hamilton reinforces this analysis in Federalist No. 9, where he praises the proposed Union as "a barrier against domestic faction and insurrection" (009-003). Madison elsewhere warns that a successful faction "may erect a tyranny on the ruins of order and law" (021-006), and that a "spirit of faction" is "apt to mingle its poison in the deliberations of all bodies of men" (015-014).

> Liberty is to faction what air is to fire, an aliment without which it instantly expires. But it could not be less folly to abolish liberty, which is essential to political life, because it nourishes faction, than it would be to wish the annihilation of air, which is essential to animal life, because it imparts to fire its destructive agency.
>
> (010-007)

## Historical Context

Madison's theory of faction draws heavily on the eighteenth-century understanding of human nature and the historical experience of republics. The classical republican tradition — from Aristotle through Machiavelli to Montesquieu — had long warned that republics were inherently fragile, prone to decay through internal strife. Montesquieu, in *The Spirit of Laws* (1748), argued that republican government required a small territory and virtuous citizenry to survive, a view Madison directly challenged.

The immediate context was the experience of the American states under the Articles of Confederation (1781–1789). State legislatures had passed paper-money laws benefiting debtor factions, Shays' Rebellion (1786–1787) in Massachusetts demonstrated the danger of armed faction, and interstate commercial disputes revealed the weakness of the Confederation. Madison's analysis in Federalist No. 10 was a direct response to these events, arguing that the cure was not smaller republics (as Montesquieu suggested) but a larger, more diverse one.

## Key Arguments

Madison advances four interconnected arguments about faction in Federalist No. 10. First, faction is inevitable: the "latent causes of faction are thus sown in the nature of man" — diverse opinions, passions, and especially the "various and unequal distribution of property" make faction unavoidable in any free society (010-009). Second, removing the causes of faction would be worse than the disease: destroying liberty to eliminate faction is tyrannical, and enforcing uniformity of opinion is impossible. The only viable approach is to control the effects (010-005).

Third, the extended republic is the cure: a large republic with many competing interests prevents any single faction from dominating, because the "greater number of citizens and extent of territory" in the proposed Union makes it harder for factions to organize and act in concert (010-023). Fourth, representation refines and enlarges public views: by passing popular opinions through a chosen body of citizens, a republic can "refine and enlarge the public views" — representatives are more likely to discern the true interest of the country than the people themselves, especially on complex constitutional questions (010-023).

## Constitutional Implementation

The Constitution implements Madison's faction-control strategy through several interlocking structural mechanisms. The extended sphere gives the national government jurisdiction over the entire Union, making it harder for localized factions to capture national policy. The separation of legislative, executive, and judicial power across three branches prevents any single faction from controlling all levers of government simultaneously. Bicameralism further divides legislative power, with the House and Senate having different constituencies and term lengths, making it harder for a transient majority faction to enact its will without deliberation. At the vertical level, federalism creates multiple arenas of political competition by dividing power between state and national governments, reducing the stakes of any single contest. Finally, regular elections — the "republican principle" of periodic voting — allow minority factions to be outvoted and majority factions to be replaced.

## Anti-Federalist Counterarguments

Anti-Federalist writers, particularly Brutus and the Federal Farmer, challenged Madison's faction theory on several grounds. They argued that the extended republic would produce a **ruling aristocracy** rather than genuine diversity of interests — representatives from distant districts would be disconnected from the people. They warned that a large republic would inevitably consolidate power in the hands of the "natural aristocracy" of wealth and influence, creating a different kind of faction problem: rule by an unrepresentative elite. The Anti-Federalists preferred small, homogeneous republics where citizens shared common interests and could directly monitor their representatives.

## Legacy and Significance

Madison's theory of faction in Federalist No. 10 has become one of the most influential arguments in American political thought. The idea that a large, diverse republic can tame faction by multiplying interests — rather than by suppressing them — represents a fundamental innovation in republican theory. It transformed the conventional understanding of republics from small, fragile city-states to large, durable commercial nations.

In the twentieth century, political scientists elaborated Madison's insight into **pluralist theory**, which holds that democratic politics consists of competition among overlapping interest groups, none of which can permanently dominate. The Supreme Court has cited Federalist No. 10 in campaign finance and political speech cases, debating Madison's vision of faction control in the modern regulatory state. Critics have questioned whether economic inequality can so concentrate power as to overcome the faction-dispersing mechanisms Madison described.
