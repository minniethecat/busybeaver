# William Blackstone (1723–1780)

## Role in The Federalist

Blackstone is the most cited legal authority in The Federalist Papers. His *Commentaries on the Laws of England* provided the Framers with a comprehensive synthesis of English common law — the legal tradition from which American constitutional concepts of due process, habeas corpus, and judicial independence were derived. When Publius invokes "the celebrated Blackstone" or refers to "the common law of England," he is appealing to an authority universally recognized by both Federalists and Anti-Federalists.

Hamilton cites Blackstone in Federalist No. 69, quoting his observation that the king of Great Britain's prerogative as commander-in-chief was "immemorial" and only disputed by "the Long Parliament of Charles I" (069-015). In Federalist No. 84, Hamilton argues that habeas corpus and other common-law protections — described at length in Blackstone's *Commentaries* — are "greater securities to liberty and republicanism than any which the State constitutions contain" (084-006).

## Biography

William Blackstone was born in London in 1723. He studied at Pembroke College, Oxford, was called to the bar, and in 1758 became the first Vinerian Professor of English Law at Oxford — the first professor of English common law at any university. His lectures formed the basis of the *Commentaries on the Laws of England*, published in four volumes between 1765 and 1769. The *Commentaries* became the standard text for legal education in both England and America, selling more copies in the American colonies than in Britain.

Blackstone later served as a Member of Parliament and as a justice of the Court of Common Pleas. His work systematized the sprawling body of English common law into a coherent framework organized around the rights of persons, property, and the state — a framework that deeply influenced the Framers' understanding of constitutional government.

## Key Ideas / Contributions

1. **The rule of law**: Government must operate through established laws, not arbitrary will — a principle fundamental to the American constitutional order.
2. **Habeas corpus as the "Great Writ"**: Blackstone's description of habeas corpus as "the most celebrated writ in the English law" informed the Constitution's explicit protection of the writ.
3. **The common law tradition**: Rights are not created by government but recognized and protected by it — a principle underlying the Ninth Amendment.

## Legacy

Blackstone's *Commentaries* remained the foundational text of American legal education well into the nineteenth century. Abraham Lincoln famously studied Blackstone as a young lawyer. The Supreme Court continues to cite Blackstone on questions of common-law rights, habeas corpus, and the original understanding of constitutional protections. His influence on the American legal tradition is second only to that of the Constitution itself.
