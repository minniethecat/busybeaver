# Republicanism

## Definition

**Republicanism** is the principle that government derives its just powers from the consent of the governed and that political authority should be exercised through elected representatives rather than directly by the people or by hereditary rulers. In The Federalist Papers, republicanism serves as the non-negotiable foundation of the American constitutional order — "the only admissible principle of republican government" being that power flows from the people through representative institutions (028-003).

## In The Federalist Papers

Madison directly addresses the meaning of republicanism in Federalist No. 10, distinguishing a republic from a democracy. In a pure democracy, "a small number of citizens... assemble and administer the government in person"; in a republic, the scheme of representation takes place, and the government is delegated to "a small number of citizens elected by the rest" (010-025). This distinction is central to the entire Federalist argument: the proposed Constitution creates a republic, not a democracy, and the difference is structural, not semantic.

Madison refutes the conventional wisdom — derived from Montesquieu — that "republican government" is limited to "a narrow district." He calls this "the error which limits republican government to a narrow district" and argues that the American innovation is to demonstrate that republican principles can operate across an extended territory through the mechanism of representation (014-004).

Hamilton, in Federalist No. 9, traces the historical reputation of republican governments, noting that the "portraits" sketched by ancient historians "of republican government were too just copies of the originals from which they were taken" — the turbulent, short-lived republics of Greece and Rome (009-005). The proposed Constitution aims to rescue republicanism from this historical record by adding stability, energy, and structure. The "fundamental maxim of republican government" requires "that the sense of the majority should prevail" (022-009), yet Madison is acutely aware that majority rule can become tyrannical without structural safeguards — a tension that drives the entire architecture of checks and balances.

> The error which limits republican government to a narrow district has been unfolded and refuted in preceding papers. I remark here only that it seems to owe its rise and prevalence chiefly to the confounding of a republic with a democracy, and applying to the former reasonings drawn from the nature of the latter.
>
> (014-004)

## Historical Context

Republicanism in the eighteenth century was understood primarily through the classical examples of Rome (before the Caesars), Athens, Sparta, and Carthage. These ancient republics were admired for their liberty but feared for their instability — they "vibrated between the extremes of tyranny and anarchy" (009-005). Montesquieu's *The Spirit of Laws* (1748) systematized the view that republics required small territories, virtuous citizens, and simple manners to survive.

The American Revolution (1776–1783) was fought in the name of republican principles against monarchical rule. But the state constitutions of 1776–1780, and the Articles of Confederation, had produced what many Federalists considered debased forms of republicanism: legislative supremacy, paper-money schemes, and debtor-protection laws that violated property rights. The Constitutional Convention of 1787 was called specifically to "render the constitution of the federal government adequate to the exigencies of the Union" — and to save republicanism from its own excesses.

## Key Arguments

Publius argues that the Constitution both preserves and perfects republican government through four interlocking principles. Representation refines and enlarges public views: by passing popular impulses through a deliberative assembly, republicanism filters out "temporary or partial considerations" in favor of the public good, and representatives are expected to possess greater wisdom and patriotism than the average citizen. The extended republic tames faction: Madison's central innovation is that a large, diverse republic prevents any single faction from dominating because the "greater variety of parties and interests" makes it "less probable that a majority of the whole will have a common motive to invade the rights of other citizens" (010-025). The separation of powers preserves republican liberty: unlike the classical republics where all power was concentrated in a single assembly, the Constitution divides legislative, executive, and judicial power — and because "in republican government, the legislative authority necessarily predominates" (051-008), bicameralism and the executive veto are needed to check it. Finally, republican government requires executive energy: Hamilton devotes Federalist No. 70 to arguing that "energy in the executive is a leading character in the definition of good government" — a vigorous, unitary executive is essential to protect the republic, administer the laws, and secure property (070-003).

## Constitutional Implementation

The Constitution operationalizes republicanism through four structural commitments. Article IV, Section 4 — the Guarantee Clause — binds the federal government to "guarantee to every State in this Union a Republican Form of Government." Bicameralism embodies the republican principle in the House of Representatives, directly elected by the people, while the Senate was originally elected by state legislatures as an expression of the federal principle. Regular elections ensure continuing accountability: House members face election every two years, the President every four, and Senators (originally) every six. Finally, Article I, Sections 9 and 10 prohibit both the federal and state governments from granting titles of nobility, reinforcing the break from aristocratic and monarchical forms.

## Anti-Federalist Counterarguments

Anti-Federalists challenged the Federalist claim to republicanism on several grounds. They argued that a genuine republic required a small territory, because a large republic would produce representatives disconnected from their constituents, creating an effective aristocracy. They insisted on annual elections, contending that the two-year House term and six-year Senate term were too long for genuine accountability. They demanded rotation in office, warning that without term limits, a permanent governing class would emerge. And they insisted on a bill of rights, arguing that republican government required explicit protection of individual liberties against the very government it created.

## Legacy and Significance

The Federalist's theory of republicanism transformed Western political thought. Madison's argument that a large republic could be *more* stable than a small one — because diversity of interests checks faction better than civic virtue alone — overturned two thousand years of received wisdom. The American republic became the model for republican movements in France (1789), Latin America (1810s–1820s), and eventually across the globe.

In American constitutional law, the Guarantee Clause has been held non-justiciable (a "political question" for Congress, not the courts, to enforce). But the broader republican principles of representation, separation of powers, and limited government remain foundational to constitutional interpretation.
