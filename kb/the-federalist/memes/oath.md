# Oath of Office

The **constitutional oath** is the solemn pledge required by Article VI of the United States Constitution, which mandates that all legislative, executive, and judicial officers of both the United States and the several states "shall be bound by oath or affirmation to support this Constitution." In the Federalist Papers, the oath provision was discussed as part of the broader analysis of the Supremacy Clause and the mechanisms by which federal law would be given effect within the states.

## In the Federalist Papers

The constitutional oath requirement appears in the Federalist Papers primarily in the context of debates over federal supremacy and the relationship between the state and national governments. The provision that state officers — as well as federal ones — must swear to support the federal Constitution provoked questioning from those who viewed it as an encroachment on state sovereignty.

Hamilton addressed the oath in Federalist No. 27, where he argued that the Constitution's extension of federal authority to individual citizens — rather than merely to the states in their collective capacity — would enable the federal government to employ "the ordinary magistracy of each" in executing its laws. The oath was the mechanism that bound those magistrates:

> It merits particular attention in this place, that the laws of the Confederacy, as to the ENUMERATED and LEGITIMATE objects of its jurisdiction, will become the SUPREME LAW of the land; to the observance of which all officers, legislative, executive, and judicial, in each State, will be bound by the sanctity of an oath. Thus the legislatures, courts, and magistrates, of the respective members, will be incorporated into the operations of the national government AS FAR AS ITS JUST AND CONSTITUTIONAL AUTHORITY EXTENDS; and will be rendered auxiliary to the enforcement of its laws. [027-008]

Madison provided the most detailed analysis of the oath in Federalist No. 44, where he addressed an objection that remains pertinent: why impose an oath on state officers to support the federal Constitution while not requiring a corresponding oath from federal officers to support state constitutions?

> It has been asked why it was thought necessary, that the State magistracy should be bound to support the federal Constitution, and unnecessary that a like oath should be imposed on the officers of the United States, in favor of the State constitutions. [044-027]

Madison's answer rested on a functional distinction that was, in his words, "obvious and conclusive":

> The members of the federal government will have no agency in carrying the State constitutions into effect. The members and officers of the State governments, on the contrary, will have an essential agency in giving effect to the federal Constitution. The election of the President and Senate will depend, in all cases, on the legislatures of the several States. And the election of the House of Representatives will equally depend on the same authority in the first instance; and will, probably, forever be conducted by the officers, and according to the laws, of the States. [044-028]

## Purpose and Function

The oath served multiple functions in the constitutional design. First, it established a formal obligation binding the conscience of every public officer to the constitutional order. Second, it provided a legal basis for holding officers accountable — violation of the oath constituted not merely a breach of duty but a breach of sworn faith. Third, and most significantly in the federal context, it created a direct relationship between state officers and the federal Constitution, bypassing the intermediate authority of state governments.

Hamilton argued that the oath, combined with the extension of federal authority to individual citizens, would make the federal government familiar and integrated rather than alien and imposed. [027-008] The oath was, in effect, a constitutional mechanism for weaving the state and federal governments into a single fabric of obligation.

## Oath vs. Affirmation

The Constitution's provision for "oath or affirmation" reflected a practical accommodation of religious diversity. Certain religious traditions — notably the Quakers — objected to oath-taking on theological grounds. By permitting an affirmation as an alternative, the Constitution ensured that conscientious scruples would not bar any person from public office. This distinction was one of several constitutional provisions demonstrating the framers' attention to religious liberty.

## Federal and State Obligation

The extension of the oath requirement to state officers was a deliberate structural choice. It signified that the Constitution was not merely a compact among states but a fundamental law binding on all who exercised public authority within the United States. State judges, legislators, and executive officers were made personally responsible for upholding the federal constitutional order — not merely through the political discipline of their state governments, but through their own sworn obligation.

This mechanism reinforced the Supremacy Clause by ensuring that every officer who might be called upon to apply federal law or to respect federal constitutional limits had personally undertaken to do so. The oath thus served as both a symbol of national unity and a practical instrument of constitutional enforcement.

## In Federalist No. 44

Madison presented the oath as part of the "sixth and last class" of constitutional provisions — those "by which efficacy is given to all the rest." [044-011] It was enumerated alongside the Necessary and Proper Clause and the Supremacy Clause as a mechanism for ensuring that the constitutional scheme would actually operate in practice. The oath was presented not as an isolated provision but as part of a comprehensive system of constitutional safeguards — along with the prohibition on bills of attainder, ex post facto laws, and laws impairing the obligation of contracts — designed to secure the foundations of the federal union against both state encroachment and momentary popular passion. [044-004]–[044-008]
