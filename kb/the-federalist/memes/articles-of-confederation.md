# Articles of Confederation

## Overview

The Articles of Confederation and Perpetual Union was the first constitution of the United States, adopted by the Continental Congress in 1777 and ratified by all thirteen states in 1781. It created a loose confederation of sovereign states with a weak central government possessing no power to tax, regulate commerce, or enforce its laws directly on individuals. Its inadequacies — dramatized by Shays' Rebellion and the Confederation's inability to fund the national debt or negotiate effectively with foreign powers — provided the impetus for the Constitutional Convention of 1787.

## In The Federalist Papers

The Articles of Confederation serve as the negative reference point for virtually every argument in The Federalist Papers. Publius's strategy is to demonstrate, through systematic analysis, that the Articles' defects are not accidental but structural — inherent in the principle of a government that operates on states rather than on individuals.

Hamilton, in Federalist No. 15, delivers the most damning indictment: "The great and radical vice in the construction of the existing Confederation is in the principle of LEGISLATION for STATES or GOVERNMENTS, in their CORPORATE or COLLECTIVE CAPACITIES, and as contradistinguished from the INDIVIDUALS of which they consist" (015-003). Because the Confederation cannot reach individuals directly, its laws are "mere recommendations which the States observe or disregard at their option" (015-017).

In Federalist No. 22, Hamilton catalogs the Confederation's specific defects: the lack of a federal judiciary, the equal representation of states regardless of population, the absence of an independent executive, and — most fatally — the requirement of unanimous consent for amendments. The Confederation "never had a ratification by the PEOPLE" and therefore lacked the moral and legal authority that only popular sovereignty can confer (022-021).

## Key Defects

1. **No power to tax**: Congress could requisition funds from the states but had no power to compel payment. The national debt went unfunded.
2. **No power to regulate commerce**: States imposed tariffs on each other's goods, leading to commercial warfare.
3. **No independent executive**: Executive functions were performed by congressional committees, producing weak and inconsistent administration.
4. **No federal judiciary**: Disputes between states could not be resolved by a neutral tribunal.
5. **Unanimous amendment requirement**: Any single state could block reform, making the system impossible to improve.

## Legacy

The Articles of Confederation served as the necessary failure that made the Constitution possible. Without the Confederation's demonstrated inadequacy, the states would never have surrendered the sovereignty the Constitution required. The Federalist Papers' systematic critique of the Articles established the method of constitutional argument — identifying structural defects, tracing their consequences, and proposing institutional remedies — that remains the standard for constitutional analysis.
