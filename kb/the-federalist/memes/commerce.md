# Commerce

## Definition

**Commerce** encompasses the production, transportation, and exchange of goods across political boundaries — whether between states or between nations. In the Federalist Papers, commerce is treated not merely as an economic activity but as a strategic instrument of national power: a flourishing commerce funds the treasury, builds a navy, projects influence abroad, and binds the states together in mutual interest.

## In The Federalist Papers

The Federalist Papers devote sustained attention to commerce, particularly in Federalist Nos. 11–13, where Hamilton argues that a unified national commercial policy is indispensable to American prosperity and security.

Hamilton opens Federalist No. 11 with a bold assertion: "The importance of the Union, in a commercial light, is one of those points about which there is least room to entertain a difference of opinion" (011-003). He envisions an "active commerce, an extensive navigation, and a flourishing marine" that would make America "the admiration and envy of the world" (011-009). A disunited America, by contrast, would see its commerce "a prey to the wanton intermeddlings of all nations at war with each other" (011-008).

> An active commerce, an extensive navigation, and a flourishing marine would then be the inevitable offspring of moral and physical necessity. We might defy the little arts of the little politicians to control or vary the irresistible and unchangeable course of nature.
>
> (011-009)

Hamilton sees commerce as a means of national leverage: European powers, dependent on American raw materials and markets, would "take care not to furnish us with provocations" if faced with "a strict and universal alliance" of the American states (011-005). A unified commercial policy would enable the United States to negotiate favorable treaties, retaliate against discriminatory trade practices, and build the naval strength that commerce both requires and sustains.

In Federalist No. 12, Hamilton links commerce to public revenue: "The prosperity of commerce is now perceived and acknowledged by all enlightened statesmen to be the most useful as well as the most productive source of national wealth" (012-004). A vigorous commerce generates customs duties — the principal source of federal revenue in the founding era — without burdening citizens with direct taxes. He calculates that "a nation cannot long exist without revenues" and that commerce provides "a great proportion of those revenues" with minimal intrusiveness (012-012).

The regulation of interstate commerce also serves as a remedy for the commercial rivalries that plagued the states under the Articles of Confederation. Hamilton warns that without federal oversight, "each State, or distinct confederacy, would pursue a system of commercial policy peculiar to itself" — resulting in "the rivalships and competitions of commerce between commercial nations" that breed conflict (006-005). The Commerce Clause would transform thirteen competing commercial systems into a single free-trade zone, preventing the retaliatory tariffs and navigation disputes that had already begun to poison relations among the states.

## Historical Context

In the 1780s, the American economy was in disarray. The Articles of Confederation gave Congress no power to regulate commerce, leaving each state free to impose tariffs on goods from neighboring states. States like New York and Virginia used their geographic advantages to tax goods destined for New Jersey and North Carolina, generating resentment and economic inefficiency. Foreign nations — particularly Britain — exploited American disunity by closing their West Indian ports to American shipping while flooding American markets with manufactured goods.

The Annapolis Convention of 1786, which called for the Constitutional Convention, was itself convened to address commercial disputes among the states. The Framers understood that commercial regulation was not a peripheral concern but a primary reason for replacing the Articles with a stronger national government. The Commerce Clause (Article I, Section 8, Clause 3) was among the least controversial grants of federal power at the Convention.

## Key Arguments

Hamilton advances four principal arguments for federal commercial power, each building on the central premise that a unified national commercial policy serves American interests in ways that thirteen separate state policies cannot.

Commercial unity, Hamilton argued first, enables the United States to negotiate favorable treaties from a position of strength. A unified America could offer access to its vast market in exchange for reciprocal concessions; a fragmented America, by contrast, would find its commerce "a prey to the wanton intermeddlings of all nations" and would be unable to extract favorable terms from European powers who could play the states against one another (011-005). The collective bargaining power of the Union was, in Hamilton's analysis, a commercial asset of the first importance.

Hamilton's second argument linked commerce to public revenue. Customs duties on imports — "taxes on consumption" as he described them — provided reliable federal revenue with far less intrusiveness than direct taxation. "The prosperity of commerce," he wrote, "is now perceived and acknowledged by all enlightened statesmen to be the most useful as well as the most productive source of national wealth" (012-004). A vigorous commerce would fill the federal treasury without burdening individual citizens, making the government both solvent and popular.

The third argument connected commerce to naval power. Hamilton saw the two as mutually reinforcing: commerce required a navy to protect shipping lanes and deter predators, while a navy required the revenues, materials, and maritime expertise that commerce alone could provide. A flourishing American commerce would, he predicted, produce "a navy [that] must, from necessary connection, follow the progress of commerce" (011-009). This navy would in turn project American power, protect neutral shipping rights, and command the respect of European empires.

Finally, Hamilton contended that federal commercial regulation was essential to preventing domestic conflict among the states. Without a common commercial policy, each state would pursue its own economic interests at the expense of its neighbors — imposing retaliatory tariffs, restricting access to ports, and generating "the rivalships and competitions of commerce" that, as European history demonstrated, had repeatedly led to war among commercial nations (006-005). The Commerce Clause would transform thirteen competing commercial systems into a single free-trade zone, eliminating the economic grievances that threatened the Union from within.

## Constitutional Implementation

The Commerce Clause (Article I, Section 8, Clause 3) empowers Congress "to regulate Commerce with foreign Nations, and among the several States, and with the Indian Tribes." Other commerce-related provisions include:

- **Export Clause (Article I, Section 9)**: No tax or duty may be laid on articles exported from any state — preventing Congress from favoring one region's exports over another's.
- **Port Preference Clause (Article I, Section 9)**: No preference shall be given to the ports of one state over those of another.
- **Tonnage Duties (Article I, Section 10)**: States may not levy tonnage duties without congressional consent.
- **Import-Export Clause (Article I, Section 10)**: States may not, without congressional consent, lay imposts or duties on imports or exports.

## Legacy and Significance

The Commerce Clause became the single most expansive source of federal regulatory authority in American constitutional history. The Supreme Court's broad interpretation — particularly after the New Deal — transformed a limited grant to prevent interstate trade barriers into authority over virtually all economic activity affecting interstate commerce. Landmark cases from *Gibbons v. Ogden* (1824) to *Wickard v. Filburn* (1942) traced a path from Hamilton's vision of commercial unity to a federal government capable of regulating wages, working conditions, environmental standards, and civil rights in private businesses.

The late 20th century saw a partial retrenchment, with *United States v. Lopez* (1995) and *National Federation of Independent Business v. Sebelius* (2012) marking the first Supreme Court decisions in decades to limit the commerce power. Yet the core insight of Federalist Nos. 11–12 — that commercial regulation is essential to national prosperity and unity — remains firmly embedded in American constitutional law.
