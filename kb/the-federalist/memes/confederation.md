## Overview

A confederation is a union of sovereign states that delegate limited authority to a common governing body while retaining the bulk of governmental power. The United States operated under a confederation — the Articles of Confederation — from 1781 until the ratification of the Constitution in 1788. The Federalist Papers are, in essence, an extended argument for replacing this confederal system with a stronger federal government capable of acting directly upon individuals rather than merely through the intermediary of state governments. Publius opens this argument by declaring that Americans have experienced "an unequivocal experience of the inefficacy of the subsisting federal government" (001-003).

## Defects of the Confederation

Publius devoted the earliest Federalist essays to diagnosing the failures of the Articles of Confederation. The defects fell into several categories, each receiving sustained analysis across Federalist Nos. 15–22.

The first and most fundamental defect was the inability to enforce treaties and laws uniformly. The Articles required states to comply with federal determinations, but provided no mechanism to compel compliance:

> The articles of confederation obliged the parties to submit to the determination of the federal judicatory. [007-005]

But without a judiciary to enforce such determinations, the obligation was merely theoretical. Other structural defects included:

- **No power to levy taxes directly** — the Confederation depended on state requisitions, which states routinely ignored. Publius notes that "the United States have an indefinite discretion to make requisitions for men and money; but they have no authority to raise either by regulations extending to the individual citizens" (015-015)
- **No power to regulate interstate commerce** — states erected trade barriers against each other, undermining the economic union
- **No unified military authority independent of state militias** — the Confederation could request troops but could not raise them
- **No federal judiciary** to interpret and apply national law uniformly across the states
- **Amendment required unanimity** — a single state could block necessary reforms

## Ancient Confederacies as Cautionary Tales

In Federalist Nos. 18–19, Madison and Hamilton examined the Amphictyonic Council and the Achaean League — two ancient Greek confederacies — to demonstrate the structural weaknesses inherent in confederal systems:

> The members of the Amphictyonic confederacy, according to the theory of their institution, were equal. [018-005]

Despite this theoretical equality, the Amphictyonic Council was repeatedly undermined by the stronger members dominating the weaker ones, ultimately resulting in fragmentation and foreign domination by Macedon and later Rome. The Achaean League, which adopted a somewhat stronger central government, fared better — but only because it moved beyond a purely confederal model toward a system in which the central authority could act directly on citizens rather than through member cities.

## Confederation vs. Federal Government

A key distinction Publius drew was between a confederation (governing states) and a national government (governing individuals). Critics of the Constitution objected that:

> This one tells us that the proposed Constitution ought to be rejected, because it is not a confederation of the States, but a government over individuals. [038-009]

Publius replied that this was precisely the point: a government over individuals was necessary to remedy the defects of a confederation. A confederation could only recommend; a federal government could command. The experience under the Articles of Confederation had demonstrated conclusively that recommendations to sovereign states were unenforceable. As Publius observed, "the sanction of considerations of public good, nor a respect for character" was sufficient to ensure state compliance — only the coercive authority of law operating directly on individuals could secure the purposes of union.

## The Prohibition on State Confederations

Under the new Constitution, states were explicitly forbidden from entering into independent confederations:

> No State shall enter into any treaty, alliance, or confederation. [044-004]

This prohibition was essential to prevent the formation of regional blocs that could undermine the Union — a threat that had already manifested under the Articles of Confederation, as evidenced by separate negotiations between states and foreign powers over navigation rights and territorial claims. Publius warned that without this prohibition, "the Constitution would create only a repetition of the very evils it was designed to cure."

## Legacy and Significance

The Federalist Papers' critique of confederation established a durable framework for understanding the distinction between confederal and federal systems of government. The experience under the Articles of Confederation became a permanent cautionary reference point in American constitutional discourse — a demonstration that governments operating only on states as collective bodies, without the authority to reach individuals directly, are incapable of sustaining effective governance. This insight, developed most fully in Federalist Nos. 15–22, remains one of the foundational contributions of the Federalist Papers to constitutional theory.
