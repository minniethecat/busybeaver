# Naturalization

**Naturalization** is the legal process by which a foreign-born person becomes a citizen of the United States. Article I, Section 8, Clause 4 of the Constitution grants Congress the power "to establish an uniform Rule of Naturalization throughout the United States," replacing the patchwork of inconsistent state naturalization laws that had prevailed under the Articles of Confederation.

## In the Federalist Papers

Madison, in Federalist No. 32, identified the naturalization power as one of three cases in which the Constitution granted exclusive authority to the federal government. He argued that the very language of the clause — requiring a "uniform rule" — necessarily implied exclusivity:

> The third will be found in that clause which declares that Congress shall have power "to establish an UNIFORM RULE of naturalization throughout the United States." This must necessarily be exclusive; because if each State had power to prescribe a DISTINCT RULE, there could not be a UNIFORM RULE. [032-004]

In Federalist No. 42, Madison returned to the naturalization power as part of his review of the "miscellaneous" powers granted to Congress under Article I, Section 8. He placed it among a cluster of powers — including the regulation of commerce, the coining of money, the punishment of counterfeiting, uniform bankruptcy laws, and the establishment of post offices and post roads — that together constituted the infrastructure of national governance [042-012].

## The Defect Under the Articles of Confederation

Madison devoted substantial attention in Federalist No. 42 to the "dissimilarity in the rules of naturalization" that had prevailed under the Confederation. He described the pre-Constitution system as a source of "intricate and delicate questions" [042-019] that threatened both legal coherence and interstate harmony.

The fourth article of the Confederation had guaranteed that the "free inhabitants" of each state would be entitled to "all privileges and immunities of free citizens in the several States," but Madison noted a "confusion of language" in the terms "free inhabitants," "free citizens," and "people" — a terminological imprecision that made it unclear whether a person naturalized by one state could claim citizenship rights in another [042-019].

Madison identified a specific danger in the Confederation system: a state with lenient naturalization requirements could effectively "naturalize aliens in every other State," allowing a person "legally incapacitated for certain rights" in one state to "elude his incapacity" through prior residence in a more permissive jurisdiction [042-019]. This arrangement threatened to render "the law of one State preposterously ... paramount to the law of another, within the jurisdiction of the other" [042-019].

## The Uniformity Principle

The Constitution's solution — a single, uniform rule of naturalization administered by Congress — addressed the Confederation's defect by removing the power from the states entirely. The uniformity principle served two purposes: it prevented states from competing with one another to confer citizenship on persons who might be undesirable to their neighbors, and it ensured that citizenship carried the same meaning and legal consequences regardless of where in the United States it was acquired.

The grant was to establish a "rule of naturalization" — not merely to regulate naturalization or to set minimum standards. The language implied that Congress would create the substantive criteria for citizenship, a power that would prove to be among the most consequential in American history, shaping the demographic composition of the republic through legislation such as the Naturalization Act of 1790, which limited naturalization to "free white persons" of good character.

## The Exclusive Character of the Power

Madison's classification of the naturalization power as exclusive, in Federalist No. 32, rested on the textual logic that uniformity and concurrent state authority are logically incompatible. If each state could set different rules, there could be no uniformity. The argument illustrates Madison's broader federalism framework: powers could be exclusive (where the Constitution expressly granted exclusive authority, or prohibited the states from acting, or where concurrent state authority would be contradictory), or concurrent (where both levels of government could act without contradiction).

## Legacy and Significance

The naturalization power has been central to American debates about national identity and immigration from the earliest Congresses to the present day. The first naturalization statute (1790), the notorious Alien and Sedition Acts (1798), the Dred Scott decision (1857), and the Fourteenth Amendment (1868) — which established birthright citizenship and overrode *Dred Scott* — all trace their constitutional roots to this clause.

The Supreme Court has consistently affirmed the exclusivity of the federal naturalization power. In *Chirac v. Chirac* (1817), Chief Justice Marshall confirmed that "the power of naturalization is exclusively in Congress," establishing a principle that has never been seriously contested.
