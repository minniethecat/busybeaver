# Federal Farmer

The **Federal Farmer** was the pseudonym of an Anti-Federalist writer who authored a series of pamphlets during the ratification debates of 1787–1788. The Federal Farmer's letters articulated concerns about the proposed Constitution, particularly regarding the scope of federal power, the adequacy of representation, and the potential erosion of state sovereignty. Hamilton acknowledged the Federal Farmer's influence in Federalist No. 68, describing him as "the most plausible" of the Constitution's opponents who had appeared in print. [068-003]

## Identity

The identity of the Federal Farmer remains uncertain among historians. The leading candidates include Richard Henry Lee of Virginia and Melancton Smith of New York, though scholarly consensus has not been reached. The letters are notable for their moderate tone compared to other Anti-Federalist writings — the Federal Farmer acknowledged the need for a stronger national government while arguing that the proposed Constitution went too far.

## In the Federalist Papers

The Federal Farmer is explicitly cited in Federalist No. 68, where Hamilton discusses the mode of electing the President. Hamilton noted that even this prominent opponent had conceded the soundness of the Electoral College mechanism:

> The mode of appointment of the Chief Magistrate of the United States is almost the only part of the system, of any consequence, which has escaped without severe censure, or which has received the slightest mark of approbation from its opponents. The most plausible of these, who has appeared in print, has even deigned to admit that the election of the President is pretty well guarded. [068-003]

The footnote to this passage explicitly identifies the Federal Farmer: "Vide federal farmer." [068-015] Hamilton's citation of an opponent as agreeing with the electoral provisions was a strategic argumentative move: if even the Constitution's critics acknowledged the wisdom of certain provisions, those provisions must be sound.

Hamilton elaborated on why the Electoral College deserved this approbation, arguing that it combined popular input with deliberative judgment:

> It was desirable that the sense of the people should operate in the choice of the person to whom so important a trust was to be confided. This end will be answered by committing the right of making it, not to any preestablished body, but to men chosen by the people for the special purpose, and at the particular conjuncture. [068-004]

## The Federal Farmer's Letters

The Federal Farmer's most significant contribution to the ratification debate was a series of letters published in late 1787 and early 1788. Key concerns raised in these letters included:

- **Representation**: The Federal Farmer argued that the House of Representatives was too small to adequately represent the diversity of American society, and that representatives would inevitably be drawn from the elite "natural aristocracy" rather than the body of the people. Hamilton addressed this class of objection in Federalist No. 35, arguing that the idea of "an actual representation of all classes of the people, by persons of each class, is altogether visionary" [035-008] — that in practice, mechanics and manufacturers would choose merchants as their natural representatives, and the learned professions would serve as impartial arbiters between competing economic interests.

- **Consolidation**: The letters warned that the proposed Constitution would transform the federal union into a consolidated national government, extinguishing the states as meaningful political entities.

- **The Senate**: The Federal Farmer was particularly critical of the Senate, viewing its combination of legislative, executive, and judicial functions as a departure from the principle of separated powers.

## Legacy

The Federal Farmer's letters are among the most widely studied Anti-Federalist writings, valued for their detailed, substantive engagement with the constitutional text rather than rhetorical excess. They represent an important voice in the ratification debates and continue to inform scholarly understanding of the Anti-Federalist constitutional vision.
