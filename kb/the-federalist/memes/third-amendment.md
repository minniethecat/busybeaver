# Third Amendment

The **Third Amendment** to the [[constitution|United States Constitution]] prohibits the quartering of soldiers in private homes without the consent of the owner during peacetime, and requires legal authorization during wartime. Ratified in 1791 as part of the [[bill-of-rights|Bill of Rights]], it was a direct response to British military practices during the colonial era, particularly the Quartering Acts of 1765 and 1774, which required colonists to house and provision British soldiers.

## In The Federalist Papers

The Federalist Papers do not address the Third Amendment directly — the [[bill-of-rights|Bill of Rights]] was drafted after the Constitution's ratification, largely in response to Anti-Federalist objections that [[publius|Publius]] had argued against. However, the underlying concern about military power over civilians appears throughout the Federalist essays. [[alexander-hamilton|Hamilton]] addresses the danger of [[standing-armies|standing armies]] in Federalist Nos. 24–29, arguing that the Constitution's two-year appropriation limit for military funding and the [[militia]] system would prevent the army from becoming a tool of oppression.[^1]

In Federalist No. 8, Hamilton warns that if the states were disunited, each would need to maintain large standing armies, leading to "the military, elevated above the civil, establishing itself in the habit of trampling upon the rights of the people." The Third Amendment addresses one specific form of such trampling — the forced intrusion of soldiers into private homes.

## Historical Background

The Third Amendment arose from colonial experience under British rule. The Quartering Act of 1765 required colonial assemblies to provide barracks, supplies, and housing for British troops stationed in America. The more intrusive Quartering Act of 1774 permitted the quartering of soldiers in private homes when barracks were insufficient. These acts were among the "Intolerable Acts" that pushed the colonies toward revolution, and the grievance was memorialized in the [[declaration-of-independence|Declaration of Independence]], which accused the King of "quartering large bodies of armed troops among us."

At the [[constitutional-convention|Constitutional Convention]], the issue of quartering was not directly addressed. Anti-Federalist critics of the Constitution, including [[patrick-henry|Patrick Henry]] and [[george-mason|George Mason]], argued that without explicit protections, the new federal government could repeat British abuses. The Third Amendment, drafted by [[james-madison|James Madison]] and introduced in the First Congress in 1789, was the direct constitutional answer to this concern.

## Key Principles

1. **Sanctity of the home**: The Third Amendment establishes the principle that the home is a private space beyond the reach of military authority in peacetime.

2. **Civilian control of the military**: By requiring legal authorization even in wartime, the amendment reinforces the broader constitutional commitment to civilian supremacy over military power.

3. **Limited but not obsolete**: Though rarely litigated, the Third Amendment has been cited in modern privacy cases, including *Griswold v. Connecticut* (1965), where the Supreme Court referenced it as creating a "zone of privacy" around the home.

## Legacy

The Third Amendment is the least litigated provision of the Bill of Rights — the Supreme Court has never decided a case primarily on Third Amendment grounds. Its principles have been invoked in cases involving police occupation of homes and government surveillance, most notably in *Engblom v. Carey* (1982), where the Second Circuit held that National Guardsmen qualify as "soldiers" for Third Amendment purposes. The amendment stands as a constitutional monument to the founding generation's determination that military power should never penetrate the sanctuary of the private home.
