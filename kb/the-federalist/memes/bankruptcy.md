# Bankruptcy

**Bankruptcy** is a legal process by which persons or entities unable to repay their debts may seek relief from some or all of those debts. Article I, Section 8, Clause 4 of the Constitution grants Congress the power "to establish ... uniform Laws on the subject of Bankruptcies throughout the United States," one of the enumerated legislative powers designed to create a coherent national commercial framework.

## In the Federalist Papers

Madison, in Federalist No. 42, addressed the bankruptcy power as part of his survey of the miscellaneous powers granted to Congress under Article I, Section 8. The power to establish uniform bankruptcy laws was listed alongside the regulation of commerce, the coining of money, the punishment of counterfeiting, the uniform rule of naturalization, and the establishment of post offices — a cluster of powers that together provided the legal infrastructure for a national economy [042-012].

Madison offered a concise but forceful justification for the federal bankruptcy power:

> The power of establishing uniform laws of bankruptcy is so intimately connected with the regulation of commerce, and will prevent so many frauds where the parties or their property may lie or be removed into different States, that the expediency of it seems not likely to be drawn into question. [042-020]

## The Commerce Connection

Madison's linkage of bankruptcy to commerce was analytically significant. In an era when debtors and creditors often resided in different states, the absence of uniform bankruptcy rules created opportunities for fraud and forum-shopping. A debtor could abscond to a state with more favorable insolvency laws, or transfer property across state lines to place it beyond the reach of creditors in another jurisdiction. The states, acting independently, could not solve this problem — each state's laws stopped at its borders.

The bankruptcy power was thus understood not as an independent grant of authority to regulate debtor-creditor relations for their own sake, but as an instrument of commercial regulation. A national market required predictable, uniform rules governing the consequences of financial failure. Without such rules, the risk of lending across state lines would be unquantifiable, and interstate credit markets would remain fragmented.

## The Uniformity Requirement

Like the naturalization power, the bankruptcy power carries an explicit uniformity requirement — Congress must establish "uniform Laws on the subject of Bankruptcies." This requirement means that federal bankruptcy law must apply equally throughout the United States, preempting conflicting state insolvency statutes. The uniformity principle prevents the kind of competitive federalism — states racing to offer the most lenient debtor protections, or the most creditor-friendly discharge rules — that would undermine the national credit market.

The Framers' decision to grant the bankruptcy power to the federal government rather than leaving it to the states reflected their broader understanding that economic regulation, like foreign affairs and national defense, required a single national voice. The experience under the Articles of Confederation, where states had issued competing paper currencies and enacted divergent debtor-relief laws, had demonstrated the costs of fragmented economic governance.

## The State Insolvency Power

The Constitution does not expressly prohibit the states from enacting their own insolvency laws, and the states have historically exercised concurrent authority in this area — subject to the Supremacy Clause and the limitation that state laws cannot impair the obligation of contracts (Article I, Section 10). The relationship between state insolvency laws and federal bankruptcy law has been a recurring theme in American constitutional law, with the Supreme Court holding that state insolvency laws are suspended when they conflict with federal bankruptcy legislation.

## Legacy and Significance

Congress did not exercise the bankruptcy power continuously. The first federal bankruptcy act was passed in 1800 but repealed in 1803; subsequent acts in 1841 and 1867 were also short-lived. It was not until the Bankruptcy Act of 1898 that Congress established a permanent federal bankruptcy system, which has been revised periodically — most significantly by the Bankruptcy Reform Act of 1978, which created the modern Bankruptcy Code.

The Supreme Court's bankruptcy jurisprudence has been shaped by the uniformity requirement. In *Hanover National Bank v. Moyses* (1902), the Court held that the uniformity requirement does not require identical geographical application, allowing bankruptcy law to incorporate state-law variations in exemptions and property definitions — a practical accommodation that preserves the principle of uniformity while respecting federalism.
