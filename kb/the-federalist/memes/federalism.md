# Federalism

**[[Federalism]]** is the constitutional system dividing political authority between a national government and state governments, each sovereign in its sphere. The [[the-federalist-papers|Federalist Papers]] are, above all, a sustained argument for this novel form of compound government — one that [[alexander-hamilton|Hamilton]] promoted as essential to national survival and [[james-madison|Madison]] theorized as a "double security" for liberty within the framework of [[separation-of-powers]].

## Definition

**Federalism** is a system of compound government in which political authority is constitutionally divided between a general (national) government and regional (state) governments, each possessing sovereign powers within its designated sphere. Under the Articles of Confederation, the United States operated as a loose confederacy; the Constitution proposed a novel form of federalism that combined national supremacy in enumerated areas with retained state sovereignty in all others.

## In The Federalist Papers

The Federalist Papers are, above all, a sustained argument for a particular kind of federal union. Publius opens by declaring that Americans are "called upon to deliberate on a new Constitution" after "an unequivocal experience of the inefficacy of the subsisting federal government" (001-003). The entire first section (Federalist Nos. 1–14) is devoted to "The Utility of the Union" — arguing that the states must remain "one nation, under one federal government" rather than splitting into "separate confederacies" (002-004).

Hamilton articulates the stakes clearly: the choice is between "one government embracing the whole" or "an association of two or more states." The former preserves peace and prosperity; the latter invites "the military establishments which must gradually poison the Springs of Morality" (008-012). He frames the proposed Union as "a barrier against domestic faction and insurrection" (009-003).

Madison develops the theoretical case in Federalist Nos. 14–20, contrasting the American federal system with the failed confederacies of ancient Greece. The Amphyctionic Council, he notes, had "the power of proposing resolutions rather than of enforcing them" — precisely the defect of the Articles of Confederation (018-019). What distinguishes the proposed Constitution is that "it must extend the laws of the federal government to the individual citizens of America" rather than operating merely upon states as collective bodies (023-010).

> It has been frequently remarked that it seems to have been reserved to the people of this country, by their conduct and example, to decide the important question, whether societies of men are really capable or not of establishing good government from reflection and choice, or whether they are forever destined to depend for their political constitutions on accident and force.
>
> (001-003)

Hamilton further argues that a federal government entrusted with "the care of the general defense" must possess the powers necessary to fulfill that trust — the means ought to be proportioned to the end (023-008). He denies that the states will be swallowed up: the federal government will operate directly on citizens in enumerated matters, but "there is no color for the assertion that they would be the measures of a general consolidation" (009-018).

## Historical Context

The Founders' understanding of federalism was shaped by two recent experiences: the failure of the British imperial system (which attempted to govern the colonies without representation) and the collapse of the Articles of Confederation (which gave too little power to the central government to be effective). The Articles created a "league of friendship" among sovereign states but provided no power to tax, regulate commerce, or enforce laws directly upon individuals. Shays' Rebellion (1786–1787) dramatized the Confederation's inability to maintain order or protect property.

The Constitution's federalism was a middle path between two extremes: a unitary national government that would abolish the states (favored by some nationalists) and a mere confederation of independent sovereigns (favored by states'-rights advocates). Montesquieu's *The Spirit of Laws* had praised confederate republics as combining "the internal advantages of a republican government with the external force of a monarchical government," and the Framers drew on this tradition while innovating beyond it.

## Key Arguments

Publius advances four principal arguments for the proposed federal system:

| # | Argument | Key PN | Core Quote |
|---|---------|--------|------------|
| 1 | The existing confederation is failing | 015-017 | "a shadow of a federal government" |
| 2 | Federal power must operate directly on individuals | 023-010 | "extend the laws... to the individual citizens" |
| 3 | States retain all non-delegated powers | 017-004 | "few and defined" — federal powers are limited |
| 4 | Competition between governments protects liberty | 051-009 | "a double security arises to the rights of the people" |

1. **The existing confederation is failing**: The Articles of Confederation are "a shadow of a federal government" — unable to regulate commerce, raise revenue, or enforce treaties (015-017). The Union must be strengthened or it will dissolve into warring confederacies.

2. **Federal power must operate directly on individuals**: The fatal defect of the Articles is that they operate on states as collective bodies rather than on citizens directly. The new Constitution "must extend the laws of the federal government to the individual citizens of America" (023-010), enabling enforcement without relying on state intermediaries.

3. **The states retain all non-delegated powers**: Federal powers are enumerated and limited — "few and defined." The states retain the general mass of governmental authority over daily life, including "the ordinary administration of criminal and civil justice" (017-004). This dual sovereignty is the essence of American federalism.

4. **Competition between governments protects liberty**: By dividing power between two levels of government, each serves as a check on the other. Citizens who find their liberty threatened by one government can appeal to the other, creating "a double security" for the rights of the people.

## Constitutional Implementation

The Constitution establishes federalism through several structural features:

- **Enumerated powers (Article I, Section 8)**: Congress may legislate only on the specific subjects listed — defense, commerce, taxation, naturalization, patents, and a few others.
- **The Supremacy Clause (Article VI)**: Federal law prevails over conflicting state law within the federal government's enumerated sphere.
- **The Tenth Amendment**: Powers not delegated to the United States nor prohibited to the states are reserved to the states or the people.
- **State representation in the Senate**: Each state, regardless of population, has two Senators — ensuring that the states as political entities have a voice in the federal government.
- **The Guarantee Clause (Article IV, Section 4)**: The United States guarantees each state a republican form of government and protection against invasion and domestic violence.

## Anti-Federalist Counterarguments

The Anti-Federalists, particularly Brutus and the Federal Farmer, argued that the proposed Constitution created not a federal republic but a **consolidated national government** that would eventually absorb the states. They contended that:
- The "necessary and proper" clause and the Supremacy Clause were open-ended invitations to federal expansion.
- A republic could only function in a small, homogeneous territory — the extended republic Madison described would inevitably become an empire ruled by a distant aristocracy.
- The federal judiciary would use its appellate jurisdiction to pull all cases into federal courts, eroding state judicial authority.
- Standing armies and direct federal taxation would give the central government coercive power that no state could resist.

## Legacy and Significance

American federalism has evolved dramatically since 1787. The Civil War (1861–1865) and the Fourteenth Amendment fundamentally altered the federal-state balance by applying the Bill of Rights to the states and authorizing federal enforcement of civil rights. The New Deal (1930s) expanded federal power through a broad reading of the Commerce Clause. The Civil Rights Movement (1950s–1960s) used federal authority to override state-sponsored segregation.

Yet the core structure described in The Federalist endures: a national government of enumerated powers, states with residual sovereignty, and a constitutional framework that channels competition between them. Federalist Nos. 39 and 46 remain touchstones in federalism jurisprudence, cited by the Supreme Court in cases defining the limits of federal power over the states.
