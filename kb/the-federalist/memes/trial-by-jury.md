# Trial by Jury

**Trial by jury** is the right of parties in legal proceedings — both criminal and civil — to have their cases heard and decided by an impartial body of fellow citizens rather than by a judge alone. In the Federalist Papers, Hamilton devoted extensive attention to this right, particularly in response to Anti-Federalist criticisms that the proposed Constitution inadequately protected jury trials in civil cases. His treatment of the subject in Federalist No. 83 is among the longest and most detailed essays in the entire series.

## Constitutional Silence on Civil Juries

One of the most potent Anti-Federalist objections to the Constitution was its failure to guarantee trial by jury in civil cases. The Constitution explicitly protected jury trial in criminal cases but was silent on civil proceedings — leading opponents to charge that civil jury trials would be abolished by implication.

Hamilton met this objection directly in Federalist No. 83, arguing that the silence of the Constitution on civil juries did not constitute their abolition:

> The Constitution has established the trial by jury in criminal cases, and is silent in respect to civil. From this silence, it is inferred that an injunction of the trial by jury in certain cases is an interdiction of it in all others. [083-006]

He maintained that the variety of practice among the states made a uniform rule impractical, and that the matter was properly left to legislative discretion:

> The truth is, that the general genius of a government is frequently better judged by its apparent than by its actual provisions. [083-008]

Hamilton's argument rested on a careful analysis of the diversity of state practices. The states differed substantially in the scope of civil jury trial — in which types of cases it applied, under what procedures, and with what exceptions. To impose a uniform federal rule, Hamilton contended, would either disrupt the established practices of many states or produce a rule so vague as to be unenforceable.

## The Safeguard against Oppression

Hamilton acknowledged the importance of jury trial as a protection of liberty, describing it in terms that aligned with the broader Federalist concern for checks on governmental power:

> Trial by jury is a safeguard against an oppressive use of the power of taxation, by which the officer of the government may enter into a man's house without the consent of the owner. [083-015]

This characterization placed jury trial alongside the separation of powers and federalism as structural mechanisms for protecting individual freedom. The jury was not merely a procedural device but a constitutional check — one of the "auxiliary precautions" that Madison would later describe in Federalist No. 51.

## Criminal Jury Trials

The Constitution's explicit guarantee of jury trial in criminal cases was uncontroversial. Article III, Section 2 provides that "the Trial of all Crimes, except in Cases of Impeachment, shall be by Jury." Hamilton defended this provision while noting that even under the Constitution, the practical scope of jury trial depended on legislative implementation:

> Under our own Constitution, the trial by jury is in most cases out of use. The truth is that in the courts of the United States it is not; but the idea has been carefully propagated. [083-017]

The criminal jury guarantee was among the few provisions of the original Constitution that even the most ardent Anti-Federalists conceded was adequate — their objection was not to what the Constitution said but to what it omitted.

## Relation to the Judiciary

The right to trial by jury is closely connected to the broader structure of the federal judiciary. In Federalist No. 81, Hamilton addressed the concern that the Supreme Court's appellate jurisdiction might supersede jury verdicts:

> The argument suggests an implied supersedure of the trial by jury, in favor of the civil-law mode of trial, which prevails in the courts of admiralty. [081-021]

He responded that appellate review of law, as distinct from fact, did not threaten the jury's proper function — a distinction that would become fundamental to American legal practice. Courts of appeal review questions of law; juries determine questions of fact. So long as this division of labor was respected, appellate jurisdiction posed no threat to the jury's constitutional role.

## The Seventh Amendment

The controversy over civil jury trials was ultimately resolved by the Seventh Amendment, ratified in 1791 as part of the Bill of Rights. The Amendment preserved the right to jury trial in suits at common law where the value in controversy exceeded twenty dollars — adopting precisely the approach Hamilton had urged: a flexible standard that accommodated diverse state practices while securing the essential right.
