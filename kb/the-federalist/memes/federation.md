# Federation

A **federation** is a political system in which sovereignty is constitutionally divided between a central governing authority and constituent political units (such as states or provinces). In the Federalist Papers, the concept of federation represents the proposed model of government under the new Constitution — a strengthened national union that preserves state autonomy while enabling effective collective governance. This model occupied the central argumentative space of the entire work, distinguishing the proposed Constitution from both the failed Confederation and a unitary national consolidation.

## In the Federalist Papers

The Federalist Papers draw a sustained contrast between the existing **Confederation** under the Articles of Confederation and the proposed **federation** under the Constitution. While the Confederation operated as a loose league of sovereign states with minimal central authority, the federation would establish a national government capable of acting directly upon citizens while preserving the states as essential components of the political order.

Publius addressed the distinction between "federal" and "national" government in several essays. Hamilton, in particular, argued that the defects of the Confederation demonstrated the necessity of a more robust federated structure:

> The articles of confederation obliged the parties to submit to the decision of Congress in all cases, but Congress could not enforce its own resolutions. [007-005]

The transition from confederation to federation was presented not as the abolition of federal principles but as their proper fulfillment. Hamilton enumerated the principal defects of the existing system:

> The principal defects of the Confederation necessary, in order to show that the evils we experience do not proceed from minute or partial imperfections, but from fundamental errors in the structure of the building. [015-007]

Madison later contributed to this argument by distinguishing the compound character of the proposed republic from both pure confederacies and simple national governments, a theoretical contribution unique to the American founding.

## Federal vs. National

A key theoretical contribution of the Federalist Papers is the articulation of a compound republic — neither purely federal (where states retain full sovereignty) nor purely national (where states are mere administrative subdivisions), but a blend of both. This "partly federal, partly national" character became the distinctive American contribution to political theory.

The Articles of Confederation represented the extreme federal end of the spectrum, in which the central authority could only recommend, not command:

> The fundamental error in the Confederation. Its repugnancy to an adequate government lies in the principle of legislation for states or governments, in their corporate or collective capacities, as contra-distinguished from the individuals of which they consist. [021-008]

Madison elaborated this distinction in Federalist No. 39, where he analyzed the proposed government along five dimensions: its foundation, the sources of its powers, the operation of those powers, the extent of them, and the mode of amendment. On each dimension, he found the Constitution to be neither wholly federal nor wholly national but a composition of both.

## Defects of the Confederation

Much of the argument for federation rests on Hamilton's catalog of Confederation defects, including:

- **Want of sanctions**: The Confederation could not enforce its laws or punish violations [021-004]
- **Lack of direct authority**: Congress legislated for states, not citizens [015-008]
- **No power to regulate commerce**: States erected trade barriers against one another [022-007]
- **Unequal representation**: The rule of equal state suffrage violated republican principles [022-009]

> Every idea of proportion and the rule of fair representation, which are the fundamental maxims of republican government, are condemned by the principle of equal suffrage among states in the Confederation. [022-009]

Hamilton organized these defects systematically, beginning with the want of sanctions and proceeding through the absence of a federal judiciary, the lack of coercive power over states, and the absence of direct authority over individuals. Each defect illustrated a structural weakness that the proposed federation was designed to remedy.

## The Proposed Federation

The Constitution proposed a federation that corrected these defects while respecting the federal character of the union. The new government would operate directly on individuals, possess independent executive and judicial branches, and have enumerated powers sufficient for national purposes — all while leaving the states as vigorous participants in the federal system.

The proposed structure included several innovations: a bicameral legislature with proportional representation in the House and equal state representation in the Senate; an independent executive with energy and responsibility; a federal judiciary with jurisdiction over cases arising under the Constitution and laws of the United States; and a supremacy clause ensuring the priority of federal law within its sphere.

Hamilton concluded his review of the Confederation by emphasizing the urgency of reform:

> In this review of the Confederation, I have confined myself to the exhibition of its most material defects. [022-019]

The cumulative force of these defects constituted what Publius called an "experiment" that had decisively failed. The choice facing the American people was not between confederation and no union, but between an effective federation and eventual disunion.
