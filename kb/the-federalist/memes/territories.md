# Territories

**Federal territories** refer to lands within the jurisdiction of the United States that are not part of any state. The Constitution grants Congress the power "to dispose of and make all needful rules and regulations respecting the territory or other property belonging to the United States." In the Federalist Papers, the discussion of territories appeared in the broader context of federal authority over common national assets and the dangers posed by foreign powers with territorial ambitions on the North American continent.

## In the Federalist Papers

The territorial dimension of American governance appears in the Federalist Papers primarily through discussions of national security and economic competition. The territories bordering the United States — held by European powers such as Britain, Spain, and France — were a persistent source of anxiety for the new republic. Publius argued that a strong federal union was essential to defend American interests against these territorial rivals.

Hamilton observed that European powers with adjacent territories were unlikely to welcome the growth of American commerce and power:

> The extension of our own commerce in our own vessels cannot give pleasure to any nations who possess territories on or near this continent, because the cheapness and excellence of our productions, added to the circumstance of vicinity, and the enterprise and address of our merchants and navigators, will give us a greater share in the advantages which those territories afford, than consists with the wishes or policy of their respective sovereigns. [011-005]

## National Security and Territorial Defense

The presence of foreign-held territories along American borders constituted a standing security concern. Madison and Hamilton both emphasized that a disunited confederacy of separate states would be incapable of defending against territorial encroachments. European powers, motivated by the universal ambition to control trade and expand dominion, would exploit American divisions:

> The petty republics of Greece and Italy evoke horror at the disorders that attended their perpetual convulsions, and at the rapid succession of revolutions by which they were kept perpetually vibrating between the extremes of tyranny and anarchy. [009-010]

The argument extended to the practical question of how a consolidated federal government — rather than thirteen disparate states — could mount a coordinated defense of frontier territories and negotiate effectively with European empires that held neighboring lands.

## Territorial Expansion and the Federal System

The Constitution's provisions for admitting new states into the Union implicitly addressed territorial governance. The power to admit new states meant that territories could eventually join the Union on equal footing with the original thirteen states, transforming the federal relationship over time. Madison noted the relative equality of the existing states as a favorable condition for union:

> Fortunately for the United States, the component states are of nearly equal size and population. [014-008]

This observation set the stage for the later Northwest Ordinance framework, in which territories would pass through stages of governance before achieving full statehood. The Federalist Papers, while not prescribing a detailed territorial policy, established the principle that a strong federal government — not a weak confederation — was the proper vehicle for managing national territory.

## Federal vs. State Claims

Territorial questions also intersected with disputes among the states over western land claims. The Confederation period had been marked by conflicts between states with extensive western claims — such as Virginia — and those without. The Constitution's grant of territorial authority to Congress was understood as removing these disputes from the arena of interstate rivalry and placing them under a neutral federal authority.

The broader principle was that territories belonged to the Union as a whole, not to individual states, and that their disposition should be decided by the national legislature for the common benefit.

## Economic Dimensions

Access to territories meant access to resources, trade routes, and settlement lands. Hamilton's observation about European territorial competition underscored that economic advantage — not merely military security — was at stake. The federal government's control over territories ensured that commercial access and settlement policy would be uniform and oriented toward national interests rather than parochial state objectives.
