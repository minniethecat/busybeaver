# Appellate Jurisdiction

**Appellate jurisdiction** is the legal authority of a superior court to review, revise, or overturn decisions made by lower courts. In the United States Constitution, the Supreme Court is vested with appellate jurisdiction in most federal cases, while its original jurisdiction — the power to hear a case in the first instance — is limited to cases affecting ambassadors, public ministers, consuls, and those in which a state is a party. The scope and limits of this appellate power formed a major subject of Hamilton's defense of the judiciary in the Federalist Papers.

## In the Federalist Papers

Hamilton devoted substantial attention to the appellate jurisdiction of the Supreme Court, particularly in Federalist Nos. 81 and 82. The proposed appellate power had provoked intense criticism from Anti-Federalists, who feared it would lead to the abolition of trial by jury and the consolidation of judicial authority in a remote national tribunal.

The Constitution provides that the Supreme Court "shall have appellate jurisdiction, both as to law and fact, with such exceptions, and under such regulations as the Congress shall make." Hamilton argued that this language preserved legislative flexibility and did not threaten the jury system.

> The propriety of this appellate jurisdiction has been scarcely called in question in regard to matters of law; but the clamors have been loud against it as applied to matters of fact. [081-020]

## Scope and Limits

### Original vs. Appellate Jurisdiction

The Constitution's distribution of judicial power assigns a very small portion of original jurisdiction to the Supreme Court and consigns the rest to subordinate tribunals. Hamilton emphasized this careful partitioning:

> In the partition of this authority a very small portion of original jurisdiction has been preserved to the Supreme Court, and the rest consigned to the subordinate tribunals. [081-025]

The classes of original jurisdiction are narrowly defined — affecting ambassadors, public ministers, consuls, and cases where a state is a party — and are "of a nature rarely to occur."

### The Exceptions Clause and Congressional Control

A critical feature of the appellate jurisdiction — one that has generated extensive constitutional debate — is the Exceptions Clause: the power of Congress to make "exceptions" to and "regulations" for the Supreme Court's appellate jurisdiction. Hamilton understood this clause as a structural safeguard against judicial overreach, vesting the people's representatives with the authority to define the boundaries of appellate review:

> From this review of the particular powers of the federal judiciary, as marked out in the Constitution, it appears that they are all conformable to the principles which ought to have governed the structure of that department, and which were necessary to the perfection of the system. [082-012]

Hamilton's confidence in legislative regulation reflected the broader Federalist view that the judiciary, while independent, was not unaccountable. Congress could structure the appellate power, create exceptions to it, and regulate its exercise — all without impairing the essential function of the judicial department.

Modern constitutional scholarship has debated the limits of the Exceptions Clause. Can Congress strip the Supreme Court of appellate jurisdiction over an entire class of constitutional claims? Hamilton's text suggests a broad view of legislative authority over the appellate docket, but he also insisted that the essential character of the judicial power — its function of resolving cases and controversies under federal law — must be preserved. An exceptions power exercised to nullify constitutional rights rather than to organize their adjudication would contravene the purposes Hamilton identified.

### Appeals as to Law and Fact

The phrase "both as to law and fact" provoked the most controversy. Critics contended it would abolish the common-law trial by jury by allowing appellate courts to re-examine facts already determined by juries. Hamilton offered several rebuttals:

First, the term "appellate" does not carry a uniform technical meaning across the states. In New England, appeal from one jury to another was familiar practice, while in New York, the term was associated with civil-law appeals in admiralty and chancery courts:

> The word "appellate," therefore, will not be understood in the same sense in New England as in New York, which shows the impropriety of a technical interpretation derived from the jurisprudence of any particular State. [081-022]

Second, even under the common-law courts of New York, a superior court exercises jurisdiction of both fact and law when it reviews the record and pronounces the law arising upon it, without instituting a new inquiry into the facts.

Third, Congress retains full authority to regulate the mode of appellate review, including the power to provide that there shall be no re-examination of facts originally tried by juries.

## Appeals from State Courts

In Federalist No. 82, Hamilton addressed whether the appellate jurisdiction of the Supreme Court extended to state courts — a question of great practical importance under the concurrent jurisdiction doctrine. He concluded that appeals from state courts to the Supreme Court were essential to the uniformity of federal law:

> An appeal would certainly lie from the State courts to the Supreme Court of the United States. The Constitution in direct terms gives an appellate jurisdiction to the Supreme Court in all the enumerated cases of federal cognizance in which it is not to have an original one, without a single expression to confine its operation to the inferior federal courts. [082-005]

This interpretation was critical to ensuring that the judiciary authority of the Union could not be "eluded at the pleasure of every plaintiff or prosecutor."

The landmark case of Martin v. Hunter's Lessee (1816) vindicated Hamilton's reading of the appellate power. Justice Story, writing for the Supreme Court, held that the Constitution obligated the Court to exercise appellate jurisdiction over state court decisions involving federal questions. Without such appellate supervision, Story reasoned, the uniformity of federal law would be impossible — state courts in different jurisdictions would interpret the Constitution and federal statutes in conflicting ways, producing the very "multiplicity of codes" that the Constitution was designed to prevent. Hamilton's argument in No. 82, written three decades before Martin, anticipated the core logic of the Supreme Court's appellate supremacy.

## Trial by Jury and Appellate Review

A central theme of Hamilton's defense was that appellate jurisdiction does not abolish trial by jury. The power to review does not imply the power to displace the original mode of trial. Hamilton emphasized that:

> The legislature of the United States would certainly have full power to provide, that in appeals to the Supreme Court there should be no re-examination of facts where they had been tried in the original causes by juries. [081-024]

The appellate power could be exercised by remanding the cause to the court below for a second trial of the fact, or by directing an issue immediately out of the Supreme Court — both preserving the role of the jury.
