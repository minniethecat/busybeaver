# Appropriation Power

The **Appropriation Power** is the constitutional principle, embedded in Article I, Section 9, that "No Money shall be drawn from the Treasury, but in Consequence of Appropriations made by Law." This power vests exclusive control over federal spending in Congress, making the legislature the indispensable gatekeeper of public funds. In the Federalist Papers, the appropriation power was treated as a critical structural safeguard — particularly against standing armies.

## The Two-Year Limitation on Military Appropriations

The Constitution's most innovative application of the appropriation power was the restriction on military funding. In Federalist No. 24, Hamilton highlighted the clause "which forbids the appropriation of money for the support of an army for any longer period than two years," calling it "a great and real security against the keeping up of troops without evident necessity" [024-006].

This biennial limit forced Congress to revisit the question of military establishments at regular intervals. Without it, a legislature might authorize permanent funding for an army, losing the power to check the executive through financial control. Hamilton argued in No. 26 that this mechanism was superior to the absolute prohibitions on standing armies found in some state constitutions:

> Let us examine whether there be any comparison, in point of efficacy, between the provision alluded to and that which is contained in the new Constitution, for restraining the appropriations of money for military purposes to the period of two years. The former, by aiming at too much, is calculated to effect nothing; the latter, by steering clear of an imprudent extreme... will have a salutary and powerful operation. [026-011]

## Congress as Guardian of the Public Purse

Hamilton returned to the appropriation power in Federalist No. 41, where he compared the American system favorably with the British model. While Britain had no fixed constitutional limit on military appropriations, Parliament's practice was to limit them to one year. The American Constitution, Hamilton argued, went further: "the American ties down the legislature to two years, as the longest admissible term" [041-018]. Given that the House of Representatives was directly elected every two years, this ensured the appropriating body remained accountable to the people.

## The Appropriations Clause and Checks and Balances

The appropriations clause is a core element of the separation of powers. By denying the executive independent access to funds, it ensures that all executive action requiring expenditure must pass through Congress. This was particularly important in the context of military affairs, where the danger of an executive-controlled standing army was a leading Anti-Federalist concern. Hamilton pointed out that even with a large army, the executive would still depend on congressional appropriations: the executive could not "dispense with supplies from the acts of the legislature" without a pretext to justify the force [026-015].

## Contrast with the Articles of Confederation

Under the Articles of Confederation, Congress could requisition funds from the states but had no independent power of appropriation or enforcement. States could and did ignore requisitions, leaving the Confederation government perpetually underfunded. The Constitution's appropriations mechanism, combined with the federal power of direct taxation, gave Congress independent financial resources while maintaining legislative control over their expenditure.
