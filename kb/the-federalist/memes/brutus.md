# Brutus

**Brutus** was the pseudonym used by the Anti-Federalist writer widely believed to be Robert Yates (1738–1801), a New York judge and delegate to the [[Constitutional Convention]]. The Brutus essays, published in the New-York Journal from October 1787 through April 1788, constituted the most systematic Anti-Federalist critique of the proposed Constitution and engaged directly with the arguments of [[The Federalist Papers]].

## Biography

Robert Yates was born in Schenectady, New York, and studied law under William Livingston before establishing a legal practice in Albany. He served on the New York Supreme Court from 1777 to 1798, eventually becoming its Chief Justice. Yates was selected as one of New York's three delegates to the Constitutional Convention of 1787, alongside [[Alexander Hamilton]] and John Lansing Jr. At the convention, Yates and Lansing formed a minority bloc opposed to the nationalist direction of the proceedings; they left Philadelphia in early July 1787, after concluding that the convention had exceeded its mandate to merely amend the Articles of Confederation. Their departure left Hamilton as New York's sole remaining delegate, though without a quorum Hamilton could not cast a binding vote for the state.

Yates's opposition to the Constitution continued after the convention adjourned. Under the pen name "Brutus" — a reference to the Roman republican who opposed tyranny — he published a series of sixteen essays that appeared in Thomas Greenleaf's New-York Journal between October 18, 1787 and April 10, 1788. A second Anti-Federalist writer, "Sydney" (likely Melancton Smith), published additional essays in the same paper. Together, the Brutus essays provided the most thorough and intellectually serious Anti-Federalist case against ratification.

## The Brutus Essays: Structure and Core Themes

The sixteen Brutus essays addressed the proposed Constitution in a systematic fashion. The first essay (October 18, 1787) set out the fundamental question: whether the proposed government could be reconciled with the preservation of liberty. Brutus framed the choice as one between a consolidated national government that would absorb the states and a federal system that preserved the states as essential components of the republic.

Subsequent essays examined specific features of the proposed Constitution. Brutus devoted particular attention to three structural concerns that he believed threatened republican liberty:

1. **The federal judiciary** (Essays XI–XII, XIV–XV): Brutus argued that the proposed federal courts would possess dangerously broad jurisdiction, that judges would be unaccountable to the people, and that the combination of life tenure and the power of judicial review would enable courts to mold the Constitution "into any shape" that suited their preferences. He predicted — accurately, in retrospect — that federal courts would interpret their powers expansively under the Necessary and Proper Clause.

2. **The taxing power** (Essays V–VI): Brutus contended that the federal government's power to levy taxes directly on individuals, combined with the Supremacy Clause, would enable Congress to drain the states of their fiscal resources and render them dependent on the federal government. He argued that concurrent federal and state taxing authority could not coexist without the former eventually consuming the latter.

3. **The Necessary and Proper Clause and the Supremacy Clause** (Essays I, V, XI): Brutus warned that these two clauses, read together, would effectively abolish the states. If federal laws were supreme and Congress could enact any law it deemed "necessary and proper" to the exercise of its enumerated powers, then the constitutional limits on federal authority would be illusory. This argument directly anticipated Hamilton's defense of these clauses in Federalist No. 33.

Brutus also addressed representation (Essay IV), arguing that the House of Representatives was too small to adequately represent the diverse interests of the American people. This criticism prompted Madison's famous defense of the extended republic in Federalist No. 10.

## Engagement with the Federalist Papers

The Federalist Papers were written in direct response to the Anti-Federalist critique, and portions of The Federalist were crafted as specific rejoinders to Brutus. Publius acknowledged the seriousness of the Anti-Federalist challenge:

> The administration was substituted by Brutus, who stepped forward with a professed intention to discuss the subject in all its parts, and whose subsequent writings have shown that he is not without talents, though his efforts are directed more to the prejudices than to the judgment of his readers. [038-004]

This passage from Federalist No. 38 — likely penned by Madison — demonstrates that Publius regarded Brutus as a formidable opponent whose arguments required a substantive response. The dismissive characterization of Brutus as appealing to "prejudices" did not prevent Publius from engaging seriously with the substance of the Anti-Federalist case throughout the series.

The Anti-Federalist position is also acknowledged elsewhere in The Federalist. In Federalist No. 63, Publius invoked historical examples to counter the Anti-Federalist argument that republican government was inherently unstable:

> Unfortunately, however, for the anti-federal argument, the British history informs us that this hereditary assembly has not even been able to defend itself against the continual encroachments of the House of Representatives. [063-021]

## Historical Significance

The Brutus essays exercised considerable influence during the New York ratification debates. Although the ratification forces ultimately prevailed — New York ratified the Constitution on July 26, 1788 by a narrow vote of 30 to 27 — the Anti-Federalist critique shaped public expectations about how the Constitution would be interpreted and implemented. Several of Brutus's predictions proved prescient: the federal judiciary did expand its power through broad interpretations of Article III; the Necessary and Proper Clause did become a vehicle for expansive federal authority; and the taxing power did become a central instrument of federal governance.

The Brutus essays have been rediscovered by modern scholars as important contributions to American constitutional thought. They articulate a coherent vision of federalism rooted in the belief that republican liberty requires strong state governments as a counterweight to centralized authority. Brutus's arguments about judicial power, in particular, continue to feature in contemporary debates about the proper scope of federal judicial authority.
