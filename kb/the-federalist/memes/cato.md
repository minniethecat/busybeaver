# Cato

**Cato** was the pseudonym of an Anti-Federalist writer who published a series of essays opposing the ratification of the Constitution in New York newspapers during 1787–1788. The author is generally believed to have been George Clinton, the long-serving governor of New York and one of the most prominent opponents of the proposed Constitution. Cato's letters were among the most influential Anti-Federalist writings, directly engaging with the arguments of [[Publius]] in the Federalist Papers. Hamilton himself cited Cato by name in Federalist No. 67, referring readers to "CATO, No. V." [067-015]

## Cato's Anti-Federalist Arguments

Cato focused on several key objections to the proposed Constitution. He warned that the new federal government would consolidate power at the expense of the states, threatening the sovereignty that the Revolution had been fought to secure. He argued that the presidency — with its unity, lengthy term, and re-eligibility — was a disguised monarchy, comparing it unfavorably to the British crown. He insisted that the extensive powers granted to Congress, particularly over taxation and the military, would overwhelm the state governments and render them mere administrative appendages of a national leviathan.

Hamilton described the tactics of Cato and other Anti-Federalist writers as deliberate misrepresentations designed to exploit public fears of monarchy:

> Here the writers against the Constitution seem to have taken pains to signalize their talent of misrepresentation. Calculating upon the aversion of the people to monarchy, they have endeavored to enlist all their jealousies and apprehensions in opposition to the intended President of the United States; not merely as the embryo, but as the full-grown progeny, of that detested parent. [067-005]

Cato also objected to the breadth of federal judicial power, arguing that the combination of original and appellate jurisdiction would erode the authority of state courts and place ordinary citizens at the mercy of a distant, expensive national judiciary. His essays on this subject provoked Hamilton's extensive defense of the judiciary in Federalist Nos. 78–83.

## The Federalist Response

Hamilton and Madison responded directly to Cato's arguments throughout the Federalist Papers. In Federalist No. 67, Hamilton explicitly cited "Cato, No. V" while defending the executive powers against the charge of monarchism. [067-015] Hamilton characterized Cato's misrepresentations as exceeding even the ordinary liberties of political debate:

> They so far exceed the usual though unjustifiable licenses of party artifice, that even in a disposition the most candid and tolerant, they must force the sentiments which favor an indulgent construction of the conduct of political adversaries to give place to a voluntary and unreserved indignation. [067-007]

Hamilton's defense of the unitary executive in No. 70 and his detailed analysis of the presidency in Nos. 67–77 were, in substantial part, point-by-point refutations of Cato's critique. In Federalist No. 67, Hamilton singled out one particular misrepresentation by Cato — the claim that the President could fill casual vacancies in the Senate — and exposed it through a detailed textual analysis of the Constitution's appointment clauses. [067-008]–[067-012]

Madison likewise engaged with Cato's arguments on federal power, representation, and the dangers of consolidation. The Federalist response to Cato illustrates the ratification debates as a genuine intellectual contest — not a one-sided propaganda campaign but a dialogue between opposing constitutional visions.

## Cato and the Bill of Rights

Cato was among those Anti-Federalists who insisted that the Constitution required a bill of rights. His argument that a written enumeration of individual liberties was necessary to restrain the new federal government contributed to the eventual adoption of the first ten amendments. While Cato opposed ratification, his critique helped produce one of the Constitution's most enduring features.

## Legacy

The identity of Cato has been debated by historians. The consensus attributes the letters to George Clinton, though some scholars have suggested other New York Anti-Federalists or even a collaborative authorship. Regardless of the author's precise identity, Cato's letters remain essential primary sources for understanding the Anti-Federalist position — and, by reflection, the Federalist arguments they provoked. Hamilton acknowledged the influence of these writings by taking the trouble to engage with them directly, noting that Cato had "had no inconsiderable share in the applauses of his party." [067-009]
