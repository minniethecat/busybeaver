# Publius

## Role in The Federalist

"Publius" is the pseudonym under which Alexander Hamilton, James Madison, and John Jay collectively wrote The Federalist Papers between October 1787 and August 1788. The name was chosen by Hamilton, who had used it previously in 1778, and was designed to invoke the authority of Publius Valerius Publicola — the Roman consul who helped overthrow the Tarquin monarchy and establish the Roman Republic in 509 BCE. By adopting this name, the three authors presented themselves not as partisan politicians but as republican statesmen in the classical tradition, deliberating on the most serious question a people can face: whether and how to constitute their government.

The pseudonym served multiple functions. It shielded the authors from personal attacks and allowed them to speak with a single, authoritative voice — a "Publius" whose arguments carried the weight of Roman republican tradition. It also reinforced the Federalist argument that the Constitution was not the work of any single faction or individual but the product of reason and reflection. The three authors maintained the fiction of a single author throughout the series, though contemporaries quickly guessed their identities.

## Biographical Reference

Publius Valerius Publicola (died 503 BCE) was one of the first Roman consuls, celebrated for his role in expelling the Tarquins and establishing the Roman Republic. His name "Publicola" means "friend of the people," reflecting his reputation as a defender of popular liberty against tyranny. Plutarch's *Life of Publicola* portrays him as the ideal republican magistrate: a man who used power to protect the people's rights rather than to advance his own. The choice of this particular Roman hero — rather than a more famous figure like Cato or Cicero — suggests Hamilton's intention to associate the Constitution with the founding of a republic, not merely its preservation.

## Key Ideas / Contributions

As a collective author, "Publius" articulates a unified political philosophy that transcends the individual views of the three authors:

1. **The necessity of energetic government**: Publius consistently argues that a government without sufficient power cannot protect liberty, enforce laws, or defend the nation. "Energy in the Executive is a leading character in the definition of good government" (070-003).

2. **The danger of faction and the remedy of the extended republic**: Publius's most famous argument — that a large, diverse republic controls the effects of faction better than a small, homogeneous one — is developed in Federalist No. 10 (010-022).

3. **Structural safeguards against tyranny**: Publius argues that the Constitution's separation of powers, checks and balances, and federalism create multiple, redundant protections against the accumulation of power that defines tyranny (047-005).

4. **The Union as essential to American prosperity and security**: From the opening essays on foreign dangers to the closing essays on the judiciary, Publius returns repeatedly to the idea that disunion would be catastrophic — inviting foreign intervention, domestic conflict, and the loss of everything the Revolution had won.

## Federalist Papers Authored

Under the name Publius, 85 essays were published in New York newspapers between October 27, 1787, and August 16, 1788. Hamilton wrote 51, Madison 29, and Jay 5 (Jay fell ill after No. 5 and contributed only No. 64 thereafter). The authorship of several essays (Nos. 18–20, 49–58, 62–63) was disputed between Hamilton and Madison for decades; modern scholarship, using stylometric analysis, largely confirms Madison's authorship of these disputed essays.

The essays were first collected in two volumes in March and May 1788; a second edition, including the final eight essays, appeared later that year. The complete collection has never been out of print and remains the most influential work of American political theory ever written.

## Relationship to the Constitution

"Publius" is not a framer but the Constitution's most authoritative expositor. The Federalist Papers have been cited by the Supreme Court in hundreds of decisions as evidence of the original understanding of constitutional provisions. In *United States v. Curtiss-Wright* (1936), Justice Sutherland called The Federalist "the most authoritative source of contemporary interpretation of the Constitution." In *Printz v. United States* (1997), Justice Scalia declared that "the Federalist is the most important source for determining the original meaning of the Constitution."

The pseudonym's authority derives from its collective nature: Publius's arguments represent not one man's opinion but the considered judgment of three of the Constitution's most knowledgeable defenders, sustained over ten months of public debate and tested by the most intense political scrutiny.

## Legacy

The name Publius has become synonymous with the Federalist Papers and, by extension, with the American constitutional tradition. The pseudonym is periodically revived: in the 20th and 21st centuries, scholars, judges, and commentators have published under the name "Publius" to signal that their arguments participate in the tradition of constitutional deliberation the original Publius inaugurated. The name endures as a reminder that the Constitution was not handed down from authority but argued for, debated, and ultimately ratified by a free people exercising "reflection and choice."
