# Post Roads

**Post roads** are the routes and highways designated by Congress for the transportation of mail. Article I, Section 8, Clause 7 of the Constitution grants Congress the power "to establish Post Offices and post Roads," providing the constitutional foundation for the national postal system and, more broadly, for federal involvement in transportation infrastructure.

## In the Federalist Papers

Madison, in Federalist No. 42, addressed the postal power as part of his review of the miscellaneous Article I, Section 8 powers. He grouped it with the regulation of commerce, the coining of money, the punishment of counterfeiting, uniform naturalization, and uniform bankruptcy laws — a cluster of powers that together constituted the legal framework for a functioning national union [042-012].

Madison offered a notably restrained defense of the postal power, characterizing it as a modest but useful grant:

> The power of establishing post roads must, in every view, be a harmless power, and may, perhaps, by judicious management, become productive of great public conveniency. Nothing which tends to facilitate the intercourse between the States can be deemed unworthy of the public care. [042-022]

## A "Harmless Power"

Madison's description of the postal power as "harmless" was a strategic choice in the ratification debates. Anti-Federalist critics had warned that every grant of power to the federal government, however innocuous in appearance, could be stretched beyond its original purpose. Madison responded by emphasizing the limited and benign character of this particular power — it was difficult to imagine how the establishment of post roads could threaten liberty.

Yet the word "harmless" concealed the power's potential. In the centuries since ratification, the postal clause has been interpreted as a source of broad federal authority over transportation infrastructure. Post roads became the arteries of the early republic, and the designation of a road as a "post road" carried legal consequences — including federal jurisdiction over crimes committed on or near postal routes.

## Facilitating Interstate Intercourse

Madison's justification for the postal power — that it facilitated "intercourse between the States" — was linked to the broader purpose of the Constitution: to transform a loose confederation of semi-sovereign states into a genuine national union. Communication was essential to union. Without reliable postal service, citizens of different states would remain strangers to one another, and the shared political identity necessary for republican government could not develop.

The postal power thus served a dual function. Its immediate purpose was practical — the delivery of letters, newspapers, and parcels. Its deeper purpose was political — the creation of the communication networks that would bind the states into a single people. The "public conveniency" Madison mentioned was not merely economic; it was civic.

## Post Offices and the Public Sphere

The establishment of post offices under the Constitution created the physical infrastructure of the early American public sphere. Newspapers — the principal medium of political discourse in the early republic — traveled through the postal system at subsidized rates. The Post Office Act of 1792 established a policy of low postal rates for newspapers, a subsidy that the historian Richard John has called "the largest single subsidy to the press in American history."

This postal policy had profound constitutional implications. By facilitating the circulation of political news and opinion across state lines, the postal system helped to create a genuinely national political discourse — the condition for the kind of extended republic that Madison had theorized in Federalist No. 10. The postal clause, though modest in Madison's presentation, proved to be one of the most consequential grants of power in the Constitution.

## The Scope of the Power

The phrase "post roads" left room for interpretation. Did it authorize Congress only to designate existing roads as postal routes, or to build new roads? In practice, Congress exercised both powers: it designated existing roads as post roads (thereby extending federal jurisdiction over them) and, eventually, funded the construction of new roads through the General Survey Act of 1824 and subsequent internal improvements legislation.

The Supreme Court, in *In re Jackson* (1877), held that the postal power authorized Congress to prohibit the use of the mails for lottery circulars — an early recognition that the power to "establish" post offices and post roads included the power to regulate the content that traveled through them. This regulatory dimension of the postal power has been the basis for federal laws governing mail fraud, obscenity, and other postal offenses.

## Legacy and Significance

The postal power illustrates a recurring pattern in American constitutional development: modest grants of power, described in unthreatening terms during ratification, prove to have far-reaching implications when exercised over decades and centuries. The post roads of the eighteenth century became the interstate highways of the twentieth; the post offices became the communications infrastructure of a continental republic. Madison's "harmless power" became, in the hands of subsequent Congresses, an instrument of national integration that the Framers could scarcely have imagined.
