# Taxation

## Definition

**Taxation** is the power of government to compel contributions from the people to fund public operations and the common defense. In the Federalist Papers, the power to tax is treated as the most essential attribute of sovereignty — "money is, with propriety, considered as the vital principle of the body politic" (030-004). The Articles of Confederation's fatal weakness was its inability to levy taxes directly, requiring instead "requisitions" upon the states that were routinely ignored.

## In The Federalist Papers

Hamilton devotes Federalist Nos. 30–36 to the taxing power, the lengthiest sustained treatment of any single power in the entire collection. He begins with a first principle: "A government ought to contain in itself every power requisite to the full accomplishment of the objects committed to its care" (030-004). Since the federal government is responsible for the common defense and general welfare, it must possess the means to fund those responsibilities — and those means must be "without limitation" because "the necessities of a nation, in every stage of its existence, will be found at least equal to its resources" (030-004).

The critical defect of the Articles of Confederation was that Congress could only make "requisitions" on the states — requests for funds that states could and did ignore. "The great and radical vice in the construction of the existing Confederation is in the principle of LEGISLATION for STATES or GOVERNMENTS, in their CORPORATE or COLLECTIVE CAPACITIES, and as contradistinguished from the INDIVIDUALS of which they consist" (015-014). The new Constitution corrects this by giving the federal government power to tax individuals directly.

Hamilton addresses the fear that federal taxation will "swallow up" state revenues. He argues that the states will retain exclusive access to certain revenue sources — particularly property taxes and internal commerce — while the federal government will rely primarily on import duties (tariffs) and excise taxes. There is "no probability that the State governments will ever lose the power of direct taxation," because it is "an independent and uncontrollable authority" essential to their existence (032-003).

## Historical Context

Taxation was the proximate cause of the American Revolution — "no taxation without representation" was the rallying cry against British rule. The Articles of Confederation deliberately denied the central government taxing power, requiring it to request funds from the states through a requisition system that proved disastrous. Between 1781 and 1786, Congress requested $15.7 million from the states and received only $2.4 million. The federal government could not pay its debts, fund the army, or conduct foreign policy — a situation Hamilton called "the present shadow of a federal government" (015-017).

## Key Arguments

1. **The power to tax must be unlimited**: "Money is, with propriety, considered as the vital principle of the body politic; as that which sustains its life and motion, and enables it to perform its most essential functions" (030-004). A complete government requires a complete power of revenue.

2. **Concurrent taxation is sustainable**: The federal government will rely primarily on tariffs (import duties), while states dominate direct taxation (property, internal commerce). Each level can access the tax base without destroying the other.

3. **Direct taxation of individuals is essential**: Requisitions on states fail because states have no incentive to comply and no enforcement mechanism exists. Direct taxation of citizens, enforced by federal collectors and courts, solves this structural problem.

4. **Tariffs are the least burdensome tax**: Import duties are paid voluntarily — consumers choose whether to purchase foreign goods — and the cost is embedded in prices. This makes tariffs less visible and less resented than direct taxes.

## Constitutional Implementation

- **Article I, Section 8, Clause 1**: Congress has power "to lay and collect Taxes, Duties, Imposts and Excises, to pay the Debts and provide for the common Defence and general Welfare."
- **Article I, Section 2, Clause 3**: Direct taxes must be apportioned among the states by population (modified by the Sixteenth Amendment, 1913, which authorized the income tax without apportionment).
- **Article I, Section 9, Clause 4**: No capitation or other direct tax shall be laid unless in proportion to the census.
- **Article I, Section 10, Clause 2**: States may not levy import or export duties without congressional consent — ensuring federal control over foreign commerce revenue.

## Anti-Federalist Counterarguments

Anti-Federalists viewed unlimited federal taxing power as the death knell of state sovereignty. Brutus argued that "the power to tax is the power to destroy" — once the federal government could reach into every citizen's pocket, no state government could resist its expansion. They demanded that direct taxation be restricted to cases where import duties proved insufficient, or that the states retain exclusive jurisdiction over direct taxation. The Federalists rejected these limitations as fatal to effective government.

## Legacy and Significance

The Sixteenth Amendment (1913) authorized the federal income tax, fundamentally altering the federal-state fiscal balance Hamilton described. With the income tax, the federal government gained access to a vastly larger revenue base than tariffs could provide, enabling the expansion of federal programs in the twentieth century. The power to tax remains at the center of constitutional debates about the scope of federal power — from the Affordable Care Act's individual mandate (upheld as a tax in *NFIB v. Sebelius*, 2012) to debates about wealth taxes and carbon taxes.
