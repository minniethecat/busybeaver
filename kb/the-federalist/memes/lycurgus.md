# Lycurgus (c. 800 BCE)

## Role in The Federalist

Lycurgus appears in The Federalist Papers as the archetypal lawgiver — the figure who, according to Plutarch, gave Sparta its unique mixed constitution combining elements of monarchy (two kings), aristocracy (the Gerousia, or council of elders), and democracy (the assembly of citizens). Publius cites Sparta's constitution, attributed to Lycurgus, as both a precedent for and a cautionary tale about the separation of powers.

In Federalist No. 9, Hamilton notes that the Spartan republic, like other ancient confederacies, was "kept together by the personal character of a single individual" — a fragility the American Constitution was designed to avoid (009-003). In Federalist No. 38, Madison compares the Constitution's novelty to "the government of Lycurgus," observing that Lycurgus's constitution, like the American one, was "an original compact" rather than an incremental development (038-005).

## Biography

Lycurgus is a semi-legendary figure. Ancient sources, particularly Plutarch's *Life of Lycurgus*, place him in Sparta around the 9th or 8th century BCE. According to tradition, he traveled to Crete, Ionia, and Egypt to study their laws and constitutions before returning to Sparta to institute a sweeping reform of the Spartan state. His constitution divided power among the dual kings, the council of elders, the assembly, and the ephors — creating a mixed government that endured for centuries and inspired later political theorists, including Montesquieu.

## Key Ideas / Contributions

1. **The mixed constitution**: The idea that the best government combines elements of monarchy, aristocracy, and democracy — a theory that deeply influenced the Framers' design of the American Constitution.
2. **The lawgiver as founder**: Lycurgus exemplified the classical idea that a single wise legislator could establish a durable constitutional order — a model the Framers both admired and departed from, preferring a collective deliberative process.

## Legacy

Lycurgus's constitutional design — particularly the idea of a mixed government balancing competing social forces — influenced Montesquieu's theory of separated powers, which in turn shaped the American Constitution. The Framers' study of ancient republics, mediated through Plutarch and Montesquieu, made Lycurgus's Sparta a permanent, if distant, reference point in American constitutional thought.
