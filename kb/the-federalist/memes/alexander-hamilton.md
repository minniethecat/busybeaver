# Alexander Hamilton (1755–1804)

[[Alexander Hamilton]] conceived, organized, and wrote the majority of [[the-federalist-papers|The Federalist Papers]], authoring 51 of the 85 essays. As the first Secretary of the Treasury and a delegate to the [[constitutional-convention|Constitutional Convention]], he was among the most influential advocates for a strong national government, an energetic [[executive-power|executive]], and an independent [[judicial-review|judiciary]].

## Role in The Federalist

Alexander Hamilton conceived, organized, and wrote the majority of The Federalist Papers. Of the 85 essays, Hamilton authored 51 — roughly sixty percent of the series. He was the driving force behind the project, recruiting John Jay and James Madison as collaborators and setting the publication schedule that sustained the series from October 1787 through August 1788.

Hamilton's essays cover the full range of constitutional topics but concentrate on three areas where his expertise and vision were strongest: national defense and the Union (Nos. 1–36), the executive branch (Nos. 67–77), and the judiciary (Nos. 78–83). In Federalist No. 1, he frames the entire enterprise as a deliberation on "whether societies of men are really capable or not of establishing good government from reflection and choice, or whether they are forever destined to depend for their political constitutions on accident and force" (001-003).

His advocacy for "energy in the executive" — the idea that a single, vigorous president was essential to good government — is among the most influential arguments in all of American political thought. In Federalist No. 70, Hamilton declares that:

> Energy in the Executive is a leading character in the definition of good government. It is essential to the protection of the community against foreign attacks; it is not less essential to the steady administration of the laws; to the protection of property against those irregular and high-handed combinations which sometimes interrupt the ordinary course of justice; to the security of liberty against the enterprises and assaults of ambition, of faction, and of anarchy.
>
> (070-003)

This essay, together with his defenses of specific presidential powers (commander-in-chief, pardon, treaty, appointment), established the intellectual foundation for the American presidency.

## Biography

Born in Nevis, British West Indies, in 1755 (or 1757 — the date remains uncertain), Hamilton was the illegitimate son of a Scottish merchant. His early life was marked by adversity: his father abandoned the family, his mother died of yellow fever when he was about thirteen, and he was left to clerk at a trading firm in St. Croix.

Recognizing his brilliance, community leaders funded his education in New York, where he arrived in 1773. At King's College (now Columbia), he wrote pamphlets supporting the revolutionary cause. When the war broke out, he joined the Continental Army, rising to become George Washington's aide-de-camp — the most trusted staff officer of the commander-in-chief. He led a decisive bayonet charge at Yorktown in 1781.

After the war, Hamilton practiced law in New York, served in the Confederation Congress, and became a leading advocate for constitutional reform. He was a delegate to the Constitutional Convention of 1787, where he proposed a plan for an extremely strong national government. Though his plan was not adopted, he became the Constitution's most energetic defender during ratification. As the first Secretary of the Treasury (1789–1795), he established the national bank, funded the federal debt, and created the foundations of American finance.

Hamilton's life ended dramatically: he was killed in a duel with Aaron Burr, the sitting Vice President, on July 11, 1804, in Weehawken, New Jersey — a death that shocked the nation and removed one of its most brilliant founders at age forty-nine.

## Key Ideas / Contributions

Hamilton's political thought centers on three core principles:

1. **Energy in government is essential to liberty**: A weak government cannot protect rights or preserve order. "A feeble executive implies a feeble execution of the government" — and a feebly executed government is "a bad government" (070-003).

2. **Commerce and national power are mutually reinforcing**: A flourishing commerce requires a strong navy, generates revenue, and binds citizens together through mutual interest. Hamilton's commercial vision, articulated in Federalist Nos. 11–13, was among the most forward-looking of any Founder.

3. **Judicial independence is foundational to limited government**: In Federalist No. 78, Hamilton argues that courts must have "an independent spirit" and the power of judicial review — "the right of the courts to pronounce legislative acts void" — because the Constitution is fundamental law, and "no legislative act... contrary to the Constitution, can be valid" (078-007).

## On the Necessity of Union

Hamilton's most urgent argument runs through the first thirty-six Federalist essays: the American states must remain united. In Federalist No. 6, he warns that disunion would lead to war among the states, driven by the same passions that had plagued Europe for centuries:

> To look for a continuation of harmony between a number of independent, unconnected sovereignties situated in the same neighborhood would be to disregard the uniform course of human events, and to set at defiance the accumulated experience of ages.
>
> (006-004)

In Federalist No. 8, he argues that disunion would militarize the continent, forcing each fragment to maintain standing armies that would "gradually poison the Springs of Morality" and lead to despotism (008-012). The proposed Union, by contrast, would serve as "a barrier against domestic faction and insurrection" (009-003).

## Federalist Papers Authored

Hamilton wrote 51 of the 85 Federalist Papers, a remarkable output sustained over ten months while also practicing law full-time. His contributions cluster around three major themes:

| Theme | Essays | Key Arguments |
|-------|--------|--------------|
| Utility of the Union | 1–36 | National defense, commerce, taxation, and the danger of disunion |
| The Executive | 67–77 | Unity, duration, powers (commander-in-chief, pardon, treaty, appointment) |
| The Judiciary | 78–83 | Judicial review, tenure, jurisdiction, jury trials |

## Relationship to the Constitution

Hamilton was both a framer and a defender of the Constitution. At the Philadelphia Convention, he advocated for a powerful national executive and a Senate serving for life — proposals that went far beyond what the Convention adopted. Yet once the final compromise was reached, Hamilton became its most tireless advocate, conceiving The Federalist as a comprehensive defense of the entire constitutional plan.

As Treasury Secretary, Hamilton's interpretation of the "necessary and proper" clause — that Congress could charter a national bank as a means to carry out its enumerated fiscal powers — established the doctrine of implied powers that would shape constitutional law for two centuries. His 1791 opinion on the constitutionality of the Bank of the United States remains a foundational document of American constitutional interpretation.

## Legacy

Hamilton's legacy is vast and contested. His financial system — the national bank, funded debt, and federal assumption of state debts — created the fiscal infrastructure of the modern American state. His vision of an industrial, commercially vigorous, globally engaged America has largely triumphed over the agrarian republic envisioned by Jefferson. His defense of judicial review in Federalist No. 78 provided the intellectual foundation for *Marbury v. Madison* (1803) and the American system of constitutional adjudication.

Yet Hamilton has also been the most polarizing Founder — celebrated by nationalists and business interests, distrusted by advocates of limited government and states' rights. The musical *Hamilton* (2015) introduced a new generation to his story, emphasizing his immigrant origins, his relentless productivity, and the duel that cut short one of the most consequential lives in American history.
