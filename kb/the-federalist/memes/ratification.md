# Ratification

## Definition

**Ratification** is the formal process through which a proposed constitution receives the assent of the sovereign people, acting through specially elected state conventions, and thereby acquires the force of fundamental law. The Constitution's ratification procedure — requiring the approval of nine of thirteen state conventions rather than unanimous consent of the state legislatures — was itself a revolutionary departure from the Articles of Confederation, designed to establish the Constitution on the authority of "We the People" rather than on a compact among the states.

## In The Federalist Papers

The ratification debate is the immediate context of every word in The Federalist Papers. The essays were written as newspaper editorials aimed at persuading the New York ratifying convention to approve the Constitution. Publius's arguments are thus not dispassionate theorizing but interventions in an ongoing political struggle over whether the proposed frame of government should be adopted.

Madison addresses ratification most directly in Federalist No. 39, where he defends the legitimacy of the ratification process. The Constitution, he argues, is "to be founded on the assent and ratification of the people of America, given by deputies elected for the special purpose" (039-012). This distinguishes it from both a mere treaty among states (which the Articles had been) and a unilateral imposition by a national government. The ratification by state conventions — rather than by state legislatures — ensures that the Constitution rests on an act of popular sovereignty: "the people themselves, in their sovereign capacity, acting through representatives chosen for that express purpose."

In Federalist No. 43, Madison justifies the provision that ratification by only nine states would suffice to establish the Constitution among the ratifying states. Critics charged that this violated the Articles of Confederation, which required unanimous consent of the states for any amendment. Madison responds that "the ratification of the conventions of nine States shall be sufficient for the establishment of this Constitution between the States so ratifying the same" was a practical necessity (043-029). Requiring unanimity would have allowed "the non-compliance of a single State to defeat the establishment of a government for the whole" (043-030).

Hamilton, in Federalist No. 22, had already condemned the unanimity requirement of the Articles as a fatal defect: "The Congress, as consisting of delegates annually appointed by the legislatures of the different States, never had a ratification by the PEOPLE" (022-021). Without popular ratification, the Articles lacked the moral and legal authority that only the consent of the governed could provide. The Constitution's ratification process was designed to remedy precisely this defect.

## Historical Context

The Articles of Confederation (ratified in 1781) had been adopted by the state legislatures acting as legislative bodies — it was, in effect, a treaty among thirteen sovereign states. This mode of adoption had two consequences: first, the Articles lacked the direct popular legitimacy of a constitution rooted in the people; second, because the Articles were a compact among states, any state could block amendments by withholding consent.

The Constitutional Convention of 1787 exceeded its original mandate (to revise the Articles) and produced an entirely new frame of government. To give this new Constitution the legitimacy and durability the Articles lacked, the Framers bypassed both Congress and the state legislatures, submitting the Constitution directly to state conventions elected by the people for the specific purpose of considering ratification. This was a deliberate act of constitutional statecraft: by grounding the Constitution in popular sovereignty, the Framers sought to make it "the supreme Law of the Land" rather than a revocable agreement among states.

## Key Arguments

The Federalist argument for the ratification process rests on three interlocking claims about the nature of constitutional legitimacy. The first and most fundamental is that popular sovereignty requires direct popular ratification: a constitution that governs the people must be ratified by the people themselves, not merely by their legislative agents. Legislative ratification, Hamilton argues, would reduce the Constitution to an ordinary statute, revocable at will and lacking the special authority that distinguishes fundamental law from everyday legislation (022-021). The second claim is one of practical necessity: requiring unanimity — as the Articles did for amendments — "would have subjected the essential interests of the whole to the caprice or corruption of a single member" (043-030). The nine-state threshold ensures that a recalcitrant minority cannot block the will of the great majority, while still requiring a supermajority sufficient to demonstrate broad national consent.

The third claim concerns institutional design: state conventions are superior to state legislatures as ratifying bodies because they are elected for the specific purpose of considering the Constitution. Unlike sitting legislators — who might oppose a Constitution that limits state power out of institutional self-interest — convention delegates are chosen by the people for this single question and are therefore more genuinely representative of the people's views on ratification itself (039-012). Together, these three arguments establish that the ratification process is not merely expedient but is the only method consistent with the republican principle that legitimate government rests on the consent of the governed.

## Constitutional Implementation

The ratification process is established in **Article VII**: "The Ratification of the Conventions of nine States, shall be sufficient for the Establishment of this Constitution between the States so ratifying the Same." The Constitution was ratified in stages: Delaware was first (December 7, 1787), and New Hampshire became the decisive ninth state on June 21, 1788. Virginia and New York — the two largest and most influential states — ratified shortly thereafter, ensuring that the Union would be geographically and economically viable. Rhode Island, the last holdout, did not ratify until May 29, 1790.

## Legacy and Significance

The ratification process established the principle that the American Constitution rests on popular sovereignty — the consent of the governed — rather than on a compact among states. This principle would later be invoked by Abraham Lincoln in denying the legitimacy of secession: the Union was not a league of states that any state could leave at will but a national government created by "the people of the United States."

The ratification debates also produced the most important body of constitutional commentary in American history: the Federalist Papers themselves, the Anti-Federalist essays, and the records of the state ratifying conventions. These materials continue to be cited by courts and scholars as evidence of the original understanding of constitutional provisions. The promise to add a Bill of Rights — extracted by the Anti-Federalists during the ratification struggle — led to the first ten amendments, ratified in 1791.
