# Marbury v. Madison

**Marbury v. Madison** (5 U.S. 137, 1803) is the landmark Supreme Court decision that established the principle of [[judicial-review|judicial review]] — the power of federal courts to declare acts of Congress unconstitutional. Chief Justice John Marshall's opinion realized the defense of judicial review that [[alexander-hamilton|Alexander Hamilton]] had laid out in Federalist No. 78, transforming a theoretical argument into operational constitutional law.

## The Case

The case arose from the transition between the Adams and Jefferson administrations. In the final days of his presidency, John Adams appointed William Marbury as a justice of the peace for the District of Columbia. The commission was signed and sealed but not delivered before Adams left office. When [[thomas-jefferson|Thomas Jefferson]] became president, his Secretary of State, [[james-madison|James Madison]], refused to deliver the commission. Marbury filed suit directly in the Supreme Court, seeking a writ of mandamus to compel delivery.

## Marshall's Decision

Chief Justice John Marshall structured his opinion around three questions:

1. **Did Marbury have a right to the commission?** Yes — the commission was signed and sealed, and delivery was a ministerial act that the Court could compel.

2. **Did the law provide a remedy?** Yes — "the very essence of civil liberty certainly consists in the right of every individual to claim the protection of the laws."

3. **Was mandamus from the Supreme Court the proper remedy?** No — the statute granting the Court original jurisdiction to issue writs of mandamus (Section 13 of the Judiciary Act of 1789) was unconstitutional because it expanded the Court's original jurisdiction beyond what Article III of the [[constitution|Constitution]] permits.

This final holding — that the Court could not order delivery of the commission because the statute authorizing it to do so was void — is the foundation of judicial review.

## Connection to The Federalist Papers

Hamilton's Federalist No. 78 provides the theoretical framework that Marshall's opinion activates. Hamilton argued that courts must have the power to declare unconstitutional laws void, because "no legislative act... contrary to the Constitution, can be valid" and courts are the "intermediate body between the people and the legislature" tasked with keeping the latter "within the limits assigned to their authority" (078-008).

Marshall's opinion echoes Hamilton's reasoning: "It is emphatically the province and duty of the judicial department to say what the law is." If the Constitution is superior to an ordinary statute, and both conflict, the Constitution must prevail — otherwise "the legislature may alter the constitution by an ordinary act."

Hamilton had defended life tenure for federal judges as essential to the "independent spirit" required for constitutional review (078-010). Marshall, himself a lifetime appointee, exercised exactly that independence in striking down a statute passed by Congress.

## Broader Impact

Marbury v. Madison is widely regarded as the most important decision in American constitutional law. It established:

- **Judicial review of federal legislation**: Courts may strike down acts of Congress that conflict with the [[constitution|Constitution]].
- **The Constitution as supreme law**: The written Constitution, not legislative will, is the highest legal authority.
- **The judiciary as constitutional guardian**: Courts have a special duty to interpret and enforce constitutional limits, consistent with the vision of the [[judiciary]] as outlined in the [[separation-of-powers]] framework.

The decision's genius was strategic: Marshall gave Jefferson a short-term victory (no mandamus issued, Marbury did not get his commission) while securing the Court's long-term institutional power. By declining to exercise power, the Court claimed a far greater one.

## Critique and Debate

The decision has generated enduring scholarly debate. Critics argue that nothing in Article III explicitly grants the Supreme Court the power of judicial review — Hamilton's argument in Federalist No. 78 was advocacy, not neutral constitutional interpretation. The "counter-majoritarian difficulty" posed by unelected judges overturning laws enacted by elected representatives remains the central tension in American constitutional theory.

Yet Marshall's opinion endures because it rests on a logic that Hamilton identified and that the constitutional structure implies: a written constitution that limits government power is meaningless if the limited body itself decides the scope of its limits. As Hamilton wrote, "the courts were designed to be an intermediate body between the people and the legislature... to keep the latter within the limits assigned to their authority" (078-008). Marbury made that design operational.
