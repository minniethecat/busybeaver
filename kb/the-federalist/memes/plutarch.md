# Plutarch (c. 46–119 CE)

## Role in The Federalist

Plutarch is the invisible presence behind many of The Federalist Papers' classical references. His *Parallel Lives* — paired biographies of Greek and Roman statesmen — was the primary source through which the Framers knew the stories of Lycurgus, Solon, Theseus, Romulus, Numa, Publicola, and the other ancient lawgivers they cited as precedents and cautionary tales. When Madison discusses "the government of Lycurgus" or Hamilton mentions "the Spartan republic," they are drawing, mediately if not immediately, on Plutarch's biographies.

Plutarch is cited by name in Federalist No. 38, where Madison observes that even "the government of Athens, of Sparta, of Rome" — all known through Plutarch — were "the work of a single illustrious citizen" rather than of a collective deliberative body like the Constitutional Convention (038-005).

## Biography

Plutarch was born in Chaeronea, a small town in Boeotia (central Greece), around 46 CE. He studied philosophy in Athens, traveled widely in the Roman Empire, and spent his later years as a priest at the Temple of Apollo at Delphi. His *Parallel Lives*, composed late in his career, paired a Greek and a Roman figure in each volume and drew moral and political lessons from their lives. His other major work, the *Moralia*, is a collection of essays on ethical, religious, and political topics.

Plutarch's influence on the American Founders is difficult to overstate. Alongside Montesquieu, he was one of the most widely read classical authors among the revolutionary generation. Hamilton, Madison, and Jay had all read Plutarch as part of their classical education, and his biographies shaped their understanding of what a republic was, how it functioned, and why it failed.

## Legacy

Plutarch's *Lives* remained a staple of American education well into the nineteenth century. His biographical method — drawing political lessons from the lives of statesmen — influenced the Founders' own approach to political argument. The classical republics Plutarch described, though imperfect by modern standards, provided the intellectual materials from which the Framers constructed their new "science of politics."
