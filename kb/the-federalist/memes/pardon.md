# Pardon Power

## Definition

The **pardon power** is the President's constitutional authority to grant reprieves (temporary suspensions of punishment) and pardons (permanent forgiveness) for offenses against the United States, with the sole exception of impeachment. It is an extraordinary power — essentially monarchical in origin — that the Framers entrusted to a single executive rather than to a legislative body or court, believing that "the benign prerogative of pardoning" would be exercised with greater wisdom and responsibility by one person than by a multitude.

## In The Federalist Papers

Hamilton provides the definitive Federalist analysis of the pardon power in Federalist No. 74. He begins by acknowledging the gravity of the power: "Humanity and good policy conspire to dictate, that the benign prerogative of pardoning should be as little as possible fettered or embarrassed" (074-005). The pardon exists to temper justice with mercy — to correct the inevitable injustices that arise when general laws are applied to particular cases.

Hamilton offers two principal justifications for vesting the pardon in a single executive. First, "the sense of responsibility is always strongest, in proportion as it is undivided" — a president who alone must answer for his pardons will exercise the power with greater caution than a council or committee (074-006). Second, in cases of national emergency — particularly treason and insurrection — "a well-timed offer of pardon to the insurgents or rebels may restore the tranquility of the commonwealth" more effectively than military force alone (074-006).

Hamilton explicitly excludes impeachment from the pardon power, noting that this limitation "takes away the motive" for a corrupt president to conspire with subordinates and then pardon them: the president himself remains subject to impeachment and removal regardless of any pardons he issues.

## Historical Context

The pardon power descends from the English royal prerogative of mercy, which the Crown exercised through the "king's peace." The Framers transferred this power from monarch to president but limited it in crucial respects: no pardon may bar impeachment, and the power extends only to federal offenses. Several state constitutions divided the pardon power between the governor and a council; the Framers rejected this model, following Hamilton's argument that shared responsibility would produce either excessive leniency (each member deflecting blame) or excessive severity (no member willing to show mercy alone).

## Key Arguments

Hamilton advances three key arguments for the pardon power:

1. **Unity ensures responsible exercise**: A single executive, bearing sole responsibility, will exercise the pardon with greater "scrupulousness and caution" than a plural body where responsibility is diffused (074-006).

2. **Mercy is essential to justice**: Laws, being general, cannot anticipate every circumstance. The pardon allows the executive to correct unjust outcomes that the law, applied literally, would produce — cases where "the code alone" cannot do justice.

3. **Strategic clemency preserves the state**: In times of rebellion, "a well-timed offer of pardon" may pacify insurgents more effectively than military suppression, preserving lives and restoring order (074-006).

## Constitutional Implementation

The pardon power is established in **Article II, Section 2, Clause 1**: the President "shall have Power to grant Reprieves and Pardons for Offenses against the United States, except in Cases of Impeachment." The Supreme Court has interpreted this power broadly: a pardon may be issued at any time after the offense is committed (including before indictment), may cover any federal offense, and cannot be limited by Congress. However, a pardon does not expunge the conviction and does not bar civil liability. The President may also commute sentences (reduce punishment without full forgiveness) and grant amnesties to classes of offenders.

## Legacy and Significance

The pardon power has been among the most controversial of presidential powers. President Washington's pardon of the Whiskey Rebellion participants (1795) established the strategic use Hamilton envisioned. President Ford's pardon of Richard Nixon (1974) — issued before any criminal charges were filed — tested the limits of preemptive clemency. President Carter's amnesty for Vietnam draft evaders (1977) demonstrated the pardon's use as an instrument of national reconciliation. Each controversial pardon reignites Hamilton's core question: does a single person, acting alone, exercise this monarchical remnant with sufficient wisdom and restraint?
