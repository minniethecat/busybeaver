# Apportionment

**Apportionment** is the constitutional process of allocating seats in the House of Representatives among the states according to population. Article I of the Constitution ties the rule of apportionment for representation to the same rule governing the apportionment of direct taxes — a linkage that the Federalist Papers, especially Federalist No. 54, explore in detail through the lens of the three-fifths compromise and the relationship between persons and property.

## In the Federalist Papers

The question of apportionment occupied a central place in the ratification debates. Federalist No. 54, authored by Madison, addressed the most contentious aspect of the apportionment rule: whether slaves, considered as property under the laws of the Southern states, should be counted in the population for purposes of representation. Madison presented the reasoning of a hypothetical Southern advocate to illuminate the logic behind the three-fifths compromise.

The Constitution provided that representation and direct taxation would be apportioned according to the same numerical rule — the whole number of free persons plus three-fifths of "all other persons" (enslaved people). Madison explained that this linkage served a salutary purpose:

> As the accuracy of the census to be obtained by the Congress will necessarily depend, in a considerable degree on the disposition, if not on the co-operation, of the States, it is of great importance that the States should feel as little bias as possible, to swell or to reduce the amount of their numbers. [054-015]

## The Three-Fifths Compromise

The central theoretical puzzle of apportionment, as Madison presented it, was whether enslaved persons should be treated as persons (for representation) or as property (for taxation). The Southern position, as Madison characterized it, conceded that slaves partook of both qualities:

> The true state of the case is, that they partake of both these qualities: being considered by our laws, in some respects, as persons, and in other respects as property. [054-005]

The federal Constitution, Madison argued, decided with propriety to view slaves "in the mixed character of persons and of property" — the character bestowed on them by the laws under which they lived. This was not a moral endorsement of slavery but a recognition of existing legal realities.

The three-fifths ratio was thus presented as a "compromising expedient" — regarding the enslaved person "as divested of two fifths of the man." Madison's rhetorical strategy was to present both Northern and Southern perspectives in dialogue, showing that the compromise, while imperfect, was the only workable resolution.

## The Decennial Census and the Enumeration Clause

The constitutional machinery of apportionment rests on the decennial census, mandated by Article I, Section 2: "The actual Enumeration shall be made within three Years after the first Meeting of the Congress of the United States, and within every subsequent Term of ten Years, in such Manner as they shall by Law direct." The census clause reflects the Framers' recognition that population is dynamic — states grow at different rates, migration reshapes the demographic landscape, and a static apportionment would quickly become unjust.

Hamilton, in Federalist No. 36, noted the practical challenge of conducting a national census in an era before professional statistical agencies, but expressed confidence that the states' self-interest in accurate counts — the same countervailing-incentive logic that Madison elaborated in No. 54 — would ensure reasonable accuracy. The first census, conducted in 1790 under the direction of Secretary of State Thomas Jefferson, counted approximately 3.9 million inhabitants and provided the baseline for the apportionment of the Third Congress.

The decennial enumeration also ties apportionment to a fixed temporal rhythm. Unlike parliamentary systems where electoral boundaries can be redrawn at the government's convenience — a power susceptible to partisan manipulation — the American system ties reapportionment to an objective, regularly occurring event beyond the control of incumbent legislators. This temporal discipline is an underappreciated feature of the constitutional design.

## Representation and Taxation: The Dual Rule

The Constitution's decision to use the same rule for both representation and taxation was deliberate. Madison argued that this dual application neutralized the incentives for states to manipulate census figures:

> Were their share of representation alone to be governed by this rule, they would have an interest in exaggerating their inhabitants. Were the rule to decide their share of taxation alone, a contrary temptation would prevail. By extending the rule to both objects, the States will have opposite interests, which will control and balance each other, and produce the requisite impartiality. [054-014]

This incentive design — using countervailing interests to produce accurate data — exemplified the Federalist approach to institutional architecture.

## Property and Representation

Madison also engaged a deeper theoretical question: whether representation should reflect property as well as persons. In several states, one branch of the legislature was intentionally designed as the guardian of property, elected by those with the greatest economic stake. The federal Constitution departed from this model, committing the rights of property into the same hands as personal rights.

Madison noted that within a state, wealthy citizens exercised influence over representation through the "imperceptible channel" of social respect and consequence — an effect that did not operate across state boundaries. The larger and richer states would not influence the choice of representatives in smaller states. Therefore, if wealth was to have any weight in the federal legislature, it had to be secured through the apportionment formula itself rather than through informal social mechanisms.

## Apportionment and the Connecticut Compromise

The apportionment of the House by population must be understood in conjunction with the Senate's equal representation of states — the "Connecticut Compromise" that resolved the most intractable dispute of the Constitutional Convention. The House gave weight to population — rewarding the large states — while the Senate gave equal weight to each state — protecting the small. This bicameral balance meant that the apportionment formula, while critical, was not the sole determinant of a state's influence in Congress. A small state that lost House seats through population decline retained its two senators and thus a voice in the legislative process disproportionate to its population. The Federalist defense of apportionment was therefore inseparable from the broader architecture of the compound republic.
