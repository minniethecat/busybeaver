# Inferior Courts

**Inferior courts** are the federal tribunals established by Congress beneath the Supreme Court of the United States. Article III of the Constitution vests the judicial power of the United States in "one Supreme Court, and in such inferior courts as the Congress may from time to time ordain and establish." The propriety of creating these subordinate federal tribunals was one of the central themes of Hamilton's defense of the judiciary in the Federalist Papers, particularly in Federalist No. 81.

## In the Federalist Papers

Hamilton addressed the power to constitute inferior courts directly and emphatically. The necessity of such courts was, in his view, self-evident given the limited capacity of a single Supreme Court to handle all cases of federal cognizance:

> The power of constituting inferior courts is evidently calculated to obviate the necessity of having recourse to the Supreme Court in every case of federal cognizance. It is intended to enable the national government to institute or authorize, in each State or district of the United States, a tribunal competent to the determination of matters of national jurisdiction within its limits. [081-006]

## Rationale for Inferior Tribunals

Hamilton offered several interconnected justifications for the establishment of inferior federal courts.

First, a single Supreme Court sitting in one location could not possibly handle the judicial business of an entire nation. The volume of cases and the geographical distances involved made subordinate tribunals a practical necessity.

Second, the inferior courts would serve as courts of original jurisdiction for most federal cases, with the Supreme Court exercising appellate review. This division of labor was essential to the efficient administration of justice:

> In all other cases of federal cognizance, the original jurisdiction would appertain to the inferior tribunals; and the Supreme Court would have nothing more than an appellate jurisdiction. [081-020]

Third, inferior courts would bring federal justice closer to the people, reducing the burden on litigants who would otherwise need to travel to the seat of the national government.

## Organization and Structure

Hamilton envisioned dividing the United States into four, five, or six districts, with a federal court in each. The judges of these courts, aided by state judges, could hold circuits for the trial of causes in various parts of their districts:

> Justice through them may be administered with ease and despatch; and appeals may be safely circumscribed within a narrow compass. This plan appears to me at present the most eligible of any that could be adopted. [081-008]

This vision of a distributed federal judiciary — district courts supplemented by circuit-riding — anticipated the structure that Congress would establish in the Judiciary Act of 1789. The first Congress, meeting in its inaugural session, enacted the bill that created thirteen district courts (one for each state), three circuit courts, and set the Supreme Court at six justices. The circuit courts, staffed by a district judge and two Supreme Court justices riding circuit, implemented Hamilton's conception of a judiciary that was both national in authority and accessible in geography.

The circuit-riding system, while burdensome for the justices who spent months each year traveling by horseback and carriage over primitive roads, served an important integrative function. It brought the Supreme Court justices into direct contact with local communities, state legal cultures, and the practical administration of justice at the trial level. This familiarity with local conditions informed the justices' appellate decision-making and helped maintain the legitimacy of the national judiciary in an era of strong state attachments.

## State Courts as Inferior Tribunals

Hamilton also considered whether state courts could serve as the inferior tribunals under the federal system — avoiding the need for an entirely separate set of federal trial courts. While he acknowledged that state courts could exercise concurrent jurisdiction in many federal cases, he identified substantial reasons against relying exclusively on state tribunals:

> The most discerning cannot foresee how far the prevalency of a local spirit may be found to disqualify the local tribunals for the jurisdiction of national causes. [081-007]

State judges holding office "during pleasure, or from year to year" would lack the independence necessary for the inflexible execution of national laws. The tenure and salary protections of Article III — life tenure during good behavior and undiminished compensation — would not apply to state judges. This structural concern reinforced the case for a distinct system of federal inferior courts.

## The "Inferior" Designation

Hamilton took care to dispel a misinterpretation: that the power to constitute "inferior courts" was intended to abolish existing county courts in the states. He clarified that the constitutional language referred to "tribunals inferior to the Supreme Court" — a hierarchical relationship within the federal judiciary, not an assault on state judicial institutions:

> It is ridiculous to imagine that county courts were in contemplation. [081-004n]

## Inferior Courts and the Supremacy of Federal Law

The inferior courts served a constitutional function beyond mere efficiency: they were essential instruments for the enforcement of federal supremacy. Without a nationwide network of federal trial courts, the vindication of federal rights would depend entirely on state courts — institutions whose judges often lacked the structural independence that Article III provided and whose loyalties could be divided between national law and local sentiment. By placing federal trial courts throughout the states, Congress ensured that litigants with federal claims had access to a forum whose independence from state political pressures was constitutionally guaranteed. This was especially important in cases involving federal revenue laws, admiralty jurisdiction, and disputes between citizens of different states — all categories where local prejudice was a foreseeable threat to impartial justice.
