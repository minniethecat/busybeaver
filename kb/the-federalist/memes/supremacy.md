# Supremacy

**Supremacy** refers to the constitutional principle, embodied in Article VI, Clause 2 of the United States Constitution, that the Constitution, federal laws made pursuant to it, and treaties are "the supreme Law of the Land," binding upon judges in every state notwithstanding anything to the contrary in the laws or constitutions of any state. This principle is foundational to American federalism, establishing the hierarchical relationship between federal and state legal authority.

## In the Federalist Papers

The concept of supremacy is addressed at multiple points throughout the Federalist Papers, reflecting its centrality to the constitutional architecture the authors sought to defend.

### Hamilton on the Logic of Supremacy

In Federalist No. 33, Hamilton addressed the Supremacy Clause by arguing that it was not a grant of additional power but a statement of logical necessity. Federal laws made pursuant to the Constitution must, by definition, prevail over conflicting state laws:

> A LAW, by the very meaning of the term, includes supremacy. It is a rule which those to whom it is prescribed are bound to observe. This results from every political association. If individuals enter into a state of society, the laws of that society must be the supreme regulator of their conduct. If a number of political societies enter into a larger political society, the laws which the latter may enact, pursuant to the powers intrusted to it by its constitution, must necessarily be supreme over those societies, and the individuals of whom they are composed. It would otherwise be a mere treaty, dependent on the good faith of the parties, and not a government, which is only another word for POLITICAL POWER AND SUPREMACY. [033-009]

Hamilton carefully qualified this principle: acts of the federal government that are not pursuant to its constitutional powers are mere usurpations, not supreme law. He noted that the clause "EXPRESSLY confines this supremacy to laws made PURSUANT TO THE CONSTITUTION" — a limitation that would have been implied even if not expressed [033-009]. This distinction between constitutional exercises of power and unconstitutional usurpations was central to the Federalist defense of the proposed Constitution against charges that it would create an unlimited national government.

### Madison on the National Government and Supremacy

In Federalist No. 39, Madison analyzed whether the proposed government was "national" or "federal" in character. Examining the extent of federal powers, he observed that while a fully national government would possess "indefinite supremacy over all persons and things," the proposed Constitution created a government of limited, enumerated jurisdiction:

> Among communities united for particular purposes, it is vested partly in the general and partly in the municipal legislatures. In the former case, all local authorities are subordinate to the supreme; and may be controlled, directed, or abolished by it at pleasure. In the latter, the local or municipal authorities form distinct and independent portions of the supremacy, no more subject, within their respective spheres, to the general authority, than the general authority is subject to them, within its own sphere. [039-016]

Madison's framing of divided supremacy — where federal and state governments each possess sovereign authority within their respective constitutional spheres — became a cornerstone of American federalism doctrine. This concept of "dual sovereignty" was later articulated by the Supreme Court as a structural principle that preserves state governmental authority even as it subordinates state law to valid federal enactments.

### Practical Necessity at the Seat of Government

In Federalist No. 43, Madison argued that the federal government's "general supremacy" was essential to its functioning, particularly at the seat of government, where without complete authority "not only the public authority might be insulted and its proceedings interrupted with impunity" but the national councils might become dependent on the host state for protection [043-007]. This argument reflected the practical experience of the Confederation Congress, which had been forced to flee Philadelphia in 1783 after Pennsylvania authorities failed to protect it from a mutinous militia.

## The Supremacy Clause and State Constitutions

In Federalist No. 44, Madison addressed concerns about the relationship between the Supremacy Clause and state constitutions:

> To be fully sensible of this, we need only suppose for a moment that the supremacy of the State constitutions had been left complete by a saving clause in their favor. [044-021]

He argued that an express saving of state constitutional supremacy would have brought every power contained in the proposed Constitution into question, particularly in states whose constitutions did not fully recognize the existing powers of the Confederation [044-023]. The Supremacy Clause thus operated not only as a rule of decision for courts but as a structural guarantee that the federal government could exercise its delegated powers without being undermined by hostile state constitutional provisions.

## The Anti-Federalist Critique

The Supremacy Clause was among the most fiercely contested provisions of the proposed Constitution. Anti-Federalist writers, including [[Brutus]], argued that the combination of the Supremacy Clause, the Necessary and Proper Clause, and the federal taxing power would inevitably lead to the consolidation of all governmental authority at the national level and the effective abolition of the states. The Federalist defense of the clause — particularly Hamilton's argument in No. 33 that supremacy was a logical incident of government rather than a grant of additional power — was designed to meet this critique by demonstrating that the clause merely stated what was already implicit in the constitutional design.

## Legal Implications

The Supremacy Clause has been the foundation of the doctrine of federal preemption: when federal and state law conflict, the federal law prevails. The Supreme Court has developed a nuanced preemption jurisprudence distinguishing between "express preemption" (where Congress explicitly displaces state law), "field preemption" (where federal regulation is so pervasive as to occupy the entire field), and "conflict preemption" (where compliance with both state and federal law is impossible or where state law obstructs federal purposes).

The Clause also provides the constitutional basis for judicial review of state laws. When a state statute or constitutional provision conflicts with federal law, the Supremacy Clause — together with Article III's grant of federal question jurisdiction — empowers federal courts to invalidate the conflicting state provision. This was established definitively in *Martin v. Hunter's Lessee* (1816) and reinforced in subsequent decisions that confirmed the Supreme Court's authority to review state court decisions interpreting federal law.
