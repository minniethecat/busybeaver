# Constitutional Convention

The **[[Constitutional Convention]]** of 1787 drafted the United States Constitution — a new frame of government defended by the [[the-federalist-papers|Federalist Papers]] and ratified by the states in 1788. Convened in Philadelphia with [[alexander-hamilton|Alexander Hamilton]], [[james-madison|James Madison]], and George Washington among its delegates, the Convention produced a system of [[federalism]] and [[separation-of-powers]] that transformed the American republic.

## Overview

The Constitutional Convention convened in Philadelphia on May 25, 1787, with delegates from twelve states (Rhode Island refused to participate) to revise the Articles of Confederation. By September 17, it had produced an entirely new frame of government — the United States Constitution — which was submitted to the states for ratification. The Federalist Papers were written to defend the Convention's work against critics who charged that it had exceeded its mandate and produced a plan that would destroy the states and individual liberty.

## In The Federalist Papers

The Convention's work is the subject of every Federalist essay, but Publius addresses the Convention's legitimacy and process most directly in Federalist Nos. 37–40. Madison, in Federalist No. 37, acknowledges the "difficulties" the Convention faced: combining "the requisite stability and energy in government with the inviolable attention due to liberty and to the republican form" (037-011). He candidly admits that the Constitution is not perfect:

> The history of almost all the great councils and consultations held among mankind for reconciling their discordant opinions, assuaging their mutual jealousies, and adjusting their respective interests, is a history of factions, contentions, and disappointments; and may be classed among the most dark and degrading pictures which display the infirmities and depravities of the human character.
>
> (037-001)

In Federalist No. 40, Madison defends the Convention's authority to propose a new Constitution rather than merely amend the Articles. The delegates were instructed to render the federal government "adequate to the exigencies of the Union" — a mandate that, Madison argues, necessarily implied the power to propose fundamental changes (040-003).

## Key Decisions

The Convention's central compromises shaped the Constitution:

- **The Great Compromise**: Equal state representation in the Senate, proportional in the House
- **The Three-Fifths Compromise**: Slaves counted as three-fifths of a person for representation and taxation
- **The Commerce Compromise**: Congress could regulate interstate and foreign commerce but not the slave trade until 1808
- **The Electoral College**: A compromise between direct popular election and congressional selection of the president

## Legacy

The Constitutional Convention of 1787 produced the oldest written national constitution still in effect. Its work — and The Federalist's defense of it — established the American model of constitutional government: a written constitution, ratified by the people, enforced by an independent judiciary, and amendable only through a supermajority process. Every subsequent national constitution has been influenced, directly or indirectly, by the Philadelphia Convention's example.
