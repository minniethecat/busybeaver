# Counterfeiting

**Counterfeiting** is the crime of forging or imitating currency, securities, or other financial instruments with intent to defraud. Article I, Section 8, Clause 6 of the Constitution grants Congress the power "to provide for the Punishment of counterfeiting the Securities and current Coin of the United States," establishing one of the few federal criminal offenses specifically enumerated in the original Constitution.

## In the Federalist Papers

Madison addressed the counterfeiting power in Federalist No. 42, alongside other Article I, Section 8 powers that together formed the legal infrastructure of the national economy. The power to punish counterfeiting was grouped with the power to coin money and regulate its value — the two powers being logically connected: the authority to create a national currency would be incomplete without the authority to protect that currency from forgery [042-012].

Madison extended the counterfeiting power beyond coinage to include public securities:

> The punishment of counterfeiting the public securities, as well as the current coin, is submitted of course to that authority which is to secure the value of both. [042-017]

## The Scope of Federal Criminal Authority

The counterfeiting clause is notable as one of the few grants of direct federal criminal jurisdiction in the original Constitution. Along with the power to define and punish "Piracies and Felonies committed on the high Seas" and "Offences against the Law of Nations" (Article I, Section 8, Clause 10), and the treason clause (Article III, Section 3), the counterfeiting power represented the Framers' recognition that certain crimes were inherently national in character and could not be adequately addressed by the states acting individually.

The decision to enumerate specific federal crimes, rather than granting Congress a general criminal jurisdiction, reflected the Framers' commitment to limited government. The federal government could punish only those offenses specifically listed in the constitutional text; all other criminal matters remained with the states.

## The Link to Coinage

Madison's analysis of the counterfeiting power in Federalist No. 42 illustrates the Framers' approach to constitutional design: powers were granted in functionally complete bundles. The authority to coin money — also enumerated in Article I, Section 8 — would be hollow if Congress could not protect the currency it created. A counterfeiter operating across state lines, beyond the reach of any single state's criminal jurisdiction, could undermine the national currency with impunity.

The Framers drew on English precedent here. The crime of counterfeiting the king's coin had been treated as treason under English law — an offense against the sovereign itself. The American Constitution, by treating counterfeiting as a distinct federal crime rather than incorporating it into the narrow definition of treason, both preserved the gravity of the offense and avoided the draconian penalties associated with treason convictions.

## Securities and Coin: The Two Categories

Madison, in Federalist No. 42, carefully distinguished between two categories of counterfeiting: counterfeiting of the "current coin" (the physical currency — gold, silver, and copper coins) and counterfeiting of the "public securities" (government bonds, notes, and other financial instruments issued by the United States). The inclusion of securities alongside coin reflected the Framers' awareness that the national government would rely on public credit — the issuance of debt instruments — as much as on coined money.

## Legacy and Significance

The counterfeiting power has been exercised by Congress from the earliest federal criminal statutes to the present day. The Crimes Act of 1790 (the first federal criminal code) included provisions punishing counterfeiting, and the modern federal criminal code (Title 18 of the United States Code) contains extensive counterfeiting provisions covering currency, coins, obligations, and securities of the United States.

The creation of the United States Secret Service in 1865 was originally motivated by the need to combat counterfeiting — its mission to protect the President and other dignitaries was added later. At the time of the Secret Service's creation, it was estimated that as much as one-third of the currency in circulation was counterfeit.

The Supreme Court, in *United States v. Marigold* (1850), confirmed that the federal counterfeiting power was plenary and exclusive, holding that Congress possessed the full authority to punish counterfeiting without limitation by state law.
