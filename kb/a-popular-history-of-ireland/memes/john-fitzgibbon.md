## Overview

John Fitzgibbon, Earl of Clare (1748–1802), was the dominant figure in Irish legal and political life during the last two decades of the Irish Parliament. As Attorney-General and later Lord Chancellor, he led the "Castle party" against the reform measures of [[Henry Grattan]] and the Patriot opposition, insisting that Ireland's connection with England must be preserved at any cost. His personality and politics made him one of the most formidable — and most resented — figures of his era. (097-127) (097-135)

Fitzgibbon's career spanned the full arc of Grattan's Parliament: from the legislative independence of 1782, through the Regency crisis, to the Act of Union of 1800, which he helped to carry against fierce opposition. (097-170)

## Rise to Power

Fitzgibbon was born to a prosperous Protestant family in 1748 and educated at Trinity College, Dublin. Called to the Irish bar, his ability and ambition quickly marked him for advancement. In 1784 he was appointed Attorney-General of Ireland, a position from which he shaped the government's legislative strategy and built a reputation as an uncompromising defender of the established order. (097-040) (097-082)

> "Fitzgibbon, Yelverton, Scott, denounced the principle of the Dungannon resolutions." (096-240)

## The Castle Party Leader

As Attorney-General and later as Lord Chancellor, Fitzgibbon became the acknowledged leader of the Castle party — the faction within the Irish Parliament that took its direction from Dublin Castle and the British ministry. He opposed every measure of parliamentary reform, Catholic relief, and commercial liberalisation that Grattan and the Patriots advanced.

His methods were direct and often personal. A dispute with one prominent Patriot "led to a duel between them," illustrating the heated atmosphere of the period. Fitzgibbon was unapologetic about his position: he believed that Ireland's security depended entirely on the English connection and that any weakening of that link would invite chaos. (097-040)

> "The spirit of Fitzgibbon — the leader of the Castle party — was supreme." (097-127)

## The Regency Crisis and the Speakership

During the Regency crisis of 1789, Fitzgibbon played a central role. When [[Henry Grattan]] and the Whig opposition sought to make the Prince of Wales Regent with full powers, Fitzgibbon led the government's resistance. The crisis brought into sharp focus the rivalry between "the Chancellor and the Speaker — Fitzgibbon and Foster, Irishmen both, but representing opposite principles." Fitzgibbon's victory in this contest secured the Castle's authority over Parliament. (097-170) (097-135)

## The Act of Union

Fitzgibbon was one of the principal architects of the Act of Union of 1800. Having spent his career warning that an independent Irish Parliament would eventually fall under Catholic control, he saw Union as the only permanent safeguard of the Protestant interest. His formidable parliamentary skills were deployed to carry the measure through a hostile Irish House of Commons. Created Earl of Clare in 1795, he did not long survive his greatest political achievement, dying in 1802. (097-170)

## Legacy

Fitzgibbon left a contested legacy. To supporters of the Union, he was a far-sighted statesman who preserved the British connection. To his critics, he was the man who helped extinguish Irish legislative independence. [[Henry Grattan]]'s biographers portrayed him as the antithesis of Grattan's liberal patriotism — the personification of "the Castle" against "the Nation." The Union he helped to create would dominate Irish politics for the next century. (095-071)
