## Overview

Armagh (Ard Mhacha, "Macha's Height") is the ecclesiastical capital of Ireland and the seat of both the Roman Catholic and Church of Ireland Primates of All Ireland. Located in present-day County Armagh in the province of [[ulster|Ulster]], the city was founded as the principal see of the Irish Church by [[saint-patrick|Saint Patrick]] in the fifth century and has remained the symbolic center of Irish Christianity ever since.

The city's religious pre-eminence survived centuries of political upheaval, invasion, and religious reformation. Its Archbishop is traditionally acknowledged as the successor of [[saint-patrick|Saint Patrick]] and the Primate of All Ireland.

## History

### Foundation by Saint Patrick

According to tradition, [[saint-patrick|Saint Patrick]] himself established the See of Armagh around 444 AD. He chose the site on the hill of Ard Mhacha, near the ancient royal fort of Emain Macha (Navan Fort), the legendary seat of the Kings of [[ulster|Ulster]]. Patrick's choice of this location—adjacent to an ancient royal center—was characteristic of his strategy of grafting Christian institutions onto existing centers of political and cultural power.

Armagh rapidly became the most important ecclesiastical center in Ireland. The Book of Armagh, compiled in the ninth century, contains some of the earliest lives of [[saint-patrick|Saint Patrick]] and asserts the primacy of the see over the Irish Church.

### The Medieval Church

Throughout the medieval period, the successors of [[saint-patrick|Saint Patrick]] at Armagh—with all his relics—exercised authority over the Irish Church. The imprisonment of an Archbishop of Armagh was considered a crisis for the entire Irish ecclesiastical establishment. The coarbs (successors) of Patrick wielded both spiritual and temporal power, often mediating between warring kings and maintaining the schools and monasteries associated with the see. (008-086)

### Reformation and Aftermath

The [[reformation|Reformation]] created a division in the Irish Church hierarchy, with rival Catholic and Protestant Archbishops of Armagh from the sixteenth century onward. Both traditions claimed the succession of [[saint-patrick|Saint Patrick]] and the primatial dignity associated with his see. Despite this division, Armagh retained its symbolic status as the spiritual capital of Ireland for both communities.

## Description

Armagh is built on and around a hill overlooking the surrounding countryside. Its two cathedrals—both dedicated to [[saint-patrick|Saint Patrick]]—dominate the city:

- **[[saint-patrick|St. Patrick]]'s Cathedral (Church of Ireland)** stands on the traditional site of Patrick's original stone church. It was extensively rebuilt in the nineteenth century but retains traces of its medieval predecessor.
- **[[saint-patrick|St. Patrick]]'s Cathedral (Roman Catholic)** was built in the nineteenth century in a Gothic Revival style and serves as the seat of the Catholic Archbishop.

The city also contains the Armagh Observatory, founded in 1790, and the Armagh Public Library, founded by Archbishop Richard Robinson in 1771, which holds important collections of early Irish manuscripts.

## Legacy

Armagh remains the ecclesiastical capital of Ireland, a status confirmed by its two Primates and its dual cathedrals. The city embodies the continuity of the Irish Christian tradition from the age of [[saint-patrick|Saint Patrick]] to the present day—a tradition that survived persecution, reformation, and political revolution. Its archbishops, both Catholic and Protestant, continue to be styled as successors of the [[saint-patrick|Apostle of Ireland]].
