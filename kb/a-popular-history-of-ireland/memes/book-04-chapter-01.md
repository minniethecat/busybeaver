CHAPTER I.

[023-001]DERMID McMURROGH'S NEGOTIATIONS AND SUCCESS—THE FIRST EXPEDITION OF THE

[023-002]NORMANS INTO IRELAND.

[023-003]The result of Dermid McMurrogh's interview with Henry II., in

[023-004]Aquitaine, was a royal letter, addressed to all his subjects,

[023-005]authorizing such of them as would, to enlist in the service of the

[023-006]Irish Prince. Armed alone with this, the expelled adulterer, chafing

[023-007]for restoration and revenge, retraced his course to England. He was at

[023-008]this time some years beyond three score, but the snows of age had no

[023-009]effect in cooling his impetuous blood; his stature is described as

[023-010]almost gigantic; his voice loud and harsh; his features stern and

[023-011]terrible. His cruel and criminal character we already know. Yet it is

[023-012]but just here to recall that much of the horror and odium which has

[023-013]accumulated on his memory is posthumous and retrospective. Some of his

[023-014]cotemporaries were no better in their private lives than he was; but

[023-015]then they had no part in bringing in the Normans. Talents both for

[023-016]peace and war he certainly had, and there was still a feeling of

[023-017]attachment, or at least of regret, cherished towards him among the

[023-018]people of his patrimony.

[023-019]Dermid proceeded at once to seek the help he so sorely needed, upon the

[023-020]marches of Chester, in the city of Bristol, and at the court of the

[023-021]Prince of North Wales. At Bristol he caused King Henry's letter to be

[023-022]publicly read, and each reading was accompanied by ample promises of

[023-023]land and recompense to those disposed to join in the expedition—but all

[023-024]in vain. From Bristol he proceeded to make the usual pilgrimage to the

[023-025]shrine of St. David, the Apostle of Wales, and then he visited the

[023-026]Court of Griffith ap Rhys, Prince of North Wales, whose family ties

[023-027]formed a true Welsh triad among the Normans, the Irish, and the Welsh.

[023-028]He was the nephew of the celebrated Nest or Nesta, the Helen of the

[023-029]Welsh, whose blood flowed in the veins of almost all the first Norman

[023-030]adventurers in Ireland, and whose story is too intimately interwoven

[023-031]with the origin of many of the highest names of the Norman-Irish to be

[023-032]left untold.

[023-033]She was, in her day, the loveliest woman of Cambria, and perhaps of

[023-034]Britain, but the fabled mantle of Tregau, which, according to her own

[023-035]mythology, will fit none but the chaste, had not rested on the white

[023-036]shoulders of Nesta, the daughter of Rhys ap Tudor. Her girlish beauty

[023-037]had attracted the notice of Henry I., to whom she bore Robert Fitz-Roy

[023-038]and Henry Fitz-Henry, the former the famous Earl of Gloucester, and the

[023-039]latter the father of two of Strongbow's most noted companions.

[023-040]Afterwards, by consent of her royal paramour, she married Gerald,

[023-041]constable of Pembroke, by whom she had Maurice Fitzgerald, the common

[023-042]ancestor of the Kildare and Desmond Geraldines. While living with

[023-043]Gerald at Pembroke, Owen, son of Cadogan, Prince of Powis, hearing of

[023-044]her marvellous beauty at a banquet given by his father at the Castle of

[023-045]Aberteivi, came by night to Pembroke, surprised the Castle, and carried

[023-046]off Nesta and her children into Powis. Gerald, however, had escaped,

[023-047]and by the aid of his father-in-law, Rhys, recovered his wife and

[023-048]rebuilt his castle (A.D. 1105). The lady survived this husband, and

[023-049]married a second time, Stephen, constable of Cardigan, by whom she had

[023-050]Robert Fitzstephen, and probably other children. One of her daughters,

[023-051]Angharad, married David de Barri, the father of Giraldus and Robert de

[023-052]Barri; another, named after herself, married Bernard of Newmarch, and

[023-053]became the father of the Fitz-Bernard, who accompanied Henry II. In the

[023-054]second and third generations this fruitful Cambrian vine, grafted on

[023-055]the Norman stock, had branched out into the great families of the

[023-056]Carews, Gerards, Fitzwilliams, and Fitzroys, of England and Wales, and

[023-057]the Geraldines, Graces, Fitz-Henries, and Fitz-Maurices, of Ireland.

[023-058]These names will show how entirely the expeditions of 1169 and 1170

[023-059]were joint-stock undertakings with most of the adventurers; Cambria,

[023-060]not England, sent them forth; it was a family compact; they were

[023-061]brothers in blood as well as in arms, those comely and unscrupulous

[023-062]sons, nephews, and grand-sons of Nesta!

[023-063]When the Leinster King reached the residence of Griffith ap Rhys, near

[023-064]St. David's, he found that for some personal or political cause he held

[023-065]in prison his near kinsman, Robert, son of Stephen, who had the

[023-066]reputation of being a brave and capable knight. Dermid obtained the

[023-067]release of Robert, on condition of his embarking in the Irish

[023-068]enterprise, and he found in him an active recruiting agent, alike among

[023-069]Welsh, Flemings, and Normans. Through him Maurice Fitzgerald, the de

[023-070]Barris, and Fitz-Henrys, and their dependents, were soon enlisted in

[023-071]the adventure. The son of Griffith ap Rhys, who may be mentioned along

[023-072]with these knights, his kinsmen, and whom the Irish annalists consider

[023-073]the most important person of the first expedition—their pillar of

[023-074]battle—also resolved to accompany them, with such forces as he could

[023-075]enlist.

[023-077]But a still more important ally waited to treat with Dermid, on his

[023-078]return to Bristol. This was Richard de Clare, called variously from his

[023-079]castles or his county, Earl of Strigul and Chepstow, or Earl of

[023-080]Pembroke. From the strength of his arms he was nicknamed Strongbow, and

[023-081]in our Annals he is usually called Earl Richard, by which title we

[023-082]prefer hereafter to distinguish him. His father, Gilbert de Clare, was

[023-083]descended from Richard of Normandy, and stood no farther removed in

[023-084]degree from that Duke than the reigning Prince. For nearly forty years

[023-085]under Henry I. and during the stormy reign of King Stephen, he had been

[023-086]Governor of Pembroke, and like all the great Barons played his game

[023-087]chiefly to his own advantage. His castle at Chepstow was one of the

[023-088]strongest in the west, and the power he bequeathed to his able and

[023-089]ambitious son excited the apprehensions of the astute and suspicious

[023-090]Henry II. Fourteen years of this King's reign had passed away, and Earl

[023-091]Richard had received no great employments, no new grants of land, no

[023-092]personal favours from his Sovereign. He was now a widower, past middle

[023-093]age, condemned to a life of inaction such as no true Norman could long

[023-094]endure. Arrived at Bristol, he read the letter of Henry, and heard from

[023-095]Dermid the story of his expulsion and the grounds on which he vested

[023-096]his hopes of restoration. A consultation ensued, at which it is

[023-097]probable the sons of Nesta assisted, as it was there agreed that the

[023-098]town of Wexford, with two cantreds of land adjoining it, should be

[023-099]given to them. The pay of the archers and men-at-arms, and the duration

[023-100]of their service, were also determined. Large grants of land were

[023-101]guaranteed to all adventurers of knightly rank, and Earl Richard was to

[023-102]marry the King's daughter and succeed him in the sovereignty of

[023-103]Leinster.

[023-104]Having by such lavish promises enlisted this powerful Earl and those

[023-105]adventurous knights, Dermid resolved to pass over in person with such

[023-106]followers as were already equipped, in order to rally the remnant of

[023-107]his adherents. The Irish Annals enter this return under the year 1167,

[023-108]within twelvemonths or thereabouts from the time of his banishment; by

[023-109]their account he came back, accompanied by a fleet of strangers whom

[023-110]they called Flemings, and who were probably hired soldiers of that

[023-111]race, then easily to be met with in Wales. The Welsh Prince already

[023-112]mentioned seems to have accompanied him personally, as he fell by his

[023-113]side in a skirmish the following year. Whatever this force may have

[023-114]amounted to, they landed at Glascarrig point, and wintered—probably

[023-115]spent the Christmas—at Ferns. The more generally received account of

[023-116]Dermid's landing alone, and disguised, and secretly preparing his

[023-117]plans, under shelter of the Austin Friary at Ferns, must be rejected,

[023-118]if we are still to follow those trite but trustworthy guides, whom we

[023-119]have so many reasons to confide in. The details differ in many very

[023-120]important particulars from those usually received, as we shall

[023-121]endeavour to make clear in a few words.

[023-122]Not only do they bring Dermid over with a fleet of Flemings, of whom

[023-123]the natives made "small account," but dating that event before the

[023-124]expiration of the year 1167, at least sixteen months must have elapsed

[023-125]between the return of the outlaw and the arrival of the Normans. By

[023-126]allowing two years instead of one for the duration of his banishment,

[023-127]the apparent difficulty as to time would be obviated, for his return

[023-128]and Fitzstephen's arrival would follow upon each other in the spring

[023-129]and winter of the same year. The difficulty, however, is more apparent

[023-130]than real. A year sufficed for the journey to Aquitaine and the Welsh

[023-131]negotiations. Another year seems to have been devoted with equal art

[023-132]and success to resuscitating a native Leinster party favourable to his

[023-133]restoration. For it is evident from our Annals that when Dermid showed

[023-134]himself to the people after his return, it was simply to claim his

[023-135]patrimony—Hy-Kinsellagh—and not to dispute the Kingdom of Leinster with

[023-136]the actual ruler, _Murrogh na Gael_. By this pretended moderation and

[023-137]humility, he disarmed hostility and lulled suspicion asleep. Roderick

[023-138]and O'Ruarc did indeed muster a host against him, and some of their

[023-139]cavalry and Kernes skirmished with the troops in his service at

[023-140]Kellistown, in Carlow, when six were killed on one side and twenty-five

[023-141]on the other, including the Welsh Prince already mentioned; afterwards

[023-142]Dermid emerged from his fastnesses, and entering the camp of O'Conor,

[023-143]gave him seven hostages for the ten cantreds of his patrimony; and to

[023-144]O'Ruarc he gave "one hundred ounces of gold for his _eineach_"—that is,

[023-145]as damages for his criminal conversation with Devorgoil. During the

[023-146]remainder of the year 1168, Dermid was left to enjoy unmolested the

[023-147]moderate territory which he claimed, while King Roderick was engaged in

[023-148]enforcing his claims on the North and South, founding lectorships at

[023-149]Armagh, and partitioning Meath between his inseparable colleague,

[023-150]O'Ruarc, and himself. He celebrated, in the midst of an immense

[023-151]multitude, the ancient national games at Tailtin, he held an assembly

[023-152]at Tara, and distributed magnificent gifts to his suffragans. Roderick

[023-153]might have spent the festival of Christmas, 1168, or of Easter, 1169,

[023-154]in the full assurance that his power was firmly established, and that a

[023-155]long succession of peaceful days were about to dawn upon Erin. But he

[023-156]was destined to be soon and sadly undeceived.

[023-157]In the month of May, a little fleet of Welsh vessels, filled with armed

[023-158]men, approached the Irish shore, and Robert Fitzstephen ran into a

[023-159]creek of the bay of Bannow, called by the adventurers, from the names

[023-160]of two of their ships, Bag-and-Bun. Fitzstephen had with him thirty

[023-161]knights, sixty esquires, and three hundred footmen. The next day he was

[023-162]joined by Maurice de Prendergast, a Welsh gentleman, with ten knights

[023-163]and sixty archers. After landing they reconnoitred cautiously, but saw

[023-164]neither ally nor enemy—the immediate coast seemed entirely deserted.

[023-165]Their messenger despatched to Dermid, then probably at Ferns, in the

[023-166]northern extremity of the county, must have been absent several anxious

[023-167]days, when, much to their relief, he returned with Donald, the son of

[023-168]Dermid, at the head of 500 horsemen. Uniting their troops, Donald and

[023-169]Fitzstephen set out for Wexford, about a day's march distant, and the

[023-170]principal town in that angle of the island which points towards Wales.

[023-171]The tradition of the neighbourhood says they were assailed upon the way

[023-172]by a party of the native population, who were defeated and dispersed.

[023-173]Within ten days or a fortnight of their landing, they were drawn up

[023-174]within sight of the walls of Wexford, where they were joined by Dermid,

[023-175]who obviously did not come unattended to such a meeting. What

[023-176]additional force he may have brought up is nowhere indicated; that he

[023-177]was not without followers or mercenaries, we know from the mention of

[023-178]the Flemings in his service, and the action of Kellistown in the

[023-179]previous year. The force that had marched from Bannow consisted, as we

[023-180]have seen, of 500 Irish horse under his son Donald, surnamed

[023-181]_Kavanagh_; 30 knights, 60 esquires, and 300 men-at-arms under

[023-182]Fitzstephen; 10 knights and 60 archers under Prendergast; in all,

[023-183]nobles or servitors, not exceeding 1,000 men. The town, a place of

[023-184]considerable strength, could muster 2,000 men capable of bearing arms,

[023-185]nor is it discreditable to its Dano-Irish artizans and seamen that they

[023-186]could boast no captain equal to Fitzstephen or Donald Kavanagh. What a

[023-187]town multitude could do they did. They burned down an exposed suburb,

[023-188]closed their gates, and manned their walls. The first assault was

[023-189]repulsed with some loss on the part of the assailants, and the night

[023-190]past in expectation of a similar conflict on the morrow. In the early

[023-191]morning the townsmen could discern that the Holy Sacrifice of the Mass

[023-192]was being offered in the camp of their besiegers as a preparative for

[023-193]the dangers of the day. Within the walls, however, the clergy exercised

[023-194]all their influence to spare the effusion of blood, and to bring about

[023-195]an accommodation. Two Bishops who were in the town especially advised a

[023-196]surrender on honourable terms, and their advice was taken. Four of the

[023-197]principal citizens were deputed to Dermid, and Wexford was yielded on

[023-198]condition of its rights and privileges, hitherto existing, being

[023-199]respected. The cantreds immediately adjoining the town on the north and

[023-200]east were conferred on Fitzstephen according to the treaty made at

[023-201]Bristol, and he at once commenced the erection of a fortress on the

[023-202]rock of Carrig, at the narrowest pass on the river Slaney. Strongbow's

[023-203]uncle, Herve, was endowed with two other cantreds, to the south of the

[023-204]town, now known as the baronies of Forth and Bargey, where the

[023-205]descendants of the Welsh and Flemish settlers then planted are still to

[023-206]be found in the industrious and sturdy population, known as Flemings,

[023-207]Furlongs, Waddings, Prendergasts, Barrys, and Walshes. Side by side

[023-208]with them now dwell in peace the Kavanaghs, Murphys, Conors, and

[023-209]Breens, whose ancestors so long and so fiercely disputed the intrusion

[023-210]of these strangers amongst them.

[023-211]With some increase of force derived from the defenders of Wexford,

[023-212]Dermid, at the head of 3000 men, including all the Normans, marched

[023-213]into the adjoining territory of Ossory, to chastise its chief, Donogh

[023-214]Fitzpatrick, one of his old enemies. This campaign appears to have

[023-215]consumed the greater part of the summer of the year, and ended with the

[023-216]submission of Ossory, after a brave but unskilful resistance. The

[023-217]tidings of what was done at Wexford and in Ossory had, however, roused

[023-218]the apprehension of the monarch Roderick, who appointed a day for a

[023-219]national muster "of the Irish" at the Hill of Tara. Thither repaired

[023-220]accordingly the monarch himself, the lords of Meath, Oriel, Ulidia,

[023-221]Breffni, and the chiefs of the farther north. With this host they

[023-222]proceeded to Dublin, which they found as yet in no immediate danger of

[023-223]attack; and whether on this pretext or some other, the Ulster chiefs

[023-224]returned to their homes, leaving Roderick to pursue, with the aid of

[023-225]Meath and Breffni only, the footsteps of McMurrogh. The latter had

[023-226]fallen back upon Ferns, and had, under the skilful directions of

[023-227]Fitzstephen, strengthened the naturally difficult approaches to that

[023-228]ancient capital, by digging artificial pits, by felling trees, and

[023-229]other devices of Norman strategy. The season, too, must have been

[023-230]drawing nearly to a close, and the same amiable desire to prevent the

[023-231]shedding of Christian blood, which characterized all the clergy of this

[023-232]age, again subserved the unworthy purposes of the traitor and invader.

[023-233]Roderick, after a vain endeavour to detach Fitzstephen from Dermid and

[023-234]to induce him to quit the country, agreed to a treaty with the Leinster

[023-235]King, by which the latter acknowledged his supremacy as monarch, under

[023-236]the ancient conditions, for the fulfilment of which he surrendered to

[023-237]him his son Conor as hostage. By a secret and separate agreement Dermid

[023-238]bound himself to admit no more of the Normans into his service—an

[023-239]engagement which he kept as he did all others, whether of a public or a

[023-240]private nature. After the usual exchange of stipends and tributes,

[023-241]Roderick returned to his home in the west; and thus, with the treaty of

[023-242]Ferns, ended the comparatively unimportant but significant campaign of

[023-243]the year 1169.
