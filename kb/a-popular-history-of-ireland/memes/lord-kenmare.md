Lord Kenmare was a leading Catholic peer during the era of the [[French Revolution]], representing the conservative wing of the [[Catholic Committee]] in the 1790s. As Viscount Kenmare, he belonged to the remnant of the ancient Catholic peerage that had survived the [[Penal Laws]] through four generations of fidelity to their faith.

His secession from the Catholic Committee in 1791, alongside sixty-seven other Catholic gentry, marked a decisive split between the aristocratic and democratic elements of the Catholic movement. (098-033)

## Background and Title

The Lords Kenmare were among the few Catholic peers who retained their social standing through the penal era. McGee describes this class as "the remnant of the ancient Catholic peerage and landed gentry, who, through four generations, had preferred civil death to religious apostasy" (098-023).

It was impossible, McGee adds, "not to revere the heroic constancy of that class, and the personal virtues of many among them" (098-025). The Kenmares held extensive estates in County Kerry, around Killarney, and their influence among the Catholic body was considerable throughout the late eighteenth century.

## Catholic Leadership

Lord Kenmare was an active member of the Catholic Committee of 1790, the body formed to press for repeal of the remaining penal restrictions. His influence balanced the rising power of the Catholic merchant class, represented by [[John Keogh]] and Edward Byrne. (098-010)

This alliance proved fragile. In 1791, alarmed by the growing influence of French revolutionary ideas within the Committee, Lord Kenmare led a dramatic secession of the Catholic aristocracy.

> "A formal remonstrance against what remained, pretending to act for the Catholic body, was signed by Lord Kenmare and sixty-seven others, who withdrew." (098-033)

McGee judged the secession harshly: "As a corrective, it was inadequate; as a preventive, useless." (098-035) He concluded that it "separated the Catholic gentry from the Catholic democracy" and pushed the latter toward the liberal Protestants who formed the [[United Irishmen]] (098-036).

The withdrawal of so many hereditary Catholic names deprived the Committee of its ability to speak for all Catholics, weakening its political position at a critical moment. (098-042)

## French Invasion Scare and Loyalism

The secession of 1791 was driven by alarm about "French revolutionary principles among their associates" (098-030). Although McGee notes this alarm "seems to have been premature," it foreshadowed the crisis that engulfed Ireland after the execution of Louis XVI in 1793.

The aristocratic Catholics stood firmly on the side of the Crown, presenting their opposition to revolutionary France as proof that emancipation could be granted without danger to the state. (098-030)

Kenmare and his circle were "constitutionally, too timid and too punctilious to conduct a popular movement to a successful issue" (098-026). Yet their loyalism served an important political purpose, distinguishing constitutional Catholics from the revolutionary [[Theobald Wolfe Tone]].

This conservative strategy, based on alliance with the Crown rather than confrontation, defined the aristocratic Catholic approach through the 1790s.

## The Prince Regent's Pledge

After the passage of the [[Act of Union 1800]], Kenmare continued to serve as an intermediary between the Catholic body and the British government. He received a private pledge of support for Catholic relief from the Prince Regent at Cheltenham.

> "Lord Kenmare, who died in the autumn of 1812, could not be induced, from a motive of delicacy, to reduce his recollection of this message to writing, but he never denied that he had received it." (110-057)

The Prince's message was delivered verbally by his Private Secretary, Colonel McMahon, though it had initially been described as given "in writing" (110-045). [[Daniel O'Connell]] later cited this pledge as one of four distinct promises made by the Regent to the Irish Catholics, and held the Prince accountable for it throughout the subsequent agitation. (110-060)

Kenmare's restraint reflected the delicate position of the Catholic peerage: they worked through private influence, maintaining loyalty to the Crown even as that Crown repeatedly disappointed their hopes. This approach was ultimately superseded by O'Connell's mass mobilization through the [[Catholic Association]].

## Assessment

Lord Kenmare embodied the dilemma of the Catholic aristocracy during the age of revolution. His principled conservatism and loyalty to the Crown were genuine, but they proved insufficient to deliver emancipation.

His secession from the Committee weakened the moderate centre at a moment when extremism was rising on both sides. Nevertheless, his career demonstrated that Catholic peers could be trusted intermediaries, a bridge that proved important in the years before [[Catholic Emancipation]] was achieved in 1829.
