CHAPTER XII.

[034-001]STATE OF SOCIETY AND LEARNING IN IRELAND DURING THE NORMAN PERIOD.

[034-002]We have already spoken of the character of the war waged by and against

[034-003]the Normans on Irish soil, and as war was then almost every man's

[034-004]business, we may be supposed to have described all that is known of the

[034-005]time in describing its wars. What we have to add of the other pursuits

[034-006]of the various orders of men into which society was divided, is neither

[034-007]very full nor very satisfactory.

[034-008]The rise, fall, and migrations of some of the clans have been already

[034-009]alluded to. In no age did more depend on the personal character of the

[034-010]chief than then. When the death of the heroic Godfrey left the free

[034-011]clansmen of Tyrconnell without a lord to lead them to battle, or rule

[034-012]them in peace, the Annalists represent them to us as meeting in great

[034-013]perplexity, and engaged "in making speeches" as to what was to be done,

[034-014]when suddenly, to their great relief, Donnell Oge, son of Donnell More,

[034-015]who had been fostered in Alba (Scotland), was seen approaching them.

[034-016]Not more welcome was Tuathal, the well-beloved, the restorer of the

[034-017]Milesian monarchy, after the revolt of the _Tuatha_. He was immediately

[034-018]elected chief, and the emissaries of O'Neil, who had been waiting for

[034-019]an answer to his demand of tribute, were brought before him. He

[034-020]answered their proposition by a proverb expressed in the Gaelic of

[034-021]Alba, which says that "every man should possess his own country," and

[034-022]Tyrconnell armed to make good this maxim.

[034-023]The Bardic order still retained much of their ancient power, and all

[034-024]their ancient pride. Of their most famous names in this period we may

[034-025]mention Murray O'Daly of Lissadil, in Sligo, Donogh O'Daly of Finvarra,

[034-026]sometimes called Abbot of Boyle, and Gilbride McNamee, laureate to King

[034-027]Brian O'Neil. McNamee, in lamenting the death of Brian, describes

[034-028]himself as defenceless, and a prey to every spoiler, now that his royal

[034-029]protector is no more. He gave him, he tells us, for a poem on one

[034-030]occasion, besides gold and raiment, a gift of twenty cows. On another,

[034-031]when he presented him a poem, he gave in return twenty horned cows, and

[034-032]a gift still more lasting, "the blessing of the King of Erin." Other

[034-033]chiefs, who fell in the same battle, and to one of whom, named Auliffe

[034-034]O'Gormley, he had often gone "on a visit of pleasure," are lamented

[034-035]with equal warmth by the bard. The poetic Abbot of Boyle is himself

[034-036]lamented in the Annals as the Ovid of Ireland, as "a poet who never had

[034-037]and never will have an equal." But the episode which best illustrates

[034-038]at once the address and the audacity of the bardic order is the story

[034-039]of Murray O'Daly of Lissadil, and Donnell More O'Donnell, Lord of

[034-040]Tyrconnell.

[034-041]In the year 1213, O'Donnell despatched Finn O'Brollaghan, his _Aes

[034-042]graidh_ or Steward, to collect his tribute in Connaught, and Finn,

[034-043]putting up at the house of O'Daly, near Drumcliff, and being a plebeian

[034-044]who knew no better, began to wrangle with the poet. The irritable

[034-045]master of song, seizing a sharp axe, slew the steward on the spot, and

[034-046]then to avoid O'Donnell's vengeance fled into Clanrickarde. Here he

[034-047]announced himself by a poem addressed to de Burgh, imploring his

[034-048]protection, setting forth the claims of the Bardic order on all

[034-049]high-descended heroes, and contending that his fault was but venial, in

[034-050]killing a clown, who insulted him. O'Donnell pursued the fugitive to

[034-051]Athenry, and de Burgh sent him away secretly into Thomond. Into

[034-052]Thomond, the Lord of Tyrconnell marched, but O'Brien sent off the Bard

[034-053]to Limerick. The enraged Ulsterman appeared at the gates of Limerick,

[034-054]when O'Daly was smuggled out of the town, and "passed from hand to

[034-055]hand," until he reached Dublin. The following spring O'Donnell appeared

[034-056]in force before Dublin, and demanded the fugitive, who, as a last

[034-057]resort, had been sent for safety into Scotland. From the place of his

[034-058]exile he addressed three deprecatory poems to the offended Lord of

[034-059]Tyrconnell, who finally allowed him to return to Lissadil in peace, and

[034-060]even restored him to his friendship.

[034-061]The introduction of the new religious orders—Dominicans, Franciscans,

[034-062]and the order for the redemption of Captives into Ireland, in the first

[034-063]quarter of this century gradually extinguished the old Columban and

[034-064]Brigintine houses. In Leinster they made way most rapidly; but Ulster

[034-065]clung with its ancient tenacity to the Columban rule. The Hierarchy of

[034-066]the northern half-kingdom still exercised a protectorate over Iona

[034-067]itself, for we read, in the year 1203, how Kellagh, having erected a

[034-068]monastery in the middle of Iona, in despite of the religious, that the

[034-069]Bishops of Derry and Raphoe, with the Abbots of Armagh and Derry and

[034-070]numbers of the Clergy of the North of Ireland, passed over to Iona,

[034-071]pulled down the unauthorized monastery, and assisted at the election of

[034-072]a new Abbot. This is almost the last important act of the Columban

[034-073]order in Ireland. By the close of the century, the Dominicans had some

[034-074]thirty houses, and the Franciscans as many more, whether in the walled

[034-075]towns or the open country. These monasteries became the refuge of

[034-076]scholars, during the stormy period we have passed, and in other days

[034-077]full as troubled, which were to come. Moreover, as the Irish student,

[034-078]like all others in that age, desired to travel from school to school,

[034-079]these orders admitted him to the ranks of widespread European

[034-080]brotherhoods, from whom he might always claim hospitality. Nor need we

[034-081]reject as anything incredible the high renown for scholarship and

[034-082]ability obtained in those times by such men as Thomas Palmeran of Naas,

[034-083]in the University of Paris; by Peter and Thomas Hibernicus in the

[034-084]University of Naples, in the age of Aquinas; by Malachy of Ireland, a

[034-085]Franciscan, Chaplain to King Edward II. of England, and Professor at

[034-086]Oxford; by the Danish Dominican, Gotofrid of Waterford; and above all,

[034-087]by John Scotus of Down, the subtle doctor, the luminary of the

[034-088]Franciscan schools, of Paris and Cologne. The native schools of Ireland

[034-089]had lost their early ascendancy, and are no longer traceable in our

[034-090]annals; but Irish scholarship, when arrested in its full development at

[034-091]home, transferred its efforts to foreign Universities, and there

[034-092]maintained the ancient honour of the country among the studious

[034-093]"nations" of Christendom. Among the "nations" involved in the college

[034-094]riots at Oxford, in the year 1274, we find mention of the Irish, from

[034-095]which fact it is evident there must have been a considerable number of

[034-096]natives of that country, then frequenting the University.

[034-097]The most distinguished native ecclesiastics of this century were

[034-098]Matthew O'Heney, Archbishop of Cashel, originally a Cistercian monk,

[034-099]who died in retirement at Holy Cross in 1207; Albin O'Mulloy, the

[034-100]opponent of _Giraldus_, who died Bishop of Ferns in 1222; and Clarus

[034-101]McMailin, Erenach of Trinity Island, Lough Key—if an _Erenach_ may be

[034-102]called an ecclesiastic. It was O'Heney made the Norman who said the

[034-103]Irish Church had no martyrs, the celebrated answer, that now men had

[034-104]come into the country who knew so well how to make martyrs, that

[034-105]reproach would soon be taken away. He is said to have written a life of

[034-106]Saint Cuthbert of Lindisfarne, and we know that he had legantine powers

[034-107]at the opening of the century. The _Erenach_ of Lough Key, who

[034-108]flourished in its second half, plays an important part in all the

[034-109]western feuds and campaigns; his guarantee often preserved peace and

[034-110]protected the vanquished. Among the church-builders of his age, he

[034-111]stands conspicuous. The ordinary churches were indeed easily built,

[034-112]seldom exceeding 60 or 70 feet in length, and one half that width, and

[034-113]the material still most in use was, for the church proper, timber. The

[034-114]towers, cashels, or surrounding walls, and the cells of the religious,

[034-115]as well as the great monasteries and collegiate and cathedral churches,

[034-116]were of stone, and many of them remain monuments of the skill and

[034-117]munificence of their founders.

[034-118]Of the consequences of the abolition of slavery by the Council of

[034-119]Armagh, at the close of the twelfth century, we have no tangible

[034-120]evidence. It is probable that the slave trade, rather than domestic

[034-121]servitude, was abolished by that decree. The cultivators of the soil

[034-122]were still divided into two orders—Biataghs and Brooees. "The former,"

[034-123]says O'Donovan, "who were comparatively few in number, would appear to

[034-124]have held their lands free of rent, but were obliged to entertain

[034-125]travellers, and the chief's soldiers when on their march in his

[034-126]direction; and the latter (the Brooees) would appear to have been

[034-127]subject to a stipulated rent and service." From "the Book of Lecan," a

[034-128]compilation of the fourteenth century, we learn that the Brooee was

[034-129]required to keep an hundred labourers, and an hundred of each kind of

[034-130]domestic animals. Of the rights or wages of the labourers, we believe,

[034-131]there is no mention made.
