CHAPTER X.

[097-001]THE ERA OF INDEPENDENCE—SECOND PERIOD.

[097-002]The second period of the era of independence may be said to embrace the

[097-003]nine years extending from the dissolution of the last Volunteer

[097-004]Convention, at the end of 1784, to the passage of the Catholic Relief

[097-005]Bill of 1793. They were years of continued interest and excitement,

[097-006]both in the popular and parliamentary affairs of the country; but the

[097-007]events are, with the exception of the last named, of a more secondary

[097-008]order than those of the previous period.

[097-010]The session of 1785 was first occupied with debates relating to what

[097-011]might be called the cross-channel trade between England and Ireland.

[097-012]The question of trade brought with it, necessarily, the question of

[097-013]revenue; of the duties levied in both kingdoms; of the conflict of

[097-014]their commercial laws, and the necessity of their assimilation; of the

[097-015]appropriations to be borne by each, to the general expense of the army

[097-016]and navy; of the exclusive right of the English East India Company to

[097-017]the Indian trade;—in short, the whole of the fiscal and commercial

[097-018]relations of the two countries were now to be examined and adjusted, as

[097-019]their constitutional relations had been in previous years.

[097-020]The first plan came from the Castle, through Mr. Thomas Orde, then

[097-021]Chief Secretary, afterwards Lord Bolton. It consisted of eleven

[097-022]propositions, embracing every division of the subject. They had been

[097-023]arrived at by consultation with Mr. Joshua Pim, a most worthy Quaker

[097-024]merchant, the founder of an equally worthy family; Mr. Grattan, Mr.

[097-025]Foster, and others. They were passed as resolutions in Ireland, and

[097-026]sent by Mr. Orde to England to see whether they would be adopted there

[097-027]also, the second Pitt, then Chancellor of the Exchequer, gave his

[097-028]concurrence, but when he introduced to the English Parliament _his_

[097-029]resolutions—twenty in number—it was found that in several important

[097-030]respects they differed from the Irish propositions. On being taken up

[097-031]and presented to the Irish Parliament, in August, the administration

[097-032]found they could command, in a full House, only a majority of sixteen

[097-033]for their introduction, and so the whole arrangement was abandoned. No

[097-034]definite commercial treaty between the two kingdoms was entered into

[097-035]until the Union, and there can be little doubt that the miscarriage of

[097-036]the Convention of 1785 was one of the determining causes of that Union.

[097-037]The next session was chiefly remarkable for an unsuccessful attempt to

[097-038]reduce the Pension List. In this debate, Curran, who had entered the

[097-039]House in 1783, particularly distinguished himself. A fierce exchange of

[097-040]personalities with Mr. Fitzgibbon led to a duel between them, in which,

[097-041]fortunately, neither was wounded, but their public hostility was

[097-042]transferred to the arena of the courts, where some of the choicest

[097-043]_morceaux_ of genuine Irish wit were uttered by Curran, at the expense

[097-044]of his rival, first as Attorney-General, and subsequently as

[097-045]Chancellor.

[097-046]The session of 1787 was introduced by a speech from the throne, in

[097-047]which the usual paragraph in favour of the Protestant Charter Schools

[097-048]was followed by another advising the establishment of a general system

[097-049]of schools. This raised the entire question of education, one of the

[097-050]most difficult to deal with in the whole range of Irish politics. On

[097-051]the 10th of April, Mr. Orde—destined to be the author of just, but

[097-052]short-lived projects—introduced his plan of what might be called

[097-053]national education. He proposed to establish four great provincial

[097-054]academies, a second university in some north-western county, to reform

[097-055]the twenty-two diocesan schools, so richly endowed under the 28th Henry

[097-056]VIII., and to affiliate on Trinity College two principal preparatory

[097-057]schools, north and south. In 1784, and again in this very year, the

[097-058]humane John Howard had reported of the Irish Charter Schools, then half

[097-059]a century established, that they were "a disgrace to all society." Sir

[097-060]J. Fitzpatrick, the Inspector of Prisons, confirmed the general

[097-061]impression of Howard: he found the children in these schools "puny,

[097-062]filthy, ill clothed, without linen, indecent to look upon." A series of

[097-063]resolutions was introduced by Mr. Orde, as the basis of better

[097-064]legislation in the next session; but it is to be regretted that the

[097-065]proposed reform never went farther than the introduction and adoption

[097-066]of these resolutions.

[097-067]The session of 1788 was signalized by a great domestic and a great

[097-068]imperial discussion—the Tithe question, and the Regency question.

[097-069]The Tithe question had slumbered within the walls of Parliament since

[097-070]the days of Swift, though not in the lonely lodges of the secret

[097-071]agrarian societies. Very recent outbreaks of the old agrarian

[097-072]combinations against both excessive rents and excessive tithes, in the

[097-073]Leinster as well as in southern counties, had called general attention

[097-074]to the subject, when Grattan, in 1787, moved that, if it should appear,

[097-075]by the commencement of the following session, that tranquillity had

[097-076]been restored in the disturbed districts, the House would take into

[097-077]consideration the subject of tithes. Accordingly, very early in the

[097-078]next ensuing session, he moved for a committee on the subject, in a

[097-079]three hours' speech, which ranks among the very highest efforts of his

[097-080]own or any other age. He was seconded by Lord Kingsborough, one of the

[097-081]most liberal men of his order, and sustained by Curran and Brownlow; he

[097-082]was opposed by Attorney-General Fitzgibbon, and by Messrs. Hobart,

[097-083]Browne, and Parsons. The vote was, _for_ the Committee of Inquiry, 49;

[097-084]_against_ it, 121. A second attempt, a little later in the session, was

[097-085]equally unsuccessful, except for the moral effect produced out of doors

[097-086]by another of those speeches, which it is impossible to read even at

[097-087]this day, without falling into the attitude, and assuming the

[097-088]intonation, and feeling the heartfelt inspiration of the orator.

[097-089]The Regency question was precipitated upon both Parliaments by the

[097-090]mental disorder, which, for the second or third time, attacked George

[097-091]III., in 1788. The question was, whether the Prince of Wales should

[097-092]reign with as full powers as if his father were actually deceased;

[097-093]whether there should be restrictions or no restrictions. Mr. Pitt and

[097-094]his colleagues contended successfully for restrictions in England,

[097-095]while Mr. Fox and the opposition took the contrary position. The

[097-096]English Houses and people went with Pitt, but the Irish Parliament went

[097-097]for an unconditional regency. They resolved to offer the crown of

[097-098]Ireland to him they considered _de_ facto their Sovereign, as freely as

[097-099]they had rendered their allegiance to the incapable king; but the Lord

[097-100]Lieutenant—the Marquis of Buckingham—declined to transmit their

[097-101]over-zealous address, and by the time their joint delegation of both

[097-102]Houses reached London, George III. had recovered! They received the

[097-103]most gracious reception at Carlton House, but they incurred the

[097-104]implacable enmity of William Pitt, and created a second determining

[097-105]cause in his mind in favour of an early legislative union.

[097-106]The prospect of the accession of the Prince to power, wrought a

[097-107]wonderful and a salutary change, though temporary, in the Irish

[097-108]Commons. In the session of 1789, Mr Grattan carried, by 105 to 85, a

[097-109]two months', in amendment to a twelve-months' supply bill. Before the

[097-110]two months expired he brought in his police bill, his pension bill, and

[097-111]his bill to prevent officers of the revenue from voting at elections,

[097-112]but ere these reforms could be passed into law, the old King recovered,

[097-113]the necessary majority was reversed, and the measures, of course,

[097-114]defeated or delayed till better times. The triumph of the oligarchy was

[097-115]in proportion to their fright. The House having passed a vote of

[097-116]censure on Lord Buckingham, the Viceroy, for refusing to transmit their

[097-117]address to the Regent, a threat was now held out that every one who had

[097-118]voted for the censure, holding an office of honour or emolument in

[097-119]Ireland, would be made "the victim of his vote." In reply to this

[097-120]threat, a "Round Robin" was signed by the Duke of Leinster, the

[097-121]Archbishop of Tuam, eighteen peers, all the leading Whig commoners—the

[097-122]Ponsonbys, Langrishes, Grattan, Connolly, Curran, O'Neil, Day, Charles

[097-123]Francis Sheridan, Bowes Daly, George Ogle, etc., etc.—declaring that

[097-124]they would regard any such proscription as an attack on the

[097-125]independence of Parliament, and would jointly oppose any administration

[097-126]who should resort to such proscription. But the bold and domineering

[097-127]spirit of Fitzgibbon—the leader of the Castle party, then, and long

[097-128]afterwards—did not shrink before even so formidable a phalanx. The Duke

[097-129]of Leinster was dismissed from the honorary office of Master of the

[097-130]Rolls; the Earl of Shannon, from the Vice-Treasurership; William

[097-131]Ponsonby from the office of Postmaster-General; Charles Francis

[097-132]Sheridan, from that of Secretary at War, and ten or twelve other

[097-133]prominent members of the _Irish_ administration lost places and

[097-134]pensions to the value of 20,000 pounds a year, for their over-zeal for

[097-135]the Prince of Wales. At the same time, Mr. Fitzgibbon was appointed

[097-136]Lord Chancellor, a vacancy having opportunely occurred, by the death of

[097-137]Lord Lifford, in the very midst of the prescriptive crisis. This

[097-138]elevation transferred him to the Upper House, where, for the remaining

[097-139]years of the Parliament, he continued to dogmatize and domineer, as he

[097-140]had done in the Commons, often rebuked, but never abashed. Indeed, the

[097-141]milder manners of the patrician body were ill suited to resist this

[097-142]ermined demagogue, whose motto through life was _audacity, again

[097-143]audacity, and always audacity_. The names of Wolfe, Toler, Corry,

[097-144]Coote, Beresford, and Cooke, are also found among the promotions to

[097-145]legal and administrative office; names familiar to the last generation

[097-146]as the pillars of the oligarchical faction, before and after the Union.

[097-147]To swamp the opposition peers, the Earls of Antrim, Tyrone, and

[097-148]Hillsborough were made Marquises of Antrim, Waterford, and Downshire;

[097-149]the Viscounts Glenawley, Enniskillen, Erne, and Carysfort, were created

[097-150]Earls of Annesley, Enniskillen, Erne, and Carysfort. Then Judge Scott

[097-151]became Viscount Clonmel; then the Lordships of Loftus, Londonderry,

[097-152]Kilmaine, Cloncurry, Mountjoy, Glentworth, and Caledon, were founded

[097-153]for as many convenient Commoners, who either paid for their patents, in

[097-154]boroughs, or in hard cash. It was the very reign and carnival of

[097-155]corruption, over which presided the invulnerable Chancellor—a true

[097-156]"King of Misrule." In reference to this appalling spectacle, well might

[097-157]Grattan exclaim—"In a free country the path of public treachery leads

[097-158]to the block; but in a nation governed like a province, to the helm!"

[097-159]But the thunders of the orator fell, and were quenched in the wide

[097-160]spreading waters of corruption.

[097-161]The Whig Club—an out-of-door auxiliary of the opposition—was a creation

[097-162]of this year. It numbered the chief signers of the "Round Robin," and

[097-163]gained many adherents. It exercised very considerable influence in the

[097-164]general election of 1790, and for the few following years, until it

[097-165]fell to pieces in the presence of the more ardent politics which

[097-166]preceded the storm of 1798.

[097-167]Backed though he was by Mr. Pitt, both as his relative and principal,

[097-168]the Marquis of Buckingham was compelled to resign the government, and

[097-169]to steal away from Dublin, under cover of night, like an absconding

[097-170]debtor. The Chancellor and the Speaker—Fitzgibbon and Foster, Irishmen

[097-171]at least by birth and name—were sworn in as Justices, until the arrival

[097-172]of the Earl of Westmoreland, in the ensuing January.

[097-173]The last two Viceroys of the decade thus closed, form a marked contrast

[097-174]worthy of particular portraiture. The Duke of Rutland, a dashing

[097-175]profligate, was sent over, it was thought, to ruin public liberty by

[097-176]undermining private virtue, a task in which he found a willing helpmate

[097-177]in his beautiful but dissipated Duchess. During his three years' reign

[097-178]were sown the seeds of that reckless private expenditure, and general

[097-179]corruption of manners, which drove so many bankrupt lords and gentlemen

[097-180]into the market overt, where Lord Castlereagh and Secretary Cooke, a

[097-181]dozen years later, priced the value of their parliamentary cattle. Lord

[097-182]Rutland died of dissipation at little over thirty, and was succeeded by

[097-183]the Marquis of Buckingham (formerly Lord Temple), the founder of the

[097-184]Irish Order of Chivalry, a person of the greatest pretensions, as a

[097-185]reformer of abuses and an enemy of government by corruption. Yet with

[097-186]all his affected superiority to the base arts of his predecessor, the

[097-187]Marquis's system was still more opposite to every idea of just

[097-188]government than the Duke's. The one outraged public morals, the other

[097-189]pensioned and ennobled the betrayers of public trusts; the one

[097-190]naturalized the gaming-table and the keeping of mistresses as customs

[097-191]of Irish society; the other sold or allowed the highest offices and

[097-192]honours of the state—from a weighership in the butter market to an

[097-193]earl's coronet—to be put up at auction, and knocked down to the highest

[097-194]bidder. How cheering in contrast with the shameful honours, flaunted

[097-195]abroad in those shameful days, are even the negative virtues of the

[097-196]Whig patricians, and how splendid the heroic constancy of Charlemont,

[097-197]Grattan, Curran, and their devoted minority of honest legislators!

[097-199]With Lord Westmoreland was associated, as Chief Secretary, Mr. Hobart,

[097-200]formerly in the army, a man of gay, convivial habits, very

[097-201]accomplished, and, politically, very unprincipled. These gentlemen,

[097-202]both favourites of Pitt, adopted the counsellors, and continued the

[097-203]policy of the late Viceroy. In pursuance of this policy, a dissolution

[097-204]took place, and the general election of 1790 was ordered. We have

[097-205]already exhibited the influences which controlled the choice of members

[097-206]of the House of Commons. Of the one hundred and five great proprietors,

[097-207]who owned two-thirds of the seats, perhaps a fourth might be found in

[097-208]the ranks of the Whig club. The only other hope for the national party

[097-209]was in the boroughs, which possessed a class of freemen, engaged in

[097-210]trade, too numerous to be bought, or too public spirited to be dictated

[097-211]to. Both influences combined might hope to return a powerful minority,

[097-212]and, on this occasion (1790) they certainly did so. Grattan and Lord

[097-213]Henry Fitzgerald were elected for Dublin, over the Lord Mayor and one

[097-214]of the Aldermen, backed by the whole power of the Castle; Curran,

[097-215]Ponsonby, Brownlow, Forbes, and nearly all "the victims of their vote"

[097-216]were re-elected. To these old familiar names were now added others

[097-217]destined to equal, if not still wider fame—Arthur Wellesley, member for

[097-218]Trim; Arthur O'Conor, member for Phillipstown; Jonah Barrington, member

[097-219]for Tuam; and Robert Stewart, one of the members for the County Down,

[097-220]then only in his twenty-second year, and, next to Lord Edward

[097-221]Fitzgerald, lately elected for Athy, the most extreme reformer among

[097-222]the new members. Arthur O'Conor, on the other hand, commenced his

[097-223]career with the Court by moving the address in answer to the speech

[097-224]from the throne!

[097-225]The new Parliament, which met in July, 1790, unanimously re-elected Mr.

[097-226]Foster, Speaker; passed a very loyal address, and, after a fortnight's

[097-227]sitting, was prorogued till the following January. The session of '91

[097-228]was marked by no event of importance, the highest opposition vote seems

[097-229]to have been from 80 to 90, and the ministerial majority never less

[097-230]than 50. The sale of Peerages, the East India trade, the Responsibility

[097-231](for money warrants) Bill, the Barren Lands Bill, and the Pension Bill,

[097-232]were the chief topics. A committee to inquire into the best means of

[097-233]encouraging breweries, and discouraging the use of spirituous liquors,

[097-234]was also granted, and some curious facts elicited. Nothing memorable

[097-235]was done, but much that was memorable was said—for the great orator had

[097-236]still a free press, and a home audience to instruct and elevate. The

[097-237]truth is, the barrenness of these two sessions was due to the general

[097-238]prosperity of the country, more even than to the dexterous management

[097-239]of Major Hobart and the Cabinet balls of Lord Westmoreland. There was,

[097-240]moreover, hanging over the minds of men the electric pressure of the

[097-241]wonderful events with which France shook the Continent, and made the

[097-242]Islands tremble. There was hasty hope, or idle exultation, or pious

[097-243]fear, or panic terror, in the hearts of the leading spectators of that

[097-244]awful drama, according to the prejudices or principles they maintained.

[097-245]Over all the three kingdoms there was a preternatural calm, resembling

[097-246]that physical stillness which in other latitude precedes the eruption

[097-247]of volcanoes.
