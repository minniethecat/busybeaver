CHAPTER II.

[002-001]THE FIRST AGES.

[002-002]Since we have no Roman accounts of the form of government or state of

[002-003]society in ancient Erin, we must only depend on the Bards and

[002-004]Story-tellers, so far as their statements are credible and agree with

[002-005]each other. On certain main points they do agree, and these are the

[002-006]points which it seems reasonable for us to take on their authority.

[002-007]As even brothers born of the same mother, coming suddenly into

[002-008]possession of a prize, will struggle to see who can get the largest

[002-009]share, so we find in those first ages a constant succession of armed

[002-010]struggles for power. The petty Princes who divided the Island between

[002-011]them were called _Righ_, a word which answers to the Latin _Rex_ and

[002-012]French _Roi_; and the chief king or monarch was called _Ard-Righ_, or

[002-013]High-King. The eldest nephew, or son of the king, was the usual heir of

[002-014]power, and was called the _Tanist_, or successor; although any of the

[002-015]family of the Prince, his brothers, cousins, or other kinsmen, might be

[002-016]chosen _Tanist_, by election of the people over whom he was to rule.

[002-017]One certain cause of exclusion was personal deformity; for if a Prince

[002-018]was born lame or a hunchback, or if he lost a limb by accident, he was

[002-019]declared unfit to govern. Even after succession, any serious accident

[002-020]entailed deposition, though we find the names of several Princes who

[002-021]managed to evade or escape this singular penalty. It will be observed

[002-022]besides of the _Tanist_, that the habit of appointing him seems to have

[002-023]been less a law than a custom; that it was not universal in all the

[002-024]Provinces; that in some tribes the succession alternated between a

[002-025]double line of Princes; and that sometimes when the reigning Prince

[002-026]obtained the nomination of a _Tanist_, to please himself, the choice

[002-027]was set aside by the public voice of the clansmen. The successor to the

[002-028]Ard-Righ, or Monarch, instead of being simply called _Tanist_, had the

[002-029]more sounding title of _Roydamna_, or King-successor.

[002-030]The chief offices about the Kings, in the first ages, were all filled

[002-031]by the Druids, or Pagan Priests; the _Brehons_, or Judges, were usually

[002-032]Druids, as were also the _Bards_, the historians of their patrons. Then

[002-033]came the Physicians; the Chiefs who paid tribute or received annual

[002-034]gifts from the Sovereigns, or Princes; the royal stewards; and the

[002-035]military leaders or Champions, who, like the knights of the middle

[002-036]ages, held their lands and their rank at court, by the tenure of the

[002-037]sword. Like the feudal _Dukes_ of France, and _Barons_ of England,

[002-038]these military nobles often proved too powerful for their nominal

[002-039]patrons, and made them experience all the uncertainty of reciprocal

[002-040]dependence. The Champions play an important part in all the early

[002-041]legends. Wherever there is trouble you are sure to find them. Their

[002-042]most celebrated divisions were the warriors of the _Red Branch_—that is

[002-043]to say, the Militia of Ulster; the _Fiann_, or Militia of Leinster,

[002-044]sometimes the royal guard of Tara, at others in exile and disgrace; the

[002-045]_Clan-Degaid_ of Munster, and the _Fiann_ of Connaught. The last force

[002-046]was largely recruited from the Belgic race who had been squeezed into

[002-047]that western province, by their Milesian conquerors, pretty much as

[002-048]Cromwell endeavoured to force the Milesian Irish into it, many hundred

[002-049]years afterwards. Each of these bands had its special heroes; its

[002-050]Godfreys and Orlandos celebrated in song; the most famous name in

[002-051]Ulster was Cuchullin: so called from _cu_, a hound, or watch-dog, and

[002-052]_Ullin_, the ancient name of his province. He lived at the dawn of the

[002-053]Christian era. Of equal fame was Finn, the father of Ossian, and the

[002-054]Fingal of modern fiction, who flourished in the latter half of the

[002-055]second century. Gall, son of Morna, the hero of Connaught (one of the

[002-056]few distinguished men of Belgic origin whom we hear of through the

[002-057]Milesian bards), flourished a generation earlier than Finn, and might

[002-058]fairly compete with him in celebrity, if he had only had an Ossian to

[002-059]sing his praises.

[002-061]The political boundaries of different tribes expanded or contracted

[002-062]with their good or ill fortune in battle. Immigration often followed

[002-063]defeat, so that a clan, or its offshoot is found at one period on one

[002-064]part of the map and again on another. As _surnames_ were not generally

[002-065]used either in Ireland or anywhere else, till after the tenth century,

[002-066]the great families are distinguishable at first, only by their tribe or

[002-067]clan names. Thus at the north we have the Hy-Nial race; in the south

[002-068]the Eugenian race, so called from Nial and Eoghan, their mutual

[002-069]ancestors.

[002-070]We have already compared the shape of Erin to a shield, in which the

[002-071]four Provinces represented the four quarters. Some shields have also

[002-072]_bosses_ or centre-pieces, and the federal province of MEATH was the

[002-073]_boss_ of the old Irish shield. The ancient Meath included both the

[002-074]present counties of that name, stretching south to the Liffey, and

[002-075]north to Armagh. It was the mensal demesne, or "board of the king's

[002-076]table:" it was exempt from all taxes, except those of the Ard-Righ, and

[002-077]its relations to the other Provinces may be vaguely compared to those

[002-078]of the District of Columbia to the several States of the North American

[002-079]Union. ULSTER might then be defined by a line drawn from Sligo Harbour

[002-080]to the mouth of the Boyne, the line being notched here and there by the

[002-081]royal demesne of Meath; LEINSTER stretched south from Dublin

[002-082]triangle-wise to Waterford Harbour, but its inland line, towards the

[002-083]west, was never very well defined, and this led to constant border wars

[002-084]with Munster; the remainder of the south to the mouth of the Shannon

[002-085]composed MUNSTER; the present county of Clare and all west of the

[002-086]Shannon north to Sligo, and part of Cavan, going with CONNAUGHT. The

[002-087]chief seats of power, in those several divisions, were TARA, for

[002-088]federal purposes; EMANIA, near Armagh, for Ulster; LEIGHLIN, for

[002-089]Leinster; CASHEL, for Munster; and CRUCHAIN, (now Rathcrogan, in

[002-090]Roscommon,) for Connaught.

[002-091]How the common people lived within these external divisions of power it

[002-092]is not so easy to describe. All histories tell us a great deal of

[002-093]kings, and battles, and conspiracies, but very little of the daily

[002-094]domestic life of the people. In this respect the history of Erin is

[002-095]much the same as the rest; but some leading facts we do know. Their

[002-096]religion, in Pagan times, was what the moderns call _Druidism_, but

[002-097]what they called it themselves we now know not. It was probably the

[002-098]same religion anciently professed by Tyre and Sidon, by Carthage and

[002-099]her colonies in Spain; the same religion which the Romans have

[002-100]described as existing in great part of Gaul, and by their accounts, we

[002-101]learn the awful fact, that it sanctioned, nay, demanded, human

[002-102]sacrifices. From the few traces of its doctrines which Christian zeal

[002-103]has permitted to survive in the old Irish language, we see that _Belus_

[002-104]or "Crom," the god of fire, typified by the sun, was its chief

[002-105]divinity—that two great festivals were held in his honour on days

[002-106]answering to the first of May and last of October. There were also

[002-107]particular gods of poets, champions, artificers and mariners, just as

[002-108]among the Romans and Greeks. Sacred groves were dedicated to these

[002-109]gods; Priests and Priestesses devoted their lives to their service; the

[002-110]arms of the champion, and the person of the king were charmed by them;

[002-111]neither peace nor war was made without their sanction; their own

[002-112]persons and their pupils were held sacred; the high place at the king's

[002-113]right hand and the best fruits of the earth and the waters were theirs.

[002-114]Old age revered them, women worshipped them, warriors paid court to

[002-115]them, youth trembled before them, princes and chieftains regarded them

[002-116]as elder brethren. So numerous were they in Erin, and so celebrated,

[002-117]that the altars of Britain and western Gaul, left desolate by the Roman

[002-118]legions, were often served by hierophants from Erin, which, even in

[002-119]those Pagan days, was known to all the Druidic countries as the "Sacred

[002-120]Island." Besides the princes, the warriors, and the Druids, (who were

[002-121]also the Physicians, Bards and Brehons of the first ages,) there were

[002-122]innumerable petty chiefs, all laying claim to noble birth and blood.

[002-123]They may be said with the warriors and priests to be the only freemen.

[002-124]The _Bruais_, or farmers, though possessing certain legal rights, were

[002-125]an inferior caste; while of the Artisans, the smiths and armorers only

[002-126]seem to have been of much consideration. The builders of those

[002-127]mysterious round towers, of which a hundred ruins yet remain, may also

[002-128]have been a privileged order. But the mill and the loom were servile

[002-129]occupations, left altogether to slaves taken in battle, or purchased in

[002-130]the market-places of Britain. The task of the herdsman, like that of

[002-131]the farm-labourer, seems to have devolved on the bondsmen, while the

[002-132]_quern_ and the shuttle were left exclusively in the hands of the

[002-133]bondswomen.

[002-134]We need barely mention the names of the first Milesian kings, who were

[002-135]remarkable for something else than cutting each other's throats, in

[002-136]order to hasten on to the solid ground of Christian tunes. The

[002-137]principal names are: Heber and Heremhon, the crowned sons of Milesians;

[002-138]they at first divided the Island fairly, but Heremhon soon became

[002-139]jealous of his brother, slew him in battle, and established his own

[002-140]supremacy. Irial the Prophet was King, and built seven royal

[002-141]fortresses; Tiern'mass; in his reign the arts of dyeing in colours were

[002-142]introduced; and the distinguishing of classes by the number of colours

[002-143]they were permitted to wear, was decreed. Ollamh ("the Wise")

[002-144]established the Convention of Tara, which assembled habitually every

[002-145]ninth year, but might be called oftener; it met about the October

[002-146]festival in honour of Beleus or _Crom_; Eocaid invented or introduced a

[002-147]new species of wicker boats, called _cassa_, and spent much of his time

[002-148]upon the sea; a solitary queen, named Macha, appears in the succession,

[002-149]from whom Armagh takes its name; except Mab, the mythological Queen of

[002-150]Connaught, she is the sole female ruler of Erin in the first ages; Owen

[002-151]or Eugene Mor ("the Great") is remembered as the founder of the notable

[002-152]families who rejoice in the common name of Eugenians; Leary, of whom

[002-153]the fable of Midas is told with variations; Angus, whom the after

[002-154]Princes of Alba (Scotland) claimed as their ancestor; Eocaid, the tenth

[002-155]of that name, in whose reign are laid the scenes of the chief

[002-156]mythological stories of Erin—such as the story of Queen Mab—the story

[002-157]of the Sons of Usna; the death of Cuchullin (a counterpart of the

[002-158]Persian tale of Roostam and Sohrab); the story of Fergus, son of the

[002-159]king; of Connor of Ulster; of the sons of Dari; and many more. We next

[002-160]meet with the first king who led an expedition abroad against the

[002-161]Romans in Crimthan, surnamed _Neea-Naari_, or Nair's Hero, from the

[002-162]good genius who accompanied him on his foray. A well-planned

[002-163]insurrection of the conquered Belgae, cut off one of Crimthan's

[002-164]immediate successors, with all his chiefs and nobles, at a banquet

[002-165]given on the Belgian-plain (Moybolgue, in Cavan); and arrested for a

[002-166]century thereafter Irish expeditions abroad. A revolution and a

[002-167]restoration followed, in which Moran the Just Judge played the part of

[002-168]Monk to _his_ Charles II., Tuathal surnamed "the Legitimate." It was

[002-169]Tuathal who imposed the special tax on Leinster, of which, we shall

[002-170]often hear—under the title of _Borooa_, or Tribute. "The Legitimate"

[002-171]was succeeded by his son, who introduced the Roman _Lex Talionis_ ("an

[002-172]eye for an eye and a tooth for a tooth") into the Brehon code; soon

[002-173]after, the Eugenian families of the south, strong in numbers, and led

[002-174]by a second Owen More, again halved the Island with the ruling race,

[002-175]the boundary this time being the _esker_, or ridge of land which can be

[002-176]easily traced from Dublin west to Galway. Olild, a brave and able

[002-177]Prince, succeeded in time to the southern half-kingdom, and planted his

[002-178]own kindred deep and firm in its soil, though the unity of the monarchy

[002-179]was again restored under Cormac Ulla, or _Longbeard_. This Cormac,

[002-180]according to the legend, was in secret a Christian, and was done to

[002-181]death by the enraged and alarmed Druids, after his abdication and

[002-182]retirement from the world (A.D. 266). He had reigned full forty years,

[002-183]rivalling in wisdom, and excelling in justice the best of his

[002-184]ancestors. Some of his maxims remain to us, and challenge comparison

[002-185]for truthfulness and foresight with most uninspired writings.

[002-186]Cormac's successors during the same century are of little mark, but in

[002-187]the next the expeditions against the Roman outposts were renewed with

[002-188]greater energy and on an increasing scale. Another Crimthan eclipsed

[002-189]the fame of his ancestor and namesake; Nial, called "of the Hostages,"

[002-190]was slain on a second or third expedition into Gaul (A.D. 405), while

[002-191]Dathy, nephew and successor to Nial, was struck dead by lightning in

[002-192]the passage of the Alps (A.D. 428). It was in one of Nial's Gallic

[002-193]expeditions that the illustrious captive was brought into Erin, for

[002-194]whom Providence had reserved the glory of its conversion to the

[002-195]Christian faith—an event which gives a unity and a purpose to the

[002-196]history of that Nation, which must always constitute its chief

[002-197]attraction to the Christian reader.
