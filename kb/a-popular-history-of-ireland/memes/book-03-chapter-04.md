CHAPTER IV.

[020-001]STATE OF RELIGION AND LEARNING AMONG THE IRISH, PREVIOUS TO THE

[020-002]ANGLO-NORMAN INVASION.

[020-003]At the end of the eighth century, before entering on the Norwegian and

[020-004]Danish wars, we cast a backward glance on the Christian ages over which

[020-005]we had passed; and now again we have arrived at the close of an era,

[020-006]when a rapid retrospect of the religious and social condition of the

[020-007]country requires to be taken.

[020-008]The disorganization of the ancient Celtic constitution has already been

[020-009]sufficiently described. The rise of the great families, and their

[020-010]struggles for supremacy, have also been briefly sketched. The

[020-011]substitution of the clan for the race, of pedigree for patriotism, has

[020-012]been exhibited to the reader. We have now to turn to the inner life of

[020-013]the people, and to ascertain what substitutes they found in their

[020-014]religious and social condition, for the absence of a fixed

[020-015]constitutional system, and the strength and stability which such a

[020-016]system confers.

[020-017]The followers of Odin, though they made no proselytes to their horrid

[020-018]creed among the children of St. Patrick, succeeded in inflicting many

[020-019]fatal wounds on the Irish Church. The schools, monasteries, and

[020-020]nunneries, situated on harbours or rivers, or within a convenient march

[020-021]of the coast, were their first objects of attack; teachers and pupils

[020-022]were dispersed, or, if taken, put to death, or, escaping, were driven

[020-023]to resort to arms in self-defence. Bishops could no longer reside in

[020-024]their sees, nor anchorites in their cells, unless they invited

[020-025]martyrdom; a fact which may, perhaps, in some degree account for the

[020-026]large number of Irish ecclesiastics, many of them in episcopal orders,

[020-027]who are found, in the ninth century, in Gaul and Germany, at Rheims,

[020-028]Mentz, Ratisbon, Fulda, Cologne, and other places, already Christian.

[020-029]But it was not in the banishment of masters, the destruction of

[020-030]libraries and school buildings, the worst consequences of the Gentile

[020-031]war were felt. Their ferocity provoked retaliation in kind, and

[020-032]effaced, first among the military class, and gradually from among all

[020-033]others, that growing gentleness of manners and clemency of temper,

[020-034]which we can trace in such princes as Nial of the Showers and Nial of

[020-035]Callan. "A change in the national spirit is the greatest of all

[020-036]revolutions;" and this change the Danish and Norwegian wars had

[020-037]wrought, in two centuries, among the Irish.

[020-038]The number of Bishops in the early Irish Church was greatly in excess

[020-039]of the number of modern dioceses. From the eighth to the twelfth

[020-040]century we hear frequently of _Episcopi Vagantes_, or itinerant, and

[020-041]_Episcopi Vacantes_, or unbeneficed Bishops; the Provincial Synods of

[020-042]England and Gaul frequently had to complain of the influx of such

[020-043]Bishops into their country. At the Synod held near the Hill of Usny, in

[020-044]the year 1111, fifty Bishops attended, and at the Synod of Rath-Brazil,

[020-045]seven years later, according to Keating, but twenty-five were present.

[020-046]To this period, then, when Celsus was Primate and Legate of the Holy

[020-047]See, we may attribute the first attempted reduction of the Episcopal

[020-048]body to something like its modern number; but so far was this salutary

[020-049]restriction from being universally observed that, at the Synod of Kells

[020-050](A.D. 1152), the hierarchy had again risen to thirty-four, exclusive of

[020-051]the four Archbishops. Three hundred priests, and three thousand

[020-052]ecclesiastics are given as the number present at the first-mentioned

[020-053]Synod.

[020-054]The religious orders, probably represented by the above proportion of

[020-055]three thousand ecclesiastics to three hundred [secular] priests had

[020-056]also undergone a remarkable revolution. The rule of all the early Irish

[020-057]monasteries and convents was framed upon an original constitution,

[020-058]which St. Patrick had obtained in France from St. Martin of Tours, who

[020-059]in turn had copied after the monachism of Egypt and the East. It is

[020-060]called by ecclesiastical writers the Columban rule, and was more rigid

[020-061]in some particulars than the rule of St. Benedict, by which it was

[020-062]afterwards supplanted. Amongst other restrictions it prohibited the

[020-063]admission of all unprofessed persons within the precincts of the

[020-064]monastery—a law as regards females incorporated in the Benedictine

[020-065]constitution; and it strictly enjoined silence on the professed—a

[020-066]discipline revived by the brethren of La Trappe. The primary difference

[020-067]between the two orders lay perhaps in this, that the Benedictine made

[020-068]study and the cultivation of the intellect subordinate to manual labour

[020-069]and implicit obedience, while the Columban Order attached more

[020-070]importance to the acquisition of knowledge and missionary enterprise.

[020-071]Not that this was their invariable, but only their peculiar

[020-072]characteristic: a deep-seated love of seclusion and meditation often,

[020-073]intermingled with this fearless and experimental zeal. It was not to be

[020-074]expected in a century like the ninth, especially when the Benedictine

[020-075]Order was overspreading the West, that its milder spirit should not act

[020-076]upon the spirit of the Columban rule. It was, in effect, more social,

[020-077]and less scientific, more a wisdom to be acted than to be taught. Armed

[020-078]with the syllogism, the Columbites issued out of their remote island,

[020-079]carrying their strongly marked personality into every controversy and

[020-080]every correspondence. In Germany and Gaul, their system blazed up in

[020-081]Virgilius, in Erigena, and Macarius, and then disappeared in the

[020-082]calmer, slower, but safer march of the Benedictine discipline. By a

[020-083]reform of the same ancient order, its last hold on native soil was

[020-084]loosened when, under the auspices of St. Malachy, the Cistercian rule

[020-085]was introduced into Ireland the very year of his first visit to

[020-086]Clairvaux (A.D. 1139). St. Mary's Abbey, Dublin, was the first to adopt

[020-087]that rule, and the great monastery of Mellifont, placed under the

[020-088]charge of the brother of the Primate, sprung up in Meath, three years

[020-089]later. The Abbeys of Bective, Boyle, Baltinglass, and Monasternenagh,

[020-090]date from the year of Malachy's second journey to Rome, and death at

[020-091]Clairvaux—A.D. 1148. Before the end of the century, the rule was

[020-092]established at Fermoy, Holycross, and Odorney; at Athlone and Knockmoy;

[020-093]at Newry and Assaroe, and in almost every tribe-land of Meath and

[020-094]Leinster. It is usually but erroneously supposed that the Cistercian

[020-095]rule came in with the Normans; for although many houses owed their

[020-096]foundation to that race, the order itself had been naturalized in

[020-097]Ireland a generation before the first landing of the formidable allies

[020-098]of Dermid on the coast of Wexford. The ancient native order had

[020-099]apparently fulfilled its mission, and long rudely lopped and shaken by

[020-100]civil commotions and Pagan war, it was prepared to give place to a new

[020-101]and more vigorous organization of kindred holiness and energy.

[020-103]As the horrors of war disturbed continually the clergy from their

[020-104]sacred calling, and led many of them, even Abbots and Bishops, to take

[020-105]up arms, so the yoke of religion gradually loosened and dropped from

[020-106]the necks of the people. The awe of the eighth century for a Priest or

[020-107]Bishop had already disappeared in the tenth, when Christian hands were

[020-108]found to decapitate Cormac of Cashel, and offer his head as a trophy to

[020-109]the Ard-Righ. In the twelfth century the Archbishop and Bishops of

[020-110]Connaught, bound to the Synod of Trim, were fallen upon by the Kern of

[020-111]Carbre the Swift, before they could cross the Shannon, their people

[020-112]beaten and dispersed and two of them killed. In the time of Thorlogh

[020-113]More O'Conor, a similar outrage was offered by Tiernan O'Ruarc to the

[020-114]Archbishop of Armagh, and one of his ecclesiastics was killed in the

[020-115]assault. Not only for the persons of ministers of religion had the

[020-116]ancient awe and reverence disappeared, but even for the sacred

[020-117]precincts of the Sanctuary. In the second century of the war with the

[020-118]Northmen we begin to hear of churches and cloisters plundered by native

[020-119]chiefs, who yet called themselves Christians, though in every such

[020-120]instance our annalists are careful to record the vengeance of Heaven

[020-121]following swift on sacrilege. Clonmacnoise, Kildare, and Lismore, were

[020-122]more than once rifled of their wealth by impious hands, and given over

[020-123]to desolation and burning by so-called Christian nobles and soldiers!

[020-124]It is some mitigation of the dreadful record thus presented to be

[020-125]informed—as we often are—especially in the annals of the twelfth

[020-126]century, that the treasures so pillaged were not the shrines of saints

[020-127]nor the sacred ornaments of the altar, but the temporal wealth of

[020-128]temporal proprietors, laid up in churches as places of greatest

[020-129]security.

[020-130]The estates of the Church were, in most instances, farmed by laymen,

[020-131]called _Erenachs_, who, in the relaxation of all discipline, seem to

[020-132]have gradually appropriated the lands to themselves, leaving to the

[020-133]Clergy and Bishops only periodical dues and the actual enclosure of the

[020-134]Church. This office of Erenach was hereditary, and must have presented

[020-135]many strong temptations to its occupants. It is indeed certain that the

[020-136]Irish Church was originally founded on the broadest voluntaryism, and

[020-137]that such was the spirit of all its most illustrious fathers. "Content

[020-138]with food and raiment," says an ancient Canon attributed to St.

[020-139]Patrick, "reject the gifts of the wicked beside, seeing that the lamb

[020-140]takes only that with which it is fed." Such, to the letter, was the

[020-141]maxim which guided the conduct of Colman and his brethren, of whom Bede

[020-142]makes such honourable mention, in the third century after the preaching

[020-143]of St. Patrick. But the munificence of tribes and Princes was not to be

[020-144]restrained, and to obviate any violation of the revered canons of the

[020-145]apostle, laymen, as treasurers and stewards over the endowments of the

[020-146]Church, were early appointed. As those possessions increased, the

[020-147]desire of family aggrandizement proved too much for the Erenachs not

[020-148]only of Armagh, but of most other sees, and left the clergy as

[020-149]practically dependent on free-will offerings, as if their Cathedrals or

[020-150]Convents had never been endowed with an acre, a mill, a ferry, or a

[020-151]fishery. The free offerings were, however, always generous, and

[020-152]sometimes munificent. When Celsus, on his elevation to the Primacy,

[020-153]made a tour of the southern half-kingdom, he received "seven cows and

[020-154]seven sheep, and half an ounce of silver from every cantred [hundred]

[020-155]in Munster." The bequests were also a fruitful source of revenue to the

[020-156]principal foundations; of the munificence of the monarchs we may form

[020-157]some opinion by what has been already recorded of the gifts left to

[020-158]churches by Thorlogh More O'Conor.

[020-159]The power of the clerical order, in these ages of Pagan warfare, had

[020-160]very far declined from what it was, when Adamnan caused the law to be

[020-161]enacted to prevent women going to battle, when Moling obtained the

[020-162]abolition of the Leinster tribute, and Columbkill the recognition of

[020-163]Scottish independence. Truces made in the presence of the highest

[020-164]dignitaries, and sworn to on the most sacred relics, were frequently

[020-165]violated, and often with impunity. Neither excommunication nor public

[020-166]penance were latterly inflicted as an atonement for such perjury: a

[020-167]fine or offering to the Church was the easy and only mulct on the

[020-168]offender. When we see the safeguard of the Bishop of Cork so flagrantly

[020-169]disregarded by the assassins of Mahon, son of Kennedy, and the solemn

[020-170]peace of the year 1094 so readily broken by two such men as the Princes

[020-171]of the North and the South, we need no other proofs of the decadence of

[020-172]the spiritual authority in that age of Irish history.

[020-173]And the morals of private life tell the same sad tale. The facility

[020-174]with which the marriage tie was contracted and dissolved is the

[020-175]strongest evidence of this degeneracy. The worst examples were set in

[020-176]the highest stations, for it is no uncommon incident, from the ninth

[020-177]century downwards, to find our Princes with more than one wife living,

[020-178]and the repudiated wife married again to a person of equal or superior

[020-179]rank. We have the authority of Saint Anselm and Saint Bernard, for the

[020-180]existence of grave scandal and irregularities of life among the clergy,

[020-181]and we can well believe that it needed a generation of Bishops, with

[020-182]all the authority and all the courage of Saint Celsus, Saint Malachy,

[020-183]and Saint Lawrence, to rescue from ruin a Priesthood and a people, so

[020-184]far fallen from the bright example of their ancestors. That the

[020-185]reaction towards a better life had strongly set in, under their

[020-186]guidance, we may infer from the horror with which, in the third quarter

[020-187]of the twelfth century, the elopement of Dermid and Dervorgoil was

[020-188]regarded by both Princes and People. A hundred years earlier, that

[020-189]event would have been hardly noticed in the general disregard of the

[020-190]marriage tie, but the frequent Synods, and the holy lives of the

[020-191]reforming Bishops, had already revived the zeal that precedes and

[020-192]ensures reformation.

[020-193]Primate Malachy died at Clairvaulx, in the arms of Saint Bernard, in

[020-194]the year 1148, after having been fourteen years Archbishop of Armagh

[020-195]and ten years Bishop of Down and Conor. His episcopal life, therefore,

[020-196]embraced the history of that remarkable second quarter of the century,

[020-197]in which the religious reaction fought its first battles against the

[020-198]worst abuses. The attention of Saint Bernard, whose eyes nothing

[020-199]escaped, from Jerusalem to the farthest west, was drawn ten years

[020-200]before to the Isle of Saints, now, in truth, become an Isle of Sinners.

[020-201]The death of his friend, the Irish Primate, under his own roof, gave

[020-202]him a fitting occasion for raising his accusing voice—a voice that

[020-203]thrilled the Alps and filled the Vatican—against the fearful degeneracy

[020-204]of that once fruitful mother of holy men and women. The attention of

[020-205]Rome was thoroughly aroused, and immediately after the appearance of

[020-206]the Life of Saint Malachy, Pope Eugenius III.—himself a monk of

[020-207]Clairvaulx—despatched Cardinal Papiron, with legantine powers, to

[020-208]correct abuses, and establish a stricter discipline. After a tour of

[020-209]great part of the Island, the Legate, with whom was associated

[020-210]Gilla-Criost, or Christianus, Bishop of Lismore, called the great Synod

[020-211]of Kells, early in the year after his arrival (March, 1152), at which

[020-212]simony, usury, concubinage, and other abuses, were formally condemned,

[020-213]and tithes were first decreed to be paid to the secular clergy. Two new

[020-214]Archbishoprics, Dublin and Tuam, were added to Armagh and Cashel,

[020-215]though not without decided opposition from the Primates both of Leath

[020-216]Mogha and Leath Conn, backed by those stern conservatives of every

[020-217]national usage, the Abbots of the Columban Order. The _pallium_, or

[020-218]Roman cape, was, by this Legate, presented to each of the Archbishops,

[020-219]and a closer conformity with the Roman ritual was enacted. The four

[020-220]ecclesiastical Provinces thus created were in outline nearly identical

[020-221]with the four modern Provinces. Armagh was declared the metropolitan

[020-222]over all; Dublin, which had been a mere Danish borough-see, gained most

[020-223]in rank and influence by the new arrangement, as Glendalough, Ferns,

[020-224]Ossory, Kildare and Leighlin, were declared subject to its presidency.

[020-225]We must always bear in mind the picture drawn of the Irish Church by

[020-226]the inspired orator of Clairvaulx, when judging of the conduct of Pope

[020-227]Adrian IV., who, in the year 1155—the second of his Pontificate—granted

[020-228]to King Henry II. of England, then newly crowned, his Bull authorising

[020-229]the invasion of Ireland. The authenticity of that Bull is now

[020-230]universally admitted; and both its preamble and conditions show how

[020-231]strictly it was framed in accordance with St. Bernard's accusation. It

[020-232]sets forth that for the eradication of vice, the implanting of virtue,

[020-233]and the spread of the true faith, the Holy Father solemnly sanctions

[020-234]the projected invasion; and it attaches as a condition, the payment of

[020-235]Peter's pence, for every house in Ireland. The bearer of the Bull, John

[020-236]of Salisbury, carried back from Rome a gold ring, set with an emerald

[020-237]stone, as a token of Adrian's friendship, or it may be, his

[020-238]subinfeudation of Henry. As a title, however powerless in modern times

[020-239]such a Bull might prove, it was a formidable weapon of invasion with a

[020-240]Catholic people, in the twelfth century. We have mainly referred to it

[020-241]here, however, as an illustration of how entirely St. Bernard's

[020-242]impeachment of the Irish Church and nation was believed at Rome, even

[020-243]after the salutary decrees of the Synod of Kells had been promulgated.

[020-244]The restoration of religion, which was making such rapid progress

[020-245]previous to the Norman invasion, was accompanied by a relative revival

[020-246]of learning. The dark ages of Ireland are not those of the rest of

[020-247]Europe—they extend from the middle of the ninth century to the age of

[020-248]Brian and Malachy II. This darkness came from the North, and cleared

[020-249]away rapidly after the eventful day of Clontarf. The first and most

[020-250]natural direction which the revival took was historical investigation,

[020-251]and the composition of Annals. Of these invaluable records, the two of

[020-252]highest reputation are those of Tigernach (Tiernan) O'Broin, brought

[020-253]down to the year of his own death, A.D. 1088, and the chronicle of

[020-254]Marianus Scotus, who died at Mentz, A.D. 1086. Tiernan was abbot of

[020-255]Clonmacnoise, and Marian is thought to have been a monk of that

[020-256]monastery, as he speaks of a superior called Tigernach, under whom he

[020-257]had lived in Ireland. Both these learned men quote accurately the works

[020-258]of foreign writers; both give the dates of eclipses, in connection with

[020-259]historical events for several centuries before their own time; both

[020-260]show a familiarity with Greek and Latin authors. _Marianus_ is the

[020-261]first writer by whom the name _Scotia Minor_ was given to the Gaelic

[020-262]settlement in Caledonia, and his chronicle was an authority mainly

[020-263]relied on in the disputed Scottish succession in the time of Edward I.

[020-264]of England. With _Tigernach_, he may be considered the founder of the

[020-265]school of Irish Annalists, which flourished in the shelter of the great

[020-266]monasteries, such as Innisfallen, Boyle and Multifernan; and culminated

[020-267]in the great compilation made by "the Four Masters" in the Abbey of

[020-268]Donegal.

[020-269]Of the Gaelic metrical chroniclers, Flann of the Monastery, and

[020-270]Gilla-Coeman; of the Bards McLiag and McCoisse; of the learned

[020-271]professors and lectors of Lismore and Armagh—now restored for a season

[020-272]to studious days and peaceful nights, we must be content with the

[020-273]mention of their names. Of Lismore, after its restoration, an old

[020-274]British writer has left us this pleasant and happy picture. "It is," he

[020-275]says, "a famous and holy city, half of which is an asylum, into which

[020-276]no woman dares enter; but it is full of cells and monasteries; and

[020-277]religious men in great abundance abide there."

[020-278]Such was the promise of better days, which cheered the hopes of the

[020-279]Pastors of the Irish, when the twelfth century had entered on its third

[020-280]quarter. The pious old Gaelic proverb, which says, "on the Cross the

[020-281]face of Christ was looking westwards—," was again on the lips and in

[020-282]the hearts of men, and though much remained to be done, much had been

[020-283]already done, and done under difficulties greater than any that

[020-284]remained to conquer.
