## Overview

Henry II (1133–1189), the first Plantagenet King of England, was the monarch whose intervention in Ireland in 1171–1172 initiated the Anglo-Norman presence that would last for over seven centuries. Coming to Ireland ostensibly to restrain the ambitions of his own Norman barons led by [[strongbow|Richard de Clare]] ([[strongbow|Strongbow]]), Henry established English royal authority over large parts of the island and secured the submission of many Irish kings. (026-009)

His expedition was the culmination of a process set in motion by the deposed Irish king [[dermid-mcmurrogh|Dermid McMurrogh]], whose appeal for Norman military assistance in 1169 brought the first bands of Norman adventurers to Irish shores. Henry's arrival transformed a private military venture into a royal enterprise. (026-015) (026-029)

## Biography

### Background and the Norman Invasion

The Anglo-Norman involvement in Ireland began in 1169 when [[dermid-mcmurrogh|Dermid McMurrogh]] (Diarmait Mac Murchada), the deposed [[dermid-mcmurrogh|King of Leinster]], enlisted Norman-Welsh mercenaries to recover his kingdom. The Normans, led initially by Robert FitzStephen and later by [[strongbow|Richard de Clare]], known as [[strongbow|Strongbow]], quickly established themselves in [[leinster|Leinster]]. When [[strongbow|Strongbow]] succeeded Dermid as Lord of [[leinster|Leinster]], Henry became alarmed at the prospect of an independent Norman state in Ireland. (026-011)

### Arrival in Ireland

In October 1171, Henry II landed at Waterford with a large army—the first English king to set foot on Irish soil. [[strongbow|Strongbow]], alarmed by Henry's proclamation, repaired to England and submitted to the king. Henry was now determined to assert royal control over the Norman adventurers and the Irish rulers alike. (026-009) (026-021)

Henry's policy was one of strategic control rather than wholesale conquest. He received the submission of many Irish kings, including the chiefs of the south and east, who saw in the distant English king a preferable overlord to their immediate Irish rivals or the aggressive Norman barons.

### The Council of Cashel and the Grant of Ireland

During his stay in Ireland, Henry convened a council of the Irish Church at Cashel in 1172, which endorsed ecclesiastical reforms aligning the Irish Church more closely with Roman practices. The Pope, Adrian IV (the only English Pope), had previously issued the bull *Laudabiliter*, granting Henry lordship over Ireland on condition of reforming the Irish Church.

Henry left Ireland in April 1172, having spent approximately six months on the island. Before departing, he granted the Kingdom of Meath to [[hugh-de-lacy|Hugh de Lacy]] and established a rudimentary administration. Though he never returned, his brief expedition had lasting consequences. (026-065)

### The Treaty of Windsor

In 1175, Henry concluded the Treaty of Windsor with Rory O'Conor (Ruaidrí Ua Conchobair), the High King of Ireland. Under its terms, Rory was recognized as King of Connacht and acknowledged as overlord of the unconquered Irish territories, while Henry was acknowledged as Lord of the conquered lands. The treaty proved impractical, and Norman expansion continued regardless of its terms.

## Legacy

Henry II's intervention in Ireland inaugurated over 750 years of English and British involvement in Irish affairs. The Lordship of Ireland, which he established, passed to his younger son John and became a permanent feature of the English crown. The Anglo-Norman settlers became the Old English of Ireland, eventually producing families—the FitzGeralds, Butlers, de Burghs—that became more Irish than the Irish themselves.

His brief visit set in motion the long and often tragic history of English-Irish relations. The legal and political structures established under his authority laid the groundwork for centuries of contested sovereignty over the island.
