CHAPTER VII.

[029-001]ASSASSINATION OF HUGH DE LACY—JOHN "LACKLAND" IN IRELAND—VARIOUS

[029-002]EXPEDITIONS OF JOHN DE COURCY—DEATH OF CONOR MOINMOY, AND RISE OF

[029-003]CATHAL, "THE RED-HANDED" O'CONOR—CLOSE OF THE CAREER OF DE COURCY AND

[029-004]DE BURGH.

[029-005]Hugh de Lacy, restored to the supreme authority on the recall of

[029-006]Fitz-Aldelm in 1179, began to conceive hopes, as Strongbow had done, of

[029-007]carving out for himself a new kingdom. After the assassination of

[029-008]O'Ruarc already related, he assumed without further parley the titles

[029-009]of Lord of Meath and Breffni. To these titles, he added that of Oriel

[029-010]or Louth, but his real strength lay in Meath, where his power was

[029-011]enhanced by a politic second marriage with Rose, daughter of O'Conor.

[029-012]Among the Irish he now began to be known as King of the foreigners, and

[029-013]some such assumption of royal authority caused his recall for a few

[029-014]months in the year 1180, and his substitution by de Courcy and Philip

[029-015]de Broasa, in 1184. But his great qualities caused his restoration a

[029-016]third time to the rank of Justiciary for Henry, or Deputy for John,

[029-017]whose title of "Lord of Ireland" was bestowed by his father, at a

[029-018]Parliament held at Oxford, in 1177.

[029-019]This founder of the Irish de Lacys is described by _Giraldus_, who knew

[029-020]him personally, as a man of Gallic sobriety, ambitious, avaricious, and

[029-021]lustful, of small stature, and deformed shape, with repulsive features,

[029-022]and dark, deep-set eyes. By the Irish of the midland districts he was

[029-023]bitterly detested as a sacrilegious spoiler of their churches and

[029-024]monasteries, and the most powerful among their invaders. The murder of

[029-025]O'Ruarc, whose title of Breffni he had usurped, was attributed to a

[029-026]deep-laid design; he certainly shared the odium with the advantage that

[029-027]ensued from it. Nor was his own end unlike that of his rival. Among

[029-028]other sites for castles, he had chosen the foundations of the ancient

[029-029]and much venerated monastery of Durrow, planted by Columbcille, seven

[029-030]centuries before, in the midst of the fertile region watered by the

[029-031]Brosna. This act of profanity was fated to be his last, for, while

[029-032]personally superintending the work, O'Meyey, a young man of good birth,

[029-033]and foster-brother to a neighbouring chief of Teffia, known as

[029-034]_Sionnach_, or "the Fox," struck off his head with a single blow of his

[029-035]axe and escaped into the neighbouring forest of Kilclare during the

[029-036]confusion which ensued. De Lacy left issue—two sons, Hugh and Walter,

[029-037]by his first wife, and a third, William _Gorm_, by his second—of whom,

[029-038]and of their posterity, we shall have many occasions to make mention.

[029-039]In one of the intervals of de Lacy's disfavour, Prince John, surnamed

[029-040]_Sans-terre_, or "lack-land," was sent over by his father to strengthen

[029-041]the English interest in Ireland. He arrived in Waterford, accompanied

[029-042]by a fleet of sixty ships, on the last of March, 1185, and remained in

[029-043]the country till the following November. If anything could excuse the

[029-044]levity, folly and misconduct of the Prince on this expedition, it would

[029-045]be his youth;—he was then only eighteen. But Henry had taken every

[029-046]precaution to ensure success to his favourite son. He was preceded into

[029-047]Ireland by Archbishop Cuming, the English successor of St. Laurence;

[029-048]the learned Glanville was his legal adviser; John de Courcy was his

[029-049]lieutenant, and the eloquent, but passionate and partial _Giraldus

[029-050]Cambrensis_, his chaplain and tutor. He had, however, other companions

[029-051]more congenial to his age and temper, young noblemen as froward and as

[029-052]extravagant as himself; yet, as he surpassed them all in birth and

[029-053]rank, so he did in wickedness and cruelty of disposition. For age he

[029-054]had no reverence, for virtue no esteem, neither truth towards man, nor

[029-055]decency towards woman. On his arrival at Waterford, the new Archbishop

[029-056]of Dublin, John de Courcy, and the principal Norman nobles, hastened to

[029-057]receive him. With them came also certain Leinster chiefs, desiring to

[029-058]live at peace with the new Galls. When, according to the custom of the

[029-059]country, the chiefs advanced to give John the kiss of peace, their

[029-060]venerable age was made a mockery by the young Prince, who met their

[029-061]proffered salutations by plucking at their beards. This appears to have

[029-062]been as deadly an insult to the Irish as it is to the Asiatics, and the

[029-063]deeply offended guests instantly quitted Waterford. Other follies and

[029-064]excesses rapidly transpired, and the native nobles began to discover

[029-065]that a royal army encumbered, rather than led by such a Prince, was not

[029-066]likely to prove itself invincible. In an idle parade from the Suir to

[029-067]the Liffey, from the Liffey to the Boyne, and in issuing orders for the

[029-068]erection of castles, (some of which are still correctly and others

[029-069]erroneously called King John's Castles,) the campaign months of the

[029-070]year were wasted by the King of England's son. One of these castles, to

[029-071]which most importance was attached, Ardfinan on the Suir, was no sooner

[029-072]built than taken by Donald More O'Brien, on midsummer day, when four

[029-073]knights and its other defenders were slain. Another was rising at

[029-074]Lismore, on the Blackwater, under the guardianship of Robert Barry, one

[029-075]of the brood of Nesta, when it was attacked and Barry slain. Other

[029-076]knights and castellans were equally unfortunate; Raymond Fitz-Hugh fell

[029-077]at Leighlin, another Raymond in Idrone, and Roger le Poer in Ossory. In

[029-078]Desmond, Cormac McCarthy besieged Theobald, ancestor of the Butlers in

[029-079]Cork, but this brave Prince—the worthy compeer of O'Brien—was cut off

[029-080]"in a parlee by them of Cork." The Clan-Colman, or O'Melaghlins, had

[029-081]risen in West-Meath to reclaim their own, when Henry, not an hour too

[029-082]soon, recalled his reckless son, and entrusted, for the last time, the

[029-083]command to Hugh de Lacy, whose fate has been already related.

[029-084]In the fluctuations of the power of the invaders after the death of de

[029-085]Lacy, and during the next reign in England, one steadfast name appears

[029-086]foremost among the adventurers—that of the gallant giant, de Courcy,

[029-087]the conqueror of the Ards of Down. Not only in prowess, but also in

[029-088]piety, he was the model of all the knighthood of his time. We are told

[029-089]that he always carried about his person a copy of the prophecies

[029-090]attributed to Columbcille, and when, in the year 1186, the relics of

[029-091]the three great saints, whose dust sanctifies Downpatrick, were

[029-092]supposed to be discovered by the Bishop of Down in a dream, he caused

[029-093]them to be translated to the altar-side with all suitable reverence.

[029-094]Yet all his devotions and pilgrimages did not prevent him from pushing

[029-095]on the work of conquest whenever occasion offered. His plantation in

[029-096]Down had time to take root from the unexpected death of Donald, Prince

[029-097]of Aileach, in an encounter with the garrison of one of the new

[029-098]castles, near Newry. (A.D. 1188.) The same year he took up the

[029-099]enterprise against Connaught, in which Milo de Cogan had so signally

[029-100]failed, and from which even de Lacy had, for reasons of his own,

[029-101]refrained. The feuds of the O'Conor family were again the pretext and

[029-102]the ground of hope with the invaders, but Donald More O'Brien,

[029-103]victorious on the Suir and the Shannon, carried his strong succours to

[029-104]Conor _Moinmoy_ on the banks of the Suca, near the present Ballinasloe,

[029-105]and both powers combined marched against de Courcy. Unprepared for this

[029-106]junction, the Norman retreated towards Sligo, and had reached

[029-107]Ballysadare, when Flaherty, Lord of Tyrconnell (Donegal), came against

[029-108]them from the opposite point, and thus placed between two fires, they

[029-109]were forced to fly through the rugged passes of the Curlieu mountains,

[029-110]skirmishing as they went. The only incidents which signalized this

[029-111]campaign on their side was the burning of Ballysadare and the plunder

[029-112]of Armagh; to the Irish it was creditable for the combinations it

[029-113]occasioned. It is cheering in the annals of those desultory wars to

[029-114]find a national advantage gained by the joint action of a Munster, a

[029-115]Connaught, and an Ulster force.

[029-116]The promise of national unity held out by the alliance of O'Brien and

[029-117]O'Conor, in the years 1188-'89, had been followed up by the adhesion of

[029-118]the lords of Breffni, Ulidia, or Down, the chiefs of the Clan-Colman,

[029-119]and McCarthy, Prince of Desmond. But the assassination of Conor

[029-120]Moinmoy, by the partizans of his cousins, extinguished the hopes of the

[029-121]country, and the peace of his own province. The old family feuds broke

[029-122]out with new fury. In vain the aged Roderick emerged from his convent,

[029-123]and sought with feeble hand to curb the fiery passions of his tribe; in

[029-124]vain the Archbishops of Armagh and of Tuam interposed their spiritual

[029-125]authority, A series of fratricidal contests, for which history has no

[029-126]memory and no heart, were fought out between the warring branches of

[029-127]the family during the last ten years of the century, until by virtue of

[029-128]the strong-arm, Cathal _Crovdearg_, son of Turlogh More, and younger

[029-129]brother of Roderick, assumed the sovereignty of Connaught about the

[029-130]year 1200.

[029-131]In the twelve years which intervened between the death of _Moinmoy_ and

[029-132]the establishment of the power of Cathal _Crovdearg_ O'Conor, the

[029-133]Normans had repeated opportunities for intervention in the affairs of

[029-134]Connaught. William de Burgh, a powerful Baron of the family of

[029-135]Fitz-Aldelm, the former Lord Justice, sided with the opponents of

[029-136]Cathal, while de Courcy, and subsequently the younger de Lacy, fought

[029-137]on his side. Once at least these restless Barons changed allies, and

[029-138]fought as desperately against their former candidate for the succession

[029-139]as they had before fought for him. In one of these engagements, the

[029-140]date assigned to which is the year 1190, Sir Armoric St. Laurence,

[029-141]founder of the Howth family, at the head of a numerous division, is

[029-142]said to have been cut off with all his troop. But the fortune of war

[029-143]frequently shifted during the contest. In the year 1199, Cathal

[029-144]_Crovdearg_, with his allies de Lacy and de Courcy, was utterly

[029-145]defeated at Kilmacduagh, in the present county of Galway, and were it

[029-146]not that the rival O'Conor was sorely defeated, and trodden to death in

[029-147]the route which ensued, three years later, Connaught might never have

[029-148]known the vigorous administration of her "red-handed" hero.

[029-150]The early career of this able and now triumphant Prince, as preserved

[029-151]to us by history and tradition, is full of romantic incidents. He is

[029-152]said to have been born out of wedlock, and that his mother, while

[029-153]pregnant of him, was subject to all the cruel persecutions and magical

[029-154]torments the jealous wife of his father could invent. No sooner was he

[029-155]born than he became an object of hatred to the Queen, so that mother

[029-156]and child, after being concealed for three years in the sanctuaries of

[029-157]Connaught, had to fly for their lives into Leinster. In this exile,

[029-158]though early informed of his origin, he was brought up among the

[029-159]labourers in the field, and was actually engaged, sickle in hand,

[029-160]cutting the harvest, when a travelling _Bollscaire_, or newsman from

[029-161]the west, related the events which enabled him to return to his native

[029-162]province. "Farewell sickle," he exclaimed, casting it from him—"now for

[029-163]the sword." Hence "Cathal's farewell to the rye" was long a proverbial

[029-164]expression for any sudden change of purpose or of condition. Fortune

[029-165]seems to have favoured him in most of his undertakings. In a storm upon

[029-166]Lough Ree, when a whole fleet foundered and its warrior crew perished,

[029-167]he was one of seven who were saved. Though in some of his early battles

[029-168]unsuccessful, he always recovered his ground, kept up his alliances,

[029-169]and returned to the contest. After the death of the celebrated Donald

[029-170]More O'Brien (A.D. 1194), he may certainly be considered the first

[029-171]soldier and first diplomatist among the Irish. Nor was his lot cast on

[029-172]more favoured days, nor was he pitted against less able men than those

[029-173]with whom the brave King of Munster—the stoutest defender of his

[029-174]fatherland—had so honourably striven. Fortunate it was for the renown

[029-175]of the Gael, that as one star of the race set over Thomond, another of

[029-176]equal brilliancy rose to guide them in the west.

[029-177]With the end of the century, the career of Cathal's allies, de Courcy

[029-178]and de Burgh, may be almost said to have ended. The obituary of the

[029-179]latter bears the date of 1204. He had obtained large grants from King

[029-180]John of lands in Connaught—if he could conquer them—which his vigorous

[029-181]descendants, the Burkes of Clanrickarde, did their best to accomplish.

[029-182]De Courcy, warring with the sons of de Lacy, and seeking refuge among

[029-183]the clansmen of Tyrone, disappears from the stage of Irish affairs. He

[029-184]is said to have passed on to England, and ended his days in prison, a

[029-185]victim to the caprice or jealousy of King John. Many tales are told of

[029-186]his matchless intrepidity. His indirect descendants, the Barons of

[029-187]Kinsale, claim the right to wear their hats before the King in

[029-188]consequence of one of these legends, which represents him as the

[029-189]champion Knight of England, taken from, a dungeon to uphold her honour

[029-190]against a French challenger. Other tales as ill authenticated are

[029-191]founded on his career, which, however, in its literal truth, is

[029-192]unexcelled for hardihood and adventure, except, perhaps, by the

[029-193]cotemporaneous story of the lion-hearted Richard, whom he closely

[029-194]resembled. The title of Earl of Ulster, created for de Courcy in 1181,

[029-195]was transferred in 1205, by royal patent, to Walter de Lacy, whose only

[029-196]daughter Maud brought it in the year 1264 to Walter de Burgh, lord of

[029-197]Connaught, from whose fourth female descendant it passed in 1354, by

[029-198]her marriage with Lionel, Duke of Clarence, into the royal family of

[029-199]England.
