CHAPTER V.

[005-001]REIGN OF HUGH II.—THE IRISH COLONY IN SCOTLAND OBTAINS ITS

[005-002]INDEPENDENCE.

[005-003]Twenty-seven years is a long reign, and the years of King-Hugh II. were

[005-004]marked with striking events. One religious and one political

[005-005]occurrence, however, threw all others into the shade—the conversion of

[005-006]the Highlands and Islands of Scotland (then called Alba or Albyn by the

[005-007]Gael, and Caledonia by the Latins), and the formal recognition, after

[005-008]an exciting controversy, of the independence of the Milesian colony in

[005-009]Scotland. These events follow each other in the order of time, and

[005-010]stand partly in the relation of cause and effect.

[005-011]The first authentic Irish immigration into Scotland seems to have taken

[005-012]place about the year of our Lord 258. The pioneers crossed over from

[005-013]Antrim to Argyle, where the strait is less than twenty-five miles wide.

[005-014]Other adventurers followed at intervals, but it is a fact to be

[005-015]deplored, that no passages in our own, and in all other histories, have

[005-016]been so carelessly kept as the records of emigration. The movements of

[005-017]rude masses of men, the first founders of states and cities, are

[005-018]generally lost in obscurity, or misrepresented by patriotic zeal.

[005-019]Several successive settlements of the Irish in Caledonia can be faintly

[005-020]traced from the middle of the third till the beginning of the sixth

[005-021]century. About the year 503, they had succeeded in establishing a

[005-022]flourishing principality among the cliffs and glens of Argyle. The

[005-023]limits of their first territory cannot be exactly laid down; but it

[005-024]soon spread north into Rosshire, and east into the present county of

[005-025]Perth. It was a land of stormy friths and fissured headlands, of deep

[005-026]defiles and snowy summits. "'Tis a far cry to Lough Awe," is still a

[005-027]lowland proverb, and Lough Awe was in the very heart of that old Irish

[005-028]settlement.

[005-029]The earliest emigrants to Argyle were Pagans, while the latter were

[005-030]Christians, and were accompanied by priests, and a bishop, Kieran, the

[005-031]son of the carpenter, whom, from his youthful piety and holy life, as

[005-032]well as from the occupation followed by his father, is sometimes

[005-033]fancifully compared to our Lord and Saviour himself. Parishes in

[005-034]Cantyre, in Islay, and in Carrick, still bear the name of St. Kieran as

[005-035]patron. But no systematic attempt—none at least of historic memory—was

[005-036]made to convert the remoter Gael and the other races then inhabiting

[005-037]Alba—the Picts, Britons, and Scandinavians, until the year of our era,

[005-038]565, Columba or COLUMBKILL, a Bishop of the royal race of Nial,

[005-039]undertook that task, on a scale commensurate with its magnitude. This

[005-040]celebrated man has always ranked with Saint Patrick and Saint Bridget

[005-041]as the most glorious triad of the Irish Calendar. He was, at the time

[005-042]he left Ireland, in the prime of life—his 44th year. Twelve companions,

[005-043]the apostolic number, accompanied him on his voyage. For thirty-four

[005-044]years he was the legislator and captain of Christianity in those

[005-045]northern regions. The King of the Picts received baptism at his hands;

[005-046]the Kings of the Scottish colony, his kinsmen, received the crown from

[005-047]him on their accession. The islet of I., or Iona, as presented to him

[005-048]by one of these princes. Here he and his companions built with their

[005-049]own hands their parent-house, and from this Hebridean rock in after

[005-050]times was shaped the destinies, spiritual and temporal, of many tribes

[005-051]and kingdoms.

[005-052]The growth of Iona was as the growth of the grain of mustard seed

[005-053]mentioned in the Gospel, even during the life of its founder. Formed by

[005-054]his teaching and example, there went out from it apostles to Iceland,

[005-055]to the Orkneys, to Northumbria, to Man, and to South Britain. A hundred

[005-056]monasteries in Ireland looked to that exiled saint as their patriarch.

[005-057]His rule of monastic life, adopted either from the far East, from the

[005-058]recluses of the Thebaid, or from his great contemporary, Saint

[005-059]Benedict, was sought for by Chiefs, Bards, and converted Druids.

[005-060]Clients, seeking direction from his wisdom, or protection through his

[005-061]power, were constantly arriving and departing from his sacred isle. His

[005-062]days were divided between manual labour and the study and transcribing

[005-063]of the Sacred Scriptures. He and his disciples, says the Venerable

[005-064]Bede, in whose age Iona still flourished, "neither thought of nor loved

[005-065]anything in _this_ world." Some writers have represented Columbkill's

[005-066]_Culdees_, (which in English means simply "Servants of God,") as a

[005-067]married clergy; so far is this from the truth, that we now know, no

[005-068]woman was allowed to land on the island, nor even a cow to be kept

[005-069]there, for, said the holy Bishop, "wherever there is a cow there will

[005-070]be a woman, and wherever there is a woman there will be mischief."

[005-071]In the reign of King Hugh, three domestic questions arose of great

[005-072]importance; one was the refusal of the Prince of Ossory to pay tribute

[005-073]to the Monarch; the other, the proposed extinction of the Bardic Order,

[005-074]and the third, the attempt to tax the Argyle Colony. The question

[005-075]between Ossory and Tara, we may pass over as of obsolete interest, but

[005-076]the other two deserve fuller mention:

[005-077]The Bards—who were the Editors, Professors, Registrars and

[005-078]Record-keepers—the makers and masters of public opinion in those days,

[005-079]had reached in this reign a number exceeding 1,200 in Meath and Ulster

[005-080]alone. They claimed all the old privileges of free quarters on their

[005-081]travels and freeholdings at home, which were freely granted to their

[005-082]order when it was in its infancy. Those chieftains who refused them

[005-083]anything, however extravagant, they lampooned and libelled, exciting

[005-084]their own people and other princes against them. Such was their

[005-085]audacity, that some of them are said to have demanded from King Hugh

[005-086]the royal brooch, one of the most highly prized heirlooms of the

[005-087]reigning family. Twice in the early part of this reign they had been

[005-088]driven from the royal residence, and obliged to take refuge in the

[005-089]little principality of Ulidia (or Down); the third time the monarch had

[005-090]sworn to expel them utterly from the kingdom. In Columbkill, however,

[005-091]they were destined to find a most powerful mediator, both from his

[005-092]general sympathy with the Order, being himself no mean poet, and from

[005-093]the fact that the then Arch-Poet, or chief of the order, Dallan

[005-094]Forgaill, was one of his own pupils.

[005-095]To settle this vexed question of the Bards, as well as to obtain the

[005-096]sanction of the estates to the taxation of Argyle, King Hugh called a

[005-097]General Assembly in the year 590. The place of meeting was no longer

[005-098]the interdicted Tara, but for the monarch's convenience a site farther

[005-099]north was chosen—the hill of Drom-Keth, in the present county of Derry.

[005-100]Here came in rival state and splendour the Princes of the four

[005-101]Provinces, and other principal chieftains. The dignitaries of the

[005-102]Church also attended, and an occasional Druid was perhaps to be seen in

[005-103]the train of some unconverted Prince. The pretensions of the

[005-104]mother-country to impose a tax upon her Colony, were sustained by the

[005-105]profound learning and venerable name of St. Colman, Bishop of Dromore,

[005-106]one of the first men of his Order.

[005-107]When Columbkill "heard of the calling together of that General

[005-108]Assembly," and of the questions to be there decided, he resolved to

[005-109]attend, notwithstanding the stern vow of his earlier life, never to

[005-110]look on Irish soil again. Under a scruple of this kind, he is said to

[005-111]have remained blindfold, from his arrival in his fatherland, till his

[005-112]return to Iona. He was accompanied by an imposing train of attendants;

[005-113]by Aidan, Prince of Argyle, so deeply interested in the issue, and a

[005-114]suite of over one hundred persons, twenty of them Abbots or Bishops.

[005-115]Columbkill spoke for his companions; for already, as in Bede's time,

[005-116]the Abbots of Iona exercised over all the clergy north of the Humber,

[005-117]but still more directly north of the Tweed, a species of supremacy

[005-118]similar to that which the successors of St. Benedict and St. Bernard

[005-119]exercised, in turn, over Prelates and Princes on the European

[005-120]Continent.

[005-121]When the Assembly was opened the holy Bishop of Dromore stated the

[005-122]arguments in favour of Colonial taxation with learning and effect. Hugh

[005-123]himself impeached the Bards for their licentious and lawless lives.

[005-124]Columbkill defended both interests, and, by combining both, probably

[005-125]strengthened the friends of each. It is certain that he carried the

[005-126]Assembly with him, both against the monarch and those of the resident

[005-127]clergy, who had selected Colman as their spokesman. The Bardic Order

[005-128]was spared. The doctors, or master-singers among them, were prohibited

[005-129]from wandering from place to place; they were assigned residence with

[005-130]the chiefs and princes; their losel attendants were turned over to

[005-131]honest pursuits, and thus a great danger was averted, and one of the

[005-132]most essential of the Celtic institutions being reformed and regulated,

[005-133]was preserved. Scotland and Ireland have good reason to be grateful to

[005-134]the founder of Iona, for the interposition that preserved to us the

[005-135]music, which is now admitted to be one of the most precious

[005-136]inheritances of both countries.

[005-137]The proposed taxation Columbkill strenuously and successfully resisted.

[005-138]Up to this time, the colonists had been bound only to furnish a

[005-139]contingent force, by land and sea, when the King of Ireland went to

[005-140]war, and to make them an annual present called "chief-rent."

[005-141]From the Book of Rights we learn that (at least at the time the

[005-142]existing transcript was made) the Scottish Princes paid out of Alba,

[005-143]seven shields, seven steeds, seven bondswomen, seven bondsmen, and

[005-144]seven hounds all of the same breed. But the "chief-rent," or "eric for

[005-145]kindly blood," did not suffice in the year 590 to satisfy King Hugh.

[005-146]The colony had grown great, and, like some modern monarchs, he proposed

[005-147]to make it pay for its success. Columbkill, though a native of Ireland,

[005-148]and a prince of its reigning house, was by choice a resident of

[005-149]Caledonia, and he stood true to his adopted country. The Irish King

[005-150]refused to continue the connection on the old conditions, and declared

[005-151]his intention to visit Alba himself to enforce the tribute due;

[005-152]Columbkill, rising in the Assembly, declared the Albanians "for ever

[005-153]free from the yoke," and this, adds an old historian, "turned out to be

[005-154]the fact." From the whole controversy we may conclude that Scotland

[005-155]never paid political tribute to Ireland; that their relation was that

[005-156]rather of allies, than of sovereign and vassal; that it resembled more

[005-157]the homage Carthage paid to Tyre, and Syracuse to Corinth, than any

[005-158]modern form of colonial dependence; that a federal connection existed

[005-159]by which, in time of war, the Scots of Argyle, and those of Hibernia,

[005-160]were mutually bound to aid, assist, and defend each other. And this

[005-161]natural and only connection, founded in the blood of both nations,

[005-162]sanctioned by their early saints, confirmed by frequent intermarriage,

[005-163]by a common language and literature, and by hostility to common

[005-164]enemies, the Saxons, Danes, and Normans, grew into a political bond of

[005-165]unusual strength, and was cherished with affection by both nations,

[005-166]long ages after the magnates assembled at Drom-Keth had disappeared in

[005-167]the tombs of their fathers.

[005-168]The only unsettled question which remained after the Assembly at

[005-169]Drom-Keth related to the Prince of Ossory. Five years afterwards (A.D.

[005-170]595), King Hugh fell in an attempt to collect the special tribute from

[005-171]all Leinster, of which we have already heard something, and shall, by

[005-172]and by, hear more. He was an able and energetic ruler, and we may be

[005-173]sure "did not let the sun rise on him in his bed at Tara," or anywhere

[005-174]else. In his time great internal changes were taking place in the state

[005-175]of society. The ecclesiastical order had become more powerful than any

[005-176]other in the state. The Bardic Order, thrice proscribed, were finally

[005-177]subjected to the laws, over which they had at one time insolently

[005-178]domineered. Ireland's only colony—unless we except the immature

[005-179]settlement in the Isle of Man, under Cormac Longbeard—was declared

[005-180]independent of the parent country, through the moral influence of its

[005-181]illustrious Apostle, whose name many of its kings and nobles were of

[005-182]old proud to bear—_Mal-Colm_, meaning "servant of Columb," or

[005-183]Columbkill. But the memory of the sainted statesman who decreed the

[005-184]separation of the two populations, so far as claims to taxation could

[005-185]be preferred, preserved, for ages, the better and far more profitable

[005-186]alliance, of an ancient friendship, unbroken by a single national

[005-187]quarrel during a thousand years.

[005-189]A few words more on the death and character of this celebrated man,

[005-190]whom we are now to part with at the close of the sixth, as we parted

[005-191]from Patrick at the close of the fifth century. His day of departure

[005-192]came in 596. Death found him at the ripe age of almost fourscore,

[005-193]_stylus_ in hand, toiling cheerfully over the vellum page. It was the

[005-194]last night of the week when the presentiment of his end came strongly

[005-195]upon him. "This day," he said to his disciple and successor, Dermid,

[005-196]"is called the day of rest, and such it will be for me, for it will

[005-197]finish my labours." Laying down the manuscript, he added, "let Baithen

[005-198]finish the rest." Just after Matins, on the Sunday morning, he

[005-199]peacefully passed away from the midst of his brethren.

[005-200]Of his tenderness, as well as energy of character, tradition, and his

[005-201]biographers have recorded many instances. Among others, his habit of

[005-202]ascending an eminence every evening at sunset, to look over towards the

[005-203]coast of his native land. The spot is called by the islanders to this

[005-204]day, "the place of the back turned upon Ireland." The fishermen of the

[005-205]Hebrides long believed they could see their saint flitting over the

[005-206]waves after every new storm, counting the islands to see if any of them

[005-207]had foundered. It must have been a loveable character of which such

[005-208]tales could be told and cherished from generation to generation.

[005-209]Both Education and Nature had well fitted Columbkill to the great task

[005-210]of adding another realm to the empire of Christendom. His princely

[005-211]birth gave him power over his own proud kindred; his golden eloquence

[005-212]and glowing verse—the fragments of which still move and delight the

[005-213]Gaelic scholar—gave him fame and weight in the Christian schools which

[005-214]had suddenly sprung up in every glen and island. As prince, he stood on

[005-215]equal terms with princes; as poet, he was affiliated to that

[005-216]all-powerful Bardic Order, before whose awful anger kings trembled, and

[005-217]warriors succumbed in superstitious dread. A spotless soul, a

[005-218]disciplined body, an indomitable energy, an industry that never

[005-219]wearied, a courage that never blanched, a sweetness and courtesy that

[005-220]won all hearts, a tenderness for others that contrasted strongly with

[005-221]his rigour towards himself—these were the secrets of the success of

[005-222]this eminent missionary—these were the miracles by which he

[005-223]accomplished the conversion of so many barbarous tribes and Pagan

[005-224]Princes.
