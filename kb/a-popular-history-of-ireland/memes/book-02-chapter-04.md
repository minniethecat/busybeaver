CHAPTER IV.

[012-001]KINGS OF THE TENTH CENTURY; NIAL IV.; DONOGH II.; CONGAL III.; DONALD

[012-002]IV.

[012-003]Nial IV. (surnamed _Black-Knee_) succeeded his father-in-law, Flan of

[012-004]the Shannon (A.D. 916), and in the third year of his reign fell in an

[012-005]assault on Dublin; Donogh II., son of Flan Siona, reigned for

[012-006]twenty-five years; Congal III. succeeded, and was slain in an ambush by

[012-007]the Dublin Danes, in the twelfth year of his reign (A.D. 956); Donald

[012-008]IV., in the twenty-fourth year of his reign, died at Armagh, (A.D.

[012-009]979); which four reigns bring us to the period of the accession of

[012-010]Malachy II. as _Ard-Righ_, and the entrance of Brian Boru, on the

[012-011]national stage, as King of Cashel, and competitor for the monarchy.

[012-012]The reign of Nial _Black-Knee_ was too brief to be memorable for any

[012-013]other event than his heroic death in battle. The Danes having recovered

[012-014]Dublin, and strengthened its defences, Nial, it is stated, was incited

[012-015]by his confessor, the Abbot of Bangor, to attempt their re-expulsion.

[012-016]Accordingly, in October, 919, he marched towards Dublin, with a

[012-017]numerous host; Conor, son of the late king and _Roydamna_; the lords of

[012-018]Ulidia (Down), Oriel (Louth), Breagh (East-Meath), and other chiefs,

[012-019]with their clans accompanying him. Sitrick and Ivar, sons of the first

[012-020]Danish leaders in Ireland, marched out to meet them, and near

[012-021]Rathfarnham, on the Dodder, a battle was fought, in which the Irish

[012-022]were utterly defeated and their monarch slain. This Nial left a son

[012-023]named Murkertach, who, according to the compact entered into between

[012-024]the Northern and Southern Hy-Nial, became the _Roydamna_ of the next

[012-025]reign, and the most successful leader against the Danes, since the time

[012-026]of Malachy I. He was the step-son of the poetic Lady Gormley, whose lot

[012-027]it was to have been married in succession to the King of Munster, the

[012-028]King of Leinster, and the Monarch. Her first husband was Cormac, son of

[012-029]Cuilenan, before he entered holy orders; her second, Kerball of

[012-030]Leinster, and her third, Nial _Black-Knee_. She was an accomplished

[012-031]poetess, besides being the daughter, wife, and mother of king's, yet

[012-032]after the death of Nial she "begged from door to door," and no one had

[012-033]pity on her fallen state. By what vices she had thus estranged from her

[012-034]every kinsman, and every dependent, we are left to imagine; but that

[012-035]such was her misfortune, at the time her brother was monarch, and her

[012-036]step-son successor, we learn from the annals, which record her penance

[012-037]and death, under the date of 948.

[012-038]The defeat sustained near Rathfarnham, by the late king, was amply

[012-039]avenged in the first year of the new _Ard-Righ_ (A.D. 920), when the

[012-040]Dublin Danes, having marched out, taken and burned Kells, in Meath,

[012-041]were on their return through the plain of Breagh, attacked and routed

[012-042]with unprecedented slaughter. "There fell of the nobles of the Norsemen

[012-043]here," say the old Annalists, "as many as fell of the nobles and

[012-044]plebeians of the Irish, at Ath-Cliath" (Dublin). The Northern Hydra,

[012-045]however, was not left headless. Godfrey, grandson of Ivar, and Tomar,

[012-046]son of Algi, took command at Dublin, and Limerick, infusing new life

[012-047]into the remnant of their race. The youthful son of the late king, soon

[012-048]after at the head of a strong force (A.D. 921), compelled Godfrey to

[012-049]retreat from Ulster, to his ships, and to return by sea to Dublin. This

[012-050]was Murkertach, fondly called by the elegiac Bards, "the Hector of the

[012-051]West," and for his heroic achievements, not undeserving to be named

[012-052]after the gallant defender of Troy. Murkertach first appears in our

[012-053]annals at the year 921, and disappears in the thick of the battle in

[012-054]938. His whole career covers seventeen years; his position throughout

[012-055]was subordinate and expectant—for King Donogh outlived his heir: but

[012-056]there are few names in any age of the history of his country more

[012-057]worthy of historical honour than his. While Donogh was king in name,

[012-058]Murkertach was king in fact; on him devolved the burden of every

[012-059]negotiation, and the brunt of every battle. Unlike his ancestor, Hugh

[012-060]of Aileach, in his opposition to Donogh's ancestor, Malachy I., he

[012-061]never attempts to counteract the king, or to harass him in his

[012-062]patrimony. He rather does what is right and needful himself, leaving

[012-063]Donogh to claim the credit, if he be so minded. True, a coolness and a

[012-064]quarrel arises between them, and even "a challenge of battle" is

[012-065]exchanged, but better councils prevail, peace is restored, and the king

[012-066]and the _Roydamna_ march as one man against the common enemy. It has

[012-067]been said of another but not wholly dissimilar form of government, that

[012-068]Crown-Princes are always in opposition; if this saying holds good of

[012-069]father and son, as occupant and expectant of a throne, how much more

[012-070]likely is it to be true of a successor and a principal, chosen from

[012-071]different dynasties, with a view to combine, or at worst to balance,

[012-072]conflicting hereditary interests? In the conduct of Murkertach, we

[012-073]admire, in turn, his many shining personal qualities, which even

[012-074]tasteless panegyric cannot hide, and the prudence, self-denial,

[012-075]patience, and preservance with which he awaits his day of power.

[012-076]Unhappily, for one every way so worthy of it, that day never arrived!

[012-077]At no former period,—not even at the height of the tyranny of

[012-078]Turgesius,—was a capable Prince more needed in Erin. The new generation

[012-079]of Northmen were again upon all the estuaries and inland waters of the

[012-080]Island. In the years 923-4 and 5, their light armed vessels swarmed on

[012-081]Lough Erne, Lough Ree, and other lakes, spreading flame and terror on

[012-082]every side. Clonmacnoise and Kildare, slowly recovering from former

[012-083]pillage, were again left empty and in ruins. Murkertach, the base of

[012-084]whose early operations was his own patrimony in Ulster, attacked near

[012-085]Newry a Northern division under the command of the son of Godfrey (A.D.

[012-086]926), and left 800 dead on the field. The escape of the remnant was

[012-087]only secured by Godfrey marching rapidly to their relief and covering

[012-088]the retreat. His son lay with the dead. In the years 933, at Slieve

[012-089]Behma, in his own Province, Murkertach won a third victory; and in 936,

[012-090]taking political advantage of the result of the great English battle of

[012-091]Brunanburgh, which had so seriously diminished the Danish strength, the

[012-092]Roydamna, in company with the King, assaulted Dublin, expelled its

[012-093]garrison, levelled its fortress, and left the dwellings of the Northmen

[012-094]in ashes. From Dublin they proceeded southward, through Leinster and

[012-095]Munster, and after taking hostages of every tribe, Donogh returned to

[012-096]his Methian home and Murkertach to Aileach. While resting in his own

[012-097]fort (A.D. 939), he was surprised by a party of Danes, and carried off

[012-098]to their ships, but, says the old translator of the Annals of

[012-099]Clonmacnoise, "he made a good escape from them, as it was God's will."

[012-100]The following season he redoubled his efforts against the enemy.

[012-101]Attacking them on their own element, he ravaged their settlements on

[012-102]the Scottish coasts and among the isles of Insi-Gall (the Hebrides),

[012-103]returned laden with spoils, and hailed with acclamations as the

[012-104]liberator of his people.

[012-106]Of the same age with Murkertach, the reigning Prince at Cashel was

[012-107]Kellachan, one of the heroes of the latter Bards and Story-tellers of

[012-108]the South. The romantic tales of his capture by the Danes, and

[012-109]captivity in their fleet at Dundalk, of the love which Sitrick's wife

[012-110]bore him, and of his gallant rescue by the Dalcassians and Eugenians,

[012-111]have no historical sanction. He was often both at war and at peace with

[012-112]the foreigners of Cork and Limerick, and did not hesitate more than

[012-113]once to employ their arms for the maintenance of his own supremacy; but

[012-114]his only authentic captivity was, as a hostage, in the hands of

[012-115]Murkertach. While the latter was absent, on his expedition to

[012-116]Insi-Gall, Kellachan fell upon the Deisi and Ossorians, and inflicted

[012-117]severe chastisement upon them-alleging, as his provocation, that they

[012-118]had given hostages to Murkertach, and acknowledged him as _Roydamna_ of

[012-119]all Erin, in contempt of the co-equal rights of Cashel. When Murkertach

[012-120]returned from his Scotch expedition, and heard what had occurred, and

[012-121]on what pretext Kellachan had acted, he assembled at Aileach all the

[012-122]branches of the Northern Hy-Nial, for whom this was cause, indeed. Out

[012-123]of these he selected 1000 chosen men, whom he provided, among other

[012-124]equipments, with those "leathern coats," which lent a _soubriquet_ to

[012-125]his name; and with these "ten hundred heroes," he set out—strong in his

[012-126]popularity and his alliances—to make a circuit of the entire island

[012-127](A.D. 940). He departed from Aileach, says his Bard, whose Itinerary we

[012-128]have, "keeping his left hand to the sea;" Dublin, once more rebuilt,

[012-129]acknowledged his title, and Sitrick, one of its lords, went with him as

[012-130]hostage for Earl Blacair and his countrymen; Leinster surrendered him

[012-131]Lorcan, its King; Kellachan, of Cashel, overawed by his superior

[012-132]fortune, advised his own people not to resist by force, and consented

[012-133]to become himself the hostage for all Munster. In Connaught, Conor,

[012-134](from whom the O'Conors take their family name), son of the Prince,

[012-135]came voluntarily to his camp, and was received with open arms.

[012-136]Kellachan alone was submitted to the indignity of wearing a fetter.

[012-137]With these distinguished hostages, Murkertach and his leather-cloaked

[012-138]"ten hundred" returned to Aileach, where, for five months, they spent a

[012-139]season of unbounded rejoicing. In the following year, the _Roydamna_

[012-140]transferred the hostages to King Donogh, as his _suzerain_, thus

[012-141]setting the highest example of obedience from the highest place. He

[012-142]might now look abroad over all the tribes of Erin, and feel himself

[012-143]without a rival among his countrymen. He stood at the very summit of

[012-144]his good fortune, when the Danes of Dublin, reinforced from abroad,

[012-145]after his "Circuit," renewed their old plundering practices. They

[012-146]marched north, at the close of winter, under Earl Blacair, their

[012-147]destination evidently being Armagh. Murkertach, with some troops

[012-148]hastily collected, disputed their passage at the ford of Ardee. An

[012-149]engagement ensued on Saturday, the 4th of March, 943, in which the

[012-150]noble _Roydamna_ fell. King Donogh, to whose reign his vigorous spirit

[012-151]has given its main historical importance, survived him but a

[012-152]twelvemonth; the Monarch died in the bed of repose; his destined

[012-153]successor in the thick of battle.

[012-154]The death of the brave and beloved Murkertach filled all Erin with

[012-155]grief and rage, and as King Donogh was too old to avenge his destined

[012-156]successor, that duty devolved on Congal, the new _Roydamna_. In the

[012-157]year after the fatal action at Ardee, Congal, with Brann, King of

[012-158]Leinster, and Kellach, heir of Leinster, assaulted and took Dublin, and

[012-159]wreaked a terrible revenge for the nation's loss. The "women, children,

[012-160]and plebeians," were carried off captive; the greater part of the

[012-161]garrison were put to the sword; but a portion escaped in their vessels

[012-162]to their fortress on Dalkey, an island in the bay of Dublin. This was

[012-163]the third time within a century that Dublin had been rid of its foreign

[012-164]yoke, and yet as the Gaelic-Irish would not themselves dwell in

[012-165]fortified towns, the site remained open and unoccupied, to be rebuilt

[012-166]as often as it might be retaken. The gallant Congal, the same year,

[012-167]succeeded on the death of Donogh to the sovereignty, and, so soon as he

[012-168]had secured his seat, and surrounded it with sufficient hostages, he

[012-169]showed that he could not only avenge the death, but imitate the

[012-170]glorious life of him whose place he held. Two considerable victories in

[012-171]his third and fourth years increased his fame, and rejoiced the hearts

[012-172]of his countrymen: the first was won at Slane, aided by the Lord of

[012-173]Breffni (O'Ruarc), and by Olaf the Crooked, a northern chief. The

[012-174]second was fought at Dublin (947), in which Blacair, the victor at

[012-175]Ardee, and 1,600 of his men were slain. Thus was the death of

[012-176]Murkertach finally avenged.

[012-177]It is very remarkable that the first conversions to Christianity among

[012-178]the Danes of Dublin should have taken place immediately after these

[012-179]successive defeats—in 948. Nor, although quite willing to impute the

[012-180]best and most disinterested motives to these first neophytes, can we

[012-181]shut our eyes to the fact that no change of life, such as we might

[012-182]reasonably look for, accompanied their change of religion. Godfrid, son

[012-183]of Sitrick, and successor of Blacair, who professed himself a Christian

[012-184]in 948, plundered and destroyed the churches of East-Meath in 949,

[012-185]burnt 150 persons in the oratory of Drumree, and carried off as

[012-186]captives 3,000 persons. If the tree is to be judged by its fruits, this

[012-187]first year's growth of the new faith is rather alarming. It compels us

[012-188]to disbelieve the sincerity of Godfrid, at least, and the fighting men

[012-189]who wrought these outrages and sacrileges. It forces us to rank them

[012-190]with the incorrigible heathens who boasted that they had twenty times

[012-191]received the Sacrament of Baptism, and valued it for the twenty white

[012-192]robes which had been presented to them on those occasions. Still, we

[012-193]must endeavour hereafter, when we can, to distinguish Christian from

[012-194]Pagan Danes, and those of Irish birth, sons of the first comers, from

[012-195]the foreign-born kinsmen of their ancestors. Between these two classes

[012-196]there grew a gulf of feeling and experience, which a common language

[012-197]and common dangers only partially bridged over. Not seldom the

[012-198]interests and inclinations of the Irish-born Dane, especially if a true

[012-199]Christian, were at open variance with the interests and designs of the

[012-200]new arrivals from Denmark, and it is generally, if not invariably, with

[012-201]the former, that the Leinster and other Irish Princes enter into

[012-202]coalitions for common political purposes. The remainder of the reign of

[012-203]Congal is one vigorous battle. The Lord of Breffni, who had fought

[012-204]beside him on the hill of Slane, advanced his claim to be recognised

[012-205]_Roydamna_, and this being denied, broke out into rebellion and

[012-206]harassed his patrimony. Donald, son of Murkertach, and grandson of

[012-207]Nial, (the first who took the name of _Uai-Nial_, or O'Neill), disputed

[012-208]these pretensions of the Lord of Breffni; carried his boats overland

[012-209]from Aileach to Lough Erne in Fermanagh, and Lough Oughter in Cavan;

[012-210]attacked the lake-islands, where the treasure and hostages of Breffni

[012-211]were kept, and carried them off to his own fortress. The warlike and

[012-212]indefatigable king was in the field summer and winter enforcing his

[012-213]authority on Munster and Connaught, and battling with the foreign

[012-214]garrisons between times. No former Ard-Righ had a severer struggle with

[012-215]the insubordinate elements which beset him from first to last. His end

[012-216]was sudden, but not inglorious. In returning from the chariot-races at

[012-217]the Curragh of Kildare, he was surprised and slain in an ambuscade laid

[012-218]for him by Godfrid at a place on the banks of the Liffey called Tyraris

[012-219]or Teeraris house. By his side, fighting bravely, fell the lords of

[012-220]Teffia and Ferrard, two of his nephews, and others of his personal

[012-221]attendants and companions. The Dublin Danes had in their turn a day of

[012-222]rejoicing and of revenge for the defeats they had suffered at Congal's

[012-223]hands.

[012-224]This reign is not only notable for the imputed first conversion of the

[012-225]Danes to Christianity, but also for the general adoption of family

[012-226]names. Hitherto, we have been enabled to distinguish clansmen only by

[012-227]tribe-names formed by prefixing _Hy_, _Kinnel_, _Sil_, _Muintir_,

[012-228]_Dal_, or some synonymous term, meaning race, kindred, sept, district,

[012-229]or part, to the proper name of a remote common ancestor, as Hy-Nial,

[012-230]Kinnel-Connel, Sil-Murray, Muintir-Eolais, Dal-g Cais, and Dal-Riada.

[012-231]But the great tribes now begin to break into families, and we are

[012-232]hereafter to know particular houses, by distinct hereditary surnames,

[012-233]as O'Neill, O'Conor, MacMurrough, and McCarthy. Yet, the whole body of

[012-234]relatives are often spoken of by the old tribal title, which, unless

[012-235]exceptions are named, is supposed to embrace all the descendants of the

[012-236]old connection to whom it was once common. At first this alternate use

[012-237]of tribe and family names may confuse the reader—for it _is_ rather

[012-238]puzzling to find a MacLoughlin with the same paternal ancestor as an

[012-239]O'Neill, and a McMahon of Thomond as an O'Brien, but the difficulty

[012-240]disappears with use and familiarity, and though the number and variety

[012-241]of newly-coined names cannot be at once committed to memory, the story

[012-242]itself gains in distinctness by the change.

[012-243]In the year 955, Donald O'Neill, son of the brave and beloved

[012-244]Murkertach, was recognised as Ard-Righ, by the required number of

[012-245]Provinces, without recourse to coercion. But it was _not_ to be

[012-246]expected that any Ard-Righ should, at this period of his country's

[012-247]fortunes, reign long in peace. War was then the business of the King;

[012-248]the first art he had to learn, and the first to practise. Warfare in

[012-249]Ireland had not been a stationary science since the arrival of the

[012-250]Norwegians and their successors, the Danes. Something they may have

[012-251]acquired from the natives, and in turn the natives were not slow to

[012-252]copy whatever seemed most effective in their tactics. Donald IV. was

[012-253]the first to imitate their habit of employing armed boats on the inland

[012-254]lakes. He even improved on their example, by carrying these boats with

[012-255]him overland, and launching them wherever he needed their co-operation;

[012-256]as we have already seen him do in his expedition against Breffni, while

[012-257]_Roydamna_, and as we find him doing again, in the seventh year of his

[012-258]reign, when he carried his boats overland from Armagh to West-Meath in

[012-259]order to employ them on Loch Ennell, near Mullingar. He was at this

[012-260]time engaged in making his first royal visitation of the Provinces,

[012-261]upon which he spent two months in Leinster, with all his forces,

[012-262]coerced the Munster chiefs by fire and sword into obedience, and

[012-263]severely punished the insubordination of Fergal O'Ruarc, King of

[012-264]Connaught. His fleet upon Loch Ennell, and his severities generally

[012-265]while in their patrimony, so exasperated the powerful families of the

[012-266]Southern Hy-Nial (the elder of which was now known as O'Melaghlin),

[012-267]that on the first opportunity they leagued with the Dublin Danes, under

[012-268]their leader, Olaf "the Crooked" (A.D. 966), and drove King Donald out

[012-269]of Leinster and Meath, pursuing him across Slieve-Fuaid, almost to the

[012-270]walls of Aileach. But the brave tribes of Tyrconnell and Tyrowen

[012-271]rallied to his support, and he pressed south upon the insurgents of

[012-272]Meath and Dublin; West-Meath he rapidly overran, and "planted a

[012-273]garrison in every cantred from the Shannon to Kells," In the campaigns

[012-274]which now succeeded each other, without truce or pause, for nearly a

[012-275]dozen years, the Leinster people generally sympathised with and

[012-276]assisted those of West-Meath, and Olaf, of Dublin, who recruited his

[012-277]ranks by the junction of the Lagmans, a warlike tribe, from Insi-Gall

[012-278](the Hebrides). Ossory, on the other hand, acted with the monarch, and

[012-279]the son of its Tanist (A.D. 974) was slain before Dublin, by Olaf and

[012-280]his Leinster allies, with 2,600 men, of Ossory and Ulster. The campaign

[012-281]of 978 was still more eventful: the Leinster men quarrelled with their

[012-282]Danish allies, who had taken their king captive, and in an engagement

[012-283]at Belan, near Athy, defeated their forces, with the loss of the heir

[012-284]of Leinster, the lords of Kinsellagh, Lea and Morett, and other chiefs.

[012-285]King Donald had no better fortune at Killmoon, in Meath, the same

[012-286]season, where he was utterly routed by the same force, with the loss of

[012-287]Ardgal, heir of Ulidia, and Kenneth, lord of Tyrconnell. But for the

[012-288]victories gained about the same period in Munster, by Mahon and Brian,

[012-289]the sons of Kennedy, over the Danes of Limerick, of which we shall

[012-290]speak more fully hereafter, the balance of victory would have strongly

[012-291]inclined towards the Northmen at this stage of the contest.

[012-293]A leader, second in fame and in services only to Brian, was now putting

[012-294]forth his energies against the common enemy, in Meath. This was

[012-295]Melaghlin, better known afterwards as Malachy II., son of Donald, son

[012-296]of King Donogh, and, therefore, great-grandson to his namesake, Malachy

[012-297]I. He had lately attained to the command of his tribe—and he resolved

[012-298]to earn the honours which were in store for him, as successor to the

[012-299]sovereignty. In the year 979, the Danes of Dublin and the Isles marched

[012-300]in unusual strength into Meath, under the command of Rannall, son of

[012-301]Olaf the Crooked, and Connail, "the Orator of Ath-Cliath," (Dublin).

[012-302]Malachy, with his allies, gave them battle near Tara, and achieved a

[012-303]complete victory. Earl Rannall and the Orator were left dead on the

[012-304]field, with, it is reported, 5,000 of the foreigners. On the Irish side

[012-305]fell the heir of Leinster, the lord of Morgallion and his son; the

[012-306]lords of Fertullagh and Cremorne, and a host of their followers. The

[012-307]engagement, in true Homeric spirit, had been suspended on three

[012-308]successive nights, and renewed three successive days. It was a genuine

[012-309]pitched battle—a trial of main strength, each party being equally

[012-310]confident of victory. The results were most important, and most

[012-311]gratifying to the national pride. Malachy, accompanied by his friend,

[012-312]the lord of Ulidia (Down), moved rapidly on Dublin, which, in its

[012-313]panic, yielded to all his demands. The King of Leinster and 2,000 other

[012-314]prisoners were given up to him without ransom. The Danish Earls

[012-315]solemnly renounced all claims to tribute or fine from any of the

[012-316]dwellers without their own walls. Malachy remained in the city three

[012-317]days, dismantled its fortresses, and carried off its hostages and

[012-318]treasure. The unfortunate Olaf the Crooked fled beyond seas, and died

[012-319]at Iona, in exile, and a Christian. In the same year, and in the midst

[012-320]of universal rejoicing, Donald IV. died peacefully and piously at

[012-321]Armagh, in the 24th year of his reign. He was succeeded by Malachy, who

[012-322]was his sister's son, and in whom all the promise of the lamented

[012-323]Murkertach seemed to revive.

[012-324]The story of Malachy II. is so interwoven with the still-more

[012-325]illustrious career of Brian _Borooa_, that it will not lose in interest

[012-326]by being presented in detail. But before entering on the rivalry of

[012-327]these great men, we must again remark on the altered position which the

[012-328]Northmen of this age hold to the Irish from that which existed

[012-329]formerly. A century and a half had now elapsed since their first

[012-330]settlement in the seaports, especially of the eastern and southern

[012-331]Provinces. More than one generation of their descendants had been born

[012-332]on the banks of the Liffey, the Shannon, and the Suir. Many of them had

[012-333]married into Irish families, had learned the language of the country,

[012-334]and embraced its religion. When Limerick was taken by Brian, Ivar, its

[012-335]Danish lord, fled for sanctuary to Scattery Island, and when Dublin was

[012-336]taken by Malachy II., Olaf the Crooked fled to Iona. Inter-marriages

[012-337]with the highest Gaelic families became frequent, after their

[012-338]conversion to Christianity. The mother of Malachy, after his father's
