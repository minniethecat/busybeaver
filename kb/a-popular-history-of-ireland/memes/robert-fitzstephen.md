## Overview

Robert Fitzstephen (c. 1120–c. 1183) was a Cambro-Norman knight and one of the very first Norman invaders of Ireland, leading the vanguard of the expedition that landed at Bannow Bay in May 1169. A grandson of the legendary Welsh princess Nesta, Fitzstephen belonged to the remarkable group of Nesta's descendants—including his half-brothers Maurice Fitzgerald and Robert de Barri—who spearheaded the Norman conquest of Ireland. (023-050) (023-158)

Fitzstephen's arrival on the Irish shore marked the beginning of the Norman presence in Ireland. His capture and imprisonment by the Norse of Wexford, his later service under Earl [[strongbow|Richard de Clare]] ([[strongbow|Strongbow]]), and his role in the defence of Cork made him a central figure in the first decade of the conquest. His descendants claimed the barony of Imokilly through his bloodline for centuries afterward. (026-006) (055-220) (056-035)

## Biography

### The Descendants of Nesta

Robert Fitzstephen was one of the many sons and grandsons of the Welsh princess Nesta, daughter of Rhys ap Tewdwr, whose children by various fathers formed the nucleus of the Cambro-Norman aristocracy that conquered Ireland. Nesta's progeny included Robert Fitzstephen, Maurice Fitzgerald, Robert de Barri, and probably other children, who between them established the Geraldine, Barry, and Fitzstephen families in Ireland. One of her daughters married into the Carew family, further extending the web of kinship that bound the Norman invaders together. (023-050)

The Fitzstephens and their cousins formed a tight-knit military aristocracy that had already proven its worth in the Norman conquest of Wales. Constable of Cardigan, Fitzstephen brought both military experience and family connections to the Irish enterprise that would prove invaluable in the years ahead. (023-128)

### The First Landing, 1169

Fitzstephen's expedition reached the Irish shore in May 1169, the first body of Norman troops to land in Ireland as part of the organized invasion force supporting [[dermid-mcmurrogh|Dermid McMurrogh]]'s bid to recover his kingdom of [[leinster|Leinster]]. He approached the Irish shore and ran into Bannow Bay with a force of thirty knights, sixty men-at-arms, and three hundred archers—a formidable military contingent by the standards of twelfth-century Ireland. (023-158)

> "Robert Fitzstephen ran into a creek, with the crews of two of their ships, at a place called Bag-and-Bun." (023-158)

Fitzstephen set out for Wexford, about a day's march distant, and the town was quickly taken—the first major Norman military success on Irish soil. The capture of Wexford established the Norman beachhead and demonstrated the superiority of Norman armour, tactics, and siege craft against the Norse-Irish defenders. This first campaign, conducted in alliance with [[dermid-mcmurrogh|Dermid McMurrogh]], set the pattern for the conquest that followed. (023-169)

### Capture and Imprisonment

During the second campaign of Earl Richard—[[henry-ii|Henry II]]'s expedition of 1171—Fitzstephen suffered a serious reverse. He was compelled to surrender his fort at Carrick to the men of Wexford, who had risen in revolt. The Wexford men imprisoned Fitzstephen and held him as a valuable prisoner, hoping to exchange him for concessions from the new English authorities in Ireland. (026-006)

Earl Richard attempted to relieve Fitzstephen by marching "by way of Idrone" with the hope of delivering him. But the Wexford men threatened to put Fitzstephen and his companions to death if they were attacked. The Earl, therefore, held his hand rather than sacrifice his lieutenant—an indication of the value placed on Fitzstephen's life. (026-059) (026-062)

Eventually, the Norse-Irish of Wexford were compelled to make their submission and to deliver up to [[henry-ii|Henry II]] their prisoner, Robert Fitzstephen. The king's attitude towards Fitzstephen, as towards all those who had anticipated his own designs on Ireland, was one of suspicion—though Henry eventually confirmed Fitzstephen in some of his Irish possessions. (026-115) (026-117)

### Later Career and Death

Fitzstephen continued to serve in Ireland through the 1170s and early 1180s. In the year 1182, a new expedition was mounted in aid of his uncle, Robert Fitzstephen—this premier invader of Ireland. However, by this time, his son Ralph and other knights including Milo de Cogan had been cut off by the Irish, and Fitzstephen himself was "afflicted with grief" over these losses. (027-184) (027-192)

This premier invader of Ireland died around 1183, his final years marked by the loss of his son and the consolidation of Norman power under new leaders such as [[hugh-de-lacy|Hugh de Lacy]] and [[john-de-courcy|John de Courcy]]. The year of Robert Fitzstephen, Milo de Cogan, and Raymond le Gros—the first generation of Norman adventurers—had passed, and a new phase of the conquest had begun. (027-196)

## Legacy

### Heraldic Claims

Fitzstephen's bloodline carried enduring legal and political weight in Ireland. The original grant made to Robert Fitzstephen of the moiety of the kingdom of Cork became the basis for territorial claims exercised by his descendants for centuries. As late as the reign of [[elizabeth-i|Elizabeth I]], claimants by virtue of their descent from Robert Fitzstephen asserted rights to the barony of Imokilly and other territories in Munster. (055-220) (056-035)

### The First Invader

Robert Fitzstephen's place in Irish history is as the first of the Norman invaders—the military vanguard of a conquest that would transform Ireland. Though overshadowed in historical memory by [[strongbow|Strongbow]] and [[hugh-de-lacy|Hugh de Lacy]], it was Fitzstephen who first demonstrated that a small force of Norman knights could overcome the military resistance of Irish and Norse-Irish chieftains. His thirty knights at Bannow were the first wave of a tide that would reshape the political, social, and cultural landscape of Ireland for eight centuries. (023-158) (027-184)
