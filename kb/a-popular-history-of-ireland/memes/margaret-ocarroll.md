## Overview

Margaret O'Carroll was a fifteenth-century Irish noblewoman, wife of O'Conor of Offally, and a descendant and progenitress of illustrious women. (046-065) She is remembered in Irish annals for her extraordinary hospitality, her patronage of the Bardic order, her extreme piety, and her public works — a combination of virtues that made her one of the most celebrated women of medieval Ireland.

## Hospitality and the Patronage of Bards

Margaret O'Carroll was renowned throughout Ireland and Scotland for her magnificent hospitality. Twice each year she received all the poets and musicians of both kingdoms at her residence in Offally.

> of the pious and splendid Margaret O'Carroll, receiving twice a year in Offally all the Bards of Albyn and Erin, we cannot but envy the professors of the gentle art their good fortune in having lived in such times, and shared in such assemblies. (046-053)

The Bardic order was still one of the recognized estates of Irish society in the later Middle Ages, and Margaret O'Carroll was among the foremost patrons of their art. (047-228) Alongside other noble patrons such as William O'Kelly of Galway, who entertained all the poets, musicians, and poor persons who chose to flock to him throughout the Christmas holydays, and Henry Avery O'Neil, who granted all manner of things "that came into his hands" to all manner of men, Margaret O'Carroll stood out as a model of noble liberality. (046-050) (047-229)

McGee observes that hospitality was accounted the first of social virtues among the Irish, and inhospitality the worst of vices — the unpopularity of a churl descending to his posterity through successive generations. (046-056) Margaret O'Carroll exemplified this virtue in its highest degree.

## A Woman of Independent Action

The high estimation in which women were held among the Irish tribes is evident from the particularity with which the historians record their obits and marriages. (046-060) Margaret O'Carroll exemplified this independence of spirit in a striking episode:

> The Margaret O'Carroll already mentioned, a descendant and progenitress of illustrious women, rode privately to Trim, as we are told, with some English prisoners, taken by her husband, O'Conor of Offally, and exchanged them for others of equal worth lying in that fortress; and "this she did," it is added, "without the knowledge of" her husband. (046-065)

The maiden name of the wife was never wholly lost in that of her husband in Gaelic Ireland, and if her family were of equal standing with his before marriage, she generally retained her full share of authority afterwards. (046-063) Margaret O'Carroll's independent prisoner exchange at Trim demonstrates the scope of action available to women of high rank in fifteenth-century Ireland.

## Piety and Public Works

Margaret O'Carroll's fame extended far beyond hospitality and social graces. She was remembered also for substantial public works that benefited her people.

> This lady was famed not only for her exceeding hospitality and her extreme piety, but for other more unexpected works. Her name is remembered in connection with the erection of bridges and the making of highways, as well as the building of churches, and the presentation of missals and mass-books. (046-070)

The combination of infrastructure — bridges and highways — with religious patronage — churches, missals, and mass-books — reveals a woman who deployed her wealth and influence across both the secular and sacred spheres of medieval Irish life.

## Legacy

The grace Margaret O'Carroll acquired through her benefactions long outlived her. Her name became a byword for noble generosity among a people who prized hospitality above all social virtues. In an age when the muses of history and poetry seemed to have abandoned Ireland to theologians, jurists, and men of science, Margaret O'Carroll stood with the foremost patrons of the Bardic order, ensuring that the ancient tradition of Irish poetry and music continued to flourish. (047-225) (047-228)
