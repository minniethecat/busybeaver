CHAPTER VIII.

[059-001]THE ULSTER CONFEDERACY—FEAGH MAC HUGH O'BYRNE—CAMPAIGN OF

[059-002]1595—NEGOTIATIONS, ENGLISH AND SPANISH—BATTLE OF THE YELLOW FORD—ITS

[059-003]CONSEQUENCES.

[059-004]In the summer of 1594 the cruel and mercenary Fitzwilliam was succeeded

[059-005]by Sir William Russell, who had served the Queen, both in Ireland "and

[059-006]in divers other places beyond sea, in martial affairs." In lieu of the

[059-007]arbitrary exaction of county cess—so grossly abused by his

[059-008]predecessor—the shires of the Pale were to pay for the future into the

[059-009]Treasury of Dublin a composition of 2,100 pounds per annum, out of

[059-010]which the fixed sum of 1,000 pounds was allowed as the Deputy's wages.

[059-011]Russell's administration lasted till May, 1597. In that month he was

[059-012]succeeded by Thomas, Lord Borough, who died in August following of the

[059-013]wounds received in an expedition against Tyrone; after which the

[059-014]administration remained in the hands of the Justices till the

[059-015]appointment of the Earl of Essex.

[059-016]On the arrival of Russell, Tyrone for the last time ventured to appear

[059-017]within the walls of Dublin. His influence in the city, and even at the

[059-018]Council table, must have been considerable to enable him to enter the

[059-019]gates of the Castle with so much confidence. He came to explain his

[059-020]wrongs against the previous Deputy, to defend himself against Bagnal's

[059-021]charges, and to discover, if possible, the instructions of Russell. If

[059-022]in one respect he was gratified by a personal triumph over his

[059-023]brother-in-law, in another he had cause for serious alarm, on learning

[059-024]that Sir John Norris, brother of the President of Munster, a commander

[059-025]of the highest reputation, was to be sent over under the title of Lord

[059-026]General, with 2,000 veterans who served in Brittany, and 1,000 of a new

[059-027]levy. He further learned that his own arrest had been discussed at the

[059-028]Council, and, leaving Dublin precipitately, he hastened to his home at

[059-029]Dungannon. All men's minds were now naturally filled with wars and

[059-030]rumours of wars.

[059-031]The first blow was struck at "the firebrand of the mountains," as he

[059-032]was called at Court, Feagh Mac Hugh O'Byrne. The truce made with him

[059-033]expired in 1594, and his application for his renewal was not honoured

[059-034]with an answer. On the contrary, his sureties at Dublin, Geoffrey, son

[059-035]of Hugh, and his own son, James, were committed to close custody in the

[059-036]Castle. His son-in-law, Sir Walter Fitzgerald, had been driven by

[059-037]ill-usage, and his friendship for Lord Baltinglass, to the shelter of

[059-038]Glenmalure, and this was, of course, made a ground of charge against

[059-039]its chief. During the last months of 1594, Mynce, Sheriff of Carlow,

[059-040]informed the Lord Deputy of warlike preparations in the Glen, and that

[059-041]Brian Oge O'Rourke had actually passed to and fro through Dublin city

[059-042]and county, as confidential agent between Feagh Mac Hugh and Tyrone. In

[059-043]January following, under cover of a hunting party among the hills, the

[059-044]Deputy, by a night march on Glenmalure, succeeded in surprising

[059-045]O'Byrne's house at Ballincor, and had almost taken the aged chieftain

[059-046]prisoner. In the flight, Rose O'Toole, his wife, was wounded in the

[059-047]breast, and a priest detected hiding in a thicket was shot dead. Feagh

[059-048]retired to Dromceat, or the Cat's-back Mountain—one of the best

[059-049]positions in the Glen—while a strong force was quartered in his former

[059-050]mansion to observe his movements. In April, his son-in-law, Fitzgerald,

[059-051]was taken prisoner, near Baltinglass, in a retreat where he was laid up

[059-052]severely wounded; in May, a party under the Deputy's command scoured

[059-053]the mountains and seized the Lady Rose, who was attainted of treason,

[059-054]and, like Fitzgerald, barbarously given up to the halter and the

[059-055]quartering knife. Two foster-brothers of the chief were, at the same

[059-056]time and in the same manner, put to death, and a large reward was

[059-057]offered for his own apprehension, alive or dead.

[059-058]Hugh O'Neil announced his resort to arms by a vigorous protest against

[059-059]the onslaught made on his friend O'Byrne. Without waiting for, or

[059-060]expecting any answer, he surprised the fort erected on the Blackwater

[059-061]which commanded the highway into his own territory. This fort, which

[059-062]was situated between Armagh and Dungannon, about five miles distant

[059-063]from either, served, before the fortification of Charlemont, as the

[059-064]main English stronghold in that part of Ulster. The river Blackwater on

[059-065]which it stood, from its source on the borders of Monaghan to its

[059-066]outlet in Lough Neagh, watered a fertile valley, which now became the

[059-067]principal theatre of war; for Hugh O'Neil, and afterwards for his

[059-068]celebrated nephew, it proved to be a theatre of victory. General

[059-069]Norris, on reaching Ireland, at once marched northward to recover the

[059-070]fort lately taken. O'Neil, having demolished the works, retreated

[059-071]before him; considering Dungannon also unfit to stand a regular siege,

[059-072]he dismantled the town, burnt his own castle to the ground, having

[059-073]first secured every portable article of value. Norris contented himself

[059-074]with reconnoitring the Earl's entrenched camp at some distance from

[059-075]Dungannon, and returned to Newry, where he established his

[059-076]head-quarters.

[059-078]The campaign in another quarter was attended with even better success

[059-079]for the Confederates. Hugh Roe O'Donnell, no longer withheld by the

[059-080]more politic O'Neil, displayed in action all the fiery energy of his

[059-081]nature. Under his banner he united almost all the tribes of Ulster not

[059-082]enlisted with O'Neil; while six hundred Scots, led by MacLeod of Ara,

[059-083]obeyed his commands. He first descended on the plains of

[059-084]Annally-O'Farrell (the present county of Longford), driving the English

[059-085]settlers before him: he next visited the undertaker's tenants in

[059-086]Connaught, ejecting them from Boyle and Ballymoate, and pursuing them

[059-087]to the gates of Tuam. On his return, the important town and castle of

[059-088]Sligo, the property of O'Conor, then in England, submitted to him. Sir

[059-089]Richard Bingham endeavoured to recover it, but was beaten off with

[059-090]loss. O'Donnell, finding it cheaper to demolish than defend it, broke

[059-091]down the castle and returned in triumph across the Erne.

[059-092]General Norris, having arranged his plan of campaign at Newry,

[059-093]attempted to victual Armagh, besieged by O'Neil, but was repulsed by

[059-094]that leader after a severe struggle. He, however, succeeded in throwing

[059-095]supplies into Monaghan, where a strong garrison was quartered, and to

[059-096]which O'Neil and O'Donnell proceeded to lay siege. While lying before

[059-097]Monaghan they received overtures of peace from the Lord Deputy, who

[059-098]continually disagreed with Sir John Norris as to the conduct of the

[059-099]war, and lost no opportunity of thwarting his plans. He did not now

[059-100]blush to address, as Earl of Tyrone, the man he had lately proclaimed a

[059-101]traitor at Dublin, by the title of the son of a blacksmith. The Irish

[059-102]leaders at the outset refused to meet the Commissioners—Chief Justice

[059-103]Gardiner and Sir Henry Wallop, Treasurer-at-War—in Dundalk, so the

[059-104]latter were compelled to wait on them in the camp before Monaghan. The

[059-105]terms demanded by O'Neil and O'Donnell, including entire freedom of

[059-106]religious worship, were reserved by the Commissioners for the

[059-107]consideration of the Council, with whose sanction, a few weeks

[059-108]afterwards, all the Ulster chiefs, except "the Queen's O'Reilly," were

[059-109]formally tried before a jury at Dublin, and condemned as traitors.

[059-110]Monaghan was thrice taken and retaken in this campaign. It was on the

[059-111]second return of General Norris from that town he found himself

[059-112]unexpectedly in presence of O'Neil's army, advantageously posted on the

[059-113]left bank of the little stream which waters the village of Clontibret.

[059-114]Norris made two attempts to force the passage, but without success. Sir

[059-115]Thomas Norris, and the general himself, were wounded; Seagrave, a

[059-116]gigantic Meathian cavalry officer, was slain in a hand to hand

[059-117]encounter with O'Neil; the English retreated hastily on Newry, and

[059-118]Monaghan was again surrendered to the Irish. This brilliant combat at

[059-119]Clontibret closed the campaign of 1595. General Norris, who, like Sir

[059-120]John Moore, two centuries later, commanded the respect, and frankly

[059-121]acknowledged the wrongs of the people against whom he fought, employed

[059-122]the winter months in endeavouring to effect a reconciliation between

[059-123]O'Neil and the Queen's Government. He had conceived a warm and

[059-124]chivalrous regard for his opponent; for he could not deny that he had

[059-125]been driven to take up arms in self-defence. At his instance a royal

[059-126]commission to treat with the Earl was issued, and the latter cheerfully

[059-127]gave them a meeting in an open field without the walls of Dundalk. The

[059-128]same terms which he had proposed before Monaghan were repeated in his

[059-129]_ultimatum_, and the Commissioners agreed to give him a positive answer

[059-130]by the 2nd day of April. On that day they attended at Dundalk, but

[059-131]O'Neil did not appear. The Commissioners delayed an entire fortnight,

[059-132]addressing him in the interim an urgent remonstrance to come in and

[059-133]conclude their negotiation. On the 17th of the month they received his

[059-134]reasons for breaking off the treaty—the principal of which was, that

[059-135]the truce had been repeatedly broken through by the English

[059-136]garrisons—and so the campaign of 1596 was to be fought with renewed

[059-137]animosity on both sides.

[059-138]Early in May the Lord Deputy made another descent on Ballincor, which

[059-139]Feagh Mac Hugh had recovered in the autumn to lose again in the spring.

[059-140]Though worn with years and infirm of body, the Wicklow chieftain held

[059-141]his devoted bands well together, and kept the garrison of Dublin

[059-142]constantly on the defensive. In the new chieftain of the O'Moores he

[059-143]found at this moment a young and active coadjutor. In an affair at

[059-144]Stradbally Bridge, O'Moore obtained a considerable victory, leaving

[059-145]among the slain Alexander and Francis Cosby, grandsons of the commander

[059-146]in the massacre at Mullaghmast.

[059-147]The arrival of three Spanish frigates with arms and ammunition in

[059-148]Donegal Bay was welcome news to the Northern Catholics. They were

[059-149]delivered to O'Donnell, who was incessantly in the field, while O'Neil

[059-150]was again undergoing the forms of diplomacy with a new royal commission

[059-151]at Dundalk. He himself disclaimed any correspondence with the King of

[059-152]Spain, but did not deny that such negotiations might be maintained by

[059-153]others. It is alleged that, while many of the chiefs had signed a

[059-154]formal invitation to the Spanish King to assume their crown, O'Neil had

[059-155]not gone beyond verbal assurances of co-operation with them. However

[059-156]this may be, he resolved that the entire season should not be wasted in

[059-157]words, so he attacked the strong garrison left in Armagh, and recovered

[059-158]the primatial city. According to the Irish practice, he dismantled the

[059-159]fortress, which, however, was again reconstructed by the English before

[059-160]the end of the war. Some other skirmishes, of which we have no very

[059-161]clear account, and which we may set down as of no decisive character,

[059-162]terminated the campaign.

[059-163]In May, 1597, Lord Borough, who had distinguished himself in the

[059-164]Netherlands, replaced Russell as Lord Deputy, and assumed the

[059-165]command-in-chief, in place of Sir John Norris. Simultaneously with his

[059-166]arrival Feagh Mac Hugh O'Byrne, was surprised in Glenmalure by a

[059-167]detachment from Dublin, and slain; he died as he had lived, a hero and

[059-168]a free man. O'Neil, who was warmly attached to the Wicklow chief,

[059-169]immediately despatched such succour as he could spare to Feagh's sons,

[059-170]and promised to continue to them the friendship he had always

[059-171]entertained for their father. Against Tyrone the new Lord Deputy now

[059-172]endeavoured to combine all the military resources at his disposal.

[059-173]Towards the end of July, Sir Conyers Clifford was ordered to muster the

[059-174]available force of Connaught at Boyle, and to march into Sligo and

[059-175]Donegal. A thousand men of the Anglo-Irish were assembled at Mullingar,

[059-176]under the command of young Barnewell of Trimbleston, who was instructed

[059-177]to effect a junction with the main force upon the borders of Ulster.

[059-178]The Lord Deputy, marching in force from Drogheda, penetrated,

[059-179]unopposed, the valley of the Blackwater, and entered Armagh. From

[059-180]Armagh he moved to the relief of the Blackwater fort, besieged by

[059-181]O'Neil. At a place called Drumfliuch, where Battleford Bridge now

[059-182]stands, Tyrone contrived to draw his enemies into an engagement on very

[059-183]disadvantageous ground. The result was a severe defeat to the new

[059-184]Deputy, who, a few days afterwards, died of his wounds at Newry, as his

[059-185]second in command, the Earl of Kildare, did at Drogheda. Sir Francis

[059-186]Vaughan, Sir Thomas Waller, and other distinguished officers, fell in

[059-187]the same action, but the fort, the main prize of the combatants,

[059-188]remained in English hands till the following year. O'Donnell, with

[059-189]equal success, held Ballyshannon, compelled Sir Conyers Clifford to

[059-190]raise the siege with the loss of the Earl of Thomond, and a large part

[059-191]of his following. Simultaneously, Captain Richard Tyrrell of

[059-192]West-Meath—one of O'Neil's favourite officers—having laid an ambuscade

[059-193]for young Barnewell at the pass in West-Meath which now bears his name,

[059-194]the Meathian regiment were sabred to a man. Mullingar and Maryborough

[059-195]were taken and sacked, and in the North, Sir John Chichester, Governor

[059-196]of Carrickfergus, was cut off with his troop by MacDonald of the Glens.

[059-197]These successes synchronize exactly with the expectation of a second

[059-198]Spanish Armada, which filled Elizabeth with her old apprehensions.

[059-199]Philip was persuaded again to tempt the fortune of the seas, and

[059-200]towards the end of October his fleet, under the Adelantado of Castille,

[059-201]appeared off the Scilly Islands, with a view to secure the Isle of

[059-202]Wight, or some other station, from which to operate an invasion the

[059-203]ensuing spring. Extraordinary means were taken for defence; the English

[059-204]troops in France were recalled, new levies raised, and the Queen's

[059-205]favourite, the young Earl of Essex, appointed to command the fleet,

[059-206]with Raleigh and Lord Thomas Howard as Vice-Admirals. But the elements

[059-207]again fought for the northern island; a storm, which swept the channel

[059-208]for weeks, drove the English ships into their ports, but scattered

[059-209]those of Spain over the Bay of Biscay. In this second expedition sailed

[059-210]Florence Conroy, and other Irish exiles, who had maintained for years a

[059-211]close correspondence with the Catholic leaders. Their presence in the

[059-212]fleet, the existence of the correspondence, and the progress of the

[059-213]revolt itself, will sufficiently account for the apparent vacillations

[059-214]of English policy in Ulster in the last months of 1597. Shortly before

[059-215]Christmas, Ormond, now Lord Lieutenant, accompanied by the Earl of

[059-216]Thomond, attended only by their personal followers, visited Dungannon,

[059-217]and remained three days in conference with O'Neil and O'Donnell. The

[059-218]Irish chiefs reiterated their old demands: freedom of worship, and the

[059-219]retention of the substantial power attached to their ancient rank. They

[059-220]would admit Sheriffs, if they were chosen from among natives of their

[059-221]counties, but they declined to give hostages out of their own families.

[059-222]These terms were referred to the Queen's consideration, who, after much

[059-223]protocoling to and fro, finally ratified them the following April, and

[059-224]affixed the great seal to O'Neil's pardon. But Tyrone, guided by

[059-225]intelligence received from Spain or England, or both, evaded the royal

[059-226]messenger charged to deliver him that instrument, and as the late truce

[059-227]expired the first week of June, devoted himself anew to military

[059-228]preparations.

[059-229]In the month of June, 1598, the Council at Dublin were in a state of

[059-230]fearful perplexity. O'Neil, two days after the expiration of the truce,

[059-231]invested the fort on the Blackwater, and seemed resolved to reduce it,

[059-232]if not by force, by famine. O'Donnell, as usual, was operating on the

[059-233]side of Connaught, where he had brought back O'Ruarc, O'Conor Sligo,

[059-234]and McDermot, to the Confederacy, from which they had been for a season

[059-235]estranged. Tyrrell and O'Moore, leading spirits in the midland counties

[059-236]were ravaging Ormond's palatinate of Tipperary almost without

[059-237]opposition. An English reinforcement, debarked at Dungarvan, was

[059-238]attacked on its march towards Dublin, and lost 400 men. In this

[059-239]emergency, before which even the iron nerve of Ormond quailed, the

[059-240]Council took the resolution of ordering one moiety of the Queen's

[059-241]troops under Ormond to march south against Tyrrell and O'Moore; the

[059-242]other under Marshal Bagnal, to proceed northward to the relief of the

[059-243]Blackwater fort. Ormond's campaign was brief and inglorious. After

[059-244]suffering a severe check in Leix, he shut himself up in Kilkenny, where

[059-245]he heard of the disastrous fate of Bagnal's expedition.

[059-246]On Sunday, the 13th of August, the Marshal reached Newry with some

[059-247]trifling loss from skirmishes on the route. He had with him, by the

[059-248]best accounts, six regiments of infantry, numbering in all about 4,000

[059-249]men and 350 horse. After resting a day, his whole force marched out of

[059-250]the city in three divisions; the first under the command of the Marshal

[059-251]and Colonel Percy, the cavalry under Sir Calisthenes Brooke and

[059-252]Captains Montague and Fleming; the rear guard under Sir Thomas

[059-253]Wingfield and Colonel Cosby. The Irish, whose numbers, both mounted and

[059-254]afoot, somewhat exceeded the Marshal's force, but who were not so well

[059-255]armed, had taken up a strong position at Ballinaboy ("the Yellow

[059-256]ford"), about two miles north of Armagh. With O'Neil were O'Donnell,

[059-257]Maguire, and McDonnell of Antrim—all approved leaders beloved by their

[059-258]men. O'Neil had neglected no auxiliary means of strengthening the

[059-259]position. In front of his lines he dug deep trenches, covered over with

[059-260]green sods, supported by twigs and branches. The pass leading into this

[059-261]plain was lined by 500 kerne, whose Parthian warfare was proverbial. He

[059-262]had reckoned on the headlong and boastful disposition of his opponent,

[059-263]and the result showed his accurate knowledge of character. Bagnal's

[059-264]first division, veterans from Brittany and Flanders, including 600

[059-265]curassiers in complete armour, armed with lances nine feet long, dashed

[059-266]into the pass before the second and third divisions had time to come

[059-267]up. The kerne poured in their rapid volleys; many of the English fell;

[059-268]the pass was yielded, and the whole power of Bagnal debouched into the

[059-269]plain. His artillery now thundered upon O'Neil's trenches, and the

[059-270]cavalry, with the plain before them, were ordered to charge; but they

[059-271]soon came upon the concealed pitfalls, horses fell, riders were thrown,

[059-272]and confusion spread among the squadron. Then it was O'Neil in turn

[059-273]gave the signal to charge; himself led on the centre, O'Donnell the

[059-274]left, and Maguire, famous for horsemanship, the Irish horse. The

[059-275]overthrow of the English was complete, and the victory most eventful.

[059-276]The Marshal, 23 superior officers, with about 1,700 of the rank and

[059-277]file fell on the field, while all the artillery baggage and 12 stand of

[059-278]colours were taken: the Irish loss in killed and wounded did not exceed

[059-279]800 men. "It was a glorious victory for the rebels," says the

[059-280]cotemporary English historian, Camden, "and of special advantage: for

[059-281]hereby they got arms and provisions, and Tyrone's name was cried up all

[059-282]over Ireland as the author of their liberty." It may also be added that

[059-283]it attracted renewed attention to the Irish war at Paris, Madrid, and

[059-284]Rome, where the names of O'Neil and O'Donnell were spoken of by all

[059-285]zealous Catholics with enthusiastic admiration.

[059-287]The battle was over by noon of the 15th of August; and the only effort

[059-288]to arrest the flight of the survivors was made by "the Queen's

[059-289]O'Reilly," who was slain in the attempt. By one o'clock the remnant of

[059-290]the cavalry under Montague were in full career for Dundalk, closely

[059-291]pressed by the mounted men of O'Hanlon. During the ensuing week the

[059-292]Blackwater fort capitulated; the Protestant garrison of Armagh

[059-293]surrendered; and were allowed to march south, leaving their arms and

[059-294]ammunition behind. The panic spread far and wide; the citizens of

[059-295]Dublin were enrolled to defend their walls; Lord Ormond continued shut

[059-296]up in Kilkenny; O'Moore and Tyrrell, who entered Munster by O'Neil's

[059-297]order, to kindle the elements of resistance, compelled the Lord

[059-298]President to retire from Kilmallock to Cork. O'Donnell established his

[059-299]head-quarters at Ballymoate, a dozen miles south of Sligo, which he had

[059-300]purchased from the chieftain of Corran for 400 pounds and 300 cows. The

[059-301]castle had served for thirteen years as an English stronghold, and was

[059-302]found staunch enough fifty years later to withstand the siege trains of

[059-303]Coote and Ludlow. From this point the Donegal chieftain was enabled to

[059-304]stretch his arm in every direction over lower Connaught. The result

[059-305]was, that before the end of the year 1598, nearly all the inhabitants

[059-306]of Clanrickarde and the surrounding districts were induced, either from

[059-307]policy or conviction, to give in their adhesion to the Northern

[059-308]Confederacy.
