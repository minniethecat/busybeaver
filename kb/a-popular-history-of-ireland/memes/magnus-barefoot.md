## Overview

**Magnus Barefoot** (c. 1073--1103), called Magnus III of Norway, was a Norse king whose ambitious expeditions into the Irish Sea region made him a significant figure in late eleventh and early twelfth century Irish history. His nickname, said to derive from his adoption of the Scottish kilt, is preserved in the eleventh Saga of "the Chronicles of the Kings of Norway." (016-101) (016-102) He began his reign in 1093 and within a decade projected his power across the [[Orkney]] Islands, the [[Hebrides]], the Isle of Man, and the coast of [[Ireland]], where he entered into both conflict and alliance with the O'Brien high kings. (016-103) His death in a skirmish on the shores of Strangford Lough in 1103 marked the end of serious Norse royal intervention in Irish affairs.

## Biography

### King of Norway

Magnus succeeded to the throne of [[Norway]] in 1093 and soon after undertook an expedition to the south "with many fine men, and good shipping." (016-104) His first target was the [[Orkney]] Islands, which he seized, sending their earls as prisoners to Norway and installing his own young son, Sigurd, as their ruler. (016-105) (016-106) He then swept through the [[Hebrides]], putting Lagman, son of Godard Crovan, to death at a time when the weakened Danish settlement at [[Dublin]] was attracting the ambition of Norse-Gaelic leaders. (016-107) (016-093) (016-094) (016-095)

During this expedition, Magnus spared only "the holy Island" of Iona, which the Northmen themselves now regarded with reverence, and where his own bones would one day be interred. (016-108) He extended his raids to Anglesey and the coast of Wales before retracing his course to Scotland, where he had his galley carried across the Isthmus of Cantyre to fulfil an old prophecy, thereby claiming possession of the land on both sides of Loch Awe. (016-110) (016-111) (016-112) (016-113)

> He sailed into the west sea, with the finest men who could be got in Norway. All the powerful men of the country followed him, such as Sigurd Hranesson, and his brother Ulf, Vidkunner Johnsson, Dag Eliffsson, Sorker of Sogn, Eyvind Olboge, the king's marshal, and many other great men. (016-120) (016-121) (016-122) (016-123)

### The Irish Expeditions

Magnus's ambition was drawn to the Irish Sea by the weakened condition of the Norse settlements in [[Ireland]]. The Dublin Vikings, who had once been a formidable power, had fallen into disarray, and the Irish Sea "paled before the mightier name of a King of Norway, whose more brilliant ambition had a still shorter span." (016-099) (016-100) During a winter in the Southern Hebrides, Magnus contracted his son Sigurd with the daughter of [[Murtogh O'Brien]], known to the Northmen as "Biadmynia." (016-114) (016-115) In the summer, he sailed homeward, not returning southward until the ninth year of his reign (A.D. 1102), when Sigurd had come of age and bore the title "King of the Orkneys and Hebrides." (016-116) (016-117) (016-118)

When the intelligence of this second fleet reached Irish waters, Murtogh O'Brien and his allies marched in force to [[Dublin]]. Magnus, however, "made peace with them for one year," and Murtogh "gave his daughter to Sigurd, with many jewels and gifts." (016-124) (016-125) (016-126) (016-127)

### Alliance with Murtogh O'Brien at Kinkora

That winter Magnus spent with [[Murtogh O'Brien]] at [[Kinkora]], the royal seat of the O'Brien dynasty and once the palace of [[Brian Boru]]. "Towards spring both kings went westward with their army all the way to Ulster." (016-128) (016-129) This was one of those annual visitations customary among Irish kings whose authority was not yet fully established. The circuit, as usual, was performed in about six weeks, after which the Irish monarch returned home, and Magnus went on board his fleet at [[Dublin]] to return to [[Norway]]. (016-130) (016-131) (016-132) (016-133) (016-134)

The alliance between Magnus and Murtogh was sealed by the marriage of Sigurd to the Irish king's daughter, creating a dynastic link between the Norwegian monarchy and the O'Brien line that, though brief, demonstrated the reach of Irish diplomacy in the early twelfth century. (016-127)

### Death in Ireland

On his voyage homeward, Magnus put into the coast of Ulidia (modern [[Down]]) expecting "cattle for ship-provision," which Murtogh had promised to send him. Some Irish accounts imply he went ashore to seize the cattle by force rather than await delivery. (016-135) (016-136) (016-137) McGee notes it seems "incredible that Murkertach should send cattle to the shore of Strangford Lough, from the pastures of Thomond, when they might be more easily driven to Dublin, or the mouth of the Boyne." (016-138) (016-139) (016-140)

> The cattle had not made their appearance on the eve of Bartholomew's Mass (August 23rd, A.D. 1103), says the Saga, so "when the sun rose in the sky, King Magnus himself went on shore with the greater part of his men." (016-141) (016-142) (016-143) (016-144)

The Saga provides a vivid description of the king's appearance that morning:

> He had a red shield, in which was inlaid a gilded lion; and was girt with the sword Legbiter, of which the hilt was of ivory, and the hand grip wound about with gold thread; and the sword was extremely sharp. He had a short spear, and a red silk short cloak over his coat, on which both before and behind was embroidered a lion, in yellow silk; and all men acknowledged that they had never seen a brisker, statelier man. (016-145) (016-146) (016-147) (016-148) (016-149) (016-150) (016-151)

A dust cloud was seen far inland, and the Northmen fell into order of battle. Messengers arrived with the promised supply of cattle, but as the Norse party returned to the shore, they were "violently assailed on all sides by the men of Down." (016-151) (016-152) (016-153) (016-154) (016-155) The Irish, in the words of the saga-writer Snorre Sturleson, "shot boldly; and although they fell in crowds, there came always two in place of one." (016-156) (016-157) Magnus, with most of his nobles, was slain on the spot. Vidkunner Johnsson escaped to the shipping "with the King's banner and the sword Legbiter." (016-158) (016-159) (016-160)

> Now when King Sigurd heard that his father had fallen, he set off immediately, leaving the Irish King's daughter behind, and proceeded in autumn, with the whole fleet directly to Norway. (016-161) (016-162) (016-163)

The annalists of Ulster record the event more tersely: "Magnus, King of Lochlan and the Isles, was slain by the Ulidians, with a slaughter of his people about him, while on a predatory excursion." They place the event in the year 1104. (016-164) (016-165) (016-166)

## Legacy

Magnus Barefoot was the last Norse king to lead a major military expedition into Irish waters. After his death, his son Sigurd abandoned the alliance with [[Murtogh O'Brien]] and sailed directly back to [[Norway]], ending the brief Norwegian intervention in Irish affairs. (016-161) (016-162) (016-163) The Irish Sea would soon be dominated not by the Northmen but by the Anglo-Normans, whose invasion of Ireland began less than seven decades later.

The Norse Sagas, particularly the account of Snorre Sturleson, preserve a romanticized but vivid portrait of Magnus --- a king who dressed in a silk cloak embroidered with a lion, wielded the sword Legbiter, and met his death on a foreign shore when the promised cattle failed to appear. His bones were buried at Iona, "the holy Island" he had spared years before. (016-108) In Irish annals he is remembered simply as "Magnus, King of Lochlan and the Isles," a Viking king who for a brief decade tried to revive the old Norse sea-empire in the west. (016-164)
