CHAPTER VII.

[058-001]BATTLE OF GLENMALURE—SIR JOHN PERROTT'S ADMINISTRATION—THE SPANISH

[058-002]ARMADA—LORD DEPUTY FITZWILLIAM—ESCAPE OF HUGH ROE O'DONNELL FROM DUBLIN

[058-003]CASTLE—THE ULSTER CONFEDERACY FORMED.

[058-004]In pursuing to its close the war in Munster, we were obliged to omit

[058-005]the mention of an affair of considerable importance, which somewhat

[058-006]consoled the Catholics for the massacre at Smerwick and the defeat of

[058-007]the Desmonds. We have already observed that what Aharlow was to the

[058-008]southern insurgents, the deep, secluded valley of Glenmalure was to the

[058-009]oppressed of Leinster. It afforded, at this period, refuge to a

[058-010]nobleman whose memory has been most improperly allowed to fall into

[058-011]oblivion. This was James Eustace, Viscount Baltinglass, who had

[058-012]suffered imprisonment in the Castle for refusing to pay an illegal tax

[058-013]of a few pounds, who was afterwards made the object of a special,

[058-014]vindictive enactment, known as "the Statute of Baltinglass," and was in

[058-015]the summer of 1580, on his keeping, surrounded by armed friends and

[058-016]retainers. His friend, Sir Walter Fitzgerald, son-in-law to the chief

[058-017]of Glenmalure, and many of the clansmen of Leix, Offally and Idrone,

[058-018]repaired to him at Slieveroe, near the modern village of Blessington,

[058-019]from which they proceeded to form a junction with the followers of the

[058-020]dauntless Feagh McHugh O'Byrne of Ballincor. Lord Grey, of Wilton, on

[058-021]reaching Dublin in August of that year, obtained information of this

[058-022]gathering, and determined to strike a decisive blow in Wicklow, before

[058-023]proceeding to the South. All the chief captains in the Queen's

[058-024]service—the Malbys, Dudleys, Cosbys, Carews, Moors—had repaired to meet

[058-025]him at Dublin, and now marched, under his command, into the

[058-026]neighbouring highlands. The Catholics, they knew, were concentrated in

[058-027]the valley, on one of the slopes of which Lord Grey constructed a

[058-028]strong camp, and then, having selected the fittest troops for the

[058-029]service, gave orders to attack the Irish camp. Sir William Stanley, one

[058-030]of the officers in command, well describes the upshot, in a letter to

[058-031]Secretary Walshingham: "When we entered the glen," he writes, "we were

[058-032]forced to slide, sometimes three or four fathoms, ere we could stay our

[058-033]feet; it was in depth, where we entered, at least a mile, full of

[058-034]stones, rocks, logs and wood; in the bottom thereof a river full of

[058-035]loose stones, which we were driven to Cross divers times * * * * before

[058-036]we were half through the glen, which is four miles in length, the enemy

[058-037]charged us very hotly * * * * it was the hottest piece of service that

[058-038]ever I saw, for the time, in any place." As might have been expected,

[058-039]the assailants were repulsed with heavy loss; among the slain were Sir

[058-040]Peter Carew, Colonel Francis Cosby of Mullaghmast memory, Colonel Moor,

[058-041]and other distinguished officers. The full extent of the defeat was

[058-042]concealed from Elizabeth, as well as it could be, in the official

[058-043]despatches; but before the end of August private letters, such as we

[058-044]have quoted, conveyed the painful intelligence to the court. The action

[058-045]was fought on the 25th day of August.

[058-046]Lord Grey's deputyship, though it lasted only two years, included the

[058-047]three decisive campaigns in the South, already described. At the period

[058-048]of his recall—or leave of absence—the summer of 1582, that "most

[058-049]populous and plentiful country," to use the forcible language of his

[058-050]eloquent Secretary, Edmund Spenser, was reduced to "a heap of carcasses

[058-051]and ashes." The war had been truly a war of extermination; nor did

[058-052]Munster recover her due proportion of the population of the island for

[058-053]nearly two centuries afterwards.

[058-054]The appointment of Sir John Perrott dates from 1583, though he did not

[058-055]enter on the duties of Lord Deputy till the following year. Like most

[058-056]of the public men of that age, he was both soldier and statesman. In

[058-057]temper he resembled his reputed father, Henry VIII.; for he was

[058-058]impatient of contradiction and control; fond of expense and

[058-059]magnificence, with a high opinion of his own abilities for diplomacy

[058-060]and legislation. The Parliament of 1585-6, as it was attended by almost

[058-061]every notable man in the kingdom, was one of his boasts, though no one

[058-062]seems to have benefited by it much, except Hugh O'Neil, whose title of

[058-063]Earl of Tyrone was then formally recognized. Subordinate to Perrott,

[058-064]the office of Governor of Connaught was held by Sir Richard

[058-065]Bingham—founder of the fortunes of the present Earls of Lucan—and that

[058-066]of President of Munster, by Sir Thomas Norris, one of four brothers,

[058-067]all employed in the Queen's service, and all destined to lose their

[058-068]lives in that employment.

[058-069]The most important events which marked the four years' administration

[058-070]of Perrott were the pacification of Thomond and Connaught, the capture

[058-071]of Hugh Roe O'Donnell, and the wreck of a large part of the Spanish

[058-072]Armada, on the northern and western coasts. The royal commission issued

[058-073]for the first-mentioned purpose exemplifies, in a striking manner, the

[058-074]exigencies of Elizabeth's policy at that moment. The persons entrusted

[058-075]with its execution were Sir Richard Bingham, the Earls of Thomond and

[058-076]Clanrickarde; Sir Turlogh O'Brien, Sir Richard Bourke (the McWilliam),

[058-077]O'Conor Sligo, Sir Brian O'Ruarc, and Sir Murrogh O'Flaherty. The chief

[058-078]duties of this singular commission were, to fix a money rental for all

[058-079]lands, free and unfree, in Clare and Connaught; to assess the taxation

[058-080]fairly due to the crown also in money; and to substitute generally the

[058-081]English law of succession for the ancient customs of Tanistry and

[058-082]gavelkind. In Clare, from fortuitous causes, the settlement they

[058-083]arrived at was never wholly reversed; in Connaught, the inhuman

[058-084]severity of Bingham rendered it odious from the first, and the

[058-085]successes of Hugh Roe O'Donnell, a few years later, were hailed by the

[058-086]people of that province as a heaven-sent deliverance.

[058-087]The treacherous capture of this youthful chieftain was one of the

[058-088]skilful devices on which Sir John Perrott most prided himself. Although

[058-089]a mere lad, the mysterious language of ancient prophecy, which seemed

[058-090]to point him out for greatness, give him consequence in the eyes of

[058-091]both friends and foes. Through his heroic mother, a daughter of the

[058-092]Lord of the Isles, he would naturally find allies in that warlike race.

[058-093]His precocious prowess and talents began to be noised abroad, and

[058-094]stimulated Perrott to the employment of an elaborate artifice, which,

[058-095]however, proved quite successful. A ship, commanded by one Bermingham,

[058-096]was sent round to Donegal, under pretence of being direct from Spain.

[058-097]She carried some casks of Spanish wine, and had a crew of 50 armed men.

[058-098]This ship dropped anchor off Rathmullen Castle on Lough Swilly, in

[058-099]which neighbourhood the young O'Donnell—then barely fifteen—was staying

[058-100]with his foster-father, McSweeny, and several companions of his own

[058-101]age. The unsuspecting youths were courteously invited on board the

[058-102]pretended Spanish ship, where, while they were being entertained in the

[058-103]cabin, the hatches were fastened down, the cable slipped, the sails

[058-104]spread to the wind, and the vessel put to sea. The threats and promises

[058-105]of the astonished clansmen as they gathered to the shore were answered

[058-106]by the mockery of the crew, who safely delivered their prize in Dublin,

[058-107]to the great delight of the Lord Deputy and his Council. Five weary

[058-108]years of fetters and privation the young captives were doomed to pass

[058-109]in the dungeons of the Castle before they breathed again the air of

[058-110]their native North.

[058-111]But now every ship that reached the English or Irish ports brought

[058-112]tidings more and more positive of the immense armada which King Philip

[058-113]was preparing to launch from the Tagus against England. The piratical

[058-114]exploits of Hawkins and Drake against the Spanish settlements in

[058-115]America, the barbarous execution of Mary, Queen of Scots, and the open

[058-116]alliance of Elizabeth with the Dutch insurgents, all acted as

[058-117]stimulants to the habitual slowness of the Spanish sovereign. Another

[058-118]event, though of minor importance, added intensity to the national

[058-119]quarrel. Sir William Stanley, whose account of the battle of Glenmalure

[058-120]we lately quoted, went over to Philip with 1,300 English troops, whom

[058-121]he commanded as Governor of Daventer, and was taken into the counsels

[058-122]of the Spanish sovereign. The fleet for the invasion of England was on

[058-123]a scale commensurate with the design. One hundred and thirty-five

[058-124]vessels of war, manned by 8,000 sailors, and carrying 19,000 soldiers,

[058-125]sailed from the Tagus, and after encountering a severe storm off Cape

[058-126]Finesterre, re-assembled at Corunna. The flower of Spanish bravery

[058-127]embarked in this fleet, named somewhat presumptuously "the invincible

[058-128]armada." The sons of Sir James Fitzmaurice, educated at Alcala, Thomas,

[058-129]son of Sir John of Desmond, with several other Irish exiles, laymen,

[058-130]and ecclesiastics, were also on board. The fate of the expedition is

[058-131]well known. A series of disasters befell it on the coasts of France and

[058-132]Belgium, and finally, towards the middle of August, a terrific storm

[058-133]swept the Spaniards northward through the British channel, scattering

[058-134]ships and men helpless and lifeless on the coasts of Scotland, and even

[058-135]as far north as Norway. On the Irish shore nineteen great vessels were

[058-136]sunk or stranded. In Lough Foyle, one galleon, manned by 1,100 men,

[058-137]came ashore, and some of the survivors, it is alleged, were given up by

[058-138]O'Donnell to the Lord Deputy, in the vain hope of obtaining in return

[058-139]the liberation of his son. Sir John O'Doherty in Innishowen, Sir Brian

[058-140]O'Ruarc at Dromahaire, and Hugh O'Neil at Dungannon, hospitably

[058-141]entertained and protected several hundreds who had escaped with their

[058-142]lives. On the iron-bound coast of Connaught, over 2,000 men perished.

[058-143]In Galway harbour, 70 prisoners were taken by the Queen's garrison, and

[058-144]executed on St. Augustine's hill. In the Shannon, the crew of a

[058-145]disabled vessel set her on fire, and escaped to another in the offing.

[058-146]On the coasts of Cork and Kerry nearly one thousand men were lost or

[058-147]cast away. In all, according to a state paper of the time, above 6,000

[058-148]of the Spaniards were either drowned, killed, or captured, on the

[058-149]north, west, and southern coasts. A more calamitous reverse could not

[058-150]have befallen Spain or Ireland in the era of the Reformation.

[058-151]It is worthy of remark that at the very moment the fear of the armada

[058-152]was most intensely felt in England—the beginning of July—Sir John

[058-153]Perrott was recalled from the government. His high and imperious

[058-154]temper, not less than his reliance on the native chiefs, rather than on

[058-155]the courtiers of Dublin Castle, had made him many enemies. He was

[058-156]succeeded by a Lord Deputy of a different character—Sir William

[058-157]Fitzwilliam—who had filled the same office, for a short period,

[058-158]seventeen years before. The administration of this nobleman was

[058-159]protracted till the year 1594, and is chiefly memorable in connection

[058-160]with the formation of the Ulster Confederacy, under the leadership of

[058-161]O'Neil and O'Donnell.

[058-163]Fitzwilliam, whose master passion was avarice, had no sooner been sworn

[058-164]into the government than he issued a commission to search for treasure,

[058-165]which the shipwrecked Spaniards were supposed to have saved. "In hopes

[058-166]to finger some of it," he at once marched into the territory of O'Ruarc

[058-167]and O'Doherty; O'Ruarc fled to Scotland, was given up by order of James

[058-168]VI., and subsequently executed at London; O'Doherty and Sir John

[058-169]O'Gallagher, "two of the most loyal subjects in Ulster," were seized

[058-170]and confined in the Castle. An outrage of a still more monstrous kind

[058-171]was perpetrated soon after on the newly elected chieftain of Oriel,

[058-172]Hugh McMahon. Though he had engaged Fitzwilliam by a bribe of 600 cows

[058-173]to recognize his succession, he was seized by order of the Deputy,

[058-174]tried by a jury of common soldiers, on a trumped up charge of

[058-175]"treason," and executed at his own door. Sir Harry Bagnal who, as

[058-176]Marshal of Ireland, had his head-quarters at Newry, next to Fitzwilliam

[058-177]himself, profited most by the consequent partition and settlement of

[058-178]McMahon's vast estates. Emboldened by the impunity which attended such

[058-179]high-handed proceedings, and instigated by the Marshal, Fitzwilliam

[058-180]began to practise, against the ablest as well as the most powerful of

[058-181]all the Northern chiefs, who had hitherto been known only as a courtier

[058-182]and soldier of the Queen. This was Hugh O'Neil, Earl of Tyrone, another

[058-183]of Sir Henry Sidney's "strong men," with the additional advantage of

[058-184]being familiar from his youth with the character of the men he was now

[058-185]to encounter.

[058-186]O'Neil, in the full prime of life, really desired to live in peace with

[058-187]Elizabeth, provided he might be allowed to govern Ulster with all the

[058-188]authority attached to his name. Bred up in England, he well knew the

[058-189]immense resources of that kingdom, and the indomitable character of its

[058-190]queen. A patriot of Ulster rather than of Ireland, he had served

[058-191]against the Desmonds, and had been a looker on at Smerwick. To suppress

[058-192]rivals of his own clan, to check O'Donnell's encroachments, and to

[058-193]preserve an interest at the English Court, were the objects of his

[058-194]earlier ambition. In pursuing these objects he did not hesitate to

[058-195]employ English troops in Ulster, nor to accompany the Queen and her

[058-196]Deputy to the service of the Church of England. If, however, he really

[058-197]believed that he could long continue to play the Celtic Prince north of

[058-198]the Boyne, and the English Earl at Dublin or London, he was soon

[058-199]undeceived when the fear of the Spanish Armada ceased to weigh on the

[058-200]Councils of Elizabeth.

[058-201]A natural son of John the Proud, called from the circumstances of his

[058-202]birth "Hugh of the fetters," communicated to Fitzwilliam the fact of

[058-203]Tyrone having sheltered the shipwrecked Spaniards, and employed them in

[058-204]opening up a correspondence with King Philip. This so exasperated the

[058-205]Earl, that, having seized the unfortunate Hugh of the fetters, he

[058-206]caused him to be hanged as a common felon—a high-handed proceeding

[058-207]which his enemies were expert in turning to account. To protect himself

[058-208]from the consequent danger, he went to England in May, 1590, without

[058-209]obtaining the license of the Lord Deputy, as by law required. On

[058-210]arriving in London he was imprisoned, but, in the course of a month,

[058-211]obtained his liberty, after signing articles, in which he agreed to

[058-212]drop the Celtic title of O'Neil; to allow the erection of gaols in his

[058-213]country; that he should execute no man without a commission from the

[058-214]Lord Deputy, except in cases of martial law; that he should keep his

[058-215]troop of horsemen in the Queen's pay, ready for the Queen's service,

[058-216]and that Tyrone should be regularly reduced to shire-ground. For the

[058-217]performance of these articles, which he confirmed on reaching Dublin,

[058-218]he was to place sureties in the hands of certain merchants of that

[058-219]city, or gentlemen of the Pale, enjoying the confidence of the Crown.

[058-220]On such hard conditions his earldom was confirmed to him, and he was

[058-221]apparently taken into all his former favour. But we may date the

[058-222]conception of his latter and more national policy from the period of

[058-223]this journey, and the brief imprisonment he had undergone in London.

[058-224]The "profound dissembling mind" which English historians, his

[058-225]cotemporaries, attribute to O'Neil, was now brought into daily

[058-226]exercise. When he discovered money to be the master passion of the Lord

[058-227]Deputy, he procured his connivance at the escape of Hugh Roe O'Donnell

[058-228]from Dublin Castle. On a dark night in the depth of winter the youthful

[058-229]chief, with several of his companions, succeeded in escaping to the

[058-230]hills in the neighbourhood of Powerscourt; but, exhausted and

[058-231]bewildered, they were again taken, and returned to their dungeons. Two

[058-232]years later, the heir of Tyrconnell was more fortunate. In Christmas

[058-233]week, 1592, he again escaped, through a sewer of the Castle, with Henry

[058-234]and Art O'Neil, sons of John the Proud. In the street they found

[058-235]O'Hagan, the confidential agent of Tyrone, waiting to guide them to the

[058-236]fastness of Glenmalure. Through the deep snows of the Dublin and

[058-237]Wicklow highlands the prisoners and their guide plodded their way.

[058-238]After a weary tramp they at length sunk down overwhelmed with fatigue.

[058-239]In this condition they were found insensible by a party despatched by

[058-240]Feagh O'Byrne; Art O'Neil, on being raised up, fell backward and

[058-241]expired; O'Donnell was so severely frost-bitten that he did not recover

[058-242]for many months the free use of his limbs. With his remaining companion

[058-243]he was nursed in the recesses of Glenmalure, until he became able to

[058-244]sit a horse, when he set out for home. Although the utmost vigilance

[058-245]was exercised by all the warders of the Pale, he crossed the Liffey and

[058-246]the Boyne undiscovered, rode boldly through the streets of Dundalk, and

[058-247]found an enthusiastic welcome, first from Tyrone in Dungannon, and soon

[058-248]after from the aged chief, his father, in the Castle of Ballyshannon.

[058-249]Early in the following year, the elder O'Donnell resigned the

[058-250]chieftaincy in favour of his popular son, who was, on the 3rd of May,

[058-251]duly proclaimed the O'Donnell, from the ancient mound of Kilmacrenan.

[058-252]The Ulster Confederacy, of which, for ten years, O'Neil and O'Donnell

[058-253]were the joint and inseparable leaders, was now imminent. Tyrone, by

[058-254]carrying off, the year previous to O'Donnell's escape, the beautiful

[058-255]sister of Marshal Bagnal, whom he married, had still further inflamed

[058-256]the hatred borne to him by that officer. Bagnal complained bitterly of

[058-257]the abduction to the Queen, charging, among other things, that O'Neil

[058-258]had a divorced wife still alive. A challenge was in consequence sent

[058-259]him by his new brother-in-law, but the cartel was not accepted. Every

[058-260]day's events were hastening a general alliance between the secondary

[058-261]chieftains of the Province and the two leading spirits. The O'Ruarc and

[058-262]Maguire were attacked by Bingham, and successfully defended themselves

[058-263]until the Lord Deputy and the Marshal also marched against them,

[058-264]summoning O'Neil to their aid. The latter, feeling that the time was

[058-265]not yet ripe, temporized with Fitzwilliam during the campaign of 1593,

[058-266]and though in the field at the head of his horsemen, nominally for the

[058-267]Queen, he seems to have rather employed his opportunities to promote

[058-268]that Northern Union which he had so much at heart.
