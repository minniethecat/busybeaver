The **Book of Rights** (Irish: *Lebor na Cert*) is an ancient Irish legal compilation traditionally attributed to the era of [[Saint Patrick]], which served as the constitutional foundation of early Gaelic Ireland. It outlines the rights, tributes, and obligations of the various kings and chieftains across the provinces, establishing a framework for governance that endured for centuries. [004-006]

## Origins and Compilation

The Book of Rights was prepared under the auspices of [[saint-patrick|Saint Patrick]] and, though not immediately nor simultaneously adopted, was in the end accepted by all of Erin as its supreme law. It represents the original institutes of Ireland under her Celtic Kings. [004-003] The printed bilingual edition published in [[dublin|Dublin]] in 1847 fills some 250 octavo pages, testifying to the comprehensive nature of this ancient legal code. [004-007]

## Relationship to the Brehon Laws

The Book of Rights bears the same relationship to the [[Brehon Law|Brehon laws]] as the Statutes at Large of England or the United States bear to the English Constitution — that is, the Book of Rights represents the constitutional framework, while the Brehon laws constitute the body of statutory and customary legal rulings derived from it. [004-010] Together, these two bodies of law formed the complete legal system of ancient Ireland. [004-009]

## Constitutional Function

Under the Constitution of Patrick, as embodied in the Book of Rights, Christianity was established as the law of the land. A prince who clung to any remnant of ancient Paganism might lawfully be refused those rents and dues which alone supported his dignity. [004-078] All national usages and customs not conflicting with this supreme Christian law were recognized and sanctioned. [004-080]

The internal revenues of each particular province were modelled upon the same general principle, with one memorable exception — the special tribute which [[leinster|Leinster]] paid to Munster, which proved to be the cause of more bloodshed than all other sources of domestic quarrel combined. [004-082]

## Tributes and Obligations

The Book of Rights meticulously records the tributes owed between the various kingdoms and provinces of Ireland. From its pages we learn that the Scottish Princes paid out of Alba seven shields, seven steeds, seven bondswomen, seven bondsmen, and seven hounds — all of the same breed — as their annual tribute to the Irish crown. [005-141] These tributes, known as "chief-rent," were distinct from the military obligations of the colonists, who were bound to furnish a contingent force by land and sea when the King of Ireland went to war. [005-139]

The annual exchanges described in the Book of Rights would, in aggregate, have required the transfer of several hundreds of slaves yearly from one set of masters to another. [021-072] This gives a sense of the scale of economic activity and obligation that the ancient constitution regulated.

## Historical Significance

The Book of Rights is one of the most important surviving documents of early Irish civilization. Alongside the Brehon laws, it provides a window into the sophisticated legal and political structures that existed in Ireland before the [[Norman Invasion of Ireland|Norman invasion]]. The text continued to be referenced and respected throughout the medieval period, and its principles influenced Irish legal thinking well into the early modern era. [004-075]
