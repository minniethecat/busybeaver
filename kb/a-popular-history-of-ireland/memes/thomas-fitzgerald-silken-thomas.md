## Overview

Thomas Fitzgerald, 10th Earl of Kildare (1513–1537), known to history as "Silken Thomas" for the silken fringes worn by his retainers, led one of the most dramatic rebellions of the Tudor era in Ireland. Summoned to defend his family's power against the machinations of his enemies at the English court, the young lord flung down the sword of state before the Irish Council in 1534 and raised the standard of revolt against Henry VIII. The rebellion, though brief and doomed, accelerated the Tudor policy of annexation and marked a turning point in the subjugation of the Geraldine interest. (049-077)

## The Fitzgerald Dynasty

Silken Thomas was the son of Gerald Fitzgerald, 9th Earl of Kildare, known as "Gearoid Mor," who for three decades had governed Ireland as the King's Deputy, ruling the Pale and much of the Gaelic marches through a network of alliances and marriages that made the Fitzgeralds the most powerful family in Ireland. In 1534, the old Earl was summoned to London to answer complaints laid against him by his enemies, principally the Butlers of Ormond and their allies at court. He left his son Thomas, then just twenty-one years of age, as his acting deputy. (049-015)

## The Rebellion of 1534

In June 1534, false rumours reached Dublin that the Earl of Kildare had been executed in the Tower of London. Enraged and fearful, the young Lord Thomas gathered a retinue of 140 horsemen and rode to the Council Chamber at St. Mary's Abbey in Dublin. (049-077)

> Lord Thomas Fitzgerald flung down the sword of State on the table of the Council, and renounced his allegiance to King Henry VIII. (049-077)

The act was one of theatrical defiance. After renouncing his allegiance, Silken Thomas withdrew from Dublin and began a campaign against the Pale, attacking the lands of the Butlers and the King's loyalists. His forces laid siege to Dublin Castle, though they failed to take it, and for a time the rebellion threatened to topple English authority in Ireland. (049-086)

## The Murder of Archbishop Allan

> One great excess stained the cause of "Silken Thomas," while it was but six weeks old. (049-090)

Towards the end of July 1534, Archbishop John Allan of Dublin — the Fitzgeralds' most implacable enemy — attempted to flee Dublin by sea under cover of darkness. His vessel, whether by design or accident, ran ashore at Clontarf, and the Archbishop went into hiding at Artane. He was discovered by Fitzgerald's followers and, despite his appeals for mercy, was dragged out and killed. The murder of an Archbishop, even one as politically active as Allan, horrified public opinion and alienated many who might otherwise have sympathized with the Geraldine cause. (049-091)

The rebellion limped on for another year. Thomas's father, the old Earl, died in the Tower of London in September 1534 — of natural causes, not execution — but by then the revolt had lost its original justification and momentum. In March 1535, a substantial English army under Sir William Skeffington arrived, equipped with artillery that Thomas's castles could not withstand. Maynooth Castle, the great Fitzgerald stronghold, was bombarded and stormed, its garrison put to the sword. (049-085)

## Surrender and Execution

In August 1535, Silken Thomas surrendered to Lord Leonard Grey, the new Lord Deputy, on a promise of his life. He was sent to England and imprisoned in the Tower of London. For over a year he languished there, while the King and his ministers debated his fate. In February 1537, he and his five uncles were taken to Tyburn and executed as traitors. (049-085)

The execution of the Fitzgeralds sent a shockwave through the Anglo-Irish nobility. Never before had an Earl of Kildare been executed by an English king. The event marked the end of the feudal autonomy that the great Anglo-Irish families had enjoyed for three centuries, and cleared the way for the direct Tudor administration of Ireland under English-born Lord Deputies. (057-030)

## Consequences

The rebellion of Silken Thomas, though "unconcerted, unprepared, and reckless," hastened the Tudor revolution in Ireland. The vast Fitzgerald estates in Kildare, Carlow, and elsewhere were seized by the Crown, and the policy of governing Ireland through native-born Anglo-Irish magnates was abandoned in favour of English appointees. The dissolution of the monasteries and the extension of the Reformation to Ireland followed hard upon, as the King's writ ran into regions that had previously acknowledged only the authority of the local lords. (057-081)

> The abortive insurrection of Silken Thomas, together with the suppression of the Geraldine league, left the northern tribes still unbroken and undismayed. (052-180)

In the longer perspective, the fall of the House of Kildare created a power vacuum that the Tudor state struggled to fill. The Gaelic lords of the north, the O'Neills and O'Donnells, observed the destruction of their hereditary rivals with mixed feelings, and the English Crown would soon find that destroying the Fitzgeralds was easier than governing the territories they had once controlled. (052-181)
