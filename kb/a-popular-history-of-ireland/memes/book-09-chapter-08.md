CHAPTER VIII.

[071-001]GLAMORGAN'S TREATY—THE NEW NUNCIO RINUCCINI—O'NEIL'S POSITION—THE

[071-002]BATTLE OF BENBURB.

[071-003]Ormond had amused the Confederates with negotiations for a permanent

[071-004]peace and settlement, from spring till midsummer, when Charles,

[071-005]dissatisfied with these endless delays, despatched to Ireland a more

[071-006]hopeful ambassador. This was Herbert, Earl of Glamorgan, one of the few

[071-007]Catholics remaining among the English nobility; son and heir to the

[071-008]Marquis of Worcester, and son-in-law to Henry O'Brien, Earl of Thomond.

[071-009]Of a family devoutly attached to the royal cause, to which it is said

[071-010]they had contributed not less than 200,000 pounds, Glamorgan's

[071-011]religion, his rank, his Irish connections, the intimate confidence of

[071-012]the King which he was known to possess, all marked out his embassy as

[071-013]one of the utmost importance.

[071-014]The story of this mission has been perplexed and darkened by many

[071-015]controversies. But the general verdict of historians seems now to be,

[071-016]that Charles I., whose many good qualities as a man and a ruler are

[071-017]cheerfully admitted on all hands, was yet utterly deficient in

[071-018]downright good faith; that duplicity was his besetting sin; and that

[071-019]Glamorgan's embassy is one, but only one, of the strongest evidences of

[071-020]that ingrained duplicity.

[071-021]It may help to the clearer understanding of the negotiations conducted

[071-022]by Glamorgan in Ireland, if we give in the first place the exact dates

[071-023]of the first transactions. The Earl arrived at Dublin about the 1st of

[071-024]August, and, after an interview with Ormond, proceeded to Kilkenny. On

[071-025]the 28th of that month, preliminary articles were agreed to and signed

[071-026]by the Earl on behalf of the King, and by Lords Mountgarrett and

[071-027]Muskerry on behalf of the Confederates. It was necessary, it seems, to

[071-028]get the concurrence of the Viceroy to these terms, and accordingly the

[071-029]negotiators on both sides repaired to Dublin. Here, Ormond contrived to

[071-030]detain them ten long weeks in discussions on the articles relating to

[071-031]religion; it was the 12th of November when they returned to Kilkenny,

[071-032]with a much modified treaty. On the next day, the 13th, the new Papal

[071-033]Nuncio, a prelate who, by his rank, his eloquence, and his imprudence,

[071-034]was destined to exercise a powerful influence on the Catholic councils,

[071-035]made his public entry into that city.

[071-036]This personage was John Baptist Rinuccini, Archbishop of Fermo, in the

[071-037]Marches of Ancona, which see he had preferred to the more exalted

[071-038]dignity of Florence. By birth a Tuscan, the new Nuncio had

[071-039]distinguished himself from boyhood by his passionate attachment to his

[071-040]studies. At Bologna, at Perugia, and at Rome, his intense application

[071-041]brought him early honours, and early physical debility. His health,

[071-042]partially restored in the seclusion of his native valley of the Arno,

[071-043]enabled him to return again to Rome. Enjoying the confidence of Gregory

[071-044]XV. and Uban VIII., he was named successively, Clerk of the Chamber,

[071-045]Secretary of the Congregation of Rites, and Archbishop of Fermo. This

[071-046]was the prelate chosen by the new Pope, Innocent X., for the nunciature

[071-047]in Ireland: a man of noble birth, in the fifty-third year of his age,

[071-048]of uncertain bodily health, of great learning, especially as a

[071-049]canonist, of a fiery Italian temperament,—"regular and even austere in

[071-050]his life, and far from any taint of avarice or corruption,"—such was

[071-051]the admission of his enemies.

[071-052]Leaving Italy in May, accompanied by the Dean of Fermo, who has left us

[071-053]a valuable record of the embassy, his other household officers, several

[071-054]Italian noblemen, and Sir Richard Belling, the special agent at Rome,

[071-055]the Nuncio, by way of Genoa and Marseilles, reached Paris. In France he

[071-056]was detained nearly five months, in a fruitless attempt to come to some

[071-057]definite arrangement as to the conduct of the Catholic war, through

[071-058]Queen Henrietta Maria, then resident with the young Prince of

[071-059]Wales—afterwards Charles II.—at the French court. The Queen, like most

[071-060]persons of her rank, overwhelmed with adversity, was often unreasonably

[071-061]suspicious and exacting. Her sharp woman's tongue did not spare those

[071-062]on whom her anger fell, and there were not wanting those, who,

[071-063]apprehensive of the effect in England of her negotiating directly with

[071-064]a papal minister, did their utmost to delay or to break off their

[071-065]correspondence. A nice point of court etiquette further embarrassed the

[071-066]business. The Nuncio could not uncover his head before the Queen, and

[071-067]Henrietta would not receive him otherwise than uncovered. After three

[071-068]months lost in Paris, he was obliged to proceed on his journey,

[071-069]contenting himself with an exchange of complimentary messages with the

[071-070]Queen, whom even the crushing blow of Naseby could not induce to waive

[071-071]a point of etiquette with a Priest.

[071-072]On reaching Rochelle, where he intended to take shipping, a further

[071-073]delay of six weeks took place, as was supposed by the machinations of

[071-074]Cardinal Mazarin. Finally, the Nuncio succeeded in purchasing a frigate

[071-075]of 26 guns, the _San Pietro_, on which he embarked with all his Italian

[071-076]suite, Sir Richard Belling, and several Franco-Irish officers. He had

[071-077]also on board a considerable sum in Spanish gold, (including another

[071-078]contribution of 36,000 dollars from Father Wadding,) 2,000 muskets,

[071-079]2,000 cartouch belts, 4,000 swords, 2,000 pike heads, 400 brace of

[071-080]pistols, 20,000 pounds of powder, with match, shot, and other stores.

[071-081]Weighing from St. Martin's in the Isle of Rhe, the _San Pietro_ doubled

[071-082]the Land's End, and stood over towards the Irish coast. The third day

[071-083]out they were chased for several hours by two Parliamentary cruisers,

[071-084]but escaped under cover of the night; on the fourth morning, being the

[071-085]21st of October, they found themselves safely embayed in the waters of

[071-086]Kenmare, on the coast of Kerry.

[071-087]The first intelligence which reached the Nuncio on landing, was the

[071-088]negotiation of Glamorgan, of which he had already heard, while waiting

[071-089]a ship at Rochelle. The next was the surrender by the Earl of Thomond,

[071-090]of his noble old castle of Bunratty, commanding the Shannon within six

[071-091]miles of Limerick, to the Puritans. This surrender had, however,

[071-092]determined the resolution of the city of Limerick, which hitherto had

[071-093]taken no part in the war, to open its gates to the Confederates. The

[071-094]loss of Bunratty was more than compensated by the gaining of one of the

[071-095]finest and strongest towns in Munster, and to Limerick accordingly the

[071-096]Nuncio paid the compliment of his first visit. Here he received the

[071-097]mitre of the diocese in dutiful submission from the hands of the

[071-098]Bishop, on entering the Cathedral; and here he celebrated a solemn

[071-099]requiem mass for the repose of the soul of the Archbishop of Tuam,

[071-100]lately slain before Sligo. From Limerick, borne along on his litter,

[071-101]such was the feebleness of his health, he advanced by slow stages to

[071-102]Kilkenny, escorted by a guard of honour, despatched on that duty, by

[071-103]the Supreme Council.

[071-104]The pomp and splendour of his public entry into the Catholic capital

[071-105]was a striking spectacle. The previous night he slept at a village

[071-106]three miles from the city, for which he set out early on the morning of

[071-107]the 13th of November, escorted by his guard, and a vast multitude of

[071-108]the people. Five delegates from the Supreme Council accompanied him. A

[071-109]band of fifty students mounted on horseback met him on the way, and

[071-110]their leader, crowned with laurel, recited some congratulatory Latin

[071-111]verses. At the city gate he left the litter and mounted a horse richly

[071-112]housed; here the procession of the clergy and the city guilds awaited

[071-113]him; at the Market Cross, a Latin oration was delivered in his honour,

[071-114]to which he graciously replied in the same language. From the Cross he

[071-115]was escorted to the Cathedral, at the door of which he was received by

[071-116]the aged Bishop, Dr. David Rothe. At the high altar he intonated the

[071-117]_Te Deum_, and gave the multitude the apostolic benediction. Then he

[071-118]was conducted to his lodgings, where he was soon waited upon by Lord

[071-119]Muskerry and General Preston, who brought him to Kilkenny Castle,

[071-120]where, in the great gallery, which elicited even a Florentine's

[071-121]admiration, he was received in stately formality by the President of

[071-122]the Council—Lord Mountgarrett. Another Latin oration on the nature of

[071-123]his embassy was delivered by the Nuncio, responded to by Heber, Bishop

[071-124]of Clogher, and so the ceremony of reception ended.

[071-125]The Nuncio brought from Paris a new subject of difficulty, in the form

[071-126]of a memorial from the English Catholics at Rome, praying that they

[071-127]might be included in the terms of any peace which might be made by

[071-128]their Irish co-religionists with the King. Nothing could be more

[071-129]natural than that the members of the same persecuted church should make

[071-130]common cause, but nothing could be more impolitic than some of the

[071-131]demands made in the English memorial. They wished it to be stipulated

[071-132]with Charles, that he would allow a distinct military organization to

[071-133]the English and Irish Catholics in his service, under Catholic general

[071-134]officers, subject only to the King's commands, meaning thereby, if they

[071-135]meant what they said, independence of all parliamentary and ministerial

[071-136]control. Yet several of the stipulations of this memorial were, after

[071-137]many modifications and discussions, adopted by Glamorgan into his

[071-138]original articles, and under the treaty thus ratified, the Confederates

[071-139]bound themselves to despatch 10,000 men, fully armed and equipped, to

[071-140]the relief of Chester and the general succour of the King in England.

[071-141]Towards the close of December, the English Earl, with two Commissioners

[071-142]from the Supreme Council, set forth for Dublin, to obtain the Viceroy's

[071-143]sanction to the amended treaty. But in Dublin a singular counterplot in

[071-144]this perplexed drama awaited them. On St. Stephen's day, while at

[071-145]dinner, Glamorgan was arrested by Ormond, on a charge of having

[071-146]exceeded his instructions, and confined a close prisoner in the castle.

[071-147]The gates of the city were closed, and every means taken to give

[071-148]_éclat_ to this extraordinary proceeding. The Confederate Commissioners

[071-149]were carried to the castle, and told they might congratulate themselves

[071-150]on not sharing the cell prepared for Glamorgan. "Go back," they were

[071-151]told, "to Kilkenny and tell the President of the Council, that the

[071-152]Protestants of England would fling the King's person out at his window,

[071-153]_if they believed it possible_ that he lent himself to such an

[071-154]undertaking." The Commissioners accordingly went back and delivered

[071-155]their errand, with a full account of all the circumstances.

[071-156]Fortunately, the General Assembly had been called for an early day in

[071-157]January, 1646, at Kilkenny. When, therefore, they met, their first

[071-158]resolution was to despatch Sir Robert Talbot to the Viceroy, with a

[071-159]letter suspending all negotiations till the Earl of Glamorgan was set

[071-160]at liberty. By the end of January, on the joint bail, for 40,000

[071-161]pounds, of the Earls of Clanrickarde and Kildare, the English envoy was

[071-162]enlarged, and, to the still further amazement of the simple-minded

[071-163]Catholics, on his arrival at Kilkenny, he justified rather than

[071-164]censured the action of Ormond. To most observers it appeared that these

[071-165]noblemen understood each other only too well.

[071-166]From January till June, Kilkenny was delivered over to cabals,

[071-167]intrigues, and recriminations. There was an "old Irish party," to which

[071-168]the Nuncio inclined, and an "Anglo-Irish party," headed by Mountgarrett

[071-169]and the majority of the Council. The former stigmatized the latter as

[071-170]Ormondists, and the latter retorted on them with the name of the

[071-171]Nuncio's party. In February came news of a foreign treaty made at Rome

[071-172]between Sir Kenelm Digby and the Pope's Ministers, most favourable to

[071-173]the English and Irish Catholics. On the 28th of March, a final

[071-174]modification of Glamorgan's articles, reduced to thirty in number, was

[071-175]signed by Ormond for the King, and Lord Muskerry and the other

[071-176]Commissioners for the Confederates. These thirty articles conceded, in

[071-177]fact, all the most essential claims of the Irish; they secured them

[071-178]equal rights as to property, in the Army, in the Universities, and at

[071-179]the Bar; they gave them seats in both Houses and on the Bench; they

[071-180]authorized a special commission of Oyer and Terminer, composed wholly

[071-181]of Confederates; they declared that "the independency of the Parliament

[071-182]of Ireland on that of England," should be decided by declaration of

[071-183]both Houses "agreeably to the laws of the Kingdom of Ireland." In

[071-184]short, this final form of Glamorgan's treaty gave the Irish Catholics,

[071-185]in 1646, all that was subsequently obtained either for the church or

[071-186]the country, in 1782, 1793, or 1829. Though some conditions were

[071-187]omitted, to which Rinuccini and a majority of the Prelates attached

[071-188]importance, Glamorgan's treaty was, upon the whole, a charter upon

[071-189]which a free church and a free people might well have stood, as the

[071-190]fundamental law of their religious and civil liberties.

[071-192]The treaty, thus concluded at the end of March, was to lie as an

[071-193]_escroll_ in the hands of the Marquis of Clanrickarde till the 1st of

[071-194]May, awaiting Sir Kenelm Digby with the Roman protocol. And then, not

[071-195]withstanding the dissuasions of Rinuccini to the contrary, it was to be

[071-196]kept secret from the world, though some of its obligations were

[071-197]expected to be at once fulfilled, on their side, by the Catholics. The

[071-198]Supreme Council, ever eager to exhibit their loyalty, gathered together

[071-199]6,000 troops for the relief of Chester and the service of the King in

[071-200]England, so soon as both treaties—the Irish and the Roman—should be

[071-201]signed by Charles. While so waiting, they besieged and took Bunratty

[071-202]castle—already referred to—but Sir Kenelm Digby did not arrive with

[071-203]May, and they now learned, to their renewed amazement, that Glamorgan's

[071-204]whole negotiation was disclaimed by the King in England. In the same

[071-205]interval Chester fell, and the King was obliged to throw himself into

[071-206]the hands of the Scottish Parliament, who surrendered him for a price

[071-207]to their English coadjutors. These tidings reached Ireland during May,

[071-208]and, varied with the capture of an occasional fortress, lost or won,

[071-209]occupied all men's minds. But the first days of June were destined to

[071-210]bring with them a victory of national—of European importance—won by

[071-211]Owen O'Neil, in the immediate vicinity of his grand-uncle's famous

[071-212]battle-field of the Yellow Ford.

[071-213]During these three years of intrigue and negotiation, the position of

[071-214]General O'Neil was hazardous and difficult in the extreme. One campaign

[071-215]he had served under a stranger, as second on his own soil. In the other

[071-216]two he was fettered by the terms of "cessation" to his own quarters;

[071-217]and to add to his embarrassments, his impetuous kinsman Sir Phelim,

[071-218]brave, rash, and ambitious, recently married to a daughter of his

[071-219]ungenerous rival, General Preston, was incited to thwart and obstruct

[071-220]him amongst their mutual clansmen and connections. The only recompense

[071-221]which seems to have been awarded to him, was the confidence of the

[071-222]Nuncio, who, either from that knowledge of character in which the

[071-223]Italians excel, or from bias received from some other source, at once

[071-224]singled him out as the man of his people. What portion of the Nuncio's

[071-225]supplies reached the Northern General we know not, but in the beginning

[071-226]of June, he felt himself in a position to bring on an engagement with

[071-227]Monroe, who, lately reinforced by both Parliaments, had marched out of

[071-228]Carrickfergus into Tyrone, with a view of penetrating as far south as

[071-229]Kilkenny. On the 4th day of June, the two armies encountered at

[071-230]Benburb, on the little river Blackwater, about six miles north of

[071-231]Armagh, and the most signal victory of the war came to recompense the

[071-232]long-enduring patience of O'Neil.

[071-233]The battle of Benburb has been often and well described. In a naturally

[071-234]strong position—with this leader the choice of ground seems to have

[071-235]been a first consideration—the Irish, for four hours, received and

[071-236]repulsed the various charges of the Puritan horse. Then as the sun

[071-237]began to descend, pouring its rays upon the opposing force, O'Neil led

[071-238]his whole force—five thousand men against eight—to the attack. One

[071-239]terrible onset swept away every trace of resistance. There were counted

[071-240]on the field, 3,243 of the Covenanters, and of the Catholics, but 70

[071-241]killed and 100 wounded. Lord Ardes, and 21 Scottish officers, 32

[071-242]standards, 1,500 draught horses, and all the guns and tents, were

[071-243]captured. Monroe fled in panic to Lisburn, and thence to Carrickfergus,

[071-244]where he shut himself up, till he could obtain reinforcements. O'Neil

[071-245]forwarded the captured colours to the Nuncio, at Limerick, by whom they

[071-246]were solemnly placed in the choir of St. Mary's Cathedral, and

[071-247]afterwards, at the request of Pope Innocent, sent to Rome. _Te Deum_

[071-248]was chanted in the Confederate Capital; penitential psalms were sung in

[071-249]the Northern fortress. "The Lord of Hosts," wrote Monroe, "had rubbed

[071-250]shame on our faces, till once we are humbled;" O'Neil emblazoned the

[071-251]cross and keys on his banner with the Red Hand of Ulster, and openly

[071-252]resumed the title originally chosen by his adherents at Clones, "the

[071-253]Catholic Army."
