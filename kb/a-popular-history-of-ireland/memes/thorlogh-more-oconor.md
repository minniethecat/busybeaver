## Overview

Thorlogh More O'Conor (Turlough Mor O'Conor, 1088–1156) was High King of Ireland during the mid-12th century and the most powerful ruler of his era. The ambition of Thorlogh O'Conor had added a fifth dynasty to the number of competitors for the sovereignty. (019-003)

He was the first of the latter kings who maintained a regular fleet at sea; at one time his Connaught galleys did service on the coast of Cork, at another they co-operated with his land forces in the harbour of Derry. (019-008) His reign marked the peak of O'Conor power before the Norman invasion.

Thorlogh More was the father of [[Roderick O'Conor]], the last High King of Ireland before the Anglo-Norman conquest. (019-163)

## Rise to Power

Thorlogh succeeded to the kingship of Connacht in the early decades of the 12th century. Through a combination of military skill and political acumen, he extended his authority over the competing Irish kingdoms.

By the opening of the twelfth century, Thorlogh More had emerged as a dominant figure among the Irish princes. (018-129) His rise was part of the larger shift in power from the O'Brien dynasty of Munster to the O'Conors of Connacht, a process that had been underway since the death of [[Brian Boru]] at Clontarf.

The year of his greatest power was the fifteenth of his reign (A.D. 1151), when his most signal success was obtained over his most formidable antagonists. (019-011)

## The Battle of Moanmore (1151)

The decisive battle of Thorlogh's reign was fought at Moanmore in 1151. Thorlogh O'Brien, King of Munster, had on foot in that year an army of three battalions, each battalion consisting of 3,000 men, with which force he overawed some and compelled others of the southern chiefs to withdraw their homage from O'Conor. (019-014)

Thorlogh More united his own forces with those of Meath and Leinster, marched southward, and encountered O'Brien's army at Moanmore:

> He encountered, at Moanmore, the army of the south, and defeated them, with the enormous loss of 7,000 men—a slaughter unparalleled throughout the war of succession. Every leading house in North Munster mourned the loss of either its chief or its tanist; some great families lost three, five, or seven brothers on that sanguinary day. (019-022, 019-025)

The household of Kinkora was left without an heir, and many a near kinsman's seat was vacant in its hospitable hall. (019-027) The O'Brien himself was banished into Ulster, where he received hospitality from Murkertach, Prince of Aileach. (019-028)

[[Dermid McMurrogh]], King of Leinster, fought gallantly on the side of the victor at Moanmore. (019-032)

## The Partition of Munster

Following his victory, Thorlogh exercised the victor's prerogative with ruthless efficiency. Earlier in the same year, King Thorlogh had divided Munster into three parts, giving Desmond to MacCarthy, Ormond to Thaddeus O'Brien, who had fought under him at Moanmore, and leaving the remainder to the O'Brien. (019-048)

> By these subdivisions the politic monarch expected to weaken to a great degree the power of the rival families of Meath and Munster. It was an arbitrary policy which could originate only on the field of battle, and could be enforced only by the sanction of victory. (019-052, 019-054)

Thorlogh O'Brien, once King of all Munster, refused to accept a mere third, and carrying away his jewels and valuables, including the drinking horn of the great Brian, he threw himself again on the protection of Murkertach of Aileach. (019-056)

## Fleet and Military Innovation

Thorlogh More was the first Irish monarch to maintain a regular fleet at sea, a significant innovation in Irish warfare. His Connaught galleys operated on both the southern and northern coasts, projecting power far beyond the inland territories that had traditionally defined Irish kingship. (019-008)

This naval capacity allowed him to threaten his enemies from multiple directions and to control the coastal trade routes that were increasingly important in the 12th-century Irish economy.

The peace which signalized the life of Thorlogh More was a new peace settlement negotiated with the northern princes. (019-109) His reign was marked by both military innovation and diplomatic engagement.

## The Dervorgoil Affair

The latter years of Thorlogh's reign were complicated by a personal scandal that would have far-reaching consequences. [[Dermid McMurrogh]], King of Leinster, eloped with Dervorgoil, wife of O'Ruarc of Breffni, and daughter of O'Melaghlin. (019-062)

Both O'Ruarc and O'Melaghlin appealed to the monarch for vengeance on Dermid. Up to this date Dermid had acted as a steadfast ally of O'Conor, but when compelled by the presence of a powerful force on his borders to restore Dervorgoil, he conceived an enmity for the aged king, which he extended, with increased virulence, to his son and successor. (019-065, 019-068)

This enmity would ultimately lead Dermid to seek Norman assistance, triggering the [[Norman Invasion of Ireland]].

## Death and Legacy

Thorlogh More's churches at Ballintober and elsewhere attest to his piety as well as his power. Churches were built by Thorlogh More O'Conor during his lifetime, contributing to the ecclesiastical revival of the 12th century. (020-158)

His son, [[Roderick O'Conor]], succeeded him and became the last native High King of Ireland before the Norman conquest. (019-163) The dynasty that Thorlogh established would remain a dominant force in Connacht for centuries afterward. However, the chronic civil war that characterised the contest for the high-kingship during his reign helped to prepare the path for the Anglo-Norman invaders.
