# Repeal Movement

The **Repeal Movement** was a political campaign of the 1830s and 1840s, led by [[Daniel O'Connell]], to repeal the [[Act of Union 1800]] and restore an independent [[Irish Parliament]] in [[dublin|Dublin]]. It was the largest mass political movement in Irish history before the Great Famine and demonstrated the power of peaceful popular agitation.

## Origins

The movement grew out of the successful campaign for [[Catholic Emancipation]], which [[daniel-oconnell|O'Connell]] had led in the 1820s through the [[Catholic Association]]. Emancipation, achieved in 1829, removed most legal disabilities on Catholics but left the Act of Union intact. [[daniel-oconnell|O'Connell]] and his followers believed that only a restored [[irish-parliament|Irish Parliament]] could address Irish grievances.

The achievement of [[catholic-emancipation|Catholic Emancipation]] had demonstrated the effectiveness of mass organization and peaceful agitation, tactics that [[daniel-oconnell|O'Connell]] now directed toward the repeal of the Union.

## O'Connell's Leadership

[[Daniel O'Connell]] was the central figure of the Repeal Movement. His oratory, political skill, and personal authority sustained the agitation through its rise and decline. [[daniel-oconnell|O'Connell]]'s position in Irish public life was unparalleled.

[[daniel-oconnell|O'Connell]]'s legal training and adherence to constitutional methods shaped the character of the movement. He insisted on peaceful means and respect for the law, even as he mobilized vast crowds in support of repeal.

## The Repeal Association and Mass Meetings

In 1840, [[daniel-oconnell|O'Connell]] founded the Loyal National Repeal Association to campaign for the restoration of the [[irish-parliament|Irish Parliament]]. The association collected a "repeal rent" from supporters across Ireland, building a national organization.

The movement reached its peak with the "monster meetings" of 1843, when enormous crowds gathered to hear [[daniel-oconnell|O'Connell]] speak. The largest of these, planned for Clontarf in October 1843, was banned by the British government, and [[daniel-oconnell|O'Connell]] called it off rather than risk bloodshed.

## Decline and Legacy

The banning of the Clontarf meeting marked the turning point of the movement. [[daniel-oconnell|O'Connell]] was arrested, convicted of conspiracy (a conviction later overturned by the House of Lords), and his health declined. After his death in 1847, the Repeal Movement fragmented.

Nevertheless, the movement left a lasting legacy. It demonstrated the power of mass political organization in Ireland and established the template for later constitutional nationalist movements. The Repeal campaign's methods — peaceful mass agitation, national organization, and the collection of a political rent — were adopted by later leaders in different forms.

> of the State. Of this party, Mr. O'Connell — [109-330]

The failure of repeal and the catastrophe of the Great Famine shifted Irish nationalism toward more radical paths, but [[daniel-oconnell|O'Connell]]'s constitutional tradition remained an enduring strand of Irish political thought.
