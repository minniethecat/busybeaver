## Overview

The Marquis de Saint Ruth (c. 1650–1691) was a French general dispatched by Louis XIV in 1691 to assume command of the Jacobite army in Ireland during the final phase of the Williamite War. Arriving with absolute military authority and a reputation for ruthless effectiveness, he reorganized the Jacobite forces and fought the last great pitched battle of the war at Aughrim — where he was killed in the moment of apparent victory. His death broke the morale of the Jacobite army and opened the way for the fall of Limerick. (083-088) (084-003)

> "When Saint Ruth arrived to take command-in-chief, he inspired new confidence in the Jacobite ranks." (083-088)

## Arrival in Ireland

Saint Ruth landed in Ireland in May 1691, accompanied by Generals D'Usson and De Tessé, bringing with them a supply of money, arms, and ammunition to replenish the depleted Jacobite treasury. His instructions from Louis XIV gave him "absolute powers" over the Irish army, and he "entered eagerly upon the discharge of his duties." His arrival was seen by the Jacobites as a turning point after the disasters of the Boyne and the first siege of Limerick. (083-100) (083-104) (084-003)

> "Saint Ruth, with Generals D'Usson and De Tessé, brought a fresh supply of arms and money to replenish the chest." (083-100)

## Reorganization of the Jacobite Army

Saint Ruth immediately set about reorganizing the Jacobite forces, which had been depleted by desertion and defeat. He concentrated the army at Athlone and Galway, imposed strict discipline, and restored a sense of purpose among officers and men. His reputation as a soldier of Louis XIV's school — the most formidable military machine in Europe — gave weight to his commands and raised the spirits of the Irish regiments. (084-003) (084-068)

## The Battle of Aughrim

The Battle of Aughrim, fought on 12 July 1691, was the decisive engagement of the Williamite War. Saint Ruth chose a strong defensive position on Kilcommodon Hill near the "little village of Aughrim," with boggy ground protecting his front and flanks. His force numbered between fifteen and twenty thousand men, well posted and confident. (084-078)

> "Saint Ruth's force must have numbered from fifteen to twenty thousand men, well posted and confident of victory." (084-078)

The battle raged through the afternoon. The Williamite assaults on the boggy approaches to Urrachree were repulsed with heavy losses, and "the assailants recoiled before the steady fire of the Irish infantry." At this moment, with victory appearing within his grasp, Saint Ruth rode forward to direct a counter-attack and was struck by a cannonball. His head was carried from the field. With their commander dead and no clear succession of command, the Jacobite army collapsed. The rout that followed was one of the bloodiest episodes in Irish military history. (084-082) (084-091) (084-043)

> "The sun went down on Aughrim, and the last great Irish battle of that war was fought and lost." (084-091)

## Legacy

Saint Ruth's death at Aughrim transformed the military situation. His loss "shook to pieces the remaining Irish army," and within months the war was effectively over. Limerick fell in October 1691, and the Jacobite cause in Ireland was extinguished. The anniversary of Aughrim, alongside that of the Boyne, was long commemorated by Irish Catholics as a day of national mourning. (084-099) (109-052)

Military historians have debated whether Saint Ruth's aggressive leadership at the critical moment of the battle was recklessness or the correct response to the tactical situation. What is certain is that his death, more than any other single event, sealed the fate of Jacobite Ireland.
