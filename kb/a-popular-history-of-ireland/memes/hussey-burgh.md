## Overview

Hussey Burgh (d. 1783) was an Irish barrister and parliamentarian of the patriot generation that transformed Irish politics in the second half of the eighteenth century. Alongside Henry Flood, Henry Grattan, and Barry Yelverton, he helped secure the legislative independence of the Irish Parliament in 1782.

## Entry into Parliament

Burgh entered the Irish House of Commons as part of a remarkable intake of new members elected to the Parliament of 1761. This cohort included men nurtured in the school of Swift and Anthony Malone, but determined to go beyond their masters in liberating the Irish legislature from the control of the British crown and Dublin Castle. (092-012, 092-013, 092-014, 092-015, 092-016)

Among those elected alongside Burgh were Dr. Charles Lucas, Dennis Bowes Daly, and the man destined to universal reputation — Henry Flood. Flood, then only twenty-eight years old and representing Kilkenny, was already regarded as the first man in Ireland in point of genius and acquirements. (092-017, 092-018, 092-019, 092-020, 092-021, 092-022)

## The Patriot Opposition

During the 1760s, Burgh aligned himself with the patriot opposition that pressed for constitutional reforms. The central demand of this period was the Septennial Bill, which sought to limit the duration of Parliament to seven years. Though repeatedly passed by the Irish Commons, the measure was repeatedly rejected in London before finally returning as an Octennial Act in 1768. (092-033, 092-034, 092-035, 092-036, 092-041, 092-042)

Burgh's standing among the patriots was evidenced at the funeral of Dr. Charles Lucas, the veteran reformer who died in 1771. At his funeral, the pall was borne by the most distinguished patriots of the age.

> At his funeral the pall was borne by the Marquis of Kildare, Lord Charlemont, Mr. Flood, Mr. Hussey Burgh, Sir Lucius O'Brien, and Mr. Ponsonby. (092-048, 092-049, 092-050)

## The Declaration of Independence

Burgh's most significant parliamentary moment came in 1782, during the crisis that followed the American Revolution. On the 19th of April, Henry Grattan rose in the Irish Commons to move his historic resolution declaring that "the King, Lords, and Commons of Ireland, are the only power competent to enact laws to bind Ireland." (095-067, 095-068, 095-069)

Burgh was among Grattan's core supporters in that debate, alongside Barry Yelverton and Forbes. The resolution faced opposition from Flood (who favoured postponement), Fitzgibbon (later Lord Clare), and John Foster (later Lord Oriel). Though a procedural amendment denied Grattan the honour of the exact wording, the substantial result was the same: the legislative independence that became known as "Grattan's Parliament." (095-069, 095-070, 095-071, 095-072, 095-073, 095-079)

## Legacy

Hussey Burgh represents the second rank of Irish patriot leadership — men whose names are less remembered than Grattan or Flood, but whose consistent support was essential to the constitutional victories of the 1780s. His career spanned the full arc of the patriot movement, from the Septennial Bill agitation of the 1760s to the legislative independence of 1782, and he died before witnessing the destruction of that achievement by the Act of Union in 1800.
