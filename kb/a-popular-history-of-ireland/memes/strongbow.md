## Overview

Richard de Clare, 2nd Earl of Pembroke (1130–1176), known to history by his nickname **Strongbow**, was the Norman lord who led the first major Anglo-Norman invasion of Ireland in 1170. Invited by the exiled King [[Dermid McMurrogh]] of [[leinster|Leinster]] to help recover his kingdom, Strongbow's landing at Waterford and subsequent conquest of [[dublin|Dublin]] fundamentally altered the course of Irish history. (023-080) (024-007)

From the strength of his arms he was nicknamed Strongbow, and his military prowess, combined with the political weakness of the Irish kings, enabled him to establish a formidable Norman presence in Ireland that drew the attention and eventual intervention of King [[Henry II]] of England. (023-080) (026-009)

## Biography

### Background and Early Career

Strongbow was the son of Gilbert de Clare, 1st Earl of Pembroke, and inherited his father's earldom and vast estates in Wales and England. His grandfather was a companion of Fitz-Stephen, one of the early Norman adventurers who had connections with Ireland. (017-108) (023-039)

By the 1160s, Strongbow's political fortunes in England had declined. He had backed the losing side in the civil war between King Stephen and the Empress Matilda, and found his estates reduced by the victorious [[henry-ii|Henry II]]. It was in this context of diminished prospects that the exiled [[Dermid McMurrogh]] arrived in Wales seeking Norman allies. (023-202)

### The Invitation to Ireland

[[Dermid McMurrogh]], [[dermid-mcmurrogh|King of Leinster]], had been driven from his kingdom by a coalition of Irish rivals led by [[Rory OConor]], the High King. Desperate for military assistance, Dermid traveled to Bristol and then to Wales, where he found Strongbow. Dermid offered Strongbow the hand of his daughter Eva in marriage and the succession to the [[leinster|Kingdom of Leinster]] in exchange for military aid. (024-007) (025-143)

Strongbow, alarmed by the proclamation of [[henry-ii|Henry II]] forbidding any subject from aiding Dermid without the king's consent, hesitated. He knew the risks of acting without royal permission, yet the prospect of a kingdom of his own was irresistible. After obtaining a reluctant and conditional consent from Henry, Strongbow made his preparations. (026-009) (026-029)

### The Invasion

Before Strongbow's departure for Ireland, three unsuccessful attempts to secure a foothold were made by his advance guard under [[robert-fitzstephen|Robert Fitz-Stephen]] and Maurice de Prendergast. These early forays tested Irish defenses and prepared the way for the main force. (026-029)

In August 1170, Strongbow landed near Waterford with a force of about 200 knights and 1,000 men-at-arms. The city of Waterford fell after a brief siege, and Strongbow married Eva in the captured city, binding himself to the royal line of [[leinster|Leinster]]. The combined Norman-Irish army then marched on [[dublin|Dublin]], which fell to a dramatic assault led by Milo de Cogan and Raymond le Gros. (026-079) (026-201)

### Consolidation and Royal Intervention

[[henry-ii|Henry II]], ever ready to raise up rivals to Strongbow, grew alarmed at the speed and scale of his vassal's success. In October 1171, the king himself landed in Ireland with a large army—not to conquer, but to ensure that Strongbow and the other Norman adventurers would hold their Irish lands as his vassals rather than as independent princes. (027-081)

Strongbow submitted to Henry and was granted the [[leinster|Kingdom of Leinster]] as a fief of the Crown, except for the city of [[dublin|Dublin]] and its environs, which Henry retained as royal demesne. This compromise established the pattern of Anglo-Norman lordship in Ireland for centuries to come. (027-104) (026-201)

### Final Years

Strongbow's fortunes fluctuated in his final years. In 1172–1173, he and his dwindling garrison in [[dublin|Dublin]] faced a massive siege by [[Rory OConor]] and a coalition of Irish kings. The defenders held out heroically, and a desperate sortie broke the siege. Strongbow retreated with his companions to the safety of the Pale, but his health was broken. (027-107) (027-149)

Strongbow died in [[dublin|Dublin]] in 1176, at the age of about forty-six. His allies and companions—Fitz-Stephen, de Cogan, Raymond le Gros—continued the work of conquest. The chronicler Giraldus Cambrensis, who left vivid personal sketches of the Norman adventurers, recorded that Strongbow's death was lamented by his followers as the loss of their greatest captain. (027-149) (027-170)

## Legacy

Strongbow's invasion of Ireland marked the beginning of seven centuries of English involvement in Irish affairs. His marriage to Eva and the subsequent Norman settlement created a hybrid Hiberno-Norman aristocracy that would dominate Irish life for generations. (027-212)

His daughter Isabella married William Marshal, one of the greatest knights of the age, and through her the inheritance of Pembroke and [[leinster|Leinster]] passed to future generations. From that union descended many of the great Anglo-Irish families, including the [[Earl of Kildare]] and the [[Earl of Desmond]]. (031-089) (031-115)

The port of Waterford, where Strongbow landed, remained an enduring symbol of the Norman presence in Ireland. His name became synonymous with the invasion itself, and later Irish historians often dated the beginning of "English" rule in Ireland from his arrival. (073-015)
