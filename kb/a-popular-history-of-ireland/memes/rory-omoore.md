## Overview

Rory O'Moore (Ruairí Óg Ó Mórdha, c. 1600–1653) was an Irish chieftain and one of the principal leaders of the Irish Rebellion of 1641 in the province of Leinster. Rory O'Moore, the chief of his name, avenged a massacre of his people by many a daring deed. (056-100)

> In rapid succession he surprised Naas, Athy, and Leighlin. From the rapidity with which his blows were struck in Kildare, Carlow, and Kilkenny, he appeared to be everywhere at once. (056-102, 056-103)

His swift campaign earned him a reputation as one of the most energetic and effective rebel commanders in the early phase of the Confederate Wars.

## The 1641 Rebellion

The Rebellion of 1641 began in Ulster but quickly spread to Leinster, where Rory O'Moore emerged as its principal leader. The O'Moore clan had been dispossessed of much of their ancestral territory in Laois during the Tudor plantations, and the rebellion offered an opportunity to reclaim what had been lost.

In rapid succession, O'Moore surprised Naas, Athy, and Leighlin, demonstrating a tactical boldness that caught the English authorities off guard. (056-102) His operations in Kildare, Carlow, and Kilkenny were characterised by speed and surprise.

Though the rebellion was marred by atrocities on both sides, O'Moore's military reputation survived the collapse of the initial rising. Unlike some leaders who were captured and executed in the immediate aftermath, O'Moore transitioned successfully into the Confederate phase of the war.

## The Confederate Wars

When the Confederate Catholics of Ireland established their own government at Kilkenny in 1642, O'Moore became one of its military commanders:

> Rory O'Moore also had never seen service abroad — unlike many of the Confederate officers who had gained experience in the armies of France and Spain — but his native skill as a guerrilla leader made him indispensable during the brief remainder of that year. (067-185, 067-188)

He fought alongside other notable Confederate commanders. At one engagement, Mountgarrett, Ikerrin, and Dunboyne, Sir Morgan Cavenagh, Rory O'Moore, and Hugh O'Byrne drew up 8,000 strong to dispute the passage of Ormond's army. (068-133)

The combat was short but murderous. The Confederates left 700 men, including Sir Morgan Cavenagh, and some other officers, dead on the field. (068-137)

O'Moore's intimate knowledge of the terrain of Laois, Carlow, and Kilkenny — the ancestral lands of his people — gave him an advantage that professional soldiers from the Continent could not match. He knew every pass, every bog, and every ford, and used that knowledge to devastating effect against English columns that ventured into the interior.

## Death and Legacy

Rory O'Moore did not live to see the final defeat of the Confederate cause. He died in 1653, as the Cromwellian conquest was reaching its grim conclusion. But his name survived in Irish memory as a byword for brave and resourceful resistance.

His rebellion preceded that of figures like [[Owen Roe ONeill]], who brought continental military expertise to the Irish cause, but O'Moore's energy in the opening months of 1641 was critical to transforming what might have been a localised Ulster rising into a nationwide war.

The O'Moore clan never recovered their ancestral lands, but Rory O'Moore's example — the dispossessed chieftain who fought back with skill and determination — became a powerful symbol in later Irish nationalist tradition.
