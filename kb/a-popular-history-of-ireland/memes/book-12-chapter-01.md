CHAPTER I.

[107-001]AFTER THE UNION—DEATH OF LORD CLARE—ROBERT EMMET'S EMEUTE.

[107-002]The plan of this brief compendium of Irish history obliges us to sketch

[107-003]for some years farther on, the political and religious annals of the

[107-004]Irish people. Having described in what manner their distinctive

[107-005]political nationality was at length lost, it only remains to show how

[107-006]their religious liberties were finally recovered.

[107-007]The first striking effect of the Union was to introduce Catholic

[107-008]Emancipation into the category of imperial difficulties, and to assign

[107-009]it the very first place on the list. By a singular retribution, the

[107-010]Pitt administration with its 200 of a House of Commons majority, its

[107-011]absolute control of the Lords, and its seventeen years' prescription in

[107-012]its favour, fell upon this very question, after they had used it to

[107-013]carry the Union, within a few weeks of the consummation of that Union.

[107-014]The cause of this crisis was the invincible obstinacy of the King, who

[107-015]had taken into his head, at the time of Lord Fitzwilliam's recall from

[107-016]Ireland, that his coronation oath bound him in conscience to resist the

[107-017]Catholic claims. The suggestion of this obstacle was originally Lord

[107-018]Clare's; and though Lord Kenyon and Lord Stowell had declared it

[107-019]unfounded in law, Lord Loughborough and Lord Eldon were unfortunately

[107-020]of a different opinion. With George III. the idea became a monomaniac

[107-021]certainty, and there is no reason to doubt that he would have preferred

[107-022]abdication to its abandonment.

[107-023]The King was not for several months aware how far his Prime Minister

[107-024]had gone on the Catholic question in Ireland. But those who were weary

[107-025]of Pitt's ascendancy, were, of course, interested in giving him this

[107-026]important information. The minister himself, wrapped in his austere

[107-027]self-reliance, did not volunteer explanations even to his Sovereign,

[107-028]and the King broke silence very unexpectedly, a few days after the

[107-029]first meeting of the Imperial Parliament (January 22nd, 1801). Stepping

[107-030]up to Mr. Dundas at the levee, he began in his usual manner, "What's

[107-031]this? what's this? this, that this young Lord (Castlereagh) has brought

[107-032]over from Ireland to throw at my head? The most Jacobinical thing I

[107-033]ever heard of! Any man who proposes such a thing is my personal enemy."

[107-034]Mr. Dundas replied respectfully but firmly, and immediately

[107-035]communicated the conversation to Mr. Pitt. The King's remarks had been

[107-036]overheard by the bystanders, so that either the minister or the

[107-037]Sovereign had now to give way. Pitt, at first, was resolute; the King

[107-038]then offered to impose silence on himself as regarded the whole

[107-039]subject, provided Mr. Pitt would agree to do likewise, but the haughty

[107-040]minister refused, and tendered his resignation. On the 5th of February,

[107-041]within five weeks of the consummation of the Union, this tender was

[107-042]most reluctantly and regretfully accepted. Lord Grenville, Mr. Dundas,

[107-043]and others of his principal colleagues went out of office with him;

[107-044]Lord Cornwallis and Lord Castlereagh following their example. Of the

[107-045]new Cabinet, Addington, the Speaker, was Premier, with Lord Hardwicke

[107-046]as Lord-Lieutenant of Ireland. By the enemies of Pitt this was looked

[107-047]upon as a mere administration _ad interim_; as a concerted arrangement

[107-048]to enable him to evade an unfavourable peace—that of Amiens—which he

[107-049]saw coming; but it is only fair to say, that the private letters of the

[107-050]period, since published, do not sanction any such imputation. It is,

[107-051]however, to be observed, _per contra_, that three weeks after his

[107-052]formal resignation, he had no hesitation in assuring the King, who had

[107-053]just recovered from one of his attacks brought on by this crisis, that

[107-054]he would never again urge the Catholic claims on his Majesty's notice.

[107-055]On this understanding he returned to office in the spring of 1804; to

[107-056]this compact he adhered till his death, in January, 1806.

[107-057]In Ireland, the events immediately consequent upon the Union, were such

[107-058]as might have been expected. Many of those who had been instrumental in

[107-059]carrying it, were disappointed and discontented with their new

[107-060]situation in the empire. Of these, the most conspicuous and the least

[107-061]to be pitied, was Lord Clare. That haughty, domineering spirit,

[107-062]accustomed to dictate with almost absolute power to the Privy

[107-063]Counsellors and peerage of Ireland, experienced nothing but

[107-064]mortification in the Imperial House of Lords. The part he hoped to play

[107-065]on that wider stage he found impossible to assume; he confronted there

[107-066]in the aged Thurlow and the astute Loughborough, law lords as absolute

[107-067]as himself, who soon made him conscious that, though a main agent of

[107-068]the Union, he was only a stranger in the united legislature. The Duke

[107-069]of Bedford reminded him that "the Union had not transferred his

[107-070]dictatorial powers to the Imperial Parliament;" other noble Lords were

[107-071]hardly less severe. Pitt was cold, and Grenville ceremonious; and in

[107-072]the arrangements of the Addington ministry he was not even consulted.

[107-073]He returned to Ireland before the first year of the Union closed, in a

[107-074]state of mind and temper which preyed upon his health. Before the

[107-075]second session of the Imperial Parliament assembled, he had been borne

[107-076]to the grave amid the revilings and hootings of the multitude. Dublin,

[107-077]true to its ancient disposition, which led the townsfolk of the twelfth

[107-078]century to bury the ancestor of Dermid McMurrogh with the carcass of a

[107-079]dog, filled the grave of the once splendid Lord Chancellor with every

[107-080]description of garbage.

[107-081]On the other hand, Lord Castlereagh, younger, suppler, and more

[107-082]accommodating to English prejudices, rose from one Cabinet office to

[107-083]another, until at length, in fifteen years from the Union, he directed

[107-084]the destinies of the Empire, as absolutely, as he had moulded the fate

[107-085]of Ireland. To Castlereagh and the Wellesley family, the Union was in

[107-086]truth, an era of honour and advancement. The sons of the spendthrift

[107-087]amateur, Lord Mornington, were reserved to rule India, and lead the

[107-088]armies of Europe; while the son of Flood's colleague in the Reform

[107-089]convention of 1783, was destined to give law to Christendom, at the

[107-090]Congress of Vienna.

[107-091]A career very different in all respects from those just mentioned,

[107-092]closed in the second year of Dublin's widowhood as a metropolis. It was

[107-093]the career of a young man of four-and-twenty, who snatched at immortal

[107-094]fame and obtained it, in the very agony of a public, but not for him, a

[107-095]shameful death. This was Robert, youngest brother of Thomas Addis

[107-096]Emmet, whose _emeute_ of 1803 would long since have sunk to the level

[107-097]of other city riots, but for the matchless dying speech of which it was

[107-098]the prelude and the occasion. This young gentleman was in his 20th year

[107-099]when expelled with nineteen others from Trinity College, in 1798, by

[107-100]order of the visitors, Lord Clare and Dr. Duigenan. His reputation as a

[107-101]scholar and debater was already established within the college walls,

[107-102]and the highest expectations were naturally entertained of him, by his

[107-103]friends. One of his early college companions—Thomas Moore—who lived to

[107-104]know all the leading men of his age, declares that of all he had ever

[107-105]known, he would place among "the highest of the few" who combined in

[107-106]"the greatest degree pure moral worth with intellectual power"—Robert

[107-107]Emmet. After the expatriation of his brother, young Emmet visited him

[107-108]at Fort-George, and proceeded from thence to the Continent. During the

[107-109]year the Union was consummated he visited Spain, and travelled through

[107-110]Holland, France, and Switzerland, till the peace of Amiens.

[107-111]Subsequently he joined his brother's family in Paris, and was taken

[107-112]into the full confidence of the exiles, then in direct communication

[107-113]with Buonaparte and Talleyrand. It was not concealed from the Irish by

[107-114]either the First Consul, or his minister, that the peace with England

[107-115]was likely to have a speedy termination; and, accordingly, they were

[107-116]not unprepared for the new declaration of war between the two

[107-117]countries, which was officially made at London and Paris, in May,

[107-118]1803—little more than twelve months after the proclamation of the peace

[107-119]of Amiens.

[107-120]It was in expectation of this rupture, and a consequent invasion of

[107-121]Ireland, that Robert Emmet returned to Dublin, in October, 1802, to

[107-122]endeavour to re-establish in some degree the old organization of the

[107-123]United Irishmen. In the same expectation, McNevin, Corbet, and others

[107-124]of the Irish in France, formed themselves, by permission of the First

[107-125]Consul, into a legion, under command of Tone's trusty aid-de-camp,

[107-126]McSheehey; while Thomas Addis Emmet and Arthur O'Conor remained at

[107-127]Paris, the plenipotentiaries of their countrymen. On the rupture with

[107-128]England Buonaparte took up the Irish negotiation with much earnestness;

[107-129]he even suggested to the exiles the colours and the motto under which

[107-130]they were to fight, when once landed on their own soil. The flag on a

[107-131]tricolour ground, was to have a green centre, bearing the letters

[107-132]_R.I.—Republique Irlandaise_. The legend at large was to be:

[107-133]_L'independence de l'Irlande—Liberte de Conscience_; a motto which

[107-134]certainly told the whole story. The First Consul also suggested the

[107-135]formation of an Irish Committee at Paris, and the preparation of

[107-136]statements of Irish grievances for the _Moniteur_, and the

[107-137]semi-official papers.

[107-138]Robert Emmet seems to have been confidently of opinion soon after his

[107-139]return to Dublin, that nineteen out of the thirty-two counties would

[107-140]rise; and, perhaps, if a sufficient French force had landed, his

[107-141]opinion might have been justified by the fact. So did not think,

[107-142]however, John Keogh, Valentine Lawless (Lord Cloncurry), and other

[107-143]close observers of the state of the country. But Emmet was

[107-144]enthusiastic, and he inspired his own spirit into many. Mr. Long, a

[107-145]merchant, placed 1,400 pounds sterling at his disposal; he had himself,

[107-146]in consequence of the recent death of his father, stock to the amount

[107-147]of 1,500 pounds converted into cash, and with these funds he entered

[107-148]actively on his preliminary preparations. His chief confidants and

[107-149]assistants were Thomas Russell and Mathew Dowdall, formerly prisoners

[107-150]at Fort-George, but now permitted to return; William Putnam McCabe, the

[107-151]most adventurous of all the party, a perfect Proteus in disguise; Gray,

[107-152]a Wexford attorney; Colonel Lumm of Kildare, an old friend of Lord

[107-153]Edward Fitzgerald; Mr. Long, before mentioned; Hamilton, an Enniskillen

[107-154]barrister, married to Russell's niece; James Hope of Templepatrick, and

[107-155]Michael Dwyer, the Wicklow outlaw, who had remained since '98

[107-156]uncaptured in the mountains.

[107-157]In the month of March, when the renewal of hostilities with France was

[107-158]decided on in England, the preparations of the conspirators were pushed

[107-159]forward with redoubled energy. The still wilder conspiracy headed by

[107-160]Colonel Despard in London, the previous winter, the secret and the fate

[107-161]of which was well known to the Dublin leaders—Dowdall being Despard's

[107-162]agent—did not in the least intimidate Emmet or his friends. Despard

[107-163]suffered death in February, with nine of his followers, but his Irish

[107-164]confederates only went on with their arrangements with a more reckless

[107-165]resolution. Their plan was the plan of O'Moore and McGuire, to surprise

[107-166]the Castle, seize the authorities and secure the capital; but the

[107-167]Dublin of 1803 was in many respects very different from the Dublin of

[107-168]1641. The discontent, however, arising from the recent loss of the

[107-169]Parliament might have turned the city scale in Emmet's favour, had its

[107-170]first stroke been successful. The emissaries at work in the Leinster

[107-171]and Ulster counties gave besides sanguine reports of success, so that,

[107-172]judging by the information in his possession, an older and cooler head

[107-173]than Robert Emmet's might well have been misled into the expectation of

[107-174]nineteen counties rising if the signal could only be given from Dublin

[107-175]Castle. If the blow could be withheld till August, there was every

[107-176]reason to expect a French invasion of England, which would drain away

[107-177]all the regular army, and leave the people merely the militia and the

[107-178]volunteers to contend against. But all the Dublin arrangements exploded

[107-179]in the melancholy _emeute_ of the 23rd of July, 1803, in which the

[107-180]Chief-Justice, Lord Kilwarden, passing through the disturbed quarter of

[107-181]the city at the time, was cruelly murdered; for which, and for his

[107-182]cause, Emmet suffered death on the same spot on the 20th of September

[107-183]following. For the same cause, the equally pure-minded and chivalrous

[107-184]Thomas Russell was executed at Downpatrick; Kearney, Roche, Redmond and

[107-185]Howley also suffered death at Dublin; Allen, Putnam, McCabe, and

[107-186]Dowdall escaped to France, where the former became an officer of rank

[107-187]in the army of Napoleon; Michael Dwyer, who had surrendered on

[107-188]condition of being allowed to emigrate to' North America, died in exile

[107-189]in Australia, in 1825. Others of Emmet's known or suspected friends,

[107-190]after undergoing two, three, and even four years' imprisonment, were

[107-191]finally discharged without trial. Mr. Long, his generous banker, and

[107-192]James Hope, his faithful emissary, were both permitted to end their

[107-193]days in Ireland.

[107-195]The trial of Robert Emmet, from the wonderful death-speech delivered at

[107-196]it, is perfectly well known. But in justice to a man of genius equal if

[107-197]not superior to his own—an Irishman, whose memory is national property,

[107-198]as well as Emmet's, it must here be observed, that the latter never

[107-199]delivered, and had no justification to deliver the vulgar diatribe

[107-200]against Plunkett, his prosecutor, now constantly printed in the common

[107-201]and incorrect versions of that speech. Plunkett, as Attorney-General,

[107-202]in 1803, had no option but to prosecute for the crown; he was a

[107-203]politician of a totally different school from that of Emmet; he shared

[107-204]all Burke and Grattan's horror of French revolutionary principles. In

[107-205]the fervour of his accusatory oration he may have gone too far; he may

[107-206]have, and in reading it now, it is clear to us that he did press too

[107-207]hard upon the prisoner in the dock. He might have performed his awful

[107-208]office with more sorrow and less vehemence, for there was no doubt

[107-209]about his jury. But withal, he gave no fair grounds for any such retort

[107-210]as is falsely attributed to Emmet, the very style of which proves its

[107-211]falsity. It is now well known that the apostrophe in the death-speech,

[107-212]commencing "you viper," alleged to have been addressed to Plunkett, was

[107-213]the interpolation many years afterwards of that literary

[107-214]Ishmaelite—Walter Cox of the _Hibernian Magazine_,—who through such

[107-215]base means endeavoured to aim a blow at Plunkett's reputation. The

[107-216]personal reputation of the younger Emmet, the least known to his

[107-217]countrymen of all the United Irish leaders, except by the crowning act

[107-218]of his death, is safe beyond the reach of calumny, or party zeal, or

[107-219]time's changes. It is embalmed in the verse of Moore and Southey, and

[107-220]the precious prose of Washington Irvine. Men of genius in England and

[107-221]America have done honour to his memory; in the annals of his own

[107-222]country his name deserves to stand with those youthful chiefs, equally

[107-223]renowned, and equally ready to seal their patriotism with their

[107-224]blood—Sir Cahir O'Doherty and Hugh Roe O'Donnell.
