## Overview

Oliver Cromwell (1599–1658) was the Lord Protector of England, Scotland, and Ireland, and the commander of the Parliamentary forces during the Wars of the Three Kingdoms. His nine-month campaign in Ireland from August 1649 to May 1650 was one of the most consequential and devastating military episodes in Irish history, marked by the capture of numerous walled towns and the brutal slaughter of garrisons and civilians at Drogheda and Wexford. (073-007) (073-028)

The popular writer of Irish history confronts Cromwell not as a neutral figure but as the agent of a revolution that permanently altered the balance of land, power, and religion in Ireland. McGee prefaces his treatment by stating his duty is "the simple, and, as far as possible, the unimpassioned narrative of facts." (073-007) (073-012)

## Biography

### Arrival in Ireland

Cromwell left London in great state early in July 1649 and arrived in [[dublin|Dublin]] on the 15th of August. He brought with him two hundred thousand pounds, eight regiments of foot, six of horse, and troops of dragoons. Among his officers were Henry Cromwell, his second son and future Lord Deputy of Ireland; General Monck; Admiral Blake; Colonel Jones; [[henry-ireton|Henry Ireton]]; and Edmund Ludlow. (073-013) (073-024)

### The Campaign of 1649–1650

Cromwell's command in Ireland lasted only nine months, from mid-August 1649 to the end of May 1650, but was remarkable for the number of sieges crowded into that brief period. It was a campaign of seventeenth-century cannon against medieval masonry. (073-028) (073-033)

Drogheda, Dundalk, Newry, and Carlingford fell in September; Arklow, Enniscorthy, and Wexford in October; Ross surrendered the same month; Waterford was attempted and abandoned in November; Dungarvan, Kinsale, Bandon, and Cork were won over in December; Fethard, Callan, and Cashel in January and February; Carrick and Kilkenny in March; and Clonmel early in May. (073-035) (073-041)

### Drogheda and Wexford

The spirit in which these sieges were conducted was one of hatred and fanaticism. At Drogheda, the slaughter occupied five entire days. Of a garrison of 3,000 men, not thirty were spared—the survivors were sent "in hands for the Barbadoes." Old men, women, children, and priests were unsparingly put to the sword. (073-050) (073-055)

Wexford was betrayed by Captain James Stafford, whose midnight interview with Cromwell outside the walls is still recounted with horror in local tradition. The town's advantageous position on the Bristol Channel had made it particularly obnoxious to the Parliament. Cromwell reported to Speaker Lenthall: "There are great quantities of iron, hides, tallow, salt, pipe and barrel staves... near a hundred cannon in the fort." The consequences for the garrison and inhabitants were the same as at Drogheda. (073-056) (073-075)

### Death of Owen Roe O'Neill

In the third month of Cromwell's campaign, the death of [[owen-roe-oneill|Owen Roe O'Neil]]l went abroad, paralyzing the Catholic resistance. By common consent of friend and foe, O'Neill was considered the ablest civil and military leader in Ireland under the Stuart kings. Whether he could have coped with Cromwell was long debated, but the consciousness of irreparable national loss struck deep amid the crash of Cromwell's cannonades across [[leinster|Leinster]] and Munster. (073-076) (073-084)

### Clonmel and Recall

The last act of Cromwell's campaign was the siege of Clonmel. After its capitulation, Cromwell was recalled to lead the Parliamentary armies into Scotland. During his nine months in Ireland, he had captured five or six county capitals and a great number of lesser places, spreading the terror of his Ironsides across three Provinces. (073-041) (073-127)

## Legacy

Cromwell's campaign in Ireland was followed by the [[cromwellian-settlement|Cromwellian Settlement]], which imposed a massive transfer of land from Catholic Irish owners to English Protestant settlers and soldiers. The Settlement permanently altered Irish society, creating a Protestant landowning class and reducing the Catholic Irish to tenants on lands they had once owned. (074-010)

[[henry-ireton|Henry Ireton]], Cromwell's son-in-law, continued as commander in Ireland after Cromwell's departure. The Parliament's declaration of 1652 formalized the confiscation of Irish lands. The memory of Cromwell—"the curse of Cromwell"—has persisted in Irish folk memory for centuries as a byword for conquest, dispossession, and religious persecution. (074-015)
