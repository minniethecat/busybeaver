## Overview

Walter de Burgh (c. 1230–1271) was an Anglo-Norman lord who consolidated the de Burgh family's hold over Connaught during the 13th century. Through marriage and conquest, he extended the family's dominion across the Shannon and laid the foundation for the Earldom of Ulster that his descendants would later hold. (029-196)

His long rivalry with Feidlim O'Conor, the Gaelic King of Connaught, defined the political landscape of the west of Ireland during a period of intense conflict between the native Irish and the Norman settlers. (030-163)

## Acquisition of Connaught

Walter de Burgh came into his inheritance through a critical marriage alliance. The lordship of Connaught, originally granted to the de Lacy family, was transferred by royal patent in 1205 to Walter de Lacy. In 1264, de Lacy's only daughter Maud brought the territory to Walter de Burgh as her marriage portion, uniting the de Burgh and de Lacy inheritances. (029-195) (029-196)

> The territory was transferred in 1205, by royal patent, to Walter de Lacy, whose only daughter Maud brought it in the year 1264 to Walter de Burgh, lord of Connaught. [029-196]

This marriage established the de Burghs as the pre-eminent Anglo-Norman family in the west of Ireland. From this union, the lordship of Connaught passed through four female descendants before being reunited later in the male line. (029-197)

## The de Burgh Lordship

The de Burghs had been among the original Anglo-Norman invaders of Ireland in the late 12th century. By the mid-13th century, they had established a powerful lordship that extended from the Shannon to the Atlantic, controlling the strategic crossing points and the major towns of the west. (030-161)

Walter inherited a vast but contested domain. The native Gaelic lords, particularly the O'Conors of Connaught, had never fully submitted to Norman authority. His lordship required constant military vigilance and strategic alliances with both Gaelic and Norman families. (030-162)

## Rivalry with Feidlim O'Conor

The defining conflict of Walter de Burgh's career was his rivalry with Feidlim (Felim) O'Conor, the Gaelic King of Connaught. O'Conor was one of the most formidable opponents the de Burghs faced, leading a sustained resistance against Norman expansion in the west. (030-163)

The two lords clashed repeatedly for control of key territories and fortifications. The castle of Galway, completed during or shortly before Walter's time, became the administrative centre of de Burgh power — and the place where Walter would eventually die. (030-163)

Walter de Burgh was repulsed by the Irish at Shannon harbour, where his men were driven into the river and many perished. This defeat demonstrated the limits of de Burgh power even at its height. (032-050)

> Two years afterwards he repulsed Walter de Burgh at Shannon harbour, driving his men into the river, where many of them perished. [032-050]

## Death and Succession

Walter de Burgh died in the year 1271, in the Castle of Galway, leaving his great rival Feidlim O'Conor still in possession of significant territories in Connaught. The struggle between the de Burghs and the O'Conors would continue through his sons into the next generation. (030-162) (030-163)

> Walter de Burgh died in the year 1271, in the Castle of Galway; his great rival, Feidlim O'Conor, still possessed of extensive power in Connaught. [030-163]

In the next generation, the sons of Walter de Burgh continued the family's dual strategy of marriage alliance and military expansion. Over time, they acquired the forfeited title of Ulster that had belonged to John de Courcy, until death and time reunited both titles — Connaught and Ulster — in the same person. (030-161)

> In the next generation, did the sons of this Walter de Burgh, until death and time reunited both titles in the same person. [030-161]

## The de Burgh Legacy

The de Burgh family, later known as the Burkes (de Burca in Gaelic), became one of the most enduring Anglo-Norman lineages in Ireland. Unlike many of the early Norman families who were absorbed or expelled, the de Burghs maintained their power in the west for centuries through strategic marriages, military force, and gradual Gaelicization. (029-196)

The territories Walter de Burgh assembled formed the core of what later became the Clanrickarde and Mayo branches of the Burke family, which remained major landowners in Connaught into the modern era. (030-162)
