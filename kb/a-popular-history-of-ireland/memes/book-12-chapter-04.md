CHAPTER IV.

[110-001]O'CONNELL'S LEADERSHIP—1813 TO 1821.

[110-002]While the Veto controversy was carried into the press and the

[110-003]Parliamentary debates, the extraordinary events of the last years of

[110-004]Napoleon's reign became of such extreme interest as to cast into the

[110-005]shade all questions of domestic policy. The Parliamentary fortunes of

[110-006]the Catholic question varied with the fortunes of the war, and the

[110-007]remoteness of external danger. Thus, in 1815, Sir Henry Parnell's

[110-008]motion for a committee was rejected by a majority of 228 to 147; in

[110-009]1816, on Mr. Grattan's similar motion, the vote was 172 to 141; in

[110-010]1817, Mr. Grattan was again defeated by 245 to 221; in this session an

[110-011]act exempting officers in the army and navy from forswearing

[110-012]Transubstantiation passed and became law. The internal condition of the

[110-013]Catholic body, both in England and Ireland, during all those years, was

[110-014]far from enviable. In England there were Cisalpine and Ultramontane

[110-015]factions; in Ireland, Vetoists and anti-Vetoists. The learned and

[110-016]amiable Charles Butler—among jurists, the ornament of his order, was

[110-017]fiercely opposed to the no less learned Dr. Milner, author of "The End

[110-018]of Controversy," and "Letters to a Prebendary." In Ireland, a very

[110-019]young barrister, who had hardly seen the second anniversary of his

[110-020]majority, electrified the aggregate meetings with a new Franco-Irish

[110-021]order of eloquence, naturally enough employed in the maintenance of

[110-022]Gallican ideas of church government. This was Richard Lalor Shiel, the

[110-023]author of two or three successful tragedies, and the man, next to

[110-024]O'Connell, who wielded the largest tribunitian power over the Irish

[110-025]populace during the whole of the subsequent agitation. Educated at

[110-026]Stoneyhurst, he imbibed from refugee professors French idioms and a

[110-027]French standard of taste, while, strangely enough, O'Connell, to whom

[110-028]he was at first opposed, and of whom he became afterwards the first

[110-029]lieutenant, educated in France by British refugees, acquired the

[110-030]cumbrous English style of the Douay Bible and the Rheims Testament. The

[110-031]contrast between the two men was every way extreme; physically,

[110-032]mentally, and politically; but it is pleasant to know that their

[110-033]differences never degenerated into distrust, envy or malice; that, in

[110-034]fact, Daniel O'Connell had throughout all his after life no more

[110-035]steadfast personal friend than Richard Lalor Shiel.

[110-036]In the progress of the Catholic agitation, the next memorable incident

[110-037]was O'Connell's direct attack on the Prince Regent. That powerful

[110-038]personage, the _de facto_ Sovereign of the realm, had long amused the

[110-039]Irish Catholics with promises and pledges of being favourable to their

[110-040]cause. At an aggregate meeting, in June, 1812, Mr. O'Connell maintained

[110-041]that there were four distinct pledges of this description in existence:

[110-042]1. One given in 1806, through the Duke of Bedford, then

[110-043]Lord-Lieutenant, to induce the Catholics to withhold their petitions

[110-044]for a time. 2. Another given the same year in the Prince's name by Mr.

[110-045]Ponsonby, then Chancellor. 3. A pledge given to Lord Kenmare, _in

[110-046]writing_, when at Cheltenham. 4. A verbal pledge given to Lord Fingal,

[110-047]in the presence of Lords Clifford and Petre, and reduced to writing and

[110-048]signed by these three noblemen, soon after quitting the Prince's

[110-049]presence. Over the meeting at which this indictment was preferred, Lord

[110-050]Fingal presided, and the celebrated "witchery" resolutions, referring

[110-051]to the influence then exercised on the Prince by Lady Hertford, were

[110-052]proposed by his lordship's son, Lord Killeen. It may, therefore, be

[110-053]fairly assumed, that the existence of the fourth pledge was proved, the

[110-054]first and second were never denied, and as to the third—that given to

[110-055]Lord Kenmare—the only correction ever made was, that the Prince's

[110-056]message was delivered verbally, by his Private Secretary, Colonel

[110-057]McMahon, and not in writing. Lord Kenmare, who died in the autumn of

[110-058]1812, could not be induced, from a motive of delicacy, to reduce his

[110-059]recollection of this message to writing, but he never denied that he

[110-060]had received it, and O'Connell, therefore, during the following years,

[110-061]always held the Prince accountable for this, as for his other promises.

[110-062]Much difference of opinion arose as to the wisdom of attacking a person

[110-063]in the position of the Prince; but O'Connell, fully persuaded of the

[110-064]utter worthlessness of the declarations made in that quarter, decided

[110-065]for himself that the bold course was the wise course. The effect

[110-066]already was various. The English Whigs, the Prince's early and constant

[110-067]friends, who had followed him to lengths that honour could hardly

[110-068]sanction, and who had experienced his hollow-heartedness when lately

[110-069]called to govern during his father's illness; they, of course, were not

[110-070]sorry to see him held up to odium in Ireland, as a dishonoured

[110-071]gentleman and a false friend. The Irish Whigs, of whom Lord Moira and

[110-072]Mr. Ponsonby were the leaders, and to whom Mr. Grattan might be said to

[110-073]be attached rather than to belong, saw the rupture with regret, but

[110-074]considered it inevitable. Among "the Prince's friends" the attacks upon

[110-075]him in the Dublin meetings were regarded as little short of treason;

[110-076]while by himself, it is well known the "witchery" resolutions of 1812

[110-077]were neither forgotten nor forgiven.

[110-078]The political position of the Holy See, at this period, was such as to

[110-079]induce and enable an indirect English influence to be exercised,

[110-080]through that channel, upon the Irish Catholic movement. Pope Pius VII.,

[110-081]a prisoner in France, had delegated to several persons at Rome certain

[110-082]vicarious powers, to be exercised in his name, in case of necessity; of

[110-083]these, more than one had followed him into exile, so that the position

[110-084]of his representative devolved at length upon Monsignor Quarrantotti,

[110-085]who, early in 1814, addressed a rescript to Dr. Poynter,

[110-086]vicar-apostolic of the London district, commendatory of the Bill of

[110-087]1813, including the Veto, and the Ecclesiastical Commission proposed by

[110-088]Canning and Castlereagh. Against these dangerous concessions, as they

[110-089]considered them, the Irish Catholics despatched their remonstrances to

[110-090]Rome, through the agency of the celebrated Wexford Franciscan, Father

[110-091]Richard Hayes; but this clergyman, having spoken with too great

[110-092]freedom, was arrested, and suffered several months' confinement in the

[110-093]Eternal City. A subsequent embassy of Dr. Murray, coadjutor to the

[110-094]Archbishop of Dublin, on behalf of his brother prelates, was attended

[110-095]with no greater advantage, though the envoy himself was more properly

[110-096]treated. On his return to Ireland, at a meeting held to hear his

[110-097]report, several strong resolutions were unanimously adopted, of which

[110-098]the spirit may be judged from the following—the concluding one of the

[110-099]series—"Though we sincerely venerate the supreme Pontiff as visible

[110-100]head of the Church, we do not conceive that our apprehensions for the

[110-101]safety of the Roman Catholic Church in Ireland can or ought to be

[110-102]removed by any determination of His Holiness, adopted or intended to be

[110-103]adopted, not only without our concurrence, but in direct opposition to

[110-104]our repeated resolutions and the very energetic memorial presented on

[110-105]our behalf, and so ably supported by our Deputy, the Most Reverend Dr.

[110-106]Murray; who, in that quality, was more competent to inform His Holiness

[110-107]of the real state and interests of the Roman Catholic Church in Ireland

[110-108]than any other with whom he is said to have consulted."

[110-109]The resolutions were transmitted to Rome, signed by the two Archbishops

[110-110]present, by Dr. Everard, the coadjutor of the Archbishop of Cashel, by

[110-111]Dr. Murray, the coadjutor of the Archbishop of Dublin, by the Bishops

[110-112]of Meath, Cloyne, Clonfert, Kerry, Waterford, Derry, Achonry, Killala,

[110-113]Killaloe, Kilmore, Ferns, Limerick, Elphin, Cork, Down and Conor,

[110-114]Ossory, Raphoe, Clogher, Dromore, Kildare and Leighlin, Ardagh, and the

[110-115]Warden of Galway. Dr. Murray, and Dr. Murphy, Bishop of Cork, were

[110-116]commissioned to carry this new remonstrance to Rome, and the greatest

[110-117]anxiety was felt for the result of their mission.

[110-118]A strange result of this new _embroglio_ in the Catholic cause was,

[110-119]that it put the people on the defensive for their religious liberties,

[110-120]not so much against England as against Rome. The unlucky Italian

[110-121]Monsignor who had volunteered his sanction of the Veto, fared scarcely

[110-122]better at the popular gatherings than Lord Castlereagh, or Mr. Peel.

[110-123]"Monsieur Forty-eight," as he was nicknamed, in reference to some

[110-124]strange story of his ancestor taking his name from a lucky lottery

[110-125]ticket of that number, was declared to be no better than a common

[110-126]Orangeman, and if the bitter denunciations uttered against him, on the

[110-127]Liffey and the Shannon, had only been translated into Italian, the

[110-128]courtly Prelate must have been exceedingly amazed at the democratic

[110-129]fury of a Catholic population, as orthodox as himself, but much more

[110-130]jealous of State interference with things spiritual. The second order

[110-131]of the clergy were hardly behind the laity, in the fervour of their

[110-132]opposition to the rescript of 1814. Their entire body, secular and

[110-133]regular, residing in and about Dublin, published a very strong protest

[110-134]against it, headed by Dr. Blake, afterwards Bishop of Dromore, in which

[110-135]it was denounced as "pregnant with mischief" and entirely

[110-136]"non-obligatory upon the Catholic Church in Ireland." The several

[110-137]ecclesiastical provinces followed up these declarations with a

[110-138]surprising unanimity, and although a Vetoistical address to His

[110-139]Holiness was despatched by the Cisalpine club in England, the Irish

[110-140]ideas of Church government triumphed at Rome. Drs. Murray and Milner

[110-141]were received with his habitual kindness by Pius VII.; the illustrious

[110-142]Cardinal Gonsalvi was appointed by the Pope to draw up an explanatory

[110-143]rescript, and Monsignor Quarrantotti was removed from his official

[110-144]position. The firmness manifested at that critical period by the Irish

[110-145]church has since been acknowledged with many encomiums by all the

[110-146]successors of Pope Pius VII.

[110-147]The Irish government under the new Viceroy, Lord Whitworth (the former

[110-148]ambassador to Napoleon), conceiving that the time had come, in the

[110-149]summer of 1814, to suppress the Catholic Board, a proclamation

[110-150]forbidding his Majesty's subjects to attend future meetings of that

[110-151]body issued from Dublin Castle, on the 3rd of June. The leaders of the

[110-152]body, after consultation at Mr. O'Connell's residence, decided to bow

[110-153]to this proclamation and to meet no more as a Board; but this did not

[110-154]prevent them, in the following winter, from holding a new series of

[110-155]Aggregate meetings, far more formidable, in some respects, than the

[110-156]deliberative meetings which had been suppressed. In the vigorous and

[110-157]somewhat aggressive tone taken at these meetings, Lord Fingal, the

[110-158]chief of the Catholic peerage, did not concur, and he accordingly

[110-159]withdrew for some years from the agitation, Mr. Shiel, the Bellews, Mr.

[110-160]Ball, Mr. Wyse of Waterford, and a few others, following his example.

[110-161]With O'Connell remained the O'Conor Don, Messrs. Finlay and Lidwell

[110-162](Protestants), Purcell O'Gorman, and other popular persons. But the

[110-163]cause sustained a heavy blow in the temporary retirement of Lord Fingal

[110-164]and his friends, and an attempt to form a "Catholic Association," in

[110-165]1815, without their co-operation, signally failed.

[110-166]During the next five years, the fortunes of the great Irish question

[110-167]fluctuated with the exigencies of Imperial parties. The second American

[110-168]war had closed, if not gloriously, at least without considerable loss

[110-169]to England; Napoleon had exchanged Elba for St. Helena: Wellington was

[110-170]the Achilles of the Empire, and Castlereagh its Ulysses. Yet it was not

[110-171]in the nature of those free Islanders, the danger and pressure of

[110-172]foreign war removed, to remain always indifferent to the two great

[110-173]questions of domestic policy—Catholic Emancipation and Parliamentary

[110-174]Reform. In the session of 1816, a motion of Sir John Newport's to

[110-175]inquire into the state of Ireland, was successfully resisted by Sir

[110-176]Robert Peel, but the condition and state of public feeling in England

[110-177]could not be as well ignored by a Parliament sitting in London. In

[110-178]returning from the opening of the Houses in January, 1817, the Regent

[110-179]was hooted in the street, and his carriage riddled with stones. A

[110-180]reward of 1,000 pounds, issued for the apprehension of the ringleaders,

[110-181]only gave additional _éclat_ to the fact, without leading to the

[110-182]apprehension of the assailants.

[110-183]The personal unpopularity of the Regent seems to have increased, in

[110-184]proportion as death removed from him all those who stood nearest to the

[110-185]throne. In November, 1817, his oldest child, the Princess Charlotte,

[110-186]married to Leopold, since King of Belgium, died in childbed; in 1818,

[110-187]the aged Queen Charlotte died; in January, 1820, the old King, in the

[110-188]eighty-second year of his age, departed this life. Immediately

[110-189]afterwards the former Princess of Wales, long separated from her

[110-190]profligate husband, returned from the Continent to claim her rightful

[110-191]position as Queen Consort. The disgraceful accusations brought against

[110-192]her, the trial before the House of Lords which followed, the courage

[110-193]and eloquence of her counsel, Brougham and Denman, the eagerness with

[110-194]which the people made her cause their own, are all well remembered

[110-195]events, and all beside the purpose of this history. The unfortunate

[110-196]lady died after a short illness, on the 7th of August, 1821; the same

[110-197]month in which Ms Majesty—George IV.—departed on that Irish journey, so

[110-198]satirized in the undying verse of Moore and Byron.

[110-200]Two other deaths, far more affecting than any among the mortalities of

[110-201]royalty, marked the period at which we have arrived. These were the

[110-202]death of Curran in 1817, and the death of Grattan, in 1820.

[110-203]Curran, after his failure to be returned for Newry, in 1812, had never

[110-204]again attempted public life. He remained in his office of Master of the

[110-205]Rolls, but his health began to fail sensibly. During the summers of

[110-206]1816 and '17, he sought for recreation in Scotland, England and France,

[110-207]but the charm which travel could not give—the charm of a cheerful

[110-208]spirit—was wanting. In October, 1817, his friend, Charles Phillips, was

[110-209]suddenly called to his bed-side at Brompton, near London, and found him

[110-210]with one side of his face and body paralyzed cold. "And this was all,"

[110-211]says his friend, "that remained of Curran—the light of society—the

[110-212]glory of the forum—the Fabricius of the senate—the idol of his

[110-213]country." Yes! even to less than this, was he soon to sink. On the

[110-214]evening of the 14th of October, he expired, in the 68th year of his

[110-215]age, leaving a public reputation as free from blemish as ever did any

[110-216]man who had acted a leading part, in times like those through which he

[110-217]had passed. He was interred in London, but twenty years afterwards, the

[110-218]committee of the Glasnevin Cemetery, near Dublin, obtained permission

[110-219]of his representatives to remove his ashes to their grounds, where they

[110-220]now finally repose. A tomb modelled from the tomb of Scipio covers the

[110-221]grave, bearing the simple but sufficient inscription—CURRAN. Thus was

[110-222]fulfilled the words he had uttered long before—"The last duties will be

[110-223]paid by that country on which they are devolved; nor will it be for

[110-224]charity that a little earth will be given to my bones. Tenderly will

[110-225]those duties be paid, as the debt of well-earned affection, and of

[110-226]gratitude not ashamed of her tears."

[110-227]Grattan's last days were characteristic of his whole life. As the

[110-228]session of 1820 progressed, though suffering from his last struggle

[110-229]with disease, he was stirred by an irresistible desire to make his way

[110-230]to London, and present once more the petition of the Catholics. Since

[110-231]the defeat of his Relief Bill of 1813, there had been some estrangement

[110-232]between him and the more advanced section of the agitators, headed by

[110-233]O'Connell. This he was anxious, perhaps, to heal or to overcome. He

[110-234]thought, moreover, that even if he should die in the effort, it would

[110-235]be, as he said himself, "a good end." Amid—

[110-236]"The trees which a nation had given, and which bowed

[110-237]As if each brought a new civic crown to his head,"

[110-238]he consulted with the Catholic delegates early in May. O'Connell was

[110-239]the spokesman, and the scene may yet be rendered immortal by some great

[110-240]national artist. All present felt that the aged patriot was dying, but

[110-241]still he would go once more to London, to fall, as he said, "at his

[110-242]post." In leaving Ireland he gave to his oldest friends directions for

[110-243]his funeral—that he might be buried in the little churchyard of

[110-244]Moyanna, on the estate the people gave him in 1782! He reached London,

[110-245]by slow stages, at the end of May, and proposed to be in his place in

[110-246]the House on the 4th of June. But this gratification was not permitted

[110-247]him: on the morning of the 4th, at six o'clock, he called his son to

[110-248]his bed-side, and ordered him to bring him a paper containing his last

[110-249]political opinions. "Add to it," he said, with all his old love of

[110-250]antithesis, "that I die with a love of liberty in my heart, and this

[110-251]declaration in favour of my country, in my hand."

[110-252]So worthily ended the mortal career of Henry Grattan. He was interred

[110-253]by the side of his old friend, Charles James Fox, in Westminster Abbey;

[110-254]the mourners included the highest imperial statesmen, and the Catholic

[110-255]orphan children; his eulogium was pronounced in the House of Commons by

[110-256]William Conyngham Plunkett, and in the Irish capital by Daniel

[110-257]O'Connell.
