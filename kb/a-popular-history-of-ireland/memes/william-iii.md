## Early Life and Ascension

William III, Prince of Orange and Stadtholder of the Dutch Republic, ascended the English, Scottish, and Irish thrones through the Glorious Revolution of 1688, which displaced his father-in-law, [[James II]]. In Ireland, [[james-ii|James II]]'s viceroy, the Earl of Tyrconnell, had consolidated Catholic control, and the country swiftly became the primary theatre of conflict between the two monarchs. By April 1689, James had arrived in Ireland with French support, and the Protestant strongholds of [[Derry]] and Enniskillen had declared for William and Mary as King and Queen, beginning the Williamite War. (081-050)

The relief of Derry at the end of July 1689, combined with the death of the Jacobite leader Viscount Dundee at the battle of Killicrankie in Scotland five days earlier, freed King William from immediate anxiety on the side of Scotland and enabled him to concentrate his whole disposable force on Ireland. (081-147, 081-148) On the 13th of August, an army of eighteen regiments of foot and four or five of horse, under the veteran Marshal Duke de Schomberg, landed in Belfast Lough and seized Carrickfergus after eight days of bombardment. (081-150, 081-151, 081-155) Schomberg, however, advanced cautiously, entrenching at Dundalk in September, where disease devastated his ranks — a setback that prevented any decisive action in the campaign of 1689. (081-163, 081-164)

## The Williamite War in Ireland (1689-1691)

The report that William was to command in person in 1690 was itself an indication of the importance he attached to the Irish campaign. (082-025) On the 14th of June, William landed at Carrickfergus, accompanied by Prince George of Denmark, the Duke of Wurtemburg, the Prince of Hesse-Darmstadt, and a host of distinguished British and foreign officers. (082-039) At Belfast he found his disposable forces numbered upwards of 40,000 men, drawn from a "strange medley of all nations" — Scandinavians, Swiss, Dutch, Prussians, Huguenot-French, English, Scotch, Scotch-Irish, and Anglo-Irish. (082-044, 082-045, 082-046)

> William, with his chief officers, breakfasting on the turf, nearly lost his life from a sudden discharge of cannon; but he was quickly in the saddle, at all points reviewing his army. (082-072, 082-073)

On the last day of June 1690, the two armies confronted each other at the [[Battle of the Boyne]]. William, strong in his veteran ranks, welcomed the battle while James, strong in his defensive position, awaited it with confidence. (082-068, 082-069, 082-070) William led his left wing across the river nearer to Drogheda, swimming his horse across in imminent danger, while the elder Schomberg and Callimotte fell mortally wounded in the centre. (082-081, 082-087, 082-088) When news was brought that the Reverend George Walker — recently appointed to the See of Derry — had also fallen, William's comment was characteristically blunt: "What brought him there?" (082-090, 082-091) After seven hours of fighting, the Jacobite army fell back on Duleek in good order, but the political consequences of the battle were momentous. Within a week, William was in [[dublin|Dublin]], and [[James II]] was en route for France. (082-092, 082-097, 082-099, 082-100)

The defeat at the Boyne uncovered the entire province of [[leinster|Leinster]]. Kilkenny, Wexford, Waterford, Duncannon, Clonmel, and other places surrendered within six weeks. The Jacobite forces fell back to the line of the Shannon, where the points of defence and attack were now shifted to [[Athlone]] and [[Siege of Limerick|Limerick]]. (082-109, 082-110, 082-112, 082-113) On the 9th of August, William, confident of an easy victory, appeared before Limerick — but more than twelve months were to elapse before all his power could reduce the city's mouldering walls. (082-139, 082-140) The defence, led by De Boisseleau and sustained by [[Patrick Sarsfield]], proved formidable. Sarsfield executed a daring raid on William's siege-train at Ballanedy, destroying the guns and ammunition in a spectacular explosion. (082-144, 082-155, 082-162, 082-165) William quickly brought up a new siege-train from Waterford and poured red-hot shot into the city, but a storming party on the 27th of August was repulsed with the loss of 30 officers and 800 men killed, and 1,200 wounded. (082-170, 082-172, 082-175, 082-177) Four days later, William abandoned the siege, retreated to Waterford, and embarked for England. (082-180, 082-181)

The campaign of 1691 was conducted by William's general, De Ginkle (later created Earl of Athlone). The capture of Athlone at the end of June and the crushing Jacobite defeat at Aughrim on the 11th of July shattered Catholic resistance in Connaught. (084-036, 084-066, 084-067, 084-092) These victories set the stage for the final act of the war: the second [[Siege of Limerick]], which ended with a truce on the 24th of September and the signing of the [[Treaty of Limerick]] on the 3rd of October, 1691. (084-121, 084-122)

## The Treaty of Limerick and Its Aftermath

From the date of the [[siege-of-limerick|Treaty of Limerick]], William was acknowledged by all but the extreme Jacobites as, at least de facto, King of Ireland. (085-002, 085-003) The civil articles of the treaty contained thirteen provisions, including an oath of allegiance prescribed in Article IX:

> I, A. B., do solemnly promise and swear that I will be faithful and bear true allegiance to their majesties, King William and Queen Mary; so help me God. (085-036, 085-037)

The articles were signed at the well-known "Treaty Stone" on the Clare side of the Shannon, by Lords Justices Porter and Coningsby for King William, and by Sarsfield, [[patrick-sarsfield|Earl of Lucan]], for the Irish. (085-053, 085-054, 085-055, 085-056) Royal letters patent confirming the treaty were issued from Westminster in February 1692, with William and Mary declaring that they ratified every clause and matter therein contained. (085-058, 085-060, 085-061)

Despite these solemn guarantees, the [[Protestant Ascendancy]] party in the [[irish-parliament|Irish Parliament]] was not to be restrained by the faith of treaties. (085-089) The Sunday following the return of the Lords Justices from Limerick, the Bishop of Meath preached before them on the crime of keeping faith with Papists. (085-090, 085-091) In 1692, the Parliament framed an oath in direct contravention of the ninth civil article, forcing Catholic peers and commoners to retire from both Houses — thus the [[irish-parliament|Irish Parliament]] assumed that exclusively Protestant character which it maintained until its extinction in 1800. (085-102, 085-104, 085-111, 085-114) A commission to inquire into forfeited estates decreed that 1,060,792 acres were escheated to the crown — almost the last fragment of the Catholic patrimony. (085-126, 085-127, 085-128)

> When King William died, there did not remain in Catholic hands "one-sixth part" of what their grandfathers held, even after the passage of the Act of Settlement. (085-129, 085-130, 085-131)

The [[Penal Laws]] enacted during William's reign were described by McGee as more shamefully violating the Articles of Limerick than the Articles of Mellifont had been violated under [[james-i|James I]]. (062-212) The revolutionary war had cost King William, or rather the people of England, at least 10,000,000 pounds sterling, and with the other wars of that reign laid the foundation of the English national debt. (084-169, 084-170, 084-171)

## Later Reign and Legacy

In 1695, Lord Capel opened the second [[irish-parliament|Irish Parliament]] summoned by King William with a speech in which he assured his auditors that the King was intent upon a firm settlement of Ireland upon a Protestant interest. (085-132, 085-133, 085-134) Large supplies were at once voted, and the Commons proceeded to add to the already existing penal legislation — including acts to deprive Catholics of the means of educating their children, to disarm them, and to banish all Catholic priests and prelates — thereby violating the treaty they purported to confirm. (085-135, 085-152, 085-155, 085-156)

The only other political incidents of William's reign important to Ireland were his promise to discourage the woollen and encourage the linen manufacture, and the publication of William Molyneux's "The Case of Ireland Stated" in 1698. (085-189, 085-190, 085-191) Molyneux's book appeared with a short, respectful, but manly dedication to King William. (085-196) The English Parliament ordered the book burned by the common hangman as of "dangerous tendency," and William assured them that he would enforce the laws securing the dependence of Ireland on the imperial crown of Great Britain. (085-228, 085-234, 085-235, 085-236) The prohibitory enactments of William's reign, including the 10th and 11th of William III which barred the export of Irish woollens, remained a grievance taken up by later reformers such as [[Henry Grattan]]. (095-017)

William's reign also saw the departure of the "Wild Geese" — the tens of thousands of Irish soldiers who went into exile after the surrender of Limerick. Sarsfield's brigade fought against King William in Flanders in 1692 and 1693; at Namur and Enghien they were greatly distinguished, and William more than once sustained heavy loss at their hands. (087-034, 087-035, 087-036) Meanwhile, the [[presbyterian|Presbyterians]] of [[ulster|Ulster]], who had shared in the revolution, endeavoured but ineffectually to obtain under King William the repeal of the Test Act. (089-034)

William died on the 8th of March, 1702, at little more than fifty years of age, from the effects of a fall from his horse. (085-237, 085-238) McGee's assessment of the King is measured and nuanced:

> His reign over Ireland is synonymous to the minds of that people of disaster, proscription and spoliation; of violated faith and broken compacts; but these wrongs were done in his name rather than by his orders; often without his knowledge, and sometimes against his will. Rigid as that will was, it was forced to bend to the anti-Popery storm which swept over the British Islands after the abdication of King James; but the vices and follies of his times ought no more be laid to the personal account of William than of James or Louis, against whom he fought. (085-239, 085-240, 085-241, 085-242, 085-243, 085-244, 085-245, 085-246, 085-247)
