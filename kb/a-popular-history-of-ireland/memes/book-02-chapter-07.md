CHAPTER VII.

[015-001]EFFECTS OF THE RIVALRY OF BRIAN AND MALACHY ON THE ANCIENT

[015-002]CONSTITUTION.

[015-003]If a great battle is to be accounted lost or won, as it affects

[015-004]principles rather than reputations, then Brian lost at Clontarf. The

[015-005]leading ideas of his long and political life were, evidently,

[015-006]centralization and an hereditary monarchy. To beat back foreign

[015-007]invasion, to conciliate and to enlist the Irish-born Danes under his

[015-008]standard, were preliminary steps. For Morrogh, his first-born, and for

[015-009]Morrogh's descendants, he hoped to found an hereditary kinship after

[015-010]the type universally copied throughout Christendom. He was not ignorant

[015-011]of what Alfred had done for England, Harold for Norway, Charlemagne for

[015-012]France, and Otho for Germany; and it was inseparable from his imperial

[015-013]genius to desire to reign in his posterity, long after his own brief

[015-014]term of sway should be for ever ended. A new centre of royal authority

[015-015]should be established on the banks of the great middle river of the

[015-016]island—itself the best bond of union, as it was the best highway of

[015-017]intercourse; the Dalgais dynasty should there flourish for ages, and

[015-018]the descendants of Brian of the Tributes, through after centuries,

[015-019]eclipse the glory of the descendants of Nial of the Hostages. It is

[015-020]idle enough to call the projector of such a change an usurper and a

[015-021]revolutionist. Usurper he clearly was not, since he was elevated to

[015-022]power by the action of the old legitimate electoral principle;

[015-023]revolutionist he was not, because his design was defeated at Clontarf,

[015-024]in the death of his eldest son and grandson. Not often have three

[015-025]generations of Princes of the same family been cut off on the same

[015-026]field; yet at Clontarf it so happened. Hence, when Brian fell, and his

[015-027]heir with him, and his heir's heir, the projected Dalgais dynasty, like

[015-028]the Royal Oak at Adair, was cut down and its very roots destroyed. For

[015-029]a new dynasty to be left suddenly without indisputable heirs is ruinous

[015-030]to its pretensions and partizans. And in this the event of the battle

[015-031]proved destructive to the Celtic Constitution. Not from the

[015-032]Anglo-Norman invasion, but from the day of Clontarf we may date the

[015-033]ruin of the old electoral monarchy. The spell of ancient authority was

[015-034]effectually broken and a new one was to be established. Time, which was

[015-035]indispensable, was not given. No Prince of the blood of Brian succeeded

[015-036]immediately to himself. On Clontarf Morrogh, and Morrogh's heir fell,

[015-037]in the same day and hour. The other sons of Brian had no direct title

[015-038]to the succession, and, naturally enough, the deposed Malachy resumed

[015-039]the rank of monarch, without the consent of Munster, but _with_ the

[015-040]approval of all the Princes, who had witnessed with ill-concealed envy

[015-041]the sudden ascendancy of the sons of Kennedy. While McLaig was

[015-042]lamenting for Brian, by the cascade of Killaloe, the Laureat of Tara,

[015-043]in an elegy over a lord of Breffni, was singing—

[015-044]"Joyful are the race of Conn after Brian's

[015-045]Fall, in the battle of Clontarf."

[015-046]A new dynasty is rarely the work of one able man. Designed by genius,

[015-047]it must be built up by a succession of politic Princes, before it

[015-048]becomes an essential part of the framework of the State. So all history

[015-049]teaches—and Irish history, after the death of Brian, very clearly

[015-050]illustrates that truth. Equally true is it that when a nation breaks up

[015-051]of itself, or from external forces, and is not soon consolidated by a

[015-052]conqueror, the most natural result is the aggrandizement of a few great

[015-053]families. Thus it was in Rome when Julius was assassinated, and in

[015-054]Italy, when the empire of the west fell to pieces of its own weight.

[015-055]The kindred of the late sovereign will be sure to have a party, the

[015-056]chief innovators will have a party, and there is likely to grow up a

[015-057]third or moderate party. So it fell out in Ireland. The Hy-Nials of the

[015-058]north, deprived of the succession, rallied about the Princes of Aileach

[015-059]as their head. Meath, left crownless, gave room to the ambition of the

[015-060]sons of Malachy, who, under the name of O'Melaghlin, took provincial

[015-061]rank. Ossory, like Issachar, long groaning beneath the burdens of Tara

[015-062]and of Cashel, cruelly revenged on the Dalgais, returning from

[015-063]Clontarf, the subjection to which Mahon and Brian had forcibly reduced

[015-064]that borderland. The Eugenians of Desmond withdrew in disgust from the

[015-065]banner of Donogh O'Brien, because he had openly proclaimed his

[015-066]hostility to the alternate succession, and left his surviving clansmen

[015-067]an easy prey to the enraged Ossorians. Leinster soon afterwards passed

[015-068]from the house of O'Byrne to that of McMurrogh. The O'Briens maintained

[015-069]their dominant interest in the south; as, after many local struggles,

[015-070]the O'Conors did in the west. For a hundred and fifty years, after the

[015-071]death of Malachy II., the history of Ireland is mainly the history of

[015-072]these five families, O'Neils, O'Melaghlins, McMurroghs, O'Briens and

[015-073]O'Conors. And for ages after the Normans enter on the scene, the same

[015-074]provincialized spirit, the same family ambitions, feuds, hates, and

[015-075]coalitions, with some exceptional passages, characterize the whole

[015-076]history. Not that there will be found any want of heroism, or piety, or

[015-077]self-sacrifice, or of any virtue or faculty, necessary to constitute a

[015-078]state, save and except the _power of combination_, alone. Thus, judged

[015-079]by what came after him, and what was happening in the world abroad,

[015-080]Brian's design to re-centralize the island, seems the highest dictate

[015-081]of political wisdom, in the condition to which the Norwegian and Danish

[015-082]wars had reduced it, previous to his elevation to the monarchy. Malachy

[015-083]II.—of the events of whose second reign some mention will be made

[015-084]hereafter—held the sovereignty after Brian's death, until the year

[015-085]1023, when he died an edifying death in one of the islands of Lough

[015-086]Ennel, near the present Mullingar. He is called, in the annals of

[015-087]Clonmacnoise, "the last king of Ireland, of Irish blood, that had the

[015-088]crown." An ancient quatrain, quoted by Geoffrey Keating, is thus

[015-089]literally translated:

[015-090]"After the happy Melaghlin

[015-091]Son of Donald, son of Donogh,

[015-092]Each noble king ruled his own tribe

[015-093]But Erin owned no sovereign Lord."

[015-094]The annals of the eleventh and twelfth centuries curiously illustrate

[015-095]the workings of this "anarchical constitution"—to employ a phrase first

[015-096]applied to the Germanic Confederation. "After Malachy's death," says

[015-097]the quaint old Annalist of Clonmacnoise, "this kingdom was without a

[015-098]king 20 years, during which time the realm was governed by two learned

[015-099]men; the one called Con O'Lochan, a well learned temporal man, and

[015-100]chief poet of Ireland; the other Corcran Claireach, a devout and holy

[015-101]man that was anchorite of all Ireland, whose most abiding was at

[015-102]Lismore. The land was governed like a free state, and not like a

[015-103]monarchy by them." Nothing can show the headlessness of the Irish

[015-104]Constitution in the eleventh century clearer than this interregnum. No

[015-105]one Prince could rally strength enough to be elected, so that two

[015-106]Arbitrators, an illustrious Poet and a holy Priest, were appointed to

[015-107]take cognizance of national causes. The associating together of a

[015-108]Priest and a layman, a southerner and a northerner, is conclusive proof

[015-109]that the bond of Celtic unity, frittered away during the Danish period,

[015-110]was never afterwards entirely restored. Con O'Lochan having been killed

[015-111]in Teffia, after a short jurisdiction, the holy Corcran exercised his

[015-112]singular jurisdiction, until his decease, which happened at Lismore,

[015-113](A.D. 1040.) His death produced a new paroxysm of anarchy, out of which

[015-114]a new organizer arose among the tribes of Leinster. This was Dermid,

[015-115]son of Donogh, who died (A.D. 1005), when Dermid must have been a mere

[015-116]infant, as he does not figure in the annals till the year 1032, and the

[015-117]acts of young Princes are seldom overlooked in Gaelic Chronicles. He

[015-118]was the first McMurrogh who became King of Leinster, that royalty

[015-119]having been in the O'Byrne family, until the son of Maelmurra, of

[015-120]Clontarf, was deposed by O'Neil in 1035, and retired to a monastery in

[015-121]Cologne, where he died in 1052. In 1036 or 1037 Dermid captured Dublin

[015-122]and Waterford, married the grand-daughter of Brian, and by '41 was

[015-123]strong enough to assume the rank of ruler of the southern half-kingdom.

[015-124]This dignity he held with a strong and warlike hand thirty years, when

[015-125]he fell in battle, at Ova, in Meath. He must have been at that time

[015-126]full threescore years and ten. He is described by the elegiac Bards as

[015-127]of "ruddy complexion," "with teeth laughing in danger," and possessing

[015-128]all the virtues of a warrior-king; "whose death," adds the lamentation,

[015-129]"brought scarcity of peace" with it, so that "there will not be peace,"

[015-130]"there will not be armistice," between Meath and Leinster. It may well

[015-131]be imagined that every new resort to the two-third test, in the

[015-132]election of Ard-Righ, should bring "scarcity of peace" to Ireland. We

[015-133]can easily understand the ferment of hope, fear, intrigue, and passion,

[015-134]which such an occasion caused among the great rival families. What

[015-135]canvassing there was in Kinkora and Cashel, at Cruachan and Aileach,

[015-136]and at Fernamore! What piecing and patching of interests, what libels

[015-137]on opposing candidates, what exultation in the successful, what

[015-138]discontent in the defeated camp!

[015-139]The successful candidate for the southern half-kingdom after Dermid's

[015-140]death was Thorlogh, grandson of Brian, and foster-son of the late

[015-141]ruler. In his reign, which lasted thirty-three years, the political

[015-142]fortunes of his house revived. He died in peace at Kinkora (A.D. 1087),

[015-143]and the war of succession again broke out. The rival candidates at this

[015-144]period were Murrogh O'Brien, son of the late king, whose ambition was

[015-145]to complete the design of Brian, and Donald, Prince of Aileach, the

[015-146]leader of the Northern Hy-Nials. Two abler men seldom divided a country

[015-147]by their equal ambition. Both are entered in the annals as "Kings of

[015-148]Ireland," but it is hard to discover that, during all the years of

[015-149]their contest, either of them submitted to the other. To chronicle all

[015-150]the incidents of the struggle would take too much space here; and, as

[015-151]was to be expected, a third party profited most by it; the West came

[015-152]in, in the person of O'Conor, to lord it over both North and South, and

[015-153]to add another element to the dynastic confusion.

[015-154]This brief abstract of our civil affairs after the death of Brian,

[015-155]presents us with the extraordinary spectacle of a country without a

[015-156]constitution working out the problem of its stormy destiny in despite

[015-157]of all internal and external dangers. Everything now depended on

[015-158]individual genius and energy; nothing on system, usage, or

[015-159]prescription. Each leading family and each province became, in turn,

[015-160]the head of the State. The supreme title seems to have been fatal for a

[015-161]generation to the family that obtained it, for in no case is there a

[015-162]lineal descent of the crown. The prince of Aileach or Kinkora naturally

[015-163]preferred his permanent patrimony to an uncertain tenure of Tara; an

[015-164]office not attached to a locality became, of course, little more than

[015-165]an arbitrary title. Hence, the titular King of Ireland might for one

[015-166]lifetime reign by the Shannon, in the next by the Bann, in a third, by

[015-167]Lough Corrib. The supremacy, thus came to be considered a merely

[015-168]personal appurtenance, was carried about in the old King's tent, or on

[015-169]the young King's crupper, deteriorating and decaying by every

[015-170]transposition it underwent. Herein, we have the origin of Irish

[015-171]disunion with all its consequences, good, bad, and indifferent.

[015-172]Are we to blame Brian for this train of events against which he would

[015-173]have provided a sharp remedy in the hereditary principle? Or, on the

[015-174]other hand, are we to condemn Malachy, the possessor of legitimate

[015-175]power, if he saw in that remedy only the ambition of an aspiring family

[015-176]already grown too great? Theirs was in fact the universal struggle of

[015-177]reform and conservatism; the reformer and the heirs of his work were

[015-178]cut off on Clontarf; the abuses of the elective principle continued

[015-179]unrestrained by ancient salutary usage and prejudice, and the land

[015-180]remained a tempting prey to such Adventurers, foreign or native, as

[015-181]dare undertake to mould power out of its chaotic materials.
