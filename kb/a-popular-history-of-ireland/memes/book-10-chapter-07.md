CHAPTER VII.

[082-001]THE REVOLUTIONARY WAR—CAMPAIGN OF 1690—BATTLE OF THE BOYNE—ITS

[082-002]CONSEQUENCES—THE SIEGES OF ATHLONE AND LIMERICK.

[082-003]The armies now destined to combat for two kings on Irish soil were

[082-004]strongly marked by those distinctions of race and religion which add

[082-005]bitterness to struggles for power, while they present striking

[082-006]contrasts to the eye of the painter of military life and manners. King

[082-007]James's troops were chiefly Celtic and Catholic. There were four

[082-008]regiments commanded by O'Neils, two by O'Briens, two by O'Kellys, one

[082-009]each by McCarthy More, Maguire, O'More, O'Donnell, McMahon, and

[082-010]Magennis, principally recruited among their own clansmen. There were

[082-011]also the regiments of Sarsfield, Nugent, De Courcy, Fitzgerald, Grace,

[082-012]and Burke, chiefly Celts, in the rank and file. On the other hand,

[082-013]Schomberg led into the field the famous blue Dutch and white Dutch

[082-014]regiments; the Huguenot regiments of Schomberg, La Millinier, Du

[082-015]Cambon, and La Callimotte; the English regiments of Lords Devonshire,

[082-016]Delamere, Lovelace, Sir John Lanier, Colonels Langston, Villiers, and

[082-017]others; the Anglo-Irish regiments of Lords Meath, Roscommon, Kingston,

[082-018]and Drogheda; with the Ulstermen, under Brigadier Wolseley, Colonels

[082-019]Gustavus Hamilton, Mitchelburne, Loyd, White, St. Johns, and Tiffany.

[082-020]Some important changes had taken place on both sides during the winter

[082-021]months. D'Avaux and De Rosen had been recalled at James's request;

[082-022]Mountcashel, at the head of the first Franco-Irish brigade, had been

[082-023]exchanged for 6,000 French, under De Lauzan, who arrived the following

[082-024]March in the double character of general and ambassador. The report

[082-025]that William was to command in person in the next campaign, was, of

[082-026]itself, an indication pregnant with other changes to the minds of his

[082-027]adherents.

[082-028]Their abundant supplies of military stores from England, wafted from

[082-029]every port upon the channel, where James had not a keel afloat, enabled

[082-030]the Williamite army to take the initiative in the campaign of 1690. At

[082-031]Cavan, Brigadier Wolseley repulsed the Duke of Berwick, with the loss

[082-032]of 200 men and some valuable officers. But the chief incident preceding

[082-033]William's arrival was the siege of Charlemont. This siege, which

[082-034]commenced apparently in the previous autumn, had continued during

[082-035]several months, till the garrison were literally starved out, in May.

[082-036]The famished survivors were kindly treated, by order of Schomberg, and

[082-037]their gallant and eccentric chief, O'Regan, was knighted by the King,

[082-038]for his persistent resistance. A month from the day on which Charlemont

[082-039]fell, (June 14th), William landed at Carrickfergus, accompanied by

[082-040]Prince George of Denmark, the Duke of Wurtemburg, the Prince of

[082-041]Hesse-Darmstadt, the second and last Duke of Ormond, Major-General

[082-042]Mackay, the Earls of Oxford, Portland, Scarborough, and Manchester,

[082-043]General Douglas, and other distinguished British and foreign officers.

[082-044]At Belfast, his first head-quarters, he ascertained the forces at his

[082-045]disposal to be upwards of 40,000 men, composed of "a strange medley of

[082-046]all nations"—Scandinavians, Swiss, Dutch, Prussians, Huguenot-French,

[082-047]English, Scotch, "Scotch-Irish," and Anglo-Irish. Perhaps the most

[082-048]extraordinary element in that strange medley was the Danish contingent

[082-049]of horse and foot. Irish tradition and Irish prophecy still teemed with

[082-050]tales of terror and predictions of evil at the hands of the Danes,

[082-051]while these hardy mercenaries observed, with grim satisfaction, that

[082-052]the memory of their fierce ancestors had not become extinct after the

[082-053]lapse of twenty generations. At the Boyne, and at Limerick, they could

[082-054]not conceal their exultation as they encamped on some of the very

[082-055]earthworks raised by men of their race seven centuries before, and it

[082-056]must be admitted they vindicated their descent, both by their courage

[082-057]and their cruelty.

[082-058]On the 16th of June, James, informed of William's arrival, marched

[082-059]northward at the head of 20,000 men, French and Irish, to meet him. On

[082-060]the 22nd, James was at Dundalk and William at Newry; as the latter

[082-061]advanced, the Jacobites retired, and finally chose their ground at the

[082-062]Boyne, resolved to hazard a battle, for the preservation of Dublin, and

[082-063]the safety of the province of Leinster.

[082-064]On the last day of June, the hostile forces confronted each other at

[082-065]the Boyne. The gentle, legendary river, wreathed in all the glory of

[082-066]its abundant foliage, was startled with the cannonade from the northern

[082-067]bank, which continued through the long summer's evening, and woke the

[082-068]early echoes of the morrow. William, strong in his veteran ranks,

[082-069]welcomed the battle; James, strong in his defensive position, and the

[082-070]goodness of his cause, awaited it with confidence. On the northern bank

[082-071]near to the ford of Oldbridge, William, with his chief officers,

[082-072]breakfasting on the turf, nearly lost his life from a sudden discharge

[082-073]of cannon; but he was quickly in the saddle, at all points reviewing

[082-074]his army. James, on the hill of Donore, looked down on his devoted

[082-075]defenders, through whose ranks rode Tyrconnell, lame and ill, the

[082-076]youthful Berwick, the adventurous Lauzan, and the beloved

[082-077]Sarsfield—everywhere received with cordial acclamations. The battle

[082-078]commenced at the ford of Oldbridge, between Sir Neil O'Neil, and the

[082-079]younger Schomberg; O'Neil fell mortally wounded, and the ford was

[082-080]forced. By this ford, William ordered his centre to advance under the

[082-081]elder Schomberg, as the hour of noon approached, while he himself moved

[082-082]with the left across the river, nearer to Drogheda. Lauzan, with

[082-083]Sarsfield's horse, dreading to be outflanked, had galloped to guard the

[082-084]bridge of Slane, five miles higher up the stream, where alone a flank

[082-085]movement was possible. The battle was now transferred from the gunners

[082-086]to the swordsmen and pikemen—from the banks to the fords and borders of

[082-087]the river, William, on the extreme left, swam his horse across, in

[082-088]imminent danger; Schomberg and Callimotte fell in the centre, mortally

[082-089]wounded. News was brought to William, that Dr. Walker—recently

[082-090]appointed to the See of Derry—had also fallen, "What brought him

[082-091]there?" was the natural comment of the soldier-prince. After seven

[082-092]hours' fighting the Irish fell back on Duleek, in good order. The

[082-093]assailants admitted five hundred killed, and as many wounded; the

[082-094]defenders were said to have lost from one thousand to fifteen hundred

[082-095]men—less than at Newtown-Butler. The carnage, compared with some great

[082-096]battles of that age, was inconsiderable, but the political consequences

[082-097]were momentous. The next day, the garrison of Drogheda, one thousand

[082-098]three hundred strong, surrendered; in another week, William was in

[082-099]Dublin, and James, terrified by the reports which had reached him, was

[082-100]_en route_ for France. It is hardly an exaggeration to say, that the

[082-101]fate of Europe was decided by the result of the battle of the Boyne. At

[082-102]Paris, at the Hague, at Vienna, at Rome, at Madrid, nothing was talked

[082-103]of but the great victory of the Prince of Orange over Louis and James.

[082-104]It is one of the strangest complications of history that the vanquished

[082-105]Irish Catholics seem to have been never once thought of by Spain,

[082-106]Austria, or the Pope. In the greater issues of the European coalition

[082-107]against France, their interests, and their very existence, were for the

[082-108]moment forgotten.

[082-109]The defeat at the Boyne, and the surrender of Dublin, uncovered the

[082-110]entire province of Leinster, Kilkenny, Wexford, Waterford, Duncannon,

[082-111]Clonmel, and other places of less importance, surrendered within six

[082-112]weeks. The line of the Shannon was fallen back upon by the Irish, and

[082-113]the points of attack and defence were now shifted to Athlone and

[082-114]Limerick. What Enniskillen and Derry had been, in the previous year, to

[082-115]the Williamite party in the north, cities of refuge, and strongholds of

[082-116]hope, these two towns upon the Shannon had now become, by the fortune

[082-117]of war, to King James's adherents.

[082-118]On the 17th of July, General Douglas appeared before Athlone, and

[082-119]summoned it to surrender. The veteran commandant, Colonel Richard

[082-120]Grace, a Confederate of 1641, having destroyed the bridge, and the

[082-121]suburbs on the Leinster side of the Shannon, replied by discharging his

[082-122]pistol over the head of the drummer who delivered the message. Douglas

[082-123]attempted to cross the river at Lanesborough, but found the ford

[082-124]strongly guarded by one of Grace's outposts; after a week's ineffectual

[082-125]bombardment, he withdrew from before Athlone, and proceeded to

[082-126]Limerick, ravaging and slaying as he went.

[082-127]Limerick had at first been abandoned by the French under Lauzan, as

[082-128]utterly indefensible. That gay intriguer desired nothing so much as to

[082-129]follow the King to France, while Tyrconnell, broken down with physical

[082-130]suffering and mental anxiety, feebly concurred in his opinion. They

[082-131]accordingly departed for Galway, leaving the city to its fate, and,

[082-132]happily for the national reputation, to bolder counsels than their own.

[082-133]De Boisseleau did not underrate the character of the Irish levies, who

[082-134]had retreated before twice their numbers at the Boyne; he declared

[082-135]himself willing to remain, and, sustained by Sarsfield, he was chosen

[082-136]as commandant. More than ten thousand foot had gathered "as if by

[082-137]instinct" to that city, and on the Clare side Sarsfield still kept

[082-138]together his cavalry, at whose head he rode to Galway and brought back

[082-139]Tyrconnell. On the 9th of August, William, confident of an easy

[082-140]victory, appeared before the town, but more than twelve months were to

[082-141]elapse before all his power could reduce those mouldering walls, which

[082-142]the fugitive French ambassador had declared "might be taken with

[082-143]roasted apples."

[082-144]An exploit, planned and executed by Sarsfield the day succeeding

[082-145]William's arrival, saved the city for another year, and raised that

[082-146]officer to the highest pitch of popularity. Along the Clare side of the

[082-147]Shannon, under cover of the night, he galloped as fast as horse could

[082-148]carry him, at the head of his dragoons, and crossed the river at

[082-149]Killaloe. One Manus O'Brien, a Protestant of Clare, who had encountered

[082-150]the flying horsemen, and learned enough to suspect their design,

[082-151]hastened to William's camp with the news, but he was at first laughed

[082-152]at for his pains. William, however, never despising any precaution in

[082-153]war, despatched Sir John Lanier with 500 horse to protect his

[082-154]siege-train, then seven miles in the rear, on the road between Limerick

[082-155]and Cashel. Sarsfield, however, was too quick for Sir John. The day

[082-156]after he had crossed at Killaloe he kept his men _perdu_ in the hilly

[082-157]country, and the next night swooped down upon the convoy in charge of

[082-158]the siege-train, who were quietly sleeping round the ruined church of

[082-159]Ballanedy. The sentinels were sabred at their posts, the guards,

[082-160]half-dressed, fled in terror or were speedily killed. The gun-carriages

[082-161]were quickly yoked, and drawn together to a convenient place, where,

[082-162]planted in pits with ammunition, they were, with two exceptions,

[082-163]successfully blown to atoms. Lanier arrived within view of the terrific

[082-164]scene in time to feel its stunning effects. The ground for miles round

[082-165]shook as from an earthquake; the glare and roar of the explosion were

[082-166]felt in William's camp, and through the beleaguered city. On the

[082-167]morrow, all was known. Sarsfield was safely back in his old encampment,

[082-168]without the loss of a single man; Limerick was in an uproar of delight,

[082-169]while William's army, to the lowest rank, felt the depression of so

[082-170]unexpected a blow. A week later, however, the provident prince had a

[082-171]new siege-train of thirty-six guns and four mortars brought up from

[082-172]Waterford, pouring red-hot shot on the devoted city. Another week—on

[082-173]the 27th of August—a gap having been made in the walls near Saint

[082-174]John's gate, a storming party of the English guards, the Anglo-Irish,

[082-175]Prussians, and Danes, was launched into the breach. After an action of

[082-176]uncommon fierceness and determination on both sides, the besiegers

[082-177]retired with the loss of 30 officers, and 800 men killed, and 1,200

[082-178]wounded. The besieged admitted 400 killed—their wounded were not

[082-179]counted. Four days later, William abandoned the siege, retreated to

[082-180]Waterford, and embarked for England, with Prince George of Denmark, the

[082-181]Dukes of Wurtemburg and Ormond, and others of his principal adherents.

[082-182]Tyrconnell, labouring with the illness of which he soon after died,

[082-183]took advantage of the honourable pause thus obtained, to proceed on his

[082-184]interrupted voyage to France, accompanied by the ambassador. Before

[082-185]leaving, however, the young Duke of Berwick was named in his stead as

[082-186]Commander-in-Chief; Fitton, Nagle, and Plowden, as Lords Justices;

[082-187]sixteen "senators" were to form a sort of Cabinet, and Sarsfield to be

[082-188]second in military command. His enemies declared that Tyrconnell

[082-189]retired from the contest because his early spirit and courage had

[082-190]failed him; he himself asserted that his object was to procure

[082-191]sufficient succours from King Louis, to give a decisive issue to the

[082-192]war. His subsequent negotiations at Paris proved that though his bodily

[082-193]health might be wretched, his ingenuity and readiness of resource had

[082-194]not deserted him. He justified himself both with James and Louis,

[082-195]outwitted Lauzan, propitiated Louvois, disarmed the prejudices of the

[082-196]English Jacobites, and, in short, placed the military relations of

[082-197]France and Ireland on a footing they had never hitherto sustained. The

[082-198]expedition of the following spring, under command of Marshal Saint

[082-199]Ruth, was mainly procured by his able diplomacy, and though he returned

[082-200]to Ireland to survive but a few weeks the disastrous day of Aughrim, it

[082-201]is impossible from the Irish point of view, not to recall with

[082-202]admiration, mixed indeed with alloy, but still with largely prevailing

[082-203]admiration, the extraordinary energy, buoyancy and talents of Richard,

[082-204]Duke of Tyrconnell.
