CHAPTER III.

[090-001]THE LAST JACOBITE MOVEMENT—THE IRISH SOLDIERS ABROAD—FRENCH EXPEDITION

[090-002]UNDER THUROT, OR O'FARRELL.

[090-003]The mention of the Scottish insurrection of 1745 brings naturally with

[090-004]it another reference to the history of the Irish soldiers in the

[090-005]military service of France. This year was in truth the most eventful in

[090-006]the annals of that celebrated legion, for while it was the year of

[090-007]Fontenoy and victory on the one hand, it was on the other the year of

[090-008]Culloden and defeat.

[090-009]The decisive battle of Fontenoy, in which the Franco-Irish troops bore

[090-010]so decisive a part, was fought on the 11th of May, 1745. The French

[090-011]army, commanded by Saxe, and accompanied by King Louis, leaving 18,000

[090-012]men to besiege Namur, and 6,000 to guard the Scheldt, took a position

[090-013]between that river and the allies, having their centre at the village

[090-014]of Fontenoy. The British and Dutch, under the King's favourite son, the

[090-015]Duke of Cumberland, were 55,000 strong; the French 45,000. After a hard

[090-016]day's fighting, victory seemed to declare so clearly against France,

[090-017]that King Louis, who was present, prepared for flight. At this moment

[090-018]Marshal Saxe ordered a final charge by the seven Irish regiments under

[090-019]Counts Dillon and Thomond. The tide was turned, beyond expectation, to

[090-020]the cry of "Remember Limerick!" France was delivered, England checked,

[090-021]and Holland reduced from a first to a second-rate power upon that

[090-022]memorable day. But the victory was dearly bought. One-fourth of all the

[090-023]Irish officers, including Count Dillon, were killed, and one-third of

[090-024]all the men. The whole number slain on the side of France was set down

[090-025]at 7,000 by English accounts, while they admitted for themselves alone,

[090-026]4,000 British and 3,300 Hanoverians and Dutch. "Foremost of all," says

[090-027]the just-minded Lord Mahon, "were the gallant brigade of Irish exiles."

[090-028]It was this defeat of his favourite son which wrung from King George

[090-029]II. the oft-quoted malediction on the laws which deprived him of such

[090-030]subjects.

[090-031]The expedition of Prince Charles Edward was undertaken and conducted by

[090-032]Irish aid, quite as much as by French or Scottish. The chief parties to

[090-033]it, besides the old Marquis of Tullibardine and the young Duke of

[090-034]Perth, were the Waterses, father and son, Irish bankers at Paris, who

[090-035]advanced one hundred and eighty thousand livres between them; Walsh, an

[090-036]Irish merchant at Nantz, who put a privateer of eighteen guns into the

[090-037]venture; Sir Thomas Geraldine, the Pretender's agent at Paris; Sir

[090-038]Thomas Sheridan, the prince's preceptor, who, with Colonels O'Sullivan

[090-039]and Lynch, Captain O'Neil, and other officers of the brigade, formed

[090-040]the staff, on which Sir John McDonald, a Scottish officer in the

[090-041]Spanish service, was also placed. Fathers Kelly and O'Brien volunteered

[090-042]in the expedition. On the 22nd of June, 1745, with seven friends, the

[090-043]prince embarked in Walsh's vessel, the Doutelle, at St. Nazaire, on the

[090-044]Loire, and on the 19th of July, landed on the northern coast of

[090-045]Scotland, near Moidart. The Scottish chiefs, little consulted or

[090-046]considered beforehand, came slowly and dubiously to the landing-place.

[090-047]Under their patriarchal control there were still in the kingdom about a

[090-048]hundred thousand men, and about one-twelfth of the Scottish population.

[090-049]Clanronald, Cameron of Lochiel, the Laird of McLeod, and a few others,

[090-050]having arrived, the royal standard was unfurled on the 19th of August

[090-051]at Glenfinin, where that evening twelve hundred men—the entire army so

[090-052]far—were formed into camp, under the orders of O'Sullivan. From that

[090-053]day until the day of Culloden, O'Sullivan seems to have manoeuvred the

[090-054]prince's forces. At Perth, at Edinburgh, at Preston, at Manchester, at

[090-055]Culloden, he took command in the field, or in garrison; and even after

[090-056]the sad result, he adhered to his sovereign's son with an honourable

[090-057]fidelity which defied despair.

[090-058]Charles, on his part, placed full confidence in his Irish officers. In

[090-059]his proclamation after the battle of Preston, he declared it was not

[090-060]his intention to enforce on the people of England, Scotland, or

[090-061]Ireland, "a religion they disliked." In a subsequent paper, he asks,

[090-062]"Have you found reason to love and cherish your governors as the

[090-063]fathers of the people of Great Britain and Ireland? Has a family upon

[090-064]whom a faction unlawfully bestowed the diadem of a rightful prince,

[090-065]retained a due sense of so great a trust and favour?" These and his

[090-066]other proclamations betrayed an Irish pen; probably Sir Thomas

[090-067]Sheridan's. One of Charles's English adherents, Lord Elcho, who kept a

[090-068]journal of the campaign, notes, complainingly, the Irish influence

[090-069]under which he acted. "The prince and his old governor, Sir Thomas

[090-070]Sheridan," are especially objected to, and the "Irish favourites" are

[090-071]censured in a body. While at Edinburgh, a French ship, containing some

[090-072]arms, supplies, and "Irish officers," arrived; at the same time efforts

[090-073]were made to recruit for the prince in Ireland; but the agents being

[090-074]taken in some cases, the channel narrowly watched, and the people not

[090-075]very eager to join the service, few recruits were obtained.

[090-076]The Irish in France, as if to cover the inaction of their countrymen at

[090-077]home, strained every nerve. The Waterses and O'Brien of Paris were

[090-078]liberal bankers to the expedition. Into their hands James "exhausted

[090-079]his treasury" to support his gallant son. At Fontainebleau, on the 23rd

[090-080]of October, Colonel O'Brien, on the part of the prince, and the Marquis

[090-081]D'Argeusson for Louis XV., formed a treaty of "friendship and

[090-082]alliance," one of the clauses of which was, that certain Irish

[090-083]regiments, and other French troops, should be sent to sustain the

[090-084]expedition. Under Lord John Drummond a thousand men were shipped from

[090-085]Dunkirk, and arrived at Montrose in the Highlands about the time

[090-086]Charles had penetrated as far south as Manchester. The officers, with

[090-087]the prince, here refused to advance on London with so small a force; a

[090-088]retreat was decided on; the sturdy defence of Carlisle, and victory of

[090-089]Falkirk, checked the pursuit; but the overwhelming force of the Duke of

[090-090]Cumberland compelled them to evacuate Edinburgh, Perth, and

[090-091]Glasgow—operations which consumed February, March, and the first half

[090-092]of April, 1746.

[090-093]The next plan of operations seems to have been to concentrate in the

[090-094]western Highlands, with Inverness for head-quarters. The town Charles

[090-095]easily got, but Fort-George, a powerful fortress, built upon the site

[090-096]of the castle where Macbeth was said to have murdered Duncan, commanded

[090-097]the Loch. Stapleton and his Irish, captured it, however, as well as the

[090-098]neighbouring Fort-Augustus. Joined by some Highlanders, they next

[090-099]attempted Fort-William, the last fortress of King George in the north,

[090-100]but on the 3rd of April were recalled to the main body.

[090-101]To cover Inverness, his head-quarters, Charles resolved to give battle.

[090-102]The ground chosen, flanked by the river Nairn, was spotted with marsh

[090-103]and very irregular; it was called Culloden, and was selected by

[090-104]O'Sullivan. Brigadier Stapleton and Colonel Kerr reported against it as

[090-105]a field of battle; but Charles adopted O'Sullivan's opinion of its

[090-106]fitness for Highland warfare. When the preparations for battle began,

[090-107]"many voices exclaimed, 'We'll give Cumberland another Fontenoy!'" The

[090-108]Jacobites were placed in position by O'Sullivan, "at once their

[090-109]adjutant and quarter-master-general," and, as the burghers of Preston

[090-110]thought, "a very likely fellow." He formed two lines, the great clans

[090-111]being in the first, the Ogilvies, Gordons, and Murrays; the French and

[090-112]Irish in the second. Four pieces of cannon flanked each wing, and four

[090-113]occupied the centre. Lord George Murray commanded the right wing, Lord

[090-114]John Drummond the left, and Brigadier Stapleton the reserve. They

[090-115]mustered in all less than five thousand men. The British formed in

[090-116]three lines, ten thousand strong, with two guns between every second

[090-117]regiment of the first and second line. The action commenced about noon

[090-118]of April 16th, and before evening half the troops of Prince Charles lay

[090-119]dead on the field, and the rest were hopelessly broken. The retreat was

[090-120]pell-mell, except where "a troop of the Irish pickets, by a spirited

[090-121]fire, checked the pursuit, which a body of dragoons commenced after the

[090-122]Macdonalds, and Lord Lewis Gordon's regiments did similar service."

[090-123]Stapleton conducted the French and Irish remnant to Inverness, and

[090-124]obtained for them by capitulation "fair quarter and honourable

[090-125]treatment."

[090-126]The unhappy prince remained on the field almost to the last. "It

[090-127]required," says Mr. Chambers, "all the eloquence, and, indeed, all the

[090-128]active exertion, of O'Sullivan to make Charles quit the field. A cornet

[090-129]in his service, when questioned on this subject at the point of death,

[090-130]declared he saw O'Sullivan, after using entreaties in vain, turn the

[090-131]head of the prince's horse and drag him away."

[090-132]From that night forth, O'Sullivan, O'Neil, and a poor sedan carrier of

[090-133]Edinburgh, called Burke, accompanied him in all his wanderings and

[090-134]adventures among the Scottish islands. At Long Island they were obliged

[090-135]to part company, the prince proceeding alone with Miss Flora McDonald.

[090-136]He had not long left, when a French cutter hove in sight and took off

[090-137]O'Sullivan, intending to touch at another point, and take in the prince

[090-138]and O'Neil. The same night she was blown off the coast, and the prince,

[090-139]after many other adventures, was finally taken off at Badenoch, on the

[090-140]15th of September, 1746, by the L'Heureux, a French armed vessel, in

[090-141]which Captain Sheridan (son of Sir Thomas), Mr. O'Beirne, a lieutenant

[090-142]in the French army, "and two other gentlemen," had adventured in search

[090-143]of him. Poor O'Neil, in seeking to rejoin his master, was taken

[090-144]prisoner, carried to London, and is lost from the record. O'Sullivan

[090-145]reached France safely, where, with Stapleton, Lynch, and the Irish and

[090-146]Scotch officers, he was welcomed and honoured of all brave men.

[090-147]Such was the last struggle of the Stuarts. For years after, the popular

[090-148]imagination in both countries clung fondly to Prince Charles. But the

[090-149]cause was dead. As if to bury it for ever, Charles, in despair, grew

[090-150]dissipated and desponding. In 1755, "the British Jacobites" sent

[090-151]Colonel McNamara, as their agent, to induce him to put away his

[090-152]mistress, Miss Walsingham, a demand with which he haughtily refused to

[090-153]comply. In 1766, when James III. died at Avignon, the French king and

[090-154]the Pope refused to acknowledge the prince by the title of Charles III.

[090-155]When the latter died, in 1788, at Rome, Cardinal York contented himself

[090-156]with having a medal struck, with the inscription "Henricus IX., Anglae

[090-157]Rex." He was the last of the Stuarts.

[090-159]Notwithstanding the utter defeat of the Scottish expedition, and the

[090-160]scatterment of the surviving companies of the brigade on all sorts of

[090-161]service from Canada to India, there were many of the exiled Irish in

[090-162]France, who did not yet despair of a national insurrection against the

[090-163]house of Hanover. In the year 1759, an imposing expedition was fitted

[090-164]out at Brest under Admiral Conflaus, and another at Dunkirk, under

[090-165]Commodore Thurot, whose real name was O'Farrell. The former, soon after

[090-166]putting to sea, was encountered at Quiberon by the English under Hawke,

[090-167]and completely defeated; but the latter entered the British channel

[090-168]unopposed, and proceeded to the appointed _rendezvous_. While cruising

[090-169]in search of Conflaus, the autumnal equinox drove the intrepid Thurot

[090-170]into the Northern ocean, and compelled him to winter among the frozen

[090-171]friths of Norway and the Orkneys. One of his five frigates returned to

[090-172]France, another was never heard of, but with the remaining three he

[090-173]emerged from the Scottish Islands, and entered Lough Foyle early in

[090-174]1760. He did not, however, attempt a landing at Derry, but appeared

[090-175]suddenly before Carrickfergus, on the 21st of February, and demanded

[090-176]its surrender. Placing himself at the head of his marines and sailors,

[090-177]he attacked the town, which, after a brave resistance by the

[090-178]commandant, Colonel Jennings, he took by assault. Here, for the first

[090-179]time, this earlier Paul Jones heard of the defeat of his admiral; after

[090-180]levying contributions on the rich burgesses and proprietors of

[090-181]Carrickfergus and Belfast, he again put to sea. His ships, battered by

[090-182]the wintry storms which they had undergone in northern latitudes, fell

[090-183]in near the Isle of Man with three English frigates, just out of port,

[090-184]under Commodore Elliott. A gallant action ensued, in which Thurot, or

[090-185]O'Farrell, and three hundred of his men were killed. The survivors

[090-186]struck to the victors, and the French ships were towed in a sinking

[090-187]state, into the port of Ramsey.

[090-188]The life thus lost in the joint service of France and Ireland, was a

[090-189]life illustrative of the Irish refugee class among whom he became a

[090-190]leader. Left an orphan in childhood, O'Farrell, though of a good

[090-191]family, had been bred in France in so menial a condition that he first

[090-192]visited England as a domestic servant. From that condition he rose to

[090-193]be a dexterous and successful captain in the contraband trade, so

[090-194]extensive in those times. In this capacity he visited almost every port

[090-195]of either channel, acquiring that accurate knowledge which, added to

[090-196]his admitted bravery and capacity, placed him at length at the head of

[090-197]a French squadron. "Throughout the expedition," says Lord Mahon, "the

[090-198]honour and humanity of this brave adventurer are warmly acknowledged by

[090-199]his enemies." "He fought his ship," according to the same author,

[090-200]"until the hold was almost filled with water, and the deck covered with

[090-201]dead bodies."
