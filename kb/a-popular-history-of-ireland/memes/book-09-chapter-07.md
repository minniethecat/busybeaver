CHAPTER VII.

[070-001]THE CESSATION AND ITS CONSEQUENCES.

[070-002]While the Confederate delegates, reverently uncovered, and Ormond, in

[070-003]hat and plume, as representing royalty, were signing "the cessation" at

[070-004]Castlemartin, the memorable Monroe, with all his men, were taking the

[070-005]covenant, on their knees, in the church of Carrickfergus, at the hands

[070-006]of the informer O'Connolly, now a colonel in the Parliamentary army,

[070-007]and high in the confidence of its chiefs. Soon after this ceremony,

[070-008]Monroe, appointed by the English Parliament Commander-in-Chief of all

[070-009]their forces in Ulster, united under his immediate leadership, of

[070-010]Scots, English, and Undertakers, not less than 10,000 men. With this

[070-011]force he marched southward as far as Newry, which he found an easy

[070-012]prey, and where he put to the sword, after surrender, sixty men,

[070-013]eighteen women, and two ecclesiastics. In vain the Confederates

[070-014]entreated Ormond to lead them against the common enemy in the North;

[070-015]pursuing always a line of policy of his own, in which their interest

[070-016]had a very slender part, that astute politician neither took the field,

[070-017]nor consented that they should do so of themselves. But the Supreme

[070-018]Council, roused by the remonstrances of the clergy, ordered Lord

[070-019]Castlehaven, with the title of Commander-in-Chief, to march against

[070-020]Monroe. This was virtually superseding O'Neil in his own province, and

[070-021]that it was so felt, even by its authors, is plain from their giving

[070-022]him simultaneously the command in Connaught. O'Neil, never greater than

[070-023]in acts of self-denial and self-sacrifice, stifled his profound

[070-024]chagrin, and cheerfully offered to serve under the English Earl, placed

[070-025]over his head. But the northern movements were, for many months,

[070-026]languid and uneventful; both parties seemed uncertain of their true

[070-027]policy; both, from day to day, awaited breathlessly for tidings from

[070-028]Kilkenny, Dublin, London, Oxford, or Edinburgh, to learn what new forms

[070-029]the general contest was to take, in order to guide their own conduct by

[070-030]the shifting phases of that intricate diplomacy.

[070-031]Among the first consequences of the cessation were the debarkation at

[070-032]Mostyn, in Scotland, of 3,000 well provided Irish troops, under

[070-033]_Colkitto_ (the left-handed,) Alexander McDonnell, brother of Lord

[070-034]Antrim. Following the banner of Montrose, these regiments performed

[070-035]great things at Saint Johnstown, at Aberdeen, at Inverlochy, all which

[070-036]have been eloquently recorded by the historians of that period. "Their

[070-037]reputation," says a cautious writer, "more than their number, unnerved

[070-038]the prowess of their enemies. No force ventured to oppose them in the

[070-039]field; and as they advanced, every fort was abandoned or surrendered."

[070-040]A less agreeable result of "the cessation," for the court at Oxford,

[070-041]was the retirement from the royal army of the Earl of Newcastle, and

[070-042]most of his officers, on learning that such favourable conditions had

[070-043]been made with Irish Papists. To others of his supporters—as the Earl

[070-044]of Shrewsbury—Charles was forced to assume a tone of apology for that

[070-045]truce, pleading the hard necessities which compelled him: the truth

[070-046]seems to be, that there were not a few then at Oxford, who, like Lord

[070-047]Spencer, would gladly have been on the other side—or at all events in a

[070-048]position of neutrality—provided they could have found "a salve for

[070-049]their honour," as gentlemen and cavaliers.

[070-050]The year 1644 opened for the Irish with two events of great

[070-051]significance—the appointment of Ormond as Viceroy, in January, and the

[070-052]execution at Tyburn, by order of the English Parliament, of Lord

[070-053]Maguire, a prisoner in the Tower since October, 1641. Maguire died with

[070-054]a courage and composure worthy of his illustrious name, and his

[070-055]profoundly religious character. His long absence had not effaced his

[070-056]memory from the hearts of his devoted clansmen of Fermanagh, and many a

[070-057]prayer was breathed, and many a vow of vengeance muttered among them,

[070-058]for what they must naturally have regarded as the cold-blooded judicial

[070-059]murder of their chief.

[070-060]Two Irish deputations—one Catholic, the other Protestant—proceeded this

[070-061]year to the King, at Oxford, with the approval of Ormond, who took care

[070-062]to be represented by confidential agents of his own. The Catholics

[070-063]found a zealous auxiliary in the queen, Henrietta Maria, who, as a

[070-064]co-religionist, felt with them, and, as a Frenchwoman, was free from

[070-065]insular prejudices against them. The Irish Protestants found a scarcely

[070-066]less influential advocate in the venerable Archbishop Usher, whose

[070-067]presence and countenance, as the most puritanical of his prelates, was

[070-068]most essential to the policy of Charles. The King heard both parties

[070-069]graciously—censured some of the demands of both as extravagant, and

[070-070]beyond his power to concede—admitted others to be reasonable and worthy

[070-071]of consideration—refused to confirm the churches they had seized to the

[070-072]Catholics—but was willing to allow them their "seminaries of

[070-073]education"—would not consent to enforce the penal laws on the demand of

[070-074]the Protestants—but declared that neither should the Undertakers be

[070-075]disturbed in their possessions or offices. In short, he pathetically

[070-076]exhorted both parties to consider his case as well as their own;

[070-077]promised them to call together the Irish Parliament at the earliest

[070-078]possible period; and so got rid of both deputations, leaving Ormond

[070-079]master of the position for some time longer.

[070-080]The agents and friends of the Irish Catholics on the Continent were

[070-081]greatly embarrassed, and not a little disheartened by the cessation. At

[070-082]Paris, at Brussels, at Madrid, but above all at Rome, it was regretted,

[070-083]blamed, or denounced, according to the temper or the insight of the

[070-084]discontented. His Catholic Majesty had some time before remitted a

[070-085]contribution of 20,000 dollars to the Confederate Treasury; one of

[070-086]Richelieu's last acts was to invite Con, son of Hugh O'Neil, to the

[070-087]French Court, and to permit the shipment of some pieces of ordnance to

[070-088]Ireland; from Rome, the celebrated Franciscan, Father Luke Wadding, had

[070-089]remitted 26,000 dollars, and the Nuncio Scarampi had brought further

[070-090]donations. The facility, therefore, with which the cessation had been

[070-091]agreed upon, against the views of the agents of the Catholic powers at

[070-092]Kilkenny, without any apparently sufficient cause, had certainly a

[070-093]tendency to check and chill the enthusiasm of those Catholic Princes

[070-094]who had been taught to look on the insurrection of the Irish as a

[070-095]species of Crusade. Remonstrances, warm, eloquent, and passionate, were

[070-096]poured in upon the most influential members of the Supreme Council,

[070-097]from those who had either by delegation, or from their own free will,

[070-098]befriended them abroad. These remonstrances reached that powerful body

[070-099]at Waterford, at Limerick, or at Galway, whither they had gone on an

[070-100]official visitation, to hear complaints, settle controversies, and

[070-101]provide for the better collection of the assessments imposed on each

[070-102]Province.

[070-103]An incident which occurred in Ulster, soon startled the Supreme Council

[070-104]from their pacific occupations. General Monroe, having proclaimed that

[070-105]all Protestants within his command should take "the solemn league and

[070-106]covenant," three thousand of that religion, still loyalists, met at

[070-107]Belfast, to deliberate on their answer. Monroe, however, apprised of

[070-108]their intentions, marched rapidly from Carrickfergus, entered the town

[070-109]under cover of night, and drove out the loyal Protestants at the point

[070-110]of the sword. The fugitives threw themselves into Lisburn, and Monroe

[070-111]appointed Colonel Hume as Governor of Belfast, for the Parliaments of

[070-112]Scotland and England. Castlehaven, with O'Neil still second in command,

[070-113]was now despatched northward against the army of the Covenant. Monroe,

[070-114]who had advanced to the borders of Meath as if to meet them, contented

[070-115]himself with gathering in great herds of cattle; as they advanced, he

[070-116]slowly fell back before them through Louth and Armagh, to his original

[070-117]head-quarters; Castlehaven then returned with the main body of the

[070-118]Confederate troops to Kilkenny, and O'Neil, depressed, but not

[070-119]dismayed, carried his contingent to their former position at Belturbet.

[070-120]In Munster, a new Parliamentary party had time to form its combinations

[070-121]under the shelter of the cessation. The Earl of Inchiquin, who had

[070-122]lately failed to obtain the Presidency of Munster from the King at

[070-123]Oxford, and the Lord Broghill, son of the great Southern Undertaker—the

[070-124]first Earl of Cork,—were at the head of this movement. Under pretence

[070-125]that the quarters allotted them by the cessation had been violated,

[070-126]they contrived to seize upon Cork, Youghal, and Kinsale. At Cork, they

[070-127]publicly executed Father Mathews, a Friar, and proceeding from violence

[070-128]to violence, they drove from the three places all the Catholic

[070-129]inhabitants. They then forwarded a petition to the King, beseeching him

[070-130]to declare the Catholics "rebels," and declaring their own

[070-131]determination to "die a thousand deaths sooner than condescend to any

[070-132]peace with them." At the same time they entered into or avowed their

[070-133]correspondence with the English Parliament, which naturally enough

[070-134]encouraged and assisted them. The Supreme Council met these

[070-135]demonstrations with more stringent instructions to General Purcell, now

[070-136]their chief in command, (Barry having retired on account of advanced

[070-137]age,) to observe the cessation, and to punish severely every infraction

[070-138]of it. At the same time they permitted or directed Purcell to enter

[070-139]into a trace with Inchiquin till the following April; and then they

[070-140]rested on their arms, in religious fidelity to the engagements they had

[070-141]signed at Castlemartin.

[070-142]The twelve-months' truce was fast drawing to a close, when the battle

[070-143]of Marston Moor stimulated Ormond to effect a renewal of the treaty.

[070-144]Accordingly, at his request, Lord Muskerry, and five other

[070-145]commissioners, left Kilkenny on the last day of August for Dublin.

[070-146]Between them and the Viceroy, the cessation was prolonged till the

[070-147]first of December following; and when that day came, it was further

[070-148]protracted, as would appear, for three months, by which time, (March,

[070-149]1645,) Ormond informed them that he had powers from the King to treat

[070-150]for a permanent settlement.

[070-151]During the six months that the original cessation was thus protracted

[070-152]by the policy of Ormond, the Supreme Council sent abroad new agents,

[070-153]"to know what they had to trust to, and what succours they might really

[070-154]depend on from abroad." Father Hugh Bourke was sent to Spain, and Sir

[070-155]Richard Belling to Rome, where Innocent X, had recently succeeded to

[070-156]that generous friend of the Catholic Irish. Urban VIII. The voyage of

[070-157]these agents was not free from hazard, for, whereas, before the

[070-158]cessation, the privateers commissioned by the Council, sheltered and

[070-159]supplied in the Irish harbours, had kept the southern coast clear of

[070-160]hostile shipping, now that they had been withdrawn under the truce, the

[070-161]parliamentary cruisers had the channel all to themselves. Waterford and

[070-162]Wexford—the two chief Catholic ports in that quarter—instead of seeing

[070-163]their waters crowded with prizes, now began to tremble for their own

[070-164]safety. The strong fort of Duncannon, on the Wexford side of Waterford

[070-165]harbour, was corruptly surrendered by Lord Esmond, to Inchiquin and the

[070-166]Puritans. After a ten-weeks' siege, however, and the expenditure of

[070-167]19,000 pounds of powder, the Confederates retook the fort, in spite of

[070-168]all the efforts made for its relief. Esmond, old and blind, escaped by

[070-169]a timely death the penalty due to his treason. Following up this

[070-170]success, Castlehaven rapidly invested other southern strongholds in

[070-171]possession of the same party, Cappoquin, Lismore, Mallow, Mitchelstown,

[070-172]Doneraile and Liscarroll surrendered on articles; Rostellan, commanded

[070-173]by Inchiquin's brother, was stormed and taken; Boghill was closely

[070-174]besieged in Youghal, but, being relieved from sea, successfully

[070-175]defended himself. In another quarter, the Parliament was equally

[070-176]active. To compensate for the loss of Galway, they had instructed the

[070-177]younger Coote, on whom they had conferred the Presidency of Connaught,

[070-178]to withdraw the regiment of Sir Frederick Hamilton, and 400 other

[070-179]troops, from the command of Monroe, and with these, Sir Robert

[070-180]Stewart's forces, and such others as he could himself raise, to invest

[070-181]Sligo. Against the force thus collected, Sligo could not hope to

[070-182]contend, and soon, from that town, as from a rallying and resting

[070-183]place, 2,000 horsemen were daily launched upon the adjoining country.

[070-184]Lord Clanrickarde, the royal president of the province, as unpopular as

[070-185]trimmers usually are in times of crisis, was unable to make head

[070-186]against this new danger. But the Confederates, under Sir James Dillon,

[070-187]and Dr. O'Kelly, the heroic Archbishop of Tuam, moved by the pitiful

[070-188]appeals of the Sligo people, boldly endeavoured to recover the town.

[070-189]They succeeded in entering the walls, but were subsequently repulsed

[070-190]and routed. The Archbishop was captured and tortured to death; some of

[070-191]the noblest families of the province and of Meath had also to mourn

[070-192]their chiefs; and several valuable papers, found or pretended to be

[070-193]found in the Archbishop's carriage, were eagerly given to the press of

[070-194]London by the Parliament of England. This tragedy at Sligo occurred on

[070-195]Sunday, October 26th, 1645.
