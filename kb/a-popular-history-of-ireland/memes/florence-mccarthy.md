## Overview

The name Florence McCarthy (Fineen, or Finghin MacCarthaigh, in Irish) was borne by two of the most remarkable chieftains of the MacCarthy dynasty of Desmond. The earlier figure, Fineen MacCarthaigh (died 1261), was Lord of Carbury and "the most celebrated man of his name," who inflicted the most complete defeat the Anglo-Normans had yet experienced on Irish soil at the Battle of Callan-Glen. (031-061) (031-072) The later figure, Florence MacCarthy Reagh (c. 1560--1640), was Lord of Carberry who joined [[hugh-oneill|Hugh O'Neill]]'s confederacy during the [[nine-years-war|Nine Years' War]] and was inaugurated as McCarthy More with O'Neill's sanction. (060-200) (060-204)

The MacCarthy Reagh branch, from which both men descended, was founded by Cormac MacCarthy after he compelled his elder brother Donald Gott to acknowledge him as lord of the four baronies of Carbury. (031-048) Their principal castles stood at Dunmanway, Baltimore, Castlehaven, Lough-Fyne, and on the islands of Inis-Sherkin and Clear Island. (031-052)

## The Battle of Callan-Glen

In the year 1260, John Fitz-Thomas of the Geraldines of Desmond undertook an expedition to assert his family's claim over the territories of Munster. He was joined by the Justiciary William Dene, Walter de Burgo Earl of Ulster, Walter de Riddlesford Baron of Bray, and Donnel Roe, a chief of a rival branch of the MacCarthy family. (031-065) (031-067)

The Lord of Carbury united under his standard the chief Eugenian families, not only of the coast but even of McCarthy More's own principality. The battle was fought with great ferocity at Callan-Glen, near Kenmare in Kerry. (031-069) (031-071)

> John Fitz-Thomas, his son Maurice, eight barons, fifteen knights, and countless numbers of common soldiers were slain. (031-073)

The Anglo-Normans received the most complete defeat they had yet experienced on Irish ground. The Monastery of Tralee received the dead body of its founder John Fitz-Thomas and his son Maurice, a testament to the scale of the Geraldine catastrophe. (031-072) (031-075)

## The Campaign Against the Norman Castles

Following his triumph at Callan-Glen, Florence McCarthy moved swiftly to exploit the disarray of the invaders. He captured and broke down in rapid succession all the English castles in his neighbourhood. (031-076)

> Florence McCarthy, following up his blow, captured and broke down in swift succession all the English castles in his neighbourhood, including those of Macroom, Dunnamark, Dunloe, and Killorglin. (031-076)

This systematic demolition of fortified positions represented a strategic masterstroke. By denying the Normans their strongpoints, McCarthy denied them the ability to re-establish a military presence in the region. The campaign pushed the Geraldine interest in Desmond back by a generation. (031-083)

However, the campaign ended in tragedy. While besieging one of the remaining Norman castles at Ringrone, the victorious chief was cut off in the full tide of his conquest. (031-079) His brother, known as the Atheleireach (or suspended priest), succeeded to his possessions. The death of the victor arrested the panic of the defeat, but McGee records that "Munster saw another generation before her invaders had shaken off the depression of the battle of Callan-glen." (031-081) (031-083)

## The Elizabethan Florence McCarthy

Three and a half centuries later, another Florence McCarthy emerged as a leader of the MacCarthy line, this time under the title Lord of Carberry. During the Nine Years' War, when [[hugh-oneill|Hugh O'Neill]] marched south in his visitation of the provinces in the year 1600, he camped at Inniscarra upon the River Lee for three weeks consolidating the Catholic party in South Munster. (060-195) (060-196)

Among the chiefs who visited O'Neill's camp were "two of the most remarkable men of the southern Province, Florence McCarthy, Lord of Carberry, and Donald O'Sullivan, Lord of Bearehaven." (060-199)

> McCarthy "like Saul, higher by the head and shoulders than any of his house," had brain in proportion to his brawn. (060-201)

Florence was inaugurated with O'Neill's sanction as McCarthy More, the traditional title of the head of the MacCarthy clan. Although the rival house of Muskerry fiercely resisted his claim to superiority at first, McGee notes that "a wiser choice could not have been made had the times tended to confirm it." (060-204) (060-207)

## Political Intrigue and the Carew Truce

The Elizabethan Florence McCarthy found himself at the centre of Sir George Carew's strategy of division. Carew, the English Lord President of Munster, prided himself on his "wit and cunning" and the composition of "sham and counterfeit letters." (061-067) After escaping with Lord Thomond from the scene of the Earl of Ormond's capture by the O'Moores, Carew's first act on reaching Cork was to conclude a month's truce with Florence McCarthy. (061-079) (061-081)

> This he did, in order to gain time to perfect a plot for the destruction of O'Neil's other friend, called in derision, by the Anglo-Irish of Munster, the *sugane* (or straw-rope) Earl of Desmond. (061-081)

This truce was a tactical move in Carew's broader campaign to isolate [[hugh-oneill|Hugh O'Neill]] by detaching his southern allies. The plot involved the seduction of Dermid O'Conor, a mercenary captain married to a Fitzgerald lady, into betraying the Desmond earl who had been set up by O'Neill as the Catholic claimant. (061-087) (061-090) Though the immediate conspiracy against the Sugane Earl ultimately failed, the truce with McCarthy demonstrated the effectiveness of Carew's "princely policie" of division in weakening the Catholic Confederation. (061-094) (061-102)

## Legacy

The two Florence McCarthys bookend the long resistance of Gaelic Munster to foreign domination. The 13th-century Fineen demonstrated that a united Gaelic coalition could defeat the largest Norman armies in the field and systematically dismantle their fortifications. His victory at Callan-Glen was the most severe reversal the Anglo-Norman interest in Munster had suffered since the initial invasion, and the depression it caused among the invaders lasted for decades. (031-072) (031-083)

The later Florence McCarthy Reagh represented the enduring resilience of the MacCarthy line well into the Elizabethan period. His alliance with [[hugh-oneill|Hugh O'Neill]] showed him as a man of strategic judgment, able to weigh the shifting currents of Irish and European politics. Though ultimately overcome by Carew's combination of military pressure and political manipulation, he maintained the MacCarthy presence in Carberry through the collapse of the Nine Years' War and the subsequent reshaping of Irish society. (061-079) (061-081)

The MacCarthy dynasty, unlike the O'Donnells, O'Neills, and O'Connors, largely retained their Munster lands into the modern period through the earldom of Clancarty, itself descended from the MacCarthy More line. Both Florence McCarthys, warrior and political survivor alike, contributed to this remarkable continuity.
