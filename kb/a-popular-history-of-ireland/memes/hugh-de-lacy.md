## Overview

Hugh de Lacy (c. 1135–1186) was an Anglo-Norman nobleman who became one of the most powerful figures of the Norman invasion of Ireland. Appointed Lord of Meath by [[henry-ii|Henry II]] and serving multiple terms as Lord Deputy and Justiciary of Ireland, de Lacy sought to establish an independent power base in the Irish midlands. His career was cut short by assassination in 1186 while superintending the construction of a castle on the ancient monastic site of Durrow. (026-091) (029-005)

De Lacy arrived in Ireland in the train of [[henry-ii|Henry II]] during the campaign of 1171, and rapidly rose to pre-eminence among the Norman adventurers. Through military prowess, strategic marriages, and castle-building, he became the dominant figure in the Irish midlands, known to the native Irish as "King of the foreigners." His descendants, the de Lacys of Meath, remained among the most powerful Anglo-Irish families for generations. (029-009) (029-012)

## Biography

### Arrival in Ireland

Hugh de Lacy came to Ireland as part of [[henry-ii|Henry II]]'s expedition of 1171, arriving alongside Earl [[strongbow|Richard de Clare]]. He was among the king's most trusted lieutenants, and Henry soon began to parcel out Irish territories to him. The grant of the kingdom of Meath to de Lacy was one of the most significant acts of the early Norman settlement, establishing a lordship that would dominate the Irish midlands for centuries. (026-091) (026-144)

De Lacy was appointed the first Lord Constable of Ireland, a hereditary military office that underscored his central role in the Norman military establishment. His authority in Meath was cemented through the construction of numerous castles and the planting of military garrisons throughout East-Meath. De Lacy returning to the government of [[dublin|Dublin]] in 1179 marked the beginning of his most ambitious period. (028-203)

### Rise to Power

Restored to the supreme authority on the recall of Fitz-Aldelm in 1179, Hugh de Lacy began to conceive hopes, as [[strongbow|Strongbow]] had done before him, of carving out for himself a new kingdom. After the assassination of O'Ruarc, he assumed without further parley the titles of Lord of Meath and Breffni. To these he added that of Oriel or Louth, though his real strength lay in Meath, where his power was enhanced by a politic second marriage with Rose, daughter of O'Conor. (029-005) (029-008) (029-011)

> "Among the Irish he now began to be known as King of the foreigners, and some such assumption of royal authority caused his recall for a few months in the year 1180." (029-012)

This founder of the Irish de Lacys is described by Giraldus Cambrensis, who knew him personally, as a man of Gallic sobriety—ambitious, avaricious, and lustful, of small stature and deformed shape, with repulsive features and dark, deep-set eyes. By the Irish of the midland districts he was bitterly detested as a sacrilegious spoiler of their churches and monasteries, and the most powerful among their invaders. The murder of O'Ruarc, whose title of Breffni he had usurped, was attributed to his deep-laid design. (029-019) (029-023) (029-025)

### Castle-Building and Governance

De Lacy was among the most energetic castle-builders of the Norman conquest, dotting the landscape of Meath and the surrounding territories with fortifications. His castles served both to project military power and to attract settlers—the "strong men" who would form the backbone of the Norman colony. Among the key sites he selected was the ancient monastery of Durrow, planted by Columbcille seven centuries earlier, in the fertile region watered by the River Brosna. This act of profanity would prove his undoing. (029-028) (029-029)

The de Lacy family established a model of Anglo-Irish lordship that combined feudal Norman structures with an acceptance of Irish customs and marriage alliances. His marriage to Rose O'Conor exemplifies this strategic blending of Norman and Gaelic power. The chronicles record that de Lacy's grants included territories "almost within sight of [[dublin|Dublin]]," underscoring the proximity and immediacy of his power. After ten years of exile, the banished de Lacys returned to their Irish estates, demonstrating how deeply rooted the family had become. (032-025) (032-030) (032-041)

### Assassination

De Lacy's career came to a violent end in 1186. While personally superintending the construction of a castle on the foundations of the ancient monastery of Durrow, he was struck down. The assassin, O'Meyey, a young man of good birth and foster-brother to a neighbouring chief of Teffia known as "the Fox," struck off his head with a single blow of his axe and escaped into the neighbouring forest of Kilclare during the confusion which ensued. (029-032) (029-034)

> "This act of profanity was fated to be his last, for, while personally superintending the work, O'Meyey... struck off his head with a single blow of his axe and escaped into the neighbouring forest of Kilclare." (029-031) (029-034)

The murder of O'Ruarc, whose title of Breffni de Lacy had usurped, was attributed to his deep-laid design; he certainly shared the odium with the advantage that ensued from it. Nor was his own end, the chroniclers observe, unlike that of his rival. De Lacy left issue—two sons, Hugh and Walter, by his first wife, and a third, William Gorm, by his second—of whom, and of their posterity, we shall have many occasions to make mention. (029-026) (029-036)

### Prince John's Expedition

In one of the intervals of de Lacy's disfavour, Prince John, surnamed Lackland, was sent over by his father to strengthen the English interest in Ireland. He arrived in Waterford with a fleet of sixty ships in 1185, but his expedition proved a disaster. The young Prince—then only eighteen—mocked the venerable Irish chiefs who came to offer him the kiss of peace, plucking at their beards. After a campaign of folly and misconduct, Henry recalled his reckless son and entrusted, for the last time, the command to Hugh de Lacy. (029-039) (029-083)

## Legacy

### The de Lacy Dynasty

The de Lacys of Meath became one of the most enduring Anglo-Irish dynasties. His son Walter succeeded to the lordship of Meath, while his younger son Hugh became [[john-de-courcy|Earl of Ulster]]. The family's power, measured in the second quarter of the thirteenth century, was immense. The co-heiresses, Margaret and Matilda de Lacy, married into the foremost Norman houses, and their bloodline merged with the Geraldine and Butler families. (032-002) (032-014)

### Historical Assessment

Among all the Normans who governed Ireland in the first generation of the conquest, McGee singles out Hugh de Lacy along with the Red [[john-de-courcy|Earl of Ulster]] for special distinction, noting that few others could compare in ability and influence. The last descendant of Hugh de Lacy and the daughter of O'Conor appears in the annals at Buree and Bruff, marking the final trace of a lineage that shaped Irish history for over four centuries. (045-402) (060-054)
