## Overview

Brian Boru (c. 941–1014), also known as Brian Boroimhe or Brian of the Tributes, was the High King of Ireland from 1002 until his death at the [[battle-of-clontarf|Battle of Clontarf]] in 1014. Rising from the Dalcassian dynasty of Thomond in the west of Ireland, Brian broke the centuries-old monopoly of the O'Neill dynasty on the High Kingship and became the first ruler from outside that line to be acknowledged as Ard-Righ of all Ireland. (014-003) (014-238)

His reign marked the high point of native Irish kingship in the medieval period. He reformed the legal system, endowed schools and churches, and dealt the decisive blow against Norse power in Ireland at the [[battle-of-clontarf|Battle of Clontarf]]. (014-053) (014-253)

## Biography

### Early Life

Brian was born around 941 at Kinkora (Ceann Coradh) near Killaloe in present-day County Clare, the youngest son of Kennedy (Cennétig), chief of the Dalcassian tribe. His early life was shaped by the violent struggle between his people and the Norse settlers of Limerick. From a very early age he served under his brother Mahon, who succeeded their father as chief of Thomond. (013-053) (013-063)

### Rise to Power

When Mahon was treacherously murdered by his enemies, Brian, then in his thirty-fifth year, was seized with a determination for justice and vengeance. He pursued and slew his brother's murderers, and soon established himself as chief of Thomond. After defeating Molloy, son of Bran, in 978, Brian was universally acknowledged as King of Munster. (013-095) (013-105) (013-126)

### Alliance with Malachy and Accession to High Kingship

Around 987, Brian entered into an alliance with [[malachy-ii|Malachy II]], the reigning High King, and together they defeated the Danes at Glen-Mama in Wicklow. The alliance, however, eventually gave way to rivalry. After a complex diplomatic struggle lasting several years, Malachy yielded the High Kingship to Brian in 1002. (013-158) (013-170)

### Reign as High King

During the remainder of his life, Brian wielded power with accustomed vigour. He enforced the laws, patronized learning, and maintained a period of relative peace from 1005 to 1010, during which he endowed churches and fortified strategic positions. At Kinkora, his royal seat, he entertained 3,000 guests at Christmas, including the chiefs of various provinces. (014-014) (014-026) (014-078)

The simple Annalists applaud Brian's administration of the laws and his generosity to the Church. To justice he added mercy, and Brian's own posterity became known as the O'Briens, one of the most distinguished families in Irish history. (014-053) (014-061)

### The Battle of Clontarf

In 1014, a coalition of Norse forces from [[dublin|Dublin]] and the Orkneys, allied with the rebellious [[leinster|Leinster]] king Maelmordha, rose against Brian. The climactic battle was fought at Clontarf, near [[dublin|Dublin]], on Good Friday. Before the battle, Brian, crucifix in hand, harangued his army: "On this day Christ died for you—remember!" (014-180) (014-203)

The battle was fiercely contested. Brian's son Morrogh led the Munster tribes with great valour, while the aged king, now in his seventies, remained apart from the fighting. In one of the most dramatic moments, a servant informed Brian of approaching enemies: "They are green-naked people," and Brian, recognizing the Norse champions, sent for his guards. (014-216) (014-218)

In the closing stages of the battle, Brian was discovered alone and unprotected by a party of fleeing Norsemen, who slew the aged king. He fell as he had lived—"Brian of the tributes," the Ard-Righ of Ireland. (014-230)

## Legacy

Brian Boru's reign represented the culmination of native Irish kingship. He broke the Uí Néill monopoly on the High Kingship, demonstrating that leadership of the Irish nation was open to merit and conquest rather than mere heredity. The [[battle-of-clontarf|Battle of Clontarf]], though fatal to Brian himself, broke Norse military power in Ireland forever. (014-253)

His descendants, the O'Briens, remained one of the most powerful families in Ireland for centuries. Brian's name was celebrated in song and story as the ideal Irish king—just, generous, learned, and fierce in battle. He stands among the greatest figures in Irish history.
