CHAPTER VIII.

[114-001]O'CONNELL'S LEADERSHIP—THE CLARE ELECTION—EMANCIPATION OF THE

[114-002]CATHOLICS.

[114-003]A very little reflection will enable us to judge, even at this day, the

[114-004]magnitude of the contest in which O'Connell was the great popular

[114-005]leader, during the reign of George IV. In Great Britain, a very

[114-006]considerable section of the ancient peerage and gentry, with the Earl

[114-007]Marshal at their head, were to be restored to political existence, by

[114-008]the act of Emancipation; a missionary, and barely tolerated clergy were

[114-009]to be clothed, in their own country, with the commonest rights of

[114-010]British subjects—protection to life and property. In Ireland,

[114-011]seven-eighths of the people, one-third of the gentry, the whole of the

[114-012]Catholic clergy, the numerous and distinguished array of the Catholic

[114-013]bar, and all the Catholic townsmen, taxed but unrepresented in the

[114-014]corporate bodies, were to enter on a new civil and social condition, on

[114-015]the passage of the act. In the colonies, except Canada, where that

[114-016]church was protected by treaty, the change of Imperial policy towards

[114-017]Catholics was to be felt in every relation of life, civil, military,

[114-018]and ecclesiastical, by all persons professing that religion. Some years

[114-019]ago, a bishop of Southern Africa declared, that, until O'Connell's

[114-020]time, it was impossible for Catholics to obtain any consideration from

[114-021]the officials at the Cape of Good Hope. Could there be a more striking

[114-022]illustration of the magnitude of the movement, which, rising in the

[114-023]latitude of Ireland, flung its outermost wave of influence on the

[114-024]shores of the Indian ocean?

[114-025]The adverse hosts to be encountered in this great contest, included a

[114-026]large majority of the rank and wealth of both kingdoms. The King, who

[114-027]had been a Whig in his youth, had grown into a Tory in his old age; the

[114-028]House of Lords were strongly hostile to the measure, as were also the

[114-029]universities, both in England and Ireland; the Tory party, in and out

[114-030]of Parliament; the Orange organization in Ireland; the civil and

[114-031]military authorities generally, with the great bulk of the rural

[114-032]magistracy and the municipal authorities. The power to overcome this

[114-033]power should be indeed formidable, well organized and wisely directed.

[114-034]The Lord Lieutenant selected by Mr. Canning, was the Marquis of

[114-035]Anglesea, a frank soldier, as little accustomed to play the politician

[114-036]as any man of his order and distinction could be. He came to Ireland,

[114-037]in many respects the very opposite of Lord Wellesley; no orator

[114-038]certainly, and so far as he had spoken formerly, an enemy rather than a

[114-039]friend to the Catholics. But he had not been three months in office

[114-040]when he began to modify his views; he was the first to prohibit, in

[114-041]Dublin, the annual Orange outrage on the 12th of July, and by

[114-042]subsequent, though slow degrees, he became fully convinced that the

[114-043]Catholic claims could be settled only by Concession. Lord Francis

[114-044]Leveson Gower, afterwards Earl of Ellesmere, accompanied the Marquis as

[114-045]Chief Secretary.

[114-046]The accession to office of a prime minister friendly to the Catholics,

[114-047]was the signal for a new attempt to raise that "No-Popery" cry which

[114-048]had already given twenty years of political supremacy to Mr. Perceval

[114-049]and Lord Liverpool. In Ireland, this feeling appeared under the guise

[114-050]of what was called "the New Reformation," which, during the summer of

[114-051]1827, raged with all the proverbial violence of the _odium theologicum_

[114-052]from Cork to Derry. Priests and parsons, laymen and lawyers, took part

[114-053]in this general politico-religious controversy, in which every possible

[114-054]subject of difference between Catholic and Protestant was publicly

[114-055]discussed. Archbishop Magee of Dublin, the Rev. Sir Harcourt Lees, son

[114-056]of a former English placeman at the Castle, and the Rev. Mr. Pope, were

[114-057]the clerical leaders in this crusade; Exeter-Hall sent over to assist

[114-058]them the Honourable and Reverend Baptist Noel, Mr. Wolff, and Captain

[114-059]Gordon, a descendant of the hero of the London riot of 1798. At Derry,

[114-060]Dublin, Carlow, and Cork, the challenged agreed to defend their

[114-061]doctrines. Father Maginn, Maguire, Maher, McSweeney, and some others

[114-062]accepted these challenges; Messrs. O'Connell, Shiel, and other laymen,

[114-063]assisted, and the oral discussion of theological and historical

[114-064]questions became as common as town talk in every Irish community.

[114-065]Whether, in any case, these debates conduced to conversion is doubtful;

[114-066]but they certainly supplied the Catholic laity with a body of facts and

[114-067]arguments very necessary at that time, and which hardly any other

[114-068]occasion could have presented. The Right Rev. Dr. Doyle, however,

[114-069]considered them far from beneficial to the cause of true religion; and

[114-070]though he tolerated a first discussion in his diocese, he positively

[114-071]forbade a second. The Archbishop of Armagh and other prelates issued

[114-072]their mandates to the clergy to refrain from these oral disputes, and

[114-073]the practice fell into disuse.

[114-074]The notoriety of "the Second Reformation" was chiefly due to the

[114-075]ostentatious patronage of it by the lay chiefs of the Irish oligarchy.

[114-076]Mr. Synge, in Clare, Lord Lorton, and Mr. McClintock at Dundalk, were

[114-077]indefatigable in their evangelizing exertions. The Earl of Roden—to

[114-078]show his entire dependence on the translated Bible—threw all his other

[114-079]books into a fish pond on his estate. Lord Farnham was even more

[114-080]conspicuous in the revival; he spared neither patronage nor writs of

[114-081]ejectment to convert his tenantry. The reports of conversions upon his

[114-082]lordship's estates, and throughout his county, attracted so much

[114-083]notice, that Drs. Curtis, Crolly, Magauran, O'Reilly, and McHale, met

[114-084]on the 9th of December, 1826, at Cavan, to inquire into the facts. They

[114-085]found, while there had been much exaggeration on the part of the

[114-086]reformers, that some hundreds of the peasantry had, by various powerful

[114-087]temptations, been led to change their former religion. The bishops

[114-088]received back some of the converts, and a jubilee established among

[114-089]them completed their reconversion. The Hon. Mr. Noel and Captain Gordon

[114-090]posted to Cavan, with a challenge to discussion for their lordships; of

[114-091]course, their challenge was not accepted. Thomas Moore's inimitable

[114-092]satire was the most effective weapon against such fanatics.

[114-093]The energetic literature of the Catholic agitation attracted much more

[114-094]attention than its oral polemics. Joined to a bright army of Catholic

[114-095]writers, including Dr. Doyle, Thomas Moore, Thomas Furlong, and Charles

[114-096]Butler, there was the powerful phalanx of the _Edinburgh Review_ led by

[114-097]Jeffrey and Sidney Smith, and the English liberal press, headed by

[114-098]William Cobbett. Thomas Campbell, the Poet of Hope, always and

[114-099]everywhere the friend of freedom, threw open his _New Monthly_, to

[114-100]Shiel, and William Henry Curran, whose sketches of the Irish Bar and

[114-101]Bench, of Dublin politics, and the county elections of 1826, will live

[114-102]as long as any periodical papers of the day. The indefatigable Shiel,

[114-103]writing French as fluently as English, contributed besides to the

[114-104]_Gazette de France_ a series of papers, which were read with great

[114-105]interest on the Continent. These articles were the precursors of many

[114-106]others, which made the Catholic question at length an European

[114-107]question. An incident quite unimportant in itself, gave additional zest

[114-108]to these French articles. The Duke de Montebello, with two of his

[114-109]friends, Messrs. Duvergier and Thayer, visited Ireland in 1826.

[114-110]Duvergier wrote a series of very interesting letters on the "State of

[114-111]Ireland," which, at the time, went through several editions. At a

[114-112]Catholic meeting at Ballinasloe, the Duke had some compliments paid

[114-113]him, which he gracefully acknowledged, expressing his wishes for the

[114-114]success of their cause. This simple act excited a great deal of

[114-115]criticism in England. The Paris press was roused in consequence, and

[114-116]the French Catholics, becoming more and more interested, voted an

[114-117]address and subscription to the Catholic Association. The Bavarian

[114-118]Catholics followed their example, and similar communications were

[114-119]received from Spain and Italy.

[114-120]But the movement abroad did not end in Europe. An address from British

[114-121]India contained a contribution of three thousand pounds sterling. From

[114-122]the West Indies and Canada, generous assistance was rendered.

[114-123]In the United States sympathetic feeling was most active. New York felt

[114-124]almost as much interested in the cause as Dublin. In 1826 and 1827,

[114-125]associations of "Friends of Ireland" were formed at New York, Boston,

[114-126]Washington, Norfolk, Charleston, Augusta, Louisville, and Bardstown.

[114-127]Addresses in English and French were prepared for these societies,

[114-128]chiefly by Dr. McNevin, at New York, and Bishop England, at Charleston.

[114-129]The American, like the French press, became interested in the subject,

[114-130]and eloquent allusions were made to it in Congress. On the 20th of

[114-131]January, 1828, the veteran McNevin wrote to Mr. O'Connell—"Public

[114-132]opinion in America is deep, and strong, and universal, in your behalf.

[114-133]This predilection prevails over the broad bosom of our extensive

[114-134]continent. Associations similar to ours are everywhere starting into

[114-135]existence—in our largest and wealthiest cities—in our hamlets and our

[114-136]villages—in our most remote sections; and at this moment, the propriety

[114-137]of convening, at Washington, delegates of the friends of Ireland, of

[114-138]all the states, is under serious deliberation. A fund will erelong be

[114-139]derived from American patriotism in the United States, which will

[114-140]astonish your haughtiest opponents."

[114-141]The Parliamentary fortunes of the great question were at the same time

[114-142]brightening. The elections of 1826, had, upon the whole, given a large

[114-143]increase of strength to its advocates. In England and Scotland, under

[114-144]the influence of the "No-Popery" cry, they had lost some ground, but in

[114-145]Ireland they had had an immense triumph. The death of the

[114-146]generous-hearted Canning, hastened as it was by anti-Catholic

[114-147]intrigues, gave a momentary check to the progress of liberal ideas; but

[114-148]they were retarded only to acquire a fresh impulse destined to bear

[114-149]them, in the next few years, farther than they had before advanced in

[114-150]an entire century.

[114-151]The _ad interim_ administration of Lord Goderich gave way, by its own

[114-152]internal discords, in January, 1828, to the Wellington and Peel

[114-153]administration. The Duke was Premier, the Baronet leader of the House

[114-154]of Commons; with Mr. Huskisson, Lord Palmerston, in the cabinet; Lord

[114-155]Anglesea remained as Lord Lieutenant. But this coalition with the

[114-156]friends of Canning was not destined to outlive the session of 1828; the

[114-157]lieutenants of the late Premier were doomed, for some time longer, to

[114-158]suffer for their devotion to his principles.

[114-159]This session of 1828, is—in the history of religious liberty—the most

[114-160]important and interesting in the annals of the British Parliament.

[114-161]Almost at its opening, the extraordinary spectacle was exhibited of a

[114-162]petition signed by 800,000 Irish Catholics, praying for the repeal of

[114-163]"the Corporation and Test Acts," enacted on the restoration of Charles

[114-164]II., against the non-Conformists. Monster petitions, both for and

[114-165]against the repeal of these acts, as well as for and against Catholic

[114-166]emancipation, soon became of common occurrence. Protestants of all

[114-167]sects petitioned for, but still more petitioned against equal rights

[114-168]for Catholics; while Catholics petitioned for the rights of Protestant

[114-169]dissenters. It is a spectacle to look back upon with admiration and

[114-170]instruction; exhibiting as it does, so much of a truly tolerant spirit

[114-171]in Christians of all creeds, worthy of all honour and imitation.

[114-172]In April, "the Corporation and Test Acts" were repealed; in May, the

[114-173]Canningites seceded from the Duke's government, and one of the

[114-174]gentlemen brought in to fill a vacant seat in the Cabinet—Mr. Vesey

[114-175]Fitzgerald, member for Clare—issued his address to his electors, asking

[114-176]a renewal of their confidence. Out of this event grew another, which

[114-177]finally and successfully brought to an issue the century-old Catholic

[114-178]question.

[114-180]The Catholic Association, on the accession of the Wellington-Peel

[114-181]Cabinet, had publicly pledged itself to oppose every man who would

[114-182]accept office under these statesmen. The memory of both as

[114-183]ex-secretaries—but especially Peel's—was odious in Ireland. When,

[114-184]however, the Duke had sustained, and ensured thereby the passage of the

[114-185]repeal of "the Corporation and Test Acts," Mr. O'Connell, at the

[114-186]suggestion of Lord John Russell the mover of the repeal, endeavoured to

[114-187]get his angry and uncompromising resolution against the Duke's

[114-188]government rescinded. Powerful as he was, however, the Association

[114-189]refused to go with him, and the resolution remained. So it happened

[114-190]that when Mr. Fitzgerald presented himself to the electors of Clare, as

[114-191]the colleague of Peel and Wellington, the Association at once

[114-192]endeavoured to bring out an opposition candidate. They pitched with

[114-193]this view on Major McNamara, a liberal Protestant of the county, at the

[114-194]head of one of its oldest families, and personally popular; but this

[114-195]gentleman, after keeping them several days in suspense, till the time

[114-196]of nomination was close at hand, positively declined to stand against

[114-197]his friend, Mr. Fitzgerald, to the great dismay of the associated

[114-198]Catholics.

[114-199]In their emergency, an idea, so bold and original, that it was at first

[114-200]received with general incredulity by the external public, was started.

[114-201]It was remembered by Sir David De Roose, a personal friend of

[114-202]O'Connell's, that the late sagacious John Keogh had often declared the

[114-203]Emancipation question would never be brought to an issue till some

[114-204]Catholic member elect stood at the bar of the House of Commons

[114-205]demanding his seat. A trusted few were at first consulted on the daring

[114-206]proposition, that O'Connell himself, in despite of the legal exclusion

[114-207]of all men of his religion, should come forward for Clare. Many were

[114-208]the consultations, and diverse the judgments delivered on this

[114-209]proposal, but at length, on the reception of information from the

[114-210]county itself, which gave strong assurance of success, the hero of the

[114-211]adventure decided for himself. The bold course was again selected as

[114-212]the wise course, and the spirit-stirring address of "the arch-Agitator"

[114-213]to the electors, was at once issued from Dublin. "Your county," he

[114-214]began by saying, "wants a representative. I respectfully solicit your

[114-215]suffrages, to raise me to that station.

[114-216]"Of my qualification to fill that station, I leave you to judge. The

[114-217]habits of public speaking, and many, many years of public business,

[114-218]render me, perhaps, equally suited with most men to attend to the

[114-219]interests of Ireland in Parliament.

[114-220]"You will be told I am not qualified to be elected; the assertion, my

[114-221]friends, is untrue. I am qualified to be elected, and to be your

[114-222]representative. It is true that as a Catholic I cannot, and of course

[114-223]never will, take the oaths at present prescribed to members of

[114-224]Parliament; but the authority which created these oaths (the

[114-225]Parliament), can abrogate them: and I entertain a confident hope that,

[114-226]if you elect me, the most bigoted of our enemies will see the necessity

[114-227]of removing from the chosen representative of the people an obstacle

[114-228]which would prevent him from doing his duty to his king and to his

[114-229]country."

[114-230]This address was followed instantly by the departure of all the most

[114-231]effective agitators to the scene of the great contest. Shiel went down

[114-232]as conducting agent for the candidate; Lawless left his Belfast

[114-233]newspaper, and Father Maguire his Leitrim flock; Messrs. Steele and

[114-234]O'Gorman Mahon, both proprietors in the county, were already in the

[114-235]field, and O'Connell himself soon followed. On the other hand, the

[114-236]leading county families, the O'Briens, McNamaras, Vandeleurs,

[114-237]Fitzgeralds and others, declared for their old favourite, Mr.

[114-238]Fitzgerald. He was personally much liked in the county; the son of a

[114-239]venerable anti-Unionist, the well-remembered Prime Sergeant, and a man

[114-240]besides of superior abilities. The county itself was no easy one to

[114-241]contest; its immense constituency (the 40-shilling freeholders had not

[114-242]yet been abolished), were scattered over a mountain and valley region,

[114-243]more than fifty miles long by above thirty wide. They were almost

[114-244]everywhere to be addressed in both languages—English and Irish—and when

[114-245]the canvass was over, they were still to be brought under the very eyes

[114-246]of the landlords, upon the breath of whose lips their subsistence

[114-247]depended, to vote the overthrow and conquest of those absolute masters.

[114-248]The little county town of Ennis, situated on the river Fergus, about

[114-249]110 miles south-west of Dublin, was the centre of attraction or of

[114-250]apprehension, and the hills that rise on either side of the little

[114-251]prosaic river soon swarmed with an unwonted population, who had

[114-252]resolved, subsist how they might, to see the election out. It is hardly

[114-253]an exaggeration to say that the eyes of the empire were turned, during

[114-254]those days of June, on the ancient patrimony of King Brian. "I fear the

[114-255]Clare election will end ill," wrote the Viceroy to the leader of the

[114-256]House of Commons. "This business," wrote the Lord Chancellor (Eldon),

[114-257]"must bring the Roman Catholic question to a crisis and a conclusion."

[114-258]"May the God of truth and justice protect and prosper you," was the

[114-259]public invocation for O'Connell's success, by the bishop of Kildare and

[114-260]Leighlin. "It was foreseen," said Sir Robert Peel, long afterwards,

[114-261]"that the Clare election would be the turning point of the Catholic

[114-262]question." In all its aspects, and to all sorts of men, this, then, was

[114-263]no ordinary election, but a national event of the utmost religious and

[114-264]political consequence. Thirty thousand people welcomed O'Connell into

[114-265]Ennis, and universal sobriety and order characterized the proceedings.

[114-266]The troops called out to overawe the peasantry, infected by the

[114-267]prevailing good humour, joined in their cheers. The nomination, the

[114-268]polling, and the declaration, have been described by the graphic pen of

[114-269]Shiel. At the close of the poll the numbers were—O'Connell, 2,057;

[114-270]Fitzgerald, 1,075; so Daniel O'Connell was declared duly elected,

[114-271]amidst the most extraordinary manifestations of popular enthusiasm. Mr.

[114-272]Fitzgerald, who gracefully bowed to the popular verdict, sat down, and

[114-273]wrote his famous despatch to Sir Robert Peel: "All the great

[114-274]interests," he said, "my dear Peel, broke down, and the desertion has

[114-275]been universal. Such a scene as we have had! Such a tremendous prospect

[114-276]as is open before us!"

[114-277]This "tremendous prospect," disclosed at the hustings of Ennis, was

[114-278]followed up by demonstrations which bore a strongly revolutionary

[114-279]character. Mr. O'Connell, on his return to Dublin, was accompanied by a

[114-280]_levee en masse_, all along the route, of a highly imposing

[114-281]description. Mr. Lawless, on his return to Belfast, was escorted

[114-282]through Meath and Monaghan by a multitude estimated at 100,000 men,

[114-283]whom only the most powerful persuasions of the Catholic clergy, and the

[114-284]appeals of the well-known liberal commander of the district, General

[114-285]Thornton, induced to disperse. Troops from England were ordered over in

[114-286]considerable numbers, but whole companies, composed of Irish Catholics,

[114-287]signalized their landing at Waterford and Dublin by cheers for

[114-288]O'Connell. Reports of the continued hostility of the government

[114-289]suggested desperate councils. Mr. Ford, a Catholic solicitor, openly

[114-290]proposed, in the Association, exclusive dealing and a run on the banks

[114-291]for specie, while Mr. John Claudius Beresford, and other leading

[114-292]Orangemen, publicly predicted a revival of the scenes and results of

[114-293]1798.

[114-294]The Clare election was, indeed, decisive; Lord Anglesea, who landed

[114-295]fully resolved to make no terms with those he had regarded from a

[114-296]distance as no better than rebels, became now one of their warmest

[114-297]partisans. His favourite counsellor was Lord Cloncurry, the early

[114-298]friend of Emmet and O'Conor; the true friend to the last of every

[114-299]national interest. For a public letter to Bishop Curtis, towards the

[114-300]close of 1828, in which he advises the Catholics to stand firm, he was

[114-301]immediately recalled from the government; but his former and his actual

[114-302]chief, within three months from the date of his recall, was equally

[114-303]obliged to surrender to the Association. The great duke was, or

[114-304]affected to be, really alarmed for the integrity of the empire, from

[114-305]the menacing aspect of events in Ireland. A call of Parliament was

[114-306]accordingly made for an early day, and, on the 5th of March, Mr. Peel

[114-307]moved a committee of the whole House, to go into a "consideration of

[114-308]the civil disabilities of his Majesty's Roman Catholic subjects." This

[114-309]motion, after two days' debate, was carried by a majority of 188. On

[114-310]the 10th of March the Relief Bill was read for the first time, and

[114-311]passed without opposition, such being the arrangement entered into

[114-312]while in committee. But in five days all the bigotry of the land had

[114-313]been aroused; nine hundred and fifty-seven petitions had already been

[114-314]presented against it; that from the city of London was signed by more

[114-315]than "an hundred thousand freeholders." On the 17th of March it passed

[114-316]to a second reading, and on the 30th to a third, with large majorities

[114-317]in each stage of debate. Out of 320 members who voted on the final

[114-318]reading, 178 were in its favour. On the 31st of March it was carried to

[114-319]the Lords by Mr. Peel, and read a first time; two days later, on the

[114-320]2nd of April, it was read a second time, on motion of the Duke of

[114-321]Wellington; a bitterly contested debate of three days followed; on the

[114-322]10th, it was read a third time, and passed by a majority of 104. Three

[114-323]days later the bill received the royal assent, and became law.

[114-324]The only drawbacks on this great measure of long-withheld justice,

[114-325]were, that it disfranchised the "forty-shilling freeholders" throughout

[114-326]Ireland, and condemned Mr. O'Connell, by the insertion of the single

[114-327]word "hereafter," to go back to Clare for re-election. In this there

[114-328]was little difficulty for him, but much petty spleen in the framers of

[114-329]the measure.

[114-330]While the Relief Bill was still under discussion, Mr. O'Connell

[114-331]presented himself, with his counsel, at the bar of the House of

[114-332]Commons, to claim his seat as member for Clare. The pleadings in the

[114-333]case were adjourned from day to day, during the months of March, April,

[114-334]and May. A committee of the House, of which Lord John Russell was

[114-335]Chairman, having been appointed in the meantime to consider the

[114-336]petition of Thomas Mahon and others, against the validity of the

[114-337]election, reported that Mr. O'Connell had been duly elected. On the

[114-338]15th of May, introduced by Lords Ebrington and Duncannon, the new

[114-339]member entered the House, and advanced to the table to be sworn by the

[114-340]Clerk. On the oath of abjuration being tendered to him, he read over

[114-341]audibly these words—"that the sacrifice of the mass, and the invocation

[114-342]of the blessed Virgin Mary, and other saints, as now practised in the

[114-343]Church of Rome, are impious and idolatrous:" at the subsequent passage,

[114-344]relative to the falsely imputed Catholic "doctrine of the dispensing

[114-345]power" of the Pope, he again read aloud, and paused. Then slightly

[114-346]raising his voice, he bowed, and added, "I decline, Mr. Clerk, to take

[114-347]this oath. Part of it I know to be false; another part I do not believe

[114-348]to be true."

[114-349]He was subsequently heard at the bar, in his own person, in explanation

[114-350]of his refusal to take the oath, and, according to custom, withdrew.

[114-351]The House then entered into a very animated discussion on the Solicitor

[114-352]General's motion "that Mr. O'Connell, having been returned a member of

[114-353]this House before the passing of the Act for the Relief of the Roman

[114-354]Catholics, he is not entitled to sit or vote in this House unless he

[114-355]first takes the oath of supremacy." For this motion the vote on a

[114-356]division was 190 against 116: majority, 74. So Mr. O'Connell had again

[114-357]to seek the suffrages of the electors of Clare.

[114-358]A strange, but well authenticated incident, struck with a somewhat

[114-359]superstitious awe both Protestants and Catholics, in a corner of

[114-360]Ireland the most remote from Clare, but not the least interested in the

[114-361]result of its memorable election. A lofty column on the walls of Derry

[114-362]bore the effigy of Bishop Walker, who fell at the Boyne, armed with a

[114-363]sword, typical of his martial inclinations, rather than of his

[114-364]religious calling. Many long years, by day and night, had his sword,

[114-365]sacred to liberty or ascendancy, according to the eyes with which the

[114-366]spectator regarded it, turned its steadfast point to the broad estuary

[114-367]of Lough Foyle. Neither wintry storms nor summer rains had loosened it

[114-368]in the grasp of the warlike churchman's effigy, until, on the 13th day
