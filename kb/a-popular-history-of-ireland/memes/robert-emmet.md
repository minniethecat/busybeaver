**Robert Emmet** (1778–1803) was an Irish nationalist and rebel leader whose failed rising of 1803 and subsequent execution made him one of the most romantic figures of Irish revolutionary history. His speech from the dock — "Let no man write my epitaph" — became one of the defining texts of Irish republicanism, inspiring later generations including the [[United Irishmen]] and the Young Ireland movement.

## Early Life and the United Irishmen

Emmet was born into a prosperous Protestant family in [[Dublin]], the youngest son of Dr. Robert Emmet, state physician. His elder brother, Thomas Addis Emmet, was a prominent member of the [[united-irishmen|Society of United Irishmen]]. The younger Emmet entered Trinity College [[dublin|Dublin]], where he distinguished himself as a student of science and mathematics, but his academic career was cut short by politics.

The [[Rebellion of 1798]], in which the [[united-irishmen|United Irishmen]] rose against British rule, ended in bloody defeat. Emmet, though not directly involved in the fighting, was radicalised by the repression that followed. He was expelled from Trinity College and went to the Continent, where he sought French military support for another rising. He met with Napoleon and Talleyrand, but the promised French invasion never materialised.

## The Rising of 1803

It was to a post-Union Ireland, seething with resentment at the recently enacted [[Act of Union 1800]], that Robert Emmet returned to [[dublin|Dublin]], in October 1802 (107-121). He began secretly manufacturing arms and explosives at depots in [[dublin|Dublin]], drawing on his scientific knowledge to design a folding pike and a new type of rocket. Robert Emmet seems to have been confidentially assured of support from several counties (107-138). But the plans went awry.

On 23 July 1803, the rising erupted prematurely. An accidental explosion at one of Emmet's arms depots forced his hand. What was intended as a coordinated seizure of [[dublin|Dublin]] Castle and other strategic points degenerated into a street riot. The Lord Lieutenant's carriage was attacked, and the Chief Justice, Lord Kilwarden, was dragged from his carriage and killed — an act that horrified public opinion and sealed the rising's fate.

> Than Robert Emmet's might well have been misled by the assurances of agents who promised what they could not deliver. The whole affair collapsed within hours. (107-173)

Emmet fled [[dublin|Dublin]] but was captured on 25 August, betrayed while attempting to see his sweetheart, Sarah Curran.

## Trial and Execution

The trial of Robert Emmet, from the wonderful death-speech delivered by the prisoner after conviction, belongs to the select annals of Irish political martyrdom (107-195). Before sentence was passed, Emmet delivered his famous speech from the dock. He spoke for over an hour, defending his motives while accepting his fate. "Let no man write my epitaph," he declared, "until my country takes her place among the nations of the earth."

He was found guilty of high treason and sentenced to be hanged, drawn, and quartered. The sentence was carried out on 20 September 1803 in Thomas Street, [[dublin|Dublin]]. He was twenty-five years old.

## Legacy

Emmet's failed rising, like those of [[Theobald Wolfe Tone]] before him and the Young Irelanders after, became part of the tradition of Irish revolutionary sacrifice. They shared the fate of Robert Emmet in 1803; O'Conor, Corbet, and other United Irish exiles carried his memory to France and America (104-320). At the time of Robert Emmet's *emeute*, the British administration tightened its grip on [[dublin|Dublin]], replacing the civil government with a more militarised presence (108-044).

The image of Emmet — young, idealistic, undaunted at the scaffold — proved more powerful in death than his rising had been in life. His speech from the dock was memorised by generations of Irish nationalists. [[Daniel O'Connell]], though a constitutional reformer who deplored violence, was deeply moved by Emmet's fate. Later, the Young Irelanders and the Fenians would invoke his name as they prepared their own risings.
