CHAPTER V.

[111-001]RETROSPECT OF THE STATE OF RELIGION AND LEARNING DURING THE REIGN OF

[111-002]GEORGE III.

[111-003]Before relating the decisive events in the contest for Catholic

[111-004]Emancipation, which marked the reign of George IV. we may be permitted

[111-005]to cast a glance backward over the religious and secular state of

[111-006]Ireland, during the sixty years' reign of George III.

[111-007]The relative position of the great religious denominations underwent a

[111-008]slow but important revolution during this long reign. In the last days

[111-009]of George II., a Chief-Justice was bold enough to declare that "the

[111-010]laws did not presume a Papist to exist in the kingdom;" but under the

[111-011]sway of his successor, though much against that successor's will, they

[111-012]advanced from one constitutional victory to another, till they stood,

[111-013]in the person of the Earl Marshal, on the very steps of the throne. In

[111-014]the towns and cities, the Catholic laity, once admitted to commerce and

[111-015]the professions, rose rapidly to wealth and honour. A Dublin Papist was

[111-016]at the head of the wine trade; another was the wealthiest grazier in

[111-017]the kingdom; a third, at Cork, was the largest provision merchant. With

[111-018]wealth came social ambition, and the heirs of these enfranchised

[111-019]merchants were by a natural consequence the judges and legislators of

[111-020]the next generation.

[111-021]The ecclesiastical organization of Ireland, as described in 1800 by the

[111-022]bishops in answer to queries of the Chief Secretary, was simple and

[111-023]inexpensive. The four archbishops and twenty bishops, were sustained by

[111-024]having certain parishes attached to their cathedrals, _in commendam_:

[111-025]other _Cathedraticum_ there seems to have been none. Armagh had then

[111-026]350 parish priests, Tuam 206, Cashel 314, and Dublin 156: in all 1126.

[111-027]The number of curates or coadjutors was at least equal to that of the

[111-028]parish priests; while of regulars then returned the number did not

[111-029]exceed 450. This large body of religious—24 prelates, nearly 3,000

[111-030]clergy—exclusive of female religious—were then, and have ever since

[111-031]been, sustained by the voluntary contributions of the laity, paid

[111-032]chiefly at the two great festivals of Christmas and Easter, or by

[111-033]customary offerings made at the close of the ceremonies of marriages,

[111-034]baptisms, and death. Though the income of some of the churches was

[111-035]considerable, in the great majority of cases the amount received barely

[111-036]sufficed to fulfil the injunction of St. Patrick to his disciples, that

[111-037]"the lamp should take but that wherewith it was fed."

[111-038]The Presbyterian clergy, though in some respects more dependent on

[111-039]their congregations than the Catholics were, did not always, nor in all

[111-040]cases, depend on the voluntary principle for their maintenance. The

[111-041]Irish Supply Bill contained an annual item before the Union of 7,700

[111-042]pounds for the Antrim Synod, and some other dissenting bodies. The

[111-043]_Regium Donum_ was not, indeed, general; but that it might be made so,

[111-044]was one of the inducements held out to many of that clergy to secure

[111-045]their countenance for the Legislative Union.

[111-046]The Established Church continued, of course, to monopolize University

[111-047]honours, and to enjoy its princely revenues and all political

[111-048]advantages. Trinity College continued annually to farm its 200,000

[111-049]acres at a rental averaging 100,000 pounds sterling. Its wealth, and

[111-050]the uses to which it is put, are thus described by a recent writer:

[111-051]"Some of Trinity's senior fellows enjoy higher incomes than Cabinet

[111-052]ministers; many of her tutors have revenues above those of cardinals;

[111-053]and junior fellows, of a few days' standing, frequently decline some of

[111-054]her thirty-one church livings with benefices which would shame the

[111-055]poverty of scores of continental, not to say Irish, Catholic

[111-056]archbishops. Even eminent judges hold her professorships; some of her

[111-057]chairs are vacated for the Episcopal bench only; and majors and field

[111-058]officers would acquire increased pay by being promoted to the rank of

[111-059]head porter, first menial, in Trinity College. Apart from her princely

[111-060]fellowships and professorships, her seventy Foundation, and sixteen

[111-061]non-Foundation Scholarships, her thirty Sizarships, and her fourteen

[111-062]valuable Studentships, she has at her disposal an aggregate, by

[111-063]bequests, benefactions, and various endowments, of 117 permanent

[111-064]exhibitions, amounting to upwards of 2,000 pounds per annum." The

[111-065]splendour of the highest Protestant dignitaries may be inferred from

[111-066]what has been said formerly of the Bishop of Derry, of the Era of

[111-067]Independence. The state maintained by the chief bishop—Primate

[111-068]Robinson, who ruled Armagh from 1765 to 1795—is thus described by Mr.

[111-069]Cumberland in his _Memoirs_. "I accompanied him," says Cumberland, "on

[111-070]Sunday forenoon to his cathedral. We went in his chariot of six horses

[111-071]attended by three footmen behind, whilst my wife and daughters, with

[111-072]Sir William Robinson, the primate's elder brother, followed in my

[111-073]father's coach, which he lent me for the journey. At our approach the

[111-074]great western door was thrown open, and my friend (in person one of the

[111-075]finest men that could be seen) entered, like another Archbishop Laud,

[111-076]in high prelatical state, preceded by his officers and ministers of the

[111-077]church, conducting him in files to the robing chamber, and back again

[111-078]to the throne. It may well be conceived with what invidious eyes the

[111-079]barely tolerated Papists of the city of Saint Patrick must have looked

[111-080]on all this pageantry, and their feelings were no doubt those in some

[111-081]degree of all their co-religionists throughout the kingdom."

[111-082]The Irish Establishment, during the reign of George III., numbered

[111-083]among its prelates and clergy many able and amiable men. At the period

[111-084]of the Union, the two most distinguished were Dr. O'Beirne, Bishop of

[111-085]Meath, an ex-priest, and Dr. Young, Bishop of Clonfert, a former fellow

[111-086]of Trinity College. As a Bible scholar, Dr. Young ranked deservedly

[111-087]high, but as a variously accomplished writer, Dr. O'Beirne was the

[111-088]first man of his order. His political papers, though occasionally

[111-089]disfigured with the bigotry natural to an apostate, are full of a

[111-090]vigorous sagacity; his contributions to general literature, such as his

[111-091]paper on _Tanistry_, in Vallency's _Collectanea_, show how much greater

[111-092]things still he was capable of. It is not a little striking that the

[111-093]most eminent bishop, as well as the most celebrated Anglican preacher

[111-094]of that age, in Ireland (Dean Kirwan), should both have been ordained

[111-095]as Catholic priests.

[111-096]The national literature which we have noted a century earlier, as

[111-097]changing gradually its tongue, was now mainly, indeed we might almost

[111-098]say solely, expressed in English. It is true the songs of "Carolan the

[111-099]Blind," were sung in Gaelic by the Longford firesides, where the author

[111-100]of "the Deserted Village" listened to their exquisite melody, moulding

[111-101]his young ear to a sense of harmony full as exquisite; but the glory of

[111-102]the Gaelic muse was past. He, too, unpromising as was his exterior, was

[111-103]to be one of the bright harbingers of another great era of

[111-104]Hiberno-English literature. When, within two generations, out of the

[111-105]same exceedingly restricted class of educated Irishmen and women, we

[111-106]count the names of Goldsmith, Samuel Madden, Arthur Murphy, Henry

[111-107]Brooke, Charles Macklin, Sheridan, Burke, Edmund Malone, Maria

[111-108]Edgeworth, Lady Morgan, "Psyche" Tighe, and Thomas Moore, it is

[111-109]impossible not to entertain a very high opinion of the mental resources

[111-110]of that population, if only they were fairly wrought and kindly valued

[111-111]by the world.

[111-112]One memorable incident of literary history—the Ossianic outbreak of

[111-113]1760—aided powerfully though indirectly in the revival of the study of

[111-114]the ancient Celtic history of Scotland and Ireland. Something was done

[111-115]then, by the Royal Irish Academy, to meet that storm of Anglo-Norman

[111-116]incredulity and indignation; much more has been done since, to place

[111-117]the original records of the Three Kingdoms on a sound critical basis.

[111-118]The dogmatism of the unbelievers in the existence of a genuine body of

[111-119]ancient Celtic literature has been rebuked; and the folly of the

[111-120]theorists who, upon imaginary grounds, constructed pretentious systems,

[111-121]has been exposed. The exact originals of MacPherson's odes have not

[111-122]been found, after a century of research, and may be given up, as

[111-123]non-existent; but the better opinion seems now to be, by those who have

[111-124]studied the fragments of undoubted antiquity attributed to the son of

[111-125]the warrior Fion, that whatever the modern translator may have

[111-126]invented, he certainly did not invent Ossian.

[111-128]To the stage, within the same range of time, Ireland gave some

[111-129]celebrated names: Quinn, Barry, Sheridan, Mrs. Woffington, Mrs. Jordan,

[111-130]and Miss O'Neill; and to painting, one pre-eminent name—the eccentric,

[111-131]honest, and original, James Barry.

[111-132]But of all the arts, that in which the Irish of the Georgian era won

[111-133]the highest and most various triumphs was the art of Oratory, What is

[111-134]now usually spoken of as "the Irish School of Eloquence," may be

[111-135]considered to have taken its rise from the growth of the Patriot party

[111-136]in Parliament, in the last years of George II. Every contemporary

[111-137]account agrees in placing its first great name—Anthony Malone—on the

[111-138]same level with Chatham and Mansfield. There were great men before

[111-139]Malone, as before Agamemnon; such as Sir Toby Butler, Baron Rice, and

[111-140]Patrick Darcy; but he was the first of our later succession of masters.

[111-141]After him came Flood and John Hely Hutchinson; then Grattan and Curran;

[111-142]then Plunkett and Bushe; then O'Connell and Shiel. In England, at the

[111-143]same time, Burke, Barre, Sheridan, and Sir Phillip Francis, upheld the

[111-144]reputation of Irish oratory; a reputation generously acknowledged by

[111-145]all parties, as it was illustrated in the ranks of all. The Tories,

[111-146]within our own recollection, applauded as heartily the Irish wit and

[111-147]fervour of Canning, Croker, and North, as the Whigs did the exhibition

[111-148]of similar qualities in their Emancipation allies.

[111-149]Nothing can be less correct, than to pronounce judgment on the Irish

[111-150]School, either of praise or blame, in sweeping general terms. Though a

[111-151]certain family resemblance may be traced among its great masters, no

[111-152]two of them will be found nearly alike. There are no echoes, no servile

[111-153]imitators, among them. In vigorous argumentation and severe simplicity,

[111-154]Plunkett resembled Flood, but the temperament of the two men—and

[111-155]Oratory is nearly as much a matter of temperament as of intellect—was

[111-156]widely different. Flood's movement was dramatic, while Plunkett's was

[111-157]mathematical. In structural arrangement, Shiel, occasionally—very

[111-158]occasionally—reminds us of Grattan; but if he has not the wonderful

[111-159]condensation of thought, neither has he the frequent antithetical

[111-160]abuses of that great orator. Burke and Sheridan are as distinguishable

[111-161]as any other two of their contemporaries; Curran stands alone;

[111-162]O'Connell never had a model, and never had an imitator who rose above

[111-163]mimicry. Every combination of powers, every description of excellence,

[111-164]and every variety of style and character, may be found among the

[111-165]masterpieces of this great school. Of their works many will live for

[111-166]ever. Most of Burke's, many of Grattan's, and one or two of Curran's

[111-167]have reached us in such preservation as promises immortality.

[111-168]Selections from Flood, Sheridan, Canning, Plunkett and O'Connell will

[111-169]survive; Shiel will be more fortunate for he was more artistic, and

[111-170]more watchful of his own fame. His exquisite finish will do, for him,

[111-171]what the higher efforts of men, more indifferent to the audience of

[111-172]posterity, will have forfeited for them.

[111-173]It is to be observed, farther, that the inspiration of all these men

[111-174]was drawn from the very hearts of the people among whom they grew. With

[111-175]one or two exceptions, sons of humble peasants, of actors, of at most

[111-176]middle class men, they were true, through every change of personal

[111-177]position, to the general interests of the people—to the common weal.

[111-178]From generous thoughts and a lofty scorn of falsehood, fanaticism and

[111-179]tyranny, they took their inspiration; and as they were true to human

[111-180]nature, so will mankind, through successive ages, dwell fondly on their

[111-181]works and guard lovingly their tombs.
