CHAPTER XIV.

[101-001]NEGOTIATIONS WITH FRANCE AND HOLLAND—THE THREE EXPEDITIONS NEGOTIATED

[101-002]BY TONE AND LEWINES.

[101-003]The close of the year 1795 saw France under the government of the

[101-004]Directory, with Carnot in the cabinet, and Pichegru, Jourdain, Moreau,

[101-005]Hoche, and Buonaparte at the head of its armies. This government, with

[101-006]some change of persons, lasted from October, 1795, to November, '99,

[101-007]when it was supplanted by the Consular Revolution. Within the compass

[101-008]of those four years lie the negotiations which were carried on and the

[101-009]three great expeditions which were fitted out by France and Holland, at

[101-010]the instance of the United Irishmen.

[101-011]On the 1st of February, 1796, Tone, who had sailed from Belfast the

[101-012]previous June, arrived at Havre from New York, possessed of a hundred

[101-013]guineas and some useful letters of introduction. One of these letters,

[101-014]written in cipher, was from the French Minister at Philadelphia to the

[101-015]Minister of Foreign Affairs, Charles Lacroix; another was to the

[101-016]American Minister in France, Mr. Monroe, afterwards President of the

[101-017]United States, by whom he was most kindly received, and wisely advised,

[101-018]on reaching Paris. Lacroix received him courteously, and referred him

[101-019]to a subordinate called Madgett, but after nearly three months wasted

[101-020]in interviews and explanations, Tone, by the advice of Monroe,

[101-021]presented himself at the Luxembourg Palace, and demanded audience of

[101-022]the "Organizer of Victory." Carnot also listened to him attentively,

[101-023]asked and obtained his true name, and gave him another _rendezvous_. He

[101-024]was next introduced to Clarke (afterwards Duc de Feltre), Secretary at

[101-025]War, the son of an Irishman, whom he found wholly ignorant of Ireland;

[101-026]and finally, on the 12th of July, General Hoche, in the most frank and

[101-027]winning manner, introduced himself. At first the Directory proposed

[101-028]sending to Ireland no more than 5,000 men, while Tone pleaded for

[101-029]20,000; but when Hoche accepted the command, he assured Tone he would

[101-030]go "in sufficient force." The "pacificator of La Vendee," as the young

[101-031]general was called—he was only thirty-two,—won at once the heart of the

[101-032]enthusiastic founder of the United Irishmen, and the latter seems to

[101-033]have made an equally favourable impression. He was at once presented

[101-034]with the commission of a _chef de brigade_ of infantry—a rank answering

[101-035]to that of colonel with us—and was placed as adjutant on the general's

[101-036]staff. Hoche was all ardour and anxiety; Carnot cheered him on by

[101-037]expressing his belief that it would be "a most brilliant operation;"

[101-038]and certainly Tone was not the man to damp such expectations, or allow

[101-039]them to evaporate in mere complimentary assurances.

[101-041]During the autumn months the expedition was busily being fitted out at

[101-042]Brest, and the general head-quarters were at Rennes. The Directory, to

[101-043]satisfy themselves that all was as represented by Tone, had sent an

[101-044]agent of their own to Ireland, by whom a meeting was arranged on the

[101-045]Swiss frontier between Lord Edward Fitzgerald, Arthur O'Conor, Dr.

[101-046]McNevin, and Hoche. From this meeting—the secret of which he kept to

[101-047]himself—the young general returned in the highest spirits, and was

[101-048]kinder than ever to his adjutant. At length, early in December, all was

[101-049]ready, and on the 16th the Brest fleet stood out to sea; 17 sail of the

[101-050]line, 13 frigates, and 13 smaller ships, carrying 15,000 picked troops,

[101-051]the _élite_ of "the Army of the Ocean," and abundance of artillery and

[101-052]munitions of war. Tone was in the _Indomptable_, 80 guns, commanded by

[101-053]a Canadian, named Bedout; Hoche and the Admiral in the frigate

[101-054]_Fraternité_; Grouchy, so memorable for the part he played then and

[101-055]afterwards, was second in command. On the third morning, after groping

[101-056]about and losing each other in Atlantic fog, one-half the fleet (with

[101-057]the fatal exception of the _Fraternité_) found themselves close in with

[101-058]the coast of Kerry. They entered Bantry Bay, and came to anchor, ten

[101-059]ships of war, and "a long line of dark hulls resting on the green

[101-060]water." Three or four days they lay dormant and idle, waiting for the

[101-061]General and Admiral; Bouvet, the Vice-Admiral, was opposed to moving in

[101-062]the absence of his chief; Grouchy was irresolute and nervous; but at

[101-063]length, on Christmas day, the council of war decided in favour of

[101-064]debarkation. The landing was to take place next morning; 6,500 veterans

[101-065]were prepared to step ashore at daylight, but without their artillery,

[101-066]their military chest, and their general. Two hours beyond midnight Tone

[101-067]was roused from sleep by the wind, which he found blowing half a gale.

[101-068]Pacing the gallery of the _Indomptable_ till day dawned, he felt it

[101-069]rising louder and angrier, every hour. The next day it was almost a

[101-070]hurricane, and the Vice-Admiral's frigate, running under the quarter of

[101-071]the great 80-gun ship, ordered them to slip anchor and stand out to

[101-072]sea. The whole fleet was soon driven off the Irish coast; that part of

[101-073]it, in which Grouchy and Tone were embarked, made its entrance into

[101-074]Brest on New Year's day; the ship which carried Hoche and the Admiral,

[101-075]only arrived at La Rochelle on the 15th. The Directory and the General,

[101-076]so far from being discouraged by this failure, consoled themselves by

[101-077]the demonstration they had made, of the possibility of a great fleet

[101-078]passing to and fro, in British waters, for nearly a month, without

[101-079]encountering a single British vessel of war. Not so the Irish

[101-080]negotiator; on him, light-hearted and daring as he was, the

[101-081]disappointment fell with crushing weight; but he magnanimously carried

[101-082]Grouchy's report to Paris, and did his utmost to defend the unlucky

[101-083]general from a cabal which had been formed against him.

[101-084]While Tone was reluctantly following his new chief to the Meuse and the

[101-085]Rhine—with a promise that the Irish expedition was delayed, not

[101-086]abandoned—another, and no less fortunate negotiator, was raising up a

[101-087]new ally for the same cause, in an unexpected quarter. The Batavian

[101-088]republic, which had risen in the steps of Pichegru's victorious army,

[101-089]in 1794, was now eager to imitate the example of France. With a

[101-090]powerful fleet, and an unemployed army, its chiefs were quite ready to

[101-091]listen to any proposal which would restore the maritime ascendancy of

[101-092]Holland, and bring back to the recollection of Europe the memory of the

[101-093]puissant Dutch republic. In this state of affairs, the new agent of the

[101-094]Irish Directory, Edward John Lewines, a Dublin attorney, a man of great

[101-095]ability and energy, addressed himself to the Batavian government. He

[101-096]had been sent abroad with very general powers, to treat with Holland,

[101-097]Spain, France, or any other government at war with England, for a loan

[101-098]of half a million sterling, and a sufficient auxiliary force to aid the

[101-099]insurrection. During two months' stay at Hamburg, the habitual route in

[101-100]those days from the British ports to the continent, he had placed

[101-101]himself in communication with the Spanish agent there, and had, in

[101-102]forty days, received an encouraging answer from Madrid. On his way,

[101-103]probably to Spain, to follow up that fair prospect, he reached the

[101-104]Netherlands, and rapidly discovering the state of feeling in the Dutch,

[101-105]or as it was then called, the _Batavian_ republic, he addressed himself

[101-106]to the Directors, who consulted Hoche, by whom in turn Tone was

[101-107]consulted. Tone had a high opinion of Lewines, and at once proceeded

[101-108]with him to the Hague, where they were joined, according to agreement,

[101-109]by Hoche. The Dutch Committee of Foreign Affairs, the

[101-110]Commander-in-Chief, General Dandaels, and the Admiral, De Winter,

[101-111]entered heartily into the project. There were in the Texel 16 ships of

[101-112]the line and 10 frigates, victualled for three months, with 15,000 men

[101-113]and 80 field guns on board. The only serious difficulty in the way was

[101-114]removed by the disinterestedness of Hoche; the French Foreign Minister

[101-115]having demanded that 5,000 French troops should be of the expedition,

[101-116]and that Hoche should command in chief; the latter, to conciliate

[101-117]Dandaels and the Dutch, undertook to withdraw the proposal, and

[101-118]gracefully yielded his own pretensions. All then was settled: Tone was

[101-119]to accompany Dandaels with the same rank he had in the Brest

[101-120]expedition, and Lewines to return, and remain, as "Minister-resident"

[101-121]at Paris. On the 8th of July, Tone was on board the flagship, the

[101-122]_Vryheid_, 74 guns, in the Texel, and "only waiting for a wind," to

[101-123]lead another navy to the aid of his compatriots.

[101-124]But the winds, "the only unsubsidized allies of England," were

[101-125]strangely adverse. A week, two, three, four, five, passed heavily away,

[101-126]without affording a single day in which that mighty fleet could make an

[101-127]offing. Sometimes for an hour or two it shifted to the desired point,

[101-128]the sails were unclewed and the anchors shortened, but then, as if to

[101-129]torture the impatient exiles on board, it veered back again and settled

[101-130]steadily in the fatal south-west. At length, at the end of August, the

[101-131]provisions being nearly consumed, and the weather still unfavourable,

[101-132]the Dutch Directory resolved to land the troops and postpone the

[101-133]expedition. De Winter, as is known, subsequently found an opportunity

[101-134]to work out, and attack Lord Duncan, by whom he was badly beaten. Thus

[101-135]ended Irish hopes of aid from Holland. The indomitable Tone rejoined

[101-136]his chief on the Rhine, where, to his infinite regret, Hoche died the

[101-137]following month—September 18th, 1797—of a rapid consumption,

[101-138]accelerated by cold and carelessness. "Hoche," said Napoleon to Barry

[101-139]O'Meara at Saint Helena, "was one of the first generals France ever

[101-140]produced. He was brave, intelligent, abounding in talent, decisive and

[101-141]penetrating. Had he landed in Ireland, he would have succeeded. He was

[101-142]accustomed to civil war, had pacified La Vendee, and was well adapted

[101-143]for Ireland. He had a fine, handsome figure, a good address, was

[101-144]prepossessing and intriguing." The loss of such a patron, who felt

[101-145]himself, according to Tone's account, especially bound to follow up the

[101-146]object of separating Ireland from England, was a calamity greater and

[101-147]more irreparable than the detention of one fleet or the dispersion of

[101-148]the other.

[101-149]The third expedition, in promoting which Tone and Lewines bore the

[101-150]principal part, was decided upon by the French Directory, immediately

[101-151]after the conclusion of peace with Austria, in October, 1797. The

[101-152]decree for the formation of "the Army of England," named Buonaparte

[101-153]Commander-in-Chief, with Desaix as his second. Buonaparte consulted

[101-154]Clarke as to who he most confided in among the numerous Irish refugees

[101-155]then in Paris—there were some twenty or thirty, all more or less known,

[101-156]and more or less in communication with the Directory—and Clarke

[101-157]answered at once, "Tone, of course." Tone, with Lewines, the one in a

[101-158]military, the other in an ambassadorial capacity, had frequent

[101-159]interviews with the young conqueror of Italy, whom they usually found

[101-160]silent and absorbed, always attentive, sometimes asking sudden

[101-161]questions betraying great want of knowledge of the British Islands, and

[101-162]occasionally, though rarely, breaking out into irresistible invectives

[101-163]against Jacobinism and the English system, both of which he so

[101-164]cordially detested. Every assurance was given by the General, by the

[101-165]Directors, by Merlin du Douai, Barras, and Talleyrand especially, that

[101-166]the expedition against England would never be abandoned. Tone, in high

[101-167]spirits as usual, joined the division under the command of his

[101-168]countryman, General Kilmaine, and took up his quarters at Havre, where

[101-169]he had landed without knowing a soul in France two years before.

[101-170]The winter wore away in busy preparations at Havre, at Brest, and at La

[101-171]Rochelle,—and, which seemed mysterious to the Irish exiles—at Toulon.

[101-172]All the resources of France, now without an enemy on the Continent,

[101-173]were put forth in these preparations. But it soon appeared they were

[101-174]not put forth for Ireland. On the 20th of May, 1798—within three days

[101-175]of the outbreak in Dublin, Wexford, and Kildare—Buonaparte sailed with

[101-176]the _elite_ of all that expedition for Alexandria, and "the Army of

[101-177]England" became, in reality, "the Army of Egypt."

[101-178]The bitterness, the despondency, and desperation which seized on the

[101-179]Irish leaders in France, and on the rank and file of the United

[101-180]Irishmen at home, on receiving this intelligence are sufficiently

[101-181]illustrated in the subsequent attempts under Humbert and Bompart, and

[101-182]the partial, ineffectual risings in Leinster, Ulster, and Connaught,

[101-183]during the summer and autumn of 1798. After all their high hopes from

[101-184]France and her allies, this was what it had come to at last! A few

[101-185]frigates, with three or four thousand men, were all that could be

[101-186]spared for the succour of a kingdom more populous than Egypt and Syria

[101-187]combined; the granary of England, and the key of her Atlantic position.

[101-188]It might have been some comfort to the family of Tone to have read,

[101-189]thirty years afterwards, in their American asylum, or for the aged

[101-190]Lewines to have read in the Parisian retreat in which he died, the

[101-191]memorable confession of Napoleon at Saint Helena: "If instead of the

[101-192]expedition to Egypt, I had undertaken that to Ireland, what," he asked,

[101-193]"could England do now? On such chances," he mournfully added, "depend

[101-194]the destinies of empires!"
