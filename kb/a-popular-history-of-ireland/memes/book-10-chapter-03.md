CHAPTER III.

[078-001]THE STATE OF RELIGION AND LEARNING IN IRELAND DURING THE SEVENTEENTH

[078-002]CENTURY.

[078-003]Before plunging into the troubled torrent of the revolution of 1688,

[078-004]let us cast a glance back on the century, and consider the state of

[078-005]learning and religion during those three generations.

[078-006]If we divide the Irish literature of this century by subjects, we shall

[078-007]find extant a respectable body, both in quantity and quality, of

[078-008]theology, history, law, politics, and poetry. If we divide it by the

[078-009]languages in which that literature was written, we may consider it as

[078-010]Latin, Gaelic, and English.

[078-011]I. Latin continued throughout Europe, even till this late day, the

[078-012]language of the learned, but especially of theologians, jurists, and

[078-013]historians. In Latin, the great tomes of O'Sullivan, Usher, Colgan,

[078-014]Wadding, and White, were written—volumes which remain as so many

[078-015]monuments of the learning and industry of that age. The chief objects

[078-016]of these illustrious writers were, to restore the ancient

[078-017]ecclesiastical history of Ireland, to rescue the memory of her saints

[078-018]and doctors from oblivion, and to introduce the native annals of the

[078-019]kingdom to the attention of Europe. Though Usher differed in religion,

[078-020]and in his theory of the early connection of the Irish with the Roman

[078-021]Church, from all the rest, yet he stands pre-eminent among them for

[078-022]labour and research. The Waterford Franciscan, Wadding, can only be

[078-023]named with him for inexhaustible patience, various learning, and

[078-024]untiring zeal. Both were honoured of princes and parliaments. The

[078-025]Confederates would have made Wadding a cardinal; King James made Usher

[078-026]an archbishop; one instructed the Westminster Assembly; the other was

[078-027]sent by the King of Spain to maintain the thesis of the Immaculate

[078-028]Conception at Rome, and subsequently was entrusted by the Pope to

[078-029]report upon the propositions of Jansenius. O'Sullivan, Conde de

[078-030]Berehaven, in Spain, and Peter White, have left us each two or three

[078-031]Latin volumes on the history of the country, highly prized by all

[078-032]subsequent writers. But the most indispensable of the legacies left us

[078-033]in this tongue, are Colgan's "Acta Sanctorum"—from January to March—and

[078-034]Dr. John Lynch's "Cambrensis Eversus." Many other works and authors

[078-035]might be mentioned, but these are the great Latinists to whom we are

[078-036]indebted for the most important services rendered to our national

[078-037]history.

[078-038]II. In the Gaelic literature of the country we count Geoffrey Keating,

[078-039]Duald McFirbis, and "the Four Masters" of Donegal. Few writers have

[078-040]been more rashly judged than Keating. A poet, as well as a historian,

[078-041]he gave a prominence in the early chapters of his history to bardic

[078-042]tales, which English critics have seized upon to damage his reputation

[078-043]for truthfulness and good sense. But these tales he gives as tales—as

[078-044]curious and illustrative—rather than as credible and unquestionable.

[078-045]The purity of his style is greatly extolled by Gaelic critics; and the

[078-046]interest of his narrative, even in a translation, is undoubted.

[078-047]McFirbis, an annalist and genealogist by inheritance, is known to us

[078-048]not only for his profound native lore, and tragic death, but also for

[078-049]the assistance he rendered Sir James Ware, Dr. Lynch, and Roderick

[078-050]O'Flaherty. The master-piece, however, of our Gaelic literature of this

[078-051]age, is the work now called "The Annals of the Four Masters." In the

[078-052]reign of James I., a few Franciscan friars, living partly in Donegal

[078-053]Abbey and partly in St. Anthony's College, at Louvain, undertook to

[078-054]collect and collate all the manuscript remains of Irish antiquity they

[078-055]could gather or borrow, or be allowed to copy. Father Hugh Ward was the

[078-056]head of this group, and by him the lay brother Michael O'Clery, one of

[078-057]the greatest benefactors his country ever saw, was sent from Belgium to

[078-058]Ireland. From 1620 to 1630, O'Clery travelled through the kingdom,

[078-059]buying or transcribing everything he could find relating to the lives

[078-060]of the Irish saints, which he sent to Louvain, where Ward and Colgan

[078-061]undertook to edit and illustrate them. Father Ward died in the early

[078-062]part of the undertaking, but Father Colgan spent twenty years in

[078-063]prosecuting the original design, so far as concerned our ecclesiastical

[078-064]biography.

[078-065]After collecting these materials, Father O'Clery waited, as he tells

[078-066]us, on "the noble Fergall O'Gara," one of the two knights elected to

[078-067]represent the county of Sligo in the Parliament of 1634, and perceiving

[078-068]the anxiety of O'Gara, "from the cloud which at present hangs over our

[078-069]ancient Milesian race," he proposed to collect the civil and military

[078-070]annals of Erin into one large digest. O'Gara, struck with this

[078-071]proposal, freely supplied the means, and O'Clery and his coadjutors set

[078-072]to work in the Franciscan Convent of Donegal, which still stood, not

[078-073]more than half in ruins.

[078-074]On the 22nd of January, 1632, they commenced this digest, and on the

[078-075]10th of August, 1636, it was finished—having occupied them four years,

[078-076]seven months and nineteen days. The MS., dedicated to O'Gara, is

[078-077]authenticated by the superiors of the convent; from that original two

[078-078]editions have recently been printed in both languages.

[078-079]These annals extend to the year 1616, the time of the compilers.

[078-080]Originally they bore the title of "Annals of the Kingdom of Ireland,"

[078-081]but Colgan having quoted them as "The Annals of the Four Masters," that

[078-082]name remains ever since. The "Four Masters" were Brother Michael

[078-083]O'Clery, Conary and Peregrine O'Clery, his brothers, both laymen and

[078-084]natives of Donegal, and Florence Conroy of Roscommon, another

[078-085]hereditary antiquary.

[078-086]The first edition of the New Testament, in the Gaelic tongue, so far as

[078-087]we are aware, appeared at Dublin, in 1603, in quarto. The translation

[078-088]was the work of a native scholar, O'Cionga (Anglicized King). It was

[078-089]made at the expense and under the supervision of Dr. William O'Donnell,

[078-090]one of the first fellows of Trinity, and published at the cost of the

[078-091]people of Connaught. Dr. O'Donnell, an amiable man, and an enemy of

[078-092]persecution, became subsequently Archbishop of Tuam, in which dignity

[078-093]he died, in 1628. A translation of the Book of Common Prayer, by

[078-094]O'Donnell, appeared early in the century, and towards its close (1685),

[078-095]a translation of the Old Testament, made for Bishop Bedell by the

[078-096]Gaelic scholars of Meath and Cavan, was published at the expense of the

[078-097]famous Robert Boyle. Bedell had also caused to be published Gaelic

[078-098]translations of certain homilies of Saint Leo and Saint John

[078-099]Chrysostom, on the importance of studying the holy Scriptures. The only

[078-100]other Gaelic publications of this period were issued from the Irish

[078-101]colleges at Louvain and Rome. Thence issued the devotional tracts of

[078-102]Conroy, of Gernon, and O'Molloy, and the Irish grammars of O'Clery and

[078-103]Stapleton. The devotional tracts, with their fanciful titles, of

[078-104]"Lamps," and "Mirrors," were smuggled across from Ostend and Dunkirk

[078-105]with other articles of contraband, and did much to keep alive the flame

[078-106]of faith and hope in the hearts of the Gaelic-speaking population.

[078-108]The bardic order also, though shorn of much of their ancient splendour,

[078-109]and under the Puritan _regime_ persecuted as vagrants, still flourished

[078-110]as an estate of the realm. The national tendency to poetic writing was

[078-111]not confined to the hereditary verse-makers, but was illustrated by

[078-112]such men as the martyred Plunkett, and the Bishops of Meath and

[078-113]Kerry—Dr. Thomas Dease, and Dr. John O'Connell. But the great body of

[078-114]Gaelic verse of the first half of this century is known under the name

[078-115]of "The Contentions of the Bards," the subject being the relative

[078-116]dignity, power, and prowess of the North and South. The gauntlet in

[078-117]this poetic warfare, was thrown down by McDaire, the Bard of Donogh

[078-118]O'Brien, fourth Earl of Thomond, and taken up on the part of Ulster by

[078-119]Lewy O'Clery. Reply led to rejoinder, and one epistle to another, until

[078-120]all the chief bards of the four provinces had taken sides. Half a dozen

[078-121]writers, _pro_ and _con_, were particularly distinguished; McDaire

[078-122]himself, Turlogh O'Brien, and Art Oge O'Keefe on behalf of the

[078-123]Southerners; O'Clery, O'Donnell, the two McEgans, and Robert McArthur

[078-124]on the side of the North.

[078-125]An immense mass of devotional Gaelic poetry may be traced to this

[078-126]period. The religious wars, the calamities of the church and of the

[078-127]people, inspired many a priest and layman to seize the harp of David,

[078-128]and pour forth his hopes and griefs in sacred song. The lament of Mac

[078-129]Ward over the Ulster princes buried at Rome, the odes of Dermod Conroy

[078-130]and Flan McNamee, in honour of our Blessed Lady, are of this class.

[078-131]Thus it happened that the bardic order, which in ancient times was the

[078-132]formidable enemy of Christianity, became, through adversity and

[078-133]affliction, its greatest supporter.

[078-134]III. Our Hiberno-English literature is almost entirely the creation of

[078-135]this century. Except some few remarkable state papers, we have no

[078-136]English writings of any reputation of an earlier period. Now, however,

[078-137]when the language of the empire, formed and enriched by the great minds

[078-138]of Elizabeth's era, began to extend its influence at home and abroad, a

[078-139]school of Hiberno-English writers appeared, both numerous and

[078-140]distinguished. This school was as yet composed mainly of two

[078-141]classes—the dramatic poets, and the pamphleteers. Of the latter were

[078-142]Bishop French, Sir Richard Nagle, Sir Richard Belling, Lord Orrery,

[078-143]Father Peter Walsh, and William Molyneux; of the former, Ludowick

[078-144]Barry, Sir John Denham, the Earl of Roscommon, and Richard

[078-145]Flecknoe,—the Mac Flecknoe of Dryden. It is true there appeared as yet

[078-146]no supreme name like Swift's; but as indicating the gradual extension

[078-147]of the English language into Ireland, the popular pamphlets and pieces

[078-148]written for the stage, are illustrations of our mental life not to be

[078-149]overlooked.

[078-150]Of the ancient schools of the island, after the final suppression of

[078-151]the college at Galway in 1652, not one remained. A diocesan college at

[078-152]Kilkenny, and the Dublin University, were alone open to the youth of

[078-153]the country. But the University remained exclusively in possession of

[078-154]the Protestant interest, nor did it give to the world during the

[078-155]century, except Usher, Ware and Orrery, any graduate of national, not

[078-156]to say, European reputation. In the bye-ways of the South and West, in

[078-157]the Irish colleges on the continent of Europe—at Paris, Louvain, Lisle,

[078-158]Salamanca, Lisbon, or Rome—the children of the proscribed majority

[078-159]could alone acquire a degree in learning, human or divine. It was as

[078-160]impossible two centuries ago, to speak of Trinity College with respect,

[078-161]as it is in our time, remembering all it has since done, to speak of it

[078-162]without veneration.

[078-163]Though the Established Church had now completed its century and a half

[078-164]of existence, it was as far from the hearts of the Irish as ever.

[078-165]Though the amiable Bedell and the learned O'Donnell had caused the

[078-166]sacred Scriptures to be translated into the Gaelic tongue, few converts

[078-167]had been made from the Catholic ranks, while the spirit of animosity

[078-168]was inflamed by a sense of the cruel and undeserved disabilities

[078-169]inflicted in the name of religion. The manifold sects introduced under

[078-170]Cromwell gave a keener edge to Catholic contempt for the doctrines of

[078-171]the reformation; and although the restoration of the monarchy threw the

[078-172]extreme sectaries into the shade, it added nothing to the influence of

[078-173]the church, except the fatal gift of political patronage. For the first

[078-174]time, the high dignity of Archbishop of Armagh began to be regarded as

[078-175]the inheritance of the leader of the House of Lords; then Brahmall and

[078-176]Boyle laid the foundation of that primatial power which Boulter and

[078-177]Stone upheld under another dynasty, but which vanished before the first

[078-178]dawn of Parliamentary independence.

[078-179]In the quarter of a century which elapsed from the restoration to the

[078-180]revolution, the condition of the Catholic clergy and laity was such as

[078-181]we have already described. In 1662, an historian of the Jesuit

[078-182]missionaries in Ireland described the sufferings of ecclesiastics as

[078-183]deplorable; they were forced to fly to the herds of cattle in remote

[078-184]places, to seek a refuge in barns and stables, or to sleep at night in

[078-185]the porticoes of temples, lest they should endanger the safety of the

[078-186]laity. In that same year, Orrery advised Ormond to purge the walled

[078-187]towns of Papists, who were still "three to one Protestant;" in 1672,

[078-188]Sir William Petty computed them at "eight to one" of the entire

[078-189]population.

[078-190]"So captive Israel multiplied in chains."

[078-191]The martyrdom of the Archbishop of Dublin, in 1680, and of the

[078-192]Archbishop of Armagh in 1681, were, however, the last of a series of

[078-193]executions for conscience' sake, from the relation of which the

[078-194]historian might well have been excused, if it was not necessary to

[078-195]remind our emancipated posterity at what a price they have been

[078-196]purchased.
