CHAPTER I.

[088-001]ACCESSION OF GEORGE I.—SWIFT'S LEADERSHIP.

[088-002]The last years of Queen Anne had been years of intrigue and preparation

[088-003]with the Jacobite leaders throughout the three kingdoms. At their head

[088-004]stood Ormond, the second and last _Duke_ of his name, and with him were

[088-005]associated at one stage or another of his design, Bolingbroke, Orrery,

[088-006]Bishop Atterbury, and other influential persons. It was thought that

[088-007]had this party acted promptly on the death of the Queen, and proclaimed

[088-008]James III. (or "the Pretender," as he was called by the partisans of

[088-009]the new dynasty), the Act of Succession might have remained a dead

[088-010]letter, and the Stuarts recovered their ancient sovereignty. But the

[088-011]partisans of the elector were the first in the field, and King George

[088-012]was accordingly proclaimed, on the 1st of August, at London, and on the

[088-013]6th of August, at Dublin.

[088-014]In Dublin, where serious apprehensions of a Jacobite rising were

[088-015]entertained, the proclamation was made by the glare of torches at the

[088-016]extraordinary hour of midnight. Two or three arrests of insignificant

[088-017]persons were made, and letters to Swift being found on one of them, the

[088-018]Dean was thought by his friends to be in some danger. But it was not

[088-019]correct to say, as many writers have done, that he found it necessary

[088-020]to retire from Dublin. The only inconvenience he suffered was from the

[088-021]hootings and revilings of the Protestant rabble in the street, and a

[088-022]brutal threat of personal violence from a young nobleman, upon whom he

[088-023]revenged himself in a characteristic petition to the House of Lords

[088-024]"for protection against the said lord." Pretending not to be quite sure

[088-025]of his assailant, he proceeds to explain: "Your petitioner is informed

[088-026]that the person who spoke the words above mentioned is of your

[088-027]Lordships' House, under the style and title of Lord Blaney; whom your

[088-028]petitioner remembers to have introduced to Mr. Secretary Addison, in

[088-029]the Earl of Wharton's government, and to have done him other good

[088-030]offices at that time, because he was represented as a young man of some

[088-031]hopes and a broken fortune." The entire document is a curious picture

[088-032]of the insolence of the ascendancy party of that day, even towards

[088-033]dignitaries of their own church who refused to go all lengths in the

[088-034]only politics they permitted or tolerated.

[088-035]It was while smarting under these public indignities, and excluded from

[088-036]the society of the highest class in his own country, with two or three

[088-037]exceptions, that Swift laid the foundations of his own and his

[088-038]country's patriotism, among the educated middle class of the Irish

[088-039]capital. From the college and the clergy he drew Dr. Sheridan—ancestor

[088-040]of six generations of men and women of genius! Doctors Delaney,

[088-041]Jackson, Helsham, Walmsley, Stopford (afterwards Bishop of Cloyne), and

[088-042]the three reverend brothers Grattan. In the city he selected as his

[088-043]friends and companions four other Grattans, one of whom was Lord-Mayor,

[088-044]another physician to the castle, one a schoolmaster, the other a

[088-045]merchant. "Do you know the Grattans?" he wrote to the Lord-Lieutenant,

[088-046]Lord Carteret; "then pray obtain their acquaintance. The Grattans, my

[088-047]lord, can raise 10,000 men." Among the class represented by this

[088-048]admirable family of seven brothers, and in that of the tradesmen

[088-049]immediately below them, of which we may take his printers, Waters and

[088-050]Faulkner for types, Swift's haughty and indignant denunciations of the

[088-051]oligarchy of the hour produced striking effects. The humblest of the

[088-052]community began to raise their heads, and to fix their eyes steadily on

[088-053]public affairs and public characters. Questions of currency, of trade,

[088-054]of the administration of justice and of patronage, were earnestly

[088-055]discussed in the press and in society, and thus by slow but gradually

[088-056]ascending steps, a spirit of independence was promoted where hitherto

[088-057]only servility had reigned.

[088-058]The obligations of his cotemporaries to Swift are not to be counted

[088-059]simply by what he was able to originate or to advocate in their

[088-060]behalf—for not much could be done in that way, in such times, and in

[088-061]such a position as his—but rather in regard to the enemies and

[088-062]maligners of that people, whom he exposed and punished. To understand

[088-063]the value of his example and inspiration, we must read over again his

[088-064]castigations of Wharton, of Burnet, of Boulter, of Whitshed, of Allan,

[088-065]and all the leaders of the oligarchy, in the Irish Parliament. When we

[088-066]have done so, we shall see at once how his imperial reputation, his

[088-067]personal position, and every faculty of his powerful mind were employed

[088-068]alike to combat injustice and proscription, to promote freedom of

[088-069]opinion and of trade, to punish the abuses of judicial power, and to

[088-070]cultivate and foster a spirit of self reliance and economy among all

[088-071]classes—especially the humblest. In his times, and in his position,

[088-072]with a cassock "entangling his course," what more could have been

[088-073]expected of him?

[088-074]The Irish Parliament met in 1715—elected, according to the then usage,

[088-075]for the lifetime of the King—commenced its career by an act of

[088-076]attainder against the Pretender, accompanied by a reward of 50,000

[088-077]pounds for his apprehension. The Lords-Justices, the Duke of Grafton

[088-078]and the Earl of Galway, recommended in their speech to the Houses, that

[088-079]they should cultivate such unanimity among themselves as "at once to

[088-080]put an end to all other distinctions in Ireland, but that of Protestant

[088-081]and Papist." In the same speech, and in all the debates of that reign,

[088-082]the Catholics were spoken of as "the common enemy," and all who

[088-083]sympathized with them, as "enemies of the constitution." But far as

[088-084]this Parliament was from all our ideas of what a national legislature

[088-085]ought to be, it was precisely at this period, when the administration

[088-086]could not be worse, that the foundation was laid of the great contest

[088-087]for legislative independence, which was to continue through three

[088-088]generations, and to constitute the main staple of the Irish history of

[088-089]this century.

[088-090]In the year 1717, the English House of Lords entertained and decided,

[088-091]as a court of last resort, an appeal from the Irish courts, already

[088-092]passed on by the Irish Lords, in the famous real-estate case of

[088-093]Annesley _versus_ Sherlock. The proceeding was novel, and was protested

[088-094]against in the English House at the time by the Duke of Leeds, and in

[088-095]the Irish, by the majority of the whole House. But the British

[088-096]Parliament, not content with claiming the power, proceeded to establish

[088-097]the principle, by the declaratory act—6th George I.—for securing the

[088-098]dependence of Ireland on the crown of Great Britain. This statute, even

[088-099]more objectionable than the law of Poynings, continued unrepealed till

[088-100]1782, notwithstanding all the arguments and all the protests of the

[088-101]Irish patriot party. The Lords of Ireland, unsupported by the bigoted

[088-102]and unprincipled oligarchy in the Commons, were shorn of their

[088-103]appellate jurisdiction, and their journals for many years contain few

[088-104]entries of business done, beyond servile addresses to successive

[088-105]Viceroys, and motions of adjournment.

[088-106]In their session of 1723, the ascendancy party in the Commons proceeded

[088-107]to their last extreme of violence against the prostrate Catholics. An

[088-108]act was introduced founded on eight resolutions, "further to prevent

[088-109]the growth of Popery." One of these resolutions, regularly transmitted

[088-110]to England by the Viceroy-proposed that every priest, arrested within

[088-111]the realm, should suffer the penalty of _castration_! For the first

[088-112]time, a penal law was rejected with horror and indignation by the

[088-113]English Privy Council, and the whole elaborate edifice, overweighted

[088-114]with these last propositions, trembled to its base. But though badly

[088-115]shaken, it was yet far from coming down.

[088-116]"Do not the corruptions and villainies of men," said Swift to his

[088-117]friend Delaney, "eat your flesh and exhaust your spirits?" They

[088-118]certainly gnawed at the heart of the courageous Dean, but at the same

[088-119]time, they excited rather than exhausted his spirits. In 1720 he

[088-120]resumed his pen, as a political writer, in his famous proposal "for the

[088-121]universal use of Irish manufactures." Waters, the printer of this

[088-122]piece, was indicted for a seditious libel, before Chief-Justice

[088-123]Whitshed, the immortal "_coram nobis_" of the Dean's political ballads.

[088-124]The jury were detained eleven hours, and sent out nine times, to compel

[088-125]them to agree on a verdict. They at length finally declared they could

[088-126]not agree, and a _nol. pros_. was soon after entered by the crown. This

[088-127]trial of Swift's printer in 1720, is the first of a long series of

[088-128]duels with the crown lawyers, which the Irish press has since

[088-129]maintained with as much firmness and self-sacrifice as any press ever

[088-130]exhibited. And it may be said that never, not even under martial law,

[088-131]was a conspicuous example of civic courage more necessary, or more

[088-132]dangerous. Browne, Bishop of Cork, had been in danger of deprivation

[088-133]for preaching a sermon against the well-known toast to the memory of

[088-134]King William; Swift was threatened, as we see, a few years earlier,

[088-135]with personal violence by a Whig lord, and pelted by a Protestant

[088-136]rabble, for his supposed Jacobitism; his friend, Dr. Sheridan, lost his

[088-137]Munster living for having accidentally chosen as his text, on the

[088-138]anniversary of King George's coronation, "sufficient for the day is the

[088-139]evil thereof." Such was the intolerance of the oligarchy towards their

[088-140]own clergy. What must it have been to others!

[088-142]The attempt to establish a National Bank, and the introduction of a

[088-143]debased copper coinage, for which a patent had been, granted to one

[088-144]William Wood, next employed the untiring pen of Swift. The halfpenny

[088-145]controversy, was not, as is often said, a small matter; it was nearly

[088-146]as important as the bank project itself. Of the 100,000 pounds worth

[088-147]coined, the intrinsic value was shown to be not more than 6,000 pounds.

[088-148]Such was the storm excited against the patentee, that his Dublin agents

[088-149]were obliged to resign their connection with him, and the royal

[088-150]letters-patent were unwillingly cancelled. The bank project was also

[088-151]rejected by Parliament, adding another to the triumphs of the

[088-152]invincible Dean.

[088-153]During the last years of this reign, Swift was the most powerful and

[088-154]popular person in Ireland, and perhaps in the empire. The freedom with

[088-155]which he advised Carteret the Viceroy, and remonstrated with Walpole,

[088-156]the Premier, on the misrule of his country, was worthy of the

[088-157]ascendancy of his genius. No man of letters, no churchman, no statesman

[088-158]of any country in any age, ever showed himself more thoroughly

[088-159]independent, in his intercourse with men of office, than Swift. The

[088-160]vice of Ireland was exactly the other way, so that in this respect

[088-161]also, the patriot was the liberator.

[088-162]Rising with the rise of public spirit, the great churchman, in his

[088-163]fourth letter, in the assumed character of _M. B. Drapier_, confronted

[088-164]the question of legislative independence. Alluding to the pamphlet of

[088-165]Molyneux, published thirty years before, he pronounced its arguments

[088-166]invincible, and the contrary system "the very definition of slavery."

[088-167]"The remedy," he concludes, addressing the Irish people, "is wholly in

[088-168]your own hands, and therefore I have digressed a little, in order to

[088-169]refresh and continue that spirit so seasonably raised among you, and to

[088-170]let you see, that, by the laws of God, of nature, of nations, and of

[088-171]your country, _you are, and ought to be, as free a people as your

[088-172]brethren in England_." For this letter also, the printer, Harding, was

[088-173]indicted, but the Dublin grand jury, infected with the spirit of the

[088-174]times, unanimously ignored the bill. A reward of 300 pounds was then

[088-175]issued from the castle for the discovery of the author, but no informer

[088-176]could be found base enough to betray him. For a time, however, to

[088-177]escape the ovations he despised, and the excitement which tried his

[088-178]health, Swift retired to his friend Sheridan's cottage on the banks of

[088-179]Lough Ramor, in Cavan, and there recreated himself with long rides

[088-180]about the country, and the composition of the Travels of the immortal

[088-181]Gulliver.

[088-182]Sir Robert Walpole, alarmed at the exhibition of popular intelligence

[088-183]and determination evoked by Swift, committed the government of Ireland

[088-184]to his rival, Lord Carteret—whom he was besides not sorry to remove to

[088-185]a distance—and appointed to the See of Armagh, which fell vacant about

[088-186]the time of the currency dispute, Dr. Hugh Boulter, Bishop of Bristol,

[088-187]one of his own creatures. This prelate, a politician by taste and

[088-188]inclination, modelled his policy on his patron's, as far as his more

[088-189]contracted sphere and inferior talents permitted. To buy members in

[088-190]market overt, with peerages, or secret service money, was his chief

[088-191]means of securing a Parliamentary majority. An Englishman by birth and

[088-192]education; the head of the Protestant establishment in Ireland, it was

[088-193]inevitable that his policy should be English and Protestant, in every

[088-194]particular. To resist, depress, disunite, and defeat the believers in

[088-195]the dangerous doctrines of Swift and Molyneux, was the sole rule of his

[088-196]nearly twenty years' political supremacy in Irish affairs. (1724-1742.)

[088-197]The master of a princely income, endowed with strong passions,

[088-198]unlimited patronage, and great activity, he may be said to have reigned

[088-199]rather than led, even when the nominal viceroyalty was in the hands of

[088-200]such able and accomplished men as Lords Carteret, Dorset and

[088-201]Devonshire. His failure in his first state trial, against Harding the

[088-202]printer, nothing discouraged him; he had come into Ireland to secure

[088-203]the English interest, by uprooting the last vestiges of Popery and

[088-204]independence, and he devoted himself to those objects with persevering

[088-205]determination. In 1727—the year of George the First's decease—he

[088-206]obtained the disfranchisement of Catholic electors by a clause quietly

[088-207]inserted without notice in a Bill regulating elections; and soon after

[088-208]he laid the foundations of those nurseries of proselytism, "the Charter

[088-209]Schools."
