CHAPTER IV.

[004-001]THE CONSTITUTION, AND HOW THE KINGS KEPT IT.

[004-002]We have fortunately still existing the main provisions of that

[004-003]constitution which was prepared under the auspices of Saint Patrick,

[004-004]and which, though not immediately, nor simultaneously, was in the end

[004-005]accepted by all Erin as its supreme law. It is contained in a volume

[004-006]called "the Book of Rights," and in its printed form (the Dublin

[004-007]bilingual edition of 1847), fills some 250 octavo pages. This book may

[004-008]be said to contain the original institutes of Erin under her Celtic

[004-009]Kings: "the Brehon laws," (which have likewise been published), bear

[004-010]the same relation to "the Book of Rights," as the Statutes at large of

[004-011]England, or the United States, bear to the English Constitution in the

[004-012]one case, or to the collective Federal and State Constitutions in the

[004-013]other. Let us endeavour to comprehend what this ancient Irish

[004-014]Constitution was like, and how the Kings received it, at first.

[004-015]There were, as we saw in the first chapter, beside the existing four

[004-016]Provinces, whose names are familiar to every one, a fifth principality

[004-017]of Meath. Each of the Provinces was subdivided into chieftainries, of

[004-018]which there were at least double or treble as many as there are now

[004-019]counties. The connection between the chief and his Prince, or the

[004-020]Prince and his monarch, was not of the nature of feudal obedience; for

[004-021]the fee-simple of the soil was never supposed to be vested in the

[004-022]sovereign, nor was the King considered to be the fountain of all

[004-023]honour. The Irish system blended the aristocratic and democratic

[004-024]elements more largely than the monarchical. Everything proceeded by

[004-025]election, but all the candidates should be of noble blood. The Chiefs,

[004-026]Princes, and Monarchs, so selected, were bound together by certain

[004-027]customs and tributes, originally invented by the genius of the Druids,

[004-028]and afterwards adopted and enforced by the authority of the Bishops.

[004-029]The tributes were paid in kind, and consisted of cattle, horses,

[004-030]foreign-born slaves, hounds, oxen, scarlet mantles, coats of mail,

[004-031]chess-boards and chess-men, drinking cups, and other portable articles

[004-032]of value. The quantity in every case due from a King to his

[004-033]subordinate, or from a subordinate to his King—for the gifts and grants

[004-034]were often reciprocal—is precisely stated in every instance. Besides

[004-035]these rights, this constitution defines the "prerogatives" of the five

[004-036]Kings on their journeys through each other's territory, their accession

[004-037]to power, or when present in the General Assemblies of the Kingdom. It

[004-038]contains, besides, a very numerous array of "prohibitions"—acts which

[004-039]neither the Ard-Righ nor any other Potentate may lawfully do. Most of

[004-040]these have reference to old local Pagan ceremonies in which the Kings

[004-041]once bore a leading part, but which were now strictly prohibited;

[004-042]others are of inter-Provincial significance, and others, again, are

[004-043]rules of personal conduct. Among the prohibitions of the monarch the

[004-044]first is, that the sun must never rise on him in his bed at Tara; among

[004-045]his prerogatives he was entitled to banquet on the first of August, on

[004-046]the fish of the Boyne, fruit from the Isle of Man, cresses from the

[004-047]Brosna river, venison from Naas, and to drink the water of the well of

[004-048]Talla: in other words, he was entitled to eat on that day, of the

[004-049]produce, whether of earth or water, of the remotest bounds, as well as

[004-050]of the very heart of his mensal domain. The King of Leinster was

[004-051]"prohibited" from upholding the Pagan ceremonies within his province,

[004-052]or to encamp for more than a week in certain districts; but he was

[004-053]"privileged" to feast on the fruits of Almain, to drink the ale of

[004-054]Cullen, and to preside over the games of Carman, (Wexford.) His

[004-055]colleague of Munster was "prohibited" from encamping a whole week at

[004-056]Killarney or on the Suir, and from mustering a martial host on the

[004-057]Leinster border at Gowran; he was "privileged" to pass the six weeks of

[004-058]Lent at Cashel (in free quarters), to use fire and force in compelling

[004-059]tribute from north Leinster; and to obtain a supply of cattle from

[004-060]Connaught, at the time "of the singing of the cuckoo." The Connaught

[004-061]King had five other singular "prohibitions" imposed on him—evidently

[004-062]with reference to some old Pagan rites—and his "prerogatives" were

[004-063]hostages from Galway, the monopoly of the chase in Mayo, free quarters

[004-064]in Murrisk, in the same neighbourhood, and to marshal his border-host

[004-065]at Athlone to confer with the tribes of Meath. The ruler of Ulster was

[004-066]also forbidden to indulge in such superstitious practices as observing

[004-067]omens of birds, or drinking of a certain fountain "between two

[004-068]darknesses;" his prerogatives were presiding at the games of Cooley,

[004-069]"with the assembly of the fleet;" the right of mustering his border

[004-070]army in the plains of Louth; free quarters in Armagh for three nights

[004-071]for his troops before setting out on an expedition; and to confine his

[004-072]hostages in Dunseverick, a strong fortress near the Giant's Causeway.

[004-073]Such were the principal checks imposed upon the individual caprice of

[004-074]Monarchs and Princes; the plain inference from all which is, that under

[004-075]the Constitution of Patrick, a Prince who clung to any remnant of

[004-076]ancient Paganism, might lawfully be refused those rents and dues which

[004-077]alone supported his dignity. In other words, disguised as it may be to

[004-078]us under ancient forms, "the Book of Rights" establishes Christianity

[004-079]as the law of the land. All national usages and customs, not

[004-080]conflicting with this supreme law, were recognized and sanctioned by

[004-081]it. The internal revenues in each particular Province were modelled

[004-082]upon the same general principle, with one memorable exception—the

[004-083]special tribute which Leinster paid to Munster—and which was the cause

[004-084]of more bloodshed than all other sources of domestic quarrel combined.

[004-085]The origin of this tax is surrounded with fable, but it appears to have

[004-086]arisen out of the reaction which took place, when Tuathal, "the

[004-087]Legitimate," was restored to the throne of his ancestors, after the

[004-088]successful revolt of the Belgic bondsmen. Leinster seems to have clung

[004-089]longest to the Belgic revolution, and to have submitted only after

[004-090]repeated defeats. Tuathal, therefore, imposed on that Province this

[004-091]heavy and degrading tax, compelling its Princes not only to render him

[004-092]and his successors immense herds of cattle, but also 150 male and

[004-093]female slaves, to do the menial offices about the palace of Tara. With

[004-094]a refinement of policy, as far-seeing as it was cruel, the proceeds of

[004-095]the tax were to be divided one-third to Ulster, one-third to Connaught,

[004-096]and the remainder between the Queen of the Monarch and the ruler of

[004-097]Munster. In this way all the other Provinces became interested in

[004-098]enforcing this invidious and oppressive enactment upon Leinster which,

[004-099]of course, was withheld whenever it could be refused with the smallest

[004-100]probability of success. Its resistance, and enforcement, especially by

[004-101]the kings of Munster, will be found a constant cause of civil war, even

[004-102]in Christian times.

[004-103]The sceptre of Ireland, from her conversion to the time of Brian, was

[004-104]almost solely in the hands of the northern Hy-Nial, the same family as

[004-105]the O'Neills. All the kings of the sixth and seventh centuries were of

[004-106]that line. In the eighth century (from 709 to 742), the southern

[004-107]annalists style Cathal, King of Munster, Ard-Righ; in the ninth century

[004-108](840 to 847), they give the same high title to Felim, King of Munster;

[004-109]and in the eleventh century Brian possessed that dignity for the twelve

[004-110]last years of his life, (1002 to 1014). With these exceptions, the

[004-111]northern Hy-Nial, and their co-relatives of Meath, called the southern

[004-112]Hy-Nial, seem to have retained the sceptre exclusively in their own

[004-113]hands, during the five first Christian centuries. Yet on every

[004-114]occasion, the ancient forms of election, (or procuring the adhesion of

[004-115]the Princes), had to be gone through. Perfect unanimity, however, was

[004-116]not required; a majority equal to two-thirds seems to have sufficed. If

[004-117]the candidate had the North in his favour, and one Province of the

[004-118]South, he was considered entitled to take possession of Tara; if he

[004-119]were a Southern, he should be seconded either by Connaught or Ulster,

[004-120]before he could lawfully possess himself of the supreme power. The

[004-121]benediction of the Archbishop of Armagh, seems to have been necessary

[004-122]to confirm the choice of the Provincials. The monarchs, like the petty

[004-123]kings, were crowned or "made" on the summit of some lofty mound

[004-124]prepared for that purpose; an hereditary officer, appointed to that

[004-125]duty, presented him with a white wand perfectly straight, as an emblem

[004-126]of the purity and uprightness which should guide all his decisions,

[004-127]and, clothed with his royal robes, the new ruler descended among his

[004-128]people, and solemnly swore to protect their rights and to administer

[004-129]equal justice to all. This was the civil ceremony; the solemn blessing

[004-130]took place in a church, and is supposed to be the oldest form of

[004-131]coronation service observed anywhere in Christendom.

[004-132]A ceremonial, not without dignity, regulated the gradations of honour,

[004-133]in the General Assemblies of Erin. The time of meeting was the great

[004-134]Pagan Feast of Samhain, the 1st of November. A feast of three days

[004-135]opened and closed the Assembly, and during its sittings, crimes of

[004-136]violence committed on those in attendance were punished with instant

[004-137]death. The monarch himself had no power to pardon any violator of this

[004-138]established law. The _Chiefs_ of territories sat, each in an appointed

[004-139]seat, under his own shield; the seats being arranged by order of the

[004-140]Ollamh, or Recorder, whose duty it was to preserve the muster-roll,

[004-141]containing the names of all the living nobles. The _Champions_, or

[004-142]leaders of military bands, occupied a secondary position, each sitting

[004-143]under his own shield. Females and spectators of an inferior rank were

[004-144]excluded; the Christian clergy naturally stepped into the empty places

[004-145]of the Druids, and were placed immediately next the monarch.

[004-146]We shall now briefly notice the principal acts of the first Christian

[004-147]kings, during the century immediately succeeding St. Patrick's death.

[004-148]Of OLLIOL, who succeeded Leary, we cannot say with certainty that he

[004-149]was a Christian. His successor, LEWY, son of Leary, we are expressly

[004-150]told was killed by lightning (A.D. 496), for "having violated the law

[004-151]of Patrick"—that is, probably, for having practised some of those Pagan

[004-152]rites forbidden to the monarchs by the revised constitution. His

[004-153]successor, MURKERTACH, son of Ere, was a professed Christian, though a

[004-154]bad one, since he died by the vengeance of a concubine named Sheen,

[004-155](that is, _storm_,) whom he had once put away at the instance of his

[004-156]spiritual adviser, but whom he had not the courage—though brave as a

[004-157]lion in battle—to keep away (A.D. 527). TUATHAL, "the Rough," succeeded

[004-158]and reigned for seven years, when he was assassinated by the tutor of

[004-159]DERMID, son of Kerbel, a rival whom he had driven into exile. DERMID

[004-160]immediately seized on the throne (A.D. 534), and for twenty eventful

[004-161]years bore sway over all Erin. He appears to have had quite as much of

[004-162]the old leaven of Paganism in his composition—at least in his youth and

[004-163]prime—as either Lewy or Leary. He kept Druids about his person,

[004-164]despised "the right of sanctuary" claimed by the Christian clergy, and

[004-165]observed, with all the ancient superstitious ceremonial, the national

[004-166]games at Tailteen. In his reign, the most remarkable event was the

[004-167]public curse pronounced on Tara, by a Saint whose sanctuary the

[004-168]reckless monarch had violated, in dragging a prisoner from the very

[004-169]horns of the altar, and putting him to death. For this offence—the

[004-170]crowning act of a series of aggressions on the immunities claimed by

[004-171]the clergy—the Saint, whose name was Ruadan, and the site of whose

[004-172]sanctuary is still known as Temple-Ruadan in Tipperary, proceeded to

[004-173]Tara, accompanied by his clergy, and, walking round the royal rath,

[004-174]solemnly excommunicated the monarch, and anathematized the place. The

[004-175]far-reaching consequences of this awful exercise of spiritual power are

[004-176]traceable for a thousand years through Irish history. No king after

[004-177]Dermid resided permanently upon the hill of Tara. Other royal houses

[004-178]there were in Meath—at Tailteen, at the hill of Usna, and on the margin

[004-179]of the beautiful Lough Ennell, near the present Castlepollard, and at

[004-180]one or other of these, after monarchs held occasional court; but those

[004-181]of the northern race made their habitual home in their own patrimony

[004-182]near Armagh, or on the celebrated hill of Aileach. The date of the

[004-183]malediction which left Tara desolate is the year of our Lord, 554. The

[004-184]end of this self-willed semi-Pagan (Dermid) was in unison with his

[004-185]life; he was slain in battle by Black Hugh, Prince of Ulster, two years

[004-186]after the desolation of Tara.

[004-188]Four kings, all fierce competitors for the succession, reigned and

[004-189]fell, within ten years of the death of Dermid, and then we come to the

[004-190]really interesting and important reign of Hugh the Second, which lasted

[004-191]twenty-seven years (A.D. 566 to 593), and was marked by the

[004-192]establishment of the Independence of the Scoto-Irish Colony in North

[004-193]Britain, and by other noteworthy events. But these twenty-seven years

[004-194]deserve a chapter to themselves.
