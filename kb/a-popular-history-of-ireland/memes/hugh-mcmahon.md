## Overview

Hugh McMahon was a colonel of the Irish forces and chieftain of Oriel (Monaghan) who played a critical role in the events leading up to the 1641 Rising. He was one of the northern leaders who joined [[Sir Phelim O'Neill]] in the conspiracy, and it was through his personal invitation that [[Owen Roe O'Neill]] became involved in the rebellion's planning.

## The Chieftain of Oriel

Hugh McMahon belonged to the MacMahon dynasty, the hereditary lords of Oriel, a territory centred on present-day County Monaghan. His succession to the chieftaincy was contested by the English administration in Dublin. The Lord Deputy, Sir William Fitzwilliam, exploited the situation for personal gain:

> [058-172] Hugh McMahon. Though he had engaged Fitzwilliam by a bribe of 600 cows to recognize his succession, he was seized by order of the Deputy. (058-172)

This act of treachery — accepting a bribe of 600 cows and then arresting the man who paid it — was characteristic of the corrupt Tudor administration in Ireland, which systematically undermined the traditional rights of Gaelic chieftains. The experience would have left McMahon with a deep distrust of English authority.

## The 1641 Conspiracy

By 1641, McMahon had emerged as one of the principal northern leaders of the planned Irish rising. Alongside Sir Phelim O'Neill of Kinnaird and Sir Con Magennis of Down, he formed part of the directing body of the Confederate movement in Ulster:

> [066-235] Colonel Hugh McMahon of Monaghan, and Dr. Heber McMahon, Administrator of Clogher. Sir Phelim O'Neill, the most considerable man of his name then in Ireland. (066-235)

In May 1641, a crucial development occurred. Captain Neil O'Neil arrived from the Netherlands with an urgent request from John, Earl of Tyrone (the exiled son of the great [[Hugh ONeill]]), calling upon his clansmen to rise. (066-254)

## The Invitation to Owen Roe O'Neill

McMahon's most consequential act was his invitation to Owen Roe O'Neill, the famed military commander then serving in the Spanish army in the Netherlands. It was Colonel Hugh McMahon who personally brought Owen Roe into the conspirators' confidence:

> [067-041] By Colonel Hugh McMahon to his lodgings, and there, on the evening of the 22nd, entrusted with a knowledge of next day's plans. (067-041)

This meeting, held the night before the rising was scheduled to commence, ensured that Owen Roe O'Neill — the nephew of the great Earl of Tyrone and a seasoned military veteran of the Spanish service — was fully informed of the rebels' intentions. Owen Roe would later return to Ireland to assume command of the Ulster army of the [[Confederate War]].

## Death and Legacy

Hugh McMahon was captured and executed shortly after the outbreak of the 1641 Rising. The seizure of the chieftain of Oriel removed one of the key organisers of the northern rebellion at a crucial moment. His brother, Dr. Heber McMahon, later became Bishop of Clogher and emerged as a significant leader of the Confederate Catholics in Ulster, commanding the northern army until his own capture and execution in 1650.

The McMahon family's involvement in the 1641 Rising was part of a broader pattern of Gaelic Ulster families — O'Neills, Magennises, O'Reillys, and others — seeking to recover their lost lands and rights after the [[Plantation of Ulster]]. Hugh McMahon's personal history of betrayal by the Dublin administration exemplified the grievances that drove the native Irish lords to rebellion.
