CHAPTER II.

[108-001]ADMINISTRATION OF LORD HARDWICKE (1801 TO 1806), AND OF THE DUKE OF

[108-002]BEDFORD (1806 TO 1808).

[108-003]During the five years in which Lord Hardwicke was Viceroy of Ireland,

[108-004]the _habeas corpus_ remained suspended, and the Insurrection Act

[108-005]continued in force. These were the years in which the power of Napoleon

[108-006]made the most astonishing strides; the years in which he remodelled the

[108-007]German Empire, placed on his head the iron crown of Lombardy, on his

[108-008]sister's that of Etruria, and on his brother's that of Holland; when

[108-009]the Consulate gave place to the Empire, and Dukedoms and Principalities

[108-010]were freely distributed among the marshals of the Grand Army. During

[108-011]all these years, Napoleon harassed England with menaces of invasion,

[108-012]and excited Ireland with corresponding hopes of intervention. The more

[108-013]far-seeing United Irishmen, however, had so little faith in these

[108-014]demonstrations that Emmet and McNevin emigrated to the United States,

[108-015]leaving behind them in the ranks of the French Army, those of their

[108-016]compatriots who, either from habit or preference, had become attached

[108-017]to a military life. It must however be borne in mind, for it is

[108-018]essential to the understanding of England's policy towards Ireland, in

[108-019]the first twelve or fourteen years after the Union, that the wild hope

[108-020]of a French invasion never forsook the hearts of a large portion of the

[108-021]Irish people, so long as Napoleon Buonaparte continued at the head of

[108-022]the government of France. During the whole of that period the British

[108-023]government were kept in constant apprehension for Ireland; under this

[108-024]feeling they kept up and increased the local militia; strengthened

[108-025]garrisons, and replenished magazines; constructed a chain of Martello

[108-026]towers round the entire coast, and maintained in full rigour the

[108-027]Insurrection Act. They refused, indeed, to the Munster magistrates in

[108-028]1803, and subsequently, the power of summary convictions which they

[108-029]possessed in '98; but they sent special Commissions of their own into

[108-030]the suspected counties, who sentenced to death with as little remorse

[108-031]as if they had been so many hydrophobic dogs. Ten, twelve, and even

[108-032]twenty capital executions was no uncommon result of a single sitting of

[108-033]one of those murderous commissions, over which Lord Norbury presided;

[108-034]but it must be added that there were other judges, who observed not

[108-035]only the decencies of everyday life, but who interpreted the law in

[108-036]mercy as well as in justice. They were a minority, it is true, but

[108-037]there were some such, nevertheless.

[108-038]The session of the Imperial Parliament of 1803-'4, was chiefly

[108-039]remarkable for its war speeches and war budget. In Ireland 50,000 men

[108-040]of the regular militia were under arms and under pay; 70,000 volunteers

[108-041]were enrolled, battalioned, and ready to be called out in case of

[108-042]emergency, to which it was proposed to add 25,000 sea-fencibles.

[108-043]General Fox, who it was alleged had neglected taking proper precaution

[108-044]at the time of Robert Emmet's _emeute_, was replaced by Lord Cathcart,

[108-045]as Commander-in-Chief. The _public_ reports at least of this officer,

[108-046]were highly laudatory of the discipline and conduct of the Irish

[108-047]militia.

[108-048]In May, 1804, Mr. Pitt returned to power, as Chancellor of the

[108-049]Exchequer and Prime Minister, when the whole Pitt policy towards

[108-050]Ireland, France, and America, was of course resumed; a policy which

[108-051]continued to be acted on during the short remainder of the life of its

[108-052]celebrated author.

[108-053]The year 1805 may be called the first year of the revival of public

[108-054]spirit and public opinion after the Union. In that year Grattan had

[108-055]allowed himself to be persuaded by Fox, into entering the Imperial

[108-056]Parliament, and his old friend Lord Fitzwilliam found a constituency

[108-057]for him, in his Yorkshire borough of Malton. About the same time, Pitt,

[108-058]or his colleagues, induced Plunkett to enter the same great assembly,

[108-059]providing him with a constituency at Midhurst, in Sussex. But they did

[108-060]not succeed—if they ever attempted—to match Plunkett with Grattan.

[108-061]Those great men were warm and close friends in the Imperial as they had

[108-062]been in the Irish Parliament; very dissimilar in their genius, they

[108-063]were both decided anti-Jacobins; both strenuous advocates of the

[108-064]Catholic claims, and both proud and fond of their original country.

[108-065]Grattan had more poetry, and Plunkett more science; but the heart of

[108-066]the man of colder exterior opened and swelled out, in one of the

[108-067]noblest tributes ever paid by one great orator to another, when

[108-068]Plunkett introduced in 1821, in the Imperial Parliament, his allusion

[108-069]to his illustrious friend, then recently deceased.

[108-070]Preparatory to the meeting of Parliament in 1805, the members of the

[108-071]old Catholic Committee, who had not met for any such purpose for

[108-072]several years, assembled in Dublin, and prepared a petition which they

[108-073]authorized their chairman, Lord Fingall, to place in such hands as he

[108-074]might choose, for presentation in both Houses. His lordship on reaching

[108-075]London waited on Mr. Pitt, and entreated him to take charge of the

[108-076]petition; but he found that the Prime Minister had promised the King

[108-077]one thing and the Catholics another, and, therefore, declined acceding

[108-078]to his request. He then gave the petition into the charge of Lord

[108-079]Grenville and Mr. Fox, and by them the subject was brought accordingly

[108-080]before the Lords and Commons. This debate in the Commons was remarkable

[108-081]in many respects, but most of all for Grattan's _debut_. A lively

[108-082]curiosity to hear one of whom so much had been said in his own country,

[108-083]pervaded the whole House, as Grattan rose. His grotesque little figure,

[108-084]his eccentric action, and his strangely cadenced sentences rather

[108-085]surprised than attracted attention, but as he warmed with the march of

[108-086]ideas, men of both parties warmed to the genial and enlarged

[108-087]philosophy, embodied in the interfused rhetoric and logic of the

[108-088]orator; Pitt was seen to beat time with his hand to every curiously

[108-089]proportioned period, and at length both sides of the House broke into

[108-090]hearty acknowledgments of the genius of the new member for Malton. But

[108-091]as yet their cheers were not followed by their votes; the division

[108-092]against going into Committee was 336 to 124.

[108-093]In sustaining Fox's motion, Sir John Cox Hippesley had suggested "the

[108-094]Veto" as a safeguard against the encroachments of Rome, which the Irish

[108-095]bishops would not be disposed to refuse. Archbishop Troy, and Dr.

[108-096]Moylan, Bishop of Cork, gave considerable praise to this speech, and

[108-097]partly at their request it was published in pamphlet form. This brought

[108-098]up directly a discussion among the Catholics, which lasted until 1810,

[108-099]was renewed in 1813, and not finally set at rest till the passage of

[108-100]the bill of 1829, without any such safeguard. Sir John C. Hippesley had

[108-101]modelled his proposal, he said, on the liberties of the Gallican

[108-102]Church. "Her privileges," he added, "depended on two prominent maxims:

[108-103]1st. That the Pope had no authority to order or interfere in anything

[108-104]in which the civil rights of the kingdom were concerned. 2nd. That

[108-105]notwithstanding the Pope's supremacy was acknowledged in cases purely

[108-106]spiritual, yet, in other respects, his power was limited by the decrees

[108-107]of the ancient councils of the realm." The Irish Church, therefore, was

[108-108]to be similarly administered, to obviate the objections of the

[108-109]opponents of complete civil emancipation.

[108-110]In February, 1806, on the death of Pitt, Mr. Fox came into power, with

[108-111]an uncertain majority and a powerful opposition. In April, the Duke of

[108-112]Bedford arrived, as Viceroy, at Dublin, and the Catholics presented,

[108-113]through Mr. Keogh, a mild address, expressive of their hopes that "the

[108-114]glorious development" of their emancipation would be reserved for the

[108-115]new government. The Duke returned an evasive answer in public, but

[108-116]privately, both at Dublin and London, the Catholics were assured that,

[108-117]as soon as the new Premier could convert the King—as soon as he was in

[108-118]a position to act—he would make their cause his own. No doubt Fox, who

[108-119]had great nobleness of soul, intended to do so; but on the 13th of

[108-120]September of the same year, he followed his great rival, Pitt, to the

[108-121]vaults of Westminster Abbey. A few months only had intervened between

[108-122]the death of the rivals.

[108-123]Lords Grey and Grenville, during the next recess, having formed a new

[108-124]administration, instructed their Irish Secretary, Mr. Elliot, to put

[108-125]himself in communication with the Catholics, in relation to a measure

[108-126]making them eligible to naval and military offices. The Catholics

[108-127]accepted this proposal with pleasure, but at the opening of the session

[108-128]of 1807, in a deputation to the Irish government, again urged the

[108-129]question of complete emancipation. The bill in relation to the army and

[108-130]navy had, originally, the King's acquiescence; but early in March,

[108-131]after it had passed the Commons, George III. changed his mind—if the

[108-132]expression may be used of him—at that time. He declared he had not

[108-133]considered it at first so important as he afterwards found it; he

[108-134]intimated that it could not receive his sanction; he went farther—he

[108-135]required a written pledge from Lords Grey and Grenville never again to

[108-136]bring forward such a measure, "nor ever to propose anything connected

[108-137]with the Catholic question." This unconstitutional pledge they refused

[108-138]to give, hurried the bill into law, and resigned. Mr. Spencer Perceval

[108-139]was then sent for, and what was called "the No-Popery Cabinet," in

[108-140]which Mr. Canning and Lord Castlereagh were the principal Secretaries

[108-141]of State, was formed. Thus, for the second time in six years, had the

[108-142]Catholic question made and unmade cabinets.

[108-143]The Catholics were a good deal dispirited in 1805, by the overwhelming

[108-144]majority by which their petition of that year was refused to be

[108-145]referred to a committee. In 1806, they contented themselves with simply

[108-146]addressing the Duke of Bedford, on his arrival at Dublin. In 1807, the

[108-147]"No-Popery Cabinet," by the result of the elections, was placed in

[108-148]possession of an immense majority—a fact which excluded all prospects

[108-149]of another change of government. But the Committee were too long

[108-150]accustomed to disappointments to despair even under these reverses.

[108-151]Early in the next session their petition was presented by Mr. Grattan

[108-152]in the Commons, and Lord Donoughmore in the Lords. The majority against

[108-153]going into committee was, in the Commons, 153; in the Lords, 87.

[108-154]Similar motions in the session of 1808, made by the same parties, were

[108-155]rejected by majorities somewhat reduced, and the question, on the

[108-156]whole, might be said to have recovered some of its former vantage

[108-157]ground, in despite of the bitter, pertinacious resistance of Mr.

[108-158]Perceval, in the one House, and the Duke of Portland, in the other.

[108-160]The short-lived administration of Mr. Fox, though it was said to

[108-161]include "all the talents," had been full of nothing but disappointment

[108-162]to his Irish supporters. The Duke of Bedford was, indeed, a great

[108-163]improvement on Lord Hardwicke, and Mr. Ponsonby on Lord Redesdale, as

[108-164]Chancellor, and the liberation of the political prisoners confined

[108-165]since 1803 did honour to the new administration. But there the measures

[108-166]of justice so credulously expected, both as to persons and interests,

[108-167]ended. Curran, whose professional claims to advancement were far beyond

[108-168]those of dozens of men who had been, during the past ten years, lifted

[108-169]over his head, was neglected, and very naturally dissatisfied; Grattan,

[108-170]never well adapted for a courtier, could not obtain even minor

[108-171]appointments for his oldest and staunchest adherents; while the

[108-172]Catholics found their Whig friends, now that they were in office, as

[108-173]anxious to exact the hard conditions of the Veto as Castlereagh

[108-174]himself.

[108-175]In truth, the Catholic body at this period, and for a few years

[108-176]subsequently, was deplorably disorganized. The young generation of

[108-177]Catholic lawyers who had grown up since the Relief Act of '93 threw the

[108-178]profession open to them, were men of another stamp from the old

[108-179]generation of Catholic merchants, who had grown up under the Relief Act

[108-180]of 1778. In the ten years before the Union, the Catholic middle class

[108-181]was headed by men of business; in the period we have now reached, their

[108-182]principal spokesmen came from "the Four Courts." John Keogh, the

[108-183]ablest, wisest and firmest of the former generation, was now passing

[108-184]into the decline of life, was frequently absent from the Committee, and

[108-185]when present, frequently overruled by younger and more ardent men. In

[108-186]1808, his absence, from illness, was regretted by Mr. O'Connell in an

[108-187]eloquent speech addressed to the Committee on the necessity of united

[108-188]action and incessant petitions. "Had he been present," said the young

[108-189]barrister, "his powers of reasoning would have frightened away the

[108-190]captious objections" to that course, "and the Catholics of Ireland

[108-191]would again have to thank their old and useful servant for the

[108-192]preservation of their honour and the support of their interests." It

[108-193]was a strange anomaly, and one which continued for some years longer,

[108-194]that the statesmen of the Catholic body should be all Protestants. A

[108-195]more generous or tolerant spirit than Grattan's never existed; a

[108-196]clearer or more fearless intellect than Plunkett's was not to be found;

[108-197]nobler and more disinterested friends than Ponsonby, Curran, Burroughs

[108-198]and Wallace, no people ever had; but still they were friends from

[108-199]without; men of another religion, or of no particular religion,

[108-200]advising and guiding an eminently religious people in their struggle

[108-201]for religious liberty. This could not always last; it was not natural,

[108-202]it was not desirable that it should last, though some years more were

[108-203]to pass away before Catholic Emancipation was to be accomplished by the

[108-204]union, the energy and the strategy of the Catholics themselves.
