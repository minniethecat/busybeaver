CHAPTER I.

[048-001]IRISH POLICY OF HENRY THE EIGHTH DURING THE LIFETIME OF CARDINAL

[048-002]WOLSEY.

[048-003]Henry the Eighth of England succeeded his father on the throne, early

[048-004]in the year 1509. He was in the eighteenth year of his age, when he

[048-005]thus found himself master of a well-filled treasury and an united

[048-006]kingdom. Fortune, as if to complete his felicity, had furnished him

[048-007]from the outset of his reign with a minister of unrivalled talent for

[048-008]public business. This was Thomas Wolsey, successively royal Chaplain,

[048-009]Almoner, Archbishop of York, Papal Legate, Lord Chancellor, and Lord

[048-010]Cardinal. From the fifth to the twentieth year of King Henry, he was,

[048-011]in effect, sovereign in the state, and it is wonderful to find how much

[048-012]time he contrived to borrow from the momentous foreign affairs of that

[048-013]eventful age for the obscurer intrigues of Irish politics.

[048-014]Wolsey kept before his mind, more prominently than any previous English

[048-015]statesman, the design of making his royal master as absolute in Ireland

[048-016]as any King in Christendom. He determined to abolish every pretence to

[048-017]sovereignty but that of the King of England, and to this end he

[048-018]resolved to circumscribe the power of the Anglo-Irish Barons, and to

[048-019]win over by "dulce ways" and "politic drifts," as he expressed it, the

[048-020]Milesian-Irish Chiefs. This policy, continued by all the Tudor

[048-021]sovereigns till the latter years of Elizabeth, so far as it

[048-022]distinguished between the Barons and Chiefs always favoured the latter.

[048-023]The Kildares and Desmonds were hunted to the death, in the same age,

[048-024]and by the same authority, which carefully fostered every symptom of

[048-025]adhesion or attachment on the part of the O'Neils and O'Briens. Neither

[048-026]were these last loved or trusted for their own sakes, but the natural

[048-027]enemy fares better in all histories than the unnatural rebel.

[048-028]We must enumerate some of the more remarkable instances of Wolsey's

[048-029]twofold policy of concession and intimidation. In the third and fourth

[048-030]years of Henry, Hugh O'Donnell, lord of Tyrconnell, passing through

[048-031]England, on a pilgrimage to Rome, was entertained with great honour at

[048-032]Windsor and Greenwich for four months each time. He returned to Ulster

[048-033]deeply impressed with the magnificence of the young monarch and the

[048-034]resources of his kingdom. During the remainder of his life he cherished

[048-035]a strong predilection for England; he dissuaded James IV. of Scotland

[048-036]from leading a liberating expedition to Ireland in 1513—previous to the

[048-037]ill-fated campaign which ended on Flodden field, and he steadily

[048-038]resisted the influx of the Islesmen into Down and Antrim. In 1521 we

[048-039]find him described by the Lord Lieutenant, Surrey, as being of all the

[048-040]Irish chiefs the best disposed "to fall into English order." He

[048-041]maintained a direct correspondence with Henry until his death, 1537,

[048-042]when the policy he had so materially assisted had progressed beyond the

[048-043]possibility of defeat. Simultaneously with O'Donnell's adhesion, the

[048-044]same views found favour with the powerful chief of Tyrone. The O'Neils

[048-045]were now divided into two great septs, those of Tyrone, whose seat was

[048-046]at Dungannon, and those of Clandeboy, whose strongholds studded the

[048-047]eastern shores of Lough Neagh. In the year 1480, Con O'Neil, lord of

[048-048]Tyrone, married his cousin-germain, Lady Alice Fitzgerald, daughter of

[048-049]the Earl of Kildare. This alliance tended to establish an intimacy

[048-050]between Maynooth and Dungannon, which subserved many of the ends of

[048-051]Wolsey's policy. Turlogh, Art, and Con, sons of Lady Alice, and

[048-052]successively chiefs of Tyrone, adhered to the fortunes of the Kildare

[048-053]family, who were, however unwillingly, controlled by the superior power

[048-054]of Henry. The Clandeboy O'Neils, on the contrary, regarded this

[048-055]alliance as nothing short of apostasy, and pursued the exactly opposite

[048-056]course, repudiating English and cultivating Scottish alliances. Open

[048-057]ruptures and frequent collisions took place between the estranged and

[048-058]exasperated kinsmen; in the sequel we will find how the last surviving

[048-059]son of Lady Alice became in his old age the first Earl of Tyrone, while

[048-060]the House of Clandeboy took up the title of "the O'Neil." The example

[048-061]of the elder branch of this ancient royal race, and of the hardly less

[048-062]illustrious family of Tyrconnell, exercised a potent influence on the

[048-063]other chieftains of Ulster.

[048-064]An elaborate report on "the State of Ireland," with "a plan for its

[048-065]Reformation"—submitted to Henry in the year 1515—gives us a tolerably

[048-066]clear view of the political and military condition of the several

[048-067]provinces. The only portions of the country in any sense subject to

[048-068]English law, were half the counties of Louth, Meath, Dublin, Kildare,

[048-069]and Wexford. The residents within these districts paid "black rent" to

[048-070]the nearest native chiefs. Sheriffs were not permitted to execute

[048-071]writs, beyond the bounds thus described, and even within thirty miles

[048-072]of Dublin, March-law and Brehon-law were in full force. Ten native

[048-073]magnates are enumerated in Leinster as "chief captains" of their

[048-074]"nations"—not one of whom regarded the English King as his Sovereign.

[048-075]Twenty chiefs in Munster, fifteen in Connaught, and three in

[048-076]West-Meath, maintained their ancient state, administered their own

[048-077]laws, and recognized no superiority, except in one another, as policy

[048-078]or custom compelled them. Thirty chief English captains, of whom

[048-079]eighteen resided in Munster, seven in Connaught, and the remainder in

[048-080]Meath, Down, and Antrim, are set down as "rebels" and followers of "the

[048-081]Irish order." Of these, the principal in the midland counties were the

[048-082]Dillons and Tyrrells, in the West the Burkes and Berminghams, in the

[048-083]South the Powers, Barrys, Roches—the Earl of Desmond and his relatives.

[048-084]The enormous growth of these Munster Geraldines, and their not less

[048-085]insatiable greed, produced many strange complications in the politics

[048-086]of the South. Not content with the moiety of Kerry, Cork, and

[048-087]Waterford, they had planted their landless cadets along the Suir and

[048-088]the Shannon, in Ormond and Thomond. They narrowed the dominions of the

[048-089]O'Briens on the one hand and the McCarthys on the other. Concluding

[048-090]peace or war with their neighbours, as suited their own convenience,

[048-091]they sometimes condescended to accept further feudal privileges from

[048-092]the Kings of England. To Maurice, tenth Earl, Henry VII. had granted

[048-093]"all the customs, cockets, poundage, prize wines of Limerick, Cork,

[048-094]Kinsale, Baltimore and Youghal, with other privileges and advantages."

[048-095]Yet Earl James, in the next reign, did not hesitate to treat with

[048-096]Francis of France and the Emperor of Germany, as an independent Prince,

[048-097]long before the pretence of resisting the Reformation could be alleged

[048-098]in his justification. What we have here to observe is, that this

[048-099]predominance of the Munster Geraldines drove first one and then another

[048-100]branch of the McCarthys, and O'Briens, into the meshes of Wolsey's

[048-101]policy. Cormac Oge, lord of Muskerry, and his cousin, the lord of

[048-102]Carbery, defeated the eleventh Earl (James), at Moore Abbey, in 1521,

[048-103]with a loss of 1,500 foot and 500 or 600 horsemen. To strengthen

[048-104]himself against the powerful adversary so deeply wounded, Cormac sought

[048-105]the protection of the Lord Lieutenant, the Earl of Surrey, and of

[048-106]Pierce Roe, the eighth Earl of Ormond, who had common wrongs to avenge.

[048-107]In this way McCarthy became identified with the English interest, which

[048-108]he steadily adhered to till his death—in 1536. Driven by the same

[048-109]necessity to adopt the same expedient, Murrogh O'Brien, lord of

[048-110]Thomond, a few years later visited Henry at London, where he resigned

[048-111]his principality, received back his lands, under a royal patent

[048-112]conveying them to him as "Earl of Thomond, and Baron of Inchiquin."

[048-113]Henry was but too happy to have raised up such a counterpoise to the

[048-114]power of Desmond, at his own door, while O'Brien was equally anxious to

[048-115]secure foreign aid against such intolerable encroachments. The policy

[048-116]worked effectually; it brought the succeeding Earl of Desmond to

[048-117]London, an humble suitor for the King's mercy and favour, which were

[048-118]after some demur granted.

[048-119]The event, however, which most directly tended to the establishment of

[048-120]an English royalty in Ireland, was the depression of the family of

[048-121]Kildare in the beginning of this reign, and its all but extinction a

[048-122]few years later. Gerald, the ninth Earl of that title, succeeded his

[048-123]father in the office of Lord Deputy in the first years of Henry. He had

[048-124]been a ward at the court of the preceding King, and by both his first

[048-125]and second marriages was closely connected with the royal family. Yet

[048-126]he stood in the way of the settled plans of Wolsey, before whom the

[048-127]highest heads in the realm trembled. His father, as if to secure him

[048-128]against the hereditary enmity of the Butlers, had married his daughter

[048-129]Margaret to Pierce Roe, Earl of Ossory, afterwards eighth Earl of

[048-130]Ormond—the restorer of that house. This lady, however, entered heartily

[048-131]into the antipathies of her husband's family, and being of masculine

[048-132]spirit, with an uncommon genius for public affairs, helped more than

[048-133]any Butler had ever done to humble the overshadowing house of which she

[048-134]was born. The weight of Wolsey's influence was constantly exercised in

[048-135]favour of Ormond, who had the skill to recommend himself quite as

[048-136]effectually to Secretary Cromwell, after the Cardinal's disgrace and

[048-137]death. But the struggles of the house of Kildare were bold and

[048-138]desperate.
