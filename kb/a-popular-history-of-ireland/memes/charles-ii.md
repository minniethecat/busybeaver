## Overview

Charles II (1630–1685) was King of England, Scotland, and Ireland from the [[restoration|Restoration]] of the monarchy in 1660 until his death. The son of the executed Charles I, he spent nine years in exile before being invited back to the throne following the collapse of the Cromwellian Commonwealth. (072-189) (074-056)

His reign was of profound significance for Ireland. The [[restoration|Restoration]] settlement under Charles II saw the partial reversal of the Cromwellian land confiscations, the re-establishment of the Protestant Church of Ireland, and the beginnings of the Catholic question that would dominate Irish politics for the next two centuries. (076-001) (077-001)

## Biography

### Exile and Restoration

After the execution of his father Charles I in 1649, the young prince fled to the Continent. At the French court, he gathered a circle of loyal followers and waited for an opportunity to reclaim his throne. In Ireland, the Catholic Confederates proclaimed his title, and at Cork and Youghal the Royalist forces rallied to his name. (071-059) (072-189)

The Cromwellian conquest of Ireland crushed these hopes, and Charles was driven into nine years of exile from which he only emerged after the death of [[Oliver Cromwell]] and the collapse of the Protectorate. (074-056)

### The Restoration

Charles II landed at Dover on the 23rd of May, 1660, and was carried in triumph to London. The [[restoration|Restoration]] was greeted with widespread relief after the turmoil of the Civil Wars and the rigid Puritanism of the Commonwealth. In Ireland, the [[restoration|Restoration]] raised expectations among both Protestants and Catholics for a settlement of the land question. (075-152)

### Ireland Under Charles II

The years of Charles II's reign saw the consolidation of Protestant power in Ireland, though the King's own sympathies leaned towards religious toleration. His brother and heir, James, Duke of York, was openly Catholic, and the Catholic interest grew increasingly assertive during the last two or three years of Charles II's reign. (077-102) (085-029)

The Duke of Ormond, as Lord Lieutenant, governed Ireland for much of Charles's reign, navigating between the demands of the Protestant interest and the King's inclination toward leniency for his Catholic subjects. The Act of Settlement, passed in the [[irish-parliament|Irish Parliament]], attempted to reconcile the claims of dispossessed Catholics with the realities of Protestant landholding. (085-016)

### Political Balance

The Catholic nobility, so powerful under Cromwell and Charles II, backed as they once again were by royal favour, pressed for a fuller restoration of their rights. Yet the Protestant interest, entrenched in the [[irish-parliament|Irish Parliament]] and the [[dublin|Dublin]] administration, resisted any significant alteration of the land settlement. This tension between royal policy and local Protestant power became a defining feature of the [[restoration|Restoration]] era in Ireland. (085-016) (085-029)

## Legacy

Charles II's reign established the framework for the [[protestant-ascendancy|Protestant Ascendancy]] that would govern Ireland through the eighteenth century. His brother and successor, [[James II]], pursued a more aggressively pro-Catholic policy, leading ultimately to the [[Williamite War]] and the final defeat of the Stuart cause in Ireland. (089-034)

The Test Act passed under Charles II, which required office-holders to take the sacrament according to the rites of the Church of Ireland, remained a cornerstone of Protestant political supremacy until its eventual repeal under [[William III]]. (089-034)
