## Overview

Sir Neil O'Neil was an Irish Jacobite officer who commanded Irish forces at the Battle of the Boyne in 1690. He fell mortally wounded at the ford of Oldbridge while leading his troops against the Williamite army under the Duke of Schomberg, in one of the most celebrated episodes of the [[Williamite War]].

The name Neil O'Neil recurs across several centuries of Irish history. An earlier Neil O'Neil was active in the 14th century, capturing Carrickfergus and making Downpatrick and Dundalk the northern barriers of Gaelic power. (040-139) Another served as a captain in the Irish regiments in the Spanish Netherlands in 1641, arriving with an urgent message from the exiled Earl of Tyrone. (066-254)

The most notable bearer of the name, however, was Sir Neil O'Neil, the Jacobite commander who gave his life for King [[James II]] at the Boyne.

## Earlier Neil O'Neils

### The Medieval Commander

An earlier Neil O'Neil was active in the decades following the Bruce Invasion of Ireland (1315–1318). He was a Gaelic commander who expanded O'Neill power eastward:

> [040-139] Towards the north Carrickfergus continued the outwork till captured by Neil O'Neil, when Downpatrick and Dundalk became the northern barriers. (040-139)

> [040-141] The latter town, which seems to have been strengthened after Bruce's defeat, was repeatedly attacked by Neil O'Neil, and at last entered into conditions, by which it procured his protection. (040-141–040-142)

This campaign extended O'Neill influence into the eastern counties that had been under Anglo-Norman control for over a century.

### Captain Neil O'Neil (1641)

> [066-254] In May, 1641, Captain Neil O'Neil arrived from the Netherlands with an urgent request from John, Earl of Tyrone, to all his clansmen. (066-254)

This Captain Neil O'Neil served as a messenger between the exiled O'Neill earls on the Continent and their clansmen in Ulster, playing a role in the preparations for the 1641 Rising that led to the [[Confederate War]].

## The Battle of the Boyne (1690)

Sir Neil O'Neil's place in Irish history was secured at the Battle of the Boyne on 1 July 1690. He commanded the Irish Jacobite forces stationed at the ford of Oldbridge, one of the principal crossing points on the River Boyne. The ford was defended against the vanguard of the Williamite army under the Duke of Schomberg, [[William III]]'s most experienced general:

> [082-078] The battle commenced at the ford of Oldbridge, between Sir Neil O'Neil, and the younger Schomberg; O'Neil fell mortally wounded, and the ford was carried by the Williamite troops. (082-078–082-079)

Sir Neil O'Neil died of his wounds shortly after the battle, joining the Duke of Schomberg himself among the notable casualties of that day. His death at the Boyne — fighting at the ford against overwhelming odds — made him one of the most remembered Irish commanders of the Williamite War.

## Legacy

Sir Neil O'Neil's death at the Boyne epitomised the sacrifice of the Irish Jacobite army in a war that determined the political and religious destiny of Ireland for over a century. The Battle of the Boyne, despite being a tactical reverse rather than a catastrophic defeat for the Jacobites, became the symbolic watershed of Irish history, celebrated by the victorious Williamite interest and mourned by the defeated Jacobites.

The recurrence of the name Neil O'Neil across four centuries — from the medieval expansion of O'Neill power, through the 1641 conspiracy, to the final Jacobite stand at the Boyne — reflects the enduring role of the O'Neill dynasty in Irish military and political affairs from the 14th century to the end of the Gaelic aristocracy.
