## Overview

Father Arthur O'Leary (1729–1802) was an Irish Capuchin friar and controversialist whose facile pen was employed in defence of both Catholic loyalty to the Crown and, later, in the cause of Volunteer reform. He was one of the most prominent Catholic writers and public figures in late 18th-century Ireland. (093-102)

His career bridged the gap between the conservative Catholic hierarchy and the reforming spirit of the Volunteer movement. He was received with military honours at the Volunteer Convention yet remained a steadfast opponent of the secret societies that disturbed the peace of the countryside. (093-102) (096-231)

## Early Life and Religious Vocation

Arthur O'Leary was born in County Cork in 1729 and educated on the Continent, where he joined the Capuchin order. The Capuchins, a branch of the Franciscans, were known for their missionary zeal and their closeness to the common people. O'Leary returned to Ireland and took up pastoral work during the era of the Penal Laws. (021-037)

The O'Learys were one of the chief families of Ithian origin in Munster, along with the O'Driscolls, Coffeys, and Clancys. This ancient lineage gave O'Leary a natural standing among the Catholic gentry and peasantry of the south. (021-037)

## Opposition to the Whiteboys

From the first, the Catholic bishops and clergy strenuously opposed the secret agrarian societies that emerged in Munster in the 1760s. Father Arthur O'Leary employed his facile pen against them, writing pamphlets denouncing the Whiteboys and their midnight legislation. (093-102)

> The Bishop of Cloyne issued a reprobatory pastoral; Father Arthur O'Leary employed his facile pen against them; the Bishop of Ossory anathematized them in his diocese. [093-102]

Priests in Kildare, Kilkenny, and Munster were often in personal danger from these midnight legislators. Their chapels had been frequently nailed up, and bishops had been often obliged to remove clergy from one neighbourhood to another to prevent worse consequences. O'Leary's writings against the Whiteboys placed him firmly on the side of law and order. (093-104) (093-106)

## The Volunteer Movement and Political Writing

When the Volunteer movement emerged in the late 1770s, O'Leary joined the press campaign that accompanied the political mobilization. He was one of the chief workers in this department of patriotic duty, alongside Dr. Jebb, Mr. Johnson, Mr. Pollock, and Mr. Charles Sheridan. (094-124) (094-125)

> Dr. Jebb, Mr. (afterwards Judge) Johnson, Mr. Pollock, Mr. Charles Sheridan, Father Arthur O'Leary, and Mr. Dobbs, M.P., were the chief workers in this department of patriotic duty. [094-125]

His writings helped to cheer, instruct, and restrain within due bounds the independent companies as they proceeded with their organization. O'Leary represented the respectable Catholic voice that sought reform through constitutional means rather than revolutionary action. (094-127) (094-129)

## The Volunteer Convention and Catholic Exclusion

In 1783, the Volunteer Convention assembled in Dublin to debate parliamentary reform. The Convention received Father Arthur O'Leary with military honours, a significant gesture of respect toward a Catholic priest from a predominantly Protestant assembly. (096-231)

> The Convention had received Father Arthur O'Leary with military honours, and contained many warm friends of Catholic rights, the majority were still intolerant of religious freedom. [096-231]

However, despite O'Leary's presence and the many warm friends of Catholic rights within the Convention, the majority — including Flood and Charlemont — remained intolerant of religious freedom. The reform programme was an excellent Protestant reform bill that stopped short of Catholic emancipation. (096-230) (096-233)

## Later Years and Literary Legacy

O'Leary continued his literary and pastoral work into the 1790s. He opposed the United Irishmen as he had opposed the Whiteboys, maintaining his consistent position against secret societies and revolutionary methods. (093-102)

His nephew, Colonel O'Leary, became Bolivar's first aide-de-camp in the wars of South American independence — a dramatic illustration of the O'Leary family's tradition of engagement with larger causes beyond Ireland. (112-111) (112-112)

## Legacy

Father Arthur O'Leary represents a distinctive strain in Irish Catholic history: the loyalist reformer who sought to advance Catholic rights through constitutional means and Protestant alliance. His facile pen served the cause of orderly reform at a time when more radical voices increasingly set the terms of political debate. (093-102)

The respect shown to him by the Protestant Volunteers demonstrated the potential for inter-confessional cooperation that briefly flourished before the polarizations of the 1790s. He is remembered as one of the most articulate Catholic voices of the Volunteer era. (094-125)
