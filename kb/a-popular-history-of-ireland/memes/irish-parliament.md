The **Irish Parliament** was the legislative assembly of the [[Ireland|Kingdom of Ireland]], originating in the 13th century as a representative body of the Anglo-Norman colony and existing until its dissolution under the [[Act of Union 1800]]. For most of its history, the Parliament was dominated by the Protestant interest, and its enactments—most notoriously the [[Penal Laws]]—shaped the political, religious, and social order of early modern Ireland. The Parliament met at various locations before settling permanently at College Green in [[Dublin]], in a building now housing the Bank of Ireland.

## Origins and Medieval Development

The Irish Parliament emerged from the feudal councils of the Anglo-Norman lordship, modeled on the English Parliament. Early sessions were convened to raise subsidies for the Crown and to provide a forum for the grievances of the colonial community. Attendance was originally restricted to those of English descent, and native Irish were explicitly excluded:

> "An Anglo-Irish Parliament having been called, the first attended by representatives of the counties and towns." (041-166)

> "The Irish Parliament passed a statute against absentees, requiring all holding lands in Ireland to reside there." (044-183)

## The Parliament under the Tudors and Stuarts

Under the Tudor monarchy, the Irish Parliament became an instrument of royal policy. [[Henry VIII]]'s proclamation as King of Ireland (1541) was enacted by the Parliament, and subsequent sessions ratified the [[Surrender and Regrant]] policy and the [[Reformation]] settlements. The Stuart period saw the Parliament increasingly aligned with the Protestant interest:

> "No Irish Parliament was convened during his reign, which he governed through executive authority alone." (052-005)

> "The minority in the Irish Parliament of James I deserve to be held in honoured remembrance." (064-357)

[[james-i|James I]]'s only Irish Parliament (1613–1615) was the first in which Protestant representatives commanded a decisive majority, achieved through the creation of new parliamentary boroughs in plantation areas.

## The Eighteenth-Century Parliament

The 18th century saw the Irish Parliament achieve a measure of legislative independence, particularly after 1782 when the Declaratory Act was repealed. [[Henry Grattan]]'s Parliament (1782–1800) was the high point of Irish parliamentary autonomy, though it remained an exclusively Protestant body:

> "The Irish Parliament having sent over to England a Bill for the security of the subject, was met with the assertion of English legislative supremacy." (076-087)

## The Act of Union and Dissolution

The Parliament's final act was its own dissolution, voting in 1800 to approve the [[Act of Union 1800|Act of Union]] with Great Britain. The Union abolished the Irish Parliament and transferred representation to Westminster, a decision contested by Irish nationalists for the next century under the banner of [[Home Rule]].
