CHAPTER VI.

[028-001]THE LAST YEARS OF THE ARD-RIGH, RODERICK O'CONOR.

[028-002]The victory of Thurles, in the year 1174, was the next important

[028-003]military event, as we have seen, after the raising of the second siege

[028-004]of Dublin, in the first campaign of Earl Richard. It seems

[028-005]irreconcilable, with the consequences of that victory, that Ambassadors

[028-006]from Roderick should be found at the Court of Henry II. before the

[028-007]close of the following year: but events personal to both sovereigns

[028-008]will sufficiently explain the apparent anomaly.

[028-009]The campaign of 1174, so unfavourable to Henry's subjects in Ireland,

[028-010]had been most fortunate for his arms in Normandy. His rebellious sons,

[028-011]after severe defeats, submitted, and did him homage; the King of France

[028-012]had gladly accepted his terms of peace; the King of Scotland, while in

[028-013]duress, had rendered him fealty as his liege man; and Queen Eleanor,

[028-014]having fallen into his power, was a prisoner for life. Tried by a

[028-015]similar unnatural conspiracy in his own family, Roderick O'Conor had

[028-016]been less fortunate in coercing them into obedience. His eldest son,

[028-017]Murray, claimed, according to ancient custom, that his father should

[028-018]resign in his favour the patrimonial Province, contenting himself with

[028-019]the higher rank of King of Ireland. But Roderick well understood that

[028-020]in his days, with a new and most formidable enemy established in the

[028-021]old Danish strongholds, with the Constitution torn to shreds by the war

[028-022]of succession, his only real power was over his patrimony; he refused,

[028-023]therefore, the unreasonable request, and thus converted some of his own

[028-024]children into enemies. Nor were there wanting Princes, themselves

[028-025]fathers, who abetted this household treason, as the Kings of France and

[028-026]Scotland had done among the sons of Henry II. Soon after the battle of

[028-027]Thurles, the recovery of Limerick, and the taking of Kilkenny, Donald

[028-028]More O'Brien, lending himself to this odious intrigue, was overpowered

[028-029]and deposed by Roderick, but the year next succeeding having made

[028-030]submission he was restored by the same hand which had cast him down. It

[028-031]was, therefore, while harassed by the open rebellion of his eldest son,

[028-032]and while Henry was rejoicing in his late success, that Roderick

[028-033]despatched to the Court of Windsor Catholicus, Archbishop of Tuam,

[028-034]Concors, Abbot of St. Brendan's, and Laurence, Archbishop of Dublin,

[028-035]whose is styled in these proceedings, "Chancellor of the Irish King,"

[028-036]to negotiate an alliance with Henry, which would leave him free to

[028-037]combat against his domestic enemies. An extraordinary treaty, agreed

[028-038]upon at Windsor, about the feast of Michaelmas, 1175, recognized

[028-039]Roderick's sovereignty over Ireland, the cantreds and cities actually

[028-040]possessed by the subjects of Henry excepted; it subinfeudated his

[028-041]authority to that of Henry, after the manner lately adopted towards

[028-042]William, King of Scotland; the payment of a merchantable hide of every

[028-043]tenth hide of cattle was agreed upon as an annual tribute, while the

[028-044]minor chiefs were to acknowledge their dependence by annual presents of

[028-045]hawks and hounds. This treaty, which proceeded on the wild assumption

[028-046]that the feudal system was of force among the free clans of Erin, was

[028-047]probably the basis of Henry's grant of the Lordship of Ireland to his

[028-048]son, John _Lackland_, a few years later; it was solemnly approved by a

[028-049]special Council, or Parliament, and signed by the representatives of

[028-050]both parties.

[028-051]Among the signers we find the name of the Archbishop of Dublin, who,

[028-052]while in England, narrowly escaped martyrdom from the hands of a

[028-053]maniac, while celebrating Mass at the tomb of St. Thomas. Four years

[028-054]afterwards, this celebrated ecclesiastic attended at Rome, with

[028-055]Catholicus of Tuam, and the Prelates of Lismore, Limerick, Waterford,

[028-056]and Killaloe, the third general council of Lateran, where they were

[028-057]received with all honour by Pope Alexander III. From Rome he returned

[028-058]with legantine powers which he used with great energy during the year

[028-059]1180. In the autumn of that year, he was entrusted with the delivery to

[028-060]Henry II. of the son of Roderick O'Conor, as a pledge for the

[028-061]fulfilment of the treaty of Windsor, and with other diplomatic

[028-062]functions. On reaching England, he found the king had gone to France,

[028-063]and following him thither, he was seized with illness as he approached

[028-064]the Monastery of Eu, and with a prophetic foretaste of death, he

[028-065]exclaimed as he came in sight of the towers of the Convent, "Here shall

[028-066]I make my resting-place." The Abbot Osbert and the monks of the Order

[028-067]of St. Victor received him tenderly, and watched his couch for the few

[028-068]days he yet lingered. Anxious to fulfil his mission, he despatched

[028-069]David, tutor of the son of Roderick, with messages to Henry, and

[028-070]awaited his return with anxiety. David brought him a satisfactory

[028-071]response from the English King, and the last anxiety only remained. In

[028-072]death, as in life, his thoughts were with his country. "Ah, foolish and

[028-073]insensible people!" he exclaimed in his latest hours, "what will become

[028-074]of you? Who will relieve your miseries? Who will heal you?" When

[028-075]recommended to make his last will, he answered, with apostolic

[028-076]simplicity—"God knows, out of all my revenues, I have not a single coin

[028-077]to bequeath." And thus on the 11th day of November, 1180, in the 48th

[028-078]year of his age, under the shelter of a Norman roof, surrounded by

[028-079]Norman mourners, the Gaelic statesman-saint departed out of this life,

[028-080]bequeathing—one more canonized memory to Ireland and to Rome.

[028-081]The prospects of his native land were, at that moment, of a cast which

[028-082]might well disturb the death-bed of the sainted Laurence. Fitz-Aldelm,

[028-083]advanced to the command at Dublin in 1177, had shown no great capacity

[028-084]for following up the conquest. But there was one among his followers

[028-085]who, unaffected by his sluggish example, and undeterred by his jealous

[028-086]interference, resolved to push the outposts of his race into the heart

[028-087]of Ulster. This was John de Courcy, Baron of Stoke Courcy, in

[028-088]Somersetshire, a cavalier of fabulous physical strength, romantic

[028-089]courage, and royal descent. When he declared his settled purpose to be

[028-090]the invasion of Ulster, he found many spirits as discontented with

[028-091]Fitz-Aldelm's inaction as himself ready to follow his banner. His

[028-092]inseparable brother-in-arms, Sir Almaric of St. Laurence, his relative,

[028-093]Jourdain de Courcy, Sir Robert de la Poer, Sir Geoffrey and Walter de

[028-094]Marisco, and other Knights to the number of twenty, and five hundred

[028-095]men at arms, marched with him out of Dublin. Hardly had they got beyond

[028-096]sight of the city, when they were attacked by a native force, near

[028-097]Howth, where Saint Laurence laid in victory the foundation of that

[028-098]title still possessed by his posterity. On the fifth day, they came by

[028-099]surprise upon the famous ecclesiastical city of Downpatrick, one of the

[028-100]first objects of their adventure. An ancient prophecy had foretold that

[028-101]the place would be taken by a chief with birds upon his shield, the

[028-102]bearings of de Courcy, mounted on a white horse, which de Courcy

[028-103]happened to ride. Thus the terrors of superstition were added to the

[028-104]terrors of surprise, and the town being entirely open, the Normans had

[028-105]only to dash into the midst of its inhabitants. But the free clansmen

[028-106]of Ulidia, though surprised, were not intimidated. Under their lord

[028-107]Rory, son of Dunlevy, they rallied to expel the invader. Cardinal

[028-108]Vivian, the Papal Legate, who had just arrived from Man and Scotland,

[028-109]on the neighbouring coast, proffered his mediation, and besought de

[028-110]Courcy to withdraw from Down. His advice was peremptorily rejected, and

[028-111]then he exhorted the Ulidians to fight bravely for their rights. Five

[028-112]several battles are enumerated as being fought, in this and the

[028-113]following year, between de Courcy and the men of Down, Louth, and

[028-114]Antrim, sometimes with success, at others without it, always with heavy

[028-115]loss and obstinate resistance.

[028-116]The barony of Lecale, in which Downpatrick stands, is almost a

[028-117]peninsula, and the barony of the Ardes on the opposite shore of

[028-118]Strangford Lough is nearly insulated by Belfast Lough, the Channel, and

[028-119]the tides of Strangford. With the active co-operation from the sea of

[028-120]Godred, King of Man, (whose daughter Africa he had married), de

[028-121]Courcy's hold on that coast became an exceedingly strong one. A ditch

[028-122]and a few towers would as effectually enclose Lecale and the Ardes from

[028-123]any landward attack, as if they were a couple of well-walled cities.

[028-124]Hence, long after "the Pale" ceased to extend beyond the Boyne, and

[028-125]while the mountain passes from Meath into Ulster were all in native

[028-126]hands, these two baronies continued to be succoured and strengthened by

[028-127]sea, and retained as English possessions. Reinforced from Dublin and

[028-128]from Man after their first success, de Courcy's companions stuck to

[028-129]their castle-building about the shores of Strangford Lough, while he

[028-130]himself made incursions into the interior, by land or by sea, fighting

[028-131]a brisk succession of engagements at Newry, in Antrim, at Coleraine,

[028-132]and on the eastern shore of Lough Foyle.

[028-133]At the time these operations were going forward in Ulster, Milo de

[028-134]Cogan quitted Dublin on a somewhat similar expedition. We have already

[028-135]said that Murray, eldest son of Roderick, had claimed, according to

[028-136]ancient usage, the O'Conor patrimony, his father being Ard-Righ; and

[028-137]had his claim refused. He now entered into a secret engagement with de

[028-138]Cogan, whose force is stated by _Giraldus_ at 500 men-at-arms, and by

[028-139]the Irish annalists as "a great army." With the smaller force he left

[028-140]Dublin, but marching through Meath, was joined at Trim by men from the

[028-141]garrisons de Lacy had planted in East-Meath. So accompanied, de Cogan

[028-142]advanced on Roscommon, where he was received by the son of Roderick

[028-143]during the absence of the Ard-Righ on a visitation among the glens of

[028-144]Connemara. After three days spent in Roscommon, these allies marched

[028-145]across the plain of Connaught, directed their course on Tuam, burning

[028-146]as they went Elphin, Roskeen, and many other churches. The western

[028-147]clansmen everywhere fell back before them, driving off their herds and

[028-148]destroying whatever they could not remove. At Tuam they found

[028-149]themselves in the midst of a solitude without food or forage, with an

[028-150]eager enemy swarming from the west and the south to surround them. They

[028-151]at once decided to retreat, and no time was to be lost, as the Kern

[028-152]were already at their heels. From Tuam to Athleague, and from Athleague

[028-153]to their castles in East-Meath, fled the remnant of de Cogan's

[028-154]inglorious expedition. Murray O'Conor being taken prisoner by his own

[028-155]kinsmen, his eyes were plucked out as the punishment of his treason,

[028-156]and Conor Moinmoy, the joint-victor with Donald O'Brien over Strongbow

[028-157]at Thurles, became the _Roydamna_ or successor of his father.

[028-158]But fresh dissensions soon broke out between the sons and grandsons of

[028-159]Roderick, and the sons of his brother Thurlogh, in one of whose deadly

[028-160]conflicts sixteen Princes of the Sil-Murray fell. Both sides looked

[028-161]beyond Connaught for help; one drew friends from the northern O'Neills,

[028-162]another relied on the aid of O'Brien. Conor Moinmoy, in the year 1186,

[028-163]according to most Irish accounts, banished his father into Munster, but

[028-164]at the intercession of the Sil-Murray, his own clan allowed him again

[028-165]to return, and assigned him a single cantred of land for his

[028-166]subsistence. From this date we may count the unhappy Roderick's

[028-167]retirement from the world.

[028-168]Near the junction of Lough Corrib with Lough Mask, on the boundary line

[028-169]between Mayo and Galway, stands the ruins of the once populous

[028-170]monastery and village of Cong. The first Christian kings of Connaught

[028-171]had founded the monastery, or enabled St. Fechin to do so by their

[028-172]generous donations. The father of Roderick had enriched its shrine by

[028-173]the gift of a particle of the true Cross, reverently enshrined in a

[028-174]reliquary, the workmanship of which still excites the admiration of the

[028-175]antiquaries. Here Roderick retired in the 70th year of his age, and for

[028-176]twelve years thereafter—until the 29th day of November, 1198, here he

[028-177]wept and prayed, and withered away. Dead to the world, as the world to

[028-178]him, the opening of a new grave in the royal corner at Clonmacnoise was

[028-179]the last incident connected with his name, which reminded Connaught

[028-180]that it had lost its once prosperous Prince, and Ireland, that she had

[028-181]seen her last Ard-Righ, according to the ancient Milesian Constitution.

[028-182]Powerful Princes of his own and other houses the land was destined to

[028-183]know for many generations, before its sovereignty was merged in that of

[028-184]England, but none fully entitled to claim the high-sounding, but often

[028-185]fallacious title, of Monarch of all Ireland.

[028-187]The public character of Roderick O'Conor has been hardly dealt with by

[028-188]most modern writers. He was not, like his father, like Murkertach

[028-189]O'Brien, Malachy II., Brian, Murkertach of the leathern cloaks, or

[028-190]Malachy I., eminent as a lawgiver, a soldier, or a popular leader. He

[028-191]does not appear to have inspired love, or awe, or reverence, into those

[028-192]of his own household and patrimony, not to speak of his distant

[028-193]cotemporaries. He was probably a man of secondary qualities, engulfed

[028-194]in a crisis of the first importance. But that he is fairly chargeable

[028-195]with the success of the invaders—or that there was any very

[028-196]overwhelming success to be charged up to the time of his enforced

[028-197]retirement from the world—we have failed to discover. From Dermid's

[028-198]return until his retreat to Cong, seventeen years had passed away.

[028-199]Seventeen campaigns, more or less energetic and systematic, the Normans

[028-200]had fought. Munster was still in 1185—when John Lackland made his

[028-201]memorable exit and entrance on the scene—almost wholly in the hands of

[028-202]the ancient clans. Connaught was as yet without a single Norman

[028-203]garrison. Hugh de Lacy returning to the government of Dublin, in 1179,

[028-204]on Fitz-Aldelm's recall, was more than half _Hibernicized_ by marriage

[028-205]with one of Roderick's daughters, and the Norman tide stood still in

[028-206]Meath. Several strong fortresses were indeed erected in Desmond and

[028-207]Leinster, by John Lackland and by de Courcy, in his newly won northern

[028-208]territory. Ardfinan, Lismore, Leighlin, Carlow, Castledermot, Leix,

[028-209]Delvin, Kilkay, Maynooth and Trim, were fortified; but considering who

[028-210]the Anglo-Normans were, and what they had done elsewhere, even these

[028-211]very considerable successes may be correctly accounted for without

[028-212]overcharging the memory of Roderick with folly and incapacity. That he

[028-213]was personally brave has not been questioned. That he was politic—or at

[028-214]least capable of conceiving the politic views of such a statesman as

[028-215]St. Laurence O'Toole, we may infer from the rank of Chancellor which he

[028-216]conferred, and the other negotiations which he entrusted to that great

[028-217]man. That he maintained his self-respect as a sovereign, both in

[028-218]abstaining from visiting Henry II. under pretence of hospitality at

[028-219]Dublin, and throughout all his difficult diplomacy with the Normans, we

[028-220]are free to conclude. With the Normans for foes—with a decayed and

[028-221]obsolete national constitution to patch up—with nominal subordinates

[028-222]more powerful than himself—with rebellion staring him in the face out

[028-223]of the eyes of his own children—Roderick O'Conor had no ordinary part

[028-224]to play in history. The fierce family pride of our fathers and the

[028-225]vices of their political system are to be deplored and avoided; let us

[028-226]not make the last of their national kings the scape-goat for all his

[028-227]cotemporaries and all his predecessors.
