## Overview

Elizabeth I (1533–1603) was Queen of England and Ireland from 1558 until her death. Her long reign transformed the relationship between England and Ireland more profoundly than any predecessor since [[henry-ii|Henry II]]. (056-003)

The Elizabethan conquest of Ireland—marked by the Desmond Rebellions, the [[nine-years-war|Nine Years' War]] against [[hugh-oneill|Hugh O'Neil]]l, and the final reduction of Gaelic Ireland—established English law and administration throughout the island for the first time. (062-003)

In Irish history, Elizabeth is remembered primarily for the wars, plantations, and religious changes imposed during her reign. The Irish chieftains considered her "the most formidable antagonist" they had ever faced—a queen whose ministers pursued the subjugation of Ireland with relentless determination. (053-089)

## Biography

### Accession and Early Irish Policy

Elizabeth succeeded her half-sister Mary I in 1558 after the death of [[edward-vi|Edward VI]], inheriting a kingdom where English authority in Ireland was largely confined to the Pale. (053-002)

The religious settlement of the early years of her reign—establishing the Protestant Church of Ireland—was imposed without the popular consent that accompanied the Henrician [[reformation|Reformation]]. (053-089) The legislation introduced during the "reign of [[edward-vi|Edward VI]]" was built upon and extended. (051-174)

When writing to her Lord Deputy in Ireland, "Queen Elizabeth, when writing to Lord Sussex," displayed a close and often impatient interest in Irish affairs, demanding results while reluctant to fund the costly military campaigns needed to subdue the Gaelic lords. (056-003) Her early deputies struggled with limited resources against the entrenched power of the great Anglo-Irish and Gaelic families.

### The Desmond Rebellions and Plantation

The great crisis of Elizabeth's middle reign in Ireland was the Desmond Rebellions in Munster. The [[earl-of-desmond|Earl of Desmond]]'s rising, crushed after years of devastating war, led to the first major English plantation scheme—the [[plantation-of-munster|Plantation of Munster]]—which sought to replace the dispossessed Gaelic and Old English population with English settlers. "The days of Queen Elizabeth were now literally numbered," the chroniclers observed, as the conflict in Munster drew to its bloody close. (062-003)

### The Nine Years' War

The most dangerous challenge to Elizabeth's rule in Ireland came from [[hugh-oneill|Hugh O'Neil]]l, [[hugh-oneill|Earl of Tyrone]], who waged the [[nine-years-war|Nine Years' War]] (1594–1603). O'Neill, educated at the English court and created Earl by Elizabeth herself, turned against the Crown and assembled a confederacy of Gaelic lords. The Queen's officers discovered that "the title of [[hugh-oneill|Earl of Tyrone]] was revived" in O'Neill's person only to become the Crown's greatest threat. (053-087) His victory at the Battle of the Yellow Ford in 1598 was the greatest defeat ever suffered by an English army in Ireland.

The Queen responded by dispatching her most capable commander, [[charles-blount-lord-mountjoy|Charles Blount, Lord Mountjoy]], who systematically reduced O'Neill's strongholds. In the final years of her reign, English forces under Mountjoy finally brought the Gaelic lords to submission. (057-114) The war ended just days after Elizabeth's death in March 1603, with O'Neill's surrender to Mountjoy.

## Legacy

### The Elizabethan Settlement

Elizabeth's Irish legacy is complex and contested. On one hand, her reign saw the completion of the Tudor conquest—the extension of English law and administration across all Ireland for the first time. Trinity College [[dublin|Dublin]] was founded by royal charter in 1592, part of a policy of cultural and religious reformation. On the other hand, the methods of conquest—scorched-earth warfare, plantation, and the destruction of the Gaelic order—left wounds that never fully healed.

### The Religious Divide

The imposition of the Protestant [[reformation|Reformation]] during Elizabeth's reign permanently associated English rule with religious alienation in the Irish mind. The Church of Ireland, established by law, never won the allegiance of the majority population, laying the foundations for the sectarian divisions that would dominate Irish politics for the next four centuries. "By order of Queen Elizabeth," the religious changes "commonly dated from the year" of her accession were implemented across the country. (063-144)
