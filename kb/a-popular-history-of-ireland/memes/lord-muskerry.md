Lord Muskerry, Donough MacCarty, was the head of the MacCarthy family of Muskerry and a leading Confederate Catholic commander in Munster during the Wars of the Confederation (1641–1653). He served as a commissioner for the Confederates in negotiations with the Marquess of Ormond, commanded the Munster army, and was among the last Irish commanders to lay down arms in 1652.

## The House of Muskerry

The MacCarthys of Muskerry were a cadet branch of the great MacCarthy dynasty of Munster, and ancient rivals of the main MacCarthy line. (055-178) The lordship of Muskerry, centred in west Cork, had been built up by Cormac Oge MacCarthy and his successors. By the outbreak of the rebellion in 1641, Lord Muskerry had emerged as one of the most influential Catholic nobles in the province.

## The Confederate Period

When Sir William St. Leger directed a series of murderous raids upon the peasantry of Cork, Lord Muskerry and other leading recusants offered their services to maintain the peace of the province. They were driven by an insulting refusal to combine with the insurgent cause. (067-257) Muskerry quickly became one of the most prominent Confederate leaders in Munster.

As the twelve-months' truce with the Crown drew to a close in 1644, the battle of Marston Moor stimulated Ormond to pursue a renewal of the treaty. At his request, Lord Muskerry and five other commissioners left Kilkenny on the last day of August for Dublin, to negotiate with the Viceroy. (070-144)

## The Ormond Peace

In March 1646, preliminary articles of peace were agreed to and signed by the Earl of Ormond on behalf of the King, and by Lords Mountgarrett and Muskerry on behalf of the Confederates. (071-025) After further negotiation, on the 28th of March a final modification of the articles, reduced to thirty in number, was signed by Ormond and by Lord Muskerry and the other commissioners for the Confederates. (071-175) These thirty articles conceded significant religious and civil liberties to the Catholics of Ireland, though they would prove deeply divisive within the Confederate ranks.

Muskerry also played a role in receiving the Papal Nuncio, [[Giovanni Battista Rinuccini]], upon his arrival: he and General Preston brought the Nuncio to Kilkenny Castle, where Rinuccini was received in stately formality in the great gallery. (071-119)

## The Cromwellian Crisis

As the Confederate coalition fractured in the late 1640s, Lord Muskerry aligned himself with the moderate faction. With Ormond, Preston, Inchiquin, and [[Earl of Clanrickarde]], Muskerry assumed the lead of the Supreme Council after the advanced age of [[Mountgarrett, Viscount|Lord Mountgarrett]] made him unable to continue. (072-192) In Munster, the army refused to follow the lead of the Earl of Glamorgan and clamoured for their old chief, Lord Muskerry — a testament to his enduring influence over the men of the province. (072-149)

In 1651, Muskerry attempted a diversion in favour of Limerick but was repulsed at Castleishen by the flying camp of Lord Broghill. (074-067) The following year, as Cromwellian forces swept through Munster, Lord Muskerry yielded Ross Castle on Killarney in June 1652. (074-115)

## Fate Under the Commonwealth

After the final defeat, the Cromwellian High Court of Justice condemned many Irish leaders to death: Sir Phelim O'Neil, Viscount Mayo, and others were executed, and Colonel Fitzpatrick's mother was burnt at the stake. Lords Muskerry and Clanmaliere, however, were set at liberty through some secret influence. (075-090) Muskerry later recovered his estates at the Restoration and was created Earl of Clancarty by Charles II.
