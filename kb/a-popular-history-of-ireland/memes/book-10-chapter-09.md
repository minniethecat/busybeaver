CHAPTER IX.

[084-001]THE REVOLUTIONARY WAR—CAMPAIGN OF 1691—BATTLE OF AUGHRIM—CAPITULATION

[084-002]OF LIMERICK.

[084-003]Saint Ruth, with absolute powers, found himself placed at the head of

[084-004]from 20,000 to 25,000 men, in the field or in garrison, regular or

[084-005]irregular, but all, with hardly an exception, Irish. His and

[084-006]Tyrconnell's recent supplies had sufficed to renew the clothing and

[084-007]equipment of the greater part of the number, but the whole contents of

[084-008]the army chest, the golden hinge on which war moves, was estimated in

[084-009]the beginning of May to afford to each soldier only "a penny a day for

[084-010]three weeks." He had under him some of the best officers that France

[084-011]could spare, or Ireland produce, and he had with him the hearts of

[084-012]nine-tenths of the natives of the country.

[084-013]A singular illustration of the popular feeling occurred the previous

[084-014]August. The Milesian Irish had cherished the belief ever since the

[084-015]disastrous day of Kinsale, that an O'Donnell from Spain, having on his

[084-016]shoulder a red mark (_ball derg_), would return to free them from the

[084-017]English yoke, in a great battle near Limerick. Accordingly, when a

[084-018]representative of the Spanish O'Donnells actually appeared at Limerick,

[084-019]bearing as we know many of his family have done, even to our day, the

[084-020]unmistakable red mark of the ancient Tyrconnell line, immense numbers

[084-021]of the country people who had held aloof from the Jacobite cause,

[084-022]obeyed the voice of prophecy, and flocked round the Celtic deliverer.

[084-023]From 7,000 to 8,000 recruits were soon at his disposal, and it was not

[084-024]without bitter indignation that the chief, so enthusiastically

[084-025]received, saw regiment after regiment drafted from among his followers,

[084-026]and transferred to other commanders. Bred up a Spanish subject—the

[084-027]third in descent from an Irish prince—it is not to be wondered at that

[084-028]he regarded the _Irish_ cause as all in all, and the interests of King

[084-029]James as entirely secondary. He could hardly consider himself as bound

[084-030]in allegiance to that king; he was in no way indebted to him or his

[084-031]family, and if we learn that when the war grew desperate, but before it

[084-032]was ended, he had entered into a separate treaty for himself and his

[084-033]adherents, with William's generals, we must remember, before we condemn

[084-034]him, that we are speaking of an Hiberno-Spaniard, to whom the house of

[084-035]Stuart was no more sacred than the house of Orange.

[084-036]The Williamite army rendezvoused at Mullingar towards the end of May,

[084-037]under Generals De Ginkle, Talmash and Mackay. On the 7th of June, they

[084-038]moved in the direction of Athlone, 18,000 strong, "the ranks one blaze

[084-039]of scarlet, and the artillery such as had never before been seen in

[084-040]Ireland." The capture of Ballymore Castle, in West-Meath, detained them

[084-041]ten days; on the 19th, joined by the Duke of Wurtemburg, the Prince of

[084-042]Hesse and the Count of Nassau, with 7,000 foreign mercenaries, the

[084-043]whole sat down before the English town of Athlone, which Saint Ruth,

[084-044]contrary to his Irish advisers, resolved to defend. In twenty-four

[084-045]hours those exposed outworks abandoned by the veteran Grace the

[084-046]previous year, fell, and the bombardment of the Irish town on the

[084-047]opposite or Connaught bank, commenced. For ten days—from the 20th to

[084-048]the 30th of June—that fearful cannonade continued. Storey, the

[084-049]Williamite chaplain, to whom we are indebted for many valuable

[084-050]particulars of this war, states that the besiegers fired above 12,000

[084-051]cannon shot, 600 shells and many tons of stone, into the place. Fifty

[084-052]tons of powder were burned in the bombardment. The castle, an imposing

[084-053]but lofty and antique structure, windowed as much for a residence as a

[084-054]fortress, tumbled into ruins; the bridge was broken down and

[084-055]impassable; the town a heap of rubbish, where two men could no longer

[084-056]walk abreast. But the Shannon had diminished in volume as the summer

[084-057]advanced, and three Danes employed for that purpose found a ford above

[084-058]the bridge, and at six o'clock on the evening of the last day of June,

[084-059]2,000 picked men, headed by Gustavus Hamilton's grenadiers, dashed into

[084-060]the ford at the stroke of a bell. At the same instant all the English

[084-061]batteries on the Leinster side opened on the Irish town, wrapping the

[084-062]river in smoke, and distracting the attention of the besiegers. Saint

[084-063]Ruth was, at this critical moment, at his camp two miles off, and

[084-064]D'Usson, the commandant, was also absent from his post. In half an hour

[084-065]the Williamites were masters of the heap of rubbish which had once been

[084-066]Athlone, with a loss of less than fifty men killed and wounded. For

[084-067]this bold and successful movement De Ginkle was created Earl of

[084-068]Athlone, and his chief officers were justly ennobled. Saint Ruth,

[084-069]over-confident, in a strange country, withdrew to Ballinasloe, behind

[084-070]the river Suck, and prepared to risk everything on the hazard of a

[084-071]pitched battle.

[084-072]De Ginkle moved slowly from Athlone in pursuit of his enemy. On the

[084-073]morning of the 11th of July, as the early haze lifted itself in wreaths

[084-074]from the landscape, he found himself within range of the Irish, drawn

[084-075]up, north and south, on the upland of Kilcommodan hill, with a morass

[084-076]on either flank, through which ran two narrow causeways—on the right,

[084-077]"the pass of Urrachree," on the left, the causeway leading to the

[084-078]little village of Aughrim. Saint Ruth's force must have numbered from

[084-079]15,000 to 20,000 men, with nine field-pieces; De Ginkle commanded from

[084-080]25,000 to 30,000, with four batteries—two of which mounted six guns

[084-081]each. During the entire day, attack after attack, in the direction of

[084-082]Urrachree or of Aughrim was repulsed, and the assailants were about to

[084-083]retire in despair. As the sun sank low, a last desperate attempt was

[084-084]made with equal ill success. "Now, my children," cried the elated Saint

[084-085]Ruth, "the day is ours! Now I shall drive them back to the walls of

[084-086]Dublin!" At that moment he fell by a cannon shot to the earth, and

[084-087]stayed the advancing tide of victory. The enemy marked the check,

[084-088]halted, rallied and returned. Sarsfield, who had not been entrusted

[084-089]with his leader's plan of action, was unable to remedy the mischief

[084-090]which ensued. Victory arrested was converted into defeat. The sun went

[084-091]down on Aughrim, and the last great Irish battle between the Reformed

[084-092]and Roman religions. Four thousand of the Catholics were killed and

[084-093]wounded, and three thousand of the Protestants littered the field.

[084-094]Above five hundred prisoners, with thirty-two pairs of colours, eleven

[084-095]standards, and a large quantity of small arms, fell into the hands of

[084-096]the victors. One portion of the fugitive survivors fled to Galway, the

[084-097]larger part, including all the cavalry, to Limerick.

[084-099]This double blow at Athlone and Aughrim shook to pieces the remaining

[084-100]Catholic power in Connaught. Galway surrendered ten days after the

[084-101]battle; Balldearg O'Donnell, after a vain attempt to throw himself into

[084-102]it in time, made terms with De Ginkle, and carried his two regiments

[084-103]into Flanders to fight on the side Spain and Rome had chosen to take in

[084-104]the European coalition. Sligo, the last western garrison, succumbed,

[084-105]and the brave Sir Teague O'Regan marched his 600 men, survivors,

[084-106]southward to Limerick.

[084-107]Thus once more all eyes and all hearts in the British Islands were

[084-108]turned towards the well-known city of the lower Shannon. There, on the

[084-109]14th of August, Tyrconnell expired, stricken down by apoplexy. On the

[084-110]25th, De Ginkle, reinforced by all the troops he could gather in with

[084-111]safety, had invested the place on three sides. Sixty guns, none of less

[084-112]than 12 pounds calibre, opened their deadly fire against it. An English

[084-113]fleet ascended the river, hurling its missiles right and left. On the

[084-114]9th of September the garrison made an unsuccessful sally, with heavy

[084-115]loss; on the 10th, a breach, forty yards wide, was made in the wall

[084-116]overhanging the river; on the night of the 15th, through the treachery

[084-117]or negligence of Brigadier Clifford, on guard at the Clare side of the

[084-118]river, a pontoon bridge was laid, and a strong English division crossed

[084-119]over in utter silence. The Irish horse, which had hitherto kept open

[084-120]communications with the country on that side, fell back to Six Mile

[084-121]Bridge. On the 24th, a truce of three days was agreed upon, and on the

[084-122]3rd of October the memorable "Treaty of Limerick" was signed by the

[084-123]Williamite and Jacobite commissioners.

[084-124]The _civil_ articles of Limerick will be mentioned farther on; the

[084-125]_military_ articles, twenty-nine in number, provided that all persons

[084-126]willing to expatriate themselves, as well officers and soldiers as

[084-127]rapparees and volunteers, should have free liberty to do so, to any

[084-128]place beyond seas, except England and Scotland; that they might depart

[084-129]in whole bodies, companies, or parties; that if plundered by the way,

[084-130]William's government should make good their loss; that fifty ships of

[084-131]200 tons each should be provided for their transportation, besides two

[084-132]men-of-war for the principal officers; that the garrison of Limerick

[084-133]might march out with all their arms, guns and baggage, "colours flying,

[084-134]drums beating, and matches lighting!" It was also agreed, that those

[084-135]who so wished might enter the service of William, retaining their rank

[084-136]and pay; but though De Ginkle was most eager to secure for his master

[084-137]some of those stalwart battalions, only 1,000 out of the 13,000 that

[084-138]marched out of Limerick filed to the left at King's Island, Two

[084-139]thousand others accepted passes and protections; 4,500 sailed with

[084-140]Sarsfield from Cork, 4,700 with D'Usson and De Tesse, embarked in the

[084-141]Shannon on board a French fleet which arrived a week too late to

[084-142]prevent the capitulation; in English ships, 3,000 embarked with General

[084-143]Wauchop; all which, added to Mountcashel's brigade, over 5,000 strong,

[084-144]gave an Irish army of from 20,000 to 25,000 men to the service of King

[084-145]Louis.

[084-146]As the ships from Ireland reached Brest and the ports of Brittany,

[084-147]James himself came down from Saint Germain to receive them. They were

[084-148]at once granted the rights of French citizenship without undergoing the

[084-149]forms of naturalization. Many of them rose to eminent positions in war

[084-150]and in diplomacy, became founders of distinguished families, or dying

[084-151]childless, left their hard-won gold to endow free bourses at Douay and

[084-152]Louvain, for poor Irish scholars destined for the service of the

[084-153]church, for which they had fought the good fight, in another sense, on

[084-154]the Shannon and the Boyne. The migration of ecclesiastics was almost as

[084-155]extensive as that of the military. They were shipped by dozens and by

[084-156]scores, from Dublin, Cork, and Galway. In seven years from the treaty,

[084-157]there remained but 400 secular and 800 regular clergy in the country.

[084-158]Nearly double that number, deported by threats or violence, were

[084-159]scattered over Europe, pensioners on the princes and bishops of their

[084-160]faith, or the institutions of their order. In Rome, 72,000 francs

[084-161]annually were allotted for the maintenance of the fugitive Irish

[084-162]clergy, and during the first three months of 1699, three remittances

[084-163]from the Holy Father, amounting to 90,000 livres, were placed in the

[084-164]hands of the Nuncio at Paris, for the temporary relief of the fugitives

[084-165]in France and Flanders. It may also be added here, that till the end of

[084-166]the eighteenth century, an annual charge of 1,000 Roman crowns was

[084-167]borne by the Papal treasury for the encouragement of Catholic

[084-168]Poor-schools in Ireland.

[084-169]The revolutionary war, thus closed, had cost King William, or rather

[084-170]the people of England, at least 10,000,000 of pounds sterling, and with

[084-171]the other wars of that reign, laid the foundation of the English

[084-172]national debt. As to the loss of life, the Williamite chaplain, Storey,

[084-173]places it "at 100,000, young and old, besides treble the number that

[084-174]are ruined and undone." The chief consolation of the vanquished in that

[084-175]struggle was, that they had wrung even from their adversaries the

[084-176]reputation of being "one of the most warlike of nations"—that they

[084-177]"buried the synagogue with honour."
