## Overview

Henry Joy McCracken (1767–1798) was a Belfast cotton manufacturer and a leading figure among the United Irishmen in Ulster. In the summer of 1798, he commanded the rebel forces that attempted to seize the town of Antrim, the most significant rising in the north of Ireland during that fateful year. (104-117)

At thirty-two years of age, McCracken was well educated and belonged to the class of Protestant radicals whose republican convictions had been forged in the ferment of the 1790s. His enterprise in Antrim represented one of the last hopes of the United Irish movement in Ulster. (104-117) (104-118)

## Early Life and the United Irishmen

McCracken was born in Belfast in 1767 into a family engaged in the cotton trade. He himself became a cotton manufacturer, part of the rising commercial and industrial class that transformed Belfast in the late 18th century. Like many of his associates, he was drawn into the United Irishmen by the ideals of the French Revolution and the demand for parliamentary reform. (104-117)

The United Irishmen in Belfast represented a particularly radical strain of northern Presbyterianism, combining democratic republicanism with a vision of Irish independence that sought to unite Catholic and Protestant. McCracken was among the most committed of the Belfast leaders. (104-117)

## The Enterprise of Antrim

In June of 1798, with the rebellion already underway in Wexford and other parts of Leinster, McCracken was chosen to lead the rising in Antrim. The plan was to seize the county town and trigger a general insurrection in Ulster, drawing support from both the surrounding countryside and the city of Belfast. (104-117) (104-118)

> The leader of this enterprise was Henry John McCracken, a cotton manufacturer of Belfast, thirty two years of age, well educated, and of much influence among the United Irishmen of the North. [104-117] [104-118]

The rebels assembled and marched on Antrim, but their expectations of substantial reinforcements from Belfast and other districts were disappointed. The government forces, forewarned of the rising, were prepared to meet them. (104-116)

## Defeat and Capture

The battle outside Antrim was brief and decisive. The rebel force, numbering several thousand but poorly armed and lacking experienced officers, was quickly thrown into confusion by disciplined government troops. Between two and three hundred of the insurgents fell in the engagement. (104-127) (104-128)

> Between two and three hundred of the rebels fell, and McCracken and his staff, deserted by their hasty levies, were arrested, wearied and hopeless, about a month later, wandering among the hills of Donegall. [104-127] [104-128] [104-129]

McCracken and his staff, deserted by their hastily assembled followers, evaded capture for about a month. They wandered wearied and hopeless among the hills before being finally arrested. McCracken was tried by court-martial and executed in Belfast, one of the last of the northern United Irish leaders to pay the ultimate price. (104-128) (104-129)

## Legacy

Henry Joy McCracken is remembered as a tragic figure of the 1798 Rebellion — a leader whose idealism and courage were not matched by the military circumstances in which he found himself. His execution, alongside that of his brother-in-law and United Irish colleague, marked the effective end of organized republican resistance in Ulster for a generation. (104-117)

His story is emblematic of the Protestant republican tradition in Irish history, which reached its zenith in the 1790s and was largely submerged in the sectarian divisions of the 19th century. McCracken remains a significant figure in the radical memory of Belfast. (104-117) (104-127)
