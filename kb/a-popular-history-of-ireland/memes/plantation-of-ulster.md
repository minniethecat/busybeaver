The **Plantation of Ulster** was the organized colonization (plantation) of the northern province of [[Ulster]] by English and Scottish Protestant settlers, undertaken by King [[James I]] following the [[Flight of the Earls]] in 1607. It was the largest and most consequential of the Irish plantations, permanently altering the demographic, political, and religious landscape of the province. (004-065)

## Background

The defeat of the Gaelic lords at the [[Battle of Kinsale]] (1601) and the subsequent [[Flight of the Earls]] (1607) — when [[Hugh O'Neill]], [[hugh-oneill|Earl of Tyrone]], and Rory O'Donnell, Earl of Tyrconnell, fled Ireland for the Continent — left vast tracts of land in [[ulster|Ulster]] forfeit to the Crown. The English administration, which had previously struggled to control [[ulster|Ulster]] through the policy of [[Surrender and Regrant]], now seized the opportunity for a more radical transformation.

The departure of the native lords extinguished the last independent Gaelic polity in Ireland and provided the legal pretext for the Crown to confiscate the six escheated counties of [[armagh|Armagh]], Cavan, Coleraine (later Londonderry), Donegal, Fermanagh, and Tyrone.

## The Settlement

The plantation scheme divided the confiscated lands into parcels allocated to three categories of grantees: English and Scottish "Undertakers" (who received the largest estates and were required to settle Protestant tenants), "Servitors" (veterans of the Irish wars who had served the Crown), and "Deserving Irish" (native grantees deemed loyal).

The Undertakers were required to build fortified dwellings, introduce English agricultural practices, and ensure that their tenants were English-speaking Protestants. Scottish settlers, predominantly [[presbyterian|Presbyterians]], were particularly concentrated in Antrim and Down, while English settlers predominated in other areas.

## Consequences

The Plantation of Ulster was the most successful of the Irish plantations in terms of settler implantation. It established a Protestant majority in parts of [[ulster|Ulster]] that endures to the present day, creating the basis for the later political division of Ireland. The native Gaelic population was displaced from the best land but not extirpated — many remained as tenants and laborers, creating a segregated society stratified by ethnicity and religion. (004-065)

The plantation also had profound economic consequences. The settlers introduced new agricultural techniques, established market towns, and developed the linen industry that would become the economic backbone of the region. [[ulster|Ulster]], previously one of the most Gaelic and least anglicized provinces, became the most thoroughly colonized.

## Legacy

The legacy of the Plantation of Ulster reverberates through Irish history. It created the sectarian geography that would, three centuries later, lead to the [[Partition of Ireland]] and the establishment of [[Northern Ireland]]. The demographic separation between the predominantly Protestant northeast and the predominantly Catholic rest of Ireland remains the most visible consequence of the plantation.

The plantation also served as a model for subsequent colonial enterprises in North America and elsewhere in the [[British Empire]]. The patterns of land allocation, settler-native relations, and legal frameworks developed in [[ulster|Ulster]] were adapted and applied in later English and British colonial ventures.
