Sir James Fitzmaurice, a cousin of the [[Earl of Desmond|Gerald Fitzgerald, 15th Earl of Desmond]], was the military leader of the first Desmond Rebellion (1569–1573) and later the commander of a Papal-sponsored expedition to liberate Ireland from Elizabethan rule. McGee describes him as a man of "unyielding firmness of character" whose resistance to English authority in Munster earned him lasting fame among his countrymen. (055-243)

Unlike many Gaelic leaders who submitted when abandoned by their confederates, Fitzmaurice continued the struggle from the mountain fastnesses of the south, and when defeated at home carried his cause to the courts of France, Spain, and Rome. (056-121)

## The First Desmond Rebellion

Fitzmaurice rose to prominence during the presidency of Sir John Perrott in Munster. The rebellion was triggered by the aggressive plantation policies of the Elizabethan administration and the determination of English "Undertakers" to seize the vast Desmond estates. Fitzmaurice assembled a confederacy of the Geraldine lords and their allies across the province. (055-244)

The English ambassador Norris obtained full indemnity for the Earl of Desmond, who submitted to the Crown. Fitzmaurice, however, refused to accept terms. Deserted by his confederates, he fell back on the mountain strongholds of the south — the forests of Aharlow and the glen of Aherlow — which for generations had served as natural fortresses dedicated to the defence of freedom. (055-243) (055-260)

> "Sir James Fitzmaurice, thus deserted by his confederates, had need of all that unyielding firmness of character for which he had obtained credit." [055-243]

> "In this fastness Fitzmaurice continued to maintain himself, until a prospect of new combinations opened to him in the West." [055-260]

Castle after castle belonging to his cousins and himself was taken by the powerful siege trains of President Perrott. Castlemaine, the last stronghold commanding an outlet by sea, fell to the Crown forces. Yet Fitzmaurice continued the struggle from the interior, where the difficult terrain made pursuit costly and dangerous. (055-246)

## Alliance with the Sons of Clanrickarde

While maintaining his position in Munster, Fitzmaurice found new allies in the west. The sons of the Earl of Clanrickarde, fearing arrest at a court summoned by President Fitton of Connaught at Galway, flew to arms and opened negotiations with the Munster leader. Fitzmaurice withdrew from Aharlow and joined them in Galway. (055-266)

> "The sons of the Earl of Clanrickarde... flew to arms and opened negotiations with Fitzmaurice." [055-266]

During the campaign that followed, Fitzmaurice aided the western confederates with what McGee calls his "iron energy and sagacious counsel." They took and demolished the works of Athenry and, in part, the Court of Athlone. Their successes induced the government to grant favourable terms to the Clanrickarde faction. The sons submitted — their main object being attained — leaving Fitzmaurice once again isolated. (055-268) (055-273)

## The Papal Expedition

Defeated but unbroken, Fitzmaurice took ship for the continent, where he became the focus of intense diplomatic activity. English spies tracked his movements from the French court to St. Malo — where he resided for some time with his family — to Madrid, and finally to Rome. No higher tribute could be paid to his character, McGee observes, than the closeness with which all his movements were watched. (056-121)

> "No higher tribute could be paid to the character of that heroic man than the closeness with which all his movements were watched by English spies, specially set upon his track." [056-121]

In Rome, Fitzmaurice obtained the backing of Pope Gregory XIII for an expedition to liberate Ireland. The expedition was fitted out at Papal expense, with a secret agreement that it would be supported by Philip II of Spain after landing. Fitzmaurice was joined by the English adventurer Thomas Stukely, who obtained from the Pope the titles of Marquis of Leinster and Baron of Idrone and Ross. A fleet was assembled, carrying several hundred Spanish and Italian soldiers. (056-138) (056-143)

> "Fitzmaurice, travelling overland to Spain, was to unite there with another party of adventurers, and to form a junction with Stukely and Pisano on the coast of Kerry." [056-145]

The expedition sailed with the Papal benediction and a most earnest exhortation from the Holy Father to the Catholics of Ireland to follow Fitzmaurice's banner. Expectation of his return, at the head of a liberating expedition, spread throughout the south and west. The coasts were watched with the utmost vigilance by government agents. (056-149) (056-156)

## Landing and Death

In July 1579, Fitzmaurice landed at Smerwick on the Kerry coast with a small force. Stukely had diverted to Portugal, where he died at the Battle of Alcácer, and the expected Spanish reinforcements did not materialise. The Earl of Desmond, under intense government pressure, refused to rally to the expedition. (057-106)

Fitzmaurice withdrew into the interior, pursued by Crown forces. He fell in a skirmish soon after the landing, killed not in a great battle but in an obscure encounter in the woods of Limerick. His son and a small party of followers survived him. (055-281)

Despite the failure of the expedition, Fitzmaurice's name lived on in Irish memory. Thirteen years after his death, his sons — educated at Alcalá in Spain — were still remembered. The rebellion he had kindled in Munster was inherited by the Earl of Desmond, whose own rebellion (1579–1583) resulted in the devastation of the province and the final destruction of the Geraldine earldom. (058-128)

> "James Fitzmaurice 'of happy memory,' and granting the same indulgence" [056-227]

McGee records the epithet "of happy memory" applied to Fitzmaurice by his contemporaries. Though his expedition failed, his indomitable resistance made him one of the most celebrated figures of the Elizabethan wars in Ireland. Sir John of Desmond, who had been nominated by Fitzmaurice as his successor in the leadership of the Geraldine cause, carried on the struggle until his own death in 1582. (057-032)

The fort at Smerwick, where Fitzmaurice had landed, became the scene of an infamous massacre the following year when Lord Grey de Wilton, the new Lord Deputy, slaughtered the surrendered garrison of a second Papal expedition under the command of an Italian officer. Fitzmaurice's vision of a Catholic Ireland liberated by foreign arms would persist in Irish political thought for centuries. (057-106)
