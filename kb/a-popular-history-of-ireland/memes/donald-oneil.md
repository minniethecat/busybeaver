## Overview

**Donald O'Neil** (also rendered Donnell O'Neil; died 1325), Prince of Ulster, was a Gaelic Irish king of the O'Neill dynasty who led the invitation of [[Edward Bruce]] to Ireland and authored the celebrated Remonstrance to [[Pope John XXII]] in 1317 or 1318.

McGee describes him as "endued with an intensely national spirit," and his career represents one of the most determined attempts in the fourteenth century to unite the Gaelic Irish against Anglo-Norman domination. (036-076) (036-083)

## Biography

### Early Kingship

Donald O'Neil was the son of King Brian of the battle of Down (Brian O'Neill), who fell at the Battle of Down in 1260 and who, half a century before, at the Conference of Caeluisge, had been formally chosen Ard-Righ by the nobles of three Provinces.

(036-078) (036-079) Following the death of Hugh Bwee O'Neill in 1283, a fierce contest for the succession arose between Brian O'Neill's son and Donald. After a twelve-year struggle, Donald prevailed over his rival in 1295, and his rule extended from that period without interruption until his death in 1325 at Leary's lake, in the present diocese of Clogher.

(032-194) (032-195) (032-196) (032-197)

Donald succeeded to the principality of Ulster not without a protracted struggle with the Red Earl, [[Richard de Burgo|Richard de Burgh]], Earl of Ulster, some twenty years before the Battle of Bannockburn. (036-081) (036-082) His house had given more monarchs to the island than any other; his father had been acknowledged by the requisite majority;

his courage, patriotism, and talents were admittedly equal to the task of sovereignty. (036-105) (036-106) (036-107) Yet he felt the utter impossibility of conciliating that fatal family pride among the chiefs which McGee identifies as the worst consequence of the Celtic system; chiefs who refused homage to the native Prince of Ulster would submit to serve a foreign Earl.

(036-109) (036-110) (036-111) (036-112) (036-113)

### The Bruce Invasion and Alliance

Donald O'Neil's darling project was to establish a unity of action against the common enemy among the chiefs, similar to that which the Primate [[Nicholas McMaelisa]] had brought about among the Bishops.

(036-102) (036-103) (036-104) He seems to have fully adopted the views of McMaelisa, one of the most resolute opponents of the Norman conquest, who had spent his episcopate resisting the interference of the Kings of England in the nomination of Bishops. (036-084) (036-085)

Seeing that his countrymen would submit to a stranger rather than to one of themselves, Donald reasoned that by the hand of some friendly stranger they might be united and liberated. (036-116) (036-117) The decisive victory of [[Robert the Bruce]] at Bannockburn in 1314 provided the opportunity.

Following this triumph, the chief mover in the negotiation to bring Edward Bruce to Ireland was Donald, Prince of Ulster. (036-075) (036-076)

> Bannockburn suggested the design, Edward Bruce, the gallant companion of all his brother's fortunes and misfortunes, was now invited to place himself at the head of the men of Ulster, in a war for Irish independence. (036-065)

Edward Bruce sailed from Ayr with 6,000 men in 300 galleys and landed on 25 May 1315 at the Glendun river near Glenarm. He was promptly joined by Donald O'Neil and twelve other chiefs. (036-128) (036-132) (036-133) Their first advance was from the coast towards Lough Neagh, where at Rathmore they defeated the Mandevilles and Savages of the Ards of Down. (036-134) (036-135)

After Bruce's campaigns through Leinster, he returned to Dundalk where he was solemnly elected King of Ireland. Donald O'Neil, by letters patent as son of Brian "of the battle of Down," the last acknowledged native king, formally resigned his right in favour of Bruce.

The ceremony of inauguration was performed in the Gaelic fashion on the hill of Knocknemelan, while the consecration took place in one of the churches of Dundalk. (037-025) (037-026) (037-027) (037-028) (037-029) (037-033) (037-034) (037-035)

### The Remonstrance to Pope John XXII

In response to King Edward II of England's appeals to the Pope at Avignon to exercise his influence with the clergy and chiefs of Ireland for the preservation of the English interest, Donald O'Neil despatched his celebrated Remonstrance to [[Pope John XXII]].

(037-180) (037-181) (037-182) The Pontiff enclosed the document to Edward II with an urgent recommendation that the wrongs therein recited might be atoned for and avoided in the future. (037-183) (037-184)

McGee describes this document as a "powerful indictment against the Anglo-Normans, which has ever since remained one of the cardinal texts of our history." (032-200) (032-201) It was evidently written after the unsuccessful attempt, in which Donald was himself a main actor, to establish Edward Bruce on the throne of Ireland. (032-202) (032-203)

In the Remonstrance, Donald defends the invitation of Edward Bruce to the Irish throne, speaking of the new sovereign as:

> the illustrious Earl of Carrick, Edward de Bruce, a nobleman descended from the same ancestors with themselves, whom they had called to their aid, and freely chosen as their king and lord. (037-031) (037-032) (037-033)

The letter demonstrates how wide and deep was the gulf then existing between the English and Irish churchmen, a division that had been fostered over generations of conquest and resistance.

(047-047) (047-048) It remains one of the most important political statements of medieval Gaelic Ireland, articulating the grievances of the native population against English rule and asserting the right of the Irish to choose their own sovereign.

### Later Years and Expulsion

Following the defeat and death of Edward Bruce at the [[Battle of Faughard]] on 14 October 1318, the victorious Anglo-Irish commander [[John de Bermingham]], newly created Earl of Louth, promptly followed up his victory by expelling Donald O'Neil, "the mainspring of the invasion," from [[Tyrone]]. (038-075) (038-076) (038-077) (038-078)

However, Donald, after a short sojourn among the mountains of [[Fermanagh]], returned during the winter and resumed his lordship, though he never wholly recovered from the losses he had sustained. (038-079) (038-080) (038-081) His power was broken but his spirit unyielding; he remained Prince of Ulster until his death in 1325 at Leary's lake, in the diocese of Clogher. (032-197)

## Legacy

Donald O'Neil stands as one of the most significant figures of fourteenth-century Gaelic Ireland. His invitation of [[Edward Bruce]] and his orchestration of a national confederacy represented the most ambitious attempt to expel the Anglo-Normans since the initial invasion.

Although the Bruce Invasion ultimately failed, it had lasting consequences: the Earldom of [[Ulster]], which had been the strongest Anglo-Norman lordship in the north, was effectively destroyed during the campaign, and the power of the O'Neills was consolidated in [[Tyrone]] for generations afterward.

The Remonstrance to [[Pope John XXII]] remains Donald's most enduring legacy. As McGee notes, it is "one of the cardinal texts of our history" (032-201), providing future generations with a powerful statement of Irish national grievances and the legal and historical basis for resistance to English rule. The document articulated themes that would recur throughout Irish history;

the right of the Irish people to self-determination, the condemnation of English misgovernment, and the appeal to a higher authority against oppression.

McGee offers this assessment of the patriotic design:

> A more patriotic design, or one with fairer omens of success, could not have entered the mind or heart of a native Prince, after the event of the battle at Bannockburn. (036-119) (036-120)
