CHAPTER I.

[052-001]EVENTS OF THE REIGN OF EDWARD SIXTH.

[052-002]On the last day of January, 1547, Edward, son of Henry, by Lady Jane

[052-003]Seymour, was crowned by the title of Edward VI. He was then only nine

[052-004]years old, and was destined to wear the crown but for six years and a

[052-005]few months. No Irish Parliament was convened during his reign, but the

[052-006]Reformation was pushed on with great vigour, at first under the

[052-007]patronage of the Protector, his uncle, and subsequently of that uncle's

[052-008]rival, the Duke of Northumberland. Archbishop Cranmer suffered the zeal

[052-009]of neither of these statesmen to flag for want of stimulus, and the

[052-010]Lord Deputy Saint Leger, judging from the cause of his disgrace in the

[052-011]next reign, approved himself a willing assistant in the work.

[052-012]The Irish Privy Council, which exercised all the powers of government

[052-013]during this short reign, was composed exclusively of partizans of the

[052-014]Reformation. Besides Archbishop Browne and Staples, Bishop of Meath,

[052-015]its members were the Chancellor, Read, and the Treasurer, Brabazon,

[052-016]both English, with the Judges Aylmer, Luttrel, Bath, Cusack, and

[052-017]Howth—all proselytes, at least in form, to the new opinions. The Earl

[052-018]of Ormond, with sixteen of his household, having been poisoned at a

[052-019]banquet in Ely House, London, in October before Henry's death, the

[052-020]influence of that great house was wielded during the minority of his

[052-021]successor by Sir Francis Bryan, an English adventurer, who married the

[052-022]widowed countess. This lady being, moreover, daughter and heir general

[052-023]to James, Earl of Desmond, brought Bryan powerful connections in the

[052-024]South, which he was not slow to turn to a politic account. His ambition

[052-025]aimed at nothing less than the supreme authority, military and civil;

[052-026]but when at length he attained the summit of his hopes, he only lived

[052-027]to enjoy them a few months.

[052-029]To enable the Deputy and Council to carry out the work they had begun,

[052-030]an additional military force was felt to be necessary, and Sir Edward

[052-031]Bellingham was sent over, soon after Edward's accession, with a

[052-032]detachment of six hundred horse, four hundred foot, and the title of

[052-033]Captain General. This able officer, in conjunction with Sir Francis

[052-034]Bryan, who appears to have been everywhere, overran Offally, Leix, Ely

[052-035]and West-Meath, sending the chiefs of the two former districts as

[052-036]prisoners to London, and making advantageous terms with those of the

[052-037]latter. He was, however, supplanted in the third year of Edward by

[052-038]Bryan, who held successively the rank of Marshal of Ireland and Lord

[052-039]Deputy. To the latter office he was chosen on an emergency, by the

[052-040]Council, in December, 1549, but died at Clonmel, on an expedition

[052-041]against the O'Carrolls, in the following February. His successes and

[052-042]those of Bellingham hastened the reduction of Leix and Offally into

[052-043]shire ground in the following reign.

[052-044]The total military force at the disposal of Edward's commanders was

[052-045]probably never less than 10,000 effective men. By the aid of their

[052-046]abundant artillery, they were enabled to take many strong places

[052-047]hitherto deemed impregnable to assault. The mounted men and infantry,

[052-048]were, as yet, but partially armed with musquetons, or firelocks—for the

[052-049]spear and the bow still found advocates among military men. The

[052-050]spearmen or lancers were chiefly recruited on the marches of

[052-051]Northumberland from the hardy race of border warriors; the mounted

[052-052]bowmen or hobilers were generally natives of Chester or North Wales.

[052-053]Between these new comers and the native Anglo-Irish troops many

[052-054]contentions arose from time to time, but in the presence of the common

[052-055]foe these bickerings were completely forgotten. The townsmen of

[052-056]Waterford marched promptly at a call, under their standard of the three

[052-057]galleys, and those of Dublin as cheerfully turned out under the

[052-058]well-known banner, decorated with three flaming towers.

[052-059]The _personnel_ of the administration, in the six years of Edward, was

[052-060]continually undergoing change. Bellingham, who succeeded St. Leger, was

[052-061]supplanted by Bryan, on whose death, St. Leger was reappointed. After

[052-062]another year Sir James Croft was sent over to replace St. Leger, and

[052-063]continued to fill the office until the accession of Queen Mary. But

[052-064]whoever rose or fell to the first rank in civil affairs, the Privy

[052-065]Council remained exclusively Protestant, and the work of innovation was

[052-066]not suffered to languish. A manuscript account, attributed to Adam

[052-067]Loftus, Browne's successor, assigns the year 1549 as the date when "the

[052-068]Mass was put down," in Dublin, "and divine service was celebrated in

[052-069]English." Bishop Mant, the historian of the Established Church in

[052-070]Ireland, does not find any account of such an alteration, nor does the

[052-071]statement appear to him consistent with subsequent facts of this reign.

[052-072]We observe, also, that in 1550, Arthur Magennis, the Pope's Bishop of

[052-073]Dromore, was allowed by the government to enter on possession of his

[052-074]temporalities after taking an oath of allegiance, while King's Bishops

[052-075]were appointed in that and the next two years to the vacant Sees of

[052-076]Kildare, Leighlin, Ossory, and Limerick. A vacancy having occurred in

[052-077]the See of Cashel, in 1551, it was unaccountably left vacant, as far as

[052-078]the Crown was concerned, during the remainder of this reign, while a

[052-079]similar vacancy in Armagh was filled, at least in name, by the

[052-080]appointment of Dr. Hugh Goodacre, chaplain to the Bishop of Winchester,

[052-081]and a favourite preacher with the Princess Elizabeth. This Prelate was

[052-082]consecrated, according to a new form, in Christ Church, Dublin, on 2nd

[052-083]of February, 1523, together with his countryman, John Bale, Bishop of

[052-084]Ossory. The officiating Prelates were Browne, Staples, and Lancaster of

[052-085]Kildare—all English. The Irish Establishment, however, does not at all

[052-086]times rest its argument for the validity of its episcopal Order upon

[052-087]these consecrations. Most of their writers lay claim to the Apostolic

[052-088]succession, through Adam Loftus, consecrated in England, according to

[052-089]the ancient rite, by Hugh Curwen, an Archbishop in communion with the

[052-090]See of Rome, at the time of his elevation to the episcopacy.

[052-091]In February, 1551, Sir Anthony St. Leger received the King's commands

[052-092]to cause the Scriptures translated into the English tongue, and the

[052-093]Liturgy and Prayers of the Church, also translated into English, to be

[052-094]read in all the churches of Ireland. To render these instructions

[052-095]effective, the Deputy summoned a convocation of the Archbishops,

[052-096]Bishops, and Clergy, to meet in Dublin on the 1st of March, 1551. In

[052-097]this meeting—the first of two in which the defenders of the old and of

[052-098]the new religion met face to face—the Catholic party was led by the

[052-099]intrepid Dowdal, Archbishop of Armagh, and the Reformers by Archbishop

[052-100]Browne. The Deputy, who, like most laymen of that age, had a strong

[052-101]theological turn, also took an active part in the discussion. Finally

[052-102]delivering the royal order to Browne, the latter accepted it in a set

[052-103]form of words, without reservation; the Anglican Bishops of Meath,

[052-104]Kildare, and Leighlin, and Coyne, Bishop of Limerick, adhering to his

[052-105]act; Primate Dowdal, with the other Bishops, having previously retired

[052-106]from the Conference. On Easter day following, the English service was

[052-107]celebrated for the first time in Christ Church, Dublin, the Deputy, the

[052-108]Archbishop, and the Mayor of the city assisting. Browne preached from

[052-109]the text: "Open mine eyes that I may see the wonders of the law"—a

[052-110]sermon chiefly remarkable for its fierce invective against the new

[052-111]Order of Jesuits.

[052-112]Primate Dowdal retired from the Castle Conference to Saint Mary's

[052-113]Abbey, on the north side of the Liffey, where he continued while these

[052-114]things were taking place in the city proper. The new Lord Deputy, Sir

[052-115]James Crofts, on his arrival in May, addressed himself to the Primate,

[052-116]to bring about, if possible, an accommodation between the Prelates.

[052-117]Fearing, as he said, an "order ere long to alter church matters, as

[052-118]well in offices as in ceremonies," the new Deputy urged another

[052-119]Conference, which was accordingly held at the Primate's lodgings, on

[052-120]the 16th of June. At this meeting Browne does not seem to have been

[052-121]present, the argument on the side of the Reformers being maintained by

[052-122]Staples. The points discussed were chiefly the essential character of

[052-123]the Holy Sacrifice of the Mass, and the invocation of Saints. The tone

[052-124]observed on both sides was full of high-bred courtesy. The letter of

[052-125]the Sacred Scriptures and the authority of Erasmus in Church History

[052-126]were chiefly relied upon by Staples; the common consent and usage of

[052-127]all Christendom, the primacy of Saint Peter, and the binding nature of

[052-128]the oath taken by Bishops at their consecration, were pointed out by

[052-129]the Primate. The disputants parted, with expressions of deep regret

[052-130]that they could come to no agreement; but the Primacy was soon

[052-131]afterwards transferred to Dublin, by order of the Privy Council, and

[052-132]Dowdal fled for refuge into Brabant. The Roman Catholic and the

[052-133]Anglican Episcopacy have never since met in oral controversy on Irish

[052-134]ground, though many of the second order of the clergy in both

[052-135]communions have, from time to time, been permitted by their superiors

[052-136]to engage in such discussions.

[052-137]Whatever obstacles they encountered within the Church itself, the

[052-138]propagation of the new religion was not confined to moral means, nor

[052-139]was the spirit of opposition at all tunes restricted to mere argument.

[052-140]Bishop Bale having begun at Kilkenny to pull down the revered images of

[052-141]the Saints, and to overturn the Market Cross, was set upon by the mob,

[052-142]five of his servants, or guard, were slain, and himself narrowly

[052-143]escaped with his life by barricading himself in his palace. The

[052-144]garrisons in the neighbourhood of the ancient seats of ecclesiastical

[052-145]power and munificence were authorized to plunder their sanctuaries and

[052-146]storehouses. The garrison of Down sacked the celebrated shrines and

[052-147]tomb of Patrick, Bridget, and Columbkill; the garrison of Carrickfergus

[052-148]ravaged Rathlin Island and attacked Derry, from which, however, they

[052-149]were repulsed with severe loss by John the Proud. But the most

[052-150]lamentable scene of spoliation, and that which excited the profoundest

[052-151]emotions of pity and anger in the public mind, was the violation of the

[052-152]churches of St. Kieran—the renowned Clonmacnoise. This city of schools

[052-153]had cast its cross-crowned shade upon the gentle current of the Upper

[052-154]Shannon for a thousand years. Danish fury, civil storm, and Norman

[052-155]hostility had passed over it, leaving traces of their power in the

[052-156]midst of the evidences of its recuperation. The great Church to which

[052-157]pilgrims flocked from every tribe of Erin, on the 9th of September—St.

[052-158]Kieran's Day; the numerous chapels erected by the chiefs of all the

[052-159]neighbouring clans; the halls, hospitals, book-houses, nunneries,

[052-160]cemeteries, granaries—all still stood, awaiting from Christian hands

[052-161]the last fatal blow. In the neighbouring town of Athlone—seven or eight

[052-162]miles distant—the Treasurer, Brabazon, had lately erected a strong

[052-163]"Court" or Castle, from which, in the year 1552, the garrison sallied

[052-164]forth to attack "the place of the sons of the nobles,"—which is the

[052-165]meaning of the name. In executing this task they exhibited a fury

[052-166]surpassing that of Turgesius and his Danes. The pictured glass was torn

[052-167]from the window frames, and the revered images from their niches;

[052-168]altars were overthrown; sacred vessels polluted. "They left not," say

[052-169]the Four Masters, "a book or a gem," nor anything to show what

[052-170]Clonmacnoise had been, save the bare walls of the temples, the mighty

[052-171]shaft of the round tower, and the monuments in the cemeteries, with

[052-172]their inscriptions in Irish, in Hebrew, and in Latin. The Shannon

[052-173]re-echoed with their profane songs and laughter, as laden with chalices

[052-174]and crucifixes, brandishing croziers, and flaunting vestments in the

[052-175]air, their barges returned to the walls of Athlone.

[052-176]In all the Gaelic speaking regions of Ireland, the new religion now

[052-177]began to be known by those fruits which it had so abundantly produced.

[052-178]Though the southern and midland districts had not yet recovered from

[052-179]the exhaustion consequent upon the suppression of the Geraldine league

[052-180]and the abortive insurrection of Silken Thomas, the northern tribes

[052-181]were still unbroken and undismayed. They had deputed George Paris, a

[052-182]kinsman of the Kildare Fitzgeralds, as their agent to the French King,

[052-183]in the latter days of Henry VIII., and had received two ambassadors on

[052-184]his behalf at Donegal and Dungannon. These ambassadors, the Baron de

[052-185]Forquevaux, and the Sieur de Montluc, who subsequently became Bishop of

[052-186]Valence, crossing over from the west of Scotland, entered into a

[052-187]league, offensive and defensive, with "the princes" of Tyrconnell and

[052-188]Tyrowen, by which the latter bound themselves to recognize, on certain

[052-189]conditions, "whoever was King of France as King of Ireland likewise."

[052-190]This alliance, though prolonged into the reign of Edward, led to

[052-191]nothing definitive, and we shall see in the next reign how the hopes

[052-192]then turned towards France were naturally transferred to Spain.

[052-193]The only native name which rises into historic importance at this

[052-194]period is that of Shane, or John O'Neil, "the Proud." He was the

[052-195]legitimate son of that Con O'Neil who had been girt with the Earl's

[052-196]baldric by the hands of Henry VIII. His father had procured at the same

[052-197]time for an illegitimate son, Ferodach, or Mathew, of Dundalk, the

[052-198]title of Baron of Dungannon, with the reversion of the Earldom. When,

[052-199]however, John the Proud came of age, he centred upon himself the hopes

[052-200]of his clansmen, deposed his father, subdued the Baron, and assumed the

[052-201]title of O'Neil. In 1552 he defeated the efforts of Sir William

[052-202]Brabazon to fortify Belfast, and delivered Derry from its plunderers.

[052-203]From that time till his tragical death, in the ninth year of Queen

[052-204]Elizabeth, he stood unquestionably the first man of his race, both in

[052-205]lineage and action.
