**Rory OConor** (c.1116 -- 29 November 1198), known in Irish as Ruaidri Ua Conchobair and referred to by McGee as Roderick OConor, was the last reigning Ard-Righ (High King) of Ireland under the ancient Milesian Constitution. The son of [[Thorlogh More OConor]], he ascended the throne at a time of chronic civil war that had prepared the path for the Normans (014-010). His reign coincided with the [[Norman Invasion of Ireland]], and though he mounted a determined resistance, he was ultimately forced by family rebellion and foreign encroachment into a twelve-year retirement at Cong Abbey.

## Early Life and Rise to Power

Rory OConor was the son of Thorlogh More OConor, King of Connaught and sometime Ard-Righ. He was already in his tenth year of rule over Connaught when, at the age of fifty, the death of Murtogh of Aileach in 1166 cleared the path to the high-kingship (019-163). Rathcrogan, the chief seat of OConor power in what is now County Roscommon, had reached the summit of its glory in this period. McGee describes it as a place "where half the tributes of the island were counted at its gate," serving as "the busy focus of intrigue, and the very elysium of bards, story-tellers, and mendicants" (019-178).

On the first tidings of Murtogh's death, Roderick advanced on [[Dublin]], which opened its gates and accepted his suzerainty, delivering four thousand head of cattle as tribute and placing hostages in his hands (019-188). From [[dublin|Dublin]] he marched to Drogheda, received the homage of O'Carroll, lord of Oriel, and then entered [[leinster|Leinster]] to demand hostages from [[Dermid McMurrogh]] (019-192).

> "Roderick proceeded southward, obtained the adhesion of Ossory and Munster; confirming Desmond to McCarthy, and Thomond to O'Brien." (019-200)

Dermid, surrounded and outmatched, burned his own city of Ferns and fled beyond seas in 1168. A solemn sentence of banishment was pronounced by the assembled princes, and Morrogh na Gael was inaugurated in his stead (019-206).

## Supreme King of Ireland

In the years following his accession, Roderick exercised the full authority of the Ard-Righ, holding national assemblies at Tailtin and [[Tara]], distributing gifts to his suffragans, and enforcing his claims across the North and South (023-148). He founded lectorships at [[armagh|Armagh]] and partitioned Meath between his ally Tiernan O'Ruarc and himself. McGee tells us that Roderick "might have spent the festival of Christmas, 1168, or of Easter, 1169, in the full assurance that his power was firmly established" (023-153).

His position was, however, precarious. The western O'Conors had long competed for supremacy with the O'Neills and O'Briens, and a chronic civil war had prepared the path for [[strongbow|Strongbow]] and the Normans (014-009). The term "Kings with Opposition" was applied to nearly all who reigned between Brian's time and Roderick's, meaning kings unable to secure general obedience (014-011).

## The Norman Invasion

[[dermid-mcmurrogh|Dermid McMurrogh]] returned to Ireland in 1167 with a fleet of Flemings, and in May 1169, [[robert-fitzstephen|Robert Fitzstephen]] landed at Bannow Bay with the first Norman force. Roderick gathered a national host at [[Tara]] and marched to oppose the invaders (023-218). After negotiations, a treaty was concluded at Ferns by which Dermid acknowledged Roderick's supremacy and surrendered his son Conor as a hostage, while secretly binding himself to admit no more Normans (023-237).

This engagement Dermid violated almost immediately, welcoming Maurice Fitzgerald and later [[Strongbow]] ([[strongbow|Richard de Clare]], [[strongbow|Earl of Pembroke]]) in 1170. Roderick assumed the protection of [[dublin|Dublin]] and encamped at Clondalkin, but [[strongbow|Strongbow]] and Dermid outmanoeuvred him, storming the city in October 1170 (025-080). In retaliation for Dermid's breach of faith, Roderick ordered the execution of the hostages he held, including Conor, Dermid's son (025-173).

> "Roderick, unprepared to besiege the enemy who had thus outmarched and outwitted him at that season of the year... broke up his encampment at Clondalkin, and retired to Connaught." (025-165)

In 1171, Roderick led a second attempt on [[dublin|Dublin]], investing the city at the head of the forces of the island. The siege was lifted after a desperate sally by the garrison, who "carried off the provisions, armour, and horses of Roderick" (026-055). When [[Henry II]] landed in Ireland in October 1171 with a massive force, Roderick remained west of the Shannon, unsupported by suffragans who had made their own peace with the invader (027-003). He convened a rival synod at Tuam, with Archbishop Catholicus O'Duffy, to counter the synod Henry had summoned at Cashel (026-170).

The high point of Roderick's military fortunes came in 1174 at the Battle of Thurles. His son Conor Moinmoy, commanding the monarch's forces alongside Donald More O'Brien of Thomond, inflicted a severe defeat on [[strongbow|Strongbow]]. Four knights and seven hundred (or, by other accounts, seventeen hundred) Normans were left dead on the field (027-106). [[strongbow|Strongbow]] retreated to Waterford in disarray.

## Treaty and Retreat

In 1175, Roderick sent ambassadors to the court of [[henry-ii|Henry II]], led by Saint Laurence O'Toole, Archbishop of [[dublin|Dublin]], whom he had appointed his Chancellor (028-034). The resulting Treaty of Windsor recognised Roderick's sovereignty over all of Ireland not yet in Norman hands, though subinfeudated to Henry, and provided for an annual tribute of hides and presents of hawks and hounds (028-038). The treaty proved unworkable, as the feudal assumptions it embodied had no force among the free clans of Erin.

The later years of Roderick's reign were consumed by family strife. His eldest son, Murray, demanded the patrimonial province of Connaught by ancient custom, and when refused, conspired with the Norman Milo de Cogan to invade Roscommon (028-136). Murray was captured by his own kinsmen and blinded for his treason. Another son, Conor Moinmoy, banished his father into Munster in 1186, though the clan allowed Roderick to return and assigned him a single cantred of land for his subsistence (028-163).

From this date, Roderick retired from the world. He entered the monastery of Cong, near the junction of Lough Corrib and Lough Mask, where his father Thorlogh More had deposited a relic of the True Cross (028-170). Here he lived for twelve years.

> "Dead to the world, as the world to him, the opening of a new grave in the royal corner at Clonmacnoise was the last incident connected with his name, which reminded Connaught that it had lost its once prosperous Prince, and Ireland, that she had seen her last Ard-Righ." (028-177)

He died on 29 November 1198, in his eighty-second year. His burial at [[Clonmacnoise]] marked the end of the high-kingship under the ancient constitution of Ireland.

## Legacy and Character

McGee defends Roderick's reputation against the harsh judgments of modern writers, arguing that he was "probably a man of secondary qualities, engulfed in a crisis of the first importance" (028-193). From Dermid's return until his retreat to Cong, seventeen campaigns were fought, yet Munster remained almost wholly in native hands, Connaught had not a single Norman garrison, and de Lacy was more than half Hibernicised by marriage to one of Roderick's daughters (028-201).

> "With the Normans for foes -- with a decayed and obsolete national constitution to patch up -- with nominal subordinates more powerful than himself -- with rebellion staring him in the face out of the eyes of his own children -- Roderick OConor had no ordinary part to play in history." (028-220)

Roderick's personal bravery was never questioned; he maintained his self-respect as a sovereign by refusing to visit [[henry-ii|Henry II]] under pretence of hospitality. The Treaty of Windsor and the appointment of Saint Laurence O'Toole as Chancellor attest to his capacity for statecraft (028-213). Though powerful princes of his own and other houses ruled Ireland for generations after, none could fully claim the title of Monarch of all Ireland. In him, the ancient Milesian line of Ard-Righ reached its final end.
