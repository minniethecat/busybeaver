CHAPTER X.

[032-001]EVENTS OF THE THIRTEENTH CENTURY—THE NORMANS IN MEATH AND ULSTER.

[032-002]We may estimate the power of the de Lacy family in the second

[032-003]generation, from the fact that their expulsion required a royal army

[032-004]and navy, commanded by the King in person, to come from England.

[032-005]Although pardoned by John, the brothers took care never to place

[032-006]themselves in that cowardly tyrant's power, and they observed the same

[032-007]precaution on the accession of his son, until well assured that he did

[032-008]not share the antipathy of his father. After their restoration the

[032-009]Lacys had no rivals among the Norman-Irish except the Marshal family,

[032-010]and though both houses in half a century became extinct, not so those

[032-011]they had planted or patronized, or who claimed from them collaterally.

[032-012]In Meath the Tuites, Cusacks, Flemings, Daltons, Petits, Husseys,

[032-013]Nangles, Tyrrells, Nugents, Verdons, and Gennevilles, struck deep into

[032-014]the soil. The co-heiresses, Margaret and Matilda de Lacy, married Lord

[032-015]Theobald de Verdon and Sir Geoffrey de Genneville, between whom the

[032-016]estate of their father was divided; both these ladies dying without

[032-017]male issue, the lordship was, in 1286, claimed by Richard de Burgo,

[032-018]Earl of Ulster, whose mother was their cousin-germain. But we are

[032-019]anticipating time.

[032-020]No portion of the island, if we except, perhaps, Wexford and the shores

[032-021]of Strangford Lough, was so thoroughly castellated as the ancient Meath

[032-022]from the sea to the Shannon. Trim, Kells and Durrow were the strongest

[032-023]holds; there were keeps or castles at Ardbraccan, Slane, Rathwyre,

[032-024]Navan, Skreen, Santry, Clontarf, and Castleknock—for even these places,

[032-025]almost within sight of Dublin, were included in de Lacy's original

[032-026]grant. None of these fortresses could have been more than a few miles

[032-027]distant from the next, and once within their thick-ribbed walls, the

[032-028]Norman, Saxon, Cambrian, or Danish serf or tenant might laugh at the

[032-029]Milesian arrows and battle-axes without. With these fortresses, and

[032-030]their own half-Irish origin and policy, the de Lacys, father and son,

[032-031]held Meath for two generations in general subjection. But the

[032-032]banishment of the brothers in 1210, and the death of Walter of Meath,

[032-033]presented the family of O'Melaghlin and the whole of the Methian tribes

[032-034]with opportunities of insurrection not to be neglected. We read,

[032-035]therefore, under the years 1211, '12 and '13, that Art O'Melaghlin and

[032-036]Cormac, his son, took the castles of Killclane, Ardinurcher, Athboy,

[032-037]and Smerhie, killing knights and wardens, and enriching themselves with

[032-038]booty; that the whole English of Ireland turned out _en masse_ to the

[032-039]rescue of their brethren in Meath; that the castles of Birr, Durrow,

[032-040]and Kinnetty were strengthened against Art, and a new one erected at

[032-041]Clonmacnoise. After ten years of exile, the banished de Lacys returned,

[032-042]and by alliance with O'Neil, no less than their own prowess, recovered

[032-043]all their former influence. Cormac, son of Art, left a son and

[032-044]successor also named Art, who, we read at the year 1264, gave the

[032-045]English of Meath a great defeat upon the Brosna, where he that was not

[032-046]slain was drowned. Following the blow, he burned their villages and

[032-047]broke the castles of the stranger throughout Devlin, Calry, and Brawny,

[032-048]and replaced in power over them the McCoghlans, Magawleys, and

[032-049]O'Breens, from whom he took hostages according to ancient custom. Two

[032-050]years afterwards he repulsed Walter de Burgh at Shannon harbour,

[032-051]driving his men into the river, where many of them perished. At his

[032-052]death (A.D. 1283) he is eulogized for having destroyed seven-and-twenty

[032-053]English castles in his lifetime. From these exploits he was called Art

[032-054]_na Caislean_, a remarkable distinction, when we remember that the

[032-055]Irish were, up to this time, wholly unskilled in besieging such

[032-056]strongholds as the Norman engineers knew so well how to construct. His

[032-057]only rival in Meath in such meritorious works of destruction was Conor,

[032-058]son of Donnell, and O'Melaghlin of East-Meath, or _Bregia_, whose death

[032-059]is recorded at the year 1277, "as one of the three men in Ireland" whom

[032-060]the midland English most feared.

[032-061]From the ancient mensal the transition is easy to the north. The

[032-062]border-land of Breffni, whose chief was the first of the native nobles

[032-063]that perished by Norman perfidy, was at the beginning of the century

[032-064]swayed by Ulgarg O'Rourke. Of Ulgarg we know little, save that in the

[032-065]year 1231 he "died on his way to the river Jordan"—a not uncommon

[032-066]pilgrimage with the Irish of those days. Nial, son of Congal,

[032-067]succeeded, and about the middle of the century we find Breffni divided

[032-068]into two lordships, from the mountain of Slieve-an-eiran eastward, or

[032-069]Cavan, being given to Art, son of Cathal, and from the mountain

[032-070]westward, or Leitrim, to Donnell, son of Conor, son of Tiernan, de

[032-071]Lacy's victim. This subdivision conduced neither to the strengthening

[032-072]of its defenders nor to the satisfaction of O'Conor, under whose

[032-073]auspices it was made. Family feuds and household treasons were its

[032-074]natural results for two or three generations; in the midst of these

[032-075]broils two neighbouring families rose into greater importance, the

[032-076]O'Reillys in Cavan and the Maguires in Fermanagh. Still, strong in

[032-077]their lake and mountain region, the tribes of Breffni were

[032-078]comparatively unmolested by foreign enemies, while the stress of the

[032-079]northern battle fell upon the men of Tyrconnell and Tyrone, of Oriel

[032-080]and of the coast country, from Carlingford to the Causeway.

[032-082]The borders of Tyrconnell and Tyrone, like every other tribe-land, were

[032-083]frequently enlarged or contracted, according to the vigour or weakness

[032-084]of their chiefs or neighbours. In the age of which we now speak,

[032-085]Tyrconnell extended from the Erne to the Foyle, and Tyrone from the

[032-086]Foyle to Lough Neagh, with the exception of the extreme north of Berry

[032-087]and Antrim, which belonged to the O'Kanes. It was not till the

[032-088]fourteenth century that the O'Neils spread their power east of Lough

[032-089]Neagh, over those baronies of Antrim long known as north and south

[032-090]_Clan-Hugh-Buidhe_, (Clandeboy.) North Antrim was still known as

[032-091]Dalriada, and South Antrim and Down, as Ulidia. Oriel, which has been

[032-092]usually spoken of in this history as Louth, included angles of Monaghan

[032-093]and Armagh, and was anciently the most extensive lordship in Ulster.

[032-094]The chieftain families of Tyrconnell were the O'Donnells; of Tyrone,

[032-095]the O'Neils and McLaughlins; of Dalriada, O'Kanes, O'Haras, and

[032-096]O'Shields; of Ulidia, the Magennis of Iveagh and the Donlevys of Down;

[032-097]of Oriel, the McMahons and O'Hanlons. Among these populous tribes the

[032-098]invaders dealt some of their fiercest blows, both by land and sea, in

[032-099]the thirteenth century. But the north was fortunate in its chiefs; they

[032-100]may fairly contest the laurel with the O'Conors, O'Briens and McCarthys

[032-101]of the west and south.

[032-102]In the first third of the century, Hugh O'Neil, who succeeded to the

[032-103]lordship of Tyrone in 1198, and died in 1230, was cotemporary with

[032-104]Donnell More O'Donnell, who, succeeding to the lordship of Tyrconnell

[032-105]in 1208, died in 1241, after an equally long and almost equally

[032-106]distinguished career. Melaghlin O'Donnell succeeded Donnell More from

[032-107]'41 to '47, Godfrey from '48 to '57, and Donnell Oge from 1257 to 1281,

[032-108]when he was slain in battle. Hugh O'Neil was succeeded in Tyrone by

[032-109]Donnell McLaughlin, of the rival branch of the same stock, who in 1241

[032-110]was subdued by O'Donnell, and the ascendancy of the family of O'Neil

[032-111]established in the person of Brian, afterwards chosen King of Ireland,

[032-112]and slain at Down. Hugh Boy, or the Swarthy, was elected O'Neil on

[032-113]Brian's death, and ruled till the year 1283, when he was slain in

[032-114]battle, as was his next successor, Brian, in the year 1295. These names

[032-115]and dates are worthy to be borne in mind, because on these two great

[032-116]houses mainly devolved the brunt of battle in their own province.

[032-117]These northern chiefs had two frontiers to guard or to assail: the

[032-118]north-eastern, extending from the glens of Antrim to the hills of

[032-119]Mourne, and the southern stretching from sea to sea, from Newry to

[032-120]Sligo. This country was very assailable by sea; to those whose castles

[032-121]commanded its harbours and rivers, the fleets of Bristol, Chester, Man,

[032-122]and Dublin could always carry supplies and reinforcements. By the

[032-123]interior line one road threaded the Mourne mountains, and deflected

[032-124]towards Armagh, while another, winding through west Breffni, led from

[032-125]Sligo into Donegal by the cataract of Assaroe,—the present

[032-126]Ballyshannon. Along these ancient lines of communication, by fords, in

[032-127]mountain passes, and near the landing places for ships, the struggle

[032-128]for the possession of that end of the Island went on, at intervals,

[032-129]whenever large bodies of men could be spared from garrisons and from

[032-130]districts already occupied.

[032-131]In the year 1210, we find that there was an English Castle at

[032-132]Cael-uisge, now Castle-Caldwell, on Lough Erne, and that it was broke

[032-133]down and its defenders slain by Hugh O'Neil and Donald More O'Donnell

[032-134]acting together. After this event we have no trace of a foreign force

[032-135]in the interior of Ulster for several years. Hugh O'Neil, who died in

[032-136]1230, is praised by the Bards for "never having given hostages,

[032-137]pledges, or tributes to English or Irish," which seems a compliment

[032-138]well founded. During several years following that date the war was

[032-139]chiefly centred in Connaught, and the fighting men of the north who

[032-140]took part in it were acting as allies to the O'Conors. Donald More

[032-141]O'Donnell had married a daughter of Cathal Crovdearg, so that ties of

[032-142]blood, as well as neighbouring interests, united these two great

[032-143]families. In the year 1247, an army under Maurice Fitzgerald, then Lord

[032-144]Justice, crossed the Erne in two divisions, one above and the other at

[032-145]Ballyshannon. Melaghlin O'Donnell was defending the passage of the

[032-146]river when he was taken unexpectedly in the rear by those who had

[032-147]crossed higher up, and thus was defeated and slain. Fitzgerald then

[032-148]ravaged Tyrconnell, set up a rival chief O'Canavan, and rebuilt the

[032-149]Castle at Cael-uisge, near Beleek. Ten years afterwards Godfrey

[032-150]O'Donnell, the successor of Melaghlin, avenged the defeat at

[032-151]Ballyshannon, in the sanguinary battle of Credran, near Sligo, where

[032-152]engaging Fitzgerald in single combat, he gave him his death-stroke.

[032-153]From wounds received at Credran, Godfrey himself, after lingering

[032-154]twelve months in great suffering, died. But his bodily afflictions did

[032-155]not prevent him discharging all the duties of a great Captain; he razed

[032-156]a second time the English Castle on Lough Erne, and stoutly protected

[032-157]his own borders against the pretensions of O'Neil, being carried on his

[032-158]bier in the front of the battle of Lough Swilly in 1258.

[032-159]It was while Tyrconnell was under the rule of this heroic soldier that

[032-160]the unfortunate feud arose between the O'Neils and O'Donnells. Both

[032-161]families, sprung from a common ancestor, of equal antiquity and equal

[032-162]pride, neither would yield a first place to the other. "Pay me my

[032-163]tribute," was O'Neil's demand; "I owe you no tribute, and if I did—-"

[032-164]was O'Donnell's reply. The O'Neil at this time—Brian—aspiring to

[032-165]restore the Irish sovereignty in his own person, was compelled to begin

[032-166]the work of exercising authority over his next neighbour. More than one

[032-167]border battle was the consequence, not only with Godfrey, but with

[032-168]Donnell Oge, his successor. In the year 1258, Brian was formally

[032-169]recognized by O'Conor and O'Brien as chief of the kingdom, in the

[032-170]conference of Cael-uisge, and two years later, at the battle of Down,

[032-171]gallantly laid down his life, in defence of the kingdom he claimed to

[032-172]govern. In this most important battle no O'Donnell is found fighting

[032-173]with King Brian, though immediately afterwards we find Donnell Oge of

[032-174]Tyrconnell endeavouring to subjugate Tyrone, and active afterwards in

[032-175]the aid of his cousins, the grandsons of Cathal Crovdearg, in

[032-176]Connaught.

[032-177]The Norman commander in this battle was Stephen de Longespay, then Lord

[032-178]Justice, Earl of Salisbury in England, and Count de Rosman in France.

[032-179]His marriage with the widow of Hugh de Lacy and daughter of de

[032-180]Riddlesford connected him closely with Irish affairs, and in the battle

[032-181]of Down he seems to have had all the Anglo-Irish chivalry, "in gold and

[032-182]iron," at his back. With King Brian O'Neil fell, on that crimson day,

[032-183]the chiefs of the O'Hanlons, O'Kanes, McLaughlins, O'Gormlys, McCanns,

[032-184]and other families who followed his banner. The men of Connaught

[032-185]suffered hardly less than those of Ulster. McDermott, Lord of Moylurgh,

[032-186]Cathal O'Conor, O'Gara, McDonogh, O'Mulrony, O'Quinn, and other chiefs

[032-187]were among the slain. In Hugh _Bwee_ O'Neil the only hope of the house

[032-188]of Tyrone seemed now to rest; and his energy and courage were all taxed

[032-189]to the uttermost to retain the place of his family in the Province,

[032-190]beating back rapacious neighbours on the one hand, and guarding against

[032-191]foreign enemies on the other. For twelve years, Hugh _Bwee_ defended

[032-192]his lordship against all aggressors. In 1283, he fell at the hands of

[032-193]the insurgent chiefs of Oriel and Breffni, and a fierce contest for the

[032-194]succession arose between his son Brian and Donald, son of King Brian

[032-195]who fell at Down. A contest of twelve years saw Donald successful over

[032-196]his rival (A.D. 1295), and his rule extended from that period until

[032-197]1325, when he died at Leary's lake, in the present diocese of Clogher.

[032-198]It was this latter Donnell or Donald O'Neil, who, towards the end of

[032-199]his reign, addressed to Pope John XXII. (elected to the pontificate in

[032-200]1316) that powerful indictment against the Anglo-Normans, which has

[032-201]ever since remained one of the cardinal texts of our history. It was

[032-202]evidently written after the unsuccessful attempt, in which Donald was

[032-203]himself a main actor, to establish Edward Bruce on the throne of

[032-204]Ireland. That period we have not yet reached, but the merciless

[032-205]character of the warfare waged against the natives of the country could

[032-206]hardly have been aggravated by Bruce's defeat. "They oblige us by open

[032-207]force," says the Ulster Prince, "to give up to them our houses and our

[032-208]lands, and to seek shelter like wild beasts upon the mountains, in

[032-209]woods, marshes, and caves. Even there we are not secure against their

[032-210]fury; they even envy us those dreary and terrible abodes; they are

[032-211]incessant and unremitting in their pursuit after us, endeavouring to

[032-212]chase us from among them; they lay claim to every place in which they

[032-213]can discover us with unwarranted audacity and injustice; they allege

[032-214]that the whole kingdom belongs to them of right, and that an Irishman

[032-215]has no longer a right to remain in his own country."

[032-216]After specifying in detail the proofs of these and other general

[032-217]charges, the eloquent Prince concludes by uttering the memorable vow

[032-218]that the Irish "will not cease to fight against and among their

[032-219]invaders until the day when they themselves, for want of power, shall

[032-220]have ceased to do us harm, and that a Supreme Judge shall have taken

[032-221]just vengeance on their crimes, which we firmly hope will sooner or

[032-222]later come to pass."
