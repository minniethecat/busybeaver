CHAPTER III.

[003-001]CHRISTIANITY PREACHED AT TARA—THE RESULT.

[003-002]The conversion of a Pagan people to Christianity must always be a

[003-003]primary fact in their history. It is not merely for the error it

[003-004]abolishes or the positive truth it establishes that a national change

[003-005]of faith is historically important, but for the complete revolution it

[003-006]works in every public and private relation. The change socially could

[003-007]not be greater if we were to see some irresistible apostle of Paganism

[003-008]ariving from abroad in Christian Ireland, who would abolish the

[003-009]churches, convents, and Christian schools; decry and bring into utter

[003-010]disuse the decalogue, the Scriptures and the Sacraments; efface all

[003-011]trace of the existing belief in One God and Three Persons, whether in

[003-012]private or public worship, in contracts, or in courts of law; and

[003-013]instead of these, re-establish all over the country, in high places and

[003-014]in every place, the gloomy groves of the Druids, making gods of the sun

[003-015]and moon, the natural elements, and man's own passions, restoring human

[003-016]sacrifices as a sacred duty, and practically excluding from the

[003-017]community of their fellows, all who presumed to question the divine

[003-018]origin of such a religion. The preaching of Patrick effected a

[003-019]revolution to the full as complete as such a counter-revolution in

[003-020]favour of Paganism could possibly be, and to this thorough revolution

[003-021]we must devote at least one chapter before going farther.

[003-022]The best accounts agree that Patrick was a native of Gaul, then subject

[003-023]to Rome; that he was carried captive into Erin on one of King Nial's

[003-024]returning expeditions; that he became a slave, as all captives of the

[003-025]sword did, in those iron times; that he fell to the lot of one Milcho,

[003-026]a chief of Dalriada, whose flocks he tended for seven years, as a

[003-027]shepherd, on the mountain called Slemish, in the present county of

[003-028]Antrim. The date of Nial's death, and the consequent return of his last

[003-029]expedition, is set down in all our annals at the year 405; as Patrick

[003-030]was sixteen years of age when he reached Ireland, he must have been

[003-031]born about the year 390; and as he died in the year 493, he would thus

[003-032]have reached the extraordinary, but not impossible age of 103 years.

[003-033]Whatever the exact number of his years, it is certain that his mission

[003-034]in Ireland commenced in the year 432, and was prolonged till his death,

[003-035]sixty-one years afterwards. Such an unprecedented length of life, not

[003-036]less than the unprecedented power, both popular and political, which he

[003-037]early attained, enabled him to establish the Irish Church, during his

[003-038]own time, on a basis so broad and deep, that neither lapse of ages, nor

[003-039]heathen rage, nor earthly temptations, nor all the arts of Hell, have

[003-040]been able to upheave its firm foundations. But we must not imagine that

[003-041]the powers of darkness abandoned the field without a struggle, or that

[003-042]the victory of the cross was achieved without a singular combination of

[003-043]courage, prudence, and determination—God aiding above all.

[003-045]If the year of his captivity was 405 or 406, and that of his escape or

[003-046]manumission seven years later (412 or 413), twenty years would

[003-047]intervene between his departure out of the land of his bondage, and his

[003-048]return to it clothed with the character and authority of a Christian

[003-049]Bishop. This interval, longer or shorter, he spent in qualifying

[003-050]himself for Holy Orders or discharging priestly duties at Tours, at

[003-051]Lerins, and finally at Rome. But always by night and day he was haunted

[003-052]by the thought of the Pagan nation in which he had spent his long years

[003-053]of servitude, whose language he had acquired, and the character of

[003-054]whose people he so thoroughly understood. These natural retrospections

[003-055]were heightened and deepened by supernatural revelations of the will of

[003-056]Providence towards the Irish, and himself as their apostle. At one

[003-057]time, an angel presented him, in his sleep, a scroll bearing the

[003-058]superscription, "the voice of the Irish;" at another, he seemed to hear

[003-059]in a dream all the unborn children of the nation crying to him for help

[003-060]and holy baptism. When, therefore, Pope Celestine commissioned him for

[003-061]this enterprise, "to the ends of the earth," he found him not only

[003-062]ready but anxious to undertake it.

[003-063]When the new Preacher arrived in the Irish Sea, in 432, he and his

[003-064]companions were driven off the coast of Wicklow by a mob, who assailed

[003-065]them with showers of stones. Running down the coast to Antrim, with

[003-066]which he was personally familiar, he made some stay at Saul, in Down,

[003-067]where he made few converts, and celebrated Mass in a barn; proceeding

[003-068]northward he found himself rejected with scorn by his old master,

[003-069]Milcho, of Slemish. No doubt it appeared an unpardonable audacity in

[003-070]the eyes of the proud Pagan, that his former slave should attempt to

[003-071]teach him how to reform his life and order his affairs. Returning again

[003-072]southward, led on, as we must believe, by the Spirit of God, he

[003-073]determined to strike a blow against Paganism at its most vital point.

[003-074]Having learned that the monarch, Leary (_Laeghaire_), was to celebrate

[003-075]his birthday with suitable rejoicings at Tara, on a day which happened

[003-076]to fall on the eve of Easter, he resolved to proceed to Tara on that

[003-077]occasion, and to confront the Druids in the midst of all the princes

[003-078]and magnates of the Island. With this view he returned on his former

[003-079]course, and landed from his frail barque at the mouth of the Boyne.

[003-080]Taking leave of the boatmen, he desired them to wait for him a certain

[003-081]number of days, when, if they did not hear from him, they might

[003-082]conclude him dead, and provide for their own safety. So saying he set

[003-083]out, accompanied by the few disciples he had made, or brought from

[003-084]abroad, to traverse on foot the great plain which stretches from the

[003-085]mouth of the Boyne to Tara. If those sailors were Christians, as is

[003-086]most likely, we can conceive with what anxiety they must have awaited

[003-087]tidings of an attempt so hazardous and so eventful.

[003-088]The Christian proceeded on his way, and the first night of his journey

[003-089]lodged with a hospitable chief, whose family he converted and baptized,

[003-090]especially marking out a fine child named Beanen, called by him

[003-091]Benignus, from his sweet disposition; who was destined to be one of his

[003-092]most efficient coadjutors, and finally his successor in the Primatial

[003-093]see of Armagh. It was about the second or third day when, travelling

[003-094]probably by the northern road, poetically called "the Slope of the

[003-095]Chariots," the Christian adventurers came in sight of the roofs of

[003-096]Tara. Halting on a neighbouring eminence they surveyed the citadel of

[003-097]Ancient Error, like soldiers about to assault an enemy's stronghold.

[003-098]The aspect of the royal hill must have been highly imposing. The

[003-099]building towards the north was the Banquet Hall, then thronged with the

[003-100]celebrants of the King's birth-day, measuring from north to south 360

[003-101]feet in length by 40 feet wide. South of this hall was the King's Rath,

[003-102]or residence, enclosing an area of 280 yards in diameter, and including

[003-103]several detached buildings, such as the house of Cormac, and the house

[003-104]of the hostages. Southward still stood the new rath of the reigning

[003-105]king, and yet farther south, the rath of Queen Mab, probably

[003-106]uninhabited even then. The intervals between the buildings were at some

[003-107]points planted, for we know that magnificent trees shaded the well of

[003-108]Finn, and the well of Newnaw, from which all the raths were supplied

[003-109]with water. Imposing at any time, Tara must have looked its best at the

[003-110]moment Patrick first beheld it, being in the pleasant season of spring,

[003-111]and decorated in honour of the anniversary of the reigning sovereign.

[003-112]One of the religious ceremonies employed by the Druids to heighten the

[003-113]solemnity of the occasion, was to order all the fires of Tara and Meath

[003-114]to be quenched, in order to rekindle them instantaneously from a sacred

[003-115]fire dedicated to the honour of their god. But Patrick, either

[003-116]designedly or innocently, anticipated this striking ceremony, and lit

[003-117]his own fire, where he had encamped, in view of the royal residence. A

[003-118]flight of fiery arrows, shot into the Banqueting Hall, would not have

[003-119]excited more horror and tumult among the company there assembled, than

[003-120]did the sight of that unlicensed blaze in the distance. Orders were

[003-121]issued to drag the offender against the laws and the gods of the Island

[003-122]before them, and the punishment in store for him was already decreed in

[003-123]every heart. The Preacher, followed by his trembling disciples,

[003-124]ascended "the Slope of the Chariots," surrounded by menacing minions of

[003-125]the Pagan law, and regarded with indignation by astonished spectators.

[003-126]As he came he recited Latin Prayers to the Blessed Trinity, beseeching

[003-127]their protection and direction in this trying hour. Contrary to

[003-128]courteous custom no one at first rose to offer him a seat. At last a

[003-129]chieftain, touched with mysterious admiration for the stranger, did him

[003-130]that kindness. Then it was demanded of him, why he had dared to violate

[003-131]the laws of the country, and to defy its ancient gods. On this text the

[003-132]Christian Missionary spoke. The place of audience was in the open air,

[003-133]on that eminence, the home of so many kings, which commands one of the

[003-134]most agreeable prospects in any landscape. The eye of the inspired

[003-135]orator, pleading the cause of all the souls that hereafter, till the

[003-136]end of time, might inhabit the land, could discern within the

[003-137]spring-day horizon, the course of the Blackwater and the Boyne before

[003-138]they blend into one; the hills of Cavan to the far north; with the

[003-139]royal hill of Tailtean in the foreground; the wooded heights of Slane

[003-140]and Skreen, and the four ancient roads, which led away towards the four

[003-141]subject Provinces, like the reins of empire laid loosely on their

[003-142]necks. Since the first Apostle of the Gentiles had confronted the

[003-143]subtle Paganism of Athens, on the hill of Mars, none of those who

[003-144]walked in his steps ever stood out in more glorious relief than

[003-145]Patrick, surrounded by Pagan Princes, and a Pagan Priesthood, on the

[003-146]hill of Tara.

[003-147]The defence of the fire he had kindled, unlicensed, soon extended into

[003-148]wider issues. Who were the gods against whom he had offended? Were they

[003-149]true gods or false? They had their priests: could they maintain the

[003-150]divinity of such gods, by argument, or by miracle? For his God, he,

[003-151]though unworthy, was ready to answer, yea, right ready to die. His God

[003-152]had become man, and had died for man. His name alone was sufficient to

[003-153]heal all diseases; to raise the very dead to life. Such, we learn from

[003-154]the old biographers, was the line of Patrick's argument. This sermon

[003-155]ushered in a controversy. The king's guests, who had come to feast and

[003-156]rejoice, remained to listen and to meditate. With the impetuosity of

[003-157]the national character—with all its passion for debate—they rushed into

[003-158]this new conflict, some on one side, some on the other. The daughters

[003-159]of the king and many others—the Arch-Druid himself—became convinced and

[003-160]were baptized. The missionaries obtained powerful protectors, and the

[003-161]king assigned to Patrick the pleasant fort of Trim, as a present

[003-162]residence. From that convenient distance, he could readily return at

[003-163]any moment, to converse with the king's guests and the members of his

[003-164]household.

[003-165]The Druidical superstition never recovered the blow it received that

[003-166]day at Tara. The conversion of the Arch-Druid and the Princesses, was,

[003-167]of itself, their knell of doom. Yet they held their ground during the

[003-168]remainder of this reign—twenty-five years longer (A.D. 458). The king

[003-169]himself never became a Christian, though he tolerated the missionaries,

[003-170]and deferred more and more every year to the Christian party. He

[003-171]sanctioned an expurgated code of the laws, prepared under the direction

[003-172]of Patrick, from which every positive element of Paganism was rigidly

[003-173]excluded. He saw, unopposed, the chief idol of his race, overthrown on

[003-174]"the Plain of Prostration," at Sletty. Yet withal he never consented to

[003-175]be baptized; and only two years before his decease, we find him

[003-176]swearing to a treaty, in the old Pagan form—"by the Sun, and the Wind,

[003-177]and all the Elements." The party of the Druids at first sought to stay

[003-178]the progress of Christianity by violence, and even attempted, more than

[003-179]once, to assassinate Patrick. Finding these means ineffectual they

[003-180]tried ridicule and satire. In this they were for some time seconded by

[003-181]the Bards, men warmly attached to their goddess of song and their lives

[003-182]of self-indulgence. All in vain. The day of the idols was fast verging

[003-183]into everlasting night in Erin. Patrick and his disciples were

[003-184]advancing from conquest to conquest. Armagh and Cashel came in the wake

[003-185]of Tara, and Cruachan was soon to follow. Driven from the high places,

[003-186]the obdurate Priests of Bel took refuge in the depths of the forest and

[003-187]in the islands of the sea, wherein the Christian anchorites of the next

[003-188]age were to replace them. The social revolution proceeded, but all that

[003-189]was tolerable in the old state of things, Patrick carefully engrafted

[003-190]with the new. He allowed much for the habits and traditions of the

[003-191]people, and so made the transition as easy, from darkness into the

[003-192]light, as Nature makes the transition from night to morning. He seven

[003-193]times visited in person every mission in the kingdom, performing the

[003-194]six first "circuits" on foot, but the seventh, on account of his

[003-195]extreme age, he was borne in a chariot. The pious munificence of the

[003-196]successors of Leary, had surrounded him with a household of princely

[003-197]proportions. Twenty-four persons, mostly ecclesiastics, were chosen for

[003-198]this purpose: a bell-ringer, a psalmist, a cook, a brewer, a

[003-199]chamberlain, three smiths, three artificers, and three embroiderers are

[003-200]reckoned of the number. These last must be considered as employed in

[003-201]furnishing the interior of the new churches. A scribe, a shepherd to

[003-202]guard his flocks, and a charioteer are also mentioned, and their proper

[003-203]names given. How different this following from the little boat's crew,

[003-204]he had left waiting tidings from Tara, in such painful apprehension, at

[003-205]the mouth of the Boyne, in 432. Apostolic zeal, and unrelaxed

[003-206]discipline had wrought these wonders, during a lifetime prolonged far

[003-207]beyond the ordinary age of man.

[003-208]The fifth century was drawing to a close, and the days of Patrick were

[003-209]numbered. Pharamond and the Franks had sway on the Netherlands; Hengist

[003-210]and the Saxons on South Britain; Clovis had led his countrymen across

[003-211]the Rhine into Gaul; the Vandals had established themselves in Spain

[003-212]and North Africa; the Ostrogoths were supreme in Italy. The empire of

[003-213]barbarism had succeeded to the empire of Polytheism; dense darkness

[003-214]covered the semi-Christian countries of the old Roman empire, but

[003-215]happily daylight still lingered in the West. Patrick, in good season,

[003-216]had done his work. And as sometimes, God seems to bring round His ends,

[003-217]contrary to the natural order of things, so the spiritual sun of Europe

[003-218]was now destined to rise in the West, and return on its light-bearing

[003-219]errand towards the East, dispelling in its path, Saxon, Frankish, and

[003-220]German darkness, until at length it reflected back on Rome herself, the

[003-221]light derived from Rome.

[003-223]On the 17th of March, in the year of our Lord 493, Patrick breathed his

[003-224]last in the monastery of Saul, erected on the site of that barn where

[003-225]he had first said Mass. He was buried with national honours in the

[003-226]Church of Armagh, to which he had given the Primacy over all the

[003-227]churches of Ireland; and such was the concourse of mourners, and the

[003-228]number of Masses offered for his eternal repose, that from the day of

[003-229]his death till the close of the year, the sun is poetically said never

[003-230]to have set—so brilliant and so continual was the glare of tapers and

[003-231]torches.
