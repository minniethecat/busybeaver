## Overview

Simon Butler (c. 1757–1797) was an Irish barrister and a founding leader of the Dublin Society of United Irishmen. As the first chairman of the Dublin branch, he was among the "new generation of Protestant patriots" who allied themselves with Catholic demands for equal civil rights. (098-019)

His imprisonment by the House of Lords in 1792 made him an early martyr for the cause of parliamentary reform in Ireland. (100-050)

## Early Life and the Volunteer Movement

Butler came of age during the Volunteer movement of the late 1770s and early 1780s. "They had contributed to the ranks and expenses of the Volunteers; they had swelled the chorus of Grattan's triumph." The Volunteers became the vehicle through which the Protestant middle class asserted its demand for legislative independence. (098-019)

Butler established himself as a leading barrister in Dublin after legislative independence was achieved in 1782. The limited nature of that reform, which left Parliament under a narrow Protestant oligarchy, turned many Volunteers toward the cause of more thoroughgoing constitutional change. (098-019)

## Founding of the Dublin United Irishmen

In the autumn of 1791, Theobald Wolfe Tone returned from Belfast to Dublin with a plan for a new political society. In November, a branch of the United Irishmen was formed in the capital. The Hon. Simon Butler, a leading barrister, was chosen Chairman, and Mr. Napper Tandy was Secretary. (100-050)

The Dublin branch adopted a solemn declaration, drafted by Tone, binding every member "to forward a brotherhood of affection, an identity of interests, a communion of rights, and a union of power among Irishmen of all religious persuasions." This became the universal bond of organization for the movement. (100-050)

> The Hon. Simon Butler, a leading barrister, was chosen Chairman, and Mr. Napper Tandy, an active middle-aged merchant, with strong republican principles, was Secretary. [100-050]

## Persecution and Imprisonment (1792)

The Dublin Society had originally conducted its proceedings openly. However, the freedom of the press and of public discussion "had been repeatedly and frequently violated" by the authorities. The Society adopted secrecy as their only shield. (100-068)

In 1792, the government struck directly at the leadership. Simon Butler and Oliver Bond were summoned to the bar of the House of Lords and condemned to six months' imprisonment with a fine of 500 pounds each. (100-068)

The charge was that they had acted as Chairman and Secretary of a meeting that adopted an address reflecting on the corrupt constitution of Parliament. (100-068)

> In 1792, Simon Butler and Oliver Bond, a prosperous Dublin merchant of northern origin, were summoned to the bar of the House of Lords, condemned to six months' imprisonment, and fined 500 pounds each. [100-068]

The imprisonment of Butler and Bond was a pivotal moment. What had begun as a constitutional reform society was, under government repression, driven toward secrecy, republicanism, and ultimately revolutionary conspiracy. (100-068)

## Later Years

After his release, Butler withdrew from active leadership. The movement continued to radicalize in a direction he could not fully embrace. Others of the original Dublin leadership — Napper Tandy, Archibald Hamilton Rowan, and Wolfe Tone — moved toward French alliance and revolutionary separatism. Butler did not live to see the Rebellion of 1798. (100-068)

## Legacy

Simon Butler represents the early, open phase of the United Irish movement, when the demand was for parliamentary reform rather than separation from the Crown. (098-019)

His imprisonment alongside Oliver Bond demonstrated the lengths to which the Ascendancy government would go to suppress peaceful demands for change. He is remembered as a pioneer who paid the price for principles that later generations took up through both constitutional and revolutionary means. (100-050)
