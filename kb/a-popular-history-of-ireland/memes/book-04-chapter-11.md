CHAPTER XI.

[033-001]RETROSPECT OF THE NORMAN PERIOD IN IRELAND—A GLANCE AT THE MILITARY

[033-002]TACTICS OF THE TIMES—NO CONQUEST OF THE COUNTRY IN THE THIRTEENTH

[033-003]CENTURY.

[033-004]Though the victorious and protracted career of Richard de Burgh, the

[033-005]"Red Earl" of Ulster, might, without overstraining, be included in the

[033-006]Norman period, yet, as introductory to the memorable advent and

[033-007]election of King Edward Bruce, we must leave it for the succeeding

[033-008]book. Having brought down the narrative, as regards all the provinces,

[033-009]to the end of the first century, from the invasion, we must now cast a

[033-010]backward glance on the events of that hundred years before passing into

[033-011]the presence of other times and new combinations.

[033-012]"There were," says _Giraldus Cambriensis_, "three sundry sorts of

[033-013]servitors which served in the realm of Ireland, Normans, Englishmen,

[033-014]and the Cambrians, which were the first conquerors of the land: the

[033-015]first were in most credit and estimation, the second next, but the last

[033-016]were not accounted or regarded of." "The Normans," adds the author,

[033-017]"were very fine in their apparel, and delicate in their diets; they

[033-018]could not feed but upon dainties, neither could their meat digest

[033-019]without wine at each meal; yet would they not serve in the marches or

[033-020]any remote place against the enemy, neither would they lie in garrison

[033-021]to keep any remote castle or fort, but, would be still about their

[033-022]lord's side to serve and guard his person; they would be where they

[033-023]might be full and have plenty; they could talk and brag, swear, and

[033-024]stare, and, standing in their own reputation, disdain all others." This

[033-025]is rather the language of a partizan than of an historian; of one who

[033-026]felt and spoke for those, his own kinsmen many of them, who, he

[033-027]complains, although the first to enter on the conquest, were yet held

[033-028]in contempt and disdain, "and only new-comers called to council."

[033-029]The Normans were certainly the captains in every campaign from Robert

[033-030]Fitzstephen to Stephen de Longespay. They made the war, and they

[033-031]maintained it. In the rank and file, and even among the knighthood, men

[033-032]of pure Welsh, English, and Flemish and Danish blood, may be singled

[033-033]out, but each host was marshalled by Norman skill, and every defeat was

[033-034]borne with Norman fortitude. It may seem strange, then, that these

[033-035]greatest masters of the art of war, as waged in the middle ages,

[033-036]invincible in England, France, Italy, and the East, should, after a

[033-037]hundred years, be no nearer to the conquest of Ireland than they were

[033-038]at the end of the tenth year.

[033-040]The main causes of the fluctuations of the war were, no doubt, the

[033-041]divided military command, and the frequent change of their civil

[033-042]authorities. They had never marched or colonized before without their

[033-043]Duke or King at their head, and in their midst. One supreme chief was

[033-044]necessary to keep to any common purpose the minds of so many proud,

[033-045]intractable nobles. The feuds of the de Lacys with the Marshals, of the

[033-046]Geraldines with the de Burghs, broke out periodically during the

[033-047]thirteenth century, and were naturally seized upon, by the Irish as

[033-048]opportunities for attacking either or both. The secondary nobles and

[033-049]all the adventurers understood their danger and its cause, when they

[033-050]petitioned Henry II. and Henry III. so often and so urgently as they

[033-051]did, that a member of the royal family might reside permanently in

[033-052]Ireland, to exercise the supreme authority, military and civil.

[033-053]The civil administration of the colonists passing into different hands

[033-054]every three or four years, suffered from the absence of permanent

[033-055]authority. The law of the marches was, of necessity, the law of the

[033-056]strong hand, and no other. But _Cambrensis_, whose personal prejudices

[033-057]are not involved in this fact, describes the walled towns as filled

[033-058]with litigation in his time. "There was," he says, "such _lawing_ and

[033-059]vexation, that the veteran was more troubled in _lawing_ within the

[033-060]town than he was in peril at large with the enemy." This being the

[033-061]case, we must take with great caution the bold assertions so often made

[033-062]of the zeal with which the natives petitioned the Henrys and Edwards

[033-063]that the law of England might be extended to them. Certain Celts whose

[033-064]lands lay within or upon the marches, others who compounded with their

[033-065]Norman invaders, a chief or prince, hard pressed by domestic enemies,

[033-066]may have wished to be in a position to quote Norman law against Norman

[033-067]spoilers, but the popular petitions which went to England, beseeching

[033-068]the extension of its laws to Ireland, went only from the townsmen of

[033-069]Dublin, and the new settlers in Leinster or Meath, harassed and

[033-070]impoverished by the arbitrary jurisdiction of manorial courts, from

[033-071]which they had no appeal. The great mass of the Irish remained as

[033-072]warmly attached to their Brehon code down to the seventeenth century as

[033-073]they were before the invasion of Norman or Dane. It may sound barbarous

[033-074]to our ears that, according to that code, murder should be compounded

[033-075]by an _eric_, or fine; that putting out the eyes should be the usual

[033-076]punishment of treason; that maiming should be judiciously inflicted for

[033-077]sundry offences; and that the land of a whole clan should be equally

[033-078]shared between the free members of that clan. We are not yet in a

[033-079]position to form an intelligent opinion upon the primitive

[033-080]jurisprudence of our ancestors, but the system itself could not have

[033-081]been very vicious which nourished in the governed such a thirst for

[033-082]justice, that, according to one of their earliest English law

[033-083]reformers, they were anxious for its execution, even against

[033-084]themselves.

[033-085]The distinction made in the courts of the adventurers against natives

[033-086]of the soil, even when long domiciled within their borders, was of

[033-087]itself a sufficient cause of war between the races. In the eloquent

[033-088]letter of the O'Neil to Pope John XXII.—written about the year 1318—we

[033-089]read, that no man of Irish origin could sue in an English court; that

[033-090]no Irishman, within the marches, could make a legal will; that his

[033-091]property was appropriated by his English neighbours; and that the

[033-092]murder of an Irishman was not even a felony punishable by fine. This

[033-093]latter charge would appear incredible, if we had not the record of more

[033-094]than one case where the homicide justified his act by the plea that his

[033-095]victim was a mere native, and where the plea was held good and

[033-096]sufficient.

[033-097]A very vivid picture of Hiberno-Norman town-life in those days is

[033-098]presented to us in an old poem, on the "Entrenchment of the Town of

[033-099]Ross," in the year 1265. We have there the various trades and

[033-100]crafts-mariners, coat-makers, fullers, cloth-dyers and sellers,

[033-101]butchers, cordwainers, tanners, hucksters, smiths, masons, carpenters,

[033-102]arranged by guilds, and marching to the sound of flute and tabor, under

[033-103]banners bearing a fish and platter, a painted ship, and other "rare

[033-104]devices." On the walls, when finished, cross-bows hung, with store of

[033-105]arrows ready to shoot; when the city horn sounded twice, burgess and

[033-106]bachelor vied with each other in warlike haste. In time of peace the

[033-107]stranger was always welcome in the streets; he was free to buy and sell

[033-108]without toll or tax, and to admire the fair dames who walked the quiet

[033-109]ramparts, clad in mantles of green, or russet, or scarlet. Such is the

[033-110]poetic picture of the town of Ross in the thirteenth century; the poem

[033-111]itself is written in Norman-French, though evidently intended for

[033-112]popular use, and the author is called "Friar Michael of Kildare." It is

[033-113]pretty evident from this instance, which is not singular, that a

[033-114]century after the first invasion, the French language was still the

[033-115]speech of part, if not the majority, of these Hiberno-Norman townsmen.

[033-116]So walls, and laws, and language arose, a triple barrier between the

[033-117]races. That common religion which might be expected to form a strong

[033-118]bond between them had itself to adopt a twofold organization.

[033-119]Distinctions of nationality were carried into the Sanctuary and into

[033-120]the Cloister. The historian _Giraldus_, in preaching at Dublin against

[033-121]the alleged vices of the native Clergy, sounded the first note of a

[033-122]long and bitter controversy. He was promptly answered from the same

[033-123]pulpit on the next occasion by Albin O'Mulloy, the patriot Abbot of

[033-124]Baltinglass. In one of the early Courts or Parliaments of the

[033-125]Adventurers, they decreed that no Monastery in those districts of which

[033-126]they had possession, should admit any but natives of England, as

[033-127]novices,—a rule which, according to O'Neil's letter, was faithfully

[033-128]acted upon by English Dominicans, Franciscans, Benedictines, and

[033-129]regular canons. Some of the great Cistercian houses on the marches, in

[033-130]which the native religious predominated, adopted a retaliatory rule,

[033-131]for which they were severely censured by the general Chapter of their

[033-132]Order. But the length to which this feud was carried may be imagined by

[033-133]the sweeping charge O'Neil brings against "Brother Symon, a relative of

[033-134]the Bishop of Coventry," and other religious of his nation, who openly

[033-135]maintained, he says, that the killing of a mere Irishman was no murder.

[033-136]When this was the feeling on one side, or was believed to be the

[033-137]feeling, we cannot wonder that the war should have been renewed as

[033-138]regularly as the seasons. No sooner was the husbandman in the field

[033-139]than the knight was upon the road. Some peculiarities of the wars of

[033-140]those days gleam out at intervals through the methodic indifference to

[033-141]detail of the old annals, and reveal to us curious conditions of

[033-142]society. In the Irish country, where castle-building was but slowly

[033-143]introduced, we see, for example, that the usual storage for provisions,

[033-144]in time of war, was in churches and churchyards. Thus de Burgh, in his

[033-145]expedition to Mayo, in 1236, "left neither rick nor basket of corn in

[033-146]the large churchyard of Mayo, or in the yard of the Church of Saint

[033-147]Michael the Archangel, and carried away eighty baskets out of the

[033-148]churches themselves." When we read, therefore, as we frequently do, of

[033-149]both Irish and Normans plundering churches in the land of their

[033-150]enemies, we are not to suppose the plunder of the sanctuary. Popularly

[033-151]this seizing the supplies of an enemy on consecrated ground was

[033-152]considered next to sacrilege; and well it was for the fugitives in the

[033-153]sanctuary in those iron times that it should be so considered. Yet not

[033-154]the less is it necessary for us to distinguish a high-handed military

[033-155]measure from actual sacrilege, for which there can be no apology, and

[033-156]hardly any earthly atonement.

[033-157]In their first campaigns the Irish had one great advantage over the

[033-158]Normans in their familiarity with the country. This helped them to

[033-159]their first victories. But when the invaders were able to set up rival

[033-160]houses against each other, and to secure the co-operation of natives,

[033-161]the advantage was soon equalized. Great importance was attached to the

[033-162]intelligence and good faith of the guides, who accompanied every army,

[033-163]and were personally consulted by the leaders in determining their

[033-164]march. A country so thickly studded with the ancient forest, and so

[033-165]netted with rivers (then of much greater volume than since they have

[033-166]been stripped of their guardian woods), afforded constant occasion for

[033-167]the display of minute local knowledge. To miss a pass or to find a ford

[033-168]might determine a campaign, almost as much as the skill of the chief,

[033-169]or the courage of the battalion.

[033-170]The Irish depended for their knowledge of the English towns and castles

[033-171]on their daring _spies_, who continually risked their necks in

[033-172]acquiring for their clansmen such needful information. This perilous

[033-173]duty, when undertaken by a native for the benefit of his country, was

[033-174]justly accounted highly honourable. Proud poets, educated in all the

[033-175]mysteries of their art, and even men of chieftain rank, did not

[033-176]hesitate to assume disguises and act the patriot spy. One of the most

[033-177]celebrated spies of this century was Donogh Fitzpatrick, son of the

[033-178]Lord of Ossory, who was slain by the English in 1250. He was said to be

[033-179]"one of the three men" most feared by the English in his day. "He was

[033-180]in the habit of going about to reconnoitre their market towns," say the

[033-181]Annalists, "in various disguises." An old quatrain gives us a list of

[033-182]some of the parts he played when in the towns of his enemies—

[033-183]"He is a carpenter, he is a turner.

[033-184]My nursling is a bookman.

[033-185]He is selling wine and hides

[033-186]Where he sees a gathering."

[033-187]An able captain, as well as an intrepid spy, he met his fate in acting

[033-188]out his favourite part, "which," adds our justice-loving Four Masters,

[033-189]"was a retaliation due to the English, for, up to that time, he had

[033-190]killed, burned, and destroyed many of them."

[033-191]Of the equipments and tactics of the belligerents we get from our

[033-192]Annals but scanty details. The Norman battalion, according to the usage

[033-193]of that people, led by the marshal of the field, charged, after the

[033-194]archers had delivered their fire. But these wars had bred a new mounted

[033-195]force, called hobiler-archers, who were found so effective that they

[033-196]were adopted into all the armies of Europe. Although the bow was never

[033-197]a favourite weapon with the Irish, particular tribes seem to have been

[033-198]noted for its use. We hear in the campaigns of this century of the

[033-199]archers of Breffni, and we may probably interpret as referring to the

[033-200]same weapon, Felim O'Conor's order to his men, in his combat with the

[033-201]sons of Roderick at Drumraitte (1237), "not to shoot but to come to a

[033-202]close fight." It is possible, however, that this order may have

[033-203]reference to the old Irish weapon, the javelin or dart. The pike, the

[033-204]battle-axe, the sword, and skein, or dagger, both parties had in

[033-205]common, though their construction was different. The favourite

[033-206]tactique, on both sides, seems to have been the old military expedient

[033-207]of outflanking an enemy, and attacking him simultaneously in front and

[033-208]rear. Thus, in the year 1225, in one of the combats of the O'Conors,

[033-209]when the son of Cathal _Crovdearg_ endeavoured to surround Turlogh

[033-210]O'Conor, the latter ordered his recruits to the van, and Donn Oge

[033-211]Magheraty, with some Tyronian and other soldiers to cover the rear, "by

[033-212]which means they escaped without the loss of a man." The flank movement

[033-213]by which the Lord Justice Fitzgerald carried the passage of the Erne

[033-214](A.D. 1247) against O'Donnell, according to the Annalists, was

[033-215]suggested to Fitzgerald by Cormac, the grandson of Roderick O'Conor. By

[033-216]that period in their intercourse the Normans and Irish had fought so

[033-217]often together that their stock of tactical knowledge must have been,

[033-218]from experience, very much common property. In the eyes of the Irish

[033-219]chiefs and chroniclers, the foreign soldiers who served with them were

[033-220]but hired mercenaries. They were sometimes repaid by the plunder of the

[033-221]country attacked, but usually they received fixed wages for the length

[033-222]of time they entered. "Hostages for the payment of wages" are

[033-223]frequently referred to, as given by native nobles to these foreign

[033-224]auxiliaries. The chief expedient for subsisting an army was driving

[033-225]before them herds and flocks; free quarters for men and horses were

[033-226]supplied by the tenants of allied chiefs within their territory, and

[033-227]for the rest, the simple outfit was probably not very unlike that of

[033-228]the Scottish borderers described by Froissart, who cooked the cattle

[033-229]they captured in their skins, carrying a broad plate of metal and a

[033-230]little bag of oatmeal trussed up behind the saddle.

[033-232]One inveterate habit clung to the ancient race, even until long after

[033-233]the times of which we now speak—their unconquerable prejudice against

[033-234]defensive armour. Gilbride McNamee, the laureate to King Brian O'Neil,

[033-235]gives due prominence to this fact in his poem on the death of his

[033-236]patron in the battle of Down (A.D. 1260). Thus sings the northern bard—

[033-237]"The foreigners from London,

[033-238]The hosts from Port-Largy *

[033-239]Came in a bright green body,

[033-240]In gold and iron armour.

[033-241]"Unequal they engage in the battle,

[033-242]The foreigners and the Gael of Tara,

[033-243]_Fine linen shirts on the race of Conn_,

[033-244]And the strangers _one mass of iron_."

[033-245][Footnote: Port-Largy, Waterford.]

[033-246]With what courage they fought, these scorners of armour, their

[033-247]victories of Ennis, of Callanglen, and of Credran, as well as their

[033-248]defeats at the Erne and at Down, amply testify. The first hundred years

[033-249]of war for native land, with their new foes, had passed over, and

[033-250]three-fourths of the _Saer Clanna_ were still as free as they had ever

[033-251]been. It was not reserved even for the Norman race—the conquest of

[033-252]Innisfail!
