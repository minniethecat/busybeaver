CHAPTER XIII.

[100-001]THE UNITED IRISHMEN.

[100-002]Half measures of justice may satisfy the generation which achieves

[100-003]them, but their successors will look with other eyes, as well on what

[100-004]has been won as on that which is withheld. The part in possession will

[100-005]appear to their youthful sense of abstract right and wrong far less

[100-006]precious than the part in expectancy, for it is in the nature of the

[100-007]young to look forward, as it is of the old to turn their regards to the

[100-008]past. The very recollection of their fathers will stimulate the new

[100-009]generation to emulate their example, and will render them averse to

[100-010]being bound by former compromises. So necessary is it for statesmen,

[100-011]when they yield to a just demand long withheld, to yield gracefully and

[100-012]to yield all that is fairly due.

[100-014]The celebrated group known to us as "the United Irishmen," were the

[100-015]birth of a new generation, entering together on the public stage. With

[100-016]few exceptions, the leading characters were all born within a few years

[100-017]of each other: Neilson in 1761, Tone, Arthur O'Conor and Lord Edward

[100-018]Fitzgerald in '62, McNevin in '63, Sampson and Thomas Addis Emmet in

[100-019]'64, and Russell in '67. They had emerged into manhood while the drums

[100-020]of the Volunteers were beating victorious marches, when the public

[100-021]hopes ran high, and the language of patriotism was the familiar speech

[100-022]of every-day life.

[100-023]In a settled state of society it would have been natural for the first

[100-024]minds of the new generation to carry their talents, gratefully and

[100-025]dutifully, into the service of the first reputations of the old; but

[100-026]Irish society, in the last years of the last century, was not in a

[100-027]settled condition; the fascination of French example, and the goading

[100-028]sense of national wrongs only half-righted, inflamed the younger

[100-029]generation with a passionate thirst for speedy and summary justice on

[100-030]their oppressors. We must not look, therefore, to see the Tones and

[100-031]Emmets continuing in the constitutional line of public conduct marked

[100-032]out by Burke in the one kingdom, and Grattan in the other. The new age

[100-033]was revolutionary, and the new men were filled with the spirit of the

[100-034]age. Their actions stand apart; they form an episode in the history of

[100-035]the century to which there may be parallels, but a chapter in the

[100-036]history of their own country original and alone.

[100-037]The United Irish Society sprung up at Belfast in October, 1791. In that

[100-038]month, Theobold Wolf Tone, then in his 28th year, a native of Kildare,

[100-039]a member of the bar, and an excellent popular pamphleteer, on a visit

[100-040]to his friend Thomas Russell, in the northern capital, was introduced

[100-041]to Samuel Neilson, proprietor of the _Northern Star_ newspaper, and

[100-042]several other kindred spirits, all staunch reformers, or "something

[100-043]more." Twenty of these gentlemen meeting together, adopted a programme

[100-044]prepared by Tone, which contained these three simple propositions: that

[100-045]"English influence" was the great danger of Irish liberty; that a

[100-046]reform of Parliament could alone create a counterpoise to that

[100-047]influence; and that such a reform to be just should include Irishmen of

[100-048]all religious denominations. On Tone's return to Dublin, early in

[100-049]November, a branch society was formed on the Belfast basis. The Hon.

[100-050]Simon Butler, a leading barrister, was chosen Chairman, and Mr. Napper

[100-051]Tandy, an active middle-aged merchant, with strong republican

[100-052]principles, was Secretary. The solemn declaration or oath, binding

[100-053]every member "to forward a brotherhood of affection, an identity of

[100-054]interests, a communion of rights, and a union of power among Irishmen

[100-055]of all religious persuasions," was drawn up by the Dublin club, and

[100-056]became the universal bond of organization. Though the Belfast leaders

[100-057]had been long in the habit of meeting in "secret committee," to direct

[100-058]and control the popular movements in their vicinage, the new society

[100-059]was not, in its inception, nor for three years afterwards, a secret

[100-060]society. When that radical change was proposed, we find it resisted by

[100-061]a considerable minority, who felt themselves at length compelled to

[100-062]retire from an association, the proceedings of which they could no

[100-063]longer approve. In justice to those who remained, adopting secrecy as

[100-064]their only shield, it must be said, that the freedom of the press and

[100-065]of public discussion had been repeatedly and frequently violated before

[100-066]they abandoned the original maxims and tactics of their body, which

[100-067]were all open, and above-board.

[100-068]In 1792, Simon Butler, and Oliver Bond—a prosperous Dublin merchant of

[100-069]northern origin—was summoned to the bar of the House of Lords,

[100-070]condemned to six months' imprisonment, and a fine of 500 pounds each,

[100-071]for having acted as Chairman and Secretary of one of the meetings, at

[100-072]which an address to the people, strongly reflecting on the corrupt

[100-073]constitution of Parliament, was adopted. In '94, Archibald Hamilton

[100-074]Rowan, one of the purest and most chivalrous characters of any age, was

[100-075]convicted, by a packed jury, of circulating the famous "Universal

[100-076]Emancipation" address of his friend, Dr. William Drennan, the

[100-077]poet-politician of the party. He was defended by Curran, in the still

[100-078]more famous speech in which occurs his apostrophe to "the genius of

[100-079]Universal Emancipation;" but he atoned in the cells of Newgate, for

[100-080]circulating the dangerous doctrine which Drennan had broached, and

[100-081]Curran had immortalized.

[100-082]The regular place of meeting of the Dublin society was the Tailors'

[100-083]Hall, in Back Lane, a spacious building, called, from the number of

[100-084]great popular gatherings held in it, "the Back Lane Parliament." Here

[100-085]Tandy, in the uniform of his new National Guard, whose standard bore

[100-086]the harp without the crown, addressed his passionate harangues to the

[100-087]applauding multitude; here Tone, whose _forte_, however, was not

[100-088]oratory, constantly attended; here, also, the leading Catholics, Keogh

[100-089]and McCormack, the "Gog" and "Magog," of Tone's extraordinary

[100-090]_Memoirs_, were occasionally present. And here, on the night of the 4th

[100-091]of May, 1794, the Dublin society found themselves suddenly assailed by

[100-092]the police, their papers seized, their officers who were present

[100-093]arrested, and their meeting dispersed. From that moment we may date the

[100-094]new and _secret_ organization of the brotherhood, though it was not in

[100-095]general operation till the middle of the following year.

[100-096]This new organization, besides its secrecy, had other revolutionary

[100-097]characteristics. For "reform of Parliament" was substituted in the

[100-098]test, or oath, representation "of all the people of Ireland," and for

[100-099]petitions and publications, the enrolment of men, by baronies and

[100-100]counties, and the appointment of officers, from the least to the

[100-101]highest in rank, as in a regular army. The unit was a lodge of twelve

[100-102]members, with a chairman and secretary, who were also their corporal

[100-103]and sergeant; five of these lodges formed a company, and the officers

[100-104]of five such companies a baronial committee, from which again, in like

[100-105]manner, the county committees were formed. Each of the provinces had

[100-106]its Directory, while in Dublin the supreme authority was established,

[100-107]in an "Executive Directory" of five members. The orders of the

[100-108]Executive were communicated to not more than one of the Provincial

[100-109]Directors, and by him to one of each County Committee, and so in a

[100-110]descending scale, till the rank and file were reached; an elaborate

[100-111]contrivance, but one which proved wholly insufficient to protect the

[100-112]secrets of the organization from the ubiquitous espionage of the

[100-113]government.

[100-114]In May, 1795, the new organization lost the services of Wolfe Tone, who

[100-115]was compromised by a strange incident, to a very serious extent. The

[100-116]incident was the arrest and trial of the Rev. William Jackson, an

[100-117]Anglican clergyman, who had imbibed the opinions of Price and

[100-118]Priestley, and had been sent to Ireland by the French Republic, on a

[100-119]secret embassy. Betrayed by a friend and countryman, named Cockayne,

[100-120]the unhappy Jackson took poison in prison, and expired in the dock.

[100-121]Tone had been seen with Jackson, and through the influence of his

[100-122]friends, was alone protected from arrest. He was compelled, however, to

[100-123]quit the country, in order to preserve his personal liberty. He

[100-124]proceeded with his family to Belfast, where, before taking shipping for

[100-125]America, he renewed with his first associates, their vows and projects,

[100-126]on the summit of "the Cave Hill," which looks down upon the rich valley

[100-127]of the Laggan, and the noble town and port at its outlet. Before

[100-128]quitting Dublin, he had solemnly promised Emmet and Russell, in the

[100-129]first instance, as he did his Belfast friends in the second, that he

[100-130]would make the United States his _route_ to France, where he would

[100-131]negotiate a formidable national alliance, for "the United Irishmen."

[100-132]In the year in which Tone left the country, Lord Edward Fitzgerald,

[100-133]brother of the Duke of Leinster, and formerly a Major in the British

[100-134]Army, joined the society; in the next year—near its close—Thomas Addis

[100-135]Emmet, who had long been in the confidence of the promoters, joined, as

[100-136]did, about the same time, Arthur O'Conor, nephew of Lord Longueville,

[100-137]and ex-member for Phillipstown, and Dr. William James McNevin, a

[100-138]Connaught Catholic, educated in Austria, then practising his profession

[100-139]with eminent success in Dublin. These were felt to be important

[100-140]accessions, and all four were called upon to act on "the Executive

[100-141]Directory," from time to time, during 1796 and 1797.

[100-142]The coercive legislation carried through Parliament, session after

[100-143]session—the Orange persecutions in Armagh and elsewhere—the domiciliary

[100-144]visits—the military outrages in town and country—the free quarters,

[100-145]whipping and tortures—the total suppression of the public press—the

[100-146]bitter disappointment of Lord Fitzwilliam's recall—the annual failure

[100-147]of Ponsonby's motion for reform—finally, the despairing secession of

[100-148]Grattan and his friends from Parliament—had all tended to expand the

[100-149]system, which six years before was confined to a few dozen enthusiasts

[100-150]of Belfast and Dublin, into the dimensions of a national confederacy.

[100-151]By the close of this year, 500,000 men had taken the test, in every

[100-152]part of the country, and nearly 300,000 were reported as armed, either

[100-153]with firelocks or pikes. Of this total, 110,000 alone were returned for

[100-154]Ulster; about 60,000 for Leinster, and the remainder from Connaught and

[100-155]Munster. A fund, ludicrously small, 1,400 pounds sterling, remained in

[100-156]the hands of the Executive, after all the outlay which had taken place,

[100-157]in procuring arms, in extending the union, and in defending prisoners

[100-158]arrested as members of the society. Lord Edward Fitzgerald was chosen

[100-159]Commander-in-Chief; but the main reliance, for munitions, artillery,

[100-160]and officers, was placed upon the French Republic.
