## Overview

William Wentworth-Fitzwilliam, the 2nd Earl Fitzwilliam (1748–1833), served as Lord Lieutenant of Ireland for a brief three-month period from January to March 1795. A prominent Whig politician closely connected to Edmund Burke and the Ponsonby family, Fitzwilliam's viceroyalty became one of the most consequential short-lived administrations in Irish history. His attempt to govern Ireland on the advice of Henry Grattan and advance Catholic emancipation led to his swift recall, an event that profoundly radicalized Irish politics.

## The Appointment to Ireland

Lord Fitzwilliam was appointed to succeed Lord Westmoreland in late 1794. "Westmoreland was recalled, and Lord Fitzwilliam, largely connected with Ireland by property, and one of the most just and liberal men in England, was to be his successor" (099-120). Fitzwilliam was the nephew of Lord Rockingham, the prime minister who first acknowledged the Irish constitution of 1782, and had married into the Ponsonby family — one of the most influential political dynasties in Ireland. McGee describes the new reign as "one of the shortest" yet "one of the most important Ireland ever saw" (099-129).

Before taking up his post, Fitzwilliam engaged with key figures in the British government. Henry Grattan traveled to London and "had two or three interviews with Pitt, the Duke of Portland, and Lord Fitzwilliam" (099-140), and made a pilgrimage to Beaconsfield for the counsel of the aged Edmund Burke. At the King's levee on the 10th of December, "when Lord Fitzwilliam was sworn in, the aged Burke, in deep mourning for his idolized son, attended" (099-154).

## The Viceroyalty of 1795

> On the 4th of January, Lord Fitzwilliam reached Dublin; and on the 25th of March he was recalled. The history of these three months — of this short-lived attempt to govern Ireland on the advice of Grattan — is full of important instruction. (099-162)

Fitzwilliam arrived in Dublin determined to pursue a policy of conciliation and reform. He appointed Grattan and the Ponsonbys to the Privy Council, where "they were free to shape the measures of the new administration" (099-152). His intention was to finally close the long-standing controversy over Catholic disabilities, a policy strongly opposed by the Protestant Ascendancy interest and by King George III himself.

The viceroy's reformist agenda quickly encountered fierce resistance from the "Castle cabal" — the entrenched Protestant interest that dominated the Irish administration. The Beresford family, who controlled extensive patronage in Ireland, led the opposition. Powerful delegations proceeded to London, and "every agency of influence was brought to bear on the King and the English cabinet" (099-178).

## The Recall

The conflict came to a head when Fitzwilliam realized he could not execute his reform policy while the Beresford faction remained in place. "From the tenor of his letters, Lord Fitzwilliam felt compelled in honour to tell Mr. Pitt, that he might choose between him and the Beresfords" (099-179). Pitt chose to support the Beresfords and the established interest, and Fitzwilliam was immediately recalled.

> Lord Fitzwilliam was immediately replaced by Lord Camden, whose Viceroyalty extended into the middle of the year 1798: a reign which embraced all that remains to us to narrate, of the Parliamentary history of the era of independence. (099-198)

## Impact of the Recall

The recall of Lord Fitzwilliam had far-reaching consequences for Ireland. McGee lists "the bitter disappointment of Lord Fitzwilliam's recall — the annual failure of Ponsonby's motion for reform — finally, the despairing secession of Grattan and his friends from Parliament" as factors that "had all tended to expand the ranks of the United Irishmen" (100-146). The dashed hopes of peaceful reform drove many moderates toward more radical positions.

King George III personally opposed Fitzwilliam's emancipation policy. In his memorandum on the recall, the King "explains his views with the business-like brevity which characterized all his communications" and "was totally opposed to Lord Fitzwilliam's emancipation policy, which he thought adopted 'in implicit obedience to the heated imagination of Mr. Burke'" (105-014, 105-017).

Lord Camden, Fitzwilliam's successor, pursued the opposite policy — one of coercion rather than conciliation. Camden's administration, which extended into the Rebellion of 1798, "embraced" the remaining Parliamentary history of independent Ireland before the Act of Union.

## Later Life

After his recall from Ireland, Fitzwilliam remained active in British politics. He continued to advocate for Catholic relief and maintained his connections with the Irish Whig interest. In later years, "his old friend Lord Fitzwilliam found a constituency" in Parliament (108-056) and he continued to speak on Irish affairs. He served in various capacities including Lord President of the Council in 1806–07.

## Legacy

Lord Fitzwilliam's three-month viceroyalty became a symbol of the possibilities and frustrations of Irish reform. His recall demonstrated the decisive power of the Ascendancy interest and the King's personal opposition to Catholic emancipation — factors that would delay reform for another three decades. The episode accelerated the drift of Irish reformers away from constitutional methods and toward the revolutionary organization of the United Irishmen, shaping the trajectory that led to the Rebellion of 1798.
