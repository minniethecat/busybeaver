## Overview

Jenico Preston, 7th Viscount Gormanston (commonly referred to as Lord Gormanstown), was one of the most prominent Anglo-Irish Catholic peers of the Pale during the early seventeenth century. As head of the Gormanstown family, which had held lands in County Meath since the Norman period, he occupied a pivotal position between the Old English Catholic gentry and the Gaelic Irish lords. His decision to join the insurgents at the Hill of Crofty in December 1641 marked a critical turning point in the early stages of the Confederate Wars, transforming what had begun as an Ulster rising into a national Catholic movement.

## The Gormanstown Family

The Preston family, ennobled as Viscounts Gormanston, were among the most distinguished of the Pale families. McGee notes that the "Gormanstown family, greatly distinguished themselves" (066-175) during the siege of Drogheda in the winter of 1641–42. The family's influence extended across Meath and into the councils of the Anglo-Irish nobility. Together with other Catholic peers — the Lords Fingal, Slane, Dunsany, Netterville, Louth, and Trimleston — the Gormanstowns represented the established Catholic interest that had survived the Tudor conquest and the plantations.

Their loyalty to the Crown was traditionally unquestioned, and they sought only the free exercise of their religion and security of their estates. As late as 1641, "the Lords Gormanstown, Slane, Killeen, Trimbleston, Dunsany, and Howth, signed an humble remonstrance to the King, concluding with a prayer for the relaxation of the penal laws" (064-293).

## The Meeting at Crofty

The decisive moment of Lord Gormanstown's career came on the 17th of December, 1641. The Lords Justices in Dublin, Parsons and Borlase, had summoned the Catholic peers of the Pale to attend in the capital. The "Justices replied by proclamation, denying most of them, and repeating their summons to Lords Fingal, Gormanstown, Slane, Dunsany, Netterville, Louth, and Trimleston, to attend in Dublin on the 17th" (067-286).

> But before the 17th came, as if to ensure the defeat of their own summons, Coote was let loose upon the flourishing villages of Fingal, and the flames kindled by his men might easily be discovered from the round tower of Swords. (067-289)

The summoned Lords, along with gentry of the neighbourhood, met by appointment "on the hill of Crofty, in the neighbouring county of Meath" (067-292). While they were deliberating, a party of armed horsemen appeared, led by Rory O'Moore, Colonel Byrne, and Captain Fox, representing the Ulster insurgents.

> Lord Gormanstown, advancing in front of his friends, demanded of the new-comers "why they came armed into the Pale?" To which O'Moore made answer "that the ground of their coming thither was for the freedom and liberty of their consciences, the maintenance of his Majesty's prerogative, in which they understood he was abridged, and the making the subjects of this kingdom as free as those of England." Lord Gormanstown, after consulting a few moments with his friends, replied: "Seeing these be your true ends, we will likewise join with you." (067-297–067-305)

The leaders embraced amid the acclamations of their followers, and a warrant was issued authorizing the Sheriff of Meath to summon the county gentry "to a final meeting at the Hill of Tara on the 24th of December" (067-308). This alliance between the Old English of the Pale and the Gaelic Ulster insurgents transformed the rebellion into a genuine national movement.

## The Confederate Period

Following the union at Crofty, Gormanstown became an active participant in the Catholic Confederation. His name appears prominently among the Confederate leadership. McGee lists "Gormanstown and Castlehaven, Sir Phelim O'Neil, Sir Richard Belling" (069-035) among the key figures gathered at Kilkenny, the Confederate capital.

The Confederate Assembly represented a broad coalition: "the Bishops of Down and of Clonfert, the Lords Gormanstown" (069-038) sat alongside Gaelic chieftains and Old English lawyers. This unprecedented unity of all Irish Catholic interests — Gaelic and Anglo-Irish, clergy and laity — was the defining achievement of the early Confederate period, and Gormanstown's defection from the government side had been instrumental in making it possible.

## The Military Phase

The Pale lords who joined at Crofty did not remain in a purely political role. The "Gormanstown family, greatly distinguished themselves" during the siege of Drogheda (066-175), where Confederate forces attempted to capture the strategic port town. Lords Gormanstown, Netterville, and Slane also attempted negotiations with the Earl of Ormond, offering "by letter to follow their example" — a reference to Lords Dunsany and Netterville who had surrendered on Ormond's guarantee (068-107). However, "the two former were, on reaching the city, thrust into the dungeons of the Castle, by order of the Justices" (068-108), and Gormanstown's proposals "were rejected with contumely" (068-110).

The Gormanstown family also had European connections. Colonel Thomas Preston, the "brave defender of Louvain, uncle to Lord Gormanstown, landed at Wexford, with three frigates and several transports, containing a few siege guns, field pieces, and munitions of war" (068-249–068-250). This military assistance from the Continent, channelled through family connections, provided the Confederates with essential matériel.

## Later Records

Gormanstown's name continues to appear in the historical record through the subsequent decades. In the 1640s he is listed alongside "Dunsany, Gormanstown, Trimblestown, and John Blake, Mayor of Dublin" (045-371) among the Catholic interest. As late as the early nineteenth century, more than 150 years after the Confederation, the name persists: "Fingal, Netterville, Gormanstown, and Killeen" (113-025) appear in the records of Catholic emancipation agitation. The family had remained Catholic through all the penal times, a testament to their enduring influence and the deep roots of the Old English peerage in Irish society.

## Legacy

Lord Gormanstown represents a critical figure in the early Confederate period — the moment when the Catholic peers of the Pale made their fateful decision to break with the Dublin government and join forces with the Ulster insurgents. His question to Rory O'Moore at Crofty — "why they came armed into the Pale?" — captures the initial wariness of the Old English establishment toward Gaelic rebellion. His decision to join them, after receiving assurances that the cause was for conscience and king, reveals how the brutal conduct of the government's commanders (Coote, in particular) drove even the most conservative Catholic interests into opposition.
