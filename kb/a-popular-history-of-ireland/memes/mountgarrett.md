## Overview

Lord Mountgarrett was an Anglo-Irish Catholic nobleman who served as the first President of the Supreme Council of the Confederate Catholics of Ireland, the governing body established at Kilkenny in 1642. An octogenarian by the time he assumed the presidency, Mountgarrett represented the "Anglo-Irish party" within the Confederation — those Old English families who sought to reconcile loyalty to the Crown with the defence of Catholic rights and Irish legislative independence. (068-183) (068-188) (077-033)

## Rise of the Confederate Movement

The Rebellion of 1641 confronted the Anglo-Irish Catholic nobility with an agonising choice. The rebellion had begun as a movement of the dispossessed Ulster Irish, but it rapidly spread to involve the Old English of the Pale and the towns. Mountgarrett was among those Anglo-Irish lords who joined the rising, not from sympathy with the Gaelic insurgents but from a conviction that only by joining the movement could they control and direct it toward constitutional ends. (060-043)

## President of the Supreme Council

In the summer of 1642, a national synod of the Catholic clergy met at [[kilkenny|Kilkenny]] and established the Confederate Catholics of Ireland. A Supreme Council was formed to govern the territories under Confederate control, and Lord Mountgarrett was elected its first President. The choice of an elderly, moderate Anglo-Irish peer reflected the desire of the Catholic leadership to present the Confederate cause as one of lawful resistance rather than rebellion. (068-183) (068-188)

Mountgarrett led the Anglo-Irish party within the Confederation, which consistently sought a negotiated settlement with the King against the more militant clerical faction who insisted on full restoration of the Catholic Church and the reversal of all plantations. (071-168)

## Military and Political Leadership

Despite his advanced age — Mountgarrett was in his seventies when he assumed the presidency — he took an active role in the military affairs of the Confederation. Together with Lord Roche and Viscount Mayo, he commanded Confederate forces in the south, with fifteen hundred men under their command. The "venerable Mountgarrett" was respected by all parties as a figure of moderation. (069-039) (069-044)

> "The venerable Mountgarrett, the octogenarian President of the Supreme Council." (077-033)

## Negotiation and Conflict

Mountgarrett's tenure as President was dominated by the effort to negotiate a peace with Charles I that would secure religious liberty and a reversal of the most oppressive measures of Strafford's administration. As the civil war in England turned against the King, the Confederate position grew stronger, but divisions within the Council between the Anglo-Irish and the clerical party deepened.

In 1645, the arrival of the Papal Nuncio [[Rinuccini]] dramatically shifted the balance of power within the Confederation. Mountgarrett and the Anglo-Irish party found themselves increasingly marginalised as Rinuccini insisted on maximalist demands that the King could never accept. (071-026) (071-122)

## Later Years and Legacy

Mountgarrett lived to see the Confederacy fragment and the eventual Cromwellian conquest of Ireland between 1649 and 1653. As "an octogenarian, and in his dotage" by the end, his influence had waned — but his early leadership had shaped the character of the Confederate experiment. He represented the tragedy of the Old English Catholics: loyal to the Crown, committed to constitutional methods, but driven by persecution into a rebellion they could neither fully control nor successfully conclude. (077-033)
