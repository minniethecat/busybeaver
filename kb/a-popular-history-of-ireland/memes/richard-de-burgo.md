Richard de Burgo, commonly called the Red Earl of Ulster, was the greatest Norman-Irish magnate of the late 13th and early 14th centuries. Lord of Connaught and Earl of Ulster, he united in his person the blood of the de Burghs, de Lacys, and O'Conors, and his authority in Meath and Connaught was such that the King of England addressed writs to him before the Lord Justice of Ireland.

## Ancestry and Rise

Richard de Burgo was the son or grandson of William Fitz-Aldelm de Burgo, the first of the family to settle in Ireland. About the year 1225, his powerful uncle Hubert de Burgh — Chief Justiciary of England and effectively sovereign of the realm during the minority of Henry III — conveyed to him the Lordship of Connaught. (030-101) When Cathal Crovdearg O'Conor, King of Connaught, died, Hubert procured grants of the entire province to be made to Richard, reserving only five cantreds about Athlone for a royal garrison. (030-131)

Richard married Hodierna, granddaughter of Cathal Crovdearg, thus strengthening his claim by blood as well as by grant. (030-134)

> In Richard de Burgo, commonly called the Red Earl of Ulster, all the genius and the vices of the race of Rollo blazed out over Ireland for the last time, and with terrible effect. (030-021)

## Character and Power

Richard was called from his ruddy complexion "the Red Earl" of Ulster. (035-010) Nobly bred in the court of Henry III of England, he attained manhood about the period when the de Lacys, Geraldines, and other great Norman houses were without natural leaders. Uniting in his own person the blood of the O'Conors, de Lacys, and de Burghs, his authority was great from the beginning in Meath and Connaught. (035-015)

In the year 1294, after many lesser conflicts with his rivals, de Verdon and the Kildare Geraldine captured the Red Earl and his brother William, carrying them in fetters to the Castle of Lea in Offaly. (035-027) The conflict was resolved when de Burgh gave his two sons as hostages to Fitzgerald, and the latter surrendered the Castle of Sligo to de Burgh. (035-031) From this peace, the power of de Burgo outgrew anything that had been known before: he held the hostages of both Ulster and Connaught, and when the King of England sent writs into Ireland, he usually addressed the Red Earl before the Lord Justice or Lord Deputy — a signal honour in that ceremonious age. (035-042)

## The Bruce Invasion

The Red Earl was the central figure in the Anglo-Irish response to Edward Bruce's invasion of Ireland. (035-118) He was deeply engaged in a protracted struggle with Bruce — a conflict that had raged some twenty years before Bruce's arrival in Ireland. (036-082) The withdrawal of Felim O'Conor from the Red Earl's camp was a critical blow to his position, and Bruce himself suspected the Red Earl's zeal for the English cause. (037-110)

When Bruce fell at the Battle of Faughard in 1318, the Red Earl's position was secured, but the invasion had permanently weakened the Norman colony and the earldom would not long survive his own death.

## Death and the Fall of His House

Richard, the Red Earl, died in the Monastery of Athassil in 1326. (038-104) He was succeeded by his son William, but the misfortunes of the Red Earl's posterity were swift and terrible. Seven years after his father's death, William de Burgh was murdered at the Fords near Carrickfergus, in the 21st year of his age, in consequence of a family feud instigated by Gilla de Burgh, wife of Walter de Mandeville. (038-107)

The Red Earl's surviving son, Edmond, was captured by his own cousin William and drowned in Lough Mask with a stone about his neck. (038-114) The posterity of William de Burgh then assumed the name McWilliam and renounced the laws, language, and allegiance of England. (038-117)

## Legacy

The era following the Red Earl's death in 1326 marked a decisive turning point in Irish history. The premier earldom of Ulster disappeared, and in the half-century after his death the native Irish chieftains gained steadily upon the weakened colony. (039-052) Historians have compared him to the first Hugh de Lacy as one of the only two Norman lords in Ireland of truly exceptional stature. (045-402) The rapid collapse of his dynasty illustrated the fragility of even the mightiest Norman-Irish lordship: greatness built over a lifetime could be undone in a single generation by family feud and the shifting allegiances of Gaelic Ireland.
