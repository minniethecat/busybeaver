CHAPTER I.

[039-001]ACTS OF THE NATIVE PRINCES—SUBDIVISION OF TRIBES AND

[039-002]TERRITORIES—ANGLO-IRISH TOWNS UNDER NATIVE PROTECTION—ATTEMPT OF

[039-003]THADDEUS O'BRIEN, PRINCE OF THOMOND, TO RESTORE THE MONARCHY—RELATIONS

[039-004]OF THE RACES IN THE FIFTEENTH CENTURY.

[039-005]The history of "the Pale" being recounted down to the period of its

[039-006]complete isolation, we have now to pass beyond its entrenched and

[039-007]castellated limits, in order to follow the course of events in other

[039-008]parts of the kingdom.

[039-009]While the highest courage was everywhere exhibited by chiefs and

[039-010]clansmen, no attempt was made to bring about another National

[039-011]Confederacy, after the fall of Edward Bruce. One result of that

[039-012]striking _denouement_ of a stormy career—in addition to those before

[039-013]mentioned—was to give new life to the jealousy which had never wholly

[039-014]subsided, between the two primitive divisions of the Island. Bruce,

[039-015]welcomed, sustained, and lamented by the Northern Irish, was

[039-016]distrusted, avoided, and execrated by those of the South. There may

[039-017]have been exceptions, but this was the rule. The Bards and Newsmen of

[039-018]subsequent times, according to their Provincial bias, charged the

[039-019]failure of Bruce upon the Eugenian race, or justified his fate by

[039-020]aspersing his memory and his adherents of the race of Conn. This

[039-021]feeling of irritation, always most deep-seated when driven in by a

[039-022]consciousness of mismanagement or of self-reproach, goes a great way to

[039-023]account for the fact, that more than one generation was to pass away,

[039-024]before any closer union could be brought about between the Northern and

[039-025]Southern Milesian Irish.

[039-026]We cannot, therefore, in the period embraced in our present book, treat

[039-027]the Provinces otherwise than as estranged communities, departing

[039-028]farther and farther from the ancient traditions of one central

[039-029]legislative council and one supreme elective chief. Special,

[039-030]short-lived alliances between lords of different Provinces are indeed

[039-031]frequent; but they were brought about mostly by ties of relationship or

[039-032]gossipred, and dissolved with the disappearance of the immediate

[039-033]danger. The very idea of national unity, once so cherished by all the

[039-034]children of _Miledh Espaigne_, seems to have been as wholly lost as any

[039-035]of those secrets of ancient handiwork, over which modern ingenuity

[039-036]puzzles itself in vain. In the times to which we have descended, it was

[039-037]every principality and every lordship for itself. As was said of old in

[039-038]Rome, "Antony had his party, Octavius had his party, but the

[039-039]Commonwealth had none."

[039-040]Not alone was the greater unity wholly forgotten, but no sooner were

[039-041]the descendants of the Anglo-Normans driven into their eastern

[039-042]enclosure, or thoroughly amalgamated in language, laws and costume with

[039-043]themselves, than the ties of particular clans began to loose their

[039-044]binding force, and the tendency to subdivide showed itself on every

[039-045]opportunity. We have already, in the book of the "War of Succession,"

[039-046]described the subdivisions of Breffni and of Meath as measures of

[039-047]policy, taken by the O'Conor Kings, to weaken their too powerful

[039-048]suffragans. But that step, which might have strengthened the hands of a

[039-049]native dynasty, almost inevitably weakened the tribes themselves in

[039-050]combating the attacks of a highly organized foreign power. Of this the

[039-051]O'Conors themselves became afterwards the most striking example. For

[039-052]half a century following the Red Earl's death, they had gained steadily

[039-053]on the foreigners settled in Connaught. The terrible defeat of Athenry

[039-054]was more than atoned for by both other victories. At length the

[039-055]descendants of the vanquished on that day ruled as proudly as ever did

[039-056]their ancestors in their native Province. The posterity of the victors

[039-057]were merely tolerated on its soil, or anxiously building up new houses

[039-058]in Meath and Louth. But in an evil hour, on the death of their last

[039-059]King (1384), the O'Conors agreed to settle the conflicting claims of

[039-060]rival candidates for the succession by dividing the common inheritance.

[039-061]From this date downwards we have an O'Conor Don and an O'Conor Roe in

[039-062]the Annals of that Province, each rallying a separate band of

[039-063]partizans; and according to the accidents of age, minority, alliance,

[039-064]or personal reputation, infringing, harassing, or domineering over the

[039-065]other. Powerful lords they long continued, but as Provincial Princes we

[039-066]meet them no more.

[039-068]This fatal example—of which there had been a faint foreshadowing in the

[039-069]division of the McCarthys in the preceding century—in the course of a

[039-070]generation or two, was copied by almost every great connection, north

[039-071]and south. The descendants of yellow Hugh O'Neil in Clandeboy claimed

[039-072]exemption from the supremacy of the elder family in Tyrone; the

[039-073]O'Farells, acknowledged two lords of Annally; the McDonoghs, two lords

[039-074]of Tirerril; there was McDermott of the Wood claiming independence of

[039-075]McDermott of the Rock; O'Brien of Ara asserted equality with O'Brien of

[039-076]Thomond; the nephews of Art McMurrogh contested the superiority of his

[039-077]sons; and thus slowly but surely the most powerful clans were hastening

[039-078]the day of their own dissolution.

[039-079]A consequence of these subdivisions was the necessity which arose for

[039-080]new and opposite alliances, among those who had formerly looked on

[039-081]themselves as members of one family, with common dangers and common

[039-082]enemies. The pivot of policy now rested on neighbourhood rather than on

[039-083]pedigree; a change in its first stages apparently unnatural and

[039-084]deplorable, but in the long run not without its compensating

[039-085]advantages. As an instance of these new necessities, we may adduce the

[039-086]protection and succour steadily extended by the O'Neils of Clandeboy,

[039-087]to the McQuillans, Bissets, of the Antrim coast, and the McDonnells of

[039-088]the Glens, against the frequent attacks of the O'Neils of Tyrone. The

[039-089]latter laid claim to all Ulster, and long refused to acknowledge these

[039-090]foreigners, though men of kindred race and speech. Had it not been that

[039-091]the interest of Clandeboy pointed the other way, it is very doubtful if

[039-092]either the Welsh or Scottish settlers by the bays of Antrim could have

[039-093]made a successful stand against the overruling power of the house of

[039-094]Dungannon. The same policy, adopted by native chiefs under similar

[039-095]circumstances, protected the minor groups of settlers of foreign origin

[039-096]in the most remote districts—like the Barretts and other Welsh people

[039-097]of Tyrawley—long after the Deputies of the Kings of England had ceased

[039-098]to consider them as fellow-subjects, or to be concerned for their

[039-099]existence.

[039-100]In like manner the detached towns, built by foreigners, of Welsh,

[039-101]Flemish, Saxon, or Scottish origin, were now taken "under the

[039-102]protection" of the neighbouring chief, or Prince, and paid to him or to

[039-103]his bailiff an annual tax for such protection. In this manner Wexford

[039-104]purchased protection of McMurrogh, Limerick from O'Brien, and Dundalk

[039-105]from O'Neil. But the yoke was not always borne with patience, nor did

[039-106]the bare relation of tax-gatherer and tax-payer generate any very

[039-107]cordial feeling between the parties. Emboldened by the arrival of a

[039-108]powerful Deputy, or a considerable accession to the Colony, or taking

[039-109]advantage of contested elections for the chieftaincy among their

[039-110]protectors, these sturdy communities sometimes sought by force to get

[039-111]rid of their native masters. Yet in no case at this period were such

[039-112]town risings ultimately successful. The appearance of a menacing force,

[039-113]and the threat of the torch, soon brought the refractory burgesses to

[039-114]terms. On such an occasion (1444) Dundalk paid Owen O'Neil the sum of

[039-115]60 marks and two tuns of wine to avert his indignation. On another, the

[039-116]townsmen of Limerick agreed about the same period to pay annually for

[039-117]ever to O'Brien the sum of 60 marks. Notwithstanding the precarious

[039-118]tenure of their existence, they all continued jealously to guard their

[039-119]exclusive privileges. In the oath of office taken by the Mayor of

[039-120]Dublin (1388) he is sworn to guard the city's franchises, so that no

[039-121]Irish rebel shall intrude upon the limits. Nicholas O'Grady, Abbot of a

[039-122]Monastery in Clare, is mentioned in 1485 as "the twelfth Irishman that

[039-123]ever possessed the freedom of the city of Limerick" up to that time. A

[039-124]special bye-law, at a still later period, was necessary to admit

[039-125]Colonel William O'Shaughnessy, of one of the first families in that

[039-126]county, to the freedom of the Corporation of the town of Galway.

[039-127]Exclusiveness on the one side, and arbitrary taxation on the other,

[039-128]were ill means of ensuring the prosperity of these new trading

[039-129]communities; Freedom and Peace have ever been as essential to commerce

[039-130]as the winds and waves are to navigation.

[039-131]The dissolution and reorganization of the greater clans necessarily

[039-132]included the removal of old, and the formation of new boundaries, and

[039-133]these changes frequently led to border battles between the contestants.

[039-134]The most striking illustration of the struggles of this description,

[039-135]which occurs in our Annals in the fifteenth century, is that which was

[039-136]waged for three generations between a branch of the O'Conors

[039-137]established at Sligo, calling themselves "lords of Lower Connaught,"

[039-138]and the O'Donnells of Donegal. The country about Sligo had anciently

[039-139]been subject to the Donegal chiefs, but the new masters of Sligo, after

[039-140]the era of Edward Bruce, not only refused any longer to pay tribute,

[039-141]but endeavoured by the strong hand to extend their sway to the banks of

[039-142]the Drowse and the Erne. The pride not less than the power of the

[039-143]O'Donnells was interested in resisting this innovation, for, in the

[039-144]midst of the debateable land rose the famous mountain of Ben Gulban

[039-145](now Benbulben), which bore the name of the first father of their

[039-146]tribe. The contest was, therefore, bequeathed from father to son, but

[039-147]the family of Sligo, under the lead of their vigorous chiefs, and with

[039-148]the advantage of actual possession, prevailed in establishing the

[039-149]exemption of their territory from the ancient tribute. The Drowse,

[039-150]which carries the surplus waters of the beautiful Lough Melvin into the

[039-151]bay of Donegal, finally became the boundary between Lower Connaught and

[039-152]Tyrconnell.

[039-153]We have already alluded to the loss of the arts of political

[039-154]combination among the Irish in the Middle Ages. This loss was

[039-155]occasionally felt by the superior minds both in church and state. It

[039-156]was felt by Donald More O'Brien and those who went with him into the

[039-157]house of Conor Moinmoy O'Conor, in 1188; it was felt by the nobles who,

[039-158]at Cael-uisge, elected Brian O'Neil in 1258; it was felt by the twelve

[039-159]reguli who, in 1315, invited Edward Bruce, "a man of kindred blood," to

[039-160]rule over them; it was imputed as a crime to Art McMurrogh in 1397,

[039-161]that he designed to claim the general sovereignty; and now in this

[039-162]century, Thaddeus O'Brien, Prince of Thomond, with the aid of the Irish

[039-163]of the southern half-kingdom, began (to use the phrase of the last

[039-164]Antiquary of Lecan) "working his way to Tara." This Prince united all

[039-165]the tribes of Munster in his favour, and needing, according to ancient

[039-166]usage, the suffrages of two other Provinces to ensure his election, he

[039-167]crossed the Shannon in the summer of 1466 at the head of the largest

[039-168]army which had followed any of his ancestors since the days of King

[039-169]Brian. He renewed his protection to the town of Limerick, entered into

[039-170]an alliance with the Earl of Desmond—which alliance seems to have cost

[039-171]Desmond his head—received in his camp the hostages of Ormond and

[039-172]Ossory, and gave gifts to the lords of Leinster. Simultaneously,

[039-173]O'Conor of Offally had achieved a great success over the Palesmen,

[039-174]taking prisoner the Earl of Desmond, the Prior of Trim, the Lords

[039-175]Barnwall, Plunkett, Nugent, and other Methian magnates—a circumstance

[039-176]which also seems to have some connection with the fate of Desmond and

[039-177]Plunkett, who were the next year tried for treason and executed at

[039-178]Drogheda, by order of the Earl of Worcester, then Deputy. The usual

[039-179]Anglo-Irish tales, as to the causes of Desmond's losing the favour of

[039-180]Edward IV., seem very like after-inventions. It is much more natural to

[039-181]attribute that sudden change to some connection with the attempt of

[039-182]O'Brien the previous year—since this only makes intelligible the

[039-183]accusation against him of "_alliance_, fosterage, and alterage with the

[039-184]King's Irish enemies."

[039-185]From Leinster O'Brien recrossed the Shannon, and overran the country of

[039-186]the Clan-William Burke. But the ancient jealousy of Leath-Conn would

[039-187]not permit its proud chiefs to render hostage or homage to a Munster

[039-188]Prince, of no higher rank than themselves. Disappointed in his hopes of

[039-189]that union which could alone restore the monarchy in the person of a

[039-190]native ruler, the descendant of Brian returned to Kinkora, where he

[039-191]shortly afterwards fell ill of fever and died. "It was commonly

[039-192]reported," says the Antiquary of Lecan, "that the multitudes' envious

[039-193]eyes and hearts shortened his days."

[039-194]The naturalized Norman noble spoke the language of the Gael, and

[039-195]retained his Brehons and Bards like his Milesian compeer. For

[039-196]generations the daughters of the elder race had been the mothers of his

[039-197]house; and the milk of Irish foster-mothers had nourished the infancy

[039-198]of its heirs. The Geraldines, the McWilliams, even the Butlers, among

[039-199]their tenants and soldiers, were now as Irish as the Irish. Whether

[039-200]allies or enemies, rivals or as relatives, they stood as near to their

[039-201]neighbours of Celtic origin as they did to the descendants of those who

[039-202]first landed at Bannow and at Waterford. The "Statute of Kilkenny" had

[039-203]proclaimed the eternal separation of the races, but up to this period

[039-204]it had failed, and the men of both origins were left free to develop

[039-205]whatever characteristics were most natural to them. What we mean by

[039-206]being left free is, that there was no general or long-sustained

[039-207]combination of one race for the suppression of the other from the

[039-208]period of Richard the Second's last reverses (A.D. 1399) till the

[039-209]period of the Reformation. Native Irish life, therefore, throughout the

[039-210]whole of the fifteenth, and during the first half of the sixteenth

[039-211]century, was as free to shape and direct itself, to ends of its own

[039-212]choosing, as it had been at almost any former period in our history.

[039-213]Private wars and hereditary blood-feuds, next after the loss of

[039-214]national unity, were the worst vices of the nation. Deeds of violence

[039-215]and acts of retaliation were as common as the succession of day and

[039-216]night. Every free clansman carried his battle-axe to church and chase,

[039-217]to festival and fairgreen. The strong arm was prompt to obey the fiery

[039-218]impulse, and it must be admitted in solemn sadness, that almost every

[039-219]page of our records at this period is stained with human blood. But

[039-220]though crimes of violence are common, crimes of treachery are rare. The

[039-221]memory of a McMahon, who betrayed and slew his guest, is execrated by

[039-222]the same stoical scribes, who set down, without a single expression of

[039-223]horror, the open murder of chief after chief. Taking off by poison, so

[039-224]common among their cotemporaries, seems to have been altogether

[039-225]unknown, and the cruelties of the State Prisons of the Middle Ages

[039-226]undreamt of by our fierce, impetuous, but not implacable ancestors. The

[039-227]facts which go to affix the imputation of cruelty on those ages are,

[039-228]the frequent entries which we find of deposed chiefs, or conspicuous

[039-229]criminals, having their eyes put out, or being maimed in their members.

[039-230]By these barbarous punishments they lost caste, if not life; but that

[039-231]indeed must have been a wretched remnant of existence which remained to

[039-232]the blinded lover, or the maimed warrior, or the crippled tiller of the

[039-233]soil. Of the social and religious relations existing between the races,

[039-234]we shall have occasion to speak more fully before closing the present

[039-235]book.
