CHAPTER III.

[050-001]SIR ANTHONY ST. LEGER, LORD DEPUTY—NEGOTIATIONS OF THE IRISH CHIEFS

[050-002]WITH JAMES THE FIFTH OF SCOTLAND—FIRST ATTEMPTS TO INTRODUCE THE

[050-003]PROTESTANT REFORMATION—OPPOSITION OF THE CLERGY—PARLIAMENT OF 1541—THE

[050-004]PROCTORS OF THE CLERGY EXCLUDED—STATE OF THE COUNTRY—THE CROWNS

[050-005]UNITED—HENRY THE EIGHTH PROCLAIMED AT LONDON AND DUBLIN.

[050-006]Upon the disgrace of Lord Leonard Gray in 1540, Sir Anthony St. Leger

[050-007]was appointed Deputy. He had previously been employed as chief of the

[050-008]commission issued in 1537, to survey land subject to the King, to

[050-009]inquire into, confirm, or cancel titles, and abolish abuses which might

[050-010]have crept in among the Englishry, whether upon the marches or within

[050-011]the Pale. In this employment he had at his disposal a guard of 340 men,

[050-012]while the Deputy and Council were ordered to obey his mandates as if

[050-013]given by the King in person. The commissioners were further empowered

[050-014]to reform the Courts of Law; to enter as King's Counsel into both

[050-015]Houses of Parliament, there to urge the adoption of measures upholding

[050-016]English laws and customs, establishing the King's supremacy, in

[050-017]spirituals as in temporals, to provide for the defence of the marches,

[050-018]and the better collection of the revenues. In the three years which he

[050-019]spent at the head of this commission, St. Leger, an eminently able and

[050-020]politic person, made himself intimately acquainted with Irish affairs;

[050-021]as a natural consequence of which knowledge he was entrusted, upon the

[050-022]first vacancy, with their supreme directions. In this situation he had

[050-023]to contend, not only with the complications long existing in the system

[050-024]itself, but with the formidable disturbing influence exercised by the

[050-025]Court of Scotland, chiefly upon and by means of the Ulster Princes.

[050-026]Up to this period, the old political intimacy of Scotland and Ireland

[050-027]had known no diminution. The Scots in Antrim could reckon, soon after

[050-028]Henry's accession to the throne, 2,000 fighting men. In 1513, in order

[050-029]to co-operate with the warlike movement of O'Donnell, the Scottish

[050-030]fleet, under the Earl of Arran, in his famous flagship, "the great

[050-031]Michael," captured Carrickfergus, putting its Anglo-Irish garrison to

[050-032]the sword. In the same Scottish reign (that of James IV.), one of the

[050-033]O'Donnells had a munificent grant of lands in Kirkcudbright, as other

[050-034]adventurers from Ulster had from the same monarch, in Galloway and

[050-035]Kincardine. In 1523, while hostilities raged between Scotland and

[050-036]England, the Irish Chiefs entered into treaty with Francis the First of

[050-037]France, who bound himself to land in Ireland 15,000 men, to expel the

[050-038]English from "the Pale," and to carry his arms across the channel in

[050-039]the quarrel of Richard de la Pole, father of the famous Cardinal, and

[050-040]at this time a formidable pretender to the English throne. The imbecile

[050-041]conduct of the Scottish Regent, the Duke of Albany, destroyed this

[050-042]enterprise, which, however, was but the forerunner, if it was not the

[050-043]model, of several similar combinations. When the Earl of Bothwell took

[050-044]refuge at the English Court, in 1531, he suggested to Henry VIII.,

[050-045]among other motives for renewing the war with James V., that the latter

[050-046]was in league "with the Emperor, the Danish King, and O'Donnell." The

[050-047]following year, a Scottish force of 4,000 men, under John, son of

[050-048]Alexander McDonald, Lord of the Isles, served, by permission of their

[050-049]King, under the banner of the Chieftain of Tyrconnell. An uninterrupted

[050-050]correspondence between the Ulster Chiefs and the Scottish Court may be

[050-051]traced through this reign, forming a curious chapter of Irish

[050-052]diplomacy. In 1535, we have a letter from O'Neil to James V., from

[050-053]which it appears that O'Neil's Secretary was then residing at the

[050-054]Scottish Court; and as the crisis of the contest for the Crown drew

[050-055]near, we find the messages and overtures from Ulster multiplying in

[050-056]number and earnestness. In that critical period, James V. was between

[050-057]twenty and thirty years old, and his powerful minister, Cardinal

[050-058]Beaton, was acting by him the part that Wolsey had played by Henry at a

[050-059]like age. The Cardinal, favouring the French and Irish alliances, had

[050-060]drawn a line of Scottish policy, in relation to both those countries,

[050-061]precisely parallel to Wolsey's. During the Geraldine insurrection,

[050-062]Henry was obliged to remonstrate with James on favours shown to his

[050-063]rebels of Ireland. This charge James' ministers, in their

[050-064]correspondence of the year 1535, strenuously denied, while admitting

[050-065]that some insignificant Islesmen, over whom he could exercise no

[050-066]control, might have gone privily thither. In the spring of 1540, Bryan

[050-067]Layton, one of the English agents at the Scottish Court, communicated

[050-068]to Secretary Cromwell that James had fitted out a fleet of 15 ships,

[050-069]manned by 2,000 men, and armed with all the ordinance that he could

[050-070]muster; that his destination was Ireland, the Crown of which had been

[050-071]offered to him, the previous Lent, by "eight gentlemen," who brought

[050-072]him written tenders of submission "from all the great men of Ireland,"

[050-073]with their seals attached; and, furthermore, that the King had declared

[050-074]to Lord Maxwell his determination to win such a prize as "never King of

[050-075]Scotland had before," or to lose his life in the attempt. It is

[050-076]remarkable that in this same spring of 1540—while such was understood

[050-077]to be the destination of the Scottish fleet—a congress of the Chiefs of

[050-078]all Ireland was appointed to be held at the Abbey of Fore, in

[050-079]West-Meath. To prevent this meeting taking place, the whole force of

[050-080]the Pale, with the judges, clergy, townsmen and husbandmen, marched out

[050-081]under the direction of the Lords of the Council (St. Leger not having

[050-082]yet arrived to replace Lord Gray), but finding no such assembly as they

[050-083]had been led to expect, they made a predatory incursion into Roscommon,

[050-084]and dispersed some armed bands belonging to O'Conor. The commander in

[050-085]this expedition was the Marshal Sir William Brereton, for the moment

[050-086]one of the Lords Justices. He was followed to the field by the last

[050-087]Prior of Kilmainham, Sir John Rawson, the Master of the Rolls, the

[050-088]Archbishop of Dublin, the Bishop of Meath, Mr. Justice Luttrell, and

[050-089]the Barons of the Exchequer-a strange medley of civil and military

[050-090]dignitaries.

[050-092]The prevention or postponement of the Congress at Fore must have

[050-093]exercised a decided influence on the expedition of James V. His great

[050-094]armada having put to sea, after coasting among the out-islands, and

[050-095]putting into a northern English port from stress of weather, returned

[050-096]home without achievement of any kind. Diplomatic intercourse was

[050-097]shortly renewed between him and Henry, but, in the following year, to

[050-098]the extreme displeasure of his royal kinsman, he assumed the

[050-099]much-prized title of "Defender of the Faith." Another rupture took

[050-100]place, when the Irish card was played over again with the customary

[050-101]effect. In a letter of July, 1541, introducing to the Irish Chiefs the

[050-102]Jesuit Fathers, Salmeron, Broet, and Capata, who passed through

[050-103]Scotland on their way to Ireland, James styles himself "Lord of

[050-104]Ireland"—another insult and defiance to Henry, whose newly-acquired

[050-105]kingly style was then but a few weeks old. By way of retaliation, Henry

[050-106]ordered the Archbishop of York to search the registers of that see for

[050-107]evidence of _his_ claim to the Crown of Scotland, and industriously

[050-108]cultivated the disaffected party amongst the Scottish nobility. At

[050-109]length these bickerings broke out into open war, and the short, but

[050-110]fatal campaign of 1542, removed another rival for the English King. The

[050-111]double defeat of Fala and of Solway Moss, the treason of his nobles,

[050-112]and the failure of his hopes, broke the heart of the high-spirited

[050-113]James V. He died in December, 1542, in the 33rd year of his age, a few

[050-114]hours after learning the birth of his daughter, so celebrated as Mary,

[050-115]Queen of Scots. In his last moments he pronounced the doom of the

[050-116]Stuart dynasty—"It came with a lass," he exclaimed, "and it will go

[050-117]with a lass," And thus it happened that the image of Ireland, which

[050-118]unfolds the first scene of the War of the Roses, which is inseparable

[050-119]from the story of the two Bruces, and which occupies so much of the

[050-120]first and last years of the Tudor dynasty, stands mournfully by the

[050-121]deathbed of the last Stuart King who reigned in Scotland—the only

[050-122]Prince of his race that had ever written under his name the title of

[050-123]"_Dominus Hiberniae_."

[050-124]The premature death of James was hardly more regretted by his immediate

[050-125]subjects than by his Irish allies. All external events now conspired to

[050-126]show the hopelessness of resistance to the power of King Henry. From

[050-127]Scotland, destined to half a century of anarchy, no help could be

[050-128]expected. Wales, another ancient ally of the Irish, had been

[050-129]incorporated with England, in 1536, and was fast becoming reconciled to

[050-130]the rule of a Prince, sprung from a Welsh ancestry. Francis of France

[050-131]and Charles V., rivals for the leadership of the Continent, were too

[050-132]busy with their own projects to enter into any Irish alliance. The

[050-133]Geraldines had suffered terrible defeats; the family of Kildare was

[050-134]without an adult representative; the O'Neils and O'Donnells had lost

[050-135]ground at Bellahoe, and were dismayed by the unlooked-for death of the

[050-136]King of Scotland. The arguments, therefore, by which many of the chiefs

[050-137]might have justified themselves to their clans in 1541, '2 and '3, for

[050-138]submitting to the inevitable laws of necessity in rendering homage to

[050-139]Henry VIII., were neither few nor weak. Abroad there was no hope of an

[050-140]alliance sufficient to counterbalance the immense resources of England;

[050-141]at home life-wasting private wars, the conflict of laws, of languages,

[050-142]and of titles to property, had become unbearable. That fatal family

[050-143]pride, which would not permit an O'Brien to obey an O'Neil, nor an

[050-144]O'Conor to follow either, rendered the establishment of a native

[050-145]monarchy—even if there had been no other obstacle—wholly impracticable.

[050-146]Among the clergy alone did the growing supremacy of Henry meet with any

[050-147]effective opposition.

[050-148]At its first presentation in Ireland, and during the whole of Henry's

[050-149]lifetime, the "Reformation" wore the guise of schism, as distinguished

[050-150]from heresy. To deny the supremacy of the Pope and admit the supremacy

[050-151]of the King were almost its sole tests of doctrine. All the ancient

[050-152]teaching in relation to the Seven Sacraments, the Holy Sacrifice of the

[050-153]Mass, the Real Presence, Purgatory, and Prayers for the Dead, were

[050-154]scrupulously retained. Subsequently, the necessity of auricular

[050-155]confession, the invocation of Saints, and the celibacy of the clergy

[050-156]came to be questioned, but they were not dogmatically assailed during

[050-157]this reign. The common people, where English was understood, were slow

[050-158]in taking alarm at these masked innovations; in the Irish-speaking

[050-159]districts—three-fourths of the whole country—they were only heard of as

[050-160]rumours from afar, but the clergy, secular and regular, were not long

[050-161]left in doubt as to where such steps must necessarily lead.

[050-162]From 1534, the year of his divorce, until 1541, the year of his

[050-163]election, Henry attempted, by fits and starts, to assert his supremacy

[050-164]in Ireland. He appointed George Browne, a strenuous advocate of the

[050-165]divorce, some time Provincial of the order of St. Augustine in England,

[050-166]Archbishop of Dublin, vacant by the murder of Archbishop Allan. On the

[050-167]12th of March, 1535, Browne was consecrated by Cranmer, whose opinions,

[050-168]as well as those of Secretary Cromwell, he echoed through life. He may

[050-169]be considered the first agent employed to introduce the Reformation

[050-170]into Ireland, and his zeal in that work seems to have been unwearied.

[050-171]He was destined, however, to find many opponents, and but few converts.

[050-172]Not only the Primate of Armagh, George Cromer, and almost all the

[050-173]episcopal order, resolutely resisted his measures, but the clergy and

[050-174]laity of Dublin refused to accept his new forms of prayer, or to listen

[050-175]to his strange teaching. He inveighs in his correspondence with

[050-176]Cromwell against Bassenet, Dean of St. Patrick's, Castele, Prior of

[050-177]Christ's Church, and generally against all the clergy. Of the

[050-178]twenty-eight secular priests in Dublin, but three could be induced to

[050-179]act with him; the regular orders he found equally intractable—more

[050-180]especially the Observantins, whose name he endeavoured to change to

[050-181]Conventuals. "The spirituality," as he calls them, refused to take the

[050-182]oaths of abjuration and supremacy; refused to strike the name of the

[050-183]Bishop of Rome from their primers and mass-books, and seduced the rest

[050-184]into like contumacy. Finding persuasion of little avail, he sometimes

[050-185]resorted to harsher measures.

[050-186]Dr. Sall, a grey friar of Waterford, was brought to Dublin and

[050-187]imprisoned for preaching the new doctrines in the Spring of 1538;

[050-188]Thaddeus Byrne, another friar, was put in the pillory, and was reported

[050-189]to have committed suicide in the Castle, on the 14th of July of the

[050-190]same year; Sir Humfrey, parson of Saint Owens, and the suffragan Bishop

[050-191]of Meath, were "clapped in ward," for publicly praying for the Pope's

[050-192]weal and the King's conversion; another Bishop and friar were arrested

[050-193]and carried to Trim, for similar offences, but were liberated without

[050-194]trial, by Lord Deputy Gray; a friar of Waterford, in 1539, by order of

[050-195]the St. Leger Commission, was executed in the habit of his order, on a

[050-196]charge of "felony," and so left hanging "as a mirror for all his

[050-197]brethren." Yet, with all this severity, and all the temptations held

[050-198]out by the wealth of confiscated monasteries, none would abide the

[050-199]preaching of the new religion except the "Lord Butler, the Master of

[050-200]the Rolls (Allan), Mr. Treasurer (Brabazon), and one or two more of

[050-201]small reputation."

[050-202]The first test to which the firmness of the clergy had been put was in

[050-203]the Parliament convoked at Dublin by Lord Deputy Gray, in May, 1537.

[050-204]Anciently in such assemblies two proctors of each diocese, within the

[050-205]Pale, had been accustomed to sit and vote in the Upper House as

[050-206]representing their order, but the proposed tests of supremacy and

[050-207]abjuration were so boldly resisted by the proctors and spiritual peers

[050-208]on this occasion that the Lord Deputy was compelled to prorogue the

[050-209]Parliament without attaining its assent to those measures. During the

[050-210]recess a question was raised by the Crown lawyers as to the competency

[050-211]of the proctors to vote, while admitting their right to be present as

[050-212]councillors and assistants; this question, on an appeal to England, was

[050-213]declared in the negative, whereupon that learned body were excluded

[050-214]from all share in the future Irish legislation of this reign. Hence,

[050-215]whoever else are answerable for the election of 1541 the proctors of

[050-216]the clergy are not.

[050-217]Having thus reduced the clerical opposition in the Upper House, the

[050-218]work of monastic spoliation, covertly commenced two years before, under

[050-219]the pretence of reforming abuses, was more confidently resumed. In

[050-220]1536, an act had been passed vesting the property of all religious

[050-221]houses in the Crown; at which time the value of their moveables was

[050-222]estimated at 100,000 pounds and their yearly value at 32,000 pounds. In

[050-223]1537, eight abbeys were suppressed during the King's pleasure; in 1538,

[050-224]a commission issued for the suppression of monasteries; and in 1539,

[050-225]twenty-four great Houses, whose Abbots and Priors had been lords of

[050-226]Parliament, were declared "surrendered" to the King, and their late

[050-227]superiors were granted pensions for life. How these "surrenders" were

[050-228]procured we may judge from the case of Manus, Abbot of St. Mary's,

[050-229]Thurles, who was carried prisoner to Dublin, and suffered a long

[050-230]confinement for refusing to yield up his trust according to the desired

[050-231]formula. The work of confiscation was in these first years confined to

[050-232]the walled towns in English hands, the district of the Pale, and such

[050-233]points of the Irish country as could be conveniently reached. The great

[050-234]order of the Cistercians, established for more than four centuries at

[050-235]Mellifont, at Monastereven, at Bective, at Jerpoint, at Tintern, and at

[050-236]Dunbrody, were the first expelled from their cloisters and gardens. The

[050-237]Canons regular of St. Augustine at Trim, at Conal, at Athassel and at

[050-238]Kells, were next assailed by the degenerate Augustinian, who presided

[050-239]over the commission. The orders of St. Victor, of Aroacia, of St. John

[050-240]of Jerusalem, were extinguished wherever the arm of the Reformation

[050-241]could reach. The mendicant orders, spread into every district of the

[050-242]island, were not so easily erased from the soil; very many of the

[050-243]Dominican and Franciscan houses standing and flourishing far into the

[050-244]succeeding century.

[050-245]If the influence of the clergy counterbalanced the policy of the

[050-246]chiefs, the condition of the mass of the population—more especially of

[050-247]the inhabitants of the Pale and the marches—was such as to make them

[050-248]cherish the expectation that any governmental change whatever should be

[050-249]for the better. It was, under these circumstances, a far-reaching

[050-250]policy, which combined the causes and the remedy for social wrongs,

[050-251]with invectives against the old, and arguments in favour of the new

[050-252]religion. In order to understand what elements of discontent there were

[050-253]to be wrought to such conclusions, it is enough to give the merest

[050-254]glance at the social state of the lower classes under English

[050-255]authority. The St. Leger Commission represents the mixed population of

[050-256]the marches, and the Englishry of "the Pale" as burthened by

[050-257]accumulated exactions. Their lords quartered upon them at pleasure

[050-258]their horses, servants, and guests. They were charged with coin and

[050-259]livery—that is, horse-meat and man's-meat —when their lords travelled

[050-260]from place to place—with summer-oats, with providing for their

[050-261]cosherings, or feasts, at Christmas and Easter, with "black men and

[050-262]black money," for border defence, and with workmen and axemen from

[050-263]every ploughland, to work in the ditches, or to hew passages for the

[050-264]soldiery through the woods. Every aggravation of feudal wrong was

[050-265]inflicted on this harassed population. When a le Poer or a Butler

[050-266]married a daughter he exacted a sheep from every flock, and a cow from

[050-267]every village. When one of his sons went to England, a special tribute

[050-268]was levied on every village and ploughland to bear the young

[050-269]gentleman's travelling expenses. When the heads of any of the great

[050-270]houses hunted, their dogs were to be supplied by the tenants "with

[050-271]bread and milk, or butter." In the towns tailors, masons, and

[050-272]carpenters, were taxed for coin and livery; "mustrons" were employed in

[050-273]building halls, castles, stables, and barns, at the expense of the

[050-274]tenantry, for the sole use of the lord. The only effective law was an

[050-275]undigested jumble of the Brehon, the Civil, and the Common law; with

[050-276]the arbitrary ordinances of the marches, known as "the Statutes of

[050-277]Kilcash"—so called from a border stronghold near the foot of

[050-278]Slievenamon—a species of wild justice, resembling too often that

[050-279]administered by Robin Hood, or Rob Roy.

[050-281]Many circumstances concurring to promote plans so long cherished by

[050-282]Henry, St. Leger summoned a Parliament for the morrow after Trinity

[050-283]Sunday, being the 13th of the month of June, 1541. The attendance on

[050-284]the day named was not so full as was expected, so the opening was

[050-285]deferred till the following Thursday—being the feast of Corpus Christi.

[050-286]On that festival the Mass of the Holy Ghost was solemnly celebrated in

[050-287]St. Patrick's Cathedral, in which "two thousand persons" had assembled.

[050-288]The Lords of Parliament rode in cavalcade to the Church doors, headed

[050-289]by the Deputy. There were seen side by side in this procession the

[050-290]Earls of Desmond and Ormond, the Lords Barry, Roche and Bermingham;

[050-291]thirteen Barons of "the Pale," and a long train of Knights; Donogh

[050-292]O'Brien, Tanist of Thomond, the O'Reilly, O'Moore and McWilliam;

[050-293]Charles, son of Art Kavanagh, lord of Leinster, and Fitzpatrick, lord

[050-294]of Ossory. Never before had so many Milesian chiefs and Norman barons

[050-295]been seen together, except on the field of battle; never before had

[050-296]Dublin beheld marshalled in her streets what could by any stretch of

[050-297]imagination be considered a national representation. For this

[050-298]singularity, not less than for the business it transacted, the

[050-299]Parliament of 1541 will be held in lasting remembrance.

[050-300]In the sanctuary of St. Patrick's, two Archbishops and twelve Bishops

[050-301]assisted at the solemn mass, and the whole ceremony was highly

[050-302]imposing. "The like thereof," wrote St. Leger to Henry, "has not been

[050-303]seen here these many years." On the next day, Friday, the Commons

[050-304]elected Sir Thomas Cusack speaker, who, in "a right solemn

[050-305]proposition," opened at the bar of the Lords' House the main business

[050-306]of the session—the establishment of King Henry's supremacy. To this

[050-307]address Lord Chancellor Allen—"well and prudentlie answered;" and the

[050-308]Commons withdrew to their own chamber. The substance of both speeches

[050-309]was "briefly and prudentlie" declared in the Irish language to the

[050-310]Gaelic Lords, by the Earl of Ormond, "greatly to their contentation."

[050-311]Then St. Leger proposed that Henry and his heirs should have the title

[050-312]of King, and caused the "bill devised for the same to be read." This

[050-313]bill having been put to the Lords' House, both in Irish and English,

[050-314]passed its three readings at the same sitting. In the Commons it was

[050-315]adopted with equal unanimity the next day, when the Lord Deputy most

[050-316]joyfully gave his consent. Thus on Saturday, June 19th, 1541, the

[050-317]royalty of Ireland was first formally transferred to an English

[050-318]dynasty. On that day the triumphant St. Leger was enabled to write his

[050-319]royal master his congratulations on having added to his dignities

[050-320]"another imperial crown." On Sunday bonfires were made in honour of the

[050-321]event, guns fired, and wine on stoop was set in the streets. All

[050-322]prisoners, except those for capital offences, were liberated; _Te Deum_

[050-323]was sung in St. Patrick's, and King Henry issued his proclamation, on

[050-324]receipt of the intelligence, for a general pardon throughout _all_ his

[050-325]dominions. The new title was confirmed with great formality by the

[050-326]English Parliament in their session of 1542. Proclamation was formally

[050-327]made of it in London, on the 1st of July of that year, when it was

[050-328]moreover declared that after that date all persons being lawfully

[050-329]convicted of opposing the new dignity should "be adjudged high

[050-330]traitors"—"and suffer the pains of death."

[050-331]Thus was consummated the first political union of Ireland with England.

[050-332]The strangely-constituted Assembly, which had given its sanction to the

[050-333]arrangement, in the language of the Celt, the Norman, and the Saxon,

[050-334]continued in session till the end of July, when they were prorogued

[050-335]till November. They enacted several statutes, in completion of the

[050-336]great change they had decreed; and while some prepared for a journey to

[050-337]the court of their new sovereign, others returned to their homes, to

[050-338]account as best they could for the part they had played at Dublin.
