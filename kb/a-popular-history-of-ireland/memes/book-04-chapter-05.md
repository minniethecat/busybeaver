CHAPTER V.

[027-001]FROM THE RETURN OF HENRY II. TO ENGLAND TILL THE DEATH OF EARL

[027-002]RICHARD AND HIS PRINCIPAL COMPANIONS.

[027-003]The Ard-Righ Roderick, during the period of Henry the Second's stay in

[027-004]Ireland, had continued west of the Shannon. Unsupported by his

[027-005]suffragans, many of whom made peace with the invader, he attempted no

[027-006]military operation, nor had Henry time sufficient to follow him into

[027-007]his strongholds. It was reserved for this ill-fated, and, we cannot but

[027-008]think, harshly judged monarch, to outlive the first generation of the

[027-009]invaders of his country, and to close a reign which promised so

[027-010]brightly at the beginning, in the midst of a distracted, war-spent

[027-011]people, having preserved through all vicissitudes the title of

[027-012]sovereign, but little else that was of value to himself or others.

[027-013]Among the guests who partook of the Christmas cheer of King Henry at

[027-014]Dublin, we find mention of Tiernan O'Ruarc, the lord of Breffni and

[027-015]East-Meath. For the Methian addition to his possessions, Tiernan was

[027-016]indebted to his early alliance with Roderick, and the success of their

[027-017]joint arms. Anciently the east of Meath had been divided between the

[027-018]four families called "the four tribes of Tara," whose names are now

[027-019]anglicized O'Hart, O'Kelly, O'Connelly, and O'Regan. Whether to balance

[027-020]the power of the great West-Meath family of O'Melaghlin, or because

[027-021]these minor tribes were unable to defend themselves successfully,

[027-022]Roderick, like his father, had partitioned Meath, and given the seaward

[027-023]side a new master in the person of O'Ruarc. The investiture of Hugh de

[027-024]Lacy by King Henry with the seignory of the same district, led to a

[027-025]tragedy, the first of its kind in our annals, but destined to be the

[027-026]prototype of an almost indefinite series, in which the gainers were

[027-027]sometimes natives, but much oftener Normans.

[027-028]O'Ruarc gave de Lacy an appointment at the hill of Ward, near Athboy,

[027-029]in the year 1173, in order to adjust their conflicting claims upon

[027-030]East-Meath. Both parties naturally guarded against surprise, by having

[027-031]in readiness a troop of armed retainers. The principals met apart on

[027-032]the summit of the hill, amid the circumvallations of its ancient fort;

[027-033]a single unarmed interpreter only was present. An altercation having

[027-034]arisen, between them, O'Ruarc lost his temper, and raised the

[027-035]battle-axe, which all our warriors carried in those days, as the

[027-036]gentlemen of the last century did their swords; this was the signal for

[027-037]both troops of guards to march towards the spot. De Lacy, in attempting

[027-038]to fly, had been twice felled to the earth, when his followers, under

[027-039]Maurice Fitzgerald and Griffith, his nephew, came to his rescue, and

[027-040]assailed the chief of Breffni. It was now Tiernan's turn to attempt

[027-041]escaping, but as he mounted his horse the spear of Griffith brought him

[027-042]to the earth mortally wounded, and his followers fled. His head was

[027-043]carried in triumph to Dublin, where it was spiked over the northern

[027-044]gate, and his body was gibbeted on the northern wall, with the feet

[027-045]uppermost. Thus, a spectacle of intense pity to the Irish, did these

[027-046]severed members of one of their most famous nobles remain exposed on

[027-047]that side of the stronghold of the stranger which looks towards the

[027-048]pleasant plains of Meath and the verdant uplands of Cavan.

[027-050]The administration of de Lacy was now interrupted by a summons to join

[027-051]his royal master, sore beset by his own sons in Normandy. The Kings of

[027-052]France and Scotland were in alliance with those unnatural Princes, and

[027-053]their mother, Queen Eleanor, might he called the author of their

[027-054]rebellion. As all the force that could be spared from Ireland was

[027-055]needed for the preservation of Normandy, de Lacy hastened to obey the

[027-056]royal summons, and Earl Richard, by virtue of his rank of Marshal, took

[027-057]for the moment the command in chief. Henry, however, who never

[027-058]cordially forgave that adventurer, first required his presence in

[027-059]France, and when alarmed by ill news from Ireland, he sent him back to

[027-060]defend the conquests already made, he associated with him in the

[027-061]supreme command—though not apparently in the civil administration—the

[027-062]gallant Raymond _le gros_. And it was full time for the best head and

[027-063]the bravest sword among the first invaders to return to their work—a

[027-064]task not to be so easily achieved as many confident persons then

[027-065]believed, and as many ill-informed writers have since described it.

[027-066]During the early rule of de Lacy, Earl Richard had established himself

[027-067]at Ferns, assuming, to such of the Irish as adhered to him, the

[027-068]demeanour of a king. After Dermid's death, he styled himself, in utter

[027-069]disregard of Irish law, "Prince of Leinster," in virtue of his wife. He

[027-070]proceeded to create feudal dignitaries, placing at their head, as

[027-071]Constable of Leinster, Robert de Quincy, to whom he gave his daughter,

[027-072]by his first wife, in marriage. At this point the male representatives

[027-073]of King Dermid came to open rupture with the Earl. Donald _Kavanagh_,

[027-074]surnamed "the Handsome," and by the Normans usually spoken of as

[027-075]"Prince" Donald, could scarcely be expected to submit to an

[027-076]arrangement, so opposed to all ancient custom, and to his own

[027-077]interests. He had borne a leading part in the restoration of his

[027-078]father, but surely not to this end—the exclusion of the male

[027-079]succession. He had been one of King Henry's guests during the Christmas

[027-080]holidays of the year 1172, and had rendered him some sort of homage, as

[027-081]Prince of Leinster. Henry, ever ready to raise up rivals to Strongbow,

[027-082]seems to have received him into favour, until Eva, the Earl's wife,

[027-083]proved, both in Ireland and England, that Donald and his brother Enna,

[027-084]were born out of wedlock, and that there was no direct male heir of

[027-085]Dermid left, after the execution of Conor, the hostage put to death by

[027-086]King Roderick. To English notions this might have been conclusive

[027-087]against Donald's title, but to the Irish, among whom the electoral

[027-088]principle was the source of all chieftainry, it was not so. A large

[027-089]proportion of the patriotic Leinstermen—what might be called the native

[027-090]party—adhered to Donald _Kavanagh_, utterly rejecting the title derived

[027-091]through the lady Eva.

[027-092]Such conflicting interests could only be settled by a resort to force,

[027-093]and the bloody feud began by the Earl executing at Ferns one of

[027-094]Donald's sons, held by him as a hostage. In an expedition against

[027-095]O'Dempsey, who also refused to acknowledge his title, the Earl lost, in

[027-096]the campaign of 1173, his son-in-law, de Quincy, several other knights,

[027-097]and the "banner of Leinster." The following year we read in the

[027-098]Anglo-Irish Annals of Leinster, that King Donald's men, being moved

[027-099]against the Earl's men, made a great slaughter of English. Nor was this

[027-100]the worst defeat he suffered in the same year—1174. Marching into

[027-101]Munster he was encountered in a pitched battle at Thurles by the troops

[027-102]of the monarch Roderick, under command of his son, Conor, surnamed

[027-103]_Moinmoy_, and by the troops of Thomond, under Donald More O'Brien.

[027-104]With Strongbow were all who could be spared of the garrison of Dublin,

[027-105]including a strong detachment of Danish origin. Four knights and seven

[027-106]hundred (or, according to other accounts, seventeen hundred) men of the

[027-107]Normans were left dead on the field. Strongbow retreated with the

[027-108]remnant of his force to Waterford, but the news of the defeat having

[027-109]reached that city before him, the townspeople ran to arms and put his

[027-110]garrison of two hundred men to the sword. After encamping for a month

[027-111]on an island without the city, and hearing that Kilkenny Castle was

[027-112]taken and razed by O'Brien, he was feign to return to Dublin as best he

[027-113]could.

[027-114]His fortunes at the close of this campaign, were at their lowest ebb.

[027-115]The loss of de Quincy and the defeat of Thurles had sorely shaken his

[027-116]military reputation. His jealousy of that powerful family connexion,

[027-117]the Geraldines, had driven Maurice Fitzgerald and Raymond the Fat to

[027-118]retire in disgust into Wales. Donald Kavanagh, O'Dempsey, and the

[027-119]native party in Leinster, set him at defiance, and his own troops

[027-120]refused to obey the orders of his uncle Herve, demanding to be led by

[027-121]the more popular and youthful Raymond. To add to his embarrassments,

[027-122]Henry summoned him to France in the very crisis of his troubles, and he

[027-123]dared not disobey that jealous and exacting master. He was, however,

[027-124]not long detained by the English King. Clothed with supreme authority,

[027-125]and with Raymond for his lieutenant, he returned to resume the work of

[027-126]conquest. To conciliate the Geraldines, he at last consented to give

[027-127]his sister Basilia in marriage to the brilliant captain, on whose sword

[027-128]so much depended. At the same time Alina, the widow of de Quincy, was

[027-129]married to the second son of Fitzgerald, and Nesta Fitzgerald was

[027-130]united to Raymond's former rival, Herve. Thus, bound together, fortune

[027-131]returned in full tide to the adventurers. Limerick, which had been

[027-132]taken and burned to the water's edge by Donald O'Brien after the battle

[027-133]of Thurles, was recaptured and fortified anew; Waterford was more

[027-134]strongly garrisoned than ever; Donald _Kavanagh_ was taken off,

[027-135]apparently by treachery (A.D. 1175), and all seemed to promise the

[027-136]enjoyment of uninterrupted power to the Earl. But his end was already

[027-137]come. An ulcer in his foot brought on a long and loathsome illness,

[027-138]which terminated in his death, in the month of May, 1176, or 1177. He

[027-139]was buried in Christ Church, Dublin, which he had contributed to

[027-140]enlarge, and was temporarily succeeded in the government of the Normans

[027-141]by his lieutenant and brother-in-law, Raymond. By the Lady Eva he left

[027-142]one daughter, Isabel, married at the age of fourteen to William

[027-143]Marshall, Earl of Pembroke, who afterwards claimed the proprietary of

[027-144]Leinster, by virtue of this marriage. Lady Isabel left again five

[027-145]daughters, who were the ancestresses of the Mortimers, Braces, and

[027-146]other historic families of England and Scotland. And so the blood of

[027-147]Earl Richard and his Irish Princess descended for many generations to

[027-148]enrich other houses and ennoble other names than his own.

[027-149]Strongbow is described by _Giraldus_, whose personal sketches, of the

[027-150]leading invaders form the most valuable part of his book, as less a

[027-151]statesman than a soldier, and more a soldier than a general. His

[027-152]complexion was freckled, his neck slender, his voice feminine and

[027-153]shrill, and his temper equable and uniform. His career in Ireland was

[027-154]limited to seven years in point of time, and his resources were never

[027-155]equal to the task he undertook. Had they been so, or had he not been so

[027-156]jealously counteracted by his suzerain, he might have founded a new

[027-157]Norman dynasty on as solid a basis as William, or as Rollo himself had

[027-158]done.

[027-159]Raymond and the Geraldines had now, for a brief moment, the supreme

[027-160]power, civil and military, in their own hands. In his haste to take

[027-161]advantage of the Earl's death, of which he had privately been informed

[027-162]by a message from his wife, Raymond left Limerick in the hands of

[027-163]Donald More O'Brien, exacting, we are told, a solemn oath from the

[027-164]Prince of Thomond to protect the city, which the latter broke before

[027-165]the Norman garrisons were out of sight of its walls. This story, like

[027-166]many others of the same age, rests on the uncertain authority of the

[027-167]vain, impetuous and passionate _Giraldus_. Whether the loss of Limerick

[027-168]discredited him with the king, or the ancient jealousy of the first

[027-169]adventurers prevailed in the royal councils, Henry, on hearing of

[027-170]Strongbow's death, at once despatched as Lord Justice, William

[027-171]Fitz-Aldelm de Burgo, first cousin to Hubert de Burgo, Chief Justiciary

[027-172]of England, and, like Fitz-Aldelm, descended from Arlotta, mother of

[027-173]William the Conqueror, by Harlowen de Burgo, her first husband. From

[027-174]him have descended the noble family of de Burgo, or Burke, so

[027-175]conspicuous in the after annals of our island. In the train of the new

[027-176]Justiciary came John de Courcy, another name destined to become

[027-177]historical, but before relating his achievements, we must conclude the

[027-178]narrative so far as regards the first set of adventurers.

[027-179]Maurice Fitzgerald, the common ancestor of the Earls of Desmond and

[027-180]Kildare, the Knights of Glyn, of Kerry, and of all the Irish

[027-181]Geraldines, died at Wexford in the year 1177. Raymond the Fat,

[027-182]superseded by Fitz-Aldelm, and looked on coldly by the King, retired to

[027-183]his lands in the same county, and appears only once more in arms—in the

[027-184]year 1182—in aid of his uncle, Robert Fitzstephen. This premier invader

[027-185]had been entrusted by the new ruler with the command of the garrison of

[027-186]Cork, as Milo de Cogan had been with that of Waterford, and both had

[027-187]been invested with equal halves of the principality of Desmond. De

[027-188]Cogan, Ralph, son of Fitzstephen, and other knights had been cut off by

[027-189]surprise, at the house of one McTire, near Lismore, in 1182, and all

[027-190]Desmond was up in arms for the expulsion of the foreign garrisons.

[027-191]Raymond sailed from Wexford to the aid of his uncle, and succeeded in

[027-192]relieving the city from the sea. But Fitzstephen, afflicted with grief

[027-193]for the death of his son, and worn down with many anxieties, suffered

[027-194]the still greater loss of his reason. From thenceforth, we hear no more

[027-195]of either uncle or nephew, and we may therefore account this the last

[027-196]year of Robert Fitzstephen, Milo de Cogan, and Raymond _le gros_. Herve

[027-197]de Montmorency, the ancient rival of Raymond, had three years earlier

[027-198]retired from the world, to become a brother in the Monastery of the

[027-199]Holy Trinity, at Canterbury. His Irish estates passed to his brother

[027-200]Geoffrey, who subsequently became Justiciary of the Normans in Ireland,

[027-201]the successful rival of the Marshals, and founder of the Irish title of

[027-202]Mountmorres. The posterity of Raymond survived in the noble family of

[027-203]Grace, Barons of Courtstown, in Ossory. It is not, therefore, strictly

[027-204]true, what Geoffrey Keating and the authors he followed have

[027-205]asserted—that the first Normans were punished by the loss of posterity

[027-206]for the crimes and outrages they had committed, in their various

[027-207]expeditions.

[027-208]Let us be just even to these spoilers of our race. They were fair

[027-209]specimens of the prevailing type of Norman character. Indomitable

[027-210]bravery was not their only virtue. In patience, in policy, and in

[027-211]rising superior to all obstacles and reverses, no group of conquerors

[027-212]ever surpassed Strongbow and his companions. Ties of blood and

[027-213]brotherhood in arms were strong between them, and whatever unfair

[027-214]advantages they allowed themselves to take of their enemy, they were in

[027-215]general constant and devoted in their friendships towards each other.

[027-216]Rivalries and intrigues were not unknown among them, but generous

[027-217]self-denial, and chivalrous self-reliance were equally as common. If it

[027-218]had been the lot of our ancestors to be effectually conquered, they

[027-219]could hardly have yielded to nobler foes. But as they proved themselves

[027-220]able to resist successfully the prowess of this hitherto invincible

[027-221]race, their honour is augmented in proportion to the energy and genius,

[027-222]both for government and war, brought to bear against them.

[027-223]Neither should we overstate the charge of impiety. If the invaders

[027-224]broke down and burned churches in the heat of battle, they built better

[027-225]and costlier temples out of the fruits of victory. Christ Church,

[027-226]Dublin, Dunbrody Abbey, on the estuary of Waterford, the Grey Friars'

[027-227]Abbey at Wexford, and other religious houses long stood, or still

[027-228]stand, to show that although the first Norman, like the first Dane,

[027-229]thirsted after spoil, and lusted after land, unlike the Dane, he

[027-230]created, he enriched, he improved, wherever he conquered.
