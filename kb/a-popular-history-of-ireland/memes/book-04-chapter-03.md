CHAPTER III.

[025-001]THE FIRST CAMPAIGN OF EARL RICHARD—SIEGE OF DUBLIN—DEATH OF KING DERMID

[025-002]McMURROGH.

[025-003]The campaigns of 1168 and 1169 had ended prosperously for Dermid in the

[025-004]treaty of Ferns. By that treaty he had bound himself to bring no more

[025-005]Normans into the country, and to send those already in his service back

[025-006]to their homes. But in the course of the same autumn or winter, in

[025-007]which this agreement was solemnly entered into, he welcomed the arrival

[025-008]at Wexford—of Maurice Fitzgerald—son of the fair Nesta by her first

[025-009]husband—and immediately employed this fresh force, consisting of 10

[025-010]knights, 30 esquires, and 100 footmen, upon a hosting which harried the

[025-011]open country about Dublin, and induced the alarmed inhabitants to send

[025-012]hostages into his camp, bearing proffers of allegiance and amity. As

[025-013]yet he did not feel in force sufficient to attack the city, for, if he

[025-014]had been, his long cherished vengeance against its inhabitants would

[025-015]not have been postponed till another season.

[025-016]In the meantime he had written most urgent letters to Earl Richard to

[025-017]hasten his arrival, according to the terms agreed upon at Bristol. That

[025-018]astute and ambitious nobleman had been as impatiently biding his time

[025-019]as Dermid had been his coming. Knowing the jealous sovereign under whom

[025-020]he served, he had gone over to France to obtain Henry's sanction to the

[025-021]Irish enterprise, but had been answered by the monarch, in oracular

[025-022]phrases, which might mean anything or nothing. Determined, however, to

[025-023]interpret these doubtful words in his own sense, he despatched his

[025-024]vanguard early in the spring of the year 1170, under the command of his

[025-025]uncle Herve and a company of 10 knights and 70 archers, under Raymond,

[025-026]son of William, lord of Carew, elder brother of Maurice Fitzgerald, and

[025-027]grandson of Nesta. In the beginning of May, Raymond, nicknamed _le

[025-028]gros_, or the Fat, entered Waterford harbour, and landed eight miles

[025-029]below the city, under the rock of Dundonolf, on the east, or Wexford

[025-030]side. Here they rapidly threw up a camp to protect themselves against

[025-031]attack, and to hold the landing place for the convenience of the future

[025-032]expedition. A tumultuous body of natives, amounting, according to the

[025-033]Norman account, to 3,000 men, were soon seen swarming across the Suir

[025-034]to attack the foreigners. They were men of Idrone and Desies, under

[025-035]their chiefs, O'Ryan and O'Phelan, and citizens of Waterford, who now

[025-036]rushed towards the little fortress, entirely unprepared for the long

[025-037]and deadly range of the Welsh and Flemish crossbows. Thrown into

[025-038]confusion by the unexpected discharge, in which every shot from behind

[025-039]the ramparts of turf brought down its man, they wavered and broke;

[025-040]Raymond and Herve then sallied out upon the fugitives, who were fain to

[025-041]escape, as many as could, to the other side of the river, leaving 500

[025-042]prisoners, including 70 chief citizens of Waterford behind them. These

[025-043]were all inhumanly massacred, according to _Giraldus_, the eulogist of

[025-044]all the Geraldines, by the order of Herve, contrary to the entreaties

[025-045]of Raymond. Their legs were first violently broken, and they were then

[025-046]hurled down the rocks into the tide. Five hundred men could not well be

[025-047]so captured and put to death by less than an equal number of hands, and

[025-048]we may, therefore, safely set down that number as holding the camp of

[025-049]Dundonolf during the summer months of the year.

[025-050]Earl Richard had not completed his arrangements until the month of

[025-051]August—so that his uncle and lieutenant had to hold the post they had

[025-052]seized for fully three months, awaiting his arrival in the deepest

[025-053]anxiety. At last, leaving his castle in Pembroke, he marched with his

[025-054]force through North Wales, by way of St. David's to Milford Haven—"and

[025-055]still as he went he took up all the best chosen and picked men he could

[025-056]get." At Milford, just as he was about to embark, he received an order

[025-057]from King Henry forbidding the expedition. Wholly disregarding this

[025-058]missive he hastened on board with 200 knights and 1,200 infantry in his

[025-059]company, and on the eve of St. Bartholomew's Day (August 23rd), landed

[025-060]safely under the earthwork of Dundonolf, where he was joyfully received

[025-061]by Raymond at the head of 40 knights, and a corresponding number of

[025-062]men-at-arms. The next day the whole force, under the Earl, "who had all

[025-063]things in readiness" for such an enterprise, proceeded to lay siege to

[025-064]Waterford. Malachy O'Phelan, the brave lord of Desies, forgetting all

[025-065]ancient enmity against his Danish neighbours, had joined the townsmen

[025-066]to assist in the defence. Twice the besieged beat back the assailants,

[025-067]until Raymond perceiving at an angle of the wall the wooden props upon

[025-068]which a house rested, ordered them to be cut away, on which the house

[025-069]fell to the ground, and a breach was effected. The men-at-arms then

[025-070]burst in, slaughtering the inhabitants without mercy. In the tower,

[025-071]long known as Reginald's, or the ring tower, O'Phelan and Reginald, the

[025-072]Dano-Irish chief, held out until the arrival of King Dermid, whose

[025-073]intercession procured them such terms as led to their surrender. Then,

[025-074]amid the ruins of the burning city, and the muttered malediction of its

[025-075]surviving inhabitants, the ill-omened marriage of Eva McMurrogh with

[025-076]Richard de Clare was gaily celebrated, and the compact entered into at

[025-077]Bristol three years before was perfected.

[025-079]The marriage revelry was hardly over when tidings came from Dublin that

[025-080]Asculph MacTorcall, its Danish lord, had, either by the refusal of the

[025-081]annual tribute, or in some other manner, declared his independence of

[025-082]Dermid, and invoked the aid of the monarch Roderick, in defence of that

[025-083]city. Other messengers brought news that Roderick had assumed the

[025-084]protection of Dublin, and was already encamped at the head of a large

[025-085]army at Clondalkin, with a view of intercepting the march of the

[025-086]invaders from the south. The whole Leinster and Norman force, with the

[025-087]exception of a troop of archers left to garrison Waterford, were now

[025-088]put in motion for the siege of the chief city of the Hibernicized

[025-089]descendants of the Northmen. Informed of Roderick's position, which

[025-090]covered Dublin on the south and west, Dermid and Richard followed

[025-091]boldly the mountain paths and difficult roads which led by the secluded

[025-092]city of Glendalough, and thence along the coast road from Bray towards

[025-093]the mouth of the Liffey, until they arrived unexpectedly within the

[025-094]lines of Roderick, to the amazement and terror of the townsmen.

[025-095]The force which now, under the command-in-chief of Dermid, sat down to

[025-096]the siege of Dublin, was far from being contemptible. For a year past

[025-097]he had been recognized in Leinster as fully as any of his predecessors,

[025-098]and had so strengthened his military position as to propose nothing

[025-099]short of the conquest of the whole country. His choice of a line of

[025-100]march sufficiently shows how thoroughly he had overcome the former

[025-101]hostility of the stubborn mountaineers of Wicklow. The exact numbers

[025-102]which he encamped before the gates of Dublin are nowhere given, but on

[025-103]the march from Waterford, the vanguard, led by Milo de Cogan, consisted

[025-104]of 700 Normans and "an Irish battalion," which, taken literally, would

[025-105]mean 3,000 men, under Donald _Kavanagh_; Raymond the Fat followed "with

[025-106]800 British;" Dermid led on "the chief part of the Irish" (number not

[025-107]given), in person; Richard commanded the rear-guard, "300 British and

[025-108]1,000 Irish soldiers." Altogether, it is not exorbitant to conjecture

[025-109]that the Leinster Prince led to the siege of Dublin an army of about

[025-110]10,000 native troops, 1,500 Welsh and Flemish archers, and 250 knights.

[025-111]Except the handful who remained with Fitzstephen to defend his fort at

[025-112]Carrick, on the Slaney, and the archers left in Waterford, the entire

[025-113]Norman force in Ireland, at this time, were united in the siege. Of the

[025-114]foreign knights many were eminent for courage and capacity, both in

[025-115]peace and war. The most distinguished among them were Maurice

[025-116]Fitzgerald, the common ancestor of the Geraldines of Desmond and

[025-117]Kildare; Raymond the Fat, ancestor of the Graces of Ossory; the two

[025-118]Fitz-Henries, grandsons of Henry I., and the fair Nesta; Walter de

[025-119]Riddlesford, first Baron of Bray; Robert de Quincy, son-in-law and

[025-120]standard-bearer to Earl Richard; Herve, uncle to the Earl, and Gilbert

[025-121]de Clare, his son; Milo de Cogan, the first who entered Dublin by

[025-122]assault, and its first Norman governor; the de Barries, and de

[025-123]Prendergast. Other founders of Norman-Irish houses, as the de Lacies,

[025-124]de Courcies, le Poers, de Burgos, Butlers, Berminghams, came not over

[025-125]until the landing of Henry II., or still later, with his son John.

[025-126]The townsmen of Dublin had every reason, from their knowledge of

[025-127]Dermid's cruel character, to expect the worst at his hands and those of

[025-128]his allies. The warning of Waterford was before them, but besides this

[025-129]they had a special cause of apprehension, Dermid's father having been

[025-130]murdered in their midst, and his body ignominiously interred with the

[025-131]carcase of a dog. Roderick having failed to intercept him, the

[025-132]citizens, either to gain time or really desiring to arrive at an

[025-133]accommodation, entered into negotiations. Their ambassador for this

[025-134]purpose was Lorcan, or Lawrence O'Toole, the first Archbishop of the

[025-135]city, and its first prelate of Milesian origin. This illustrious man,

[025-136]canonized both by sanctity and patriotism, was then in the thirty-ninth

[025-137]year of his age, and the ninth of his episcopate. His father was lord

[025-138]of Imayle and chief of his clan; his sister had been wife of Dermid and

[025-139]mother of Eva, the prize-bride of Earl Richard. He himself had been a

[025-140]hostage with Dermid in his youth, and afterwards Abbot of Glendalough,

[025-141]the most celebrated monastic city of Leinster. He stood, therefore, to

[025-142]the besieged, being their chief pastor, in the relation of a father; to

[025-143]Dermid, and strangely enough to Strongbow also, as brother-in-law and

[025-144]uncle by marriage. A fitter ambassador could not be found.

[025-145]Maurice Regan, the "_Latiner_," or Secretary of Dermid, had advanced to

[025-146]the walls, and summoned the city to surrender, and deliver up "30

[025-147]pledges" to his master, their lawful Prince. Asculph, son of Torcall,

[025-148]was in favour of the surrender, but the citizens could not agree among

[025-149]themselves as to hostages. No one was willing to trust himself to the

[025-150]notoriously untrustworthy Dermid. The Archbishop was then sent out on

[025-151]the part of the citizens to arrange the terms in detail. He was

[025-152]received with all reverence in the camp, but while he was deliberating

[025-153]with the commanders without, and the townsmen were anxiously awaiting

[025-154]his return, Milo de Cogan and Raymond the Fat, seizing the opportunity,

[025-155]broke into the city at the head of their companies, and began to put

[025-156]the inhabitants ruthlessly to the sword. They were soon followed by the

[025-157]whole force eager for massacre and pillage. The Archbishop hastened

[025-158]back to endeavour to stay the havoc which was being made of his people.

[025-159]He threw himself before the infuriated Irish and Normans, he

[025-160]threatened, he denounced, he bared his own breast to the swords of the

[025-161]assassins. All to little purpose; the blood fury exhausted itself

[025-162]before peace settled over the city. Its Danish chief, Asculph, with

[025-163]many of his followers, escaped to their ships, and fled to the Isle of

[025-164]Man and the Hebrides in search of succour and revenge. Roderick,

[025-165]unprepared to besiege the enemy who had thus outmarched and outwitted

[025-166]him at that season of the year—it could not be earlier than

[025-167]October—broke up his encampment at Clondalkin, and retired to

[025-168]Connaught. Earl Richard having appointed de Cogan his governor of

[025-169]Dublin, followed on the rear of the retreating _Ard-Righ_, at the

[025-170]instigation of McMurrogh, burning and plundering the churches of Kells,

[025-171]Clonard and Slane, and carrying off the hostages of East-Meath.

[025-172]Though Dermid seemed to have forgotten altogether the conditions of the

[025-173]treaty of Ferns, yet not so Roderick. When he reached Athlone he caused

[025-174]Conor, son of Dermid, and the son of Donald _Kavanagh_, and the son of

[025-175]Dermid's fosterer, who had been given him as hostages for the

[025-176]fulfilment of that treaty, so grossly violated in every particular, to

[025-177]be beheaded. Dermid indulged in impotent vows of vengeance against

[025-178]Roderick, when he heard of these executions which his own perjuries had

[025-179]provoked; he swore that nothing short of the conquest of Connaught in

[025-180]the following spring would satisfy his revenge, and he sent the

[025-181]Ard-Righ his defiance to that purport. Two other events of military

[025-182]consequence marked the close of the year 1170. The foreign garrison of

[025-183]Waterford was surprised and captured by Cormac McCarthy, Prince of

[025-184]Desmond, and Henry II. having prohibited all intercourse between his

[025-185]lieges and his disobedient subject, Earl Richard, the latter had

[025-186]despatched Raymond the Fat, with the most humble submission of himself

[025-187]and his new possessions to his Majesty's decision. And so with Asculph,

[025-188]son of Torcall, recruiting in the isles of Insi-Gall, Lawrence, the

[025-189]Archbishop, endeavouring to unite the proud and envious Irish lords

[025-190]into one united phalanx, and Roderick, preparing for the new year's

[025-191]campaign, the winter of 1170-'71, came, and waned, and went.

[025-192]One occurrence of the succeeding spring may most appropriately be

[025-193]dismissed here—the death of the wretched and odious McMurrogh. This

[025-194]event happened, according to _Giraldus_, in the kalends of May. The

[025-195]Irish Annals surround his death-bed with all the horrors appropriate to

[025-196]such a scene. He became, they say, "putrid while living," through the

[025-197]miracles of St. Columbcille and St. Finian, whose churches he had

[025-198]plundered; "and he died at Fernamore, without making a will, without

[025-199]penance, without the body of Christ, without unction, as his evil deeds

[025-200]deserved." We have no desire to meditate over the memory of such a man.

[025-201]He, far more than his predecessor, whatever that predecessor's crimes

[025-202]might have been, deserved to have been buried with a dog.
