## Overview

The Act of Union, passed by both the Irish and British Parliaments in 1800, abolished the independent [[irish-parliament|Irish Parliament]] and created a legislative union between Great Britain and Ireland effective January 1, 1801. It was one of the most consequential political events in Irish history, transferring all legislative authority to the Parliament of the United Kingdom at Westminster. (106-004)

The Union was promoted by the British government of William Pitt the Younger in response to the Irish Rebellion of 1798, and was passed by the [[irish-parliament|Irish Parliament]] after an intense and controversial campaign involving extensive bribery and patronage. (106-233)

## Background

### Grattan's Parliament

Since 1782, Ireland had enjoyed substantial legislative independence under what was known as "Grattan's Parliament," named for [[henry-grattan|Henry Grattan]], its most celebrated orator. This period saw the removal of many commercial restrictions and the beginnings of Catholic relief. However, the [[irish-parliament|Irish Parliament]] remained exclusively Protestant, and the executive remained under the control of the British-appointed Lord Lieutenant.

### The Rebellion of 1798

The [[united-irishmen|United Irishmen]] rebellion of 1798, with its appeal for French intervention, convinced Pitt's government that the separate [[irish-parliament|Irish Parliament]] was a security risk. The British cabinet determined that only a full legislative union could secure Ireland against future rebellion and French influence.

### Parliamentary Maneuvering

By January 1800, the Union question stood thus: 27 new pro-Union members had been added to the [[irish-parliament|Irish House of Commons]] through the purchase of pocket boroughs. Lord Clare marshaled support in the House of Lords, where he commanded a majority of 75 to 26 for his Union resolutions. (106-004) (106-031)

## The Passage of the Union

### Opposition

The Union faced fierce resistance. [[henry-grattan|Henry Grattan]], though in failing health, returned to Parliament to oppose it. John Foster, the Speaker, led the anti-Union forces. The vast majority of the Irish people were opposed: the Catholic middle classes feared the loss of the Parliament that had begun to show them some relief, while the Protestant patriots saw it as the extinction of Irish nationhood. (106-020)

### The Union Bill

On the 5th of February, a formal message proposing a basis of Union was communicated to the [[irish-parliament|Irish Parliament]]. The proposed articles included the abolition of the [[irish-parliament|Irish Parliament]], representation of Ireland by 100 MPs at Westminster, and the establishment of a united Church of England and Ireland. Lord Clare sketched his plan of Union, which, while complex in its details, had the simple effect of transferring all legislative power to Westminster. (106-103) (106-034)

The bill proceeded through the Irish Commons. On the 7th of June, the third reading of the bill for a "legislative union between Great Britain and Ireland" was carried. The Union became law, effective January 1, 1801. (106-145)

### Means of Passage

The means by which the Union was passed were controversial and remain so. It was said that in both Houses only six or seven members were known to have changed their votes from conviction; the vast majority were swayed by patronage—peerages, pensions, offices, and cash payments. Borough owners were compensated for the abolition of their seats. (106-210)

## Aftermath

The Union was never utilized for Ireland in the way its proponents had promised. [[catholic-emancipation|Catholic Emancipation]], which many pro-Union Catholics had been led to expect, was delayed for nearly thirty years. Economic benefits failed to materialize for most Irish people. The abolition of the [[irish-parliament|Irish Parliament]] became a central grievance of Irish nationalism, and the movement for its Repeal—led first by [[daniel-oconnell|Daniel O'Connell]] and later by Charles Stewart Parnell—dominated Irish politics throughout the nineteenth century. (106-239)

The Union Jack, incorporating the cross of [[saint-patrick|St. Patrick]], became the flag of the new United Kingdom—the most visible daily reminder, to those who resented it, of the Union's permanence. (106-201)
