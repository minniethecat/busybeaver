CHAPTER V.

[056-001]THE "UNDERTAKERS" IN ULSTER AND LEINSTER—DEFEAT AND DEATH OF SIR JAMES

[056-002]FITZMAURICE.

[056-003]Queen Elizabeth, when writing to Lord Sussex of a rumoured rising by

[056-004]O'Neil, desired him to assure her lieges at Dublin, that if O'Neil did

[056-005]rise, "it would be for their advantage; for there will be estates for

[056-006]them who want." The Sidney policy of treating Ireland as a discovered

[056-007]country, whose inhabitants had no right to the soil, except such as the

[056-008]discoverers graciously conceded to them—begat a new order of men,

[056-009]unknown to the history of other civilized states, which order we must

[056-010]now be at some pains to introduce to the reader.

[056-011]These "Undertakers," as they were called, differed widely from the

[056-012]Norman invaders of a former age. The Norman generally espoused the

[056-013]cause of some native chief, and took his pay in land; what he got by

[056-014]the sword he held by the sword. But the Undertaker was usually a man of

[056-015]peace—a courtier like Sir Christopher Hatton—a politician like Sir

[056-016]Walter Raleigh—a poet like Edmund Spencer, or a spy and forger like

[056-017]Richard Boyle, first Earl of Cork. He came, in the wake of war, with

[056-018]his elastic "letters patent," or, if he served in the field, it was

[056-019]mainly with a view to the subsequent confiscations. He was adroit at

[056-020]finding flaws in ancient titles, skilled in all the feudal quibbles of

[056-021]fine and recovery, and ready to employ the secret dagger where hard

[056-022]swearing and fabricated documents might fail to make good his title.

[056-023]Sometimes men of higher mark and more generous dispositions, allured by

[056-024]the temptations of the social revolution, would enter on the same

[056-025]pursuits, but they generally miscarried from want of what was then

[056-026]cleverly called "subtlety," but which plain people could not easily

[056-027]distinguish from lying and perjury. What greatly assisted them in their

[056-028]designs was the fact that feudal tenures had never been general in

[056-029]Ireland, so that by an easy process of reasoning they could prove

[056-030]nineteen-twentieths of all existing titles "defective," according to

[056-031]their notions of the laws of property.

[056-032]Sir Peter Carew, already mentioned, was one of the earliest of the

[056-033]Undertakers. He had been bred up as page to the Prince of Orange, and

[056-034]had visited the Courts of France, Germany, and Constantinople. He

[056-035]claimed, by virtue of his descent from Robert Fitzstephen, the barony

[056-036]of Idrone, in Carlow, and one half the kingdom of Desmond. Sir Henry

[056-037]Sidney had admitted these pretensions, partly as a menace against the

[056-038]Kavanaghs and Geraldines, and Sir Peter established himself at

[056-039]Leighlin, where he kept great house, with one hundred servants, over

[056-040]one hundred kerne, forty horse, a stall in his stable, a seat at his

[056-041]board for all comers. He took an active part in all military

[056-042]operations, and fell fighting gallantly on a memorable day to be

[056-043]hereafter mentioned.

[056-045]After the attainder of John the Proud in 1569, Sir Thomas Smith,

[056-046]Secretary to the Queen, obtained a grant of the district of the Ards of

[056-047]Down, for his illegitimate son, who accordingly entered on the task of

[056-048]its plantation. But the O'Neils of Clandeboy, the owners of the soil,

[056-049]attacked the young Undertaker, who met a grave where he had come to

[056-050]found a lordship. A higher name was equally unfortunate in the same

[056-051]field of adventure. Walter Devereux, Earl of Essex (father of the Essex

[056-052]still more unfortunate), obtained in 1573 a grant of one moiety of

[056-053]Farney and Clandeboy, and having mortgaged his English estates to the

[056-054]Queen for 10,000 pounds, associated with himself many other

[056-055]adventurers. On the 16th of August, he set sail from Liverpool,

[056-056]accompanied by the Lords Dacre and Rich, Sir Henry Knollys, the three

[056-057]sons of Lord Norris, and a multitude of the common people. But as he

[056-058]had left one powerful enemy at court in Leicester—so he found a second

[056-059]at Dublin, in the acting deputy, Fitzwilliam. Though gratified with the

[056-060]title of President of Ulster and afterwards that of Marshal of Ireland,

[056-061]he found his schemes constantly counteracted by orders from Dublin or

[056-062]from England. He was frequently ordered off from his head-quarters at

[056-063]Newry, on expeditions into Munster, until those who had followed his

[056-064]banner became disheartened and mutinous. The O'Neils and the Antrim

[056-065]Scots harassed his colony and increased his troubles. He attempted by

[056-066]treachery to retrieve his fortunes. Having invited the alliance of Con

[056-067]O'Donnell, he seized that chief and sent him prisoner to Dublin.

[056-068]Subsequently his chief opponent, Brian, lord of Clandeboy, paid him an

[056-069]amicable visit, accompanied by his wife, brother, and household. As

[056-070]they were seated at table on the fourth day of their stay, the soldiers

[056-071]of Essex burst into the banquet hall, put them all, "women, youths and

[056-072]maidens," to the sword. Brian and his wife were saved from the

[056-073]slaughter only to undergo at Dublin the death and mutilation inflicted

[056-074]upon traitors. Yet the ambitious schemes of Walter of Essex did not

[056-075]prosper the more of all these crimes. He died at Dublin, two years

[056-076]afterwards (1576), in the 36th year of his age, as was generally

[056-077]believed from poison administered by the orders of the arch-poisoner,

[056-078]Leicester, who immediately upon his death married his widow.

[056-079]It is apparent that the interest of the Undertakers could not be to

[056-080]establish peace in Ireland so long as war might be profitably waged.

[056-081]The new "English interest" thus created was often hostile to the

[056-082]soundest rules of policy and always opposed to the dictates of right

[056-083]and justice; but the double desire to conquer and to convert—to

[056-084]anglicize and Protestantize—blinded many to the lawless means by which

[056-085]they were worked out. The massacre of 400 persons of the chief families

[056-086]of Leix and Offally, which took place at Mullaghmast in 1577, is an

[056-087]evidence of how the royal troops were used to promote the ends of the

[056-088]Undertakers. To Mullaghmast, one of the ancient raths of Leinster,

[056-089]situated about five miles from Athy in Kildare, the O'Moores, O'Kellys,

[056-090]Lalors, and other Irish tribes were invited by the local commander of

[056-091]the Queen's troops, Francis Cosby. The Bowens, Hartpoles, Pigotts,

[056-092]Hovendons, and other adventurers who had grants or designs upon the

[056-093]neighbouring territory were invited to meet them. One of the Lalors,

[056-094]perceiving that none of those who entered the rath before him emerged

[056-095]again, caused his friends to fall back while he himself advanced alone.

[056-096]At the very entrance he beheld the dead bodies of some of his

[056-097]slaughtered kinsmen; drawing his sword, he fought his way back to his

[056-098]friends, who barely escaped with their lives to Dysart. Four hundred

[056-099]victims, including 180 of the name of O'Moore, are said to have fallen

[056-100]in this deliberate butchery. Rory O'Moore, the chief of his name,

[056-101]avenged this massacre by many a daring deed. In rapid succession he

[056-102]surprised Naas, Athy, and Leighlin. From the rapidity with which his

[056-103]blows were struck in Kildare, Carlow, and Kilkenny, he appeared to be

[056-104]ubiquitous. He was the true type of a guerilla leader, yet merciful as

[056-105]brave. While Naas was burning, he sat coolly at the market cross

[056-106]enjoying the spectacle, but he suffered no lives to be taken. Having

[056-107]captured Cosby, he did not, as might be expected, put him to death. His

[056-108]confidence in his own prowess and resources amounted to rashness, and

[056-109]finally caused his death. Coming forth from a wood to parley with a

[056-110]party of the Queen's troops led by his neighbour, the Lord of Ossory, a

[056-111]common soldier ran him through the body with a sword. This was on the

[056-112]last day of June, 1578—a day mournful through all the midland districts

[056-113]for the loss of their best and bravest captain.

[056-114]While these events occupied the minds and tongues of men in the North

[056-115]and East, a brief respite from the horrors of war was permitted to the

[056-116]province of Munster. The Earl of Desmond, only too happy to be

[056-117]tolerated in the possession of his 570,000 acres, was eager enough to

[056-118]testify his allegiance by any sort of service. His brothers, though

[056-119]less compliant, followed his example for the moment, and no danger was

[056-120]to be apprehended in that quarter, except from the indomitable James

[056-121]Fitzmaurice, self-exiled on the continent. No higher tribute could be

[056-122]paid to the character of that heroic man than the closeness with which

[056-123]all his movements were watched by English spies, specially set upon his

[056-124]track. They followed him to the French court, to St. Malo's (where he

[056-125]resided for some time with his family), to Madrid, whence he sent his

[056-126]two sons to the famous University of Alcala, and from Madrid to Rome.

[056-127]The honourable reception he received at the hands of the French and

[056-128]Spanish Sovereigns was duly reported; yet both being at peace with

[056-129]England, his plans elicited no open encouragement from either. At Rome,

[056-130]however, he obtained some material and much moral support. Here he

[056-131]found many zealous advocates among the English and Irish refugees—among

[056-132]them the celebrated Saunders, Allen, sometimes called Cardinal Alien,

[056-133]and O'Mulrian, Bishop of Killaloe. A force of about 1,000 men was

[056-134]enlisted at the expense of Pope Gregory XIII., in the Papal States, and

[056-135]placed under an experienced captain, Hercules Pisano. They were shipped

[056-136]at Civita Vecchia by a squadron under the command of Thomas Stukely, an

[056-137]English adventurer, who had served both for and against the Irish

[056-138]Catholics, but had joined Fitzmaurice in Spain and accompanied him to

[056-139]Rome. On the strength of some remote or pretended relationship to the

[056-140]McMurroghs, Stukely obtained from the Pope the titles of Marquis of

[056-141]Leinster and Baron of Idrone and Ross; at Fitzmaurice's urgent

[056-142]request—so it is stated—he was named Vice-Admiral of the fleet. The

[056-143]whole expedition was fitted out at the expense of the Pope, but it was

[056-144]secretly agreed that it should be supported, after landing in Ireland,

[056-145]at the charge of Philip II. Fitzmaurice, travelling overland to Spain,

[056-146]was to unite there with another party of adventurers, and to form a

[056-147]junction with Stukely and Pisano on the coast of Kerry. So with the

[056-148]Papal benediction gladdening his heart, and a most earnest exhortation

[056-149]from the Holy Father to the Catholics of Ireland to follow his banner,

[056-150]this noblest of all the Catholic Geraldines departed from Rome, to try

[056-151]again the hazard of war in his own country.

[056-152]This was in the spring of the year 1579. Sir Henry Sidney, after many

[056-153]years' direction of the government, had been recalled at his own

[056-154]request; Sir William Drury was acting as Lord Justice; and Sir Nicholas

[056-155]Malby as President of Munster. Expectation of the return of

[056-156]Fitzmaurice, at the head of a liberating expedition, began to be rife

[056-157]throughout the south and west, and the coasts were watched with the

[056-158]utmost vigilance. In the month of June, three persons having landed in

[056-159]disguise from a Spanish ship, at Dingle, were seized by government

[056-160]spies, and carried before the Earl of Desmond. On examination, one of

[056-161]them proved to be O'Haly, Bishop of Mayo, and another a friar named

[056-162]O'Rourke; the third is not named. By the timid, temporizing Desmond,

[056-163]they were forwarded to Kilmallock to Drury, who put them to every

[056-164]conceivable torture, in order to extract intelligence of Fitzmaurice's

[056-165]movements. After their thighs had been broken with hammers, they were

[056-166]hanged on a tree, and their bodies used as targets by the brutal

[056-167]soldiery. Fitzmaurice, with his friends, having survived shipwreck on

[056-168]the coast of Galicia, entered the same harbour (Dingle) on the 17th of

[056-169]July. But no tidings had yet reached Munster of Stukely and Pisano; and

[056-170]his cousin, the Earl, sent him neither sign of friendship nor promise

[056-171]of co-operation. He therefore brought his vessels round to the small

[056-172]harbour of Smerwick, and commenced fortifying the almost isolated rock

[056-173]of _Oilen-an-oir_—or golden island, so called from the shipwreck at

[056-174]that point of one of Martin Forbisher's vessels, laden with golden

[056-175]quartz, some years before. Here he was joined by John and James of

[056-176]Desmond, and by a band of 200 of the O'Flaherties of Galway, the only

[056-177]allies who presented themselves. These latter, on finding the expected

[056-178]Munster rising already dead, and the much-talked-of Spanish auxiliary

[056-179]force so mere a handful, soon withdrew in their own galleys, upon which

[056-180]an English ship and pinnace, sweeping round from Kinsale, carried off

[056-181]the Spanish vessels in sight of the powerless little fort. These

[056-182]desperate circumstances inspired desperate councils, and it was decided

[056-183]by the cousins to endeavour to gain the great wood of Kilmore, near

[056-184]Charleville—in the neighbourhood of Sir James' old retreat among the

[056-185]Galtee Mountains. In this march they were closely pursued by the Earl

[056-186]of Desmond, either in earnest or in sham, and were obliged to separate

[056-187]into three small bands, the brothers of the Earl retiring respectively

[056-188]to the fastnesses of Lymnamore and Glenfesk, while Fitzmaurice, with "a

[056-189]dozen horsemen and a few kerne," made a desperate push to reach the

[056-190]western side of the Shannon, where he hoped, perhaps, for better

[056-191]opportunity and a warmer reception. This proved for him a fatal

[056-192]adventure. Jaded after a long day's ride he was compelled to seize some

[056-193]horses from the plough, in the barony of Clanwilliam, in order to

[056-194]remount his men. These horses were the property of his relative, Sir

[056-195]William Burke, who, with his neighbour, Mac-I-Brien of Ara, pursued the

[056-196]fugitives to within six miles of Limerick, where Fitzmaurice, having

[056-197]turned to remonstrate with his pursuers, was fired at and mortally

[056-198]wounded. He did not instantly fall. Dashing into the midst of his

[056-199]assailants he cleft down the two sons of Burke, whose followers

[056-200]immediately turned and fled. Then alighting from his saddle, the

[056-201]wounded chief received the last solemn rites of religion from the hands

[056-202]of Dr. Allen. His body was decapitated by one of his followers, that

[056-203]the noble head might not be subjected to indignity; but the trunk being

[056-204]but hastily buried was soon afterwards discovered, carried to

[056-205]Kilmallock, and there hung up for a target and a show. This tragical

[056-206]occurrence took place near the present site of "Barrington's bridge,"

[056-207]on the little river Mulkern, county of Limerick, on the 18th day of

[056-208]August, 1579. In honour of his part in the transaction William Burke

[056-209]was created Baron of Castleconnell, awarded a pension of 100 marks per

[056-210]annum, and received from Elizabeth an autograph letter of condolence on

[056-211]the loss of his sons: it is added by some writers that he died of joy

[056-212]on the receipt of so many favours. Such was the fate of the glorious

[056-213]hopes of Sir James Fitzmaurice. So ended in a squabble with churls

[056-214]about cattle, on the banks of an insignificant stream, a career which

[056-215]had drawn the attention of Europe, and had inspired with apprehension

[056-216]the lion-hearted Queen.

[056-218]As to the expedition under Stukely, its end was even more romantic. His

[056-219]squadron having put into the Tagus, he found the King of Portugal, Don

[056-220]Sebastian, on the eve of sailing against the Moors, and from some

[056-221]promise of after aid was induced to accompany that chivalrous Prince.

[056-222]On the fatal field of Alcacar, Stukely, Pisano, and the Italians under

[056-223]their command shared the fate of the Portuguese monarch and army.

[056-224]Neither Italy nor Ireland heard of them more.

[056-225]Gregory XIII. did not abandon the cause. On the receipt of all these

[056-226]ill-tidings he issued another Bull, highly laudatory of the virtues of

[056-227]James Fitzmaurice "of happy memory," and granting the same indulgence

[056-228]to those who would fight under John or James of Desmond, "as that which

[056-229]was imparted to those who fought against the Turks for the recovery of

[056-230]the Holy Land." This remarkable document is dated from Rome, the 13th

[056-231]of May, 1580.
