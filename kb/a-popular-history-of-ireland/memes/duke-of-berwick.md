## Overview

James FitzJames, Duke of Berwick, was the illegitimate son of King [[James II]] and Arabella Churchill. During the [[Williamite War]] in Ireland (1689–1691), the young Duke served as the nominal Commander-in-Chief of the Jacobite forces under his father, participating in the Siege of Derry and the campaign leading to the [[Battle of the Boyne]].

## Biography

### Arrival in Ireland

The Duke of Berwick accompanied his father James II to Ireland in March 1689:

> [080-033] Accompanied by his natural sons, the Duke of Berwick and the Grand Prior Fitzjames, by Lieutenant-Generals de Rosen and de Maumont, James landed at Kinsale. (080-033–080-034)

### The Northern Campaign

During the operations in Ulster, Berwick was stationed at key points in the Jacobite line. When the Williamite forces under General Kirke moved to the relief of Derry:

> [081-113] the relief of Derry, they beat up the Duke of Berwick's quarters at Trellick, but were repulsed with some loss. (081-113–081-114)

Berwick's presence at Newry posed a serious obstacle to the Williamite commander Schomberg, who wasted nearly a month advancing from Belfast to Dundalk (081-162–081-163). At Cavan, Brigadier Wolseley repulsed the Duke of Berwick with the loss of 200 men and some valuable officers (082-031–082-032).

### The Road to the Boyne

At the Boyne campaign, Berwick was counted among the most prominent officers of the Jacobite army:

> [082-076] youthful Berwick, the adventurous Lauzan, and the beloved Sarsfield—everywhere received with cordial acclamations. (082-076–082-077)

Though James II departed Ireland after the Boyne, he left the young Duke of Berwick named in his stead as commander (082-185). Berwick continued through nearly all the campaigns of the Williamite War in Ireland (087-045).

### Later Career

After the Treaty of Limerick in 1691, Berwick left Ireland with the other Jacobite forces. He went on to have a distinguished military career in the service of France, rising to the rank of Marshal of France and serving with distinction in the War of the Spanish Succession. He was killed at the Siege of Philippsburg in 1734.

## Legacy

The Duke of Berwick is a transitional figure in Irish military history — a Stuart prince who commanded Irish Jacobite forces against the Williamites, and later became one of the most successful Irish exiles in continental service. His career illustrates the path of the Wild Geese: Irish and Anglo-Irish soldiers who sought military fortune abroad after the Jacobite defeat.
