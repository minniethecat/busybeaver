CHAPTER V.

[092-001]ACCESSION OF GEORGE III.—FLOOD'S LEADERSHIP—OCTENNIAL PARLIAMENTS

[092-002]ESTABLISHED.

[092-003]George III., grandson of the late king, commenced, in October, 1760, at

[092-004]the age of two and twenty, the longest reign in British history.

[092-005]Including the period of the regency, he reigned over his empire nearly

[092-006]sixty years—an extraordinary term of royal power, and quite as

[092-007]extraordinary for its events as for its extreme length.

[092-008]The great movement of the Irish mind, at the beginning of this reign,

[092-009]was the limitation of the duration of Parliament, hitherto elected for

[092-010]the King's life. This reform, long advocated out of doors, and by the

[092-011]more progressive members within the House, was reserved for the new

[092-012]Parliament under the new reign. To this Parliament were returned

[092-013]several men of great promise, men of a new generation, nurtured in the

[092-014]school of Swift and Malone, but going even beyond their masters in

[092-015]their determination to liberate the legislature of their country from

[092-016]the undue influence of the crown and the castle. Among those new

[092-017]members were three destined to national celebrity, Dr. Lucas, Mr.

[092-018]Hussey Burgh, and Mr. Dennis Bowes Daly; and one destined to universal

[092-019]reputation—Henry Flood. This gentleman, the son of a former Chief

[092-020]Justice, intermarried into the powerful oligarchical family of the

[092-021]Beresfords, was only in his 28th year when first elected member for

[092-022]Kilkenny; but, in point of genius and acquirements, he was even then

[092-023]the first man in Ireland, and one of the first in the empire. For a

[092-024]session or two he silently observed the forms of the House, preparing

[092-025]himself for the great contest to come; but when at last he obtained the

[092-026]ear of his party he was heard to some purpose. Though far from

[092-027]advocating extreme measures, he had abundant boldness; he was not open

[092-028]to the objection levelled against the leader of the past generation,

[092-029]Mr. Malone, of whom Grattan said, "he was a colony-bred man, and he

[092-030]feared to bring down England upon Ireland."

[092-031]The Duke of Bedford vacated the viceroyalty in 1761, and Lord Halifax

[092-032]took his place. In the first parliamentary session, Dr. Lucas

[092-033]introduced his resolutions limiting the duration of Parliament to seven

[092-034]years, a project which Flood afterwards adopted and mainly contributed

[092-035]to carry. The heads of the bill embodying these resolutions were

[092-036]transmitted to London by the Lord-Lieutenant, but never returned. In

[092-037]1763, under the government of the Marquis of Hertford, similar

[092-038]resolutions were introduced and carried, but a similar fate awaited

[092-039]them. Again they were passed, and again rejected, the popular

[092-040]dissatisfaction rising higher and higher with every delay of the

[092-041]reform. At length, in the session of 1767, "the Septennial Bill," as it

[092-042]was called, was returned from England, changed to octennial, and with

[092-043]this alteration it passed into law, in February, 1768. A new Parliament

[092-044]the same year was elected under the new act, to which all the friends

[092-045]of the measure were triumphantly returned. The faithful Lucas, however,

[092-046]survived his success little better than two years; he died amid the

[092-047]very sincere regrets of all men who were not enemies of their country.

[092-048]At his funeral the pall was borne by the Marquis of Kildare, Lord

[092-049]Charlemont, Mr. Flood, Mr. Hussey Burgh, Sir Lucius O'Brien, and Mr.

[092-050]Ponsonby.

[092-051]Lord Halifax, and his chief secretary, Mr. Hamilton (known to us as

[092-052]"the single-speech Hamilton," of literary history), received very

[092-053]graciously the loyal addresses presented by the Catholics, soon after

[092-054]his Majesty's accession. In a speech from the throne, the Viceroy

[092-055]proposed, but was obliged to abandon the proposition, to raise six

[092-056]regiments of Catholics, under their own officers, to be taken into the

[092-057]service of Portugal, the ally of Great Britain. His administration was

[092-058]otherwise remarkable neither for its length nor its importance; nor is

[092-059]there anything else of consequence to be mentioned of his lordship,

[092-060]except that his nephew, and chief secretary, had the honour to have

[092-061]Edmund Burke for his private secretary, and the misfortune to offend

[092-062]him.

[092-063]During the government of the Marquis of Hertford, and his successor,

[092-064]Lord Townsend (appointed in 1768), the Patriot party contended on the

[092-065]ground of rendering the judges independent, diminishing the pension

[092-066]list, and modifying the law of Poynings, requiring heads of bills to be

[092-067]sent into England, and certified by both Privy Councils, before they

[092-068]could be passed upon by the legislature. The question of supply, and

[092-069]that of the duration of Parliament, being settled, these reforms were

[092-070]the next objects of exertion. When we know that the late King's

[092-071]mistresses, the Queen Dowager of Prussia, Prince Ferdinand, and other

[092-072]connections of the royal family, equally alien to the country, were

[092-073]pensioners to the amount of thousands of pounds annually on the Irish

[092-074]establishment, we can understand more clearly the bitterness of the

[092-075]battle Mr. Flood and his colleagues were called upon to fight in

[092-076]assailing the old system. But they fought it resolutely and

[092-077]perseveringly. Death had removed their most unscrupulous enemy, Primate

[092-078]Stone, during the Hertford administration, and the improved tone and

[092-079]temper of public opinion would not tolerate any attempt to raise up a

[092-080]successor of similar character. Lord Townsend, an old campaigner and

[092-081]_bon vivant_, was expressly chosen as most capable of restoring the old

[092-082]system of government by closeting and corruption, but he found the

[092-083]Ireland of his day very materially altered from the defenceless

[092-084]province, which Stone and Dorset had attempted to cajole or to coerce,

[092-085]twenty years before.

[092-086]The Parliament of 1769—the first limited Parliament which Ireland had

[092-087]seen since the revolution—proved, in most respects, worthy of the

[092-088]expectations formed of it. John Ponsonby was chosen Speaker, and Flood

[092-089]regarded, around him, well-filled benches and cheering countenances.

[092-090]The usual supply bill was passed and sent up to the castle, but on its

[092-091]return from England was found to be altered—15,000 men, among other

[092-092]changes, being charged to the Irish military establishment, instead of

[092-093]12,000, as formerly. The Commons, resolute to assert their rights,

[092-094]threw out the bill, as had been done in 1753, and the Lord Lieutenant,

[092-095]protesting in the House of Lords against their conduct, ordered them to

[092-096]be prorogued. Prorogation followed prorogation, till February, 1771,

[092-097]the interval being occupied in closeting and coquetting with members of

[092-098]the opposition, in the creation of new places, and the disposal of them

[092-099]to the relatives of those capable of being bought. No one was

[092-100]surprised, when the Houses reassembled, to find that a bare majority of

[092-101]the Commons voted a fulsome address of confidence to the Lord

[092-102]Lieutenant. But this address, Speaker Ponsonby indignantly refused to

[092-103]present. He preferred resignation to disgrace, and great was the

[092-104]amazement and indignation when his friend, Mr. Perry, elected by a bare

[092-105]majority, consented to take the post—no longer a post of honour. In

[092-106]justice to Mr, Perry, however, it must be added, that in the chair as

[092-107]on the floor of Parliament, he still continued the patriot—that if he

[092-108]advanced his own fortunes, it was not at the expense of the

[092-109]country—that some of the best measures passed by this and the

[092-110]subsequent Parliament, owed their final success, if not their first

[092-111]suggestion, to his far-seeing sagacity.

[092-113]The methods taken by Lord Townsend to effect his ends, not less than

[092-114]those ends themselves, aroused the spirit and combined the ranks of the

[092-115]Irish opposition. The press of Dublin teemed with philippics and

[092-116]satires, upon his creatures and himself. The wit, the scholarship, the

[092-117]elegant fancy, the irresistible torrent of eloquence, as well as the

[092-118]popular enthusiasm, were against him, and in 1772, borne down by these

[092-119]combined forces, he confessed his failure by resigning the sword of

[092-120]state into the hands of Lord Harcourt.

[092-121]The new Viceroy, according to custom, began his reign by taking an

[092-122]exactly opposite course to his predecessor, and ended it by falling

[092-123]into nearly the same errors and abuses. He suggested an Absentee-tax,

[092-124]which was introduced by Flood, but rejected through the preponderating

[092-125]influence of the landed aristocracy. In preparing the tables of

[092-126]expenditure, he had caused arrears amounting to 265,000 pounds, and an

[092-127]annual increase of 100,000 pounds, to be added to the estimates.

[092-128]Moreover, his supply bill was discovered, at the second reading, to

[092-129]extend over _two years_ instead of one—a discovery which occasioned the

[092-130]greatest indignation. Flood raised his powerful voice in warning, not

[092-131]unmingled with menace; Burgh declared, that if any member should again

[092-132]bring in such a bill, he would himself move his expulsion from the

[092-133]House; while George Ogle, member for Wexford, proposed that the bill

[092-134]itself should be burned before the porch, by the common hangman. He was

[092-135]reminded that the instrument bore the great seal; to which he boldly

[092-136]answered, that the seal would help to make it burn the better. It was

[092-137]not thought politic to take notice of this revolutionary retort.
