# Lord Townsend

**Lord Townsend** (George Townshend, 1st Marquess Townshend) served as Lord Lieutenant of Ireland from 1767 to 1772. He was appointed to restore the old system. His tenure coincided with the rise of the Patriot party in the [[Irish Parliament]], led by [[Henry Flood]]. His controversial methods failed and galvanized an opposition including [[Henry Grattan]]. (092-064) (092-080)

## Background and appointment

Lord Townsend succeeded the Marquis of Hertford as Lord Lieutenant in 1767, receiving the sword of state in 1768. The political landscape he entered had been transformed by the Octennial Act, which limited the duration of Parliament to eight years and was passed in February 1768 after a long campaign led by Flood and Dr. Charles Lucas. (092-041) (092-043)

Townsend was selected specifically for his reputation as a manager of men. The government in London believed that a Viceroy of his character could reverse the growing independence of the Irish legislature. (092-080)

> Lord Townsend, an old campaigner and _bon vivant_, was expressly chosen as most capable of restoring the old system of government by closeting and corruption, but he found the Ireland of his day very materially altered from the defenceless province, which Stone and Dorset had attempted to cajole or to coerce, twenty years before. (092-080)

The new Lord Lieutenant faced a Parliament emboldened by recent reforms and unwilling to accept the subservient role that had characterized earlier Irish legislatures. The death of Primate Stone, the government's most unscrupulous agent in the previous generation, had left the castle without an equally effective instrument of control. (092-077) (092-079)

## Methods of parliamentary management

Townsend's approach relied on patronage, place-creation, and the strategic use of prorogation to wear down opposition. When the Commons threw out an altered supply bill in 1769, he responded by repeatedly proroguing Parliament from 1769 until February 1771. (092-094) (092-096)

> The interval being occupied in closeting and coquetting with members of the opposition, in the creation of new places, and the disposal of them to the relatives of those capable of being bought. (092-097)

During this eighteen-month recess, Townsend systematically worked to build a government majority through the distribution of offices and favors. When Parliament finally reassembled, a bare majority of the Commons voted a fulsome address of confidence to the Lord Lieutenant. (092-100)

Speaker John Ponsonby refused to present the address, choosing resignation over complicity. His successor, Mr. Perry, was elected by only a narrow margin, a development that caused widespread indignation. (092-103) (092-104)

The manipulation of the supply bill was the immediate trigger for the conflict. The bill returned from England altered, increasing the military establishment from 12,000 to 15,000 men, a change the Commons regarded as a violation of their sole right over finance. The resulting standoff echoed the money bill crisis of 1753. (092-091) (092-093)

## Key policies and conflicts

The Patriot party, led by Flood and supported by figures including [[Hussey Burgh]], pressed for three major reforms during Townsend's administration: rendering the judges independent of the crown, diminishing the pension list, and modifying Poynings' Law. The last of these required Irish bills to be certified by both Privy Councils before they could be enacted. (092-064) (092-066)

The pension list was a particular source of grievance. The late King's mistresses, the Queen Dowager of Prussia, Prince Ferdinand, and other royal connections entirely foreign to Ireland drew thousands of pounds annually from the Irish establishment. Flood and his colleagues attacked this system with growing determination. (092-070) (092-074)

Townsend's later involvement in Irish affairs came in 1775, three years after his recall, when he delivered a message calling for the dispatch of 4,000 men from the Irish establishment to serve in the American war.

He offered to replace them with foreign Protestant German troops, but the Commons rejected this proposition by a majority of thirty-eight. The contingent was ultimately dispatched after Flood described them as "4,000 armed negotiators." (094-012) (094-013) (094-016) (094-017)

## Recall and legacy

Townsend's methods provoked a fierce backlash. The Dublin press produced a stream of satires and philippics targeting both the Lord Lieutenant and his supporters. The intellectual and rhetorical forces of the nation were arrayed against him, and by 1772 he was compelled to acknowledge defeat. (092-113) (092-115)

> The wit, the scholarship, the elegant fancy, the irresistible torrent of eloquence, as well as the popular enthusiasm, were against him, and in 1772, borne down by these combined forces, he confessed his failure by resigning the sword of state into the hands of Lord Harcourt. (092-117)

His successor, Lord Harcourt, initially pursued an opposite course, even proposing an Absentee-tax through Flood, but ultimately fell into similar patterns of abuse. The opposition that Townsend had helped to unify and strengthen would continue to grow, eventually producing the parliamentary revolution of 1782 under [[Henry Grattan]]. (092-121) (092-123)

Townsend's legacy is that of a Viceroy whose heavy-handed methods, intended to restore English control over the [[Irish Parliament]], instead accelerated the development of an independent patriotic opposition.

His failure demonstrated that the old system of management, which had worked for earlier lords lieutenant, could no longer succeed against a Parliament and public opinion transformed by the Octennial Act and the leadership of Flood. (092-082) (092-113)
