CHAPTER VI.

[112-001]THE IRISH ABROAD, DURING THE REIGN OF GEORGE III.

[112-002]The fond tenacity with which the large numbers of the Irish people who

[112-003]have established themselves in foreign states have always clung to

[112-004]their native country; the active sympathy they have personally shown

[112-005]for their relatives at home; the repeated efforts they have made to

[112-006]assist the Irish in Ireland, in all their public undertakings, requires

[112-007]that, as an element in O'Connell's final and successful struggle for

[112-008]Catholic Emancipation, we should take a summary view of the position of

[112-009]"the Irish abroad."

[112-010]While the emigrants of that country to America naturally pursued the

[112-011]paths of peace, those who, from choice or necessity, found their way to

[112-012]the European Continent, were, with few exceptions, employed mainly in

[112-013]two departments—war and diplomacy. An Irish Abbé, liked the celebrated

[112-014]preacher, McCarthy—or an Irish merchant firm, such as the house of the

[112-015]same name at Bordeaux, might be met with, but most of those who

[112-016]attained any distinction did so by the sword or the pen, in the field

[112-017]or the cabinet.

[112-018]In France, under the revolutionary governments from '91 to '99, the

[112-019]Irish were, with their old-world notions of God and the Devil, wholly

[112-020]out of place; but under the Consulate and the Empire, they rose to many

[112-021]employments of the second class, and a few of the very first. From the

[112-022]ranks of the expatriated of '98, Buonaparte promoted Arthur O'Conor and

[112-023]William Corbet to the rank of General; Ware, Allen, Byrne, the younger

[112-024]Tone, and Keating, to that of Colonel. As individuals, the Emperor was

[112-025]certainly a benefactor to many Irishmen; but, as a nation, it was one

[112-026]of their most foolish delusions, to expect in him a deliverer. On the

[112-027]restoration of the Bourbons, the Irish officers who had acquired

[112-028]distinction under Napoleon adhered generally to his fortunes, and

[112-029]tendered their resignations; in their place, a new group of

[112-030]Franco-Irish descendants of the old Brigades-men, began to show

[112-031]themselves in the _salons_ of Paris, and the Bureaus of the Ministers.

[112-032]The last swords drawn for "the legitimate branch" in '91, was by Count

[112-033]Dillon and his friend Count Wall; their last defender, in 1830, was

[112-034]General Wall, of the same family.

[112-035]Though the Irish in France, especially those resident at Paris,

[112-036]exercised the greatest influence in favour of their original country—an

[112-037]influence which met all travelled Englishmen, wherever the French

[112-038]language was understood—their compatriots in Spain and Austria had also

[112-039]contributed their share to range Continental opinion on the side of

[112-040]Ireland. Three times, during the century, Spain was represented at

[112-041]London by men of Irish birth, or Irish origin. The British merchant who

[112-042]found Alexander O'Reilly Governor of Cadiz, or the diplomatist who met

[112-043]him as Spanish ambassador, at the Court of Louis XVI., could hardly

[112-044]look with uninstructed eyes, upon the lot of his humblest namesake in

[112-045]Cavan. This family, indeed, produced a succession of eminent men, both

[112-046]in Spain and Austria. "It is strange," observed Napoleon to those

[112-047]around him, on his second entry into Vienna, in 1809, "that on each

[112-048]occasion—in November, 1805, as this day—on arriving in the Austrian

[112-049]capital, I find myself in treaty and in intercourse with the

[112-050]respectable Count O'Reilly." Napoleon had other reasons for remembering

[112-051]this officer; it was his dragoon regiment which saved the remnant of

[112-052]the Austrians, at Austerlitz. In the Austrian army list at that period,

[112-053]when she was the ally of England, there were above forty Irish names,

[112-054]from the grading of Colonel up to that of Field-Marshal. In almost

[112-055]every field of the Peninsula, Wellington and Anglesea learned the value

[112-056]of George the Second's imprecation on the Penal Code, which deprived

[112-057]him of such soldiers as conquered at Fontenoy. It cannot be doubted

[112-058]that even the constant repetition of the names of the Blakes,

[112-059]O'Donnells, and Sarsfields, in the bulletins sent home to England,

[112-060]tended to enforce reflections of that description on the statesmen and

[112-061]the nation, and to inspirit and sustain the struggling Catholics. A

[112-062]powerful argument for throwing open the British army and navy to men of

[112-063]all religions, was drawn from these foreign experiences; and, if such

[112-064]men were worthy to hold military commissions, why not also to sit in

[112-065]Parliament, and on the Bench?

[112-066]The fortunes of the Irish in America, though less brilliant for the

[112-067]few, were more advantageous as to the many. They were, during the war

[112-068]of the revolution, and the war of 1812, a very considerable element in

[112-069]the American republic. It was a violent exaggeration to say, as Lord

[112-070]Mountjoy did in moving for the repeal of the Penal laws, "that England

[112-071]lost America by Ireland;" but it is very certain that Washington placed

[112-072]great weight on the active aid of the gallant Pennsylvania, Maryland,

[112-073]and Southern Irish troops, and the sturdy Scotch-Irish of New

[112-074]Hampshire. Franklin, in his visit to Ireland, before the rupture, and

[112-075]Jefferson in his correspondence, always enumerates the Irish, as one

[112-076]element of reliance, in the contest between the Colonies and the

[112-077]Empire.

[112-078]In the immediate cause of the war of 1812, this people were peculiarly

[112-079]interested. If the doctrines of "the right of search" and "once a

[112-080]subject always a subject," were to prevail, no Irish emigrant could

[112-081]hope to become—or having become, could hope to enjoy the protection

[112-082]of—an American citizen. It was, therefore, natural that men of that

[112-083]origin should take a deep interest in the war, and it seems something

[112-084]more than a fortuitous circumstance, when we find in the chairman of

[112-085]the Senatorial Committee of 1812, which authorized the President to

[112-086]raise the necessary levies—an Irish emigrant, John Smilie, and in the

[112-087]Secretary-at-war, who acted under the powers thus granted, the son of

[112-088]an Irish emigrant, John Caldwell Calhoun. On the Canadian frontier,

[112-089]during the war which followed, we find in posts of importance, Brady,

[112-090]Mullany, McComb, Croghan and Reilly; on the lakes, Commodore McDonough,

[112-091]and on the ocean, Commodores Shaw and Stewart—all Irish. On the

[112-092]Mississippi, another son of Irish emigrant parents, with his favourite

[112-093]lieutenants, Carroll, Coffee, and Butler, brought the war to a close by

[112-094]their brilliant defence of New Orleans. The moral of that victory was

[112-095]not lost upon England; the life of Andrew Jackson, with a dedication

[112-096]"to the People of Ireland" was published at London and Dublin, by the

[112-097]most generally popular writer of that day—William Cobbett.

[112-098]In the cause of South American independence, the Irish under O'Higgins

[112-099]and McKenna in Chili, and under Bolivar and San Martin in Colombia and

[112-100]Peru, were largely engaged, and honourably distinguished. Colonel

[112-101]O'Conor, nephew to Arthur, was San Martin's chief of the staff; General

[112-102]Devereux, with his Irish legion, rendered distinguished services to

[112-103]Bolivar and Don Bernardo. O'Higgins was hailed as the Liberator of

[112-104]Chili. During that long ten years' struggle, which ended with the

[112-105]evacuation of Carraccas in 1823, Irish names are conspicuous on almost

[112-106]every field of action. Bolivar's generous heart was warmly attached to

[112-107]persons of that nation. "The doctor who constantly attends him," says

[112-108]the English General, Miller, "is Dr. Moore, an Irishman, who had

[112-109]followed the Liberator from Venezuela to Peru. He is a man of great

[112-110]skill in his profession, and devotedly attached to the person of the

[112-111]Liberator. Bolivar's first aide-de-camp, Colonel O'Leary, is a nephew

[112-112]of the celebrated Father O'Leary. In 1818, he embarked, at the age of

[112-113]seventeen, in the cause of South American independence, in which he has

[112-114]served with high distinction, having been present at almost every

[112-115]general action fought in Colombia, and has received several wounds. He

[112-116]has been often employed on diplomatic missions, and in charges of great

[112-117]responsibility, in which he has always acquitted himself with great

[112-118]ability."

[112-119]That these achievements of the Irish abroad produced a favourable

[112-120]influence on the situation of the Irish at home, we know from many

[112-121]collateral sources; we know it also from the fact, that when O'Connell

[112-122]succeeded in founding a really national organization, subscriptions and

[112-123]words of encouragement poured in on him, not only from France, Spain,

[112-124]and Austria, but from North and South America, not only from the Irish

[112-125]residents in those countries, but from their native

[112-126]inhabitants—soldiers and statesmen—of the first consideration. The

[112-127]services and virtues of her distinguished children in foreign climes,

[112-128]stood to the mother country instead of treaties and alliances.
