## Overview

Richard II (1367–1400) was King of England and Lord of Ireland from 1377 until his deposition in 1399. He was the first English monarch since King John to visit Ireland in person, leading two royal expeditions aimed at reasserting the authority of the Crown over the Gaelic and Anglo-Irish lords.

His interventions, though temporarily successful, failed to secure lasting results, and his deposition by Henry Bolingbroke had profound and extending consequences for English rule in Ireland. (042-448)

## First Expedition to Ireland (1394–95)

By the late fourteenth century, the English lordship in Ireland had contracted to a narrow strip of territory around Dublin — the Pale — while Gaelic chieftains such as Art McMurrogh, Lord of Leinster, held sway over vast territories. Richard II determined to reverse this decline through a massive show of force.

> Richard II. of England transported two royal armies across the channel, in 1394 and 1399. (041-075 to 041-076)

When, in the year 1389, Richard II had attained his majority and thrown off the tutelage of his uncles, he turned his attention increasingly to Irish affairs. (041-101) The expedition of 1394–95 saw the submission of numerous Gaelic chieftains, including the powerful O'Neil of Ulster, who performed homage to Richard in person.

The King received these submissions with ceremony, bestowing knighthoods upon several Irish lords and attempting to integrate them into the English political order.

## Art McMurrogh and the Second Expedition

Richard returned to Ireland in 1399, this time determined to crush the resurgent power of Art McMurrogh, who had recovered much of his Leinster territory. The campaign, however, was interrupted by the news that Henry Bolingbroke, Duke of Lancaster, had invaded England to reclaim his inheritance and, soon, the Crown itself.

Richard hastened back to England, where he was captured, deposed, and subsequently murdered at Pontefract Castle.

> In considering all these far extending consequences of the deposition of Richard II., and the substitution of Henry of Lancaster in his stead, we must give due weight to his unsuccessful Irish wars as proximate causes of that revolution. (042-448 to 042-450)

The Anglo-Irish lords, whose loyalty had been personal to Richard, found themselves under a usurper with no connection to or interest in the island. The Lancastrian succession placed a diminished priority on Irish governance, and the Pale continued to shrink throughout the fifteenth century.

## Religious Policy

During the reign of Richard II, English statutes were applied in Ireland in order to diminish the power of the Supreme Pontiffs over appointments to Irish benefices — an early manifestation of the tension between royal and papal authority that would define much of later Irish history.

(047-010) This policy was continued from the precedents set in the reigns of Edward III and Richard II, when the Crown sought to tighten its control over the Irish Church. (047-155)

## Legacy

Richard II's Irish expeditions, though bold in conception, left no durable imprint on the governance of the island.

His most significant legacy in Ireland was indirect: the distraction of the Wars of the Roses that followed his deposition allowed the Gaelic chieftains to recover additional territory, and the Pale contracted to its smallest extent ever by the end of the fifteenth century.

The memory of Richard's personal kingship lingered among the Anglo-Irish, who would later contrast the attentions of a resident English monarch with the neglect of absentee successors.
