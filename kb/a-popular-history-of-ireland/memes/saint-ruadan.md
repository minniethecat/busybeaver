## Overview

Saint Ruadan (c. 500–584 AD) was an early Irish saint and abbot whose dramatic confrontation with the High King Dermid (Diarmait mac Cerbaill) resulted in the cursing and abandonment of Tara, the ancient seat of the Irish monarchy. His sanctuary of Temple-Ruadan in Tipperary remains a place of historical memory. (004-171) (004-172)

The curse pronounced upon Tara in the year 554 AD had far-reaching consequences traceable for a thousand years through Irish history. It marked the moment when ecclesiastical power definitively asserted its authority over the temporal monarchy in early Christian Ireland. (004-175)

## Early Life and Monastic Foundation

Ruadan was born into the early Irish church of the sixth century, a period of remarkable monastic expansion and missionary activity. He founded the monastery of Lorrha in what is now County Tipperary, establishing a centre of learning and spiritual authority in the heart of Munster. (004-171)

The site of his sanctuary became known as Temple-Ruadan, a name that preserved his memory in the landscape of Tipperary. The early Irish church was organized around such monastic centres rather than diocesan structures, giving abbots like Ruadan immense spiritual and political influence. (004-172)

## The Violation of Sanctuary

The crisis that led to the cursing of Tara began when King Dermid violated the ancient right of sanctuary claimed by the clergy. A prisoner had fled to Ruadan's church seeking the protection of the altar. The King, in defiance of ecclesiastical immunity, dragged the prisoner from the very horns of the altar and put him to death. (004-167) (004-168)

> The King had violated the sanctuary, dragging a prisoner from the very horns of the altar, and putting him to death — the crowning act of a series of aggressions on the immunities claimed by the clergy. [004-168] [004-170]

This offence was the culmination of a series of aggressions by the semi-pagan monarch against the immunities of the Church. Dermid, though nominally Christian, had persistently encroached upon clerical privileges, and the violation of sanctuary could not go unanswered. (004-169)

## The Curse of Tara

In response to the King's sacrilege, Saint Ruadan proceeded to Tara, the royal seat of the High Kings of Ireland, accompanied by his clergy. There he performed a solemn ritual: walking round the royal rath, he excommunicated the monarch and anathematized the place. (004-171) (004-173)

> The Saint, whose name was Ruadan, and the site of whose sanctuary is still known as Temple-Ruadan in Tipperary, proceeded to Tara, accompanied by his clergy, and, walking round the royal rath, solemnly excommunicated the monarch, and anathematized the place. [004-171] [004-174]

The date of the malediction that left Tara desolate is recorded as the year of Our Lord 554. The end of this self-willed semi-Pagan Dermid was in unison with his reign. No king after Dermid resided permanently upon the hill of Tara. (004-183) (004-176)

## The Consequences of the Curse

The far-reaching consequences of this awful exercise of spiritual power are traceable for a thousand years through Irish history. The abandonment of Tara as a royal residence severed the link between the Irish monarchy and its most ancient and symbolically potent site. (004-175)

> The far-reaching consequences of this awful exercise of spiritual power are traceable for a thousand years through Irish history. No king after Dermid resided permanently upon the hill of Tara. [004-175] [004-176]

Other royal houses continued to exist in Meath — at Tailteen, at the hill of Usna, and on the margin of the beautiful Lough Ennell, near the present Castlepollard. But the monarchs of the northern race made their habitual home in their own patrimony near Armagh, or on the celebrated hill of Aileach. (004-178) (004-181)

## Saint Ruadan in the Ecclesiastical Tradition

Saint Ruadan's curse upon Tara was remembered as one of the defining demonstrations of clerical power in early Irish history. The chroniclers placed it alongside Saint Patrick's expurgation of the old pagan constitution and Saint Columbkill's establishment of the Iona community as the three great ecclesiastical acts that shaped medieval Ireland. (006-182)

> St. Patrick had expurgated the old constitution; St. Ruadan's curse drove the kings from Tara; St. Columbkill had established the Irish church abroad. [006-182]

The power of the clergy had been omnipotent in politics for above a century, and Ruadan's act represented the high-water mark of ecclesiastical authority over the temporal power. The kings might rule, but the saints could curse — and a curse from a man like Ruadan could empty a royal seat for a thousand years. (006-181) (006-182)

## Legacy

Saint Ruadan's historical significance lies in his dramatic assertion of ecclesiastical authority over the Irish monarchy. His curse upon Tara in 554 AD established a precedent that shaped the relationship between church and state in Ireland for centuries. (004-175)

The memory of his sanctuary at Temple-Ruadan in Tipperary preserved his name in local tradition. He is venerated as one of the early saints whose spiritual power could humble kings and alter the geography of royal power in ancient Ireland. (004-172)
