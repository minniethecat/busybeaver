Hugh O'Byrne was the senior chief of the [[O'Byrne]] clan of [[Wicklow]] and the father of the celebrated [[Feagh Mac Hugh O'Byrne]]. As head of the powerful O'Byrne sept, Hugh held sway over the mountainous territory of Glenmalure — a region that had long served as a bastion of Gaelic resistance against English authority.

> "With the exception of Feagh, son of Hugh, the senior of the O'Byrnes, and one of the noblest spirits of his race and age." (057-194)

## The O'Byrne Lordship

The O'Byrnes traced their descent from the ancient kings of [[Leinster]] and had been established in the Wicklow mountains since the twelfth century. Their strategic position in Glenmalure — a deep, wooded valley accessible only through narrow passes — made them among the most resilient opponents of Crown authority.

Maelmurra of Leinster, who led the coalition against [[Brian Boru]] at the [[Battle of Clontarf]] in 1014, was himself the head of the O'Byrne family, demonstrating the clan's ancient prominence.

> "Maelmurra of Leinster had mustered all the forces he could command for such an expedition. He was himself the head of the powerful family of O'Byrne, and was followed in his alliances by others of the descendants of Cahir More." (014-152)

## Father of a Legend

Hugh O'Byrne's most important legacy was his son, Feagh Mac Hugh O'Byrne. Feagh — described as "one of the noblest spirits of his race and age" — became the terror of the English Pale. He defeated Lord Grey de Wilton at the [[Battle of Glenmalure]] in 1580 and was a central figure in the [[Nine Years War]].

Hugh himself attended the Parliament of 1585 convened by Sir John Perrott, which saw native chiefs sit alongside Anglo-Irish peers and burgesses. The Leinster chiefs recently in arms with the [[Earl of Desmond]] generally absented themselves, but Hugh's son Feagh appeared under the protection of powerful sureties.

## Commander in the Confederate War

A later Hugh O'Byrne — likely a grandson or namesake descendant — appears as a military commander of the [[Catholic Confederation]] during the War of the 1640s. At the battle of Kilrush on 13 April 1642, he was among the Confederate commanders who confronted the Earl of Ormond in [[Kildare]].

> "Lord Ormond, returning from a second reinforcement of Naas and other Kildare forts, at the head, by English account, of 4,000 men, found on the 13th of April the Catholics of the midland counties, under Lords Mountgarrett, Ikerrin, and Dunboyne, Sir Morgan Cavenagh, Rory O'Moore, and Hugh O'Byrne, drawn up, by his report, 8,000 strong, to dispute his passage." (068-134)

The battle was short but murderous. The Confederates left seven hundred men — including Sir Morgan Cavenagh — dead on the field. Ormond continued his march to relieve Naas and other garrisons with minimal losses.

## Legacy

The O'Byrne name exemplifies the endurance of Gaelic lineage through centuries of conquest. Hugh O'Byrne stands at the junction of two eras: the father of the last great Gaelic champion of Elizabethan Leinster, and the progenitor of a line that continued to resist through arms and through the stubborn survival of the old order in Wicklow.
