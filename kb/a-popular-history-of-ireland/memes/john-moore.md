John Moore of Moore Hall, County [[Mayo]], was an Irish rebel who held the distinction of being appointed President of the Provisional Government of [[Connacht]] during the [[French expedition to Ireland]] of 1798. His brief moment of authority, though lasting only days, represented one of the most dramatic episodes of the [[1798 Rebellion]].

## The French Landing

In August 1798, three months after the main rebellion had been suppressed in [[Wexford]] and [[Ulster]], General Humbert landed at Killala Bay with a small French force. On the 4th of August, Humbert entered Ballina without resistance, and by the 5th had advanced toward [[Castlebar]], the county town of Mayo (104-172). At Castlebar, Humbert's force — against all expectations — routed a much larger British force under General Lake in what became known as the "Races of Castlebar" (104-190).

## President of Connacht

Immediately after this stunning victory, a Provisional Government was established at Castlebar:

> "Immediately after the battle a Provisional Government was established at Castlebar, with Mr. Moore of Moore Hall, as President; proclamations addressed to the inhabitants at large, commissions to raise men, and assignats payable by the future Irish Republic, were issued in its name." (104-197)

This Provisional Government represented the first and only attempt during the 1798 Rebellion to establish a formal revolutionary civil administration on Irish soil. Moore, a local landowner whose family seat at Moore Hall gave him standing in Mayo society, presided over this short-lived republican experiment.

## The Collapse

The republic of Connacht was short-lived. The entire British force in Ireland was now in movement toward Mayo. [[Lord Cornwallis]], the Viceroy, left Dublin in person and advanced rapidly through the midland counties to Kilbeggan (104-207). Humbert, finding himself surrounded by forces outnumbering him ten to one, marched his men 110 miles in three and a half days, attempting to reach the mountains of [[Leitrim]] and open communications with Ulster (104-218).

At Ballinamuck, County [[Longford]], on the 8th of September, Humbert was finally surrounded and forced to surrender. The French were treated as prisoners of war, but for the Irish rebels no terms were thought of:

> "Mr. Moore, President of the Provisional Government, was sentenced to banishment by the clemency of Lord Cornwallis, but died on shipboard." (104-235)

Moore's death at sea, while being transported into exile, marked the end of Connacht's brief experiment in revolutionary self-government. His fate — spared the gallows but lost to the sea — epitomized the tragedy of the 1798 leaders who survived the battlefield only to perish in its aftermath.
