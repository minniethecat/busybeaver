CHAPTER III.

[041-001]ART McMURROGH, LORD OF LEINSTER—FIRST EXPEDITION OF RICHARD II., OF

[041-002]ENGLAND, TO IRELAND.

[041-003]Whether Donald Kavanagh McMurrogh, son of Dermid, was born out of

[041-004]wedlock, as the Lady Eva was made to depose, in order to create a claim

[041-005]of inheritance for herself as sole heiress, this, at least, is certain,

[041-006]that his descendants continued to be looked upon by the kindred clans

[041-007]of Leinster as the natural lords of that principality. Towards the

[041-008]close of the thirteenth century, in the third or fourth generation,

[041-009]after the death of their immediate ancestor, the Kavanaghs of Leighlin

[041-010]and Ballyloughlin begin to act prominently in the affairs of their

[041-011]Province, and their chief is styled both by Irish and English "the

[041-012]McMurrogh." In the era of King Edward Bruce, they were sufficiently

[041-013]formidable to call for an expedition of the Lord Justice into their

[041-014]patrimony, by which they are said to have been defeated. In the next

[041-015]age, in 1335, Maurice, "the McMurrogh," was granted by the Anglo-Irish

[041-016]Parliament or Council, the sum of 80 marks annually, for keeping open

[041-017]certain roads and preserving the peace within its jurisdiction. In

[041-018]1358, Art, the successor of Maurice, and Donald Revagh, were proclaimed

[041-019]"rebels" in a Parliament held at Castledermot, by the Lord Deputy

[041-020]Sancto Amando, the said Art being further branded with deep ingratitude

[041-021]to Edward III., who had acknowledged him as "the Mac-Murch." To carry

[041-022]on a war against him the whole English interest was assessed with a

[041-023]special tax. Louth contributed 20 pounds; Meath and Waterford, 2

[041-024]shillings on every carucate (140 acres) of tilled land; Kilkenny the

[041-025]same sum, with the addition of 6 pence in the pound on chattels. This

[041-026]Art captured the strong castles of Kilbelle, Galbarstown, Rathville,

[041-027]and although his career was not one of invariable success, he

[041-028]bequeathed to his son, also called Art, in 1375, an inheritance,

[041-029]extending over a large portion—perhaps one-half—of the territory ruled

[041-030]by his ancestors before the invasion.

[041-031]Art McMurrogh, or Art Kavanagh, as he is more commonly called, was born

[041-032]in the year 1357, and from the age of sixteen and upwards was

[041-033]distinguished by his hospitality, knowledge, and feats of arms. Like

[041-034]the great Brian, he was a younger son, but the fortune of war removed

[041-035]one by one those who would otherwise have preceded him in the captaincy

[041-036]of his clan and connections. About the year 1375—while he was still

[041-037]under age—he was elected successor to his father, according to the

[041-038]Annalists, who record his death in 1417, "after being forty-two years

[041-039]in the government of Leinster." Fortunately he attained command at a

[041-040]period favourable to his genius and enterprise. His own and the

[041-041]adjoining tribes were aroused by tidings of success from other

[041-042]Provinces, and the partial victories of their immediate predecessors,

[041-043]to entertain bolder schemes, and they only waited for a chief of

[041-044]distinguished ability to concentrate their efforts. This chief they

[041-045]found, where they naturally looked for him, among the old ruling family

[041-046]of the Province. Nor were the English settlers ignorant of his promise.

[041-047]In the Parliament held at Castledermot in 1377, they granted to him the

[041-048]customary annual tribute paid to his house, the nature of which calls

[041-049]for a word of explanation. This tribute was granted, "as the late King

[041-050]had done to his ancestors;" it was again voted in a Parliament held in

[041-051]1380, and continued to be paid so late as the opening of the

[041-052]seventeenth century (A.D. 1603). Not only was a fixed sum paid out of

[041-053]the Exchequer for this purpose—inducing the native chiefs to grant a

[041-054]right of way through their territories—but a direct tax was levied on

[041-055]the inhabitants of English origin for the same privilege. This tax,

[041-056]called "black mail," or "black rent," was sometimes differently

[041-057]regarded by those who paid and those who received it. The former looked

[041-058]on it as a stipend, the latter as a tribute; but that it implied a

[041-059]formal acknowledgment of the local jurisdiction of the chief cannot be

[041-060]doubted. Two centuries after the time of which we speak, Baron Finglas,

[041-061]in his suggestions to King Henry VIII. for extending his power in

[041-062]Ireland, recommends that "no black rent be paid to any Irishman _for

[041-063]the four shires_"—of the Pale—"and any black rent they had afore this

[041-064]time be paid to them for ever." At that late period "the McMurrogh" had

[041-065]still his 80 marks annually from the Exchequer, and 40 pounds from the

[041-066]English settled in Wexford; O'Carroll of Ely had 40 pounds from the

[041-067]English in Kilkenny, and O'Conor of Offally 20 pounds from those of

[041-068]Kildare, and 300 pounds from Meath. It was to meet these and other

[041-069]annuities to more distant chiefs, that William of Windsor, in 1369,

[041-070]covenanted for a larger revenue than the whole of the Anglo-Irish

[041-071]districts then yielded, and which led him besides to stipulate that he

[041-072]was to undertake no new expeditions, but to act entirely on the

[041-073]defensive. We find a little later, that the necessity of sustaining the

[041-074]Dublin authorities at an annual loss was one of the main motives which

[041-075]induced Richard II. of England to transport two royal armies across the

[041-076]channel, in 1394 and 1399.

[041-077]Art McMurrogh, the younger, not only extended the bounds of his own

[041-078]inheritance and imposed tribute on the English settlers in adjoining

[041-079]districts, during the first years of his rule, but having married a

[041-080]noble lady of the "Pale," Elizabeth, heiress to the barony of Norragh,

[041-081]in Kildare, which included Naas and its neighbourhood, he claimed her

[041-082]inheritance in full, though forfeited under "the statute of Kilkenny,"

[041-083]according to English notions. So necessary did it seem to the Deputy

[041-084]and Council of the day to conciliate their formidable neighbour, that

[041-085]they addressed a special representation to King Richard, setting forth

[041-086]the facts of the case, and adding that McMurrogh threatened, until this

[041-087]lady's estates were restored and the arrears of tribute due to him

[041-088]fully discharged, he should never cease from war, "but would join with

[041-089]the Earl of Desmond against the Earl of Ormond, and afterwards return

[041-090]with a great force out of Munster to ravage the country." This allusion

[041-091]most probably refers to James, second Earl of Ormond, who, from being

[041-092]the maternal grandson of Edward I., was called the noble Earl, and was

[041-093]considered in his day the peculiar representative of the English

[041-094]interest. In the last years of Edward III., and the first of his

[041-095]successor, he was constable of the Castle of Dublin, with a fee of 18

[041-096]pounds 5 shillings per annum. In 1381—the probable date of the address

[041-097]just quoted—he had a commission to treat with certain rebels, in order

[041-098]to reform them and promote peace. Three years later he died, and was

[041-099]buried in the Cathedral of St. Canice, Kilkenny, the place of sepulture

[041-100]of his family.

[041-101]When, in the year 1389, Richard II., having attained his majority,

[041-102]demanded to reign alone, the condition of the English interest was most

[041-103]critical. During the twelve years of his minority the Anglo-Irish

[041-104]policy of the Council of Regency had shifted and changed, according to

[041-105]the predominance of particular influences. The Lord Lieutenancy was

[041-106]conferred on the King's relatives, Edward Mortimer, Earl of March

[041-107](1379), and continued to his son, Roger Mortimer, a minor (1381); in

[041-108]1383, it was transferred to Philip de Courtenay, the King's cousin. The

[041-109]following year, de Courtenay having been arrested and fined for

[041-110]mal-administration, Robert de Vere, Earl of Oxford, the special

[041-111]favourite of Richard, was created Marquis of Dublin and Duke of

[041-112]Ireland, with a grant of all the powers and authority exercised at any

[041-113]period in Ireland by that King or his predecessors. This extraordinary

[041-114]grant was solemnly confirmed by the English Parliament, who, perhaps

[041-115]willing to get rid of the favourite at any cost, allotted the sum of

[041-116]30,000 marks due from the King of France, with a guard of 500

[041-117]men-at-arms and 1,000 archers for de Vere's expedition. But that

[041-118]favoured nobleman never entered into possession of the principality

[041-119]assigned him; he experienced the fate of the Gavestons and de Spencers

[041-120]of a former reign; fleeing, for his life, from the Barons, he died in

[041-121]exile in the Netherlands. The only real rulers of the Anglo-Irish in

[041-122]the years of the King's minority, or previous to his first expedition

[041-123]in 1394, (if we except Sir John Stanley's short terms of office in 1385

[041-124]and 1389,) were the Earls of Ormond, second and third, Colton, Dean of

[041-125]Saint Patrick's, Petit, Bishop of Meath, and White, Prior of

[041-126]Kilmainham. For thirty years after the death of Edward III., no

[041-127]Geraldine was entrusted with the highest office, and no Anglo-Irish

[041-128]layman of any other family but the Butlers. In 1393, Thomas of

[041-129]Woodstock, Duke of Gloucester, uncle to Richard, was appointed Lord

[041-130]Lieutenant, and was on the point of embarking, when a royal order

[041-131]reached him announcing the determination of the King to take command of

[041-132]the forces in person.

[041-134]The immediate motives for Richard's expedition are variously stated by

[041-135]different authors. That usually assigned by the English—a desire to

[041-136]divert his mind from brooding over the loss of his wife, "the good

[041-137]Queen Anne," seems wholly insufficient. He had announced his intention

[041-138]a year before her death; he had called together, before the Queen fell

[041-139]ill, the Parliament at Westminster, which readily voted him "a tenth"

[041-140]of the revenues of all their estates for the expedition. Anne's

[041-141]sickness was sudden, and her death took place in the last week of July.

[041-142]Richard's preparations at that date were far advanced towards

[041-143]completion, and Sir Thomas Scroope had been already some months in

[041-144]Dublin to prepare for his reception. The reason assigned by Anglo-Irish

[041-145]writers is more plausible: he had been a candidate for the Imperial

[041-146]Crown of Germany, and was tauntingly told by his competitors to conquer

[041-147]Ireland before he entered the lists for the highest political honour of

[041-148]that age. This rebuke, and the ill-success of his arms against France

[041-149]and Scotland, probably made him desirous to achieve in a new field some

[041-150]share of that military glory which was always so highly prized by his

[041-151]family:

[041-152]Some events which immediately preceded Richard's expedition may help us

[041-153]to understand the relative positions of the natives and the naturalized

[041-154]to the English interest in the districts through which he was to march.

[041-155]By this time the banner of Art McMurrogh floated over all the castles

[041-156]and raths, on the slope of the Ridge of Leinster, or the steps of the

[041-157]Blackstair hills; while the forests along the Barrow and the Upper

[041-158]Slaney, as well as in the plain of Carlow and in the South-western

[041-159]angle of Wicklow (now the barony of Shillelagh), served still better

[041-160]his purposes of defensive warfare; So entirely was the range of country

[041-161]thus vaguely defined under native sway that John Griffin, the English

[041-162]Bishop of Leighlin, and Chancellor of the Exchequer, obtained a grant

[041-163]in 1389 of the town of Gulroestown, in the county of Dublin, "near the

[041-164]marches of O'Toole, seeing he could not live within his own see for the

[041-165]rebels." In 1390, Peter Creagh, Bishop of Limerick, on his way to

[041-166]attend an Anglo-Irish Parliament, was taken prisoner in that region,

[041-167]and in consequence the usual fine was remitted in his favour. In 1392,

[041-168]James, the third Earl of Ormond, gave McMurrogh a severe check at

[041-169]Tiscoffin, near Shankill, where 600 of his clansmen were left dead

[041-170]among the hills.

[041-171]This defeat, however, was thrown into the shade by the capture of New

[041-172]Ross, on the very eve of Richard's arrival at Waterford. In a previous

[041-173]chapter we have described the fortifications erected round this

[041-174]important seaport towards the end of the thirteenth century. Since that

[041-175]period its progress had been steadily onward. In the reign of Edward

[041-176]III. the controversy which had long subsisted between the merchants of

[041-177]Ross and those of Waterford, concerning the trade monopolies claimed by

[041-178]the latter, had been decided in favour of Ross. At this period it could

[041-179]muster in its own defence 363 cross-bowmen, 1,200 long-bowmen, 1,200

[041-180]pikemen, and 104 horsemen—a force which would seem to place it second

[041-181]to Dublin in point of military strength. The capture of so important a

[041-182]place by McMurrogh was a cheering omen to his followers. He razed the

[041-183]walls and towers, and carried off gold, silver, and hostages.

[041-184]On the 2nd of October, 1394, the royal fleet of Richard arrived from

[041-185]Milford Haven, at Waterford. To those who saw Ireland for the first

[041-186]time, the rock of Dundonolf, famed for Raymond's camp, the abbey of

[041-187]Dunbrody, looking calmly down on the confluence of the three rivers,

[041-188]and the half-Danish, half-Norman port before them, must have presented

[041-189]scenes full of interest. To the townsmen the fleet was something

[041-190]wonderful. The endless succession of ships of all sizes and models,

[041-191]which had wafted over 30,000 archers and 4,000 men-at-arms; the royal

[041-192]galley leading on the fluttering pennons of so many great nobles, was a

[041-193]novel sight to that generation. Attendant on the King were his uncle,

[041-194]the Duke of Gloucester, the young Earl of March, heir apparent, Thomas

[041-195]Mowbray, Earl of Nottingham, the Earl of Rutland, the Lord Thomas

[041-196]Percy, afterwards Earl of Westmoreland, and father of Hotspur, and Sir

[041-197]Thomas Moreley, heir to the last Lord Marshal of the "Pale." Several

[041-198]dignitaries of the English Church, as well Bishops as Abbots, were also

[041-199]with the fleet. Immediately after landing, a _Te Deum_ was sung in the

[041-200]Cathedral, where Earl Richard had wedded the Princess Eva, where Henry

[041-201]II. and John had offered up similar thanksgivings.

[041-202]Richard remained a week at Waterford; gave splendid _fetes_, and

[041-203]received some lords of the neighbouring country, Le Poers, Graces, and

[041-204]Butlers. He made gifts to churches, and ratified the charter given by

[041-205]John to the abbey of Holy Cross in Munster. He issued a summons to

[041-206]Gerald, Earl of Desmond, to appear before him by the feast of the

[041-207]Purification "in whatever part of Ireland he should then be," to answer

[041-208]to the charge of having usurped the manor, revenues, and honour of

[041-209]Dungarvan. Although it was then near the middle of October, he took the

[041-210]resolution of marching to Dublin, through the country of McMurrogh, and

[041-211]knowing the memory of Edward the Confessor to be popular in Leinster,

[041-212]he furled the royal banner, and hoisted that of the saintly Saxon king,

[041-213]which bore "a cross patence, or, on a field gules, with four doves

[041-214]argent on the shield." His own proper banner bore lioncels and

[041-215]fleur-de-lis. His route was by Thomastown to Kilkenny, a city which had

[041-216]risen into importance with the Butlers. Nearly half a century before,

[041-217]this family had brought artizans from Flanders, who established the

[041-218]manufacture of woollens, for which the town was ever after famous. Its

[041-219]military importance was early felt and long maintained. At this city

[041-220]Richard was joined by Sir William de Wellesley, who claimed to be

[041-221]hereditary standard-bearer for Ireland, and by other Anglo-Irish

[041-222]nobles. From thence he despatched his Earl Marshal into "Catherlough"

[041-223]to treat with McMurrogh. On the plain of Ballygorry, near Carlow, Art,

[041-224]with his uncle, Malachy, O'Moore, O'Nolan, O'Byrne, MacDavid, and other

[041-225]chiefs, met the Earl Marshal. The terms proposed were almost equivalent

[041-226]to extermination. They were, in effect, that the Leinster chieftains,

[041-227]under fines of enormous amount, payable into the Apostolic chamber,

[041-228]should, before the first Sunday of Lent, surrender to the English King

[041-229]"the full possession of all their lands, tenements, castles, woods, and

[041-230]forts, which by them and all other of the Kenseologhes, their

[041-231]companions, men, or adherents, late were occupied within the province

[041-232]of Leinster." And the condition of this surrender was to be, that they

[041-233]should have unmolested possession of any and all lands they could

[041-234]conquer from the King's other Irish enemies elsewhere in the kingdom.

[041-235]To these hard conditions some of the minor chiefs, overawed by the

[041-236]immense force brought against them, would, it seems, have submitted,

[041-237]but Art sternly refused to treat, declaring that if he made terms at

[041-238]all, it should be with the King and not with the Earl Marshal; and that

[041-239]instead of yielding his own lands, his wife's patrimony in Kildare

[041-240]should be restored. This broke up the conference, and Mowbray returned

[041-241]discomfitted to Kilkenny.

[041-242]King Richard, full of indignation, put himself at the head of his army

[041-243]and advanced against the Leinster clans. But his march was slow and

[041-244]painful: the season and the forest fought against him; he was unable to

[041-245]collect by the way sufficient fodder for the horses or provisions for

[041-246]the men. McMurrogh swept off everything of the nature of food—took

[041-247]advantage of his knowledge of the country to burst upon the enemy by

[041-248]night, to entrap them into ambuscades, to separate the cavalry from the

[041-249]foot, and by many other stratagems to thin their ranks and harass the

[041-250]stragglers. At length Richard, despairing of dislodging him from his

[041-251]fastnesses in Idrone, or fighting a way out of them, sent to him

[041-252]another deputation of "the English and Irish of Leinster," inviting him

[041-253]to Dublin to a personal interview. This proposal was accepted, and the

[041-254]English king continued his way to Dublin, probably along the sea coast

[041-255]by Bray and the white strand, over Killiney and Dunleary. Soon after

[041-256]his arrival at Dublin, care was taken to repair the highway which ran

[041-257]by the sea, towards Wicklow and Wexford.
