CHAPTER VIII.

[030-001]EVENTS OF THE THIRTEENTH CENTURY—THE NORMANS IN CONNAUGHT.

[030-002]Ireland, during the first three quarters of the thirteenth century,

[030-003]produced fewer important events, and fewer great men, than in the

[030-004]thirty last years of the century preceding. From the side of England,

[030-005]she was subjected to no imminent danger in all that interval. The reign

[030-006]of John ending in 1216, and that of Henry III. extending till 1271,

[030-007]were fully occupied with the insurrections of the Barons, with French,

[030-008]Scotch, and Welsh wars, family feuds, the rise and fall of royal

[030-009]favourites, and all those other incidents which naturally, befall in a

[030-010]state of society where the King is weak, the aristocracy strong and

[030-011]insolent, and the commons disunited and despised. During this period

[030-012]the fusion of Norman, Saxon, and Briton went slowly on, and the next

[030-013]age saw for the first time a population which could be properly called

[030-014]English. "Do you take me for an Englishman?" was the last expression of

[030-015]Norman arrogance in the reign of King John; but the close of the reign

[030-016]of Henry III., through the action of commercial and political causes,

[030-017]saw a very different state of feeling growing up between the

[030-018]descendants of the races which contended for mastery under Harold and

[030-019]William. The strongly marked Norman characteristics lingered in Ireland

[030-020]half a century later, for it is usually the case that traits of caste

[030-021]survive longest in colonies and remote provinces. In Richard de Burgo,

[030-022]commonly called the Red Earl of Ulster, all the genius and the vices of

[030-023]the race of Rollo blazed out over Ireland for the last time, and with

[030-024]terrible effect.

[030-025]During the first three quarters of the century, our history, like that

[030-026]of England, is the history of a few great houses; nation there is,

[030-027]strictly speaking, none. It will be necessary, therefore, to group

[030-028]together the acts of two or three generations of men of the same name,

[030-029]as the only method of finding our way through the shifting scenes of

[030-030]this stormy period.

[030-031]The power of the great Connaught family of O'Conor, so terribly shaken

[030-032]by the fratricidal wars and unnatural alliances of the sons and

[030-033]grandsons of Roderick, was in great part restored by the ability and

[030-034]energy of Cathal _Crovdearg_. In his early struggles for power he was

[030-035]greatly assisted by the anarchy which reigned among the English nobles.

[030-036]Mayler Fitz-Henry, the last of Strongbow's companions, who rose to such

[030-037]eminence, being Justiciary in the first six years of the century, was

[030-038]aided by O'Conor to besiege William de Burgo in Limerick, and to

[030-039]cripple the power of the de Lacys in Meath. In the year 1207, John

[030-040]Gray, Bishop of Norwich, was sent over, as more likely to be impartial

[030-041]than any ruler personally interested in the old quarrels, but during

[030-042]his first term of office, the interdict with which Innocent III. had

[030-043]smitten England, hung like an Egyptian darkness over the Anglo-Norman

[030-044]power in Ireland. The native Irish, however, were exempt from its

[030-045]enervating effects, and Cathal O'Conor, by the time King John came over

[030-046]in person—in the year 1210—to endeavour to retrieve the English

[030-047]interest, had warred down all his enemies, and was of power sufficient

[030-048]to treat with the English sovereign as independently as Roderick had

[030-049]done with Henry II. thirty-five years before. He personally conferred

[030-050]with John at Dublin, as the O'Neil and other native Princes did; he

[030-051]procured from the English King the condemnation of John de Burgo, who

[030-052]had maintained his father's claims on a portion of Connaught, and he

[030-053]was formally recognised, according to the approved forms of Norman

[030-054]diplomacy, as seized of the whole of Connaught, in his own right.

[030-055]The visit of King John, which lasted from the 20th of June till the

[030-056]25th of August, was mainly directed to the reduction of those

[030-057]intractable Anglo-Irish Barons whom Fitz-Henry and Gray had proved

[030-058]themselves unable to cope with. Of these the de Lacys of Meath were the

[030-059]most obnoxious. They not only assumed an independent state, but had

[030-060]sheltered de Braos, Lord of Brecknock, one of the recusant Barons of

[030-061]Wales, and refused to surrender him on the royal summons. To assert his

[030-062]authority, and to strike terror into the nobles of other possessions,

[030-063]John crossed the channel with a prodigious fleet—in the Irish annals

[030-064]said to consist of 700 sail. He landed at Crook, reached Dublin, and

[030-065]prepared at once to subdue the Lacys. With his own army, and the

[030-066]co-operation of Cathal O'Conor, he drove out Walter de Lacy, Lord of

[030-067]Meath, who fled to his brother, Hugh de Lacy, since de Courcy's

[030-068]disgrace, Earl of Ulster. From Meath into Louth John pursued the

[030-069]brothers, crossing the lough at Carlingford with his ships, which must

[030-070]have coasted in his company. From Carlingford they retreated, and he

[030-071]pursued to Carrickfergus, and from that fortress, unable to resist a

[030-072]royal fleet and navy, they fled into Man or Scotland, and thence

[030-073]escaped in disguise into France. With their guest de Braos, they

[030-074]wrought as gardeners in the grounds of the Abbey of Saint Taurin

[030-075]Evreux, until the Abbot, having discovered by their manners the key to

[030-076]their real rank, negotiated successfully with John for their

[030-077]restoration to their estates. Walter agreed to pay a fine of 2,500

[030-078]marks for his lordship in Meath, and Hugh 4,000 marks for his

[030-079]possessions in Ulster. Of de Braos we have no particulars; his

[030-080]high-spirited wife and children were thought to have been starved to

[030-081]death by order of the unforgiving tyrant in one of his castles. The de

[030-082]Lacys, on their restoration, were accompanied to Ireland by a nephew of

[030-083]the Abbot of St. Taurin, on whom they conferred an estate and the

[030-084]honour of knighthood.

[030-085]The only other acts of John's sojourn in Ireland was his treaty with

[030-086]O'Conor, already mentioned, and the mapping out, on paper, of the

[030-087]intended counties of Oriel (or Louth), Meath, Dublin, Kildare,

[030-088]Kilkenny, Katherlough (or Carlow), Wexford, Waterford, Cork, Kerry,

[030-089]Limerick, and Tipperary, as the only districts in which those he

[030-090]claimed as his subjects had any possessions. He again installed the

[030-091]Bishop of Norwich as his justiciary or lieutenant, who, three years,

[030-092]later, was succeeded by Henry de Londres, the next Archbishop of

[030-093]Dublin, and he again (A.D. 1215), by Geoffrey de Marisco, the last of

[030-094]John's deputies. In the year 1216, Henry III., an infant ten years of

[030-095]age, succeeded to the English throne, and the next dozen years the

[030-096]history of the two islands is slightly connected, except by the

[030-097]fortunes of the family of de Burgh, whose head, Hubert de Burgh, the

[030-098]Chief Justiciary, from the accession of the new King, until the first

[030-099]third of the century had closed, was in reality the Sovereign of

[030-100]England. Among his other titles he held that of Lord of Connaught,

[030-101]which he conveyed to his relative, Richard de Burgo, the son or

[030-102]grandson of William Fitz-Aldelm de Burgo, about the year 1225. And this

[030-103]brings us to relate how the house of Clanrickarde rose upon the flank

[030-104]of the house of O'Conor, and after holding an almost equal front for

[030-105]two generations, finally overshadowed its more ancient rival.

[030-106]While Cathal _Crovdearg_ lived, the O'Conor's held their own, and

[030-107]rather more than their own, by policy or arms. Not only did his own

[030-108]power suffer no diminution, but he more than once assisted the Dalgais

[030-109]and the Eugenians to expel their invaders from North and South Munster,

[030-110]and to uphold their ancient rights and laws. During the last years of

[030-111]John's reign that King and his Barons were mutually too busy to set

[030-112]aside the arrangement entered into in 1210. In the first years of Henry

[030-113]it was also left undisturbed by the English court. In 1221 we read that

[030-114]the de Lacys, remembering, no doubt, the part he had played in their

[030-115]expulsion, endeavoured to fortify Athleague against him, but the

[030-116]veteran King, crossing the Shannon farther northward, took them in the

[030-117]rear, compelled them to make peace, and broke down their Castle. This

[030-118]was almost the last of his victories. In the year 1213 we read in the

[030-119]Annals of "an awful and heavy shower which fell over Connaught," and

[030-120]was held to presage the death of its heroic King. Feeling his hour had

[030-121]come, this Prince, to whom are justly attributed the rare union of

[030-122]virtues, ardour of mind, chastity of body, meekness in prosperity,

[030-123]fortitude under defeat, prudence in civil business, undaunted bravery

[030-124]in battle, and a piety of life beyond all his cotemporaries—feeling the

[030-125]near approach of death, retired to the Abbey of Knockmoy, which he had

[030-126]founded and endowed, and there expired in the Franciscan habit, at an

[030-127]age which must have bordered on fourscore. He was succeeded by his son,

[030-128]Hugh O'Conor, "the hostages of Connaught being in his house" at the

[030-129]time of his illustrious father's death.

[030-131]No sooner was Cathal _Crovdearg_ deceased than Hubert de Burgo procured

[030-132]the grants of the whole Province, reserving only five cantreds about

[030-133]Athlone for a royal garrison to be made to Richard de Burgo, his

[030-134]nephew. Richard had married Hodierna, granddaughter to Cathal, and

[030-135]thus, like all the Normans, though totally against the Irish custom,

[030-136]claimed a part of Connaught in right of his wife. But in the sons of

[030-137]Cathal he found his equal both in policy and arms, and with the fall of

[030-138]his uncle at the English court (about the year 1233), Feidlim O'Conor,

[030-139]the successor of Hugh, taking advantage of the event, made interest at

[030-140]the Court of Henry III. sufficient to have his overgrown neighbour

[030-141]stripped of some of his strongholds by royal order. The King was so

[030-142]impressed with O'Conor's representations that he wrote peremptorily to

[030-143]Maurice Fitzgerald, second Lord Offally, then his deputy, "to root out

[030-144]that barren tree planted in Offally by Hubert de Burgh, in the madness

[030-145]of his power, and not to suffer it to shoot forth." Five years later,

[030-146]Feidlim, in return, carried some of his force, in conjunction with the

[030-147]deputy, to Henry's aid in Wales, though, as their arrival was somewhat

[030-148]tardy, Fitzgerald was soon after dismissed on that account.

[030-149]Richard de Burgo died in attendance on King Henry in France (A.D.

[030-150]1243), and was succeeded by his son, Walter de Burgo, who continued,

[030-151]with varying fortunes, the contest for Connaught with Feidlim, until

[030-152]the death of the latter, in the Black Abbey of Roscommon, in the year

[030-153]1265. Hugh O'Conor, the son and successor of Feidlim, continued the

[030-154]intrepid guardian of his house and province during the nine years he

[030-155]survived his father. In the year 1254, by marriage with the daughter of

[030-156]de Lacy, Earl of Ulster, that title had passed into the family of de

[030-157]Burgh, bringing with it, for the time, much substantial, though

[030-158]distant, strength. It was considered only a secondary title, and as the

[030-159]eldest son of the first de Lacy remained Lord of Meath, while the

[030-160]younger took de Courcy's forfeited title of Ulster, so, in the next

[030-161]generation, did the sons of this Walter de Burgh, until death and time

[030-162]reunited both titles in the same person. Walter de Burgh died in the

[030-163]year 1271, in the Castle of Galway; his great rival, Feidlim O'Conor,

[030-164]in 1274, was buried in the Abbey of Boyle. The former is styled King of

[030-165]the English of Connaught by the Irish Annalists, who also speak of

[030-166]Feidlim as "the most triumphant and the most feared (by the invaders)

[030-167]of any King that had been in Connaught before his time." The relative

[030-168]position of the Irish and English in that Province, towards the end of

[030-169]this century, may be judged by the fact, that of the Anglo-Normans

[030-170]summoned by Edward I. to join him in Scotland in 1299, but two, Richard

[030-171]de Burgo and Piers de Bermingham, Baron of Athenry, had then

[030-172]possessions in Connaught. There were Norman Castles at Athlone, at

[030-173]Athenry, at Galway, and perhaps at other points; but the natives still

[030-174]swayed supreme over the plains of Rathcrogan, the plains of Boyle, the

[030-175]forests and lakes of Roscommon, and the whole of _Iar_, or West

[030-176]Connaught, from Lough Corrib to the ocean, with the very important

[030-177]exception of the castle and port of Galway. A mightier de Burgo than

[030-178]any that had yet appeared was to see in his house, in the year 1286,

[030-179]"the hostages of all Connaught;" but his life and death form a distinct

[030-180]epoch in our story and must be treated separately.
