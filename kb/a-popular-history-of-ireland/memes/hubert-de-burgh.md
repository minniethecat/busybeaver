Hubert de Burgh was a powerful Anglo-Norman noble who served as Chief Justiciary of England from the accession of [[Henry III]] until the early 1230s,.

> "Hubert de Burgh, the Chief Justiciary, from the accession of the new King, until the first third of the century had closed, was in reality the Sovereign of England." (030-097)


during which time he was effectively the Sovereign of England (030-097). Though his primary sphere of influence was England, his family's fortunes became deeply entangled with Irish affairs, and his descendants — including the de Burghs, Burkes, and Clanrickardes — would number among the most influential Anglo-Irish dynasties for centuries to come.

## Rise to Power

De Burgh rose to prominence in the service of King John and consolidated his authority after John's death in 1216. During the minority of Henry III, de Burgh effectively governed England as Chief Justiciary, exercising powers that made him the de facto ruler of the realm (030-097). His administration spanned the first third of the thirteenth century and coincided with a period of significant Anglo-Norman expansion in [[Ireland]].

## Irish Connections and the de Burgh Legacy

Though de Burgh himself was primarily based in England, his family established deep roots in Ireland. The head of the de Burgh family became one of the most potent Anglo-Norman lords in the country. His Irish interests were administered through deputies, including [[Maurice Fitzgerald]], second Lord Offally,.

> "Maurice Fitzgerald, second Lord Offally, then his deputy, [was ordered] to root out that barren tree planted in Offally by Hubert de Burgh, in the madness of his power, and not to suffer it to shoot forth." (030-144)


who was instructed to dismantle de Burgh's Offally settlement (030-144).

The conflict with the native Irish nobility was a persistent theme of de Burgh's Irish policy. When [[Feidlimid mac Aedh|Feidlim]], the Irish king, carried forces in conjunction with allies against the Anglo-Norman settlements, it marked another chapter in the long contest between the new Norman lords and the old Gaelic order for control of the Irish midlands (030-145).

De Burgh's dealings with the Irish nobility shaped the legal framework through which Anglo-Norman authority was asserted. When [[Richard de Burgo]], successor to the Marshall title, "made himself obnoxious" to the young King Henry, or to de Burgh himself, letters of outlawry were despatched to the Justiciary Fitzgerald, ordering "de Burgo, [[de Lacy]], and other Anglo-Irish lords" to be intercepted if they landed in Ireland (031-095).

## Downfall

De Burgh's extraordinary power could not last indefinitely. His fall was precipitated by events that shocked English society and turned opinion against him.

> "Such was the horror which the deed excited in England, that it hastened the fall of Hubert de Burgh, though Maurice Fitzgerald, of Offally — ancestor of the Kildare family — having cleared himself of all complicity in it by oath — was continued as Justiciary for ten years." (031-119)


Though his loyal lieutenant [[Maurice Fitzgerald]] of Offally — ancestor of the powerful Kildare family — survived the political storm, having "cleared himself of all complicity in it by oath" and continuing as Justiciary for another ten years (031-119). The de Burgh influence in Ireland, however, endured through the family's many branches, including the Burkes of [[Connaught]] and the [[Clanrickarde|Earls of Clanrickarde]].
