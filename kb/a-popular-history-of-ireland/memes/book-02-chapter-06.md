CHAPTER VI.

[014-001]BRIAN, ARD-RIGH—BATTLE OF CLONTARF.

[014-002]By the deposition of Malachy II., and the transfer of supreme power to

[014-003]the long-excluded line of Heber, Brian completed the revolution which

[014-004]Time had wrought in the ancient Celtic constitution. He threw open the

[014-005]sovereignty to every great family as a prize to be won by policy or

[014-006]force, and no longer an inheritance to be determined by usage and law.

[014-007]The consequences were what might have been expected. After his death

[014-008]the O'Conors of the west competed with both O'Neills and O'Briens for

[014-009]supremacy, and a chronic civil war prepared the path for Strongbow and

[014-010]the Normans. The term "Kings with Opposition" is applied to nearly all

[014-011]who reigned between Brian's time and Roderick O'Conor's, meaning,

[014-012]thereby, kings who were unable to secure general obedience to their

[014-013]administration of affairs.

[014-014]During the remainder of his life, Brian wielded with accustomed vigour

[014-015]the supreme power. The Hy-Nials were, of course, his chief difficulty.

[014-016]In the year 1002, we find him at Ballysadare, in Sligo, challenging

[014-017]their obedience; in 1004, we find him at Armagh "offering twenty ounces

[014-018]of gold on Patrick's altar," staying a week there and receiving

[014-019]hostages; in 1005, he marched through Connaught, crossed the river Erne

[014-020]at Ballyshannon, proceeded through Tyrconnell and Tyrowen, crossed the

[014-021]Bann into Antrim, and returned through Down and Dundalk, "about

[014-022]Lammas," to Tara. In this and the two succeeding years, by taking

[014-023]similar "circuits," he subdued Ulster, without any pitched battle, and

[014-024]caused his authority to be feared and obeyed nearly as much at the

[014-025]Giant's Causeway as at the bridge of Athlone. In his own house of

[014-026]Kinkora, Brian entertained at Christmas 3,000 guests, including the

[014-027]Danish Lords of Dublin and Man, the fugitive Earl of Kent, the young

[014-028]King of Scots, certain Welsh Princes, and those of Munster, Ulster,

[014-029]Leinster and Connaught, beside his hostages. At the same time Malachy,

[014-030]with the shadow, of independence, kept his unfrequented court in

[014-031]West-Meath, amusing himself with wine and chess and the taming of

[014-032]unmanageable horses, in which last pursuit, after his abdication, we

[014-033]hear of his breaking a limb. To support the hospitalities of Kinkora,

[014-034]the tributes of every province were rendered in kind at his gate, on

[014-035]the first day of November. Connaught sent 800 cows and 800 hogs; Ulster

[014-036]alone 500 cows, and as many hogs, and "sixty loads of iron;" Leinster

[014-037]300 bullocks, 300 hogs, and 300 loads of iron; Ossory, Desmond, and the

[014-038]smaller territories, in proportion; the Danes of Dublin 150 pipes of

[014-039]wine, and the Danes of Limerick 365 of red wine. The Dalcassians, his

[014-040]own people, were exempt from all tribute and taxation—while the rest of

[014-041]Ireland was thus catering for Kinkora.

[014-042]The lyric Poets, in their nature courtiers and given to enjoyment,

[014-043]flocked, of course, to this bountiful palace. The harp was seldom

[014-044]silent night or day, the strains of panegyric were as prodigal and

[014-045]incessant as the falling of the Shannon over Killaloe. Among these

[014-046]eulogiums none is better known than that beautiful allegory of the poet

[014-047]McLaig, who sung that "a young lady of great beauty, adorned with

[014-048]jewels and costly dress, might perform unmolested a journey on foot

[014-049]through the Island, carrying a straight wand, on the top of which might

[014-050]be a ring of great value." The name of Brian was thus celebrated as in

[014-051]itself a sufficient protection of life, chastity, and property, in

[014-052]every corner of the Island. Not only the Poets, but the more exact and

[014-053]simple Annalists applaud Brian's administration of the laws, and his

[014-054]personal virtues. He laboured hard to restore the Christian

[014-055]civilization, so much defaced by two centuries of Pagan warfare. To

[014-056]facilitate the execution of the laws he enacted the general use of

[014-057]surnames, obliging the clans to take the name of a common ancestor,

[014-058]with the addition of "Mac," or "O"—words which signify "of," or "son

[014-059]of," a forefather. Thus, the Northern Hy-Nials divided into O'Neils,

[014-060]O'Donnells, McLaughlins, &c.; the Sil-Murray took the name of O'Conor,

[014-061]and Brian's own posterity became known as O'Briens. To justice he added

[014-062]munificence, and of this the Churches and Schools of the entire Island

[014-063]were the recipients. Many a desolate shrine he adorned, many a bleak

[014-064]chancel he hung with lamps, many a long silent tower had its bells

[014-065]restored. Monasteries were rebuilt, and the praise of God was kept up

[014-066]perpetually by a devoted brotherhood. Roads and bridges were repaired

[014-067]and several strong stone fortresses were erected, to command the passes

[014-068]of lakes and rivers. The vulnerable points along the Shannon, and the

[014-069]Suir, and the lakes, as far north as the Foyle, were secured by forts

[014-070]of clay and stone. Thirteen "royal houses" in Munster alone are said to

[014-071]have been by him restored to their original uses. What increases our

[014-072]respect for the wisdom and energy thus displayed, is the fact, that the

[014-073]author of so many improvements, enjoyed but five short years of peace,

[014-074]after his accession to the Monarchy. His administrative genius must

[014-075]have been great when, after a long life of warfare, he could apply

[014-076]himself to so many works of internal improvement and external defence.

[014-078]In the five years of peace just spoken of (from 1005 to 1010), Brian

[014-079]lost by death his second wife, a son called Donald, and his brother

[014-080]Marcan, called in the annals "head of the clergy of Munster;" Hugh, the

[014-081]son of Mahon, also died about the same period. His favourite son and

[014-082]heir, Morrogh, was left, and Morrogh had, at this time, several

[014-083]children. Other sons and daughters were also left him, by each of his

[014-084]wives, so that there was every prospect that the posterity for whom he

[014-085]had so long sought the sovereignty of Ireland, would continue to

[014-086]possess it for countless generations. But God disposes of what man only

[014-087]proposes!

[014-088]The Northmen had never yet abandoned any soil on which they had once

[014-089]set foot, and the policy of conciliation which the veteran King adopted

[014-090]in his old age, was not likely to disarm men of their stamp. Every

[014-091]intelligence of the achievements of their race in other realms

[014-092]stimulated them to new exertions and shamed them out of peaceful

[014-093]submission. Rollo and his successors had, within Brian's lifetime,

[014-094]founded in France the great dukedom of Normandy; while Sweyn had swept

[014-095]irresistibly over England and Wales, and prepared the way for a Danish

[014-096]dynasty. Pride and shame alike appealed to their warlike compatriots

[014-097]not to allow the fertile Hibernia to slip from their grasp, and the

[014-098]great age of its long-dreaded king seemed to promise them an easier

[014-099]victory than heretofore was possible. In 1012 we find Brian at Lough

[014-100]Foyle repelling a new Danish invasion, and giving "freedom to Patrick's

[014-101]Churches;" the same year, an army under Morrogh and another under

[014-102]Malachy was similarly engaged in Leinster and Meath; the former

[014-103]carrying his arms to Kilmainham, on the south side of Dublin, the other

[014-104]to Howth, on the north; in this year also "the Gentiles," or Pagan

[014-105]Northmen, made a descent on Cork, and burned the city, but were driven

[014-106]off by the neighbouring chiefs.

[014-107]The great event, however, of the long war which had now been waged for

[014-108]full two hundred years between the men of Erin and the men of

[014-109]Scandinavia was approaching. What may fairly be called the last field

[014-110]day of Christianity and Paganism on Irish soil, was near at hand. A

[014-111]taunt thrown out over a game of chess, at Kinkora, is said to have

[014-112]hastened this memorable day. Maelmurra, Prince of Leinster, playing or

[014-113]advising on the game, made, or recommended, a false move, upon which

[014-114]Morrogh, son of Brian, observed, it was no wonder his friends, the

[014-115]Danes, (to whom he owed his elevation,) were beaten at Glen-Mama, if he

[014-116]gave them advice like that. Maelmurra, highly incensed by this

[014-117]allusion—all the more severe for its bitter truth—arose, ordered his

[014-118]horse, and rode away in haste. Brian, when he heard it, despatched a

[014-119]messenger after the indignant guest, begging him to return, but

[014-120]Maelmurra was not to be pacified, and refused. We next hear of him as

[014-121]concerting with certain Danish agents, always open to such

[014-122]negotiations, those measures which led to the great invasion of the

[014-123]year 1014, in which the whole Scanian race, from Anglesea and Man,

[014-124]north to Norway, bore an active share.

[014-125]These agents passing over to England and Man, among the Scottish isles,

[014-126]and even to the Baltic, followed up the design of an invasion on a

[014-127]gigantic scale. Suibne, Earl of Man, entered warmly into the

[014-128]conspiracy, and sent the "war arrow" through all those "out-islands"

[014-129]which obeyed him as Lord. A yet more formidable potentate, Sigurd, of

[014-130]the Orkneys, next joined the league. He was the fourteenth Earl of

[014-131]Orkney of Norse origin, and his power was, at this period, a balance to

[014-132]that of his nearest neighbour, the King of Scots. He had ruled since

[014-133]the year 996, not only over the Orkneys, Shetland, and Northern

[014-134]Hebrides, but the coasts of Caithness and Sutherland, and even Ross and

[014-135]Moray rendered him homage and tribute. Eight years before the battle of

[014-136]Clontarf, Malcolm II., of Scotland, had been feign to purchase his

[014-137]alliance, by giving him his daughter in marriage, and the Kings of

[014-138]Denmark and Norway treated with him on equal terms. The hundred

[014-139]inhabited isles which lie between Yell and Man,—isles which after their

[014-140]conversion contained "three hundred churches and chapels"—sent in their

[014-141]contingents, to swell the following of the renowned Earl Sigurd. As his

[014-142]fleet bore southward from Kirkwall it swept the subject coast of

[014-143]Scotland, and gathered from every lough its galleys and its fighting

[014-144]men. The rendezvous was the Isle of Man, where Suibne had placed his

[014-145]own forces under the command of Brodar or Broderick, a famous leader

[014-146]against the Britons of Wales and Cornwall. In conjunction with Sigurd,

[014-147]the Manxmen sailed over to Ireland, where they were joined, in the

[014-148]Liffey, by Carl Canuteson, Prince of Denmark, at the head of 1400

[014-149]champions clad in armour. Sitrick of Dublin stood, or affected to

[014-150]stand, neutral in these preparations, but Maelmurra of Leinster had

[014-151]mustered all the forces he could command for such an expedition. He was

[014-152]himself the head of the powerful family of O'Byrne, and was followed in

[014-153]his alliances by others of the descendants of Cahir More. O'Nolan and

[014-154]O'More, with a truer sense of duty, fought on the patriotic side.

[014-155]Brian had not been ignorant of the exertions which were made during the

[014-156]summer and winter of the year 1013, to combine an overwhelming force

[014-157]against him. In his exertions to meet force with force, it is

[014-158]gratifying to every believer in human excellence to find him actively

[014-159]supported by the Prince whom he had so recently deposed. Malachy,

[014-160]during the summer of 1013, had, indeed, lost two sons in skirmishes

[014-161]with Sitrick and Maelmurra, and had, therefore, his own personal wrongs

[014-162]to avenge; but he cordially co-operated with Brian before those

[014-163]occurrences, and now loyally seconded all his movements. The Lords of

[014-164]the southern half-kingdom—the Lords of Desies, Fermoy, Inchiquin,

[014-165]Corca-Baskin, Kinalmeaky, Kerry, and the Lords of Hy-Many and

[014-166]Hy-Fiachra, in Connaught, hastened to his standard. O'More and O'Nolan

[014-167]of Leinster, and Donald, Steward of Marr, in Scotland, were the other

[014-168]chieftains who joined him before Clontarf, besides those of his own

[014-169]kindred. None of the Northern Hy-Nial took part in the battle—they had

[014-170]submitted to Brian, but they never cordially supported him.

[014-171]Clontarf, the lawn or meadow of bulls, stretches along the

[014-172]crescent-shaped north strand of Dublin harbour, from the ancient

[014-173]salmon-weir at Ballyboght bridge, towards the promontory of Howth. Both

[014-174]horns of the crescent were held by the enemy, and communicated with his

[014-175]ships: the inland point terminating in the roofs of Dublin, and the

[014-176]seaward marked by the lion-like head of Howth. The meadow land between

[014-177]sloped gently upward and inward from the beach, and for the myriad

[014-178]duels which formed the ancient battle, no field could present less

[014-179]positive vantage-ground to combatants on either side. The invading

[014-180]force had possession of both wings, so that Brian's army, which had

[014-181]first encamped at Kilmainham, must have crossed the Liffey higher up,

[014-182]and marched round by the present Drumcondra in order to reach the

[014-183]appointed field. The day seems to have been decided on by formal

[014-184]challenge, for we are told Brian did not wish to fight in the last week

[014-185]of Lent, but a Pagan oracle having assured victory to Brodar, one of

[014-186]the northern leaders, if he engaged on a Friday, the invaders insisted

[014-187]on being led to battle on that day. And it so happened that, of all

[014-188]Fridays in the year, it fell on the Friday before Easter: that awful

[014-189]anniversary when the altars of the Church are veiled throughout

[014-190]Christendom, and the dark stone is rolled to the door of the mystic

[014-191]sepulchre.

[014-192]The forces on both sides could not have fallen short of twenty thousand

[014-193]men. Under Carl Canuteson fought "the ten hundred in armour," as they

[014-194]are called in the Irish annals, or "the fourteen hundred," as they are

[014-195]called in northern chronicles; under Brodar, the Manxmen and the Danes

[014-196]of Anglesea and Wales; under Sigurd, the men of Orkney and its

[014-197]dependencies; under Maelmurra, of Leinster, his own tribe, and their

[014-198]kinsmen of Offally and Cullen—the modern Kildare and Wicklow; under

[014-199]Brian's son, Morrogh, were the tribes of Munster; under the command of

[014-200]Malachy, those of Meath; under the Lord of Hy-Many, the men of

[014-201]Connaught; and the Stewart of Marr had also his command. The engagement

[014-202]was to commence with the morning, so that, as soon as it was day,

[014-203]Brian, Crucifix in hand, harangued his army. "On this day Christ died

[014-204]for _you_!" was the spirit-stirring appeal of the venerable Christian

[014-205]King. At the entreaty of his friends, after this review, he retired to

[014-206]his tent, which stood at some distance, and was guarded by three of his

[014-207]aids. Here, he alternately prostrated himself before the Crucifix, or

[014-208]looked out from the tent door upon the dreadful scene that lay beyond.

[014-209]The sun rose to the zenith and took his way towards the west, but still

[014-210]the roar of the battle did not abate. Sometimes as their right hands

[014-211]swelled with the sword-hilts, well-known warriors might be seen falling

[014-212]back to bathe them, in a neighbouring spring, and then rushing again

[014-213]into the melee. The line of the engagement extended from the

[014-214]salmon-weir towards Howth, not less than a couple of miles, so that it

[014-215]was impossible to take in at a glance the probabilities of victory.

[014-216]Once during the heat of the day one of his servants said to Brian, "A

[014-217]vast multitude are moving towards us." "What sort of people are they?"

[014-218]inquired Brian. "They are green-naked people." said the attendant.

[014-219]"Oh!" replied the king, "they are the Danes in armour!" The utmost fury

[014-220]was displayed on all sides. Sigurd, Earl of Orkney, fell by Thurlogh,

[014-221]grandson of Brian; and Anrud, one of the captains of the men in armour,

[014-222]by the hand of his father, Morrogh; but both father and son perished in

[014-223]the dreadful conflict; Maelmurra of Leinster, with his lords, fell on

[014-224]one side, and Conaing, nephew of Brian, O'Kelly, O'Heyne, and the

[014-225]Stewart of Marr, on the other. Hardly a nobly-born man escaped, or

[014-226]sought to escape. The ten hundred in armour, and three thousand others

[014-227]of the enemy, with about an equal number of the men of Ireland, lay

[014-228]dead upon the field. One division of the enemy were, towards sunset,

[014-229]retreating to their ships, when Brodar, the Viking, perceiving the tent

[014-230]of Brian, standing apart, without a guard, and the aged king on his

[014-231]knees before the Crucifix, rushed in, cut him down with a single blow,

[014-232]and then continued his flight. But he was overtaken by the guard, and

[014-233]despatched by the most cruel death they could devise. Thus, on the

[014-234]field of battle, in the act of prayer, on the day of our Lord's

[014-235]Crucifixion, fell the Christian King in the cause of native land and

[014-236]Holy Cross. Many elegies have been dedicated to his memory, and not the

[014-237]least noble of these strains belong to his enemies. In death as in life

[014-238]he was still Brian "of the tributes."

[014-239]The deceased hero took his place at once in history, national and

[014-240]foreign. On hearing of his death, Maelmurra, Archbishop of Armagh, came

[014-241]with his clergy to Swords, in Meath, and conducted the body to Armagh,

[014-242]where, with his son and nephew and the Lord of Desies, he was solemnly

[014-243]interred "in a new tomb." The fame of the event went out through all

[014-244]nations. The chronicles of Wales, of Scotland, and of Man; the annals

[014-245]of Ademar and Marianus; the Sagas of Denmark and the Isles all record

[014-246]the event. In "the Orcades" of Thormodus Torfaeus, a wail over the

[014-247]defeat of the Islesmen is heard, which they call

[014-248]"Orkney's woe and Randver's bane."

[014-249]The Norse settlers in Caithness saw terrific visions of Valhalla "the

[014-250]day after the battle." In the NIALA SAGA a Norwegian prince is

[014-251]introduced as asking after his men, and the answer is, "they were all

[014-252]killed." Malcolm of Scotland rejoiced in the defeat and death of his

[014-253]dangerous and implacable neighbour. "Brian's battle," as it is called

[014-254]in the Sagas, was, in short, such a defeat as prevented any general

[014-255]northern combination for the subsequent invasion of Ireland. Not that

[014-256]the country was entirely free from their attacks till the end of the

[014-257]eleventh century, but from the day of Clontarf forward, the long

[014-258]cherished Northern idea of a conquest of Ireland, seems to have been

[014-259]gloomily abandoned by that indomitable people.
