Sir Cahir O'Doherty was the youthful chieftain of Innishowen in [[Ulster]] whose rebellion and death in 1608 provided the English Crown with the final pretext needed to confiscate and plant the six counties of Ulster. Though his rising was brief, its consequences were among the most far-reaching in Irish history.

## Background and Knighthood

O'Doherty had earned his knighthood while still a mere lad, fighting by the side of Sir Henry Dowcra, the English commander who had established a garrison at [[Derry]] (064-187). As the chief of Innishowen, a peninsula in the north of [[Donegal]], he was initially regarded as a loyal ally of the Crown in the years following the [[Nine Years War]] and the surrender of [[Hugh O'Neill]].

The political landscape of Ulster had been transformed by the Flight of the Earls in 1607. With O'Neill and [[Rory O'Donnell]] gone into exile, the English "Undertakers" saw few remaining barriers to the wholesale confiscation of Ulster (064-185). O'Doherty was one of the last remaining native chiefs of standing.

## The Outrage at Derry

The immediate cause of O'Doherty's rebellion was a personal affront. In an altercation with Sir George Paulett, the Governor of Derry, O'Doherty was taunted with conniving at the escape of the Earls — and Paulett, in his passion, struck him in the face (064-189).

> "The youthful chief — he was scarcely one and twenty — was driven almost to madness by this outrage." (064-190)

On the night of the 3rd of May, 1608, by a successful stratagem, O'Doherty got possession of Culmore fort at the mouth of [[Lough Foyle]], and before morning dawned had surprised Derry itself. Paulett, his insulter, he slew with his own hand; most of the garrison were slaughtered; and the town was reduced to ashes (064-192).

## The Rising and Death

Nial Garve O'Donnell, who had been cast off by his old English protectors, was charged with sending O'Doherty supplies and men. For three months O'Doherty kept the field, hoping that every gale might bring him assistance from abroad (064-198). But the summer months that might have brought foreign aid proved fatal to many of the exiles whose co-operation he invoked. In July, Rory O'Donnell expired at [[Rome]]; in August, Maguire died at [[Genoa]] on his way to [[Spain]]; and in September, Caffar O'Donnell was laid in the same grave with his brother (064-201).

> "O'Doherty, enclosed in his native peninsula, between the forces of the Marshal Wingfield and Sir Oliver Lambert, Governor of Connaught, fell by a chance shot, at the rock of Doon, in Kilmacrenan." (064-206)

## Legacy: The Plantation of Ulster

O'Doherty's death did not end the consequences of his rising. A royal commission was issued to Chichester, Lambert, St. John, Ridgeway, Moore, Davis, and Parsons, with the Archbishop of [[Armagh]] and the Bishop of Derry, to inquire into the portions forfeited (064-225). Before these Commissioners, juries duly found that, in consequence of "the rebellion" of O'Neil, O'Donnell, and O'Doherty, the entire six counties of Ulster — enumerated by baronies and parishes — were forfeited to the Crown (064-230).

This sweeping forfeiture became the legal basis for the [[Plantation of Ulster]], which transformed the demographic and political character of the province for centuries to come. O'Doherty's rebellion, sparked by a blow to his honour, thus became the final link in the chain of events that led to the systematic colonization of Gaelic Ulster. His name, alongside O'Neill and O'Donnell, was inscribed in the acts of attainder passed by the Irish Parliament of 1614–1616 (064-347).
