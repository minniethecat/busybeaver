## Overview

Arthur O'Conor (1763–1852) was a leading member of the Society of United Irishmen and a Member of Parliament for Phillipstown. A nephew of Lord Longueville, he moved from constitutional reform to revolutionary conspiracy.

O'Conor became one of the most important Irish figures to ally with revolutionary France. He later served as a general in Napoleon's army, spending most of his long life in French exile. His political writings influenced Irish republican thinking for generations.

## Early Parliamentary Career

O'Conor entered the Irish Parliament as a reformer. He was counted among the new members who sought Catholic emancipation and parliamentary reform. "Arthur O'Conor, member for Phillipstown" (097-218) was listed alongside other radical members. His election to the seat at Trim reflected the growing reform sentiment (097-218).

> Arthur O'Conor, on the other hand, commenced engaging with more revolutionary methods after the frustration of constitutional reform following Lord Fitzwilliam's recall. (097-222)

## United Irishmen Leadership

As reform prospects dimmed, O'Conor became central to the revolutionary conspiracy. McGee places him in the inner circle alongside "Fitzgerald, Arthur O'Conor, Addis Emmet, Wolfe Tone" (099-077). These men formed the leadership that planned the rising of 1798.

> Neilson in 1761, Tone, Arthur O'Conor and Lord Edward Fitzgerald joined the United Irishmen, bringing their talents and connections to the revolutionary cause. (100-017)

O'Conor's pamphlet "The State of Ireland" articulated a republican vision. As "Arthur O'Conor, nephew of Lord Longueville" (100-136), his embrace of radical politics represented the defection of the Protestant gentry to the popular cause. This particularly alarmed the Ascendancy.

The leadership maintained close coordination through secret channels. Communications passed "between Lord Edward Fitzgerald, Arthur O'Conor, Dr." (101-045) and other key figures in the conspiracy.

The organization spread rapidly across the country. "Towards the end of February, Arthur O'Conor, Father James" (102-169) and other leaders actively prepared for the rising. Government surveillance intensified as the conspiracy grew.

## Arrest and Imprisonment

In the crackdown preceding the Rebellion of 1798, O'Conor was among the leaders arrested. He was tried at Maidstone on charges of high treason. Leading Whig politicians, including Charles James Fox, testified to his character. Though acquitted of treason, O'Conor remained in custody for an extended period.

## French Service Under Napoleon

After his release, O'Conor followed other exiled United Irishmen into French service. McGee records that "Buonaparte promoted Arthur O'Conor" (112-022) in the French army, where he eventually attained the rank of general. The French revolutionary government valued the Irish exiles for their knowledge of British affairs.

O'Conor, along with "Thomas Addis Emmet and Arthur O'Conor remained" (107-126) in France, sustaining the Irish revolutionary interest abroad. He married Elisa de Condorcet, daughter of the Marquis de Condorcet, the renowned French philosopher. This marriage cemented his permanent connection to French society.

## Legacy

Arthur O'Conor lived until 1852, surviving most of his United Irishmen contemporaries by decades. His long life in French exile, high military rank under Napoleon, and political writings made him a towering figure in Irish nationalist memory.

His career illustrated the tragedy of the United Irishmen generation. These were talented men who might have led Ireland's government but instead ended their lives as exiles or martyrs. O'Conor's republican writings, surviving into the age of Young Ireland, bridged the revolutionary generations of 1798 and 1848.
