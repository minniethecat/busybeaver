## Overview

Sir Anthony St. Leger (c. 1496–1559) was one of the most durable English administrators in Tudor Ireland, serving as Lord Deputy under three successive monarchs: Henry VIII, Edward VI, and Mary I. His tenure encompassed the constitutional revolution of 1541, when the Irish Parliament declared Henry King of Ireland, and the first systematic efforts to impose the Protestant Reformation.

## Appointment and Early Commission

Upon the disgrace of Lord Leonard Gray in 1540, St. Leger was appointed Lord Deputy. He arrived in Ireland with unusual prior experience: he had previously led the commission of 1537, which was charged with surveying lands subject to the crown, confirming or cancelling titles, and reforming the courts of law. (050-006, 050-007, 050-008, 050-009, 050-010)

In that earlier role, St. Leger had at his disposal a guard of 340 men, and the Deputy and Council were instructed to obey his mandates as if they came from the King in person. The commissioners were also empowered to enter both Houses of Parliament as King's Counsel. (050-011, 050-012, 050-013, 050-014)

When he was named Deputy, St. Leger had not yet arrived in Ireland to replace Lord Gray. An expedition marched out of the Pale under the Lords of the Council to prevent a feared congress of Irish chiefs at the Abbey of Fore in West-Meath, a mission that found no such assembly. (050-079, 050-080, 050-081, 050-082)

## The Parliament of 1541

St. Leger's most enduring constitutional achievement was the Parliament of 1541, which proclaimed Henry VIII King of Ireland, transforming the island's constitutional status from a lordship under the Pope to a kingdom under the English crown.

The assembly was remarkable in its composition: two archbishops and twelve bishops assisted at the solemn mass in St. Patrick's, and Gaelic lords attended alongside the Anglo-Irish nobility.

> "The like thereof," wrote St. Leger to Henry, "has not been seen here these many years." (050-302)

Sir Thomas Cusack was elected Speaker of the Commons, and the substance of both opening speeches was declared in the Irish language to the Gaelic lords by the Earl of Ormond, "greatly to their contentation." (050-304, 050-305, 050-306, 050-307, 050-308, 050-309, 050-310)

## The Reformation under Edward VI

In February 1551, under the Protestant Edward VI, St. Leger received instructions to introduce the English-language liturgy. He was commanded to cause the Scriptures translated into English, and the Liturgy and Prayers of the Church also translated into English, to be read in all the churches of Ireland. (052-091, 052-092, 052-093, 052-094)

To implement these orders, St. Leger summoned a convocation of the archbishops, bishops, and clergy to Dublin on the 1st of March, 1551. This was the first formal confrontation between the defenders of the old and the new religion in Ireland. The Catholic party was led by Archbishop Dowdal of Armagh, and the reformers by Archbishop Browne. (052-095, 052-096, 052-097, 052-098, 052-099, 052-100)

## Service under Mary

The accession of the Catholic Queen Mary in 1553 brought a dramatic reversal. Archbishop Dowdal was called from exile and the primacy restored to Armagh. (053-025, 053-026)

St. Leger, his ancient antagonist, conformed to the new Court fashion and was sent back to Ireland as Deputy, this time charged with restoring the old religion he had spent years attempting to dismantle. (053-026, 053-027, 053-028, 053-029)

Under his administration, the married Protestant bishops, including Browne, Staples, Lancaster, and Travers, were formally deprived of their sees, while Bale and Casey of Limerick fled abroad. Married clergymen were silenced, and the children of Archbishop Browne were declared illegitimate by statute. (053-030, 053-031, 053-032, 053-033)

## Legacy

St. Leger's career illustrates the instability of Tudor religious policy in Ireland, where the same administrator was obliged to enforce Protestantism under one monarch and Catholicism under the next.

His constitutional work in the Parliament of 1541, however, had lasting effect. The designation of Ireland as a kingdom rather than a lordship fundamentally altered the legal framework of English rule and remained the constitutional basis of the connection until 1800.
