## Overview

Sir Phelim O'Neill of Kinnaird, in Tyrone, was the most considerable man of the O'Neill name tolerated in Ulster during the early 17th century. He initiated the Rebellion of 1641 by seizing Charlemont and Mountjoy forts, and served as the first commander of the northern Catholic forces during the early years of the [[Confederate War]].

## Biography

### Early Life and Education

Sir Phelim O'Neill was educated at the School of Wards established by Elizabeth I and enlarged by James I, alongside other sons of noble Irish families including the great Duke of Ormond and Murrogh, Lord Inchiquin (063-218–063-221). He was bred to the profession of the law and was a civilian by training (067-184–067-185).

### Joining the Confederate Conspiracy

In the autumn of 1641, the leaders of the northern conspiracy sought to bring Sir Phelim into their plans:

> [066-234] Sir Phelim O'Neil of Kinnaird, in Tyrone, Sir Con Magennis of Down, Colonel Hugh McMahon of Monaghan, and Dr. Heber McMahon, Administrator of Clogher. Sir Phelim O'Neil, the most considerable man of his name tolerated in Ulster, was looked upon as the greatest acquisition. (066-234–066-238)

At his castle of Kinnaird, Sir Phelim and his associates planned the rising, timed for the festival of All Hallows — the 1st of November, 1641 (066-258–066-261).

### The Rising of 1641

The northern plan called for a coordinated uprising:

> [067-018] The northern movements were to be arranged with Sir Phelim O'Neil, while McMahon, Plunkett, and Byrne, with 200 picked men, were to surprise the Castle guard. (067-018–067-020)

On the night of October 22nd, the forts of Charlemont and Mountjoy and the town of Dungannon were seized by Sir Phelim O'Neil or his lieutenants. The next day Sir Conor Magennis took Newry, and the McMahons possessed themselves of Carrickmacross and Castleblaney (067-065–067-068).

### Military Leadership

Despite his civilian background, Sir Phelim boldly assumed the title of "Lord General of the Catholic Army in Ulster" and the still more popular Gaelic title (067-188–067-190). However, his forces were poorly equipped initially:

> [067-176] Before the surprise of Charlemont and Mountjoy forts, Sir Phelim O'Neil had but a barrel or two of gunpowder; the stores of those forts, with 70 barrels taken at Newry by Magennis, and all the arms captured in the neighbouring garrisons, greatly strengthened the insurrection. (067-176–067-178)

> [067-184] Sir Phelim was a civilian, bred to the profession of the law; Rory O'Moore, also, had never seen service; and although Colonel Owen O'Neil and others had promised to join them "at fourteen days' notice," a variety of accidents prevented the arrival of any officer of distinction during the brief remainder of that year. (067-184–067-188)

### Siege of Drogheda and Aftermath

Sir Phelim marched to besiege Drogheda, which had been invested by his forces (068-093). He was joined by his impetuous kinsman at the fort of Charlemont, and remained active in the northern theatre throughout the early Confederate period (068-272–068-275, 069-035).

### Later Life and Death

McGee describes Sir Phelim O'Neil as "the first author of the war" (074-148), recognizing his role as the initiator of the 1641 Rebellion. He was later captured and executed in 1653 by the Cromwellian authorities.

## Legacy

Sir Phelim O'Neill's initiation of the 1641 Rebellion set in motion eleven years of war in Ireland. Though a civilian lawyer rather than a professional soldier, his bold seizure of the northern forts gave the Confederate movement its first military foothold. The rebellion he began ultimately led to the establishment of the [[Confederate War|Confederate Catholic Association]] at Kilkenny.
