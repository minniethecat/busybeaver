CHAPTER VI.

[081-001]THE REVOLUTIONARY WAR—CAMPAIGN OF 1689—SIEGES OF DERRY AND ENNISKILLEN.

[081-002]When Tyrconnell met the King at Cork, he gave his Majesty a plain

[081-003]account of the posture of military affairs. In Ulster,

[081-004]Lieutenant-General Richard Hamilton, at the head of 2,500 regular

[081-005]troops, was holding the rebels in check, from Charlemont to Coleraine;

[081-006]in Munster, Lieutenant-General Justin McCarthy, Lord Mountcashel, had

[081-007]taken Bandon and Castlemartyr; throughout the four provinces, the

[081-008]Catholics, to the number of fifty regiments (probably 30,000 men), had

[081-009]volunteered their services; but for all these volunteers he had only

[081-010]20,000 old arms of all kinds, not over 1,000 of which were found really

[081-011]valuable. There were besides these, regiments of horse, Tyrconnell's,

[081-012]Russell's, and Galmony's, and one of dragoons, eight small pieces of

[081-013]artillery, but neither stores in the magazines, nor cash in the chest.

[081-014]While at Cork, Tyrconnell, in return for his great exertions, was

[081-015]created a Duke, and General-in-Chief, with De Rosen as second in

[081-016]command.

[081-017]A week before James reached Dublin, Hamilton had beaten the rebels at

[081-018]Dromore, and driven them in on Coleraine, from before which he wrote

[081-019]urgently for reinforcements. On receipt of this communication, the

[081-020]Council exhibited, for the first time, those radical differences of

[081-021]opinion, amounting almost to factious opposition, which crippled all

[081-022]King James's movements at this period. One party strenuously urged that

[081-023]the King himself should march northward with such troops as could be

[081-024]spared; that his personal appearance before Derry, would immediately

[081-025]occasion the surrender of that city, and that he might in a few weeks,

[081-026]finish in person the campaign of Ulster. Another, at whose head was

[081-027]Tyrconnell, endeavoured to dissuade his Majesty from this course, but

[081-028]he at length decided in favour of the plan of Melfort and his friends.

[081-029]Accordingly, he marched out of Dublin, amid torrents of April rain, on

[081-030]the eighth of that month, intending to form a junction with Hamilton,

[081-031]at Strabane, and thence to advance to Derry. The march was a weary one

[081-032]through a country stripped bare of every sign of life, and desolate

[081-033]beyond description. A week was spent between Dublin and Omagh; at Omagh

[081-034]news of an English fleet on the Foyle caused the King to retrace his

[081-035]steps hastily to Charlemont. At Charlemont, however, intelligence of

[081-036]fresh successes gained by Hamilton and De Rosen, at Cladyford and

[081-037]Strabane, came to restore his confidence; he instantly set forward,

[081-038]despite the tempestuous weather, and the almost impassable roads, and

[081-039]on the eighteenth reached the Irish camp at Johnstown, within four or

[081-040]five miles of Derry.

[081-041]It was now four months since "the youthhood" of Derry had shut the

[081-042]Watergate against Lord Antrim's regiment, and established within their

[081-043]walls a strange sort of government, including eighteen clergymen and

[081-044]the town democracy. The military command remained with

[081-045]Lieutenant-Colonel Lundy, of Mountjoy's regiment, but the actual

[081-046]government of the town was vested, first, in "Governor" Baker, and

[081-047]afterwards in the Reverend George Walker, rector of Donaghmore, best

[081-048]known to us as _Governor_ Walker. The Town Council had despatched Mr.

[081-049]Cairnes, and subsequently Captain Hamilton, founder of the Abercorn

[081-050]peerage, to England for succour, and had openly proclaimed William and

[081-051]Mary as King and Queen. Defensive works were added, where necessary,

[081-052]and on the very day of the affair of Cladyford, 480 barrels of

[081-053]gunpowder were landed from English ships and conveyed within the walls.

[081-054]As the Royalist forces concentrated towards Derry, the chiefs of the

[081-055]Protestant Association fell back before them, each bringing to its

[081-056]garrison the contribution of his own followers. From the valley of the

[081-057]Bann, over the rugged summits of Carntogher, from the glens of Donegal,

[081-058]and the western sea coast round to Mayo, troops of the fugitives

[081-059]hurried to the strong town of the London traders, as to a city of

[081-060]refuge. Enniskillen alone, resolute in its insular situation, and in a

[081-061]courage akin to that which actuated the defenders of Derry, stood as an

[081-062]outpost of the main object of attack, and delayed the junction of the

[081-063]Royalists under Mountcashel with those under Hamilton and De Rosen.

[081-064]Coleraine was abandoned. Captain Murray, the commander of Culmore,

[081-065]forced his way at the head of 1,500 men into Derry, contrary to the

[081-066]wishes of the vacillating and suspected Lundy, and, from the moment of

[081-067]his arrival, infused his own determined spirit into all ranks of the

[081-068]inhabitants.

[081-069]Those who had advised King James to present himself in person before

[081-070]the Protestant stronghold, had not acted altogether, upon presumption.

[081-071]It is certain that there were Jacobites, even in Derry. Lundy, the

[081-072]governor, either despairing of its defence, or undecided in his

[081-073]allegiance between James and William, had opened a correspondence with

[081-074]Hamilton and De Rosen. But the true answer of the brave townsmen, when

[081-075]the King advanced too near their walls, was a cannon shot which killed

[081-076]one of his staff, and the cry of "No Surrender" thundered from the

[081-077]walls. James, awakened from his self-complacent dream by this

[081-078]unexpected reception, returned to Dublin, to open his Parliament,

[081-079]leaving General Hamilton to continue the siege. Colonel Lundy,

[081-080]distrusted, overruled, and menaced, escaped over the walls by night,

[081-081]disguised as a common labourer, and the party of Murray, Baker, Walker,

[081-082]and Cairnes, reigned supreme.

[081-083]The story of the siege of Derry—of the heroic constancy of its

[081-084]defenders—of the atrocities of De Rosen and Galmoy—the clemency of

[081-085]Maumont—the forbearance of Hamilton—the struggles for supremacy among

[081-086]its magnates—the turbulence of the townsfolk—the joyful raising of the

[081-087]siege—all these have worthily employed some of the most eloquent pens

[081-088]in our language. The relief came by the breaking of the boom across the

[081-089]harbour's mouth on the last day of July; the bombardment had commenced

[081-090]on the 21st of April; the gates had been shut on the 7th of December.

[081-091]The actual siege had lasted above three months, and the blockade about

[081-092]three weeks. The destruction of life on both sides has never been

[081-093]definitely stated. The besieged admit a loss of 4,000 men; the

[081-094]besiegers of 6,000. The want of siege guns in the Jacobite camp is

[081-095]admitted by both parties, but, nevertheless, the defence of the place

[081-096]well deserves to be celebrated, as it has been by an imperial

[081-097]historian, "as the most memorable in British annals."

[081-098]Scarcely inferior in interest and importance to the siege of Derry, was

[081-099]the spirited defence of Enniskillen. That fine old town, once the seat

[081-100]of the noble family of Maguire, is naturally dyked and moated round

[081-101]about, by the waters of Lough Erne. In December, '88, it had closed its

[081-102]gates, and barricaded its causeways to keep out a Jacobite garrison. In

[081-103]March, on Lord Galmoy's approach, all the outlying garrisons, in

[081-104]Fermanagh and Cavan, had destroyed their posts, and gathered into

[081-105]Enniskillen. The cruel and faithless Galmoy, instead of inspiring

[081-106]terror into the united garrison, only increased their determination to

[081-107]die in the breach. So strong in position and numbers did they find

[081-108]themselves, with the absolute command of the lower Lough Erne to bring

[081-109]in their supplies, that in April they sent off a detachment to the

[081-110]relief of Derry, and in the months of May and June, made several

[081-111]successful forays to Ballincarrig, Omagh, and Belturbet. In July,

[081-112]provided with a fresh supply of ammunition from the fleet intended for

[081-113]the relief of Derry, they beat up the Duke of Berwick's quarters at

[081-114]Trellick, but were repulsed with some loss. The Duke being soon after

[081-115]recalled to join De Rosen, the siege of Enniskillen was committed to

[081-116]Lord Mountcashel, under whom, as commander of the cavalry, served Count

[081-117]Anthony Hamilton, author of the witty but licentious "Memoirs of

[081-118]Grammont," and other distinguished officers. Mountcashel's whole force

[081-119]consisted of three regiments of foot, two of dragoons, and some horse;

[081-120]but he expected to be joined by Colonel Sarsfield from Sligo, and

[081-121]Berwick from Derry. The besieged had drawn four regiments of foot from

[081-122]Cavan alone, and were probably twice that number in all; and they had,

[081-123]in Colonels Wolseley and Berry, able and energetic officers. The

[081-124]Enniskilleners did not await the attack within their fortress. At

[081-125]Lisnaskea, under Berry, they repulsed the advanced guard of the

[081-126]Jacobites under Anthony Hamilton; and the same day—the day of the

[081-127]relief of Derry—their whole force were brought into action with

[081-128]Mountcashel's at Newtown-Butler. To the cry of "No Popery," Wolseley

[081-129]led them into an action, the most considerable yet fought. The raw

[081-130]southern levies on the Royalist side, were routed by the hardy

[081-131]Enniskilleners long familiar with the use of arms, and well acquainted

[081-132]with every inch of the ground; 2,000 of them were left on the field;

[081-133]400 prisoners were taken, among them dangerously, but not mortally

[081-134]wounded, was the Lieutenant-General himself.

[081-135]The month of August was a month of general rejoicing for the

[081-136]Williamites of Ulster, De Rosen and Berwick had retreated from Derry;

[081-137]Sarsfield, on his way to join Mountcashel, fell back to Sligo on

[081-138]hearing of his defeat at Newtown-Butler; Culmore, Coleraine, and

[081-139]Ballyshannon, were retaken and well supplied; fugitives returned

[081-140]triumphantly to their homes, in Cavan, Fermanagh, Tyrone, and Armagh. A

[081-141]panic created by false reports spread among his troops at Sligo,

[081-142]compelled Sarsfield to fall still further back to Athlone. Six months

[081-143]after his arrival, with the exception of the forts of Charlemont and

[081-144]Carrickfergus, King James no longer possessed a garrison in that

[081-145]province, which had been bestowed by his grandfather upon the ancestors

[081-146]of those who now unanimously rejected and resisted him.

[081-147]The fall of the gallant Dundee in the battle of Killicrankie, five days

[081-148]before the relief of Derry, freed King William from immediate anxiety

[081-149]on the side of Scotland, and enabled him to concentrate his whole

[081-150]disposable force on Ireland. On the 13th of August, an army of eighteen

[081-151]regiments of foot, and four or five of horse, under the Marshal Duke de

[081-152]Schomberg, with Count Solmes as second in command, sailed into Belfast

[081-153]Lough, and took possession of the town. On the 20th, the Marshal opened

[081-154]a fierce cannonade on Carrickfergus, defended by Colonels McCarthy More

[081-155]and Cormac O'Neil, while the fleet bombarded it from sea. After eight

[081-156]days' incessant cannonade, the garrison surrendered on honourable

[081-157]terms, and Schomberg faced southward towards Dublin. Brave, and long

[081-158]experienced, the aged Duke moved according to the cautious maxims of

[081-159]the military school in which he had been educated. Had he advanced

[081-160]rapidly on the capital, James must have fallen back, as De Rosen

[081-161]advised, on the line of the Shannon; but O'Regan, at Charlemont, and

[081-162]Berwick, at Newry, seemed to him obstacles so serious, that nearly a

[081-163]month was wasted in advancing from Belfast to Dundalk, where he

[081-164]entrenched himself in September, and went into winter quarters. Here a

[081-165]terrible dysentery broke out among his troops, said to have been

[081-166]introduced by some soldiers from Derry, and so destructive were its

[081-167]ravages, that there were hardly left healthy men enough to bury the

[081-168]dead. Several of the French Catholics under his command, also, deserted

[081-169]to James, who, from his head-quarters at Drogheda, offered every

[081-170]inducement to the deserters. Others discovered in the attempt were

[081-171]tried and hanged, and others, still suspected of similar designs, were

[081-172]marched down to Carlingford, and shipped for England. In November,

[081-173]James returned from Drogheda to Dublin, much elated that Duke

[081-174]Schomberg, whose fatal camp at Dundalk he had in vain attempted to

[081-175]raise, had shrunk from meeting him in the field.
