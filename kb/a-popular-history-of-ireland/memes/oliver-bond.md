## Overview

Oliver Bond (c. 1760–1798) was a prosperous Dublin merchant of northern origin who became a central figure in the United Irishmen conspiracy of the 1790s. His house in Bridge Street, Dublin, served as the meeting-place of the Leinster provincial executive. He was arrested there in March 1798 during the government crackdown that decapitated the movement on the eve of the rebellion, and died in prison later that year. (100-068) (102-176) (104-300)

## Early Life and Business

Bond was a merchant of "northern origin" who established himself in Dublin, becoming sufficiently prosperous to command respect in the commercial life of the capital. Like many of the United Irish leaders, he belonged to the Protestant middle class that had been energized by the Volunteer movement and the achievement of legislative independence in 1782. (100-068)

## The United Irishmen

Bond attached himself early to the United Irish movement. When the original Dublin Society, which had operated openly under the chairmanship of Simon Butler, was driven underground by government repression, Bond remained with the organization. In 1792, he was summoned alongside Butler to the bar of the House of Lords and sentenced to six months' imprisonment and a fine of 500 pounds for his role in a meeting that adopted an address critical of the corrupt constitution of Parliament. (100-068)

> In 1792, Simon Butler, and Oliver Bond — a prosperous Dublin merchant of northern origin — was summoned to the bar of the House of Lords, condemned to six months' imprisonment, and a fine of 500 pounds each. [100-068]

## The Bridge Street Conspiracy

After his release, Bond became more deeply involved in the revolutionary wing of the United Irishmen. His house in Bridge Street, Dublin, became the regular meeting-place of the Leinster provincial executive — the body that coordinated preparations for insurrection across the province. There, plans were laid for a French-assisted rising, communications were maintained with the Ulster executive, and the military organization of the United Irishmen was directed. (102-176)

On the 12th of March 1798, acting on the information of the informer Thomas Reynolds, the authorities struck. "The Leinster delegates were seized in conclave, with all their papers, at the house of Oliver Bond, in Bridge Street, Dublin." On the same day, Thomas Addis Emmet and Dr. McNevin were taken in their own houses, and William Sampson was arrested in the north of England. Of the entire Leinster executive, only Lord Edward Fitzgerald initially escaped. Wolfe Tone, receiving the news in France, noted in his journal that it was "a terrible blow." (102-176)

> The Leinster delegates were seized in conclave, with all their papers, at the house of Oliver Bond, in Bridge Street, Dublin. [102-176]

## Death in Prison

The blow dealt to the United Irishmen by the arrests of March 1798 proved fatal to their organization. The rebellion, when it did break out in May, lacked the coordination its leaders had planned. Bond himself did not survive to face trial. "Lord Edward had died of his wounds and vexation; Oliver Bond of apoplexy." He died in prison in the summer of 1798, one of the many United Irish leaders who perished in the government crackdown. (104-300)

> Lord Edward had died of his wounds and vexation; Oliver Bond of apoplexy. [104-300]

## Legacy

Oliver Bond's Bridge Street house occupies a mythic place in the history of the United Irishmen — the site where the revolutionary conspiracy reached its highest organization, and where it was most decisively broken. His death in prison, alongside the executions of the Sheares brothers and Father Quigley and the suicide of Wolfe Tone, marked the extinguishing of the original United Irish leadership. In the nationalist memory, Bond is remembered as a merchant who gave his life to the cause of Irish liberty. (102-176) (104-300)
