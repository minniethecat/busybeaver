## Overview

General Gerard Lake, 1st Viscount Lake (1744–1808), served as Commander-in-Chief of the British forces in Ireland during the critical summer of 1798, succeeding Sir Ralph Abercromby. Under his command, the military suppression of the Rebellion of 1798 was carried out with uncompromising rigour, and the final engagements of the insurrection — including the encirclement of Wexford — were concluded. His tenure exemplified the shift from Abercromby's restraint to a policy of maximum military force.

## Appointment to Ireland

Lord Lake was appointed to the Irish command as the temporary successor to Sir Ralph Abercromby, who had resigned after clashing with the Castle administration over the conduct of the military. Whereas Abercromby had condemned the army's indiscipline and refused to employ provocative tactics against the population, Lake had no such reservations.

> His temporary successor, Lord Lake, was troubled with no such scruples as the gallant old Scotsman. (102-166)

The appointment was part of a broader concentration of military authority. In May 1798, martial law was rigorously enforced by General Johnstone in the South, General Hutchinson in the West, and Lord Lake in the North. Across the country, two hundred thousand pikes and pike-heads were said to have been surrendered or seized. (102-061) (102-060)

## The Campaign of June 1798

The outbreak of the rebellion in Wexford at the end of May 1798 transformed Lake's role from regional commander to overall director of the counter-insurgency. The insurrection in Wexford proved far more formidable than the risings in the north, and Lake was forced to concentrate his forces for a major campaign in the south-east.

By the middle of June, regulars and militia within fifty miles of the insurgent positions were concentrated by orders of Lord Lake, the Commander-in-Chief. General Dundas from Wicklow was to join General Loftus at Carnew on the 18th of June; General Needham was ordered to approach from the Arklow direction; and Lake himself moved to take direct command of the converging columns. (103-330) (103-331)

Lake's plan called for a multi-pronged encirclement of the Wexford insurgent camp at Vinegar Hill. However, the plan did not fully account for the speed and determination of the insurgent forces under Father John Murphy and other leaders.

## The Encirclement of Wexford

The execution of Lake's plan encountered difficulties. General Needham's delayed arrival at his appointed position left a gap in the cordon through which the main insurgent force escaped:

> The other departure from Lord Lake's plan was on the side of General Needham, who was ordered to approach from the Arklow direction, but whose advance was so slow that the main body of the insurgents escaped from the trap that had been set for them. (103-355)

Despite this partial escape, the insurgent position was broken. Moore advanced and eventually halted within sight of the walls of Wexford. The town itself, on the 22nd of June, was abandoned to Lord Lake, who established himself in the house of Governor Keogh — the owner being then in the field with the insurgents. (103-386) (103-387)

## Aftermath

Following the fall of Wexford, Lord Lake swept up the camps of the Wexford insurgents, dispersing the remaining bodies of armed men. Together with General Hutchinson, Lake had advanced from the north while Moore operated in the south, and the organized phase of the rebellion in Wexford was brought to an end. (104-029) (104-175)

Lake's command in Ireland was brief but decisive. He was subsequently raised to the peerage as Viscount Lake for his services. His later career included service in India, where he commanded British forces in the Second Anglo-Maratha War.

## Legacy

Lord Lake's command during the 1798 Rebellion represents the military dimension of the British government's response to the United Irishmen insurrection. While his predecessor Abercromby had attempted to restrain the excesses of the military, Lake's tenure marked the full application of coercive force. The efficiency — and the cost in lives — of his campaign in Wexford became a lasting element of the contested memory of 1798.
