## Overview

Charles I (1600–1649) was King of England, Scotland, and Ireland from 1625 until his execution in 1649. His reign in Ireland was marked by the great Insurrection of 1641, the ensuing Catholic Confederation, and a protracted civil war that ended only with the Cromwellian conquest. Charles's relationship with his Irish subjects was shaped by his financial needs for the English Civil War, leading to intricate and often duplicitous negotiations with both the Confederate Catholics and his Protestant administrators in Dublin.

## Early Reign and Irish Policy

Charles inherited the Irish kingdom from his father James I, along with the unresolved tensions created by the extensive plantations of Ulster and elsewhere. The Irish Parliament, known as the 14th, 15th and 16th of Charles I, met during the early years of his reign amid growing sectarian anxieties. (066-040) The King's financial difficulties, especially his conflict with the English Parliament, made him dependent on Irish revenues and on the loyalty of his Lord Deputy in Ireland, Thomas Wentworth, Earl of Strafford. (065-004)

## The Insurrection of 1641 and the Confederate Wars

In October 1641, a widespread rebellion broke out in Ulster, led by Sir Phelim O'Neil and other native Irish leaders. The rising, which began as an attempt to seize Dublin Castle and other strongholds, quickly escalated into a general insurrection. The insurgents claimed to act in the King's name, producing a forged commission purporting to bear Charles's authority.

As the rebellion spread across the island, the Anglo-Irish nobility and Catholic clergy convened at Kilkenny in 1642 to form the Catholic Confederation — a provisional government pledged to restore the rights of the Catholic Church and the liberties of the Irish Parliament, while professing loyalty to the Crown. Charles, engaged in his own struggle with the English Parliament, could spare few resources for Ireland and relied on the Marquis of Ormond, his Lord Lieutenant, to manage the deteriorating situation.

## Glamorgan's Embassy and Royal Duplicity

> Charles I., whose many good qualities as a man and a ruler are cheerfully admitted on all hands, was yet utterly deficient in downright good faith; that duplicity was his besetting sin; and that Glamorgan's embassy is one, but only one, of the strongest evidences of that ingrained duplicity. (071-016 to 071-020)

The most controversial episode of Charles's Irish policy came in 1645, when he despatched the Earl of Glamorgan — a Catholic nobleman and son-in-law to the Earl of Thomond — as a secret ambassador to the Confederates at Kilkenny. Glamorgan arrived at Dublin about the 1st of August and, after an interview with Ormond, proceeded to Kilkenny, where on the 28th of the month preliminary articles were agreed upon and signed on behalf of the King with Lords Mountgarrett and Muskerry representing the Confederates. (071-023 to 071-027)

The negotiations proved immensely controversial. Ormond, informed of the secret concessions Glamorgan had offered, contrived to detain the negotiators ten weeks in discussions before returning a much modified treaty to Kilkenny on the 12th of November. (071-030 to 071-032) Charles's willingness to promise far-reaching religious concessions to the Confederates while disavowing them publicly confirmed the impression of royal duplicity that fatally undermined his standing with all parties.

## Execution and Aftermath

The defeat of the royalist cause in England sealed Charles's fate. In January 1649, he was tried and executed by the parliamentary regime. News of the execution of Charles I reached Ireland shortly after the signing of the treaty between Ormond and the Confederates, transforming the political landscape entirely. (072-187) The Confederate Catholics, who had always professed loyalty to the Stuart Crown, now found themselves confronting a Puritan republic determined to impose its authority on Ireland by force.

> The intervening years between the death of Charles I. and the death of Cromwell saw the wholesale confiscation of Catholic estates, the transplantation of the native population to Connacht, and the consolidation of Protestant ascendancy. (075-140)

## Legacy

The death of Charles I and the subsequent Cromwellian conquest of Ireland marked one of the great turning points in Irish history. His personal tragedy — a monarch of cultivated taste and genuine piety, undone by his own want of candour — was felt with particular bitterness in Ireland, where his secret diplomacy had raised hopes only to dash them. The Stuart cause, however, survived his death: his son was proclaimed as Charles II at Cork and Youghal by Prince Rupert and the loyalist forces, inaugurating another chapter in Ireland's prolonged agony. (072-189)
