CHAPTER IV.

[079-001]ACCESSION OF JAMES II.—TYRCONNELL'S ADMINISTRATION.

[079-002]From the accession of King James till his final flight from Ireland, in

[079-003]July, 1690, there elapsed an interval of five years and five months; a

[079-004]period fraught with consequences of the highest interest to this

[079-005]history. The new King was, on his accession, in his fifty-second year;

[079-006]he had served, as Duke of York, with credit both by land and sea, was

[079-007]an avowed Catholic, and married to a Catholic princess, the beautiful

[079-008]and unfortunate Mary of Modena.

[079-009]Within a month from the proclamation of the King, Ormond quitted the

[079-010]government for the last time, leaving Primate Boyle, and Lord Granard,

[079-011]as Justices. In January, 1686, Lord Clarendon, son of the historian,

[079-012]assumed the government, in which he continued, till the 16th of March,

[079-013]1687. The day following the national anniversary, Colonel Richard

[079-014]Talbot, Earl of Tyrconnell, a Catholic, and the former agent for the

[079-015]Catholics, was installed as Lord Deputy. Other events, connecting these

[079-016]with each other, had filled with astonishment and apprehension the

[079-017]ascendancy party.

[079-018]James proceeded openly with what he hoped to make a counter-reformation

[079-019]of England, and to accomplish which he relied on France on the one

[079-020]hand, and Ireland on the other. In both cases he alarmed the fears and

[079-021]wounded the pride of England; but when he proceeded from one illegality

[079-022]to another, when he began to exercise a dispensing power above the

[079-023]laws—to instruct the judges, to menace the parliament, and imprison the

[079-024]bishops—the nobility, the commons, and the army gradually combined

[079-025]against him, and at last invited over the Prince of Orange, as the most

[079-026]capable vindicator of their outraged constitution.

[079-027]The headlong King had a representative equally rash, in Tyrconnell. He

[079-028]was a man old enough to remember well the uprising of 1641, had lived

[079-029]in intimacy with James as Duke of York, was personally brave, well

[079-030]skilled in intrigue, but vain, loud-spoken, confident, and incapable of

[079-031]a high command in military affairs. The colonelcy of an Irish regiment,

[079-032]the earldom of Tyrconnell, and a seat in the secret council or cabinet

[079-033]of the King, were honours conferred on him during the year of James's

[079-034]accession. When Clarendon was named Lord-Lieutenant at the beginning of

[079-035]1686, Tyrconnell was sent over with him as Lieutenant-General of the

[079-036]army. At his instigation, a proclamation was issued, that "all classes"

[079-037]of his Majesty's subjects might be allowed to serve in the army; and

[079-038]another, that all arms hitherto given out should be deposited, for

[079-039]greater security, at one of the King's stores provided for the purpose

[079-040]in each town or county. Thus that exclusively Protestant militia, which

[079-041]for twenty years had executed the Act of Settlement and the Act of

[079-042]Uniformity in every quarter of the kingdom, found themselves suddenly

[079-043]disarmed, and a new Catholic army rising on their ruins. The numbers

[079-044]disbanded are nowhere stated; they probably amounted to 10,000 or

[079-045]15,000 men and very naturally they became warm partisans of the

[079-046]Williamite revolution. The recriminations which arose between the new

[079-047]and the old militia were not confined to the nicknames, Whig and Tory,

[079-048]or to the bandying of sarcasms on each others' origin; swords were not

[079-049]unfrequently drawn, and muskets discharged, even in the streets of

[079-050]Dublin, under the very walls of the Castle.

[079-051]Through Tyrconnell's influence, a similar revolution had been wrought

[079-052]in the exclusive character of the courts of justice, and the

[079-053]corporations of towns, to that which remodelled the militia. Rice,

[079-054]Daly, and Nugent, were elevated to the bench during Lord Clarendon's

[079-055]time; the Corporation of Dublin having refused to surrender their

[079-056]exclusive charter, were summarily rejected by a _quo warranto_, issued

[079-057]in the exchequer; other towns were similarly treated, or induced to

[079-058]make surrender, and a new series of charters at once granted by James,

[079-059]entitling Catholics to the freedom of the boroughs, and the highest

[079-060]municipal offices. And now, for the first time in that generation,

[079-061]Catholic mayors and sheriffs, escorted by Catholic troops as guards of

[079-062]honour, were seen marching in open day to their own places of worship,

[079-063]to the dismay and astonishment of the ascendancy party. Not that all

[079-064]Protestants were excluded either from town councils, the militia, or

[079-065]the bench, but those only were elected or appointed who concurred in

[079-066]the new arrangements, and were, therefore, pretty certain to forfeit

[079-067]the confidence of their co-religionists in proportion as they deserved

[079-068]that of the Deputy. Topham and Coghill, Masters in Chancery, were

[079-069]deprived of their offices, and the Protestant Chancellor was

[079-070]arbitrarily removed to make way for Baron Rice, a Catholic. The

[079-071]exclusive character of Trinity College was next assailed, and though

[079-072]James did not venture to revoke the charter of Elizabeth, establishing

[079-073]communion with the Church of England as the test of fellowship, the

[079-074]internal administration was in several particulars interfered with, its

[079-075]plate was seized in the King's name under plea of being public

[079-076]property, and the annual parliamentary grant of 388 pounds was

[079-077]discontinued. These arbitrary acts filled the more judicious Catholics

[079-078]with apprehension, but gained the loud applause of the unreasoning

[079-079]multitude. Dr. Macguire, the successor of the martyred Plunkett, who

[079-080]felt in Ulster the rising tide of resistance, was among the signers of

[079-081]a memorial to the King, dutifully remonstrating against the violent

[079-082]proceedings of his Deputy. From Rome also, disapprobation was more than

[079-083]once expressed, but all without avail; neither James nor Talbot could

[079-084]be brought to reason. The Protestants of the eastern and southern towns

[079-085]and counties who could contrive to quit their homes, did so; hundreds

[079-086]fled to Holland to return in the ranks of the Prince of Orange;

[079-087]thousands fled to England, bringing with them their tale of oppression,

[079-088]embellished with all the bitter exaggeration of exiles; ten thousand

[079-089]removed from Leinster into Ulster, soon to recross the Boyne, under

[079-090]very different auspices. Very soon a close correspondence was

[079-091]established between the fugitives in Holland, England, and Ulster, and

[079-092]a powerful lever was thus placed in the hands of the Prince of Orange,

[079-093]to work the downfall of his uncle and father-in-law. But the best

[079-094]allies of William were, after all, the folly and fatuity of James. The

[079-095]importation of Irish troops, by entire battalions, gave the last and

[079-096]sorest wound to the national pride of England, and still further

[079-097]exasperated the hatred and contempt which his majesty's English

[079-098]regiments had begun to feel for their royal master.

[079-100]Tyrconnell, during the eventful summer months when the revolution was

[079-101]ripening both in Holland and England, had taken, unknown even to James,

[079-102]a step of the gravest importance. To him the first intelligence of the

[079-103]preparations of William were carried by a ship from Amsterdam, and by

[079-104]him they were communicated to the infatuated King, who had laughed at

[079-105]them as too absurd for serious consideration. But the Irish ruler,

[079-106]fully believing his informants, and never deficient in audacity, had at

[079-107]once entered into a secret treaty with Louis XIV. to put Ireland under

[079-108]the protection of France, in the event of the Prince of Orange

[079-109]succeeding to the British throne. No proposition could more entirely

[079-110]suit the exigencies of Louis, of whom William was by far the ablest and

[079-111]most relentless enemy. The correspondence which has come to light in

[079-112]recent times, shows the importance which he attached to Tyrconnell's

[079-113]proposition—an importance still further enhanced by the direct but

[079-114]unsuccessful overture made to the earl by William himself, on landing

[079-115]in England, and before embarking in the actual invasion of Ireland.

[079-116]William Henry, Prince of Orange, now about to enter on the scene, was

[079-117]in 1688 in the thirty-seventh year of his age. Fearless of danger,

[079-118]patient, silent, impervious to his enemies, rather a soldier than a

[079-119]statesman, indifferent in religion, and personally adverse to

[079-120]persecution for conscience' sake, his great and almost his only public

[079-121]passion was the humiliation of France through the instrumentality of a

[079-122]European coalition. As an anti-Gallican, as the representative of the

[079-123]most illustrious Protestant family in Europe, as allied by blood and

[079-124]marriage to their kings, he was a very fit and proper chief for the

[079-125]English revolutionists; but for the two former of these reasons he was

[079-126]just as naturally antipathetic to the Catholic and Celtic majority of

[079-127]the Irish. His designs had been long gradually maturing, when James's

[079-128]incredible imprudence hastened his movements. Twenty-four ships of war

[079-129]were assembled at Helvoetsluys; 7,000 sailors were put on board; all

[079-130]the veterans of the Netherlands were encamped at Nimeguen, where 6,000

[079-131]recruits were added to their numbers. On the 5th of November, the

[079-132]anniversary of the gunpowder plot, "the Deliverer," as he was fondly

[079-133]called in England, landed at Torbay; on the 25th of December, James,

[079-134]deserted by his nobles, his army, and even his own unnatural children,

[079-135]arrived, a fugitive and a suppliant, at the court of France.

[079-136]A few Irish incidents of this critical moment deserve mention. The

[079-137]mania against everything Irish took in England forms the most ludicrous

[079-138]and absurd. Wharton's doggerel refrain of Lillibullero, was heard in

[079-139]every circle outside the court; all London, lighted with torches, and

[079-140]marshalled under arms, awaited during the memorable "Irish night" the

[079-141]advent of the terrible and detested regiments brought over by

[079-142]Tyrconnell; some companies of these troops quartered in the country

[079-143]were fallen upon by ten times their numbers, and cut to pieces. Others,

[079-144]fighting and inquiring their way, forced a passage to Chester or

[079-145]Bristol, and obtained a passage home. They passed at sea, or

[079-146]encountered on the landing-places, multitudes of the Protestant Irish,

[079-147]men, women and children, flying in exactly the opposite direction.

[079-148]Tyrconnell was known to meditate the repeal of the Act of Settlement;

[079-149]the general rumour of a Protestant massacre fixed for the 9th of

[079-150]December, originated no one knew how, was spread about no one knew by

[079-151]whom. In vain the Lord Deputy tried to stay the panic—his assurance of

[079-152]protection, and the still better evidence of their own experience,

[079-153]which proved the Irish Catholics incapable of such a project, could not

[079-154]allay their terrors. They rushed into England by every port, and

[079-155]inflamed still more the hostility which already prevailed against King

[079-156]James.

[079-157]In Ulster, David Cairnes of Knockmany, the Rev. John Kelso of

[079-158]Enniskillen, a Presbyterian, and Rev. George Walker of Donaghmore, an

[079-159]Anglican minister, were active instruments of the Prince of Orange. On

[079-160]the 7th of December the gates of Derry were shut by "the youthhood"

[079-161]against the Earl of Antrim and his Highlanders. Enniskillen was seized

[079-162]by a similar impulse of the popular will, and an association was

[079-163]quickly formed throughout Ulster in imitation of the English

[079-164]association which had invited over William, under the auspices of Lord

[079-165]Blaney, Sir Arthur Rawdon, Sir Clotworthy Skeffington, and others, "for

[079-166]the maintenance of the Protestant religion and the dependency of

[079-167]Ireland upon England." By these associates, Sligo, Coleraine, and the

[079-168]fort of Culmore, at the mouth of the Foyle, were seized for King

[079-169]William; while the Town Council of Derry, in order to gain time,

[079-170]despatched one ambassador with one set of instructions to Tyrconnell,

[079-171]and another, with a very different set, to "the Committee for Irish

[079-172]Affairs," which sat at Whitehall, under the presidency of the Earl of

[079-173]Shrewsbury.
