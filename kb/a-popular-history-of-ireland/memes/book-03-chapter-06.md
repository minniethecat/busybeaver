CHAPTER VI.

[022-001]FOREIGN RELATIONS OF THE IRISH PREVIOUS TO THE ANGLO-NORMAN INVASION.

[022-002]The relations of the Irish with other nations, notwithstanding the

[022-003]injurious effects of their War of Succession on national unity and

[022-004]reputation, present several points of interest. After the defeat of

[022-005]Magnus Barefoot, we may drop the Baltic countries out of the map of the

[022-006]relations of Ireland. Commencing, therefore, at the north of the

[022-007]neighbouring island—which, in its entirety, they sometimes called

[022-008]_Inismore_—the most intimate and friendly intercourse was always upheld

[022-009]with the kingdom of Scotland. Bound together by early ecclesiastical

[022-010]and bardic ties, confronting together for so many generations a common

[022-011]enemy, those two countries were destined never to know an international

[022-012]quarrel. About the middle of the ninth century (A.D. 843), when the

[022-013]Scoto-Irish in Caledonia had completely subdued the Picts and other

[022-014]ancient tribes, the first national dynasty was founded by Kenneth

[022-015]McAlpine. The constitution given by this Prince to the whole country

[022-016]seems to have been a close copy of the Irish—it embraced the laws of

[022-017]Tanistry and succession, and the whole Brehon code, as administered in

[022-018]the parent state. The line of Kenneth may be said to close with Donald

[022-019]Bane, brother of Malcolm III., who died in 1094, and not only his

[022-020]dynasty but his system ended with that century. Edgar, Alexander I.,

[022-021]and David I., all sons of Malcolm III., were educated in England among

[022-022]the victorious Normans, and in the first third of the twelfth century,

[022-023]devoted themselves with the inauspicious aid of Norman allies, to the

[022-024]introduction of Saxon settlers and the feudal system, first into the

[022-025]lowlands, and subsequently into Moray-shire. This innovation on their

[022-026]ancient system, and confiscation of their lands, was stoutly resisted

[022-027]by the Scottish Gael. In Somerled, lord of the Isles, and ancestor of

[022-028]the Macdonalds, they found a powerful leader, and Somerled found Irish

[022-029]allies always ready to assist him, in a cause which appealed to all

[022-030]their national prejudices. In the year 1134, he led a strong force of

[022-031]Irish and Islesmen to the assistance of the Gaelic insurgents, but was

[022-032]defeated and slain, near Renfrew, by the royal troops, under the

[022-033]command of the Steward of Scotland. During the reigns of William the

[022-034]Lion, Alexander II., and Alexander III., the war of systems raged with

[022-035]all its fierceness, and in nearly all the great encounters Irish

[022-036]auxiliaries, as was to be expected, were found on the side of the

[022-037]Gaelic race and Gaelic rights. Nor did this contest ever wholly cease

[022-038]in Scotland, until the last hopes of the Stuart line were extinguished

[022-039]on the fatal field of Culloden, where Irish captains formed the battle,

[022-040]and Irish blood flowed freely, intermingled with the kindred blood of

[022-041]Highlanders and Islesmen.

[022-042]The adoption of Norman usages, laws, and tactics, by the Scottish

[022-043]dynasties of the twelfth and succeeding centuries, did not permanently

[022-044]affect the national relations of Ireland and Scotland. It was otherwise

[022-045]with regard to England. We have every reason to believe—we have the

[022-046]indirect testimony of every writer from Bede to Malmsbury—that the

[022-047]intercourse between the Irish and Saxons, after the first hostility

[022-048]engendered by the cruel treatment of the Britons had worn away, became

[022-049]of the most friendly character. The "Irish" who fought at Brunanburgh

[022-050]against Saxon freedom were evidently the natural allies of the

[022-051]Northmen, the Dano-Irish of Dublin, and the southern seaports. The

[022-052]commerce of intelligence between the islands was long maintained; the

[022-053]royalty of Saxon England had more than once, in times of domestic

[022-054]revolution, found a safe and desired retreat in the western island. The

[022-055]fair Elgiva and the gallant Harold had crossed the western waves in

[022-056]their hour of need. The fame of Edward the Confessor took such deep

[022-057]hold on the Irish mind that, three centuries after his death, his

[022-058]banner was unfurled and the royal leopards laid aside to facilitate the

[022-059]march of an English King, through the fastnesses of Leinster. The

[022-060]Irish, therefore, were not likely to look upon the establishment of a

[022-061]Norman dynasty, in lieu of the old Saxon line, as a matter of

[022-062]indifference. They felt that the Norman was but a Dane disguised in

[022-063]armour. It was true he carried the cross upon his banner, and claimed

[022-064]the benediction of the successor of St. Peter; true also he spoke the

[022-065]speech of France, and claimed a French paternity; but the lust for

[022-066]dominion, the iron self-will, the wily devices of strategy, bespoke the

[022-067]Norman of the twelfth, the lineal descendant of the Dane of the tenth

[022-068]century. When, therefore, tidings reached Ireland of the battle of

[022-069]Hastings and the death of Harold, both the apprehensions and the

[022-070]sympathies of the country were deeply excited. Intelligence of the

[022-071]coronation of William the Conqueror quickly followed, and emphatically

[022-072]announced to the Irish the presence of new neighbours, new dangers, and

[022-073]new duties.

[022-074]The spirit with which our ancestors acted towards the defeated Saxons,

[022-075]whatever we may think of its wisdom, was, at least, respectable for

[022-076]decision and boldness. Godwin, Edmund, and Magnus, sons of Harold, had

[022-077]little difficulty in raising in Ireland a numerous force to co-operate

[022-078]with the Earls Edwin and Morcar, who still upheld the Saxon banner.

[022-079]With this force, wafted over in sixty-six vessels, they entered the

[022-080]Avon, and besieged Bristol, then the second commercial city of the

[022-081]kingdom. But Bristol held out, and the Saxon Earls had fallen back into

[022-082]Northumberland, so the sons of Harold ran down the coast, and tried

[022-083]their luck in Somersetshire with a better prospect. Devonshire and

[022-084]Dorsetshire favoured their cause; the old Britons of Cornwall swelled

[022-085]their ranks, and the rising spread like flame over the west. Eadnoth, a

[022-086]renegade Saxon, formerly Harold's Master of Horse, despatched by

[022-087]William against Harold's sons, was defeated and slain. Doubling the

[022-088]Land's End, the victorious force entered the Tamar, and overran South

[022-089]Devon. The united garrisons of London, Winchester, and Salisbury, were

[022-090]sent against them, under the command of the martial Bishop of

[022-091]Coutances; while a second force advanced along the Tamar, under Brian,

[022-092]heir of the Earl of Brittany, who routed them with a loss of 2,000 men,

[022-093]English, Welsh, and Irish. The sons of Harold retreated to their

[022-094]vessels with all their booty, and returned again into Ireland, where

[022-095]they vanish from history. Such, in the vale of Tamar, was the first

[022-096]collision of the Irish and Normans, and as the race of Rollo never

[022-097]forgot an enemy, nor forewent a revenge, we may well believe that, even

[022-098]thus early, the invasion of Ireland was decided upon. Meredith Hanmer

[022-099]relates in his Chronicle that William Rufus, standing on a high rock,

[022-100]and looking towards Ireland said: "I will bring hither my ships, and

[022-101]pass over and conquer that land;" and on these words of the son of the

[022-102]Conqueror being repeated to Murkertach O'Brien, he replied: "Hath the

[022-103]King in his great threatening said _if it please God?_" and when

[022-104]answered "No;" "Then," said the Irish monarch, "I fear him not, since

[022-105]he putteth his trust in man and not in God."

[022-107]Ireland, however, was destined to be reached through Wales, and along

[022-108]that mountain coast we early find Norman castles and Norman ships. It

[022-109]was the special ambition of William Rufus to add the principality to

[022-110]the conquests of his father, and the active sympathy of the Welsh with

[022-111]the Saxons on their inland border gave him pretexts enough. A bitter

[022-112]feud between North and South Wales hastened an invasion, in which

[022-113]Robert Fitz-Aymon and his companions played, by anticipation, the parts

[022-114]of Strongbow and Fitz-Stephen, in the invasion of Ireland.

[022-115]The struggle, commenced under them, was protracted through the reign of

[022-116]Rufus, who led an army in person (A.D. 1095) against the Welsh, but

[022-117]with little gain and less glory. As an after thought he adopted the

[022-118]device of his father, (followed, too, in Ireland by Henry II.,) of

[022-119]partitioning the country among the most enterprising nobles, gravely

[022-120]accepting their homage in advance of possession, and authorizing them

[022-121]to maintain troops at their own charges, for making good his grant of

[022-122]what never belonged to him. Robert Fitz-Aymon did homage for Glamorgan,

[022-123]Bernard Newmarch for Brecknock, Roger de Montgomery for Cardigan, and

[022-124]Gilbert de Clare for Pembroke: the best portions of North Wales were

[022-125]partitioned between the Mortimers, Latimers, De Lacys, Fitz-Alans, and

[022-126]Montgomerys. Rhys, Prince of Cambria, with many of his nobles, fell in

[022-127]battle defending bravely his native hills; but Griffith, son of Rhys,

[022-128]escaped into Ireland, from which he returned some twenty years later,

[022-129]and recovered by arms and policy a large share of his ancestral

[022-130]dominions. In the reign of Henry I. (A.D. 1110), a host of Flemings,

[022-131]driven from their own country by an inundation of the sea, were planted

[022-132]upon the Welsh marches, from which they soon swarmed into all the

[022-133]Cambrian glens and glades. The industry and economy of this new people,

[022-134]in peaceful times, seemed almost inconsistent with their stubborn

[022-135]bravery in battle; but they demonstrated to the Welsh, and afterwards

[022-136]to the Irish, that they could handle the halbert as well as throw the

[022-137]shuttle; that men of trade may on occasion prove themselves capable men

[022-138]of war.

[022-139]The Norman Kings of England were not insensible to the fact that the

[022-140]Cymric element in Wales, the Saxon element in England, and the Gaelic

[022-141]element in Scotland, were all more agreeable to the Irish than the race

[022-142]of Rollo and William. They were not ignorant that Ireland was a refuge

[022-143]for their victims and a recruiting ground for their enemies. They knew,

[022-144]furthermore, that most of the strong points on the Irish coast, from

[022-145]the Shannon to the Liffey, were possessed by Christian Northmen kindred

[022-146]to themselves. They knew that the land was divided within itself,

[022-147]weakened by a long war of succession; groaning under the ambition of

[022-148]five competitors for the sovereignty; and suffering in reputation

[022-149]abroad under the invectives of Saint Bernard, and the displeasure of

[022-150]Rome. More tempting materials for intrigue, or fairer opportunities of

[022-151]aggrandizement, nowhere presented themselves, and it was less want of

[022-152]will than of leisure from other and nearer contests, which deferred

[022-153]this new invasion for a century after the battle of Hastings.

[022-154]While that century was passing over their heads, an occasional

[022-155]intercourse, not without its pleasing incidents, was maintained between

[022-156]the races. In the first year of the twelfth, Arnulph de Montgomery,

[022-157]Earl of Chester, obtained a daughter of Murkertach O'Brien in marriage;

[022-158]the proxy on the occasion being Gerald, son of the Constable of

[022-159]Windsor, and ancestor of the Geraldines. Murkertach, according to

[022-160]Malmsbury, maintained a close correspondence with Henry I., for whose

[022-161]advice he professed great deference. He was accused of aiding the

[022-162]rebellion of the Montgomerys against that Prince; and if at one time he

[022-163]did so, seems to have abandoned their alliance, when threatened with

[022-164]reprisals on the Irish engaged in peaceful commerce with England. The

[022-165]argument used on this occasion seems to be embodied in the question of

[022-166]Malmsbury—and has since become familiar—"What would Ireland do," says

[022-167]the old historian, "if the merchandize of England were not carried to

[022-168]her shores?"

[022-169]The estimation in which the Irish Princes were held in the century

[022-170]preceding the invasion, at the Norman Court, may be seen in the style

[022-171]of Lanfranc and Anselm, when addressing the former King Thorlogh, and

[022-172]the latter King Murkertach O'Brien. The first generation of the

[022-173]conquerors had passed away before the second of these epistles was

[022-174]written. In the first, the address runs—"Lanfrancus, a sinner, and the

[022-175]unworthy Bishop of the Holy Church of Dover, to the illustrious

[022-176]Terdelvacus, King of Ireland, blessing," &c., &c.; and the epistle of

[022-177]Anselm is addressed—"To Muriardachus, by the grace of God, glorious

[022-178]King of Ireland, Anselm, servant of the Church of Canterbury, greeting

[022-179]health and salvation," &c., &c. This was the tone of the highest

[022-180]ecclesiastics in England towards the ruler of Ireland, in the reigns of

[022-181]William I. and Henry I., and equally obsequious were the replies of the

[022-182]Irish Princes.

[022-183]After the death of Henry I., nineteen years of civil war and anarchy

[022-184]diverted the Anglo-Normans from all other objects. In the year 1154,

[022-185]however, Henry of Anjou succeeded to the throne, on which he was

[022-186]destined to act so important a part. He was born in Anjou in the year

[022-187]1133, and married at eighteen the divorced wife of the King of France.

[022-188]Uniting her vast dominions to his own patrimony, he became the lord of

[022-189]a larger part of France than was possessed by the titular king. In his

[022-190]twenty-first year he began to reign in England, and in his thirty-fifth

[022-191]he received the fugitive Dermid of Leinster, in some camp or castle of

[022-192]Aquitaine, and took that outlaw, by his own act, under his protection.

[022-193]The centenary of the victory of Hastings had just gone by, and it

[022-194]needed only this additional agent to induce him to put into execution a

[022-195]plan which he must have formed in the first months of his reign, since

[022-196]the Bull he had procured from Pope Adrian, bears the date of that

[022-197]year—1154. The return from exile, and martyrdom of Beckett, disarranged

[022-198]and delayed the projects of the English King; nor was he able to lead

[022-199]an expedition into Ireland until four years after his reception of the

[022-200]Leinster fugitive in France.

[022-201]Throughout the rest of Christendom—if we except Rome—the name of

[022-202]Ireland was comparatively little known. The commerce of Dublin,

[022-203]Limerick, and Galway, especially in the article of wine, which was

[022-204]already largely imported, may have made those ports and their merchants

[022-205]somewhat known on the coasts of France and Spain. But we have no

[022-206]statistics of Irish commerce at that early period. Along the Rhine and

[022-207]even upon the Danube, the Irish missionary and the Irish schoolmaster

[022-208]were still sometimes found. The chronicle of Ratisbon records with

[022-209]gratitude the munificence of Conor O'Brien, King of Munster, whom it

[022-210]considers the founder of the Abbey of St. Peter in that city. The

[022-211]records of the same Abbey credit its liberal founder with having sent

[022-212]large presents to the Emperor Lothaire, in aid of the second crusade

[022-213]for the recovery of the Holy Land. Some Irish adventurers joined in the

[022-214]general European hosting to the plains of Palestine, but though neither

[022-215]numerous nor distinguished enough to occupy the page of history, their

[022-216]_glibs_ and _cooluns_ did not escape the studious eye of him who sang

[022-217]Jerusalem Delivered and Regained.
