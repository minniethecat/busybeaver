CHAPTER IV.

[091-001]REIGN OF GEORGE II. (CONCLUDED)—MALONE'S LEADERSHIP.

[091-002]The Earl of Harrington, afterwards Duke of Devonshire, succeeded Lord

[091-003]Chesterfield in the government, in 1746. He was provided with a prime

[091-004]minister in the person of the new Archbishop of Armagh, Dr. George

[091-005]Stone, whose character, if he was not exceedingly calumniated by his

[091-006]cotemporaries, might be compared to that of the worst politicians of

[091-007]the worst ages of Europe. Originally, the son of the jailer of

[091-008]Winchester, he had risen by dint of talents, and audacity, to receive

[091-009]from the hands of his sovereign, the illustrious dignity of Primate of

[091-010]Ireland. But even in this exalted office, the abominable vices of his

[091-011]youth accompanied him. His house at Leixlip, was at once a tavern and a

[091-012]brothel, and crimes, which are nameless, were said to be habitual under

[091-013]his roof. "May the importation of Ganymedes into Ireland, be soon

[091-014]discontinued," was the public toast, which disguised under the

[091-015]transparent gauze of a mythological allusion, the infamies of which he

[091-016]was believed to be the patron. The prurient page of Churchill was not

[091-017]quite so scrupulous, and the readers of the satire entitled "The

[091-018]Times," will need no further key to the horrible charges commonly

[091-019]received on both sides of the channel, against Primate Stone.

[091-020]The viceroyalty of Ireland, which had become an object of ambition to

[091-021]the first men in the empire, was warmly contested by the Earl of

[091-022]Harrington and the Duke of Dorset. The former, through his Stanhope

[091-023]influence and connections, prevailed over his rival, and arrived in

[091-024]Ireland, warmly recommended by the popular Chesterfield. During his

[091-025]administration, Primate Stone, proceeding from one extreme to another,

[091-026]first put forward the dangerous theory, that all surplus revenue

[091-027]belonged of right to the crown, and might be paid over by the

[091-028]Vice-Treasurers, to his majesty's order, without authority of

[091-029]Parliament. At this period, notwithstanding the vicious system of her

[091-030]land tenures, and her recent losses by emigration, Ireland found

[091-031]herself in possession of a considerable surplus revenue.

[091-032]Like wounds and bruises in a healthy body, the sufferings and

[091-033]deprivations of the population rapidly disappeared under the appearance

[091-034]even of improvement in the government. The observant Chesterfield, who

[091-035]continued through life warmly attached to the country in which his name

[091-036]was remembered with so much affection, expresses to his friend,

[091-037]Chevenix, Bishop of Waterford, in 1751, his satisfaction at hearing

[091-038]"that Ireland improves daily, and that a spirit of industry spreads

[091-039]itself, to the great increase of trade and manufactures." This new-born

[091-040]prosperity the Primate and politicians of his school would have met by

[091-041]an annual depletion of the treasury, instead of assisting its march by

[091-042]the reduction of taxes, and the promotion of necessary public works.

[091-043]The surplus was naturally regarded, by the Patriot party, in the light

[091-044]of so much national capital; they looked upon it as an improvement

[091-045]fund, for the construction of canals, highways, and breakwaters, for

[091-046]the encouragement of the linen and other manufactures, and for the

[091-047]adornment of the capital with edifices worthy of the chief city of a

[091-048]flourishing kingdom.

[091-049]The leader of the Patriot party, Anthony Malone, was compared at this

[091-050]period, by an excellent authority, to "a great sea in a calm." He was

[091-051]considered, even by the fastidious Lord Shelburne, the equal, in

[091-052]oratory, of Chatham and Mansfield. He seems to have at all times,

[091-053]however, sunk the mere orator in the statesman, and to have used his

[091-054]great powers of argument even more in Council than in the arena. His

[091-055]position at the bar, as Prime Sergeant, by which he took precedence

[091-056]even of the Attorney-General, gave great weight to his opinions on all

[091-057]questions of constitutional law. The roystering country gentlemen, who

[091-058]troubled their heads but little with anything besides dogs and horses,

[091-059]pistols and claret, felt secure in their new-fledged patriotism, under

[091-060]the broad aegis of the law extended over them by the most eminent

[091-061]lawyer of his age. The Speaker of the Commons, Henry Boyle, aided and

[091-062]assisted Malone, and when left free to combat on the floor, his high

[091-063]spirit and great fortune gave additional force to his example and

[091-064]confidence to his followers. Both were men too cautious to allow their

[091-065]adversaries any parliamentary advantage over them, but not so their

[091-066]intrepid coadjutor out of doors, Apothecary Lucas. He, like Swift,

[091-067]rising from local and municipal grievances to questions affecting the

[091-068]constitution of Parliament itself, was in 1749, against all the efforts

[091-069]of his friends in the House of Commons, declared by the majority of

[091-070]that House to be "an enemy to his country," and a reward was

[091-071]accordingly issued for his apprehension. For a time he was compelled to

[091-072]retire to England; but he returned, to celebrate in his Freeman's

[091-073]Journal the humiliation of the primate, and the defeat of the policy

[091-074]both of Lord Harrington, and his successor, the Duke of Dorset.

[091-075]This nobleman, resolved to cast his predecessor into the shade by the

[091-076]brilliancy of his success, proceeded to take vigorous measures against

[091-077]the patriots. In his first speech to Parliament in 1751, he informed

[091-078]them his Majesty "consented" to the appropriation of the surplus

[091-079]revenue, by the House of Commons, and a clause was added to the annual

[091-080]supply bill in the English Council, containing the same obnoxious word,

[091-081]"consent." On this occasion, not feeling themselves strong enough to

[091-082]throw out the bill, and there being no alternative but rejection or

[091-083]acceptance, the Patriots permitted it to pass under protest. But the

[091-084]next session, when a similar addition was made, the Commons rejected

[091-085]the supply bill altogether, by a majority of 122 to 117. This was a

[091-086]measure of almost revolutionary consequence, since it left every branch

[091-087]of the public service unprovided for, for the ensuing twelve months.

[091-088]Both the advisers of the King in England, and the Viceroy in Ireland,

[091-089]seemed by their insane conduct as if they desired to provoke such a

[091-090]collision. Malone's patent of precedence as Prime Sergeant was

[091-091]cancelled; the speaker was dismissed from the Privy Council, and the

[091-092]surplus revenue was withdrawn from the Vice-Treasurer, by a King's

[091-093]letter. The indignation of the Dubliners at these outrages rose to the

[091-094]utmost pitch. Stone, Healy, Hutchinson, and others of the Castle party,

[091-095]were waylaid and menaced in the streets, and the Viceroy himself hooted

[091-096]wherever he appeared. Had the popular leaders been men less cautious,

[091-097]or less influential, the year 1753 might have witnessed a violent

[091-098]revolutionary movement. But they planted themselves on the authority of

[091-099]the constitution, they united boldness with prudence, and they

[091-100]triumphed. The Primate and his creatures raised against them in vain

[091-101]the cuckoo cry of disloyalty, both in Dublin and London. The English

[091-102]Whigs, long engaged themselves in a similar struggle with the overgrown

[091-103]power of the crown, sympathized with the Irish opposition, and defended

[091-104]their motives both in society and in Parliament. The enemies of the

[091-105]Dorset family as naturally took their part, and the duke himself was

[091-106]obliged to go over to protect his interest at court, leaving the odious

[091-107]Primate as one of the Lords-Justices. At his departure his guards were

[091-108]hardly able to protect him from the fury of the populace, to that

[091-109]waterside to which Chesterfield had walked on foot, seven years before,

[091-110]amid the benedictions of the same people.

[091-111]The Patriots had at this crisis a great addition to their strength, in

[091-112]the accession of James, the twentieth Earl of Kildare, successively

[091-113]Marquis and Duke of Leinster. This nobleman, in the prime of life,

[091-114]married to the beautiful Emily Lennox, daughter of the Duke of

[091-115]Richmond, followed Dorset to England, and presented to the King, with

[091-116]his own hand, one of the boldest memorials ever addressed to a

[091-117]sovereign by a subject. After reciting the past services of his family

[091-118]in maintaining the imperial connection, he declared himself the organ

[091-119]of several thousands of his Majesty's liege subjects, "as well the

[091-120]nobles as the clergy, the gentry, and the commonalty of the kingdom."

[091-121]He dwells on the peculation and extravagance of the administration,

[091-122]under "the Duumvirate" of the Viceroy and the Primate, which he

[091-123]compares with the league of Strafford and Laud. He denounces more

[091-124]especially Lord George Sackville, son to Dorset, for his intermeddling

[091-125]in every branch of administration. He speaks of Dr. Stone as "a greedy

[091-126]churchman, who affects to be a second Wolsey in the senate." This

[091-127]high-toned memorial struck with astonishment the English ministers, who

[091-128]did not hesitate to hint, that, in a reign less merciful, it would not

[091-129]have passed with impunity. In Ireland it raised the hardy earl to the

[091-130]pinnacle of popular favour. A medal was struck in his honour,

[091-131]representing him guarding a heap of treasure with a drawn sword, and

[091-132]the motto—"Touch not, says Kildare." At the opening of the next

[091-133]Parliament, he was a full hour making his way among the enthusiastic

[091-134]crowd, from his house in Kildare street to College Green. In little

[091-135]more than a year, the Duke of Dorset, whom English ministers had in

[091-136]vain endeavoured to sustain, was removed, and the Primate, by his

[091-137]Majesty's orders, was struck from the list of privy counsellors.

[091-139]Lord Harrington, now Duke of Devonshire, replaced the disgraced and

[091-140]defeated Dorset, and at once surrounded himself with advisers from the

[091-141]ranks of the opposition. The Earl of Kildare was his personal and

[091-142]political friend, and his first visit, on arriving, was paid at Carton.

[091-143]The Speaker, Mr. Boyle, the Earl of Bessborough, head of the popular

[091-144]family of the Ponsonbys, and Mr. Malone, were called to the Privy

[091-145]Council. Lucas, exalted rather than injured by years of exile, was

[091-146]elected one of the members for the city of Dublin, and the whole face

[091-147]of affairs promised a complete and salutary change of administration.

[091-148]After a year in office, Devonshire returned to England in ill-health,

[091-149]leaving Lord Kildare as one of the Justices, an office which he

[091-150]continued to fill, till the arrival in September, 1756, of John, fourth

[091-151]Duke of Bedford, as Lord-Lieutenant, with Mr. Rigby, "a good four

[091-152]bottle man," as chief secretary.

[091-153]The instructions of the Duke of Bedford, dictated by the genius and

[091-154]wisdom of Chatham, were, to employ "all softening and healing arts of

[091-155]government." His own desire, as a Whig, at the head of the Whig

[091-156]families of England, was to unite and consolidate the same party in

[091-157]Ireland, so as to make them a powerful auxiliary force to the English

[091-158]Whigs. Consistently with this design, he wished well to the country he

[091-159]was sent to rule, and was sincerely desirous of promoting measures of

[091-160]toleration. But he found the Patriots distracted by success, and

[091-161]disorganized by the possession of power. The Speaker, who had struggled

[091-162]so successfully against his predecessors, was in the Upper House as

[091-163]Earl of Shannon, and the chair of the Commons was filled by John

[091-164]Ponsonby, of the Bessborough family. The Ponsonby following, and the

[091-165]Earl of Kildare's friends were at this period almost as much divided

[091-166]from each other in their views of public policy, as either were from

[091-167]the party of the Primate. The Ponsonby party, still directed by Malone,

[091-168]wished to follow up the recent victory on the money bills, by a measure

[091-169]of Catholic relief, a tax upon absentees, and a reduction of the

[091-170]pension list, shamelessly burthened beyond all former proportion. Lord

[091-171]Kildare and his friends were not then prepared to go such lengths,

[091-172]though that high spirited nobleman afterwards came into most of these

[091-173]measures. After endeavouring in vain to unite, these two interests, the

[091-174]Duke of Bedford found, or fancied himself compelled, in order to secure

[091-175]a parliamentary majority, to listen to the overtures of the, obsequious

[091-176]Primate, to restore him to the Council, and to leave him, together with

[091-177]his old enemy, Lord Shannon, in the situation of joint administrators,

[091-178]during his journey to England, in 1758. The Earl of Kildare, it should

[091-179]be remarked, firmly refused to be associated with Stone, on any terms,

[091-180]or for any time, long or short.

[091-181]The closing of this important reign is notable for the first Catholic

[091-182]meeting held since the reign of Queen Anne. In the spring of 1757, four

[091-183]hundred respectable gentlemen attended by mutual agreement, at Dublin,

[091-184]among whom were Lords Devlin, Taafe, and Fingal, the antiquary, Charles

[091-185]O'Conor, of Balanagar, the historian of the _Civil Wars_, Dr. Curry,

[091-186]and Mr. Wyse, a merchant of Waterford, the ancestor of a still better

[091-187]known labourer in the same cause. The then recent persecution of Mr.

[091-188]Saul, a Dublin merchant, of their faith, for having harboured a young

[091-189]lady whose friends wished to coerce her into a change of religion, gave

[091-190]particular significance to this assembly. It is true the proceedings

[091-191]were characterized by caution amounting almost to timidity, but the

[091-192]unanimous declaration of their loyal attachment to the throne, at a

[091-193]moment when French invasion was imminent, produced the best effect, and

[091-194]greatly strengthened the hands of the Clanbrassils, Ponsonbys, Malones,

[091-195]Dalys, and other advocates of an enlarged toleration in both Houses. It

[091-196]is true no immediate legislation followed, but the way was prepared for

[091-197]future ameliorations by the discretion and tact of the Catholic

[091-198]delegates of 1757. They were thenceforth allowed at least the right of

[091-199]meeting and petitioning, of which they had long been deprived, and the

[091-200]restoration of which marks the first step in their gradual recovery of

[091-201]their civil liberties.

[091-202]In 1759 a rumour broke out in Dublin that a legislative union was in

[091-203]contemplation by the Primate and his faction. On the 3rd of December,

[091-204]the citizens rose _en masse_, and surrounded the Houses of Parliament.

[091-205]They stopped the carriages of members, and obliged them to swear

[091-206]opposition to such a measure. Some of the Protestant bishops, and the

[091-207]Lord Chancellor were roughly handled; a privy counsellor was thrown

[091-208]into the river; the Attorney General was wounded and obliged to take

[091-209]refuge in the college; Lord Inchiquin was abused till he said his name

[091-210]was O'Brien, when the rage of the people "was turned into

[091-211]acclamations." The Speaker, Mr. Ponsonby, and the Chief Secretary, Mr.

[091-212]Rigby, had to appear in the porch of the House of Commons, solemnly to

[091-213]assure the citizens that no union was dreamed of, and if it was

[091-214]proposed, that they would be the first to resist it. Public spirit had

[091-215]evidently grown bold and confident, and we can well believe Secretary

[091-216]Rigby when he writes to the elder Pitt, that "the mob" declared, "since

[091-217]they have no chance of numbers in the House, they must have recourse to

[091-218]numbers out of doors."
