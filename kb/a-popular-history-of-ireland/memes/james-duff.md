Sir James Duff was a British military officer who commanded forces during the [[Rebellion of 1798|Irish Rebellion of 1798]]. His reputation was shaped by both his military effectiveness and the severity of his conduct, which earned him the approval of the "extermination party" in Dublin while drawing condemnation from others. He played a key role in the campaign to suppress the Wexford insurrection and later sat on the military commission that tried captured rebel leaders.

## Arrival and Early Actions

In the early summer of 1798, as the rebellion spread through [[Leinster]], Duff was ordered to reinforce Crown forces. He brought 800 regulars by forced marches from Limerick to join the campaign against the insurgents (102-260). His arrival was timely, as the United Irish forces had demonstrated considerable capability.

> "At Monastereven they were [driven back]... they showed qualities, which, if well directed, would have established for their possessors a high military reputation." (102-260)


At Monastereven, they had shown "qualities, which, if well directed, would have established for their possessors a high military reputation" (102-260).

## The Curragh Massacre

Duff's conduct at the Curragh of [[Kildare]] became one of the most notorious episodes of the 1798 campaign. After a skirmish in which General Dundas' advance was driven back with the loss of 22 regulars and Captain Erskine killed, Duff ordered his men to attack an unarmed multitude on the Curragh plain.

> "Sir James Duff's wanton cruelty in sabring and shooting down an unarmed multitude on the Curragh, won him the warm approval of the extermination party in the Capital." (102-268)


His conduct on the Curragh won him "the warm approval of the extermination party in the Capital" (102-268). This episode exemplified the brutal counter-insurgency tactics employed by some Crown commanders.

## The Wexford Campaign

As the rebellion concentrated in [[Wexford]], Duff was integrated into the broader military strategy under [[Charles Cornwallis|Lord Cornwallis]]. The plan called for a coordinated multi-column assault: General Needham advancing to Gorey, General Sir Henry Johnson to unite at Old Ross with Sir James Duff from Carlow, and Sir Charles Asgill to occupy strategic bridges and passes (103-333).

At the decisive engagement, Duff's forces played a critical role. He "obtained possession of an eminence which commanded the lower line of the rebel encampment," and from this position a "brisk cannonade was opened against the opposing force" (103-361). The combined columns eventually encircled the insurgents, with Duff's troops forming part of the pincer movement that trapped them between the forces of General Needham and the [[Marquis of Huntley]] (104-046).

## Trial of Rebel Leaders

After the suppression of the rising, Duff served on military commissions that tried captured insurgents. At his headquarters in Tullow, he presided over the trial of [[Billy Byrne of Ballymanus]], "the hero of his county" and one of the last Wicklow rebel leaders to be captured (104-078). The commission — composed of Duff, Lord Roden, Colonels Eden and Foster, and Major Hall — tried Byrne and sentenced him to execution. The trial was marked by the impropriety of Major Hall, who "had the meanness to put to him, prisoner as he was, several insulting questions" (104-080).
