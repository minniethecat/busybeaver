CHAPTER VII.

[044-001]CONTINUED DIVISION AND DECLINE OF "THE ENGLISH INTEREST"—RICHARD, DUKE

[044-002]OF YORK, LORD LIEUTENANT—CIVIL WAR AGAIN IN ENGLAND—EXECUTION OF THE

[044-003]EARL OF DESMOND—ASCENDANCY OF THE KILDARE GERALDINES.

[044-004]We have already described the limits to which "the Pale" was

[044-005]circumscribed at the beginning of the fourteenth century. The fortunes

[044-006]of that inconsiderable settlement during the following century hardly

[044-007]rise to the level of historical importance, nor would the recital of

[044-008]them be at all readable but for the ultimate consequences which ensued

[044-009]from the preservation of those last remains of foreign power in the

[044-010]island. On that account, however, we have to consult the barren annals

[044-011]of "the Pale" through the intermediate period, that we may make clear

[044-012]the accidents by which it was preserved from destruction, and enabled

[044-013]to play a part in after-times, undreamt of and inconceivable, to those

[044-014]who tolerated its existence in the ages of which we speak.

[044-016]On the northern coasts of Ireland the co-operation of the friendly

[044-017]Scots with the native Irish had long been a source of anxiety to the

[044-018]Palesmen. In the year 1404, Dongan, Bishop of Derry, and Sir Jenico

[044-019]d'Artois, were appointed Commissioners by Henry IV., to conclude a

[044-020]permanent peace with McDonald, Lord of the Isles, but, notwithstanding

[044-021]that form was then gone through during the reigns of all the

[044-022]Lancasterian Kings, evidence of the Hiberno-Scotch alliance being still

[044-023]in existence, constantly recurs. In the year 1430 an address or

[044-024]petition of the Dublin Council to the King sets forth "that the enemies

[044-025]and rebels, _aided by the Scots_, had conquered or rendered tributary

[044-026]almost every part of the country, _except the county of Dublin_." The

[044-027]presence of Henry V. in Ireland had been urgently solicited by his

[044-028]lieges in that kingdom, but without effect. The hero of Agincourt

[044-029]having set his heart upon the conquest of France, left Ireland to his

[044-030]lieutenants and their deputies. Nor could his attention be aroused to

[044-031]the English interest in that country, even by the formal declaration of

[044-032]the Speaker of the English Parliament, that "the greater part of the

[044-033]lordship of Ireland" had been "conquered" by the natives.

[044-034]The comparatively new family of Talbot, sustained by the influence of

[044-035]the great Earl of Shrewsbury, now Seneschal of France, had risen to the

[044-036]highest pitch of influence. When on the accession of Henry VI., Edward

[044-037]Mortimer, Earl of March, was appointed Lord Lieutenant, and Dantsey,

[044-038]Bishop of Meath, his deputy, Talbot, Archbishop of Dublin, and Lord

[044-039]Chancellor, refused to acknowledge Dantsey's pretensions because his

[044-040]commission was given under the private seal of Lord Mortimer. Having

[044-041]effected his object in this instance, the Archbishop directed his

[044-042]subsequent attacks against the House of Ormond, the chief favourites of

[044-043]the King, or rather of the Council, in that reign. In 1441, at a Dublin

[044-044]Parliament, messengers were appointed to convey certain articles to the

[044-045]King, the purport of which was to prevent the Earl of Ormond from being

[044-046]made Lord Lieutenant, alleging against him many misdemeanours in his

[044-047]former administration, and praying that some "mighty lord of England"

[044-048]might be named to that office to execute the laws more effectually

[044-049]"than any Irishman ever did or ever will do."

[044-050]This attempt to destroy the influence of Ormond led to an alliance

[044-051]between that Earl and Sir James, afterwards seventh Earl of Desmond.

[044-052]Sir James was son of Gerald, fourth Earl (distinguished as "the

[044-053]Rhymer," or Magician), by the lady Eleanor Butler, daughter of the

[044-054]second Earl of Ormond. He stood, therefore, in the relation of cousin

[044-055]to the cotemporary head of the Butler family. When his nephew Thomas

[044-056]openly violated the Statute of Kilkenny, by marrying the beautiful

[044-057]Catherine McCormac, the ambitious and intriguing Sir James, anxious to

[044-058]enforce that statute, found a ready seconder in Ormond. Earl Thomas,

[044-059]forced to quit the country, died an exile at Rouen, in France, and Sir

[044-060]James, after many intrigues and negotiations, obtained the title and

[044-061]estates. For once the necessities of Desmond and Ormond united these

[044-062]houses, but the money of the English Archbishop of Dublin, backed by

[044-063]the influence of his illustrious brother, proved equal to them both. In

[044-064]the first twenty-five years of the reign of Henry VI. (1422-1447,)

[044-065]Ormond was five times Lieutenant or Deputy, and Talbot five times

[044-066]Deputy, Lord Justice, or Lord Commissioner. Their factious controversy

[044-067]culminated with "the articles" adopted in 1441, which altogether failed

[044-068]of the intended effect; Ormond was reappointed two years afterwards to

[044-069]his old office; nor was it till 1446, when the Earl of Shrewsbury was a

[044-070]third time sent over, that the Talbots had any substantial advantage

[044-071]over their rivals. The recall of the Earl for service in France, and

[044-072]the death of the Archbishop two years later, though it deprived the

[044-073]party they had formed of a resident leader, did not lead to its

[044-074]dissolution. Bound together by common interests and dangers, their

[044-075]action may be traced in opposition to the Geraldines, through the

[044-076]remaining years of Henry VI., and perhaps so late as the earlier years

[044-077]of Henry VII. (1485-1500).

[044-078]In the struggle of dynasties from which England suffered so severely

[044-079]during the fifteenth century, the drama of ambition shifted its scenes

[044-080]from London and York to Calais and Dublin. The appointment of Richard,

[044-081]Duke of York, as Lord Lieutenant, in 1449, presented him an opportunity

[044-082]of creating a Yorkist party among the nobles and people of "the Pale."

[044-083]This able and ambitious Prince possessed in his hereditary estate

[044-084]resources equal to great enterprises. He was in the first place the

[044-085]representative of the third son of Edward III.; on the death of his

[044-086]cousin the Earl of March, in 1424, he became heir to that property and

[044-087]title. He was Duke of York, Earl of March, and Earl of Rutland, in

[044-088]England; Earl of Ulster and Earl of Cork, Lord of Connaught, Clare,

[044-089]Meath, and Trim, in Ireland. He had been twice Regent of France, during

[044-090]the minority of Henry, where he upheld the cause of the Plantagenet

[044-091]King with signal ability. By the peace concluded at Tours, between

[044-092]England, France, and Burgundy, in 1444, he was enabled to return to

[044-093]England, where the King had lately come of age, and begun to exhibit

[044-094]the weak though amiable disposition which led to his ruin. The events

[044-095]of the succeeding two or three years were calculated to expose Henry to

[044-096]the odium of his subjects and the machinations of his enemies. Town

[044-097]after town and province after province were lost in France; the Regent

[044-098]Somerset returned to experience the full force of this unpopularity;

[044-099]the royal favourite, Suffolk, was banished, pursued, and murdered at

[044-100]sea; the King's uncles, Cardinal Beaufort and the Duke of Gloucester,

[044-101]were removed by death—so that every sign and circumstance of the time

[044-102]whispered encouragement to the ambitious Duke. When, therefore, the

[044-103]Irish lieutenancy was offered, in order to separate him from his

[044-104]partizans, he at first refused it; subsequently, however, he accepted,

[044-105]on conditions dictated by himself, calculated to leave him wholly his

[044-106]own master. These conditions, reduced to writing in the form of an

[044-107]Indenture between the King and the Duke, extended his lieutenancy to a

[044-108]period of ten years; allowed him, besides the entire revenue of

[044-109]Ireland, an annual subsidy from England; full power to let the King's

[044-110]land, to levy and maintain soldiers, to place or displace all officers,

[044-111]to appoint a Deputy, and to return to England at his pleasure. On these

[044-112]terms the ex-Regent of France undertook the government of the English

[044-113]settlement in Ireland.

[044-114]Arrived at Dublin, _the_ Duke (as in his day he was always called,)

[044-115]employed himself rather to strengthen his party than to extend the

[044-116]limits of his government. Soon after his arrival a son was born to him,

[044-117]and baptized with great pomp in the Castle. James, fifth Earl of

[044-118]Ormond, and Thomas, eighth Earl of Desmond, were invited to stand as

[044-119]sponsors. In the line of policy indicated by this choice, he steadily

[044-120]persevered during his whole connection with Ireland—which lasted till

[044-121]his death, in 1460. Alternately he named a Butler and a Geraldine as

[044-122]his deputy, and although he failed ultimately to win the Earl of Ormond

[044-123]from the traditional party of his family, he secured the attachment of

[044-124]several of his kinsmen. Stirring events in England, the year after his

[044-125]appointment, made it necessary for him to return immediately. The

[044-126]unpopularity of the administration which had banished him had rapidly

[044-127]augmented. The French King had recovered the whole of Normandy, for

[044-128]four centuries annexed to the English Crown. Nothing but Calais

[044-129]remained of all the Continental possessions which the Plantagenets had

[044-130]inherited, and which Henry V. had done so much to strengthen and

[044-131]extend. Domestic abuses aggravated the discontent arising from foreign

[044-132]defeats. The Bishop of Chichester, one of the ministers, was set upon

[044-133]and slain by a mob at Portsmouth. Twenty thousand men of Kent, under

[044-134]the command of Jack Cade, an Anglo-Irishman, who had given himself out

[044-135]as a son of the last Earl of March, who died in the Irish government

[044-136]twenty-five years before, marched upon London. They defeated a royal

[044-137]force at Sevenoaks, and the city opened its gate at the summons of

[044-138]Cade. The Kentish men took possession of Southwark, while their Irish

[044-139]leader for three days, entering the city every morning, compelled the

[044-140]mayor and the judges to sit in the Guildhall, tried and sentenced Lord

[044-141]Say to death, who, with his son-in-law, Cromer, Sheriff of Kent, was

[044-142]accordingly executed. Every evening, as he had promised the citizens,

[044-143]he retired with his guards across the river, preserving the strictest

[044-144]order among them. But the royalists were not idle, and when, on the

[044-145]fourth morning Cade attempted as usual to enter London proper, he found

[044-146]the bridge of Southwark barricaded and defended by a strong force under

[044-147]the Lord Scales. After six hours' hard fighting his raw levies were

[044-148]repulsed, and many of them accepted a free pardon tendered to them in

[044-149]the moment of defeat. Cade retired with the remainder on Deptford and

[044-150]Rochester, but gradually abandoned by them, he was surprised, half

[044-151]famished in a garden at Heyfield, and put to death. His captor claimed

[044-152]and received the large reward of a thousand marks offered for his head.

[044-153]This was in the second week of July; on the 1st of September, news was

[044-154]brought to London that the Duke of York had suddenly landed from

[044-155]Ireland. His partizans eagerly gathered round him at his castle of

[044-156]Fotheringay, but for five years longer, by the repeated concessions of

[044-157]the gentle-minded Henry, and the interposition of powerful mediators,

[044-158]the actual war of the roses was postponed.

[044-159]It is beyond our province to follow the details of that ferocious

[044-160]struggle, which was waged almost incessantly from 1455 till 1471—from

[044-161]the first battle of St. Albans till the final battle at Tewksbury. We

[044-162]are interested in it mainly as it connects the fortunes of the

[044-163]Anglo-Irish Earls with one or other of the dynasties; and their

[044-164]fortunes again, with the benefit or disadvantage of their allies and

[044-165]relatives among our native Princes. Of the transactions in England, it

[044-166]may be sufficient to say that the Duke of York, after his victory at

[044-167]St. Albans in '55, was declared Lord Protector of the realm during

[044-168]Henry's imbecility; that the next year the King recovered and the

[044-169]Protector's office was abolished; that in '57 both parties stood at

[044-170]bay; in '58 an insecure peace was patched up between them; in '59 they

[044-171]appealed to arms, the Yorkists gained a victory at Bloreheath, but

[044-172]being defeated at Ludiford, Duke Richard, with one of his sons, fled

[044-173]for safety into Ireland.

[044-174]It was the month of November when the fugitive Duke arrived to resume

[044-175]the Lord Lieutenancy which he had formerly exercised. Legally, his

[044-176]commission, for those who recognized the authority of King Henry, had

[044-177]expired four months before—as it bore date from July 5th, 1449; but it

[044-178]is evident the majority of the Anglo-Irish received him as a Prince of

[044-179]their own election rather than as an ordinary Viceroy. He held, soon

[044-180]after his arrival, a Parliament at Dublin, which met by adjournment at

[044-181]Drogheda the following spring. The English Parliament having declared

[044-182]him, his duchess, sons, and principal adherents traitors, and writs to

[044-183]that effect having been sent over, the Irish Parliament passed a

[044-184]declaratory Act (1460) making the service of all such writs treason

[044-185]against _their_ authority—"it having been ever customary in their land

[044-186]to receive and entertain strangers with due respect and hospitality."

[044-187]Under this law, an emissary of the Earl of Ormond, upon whom English

[044-188]writs against the fugitives were found, was executed as a traitor. This

[044-189]independent Parliament confirmed the Duke in his office; made it high

[044-190]treason to imagine his death, and—taking advantage of the favourable

[044-191]conjuncture of affairs—they further declared that the inhabitants of

[044-192]Ireland could only be bound by laws made in Ireland; that no writs were

[044-193]of force unless issued under the great seal of Ireland; that the realm

[044-194]had of ancient right its own Lord Constable and Earl Marshal, by whom

[044-195]alone trials for treason alleged to have been committed in Ireland

[044-196]could be conducted. In the same busy spring, the Earl of Warwick (so

[044-197]celebrated as "the Kingmaker" of English history) sailed from Calais,

[044-198]of which he was Constable, with the Channel-fleet, of which he was also

[044-199]in command, and doubling the Land's End of England, arrived at Dublin

[044-200]to concert measures for another rising in England. He found the Duke at

[044-201]Dublin "surrounded by his Earls and homagers," and measures were soon

[044-202]concerted between them.

[044-204]An appeal to the English nation was prepared at this Conference,

[044-205]charging upon Henry's advisers that they had written to the French King

[044-206]to besiege Calais, and to the Irish Princes to expel the English

[044-207]settlers. The loyalty of the fugitive lords, and their readiness to

[044-208]prove their innocence before their sovereign, were stoutly asserted.

[044-209]Emissaries were despatched in every direction; troops were raised;

[044-210]Warwick soon after landed in Kent-always strongly pro-Yorkist-defeated

[044-211]the royalists at Northampton in July, and the Duke reaching London in

[044-212]October, a compromise was agreed to, after much discussion, in which

[044-213]Henry was to have the crown for life, while the Duke was acknowledged

[044-214]as his successor, and created president of his council.

[044-215]We have frequently remarked in our history the recurrence of conflicts

[044-216]between the north and south of the island. The same thing is distinctly

[044-217]traceable through the annals of England down to a quite recent period.

[044-218]Whether difference of race, or of admixture of race may not lie at the

[044-219]foundation of such long-living enmities, we will not here attempt to

[044-220]discuss; such, however, is the fact. Queen Margaret had fled northward

[044-221]after the defeat of Northampton towards the Scottish border, from which

[044-222]she now returned at the head of 20,000 men. The Duke advanced rapidly

[044-223]to meet her, and engaging with a far inferior force at Wakefield, was

[044-224]slain in the field, or beheaded after the battle. All now seemed lost

[044-225]to the Yorkist party, when young Edward, son of Duke Richard, advancing

[044-226]from the marches of Wales at the head of an army equal in numbers to

[044-227]the royalists, won, in the month of February, 1461, the battles of

[044-228]Mortimers-cross and Barnet, and was crowned at Westminster in March, by

[044-229]the title of Edward IV. The sanguinary battle of Towton, soon after his

[044-230]coronation, where 38,000 dead were reckoned by the heralds, confirmed

[044-231]his title and established his throne. Even the subsequent hostility of

[044-232]Warwick—though it compelled him once to surrender himself a prisoner,

[044-233]and once to fly the country—did not finally transfer the sceptre to his

[044-234]rival. Warwick was slain in the battle of Tewkesbury (1471), the

[044-235]Lancasterian Prince Edward was put to death on the field, and his

[044-236]unhappy father was murdered in prison. Two years later, Henry, Earl of

[044-237]Richmond, grandson of Catherine, Queen of Henry V. and Owen Ap Tudor,

[044-238]the only remaining leader capable of rallying the beaten party, was

[044-239]driven into exile in France, from which he returned fourteen years

[044-240]afterwards to contest the crown with Richard III.

[044-241]In these English wars, the only Irish nobleman who sustained the

[044-242]Lancasterian cause was James, fifth Earl of Ormond. He had been created

[044-243]by Henry, Earl of Wiltshire, during his father's lifetime, in the same

[044-244]year in which his father stood sponsor in Dublin for the son of the

[044-245]Duke. He succeeded to the Irish title and estates in 1451: held a

[044-246]foremost rank in almost all the engagements from the battle of Saint

[044-247]Albans to that of Towton, in which he was taken prisoner and executed

[044-248]by order of Edward IV. His blood was declared attainted, and his

[044-249]estates forfeited; but a few years later both the title and property

[044-250]were restored to Sir John Butler, the sixth Earl. On the eve of the

[044-251]open rupture between the Roses, another name intimately associated with

[044-252]Ireland disappeared from the roll of the English nobility. The veteran

[044-253]Talbot, Earl of Shrewsbury, in the eightieth year of his age, accepted

[044-254]the command of the English forces in France, retook the city of

[044-255]Bordeaux, but fell in attack on the French camp at Chatillon, in the

[044-256]subsequent campaign—1453. His son, Lord Lisle, was slain at the same

[044-257]time, defending his father's body. Among other consequences which

[044-258]ensued, the Talbot interest in Ireland suffered from the loss of so

[044-259]powerful a patron at the English court. We have only to add that at

[044-260]Wakefield, and in most of the other engagements, there was a strong

[044-261]Anglo-Irish contingent in the Yorkist ranks, and a smaller one—chiefly

[044-262]tenants of Ormond—on the opposite side. Many writers complain that the

[044-263]House of York drained "the Pale" of its defenders, and thus still

[044-264]further diminished the resources of the English interest in Ireland.

[044-265]In the last forty years of the fifteenth century, the history of "the

[044-266]Pale" is the biography of the family of the Geraldines. We must make

[044-267]some brief mention of the remarkable men to whom we refer.

[044-268]Thomas, eighth Earl of Desmond, for his services to the House of York,

[044-269]was appointed Lord Deputy in the first years of Edward IV. He had

[044-270]naturally made himself obnoxious to the Ormond interest, but still more

[044-271]so to the Talbots, whose leader in civil contests was Sherwood, Bishop

[044-272]of Meath—for some years, in despite of the Geraldines, Lord Chancellor.

[044-273]Between him and Desmond there existed the bitterest animosity. In 1464,

[044-274]nine of the Deputy's men were slain in a broil in Fingall, by tenants

[044-275]or servants of the Bishop. The next year each party repaired to London

[044-276]to vindicate himself and criminate his antagonist. The Bishop seems to

[044-277]have triumphed, for in 1466, John Tiptoft, Earl of Worcester, called in

[044-278]England, for his barbarity to Lancasterian prisoners, "the Butcher,"

[044-279]superseded Desmond. The movement of Thaddeus O'Brien, already related,

[044-280]the same year, gave Tiptoft grounds for accusing Desmond, Kildare, Sir

[044-281]Edward Plunkett, and others, of treason. On this charge he summoned

[044-282]them before him at Drogheda in the following February. Kildare wisely

[044-283]fled to England, where he pleaded his innocence successfully with the

[044-284]King. But Desmond and Plunkett, over-confident of their own influence,

[044-285]repaired to Drogheda, were tried, condemned, and beheaded. Their

[044-286]execution took place on the 15th day of February, 1467. It is

[044-287]instructive to add that Tiptoft, a few years later, underwent the fate

[044-288]in England, without exciting a particle of the sympathy felt for

[044-289]Desmond.

[044-290]Thomas, seventh Earl of Kildare, succeeded on his safe return from

[044-291]England to more than the power of his late relative. The office of

[044-292]Chancellor, after a sharp struggle, was taken from Bishop Sherwood, and

[044-293]confirmed to him for life by an act of the twelfth, Edward III. He had

[044-294]been named Lord Justice after Tiptoft's recall, in 1467, and four years

[044-295]later exchanged the title for that of Lord Deputy to the young Duke of

[044-296]Clarence—the nominal Lieutenant. In 1475, on some change of Court

[044-297]favour, the supreme power was taken from him, and conferred on the old

[044-298]enemy of his House, the Bishop of Meath. Kildare died two years later,

[044-299]having signalized his latter days by founding an Anglo-Irish order of

[044-300]chivalry, called "the Brothers of St. George." This order was to

[044-301]consist of 13 persons of the highest rank within the Pale, 120 mounted

[044-302]archers, and 40 horsemen, attended by 40 pages. The officers were to

[044-303]assemble annually in Dublin, on St. George's Day, to elect their

[044-304]Captain from their own number. After having existed twenty years the

[044-305]Brotherhood was suppressed by the jealousy of Henry VII., in 1494.

[044-306]Gerald, eighth Earl of Kildare (called in the Irish Annals Geroit More,

[044-307]or "the Great"), succeeded his father in 1477. He had the gratification

[044-308]of ousting Sherwood from the government the following year, and having

[044-309]it transferred to himself. For nearly forty years he continued the

[044-310]central figure among the Anglo-Irish, and as his family were closely

[044-311]connected by marriage with the McCarthys, O'Carrolls of Ely, the

[044-312]O'Conors of Offally, O'Neils and O'Donnells, he exercised immense

[044-313]influence over the affairs of all the Provinces. In his time, moreover,

[044-314]the English interest, under the auspices of an undisturbed dynasty, and

[044-315]a cautious, politic Prince (Henry VII.), began by slow and almost

[044-316]imperceptible degrees to recover the unity and compactness it had lost

[044-317]ever since the Red Earl's death.
