## Overview

Charles Cornwallis, 1st Marquess Cornwallis (1738–1805), served as Lord Lieutenant of Ireland from June 1798 to 1801. He arrived in the immediate aftermath of the Rebellion of 1798, tasked with restoring order and carrying the Act of Union.

Though remembered in America for his surrender at Yorktown, Cornwallis conducted his Irish administration with clemency and political skill. His viceroyalty achieved the legislative union of Great Britain and Ireland.

## Arrival in Ireland

Cornwallis reached Ireland in summer 1798 as the rebellion was being suppressed. Lord Camden's administration had pursued a "policy of torture and provocation" (104-013) that drove the country into insurrection. McGee observes this policy "had, indeed, served its pernicious purpose" (104-014), making a change of direction essential.

The new viceroy found conditions favorable for a fresh approach. "It was now possible for a new ruler to turn a new leaf; this Lord Cornwallis did from the hour of his arrival" (104-015). He faced a country devastated by rebellion and eager for peace.

His approach to the defeated rebels combined firmness with mercy. "The clemency of Lord Cornwallis" was extended to many prisoners, though "ninety of the Longford and Kilkenny militia who had joined the French were hanged" (104-237). This balance served the needs of both justice and reconciliation.

## The Irish Parliament and the Act of Union

The Irish Parliament assembled within days of Cornwallis's arrival. "Both Houses voted highly loyal addresses to the King and Lord-Lieutenant" (105-081), signaling a willingness to cooperate with the new administration.

> At the close of the year of the rebellion, Lord Cornwallis was able to report to Mr. Pitt that the prospects of carrying the measure were better than could have been expected, and on this report the English ministry proceeded. (105-164)

The methods employed to pass the Union proved highly controversial. Cornwallis used both persuasion and material inducement. "There were the more convincing arguments of titles, employments, and direct pecuniary purchase" (105-164) to secure the necessary votes. Borough owners received compensation for lost seats.

The Union debate followed coordinated strategy. "A paragraph identical with that employed by Lord Cornwallis in introducing the subject" (105-272) appeared in both parliaments. Cornwallis prorogued the Irish Houses on the 1st of June, alluding to "the anxiety of the King, as the common father of his people" (105-397) to see the kingdoms united.

## The King's Restrictive Instructions

Cornwallis operated under strict royal instructions on the Catholic question. The King directed him "that no further indulgence could be granted to Catholics" (105-021), though Cornwallis was to "steadily pursue the object of effecting the union of Ireland and England" (105-023).

> To Lord Camden his instructions were to support the old English interest as well as the Protestant religion, and to Lord Cornwallis, that no further indulgence could be granted to Catholics. (105-019)

This restriction became deeply damaging. Many Catholics had supported the Union expecting emancipation would follow. McGee reflects that as Englishmen, ministers "should consider the empire in the first place; that they should desire to strengthen the central authority" (105-050). But the broken promise poisoned Irish politics for a generation.

## Resignation with Pitt

In 1801, the Catholic question brought down Pitt's ministry. When George III refused emancipation, Pitt resigned, and "Lord Cornwallis and Lord Castlereagh following their example" (107-044). This collective resignation demonstrated the commitment to Catholic relief that Cornwallis and Castlereagh shared.

## Death and Legacy

After Ireland, Cornwallis returned to India as Governor-General. He died at Ghazipur in 1805. His soldier-administrator career survived the Yorktown disaster.

His Irish administration achieved the Union but at a lasting cost. The Union was consummated without Catholic emancipation, creating a betrayal that fueled O'Connell's emancipation movement. Cornwallis's personal clemency won respect, but the corrupt methods used to pass the Union permanently compromised its legitimacy.
