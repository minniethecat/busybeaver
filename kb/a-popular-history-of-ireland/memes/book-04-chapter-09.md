CHAPTER IX.

[031-001]EVENTS OF THE THIRTEENTH CENTURY—THE NORMANS IN MUNSTER AND LEINSTER.

[031-002]We have already told the tragic fate of the two adventurers—Fitzstephen

[031-003]and de Cogan—between whom the whole of Desmond was first partitioned by

[031-004]Henry II. But there were not wanting other claimants, either by

[031-005]original grant from the crown, by intermarriage with Irish, or

[031-006]Norman-Irish heiresses, or new-comers, favourites of John or of Henry

[031-007]III., or of their Ministers, enriched at the expense of the native

[031-008]population. Thomas, third son of Maurice Fitzgerald, claimed partly

[031-009]through his uncle Fitzstephen, and partly through his marriage with the

[031-010]daughter of another early adventurer, Sir William Morrie, whose vast

[031-011]estates on which his descendants were afterwards known as Earls of

[031-012]Desmond, the White Knight, the Knight of Glyn, and the Knight of Kerry.

[031-013]Robert de Carew and Patrick de Courcy claimed as heirs general to de

[031-014]Cogan. The de Mariscoes, de Barris, and le Poers, were not extinct; and

[031-015]finally Edward I., soon after his accession, granted the whole land of

[031-016]Thomond to Thomas de Clare, son of the Earl of Gloucester, and

[031-017]son-in-law of Maurice, third Baron of Offally. A contest very similar

[031-018]to that which was waged in Connaught between the O'Conors and de Burghs

[031-019]was consequently going on in Munster at the same time, between the old

[031-020]inhabitants and the new claimants, of all the three classes just

[031-021]indicated.

[031-022]The principality of Desmond, containing angles of Waterford and

[031-023]Tipperary, with all Cork and Kerry, seemed at the beginning of the

[031-024]thirteenth century in greatest danger of conquest. The O'Callaghans,

[031-025]Lords of Cinel-Aedha, in the south of Cork, were driven into the

[031-026]mountains of Duhallow, where they rallied and held their ground for

[031-027]four centuries; the O'Sullivans, originally settled along the Suir,

[031-028]about Clonmel, were forced towards the mountain seacoast of Cork and

[031-029]Kerry, where they acquired new vigour in the less fertile soil of Beare

[031-030]and Bantry. The native families of the Desies, from their proximity to

[031-031]the port of Waterford, were harassed and overrun, and the ports of

[031-032]Dungarvan, Youghal, and Cork, being also taken and garrisoned by the

[031-033]founder of the earldom of Desmond, easy entrance and egress by sea

[031-034]could always be obtained for his allies, auxiliaries, and supplies. It

[031-035]was when these dangers were darkening and menacing on every side that

[031-036]the family of McCarthy, under a succession of able and vigorous chiefs,

[031-037]proved themselves worthy of the headship of the Eugenian race. Cormac

[031-038]McCarthy, who had expelled the first garrison from Waterford, ere he

[031-039]fell in a parley before Cork, had defeated the first enterprises of

[031-040]Fitzstephen and de Cogan; he left a worthy son in Donald na Curra, who,

[031-041]uniting his own co-relations, and acting in conjunction with O'Brien

[031-042]and O'Conor, retarded by his many exploits the progress of the invasion

[031-043]in Munster. He recovered Cork and razed King John's castle at

[031-044]Knockgraffon on the Suir. He left two surviving sons, of whom the

[031-045]eldest, Donald _Gott_, or the Stammerer, took the title of _More_, or

[031-046]Great, and his posterity remained princes of Desmond, until that title

[031-047]merged in the earldom of Glencare (A.D. 1565); the other, Cormac, after

[031-048]taking his brother prisoner compelled him to acknowledge him as lord of

[031-049]the four baronies of Carbury. From this Cormac the family of McCarthy

[031-050]Reagh descended, and to them the O'Driscolls, O'Donovans, O'Mahonys,

[031-051]and other Eugenian houses became tributary. The chief residence of

[031-052]McCarthy Reagh was long fixed at Dunmanway; his castles were also at

[031-053]Baltimore, Castlehaven, Lough-Fyne, and in Inis-Sherkin and Clear

[031-054]Island. The power of McCarthy More extended at its greatest reach from

[031-055]Tralee in Kerry to Lismore in Waterford. In the year 1229, Dermid

[031-056]McCarthy had peaceable possession of Cork, and founded the Franciscan

[031-057]Monastery there. Such was his power, that, according to Hamner and his

[031-058]authorities, the Geraldines "dare not for twelve years put plough into

[031-059]the ground in Desmond." At last, another generation rose, and fierce

[031-060]family feuds broke out between the branches of the family. The Lord of

[031-061]Carbury now was Fineen, or Florence, the most celebrated man of his

[031-062]name, and one whose power naturally encroached upon the possession of

[031-063]the elder house. John, son of Thomas Fitzgerald of Desmond, seized the

[031-064]occasion to make good the enormous pretension of his family. In the

[031-065]expedition which he undertook for this purpose, in the year 1260, he

[031-066]was joined by the Justiciary, William Dene, by Walter de Burgo, Earl of

[031-067]Ulster, by Walter de Riddlesford, Baron of Bray, by Donnel Roe, a chief

[031-068]of the hostile house of McCarthy. The Lord of Carbury united under his

[031-069]standard the chief Eugenian families, not only of the Coast, but even

[031-070]of McCarthy More's principality, and the battle was fought with great

[031-071]ferocity at Callan-Glen, near Kenmare, in Kerry. There the

[031-072]Anglo-Normans received the most complete defeat they had yet

[031-073]experienced on Irish ground. John Fitz-Thomas, his son Maurice, eight

[031-074]barons, fifteen knights, and "countless numbers of common soldiers were

[031-075]slain." The Monastery of Tralee received the dead body of its founder

[031-076]and his son, while Florence McCarthy, following up his blow, captured

[031-077]and broke down in swift succession all the English castles in his

[031-078]neighbourhood, including those of Macroom, Dunnamark, Dunloe, and

[031-079]Killorglin. In besieging one of these castles, called Ringrone, the

[031-080]victorious chief, in the full tide of conquest, was cut off, and his

[031-081]brother, called the _Atheleireach_ (or suspended priest), succeeded to

[031-082]his possessions. The death of the victor arrested the panic of the

[031-083]defeat, but Munster saw another generation before her invaders had

[031-084]shaken off the depression of the battle of Callan-glen.

[031-085]Before the English interest had received this severe blow in the south,

[031-086]a series of events had transpired in Leinster, going to show that its

[031-087]aspiring barons had been seized with the madness which precedes

[031-088]destruction. William, Earl Marshal and Protector of England during the

[031-089]minority of Henry III., had married Isabella, the daughter of Strongbow

[031-090]and granddaughter of Dermid, through whom he assumed the title of Lord

[031-091]of Leinster. He procured the office of Earl Marshal of

[031-092]Ireland—originally conferred on the first de Lacy—for his own nephew,

[031-093]and thus converted the de Lacys into mortal enemies. His son and

[031-094]successor Richard, having made himself obnoxious, soon after his

[031-095]accession to that title, to the young King, or to Hubert de Burgh, was

[031-096]outlawed, and letters were despatched to the Justiciary, Fitzgerald, to

[031-097]de Burgo, de Lacy, and other Anglo-Irish lords, if he landed in

[031-098]Ireland, to seize his person, alive or dead, and send it to England.

[031-099]Strong in his estates and alliances, the young Earl came; while his

[031-100]enemies employed the wily Geoffrey de Mountmorres to entrap him into a

[031-101]conference, in order to his destruction. The meeting was appointed for

[031-102]the first day of April, 1234, and while the outlawed Earl was

[031-103]conversing with those who had invited him, an affray began among their

[031-104]servants by design, he himself was mortally wounded and carried to one

[031-105]of Fitzgerald's castles, where he died. He was succeeded in his Irish

[031-106]honours by three of his brothers, who all died without heirs male.

[031-107]Anselme, the last Earl Marshal of his family, dying in 1245, left five

[031-108]co-heiresses, Maud, Joan, Isabel, Sybil, and Eva, between whom the

[031-109]Irish estates—or such portions of them in actual possession—were

[031-110]divided. They married respectively the Earls of Norfolk, Suffolk,

[031-111]Gloucester, Ferrers, and Braos, or Brace, Lord of Brecknock, in whose

[031-112]families, for another century or more, the secondary titles were

[031-113]Catherlogh, Kildare, Wexford, Kilkenny, and Leix,—those five districts

[031-114]being supposed, most absurdly, to have come into the Marshal family,

[031-115]from the daughter of Strongbow. The false knights and dishonoured

[031-116]nobles concerned in the murder of Richard Marshal were disappointed of

[031-117]the prey which had been promised them—the partition of his estates. And

[031-118]such was the horror which the deed excited in England, that it hastened

[031-119]the fall of Hubert de Burgh, though Maurice Fitzgerald, of

[031-120]Offally—ancestor of the Kildare family—having cleared himself of all

[031-121]complicity in it by oath—was continued as Justiciary for ten years

[031-122]longer. In the year 1245, for his tardiness in joining the King's army

[031-123]in Wales, he was succeeded by the false-hearted Geoffrey de

[031-124]Mountmorres, who held the office till 1247. During the next twenty-five

[031-125]years, about half as many Justices were placed and displaced, according

[031-126]to the whim of the successive favourites at the English Court. In 1252,

[031-127]Prince Edward, afterwards Edward I., was appointed with the title of

[031-128]Lord Lieutenant, but never came over. Nor is there in the series of

[031-129]rulers we have numbered, with, perhaps, two exceptions, any who have

[031-130]rendered their names memorable by great exploits, or lasting

[031-131]legislation. So little inherent power had the incumbents of the highest

[031-132]office—unless when, they employed their own proper forces in their

[031-133]sovereign's name—that we read without surprise, how the bold

[031-134]mountaineers of Wicklow, at the opening of the century (A.D. 1209)

[031-135]slaughtered the Bristolians of Dublin, engaged at their archery in

[031-136]Cullenswood, and at the close of it, how "one of the Kavanaghs, of the

[031-137]blood of McMurrogh, living at Leinster," "displayed his standards

[031-138]within sight of the city." Yet this is commonly spoken of as a country

[031-139]overrun by a few score Norman Knights, in a couple of campaigns!

[031-141]The maintenance of the conquest was in these years less the work of the

[031-142]King's Justices than of the great houses. Of these, two principally

[031-143]profited, by the untimely felling of that great tree which overshadowed

[031-144]all others in Leinster, the Marshals. The descendants of the eldest son

[031-145]of Maurice Fitzgerald clung to their Leinster possessions, while their

[031-146]equally vigorous cousins pushed their fortunes in Desmond. Maurice,

[031-147]grandson of Maurice, and second Baron of Offally, from the year 1229 to

[031-148]the year 1246, was three times Lord Justice. "He was a valiant Knight,

[031-149]a very pleasant man, and inferior to none in the kingdom," by Matthew

[031-150]Paris's account. He introduced the Franciscan and Dominican orders into

[031-151]Ireland, built many castles, churches, and abbeys at Youghal, at Sligo,

[031-152]at Armagh, at Maynooth, and in other places. In the year 1257, he was

[031-153]wounded in single combat by O'Donnell, Lord of Tyrconnell, near Sligo,

[031-154]and died soon after in the Franciscan habit in Youghal. He left his

[031-155]successor so powerful, that in the year 1264, there being a feud

[031-156]between the Geraldines and de Burghs, he seized the Lord Justice and

[031-157]the whole de Burgh party at a conference at Castledermot, and carried

[031-158]them to his own castles of Lea and Dunamase as prisoners. In 1272, on

[031-159]the accidental death of the Lord Justice Audley, by a fall from his

[031-160]horse, "the council" elected this the third Baron of Offally in his

[031-161]stead.

[031-162]The family of Butler were of slower growth, but of equal tenacity with

[031-163]the Geraldines. They first seem to have attached themselves to the

[031-164]Marshals, for whom they were indebted for their first holding in

[031-165]Kilkenny. At the Conference of Castledermot, Theobald Butler, the

[031-166]fourth in descent from the founder of the house, was numbered among the

[031-167]adherents of de Burgh, but a few years later we find him the ally of

[031-168]the Geraldines in the invasion of Thomond. In the year 1247, the title

[031-169]of Lord of Carrick had been conferred on him, which in 1315 was

[031-170]converted into Earl of Carrick, and this again into that of Ormond. The

[031-171]Butlers of this house, when they had attained their growth of power,

[031-172]became the hereditary rivals of the Kildare Geraldines, whose earldom

[031-173]dates from 1316, as that of Ormond does from 1328, and Desmond from

[031-174]1329.

[031-175]The name of Maurice, the third Baron of Offally, and uncle of John, the

[031-176]first Earl of Kildare, draws our attention naturally to the last

[031-177]enterprise of his life—the attempt to establish his son-in-law, Thomas

[031-178]de Clare, in possession of Thomond. The de Clares, Earls of Gloucester,

[031-179]pretended a grant from Henry II. of the whole of Thomond, as their

[031-180]title to invade that principality; but their real grant was bestowed by

[031-181]Edward I., in the year 1275. The state of the renowned patrimony of

[031-182]Brian had long seemed to invite such an aggression. Murtogh, son of

[031-183]Donnell More, who succeeded his father in 1194, had early signalized

[031-184]himself by capturing the castles of Birr, Kinnetty, Ballyroane and

[031-185]Lothra, in Leix, and razing them to the ground. But these castles were

[031-186]reconstructed in 1213, when the feuds between the rival

[031-187]O'Briens—Murtogh and Donogh Cairbre—had paralyzed the defence force of

[031-188]Thomond. It was, doubtless, in the true divide-and-conquer spirit, that

[031-189]Henry the Third's advisers confirmed to Donogh the lordship of Thomond

[031-190]in 1220, leaving to his elder brother the comparatively barren title of

[031-191]King of Munster. Both brothers, by alternately working on their hopes

[031-192]and fears, were thus for many years kept in a state of dependence on

[031-193]the foreigner. One gleam of patriotic virtue illumines the annals of

[031-194]the house of O'Brien, during the first forty years of the century—when,

[031-195]in the year 1225, Donogh Cairbre assisted Felim O'Conor to resist the

[031-196]Anglo-Norman army, then pouring over Connaught, in the quarrel of de

[031-197]Burgh. Conor, the son of Donogh, who succeeded his father in the year

[031-198]1242, animated by the example of his cotemporaries, made successful war

[031-199]against the invaders of his Province, more especially in the year 1257,

[031-200]and the next year; attended with O'Conor the meeting at Beleek, on the

[031-201]Erne, where Brian O'Neil was acknowledged, by both the Munster and the

[031-202]Connaught Prince, as _Ard-Righ_. The untimely end of this attempt at

[031-203]national union will be hereafter related; meantime, we proceed to

[031-204]mention that, in 1260, the Lord of Thomond defeated the Geraldines and

[031-205]their Welsh auxiliaries, at Kilbarran, in Clare. He was succeeded the

[031-206]following season by his son, Brian Roe, in whose time Thomas de Clare

[031-207]again put to the test of battle his pretensions to the lordship of

[031-208]Thomond.

[031-209]It was in the year 1277, that, supported by his father-in-law, the

[031-210]Kildare Fitzgerald, de Clare marched into Munster, and sought an

[031-211]interview with the O'Brien. The relation of gossip, accounted sacred

[031-212]among the Irish, existed between them, but Brien Roe, having placed

[031-213]himself credulously in the hands of his invaders, was cruelly drawn to

[031-214]pieces between two horses. All Thomond rose in arms, under Donogh, son

[031-215]of Brian, to revenge this infamous murder. Near Ennis the Normans met a

[031-216]terrible defeat, from which de Clare and Fitzgerald fled for safety

[031-217]into the neighbouring Church of Quin. But Donogh O'Brien burned the

[031-218]Church over their heads, and forced them to surrender at discretion.

[031-219]Strange to say they were held to ransom, on conditions, we may suppose,

[031-220]sufficiently hard. Other days of blood were yet to decide the claims of

[031-221]the family of de Clare. In 1287, Turlogh, then the O'Brien, defeated an

[031-222]invasion similar to the last, in which Thomas de Clare was slain,

[031-223]together with Patrick Fitzmaurice of Kerry, Richard Taafe, Richard

[031-224]Deriter, Nicholas Teeling, and other knights, and Gerald, the fourth

[031-225]Baron of Offally, brother-in-law to de Clare, was mortally wounded.

[031-226]After another interval, Gilbert de Clare, son of Thomas, renewed the

[031-227]contest, which he bequeathed to his brother Richard. This Richard,

[031-228]whose name figures more than his brother's in the events of his time,

[031-229]made a last effort, in the year 1318, to make good the claims of his

[031-230]family. On the 5th of May, in that year, he fell in battle against

[031-231]McCarthy and O'Brien, and there fell with him Sir Thomas de Naas, Sir

[031-232]Henry Capell, Sir James and Sir John Caunton, with four other knights,

[031-233]and a proportion of men-at-arms. From thenceforth that proud offshoot

[031-234]of the house of Gloucester, which, at its first settling in Munster,

[031-235]flourished as bravely as the Geraldines themselves, became extinct in

[031-236]the land.

[031-237]Such were the varying fortunes of the two races in Leinster and

[031-238]Munster, and such the men who rose and fell. We must now turn to the

[031-239]contest as maintained at the same period in Meath and Ulster.
