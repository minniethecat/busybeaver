CHAPTER I.

[035-001]THE RISE OF "THE RED EARL"—RELATIONS OF IRELAND AND SCOTLAND.

[035-002]During the half century which comprised the reigns of Edward I. and II.

[035-003]in England (A.D. 1272 to 1327), Scotland saw the last of her first race

[035-004]of Kings, and the elevation of the family of Bruce, under whose

[035-005]brilliant star Ireland was, for a season, drawn into the mid-current of

[035-006]Scottish politics. Before relating the incidents of that revolution of

[035-007]short duration but long enduring consequences, we must note the rise to

[035-008]greatness of the one great Norman name, which in that era mainly

[035-009]represented the English interest and influence in Ireland.

[035-010]Richard de Burgh, called from his ruddy complexion "the Red Earl" of

[035-011]Ulster, nobly bred in the court of Henry III. of England, had attained

[035-012]man's age about the period when the de Lacys, the Geraldines, de

[035-013]Clares, and other great Anglo-Irish, families, either through the

[035-014]fortune of war or failure of issue, were deprived of most of their

[035-015]natural leaders. Uniting in his own person the blood of the O'Conors,

[035-016]de Lacys, and de Burghs, his authority was great from the beginning in

[035-017]Meath and Connaught. In his inroads on West-Meath he seems to have been

[035-018]abetted by the junior branches of the de Lacys, who were with his host

[035-019]in the year 1286, when he besieged Theobald de Verdon in Athlone, and

[035-020]advanced his banner as far eastward as the strong town of Trim, upon

[035-021]the Boyne. Laying claim to the possessions of the Lord of Meath, which

[035-022]touched the Kildare Geraldines at so many points, he inevitably came

[035-023]into contact with that powerful family. In 1288, in alliance with Manus

[035-024]O'Conor, they compelled him to retreat from Roscommon into

[035-025]Clanrickarde, in Mayo. De Verdon, his competitor for West-Meath,

[035-026]naturally entered into alliance with the Kildare Geraldine, and in the

[035-027]year 1294, after many lesser conflicts, they took the Red Earl and his

[035-028]brother William prisoners, and carried them in fetters to the Castle of

[035-029]Lea, in Offally. This happened on the 6th day of December; a Parliament

[035-030]assembled at Kilkenny on the 12th of March following, ordered their

[035-031]release; and a peace was made between these powerful houses. De Burgh

[035-032]gave his two sons as hostages to Fitzgerald, and the latter surrendered

[035-033]the Castle of Sligo to de Burgh. From the period of this peace the

[035-034]power of the last named nobleman outgrew anything that had been known

[035-035]since the Invasion. In the year 1291, he banished the O'Donnell out of

[035-036]his territory, and set up another of his own choosing; he deposed one

[035-037]O'Neil and raised up another; he so straitened O'Conor in his patrimony

[035-038]of Roscommon, that that Prince also entered his camp at Meelick, and

[035-039]gave him hostages. He was thus the first and only man of his race who

[035-040]had ever had in his hand the hostages both of Ulster and Connaught.

[035-041]When the King of England sent writs into Ireland, he usually addressed

[035-042]the Red Earl, before the Lord Justice or Lord Deputy—a compliment

[035-043]which, in that ceremonious age, could not be otherwise than flattering

[035-044]to the pride of de Burgh. Such was the order of summons, in which, in

[035-045]the year 1296, he was required by Edward I. to attend him into

[035-046]Scotland, which was then experiencing some of the worst consequences of

[035-047]a disputed succession. As Ireland's interest in this struggle becomes

[035-048]in the sequel second only to that of Scotland, we must make brief

[035-049]mention of its origin and progress.

[035-051]By the accidental death of Alexander III., in 1286, the McAlpine, or

[035-052]Scoto-Irish dynasty, was suddenly terminated. Alexander's only

[035-053]surviving child, Margaret, called from her mother's country, "the Maid

[035-054]of Norway," soon followed her father; and no less than eight

[035-055]competitors, all claiming collateral descent from the former Kings,

[035-056]appeared at the head of as many factions to contest the succession.

[035-057]This number was, however, soon reduced to two men—John Baliol and

[035-058]Robert Bruce—the former the grandson of the eldest, the latter the son

[035-059]of the second daughter of King David I. After many bickerings these

[035-060]powerful rivals were induced to refer their claims to the decision of

[035-061]Edward I. of England, who, in a Great Court held at Berwick in the year

[035-062]1292, decided in favour of Baliol, not in the character of an

[035-063]indifferent arbitrator, but as lord paramount of Scotland. As such,

[035-064]Baliol there and then rendered him feudal homage, and became, in the

[035-065]language of the age, "his man." This sub-sovereignty could not but be

[035-066]galling to the proud and warlike nobles of Scotland, and accordingly,

[035-067]finding Edward embroiled about his French possessions, three years

[035-068]after the decision, they caused Baliol to enter into an alliance,

[035-069]offensive and defensive, with Philip IV. of France, against his English

[035-070]suzerain. The nearer danger compelled Edward to march with 40,000 men,

[035-071]which he had raised for the war in France, towards the Scottish border,

[035-072]whither he summoned the Earl of Ulster, the Geraldines, Butlers, de

[035-073]Verdons, de Genvilles, Berminghams, Poers, Purcells, de Cogans, de

[035-074]Barrys, de Lacys, d'Exeters, and other minor nobles, to come to him in

[035-075]his camp early in March, 1296. The Norman-Irish obeyed the call, but

[035-076]the pride of de Burgh would not permit him to embark in the train of

[035-077]the Lord Justice Wogan, who had been also summoned; he sailed with his

[035-078]own forces in a separate fleet, having conferred the honour of

[035-079]knighthood on thirty of his younger followers before embarking at

[035-080]Dublin. Whether these forces arrived in time to take part in the bloody

[035-081]siege of Berwick, and the panic-route at Dunbar, does not appear; they

[035-082]were in time, however, to see the strongest places in Scotland yielded

[035-083]up, and John Baliol a prisoner on his way to the Tower of London. They

[035-084]were sumptuously entertained by the conqueror in the Castle of

[035-085]Roxburgh, and returned to their western homes deeply impressed with the

[035-086]power of England, and the puissance of her warrior-king.

[035-087]But the independence of Scotland was not to be trodden out in a single

[035-088]campaign. During Edward's absence in France, William Wallace and other

[035-089]guerilla chiefs arose, to whom were soon united certain patriot nobles

[035-090]and bishops. The English deputy de Warrane fought two unsuccessful

[035-091]campaigns against these leaders, until his royal master, having

[035-092]concluded peace with France, summoned his Parliament to meet him at

[035-093]York, and his Norman-Irish lieges to join him in his northern camp,

[035-094]with all their forces, on the 1st of March, 1299. In June the English

[035-095]King found himself at Roxburgh, at the head of 8,000 horse, and 80,000

[035-096]foot, "chiefly Irish and Welsh." With this immense force he routed

[035-097]Wallace at Falkirk on the 22nd of July, and reduced him to his original

[035-098]rank of a guerilla chief, wandering with his bands of partizans from

[035-099]one fastness to another. The Scottish cause gained in Pope Boniface

[035-100]VII. a powerful advocate soon after, and the unsubdued districts

[035-101]continued to obey a Regency composed of the Bishop of St. Andrews,

[035-102]Robert Bruce, and John Comyn. These regents exercised their authority

[035-103]in the name of Baliol, carried on negotiations with France and Rome,

[035-104]convoked a Parliament, and, among other military operations, captured

[035-105]Stirling Castle. In the documentary remains of this great controversy,

[035-106]it is curious to find Edward claiming the entire island of Britain in

[035-107]virtue of the legend of Brute the Trojan, and the Scots rejecting it

[035-108]with scorn, and displaying their true descent and origin from Scota,

[035-109]the fabled first mother of the Milesian Irish. There is ample evidence

[035-110]that the claims of kindred were at this period keenly felt by the Gael

[035-111]of Ireland, for the people of Scotland, and men of our race are

[035-112]mentioned among the companions of Wallace and the allies of Bruce. But

[035-113]the Norman-Irish were naturally drawn to the English banner, and when,

[035-114]in 1303, it was again displayed north of the Tweed, the usual noble

[035-115]names are found among its followers. In 1307 Scotland lost her most

[035-116]formidable foe, by the death of Edward, and at the same time began to

[035-117]recognize her appointed deliverer in the person of Robert Bruce. But we

[035-118]must return to "the Red Earl," the central figure in our own annals

[035-119]during this half century.

[035-120]The new King, Edward II., compelled by his English barons to banish his

[035-121]minion, Gaveston, Earl of Cornwall, had created him his lieutenant of

[035-122]Ireland, endowed him with a grant of the royalties of the whole island,

[035-123]to the prejudice of the Earl and other noblemen. The sojourn of this

[035-124]brilliant parasite in Ireland lasted but a year—from June, 1308, till

[035-125]the June following. He displayed both vigour and munificence, and

[035-126]acquired friends. But the Red Earl, sharing to the full the antipathy

[035-127]of the great barons of England, kept apart from his court, maintained a

[035-128]rival state at Trim, as Commander-in-Chief, conferring knighthood,

[035-129]levying men, and imposing taxes at his own discretion. A challenge of

[035-130]battle is said to have passed between him and the Lieutenant, when the

[035-131]latter was recalled into England by the King, where he was three years

[035-132]later put to death by the barons, into whose hands he had fallen. Sir

[035-133]John Wogan and Sir Edmund Butler succeeded him in the Irish

[035-134]administration; but the real power long remained with Richard de Burgh.

[035-135]He was appointed plenipotentiary to treat with Robert Bruce, on behalf

[035-136]of the King of England, "upon which occasion the Scottish deputies

[035-137]waited on him in Ireland." In the year 1302 Bruce had married his

[035-138]daughter, the Lady Ellen, while of his other daughters one was Countess

[035-139]of Desmond, and another became Countess of Kildare in 1312. A thousand

[035-140]marks—the same sum at which the town and castle of Sligo were then

[035-141]valued—was allowed by the Earl for the marriage portion of his

[035-142]last-mentioned daughter. His power and reputation, about the period of

[035-143]her marriage, were at the full. He had long held the title of Commander

[035-144]of the Irish forces, "in Ireland, Scotland, Wales, and Gascony;" he had

[035-145]successfully resisted Gaveston in the meridian of his court favour; the

[035-146]father-in-law of a King, and of Earls of almost royal power, lord

[035-147]paramount of half the island—such a subject England had not seen on

[035-148]Irish ground since the Invasion. This prodigious power he retained, not

[035-149]less by his energy than his munificence. He erected castles at

[035-150]Carlingford, at Sligo, on the upper Shannon, and on Lough Foyle. He was

[035-151]a generous patron of the Carmelite Order, for whom he built the Convent

[035-152]of Loughrea. He was famed as a princely entertainer, and before

[035-153]retiring from public affairs, characteristically closed his career with

[035-154]a magnificent banquet at Kilkenny, where the whole Parliament were his

[035-155]guests. Having reached an age bordering upon fourscore he retired to

[035-156]the Monastery of Athassil, and there expired within sight of his family

[035-157]vault, after half a century of such sway as was rarely enjoyed in that

[035-158]age, even by Kings. But before that peaceful close he was destined to

[035-159]confront a storm the like of which had not blown over Ireland during

[035-160]the long period since he first began to perform his part in the affairs

[035-161]of that kingdom.
