CHAPTER VIII.

[016-001]LATTER DAYS OF THE NORTHMEN IN IRELAND.

[016-002]Though Ireland dates the decay of Scandinavian power from Good Friday,

[016-003]1014, yet the North did not wholly cease to send forth its warriors,

[016-004]nor were the shores of the Western Island less tempting to them than

[016-005]before. The second year after the battle of Clontarf, Canute founded

[016-006]his Danish dynasty in England, which existed in no little splendour

[016-007]during thirty-seven years. The Saxon line was restored by Edward "the

[016-008]Confessor;" in the forty-third year of the century, only to be

[016-009]extinguished for ever by the Norman conquest twenty-three years later.

[016-010]Scotland, during the same years was more than once subject to invasion

[016-011]from the same ancient enemy. Malcolm II., and the brave usurper

[016-012]Macbeth, fought several engagements with the northern leaders, and

[016-013]generally with brilliant success. By a remarkable coincidence, the

[016-014]Scottish chronicles also date the decadence of Danish power on their

[016-015]coasts from 1014, though several engagements were fought in Scotland

[016-016]after that year.

[016-017]Malachy II. had promptly followed up the victory of Clontarf by the

[016-018]capture of Dublin, the destruction of its fort, and the exemplary

[016-019]chastisement of the tribes of Leinster, who had joined Maelmurra as

[016-020]allies of the Danes. Sitrick himself seems to have eluded the

[016-021]suspicions and vengeance of the conquerors by a temporary exile, as we

[016-022]find in the succession of the Dublin Vikings, "one Hyman, an usurper,"

[016-023]entered as ruling "part of a year while Sitrick was in banishment." His

[016-024]family interest, however, was strong among the native Princes, and

[016-025]whatever his secret sympathies may have been, he had taken no active

[016-026]part against them in the battle of Clontarf. By his mother, the Lady

[016-027]Gormley of Offally, he was a half O'Conor; by marriage he was

[016-028]son-in-law of Brian, and uterine brother of Malachy. After his return

[016-029]to Dublin, when, in 1018, Brian, son of Maelmurra, fell prisoner into

[016-030]his hands, as if to clear himself of any lingering suspicion of an

[016-031]understanding with that family, he caused his eyes to be put out—a

[016-032]cruel but customary punishment in that age. This act procured for him

[016-033]the deadly enmity of the warlike mountaineers of Wicklow, who, in the

[016-034]year 1022, gave him a severe defeat at Delgany. Even this he outlived,

[016-035]and died seven years later, the acknowledged lord of his town and

[016-036]fortress, forty years after his first accession to that title. He was

[016-037]succeeded by his son, grandson, and great-grandson during the remaining

[016-038]half century.

[016-039]The kingdom of Leinster, in consequence of the defeat of Maelmurra, the

[016-040]incapacity of Brian, and the destruction of other claimants of the same

[016-041]family, passed to the family of McMurrogh, another branch of the same

[016-042]ancestry. Dermid, the first and most distinguished King of Leinster of

[016-043]this house, took Waterford (A.D. 1037), and so reduced its strength,

[016-044]that we find its hosts no longer formidable in the field. Those of

[016-045]Limerick continued their homage to the house of Kinkora, while the

[016-046]descendants of Sitrick recognised Dermid of Leinster as their

[016-047]sovereign. In short, all the Dano-Irish from thenceforward began to

[016-048]knit themselves kindly to the soil, to obey the neighbouring Princes,

[016-049]to march with them to battle, and to pursue the peaceful calling of

[016-050]merchants, upon sea. The only peculiarly _Danish_ undertaking we hear

[016-051]of again, in our Annals, was the attempt of a united fleet, equipped by

[016-052]Dublin, Wexford, and Waterford, in the year 1088, to retake Cork from

[016-053]the men of Desmond, when they were driven with severe loss to their

[016-054]ships. Their few subsequent expeditions were led abroad, into the

[016-055]Hebrides, the Isle of Man, or Wales, where they generally figure as

[016-056]auxiliaries or mercenaries in the service of local Princes. They appear

[016-057]in Irish battles only as contingents to the native armies—led by their

[016-058]own leaders and recognized as a separate, but subordinate force. In the

[016-059]year 1073, the Dublin Danes did homage to the monarch Thorlogh, and

[016-060]from 1095, until his death (A.D. 1119), they recognized no other lord

[016-061]but Murkertach More O'Brien; this king, at their own request, had also

[016-062]nominated one of his family as Lord of the Danes and Welsh of the Isle

[016-063]of Man.

[016-064]The wealth of these Irish-Danes, before and after the time of Brian,

[016-065]may be estimated by the annual tribute which Limerick paid to that

[016-066]Prince—a pipe of red wine for every day in the year. In the year 1029,

[016-067]Olaf, son of Sitrick, of Dublin, being taken prisoner by O'Regan, the

[016-068]Lord of East-Meath, paid for his ransom—"twelve hundred cows, seven

[016-069]score British horses, three score ounces of gold!" sixty ounces of

[016-070]white silver as his "fetter-ounce;" the sword of Carlus, besides the

[016-071]usual legal fees, for recording these profitable formalities.

[016-072]Being now Christians, they also began to found and endow churches, with

[016-073]the same liberality with which their Pagan fathers had once enriched

[016-074]the temples of Upsala and Trondheim. The oldest religious foundations

[016-075]in the seaports they possessed owe their origin to them; but even as

[016-076]Christians, they did not lose sight of their nationality. They

[016-077]contended for, and obtained Dano-Irish Bishops, men of their own race,

[016-078]speaking their own speech, to preside over the sees of Dublin,

[016-079]Waterford, and Limerick. When the Irish Synods or Primates asserted

[016-080]over them any supervision which they were unwilling to admit—except in

[016-081]the case of St. Malachy—they usually invoked the protection of the See

[016-082]of Canterbury, which, after the Norman conquest of England, became by

[016-083]far the most powerful Archbishopric in either island.

[016-084]In the third quarter of this century there arose in the Isle of Man a

[016-085]fortunate leader, who may almost be called the last of the sea kings.

[016-086]This was Godard _Crovan_ (the white-handed), son of an Icelandic

[016-087]Prince, and one of the followers of Harald Harfagar and Earl Tosti, in

[016-088]their invasion of Northumbria (A.D. 1066). Returning from the defeat of

[016-089]his chiefs, Godard saw and seized upon Man as the centre of future

[016-090]expeditions of his own, in the course of which he subdued the Hebrides,

[016-091]divided them with the gallant Somerled (ancestor of the MacDonalds of

[016-092]the Isles), and established his son Lagman (afterwards put to death by

[016-093]King Magnus _Barefoot_) as his viceroy in the Orkneys and Shetlands.

[016-094]The weakened condition of the Danish settlement at Dublin attracted his

[016-095]ambition, and where he entered as a mediator he remained as a master.

[016-096]In the succession of the Dublin Vikings he is assigned a reign of ten

[016-097]years, and his whole course of conquest seems to have occupied some

[016-098]twenty years (A.D. 1077 to 1098). At length the star of this Viking of

[016-099]the Irish sea paled before the mightier name of a King of Norway, whose

[016-100]more brilliant ambition had a still shorter span. The story of this

[016-101]_Magnus_ (called, it is said, from his adoption of the Scottish kilt,

[016-102]Magnus _Barefoot_) forms the eleventh Saga in "the Chronicles of the

[016-103]Kings of Norway." He began to reign in the year 1093, and soon after

[016-104]undertook an expedition to the south, "with many fine men, and good

[016-105]shipping." Taking the Orkneys on his way, he sent their Earls prisoners

[016-106]to Norway, and placed his own son, Sigurd, in their stead. He overran

[016-107]the Hebrides, putting Lagman, son of Godard Crovan, to death. He spared

[016-108]only "the holy Island," as Iona was now called, even by the Northmen,

[016-109]and there, in after years, his own bones were buried. The Isles of Man

[016-110]and Anglesea, and the coast of Wales, shared the same fate, and thence

[016-111]he retraced his course to Scotland, where, borne in his galley across

[016-112]the Isthmus of Cantyre, to fulfil an old prophecy, he claimed

[016-113]possession of the land on both sides of Loch Awe. It was while he

[016-114]wintered in the Southern Hebrides, according to the Saga, that he

[016-115]contracted his son Sigurd with the daughter of Murkertach O'Brien,

[016-116]called by the Northmen "Biadmynia." In summer he sailed homeward, and

[016-117]did not return southward till the ninth year of his reign (A.D. 1102),

[016-118]when his son, Sigurd, had come of age, and bore the title of "King of

[016-119]the Orkneys and Hebrides." "He sailed into the west sea," says the

[016-120]Saga, "with the finest men who could be got in Norway. All the powerful

[016-121]men of the country followed him, such as Sigurd Hranesson, and his

[016-122]brother Ulf, Vidkunner Johnsson, Dag Eliffsson, Sorker of Sogn, Eyvind

[016-123]Olboge, the king's marshal, and many other great men." On the

[016-124]intelligence of this fleet having arrived in Irish waters, according to

[016-125]the annals, Murkertach and his allies marched in force to Dublin,

[016-126]where, however, Magnus "made peace with them for one year," and

[016-127]Murkertach "gave his daughter to Sigurd, with many jewels and gifts."

[016-128]That winter Magnus spent with Murkertach at Kinkora, and "towards

[016-129]spring both kings went westward with their army all the way to Ulster."

[016-130]This was one of those annual visitations which kings, whose authority

[016-131]was not yet established, were accustomed to make. The circuit, as

[016-132]usual, was performed in about six weeks, after which the Irish monarch

[016-133]returned home, and Magnus went on board his fleet at Dublin, to return

[016-134]to Norway. According to the Norse account he landed again on the coast

[016-135]of Ulidia (Down), where he expected "cattle for ship-provision," which

[016-136]Murkertach had promised to send him, but the Irish version would seem

[016-137]to imply that he went on shore to seize the cattle perforce. It

[016-138]certainly seems incredible that Murkertach should send cattle to the

[016-139]shore of Strangford Lough, from the pastures of Thomond, when they

[016-140]might be more easily driven to Dublin, or the mouth of the Boyne. "The

[016-141]cattle had not made their appearance on the eve of Bartholomew's Mass"

[016-142](August 23rd, A.D. 1103), says the Saga, so "when the sun rose in the

[016-143]sky, King Magnus himself went on shore with the greater part of his

[016-144]men. King Magnus," continues the scald, "had a helmet on his head; a

[016-145]red shield, in which was inlaid a gilded lion; and was girt with the

[016-146]sword Legbiter, of which the hilt was of ivory, and the hand grip wound

[016-147]about with gold thread; and the sword was extremely sharp. In his hand

[016-148]he had a short spear, and a red silk short cloak over his coat, on

[016-149]which both before and behind was embroidered a lion, in yellow silk;

[016-150]and all men acknowledged that they had never seen a brisker, statelier

[016-151]man." A dust cloud was seen far inland, and the Northmen fell into

[016-152]order of battle. It proved, however, by their own account to be the

[016-153]messengers with the promised supply of cattle; but, after they came up,

[016-154]and while returning to the shore, they were violently assailed on all

[016-155]sides by the men of Down. The battle is described, with true Homeric

[016-156]vigour, by Sturleson. "The Irish," he says, "shot boldly; and although

[016-157]they fell in crowds, there came always two in place of one." Magnus,

[016-158]with most of his nobles, were slain on the spot, but Vidkunner Johnsson

[016-159]escaped to the shipping, "with the King's banner and the sword

[016-160]Legbiter." And the Saga of Magnus Barefoot concludes thus: "Now when

[016-161]King Sigurd heard that his father had fallen, he set off immediately,

[016-162]leaving the Irish King's daughter behind, and proceeded in autumn, with

[016-163]the whole fleet directly to Norway." The annalists of Ulster barely

[016-164]record the fact, that "Magnus, King of Lochlan and the Isles, was slain

[016-165]by the Ulidians, with a slaughter of his people about him, while on a

[016-166]predatory excursion." They place the event in the year 1104.

[016-167]Our account with the Northmen may here be closed. Borne along by the

[016-168]living current of events, we leave them behind, high up on the remoter

[016-169]channels of the stream. Their terrible ravens shall flit across our

[016-170]prospect no more. They have taken wing to their native north, where

[016-171]they may croak yet a little while over the cold and crumbling altars of

[016-172]Odin and Asa Thor. The bright light of the Gospel has penetrated even

[016-173]to those last haunts of Paganism, and the fierce but not ungenerous

[016-174]race, with which we have been so long familiar, begin to change their

[016-175]natures under its benign influence.

[016-177]Although both the scalds and chroniclers of the North frequently refer

[016-178]to Ireland as a favourite theatre of their heroes, we derive little

[016-179]light from those of their works which have yet been made public. All

[016-180]connection between the two races had long ceased, before the first

[016-181]scholars of the North began to investigate the earlier annals of their

[016-182]own country, and then they were content with a very vague and general

[016-183]knowledge of the western Island, for which their ancestors had so

[016-184]fiercely contended throughout so many generations. The oldest maps,

[016-185]known in Scandinavia, exhibit a mere outline of the Irish coast, with a

[016-186]few points in the interior; fiords, with Norse names, are shown,

[016-187]answering to Loughs Foyle, Swilly, Larne, Strang_ford_, and

[016-188]Carling_ford_; the Provincial lines of Ulster and of Connaught are

[016-189]rudely traced; and the situation of Enniskillen, Tara, Dublin,

[016-190]Glendaloch, Water_ford_, Limer_ick_, and Swer_wick_, accurately laid

[016-191]down. It is thought that all those places ending in _wick_ or _ford_,

[016-192]on the Irish map, are of Scandinavian origin; as well as the names of

[016-193]the islets, Skerries, Lambey, and Saltees. Many noble families, as the

[016-194]Plunkets, McIvers, Archbolds, Harolds, Stacks, Skiddies, Cruises, and

[016-195]McAuliffes, are derived from the same origin.

[016-196]During the contest we have endeavoured to describe, three hundred and

[016-197]ten years had passed since the warriors of Lochlin first landed on the

[016-198]shores of Erin. Ten generations, according to the measured span of

[016-199]adult life, were born, and trained to arms and marshalled in battle,

[016-200]since the enemy, "powerful on sea," first burst upon the shield-shaped

[016-201]Isle of Saints. At the close of the eighth century we cast back a

[016-202]grateful retrospect on the Christian ages of Ireland. Can we do so now,

[016-203]at the close of the eleventh? Alas! far from it. Bravely and in the

[016-204]main successfully as the Irish have borne themselves, they come out of

[016-205]that cruel, treacherous, interminable war with many rents and stains in

[016-206]that vesture of innocence in which we saw them arrayed at the close of

[016-207]their third Christian century. Odin has not conquered, but all the

[016-208]worst vices of warfare—its violence, its impiety, discontent,

[016-209]self-indulgence, and contempt for the sweet paths of peace and mild

[016-210]counsels of religion—these must and did remain, long after Dane and

[016-211]Norwegian have for ever disappeared!
