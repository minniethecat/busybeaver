CHAPTER XIX.

[106-001]LAST SESSION OF THE IRISH PARLIAMENT—THE LEGISLATIVE UNION OF GREAT

[106-002]BRITAIN AND IRELAND.

[106-003]When the Irish Parliament met for the last time, on the 15th of

[106-004]January, 1800, the position of the Union question stood thus: 27 new

[106-005]Peers had been added to the House of Lords, where the Castle might

[106-006]therefore reckon with safety on a majority of three to one. Of the

[106-007]Lords spiritual, only Dr. Marlay of Waterford, and Dr. Dixon of Down

[106-008]and Conor, had the courage to side with their country against their

[106-009]order. In the Commons there was an infusion of some 50 new borough

[106-010]members, many of them general officers, such as Needham, and Pakenham,

[106-011]all of them nominees of the Castle, except Mr. Saurin, returned for

[106-012]Blessington, and Mr. Grattan, at the last moment, for Wicklow. The

[106-013]great constitutional body of the bar had, at a general meeting, the

[106-014]previous December, declared against the measure by 162 to 33. Another

[106-015]powerful body, the bankers, had petitioned against it, in the interest

[106-016]of the public credit. The Catholic bishops, in their annual meeting,

[106-017]had taken up a position of neutrality as a body, but under the artful

[106-018]management of Lord Castlereagh, the Archbishops of Dublin and Tuam,

[106-019]with the Bishop of Cork, and some others, were actively employed in

[106-020]counteracting anti-Union movements among the people. Although the vast

[106-021]majority of that people had too much reason to be disgusted and

[106-022]discontented with the legislation of the previous three years, above

[106-023]700,000 of them petitioned against the measure, while all the

[106-024]signatures which could be obtained in its favour, by the use of every

[106-025]means at the command of the Castle, did not much exceed 7,000.

[106-026]The Houses were opened on the 15th of January. The Viceroy not going

[106-027]down, his message was read in the Lords, by the Chancellor, and in the

[106-028]Commons, by the Chief Secretary. It did not directly refer to the basis

[106-029]laid down in England, nor to the subject matter itself; but the leaders

[106-030]of the Castle party in both Houses, took care to supply the deficiency.

[106-031]In the Lords, proxies included, Lord Clare had 75 to 26 for his Union

[106-032]address: in the Commons, Lord Castlereagh congratulated the country on

[106-033]the improvement which had taken place in public opinion, since the

[106-034]former session. He briefly sketched his plan of Union, which, while

[106-035]embracing the main propositions of Mr. Pitt, secured the Church

[106-036]establishment, bid high for the commercial interests, hinted darkly of

[106-037]emancipation to the Catholics, and gave the proprietors of boroughs to

[106-038]understand that their interest in those convenient constituencies would

[106-039]be capitalized, and a good round sum given to buy out their perpetual

[106-040]patronage. In amendment to the address, Sir Lawrence Parsons moved,

[106-041]seconded by Mr. Savage of Down, that the House would maintain _intact_

[106-042]the Constitution of '82, and the debate proceeded on this motion.

[106-043]Ponsonby replied to Castlereagh; Plunkett and Bushe were answered by

[106-044]the future judges, St. George Daly and Luke Fox; Toler contributed his

[106-045]farce, and Dr. Duigenan his fanaticism. Through the long hours of the

[106-046]winter's night the eloquent war was vigorously maintained. One who was

[106-047]himself a distinguished actor in the struggle, (Sir Jonah Barrington,)

[106-048]has thus described it: "Every mind," he says, "was at its stretch,

[106-049]every talent was in its vigour: it was a momentous trial; and never was

[106-050]so general and so deep a sensation felt in any country. Numerous

[106-051]British noblemen and commoners were present at that and the succeeding

[106-052]debate, and they expressed opinions of Irish eloquence which they had

[106-053]never before conceived, nor ever after had an opportunity of

[106-054]appreciating. Every man on that night seemed to be inspired by the

[106-055]subject. Speeches more replete with talent and energy, on both sides,

[106-056]never were heard in the Irish Senate; it was a vital subject. The

[106-057]sublime, the eloquent, the figurative orator, the plain, the connected,

[106-058]the metaphysical reasoner, the classical, the learned, and the solemn

[106-059]declaimer, in a succession of speeches so full of energy and

[106-060]enthusiasm, so interesting in their nature, so important in their

[106-061]consequence, created a variety of sensations even in the bosom of a

[106-062]stranger, and could scarcely fail of exciting some sympathy with a

[106-063]nation which was doomed to close for ever that school of eloquence

[106-064]which had so long given character and celebrity to Irish talent."

[106-066]At the early dawn, a special messenger from Wicklow, just arrived in

[106-067]town, roused Henry Grattan from his bed. He had been elected the

[106-068]previous night for the borough of Wicklow, (which cost him 2,400 pounds

[106-069]sterling), and this was the bearer of the returning officer's

[106-070]certificate. His friends, weak and feeble as he was, wished him to go

[106-071]down to the House, and his heroic wife seconded their appeals. It was

[106-072]seven o'clock in the morning of the 16th when he reached College Green,

[106-073]the scene of his first triumphs twenty years before. Mr. Egan, one of

[106-074]the staunchest anti-Unionists, was at the moment, on some rumour,

[106-075]probably, of his approach, apostrophising warmly the father of the

[106-076]Constitution of '82, when that striking apparition appeared at the bar.

[106-077]Worn and emaciated beyond description, he appeared leaning on two of

[106-078]his friends, Arthur Moore and W. B. Ponsonby. He wore his volunteer

[106-079]uniform, blue with red facings, and advanced to the table, where he

[106-080]removed his cocked hat, bowed to the Speaker, and took the oaths. After

[106-081]Mr. Egan had concluded, he begged permission from his seat beside

[106-082]Plunkett, to address the House sitting, which was granted, and then in

[106-083]a discourse of two hours' duration, full of his ancient fire and

[106-084]vigour, he asserted once again, by the divine right of intellect, his

[106-085]title to be considered the first Commoner of Ireland. Gifted men were

[106-086]not rare in that assembly; but the inspiration of the heart, the

[106-087]uncontrollable utterance of a supreme spirit, not less than the

[106-088]extraordinary faculty of condensation, in which, perhaps, he has never

[106-089]had a superior in our language, gave the Grattan of 1800 the same

[106-090]pre-eminence among his cotemporaries, that was conceded to the Grattan

[106-091]of 1782. After eighteen hours' discussion the division was taken, when

[106-092]the result of the long recess was clearly seen; for the amendment there

[106-093]appeared 96, for the address 138 members. The Union majority,

[106-094]therefore, was 42. It was apparent from that moment that the

[106-095]representation of the people in Parliament had been effectually

[106-096]corrupted; that that assembly was no longer the safeguard of the

[106-097]liberties of the people. Other ministerial majorities confirmed this

[106-098]impression. A measure to enable 10,000 of the Irish militia to enter

[106-099]the regular army, and to substitute English militia in their stead,

[106-100]followed; an inquiry into outrages committed by the sheriff and

[106-101]military in King's county, was voted down; a similar motion somewhat

[106-102]later, in relation to officials in Tipperary met the same fate. On the

[106-103]5th of February, a formal message proposing a basis of Union was

[106-104]received from his Excellency, and debated for twenty consecutive

[106-105]hours—from 4 o'clock of one day, till 12 of the next. Grattan,

[106-106]Plunkett, Parnell, Ponsonby, Saurin, were, as always, eloquent and

[106-107]able, but again the division told for the minister, 160 to 117—majority

[106-108]43. On the 17th of February, the House went into Committee on the

[106-109]proposed articles of Union, and the Speaker (John Foster) being now on

[106-110]the floor, addressed the House with great ability in review of Mr.

[106-111]Pitt's recent Union speech, which he designated "a paltry production."

[106-112]But again, a majority mustered, at the nod of the minister, 161 to

[106-113]140—a few not fully committed showing some last faint spark of

[106-114]independence. It was on this occasion that Mr. Corry, Chancellor of the

[106-115]Exchequer, member for Newry, made for the third or fourth time that

[106-116]session, an attack on Grattan, which brought out, on the instant, that

[106-117]famous "philippic against Corry," unequalled in our language, for its

[106-118]well-suppressed passion, and finely condensed denunciation. A duel

[106-119]followed, as soon as there was sufficient light; the Chancellor was

[106-120]wounded, after which the Castlereagh tactics of "fighting down the

[106-121]opposition," received an immediate and lasting check.

[106-122]Throughout the months of February and March, with an occasional

[106-123]adjournment, the Constitutional battle was fought on every point

[106-124]permitted by the forms of the House. On the 25th of March, the

[106-125]Committee, after another powerful speech from the Speaker, finally

[106-126]reported the resolutions which were passed by 154 to 107—a majority of

[106-127]47. The Houses then adjourned for six weeks, to allow time for

[106-128]corresponding action to be taken in England. There was little

[106-129]difficulty in carrying the measure. In the Upper House, Lords Derby,

[106-130]Holland, and King only opposed it; in the Lower, Sheridan, Tierney,

[106-131]Grey, and Lawrence mustered on a division, 30 votes against Pitt's 206.

[106-132]On the 21st of May, in the Irish Commons, Lord Castlereagh obtained

[106-133]leave to bring in the Union Bill by 160 to 100; on the 7th of June the

[106-134]final passage of the measure was effected. That closing scene has been

[106-135]often described, but never so graphically, as by the diamond pen of

[106-136]Jonah Barrington.

[106-137]"The galleries were full, but the change was lamentable. They were no

[106-138]longer crowded with those who had been accustomed to witness the

[106-139]eloquence and to animate the debates of that devoted assembly. A

[106-140]monotonous and melancholy murmur ran through the benches; scarcely a

[106-141]word was exchanged amongst the members; nobody seemed at ease; no

[106-142]cheerfulness was apparent; and the ordinary business, for a short time,

[106-143]proceeded in the usual manner.

[106-144]"At length, the expected moment arrived: the order of the day for the

[106-145]third reading of the bill for a 'legislative union between Great

[106-146]Britain and Ireland' was moved by Lord Castlereagh. Unvaried, tame,

[106-147]cold-blooded, the words seemed frozen as they issued from his lips;

[106-148]and, as if a simple citizen of the world, he seemed to have no

[106-149]sensation on the subject.

[106-150]"At that moment he had no country, no God, but his ambition. He made

[106-151]his motion, and resumed his seat, with the utmost composure and

[106-152]indifference.

[106-153]"Confused murmurs again ran through the House. It was visibly affected.

[106-154]Every character, in a moment, seemed involuntarily rushing to its

[106-155]index—some pale, some flushed, some agitated—there were few

[106-156]countenances to which the heart did not despatch some messenger.

[106-157]Several members withdrew before the question could be repeated, and an

[106-158]awful, momentary silence succeeded their departure. The Speaker rose

[106-159]slowly from that chair which had been the proud source of his honours

[106-160]and of his high character. For a moment he resumed his seat, but the

[106-161]strength of his mind sustained him in his duty, though his struggle was

[106-162]apparent. With that dignity which never failed to signalize his

[106-163]official actions, he held up the bill for a moment in silence. He

[106-164]looked steadily around him on the last agony of the expiring

[106-165]Parliament. He at length repeated, in an emphatic tone, 'As many as are

[106-166]of opinion that THIS BILL do pass, say _ay_! The affirmative was

[106-167]languid, but indisputable. Another momentary pause ensued. Again his

[106-168]lips seemed to decline their office. At length, with an eye averted

[106-169]from the object he hated, he proclaimed, with a subdued voice, '_The,

[106-170]AYES have it_.' The fatal sentence was now pronounced. For an instant

[106-171]he stood statue-like; then indignantly, and with disgust, flung the

[106-172]bill upon the table, and sank into his chair with an exhausted spirit.

[106-173]An independent country was thus degraded into a province. Ireland, as a

[106-174]nation, was extinguished."

[106-175]The final division in the Commons was 153 to 88, nearly 60 members

[106-176]absenting themselves, and in the Lords, 76 to 17. In England all the

[106-177]stages were passed in July, and on the 2nd of August, the anniversary

[106-178]of the King's accession, the royal assent was given to the twofold

[106-179]legislation, which declared the kingdoms of Great Britain and Ireland

[106-180]one and inseparable!

[106-181]By the provisions of this statute, compact, or treaty, the Sovereignty

[106-182]of the United Kingdom was to follow the order of the Act of Succession;

[106-183]the Irish peerage was to be reduced by the filling of one vacancy for

[106-184]every three deaths, to the number of one hundred; from among these,

[106-185]twenty-eight representative Peers were to be elected for life, and four

[106-186]spiritual Lords to sit in succession. The number of Irish

[106-187]representatives in the Imperial Parliament was fixed at one hundred

[106-188](increased to one hundred and five); the churches of England and

[106-189]Ireland were united like the kingdoms, and declared to be one in

[106-190]doctrine and discipline. The debt of Ireland, which was less than

[106-191]4,000,000 pounds in 1797, increased to 14,000,000 pounds in '99, and

[106-192]had risen to nearly 17,000,000 pounds in 1801, was to be alone

[106-193]chargeable to Ireland, whose proportionate share of general taxation

[106-194]was then estimated at 2-17ths of that of the United Kingdom. The Courts

[106-195]of Law, the Privy Council, and the Viceroyalty, were to remain at

[106-196]Dublin, the cenotaph and the shadows of departed nationality.

[106-197]On the 1st day of January, 1801, in accordance with this great

[106-198]Constitutional change, a new imperial standard was run up on London

[106-199]Tower, Edinburgh Castle, and Dublin Castle. It was formed of the three

[106-200]crosses of St. Patrick, Saint Andrew, and Saint George, and is that

[106-201]popularly known to us as "the Union Jack." The _fleur de lis_, and the

[106-202]word "France," were struck from the royal title, which was settled, by

[106-203]proclamation, to consist henceforth of the words _Dei Gratia,

[106-204]Britanniarum Rex, Fidei Defensor_.

[106-205]The foul means by which this counter revolution was accomplished, have,

[106-206]perhaps, been already sufficiently indicated. It may be necessary,

[106-207]however, in order to account for the continued hostility of the Irish

[106-208]people to the measure, after more than sixty years' experience of its

[106-209]results, to recapitulate them very briefly. Of all who voted for the

[106-210]Union, in both Houses, it was said that only six or seven were known to

[106-211]have done so on conviction. Great borough proprietors, like Lord Ely

[106-212]and Lord Shannon, received as much as 45,000 pounds sterling in

[106-213]"compensation" for their loss of patronage; while proprietors of single

[106-214]seats received 15,000 pounds. That the majority was avowedly purchased,

[106-215]in both Houses, is no longer matter of inference, nay, that some of

[106-216]them were purchased twice over is now well known. Lord Carysfort, an

[106-217]active partisan of the measure, writing in February, 1800, to his

[106-218]friend the Marquis of Buckingham, frankly says: "The majority, which

[106-219]has been bought at an enormous price, must be bought over again,

[106-220]perhaps more than once, before all the details can be gone through."

[106-221]His lordship himself, and the order to which he belonged, and those who

[106-222]aspired to enter it, were, it must be added, among the most insatiable

[106-223]of these purchased supporters. The Dublin _Gazette_ for July, 1800,

[106-224]announced not less than sixteen new peerages, and the same publication

[106-225]for the last week of the year, contained a fresh list of twenty-six

[106-226]others. Forty-two creations in six months was a stretch of prerogative

[106-227]far beyond the most arbitrary of the Stuarts or Tudors, and forms one,

[106-228]not of the least unanswerable evidences, of the utterly corrupt

[106-229]considerations which secured the support of the Irish majority in both

[106-230]Houses.

[106-231]It was impossible that a people like the Irish, disinterested and

[106-232]unselfish to a fault, should ever come to respect a compact brought

[106-233]about by such means and influences as these. Had, however, the Union,

[106-234]vile as were the means by which it was accomplished, proved to the real

[106-235]benefit of the country—had equal civil and religious rights been freely

[106-236]and at once extended to the people of the lesser kingdom—there is no

[106-237]reason to doubt that the measure would have become popular in time, and

[106-238]the vices of the old system be better remembered than its benefits,

[106-239]real or imaginary. But the Union was never utilized for Ireland; it

[106-240]proved in reality what Samuel Johnson had predicted, when spoken of in

[106-241]his day: "Do not unite with us, sir," said the gruff old moralist to an

[106-242]Irish acquaintance; "it would be the union of the shark with his prey;

[106-243]we should unite with you only to destroy you."

[106-244]In glancing backward over the long political connexion of Ireland and

[106-245]England, we mark four great epochs. The Anglo-Norman invasion in 1169;

[106-246]the statute of Kilkenny decreeing eternal separation between the races,

[106-247]"the English pale" and "the Irish enemy," 1367; the Union of the

[106-248]Crowns, in 1541, and the Legislative Union, in 1801. One more cardinal

[106-249]event remains to be recorded—the Emancipation of the Catholics, in

[106-250]1829.
