Flan of the Shannon — also known as Flan Siona (or Flann Sinna) — was High King of Ireland from AD 877 to 916. His reign of thirty-eight years carried Ireland far into the tenth century, and though marked by military campaigns in Munster and Connaught, its generally peaceful tenor earned him an enduring place in Irish memory.

## Family and Connections

Flan was the son of Maelseachlainn I ([[Malachy I]]) and the daughter of Dungal, Prince of Ossory, placing him in the line of the Southern Uí Néill. (011-033) Upon succeeding Hugh VII in 877, he strengthened his position through strategic marriages. He married Maelmara — whose name means "follower of Mary" — the daughter of Kenneth M'Alpine, King of the Caledonian Scots. (011-031) He also gave his daughter Gormley, celebrated for her beauty, her talents, and her heartlessness, in marriage to Niall Glundubh, who became his successor and _Roydamna_. (011-039)

These family ties — uniting Flan with Ossory, with the Scots of Alba, and with his heir — did much to shape the wars and politics of his reign. (011-043)

## Military Campaigns

The first act of Flan Siona on his accession was to dash into Munster, demanding hostages at the point of the sword and sweeping over both Thomond and Desmond with irresistible force, from Clare to the sea. (011-091) His principal domestic difficulty followed from the elevation of Cormac mac Cuillenáin from the See of Emly to the throne of Cashel — a scholar-king whose ambitions clashed with Flan's authority. (011-067)

Flan experienced greater difficulty with the tribes of Connaught, and it was not until the thirteenth year of his reign that Cathal, their prince, "came into his house" in Meath and submitted. (011-104)

In 885, Flan was repulsed in an attack on Dublin, in which the Abbots of Kildare and Kildalkey were slain. (011-172) Yet the Viking pressure during his reign was relatively light: the exploits of Harald Haarfager upon the Baltic drew the usual northern force away from Irish waters during Flan's lifetime. (011-167)

## Family Tragedy

The closing days of Flan of the Shannon were embittered and darkened by the unnatural rebellion of his sons, Connor and Donogh, and his successor Niall Glundubh, the husband of his daughter Gormley. (011-191) At the age of three score and upwards, Flan was frequently obliged to protect his mensal lands in Meath by recourse to arms. His daughter Gormley, wedded to a husband in arms against her father, proved as little dutiful as his sons. (011-200)

At length, in the 37th year of his reign and the 68th of his age, King Flan was at the end of his sorrows. As became the prevailing character of his life, he died peacefully in a religious house at Kyneigh. (011-208)

## Legacy

Flan of the Shannon's long reign stabilised the High Kingship at a critical juncture between the Viking age of Turgesius and the resurgence of Norse power in the mid-10th century. His thirty-eight years on the throne, his success in bringing Munster and Connaught to submission, and his generally peaceful rule endeared his memory to later generations. (011-226) His son Donogh II reigned for twenty-five years after the brief kingship of Niall Glundubh, extending the dynasty's hold on power. (012-005)
