CHAPTER IV.

[051-001]ADHESION OF O'NEIL, O'DONNELL AND O'BRIEN—A NEW ANGLO-IRISH PEERAGE—NEW

[051-002]RELATIONS OF LORD AND TENANT—BISHOPS APPOINTED BY THE CROWN—RETROSPECT.

[051-003]The Act of Election could hardly be considered as the Act of the Irish

[051-004]nation, so long as several of the most distinguished chiefs withheld

[051-005]their concurrence. With these, therefore, Saint Leger entered into

[051-006]separate treaties, by separate instruments, agreed upon, at various

[051-007]dates, during the years 1542 and 1543. Manus O'Donnell, lord of

[051-008]Tyrconnell, gave in his adhesion in August, 1541, Con O'Neil, lord of

[051-009]Tyrowen, Murrogh O'Brien, lord of Thomond, Art O'Moore, lord of Leix,

[051-010]and Ulick Burke, lord of Clanrickarde, 1542 and 1543; but, during the

[051-011]reign of Henry, no chief of the McCarthys, the O'Conors of Roscommon or

[051-012]of Offally, entered into any such engagement. The election, therefore,

[051-013]was far from unanimous, and Henry VIII. would perhaps be classed by our

[051-014]ancient Senachies among the "Kings with opposition," who figure so

[051-015]often in our Annals during the Middle Ages.

[051-016]Assuming, however, the title conferred upon him with no little

[051-017]complacency, Henry proceeded to exercise the first privilege of a

[051-018]sovereign, the creation of honours. Murrogh O'Brien, chief of his name,

[051-019]became Earl of Thomond, and Donogh, his nephew, Baron of Ibrackan;

[051-020]Ulick McWilliam Burke became Earl of Clanrickarde and Baron of

[051-021]Dunkellin; Hugh O'Donnell was made Earl of Tyrconnell; Fitzpatrick,

[051-022]became Baron of Ossory, and Kavanagh, Baron of Ballyan; Con O'Neil was

[051-023]made Earl of Tyrone, having asked, and been refused, the higher title

[051-024]of Earl of Ulster. The order of Knighthood was conferred on several of

[051-025]the principal attendants, and to each of the new peers the King granted

[051-026]a house in or near Dublin, for their accommodation, when attending the

[051-027]sittings of Parliament.

[051-028]The imposing ceremonial of the transformation of these Celtic chiefs

[051-029]into English Earls has been very minutely described by an eye-witness.

[051-030]One batch were made at Greenwich Palace, after High Mass on Sunday, the

[051-031]1st of July, 1543. The Queen's closet "was richly hanged with cloth of

[051-032]arras and well strawed with rushes," for their robing room. The King

[051-033]received them under a canopy of state, surrounded by his Privy Council,

[051-034]the peers, spiritual and temporal, the Earl of Glencairn, Sir George

[051-035]Douglas, and the other Scottish Commissioners. The Earls of Derby and

[051-036]Ormond led in the new Earl of Thomond, Viscount Lisle carrying before

[051-037]them the sword. The Chamberlain handed his letters patent to the

[051-038]Secretary who read them down to the words _Cincturam gladii_, when the

[051-039]King girt the kneeling Earl, baldric-wise, with the sword, all the

[051-040]company standing. A similar ceremony was gone through with the others,

[051-041]the King throwing a gold chain having a cross hanging to it round each

[051-042]of their necks. Then, preceded by the trumpeters blowing, and the

[051-043]officers at arms, they entered the dining hall, where, after the second

[051-044]course, their titles were proclaimed aloud in Norman-French by Garter,

[051-045]King at Arms. Nor did Henry, who prided himself on his munificence,

[051-046]omit even more substantial tokens of his favour to the new Peers.

[051-047]Besides the town houses near Dublin, before mentioned, he granted to

[051-048]O'Brien all the abbeys and benefices of Thomond, bishoprics excepted;

[051-049]to McWilliam Burke, all the parsonages and vicarages of Clanrickarde,

[051-050]with one-third of the first-fruits, the Abbey of _Via Nova_ and 30

[051-051]pounds a year compensation for the loss of the customs of Galway; to

[051-052]Donogh O'Brien, the Abbey of Ellenegrane, the moiety of the Abbey of

[051-053]Clare, and an annuity of 20 pounds a year. To the new lord of Ossory he

[051-054]granted the monasteries of Aghadoe and Aghmacarte, with the right of

[051-055]holding court lete and market, every Thursday, at his town of Aghadoe.

[051-056]For these and other favours the recipients had been instructed to

[051-057]petition the King, and drafts of such petitions had been drawn up in

[051-058]anticipation of their arrival in England, by some official hand. The

[051-059]petitions are quoted by most of our late historians as their own proper

[051-060]act, but it is quite clear, though willing enough to present them and

[051-061]to accept such gifts, they had never dictated them.

[051-062]In the creation of this Peerage Henry proclaimed, in the most practical

[051-063]manner possible, his determination to assimilate the laws and

[051-064]institutions of Ireland to those of England. And the new made Earls,

[051-065]forgetting their ancient relations to their clans—forgetting, as

[051-066]O'Brien had answered St. Leger's first overtures three years before,

[051-067]"that though he was captain of his nation he was still but one man," by

[051-068]suing out royal patents for their lands, certainly consented to carry

[051-069]out the King's plans. The Brehon law was doomed from the date of the

[051-070]creation of the new Peers at Greenwich, for such a change entailed

[051-071]among its first consequences a complete abrogation of the Gaelic

[051-072]relations of clansman and chief.

[051-073]By the Brehon law every member of a free clan was as truly a proprietor

[051-074]of the tribe-land as the chief himself. He could sell his share, or the

[051-075]interest in it, to any other member of the tribe—the origin, perhaps,

[051-076]of what is now called tenant-right; he could not, however, sell to a

[051-077]stranger without the consent of the tribe and the chief. The stranger

[051-078]coming in under such an arrangement, held by a special tenure, yet if

[051-079]he remained during the time of three lords he became thereby

[051-080]naturalized. If the unnaturalized tenant withdrew of his own will from

[051-081]the land he was obliged to leave all his improvements behind; but if he

[051-082]was ejected he was entitled to get their full value. Those who were

[051-083]immediate tenants of the chief, or of the church, were debarred this

[051-084]privilege of tenant-right, and if unable to keep their holdings were

[051-085]obliged to surrender them unreservedly to the church or the chief. All

[051-086]the tribesmen, according to the extent of their possessions, were bound

[051-087]to maintain the chief's household, and to sustain him, with men and

[051-088]means, in his offensive and defensive wars. Such were, in brief, the

[051-089]land laws in force over three-fourths of the country in the sixteenth

[051-090]century; laws which partook largely of the spirit of an ancient

[051-091]patriarchal justice, but which, in ages of movement, exchange, and

[051-092]enterprise, would have been found the reverse of favourable to

[051-093]individual freedom and national strength. There were not wanting, we

[051-094]may be assured, many minds to whom this truth was apparent so early as

[051-095]the age of Henry VIII. And it may not be unreasonable to suppose that

[051-096]one of the advantages which the chief found in exchanging this

[051-097]patriarchal position for a feudal Earldom would be the greater degree

[051-098]of independence on the will of the tribe, which the new system

[051-099]conferred on him. With the mass of the clansmen, however, for the very

[051-100]same reason, the change was certain to be unpopular, if not odious. But

[051-101]a still more serious change—a change of religion—was evidently

[051-102]contemplated by those Earls who accepted the property of the

[051-103]confiscated religious houses. The receiver of such estates could hardly

[051-104]pretend to belong to the ancient religion of the country.

[051-105]It is impossible to understand Irish history from the reign of Henry

[051-106]VIII. till the fall of James II.—nearly two hundred years—without

[051-107]constantly keeping in mind the dilemma of the chiefs and lords between

[051-108]the requirements of the English Court on the one hand and of the native

[051-109]clans on the other. Expected to obey and to administer conflicting

[051-110]laws, to personate two characters, to speak two languages, to uphold

[051-111]the old, yet to patronize the new order of things; distrusted at Court

[051-112]if they inclined to the people, detested by the people if they leaned

[051-113]towards the Court—a more difficult situation can hardly be conceived.

[051-114]Their perilous circumstances brought forth a new species of Irish

[051-115]character in the Chieftain-Earls of the Tudor and Stuart times. Not

[051-116]less given to war than their forefathers, they were now compelled to

[051-117]study the politician's part, even more than the soldier's. Brought

[051-118]personally in contact with powerful Sovereigns, or pitted at home

[051-119]against the Sydneys, Mountjoys, Chichesters, and Straffords, the

[051-120]lessons of Bacon and Machiavelli found apt scholars in the halls of

[051-121]Dunmanway and Dungannon. The multitude, in the meanwhile, saw only the

[051-122]broad fact that the Chief had bowed his neck to the hated Saxon yoke,

[051-123]and had promised, or would be by and by compelled, to introduce foreign

[051-124]garrisons, foreign judges, and foreign laws, amongst the sons of the

[051-125]Gael. Very early they perceived this; on the adhesion of O'Donnell to

[051-126]the Act of Election, a part of his clansmen, under the lead of his own

[051-127]son, rose up against his authority. A rival McWilliam was at once

[051-128]chosen to the new Earl of Clanrickarde, in the West. Con O'Neil, the

[051-129]first of his race who had accepted an English title, was imprisoned by

[051-130]his son, John the Proud, and died of grief during his confinement.

[051-131]O'Brien found, on his return from Greenwich, half his territory in

[051-132]revolt; and this was the general experience of all Henry's electors.

[051-133]Yet such was the power of the new Sovereign that, we are told in our

[051-134]Annals, at the year 1547—the year of Henry's death—"no one dared give

[051-135]food or protection" to those few patriotic chiefs who still held

[051-136]obstinately out against the election of 1541.

[051-138]The creation of a new peerage coincided in point of time with the first

[051-139]unconditional nomination of new Bishops by the Crown. The Plantagenet

[051-140]Kings, in common with all feudal Princes, had always claimed the right

[051-141]of investing Bishops with their temporalities and legal dignities;

[051-142]while, at the same time, they recognized in the See of Rome the seat

[051-143]and centre of Apostolic authority. But Henry, excommunicated and

[051-144]incorrigible, had procured from the Parliament of "the Pale," three

[051-145]years before the Act of Election, the formal recognition of his

[051-146]spiritual supremacy, under which he proceeded, as often as he had an

[051-147]opportunity, to promote candidates for the episcopacy to vacant sees.

[051-148]Between 1537 and 1547, thirteen or fourteen such vacancies having

[051-149]occurred, he nominated to the succession whenever the diocese was

[051-150]actually within his power. In this way the Sees of Dublin, Kildare,

[051-151]Ferns, Ardagh, Emly, Tuam and Killaloe were filled up; while the

[051-152]vacancies which occurred about the same period in Armagh, Clogher,

[051-153]Clonmacnoise, Clonfert, Kilmore, and Down and Conor were supplied from

[051-154]Rome. Many of the latter were allowed to take possession of their

[051-155]temporalities—so far as they were within English power—by taking an

[051-156]oath of allegiance, specially drawn for them. Others, when prevented

[051-157]from so doing by the penalties of _praemunire_, delegated their

[051-158]authority to Vicars General, who contrived to elude the provisions of

[051-159]the statute. On the other hand, several of the King's Bishops, excluded

[051-160]by popular hostility from the nominal sees, never resided upon them;

[051-161]some of them spent their lives in Dublin, and others were entertained

[051-162]as suffragans by Bishops in England.

[051-163]In March, 1543, Primate Cromer, who had so resolutely led the early

[051-164]opposition to Archbishop Browne, died, whereupon Pope Paul III.

[051-165]appointed Robert Waucop, a Scotsman (by some writers called

[051-166]_Venantius_), to the See of Armagh. This remarkable man, though

[051-167]afflicted with blindness from his youth upwards, was a doctor of the

[051-168]Sorbonne, and one of the most distinguished Prelates of his age. He

[051-169]introduced the first Jesuit Fathers into Ireland, and to him is

[051-170]attributed the establishment of that intimate intercourse between the

[051-171]Ulster Princes and the See of Rome, which characterized the latter half

[051-172]of the century. He assisted at the Council of Trent from 1545 to 1547,

[051-173]was subsequently employed as Legate in Germany, and died abroad during

[051-174]the reign of Edward VI. Simultaneously with the appointment of Primate

[051-175]Waucop, Henry VIII. had nominated to the same dignity George Dowdal, a

[051-176]native of Louth, formerly Prior of the crutched friars at Ardee, in

[051-177]that county. Though Dowdal accepted the nomination, he did so without

[051-178]acknowledging the King's supremacy in spirituals. On the contrary he

[051-179]remained attached to the Holy See, and held his claims in abeyance,

[051-180]during the lifetime of Waucop. On the death of the latter, he assumed

[051-181]his rank, but was obliged to fly into exile, during the reign of

[051-182]Edward. On the accession of Mary he was recalled from his place of

[051-183]banishment in Brabant, and his first official act on returning home was

[051-184]to proclaim a Jubilee for the public restoration of the Catholic

[051-185]worship.

[051-186]The King's Bishops during the last years of Henry, and the brief reign

[051-187]of Edward, were, besides Browne of Dublin, Edward Staples, Bishop of

[051-188]Meath, Matthew Saunders and Robert Travers, successively Bishops of

[051-189]Leighlin, William Miagh and Thomas Lancaster, successively Bishops of

[051-190]Kildare, and John Bale, Bishop of Ossory—all Englishmen. The only

[051-191]native names, before the reign of Elizabeth, which we find associated

[051-192]in any sense with the "reformation," are John Coyn, or Quin, Bishop of

[051-193]Limerick, and Dominick Tirrey, Bishop of Cork and Cloyne. Dr. Quin was

[051-194]promoted to the See in 1522, and resigned his charge in the year 1551.

[051-195]He is called a "favourer" of the new doctrines, but it is not stated

[051-196]how far he went in their support. His successor, Dr. William Casey, was

[051-197]one of the six Bishops deprived by Queen Mary on her accession to the

[051-198]throne. As Bishop Tirrey is not of the number—although he lived till

[051-199]the third year of Mary's reign—we may conclude that he became

[051-200]reconciled to the Holy See.

[051-201]The native population became, before Henry's death, fully aroused to

[051-202]the nature of the new doctrines, to which at first they had paid so

[051-203]little attention. The Commission issued in 1539 to Archbishop Browne

[051-204]and others for the destruction of images and relics, and the prevention

[051-205]of pilgrimages, as well as the ordering of English prayers as a

[051-206]substitute for the Mass, brought home to all minds the sweeping

[051-207]character of the change. Our native Annals record the breaking out of

[051-208]the English schism from the year 1537, though its formal introduction

[051-209]into Ireland may, perhaps, be more accurately dated from the issuing of

[051-210]the Ecclesiastical Commission of 1539. In their eyes it was the

[051-211]offspring of "pride, vain-glory, avarice, and lust," and its first

[051-212]manifestations were well calculated to make it for ever odious on Irish

[051-213]soil. "They destroyed the religious orders," exclaimed the Four

[051-214]Masters! "They broke down the monasteries, and sold their roofs and

[051-215]bells, from Aran of the Saints to the Iccian Sea!" "They burned the

[051-216]images, shrines, and relics of the Saints; they destroyed the Statue of

[051-217]our Lady of Trim, and the Staff of Jesus, which had been in the hand of

[051-218]St. Patrick!" Such were the works of that Commission as seen by the

[051-219]eyes of Catholics, natives of the soil. The Commissioners themselves,

[051-220]however, gloried in their work, and pointed with complacency to their

[051-221]success. The "innumerable images" which adorned the churches were

[051-222]dashed to pieces; the ornaments of shrines and altars, when not

[051-223]secreted in time, were torn from their places, and beaten into

[051-224]shapeless masses of metal. This harvest yielded in the first year

[051-225]nearly 3,000 pounds, on an inventory, wherein we find 1,000 lbs. weight

[051-226]of wax, manufactured into candles and tapers, valued at 20 pounds. Such

[051-227]was the return made to the revenue; what share of the spoil was

[051-228]appropriated by the agents employed may never be known. It would be

[051-229]absurd, however, to expect a scrupulous regard to honesty in men

[051-230]engaged in the work of sacrilege! And this work, it must be added, was

[051-231]carried on in the face of the stipulation entered into with the

[051-232]Parliament of 1541, that "the Church of Ireland shall be free, and

[051-233]enjoy all its accustomed privileges."

[051-234]The death of Henry, in January, 1547, found the Reformation in Ireland

[051-235]at the stage just described. But though all attempts to diffuse a

[051-236]general recognition of his spiritual power had failed, his reign will

[051-237]ever be memorable as the epoch of the union of the English and Irish

[051-238]Crowns. Before closing the present Book of our History, in which we

[051-239]have endeavoured to account for that great fact, and to trace the

[051-240]progress of the negotiations which led to its accomplishment, we must

[051-241]briefly review the relations existing between the Kings of England and

[051-242]the Irish nation, from Henry II. to Henry VIII.

[051-243]If we are to receive a statement of considerable antiquity, a memorable

[051-244]compromise effected at the Council of Constance, between the

[051-245]ambassadors of France and England, as to who should take precedence,

[051-246]turned mainly on this very point. The French monarchy was then at its

[051-247]lowest, the English at its highest pitch, for Charles VI. was but a

[051-248]nominal sovereign of France, while the conqueror of Agincourt sat on

[051-249]the throne of England. Yet in the first assembly of the Prelates and

[051-250]Princes of Europe, we are told that the ambassadors of France raised a

[051-251]question of the right of the English envoys to be received as

[051-252]representing a nation, seeing that they had been conquered not only by

[051-253]the Romans, but by the Saxons. Their argument further was, that, "as

[051-254]the Saxons were tributaries to the German Empire, and never governed by

[051-255]native sovereigns, they [the English] should take place as a branch

[051-256]only of the German empire, and not as a free nation. For," argued the

[051-257]French, "it is evident from Albertus Magnus and Bartholomew Glanville,

[051-258]that the world is divided into three parts, Europe, Asia, and

[051-259]Africa;—that Europe is divided into four empires, the Roman,

[051-260]Constantinopolitan, the Irish, and the Spanish." "The English

[051-261]advocates," we are told, "admitting the force of these allegations,

[051-262]claimed their precedency and rank from Henry's being monarch of

[051-263]Ireland, and it was accordingly granted."

[051-264]If this often-told anecdote is of any historical value, it only shows

[051-265]the ignorance of the representatives of France in yielding their

[051-266]pretensions on so poor a quibble. Neither Henry V., nor any other

[051-267]English sovereign before him, had laid claim to the title of "Monarch

[051-268]of Ireland." The indolence or ignorance of modern writers has led them,

[051-269]it is true, to adopt the whole series of the Plantagenet Kings as

[051-270]sovereigns of Ireland—to set up in history a dynasty which never

[051-271]existed for us; to leave out of their accounts of a monarchical people

[051-272]all question of their crown; and to pass over the election of 1541

[051-273]without adequate, or any inquiry.

[051-274]It is certain that neither Henry II., nor Richard I., ever used in any

[051-275]written instrument, or graven sign, the style of king, or even lord of

[051-276]Ireland; though in the Parliament held at Oxford in the year 1185,

[051-277]Henry conferred on his youngest son, John _lack-land_, a title which he

[051-278]did not himself possess, and John is thenceforth known in English

[051-279]history as "Lord of Ireland." This honour was not, however, of the

[051-280]exclusive nature of sovereignty, else John could hardly have borne it

[051-281]during the lifetime of his father and brother. And although we read

[051-282]that Cardinal Octavian was sent into England by Pope Urban III.,

[051-283]authorized to consecrate John, _King_ of Ireland, no such consecration

[051-284]took place, nor was the lordship looked upon, at any period, as other

[051-285]than a creation of the royal power of England existing in Ireland,

[051-286]which could be recalled, transferred, or alienated, without detriment

[051-287]to the prerogative of the King.

[051-288]Neither had this original view of the relations existing between

[051-289]England and Ireland undergone any change at the time of the Council of

[051-290]Constance. Of this we have a curious illustration in the style employed

[051-291]by the Queen Dowager of Henry V., who, during the minority of her son,

[051-292]granted charters, as "Queen of England and France, and lady of

[051-293]Ireland." The use of different crowns in the coronations of all the

[051-294]Tudors subsequent to Henry VIII. shows plainly how the recent origin of

[051-295]their secondary title was understood and acknowledged during the

[051-296]remainder of the sixteenth century. Nothing of the kind was practised

[051-297]at the coronation of the Plantagenet Princes, nor were the arms of

[051-298]Ireland quartered with those of England previous to the period we have

[051-299]described—the memorable year, 1541.
