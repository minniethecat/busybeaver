## Overview

Roger Boyle, Baron Broghill, later 1st Earl of Orrery (1621–1679), was one of the most accomplished political figures of seventeenth-century Ireland.

The third surviving son of Richard Boyle, the "great Southern Undertaker" and 1st Earl of Cork, Broghill rose to prominence as a Parliamentary commander in Munster during the Cromwellian conquest. He later became a key architect of the Restoration settlement and one of the most powerful men in Ireland under Charles II, exemplifying the fluid loyalties and ruthless pragmatism of the Anglo-Irish Protestant elite during the revolutionary decades.

## Family Background and Early Career

Lord Broghill was "son of the great Southern Undertaker — the first Earl of Cork" (070-123), one of the wealthiest and most ambitious English planters in Ireland. The Earl of Cork had amassed enormous estates in Munster through the plantation system and his offspring — known as "the five brothers" — were strategically placed throughout the English-speaking world. Roger was destined for Ireland.

During the Confederate Wars, Broghill initially sided with the Parliament. In 1644, "a new Parliamentary party had time to form its combinations under the shelter of the cessation. The Earl of Inchiquin, who had lately failed to obtain the Presidency of Munster from the King at Oxford, and the Lord Broghill" were at its head (070-120–070-123). Under pretence that their quarters had been violated, "they contrived to seize upon Cork, Youghal, and Kinsale" (070-126), driving out the Catholic inhabitants and publicly executing a Friar named Father Mathews.

## The Cromwellian Campaign

Broghill proved one of Cromwell's most effective commanders in Ireland. During the 1649–1650 campaign, "Drogheda, Dundalk, Newry, and Carlingford fell in September; Arklow, Enniscorthy, and Wexford in October; Ross surrendered the same month; Waterford was attempted and abandoned in November; Dungarvan, Kinsale, Bandon, and Cork were won over by Lord Broghill in December; Fethard, Callan, and Cashel in January and February; Carrick and Kilkenny in March; and Clonmel, early in May" (073-035–073-040). Broghill's success in winning over the Munster towns without protracted sieges was a critical contribution to the speed of the Cromwellian conquest.

His most celebrated military exploit was his flying column operations. When Lord Muskerry "attempted a diversion in favour of Limerick," he was "repulsed at Castleishen, by 'the flying camp' of Lord Broghill" (074-069). This highly mobile force repeatedly intercepted Confederate attempts to relieve besieged towns or threaten Parliamentary lines of communication.

## The Restoration Settlement

Broghill's finest political hour came after Cromwell's death. During the confused period following the Protectorate's collapse, Broghill positioned himself as an essential bridge between the Cromwellian interest in Ireland and the returning Stuart monarchy.

> The chief of these greater leaders was the accomplished Lord Broghill, whom we are to know during this reign under his more famous title of Earl of Orrery. (076-015–076-016)

Under Charles II, "Coote and Broghill established as Lords Justices" (076-051) governed Ireland for the restored monarchy. Both men — former Parliamentary commanders who had prosecuted the war against the Irish Catholics — now held the highest offices under the King. Coote "became Earl of Mountrath, and Broghill, Earl of Orrery" (074-127), a striking elevation for the sons of English planters.

## The Restoration Parliament and Later Career

As President of Munster and a member of the Irish Privy Council, Orrery exercised immense influence over the Restoration settlement. When Irish Catholic representatives came to Dublin to urge their claims:

> To their dismay, they found in Dublin, Coote and Broghill established as Lords Justices, and the new Parliament — the first that sat for twenty years — composed of an overwhelming majority of Undertakers, adventurers, and Puritan officers. (076-051–076-053)

Unsurprisingly, the settlement largely confirmed the Cromwellian land distribution. In the Irish Council, Broghill served alongside figures such as "Sir Richard Nagle, author of the famous 'Coventry Letter,' of Nugent, afterwards Lord Riverston, and other able men" (076-073–076-074).

Beyond Ireland, Orrery was also a noted playwright and man of letters, author of several heroic plays and romances popular in the Restoration court. His literary reputation earned him the friendship of John Dryden and a place in the cultural life of Charles II's London.

## Legacy

Lord Broghill, Earl of Orrery, died in 1679, having navigated the treacherous waters of civil war, regicide, protectorate, and restoration with remarkable success. His career illustrates how the sons of Elizabethan and Jacobean planters consolidated their power through the Cromwellian conquest and emerged as the dominant political class of Restoration Ireland. The Boyle family remained a force in Irish and British politics for generations.
