**Leinster** (Irish: _Laighin_) is the easternmost of [[Ireland]]'s four historic provinces, bounded by [[Ulster]] to the north, [[Munster]] to the south, [[Connaught]] to the west, and the Irish Sea to the east. In ancient times, it was the territory of the Laigin people, from whom its name derives. The province encompasses the modern counties of Carlow, [[Dublin]], Kildare, Kilkenny, Laois, Longford, Louth, Meath, Offaly, Westmeath, Wexford, and Wicklow. As the seat of [[Dublin]]—the administrative center of English and later British rule in Ireland—Leinster became the most Anglicized of the four provinces and the primary stage upon which the political contests between Gaelic and English authority were played out.

## Ancient Leinster

In pre-Christian Ireland, Leinster was a distinct kingdom ruled by the Laigin dynasty. The province was the principal target of the special tax (_Boruma_) imposed by the Kings of [[Tara]], a levy that McGee describes as a source of recurring conflict:

> "The King of Leinster was originally the owner of the great fortress of Dun Ailinne." (004-050)

The border at Gowran marked a persistent frontier contested between Leinster and [[Munster]]:

> "Leinster border at Gowran; he was 'priced' equal to the King of Tara." (004-057)

## The Boruma Tribute

One of the most enduring traditions in early Irish history was the Boruma (or Boromian) tribute—a heavy tax levied on Leinster by the High Kings of [[Tara]]. This tribute was the cause of numerous wars and is a central theme in the [[ulster|Ulster]] Cycle of tales:

> "The special tribute which Leinster paid to Munster—and which was known as the Boromian tribute—was a heavy burden." (004-083)

> "The oppressive enactment upon Leinster which followed on that event." (004-098)

## Medieval and Modern Leinster

Following the [[Norman Invasion of Ireland]], Leinster became the heartland of the Anglo-Norman colony. The province was granted to [[Strongbow]] ([[strongbow|Richard de Clare]]) through his marriage to Aoife, daughter of Diarmait Mac Murchada, the deposed [[dermid-mcmurrogh|King of Leinster]]. This grant established the basis for the medieval Lordship of Ireland, with [[dublin|Dublin]] as its capital.

The power of the [[Earl of Kildare]] in the 15th and 16th centuries was rooted in their vast Leinster estates, and the province remained the stronghold of English interest in Ireland throughout the Tudor and Stuart periods.

## The Militia of Leinster

In the pre-Christian era, Leinster maintained its own warrior class, the _Fiann_ of Leinster, distinct from the better-known Fianna of [[Ulster]]:

> "The _Fiann_, or Militia of Leinster, distinguished from the Red Branch of Ulster." (002-043)
