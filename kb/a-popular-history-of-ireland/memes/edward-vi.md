## Overview

Edward VI (1537–1553) was King of England and Ireland from 1547 until his death at the age of fifteen. The son of [[Henry VIII]] by Jane Seymour, he was crowned at the age of nine and reigned for only six years and a few months. Despite the brevity of his reign, his government drove forward the Protestant [[reformation|Reformation]] with great vigour, transforming the religious landscape of both England and Ireland. (052-003)

No [[irish-parliament|Irish Parliament]] was convened during Edward's reign, but the [[reformation|Reformation]] was pushed on in Ireland under the patronage of the Protector Somerset, the King's uncle, and subsequently under the Duke of Northumberland. The Lord Deputy Saint Leger proved a willing assistant in the work, judging from the cause of his disgrace in the next reign. (052-003)

## Biography

### Accession and Minority

Edward was born in 1537, the long-awaited male heir of [[henry-viii|Henry VIII]] by his third wife, Jane Seymour. On the last day of January 1547, following Henry's death, Edward was crowned by the title of Edward VI. He was then only nine years old and "was destined to wear the crown but for six years and a few months." The government was conducted in his name by a Regency Council, first under the Protectorate of his uncle, Edward Seymour, Duke of Somerset, and later under John Dudley, Duke of Northumberland. (052-003)

> "On the last day of January, 1547, Edward, son of Henry, by Lady Jane Seymour, was crowned by the title of Edward VI. He was then only nine years old, and was destined to wear the crown but for six years and a few months." (052-003)

### The Reformation Under Edward

Under Edward's authority, Archbishop Thomas Cranmer suffered the zeal of neither Somerset nor Northumberland to flag. The [[reformation|Reformation]] proceeded rapidly: the English Prayer Book was introduced, altars were replaced with communion tables, images were removed from churches, and the doctrine of transubstantiation was formally rejected. (052-003)

In Ireland, the [[reformation|Reformation]] was advanced through administrative decree rather than parliamentary legislation. The Lord Deputy Saint Leger, who "approved himself a willing assistant in the work," implemented the Edwardian reforms. The requirements prescribed by the statutes of Edward's reign became the benchmark for religious conformity, later referenced in the celebrated [[Act of Union|Act of Uniformity]] of the 2nd year of Elizabeth, which prescribed observances "as were prescribed by the 2nd Edward VI." (054-080)

> "No Irish Parliament was convened during his reign, but the Reformation was pushed on with great vigour, at first under the patronage of the Protector, his uncle, and subsequently of that uncle's rival, the Duke of Northumberland." (052-003)

### Religious Appointments in Ireland

The ecclesiastical politics of Edward's reign had lasting consequences for Ireland. Primate Robert Waucop, appointed by Pope Paul III to the See of [[armagh|Armagh]], was one of the most distinguished prelates of his age—a doctor of the Sorbonne who introduced the first Jesuit Fathers into Ireland. He assisted at the Council of Trent and "died abroad during the reign of Edward VI." (051-174)

Simultaneously, [[henry-viii|Henry VIII]] had nominated George Dowdal to the same dignity. Dowdal remained attached to the Holy See despite his nomination, and "was obliged to fly into exile, during the reign of Edward," returning only on the accession of the Catholic Queen Mary. This dual ecclesiastical authority—papal and royal—created a fault line in the Irish Church that persisted throughout the Tudor period. (051-174)

### Death and the Succession

The death of Edward VI and the accession of the Lady Mary were known in [[dublin|Dublin]] by the middle of July 1553, and the news soon spread across the kingdom. A curious legal difficulty existed in relation to Mary's title to the Crown of Ireland: by the Irish Statute 33 [[henry-viii|Henry VIII]], the Irish crown was entailed on the Lady Elizabeth, and that act had not been repealed. It was held, however, to have been superseded by the English Statute 35 [[henry-viii|Henry VIII]], which declared the Crown of Ireland "united and knit to the Imperial Crown of the Realm of England." (053-002)

Mary did not consider the Irish sovereignty a mere appurtenance of the English crown. At her coronation, a separate crown was used for Ireland, and she did not feel assured of the validity of her claim until she had obtained a formal dispensation from the Pope. (053-002)

## The Lambert Simnel Pretender

A separate historical figure also bore the title "Edward VI" in Ireland—the pretender Lambert Simnel, who was crowned in [[dublin|Dublin]] in 1487 with the support of the [[Earl of Kildare]] and the Yorkist faction. Simnel, a joiner's son from Oxford, was presented as the escaped Earl of Warwick and prepared for coronation "by the title of Edward VI." (045-132)

> "He was at once recognized as the son of Clarence by the Earl of Kildare and his party, and preparations were made for his coronation by the title of Edward VI." (045-132)

The expedition that sailed from [[dublin|Dublin]] landed in Lancashire and ended in defeat at the Battle of Stoke-upon-Trent on 16 June 1487. Simnel was captured and spared by Henry VII "only to be made a scullion in his kitchen." (045-166)

## Legacy

Edward VI's reign, though brief, established the Protestant direction of the English state. The religious legislation of his era provided the template for the Elizabethan Settlement, and the Irish Church felt the first systematic pressure towards conformity with English Protestantism during these years.

The legacy of Edward's [[reformation|Reformation]] was ambiguous in Ireland. The administrative imposition of religious change without parliamentary consultation anticipated the pattern of later Tudor governance. When Elizabeth's Parliament met in 1560, its statutes looked back to "the 2nd Edward VI" as the standard of reformed practice—a reminder that the short reign of the boy-king had set the terms of religious debate for a generation. (054-080)
