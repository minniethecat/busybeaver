Justin McCarthy, Viscount Mountcashel, was one of the most distinguished Jacobite military commanders of the Williamite War in Ireland. A member of the powerful McCarthy family of Munster, he rose to the rank of Lieutenant-General in the army of James II and later commanded the first Irish brigade in the service of France. (087-029)

McGee records his career from the early successes in Munster through the defeat at Newtown-Butler to his final command under Marshal Catinat in the campaigns of Savoy and Italy. His life illustrates the trajectory of the Irish Jacobite military diaspora that followed the Treaty of Limerick. (081-006)

## The Williamite War in Munster

When James II landed in Ireland in 1689, Mountcashel was placed in command of Jacobite forces in Munster. He achieved early successes against the Williamite positions, capturing the towns of Bandon and Castlemartyr from their Protestant garrisons. These victories helped secure the southern province for the Jacobite cause in the opening months of the war. (081-006)

> "in Munster, Lieutenant-General Justin McCarthy, Lord Mountcashel, had taken Bandon and Castlemartyr." [081-006]

At the war council in Dublin, Mountcashel sat alongside the principal figures of the Jacobite administration: the Duke of Tyrconnell, Chief Justice Nugent, and the superior officers of the army, both French and Irish. The council resolved to dispatch the gallant Viscount Dundee to Scotland to raise the Highland clans for James. (080-071)

## The Battle of Newtown-Butler

In the summer of 1689, as General De Rosen was recalled from the north, command of the siege of Enniskillen was entrusted to Mountcashel. His force consisted of three regiments of foot, two of dragoons, and some horse — a substantial body of troops. He expected to be reinforced by Colonel [[Patrick Sarsfield]] marching from Sligo with additional cavalry. (081-116)

> "Lord Mountcashel, under whom, as commander of the cavalry, served Count Anthony Hamilton, author of the witty but licentious 'Memoirs of Grammont,' and other distinguished officers." [081-116]

The engagement at Newtown-Butler proved disastrous for the Jacobite cause. Mountcashel's force was routed by the Enniskillen Protestants, and he himself was wounded and taken prisoner. Sarsfield, on his way to join Mountcashel, fell back to Sligo upon learning of the defeat. The loss of the battle was a significant blow to James's position in Ulster. (081-128) (081-137)

McGee notes that the Enniskillen men had earlier delayed the junction of Mountcashel's Royalists with those under Hamilton and De Rosen. The battle effectively ended Jacobite hopes of reducing the western Ulster strongholds. Coleraine had already been abandoned, and the focus of the northern campaign shifted decisively to the great siege of Derry. (081-063)

## The Irish Brigade in French Service

Following his capture and subsequent release, Mountcashel was among the first Irish officers to enter French service. He commanded the vanguard of what became the famous Irish Brigade — the soldiers who left Ireland under the terms of the Treaty of Limerick and fought with distinction in the armies of Louis XIV. At the head of the first Franco-Irish brigade, Mountcashel had been among the earliest to exchange the hopeless struggle at home for the continental battlefields. (082-022) (084-143)

McGee records that the Irish Brigade, with Mountcashel's men as its nucleus, grew to over five thousand strong, augmented by additional regiments under commanders such as Wauchop. They became one of the most renowned fighting forces in Europe. (084-143)

Mountcashel served under Marshal Catinat in the campaigns of Savoy and Italy, facing the celebrated Prince Eugene of Savoy. He died at the baths of Bareges in the Pyrenees from wounds received at the Battle of Staffarda in 1690 — an engagement in which the French and their Irish allies engaged the forces of the Duke of Savoy. (087-029)

> "Justin McCarthy, Lord Mountcashel, who commanded them, died at Bareges of wounds received at Staffardo." [087-029]

His death in French service, far from his native Munster, exemplified the fate of many Irish Jacobite officers. The Irish Brigade he helped to found would continue to serve France for another century, becoming legendary for their courage at battles such as Fontenoy, where they were said to have turned the tide against the British. (087-031)
