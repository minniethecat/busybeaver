## Overview

**Saint Moling** (c. 630--697), of the royal house of [[Leinster]] and Bishop of Fernamore (now [[Ferns]]), was one of the most influential ecclesiastical figures of seventh-century [[Ireland]].

He is best remembered for his successful intercession with King Finnacta the Hospitable to abolish the Borooa tribute, a burdensome annual levy of cattle that had been imposed upon the men of Leinster for generations.

(006-154) (006-155) (006-156) (006-157) His achievement stands alongside those of [[Saint Columba]] and [[Adamnan]] as among the great legislative successes of the early Irish Church. (008-018)

## Biography

### Royal Lineage and Early Life

Moling was born into the royal dynasty of [[Leinster]], a province whose kings had long struggled under the weight of the Borooa tribute.

The precise circumstances of his early years are not recorded in McGee's narrative, but his royal lineage placed him among the ruling elite of Leinster at a time when the province was locked in a bitter contest with the northern monarchs over the exaction of this ancient levy.

(006-156) His noble birth and religious vocation would later converge in his role as the champion of his people's cause before the Ard-Righ.

### Bishop of Ferns

Moling served as Bishop of Fernamore, the see known in later times as [[Ferns]], in the south-eastern part of [[Leinster]]. The diocese of Ferns was, and remains, one of the principal episcopal seats of the province.

McGee lists him among the most eminent Irish saints of the seventh century, alongside Saint Fursey, Saint Laserian of Leighlin, Saint Kieran of Clonmacnoise, Saint Comgall of Bangor, and Saint Colman of Dromore. (008-169) (008-170) (008-171)

His episcopal authority, grounded both in his royal blood and in his spiritual reputation, gave him the standing necessary to confront the monarch of Ireland on a matter of national policy.

### The Abolition of the Borooa Tribute

The defining episode of Moling's public life occurred during the reign of Finnacta the Hospitable, a monarch who reigned for twenty years and is himself commemorated as a saint in the Irish calendar. (006-171) (006-172) The Borooa; a special tax levied by the Ard-Righ upon the men of Leinster; had been a source of bitter contention for generations.

In the early part of his reign, Finnacta seemed disinclined to collect this invidious tax by force; but yielding to other motives, he later marched into Leinster at the head of an army to compel its payment. (006-157) (006-158) (006-159) (006-160)

> Here the holy Prelate of Ferns met him, and related a Vision in which he had been instructed to demand the abolition of the impost. The abolition, he contended, should not be simply a suspension, but final and for ever. (006-160) (006-161) (006-162) (006-163)

The king, confronted by this spiritual authority, relented. The tribute at this period was enormous; 15,000 head of cattle annually; and its abolition represented a staggering relief to the people of [[Leinster]]. (006-164) The decision was made about the time that Abbot [[Adamnan]] was in [[Ireland]] (A.D.

684), and that illustrious personage is said to have been opposed to the abolition; a disagreement among saints that underscores the political complexity of the question. (006-165) (006-166) (006-167)

The abolition was carried, and though its re-enactment was often attempted in subsequent centuries, the authority of Saint Moling's solemn settlement prevented it from being re-enforced for any length of time, except as a political or military infliction. (006-168) (006-169) (006-170)

> ...the authority of Saint Moling's solemn settlement, prevented it from being re-enforced for any length of time, except as a political or military infliction. (006-168) (006-169) (006-170)

### Clerical Power and the Leinster Settlement

The abolition of the Borooa tribute was not an isolated event but part of a broader pattern in which the Irish clergy exercised decisive influence over the politics of the island. McGee observes that in the sixth and seventh centuries, the power of the clergy had been omnipotent in politics. [[Saint Patrick]] had expurgated the old constitution;

Saint Ruadan's curse drove the kings from [[Tara]]; [[Saint Columba]] had established the independence of Alba and preserved the Bardic Order; and Saint Moling had abolished the Leinster tribute. (006-182) (006-183) (006-184) (006-185)

> St. Patrick had expurgated the old constitution; St. Ruadan's curse drove the kings from Tara; St. Columbkill had established the independence of Alba, and preserved the Bardic Order; St. Moling had abolished the Leinster tribute. (006-182) (006-183) (006-184) (006-185)

McGee credits these celebrated Abbots and Bishops with exercising their irresistible power always against the oppression of the weak by the strong, to mitigate the horrors of war, to uphold the right of sanctuary, and for the maintenance and spread of sound Christian principles. ### Later Life and Death

Saint Moling survived Finnacta by three years. (006-172) (006-173) Adamnan, so intimately connected with the events of Finnacta's reign, survived the king by ten years and revisited [[Ireland]] in 697, during the short reign of Loingsech.

(006-173) (006-174) (006-175) Moling's death thus occurred around 697, placing the end of his life in the closing years of the seventh century.

### Prophetic Tradition

Beyond his political and ecclesiastical achievements, Saint Moling became a figure of prophetic legend in Irish tradition. Quatrains fathered on Saint Moling; together with triads attributed to Merlin;

were freely showered in the path of the Norman invaders when they landed in [[Ireland]] in the twelfth century, testifying to the enduring reputation of Moling as a seer whose words carried weight across the centuries. (026-100) (026-101)

## Legacy

Saint Moling is remembered primarily for his role in the abolition of the Borooa tribute, an act that permanently relieved [[Leinster]] of a crushing annual burden.

His "solemn settlement" (006-168) proved so authoritative that even when later monarchs of [[Munster]] claimed the Borooa as an inseparable adjunct of their southern kingdom, they could not revive it as a regular institution. (011-087) (011-088) (011-089)

By the time of the later Middle Ages, when the clerical power had declined from its seventh-century heights, McGee notes that the age of Moling and Adamnan and Columba was looked back upon as a golden era in which the Church had been able to check the excesses of secular power. (020-159) (020-160) (020-161) (020-162)

Saint Moling is commemorated in the Irish calendar as a saint of the early Church, his name preserved among the most eminent Irish saints of the seventh century alongside Saint Adamnan, Saint Fursey, and Saint Laserian. (008-171) (008-172) His memory endures as a symbol of the spiritual authority that the early Irish Church wielded on behalf of the oppressed.
