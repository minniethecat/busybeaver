## Overview

Thomas Wentworth, Earl of Strafford (1593–1641) was a statesman "born to be the wonder and the bane of three kingdoms." As Lord Deputy of Ireland from 1632 to 1640, he pursued a policy of "Thorough" — absolute royal authority backed by arbitrary government — that left a lasting mark on Irish political memory. His administration extracted revenue for [[Charles I|Charles I]], built up an Irish army, and crushed opposition through the machinery of prerogative courts. Recalled to England on the eve of the Scottish crisis, he was impeached by the Long Parliament, attainted, and executed in May 1641. (065-185) (065-186) (066-030) (066-084)

Strafford's seven-year rule in Ireland was remembered long after his death as a "reign of terror" that shaped the fears and grievances exploding in the Rebellion of 1641. (066-024) (068-026)

## Early Career and Rise

Thomas Wentworth began his political career as a critic of the Crown, aligning with the parliamentary opposition to Charles I in the 1620s. Disappointed by the lack of advancement, he shifted his allegiance to the King and was created Viscount Wentworth in 1628. His administrative talents brought him to the King's attention, and in 1632 he was appointed Lord Deputy of Ireland. (065-185)

> "Strafford applied himself earnestly to the self-elected task of making the King absolute in all three kingdoms." (065-289)

## Administration in Ireland

### The Policy of "Thorough"

Strafford's Irish policy aimed at three objectives: to make the Crown financially self-sufficient, to enforce religious conformity to the established Church, and to break the power of all intermediate interests — whether Old English, Gaelic, or New English planter — that stood between the Crown and absolute authority. His instrument was the Court of Castle Chamber and the Commission for Defective Titles, through which he could revoke land grants and extract fines at will. (065-289) (066-009)

> "During the seven years of Strafford's iron rule, every interest in Ireland had been made to feel the weight of his hand." (066-024)

### Plantation and Confiscation

Strafford extended the policy of plantation into Connaught and Clare, reviving dormant royal claims to land titles. Juries that refused to find for the Crown were threatened and punished. Although his schemes in Connaught were interrupted by his recall to England, the fear they generated contributed directly to the outbreak of rebellion in 1641. (068-026) (068-032)

### The Irish Army

One of Strafford's most consequential acts was the embodiment of a "native army" of 8,000 Irish Catholic recruits, ostensibly for the King's service. These regiments, raised and trained under Strafford's authority, became a source of profound suspicion in England and Scotland. After his fall, the army remained in being and was later proposed for service in Spain — a plan that the Long Parliament moved to block. (066-246)

## Impeachment and Execution

Recalled from Ireland in 1640 to deal with the Scottish crisis, Strafford found himself the target of the newly convened Long Parliament. "One of its first acts was the impeachment of Strafford for high crimes and misdemeanours." The impeachment occupied the greater part of the parliamentary session. When the legal case faltered, his enemies resorted to a Bill of Attainder — a legislative death sentence. Abandoned by Charles I, who signed the bill under political duress, Strafford was executed on Tower Hill on 12 May 1641. (066-034) (066-084)

## Legacy

Strafford's Irish administration left a paradoxical legacy. To the Crown, he demonstrated how effectively Ireland could be governed as a dependency — and how lucrative that dependency could be. To the Irish, his "reign of terror" provided a compelling argument for armed resistance, and the Rebellion of 1641 broke out within months of his execution. His fall also fatally weakened Charles I's position in England, contributing to the slide toward civil war.

Historians have compared Strafford's authority with Cromwell's: "a court of claims for the distribution of forfeited property, greater than even Strafford's Court of Claims or Cromwell's High Court of Justice." His name became shorthand for absolutist ambition in Irish affairs. (075-084)
