CHAPTER IV.

[026-001]SECOND CAMPAIGN OF EARL RICHARD—HENRY II. IN IRELAND.

[026-002]The campaign of the year 1171 languished from a variety of causes. At

[026-003]the very outset, the invaders lost their chief patron, who had been so

[026-004]useful to them. During the siege of Dublin, in the previous autumn, the

[026-005]townsmen of Wexford, who were in revolt, had, by stratagem, induced

[026-006]Robert Fitzstephen to surrender his fort at Carrick, and had imprisoned

[026-007]him in one of the islands of their harbour. Waterford had been

[026-008]surprised and taken by Cormac McCarthy, Prince of Desmond, and

[026-009]Strongbow, alarmed by the proclamation of Henry, knew hardly whether to

[026-010]consider himself outlaw, subject, or independent sovereign.

[026-011]Raymond the Fat had returned from his embassy to King Henry, with no

[026-012]comfortable tidings. He had been kept day after day waiting the

[026-013]pleasure of the King, and returned with sentences as dubious in his

[026-014]mouth, as those on which Earl Richard had originally acted. It was

[026-015]evidently not the policy of Henry to abandon the enterprise already so

[026-016]well begun, but neither was it his interest or desire that any subject

[026-017]should reap the benefit, or erect an independent power, upon his mere

[026-018]permission to embark in the service of McMurrogh. Herve, the Earl's

[026-019]uncle, had been despatched as ambassador in Raymond's place, but with

[026-020]no better success. At length, Richard himself, by the advice of all his

[026-021]counsellors, repaired to England, and waited on Henry at Newenham, in

[026-022]Gloucestershire. At first he was ignominiously refused an audience, but

[026-023]after repeated solicitations he was permitted to renew his homage. He

[026-024]then yielded in due form the city of Dublin, and whatever other

[026-025]conquests he claimed, and consented to hold his lands in Leinster, as

[026-026]chief tenant from the crown: in return for which he was graciously

[026-027]forgiven the success that had attended his adventure, and permitted to

[026-028]accompany the King's expedition, in the ensuing autumn.

[026-029]Before Strongbow's departure for England three unsuccessful attempts

[026-030]had been made for the expulsion of the Norman garrison from Dublin.

[026-031]They were unfortunately not undertaken in concert, but rather in

[026-032]succession. The first was an attempt at surprising the city by Asculph

[026-033]MacTorcall, probably relying on the active aid of the inhabitants of

[026-034]his own race. He had but "a small force," chiefly from the isles of

[026-035]Insi-Gall and the Orkneys. The Orcadians were under the command of a

[026-036]warrior called John the Furious or Mad, the last of those wild

[026-037]Berserkers of the North, whose valour was regarded in Pagan days as a

[026-038]species of divine frenzy. This redoubted champion, after a momentary

[026-039]success, was repulsed by Milo and Richard de Cogan, and finally fell by

[026-040]the hand of Walter de Riddlesford. Asculph was taken prisoner, and,

[026-041]avowing boldly his intention never to desist from attempting to recover

[026-042]the place, was put to death. The second attack has been often described

[026-043]as a regular investment by Roderick O'Conor, at the head of all the

[026-044]forces of the Island, which was only broken up in the ninth week of its

[026-045]duration, by a desperate sally on the part of the famished garrison.

[026-046]Many details and episodes, proper to so long a beleaguerment, are given

[026-047]by _Giraldus_, and reproduced by his copyists. We find, however, little

[026-048]warrant for these passages in our native annals, any more than for the

[026-049]antithetical speeches which the same partial historian places in the

[026-050]mouths of his heroes. The Four Masters limit the time to "the course of

[026-051]a fortnight." Roderick, according to their account, was accompanied by

[026-052]the lords of Breffni and Oriel only; frequent skirmishes and conflicts

[026-053]took place; an excursion was made against the Leinster Allies of the

[026-054]Normans, "to cut down and burn the corn of the Saxons." The surprise by

[026-055]night of the monarch's camp is also duly recorded; and that the enemy

[026-056]carried off "the provisions, armour, and horses of Roderick." By which

[026-057]sally, according to _Giraldus_, Dublin having obtained provisions

[026-058]enough for a year, Earl Richard marched to Wexford, "taking the higher

[026-059]way by Idrone," with the hope to deliver Fitzstephen. But the Wexford

[026-060]men having burned their suburbs, and sent their goods and families into

[026-061]the stockaded island, sent him word that at the first attack they would

[026-062]put Fitzstephen and his companions to death. The Earl, therefore, held

[026-063]sorrowfully on his way to Waterford, where, leaving a stronger force

[026-064]than the first garrison, to which he had entrusted it, he sailed for

[026-065]England to make his peace with King Henry. The third attempt on Dublin

[026-066]was made by the lord of Breffni during the Earl's absence, and when the

[026-067]garrison were much reduced; it was equally unsuccessful with those

[026-068]already recorded. De Cogan displayed his usual courage, and the lord of

[026-069]Breffni lost a son and some of his best men in the assault.

[026-071]It was upon the marches of Wales that the Earl found King Henry busily

[026-072]engaged in making preparations for his own voyage into Ireland. He had

[026-073]levied on the landholders throughout his dominions an escutage or

[026-074]commutation for personal service, and the Pipe roll, which contains his

[026-075]disbursements for the year, has led an habitually cautious writer to

[026-076]infer "that the force raised for the expedition was much more numerous

[026-077]than has been represented by historians." During the muster of his

[026-078]forces he visited Pembroke, and made a progress through North Wales,

[026-079]severely censuring those who had enlisted under Strongbow, and placing

[026-080]garrisons of his own men in their castles. At Saint David's he made the

[026-081]usual offering on the shrine of the Saint and received the

[026-082]hospitalities of the Bishop. All things being in readiness, he sailed

[026-083]from Milford Haven, with a fleet of 400 transports, having on board

[026-084]many of the Norman nobility, 500 knights, and an army usually estimated

[026-085]at 4,000 men at arms. On the 18th of October, 1171, he landed safely at

[026-086]Crook, in the county of Waterford, being unable, according to an old

[026-087]local tradition, to sail up the river from adverse winds. As one

[026-088]headland of that harbour is called _Hook_, and the other _Crook_, the

[026-089]old adage, "by hook or by crook," is thought to have arisen on this

[026-090]occasion.

[026-091]In Henry's train, beside Earl Richard, there came over Hugh de Lacy,

[026-092]some time Constable of Chester; William, son of Aldelm, ancestor of the

[026-093]Clanrickardes; Theobald Walter, ancestor of the Butlers; Robert le

[026-094]Poer, ancestor of the Powers; Humphrey de Bohun, Robert Fitz-Barnard,

[026-095]Hugh de Gundeville, Philip de Hastings, Philip de Braos, and many other

[026-096]cavaliers whose names were renowned throughout France and England. As

[026-097]the imposing host formed on the sea side, a white hare, according to an

[026-098]English chronicler, leapt from a neighbouring hedge, and was

[026-099]immediately caught and presented to the King as an omen of victory.

[026-100]Prophecies, pagan and Christian—quatrains fathered on Saint Moling and

[026-101]triads attributed to Merlin—were freely showered in his path. But the

[026-102]true omen of his success he might read for himself, in a constitution

[026-103]which had lost its force, in laws which had ceased to be sacred, and in

[026-104]a chieftain race, brave indeed as mortal men could be, but envious,

[026-105]arrogant, revengeful, and insubordinate. For their criminal indulgence

[026-106]of these demoniacal passions a terrible chastisement was about to fall

[026-107]on them, and not only on them, but also, alas! on their poor people.

[026-108]The whole time passed by Henry II. in Ireland was from the 18th

[026-109]October, 1171, till the 17th of April following, just seven months. For

[026-110]the first politician of his age, with the command of such troops, and

[026-111]so much treasure, these seven months could not possibly be barren of

[026-112]consequences. Winter, the season of diplomacy, was seldom more

[026-113]industriously or expertly employed. The townsmen of Wexford, aware of

[026-114]his arrival as soon as it had taken place, hastened to make their

[026-115]submission and to deliver up to him their prisoner, Robert Fitzstephen,

[026-116]the first of the invaders. Henry, affecting the same displeasure

[026-117]towards Fitzstephen he did for all those who had anticipated his own

[026-118]expedition, ordered him to be fettered and imprisoned in Reginald's

[026-119]tower. At Waterford he also received the friendly overtures of the

[026-120]lords of Desies and Ossory, and probably some form of feudal submission

[026-121]was undergone by those chiefs. Cormac, Prince of Desmond, followed

[026-122]their example, and soon afterwards Donald O'Brien of Thomond met him on

[026-123]the banks of the Suir, not far from Cashel, made his peace, and agreed

[026-124]to receive a Norman garrison in his Hiberno-Danish city of Limerick.

[026-125]Having appointed commanders over these and other southern garrisons,

[026-126]Henry proceeded to Dublin, where a spacious cage-work palace, on a lawn

[026-127]without the city, was prepared for winter quarters. Here he continued

[026-128]those negotiations with the Irish chiefs, which we are told were so

[026-129]generally successful. Amongst others whose adhesion he received,

[026-130]mention is made of the lord of Breffni, the most faithful follower the

[026-131]Monarch Roderick could count. The chiefs of the Northern Hy-Nial

[026-132]remained deaf to all his overtures, and though Fitz-Aldelm and de Lacy,

[026-133]the commissioners despatched to treat with Roderick, are said to have

[026-134]procured from the deserted _Ard-Righ_ an act of submission, it is

[026-135]incredible that a document of such consequence should have been allowed

[026-136]to perish. Indeed, most of the confident assertions about submissions

[026-137]to Henry are to be taken with great caution; it is quite certain he

[026-138]himself, though he lived nearly twenty years after his Irish

[026-139]expedition, never assumed any Irish title whatever. It is equally true

[026-140]that his successor, Richard I., never assumed any such title, as an

[026-141]incident of the English crown. And although Henry in the year 1185

[026-142]created his youngest son, John _Lackland_, "lord of Ireland," it was

[026-143]precisely in the same spirit and with as much ground of title as he had

[026-144]for creating Hugh de Lacy, Lord of Meath, or John de Courcy, Earl of

[026-145]Ulster. Of this question of title we shall speak more fully hereafter,

[026-146]for we do not recognize any English sovereign as _King_ of Ireland,

[026-147]previous to the year 1541; but it ought surely to be conclusive

[026-148]evidence, that neither had Henry claimed the crown, nor had the Irish

[026-149]chiefs acknowledged him as their _Ard-Righ_, that in the two authentic

[026-150]documents from his hand which we possess, he neither signs himself

[026-151]_Rex_ nor _Dominus Hibernioe_. These documents are the Charter of

[026-152]Dublin, and the Concession of Glendalough, and their authenticity has

[026-153]never been disputed.

[026-154]After spending a right merry Christmas with Norman and Milesian guests

[026-155]in abundance at Dublin, Henry proceeded to that work of religious

[026-156]reformation, under plea of which he had obtained the Bill of Pope

[026-157]Adrian, seventeen years before, declaring such an expedition undertaken

[026-158]with such motives, lawful and praiseworthy. Early in the new year, by

[026-159]his desire, a synod was held at Cashel, where many salutary decrees

[026-160]were enacted. These related to the proper solemnization of marriage;

[026-161]the catechising of children before the doors of churches; the

[026-162]administration of baptism in baptismal or parish churches; the

[026-163]abolition of _Erenachs_ or lay Trustees of church property, and the

[026-164]imposition of tithes, both of corn and cattle. By most English writers

[026-165]this synod is treated as a National Council, and inferences are thence

[026-166]drawn of Henry's admitted power over the clergy of the nation. There

[026-167]is, however, no evidence that the Bishops of Ulster or Connaught were

[026-168]present at Cashel, but strong negative testimony to the contrary. We

[026-169]read under the date of the same year in the Four Masters, that a synod

[026-170]of the clergy and laity of Ireland was convened at Tuam by Roderick

[026-171]O'Conor and the Archbishop Catholicus O'Duffy. It is hardly possible

[026-172]that this meeting could be in continuation or in concord with the

[026-173]assembly convoked at the instance of Henry.

[026-174]Following quickly upon the Cashel Synod, Henry held a "Curia Regis" or

[026-175]Great Court at Lismore, in which he created the offices of Marshal,

[026-176]Constable, and Seneschal for Ireland. Earl Richard was created the

[026-177]first Lord Marshal; de Lacy, the first Lord Constable. Theobald,

[026-178]ancestor of the Ormond family, was already chief Butler, and de Vernon

[026-179]was created the first high Steward or Seneschal. Such other order as

[026-180]could be taken for the preservation of the places already captured, was

[026-181]not neglected. The surplus population of Bristol obtained a charter of

[026-182]Dublin to be held of Henry and his heirs, "with all the same liberties

[026-183]and free customs which they enjoyed at Bristol." Wexford was committed

[026-184]to the charge of Fitz-Aldelm, Waterford to de Bohun, and Dublin to de

[026-185]Lacy. Castles were ordered to be erected in the towns and at other

[026-186]points, and the politic king, having caused all those who remained

[026-187]behind to renew their homage in the most solemn form, sailed on Easter

[026-188]Monday from Wexford Haven, and on the same day, landed at Port-Finan in

[026-189]Wales. Here he assumed the Pilgrim's staff, and proceeded humbly on

[026-190]foot to St. David's, preparatory to meeting the Papal Commissioners

[026-191]appointed to inquire into Beckett's murder.

[026-192]It is quite apparent that had Henry landed in Ireland at any other

[026-193]period of his life except in the year of the martyrdom of the renowned

[026-194]Archbishop of Canterbury, while the wrath of Rome was yet hanging

[026-195]poised in the air, ready to be hurled against him, he would not have

[026-196]left the work he undertook but half begun. The nett result of his

[026-197]expedition, of his great fleet, mighty army, and sagacious counsels,

[026-198]was the infusion of a vast number of new adventurers (most of them of

[026-199]higher rank and better fortunes than their precursors), into the same

[026-200]old field. Except the garrisons admitted into Limerick and Cork, and

[026-201]the displacing of Strongbow's commandants by his own at Waterford,

[026-202]Wexford, and Dublin, there seems to have been little gained in a

[026-203]military sense. The decrees of the Synod of Cashel would, no doubt,

[026-204]stand him in good stead with the Papal legates as evidences of his

[026-205]desire to enforce strict discipline, even on lands beyond those over

[026-206]which he actually ruled. But, after all, harassed as he was with

[026-207]apprehensions of the future, perhaps no other Prince could have done

[026-208]more in a single winter in a strange country than Henry II. did for his

[026-209]seven months' sojourn in Ireland.
