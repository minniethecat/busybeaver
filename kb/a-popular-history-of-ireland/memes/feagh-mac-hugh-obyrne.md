## Overview

Feagh Mac Hugh O'Byrne (c. 1544 – May 1597) was the chief of the O'Byrne clan of Wicklow and a prominent figure in the Elizabethan wars in Ireland. Known to the English authorities as "the firebrand of the mountains," he waged a sustained guerrilla campaign from his stronghold in Glenmalure against the expanding Tudor administration. He was a close ally of Hugh O'Neill, Earl of Tyrone, serving as a crucial link between the Ulster confederacy and the Leinster clans during the Nine Years War. (059-031) (059-042) (057-194)

## Early Career and the Baltinglass Rebellion

The O'Byrnes were among the most resilient of the Gaelic clans of Leinster, maintaining their independence in the Wicklow mountains long after the Pale had been established around Dublin. Feagh Mac Hugh emerged as the senior figure of the O'Byrnes during a period of intensifying English pressure on the remaining Gaelic territories. (057-194)

He appears to have been drawn into wider conflict through his connection to James Eustace, Viscount Baltinglass, who rose in rebellion in 1580. Feagh's daughter married Sir Walter Fitzgerald, whose friendship with Lord Baltinglass brought him under suspicion from the Dublin authorities. This family alliance with the rebellious Anglo-Irish lords of the Pale drew the O'Byrnes deeper into the anti-English confederacy. (059-036) (057-186)

McGee described Feagh as "one of the noblest spirits of his race and age," noting that he attended the Dublin parliament of 1585 not as a member but under the protection of powerful friends, observing the proceedings of a government that was steadily encroaching on his clan's territory. (057-194)

## "The Firebrand of the Mountains"

The Nine Years War began in earnest in 1594, and the first blow was struck against Feagh Mac Hugh. As McGee records:

> The first blow was struck at "the firebrand of the mountains," as he was called at Court, Feagh Mac Hugh O'Byrne. The truce made with him expired in 1594, and his application for his renewal was not honoured with an answer. (059-031)

In January 1595, the Lord Deputy Russell launched a surprise attack on O'Byrne's house at Ballincor in Glenmalure. The raid nearly captured the aged chieftain; his wife, Rose O'Toole, was wounded in the breast during the flight, and a priest was shot dead. Despite these losses, Feagh retreated to Dromceat, or the Cat's-back Mountain, and continued his resistance. His wife was later captured in a follow-up operation, attainted of treason, and executed. (059-044) (059-047) (059-053)

The relentless campaign against the O'Byrnes was not merely punitive. As a confidant of Hugh O'Neill, Feagh Mac Hugh served as a vital intermediary between the Ulster earls and the discontented Leinster clans. Brian Oge O'Rourke passed through Dublin acting as a confidential agent between Feagh Mac Hugh and Tyrone, weaving the strands of a national confederacy against English rule. (059-042) (059-041)

## The War of the Mountains

Throughout 1595 and 1596, Feagh Mac Hugh maintained a dogged resistance. Despite the loss of Ballincor, he recovered his position each autumn only to see it overrun again each spring. McGee writes of his resilience:

> Though worn with years and infirm of body, the Wicklow chieftain held his devoted bands well together, and kept the garrison of Dublin constantly on the defensive. (059-140)

He found an active coadjutor in the new chieftain of the O'Moores, and together they inflicted significant losses on the English forces, including an engagement at Stradbally Bridge where Alexander and Francis Cosby, grandsons of the commander at the notorious massacre of Mullaghmast, were slain. (059-143) (059-144)

## Death at Glenmalure

In May 1597, Lord Borough replaced Russell as Lord Deputy and assumed the command-in-chief from Sir John Norris. Feagh Mac Hugh's end came swiftly:

> Simultaneously with his arrival Feagh Mac Hugh O'Byrne, was surprised in Glenmalure by a detachment from Dublin, and slain; he died as he had lived, a hero and a free man. (059-166)

Hugh O'Neill, who was warmly attached to the Wicklow chief, immediately despatched succour to Feagh's sons and promised to continue the friendship he had always entertained for their father. Phelim, son of Feagh, succeeded as the new chief of the O'Byrnes. (059-169) (060-117)

## Legacy

Feagh Mac Hugh O'Byrne represented a dying order—the Gaelic clan chief who defied the centralizing Tudor state from his mountain fastness. His significance to the Nine Years War lay not only in his own martial exploits but in his role as the connective tissue between Ulster and Leinster. The O'Byrnes of Wicklow, along with the O'Moores and O'Connors of the midlands, provided the strategic depth that allowed Hugh O'Neill to sustain his war against the Crown.

His death in 1597 was a severe blow to the confederacy, removing one of its most experienced and determined commanders at a critical juncture. The O'Byrnes continued the struggle under his son Phelim, but the momentum of the war in Leinster never fully recovered.
