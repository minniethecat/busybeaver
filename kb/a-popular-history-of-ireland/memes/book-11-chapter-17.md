CHAPTER XVII.

[104-001]THE INSURRECTION ELSEWHERE—FATE OF THE LEADING UNITED IRISHMEN.

[104-002]On the 21st of June, the Marquis Cornwallis, whose name is so familiar

[104-003]in American and East Indian history, arrived in Dublin, to assume the

[104-004]supreme power, both civil and military. As his Chief Secretary, he

[104-005]recommended Lord Castlereagh, who had acted in that capacity during the

[104-006]latter part of Lord Camden's administration in consequence of Mr

[104-007]Pelham's illness; and the Pitt-Portland administration appointed his

[104-008]lordship accordingly, because, among other good and sufficient reasons,

[104-009]"he was so unlike an Irishman."

[104-010]While the new Viceroy came to Ireland still more resolute than his

[104-011]predecessor to bring about the long-desired legislative union, it is

[104-012]but justice to his memory to say, that he as resolutely resisted the

[104-013]policy of torture and provocation pursued under Lord Camden. That

[104-014]policy had, indeed, served its pernicious purpose, and it was now

[104-015]possible for a new ruler to turn a new leaf; this Lord Cornwallis did

[104-016]from the hour of his arrival, not without incurring the ill-concealed

[104-017]displeasures of the Castle cabal. But his position gave him means of

[104-018]protection which Sir Ralph Abercromby had not; he was known to enjoy

[104-019]the personal confidence of the King; and those who did not hesitate

[104-020]three months before to assail by every abusive epithet the humane

[104-021]Scottish Baronet, hesitated long before criticising with equal freedom

[104-022]the all-powerful Viceroy.

[104-023]The sequel of the insurrection may be briefly related: next to Wexford,

[104-024]the adjoining county of Wicklow, famous throughout the world for its

[104-025]lakes and glens, maintained the chief brunt of the Leinster battle. The

[104-026]brothers Byrne, of Ballymanus, with Holt, Hackett, and other local

[104-027]leaders, were for months, from the difficult nature of the country,

[104-028]enabled to defy those combined movements by which, as in a huge net,

[104-029]Lord Lake had swept up the camps of Wexford. At Hacketstown, on the

[104-030]25th of June, the Byrnes were repulsed with considerable loss, but at

[104-031]Ballyellis, on the 30th, fortune and skill gave them and their Wexford

[104-032]comrades a victory, resembling in many respects that of Clough. General

[104-033]Needham, who had again established his head-quarters at Gorey, detached

[104-034]Colonel Preston, with some troops of Ancient Britons, the 4th and 5th

[104-035]dragoons, and three yeomanry corps, to attack the insurgents who were

[104-036]observed in force in the neighbourhood of Monaseed. Aware of this

[104-037]movement, the Byrnes prepared in the ravine of Ballyellis a well-laid

[104-038]ambuscade, barricading with carts and trees the farther end of the

[104-039]pass. Attacked by the royalists they retreated towards this pass, were

[104-040]hotly pursued, and then turned on their pursuers. Two officers and

[104-041]sixty men were killed in the trap, while the terrified rear-rank fled

[104-042]for their lives to the shelter of their head-quarters. At Ballyraheene,

[104-043]on the 2nd of July, the King's troops sustained another check in which

[104-044]they lost two officers and ten men, but at Ballygullen, on the 4th, the

[104-045]insurgents were surrounded between the forces of General Needham, Sir

[104-046]James Duff, and the Marquis of Huntley. This was the last considerable

[104-047]action in which the Wicklow and Wexford men were unitedly engaged. In

[104-048]the dispersion which followed, "Billy Byrne of Ballymanus," the hero of

[104-049]his county, paid the forfeit of his life; while his brother, Garrett,

[104-050]subsequently surrendered, and was included in the Banishment Act.

[104-052]Anthony Perry of Inch, and Father Kearns, leading a much diminished

[104-053]band into Kildare, formed a junction with Aylmer and Reynolds of that

[104-054]county, and marched into Meath, with a view of reaching and surprising

[104-055]Athlone. The plan was boldly and well conceived, but their means of

[104-056]execution were deplorably deficient. At Clonard they were repulsed by a

[104-057]handful of troops well armed and posted; a combined movement always

[104-058]possible in Meath, drove them from side to side during the midweek of

[104-059]July, until at length, hunted down as they were, they broke up in twos

[104-060]and threes to seek any means of escape. Father Kearns and Mr. Perry

[104-061]were, however, arrested, and executed by martial law at Edenderry. Both

[104-062]died bravely; the priest sustaining and exhorting his companion to the

[104-063]last.

[104-064]Still another band of the Wexford men, under Father John Murphy and

[104-065]Walter Devereux, crossed the Barrow at Gore's bridge, and marched upon

[104-066]Kilkenny. At Lowgrange they surprised an outpost; at Castlecomer, after

[104-067]a sharp action, they took the town, which Sir Charles Asgill

[104-068]endeavoured, but without success, to relieve. Thence they continued

[104-069]their march towards Athy in Kildare, but being caught between two or

[104-070]rather three fires, that of Major Mathews, from Maryboro', General

[104-071]Dunne, from Athy, and Sir Charles Asgill, they retreated on old

[104-072]Leighlin, as if seeking the shelter of the Carlow mountains. At

[104-073]Killcomney Hill, however, they were forced into action under most

[104-074]unfavourable circumstances, and utterly routed. One, Father Murphy,

[104-075]fell in the engagement, the other, the precursor of the insurrection,

[104-076]was captured three days afterward, and conveyed a prisoner to General

[104-077]Duff's headquarters at Tullow. Here he was put on his trial before a

[104-078]Military Commission composed of Sir James Duff, Lord Roden, Colonels

[104-079]Eden and Foster, and Major Hall. Hall had the meanness to put to him,

[104-080]prisoner as he was, several insulting questions, which at length the

[104-081]high-spirited rebel answered with a blow. The Commission thought him

[104-082]highly dangerous, and instantly ordered him to execution. His body was

[104-083]burned, his head spiked on the market-house of Tullow, and his memory

[104-084]gibbeted in all the loyal publications of the period. On his person,

[104-085]before execution, were found a crucifix, a pix, and letters from many

[104-086]Protestants, asking his protection; as to his reputation, the priest

[104-087]who girded on the sword only when he found his altar overthrown and his

[104-088]flock devoured by wolves, need not fear to look posterity in the face.

[104-089]Of the other Leinster leaders, Walter Devereux, the last colleague of

[104-090]Father Murphy, was arrested at Cork, on the eve of sailing for America,

[104-091]tried and executed; Fitzgerald and Aylmer were spared on condition of

[104-092]expatriation; months afterwards, Holt surrendered, was transported, and

[104-093]returned after several years, to end his days where he began his

[104-094]career; Dwyer alone maintained the life of a Rapparee for five long

[104-095]years among the hills of Wicklow, where his adventures were often of

[104-096]such a nature as to throw all fictitious conceptions of an outlaw's

[104-097]life into commonplace by comparison. Except in the fastnesses

[104-098]frequented by this extraordinary man, and in the wood of Killaughram,

[104-099]in Wexford, where the outlaws, with the last stroke of national humour,

[104-100]assumed the name of _The Babes in the Wood_, the Leinster insurrection

[104-101]was utterly trodden out within two months from its first beginning, on

[104-102]the 23rd of May. So weak against discipline, arms, munitions and money,

[104-103]are all that mere naked valour and devotion can accomplish!

[104-104]In Ulster, on the organization of which so much time and labour had

[104-105]been expended for four or five years preceding, the rising was not more

[104-106]general than in Leinster, and the actual struggle lasted only a week.

[104-107]The two counties which moved _en masse_ were Down and Antrim, the

[104-108]original chiefs of which, such as Thomas Russell and Samuel Neilson,

[104-109]were unfortunately in prison. The next leader on whom the men of Antrim

[104-110]relied, resigned his command on the very eve of the appointed day; this

[104-111]disappointment and the arrest of the Rev. Steele Dickson in Down,

[104-112]compelled a full fortnight's delay. On the 7th of June, however, the

[104-113]more determined spirits resolved on action, and the first movement was

[104-114]to seize the town of Antrim, which, if they could have held it, would

[104-115]have given them command of the communications with Donegal and Down,

[104-116]from both of which they might have expected important additions to

[104-117]their ranks. The leader of this enterprise was Henry John McCracken, a

[104-118]cotton manufacturer of Belfast, thirty two years of age, well educated,

[104-119]accomplished and resolute, with whom was associated a brother of

[104-120]William Orr, the proto-martyr of the Ulster Union. The town of Antrim

[104-121]was occupied by the 22nd light dragoons, Colonel Lumley, and the local

[104-122]yeomanry under Lord O'Neil. In the first assault the insurgents were

[104-123]successful, Lord O'Neil, five officers, forty-seven rank and file

[104-124]having fallen, and two guns being captured; but Lumley's dragoons had

[104-125]hardly vanished out of sight, when a strong reinforcement from Blaris

[104-126]camp arrived and renewed the action, changing premature exultation into

[104-127]panic and confusion. Between two and three hundred of the rebels fell,

[104-128]and McCracken and his staff, deserted by their hasty levies, were

[104-129]arrested, wearied and hopeless, about a month later, wandering among

[104-130]the Antrim hills. The leaders were tried at Belfast and executed.

[104-131]In Down two actions were fought, one at Saintfield on the 7th of June,

[104-132]under Dr. Jackson—where Colonel Stapleton was severely handled—and

[104-133]another and more important one at Ballynahinch, under Henry Munro, on

[104-134]the 13th, where Nugent, the district General, commanded in person.

[104-135]Here, after a gallant defence, the men of Down were utterly routed;

[104-136]their leader, alone and on foot, was captured some five or six miles

[104-137]from the field, and executed two days afterwards before his own door at

[104-138]Lisburn. He died with the utmost composure; his wife and mother looking

[104-139]down, on the awful scene from the windows of his own house.

[104-140]In Munster, with the exception of a trifling skirmish between the

[104-141]West-Meath yeomanry under Sir Hugh O'Reilly, with whom were the

[104-142]Caithness legion, under Major Innes, and a body of 300 or 400 ill-armed

[104-143]peasants, who attacked them on the 19th of June, on the road from

[104-144]Clonakilty to Bandon, there was no notable attempt at insurrection. But

[104-145]in Connaught, very unexpectedly, as late as the end of August, the

[104-146]flame extinguished in blood in Leinster and Ulster, again blazed up for

[104-147]some days with portentous brightness. The counties of Mayo, Sligo,

[104-148]Roscommon and Galway had been partially organized by those fugitives

[104-149]from Orange oppression in the North, who, in the years '95, '96, and

[104-150]'97, had been compelled to flee for their lives into Connaught, to the

[104-151]number of several thousands. They brought with the tale of their

[104-152]sufferings the secret of Defenderism; they first taught the peasantry

[104-153]of the West, who, safe in their isolated situation and their

[104-154]overwhelming numbers, were more familiar with poverty than with

[104-155]persecution, what manner of men then held sway over all the rest of the

[104-156]country, and how easily it would be for Irishmen once united and backed

[104-157]by France, to establish under their own green flag, both religious and

[104-158]civil liberty.

[104-159]When, therefore, three French frigates cast anchor in Killalla Bay, on

[104-160]the 22nd of August, they did not find the country wholly unprepared,

[104-161]though far from being as ripe for revolt as they expected. These ships

[104-162]had on board 1,000 men, with arms for 1,000 more, under command of

[104-163]General Humbert, who had taken on himself, in the state of anarchy

[104-164]which then prevailed in France, to sail from La Rochelle with this

[104-165]handful of men, in aid of the insurrection. With Humbert were Mathew

[104-166]Tone and Bartholomew Teeling; and immediately on his arrival he was

[104-167]joined by Messrs. McDonnell, Moore, Bellew, Barrett, O'Dowd, and

[104-168]O'Donnell of Mayo, Blake of Galway, Plunkett of Roscommon, and a few

[104-169]other influential gentlemen of that Province—almost all Catholics.

[104-170]Three days were spent at Killalla, which was easily taken, in landing

[104-171]stores, enrolling recruits, and sending out parties of observation. On

[104-172]the 4th, (Sunday,) Humbert entered Ballina without resistance, and on

[104-173]the same night set out for Castlebar, the county town. By this time

[104-174]intelligence of his landing was spread over the whole country, and both

[104-175]Lord Lake and General Hutchinson had advanced to Castlebar, where they

[104-176]had from 2,000 to 3,000 men under their command. The place could be

[104-177]reached only by two routes from the north-west, by the Foxford road, or

[104-178]a long deserted mountain road which led over the pass of Barnagee,

[104-179]within sight of the town. Humbert, accustomed to the long marches and

[104-180]difficult country of La Vendee, chose the unfrequented and therefore

[104-181]unguarded route, and, to the consternation of the British generals,

[104-182]descended through the pass of Barnagee, soon after sunrise, on the

[104-183]morning of Monday, August 27th. His force consisted of 900 French

[104-184]bayonets, and between 2,000 and 3,000 new recruits. The action, which

[104-185]commenced at 7 o'clock, was short, sharp, and decisive; the yeomanry

[104-186]and regulars broke and fled, some of them never drawing rein till they

[104-187]reached Tuam, while others carried their fears and their falsehoods as

[104-188]far inland as Athlone—more than sixty miles from the scene of action.

[104-189]In this engagement, still remembered as "the races," the royalists

[104-190]confessed to the loss, killed, wounded, or prisoners, of 18 officers,

[104-191]and about 350 men, while the French commander estimated the killed

[104-192]alone at 600. Fourteen British guns and five stand of colours were also

[104-193]taken. A hot pursuit was continued for some distance by the native

[104-194]troops under Mathew Tone, Teeling, and the Mayo officers; but Lord

[104-195]Roden's famous corps of "Fox hunters" covered the retreat and checked

[104-196]the pursuers at French Hill. Immediately after the battle a Provisional

[104-197]Government was established at Castlebar, with Mr. Moore of Moore Hall,

[104-198]as President; proclamations addressed to the inhabitants at large,

[104-199]commissions to raise men, and _assignats_ payable by the future Irish

[104-200]Republic, were issued in its name.

[104-201]Meanwhile the whole of the royalist forces were now in movement toward

[104-202]the capital of Mayo, as they had been toward Vinegar Hill two months

[104-203]before. Sir John Moore and General Hunter marched from Wexford toward

[104-204]the Shannon. General Taylor, with 2,500 men, advanced from Sligo

[104-205]towards Castlebar; Colonel Maxwell was ordered from Enniskillen to

[104-206]assume command at Sligo; General Nugent from Lisburn occupied

[104-207]Enniskillen, and the Viceroy, leaving Dublin in person, advanced

[104-208]rapidly through the midland counties to Kilbeggan, and ordered Lord

[104-209]Lake and General Hutchinson, with such of their command as could be

[104-210]depended on, to assume the aggressive from the direction of Tuam. Thus

[104-211]Humbert and his allies found themselves surrounded on all sides—their

[104-212]retreat cut off by sea, for their frigates had returned to France

[104-213]immediately on their landing; three thousand men against not less than

[104-214]thirty thousand, with at least as many more in reserve, ready to be

[104-215]called into action at a day's notice.

[104-216]The French general determined if possible to reach the mountains of

[104-217]Leitrim, and open communications with Ulster, and the northern coast,

[104-218]upon which he hoped soon to see succour arrive from France. With this

[104-219]object he marched from Castlebar to Cooloney (35 miles), in one day;

[104-220]here he sustained a check from Colonel Vereker's militia, which

[104-221]necessitated a change of route; turning aside, he passed rapidly

[104-222]through Dromahaine, Manor-Hamilton, and Ballintra, making for Granard,

[104-223]from which accounts of a formidable popular outbreak had just reached

[104-224]him. In three days and a half he had marched 110 miles, flinging half

[104-225]his guns into the rivers that he crossed, lest they should fall into

[104-226]the hands of his pursuers. At Ballinamuck, county Longford, on the

[104-227]borders of Leitrim, he found himself fairly surrounded, on the morning

[104-228]of the 8th of September; and here he prepared to make a last desperate

[104-229]stand. The end could not be doubtful, the numbers against him being ten

[104-230]to one; after an action of half an hour's duration, two hundred of the

[104-231]French having thrown down their arms, the remainder surrendered, as

[104-232]prisoners of war. For the rebels no terms were thought of, and the full

[104-233]vengeance of the victors was reserved for them. Mr. Blake, who had

[104-234]formerly been a British officer, was executed on the field; Mathew Tone

[104-235]and Teeling were executed within the week in Dublin; Mr. Moore,

[104-236]President of the Provisional Government, was sentenced to banishment by

[104-237]the clemency of Lord Cornwallis, but died on shipboard; ninety of the

[104-238]Longford and Kilkenny militia who had joined the French were hanged,

[104-239]and the country generally given up to pillage and massacre. As an

[104-240]evidence of the excessive thirst for blood, it may be mentioned that at

[104-241]the re-capture of Killalla a few days later, four hundred persons were

[104-242]killed, of whom fully one-half were non-combatants.

[104-244]The disorganization of all government in France in the latter half of

[104-245]'98, was illustrated not only by Humbert's unauthorized adventure, but

[104-246]by a still weaker demonstration under General Reay and Napper Tandy,

[104-247]about the same time. With a single armed brig these daring allies made

[104-248]a descent, on the 17th of September, on Rathlin Island, well equipped

[104-249]with eloquent proclamations, bearing the date "first year of Irish

[104-250]liberty." From the postmaster of the island they ascertained Humbert's

[104-251]fate, and immediately turned the prow of their solitary ship in the

[104-252]opposite direction; Reay, to rise in after times to honour and power;

[104-253]Tandy, to continue in old age the dashing career of his manhood, and to

[104-254]expiate in exile the crime of preferring the country of his birth to

[104-255]the general centralizing policy of the empire with which he was united.

[104-256]Twelve days after the combat at Ballinamuck, while Humbert and his men

[104-257]were on their way through England to France, a new French fleet, under

[104-258]Admiral Bompart, consisting of one 74-gun ship, "the Hoche," eight

[104-259]frigates, and two smaller vessels, sailed from Brest. On board this

[104-260]fleet were embarked 3,000 men under General Hardi, the remnant of the

[104-261]army once menacing England. In this fleet sailed Theobold Wolfe Tone,

[104-262]true to his motto, _nil desperandum_, with two or three other refugees

[104-263]of less celebrity. The troops of General Hardi, however, were destined

[104-264]never to land. On the 12th of October, after tossing about for nearly a

[104-265]month in the German ocean and the North Atlantic, they appeared off the

[104-266]coast of Donegal, and stood in for Lough Swilly. But another fleet also

[104-267]was on the horizon. Admiral Sir John Borlase Warren, with an equal

[104-268]number of ships, but a much heavier armament, had been cruising on the

[104-269]track of the French during the whole time they were at sea. After many

[104-270]disappointments, the flag-ship and three of the frigates were at last

[104-271]within range and the action began. Six hours' fighting laid the Hoche a

[104-272]helpless log upon the water; nothing was left her but surrender; two of

[104-273]the frigates shared the same fate on the same day; another was captured

[104-274]on the 14th, and yet another on the 17th. The remainder of the fleet

[104-275]escaped back to France.

[104-276]The French officers landed in Donegal were received with courtesy by

[104-277]the neighbouring gentry, among whom was the Earl of Cavan, who

[104-278]entertained them at dinner. Here it was that Sir George Hill,

[104-279]son-in-law to Commissioner Beresford, an old college friend of Tone's,

[104-280]identified the founder of the United Irishmen under the uniform of a

[104-281]French Adjutant-General. Stepping up to his old schoolmate he addressed

[104-282]him by name, which Tone instantly acknowledged, inquiring politely for

[104-283]Lady Hill, and other members of Sir George's family. He was instantly

[104-284]arrested, ironed, and conveyed to Dublin under a strong guard. On the

[104-285]10th of November he was tried by court-martial and sentenced to be

[104-286]hanged: he begged only for a soldier's death—"to be shot by a platoon

[104-287]of grenadiers." This favour was denied him, and the next morning he

[104-288]attempted to commit suicide. The attempt did not immediately succeed;

[104-289]but one week later—on the 19th of November—he died from the results of

[104-290]his self-inflicted wound, with a compliment to the attendant physician

[104-291]upon his lips. Truth compels us to say he died the death of a Pagan;

[104-292]but it was a Pagan of the noblest and freest type of Grecian and Roman

[104-293]times. Had it occurred in ancient days, beyond the Christian era, it

[104-294]would have been a death every way admirable; as it was, that fatal

[104-295]final act must always stand between Wolfe Tone and the Christian people

[104-296]for whom he suffered, sternly forbidding them to invoke him in their

[104-297]prayers, or to uphold him as an example to the young men of their

[104-298]country. So closed the memorable year 1798, on the baffled and

[104-299]dispersed United Irishmen. Of the chiefs imprisoned in March and May,

[104-300]Lord Edward had died of his wounds and vexation; Oliver Bond of

[104-301]apoplexy; the brothers Sheares, Father Quigley, and William Michael

[104-302]Byrne on the gibbet. In July, on Samuel Nelson's motion, the remaining

[104-303]prisoners in Newgate, Bridewell, and Kilmainham, agreed, in order to

[104-304]stop the effusion of blood, to expatriate themselves to any country not

[104-305]at war with England, and to reveal the general secrets of their system,

[104-306]without inculpating individuals. These terms were accepted, as the

[104-307]Castle party needed their evidence to enable them to promote the

[104-308]cherished scheme of legislative Union. But that evidence delivered

[104-309]before the Committees of Parliament by Emmet, McNevin, and O'Conor, did

[104-310]not altogether serve the purposes of government. The patriotic

[104-311]prisoners made it at once a protest against, and an exposition of, the

[104-312]despotic policy under which their country had been goaded into

[104-313]rebellion. For their firmness they were punished by three years'

[104-314]confinement in Fort George, in the Scottish Highlands, where, however,

[104-315]a gallant old soldier, Colonel Stuart, endeavoured to soften the hard

[104-316]realities of a prison by all the kind attentions his instructions

[104-317]permitted him to show these unfortunate gentlemen. At the peace of

[104-318]Amiens, (1802), they were at last allowed the melancholy privilege of

[104-319]expatriation. Russell and Dowdall were permitted to return to Ireland,

[104-320]where they shared the fate of Robert Emmet in 1803; O'Conor, Corbet,

[104-321]Allen, Ware, and others, cast their lot in France, where they all rose

[104-322]to distinction; Emmet, McNevin, Sampson, and the family of Tone were

[104-323]reunited in New York, where the many changes and distractions of a

[104-324]great metropolitan community have not even yet obliterated the memories

[104-325]of their virtues, their talents, and their accomplishments.

[104-326]It is impossible to dismiss this celebrated group of men, whose

[104-327]principles and conduct so greatly influenced their country's destiny,

[104-328]without bearing explicit testimony to their heroic qualities as a

[104-329]class. If ever a body of public men deserved the character of a

[104-330]brotherhood of heroes, so far as disinterestedness, courage,

[104-331]self-denial, truthfulness and glowing love of country constitute

[104-332]heroism, these men deserved that character. The wisdom of their

[104-333]conduct, and the intrinsic merit of their plans, are other questions.

[104-334]As between their political system and that of Burke, Grattan and

[104-335]O'Connell, there always will be, probably, among their countrymen, very

[104-336]decided differences of opinion. That is but natural: but as to the

[104-337]personal and political virtues of the United Irishmen there can be no

[104-338]difference; the world has never seen a more sincere or more

[104-339]self-sacrificing generation.
