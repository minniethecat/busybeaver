William de Windsor was an English knight and administrator who served as Lord Lieutenant of [[Ireland]] during the latter half of the reign of [[Edward III]] of [[England]]. His tenure coincided with a period of significant Gaelic resurgence, when the native Irish princes steadily regained territory from the Anglo-Norman settlers, and the English colony in Ireland was reduced to a defensive posture.

## Arrival in Ireland

De Windsor first arrived in Ireland in September 1361 as part of the expedition of Lionel, Duke of Clarence, the King's son. The force that landed at [[Dublin]] numbered no more than 1,500 men, commanded by Ralph, Earl of Strafford, James, Earl of Ormond, Sir William de Windsor, Sir John Carew, and other knights (040-042). Lionel immediately issued a proclamation prohibiting natives of the country, of all origins, from approaching his camp or court — a measure that he soon found counterproductive and was compelled to revoke (040-051).

> "The Prince arrived in Ireland with no more than 1,500 men, under the command of Ralph, Earl of Strafford, James, Earl of Ormond, Sir William Windsor, Sir John Carew, and other knights." (040-042)

When Lionel returned to England in 1364, he left de Windsor as his deputy in Ireland (040-061). Lionel returned briefly in 1365 and again in 1367, the latter year being memorable for the enactment of the [[Statute of Kilkenny]] — a comprehensive Penal Code designed to prevent the assimilation of the English colonists into Irish society.

## Lord Lieutenant

After the departure of the Duke of Clarence, de Windsor held the office of Lord Lieutenant during the remaining years of Edward III's reign. During his absences in England, the Prior of Kilmainham or the Earl of [[Kildare]] or [[Ormond]] discharged the duties with the title of Lord Deputy or Lord Justice (040-118).

De Windsor's tenure was marked by constant military pressure from the native Irish. In 1370, the Earl of [[Desmond]] was defeated and slain near Adare by Brian O'Brien, Prince of Thomond, with several knights of his name and "an indescribable number of others" (040-199). [[Limerick]] was assailed and capitulated to O'Brien, who created Sheedy McNamara as Warden of the City. When the English burghers rose, murdered the new Warden, and reopened the city, they opened:

> "the gates to Sir William de Windsor, the Lord Lieutenant, who had hastened to their relief." (040-204)

## Return as Lord Lieutenant

The contest with the native princes became so desperate that de Windsor only consented to return a second time as Lord Lieutenant in 1374 on strict conditions: he was to act strictly on the defensive and to receive annually the enormous sum of 11,213 pounds 6 shillings 8 pence — an amount exceeding the entire revenue which the English King derived from Ireland at that period (040-208). According to Sir John Davies, the Irish revenue fell short of 11,000 pounds (040-213).

Although the English interest was in such a critical state, de Windsor obtained from successive Parliaments annual subsidies of 2,000 and 3,000 pounds. The deputies from [[Louth]], having voted against his demand, were thrown into prison. However, a direct petition from the Anglo-Irish to the King brought an order to de Windsor not to enforce the collection of these grants, and to remit in favour of the petitioners the scutage on all those lands of which the Irish enemy had deprived them (040-218).

## The Wider Context

De Windsor's administration operated against the backdrop of the continuous advance of the native Irish. In the generation following the death of the Earl of Ulster in 1333, and especially after the first avowal of the policy of proscription in 1342, the native tribes gained on all sides against the descendants of their invaders (040-128). In [[Connaught]], the McWilliams, McWattins, and O'Connors reasserted their authority; in [[Leinster]], the house of O'Moore under Lysaght burned the town of Killahan (040-191); in the south, Brian O'Brien pressed upon Limerick itself.

De Windsor's conditions for office — demanding an annual subsidy larger than the entire Irish revenue — reflected the fundamental weakness of the English position. The English colony in Ireland could no longer sustain itself, and de Windsor's defensive mandate acknowledged that the tide had turned decisively in favour of the native princes.
