CHAPTER IV.

[055-001]SIR HENRY SIDNEY'S DEPUTYSHIP—PARLIAMENT OF 1569—THE SECOND "GERALDINE

[055-002]LEAGUE"—SIR JAMES FITZ-MAURICE.

[055-003]Sir Henry Sidney, in writing to his court, had always reported John

[055-004]O'Neil as "the only strong man in Ireland." Before his rout at Lough

[055-005]Swilly, he could commonly call into the field 4,000 foot and 1,000

[055-006]horse; and his two years' revolt cost Elizabeth, in money, about

[055-007]150,000 pounds sterling "over and above the cess laid on the

[055-008]country"—besides "3,500 of her Majesty's soldiers" slain in battle. The

[055-009]removal of such a leader in the very prime of life was therefore a

[055-010]cause of much congratulation to Sidney and his royal mistress, and as

[055-011]no other "strong man" was likely soon to arise, the Deputy now turned

[055-012]with renewed ardour to the task of establishing the Queen's supremacy,

[055-013]in things spiritual as well as temporal. With this view he urged that

[055-014]separate governments, with large though subordinate military as well as

[055-015]civil powers, should be created for Munster and Connaught—with

[055-016]competent Presidents, who should reside in the former Province at

[055-017]Limerick, and in the latter, at Athlone. In accordance with this

[055-018]scheme—which continued to be acted upon for nearly a century—Sir Edward

[055-019]Fitton was appointed first President of Connaught, and Sir John

[055-020]Perrott, the Queen's illegitimate brother, President of Munster.

[055-021]Leinster and Ulster were reserved as the special charge of the Lord

[055-022]Deputy.

[055-023]About the time of O'Neil's death Sidney made an official progress

[055-024]through the South and West, which he describes as wofully wasted by

[055-025]war, both town and country. The earldom of the loyal Ormond was far

[055-026]from being well ordered; and the other great nobles were even less

[055-027]favourably reported; the Earl of Desmond could neither rule nor be

[055-028]ruled; the Earl of Clancarty "wanted force and credit;" the Earl of

[055-029]Thomond had neither wit to govern "nor grace to learn of others;" the

[055-030]Earl of Clanrickarde was well intentioned, but controlled wholly by his

[055-031]wife. Many districts had but "one-twentieth" of their ancient

[055-032]population; Galway was in a state of perpetual defence. Athenry had but

[055-033]four respectable householders left, and these presented him with the

[055-034]rusty keys of their once famous town, which they confessed themselves

[055-035]unable to defend, impoverished as they were by the extortions of their

[055-036]lords. All this to the eye of the able Englishman had been the result

[055-037]of that "cowardly policy, or lack of policy," whose sole maxims had

[055-038]been to play off the great lords against each other and to retard the

[055-039]growth of population, least "through their quiet might follow" future

[055-040]dangers to the English interest. His own policy was based on very

[055-041]different principles. He proposed to make the highest heads bow to the

[055-042]supremacy of the royal sword—to punish with exemplary rigour every sign

[055-043]of insubordination, especially in the great—and, at the same time, to

[055-044]encourage with ample rewards, adventurers, and enterprises of all

[055-045]kinds. He proposed to himself precisely the part Lord Stafford acted

[055-046]sixty years later, and he entered on it with a will which would have

[055-047]won the admiration of that unbending despot. He prided himself on the

[055-048]number of military executions which marked his progress. "Down they go

[055-049]in every corner," he writes, "and down they shall go, God willing!" He

[055-050]seized the Earl of Desmond in his own town of Kilmallock; he took the

[055-051]sons of Clanrickarde, in Connaught, and carried them prisoners to

[055-052]Dublin. Elizabeth became alarmed at these extreme measures, and Sidney

[055-053]obtained leave to explain his new policy in person to her Majesty.

[055-054]Accordingly in October he sailed for England, taking with him the Earl

[055-055]and his brother John of Desmond, who had been invited to Dublin, and

[055-056]were detained as prisoners of State; Hugh O'Neil, as yet known by no

[055-057]other title than Baron of Dungannon; the O'Conor Sligo, and other

[055-058]chiefs and noblemen. He seems to have carried his policy triumphantly

[055-059]with the Queen, and from henceforth for many a long year "the dulce

[055-060]ways" and "politic drifts" recommended by the great Cardinal Statesman

[055-061]of Henry VIII. were to give way to that remorseless struggle in which

[055-062]the only alternative offered to the Irish was—uniformity or

[055-063]extermination. Of this policy, Sir Henry Sidney may, it seems to me, be

[055-064]fairly considered the author; Stafford, and even Cromwell were but

[055-065]finishers of his work. One cannot repress a sigh that so ferocious a

[055-066]design as the extermination of a whole people should be associated in

[055-067]any degree with the illustrious name of Sidney.

[055-068]The triumphant Deputy arrived at Carrickfergus in September, 1568, from

[055-069]England. Here he received the "submission," as it is called, of

[055-070]Tirlogh, the new O'Neil, and turned his steps southwards in full

[055-071]assurance that this chief of Tyrone was not another "strong man" like

[055-072]the last. A new Privy Council was sworn in on his arrival at Dublin,

[055-073]with royal instructions "to concur with" the Deputy, and 20,000 pounds

[055-074]a year in addition to the whole of the cess levied in the country were

[055-075]guaranteed to enable him to carry out his great scheme of the

[055-076]"reduction." A Parliament was next summoned for the 17th of January,

[055-077]1569, the first assembly of that nature which had been convened since

[055-078]Lord Sussex's rupture with _his_ Parliament nine years before.

[055-079]The acts of this Parliament, of the 11th of Elizabeth, are much more

[055-080]voluminous than those of the 2nd of the same reign. The constitution of

[055-081]the houses is also of interest, as the earlier records of every form of

[055-082]government must always be. Three sessions were held in the first year,

[055-083]one in 1570, and one in 1571. After its dissolution, no Parliament sat

[055-084]in Ireland for fourteen years—so unstable was the system at that time,

[055-085]and so dependent upon accidental causes for its exercise. The first

[055-086]sittings of Sidney's Parliament were as stormy as those of Sussex. It

[055-087]was found that many members presented themselves pretending to

[055-088]represent towns not incorporated, and others, officers of election, had

[055-089]returned themselves. Others, again, were non-resident Englishmen,

[055-090]dependent on the Deputy who had never seen the places for which they

[055-091]claimed to sit. The disputed elections of all classes being referred to

[055-092]the judges, they decided that non-residence did not disqualify the

[055-093]latter class; but that those who had returned themselves, and those

[055-094]chosen for non-corporate towns, were inadmissible. This double decision

[055-095]did not give the new House of Commons quite the desired complexion,

[055-096]though Stanihurst, Recorder of Dublin, the Court candidate, was chosen

[055-097]Speaker. The opposition was led by Sir Christopher Barnewall, an able

[055-098]and intrepid man, to whose firmness it was mainly due that a more

[055-099]sweeping proscription was not enacted, under form of law, at this

[055-100]period. The native Englishmen in the House were extremely unpopular out

[055-101]of doors, and Hooker, one of their number, who sat for the deserted

[055-102]borough of Athenry, had to be escorted to his lodgings by a strong

[055-103]guard, for fear of the Dublin mob. The chief acts of the first session

[055-104]were a subsidy, for ten years, of 13 shillings 4 pence for every

[055-105]ploughland granted to the Queen; an act suspending Poyning's act for

[055-106]the continuance of _that_ Parliament; an act for the attainder of John

[055-107]O'Neil; an act appropriating to her Majesty the lands of the Knight of

[055-108]the Valley; an act authorizing the Lord Deputy to present to vacant

[055-109]benefices in Munster and Connaught for ten years; an act abolishing the

[055-110]title of "Captain," or _ruler_ of counties or districts, unless by

[055-111]special warrant under the great seal; an act for reversing the

[055-112]attainder of the Earl of Kildare. In the sittings of 1570 and '71, the

[055-113]chief acts were for the erection of free schools, for the preservation

[055-114]of the public records, for establishing an uniform measure in the sale

[055-115]of corn, and for the attainder of the White Knight, deceased. Though

[055-116]undoubtedly most of these statutes strengthened Sidney's hands and

[055-117]favoured his policy, they did not go the lengths which in his official

[055-118]correspondence he advocated. For the last seven years of his connection

[055-119]with Irish affairs, he was accordingly disposed to dispense with the

[055-120]unmanageable machinery of a Parliament. Orders in council were much

[055-121]more easily procured than acts of legislation, even when every care had

[055-122]been taken to pack the House of Commons with the dependents of the

[055-123]executive.

[055-124]The meeting of Parliament in 1569 was nearly coincident with the formal

[055-125]excommunication of Elizabeth by Pope Pius V. Though pretending to

[055-126]despise the bull, the Queen was weak enough to seek its revocation,

[055-127]through the interposition of the Emperor Maximilian. The high tone of

[055-128]the enthusiastic Pontiff irritated her deeply, and perhaps the

[055-129]additional severities which she now directed against her Catholic

[055-130]subjects, may be, in part, traced to the effects of the

[055-131]excommunication. In Ireland, the work of reformation, by means of civil

[055-132]disabilities and executive patronage, was continued with earnestness.

[055-133]In 1564, all Popish priests and friars were prohibited from meeting in

[055-134]Dublin, or even coming within the city gates. Two years later, _The

[055-135]Book of Articles_, copied from the English Articles, was published, by

[055-136]order of "the Commissioners for Causes Ecclesiastical." The articles

[055-137]are twelve in number:—1. The Trinity in Unity; 2. The Sufficiency of

[055-138]the Scriptures to Salvation; 3. The Orthodoxy of Particular Churches;

[055-139]4. The Necessity of Holy Orders; 5. The Queen's Supremacy; 6. Denial of

[055-140]the Pope's authority "to be more than other Bishops have;" 7. The

[055-141]Conformity of the Book of Common Prayer to the Scriptures; 8. The

[055-142]Ministration of Baptism does not depend on the Ceremonial; 9. Condemns

[055-143]"Private Masses," and denies that the Mass can be a propitiatory

[055-144]Sacrifice for the Dead; 10. Asserts the Propriety of Communion in Both

[055-145]Kinds; 11. Utterly disallows Images, Relics and Pilgrimages; 12.

[055-146]Requires a General Subscription to the foregoing Articles. With this

[055-147]creed, the Irish Establishment started into existence, at the command

[055-148]and, of course, with all the aid of the civil power. The Bishops of

[055-149]Meath and Kildare, the nearest to Dublin, for resisting it were

[055-150]banished their sees; the former to die an exile in Spain, the latter to

[055-151]find refuge and protection with the Earl of Desmond. Several Prelates

[055-152]were tolerated in their sees, on condition of observing a species of

[055-153]neutrality; but all vacancies, if within the reach of the English

[055-154]power, were filled as they occurred by nominees of the crown. Those who

[055-155]actively and energetically resisted the new doctrines were marked out

[055-156]for vengeance, and we shall see in the next decade how Ireland's martyr

[055-157]age began.

[055-159]The honour and danger of organizing resistance to the progress of the

[055-160]new religion now devolved upon the noble family of the Geraldines of

[055-161]Munster, of whose principal members we must, therefore, give some

[055-162]account. The fifteenth Earl, who had concurred in the act of Henry's

[055-163]election, died in the year of Elizabeth's accession (1558), leaving

[055-164]three sons, Gerald the sixteenth Earl, John, and James. He had also an

[055-165]elder son by a first wife, from whom he had been divorced on the ground

[055-166]of consanguinity. This son disputed the succession unsuccessfully,

[055-167]retired to Spain, and there died. Earl Gerald, though one of the Peers

[055-168]who sat in the Parliament of the second year of Elizabeth, was one of

[055-169]those who strenuously opposed the policy of Sussex, and still more

[055-170]strenuously, as may be supposed, the more extreme policy of Sidney. His

[055-171]reputation, however, as a leader, suffered severely by the combat of

[055-172]Affane, in which he was taken prisoner by Thomas, the tenth Earl of

[055-173]Ormond, with whom he was at feud on a question of boundaries. By order

[055-174]of the Queen, the Lord Deputy was appointed arbitrator in this case,

[055-175]and though the decision was in favour of Ormond, Desmond submitted,

[055-176]came to Dublin, and was reconciled with his enemy in the chapter house

[055-177]of St. Patrick's. A year or two later, Gerald turned his arms against

[055-178]the ancient rivals of his house—the McCarthys of Muskerry and

[055-179]Duhallow—but was again taken prisoner, and after six months' detention,

[055-180]held to ransom by the Lord of Muskerry. After his release, the old feud

[055-181]with Ormond broke out anew—a most impolitic quarrel, as that Earl was

[055-182]not only personally a favourite with the Queen, but was also nearly

[055-183]connected with her in blood through the Boleyns. In 1567, as before

[055-184]related, Desmond was seized by surprise in his town of Kilmallock by

[055-185]Sidney's order, and the following autumn conveyed to London on a charge

[055-186]of treason and lodged in the Tower. This was the third prison he had

[055-187]lodged in within three years, and by far the most hopeless of the

[055-188]three. His brother, Sir John of Desmond, through the representations of

[055-189]Ormond, was the same year arrested and consigned to the same ominous

[055-190]dungeon, from which suspected noblemen seldom emerged, except when the

[055-191]hurdle waited for them at the gate.

[055-192]This double capture aroused the indignation of all the tribes of

[055-193]Desmond, and led to the formidable combination which, in reference to

[055-194]the previous confederacy in the reign of Henry, may be called "the

[055-195]second Geraldine League." The Earl of Clancarty, and such of the

[055-196]O'Briens, McCarthys, and Butlers, as had resolved to resist the

[055-197]complete revolution in property, religion, and law, which Sidney

[055-198]meditated, united together to avenge the wrongs of those noblemen,

[055-199]their neighbours, so treacherously arrested and so cruelly confined.

[055-200]Sir James, son of Sir Maurice Fitzgerald of Kerry, commonly called

[055-201]James Fitz-Maurice, cousin-germain to the imprisoned noblemen, was

[055-202]chosen leader of the insurrection. He was, according to the testimony

[055-203]of an enemy, Hooker, member for Athenry, "a deep dissembler, passing

[055-204]subtile, and able to compass any matter he took in hand; courteous,

[055-205]valiant, expert in martial affairs." To this we may add that he had

[055-206]already reached a mature age; was deeply and sincerely devoted to his

[055-207]religion; and, according to the eulogist of the rival house of Ormond,

[055-208]one whom nothing could deject or bow down, a scorner of luxury and

[055-209]ease, insensible to danger, impervious to the elements, preferring,

[055-210]after a hard day's fighting, the bare earth to a luxurious couch.

[055-211]One of the first steps of the League was to despatch an embassy for

[055-212]assistance to the King of Spain and the Pope. The Archbishop of Cashel,

[055-213]the Bishop of Emly, and James, the youngest brother of Desmond, were

[055-214]appointed on this mission, of which Sidney was no sooner apprised than

[055-215]he proclaimed the confederates traitors, and at once prepared for a

[055-216]campaign in Munster. The first blow was struck by the taking of

[055-217]Clogrennan Castle, which belonged to Sir Edmond Butler, one of the

[055-218]adherents of the League. The attack was led by Sir Peter Carew, an

[055-219]English adventurer, who had lately appeared at Dublin to claim the

[055-220]original grant made to Robert Fitzstephen of the moiety of the kingdom

[055-221]of Cork, and who at present commanded the garrison of Kilkenny. The

[055-222]accomplished soldier of fortune anticipated the Deputy's movements by

[055-223]this blow at the confederated Butlers, who retaliated by an abortive

[055-224]attack on Kilkenny, and a successful foray into Wexford, in which they

[055-225]took the Castle of Enniscorthy. Sidney, taking the field in person,

[055-226]marched through Waterford and Dungarvan against Desmond's strongholds

[055-227]in the vicinity of Youghal. After a week's siege he took Castlemartyr,

[055-228]and continued his route through Barrymore to Cork, where he established

[055-229]his head-quarters. From Cork, upon receiving the submission of some

[055-230]timid members of the League, he continued his route to Limerick, where

[055-231]Sir Edmond Butler and his brothers were induced to come in by their

[055-232]chief the Earl of Ormond. From Limerick he penetrated Clare, took the

[055-233]Castles of Clonoon and Ballyvaughan; he next halted some time at

[055-234]Galway, and returned to Dublin by Athlone. Overawed by the activity of

[055-235]the Deputy, many others of the confederates followed the example of the

[055-236]Butlers. The Earl of Clancarty sued for pardon and delivered up his

[055-237]eldest son as a hostage for his good faith; the Earl of Thomond—more

[055-238]suspected than compromised—yielded all his castles, with the sole

[055-239]exception of Ibrackan. But the next year, mortified at the

[055-240]insignificance to which he had reduced himself, he sought refuge in

[055-241]France, from which he only returned when the intercession of the

[055-242]English ambassador, Norris, had obtained him full indemnity for the

[055-243]past. Sir James Fitzmaurice, thus deserted by his confederates, had

[055-244]need of all that unyielding firmness of character for which he had

[055-245]obtained credit. Castle after castle belonging to his cousins and

[055-246]himself was taken by the powerful siege trains of President Perrott;

[055-247]Castlemaine, the last stronghold which commanded an outlet by sea,

[055-248]surrendered after a three months' siege, gallantly maintained. The

[055-249]unyielding leader had now, therefore, no alternative but to retire into

[055-250]the impregnable passes of the Galtees, where he established his

[055-251]head-quarters. This mountain range, towering from two to three thousand

[055-252]feet over the plain of Ormond, stretches from north-west to south-east,

[055-253]some twenty miles, descending with many a gentle undulation towards the

[055-254]Funcheon and the Blackwater in the earldom of Desmond. Of all its

[055-255]valleys Aharlow was the fairest and most secluded. Well wooded, and

[055-256]well watered, with outlets and intricacies known only to the native

[055-257]population, it seemed as if designed for a nursery of insurrection. It

[055-258]now became to the patriots of the South what the valley of Glenmalure

[055-259]had long been for those of Leinster—a fortress dedicated by Nature to

[055-260]the defence of freedom. In this fastness Fitzmaurice continued to

[055-261]maintain himself, until a prospect of new combinations opened to him in

[055-262]the West.

[055-263]The sons of the Earl of Clanrickarde, though released from the custody

[055-264]of Sidney, receiving intimation that they were to be arrested at a

[055-265]court which Fitton, President of Connaught, had summoned at Galway,

[055-266]flew to arms and opened negotiations with Fitzmaurice. The latter,

[055-267]withdrawing from Aharlow, promptly joined them in Galway, and during

[055-268]the campaign which followed, aided them with his iron energy and

[055-269]sagacious counsel. They took and demolished the works of Athenry, and,

[055-270]in part, those of the Court of Athlone. Their successes induced the

[055-271]Deputy to liberate Clanrickarde himself, who had been detained a

[055-272]prisoner in Dublin, from the outbreak of his sons. On his return—their

[055-273]main object being attained—they submitted as promptly as they had

[055-274]revolted, and this hope also being quenched, Fitzmaurice found his way

[055-275]back again, with a handful of Scottish retainers, to the shelter of

[055-276]Aharlow. Sir John Perrott, having by this time no further sieges to

[055-277]prosecute, drew his toils closer and closer round the Geraldine's

[055-278]retreat. For a whole year, the fidelity of his adherents and the

[055-279]natural strength of the place enabled him to baffle all the President's

[055-280]efforts. But his faithful Scottish guards being at length surprised and

[055-281]cut off almost to a man, Fitzmaurice, with his son, his kinsman, the

[055-282]Seneschal of Imokilly, and the son of Richard Burke, surrendered to the

[055-283]President at Kilmallock, suing on his knees for the Queen's pardon,

[055-284]which was, from motives of policy, granted.

[055-285]On this conclusion of the contest in Munster, the Earl of Desmond and

[055-286]his brother, Sir John, were released from the Tower, and transferred to

[055-287]Dublin, where they were treated as prisoners on parole. The Mayor of

[055-288]the city, who was answerable for their custody, having taken them upon

[055-289]a hunting party in the open country, the brothers put spurs to their

[055-290]horses and escaped into Munster (1574). They were stigmatized as having

[055-291]broken their parole, but they asserted that it was intended on that

[055-292]party to waylay and murder them, and that their only safety was in

[055-293]flight. Large rewards were offered for their capture, alive or dead,

[055-294]but the necessities of both parties compelled a truce during the

[055-295]remainder of Sidney's official career—which terminated in his

[055-296]resignation—about four years after the escape of the Desmonds from

[055-297]Dublin. Thus were new elements of combination, at the moment least

[055-298]expected, thrown, into the hands of the Munster Catholics.
