## Overview

Dr. William Drennan (1754–1820) was a Belfast-born physician who became the poet-politician of the United Irishmen. His most famous contribution to the movement was the "Universal Emancipation" address, a powerful statement of republican principles that became one of the celebrated documents of the revolutionary era. (100-076) (100-077)

Drennan's literary gifts gave the United Irish movement an eloquence that matched its political energy. His address, circulated among the United societies, expressed the aspiration for a brotherhood that would transcend the sectarian divisions of Irish society. (100-076)

## The "Universal Emancipation" Address

The "Universal Emancipation" address was the defining document of Drennan's political career. Written for his friends in the United Irishmen, it called for a complete emancipation from both religious bigotry and political subordination, setting forth the ideal of an Irish nation united across the lines of creed and class. (100-075) (100-076)

> The "Universal Emancipation" address of his friend, Dr. William Drennan, the poet-politician of the party, was circulated among the United societies and became one of the most celebrated documents of the movement. [100-076] [100-077]

The address proved too dangerous for the authorities to ignore. One of Drennan's associates was convicted by a packed jury of circulating the address, and he was defended by John Philpot Curran in what became a still more famous speech. (100-075) (100-078)

## The Curran Defence and Its Aftermath

The trial of Drennan's associate produced one of the great moments of Irish legal oratory. Curran, in his defence, delivered the celebrated apostrophe to "the genius of Universal Emancipation" that immortalized Drennan's phrase and gave it a permanent place in Irish political rhetoric. (100-077) (100-078) (100-079)

> He was defended by Curran, in the still more famous speech in which occurs his apostrophe to "the genius of Universal Emancipation;" but he atoned in the cells of Newgate, for circulating the dangerous doctrine which Drennan had broached, and Curran had immortalized. [100-078] [100-079] [100-080]

Despite Curran's eloquence, Drennan's friend was convicted and imprisoned in Newgate. The trial demonstrated the determination of the Dublin Castle administration to suppress the United Irish press and to punish those who disseminated republican doctrine, however eloquently expressed. (100-079) (100-080)

## The Poet-Politician

Drennan's role as the "poet-politician" of the United Irishmen reflected a broader tradition in Irish political life, where literary talent and political commitment were often combined. His writing provided the movement with memorable phrases and elevated its aspirations beyond mere organizational politics. (100-076) (100-077)

The dangerous doctrine which Drennan had broached — "Universal Emancipation" — represented the most radical ideal of the United Irish project: a nation in which Catholic, Protestant, and Dissenter would stand together as equal citizens. Though the movement failed in its immediate aims, Drennan's phrase remained a touchstone for later generations of Irish reformers. (100-080)

## Legacy

William Drennan is remembered as one of the intellectual architects of Irish republicanism. His "Universal Emancipation" address, preserved and amplified by Curran's immortal defence, ensured that the United Irish ideal of a non-sectarian Irish nation survived the defeat of 1798. (100-076) (100-078)

His career as a physician-poet in the service of political emancipation established a model of the engaged intellectual that would influence Irish political culture throughout the 19th century. The doctrine he broached — of a universal emancipation transcending religious divisions — remained an unfulfilled aspiration that shaped Irish nationalism long after his death. (100-080) (100-076)
