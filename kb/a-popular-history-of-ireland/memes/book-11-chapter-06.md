CHAPTER VI.

[093-001]FLOOD'S LEADERSHIP—STATE OF THE COUNTRY BETWEEN 1760 AND 1776.

[093-002]England was engaged in two great wars during the period of Flood's

[093-003]supremacy in the Irish Parliament—the seven years' war, concluded by

[093-004]the peace of Paris in 1763, and the American war, concluded by the

[093-005]treaty of Versailles, in 1783. To each of these wars Ireland was the

[093-006]second largest contributor both as to men and money; and by both she

[093-007]was the severest sufferer, in her manufactures, her provision trade,

[093-008]and her general prosperity. While army contracts, and all sorts of

[093-009]military and naval expenditure in a variety of ways returned to the

[093-010]people of England the produce of their taxes, the Irish had no such

[093-011]compensation for the burdens imposed on their more limited resources.

[093-012]The natural result was, that that incipient prosperity which

[093-013]Chesterfield hailed with pleasure in 1751, was arrested in its growth,

[093-014]and fears began to be seriously entertained that the country would be

[093-015]driven back to the lamentable condition from which it had slowly and

[093-016]laboriously emerged during the reign of George II.

[093-017]The absence of employment in the towns threw the labouring classes more

[093-018]and more upon the soil for sustenance, while the landlord legislation

[093-019]of the period threw them as helplessly back upon other pursuits than

[093-020]agriculture. Agrarian injustice was encountered by conspiracy, and for

[093-021]the first time in these pages, we have to record the introduction of

[093-022]the diabolical machinery of secret oath-bound associations among the

[093-023]Irish peasantry. Of the first of these combinations in the southern

[093-024]counties, a cotemporary writer gives the following account: "Some

[093-025]landlords in Munster," he says, "have let their lands to cotters far

[093-026]above their value, and, to lighten their burden, allowed commonange to

[093-027]their tenants by way of recompense: afterwards, in despite of all

[093-028]equity, contrary to all compacts, the landlords enclosed these commons,

[093-029]and precluded their unhappy tenants from the only means of making their

[093-030]bargains tolerable." The peasantry of Waterford, Cork, and other

[093-031]southern counties met in tumultuous crowds, and demolished the new

[093-032]enclosures. The oligarchical majority took their usual cue on such

[093-033]occasions: they pronounced, at once, that the cause of the riots was

[093-034]"treason against the state;" they even obtained a select committee to

[093-035]"inquire into the cause and progress of the Popish insurrection in

[093-036]Munster." Although the London Gazette, on the authority of royal

[093-037]commissioners, declared that the rioters "consisted indiscriminately of

[093-038]persons of different persuasions," the Castle party would have it

[093-039]"another Popish plot." Even Dr. Lucas was carried away by the passions

[093-040]of the hour, and declaimed against all lenity, as cowardly and

[093-041]criminal.

[093-042]A large military force, under the Marquis of Drogheda, was accordingly

[093-043]despatched to the south. The Marquis fixed his head-quarters at

[093-044]Clogheen, in Tipperary, the parish priest of which was the Rev.

[093-045]Nicholas Sheehy. The magistracy of the county, especially Sir Thomas

[093-046]Maude, William Bagnel, John Bagwell, Daniel Toler, and Parson Hewitson,

[093-047]were among the chief maintainers of the existence of a Popish plot, to

[093-048]bring in the French and the Pretender. Father Sheehy had long been

[093-049]fixed upon as their victim: largely connected with the minor gentry,

[093-050]educated in France, young, popular, eloquent and energetic, a stern

[093-051]denouncer of the licentious lives of the squires, and of the exacting

[093-052]tithes of the parsons, he was particularly obnoxious. In 1763 he was

[093-053]arrested on a charge of high treason, for drilling and enrolling

[093-054]Whiteboys, but was acquitted. Towards the close of that year, Bridge,

[093-055]one of the late witnesses against him, suddenly disappeared. A charge

[093-056]of murder was then laid against the priest of Clogheen, and a

[093-057]prostitute named Dunlea, a vagrant lad named Lonergan, and a convicted

[093-058]horse stealer called Toohey, were produced in evidence against him,

[093-059]after he had lain nearly a year in prison, heavily fettered. On the

[093-060]12th of March, 1765, he was tried at Clonmel, on this evidence; and

[093-061]notwithstanding an _alibi_ was proved, he was condemned, and beheaded

[093-062]on the third day afterwards. Beside the old ruined church of

[093-063]Shandraghan, his well-worn tomb remains till this day. He died in his

[093-064]thirty-eighth year. Two months later, Edward Sheehy, his cousin, and

[093-065]two respectable young farmers, named Buxton and Farrell, were executed

[093-066]under a similar charge, and upon the same testimony. All died with

[093-067]religious firmness and composure. The fate of their enemies is

[093-068]notorious; with a single exception, they met deaths violent, loathsome,

[093-069]and terrible. Maude died insane, Bagwell in idiocy, one of the jury

[093-070]committed suicide, another was found dead in a privy, a third was

[093-071]killed by his horse, a fourth was drowned, a fifth shot, and so through

[093-072]the entire list. Toohey was hanged for felony, the prostitute Dunlea

[093-073]fell into a cellar and was killed, and the lad Lonergan, after

[093-074]enlisting as a soldier, died of a loathsome disease in a Dublin

[093-075]infirmary.

[093-076]In 1767, an attempt to revive the plot was made by the Munster

[093-077]oligarchy, without success. Dr. McKenna, Bishop of Cloyne, was arrested

[093-078]but enlarged; Mr. Nagle, of Garnavilla (a relative of Edmund Burke),

[093-079]Mr. Robert Keating, and several respectable Catholic gentlemen, were

[093-080]also arrested. It appears that Edmund Burke was charged by the

[093-081]ascendancy party with having "sent his brother Richard, recorder of

[093-082]Bristol, and Mr. Nagle, a relation, on a mission to Munster, to levy

[093-083]money on the Popish body for the use of the Whiteboys, who were

[093-084]exclusively Papists." The fact was, that Burke did originate a

[093-085]subscription for the defence of the second batch of victims, who,

[093-086]through his and other exertions, were fortunately saved from the fate

[093-087]of their predecessors.

[093-088]Contemporaneous with the Whiteboys were the northern agrarians, called

[093-089]"Hearts of Steel," formed among the absentee Lord Downshire's tenants,

[093-090]in 1762; the "Oak Boys," so called from wearing oak leaves in their

[093-091]hats; and the "Peep o' Day Boys," the precursors of the Orange

[093-092]Association. The infection of conspiracy ran through all Ireland, and

[093-093]the disorder was neither short-lived nor trivial. Right-boys,

[093-094]Defenders, and a dozen other denominations descended from the same evil

[093-095]genius, whoever he was, that first introduced the system of signs, and

[093-096]passwords, and midnight meetings, among the peasantry of Ireland. The

[093-097]celebrated society of United Irishmen was the highest form which that

[093-098]principle, in our politics, ever reached. In its origin, it was mainly

[093-099]a Protestant organization.

[093-100]From the first, the Catholic bishops and clergy strenuously opposed

[093-101]these secret societies. The Bishop of Cloyne issued a reprobatory

[093-102]pastoral; Father Arthur O'Leary employed his facile pen against them;

[093-103]the Bishop of Ossory anathematized them in his diocese. Priests in

[093-104]Kildare, Kilkenny, and Munster, were often in personal danger from

[093-105]these midnight legislators; their chapels had been frequently nailed

[093-106]up, and their bishops had been often obliged to remove them from one

[093-107]neighbourhood to another to prevent worse consequences. The infatuation

[093-108]was not to be stayed; the evil was engrafted on society, and many a

[093-109]long year, and woeful scene, and blighted life, and broken heart, was

[093-110]to signalize the perpetuation of secret societies among the population.

[093-111]These startling symptoms of insubordination and lawlessness, while they

[093-112]furnished plausible pretexts to the advocates of repression, still

[093-113]further confirmed the Patriot party in their belief, that, nothing

[093-114]short of a free trade in exports and imports, and a thorough system of

[093-115]retrenchment in every branch of the public service, could save the

[093-116]nation from bankruptcy and ruin. This was Flood's opinion, and he had

[093-117]been long recognized as the leading spirit of the party. The aged

[093-118]Malone, true to his principles of conciliation and constitutionalism to

[093-119]the last, passed away from the scene, in the midst of the exciting

[093-120]events of 1776. For some years before his death, his former place had

[093-121]been filled by the younger and more vigorous member for Kilkenny, who,

[093-122]however, did not fail to consult him with all the deference due to his

[093-123]age, his services, and his wisdom. One of his last official acts was

[093-124]presiding over the committee of the whole House, which voted the

[093-125]American contingent, but rejected the admission of German troops to

[093-126]supply their place.
