CHAPTER III.

[037-001]BRUCE'S SECOND CAMPAIGN, AND CORONATION AT DUNDALK—THE RISING IN

[037-002]CONNAUGHT—BATTLE OF ATHENRY—ROBERT BRUCE IN IRELAND.

[037-003]From Loughsweedy, Bruce broke up his quarters, and marched into

[037-004]Kildare, encamping successively at Naas, Kildare, and Rathangan.

[037-005]Advancing in a southerly direction, he found an immense, but disorderly

[037-006]Anglo-Irish host drawn out, at the moat of Ardscull, near Athy, to

[037-007]dispute his march. They were commanded by the Lord Justice Butler, the

[037-008]Baron of Offally, the Lord Arnold Poer, and other magnates; but so

[037-009]divided were these proud Peers, in authority and in feeling, that,

[037-010]after a severe skirmish with Bruce's vanguard, in which some knights

[037-011]were killed on both sides, they retreated before the Hiberno-Scottish

[037-012]army, which continued its march unmolested, and took possession of

[037-013]Castledermot.

[037-014]Animated by these successes, won in their midst, the clans of Leinster

[037-015]began in succession to raise their heads. The tribes of Wicklow, once

[037-016]possessors of the fertile plains to the east and west, rallied in the

[037-017]mountain glens to which they had been driven, and commenced that long

[037-018]guerilla war, which centuries only were to extinguish. The McMurroghs

[037-019]along the ridge of Leinster, and all their kindred upon the Barrow and

[037-020]the Slaney, mustered under a chief, against whom the Lord Justice was

[037-021]compelled to march in person, later in the campaign of 1316. The Lord

[037-022]of Dunamase was equally sanguine, but 800 men of the name of O'Moore,

[037-023]slain in one disastrous encounter, crippled for the time the military

[037-024]strength of that great house. Having thus kindled the war, in the very

[037-025]heart of Leinster, Bruce retraced his march through Meath and Louth,

[037-026]and held at Dundalk that great assembly in which he was solemnly

[037-027]elected King of Ireland. Donald O'Neil, by letters patent, as son of

[037-028]Brian "of the battle of Down," the last acknowledged native king,

[037-029]formally resigned his right, in favour of Bruce, a proceeding which he

[037-030]defends in his celebrated letter to Pope John XXII., where he speaks of

[037-031]the new sovereign as the illustrious Earl of Carrick, Edward de Bruce,

[037-032]a nobleman descended from the same ancestors with themselves, whom they

[037-033]had called to their aid, and freely chosen as their king and lord. The

[037-034]ceremony of inauguration seems to have been performed in the Gaelic

[037-035]fashion, on the hill of Knocknemelan, within a mile of Dundalk, while

[037-036]the solemn consecration took place in one of the churches of the town.

[037-037]Surrounded by all the external marks of royalty, Bruce established his

[037-038]court in the castle of Northburgh (one of de Courcy's or de Verdon's

[037-039]fortresses), adjoining Dundalk, where he took cognizance of all pleas

[037-040]that were brought before him. At that moment his prospects compared

[037-041]favourably with those of his illustrious brother a few years earlier.

[037-042]The Anglo-Irish were bitterly divided against each other; while,

[037-043]according to their joint declaration of loyalty, signed before de

[037-044]Hothun, King Edward's special agent, "all the Irish of Ireland, several

[037-045]great lords, and many English people," had given in their adhesion to

[037-046]Bruce. In Ulster, except Carrickfergus, no place of strength remained

[037-047]in the hands of any subject of Edward of England. The arrival of

[037-048]supplies from Scotland enabled Bruce to resume that siege in the autumn

[037-049]of 1316, and the castle, after a heroic defence by Sir Thomas de

[037-050]Mandeville, was surrendered in mid-winter. Here, in the month of

[037-051]February, 1317, the new King of Ireland had the gratification of

[037-052]welcoming his brother of Scotland, at the head of a powerful auxiliary

[037-053]force, and here, according to Barbour's _Chronicle_, they feasted for

[037-054]three days, in mirth and jollity, before entering on the third campaign

[037-055]of this war.

[037-056]We have before mentioned that one of the first successes obtained by

[037-057]Bruce was through the withdrawal of Felim O'Conor from the Red Earl's

[037-058]alliance. The Prince thus won over to what may be fairly called the

[037-059]national cause, had just then attained his majority, and his martial

[037-060]accomplishments reflected honour on his fosterer, McDermott of Moylurg,

[037-061]while they filled with confidence the hearts of his own clansmen. After

[037-062]his secession from de Burgh at Coleraine, he had spent a whole year in

[037-063]suppressing the formidable rival who had risen to dispute his title.

[037-064]Several combats ensued between their respective adherents, but at

[037-065]length Roderick, the pretender, was defeated and slain, and Felim

[037-066]turned all his energies to co-operate with Bruce, by driving the

[037-067]foreigner out of his own province. Having secured the assistance of all

[037-068]the chief tribes of the west, and established the ancient supremacy of

[037-069]his house over Breffni, he first attacked the town of Ballylahen, in

[037-070]Mayo, the seat of the family of de Exeter, slew Slevin de Exeter, the

[037-071]lord de Cogan, and other knights and barons, and plundered the town. At

[037-072]the beginning of August in the same year, in pursuance of his plan,

[037-073]Felim mustered the most numerous force which Connaught had sent forth,

[037-074]since the days of Cathal More. Under his leadership marched the Prince

[037-075]of Meath, the lords of Breffni, Leyny, Annally, Teffia, Hy-Many, and

[037-076]Hy-Fiachra, with their men. The point of attack was the town of

[037-077]Athenry, the chief fortified stronghold of the de Burghs and

[037-078]Berminghams in that region. Its importance dated from the reign of King

[037-079]John; it had been enriched with convents and strengthened by towers; it

[037-080]was besides the burial place of the two great Norman families just

[037-081]mentioned, and their descendants felt that before the walls of Athenry

[037-082]their possessions were to be confirmed to them by their own valour, or

[037-083]lost for ever. A decisive battle was fought on St. Laurence's day—the

[037-084]10th of August—in which the steel-clad Norman battalion once more

[037-085]triumphed over the linen-shirted clansmen of the west. The field was

[037-086]contested with heroic obstinacy; no man gave way; none thought of

[037-087]asking or giving quarter. The standard bearer, the personal guard, and

[037-088]the Brehon of O'Conor fell around him. The lords of Hy-Many, Teffia,

[037-089]and Leyny, the heir of the house of Moylurg, with many other chiefs,

[037-090]and, according to the usual computation, 8,000 men were slain. Felim

[037-091]O'Conor himself, in the twenty-third year of his age, and the very

[037-092]morning of his fame, fell with the rest, and his kindred, the

[037-093]Sil-Murray, were left for a season an easy prey to William de Burgh and

[037-094]John de Bermingham, the joint commanders in the battle. The spirit of

[037-095]exaggeration common in most accounts of killed and wounded, has

[037-096]described this day as fatal to the name and race of O'Conor, who are

[037-097]represented as cut off to a man in the conflict; the direct line which

[037-098]Felim represented was indeed left without an immediate adult

[037-099]representative; but the offshoots of that great house had spread too

[037-100]far and flourished too vigorously to be shorn away, even by so terrible

[037-101]a blow as that dealt at Athenry. The very next year we find chiefs of

[037-102]the name making some figure in the wars of their own province, but it

[037-103]is observable that what may be called the national party in Connaught

[037-104]for some time after Athenry, looked to McDermott of Moylurg as their

[037-105]most powerful leader.

[037-106]The moral effect of the victory of Athenry was hardly to be compensated

[037-107]for by the capture of Carrickfergus the next winter. It inspired the

[037-108]Anglo-Irish with new courage. De Bermingham was created

[037-109]commander-in-chief. The citizens of Dublin burned their suburbs to

[037-110]strengthen their means of defence. Suspecting the zeal of the Red Earl,

[037-111]so nearly connected with the Bruces by marriage, their Mayor proceeded

[037-112]to Saint Mary's abbey, where he lodged, arrested and confined him to

[037-113]the castle. To that building the Bermingham tower was added about this

[037-114]time, and the strength of the whole must have been great when the

[037-115]skilful leaders, who had carried Stirling and Berwick, abandoned the

[037-116]siege of Dublin as hopeless. In Easter week, 1317, Roger Mortimer,

[037-117]afterwards Earl of March, nearly allied to the English King on the one

[037-118]hand, and maternally descended from the Marshals and McMurroghs on the

[037-119]other, arrived at Youghal, as Lord Justice, released the Earl of Ulster

[037-120]on reaching Dublin, and prepared to dispute the progress of the Bruces

[037-121]towards the South.

[037-122]The royal brothers had determined, according to their national Bard, to

[037-123]take their way with all their host, from one end of Ireland to the

[037-124]other. Their destination was Munster, which populous province had not

[037-125]yet ratified the recent election. Ulster and Meath were with them;

[037-126]Connaught, by the battle of Athenry, was rendered incapable of any

[037-127]immediate effort, and therefore Edward Bruce, in true Gaelic fashion,

[037-128]decided to proceed on his royal visitation, and so secure the hostages

[037-129]of the southern half-kingdom. At the head of 20,000 men, in two

[037-130]divisions, the brothers marched from Carrickfergus; meeting, with the

[037-131]exception of a severe skirmish in a wood near Slane, with no other

[037-132]molestation till they approached the very walls of Dublin. Finding the

[037-133]place stronger than they expected, or unwilling to waste time at that

[037-134]season of the year, the Hiberno-Scottish army, after occupying

[037-135]Castleknock, turned up the valley of the Liffey, and encamped for four

[037-136]days by the pleasant waterfall of Leixlip. From Leixlip to Naas they

[037-137]traversed the estates of one of their active foes, the new made Earl of

[037-138]Kildare, and from Naas they directed their march to Callan in Ossory,

[037-139]taking special pleasure, according to Anglo-Irish Annals, in harrying

[037-140]the lands of another enemy, the Lord Butler, afterwards Earl of Ormond.

[037-141]From Callan their route lay to Cashel and Limerick, at each of which

[037-142]they encamped two or three days without seeing the face of an enemy.

[037-143]But if they encountered no enemies in Munster, neither did they make

[037-144]many friends by their expedition. It seems that on further acquaintance

[037-145]rivalries and enmities sprung up between the two nations who composed

[037-146]the army; that Edward Bruce, while styling himself King of Ireland,

[037-147]acted more like a vigorous conqueror exhausting his enemies, than a

[037-148]prudent Prince careful for his friends and adherents. His army is

[037-149]accused, in terms of greater vehemence than are usually employed in our

[037-150]cautious chronicles, of plundering churches and monasteries, and even

[037-151]violating the tombs of the dead in search of buried treasure. The

[037-152]failure of the harvest, added to the effect of a threefold war, had so

[037-153]diminished the stock of food that numbers perished of famine, and this

[037-154]dark, indelible remembrance was, by an arbitrary notion of cause and

[037-155]effect, inseparably associated in the popular mind, both English and

[037-156]Irish, with the Scottish invasion. One fact is clear, that the election

[037-157]of Dundalk was not popular in Munster, and that the chiefs of Thomond

[037-158]and Desmond were uncommitted, if not hostile towards Bruce's

[037-159]sovereignty. McCarthy and O'Brien seized the occasion, indeed, while he

[037-160]was campaigning in the North, to root out the last representative of

[037-161]the family of de Clare, as we have already related, when tracing the

[037-162]fortunes of the Normans in Munster. But of the twelve reguli, or

[037-163]Princes in Bruce's train, none are mentioned as having come from the

[037-164]Southern provinces.

[037-166]This visitation of Munster occupied the months of February and March.

[037-167]In April, the Lord Justice Mortimer summoned a Parliament at Kilkenny,

[037-168]and there, also, the whole Anglo-Irish forces, to the number of 30,000

[037-169]men, were assembled. The Bruces on their return northward might easily

[037-170]have been intercepted, or the genius which triumphed at Bannockburn

[037-171]might have been as conspicuously signalized on Irish ground. But the

[037-172]military authorities were waiting orders from the Parliament, and the

[037-173]Parliament were at issue with the new Justice, and so the opportunity

[037-174]was lost. Early in May, the Hiberno-Scottish army re-entered Ulster, by

[037-175]nearly the same route as they had taken going southwards, and King

[037-176]Robert soon after returned into Scotland, promising faithfully to

[037-177]rejoin his brother, as soon as he disposed of his own pressing affairs.

[037-178]The King of England in the meantime, in consternation at the news from

[037-179]Ireland, applied to the Pope, then at Avignon, to exercise his

[037-180]influence with the Clergy and Chiefs of Ireland, for the preservation

[037-181]of the English interest in that country. It was in answer to the Papal

[037-182]rescripts so procured that Donald O'Neil despatched his celebrated

[037-183]Remonstrance, which the Pontiff enclosed to Edward II., with an urgent

[037-184]recommendation that the wrongs therein recited might be atoned for, and

[037-185]avoided in the future.
