Oliver St John, 1st Viscount Grandison, served as Lord Deputy of Ireland from 1616 to 1622, succeeding Sir Arthur Chichester in the office. His administration, together with that of his successor [[Lord Falkland|Henry Carey, Viscount Falkland]], spanned the middle years of James I's reign. This was a period when the Dublin government consolidated its authority and extended new mechanisms of control over Irish land tenure. (065-008)

McGee describes Grandison's deputyship in the context of a series of English governors who ruled Ireland without recourse to Parliament. Nothing, he notes, could well be more significant than the absence of any effort to convene the Irish legislature during these years. (065-010)

## Administration and the Commission for Defective Titles

The most notable innovation of Grandison's administration was the establishment of "the Commission for the Discovery of Defective Titles." This body, placed under the direction of Sir William Parsons, Surveyor-General, was empowered to investigate flaws in Irish land grants. Any technical defect in title could provide grounds for confiscation or forced surrender and re-grant at increased rents. (065-029)

> "A new instrument of oppression was also, in Lord Grandison's time, invented—'the Commission for the Discovery of Defective Titles.'" [065-029]

The Commission represented a departure from the earlier policy of negotiated surrenders followed by re-grants under the English legal system. Under Grandison, the search for defective titles became systematic. Parsons, who had arrived in Ireland in humble circumstances, rose to prominence through this mechanism. McGee records that he had come into the kingdom "with hardly the price of a horse." (065-031)

## The Edicts and Recusancy Fines

Grandison's administration issued several edicts that McGee singles out as characteristic of the period. These included the banishment of Catholic clergy on pain of death and the imposition of heavy fines upon recusants — those who refused to attend the established Protestant church. (065-019)

The recusancy fines fell particularly hard upon the older Anglo-Irish families of the Pale, who remained overwhelmingly Catholic. By Grandison's time, the Tudor religious settlement had been in force for over half a century, yet the great majority of the population continued to practice the old religion. The enforcement of conformity through financial penalties became a persistent feature of English administration in Ireland. (065-020)

The administration also witnessed the growing influence of the Presbyterian Scots in Ulster. The Antrim Scots could already reckon a large population, and their political sympathies were decidedly anti-monarchical. McGee notes that they were "far from being politically powerless" and that their influence upon the King was a factor in the politics of the period. (065-057)

## Recall and Legacy

Grandison's recall from the government in 1622 was attributed to the influence of the Ulster Scots, whose growing strength and distinct political and religious character increasingly concerned the Dublin administration. When James Usher, then Bishop of Meath, preached before Grandison's successor from the text "He beareth not the sword in vain," the warning was directed at an administration that had alienated powerful constituencies. (065-058)

Grandison was succeeded by Henry Carey, Viscount Falkland, who governed from 1622 to 1629. The Commission for Defective Titles continued its work under Falkland and subsequent deputies, becoming one of the most potent instruments of English authority in Ireland throughout the seventeenth century. (065-009)
