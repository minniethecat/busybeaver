Thomas Radclyffe, 3rd Earl of Sussex, served as Lord Deputy of Ireland from 1556 to 1564, spanning the reigns of [[Mary I]] and [[Elizabeth I]]. His deputyship was dominated by the struggle against [[Shane O'Neill|John O'Neil "the Proud"]] in [[Ulster]] and the enforcement of the Elizabethan religious settlement. (053-060)

McGee calls him one of the ablest of Elizabeth's councillors. Yet his reputation was tarnished by assassination plots against O'Neil. After a second plot was traced to him, a royal commission investigated and Sussex was recalled, replaced by [[Henry Sidney|Sir Henry Sidney]]. (054-102) (054-297)

## Early Career and Appointment

Sussex succeeded Sir Anthony St. Leger as Lord Deputy under Queen Mary. McGee notes he was the "abler successor" to St. Leger. With the Lord Treasurer [[Henry Sidney|Sir Henry Sidney]], he led many expeditions to relieve the garrisons at Maryborough and Philipstown. These forts in the newly shired King's and Queen's Counties faced constant assault from displaced Gaelic clans. (053-060)

> "St. Leger, and his still abler successor, the Earl of Sussex, and the new Lord Treasurer, Sir Henry Sidney, were forced to lead many an expedition to the relief of those garrisons, and the dispersion of their assailants." [053-062]

In 1556, Scottish Islesmen under James, son of Conal, laid siege to [[Carrickfergus]] in [[Antrim]]. The campaign occupied Lord Sussex for some six weeks in the glens of Antrim. Their leader finally entered into conditions and received the honour of knighthood. The incursions of Highland mercenaries crossing from Argyle were a persistent problem throughout Sussex's tenure. (053-098)

## Campaigns Against Shane O'Neill

The defining conflict of Sussex's deputyship was his struggle against John O'Neil "the Proud," who had been proclaimed O'Neil on the rath of Tullahoge. In spring 1561, Sussex marched north with the [[Earl of Ormond]] as second in command. He entered [[Armagh]] and began fortifying the city to place a powerful garrison there. (054-193)

O'Neil responded by making an irruption into the plain of [[Meath]] and menacing [[Dublin]]. The utmost consternation prevailed at his approach. Sussex was forced to abandon Armagh and return south, while O'Neil erected his standard as far south as [[Drogheda]], within twenty miles of the capital. (054-203)

In autumn 1561, Sussex led an expedition northward with five Earls: [[Earl of Kildare|Kildare]], [[Earl of Ormond|Ormond]], [[Earl of Desmond|Desmond]], Thomond, and Clanrickarde. The army reached Lough Foyle without bringing O'Neil to an engagement and without any military advantage. Frustrated, Sussex did not hesitate to propose assassination. (054-207) (054-214)

> "The Lord Deputy, though one of the wisest and most justly celebrated of Elizabeth's Counsellors, did not hesitate to communicate to his royal mistress the project of hiring an assassin, named Nele Gray, to take off the Prince of Ulster, but the plot, though carefully elaborated, miscarried." [054-218]

After the plot failed, Sussex abruptly changed tactics. He employed the young [[Earl of Kildare|Gerald, 11th Earl of Kildare]], O'Neil's cousin, to negotiate a truce. Sussex agreed to withdraw his wardens from Armagh. O'Neil undertook to live at peace with the Queen. Both leaders visited the English court in early 1562. (054-220) (054-238)

A second assassination attempt was later traced to Sussex, involving a man named Smith. The Queen issued a formal commission to investigate the case. The result was decisive: Sussex was recalled and [[Henry Sidney|Sir Henry Sidney]] was appointed in his place. This ended Sussex's deputyship in disgrace, despite his earlier reputation. (054-297)

## Parliament of 1560 and Religious Legislation

In the second year of Elizabeth's reign, Sussex returned from England and summoned Parliament to meet in Dublin on 12 January 1560. The Parliament sat for one short but busy month. Its principal business was reversing the Catholic restoration of Mary's reign and establishing the Protestant religious settlement. (054-043) (054-057)

Sussex's instructions were "to make such statutes (concerning religion) as were made in England, mutatis mutandis." Mary's ecclesiastical statutes were repealed, jurisdiction was vested in the Crown, and the Act of Uniformity was passed. The Parliament of only ten counties enacted statutes that defined Ireland's religious constitution for centuries. (054-050) (054-070)

The session was fiercely contested. A majority of the Upper House, still composed chiefly of Catholic peers, resisted the legislation. Sussex gave his pledge that the Act of Uniformity would not be strictly enforced if passed. So severe was the struggle that he hastily dissolved the houses and went to England. (054-088)

No other Parliament was summoned in Ireland for nine years afterward. When [[Henry Sidney|Sir Henry Sidney]] finally convened a new Parliament in 1569, its first sittings were "as stormy as those of Sussex." The precedent of executive frustration with an unmanageable legislature had been firmly established. (054-093) (055-086)

## Recall and Legacy

After his recall in 1564, Sussex remained a figure of significance at court. Queen Elizabeth, writing to him about a rumoured rising by O'Neil, displayed an opportunistic attitude toward Irish land. She desired him to assure her lieges at Dublin that if O'Neil did rise, "it would be for their advantage; for there will be estates for them who want." (056-003)

This attitude anticipated the plantation system that would transform [[Ulster]] and [[Munster]] in the decades to come. The correspondence between Elizabeth and Sussex reveals the early origins of treating Irish rebellions as opportunities for English expropriation. (056-003)

Sussex's policy met determined opposition among the Anglo-Irish nobility. [[Earl of Desmond|Gerald, 16th Earl of Desmond]], strenuously opposed his measures in Parliament and later resisted the more aggressive policies of [[Henry Sidney|Sidney]]. Sussex laid foundations of Tudor rule while his methods foreshadowed the ruthlessness of later deputies. (055-169)
