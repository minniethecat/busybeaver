CHAPTER IV.

[067-001]THE INSURRECTION OF 1641.

[067-002]The plan agreed upon by the Confederates included four main features.

[067-003]I. A rising after the harvest was gathered in, and a campaign during

[067-004]the winter months, when supplies from England were most difficult to be

[067-005]obtained by their enemies. II. A simultaneous attack on one and the

[067-006]same day or night on all the fortresses within reach of their friends.

[067-007]III. To surprise the Castle of Dublin, which was said to contain arms

[067-008]for 12,000 men. IV. Aid in officers, munitions, and money from abroad.

[067-009]All the details of this project were carried successfully into effect,

[067-010]except the seizure of Dublin Castle—the most difficult as it would have

[067-011]been the most decisive blow to strike.

[067-012]Towards the end of August, a meeting of those who could most

[067-013]conveniently attend was held in Dublin. There were present O'Moore and

[067-014]Maguire, of the civilians, and Colonels Plunkett, Byrne, and McMahon of

[067-015]the army. At this meeting the last week of October, or first of

[067-016]November, was fixed upon as the time to rise; subsequently Saturday,

[067-017]the 23rd of the first named month, a market day in the capital was

[067-018]selected. The northern movements were to be arranged with Sir Phelim

[067-019]O'Neil, while McMahon, Plunkett, and Byrne, with 200 picked men, were

[067-020]to surprise the Castle guard—consisting of only a few pensioners and 40

[067-021]halbediers—turn the guns upon the city to intimidate the Puritan party,

[067-022]and thus make sure of Dublin; O'Moore, Lord Maguire, and other

[067-023]civilians, were to be in town, in order to direct the next steps to be

[067-024]taken. As the day approached, the arrangements went on with perfect

[067-025]secrecy but with perfect success. On the 22nd of October half the

[067-026]chosen band were in waiting, and the remainder were expected in during

[067-027]the night. Some hundreds of persons, in and about Dublin, and many

[067-028]thousands throughout the country, must have been in possession of that

[067-029]momentous secret, yet it was by the mere accident of trusting a drunken

[067-030]dependent out of sight, that the first knowledge of the plot was

[067-031]conveyed to the Lords Justices on the very eve of its execution.

[067-032]Owen O'Connolly, the informant on this occasion, was one of those

[067-033]ruffling squires or henchmen, who accompanied gentlemen of fortune in

[067-034]that age, to take part in their quarrels, and carry their confidential

[067-035]messages. That he was not an ordinary domestic servant, we may learn

[067-036]from the fact of his carrying a sword, after the custom of the class to

[067-037]which we have assigned him. At this period he was in the service of Sir

[067-038]John Clotworthy, one of the most violent of the Puritan Undertakers,

[067-039]and had conformed to the established religion. Through what

[067-040]recklessness, or ignorance of his true character, he came to be invited

[067-041]by Colonel Hugh McMahon to his lodgings, and there, on the evening of

[067-042]the 22nd, entrusted with a knowledge of next day's plans, we have now

[067-043]no means of deciding. O'Connolly's information, as tendered to the

[067-044]Justices, states that on hearing of the proposed attack on the Castle,

[067-045]he pretended an occasion to withdraw, leaving his sword in McMahon's

[067-046]room to avoid suspicion, and that after jumping over fences and

[067-047]palings, he made his way from the north side of the city to Sir William

[067-048]Parsons at the Castle. Parsons at first discredited the tale, which

[067-049]O'Connolly (who was in liquor) told in a confused and rambling manner,

[067-050]but he finally decided to consult his colleague, Borlase, by whom some

[067-051]of the Council were summoned, the witness's deposition taken down,

[067-052]orders issued to double the guard, and officers despatched, who

[067-053]arrested McMahon at his lodgings. When McMahon came to be examined

[067-054]before the Council, it was already the morning of the 23rd; he boldly

[067-055]avowed his own part in the plot, and declared that what was that day to

[067-056]be done was now beyond the power of man to prevent. He was committed

[067-057]close prisoner to the Castle where he had hoped to command, and search

[067-058]was made for the other leaders in town. Maguire was captured the next

[067-059]morning, and shared McMahon's captivity; but O'Moore, Plunkett, and

[067-060]Byrne succeeded in escaping out of the city. O'Connolly was amply

[067-061]rewarded in lands and money; and we hear of him once afterwards, with

[067-062]the title of Colonel, in the Parliamentary army.

[067-063]As McMahon had declared to the Justices, the rising was now beyond the

[067-064]power of man to prevent. In Ulster, by stratagem, surprise, or force,

[067-065]the forts of Charlemont and Mountjoy, and the town of Dungannon, were

[067-066]seized on the night of the 22nd by Sir Phelim O'Neil or his

[067-067]lieutenants; on the next day Sir Conor Magennis took the town of Newry,

[067-068]the McMahons possessed themselves of Carrickmacross and Castleblaney,

[067-069]the O'Hanlons Tandragee, while Philip O'Reilly and Roger Maguire razed

[067-070]Cavan and Fermanagh. A proclamation of the northern leaders appeared

[067-071]the same day, dated from Dungannon, setting forth their "true intent

[067-072]and meaning" to be, not hostility to his Majesty the King, "nor to any

[067-073]of his subjects, neither English nor Scotch; but only for the defence

[067-074]and liberty of ourselves and the Irish natives of this kingdom." A more

[067-075]elaborate manifesto appeared shortly afterwards from the pen of Rory

[067-076]O'Moore, in which the oppressions of the Catholics for conscience' sake

[067-077]were detailed, the King's intended "graces" acknowledged, and their

[067-078]frustration by the malice of the Puritan party exhibited: it also

[067-079]endeavoured to show that a common danger threatened the Protestants of

[067-080]the Episcopal Church with Roman Catholics, and asserted in the

[067-081]strongest terms the devotion of the Catholics to the Crown. In the same

[067-082]politic and tolerant spirit, Sir Conor Magennis wrote from Newry on the

[067-083]25th to the officers commanding at Down. "We are," he wrote, "for our

[067-084]lives and liberties. We desire no blood to be shed, but if you mean to

[067-085]shed our blood, be sure we shall be as ready as you for that purpose."

[067-086]This threat of retaliation, so customary in all wars, was made on the

[067-087]third day of the rising, and refers wholly to future contingencies; the

[067-088]monstrous fictions which were afterwards circulated of a wholesale

[067-089]massacre committed on the 23rd were not as yet invented, nor does any

[067-090]public document or private letter, written in Ireland in the last week

[067-091]of October, or during the first days of November, so much as allude to

[067-092]those tales of blood and horror, afterwards so industriously

[067-093]circulated, and so greedily swallowed.

[067-094]Fully aroused from their lethargy by McMahon's declaration, the Lords

[067-095]Justices acted with considerable vigour. Dublin was declared to be in a

[067-096]state of siege; courts martial were established; arms were distributed

[067-097]to the Protestant citizens, and some Catholics; and all strangers were

[067-098]ordered to quit the city under pain of death. Sir Francis Willoughby,

[067-099]Governor of Galway, who arrived on the night of the 22nd, was entrusted

[067-100]with the command of the Castle, Sir Charles Coote was appointed

[067-101]Military Governor of the city, and the Earl, afterwards Duke of Ormond,

[067-102]was summoned from Carrick-on-Suir to take command of the army. As Coote

[067-103]played a very conspicuous part in the opening scenes of this war, and

[067-104]Ormond till its close, it may be well to describe them both, more

[067-105]particularly, to the reader.

[067-106]Sir Charles Coote, one of the first Baronets of Ireland, like Parsons,

[067-107]Boyle, Chichester, and other Englishmen, had come over to Ireland

[067-108]during the war against Tyrone, in quest of fortune. His first

[067-109]employments were in Connaught, where he filled the offices of

[067-110]Provost-Marshal and Vice-Governor in the reign of James I. His success

[067-111]as an Undertaker entitles him to rank with the fortunate adventurers we

[067-112]have mentioned; in Roscommon, Sligo, Leitrim, Queen's, and other

[067-113]counties, his possessions and privileges raised him to the rank of the

[067-114]richest subjects of his time. In 1640 he was a colonel of foot, with

[067-115]the estates of a Prince and the habits of a Provost-Marshal. His

[067-116]reputation for ferocious cruelty has survived the remembrance even of

[067-117]his successful plunder of other people's property; before the campaigns

[067-118]of Cromwell there was no better synonym for wanton cruelty than the

[067-119]name of Sir Charles Coote.

[067-120]James Butler, Earl, Marquis, and Duke of Ormond deservedly ranks

[067-121]amongst the principal statesmen of his time. During a public career of

[067-122]more than half a century his conduct in many eminent offices of trust

[067-123]was distinguished by supreme ability, life-long firmness and

[067-124]consistency. As a courtier of the House of Stuart, it was impossible

[067-125]that he should have served and satisfied both Charleses without

[067-126]participating in many indefensible acts of government, and originating

[067-127]some of them. Yet judged, not from the Irish but the Imperial point of

[067-128]view, not by an abstract standard but by the public morality of his

[067-129]age, he will be found fairly deserving of the title of "the great Duke"

[067-130]bestowed on him during his lifetime. When summoned by the Lords

[067-131]Justices to their assistance in 1641, he was in the thirty-first year

[067-132]of his age, and had so far only distinguished himself in political life

[067-133]as the friend of the late Lord Strafford. He had, however, the good

[067-134]fortune to restore in his own person the estates of his family,

[067-135]notwithstanding that they were granted in great part to others by King

[067-136]James; his attachment to the cause of King Charles was very naturally

[067-137]augmented by the fact that the partiality of that Prince and his

[067-138]ill-fated favourite had enabled him to retrieve both the hereditary

[067-139]wealth and the high political influence which formerly belonged to the

[067-140]Ormond Butlers. Such an ally was indispensable to the Lords Justices in

[067-141]the first panic of the insurrection; but it was evident to near

[067-142]observers that Ormond, a loyalist and a churchman, could not long act

[067-143]in concert with such devoted Puritans as Parsons, Borlase, and Coote.

[067-144]The military position of the several parties—there were at least

[067-145]three—when Ormond arrived at Dublin, in the first week of November, may

[067-146]be thus stated: I. In Munster and Connaught there was but a single

[067-147]troop of royal horse, each, left as a guard with the respective

[067-148]Presidents, St. Leger and Willoughby; in Kilkenny, Dublin, and other of

[067-149]the midland counties, the gentry, Protestant and Catholic, were relied

[067-150]on to raise volunteers for their own defence; in Dublin there had been

[067-151]got together 1,500 old troops; six new regiments of foot were embodied;

[067-152]and thirteen volunteer companies of 100 each. In the Castle were arms

[067-153]and ammunition for 12,000 men, with a fine train of field artillery,

[067-154]provided by Stafford for his campaign in the north of England. Ormond,

[067-155]as Lieutenant-General, had thus at his disposal, in one fortnight after

[067-156]the insurrection broke out, from 8,000 to 10,000 well appointed men;

[067-157]his advice was to take the field at once against the northern leaders

[067-158]before the other Provinces became equally inflamed. But his judgment

[067-159]was overruled by the Justices, who would only consent, while awaiting

[067-160]their cue from the Long Parliament, to throw reinforcements into

[067-161]Drogheda, which thus became their outpost towards the north. II. In

[067-162]Ulster there still remained in the possession of "the Undertakers"

[067-163]Enniskillen, Derry, the Castles of Killeagh and Crohan in Cavan,

[067-164]Lisburn, Belfast, and the stronghold of Carrickfergus, garrisoned by

[067-165]the regiments of Colonel Chichester and Lord Conway. King Charles, who

[067-166]was at Edinburgh endeavouring to conciliate the Scottish Parliament

[067-167]when news of the Irish rising reached him, procured the instant

[067-168]despatch of 1,500 men to Ulster, and authorized Lords Chichester, Ardes

[067-169]and Clandeboy, to raise new regiments from among their own tenants. The

[067-170]force thus embodied—which may be called from its prevailing element the

[067-171]_Scottish_ army—cannot have numbered less than 5,000 foot, and the

[067-172]proportionate number of horse. III. The Irish in the field by the first

[067-173]of November are stated in round numbers at 30,000 men in the northern

[067-174]counties alone; but the whole number supplied with arms and ammunition

[067-175]could not have reached one-third of that nominal total. Before the

[067-176]surprise of Charlemont and Mountjoy forts, Sir Phelim O'Neil had but a

[067-177]barrel or two of gunpowder; the stores of those forts, with 70 barrels

[067-178]taken at Newry by Magennis, and all the arms captured in the

[067-179]simultaneous attack, which at the outside could not well exceed 4,000

[067-180]or 5,000 stand—constituted their entire equipment. One of Ormond's

[067-181]chief reasons for an immediate campaign in the North was to prevent

[067-182]them having time to get "pikes made"—which shows their deficiency even

[067-183]in that weapon. Besides this defect there was one, if possible, still

[067-184]more serious. Sir Phelim was a civilian, bred to the profession of the

[067-185]law; Rory O'Moore, also, had never seen service; and although Colonel

[067-186]Owen O'Neil and others had promised to join them "at fourteen days'

[067-187]notice," a variety of accidents prevented the arrival of any officer of

[067-188]distinction during the brief remainder of that year. Sir Phelim,

[067-189]however, boldly assumed the title of "Lord General of the Catholic Army

[067-190]in Ulster," and the still more popular title with the Gaelic speaking

[067-191]population of "The O'Neil."

[067-193]The projected winter campaign, after the first week's successes, did

[067-194]not turn out favourably for the northern Insurgents. The beginning of

[067-195]November was marked by the barbarous slaughter committed by the

[067-196]Scottish garrison of Carrickfergus in the Island Magee. Three thousand

[067-197]persons are said to have been driven into the fathomless north sea,

[067-198]over the cliffs of that island, or to have perished by the sword. The

[067-199]ordinary inhabitants could not have exceeded one-tenth as many, but the

[067-200]presence of so large a number may be accounted for by the supposition

[067-201]that they had fled from the mainland across the peninsula, which is

[067-202]left dry at low water, and were pursued to their last refuge by the

[067-203]infuriated Covenanters. From this date forward until the accession of

[067-204]Owen Roe O'Neil to the command, the northern war assumed a ferocity of

[067-205]character foreign to the nature of O'Moore, O'Reilly and Magennis. That

[067-206]Sir Phelim permitted, if he did not sometimes in his gusts of stormy

[067-207]passion instigate, those acts of cruelty, which have stained his

[067-208]otherwise honourable conduct, is too true; but he stood alone among his

[067-209]confederates in that crime, and that crime stands alone in his

[067-210]character. Brave to rashness and disinterested to excess, few rebel

[067-211]chiefs ever made a more heroic end out of a more deplorable beginning.

[067-212]The Irish Parliament, which was to have met on the 16th of November,

[067-213]was indefinitely prorogued by the Lords Justices, who preferred to act

[067-214]only with their chosen quorum of Privy Counsellors. The Catholic Lords

[067-215]of the Pale, who at first had arms granted for their retainers out of

[067-216]the public stores, were now summoned to surrender them by a given day;

[067-217]an insult not to be forgiven. Lords Dillon and Taafe, then deputies to

[067-218]the King, were seized at Ware by the English Puritans, their papers

[067-219]taken from them, and themselves imprisoned. O'Moore, whose clansmen had

[067-220]recovered Dunamase and other strongholds in his ancient patrimony, was

[067-221]still indefatigable in his propaganda among the Anglo-Irish. By his

[067-222]advice Sir Phelim marched to besiege Drogheda, at the head of his

[067-223]tumultuous bands. On the way southward he made an unsuccessful attack

[067-224]upon Lisburn, where he lost heavily; on the 24th of November he took

[067-225]possession of Mellifont Abbey, from whose gate the aged Tyrone had

[067-226]departed in tears, twenty-five years before. From Mellifont he

[067-227]proceeded to invest Drogheda; Colonel Plunkett, with the title of

[067-228]General, being the sole experienced officer as yet engaged in his

[067-229]ranks. A strongly walled town as Drogheda was, well manned, and easily

[067-230]accessible from the sea, cannot be carried without guns and engineers

[067-231]by any amount of physical courage. Whenever the Catholics were fairly

[067-232]matched in the open field, they were generally successful, as at

[067-233]Julianstown, during this siege, where one of their detachments cut off

[067-234]five out of six companies marching from Dublin to reinforce the town;

[067-235]but though the investment was complete, the vigilant governor, Sir

[067-236]Henry Tichburne, successfully repulsed the assailants. O'Moore, who lay

[067-237]between Ardee and Dundalk with a reserve of 2,000 men, found time

[067-238]during the siege to continue his natural career, that of a diplomatist.

[067-239]The Puritan party, from the Lord Justice downwards, were, indeed, every

[067-240]day hastening that union of Catholics of all origins which the founder

[067-241]of the Confederacy so ardently desired to bring about. Their avowed

[067-242]maxim was that the more men rebelled, the more estates there would be

[067-243]to confiscate. In Munster, their chief instruments were the aged Earl

[067-244]of Cork, still insatiable as ever for other men's possessions, and the

[067-245]President St. Leger; in Leinster, Sir Charles Coote. Lord Cork prepared

[067-246]1,100 indictments against men of property in his Province, which he

[067-247]sent to the Speaker of the Long Parliament, with an urgent request that

[067-248]they might be returned to him, with authority to proceed against the

[067-249]parties named, as outlaws. In Leinster, 4,000 similar indictments were

[067-250]found in the course of two days by the free use of the rack with

[067-251]witnesses. Sir John Read, an officer of the King's Bedchamber, and Mr.

[067-252]Barnwall, of Kilbrue, a gentleman of threescore and six, were among

[067-253]those who underwent the torture. When these were the proceedings of the

[067-254]tribunals in peaceable cities, we may imagine what must have been the

[067-255]excesses of the soldiery in the open county. In the South, Sir William

[067-256]St. Leger directed a series of murderous raids upon the peasantry of

[067-257]Cork, which at length produced their natural effect. Lord Muskerry and

[067-258]other leading recusants, who had offered their services to maintain the

[067-259]peace of the Province, were driven by an insulting refusal to combine

[067-260]for their own protection. The 1,100 indictments of Lord Cork soon

[067-261]swelled their ranks, and the capture of the ancient city of Cashel by

[067-262]Philip O'Dwyer announced the insurrection of the South. Waterford soon

[067-263]after opened its gates to Colonel Edmund Butler; Wexford declared for

[067-264]the Catholic cause, and Kilkenny surrendered to Lord Mountgarret. In

[067-265]Wicklow, Coote's troopers committed murders such as had not been

[067-266]equalled since the days of the Pagan Northmen. Little children were

[067-267]carried aloft writhing on the pikes of these barbarians, whose worthy

[067-268]commander confessed that "he liked such frolics." Neither age nor sex

[067-269]was spared, and an ecclesiastic was especially certain of instant

[067-270]death. Fathers Higgins and White of Naas, in Kildare, were given up by

[067-271]Coote to these "lambs," though each had been granted a safe conduct by

[067-272]his superior officer, Lord Ormond. And these murders were taking place

[067-273]at the very time when the Franciscans and Jesuits of Cashel were

[067-274]protecting Dr. Pullen, the Protestant Chancellor of that Cathedral and

[067-275]other Protestant prisoners; while also the Castle of Cloughouter, in

[067-276]Cavan, the residence of Bishop Bedell, was crowded with Protestant

[067-277]fugitives, all of whom were carefully guarded by the chivalrous Philip

[067-278]O'Reilly.

[067-279]At length the Catholic Lords of the Pale began to feel the general glow

[067-280]of an outraged people, too long submissive under every species of

[067-281]provocation. The Lords Justices having summoned them to attend in

[067-282]Dublin on the 8th of December, they met at Swords, at the safe distance

[067-283]of seven miles, and sent by letter their reasons for not trusting

[067-284]themselves in the capital. To the allegations in this letter the

[067-285]Justices replied by proclamation, denying most of them, and repeating

[067-286]their summons to Lords Fingal, Gormanstown, Slane, Dunsany,

[067-287]Netterville, Louth, and Trimleston, to attend in Dublin on the 17th.

[067-288]But before the 17th came, as if to ensure the defeat of their own

[067-289]summons, Coote was let loose upon the flourishing villages of Fingal,

[067-290]and the flames kindled by his men might easily be discovered from the

[067-291]round tower of Swords. On the 17th, the summoned Lords, with several of

[067-292]the neighbouring gentry, met by appointment on the hill of Crofty, in

[067-293]the neighbouring county of Meath; while they were engaged in discussing

[067-294]the best course to be taken, a party of armed men on horseback,

[067-295]accompanied by a guard of musketeers, was seen approaching. They proved

[067-296]to be O'Moore, O'Reilly, Costelloe McMahon, brother of the prisoner,

[067-297]Colonel Byrne, and Captain Fox. Lord Gormanstown, advancing in front of

[067-298]his friends, demanded of the new-comers "why they came armed into the

[067-299]Pale?" To which O'Moore made answer "that the ground of their coming

[067-300]thither was for the freedom and liberty of their consciences, the

[067-301]maintenance of his Majesty's prerogative, in which they understood he

[067-302]was abridged, and the making the subjects of this kingdom as free as

[067-303]those of England." Lord Gormanstown, after consulting a few moments

[067-304]with his friends, replied: "Seeing these be your true ends, we will

[067-305]likewise join with you." The leaders then embraced, amid the

[067-306]acclamations of their followers, and the general conditions of their

[067-307]union having been unanimously agreed upon, a warrant was drawn out

[067-308]authorizing the Sheriff of Meath to summon the gentry of the county to

[067-309]a final meeting at the Hill of Tara on the 24th of December.
