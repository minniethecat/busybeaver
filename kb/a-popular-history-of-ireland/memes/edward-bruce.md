## Overview

Edward Bruce (c. 1280 – 14 October 1318) was a Scottish nobleman and military commander, the younger brother of King Robert the Bruce of Scotland. Invited by Irish chieftains seeking to expel the Anglo-Norman presence, he landed in Ireland in May 1315 and was crowned King of Ireland in 1316. His three-year campaign, known as the Bruce Invasion, initially achieved remarkable success before ending in his defeat and death at the Battle of Faughard in 1318. (033-007) (036-127)

## Arrival in Ireland and Early Campaigns

The northern Irish chieftains, led by Donald O'Neill, Prince of Ulster, had long sought to resist the encroaching Anglo-Norman power. Following Robert the Bruce's decisive victory at Bannockburn in 1314, the opportunity arose for an alliance with Scotland.

> Bannockburn suggested the design, Edward Bruce, the gallant companion of all his brother's fortunes and misfortunes, was now invited to place himself at the head of the men of Ulster, in a war for Irish independence. (036-065)

Donald O'Neill, described by McGee as "endued with an intensely national spirit," was the chief mover in bringing Edward Bruce to Ireland. O'Neill had succeeded to the principality of Ulster after a protracted struggle with the Red Earl of Ulster, and had fully adopted the views of Nicholas McMaelisa, the Primate of Armagh, one of the most resolute opponents of the Norman conquest. (036-076) (036-084)

Edward Bruce sailed from Ayr with 6,000 men in 300 galleys, accompanied by Thomas Randolph, Earl of Moray, Sir John Stuart, Sir Philip Moubray, and other distinguished knights. He landed on 25 May 1315 at the Glendun river near Glenarm in present-day County Antrim, and was promptly joined by Donald O'Neill and twelve other chiefs. (036-128) (036-132)

## Kingship and the War for Ireland

Following his initial successes, Edward Bruce was formally elected and crowned King of Ireland in 1316. The Irish reguli who had invited him described him as "a man of kindred blood," recognizing the shared Gaelic heritage between the Scots and the Irish through their common ancestor in the ancient kingdom of Dál Riata. (039-159) (037-031)

Bruce conducted his campaign in a manner described by McGee as being conducted "in true Gaelic fashion," moving rapidly across the country and compelling the Anglo-Norman lords to defend their territories. However, his style of warfare proved as destructive to the Irish countryside as it was to his enemies. The harvest failures of 1317 forced a temporary truce between all belligerents, with the scarcity extending to England and Scotland as well. (037-127) (038-015)

## Character and Leadership

McGee offers a balanced assessment of Edward Bruce's character, acknowledging his personal bravery while recognizing the flaws that ultimately undermined his cause:

> Edward Bruce, King of Ireland. That he was as gallant a knight as any in that age of gallantry, we know; that he could confront the gloomiest aspect of adversity with cheerfulness, we also know. But the united testimony, both of history and tradition, in his own country, so tenacious of its anecdotical treasures, describes him as rash, headstrong, and intractable, beyond all the captains of his time. (038-006)

This proverbial rashness proved fatal to his enterprise. Bruce's "total disregard of the opinion of the country into which he came" gradually alienated those Irish chieftains who had initially welcomed him with enthusiasm. His impetuosity, while useful in the first shock of invasion, prevented him from consolidating his gains or building a sustainable coalition. (038-137) (038-138) (036-127)

## Death at the Battle of Faughard

In October 1318, despite being outnumbered, Edward Bruce resolved to give battle to the Anglo-Irish forces under John de Bermingham at Faughard, near Dundalk. He did not wait for reinforcements that were reportedly on their way from Scotland. The battle was a decisive defeat for the Scottish-Irish army, and Edward Bruce was killed on the field. (038-023) (038-033) (038-135)

The Irish annalists, who had initially supported Bruce, recorded his death with a striking sense of relief at the departure of a foreign liberator who had become as burdensome as the oppressors he had come to replace:

> "No achievement had been performed in Ireland, for a long time," say the Four Masters, "from which greater benefit had accrued to the country than from this." "There was not a better deed done in Ireland since the banishment of the Formorians," says the Annalist of Clonmacnoise! (038-143)

So detested may a foreign liberating chief become who outrages the feelings and usages of the people he pretends, or really means, to emancipate. (038-148)

## Legacy

The Bruce Invasion, though ultimately a failure, had lasting consequences for Ireland. The Earldom of Ulster, which had been the strongest Anglo-Norman lordship in the north, was effectively destroyed during the campaign. After the fall of Edward Bruce, no attempt was made to organize another national confederacy on such a scale for generations. (039-011) (038-123)

The campaign also demonstrated the potent combination of Gaelic Irish and Scottish resistance to English power—a pattern that would recur throughout Irish history. In the era of Edward Bruce, the Irish chieftains not only refused any longer to pay tribute to the Anglo-Norman lords but were emboldened to reclaim territories that had been lost over the preceding century and a half. The treaty negotiations between Robert Bruce and the Irish chiefs marked an early attempt at formalizing a Hiberno-Scottish alliance against English domination. (039-140) (038-123)
