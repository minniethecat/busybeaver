# Navigation Acts

The **Navigation Acts** were a series of British laws that restricted Irish maritime commerce, prohibiting direct trade between Ireland and foreign markets and requiring that goods be shipped through English ports. Originally devised under [[Oliver Cromwell]]'s Commonwealth and extended after the [[Restoration]], these acts were a long-standing grievance in Irish economic history.

## Origins under Cromwell

The Navigation Act was first applied to Ireland during the Commonwealth period, reflecting Cromwell's policy of economic subordination. The act prevented Irish merchants from trading directly with British colonies or with continental Europe, channelling all commerce through English ports and English ships.

This legislation was part of a broader pattern of economic restriction that treated Ireland as a dependent territory rather than an equal partner. Irish wool, cattle, and other exports faced prohibitive duties or outright bans.

## Extension and Enforcement

The provisions of the Navigation Act were maintained and extended after the [[Restoration]] of [[Charles II]]. Under the later Stuart kings, the act continued to govern Irish trade, imposing restrictions on both exports and imports. The laws were enforced by English customs officials stationed in Irish ports.

The economic impact on Ireland was severe. Irish producers were cut off from profitable colonial markets, while Irish consumers paid higher prices for imported goods that had passed through English intermediaries. The restrictions stifled the development of Irish manufacturing and maritime enterprise.

## Economic Consequences

The Navigation Acts, combined with other trade restrictions, shaped the economic landscape of 17th and 18th-century Ireland. Irish merchants and landowners repeatedly protested the acts, arguing that they violated the rights of the [[Irish Parliament]] and impoverished the kingdom. However, the acts remained in place, enforced by the political dominance of the [[Protestant Ascendancy]].

The acts became a focal point of Irish economic grievances. The demand for free trade gained momentum during the 18th century, culminating in the partial relaxation of the Navigation Acts in 1779–1780, when the threat of the [[American Revolution]] persuaded the British government to grant concessions.

## Legacy

Though the Navigation Acts were eventually repealed, their legacy endured. The acts symbolised the economic subordination of Ireland within the British system and contributed to the arguments advanced by later movements for legislative independence. The resentment generated by such trade restrictions fed into the broader campaign for Irish self-government, culminating in the [[Act of Union 1800]] and, ultimately, the repeal movement of the 19th century.
