CHAPTER XII.

[075-001]IRELAND UNDER THE PROTECTORATE—ADMINISTRATION OF HENRY CROMWELL—DEATH

[075-002]OF OLIVER.

[075-003]The English republic rose from the scaffold of the King, in 1649; its

[075-004]first government was a "Council of State" of forty-one members; under

[075-005]this council, Cromwell held at first the title of Lord General; but, on

[075-006]the 16th December, 1653, he was solemnly installed, in Westminster

[075-007]Hall, as "Lord Protector of the Commonwealth of England, Scotland, and

[075-008]Ireland." He was then in his fifty-fourth year; his reign—if such it

[075-009]may be called—lasted less than five years.

[075-010]The policy of the Protector towards Ireland is even less defensible

[075-011]than his military severities. For the barbarities of war there may be

[075-012]some apology, the poor one at least that such outrages are inseparable

[075-013]from war itself; but for the cold-blooded, deliberate atrocities of

[075-014]peace, no such defence can be permitted before the tribunal of a free

[075-015]posterity.

[075-016]The Long Parliament, still dragging out its date, under the shadow of

[075-017]Cromwell's great name, declared in its session of 1652, the rebellion

[075-018]in Ireland "subdued and ended," and proceeded to legislate for that

[075-019]kingdom as a conquered country. On the 12th of August, they passed

[075-020]their Act of Settlement, the authorship of which was attributed to Lord

[075-021]Orrery, in this respect the worthy son of the first Earl of Cork. Under

[075-022]this Act, there were four chief descriptions of persons whose status

[075-023]was thus settled: 1st. All ecclesiastics and royalist proprietors were

[075-024]exempted from pardon of life or estate. 2nd. All royalist commissioned

[075-025]officers were condemned to banishment, and the forfeit of two-thirds of

[075-026]their property, one-third being retained for the support of their wives

[075-027]and children. 3rd. Those who had not been in arms, but could be shown,

[075-028]by a Parliamentary commission, to have manifested "a constant, good

[075-029]affection" to the war, were to forfeit one-third of their estates, and

[075-030]receive "an equivalent" for the remaining two-thirds west of the

[075-031]Shannon. 4th. All husbandmen and others of the inferior sort, "not

[075-032]possessed of lands or goods exceeding the value of 10 pounds," were to

[075-033]have a free pardon, on condition also of transporting themselves across

[075-034]the Shannon.

[075-035]This last condition of the Cromwellian settlement distinguished it, in

[075-036]our annals, from every other proscription of the native population

[075-037]formerly attempted. The great river of Ireland, rising in the mountains

[075-038]of Leitrim, nearly severs the five western counties from the rest of

[075-039]the kingdom. The province thus set apart, though one of the largest in

[075-040]superficial extent, had also the largest proportion of waste and water,

[075-041]mountain and moorland. The new inhabitants were there to congregate

[075-042]from all the other provinces before the 1st day of May, 1654, under

[075-043]penalty of outlawry and all its consequences; and when there, they were

[075-044]not to appear within two miles of the Shannon or four miles of the sea.

[075-045]A rigorous passport system, to evade which was death without form of

[075-046]trial, completed this settlement, the design of which was to shut up

[075-047]the remaining Catholic inhabitants from all intercourse with mankind,

[075-048]and all communion with the other inhabitants of their own country.

[075-049]A new survey of the whole kingdom was also ordered, under the direction

[075-050]of Dr. William Petty, the fortunate economist, who founded the house of

[075-051]Lansdowne. By him the surface of the kingdom was estimated at ten

[075-052]millions and a half plantation acres, three of which were deducted for

[075-053]waste and water. Of the remainder, above 5,000,000 were in Catholic

[075-054]hands in 1641; 300,000 were church and college lands; and 2,000,000

[075-055]were in possession of the Protestant settlers of the reigns of James

[075-056]and Elizabeth. Under the Protectorate, 5,000,000 acres were confiscate;

[075-057]this enormous spoil, two-thirds of the whole island, went to the

[075-058]soldiers and adventurers who had served against the Irish, or had

[075-059]contributed to the military chest, since 1641—except 700,000 acres

[075-060]given in "exchange" to the banished in Clare and Connaught; and

[075-061]1,200,000 confirmed to "innocent Papists." Such was the complete

[075-062]uprooting of the ancient tenantry or clansmen, from their original

[075-063]holdings, that during the survey, orders of Parliament were issued to

[075-064]bring back individuals from Connaught to point out the boundaries of

[075-065]parishes in Munster. It cannot be imputed among the sins so freely laid

[075-066]to the historical account of the native legislature, that an Irish

[075-067]parliament had any share in sanctioning this universal spoliation.

[075-068]Cromwell anticipated the union of the kingdoms by a hundred and fifty

[075-069]years, when he summoned, in 1653, that assembly over which "Praise-God

[075-070]Barebones" presided; members for Ireland and Scotland sat on the same

[075-071]benches with the commons of England. Oliver's first deputy in the

[075-072]government of Ireland was his son-in-law, Fleetwood, who had married

[075-073]the widow of Ireton; but his real representative was his fourth son,

[075-074]Henry Cromwell, Commander-in-Chief of the army. In 1657, the title of

[075-075]Lord Deputy was transferred from Fleetwood to Henry, who united the

[075-076]supreme civil and military authority in his own person, until the eve

[075-077]of the restoration, of which he became an active partisan. We may thus

[075-078]properly embrace the five years of the Protectorate as the period of

[075-079]Henry Cromwell's administration.

[075-080]In the absence of a Parliament, the government of Ireland was vested in

[075-081]the Deputy, the Commander-in-Chief, and four commissioners, Ludlow,

[075-082]Corbett, Jones, and Weaver. There was, moreover, a High Court of

[075-083]Justice, which perambulated the kingdom, and exercised an absolute

[075-084]authority over life and property, greater than even Strafford's Court

[075-085]of Castle Chamber had pretended to. Over this court presided Lord

[075-086]Lowther, assisted by Mr. Justice Donnellan, by Cooke, solicitor to the

[075-087]Parliament on the trial of King Charles, and the regicide, Reynolds. By

[075-088]this court, Sir Phelim O'Neil, Viscount Mayo, and Colonels O'Toole and

[075-089]Bagnall, were condemned and executed; by them the mother of Colonel

[075-090]Fitzpatrick was burnt at the stake; and Lords Muskerry and Clanmaliere

[075-091]set at liberty, through some secret influence. The commissioners were

[075-092]not behind the High Court of Justice in executive offices of severity.

[075-093]Children under age, of both sexes, were captured by thousands, and sold

[075-094]as slaves to the tobacco planters of Virginia and the West Indies.

[075-095]Secretary Thurloe informs Henry Cromwell that "the Committee of the

[075-096]Council have authorized 1,000 girls and as many youths, to be taken up

[075-097]for that purpose." Sir William Petty mentions 6,000 Irish boys and

[075-098]girls shipped to the West Indies. Some cotemporary accounts make the

[075-099]total number of children and adults so transported 100,000 souls. To

[075-100]this decimation, we may add 34,000 men of fighting age, who had

[075-101]permission to enter the armies of foreign powers, at peace with the

[075-102]commonwealth. The chief commissioners, sitting at Dublin, had their

[075-103]deputies in a commission of delinquencies, sitting at Athlone, and

[075-104]another of transportation, sitting at Loughrea. Under their

[075-105]superintendence, the distribution made of the soil among the Puritans

[075-106]"was nearly as complete as that of Canaan by the Israelites." Whenever

[075-107]native labourers were found absolutely necessary for the cultivation of

[075-108]the estates of their new masters, they were barely tolerated "as the

[075-109]Gibeonites had been by Joshua." Such Irish gentlemen as had obtained

[075-110]pardons, were obliged to wear a distinctive mark on their dress under

[075-111]pain of death; those of inferior rank were obliged to wear a round

[075-112]black spot on the right cheek under pain of the branding iron and the

[075-113]gallows; if a Puritan lost his life in any district inhabited by

[075-114]Catholics, the whole population were held subject to military

[075-115]execution. For the rest, whenever "Tory" or recusant fell into the

[075-116]hands of these military colonists, or the garrisons which knitted them

[075-117]together, they were assailed with the war cry of the Jews—"That thy

[075-118]feet may be dipped in the blood of thine enemies, and that the tongues

[075-119]of thy dogs may be red with the same." Thus penned in between "the mile

[075-120]line" of the Shannon, and "the four mile-line" of the sea, the remnant

[075-121]of the Irish nation passed seven years of a bondage unequalled in

[075-122]severity by anything which can be found in the annals of Christendom.

[075-123]The conquest was not only a military but a religious subjugation. The

[075-124]27th of Elizabeth—the old act of uniformity—was rigorously enforced.

[075-125]The Catholic lawyers were disbarred and silenced; the Catholic

[075-126]schoolmasters were forbidden to teach, under pain of felony. Recusants,

[075-127]surrounded in glens and caves, offering up the holy sacrifice through

[075-128]the ministry of some daring priest, were shot down or smoked out like

[075-129]vermin. The ecclesiastics never, in any instance, were allowed to

[075-130]escape. Among those who suffered death during the short space of the

[075-131]Protectorate, are counted "three bishops and three hundred

[075-132]ecclesiastics." The surviving prelates were in exile, except the

[075-133]bedridden Bishop of Kilmore, who for years had been unable to

[075-134]officiate. So that, now, that ancient hierarchy which in the worst

[075-135]Danish wars had still recruited its ranks as fast as they were broken,

[075-136]seemed on the very eve of extinction. Throughout all the island no

[075-137]episcopal hand remained to bless altars, to ordain priests, or to

[075-138]confirm the faithful. The Irish church as well as the Irish state,

[075-139]touched its lowest point of suffering and endurance in the decade which

[075-140]intervened between the death of Charles I. and the death of Cromwell.

[075-141]The new population imposed upon the kingdom, soon split up into a

[075-142]multitude of sects. Some of them became Quakers: many adhered to the

[075-143]Anabaptists; others, after the Restoration, conformed to the

[075-144]established church. That deeper tincture of Puritanism which may be

[075-145]traced in the Irish, as compared with the English establishment, took

[075-146]its origin even more from the Cromwellian settlement than from the

[075-147]Calvinistic teachings of Archbishop Usher.

[075-148]Oliver died in 1658, on his "fortunate day," the 3rd of September,

[075-149]leaving England to experience twenty months of republican intrigue and

[075-150]anarchy. Richard Cromwell—Lambert—Ludlow—Monck—each played his part in

[075-151]this stormy interval, till, the time being ripe for a restoration,

[075-152]Charles II. landed at Dover on the 23rd of May, 1660 and was carried in

[075-153]triumph to London.
