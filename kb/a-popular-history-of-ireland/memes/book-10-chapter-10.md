CHAPTER X.

[085-001]REIGN OF KING WILLIAM.

[085-002]From the date of the treaty of Limerick, William was acknowledged by

[085-003]all but the extreme Jacobites, at least _de facto_—King of Ireland. The

[085-004]prevailing party in Ulster had long recognized him, and the only

[085-005]expression of the national will then possible accepted his title, in

[085-006]the treaty signed at Limerick on the 3rd of October, 1691. For three

[085-007]years Ireland had resisted his power, for twelve years longer she was

[085-008]to bear the yoke of his government.

[085-009]Though the history of William's twelve years' reign in Ireland is a

[085-010]history of proscription, the King himself is answerable only as a

[085-011]consenting party to such proscription. He was neither by temper nor

[085-012]policy a persecutor; his allies were Spain, Austria and Rome; he had

[085-013]thousands of Catholics in his own army, and he gave his confidence as

[085-014]freely to brave and capable men of one creed as of another. But the

[085-015]oligarchy, calling itself the "Protestant Ascendancy," which had grown

[085-016]so powerful under Cromwell and Charles II., backed as they once again

[085-017]were by all the religious intolerance of England, proved too strong for

[085-018]William's good intentions. He was, moreover, pre-occupied with the

[085-019]grand plans of the European coalition, in which Ireland, without an

[085-020]army, was no longer an element of calculation. He abandoned, therefore,

[085-021]not without an occasional grumbling protest, the vanquished Catholics

[085-022]to the mercy of that oligarchy, whose history, during the eighteenth

[085-023]century, forms so prominent a feature of the history of the kingdom.

[085-024]The civil articles of Limerick, which Sarsfield vainly hoped might

[085-025]prove the _Magna Charta_ of his co-religionists, were thirteen in

[085-026]number. Art. I. guaranteed to members of that denomination, remaining

[085-027]in the kingdom, "such privileges in the exercise of their religion as

[085-028]are consistent with the law of Ireland, or as they enjoyed in the reign

[085-029]of King Charles II.;" this article further provided, that "their

[085-030]majesties, as soon as their affairs will permit them to summon a

[085-031]Parliament in this kingdom, will endeavour to procure the said Roman

[085-032]Catholics such further security in that particular as may preserve them

[085-033]from any disturbance on account of their said religion." Art. II.

[085-034]guaranteed pardon and protection to all who had served King James, on

[085-035]taking the oath of allegiance prescribed in Art. IX., as follows:

[085-036]"I, A. B., do solemnly promise and swear that I will be faithful and

[085-037]bear true allegiance to their majesties, King William and Queen Mary;

[085-038]so help me God."

[085-039]Arts. III., IV., V. and VI. extended the provisions of Arts. I. and II.

[085-040]to merchants and other classes of men. Art. VII. permits "every

[085-041]nobleman and gentleman compromised in the said articles" to carry side

[085-042]arms and keep "a gun in their houses." Art. VIII. gives the right of

[085-043]removing goods and chattels without search. Art. IX. is as follows:

[085-044]"The oath to be administered to such Roman Catholics as submit to their

[085-045]majesties' government _shall be the oath aforesaid, and no other_."

[085-046]Art. X. guarantees that "no person or persons who shall at any time

[085-047]hereafter break these articles, or any of them, shall thereby make or

[085-048]cause any other person or persons to _forfeit or lose the benefit of

[085-049]them_." Arts. XI. and XII. relate to the ratification of the articles

[085-050]"within eight months or sooner." Art. XIII. refers to the debts of

[085-051]"Colonel John Brown, commissary of the Irish army, to several

[085-052]Protestants," and arranges for their satisfaction.

[085-053]These articles were signed before Limerick, at the well known "Treaty

[085-054]Stone," on the Clare side of the Shannon, by Lord Scravenmore, Generals

[085-055]Mackay, Talmash, and De Ginkle, and the Lords Justices Porter and

[085-056]Coningsby, for King William, and by Sarsfield, Earl of Lucan, Viscount

[085-057]Galmoy, Sir Toby Butler, and Colonels Purcell, Cusack, Dillon, and

[085-058]Brown, for the Irish. On the 24th of February following, royal letters

[085-059]patent confirmatory of the treaty were issued from Westminster, in the

[085-060]name of the King and Queen, whereby they declared, that "we do for us,

[085-061]our heirs, and successors, as far as in us lies, ratify and confirm the

[085-062]same and every clause, matter, and thing therein contained. And as to

[085-063]such parts thereof, for which an act of Parliament shall be found to be

[085-064]necessary, we shall recommend the same to be made good by Parliament,

[085-065]and shall give our royal assent to any bill or bills that shall be

[085-066]passed by our two Houses of Parliament to that purpose. And whereas it

[085-067]appears unto us, that it was agreed between the parties to the said

[085-068]articles, that after the words Limerick, Clare, Kerry, Cork, Mayo, or

[085-069]any of them, in the second of the said articles; which words having

[085-070]been casually omitted by the writer of the articles, the words

[085-071]following, viz.: 'And all such as are under their protection in the

[085-072]said counties' should be inserted, and be part of the said omission,

[085-073]was not discovered till after the said articles were signed, but was

[085-074]taken notice of before the second town was surrendered, and that our

[085-075]said justices and generals, or one of them, did promise that the said

[085-076]clause should be made good, it being within the intention of the

[085-077]capitulation, and inserted in the foul draft thereof: Our further will

[085-078]and pleasure is, and we do hereby ratify and confirm the said omitted

[085-079]words, viz., 'And all such as are under their protection in the said

[085-080]counties,' hereby for us, our heirs and successors, ordaining and

[085-081]declaring that all and every person and persons therein concerned shall

[085-082]and may have, receive, and enjoy the benefit thereof, in such and the

[085-083]same manner as if the said words had been inserted in their proper

[085-084]place in the said second article, any omission, defect, or mistake in

[085-085]the said second article in any wise notwithstanding. Provided always,

[085-086]and our will and pleasure is, that these our letters patent shall be

[085-087]enrolled in our Court of Chancery, in our said kingdom of Ireland,

[085-088]within the space of one year next ensuing."

[085-089]But the Ascendancy party were not to be restrained by the faith of

[085-090]treaties, or the obligations of the Sovereign. The Sunday following the

[085-091]return of the Lords Justices from Limerick, Dopping, Bishop of Meath,

[085-092]preached before them at Christ's church, on the crime of keeping faith

[085-093]with Papists. The grand jury of Cork, urged on by Cox, the Recorder of

[085-094]Kinsale, one of the historians of those times, returned in their

[085-095]inquest that the restoration of the Earl of Clancarty's estates "would

[085-096]be dangerous to the Protestant interest." Though both William and

[085-097]George I., interested themselves warmly for that noble family, the

[085-098]hatred of the new oligarchy proved too strong for the clemency of

[085-099]kings, and the broad acres of the disinherited McCarthys, remained to

[085-100]enrich an alien and bigoted aristocracy.

[085-102]In 1692, when the Irish Parliament met, a few Catholic peers, and a

[085-103]very few Catholic commoners took their seats. One of the first acts of

[085-104]the victorious majority was to frame an oath in direct contravention to

[085-105]the oath prescribed by the ninth civil article of the treaty, to be

[085-106]taken by members of both Houses. This oath solemnly and explicitly

[085-107]denied "that in the sacrament of the Lord's supper there is any

[085-108]transubstantiation of the elements;" and as solemnly affirmed, "that

[085-109]the invocation or adoration of the Virgin Mary, or any other saint, and

[085-110]the sacrifice of the mass, as they are now used in the church of Rome,

[085-111]are damnable and idolatrous." As a matter of course, the Catholic peers

[085-112]and commoners retired from both Houses, rather than take any such oath,

[085-113]and thus the Irish Parliament assumed, in 1692, that exclusively

[085-114]Protestant character which it continued to maintain, till its

[085-115]extinction in 1800. The Lord Justice Sydney, acting in the spirit of

[085-116]his original instructions, made some show of resistance to the

[085-117]proscriptive spirit thus exhibited. But to teach him how they regarded

[085-118]his interference, a very small supply was voted, and the assertion of

[085-119]the absolute control of the Commons over all supplies—a sound doctrine

[085-120]when rightly interpreted—was vehemently asserted. Sydney had the

[085-121]satisfaction of proroguing and lecturing the House, but they had the

[085-122]satisfaction soon after of seeing him recalled through their influence

[085-123]in England, and a more congenial Viceroy in the person of Lord Capel

[085-124]sent over.

[085-125]About the same time, that ancient engine of oppression, a Commission to

[085-126]inquire into estates forfeited, was established, and, in a short time,

[085-127]decreed that 1,060,792 acres were escheated to the crown. This was

[085-128]almost the last fragment of the patrimony of the Catholic inhabitants.

[085-129]When King William died, there did not remain in Catholic hands

[085-130]"one-sixth part" of what their grandfathers held, even after the

[085-131]passage of the Act of Settlement.

[085-132]In 1695, Lord Capel opened the second Irish Parliament, summoned by

[085-133]King William, in a speech in which he assured his delighted auditors

[085-134]that the King was intent upon a firm settlement of Ireland upon a

[085-135]Protestant interest. Large supplies were at once voted to his majesty,

[085-136]and the House of Commons then proceeded to the appointment of a

[085-137]committee to consider what penal laws were already in force against the

[085-138]Catholics, not for the purpose of repealing them, but in order to add

[085-139]to their number. The principal penal laws then in existence were:

[085-140]1. An act, subjecting all who upheld the jurisdiction of the See of

[085-141]Rome, to the penalties of a _premunire_; and ordering the oath of

[085-142]supremacy to be a qualification for office of every kind, for holy

[085-143]orders, and for a degree in the university.

[085-144]2. An act for the uniformity of Common Prayer, imposing a fine of a

[085-145]shilling on all who should absent themselves from places of worship of

[085-146]the Established Church on Sundays.

[085-147]3. An act, allowing the Chancellor to name a guardian to the child of a

[085-148]Catholic.

[085-149]4. An act to prevent Catholics from becoming private tutors in

[085-150]families, without license from the ordinaries of their several

[085-151]parishes, and taking the oath of supremacy.

[085-152]To these, the new Parliament added, 1. An act to deprive Catholics of

[085-153]the means of educating their children at home or abroad, and to render

[085-154]them incapable of being guardians of their own or any other person's

[085-155]children; 2. An act to disarm the Catholics; and, 3. Another to banish

[085-156]all the Catholic priests and prelates. Having thus violated the treaty,

[085-157]they gravely brought in a bill "to confirm the Articles of Limerick."

[085-158]"The very title of the bill," says Dr. Cooke Taylor, "contains evidence

[085-159]of its injustice." It is styled "A Bill for the Confirmation of

[085-160]Articles (not _the_ articles) made at the Surrender of Limerick." And

[085-161]the preamble shows that the little word _the_ was not accidentally

[085-162]omitted. It runs thus:—"That the said articles, or _so much of them as

[085-163]may consist with the safety and welfare of your majesty's subjects in

[085-164]these kingdoms_, may be confirmed," &c. The parts that appeared to

[085-165]these legislators inconsistent with "the safety and welfare of his

[085-166]majesty's subjects," were the first article, which provided for the

[085-167]security of the Catholics from all disturbances on account of their

[085-168]religion; those parts of the second article which confirmed the

[085-169]Catholic gentry of Limerick, Clare, Cork, Kerry, and Mayo, in the

[085-170]possession of their estates, and allowed all Catholics to exercise

[085-171]their trades and professions without obstruction; the fourth article,

[085-172]which extended the benefit of the peace to certain Irish officers then

[085-173]abroad; the seventh article, which allowed the Catholic gentry to ride

[085-174]armed; the ninth article, which provides that the oath of allegiance

[085-175]shall be the only oath required from Catholics; and one or two others

[085-176]of minor importance. All of these are omitted in the bill for "The

[085-177]confirmation of Articles made at the Surrender of Limerick."

[085-178]The Commons passed the bill without much difficulty. The House of

[085-179]Lords, however, contained some few of the ancient nobility, and some

[085-180]prelates, who refused to acknowledge the dogma, "that no faith should

[085-181]be kept with Papists," as an article of their creed. The bill was

[085-182]strenuously resisted, and when it was at length carried, a strong

[085-183]protest against it was signed by Lords Londonderry, Tyrone, and

[085-184]Duncannon, the Barons of Ossory, Limerick, Killaloe, Kerry, Howth,

[085-185]Kingston, and Strabane, and, to their eternal honour be it said, the

[085-186]Protestant bishops of Kildare, Elphin, Derry, Clonfert, and Killala!

[085-187]The only other political incidents of this reign, important to Ireland,

[085-188]were the speech from the throne in answer to an address of the English

[085-189]Houses, in which William promised to discourage the woollen and

[085-190]encourage the linen manufacture in Ireland, and the publication of the

[085-191]famous argument for legislative independence, "The Case of Ireland

[085-192]Stated." The author of this tract, the bright precursor of the glorious

[085-193]succession of men, who, often defeated or abandoned by their

[085-194]colleagues, finally triumphed in 1782, was William Molyneux, member for

[085-195]the University of Dublin. Molyneux's book appeared in 1698, with a

[085-196]short, respectful, but manly dedication to King William. Speaking of

[085-197]his own motives in writing it, he says, "I am not at all concerned in

[085-198]wool or the wool trade. I am no ways interested in forfeitures or

[085-199]grants. I am not at all concerned whether the bishop or the society of

[085-200]Derry recover the lands they contest about." Such were the domestic

[085-201]politics of Ireland at that day; but Molyneux raised other and nobler

[085-202]issues when he advanced these six propositions, which lie supported

[085-203]with incontestible ability.

[085-204]"1. How Ireland became a kingdom _annexed_ to the crown of England. And

[085-205]here we shall at large give a faithful narrative of the first

[085-206]expedition of the Britons into this country, and King Henry II.'s

[085-207]arrival here, such as our best historians give us.

[085-208]"2. We shall inquire whether this expedition and the English settlement

[085-209]that afterwards followed thereon, can properly be called a _conquest_;

[085-210]or whether any victories obtained by the English in any succeeding ages

[085-211]in this kingdom, upon any rebellion, may be called a _conquest_

[085-212]thereof.

[085-213]"3. Granting that it were a _conquest_, we shall inquire what _title_ a

[085-214]conquest gives.

[085-215]"4. We shall inquire what _concessions_ have been from time to time

[085-216]made to Ireland, to take off what even the most rigorous asserters of a

[085-217]conqueror's title do pretend to. And herein we shall show by what

[085-218]degrees the English form of government, and the English statute laws,

[085-219]came to be received among us; and this shall appear to be wholly by the

[085-220]_consent_ of the people and the Parliament of Ireland.

[085-221]"5. We shall inquire into the precedents and opinions of the learned in

[085-222]the laws relating to this matter, with observations thereon.

[085-223]"6. We shall consider the reasons and arguments that may be further

[085-224]offered on one side and t'other; and we shall draw some general

[085-225]conclusions from the whole."

[085-226]The English Parliament took alarm at these bold doctrines, seldom heard

[085-227]across the channel since the days of Patrick Darcy and the Catholic

[085-228]Confederacy. They ordered the book to be burned by the hands of the

[085-229]common hangman, as of "dangerous tendency to the crown and people of

[085-230]England, by denying the power of the King and Parliament of England to

[085-231]bind the kingdom and people of Ireland, and the subordination and

[085-232]dependence that Ireland had, and ought to have, upon England, as being

[085-233]united and annexed to the imperial crown of England." They voted an

[085-234]address to the King in the same tone, and received an answer from his

[085-235]majesty, assuring them that he would enforce the laws securing the

[085-236]dependence of Ireland on the imperial crown of Great Britain.

[085-237]But William's days were already numbered. On the 8th of March, 1702,

[085-238]when little more than fifty years of age, he died from the effects of a

[085-239]fall from his horse. His reign over Ireland is synonymous to the minds

[085-240]of that people of disaster, proscription and spoliation; of violated

[085-241]faith and broken compacts; but these wrongs were done in his name

[085-242]rather than by his orders; often without his knowledge, and sometimes

[085-243]against his will. Rigid as that will was, it was forced to bend to the

[085-244]anti-Popery storm which swept over the British Islands after the

[085-245]abdication of King James; but the vices and follies of his times ought

[085-246]no more be laid to the personal account of William than of James or

[085-247]Louis, against whom he fought.
