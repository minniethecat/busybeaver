CHAPTER VI.

[006-001]KINGS OF THE SEVENTH CENTURY.

[006-002]THE five years of the sixth century, which remained after the death of

[006-003]Hugh II., were filled by Hugh III., son of Dermid, the semi-Pagan. Hugh

[006-004]IV. succeeded (A.D. 599) and reigned for several years; two other

[006-005]kings, of small account, reigned seven years; Donald II. (A.D. 624)

[006-006]reigned sixteen years; Connall and Kellach, brothers, (A.D. 640)

[006-007]reigned jointly sixteen years; they were succeeded (A.D. 656) by Dermid

[006-008]and Blathmac, brothers, who reigned jointly seven years; Shanasagh, son

[006-009]of the former, reigned six years; Kenfala, four; Finnacta, "the

[006-010]hospitable," twenty years, and Loingsech (A.D. 693) eight years.

[006-011]Throughout this century the power of the Church was constantly on the

[006-012]increase, and is visible in many important changes. The last armed

[006-013]struggle of Druidism, and the only invasion of Ireland by the

[006-014]Anglo-Saxons, are also events of the civil history of the seventh

[006-015]century.

[006-016]The reign, of Donald II. is notable for the passing away of most of

[006-017]those saintly men, the second generation of Irish abbots and bishops;

[006-018]for the foundation of the celebrated school of Lismore on the Munster

[006-019]Blackwater; and the battle of Moira, in the present county of Down. Of

[006-020]the school and the saints we shall speak hereafter; the battle deserves

[006-021]more immediate mention.

[006-022]The cause of the battle was the pretension of the petty Prince of

[006-023]Ulidia, which comprised little more than the present county of Down, to

[006-024]be recognised as Prince of all Ulster. Now the Hy-Nial family, not only

[006-025]had long given monarchs to all Ireland, but had also the lion's share

[006-026]of their own Province, and King Donald as their head could not permit

[006-027]their ascendency to be disputed. The ancestors of the present

[006-028]pretender, Congal, surnamed "the squint-eyed," had twice received and

[006-029]cherished the licentious Bards when under the ban of Tara, and his

[006-030]popularity with that still powerful order was one prop of his ambition.

[006-031]It is pretty clear also that the last rally of Druidism against

[006-032]Christianity took place behind his banner, on the plain of Moira. It

[006-033]was the year 637, and preparations had long gone on on both sides for a

[006-034]final trial of strength. Congal had recruited numerous bands of Saxons,

[006-035]Britons, Picts and Argyle Scots, who poured into the harbours of Down

[006-036]for months, and were marshalled on the banks of the Lagan, to sustain

[006-037]his cause. The Poets of succeeding ages have dwelt much in detail on

[006-038]the occurrences of this memorable day. It was what might strictly be

[006-039]called a pitched battle, time and place being fixed by mutual

[006-040]agreement. King Donald was accompanied by his Bard, who described to

[006-041]him, as they came in sight, the several standards of Congal's host, and

[006-042]who served under them. Conspicuous above all, the ancient banner of the

[006-043]Red Branch Knights-"a yellow lion wrought on green satin"—floated over

[006-044]Congal's host. On the other side the monarch commanded in person,

[006-045]accompanied by his kinsmen, the sons of Hugh III. The red hand of

[006-046]Tirowen, the cross of Tirconnell, the eagle and lion of Innishowen, the

[006-047]axes of Fanad, were in his ranks, ranged closely round his own

[006-048]standard. The cause of the Constitution and the Church prevailed, and

[006-049]Druidism mourned its last hope extinguished on the plains of Moira, in

[006-050]the death of Congal, and the defeat of his vast army. King Donald

[006-051]returned in triumph to celebrate his victory at Emania and to receive

[006-052]the benediction of the Church at Armagh.

[006-053]The sons of Hugh III., Dermid and Blathmac, zealous and pious Christian

[006-054]princes, survived the field of Moira and other days of danger, and

[006-055]finally attained the supreme power—A.D. 656. Like the two kings of

[006-056]Sparta they reigned jointly, dividing between them the labours and

[006-057]cares of State. In their reign, that terrible scourge, called in Irish,

[006-058]"the yellow plague," after ravaging great part of Britain, broke out

[006-059]with undiminished virulence in Erin (A.D. 664). To heighten the awful

[006-060]sense of inevitable doom, an eclipse of the sun occurred concurrently

[006-061]with the appearance of the pestilence on the first Sunday in May. It

[006-062]was the season when the ancient sun-god had been accustomed to receive

[006-063]his annual oblations, and we can well believe that those whose hearts

[006-064]still trembled at the name of Bel, must have connected the eclipse and

[006-065]the plague with the revolution in the national worship, and the

[006-066]overthrow of the ancient gods on that "plain of prostration," where

[006-067]they had so long received the homage of an entire people. Among the

[006-068]victims of this fearful visitation—which, like the modern cholera,

[006-069]swept through all ranks and classes of society, and returned in the

[006-070]same track for several successive seasons—were very many of those

[006-071]venerated men, the third and fourth generation of the Abbots and

[006-072]Bishops. The Munster King, and many of the chieftain class shared the

[006-073]common lot. Lastly, the royal brothers fell themselves victims to the

[006-074]epidemic, which so sadly signalizes their reign.

[006-075]The only conflicts that occurred on Irish soil with a Pictish or an

[006-076]Anglo-Saxon force—if we except those who formed a contingent of

[006-077]Congal's army at Moira—occurred in the time of the hospitable Finnacta.

[006-078]The Pictish force, with their leaders, were totally defeated at

[006-079]Rathmore, in Antrim (A.D. 680), but the Anglo-Saxon expedition (A.D.

[006-080]684) seems not to have been either expected or guarded against. As

[006-081]leading to the mention of other interesting events, we must set this

[006-082]inroad clearly before the reader.

[006-083]The Saxons had now been for four centuries in Britain, the older

[006-084]inhabitants of which—Celts like the Gauls and Irish—they had cruelly

[006-085]harassed, just as the Milesian Irish oppressed their Belgic

[006-086]predecessors, and as the Normans, in turn, will be found oppressing

[006-087]both Celt and Saxon in England and Ireland. Britain had been divided by

[006-088]the Saxon leaders into eight separate kingdoms, the people and princes

[006-089]of several of which were converted to Christianity in the fifth, sixth,

[006-090]and seventh century, though some of them did not receive the Gospel

[006-091]before the beginning of the eighth. The Saxons of Kent and the Southern

[006-092]Kingdoms generally were converted by missionaries from France or Rome,

[006-093]or native preachers of the first or second Christian generation; those

[006-094]of Northumbria recognise as their Apostles St. Aidan and St. Cuthbert,

[006-095]two Fathers from Iona. The Kingdom of Northumbria, as the name implies,

[006-096]embraced nearly all the country from the Humber to the Pictish border.

[006-097]York was its capital, and the seat of its ecclesiastical primacy,

[006-098]where, at the time we speak of, the illustrious Wilfrid was

[006-099]maintaining, with a wilful and unscrupulous king, a struggle not unlike

[006-100]that which Becket maintained with Henry II. This Prince, Egfrid by

[006-101]name, was constantly engaged in wars with his Saxon cotemporaries, or

[006-102]the Picts and Scots. In the summer of 683 he sent an expedition under

[006-103]the command of Beort, one of his earls, to ravage the coast of

[006-104]Leinster. Beort landed probably in the Boyne, and swept over the rich

[006-105]plain of Meath with fire and sword, burning churches, driving off herds

[006-106]and flocks, and slaughtering the clergy and the husbandmen. The piety

[006-107]of an after age saw in the retribution which overtook Egfrid the

[006-108]following year, when he was slain by the Picts and Scots, the judgment

[006-109]of Heaven, avenging the unprovoked wrongs of the Irish. His Scottish

[006-110]conquerors, returning good for evil, carried his body to Iona, where it

[006-111]was interred with all due honour.

[006-112]Iona was now in the zenith of its glory. The barren rock, about three

[006-113]miles in length, was covered with monastic buildings, and its cemetery

[006-114]was already adorned with the tombs of saints and kings. Five successors

[006-115]of Columbkill slept in peace around their holy Founder, and a sixth,

[006-116]equal in learning and sanctity to any who preceded him, received the

[006-117]remains of King Egfrid from the hands of his conquerors. This was Abbot

[006-118]Adamnan, to whom Ireland and Scotland are equally indebted for his

[006-119]admirable writings, and who might almost dispute with Bede himself, the

[006-120]title of Father of British History. Adamnan regarded the fate of

[006-121]Egfrid, we may be sure, in the light of a judgment on him for his

[006-122]misdeeds, as Bede and British Christians very generally did. He

[006-123]learned, too, that there were in Northumbria several Christian

[006-124]captives, carried off in Beort's expedition and probably sold into

[006-125]slavery. Now every missionary that ever went out from Iona, had taught

[006-126]that to reduce Christians to slavery was wholly inconsistent with a

[006-127]belief in the doctrines of the Gospel. St. Aidan, the Apostle of

[006-128]Northumbria, had refused the late Egfrid's father absolution, on one

[006-129]occasion, until he solemnly promised to restore their freedom to

[006-130]certain captives of this description. In the same spirit Adamnan

[006-131]voluntarily undertook a journey to York, where Aldfrid (a Prince

[006-132]educated in Ireland, and whose "Itinerary" of Ireland we still have)

[006-133]now reigned. The Abbot of Iona succeeded in his humane mission, and

[006-134]crossing over to his native land, he restored sixty of the captives to

[006-135]their homes and kindred. While the liberated exiles rejoiced on the

[006-136]plain of Meath, the tent of the Abbot of Iona was pitched on the rath

[006-137]of Tara—a fact which would seem to indicate that already, in little

[006-138]more than a century since the interdict had fallen on it, the edifices

[006-139]which made so fine a show in the days of Patrick were ruined and

[006-140]uninhabitable. Either at Tara, or some other of the royal residences,

[006-141]Adamnan on this visit procured the passing of a law, (A.D. 684,)

[006-142]forbidding women to accompany an army to battle, or to engage

[006-143]personally in the conflict. The mild maternal genius of Christianity is

[006-144]faithfully exhibited in such a law, which consummates the glory of the

[006-145]worthy successor of Columbkill. It is curious here to observe that it

[006-146]was not until another hundred years had past—not till the beginning of

[006-147]the ninth century—that the clergy were "exempt" from military service.

[006-148]So slow and patient is the process by which Christianity infuses itself

[006-149]into the social life of a converted people!

[006-151]The long reign of FINNACTA, the hospitable, who may, for his many other

[006-152]virtues, be called also the pious, was rendered farther remarkable in

[006-153]the annals of the country by the formal abandonment of the special tax,

[006-154]so long levied upon, and so long and desperately resisted by, the men

[006-155]of Leinster. The all-powerful intercessor in this case was Saint

[006-156]Moling, of the royal house of Leinster, and Bishop of Fernamore (now

[006-157]Ferns). In the early part of his reign Finnacta seems not to have been

[006-158]disposed to collect this invidious tax by force; but, yielding to other

[006-159]motives, he afterwards took a different view of his duty, and marched

[006-160]into Leinster to compel its payment. Here the holy Prelate of Ferns met

[006-161]him, and related a Vision in which he had been instructed to demand the

[006-162]abolition of the impost. The abolition, he contended, should not be

[006-163]simply a suspension, but final and for ever. The tribute was, at this

[006-164]period, enormous; 15,000 head of cattle annually. The decision must

[006-165]have been made about the time that Abbot Adamnan was in Ireland, (A.D.

[006-166]684,) and that illustrious personage is said to have been opposed to

[006-167]the abolition. Abolished it was, and though its re-enactment was often

[006-168]attempted, the authority of Saint Moling's solemn settlement, prevented

[006-169]it from being re-enforced for any length of time, except as a political

[006-170]or military infliction.

[006-171]Finnacta fell in battle in the 20th year of his long and glorious

[006-172]reign; and is commemorated as a saint in the Irish calendar. St. Moling

[006-173]survived him three years, and St. Adamnan, so intimately connected with

[006-174]his reign, ten years. The latter revisited Ireland in 697, under the

[006-175]short reign of Loingsech, and concerned himself chiefly in endeavouring

[006-176]to induce his countrymen to adopt the Roman rule, as to the tonsure,

[006-177]and the celebration of Easter. On this occasion there was an important

[006-178]Synod of the Clergy, under the presidency of Flan, Archbishop of

[006-179]Armagh, held at Tara. Nothing could be more natural than such an

[006-180]assembly in such a place, at such a period. In every recorded instance

[006-181]the power of the clergy had been omnipotent in politics for above a

[006-182]century. St. Patrick had expurgated the old constitution; St. Ruadan's

[006-183]curse drove the kings from Tara; St. Columbkill had established the

[006-184]independence of Alba, and preserved the Bardic Order; St. Moling had

[006-185]abolished the Leinster tribute. If their power was irresistible in the

[006-186]sixth and especially in the seventh centuries, we must do these

[006-187]celebrated Abbots and Bishops the justice to remember that it was

[006-188]always exercised against the oppression of the weak by the strong, to

[006-189]mitigate the horrors of war, to uphold the right of sanctuary (the

[006-190]_Habeus Corpus_ of that rude age), and for the maintenance and spread

[006-191]of sound Christian principles.
