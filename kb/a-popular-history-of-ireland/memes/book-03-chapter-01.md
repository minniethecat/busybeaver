CHAPTER I.

[017-001]THE FORTUNES OF THE FAMILY OF BRIAN.

[017-002]The last scene of the Irish monarchy, before it entered on the

[017-003]anarchical period, was not destitute of an appropriate grandeur. It was

[017-004]the death-bed scene of the second Malachy, the rival, ally, and

[017-005]successor of the great Brian. After the eventful day of Clontarf he

[017-006]resumed the monarchy, without opposition, and for eight years he

[017-007]continued in its undisturbed enjoyment. The fruitful land of Meath

[017-008]again gave forth its abundance, unscourged by the spoiler, and beside

[017-009]its lakes and streams the hospitable Ard-Righ had erected, or restored,

[017-010]three hundred fortified houses, where, as his poets sung, shelter was

[017-011]freely given to guests from the king of the elements. His own favourite

[017-012]residence was at Dunnasciath ("the fort of shields"), in the north-west

[017-013]angle of Lough Ennel, in the present parish of Dysart. In the eighth

[017-014]year after Clontarf—the summer of 1022—the Dublin Danes once again

[017-015]ventured on a foray into East-Meath, and the aged monarch marched to

[017-016]meet them. At Athboy he encountered the enemy, and drove them, routed

[017-017]and broken, out of the ancient mensal land of the Irish kings.

[017-018]Thirty days after that victory he was called on to confront the

[017-019]conqueror of all men, even Death. He had reached the age of

[017-020]seventy-three, and he prepared to meet his last hour with the zeal and

[017-021]humility of a true Christian. To Dunnasciath repaired Amalgaid,

[017-022]Archbishop of Armagh, the Abbots of Clonmacnoise and of Durrow, with a

[017-023]numerous train of the clergy. For greater solitude, the dying king was

[017-024]conveyed into an island of the lake opposite his fort—then called

[017-025]Inis-Cro, now Cormorant Island—and there, "after intense penance," on

[017-026]the fourth of the Nones of September precisely, died Malachy, son of

[017-027]Donald, son of Donogh, in the fond language of the bards, "the pillar

[017-028]of the dignity and nobility of the western world:" and "the seniors of

[017-029]all Ireland sung masses, hymns, psalms, and canticles for the welfare

[017-030]of his soul."

[017-031]"This," says the old Translator of the Clonmacnoise Annals, "was the

[017-032]last king of Ireland of Irish blood, that had the crown; yet there were

[017-033]seven kings after without crown, before the coming in of the English."

[017-034]Of these seven subsequent kings we are to write under the general title

[017-035]of "the War of Succession." They are called Ard-Righ _go Fresabra_,

[017-036]that is, kings opposed, or unrecognised, by certain tribes, or

[017-037]Provinces. For it was essential to the completion of the title, as we

[017-038]have before seen, that when the claimant was of Ulster, he should have

[017-039]Connaught and Munster, or Leinster and Munster, in his obedience: in

[017-040]other words, he should be able to command the allegiance of two-thirds

[017-041]of his suffragans. If of Munster, he should be equally potent in the

[017-042]other Provinces, in order to rank among the recognised kings of Erin.

[017-043]Whether some of the seven kings subsequent to Malachy II., who assumed

[017-044]the title, were not fairly entitled to it, we do not presume to say; it

[017-045]is our simpler task to narrate the incidents of that brilliant war of

[017-046]succession, which occupies almost all the interval between the Danish

[017-047]and Anglo-Norman invasions. The chaunt of the funeral Mass of Malachy

[017-048]was hardly heard upon Lough Ennel, when Donogh O'Brien despatched his

[017-049]agents, claiming the crown from the Provincial Princes. He was the

[017-050]eldest son of Brian by his second marriage, and his mother was an

[017-051]O'Conor, an additional source of strength to him, in the western

[017-052]Province. It had fallen to the lot of Donogh, and his elder brother,

[017-053]Teigue or Thaddeus, to conduct the remnant of the Dalcassians from

[017-054]Clontarf to their home. Marching through Ossory, by the great southern

[017-055]road, they were attacked in their enfeebled state by the lord of that

[017-056]brave little border territory, on whom Brian's hand had fallen with

[017-057]heavy displeasure. Wounded as many of them were, they fought their way

[017-058]desperately towards Cashel, leaving 150 men dead in one of their

[017-059]skirmishes. Of all who had left the Shannon side to combat with the

[017-060]enemy, but 850 men lived to return to their homes.

[017-061]No sooner had they reached Kinkora, than a fierce dispute arose,

[017-062]between the friends of Teigue and Donogh, as to which should reign over

[017-063]Munster. A battle ensued, with doubtful result, but by the intercession

[017-064]of the Clergy this unnatural feud was healed, and the brothers reigned

[017-065]conjointly for nine years afterwards, until Teigue fell in an

[017-066]engagement in Ely (Queen's County), as was charged and believed, by the

[017-067]machinations of his colleague and brother. Thorlogh, son of Teigue, was

[017-068]the foster-son, and at this time the guest or hostage of Dermid of

[017-069]Leinster, the founder of the McMurrogh family, which had now risen into

[017-070]the rank justly forfeited by the traitor Maelmurra. When he reached

[017-071]man's age he married the daughter of Dermid, and we shall soon hear of

[017-072]him again asserting in Munster the pretensions of the eldest surviving

[017-073]branch of the O'Brien family.

[017-074]The death of his brother and of Malachy within the same year, proved

[017-075]favourable to the ambition of Donogh O'Brien. All Munster submitted to

[017-076]his sway; Connaught was among the first to recognise his title as

[017-077]Ard-Righ. Ossory and Leinster, though unwillingly, gave in their

[017-078]adhesion. But Meath refused to recognise him, and placed its government

[017-079]in commission, in the hands of Con O'Lochan, the arch-poet, and

[017-080]Corcran, the priest, already more than once mentioned. The country,

[017-081]north of Meath, obeyed Flaherty O'Neil, of Aileach, whose ambition, as

[017-082]well as that of all his house, was to restore the northern supremacy,

[017-083]which had continued unbroken, from the fourth to the ninth century.

[017-084]This Flaherty was a vigorous, able, and pious Prince, who held stoutly

[017-085]on to the northern half-kingdom. In the year 1030 he made the frequent

[017-086]but adventurous pilgrimage to Rome, from which he is called, in the

[017-087]pedigree of his house, _an Trostain_, or the cross-bearer.

[017-088]The greatest obstacle, however, to the complete ascendency of Donogh,

[017-089]arose in the person of his nephew, now advanced to manhood. Thorlogh

[017-090]O'Brien possessed much of the courage and ability of his grandfather,

[017-091]and he had at his side, a faithful and powerful ally in his

[017-092]foster-father, Dermid, of Leinster. Rightly or wrongly, on proof or on

[017-093]suspicion, he regarded his uncle as his father's murderer, and he

[017-094]pursued his vengeance with a skill and constancy worthy of _Hamlet_. At

[017-095]the time of his father's death, he was a mere lad—in his fourteenth

[017-096]year. But, as he grew older, he accompanied his foster-father in all

[017-097]his expeditions, and rapidly acquired a soldier's fame. By marriage

[017-098]with Dervorgoil, daughter of the Lord of Ossory, he strengthened his

[017-099]influence at the most necessary point; and what, with so good a cause

[017-100]and such fast friends as he made in exile, his success against his

[017-101]uncle is little to be wondered at. Leinster and Ossory, which had

[017-102]temporarily submitted to Donogh's claim, soon found good pretexts for

[017-103]refusing him tribute, and a border war, marked by all the usual

[017-104]atrocities, raged for several successive seasons. The contest, is

[017-105]relieved, however, of its purely civil character, by the capture of

[017-106]Waterford, still Danish, in 1037, and of Dublin, in 1051. On this

[017-107]occasion, Dermid, of Leinster, bestowed the city on his son Morrogh

[017-108](grandfather of Strongbow's ally), to whom the remnant of its

[017-109]inhabitants, as well as their kinsmen in Man, submitted for the time

[017-110]with what grace they could.

[017-111]The position of Donogh O'Brien became yearly weaker. His rival had

[017-112]youth, energy, and fortune on his side. The Prince of Connaught finally

[017-113]joined him, and thus, a league was formed, which overcame all

[017-114]opposition. In the year 1058, Donogh received a severe defeat at the

[017-115]base of the Galtees; and although he went into the house of O'Conor the

[017-116]same year, and humbly submitted to him, it only postponed his day of

[017-117]reckoning. Three years after O'Conor took Kinkora, and Dermid, of

[017-118]Leinster, burned Limerick, and took hostages as far southward as Saint

[017-119]Brendan's hill (Tralee). The next year Donogh O'Brien, then fully

[017-120]fourscore years of age, weary of life and of the world, took the

[017-121]cross-staff, and departed on a pilgrimage to Rome, where he died soon

[017-122]after, in the monastery of St. Stephen. It is said by some writers that

[017-123]Donogh brought with him to Rome and presented to the Pope, Alexander

[017-124]II., the crown of his father—and from this tradition many theories and

[017-125]controversies have sprung. It is not unlikely that a deposed monarch

[017-126]should have carried into exile whatever portable wealth he still

[017-127]retained, nor that he should have presented his crown to the Sovereign

[017-128]Pontiff before finally quitting the world. But as to conferring with

[017-129]the crown, the sovereignty of which it was once an emblem, neither

[017-130]reason nor religion obliges us to believe any such hypothesis.

[017-131]Dermid of Leinster, upon the banishment of Donogh, son of Brian (A.D.

[017-132]1063), became actual ruler of the southern half-kingdom and nominal

[017-133]Ard-Righ, "with opposition." The two-fold antagonism to this Prince,

[017-134]came, as might be expected from Conor, son of Malachy, the head of the

[017-135]southern Hy-Nial dynasty, and from the chiefs of the elder dynasty of

[017-136]the North. Thorlogh O'Brien, now King of Cashel, loyally repaid, by his

[017-137]devoted adherence, the deep debt he owed in his struggles and his early

[017-138]youth to Dermid. There are few instances in our Annals of a more

[017-139]devoted friendship than existed between these brave and able Princes

[017-140]through all the changes of half a century. No one act seems to have

[017-141]broken the life-long intimacy of Dermid and Thorlogh; no cloud ever

[017-142]came between them; no mistrust, no distrust. Rare and precious felicity

[017-143]of human experience! How many myriads of men have sighed out their

[017-144]souls in vain desire for that best blessing which Heaven can bestow, a

[017-145]true, unchanging, unsuspecting friend!

[017-147]To return: Conor O'Melaghlin could not see, without deep-seated

[017-148]discontent, a Prince of Leinster assume the rank which his father and

[017-149]several of his ancestors had held. A border strife between Meath and

[017-150]Leinster arose not unlike that which had been waged a few years before

[017-151]for the deposition of Donogh, between Leinster and Ossory on the one

[017-152]part, and Munster on the other. Various were the encounters, whose

[017-153]obscure details are seldom preserved to us. But the good fortune of

[017-154]Dermid prevailed in all, until, in the year 1070, he lost Morrogh, his

[017-155]heir, by a natural death at Dublin, and Gluniarn, another son, fell in

[017-156]battle with the men of Meath. Two years later, in the battle of Ova, in

[017-157]the same territory, and against the same enemy, Dermid himself fell,

[017-158]with the lord of Forth, and a great host of Dublin Danes and Leinster

[017-159]men. The triumph of the son of Malachy, and the sorrow and anger of

[017-160]Leinster, were equally great. The bards have sung the praise of Dermid

[017-161]in strains which history accepts: they praise his ruddy aspect and

[017-162]laughing teeth; they remember how he upheld the standard of war, and

[017-163]none dared contend with him in battle; they denounce vengeance on Meath

[017-164]as soon as his death-feast is over—a vengeance too truly pursued.

[017-165]As a picture of the manners and habits of thought in those tunes, the

[017-166]fate of Conor, son of Melaghlin, and its connection with the last

[017-167]illness and death of Thorlogh O'Brien, are worthy of mention. Conor was

[017-168]treacherously slain, the year after the battle of Ova, in a parley with

[017-169]his own nephew, though the parley was held under the protection of the

[017-170]_Bachall-Isa_, or Staff, of Christ, the most revered relic of the Irish

[017-171]Church. After his death, his body was buried in the great Church of

[017-172]Clonmacnoise, in his own patrimony. But Thorlogh O'Brien perhaps, from

[017-173]his friendship for Dermid, carried off his head, as the head of an

[017-174]enemy, to Kinkora. When it was placed in his presence in his palace, a

[017-175]mouse ran out from the dead man's head, and under the king's mantle,

[017-176]which occasioned him such a fright that he grew suddenly sick, his hair

[017-177]fell off, and his life was despaired of. It was on Good Friday that the

[017-178]buried head was carried away, and on Easter Sunday, it was tremblingly

[017-179]restored again, with two rings of gold as a peace offering to the

[017-180]Church. Thus were God and Saint Kieran vindicated. Thorlogh O'Brien

[017-181]slowly regained his strength, though Keating, and the authors he

[017-182]followed, think he was never the same man again, after the fright he

[017-183]received from the head of Conor O'Melaghlin. He died peaceably and full

[017-184]of penitence, at Kinkora, on the eve of the Ides of July, A.D. 1086,

[017-185]after severe physical suffering. He was in the 77th year of his age,

[017-186]the 32nd of his rule over Munster, and the 13th—since the death of

[017-187]Dermid of Leinster—in his actual sovereignty of the southern half, and

[017-188]nominal rule of the whole kingdom. He was succeeded by his son

[017-189]Murkertach, or Murtogh, afterwards called _More_, or the great.

[017-190]We have thus traced to the third generation the political fortunes of

[017-191]the family of Brian, which includes so much of the history of those

[017-192]times. That family had become, and was long destined to remain, the

[017-193]first in rank and influence in the southern half-kingdom. But internal

[017-194]discord in a great house, as in a great state, is fatal to the

[017-195]peaceable transmission of power. That "acknowledged right of birth" to

[017-196]which a famous historian attributes "the peaceful successions" of

[017-197]modern Europe, was too little respected in those ages, in many

[017-198]countries of Christendom—and had no settled prescription in its favour

[017-199]among the Irish. Primogeniture and the whole scheme of feudal

[017-200]dependence seems to have been an essential preparative for modern

[017-201]civilization: but as Ireland had escaped the legions of Rome, so she

[017-202]existed without the circle of feudal organization. When that system did

[017-203]at length appear upon her soil it was embodied in an invading host, and

[017-204]patriot zeal could discern nothing good, nothing imitable in the laws

[017-205]and customs of an enemy, whose armed presence in the land was an insult

[017-206]to its inhabitants. Thus did our Island twice lose the discipline which

[017-207]elsewhere laid the foundation of great states: once in the Roman, and

[017-208]again in the Feudal era.
