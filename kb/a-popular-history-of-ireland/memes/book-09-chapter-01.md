CHAPTER I.

[064-001]JAMES I.—FLIGHT OF THE EARLS—CONFISCATION OF ULSTER—PENAL

[064-002]LAWS—PARLIAMENTARY OPPOSITION.

[064-003]James the Sixth of Scotland was in his 37th year when he ascended the

[064-004]throne under the title of "James the First, King of Great Britain and

[064-005]Ireland." His accession naturally excited the most hopeful expectations

[064-006]of good government in the breasts of the Irish Catholics. He was son of

[064-007]Mary Queen of Scots, whom they looked upon as a martyr to her religion,

[064-008]and grandson of that gallant King James who styled himself "Defender of

[064-009]the Faith," and "_Dominus Hiberniae_" in introducing the first Jesuits

[064-010]to the Ulster Princes. His ancestors had always been in alliance with

[064-011]the Irish, and the antiquaries of that nation loved to trace their

[064-012]descent from the Scoto-Irish chiefs who first colonized Argyle, and

[064-013]were for ages crowned at Scone. He himself was known to have assisted

[064-014]the late Catholic struggle as effectually, though less openly than the

[064-015]King of Spain, and it is certain that he had employed Catholic agents,

[064-016]like Lord Home and Sir James Lindsay, to excite an interest in his

[064-017]succession among the Catholics, both in the British Islands and on the

[064-018]Continent.

[064-019]The first acts of the new sovereign were calculated to confirm the

[064-020]expectations of Catholic liberty thus entertained. He was anxious to

[064-021]make an immediate and lasting peace with Spain; refused to receive a

[064-022]special embassy from the Hollanders; his ambassador at Paris was known

[064-023]to be on terms of intimacy with the Pope's Nuncio; and although

[064-024]personally he assumed the tone of an Anglican Churchman, on crossing

[064-025]the border he had invited leading Catholics to his Court, and conferred

[064-026]the honour of Knighthood on some of their number. The imprudent

[064-027]demonstrations in the Irish towns were easily quieted, and no immediate

[064-028]notice was taken of their leaders. In May, 1603, Mountjoy, on whom

[064-029]James had conferred the higher rank of Lord Lieutenant, leaving Carew

[064-030]as Lord Deputy, proceeded to England, accompanied by O'Neil, Roderick

[064-031]O'Donnell, Maguire, and other Irish gentlemen. The veteran Tyrone, now

[064-032]past threescore, though hooted by the London rabble, was graciously

[064-033]received in that court, with which he had been familiar forty years

[064-034]before. He was at once confirmed in his title, the Earldom of

[064-035]Tyrconnell was created for O'Donnell, and the Lordship of Enniskillen

[064-036]for Maguire. Mountjoy, created Earl of Devonshire, retained the title

[064-037]of Lord Lieutenant, with permission to reside in England, and was

[064-038]rewarded by the appointment of Master of the Ordnance and Warden of the

[064-039]New Forest, with an ample pension from the Crown to him and his heirs

[064-040]for ever, the grant of the county of Lecale (Down), and the estate of

[064-041]Kingston Hall, in Dorsetshire, He survived but three short years to

[064-042]enjoy all these riches and honours; at the age of 44, wasted with

[064-043]dissipation and domestic troubles, he passed to his final account.

[064-044]The necessity of conciliating the Catholic party in England, of

[064-045]maintaining peace in Ireland, and prosecuting the Spanish negotiations,

[064-046]not less, perhaps, than his own original bias, led James to deal

[064-047]favourably with the Catholics at first. But having attempted to enforce

[064-048]the new Anglican Canons, adopted in 1604, against the Puritans, that

[064-049]party retaliated by raising against him the cry of favouring the

[064-050]Papists. This cry alarmed the King, who had always before his eyes the

[064-051]fear of Presbyterianism, and he accordingly made a speech in the Star

[064-052]Chamber, declaring his utter detestation of Popery, and published a

[064-053]proclamation banishing all Catholic missionaries from the country. All

[064-054]magistrates were instructed to enforce the penal laws with rigour, and

[064-055]an elaborate spy system for the discovery of concealed recusants was

[064-056]set on foot. This reign of treachery and terror drove a few desperate

[064-057]men into the gunpowder plot of the following year, and rendered it

[064-058]difficult, if not impossible, for the King to return to the policy of

[064-059]toleration, with which, to do him justice, he seems to have set out

[064-060]from Scotland.

[064-061]Carew, President of Munster during the late war, became Deputy to

[064-062]Mountjoy on his departure for England. He was succeeded in October,

[064-063]1604, by Sir Arthur Chichester, who, with the exception of occasional

[064-064]absences at Court, continued in office for a period of eleven years.

[064-065]This nobleman, a native of England, furnishes, in many points, a

[064-066]parallel to his cotemporary and friend, Robert Boyle, Earl of Cork. The

[064-067]object of his life was to found and to endow the Donegal peerage out of

[064-068]the spoils of Ulster, as richly as Boyle endowed his earldom out of the

[064-069]confiscation of Munster. Both were Puritans rather than Churchmen, in

[064-070]their religious opinions; Chichester, a pupil of the celebrated

[064-071]Cartwright, and a favourer all his life of the congregational clergy in

[064-072]Ulster. But they carried their repugnance to the interference of the

[064-073]civil magistrate in matters of conscience so discreetly as to satisfy

[064-074]the high church notions both of James and Elizabeth. For the violence

[064-075]they were thus compelled to exercise against themselves, they seem to

[064-076]have found relief in bitter and continuous persecution of others.

[064-077]Boyle, as the leading spirit in the government of Munster, as Lord

[064-078]Treasurer, and occasionally as Lord Justice, had ample opportunities,

[064-079]during his long career of forty years, to indulge at once his avarice

[064-080]and his bigotry; and no situation was ever more favourable than

[064-081]Chichester's for a proconsul, eager to enrich himself at the expense of

[064-082]a subjugated Province.

[064-083]In the projected work of the reduction of the whole country to the laws

[064-084]and customs of England, it is instructive to observe that a Parliament

[064-085]was not called in the first place. The reformers proceeded by

[064-086]proclamations, letters patent, and orders in council, not by

[064-087]legislation. The whole island was divided into 32 counties and 6

[064-088]judicial circuits, all of which were visited by Justices in the second

[064-089]or third year of this reign, and afterwards semi-annually. On the

[064-090]Northern Circuit Sir Edward Pelham and Sir John Davis were accompanied

[064-091]by the Deputy in person, with a numerous retinue. In some places the

[064-092]towns were so wasted by the late war, pestilence, and famine, that the

[064-093]Viceregal party were obliged to camp out in the fields, and to carry

[064-094]with them their own provisions. The Courts were held in ruined castles

[064-095]and deserted monasteries; Irish interpreters were at every step found

[064-096]necessary; sheriffs were installed in Tyrone and Tyrconnell for the

[064-097]first time; all lawyers appearing in court and all justices of the

[064-098]peace were tendered the oath of supremacy—the refusal of which

[064-099]necessarily excluded Catholics both from the bench and the bar. An

[064-100]enormous amount of litigation as to the law of real property was

[064-101]created by a judgment of the Court of King's Bench at Dublin, in 1605,

[064-102]by which the ancient Irish customs, of tanistry and gavelkind, were

[064-103]declared null and void, and the entire Feudal system, with its rights

[064-104]of primogeniture, hereditary succession, entail, and vassalage, was

[064-105]held to exist in as full force in England. Very evidently this decision

[064-106]was not less a violation of the articles of Mellifont than was the

[064-107]King's proclamation against freedom of conscience issued about the same

[064-108]time.

[064-109]Sir John Davis, who has left us two very interesting tracts on Irish

[064-110]affairs, speaking of the new legal regulations of which he was one of

[064-111]the principal superintendents, observes that the old-fashioned

[064-112]allowances to be found so often in the Pipe-Rolls, _pro guidagio et

[064-113]spiagio_, into the interior, may well be spared thereafter, since "the

[064-114]under sheriffs and bailiffs errant are better guides and spies in time

[064-115]of peace than they were found in time of war." He adds, what we may

[064-116]very well believe, that the Earl of Tyrone complained he had so many

[064-117]eyes upon him, that he could not drink a cup of sack without the

[064-118]government being advertised of it within a few hours afterwards. This

[064-119]system of social _espionage_, so repugnant to all the habits of the

[064-120]Celtic family, was not the only mode of annoyance resorted to against

[064-121]the veteran chief. Every former dependent who could be induced to

[064-122]dispute his claims as a landlord, under the new relations established

[064-123]by the late decision, was sure of a judgment in his favour. Disputes

[064-124]about boundaries with O'Cane, about the commutation of chieftain-rents

[064-125]into tenantry, about church lands claimed by Montgomery, Protestant

[064-126]Bishop of Derry, were almost invariably decided against him. Harassed

[064-127]by these proceedings, and all uncertain of the future, O'Neil listened

[064-128]willingly to the treacherous suggestion of St. Lawrence and Lord Howth,

[064-129]that the leading Catholics of the Pale, and those of Ulster, should

[064-130]endeavour to form another confederation. The execution of Father

[064-131]Garnet, Provincial of the Jesuits in England, the heavy fines inflicted

[064-132]on Lords Stourton, Mordaunt, and Montague, and the new oath of

[064-133]allegiance, framed by Archbishop Abbott, and sanctioned by the English

[064-134]Parliament—all events of the year 1606—were calculated to inspire the

[064-135]Irish Catholics with desperate councils. A dutiful remonstrance against

[064-136]the Act of Uniformity the previous year had been signed by the

[064-137]principal Anglo-Irish Catholics for transmission to the King, but their

[064-138]delegates were seized and imprisoned in the Castle, while their

[064-139]principal agent, Sir Patrick Barnwell, was sent to London and confined

[064-140]in the Tower. A meeting, at Lord Howth's suggestion, was held about

[064-141]Christmas, 1606, at the Castle of Maynooth, then in possession of the

[064-142]dowager Countess of Kildare, one of whose daughters was married to

[064-143]Christopher Nugent, Baron of Delvin, and her granddaughter to Rory,

[064-144]Earl of Tyrconnell. There were present O'Neil, O'Donnell, and O'Cane,

[064-145]on the one part, and Lords Delvin and Howth on the other. The precise

[064-146]result of this conference, disguised under the pretext of a Christmas

[064-147]party, was never made known, but the fact that it had been held, and

[064-148]that the parties present had entertained the project of another

[064-149]confederacy for the defence of the Catholic religion, was mysteriously

[064-150]communicated in an anonymous letter, directed to Sir William Usher,

[064-151]Clerk of the Council, which was dropped in the Council Chamber of

[064-152]Dublin Castle, in March, 1607. This letter, it is now generally

[064-153]believed, was written by Lord Howth, who was thought to have been

[064-154]employed by Secretary Cecil, to entrap the northern Earls, in order to

[064-155]betray them. In May, O'Neil and O'Donnell were cited to attend the Lord

[064-156]Deputy in Dublin, but the charges were for the time kept in abeyance,

[064-157]and they were ordered to appear in London before the feast of

[064-158]Michaelmas. Early in September O'Neil was with Chichester at Slane, in

[064-159]Meath, when he received a letter from Maguire, who had been out of the

[064-160]country, conveying information on which he immediately acted. Taking

[064-161]leave of the Lord Deputy as if to prepare for his journey to London, he

[064-162]made some stay with his old friend, Sir Garrett Moore, at Mellifont, on

[064-163]parting from whose family he tenderly bade farewell to the children and

[064-164]even the servants, and was observed to shed tears. At Dungannon he

[064-165]remained two days, and on the shore of Lough Swilly he joined O'Donnell

[064-166]and others of his connexions. The French ship, in which Maguire had

[064-167]returned, awaited them off Rathmullen, and there they took shipping for

[064-168]France. With O'Neil, in that sorrowful company, were his last countess,

[064-169]Catherine, daughter of Magenniss, his three sons, Hugh, John, and

[064-170]Brian; his nephew, Art, son of Cormac, Rory O'Donnell, Caffar, his

[064-171]brother, Nuala, his sister, who had forsaken her husband Nial _Garve_,

[064-172]when he forsook his country; the lady Rose O'Doherty, wife of Caffar,

[064-173]and afterwards of Owen Roe O'Neil; Maguire, Owen MacWard, chief bard of

[064-174]Tyrconnell, and several others. "Woe to the heart that meditated, woe

[064-175]to the mind that conceived, woe to the council that decided on the

[064-176]project of that voyage!" exclaimed the Annalists of Donegal, in the

[064-177]next age. Evidently it was the judgment of their immediate successors

[064-178]that the flight of the Earls was a rash and irremediable step for them;

[064-179]but the information on which they acted, if not long since destroyed,

[064-180]has, as yet, never been made public. We can pronounce no judgment as to

[064-181]the wisdom of their conduct, from the incomplete statements at present

[064-182]in our possession.

[064-184]There remained now few barriers to the wholesale confiscation of

[064-185]Ulster, so long sought by "the Undertakers," and these were rapidly

[064-186]removed. Sir Cahir O'Doherty, chief of Innishowen, although he had

[064-187]earned his Knighthood while a mere lad, fighting by the side of Dowcra,

[064-188]in an altercation with Sir George Paulett, Governor of Derry, was

[064-189]taunted with conniving at the escape of the Earls, and Paulett in his

[064-190]passion struck him in the face. The youthful chief—he was scarcely one

[064-191]and twenty—was driven almost to madness by this outrage. On the night

[064-192]of the 3rd of May, by a successful stratagem, he got possession of

[064-193]Culmore fort, at the month of Lough Foyle, and before morning dawned

[064-194]had surprised Derry; Paulett, his insulter, he slew with his own hand,

[064-195]most of the garrison were slaughtered, and the town reduced to ashes.

[064-196]Nial _Garve_ O'Donnell, who had been cast off by his old protectors,

[064-197]was charged with sending him supplies and men, and for three months he

[064-198]kept the field, hoping that every gale might bring him assistance from

[064-199]abroad. But those same summer months and foreign climes had already

[064-200]proved fatal to many of the exiles, whose co-operation he invoked. In

[064-201]July, Rory O'Donnell expired at Rome, in August, Maguire died at Genoa,

[064-202]on his way to Spain, and in September, Caffar O'Donnell was laid in the

[064-203]same grave with his brother, on St. Peter's hill. O'Neil survived his

[064-204]comrades, as he had done his fortunes, and like another Belisarius,

[064-205]blind and old, and a pensioner on the bounty of strangers, he lived on,

[064-206]eight weary years, in Rome. O'Doherty, enclosed in his native

[064-207]peninsula, between the forces of the Marshal Wingfield and Sir Oliver

[064-208]Lambert, Governor of Connaught, fell by a chance shot, at the rock of

[064-209]Doon, in Kilmacrenan. The superfluous traitor, Nial Garve, was, with

[064-210]his sons, sent to London, and imprisoned in the Tower for life. In

[064-211]those dungeons, Cormac, brother of Hugh O'Neil, and O'Cane also

[064-212]languished out their days, victims to the careless or vindictive temper

[064-213]of King James. Sir Arthur Chichester received, soon after these events,

[064-214]a grant of the entire barony of Innishowen, and subsequently a grant of

[064-215]the borough of Dungannon, with 1,300 acres adjoining; Wingfield

[064-216]obtained the district of Fercullan near Dublin, with the title of

[064-217]Viscount Powerscourt; Lambert was soon after made Earl of Cavan, and

[064-218]enriched with the lands of Carig, and other estates in that county.

[064-219]To justify at once the measures he proposed, as well as to divert from

[064-220]the exiles the sympathies of Europe, King James issued a proclamation

[064-221]bearing date the 5th of November, 1608, giving to the world the English

[064-222]version of the flight of the Earls. The whole of Ulster was then

[064-223]surveyed in a cursory manner by a staff over which presided Sir William

[064-224]Parsons as Surveyor-General. The surveys being completed early in 1609,

[064-225]a royal commission was issued to Chichester, Lambert, St. John,

[064-226]Ridgeway, Moore, Davis, and Parsons, with the Archbishop of Armagh, and

[064-227]the Bishop of Derry, to inquire into the portions forfeited. Before

[064-228]these Commissioners Juries were sworn on each particular case, and

[064-229]these Juries duly found that, in consequence of "the rebellion" of

[064-230]O'Neil, O'Donnell, and O'Doherty, the entire six counties of Ulster,

[064-231]enumerated by baronies and parishes, were forfeited to the Crown. By

[064-232]direction from England the Irish Privy Council submitted a scheme for

[064-233]planting these counties "with colonies of civil men well affected in

[064-234]religion," which scheme, with several modifications suggested by the

[064-235]English Privy Council, was finally promulgated by the royal legislator

[064-236]under the title of "Orders and Conditions for the Planters." According

[064-237]to the division thus ordered, upwards of 43,000 acres were claimed and

[064-238]conceded to the Primate and the Protestant Bishops of Ulster; in

[064-239]Tyrone, Derry, and Armagh, Trinity College got 30,000 acres, with six

[064-240]advowsons in each county. The various trading guilds of the city of

[064-241]London—such as the drapers, vintners, cordwainers, drysalters—obtained

[064-242]in the gross 209,800 acres, including the city of Derry, which they

[064-243]rebuilt and fortified, adding _London_ to its ancient name. The grants

[064-244]to individuals were divided into three classes—2,000, 1,500, and 1,000

[064-245]acres each. Among the conditions on which these grants were given was

[064-246]this—"that they should not suffer any labourer, that would not take the

[064-247]oath of supremacy," to dwell upon their lands. But this despotic

[064-248]condition—equivalent to sentence of death on tens of thousands of the

[064-249]native peasantry—was fortunately found impracticable in the execution.

[064-250]Land was little worth without hands to till it; labourers enough could

[064-251]not be obtained from England and Scotland, and the Hamiltons, Stewarts,

[064-252]Folliots, Chichesters, and Lamberts, having, from sheer necessity, to

[064-253]choose between Irish cultivators and letting their new estates lie

[064-254]waste and unprofitable, it is needless to say what choice they made.

[064-255]The spirit of religious persecution was exhibited not only in the means

[064-256]taken to exterminate the peasantry, to destroy the northern chiefs, and

[064-257]to intimidate the Catholics of "the Pale" by abuse of law, but by many

[064-258]cruel executions. The Prior of the famous retreat of Lough Derg was one

[064-259]of the victims of this persecution; a Priest named O'Loughrane, who had

[064-260]accidentally sailed in the same ship with the Earls to France, was

[064-261]taken prisoner on his return, hanged and quartered. Conor O'Devany,

[064-262]Bishop of Down and Conor, an octogenarian, suffered martyrdom with

[064-263]heroic constancy at Dublin, in 1611. Two years before, John, Lord Burke

[064-264]of Brittas, was executed in like manner on a charge of having

[064-265]participated in the Catholic demonstrations which took place at

[064-266]Limerick on the accession of King James. The edict of 1610 in relation

[064-267]to Catholic children educated abroad has been quoted in a previous

[064-268]chapter, _apropos_ of education, but the scheme submitted by Knox,

[064-269]Bishop of Raphoe, to Chichester in 1611 went even beyond that edict. In

[064-270]this project it was proposed that whoever should be found to harbour a

[064-271]Priest should forfeit all his possessions to the Crown—that quarterly

[064-272]returns should be made out by counties of all who refused to take the

[064-273]oath of supremacy, or to attend the English Church service—that no

[064-274]Papist should be permitted to exercise the function of a schoolmaster;

[064-275]and, moreover, that all churches injured during the late war should be

[064-276]repaired at the expense of the Papist inhabitants for the use of the

[064-277]Anglican congregation.

[064-278]Very unexpectedly to the nation at large, after a lapse of 27 years,

[064-279]during which no Parliament had been held, writs were issued for the

[064-280]attendance of both Houses, at Dublin, on the 18th of May, 1613. The

[064-281]work of confiscation and plantation had gone on for several years

[064-282]without the sanction of the legislature, and men were at a loss to

[064-283]conceive for what purpose elections were now ordered, unless to invent

[064-284]new penal laws, or to impose fresh burdens on the country. With all the

[064-285]efforts which had been made to introduce civil men, well affected in

[064-286]religion, it was certain that the Catholics would return a large

[064-287]majority of the House of Commons, not only in the chief towns, but from

[064-288]the fifteen old, and seventeen new counties, lately created. To

[064-289]counterbalance this majority, over forty boroughs, returning two

[064-290]members each, were created, by royal charter, in places thinly or not

[064-291]at all inhabited, or where towns were merely projected on the estates

[064-292]of leading "Undertakers." Against the issue of writs returnable by

[064-293]these fictitious corporations, the Lords Gormanstown, Slane, Killeen,

[064-294]Trimbleston, Dunsany, and Howth, signed an humble remonstrance to the

[064-295]King, concluding with a prayer for the relaxation of the penal laws

[064-296]affecting religion. The King, whose notions of prerogative were

[064-297]extravagantly high, was highly incensed at this petition of the

[064-298]Catholic peers of Leinster, and Chichester proceeded with his full

[064-299]approbation to pack the Parliament. At the elections, however, many

[064-300]"recusant lawyers" and other Catholic candidates were returned, so that

[064-301]when the day of meeting arrived, 101 Catholic representatives assembled

[064-302]at Dublin, some accompanied by bands of from 100 to 200 armed

[064-303]followers. The supporters of the government claimed 125 votes, and six

[064-304]were found to be absent, making the whole number of the House of

[064-305]Commons 232. The Upper House consisted of 50 Peers, of whom there were

[064-306]25 Protestant Bishops, so that the Deputy was certain of a majority in

[064-307]that chamber, on all points of ecclesiastical legislation, at least.

[064-308]Although, with the facts before us, we cannot agree with Sir John Davis

[064-309]that King James I. gave Ireland her "first free Parliament," it is

[064-310]impossible not to entertain a high sense of admiration for the

[064-311]constitutional firmness of the recusant or Catholic party in that

[064-312]assembly. At the very outset they successfully resisted the proposition

[064-313]to meet in the Castle, surrounded by the Deputy's guards, as a silent

[064-314]menace. They next contended that before proceeding to the election of

[064-315]Speaker the Council should submit to the Judges the decision of the

[064-316]alleged invalid elections. A tumultous and protracted debate was had on

[064-317]this point. The Castle party argued that they should first elect a

[064-318]Speaker and then proceed to try the elections; the Catholics contended

[064-319]that there were persons present whose votes would determine the

[064-320]Speakership, but who had no more title in law than the horseboys at the

[064-321]door. This was the preliminary trial of strength. The candidate of the

[064-322]Castle for the Speakership was Sir John Davis; of the Catholics, Sir

[064-323]John Everard, who had resigned his seat on the bench rather than take

[064-324]the oath of supremacy framed by Archbishop Abbott. The Castle party

[064-325]having gone into the lobby to be counted, the Catholics placed Sir John

[064-326]Everard in the Chair. On their return the government supporters placed

[064-327]Sir John Davis in Everard's lap, and a scene of violent disorder

[064-328]ensued. The House broke up in confusion; the recusants in a body

[064-329]declared their intention not to be present at its deliberations, and

[064-330]the Lord Deputy, finding them resolute, suddenly prorogued the session.

[064-331]Both parties sent deputies to England to lay their complaints at the

[064-332]foot of the throne. The Catholic spokesmen, Talbot and Lutrell, were

[064-333]received with a storm of reproaches, and committed, the former to the

[064-334]Tower, the other to the Fleet Prison. They were, however, released

[064-335]after a brief confinement, and a Commission was issued to inquire into

[064-336]the alleged electoral frauds. By the advice of Everard and others of

[064-337]their leaders, a compromise was effected with the Castle party; members

[064-338]returned for boroughs incorporated after the writs were issued were

[064-339]declared excluded, the contestation of seats on other grounds of

[064-340]irregularity were withdrawn, and the House accordingly proceeded to the

[064-341]business for which they were called together. The chief acts of the

[064-342]sessions of 1614, '15, and '16, beside the grant of four entire

[064-343]subsidies to the Crown, were an act joyfully recognizing the King's

[064-344]title; acts repealing statutes of Elizabeth and Henry VIII., as to

[064-345]distinctions of race; an act repealing the 3 and 4 of Philip and Mary,

[064-346]against "bringing Scots into Ireland," and the acts of attainder

[064-347]against O'Neil, O'Donnell, and O'Doherty. The recusant minority have

[064-348]been heavily censured by our recent historians for consenting to these

[064-349]attainders. Though the censure may be in part deserved, it is,

[064-350]nevertheless, clear that they had not the power to prevent their

[064-351]passage, even if they had been unanimous in their opposition; but they

[064-352]had influence enough, fortunately, to oblige the government to withdraw

[064-353]a sweeping penal law which it was intended to propose. An Act of

[064-354]oblivion and amnesty was also passed, which was of some advantage. On

[064-355]the whole, both for the constitutional principles which they upheld,

[064-356]and the religious proscription which they resisted, the recusant

[064-357]minority in the Irish Parliament of James I. deserve to be held in

[064-358]honour by all who value religious and civil liberty.
