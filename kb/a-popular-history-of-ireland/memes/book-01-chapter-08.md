CHAPTER VIII.

[008-001]WHAT THE IRISH SCHOOLS AND SAINTS DID IN THE THREE FIRST CHRISTIAN

[008-002]CENTURIES.

[008-003]We have now arrived at the close of the third century, from the death

[008-004]of Saint Patrick, and find ourselves on the eve of a protracted

[008-005]struggle with the heathen warriors of Scandinavia; it is time,

[008-006]therefore, to look back on the interval we have passed, and see what

[008-007]changes have been wrought in the land, since its kings, instead of

[008-008]waiting to be attacked at home, had made the surrounding sea "foam with

[008-009]the oars" of their outgoing expeditions.

[008-010]The most obvious change in the condition of the country is traceable in

[008-011]its constitution and laws, into every part of which, as was its wont

[008-012]from the beginning, the spirit of Christianity sought patiently to

[008-013]infuse itself. We have already spoken of the expurgation of the

[008-014]constitution, which prohibited the observance of Pagan rites to the

[008-015]kings, and imposed on them instead, certain social obligations. This

[008-016]was a first change suggested by Saint Patrick, and executed mainly by

[008-017]his disciple, Saint Benignus. We have seen the legislative success

[008-018]which attended the measures of Columbkill, Moling, and Adamnan; in

[008-019]other reforms of minor importance the paramount influence of the

[008-020]clerical order may be easily traced.

[008-021]But it is in their relation as teachers of human and divine science

[008-022]that the Irish Saints exercised their greatest power, not only over

[008-023]their own countrymen, but over a considerable part of Europe. The

[008-024]intellectual leadership of western Europe—the glorious ambition of the

[008-025]greatest nations—has been in turn obtained by Italy, France, Britain

[008-026]and Germany. From the middle of the sixth to the middle of the eighth

[008-027]century, it will hardly be disputed that that leadership devolved on

[008-028]Ireland. All the circumstances of the sixth century helped to confer it

[008-029]upon the newly converted western isle; the number of her schools, and

[008-030]the wisdom, energy, and zeal of her masters, retained for her the proud

[008-031]distinction for two hundred years. And when it passed away from her

[008-032]grasp, she might still console herself with the grateful reflection

[008-033]that the power she had founded and exercised, was divided among British

[008-034]and continental schools, which her own _alumni_ had largely contributed

[008-035]to form and establish. In the northern Province, the schools most

[008-036]frequented were those of Armagh, and of Bangor, on Belfast lough; in

[008-037]Meath, the school of Clonard, and that of Clomnacnoise, (near Athlone);

[008-038]in Leinster, the school of Taghmon (_Ta-mun_), and Beg-Erin, the former

[008-039]near the banks of the Slaney, the latter in Wexford harbour; in

[008-040]Munster, the school of Lismore on the Blackwater, and of Mungret (now

[008-041]Limerick), on the Shannon; in Connaught, the school of "Mayo of the

[008-042]Saxons," and the schools of the Isles of Arran. These seats of learning

[008-043]were almost all erected on the banks of rivers, in situations easy of

[008-044]access, to the native or foreign student; a circumstance which proved

[008-045]most disastrous to them when the sea kings of the north began to find

[008-046]their way to the shores of the island. They derived their

[008-047]maintenance—not from taxing their pupils—but in the first instance from

[008-048]public endowments. They were essentially free schools; not only free as

[008-049]to the lessons given, but the venerable Bede tells us they supplied

[008-050]free bed and board and books to those who resorted to them from abroad.

[008-051]The Prince and the Clansmen of every principality in which a school was

[008-052]situated, endowed it with a certain share—often an ample one—of the

[008-053]common land of the clan. Exclusive rights of fishery, and exclusive

[008-054]mill-privileges seem also to have been granted. As to timber for

[008-055]building purposes and for fuel, it was to be had for carrying and

[008-056]cutting. The right of quarry went with the soil, wherever building

[008-057]stone was found. In addition to these means of sustenance, a portion of

[008-058]the collegiate clergy appeared to have discharged missionary duty, and

[008-059]received offerings of the produce of the land. We hear of periodical

[008-060]_quests_ or collections made for the sustenance of these institutions,

[008-061]wherein the learned Lectors and Doctors, no doubt, pleaded their claims

[008-062]to popular favour, with irresistible eloquence. Individuals, anxious to

[008-063]promote the spread of religion and of science, endowed particular

[008-064]institutions out of their personal means; Princes, Bishops, and pious

[008-065]ladies, contributed to enlarge the bounds and increase the income of

[008-066]their favourite foundations, until a generous emulation seems to have

[008-067]seized on all the great families as well as on the different Provinces,

[008-068]as to which could boast the most largely attended schools, and the

[008-069]greatest number of distinguished scholars. The love of the _alma

[008-070]mater_—that college patriotism which is so sure a sign of the

[008-071]noble-minded scholar—never received more striking illustration than

[008-072]among the graduates of those schools. Columbkill, in his new home among

[008-073]the Hebrides, invokes blessings on blessings, on "the angels" with whom

[008-074]it was once his happiness to walk in Arran, and Columbanus, beyond the

[008-075]Alps, remembers with pride the school of Bangor—the very name of which

[008-076]inspires him with poetic rapture.

[008-077]The buildings, in which so many scholars were housed and taught, must

[008-078]have been extensive. Some of the schools we have mentioned were, when

[008-079]most flourishing, frequented by one, two, three, and even, at some

[008-080]periods, as many as seven thousand scholars. Such a population was

[008-081]alone sufficient to form a large village; and if we add the requisite

[008-082]number of teachers and attendants, we will have an addition of at least

[008-083]one-third to the total. The buildings seem to have been separately of

[008-084]no great size, but were formed into streets, and even into something

[008-085]like wards. Armagh was divided into three parts—_trian-more_ (or the

[008-086]town proper), _trian-Patrick_, the Cathedral close, and

[008-087]_trian-Sassenagh_, the Latin quarter, the home of the foreign students.

[008-088]A tall sculptured Cross, dedicated to some favourite saint, stood at

[008-089]the bounds of these several wards, reminding the anxious student to

[008-090]invoke their spiritual intercession as he passed by. Early hours and

[008-091]vigilant night watches had to be exercised to prevent conflagrations in

[008-092]such village-seminaries, built almost wholly of wood, and roofed with

[008-093]reeds or shingles. A Cathedral, or an Abbey Church, a round tower, or a

[008-094]cell of some of the ascetic masters, would probably be the only stone

[008-095]structure within the limits. To the students, the evening star gave the

[008-096]signal for retirement, and the morning sun for awaking. When, at the

[008-097]sound of the early bell, two or three thousand of them poured into the

[008-098]silent streets and made their way towards the lighted Church, to join

[008-099]in the service of matins, mingling, as they went or returned, the

[008-100]tongues of the Gael, the Cimbri, the Pict, the Saxon, and the Frank, or

[008-101]hailing and answering each other in the universal language of the Roman

[008-102]Church, the angels in Heaven must have loved to contemplate the union

[008-103]of so much perseverance with so much piety.

[008-104]The lives of the masters, not less than their lessons, were studied and

[008-105]observed by their pupils. At that time, as we gather from every

[008-106]authority, they were models of simplicity. One Bishop is found,

[008-107]erecting with his own hands, the _cashel_ or stone enclosure which

[008-108]surrounded his cell; another is labouring in the field, and gives his

[008-109]blessing to his visitors, standing between the stilts of the plough.

[008-110]Most ecclesiastics work occasionally either in wood, in bronze, in

[008-111]leather, or as scribes. The decorations of the Church, if not the

[008-112]entire structure, was the work of those who served at the altar. The

[008-113]tabernacle, the rood-screen, the ornamental font; the vellum on which

[008-114]the Psalms and Gospels were written; the ornamented case which

[008-115]contained the precious volume, were often of their making. The music

[008-116]which made the vale of Bangor resound as if inhabited by angels, was

[008-117]their composition; the hymns that accompanied it were their own. "It is

[008-118]a poor Church that has no music," is one of the oldest Irish proverbs;

[008-119]and the _Antiphonarium_ of Bangor, as well as that of Armagh, remains

[008-120]to show that such a want was not left unsupplied in the early Church.

[008-121]All the contemporary schools were not of the same grade nor of equal

[008-122]reputation. We constantly find a scholar, after passing years in one

[008-123]place, transferring himself to another, and sometimes to a third and a

[008-124]fourth. Some masters were, perhaps, more distinguished in human

[008-125]Science; others in Divinity. Columbkill studied in two or three

[008-126]different schools, and _visited_ others, perhaps as disputant or

[008-127]lecturer—a common custom in later years. Nor should we associate the

[008-128]idea of under-age with the students of whom we speak. Many of them,

[008-129]whether as teachers or learners, or combining both characters together,

[008-130]reached middle life before they ventured as instructors upon the world.

[008-131]Forty years is no uncommon age for the graduate of those days, when as

[008-132]yet the discovery was unmade, that all-sufficient wisdom comes with the

[008-133]first trace of down upon the chin of youth.

[008-134]The range of studies seems to have included the greater part of the

[008-135]collegiate course of our own times. The language of the country, and

[008-136]the language of the Roman Church; the languages of Scripture—Greek and

[008-137]Hebrew; the logic of Aristotle, the writings of the Fathers, especially

[008-138]of Pope Gregory the Great—who appears to have been a favourite author

[008-139]with the Irish Church; the defective Physics of the period;

[008-140]Mathematics, Music, and Poetical composition went to complete the

[008-141]largest course. When we remember that all the books were manuscripts;

[008-142]that even paper had not yet been invented; that the best parchment was

[008-143]equal to so much beaten gold, and a perfect MS. was worth a king's

[008-144]ransom, we may better estimate the difficulties in the way of the

[008-145]scholar of the seventh century. Knowing these facts, we can very well

[008-146]credit that part of the story of St. Columbkill's banishment into

[008-147]Argyle, which turns on what might be called a copyright dispute, in

[008-148]which the monarch took the side of St. Finian of Clonard, (whose

[008-149]original MSS. his pupil seems to have copied without permission,) and

[008-150]the Clan-Conal stood up, of course, for their kinsman. This dispute is

[008-151]even said to have led to the affair of Culdrum, in Sligo, which is

[008-152]sometimes mentioned as "the battle of the book." The same tendency of

[008-153]the national character which overstocked the Bardic Order, becomes

[008-154]again visible in its Christian schools; and if we could form anything

[008-155]like an approximate census of the population, anterior to the northern

[008-156]invasions, we would find that the proportion of ecclesiastics was

[008-157]greater than has existed either before or since in any Christian

[008-158]country. The vast designs of missionary zeal drew off large bodies of

[008-159]those who had entered Holy Orders; still the numbers engaged as

[008-160]teachers in the great schools, as well as of those who passed their

[008-161]lives in solitude and contemplation, must have been out of all modern

[008-162]proportion to the lay inhabitants of the Island.

[008-164]The most eminent Irish Saints of the fifth century were St. Ibar, St.

[008-165]Benignus and St. Kieran, of Ossory; in the sixth, St. Bendan, of

[008-166]Clonfert; St. Brendan, of Birr; St. Maccartin, of Clogher; St. Finian,

[008-167]of Moville; St. Finbar, St. Cannice, St. Finian, of Clonard; and St.

[008-168]Jarlath, of Tuam; in the seventh century, St. Fursey, St. Laserian,

[008-169]Bishop of Leighlin; St. Kieran, Abbot of Clonmacnoise; St. Comgall,

[008-170]Abbot of Bangor; St. Carthage, Abbot of Lismore; St. Colman, Bishop of

[008-171]Dromore; St. Moling, Bishop of Ferns; St. Colman Ela, Abbot; St.

[008-172]Cummian, "the White;" St. Finian, Abbot; St. Gall, Apostle of

[008-173]Switzerland; St. Fridolin, "the Traveller;" St. Columbanus, Apostle of

[008-174]Burgundy and Lombardy; St. Killian, Apostle of Franconia; St.

[008-175]Columbkill, Apostle of the Picts; St. Cormac, called "the Navigator;"

[008-176]St. Cuthbert; and St. Aidan, Apostle of Northumbria. In the eighth

[008-177]century the most illustrious names are St. Cataldus, Bishop of

[008-178]Tarentum; St. Adamnan, Abbot of Iona; St. Rumold, Apostle of Brabant;

[008-179]Clement and Albinus, "the Wisdom-seekers;" and St. Feargal or

[008-180]Virgilius, Bishop of Saltzburgh. Of holy women in the same ages, we

[008-181]have some account of St. Samthan, in the eighth century; of St. Bees,

[008-182]St. Dympna and St. Syra, in the seventh century, and of St. Monina, St.

[008-183]Ita of Desies, and St. Bride, or Bridget, of Kildare, in the sixth. The

[008-184]number of conventual institutions for women established in those ages,

[008-185]is less easily ascertained than the number of monastic houses for men;

[008-186]but we may suppose them to have borne some proportion to each other,

[008-187]and to have even counted by hundreds. The veneration in which St.

[008-188]Bridget was held during her life, led many of her countrywomen to

[008-189]embrace the religious state, and no less than fourteen _Saints_, her

[008-190]namesakes, are recorded. It was the custom of those days to call all

[008-191]holy persons who died in the odour of sanctity, _Saints_, hence

[008-192]national or provincial tradition venerates very many names, which the

[008-193]reader may look for in vain, in the Roman calendar.

[008-194]The intellectual labours of the Irish schools, besides the task of

[008-195]teaching such immense numbers of men of all nations on their own soil,

[008-196]and the missionary conquests to which I have barely alluded, were

[008-197]diversified by controversies, partly scientific and partly

[008-198]theological—such as the "Easter Controversy," the "Tonsure

[008-199]Controversy," and that maintained by "Feargal the Geometer," as to the

[008-200]existence of the Antipodes.

[008-201]The discussion, as to the proper time of observing Easter, which had

[008-202]occupied the doctors of the Council of Nice in the fourth century, was

[008-203]raised in Ireland and in Britain early in the sixth, and complete

[008-204]uniformity was not established till far on in the eighth. It occupied

[008-205]the thoughts of several generations of the chief men of the Irish

[008-206]Church, and some of their arguments still fortunately survive, to

[008-207]attest their learning and tolerance, as well as their zeal. St. Patrick

[008-208]had introduced in the fifth century the computation of time then

[008-209]observed in Gaul, and to this custom many of the Irish doctors rigidly

[008-210]adhered, long after the rest of Christendom had agreed to adopt the

[008-211]Alexandrian computation. Great names were found on both sides of the

[008-212]controversy: Columbanus, Finian, and Aidan, for adhering exactly to the

[008-213]rule of St. Patrick; Cummian, the White, Laserian and Adamnan, in

[008-214]favour of strict agreement with Rome and the East. Monks of the same

[008-215]Monastery and Bishops of the same Province maintained opposite opinions

[008-216]with equal ardour and mutual charity. It was a question of discipline,

[008-217]not a matter of faith; but it involved a still greater question,

[008-218]whether national churches were to plead the inviolability of their

[008-219]local usages, even on points of discipline, against the sense and

[008-220]decision of the Universal Church.

[008-221]In the year of our Lord 630, the Synod of Leighlin was held, under the

[008-222]shelter of the ridge of Leinster, and the presidency of St. Laserian.

[008-223]Both parties at length agreed to send deputies to Rome, as "children to

[008-224]their mother," to learn her decision. Three years later, that decision

[008-225]was made known, and the midland and southern dioceses at once adopted

[008-226]it. The northern churches, however, still held out, under the lead of

[008-227]Armagh and the influence of Iona, nor was it till a century later that

[008-228]this scandal of celebrating Easter on two different days in the same

[008-229]church was entirely removed. In justification of the Roman rule, St.

[008-230]Cummian, about the middle of the seventh century, wrote his famous

[008-231]epistle to Segenius, Abbot of Iona, of the ability and learning of

[008-232]which all modern writers from Archbishop Usher to Thomas Moore, speak

[008-233]in terms of the highest praise. It is one of the few remaining

[008-234]documents of that controversy. A less vital question of discipline

[008-235]arose about the tonsure. The Irish shaved the head in a semicircle from

[008-236]temple to temple, while the Latin usage was to shave the crown, leaving

[008-237]an external circle of hair to typify the crown of thorns. At the

[008-238]conference of Whitby (A.D. 664) this was one of the subjects of

[008-239]discussion between the clergy of Iona, and those who followed the Roman

[008-240]method—but it never assumed the importance of the Easter controversy.

[008-241]In the following century an Irish Missionary, Virgilius, of Saltzburgh,

[008-242](called by his countrymen "Feargal, the Geometer,") was maintaining in

[008-243]Germany against no less an adversary than St. Boniface, the sphericity

[008-244]of the earth and the existence of antipodes. His opponents endeavoured

[008-245]to represent him, or really believed him to hold, that there were other

[008-246]men, on our earth, for whom the Redeemer had not died; on this ground

[008-247]they appealed to Pope Zachary against him; but so little effect had

[008-248]this gross distortion of his true doctrine at Rome, when explanations

[008-249]were given, that Feargal was soon afterwards raised to the See of

[008-250]Saltzburgh, and subsequently canonized by Pope Gregory IX. In the ninth

[008-251]century we find an Irish geographer and astronomer of something like

[008-252]European reputation in Dicuil and Dungal, whose treatises and epistles

[008-253]have been given to the press. Like their compatriot, Columbanus, these

[008-254]accomplished men had passed their youth and early manhood in their own

[008-255]country, and to its schools are to be transferred the compliments paid

[008-256]to their acquirements by such competent judges as Muratori, Latronne,

[008-257]and Alexander von Humboldt. The origin of the scholastic

[008-258]philosophy—which pervaded Europe for nearly ten centuries—has been

[008-259]traced by the learned Mosheim to the same insular source. Whatever may

[008-260]now be thought of the defects or shortcomings of that system, it

[008-261]certainly was not unfavourable either to wisdom or eloquence, since

[008-262]among its professors may be reckoned the names of St. Thomas and St.

[008-263]Bernard.

[008-264]We must turn away our eyes from the contemplation of those days in

[008-265]which were achieved for Ireland the title of the land of saints and

[008-266]doctors. Another era opens before us, and we can already discern the

[008-267]long ships of the north, their monstrous beaks turned towards the holy

[008-268]Isle, their sides hung with glittering shields and their benches

[008-269]thronged with fair-haired warriors, chanting as they advance the fierce

[008-270]war songs of their race. Instead of the monk's familiar voice on the

[008-271]river banks we are to hear the shouts of strange warriors from a

[008-272]far-off country; and for matin hymn and vesper song, we are to be beset

[008-273]through a long and stormy period, with sounds of strife and terror, and

[008-274]deadly conflict.
