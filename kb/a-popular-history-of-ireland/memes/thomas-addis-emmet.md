## Overview

Thomas Addis Emmet (1764–1827) was an Irish patriot, barrister, and a leading member of the Society of [[United Irishmen]].

The elder brother of [[Robert Emmet]], he was among the founding generation of the United Irish movement and later served as the organisation's plenipotentiary in Paris during the [[Napoleonic Wars]].

A close associate of [[Theobald Wolfe Tone]], Emmet represented the moderate constitutional strand of Irish republicanism before the [[Act of Union 1800]] transformed the political landscape. After the movement's collapse, he emigrated to New York where he built a distinguished legal career. (104-322)

## Biography

### The United Irishmen Generation

Thomas Addis Emmet was born in 1764, part of an extraordinary cohort of Irish patriots who came of age during the Volunteer movement and the era of [[Henry Grattan]]'s parliamentary triumphs. The United Irishmen, as McGee records, "were the birth of a new generation, entering together on the public stage." (100-014) The leading figures were "all born within a few years of each other: Neilson in 1761, Tone, Arthur O'Conor and Lord Edward Fitzgerald in '62, McNevin in '63, Sampson and Thomas Addis Emmet in '64, and Russell in '67." (100-017)

> The new age was revolutionary, and the new men were filled with the spirit of the age. Their actions stand apart; they form an episode in the history of the century to which there may be parallels, but a chapter in the history of their own country original and alone. (100-034)

Emmet trained as a barrister and quickly rose to prominence in Dublin's reform circles. By 1790, he had become one of the leading Protestant patriots allied with the [[Catholic Committee]] in the campaign for Catholic Emancipation. McGee places him among "the new generation of Protestant patriots—such men as the Hon. Simon Butler, Wolfe Tone, and Thomas Addis Emmet" who regarded Catholic exclusion "from the pale of the constitution as a public calamity." (098-019)

### Joining the United Irishmen

Emmet was not among the original founders in 1791, but joined at a pivotal moment. McGee records that "near its close" of the year following Tone's departure, "Thomas Addis Emmet, who had long been in the confidence of the promoters, joined, as did, about the same time, Arthur O'Conor... and Dr. William James McNevin." (100-135) These accessions brought legal expertise, political connections, and Catholic representation to the society's leadership.

Emmet's legal training and family standing — the Emmets were a respected Protestant family — lent the movement credibility among Dublin's professional classes. Unlike some more radical members, Emmet initially favoured constitutional methods, working alongside [[John Keogh]] and [[Edward Byrne]] of the Catholic Committee. (098-010)

### Imprisonment and the 1798 Rising

During the lead-up to the [[Irish Rebellion of 1798]], Emmet was arrested with other United Irish leaders in March 1798. The government had penetrated the organisation through informers, and the arrests decapitated the Leinster branch on the eve of the rising. From prison, Emmet and his colleagues negotiated with the government. On Samuel Neilson's motion in July 1798, the state prisoners agreed, "in order to stop the effusion of blood, to expatriate themselves to any country not at war with England." (104-303)

> But that evidence delivered before the Committees of Parliament by Emmet, McNevin, and O'Conor, did not altogether serve the purposes of government. The patriotic prisoners made it at once a protest against, and an exposition of, the despotic policy under which their country had been goaded into rebellion. (104-309)

For their firmness, Emmet and his colleagues were confined for three years at Fort George in the Scottish Highlands, where "a gallant old soldier, Colonel Stuart, endeavoured to soften the hard realities of a prison." (104-314) At the peace of Amiens in 1802, they were permitted the "melancholy privilege of expatriation." (104-318)

### Exile in Paris

Following his release, Emmet chose exile in France. In Paris, he became one of the principal Irish plenipotentiaries, negotiating with Napoleon for French military support. When his younger brother Robert Emmet returned to Dublin in October 1802 to re-establish the organisation, "Thomas Addis Emmet and Arthur O'Conor remained at Paris, the plenipotentiaries of their countrymen." (107-126)

> On the rupture with England Buonaparte took up the Irish negotiation with much earnestness; he even suggested to the exiles the colours and the motto under which they were to fight. The flag on a tricolour ground, was to have a green centre, bearing the letters R.I.—Republique Irlandaise. (107-130)

The failure of Robert Emmet's rising in July 1803 and his brother's execution that September were bitter blows. The Paris exiles' hopes had been invested in the renewal of hostilities between France and England, declared in May 1803 — "little more than twelve months after the proclamation of the peace of Amiens." (107-118) But the invasion never came for Ireland.

## Legacy

After the collapse of Napoleonic France, Emmet emigrated to the United States, becoming Attorney General of New York and arguing landmark constitutional cases. McGee records that "Emmet, McNevin, Sampson, and the family of Tone were reunited in New York, where the many changes and distractions of a great metropolitan community have not even yet obliterated the memories of their virtues, their talents, and their accomplishments." (104-323)

The United Irishmen as a group embodied what McGee called "heroic qualities" — "If ever a body of public men deserved the character of a brotherhood of heroes, so far as disinterestedness, courage, self-denial, truthfulness and glowing love of country constitute heroism, these men deserved that character." (104-330) Emmet lived those virtues to the end of his days.
