The **Reformation in Ireland** refers to the process by which the Tudor monarchy attempted to impose Protestant religious reforms on Ireland during the sixteenth century. Unlike in England, where the Reformation achieved broad (if contested) acceptance, in Ireland it largely failed to convert the native population, instead becoming a source of enduring religious and political division. (050-149)

## The Henrician Reformation

The Reformation in Ireland first "wore the guise of schism, as distinguished from heresy" (050-149) under [[Henry VIII]]. The initial phase involved the repudiation of papal supremacy and the establishment of the King as Supreme Head of the [[Church of Ireland]], mirroring the English Reformation. Religious doctrine remained largely Catholic in substance, and the monasteries were dissolved and their lands redistributed to loyal Anglo-Irish and English nobles.

An elaborate report on "the State of Ireland" with "a plan for its Reformation" was submitted to Henry in 1515, providing a clear view of the political and military condition of the several territories. (048-065) This report set the template for Tudor policy in Ireland for the remainder of the century.

## The Edwardian and Elizabethan Phases

Under [[Edward VI]] and [[Elizabeth I]], the Reformation in Ireland took on a more distinctly Protestant character. The [[Book of Common Prayer]] was introduced in English, a language not spoken by the majority of the Irish population, which severely limited its appeal. The established Church was furnished with Protestant bishops and clergy, but the majority of the population remained Catholic in practice and allegiance.

The first agent specifically employed to introduce the Reformation into Ireland displayed unwearied zeal, though his efforts had limited effect beyond the Pale. (050-169) The orders of monastic and military religious houses "were extinguished wherever the arm of the Reformation could reach." (050-240)

## Resistance and Failure

The Reformation's failure in Ireland had multiple causes. The use of English in liturgy and scripture — incomprehensible to the Irish-speaking majority — was a critical barrier. The identification of Protestantism with English conquest and the expropriation of Catholic lands made the new religion appear as the creed of the oppressor. The Catholic Counter-Reformation, spearheaded by continental-trained Irish priests, provided effective doctrinal and pastoral resistance.

The "tempest of the Reformation" (047-150) involved both orders of the old religious establishment in a common danger, and demonstrated their equal heroism in resistance. By the end of Elizabeth's reign, Irish Catholicism had not only survived but had been strengthened by the experience of persecution.

## Legacy

The failure of the Reformation in Ireland created the enduring sectarian divide that has shaped Irish history. The identification of Catholicism with Irish national identity and Protestantism with English rule became a central dynamic of Irish politics from the seventeenth century through the twentieth century.

The confiscation of monastic lands and the creation of a Protestant landowning class laid the economic foundation for the later [[Protestant Ascendancy]] and the [[Penal Laws]]. The religious conflict set in motion by the Reformation would manifest in the [[Rebellion of 1641]], the [[Cromwellian Settlement]], the [[Williamite War]], and the sectarian divisions that persist to the present day.
