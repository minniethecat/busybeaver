## Overview

Henry Carey, 1st Viscount Falkland (c. 1575–1633), served as Lord Deputy of Ireland from 1622 to 1629. His administration spanned the latter years of James I and the opening years of Charles I. It was marked by a contradictory policy of religious toleration toward Catholics on the one hand and political manipulation on the other. (065-064) (065-218)

Falkland's seven-year tenure was one of the longest of any early Stuart governor in Ireland. His policies reflected the shifting diplomatic priorities of the Stuart court, particularly the proposed Spanish Match between Prince Charles and the Infanta of Spain. (065-064)

## Appointment and Early Administration

Falkland succeeded Lord Grandison as Lord Deputy in 1622. At that time, James I was pursuing a diplomatic marriage between Prince Charles and the Infanta of Spain. This Spanish Match required a conciliatory policy toward Catholics both in England and in Ireland. (065-064)

The toleration extended by Falkland was "an effect of the diplomacy then going on." When Pope Gregory XV granted a dispensation for the marriage in 1623, James solemnly swore to a private article pledging toleration for his Catholic subjects. Falkland's administration was characterized by abrupt swings in policy: "violent attempts at coercion alternated with periods of indulgence." (065-064)

> The comparative toleration, extended by Lord Falkland, was an effect of the diplomacy then going on, for the marriage of Prince Charles to the Infanta of Spain. [065-064]

## The Graces and the General Assembly

One of the most significant episodes of Falkland's administration involved the "Graces," proposed concessions to Irish Catholics in exchange for financial contributions. Faced with the murmurs of the inhabitants, Falkland assured the Catholic leaders that the King was their friend. Any just representation of their grievances, he promised, would secure the King's attention. (065-142)

So encouraged, the leading Catholics convoked a General Assembly to formulate their demands. The King promised that a Parliament would be immediately called in Ireland to confirm these concessions. However, "at the same time he secretly instructed Lord Falkland to see that the writs of election were informally prepared, so that no Parliament could be held." (065-196)

> At the same time he secretly instructed Lord Falkland to see that the writs of election were informally prepared, so that no Parliament could be held. [065-196]

The deception was executed as planned. The agents of the General Assembly paid their first instalment and the subscribers held the King's autograph promising redress, but no Parliament ever met. (065-196)

## Departure and Later Years

In 1629 Falkland sought leave to withdraw. "Lord Falkland, to disarm the Puritan outcry against him, had leave to withdraw." His departure was prompted in part by the shifting religious politics at court. The failure of the Spanish Match and the rise of Puritan influence made a Catholic-tolerating Lord Deputy politically untenable. (065-218)

For four years, "an unusually long interregnum," the government of Ireland was left in the hands of Lords Justices: Robert Boyle, now Earl of Cork, and Adam Loftus, Viscount Ely. (065-218)

> In 1629 Lord Falkland, to disarm the Puritan outcry against him, had leave to withdraw, and for four years — an unusually long interregnum — the government was left in the hands of Robert Boyle, now Earl of Cork, and Adam Loftus, Viscount Ely. [065-218]

## Legacy

Lord Falkland's administration illustrates the contradictions of early Stuart policy in Ireland. His government extended toleration to Catholics not from principle but from diplomatic expediency. When the diplomatic winds shifted, the toleration was withdrawn. (065-064)

The episode of the Graces, in which Falkland was a willing instrument of royal deception, poisoned relations between the Catholic community and the Crown. It contributed to the grievances that would erupt in the Rebellion of 1641. Falkland's departure in 1629 and the subsequent interregnum marked the end of any pretense of even-handed Stuart governance in Ireland. (065-196) (065-218)
