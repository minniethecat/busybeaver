## Overview

Tiernan O'Ruarc was the lord of Breffni and an angle of Meath in the 12th century. His long-running feud with [[Dermid McMurrogh]], King of Leinster, was one of the precipitating factors in the [[Norman Invasion of Ireland]], as McMurrogh's flight to seek foreign aid was partly driven by O'Ruarc's military pressure.

## Biography

### Lord of Breffni and Meath

Tiernan O'Ruarc held the lordship of Breffni and received part of Meath as his territory:

> [019-046] Tiernan O'Ruarc, lord of Breffni, and an angle of Meath, including Athboy and the hill of Ward, was given him instead. (019-046–019-047)

For this Methian addition to his possessions, Tiernan was a significant power in the midland region (027-014–027-015). His territory spanned the modern counties of Leitrim, Cavan, and parts of Meath.

### The Feud with Dermid McMurrogh

The conflict between Tiernan O'Ruarc and Dermid McMurrogh was one of the central political dramas of 12th-century Ireland. The rivalry was partly personal — Dermid had abducted O'Ruarc's wife Dervorgilla — and partly territorial, as both lords competed for influence in Meath.

When Dermid McMurrogh was driven from his kingdom, Tiernan O'Ruarc played a decisive role:

> [019-203] found that Tiernan O'Ruarc had entered the province, at the head of an auxiliary army, and Dermid, thus surrounded, deserted by most of his followers, fled. (019-203–019-204)

This pressure forced McMurrogh to flee Ireland and seek assistance from [[Henry II]] of England, setting in motion the chain of events that led to the Norman invasion of 1169–1171.

### Later Events

Tiernan O'Ruarc was also known for violence against the Church. At one point, a similar outrage was offered by Tiernan O'Ruarc to the Archbishop of Armagh, and one of the Archbishop's ecclesiastics was killed in the incident (020-113–020-114).

## Legacy

Tiernan O'Ruarc occupies a pivotal, if often overlooked, position in Irish history. His pursuit of Dermid McMurrogh contributed directly to the circumstances that brought the Anglo-Normans to Ireland.

While McMurrogh is typically blamed for inviting foreign intervention, O'Ruarc's role as the aggressor who forced McMurrogh into exile is equally consequential to the chain of causation that led to the Norman invasion.
