## Overview

Samuel Neilson (1761–1803) was an Irish patriot, newspaper proprietor, and one of the original founders of the Society of [[United Irishmen]] at Belfast.

As proprietor of the _Northern Star_, he controlled the most influential reform newspaper in late eighteenth-century Ireland. Neilson was among the inner circle of Ulster radicals who, alongside [[Theobald Wolfe Tone]], transformed Belfast into the intellectual capital of Irish republicanism. His motion from prison in July 1798 to negotiate an end to the bloodshed shaped the fate of the United Irish leadership. (104-302)

## Biography

### Founding the United Irishmen

Samuel Neilson was born in 1761 and came of age during the Volunteer movement of the 1780s. A successful Belfast businessman, he became proprietor of the _Northern Star_ newspaper, which under his direction became the principal organ of advanced reform opinion in the north of Ireland.

In October 1791, Neilson played a pivotal role in founding the United Irishmen. As McGee recounts, Tone, "on a visit to his friend Thomas Russell, in the northern capital, was introduced to Samuel Neilson, proprietor of the _Northern Star_ newspaper, and several other kindred spirits, all staunch reformers, or 'something more.'" (100-041)

> Twenty of these gentlemen meeting together, adopted a programme prepared by Tone, which contained these three simple propositions: that "English influence" was the great danger of Irish liberty; that a reform of Parliament could alone create a counterpoise to that influence; and that such a reform to be just should include Irishmen of all religious denominations. (100-043)

### The Northern Star

The _Northern Star_ became the voice of the United Irish movement, circulating throughout Ulster and beyond. Through its columns, Neilson propagated the society's principles of religious equality, parliamentary reform, and national self-determination.

Tone, before leaving for America, "renewed with his first associates, their vows and projects, on the summit of 'the Cave Hill,' which looks down upon the rich valley of the Laggan." (100-125) Before quitting Dublin, Tone "had solemnly promised Emmet and Russell, in the first instance, as he did his Belfast friends in the second, that he would make the United States his route to France, where he would negotiate a formidable national alliance, for 'the United Irishmen.'" (100-128)

### Imprisonment and the 1798 Rebellion

When the rising erupted in May 1798, Neilson was already in prison — a critical blow to the Ulster organisation. The two counties which rose _en masse_ were Down and Antrim, the "original chiefs of which, such as Thomas Russell and Samuel Neilson, were unfortunately in prison." (104-108) The next leader on whom the men of Antrim relied "resigned his command on the very eve of the appointed day." (104-110) The absence of experienced leaders like Neilson contributed to the swift suppression of the northern rising.

From his prison cell, Neilson made a crucial intervention. In July 1798, on his motion, the state prisoners agreed, "in order to stop the effusion of blood, to expatriate themselves to any country not at war with England, and to reveal the general secrets of their system, without inculpating individuals." (104-302) These terms were accepted by the government, who needed the prisoners' evidence to promote their scheme of legislative union. (104-306)

> But that evidence delivered before the Committees of Parliament by Emmet, McNevin, and O'Conor, did not altogether serve the purposes of government. The patriotic prisoners made it at once a protest against, and an exposition of, the despotic policy under which their country had been goaded into rebellion. (104-309)

For their firmness, the prisoners were confined for three years at Fort George in the Scottish Highlands, where "a gallant old soldier, Colonel Stuart, endeavoured to soften the hard realities of a prison." (104-314) At the peace of Amiens in 1802, they were permitted the "melancholy privilege of expatriation." (104-318)

## Legacy

Neilson was released in 1802 and emigrated to the United States, where he died the following year, broken in health by years of confinement. He represents the Ulster Presbyterian strand of Irish republicanism — men of the merchant and professional classes who, motivated by Enlightenment ideals, sought to build a non-sectarian Irish nation. His _Northern Star_ set a precedent for the radical press in Ireland, and his role in convening the first meeting of the United Irishmen at Belfast places him among the indispensable founders of modern Irish republicanism.
