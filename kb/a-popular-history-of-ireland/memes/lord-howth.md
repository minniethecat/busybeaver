## Overview

Lord Howth (Baron of Howth), of the St. Lawrence family, was an Anglo-Irish peer who played a controversial role in the downfall of the northern Earls — Hugh O'Neill, Earl of Tyrone, and Rory O'Donnell, Earl of Tyrconnell — in the early years of the seventeenth century. His complicity in the schemes of Secretary Cecil to entrap the Gaelic lords placed him at the centre of one of the most consequential episodes of Jacobean Ireland: the Flight of the Earls and the subsequent Plantation of Ulster.

## The Entrapment of the Northern Earls

Following the Treaty of Mellifont (1603) and the accession of James I, the position of the Ulster Earls became increasingly precarious. The new English administration, led by Lord Deputy Chichester, sought to undermine their authority, while courtiers in London eyed the vast territories of Tyrone and Tyrconnell. Lord Howth was drawn into this intrigue.

McGee records that a key document used to incriminate the Earls "was written by Lord Howth, who was thought to have been employed by Secretary Cecil, to entrap the northern Earls, in order to find a pretext for seizing the six escheated counties of Ulster" (064-153). This letter, purporting to reveal treasonable correspondence, provided the Crown with the justification it needed to move against O'Neill and O'Donnell.

> A meeting, at Lord Howth's suggestion, was held about this time at the Castle of Dublin, of the principal Catholic nobles and gentry — Lords Gormanstown, Slane, Killeen, Trimbleston, Dunsany, and Howth — to consider their common danger. (064-140)

The meeting reflected the dilemma of the Pale lords: caught between loyalty to the Crown and fear that the anti-Catholic laws would be extended to them as well. Their remonstrance prayed "for the relaxation of the penal laws" (064-294), but the petition was ignored, and the position of the Ulster Earls grew steadily worse until their flight from Rathmullan in September 1607.

## The Remonstrance of the Pale Lords

Lord Howth joined his fellow Catholic peers in the remonstrance to James I, protesting their loyalty while seeking relief from the growing burden of anti-Catholic legislation. The signatories included "Lords Gormanstown, Slane, Killeen, Trimbleston, Dunsany, and Howth" (064-294). This collective action by the Old English nobility represented one of the earliest organised attempts to secure religious toleration through constitutional means.

However, the remonstrance achieved little. The flight of the Earls in 1607, followed by O'Doherty's rebellion in 1608, hardened the government's determination to proceed with the Ulster Plantation. The Pale lords, who had hoped to distinguish their loyalty from that of the Gaelic chieftains, found themselves increasingly marginalised.

## Later Years and Imprisonment

The political manoeuvres that Lord Howth had engaged in ultimately brought danger upon himself and his family. McGee notes that he, like other leading figures of the period, ended his days in confinement: one account records that figures were "sent to London, and imprisoned in the Tower for life" (064-210), while another reference places him in "the Tower, the other to the Fleet Prison" (064-334). The exact date and circumstances of his death are uncertain.

## Historical Assessment

Lord Howth's career illustrates the precarious position of the Old English Catholic nobility in the early Stuart period. Caught between their traditional loyalty to the English Crown and their Catholic faith, men like Howth navigated a course that ultimately satisfied neither side. His role in Cecil's plot against the northern Earls earned him the distrust of the Gaelic Irish, while his Catholicism made him permanently suspect to the Protestant administration. The collapse of his political fortunes — from trusted agent of the Crown to prisoner in the Tower — mirrored the wider decline of the Old English interest in the decades before the Confederate Wars.
