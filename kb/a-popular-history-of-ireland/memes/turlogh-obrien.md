## Overview

Sir Turlogh O'Brien was an Irish nobleman of the O'Brien dynasty of Thomond who served as a military commander in the late 16th and early 17th centuries. The son of the Lord of Thomond, he fought at various times both with and against the Crown forces, reflecting the complex loyalties of the Gaelic aristocracy during the Tudor conquest and the [[Nine Years' War]].

## Early Career and the Nine Years' War

Turlogh O'Brien was the son of the Lord of Thomond, a descendant of the ancient O'Brien kings of Munster. His family, the O'Briens of Thomond, had accepted English law and titles under Henry VIII's policy of [[Surrender and Regrant]] in the 1540s, when the first Earl of Thomond was created.

During the Nine Years' War (1594–1603), Turlogh O'Brien served under the Earl of Clanrickarde. At the siege of Kinsale or in one of the campaigns of the period, he was recorded fighting alongside Clanrickarde's forces:

> [045-372] With Clanrickarde were Turlogh O'Brien, son of the Lord of Thomond, McNamara of Clare, O'Carroll of Ely, O'Brien of Burren. (045-372–045-373)

The alliance with Clanrickarde placed O'Brien in the camp of the Anglo-Irish lords who, while Catholic, remained loyal to the Crown against the Ulster earls [[Hugh ONeill]] and [[Hugh Roe ODonnell]].

## Later Military Service

Turlogh O'Brien continued in active military service through the early decades of the 17th century. He fought alongside Clanrickarde and the McNamaras in several engagements:

> [045-393] The old allies, Turlogh O'Brien, Clanrickarde, and the McNamaras, attacked them at the bridge of Portrush, near Castleconnell. (045-393)

In later years, the Crown came to rely on these experienced Irish commanders. When the English administration under Sir Richard Bingham sought to establish control over the western provinces, they appointed local notables as commissioners:

> [058-076] Clanrickarde; Sir Turlogh O'Brien, Sir Richard Bourke (the McWilliam), O'Conor Sligo, Sir Brian O'Ruarc, and Sir Murrogh O'Flaherty. (058-075–058-076)

By this point, O'Brien had received a knighthood and was recognised as one of the principal gentlemen of the province. He served alongside Sir Richard Bourke and other leading Connacht and Munster lords.

## The Catholic Confederacy Period

During the period of the [[Confederate War]] in the 1640s, Turlogh O'Brien appears as one of the representatives of the southern interest. He participated in the literary and political disputes of the Confederate era:

> [078-122] Himself, Turlogh O'Brien, and Art Oge O'Keefe on behalf of the Southerners; O'Clery, O'Donnell, the two McEgans, and Robert McArthur for the Northerners. (078-122)

This positioning on the side of the "Southerners" — the Old English and Munster Confederates — aligns with the O'Brien tradition of engagement with the Anglo-Irish political community.

## Historical Context

Turlogh O'Brien's career illustrates the complex position of the Gaelic nobility in the early modern period. The O'Briens of Thomond had accepted the English Crown's authority under Henry VIII and received English titles, yet they retained their Catholic faith and their connection to the Gaelic aristocratic tradition. During the Nine Years' War, they sided with the Crown against the Ulster earls; during the Confederate period, they defended Catholic interests within the framework of loyalty to the Stuart monarchy.

This balancing act was characteristic of the Old English and Gaelic noble families who sought to preserve their lands, religion, and status through the tumultuous century between the Tudor conquest and the Cromwellian settlement.
