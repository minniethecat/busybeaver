CHAPTER XI.

[074-001]CLOSE OF THE CONFEDERATE WAR.

[074-002]The tenth year of the contest of which we have endeavoured to follow

[074-003]the most important events, opened upon the remaining Catholic leaders,

[074-004]greatly reduced in numbers and resources, but firm and undismayed. Two

[074-005]chief seaports, and some of the western counties still remained to

[074-006]them; and accordingly we find meetings of the Bishops and other

[074-007]notables during this year (1650), at Limerick, at Loughrea, and finally

[074-008]at Jamestown, in the neighbourhood of Owen O'Neil's nursery of the

[074-009]first "Catholic Army."

[074-010]The Puritan commander was now Henry Ireton, son-in-law of Cromwell, by

[074-011]a marriage contracted about two years before. The completion of the

[074-012]Protector's policy could have devolved upon few persons more capable of

[074-013]understanding, or more fearless in executing it; and in two eventful

[074-014]campaigns he proved himself the able successor of the Protector. In

[074-015]August following Cromwell's departure, Waterford and Duncannon were

[074-016]taken by Ireton; and there only remained to the Confederates the

[074-017]fortresses of Sligo, Athlone, Limerick, and Galway, with the country

[074-018]included within the irregular quadrangle they describe. The younger

[074-019]Coote making a feint against Sligo, which Clanrickarde hastened to

[074-020]defend, turned suddenly on his steps, and surprised Athlone. Sligo,

[074-021]naturally a place of no great strength after the invention of

[074-022]artillery, soon after fell, so that Galway and Limerick alone were

[074-023]left, at the beginning of 1651, to bear all the brunt of Puritan

[074-024]hostility.

[074-025]Political events of great interest happened during the two short years

[074-026]of Ireton's command. The Assembly, which met at Jamestown in August,

[074-027]and again at Loughrea in November, 1650, made the retirement of Ormond

[074-028]from the Government a condition of all future efforts in the royal

[074-029]cause, and that nobleman, deeply wounded by this condition, had finally

[074-030]sailed from Galway, in December, leaving to Clanrickarde the title of

[074-031]Lord Deputy, and to Castlehaven the command of the forces which still

[074-032]kept the field. The news from Scotland of the young king's subscription

[074-033]to the covenant, and denunciation of all terms with Irish Papists, came

[074-034]to aid the councils of those, who, like the eloquent French, Bishop of

[074-035]Ferns, demanded a national policy, irrespective of the exigencies of

[074-036]the Stuart family. An embassy was accordingly despatched to Brussels,

[074-037]to offer the title of King-Protector to the Duke of Lorraine, or

[074-038]failing with him, to treat with any "other Catholic prince, state,

[074-039]republic, or person, as they might deem expedient for the preservation

[074-040]of the Catholic religion and nation." A wide latitude, dictated by

[074-041]desperate circumstances. The ambassadors were Bishop French and Hugh

[074-042]Rochfort; the embassy one of the most curious and instructive in our

[074-043]annals.

[074-044]The Duke expressed himself willing to undertake an expedition to

[074-045]Ireland—to supply arms and money to the Confederates—on the condition

[074-046]of receiving Athlone, Limerick, Athenry and Galway into his custody,

[074-047]with the title of Protector. A considerable sum of money (20,000

[074-048]pounds) was forwarded at once; four Belgian frigates laden with stores

[074-049]were made ready for sea; the Canon De Henin was sent as envoy to the

[074-050]Confederates, and this last venture looked most promising of success,

[074-051]had not Clanrickarde in Galway, and Charles and Ormond in Paris, taking

[074-052]alarm at the new dignity conferred upon the Duke, countermined the

[074-053]Bishop of Ferns and Mr. Rochfort, and defeated by intrigue and

[074-054]correspondence their hopeful enterprise.

[074-055]The decisive battle of Worcester, fought on the 3rd of September, 1651,

[074-056]drove Charles II. into that nine years' exile, from which he only

[074-057]returned on the death of Cromwell. It may be considered the last

[074-058]military event of importance in the English civil war. In Ireland the

[074-059]contest was destined to drag out another campaign, before the walls of

[074-060]the two gallant cities, Galway and Limerick.

[074-061]Limerick was the first object of attack. Ireton, leaving Sankey to

[074-062]administer martial law in Tipperary, struck the Shannon opposite

[074-063]Killaloe, driving Castlehaven before him. Joined by Coote and Reynolds,

[074-064]fresh from the sieges of Athenry and Athlone, he moved upon Limerick by

[074-065]the Connaught bank of the river, while Castlehaven fled to Clanrickarde

[074-066]in Galway, with a guard of forty horse, all that remained intact of the

[074-067]4,000 men bequeathed him by Ormond. From the side of Munster, Lord

[074-068]Muskerry attempted a diversion in favour of Limerick, but was repulsed

[074-069]at Castleishen, by "the flying camp" of Lord Broghill. The besiegers

[074-070]were thus not only delivered of a danger, but reinforced by native

[074-071]troops—if the "Undertakers" could be properly called so—which made them

[074-072]the most formidable army that had ever surrounded an Irish city. From

[074-073]early summer till the last week of October, the main force of the

[074-074]English and Anglo-Irish, supplied with every species of arm then

[074-075]invented, assailed the walls of Limerick. The plague, which during

[074-076]these months swept with such fearful mortality over the whole kingdom,

[074-077]struck down its defenders, and filled all its streets with desolation

[074-078]and grief. The heroic bishops, O'Brien of Emly, and O'Dwyer of

[074-079]Limerick, exerted themselves to uphold, by religious exhortations, the

[074-080]confidence of the besieged; while Hugh O'Neil and General Purcell

[074-081]maintained the courage of their men. Clanrickarde had offered to charge

[074-082]himself with the command, but the citizens preferred to trust in the

[074-083]skill and determination of the defender of Clonmel, whose very name was

[074-084]a talisman among them. The municipal government, however, composed of

[074-085]the men of property in the city, men whose trade was not war, whose

[074-086]religion was not enthusiastic, formed a third party,—a party in favour

[074-087]of peace at any price. With the Mayor at their head, they openly

[074-088]encouraged the surrender of one of the outworks to the besiegers, and

[074-089]this betrayal, on the 27th of October, compelled the surrender of the

[074-090]entire works. Thus Limerick fell, divided within itself by military,

[074-091]clerical, and municipal factions; thus glory and misfortune combined to

[074-092]consecrate its name in the national veneration, and the general memory

[074-093]of mankind. The Bishop of Emly and General Purcell were executed as

[074-094]traitors; the Bishop of Limerick escaped in the disguise of a common

[074-095]soldier, and died at Brussels; O'Neil's life was saved by a single

[074-096]vote; Sir Geoffrey Gabney, Aldermen Stritch and Fanning, and other

[074-097]leading Confederates, expiated their devotion upon the scaffold.

[074-098]On the 12th of May following—seven months after the capture of

[074-099]Limerick, Galway fell. Ireton, who survived the former siege but a few

[074-100]days, was succeeded by Ludlow, a sincere republican of the school of

[074-101]Pym and Hampden—if that school can be called, in our modern sense,

[074-102]republican. It was the sad privilege of General Preston, whose name is

[074-103]associated with so many of the darkest, and with some of the brightest

[074-104]incidents of this war, to order the surrender of Galway, as he had two

[074-105]years previously given up Waterford. Thus the last open port, the last

[074-106]considerable town held by the Confederates, yielded to the overwhelming

[074-107]power of numbers and munitions, in the twelfth year of that illustrious

[074-108]war which Ireland waged for her religious and civil liberties, against

[074-109]the forces of the two adjoining kingdoms, sometimes estranged from one

[074-110]another, but always hostile alike to the religious belief and the

[074-111]political independence of the Irish people.

[074-112]With the fall of Galway, the Confederate war drew rapidly to a close.

[074-113]Colonels Fitzpatrick, O'Dwyer, Grace, and Thorlogh O'Neil, surrendered

[074-114]their posts; Lords Enniskillen and West-Meath followed their example;

[074-115]Lord Muskerry yielded Ross Castle, on Killarney, in June; Clanrickarde

[074-116]laid down his arms at Carrick, in October. The usual terms granted were

[074-117]liberty to transport themselves and followers to the service of any

[074-118]foreign state or prince at peace with the commonwealth; a favoured few

[074-119]were permitted to live and die in peace on their own estates, under the

[074-120]watchful eye of some neighbouring garrison.

[074-121]The chief actors in the Confederate war not already accounted for,

[074-122]terminated their days under many different circumstances. Mountgarrett

[074-123]and Bishop Rothe died before Galway fell, and were buried in the

[074-124]capital of the Confederacy; Bishop McMahon of Clogher, surrendered to

[074-125]Sir Charles Coote, and was executed like a felon by one he had saved

[074-126]from destruction a year before at Derry; Coote, after the Restoration,

[074-127]became Earl of Mountrath, and Broghill, Earl of Orrery; Clanrickarde

[074-128]died unnoticed on his English estate, under the Protectorate;

[074-129]Inchiquin, after many adventures in foreign lands, turned Catholic in

[074-130]his old age, and this burner of churches bequeathed an annual alms for

[074-131]masses for his soul; Jones, Corbet, Cook, and the fanatical preacher,

[074-132]Hugh Peters, perished on the scaffold with the other regicides executed

[074-133]by order of the English Parliament; Ormond having shared the evils of

[074-134]exile with the King, shared also the splendour of his restoration,

[074-135]became a Duke, and took his place, as if by common consent, at the head

[074-136]of the peerage of the empire; his Irish rental, which before the war

[074-137]was but 7,000 pounds a year, swelled suddenly on the Restoration to

[074-138]80,000 pounds; Nicholas French, after some sojourn in Spain, where he

[074-139]was coadjutor to the Archbishop of Saint James, returned to Louvain,

[074-140]where he made his first studies, and there spent the evening of his

[074-141]days in the composition of those powerful pamphlets which kept alive

[074-142]the Irish cause at home and on the continent; a Roman patrician did the

[074-143]honours of sepulture to Luke Wadding, and Cromwell interred James Usher

[074-144]in Westminster Abbey; the heroic defender of Clonmel and Limerick, and

[074-145]the gallant, though vacillating Preston, were cordially received in

[074-146]France; while the consistent republican, Ludlow, took refuge as a

[074-147]fugitive in Switzerland.

[074-148]Sir Phelim O'Neil, the first author of the war, was among the last to

[074-149]suffer the penalties of defeat. For a moment, towards the end, he

[074-150]renewed his sway over the remnant of Owen's soldiers, took

[074-151]Ballyshannon, and two or three other places. Compelled at last to

[074-152]surrender, he was carried to Dublin, and tried on a charge of treason,

[074-153]a committee closeted behind the bench dictating the interrogatories to

[074-154]his judges, and receiving his answers in reply. Condemned to death, as

[074-155]was expected, he was offered his life by the Puritan colonel, Hewson,

[074-156]on the very steps of the scaffold, if he would inculpate the late King

[074-157]Charles in the rising of 1641. This he "stoutly refused to do," and the

[074-158]execution proceeded with all its atrocious details. Whatever may have

[074-159]been the excesses committed under his command by a plundered people, at

[074-160]their first insurrection—and we know that they have been exaggerated

[074-161]beyond all bounds—it must be admitted he died the death of a Christian,

[074-162]a soldier, and a gentleman.
