## Overview

Theobald Wolfe Tone (1763–1798) was the preeminent Irish revolutionary of the eighteenth century and the founding spirit of the [[united-irishmen|United Irishmen]]. A [[dublin|Dublin]]-born Protestant lawyer, Tone argued that Ireland could only be free when Catholic, Protestant, and Dissenter united under the common name of Irishman. His vision of non-sectarian republicanism, his diplomacy with Revolutionary France, and his death following the failed rebellion of 1798 made him the father of Irish republicanism. (098-019) (099-077)

Tone was a prolific writer, diarist, and political organizer whose ideas shaped Irish nationalism for two centuries. Though the rising he helped plan was crushed, the principles he articulated—separation from England, republican government, and religious equality—became the bedrock of Irish revolutionary thought. His death in British custody, a prisoner of war who refused the dishonour of a traitor's execution, sealed his place in Irish national memory. (104-261) (105-083)

## Biography

### Early Life and Political Awakening

Theobald Wolfe Tone was born in [[dublin|Dublin]] in 1763, the son of a Church of Ireland coach-maker. He studied at Trinity College [[dublin|Dublin]], where he formed a lifelong friendship with Thomas Addis [[robert-emmet|Emmet]], and was called to the bar in 1789. His early political writings, particularly "An Argument on Behalf of the Catholics of Ireland" (1791), established his reputation as an advocate of Catholic emancipation. (098-019)

### The United Irishmen

In October 1791, Tone joined with "the Hon. Simon Butler, Wolfe Tone, and Thomas Addis [[robert-emmet|Emmet]]" to found the [[united-irishmen|Society of United Irishmen]] in Belfast. (098-019) The society's original purpose—parliamentary reform—soon gave way to more radical goals under the influence of the French Revolution. As British repression intensified, the [[united-irishmen|United Irishmen]] became an underground revolutionary organization committed to establishing an independent Irish republic.

Tone served as secretary of the Catholic Committee and acted as an intermediary between the Catholic population and the United Irish leadership. His ability to bridge the sectarian divide was central to the movement's strategy. However, "the Northerners were more numerous than Wolfe Tone liked or expected," and balancing the revolutionary ardour of [[ulster|Ulster]] [[presbyterian|Presbyterians]] with the cautious constitutionalism of Catholic leaders proved a constant challenge. (098-196)

### Diplomacy with Revolutionary France

Forced into exile in 1795, Tone travelled to France to seek military assistance for an Irish rising. The French Directory recognized his ability and commissioned him as an adjutant-general. Through years of negotiation, Tone secured French commitments to send expeditionary forces to Ireland in 1796 and 1798. "Arthur O'Conor, Addis [[robert-emmet|Emmet]], Wolfe Tone, and all those" who worked with him in Paris formed the nucleus of an Irish revolutionary government in exile. (099-077)

### The 1798 Rebellion and Death

The rebellion of 1798 broke out without the promised French support, and the risings in Wexford, [[ulster|Ulster]], and Connacht were crushed in bloody succession. In October, a small French squadron under Admiral Bompard sailed for Ireland with Tone aboard. "In this fleet sailed Theobold Wolfe Tone," hoping to salvage the revolution. (104-261) The squadron was intercepted by the Royal Navy off the coast of Donegal, and Tone was captured.

Brought to [[dublin|Dublin]] in chains, Tone was tried by court-martial. He refused to plead for mercy, delivering instead a defiant statement of his republican principles. "The act must always stand between Wolfe Tone and the Christian people of Ireland," he declared—an act of revolutionary conviction, not treason. (104-295) Sentenced to hang, he died in prison on 19 November 1798 from a self-inflicted wound, escaping the gallows. His final letter to his wife was addressed from the Provost Prison, the same place where "the college companion of Wolfe Tone" had once studied. (105-083)

## Legacy

### Father of Irish Republicanism

Wolfe Tone is universally acknowledged as the father of Irish republicanism. His vision of a non-sectarian Irish republic, his willingness to ally with Revolutionary France against the British Crown, and his martyr's death established the template for Irish revolutionary nationalism. Every Irish republican movement since—from the Young Irelanders of 1848 to the Fenians and beyond—has looked back to Tone as its founding inspiration.

### The Tone Tradition

Tone's political writings, especially his diaries and autobiography, remain foundational texts of Irish nationalism. His insistence that the Irish nation must transcend religious division—that the quarrel was not between Catholic and Protestant but between the people of Ireland and the Crown of England—was a generation ahead of its time. His grave at Bodenstown, County Kildare, is the site of annual republican commemorations, and his name is invoked by every strand of Irish nationalism.
