## Overview

Sir Lawrence Parsons (c. 1750–1830), of Parsonstown (now Birr, County Offaly), was an Anglo-Irish politician and landowner who served in the Irish House of Commons during the final decades of the independent Irish Parliament. A member of the Patriot opposition and a political ally of Henry Grattan, Parsons opposed the Act of Union and supported Catholic emancipation. His family, descended from English settlers of the Elizabethan period, had long been established in the Irish midlands, and the town of Parsonstown (Birr) took its name from their surname.

## Family Origins and the Parsons of Birr

The Parsons family were among the earliest English planters in Ireland. The name is linked to Sir William Parsons, Surveyor-General of Ireland under James I, who "presided as Surveyor-General" over the plantation surveys of Ulster "early in 1609" (064-223–064-224). A "royal commission was issued to Chichester, Lambert, St. John, Ridgeway, Moore, Davis, and Parsons, with the Archbishop of Armagh, and the Bishop of Derry, to inquire into the portions forfeited" (064-225–064-226). This made the Parsons family one of the founding dynasties of the Ulster Plantation, though Sir Lawrence Parsons's own branch had settled in the midlands.

Sir William Parsons's son, also Sir William, served as Lord Justice of Ireland during the critical period of 1641. In the days before the rising, "Parsons at first discredited the tale" of the plot revealed by Owen O'Connolly — a misjudgment that some historians believe allowed the insurrection to catch Dublin Castle unprepared (067-048). Parsons was closely associated with the Puritan faction: McGee observes that "observers that Ormond, a loyalist and a churchman, could not long act in concert with such devoted Puritans as Parsons, Borlase, and Coote" (067-143). The family seat at Parsonstown — "the family borough of the famous Undertaker" (083-025) — was established during the plantations.

## The Williamite Period

During the Williamite War, Parsonstown and its castle became a strategic point. The "castle of Sir Lawrence Parsons" (083-032) was the scene of military operations as Jacobite and Williamite forces manoeuvred along the Shannon. The town, situated on the river, guarded an important crossing point. "About the middle of September, the former made a dash over the bridge of Banagher, against Birr, or Parsonstown" (083-025), and English forces "under Lanier, Kirke, and Douglas, hastened to its relief" (083-027). The Irish forces, "after a fortnight's intense suffering from exposure to the weather, were glad to get back again to their snug quarters at Parsonstown" (083-054–083-055), indicating the town changed hands or was occupied by both sides at different times.

## Parliamentary Career

The Sir Lawrence Parsons of the late eighteenth century represented the family's political interests in the Irish Parliament. He was associated with the Patriot Party and the Whig opposition. During the debates on Catholic relief, "Curran, Hardy, Sir Lawrence Parsons, Charles Kendall Bushe" (099-246) were among those who spoke in favour of reform. Parsons was part of the circle of liberal Protestant politicians who, influenced by Grattan and the ideals of 1782, sought to broaden the basis of the Irish constitution to include Catholics.

His most notable parliamentary intervention came in the debates surrounding the Act of Union.

> In amendment to the address, Sir Lawrence Parsons moved (106-040)

a resolution seeking to preserve Ireland's legislative independence. In the last session of the Irish Parliament, "Sir Lawrence Parsons moved" an amendment to the address in reply to the Lord Lieutenant's speech, seeking to preserve Ireland's legislative independence (106-040). The amendment was defeated, and the Union was carried, but Parsons's opposition placed him on the right side of Irish patriotic memory.

## Post-Union Years

After the Union, Sir Lawrence Parsons withdrew largely from national politics but remained an influential figure in King's County (Offaly). The Parsons family continued to reside at Birr Castle, which would later become famous as the seat of the 3rd Earl of Rosse, builder of the great telescope — the "Leviathan of Parsonstown." The family's evolution from Elizabethan undertakers to Whig patriots to scientific patrons illustrates the long arc of Anglo-Irish assimilation.

## Legacy

Sir Lawrence Parsons exemplifies the eighteenth-century Anglo-Irish Patriot — a member of the established Protestant elite who nonetheless opposed the Union and supported Catholic relief. His family's long history, stretching from the Ulster Plantation surveys of 1609 to the parliamentary debates of 1800, mirrors the transformation of English settlers into Irish patriots over two centuries. The name of Parsonstown (later Birr) preserves the family's association with the midlands, and Birr Castle remains one of Ireland's most historic houses.
