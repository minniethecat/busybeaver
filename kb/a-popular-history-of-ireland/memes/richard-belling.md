Richard Belling was an Irish lawyer, writer, and political figure who served as secretary to the Supreme Council of the [[Catholic Association|Catholic Confederation]] during the 1640s. An accomplished man of letters, he was the continuator of Sir Philip Sidney's _Arcadia_ and later served as envoy to Rome on behalf of the Confederate Catholics. He was a central figure in the civil administration of the Confederation and one of the most influential pamphleteers of his era.

## Early Life and Literary Career

Belling was described by his contemporaries as "an accomplished writer and lawyer" (068-184). Before the Confederate period, he had gained literary recognition as the continuator of Sir Philip Sidney's unfinished prose romance _Arcadia_, a work of considerable prestige in seventeenth-century letters.

> "Mr. Richard Belling, an accomplished writer and lawyer, Secretary." (068-184)


This literary background marked him as an unusual figure among the Confederation's leadership — a man equally at home in the world of letters and the arena of political negotiation.

## Secretary to the Supreme Council

In the summer of 1642, the Catholic Confederation was formally organized at [[Kilkenny]], establishing a national government with a senate, executive, and administration. The Supreme Council, with [[Mountgarrett|Lord Mountgarrett]] as its president, appointed Belling as its secretary (068-183). His role was central: he was responsible for the Confederation's official correspondence, the drafting of its public declarations, and the maintenance of its records.

In the subsequent General Assembly, Belling's position was confirmed and his duties expanded. He served on key diplomatic missions,.

> "A Supreme Council of six members for each province was recommended, approved, and elected." (069-036)


Belling was named to a committee alongside Lords Gormanstown and Castlehaven, [[Phelim O'Neill|Sir Phelim O'Neil]], and Mr. Darcy to negotiate on behalf of the Confederation (069-035).

## Envoy to Rome

One of Belling's most significant assignments was his diplomatic mission to the Holy See. He was sent to Rome where [[Innocent X|Pope Innocent X]] had recently succeeded Urban VIII, the "generous friend of the Catholic Irish" (070-155). At Rome, Belling served as the Confederation's special agent, working alongside Father Hugh Bourke who had been dispatched to Spain.

Belling later joined the entourage of the Papal Nuncio, [[Rinuccini|Giovanni Battista Rinuccini]], on his journey from Rome to Ireland. He accompanied the Nuncio through Genoa and Marseilles to Paris, where they were detained for nearly five months (071-054). When Rinuccini finally embarked from France, Belling was among the Franco-Irish officers and Italian noblemen who sailed with him aboard the _San Pietro_, a frigate carrying Spanish gold and military supplies for the Confederate cause (071-076).

## The Remonstrance and Later Years

After the collapse of the Confederation in the early 1650s, Belling remained a prominent figure in Irish Catholic intellectual life. During the Restoration period, he was among those who submitted the celebrated "Remonstrance" — a document ".

> "Richard Belling, the secretary to the first Confederate Council, and Envoy to Rome, submitted the celebrated document known as 'The Remonstrance.'" (076-131)


deeply imbued with the spirit of" loyalty to the Crown while asserting Catholic rights (076-131). This Remonstrance, drafted by [[Peter Walsh|Father Peter Walsh]] and supported by Belling, became one of the most debated documents of Restoration Ireland.

Belling was recognized as one of the leading pamphleteers of his generation, ranked alongside [[Nicholas French|Bishop French]], [[Richard Nagle|Sir Richard Nagle]], [[Roger Boyle|Lord Orrery]], and [[William Molyneux]] (078-142). His contributions to Irish political thought bridged the gap between the Confederate experiment and the later struggles for [[Catholic Emancipation]].
