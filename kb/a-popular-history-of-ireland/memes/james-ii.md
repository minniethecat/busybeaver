**James II** (1633-1701) was the last Catholic monarch of England, Scotland, and Ireland. His brief reign (1685-1688) and subsequent attempt to recover his throne through Ireland transformed the country into the principal theatre of the [[Williamite War]], with consequences that shaped Irish history for generations. Deposed in the Glorious Revolution, James landed in Ireland with French support in 1689, convened a Catholic-majority Parliament in [[Dublin]], and fought for his crown at the [[Battle of the Boyne]] before fleeing into permanent exile.

## Accession and Tyrconnell's Administration

James succeeded his brother [[charles-ii|Charles II]] in 1685 at the age of fifty-two, having served as Duke of York with credit both by land and sea. He was an avowed Catholic, married to the Catholic princess Mary of Modena. (079-005, 079-007) Within a month of his proclamation, the Duke of Ormond quitted the government of Ireland for the last time, and in January 1686 Lord Clarendon assumed the Lord-Lieutenancy. (079-009, 079-011) He was soon replaced by Colonel Richard Talbot, Earl of Tyrconnell, a Catholic and former agent for Catholic interests, who was installed as Lord Deputy on 17 March 1687. (079-013, 079-014)

James proceeded openly with what he hoped would be a counter-reformation, relying on France on the one hand and Ireland on the other. (079-018, 079-019) Through Tyrconnell's influence, a revolution was wrought in the militia, the courts of justice, and the corporations of towns. The exclusively Protestant militia was disarmed and a new Catholic army raised in its place. (079-040, 079-043) Catholic mayors and sheriffs, escorted by Catholic troops, were seen for the first time in that generation marching to their own places of worship. (079-061, 079-062) But these rapid changes alarmed the Protestant establishment and drove thousands to flee to England and Holland, where they returned in the ranks of the Prince of Orange. (079-085, 079-086)

> But the best allies of William were, after all, the folly and fatuity of James. (079-093, 079-094)

The importation of Irish troops into England by entire battalions gave the sorest wound to English national pride. (079-095, 079-096) When William landed at Torbay on 5 November 1688, James, deserted by his nobles, his army, and even his own children, arrived as a fugitive at the court of France on Christmas Day. (079-131, 079-133)

## King James in Ireland and the Patriot Parliament

A few days after his arrival in France, James wrote to Tyrconnell urging him to hold out, and Louis XIV supplied 7,000 or 8,000 muskets. (080-002, 080-007) Accompanied by his natural son the Duke of Berwick, about a hundred officers, and 1,200 veterans, James sailed from Brest with a fleet of thirty-three vessels and landed at Kinsale on 12 March 1689. (080-033, 080-036, 080-037) His reception by the southern population was enthusiastic in the extreme. From Kinsale to Cork, from Cork to [[dublin|Dublin]], his progress was accompanied by Gaelic songs and dances, Latin orations, and loyal addresses. (080-038, 080-039, 080-041)

The royal entry into [[dublin|Dublin]] was the crowning pageant. Two venerable harpers played on their national instruments near the gate by which he entered; a number of religious in their robes, with a huge cross at their head, chanted as they went; forty young girls, dressed in white, danced the ancient _Rinka_, scattering flowers as they danced. (080-048, 080-053, 080-054) A green flag floated from the main tower of the Castle bearing the inscription: "_Now or Never -- Now and Forever_." (080-064, 080-066)

James convened his Parliament on 7 May 1689 -- the so-called "Patriot Parliament" -- with 228 Commoners and 46 members of the Upper House. (080-087, 080-089) The King made a judicious opening speech, declaring his intention to uphold the rights of property and establish liberty of conscience for both Protestant and Catholic. (080-097, 080-098) Three measures passed by this Parliament were of lasting importance: an Act for establishing Liberty of Conscience, an act abolishing writs of error and appeal into England to establish the judicial independence of Ireland, and an act repealing the Act of Settlement. (080-103, 080-108, 080-111)

> "Though Papists," said Mr. Grattan, "they were not slaves; they wrung a constitution from King James before they accompanied him to the field." (080-118, 080-120)

Unfortunately, James had not abandoned the arbitrary principles of his family even in his worst adversity. He doubled by mere proclamation the enormous subsidy of 20,000 pounds monthly voted him by the Houses, debased the coinage, and attempted to force fellows and scholars on the University of [[dublin|Dublin]] contrary to its statutes. (080-122, 080-126, 080-129, 080-132)

## The War for the Crown -- Campaign of 1689

The lesson taught to James II at [[Enniskillen]] and at [[Derry]] was that the [[ulster|Ulster]] Protestants would not submit. (089-075) When James appeared in person before Derry in April 1689, expecting its surrender, the true answer of the brave townsmen was a cannon shot that killed one of his staff, and the cry of "No Surrender" thundered from the walls. (081-069, 081-075, 081-077) James, awakened from his self-complacent dream, returned to [[dublin|Dublin]] to open his Parliament, leaving General Hamilton to continue the siege. (081-077, 081-078) The siege of Derry lasted above three months; the relief came by the breaking of the boom across the harbour on the last day of July, with a loss of 4,000 among the besieged and 6,000 among the besiegers. (081-088, 081-090, 081-093, 081-094)

Scarcely inferior in importance was the spirited defence of Enniskillen. On the same day that Derry was relieved, the Enniskilleners brought Mountcashel's forces to battle at Newtown-Butler, routing the raw southern levies and leaving 2,000 of them on the field. (081-098, 081-127, 081-130, 081-132) By the end of August, with the exception of Charlemont and Carrickfergus, King James no longer possessed a garrison in the province of [[ulster|Ulster]]. (081-143, 081-144)

In August 1689, the veteran Marshal Duke de Schomberg landed at Belfast Lough with 18,000 men. Carrickfergus fell after eight days' bombardment, and Schomberg advanced to Dundalk, where he entrenched and went into winter quarters. (081-150, 081-153, 081-163) A terrible dysentery broke out among his troops, and James, from his headquarters at Drogheda, attempted in vain to raise the camp. (081-164, 081-173, 081-174) By November, James returned to [[dublin|Dublin]] much elated that Schomberg had shrunk from meeting him in the field. (081-173, 081-175)

## The Boyne and Flight to France

In the spring of 1690, William resolved to command in Ireland in person. On 14 June he landed at Carrickfergus with a formidable retinue, finding at his disposal upwards of 40,000 men drawn from a "strange medley of all nations." (082-025, 082-039, 082-044) James, informed of William's arrival, marched northward at the head of 20,000 men, French and Irish, and chose his ground at the Boyne, resolved to hazard a battle for the preservation of [[dublin|Dublin]]. (082-058, 082-061, 082-062)

On the last day of June 1690, the two kings confronted each other at the [[Battle of the Boyne]]. James, strong in his defensive position on the hill of Donore, awaited the battle with confidence. (082-064, 082-069, 082-074) The battle commenced at the ford of Oldbridge and continued for seven hours, at which point the Irish fell back on Duleek in good order. (082-078, 082-092) The assailants admitted five hundred killed and as many wounded; the defenders lost from one thousand to fifteen hundred. (082-093, 082-094) The political consequences, however, were momentous. William was in [[dublin|Dublin]] within a week, and James, terrified by reports that reached him, was _en route_ for France. (082-099, 082-100)

The Irish cause survived the King's departure. The Jacobite army under [[Patrick Sarsfield]] and the French general Saint Ruth fought on through the campaigns of 1690 and 1691, culminating in the [[Siege of Limerick]] and the [[Treaty of Limerick]] signed in October 1691. James, from his exile at Saint-Germain-en-Laye, could only watch as the articles he had promised were steadily violated by the Ascendancy Parliament. (084-147, 085-089)

## Exile and the Jacobite Legacy

James spent the remainder of his life as a pensioner of Louis XIV at the French court. When the ships from Ireland reached Brest after the surrender of Limerick, James himself came down from Saint Germain to receive the Irish soldiers who had fought for his cause. (084-147, 084-148) Those who went abroad -- the "Wild Geese" -- were at once granted the rights of French citizenship and formed into the Irish brigades that would serve France with distinction for generations. (084-148, 084-149, 087-033)

James died at Saint-Germain-en-Laye in 1701. His son, recognised by France and the Jacobites as James III -- "the Pretender," as he was called by the partisans of the new dynasty -- inherited the claim. (088-008) The Jacobite cause persisted through the eighteenth century: the rising of 1715, the expedition of 1719, and the great rising of 1745 under Prince Charles Edward Stuart, all aimed at restoring the Stuart line. (088-076, 090-003) In 1766, when James III died at Avignon, the French court ceased to recognise the Stuart claim. (090-153)

The legacy of James II in Ireland is profound and paradoxical. His personal failures as a military leader are undeniable, yet his brief reign kindled Catholic hopes that sustained Jacobite loyalty for generations. The prohibitory enactments passed in the name of preventing another "James" -- including the 10th and 11th of [[william-iii|William III]] -- became the legal architecture of Catholic dispossession. (095-017) The reign of [[henry-viii|Henry VIII]] till the fall of James II -- nearly two hundred years -- had witnessed the progressive dispossession of the native Irish and Old English Catholics, a process that James's policies dramatically but briefly reversed. (051-106)
