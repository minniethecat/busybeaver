## Overview

Art McMurrogh (c. 1357–1417), also known as Art Kavanagh, was the most formidable Gaelic adversary the English Crown faced in Ireland during the late medieval period. His career of resistance in Leinster frustrated the military expeditions of Richard II — the only English king to personally lead an army in Ireland between Henry II and William III — and his success inspired a wider Gaelic resurgence across the island. (040-059) (041-031)

"Of all the native chieftains of Leinster, of great ability and good fortune, was Art McMurrogh." His defiance of two royal expeditions earned him a place in Irish memory as a symbol of unconquered Leinster. (040-236) (041-077)

## Background and Rise

Art McMurrogh belonged to the MacMurrough-Kavanagh dynasty, the descendants of Diarmuid MacMurrough, the Leinster king whose appeal to Henry II had first brought the Anglo-Normans to Ireland. By the late fourteenth century, the dynasty had recovered much of its former power. "The nephews of Art McMurrogh contested the superiority of the southern Hy Nial," indicating the broad reach of the family's influence. (039-076)

McMurrogh came to prominence in the 1390s, when he renewed the dynastic claim to the kingship of Leinster and began a campaign to expel English settlers from the province. (039-160)

> "It was imputed as a crime to Art McMurrogh in 1397, that he had recovered the whole of his patrimony in Leinster." (039-160)

## The First Expedition of Richard II (1394–1395)

Richard II's first Irish expedition, launched in 1394, was the largest English military force sent to Ireland since the original invasion. Faced with overwhelming numbers, many Gaelic chieftains submitted. McMurrogh, however, held out in the fastnesses of the Wicklow Mountains and Leinster forests, refusing to surrender either his lands or his title. The campaign achieved limited results; Richard returned to England having received submissions but without having broken McMurrogh's power. (040-059)

## The Second Expedition (1399) and Aftermath

Richard's second expedition in 1399 was mounted specifically to deal with McMurrogh, who had exploited the King's absence to extend his control over much of Leinster. "By this time the banner of Art McMurrogh floated over all the castles of Leinster." The campaign, however, proved disastrous for Richard. While he was absent in Ireland, Henry Bolingbroke returned from exile and seized the English throne. Richard's deposition and death removed the threat to McMurrogh, who continued to consolidate his power. (041-077) (041-155)

> "Art McMurrogh, the younger, not only extended his sway over all Leinster, but exacted tribute from the English settlers." (041-077)

## Later Years and Enduring Resistance

McMurrogh remained active into the early fifteenth century, "now some years beyond middle age, but still full of martial vigour." His "activity defied the military resources of the Pale," and English attempts to subdue him by force or negotiation repeatedly failed. His success encouraged other Gaelic lords — the O'Neills in Ulster, the O'Briens in Thomond — to assert their own independence. (042-235) (043-075)

The English administration eventually resorted to paying McMurrogh an annual tribute — the "black rent" — to secure a precarious peace. For the Gaelic Irish, this was proof that the tide had turned: the descendants of the conquerors were now paying tribute to the descendants of the conquered. (043-213)

## Legacy

Art McMurrogh's career marked the high point of the Gaelic resurgence in late medieval Ireland. His ability to withstand two royal expeditions demonstrated the limits of English power on the island and established a pattern of Gaelic resistance that persisted until the Tudor reconquest in the sixteenth century. In the nationalist tradition, he was remembered as a patriot who "cleared Leinster of its foreign garrisons" and restored the dignity of Gaelic kingship. (040-236) (041-031)
