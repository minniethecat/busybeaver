CHAPTER XI.

[086-001]REIGN OF QUEEN ANNE.

[086-002]The reign of Queen Anne occupies twelve years (1702 to 1714. The new

[086-003]sovereign, daughter of James by his first marriage, inherited the

[086-004]legacy of William's wars, arising out of the European coalition. Her

[086-005]diplomatists, and her troops, under the leadership of Marlborough,

[086-006]continued throughout her reign to combat against France, in Spain,

[086-007]Germany, and the Netherlands; the treaty of Utrecht being signed only

[086-008]the year before her majesty's decease. In domestic politics, the main

[086-009]occurrences were the struggle of the Whigs and Tories, immortalized for

[086-010]us in the pages of Swift, Steele, Addison, and Bolingbroke; the

[086-011]limitation of the succession to the descendants of the Electress

[086-012]Sophia, in the line of Hanover; and the abortive Jacobite movement on

[086-013]the Queen's death which drove Ormond and Atterbury into exile.

[086-014]In Ireland, this is the reign, _par excellence_, of the penal code.

[086-015]From the very beginning of the Queen's reign, an insatiate spirit of

[086-016]proscription dictated the councils of the Irish oligarchy. On the

[086-017]arrival of the second and last Duke of Ormond, in 1703, as

[086-018]Lord-Lieutenant, the Commons waited on him in a body, with a bill "for

[086-019]discouraging the further growth of Popery," to which the duke having

[086-020]signified his entire concurrence, it was accordingly introduced, and

[086-021]became law. The following are among the most remarkable clauses of this

[086-022]act: The third clause provides, that if the son of an estated Papist

[086-023]shall conform to the established religion, the father shall be

[086-024]incapacitated from selling or mortgaging his estate, or disposing of

[086-025]any portion of it by will. The fourth clause prohibits a Papist from

[086-026]being the guardian of his own child; and orders, that if at any time

[086-027]the child, though ever so young, pretends to be a Protestant, it shall

[086-028]be taken from its own father, and placed under the guardianship of the

[086-029]nearest Protestant relation. The sixth clause renders Papists incapable

[086-030]of purchasing any manors, tenements, hereditaments, or any rents or

[086-031]profits arising out of the same, or of holding any lease of lives, or

[086-032]other lease whatever, for any term exceeding thirty-one years. And with

[086-033]respect even to such limited leases, it further enacts, that if a

[086-034]Papist should hold a farm producing a profit greater than one-third of

[086-035]the amount of the rent, his right to such should immediately cease, and

[086-036]pass over entirely to the first Protestant who should discover the rate

[086-037]of profit. The seventh clause prohibits Papists from succeeding to the

[086-038]properties or estates of their Protestant relations. By the tenth

[086-039]clause, the estate of a Papist, not having a Protestant heir, is

[086-040]ordered to be gavelled, or divided in equal shares between all his

[086-041]children. The sixteenth and twenty-fourth clauses impose the oath of

[086-042]abjuration, and the sacramental test, as a qualification for office,

[086-043]and for voting at elections. The twenty-third clause deprives the

[086-044]Catholics of Limerick and Galway of the protection secured to them by

[086-045]the articles of the treaty of Limerick. The twenty-fifth clause vests

[086-046]in her majesty all advowsons possessed by Papists.

[086-048]Certain Catholic barristers, living under protection, not yet excluded

[086-049]from the practice of their profession, petitioned to be heard at the

[086-050]bar of the House of Commons. Accordingly, Mr. Malone, the ancestor of

[086-051]three generations of scholars and orators, Sir Stephen Rice, one of the

[086-052]most spotless characters of the age, formerly chief-justice under King

[086-053]James, and Sir Theobald Butler, were heard against the bill. The

[086-054]argument of Butler, who stood at the very head of his profession,

[086-055]remains to us almost in its entirety, and commands our admiration by

[086-056]its solidity and dignity. Never was national cause more worthily

[086-057]pleaded; never was the folly of religious persecution more forcibly

[086-058]exhibited. Alluding to the monstrous fourth clause of the bill, the

[086-059]great advocate exclaimed:—

[086-060]"It is natural for the father to love the child; but we all know that

[086-061]children are but too apt and subject, without any such liberty as this

[086-062]bill gives, to slight and neglect their duty to their parents; and

[086-063]surely such an act as this will not be an instrument of restraint, but

[086-064]rather encourage them more to it.

[086-065]"It is but too common with the son, who has a prospect of an estate,

[086-066]when once he arrives at the age of one and twenty, to think the old

[086-067]father too long in the way between him and it; and how much more will

[086-068]he be subject to it, when, by this act, he shall have liberty, before

[086-069]he comes to that age, to compel and force my estate from me, without

[086-070]asking my leave, or being liable to account with me for it, or out of

[086-071]his share thereof, to a moiety of the debts, portions, or other

[086-072]encumbrances, with which the estate might have been charged before the

[086-073]passing of this act!

[086-074]"Is not this against the laws of God and man? Against the rules of

[086-075]reason and justice, by which all men ought to be governed? Is not this

[086-076]the only way in the world to make children become undutiful? and to

[086-077]bring the grey head of the parent to the grave with grief and tears?

[086-078]"It would be hard from any man; but from a son, a child, the fruit of

[086-079]my body, whom I have nursed in my bosom, and tendered more dearly than

[086-080]my own life, to become my plunderer, to rob me of my estate, to cut my

[086-081]throat, and to take away my bread, is much more grievous than from any

[086-082]other, and enough to make the most flinty hearts to bleed to think on

[086-083]it. And yet this will be the case if this bill pass into a law; which I

[086-084]hope this honourable assembly will not think of, when they shall more

[086-085]seriously consider, and have weighed these matters.

[086-086]"For God's sake, gentlemen, will you consider whether this is according

[086-087]to the golden rule, to do as you would be done unto? And if not, surely

[086-088]you will not, nay, you cannot, without being liable to be charged with

[086-089]the most manifest injustice imaginable, take from us our birthrights,

[086-090]and invest them in others, before our faces."

[086-091]When Butler and Malone had closed, Sir Stephen Rice was heard, not in

[086-092]his character of council, but as one of the petitioners affected by the

[086-093]act. But neither the affecting position of that great jurist, who, from

[086-094]the rank of chief baron had descended to the outer bar, nor the purity

[086-095]of his life, nor the strength of his argument, had any effect upon the

[086-096]oligarchy who heard him. He was answered by quibbles and cavils,

[086-097]unworthy of record, and was finally informed that any rights which

[086-098]Papists "pretended to be taken from them by the Bill, was in their own

[086-099]power to remedy, by conforming, which in prudence they ought to do; and

[086-100]that they had none to blame but themselves." Next day the bill passed

[086-101]into law.

[086-102]The remnant of the clergy were next attacked. On the 17th of March,

[086-103]1705, the Irish Commons resolved, that "informing against Papists was

[086-104]an honourable service to the government," and that all magistrates and

[086-105]others who failed to put the penal laws into execution, "were betrayers

[086-106]of the liberties of the kingdom." But even these resolutions, rewards,

[086-107]and inducements were insufficient to satisfy the spirit of persecution.

[086-108]A further act was passed, in 1709, imposing additional penalties. The

[086-109]first clause declares, that no Papist shall be capable of holding an

[086-110]annuity for life. The third provides, that the child of a Papist, on

[086-111]conforming, shall at once receive an annuity from his father; and that

[086-112]the Chancellor shall compel the father to discover, upon oath, the full

[086-113]value of his estate, real and personal, and thereupon make an order for

[086-114]the support of such conforming child or children, and for securing such

[086-115]a share of the property, after the father's death, as the court shall

[086-116]think fit. The fourteenth and fifteenth clauses secure jointures to

[086-117]Popish wives who shall conform. The sixteenth prohibits a Papist from

[086-118]teaching, even as assistant to a Protestant master. The eighteenth

[086-119]gives a salary of 30 pounds per annum to Popish priests who shall

[086-120]conform. The twentieth provides rewards for the discovery of Popish

[086-121]prelates, priests, and teachers, according to the following whimsical

[086-122]scale:—For discovering an archbishop, bishop, vicar-general, or other

[086-123]person, exercising any foreign ecclesiastical jurisdiction, 50 pounds;

[086-124]for discovering each regular clergyman, and each secular clergyman, not

[086-125]registered, 20 pounds; and for discovering each Popish schoolmaster or

[086-126]usher, 10 pounds. The twenty-first clause empowers two justices to

[086-127]summon before them any Papist over eighteen years of age, and

[086-128]interrogate him when and where he last heard mass said, and the names

[086-129]of the persons present, and likewise touching the residence of any

[086-130]Popish priest or schoolmaster; and if he refuse to give testimony,

[086-131]subjects him to a fine of 20 pounds, or imprisonment for twelve months.

[086-132]Several other penal laws were enacted by the same Parliament, of which

[086-133]we can only notice one; it excluded Catholics from the office of

[086-134]sheriff, and from grand juries, and enacts, that, in trials upon any

[086-135]statute for strengthening the Protestant interest, the plaintiff might

[086-136]challenge a juror for being a Papist, which challenge the judge was to

[086-137]allow.

[086-138]By a royal proclamation of the same year, "all registered priests" were

[086-139]to take "the oath of abjuration before the 25th of March, 1710," under

[086-140]penalty of _premunire_. Under this proclamation and the tariff of

[086-141]rewards just cited, there grew up a class of men, infamous and

[086-142]detestable, known by the nickname of "priest hunters." One of the most

[086-143]successful of these traffickers in blood was a Portuguese Jew, named

[086-144]Garcia, settled at Dublin. He was very skilful at disguises. "He

[086-145]sometimes put on the mien of a priest, for he affected to be one, and

[086-146]thus worming himself into the good graces of some confiding Catholic

[086-147]got a clue to the whereabouts of the clergy." In 1718, Garcia succeeded

[086-148]in arresting seven unregistered priests, for whose detection he had a

[086-149]sum equal to two or three thousand dollars of American money. To such

[086-150]an excess was this trade carried, that a reaction set in, and a

[086-151]Catholic bishop of Ossory, who lived at the time these acts were still

[086-152]in force, records that "the priest-catchers' occupation became

[086-153]exceedingly odious both to Protestants and Catholics," and that himself

[086-154]had seen "ruffians of this calling assailed with a shower of stones,

[086-155]flung by both Catholics and Protestants." But this creditable reaction

[086-156]only became general under George II., twenty years after the passage of

[086-157]the act of Queen Anne.

[086-158]We shall have to mention some monstrous additions made to the code

[086-159]during the first George's reign, and some attempts to repair and

[086-160]perfect its diabolical machinery, even so late as George III.; but the

[086-161]great body of the penal law received its chief accessions from the

[086-162]oligarchical Irish Parliament, under Queen Anne. Hitherto, we have

[086-163]often had to point out, how with all its constitutional defects—with

[086-164]the law of Poynings, obliging heads of bills to be first sent to

[086-165]England—fettering its freedom of initiative;—how, notwithstanding all

[086-166]defects, the Irish Parliament had asserted, at many critical periods,

[086-167]its own and the people's rights, with an energy worthy of admiration.

[086-168]But the collective bigots of this reign were wholly unworthy of the

[086-169]name of a parliament. They permitted the woollen trade to be sacrificed

[086-170]without a struggle,—they allowed the bold propositions of Molyneux, one

[086-171]of their own number, to be condemned and reprobated without a protest.

[086-172]The knotted lash of Jonathan Swift was never more worthily applied,

[086-173]than to "the Legion Club," which he has consigned to such an unenviable

[086-174]immortality. Swift's inspiration may have been mingled with bitter

[086-175]disappointment and personal revenge; but, whatever motives animated

[086-176]him, his fearless use of his great abilities must always make him the

[086-177]first political, as he was certainly the first literary character of

[086-178]Ireland at that day. In a country so bare and naked as he found it;

[086-179]with a bigotry so rampant and united before him; it needed no ordinary

[086-180]courage and capacity to evoke anything like public opinion or public

[086-181]spirit. Let us be just to that most unhappy man of genius; let us

[086-182]proclaim that Irish nationality, bleeding at every pore, and in danger

[086-183]of perishing by the wayside, found shelter on the breast of Swift, and

[086-184]took new heart from the example of that bold churchman, before whom the

[086-185]Parliament, the bench of Bishops, and the Viceroy, trembled.
