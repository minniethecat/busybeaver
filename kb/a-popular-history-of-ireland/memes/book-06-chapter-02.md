CHAPTER II.

[040-001]LIONEL, DUKE OF CLARENCE, LORD LIEUTENANT—THE PENAL CODE OF RACE—"THE

[040-002]STATUTE OF KILKENNY," AND SOME OF ITS CONSEQUENCES.

[040-003]While the grand experiment for the separation of the population of

[040-004]Ireland into two hostile camps was being matured in England, the Earls

[040-005]of Kildare and Ormond were, for four or five years, alternately

[040-006]entrusted with the supreme power. Fresh ordinances, in the spirit of

[040-007]those despatched to Darcy, in 1342, continued annually to arrive. One

[040-008]commanded all lieges of the English King, having grants upon the

[040-009]marches of the Irish enemy, to reside upon and defend them, under pain

[040-010]of revocation. By another entrusted to the Earl of Ormond for

[040-011]promulgation, "no mere Irishman" was to be made a Mayor or bailiff, or

[040-012]other officer of any town within the English districts; nor was any

[040-013]mere Irishman "thereafter, under any pretence of kindred, or from any

[040-014]other cause, to be received into holy orders, or advanced to any

[040-015]ecclesiastical benefice." A modification of this last edict was made

[040-016]the succeeding year, when a royal writ explained that exception was

[040-017]intended to be made of such Irish clerks as had given individual proofs

[040-018]of their loyalty.

[040-019]Soon after the peace of Bretigni had been solemnly ratified at Calais,

[040-020]in 1360, by the Kings of France and England, and the latter had

[040-021]returned to London, it was reported that one of the Princes would be

[040-022]sent over to exercise the supreme power at Dublin. As no member of the

[040-023]royal family had visited Ireland since the reign of John—though Edward

[040-024]I., when Prince, had been appointed his father's lieutenant—this

[040-025]announcement naturally excited unusual expectations. The Prince chosen

[040-026]was the King's third son, Lionel, Duke of Clarence; and every

[040-027]preparation was made to give _eclat_ and effect to his administration.

[040-028]This Prince had married, a few years before, Elizabeth de Burgh, who

[040-029]brought him the titles of Earl of Ulster and Lord of Connaught, with

[040-030]the claims which they covered. By a proclamation, issued in England,

[040-031]all who held possessions in Ireland were commanded to appear before the

[040-032]King, either by proxy or in person, to take measures for resisting the

[040-033]continued encroachments of the Irish enemy. Among the absentees

[040-034]compelled to contribute to the expedition accompanying the Prince, are

[040-035]mentioned Maria, Countess of Norfolk, Agnes, Countess of Pembroke,

[040-036]Margery de Boos, Anna le Despenser, and other noble ladies, who, by a

[040-037]strange recurrence, represented in this age the five co-heiresses of

[040-038]the first Earl Marshal, granddaughters of Eva McMurrogh. What exact

[040-039]force was equipped from all these contributions is not mentioned; but

[040-040]the Prince arrived in Ireland with no more than 1,500 men, under the

[040-041]command of Ralph, Earl of Strafford, James, Earl of Ormond, Sir William

[040-042]Windsor, Sir John Carew, and other knights. He landed at Dublin on the

[040-043]15th of September, 1361, and remained in office for three years. On

[040-044]landing he issued a proclamation, prohibiting natives of the country,

[040-045]of all origins, from approaching his camp or court, and having made

[040-046]this hopeful beginning he marched with his troops into Munster, where

[040-047]he was defeated by O'Brien, and compelled to retreat. Yet by the

[040-048]flattery of courtiers he was saluted as the conqueror of Clare, and

[040-049]took from the supposed fact, his title of _Clarence_. But no adulation

[040-050]could blind him to the real weakness of his position: he keenly felt

[040-051]the injurious consequences of his proclamation, revoked it, and

[040-052]endeavoured to remove the impression he had made, by conferring

[040-053]knighthood on the Prestons, Talbots, Cusacks, De la Hydes, and members

[040-054]of other families, not immediately connected with the Palatine Earls.

[040-055]He removed the Exchequer from Dublin to Carlow, and expended 500

[040-056]pounds—a large sum for that age—in fortifying the town. The barrier of

[040-057]Leinster was established at Carlow, from which it was removed, by an

[040-058]act of the English Parliament ten years afterwards; the town and castle

[040-059]were retaken in 1397, by the celebrated Art McMurrogh, and long

[040-060]remained in the hands of his posterity.

[040-061]In 1364, Duke Lionel went to England, leaving de Windsor as his deputy,

[040-062]but in 1365, and again in 1367, he twice returned to his government.

[040-063]This latter year is memorable as the date of the second great stride

[040-064]towards the establishment of a Penal Code of race, by the enactment of

[040-065]the "Statute of Kilkenny." This memorable Statute was drawn with

[040-066]elaborate care, being intended to serve as the corner stone of all

[040-067]future legislation, and its provisions are deserving of enumeration.

[040-068]The Act sets out with this preamble: "Whereas, at the conquest of the

[040-069]land of Ireland, and for a long time after, the English of the said

[040-070]land used the English language, mode of riding, and apparel, and were

[040-071]governed and ruled, both they and their subjects, called Betaghese

[040-072](villeins), according to English law, &c., &c.,—but now many English of

[040-073]the said land, forsaking the English language, manners, mode of riding,

[040-074]laws, and usages, live, and govern themselves according to the manners,

[040-075]fashion, and language of the Irish enemies, and also have made divers

[040-076]marriages and alliances between themselves and the Irish enemies

[040-077]aforesaid—it is therefore enacted, among other provisions, that all

[040-078]intermarriages, fosterings, gossipred, and buying or selling with the

[040-079]'enemie,' shall be accounted treason—that English names, fashions, and

[040-080]manners shall be resumed under penalty of the confiscation of the

[040-081]delinquent's lands—that March-law and Brehon-law are illegal, and that

[040-082]there shall be no law but English law—that the Irish shall not pasture

[040-083]their cattle on English lands—that the English shall not entertain

[040-084]Irish rhymers, minstrels, or newsmen; and, moreover, that no 'mere

[040-085]Irishmen' shall be admitted to any ecclesiastical benefice, or

[040-086]religious house, situated within the English districts."

[040-087]All the names of those who attended at this Parliament of Kilkenny are

[040-088]not accessible to us; but that the Earls of Kildare, Ormond, and

[040-089]Desmond, were of the number need hardly surprise us, alarmed as they

[040-090]all were by the late successes of the native princes, and overawed by

[040-091]the recent prodigious victories of Edward III. at Cressy and Poictiers.

[040-092]What does at first seem incomprehensible is that the Archbishop not

[040-093]only of Dublin, but of Cashel and Tuam—in the heart of the Irish

[040-094]country—and the Bishops of Leighlin, Ossory, Lismore, Cloyne, and

[040-095]Killala, should be parties to this statute. But on closer inspection

[040-096]our surprise at their presence disappears. Most of these prelates were

[040-097]at that day nominees of the English King, and many of them were English

[040-098]by birth. Some of them never had possession of their sees, but dwelt

[040-099]within the nearest strong town, as pensioners on the bounty of the

[040-100]Crown, while the dioceses were administered by native rivals, or

[040-101]tolerated vicars. Le Reve, Bishop of Lismore, was Chancellor to the

[040-102]Duke in 1367; Young, Bishop of Leighlin, was Vice-Treasurer; the Bishop

[040-103]of Ossory, John of Tatendale, was an English Augustinian, whose

[040-104]appointment was disputed by Milo Sweetman, the native Bishop elect; the

[040-105]Bishop of Cloyne, John de Swasham, was a Carmelite of Lyn, in the

[040-106]county of Norfolk, afterwards Bishop of Bangor, in Wales, where he

[040-107]distinguished himself in the controversy against Wycliffe; the Bishop

[040-108]of Killala we only know by the name of Robert—at that time very unusual

[040-109]among the Irish. The two native names are those of the Archbishops of

[040-110]Cashel and Tuam, Thomas O'Carrol and John O'Grady. The former was

[040-111]probably, and the latter certainly, a nominee of the Crown. We know

[040-112]that Dr. O'Grady died an exile from his see—if he ever was permitted to

[040-113]enter it—in the city of Limerick, four years after the sitting of the

[040-114]Parliament of Kilkenny. Shortly after the enactment of this law, by

[040-115]which he is best remembered, the Duke of Clarence returned to England,

[040-116]leaving to Gerald, fourth Earl of Desmond, the task of carrying it into

[040-117]effect. In the remaining years of this reign the office of Lord

[040-118]Lieutenant was held by Sir William de Windsor, during the intervals of

[040-119]whose absence in England the Prior of Kilmainham, or the Earl of

[040-120]Kildare or of Ormond, discharged the duties with the title of Lord

[040-121]Deputy or Lord Justice.

[040-122]It is now time that we should turn to the native annals of the country

[040-123]to show how the Irish princes had carried on the contest during the

[040-124]eventful half century which the reign of Edward III. occupies in the

[040-125]history of England.

[040-126]In the generation which elapsed from the death of the Earl of Ulster,

[040-127]or rather from the first avowal of the policy of proscription in 1342,

[040-128]the native tribes had on all sides and continuously gained on the

[040-129]descendants of their invaders. In Connaught, the McWilliams, McWattins,

[040-130]and McFeoriss retained part of their estates only by becoming as Irish

[040-131]as the Irish. The lordships of Leyny and Corran, in Sligo and Mayo,

[040-132]were recovered by the heirs of their former chiefs, while the powerful

[040-133]family of O'Conor Sligo converted that strong town into a formidable

[040-134]centre of operations. Rindown, Athlone, Roscommon, and Bunratty, all

[040-135]frontier posts fortified by the Normans, were in 1342, as we learn from

[040-136]the Remonstrance of Kilkenny, in the hands of the elder race.

[040-137]The war, in all the Provinces, was in many respects a war of posts.

[040-138]Towards the north Carrickfergus continued the outwork till captured by

[040-139]Neil O'Neil, when Downpatrick and Dundalk became the northern barriers.

[040-140]The latter town, which seems to have been strengthened after Bruce's

[040-141]defeat, was repeatedly attacked by Neil O'Neil, and at last entered

[040-142]into conditions, by which it procured his protection. At Downpatrick

[040-143]also, in the year 1375, he gained a signal victory over the English of

[040-144]the town and their allies, under Sir James Talbot of Malahide, and

[040-145]Burke of Camline, in which both these commanders were slain. This

[040-146]O'Neil, called from his many successes Neil _More_, or the Great, dying

[040-147]in 1397, left the borders of Ulster more effectually cleared of foreign

[040-148]garrisons than they had been for a century and a half before. He

[040-149]enriched the churches of Armagh and Derry, and built a habitation for

[040-150]students resorting to the primatial city, on the site of the ancient

[040-151]palace of Emania, which had been deserted before the coming of St.

[040-152]Patrick.

[040-153]The northern and western chiefs seem in this age to have made some

[040-154]improvements in military equipments, and tactics. _Cooey-na-gall_, a

[040-155]celebrated captain of the O'Kanes, is represented on his tomb at

[040-156]Dungiven as clad in complete armour—though that may be the fancy of the

[040-157]sculptor. Scottish gallowglasses—heavy-armed infantry, trained in

[040-158]Bruce's campaigns, were permanently enlisted in their service. Of their

[040-159]leaders the most distinguished were McNeil _Cam_, or the Crooked, and

[040-160]McRory, in the service of O'Conor, and McDonnell, McSorley, and

[040-161]McSweeney, in the service of O'Neil, O'Donnell, and O'Conor Sligo. The

[040-162]leaders of these warlike bands are called the Constables of Tyr-Owen,

[040-163]of North Connaught, or of Connaught, and are distinguished in all the

[040-164]warlike encounters in the north and west.

[040-166]The midland country—the counties now of Longford, West-Meath, Meath,

[040-167]Dublin, Kildare, King's and Queen's, were almost constantly in arms,

[040-168]during the latter half of this century. The lords of Annally,

[040-169]Moy-Cashel, Carbry, Offally, Ely, and Leix, rivalled each other in

[040-170]enterprise and endurance. In 1329, McGeoghegan of West-Meath defeated

[040-171]and slew Lord Thomas Butler, with the loss of 120 men at Mullingar; but

[040-172]the next year suffered an equal loss from the combined forces of the

[040-173]Earls of Ormond and Ulster; his neighbour, O'Farrell, contended with

[040-174]even better fortune, especially towards the close of Edward's reign

[040-175](1372), when in one successful foray he not only swept their garrisons

[040-176]out of Annally, but rendered important assistance to the insurgent

[040-177]tribes of Meath. In Leinster, the house of O'Moore, under Lysaght their

[040-178]Chief, by a well concerted conspiracy, seized in one night (in 1327) no

[040-179]less than eight castles, and razed the fort of Dunamase, which they

[040-180]despaired of defending. In 1346, under Conal O'Moore, they destroyed

[040-181]the foreign strongholds of Ley and Kilmehedie; and though Conal was

[040-182]slain by the English, and Rory, one of their creatures, placed in his

[040-183]stead, the tribe put Rory to death as a traitor in 1354, and for two

[040-184]centuries thereafter upheld their independence. Simultaneously, the

[040-185]O'Conors of Offally, and the O'Carrolls of Ely, adjoining and kindred

[040-186]tribes, so straightened the Earl of Kildare on the one hand, and the

[040-187]Earl of Ormond on the other, that a cess of 40 pence on every carucate

[040-188](140 acres) of tilled land, and of 40 pence on chattels of the value of

[040-189]six pounds, was imposed on all the English settlements, for the defence

[040-190]of Kildare, Carlow, and the marches generally. Out of the amount

[040-191]collected in Carlow, a portion was paid to the Earl of Kildare, "for

[040-192]preventing the O'Moores from burning the town of Killahan." The same

[040-193]nobleman was commanded, by an order in Council, to strengthen his

[040-194]Castles of Rathmore, Kilkea, and Ballymore, under pain of forfeiture.

[040-195]These events occurred in 1356, '7, and '8.

[040-196]In the south the same struggle for supremacy proceeded with much the

[040-197]same results. The Earl of Desmond, fresh from his Justiceship in

[040-198]Dublin, and the penal legislation of Kilkenny, was, in 1370, defeated

[040-199]and slain near Adare, by Brian O'Brien, Prince of Thomond, with several

[040-200]knights of his name, and "an indescribable number of others." Limerick

[040-201]was next assailed, and capitulated to O'Brien, who created Sheedy

[040-202]McNamara, Warden of the City. The English burghers, however, after the

[040-203]retirement of O'Brien, rose, murdered the new Warden, and opened the

[040-204]gates to Sir William de Windsor, the Lord Lieutenant, who had hastened

[040-205]to their relief. Two years later the whole Anglo-Irish force, under the

[040-206]fourth Earl of Kildare, was, summoned to Limerick, in order to defend

[040-207]it against O'Brien. So desperate now became the contest, that William

[040-208]de Windsor only consented to return a second time as Lord Lieutenant in

[040-209]1374, on condition that he was to act strictly on the defensive, and to

[040-210]receive annually the sum of 11,213 pounds 6 shillings 8 pence—a sum

[040-211]exceeding the whole revenue which the English King derived from Ireland

[040-212]at that period; which, according to Sir John Davies, fell short of

[040-213]11,000 pounds. Although such was the critical state of the English

[040-214]interest, this lieutenant obtained from the fears of successive

[040-215]Parliaments annual subsidies of 2,000 pounds and 3,000 pounds. The

[040-216]deputies from Louth having voted against his demand, were thrown into

[040-217]prison; but a direct petition from the Anglo-Irish to the King brought

[040-218]an order to de Windsor not to enforce the collection of these grants,

[040-219]and to remit in favour of the petitioners the scutage "on all those

[040-220]lands of which the Irish enemy had deprived them."

[040-221]In the last year of Edward III. (1376), he summoned the magnates and

[040-222]the burghers of towns to send representatives to 'London to consult

[040-223]with him on the state of the English settlements in Ireland. But those

[040-224]so addressed having assembled together, drew up a protest, setting

[040-225]forth that the great Council of Ireland had never been accustomed to

[040-226]meet out of that kingdom, though, saving the rights of their heirs and

[040-227]successors, they expressed their willingness to do so, for the King's

[040-228]convenience on that occasion. Richard Dene and William Stapolyn were

[040-229]first sent over to England to exhibit the evils of the Irish

[040-230]administration; the proposed general assembly of representatives seems

[040-231]to have dropped. The King ordered the two delegates just mentioned to

[040-232]be paid ten pounds out of the Exchequer for their expenses.

[040-233]The series of events, however, which most clearly exhibits the decay of

[040-234]the English interest, transpired within the limits of Leinster, almost

[040-235]within sight of Dublin. Of the actors in these events, the most

[040-236]distinguished for energy, ability, and good fortune, was Art McMurrogh,

[040-237]whose exploits are entitled to a separate and detailed account.
