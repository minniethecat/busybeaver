## Overview

Dublin (from the Irish *Dubh Linn*, "black pool") is the capital and largest city of Ireland. Originally a Norse (Viking) settlement established in the ninth century at the mouth of the River Liffey, Dublin became the center of English royal administration in Ireland following the Norman invasion and has remained the political, economic, and cultural heart of the nation ever since.

The city's history encompasses its Norse origins, its emergence as the seat of the Lordship of Ireland under the English Crown, its development as a Georgian capital of the [[protestant-ascendancy|Protestant Ascendancy]], and its eventual role as the capital of an independent Irish state.

## History

### Norse Dublin

Dublin was founded as a Norse longphort (ship fortress) around 841 AD. The Norse settlers developed it into a major trading center, linking Ireland with the Norse networks of the North Atlantic. The kingdom of Dublin remained a significant political and military power through the eleventh century, when [[brian-boru|Brian Boru]]'s victory at the [[battle-of-clontarf|Battle of Clontarf]] in 1014 broke its military ambitions. The Norse continued to inhabit Dublin as traders, and the city remained under Norse-Irish rule until the Norman invasion of 1169–1171.

### Anglo-Norman Dublin

After the Norman invasion, Dublin became the administrative capital of the Lordship of Ireland under [[henry-ii|Henry II]]. The Normans fortified the city with walls and a castle, and Dublin Castle became the seat of English royal government in Ireland. The city drew its first Anglo-Norman colony from Bristol and the Welsh marches. For the next seven centuries, Dublin would serve as the headquarters of English and later British administration in Ireland.

### Georgian and Modern Dublin

In the eighteenth century, Dublin underwent a transformation into one of the finest Georgian cities in Europe, with wide streets, elegant squares, and public buildings including the Parliament House (now the Bank of Ireland) and Trinity College. The Act of Union of 1800, which abolished the [[irish-parliament|Irish Parliament]], dealt a severe blow to Dublin's prestige and economy, as many of the Anglo-Irish aristocracy returned to London.

## Description

Dublin lies on the River Liffey, which flows through the city into Dublin Bay. The city's historic core is on the south bank of the river, centered on Dublin Castle, Christ Church Cathedral, and [[saint-patrick|St. Patrick]]'s Cathedral. The northern side developed primarily in the eighteenth century with the construction of Sackville Street (now [[daniel-oconnell|O'Connell]] Street) and the Custom House.

Key landmarks include:
- Dublin Castle, the seat of English and British administration for over 700 years
- Christ Church Cathedral, founded by the Norse king Sitric Silkenbeard and rebuilt by the Normans
- [[saint-patrick|St. Patrick]]'s Cathedral, associated with [[jonathan-swift|Jonathan Swift]]
- Trinity College, founded in 1592
- The Parliament House on College Green, now the Bank of Ireland

## Legacy

Dublin's dual heritage—Norse and Norman, Gaelic and English—makes it unique among European capitals. The city has been the stage for the most dramatic events of Irish history, from the arrival of [[henry-ii|Henry II]] to the Easter Rising of 1916. As the seat of the [[irish-parliament|Irish Parliament]], the center of the literary revival of Yeats and Joyce, and the capital of the modern Irish Republic, Dublin remains central to Irish identity and national life.
