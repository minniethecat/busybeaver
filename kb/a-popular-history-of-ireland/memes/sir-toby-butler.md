## Overview

Sir Toby Butler (Theobald Butler) was an Irish barrister and Jacobite who served as Solicitor-General for Ireland during the reign of James II. A leading figure in the Irish legal profession of the late seventeenth century, he was closely involved in the events of the Williamite War and the negotiation of the Treaty of Limerick in 1691. His reputation as a jurist endured long after the Jacobite defeat, and later Irish legal figures regarded him as one of the foundational masters of the Irish bar.

## Legal Career Under James II

Butler belonged to a cadet branch of the powerful Butler family of Ormond, but his career was built on legal ability rather than aristocratic connection. Following the accession of James II in 1685, the King's policy of replacing Protestant officeholders with Catholics opened new opportunities for qualified Catholic lawyers. Butler was appointed Solicitor-General for Ireland, one of the highest law offices in the kingdom.

His name appears in the war councils of the Jacobite administration. McGee records that the leading Jacobite figures included "Galmoy, Sir Toby Butler, and Colonels Purcell, Cusack, Dillon" (085-057), placing Butler among the inner circle of James's Irish government. These men directed the military and civil affairs of the Jacobite regime during the Williamite War of 1689–1691.

## The Treaty of Limerick

Butler's most significant historical contribution came at the end of the Williamite War. After the Jacobite defeat at Aughrim and the fall of Galway, the remaining Irish forces withdrew behind the walls of Limerick for a final stand. When both sides recognised that further bloodshed was futile, negotiations commenced. Butler was selected as one of the commissioners to negotiate the terms of surrender.

> Galmoy, Sir Toby Butler, and Colonels Purcell, Cusack, Dillon, and others were appointed on the part of the besieged to treat with the officers of the Earl of Marlborough. (085-057)

The resulting Treaty of Limerick, signed on 3 October 1691, contained both military articles — allowing Jacobite soldiers free passage to France — and civil articles promising religious toleration to Irish Catholics. Butler's legal expertise was instrumental in drafting and securing these civil articles, which became the foundation of Catholic hopes for the following century.

## Later Reputation

After the Jacobite defeat, Butler withdrew from public life but retained the respect of the Irish legal community. His name was invoked by subsequent generations as an exemplar of Irish legal achievement. McGee notes that among the great Irish lawyers, "there were great men before Malone, as before Agamemnon; such as Sir Toby Butler, Baron Rice, and Patrick Darcy" (111-139).

His connection to Anthony Malone, the celebrated eighteenth-century lawyer and Chancellor of the Exchequer, further cemented his legacy. Malone is described as "_confrere_ of Sir Toby Butler, and afterwards Chancellor of the Exchequer" (089-122), indicating the professional lineage that connected the Jacobite-era bar to the Hanoverian legal establishment. This continuity — from Butler through Malone — demonstrates how Catholic legal talent survived the penal era, preserving a tradition of Irish jurisprudence despite political exclusion.

## Assessment

Sir Toby Butler represents the Catholic professional class that briefly flourished under James II, only to be excluded from public life after the Williamite victory. His role in negotiating the Treaty of Limerick gave him a lasting place in Irish history, while his legal reputation survived the political defeat of his cause. Together with Patrick Darcy and John Keogh, he forms part of a tradition of Irish Catholic lawyers who advanced their community's rights through legal argument rather than armed resistance.
