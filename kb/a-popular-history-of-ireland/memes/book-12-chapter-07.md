CHAPTER VII.

[113-001]O'CONNELL'S LEADERSHIP—THE CATHOLIC ASSOCIATION—1821 TO 1826.

[113-002]At the beginning of the year 1821, O'Connell, during the intervals of

[113-003]his laborious occupations in court and on circuit, addressed a series

[113-004]of stirring letters to "the People of Ireland," remarkable as

[113-005]containing some of the best and most trenchant of his political

[113-006]writings. His object was to induce the postponement of the annual

[113-007]petition for Emancipation, and the substitution instead of a general

[113-008]agitation for Parliamentary reform, in conjunction with the English

[113-009]reformers. Against this conclusion—which he ridiculed "as the fashion

[113-010]for January, 1821"—Mr. Shiel published a bitter, clever, rhetorical

[113-011]reply, to which O'Connell at once sent forth a severe and rather

[113-012]contemptuous rejoinder. Shiel was quite content to have Mr. Plunkett

[113-013]continue Grattan's annual motion, with all its "conditions" and

[113-014]"securities." O'Connell declared he had no hope in petitions except

[113-015]from a reformed Parliament, and he, therefore, was opposed to such

[113-016]motions altogether, especially as put by Mr. Plunkett, and the other

[113-017]advocates of a Veto. Another session was lost in this controversy, and

[113-018]when Parliament rose, it was announced that George IV. was coming to

[113-019]Ireland "on a mission of Conciliation."

[113-021]On this announcement, Mr. O'Connell advised that the Catholics should

[113-022]take advantage of his Majesty's presence to assemble and consider the

[113-023]state of their affairs; but a protest against "connecting in any manner

[113-024]the King's visit with Catholic affairs," was circulated by Lords

[113-025]Fingal, Netterville, Gormanstown, and Killeen, Messrs. Baggott, Shiel,

[113-026]Wyse, and other Commoners. O'Connell yielded, as he often did, for the

[113-027]sake of unanimity. The King's visit led to many meetings and

[113-028]arrangements, in some of which his advice was taken, while in others he

[113-029]was outvoted or overruled. Nothing could exceed the patience he

[113-030]exhibited at this period of his life, when his natural impetuous

[113-031]temperament was still far from being subdued by the frosts of age.

[113-032]Many liberal Protestants at this period—the King's brief visit—were so

[113-033]moved with admiration of the judicious and proper conduct of the

[113-034]Catholic leaders, that a new but short-lived organization, called "the

[113-035]Conciliation Committee," was formed. The ultra Orange zealots, however,

[113-036]were not to be restrained even by the presence of the Sovereign for

[113-037]whom they professed so much devotion. In the midst of the preparations

[113-038]for his landing, they celebrated, with all its offensive

[113-039]accompaniments, the 12th of July, and at the Dublin dinner to the

[113-040]King—though after he had left the room—they gave their charter toast of

[113-041]"the glorious, pious, and immortal memory." The Committee of

[113-042]Conciliation soon dwindled away, and, like the visit of George IV.,

[113-043]left no good result behind.

[113-044]The year 1822 was most remarkable, at its commencement, for the arrival

[113-045]of the Marquis of Wellesley, as Lord-Lieutenant, and at its close, for

[113-046]the assault committed on him in the theatre by the Dublin Orangemen.

[113-047]Though the Marquis had declined to interfere in preventing the annual

[113-048]Orange celebration, he was well known to be friendly to the Catholics;

[113-049]their advocate, Mr. Plunkett, was his Attorney General; and many of

[113-050]their leaders were cordially welcomed at the Castle. These proofs were

[113-051]sufficient for the secret tribunals which sat upon his conduct, and

[113-052]when his Lordship presented himself, on the night of the 14th of

[113-053]December, at the theatre, he was assailed by an organized mob, one of

[113-054]whom flung a heavy piece of wood, and another a quart bottle, towards

[113-055]the state box. Three Orangemen, mechanics, were arrested and tried for

[113-056]the offence, but acquitted on a technical defect of evidence; a general

[113-057]feeling of indignation was excited among all classes in consequence,

[113-058]and it is questionable if Orangeism, in Dublin, ever recovered the

[113-059]disgust occasioned by that dastardly outrage.

[113-060]The great and fortunate event, however, for the Catholics, was the

[113-061]foundation of their new Association, which was finally resolved upon at

[113-062]an Aggregate Meeting held in "Townsend Street Chapel," on the 10th of

[113-063]May, 1823. This meeting had been called by an imposing requisition

[113-064]signed with singular unanimity by all the principal Catholic gentlemen.

[113-065]Lord Killeen presided. Mr. O'Connell moved the formation of the

[113-066]Association; Sir Thomas Esmonde seconded the motion; Mr. Shiel—lately

[113-067]and sincerely reconciled to O'Connell—sustained it. The plan was simple

[113-068]and popular. The Association was to consist of members paying a guinea

[113-069]a year, and associates paying a shilling; a standing committee was to

[113-070]form the government; the regular meetings were to be weekly—every

[113-071]Saturday; and the business to consist of organization, correspondence,

[113-072]public discussions, and petitions. It was, in effect, to be a sort of

[113-073]extern and unauthorized Parliament, acting always within the

[113-074]Constitution, with a view to the modification of the existing laws, by

[113-075]means not prohibited in those laws themselves. It was a design, subtle

[113-076]in conception, but simple in form; a natural design for a

[113-077]lawyer-liberator to form; and for a people strongly prepossessed in his

[113-078]favour to adopt; but one, at the same time, which would require a rare

[113-079]combination of circumstances to sustain for any great length of time,

[113-080]under a leader less expert, inventive, and resolute.

[113-081]The Parliamentary position of the Catholic question, at the moment of

[113-082]the formation of the Association, had undergone another strange

[113-083]alteration. Lord Castlereagh, having attained the highest honours of

[113-084]the empire, died by his own hand the previous year. Lord Liverpool

[113-085]remained Premier, Lord Eldon Chancellor, Mr. Canning became Foreign

[113-086]Secretary, with Mr. Peel, Home Secretary, the Duke of Wellington

[113-087]continuing Master-General of the Ordnance. To this cabinet, so largely

[113-088]anti-Catholic, the chosen organ of the Irish Catholics, Mr. Plunkett,

[113-089]was necessarily associated as Irish Attorney General. His situation,

[113-090]therefore, was in the session of 1823 one of great difficulty; this Sir

[113-091]Francis Burdett and the radical reformers at once perceived, and in the

[113-092]debates which followed, pressed him unmercifully. They quoted against

[113-093]him his own language denouncing cabinet compromises on so vital a

[113-094]question, in 1813, and to show their indignation, when he rose to

[113-095]reply, they left the House in a body. His speech, as always, was most

[113-096]able, but the House, when he sat down, broke into an uproar of

[113-097]confusion. Party spirit ran exceedingly high; the possibility of

[113-098]advancing the question during the session was doubtful, and a motion to

[113-099]adjourn prevailed. A fortnight later, at the first meeting of the

[113-100]Catholic Association, a very cordial vote of thanks to Plunkett was

[113-101]carried by acclamation.

[113-102]The new Catholic organization was labouring hard to merit popular

[113-103]favour. Within the year of its organization we find the Saturday

[113-104]meetings engaged with such questions as church rates; secret societies;

[113-105]correspondence with members of both Houses; voting public thanks to Mr.

[113-106]Brougham; the penal laws relating to the rights of sepulture; the

[113-107]purchase of a Catholic cemetery near Dublin; the commutation of tithes;

[113-108]the admission of Catholic freemen into corporations; the extension of

[113-109]the Association into every county in Ireland, and other more incidental

[113-110]subjects. The business-like air of the weekly meetings, at this early

[113-111]period, is remarkable: they were certainly anything but mere occasions

[113-112]for rhetorical display. But though little could be objected against,

[113-113]and so much might be said in favour of the labours of the Association,

[113-114]it was not till nearly twelve months after its organization, when

[113-115]O'Connell proposed and carried his system of monthly penny

[113-116]subscriptions to the "Catholic Rent," that it took a firm and

[113-117]far-reaching hold on the common people, and began to excite the serious

[113-118]apprehensions of the oligarchical factions in Ireland and England.

[113-119]This bold, and at this time much ridiculed step, infused new life and a

[113-120]system hitherto unknown into the Catholic population. The parish

[113-121]collectors, corresponding directly with Dublin, established a local

[113-122]agency, co-extensive with the kingdom; the smallest contributor felt

[113-123]himself personally embarked in the contest; and the movement became, in

[113-124]consequence, what it had not been before, an eminently popular one.

[113-125]During the next six months the receipts from penny subscriptions

[113-126]exceeded 100 pounds sterling per month, representing 24,000

[113-127]subscribers; during the next year they averaged above 500 pounds a

[113-128]week, representing nearly half a million enrolled Associates!

[113-129]With the additional means at the disposal of the Finance Committee of

[113-130]the Association, its power rose rapidly. A morning and an evening

[113-131]journal were at its command in Dublin; many thousands of pounds were

[113-132]expended in defending the people in the courts, and prosecuting their

[113-133]Orange and other enemies. Annual subsidies, of 5,000 pounds each, were

[113-134]voted for the Catholic Poor schools, and the education of missionary

[113-135]priests for America; the expenses of Parliamentary and electioneering

[113-136]agents were also heavy. But for all these purposes "the Catholic Rent,"

[113-137]of a penny per month from each associate, was found amply sufficient.

[113-138]At the close of 1824, the government, really alarmed at the formidable

[113-139]proportions assumed by the agitation, caused criminal informations to

[113-140]be filed against Mr. O'Connell, for an alleged seditious allusion to

[113-141]the example of Bolivar, the liberator of South America; but the Dublin

[113-142]grand jury ignored the bills of indictment founded on these

[113-143]informations. Early in the following session, however, a bill to

[113-144]suppress "Unlawful Associations in Ireland," was introduced by Mr.

[113-145]Goulburn, who had succeeded Sir Robert Peel as Chief Secretary, and was

[113-146]supported by Plunkett—a confirmed enemy of all extra-legal

[113-147]combinations. It was aimed directly at the Catholic Association, and

[113-148]passed both Houses; but O'Connell found means "to drive," as he said,

[113-149]"a coach and six through it." The existing Association dissolved on the

[113-150]passage of the act; another, called "the _New_ Catholic Association,"

[113-151]was formed for "charitable and other purposes," and the agitators

[113-152]proceeded with their organization, with one word added to their title,

[113-153]and immensely additional _éclat_ and success.

[113-154]In Parliament, the measure thus defeated was followed by another, the

[113-155]long-promised Relief Bill. It passed in the Commons in May, accompanied

[113-156]by two clauses, or as they were called, "wings," most unsatisfactory to

[113-157]the Catholic body. One clause disfranchised the whole class of electors

[113-158]known as the "forty-shilling freeholders;" the other provided a scale

[113-159]of state maintenance for the Catholic clergy. A bishop was to have

[113-160]1,000 pounds per annum; a dean 300 pounds; a parish priest 200 pounds;

[113-161]a curate 60 pounds. This measure was thrown out by the House of Lords,

[113-162]greatly to the satisfaction, at least, of the Irish Catholics. It was

[113-163]during this debate in the Upper House that the Duke of York,

[113-164]presumptive heir to the throne, made what was called his "ether

[113-165]speech"—from his habit of dosing himself with that stimulant on trying

[113-166]occasions. In this speech he declared, that so "help him God," he would

[113-167]never, never consent to acknowledge the claims put forward by the

[113-168]Catholics. Before two years were over, death had removed him to the

[113-169]presence of that Awful Being whose name he had so rashly invoked, and

[113-170]his brother, the Duke of Clarence, assumed his position, as next in

[113-171]succession to the throne.

[113-172]The Catholic delegates, Lord Killeen, Sir Thomas Esmonde, Lawless, and

[113-173]Shiel, were in London at the time the Duke of York made his memorable

[113-174]declaration. If, on the one hand, they were regarded with dislike

[113-175]amounting to hatred, on the other, they were welcomed with cordiality

[113-176]by all the leaders of the liberal party. The venerable Earl Fitzwilliam

[113-177]emerged from his retirement to do them honour; the gifted and energetic

[113-178]Brougham entertained them with all hospitality; at Norfolk House they

[113-179]were banqueted in the room in which George III. was born: the

[113-180]millionaire-demagogue Burdett, the courtly, liberal Lord Grey, and the

[113-181]flower of the Catholic nobility, were invited to meet them. The

[113-182]delegates were naturally cheered and gratified; they felt, they must

[113-183]have felt, that their cause had a grasp upon Imperial attention, which

[113-184]nothing but concession could ever loosen.

[113-185]Committees of both Houses, to inquire into the state of Ireland, had

[113-186]sat during a great part of this Session, and among the witnesses were

[113-187]the principal delegates, with Drs. Murray, Curtis, Kelly, and Doyle.

[113-188]The evidence of the latter—the eminent Prelate of Kildare and

[113-189]Leighlin—attracted most attention. His readiness of resource, clearness

[113-190]of statement, and wide range of information, inspired many of his

[113-191]questioners with a feeling of respect, such as they had never before

[113-192]entertained for any of his order. His writings had already made him

[113-193]honourably distinguished among literary men; his examination before the

[113-194]Committees made him equally so among statesmen. From that period he

[113-195]could reckon the Marquises of Anglesea and Wellesley, Lord Lansdowne

[113-196]and Mr. Brougham, among his correspondents and friends, and, what he

[113-197]valued even more, among the friends of his cause. Mr. O'Connell, on the

[113-198]other hand, certainly lost ground in Ireland by his London journey. He

[113-199]had, unquestionably, given his assent to both "wings," in 1825, as he

[113-200]did to the remaining one in 1828, and thereby greatly injured his own

[113-201]popularity. His frank and full recantation of his error, on his return,

[113-202]soon restored him to the favour of the multitude, and enabled him to

[113-203]employ, with the best effect, the enormous influence which he showed he

[113-204]possessed at the general elections of 1826. By him mainly the

[113-205]Beresfords were beaten in Waterford, the Fosters in Louth, and the

[113-206]Leslies in Monaghan. The independence of Limerick city, of Tipperary,

[113-207]Cork, Kilkenny, Longford, and other important constituencies, was

[113-208]secured. The parish machinery of the Association was found invaluable

[113-209]for the purpose of bringing up the electors, and the people's treasury

[113-210]was fortunately able to protect to some extent the fearless voter, who,

[113-211]in despite of his landlord, voted according to the dictates of his own

[113-212]heart.

[113-214]The effect of these elections on the empire at large was very great.

[113-215]When, early in the following spring, Lord Liverpool, after fifteen

[113-216]years' possession of power, died unexpectedly, George IV. sent for

[113-217]Canning and gave him _carte blanche_ to form a cabinet without

[113-218]excepting the question of Emancipation. That high spirited and really

[113-219]liberal statesman associated with himself a ministry, three-fourths of

[113-220]whom were in favour of granting the Catholic claims. This was in the

[113-221]month of April; but to the consternation of those whose hopes were now

[113-222]so justly raised, the gifted Premier held office only four months; his

[113-223]lamented death causing another "crisis," and one more postponement of

[113-224]"the Catholic question."
