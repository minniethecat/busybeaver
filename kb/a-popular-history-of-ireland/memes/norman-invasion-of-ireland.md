## Overview

The Norman Invasion of Ireland (1169–1172) was initiated by [[dermid-mcmurrogh|Dermid McMurrogh]] (Diarmait Mac Murchada), the deposed [[dermid-mcmurrogh|King of Leinster]], who recruited Norman-Welsh mercenaries to recover his kingdom. What began as a private military adventure escalated dramatically when [[strongbow|Richard de Clare]], known as [[strongbow|Strongbow]], succeeded to the lordship of [[leinster|Leinster]] and [[henry-ii|King Henry II of England]] intervened to assert royal authority over the Norman adventurers. (023-003)

Henry's arrival in October 1171 transformed the invasion into a royal enterprise, establishing the Lordship of Ireland under the English Crown—a political and legal structure that would endure for over 750 years. (026-015)

## Background

### Dermid McMurrogh's Appeal

[[dermid-mcmurrogh|Dermid McMurrogh]], [[dermid-mcmurrogh|King of Leinster]], was deposed by his Irish rivals and fled to [[henry-ii|Henry II]]'s court in Aquitaine seeking assistance. Henry, preoccupied with his continental domains, granted Dermid permission to recruit among his Norman-Welsh subjects. Dermid proceeded at once to seek the help he sorely needed upon the marches of Wales. (023-019)

### The Norman Adventurers

The Normans of south Wales—descendants of the conquerors of England—were experienced knights seeking new opportunities. Dermid obtained the support of several key figures, most importantly [[strongbow|Richard de Clare]], [[strongbow|Earl of Pembroke]], nicknamed [[strongbow|Strongbow]] for the strength of his arms. In the Irish annals he is usually called Earl Richard. (023-077) (023-080)

## The Invasion

### First Campaign (1169)

The first band of Norman adventurers, led by Robert FitzStephen, landed at Bannow Bay in Wexford in May 1169. They were soon joined by other contingents from south Wales. Operating in conjunction with Dermid's Irish forces, the Normans quickly captured Wexford town and began campaigning in [[leinster|Leinster]]. Their superior armour, tactics, and experience gave them a decisive advantage over the Irish forces they encountered. (023-039) (023-066)

### Second Campaign (1170)

In August 1170, [[strongbow|Strongbow]] himself landed near Waterford with a larger force. He captured Waterford by assault and there married Dermid's daughter Aoife (Eva), becoming Dermid's heir under Norman law. The combined force then marched on [[dublin|Dublin]], which fell to the invaders. (026-030)

### Henry II's Intervention

When Dermid died in 1171, [[strongbow|Strongbow]] claimed the lordship of [[leinster|Leinster]] in right of his wife. [[henry-ii|Henry II]], alarmed by the prospect of an independent Norman state in Ireland, determined to assert his royal authority. In October 1171, Henry landed at Waterford with a large army—the first English king to set foot on Irish soil. (026-009) (026-015)

[[strongbow|Strongbow]], alarmed by Henry's proclamation, submitted to the king. Henry received the submission of many Irish kings and, at the Council of Cashel in 1172, initiated reforms aligning the Irish Church with Roman practices. Before departing in April 1172, he granted Meath to [[hugh-de-lacy|Hugh de Lacy]] and established the rudiments of a royal administration. (026-021) (026-065)

## Aftermath

### The Lordship of Ireland

[[henry-ii|Henry II]]'s intervention established the Lordship of Ireland as a possession of the English Crown. His younger son John was designated Lord of Ireland, and when John later succeeded to the English throne, the Lordship became permanently attached to the Crown.

### The Anglo-Norman Settlement

The Anglo-Norman settlers—the FitzGeralds (Geraldines), Butlers, de Burghs ([[edmund-burke|Burke]]s), and others—established themselves as the dominant landowning class across large parts of [[leinster|Leinster]] and Munster. Over time, many of these families became "more Irish than the Irish themselves," adopting Irish language, law, and customs.

The invasion initiated over seven centuries of English involvement in Ireland. The legal and political structures established under [[henry-ii|Henry II]]—and the precedent of papal sanction through the bull *Laudabiliter*—provided the framework for subsequent assertions of English sovereignty over the island.
