## Overview

Sir Charles Coote (d. 1642) was an English-born military commander and one of the first Baronets of Ireland, who served the Dublin government during the outbreak of the Irish Rebellion of 1641.

As President of Connaught and a military commander in Leinster, Coote acquired a fearsome reputation for the severity of his operations against the Catholic population, becoming one of the most controversial figures of the early Confederate period.

## Background and Rise to Power

Sir Charles Coote, one of the first Baronets of Ireland, like Parsons, Boyle, Chichester, and other Englishmen, had come over to Ireland during the plantation period and established himself within the inner circle of English-born administrators who dominated the Dublin Castle government on the eve of the rebellion. (067-106 to 067-107)

When the insurrection broke out in October 1641, Coote was entrusted with the command of the Castle of Dublin and appointed to take command of the army alongside the Lords Justices. (067-100)

Coote was summoned from Carrick-on-Suir to take command of the army as the rising spread from Ulster into the other provinces. (067-102) The government placed extraordinary confidence in his military abilities, appointing him to one of the four provincial presidencies — in Leinster, Sir Charles Coote — alongside Lord Cork, who prepared to defend Munster. (067-245)

## The Wicklow Campaign and Atrocities

> In Wicklow, Coote's troopers committed murders such as had not been equalled since the days of the Pagan Northmen. Little children were carried aloft writhing on the pikes of these barbarians, whose worthy commander confessed that "he liked such frolics." (067-265 to 067-268)

From the earliest days of the rebellion, Coote's methods attracted sharp condemnation. Neither age nor sex was spared, and an ecclesiastic was especially certain of instant death. (067-268 to 067-269) Fathers Higgins and White of Naas, in Kildare, were given up by the soldiery to summary execution, illustrating the particular savagery reserved for Catholic clergy. (067-270)

> When summoned to account for these excesses, Coote was entrusted by the government, on all possible occasions, with a command distinct from that of Ormond. Having proclaimed the Lords of the Pale rebels for refusing to trust their persons within the walls of Dublin, Coote was employed during January to destroy Swords, their place of rendezvous, and to ravage the estates of their adherents in that neighbourhood. (068-077 to 068-082)

The Lords Justices' continued confidence in Coote, despite widespread knowledge of the atrocities committed by his troopers, strongly suggests that they approved of his severity as a deliberate policy of terror.

His conduct drew protests not only from the Catholic gentry but from certain Protestant observers who feared that indiscriminate violence was driving moderate Catholics into the arms of the Confederates. (068-080)

## Death and Assessment

The death of Sir Charles Coote, occurring in action in 1642, might have afforded the Catholics some consolation had it been followed by a change in the government's approach. (068-144) That it did not reflected the deeply rooted nature of the Puritan ascendancy in Dublin.

Coote's legacy — of unsparing military repression unleashed upon a civilian population — anticipated the far larger-scale atrocities that would follow under Oliver Cromwell and his successors. The Coote family remained influential in Irish affairs, with later members serving in the Williamite and Hanoverian administrations.
