## Overview

Lord Moore (Charles Moore, later styled Earl of Drogheda) was an Anglo-Irish nobleman and military commander who served the Crown during the Confederate Wars of the 1640s. As one of the principal commanders of the government forces in Leinster, he fought alongside Sir Henry Tichburne and General George Monk against the Irish insurgents.

He ultimately lost his life in battle against the forces of Owen Roe O'Neill in the ancient territory of Meath, becoming one of the notable Crown casualties of the Leinster campaign.

## The Outbreak of the 1641 Rebellion

When the Ulster rebellion broke out in October 1641, the government in Dublin was ill-prepared to meet the crisis. The Lords Justices Parsons and Borlase scrambled to organise a defence, calling upon the Anglo-Irish gentry and the remaining military resources of the Pale. Lord Moore was among those summoned to the capital.

In the confused weeks following the outbreak, the insurgent forces of Sir Phelim O'Neill moved southward from Ulster, threatening to join with the Catholic lords of the Pale. The government recalled the Earl of Ormond to Dublin, and "Sir Henry Tichburne and Lord Moore were despatched with a strong force, on the rear of the Ulster forces" (068-100). This mission aimed to cut the communications of the northern army and prevent a junction with the Meath insurgents.

## The Meath Campaign

The war in Meath during 1642–43 was characterised by rapid movements and shifting alliances. After the Pale lords joined the insurgent cause at the Hill of Crofty in December 1641, the strategic situation deteriorated sharply for the Dublin government. Owen Roe O'Neill, returning from service in the Spanish army of Flanders, assumed command of the Ulster forces and descended into the midlands.

> General Monk and Lord Moore were despatched against him, but reinforced by a considerable body of Meathian auxiliaries, O'Neil was enabled to give them battle. (069-123)

The encounter proved disastrous for the government forces. The Irish troops, fighting on familiar ground and reinforced by local supporters, overwhelmed the Crown detachment:

> Lord Moore, and a large portion of the assailants were slain, and Monk fled back to Dublin. O'Neil, gathering fresh strength from these encounters, pressed his advance southward. (069-130)

Moore's death was a significant blow to the government cause. As one of the few experienced commanders available to the Lords Justices, his loss weakened the defence of the Pale at a critical moment. General Monk — the future Duke of Albemarle and architect of the Restoration — narrowly escaped the same fate, fleeing to Dublin with the remnants of the defeated force.

## Association with Drogheda

Lord Moore's military career was closely connected with the town of Drogheda, a strategic port at the mouth of the Boyne that commanded the main road between Dublin and Ulster. His name appears frequently in the accounts of the defence of Drogheda, where the garrison under Tichburne held out against a prolonged siege by the Ulster forces. The Moore family, who held extensive lands in County Louth and were later created Earls of Drogheda, maintained their connection with the town into the eighteenth century.

## Historical Significance

Lord Moore exemplifies the Anglo-Irish military class that served the Crown during the Confederate Wars — men of local standing and local knowledge, commanding local forces in a conflict that was at once civil war, religious war, and national insurgency. His death in battle against Owen Roe O'Neill, one of the most capable Irish commanders of the period, places him among the substantial casualties of a war that would devastate Ireland for more than a decade.

The Moore family survived the upheavals of the seventeenth century and were subsequently elevated to the peerage as Earls of Drogheda, a title that recognised both their military service to the Crown and their enduring connection with one of the most important towns of the Pale.
