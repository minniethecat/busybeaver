## Overview

Saint Aidan (d. 651) is venerated as the Apostle of [[Northumbria]], the Irish missionary who, together with [[Saint Cuthbert]], brought Christianity to the great Anglo-Saxon kingdom that stretched from the Humber to the Pictish border.

Sent from [[Iona]] at the invitation of King Oswald, Aidan founded the monastery of [[Lindisfarne]] in 635, which became the spiritual heart of Northumbrian Christianity for generations. (006-094) (006-095)

McGee numbers Aidan among the most illustrious Irish saints of the seventh century, placing him in the company of [[Saint Columbkill]] (Apostle of the Picts) and [[Saint Cuthbert]], and describes him as a vigorous opponent of the slave trade who refused absolution to the King of Northumbria until Christian captives were freed. (008-176) (006-127) (006-128)

## Biography

### Irish Origins and Education

Aidan is believed to have been born in Ireland around the year 590. He received his monastic training at [[Iona]], the great monastery founded by [[Saint Columba]] half a century earlier, which had already become the foremost centre of Christian learning and missionary enterprise in the Celtic world.

From Iona, as McGee records, apostles had gone forth "to Iceland, to the Orkneys, to Northumbria, to Man, and to South Britain." (005-054) (005-055)

Iona in Aidan's time was a community of extraordinary spiritual discipline. The monks "neither thought of nor loved anything in this world," in the words of the Venerable Bede, and their rule of life combined manual labour with the study and transcription of the Sacred Scriptures. (005-064) It was from this rigorous formation that Aidan emerged as a missionary bishop. (005-062)

### Apostle of Northumbria

The conversion of the Saxons of the southern kingdoms had been accomplished by missionaries from France and Rome, but those of Northumbria, as McGee observes, "recognise as their Apostles St. Aidan and St.

Cuthbert, two Fathers from Iona." (006-092) (006-094) (006-095) King Oswald of Northumbria, who had spent his exile at Iona and been converted there, invited Aidan to undertake the mission to his people.

> "The Saxons of Kent and the Southern Kingdoms generally were converted by missionaries from France or Rome, or native preachers of the first or second Christian generation; those of Northumbria recognise as their Apostles St. Aidan and St. Cuthbert, two Fathers from Iona." (006-092) (006-093) (006-094) (006-095)

Upon his arrival in 635, Aidan established his episcopal see on the island of [[Lindisfarne]], also known as Holy Island, off the coast of Northumbria. From this base, which mirrored the monastic foundation of Iona, he travelled throughout the kingdom preaching the Gospel, supported by King Oswald who often served as his interpreter.

Aidan's gentle and ascetic manner won the hearts of the Northumbrian people, and the church he planted endured long after his death.

Like all missionaries who went out from Iona, Aidan taught that "to reduce Christians to slavery was wholly inconsistent with a belief in the doctrines of the Gospel." (006-125) (006-126) He demonstrated this conviction with fearless moral authority:

> "St. Aidan, the Apostle of Northumbria, had refused the late Egfrid's father absolution, on one occasion, until he solemnly promised to restore their freedom to certain captives of this description." (006-127) (006-128) (006-129)

His preaching against the slave trade set a precedent that was remembered centuries later. McGee notes that while "Christian morality, though it may have mitigated the horrors of this odious traffic, did not at once lead to its abolition," Saint Aidan had preached against it "long before" Saint Wulfstan did so in the South of England. (021-058) (021-059) (021-060) (021-061)

### The Easter Controversy

One of the defining controversies of the early Celtic Church concerned the correct computation of the date of Easter. Saint Patrick had introduced in the fifth century the computation then observed in Gaul, and to this custom many of the Irish doctors rigidly adhered "long after the rest of Christendom had agreed to adopt the Alexandrian computation." (008-208) (008-209) (008-210) (008-211)

Aidan stood with those who maintained the older Irish observance. McGee records that "great names were found on both sides of the controversy," and that Aidan was counted among those adhering "exactly to the rule of St. Patrick," alongside Columbanus and Finian.

(008-212) This was a question of discipline, not of faith, and "monks of the same Monastery and Bishops of the same Province maintained opposite opinions with equal ardour and mutual charity." (008-215) (008-216) (008-217)

Aidan's position in this controversy reflects the broader independence of the Celtic Church during this period. The issue was not fully resolved in Northumbria until the Synod of Whitby (664), more than a decade after Aidan's death, when the Roman computation was adopted.

### Death and Succession

Saint Aidan died in the year 651 at the royal town of Bamburgh. His death came just as the tides of war were turning against the Christian kingdom he had helped to build. According to Bede, he was leaning against the wooden buttress of the church when he expired, his last moments spent in prayer.

He was succeeded at Lindisfarne by a series of bishops who maintained the traditions he had established, including [[Saint Cuthbert]], who would become the most beloved saint of northern England. Cuthbert, though of Anglo-Saxon rather than Irish origin, perpetuated the ascetic spirituality that Aidan had brought from Iona.

## Legacy

Saint Aidan is remembered as the founder of the Church in Northumbria and one of the great missionary figures of the early medieval period. His monastery at Lindisfarne became a centre of learning and evangelisation that produced some of the finest manuscripts of the Anglo-Saxon age, including the famous Lindisfarne Gospels.

After his death, the Abbots of Iona exercised ecclesiastical jurisdiction over all the clergy north of the Humber, carrying forward the work that Aidan had begun. (005-116)

McGee lists Aidan among the most distinguished Irish saints of the seventh century, in the company of [[Saint Columbanus]] (Apostle of Burgundy and Lombardy), [[Saint Killian]] (Apostle of Franconia), and [[Saint Columbkill]] (Apostle of the Picts). (008-174) (008-175) (008-176) His feast day is celebrated on 31 August.

Aidan's fearless opposition to the enslavement of Christians, and his willingness to confront royal power in defence of the Gospel, established a model of pastoral integrity that influenced the Irish and Anglo-Saxon churches for generations. The king of Northumbria, as McGee writes, had been refused absolution "until he solemnly promised to restore their freedom to certain captives" ;

a testament to Aidan's moral authority. (006-127) (006-128) (006-129)
