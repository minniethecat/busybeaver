CHAPTER XII.

[099-001]THE ERA OF INDEPENDENCE—EFFECTS OF THE FRENCH REVOLUTION IN

[099-002]IRELAND—SECESSION OF GRATTAN, CURRAN, AND THEIR FRIENDS, FROM

[099-003]PARLIAMENT, IN 1797.

[099-004]The era of independence which we have desired to mark distinctly to the

[099-005]reader's mind, may be said to terminate in 1797, with the hopeless

[099-006]secession of Grattan and his friends from Parliament. Did the events

[099-007]within and without the House justify that extreme measure? We shall

[099-008]proceed to describe them as they arose, leaving the decision of the

[099-009]question to the judgment of the reader.

[099-010]The session of 1793, which extended into July, was, besides the

[099-011]Catholic Relief Bill, productive of other important results. Under the

[099-012]plea of the spread of French principles, and the widespread

[099-013]organization of seditious associations—a plea not wanting in

[099-014]evidence—an Arms Act was introduced and carried, prohibiting the

[099-015]importation of arms and gunpowder, and authorizing domiciliary visits,

[099-016]at any hour of the night or day, in search of such arms. Within a month

[099-017]from the passage of this bill, bravely but vainly opposed by Lord

[099-018]Edward Fitzgerald, and the opposition generally, the surviving

[099-019]Volunteer corps, in Dublin and its vicinity, were disbanded, their

[099-020]arms, artillery, and ammunition taken possession of either by force or

[099-021]negotiation, and the very wreck of that once powerful patriot army

[099-022]swept away. In its stead, by nearly the same majority, the militia were

[099-023]increased to 16,000 men, and the regulars from 12,000 to 17,000—thus

[099-024]placing at the absolute control of the Commander-in-Chief, and the

[099-025]chiefs of the oligarchy, a standing army of 33,000 men. At the same

[099-026]period, Lord Clare (he had been made an earl in 1792), introduced his

[099-027]Convention Act, against the assemblage in convention of delegates

[099-028]purporting to represent the people. With Grattan only 27 of the Commons

[099-029]divided against this measure, well characterized as "the boldest step

[099-030]that ever yet was made to introduce military government." "If this bill

[099-031]had been law," Grattan added, "the independence of the Irish

[099-032]Parliament, the emancipation of the Catholics, and even the English

[099-033]revolution of 1688, could never have taken place!" The teller in favour

[099-034]of the Convention Act was Major Wellesley, member for Trim, twenty

[099-035]years later—Duke of Wellington! It became and still remains the law of

[099-036]Ireland.

[099-037]Against this reactionary legislation we must credit the session of '93,

[099-038]besides the Catholic Relief Bill and the East India Trade Bill, with

[099-039]Mr. Grattan's Barren Lands Bill, exempting all newly reclaimed lands

[099-040]from the payment of tithes for a period of seven years; Mr. Forbes's

[099-041]Pension Bill, limiting the pension list to 80,000 pounds sterling per

[099-042]annum, and fixing the permanent civil list at 250,000 pounds per annum;

[099-043]and the excellent measure of the same invaluable member, excluding from

[099-044]Parliament all persons holding offices of profit under the crown,

[099-045]except the usual ministerial officers, and those employed in the

[099-046]_revenue service_. This last salvo was forced into the bill by the

[099-047]oligarchical faction, for whose junior branches the revenue had long

[099-048]been a fruitful source of provision.

[099-049]Parliament met next, on the 21st of January, '94, and held a short

[099-050]two-months' session. The most remarkable incidents of these two months

[099-051]were the rejection of Mr. George Ponsonby's annual motion for

[099-052]parliamentary reform, and the striking position taken by Grattan,

[099-053]Curran, and all but seven or eight of their friends, in favour of the

[099-054]war against the French republic. Mr. Ponsonby proposed, in the spirit

[099-055]of Flood's plan ten years earlier, to unite to the boroughs four miles

[099-056]square of the adjoining country, thus creating a counterpoise to the

[099-057]territorial aristocracy on the one hand, and the patrons of boroughs on

[099-058]the other; he also proposed to extend the suffrage to every tradesman

[099-059]who had served five years' apprenticeship, and gave each county _three_

[099-060]instead of two members, leaving intact, of course, the forty-shilling

[099-061]freehold franchise. Not more than 44 members, however, divided in

[099-062]favour of the new project, while 142 voted against it! Had it passed,

[099-063]the parliamentary history of the next six years could never have been

[099-064]written.

[099-065]It was on this Reform bill, and on the debate on the address, that

[099-066]Grattan took occasion to declare his settled and unalterable hostility

[099-067]to those "French principles," then so fashionable with all who called

[099-068]themselves friends of freedom, in the three kingdoms. In the great

[099-069]social schism which had taken place in Europe, in consequence of the

[099-070]French revolution of 1789-'91, those kingdoms, the favourite seat of

[099-071]free inquiry and free discussion, could not hope to escape. The effects

[099-072]were visible in every circle, among every order of men; in all the

[099-073]churches, workshops, saloons, professions, into which men were divided.

[099-074]Among publicists, most of all, the shock was most severely felt; in

[099-075]England it separated Burke and Windham from Fox, Erskine, Sheridan, and

[099-076]Grey; in Ireland it separated Grattan and Curran from Lord Edward

[099-077]Fitzgerald, Arthur O'Conor, Addis Emmet, Wolfe Tone, and all those

[099-078]ardent, able, and honest men, who hailed the French, as the forerunner

[099-079]of a complete series of European republics, in which Ireland should

[099-080]shine out, among the brightest and the best.

[099-081]Grattan, who agreed with and revered Burke, looked upon the

[099-082]"anti-Jacobin war," as a just and necessary war. It was not in his

[099-083]nature to do anything by halves, and he therefore cordially supported

[099-084]the paragraph in the address pledging Ireland's support to that war. He

[099-085]was a constitutionalist of the British, not of the French type. In the

[099-086]subsequent Reform debate he declared that he would always and ever

[099-087]resist those who sought to remodel the Irish constitution on a French

[099-088]original. He asserted, moreover, that great mischief had been already

[099-089]done by the advocates of such a design, "It"—this design—"has thrown

[099-090]back for the present the chance of any rational improvement in the

[099-091]representation of the people," he cried, "and has betrayed a good

[099-092]reform _to the hopes of a shabby insurrection_." Proceeding in his own

[099-093]condensed, crystalline antithesis, he thus enlarged on his own

[099-094]opinions: "There are two characters equally enemies to the reform of

[099-095]Parliament, and equally enemies to the government—the leveller of the

[099-096]constitution, and the friend of its abuses; they take different roads

[099-097]to arrive at the same end. The levellers propose to subvert the King

[099-098]and parliamentary constitution by a rank and unqualified democracy—the

[099-099]friends of its abuses propose to support the King and buy the

[099-100]Parliament, and in the end to overset both, by a rank and avowed

[099-101]corruption. They are both incendiaries; the one would destroy

[099-102]government to pay his court to liberty; the other would destroy liberty

[099-103]to pay his court to government; but the liberty of the one would be

[099-104]confusion, and the government of the other would be pollution."

[099-106]We can well understand that this language pleased as little the United

[099-107]Irishmen as the Castle. It was known that in private he was accustomed

[099-108]to say, that, "the wonder was not that Mr. Sheares should die on the

[099-109]scaffold, but that Lord Clare was not there beside him." He stood in

[099-110]the midst of the ways, crying aloud, with the wisdom of his age and his

[099-111]genius, but there were few to heed his warnings. The sanguine innovator

[099-112]sneered or pitied; the truculent despot scowled or menaced; to the one

[099-113]his authority was an impediment, to the other his reputation was a

[099-114]reproach. It was a public situation as full of conflict as man ever

[099-115]occupied, and we are not astonished, on a nearer view, that it led,

[099-116]after three years hoping against hope, to the despairing secession of

[099-117]1797.

[099-118]A bright gleam of better things shot for an instant across the gloomy

[099-119]prospect, with which the year '94 closed for the country. Lord

[099-120]Westmoreland was recalled, and Lord Fitzwilliam, largely connected with

[099-121]Ireland by property, and one of the most just and liberal men in

[099-122]England, was to be his successor. The highest expectations were

[099-123]excited; the best men congratulated each other on the certain promise

[099-124]of better times close at hand; and the nation, ever ready to believe

[099-125]whatever it wished to believe, saw in prospect, the oligarchy

[099-126]restrained, the patriots triumphant, and the unfinished fabric of

[099-127]independence completed, and crowned with honour.

[099-128]This new reign, though one of the shortest, was one of the most

[099-129]important Ireland ever saw. Lord Fitzwilliam, the nephew of Lord

[099-130]Rockingham, the first to acknowledge the constitution of 1782, had

[099-131]married a Ponsonby; he was a Burke whig—one of those who, with the Duke

[099-132]of Portland, Earl Spencer, and Mr. Windham, had followed the "great

[099-133]Edmund," in his secession from the Fox-and-Sheridan majority of that

[099-134]party, in 1791. Pitt, anxious to conciliate these new allies, had

[099-135]brought them all into office in 1794—Earl Fitzwilliam being placed in

[099-136]the dignified position of President of the Council. When spoken of for

[099-137]the Viceroyalty he wrote to Grattan, bespeaking his support, and that

[099-138]of "his friends, the Ponsonbys;" this letter and some others brought

[099-139]Grattan to London, where he had two or three interviews with Pitt, the

[099-140]Duke of Portland, and Lord Fitzwilliam. Better still, he made a

[099-141]pilgrimage to Beaconsfield, and had the benefit of the last advice of

[099-142]the aged Burke. With Pitt he was disappointed and dissatisfied, but he

[099-143]still hoped and expected great good from the appointment of Lord

[099-144]Fitzwilliam to the office of Viceroy. It seems to have been fully

[099-145]understood that the new Lord Lieutenant would have very full powers to

[099-146]complete the gracious work of Catholic emancipation: with this express

[099-147]understanding, Mr. Grattan was pressed to accept the Chancellorship of

[099-148]the Exchequer, but steadily declined; he upheld in that position Sir

[099-149]Henry Parnell, an old personal, rather than political friend, one of a

[099-150]family of whom Ireland has reason to retain a grateful recollection. He

[099-151]was, however, with Ponsonby, Curran, and others of his friends in both

[099-152]Houses, added to the Privy Council, where they were free to shape the

[099-153]measures of the new administration. At the King's levee, on the 10th of

[099-154]December, when Lord Fitzwilliam was sworn in, the aged Burke, in deep

[099-155]mourning for his idolized son, attended; Grattan was so much spoken to

[099-156]by the King as to draw towards him particular attention; Mr. Pitt, the

[099-157]Duke of Portland, and other ministers, were present. All took and held

[099-158]the tone that complete emancipation was a thing settled: Burke

[099-159]congratulated Grattan on the event, and the new Viceroy was as jubilant

[099-160]and as confident as anybody, that the great controversy was at length

[099-161]to be finally closed under his auspices.

[099-162]On the 4th of January, Lord Fitzwilliam reached Dublin; and on the 25th

[099-163]of March he was recalled. The history of these three months—of this

[099-164]short-lived attempt to govern Ireland on the advice of Grattan—is full

[099-165]of instruction. The Viceroy had not for a moment concealed his

[099-166]intention of thoroughly reforming the Irish administration. On his

[099-167]arrival at the Castle, Mr. Cooke was removed from the Secretaryship,

[099-168]and Mr. Beresford from the Revenue Board. Great was the consternation,

[099-169]and unscrupulous the intrigues of the dismissed. When the Parliament

[099-170]met at the end of January, Grattan assumed the leadership of the House

[099-171]of Commons, and moved the address in answer to the speech from the

[099-172]throne. No opposition was offered—and it passed without a division.

[099-173]Immediately, a bill granting the Catholics complete

[099-174]emancipation—rendering them eligible even to the office of Chancellor,

[099-175]withheld in 1829—was introduced by Grattan. Then the oligarchy found

[099-176]their voices. The old cry of "the Church in danger" was raised,

[099-177]delegations proceeded to London, and every agency of influence was

[099-178]brought to bear on the King and the English cabinet. From the tenor of

[099-179]his letters, Lord Fitzwilliam felt compelled in honour to tell Mr.

[099-180]Pitt, that he might choose between him and the Beresfords. He did

[099-181]choose—but not till the Irish Parliament, in the exuberance of its

[099-182]confidence and gratitude, had voted the extraordinary subsidy of 20,000

[099-183]men for the navy, and _a million, eight hundred thousand pounds,

[099-184]towards the expenses of the war with France!_ Then, the popular Viceroy

[099-185]was recalled amid the universal regrets of the people. The day of his

[099-186]departure from Dublin was a day of general mourning, except with the

[099-187]oligarchical clique, whose leaders he had so resolutely thrust aside.

[099-188]To them it was a day of insolent and unconcealed rejoicing; and, what

[099-189]is not at all uncommon under such circumstances, the infatuated

[099-190]partisans of the French revolution, rejoiced hardly less than the

[099-191]extremest Tories, at the sudden collapse of a government equally

[099-192]opposed to the politics of both. Grattan, than whom no public man was

[099-193]ever more free from unjust suspicion of others, always remained under

[099-194]the conviction that Pitt had made merely a temporary use of Lord

[099-195]Fitzwilliam's popularity, in order to cheat the Irish out of the

[099-196]immense supplies they had voted; and all the documents of the day,

[099-197]which have since seen the light, accord well with that view of the

[099-198]transaction. Lord Fitzwilliam was immediately replaced by Lord Camden,

[099-199]whose Viceroyalty extended into the middle of the year 1798: a reign

[099-200]which embraced all that remains to us to narrate, of the Parliamentary

[099-201]politics of the era of Independence.

[099-202]The sittings of Parliament were resumed during April, May, and June,

[099-203]but the complete emancipation bill was rejected three to one—155 to 55;

[099-204]the debates were now marked, on the part of Toler, Duigenan, Johnson,

[099-205]and others, with the most violent anti-Catholic spirit. All this tended

[099-206]to inflame still more the exasperated feeling which already prevailed

[099-207]in the country between Orangemen and Defenders. Thus it came, that the

[099-208]High Court of Parliament, which ought to have been the chief school of

[099-209]public wisdom—the calm correcting tribunal of public opinion—was made a

[099-210]principal engine in the dissemination of those prejudices and passions,

[099-211]which drove honest men to despair of constitutional redress, and

[099-212]swelled the ranks of the secret political societies, till they became

[099-213]co-extensive with the population.

[099-214]The session of 1796 was even more hopeless than the immediately

[099-215]preceding one. A trade motion of Grattan's on the address commanded

[099-216]only 14 votes out of 140; in the next session his motion in favour of

[099-217]equal rights to persons of all religious creeds, obtained but 12 votes

[099-218]out of 160! From these figures it is clear that above a third of the

[099-219]members of the House no longer attended; that of those who did attend,

[099-220]the overwhelming and invariable majority—ten to one—were for all the

[099-221]measures of repression and coercion which marked these two sessions.

[099-222]The Insurrection Act, giving power to the magistrates of any county to

[099-223]proclaim martial law; the Indemnity Act, protecting magistrates from

[099-224]the consequences of exercising "a vigour beyond the law;" the Riot Act,

[099-225]giving authority to disperse any number of persons by force of arms

[099-226]without notice; the Suspension of the _habeas corpus_ (against which

[099-227]only 7 members out of a House of 164 voted)—all were evidences to

[099-228]Grattan, that the usefulness of the House of Commons, as then

[099-229]constituted, was, for the tune, lost or destroyed. It is quite clear

[099-230]that he came to this conviction slowly and reluctantly; that he

[099-231]struggled against it with manly fortitude through three sessions; that

[099-232]he yielded to it at length, when there was no longer a possibility of

[099-233]resistance,—when to move or to divide the House, had become a wretched

[099-234]farce, humiliating to the country, and unworthy of his own earnest and

[099-235]enthusiastic patriotism.

[099-236]Under these circumstances, the powerless leader and his devoted staff

[099-237]resolved to withdraw, formally and openly, from further attendance on

[099-238]the House of Commons. The deplorable state of the country, delivered

[099-239]over to an irresponsible magistracy and all the horrors of martial law;

[099-240]the spread among the patriotic rising generation of French principles;

[099-241]the scarcely concealed design of the Castle to goad the people into

[099-242]insurrection, in order to deprive them of their liberties; all

[099-243]admonished the faithful few that the walls of Parliament were no longer

[099-244]their sphere of usefulness. One last trial was, however, made in May,

[099-245]1797, for a reform of Parliament. Mr. George Ponsonby moved his usual

[099-246]motion, and Curran, Hardy, Sir Lawrence Parsons, Charles Kendall Bushe,

[099-247]and others, ably supported him. The division was 30 to 117. It was on

[099-248]this debate, that Grattan, whose mournful manner contrasted so strongly

[099-249]with his usual enthusiasm, concluded a solemn exposition of the evils

[099-250]the administration were bringing on the country, by these affecting

[099-251]words:—"We have offered you our measure—you will reject it; we

[099-252]deprecate yours—you will persevere; having no hopes left to persuade or

[099-253]to dissuade, and having discharged our duty, we shall trouble you no

[099-254]more, _and after this day shall not attend the House of Commons_." The

[099-255]secession thus announced was accomplished; at the general election, two

[099-256]months later, Grattan and his colleague, Lord Henry Fitzgerald, refused

[099-257]to stand again for Dublin; Curran, Lord Edward Fitzgerald, Arthur

[099-258]O'Conor, and others, followed his example. A few patriots, hoping

[099-259]against hope, were, however, returned, a sort of forlorn hope, to man

[099-260]the last redoubt of the Constitution. Of these was William Conyngham

[099-261]Plunkett, member for Charlemont, Grattan's old borough, a

[099-262]constitutionalist of the school of Edmund Burke, worthy to be named

[099-263]among the most illustrious of his disciples.

[099-264]In the same July, on the 7th of the month, on which the Irish elections

[099-265]were held, that celebrated Anglo-Irish statesman expired at

[099-266]Beaconsfield, in the sixty-seventh year of his age. His last

[099-267]thoughts—his last wishes, like his first—were with his native land. His

[099-268]regards continued fixed on the state of Ireland, while vision and

[099-269]faculty remained. His last efforts in writing and conversation were to

[099-270]plead for toleration, concession and conciliation towards Ireland. The

[099-271]magisterial gravity of Burke was not calculated to permit him to be

[099-272]generally popular with an impulsive people, but as years roll on, and

[099-273]education extends its dominion, his reputation rises and brightens

[099-274]above every other reputation of his age, British or Irish. Of him no

[099-275]less truly than powerfully did Grattan say in the Imperial Parliament,

[099-276]in 1815: "He read everything, he saw everything, he foresaw everything.

[099-277]His knowledge of history amounted to a power of foretelling; and when

[099-278]he perceived the wild work that was doing in France, that great

[099-279]political physician, intelligent of symptoms, distinguished between the

[099-280]access of fever and the force of health; and what other men conceived

[099-281]to be the vigour of her constitution, he knew to be no more than the

[099-282]paroxysm of her madness; and then, prophet-like, he denounced the

[099-283]destinies of France, and in his prophetic fury, admonished nations."
