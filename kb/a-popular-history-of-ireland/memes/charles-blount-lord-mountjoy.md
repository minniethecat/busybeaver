## Overview

Charles Blount, Lord Mountjoy (1563–1606) was the English Lord Deputy who brought the Nine Years War to its decisive conclusion. Arriving in Ireland in February 1600, Mountjoy succeeded the disastrous Earl of Essex and demonstrated a military competence and strategic vision that his predecessor had entirely lacked. His energetic twofold campaign against [[ulster|Ulster]], his systematic encirclement of [[Hugh ONeill]] with fortified garrisons, and his relentless pursuit of the war through the difficult terrain of the Irish midlands and north ultimately broke the back of Gaelic resistance. (060-231) (061-005)

Mountjoy was "one of a class of soldier-statesmen, peculiar to the second half of Elizabeth's reign, who affected authorship and the patronage of letters as a necessary complement to the manners of a courtier and commander." His combination of personal courage, judgment, and administrative ability made him the most effective English governor in Ireland since the Tudor conquest began. (060-231)

## Biography

### Early Career and Appointment

Charles Blount was born into a noble family and rose to prominence during the latter part of Elizabeth's reign. He had been the rival, the enemy, and ultimately the devoted friend of the unfortunate Robert Devereux, Earl of Essex, whom he "equalled in personal gifts, in courage, and in gallantry, but far exceeded in judgment, firmness, and foresight." After Essex's failed campaign in Ireland and subsequent disgrace, Mountjoy was appointed Lord Deputy. (060-231)

> "A new Deputy had landed at Howth on the 24th of February, 1600, and was sworn in at Dublin the day following. This was Charles Blount, Lord Mountjoy, afterwards Earl of Devonshire, a nobleman now in his 37th year." (060-231)

### The Campaign Against Ulster

Mountjoy's strategy for defeating the Irish confederacy was as ambitious as it was methodical. He pressed forward the twofold operations against [[ulster|Ulster]] that Essex had neglected. In May 1600, a fleet carrying 4,000 foot and 200 horse under Sir Henry Dowcra arrived in Lough Foyle with stores, building materials, and ordnance. Simultaneously, Mountjoy forced the Moira pass and made a feigned demonstration against [[armagh|Armagh]] to draw attention from the landing. (061-005)

The lodgment at Derry was secured, and Dowcra fortified Culmore at the harbour entrance and seized the ancient fort of Aileach at the head of Lough Swilly. Mountjoy retired to [[dublin|Dublin]] after increasing garrisons at Newry, Carlingford, and Dundalk. By the end of the campaign season, the position of English garrisons across [[ulster|Ulster]] formed a tightening cordon against the O'Neill heartland. (061-005) (061-197)

> "The twofold operations against Ulster, neglected by Essex, were vigorously pressed forward by the energetic Mountjoy." (061-005)

### Fortifications and the Encirclement of Tyrone

Mountjoy constructed a chain of strong forts that gave him permanent access to the field of battle. He raised a strong fort at the northern outlet of the Moira pass, which he named Mount Norris after his late master in the art of war. This work, strongly built and manned, gave him "the free entree of the field of battle whenever he chose to take it." (061-169)

In the summer of 1601, Mountjoy advanced into the Blackwater valley, menaced O'Neill's castle of Benburb, and left Sir Charles Danvers with a garrison in [[armagh|Armagh]]. Later that summer he laid the foundation of Charlemont fort, named after himself, on the Blackwater. (061-183)

By the end of the 1601 campaign season, Mountjoy's grip on [[ulster|Ulster]] was formidable:

### The Ormond Affair and Retribution

One of the most dramatic episodes of Mountjoy's early administration was the capture of the Earl of Ormond by the O'Moores in April 1600. Ormond was taken prisoner at Corroneduff during an interview with Owny O'Moore and the Jesuit Father Archer. He remained a prisoner from April to June and was received by Archer into the Catholic Church. (060-262)

On Ormond's liberation, Mountjoy took swift vengeance. The lands of Leix were cruelly ravaged, and the chief Owny, son of Rory O'Moore, was killed by a musket shot while attacking Maryborough on 17 August. (060-262)

> "The fair, well-fenced, and well-cultivated land of Leix was cruelly ravaged immediately after Ormond's release—the common soldiers cut down with their swords corn to the value of 10,000 pounds and upwards." (060-262)

## The Irish Context

Mountjoy was one of the great English adversaries whom the Gaelic and Anglo-Irish lords had to confront during the Tudor conquest. The Chieftain-Earls of the period were caught between the requirements of the English Court and the demands of their own clans—"distrusted at Court if they inclined to the people, detested by the people if they leaned towards the Court." The chiefs were "pitted at home against the Sydneys, Mountjoys, Chichesters, and Straffords," and the lessons of Machiavelli found apt scholars in the halls of Dungannon. (051-119)

## Legacy

Mountjoy's victory over [[hugh-oneill|Hugh O'Neil]]l at the Battle of Kinsale in December 1601, followed by O'Neill's eventual submission at Mellifont in March 1603, brought the Nine Years War to an end just days after [[elizabeth-i|Elizabeth I]] died. The war that had consumed English resources for nearly a decade was concluded by a Lord Deputy who combined military skill with strategic patience.

His administration demonstrated the effectiveness of what became the standard English approach to Irish warfare: simultaneous amphibious operations, permanent fortified garrisons deep in Gaelic territory, and the relentless application of overwhelming force. Though Mountjoy himself died in 1606, only three years after his triumph, the pattern he established would be followed by English commanders in Ireland for generations.
