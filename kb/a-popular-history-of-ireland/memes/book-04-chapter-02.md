CHAPTER II.

[024-001]THE ARMS, ARMOUR AND TACTICS OF THE NORMANS AND IRISH.

[024-002]This would seem to be the proper place to point out the peculiarities

[024-003]in arms, equipment, and tactics, which gave the first Normans those

[024-004]military advantages over the Irish and Dano-Irish, which they had

[024-005]hitherto maintained over the Saxons, Welsh and Scots. In instituting

[024-006]such a comparison, we do not intend to confine it strictly to the age

[024-007]of Strongbow and Dermid; the description will extend to the entire

[024-008]period from the arrival of Fitzstephen to the death of Richard, Earl of

[024-009]Ulster—from 1169 to 1333—a period of five or six generations, which we

[024-010]propose to treat of in the present book. After this Earl's decease, the

[024-011]Normans and Irish approximated more closely in all their customs, and

[024-012]no longer presented those marked contrasts which existed in their

[024-013]earlier intercourse and conflicts with each other. The armour of the

[024-014]first adventurers, both for man and horse, excited the wonder, the

[024-015]sarcasms, and the fears of the Irish. No such equipments had yet been

[024-016]seen in that country, nor indeed in any other, where the Normans were

[024-017]still strangers. As the Knights advanced on horseback, in their metal

[024-018]coating, they looked more like iron cylinders filled with flesh and

[024-019]blood, than like lithe and limber human combatants. The man-at-arms,

[024-020]whether Knight or Squire, was almost invariably mounted; his war-horse

[024-021]was usually led, while he rode a hackney, to spare the _destrier_. The

[024-022]body armour was a hauberk of netted iron or steel, to which were joined

[024-023]a hood, sleeves, breeches, hose and sabatons, or shoes, of the same

[024-024]material. Under the hauberk was worn a quilted gambeson of silk or

[024-025]cotton, reaching to the knees; over armour, except when actually

[024-026]engaged, all men of family wore costly coats of satin, velvet, cloth of

[024-027]gold or cloth of silver, emblazoned with their arms. The shields of the

[024-028]thirteenth century were of triangular form, pointed at the bottom; the

[024-029]helmet conical, with or without bars; the beaver, vizor and plate

[024-030]armour, were inventions of a later day. Earls, Dukes, and Princes, wore

[024-031]small crowns upon their helmets; lovers wore the favours of their

[024-032]mistresses; and victors the crests of champions they had overthrown.

[024-033]The ordinary weapons of these cavaliers were sword, lance, and knife;

[024-034]the demi-launce, or light horsemen, were similarly armed; and a force

[024-035]of this class, common in the Irish wars, was composed of mounted

[024-036]cross-bow men, and called from the swift, light _hobbies_ they rode,

[024-037]Hobiler-Archers. Besides many improvements in arms and manual exercise,

[024-038]the Normans perfected the old Roman machines and engines used in

[024-039]sieges. The scorpion was a huge cross-bow, the catapults showered

[024-040]stones to a great distance; the ballista discharged flights of darts

[024-041]and arrows. There were many other varieties of stone-throwing

[024-042]machinery; "the war-wolf" was long the chief of projectile machines, as

[024-043]the ram was of manual forces. The power of a battering-ram of the

[024-044]largest size, worked by a thousand men, has been proven to be equal to

[024-045]a point-blank shot from a thirty-six pounder. There were moveable

[024-046]towers of all sizes and of many names: "the sow" was a variety which

[024-047]continued in use in England and Ireland till the middle of the

[024-048]seventeenth century. The divisions of the cavalry were: first, the

[024-049]_Constable's_ command, some twenty-five men; next, the _Banneret_ was

[024-050]entitled to unfurl his own colours with consent of the Marshal, and

[024-051]might unite under his pennon one or more constabularies; the _Knight_

[024-052]led into the field all his retainers who held of him by feudal tenure,

[024-053]and sometimes the retainers of his squires, wards, or valets, and

[024-054]kinsmen. The laws of chivalry were fast shaping themselves into a code

[024-055]complete and coherent in all its parts, when these iron-clad, inventive

[024-056]and invincible masters of the art of war first entered on the invasion

[024-057]of Ireland.

[024-059]The body of their followers in this enterprise, consisting of Flemish,

[024-060]Welsh, and Cornish archers, may be best described by the arms they

[024-061]carried. The irresistible cross-bow was their main reliance. Its shot

[024-062]was so deadly that the Lateran Council, in 1139, strictly forbade its

[024-063]employment among Christian enemies. It combined with its stock, or bed,

[024-064]wheel, and trigger, almost all the force of the modern musket, and

[024-065]discharged square pieces of iron, leaden balls, or, in scarcity of

[024-066]ammunition, flint stones. The common cross-bow would kill, point blank,

[024-067]at forty or fifty yards distance, and the best improved at fully one

[024-068]hundred yards. The manufacture of these weapons must have been

[024-069]profitable, since their cost was equal, in the relative value of money,

[024-070]to that of the rifle, in our times. In the reign of Edward II. each

[024-071]cross-bow, purchased for the garrison of Sherborne Castle, cost 3

[024-072]shillings and 8 pence; and every hundred of _quarrels_—the ammunition

[024-073]just mentioned—1 shilling and 6 pence. Iron, steel, and wood, were the

[024-074]materials used in the manufacture of this weapon.

[024-075]The long-bow had been introduced into England by the Normans, who are

[024-076]said to have been more indebted to that arm than any other, for their

[024-077]victory at Hastings. To encourage the use of the long-bow many statutes

[024-078]were passed, and so late as the time of the Stuarts, royal commissions

[024-079]were issued for the promotion of this national exercise. Under the

[024-080]early statutes no archer was permitted to practise at any standing mark

[024-081]at less than "eleven score yards distant;" no archer under twenty-four

[024-082]years of age was allowed to shoot twice from the same stand-point;

[024-083]parents and masters were subject to a fine of 6 shillings and 8 pence

[024-084]if they allowed their youth, under the age of seventeen, "to be without

[024-085]a bow and two arrows for one month together;" the walled towns were

[024-086]required to set up their butts, to keep them in repair, and to turn out

[024-087]for target-practice on holidays, and at other convenient times. Aliens

[024-088]residing in England were forbidden the use of this weapon—a jealous

[024-089]precaution showing the great importance attached to its possession. The

[024-090]usual length of the bow—which was made of yew, witch-hazel, ash, or

[024-091]elm—was about six feet; and the arrow, about half that length. Arrows

[024-092]were made of ash, feathered with part of a goose's wing, and barbed

[024-093]with iron or steel. In the reign of Edward III., a painted bow cost 1

[024-094]shilling and 6 pence, a white bow, 1 shilling; a sheaf of steel-tipped

[024-095]arrows (24 to the sheaf), 1 shilling and 2 pence, and a sheaf of _non

[024-096]accerata_ (the blunt sort), 1 shilling. The range of the long-bow, at

[024-097]its highest perfection, was, as we have seen, "eleven score yards,"

[024-098]more than double that of the ordinary cross-bow. The common sort of

[024-099]both these weapons carried about the same distance—nearly 100 yards.

[024-100]The natural genius of the Normans for war had been sharpened and

[024-101]perfected by their campaigns in France and England, but more especially

[024-102]in the first and second Crusades. All that was to be learned of

[024-103]military science in other countries—all that Italian skill, Greek

[024-104]subtlety, or Saracen invention could teach, they knew and combined into

[024-105]one system. Their feudal discipline, moreover, in which the youth who

[024-106]entered the service of a veteran as page, rose in time to the rank of

[024-107]esquire and bachelor-at-arms, and finally won his spurs on some

[024-108]well-contested field, was eminently favourable to the training and

[024-109]proficiency of military talents. Not less remarkable was the skill they

[024-110]displayed in seizing on the strong and commanding points of

[024-111]communication within the country, as we see at this day, from the sites

[024-112]of their old Castles, many of which must have been, before the

[024-113]invention of gunpowder, all but impregnable.

[024-114]The art of war, if art it could in their case be called, was in a much

[024-115]less forward stage among the Irish in the twelfth and thirteenth

[024-116]centuries than amongst the Normans. Of the science of fortification

[024-117]they perhaps knew no more than they had learned in their long struggle

[024-118]with the Danes and Norwegians. To render roads impassable, to

[024-119]strengthen their islands by stockades, to hold the naturally difficult

[024-120]passes which connect one province or one district with another—these

[024-121]seem to have been their chief ideas of the aid that valour may derive

[024-122]from artificial appliances. The fortresses of which we hear so

[024-123]frequently, during and after the Danish period, and which are

[024-124]erroneously called _Danes'-forts_, were more numerous than formidable

[024-125]to such enemies as the Normans. Some of these earth-and-stone-works are

[024-126]older than the Milesian invasion, and of Cyclopean style and strength.

[024-127]Those of the Milesians are generally of larger size, contain much more

[024-128]earth, and the internal chambers are of less massive masonry. They are

[024-129]almost invariably of circular form, and the largest remaining specimens

[024-130]are the Giant's Ring, near Belfast; the fort at Netterville, which

[024-131]measures 300 paces in circumference round the top of the embankment;

[024-132]the Black Rath, on the Boyne, which measures 321 paces round the outer

[024-133]wall of circumvallation; and the King's Rath, at Tara, upwards of 280

[024-134]in length. The height of the outer embankment in forts of this size

[024-135]varied from fifteen to twenty feet; this embankment was usually

[024-136]surrounded by a fosse; within the embankment there was a platform,

[024-137]depressed so as to leave a circular parapet above its level. Many of

[024-138]these military raths have been found to contain subterranean chambers

[024-139]and circular winding passages, supposed to be used as granaries and

[024-140]armories. They are accounted capable of containing garrisons of from

[024-141]200 to 500 men; but many of the fortresses mentioned from age to age in

[024-142]our annals were mere private residences, enclosing within their outer

[024-143]and inner walls space enough for the immediate retainers and domestics

[024-144]of the chief. Although coats of mail are mentioned in manuscripts long

[024-145]anterior to the Norman invasion, the Irish soldiers seem seldom or

[024-146]never to have been completely clothed in armour. Like the northern

[024-147]_Berserkers_, they prided themselves in fighting, if not naked, in

[024-148]their orange coloured shirts, dyed with saffron. The helmet and the

[024-149]shield were the only defensive articles of dress; nor do they seem to

[024-150]have had trappings for their horses. Their favourite missile weapon was

[024-151]the dart or javelin, and in earlier ages the sling. The spear or lance,

[024-152]the sword, and the sharp, short-handled battle-axe, were their

[024-153]favourite manual weapons. Their power with the battle-axe was

[024-154]prodigious; _Giraldus_ says they sometimes lopped off a horseman's leg

[024-155]at a single blow, his body falling over on the other side. Their

[024-156]bridle-bits and spurs were of bronze, as were generally their spear

[024-157]heads and short swords. Of siege implements, beyond the torch and the

[024-158]scaling-ladder, they seem to have had no knowledge, and to have desired

[024-159]none. The Dano-Irish alone were accustomed to fortify and defend their

[024-160]towns, on the general principles, which then composed the sum of what

[024-161]was known in Christendom of military engineering. Quick to acquire in

[024-162]almost every department of the art, the native Irish continued till the

[024-163]last obstinately insensible to the absolute necessity of learning how

[024-164]modern fortifications are constructed, defended, and captured; a

[024-165]national infatuation, of which we find melancholy evidence in every

[024-166]recurring native insurrection.

[024-167]The two divisions of the Irish infantry were the _galloglass_, or

[024-168]heavily armed foot soldier, called _gall_, either as a mercenary, or

[024-169]from having been equipped after the Norman method, and the _kerne_, or

[024-170]light infantry. The horsemen were men of the free tribes, who followed

[024-171]their chief on terms almost of equality, and who, except his immediate

[024-172]retainers, equipped and foraged for themselves. The highest unit of

[024-173]this force was a _Cath_, or battalion of 3,000 men; but the subdivision

[024-174]of command and the laws which established and maintained discipline

[024-175]have yet to be recovered and explained. The old Spanish "right of

[024-176]insurrection" seems to have been recognized in every chief of a free

[024-177]tribe, and no Hidalgo of old Spain, for real or fancied slight, was

[024-178]ever more ready to turn his horse's head homeward than were those

[024-179]refractory lords, with whom Roderick O'Conor and his successors, in the

[024-180]front of the national battle, had to contend or to co-operate.
