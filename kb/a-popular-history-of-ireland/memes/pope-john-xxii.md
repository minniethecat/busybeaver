Pope John XXII, elected to the pontificate in 1316, was the second of the Avignon Popes. He is remembered in Irish history as the recipient of one of the most significant documents of the medieval period — the Remonstrance of Prince [[Donald O'Neil]] of Ulster.

> "It was this latter Donnell or Donald O'Neil, who, towards the end of his reign, addressed to Pope John XXII. (elected to the pontificate in 1316) that powerful indictment against the Anglo-Normans, which has ever since remained one of the cardinal texts of our history." (032-199)

This powerful indictment of Anglo-Norman rule in Ireland, written about the year 1317 or 1318, has ever since remained one of the cardinal texts of Irish political history.

## The Remonstrance of Donald O'Neil

The Remonstrance — composed in the context of the [[Bruce Invasion]] of Ireland — detailed the grievances of the native Irish against their Anglo-Norman overlords. It was written after the unsuccessful joint campaign of Donald O'Neil and [[Edward Bruce]] against the English settlements.

Donald O'Neil, as son of King Brian of the battle of Down, formally resigned his hereditary right in favour of Edward Bruce in a great assembly held at [[Dundalk]]. King Brian had been the last acknowledged native king, elected Ard-Righ at the Conference of Caeluisge half a century earlier.

> "Donald O'Neil, by letters patent, as son of Brian 'of the battle of Down,' the last acknowledged native king, formally resigned his right, in favour of Bruce, a proceeding which he defends in his celebrated letter to Pope John XXII." (037-030)

## The Grievances Documented

The letter presented to Pope John XXII offered a detailed catalogue of legal disabilities suffered by the native Irish under Anglo-Norman rule. No man of Irish origin could sue in an English court; no Irishman within the marches could make a legal will.

> "In the eloquent letter of the O'Neil to Pope John XXII.—written about the year 1318—we read, that no man of Irish origin could sue in an English court; that no Irishman, within the marches, could make a legal will; that his property was appropriated by his English neighbours; and that the murder of an Irishman was not even a felony punishable by fine." (033-088)

The letter further stated that property was systematically appropriated by English neighbours, and — most shockingly — the murder of an Irish person was not even accounted a felony punishable by fine.

## Ecclesiastical Significance

The Remonstrance also illuminated deep divisions within the Irish Church between the native clergy and the English religious establishment. McGee noted the "wide and deep gulf" between English and Irish churchmen in the early fourteenth century.

> "In our own comments on the memorable letter of Prince Donald O'Neil to Pope John XXII., written in the year 1317 or '18, we have seen how wide and deep was the gulf then existing between the English and Irish churchmen." (047-047)

In 1324, an attempt to heal this breach was made by Philip of Slane, a Dominican who had presided at the trial of the Knights Templars in Ireland, but the divisions proved deep and enduring.

## Legacy

Pope John XXII reigned during a transformative period in both European and Irish history. The petition from Ireland reached him as the Avignon Papacy consolidated its authority and grappled with its own relationship to secular powers.

The Remonstrance remains a landmark of Irish political literature — a systematic legal and moral argument against colonial governance. The Pope's response — or lack thereof — reflected the broader papal reluctance to antagonise the English Crown. The document preserved through John XXII's reception remains one of the earliest statements of Irish grievances against foreign rule.
