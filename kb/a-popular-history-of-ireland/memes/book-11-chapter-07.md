CHAPTER VII.

[094-001]GRATTAN'S LEADERSHIP—"FREE TRADE," AND THE VOLUNTEERS.

[094-002]The revolt of the American colonies against the oppressive legislation

[094-003]of the British Parliament, was the next circumstance that deeply

[094-004]affected the constitutional struggle, in which the Irish Parliament had

[094-005]so long been engaged. The similarity in the grievances of Ireland and

[094-006]the colonies, the close ties of kindred established between them, the

[094-007]extent of colonial commerce involved in the result, contributed to give

[094-008]the American Declaration of Independence more importance in men's eyes

[094-009]at Dublin, than anywhere else out of the colonies, except, perhaps,

[094-010]London.

[094-011]The first mention made of American affairs to the Irish legislature,

[094-012]was in Lord Townsend's message in 1775, calling for the despatch of

[094-013]4,000 men from the Irish establishment, to America, and offering to

[094-014]supply their place by as many foreign Protestant (German) troops. The

[094-015]demand was warmly debated. The proposition to receive the proffered

[094-016]foreign troops was rejected by a majority of thirty-eight, and the

[094-017]contingent for America passed on a division, upon Flood's plea that

[094-018]they would go out merely as "4,000 armed negotiators." This expression

[094-019]of the great parliamentary leader was often afterwards quoted to his

[094-020]prejudice, but we must remember, that, at the time it was employed, no

[094-021]one on either side of the contest had abandoned all hopes of

[094-022]accommodation, and that the significance of the phrase was rather

[094-023]pointed against Lord North than against the colonies. The 4,000 men

[094-024]went out, among them Lord Rawdon (afterwards Lord Moira), Lord Edward

[094-025]Fitzgerald, and many others, both officers and men, who were certainly

[094-026]no enemies of liberty, or the colonies.

[094-027]Some slight relaxation of the commercial restrictions which operated so

[094-028]severely against Irish industry were made during the same year, but

[094-029]these were more than counterbalanced by the embargo on the export of

[094-030]provisions to America, imposed in February, 1776. This arbitrary

[094-031]measure—imposed by order in Council—was so near being censured by the

[094-032]Parliament then sitting, that the House was dissolved a month

[094-033]afterwards, and a new election ordered. To meet the new Parliament it

[094-034]was thought advisable to send over a new Viceroy, and accordingly Lord

[094-035]Buckinghamshire entered into office, with Sir Richard Heron as chief

[094-036]secretary.

[094-037]In the last session of the late Parliament, a young _protege_ of Lord

[094-038]Charlemont—he was only in his twenty-ninth year—had taken his seat for

[094-039]the borough of Charlemont. This was Henry Grattan, son of the Recorder

[094-040]of Dublin, and grandson of one of those Grattans who, according to Dean

[094-041]Swift, "could raise 10,000 men." The youth of Grattan had been neither

[094-042]joyous nor robust; in early manhood he had offended his father's

[094-043]conservatism; the profession of the law, to which he was bred, he found

[094-044]irksome and unsuited to his tastes; society, as then constituted, was

[094-045]repulsive to his over-sensitive spirit and high Spartan ideal of manly

[094-046]duty; no letters are sadder to read than the early correspondence of

[094-047]Grattan, till he had fairly found his inspiration in listening

[094-048]enraptured to the eloquent utterances of Chatham, or comparing

[094-049]political opinions with such a friend as Flood. At length he found a

[094-050]seat in the House of Commons, where, during his first session, he spoke

[094-051]on three or four occasions, briefly, modestly, and with good effect;

[094-052]there had been no sitting during 1776, nor before October of the

[094-053]following year; it was, therefore, in the sessions from '78 to '82

[094-054]inclusive, that this young member raised himself to the head of the

[094-055]most eloquent men, in one of the most eloquent assemblies the world has

[094-056]ever seen.

[094-058]The fact of Mr. Flood, after fourteen years of opposition, having

[094-059]accepted office under Lord Harcourt's administration, and defended the

[094-060]American expedition and the embargo, had greatly lessened the

[094-061]popularity of that eminent man. There was indeed, no lack of ability

[094-062]still left in the ranks of the opposition—for Burgh, Daly, and

[094-063]Yelverton were there; but for a supreme spirit like Grattan—whose

[094-064]burning tongue was ever fed from his heart of fire—there is always room

[094-065]in a free senate, how many soever able and accomplished men may

[094-066]surround him.

[094-067]The fall of 1777 brought vital intelligence from America. General

[094-068]Burgoyne had surrendered at Saratoga, and France had decided to ally

[094-069]herself with the Americans. The effect in England and in Ireland was

[094-070]immense. When the Irish Houses met, Mr. Grattan moved an address to the

[094-071]King in favour of retrenchment, and against the pension list, and Mr.

[094-072]Daly moved and carried an address deploring the continuance of the

[094-073]American war, with a governmental amendment assuring his Majesty that

[094-074]he might still rely on the services of his faithful Commons. The second

[094-075]Catholic relief bill, authorizing Papists to loan money on mortgage, to

[094-076]lease lands for any period not exceeding 999 years—to inherit and

[094-077]bequeath real property, so limited, passed, not without some

[094-078]difficulty, into law. The debate had been protracted, by adjournment

[094-079]after adjournment, over the greatest part of three months; the main

[094-080]motion had been further complicated by an amendment repealing the Test

[094-081]Act in favour of Dissenters, which was, fortunately, engrafted on the

[094-082]measure. The vote in the Commons, in favour of the bill so amended, was

[094-083]127 _yeas_ to 89 _nays_, and in the Lords, 44 _Contents_ to 28

[094-084]_Noncontents_.

[094-085]In the English House of Commons, Lord Nugent moved, in April, a series

[094-086]of resolutions raising the embargo on the Irish provision trade;

[094-087]abolishing, so far as Ireland was concerned, the most restrictive

[094-088]clauses of the Navigation Act, both as to exports and imports, with the

[094-089]exception of the article of tobacco. Upon this the manufacturing and

[094-090]shipping interest of England, taking the alarm, raised such a storm in

[094-091]the towns and cities that the ministry of the day were compelled to

[094-092]resist the proposed changes, with a few trifling exceptions. But

[094-093]Grattan had caught up, in the other island, the cry of "free trade,"

[094-094]and the people echoed it after their orator, until the whole empire

[094-095]shook with the popular demand.

[094-096]But what gave pith and power to the Irish demands was the enrolment and

[094-097]arming of a numerous volunteer force, rendered absolutely necessary by

[094-098]the defenceless state of the kingdom. Mr. Flood had long before

[094-099]proposed a national militia, but being in opposition and in the

[094-100]minority, he had failed. To him and to Mr. Perry, as much as to Lord

[094-101]Charlemont and Mr. Grattan, the militia bill of 1778, and the noble

[094-102]army of volunteers equipped under its provisions, owed their origin.

[094-103]Whether this force was to be a regular militia, subject to martial law,

[094-104]or composed of independent companies, was for some months a subject of

[094-105]great anxiety at the castle; but necessity at length precipitated a

[094-106]decision in favour of volunteer companies, to be supplied with arms by

[094-107]the state, but drilled and clothed at their own expense, with power to

[094-108]elect their own officers. The official announcement of this decision

[094-109]once made, the organization spread rapidly over the whole kingdom. The

[094-110]Ulster corps, first organized, chose as their commander the Earl of

[094-111]Charlemont, while those of Leinster elected the Duke of Leinster.

[094-112]Simultaneously, resolutions against the purchase of English goods and

[094-113]wares were passed at public meetings, and by several of the corporate

[094-114]bodies. Lists of the importers of such goods were obtained at the

[094-115]custom houses, and printed in handbills, to the alarm of the importers.

[094-116]Swift's sardonic maxim, "to burn everything coming from England,

[094-117]_except the coals_," began to circulate as a toast in all societies,

[094-118]and the consternation of the Castle, at this resurrection of the

[094-119]redoubtable Dean, was almost equal to the apprehension entertained of

[094-120]him while living.

[094-121]While the Castle was temporizing with both the military and the

[094-122]manufacture movement, in a vague expectation to defeat both, the press,

[094-123]as is usual in such national crises, teemed with publications of great

[094-124]fervour and ability. Dr. Jebb, Mr. (afterwards Judge) Johnson, Mr.

[094-125]Pollock, Mr. Charles Sheridan, Father Arthur O'Leary, and Mr. Dobbs,

[094-126]M.P., were the chief workers in this department of patriotic duty.

[094-127]Cheered, instructed, restrained within due bounds by these writings and

[094-128]the reported debates of Parliament, the independent companies proceeded

[094-129]with their organization. In July, 1779, after all the resources of

[094-130]prevarication had been exhausted, arms were issued to the several

[094-131]recognized corps, and the Irish volunteers became in reality a national

[094-132]army for domestic protection and defence.

[094-133]When this point was reached, Mr. Grattan and his friends took anxious

[094-134]council as to their future movements. Parliament was to meet on the

[094-135]12th of October, and in that sweet autumnal month, Grattan, Burgh, and

[094-136]Daly, met upon the sea-shore, near Bray, in view of one of the

[094-137]loveliest landscapes on earth, to form their plan for the session. They

[094-138]agreed on an amendment to the address in answer to the royal speech,

[094-139]demanding in explicit terms "free export and import" for Irish

[094-140]commerce. When Parliament met, and the address and amendment were

[094-141]moved, it was found that Flood, Burgh, Hutchinson, and Gardiner, though

[094-142]all holding offices of honour and emolument under government, would

[094-143]vote for it. Flood suggested to substitute the simple term "free

[094-144]trade," and with this and one other verbal alteration suggested by

[094-145]Burgh, the amendment passed with a single dissenting voice.

[094-146]The next day the Speaker, Mr. Perry, who was all along in the

[094-147]confidence of the movers of the amendment, Daly, Grattan, Burgh, Flood,

[094-148]Hutchinson, Ponsonby, Gardiner, and the whole House, went up with the

[094-149]amended address to the castle. The streets were lined with volunteers,

[094-150]commanded in person by the Duke of Leinster, who presented arms to the

[094-151]patriotic Commons as they passed. Most of the leading members wore the

[094-152]uniform of one or other of the national companies, and the people saw

[094-153]themselves at the same moment under the protection of a patriotic

[094-154]majority in the legislature, and a patriotic force in the field. No

[094-155]wonder their enthusiastic cheers rang through the corridors of the

[094-156]castle with a strangely jubilant and defiant emphasis. It was not

[094-157]simply the spectacle of a nation recovering its spirit, but recovering

[094-158]it with all military _éclat_ and pageantry. It was the disarmed armed

[094-159]and triumphant—a revolution not only in national feeling, but in the

[094-160]external manifestation of that feeling. A change so profound stirred

[094-161]sentiments and purposes even deeper than itself, and suggested to the

[094-162]ardent imagination of Grattan the establishment of entire national

[094-163]independence, saving always the rights of the crown.

[094-164]The next day, the Houses, not to be outdone in courtesy, voted their

[094-165]thanks to the volunteers for "their just and necessary exertions in

[094-166]defence of their country!"
