CHAPTER II.

[018-001]THE CONTEST BETWEEN THE NORTH AND SOUTH—RISE OF THE FAMILY OF O'CONOR.

[018-002]Four years before the death of Thorlogh O'Brien, a Prince destined to

[018-003]be the life-long rival of his great son, had succeeded to the kingship

[018-004]of the northern tribes. This was Donald, son of Ardgall, Prince of

[018-005]Aileach, sometimes called "O" and sometimes "Mac" Laughlin. Donald had

[018-006]reached the mature age of forty when he succeeded in the course of

[018-007]nature to his father, Ardgall, and was admitted the first man of the

[018-008]North, not only in station but for personal graces and accomplishments;

[018-009]for wisdom, wealth, liberality, and love of military adventure.

[018-010]Murkertach, or Murtogh O'Brien, was of nearly the same age as his

[018-011]rival, and his equal, if not superior in talents, both for peace and

[018-012]war. During the last years of his father's reign and illness, he had

[018-013]been the real ruler of the south, and had enforced the claims of Cashel

[018-014]on all the tribes of Leath Mogha, from Dublin to Galway. In the year

[018-015]1094, by mutual compact, brought about through the intercession of the

[018-016]Archbishop of Armagh and the great body of the clergy, north and

[018-017]south—and still more perhaps by the pestilence and famine which raged

[018-018]at intervals during the last years of the eleventh century—this ancient

[018-019]division of the midland _asker_, running east and west, was solemnly

[018-020]restored by consent of both parties, and Leath Mogha and Leath Conn

[018-021]became for the moment independent territories. So thoroughly did the

[018-022]Church enter into the arrangement, that, at the Synod of Rath-Brazil,

[018-023]held a few years later, the seats of the twelve Bishops of the southern

[018-024]half were grouped round the Archbishop of Cashel, while the twelve of

[018-025]the northern half were ranged round the Archbishop of Armagh. The

[018-026]Bishops of Meath, the ancient mensal of the monarchy, seem to have

[018-027]occupied a middle station between the benches of the north and south.

[018-028]Notwithstanding the solemn compact of 1094, Murtogh did not long cease

[018-029]to claim the title, nor to seek the hostages of all Ireland. As soon as

[018-030]the fearful visitations with which the century had closed were passed

[018-031]over, he resumed his warlike forays, and found Donald of Aileach

[018-032]nothing loath to try again the issue of arms. Each prince, however,

[018-033]seems to have been more anxious to coerce or interest the secondary

[018-034]chiefs in his own behalf than to meet his rival in the old-style

[018-035]pitched battle. Murtogh's annual march was usually along the Shannon,

[018-036]into Leitrim, thence north by Sligo, and across the Erne and Finn into

[018-037]Donegal and Derry. Donald's annual excursion led commonly along the

[018-038]Bann, into Dalriada and Ulidia, Whence by way of Newry, across the

[018-039]Boyne, into Meath, and from West-Meath into Munster. In one of these

[018-040]forays, at the very opening of the twelfth century, Donald surprised

[018-041]Kinkora in the absence of its lord, razed the fort and levelled the

[018-042]buildings to the earth. But the next season the southern king paid him

[018-043]back in kind, when he attacked and demolished Aileach, and caused each

[018-044]of his soldiers to carry off a stone of the ruin in his knapsack. "I

[018-045]never heard of the billeting of grit stones," exclaims a bard of those

[018-046]days, "though I have heard of the billeting of soldiers: but now we see

[018-047]the stones of Aileach billeted on the horses of the King of the West!"

[018-048]Such circuits of the Irish kings, especially in days of opposition,

[018-049]were repeated with much regularity. They seem to have set out commonly

[018-050]in May—or soon after the festival of Easter—and when the tour of the

[018-051]island was made, they occupied about six weeks in duration. The precise

[018-052]number of men who took part in these visitations is nowhere stated, but

[018-053]in critical times no prince, claiming the perilous honour of

[018-054]_Ard-Righ_, would be likely to march with less than from five to ten

[018-055]thousand men. The movements of such a multitude must have been attended

[018-056]with many oppressions and inconveniences; their encampment for even a

[018-057]week in any territory must have been a serious burthen to the resident

[018-058]inhabitants, whether hostile or hospitable. Yet this was one inevitable

[018-059]consequence of the breaking up of the federal centre at Tara. In

[018-060]earlier days, the _Ard-Righ_, on his election, or in an emergency, made

[018-061]an armed procession through the island. Ordinarily, however, his

[018-062]suffragans visited him, and not he them; all Ireland went up to Tara to

[018-063]the _Feis_, or to the festivals of Baaltine and Samhain. Now that there

[018-064]was no Tara to go to, the monarch, or would-be monarch, found it

[018-065]indispensable to show himself often, and to exercise his authority in

[018-066]person, among every considerable tribe in the island. To do justice to

[018-067]Murtogh O'Brien, he does not appear to have sought occasions of

[018-068]employing force when on these expeditions, but rather to have acted the

[018-069]part of an armed negotiator. On his return from the demolition of

[018-070]Aileach (A.D. 1101), among other acts of munificence, he, in an

[018-071]assembly of the clergy of Leath Mogha, made a solemn gift of the city

[018-072]of Cashel, free of all rents and dues, to the Archbishop and the

[018-073]Clergy, for ever. His munificence to churches, and his patronage of

[018-074]holy men, were eminent traits in this Prince's character. And the

[018-075]clergy of that age were eminently worthy of the favours of such

[018-076]Princes. Their interposition frequently brought about a truce between

[018-077]the northern and southern kings. In the year 1103, the hostages of both

[018-078]were placed in custody with Donald, Archbishop of Armagh, to guarantee

[018-079]a twelvemonth's peace. But the next season the contest was renewed.

[018-080]Murtogh besieged Armagh for a week, which Donald of Aileach

[018-081]successfully defended, until the siege was abandoned. In a subsequent

[018-082]battle the northern force defeated one division of Murtogh's allies in

[018-083]Iveagh, under the Prince of Leinster, who fell on the field, with the

[018-084]lords of Idrone, Ossory, Desies, Kerry, and the Dublin Danes. Murtogh

[018-085]himself, with another division of his troops, was on an incursion into

[018-086]Antrim when he heard of this defeat. The northern visitors carried off

[018-087]among other spoils the royal tent and standard, a trophy which gave new

[018-088]bitterness on the one side, and new confidence on the other. Donald,

[018-089]the good Archbishop, the following year (A.D. 1105) proceeded to

[018-090]Dublin, where Murtogh was, or was soon expected, to renew the previous

[018-091]peace between North and South, but he fell suddenly ill soon after his

[018-092]arrival, and caused himself to be carried homewards in haste. At a

[018-093]church by the wayside, not far from Dublin, he was anointed and

[018-094]received the viaticum. He survived, however, to reach Armagh, where he

[018-095]expired on the 12th day of August. Kellach, latinized Celsus, his

[018-096]saintly successor, was promoted to the Primacy, and solemnly

[018-097]consecrated on Saint Adamnan's day following—the 23rd of September,

[018-098]1105.

[018-099]Archbishop Celsus, whose accession was equally well received in Munster

[018-100]as in Ulster, followed in the footsteps of his pious predecessor, in

[018-101]taking a decided part with neither Leath Mogha nor Leath Conn. When, in

[018-102]the year 1110, both parties marched to Slieve-Fuaid, with a view to a

[018-103]challenge of battle, Celsus interposed between them the

[018-104]_Bachall-Isa_—and a solemn truce followed; again, three years later,

[018-105]when they confronted each other in Iveagh, in Down, similar success

[018-106]attended a similar interposition. Three years later Murtogh O'Brien was

[018-107]seized with so severe an illness, that he became like to a living

[018-108]skeleton, and though he recovered sufficiently to resume the exercise

[018-109]of authority he never regained his full health. He died in a spiritual

[018-110]retreat, at Lismore, on the 4th of the Ides of March, A.D. 1119, and

[018-111]was buried at Killaloe. His great rival, Donald of Leath Conn, did not

[018-112]long survive him: he died at Derry, also in a religious house, on the

[018-113]5th of the Ides of February, A.D. 1121.

[018-115]While these two able men were thus for more than a quarter of a century

[018-116]struggling for the supremacy, a third power was gradually strengthening

[018-117]itself west of the Shannon, destined to profit by the contest, more

[018-118]than either of the principals. This was the family of O'Conor, of

[018-119]Roscommon, who derived their pedigree from the same stock as the

[018-120]O'Neils, and their name from Conor, an ancestor, who ruled over

[018-121]Connaught, towards the end of the ninth century. Two or three of their

[018-122]line before Conor had possessed the same rank and title, but it was by

[018-123]no means regarded as an adjunct of the house of Rathcrogan, before the

[018-124]time at which we have arrived. Their co-relatives, sometimes their

[018-125]rivals, but oftener their allies, were the O'Ruarcs of Breffny,

[018-126]McDermots of Moylurg, the O'Flahertys of _Iar_ or West Connaught, the

[018-127]O'Shaughnessys, O'Heynes, and O'Dowdas. The great neighbouring family

[018-128]of O'Kelly had sprung from a different branch of the far-spreading

[018-129]Gaelic tree. At the opening of the twelfth century, Thorlogh More

[018-130]O'Conor, son of Ruari of the Yellow Hound, son of Hugh of the Broken

[018-131]Spear, was the recognised head of his race, both for valour and

[018-132]discretion. By some historians he is called the half-brother of Murtogh

[018-133]O'Brien, and it is certain that he was the faithful ally of that

[018-134]powerful prince. In the early stages of the recent contest between

[018-135]North and South, Donald of Aileach had presented himself at Rathcrogan,

[018-136]the residence of O'Conor, who entertained him for a fortnight, and gave

[018-137]him hostages; but Connaught finally sided with Munster, and thus, by a

[018-138]decided policy, escaped being ground to powder, as corn is ground

[018-139]between the mill-stones. But the nephew and successor of Murtogh was

[018-140]not prepared to reciprocate to Connaught the support it had rendered to

[018-141]Munster, but rather looked for its continuance to himself. Conor

[018-142]O'Brien, who became King of Munster in 1120, resisted all his life the

[018-143]pretensions of any house but his own to the southern half-kingdom, and

[018-144]against a less powerful or less politic antagonist, his energy and

[018-145]capacity would have been certain to prevail. The posterity of Malachy

[018-146]in Meath, as well as the Princes of Aileach, were equally hostile to

[018-147]the designs of the new aspirant. One line had given three, another

[018-148]seven, another twenty kings to Erin—but who had ever heard of an

[018-149]_Ard-Righ_ coming out of Connaught? 'Twas so they reasoned in those

[018-150]days of fierce family pride, and so they acted. Yet Thorlogh, son of

[018-151]Ruari, son of Hugh, proved himself in the fifteen years' war, previous

[018-152]to his accession (1021 to 1136), more than a match for all his enemies.

[018-153]He had been chief of his tribe since the year 1106, and from the first

[018-154]had begun to lay his far forecasting plans for the sovereignty. He had

[018-155]espoused the cause of the house of O'Brien, and had profited by that

[018-156]alliance. Nor were all his thoughts given to war. He had bridged the

[018-157]river Suca at Ballinasloe, and the Shannon at Athlone and Shannon

[018-158]harbour, and the same year these works were finished (1120 or '21) he

[018-159]celebrated the ancient games at Tailtean, in assertion of his claim to

[018-160]the monarchy. His main difficulty was the stubborn pride of Munster,

[018-161]and the valour and enterprise of Conor O'Brien, surnamed Conor "of the

[018-162]fortresses." Of the years following his assertion of his title, few

[018-163]passed without war between those Provinces. In 1121 and 1127, Thorlogh

[018-164]triumphed in the south, took hostages from Lismore to Tralee, and

[018-165]returned home exultingly; a few years later the tide turned, and Conor

[018-166]O'Brien was equally victorious against him, in the heart of his own

[018-167]country. Thorlogh played off in the south the ancient jealousy of the

[018-168]Eugenian houses against the Dalcassians, and thus weakened both, to his

[018-169]own advantage. In the year 1126 he took Dublin and raised his son to

[018-170]the lordship, as Dermid of Leinster, and Thorlogh O'Brien had done

[018-171]formerly: marching southward he encamped in Ormond, from Lammas to St.

[018-172]Bridget's day, and overran Munster with his troops in all directions,

[018-173]taking Cork, Cashel, Ardfinnan, and Tralee. Celsus, the holy Primate of

[018-174]Armagh, deploring the evils of this protracted year, left his peaceful

[018-175]city, and spent thirteen months in the south and west, endeavouring to

[018-176]reconcile, and bind over to the peace, the contending kings. In these

[018-177]days the Irish hierarchy performed, perhaps, their highest part—that of

[018-178]peacemakers and preachers of good will to men. When in 1132 and '33 the

[018-179]tide had temporarily turned against Thorlogh, and Conor O'Brien had

[018-180]united Munster, Leinster, and Meath, against him, the Archbishop of

[018-181]Tuam performed effectually the office of mediator, preserving not only

[018-182]his own Province, but the whole country from the most sanguinary

[018-183]consequences. In the year 1130, the holy Celsus had rested from his

[018-184]labours, and Malachy, the illustrious friend of St. Bernard, was

[018-185]nominated as his successor. At the time he was absent in Munster, as

[018-186]the Vicar of the aged Primate, engaged in a mission of peace, when the

[018-187]crozier and the dying message of his predecessor were delivered to him.

[018-188]He returned to Armagh, where he found that Maurice, son of Donald, had

[018-189]been intruded as Archbishop in the _interim_, to this city peace,

[018-190]order, and unity, were not even partially restored, until two years

[018-191]later—A.D., 1132.

[018-192]The reign of Thorlogh O'Conor over Leath Mogha, or as Ard-Righ "with

[018-193]opposition," is dated by the best authorities from the year 1136. He

[018-194]was then in his forty-eighth year, and had been chief of his tribe from

[018-195]the early age of eighteen. He afterwards reigned for twenty years, and

[018-196]as those years, and the early career of his son Roderick are full of

[018-197]instruction, in reference to the events which follow, we must relate

[018-198]them somewhat in detail. We again beg the reader to observe the

[018-199]consequences of the destruction of the federal bond among the Irish;

[018-200]how every province has found an ambitious dynasty of its own, which

[018-201]each contends shall be supreme; how the ambition of the great families

[018-202]grows insatiable as the ancient rights and customs decay; how the law

[018-203]of Patrick enacted in the fifth century is no longer quoted or

[018-204]regarded; how the law of the strong hand alone decides the quarrel of

[018-205]these proud, unyielding Princes.
