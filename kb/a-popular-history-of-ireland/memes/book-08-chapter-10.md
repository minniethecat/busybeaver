CHAPTER X.

[061-001]MOUNTJOY'S ADMINISTRATION—OPERATIONS IN ULSTER AND MUNSTER—CAREW'S "WIT

[061-002]AND CUNNING"—LANDING OF SPANIARDS IN THE SOUTH—BATTLE OF KINSALE—DEATH

[061-003]OF O'DONNELL IN SPAIN.

[061-004]The twofold operations against Ulster, neglected by Essex, were

[061-005]vigorously pressed forward by the energetic Mountjoy. On the 16th of

[061-006]May, a fleet arrived in Lough Foyle, having on board 4,000 foot and 200

[061-007]horse, under the command of Sir Henry Dowcra, with abundance of stores,

[061-008]building materials, and ordnance. At the same moment, the Deputy forced

[061-009]the Moira pass, and made a feigned demonstration against Armagh, to

[061-010]draw attention from the fleet in the Foyle. This feint served its

[061-011]purpose; Dowcra was enabled to land and throw up defensive works at

[061-012]Derry, which he made his head-quarters, to fortify Culmore at the

[061-013]entrance to the harbour, where he placed 600 men, under the command of

[061-014]Captain Atford, and to seize the ancient fort of Aileach, at the head

[061-015]of Lough Swilly, where Captain Ellis Flood was stationed with 150 men.

[061-016]The attempt against Ballyshannon was, on a nearer view, found

[061-017]impracticable, and deferred; the Deputy, satisfied that the lodgment

[061-018]had been made upon Lough Foyle, retired to Dublin, after increasing the

[061-019]garrisons at Newry, Carlingford, and Dundalk. The Catholic chieftains

[061-020]immediately turned their attention to the new fort at Derry, appeared

[061-021]suddenly before it with 5,000 men, but failing to draw out its

[061-022]defenders, and being wholly unprovided with a siege train and

[061-023]implements—as they appear to have been throughout—they withdrew the

[061-024]second day, O'Donnell leaving a party in hopes to starve out the

[061-025]foreigners. This party were under the command of O'Doherty, of

[061-026]Innishowen, and Nial Garve O'Donnell, the most distinguished soldier of

[061-027]his name, after his illustrious cousin and chief. On the 28th of June,

[061-028]a party of the besieged, headed by Sir John Chamberlaine, made a sally

[061-029]from the works, but were driven in with loss, and Chamberlaine killed.

[061-030]On the 29th of July, O'Donnell, who had returned from his annual

[061-031]incursion into Connaught and Thomond, seized the English cavalry

[061-032]horses, and defeated the main force of the besieged, who had issued out

[061-033]to their rescue. From this affair Dowcra was carried back wounded into

[061-034]Derry.

[061-035]But treason was busy in the Irish camp and country among the

[061-036]discontented members of the neighbouring clans. The election of chiefs

[061-037]for life, always a fruitful source of bickering and envy, supplied the

[061-038]very material upon which "the princely policie" of division,

[061-039]recommended by Bacon to Essex, might be exercised. Dowcra succeeded in

[061-040]the summer in winning over Art O'Neil, son of Turlogh, the early

[061-041]adversary of the great Hugh; before the year was over, by bribes and

[061-042]promises, he seduced Nial Garve, in the absence of his chief in

[061-043]Connaught, and Nial, having once entered on the career of treason,

[061-044]pursued it with all the dogged courage of his disposition. Though his

[061-045]wife, sister to Red Hugh, forsook him, though his name was execrated

[061-046]throughout the Province, except by his blindly devoted personal

[061-047]followers, he served the English during the remainder of the war with a

[061-048]zeal and ability to which they acknowledged themselves deeply indebted.

[061-049]By a rapid march, at the head of 1,000 men, supplied by Dowcra, he

[061-050]surprised the town of Lifford, which his new allies promptly fortified

[061-051]with walls of stone, and entrusted to him to defend. Red Hugh, on

[061-052]learning this alarming incident, hastened from the West to invest the

[061-053]place. After sitting before it an entire month, with no other advantage

[061-054]than a sally repulsed, he concluded to go into winter quarters. Arthur

[061-055]O'Neil and Nial Garve had the dignity of knighthood conferred upon

[061-056]them, and were, besides, recognized for the day by the English

[061-057]officials as the future O'Neil and O'Donnell. In like manner, "a

[061-058]Queen's Maguire" had been raised up in Fermanagh, "a Queen's O'Reilly"

[061-059]in Cavan, and other chiefs of smaller districts were provided with

[061-060]occupation enough at their own doors by the "princely policie" of Lord

[061-061]Bacon.

[061-062]The English interest in Munster during the first year of Mountjoy's

[061-063]administration had recovered much of its lost predominance. The new

[061-064]President, Sir George Carew, afterwards Earl of Totness, was brother to

[061-065]that knightly "undertaker" who claimed the moiety of Desmond, and met

[061-066]his death at Glenmalure. He was a soldier of the new school, who prided

[061-067]himself especially on his "wit and cunning," in the composition of

[061-068]"sham and counterfeit letters." He had an early experience in the Irish

[061-069]wars, first as Governor of Askeaton Castle, and afterwards as

[061-070]Lieutenant General of the Ordnance. Subsequently he was employed in

[061-071]putting England in a state of defence against the Spaniards, and had

[061-072]just returned from an embassy to Poland, when he was ordered to join

[061-073]Mountjoy with the rank of Lord President. He has left us a memoir of

[061-074]his administration, civil and military, edited by his natural son and

[061-075]Secretary, Thomas Stafford—exceedingly interesting to read both as to

[061-076]matter and manner, but the documents embodied in which are about as

[061-077]reliable as the speeches which are read in Livy. Some of them are

[061-078]admitted forgeries; others are at least of doubtful authenticity. After

[061-079]escaping with Lord Thomond from the scene of Ormond's capture, his

[061-080]first act on reaching Cork was to conclude a month's truce with

[061-081]Florence McCarthy. This he did, in order to gain time to perfect a plot

[061-082]for the destruction of O'Neil's other friend, called in derision, by

[061-083]the Anglo-Irish of Munster, the _sugane_ (or straw-rope) Earl of

[061-084]Desmond.

[061-085]This plot, so characteristic of Carew and of the turn which English

[061-086]history was about to take in the next reign, deserves to be

[061-087]particularly mentioned. There was, in the service of the Earl, one

[061-088]Dermid O'Conor, captain of 1,400 hired troops, who was married to lady

[061-089]Margaret Fitzgerald, daughter to the late, and niece to the new-made

[061-090]Earl of Desmond. This lady, naturally interested in the restoration of

[061-091]her young brother, then the Queen's ward or prisoner at London, to the

[061-092]title and estates, was easily drawn into the scheme of seducing her

[061-093]husband from his patron. To justify and cloak the treachery a letter

[061-094]was written by Carew to the _sugane_ Earl reminding him of _his_

[061-095]engagement to deliver up O'Conor; this _letter_, as pre-arranged, was

[061-096]intercepted by the latter, who, watching his opportunity, rushed with

[061-097]it open into the Earl's presence, and arrested him, in the name of

[061-098]O'Neil, as a traitor to the Catholic cause! Anxious to finger his

[061-099]reward—1,000 pounds and a royal commission for himself—before giving up

[061-100]his capture, O'Conor imprisoned the Earl in the keep of Castle-Ishin,

[061-101]but the White Knight, the Knight of Glynn, Fitzmaurice of Kerry, and

[061-102]Pierce Lacy, levying rapidly 2,000 men, speedily delivered him from

[061-103]confinement, while his baffled betrayer, crest-fallen and dishonoured,

[061-104]was compelled to quit the Province. The year following he was attacked

[061-105]while marching through Galway, and remorselessly put to death by

[061-106]Theobald Burke, usually called Theobald of the ships.

[061-108]Another device employed to destroy the influence of O'Neil's Desmond

[061-109]was the liberation of the young son of the late Earl from the Tower and

[061-110]placing him at the disposal of Carew. The young nobleman, attended by a

[061-111]Captain Price, who was to watch all his movements, landed at Youghal,

[061-112]where he was received by the Lord President, the Clerk of the Council,

[061-113]Mr. Boyle, afterwards Earl of Cork, and Miler Magrath, an apostate

[061-114]ecclesiastic, who had been the Queen's Archbishop of Cashel. By his

[061-115]influence with the warders, Castlemaine, in Kerry, surrendered to the

[061-116]President. On reaching Kilmallock, he was received with such enthusiasm

[061-117]that it required the effort of a guard of soldiers to make way for him

[061-118]through the crowd. According to their custom the people showered down

[061-119]upon him from the windows handfuls of wheat and salt—emblems of plenty

[061-120]and of safety—but the next day, being Sunday, turned all this joy into

[061-121]mourning, not unmingled with anger and shame. The young lord, who had

[061-122]been bred up a Protestant by his keepers, directed his steps to the

[061-123]English Church, to the consternation of the devoted adherents of his

[061-124]house. They clung round him in the street and endeavoured to dissuade

[061-125]him from proceeding, but he continued his course, and on his return was

[061-126]met with hootings and reproaches by those who had hailed him with

[061-127]acclamations the day before. Deserted by the people, and no longer

[061-128]useful to the President, he was recalled to London, where he resumed

[061-129]his quarters in the Tower, and shortly afterwards died. The capture of

[061-130]the strong castle of Glynn from the knight of that name, and the

[061-131]surrender of Carrigafoyle by O'Conor of Kerry, were the other English

[061-132]successes which marked the campaign of 1600 in Munster. On the other

[061-133]hand, O'Donnell had twice exercised his severe supremacy over southern

[061-134]Connaught, burning the Earl of Thomond's new town of Ennis, and

[061-135]sweeping the vales and plains of Clare, and of Clanrickarde, of the

[061-136]animal wealth of their recreant Earls, now actively enlisted against

[061-137]the national confederacy.

[061-138]The eventful campaign of 1601 was fought out in almost every quarter of

[061-139]the kingdom. To hold the coast line, and prevent the advantages being

[061-140]obtained, which the possession of Derry, and other harbours on Lough

[061-141]Foyle gave them, were the tasks of O'Donnell; while to defend the

[061-142]southern frontier was the peculiar charge of O'Neil. They thus fought,

[061-143]as it were, back to back against the opposite lines of attack. The

[061-144]death of O'Doherty, early in this year, threw the succession to

[061-145]Innishowen into confusion, and while O'Donnell was personally

[061-146]endeavouring to settle conflicting claims, Nial Garve seized on the

[061-147]famous Franciscan monastery which stood at the head of the bay, within

[061-148]sight of the towers of Donegal Castle. Hugh Roe immediately invested

[061-149]the place, which his relative as stoutly defended. Three months, from

[061-150]the end of June till the end of September, the siege was strictly

[061-151]maintained, the garrison being regularly supplied with stores and

[061-152]ammunition from sea. On the night of the 29th of September an explosion

[061-153]of gunpowder occurred, and soon the monastery was wrapped in flames.

[061-154]This was the moment chosen for the final attack. The glare of the

[061-155]burning Abbey reflected over the beautiful bay, the darkness of night

[061-156]all round, the shouts of the assailants, and the shrieks of the

[061-157]fugitives driven by the flames upon the spears of their enemies, must

[061-158]have formed a scene of horrors such as even war rarely combines.

[061-159]Hundreds of the besieged were slain, but Nial Garve himself, with the

[061-160]remainder, covered by the fire of an English ship in the harbour,

[061-161]escaped along the strand to the neighbouring monastery of Magherabeg,

[061-162]which he quickly put into a state of defence. All that was left to

[061-163]O'Donnell of that monastery, the burial place of his ancestors, and the

[061-164]chief school of his kinsmen, was a skeleton of stone, standing amid

[061-165]rubbish and ashes. It was never re-inhabited by the Franciscans. A

[061-166]group of huts upon the shore served them for shelter, and the ruined

[061-167]chapel for a place of worship, while they were still left in the land.

[061-168]While Hugh Roe was investing Donegal Abbey the war had not paused on

[061-169]the southern frontier. We have said that Mountjoy had made a second and

[061-170]a third demonstration against Armagh the previous year; in one of these

[061-171]journeys he raised a strong fort at the northern outlet of the Moira

[061-172]pass, which he called Mount Norris, in honour of his late master in the

[061-173]art of war. This work, strongly built and manned, gave him the free

[061-174]_entree_ of the field of battle whenever he chose to take it. In June

[061-175]of this year he was in the valley of the Blackwater, menaced O'Neil's

[061-176]castle of Benburb, and left Sir Charles Danvers with 750 foot and 100

[061-177]horse in possession of Armagh. He further proclaimed a reward of 2,000

[061-178]pounds for the capture of Tyrone alive, or 1,000 pounds for his head.

[061-179]But no Irishman was found to entertain the thought of that bribe. An

[061-180]English assassin was furnished with passports by Danvers, and actually

[061-181]drew his sword on the Earl in his own tent, but he was seized,

[061-182]disarmed, and on the ground of insanity was permitted to escape. Later

[061-183]in the summer Mountjoy was again on the Blackwater, where he laid the

[061-184]foundation of Charlemont, called after himself, and placed 350 men in

[061-185]the works under the command of Captain Williams, the brave defender of

[061-186]the old fort in the same neighbourhood. There were thus quartered in

[061-187]Ulster at this period the 4,000 foot and 400 horse under Dowcra,

[061-188]chiefly on the Foyle, with whatever companies of Kerne adhered to

[061-189]Arthur O'Neil and Nial Garve; with Chichester in Carrickfergus there

[061-190]were 850 foot and 150 horse; with Danvers in Armagh, 750 foot and 100

[061-191]horse; in Mount Norris, under Sir Samuel Bagnal, 600 foot and 50 horse;

[061-192]in and about Downpatrick, lately taken by the Deputy, under Moryson,

[061-193]300 foot; in Newry, under Stafford, 400 foot and 50 horse; in

[061-194]Charlemont, with Williams, 300 foot and 50 horse; or, in all, of

[061-195]English regulars in Ulster alone, 7,000 foot and 800 horse. The

[061-196]position of the garrisons on the map will show how firm a grasp

[061-197]Mountjoy had taken of the Northern Province.

[061-198]The last scene of this great struggle was now about to shift to the

[061-199]opposite quarter of the kingdom. The long-looked for Spanish fleet was

[061-200]known to have left the Tagus—had been seen off the Scilly Islands. On

[061-201]the 23rd of September the Council, presided over by Mountjoy, was

[061-202]assembled in Kilkenny Castle: there were present Carew, Ormond, Sir

[061-203]Richard Wingfield, Marshal of the Queen's troops, uncle to Carew, and

[061-204]founder of the family of Powerscourt; also Chief Justice Gardiner, and

[061-205]other members less known. While they were still sitting a message

[061-206]arrived from Cork that the Spanish fleet was off that harbour, and soon

[061-207]another that they had anchored in Kinsale, and taken possession of the

[061-208]town without opposition. The course of the Council was promptly taken.

[061-209]Couriers were at once despatched to call in the garrisons far and near

[061-210]which could possibly be dispensed with for service in Munster. Letters

[061-211]were despatched to England for reinforcements, and a winter campaign in

[061-212]the South was decided on.

[061-213]The Spanish auxiliary force, when it sailed from the Tagus, consisted

[061-214]originally of 6,000 men in fifteen armed vessels and thirty transports.

[061-215]When they reached Kinsale, after suffering severely at sea, and parting

[061-216]company with several of their comrades, the soldiers were reduced to

[061-217]3,400 men—a number inferior to Dowcra's force on the Foyle. The

[061-218]General, Don Jaun del Aguila, was a brave, but testy, passionate and

[061-219]suspicious officer. He has been severely censured by some Irish writers

[061-220]for landing in the extreme South, within fourteen miles of the English

[061-221]arsenal and head-quarters at Cork, and for his general conduct as a

[061-222]commander. However vulnerable he may be on the general charge, he does

[061-223]not seem fairly to blame for the choice of the point of debarkation. He

[061-224]landed in the old Geraldine country, unaware, of course, of the events

[061-225]of the last few weeks, in which the _sugane_ Earl, and Florence

[061-226]McCarthy, had been entrapped by Carew's "wit and cunning," and shipped

[061-227]for London, from which they never returned. Even the northern chiefs,

[061-228]up to this period, evidently thought their cause much stronger in the

[061-229]South, and Munster much farther restored to vigour and courage than it

[061-230]really was. To the bitter disappointment and disgust of the Spaniards,

[061-231]only O'Sullivan Beare, O'Driscoll, and O'Conor of Kerry, declared

[061-232]openly for them; while they could hear daily of chiefs they had been

[061-233]taught to count as friends, either as prisoners or allies of the

[061-234]English. On the 17th of October—three weeks from their first

[061-235]arrival—they were arrested in Kinsale by a mixed army of English and

[061-236]Anglo-Irish, 15,000 strong, under the command of the Deputy and

[061-237]President, of whom above 5,000 had freshly arrived at Cork from

[061-238]England. With Mountjoy were the Earls of Thomond and Clanrickarde, more

[061-239]zealous than the English themselves for the triumph of England. The

[061-240]harbour was blockaded by ten ships of war, under Sir Richard Leviston,

[061-241]and the forts at the entrance, Rincorran and Castlenepark, being taken

[061-242]by cannonade, the investment on all sides was complete. Don Juan's

[061-243]messengers found O'Neil and O'Donnell busily engaged on their own

[061-244]frontiers, but both instantly resolved to muster all their strength for

[061-245]a winter campaign in Munster. O'Donnell _rendezvoused_ at Ballymote,

[061-246]from which he set out, at the head of 2,500 men, of Tyrconnell and

[061-247]Connaught, on the 2nd day of November. O'Neil, with McDonnell of

[061-248]Antrim, McGennis of Down, McMahon of Monaghan, and others, his

[061-249]suffragans, marched at the head of between 3,000 and 4,000 men, through

[061-250]West-Meath towards Ormond. Holy Cross was their appointed place of

[061-251]meeting, where they expected to be joined by such of the neighbouring

[061-252]Catholics as were eager to strike a blow for liberty of worship.

[061-253]O'Donnell reached the neighbourhood first, and encamped in a strongly

[061-254]defensible position, "plashed on every quarter" for greater security.

[061-255]Mountjoy, anxious to engage him before O'Neil should come up, detached

[061-256]a numerically superior force, under Carew, for that purpose: but

[061-257]O'Donnell, evacuating his quarters by night, marched over the mountain

[061-258]of Slieve Felim, casting away much of his heavy baggage, and before

[061-259]calling halt was 32 _Irish_ miles distant from his late encampment.

[061-260]After this extraordinary mountain march, equal to 40 of our present

[061-261]miles, he made a detour to the westward, descended on Castlehaven, in

[061-262]Cork, and formed a junction with 700 Spaniards, who had just arrived to

[061-263]join Del Aguila. A portion of these veterans were detailed to the forts

[061-264]of Castlehaven, Baltimore, and Dunboy, commanding three of the best

[061-265]havens in Munster; the remainder joined O'Donnell's division.

[061-266]During the whole of November the siege of Kinsale was pressed with the

[061-267]utmost vigour by Mountjoy. The place mounted but three or four

[061-268]effective guns, while 20 great pieces of ordnance were continually

[061-269]playing on the walls. On the 1st of December a breach was found

[061-270]practicable, and an assault made by a party of 2,000 English was

[061-271]bravely repulsed by the Spaniards. The English fleet, ordered round to

[061-272]Castlehaven on the 3rd, were becalmed, and suffered some damage from a

[061-273]battery, manned by Spanish gunners, on the shore. The lines were

[061-274]advanced closer towards the town, and the bombardment became more

[061-275]effective. But the English ranks were considerably thinned by disease

[061-276]and desertion, so that on the last day of December, when the united

[061-277]Irish force took up their position at Belgoley, a mile to the north of

[061-278]their lines, the Lord Deputy's effective force did not, it is thought,

[061-279]exceed 10,000 men. The Catholic army has generally been estimated at

[061-280]6,000 native foot and 500 horse; to these are to be added 300

[061-281]Spaniards, under Don Alphonso Ocampo, who joined O'Donnell at

[061-282]Castlehaven.

[061-284]The prospect for the besiegers was becoming exceedingly critical, but

[061-285]the Spaniards in Kinsale were far from being satisfied with their

[061-286]position. They had been fully three months within walls, in a region

[061-287]wholly unknown to them before their allies appeared. They neither

[061-288]understood nor made allowance for the immense difficulties of a winter

[061-289]campaign in a country trenched with innumerable swollen streams, thick

[061-290]with woods, which, at that season, gave no shelter, and where camping

[061-291]out at nights was enough to chill the hottest blood. They only felt

[061-292]their own inconveniences: they were cut off from escape by sea by a

[061-293]powerful English fleet, and Carew was already practising indirectly on

[061-294]their commander his "wit and cunning," in the fabrication of rumours,

[061-295]and the forging of letters. Don Juan wrote urgent appeals to the

[061-296]northern chiefs to attack the English lines without another day's

[061-297]delay, and a council of war, the third day after their arrival at

[061-298]Belgoley, decided that the attack should be made on the morrow. This

[061-299]decision was come to on the motion of O'Donnell, contrary to the

[061-300]judgment of the more circumspect and far-seeing O'Neil. Overruled, the

[061-301]latter acquiesced in the decision, and cheerfully prepared to discharge

[061-302]his duty.

[061-303]A story is told by Carew that information was obtained of the intended

[061-304]attack from McMahon, in return for a bottle of _aquavitae_ presented to

[061-305]him by the President. This tale is wholly unworthy of belief, told of a

[061-306]chief of the first rank, encamped in the midst of a friendly country.

[061-307]It is also said—and it seems credible enough—that an intercepted letter

[061-308]of Don Juan's gave the English in good time this valuable piece of

[061-309]information. On the night of the 2nd of January, new style (24th of

[061-310]December, O.S.—in use among the English), the Irish army left their

[061-311]camp in three divisions, the vanguard led by Tyrrell, the centre by

[061-312]O'Neil, and the rear by O'Donnell. The night was stormy and dark, with

[061-313]continuous peals and flashes of thunder and lightning. The guides lost

[061-314]their way, and the march, which, even by the most circuitous route,

[061-315]ought not to have exceeded four or five miles, was protracted through

[061-316]the entire night. At dawn of day, O'Neil, with whom were O'Sullivan and

[061-317]Ocampo, came in sight of the English lines, and, to his infinite

[061-318]surprise, found the men under arms, the cavalry in troop posted in

[061-319]advance of their quarters. O'Donnell's division was still to come up,

[061-320]and the veteran Earl now found himself in the same dilemma into which

[061-321]Bagnal had fallen at the Yellow Ford. His embarrassment was perceived

[061-322]from the English camp; the cavalry were at once ordered to advance. For

[061-323]an hour O'Neil maintained his ground alone; at the end of that time he

[061-324]was forced to retire. Of Ocampo's 300 Spaniards, 40 survivors were,

[061-325]with their gallant leader, taken prisoners; O'Donnell at length

[061-326]arrived, and drove back a wing of the English cavalry; Tyrrell's

[061-327]horsemen also held their ground tenaciously. But the rout of the centre

[061-328]proved irremediable. Fully 1,200 of the Irish were left dead on the

[061-329]field, and every prisoner taken was instantly executed. On the English

[061-330]side fell Sir Richard Graeme; Captains Danvers and Godolphin, with

[061-331]several others, were wounded; their total loss they stated at 200, and

[061-332]the Anglo-Irish, of whom they seldom made count in their reports, must

[061-333]have lost in proportion. The Earls of Thomond and Clanrickarde were

[061-334]actively engaged with their followers, and their loss could hardly have

[061-335]been less than that of the English regulars. On the night following

[061-336]their defeat, the Irish leaders held council together at Innishanon, on

[061-337]the river Bandon, where it was agreed that O'Donnell should instantly

[061-338]take shipping for Spain to lay the true state of the contest before

[061-339]Philip III.; that O'Sullivan should endeavour to hold the Castle of

[061-340]Dunboy, as commanding a most important harbour; that Rory O'Donnell,

[061-341]second brother of Hugh Roe, should act as Chieftain of Tyrconnell, and

[061-342]that O'Neil should return into Ulster to make the best defence in his

[061-343]power. The loss in men was not irreparable; the loss in arms, colours,

[061-344]and reputation, was more painful to bear, and far more difficult to

[061-345]retrieve.

[061-346]On the 12th of January, nine days after the battle, Don Juan

[061-347]surrendered the town, and agreed to give up at the same time Dunboy,

[061-348]Baltimore, and Castlehaven. He had lost 1,000 men out of his 3,000

[061-349]during a ten weeks' siege, and was heartily sick of Irish warfare. On

[061-350]his return to Spain he was degraded from his rank, for his too great

[061-351]intimacy with Carew, and confined a prisoner in his own house. He is

[061-352]said to have died of a broken heart occasioned by these indignities.

[061-353]O'Donnell sailed from Castlehaven in a Spanish ship, on the 6th of

[061-354]January, three clays after the battle, and arrived at Corunna on the

[061-355]14th. He was received with all the honours due to a crown prince by the

[061-356]Conde de Caracena, Governor of Galicia. Among other objects, he visited

[061-357]the remains of the tower of Betanzos, from which, according to Bardic
