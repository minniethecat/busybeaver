CHAPTER II.

[065-001]LAST YEARS OF JAMES—CONFISCATION OF THE MIDLAND COUNTIES—ACCESSION OF

[065-002]CHARLES I.—GRIEVANCES AND "GRACES"—ADMINISTRATION OF LORD STRAFFORD.

[065-003]From the dissolution of James's only Irish Parliament in October, 1615,

[065-004]until the tenth of Charles I.—an interval of twenty years—the

[065-005]government of the country was again exclusively regulated by arbitrary

[065-006]proclamations and orders in Council. Chichester, after the unusually

[065-007]long term of eleven years, had leave to retire in 1616; he was

[065-008]succeeded by the Lord Grandison, who held the office of Lord Deputy for

[065-009]six years, and he, in turn, by Henry Carey, Viscount Falkland, who

[065-010]governed from 1622 till 1629—seven years. Nothing could well be more

[065-011]fluctuating than the policy pursued at different periods by these

[065-012]Viceroys and their advisers; violent attempts at coercion alternated

[065-013]with the meanest devices to extort money from the oppressed; general

[065-014]declarations against recusants were repeated with increased vehemence,

[065-015]while particular treaties for a local and conditional toleration were

[065-016]notoriously progressing; in a word, the administration of affairs

[065-017]exhibited all the worst vices and weaknesses of a despotism, without

[065-018]any of the steadiness or magnanimity of a really paternal government.

[065-019]Some of the edicts issued deserve particular notice, as characterizing

[065-020]the administrations of Grandison and Falkland.

[065-022]The municipal authorities of Waterford, having invariably refused to

[065-023]take the oath of supremacy, were, by an order in Council, deprived of

[065-024]their ancient charter, which was withheld from them for nine years. The

[065-025]ten shilling tax on recusants for non-attendance at the Anglican

[065-026]service was rigorously enforced in other cities, and was almost

[065-027]invariably levied with costs, which not seldom swelled the ten

[065-028]shillings to ten pounds. A new instrument of oppression was also, in

[065-029]Lord Grandison's time, invented—"the Commission for the Discovery of

[065-030]Defective Titles." At the head of this Commission was placed Sir

[065-031]William Parsons, the Surveyor-General, who had come into the kingdom in

[065-032]a menial situation, and had, through a long half century of guile and

[065-033]cruelty, contributed as much to the destruction of its inhabitants, by

[065-034]the perversion of law, as any armed conqueror could have done by the

[065-035]edge of the sword. Ulster being already applotted, and Munster

[065-036]undergoing the manipulation of the new Earl of Cork, there remained as

[065-037]a field for the Parsons Commission only the Midland Counties and

[065-038]Connaught. Of these they made the most in the shortest space of time. A

[065-039]horde of clerkly spies were employed under the name of "Discoverers,"

[065-040]to ransack old Irish tenures in the archives of Dublin and London, with

[065-041]such good success, that in a very short time 66,000 acres in Wicklow,

[065-042]and 385,000 acres in Leitrim, Longford, the Meaths, and King's and

[065-043]Queen's Counties, were "found by inquisition to be vested in the

[065-044]Crown." The means employed by the Commissioners, in some cases, to

[065-045]elicit such evidence as they required, were of the most revolting

[065-046]description. In the Wicklow case, courts-martial were held, before

[065-047]which unwilling witnesses were tried on the charge of treason, and some

[065-048]actually put to death. Archer, one of the number, had his flesh burned

[065-049]with red hot iron, and was placed on a gridiron over a charcoal fire,

[065-050]till he offered to testify anything that was necessary. Yet on evidence

[065-051]so obtained whole baronies and counties were declared forfeited to the

[065-052]Crown.

[065-053]The recusants, though suffering under every sort of injustice, and kept

[065-054]in a state of continual apprehension—a condition worse even than the

[065-055]actual horrors they endured—counted many educated and wealthy persons

[065-056]in their ranks, besides mustering fully ninety per cent, of the whole

[065-057]population. They were, therefore, far from being politically powerless.

[065-058]The recall of Lord Grandison from the government was attributed to

[065-059]their direct or indirect influence upon the King. When James Usher,

[065-060]then Bishop of Meath, preached before his successor from the text "He

[065-061]beareth not the sword in vain," they were sufficiently formidable to

[065-062]compel him publicly to apologise for his violent allusions to their

[065-063]body. Perhaps, however, we should mainly see in the comparative

[065-064]toleration, extended by Lord Falkland, an effect of the diplomacy then

[065-065]going on, for the marriage of Prince Charles to the Infanta of Spain.

[065-066]When, in 1623, Pope Gregory XV. granted a dispensation for this

[065-067]marriage, James solemnly swore to, a private article of the marriage

[065-068]treaty, by which he bound himself to suspend the execution of the Penal

[065-069]laws, to procure their repeal in Parliament, and to grant a toleration

[065-070]of Catholic worship in private houses. But the Spanish match was

[065-071]unexpectedly broken off, immediately after his decease (June, 1625),

[065-072]whereupon Charles married Henrietta Maria, daughter of Henry IV. of

[065-073]France.

[065-074]The new monarch inherited from his father three kingdoms heaving in the

[065-075]throes of disaffection and rebellion. In England the most formidable of

[065-076]the malcontents were the Puritans, who reckoned many of the first

[065-077]nobility, and the ablest members of the House of Commons among their

[065-078]chiefs; the restoration of episcopacy, and the declaration by the

[065-079]subservient Parliament of Scotland, that no General Assembly should be

[065-080]called without the King's sanction, had laid the sure foundations of a

[065-081]religious insurrection in the North; while the events, which we have

[065-082]already described, filled the minds of all orders of men in Ireland

[065-083]with agitation and alarm. The marriage of Charles with Henrietta Maria

[065-084]gave a ray of assurance to the co-religionists of the young Queen, for

[065-085]they had not then discovered that it was ever the habit of the Stuarts

[065-086]"to sacrifice their friends to the fear of their enemies." While he was

[065-087]yet celebrating his nuptials at Whitehall, surrounded by Catholic

[065-088]guests, the House of Commons presented Charles "a pious petition,"

[065-089]praying him to put into force the laws against recusants; a prayer

[065-090]which he was compelled by motives of policy to answer in the

[065-091]affirmative. The magistrates of England received orders accordingly,

[065-092]and when the King of France remonstrated against this flagrant breach

[065-093]of one of the articles of the marriage treaty (the same included in the

[065-094]terms of the Spanish match), Charles answered that he had never looked

[065-095]on the promised toleration as anything but an artifice to secure the

[065-096]Papal dispensation. But the King's compliance failed to satisfy the

[065-097]Puritan party in the House of Commons, and that same year began their

[065-098]contest with the Crown, which ended only on the scaffold before

[065-099]Whitehall in 1648. Of their twenty-three years' struggle, except in so

[065-100]far as it enters directly into our narrative, we shall have little to

[065-101]say, beyond reminding the reader, from time to time, that though it

[065-102]occasionally lulled down it was never wholly allayed on either side.

[065-103]Irish affairs, in the long continued suspension of the functions of

[065-104]Parliament, were administered in general by the Privy Council, and in

[065-105]detail by three special courts, all established in defiance of ancient

[065-106]constitutional usage. These were the Court of Castle Chamber, modelled

[065-107]on the English Star Chamber, and the Ecclesiastical High Commissioners

[065-108]Court, both dating from 1563; and the Court of Wards and Liveries,

[065-109]originally founded by Henry VIII., but lately remodelled by James. The

[065-110]Castle Chamber was composed of certain selected members of the Privy

[065-111]Council acting in secret with absolute power; the High Commission Court

[065-112]was constituted under James and Charles, of the principal Archbishops

[065-113]and Bishops, with the Lord Deputy, Chancellor, Chief Justice, Master of

[065-114]the Rolls, Master of the Wards, and some others, laymen and jurists.

[065-115]They were armed with unlimited power "to visit, reform, redress, order,

[065-116]correct and amend, all such errors, heresies, schisms, abuses,

[065-117]offences, contempts and enormities," as came under the head of

[065-118]spiritual or ecclesiastical jurisdiction. They were, in effect, the

[065-119]Castle Chamber, acting as a spiritual tribunal of last resort; and were

[065-120]provided with their own officers, Registers and Receivers of Fines,

[065-121]Pursuivants, Criers and Gaolers. The Court of Wards exercised a

[065-122]jurisdiction, if possible, more repugnant to our first notions of

[065-123]liberty than that of the High Commission Court. It retained its

[065-124]original power "to bargain and sell the custody, wardship and

[065-125]marriage," of all the heirs of such persons of condition as died in the

[065-126]King's homage; but their powers, by royal letters patent of the year

[065-127]1617, were to be exercised by a Master of Wards, with an Attorney and

[065-128]Surveyor, all nominated by the Crown. The Court was entitled to farm

[065-129]all the property of its Wards during nonage, for the benefit of the

[065-130]Crown, "taking one year's rent from heirs male, and two from heirs

[065-131]female," for charges of stewardship. The first master, Sir William

[065-132]Parsons, was appointed in 1622, and confirmed at the beginning of the

[065-133]next reign, with a salary of 300 pounds per annum, and the right to

[065-134]rank next to the Chief Justice of the King's Bench at the Privy

[065-135]Council. By this appointment the minor heirs of all the Catholic

[065-136]proprietors were placed, both as to person and property, at the

[065-137]absolute disposal of one of the most intense anti-Catholic bigots that

[065-138]ever appeared on the scene of Irish affairs.

[065-139]In addition to these civil grievances an order had lately been issued

[065-140]to increase the army in Ireland by 5,000 men, and means of subsistence

[065-141]had to be found for that additional force, within the kingdom. In reply

[065-142]to the murmurs of the inhabitants, they were assured by Lord Falkland

[065-143]that the King was their friend, and that any just and temperate

[065-144]representation of their grievances would secure his careful and instant

[065-145]attention. So encouraged, the leading Catholics convoked a General

[065-146]Assembly of their nobility and gentry, "with several Protestants of

[065-147]rank," at Dublin, in the year 1628, in order to present a dutiful

[065-148]statement of their complaints to the King. The minutes of this

[065-149]important Assembly, it is to be feared, are for ever lost to us. We

[065-150]only know that it included a large number of landed proprietors, of

[065-151]whom the Catholics were still a very numerous section. "The entire

[065-152]proceedings of this Assembly," says Dr. Taylor, "were marked by wisdom

[065-153]and moderation. They drew up a number of articles, in the nature of a

[065-154]Bill of Rights, to which they humbly solicited the royal assent, and

[065-155]promised that, on their being granted, they would raise a voluntary

[065-156]assessment of 100,000 pounds for the use of the Crown. The principal

[065-157]articles in these 'graces,' as they were called, were provisions for

[065-158]the security of property, the due administration of justice, the

[065-159]prevention of military exactions, the freedom of trade, the better

[065-160]regulation of the clergy, and the restraining of the tyranny of the

[065-161]ecclesiastical courts. Finally, they provided that the Scots, who had

[065-162]been planted in Ulster, should be seemed in their possessions, and a

[065-163]general pardon granted for all offences." Agents were chosen to repair

[065-164]to England with this petition, and the Assembly, hoping for the best

[065-165]results, adjourned. But the ultra Protestant party had taken the alarm,

[065-166]and convoked a Synod at Dublin to counteract the General Assembly. This

[065-167]Synod vehemently protested against selling truth "as a slave," and

[065-168]"establishing for a price idolatry in its stead." They laid it down as

[065-169]a dogma of _their_ faith that "to grant Papists a toleration, or to

[065-170]consent that they may freely exercise their religion and profess their

[065-171]faith and doctrines, was a grievous sin;" wherefore they prayed God "to

[065-172]make those in authority zealous, resolute, and courageous against all

[065-173]Popery, superstition, and idolatry." This declaration of the extreme

[065-174]Protestants, including not only Usher, and the principal Bishops, but

[065-175]Chichester, Boyle, Parsons, and the most successful "Undertakers," all

[065-176]deeply imbued with Puritan notions, naturally found among their English

[065-177]brethren advocates and defenders. The King, who had lately, for the

[065-178]third time, renewed with France the articles of his marriage treaty,

[065-179]was placed in a most difficult position. He desired to save his own

[065-180]honour, he sorely needed the money of the Catholics, but he trembled

[065-181]before the compact, well organized fanaticism of the Puritans. In his

[065-182]distress he had recourse to a councillor, who, since the assassination

[065-183]of Buckingham, his first favourite, divided with Laud the royal

[065-184]confidence. This was Thomas, Lord Wentworth, better known by his

[065-185]subsequent title of Earl of Strafford, a statesman born to be the

[065-186]wonder and the bane of three kingdoms. Strafford (for such for

[065-187]clearness we must call him) boldly advised the King to grant "the

[065-188]graces" as his own personal act, to pocket the proposed subsidy, but to

[065-189]contrive that the promised concessions he was to make should never go

[065-190]into effect. This infamous deception was effected in this wise: the

[065-191]King signed, with his own hand, a schedule of fifty-one "graces," and

[065-192]received from the Irish agents in London bonds for 120,000 pounds,

[065-193](equal to ten times the amount at present), to be paid in three annual

[065-194]instalments of 40,000 pounds. He also agreed that Parliament should be

[065-195]immediately called in Ireland, to confirm these concessions, while at

[065-196]the same time he secretly instructed Lord Falkland to see that the

[065-197]writs of election were informally prepared, so that no Parliament could

[065-198]be held. This was accordingly done; the agents of the General Assembly

[065-199]paid their first instalment; the subscribers held the King's autograph;

[065-200]the writs were issued, but on being returned, were found to be

[065-201]technically incorrect, and so the legal confirmation of the graces was

[065-202]indefinitely postponed, under one pretext or another. As evidence of

[065-203]the national demands at this period, we should add, that beside the

[065-204]redress of minor grievances, the articles signed by the King provided

[065-205]that the recusants should be allowed to practise in the courts of law;

[065-206]to sue the livery of their lands out of the Court of Wards, on taking

[065-207]an oath of civil allegiance in lieu of the oath of supremacy; that the

[065-208]claims of the Crown to the forfeiture of estates, under the plea of

[065-209]defects of title, should not be held to extend beyond sixty years

[065-210]anterior to 1628; that the "Undertakers" should have time allowed them

[065-211]to fulfil the conditions of their leases; that the proprietors of

[065-212]Connaught should be allowed to make a new enrollment of their estates,

[065-213]and that a Parliament should be held. A royal proclamation announced

[065-214]these concessions, as existing in the royal intention, but, as we have

[065-215]already related, such promises proved to be worth no more than the

[065-216]paper on which they were written.

[065-218]In 1629 Lord Falkland, to disarm the Puritan outcry against him, had

[065-219]leave to withdraw, and for four years—an unusually long interregnum—the

[065-220]government was left in the hands of Robert Boyle, now Earl of Cork, and

[065-221]Adam Loftus, Viscount Ely, one of the well dowered offspring of Queen

[065-222]Elizabeth's Archbishop of Dublin. Ely held the office of Lord

[065-223]Chancellor, and Cork that of Lord High Treasurer; as Justices, they now

[065-224]combined in their own persons almost all the power and patronage of the

[065-225]kingdom. Both affected a Puritan austerity and enthusiasm, which barely

[065-226]cloaked a rapacity and bigotry unequalled in any former administration.

[065-227]In Dublin, on Saint Stephen's Day, 1629, the Protestant Archbishop,

[065-228]Bulkley, and the Mayor of the city, entered the Carmelite Chapel, at

[065-229]the head of a file of soldiers, dispersed the congregation, desecrated

[065-230]the altar, and arrested the officiating friars. The persecution was

[065-231]then taken up and repeated wherever the executive power was strong

[065-232]enough to defy the popular indignation. A Catholic seminary lately

[065-233]established in the capital was confiscated, and turned over to Trinity

[065-234]College as a training school. Fifteen religious houses, chiefly

[065-235]belonging to the Franciscan Order, which had hitherto escaped from the

[065-236]remoteness of their situation, were, by an order of the English

[065-237]Council, confiscated to the Crown, and their novices compelled to

[065-238]emigrate in order to complete their studies abroad. A reprimand from

[065-239]the King somewhat stayed the fury of the Justices, whose supreme power

[065-240]ended with Stafford's appointment in 1633.

[065-241]The advent of Stafford was characteristic of his whole course. The King

[065-242]sent over another letter concerning recusants, declaring that the laws

[065-243]against them, at the suggestion of the Lords Justices, should be put

[065-244]strictly in force. The Justices proved unwilling to enter this letter

[065-245]on the Council book, and it was accordingly withheld till Stafford's

[065-246]arrival, but the threat had the desired effect of drawing "a voluntary

[065-247]contribution" of 20,000 pounds out of the alarmed Catholics. Equipped

[065-248]partly with this money Stafford arrived in Dublin in July, 1633, and

[065-249]entered at once on the policy, which he himself designated by the one

[065-250]emphatic word—"THOROUGH." He took up his abode in the Castle,

[065-251]surrounded by a Body Guard, a force hitherto unknown at the Irish

[065-252]Court; he summoned only a select number of the Privy Council, and,

[065-253]having kept them waiting for hours, condescended to address them in a

[065-254]speech full of arrogance and menace. He declared his intention of

[065-255]maintaining and augmenting the army; advised them to amend their grants

[065-256]forthwith; told them frankly he had called them to Council, more out of

[065-257]courtesy than necessity, and ended by requiring from them a year's

[065-258]subsidy in advance. As this last request was accompanied by a positive

[065-259]promise to obtain the King's consent to the assembling of Parliament,

[065-260]it was at once granted; and soon after writs were issued for the

[065-261]meeting of both Houses in July following.

[065-262]When this long-prayed-for Parliament at last met, the Lord Deputy took

[065-263]good care that it should be little else than a tribunal to register his

[065-264]edicts. A great many officers of the army had been chosen as Burgesses,

[065-265]while the Sheriffs of counties were employed to secure the election of

[065-266]members favourable to the demands of the Crown. In the Parliament of

[065-267]1613 the recusants were, admitting all the returns to be correct,

[065-268]nearly one-half; but in that of 1634 they could not have exceeded

[065-269]one-third. The Lord Deputy nominated their Speaker, whom they did not

[065-270]dare to reject, and treated them invariably with the supreme contempt

[065-271]which no one knows so well how to exhibit towards a popular assembly as

[065-272]an apostate liberal. "Surely," he said in his speech from the throne,

[065-273]"so great a meanness cannot enter your hearts, as once to suspect his

[065-274]Majesty's gracious regard of you, and performance with you, once you

[065-275]affix yourselves upon his grace." His object in this appeal was the

[065-276]sordid and commonplace one—to obtain more money without rendering value

[065-277]for it. He accordingly carried through four whole subsidies of 50,000

[065-278]pounds sterling each in the session of 1634; and two additional

[065-279]subsidies of the same amount at the opening of the next session. The

[065-280]Parliament, having thus answered his purpose, was summarily dissolved

[065-281]in April, 1635, and for four years more no other was called. During

[065-282]both sessions he had contrived, according to his agreement with the

[065-283]King, to postpone indefinitely the act which was to have confirmed "the

[065-284]graces," guaranteed in 1628. He even contrived to get a report of a

[065-285]Committee of the House of Commons, and the opinions of some of the

[065-286]Judges, against legislating on the subject at all, which report gave

[065-287]King Charles "a great deal of contentment."

[065-288]With sufficient funds in hand for the ordinary expenses of the

[065-289]government, Strafford applied himself earnestly to the self-elected

[065-290]task of making his royal master "as absolute as any King in

[065-291]Christendom" on the Irish side of the channel. The plantation of

[065-292]Connaught, delayed by the late King's death, and abandoned among the

[065-293]new King's graces, was resumed as a main engine of obtaining more

[065-294]money. The proprietary of that Province had, in the thirteenth year of

[065-295]the late reign, paid 3,000 pounds into the Record Office at Dublin, for

[065-296]the registration of their deeds, but the entries not being made by the

[065-297]clerk employed, the title to every estate in the five western counties

[065-298]was now called in question. The "Commissioners to Inquire into

[065-299]Defective Titles" were let loose upon the devoted Province, with Sir

[065-300]William Parsons at their head, and the King's title to the whole of

[065-301]Mayo, Sligo and Roscommon, was found by packed, bribed, or intimidated

[065-302]juries; the grand jury of Galway having refused to find a similar

[065-303]verdict, were summoned to the Court of Castle Chamber, sentenced to pay

[065-304]a fine of 4,000 pounds each to the Crown, and the Sheriff that

[065-305]empanelled them, a fine of 1,000 pounds. The lawyers who pleaded for

[065-306]the actual proprietors were stripped of their gowns, the sheriff died

[065-307]in prison, and the work of spoliation proceeded. The young Earl of

[065-308]Ormond was glad to compound for a portion of his estates; the Earl of

[065-309]Kildare was committed to prison for refusing a similar composition; the

[065-310]Earl of Cork was compelled to pay a heavy fine for his intrusion into

[065-311]lands originally granted to the Church; the O'Byrnes of Wicklow

[065-312]commuted for 15,000 pounds, and the London Companies, for their Derry

[065-313]estates, paid no less than 70,000 pounds: a forced contribution for

[065-314]which those frugal citizens never forgave the thorough-going Deputy. By

[065-315]these means, and others less violent, such as bounties to the linen

[065-316]trade, he raised the annual revenue of the kingdom to 80,000 pounds a

[065-317]year, and was enabled to embody for the King's service an army of

[065-318]10,000 foot and 1,000 horse.

[065-319]These arbitrary measures were entirely in consonance with the wishes of

[065-320]Charles. In a visit to England in 1636, the King assured Strafford

[065-321]personally of his cordial approbation of all he had done, encouraged

[065-322]him to proceed fearlessly in the same course, and conferred on him the

[065-323]higher rank of Lord Lieutenant. Three years later, on the first rumour

[065-324]of a Scottish invasion of England, Strafford was enabled to remit his

[065-325]master 30,000 pounds from the Irish Treasury, and to tender the

[065-326]services of the Anglo-Irish army, as he thought they could be safely

[065-327]dispensed with by the country in which they had been thus far recruited

[065-328]and maintained.
