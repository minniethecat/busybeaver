## Overview

Malachy II (Mael Sechnaill mac Domnaill, 948–1022), better known in Irish annals as Melaghlin, was High King of Ireland from the Southern Uí Néill dynasty. Fifth in direct descent from Malachy I, he was the last great representative of the Uí Néill monopoly on the High Kingship before the rise of [[Brian Boru]]. (012-295) (013-002)

His story is so interwoven with the still-more famous career of [[brian-boru|Brian Boru]] that the two cannot be understood separately. Malachy yielded the High Kingship to Brian in 1002, fought alongside him at the [[battle-of-clontarf|Battle of Clontarf]] in 1014, and then reclaimed the throne after Brian's death, reigning until his own death in 1022. (012-324) (014-002) (016-017)

## Biography

### Rise to the High Kingship

Malachy II was the son of Donald, son of Donogh, of the Clan Colman branch of the Southern Uí Néill. The Uí Néill dynasty had held the High Kingship almost continuously since the time of Niall of the Nine Hostages in the fifth century. Malachy succeeded to the throne of Meath and, through a combination of military skill and dynastic prestige, was recognized as Ard-Righ (High King) of Ireland. (012-295) (012-010)

Malachy II was in the most favourable position possible to restore the ancient supremacy of the Uí Néill. He commanded the resources of Meath, the richest and most central of the Irish kingdoms, and inherited the prestige of his ancestors. (013-029)

### Alliance with Brian Boru

In the late tenth century, Malachy formed an alliance with [[Brian Boru]], then King of Munster, to confront the growing power of the Norse in [[dublin|Dublin]] and the threat of the [[dermid-mcmurrogh|King of Leinster]]. Around 987, Brian and Malachy jointly defeated the Danes at the Battle of Glen-Mama in Wicklow, a victory that established their military partnership. (020-248)

However, the alliance eventually gave way to rivalry. Brian's ambition and Malachy's weakening political position led to a transfer of power. After a complex diplomatic struggle lasting several years, Malachy yielded the High Kingship to Brian in 1002, marking the first time the supreme power passed outside the Uí Néill dynasty. (014-002)

### The Battle of Clontarf

Malachy II did not contest Brian's supremacy but instead became his most important ally. At the climactic [[battle-of-clontarf|Battle of Clontarf]] in 1014, Malachy fought on Brian's side against the Norse and [[leinster|Leinster]] coalition, contributing his Meath forces to the Irish victory.

After Brian's death at Clontarf, Malachy II promptly followed up the victory by the destruction of the remaining Norse strongholds. He reclaimed the High Kingship, which he held until his death in 1022, providing a period of stability after the upheavals of Brian's reign. (016-017) (015-071)

### Final Reign

The death of Malachy II marks an important turning point in Irish history. After his passing, the history of Ireland is mainly the history of contested successions and the rise of provincial powers. The question of whether some of the seven kings subsequent to Malachy II who assumed the title of Ard-Righ were truly High Kings in the old sense remained a matter of debate among the Annalists. (015-071) (017-043)

Malachy's reign, both before and after Brian, represented the last flowering of the old Uí Néillé high-kingship. His collaboration with [[brian-boru|Brian Boru]] and his steadfast support at Clontarf demonstrated a statesmanship that transcended mere dynastic rivalry. (013-002)

## Legacy

Malachy II is remembered as a king of both peace and war. He held the High Kingship twice—first by inheritance, then by merit. His willingness to yield to [[brian-boru|Brian Boru]] when the national interest required it, and his courage in fighting alongside his former rival at Clontarf, mark him as a figure of unusual political wisdom for his age. (012-324) (020-248)

The Uí Néill dynasty declined rapidly after Malachy's death, never again producing a king who could command the allegiance of all Ireland. His descendants remained powerful in Meath, but the age of the provincial kingdoms had arrived, paving the way for the [[Norman Invasion of Ireland]] a century and a half later. (015-071)
