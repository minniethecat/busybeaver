## Overview

William Pitt the Younger (1759–1806) was twice Prime Minister of Great Britain and the dominant figure in British politics during the revolutionary era. His Irish policy, driven by the Regency Crisis of 1789 and the security imperatives of the French Revolutionary Wars, culminated in the Act of Union of 1800 — the most consequential British intervention in Irish constitutional affairs of the century.

## The Regency Crisis and Its Aftermath

Pitt's determination to bring about a legislative union between Great Britain and Ireland was powerfully influenced by the conduct of the Irish Parliament during the Regency Crisis of 1788–89. When George III fell incapacitated, the British Houses and people supported Pitt's restrictive regency bill, but the Irish Parliament resolved to offer the crown of Ireland unconditionally to the Prince of Wales. (097-095, 097-096, 097-097, 097-098)

The Lord Lieutenant, the Marquis of Buckingham, declined to transmit their address, and by the time the Irish delegation reached London, George III had recovered. They received a gracious reception at Carlton House, but they incurred the implacable enmity of William Pitt, and created a second determining cause in his mind in favour of an early legislative union. (097-100, 097-101, 097-102, 097-103, 097-104)

## The Path to Union

In the years following the Regency Crisis, Pitt's Irish policy converged with the security anxieties generated by the French Revolution. The outbreak of war with revolutionary France in 1793, the attempted French invasion of Ireland in 1796, and the rebellion of 1798 all reinforced Pitt's conviction that the Irish Parliament could not be trusted with the security of the empire.

Pitt brought to the union project his considerable political resources. His ally Lord Castlereagh served as Chief Secretary in Dublin and managed the passage of the Union through the Irish Parliament. Pitt committed the British government's credit and patronage to securing the necessary majority, promising Catholic emancipation as the corollary of union — a promise that would prove politically fatal when George III refused to honour it.

> No English statesman, since the death of William Pitt, has wielded so great a power in Irish affairs as Sir Robert Peel. (109-184, 109-185, 109-186)

## Pitt's Resignation and Death

When the Union passed, Pitt pressed for Catholic emancipation as he had promised. George III's refusal led to Pitt's resignation in 1801. He returned to office in 1804, but the emancipation question remained unresolved. Pitt died in January 1806, at the age of forty-six, worn out by the burdens of wartime premiership.

His death removed from British politics the one figure of sufficient authority to have carried Catholic emancipation a generation earlier than it was eventually conceded. The delay condemned Ireland to another quarter-century of sectarian grievance, culminating in the mass mobilisation that brought about emancipation in 1829.

## Legacy

Pitt's Irish legacy is deeply contested. To his admirers, the Union was a necessary measure of imperial security in an age of revolution. To his critics, it was an act of political revenge and colonial subordination, purchased with corruption and made permanent by broken promises. Whichever view one takes, Pitt's influence on the course of modern Irish history was decisive — and the constitutional settlement he imposed in 1800 defined Anglo-Irish relations for the next 120 years.
