## Overview

Frederick North, 2nd Earl of Guilford (1732–1792), commonly known as Lord North, served as Prime Minister of Great Britain from 1770 to 1782. His administration confronted two simultaneous imperial crises — the American Revolution and the Irish demand for free trade and legislative autonomy.

North's handling of Irish affairs, culminating in the repeal of Poynings' Law and the Constitution of 1782, marked a decisive shift in the relationship between Britain and Ireland.

## The Irish Volunteers and Free Trade

By 1779, the American war had drained British military resources from Ireland, leaving the country exposed to the threat of French invasion. The Irish Volunteers — a citizen militia raised for self-defence — swelled to over 40,000 armed men, and their demands rapidly extended from defence to commercial and constitutional reform.

The Irish Parliament, emboldened by the Volunteer movement, passed resolutions demanding the removal of trade restrictions. The language of the opposition was directed more fiercely at the British ministry than at the American insurgents:

> The 4,000 men who first volunteered were pointed against Lord North than against the colonies. (094-023)

The Volunteers paraded before Parliament with cannon bearing the inscription "Free Trade or This." North's government, unable to resist the combination of Volunteer pressure and parliamentary agitation, conceded free trade for Ireland in December 1779. The concession was a significant retreat from the mercantilist restrictions that had limited Irish commerce for the preceding century.

## The Road to Legislative Independence

The free trade victory only intensified the demand for constitutional reform. The Irish Parliament, at the urging of Henry Grattan and the Patriot Party, pressed for the repeal of Poynings' Law and the Declaratory Act of 1719 — the twin pillars of British legislative supremacy over Ireland. (095-036)

At length, Lord North's administration was forced to confront the Irish question directly. The Patriot leaders, led by Grattan, were determined to achieve full legislative independence, and the Volunteer convention at Dungannon in February 1782 gave their demands the backing of an armed national movement. (095-099)

> Lord North, who, with the King, had long resisted such a concession, was now at length driven to yield. (095-099)

## The Fall of North's Ministry

The fall of Lord North's administration in March 1782 — precipitated by the British defeat at Yorktown — opened the door to the final settlement of the Irish question. The new Rockingham ministry, which included Charles James Fox, moved quickly to repeal the Declaratory Act and grant Ireland legislative independence. (095-168)

North briefly returned to office in 1783 as part of the famous Fox-North coalition ministry. This unlikely alliance between the former prime minister and his long-time opponent Charles James Fox governed for a short period, but it did not significantly alter the settlement that had been reached with Ireland the previous year. (096-186)

## Legacy

Lord North's role in Irish history is primarily that of the reluctant conceder. His administration resisted Irish demands for free trade and constitutional reform until the combination of Volunteer arms and Patriot eloquence made continued resistance untenable.

By yielding when he did, North permitted the peaceful achievement of legislative independence in 1782 — a settlement that established the Irish Parliament's autonomy for the next eighteen years until the Act of Union of 1800.
