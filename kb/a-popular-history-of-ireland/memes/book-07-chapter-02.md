CHAPTER II.

[049-001]THE INSURRECTION OF SILKEN THOMAS—THE GERALDINE LEAGUE—ADMINISTRATION

[049-002]OF LORD LEONARD GRAY.

[049-003]The ninth and last _Catholic_ Earl of Kildare, in the ninth year of

[049-004]Henry VIII., had been summoned to London to answer two charges

[049-005]preferred against him by his political enemies: "1st, That he had

[049-006]enriched himself and his followers out of the crown lands and revenues.

[049-007]2nd, That he had formed alliances and corresponded with divers Irish

[049-008]enemies of the State." Pending these charges the Earl of Surrey, the

[049-009]joint-victor with his father at Flodden field, was despatched to Dublin

[049-010]in his stead, with the title of Lord Lieutenant.

[049-011]Kildare, by the advice of Wolsey, was retained in a sort of honourable

[049-012]attendance on the person of the King for nearly four years. During this

[049-013]interval he accompanied Henry to "the field of the cloth of Gold," so

[049-014]celebrated in French and English chronicles. On his return to Dublin,

[049-015]in 1523, he found his enemy, the Earl of Ormond, in his old office, but

[049-016]had the pleasure of supplanting him one year afterwards. In 1525, on

[049-017]the discovery of Desmond's correspondence with Francis of France, he

[049-018]was ordered to march into Munster and arrest that nobleman. But, though

[049-019]he obeyed the royal order, Desmond successfully evaded him, not, as was

[049-020]alleged, without his friendly connivance. The next year this evasion

[049-021]was made the ground of a fresh impeachment by the implacable Earl of

[049-022]Ormond; he was again summoned to London, and committed to the Tower. In

[049-023]1530 he was liberated, and sent over with Sir William Skeffington,

[049-024]whose authority to some extent he shared. The English Knight had the

[049-025]title of Deputy, but Kildare was, in effect, Captain General, as the

[049-026]Red Earl had formerly been. Skeffington was instructed to obey him in

[049-027]the field, while it was expected that the Earl, in return, would

[049-028]sustain his colleague in the Council. A year had not passed before they

[049-029]were declared enemies, and Skeffington was recalled to England, where

[049-030]he added another to the number of Kildare's enemies. After a short term

[049-031]of undisputed power, the latter found himself, in 1533, for the third

[049-032]time, an inmate of the Tower. It is clear that the impetuous Earl,

[049-033]after his second escape, had not conducted himself as prudently as one

[049-034]so well forewarned ought to have done. He played more openly than ever

[049-035]the twofold part of Irish Chief among the Irish, and English Baron

[049-036]within the Pale. His daughters were married to the native lords of

[049-037]Offally and Ely, and he frequently took part as arbitrator in the

[049-038]affairs of those clans. The anti-Geraldine faction were not slow to

[049-039]torture these facts to suit themselves. They had been strengthened at

[049-040]Dublin by three English officials, Archbishop Allan, his relative John

[049-041]Allan, afterwards Master of the Rolls, and Robert Cowley, the Chief

[049-042]Solicitor, Lord Ormond's confidential agent. The reiterated

[049-043]representations of these personages induced the suspicious and

[049-044]irascible King to order the Earl's attendance at London, authorizing

[049-045]him at the same time to appoint a substitute, for whose conduct he

[049-046]would be answerable. Kildare nominated his son, Lord Thomas, though not

[049-047]yet of man's age; after giving him many sage advices, he sailed for

[049-048]England, no more to return.

[049-050]The English interest at that moment had apparently reached the lowest

[049-051]point. The O'Briens had bridged the Shannon, and enforced their ancient

[049-052]claims over Limerick. So defenceless, at certain periods, was Dublin

[049-053]itself that Edmond Oge O'Byrne surprised the Castle by night, liberated

[049-054]the prisoners, and carried off the stores. This daring achievement,

[049-055]unprecedented even in the records of the fearless mountaineers of

[049-056]Wicklow, was thrown in to aggravate the alleged offences of Kildare. He

[049-057]was accused, moreover, of having employed the King's great guns and

[049-058]other munitions of war to strengthen his own Castles of Maynooth and

[049-059]Ley—a charge more direct and explicit than had been alleged against him

[049-060]at any former period.

[049-061]While the Earl lay in London Tower, an expedient very common afterwards

[049-062]in our history—the forging of letters and despatches—was resorted to by

[049-063]his enemies in Dublin, to drive the young Lord Thomas into some rash

[049-064]act which might prove fatal to his father and himself. Accordingly the

[049-065]packets brought from Chester, in the spring of 1534, repeated reports,

[049-066]one confirming the other, of the execution of the Earl in the Tower.

[049-067]Nor was there anything very improbable in such an occurrence. The cruel

[049-068]character of Henry had, in these same spring months, been fully

[049-069]developed in the execution of the reputed prophetess, Elizabeth Barton,

[049-070]and all her abettors. The most eminent layman in England, Sir Thomas

[049-071]More, and the most illustrious ecclesiastic, Bishop Fisher, had at the

[049-072]same time been found guilty of misprision of treason for having known

[049-073]of the pretended prophecies of Elizabeth without communicating their

[049-074]knowledge to the King. That an Anglo-Irish Earl, even of the first

[049-075]rank, could hope to fare better at the hands of the tyrant than his

[049-076]aged tutor and his trusted Chancellor, was not to be expected. When,

[049-077]therefore, Lord Thomas Fitzgerald flung down the sword of State on the

[049-078]Council table, in the hall of St. Mary's Abbey, on the 11th day of

[049-079]June, 1534, and formally renounced his allegiance to King Henry as the

[049-080]murderer of his father, although he betrayed an impetuous and impolitic

[049-081]temper, there was much in the events of the times to justify his belief

[049-082]in the rumours of his father's execution.

[049-083]This renunciation of allegiance was a declaration of open war. The

[049-084]chapter thus opened in the memoirs of the Leinster Geraldines closed at

[049-085]Tyburn on the 3rd of February, 1537. Within these three years, the

[049-086]policy of annexation was hastened by several events—but by none more

[049-087]than this unconcerted, unprepared, reckless revolt. The advice of the

[049-088]imprisoned Earl to his son had been "to play the gentlest part," but

[049-089]youth and rash counsels overcame the suggestions of age and experience.

[049-090]One great excess stained the cause of "Silken Thomas," while it was but

[049-091]six weeks old. Towards the end of July, Archbishop Allan, his father's

[049-092]deadly enemy, left his retreat in the Castle, and put to sea by night,

[049-093]hoping to escape into England. The vessel, whether by design or

[049-094]accident, ran ashore at Clontarf, and the neighbourhood being overrun

[049-095]by the insurgents, the Archbishop concealed himself at Artane. Here he

[049-096]was discovered, dragged from his bed, and murdered, if not in the

[049-097]actual presence, under the same roof with Lord Thomas. King Henry's

[049-098]Bishops hurled against the assassins the greater excommunication, with

[049-099]all its penalties; a terrific malediction, which was, perhaps, more

[049-100]than counterbalanced by the Papal Bull issued against Henry and Anne

[049-101]Boleyn on the last day of August—the knowledge of which must have

[049-102]reached Ireland before the end of the year. This Bull cited Henry to

[049-103]appear within ninety days in person, or by attorney, at Rome, to answer

[049-104]for his offences against the Apostolic See; failing which, he was

[049-105]declared excommunicated, his subjects were absolved from their

[049-106]allegiance, and commanded to take up arms against their former

[049-107]sovereign. The ninety days expired with the month of November, 1534.

[049-108]Lord Thomas, as he acted without consultation with others, so he was

[049-109]followed but by few persons of influence. His brothers-in-law, the

[049-110]chiefs of Ely and Offally, O'Moore of Leix, two of his five uncles, his

[049-111]relatives, the Delahides, mustered their adherents, and rallied to his

[049-112]standard. He held the castles of Carlow, Maynooth, Athy, and other

[049-113]strongholds in Kildare. He besieged Dublin, and came to a composition

[049-114]with the citizens, by which they agreed to allow him free ingress to

[049-115]assail the Castle, into which his enemies had withdrawn. He despatched

[049-116]agents to the Emperor, Charles V., and the Pope, but before those

[049-117]agents could well have returned—March, 1535—Maynooth had been assaulted

[049-118]and taken by Sir William Skeffington—and the bands collected by the

[049-119]young lord had melted away. Lord Leonard Gray, his maternal uncle,

[049-120]assumed the command for the King of England, instead of Skeffington,

[049-121]disabled by sickness, and the abortive insurrection was extinguished in

[049-122]one campaign. Towards the end of August, 1535, the unfortunate Lord

[049-123]Thomas surrendered on the guarantee of Lord Leonard and Lord Butler; in

[049-124]the following year his five uncles—three of whom had never joined in

[049-125]the rising—were treacherously seized at a banquet given to them by

[049-126]Gray, and were all, with their nephew, executed at Tyburn, on the 3rd

[049-127]of February, 1537. The imprisoned Earl having died in the Tower on the

[049-128]12th of December, 1534, the sole survivor of this historic house was

[049-129]now a child of twelve years of age, whose life was sought with an

[049-130]avidity equal to Herod's, but who was protected with a fidelity which

[049-131]defeated every attempt to capture him. Alternately the guest of his

[049-132]aunts married to the chiefs of Offally and Donegal, the sympathy

[049-133]everywhere felt for him led to a confederacy between the Northern and

[049-134]Southern Chiefs, which had long been wanting. A loose league was

[049-135]formed, including the O'Neils of both branches, O'Donnell, O'Brien, the

[049-136]Earl of Desmond, and the chiefs of Moylurg and Breffni. The lad, the

[049-137]object of so much natural and chivalrous affection, was harboured for a

[049-138]time in Munster, thence transported through Connaught into Donegal, and

[049-139]finally, after four years, in which he engaged more of the minds of

[049-140]statesmen than any other individual under the rank of royalty, was

[049-141]safely landed in France. We shall meet him again in another reign,

[049-142]under more fortunate auspices.

[049-143]Lord Leonard Gray continued in office as Deputy for nearly five years

[049-144](1535-40). This interval was marked by several successes against

[049-145]detached clans and the parties to the Geraldine league, whom he was

[049-146]careful to attack only in succession. In his second campaign, O'Brien's

[049-147]bridge was carried and demolished, one O'Brien was set up against

[049-148]another, and one O'Conor against another; the next year the Castle of

[049-149]Dungannon was taken from O'Neil, and Dundrum from Magennis. In 1539, he

[049-150]defeated O'Neil and O'Donnell, at Bolahoe, on the borders of Farney, in

[049-151]Monaghan, with a loss of 400 men, and the spoils they had taken from

[049-152]the English of Navan and Ardee. The Mayors of Dublin and Drogheda were

[049-153]knighted on the field for the valour they had shown at the head of

[049-154]their train-bands. The same year, he made a successful incursion into

[049-155]the territory of the Earl of Desmond, receiving the homage of many of

[049-156]the inferior lords, and exonerating them from the exactions of those

[049-157]haughty Palatines. Recalled to England in 1540, he, too, in turn, fell

[049-158]a victim to the sanguinary spirit of King Henry, and perished on the

[049-159]scaffold.
