## Overview

Henry Grattan (1746–1820) was the greatest orator and parliamentary leader of eighteenth-century Ireland. Son of the Recorder of [[dublin|Dublin]], Grattan rose to prominence as the leader of the Patriot Party in the [[irish-parliament|Irish Parliament]], where he secured legislative independence from Great Britain in 1782. (094-039)

His later career was defined by his principled opposition to the Act of Union in 1800, and his subsequent service as a Member of the British Parliament at Westminster, where he continued to advocate for Catholic emancipation and Irish rights until his death. (105-109) (110-252)

## Biography

### Early Life and Education

Henry Grattan was born in [[dublin|Dublin]] in 1746, the son of James Grattan, the Recorder of [[dublin|Dublin]] and a member of the [[irish-parliament|Irish House of Commons]]. He was educated at Trinity College [[dublin|Dublin]] and subsequently studied law at the Middle Temple in London, though he was drawn to politics rather than legal practice. (094-039)

He entered the [[irish-parliament|Irish Parliament]] in 1775 as the protégé of Lord Charlemont, the leader of the Patriot Party, and quickly established himself as one of the most powerful speakers in the House. His oratory combined classical elegance with fierce moral conviction, and his popularity in the country grew rapidly. (105-412)

### Legislative Independence

The defining achievement of Grattan's career was the securing of Irish legislative independence in 1782. Following the American Revolution, the Irish Volunteers, a citizen militia formed to defend Ireland against French invasion, demanded constitutional reform. Grattan seized the moment to press for the repeal of the Declaratory Act (1720), which had subordinated the [[irish-parliament|Irish Parliament]] to Westminster.

On April 16, 1782, Grattan moved the Declaration of Irish Rights in the [[irish-parliament|Irish House of Commons]]. The motion carried, and the British government, weakened by the American war, conceded. The period from 1782 to 1800 became known as "Grattan's Parliament."

### Rivalry and Division

The unity of the Patriot Party was soon tested by disputes over parliamentary reform and Catholic relief. On one major dispute, Henry Flood and Henry Grattan found themselves on opposite sides, and the rivalry between the two leading patriots weakened the movement. (096-052)

Grattan, to his credit, consistently supported the admission of Catholics to political rights, a position that placed him in advance of many of his Protestant contemporaries. His advocacy for the Catholic cause earned him the lasting respect of Catholic leaders, including [[Daniel O'Connell]].

### Opposition to the Union

When the British government proposed the legislative [[act-of-union|Union of Great Britain and Ireland]] in 1799–1800, Grattan emerged as its most eloquent opponent. Though seriously ill, he was carried into the House to deliver a speech against the measure. Despite his efforts, the Act of Union passed in 1800, extinguishing the [[irish-parliament|Irish Parliament]] he had done so much to empower. (106-067)

Grattan's opposition to the Union was not based on mere sentiment but on a reasoned conviction that Ireland's interests could not be adequately represented in a distant parliament at Westminster. His speeches against the Union remain among the classics of Irish political oratory.

### Final Years at Westminster

After the Union, Grattan reluctantly accepted a seat in the British House of Commons, where he served from 1805 until his death in 1820. He continued to press for Catholic emancipation, though his health was failing and his influence diminished in the larger imperial parliament. (105-109)

So worthily ended the mortal career of Henry Grattan. He was buried in Westminster Abbey, a tribute to his standing among British as well as Irish statesmen. His legacy as the father of Irish parliamentary nationalism endured, inspiring the next generation of Irish leaders. (110-252)

## Legacy

Grattan's Parliament, though limited in its achievements by the restricted franchise and the exclusion of Catholics, represented a golden age of Irish political independence. The principles he articulated—legislative autonomy, religious equality, and the rights of the Irish people to govern themselves—became the foundation of the Irish nationalist tradition.

His name was a significant coincidence in the campaign for Catholic emancipation, formally invoked by later reformers. The ideals of 1782 were repeatedly recalled by Irish nationalists throughout the nineteenth century as the standard towards which they strove. (105-109)
