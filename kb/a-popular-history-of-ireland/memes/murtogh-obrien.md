## Overview

Murtogh O'Brien, also known as Murkertach O'Brien and surnamed _Mór_ ("the Great"), was a High King of Ireland and King of Munster from the Dál gCais dynasty. He was the grandson of [[Brian Boru]] and one of the most powerful Irish rulers of the early twelfth century, extending his authority from Munster across much of the island. (018-010) (017-189)

## Rise to Power

Murtogh O'Brien was of nearly the same age as his northern rival Domnall Ua Lochlainn (Donald of Aileach). (018-010) As a descendant of Brian Boru, he inherited the O'Brien claim to supremacy over [[Ireland]], and by the end of the eleventh century he had established himself as the dominant figure in the southern half of the country.

> Murkertach, or Murtogh, afterwards called _More_, or the great. (017-189)

The Norse-Gaelic lords of [[Dublin]] recognised his authority: the Norse king Magnus Barefoot contracted his son Sigurd with the daughter of Murkertach O'Brien, sealing a political alliance between the Irish and Norse dynasties. (016-115)

## Conflict with the North

Murtogh's power was contested by the northern Uí Néill under Domnall Ua Lochlainn. The rivalry between north and south defined Murtogh's reign. Notwithstanding the solemn compact of 1094, Murtogh did not long cease hostilities with the northern power. (018-028) His annual military campaign was usually along the River Shannon, the traditional boundary between the northern and southern spheres of influence. (018-035)

Murtogh besieged Armagh for a week, which Domnall of Aileach defended. (018-080) In a subsequent battle, the northern force defeated one division of Murtogh's allies. (018-082) The conflict between the O'Brien of the south and the Ua Lochlainn of the north was the central political dynamic of the period.

## Relations with the Normans

Murtogh's reign coincided with the period of Norman expansion following the conquest of England in 1066. When a boast attributed to William the Conqueror was repeated to Murkertach O'Brien, he replied:

> "Hath the great William come hither with his ships, or is he coming? If he be in the land, let him know that I, Murkertach, am King of [[Ireland]]." (022-102)

This defiant response captured the self-confidence of the Irish kings in the pre-Norman period. An alliance was later formed when the Earl of Chester obtained a daughter of Murkertach O'Brien in marriage. (022-157)

## Later Years and Death

In his later years, Murtogh O'Brien attended a synod at which the ecclesiastical affairs of the kingdom were regulated, a significant interposition of royal authority into church matters. (018-106) Three years later, Murtogh O'Brien died, having been the dominant political figure in [[Ireland]] for over two decades. (018-106)

His nephew succeeded him, but the O'Brien power declined after Murtogh's death. (018-139) Some historians refer to his successor as his half-brother rather than nephew. (018-132) Murtogh gave law to territories far south of his Munster homeland, extending O'Brien authority to its greatest reach since the days of Brian Boru. (019-106)

## Legacy

Murtogh O'Brien was the last great king of the O'Brien line before the Norman invasion of 1169–1171. His reign represented the high-water mark of Munster power in the twelfth century. His diplomatic marriages with the Norse and Norman aristocracy demonstrated the integration of Irish dynasties into the wider political world of medieval Europe. After his death in 1119, no O'Brien ruler would again command the same degree of authority across [[Ireland]], and the path was laid for the eventual intervention of the Anglo-Normans under [[Henry II]].
