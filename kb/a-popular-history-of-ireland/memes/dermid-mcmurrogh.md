**Dermid McMurrogh** (c. 1110–1171), also known as Diarmaid Mac Murchadha, was King of Leinster and a central figure in one of the most consequential episodes of Irish history: the Anglo-Norman invasion. His personal ambition and political misfortunes opened the door for [[Henry II]] and [[Strongbow]], transforming the course of Irish sovereignty for the next eight centuries.

## King of Leinster

Dermid McMurrogh succeeded to the kingship of [[leinster|Leinster]] as a young man, inheriting the ancestral lands of the Uí Cheinnselaig dynasty. Of Moanmore, Dermid McMurrogh, King of Leinster, of whom we now speak, was one of the most remarkable figures of his age (019-032). [[leinster|Leinster]] was then one of the five major provinces, its territory encompassing the rich lands of the southeast, including the Norse-Irish trading port of [[Dublin]].

As a provincial king, Dermid pursued an aggressive policy of expansion, which brought him into conflict with his neighbours. His most fateful rivalry was with [[Rory O'Conor]], the High King of Ireland. The O'Conors of the west competed with Dermid's dynasty for supremacy, and hostilities between them escalated into open war. Dermid was compelled to submit to Roderick, and the ravager burned his own city and retreated into his fastnesses (019-199).

## Exile and Appeal to Henry II

Dermid's fortunes reached their nadir when his enemies combined against him. Rory O'Conor and his allies invaded [[leinster|Leinster]], and demanded hostages from Dermid McMurrogh (019-195). Thirteen hostages were given, but the submission was only temporary. Dermid resolved on a desperate remedy — to seek foreign aid.

He crossed the sea to Aquitaine, where [[henry-ii|Henry II]] of England was then holding court. The result of Dermid McMurrogh's interview with [[henry-ii|Henry II]], in Aquitaine, determined the fate of Ireland for centuries (023-003). Henry, preoccupied with continental affairs, gave Dermid letters patent authorising him to recruit mercenaries from among the Norman-Welsh marcher lords. This seemingly limited concession would prove the thin end of a very large wedge.

## Alliance with Strongbow and the Invasion

The most significant recruit Dermid found was [[strongbow|Richard de Clare]], [[strongbow|Earl of Pembroke]], known to history as [[Strongbow]]. In exchange for [[strongbow|Strongbow]]'s military assistance, Dermid offered the hand of his daughter Aoife in marriage and — critically — the succession to the kingdom of [[leinster|Leinster]] itself. This promise, though legally dubious under Irish succession law, was precisely the kind of personal arrangement that Norman feudal tradition recognised.

> Dermid confederated with Dermid McMurrogh, and from which Dublin drew its formidable garrison of Norman adventurers. (073-016)

With Norman-Welsh knights and archers at his back, Dermid returned to Ireland in 1167. The initial force was modest — a few hundred men — but their armoured cavalry and disciplined archers proved devastating against the more lightly armed Irish. [[dublin|Dublin]] fell to the allies, and Dermid was restored to his kingdom with a vengeance.

## Death and Legacy

Dermid McMurrogh died in 1171, before he could witness the full consequences of his actions. [[strongbow|Strongbow]], by the terms of their agreement, claimed the kingship of [[leinster|Leinster]]. [[henry-ii|Henry II]], alarmed at the prospect of an independent Norman kingdom on his western flank, arrived in Ireland himself in October 1171 to assert his authority — the first English king to set foot on Irish soil.

The judgment of history upon Dermid has been uniformly harsh. Generations of Irish historians have seen him as the traitor who sold his country. Even in death, his reputation was pursued — in the nineteenth century, his burial place was said to have been desecrated. A later generation sought to bury the ancestor of Dermid McMurrogh with the carcass of a dog, such was the odium that clung to his name (107-078).

Yet the forces that brought about the Norman invasion were larger than any one man. The fragmented polity of twelfth-century Ireland, the expansionary energy of the Norman lords, and the ambitions of the Angevin monarchy made collision all but inevitable. Dermid McMurrogh was the spark, but the kindling had been gathering for a generation.
