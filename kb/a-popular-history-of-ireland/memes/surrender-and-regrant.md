**Surrender and Regrant** was the cornerstone of Tudor policy in 16th-century [[Ireland]], introduced under King [[Henry VIII]].

Under this policy, Irish chieftains and Anglo-Norman lords surrendered their traditional lands and titles to the Crown, receiving them back as grants under English feudal law. This transformed Gaelic elective chieftaincy into a system of primogeniture and individual property rights recognized by Crown law.

## Origins and Rationale

The policy emerged from the recognition that military conquest alone could not subdue Gaelic [[Ireland]]. The Tudor administration, guided by Sir Anthony St. Leger, sought a legal mechanism to convert Irish customs into English forms without continuous warfare.

> "The policy of Surrender and Regrant—persuading Irish chieftains to surrender their lands to the Crown and receive them back as feudal grants—was the preferred instrument of Tudor statecraft." (077-113)

## Implementation

The policy was first applied systematically in the 1540s. Gaelic lords who accepted the terms were required to surrender their lands and titles, receive a new patent under English law, adopt English as the language of official communication, and enforce the abolition of brehon law.

Among the notable early adopters was Conn O'Neill, who surrendered his lands to [[henry-viii|Henry VIII]] and was created [[hugh-oneill|Earl of Tyrone]] in 1542.

## Consequences and Legacy

The policy's long-term effects were profound but uneven. While it extended nominal Crown sovereignty, it also generated new conflicts. The introduction of primogeniture disrupted traditional succession, and the requirement to adopt English customs alienated many followers.

> "The close of the second reign from the siege of Limerick imposes the necessity of reviewing the entire period of Tudor reform." (087-002)

The policy's legacy fed into later Tudor conflicts, including the [[Desmond Rebellions]] and the [[Nine Years' War]], as lords chafed under feudal constraints.

| Aspect | Before Surrender and Regrant | After |
|--------|------------------------------|-------|
| Land tenure | Tribal/clan ownership | Individual feudal grant |
| Succession | Elective (tanistry) | Primogeniture |
| Legal system | Brehon law | English common law |
| Sovereignty | Autonomous lordship | Crown vassalage |
