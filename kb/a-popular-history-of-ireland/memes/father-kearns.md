## Overview

Father Kearns was one of the Catholic priests who took a leading role in the 1798 Rebellion in Ireland. He served as a principal leader of the first division of Wexford rebels under Father Clinch and later commanded a diminished band of insurgents in Kildare before being captured and executed. (103-171) (104-052)

His participation in the rebellion exemplified the complex relationship between the Catholic clergy and the revolutionary movement. While the bishops generally opposed the rising, a number of local priests — particularly in Wexford — threw in their lot with the insurgents and paid for it with their lives. (103-171) (104-060)

## Leadership in Wexford

In the initial phase of the Wexford rising, Father Kearns emerged as one of the principal military leaders alongside Father Clinch. Together with Messrs. Fitzgerald, Doyle, and Redmond, they commanded the first division of the rebel forces that drove southward from Gorey in the early days of the insurrection. (103-170) (103-171)

> The principal leaders of the first division were Fathers Kearns and Clinch, and Messrs. Fitzgerald, Doyle, and Redmond; of the second division, Fathers Murphy and Roche, and Messrs. Byrne and Brenan. [103-170] [103-171]

The presence of priests in command positions reflected the deep involvement of the Catholic clergy at the local level in the 1798 Rebellion, particularly in County Wexford where the rising took on something of the character of a religious war. (103-171)

## The Kildare Campaign

After the defeat of the main Wexford forces at Vinegar Hill, Father Kearns and Anthony Perry of Inch led a much diminished band of insurgents into County Kildare. There they formed a junction with the rebel forces under Aylmer and Reynolds, attempting to keep the rebellion alive in the midlands. (104-052) (104-053)

> Anthony Perry of Inch, and Father Kearns, leading a much diminished band into Kildare, formed a junction with Aylmer and Reynolds of that county, and for several weeks kept up a spirit of resistance to the overwhelming forces of the government. [104-052] [104-053]

For several weeks, the combined force maintained a spirit of resistance against the overwhelming forces of the Crown. They moved through the countryside, harassing government patrols and attempting to rouse fresh support among the population of Kildare. (104-053)

## Capture and Execution

As the weeks wore on through July, the rebel band was progressively weakened. Hunted down by government forces, they were at length compelled to break up into small parties of two and three men, each seeking any means of escape. (104-059) (104-060)

> Until at length, hunted down as they were, they broke up in twos and threes to seek any means of escape. Father Kearns and Mr. Perry were, however, arrested, and executed by martial law at Edenderry. [104-059] [104-060] [104-061]

Father Kearns and Anthony Perry were arrested and executed by martial law at Edenderry. Both met their deaths with the fortitude that characterized many of the rebel leaders who faced the government's retribution in the summer of 1798. Their execution marked the effective end of organized rebel resistance in Kildare. (104-060) (104-061)

## Legacy

Father Kearns represents the complex figure of the priest-rebel in Irish history — a man of religion who took up arms in what he believed to be a just cause. His participation in the 1798 Rebellion illustrates the ways in which the revolutionary ideals of the United Irishmen penetrated even the ranks of the Catholic clergy. (103-171) (104-052)

The execution of Father Kearns and other priest-rebels at Edenderry became part of the martyrology of the 1798 Rebellion, contributing to the growing identification of Catholicism with Irish nationalism in the 19th century. His sacrifice was remembered by later generations as evidence of the clergy's solidarity with the people in their struggle. (104-060)
