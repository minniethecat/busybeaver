## Overview

James Hope (1764–1847) was a United Irishman of Templepatrick, County Antrim, who served as a trusted emissary for [[Robert Emmet]] during the conspiracy of 1802–1803.

A weaver by trade, Hope was one of the few United Irish leaders who survived both the [[Irish Rebellion of 1798]] and Emmet's rising of 1803 to end his days peacefully in Ireland. His long life — he lived into his eighties — made him a living link between the revolutionary generation and the later constitutional nationalist movements. (107-192)

## Biography

### Early Life and the United Irishmen

James Hope was born in Templepatrick, County Antrim, in 1764, into a Presbyterian farming and weaving family. The eighteenth-century Presbyterian community of Ulster, though subject to the Penal Laws alongside Catholics, had a strong tradition of democratic church governance that produced a politically conscious class of small farmers and artisans.

When the [[United Irishmen]] was established at Belfast in October 1791, Hope responded to its call for a "brotherhood of affection" among Irishmen of all denominations. The society's founding document, drafted by [[Theobald Wolfe Tone]], declared that reform must "include Irishmen of all religious denominations." (100-045) Hope's background as a working man connected him to the rank and file of the northern organisation, distinguishing him from the barristers and merchants who led the Dublin society.

### The 1798 Rebellion in Antrim

When the [[Irish Rebellion of 1798]] erupted, Antrim was one of the two northern counties that rose _en masse_. The original chiefs — [[Thomas Russell]] and [[Samuel Neilson]] — were in prison, and "the next leader on whom the men of Antrim relied, resigned his command on the very eve of the appointed day." (104-110) Despite this, the insurgents resolved on action, and "on the 7th of June... the first movement was to seize the town of Antrim." (104-112) Hope fought under [[Henry Joy McCracken]], but the northern rising was crushed within a week.

### Emmet's Emissary

In 1802, when [[Robert Emmet]] returned to Dublin from Paris to rebuild the United Irish organisation, he recruited Hope. McGee lists him among Emmet's chief confidants:

> His chief confidants and assistants were Thomas Russell and Mathew Dowdall... Hamilton, an Enniskillen barrister, married to Russell's niece; James Hope of Templepatrick, and Michael Dwyer, the Wicklow outlaw, who had remained since '98 uncaptured in the mountains. (107-154)

Hope served as Emmet's emissary to the northern counties, carrying messages and assessing readiness for a coordinated rising. His knowledge of the Antrim countryside made him invaluable. Emmet's plan was ambitious — "nineteen out of the thirty-two counties would rise" (107-139) — and he "was enthusiastic, and he inspired his own spirit into many." (107-144)

The rising of 23 July 1803 was a catastrophic failure. Emmet was executed on 20 September. Russell was executed at Downpatrick. (107-184) Other associates "escaped to France, where the former became an officer of rank in the army of Napoleon." (107-187) But Hope's fate was different:

> Mr. Long, his generous banker, and James Hope, his faithful emissary, were both permitted to end their days in Ireland. (107-192)

### Later Life

Hope lived quietly in Antrim and later Belfast for the remainder of his long life, working at his trade and bearing witness to the ideals for which he had risked everything. He died in 1847, having witnessed the rise of [[Daniel O'Connell]]'s Catholic Emancipation movement.

His memoirs, later published, became an invaluable primary source for historians, preserving the perspective of a participant from the movement's popular wing. McGee's epitaph for the United Irishmen applies with particular force to Hope: "the world has never seen a more sincere or more self-sacrificing generation." (104-338)

## Legacy

James Hope represents the genuinely popular character of the United Irish movement in Ulster. A working man who became Robert Emmet's trusted emissary, he bridged the gap between revolutionary generations and social classes. His survival into the era of O'Connell enabled him to transmit the ideals of 1798 to a new generation. In a movement whose leaders mostly died on the scaffold or in exile, Hope's long life stands as a quiet testament to perseverance.
