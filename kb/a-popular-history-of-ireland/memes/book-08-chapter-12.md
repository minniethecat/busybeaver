CHAPTER XII.

[063-001]STATE OF RELIGION AND LEARNING DURING THE REIGN OF ELIZABETH.

[063-002]During the greater part of the reign of Elizabeth, the means relied

[063-003]upon for the propagation of the reformed doctrines were more

[063-004]exclusively those of force and coercion than even in the time of Edward

[063-005]VI. Thus, when Sir William Drury was Deputy, in 1578, he bound several

[063-006]citizens of Kilkenny, under a penalty of 40 pounds each, to attend the

[063-007]English Church service, and authorized the Anglican Bishop "to make a

[063-008]rate for the repair of the Church, and to distrain for the payment of

[063-009]it"—the first mention of Church rates we remember to have met with.

[063-010]Drury's method of proceeding may be further inferred from the fact,

[063-011]that of the thirty-six executions ordered by him in the same city, "one

[063-012]was a blackamoor and two were witches, who were condemned by the law of

[063-013]nature, for there was no positive law against witchcraft [in Ireland]

[063-014]in those days." That defect was soon supplied, however, by the statute

[063-015]27th of Elizabeth, "against witchcraft and sorcery." Sir John Perrott,

[063-016]successor to Drury, trod in the same path, as we judge from the charge

[063-017]of severity against recusants, upon which, among other articles, he was

[063-018]recalled from the government. Towards the end of the sixteenth century,

[063-019]however, it began to be discovered by the wisest observers that violent

[063-020]methods were worse than useless with the Irish. Edmund Spenser urged

[063-021]that "religion should not be forcibly impressed into them with terror

[063-022]and sharp penalties, as now is the manner, but rather delivered and

[063-023]intimated with mildness and gentleness." Lord Bacon, in his

[063-024]"Considerations touching the Queen's Service in Ireland," addressed to

[063-025]Secretary Cecil, recommends "the recovery of the hearts of the people,"

[063-026]as the first step towards their conversion. With this view he suggested

[063-027]"a toleration of religion (for a time not definite), except it be in

[063-028]some principal towns and cities," as a measure "warrantable in

[063-029]religion, and in policy of absolute necessity." The philosophic

[063-030]Chancellor farther suggested, as a means to this desired end, the

[063-031]preparation of "versions of Bibles and Catechisms, and other works of

[063-032]instruction in the Irish language." In accordance with these views of

[063-033]conversion, the University of Trinity College was established by a

[063-034]royal charter, in the month of January, 1593. The Mayor and Corporation

[063-035]of Dublin had granted the ancient monastery of All Hallows as a site

[063-036]for the buildings; some contributions were received from the Protestant

[063-037]gentry, large grants of confiscated Abbey and other lands, which

[063-038]afterwards yielded a princely revenue, were bestowed upon it, and the

[063-039]Lord Treasurer Burleigh graciously accepted the office of its

[063-040]Chancellor. The first Provost was Archbishop Loftus, and of the first

[063-041]three students entered, one was the afterwards illustrious James Usher.

[063-042]The commanders and officers engaged at Kinsale presented it with the

[063-043]sum of 1,800 pounds for the purchase of a library; and at the

[063-044]subsequent confiscations in Munster and Ulster, the College came in for

[063-045]a large portion of the forfeited lands.

[063-047]Although the Council in England generally recommended the adoption of

[063-048]persuasive arts and a limited toleration, those who bore the sword

[063-049]usually took care that they should not bear it in vain. A High

[063-050]Commission Court, armed with ample powers to enforce the Act of

[063-051]Uniformity, had been established at Dublin in 1593; but its members

[063-052]were ordered to proceed cautiously after the Ulster Confederacy became

[063-053]formidable, and their powers lay dormant in the last two or three years

[063-054]of the century. Essex and Mountjoy were both fully convinced of the

[063-055]wisdom of Bacon's views; the former showed a partial toleration,

[063-056]connived at the celebration of the Holy Sacrifice, even in the capital,

[063-057]and liberated some priests from prison. Mountjoy, in answer to the

[063-058]command of the English Council "to deal moderately in the great matter

[063-059]of religion," replied by letter that he had already advised "such as

[063-060]dealt in it for a time to hold a restrained hand therein." "The other

[063-061]course," he adds, "might have overthrown the means of our own end of a

[063-062]reformation of religion." This conditional toleration—such as it

[063-063]was—excited the indignation of the more zealous Reformers, whose

[063-064]favourite preacher, the youthful Usher, did not hesitate to denounce it

[063-065]from the pulpit of Christ Church, as an unhallowed compromise with

[063-066]antichrist. In 1601, Usher, then but 21 years of age, preached his

[063-067]well-known sermon from the text of the forty days, in which Ezekiel

[063-068]"was to bear the iniquity of the house of Judah—a day for a year."

[063-069]"From this year," cried the youthful zealot, "will I reckon the sin of

[063-070]Ireland, that those whom you now embrace shall be your ruin, and you

[063-071]shall bear their iniquity." When the northern insurrection of 1641 took

[063-072]place, this rhetorical menace was exalted, after the fact, into the

[063-073]dignity of a prophecy fulfilled. After the victory of Kinsale, however,

[063-074]the Ultra Protestant party had less cause to complain of the

[063-075]temporizing of the civil power; the pecuniary mulct of twelve pence for

[063-076]each absence from the English service was again enforced at least in

[063-077]Dublin, and several priests, then in prison, were, on various

[063-078]pretences, put to death. Among those who suffered in the capital was

[063-079]the learned Jesuit, Henry Fitzsimons, son of a Mayor of the city, the

[063-080]author of _Brittanomachia_, with whom, while in the Castle, Usher

[063-081]commenced a controversy, which was never finished. But the terms agreed

[063-082]upon at Mellifont, between Mountjoy and Tyrone, again suspended for a

[063-083]short interval the sword of persecution.

[063-084]Notwithstanding its manifold losses by exile and the scaffold, the

[063-085]ancient Church was enabled, through the abundance of vocations, and the

[063-086]zeal of the ordained, to keep up a still powerful organization. Philip

[063-087]O'Sullivan states, under the next reign that the government had

[063-088]ascertained through its spies, the names of 1,160 priests, secular and

[063-089]regular, still in the country. There must have been between 300 and 400

[063-090]others detained abroad, either as Professors in the Irish Colleges in

[063-091]Spain, France, and Flanders, or as ecclesiastics, awaiting major

[063-092]orders. Of the regulars at home, 120 were Franciscans, and about 50

[063-093]Jesuits. There are said to have been but four Fathers of the Order of

[063-094]St. Dominick remaining at the time of Elizabeth's death. The reproach

[063-095]of Cambrensis had long been taken away, since every Diocese might now

[063-096]point to its martyrs. Of these we recall among the Hierarchy the names

[063-097]of O'Hely, Bishop of Killala, executed at Kilmallock in 1578; O'Hurley,

[063-098]Archbishop of Cashel, burned at the stake in Dublin in 1582; Creagh,

[063-099]Archbishop of Armagh, who died a prisoner in the Tower in 1585;

[063-100]Archbishop McGauran, his successor, slain in the act of ministering to

[063-101]the wounded in the engagement at Tulsk, in Roscommon, in 1593; McEgan,

[063-102]Bishop of Ross, who met his death under precisely similar circumstances

[063-103]in Carberry in 1603. Yet through all these losses the episcopal

[063-104]succession was maintained unbroken. In the early part of the next reign

[063-105]O'Sullivan gives the names of the four Archbishops, Peter Lombard of

[063-106]Armagh, Edward McGauran of Dublin, David O'Carny of Cashel, and

[063-107]Florence Conroy of Tuam. On the other hand, the last trying half

[063-108]century had furnished, so far as we can learn, no instance of apostacy

[063-109]among the Bishops, and but half a dozen at most from all orders of the

[063-110]clergy. We read that Owen O'Conor, an apostate, was advanced by letters

[063-111]patent to Killala in 1591; that Maurice O'Brien of Ara was, in 1570, by

[063-112]the same authority, elevated to the See of Killaloe, which he resigned

[063-113]in 1612; that Miler Magrath, in early life a Franciscan friar, was

[063-114]promoted by the Queen to the Sees of Clogher, Killala, Anchory and

[063-115]Lismore successively. He finally settled in the See of Cashel, in which

[063-116]he died, having secretly returned to the religion of his ancestors. For

[063-117]the rest, "the Queen's Bishops" were chiefly chosen out of England,

[063-118]though some few natives of the Pale, or of the walled towns, educated

[063-119]at Oxford, may be found in the list.

[063-120]Of the state of learning in those troubled times the brief story is

[063-121]easily told. The Bardic Order still flourished and was held in honour

[063-122]by all ranks of the native population. The national adversity brought

[063-123]out in them, as in others, many noble traits of character. The Harper,

[063-124]O'Dugan, was the last companion that clung to the last of the Desmonds;

[063-125]the Bard of Tyrconnell, Owen Ward, accompanied the Ulster chiefs in

[063-126]their exile, and poured out his Gaelic dirge above their Roman graves.

[063-127]Although the Bardic compositions continued to be chiefly personal,

[063-128]relating to the inauguration, journeys, exploits, or death of some

[063-129]favourite chief, a large number of devotional poems on the passion of

[063-130]our Lord and the glories of the Blessed Virgin are known to be of this

[063-131]age. The first forerunners of what was destined to be a numerous

[063-132]progeny, the controversial ode or ballad, appeared in Elizabeth's

[063-133]reign, in the form of comparisons between the old and new religions,

[063-134]lamentations over the ruin of religious houses, and the apostacy of

[063-135]such persons as Miler Magrath and the son of the Earl of Desmond. The

[063-136]talents of many of the authors are admitted by Spenser, a competent

[063-137]judge, but the tendency of their writings, he complains, was to foster

[063-138]the love of lawlessness and rebellion rather than of virtue and

[063-139]loyalty. He recommended them for correction to the mercies of the

[063-140]Provost Marshal, whom he would have "to walk the country with half a

[063-141]dozen or half a score of horsemen," in quest of the treasonable poets.

[063-142]As this was the age of the general diffusion of printing, we may

[063-143]observe that the casting of Irish type for the use of Trinity College,

[063-144]by order of Queen Elizabeth, is commonly dated from the year 1591; but

[063-145]as the College was not opened for two years later, the true date must

[063-146]be anticipated. John Kearney, Treasurer of St. Patrick's Church, who

[063-147]died about the year 1600, published a Protestant Catechism from the

[063-148]College Press, which, says O'Reilly, "was the first book ever printed

[063-149]in Irish types." In the year 1593, Florence Conroy translated from the

[063-150]Spanish into Irish a catechism entitled "Christian Instruction," which,

[063-151]he states in the preface, he had no opportunity of sending into Ireland

[063-152]"until the year of the age of our Lord 1598." Whether it was then

[063-153]printed we are not informed, but there does not seem to have been any

[063-154]Irish type in Catholic hands before the foundation of the Irish College

[063-155]at Louvain in 1616.

[063-156]The merit of first giving to the press, in the native language of the

[063-157]country, a version of the Sacred Scriptures, belongs clearly to Trinity

[063-158]College. Nicholas Walsh, Bishop of Ossory, who died in 1585, had

[063-159]commenced, with the assistance of John Kearney, to translate the Greek

[063-160]Testament into Gaelic. He had also the assistance of Dr. Nehemiah

[063-161]Donnellan, and Dr. William Daniel, or O'Daniel, both of whom

[063-162]subsequently filled the See of Tuam. This translation, dedicated to

[063-163]King James, and published by O'Daniel in 1603, is still reprinted by

[063-164]the Bible Societies. The first Protestant translation of the Old

[063-165]Testament, made under Bishop Bedel's eye, and with such revision of

[063-166]particular passages as his imperfect knowledge of the language enabled

[063-167]him to suggest, though completed in the reign of Charles I., was not

[063-168]published before the year 1680. It was Bedel, also, who caused the

[063-169]English liturgy to be recited in Irish, in his Cathedral, as early as

[063-170]1630.

[063-171]Ireland and her affairs naturally attracted, during Elizabeth's reign,

[063-172]the attention of English writers. Of these it is enough to mention the

[063-173]Poet Spenser, Secretary to Lord Grey de Wilton, Fynes Moryson,

[063-174]Secretary to Lord Mountjoy, and the Jesuit Father, Campian. Campian,

[063-175]early distinguished at Oxford, was employed as Cambrensis had been four

[063-176]centuries earlier, and as Plowden was two centuries later, to write

[063-177]down everything Irish. He crossed the Channel in 1570, and composed two

[063-178]books rapidly, without accurate or full information as to the condition

[063-179]or history of the country. The nearer view of Catholic suffering and

[063-180]Catholic constancy exercised a powerful influence on this accomplished

[063-181]scholar; he became a convert and a Jesuit. For members of that order

[063-182]there was but one exit out of life, under the law of England: he

[063-183]suffered death at Tyburn in 1581. Richard Stanihurst, son of the

[063-184]Recorder of Dublin, and uncle of Archbishop Usher, went through

[063-185]precisely the same experiences as his friend Campian, except that he

[063-186]died, a quarter of a century later, Chaplain to the Archdukes at

[063-187]Brussels, instead of expiring at the stake. His English hexameters are

[063-188]among the curiosities of literature, but his contributions to the

[063-189]history of his country, especially his allusions to events and

[063-190]characters in and about his own time, are not without their use.

[063-191]Stanihurst wrote his historical tracts, as did Lombard the Catholic and

[063-192]Usher the Protestant Primate, O'Sullivan, White, O'Meara, and almost

[063-193]all the Irish writers of that age, without exception, in the Latin

[063-194]language. The first Latin book printed in Ireland is thought to be

[063-195]O'Meara's poem in praise of Thomas, Earl of Ormond and Ossory,

[063-196]published in 1615. The earliest English books printed in Ireland are

[063-197]unknown to me; the collection of Anglo-Irish statutes, ordered to be

[063-198]published while Sir Henry Sidney was Deputy, was the most important

[063-199]undertaking of that class in the reign of Elizabeth.

[063-200]As to institutions of learning, if we except Trinity College, which

[063-201]increased rapidly in numbers and reputation under the patronage of the

[063-202]Crown, and the College of Saint Nicholas, at Galway—protected by its

[063-203]remote situation on the brink of the Atlantic—there was no famous seat

[063-204]of learning left in the island. In the next reign 1,300 scholars are

[063-205]stated to have attended that western "school of humanity," when the

[063-206]Ecclesiastical Commissioners despotically ordered it to be closed,

[063-207]because the learned Principal, John Lynch, "would not confirm to the

[063-208]religion established." But the greater number of the children of

[063-209]Catholics, who still retained property enough to educate them, were

[063-210]sent beyond seas, a fact with which King James, soon after his

[063-211]accession, reproached the deputation of that body. A proclamation

[063-212]issued by Lord Deputy Chichester, in 1610, alludes to the same custom,

[063-213]and commands all noblemen, merchants, and others, whose children are

[063-214]abroad for educational purposes, to recall them within one year from

[063-215]the date thereof; and in case they refuse to return, all parents,

[063-216]friends, &c., sending them money, directly or indirectly, will be

[063-217]punished as severely as the law permits. It was mainly to guard against

[063-218]this danger that "the School of Wards" was established by Elizabeth,

[063-219]and enlarged by James I., in which the great Duke of Ormond, Sir Phelim

[063-220]O'Neil, Murrogh, Lord Inchiquin, and other sons of noble families, were

[063-221]educated for the next generation. Early in the reign of James there

[063-222]were not less than 300 of these Irish children in the Tower, or at the

[063-223]Lambeth School,—and it is humiliating to find the great name of Sir

[063-224]Edward Coke among those who gloried in the success of this unnatural

[063-225]substitution of the State for the Parent in the work of education.
