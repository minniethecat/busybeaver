The **Restoration** of 1660 brought [[Charles II]] to the throne of [[Ireland]], ending the [[Cromwellian Settlement|Cromwellian interregnum]].

It raised the hopes of displaced Catholics for the recovery of their confiscated lands. The Restoration Parliament enacted the Act of Settlement (1662) and the Act of Explanation (1665), attempting to reconcile competing claims of royalists, soldiers, and Adventurers.

The resulting settlement fell far short of Catholic expectations, restoring only a fraction of pre-Cromwellian Catholic landownership. It embedded the [[Protestant Ascendancy]] as the dominant political force in Irish life.

## The King Returns

News of [[charles-ii|Charles II]]'s restoration to the English throne in May 1660 was received with particular hope in Ireland, where the Cromwellian confiscations had devastated Catholic landholding.

> "Coote, after the Restoration, was created Earl of Mountrath, and Broghill, Earl of Orrery." (074-126)

> "Swelled suddenly on the Restoration to a majority of twenty to one in the House of Commons." (074-137)

## The Act of Settlement (1662)

The [[irish-parliament|Irish Parliament]] faced the impossible task of reconciling three irreconcilable claims: Catholics who had supported the royalist cause and expected full restitution; Protestant adventurers and soldiers who had acquired lands under the [[Cromwellian Settlement]]; and the Crown, which needed a stable Protestant interest.

> "They framed their Act of Settlement, the authorship of which was attributed to the King himself." (075-020)

## The Religious Settlement

The Restoration re-established the Church of Ireland as the state church, ending Cromwellian toleration of dissenting sects.

> "Anabaptists; others, after the Restoration, conformed to the Established Church." (075-143)

This laid the foundation for subsequent [[Penal Laws]], as the Protestant-dominated [[Irish Parliament]] tightened restrictions on Catholic worship and property.

## The Ormond Administration

The Duke of Ormond, appointed Lord Lieutenant in 1662, governed through a coalition of moderate interests, navigating between Crown demands and Irish factional politics.

> "The historian and statesman of the Restoration, Clarendon, observed the unusual calm." (077-113)
