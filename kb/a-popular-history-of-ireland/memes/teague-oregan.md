Sir Teague O'Regan was an Irish Jacobite commander who won renown during the [[Williamite War in Ireland]] (1689–1691) for his stubborn defence of Charlemont, one of the last northern garrisons to hold out for [[James II]]. His prolonged resistance delayed the Williamite advance and earned him the respect even of his enemies.

## The Defence of Charlemont

In the campaign of 1689, following the relief of [[Derry]] and the landing of the Williamite army under the Marshal Duke de Schomberg, the Jacobite position in [[Ulster]] collapsed rapidly. Six months after Schomberg's arrival, with the exception of the forts of Charlemont and Carrickfergus, King James no longer possessed a garrison in the province that his grandfather had bestowed upon the ancestors of those who now unanimously rejected him (081-143).

O'Regan's stand at Charlemont proved a significant obstacle. The aged Duke de Schomberg, advancing southward toward [[Dublin]], found O'Regan at Charlemont — along with the Duke of [[Berwick]] at Newry — to be obstacles so serious that nearly a month was wasted in advancing from Belfast to [[Dundalk]] (081-161). This delay contributed to Schomberg's decision to entrench at Dundalk for the winter, where a terrible dysentery broke out among his troops and ravaged his army (081-165).

> "O'Regan, at Charlemont, and Berwick, at Newry, seemed to him obstacles so serious, that nearly a month was wasted in advancing from Belfast to Dundalk." (081-161)

## The Siege and Surrender

The siege of Charlemont, which commenced apparently in the autumn of 1689, continued during several months into the spring of 1690. The garrison held out until they were literally starved out in May (082-034). The famished survivors were treated kindly by order of Schomberg — a mark of the respect O'Regan's defence had earned.

> "Their gallant and eccentric chief, O'Regan, was knighted by the King, for his persistent resistance." (082-037)

The knighthood from [[William III]] was a remarkable gesture — an enemy commander honoured by the king he had defied. A month from the day Charlemont fell, William himself landed at Carrickfergus to take personal command of the campaign (082-038).

## From Charlemont to Sligo

O'Regan's service did not end with Charlemont. By the following year, he had taken command of the Jacobite garrison at [[Sligo]]. During the weary winter of 1691, while the Williamite troops were sorely afflicted by disease, some relief was sent by sea to the Jacobite garrison of Sligo, then "commanded by the stout old Sir Teague O'Regan, the former defender of Charlemont" (083-094).

After the twin blows of Athlone and Aughrim shattered Catholic power in [[Connaught]] in the summer of 1691, Sligo — the last western garrison — finally succumbed. O'Regan, still undefeated in the field, marched his 600 surviving men southward to [[Limerick]] for the final stand of the war (084-105).
