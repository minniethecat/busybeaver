## Overview

Thomas de Clare (c. 1245–1287) was an Anglo-Norman nobleman who sought to establish a territorial lordship in Thomond, in the west of Ireland, during the late thirteenth century. The son of the Earl of Gloucester and son-in-law of [[Maurice Fitzgerald]], third Baron of Offaly, de Clare received a royal grant from [[King Edward I of England]] entitling him to the whole land of Thomond. His attempts to make good this claim through military force provoked sustained resistance from the O'Brien dynasty and ended with his death on the battlefield in 1287.

## The Grant of Thomond

In the first years of his reign, King Edward I of England sought to reward supporters and extend the Anglo-Norman interest into regions of Ireland that remained under Gaelic control. Shortly after his accession, Edward granted the entire territory of Thomond, the ancient patrimony of the O'Briens and descendants of [[Brian Boru]], to Thomas de Clare.

> "Edward I., soon after his accession, granted the whole land of Thomond to Thomas de Clare, son of the Earl of Gloucester, and son-in-law of Maurice, third Baron of Offally." [031-016]

The de Clare family had long pretended a grant from [[Henry II]] of the whole of Thomond as their title to invade that principality, but in reality their claim rested on Edward's grant, bestowed in 1275. (031-179) (031-180) This royal favour ignited a contest in Munster similar to that which raged simultaneously in Connaught between the O'Conors and the de Burghs, in which old inhabitants and new claimants fought for supremacy. (031-017) (031-018)

## Family Connections and Ambitions

Thomas de Clare was the son of Richard de Clare, Earl of Gloucester, one of the most powerful magnates of the Welsh Marches. Through his marriage to a daughter of Maurice Fitzgerald, third Baron of Offaly, de Clare secured a dynastic alliance with the rising [[Geraldines]] family, whose influence in Leinster and Munster was expanding rapidly during the thirteenth century.

His father-in-law, Maurice Fitzgerald, had served three times as Lord Justice of Ireland and was deeply invested in the military subjugation of Gaelic territories. (031-147) The attempt to establish de Clare in Thomond was the final enterprise of Maurice Fitzgerald's life. (031-177) The combined forces of the Gloucester de Clares and the Offaly Geraldines represented one of the most formidable Anglo-Norman alliances of the period.

## The 1277 Campaign and the Murder of Brian Roe

In 1277, supported by his father-in-law the Kildare Fitzgerald, de Clare marched into Munster to assert his claim to Thomond by force of arms. (031-209) The Lord of Thomond at this time was Brian Roe O'Brien, who had succeeded his father Conor na Siudaine after the latter's victory over the Geraldines at Kilbarran in 1260. (031-204) (031-205)

De Clare sought an interview with Brian Roe under the relation of _gossip_, a bond of spiritual kinship through sponsorship at baptism that was considered sacred among the Irish. (031-210) (031-211) Trusting in the sanctity of this bond, Brian Roe placed himself in de Clare's power, only to be betrayed:

> "Brien Roe, having placed himself credulously in the hands of his invaders, was cruelly drawn to pieces between two horses." [031-213]

This act of treachery, the murder of a Gaelic lord under the cover of a sacred bond, provoked a furious rising throughout Thomond. The O'Brien clansmen, led by Donogh, son of the murdered Brian Roe, rose in arms to exact vengeance. (031-214)

## Defeat at Ennis

The combined forces of de Clare and Fitzgerald met the O'Brien army near Ennis, in the heart of Thomond. The battle resulted in a decisive defeat for the Anglo-Normans. (031-215) De Clare and Fitzgerald fled the field and sought refuge in the Church of Quin, hoping the sanctuary of holy ground would protect them.

Donogh O'Brien, however, was determined to avenge his father's murder and refused to respect the church's sanctuary. He ordered the church to be set alight, burning it over the heads of the fugitives and forcing them to surrender. (031-217) De Clare and Fitzgerald were taken prisoner and held for ransom, permitted to depart only on conditions that were, in the words of the chronicler, "sufficiently hard." (031-219)

The disaster at Ennis demonstrated that the Gaelic lords of Thomond, when united under a common cause, remained capable of defeating the best-equipped Anglo-Norman forces. It was a severe check on de Clare's ambitions, but not the end of his efforts.

## The 1287 Invasion and Death

A decade after his humiliating defeat at Ennis, Thomas de Clare mounted another invasion of Thomond. The intervening years had not diminished his determination to make good the royal grant. By 1287, the O'Brien lordship had passed to Turlogh O'Brien, who proved an even more determined defender of Thomond than his predecessors.

Turlogh met the second invasion with overwhelming force. The battle proved catastrophic for de Clare and his followers:

> "In 1287, Turlogh, then the O'Brien, defeated an invasion similar to the last, in which Thomas de Clare was slain, together with Patrick Fitzmaurice of Kerry, Richard Taafe, Richard Deriter, Nicholas Teeling, and other knights, and Gerald, the fourth Baron of Offally, brother-in-law to de Clare, was mortally wounded." [031-222]

The death of de Clare on the field, along with a host of Anglo-Norman knights and his own brother-in-law mortally wounded, marked the collapse of the Clare claim to Thomond. (031-225) The invasion had ended in a slaughter of the Anglo-Norman leadership.

## Death and Legacy

Thomas de Clare's death in 1287 did not immediately extinguish his family's designs on Thomond. After an interval, his son Gilbert de Clare renewed the contest, and the claim was subsequently bequeathed to his younger son Richard de Clare. (031-226) (031-227) Richard made a final effort in 1318, but he too fell in battle against the combined forces of McCarthy and O'Brien, along with several knights and a body of men-at-arms. (031-229) (031-230)

From that point forward, "that proud offshoot of the house of Gloucester, which, at its first settling in Munster, flourished as bravely as the Geraldines themselves, became extinct in the land." (031-234) The de Clare venture in Thomond spanned three generations and ended in complete failure, a testament to the resilience of the O'Brien dynasty and the limits of Anglo-Norman military expansion in thirteenth-century Ireland.

The conflict de Clare initiated contributed to the broader pattern of contestation in Munster that pitted the old Gaelic inhabitants against new claimants under royal grants, a struggle that defined the military and political history of the province throughout the thirteenth century. (031-019) (031-020)
