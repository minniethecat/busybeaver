## Overview

Barry Yelverton (1736–1805), later 1st Viscount Avonmore, was an Irish politician and judge who rose to prominence during the era of Grattan's Parliament. As a leading member of the Patriot Party, he was instrumental in the legislative revolution of 1782.

He moved the repeal of Poynings' Law and advocated for the constitutional independence of the Irish Parliament. He later served as Attorney-General for Ireland and as Lord Chief Baron of the Exchequer.

## Political Rise in Grattan's Parliament

Yelverton entered the Irish House of Commons during the ferment of the Volunteer movement, which had created unprecedented pressure for constitutional reform. Alongside other Patriot leaders, he took a prominent part in the debates that culminated in the legislative independence of 1782. (094-063)

> He was supported by Hussey Burgh, Yelverton, and Forbes; Flood favoured postponement, and laid the foundation of his later discontents. (095-069 to 095-070)

In the debates on the right of the British Parliament to legislate for Ireland, Yelverton supported Henry Grattan's declaration that "the King, Lords and Commons of Ireland are the only power competent to make laws to bind Ireland." Yelverton and other leading members asserted Irish constitutional rights with vigour and eloquence. (095-069)

## The Repeal of Poynings' Law

Ten days later, a motion of Mr. Yelverton's to repeal Poyning's law, as far as related to the Irish privy council's supervision of heads of bills, carried the House and marked one of the decisive achievements of Grattan's Parliament.

(095-088 to 095-089) Poynings' Law, enacted in 1494, had subordinated the Irish Parliament to the English Privy Council, requiring all Irish legislation to receive prior approval from London. Its repeal was therefore a cornerstone of the new constitutional settlement.

> The bills repealing the Mutiny Act, the 6th George I., and Poyning's law, were entrusted to Burgh, Yelverton, and Forbes; the thanks of the House were voted to Grattan alone after the victory, with the substantial addition of 50,000 pounds to purchase for him an estate, which should become an enduring monument of the national gratitude. (096-077 to 096-081)

## Later Career as a Judge

During the session, Mr. Yelverton, having abandoned his promised motion for further reforms, accepted appointment as Attorney-General for Ireland and subsequently as Lord Chief Baron of the Exchequer.

(095-142) His elevation to the bench, while a recognition of his legal abilities, was viewed by some former Patriot colleagues as a retreat from the more radical demands of the popular party. In this capacity, Yelverton—alongside Fitzgibbon and Scott—denounced the United Irish movement as an audacious attempt to overturn the established constitution. (096-240)

## Legacy

Yelverton occupies an intriguing position in Irish constitutional history: a Patriot reformer who helped dismantle the legislative subordination of Ireland, yet who later aligned with the conservative judiciary in resisting the more democratic currents of the 1790s.

His role in the repeal of Poynings' Law remains his most significant legacy, representing a definitive moment in the long campaign for Irish parliamentary sovereignty. Created 1st Viscount Avonmore, he died in 1805, five years after the Act of Union extinguished the Parliament he had helped to liberate.
