## Overview

James I (1566–1625), also known as James VI of Scotland, ascended to the English and Irish thrones in 1603 as the first Stuart monarch. His reign in Ireland was marked by the Flight of the Earls, the wholesale confiscation and plantation of [[ulster|Ulster]], and the systematic enforcement of penal laws against Catholics, establishing patterns of religious and political conflict that would shape Irish history for centuries. (064-003) (064-004)

James was the son of Mary, Queen of Scots, and inherited a complex relationship with Irish Catholics, who initially welcomed his accession. However, his reign quickly turned towards repression, culminating in the plantation scheme that transformed the demographic and political landscape of [[ulster|Ulster]]. (064-006) (064-184)

## Biography

### Accession and Early Hopes

James the Sixth of Scotland was in his thirty-seventh year when he ascended the English throne under the title of "James the First, King of Great Britain and Ireland." His accession naturally excited the most hopeful expectations among Irish Catholics. He was the son of Mary, Queen of Scots, whom they looked upon as a martyr to her religion, and grandson of that gallant King James who styled himself "Defender of the Faith" and "Dominus Hiberniae." (064-003) (064-006) (064-008)

The first acts of the new sovereign were calculated to confirm these expectations. James was anxious to make an immediate and lasting peace with Spain, and his ambassador at Paris was known to be on terms of intimacy with the Pope's Nuncio. On crossing the border from Scotland, he had invited leading Catholics to his Court and conferred the honour of Knighthood on some of their number. (064-019) (064-023) (064-025)

### Shift to Persecution

The King's initial tolerance proved short-lived. Having attempted to enforce the new Anglican Canons against the Puritans, that party retaliated by raising the cry of favouring the Papists. Alarmed by the fear of [[presbyterian|Presbyterianism]], James made a speech in the Star Chamber declaring his utter detestation of Popery and published a proclamation banishing all Catholic missionaries from the country. All magistrates were instructed to enforce the penal laws with rigour, and an elaborate spy system was set on foot. (064-048) (064-051) (064-053) (064-055)

This reign of treachery and terror rendered it difficult, if not impossible, for the King to return to the policy of toleration with which he seems to have set out from Scotland. (064-058) (064-059)

### The Flight of the Earls

In September 1607, [[hugh-oneill|Hugh O'Neil]]l, [[hugh-oneill|Earl of Tyrone]], and Rory O'Donnell, Earl of Tyrconnell, together with Maguire of Fermanagh and other northern chiefs, sailed from Lough Swilly to the Continent in what became known as the Flight of the Earls. This fateful departure was partly the result of intrigues by Secretary Cecil to entrap the northern Earls, and the chiefs feared for their safety under the new regime. (064-319)

To justify his measures and divert European sympathy from the exiles, James issued a proclamation on 5 November 1608, giving the English version of the flight. The chiefs' departure had fateful consequences: Rory O'Donnell expired at Rome in July 1608, Maguire died at Genoa on his way to Spain, and [[hugh-oneill|Hugh O'Neil]]l survived his comrades, living on as a blind pensioner in Rome for eight weary years. (064-220) (064-201) (064-202) (064-204)

### The Plantation of Ulster

The flight of the Earls removed the last barriers to the wholesale confiscation of [[ulster|Ulster]], long sought by "the Undertakers." The entire province was surveyed under Sir William Parsons as Surveyor-General, and a royal commission was issued to inquire into the portions forfeited. The [[plantation-of-ulster|Plantation of Ulster]], the largest colonial settlement scheme in Irish history, involved the confiscation of six counties and their redistribution to English and Scottish Protestant settlers. (064-184) (064-223) (064-225) (069-135)

### The Irish Parliament

In 1613, after an interval during which no Parliament had been held, writs were issued for elections. The recusant (Catholic) lawyers and other Catholic candidates were returned in significant numbers, leading to a packed Parliament through the creation of new boroughs favourable to the Crown. (064-279) (064-299) (064-300)

Though we cannot agree with Sir John Davis that James I gave Ireland her "first free Parliament," it is impossible not to entertain a high sense of admiration for the constitutional firmness of the recusant party in that assembly. The Catholic minority, who resisted both the political machinations and the religious proscription directed against them, deserve to be held in honour by all who value religious and civil liberty. (064-309) (064-311) (064-356) (064-357)

## Legacy

James I's reign fundamentally reshaped Ireland. The [[plantation-of-ulster|Plantation of Ulster]] created a Protestant settler class whose presence in the north of Ireland would define political divisions for the following four centuries. The penal laws enforced under his authority established a regime of religious discrimination that persisted until [[catholic-emancipation|Catholic Emancipation]] in 1829. The precedent of confiscation and plantation, perfected under James, was repeated under [[Oliver Cromwell]] and [[William III]], each wave deepening the dispossession of the native Irish. (069-135)

The "first free Parliament" of James I, though far from free by modern standards, marked the beginning of constitutional opposition by Irish Catholics, a tradition that would culminate in the career of [[Henry Grattan]] and the struggle for legislative independence in 1782.
