The **Siege of Limerick** (1690–1691) was the decisive engagement of the [[Williamite War]] in Ireland, fought between the forces of [[William III]] and the Jacobite army loyal to [[James II]].

The city of [[Limerick]], a strategic stronghold on the River Shannon, withstood a first siege in August 1690 under [[patrick-sarsfield|Patrick Sarsfield]]'s leadership. It fell after a second siege in 1691.

The resulting Treaty of Limerick, signed on 3 October 1691, promised religious toleration and security of property to Irish Catholics. These terms were soon violated by the [[Irish Parliament]], leaving a deep wound in Irish historical memory.

## The First Siege (August 1690)

Following [[william-iii|William III]]'s victory at the [[Battle of the Boyne]], the Jacobite army retreated westward to Limerick. [[patrick-sarsfield|Patrick Sarsfield]] executed a daring cavalry raid that destroyed the Williamite siege train at Ballyneety, compelling William to lift the siege.

> "The story of the siege of Derry—of the heroic constancy of its defenders—was matched by the endurance of Limerick." (081-098)

## The Second Siege and Surrender (1691)

A second Williamite siege, led by General Godert de Ginkel, began in August 1691. After fierce bombardment, the Jacobite commander signed terms of surrender.

> "The close of the second reign from the siege of Limerick imposes the necessity of reviewing the entire period." (087-002)

## The Treaty and Its Betrayal

The Treaty of Limerick's military articles allowed Jacobite soldiers to depart for France (the "Flight of the Wild Geese"). The civil articles guaranteed religious freedom and property rights to Catholics who remained.

> "The Articles made at the Surrender of Limerick." (085-160)

The civil articles were repudiated by the [[Irish Parliament]], which enacted the [[Penal Laws]] systematically excluding Catholics from political life.

> "The ratification of the Articles made at the Surrender of Limerick." (085-177)

The phrase "the broken treaty" entered Irish political vocabulary as shorthand for the unreliability of English promises.
