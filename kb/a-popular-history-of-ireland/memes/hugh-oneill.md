## Overview

Hugh O'Neill (c. 1550–1616), Earl of Tyrone, was the last and greatest of the Gaelic lords of [[ulster|Ulster]]—"Hugh O'Neil, Earl of Tyrone, another Queen" Elizabeth's foe, as the chroniclers recorded. (058-182) Raised in the English Pale and educated in the ways of the Tudor court, O'Neill used his dual identity as an English-educated earl and a Gaelic chieftain to build the most formidable challenge to English rule in Ireland since the Norman invasion. His [[nine-years-war|Nine Years' War]] (1594–1603) brought Ireland closer to throwing off English domination than at any point in the preceding four centuries.

O'Neill's defeat and subsequent flight from Ireland in 1607—the Flight of the Earls—marked the end of the old Gaelic order. He became "the first Earl of Tyrone" of the revived title, an honour that carried both English recognition and Gaelic legitimacy. (048-059) His departure opened [[ulster|Ulster]] to the Plantation, the single most consequential event in the making of modern Ireland. (053-087) (058-063)

## Biography

### Rise to Power

Hugh O'Neill was born into the ruling dynasty of Tir Eoghain (Tyrone) in [[ulster|Ulster]]. After his father was killed by Shane O'Neill, the boy Hugh was taken into the protection of the English government and raised in the household of [[henry-sidney|Sir Henry Sidney]] in the Pale. This education gave him a unique understanding of English politics and military methods that he would later turn against the Crown.

In 1585, Hugh was created Earl of Tyrone by [[elizabeth-i|Queen Elizabeth]]. He was "made Earl of Tyrone, having asked, and been refused" no honour that the Crown could bestow. (051-023) "The Earl of Tyrone was then formally recognized" as the chief lord of his territory. (058-063) For the next decade, O'Neill walked a careful line between loyalty to the Crown and the assertion of his traditional authority as The O'Neill. As one of his former supporters would later ruefully admit, Elizabeth's ministers would "blush to address, as Earl of Tyrone, the man he had lately proclaimed" a traitor. (059-100)

### The Nine Years' War

By 1594, O'Neill had concluded that accommodation with the English was impossible. Assembling a confederacy of [[ulster|Ulster]] lords—O'Donnell of Tirconnell, Maguire of Fermanagh, and others—he launched what became the [[nine-years-war|Nine Years' War]]. O'Neill's forces, trained and equipped with Spanish arms, achieved a series of stunning victories over English armies that culminated at the Battle of the Yellow Ford in 1598.

"The title of Earl of Tyrone was revived," O'Neill's enemies noted bitterly, "it was in" direct challenge to the Queen's sovereignty. (053-087) O'Neill's strategy of guerilla warfare combined with set-piece battles proved devastatingly effective against an enemy accustomed to fighting in the open fields of Europe. At the height of his power, O'Neill's authority extended across all of [[ulster|Ulster]] and into Connacht and [[leinster|Leinster]].

### Defeat and the Flight of the Earls

The turning point came with the arrival of [[charles-blount-lord-mountjoy|Lord Mountjoy]] as English commander and the Spanish landing at Kinsale in 1601. O'Neill marched his army the length of Ireland to join the Spanish force, but the combined army was decisively defeated at the Battle of Kinsale. The war dragged on until 1603, when O'Neill submitted to Mountjoy just days after Elizabeth's death.

Though O'Neill was allowed to retain his title under [[james-i|King James I]], the old Gaelic order had been broken beyond repair. In 1607, facing relentless encroachment by English officials, O'Neill and Rory O'Donnell, Earl of Tirconnell, secretly sailed from Lough Swilly with their families and followers—the Flight of the Earls. "Very well believe, that the Earl of Tyrone complained he had so many" grievances that exile was his only recourse. (064-116) O'Neill died in Rome in 1616, a blind and broken exile, never seeing Ireland again.

## Legacy

### The End of Gaelic Ireland

Hugh O'Neill's defeat and flight marked the definitive end of Gaelic Ireland as a political reality. The attainder of his lands cleared the way for the [[plantation-of-ulster|Plantation of Ulster]], which brought tens of thousands of Scottish and English Protestant settlers into the six counties that would later become Northern Ireland. No single event in Irish history has had more lasting consequences than the Flight of the Earls.

### Historical Judgment

O'Neill's legacy is profoundly ambivalent. To Gaelic Ireland, he was the last champion of a civilisation that stretched back unbroken to pre-Christian times. His "urgent request from John, Earl of Tyrone, to all his clansmen" in his final years spoke of a leader who never abandoned his people even in exile. (066-255) To the English, he was a treacherous vassal who had been given every honour and had repaid trust with rebellion. Modern historians see him as a transitional figure: a Gaelic lord who understood the new world of Renaissance statecraft and nearly succeeded in creating an independent Irish state.

His name resonates through Irish history as the man who came closest to defeating English power in Ireland before the twentieth century. Had the Spanish landed in strength at a different port, had Mountjoy been less capable a commander, the history of Ireland—and of Britain—might have been very different.
