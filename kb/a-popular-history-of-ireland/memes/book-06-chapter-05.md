CHAPTER V.

[043-001]PARTIES WITHIN "THE PALE"—BATTLES OF KILMAINHAM AND KILLUCAN—SIR JOHN

[043-002]TALBOT'S LORD LIEUTENANCY.

[043-003]One leading fact, which we have to follow in all its consequences

[043-004]through the whole of the fifteenth century, is the division of the

[043-005]English and of the Anglo-Irish interest into two parties, Lancasterians

[043-006]and Yorkists. This division of the foreign power will be found to have

[043-007]produced a corresponding sense of security in the minds of the native

[043-008]population, and thus deprived them of that next best thing to a united

[043-009]national action, the combining effects of a common external danger.

[043-010]The new party lines were not drawn immediately upon the English

[043-011]revolution of 1399, but a very few years sufficed to infuse among

[043-012]settlers of English birth or descent the partizan passions which

[043-013]distracted the minds of men in their original country. The third Earl

[043-014]of Ormond, although he had received so many favours from the late King

[043-015]and his grandfather, yet by a common descent of five generations from

[043-016]Edward I., stood in relation of cousinship to the Usurper. On the

[043-017]arrival of the young Duke of Lancaster as Lord Lieutenant, in 1402,

[043-018]Ormond became one of his first courtiers, and dying soon after, he

[043-019]chose the Duke guardian to his heir, afterwards the fourth Earl. This

[043-020]heir, while yet a minor (1407), was elected or appointed deputy to his

[043-021]guardian, the Lord Lieutenant; during almost the whole of the short

[043-022]reign of Henry V. (1413-1421) he resided at the English Court, or

[043-023]accompanied the King in his French campaigns, thus laying the

[043-024]foundations of that influence which, six several times during the reign

[043-025]of Henry VI., procured his appointment to office as Lord Deputy, Lord

[043-026]Justice, or Lord Lieutenant. At length, in the mid-year of the century,

[043-027]his successor was created Earl of Wiltshire, and entrusted with the

[043-028]important duties of one of the Commissioners for the fleet, and Lord

[043-029]Treasurer of England; favours and employments which sufficiently

[043-030]account for how the Ormond family became the leaders of the Lancaster

[043-031]party among the Anglo-Irish.

[043-032]The bestowal of the first place on another house tended to estrange the

[043-033]Geraldines, who, with some reason, regarded themselves as better

[043-034]entitled to such honours. During the first official term of the Duke of

[043-035]Lancaster, no great feeling was exhibited, and on his departure in

[043-036]1405, the fifth Earl of Kildare was, for a year, entrusted with the

[043-037]office of Deputy. On the return of the Duke, in August, 1408, the Earl

[043-038]rode out to meet him, but was suddenly arrested with three other

[043-039]members of his family, and imprisoned in the Castle, His house in

[043-040]Dublin was plundered by the servants of the Lord Lieutenant, and the

[043-041]sum of 300 marks was exacted for his ransom. Such injustice and

[043-042]indignity, as well as the subsequent arrest of the sixth Earl, in 1418,

[043-043]"for having communicated with the Prior of Kilmainham"—still more than

[043-044]their rivalry with the Ormonds, drove the Kildare family into the ranks

[043-045]of the adherents of the Dukes of York. We shall see in the sequel the

[043-046]important reacting influence of these Anglo-Irish combinations upon the

[043-047]fortunes of the white rose and the red.

[043-048]To signalize his accession and remove the reproach of inaction which

[043-049]had been so often urged against his predecessor, Henry IV, was no

[043-050]sooner seated on the throne than he summoned the military tenants of

[043-051]the Crown to meet him in arms upon the Tyne, for the invasion of

[043-052]Scotland. It seems probable that he summoned those of Ireland with the

[043-053]rest, as we find in that year (1400) that an Anglo-Irish fleet,

[043-054]proceeding northwards from Dublin, encountered a Scottish, fleet in

[043-055]Strangford Lough, where a fierce engagement was fought, both sides

[043-056]claiming the victory. Three years later the Dubliners landed at Saint

[043-057]Ninians, and behaved valiantly, as their train bands did the same

[043-058]summer against the mountain tribes of Wicklow. Notwithstanding the

[043-059]personal sojourn of the unfortunate Richard, and his lavish expenditure

[043-060]among them, these warlike burghers cordially supported the new dynasty.

[043-061]Some privileges of trade were judiciously extended to them, and, in

[043-062]1407, Henry granted to the Mayors of the city the privilege of having a

[043-063]gilded sword carried before them, in the same manner as the Mayors of

[043-064]London.

[043-065]At the period when these politic favours were bestowed on the citizens

[043-066]of Dublin, Henry was contending with a formidable insurrection in

[043-067]Wales, under the leadership of Owen Glendower, who had learned in the

[043-068]fastnesses of Idrone, serving under King Richard, how brave men, though

[043-069]not formed to war in the best schools, can defend their country against

[043-070]invasion. In the struggle which he maintained so gallantly during this

[043-071]and the next reign, though the fleet of Dublin at first assisted his

[043-072]enemies, he was materially aided afterwards by the constant occupation

[043-073]furnished them by the clans of Leinster. The early years of the

[043-074]Lancasterian dynasty were marked by a series of almost invariable

[043-075]defeats in the Leinster counties. Art McMurrogh, whose activity defied

[043-076]the chilling effects of age, poured his cohorts through Sculloge gap,

[043-077]on the garrisons of Wexford, taking in rapid possession in one campaign

[043-078](1406) the castles of Camolin, Ferns, and Enniscorthy. Returning

[043-079]northward he retook Castledermot, and inflicted chastisement on the

[043-080]warlike Abbot of Conal, near Naas, who shortly before attacked some

[043-081]Irish forces on the Curragh of Kildare, slaying two hundred men.

[043-082]Castledermot was retaken by the Lord Deputy Scrope the next year, with

[043-083]the aid of the Earls of Ormond and Desmond, and the Prior of

[043-084]Kilmainham, at the head of his Knights. These allies were fresh from a

[043-085]Parliament in Dublin, where the Statute of Kilkenny had been, according

[043-086]to custom, solemnly re-enacted as the only hope of the English

[043-087]interest, and they naturally drew the sword in maintenance of their

[043-088]palladium. Within six miles of Callan, in "McMurrogh's country," they

[043-089]encountered that chieftain and his clansmen. In the early part of the

[043-090]day the Irish are stated to have had the advantage, but some Methian

[043-091]captains coming up in the afternoon turned the tide in favour of the

[043-092]English. According to the chronicles of the Pale, they won a second

[043-093]victory before nightfall at the town of Callan, over O'Carroll of Ely,

[043-094]who was marching to the aid of McMurrogh. But so confused and

[043-095]unsatisfactory are the accounts of this twofold engagement on the same

[043-096]day, in which the Deputy in person, and such important persons as the

[043-097]Earls of Desmond, of Ormond, and the Prior of Kilmainham commanded,

[043-098]that we cannot reconcile it with probability. The Irish Annals simply

[043-099]record the fact that a battle was gained at Callan over the Irish of

[043-100]Munster, in which O'Carroll was slain. Other native authorities add

[043-101]that 800 of his followers fell with O'Carroll, but no mention whatever

[043-102]is made of the battle with McMurrogh. The English accounts gravely add,

[043-103]that the evening sun stood still, while the Lord Deputy rode six miles,

[043-104]from the place of the first engagement to that of the second. This was

[043-105]the last campaign of Sir Stephen Scrope; he died soon after by the

[043-106]pestilence which swept over the island, sparing neither rich nor poor.

[043-107]The Duke of Lancaster resumed the Lieutenancy, arrested the Earl of

[043-108]Kildare as before related, convoked a Parliament at Dublin, and with

[043-109]all the forces he could muster, determined on an expedition southwards.

[043-110]But McMurrogh and the mountaineers of Wicklow now felt themselves

[043-111]strong enough to take the initiative. They crossed the plain which lies

[043-112]to the north of Dublin, and encamped at Kilmainham, where Roderick when

[043-113]he besieged the city, and Brien before the battle of Clontarf, had

[043-114]pitched their tents of old. The English and Anglo-Irish forces, under

[043-115]the eye of their Prince, marched out to dislodge them, in four

[043-116]divisions. The first was led by the Duke in person; the second by the

[043-117]veteran knight, Jenico d'Artois, the third by Sir Edward Perrers, an

[043-118]English knight, and the fourth by Sir Thomas Butler, Prior of the Order

[043-119]of Saint John, afterwards created by Henry V., for his distinguished

[043-120]service, Earl of Kilmain. With McMurrogh were O'Byrne, O'Nolan, and

[043-121]other chiefs, besides his sons, nephews, and relatives. The numbers on

[043-122]each side could hardly fall short of ten thousand men, and the action

[043-123]may be fairly considered one of the most decisive of those times. The

[043-124]Duke was carried back wounded into Dublin; the slopes of Inchicore and

[043-125]the valley of the Liffey were strewn with the dying and the dead; the

[043-126]river at that point obtained from the Leinster Irish the name of

[043-127]_Athcroe_, or the ford of slaughter; the widowed city was filled with

[043-128]lamentation and dismay. In a petition addressed to King Henry by the

[043-129]Council, apparently during his son's confinement from the effects of

[043-130]his wound, they thus describe the Lord Lieutenant's condition: "His

[043-131]soldiers have deserted him; the people of his household are on the

[043-132]point of leaving him; and though they were willing to remain, our lord

[043-133]is not able to keep them together; our said lord, your son, is so

[043-134]destitute of money, that he hath not a penny in the world, nor a penny

[043-135]can he get credit for."

[043-136]One consequence of this battle of Kilmainham was, that while Art

[043-137]McMurrogh lived, no further attacks were made upon his kindred or

[043-138]country. He died at Ross, on the first day of January, 1417, in the

[043-139]60th year of his age. His Brehon, O'Doran, having also died suddenly on

[043-140]the same day, it was supposed they were both poisoned by a drink

[043-141]prepared for them by a woman of the town. "He was," say our impartial

[043-142]_Four Masters_, who seldom speak so warmly of any Leinster Prince, "a

[043-143]man distinguished for his hospitality, knowledge, and feats of arms; a

[043-144]man full of prosperity and royalty; a founder of churches and

[043-145]monasteries by his bounty and contributions," and one who had defended

[043-146]his Province from the age of sixteen to sixty.

[043-148]On his recovery from the effects of his wound, the Duke of Lancaster

[043-149]returned finally to England, appointing Prior Butler his Deputy, who

[043-150]filled that office for five consecutive years. Butler was an

[043-151]illegitimate son of the late Earl of Ormond, and naturally a

[043-152]Lancasterian: among the Irish he was called Thomas _Baccagh_, on

[043-153]account of his lameness. He at once abandoned South Leinster as a field

[043-154]of operations, and directed all his efforts to maintain the Pale in

[043-155]Kildare, Meath, and Louth. His chief antagonist in this line of action

[043-156]was Murrogh or Maurice O'Conor, of Offally. This powerful chief had

[043-157]lost two or three sons, but had gamed as many battles over former

[043-158]deputies. He was invariably aided by his connexions and neighbours, the

[043-159]MacGeoghegans of West-Heath. Conjointly they captured the castles and

[043-160]plundered the towns of their enemies, holding their prisoners to ransom

[043-161]or carrying off their flocks. In 1411 O'Conor held to ransom the

[043-162]English Sheriff of Meath, and somewhat later defeated Prior Butler in a

[043-163]pitched battle. His greatest victory was the battle of Killucan, fought

[043-164]on the 10th day of May, 1414. In this engagement MacGeoghegan was, as

[043-165]usual, his comrade. All the power of the English Pale was arrayed

[043-166]against them. Sir Thomas Mereward, Baron of Screen, "and a great many

[043-167]officers and common soldiers were slain," and among the prisoners were

[043-168]Christopher Fleming, son of the Baron of Slane, for whom a ransom of

[043-169]1,400 marks was paid, and the ubiquitous Sir Jenico d'Artois, who, with

[043-170]some others, paid "twelve hundred marks, beside a reward and fine for

[043-171]intercession." A Parliament which sat at Dublin for thirteen weeks, in

[043-172]1413, and a foray into Wicklow, complete the notable acts of Thomas

[043-173]_Baccagh's_ viceroyalty. Soon after the accession of Henry V. (1413),

[043-174]he was summoned to accompany that warlike monarch into France, and for

[043-175]a short interval the government was exercised by Sir John Stanley, who

[043-176]died shortly after his arrival, and by the Archbishop of Dublin, as

[043-177]Commissioner. On the eve of St. Martin's Day, 1414, Sir John Talbot,

[043-178]afterwards so celebrated as first Earl of Shrewsbury, landed at Dalkey,

[043-179]with the title of Lord Lieutenant.

[043-180]The appointment of this celebrated Captain, on the brink of a war with

[043-181]France, was an admission of the desperate strait to which the English

[043-182]interest had been reduced. And if the end could ever justify the means,

[043-183]Henry V., from his point of view, might have defended on that ground

[043-184]the appointment of this inexorable soldier. Adopting the system of Sir

[043-185]Thomas Butler, Talbot paid little or no attention to South Leinster,

[043-186]but aimed in the first place to preserve to his sovereign, Louth and

[043-187]Meath. His most southern point of operation, in his first Lieutenancy,

[043-188]was Leix, but his continuous efforts were directed against the O'Conors

[043-189]of Offally and the O'Hanlons and McMahons of Oriel. For three

[043-190]succeeding years he made circuits through these tribes, generally by

[043-191]the same route, west and north, plundering chiefs and churches, sparing

[043-192]"neither saint nor sanctuary." On his return to Dublin after these

[043-193]forays, he exacted with a high hand whatever he wanted for his

[043-194]household. When he returned to England, 1419, he carried along with

[043-195]him, according to the chronicles of the Pale—"the curses of many,

[043-196]because he, being run much in debt for victuals, and divers other

[043-197]things, would pay little or nothing at all." Among the natives he left

[043-198]a still worse reputation. The plunder of a bard was regarded by them as

[043-199]worse, if possible, than the spoliation of a sanctuary. One of Talbot's

[043-200]immediate predecessors was reputed to have died of the malediction of a

[043-201]bard of West-Meath, whose property he had appropriated; but as if to

[043-202]show his contempt of such superstition, Talbot suffered no son of song

[043-203]to escape him. Their satires fell powerless on his path. Not only did

[043-204]he enrich himself, by means lawful and unlawful, but he created

[043-205]interest, which, a few years afterwards, was able to checkmate the

[043-206]Desmonds and Ormonds. The see of Dublin falling vacant during his

[043-207]administration, he procured the appointment of his brother Richard as

[043-208]Archbishop, and left him, at his departure, in temporary possession of

[043-209]the office of Lord Deputy. Branches of his family were planted at

[043-210]Malahide, Belgarde, and Talbotstown, in Wicklow, the representatives of

[043-211]which survive till this day.

[043-212]One of this Lieutenant's most acceptable offices to the State was the

[043-213]result of stratagem rather than of arms. The celebrated Art McMurrogh

[043-214]was succeeded, in 1417, by his son, Donogh, who seems to have inherited

[043-215]his valour, without his prudence. In 1419, in common with the O'Conor

[043-216]of Offally, his father's friend, he was entrapped into the custody of

[043-217]Talbot. O'Conor, the night of his capture, escaped with his companions,

[043-218]and kept up the war until his death: McMurrogh was carried to London

[043-219]and confined in the Tower. Here he languished for nine weary years. At

[043-220]length, in 1428, Talbot, having "got license to make the best of him,"

[043-221]held him to ransom. The people of his own province released him, "which

[043-222]was joyful news to the Irish."

[043-223]But neither the aggrandizement of new nor the depression of old

[043-224]families effected any cardinal change in the direction of events. We

[043-225]have traced for half a century, and are still farther to follow out,

[043-226]the natural consequences of the odious _Statute of Kilkenny_. Although

[043-227]every successive Parliament of the Pale recited and re-enacted that

[043-228]statute, every year saw it dispensed in particular cases, both as to

[043-229]trading, intermarriage, and fostering with the natives. Yet the virus

[043-230]of national proscription outlived all the experience of its futility.

[043-231]In 1417, an English petition was presented to the English Parliament,

[043-232]praying that the law, excluding Irish ecclesiastics from Irish

[043-233]benefices, should be strictly enforced; and the same year they

[043-234]prohibited the influx of fugitives from Ireland, while the Pale

[043-235]Parliament passed a corresponding act against allowing any one to

[043-236]emigrate without special license. At a Parliament held at Dublin in

[043-237]1421, O'Hedian, Archbishop of Cashel, was impeached by Gese, Bishop of

[043-238]Waterford, the main charges being that he loved none of the English

[043-239]nation; that he presented no Englishman to a living; and that he

[043-240]designed to make himself King of Munster. This zealous assembly also

[043-241]adopted a petition of grievances to the King, praying that as the

[043-242]Irish, who had done homage to King Richard, "had long since taken arms

[043-243]against the government notwithstanding their recognizances payable in

[043-244]the Apostolic chamber, his Highness the King would lay their conduct

[043-245]before the Pope, and prevail on the Holy Father to publish _a crusade

[043-246]against them_, to follow up the intention of his predecessor's grant to

[043-247]Henry II.!"

[043-248]In the temporal order, as we have seen, the policy of hatred brought

[043-249]its own punishment. "The Pale," which may be said to date from the

[043-250]passing of the _Statute of Kilkenny_ (1367), was already abridged more

[043-251]than one-half. The Parliament of Kilkenny had defined it as embracing

[043-252]"Louth, Meath, Dublin, Kildare, Catherlough, Kilkenny, Wexford,

[043-253]Waterford, and Tipperary," each governed by Seneschals or Sheriffs. In

[043-254]1422 Dunlavan and Ballymore are mentioned as the chief keys of Dublin

[043-255]and Kildare—and in the succeeding reign Callan in Oriel is set down as

[043-256]the chief key of that part. Dikes to keep out the enemy were made from

[043-257]Tallaght to Tassagard, at Rathconnell in Meath, and at other places in

[043-258]Meath and Kildare. These narrower limits it long retained, and the

[043-259]usual phrase in all future legislation by which the assemblies of the

[043-260]Anglo-Irish define their jurisdiction is "the four shires." So

[043-261]completely was this enclosure isolated from the rest of the country

[043-262]that, in the reign at which we have now arrived, both the Earls of

[043-263]Desmond and Ormond were exempted from attending certain sittings of

[043-264]Parliament, and the Privy Council, on the ground that they could not do

[043-265]so without marching through the enemy's country at great risk and

[043-266]inconvenience. It is true occasional successes attended the military

[043-267]enterprises of the Anglo-Irish, even in these days of their lowest

[043-268]fortunes. But they had chosen to adopt a narrow, bigoted, unsocial

[043-269]policy; a policy of exclusive dealing and perpetual estrangement from

[043-270]their neighbours dwelling on the same soil, and they had their reward.

[043-271]Their borders were narrowed upon them; they were penned up in one

[043-272]corner of the kingdom, out of which they could not venture a league

[043-273]without license and protection, from the free clansmen they insincerely

[043-274]affected to despise.
