Sir John Davis (also spelt Davies) was Attorney-General for Ireland under [[James I]] and a principal superintendent of the legal reforms introduced after the [[Nine Years' War]] and the [[Flight of the Earls]]. He left two very interesting tracts on Irish affairs and was the Crown's candidate for the Speakership of the Irish Parliament of 1613, in a contest that descended into violent disorder.

## Legal Reformer

Davis arrived in Ireland as part of the wave of English administrators who followed the military conquest of the early 1600s. On the Northern Circuit, Sir Edward Pelham and Sir John Davis were accompanied by the Deputy in person, with a numerous retinue, as the Crown sought to extend the reach of English law across the province of Ulster. (064-090) Davis was one of the principal superintendents of the new legal regulations, and he observed with satisfaction the gradual displacement of the old-fashioned Brehon legal customs by the English common law system. (064-109)

Davis also served on the royal commission issued to [[Sir Arthur Chichester]], Lambert, St. John, Ridgeway, Moore, Parsons, and the Archbishop of Armagh and Bishop of Derry, to inquire into the portions of land forfeited after the Flight of the Earls — the inquiry that laid the legal groundwork for the [[Plantation of Ulster]]. (064-226)

## Writings on Ireland

Sir John Davis left two very interesting tracts on Irish affairs which provide an invaluable contemporary account of the state of Ireland in the early 17th century. (064-109) He was a fervent advocate of the Crown's policy and famously described James I as having given Ireland her "first free Parliament" — a claim that later historians, with the facts before them, cannot agree with. (064-308) Nevertheless, his writings remain a primary source of the first importance for the legal and political history of Jacobean Ireland.

> Sir John Davis, who has left us two very interesting tracts on Irish affairs, speaking of the new legal regulations of which he was one of the principal superintendents. (064-109)

## The Speakership Contest of 1613

The most dramatic episode of Davis's Irish career came in the Parliament of 1613. The Crown had engineered a Protestant majority in the Commons, with 25 Protestant bishops in the Lords, so that the Deputy was certain of a majority on all points of ecclesiastical legislation. (064-306)

The preliminary trial of strength was the election of the Speaker. The candidate of the Castle was Sir John Davis; the candidate of the Catholic recusants was Sir John Everard, who had resigned his seat on the bench rather than take the oath of supremacy. (064-322) When the Catholic members attempted to install Everard in the Chair, the government supporters on their return placed Sir John Davis in Everard's lap, and a scene of violent disorder ensued. (064-327) The House broke up in confusion, and the recusants in a body withdrew — a parliamentary debacle that foreshadowed the deepening sectarian divisions of the coming decades.

## Legacy

Sir John Davis exemplified the type of the Jacobean administrator-scholar: energetic in extending English law, prolific in recording his observations, and unyielding in promoting the Crown's interest against the Catholic majority. His tracts on Irish affairs remained influential for generations, shaping English understanding of Irish legal and political conditions. Yet his role in the packed Parliament of 1613 and his zeal for the forfeiture commissions placed him firmly on the side of those who sought to remake Ireland through conquest and plantation rather than conciliation.
