## Overview

William Sampson was an Irish lawyer and a leading member of the Society of [[United Irishmen]]. (100-014) Born in 1764, he belonged to the generation that came of age during the Volunteer movement and the high hopes of Irish patriotic reform, only to be drawn into the revolutionary conspiracies of the 1790s. (100-018) Arrested in March 1798 alongside [[Lord Edward Fitzgerald]], he was imprisoned for four years before being expatriated to New York, where he lived out the remainder of his life. (102-173) (104-322)

## The United Irishmen

Sampson belonged to the celebrated group known to history as the United Irishmen, a body of public men who sought to unite Catholic, Protestant, and Dissenter in a common cause for Irish liberty. The leading characters of the movement were all born within a few years of each other:

> Neilson in 1761, Tone, Arthur O'Conor and Lord Edward Fitzgerald in '62, McNevin in '63, Sampson and Thomas Addis Emmet in '64, and Russell in '67. They had emerged into manhood while the drums of the Volunteers were beating victorious marches, when the public hopes ran high, and the language of patriotism was the familiar speech of every-day life. (100-017)

In a settled state of society, these young men of talent would have carried their abilities into the service of established institutions. But Irish society in the last years of the eighteenth century was not in a settled condition. (100-023) The fascination of French revolutionary example and the goading sense of national wrongs only half-righted inflamed the younger generation with a passionate thirst for speedy and summary justice. (100-027)

## The Arrests of March 1798

The first months of 1798 saw events follow each other fast and furiously. (102-168) Towards the end of February, [[Arthur O'Conor]], Father James Quigley, and the brothers John and Benjamin Binns were arrested at Margate on their way to France. (102-169) On the 6th of March, the government struck at the heart of the United Irish organization in Dublin:

> the _Press_ newspaper, the Dublin organ of the party, as the _Star_ had been the Ulster organ, was seized by Government, Lord Edward Fitzgerald and William Sampson being at the time in the office. (102-173)

The blow fell more heavily still on the 12th of March. On the information of the traitor [[Thomas Reynolds]], the Leinster delegates were seized in conclave with all their papers at the house of Oliver Bond in Bridge Street, Dublin. (102-175)

> On the same information, Addis Emmet and Dr. McNevin were taken in their own houses, and Sampson in the north of England: of all the executive, Lord Edward alone escaping those sent in search of him. (102-178)

Tone, receiving the news in France, recorded in his journal that it was "a terrible blow." (102-180) O'Conor's arrest in Kent, Sampson's in Carlisle, and the other arrests in Belfast and Dublin proved too truly that treason was at work and that the much-prized oath of secrecy was no protection whatever against the devices of the Castle and the depravity of its secret agents. (102-181)

## Imprisonment and Expatriation

Sampson spent four years in confinement following his arrest. A gallant old soldier, Colonel Stuart, endeavoured to soften the hard realities of a prison by all the kind attentions his instructions permitted him to show these unfortunate gentlemen. (104-315)

At the Peace of Amiens in 1802, the surviving United Irish prisoners were at last allowed the melancholy privilege of expatriation. (104-318) While some cast their lot in France, Sampson joined those who sailed for America:

> Emmet, McNevin, Sampson, and the family of Tone were reunited in New York, where the many changes and distractions of a great metropolitan community have not even yet obliterated the memories of their virtues, their talents, and their accomplishments. (104-322)

In New York, Sampson rebuilt his legal career and became a respected figure in the city's public life. He died there in 1836, one of the last survivors of the United Irish leadership.
