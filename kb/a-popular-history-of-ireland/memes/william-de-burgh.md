William de Burgh was a powerful Anglo-Norman baron of the family of Fitz-Aldelm. He played a decisive role in extending Norman influence into [[Connaught]] during the late twelfth and early thirteenth centuries, and became the progenitor of the Burkes of Connaught.

> "William de Burgh, a powerful Baron of the family of Fitz-Aldelm, the former Lord Justice, sided with the opponents of Cathal [Crovdearg O'Conor], while de Courcy, and subsequently the younger de Lacy, fought on his side." (029-134)

## Intervention in Connaught

De Burgh arrived in Ireland during a period of intense succession conflict in Connaught. In the twelve years following the death of Moinmoy O'Conor, the Normans found repeated opportunities to intervene in provincial affairs.

> "In the twelve years which intervened between the death of Moinmoy and the establishment of the power of Cathal Crovdearg O'Conor, the Normans had repeated opportunities for intervention in the affairs of Connaught." (029-131)

De Burgh consistently backed the opponents of Cathal Crovdearg O'Conor, while [[John de Courcy]] and the younger de Lacy fought on Cathal's side. The rival barons switched allegiances frequently, fighting as desperately against their former candidates as they had previously fought for them.

## The Battle of Athenry

De Burgh's most significant military achievement came at the Second Battle of [[Athenry]], where he and John de Bermingham jointly commanded forces that defeated Felim O'Conor, King of Connacht. The young king — only twenty-three years of age — fell in the very morning of his fame.

> "Felim O'Conor himself, in the twenty-third year of his age, and the very morning of his fame, fell with the rest, and his kindred, the Sil-Murray, were left for a season an easy prey to William de Burgh and John de Bermingham, the joint commanders in the battle." (037-093)

The victory devastated the O'Conor dynasty, though the chroniclers' claim that the name and race of O'Conor were cut off to a man proved exaggerated.

## The McWilliam Legacy

De Burgh's most enduring contribution to Irish history was the dynasty he founded. His posterity gradually adopted Gaelic customs and identity, assuming the name McWilliam and renouncing English allegiance.

> "The posterity of William de Burgh then assumed the name of McWilliam, and renounced the laws, language, and allegiance of England." (038-117)

This Gaelicisation produced the McWilliam Burkes — later the Earls of Clanrickarde and the Burkes of Mayo — who became powerful Gaelic-Irish lords. By the fifteenth century, Turlogh O'Conor was asserting supremacy over the McWilliams, practising against de Burgh's descendants the very same policy of intervention that the first de Burgh had employed against the O'Conors.

## Legacy

William de Burgh exemplifies the ambiguous legacy of the Norman invasion. His family arrived as conquerors, fought alongside and against native Irish nobility, and ultimately became absorbed into the Gaelic order they had sought to dominate.

The name de Burgh — transformed into Burke, Bourke, and McWilliam — remains one of the most widespread surnames in Connaught, a living monument to the complex processes of conquest, adaptation, and assimilation that defined medieval Irish history.
