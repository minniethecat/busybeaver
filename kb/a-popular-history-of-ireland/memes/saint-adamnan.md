## Overview

Saint Adamnan (c. 624–704), the ninth Abbot of [[Iona]], was one of the most influential Irish churchmen of the seventh century. Succeeding as the sixth abbot after [[Saint Columba]] (006-115), he is remembered for his writings; which almost earned him the title of Father of British History alongside [[Bede]] (006-119–006-120);

and for his humanitarian legislation protecting women from the horrors of war (006-141–006-143). His feast day is celebrated on the 23rd of September (018-097).

## Biography

### Early Life and Elevation to Abbacy

Adamnan was born into the Cenel Conaill, a branch of the northern Ui Neill, around the year 624. He was a kinsman of Saint Columba, the founder of Iona, and like his great predecessor dedicated his life to the monastic community established on that island. Little is recorded of his early career before he became abbot, but his erudition and sanctity marked him as a worthy successor.

The remains of King Egfrid of Northumbria were received from the hands of his conquerors by the sixth abbot after Columbkill, "equal in learning and sanctity to any who preceded him" (006-116–006-117).

### The Liberation of the Captives

During the reign of King Egfrid of Northumbria, a punitive expedition under Beort had carried off Christian captives from Ireland and sold them into slavery. This practice was directly contrary to the teachings of the Columban mission, and Adamnan resolved to act:

> Now every missionary that ever went out from Iona, had taught that to reduce Christians to slavery was wholly inconsistent with a belief in the doctrines of the Gospel. (006-126)

Adamnan voluntarily undertook a journey to York, where Aldfrid; a prince educated in Ireland and the author of an "Itinerary" of Ireland that survives to this day; now reigned as king (006-131–006-132). The Abbot of Iona succeeded in his humane mission, and crossing over to his native land, he restored sixty of the captives to their homes and kindred (006-133–006-135).

### The Law of the Innocents (A.D. 684)

While the liberated exiles rejoiced on the plain of Meath, the tent of the Abbot of Iona was pitched on the rath of [[Tara]] (006-136–006-137). It was there, or at some other royal residence, that Adamnan procured the passing of a landmark law in the year 684:

> Adamnan on this visit procured the passing of a law, (A.D. 684,) forbidding women to accompany an army to battle, or to engage personally in the conflict. (006-141–006-143)

This law, known later as the "Law of the Innocents" (Cain Adomnan), is a remarkable testament to the humanitarian influence of the early Irish Church. McGee observes that "the mild maternal genius of Christianity is faithfully exhibited in such a law, which consummates the glory of the worthy successor of Columbkill" (006-143–006-145).

### Relations with the High Kings

Adamnan's influence extended to the highest political circles of Ireland. During the reign of Finnacta the Hospitable, a formal debate arose over the abolition of the ancient Boru tribute levied upon Leinster. The decision must have been made around the time that Abbot Adamnan was in Ireland in 684, and "that illustrious personage is said to have been opposed to the abolition" (006-165–006-167).

Though the tribute was ultimately abolished through the intercession of Saint Moling, Adamnan's voice carried weight in the councils of the kingdom.

Saint Finnacta fell in battle in the twentieth year of his reign, and Adamnan survived him by ten years (006-173–006-174). He revisited Ireland in 697 under the short reign of Loingsech and concerned himself chiefly with endeavouring to induce his countrymen to adopt the Roman rule regarding the tonsure and the celebration of Easter (006-175–006-177).

### The Synod of Tara and the Easter Controversy

On this later visit, an important Synod of the Clergy was held at [[Tara]] under the presidency of Flan, Archbishop of [[Armagh]] (006-178–006-179). The Easter controversy divided the Irish Church for much of the seventh century. Adamnan aligned himself with those who favoured strict agreement with the practice of Rome and the Eastern Church.

McGee records the great names on both sides of the controversy: Columbanus, Finian, and Aidan for the traditional Irish computation; and "Cummian, the White, Laserian and Adamnan" in favour of conformity with the broader Catholic world (008-212–008-214).

### Writings and Literary Legacy

Adamnan's most enduring contribution to posterity lies in his writings. McGee describes him as "Adamnan, to whom Ireland and Scotland are equally indebted for his admirable writings, and who might almost dispute with Bede himself, the title of Father of British History" (006-118–006-120).

His work on the "situation of the Holy Places" (De Locis Sanctis), of which [[Bede]] gives an abstract, drew upon the account of the Frankish bishop Arculf, who had visited the Holy Land and was driven by storms to Iona (007-113–007-114). This work preserved for later ages a detailed description of Jerusalem and the sacred sites of Palestine as they stood in the seventh century.

Adamnan is also the author of the "Life of Saint Columba" (Vita Columbae), the most important hagiographical work to survive from the early medieval period in Britain and Ireland, which remains an invaluable source for the history of the Columban monastic community.

### Place Among the Irish Saints

McGee numbers Adamnan among the most illustrious Irish saints of the eighth century, listing him alongside Saint Cataldus of Tarentum, Saint Rumold of Brabant, and Clement and Albinus, "the Wisdom-seekers" (008-177–008-179). His legislative success is classed with that of Columbkill and Moling as one of the great achievements of the Irish clerical order in shaping society (008-018).

The veneration of Adamnan endured for centuries: as late as 1105, the primate Celsus was solemnly consecrated on "Saint Adamnan's day following; the 23rd of September" (018-097).

## Legacy

Saint Adamnan's legacy is threefold. First, his Law of the Innocents stands as one of the earliest humanitarian protections for women in European history, a testament to the civilising influence of the Irish Church. Second, his writings; the "Life of Columba" and "De Locis Sanctis"; are foundational sources for the history of early medieval Ireland, Scotland, and the Holy Land.

Third, his role in the Easter controversy helped bring the Irish Church into alignment with the wider Christian world. By the time of the Danish wars, the power of the clerical order had "very far declined from what it was, when Adamnan caused the law to be enacted to prevent women going to battle" (020-160–020-161), but the memory of his influence endured.
