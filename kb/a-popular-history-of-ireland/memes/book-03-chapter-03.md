CHAPTER III.

[019-001]THORLOGH MORE O'CONOR—MURKERTACH OF AILEACH—ACCESSION OF RODERICK

[019-002]O'CONOR.

[019-003]The successful ambition of Thorlogh O'Conor had thus added, as we have

[019-004]seen in the last chapter, a fifth dynasty to the number of competitors

[019-005]for the sovereignty. And if great energy and various talents could

[019-006]alone entitle a chief to rule over his country, this Prince well

[019-007]merited the obedience of his cotemporaries. He is the first of the

[019-008]latter kings who maintained a regular fleet at sea; at one time we find

[019-009]these Connaught galleys doing service on the coast of Cork, at another

[019-010]co-operating with his land forces, in the harbour of Derry. The year of

[019-011]his greatest power was the fifteenth of his reign (A.D. 1151), when his

[019-012]most signal success was obtained over his most formidable antagonists.

[019-013]Thorlogh O'Brien, King of Munster, successor to Conor of the

[019-014]fortresses, had on foot, in that year, an army of three battalions (or

[019-015]_caths_), each battalion consisting of 3,000 men, with which force he

[019-016]overawed some, and compelled others of the southern chiefs to withdraw

[019-017]their homage from his western namesake. The latter, uniting to his own

[019-018]the forces of Meath, and those of Leinster, recently reconciled to his

[019-019]supremacy, marched southward, and, encamping at Glanmire, received the

[019-020]adhesion of such Eugenian families as still struggled with desperation

[019-021]against the ascendency of the O'Briens. With these forces he

[019-022]encountered, at Moanmore, the army of the south, and defeated them,

[019-023]with the enormous loss of 7,000 men—a slaughter unparalleled throughout

[019-024]the war of succession. Every leading house in North Munster mourned the

[019-025]loss of either its chief or its tanist; some great families lost three,

[019-026]five, or seven brothers on that sanguinary day. The household of

[019-027]Kinkora was left without an heir, and many a near kinsman's seat was

[019-028]vacant in its hospitable hall. The O'Brien himself was banished into

[019-029]Ulster, where, from Murkertach, Prince of Aileach, he received the

[019-030]hospitality due to his rank and his misfortunes, not without an

[019-031]ulterior politic view on the part of the Ulster Prince. In this battle

[019-032]of Moanmore, Dermid McMurrogh, King of Leinster, of whom we shall hear

[019-033]hereafter, fought gallantly on the side of the victor. In the same

[019-034]year—but whether before or after the Munster campaign is uncertain—an

[019-035]Ulster force having marched into Sligo, Thorlogh met them near the

[019-036]Curlew mountains, and made peace with their king. A still more

[019-037]important interview took place the next year in the plain, or _Moy_,

[019-038]between the rivers Erne and Drowse, near the present Ballyshannon. On

[019-039]the _Bachall-Isa_ and the relics of Columbkill, Thorlogh and Murkertach

[019-040]made a solemn peace, which is thought to have included the recognition

[019-041]of O'Conor's supremacy. A third meeting was had during the summer in

[019-042]Meath, where were present, beside the Ard-Righ, the Prince of Aileach,

[019-043]Dermid of Leinster, and other chiefs and nobles. At this conference

[019-044]they divided Meath into east and west, between two branches of the

[019-045]family of Melaghlin. Part of Longford and South Leitrim were taken from

[019-046]Tiernan O'Ruarc, lord of Breffni, and an angle of Meath, including

[019-047]Athboy and the hill of Ward, was given him instead. Earlier in the same

[019-048]year, King Thorlogh had divided Munster into three parts, giving

[019-049]Desmond to MacCarthy, Ormond to Thaddeus O'Brien, who had fought under

[019-050]him at Moanmore, and leaving the remainder to the O'Brien, who had only

[019-051]two short years before competed with him for the sovereignty. By these

[019-052]subdivisions the politic monarch expected to weaken to a great degree

[019-053]the power of the rival families of Meath and Munster. It was an

[019-054]arbitrary policy which could originate only on the field of battle, and

[019-055]could be enforced only by the sanction of victory. Thorlogh O'Brien,

[019-056]once King of all Munster, refused to accept a mere third, and carrying

[019-057]away his jewels and valuables, including the drinking horn of the great

[019-058]Brian, he threw himself again on the protection of Murkertach of

[019-059]Aileach. The elder branch of the family of O'Melaghlin were equally

[019-060]indisposed to accept half of Meath, where they had claimed the whole

[019-061]from the Shannon to the sea. To complicate still more this tangled web,

[019-062]Dermid, King of Leinster, about the same time (A.D. 1153), eloped with

[019-063]Dervorgoil, wife of O'Ruarc of Breffni, and daughter of O'Melaghlin,

[019-064]who both appealed to the monarch for vengeance on the ravager. Up to

[019-065]this date Dermid had acted as a steadfast ally of O'Conor, but when

[019-066]compelled by the presence of a powerful force on his borders to restore

[019-067]the captive, or partner of his guilt, he conceived an enmity for the

[019-068]aged king, which he extended, with increased virulence, to his son and

[019-069]successor.

[019-070]What degree of personal criminality to attach to this elopement it is

[019-071]hard to say. The cavalier in the case was on the wintry side of fifty,

[019-072]while the lady had reached the mature age of forty-four. Such examples

[019-073]have been, where the passions of youth, surviving the period most

[019-074]subject to their influence, have broken out with renewed frenzy on the

[019-075]confines of old age. Whether the flight of Dermid and Dervorgoil arose

[019-076]from a mere criminal passion, is not laid down with certainty in the

[019-077]old Annals, though national and local tradition strongly point to that

[019-078]conclusion. The Four Masters indeed state that after the restoration of

[019-079]the lady she "returned to O'Ruarc," another point wanting confirmation.

[019-080]We know that she soon afterwards retired to the shelter of Mellifont

[019-081]Abbey, where she ended her days towards the close of the century, in

[019-082]penitence and alms-deeds.

[019-083]Murtogh of Aileach now became master of the situation. Thorlogh was old

[019-084]and could not last long; Dermid of Leinster was for ever estranged from

[019-085]him; the new arbitrary divisions, though made with the general consent,

[019-086]satisfied no one. With a powerful force he marched southward, restored

[019-087]to the elder branch of the O'Melaghlins the whole of Meath, defeated

[019-088]Thaddeus O'Brien, obliterated Ormond from the map, restored the old

[019-089]bounds of Thomond and Desmond, and placed his guest, the banished

[019-090]O'Brien, on the throne of Cashel. A hostile force, under Roderick

[019-091]O'Conor, was routed, and retreated to their own territory. The next

[019-092]year (A.D. 1154) was signalized by a fierce naval engagement between

[019-093]the galleys of King Thorlogh and those of Murtogh, on the coast of

[019-094]Innishowen. The latter, recruited by vessels hired from the Gael and

[019-095]Galls of Cantire, the Arran Isles, and Man, were under the command of

[019-096]MacScellig; the Connaught fleet was led by O'Malley and O'Dowda. The

[019-097]engagement, which lasted from the morning till the evening, ended in

[019-098]the repulse of the Connaught fleet, and the death of O'Dowda. The

[019-099]occurrence is remarkable as the first general sea-fight between vessels

[019-100]in the service of native Princes, and as reminding us forcibly of the

[019-101]lessons acquired by the Irish during the Danish period.

[019-103]During the two years of life—which remained to King Thorlogh O'Conor,

[019-104]he had the affliction of seeing the fabric of power, which had taken

[019-105]him nearly half a century to construct, abridged at many points, by his

[019-106]more vigorous northern rival. Murtogh gave law to territories far south

[019-107]of the ancient _esker_. He took hostages from the Danes of Dublin, and

[019-108]interposed in the affairs of Munster. In the year 1156, the closing

[019-109]incidents which signalized the life of Thorlogh More, was a new peace

[019-110]which he made between the people of Breffni, Meath, and Connaught, and

[019-111]the reception of hostages from his old opponent, the restored O'Brien.

[019-112]While this new light of prosperity was shining on his house, he passed

[019-113]away from this life, on the 13th of the Kalends of June, in the 68th

[019-114]year of his age, and the 50th of his government. By his last will he

[019-115]bequeathed to the clergy numerous legacies, which are thus enumerated

[019-116]by Geoffrey Keating: "namely, four hundred and forty ounces of gold,

[019-117]and forty marks of silver; and all the other valuable treasures he

[019-118]possessed, both cups and precious stones, both steeds and cattle and

[019-119]robes, chess-boards, bows, quivers, arrows, equipments, weapons,

[019-120]armour, and utensils." He was interred beside the high altar of the

[019-121]Cathedral of Clonmacnoise, to which he had been in life and in death a

[019-122]munificent benefactor.

[019-123]The Prince of Aileach now assumed the title of Monarch, and after some

[019-124]short-lived opposition from Roderick O'Conor, his sovereignty was

[019-125]universally acknowledged. From the year 1161 until his death, he might

[019-126]fairly be called Ard-Righ, without opposition, since the hostages of

[019-127]all Ireland were in those last five years in his hands. These hostages

[019-128]were retained at the chief seat of power of the northern dynasty, the

[019-129]fortress of Aileach, which crowns a hill nearly a thousand feet high,

[019-130]at the head of Lough Swilly. To this stronghold the ancestor of Murtogh

[019-131]had removed early in the Danish period, from the more exposed and more

[019-132]ancient Emania, beside Armagh. On that hill-summit the ruins of Aileach

[019-133]may still be traced, with its inner wall twelve feet thick, and its

[019-134]three concentric ramparts, the first enclosing one acre, the second

[019-135]four, and the last five acres. By what remains we can still judge of

[019-136]the strength of the stronghold which watched over the waters of Lough

[019-137]Swilly like a sentinel on an outpost. No Prince of the Northern Hy-Nial

[019-138]had for two centuries entered Aileach in such triumph or with so many

[019-139]nobles in his train, as did Murtogh in the year 1161, But whether the

[019-140]supreme power wrought a change for the worse in his early character, or

[019-141]that the lords of Ulster had begun to consider the line of Conn as

[019-142]equals rather than sovereigns, he was soon involved in quarrels with

[019-143]his own Provincial suffragans which ended in his defeat and death. Most

[019-144]other kings of whom we have read found their difficulties in rival

[019-145]dynasties and provincial prejudices; but this ruler, when most freely

[019-146]acknowledged abroad, was disobeyed and defeated at home. Having taken

[019-147]prisoner the lord of Ulidia (Down), with whom he had previously made a

[019-148]solemn peace, he ordered his eyes to be put out, and three of his

[019-149]principal relatives to be executed. This and other arbitrary acts so

[019-150]roused the lords of Leath Conn, that they formed a league against him,

[019-151]at the head of which stood Donogh O'Carroll, lord of Oriel, the next

[019-152]neighbour to the cruelly ill-treated chief of Ulidia. In the year 1166,

[019-153]this chief, with certain tribes of Tyrone and North Leitrim, to the

[019-154]number of three battalions (9,000 men), attacked the patrimony of the

[019-155]monarch—that last menace and disgrace to an Irish king. Murtogh with

[019-156]his usual valour, but not his usual fortune, encountered them in the

[019-157]district of the Fews, with an Inferior force, chiefly his own

[019-158]tribesmen. Even these deserted him on the eve of the battle, so that he

[019-159]was easily surprised and slain, only thirteen men falling in the

[019-160]affray. This action, of course, is unworthy the name of a battle, but

[019-161]resulting in the death of the monarch, it became of high political

[019-162]importance.

[019-163]Roderick O'Conor, son of Thorlogh More, was at this period in the tenth

[019-164]year of his reign over Connaught, and the fiftieth year of his age.

[019-165]Rathcrogan, the chief seat of his jurisdiction, had just attained to

[019-166]the summit of its glory. The site of this now almost forgotten palace

[019-167]is traceable in the parish of Elphin, within three miles of the modern

[019-168]village of Tulsk. Many objects contributed to its interest and

[019-169]importance in Milesian times. There were the _Naasteaghna_, or place of

[019-170]assembly of the clans of Connaught, "the Sacred Cave," which in the

[019-171]Druidic era was supposed to be the residence of a god, and the _Relig

[019-172]na Righ_—the venerable cemetery of the Pagan kings of the West, where

[019-173]still the red pillar stone stood over the grave of Dathy, and many

[019-174]another ancient tomb could be as clearly distinguished. The relative

[019-175]importance of Rathcrogan we may estimate by the more detailed

[019-176]descriptions of the extent and income of its rivals—Kinkora and

[019-177]Aileach. In an age when Roscommon alone contained 470 fortified _duns_,

[019-178]over all which the royal rath presided; when half the tributes of the

[019-179]island were counted at its gate, it must have been the frequent

[019-180]_rendezvous_ of armies, the home of many guests, the busy focus of

[019-181]intrigue, and the very elysium of bards, story-tellers, and mendicants.

[019-182]In an after generation, Cathal, the red-handed O'Conor, from some

[019-183]motive of policy or pleasure, transferred the seat of government to the

[019-184]newly-founded Ballintober: in the lifetime of Thorlogh More, and the

[019-185]first years of Roderick, when the fortunes of the O'Conors were at

[019-186]their full, Rathcrogan was the co-equal in strength and in splendour of

[019-187]Aileach and Kinkora.

[019-188]Advancing directly from this family seat, on the first tidings of

[019-189]Murtogh's death, Roderick presented himself before the walls of Dublin,

[019-190]which opened its gates, accepted his stipend of four thousand head of

[019-191]cattle, and placed hostages for its fidelity in his hands. He next

[019-192]marched rapidly to Drogheda, with an auxiliary force of Dublin Danes,

[019-193]and there O'Carroll, lord of Oriel (Louth), came into his camp, and

[019-194]rendered him homage. Retracing his steps he entered Leinster, with an

[019-195]augmented force, and demanded hostages from Dermid McMurrogh. Thirteen

[019-196]years had passed since his father had taken up arms to avenge the rape

[019-197]of Dervorgoil, and had earned the deadly hatred of the abductor. That

[019-198]hatred, in the interim, had suffered no decrease, and sooner than

[019-199]submit to Roderick, the ravager burned his own city of Ferns to the

[019-200]ground, and retreated into his fastnesses. Roderick proceeded

[019-201]southward, obtained the adhesion of Ossory and Munster; confirming

[019-202]Desmond to McCarthy, and Thomond to O'Brien. Returning to Leinster, he

[019-203]found that Tiernan O'Ruarc had entered the province, at the head of an

[019-204]auxiliary army, and Dermid, thus surrounded, deserted by most of his

[019-205]own followers, outwitted and overmatched, was feign to seek safety in

[019-206]flight beyond seas (A.D. 1168). A solemn sentence of banishment was

[019-207]publicly pronounced against him by the assembled Princes, and Morrogh,

[019-208]his cousin, commonly called Morrogh _na Gael_, or "of the Irish," to

[019-209]distinguish him from Dermid _na Gall_, or "of the Stranger," was

[019-210]inaugurated in his stead. From Morrogh _na Gael_ they took seventeen

[019-211]hostages, and so Roderick returned rejoicing to Rathcrogan, and O'Ruarc

[019-212]to Breffni, each vainly imagining that he had heard the last of the

[019-213]dissolute and detested King of Leinster.
