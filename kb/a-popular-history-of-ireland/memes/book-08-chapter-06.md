CHAPTER VI.

[057-001]SEQUEL OF THE SECOND GERALDINE LEAGUE—PLANTATION OF MUNSTER—EARLY

[057-002]CAREER OF HUGH O'NEIL, EARL OF TYRONE—PARLIAMENT OF 1585.

[057-003]We must continue to read the history of Ireland by the light of foreign

[057-004]affairs, and our chief light at this period is derived from Spain. The

[057-005]death of Don Sebastian concentrated the thoughts of Philip II. on

[057-006]Portugal, which he forcibly annexed to the Spanish crown. The progress

[057-007]of the insurrection in the Netherlands also occupied so large a place

[057-008]in his attention, that his projects against Elizabeth were postponed,

[057-009]year after year, to the bitter disappointment of the Irish leaders. It

[057-010]may seem far-fetched to assert, but it is not the less certainly true,

[057-011]that the fate of Catholic Munster was intimately involved in the change

[057-012]of masters in Portugal, and the fluctuations of war in the Netherlands,

[057-013]The "Undertakers," who had set their hearts on having the Desmond

[057-014]estates, determined that the Earl and his brothers should not live long

[057-015]in peace, however peaceably they might be disposed. The old trick of

[057-016]forging letters, already alluded to, grew into a common and familiar

[057-017]practice during this and the following reign. Such a letter, purporting

[057-018]to be written by the Earl of Desmond—at that period only too anxious to

[057-019]be allowed to live in peace—was made public at Dublin and London. It

[057-020]was addressed to Sir William Pelham, the temporary Lord Justice, and

[057-021]among other passages contained this patent invention—that he (the Earl

[057-022]and his brethren) "had taken this matter in hand with great authority,

[057-023]both from the Pope's holiness and King Philip, who do undertake to

[057-024]further us in our affairs, as we shall need." It is utterly incredible

[057-025]that any man in Desmond's position could have written such a

[057-026]letter—could have placed in the hands of his enemies a document which

[057-027]must for ever debar him from entering into terms with Elizabeth or her

[057-028]representatives in Ireland. We have no hesitation, therefore, in

[057-029]classing this pretended letter to Pelham with those admitted forgeries

[057-030]which drove the unfortunate Lord Thomas Fitzgerald into premature

[057-031]revolt, in the reign of Henry VIII.

[057-032]Sir John of Desmond had been nominated by the gallant Fitzmaurice in

[057-033]his last moments as the fittest person to rally the remaining defenders

[057-034]of religion and property in Munster. The Papal standard and benediction

[057-035]were almost all he could bequeath his successor, but the energy of

[057-036]John, aided by some favourable local occurrences, assembled a larger

[057-037]force for the campaign of 1579 than had lately taken the field. Without

[057-038]the open aid of the Earl, he contrived to get together at one time as

[057-039]many as 2,000 men, amongst whom not the least active officer was his

[057-040]younger brother, Sir James, hardly yet of man's age. Drs. Saunders and

[057-041]Allen, with several Spanish officers, accompanied this devoted but

[057-042]undisciplined multitude, sharing all the hardships of the men, and the

[057-043]counsels of the chiefs. Their first camp, and, so to speak, the nursery

[057-044]of their army, was among the inaccessible mountains of Slievelogher in

[057-045]Kerry, where the rudiments of discipline were daily inculcated. When

[057-046]they considered the time ripe for action, they removed their camp to

[057-047]the great wood of Kilmore, near Charleville, from which they might

[057-048]safely assail the line of communication between Cork and Limerick, the

[057-049]main depots of Elizabeth's southern army. Nearly half-way between these

[057-050]cities, and within a few miles of their new encampment, stood the

[057-051]strong town of Kilmallock on the little river Lubach. This famous old

[057-052]Geraldine borough, the focus of several roads, was the habitual

[057-053]stopping place of the Deputies in their progress, as well as of English

[057-054]soldiers on their march. The ancient fortifications, almost obliterated

[057-055]by Fitzmaurice eleven years before, had been replaced by strong walls,

[057-056]lined with earthworks, and crowned by towers. Here Sir William Drury

[057-057]fixed his head-quarters in the spring of 1579, summoning to his aid all

[057-058]the Queen's lieges in Munster. With a force of not less than 1,000

[057-059]English regulars under his own command, and perhaps twice that number

[057-060]under the banner of the Munster "Undertakers" and others, who obeyed

[057-061]the summons, he made an unsuccessful attempt to beat up the Geraldine

[057-062]quarters at Kilmore. One division of his force, consisting of 300 men

[057-063]by the Irish, and 200 by the English account, was cut to pieces, with

[057-064]their captains, Herbert, Price, and Eustace. The remainder retreated in

[057-065]disorder to their camp at Athneasy, a ford on the Morning Star River,

[057-066]four miles east of Kilmallock. For nine weeks Drury continued in the

[057-067]field, without gaining any advantage, yet so harassed day and night by

[057-068]his assailants that his health gave way under his anxieties. Despairing

[057-069]of recovery, he was removed by slow stages to Waterford—which would

[057-070]seem to indicate that his communications both with Cork and Limerick

[057-071]were impracticable—but died before reaching the first mentioned city.

[057-072]The chief command in Munster now devolved upon Sir Nicholas Malby, an

[057-073]officer who had seen much foreign service, while the temporary vacancy

[057-074]in the government was filled by the Council at Dublin, whose choice

[057-075]fell on Sir William Pelham, another distinguished military man, lately

[057-076]arrived from England.

[057-077]Throughout the summer and autumn months the war was maintained, with

[057-078]varying fortune on either side. In the combats of Gortnatibrid and

[057-079]Enagbeg, in Limerick, the final success, according to Irish accounts,

[057-080]was with the Geraldines, though they had the misfortune to lose

[057-081]Cardinal Allen, Sir Thomas Fitzgerald and Sir Thomas Browne. Retiring

[057-082]into winter quarters at Aharlow, they had a third engagement with the

[057-083]garrison of Kilmallock, which attempted, without success, to intercept

[057-084]their march. The campaign of 1580 was, however, destined to be

[057-085]decisive. Sir John of Desmond, being invited to an amicable conference

[057-086]by the Lord Barry, was entrapped by an English force under Captain

[057-087]Zouch, in the woods surrounding Castle Lyons, and put to death on the

[057-088]spot. The young Sir James had previously been captured on a foray into

[057-089]Muskerry, and executed at Cork, so that of the brothers there now

[057-090]remained but Earl Gerald, the next victim of the machinations which had

[057-091]already proved so fatal to his family. Perceiving at length the true

[057-092]designs cherished against him, the Earl took the field in the spring of

[057-093]1580, and obtained two considerable advantages, one at Pea-field,

[057-094]against the English under Roberts, and a second at Knockgraffon against

[057-095]the Anglo-Irish, under the brothers of the Earl of Ormond, the recusant

[057-096]members of the original league. Both these actions were fought in

[057-097]Tipperary, and raised anew the hopes of the Munster Catholics. An

[057-098]unsuccessful attempt on Adare was the only other military event in

[057-099]which the Earl bore a part; he wintered in Aharlow, where his Christmas

[057-100]was rather that of an outlaw than of the Lord Palatine of Desmond. In

[057-101]Aharlow he had the misfortune to lose the gifted and heroic Nuncio, Dr.

[057-102]Saunders, whose great services, at that period, taken together with

[057-103]those of Cardinal Allen, long endeared the faithful English to the

[057-104]faithful Irish Catholics.

[057-105]The sequel of the second Geraldine League may be rapidly narrated. In

[057-106]September, 1580, the fort at Smerwick, where Fitzmaurice had landed

[057-107]from Galicia, received a garrison of 800 men, chiefly Spaniards and

[057-108]Italians, under Don Stephen San Joseph. The place was instantly

[057-109]invested by sea and land, under the joint command of the new

[057-110]Lieutenant, Lord Grey de Wilton, and the Earl of Ormond. Among the

[057-111]officers of the besieging force were three especially notable men—Sir

[057-112]Walter Raleigh, the poet Spenser, and Hugh O'Neil, afterwards Earl of

[057-113]Tyrone, but at this time commanding a squadron of cavalry for her

[057-114]Majesty Queen Elizabeth. San Joseph surrendered the place on

[057-115]conditions; that savage outrage ensued, which is known in Irish history

[057-116]as "the massacre of Smerwick." Raleigh and Wingfield appear to have

[057-117]directed the operations by which 800 prisoners of war were cruelly

[057-118]butchered and flung over the rocks. The sea upon that coast is deep and

[057-119]the tides swift; but it has not proved deep enough to hide that horrid

[057-120]crime, or to wash the stains of such wanton bloodshed from the memory

[057-121]of its authors!

[057-122]For four years longer the Geraldine League flickered in the South.

[057-123]Proclamations offering pardon to all concerned, except Earl Gerald and

[057-124]a few of his most devoted adherents, had their effect. Deserted at

[057-125]home, and cut off from foreign assistance, the condition of Desmond

[057-126]grew more and more intolerable. On one occasion he narrowly escaped

[057-127]capture by rushing with his Countess into a river, and remaining

[057-128]concealed up to the chin in water. His dangers can hardly be paralleled

[057-129]by those of Bruce after the battle of Falkirk, or by the more familiar

[057-130]adventures of Charles Edward. At length, on the night of the 11th of

[057-131]November, 1584, he was surprised with only two followers in a lonesome

[057-132]valley about five miles distant from Tralee, among the mountains of

[057-133]Kerry. The spot is still remembered, and the name of "the Earl's road"

[057-134]transports the fancy of the traveller to that tragical scene. Cowering

[057-135]over the embers of a half-extinct fire in a miserable hovel, the lord

[057-136]of a country, which in time of peace had yielded an annual rental of

[057-137]"40,000 golden pieces," was despatched by the hands of common soldiers,

[057-138]without pity, or time, or hesitation. A few followers watching their

[057-139]_creaghts_ or herds, farther up the valley, found his bleeding trunk

[057-140]flung out upon the highway; the head was transported over seas, to rot

[057-141]upon the spikes of London Tower.

[057-142]The extirpation of the Munster Geraldines, in the right line, according

[057-143]to the theory of the "Undertakers" and the Court of England in general,

[057-144]vested in the Queen the 570,000 acres belonging to the late Earl.

[057-145]Proclamation was accordingly made throughout England, inviting "younger

[057-146]brothers of good families" to undertake the plantation of Desmond—each

[057-147]planter to obtain a certain scope of land, on condition of settling

[057-148]thereupon so many families—"none of the native Irish to be admitted."

[057-149]Under these conditions, Sir Christopher Hatton took up 10,000 acres in

[057-150]Waterford; Sir Walter Raleigh 12,000 acres, partly in Waterford and

[057-151]partly in Cork; Sir William Harbart, or Herbert, 13,000 acres in Kerry;

[057-152]Sir Edward Denny 6,000 in the same county; Sir Warham, St. Leger, and

[057-153]Sir Thomas Norris, 6,000 acres each in Cork; Sir William Courtney

[057-154]10,000 acres in Limerick; Sir Edward Fitton 11,500 acres in Tipperary

[057-155]and Waterford, and Edmund Spenser a modest 3,000 acres in Cork, on the

[057-156]beautiful Blackwater. The other notable Undertakers were the Hides,

[057-157]Butchers, Wirths, Berklys, Trenchards, Thorntons, Bourchers,

[057-158]Billingsleys, &c., &c. Some of these grants, especially Raleigh's, fell

[057-159]in the next reign into the ravening maw of Richard Boyle, the so-called

[057-160]"_great_ Earl of Cork"—probably the most pious hypocrite to be found in

[057-161]the long roll of the "Munster Undertakers."

[057-162]Before closing the present chapter, we must present to the reader, in a

[057-163]formal manner, the personage whose career is to occupy the chief

[057-164]remaining part of the present Book—Hugh O'Neil, best known by the title

[057-165]of Earl of Tyrone. We have seen him in the camp of the enemies of his

[057-166]country, learning the art of war on the shores of Dingle Bay—a witness

[057-167]to the horrors perpetrated at Smerwick. We may find him later in the

[057-168]same war—in 1584—serving under Perrott and Norris, along the Foyle and

[057-169]the Bann, for the expulsion of the Antrim Scots. The following year,

[057-170]for these and other good services, he received the patent of the

[057-171]Earldom originally conferred on his grandfather, Con O'Neil, but

[057-172]suffered to sink into abeyance by the less politic "John the Proud," in

[057-173]the days when he made his peace with the Queen. The next year he

[057-174]obtained from his clansmen the still higher title of O'Neil, and thus

[057-175]he contrived to combine, in his own person, every principle of

[057-176]authority likely to ensure him following and obedience, whether among

[057-177]the clansmen of Tyrone, or the townsmen upon its borders.

[057-179]O'Neil's last official act of co-operation with the Dublin government

[057-180]may be considered his participation in the Parliament convoked by Sir

[057-181]John Perrott in 1585, and prorogued till the following year. It is

[057-182]remarkable of this Parliament, the third and last of Elizabeth's long

[057-183]reign, that it was utterly barren of ecclesiastical legislation, if we

[057-184]except "an act against sorcery and witchcraft" from that category. The

[057-185]attainder of the late Earl of Desmond, and the living Viscount of

[057-186]Baltinglass, in arms with the O'Byrnes in Glenmalure, are the only

[057-187]measures of consequence to be found among the Irish statutes of the

[057-188]27th and 28th of Elizabeth. But though not remarkable for its

[057-189]legislation, the Parliament of 1585 is conspicuously so for its

[057-190]composition. Within its walls with the peers, knights, and burgesses of

[057-191]the anglicized counties, sat almost all the native chiefs of Ulster,

[057-192]Connaught, and Munster. The Leinster chiefs recently in arms, in

[057-193]alliance with the Earl of Desmond, generally absented themselves, with

[057-194]the exception of Feagh, son of Hugh, the senior of the O'Byrnes, and

[057-195]one of the noblest spirits of his race and age. He appears not to have

[057-196]had a seat in either House; but attended, on his own business, under

[057-197]the protection of his powerful friends and sureties.
