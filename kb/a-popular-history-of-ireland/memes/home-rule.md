## Overview

Home Rule was the political demand for the restoration of an [[irish-parliament|Irish Parliament]] with authority over domestic affairs, while Ireland remained part of the United Kingdom under the Crown. It was the dominant issue in Irish politics from the 1870s until the Easter Rising of 1916, succeeding the earlier Repeal movement of [[daniel-oconnell|Daniel O'Connell]] and preceding the full independence movement of Sinn Féin.

The term "Home Rule" encapsulated a range of constitutional proposals, from limited devolution within a federal United Kingdom to something approaching full legislative independence. Three Home Rule Bills were introduced in the British Parliament (1886, 1893, 1912–1914), each defeated or suspended, before the Government of Ireland Act 1920 established two devolved parliaments in Ireland.

## Background

### Repeal and Federalism

The Home Rule movement grew out of [[daniel-oconnell|O'Connell]]'s earlier campaign for the Repeal of the Act of Union (1800). [[daniel-oconnell|O'Connell]] organized mass "monster meetings" in the 1840s demanding the restoration of the [[irish-parliament|Irish Parliament]], but the movement collapsed after the government banned the planned meeting at Clontarf and [[daniel-oconnell|O'Connell]], always committed to constitutional methods, backed down.

After [[daniel-oconnell|O'Connell]]'s death and the catastrophe of the Great Famine (1845–1852), Irish constitutional nationalism revived in a new form. Isaac Butt, a Protestant lawyer and former Conservative, founded the Home Government Association in 1870 and the Home Rule League in 1873, arguing that Ireland could be self-governing while remaining loyal to the Crown.

### Parnell and the Irish Parliamentary Party

Under Charles Stewart Parnell's leadership in the 1880s, the Home Rule movement became a disciplined political force. Parnell's [[irish-parliament|Irish Parliament]]ary Party held the balance of power at Westminster, forcing the Liberal Party under William Ewart Gladstone to adopt Home Rule as government policy.

## Key Features

### The Home Rule Bills

- **First Home Rule Bill (1886):** Introduced by Gladstone, defeated in the House of Commons by a coalition of Conservatives and Liberal Unionists. The defeat split the Liberal Party.
- **Second Home Rule Bill (1893):** Passed the Commons but was rejected by the House of Lords, whose veto power over legislation was then absolute.
- **Third Home Rule Bill (1912–1914):** Passed by the Commons under the Parliament Act 1911, which had removed the Lords' absolute veto. The Bill was, however, suspended at the outbreak of the First World War and never implemented.

### Unionist Opposition

Home Rule was fiercely opposed by Irish Unionists, particularly those concentrated in [[ulster|Ulster]]. Unionists argued that Home Rule would mean "Rome Rule"—Catholic domination—and threatened armed resistance. The [[ulster|Ulster]] Volunteer Force was formed in 1913 to oppose Home Rule by force if necessary.

### Partition and the Government of Ireland Act 1920

The Government of Ireland Act 1920, a modified form of Home Rule, provided for two devolved parliaments: one in [[dublin|Dublin]] for the 26 southern and western counties, and one in Belfast for the 6 north-eastern counties of [[ulster|Ulster]]. This partition, intended as a solution, was overtaken by the War of Independence (1919–1921) and the Anglo-Irish Treaty, which established the Irish Free State as a dominion.

## Legacy

Home Rule was never implemented in the form its proponents envisioned. The failure to achieve constitutional self-government peacefully contributed to the rise of more militant nationalist movements, culminating in the Easter Rising of 1916 and the War of Independence. The partition of Ireland, originally conceived as a temporary measure under Home Rule, became permanent with the establishment of Northern Ireland.

The Home Rule movement remains a pivotal chapter in Irish history—the last and most sustained attempt to achieve Irish self-government within the constitutional framework of the United Kingdom before the revolutionary alternative prevailed.
