## Overview

Owen Roe O'Neill (c. 1585–1649), nephew of the great [[hugh-oneill|Hugh O'Neil]]l, [[hugh-oneill|Earl of Tyrone]], was the most skilled Irish military commander of the Confederate Wars. After decades of service in the Spanish army, he returned to Ireland in 1642 to lead the [[ulster|Ulster]] forces of the Irish Catholic Confederation. His greatest victory came at the Battle of Benburb in 1646, where his army destroyed a superior Scottish Covenanter force. (064-173) (066-184)

Unlike many of his contemporaries, Owen Roe was a professional soldier trained in the continental school of warfare. His tactics at Benburb demonstrated a mastery of combined arms and terrain that was rare in the irregular warfare typical of Ireland at the time. The King of France recognized his abilities, and his return to Ireland was eagerly awaited by the Confederate leaders. (066-184) He died in 1649, shortly before the arrival of [[oliver-cromwell|Oliver Cromwell]]. (067-204)

## Biography

### Continental Service

Owen Roe O'Neill left Ireland as a young man in the Flight of the Earls of 1607, accompanying his uncle [[hugh-oneill|Hugh O'Neil]]l into continental exile. He entered Spanish military service and rose through the ranks over three decades of warfare in the Low Countries. By the time the Irish Rebellion of 1641 broke out, he had become a seasoned commander with a reputation that preceded him. "Maguire, Owen MacWard," and other exiles in the Spanish service kept him informed of events at home. (064-173)

### Return to Ireland

In July 1642, "Colonel Owen Roe O'Neil arrived off Donegal" with a small force of veteran officers and supplies, landing in the north to take command of the [[ulster|Ulster]] Irish forces. (068-269) The situation he found was chaotic—the rising of 1641 had degenerated into a sectarian war, and the Scottish Covenanters had landed a large army in [[ulster|Ulster]]. The Irish forces in [[ulster|Ulster]], described as "under the command of Colonel Owen Roe O'Neil," urgently needed the discipline he brought. (066-184) O'Neill immediately set about organizing the irregular Irish forces into a disciplined field army.

### Confederate Command

The Irish Catholic Confederation, formed at Kilkenny in 1642, appointed "Owen Roe O'Neil to the command" of the [[ulster|Ulster]] army, recognizing his superior experience and his lineage as nephew of the great [[hugh-oneill|Earl of Tyrone]]. (067-204) O'Neill found himself caught between the military demands of the Confederacy and the political factionalism that plagued its councils. The Old English faction, led by the Supreme Council, often pursued policies at odds with O'Neill's insistence on full Catholic emancipation and the restoration of Gaelic lands.

### The Battle of Benburb

O'Neill's masterpiece was the Battle of Benburb, fought on 5 June 1646. Marching south from Charlemont, he intercepted a Scottish Covenanter army under Major-General Robert Monro that was advancing to join forces with English Parliamentarian allies. "This legion, Owen Roe O'Neil, nephew of Tyrone, led" with tactical brilliance—using the terrain of the River Blackwater to channel the enemy advance and launching a decisive counter-attack. (066-154)

The result was a complete victory: over three thousand Scots were killed or captured, and the Covenanter threat to [[ulster|Ulster]] was broken. Benburb was the most significant Irish military victory of the entire Confederate period and a demonstration of what a properly trained and led Irish army could achieve.

## Legacy

### The Lost Opportunity

Benburb should have been the prelude to a decisive Confederate advance. Instead, O'Neill's army was recalled to Kilkenny by the faction-ridden Supreme Council, and the fruits of victory were squandered in political intrigue. The failure to follow up Benburb remains one of the great "what-ifs" of Irish history.

Owen Roe died in November 1649 at Cloughoughter Castle, just as [[oliver-cromwell|Oliver Cromwell]] was landing at [[dublin|Dublin]] with his New Model Army. Many historians have speculated on how differently the Cromwellian conquest might have gone had the one Irish general capable of defeating a professional army in open battle still been alive to oppose it.

### Military Reputation

Owen Roe O'Neill stands as the most accomplished Irish soldier of his generation. His reputation among both allies and enemies was that of a commander of singular ability—a reputation that had first been forged "afterwards of Owen Roe O'Neil" in the Spanish service. (064-173) His death left the Irish Confederacy without its ablest military leader at the moment of its greatest peril, and his memory would inspire Irish soldiers in continental service for generations to come.
