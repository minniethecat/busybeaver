CHAPTER IV.

[038-001]BATTLE OF FAUGHARD AND DEATH OF KING EDWARD BRUCE—CONSEQUENCES OF HIS

[038-002]INVASION—EXTINCTION OF THE EARLDOM OF ULSTER—IRISH OPINION OF EDWARD

[038-003]BRUCE.

[038-004]It is too commonly the fashion, as well with historians as with others,

[038-005]to glorify the successful and censure severely the unfortunate. No such

[038-006]feeling actuates us in speaking of the character of Edward Bruce, King

[038-007]of Ireland. That he was as gallant a knight as any in that age of

[038-008]gallantry, we know; that he could confront the gloomiest aspect of

[038-009]adversity with cheerfulness, we also know. But the united testimony,

[038-010]both of history and tradition, in his own country, so tenacious of its

[038-011]anecdotical treasures, describes him as rash, headstrong, and

[038-012]intractable, beyond all the captains of his time. And in strict

[038-013]conformity with this character is the closing scene of his Irish

[038-014]career.

[038-015]The harvest had again failed in 1317, and enforced a melancholy sort of

[038-016]truce between all the belligerents. The scarcity was not confined to

[038-017]Ireland, but had severely afflicted England and Scotland, compelling

[038-018]their rulers to bestow a momentary attention on the then abject class,

[038-019]the tillers of the soil. But the summer of 1318 brightened above more

[038-020]prosperous fields, from which no sooner had each party snatched or

[038-021]purchased his share of the produce, than the war-note again resounded

[038-022]through all the four Provinces. On the part of the Anglo-Irish, John de

[038-023]Bermingham was confirmed as Commander-in-Chief, and departed from

[038-024]Dublin with, according to the chronicles of the Pale, but 2,000 chosen

[038-025]troops, while the Scottish biographer of the Bruces gives him "20,000

[038-026]trapped horse." The latter may certainly be considered an exaggerated

[038-027]account, and the former must be equally incorrect. Judged by the other

[038-028]armaments of that period, from the fact that the Normans of Meath,

[038-029]under Sir Miles de Verdon and Sir Richard Tuit, were in his ranks, and

[038-030]that he then held the rank of Commander-in-Chief of all the English

[038-031]forces in Ireland, it is incredible that de Bermingham should have

[038-032]crossed the Boyne with less than eight or ten thousand men. Whatever

[038-033]the number may have been, Bruce resolved to risk the issue of battle

[038-034]contrary to the advice of all his officers, and without awaiting the

[038-035]reinforcements hourly expected from Scotland, and which shortly after

[038-036]the engagement did arrive. The native chiefs of Ulster, whose counsel

[038-037]was also to avoid a pitched battle, seeing their opinions so lightly

[038-038]valued, are said to have withdrawn from Dundalk. There remained with

[038-039]the iron-headed King the Lords Moubray, de Soulis, and Stewart, with

[038-040]the three brothers of the latter; MacRory, lord of the Isles, and

[038-041]McDonald, chief of his clan. The neighbourhood of Dundalk, the scene of

[038-042]his triumphs and coronation, was to be the scene of this last act of

[038-043]Bruce's chivalrous and stormy career.

[038-044]On the 14th of October, 1318, at the hill of Faughard, within a couple

[038-045]of miles of Dundalk, the advance guard of the hostile armies came into

[038-046]the presence of each other, and made ready for battle. Roland de Jorse,

[038-047]the foreign Archbishop of Armagh—who had not been able to take

[038-048]possession of his see, though appointed to it seven years

[038-049]before—accompanied the Anglo-Irish, and moving through their ranks,

[038-050]gave his benediction to their banners. But the impetuosity of Bruce

[038-051]gave little time for preparation. At the head of the vanguard, without

[038-052]waiting for the whole of his company to come up, he charged the enemy

[038-053]with impetuosity. The action became general, and the skill of de

[038-054]Bermingham as a leader was again demonstrated. An incident common to

[038-055]the warfare of that age was, however, the immediate cause of the

[038-056]victory. Master John de Maupas, a burgher of Dundalk, believing that

[038-057]the death of the Scottish leader would be the signal for the retreat of

[038-058]his followers, disguised as a jester or fool, sought him throughout the

[038-059]field. One of the royal esquires, named Gilbert Harper, wearing the

[038-060]surcoat of his master, was mistaken for him, and slain; but the true

[038-061]leader was at length found by de Maupas, and struck down with the blow

[038-062]of a leaden plummet or slung-shot. After the battle, when the field was

[038-063]searched for his body, it was found under that of de Maupas, who had

[038-064]bravely yielded up life for life. The Hiberno-Scottish forces dispersed

[038-065]in dismay, and when King Robert of Scotland landed a day or two

[038-066]afterwards, he was met by the fugitive men of Carrick, under their

[038-067]leader Thompson, who informed him of his brother's fate. He returned at

[038-068]once into his own country, carrying off the few Scottish survivors. The

[038-069]head of the impetuous Edward was sent to London; but the body was

[038-070]interred in the churchyard of Faughard, where, within living memory, a

[038-071]tall pillar stone was pointed out by every peasant of the neighbourhood

[038-072]as marking the grave of "King Bruce."

[038-073]The fortunes of the principal actors, native and Norman, in the

[038-074]invasion of Edward Bruce, may be briefly recounted before closing this

[038-075]book of our history, John de Bermingham, created for his former victory

[038-076]Baron of Athenry, had now the Earldom of Louth conferred on him with a

[038-077]royal pension. He promptly followed up his blow at Faughard by

[038-078]expelling Donald O'Neil, the mainspring of the invasion, from Tyrone;

[038-079]but Donald, after a short sojourn among the mountains of Fermanagh,

[038-080]returned during the winter and resumed his lordship, though he never

[038-081]wholly recovered from the losses he had sustained. The new Earl of

[038-082]Louth continued to hold the rank of Commander-in-Chief in Ireland, to

[038-083]which he added in 1322 that of Lord Justice. He was slain in 1329, with

[038-084]some 200 of his personal adherents, in an affair with the natives of

[038-085]his new earldom, at a place called Ballybeagan. He left by a daughter

[038-086]of the Earl of Ulster three daughters; the title was perpetuated in the

[038-087]family of his brothers.

[038-088]In 1319, the Earls of Kildare and Louth, and the Lord Arnold le Poer,

[038-089]were appointed a commission to inquire into all treasons committed in

[038-090]Ireland during Bruce's invasion. Among other outlawries they decreed

[038-091]those of the three de Lacys, the chiefs of their name, in Meath and

[038-092]Ulster. That illustrious family, however, survived even this last

[038-093]confiscation, and their descendants, several centuries later, were

[038-094]large proprietors in the midland counties.

[038-095]Three years after the battle of Faughard, died Roland de Jorse,

[038-096]Archbishop of Armagh, it was said, of vexations arising out of Bruce's

[038-097]war, and other difficulties which beset him in taking possession of his

[038-098]see. Adam, Bishop of Ferns, was deprived of his revenues for taking

[038-099]part with Bruce, and the Friars Minor of the Franciscan order, were

[038-100]severely censured in a Papal rescript for their zeal on the same side.

[038-101]The great families of Fitzgerald and Butler obtained their earldoms of

[038-102]Kildare, Desmond, and Ormond, out of this dangerous crisis, but the

[038-103]premier earldom of Ulster disappeared from our history soon afterwards.

[038-104]Richard, the Red Earl, having died in the Monastery of Athassil, in

[038-105]1326, was succeeded by his son, William, who, seven years later, in

[038-106]consequence of a family feud, instigated by one of his own female

[038-107]relatives, Gilla de Burgh, wife of Walter de Mandeville, was murdered

[038-108]at the Fords, near Carrickfergus, in the 21st year of his age. His

[038-109]wife, Maud, daughter of Henry Plantagenet, Earl of Lancaster, fled into

[038-110]England with her infant, afterwards married to Lionel, Duke of

[038-111]Clarence, son of King Edward III., who thus became personally

[038-112]interested in the system which he initiated by the odious Statute of

[038-113]Kilkenny. But the misfortunes of the Red Earl's posterity did not end

[038-114]with the murder of his immediate successor. Edmond, his surviving son,

[038-115]five years subsequently, was seized by his cousin, Edmond, the son of

[038-116]William, and drowned in Lough Mask, with a stone about his neck. The

[038-117]posterity of William de Burgh then assumed the name of McWilliam, and

[038-118]renounced the laws, language, and allegiance of England. Profiting by

[038-119]their dissensions, Turlogh O'Conor, towards the middle of the century,

[038-120]asserted supremacy over them, thus practising against the descendants

[038-121]the same policy which the first de Burghs had successfully employed

[038-122]among the sons of Roderick.

[038-123]We must mention here a final consequence of Edward Bruce's invasion

[038-124]seldom referred to,—namely, the character of the treaty between

[038-125]Scotland and England, concluded and signed at Edinburgh, on St.

[038-126]Patrick's Day, 1328. By this treaty, after arranging an intermarriage

[038-127]between the royal families, it was stipulated in the event of a

[038-128]rebellion against Scotland, in Skye, Man, or the Islands, or against

[038-129]England, in Ireland, that the several Kings would not abet or assist

[038-130]each other's rebel subjects. Remembering this article, we know not what

[038-131]to make of the entry in our own Annals, which states that Robert Bruce

[038-132]landed at Carrickfergus in the same year, 1328, "and sent word to the

[038-133]Justiciary and Council, that he came to make peace between Ireland and

[038-134]Scotland, and that he would meet them at Green Castle; but that the

[038-135]latter failing to meet him, he returned to Scotland." This, however, we

[038-136]know: high hopes were entertained, and immense sacrifices were made,

[038-137]for Edward Bruce, but were made in vain. His proverbial rashness in

[038-138]battle, with his total disregard of the opinion of the country into

[038-139]which he came, alienated from him those who were at first disposed to

[038-140]receive him with enthusiasm. It may be an instructive lesson to such as

[038-141]look to foreign leaders and foreign forces for the means of national

[038-142]deliverance to read the terms in which the native Annalists record the

[038-143]defeat and death of Edward Bruce: "No achievement had been performed in

[038-144]Ireland, for a long time," say the Four Masters, "from which greater

[038-145]benefit had accrued to the country than from this." "There was not a

[038-146]better deed done in Ireland since the banishment of the Formorians,"

[038-147]says the Annalist of Clonmacnoise! So detested may a foreign liberating

[038-148]chief become, who outrages the feelings and usages of the people he

[038-149]pretends, or really means to emancipate!
