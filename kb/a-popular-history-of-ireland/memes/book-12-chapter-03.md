CHAPTER III.

[109-001]ADMINISTRATION OF THE DUKE OF RICHMOND (1807 TO 1813).

[109-002]Charles, fourth Duke of Richmond, succeeded the Duke of Bedford, as

[109-003]Viceroy, in April, 1807, with Lord Manners as Lord Chancellor, John

[109-004]Foster, Chancellor of the Exchequer—for the separate exchequer of

[109-005]Ireland continued to exist till 1820—and Sir Arthur Wellesley as Chief

[109-006]Secretary. Of these names, the two last were already familiar to their

[109-007]countrymen, in connection with the history of their own Parliament; but

[109-008]the new Chief Secretary had lately returned home covered with Indian

[109-009]laurels, and full of the promise of other honours and victories to

[109-010]come.

[109-011]The spirit of this administration was repressive, anti-Catholic and

[109-012]high Tory. To maintain and strengthen British power, to keep the

[109-013]Catholics quiet, to get possession of the Irish representation and

[109-014]convert it into a means of support for the Tory party in England, these

[109-015]were the leading objects of the seven years' administration of the Duke

[109-016]of Richmond. Long afterwards, when the Chief Secretary of 1807 had

[109-017]become "the most high, mighty and noble prince," whom all England and

[109-018]nearly all Europe delighted to honour, he defended the Irish

[109-019]administration of which he had formed a part, for its habitual use of

[109-020]corrupt means and influence, in arguments which do more credit to his

[109-021]frankness than his morality. He had "to turn the moral weakness of

[109-022]individuals to good account," such was his argument. He stoutly denied

[109-023]that "the whole nation is, or ever was corrupt;" but as "almost every

[109-024]man of mark has his price," the Chief Secretary was obliged to use

[109-025]corrupt influences "to command a majority in favour of order;" however

[109-026]the particular kinds of influence employed might go against his grain,

[109-027]he had, as he contended, no other alternative but to employ them.

[109-028]With the exception of a two months' campaign in Denmark—July to

[109-029]September, 1807—Sir Arthur Wellesley continued to fill the office of

[109-030]Chief Secretary, until his departure for the Peninsula, in July, 1808.

[109-031]Even then he was expressly requested to retain the nominal office, with

[109-032]power to appoint a deputy, and receive meanwhile the very handsome

[109-033]salary of 8,000 pounds sterling a year. In the wonderful military

[109-034]events, in which during the next seven years Sir Arthur was to play a

[109-035]leading part, the comparatively unimportant particulars of his Irish

[109-036]Secretariate have been long since forgotten. We have already described

[109-037]the general spirit of that administration: it is only just to add, that

[109-038]the dispassionate and resolute secretary, though he never shrank from

[109-039]his share of the jobbery done daily at the Castle, repressed with as

[109-040]much firmness the over-zeal of those he calls "red-hot Protestants," as

[109-041]he showed in resisting, at that period, what he considered the

[109-042]unconstitutional pretensions of the Catholics. An instance of the

[109-043]impartiality to which he was capable of rising, when influenced by

[109-044]partisans or religious prejudices, is afforded by his letter dissuading

[109-045]the Wexford yeomanry from celebrating the anniversary of the battle of

[109-046]Vinegar Hill. He regarded such a celebration as certain "to exasperate

[109-047]party spirit," and "to hurt the feelings of others;" he, therefore, in

[109-048]the name of the Lord-Lieutenant, strongly discouraged it, and the

[109-049]intention was accordingly abandoned. It is to be regretted that the

[109-050]same judicious rule was not at the same time enforced by government as

[109-051]to the celebration of the much more obsolete and much more invidious

[109-052]anniversaries of Aughrim and the Boyne.

[109-053]The general election which followed the death of Fox, in November,

[109-054]1806, was the first great trial of political strength under the Union.

[109-055]As was right and proper, Mr. Grattan, no longer indebted for a seat to

[109-056]an English patron, however liberal, was returned at the head of the

[109-057]poll for the city of Dublin. His associate, however, the banker, La

[109-058]Touche, was defeated; the second member elect being Mr. Robert Shaw,

[109-059]the Orange candidate. The Catholic electors to a man, under the

[109-060]vigorous prompting of John Keogh and his friends, polled their votes

[109-061]for their Protestant advocate; they did more, they subscribed the sum

[109-062]of 4,000 pounds sterling to pay the expenses of the contest, but this

[109-063]sum Mrs. Grattan induced the treasurer to return to the subscribers.

[109-064]Ever watchful for her husband's honour, that admirable woman, as ardent

[109-065]a patriot as himself, refused the generous tender of the Catholics of

[109-066]Dublin. Although his several elections had cost Mr. Grattan above

[109-067]54,000 pounds—more than the whole national grant of 1782—she would not,

[109-068]in this case, that any one else should bear the cost of his last

[109-069]triumph in the widowed capital of his own country.

[109-070]The great issue tried in this election of 1807, in those of 1812, 1818,

[109-071]and 1826, was still the Catholic question. All other Irish, and most

[109-072]other imperial domestic questions were subordinate to this. In one

[109-073]shape or another, it came up in every session of Parliament. It entered

[109-074]into the calculations of every statesman of every party; it continued

[109-075]to make and unmake cabinets; in the press and in every society, it was

[109-076]the principal topic of discussion. While tracing, therefore, its

[109-077]progress, from year to year, we do but follow the main stream of

[109-078]national history; all other branches come back again to this centre, or

[109-079]exhaust themselves in secondary and forgotten results.

[109-080]The Catholics themselves, deprived in Ireland of a Parliament on which

[109-081]they could act directly, were driven more and more into permanent

[109-082]association, as the only means of operating a change in the Imperial

[109-083]legislature. The value of a legal, popular, systematic, and continuous

[109-084]combination of "the people" acting within the law, by means of

[109-085]meetings, resolutions, correspondence, and petitions, was not made

[109-086]suddenly, nor by all the party interested, at one and the same time. On

[109-087]the minds of the more sagacious, however, an impression, favourable to

[109-088]such organized action, grew deeper year by year, and at last settled

[109-089]into a certainty which was justified by success.

[109-090]In May, 1809, the Catholic Committee had been reconstructed, and its

[109-091]numbers enlarged. In a series of resolutions it was agreed that the

[109-092]Catholic lords, the surviving delegates of 1793, the committee which

[109-093]managed the petitions of 1805 and 1807, and such persons "as shall

[109-094]distinctly appear to them to possess the confidence of the Catholic

[109-095]body," do form henceforth the General Committee. It was proposed by

[109-096]O'Connell, to avoid "the Convention Act," "that the noblemen and

[109-097]gentlemen aforesaid are not representatives of the Catholic body, or

[109-098]any portion thereof." The Committee were authorized to collect funds

[109-099]for defraying expenses; a Treasurer was chosen, and a permanent

[109-100]Secretary, Mr. Edward Hay, the historian of the Wexford rebellion—an

[109-101]active and intelligent officer. The new Committee acted with great

[109-102]judgment in 1810, but in 1811 Lord Fingal and his friends projected a

[109-103]General Assembly of the leading Catholics, contrary to the Convention

[109-104]Act, and to the resolution just cited. O'Connell was opposed to this

[109-105]proposition; yet the assembly met, and were dispersed by the

[109-106]authorities. The Chairman, Lord Fingal, and Drs. Sheridan and Kirwan,

[109-107]Secretaries, were arrested. Lord Fingal, however, was not prosecuted,

[109-108]but the Secretaries were, and one of them expiated by two years'

[109-109]imprisonment his violation of the act. To get rid of the very pretext

[109-110]of illegality, the Catholic Committee dissolved, but only to reappear

[109-111]under a less vulnerable form, as "the Catholic Board."

[109-112]It is from the year 1810 that we must date the rise, among the

[109-113]Catholics themselves, of a distinctive line of policy, suited to the

[109-114]circumstances of the present century, and the first appearance of a

[109-115]group of public men, capable of maintaining and enforcing that policy.

[109-116]Not that the ancient leaders of that body were found deficient, in

[109-117]former times, either in foresight or determination; but new times

[109-118]called for new men; the Irish Catholics were now to seek their

[109-119]emancipation from the imperial government; new tactics and new

[109-120]combinations were necessary to success; and, in brief, instead of being

[109-121]liberated from their bonds at the good will and pleasure of benevolent

[109-122]Protestants, it was now to be tested whether they were capable of

[109-123]contributing to their own emancipation,—whether they were willing and

[109-124]able to assist their friends and to punish their enemies.

[109-125]Though the Irish Catholics could not legally meet in convention any

[109-126]more than their Protestant fellow-countrymen, there was nothing to

[109-127]prevent them assembling voluntarily, from every part of the kingdom,

[109-128]without claim to delegation. With whom the happy idea of "the aggregate

[109-129]meetings" originated is not certainly known, but to O'Connell and the

[109-130]younger set of leading spirits this was a machinery capable of being

[109-131]worked with good effect. No longer confined to a select Committee,

[109-132]composed mainly of a few aged and cautious, though distinguished

[109-133]persons, the fearless "agitators," as they now began to be called,

[109-134]stood face to face with the body of the people themselves. The disused

[109-135]theatre in Fishamble Street was their habitual place of meeting in

[109-136]Dublin, and there, in 1811 and 1812, the orators met to criticise the

[109-137]conduct of the Duke of Richmond—to denounce Mr. Wellesley Pole—to

[109-138]attack Secretaries of State and Prime Ministers—to return thanks to

[109-139]Lords Grey and Grenville for refusing to give the unconstitutional

[109-140]anti-Catholic pledge required by the King, and to memorial the Prince

[109-141]Regent. From those meetings, especially in the year 1812, the

[109-142]leadership of O'Connell must be dated. After seven years of wearisome

[109-143]probation, after enduring seven years the envy and the calumny of many

[109-144]who, as they were his fellow-labourers, should have been his friends;

[109-145]after demonstrating for seven years that his judgment and his courage

[109-146]were equal to his eloquence, the successful Kerry barrister, then in

[109-147]his thirty-seventh year, was at length generally recognized as "the

[109-148]counsellor" of his co-religionists—as the veritable "Man of the

[109-149]People." Dangers, delays and difficulties lay thick and dark in the

[109-150]future, but from the year, when in Dublin, Cork and Limerick, the voice

[109-151]of the famous advocate was recognized as the voice of the Catholics of

[109-152]Ireland, their cause was taken out of the category of merely

[109-153]ministerial measures, and exhibited in its true light as a great

[109-154]national contest, entered into by the people themselves for complete

[109-155]civil and religious freedom.

[109-157]Sir Arthur Wellesley had been succeeded in 1810 in the Secretaryship by

[109-158]his brother, Mr. Wellesley Pole, who chiefly signalized his

[109-159]administration by a circular against conventions, and the prosecution

[109-160]of Sheridan and Kirwan, in 1811. He was in turn succeeded by a much

[109-161]more able and memorable person—_Mr_., afterwards Sir Robert Peel. The

[109-162]names of Peel and Wellington come thus into juxtaposition in Irish

[109-163]politics in 1812, as they will be found in juxtaposition on the same

[109-164]subject twenty and thirty years later.

[109-165]Early in the session of 1812, Mr. Perceval, the Premier, had been

[109-166]assassinated in the lobby of the House of Commons, by Bellingham, and a

[109-167]new political crisis was precipitated on the country. In the government

[109-168]which followed, Lord Liverpool became the chief, with Castlereagh and

[109-169]Canning as members of his administration. In the general election which

[109-170]followed, Mr. Grattan was again returned for Dublin, and Mr. Plunkett

[109-171]was elected for Trinity College, but Mr. Curran was defeated at Newry,

[109-172]and Mr. Christopher Hely Hutchinson, the liberal candidate, at Cork.

[109-173]Upon the whole, however, the result was favourable to the Catholic

[109-174]cause, and the question was certain to have several additional Irish

[109-175]supporters in the new House of Commons.

[109-176]In the administrative changes that followed, Mr. Peel, though only in

[109-177]his twenty-fourth year, was appointed to the important post of Chief

[109-178]Secretary. The son of the first baronet of the name—this youthful

[109-179]statesman had first been elected for Cashel, almost as soon as he came

[109-180]of age, in 1809. He continued Chief Secretary for six years, from the

[109-181]twenty-fourth to the thirtieth year of his age. He distinguished

[109-182]himself in the House of Commons almost as soon as he entered it, and

[109-183]the predictions of his future premiership were not, even then, confined

[109-184]to members of his own family. No English statesman, since the death of

[109-185]William Pitt, has wielded so great a power in Irish affairs as Sir

[109-186]Robert Peel, and it is, therefore, important to consider, under what

[109-187]influence, and by what maxims he regulated his public conduct during

[109-188]the time he filled the most important administrative office in that

[109-189]country.

[109-190]Sir Robert Peel brought to the Irish government, notwithstanding his

[109-191]Oxford education and the advantages of foreign travel which he had

[109-192]enjoyed, prejudices the most illiberal, on the subject of all others on

[109-193]which a statesman should be most free from prejudice—religion. An

[109-194]anti-Catholic of the school of Mr. Perceval and Lord Eldon, he at once

[109-195]constituted himself the principal opponent of Grattan's annual motion

[109-196]in favour of Catholic Emancipation. That older men, born in the evil

[109-197]time, should be bigots and defenders of the Penal Code, was hardly

[109-198]wonderful, but a young statesman, exhibiting at that late day, such

[109-199]studied and active hostility to so large a body of his fellow subjects,

[109-200]naturally drew upon his head the execrations of all those whose

[109-201]enfranchisement he so stubbornly resisted. Even his great abilities

[109-202]were most absurdly denied, under this passionate feeling of wrong and

[109-203]injustice. His Constabulary and his Stipendiary Magistracy were

[109-204]resisted, ridiculed, and denounced, as outrages on the liberty of the

[109-205]subject, and assaults on the independence of the bench. The term

[109-206]_Peeler_ became synonymous with spy, informer, and traitor, and the

[109-207]Chief Secretary was detested not only for the illiberal sentiments he

[109-208]had expressed, but for the machinery of order he had established. After

[109-209]half a century's experience, we may safely say, that the Irish

[109-210]Constabulary have shown themselves to be a most valuable police, and as

[109-211]little deserving of popular ill-will as any such body can ever expect

[109-212]to be, but they were judged very differently during the Secretaryship

[109-213]of their founder; for, at that time, being new and intrusive, they may,

[109-214]no doubt, have deserved many of the hard and bitter things which were

[109-215]generally said of them.

[109-216]The first session of the new Parliament in the year 1813—the last of

[109-217]the Duke of Richmond's Viceroyalty—was remarkable for the most

[109-218]important debate which had yet arisen on the Catholic question. In the

[109-219]previous year, a motion of Canning's, in favour of "a final and

[109-220]conciliatory adjustment," which was carried by an unexpected majority

[109-221]of 235 to 106, encouraged Grattan to prepare a detailed Emancipation

[109-222]Bill, instead of making his usual annual motion of referring the

[109-223]Catholic petitions to the consideration of the Committee. This bill

[109-224]recited the establishment of the Protestant succession to the crown,

[109-225]and the establishment of the Protestant religion in the State. It then

[109-226]proceeded to provide that Roman Catholics might sit and vote in

[109-227]Parliament; might hold all offices, civil and military, except the

[109-228]offices of Chancellor or Keeper of the Great Seal in England, or

[109-229]Lord-Lieutenant, Lord Deputy, or Chancellor of Ireland; another section

[109-230]threw open to Roman Catholics all lay corporations, while a proviso

[109-231]excluded them either from holding or bestowing benefices in the

[109-232]Established Church. Such was the Emancipation Act of 1813, proposed by

[109-233]Grattan; an act far less comprehensive than that introduced by the same

[109-234]statesman in 1795, into the Parliament of Ireland, but still, in many

[109-235]of its provisions, a long stride in advance.

[109-236]Restricted and conditioned as this measure was, it still did not meet

[109-237]the objections of the opponents of the question, in giving the crown a

[109-238]Veto in the appointment of the bishops. Sir John Hippesley's pernicious

[109-239]suggestion—reviving a very old traditional policy—was embodied by

[109-240]Canning in one set of amendments, and by Castlereagh in another.

[109-241]Canning's amendments, as summarised by the eminent Catholic jurist,

[109-242]Charles Butler, were to this effect:—

[109-243]"He first appointed a certain number of Commissioners, who were to

[109-244]profess the Catholic religion, and to be lay peers of Great Britain or

[109-245]Scotland, possessing a freehold estate of one thousand pounds a year;

[109-246]to be filled up, from time to time, by his Majesty, his heirs, or

[109-247]successors. The Commissioners were to take an oath for the faithful

[109-248]discharge of their office, and the observance of secrecy in all matters

[109-249]not thereby required to be disclosed, with power to appoint a Secretary

[109-250]with salary (proposed to be five hundred pounds a year), payable out of

[109-251]the consolidated fund. The Secretary was to take an oath similar to

[109-252]that of the Commissioners.

[109-253]"It was then provided, that every person elected to the discharge of

[109-254]Roman Catholic episcopal functions in Great Britain or Scotland should,

[109-255]previously to the discharge of his office, notify his then election to

[109-256]the Secretary; that the Secretary should notify it to the

[109-257]Commissioners, and they to the Privy Council, with a certificate 'that

[109-258]they did not know or believe anything of the person nominated, which

[109-259]tended to impeach his loyalty or peaceable conduct;' unless they had

[109-260]knowledge of the contrary, in which case they should refuse their

[109-261]certificate. Persons obtaining such a certificate were rendered capable

[109-262]of exercising episcopal functions within the United Kingdom; if they

[109-263]exercised them without a certificate, they were to be considered guilty

[109-264]of a misdemeanor, and liable to be sent out of the kingdom.

[109-265]"Similar provisions respecting Ireland were then introduced."

[109-266]"The second set of clauses," says Mr. Butler, "was suggested by Lord

[109-267]Castlereagh, and provided that the Commissioners under the preceding

[109-268]clauses—with the addition, as to Great Britain, of the Lord Chancellor,

[109-269]or Lord Keeper, or first Commissioner of the Great Seal for the time

[109-270]being, and of one of his Majesty's principal Secretaries of State,

[109-271]being a Protestant, or such other Protestant member of his Privy

[109-272]Council as his Majesty should appoint—and with a similar addition in

[109-273]respect to Ireland—and with the further addition, as to Great Britain,

[109-274]of the person then exercising episcopal functions among the Catholics

[109-275]in London—and, in respect to Ireland, of the titular Roman Catholic

[109-276]Archbishops of Armagh and Dublin,—should be Commissioners for the

[109-277]purposes thereinafter mentioned.

[109-278]"The Commissioners thus appointed were to take an oath for the

[109-279]discharge of their office, and observance of secrecy, similar to the

[109-280]former, and employ the same Secretary, and three of them were to form a

[109-281]quorum.

[109-282]"The bill then provided, that subjects of his Majesty, receiving any

[109-283]bull, dispensation, or other instrument, from the See of Rome, or any

[109-284]person in foreign parts, acting under the authority of that See,

[109-285]should, within six weeks, send a copy of it, signed with his name, to

[109-286]the Secretary of the Commissioners, who should transmit the same to

[109-287]them.

[109-288]"But with a proviso, that if the person receiving the same should

[109-289]deliver to the Secretary of the Commission, within the time before

[109-290]prescribed, a writing under his hand, certifying the fact of his having

[109-291]received such a bull, dispensation, or other instrument, and

[109-292]accompanying his certificate with an oath, declaring that 'it related,

[109-293]wholly and exclusively, to spiritual concerns, and that it did not

[109-294]contain, or refer to, any matter or thing which did or could, directly

[109-295]or indirectly, affect or interfere with the duty and allegiance which

[109-296]he owed to his Majesty's sacred person and government, or with the

[109-297]temporal, civil, or social rights, properties, or duties of any other

[109-298]of his Majesty's subjects, then the Commissioners were, in their

[109-299]discretion, to receive such certificate and oath, in lieu of the copy

[109-300]of the bull, dispensation, or other instrument.

[109-301]"Persons conforming to these provisions were to be exempted from all

[109-302]pains and penalties, to which they would be liable under the existing

[109-303]statutes; otherwise, they were to be deemed guilty of a high

[109-304]misdemeanor; and in lieu of the pains and penalties, under the former

[109-305]statutes, be liable to be sent out of the kingdom.

[109-306]"The third set of clauses provided that, within a time to be specified,

[109-307]the Commissioners were to meet and appoint their Secretary, and give

[109-308]notice of it to his Majesty's principal Secretaries of State in Great

[109-309]Britain and Ireland; and the provisions of the act were to be in force

[109-310]from that time."

[109-311]On the second reading, in May, the Committee of Parliament, on motion

[109-312]of the Speaker, then on the floor, struck out the clause enabling

[109-313]Catholics "to sit and vote in either House of Parliament," by a

[109-314]majority of four votes: 251 against 247. Mr. Ponsonby immediately rose,

[109-315]and, observing that, as "the bill without the clause," was unworthy

[109-316]both of the Catholics and its authors, he moved the chairman do leave

[109-317]the chair. The committee rose, without a division, and the Emancipation

[109-318]Bill of 1813 was abandoned.

[109-319]Unhappily, the contest in relation to the Veto, which had originated in

[109-320]the House of Commons, was extended to the Catholic body at large.

[109-321]Several of the noblemen, members of the board, were not averse to

[109-322]granting some such power as was claimed to the crown; some of the

[109-323]professional class, more anxious to be emancipated than particular as

[109-324]to the means, favoured the same view. The bishops at the time of the

[109-325]Union, were known to have entertained the idea, and Sir John Hippesley

[109-326]had published their letters, which certainly did not discourage his

[109-327]proposal. But the second order of the clergy, the immense majority of

[109-328]the laity, and all the new prelates, called to preside over vacant

[109-329]sees, in the first decade of the century, were strongly opposed to any

[109-330]such connexion with the head of the State. Of this party, Mr. O'Connell

[109-331]was the uncompromising organ, and, perhaps, it was his course on this

[109-332]very subject of the Veto, more than anything else, which established

[109-333]his pretensions to be considered the leader of the Catholic body. Under

[109-334]the prompting of the majority, the Catholic prelates met and passed a

[109-335]resolution declaring that they could not accept the bill of 1813 as a

[109-336]satisfactory settlement. This resolution they formally communicated to

[109-337]the Catholic Board, who voted them, on O'Connell's motion, enthusiastic

[109-338]thanks. The minority of the Board were silent rather than satisfied,

[109-339]and their dissatisfaction was shown rather by their absence from the

[109-340]Board meetings than by open opposition.

[109-341]Mr. O'Connell's position, from this period forward, may be best

[109-342]understood from the tone in which he was spoken of in the debates of

[109-343]Parliament. At the beginning of the session of 1815, we find the Chief

[109-344]Secretary (Mr. Peel) stating that he "possesses more influence than any

[109-345]other person" with the Irish Catholics, and that no meeting of that

[109-346]body was considered complete unless a vote of thanks to Mr. O'Connell

[109-347]was among the resolutions.
