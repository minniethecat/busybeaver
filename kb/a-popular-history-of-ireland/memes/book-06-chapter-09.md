CHAPTER IX.

[046-001]STATE OF IRISH AND ANGLO-IRISH SOCIETY DURING THE FOURTEENTH AND

[046-002]FIFTEENTH CENTURIES.

[046-003]The main peculiarities of social life among the Irish and Anglo-Irish

[046-004]during the fourteenth and fifteenth centuries are still visible to us.

[046-005]Of the drudges of the earth, as in all other histories, we see or hear

[046-006]little or nothing, but of those orders of men of whom the historic muse

[046-007]takes count, such as bards, rulers, builders, and religious, there is

[046-008]much information to be found scattered up and down our annals, which,

[046-009]if properly put together and clearly interpreted, may afford us a

[046-010]tolerably clear view of the men and their times.

[046-011]The love of learning, always strong in this race of men and women,

[046-012]revived in full force with their exemption from the immediate pressure

[046-013]of foreign invasion. The person of Bard and Brehon was still held

[046-014]inviolable; to the malediction of the Bard of Usnagh was attributed the

[046-015]sudden death of the Deputy, Sir John Stanley; to the murder of the

[046-016]Brehon McEgan is traced all the misfortunes which befell the sons of

[046-017]Irial O'Farrell. To receive the poet graciously, to seat him in the

[046-018]place of honour at the feast, to listen to him with reverence, and to

[046-019]reward him munificently, were considered duties incumbent on the

[046-020]princes of the land. And these duties, to do them justice, they never

[046-021]neglected. One of the O'Neils is specially praised for having given

[046-022]more gifts to poets, and having "a larger collection of poems" than any

[046-023]other man of his age. In the struggle between O'Donnell and O'Conor for

[046-024]the northern corner of Sligo, we find mention made of books

[046-025]accidentally burned in "the house of the manuscripts," in Lough Gill.

[046-026]Among the spoils carried off by O'Donnell, on another occasion, were

[046-027]two famous books—one of which, the Leahar Gear (Short Book), he

[046-028]afterwards paid back, as part of the ransom for the release of his

[046-029]friend, O'Doherty.

[046-030]The Bards and Ollams, though more dependent on their Princes than we

[046-031]have seen them in their early palmy days, had yet ample hereditary

[046-032]estates in every principality and lordship. If natural posterity

[046-033]failed, the incumbent was free to adopt some capable person as his

[046-034]heir. It was in this way the family of O'Clery, originally of Tyrawley,

[046-035]came to settle in Tyrconnell, towards the end of the fourteenth

[046-036]century. At that time O'Sgingin, chief Ollam to O'Donnell, offered his

[046-037]daughter in marriage to Cormac O'Clery, a young professor of both laws,

[046-038]in the monastery near Ballyshannon, on condition that the first male

[046-039]child born of the marriage should be brought up to his own profession.

[046-040]This was readily agreed to, and from this auspicious marriage descended

[046-041]the famous family, which produced three of the Four Masters of Donegal.

[046-042]The virtue of hospitality was, of all others, that which the old Irish

[046-043]of every degree in rank and wealth most cheerfully practised. In many

[046-044]cases it degenerated into extravagance and prodigality. But in general

[046-045]it is presented to us in so winning a garb that our objections on the

[046-046]score of prudence vanish before it. When we read of the freeness of

[046-047]heart of Henry Avery O'Neil, who granted all manner of things "that

[046-048]came into his hands," to all manner of men, we pause and doubt whether

[046-049]such a virtue in such excess may not lean towards vice. But when we

[046-050]hear of a powerful lord, like William O'Kelly of Galway, entertaining

[046-051]throughout the Christmas holydays all the poets, musicians, and poor

[046-052]persons who choose to flock to him, or of the pious and splendid

[046-053]Margaret O'Carroll, receiving twice a year in Offally all the Bards of

[046-054]Albyn and Erin, we cannot but envy the professors of the gentle art

[046-055]their good fortune in having lived in such times, and shared in such

[046-056]assemblies. As hospitality was the first of social virtues, so

[046-057]inhospitality was the worst of vices; the unpopularity of a churl

[046-058]descended to his posterity through successive generations.

[046-060]The high estimation in which women were held among the tribes is

[046-061]evident from the particularity with which the historians record their

[046-062]obits and marriages. The maiden name of the wife was never wholly lost

[046-063]in that of her husband, and if her family were of equal standing with

[046-064]his before marriage, she generally retained her full share of authority

[046-065]afterwards. The Margaret O'Carroll already mentioned, a descendant and

[046-066]progenitress of illustrious women, rode privately to Trim, as we are

[046-067]told, with some English prisoners, taken by her husband, O'Conor of

[046-068]Offally, and exchanged them for others of equal worth lying in that

[046-069]fortress; and "this she did," it is added, "without the knowledge of"

[046-070]her husband. This lady was famed not only for her exceeding hospitality

[046-071]and her extreme piety, but for other more unexpected works. Her name is

[046-072]remembered in connection with the erection of bridges and the making of

[046-073]highways, as well as the building of churches, and the presentation of

[046-074]missals and mass-books. And the grace she thus acquired long brought

[046-075]blessings upon her posterity, among whom there never were wanting able

[046-076]men and heroic women while they kept their place in the land. An

[046-077]equally celebrated but less amiable woman was Margaret Fitzgerald,

[046-078]daughter of the eighth Earl of Kildare, and wife of Pierce, eighth Earl

[046-079]of Ormond. "She was," says the Dublin Annalist, "a lady of such port

[046-080]that all the estates of the realm couched to her, so politique that

[046-081]nothing was thought substantially debated without her advice." Her

[046-082]decision of character is preserved in numerous traditions in and around

[046-083]Kilkenny, where she lies buried. Of her is told the story that when

[046-084]exhorted on her death-bed to make restitution of some ill-got lands,

[046-085]and being told the penalty that awaited her if she died impenitent, she

[046-086]answered, "it was better one old woman should burn for eternity than

[046-087]that the Butlers should be curtailed of their estates."

[046-088]The fame of virtuous deeds, of generosity, of peace-making, of

[046-089]fidelity, was in that state of society as easily attainable by women as

[046-090]by men. The Unas, Finolas, Sabias, Lasarinas, were as certain of

[046-091]immortality as the Hughs, Cathals, Donalds and Conors, their sons,

[046-092]brothers, or lovers. Perhaps it would be impossible to find any history

[046-093]of those or of later ages in which women are treated upon a more

[046-094]perfect equality with men, where their virtues and talents entitled

[046-095]them to such consideration.

[046-096]The piety of the age, though it had lost something of the simplicity

[046-097]and fervour of older times, was still conspicuous and edifying. Within

[046-098]the island, the pilgrimage of Saint Patrick's purgatory, the shrine of

[046-099]our Lady of Trim, the virtues of the holy cross of Raphoe, the miracles

[046-100]wrought by the _Baculum Christi_, and other relics of Christ Church,

[046-101]Dublin, were implicitly believed and piously frequented. The long and

[046-102]dangerous journeys to Rome and Jerusalem were frequently taken, but the

[046-103]favourite foreign vow was to Compostella, in Spain. Chiefs, Ladies, and

[046-104]Bards, are almost annually mentioned as having sailed or returned from

[046-105]the city of St. James; generally these pilgrims left in companies, and

[046-106]returned in the same way. The great Jubilee of 1450, so

[046-107]enthusiastically attended from every corner of Christendom, drew vast

[046-108]multitudes from our island to Rome. By those who returned tidings were

[046-109]first brought to Ireland of the capture of Constantinople by the Turks.

[046-110]On receipt of this intelligence, which sent a thrill through the heart

[046-111]of Europe, Tregury, Archbishop of Dublin, proclaimed a fast of three

[046-112]days, and on each day walked in sackcloth, with his clergy, through the

[046-113]streets of the city, to the Cathedral. By many in that age the event

[046-114]was connected with the mystic utterances of the Apocalypse, and the

[046-115]often-apprehended consummation of all Time.

[046-116]Although the Irish were then, as they still are, firm believers in

[046-117]supernatural influence working visibly among men, they do not appear to

[046-118]have ever been slaves to the terrible delusion of witchcraft. Among the

[046-119]Anglo-Irish we find the first instance of that mania which appears in

[046-120]our history, and we believe the only one, if we except the Presbyterian

[046-121]witches of Carrickfergus, in the early part of the eighteenth century.

[046-122]The scene of the ancient delusion was Kilkenny, where Bishop Ledred

[046-123]accused the Lady Alice Kettel, and William her son, of practising black

[046-124]magic, in the year 1327. Sir Roger Outlaw, Prior of Kilmainham, and

[046-125]stepson to Lady Alice, undertook to protect her; but the fearful charge

[046-126]was extended to him also, and he was compelled to enter on his defence.

[046-127]The tribunal appointed to try the charge—one of the main grounds on

[046-128]which the Templars had been suppressed twenty-five years before—was

[046-129]composed of the Dean of St. Patrick's, the Prior of Christ Church, the

[046-130]Abbots of St. Mary's and St. Thomas's, Dublin, Mr. Elias Lawless, and

[046-131]Mr. Peter Willeby, lawyers. Outlaw was acquitted, and Ledred forced to

[046-132]fly for safety to England, of which he was a native. It is pleasant to

[046-133]remember that, although Irish credulity sometimes took shapes absurd

[046-134]and grotesque enough, it never was perverted into diabolical channels,

[046-135]or directed to the barbarities of witch-finding.

[046-136]About the beginning of the fifteenth century we meet with the first

[046-137]mention of the use of Usquebagh, or _Aqua Vitae_, in our Annals. Under

[046-138]the date of 1405 we read that McRannal, or Reynolds, chief of

[046-139]Muntireolais, died of a surfeit of it, about Christmas. A quaint

[046-140]Elizabethan writer thus descants on the properties of that liquor, as

[046-141]he found them, by personal experience: "For the rawness (of the air)

[046-142]they (the Irish) have an excellent remedy by their _Aqua Vitae_,

[046-143]vulgarly called _Usquebagh_, which binds up the belly and drieth up

[046-144]moisture more than our _Aqua Vitae_, yet inflameth not so much."

[046-145]And as the opening of the century may be considered notable for the

[046-146]first mention of _Usquebagh_, so its close is memorable for the first

[046-147]employment of fire-arms. In the year 1489, according to Anglo-Irish

[046-148]Annals, "six hand guns or musquets were sent to the Earl of Kildare out

[046-149]of Germany," which his guard bore while on sentry at Thomas Court—his

[046-150]Dublin residence. But two years earlier (1487) we have positive mention

[046-151]of the employment of guns at the siege of Castlecar, in Leitrim, by

[046-152]Hugh Roe O'Donnell. Great guns were freely used ten years later in the

[046-153]taking of Dungannon and Omagh, and contributed, not a little to the

[046-154]victory of Knock-doe—in 1505. About the same time we begin to hear of

[046-155]their employment by sea in rather a curious connection. A certain

[046-156]French Knight, returning from the pilgrimage of Lough Derg, visiting

[046-157]O'Donnell at Donegal, heard of the anxiety of his entertainer to take a

[046-158]certain Castle which stood by the sea, in Sligo. This Knight promised

[046-159]to send him, on his return to France, "a vessel carrying great guns,"

[046-160]which he accordingly did, and the Castle was in consequence taken.

[046-161]Nevertheless the old Irish, according to their habit, took but slowly

[046-162]to this wonderful invention, though destined to revolutionize the art

[046-163]to which they were naturally predisposed—the art of war.

[046-164]The dwellings of the chiefs, and of the wealthy among the proprietors,

[046-165]near the marches, were chiefly situated amid pallisaded islands, or on

[046-166]promontories naturally moated by lakes. The houses, in those

[046-167]circumstances, were mostly of framework, though the Milesian nobles, in

[046-168]less exposed districts, had castles of stone, after the Norman fashion.

[046-169]The Castle "bawn" was usually enclosed by one or more strong walls, the

[046-170]inner sides of which were lined with barns, stables, and the houses of

[046-171]the retainers. Not unfrequently the thatched roofs of these

[046-172]outbuildings taking fire, compelled the castle to surrender. The Castle

[046-173]"green," whether within or without the walls, was the usual scene of

[046-174]rural sports and athletic games, of which, at all periods, our

[046-175]ancestors were so fond. Of the interior economy of the Milesian rath,

[046-176]or dun, we know less than of the Norman tower, where, before the huge

[046-177]kitchen chimney, the heavy-laden spit was turned by hand, while the

[046-178]dining-hall was adorned with the glitter of the dresser, or by tapestry

[046-179]hangings;—the floors of hall and chambers being strewn with rushes and

[046-180]odorous herbs. We have spoken of the zeal of the Milesian Chiefs in

[046-181]accumulating MSS. and in rewarding Bards and Scribes. We are enabled to

[046-182]form some idea of the mental resources of an Anglo-Irish nobleman of

[046-183]the fifteenth century, from the catalogue of the library remaining in

[046-184]Maynooth Castle, in the reign of Henry VIII. Of Latin books, there were

[046-185]the works of several of the schoolmen, the dialogues of St. Gregory,

[046-186]Virgil, Juvenal, and Terence; the Holy Bible; Boethius' Consolations of

[046-187]Philosophy, and Saint Thomas's Summa; of French works, Froissart,

[046-188]Mandeville, two French Bibles, a French Livy and Caesar, with the most

[046-189]popular romances; in English, there were the Polychronicon, Cambrensis,

[046-190]Lyttleton's Tenures, Sir Thomas More's book on Pilgrimages, and several

[046-191]romances. Moreover, there were copies of the Psalter of Cashel, a book

[046-192]of Irish chronicles, lives of St. Beraghan, St. Fiech and St. Finian,

[046-193]with various religious tracts, and romantic tales. This was, perhaps,

[046-194]the most extensive private collection to be found within the Pale; we

[046-195]have every reason to infer, that, at least in Irish and Latin works,

[046-196]the Castles of the older race—lovers of learning and entertainers of

[046-197]learned men—were not worse furnished than Maynooth.
