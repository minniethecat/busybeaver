## Overview

Saint Columba (521–597), known in Irish as Columkille, was a Bishop of the royal race of Niall and one of the three patron saints of Ireland, ranking with [[Saint Patrick]] and [[Saint Bridget]] as the most glorious triad of the Irish Calendar. In the year 565, at the age of forty-four, he undertook the mission to convert the Picts and other peoples of northern Britain, an enterprise that established him as one of the most influential figures of early medieval Christianity. (005-038) (005-039) (005-040)

He was accompanied on his voyage by twelve companions, the apostolic number, and for thirty-four years he served as the legislator and captain of Christianity in the northern regions. The monastery he founded on the isle of Iona became the spiritual centre from which Christianity spread across Scotland, Northumbria, and beyond. (005-042) (005-044) (005-047)

## Biography

### Early Life

Columba was born into the royal dynasty of Niall of the Nine Hostages, the Ui Néill, which had dominated the high-kingship of Ireland for centuries. His birth name, Colum Cille, means "Dove of the Church" in Irish. From an early age he was dedicated to the religious life, and he received his education in the great monastic schools of Ireland.

### The Mission to Scotland

In the year of our era 565, Columba undertook the systematic conversion of the remoter Gael and other races then inhabiting Alba — the Picts, Britons, and Scandinavians — on a scale commensurate with its magnitude. At the time he left Ireland he was in the prime of life, his forty-fourth year. (005-038) (005-039) (005-042)

He and his twelve companions crossed the narrow sea from Ireland to the western isles of Scotland. The King of the Picts received baptism at Columba's hands, and the kings of the Scottish colony, who were his own kinsmen, received the crown from him on their accession. The islet of I, or Iona, was presented to him by one of these princes. (005-045) (005-046) (005-047)

### The Monastery of Iona

On Iona, Columba and his companions built with their own hands their parent-house. From this Hebridean rock, in after times, was shaped the destinies, spiritual and temporal, of many tribes and kingdoms. The growth of Iona was as the growth of the grain of mustard seed, even during the life of its founder. (005-048) (005-049) (005-050) (005-052)

Apostles went out from Iona to Iceland, to the Orkneys, to Northumbria, to the Isle of Man, and to South Britain. A hundred monasteries in Ireland looked to that exiled saint as their patriarch. His rule of monastic life was sought for by Chiefs, Bards, and converted Druids. The Venerable Bede, in whose age Iona still flourished, recorded that Columba and his disciples "neither thought of nor loved anything in this world." (005-054) (005-055) (005-056) (005-059) (005-064)

Columba's days were divided between manual labour and the study and transcribing of the Sacred Scriptures. The monastic rule he established — the Columban rule — was characterised by strict asceticism. No woman was allowed to land on the island, nor even a cow to be kept there, for, as Columba taught, "wherever there is a cow there will be a woman, and wherever there is a woman there will be mischief." (005-062) (005-068) (005-069) (005-070)

### The Columban Order

The monastic order that Columba founded was known as the Culdees, meaning simply "Servants of God." After his death, the Abbots of Iona exercised ecclesiastical jurisdiction over all the clergy north of the Humber, and the Columban rule was followed widely in Ireland and Scotland. (005-066) (005-116)

Over subsequent centuries, the Columban order maintained its distinctive traditions against the centralising pressure of the Roman Church. The Columban rule attached more importance to implicit obedience than to external uniformity, and it was only gradually that the Roman hierarchy extinguished the old Columban observances, particularly at the Synod of [[Cashel]]. (020-069) (034-063)

## Legacy

Saint Columba ranks as one of the three patron saints of Ireland, alongside [[saint-patrick|Saint Patrick]] and Saint Bridget. Through the monastery of Iona, his influence extended far beyond his own lifetime, shaping the course of Christianity in Scotland, northern England, and across the Irish Sea. The Book of Kells, one of the greatest illuminated manuscripts of the Middle Ages, is believed to have been produced by Columban monks. (005-040)

His feast day is celebrated on 9 June, and Iona remains a place of pilgrimage to this day. The memory of Columba, the exiled saint who built a kingdom of the spirit on a Hebridean rock, endures as a symbol of Irish missionary zeal and monastic dedication.
