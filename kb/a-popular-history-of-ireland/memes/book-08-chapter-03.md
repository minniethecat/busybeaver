CHAPTER III.

[054-001]ACCESSION OF QUEEN ELIZABETH—PARLIAMENT OF 1560—THE ACT OF

[054-002]UNIFORMITY—CAREER AND DEATH OF JOHN O'NEIL "THE PROUD."

[054-003]The daughter of Anna Boleyn was promptly proclaimed Queen the same day

[054-004]on which Mary died—the 17th of November, 1558. Elizabeth was then in

[054-005]her 26th year, proud of her beauty, and confident in her abilities. Her

[054-006]great capacity had been cultivated by the best masters of the age, and

[054-007]the best of all ages, early adversity. Her vices were hereditary in her

[054-008]blood, but her genius for government so far surpassed any of her

[054-009]immediate predecessors as to throw her vices into the shade. During the

[054-010]forty-four years in which she wielded the English sceptre, many of the

[054-011]most stirring occurrences of our history took place; it could hardly

[054-012]have fallen out otherwise, under a sovereign of so much vigour, having

[054-013]the command of such immense resources.

[054-014]On the news of Mary's death reaching Ireland, the Lord Deputy Sussex

[054-015]returned to England, and Sir Henry Sidney, the Treasurer, was appointed

[054-016]his successor _ad interim_. As in England, so in Ireland, though for

[054-017]somewhat different reasons, the first months of the new reign were

[054-018]marked by a conciliating and temporizing policy. Elizabeth, who had not

[054-019]assumed the title of "Head of the Church," continued to hear Mass for

[054-020]several months after her accession. At her coronation she had a High

[054-021]Mass sung, accompanied, it is true, by a Calvinistic sermon. Before

[054-022]proceeding with the work of "reformation," inaugurated by her father,

[054-023]and arrested by her sister, she proceeded cautiously to establish

[054-024]herself, and her Irish deputy followed in the same careful line of

[054-025]conduct. Having first made a menacing demonstration against John the

[054-026]Proud, he entered into friendly correspondence with him, and finally

[054-027]ended the campaign by standing godfather to one of his children. This

[054-028]relation of gossip among the old Irish was no mere matter of ceremony,

[054-029]but involved obligations lasting as life, and sacred as the ties of

[054-030]kindred blood. By seeking such a sponsor, O'Neil placed himself in

[054-031]Sidney's power, rather than Sidney in his, since the two men must have

[054-032]felt very differently bound by the connection into which they had

[054-033]entered. As an evidence of the Imperial policy of the moment, the

[054-034]incident is instructive.

[054-035]Round the personal history of this splendid, but by no means stainless

[054-036]Ulster Prince, the events of the first nine years of Elizabeth's reign

[054-037]over Ireland naturally group themselves. Whether at her Majesty's

[054-038]council-board, or among the Scottish islands, or in hall or hut at

[054-039]home, the attention of all manner of men interested in Ireland was

[054-040]fixed upon the movements of John the Proud. In tracing his career, we

[054-041]therefore naturally gather all, or nearly all, the threads of the

[054-042]national story, during the first ten years of Queen Mary's successor.

[054-043]In the second year of Elizabeth, Lord Deputy Sussex, who returned fully

[054-044]possessed of her Majesty's views, summoned the Parliament to meet in

[054-045]Dublin on the 12th day of January, 1560. It is to be observed, however,

[054-046]that though the union of the crowns was now of twenty years' standing,

[054-047]the writs were not issued to the nation at large, but only to the ten

[054-048]counties of Dublin, Meath, Louth, West-Meath, Kildare, Carlow,

[054-049]Kilkenny, Wexford, Waterford, and Tipperary, with their boroughs. The

[054-050]published instructions of Lord Sussex were "to make such statutes

[054-051](concerning religion) as were made in England, _mutatis mutandis_." As

[054-052]a preparation for the legislature, St. Patrick's Cathedral and Christ

[054-053]Church were purified by paint; the niches of the Saints were for the

[054-054]second time emptied of their images; texts of Scripture were blazoned

[054-055]upon the walls, and the Litany was chanted in English. After these

[054-056]preparatory demonstrations, the Deputy opened the new Parliament, which

[054-057]sat for one short but busy month. The Acts of Mary's Parliament,

[054-058]re-establishing ecclesiastical relations with Rome, were the first

[054-059]thing repealed; then so much of the Act 33, Henry VIII., as related to

[054-060]the succession, was revived; all ecclesiastical jurisdiction was next

[054-061]declared vested in the Crown, and all "judges, justices, mayors, and

[054-062]temporal officers were declared bound to take tie oath of supremacy;"

[054-063]the penalty attached to the refusal of the oath, by this statute, being

[054-064]"forfeiture of office and promotion during life." Proceeding rapidly in

[054-065]the same direction, it was declared that commissioners in

[054-066]ecclesiastical causes should adjudge nothing as heresy which was not

[054-067]expressly so condemned by the Canonical Scriptures, the received

[054-068]General Councils, or by Parliament. The penalty of _praemunire_ was

[054-069]declared in force, and, to crown the work, the celebrated "Act of

[054-070]Uniformity" was passed. This was followed by other statutes for the

[054-071]restoration of first fruits and twentieths, and for the appointment of

[054-072]Bishops by the royal prerogative, or _conge d'elire_—elections by the

[054-073]chapter being declared mere "shadows of election, and derogatory to the

[054-074]prerogative." Such was, in brief, the legislation of that famous

[054-075]Parliament of ten counties—the often quoted statutes of the "2nd of

[054-076]Elizabeth." In the Act of Uniformity, the best known of all its

[054-077]statutes, there was this curious saving clause inserted: that whenever

[054-078]the "priest or common minister" could not speak English, he might still

[054-079]continue "to celebrate the service in the Latin tongue." Such other

[054-080]observances were to be had as were prescribed by the 2nd Edward VI.,

[054-081]until her Majesty should "publish further ceremonies or rites." We have

[054-082]no history of the debates of this Parliament of a month, but there is

[054-083]ample reason to believe that some of these statutes were resisted

[054-084]throughout by a majority of the Upper House, still chiefly composed of

[054-085]Catholic Peers; that the clause saving the Latin ritual was inserted as

[054-086]a compromise with this opposition; that some of the other Acts were

[054-087]passed by stealth in the absence of many members, and that the Lord

[054-088]Deputy gave his solemn pledge the statute of Uniformity should be

[054-089]enforced, if passed. So severe was the struggle, and so little

[054-090]satisfied was Sussex with his success, that he hastily dissolved the

[054-091]Houses and went over personally to England to represent the state of

[054-092]feeling he had encountered. Finally, it is remarkable that no other

[054-093]Parliament was called in Ireland till nine years afterwards—a

[054-094]convincing proof of how unmanageable that body, even constituted as it

[054-095]was, had shown itself to be in matters affecting religion.

[054-097]The non-invitation of the Irish chiefs to this Parliament, contrary to

[054-098]the precedent set in Mary's reign and in 1541, the laws enacted, and

[054-099]the commotion they excited in the minds of the clergy, were

[054-100]circumstances which could not fail to attract the attention of John

[054-101]O'Neil. Even if insensible to what transpired at Dublin, the

[054-102]indefatigable Sussex—one of the ablest of Elizabeth's able Court—did

[054-103]not suffer him long to misunderstand his relations to the new Queen. He

[054-104]might be Sidney's gossip, but he was not the less Elizabeth's enemy. He

[054-105]had been proclaimed "O'Neil" on the rath of Tullahoge, and had reigned

[054-106]at Dungannon, adjudging life and death. It was clear that two such

[054-107]jurisdictions as the Celtic and the Norman kingship could not stand

[054-108]long on the same soil, and the Ulster Prince soon perceived that he

[054-109]must establish his authority, by arms, or perish with it. We must also

[054-110]read all Irish events of the time of Elizabeth by the light of foreign

[054-111]politics; during the long reign of that sovereign, England was never

[054-112]wholly free from fears of invasion, and many movements which now seem

[054-113]inexplicable will be readily understood when we recollect that they

[054-114]took place under the menaces of foreign powers.

[054-115]The O'Neils had anciently exercised a high-handed superiority over all

[054-116]Ulster, and John the Proud was not the man to let his claim lie idle in

[054-117]any district of that wide-spread Province. But authority which has

[054-118]fallen into decay must be asserted only at a propitious time, and with

[054-119]the utmost tact; and here it was that Elizabeth's statesmen found their

[054-120]most effective means of attacking O'Neil. O'Donnell, who was his

[054-121]father-in-law, was studiously conciliated; his second wife, a lady of

[054-122]the Argyle family, received costly presents from the Queen; O'Reilly

[054-123]was created Earl of Breffni, and encouraged to resist the superiority

[054-124]to which the house of Dungannon laid claim. The natural consequences

[054-125]followed; John the Proud swept like a storm over the fertile hills of

[054-126]Cavan, and compelled the new-made Earl to deliver him tribute and

[054-127]hostages. O'Donnell, attended only by a few of his household, was

[054-128]seized in a religious house upon Lough Swilly, and subjected to every

[054-129]indignity which an insolent enemy could devise. His Countess, already

[054-130]alluded to, supposed to have been privy to this surprise of her

[054-131]husband, became the mistress of his captor and jailer, to whom she bore

[054-132]several children. What deepens the horror of this odious domestic

[054-133]tragedy is the fact that the wife of O'Neil, the daughter of O'Donnell,

[054-134]thus supplanted by her shameless stepmother, under her own roof, died

[054-135]soon afterwards of "horror, loathing, grief, and deep anguish," at the

[054-136]spectacle afforded by the private life of O'Neil, and the severities

[054-137]inflicted upon her wretched father. All the patriotic designs, and all

[054-138]the shining abilities of John the Proud, cannot abate a jot of our

[054-139]detestation of such a private life; though slandered in other respects

[054-140]as he was, by hostile pens, no evidence has been adduced to clear his

[054-141]memory of these indelible stains; nor after becoming acquainted with

[054-142]their existence can we follow his after career with that heartfelt

[054-143]sympathy with which the lives of purer patriots must always inspire us.

[054-144]The pledge given by Sussex, that the penal legislation of 1560 should

[054-145]lie a dead letter, was not long observed. In May of the year following

[054-146]its enactment, a commission was appointed to enforce the 2nd Elizabeth,

[054-147]in West-Meath; and in 1562 a similar commission was appointed for Meath

[054-148]and Armagh. By these commissioners Dr. William Walsh, Catholic Bishop

[054-149]of Meath, was arraigned and imprisoned for preaching against the new

[054-150]liturgy; a Prelate who afterwards died an exile in Spain. The primatial

[054-151]see was for the moment vacant, Archbishop Dowdal having died at London

[054-152]three months before Queen Mary—on the Feast of the Assumption, 1558.

[054-153]Terence, Dean of Armagh, who acted as administrator, convened a Synod

[054-154]of the English-speaking clergy of the Province in July, 1559, at

[054-155]Drogheda, but as this dignitary followed in the steps of his faithful

[054-156]predecessors, his deanery was conferred upon Dr. Adam Loftus, Chaplain

[054-157]of the Lord Lieutenant; two years subsequently the dignity of

[054-158]Archbishop of Armagh was conferred upon the same person. Dr. Loftus, a

[054-159]native of Yorkshire, had found favour in the eyes of the Queen at a

[054-160]public exhibition at Cambridge University; he was but 28 years old,

[054-161]according to Sir James Ware, when consecrated Primate—but Dr. Mant

[054-162]thinks he must have attained at least the canonical age of 30. During

[054-163]the whole of this reign he continued to reside at Dublin, which see was

[054-164]early placed under his jurisdiction in lieu of the inaccessible Armagh.

[054-165]For forty years he continued one of the ruling spirits at Dublin,

[054-166]whether acting as Lord Chancellor, Lord Justice, Privy Councillor, or

[054-167]First Provost of Trinity College. He was a pluralist in Church and

[054-168]State, insatiable of money and honours; if he did not greatly assist in

[054-169]establishing his religion, he was eminently successful in enriching his

[054-170]family.

[054-171]Having subdued every hostile neighbour and openly assumed the high

[054-172]prerogative of Prince of Ulster, John the Proud looked around him for

[054-173]allies in the greater struggle which he foresaw could not be long

[054-174]postponed. Calvagh O'Donnell was yielded up on receiving a munificent

[054-175]ransom, but his infamous wife remained with her paramour. A negotiation

[054-176]was set on foot with the chiefs of the Highland and Island Scots, large

[054-177]numbers of whom entered into O'Neil's service. Emissaries were

[054-178]despatched to the French Court, where they found a favourable

[054-179]reception, as Elizabeth was known to be in league with the King of

[054-180]Navarre and the Huguenot leaders against Francis II. The unexpected

[054-181]death of the King at the close of 1560; the return of his youthful

[054-182]widow, Queen Mary, to Scotland; the vigorous regency of Catherine de

[054-183]Medicis during the minority of her second son; the ill-success of

[054-184]Elizabeth's arms during the campaigns of 1561-2-3, followed by the

[054-185]humiliating peace of April, 1564—these events are all to be borne in

[054-186]memory when considering the extraordinary relations which were

[054-187]maintained during the same years by the proud Prince of Ulster, with

[054-188]the still prouder Queen of England. The apparently contradictory

[054-189]tactics pursued by the Lord Deputy Sussex, between his return to Dublin

[054-190]in the spring of 1561, and his final recall in 1564, when read by the

[054-191]light of events which transpired at Paris, London, and Edinburgh,

[054-192]become easily intelligible. In the spring of the first mentioned year,

[054-193]it was thought possible to intimidate O'Neil, so Lord Sussex, with the

[054-194]Earl of Ormond as second in command, marched northwards, entered

[054-195]Armagh, and began to fortify the city, with a view to placing in it a

[054-196]powerful garrison. O'Neil, to remove the seat of hostilities, made an

[054-197]irruption into the plain of Meath, and menaced Dublin. The utmost

[054-198]consternation prevailed at his approach, and the Deputy, while

[054-199]continuing the fortification of Armagh, despatched the main body of his

[054-200]troops to press on the rear of the aggressor. By a rapid countermarch,

[054-201]O'Neil came up with this force, laden with spoils, in Louth, and after

[054-202]an obstinate engagement routed them with immense loss. On receipt of

[054-203]this intelligence, Sussex promptly abandoned Armagh, and returned to

[054-204]Dublin, while O'Neil erected his standard, as far South as Drogheda,

[054-205]within twenty miles of the capital. So critical at this moment was the

[054-206]aspect of affairs, that all the energies of the English interest were

[054-207]taxed to the utmost. In the autumn of the year, Sussex marched again

[054-208]from Dublin northward, having at his side the five powerful Earls of

[054-209]Kildare, Ormond, Desmond, Thomond, and Clanrickarde—whose mutual feuds

[054-210]had been healed or dissembled for the day. O'Neil prudently fell back

[054-211]before this powerful expedition, which found its way to the shores of

[054-212]Lough Foyle, without bringing him to an engagement, and without any

[054-213]military advantage. As the shortest way of getting rid of such an

[054-214]enemy, the Lord Deputy, though one of the wisest and most justly

[054-215]celebrated of Elizabeth's Counsellors, did not hesitate to communicate

[054-216]to his royal mistress the project of hiring an assassin, named Nele

[054-217]Gray, to take off the Prince of Ulster, but the plot, though carefully

[054-218]elaborated, miscarried. Foreign news, which probably reached him only

[054-219]on reaching the Foyle, led to a sudden change of tactics on the part of

[054-220]Sussex, and the young Lord Kildare—O'Neil's cousin-germain, was

[054-221]employed to negotiate a peace with the enemy they had set out to

[054-222]demolish.

[054-223]This Lord Kildare was Gerald, the eleventh Earl, the same whom we have

[054-224]spoken of as a fugitive lad, in the last years of Henry VIII., and as

[054-225]restored to his estates and rank by Queen Mary. Although largely

[054-226]indebted to his Catholicity for the protection he had received while

[054-227]abroad from Francis I., Charles V., the Duke of Tuscany and the Roman

[054-228]See—especially the Cardinals Pole and Farnese—and still more indebted

[054-229]to the late Catholic Queen for the restoration of his family honours,

[054-230]this finished courtier, now in the very midsummer of life, one of the

[054-231]handsomest and most accomplished persons of his time, did not hesitate

[054-232]to conform himself, at least outwardly, to the religion of the State.

[054-233]Shortly before the campaign of which we have spoken, he had been

[054-234]suspected of treasonable designs, but had pleaded his cause

[054-235]successfully with the Queen in person. From Lough Foyle, accompanied by

[054-236]the Lord Slane, the Viscount Baltinglass, and a suitable guard, Lord

[054-237]Kildare set out for John O'Neil's camp, where a truce was concluded

[054-238]between the parties, Lord Sussex undertaking to withdraw his wardens

[054-239]from Armagh, and O'Neil engaging himself to live in peace with her

[054-240]Majesty, and to serve "when necessary against her enemies." The cousins

[054-241]also agreed personally to visit the English Court the following year,

[054-242]and accordingly in January ensuing they went to England, from which

[054-243]they returned home in the latter end of May.

[054-244]The reception of John the Proud, at the Court of Elizabeth, was

[054-245]flattering in the extreme. The courtiers stared and smiled at his

[054-246]bareheaded body-guard, with their crocus-dyed vests, short jackets, and

[054-247]shaggy cloaks. But the broad-bladed battle-axe, and the sinewy arm

[054-248]which wielded it, inspired admiration for all the uncouth costume. The

[054-249]haughty indifference with which the Prince of Ulster treated every one

[054-250]about the Court, except the Queen, gave a keener edge to the satirical

[054-251]comments which were so freely indulged in at the expense of his style

[054-252]of dress. The wits proclaimed him "O'Neil the Great, cousin to Saint

[054-253]Patrick, friend to the Queen of England, and enemy to all the world

[054-254]besides!" O'Neil was well pleased with his reception by Elizabeth. When

[054-255]taxed upon his return with having made peace with her Majesty, he

[054-256]answered—"Yes, in her own bed-chamber." There were, indeed, many points

[054-257]in common in both their characters.

[054-258]Her Majesty, by letters patent dated at Windsor, on the 15th of

[054-259]January, 1563, recognized in John the Proud "the name and title of

[054-260]O'Neil, with the like authority, jurisdiction, and pre-eminence, as any

[054-261]of his ancestors." And O'Neil, by articles, dated at Benburb, the 18th

[054-262]of November of the same year, reciting the letters patent aforesaid,

[054-263]bound himself and his suffragans to behave as "the Queen's good and

[054-264]faithful subjects against all persons whatever." Thus, so far as an

[054-265]English alliance could guarantee it, was the supremacy of this daring

[054-266]chief guaranteed in Ulster from the Boyne to the North Sea.

[054-267]In performing his part of the engagements thus entered into, O'Neil is

[054-268]placed in a less invidious light by English writers than formerly. They

[054-269]now describe him as scrupulously faithful to his word; as charitable to

[054-270]the poor, always carving and sending meat from his own table to the

[054-271]beggar at the gate before eating himself. Of the sincerity with which

[054-272]he carried out the expulsion of the Islesmen and Highlanders from

[054-273]Ulster, the result afforded the most conclusive evidence. It is true he

[054-274]had himself invited those bands into the Province to aid him against

[054-275]the very power with which he was now at peace, and, therefore, they

[054-276]might in their view allege duplicity and desertion against him. Yet

[054-277]enlisted as they usually were but for a single campaign, O'Neil

[054-278]expected them to depart as readily as they had come. But in this

[054-279]expectation he was disappointed. Their leaders, Angus, James, and

[054-280]Sorley McDonald, refused to recognize the new relations which had

[054-281]arisen, and O'Neil was, therefore, compelled to resort to force. He

[054-282]defeated the Scottish troops at Glenfesk, near Ballycastle, in 1564, in

[054-283]an action wherein Angus McDonald was slain, James died of his wounds,

[054-284]and Sorley was carried prisoner to Benburb. An English auxiliary force,

[054-285]under Colonel Randolph, sent round by sea, under pretence of

[054-286]co-operating against the Scots, took possession of Derry and began to

[054-287]fortify it. But their leader was slain in a skirmish with a party of

[054-288]O'Neil's people who disliked the fortress, and whether by accident or

[054-289]otherwise their magazine exploded, killing a great part of the garrison

[054-290]and destroying their works. The remnant took to their shipping and

[054-291]returned to Dublin.

[054-293]In the years 1565, '6 and '7, the internal dissensions of both Scotland

[054-294]and France, and the perturbations in the Netherlands giving full

[054-295]occupation to her foreign foes, Elizabeth had an interval of leisure to

[054-296]attend to this dangerous ally in Ulster. A second unsuccessful attempt

[054-297]on his life, by an assassin named Smith, was traced to the Lord Deputy,

[054-298]and a formal commission issued by the Queen to investigate the case.

[054-299]The result we know only by the event; Sussex was recalled, and Sir

[054-300]Henry Sidney substituted in his place! Death had lately made way in

[054-301]Tyrconnell and Fermanagh for new chiefs, and these leaders, more

[054-302]vigorous than their predecessors, were resolved to shake off the

[054-303]recently imposed and sternly exercised supremacy of Benburb. With these

[054-304]chiefs, Sidney, at the head of a veteran armament, cordially

[054-305]co-operated, and O'Neil's territory was now attacked simultaneously at

[054-306]three different points—in the year 1566. No considerable success was,

[054-307]however, obtained over him till the following year, when, at the very

[054-308]opening of the campaign, the brave O'Donnell arrested his march along

[054-309]the strand of the Lough Swilly, and the tide rising impetuously, as it

[054-310]does on that coast, on the rear of the men of Tyrone, struck them with

[054-311]terror, and completed their defeat. From 1,500 to 3,000 men perished by

[054-312]the sword or by the tide; John the Proud fled alone, along the river

[054-313]Swilly, and narrowly escaped by the fords of rivers and by solitary

[054-314]ways to his Castle on Lough Neagh. The Annalists of Donegal, who were

[054-315]old enough to have conversed with survivors of the battle, say that his

[054-316]mind became deranged by this sudden fall from the summit of prosperity

[054-317]to the depths of defeat. His next step would seem to establish the

[054-318]fact, for he at once despatched Sorley McDonald, the survivor of the

[054-319]battle of Glenfesk, to recruit a new auxiliary force for him amongst

[054-320]the Islesmen, whom he had so mortally offended. Then, abandoning his

[054-321]fortress upon the Blackwater, he set out with 50 guards, his secretary,

[054-322]and his mistress, the wife of the late O'Donnell, to meet these

[054-323]expected allies whom he had so fiercely driven off but two short years

[054-324]before. At Cushendun, on the Antrim coast, they met with all apparent

[054-325]cordiality, but an English agent, Captain Piers, or Pierce, seized an

[054-326]opportunity during the carouse which ensued to recall the bitter

[054-327]memories of Glenfesk. A dispute and a quarrel ensued; O'Neil fell

[054-328]covered with wounds, amid the exulting shouts of the avenging Islesmen.

[054-329]His gory head was presented to Captain Piers, who hastened with it to

[054-330]Dublin, where he received a reward of a thousand marks for his success.

[054-331]High spiked upon the towers of the Castle, that proud head remained and

[054-332]rotted; the body, wrapped in a Kerns saffron shirt, was interred where

[054-333]he fell, a spot familiar to all the inhabitants of the Antrim glens as

[054-334]"the grave of Shane O'Neil." And so may be said to close the first

[054-335]decade of Elizabeth's reign over Ireland!
