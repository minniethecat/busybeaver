CHAPTER V.

[021-001]SOCIAL CONDITION OF THE IRISH PREVIOUS TO THE NORMAN INVASION.

[021-002]The total population of Ireland, when the Normans first entered it, can

[021-003]only be approximated by conjecture. Supposing the whole force with

[021-004]which Roderick and his allies invested the Normans in Dublin, to be, as

[021-005]stated by a cotemporary writer, some 50,000 men, and that that force

[021-006]included one-fourth of all the men of the military age in the country;

[021-007]and further, supposing the men of military age to bear the proportion

[021-008]of one-fifth to the whole number of inhabitants, this would give a

[021-009]total population of about one million. Even this conjecture is to be

[021-010]taken with great diffidence and distrust, but, for the sake of

[021-011]clearness, it is set down as a possible Irish census, towards the close

[021-012]of the twelfth century.

[021-013]This population was divided into two great classes, the _Saer-Clanna_,

[021-014]or free tribes, chiefly, if not exclusively, of Milesian race; and the

[021-015]_Daer-Clanna_, or unfree tribes, consisting of the descendants of the

[021-016]subjugated older races, or of clans once free, reduced to servitude by

[021-017]the sword, or of the posterity of foreign mercenary soldiers. Of the

[021-018]free clans, the most illustrious were those of whose Princes we have

[021-019]traced the record—the descendants of Nial in Ulster and Meath, of

[021-020]Cathaeir More in Leinster, of Oliold in Munster, and of Eochaid in

[021-021]Connaught. An arbitrary division once limited the free clans to six in

[021-022]the southern half-kingdom, and six in the north; and the unfree also to

[021-023]six. But Geoffrey Keating, whose love of truth was quite as strong as

[021-024]his credulity in ancient legends—and that is saying much—disclaimed

[021-025]that classification, and collected his genealogies from principal

[021-026]heads—branching out into three families of tribes, descended from Eber

[021-027]Finn, one from Ir, and four from Eremhon, sons of Milesians of Spain;

[021-028]and ninth tribe sprung from Ith, granduncle to the sons of Milesius.

[021-029]The principal Eberian families' names were McCarthy, O'Sullivan,

[021-030]O'Mahony, O'Donovan, O'Brien, O'Dea, O'Quin, McMahon (of Clare),

[021-031]McNamara, O'Carroll (of Ely), and O'Gara; the Irian families were

[021-032]Magennis, O'Farrall, and O'Conor (of Kerry); the posterity of Eremhon

[021-033]branched out into the O'Neils, O'Donnells, O'Dohertys, O'Gallahers,

[021-034]O'Boyles, McGeoghegans, O'Conors (of Connaught), O'Flahertys, O'Heynes,

[021-035]O'Shaughnessys, O'Clerys, O'Dowdas, McDonalds (of Antrim), O'Kellys,

[021-036]Maguires, Kavanaghs, Fitzpatricks, O'Dwyers, and O'Conors (of Offally).

[021-037]The chief families of Ithian origin were the O'Driscolls, O'Learys,

[021-038]Coffeys, and Clancys. Out of the greater tribes many subdivisions arose

[021-039]from time to time, when new names were coined for some intermediate

[021-040]ancestor; but the farther enumeration of these may be conveniently

[021-041]dispensed with.

[021-042]The _Daer-Clanna_, or unfree tribes, have left no history. Under the

[021-043]despotism of the Milesian kings, it was high treason to record the

[021-044]actions of the conquered race; so that the Irish Belgae fared as badly

[021-045]in this respect, at the hands of the Milesian historians, as the latter

[021-046]fared in after times from the chroniclers of the Normans. We only know

[021-047]that such tribes were, and that their numbers and physical force more

[021-048]than once excited the apprehension of the children of the conquerors.

[021-049]What proportion they bore to the _Saer-Clanna_ we have no positive data

[021-050]to determine. A fourth, a fifth, or a sixth, they may have been; but

[021-051]one thing is certain, the jealous policy of the superior race never

[021-052]permitted them to reascend the plane of equality, from which they had

[021-053]been hurled, at the very commencement of the Milesian ascendency.

[021-054]In addition to the enslaved by conquest and the enslaved by crime,

[021-055]there were also the enslaved by purchase. From the earliest period,

[021-056]slave dealers from Ireland had frequented Bristol, the great British

[021-057]slave market, to purchase human beings. Christian morality, though it

[021-058]may have mitigated the horrors of this odious traffic, did not at once

[021-059]lead to its abolition. In vain Saint Wulfstan preached against it in

[021-060]the South, as Saint Aidan had done long before him in the North of

[021-061]England. Files of fair-haired Saxon slaves, of both sexes, yoked

[021-062]together with ropes, continued to be shipped at Bristol, and bondmen

[021-063]and bondwomen continued to be articles of value—exchanged between the

[021-064]Prince and his subordinates, as stipend or tribute. The King of Cashel

[021-065]alone gave to the chief of the Eugenians, as part of his annual

[021-066]stipend, ten bondmen and ten women; to the lord of Bruree, seven pages

[021-067]and seven bondwomen; to the lord of Deisi, eight slaves of each sex,

[021-068]and seven female slaves to the lord of Kerry; among the items which

[021-069]make up the tribute from Ossory to Cashel are ten bondmen and ten grown

[021-070]women; and from the Deisi, eight bondmen and eight "brown-haired"

[021-071]women. The annual exchanges of this description, set down as due in the

[021-072]Book of Rights, would require the transfer of several hundreds of

[021-073]slaves yearly, from one set of masters to another. Cruelties and

[021-074]outrages must have been inseparable from the system, and we can hardly

[021-075]wonder at the sweeping decree by which the Synod of Armagh (A.D. 1171)

[021-076]declared all the English slaves in Ireland free to return to their

[021-077]homes, and anathematized the whole inhuman traffic. The fathers of that

[021-078]council looked upon the Norman invasion as a punishment from Heaven on

[021-079]the slave trade; for they believed in their purity of heart, that power

[021-080]_is_ transferred from one nation to another, because of injustices,

[021-081]oppressions, and divers deceits.

[021-082]The purchased slaves and unfree tribes tilled the soil, and practised

[021-083]the mechanic arts. Agriculture seems first to have been lifted into

[021-084]respectability by the Cistercian Monks, while spinning, weaving, and

[021-085]almost every mechanic calling, if we except the scribe, the armorer,

[021-086]and the bell-founder, continued down to very recent tunes to be held in

[021-087]contempt among the Gael. A brave man is mentioned as having been a

[021-088]"weaving woman's son," with much the same emphasis as Jeptha is spoken

[021-089]of as the son of an Harlot. Mechanic wares were disposed of at those

[021-090]stated gatherings, which combined popular games, chariot races for the

[021-091]nobles, and markets for the merchants. A Bard of the tenth or eleventh

[021-092]century, in a desperate effort to vary the usual high-flown

[021-093]descriptions of the country, calls it "Erin of the hundred fair

[021-094]greens,"—a very graphic, if not a very poetic illustration.

[021-095]The administration of justice was an hereditary trust, committed to

[021-096]certain judicial families, who held their lands, as the Monks did, by

[021-097]virtue of their profession. When the posterity of the Brehon, or Judge

[021-098]failed, it was permitted to adopt from the class of students, a male

[021-099]representative, in whom the judicial authority was perpetuated: the

[021-100]families of O'Gnive and O'Clery in the North, of O'Daly in Meath,

[021-101]O'Doran in Leinster, McEgan in Munster, Mulconry or Conroy in

[021-102]Connaught, were the most distinguished Brehon houses. Some

[021-103]peculiarities of the Brehon law, relating to civil succession and

[021-104]sovereignty, such as the institution of Tanistry, and the system of

[021-105]stipends and tributes, have been already explained; parricide and

[021-106]murder were in latter ages punished with death; homicide and rape by

[021-107]_eric_ or fine. There were, besides, the laws of gavelkind or division

[021-108]of property among the members of the clan; laws relating to boundaries;

[021-109]sumptuary laws regulating the dress of the various castes into which

[021-110]society was divided; laws relating to the planting of trees, the

[021-111]trespass of cattle, and billeting of troops. These laws were either

[021-112]written in detail, or consisted of certain acknowledged ancient maxims

[021-113]of which the Brehon made the application in each particular case,

[021-114]answering to what we call "Judge-made law." Of such ancient tracts as

[021-115]composed the Celtic code, an immense number have, fortunately survived,

[021-116]even to this late day, and we may shortly expect a complete digest of

[021-117]all that are now known to exist, in a printed and imperishable form,

[021-118]from the hands of native scholars, every way competent to the task.

[021-119]The commerce of the country, in the eleventh and twelfth centuries, was

[021-120]largely in the hands of the Christian Hiberno-Danes, of the eastern and

[021-121]southern coast. By them the slave trade with Bristol was mostly

[021-122]maintained, and the Irish oak, with which William Rufus roofed

[021-123]Westminster Abbey, was probably rafted by them in the Thames. The

[021-124]English and Welsh coasts, at least, were familiar to their pilots, and

[021-125]they combined, as was usual in that age, the military with the

[021-126]mercantile character. In 1142, and again in 1165, a troop of Dublin

[021-127]Danes fought under Norman banners against the brave Britons of Cambria,

[021-128]and in the camps of their allies, sung the praises of the fertile

[021-129]island of the west. The hundred fairs of Erin—after their conversion

[021-130]and submission to native authority—afforded them convenient markets for

[021-131]disposing of the commodities they imported from abroad.

[021-132]The Gaelic mind, long distracted by the din of war from the purifying

[021-133]and satisfying influences of a Christian life, naturally fell back upon

[021-134]the abandoned, half-forgotten superstitions of the Pagan period.

[021-135]Preceding every fresh calamity, we hear of signs and wonders, of

[021-136]migratory lakes disappearing in a night, of birds and wolves speaking

[021-137]with human voices, of showers of blood falling in the fields, of a

[021-138]whale with golden teeth stranded at Carlingford, of cloud ships, with

[021-139]their crews, seen plainly sailing in the sky. One of the marvels of

[021-140]this class is thus gravely entered in our Annals, under the year

[021-141]1054—"A steeple of fire was seen in the air over Rossdala, on the

[021-142]Sunday of the festival of St. George, for the space of five hours;

[021-143]innumerable black birds passed into and out of it, and one large bird

[021-144]in the middle of them; and the little birds went under his wings when

[021-145]they went into the steeple. They came out and raised up a greyhound

[021-146]that was in the middle of the town aloft in the air, and let it drop

[021-147]down again, so that it died immediately; and they took up three cloaks

[021-148]and two shirts, and let them drop down in the same manner. The wood on

[021-149]which these birds perched fell under them; and the oak tree on which

[021-150]they perched shook with its roots in the earth." In many other

[021-151]superstitions of the same age we see the latent moral sentiment, as

[021-152]well as the over-excited imagination of the people. Such is the story

[021-153]of the stolen jewels of Clonmacnoise, providentially recovered in the

[021-154]year 1130. The thief in vain endeavoured to escape out of the country,

[021-155]from Cork, Lismore, and Waterford, "but no ship into which he entered

[021-156]found a wind to sail, while all the other ships did." And the

[021-157]conscience stricken thief declared, in his dying confession, that he

[021-158]used to see Saint Kieran "stopping with his crozier, every ship into

[021-159]which he entered." It was also an amiable popular illusion that

[021-160]abundant harvests followed the making of peace, the enacting of

[021-161]salutary laws, and the accession of a King who loved justice; and

[021-162]careful entry is made in our chronicles of every evidence of this

[021-163]character.

[021-164]The literature of the masses of the people was pretty equally composed

[021-165]of the legends of the Saints and the older Ossianic legend, so much

[021-166]misunderstood and distorted by modern criticism. The legends of the

[021-167]former class were chiefly wonders wrought by the favourite Saints of

[021-168]the district or the island, embellished with many quaint fancies and

[021-169]tagged out with remnants of old Pagan superstition. St. Columbkill and

[021-170]St. Kieran were, most commonly, the heroes of those tales, which,

[021-171]perhaps, were never intended by their authors to be seriously believed.

[021-172]Such was the story of the great founder of Iona having transformed the

[021-173]lady and her maid, who insulted him on his way to Drom-Keth, into two

[021-174]herons, who are doomed to hover about the neighbouring ford till the

[021-175]day of doom; and such that other story of "the three first monks" who

[021-176]joined St. Kieran in the desert, being a fox, a badger, and a bear, all

[021-177]endowed with speech, and all acting a part in the legend true to their

[021-178]own instincts. Of higher poetic merit is the legend of the voyage of

[021-179]St. Brendan over the great sea, and how the birds which sung vespers

[021-180]for him in the groves of the Promised Land were inhabited by human

[021-181]souls, as yet in a state of probation waiting for their release!

[021-183]In the Ossianic legend we have the common stock of Oriental ideas—the

[021-184]metamorphosis of guilty wives and haughty concubines into dogs and

[021-185]birds; the speaking beasts and fishes; the enchanted swans, originally

[021-186]daughters of Lir; the boar of Ben Bulben, by which the champion,

[021-187]Diarmid, was slain; the Phoenix in the stork of Inniskea, of which

[021-188]there never was but one, yet that one perpetually reproduced itself;

[021-189]the spirits of the wood, and the spirits inhabiting springs and

[021-190]streams; the fairy horse; the sacred trees; the starry influences.

[021-191]Monstrous and gigantic human shapes, like the Jinns of the Arabian

[021-192]tales, occasionally enter into the plot, and play a midnight part,

[021-193]malignant to the hopes of good men. At their approach the earth is

[021-194]troubled, the moon is overcast, gusts of storm are shaken out from the

[021-195]folds of their garments, the watch dogs and the war dogs cower down, in

[021-196]camp and rath, and whine piteously, as if in pain.

[021-197]The variety of grace, and peculiarities of organization, with which, if

[021-198]not the original, certainly the Christianized Irish imagination,

[021-199]endowed and equipped the personages of the fairy world, were of almost

[021-200]Grecian delicacy. There is no personage who rises to the sublime height

[021-201]of Zeus, or the incomparable union of beauty and wisdom in Pallas

[021-202]Athene: what forms Bel, or Crom, or Bride, the queen of Celtic song,

[021-203]may have worn to the pre-Christian ages we know not, nor can know; but

[021-204]the minor creations of Grecian fancy, with which they peopled their

[021-205]groves and fountains, are true kindred of the brain, to the innocent,

[021-206]intelligent, and generally gentle inhabitants of the Gaelic Fairyland.

[021-207]The _Sidhe_, a tender, tutelary spirit, attached herself to heroes,

[021-208]accompanied them in battle, shrouded them with invisibility, dressed

[021-209]their wounds with more than mortal skill, and watched over them with

[021-210]more than mortal love; the _Banshee_, a sad, Cassandra-like spirit,

[021-211]shrieked her weird warning in advance of death, but with a prejudice

[021-212]eminently Milesian, watched only over those of pure blood, whether

[021-213]their fortunes abode in hovel or hall. The more modern and grotesque

[021-214]personages of the Fairy world are sufficiently known to render

[021-215]description unnecessary.

[021-216]Two habitual sources of social enjoyment and occupation with the Irish

[021-217]of those days were music and chess. The harp was the favourite

[021-218]instrument, but the horn or trumpet, and the pibroch or bagpipe, were

[021-219]also in common use. Not only professional performers, but men and women

[021-220]of all ranks, from the humblest to the highest, prided themselves on

[021-221]some knowledge of instrumental music. It seems to have formed part of

[021-222]the education of every order, and to have been cherished alike in the

[021-223]palace, the shieling, and the cloister. "It is a poor church that has

[021-224]no music," is a Gaelic proverb, as old, perhaps, as the establishment

[021-225]of Christianity in the land; and no house was considered furnished

[021-226]without at least one harp. Students from other countries, as we learn

[021-227]from _Giraldus_, came to Ireland for their musical education in the

[021-228]twelfth century, just as our artists now visit Germany and Italy with

[021-229]the same object in view.

[021-230]The frequent mention of the game of chess, in ages long before those at

[021-231]which we have arrived, shows how usual was that most intellectual

[021-232]amusement. The chess board was called in Irish _fithcheall_, and is

[021-233]described in the Glossary of Cormac, of Cashel, composed towards the

[021-234]close of the ninth century, as quadrangular, having straight spots of

[021-235]black and white. Some of them were inlaid with gold and silver, and

[021-236]adorned with gems. Mention is made in a tale of the twelfth century of

[021-237]a "man-bag of woven brass wire." No entire set of the ancient men is

[021-238]now known to exist, though frequent mention is made of "the brigade or

[021-239]family of chessmen," in many old manuscripts. Kings of bone, seated in

[021-240]sculptured chairs, about two inches in height, have been found, and

[021-241]specimens of them engraved in recent antiquarian publications.

[021-242]It only remains to notice, very briefly, the means of locomotion which

[021-243]bound and brought together this singular state of society. Five great

[021-244]roads, radiating from Tara, as a centre, are mentioned in our earliest

[021-245]record; the road _Dala_ leading to Ossory, and so on into Munster; the

[021-246]road _Assail_, extending western through Mullingar towards the Shannon;

[021-247]the road _Cullin_, extending towards Dublin and Bray; the exact route

[021-248]of the northern road, _Midhluachra_, is undetermined; _Slighe Mor_, the

[021-249]great western road, followed the course of the _esker_, or hill-range,

[021-250]from Tara to Galway. Many cross-roads are also known as in common use

[021-251]from the sixth century downwards. Of these, the Four Masters mention,

[021-252]at various dates, not less than forty, under their different local

[021-253]names, previous to the Norman invasion. These roads were kept in

[021-254]repair, according to laws enacted for that purpose, and were traversed

[021-255]by the chiefs and ecclesiastics in _carbads_, or chariots; a main road

[021-256]was called a _slighe_ (_sleigh_), because it was made for the free

[021-257]passage of two chariots—"i.e. the chariot of a King and the chariot of

[021-258]a Bishop." Persons of that rank were driven by an _ara_, or charioteer,

[021-259]and, no doubt, made a very imposing figure. The roads were legally to

[021-260]be repaired at three seasons, namely, for the accommodation of those

[021-261]going to the national games, at fair-time, and in time of war. Weeds

[021-262]and brushwood were to be removed, and water to be drained off; items of

[021-263]road-work which do not give us a very high idea of the comfort or

[021-264]finish of those ancient highways.

[021-265]Such, faintly seen from afar, and roughly sketched, was domestic life

[021-266]and society among our ancestors, previous to the Anglo-Norman invasion,

[021-267]in the reign of King Roderick O'Conor.
