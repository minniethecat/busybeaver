George Browne served as the first Protestant Archbishop of Dublin, appointed by Henry VIII in 1535 to replace Archbishop John Alen, who had been murdered during the rebellion of [[Silken Thomas|Thomas Fitzgerald, "Silken Thomas"]]. A former Provincial of the Augustinian order in England and a strenuous advocate of Henry's divorce from Catherine of Aragon, Browne was to be the principal instrument of the English Reformation in Ireland. (050-164)

McGee describes him as a man who echoed the opinions of Archbishop Cranmer and Secretary Cromwell throughout his life. His tenure, however, was marked by fierce resistance from both the Gaelic Irish and the Old English of the Pale. (050-167)

## Appointment and Early Years

Browne was consecrated Archbishop of Dublin by Thomas Cranmer on 12 March 1535. He arrived in Ireland at a time of acute crisis: the Kildare rebellion had only recently been suppressed, [[Silken Thomas]] and his uncles were awaiting execution in London, and Henry's authority had been proclaimed supreme over the Irish Church by the Irish Parliament. (050-166)

The new Archbishop was tasked with advancing the royal supremacy in a country where the great majority of both clergy and laity remained firmly attached to the old religion. He was an Englishman with little knowledge of Ireland, appointed to a see whose previous occupant had been murdered in the course of resisting the very policies Browne was sent to enforce. (050-168)

Nevertheless, Browne brought energy to the task. He joined with other reform-minded churchmen — notably Edward Staples, Bishop of Meath — to form the nucleus of a Protestant episcopate. Together they constituted the Irish ecclesiastical establishment, such as it was, under the Henrician supremacy. (052-014)

## The Commission for the Destruction of Images

In 1539, a royal commission was issued to Archbishop Browne and others for the destruction of images and relics and the suppression of pilgrimages throughout Ireland. The commission also mandated the substitution of English prayers for the Mass — a measure that brought home to the population the sweeping character of the religious changes. (051-203)

> "The Commission issued in 1539 to Archbishop Browne and others for the destruction of images and relics, and the prevention of pilgrimages, as well as the ordering of English prayers as a substitute for the Mass, brought home to all minds the sweeping character of the change." [051-203]

The native Irish annals record the breaking out of fresh wars and disturbances following the enforcement of these measures. The destruction of relics — objects of profound veneration that had been central to Irish religious practice for centuries — provoked particular outrage. Pilgrimage sites associated with Ireland's early saints were suppressed or destroyed. (051-207)

## Opposition and Resistance

The most resolute opponent of Browne's Reformation was Archbishop George Cromer of Armagh, the Primate of All Ireland. Cromer led the early opposition to Browne, maintaining the traditional faith at the highest level of the Irish hierarchy. His resistance was one of the most significant obstacles to the progress of the Reformation in Ireland. (051-163)

> "In March, 1543, Primate Cromer, who had so resolutely led the early opposition to Archbishop Browne, died." [051-164]

Upon Cromer's death in 1543, Pope Paul III appointed Robert Waucop, a blind Scottish doctor of the Sorbonne, to the See of Armagh. Waucop was one of the most distinguished prelates of his age. Though afflicted with blindness from his youth, he attended the Council of Trent and was regarded as a formidable champion of the Catholic cause. His appointment demonstrated the determination of Rome to maintain the old faith in Ireland. (051-165)

The death of Cromer did not end resistance to Browne. Throughout his episcopate, the majority of the Irish clergy and laity continued to adhere to Catholicism. Browne's authority was effective mainly within the Pale, and even there, the old Anglo-Irish families remained predominantly Catholic in sympathy. (051-168)

## Later Years and Legacy

Browne survived the fall of his patrons Cromwell and Cranmer. Under Queen Mary, the Catholic restoration brought about the deprivation of married bishops — Browne, who had married, was removed from his see. The officiating prelates at the consecration of new bishops included Browne alongside Staples and Lancaster of Kildare. (052-083)

Under [[Elizabeth I]], the religious settlement was reversed once more. Browne's legacy was the establishment of a Protestant episcopal succession in Dublin, though one whose validity would be the subject of later controversy. Irish Protestant writers would later rest their claim to apostolic succession not primarily upon Browne's consecration but upon that of [[Adam Loftus]], consecrated in England according to the reformed rite. (052-087)

Browne's career illustrates the difficulty of transplanting the English Reformation to Irish soil. Appointed to advance a religious revolution, he confronted a population whose attachment to the old faith proved far more durable than the political calculations of Tudor ministers had allowed. The Reformation he championed remained, in McGee's telling, a thing imposed from without rather than embraced from within. (052-085)
