## Overview

James Butler, 12th Earl and 1st Duke of Ormond (1610–1688), was the greatest Anglo-Irish statesman of the seventeenth century. Over a public career spanning more than half a century, he served the House of Stuart as Lord Lieutenant of Ireland on three separate occasions, commanded the royalist forces in Ireland during the Confederate Wars, and negotiated the complex triangulation between Catholic Confederates, Protestant royalists, and English Parliamentarians. His political longevity and personal integrity earned him a reputation as one of the most capable and trustworthy servants of the Crown. (067-120)

> James Butler, Earl, Marquis, and Duke of Ormond deservedly ranks amongst the principal statesmen of his time. During a public career of more than half a century his conduct in many eminent offices of trust was distinguished by supreme ability, life-long firmness and consistency. (067-120)

## The Butler Family

The Butlers of Ormond were among the oldest and most powerful of the Anglo-Norman families in Ireland. The first of the line, Theobald Walter, had arrived with Prince John in 1185 and been granted the hereditary butlership of Ireland. Successive Earls of Ormond played central roles in Irish affairs throughout the medieval period. James, the second Earl of Ormond, was the maternal grandson of King Edward I and was known as "the noble Earl"; his influence in the fourteenth century was such that Gaelic chieftains threatened to ally with the Earl of Desmond against him. (041-089)

> This most probably refers to James, second Earl of Ormond, who, from being the maternal grandson of Edward I, was called the noble Earl, and was considered in his day the peculiar representative of the English interest. (041-091)

James, the fifth Earl of Ormond, sustained the Lancastrian cause during the Wars of the Roses and paid for his loyalty with his life. He fought at almost all the major engagements from the first Battle of Saint Albans in 1455 to the Battle of Towton in 1461, where he was taken prisoner and executed by the victorious Yorkists. (044-242)

> In these English wars, the only Irish nobleman who sustained the Lancasterian cause was James, fifth Earl of Ormond. He had been created by Henry, Earl of Wiltshire, during his father's lifetime, and held a foremost rank in almost all the engagements from the battle of Saint Albans to that of Towton, in which he was taken prisoner and executed. (044-242)

## The Confederate Wars

The twelfth Earl, James Butler, inherited the earldom in 1633 and immediately assumed the foremost place in Irish public life. When the Great Rebellion of 1641 erupted, Ormond was appointed Lieutenant-General of the royal army in Ireland. For the next eight years, he navigated the treacherous currents of the Confederate Wars — fighting the Catholic Confederates when necessary, negotiating with them when expedient, and always striving to preserve Ireland for the King against the encroaching power of the English Parliament. (067-121)

In 1649, with the King executed and Cromwell's invasion imminent, Ormond attempted a last-ditch alliance of Confederate Catholics and Protestant royalists to defend Dublin. The plan unravelled when Colonel Michael Jones, the Parliamentarian commander, routed Ormond's forces at the Battle of Rathmines in August 1649 — a defeat that left the road to Dublin open for Cromwell. (067-122)

## Exile and Restoration

Following the Cromwellian conquest, Ormond followed Charles II into exile on the Continent. He spent the Interregnum years shuttling between the exiled court and various European capitals, a living link between the Stuart cause and the Irish interest. At the Restoration in 1660, Charles II rewarded his loyalty handsomely: Ormond was created Duke of Ormond in the Irish peerage and Duke of Ormonde in the English peerage, appointed Lord Lieutenant of Ireland, and showered with restored estates. (067-123)

His second viceroyalty (1662–1669) was marked by the delicate task of implementing the Act of Settlement, which sought to balance the claims of Cromwellian planters against those of dispossessed royalists. Ormond's instinct was to favour the old English Catholic interest where possible, but the political reality of a Protestant Parliament in Dublin constrained his latitude. (067-124)

## Later Career and Character

Ormond's third and final viceroyalty (1677–1685) saw him grappling with the aftershocks of the Popish Plot hysteria and the renewed assault on Catholic civil rights. Though a Protestant himself, Ormond resisted the more extreme demands of the exclusionists and sought to preserve a measure of stability in the kingdom. His removal from office by James II in 1685, and his death in 1688 on the eve of the Glorious Revolution, marked the end of an era. (067-125)

As a courtier of the House of Stuart, it was impossible that Ormond should have served and satisfied both Charleses without making enemies; yet even his adversaries conceded his probity and his genuine affection for the country of his birth. He founded the Royal Hospital at Kilmainham for old soldiers, patronized Trinity College Dublin as its Chancellor, and left behind a reputation for magnanimity rare in the bitter politics of his age. (067-120)
