The **Protestant Ascendancy** was the oligarchic ruling class of Ireland from the late seventeenth century through the eighteenth century, composed primarily of members of the established [[Church of Ireland]] who monopolized political power, land ownership, and social privilege in a country whose population was overwhelmingly Catholic. McGee described it as the "oligarchy, calling itself the 'Protestant Ascendancy,' which had grown so powerful under Cromwell and [[charles-ii|Charles II]]." (085-015)

## Origins

The foundations of the Protestant Ascendancy were laid during the Cromwellian conquest and the subsequent [[Cromwellian Settlement]]. The confiscation of Catholic-owned land and its redistribution to Protestant soldiers and adventurers created a new landowning class whose interests were tied to the English connection. (075-012) This process was reinforced after the [[Williamite War]] when further confiscations under [[William III]] reduced Catholic landownership to a fraction of its pre-war level.

The Ascendancy consolidated its power through the exclusively Protestant [[Irish Parliament]], which enacted the [[Penal Laws]] beginning in 1695. These laws systematically excluded Catholics — and, to a lesser extent, Protestant Dissenters ([[Presbyterian|Presbyterians]]) — from political participation, the legal profession, the military, and higher education. (085-016)

## The Penal Era

Under the Ascendancy's rule, Catholics were barred from sitting in Parliament, holding public office, practicing law, bearing arms, or owning a horse worth more than five pounds. The [[Penal Laws]] also restricted Catholic land ownership, mandated the subdivision of Catholic estates among all sons (unless the eldest converted to Protestantism), and prohibited Catholics from purchasing land or taking long leases.

The Ascendancy's control extended beyond the political sphere into the economic realm. The [[Navigation Acts]] and other trade restrictions favored English commercial interests, and the Ascendancy generally supported these arrangements as the price of English protection of their privileged position.

## Culture and Society

[[dublin|Dublin]] flourished as a Georgian capital under the Ascendancy, with elegant squares, public buildings, and a vibrant intellectual life centered on [[Trinity College Dublin]]. The Ascendancy produced notable figures in literature, philosophy, and politics, including [[Jonathan Swift]], [[Edmund Burke]], and [[Henry Grattan]].

However, this cultural achievement rested on a foundation of sectarian exclusion. The vast majority of the Irish population — Catholic and Dissenter alike — were excluded from the Ascendancy's world by law and by custom.

## Grattan's Parliament and Decline

The late eighteenth century saw the emergence of a "Patriot" faction within the Ascendancy, led by figures such as [[Henry Grattan]], who sought greater legislative independence for Ireland. Grattan's Parliament (1782–1800) represented the high water mark of Ascendancy self-government but remained an exclusively Protestant body.

The [[Rebellion of 1798]] and the subsequent [[Act of Union|Act of Union 1800]] spelled the end of the Ascendancy's political independence. The [[irish-parliament|Irish Parliament]] was abolished, and Irish representatives sat thereafter at Westminster. The Ascendancy survived as a social class but its political monopoly was broken.

## Abolition of the Penal Laws

The Ascendancy's legal foundations were gradually dismantled through a series of Catholic Relief Acts in the late eighteenth century. The final and most significant step came with [[Catholic Emancipation]] in 1829, achieved through the campaigning of [[Daniel O'Connell]] and the [[Catholic Association]]. This removed the last legal barriers to Catholic participation in public life, though the social and economic legacy of the Ascendancy persisted well into the nineteenth century.
