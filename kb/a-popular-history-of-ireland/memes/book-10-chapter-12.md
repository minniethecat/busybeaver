CHAPTER XII.

[087-001]THE IRISH SOLDIERS ABROAD DURING THE REIGNS OF WILLIAM AND ANNE.

[087-002]The close of the second reign from the siege of Limerick imposes the

[087-003]duty of casting our eyes over the map of Europe, in quest of those

[087-004]gallant exiles whom we have seen, in tens of thousands, submitting to

[087-005]the hard necessity of expatriation.

[087-006]Many of the Meath and Leinster Irish, under their native commanders,

[087-007]the Kavanaghs and Nugents, carried their swords into the service of

[087-008]William's ally, the Emperor of Austria, and distinguished themselves in

[087-009]all the campaigns of Prince Eugene. Spain attracted to her standard the

[087-010]Irish of the north-west, the O'Donnells, the O'Reillys, and O'Garas,

[087-011]whose regiments, during more than one reign, continued to be known by

[087-012]flames of Ulster origin. In 1707, the great battle of Almanza, which

[087-013]decided the Spanish succession, was determined by O'Mahony's foot and

[087-014]Fitzjames's Irish horse. The next year Spain had five Irish regiments

[087-015]in her regular army, three of foot and two of dragoons, under the

[087-016]command of Lacy, Lawless, Wogan, O'Reilly, and O'Gara. But it was in

[087-017]France that the Irish served in the greatest number, and made the most

[087-018]impressive history for themselves and their descendants.

[087-019]The recruiting agents of France had long been in the habit of crossing

[087-020]the narrow seas, and bringing back the stalwart sons of the western

[087-021]Island to serve their ambitious kings, in every corner of the

[087-022]continent. An Irish troop of horse served, in 1652, under Turenne,

[087-023]against the great Conde. In the campaigns of 1673, 1674 and 1675, under

[087-024]Turenne, two or three Irish regiments were in every engagement along

[087-025]the Rhine. At Altenheim, their commander, Count Hamilton, was created a

[087-026]major-general of France. In 1690, these old regiments, with the six new

[087-027]ones sent over by James, were formed into a brigade, and from 1690 to

[087-028]1693, they went through the campaigns of Savoy and Italy, under Marshal

[087-029]Catinat, against Prince Eugene. Justin McCarthy, Lord Mountcashel, who

[087-030]commanded them, died at Bareges of wounds received at Staffardo. At

[087-031]Marsiglia, they routed, in 1693, the allies, killing Duke Schomberg,

[087-032]son to the Huguenot general who fell at the Boyne.

[087-033]The "New" or Sarsfield's brigade was employed under Luxembourg, against

[087-034]King William, in Flanders, in 1692 and 1693. At Namur and Enghien, they

[087-035]were greatly distinguished, and William more than once sustained heavy

[087-036]loss at their hands. Sarsfield, their brigadier, for these services,

[087-037]was made mareschal-de-camp. At Landen, on the 29th of July, '93, France

[087-038]again triumphed to the cry, "Remember Limerick!" Sarsfield, leading on

[087-039]the fierce pursuers, fell, mortally wounded. Pressing his hand upon the

[087-040]wound, he took it away dripping with blood, and only said, "Oh, that

[087-041]this was for Ireland!"

[087-043]In the war of the Spanish succession, the remnants of both brigades,

[087-044]consolidated into one, served under their favourite leader, the Marshal

[087-045]Duke of Berwick, through nearly all his campaigns in Belgium, Spain and

[087-046]Germany. The third Lord Clare, afterwards Field-Marshal Count Thomond,

[087-047]was by the Duke's side at Phillipsburg, in 1733, when he received his

[087-048]death-wound from the explosion of a mine. These exiled Clare O'Briens

[087-049]commanded for three generations their famous family regiment of

[087-050]dragoons. The first who followed King James abroad died of wounds

[087-051]received at the battle of Ramillies; the third, with better fortune,

[087-052]outlived for nearly thirty years the glorious day of Fontenoy. The

[087-053]Irish cavalry regiments in the service of France were Sheldon's,

[087-054]Galmoy's, Clare's, and Killmallock's; the infantry were known as the

[087-055]regiments of Dublin, Charlemont, Limerick, and Athlone. There were two

[087-056]other infantry regiments, known as Luttrel's and Dorrington's—and a

[087-057]regiment of Irish marines, of which the Grand Prior, Fitzjames, was

[087-058]colonel. During the latter years of Louis XIV., there could not have

[087-059]been less, at any one time, than from 20,000 to 30,000 Irish in his

[087-060]armies, and during the succeeding century, authentic documents exist to

[087-061]prove that 450,000 natives of Ireland died in the military service of

[087-062]France.

[087-063]In the dreary reigns of William, Anne, and the two first Georges, the

[087-064]pride and courage of the disarmed and disinherited population abiding

[087-065]at home, drew new life and vigour from the exploits of their exiled

[087-066]brethren. The channel smuggler and the vagrant ballad-singer kept alive

[087-067]their fame for the lower class of the population, while the memoirs of

[087-068]Marlborough and Eugene, issuing from the Dublin press, communicated

[087-069]authentic accounts of their actions, to the more prejudiced, or better

[087-070]educated. The blows they struck at Landen, at Cremona, and at Almanza,

[087-071]were sensibly felt by every British statesman; when, in the bitterness

[087-072]of defeat, an English King cursed "the laws that deprived him of such

[087-073]subjects," the doom of the penal code was pronounced.

[087-074]The high character of the famous captains of these brigades was not

[087-075]confined to the field of battle. At Paris, Vienna, and Madrid, their

[087-076]wit and courtesy raised them to the favour of princes, over the

[087-077]jealousy of all their rivals. Important civil and diplomatic offices

[087-078]were entrusted to them—embassies of peace and war—the government of

[087-079]provinces, and the highest administrative offices of the state. While

[087-080]their kinsmen in Ireland were declared incapable of filling the

[087-081]humblest public employments, or of exercising the commonest franchise,

[087-082]they met British ambassadors abroad as equals, and checked or

[087-083]countermined the imperial policy of Great Britain. It was impossible

[087-084]that such a contrast of situations should not attract the attention of

[087-085]all thinking men! It was impossible that such reputations should shine

[087-086]before all Europe without reacting powerfully on the fallen fortunes of

[087-087]Ireland!
