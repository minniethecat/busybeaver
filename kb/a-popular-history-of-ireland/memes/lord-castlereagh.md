## Overview

Robert Stewart, Viscount Castlereagh (1769–1822) was the Anglo-Irish statesman who, as Chief Secretary for Ireland, engineered the passage of the [[Act of Union]] through the Irish Parliament in 1800. Rising to become British Foreign Secretary, he was one of the principal architects of the post-Napoleonic settlement at the Congress of Vienna. In Irish history, however, his name is inseparable from the means by which the Union was carried — a mixture of patronage, bribery, and political manipulation that set the pattern for Anglo-Irish relations throughout the nineteenth century. (102-008) (105-045) (108-140)

## Early Life and Irish Career

Robert Stewart was born into a prominent Ulster landowning family, the son of the first Marquess of Londonderry. He entered the Irish House of Commons in 1790 and quickly established himself as a capable administrator. During the Rebellion of 1798, he served as Chief Secretary to Lord Cornwallis and was intimately involved in the government's response to the rising. (102-008) (107-044)

> "Lord Castlereagh was stealthy, imperturbable, insidious, bland, imperturbable in the cabinet, in the senate, in the drawing-room." (105-072)

## The Act of Union

### Engineering the Union

Following the Rebellion of 1798, the British government under [[William Pitt the Younger|William Pitt]] resolved to abolish the Irish Parliament and unite the two kingdoms. Castlereagh was the principal agent for carrying the measure through the Irish House of Commons. On the 21st of May 1800, in the Irish Commons, he obtained leave to introduce the Bill of Union. His management involved the distribution of peerages, offices, and compensation to borough-owners whose patronage would be extinguished by the Union. (106-132) (106-146)

> "On the 21st of May, in the Irish Commons, Lord Castlereagh obtained leave to introduce the Bill." (106-132)

The measure passed in the face of fierce opposition from [[Henry Grattan]], who emerged from retirement to denounce the Union as the death of Irish legislative independence. Castlereagh's management, however, proved decisive. By the summer of 1800, the Irish Parliament had voted itself out of existence. (105-249) (106-018)

### The Means of Persuasion

Castlereagh employed a network of patronage including the Archbishops of Dublin and Tuam. In the Commons, he congratulated the country on the prospect of the Union as an address was prepared. The purchase of parliamentary seats — putting boroughs "into the market overt" — became a central feature of his strategy. Together with Secretary Cooke, he managed the funds allocated for securing the necessary votes. (106-032) (097-180)

## British Political Career

After the Union, Castlereagh entered British politics. He became Secretary of State for War and later Foreign Secretary. Together with George Canning, he was one of the two principal Secretaries of State directing British policy during the Napoleonic Wars. At the Congress of Vienna in 1815, he helped shape the settlement that redrew the map of Europe. (108-140)

## Legacy in Ireland

Castlereagh's legacy in Irish history is deeply contested. To his admirers, he was a statesman of the first rank who secured the Union at a moment of grave danger to the British connection. To his critics, he was the man who "took means to make it explode" — a manipulator who corrupted the Irish Parliament and set back the cause of Irish self-government for more than a century. His methods became the template for later Unionist management of Irish affairs. (102-223) (110-122)

> "Lord Castlereagh, having attained the highest honours of the state, died by his own hand in 1822." (113-083)
