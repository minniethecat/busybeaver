CHAPTER II.

[077-001]REIGN OF CHARLES II. (CONCLUDED.)

[077-002]For the third time, the aged Ormond, now arrived at the period usually

[077-003]allotted to the life of man, returned to Ireland, with the rank of

[077-004]Viceroy. During the ensuing seven years, he clung to power with all the

[077-005]tenacity of his youth, and all the policy of his prime; they were seven

[077-006]years of extraordinary sectarian panic and excitement—the years of the

[077-007]Cabal, the Popish plot, and the Exclusion Bill, in England—and of

[077-008]fanatical conspiracies and explosions almost as dangerous in Ireland.

[077-009]The Popish plot mania held possession of the English people much longer

[077-010]than any other moral epidemic of equal virulence. In the month of

[077-011]October, 1678, its alleged existence in Ireland was communicated to

[077-012]Ormond; in July, 1681, its most illustrious victim, Archbishop

[077-013]Plunkett, perished on the scaffold at Tyburn. Within these two points

[077-014]of time what a chronicle of madness, folly, perjury, and cruelty, might

[077-015]be written?

[077-016]Ormond, too old in statecraft to believe in the existence of these

[077-017]incredible plots, was also too well aware of the dangerous element of

[077-018]fanaticism represented by Titus Oates, and his imitators, to subject

[077-019]himself to suspicion. On the first intelligence of the plot, he

[077-020]instantly issued his proclamation for the arrest of Archbishop Talbot,

[077-021]of Dublin, who had been permitted to return from exile under the rule

[077-022]of Lord Berkely, and had since resided with his brother, Colonel

[077-023]Talbot, at Cartown, near Maynooth. This prelate was of Ormond's own

[077-024]age, and of a family as ancient; while his learning, courage, and

[077-025]morality, made him an ornament to his order. He was seized in his sick

[077-026]bed at Cartown, carried to Dublin in a chair, and confined a close

[077-027]prisoner in the castle, where he died two years later. He was the last

[077-028]distinguished captive destined to end his days in that celebrated state

[077-029]prison, which has since been generally dedicated to the peaceful

[077-030]purposes of reflected royalty.

[077-032]Colonel Talbot was at the same time arrested, but allowed to retire

[077-033]beyond seas; Lord Mountgarrett, an octogenarian, and in his dotage, was

[077-034]seized, but nothing could be made out against him; a Colonel Peppard

[077-035]was also denounced from England, but no such person was found to exist.

[077-036]So far the first year of the plot had passed over, and proved nothing

[077-037]against the Catholic Irish. But the example of successful villainy in

[077-038]England, of Oates idolized, pensioned, and all-powerful, extended to

[077-039]the sister kingdom, and brought an illustrious victim to the scaffold.

[077-040]This was Oliver Plunkett, a scion of the noble family of Fingal, who

[077-041]had been Archbishop of Armagh, since the death of Dr. O'Reilly, in

[077-042]exile, in 1669. Such had been the prudence and circumspection of Dr.

[077-043]Plunkett, during his perilous administration, that the agents of Lord

[077-044]Shaftesbury, sent over to concoct evidence for the occasion, were

[077-045]afraid to bring him to trial in the vicinage of his arrest, or in his

[077-046]own country. Accordingly, they caused him to be removed from Dublin to

[077-047]London, contrary to the laws and customs of both Kingdoms, which had

[077-048]first been violated towards state prisoners in the case of Lord

[077-049]Maguire, forty years before.

[077-050]Dr. Plunkett, after ten months' confinement without trial in Ireland,

[077-051]was removed, 1680, and arraigned at London, on the 8th of June, 1681,

[077-052]without having had permission to communicate with his friends or to

[077-053]send for witnesses. The prosecution was conducted by Maynard and

[077-054]Jeffries, in violation of every form of law, and every consideration of

[077-055]justice. A "crown agent," whose name is given as Gorman, was introduced

[077-056]by "a stranger" in court, and volunteered testimony in his favour. The

[077-057]Earl of Essex interceded with the King on his behalf, but Charles

[077-058]answered, almost in the words of Pilate—"I cannot pardon him, because I

[077-059]dare not. His blood be upon your conscience; you could have saved him

[077-060]if you pleased." The Jury, after a quarter of an hour's deliberation,

[077-061]brought in their verdict of guilty, and the brutal Chief-Justice

[077-062]condemned him to be hung, emboweled, and quartered on the 1st day of

[077-063]July, 1681. The venerable martyr, for such he may well be called, bowed

[077-064]his head to the bench, and exclaimed: _Deo gratias!_ Eight years from

[077-065]the very day of his execution, on the banks of that river beside which

[077-066]he had been seized and dragged from his retreat, the last of the Stuart

[077-067]kings was stricken from his throne, and his dynasty stricken from

[077-068]history! Does not the blood of the innocent cry to Heaven for

[077-069]vengeance?

[077-070]The charges against Dr. Plunkett were, that he maintained treasonable

[077-071]correspondence with France and Rome, and the Irish on the continent;

[077-072]that he had organised an insurrection in Louth, Monaghan, Cavan, and

[077-073]Armagh; that he made preparations for the landing of a French force at

[077-074]Carlingford; and that he had held several meetings to raise men for

[077-075]these purposes. Utterly absurd and false as these charges were, they

[077-076]still indicate the troubled apprehensions which filled the dreams of

[077-077]the ascendency party. The fear of French invasion, of new

[077-078]insurrections, of the resumption of estates, haunted them by night and

[077-079]day. Every sign was to them significant of danger, and every rumour of

[077-080]conspiracy was taken for fact. The report of a strange fleet off the

[077-081]Southern coast, which turned out to be English, threw them all into

[077-082]panic; and the Corpus Christi crosses which the peasantry affixed to

[077-083]their doors, were nothing but signs for the Papist destroyer to pass

[077-084]by, and to spare his fellows in the general massacre of Protestants.

[077-085]Under the pressure of these panics, real or pretended, proclamation

[077-086]after proclamation issued from the Castle. By one of these instruments,

[077-087]Ormond prohibited Catholics from entering the Castle of Dublin, or any

[077-088]other fortress; from holding fairs or markets within the walls of

[077-089]corporate towns, and from carrying arms to such resorts. By another, he

[077-090]declared all relatives of known _Tories_—a Gaelic term for a driver of

[077-091]prey—to be arrested, and banished the kingdom, within fourteen days,

[077-092]unless such Tories were killed, or surrendered, within that time. Where

[077-093]this device failed to reach the destined victims—as in the celebrated

[077-094]case of Count Redmond O'Hanlon—it is to be feared that he did not

[077-095]hesitate to whet the dagger of the assassin, which was still sometimes

[077-096]employed, even in the British Islands, to remove a dangerous

[077-097]antagonist. Count O'Hanlon, a gentleman of ancient lineage, as

[077-098]accomplished as Orrery, or Ossory, was indeed an outlaw to the code

[077-099]then in force; but the stain of his cowardly assassination must for

[077-100]ever blot and rot the princely escutcheon of James, Duke of Ormond.

[077-101]The violence of religious and social persecution began to subside

[077-102]during the last two or three years of Charles II. Monmouth's

[077-103]banishment, Shaftesbury's imprisonment, the execution of Russell and

[077-104]Sidney on the scaffold, marked the return of the English public mind to

[077-105]political pursuits and objects. Early in 1685, the king was taken

[077-106]mortally ill. In his last moments he received the rites of the Catholic

[077-107]church, from the hands of Father Huddleston, who was said to have saved

[077-108]his life at the battle of Worcester, and who was now even more anxious

[077-109]to save his soul.

[077-110]This event took place on the 16th of February. King James was

[077-111]immediately proclaimed successor to his brother. One of his first acts

[077-112]was to recall Ormond from Ireland and to appoint in his place the Earl

[077-113]of Clarendon, son of the historian and statesman of the Restoration.

[077-114]Ormond obeyed, not without regret; he survived his fall about three

[077-115]years. He was interred in Westminster in 1688, three months before the

[077-116]landing of William, and the second banishment of the Stuarts.
