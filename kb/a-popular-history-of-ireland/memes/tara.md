## Overview

Tara (Teamhair or Temair) was the ancient seat of the High Kings (Ard-Righ) of Ireland and the most important royal and ceremonial site in pre-Christian and early Christian Ireland. Located in present-day County Meath, in the province of [[leinster|Leinster]], Tara was the political and symbolic center of the Irish nation for over a thousand years. (004-044)

According to tradition, every High King was required to observe certain rituals at Tara, the most famous of which was that "the sun must never rise on him in his bed at Tara"—a prohibition that underscored the king's obligation to be abroad and vigilant. (004-044)

## History

### The Seat of the High Kings

For centuries, Tara was the inauguration site of the High Kings. Each king who succeeded to the throne was expected to take possession of Tara as the visible symbol of his sovereignty. The rituals associated with the kingship, including the "Feast of Tara" (Feis Temrach), bound the provinces to the central authority through ceremonial tribute and renewal of allegiance. (004-118) (004-177)

### The Arrival of Christianity

Tara acquired a different significance with the coming of Christianity. It was at Tara that [[saint-patrick|Saint Patrick]] confronted the pagan establishment, kindling the Paschal fire at nearby Slane in defiance of Druidical law and preaching before King Laoghaire and his court. The conversion of the king's daughters and the Arch-Druid himself at Tara marked a turning point in the Christianization of Ireland. (003-110) (003-115)

### The Desolation of Tara

In the year 554 AD, Tara was famously cursed by Saint Ruadan and abandoned as a royal residence. According to tradition, King Dermid (Diarmait mac Cerbaill) had violated the sanctuary of Saint Ruadan, and the saint responded by walking around the royal rath with his clergy and pronouncing a public malediction. From that day, Tara was left desolate as a royal residence, though it retained its symbolic importance. (004-167) (004-173) (004-183)

Later tradition held that [[saint-patrick|Saint Patrick]] himself had laid a curse on Tara, promising dominion to Cashel and [[dublin|Dublin]] instead—a legend that reflected the shifting political geography of medieval Ireland as the southern and eastern powers eclipsed the ancient central monarchy. (004-186)

## Description

Tara is a low hill rising from the fertile plain of Meath. The site contains a complex of ancient earthworks, including:

- **The Forradh** (Royal Seat): A large ring-fort traditionally identified as the king's residence
- **The Banqueting Hall** (Teach Miodhchuarta): A long rectangular earthwork, said to have been the great hall where the Feis Temrach was held
- **The Rath of the Synods**: Associated with early Christian gatherings
- **The Mound of the Hostages** (Dumha na nGiall): A Neolithic passage tomb reused in later eras as a site for the exchange of royal hostages
- **Lia Fáil**: The Stone of Destiny, which according to legend cried out when touched by the rightful king

The site required constant maintenance in the royal era. Female slaves performed the menial offices about the palace, and the complex included granaries, armouries, and quarters for the king's retinue. (004-093)

## Legacy

Though abandoned as a royal seat after the sixth century, Tara remained the supreme symbol of Irish sovereignty in national memory. Medieval poets and annalists looked back to the age of the High Kings at Tara as a golden age of unity and independence. The hill gave its name to the Barony of Tara and to the title held by the descendants of the Gaelic nobility.

Even in modern times, Tara retains its power as a national symbol. [[daniel-oconnell|Daniel O'Connell]] chose Tara for one of his great "monster meetings" during the Repeal campaign, deliberately invoking the ancient seat of Irish kingship to legitimize the demand for legislative independence.
