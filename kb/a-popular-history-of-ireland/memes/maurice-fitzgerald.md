Maurice Fitzgerald was a Norman-Welsh nobleman and one of the principal leaders of the first Anglo-Norman expedition into Ireland. He was the son of the legendary Nesta, daughter of Rhys ap Tudor, and Gerald, constable of Pembroke. Through his mother's earlier liaison with King Henry I of England, Maurice was half-brother to Robert Fitz-Roy, Earl of Gloucester, and Henry Fitz-Henry.

> "Afterwards, by consent of her royal paramour, she married Gerald, constable of Pembroke, by whom she had Maurice Fitzgerald, the common ancestor of the Kildare and Desmond Geraldines." (023-041)

Through his descendants — the Earls of [[Desmond]], the Earls of [[Kildare]], and the Knights of Glyn and Kerry — Maurice Fitzgerald became the progenitor of the most influential Anglo-Norman family in Irish history, the [[Geraldines]].

## Arrival in Ireland

In the winter following the treaty of Ferns (1169), [[Dermid McMurrogh]] — in violation of his agreement to bring no more Normans into the country — welcomed the arrival of Maurice Fitzgerald at [[Wexford]]. Fitzgerald landed with a fresh force of ten knights, thirty esquires, and one hundred footmen.

> "In the course of the same autumn or winter, in which this agreement was solemnly entered into, he welcomed the arrival at Wexford—of Maurice Fitzgerald—son of the fair Nesta by her first husband—and immediately employed this fresh force, consisting of 10 knights, 30 esquires, and 100 footmen, upon a hosting which harried the open country about Dublin." (025-008)

This expedition induced the inhabitants around [[Dublin]] to send hostages to Dermid's camp, bearing proffers of allegiance, though Fitzgerald's force was not yet sufficient to assault the city itself.

## Rescue of Hugh de Lacy

During the early Norman campaigns, Fitzgerald played a decisive role in rescuing [[Hugh de Lacy]] from an ambush by [[Tiernan O'Ruarc]], the chief of Breffni. De Lacy, twice felled in attempting flight, was saved when Fitzgerald and his nephew Griffith led a counter-attack.

> "De Lacy, in attempting to fly, had been twice felled to the earth, when his followers, under Maurice Fitzgerald and Griffith, his nephew, came to his rescue, and assailed the chief of Breffni. It was now Tiernan's turn to attempt escaping, but as he mounted his horse the spear of Griffith brought him to the earth mortally wounded." (027-039)

Tiernan's head was afterwards carried in triumph to Dublin and spiked over the northern gate of the city.

## Lord Justice and Later Campaigns

Fitzgerald rose to positions of high authority under the Crown. As second Lord Offally, he served as Lord Justice of Ireland and received direct orders from King Henry III. When Feidlim O'Conor complained to the English Court about encroachments on his territory, the King wrote peremptorily to Fitzgerald.

> "The King was so impressed with O'Conor's representations that he wrote peremptorily to Maurice Fitzgerald, second Lord Offally, then his deputy, 'to root out that barren tree planted in Offally by Hubert de Burgh, in the madness of his power, and not to suffer it to shoot forth.'" (030-143)

In 1247, Fitzgerald led a military expedition into the north against the O'Donnells. His army crossed the Erne in two divisions, taking Melaghlin O'Donnell unexpectedly in the rear.

> "In the year 1247, an army under Maurice Fitzgerald, then Lord Justice, crossed the Erne in two divisions, one above and the other at Ballyshannon. Melaghlin O'Donnell was defending the passage of the river when he was taken unexpectedly in the rear." (032-143)

## Legacy and the Geraldine Dynasty

Maurice Fitzgerald died at [[Wexford]] in the year 1177, but his posterity transformed the political landscape of Ireland.

> "Maurice Fitzgerald, the common ancestor of the Earls of Desmond and Kildare, the Knights of Glyn, of Kerry, and of all the Irish Geraldines, died at Wexford in the year 1177." (027-179)

His descendants became the most powerful Anglo-Norman family in the country. The Earls of Desmond ruled vast territories in [[Munster]], while the Earls of Kildare became the political mainstay of English rule in [[Leinster]]. A later descendant, Sir James Fitz-Maurice, led a major insurrection against the Elizabethan administration in the sixteenth century.

> "Sir James, son of Sir Maurice Fitzgerald of Kerry, commonly called James Fitz-Maurice, cousin-germain to the imprisoned noblemen, was chosen leader of the insurrection." (055-200)

Through his mother Nesta — whose beauty had captivated a king — and through his own military service, Maurice Fitzgerald stood at the intersection of Welsh, Norman, and Irish history. The family he founded became, in the words of Irish historians, as Irish as the Irish themselves.
