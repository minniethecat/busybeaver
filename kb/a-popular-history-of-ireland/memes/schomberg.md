## Overview

Frederick Schomberg, Duke of Schomberg (1615–1690), was one of the most experienced soldiers in Europe when he took command of the Williamite army in Ireland in 1689. A German-born Protestant who had served under the flags of Sweden, France, Portugal, and Brandenburg, Schomberg brought a lifetime of military experience to the campaign against [[James II]]. He led the Williamite forces through a difficult first year in Ireland and fell in the hour of victory at the Battle of the Boyne. (081-152) (082-081)

Schomberg's tenure in Ireland was marked by the disastrous camp at Dundalk, where disease and privation destroyed a significant portion of his army, but his leadership laid the groundwork for [[William III]]'s decisive campaign the following year. (081-174)

## European Career

Before arriving in Ireland, Schomberg had already compiled an extraordinary military career spanning half a century. A veteran of the Thirty Years' War, he rose to the rank of Marshal of France under Louis XIV. As a Huguenot, the Revocation of the Edict of Nantes forced him into exile, and he transferred his services to [[William III]], who created him Duke of Schomberg. At the age of seventy-three, he was entrusted with the Irish command. (082-013)

## The Irish Campaign of 1689

Schomberg landed in Ireland in August 1689 with a force of some 10,000 men. His army included "the famous Huguenot regiments of Schomberg, La Millinier, Du Cambon, and La Callimotte" — fellow Protestant exiles from France who had followed their commander into William's service. The campaign began cautiously: "Schomberg, with Count Solmes as second in command," advanced southwards from Ulster into Leinster, while the Jacobite army under [[James II]] fell back before him. (081-152) (081-157) (082-013)

> "Schomberg faced southward towards Dublin, the Jacobite army retiring before him." (081-157)

## The Camp at Dundalk

The pivotal event of Schomberg's command was the camp at Dundalk in the autumn of 1689. Encamped on low-lying ground through a wet season, the Williamite army was ravaged by disease. Hundreds died weekly from fever, dysentery, and exposure. "Schomberg, whose fatal camp at Dundalk," became a byword for the perils of autumn campaigning in Ireland. Despite the losses, Schomberg maintained discipline, held his position, and preserved the army as a fighting force through the winter. (081-174)

> "Schomberg, whose fatal camp at Dundalk, cost the lives of thousands of his best soldiers." (081-174)

## The Battle of the Boyne

On 1 July 1690, Schomberg led the Williamite centre across the Boyne at Oldbridge. The crossing was fiercely contested by the Jacobite infantry. "The elder Schomberg, as the hour of noon approached," seeing his men wavering under heavy fire, placed himself at their head and urged them forward. In the moment of assault, he was cut down. "Schomberg and Callimotte fell in the centre of the river." His death, though a loss to the army, became a rallying cry for the Williamite cause. (082-079) (082-081) (082-088)

His son, the younger Schomberg, was also mortally wounded in the same action. The double loss was deeply felt by William III, who nevertheless pressed the attack to victory. (082-079)

## Legacy

Schomberg's Irish campaign illustrated the immense difficulties of warfare in seventeenth-century Ireland — logistical, epidemiological, and tactical. His grave in Christ Church Cathedral, Dublin, became a monument to the Williamite cause. Later generations remembered him as a soldier who, though not Irish by birth, gave his life in the struggle that decided the fate of the Irish Crown. (087-031)
