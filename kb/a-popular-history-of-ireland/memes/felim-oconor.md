## Overview

Felim O'Conor (c. 1295–1316) was King of Connaught during the Scottish invasion of Ireland led by Edward Bruce. He was one of the most significant Gaelic allies of the Bruce campaign, mustering the largest Connaught army since the days of Cathal More and leading a sustained war against the Anglo-Norman settlers of the west. (037-057)

His career illustrates the complex dynamics of 14th-century Ireland: a Gaelic prince who had been compelled to serve the Red Earl of Ulster before seceding to the national cause and driving the foreigners from his province. (037-058)

## The O'Conor Lordship in Connaught

The O'Conors had established themselves as the dominant family in the west of Ireland from the 12th century. After the death of Brian Boru, the O'Conors of the west competed with both O'Neills and O'Briens for supremacy, and their house provided the last High King of Ireland before the Norman invasion. (014-008)

The family took its name from Conor (Conchobhar), from whom the O'Conors derive their patronymic. As one of the five great families that dominated Irish history after the death of Malachy II — O'Neils, O'Melaghlins, McMurroghs, O'Briens and O'Conors — their provincial ambitions shaped the political landscape for centuries. (015-071) (015-073)

By the early 14th century, Connaught had come largely under the sway of the de Burgh family, whose lordship extended across the Shannon. Gaelic princes like Felim O'Conor were obliged to furnish military service to the Anglo-Norman Earl of Ulster. (036-147)

## Alliance with the Red Earl

At the outset of the Bruce invasion in 1315, Felim O'Conor was among the Gaelic lords compelled to serve in the army of Richard de Burgh, the Red Earl of Ulster. When the Earl mustered his forces at Athlone to oppose the Scottish landing, he compelled the unwilling attendance of Felim O'Conor, with his clansmen. (036-146) (036-147)

> The Earl, on hearing of the landing of the Scots, had mustered his forces at Athlone, and compelled the unwilling attendance of Felim O'Conor, with his clansmen. [036-147]

The combined army — some 25,000 or 30,000 men — marched towards Drogheda, but Edward Bruce retreated before such overwhelming numbers. De Burgh's force, though massive, failed to bring the Scots to battle. (036-149) (036-151)

## Secession to the National Cause

One of the first significant successes obtained by Edward Bruce was through the withdrawal of Felim O'Conor from the Red Earl's alliance. The Prince, won over to what may be fairly called the national cause, had just then attained his majority. His martial accomplishments reflected honour on his fosterer, McDermott of Moylurg, while they filled with confidence the hearts of his own clansmen. (037-057) (037-058) (037-060)

> One of the first successes obtained by Bruce was through the withdrawal of Felim O'Conor from the Red Earl's alliance. The Prince thus won over to what may be fairly called the national cause, had just then attained his majority, and his martial accomplishments reflected honour on his fosterer, McDermott of Moylurg. [037-058] [037-060]

After his secession from de Burgh at Coleraine, Felim spent a whole year suppressing a rival who had risen to dispute his title. The pretender, Roderick, was defeated and slain, after which Felim turned all his energies to co-operating with Bruce by driving the foreigner out of his own province. (037-062) (037-065) (037-066)

## The War in Connaught

Having secured the assistance of all the chief tribes of the west and re-established the ancient supremacy of his house over Breffni, Felim launched a sustained offensive against the Anglo-Norman settlements. He first attacked the town of Ballylahen in Mayo, the seat of the family of de Exeter, slaying Slevin de Exeter, the lord de Cogan, and other knights and barons, and plundered the town. (037-068) (037-069) (037-070)

In August of the same year, Felim mustered the most numerous force which Connaught had sent forth since the days of Cathal More. Under his leadership marched the Prince of Meath, the lords of Breffni, Leyny, Annally, Teffia, Hy-Many, and Hy-Fiachra, with their men. The target was Athenry, the chief fortified stronghold of the de Burghs. (037-073) (037-074) (037-075) (037-077)

## Earlier Felim O'Conors

The name Felim (Feidlim) recurs throughout O'Conor history. An earlier Felim O'Conor, in the year 1225, was assisted by Donogh Cairbre to resist the Anglo-Norman army then pouring over Connaught in the quarrel of de Burgh. (031-195)

Another Felim O'Conor fought the sons of Roderick at the battle of Drumraitte in 1237, where he famously ordered his men "not to shoot but to come to a close fight" — a tactical instruction that reveals the changing nature of Irish warfare in the face of Norman military technology. (033-200) (033-201)

## Legacy

Felim O'Conor's career demonstrates the capacity of Gaelic lords to unite broad coalitions against the Anglo-Norman presence in Ireland during the early 14th century. His ability to rally the lords of Meath, Breffni, Leyny, Annally, Teffia, Hy-Many, and Hy-Fiachra under a single banner represented a significant challenge to de Burgh hegemony in the west. (037-075)

The O'Conor alliance with Edward Bruce, though ultimately unsuccessful, represented a strategic alignment of Gaelic and Scottish interests against their common Anglo-Norman adversaries. Felim stands as one of the notable Gaelic commanders of the Bruce wars in Ireland. (037-057)
