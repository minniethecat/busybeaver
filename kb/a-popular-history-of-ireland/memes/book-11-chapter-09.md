CHAPTER IX.

[096-001]THE ERA OF INDEPENDENCE—FIRST PERIOD.

[096-002]The accession of the Rockingham administration to power, in 1782, was

[096-003]followed by the recall of Lord Carlisle, and the substitution, as

[096-004]Viceroy, of one of the leading Lords of the Whig party. The nobleman

[096-005]selected to this office was William Henry, third Duke of Portland,

[096-006]afterwards twice prime minister; then in the prime of life, possessed

[096-007]of a very ample fortune, and uniting in his own person the two great

[096-008]Whig families of Bentinck and Cavendish. The policy he was sent to

[096-009]represent at Dublin was undoubtedly an imperial policy; a policy which

[096-010]looked as anxiously to the integrity of the empire as any Tory cabinet

[096-011]could have desired; but it was, in most other respects, a policy of

[096-012]conciliation and concession, dictated by the enlarged wisdom of Burke,

[096-013]and adopted by the magnanimous candour of Fox. Yet by a generous

[096-014]people, who always find it more difficult to resist a liberal than an

[096-015]illiberal administration, it was, in reality, a policy more to be

[096-016]feared than welcomed; for its almost certain effects were to divide

[096-017]their ranks into two sections—a moderate and an extreme party—between

[096-018]whom the national cause, only half established, might run great danger

[096-019]of being lost, almost as soon as it was won.

[096-020]With the Duke of Portland was associated, as Chief Secretary, Colonel

[096-021]Fitzpatrick, of the old Ossory family, one of those Irish wits and men

[096-022]of fashion, who form so striking a group in the middle and later years

[096-023]of King George III. As the personal and political friend of Flood,

[096-024]Charlemont, and Grattan, and the first Irish secretary for several

[096-025]administrations, he shared the brilliant ovation with which the Duke of

[096-026]Portland was received, on his arrival at Dublin; but for the reason

[096-027]already mentioned, the imperial, in so far as opposed to the national

[096-028]policy, found an additional advantage in the social successes and great

[096-029]personal popularity of the new secretary.

[096-030]The critical months which decided the contest for independence—April

[096-031]and May—passed over fortunately for Ireland. The firmness of the

[096-032]leaders in both Houses, the energy especially of Grattan, whose cry was

[096-033]"No time, no time!" and the imposing attitude of the volunteers,

[096-034]carried the question. Lord Rockingham and Mr. Fox by letter, the new

[096-035]Viceroy and Secretary in person, had urged every argument for

[096-036]adjournment and delay, but Grattan's _ultimatum_ was sent over to

[096-037]England, and finally and formally accepted. The demands were _five_. I.

[096-038]The repeal of the 6th of George I. II. The repeal of the Perpetual

[096-039]Mutiny Act. III. An Act to abolish the alteration or suppression of

[096-040]Bills. IV. An Act to establish the final jurisdiction of the Irish

[096-041]Courts and the Irish House of Lords. V. The repeal of Poyning's Law.

[096-042]This was the constitutional charter of 1782, which restored Ireland,

[096-043]for the first time in that century, to the rank and dignity of a free

[096-044]nation.

[096-045]Concession once determined on, the necessary bills were introduced in

[096-046]both Parliaments simultaneously, and carried promptly into law. On the

[096-047]27th of May, the Irish Houses were enabled to congratulate the Viceroy

[096-048]that "no constitutional question any longer existed between the two

[096-049]countries." In England it was proclaimed no less explicitly by Fox and

[096-050]his friends, that the independency of the two legislatures "was fixed

[096-051]and ascertained for ever." But there was, unfortunately, one ground for

[096-052]dispute still left, and on that ground Henry Flood and Henry Grattan

[096-053]parted, never to be reconciled.

[096-054]The elder Patriot, whose conduct from the moment of his retirement from

[096-055]office, in consequence of his Free Trade vote and speech in '79, had

[096-056]been, with occasional exceptions, arising mostly from bodily infirmity,

[096-057]as energetic and consistent as that of Grattan himself, saw no

[096-058]sufficient constitutional guarantee in mere acts of Parliament

[096-059]repealing other acts. He demanded "express renunciation" of legislative

[096-060]supremacy on the part of England; while Grattan maintained the

[096-061]sufficiency of "simple repeal." It is possible even in such noble

[096-062]natures as these men had—so strangely are we constituted—that there was

[096-063]a latent sense of personal rivalry, which prompted them to grasp, each,

[096-064]at the larger share of patriotic honour. It is possible that there were

[096-065]other, and inferior men, who exasperated this latent personal rivalry.

[096-066]Flood had once reigned supreme, until Grattan eclipsed him in the

[096-067]sudden splendour of his career. In scholarship and in genius the elder

[096-068]Patriot was, taken all in all, the full peer of his successor; but

[096-069]Grattan had the national temperament, and he found his way more readily

[096-070]into the core of the national heart; he was the man of the later, the

[096-071]bolder, and the more liberal school; and such was the rapidity of his

[096-072]movements, that even Flood, from '79 to '82, seemed to be his follower,

[096-073]rather than his coadjutor. In the hopeful crisis of the struggle, the

[096-074]slower and more experienced statesman was for the moment lost sight of.

[096-075]The leading motions were all placed or left in the hands of Grattan by

[096-076]the consent of their leading friends; the bills repealing the Mutiny

[096-077]Act, the 6th George I., and Poyning's law, were entrusted to Burgh,

[096-078]Yelverton, and Forbes; the thanks of the House were voted to Grattan

[096-079]alone after the victory, with the substantial addition of 50,000 pounds

[096-080]to purchase for him an estate, which should become an enduring monument

[096-081]of the national gratitude.

[096-082]The open rupture between the two great orators followed fast on the

[096-083]triumph of their common efforts. It was still the first month—the very

[096-084]honeymoon of independence. On the 13th of June, Mr. Grattan took

[096-085]occasion to notice in his place, that a late British act relating to

[096-086]the importation of sugars, was so generally worded as apparently to

[096-087]include Ireland; but this was explained to be a mere error of the

[096-088]clerk, the result of haste, and one which would be promptly corrected.

[096-089]Upon this Mr. Flood first took occasion to moot the insufficiency of

[096-090]"simple repeal," and the necessity of "express renunciation," on the

[096-091]part of England. On the 19th, he moved a formal resolution on the

[096-092]subject, which was superseded by the order of the day; but on the 19th

[096-093]of July, he again moved, at great length, and with great power of

[096-094]logical and historical argument, for leave to bring in an Irish Bill of

[096-095]Rights, declaring "the sole and exclusive right of the Irish Parliament

[096-096]to make laws in all cases whatsoever, _external and internal_." He was

[096-097]supported by Sir Simon Bradstreet, Mr. English, and Mr. Walshe, and

[096-098]opposed by Grattan, who, in one of his finest efforts, proposed a

[096-099]counter resolution, "that the legislature of Ireland is independent;

[096-100]and that any person who shall, by writing or otherwise, maintain that a

[096-101]right in any other country, to make laws for Ireland, _internally_ or

[096-102]_externally_, exists or can be revived, _is inimical to the peace of

[096-103]both kingdoms_." This extreme proposition—pointing out all who differed

[096-104]from himself as public enemies—the mover, however, withdrew, and

[096-105]substituted in its stead the milder formula, that leave was refused to

[096-106]bring in the bill, because the sole and exclusive right of legislation

[096-107]in the Irish Parliament in all cases, whether externally or internally,

[096-108]hath been already asserted by Ireland, and fully, finally, and

[096-109]irrevocably acknowledged by the British Parliament. Upon this motion

[096-110]Flood did not think it advisable to divide the House, so it passed

[096-111]without a division.

[096-113]But the moot point thus voted down in Parliament disquieted and alarmed

[096-114]the minds of many out of doors. The volunteers as generally sided with

[096-115]Flood as the Parliament had sided with Grattan. The lawyer corps of the

[096-116]city of Dublin, containing all the great names of the legal profession,

[096-117]endorsed the constitutional law of the member for Kilkenny; the Belfast

[096-118]volunteers did likewise; and Grattan's own corps, in a respectful

[096-119]address, urged him to give his adherence to the views of "the best

[096-120]informed body of men in the kingdom,"—the lawyers' corps. Just at that

[096-121]moment Lord Abingdon, in the English House of Lords, gave notice of a

[096-122]mischievous motion to assert the external supremacy of the English

[096-123]Parliament; and Lord Mansfield, in the King's Bench, decided an Irish

[096-124]appeal case, notwithstanding the recent statute establishing the

[096-125]judicial independence of the Irish courts. It is true the case had been

[096-126]appealed before the statute was passed; and that Lord Abingdon withdrew

[096-127]his motion for want of a seconder; but the alarm was given, and the

[096-128]popular mind in Ireland, jealously watchful of its new-born liberties,

[096-129]saw in these attempts renewed cause for apprehension. In opposition to

[096-130]all this suddenly awakened suspicion and jealousy, Grattan, who

[096-131]naturally enough assumed his own interest in preserving the new

[096-132]constitution to be quite equal to those who cast doubts on its

[096-133]security, invariably held one language. The settlement already made,

[096-134]according to his view, was final; it was an international treaty; its

[096-135]maintenance must depend on the ability and disposition of the parties

[096-136]to uphold it, rather than on the multiplication of declaratory acts.

[096-137]Ireland had gone to England with a charter, not for a charter, and the

[096-138]nation which would insist upon the humiliation of another, was a

[096-139]foolish nation. This was the lofty light in which he viewed the whole

[096-140]transaction, and in this light, it must be added, he continued to view

[096-141]it till the last. Many of the chief English and Irish jurists of his

[096-142]time, Lord Camden, Lord Kenyon, Lord Erskine, Lord Kilwarden, Judges

[096-143]Chamberlain, Smith, and Kelly, Sir Samuel Rommilly, Sir Arthur Pigott,

[096-144]and several others, agreed fully in Grattan's doctrine, that the

[096-145]settlement of '82 was final and absolute, and "terminated all British

[096-146]jurisdiction over Ireland." But although these are all great names, the

[096-147]instinct of national self-preservation may be considered in such

[096-148]critical moments more than a counterpoise to the most matured opinions

[096-149]of the oracles of the law. Such must have been the conviction also of

[096-150]the English Parliament, for, immediately on their meeting in January,

[096-151]1783, they passed the _Act of Renunciation_ (23rd George III.),

[096-152]expressly declaring their admission of the "exclusive rights of the

[096-153]Parliament and Courts of Ireland in matters of legislature and

[096-154]judicature." This was Flood's greatest triumph. Six months before his

[096-155]doctrine obtained but three supporters in the Irish Commons; now, at

[096-156]his suggestion, and on his grounds, he saw it unanimously affirmed by

[096-157]the British Parliament.

[096-158]On two other questions of the utmost importance these leading spirits

[096-159]also widely differed. Grattan was in favour of, and Flood opposed to,

[096-160]Catholic emancipation; while Flood was in favour of, and Grattan, at

[096-161]that moment, opposed to, a complete reform of parliamentary

[096-162]representation. The Catholic question had its next great triumph after

[096-163]Flood's death, as will be mentioned further on; but the history of the

[096-164]Irish reform movement of 1783, '84, and '85, may best be disposed of

[096-165]here.

[096-166]The Reformers were a new party rising naturally out of the popular

[096-167]success of 1782. They were composed of all but a few of the more

[096-168]aristocratic corps of the volunteers, of the townsmen, especially in

[096-169]the seaports and manufacturing towns, of the admirers of American

[096-170]example, of the Catholics who had lately acquired property and

[096-171]recognition, but not the elective franchise, of the gentry of the

[096-172]second and third degree of wealth, overruled and overshadowed by the

[096-173]greater lords of the soil. The substantial grievance of which they

[096-174]complained was, that of the 300 members of the House of Commons, only

[096-175]72 were returned by the people; 53 Peers having the power to nominate

[096-176]123 and secure the election of 10 others; while 52 Commoners nominated

[096-177]91 and controlled the choice of 4 others. The constitution of what

[096-178]ought to have been the people's house was, therefore, substantially in

[096-179]the hands of an oligarchy of about a hundred great proprietors, bound

[096-180]together by the spirit of their class, by intermarriage, and by the

[096-181]hereditary possession of power. To reduce this exorbitant influence

[096-182]within reasonable bounds, was the just and wise design to which Flood

[096-183]dedicated all his energies, after the passage of the _Act of

[096-184]Renunciation_, and the success of which would certainly have restored

[096-185]him to complete equality with Grattan.

[096-186]In the beginning of 1783, the famous coalition ministry of Lord North

[096-187]and Mr. Fox was formed in England. They were at first represented at

[096-188]Dublin Castle, for a few months, by Lord Temple, who succeeded the Duke

[096-189]of Portland, and established the order of _Knights of Saint Patrick_;

[096-190]then by Lord Northington, who dissolved Parliament early in July. A

[096-191]general election followed, and the reform party made their influence

[096-192]felt in all directions. County meetings were held; conventions by

[096-193]districts and by provinces were called by the reforming Volunteers, in

[096-194]July, August, and September. The new Parliament was to be opened on the

[096-195]14th of October, and the Volunteers resolved to call a convention of

[096-196]their whole body at Dublin, for the 10th of November.

[096-197]The Parliament met according to summons, but though searching

[096-198]retrenchment was spoken of, no promise was held out of a constitutional

[096-199]reform; the limitation of the regular troops to a fixed number was

[096-200]declared advisable, and a vote of thanks to the Volunteers was passed

[096-201]without demur. But the proceedings of the Houses were soon eclipsed by

[096-202]the portentous presence of the Volunteer Convention. One hundred and

[096-203]sixty delegates of corps attended on the appointed day. The Royal

[096-204]Exchange was too small to accommodate them, so they adjourned to the

[096-205]Rotunda, accompanied by mounted guards of honour. The splendid and

[096-206]eccentric Bishop of Derry (Earl of Bristol), had his dragoon guards;

[096-207]the courtly but anxious Charlemont had his troop of horse; Flood, tall,

[096-208]emaciated, and solemn to sadness, was hailed with popular acclamations;

[096-209]there also marched the popular Mr. Day, afterwards Judge; Robert

[096-210]Stewart, father of Lord Castlereagh; Sir Richard Musgrave, a reformer

[096-211]also, in his youth, who lived to confound reform with rebellion in his

[096-212]old age. The Earl of Charlemont was elected president of this imposing

[096-213]body, and for an entire month Dublin was divided between the

[096-214]extraordinary spectacle of two legislatures—one sitting at the Rotunda,

[096-215]and the other at College Green, many members of each being members of

[096-216]the other; the uniform of the volunteer sparkling in the Houses, and

[096-217]the familiar voices of both Houses being heard deliberating and

[096-218]debating among the Volunteers.

[096-219]At length, on the 29th of November, after three weeks' laborious

[096-220]gestation, Flood brought before Parliament the plan of reform agreed to

[096-221]by the Convention. It proposed to extend the franchise to every

[096-222]_Protestant_ freeholder possessed of a lease worth forty shillings

[096-223]yearly; to extend restricted borough constituencies by annexing to them

[096-224]neighbouring populous parishes; that the voting should be held on one

[096-225]and the same day; that pensioners of the crown should be incapable of

[096-226]election; that members accepting office should be subject to

[096-227]re-election; that a stringent bribery oath should be administered to

[096-228]candidates returned; and, finally, that the duration of Parliament

[096-229]should be limited to three years. It was, indeed, an excellent

[096-230]Protestant Reform Bill, for though the Convention had received Father

[096-231]Arthur O'Leary with military honours, and contained many warm friends

[096-232]of Catholic rights, the majority were still intolerant of _religious_

[096-233]freedom. In this majority it is painful to have to record the names of

[096-234]Flood and Charlemont.

[096-235]The debate which followed the introduction of this proposed change in

[096-236]the constitution was stormy beyond all precedent. Grattan, who just one

[096-237]month before (Oct. 28th) had that fierce vituperative contest with

[096-238]Flood familiar to every school-boy, in its worst and most exaggerated

[096-239]form, supported the proposal. The law officers of the crown,

[096-240]Fitzgibbon, Yelverton, Scott, denounced it as an audacious attempt of

[096-241]armed men to dictate to the House its own constitution. The cry of

[096-242]privilege and prerogative was raised, and the measure was rejected by

[096-243]157 to 77. Flood, weary in mind and body, retired to his home; the

[096-244]Convention, which outsat the House, adjourned, amid the bitter

[096-245]indignation of some, and the scarcely concealed relief of others. Two

[096-246]days later they met and adopted a striking address to the throne, and

[096-247]adjourned _sine die_. This was, in fact, the last important day of the

[096-248]Volunteers as a political institution. An attempt a month later to

[096-249]re-assemble the Convention was dexterously defeated by the President,

[096-250]Lord Charlemont. The regular army was next session increased to 15,000

[096-251]men; 20,000 pounds were voted to clothe and equip a rival force—"the

[096-252]Militia"—and the Parliament, which had three times voted them its

[096-253]thanks, now began to look with satisfaction on their rapid

[096-254]disorganization and disbandment.

[096-255]This, perhaps, is the fittest place to notice the few remaining years

[096-256]of the public life of Henry Flood. After the session of 1785, in which

[096-257]he had been outvoted on every motion he proposed, he retired from the

[096-258]Irish Parliament, and allowed himself to be persuaded, at the age of

[096-259]fifty-three, to enter the English. He was elected for Winchester, and

[096-260]made his first essay on the new scene, on his favourite subject of

[096-261]representative reform. But his health was undermined; he failed, except

[096-262]on one or two occasions, to catch the ear of that fastidious assembly,

[096-263]and the figure he made there somewhat disappointed his friends. He

[096-264]returned to Kilkenny to die in 1791, bequeathing a large portion of his

[096-265]fortune to Trinity College, to enrich its MS. library, and to found a

[096-266]permanent professorship of the Irish language. "He was an oak of the

[096-267]forest," said Grattan, "too old to be transplanted at fifty." "He was a

[096-268]man," said one who also knew him well, Sir Jonah Barrington, "of

[096-269]profound abilities, high manners, and great experience in the affairs

[096-270]of Ireland. He had deep information, an extensive capacity, and a solid

[096-271]judgment." In his own magnificent "Ode to Fame," he has pictured his

[096-272]ideal of the Patriot-orator, who finds some consolation amid the

[096-273]unequal struggle with the enemies of his country, foreign and domestic,

[096-274]in a prophetic vision of his own renown. Unhappily, the works of this

[096-275]great man come down to us in as fragmentary a state as those of

[096-276]Chatham; but enough remains to enable us to class him amongst the

[096-277]greatest masters of our speech, and, as far as the drawbacks allowed,

[096-278]among the foremost statesmen of his country.

[096-279]It is painful to be left in doubt, as we are, whether he was ever

[096-280]reconciled to Grattan. The presumption, from the silence of their

[096-281]cotemporaries, is, that they never met again as friends. But it is

[096-282]consoling to remember that in his grave, the survivor rendered him that

[096-283]tribute of justice which almost takes the undying sting out of the

[096-284]philippic of 1783; it is well to know, also, that one of Grattan's

[096-285]latest wishes, thirty years after the death of Flood, when he felt his

[096-286]own last hours approaching, was, that it should be known that he "did

[096-287]not speak the vile abuse reported in the Debates" in relation to his

[096-288]illustrious rival. The best proof that what he did say was undeserved,

[096-289]is that that rival's reputation for integrity and public spirit has

[096-290]survived even his terrible onslaught.
