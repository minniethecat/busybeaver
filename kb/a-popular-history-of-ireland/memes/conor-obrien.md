## Overview

Conor O'Brien, surnamed Conor "of the fortresses" (Conor na gcathar), was King of Munster in the early twelfth century. A prince of the Dalcassian line descended from [[Brian Boru]], he waged protracted wars against [[Thorlogh O'Conor]] for supremacy over Ireland. (018-161) Beyond his military career, Conor O'Brien is remembered as a generous patron of continental religious houses, notably the Abbey of St. Peter at Ratisbon (Regensburg) in Bavaria. (022-209)

## Munster under Conor O'Brien

Conor O'Brien succeeded to the kingship of Munster following the death of Thorlogh O'Brien. (017-167) Thorlogh O'Brien had been King of Cashel and ruled the province loyally under the high king. (017-136) (019-013) The early twelfth century saw Munster's power deeply contested between the Dalcassian dynasty of Thomond and the Eugenian houses of Desmond, an ancient rivalry that Thorlogh O'Conor of Connaught exploited to weaken both factions to his own advantage. (018-168)

> and the valour and enterprise of Conor O'Brien, surnamed Conor "of the fortresses." Of the years following his assertion of his title, few passed without war between those Provinces. In 1121 and 1127, Thorlogh triumphed in the south, took hostages from Lismore to Tralee, and returned home exultingly; a few years later the tide turned, and Conor O'Brien was equally victorious against him, in the heart of his own country. (018-161)

## Wars with Thorlogh O'Conor

The dominant figure of the era was Thorlogh O'Conor, who claimed the high kingship with opposition from the year 1136. (018-192) Conor O'Brien emerged as his most determined adversary in the south, repeatedly contesting Thorlogh's authority over Leath Mogha. (018-160)

Thorlogh employed a strategy of divide and rule, pitting the Eugenian houses against the Dalcassians. (018-168) In the year 1126, he took Dublin and raised his son to the lordship, as Dermid of Leinster and Thorlogh O'Brien had done before him. (018-169) Marching southward, he encamped in Ormond from Lammas to St. Bridget's day and overran Munster with his troops in all directions, taking Cork, Cashel, Ardfinnan, and Tralee. (018-171)

The holy Primate Celsus of Armagh, deploring the evils of this protracted war, left his peaceful city and spent thirteen months in the south and west, endeavouring to reconcile the contending kings. (018-174) In these days the Irish hierarchy performed perhaps their highest part — that of peacemakers and preachers of good will to men. (018-177)

In 1132 and 1133, Conor O'Brien achieved his most significant political success:

> When in 1132 and '33 the tide had temporarily turned against Thorlogh, and Conor O'Brien had united Munster, Leinster, and Meath, against him, the Archbishop of Tuam performed effectually the office of mediator, preserving not only his own Province, but the whole country from the most sanguinary consequences. (018-179)

This alliance brought together three of the five provinces of Ireland, demonstrating Conor's diplomatic as well as military capabilities. The intervention of the Archbishop of Tuam as mediator averted a catastrophic escalation. (018-181)

## Patronage of the Church Abroad

Beyond his martial endeavours, Conor O'Brien was celebrated across Europe for his generosity to religious foundations. The chronicle of Ratisbon records his munificence as founder of the Abbey of St. Peter in that city, and the records of the same abbey credit him with sending large presents to the Emperor Lothaire in aid of the Second Crusade for the recovery of the Holy Land. (022-209) (022-211)

> The chronicle of Ratisbon records with gratitude the munificence of Conor O'Brien, King of Munster, whom it considers the founder of the Abbey of St. Peter in that city. The records of the same Abbey credit its liberal founder with having sent large presents to the Emperor Lothaire, in aid of the second crusade for the recovery of the Holy Land. (022-209)

Some Irish adventurers joined in the general European hosting to the plains of Palestine, though neither numerous nor distinguished enough to occupy the page of history. (022-214) This patronage connected the Irish king to the broader currents of European politics and religious enthusiasm in the twelfth century.

## Death and Legacy

The exact date of Conor O'Brien's death is uncertain, though he appears to have died about the year 1142. His reign represents the last period of Munster ascendancy under the Dalcassian line before the [[Norman Invasion of Ireland]] later in the twelfth century. The holy Malachy, the illustrious friend of St. Bernard, had been nominated as successor to Celsus at Armagh in 1130, and the era of ecclesiastical reform coincided with the last years of Conor's kingship. (018-184) The subsequent O'Brien kings of Thomond continued to claim descent from his illustrious lineage, and the name Conor remained a favoured one among the O'Briens of later centuries.
