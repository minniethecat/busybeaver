## Overview

Sir Arthur Chichester (1563–1625) was Lord Deputy of Ireland for eleven years under James I, presiding over the most decisive period of English consolidation in Ulster. His administration witnessed the Flight of the Earls in 1607, the subsequent plantation of the six escheated counties, and the legal abolition of the ancient Irish customs of tanistry and gavelkind.

## Rise to Power

Chichester succeeded Sir George Carew as Lord Deputy in October 1604. A native of England, he furnishes in many points a parallel to his contemporary Robert Boyle, Earl of Cork. (064-062, 064-063, 064-065, 064-066)

The object of his life was to found and endow a peerage out of the spoils of Ulster, as richly as Boyle endowed his earldom from the confiscation of Munster. (064-067, 064-068)

In his religious opinions, Chichester was a Puritan rather than a Churchman: a pupil of the celebrated Thomas Cartwright and a favourer all his life of the congregational clergy in Ulster. (064-069, 064-070, 064-071)

Yet like Boyle, he carried his repugnance to the interference of the civil magistrate in matters of conscience so discreetly as to satisfy the high-church notions of both Elizabeth and James. (064-072, 064-073)

## Judicial and Administrative Reforms

Chichester's administration proceeded initially not by parliament but by proclamation, letters patent, and orders in council. The whole island was divided into thirty-two counties and six judicial circuits, all visited semi-annually by justices. (064-083, 064-084, 064-085, 064-086, 064-087, 064-088)

On the Northern Circuit, the Lord Deputy accompanied the judges in person with a numerous retinue. In places so wasted by war, pestilence, and famine that the viceregal party were obliged to camp in the fields, courts were held in ruined castles and deserted monasteries. (064-090, 064-091, 064-092, 064-093, 064-094)

Sheriffs were installed in Tyrone and Tyrconnell for the first time. All lawyers appearing in court and all justices of the peace were tendered the oath of supremacy, which necessarily excluded Catholics both from the bench and the bar. (064-096, 064-097, 064-098, 064-099)

A judgment of the Court of King's Bench in 1605 declared the native Irish customs of tanistry and gavelkind null and void, imposing the full feudal system of primogeniture and hereditary succession. This amounted to a legal revolution in landholding. (064-101, 064-102, 064-103, 064-104)

## The Flight of the Earls and Plantation

Chichester was at the centre of the events surrounding the Flight of the Earls in September 1607. He was with Hugh O'Neill at Slane, in Meath, when O'Neill received the letter from Maguire that precipitated his sudden departure. (064-158, 064-159, 064-160)

Chichester had cited O'Neill and O'Donnell to attend him in Dublin on charges connected with a suspected new confederacy, and they had been ordered to appear in London. (064-155, 064-156, 064-157)

After O'Neill's departure, the remaining barriers to the wholesale confiscation of Ulster were rapidly removed. Chichester received a grant of the entire barony of Innishowen, and subsequently a grant of the borough of Dungannon with 1,300 acres adjoining. (064-184, 064-185, 064-213, 064-214)

His colleague Wingfield obtained the district of Fercullan with the title of Viscount Powerscourt, and Lambert was made Earl of Cavan. (064-215, 064-216, 064-217, 064-218)

In 1609, a royal commission was issued to Chichester and his colleagues, including Lambert, Sir John Davis, Sir William Parsons, the Archbishop of Armagh, and the Bishop of Derry, to inquire into the forfeited portions. (064-224, 064-225, 064-226, 064-227)

Before these commissioners, juries duly found that in consequence of the rebellion of O'Neill, O'Donnell, and O'Doherty, the entire six counties of Ulster were forfeit to the crown. (064-228, 064-229, 064-230)

## Legacy

Chichester's eleven-year deputyship was one of the most transformative in Irish history. The abolition of native landholding customs, the establishment of English circuits and sheriffs throughout the island, and the plantation of Ulster permanently altered the demographic and political landscape of Ireland.

He founded a family that would remain prominent in Irish affairs for generations, and Belfast, of which he was the first Baron, would become the industrial capital of nineteenth-century Ireland.
