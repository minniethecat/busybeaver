Thaddeus O'Brien was a Prince of [[Thomond]] and a recurring figure in the military and political history of medieval [[Munster]]. The name appears across multiple centuries in the O'Brien dynasty, suggesting it was a regnal or dynastic name adopted by successive claimants to the O'Brien inheritance. > "King Thorlogh had divided Munster into three parts, giving Desmond to MacCarthy, Ormond to Thaddeus O'Brien, who had fought under him at Moanmore, and leaving the remainder to the O'Brien." (019-049)

The most notable bearers of the name were active in the twelfth century, resisting the reassertion of royal authority under King Thorlogh, and again in the fifteenth century, when a Thaddeus O'Brien led a movement to claim the high sovereignty.

## Twelfth-Century Origins

The first prominent Thaddeus O'Brien appears in the aftermath of the battle of Moanmore. King Thorlogh, seeking to consolidate his authority over Munster, divided the province into three parts: [[Desmond]] to [[MacCarthy]], Ormond to Thaddeus O'Brien — "who had fought under him at Moanmore" — and the remainder to the O'Brien who had only recently competed with Thorlogh for the sovereignty (019-049). This partition demonstrated the complex web of alliances and rivalries that characterized twelfth-century Munster politics.

However, the settlement was short-lived. The reassertion of central authority saw Thaddeus O'Brien defeated, "Ormond obliterated from the map," and "the old bounds of Thomond and Desmond restored," with a banished O'Brien placed on "the throne of [[Cashel]]" (019-088).

## The Fifteenth-Century Thaddeus O'Brien

A later Thaddeus O'Brien, styling himself Prince of Thomond, emerged in the fifteenth century amid renewed Gaelic resistance to English authority. With "the aid of the Irish of the southern half-kingdom," this Thaddeus O'Brien began — in the phrase of the last Antiquary of Lecan — "working his way to [[Tara]]" (039-162). > "Thaddeus O'Brien, Prince of Thomond, with the aid of the Irish of the southern half-kingdom, began (to use the phrase of the last Antiquary of Lecan) 'working his way to Tara.'" (039-162)


> "Thaddeus O'Brien, Prince of Thomond, with the aid of the Irish of the southern half-kingdom, began (to use the phrase of the last Antiquary of Lecan) 'working his way to Tara.'" (039-162)

This Prince united the southern Irish in a movement that revived the ancient claims of the O'Brien dynasty to the high kingship.

## The Movement of 1466

In 1466, the movement led by Thaddeus O'Brien prompted a severe response from the Crown authorities under the Earl of Worcester, John Tiptoft — known in England as "the Butcher" for his barbarity to Lancastrian prisoners. Tiptoft had superseded the [[Earl of Desmond]] as Lord Deputy, and he used the Thaddeus O'Brien rebellion as grounds for accusing Desmond, [[Earl of Kildare|Kildare]], [[Edward Plunkett|Sir Edward Plunkett]], and other leading Anglo-Irish lords of treason (044-279). > "The movement of Thaddeus O'Brien, already related, the same year, gave Tiptoft grounds for accusing Desmond, Kildare, Sir Edward Plunkett, and others, of treason." (044-279)


> "The movement of Thaddeus O'Brien, already related, the same year, gave Tiptoft grounds for accusing Desmond, Kildare, Sir Edward Plunkett, and others, of treason." (044-279)

The attainder that followed became one of the most controversial judicial proceedings of the period, deepening the rift between the Anglo-Irish nobility and the English Crown.
