## Overview

Lord Edward Fitzgerald (1763–1798) was an Irish aristocrat, the younger brother of the Duke of Leinster, a former British Army officer, and ultimately the Commander-in-Chief of the revolutionary [[United Irishmen]]. (100-158)

His conversion from Whig reformer to republican revolutionary illustrates the radicalization of Irish politics in the 1790s. (100-132)

Fitzgerald was arrested on the eve of the 1798 Rebellion and died of wounds sustained during his capture, becoming a martyr to the United Irish cause. His social rank, military experience, and personal courage made him the most dangerous of the United Irish leaders in the eyes of the government. (102-173)

## Early Life and Military Career

Born into Ireland's highest aristocracy as the twelfth child of the first Duke of Leinster, Fitzgerald served with distinction in the British Army in America during the Revolutionary War. He was elected to the Irish Parliament for the borough of Kildare and initially aligned himself with the Patriot opposition led by [[Henry Grattan]].

The French Revolution transformed his political outlook. Where Grattan sought reform within the existing constitution, Fitzgerald came to believe that nothing short of a democratic republic would suffice. He visited Paris in 1792 and openly associated with the French revolutionaries, an act that caused scandal in Dublin and London.

## The United Irishmen

In the year in which Tone left the country, Lord Edward Fitzgerald joined the United Irishmen and rapidly rose to a position of leadership. The society, driven underground after its suppression in 1794, had transformed itself into a secret revolutionary organisation.

> "Lord Edward Fitzgerald was chosen Commander-in-Chief" of its projected army — a position for which his military experience uniquely qualified him. (100-158)

His participation gave the movement something it had previously lacked: a leader of the highest social rank whose presence reassured both the Catholic peasantry and the Presbyterian radicals of the north. Fitzgerald became the vital link between the aristocratic and popular wings of the movement. (100-132)

## The Continental Missions

Fitzgerald was deeply involved in the United Irishmen's efforts to secure French military assistance.

> "The Swiss frontier between Lord Edward Fitzgerald, Arthur O'Conor, Dr. McNevin, and Hoche" was the scene of a crucial meeting with the French general who was to have led the invasion of Ireland. (101-045)

This meeting laid the plans for the French expeditions of 1796 and 1798, though Hoche's death and changing French priorities prevented the promised descent.

## Arrest and Death

In March 1798, as the government moved against the United Irish leadership, the authorities finally closed in on Fitzgerald.

> "The papers were seized by Government, Lord Edward Fitzgerald and William Sampson being at the time in the office." (102-173)

On the 19th of May, he was tracked to a house in Thomas Street, Dublin, where he was arrested after a desperate struggle during which he was shot and mortally wounded. Fitzgerald died of his wounds in Newgate Prison on the 4th of June 1798. The rebellion he had helped to plan erupted in his absence, lacking the coordinated leadership that he had been expected to provide.

## Legacy

Lord Edward Fitzgerald's death made him the most romantic figure of the 1798 Rebellion. His sacrifice of rank, fortune, and life for the cause of Irish liberty was celebrated in ballad and memory.

To later generations of Irish nationalists, he represented the ideal of the patriot aristocrat who chose his country over his class. [[Thomas Russell]], his close friend and fellow United Irishman, was executed at Downpatrick five years later, sharing the same fate. (099-018) (107-184)
