CHAPTER II.

[036-001]THE NORTHERN IRISH ENTER INTO ALLIANCE WITH KING ROBERT BRUCE—ARRIVAL

[036-002]AND FIRST CAMPAIGN OF EDWARD BRUCE.

[036-003]No facts of the ages over which we have already passed are better

[036-004]authenticated than the identity of origin and feeling which existed

[036-005]between the Celts of Erin and of Albyn. Nor was this sympathy of race

[036-006]diminished by their common dangers from a common enemy. On the eve of

[036-007]the Norman invasion we saw how heartily the Irish were with Somerled

[036-008]and the men of Moray in resisting the feudal polity of the successors

[036-009]of Malcolm _Caen-More_. As the Plantagenet Princes in person led their

[036-010]forces against Scotland, the interest of the Irish, especially those of

[036-011]the North, increased, year by year, in the struggles of the Scots.

[036-012]Irish adherents followed the fortunes of Wallace to the close; and when

[036-013]Robert Bruce, after being crowned and seated in the chair of the

[036-014]McAlpin line, on the summit of the hill of Scone, had to flee into

[036-015]exile, he naturally sought refuge where he knew he would find friends.

[036-016]Accompanied by three of his brothers, several adherents, and even by

[036-017]some of the females of his family, he steered, in the autumn of 1306,

[036-018]for the little island of Rathlin—seven miles long by a mile wide—one

[036-019]point of which is within three miles of the Antrim beach. In its most

[036-020]populous modern day Rathlin contained not above 1,000 souls, and little

[036-021]wonder if its still smaller population, five centuries ago, fled in

[036-022]terror at the approach of Bruce. They were, however, soon disarmed of

[036-023]their fears, and agreed to supply the fugitive King daily with

[036-024]provisions for 300 persons, the whole number who accompanied or

[036-025]followed him into exile. His faithful adherents soon erected for him a

[036-026]castle, commanding one of the few landing places on the island, the

[036-027]ruins of which are still shown to strangers as "Bruce's Castle." Here

[036-028]he passed in perfect safety the winter of 1306, while his emissaries

[036-029]were recruiting in Ulster, or passing to and fro, in the intervals of

[036-030]storm, among the western islands. Without waiting for the spring to

[036-031]come round again, they issued from their retreat in different

[036-032]directions; one body of 700 Irish sailed under Thomas and Alexander,

[036-033]the King's brothers, for the Clyde, while Robert and Edward took the

[036-034]more direct passage towards the coast of Argyle, and, after many

[036-035]adventures, found themselves strong enough to attack the foreign forces

[036-036]in Perth and Ayrshire. The opportune death of Edward of England the

[036-037]same summer, and the civil strife bred by his successor's inordinate

[036-038]favour towards Gaveston, enabled the Bruces gradually to root out the

[036-039]internal garrisons of their enemies; but the party that had sailed,

[036-040]under the younger brothers, from Rathlin, were attacked and captured in

[036-041]Loch Ryan by McDowell, and the survivors of the engagement, with Thomas

[036-042]and Alexander Bruce, were carried prisoners to Carlisle and there put

[036-043]to death.

[036-044]The seven years' war of Scottish independence was drawn to a close by

[036-045]the decisive campaign of 1314. The second Edward prepared an

[036-046]overwhelming force for this expedition, summoning, as usual, the

[036-047]Norman-Irish Earls, and inviting in different language his "beloved"

[036-048]cousins, the native Irish Chiefs, not only such as had entered into

[036-049]English alliances at any time, but also notorious allies of Bruce, like

[036-050]O'Neil, O'Donnell, and O'Kane. These writs were generally unheeded; we

[036-051]have no record of either Norman-Irish or native-Irish Chief having

[036-052]responded to Edward's summons, nor could nobles so summoned have been

[036-053]present without some record remaining of the fact. On the contrary all

[036-054]the wishes of the old Irish went with the Scots, and the Normans were

[036-055]more than suspected of leaning the same way. Twenty-one clans,

[036-056]Highlanders and Islemen, and many Ulstermen, fought on the side of

[036-057]Bruce, on the field of Bannockburn; the grant of "Kincardine-O'Neil,"

[036-058]made by the victor-King to his Irish followers, remains a striking

[036-059]evidence of their fidelity to his person, and their sacrifices in his

[036-060]cause. The result of that glorious day was, by the testimony of all

[036-061]historians, English as well as Scottish, received with enthusiasm on

[036-062]the Irish side of the channel.

[036-063]Whether any understanding had been come to between the northern Irish

[036-064]and Bruce, during his sojourn in Rathlin, or whether the victory of

[036-065]Bannockburn suggested the design, Edward Bruce, the gallant companion

[036-066]of all his brother's fortunes and misfortunes, was now invited to place

[036-067]himself at the head of the men of Ulster, in a war for Irish

[036-068]independence. He was a soldier of not inferior fame to his brother for

[036-069]courage and fortitude, though he had never exhibited the higher

[036-070]qualities of general and statesman which crowned the glory of King

[036-071]Robert. Yet as he had never held a separate command of consequence, his

[036-072]rashness and obstinacy, though well known to his intimates, were lost

[036-073]sight of, at a distance, by those who gazed with admiration on the

[036-074]brilliant achievements, in which he had certainly borne the second

[036-075]part. The chief mover in the negotiation by which this gallant soldier

[036-076]was brought to embark his fortunes in an Irish war, was Donald, Prince

[036-077]of Ulster. This Prince, whose name is so familiar from his celebrated

[036-078]remonstrance addressed to Pope John XXII., was son of King Brian of the

[036-079]battle of Down, who, half a century before, at the Conference of

[036-080]Caeluisge, was formally chosen Ard-Righ, by the nobles of three

[036-081]Provinces. He had succeeded to the principality—not without a

[036-082]protracted struggle with the Red Earl—some twenty years before the date

[036-083]of the battle of Bannockburn. Endued with an intensely national spirit,

[036-084]he seems to have fully adopted the views of Nicholas McMaelisa, the

[036-085]Primate of Armagh, his early cotemporary. This Prelate—one of the most

[036-086]resolute opponents of the Norman conquest—had constantly refused to

[036-087]instal any foreigner in a northern diocese. When the Chapter of Ardagh

[036-088]delayed their election, he nominated a suitable person to the Holy See;

[036-089]when the See of Meath was distracted between two national parties he

[036-090]installed his nominee; when the Countess of Ulster caused Edward I. to

[036-091]issue his writ for the installation of John, Bishop of Conor, he

[036-092]refused his acquiescence. He left nearly every See in his Province, at

[036-093]the time of his decease (the year 1303), under the administration of a

[036-094]native ecclesiastic; a dozen years before he had established a formal

[036-095]"association" among the Prelates at large, by which they bound

[036-096]themselves to resist the interference of the Kings of England in the

[036-097]nomination of Bishops, and to be subject only to the sanction of the

[036-098]See of Rome. In the Provinces of Cashel and Tuam, in the fourteenth

[036-099]century, we do not often find a foreign born Bishop; even in Leinster

[036-100]double elections and double delegations to Rome, show how deeply the

[036-101]views of the patriotic Nicholas McMaelisa had seized upon the clergy of

[036-102]the next age. It was Donald O'Neil's darling project to establish a

[036-103]unity of action against the common enemy among the chiefs, similar to

[036-104]that which the Primate had brought about among the Bishops. His own

[036-105]pretensions to the sovereignty were greater than that of any Prince of

[036-106]his age; his house had given more monarchs to the island than any

[036-107]other; his father had been acknowledged by the requisite majority; his

[036-108]courage, patriotism, and talents, were admittedly equal to the task.

[036-109]But he felt the utter impossibility of conciliating that fatal family

[036-110]pride, fed into extravagance by Bards and Senachies, which we have so

[036-111]often pointed out as the worst consequence of the Celtic system. He saw

[036-112]chiefs, proud of their lineage and their name, submit to serve a

[036-113]foreign Earl of Ulster, who refused homage to the native Prince of

[036-114]Ulster; he saw the seedlings of a vice of which we have seen the

[036-115]fruit—that his countrymen would submit to a stranger rather than to one

[036-116]of themselves, and he reasoned, not unnaturally, that, by the hand of

[036-117]some friendly stranger, they might be united and liberated. The attempt

[036-118]of Edward Bruce was a failure, and was followed by many disasters; but

[036-119]a more patriotic design, or one with fairer omens of success, could not

[036-120]have entered the mind or heart of a native Prince, after the event of

[036-121]the battle at Bannockburn. Edward of England, having intelligence of

[036-122]the negotiations on foot between the Irish and Scots, after his great

[036-123]defeat, summoned over to Windsor during the winter, de Burgh,

[036-124]Fitzgerald, de Verdon, and Edmund Butler, the Lord Deputy. After

[036-125]conferring with them, and confirming Butler in his office, they were

[036-126]despatched back in all haste to defend their country. Nor was there

[036-127]time to lose. Edward Bruce, with his usual impetuosity, without waiting

[036-128]for his full armament, had sailed from Ayr with 6,000 men in 300

[036-129]galleys, accompanied by Thomas Randolph, Earl of Moray, Sir John

[036-130]Stuart, Sir Philip Moubray, Sir Fergus of Ardrossan, and other

[036-131]distinguished knights. He landed on the 25th day of May, 1315, in the

[036-132]Glendun river, near Glenarm, and was promptly joined by Donald O'Neil,

[036-133]and twelve other chiefs. Their first advance was from the coast towards

[036-134]that angle of Lough Neagh, near which stands the town of Antrim. Here,

[036-135]at Rathmore, in the plain of Moylinny, they were attacked by the

[036-136]Mandevilles and Savages of the Ards of Down, whom they defeated. From

[036-137]Antrim they continued their route evidently towards Dublin, taking

[036-138]Dundalk and Ardee, after a sharp resistance. At Ardee they were but 35

[036-139]miles north of Dublin, easy of conquest, if they had been provided with

[036-140]siege trains—which it seemed they were not.

[036-142]While Bruce and O'Neil were coming up from the north, Hugh O'Donnell,

[036-143]lord of Tyrconnell, as if to provide occupation for the Earl of Ulster,

[036-144]attacked and sacked the castle and town of Sligo, and wasted the

[036-145]adjacent country. The Earl, on hearing of the landing of the Scots, had

[036-146]mustered his forces at Athlone, and compelled the unwilling attendance

[036-147]of Felim O'Conor, with his clansmen. From Athlone he directed his march

[036-148]towards Drogheda, where he arrived with "20 cohorts," about the same

[036-149]time that the Lord Deputy Butler came up with "30 cohorts." Bruce,

[036-150]unprepared to meet so vast a force—taken together some 25,000 or 30,000

[036-151]men—retreated slowly towards his point of debarkation. De Burgh, who,

[036-152]as Commander-in-Chief, took precedence in the field of the Lord Deputy,

[036-153]ordered the latter to protect Meath and Leinster, while he pursued the

[036-154]enemy. Bruce, having despatched the Earl of Moray to his brother, was

[036-155]now anxious to hold some northern position where they could most easily

[036-156]join him. He led de Burgh, therefore, into the North of Antrim, thence

[036-157]across the Bann at Coleraine, breaking down the bridge at that point.

[036-158]Here the armies encamped for some days, separated by the river, the

[036-159]outposts occasionally indulging in a "shooting of arrows." By

[036-160]negotiation, Bruce and O'Neil succeeded in detaching O'Conor from de

[036-161]Burgh. Under the plea—which really had sufficient foundation—of

[036-162]suppressing an insurrection headed by one of his rivals, O'Conor

[036-163]returned to his own country. No sooner had he left than Bruce assumed

[036-164]the offensive, and it was now the Red Earl's turn to fall back. They

[036-165]retreated towards the castle of Conyre (probably Conor, near Ballymena,

[036-166]in Antrim), where an engagement was fought, in which de Burgh was

[036-167]defeated, his brother William, Sir John Mandeville, and several other

[036-168]knights being taken prisoners. The Earl continued his retreat through

[036-169]Meath towards his own possession; Bruce followed, capturing in

[036-170]succession Granard, Fenagh, and Kells, celebrating his Christmas at

[036-171]Loughsweedy, in West-Meath, in the midst of the most considerable

[036-172]chiefs of Ulster, Meath, and Connaught. It was probably at this stage

[036-173]of his progress that he received the adhesion of the junior branches of

[036-174]the Lacys—the chief Norman family that openly joined his standard.

[036-175]This termination of his first campaign on Irish soil might be

[036-176]considered highly favourable to Bruce. More than half the clans had

[036-177]risen, and others were certain to follow their example; the clergy were

[036-178]almost wholly with him; and his heroic brother had promised to lead an

[036-179]army to his aid in the ensuing spring.
