CHAPTER I.

[001-001]THE FIRST INHABITANTS.

[001-002]Ireland is situated in the North Atlantic, between the degrees

[001-003]fifty-one and a half and fifty-five and a half North, and five and a

[001-004]quarter and ten and a third West longitude from Greenwich. It is the

[001-005]last land usually seen by ships leaving the Old World, and the first by

[001-006]those who arrive there from the Northern ports of America. In size it

[001-007]is less than half as large as Britain, and in shape it may be compared

[001-008]to one of those shields which we see in coats-of-arms, the four

[001-009]Provinces—Ulster, Connaught, Leinster, and Munster—representing the

[001-010]four quarters of the shield.

[001-011]Around the borders of the country, generally near the coast, several

[001-012]ranges of hills and mountains rear their crests, every Province having

[001-013]one or more such groups. The West and South have, however, the largest

[001-014]and highest of these hills, from the sides of all which descend

[001-015]numerous rivers, flowing in various directions to the sea. Other rivers

[001-016]issue out of large lakes formed in the valleys, such as the Galway

[001-017]river which drains Lough Corrib, and the Bann which carries off the

[001-018]surplus waters of Lough Neagh (_Nay_). In a few districts where the

[001-019]fall for water is insufficient, marshes and swamps were long ago

[001-020]formed, of which the principal one occupies nearly 240,000 acres in the

[001-021]very heart of the country. It is called "the Bog of Allen," and, though

[001-022]quite useless for farming purposes, still serves to supply the

[001-023]surrounding district with fuel, nearly as well as coal mines do in

[001-024]other countries.

[001-025]In former times, Ireland was as well wooded as watered, though hardly a

[001-026]tree of the primitive forest now remains. One of the earliest names

[001-027]applied to it was "the wooded Island," and the export of timber and

[001-028]staves, as well as of the furs of wild animals, continued, until the

[001-029]beginning of the seventeenth century, to be a thriving branch of trade.

[001-030]But in a succession of civil and religious wars, the axe and the torch

[001-031]have done their work of destruction, so that the age of most of the

[001-032]wood now standing does not date above two or three generations back.

[001-033]Who were the first inhabitants of this Island, it is impossible to say,

[001-034]but we know it was inhabited at a very early period of the world's

[001-035]lifetime—probably as early as the time when Solomon the Wise, sat in

[001-036]Jerusalem on the throne of his father David. As we should not

[001-037]altogether reject, though neither are we bound to believe, the wild and

[001-038]uncertain traditions of which we have neither documentary nor

[001-039]monumental evidence, we will glance over rapidly what the old Bards and

[001-040]Story-tellers have handed down to us concerning Ireland before it

[001-041]became Christian.

[001-042]The _first_ story they tell is, that about three hundred years after

[001-043]the Universal Deluge, Partholan, of the stock of Japhet, sailed down

[001-044]the Mediterranean, "leaving Spain on the right hand," and holding

[001-045]bravely on his course, reached the shores of the wooded western Island.

[001-046]This Partholan, they tell us, was a double parricide, having killed his

[001-047]father and mother before leaving his native country, for which horrible

[001-048]crimes, as the Bards very morally conclude, his posterity were fated

[001-049]never to possess the land. After a long interval, and when they were

[001-050]greatly increased in numbers, they were cut off to the last man, by a

[001-051]dreadful pestilence.

[001-052]The story of the _second_ immigration is almost as vague as that of the

[001-053]first. The leader this time is called Nemedh, and his route is

[001-054]described as leading from the shores of the Black Sea, across what is

[001-055]now Russia in Europe, to the Baltic Sea, and from the Baltic to

[001-056]Ireland. He is said to have built two royal forts, and to have "cleared

[001-057]twelve plains of wood" while in Ireland. He and his posterity were

[001-058]constantly at war, with a terrible race of Formorians, or Sea Kings,

[001-059]descendants of Ham, who had fled from northern Africa to the western

[001-060]islands for refuge from their enemies, the sons of Shem. At length the

[001-061]Formorians prevailed, and the children of the second immigration were

[001-062]either slain or driven into exile, from which some of their posterity

[001-063]returned long afterwards, and again disputed the country, under two

[001-064]different denominations.

[001-065]The _Firbolgs_ or Belgae are the _third_ immigration. They were

[001-066]victorious under their chiefs, the five sons of Dela, and divided the

[001-067]island into five portions. But they lived in days when the earth—the

[001-068]known parts of it at least—was being eagerly scrambled for by the

[001-069]overflowing hosts of Asia, and they were not long left in undisputed

[001-070]possession of so tempting a prize. Another expedition, claiming descent

[001-071]from the common ancestor, Nemedh, arrived to contest their supremacy.

[001-072]These last—the _fourth_ immigration—are depicted to us as accomplished

[001-073]soothsayers and necromancers who came out of Greece. They could quell

[001-074]storms; cure diseases; work in metals; foretell future events; forge

[001-075]magical weapons; and raise the dead to life; they are called the

[001-076]_Tuatha de Danans_, and by their supernatural power, as well as by

[001-077]virtue of "the Lia Fail," or fabled "stone of destiny," they subdued

[001-078]their Belgic kinsmen, and exercised sovereignty over them, till they in

[001-079]turn were displaced by the Gaelic, or _fifth_ immigration.

[001-080]This fifth and final colony called themselves alternately, or at

[001-081]different periods of their history, _Gael_, from one of their remote

[001-082]ancestors; _Milesians_, from the immediate projector of their

[001-083]emigration; or _Scoti_, from Scota, the mother of Milesius. They came

[001-084]from Spain under the leadership of the sons of Milesius, whom they had

[001-085]lost during their temporary sojourn in that country. In vain the

[001-086]skilful _Tuatha_ surrounded themselves and their coveted island with

[001-087]magic-made tempest and terrors; in vain they reduced it in size so as

[001-088]to be almost invisible from sea; Amergin, one of the sons of Milesius,

[001-089]was a Druid skilled in all the arts of the east, and led by his wise

[001-090]counsels, his brothers countermined the magicians, and beat them at

[001-091]their own weapons. This Amergin was, according to universal usage in

[001-092]ancient times, at once Poet, Priest, and Prophet; yet when his warlike

[001-093]brethren divided the island between them, they left the Poet out of

[001-094]reckoning. He was finally drowned in the waters of the river Avoca,

[001-095]which is probably the reason why that river has been so suggestive of

[001-096]melody and song ever since.

[001-097]Such are the stories told of the _five_ successive hordes of

[001-098]adventurers who first attempted to colonize our wooded Island. Whatever

[001-099]moiety of truth may be mixed up with so many fictions, two things are

[001-100]certain, that long before the time when our Lord and Saviour came upon

[001-101]earth, the coasts and harbours of Erin were known to the merchants of

[001-102]the Mediterranean, and that from the first to the fifth Christian

[001-103]century, the warriors of the wooded Isle made inroads on the Roman

[001-104]power in Britain and even in Gaul. Agricola, the Roman governor of

[001-105]Britain in the reign of Domitian—the first century—retained an Irish

[001-106]chieftain about his person, and we are told by his biographer that an

[001-107]invasion of Ireland was talked of at Rome. But it never took place; the

[001-108]Roman eagles, although supreme for four centuries in Britain, never

[001-109]crossed the Irish Sea; and we are thus deprived of those Latin helps to

[001-110]our early history, which are so valuable in the first period of the

[001-111]histories of every western country, with which the Romans had anything

[001-112]to do.
