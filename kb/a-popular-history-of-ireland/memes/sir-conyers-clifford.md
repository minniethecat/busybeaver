## Overview

Sir Conyers Clifford (d. 1599) was an English military commander who served as Governor of Connaught during the Nine Years' War (1594–1603). A trusted officer of the Elizabethan administration in Ireland, Clifford played an important role in the bitter warfare against the Ulster Confederacy led by Hugh O'Neil and Hugh Roe O'Donnell. He fell in action at the Battle of the Curlew Mountains, one of the most celebrated Irish victories of the war.

## Governor of Connaught

Clifford was appointed Governor of Connaught at a critical juncture in the Nine Years' War. In this capacity, he was responsible for maintaining English authority over the western province and coordinating operations against the northern confederacy. (060-041) His command encompassed the territories of Galway, Mayo, Sligo, and Roscommon, and Thomond, where he was tasked with protecting the loyalist settlements and containing the influence of O'Donnell's expanding power.

> Towards the end of July, Sir Conyers Clifford was ordered to muster the forces of Connaught for a decisive thrust against the Ulster Confederacy's western flank. (059-173)

## The Battle of the Curlew Mountains

In 1599, during the Earl of Essex's ill-fated campaign against the northern confederates, Clifford was ordered to march to the relief of O'Conor Sligo with all the power he could muster. (060-075) Clifford despatched from Galway, by sea and by land, a considerable force intended to break through O'Donnell's lines and relieve the isolated loyalist garrison in Sligo. (060-076)

As Clifford's column appeared in the defiles of the Curlew Mountains, they were ambushed by the forces of Hugh Roe O'Donnell. The English troops, caught in a narrow pass, were thrown into confusion and overwhelmed by the Irish horse and foot. The gallant Clifford, scorning to fly, was found among the slain, and his death was a severe blow to the morale of the Queen's forces in the west. (060-102)

> The gallant Clifford, scorning to fly, was found among the slain, and his head was taken as a trophy to O'Donnell's camp. (060-102)

## Legacy

Clifford's defeat at the Curlew Mountains represented one of the most complete Irish victories of the Nine Years' War. His death exposed the vulnerability of the English position in Connaught and contributed to the general atmosphere of crisis that attended Essex's failing campaign. The battle is remembered as a testament to both the tactical skill of O'Donnell and the grim determination of Elizabethan commanders like Clifford, who often paid with their lives for the strategic failures of their superiors in Dublin and London.
