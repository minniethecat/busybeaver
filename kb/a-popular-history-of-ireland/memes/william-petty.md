## Overview

Sir William Petty (1623–1687) was an English physician, economist, and surveyor who played a central role in the Cromwellian confiscation and redistribution of Irish land. Dr. William Petty, the fortunate economist, founded the house of Lansdowne through his activities in Ireland. (075-050)

By him the surface of the kingdom was estimated at ten millions and a half plantation acres, three of which were deducted for waste and water; of the remainder, above five million acres were in Catholic hands before the Cromwellian confiscations. (075-051)

Petty's Down Survey (1654–1659) provided the cartographic basis for the most extensive transfer of land ownership in Irish history, from the native Catholic proprietors to English Protestant adventurers and soldiers.

## The Down Survey

The Cromwellian conquest of Ireland required a systematic inventory of the conquered territory. A new survey of the whole kingdom was ordered, under the direction of Dr. William Petty. (075-049) This became known as the Down Survey — so called because the results were "laid down" on maps — and it was the first cadastral survey of its scale in Europe.

Petty employed soldiers of the New Model Army as surveyors and developed innovative techniques to complete the work with remarkable speed. Within a few years, he had mapped every barony in Ireland, recording not only boundaries but the quality and value of the land.

> Of the remainder, above 5,000,000 acres were in Catholic hands in 1641; 300,000 were church and college lands; and 2,000,000 were in possession of the Protestant settlers of the reigns of James and Elizabeth. Under the Protectorate, 5,000,000 acres were confiscated; this enormous spoil, two-thirds of the whole island, went to the soldiers and adventurers who had served against the Irish. (075-054, 075-056, 075-057)

The survey was designed to facilitate the redistribution of confiscated Catholic estates to the "adventurers" who had financed Cromwell's Irish campaign and the soldiers who had fought in it.

## The Transplantation and Population Estimates

Petty was both an architect and a chronicler of the Cromwellian transplantation. He estimated the pre-war Catholic population of Ireland at above one million, and he recorded with a statistician's detachment the effects of war, famine, and forced migration.

> Sir William Petty mentions 6,000 Irish boys and girls shipped to the West Indies as slaves or indentured labourers during the Cromwellian period. (075-097)

Some contemporary accounts make the total number of children and adults so transported as many as 100,000 souls. (075-099) To this decimation, contemporaries added 34,000 men of fighting age who had emigrated to the Continent to serve in foreign armies. (075-100)

Petty himself profited enormously from the confiscations. As payment for his services, he received large estates in County Kerry, which became the foundation of the Lansdowne fortune.

## Scientific and Economic Work

Beyond Ireland, Petty was a founding member of the Royal Society and a pioneer of what he called "political arithmetic" — now recognized as an early form of economics and statistics:

> Sir William Petty computed them at "eight to one" of the entire population. "So captive Israel multiplied in chains." (078-188, 078-190)

His economic writings, including his "Political Anatomy of Ireland," applied quantitative methods to social analysis in a manner that was wholly original for the 17th century. He was a friend and collaborator of Thomas Hobbes and John Evelyn, and his intellectual range encompassed medicine, naval architecture, and mechanical invention as well as surveying and economics.

Petty's Irish surveys and statistical estimates remain important primary sources for historians of 17th-century Ireland, even as his role in the dispossession of the native Irish has marked him as a controversial figure in Irish historical memory.
