## Overview

Walter Devereux, 1st Earl of Essex (1541–1576), was an English nobleman and military adventurer who undertook one of the most ambitious plantation schemes of the Elizabethan era in Ireland.

He obtained a grant of vast territories in Ulster in 1573 and mortgaged his English estates to finance an expedition that ended in his own death and the ruin of his family fortunes. He was the father of Robert Devereux, 2nd Earl of Essex, whose own career ended on the scaffold in 1601. (056-051)

> Walter Devereux, Earl of Essex (father of the Essex still more unfortunate), obtained in 1573 a grant of one moiety of Farney and Clandeboy, and having mortgaged his English estates to the Queen for 10,000 pounds, associated with himself many other adventurers. (056-051)

## Background and Ambition

Walter Devereux was born in 1541 into an ancient noble family with Welsh border origins. He succeeded his grandfather as Viscount Hereford in 1558 and was created Earl of Essex by Queen Elizabeth I in 1572. (056-046)

Ambitious for martial glory and territorial wealth, Essex turned his attention to Ireland, where the Tudor Crown sought to extend its authority into the Gaelic north through private plantation. His commission authorized him to expel the native Irish inhabitants and plant the land with English settlers. (056-046)

## The Ulster Enterprise

On the 16th of August, 1573, Essex set sail from Liverpool with a considerable retinue. He was accompanied by the Lords Dacre and Rich, Sir Henry Knollys, and a force of adventurers and soldiers drawn from the restless gentry of the Welsh marches. (056-055)

The O'Neills of Clandeboy, the hereditary lords of the territory, did not submit quietly to Essex's claims. Under their chieftain, Sir Brian MacPhelim O'Neill, they waged a determined guerilla campaign against the English settlers. (056-048)

In November 1574, Essex invited Sir Brian and his entourage to a feast at Belfast Castle and then had them seized and executed — an act of treachery that stained his reputation even among Elizabethan observers. Despite such ruthless measures, the enterprise foundered as the promised settlers failed to materialize. (056-048)

## Death and Aftermath

By 1575, Essex was heavily in debt and petitioning the Crown for relief. The Queen ordered him to abandon his Ulster venture and accept the post of Earl Marshal of Ireland, but his health was broken. (056-049)

In September 1576 he died at Dublin Castle, probably of dysentery, at the age of thirty-five. Persistent rumours suggested he had been poisoned by the Earl of Leicester, his rival for the Queen's favour. (056-050)

The Ulster enterprise died with him. The Clandeboy O'Neills repossessed their lands, and the surviving English settlers either withdrew to the Pale or were absorbed into the native population. (056-054)

## Historical Assessment

Essex's Ulster venture was a portent of the brutal logic of Elizabethan colonization — private enterprise underwritten by the Crown, indifferent to native title, and prepared to employ any measure of violence to achieve its ends. (056-053)

> A higher name was equally unfortunate in the same field of adventure. (056-051)

His failure illustrated the limits of private plantation as a strategy for conquest. Later English administrators, from Sir Henry Sidney to Lord Mountjoy, would learn that only sustained military occupation backed by the full resources of the state could subdue Gaelic Ulster. (056-053)
