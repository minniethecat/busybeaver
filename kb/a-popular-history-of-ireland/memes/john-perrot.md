## Overview

Sir John Perrot (c. 1528–1592) was an English statesman and soldier who served as President of Munster and later as Lord Deputy of Ireland during the reign of [[Elizabeth I]]. Reputed to be the illegitimate son of [[Henry VIII]], Perrot resembled his supposed father in temperament—"impatient of contradiction and control; fond of expense and magnificence, with a high opinion of his own abilities for diplomacy and legislation." His tenure as Lord Deputy from 1584 was marked by both administrative innovation and political controversy. (058-054)

Perrot was appointed first President of Munster under the provincial presidency system established by [[Henry Sidney]], and his experience there shaped his approach to Irish governance. He prided himself on his "skilful devices" and diplomatic cunning, though his methods ultimately led to his recall and disgrace. (058-088) (063-015)

## Biography

### Early Career and the Presidency of Munster

John Perrot rose to prominence as a soldier and courtier in Elizabethan England. His reputed parentage—he was widely considered the bastard son of [[henry-viii|Henry VIII]]—gave him connections at court and a degree of royal favour that aided his advancement. Like most public men of his age, he was "both soldier and statesman." (058-054)

Under the presidency system established by [[henry-sidney|Sir Henry Sidney]], Perrot was appointed the first President of Munster, with large though subordinate military and civil powers. Alongside Sir Edward Fitton, first President of Connaught, Perrot was tasked with extending English authority in the southern province. His early service in Munster involved the pursuit of the rebel James Fitzmaurice Fitzgerald, whom Perrot hunted through the difficult terrain of the Geraldine territories. (055-276) (055-014)

> "Sir John Perrott, having by this time no further sieges to prosecute, drew his toils closer and closer round the Geraldine's retreat. For a whole year, the fidelity of his adherents and the natural strength of the place enabled him to baffle all the President's efforts." (055-276)

### Lord Deputyship

In 1583, Perrot was appointed Lord Deputy of Ireland, though he did not enter on the duties of the office until the following year. His reputation as a skilful operator preceded him, and he brought to the office a combination of military experience and political ambition. His administration coincided with a period of relative calm following the suppression of the Desmond Rebellions. (058-054)

> "The appointment of Sir John Perrott dates from 1583, though he did not enter on the duties of Lord Deputy till the following year. Like most of the public men of that age, he was both soldier and statesman. In temper he resembled his reputed father, Henry VIII.; for he was impatient of contradiction and control; fond of expense and magnificence, with a high opinion of his own abilities for diplomacy and legislation." (058-054)

### The Parliament of 1585

One of Perrot's most significant acts as Lord Deputy was the convening of a Parliament in 1585, which was prorogued until the following year. This was the third and last Parliament of Elizabeth's long reign called in Ireland, and it was notable for being "utterly barren of ecclesiastical legislation," with the sole exception of "an act against sorcery and witchcraft." The absence of further religious legislation reflected the political impossibility of advancing the [[reformation|Reformation]] through an [[irish-parliament|Irish Parliament]]. (057-181)

The Parliament saw the participation of [[Hugh ONeill]], whose last official act of cooperation with the [[dublin|Dublin]] government was his attendance at this assembly. The inclusion of Gaelic lords in the parliamentary process was a deliberate strategy of Perrot's administration, though it would prove to be a temporary accommodation. (057-179)

> "O'Neil's last official act of co-operation with the Dublin government may be considered his participation in the Parliament convoked by Sir John Perrott in 1585, and prorogued till the following year." (057-181)

### Controversy and Recall

Perrot prided himself on his "skilful devices"—the diplomatic and strategic manoeuvres by which he sought to extend English control. One such device was the treacherous capture of a youthful Gaelic chieftain, whom Perrot lured into custody through guile. Though the captive was a mere lad, "the mysterious language of ancient prophecy, which seemed to point him out for greatness, gave him consequence in the eyes of both friends and foes." (058-088)

> "The treacherous capture of this youthful chieftain was one of the skilful devices on which Sir John Perrott most prided himself." (058-088)

Perrot's methods, however, eventually brought about his downfall. He was charged with severity against recusants, and this formed one of the principal articles upon which he was recalled from the government. The charge of religious persecution reflected the growing tension between the Catholic majority and the Protestant administration—a tension that would intensify dramatically in the decades following Perrot's departure. (063-015)

## Character and Methods

Perrot's dual nature—the capable administrator and the ruthless political operator—reflected the contradictions of Elizabethan governance in Ireland. His "skilful devices" embodied the Machiavellian statecraft that Tudor administrators increasingly employed against the Gaelic lords. At the same time, his convening of Parliament and his efforts to bring Gaelic chiefs into the political process demonstrated an awareness that military force alone could not secure the English position.

The witchcraft statute of Elizabeth's 27th year, for which Perrot's administration was partly responsible, was described as one of the characteristic legislative acts of the period. The preoccupation with sorcery reflected both the cultural divide between English and Irish society and the legalistic approach of Tudor administrators. (063-015)

## Legacy

Perrot's Lord Deputyship occupies an intermediate position in the history of Tudor Ireland—after the military campaigns of the Desmond suppression and before the cataclysm of the [[Nine Years War]]. His Parliament of 1585 represented one of the last occasions when Gaelic lords participated in the formal institutions of English governance before the final breakdown of relations in the 1590s.

His recall and disgrace illustrated the precarious position of English governors in Ireland, who were required to enforce unpopular policies while maintaining sufficient local support to govern. The "severity against recusants" that formed the charge against him would be repeated against his successors, as the religious divide deepened and the path towards the [[ulster|Ulster]] plantation became clearer.
