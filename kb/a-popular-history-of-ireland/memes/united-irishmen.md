## Overview

The Society of United Irishmen was a revolutionary republican organization founded in Belfast in 1791 by a group of [[presbyterian|Presbyterian]] radicals inspired by the French Revolution. Its declared aim was to unite "Catholic, Protestant, and Dissenter" in a common struggle to establish an independent Irish republic based on democratic principles. (106-004)

Under the leadership of [[theobald-wolfe-tone|Theobald Wolfe Tone]], the United Irishmen organized the Irish Rebellion of 1798, the largest popular uprising in modern Irish history. The rebellion was suppressed with great violence, leading to the death of an estimated 30,000 people and the subsequent passage of the Act of Union in 1800.

## History

### Foundation and Ideals

The Society was founded in October 1791 by a group that included [[theobald-wolfe-tone|Wolfe Tone]], a [[dublin|Dublin]]-born Protestant lawyer; Thomas Russell, a former army officer; and several prominent Belfast [[presbyterian|Presbyterians]]. The United Irishmen drew inspiration from the American and French Revolutions, declaring that religious divisions had been deliberately fostered by the British government to prevent Irish unity.

Their central principle—the union of all Irish people regardless of religion—was revolutionary in an era when religious discrimination was institutionalized through the [[penal-laws|Penal Laws]]. The Society advocated for [[catholic-emancipation|Catholic Emancipation]], parliamentary reform, and ultimately the establishment of an independent Irish republic.

### Radicalization and Suppression

Initially, the United Irishmen functioned as an open political society, publishing newspapers and holding public meetings. However, as the French Revolutionary Wars intensified and Britain entered the conflict against France, the government increasingly viewed the Society as a seditious organization.

In 1794, the Society was suppressed. It went underground and transformed into a revolutionary military organization, establishing cells across the country and seeking French military assistance for an insurrection. [[theobald-wolfe-tone|Wolfe Tone]] traveled to France to negotiate with the Directory for a French expeditionary force to Ireland.

### The Rebellion of 1798

The rebellion broke out in May 1798. It was most intense in Wexford in the south-east and in Antrim and Down in [[ulster|Ulster]]. The United Irishmen achieved some initial successes, particularly in Wexford, but the rebellion lacked coordination and was suppressed with great violence by government forces under General Lake and Lord Cornwallis.

A French expeditionary force of approximately 1,000 men under General Humbert landed at Killala, County Mayo, in August 1798, too late to coordinate with the main rebellion. Humbert achieved a notable victory at Castlebar before being surrounded and forced to surrender.

[[theobald-wolfe-tone|Wolfe Tone]] himself was captured aboard a French ship off the coast of Donegal in October 1798. He was tried and sentenced to death, but died in prison from a self-inflicted wound before his execution could be carried out.

### Aftermath

The failure of the rebellion and the severity of its suppression had two profound consequences. First, the British government of William Pitt determined that only a legislative union could prevent future Irish rebellions, leading to the Act of Union of 1800. Second, the memory of 1798 became a central element of Irish republican tradition, inspiring later generations of nationalists.

[[robert-emmet|Robert Emmet]]'s attempted rising in 1803 was the final echo of the United Irish movement, and its failure marked the end of the organization.

## Legacy

The United Irishmen left a complex and enduring legacy. Their ideal of uniting Catholic, Protestant, and Dissenter in a common Irish nation was far ahead of its time and would be invoked by later nationalist leaders across the political spectrum. The rebellion of 1798, though a military failure, established the tradition of physical-force republicanism in Ireland that would culminate in the Easter Rising of 1916 and the War of Independence.

The memory of [[theobald-wolfe-tone|Wolfe Tone]], in particular, remains central to Irish republicanism. His grave at Bodenstown, County Kildare, is a site of annual pilgrimage for Irish republicans.
