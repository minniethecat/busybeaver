Lord Ranelagh served as President of the Province of [[Connaught]] during the early years of the [[Irish Confederate Wars]]. He held office under the Crown against the rising [[Catholic Confederation]] in the 1640s. His tenure saw English authority west of the [[River Shannon|Shannon]] deteriorate rapidly, ending with his withdrawal and the collapse of the royalist position in [[Galway]].

## Early career

Ranelagh belonged to a Protestant family of the Anglo-Irish ascendancy. He was appointed to the Presidency of Connaught, one of four provincial governorships under the Tudor and Stuart administrations in Ireland. (068-217) The Presidency carried both civil and military authority, making its holder the Crown's principal representative in the west.

At the outbreak of the Confederate rising, Ranelagh shared command in Connaught with [[Charles Coote the younger|Sir Charles Coote the younger]] and Sir Francis Willoughby, the Governor of Galway. (068-217) Together they formed the Crown's military leadership in the province, tasked with suppressing the insurrection.

## President of Connaught

As President, Ranelagh found his efforts complicated by the ambiguous position of [[Ulick Burke, Earl of Clanrickarde]], the most powerful nobleman in Connaught. Clanrickarde, though a Catholic, sought to prevent his province from joining the Confederation and attempted to mediate between the Crown authorities and the disaffected population. (068-215)

> Clanrickarde continued to play a middle part between the President, Lord Ranelagh, Sir Charles Coote the younger, and Willoughby, Governor of Galway, until the popular impatience burst all control. (068-217)

Ranelagh's position grew increasingly untenable through the summer of 1642. The western bishops had urged Clanrickarde to lead the province's Catholics, while the young men of Galway seized an English ship carrying arms, took the Confederate oath, and besieged Willoughby in the citadel. (068-220)

Although Clanrickarde briefly restored order, the arrival of reinforcements under [[Lord Forbes]] triggered fresh violence in which suburbs were burned and churches sacked. (068-228)

## The Siege of Athlone

The crisis intensified in the late summer of 1642 when local risings broke out across Sligo, Mayo, and Roscommon. The insurgents achieved such immediate success that Ranelagh was forced to take refuge:

> The President of the Province, Lord Ranelagh, shut himself up in the castle of Athlone, where he was closely besieged. (068-231)

Athlone, strategically located on the Shannon at the eastern gateway to Connaught, was one of the most important fortified positions in the west.

Ranelagh's predicament reflected the broader collapse of English authority in Ireland. The Long Parliament remitted only 11,500 pounds to the Dublin government that June, and the troops were close to mutiny for want of pay. (068-236) A force of 2,000 men was eventually despatched under [[James Butler, Duke of Ormond|the Marquis of Ormond]] to relieve Athlone. (068-237)

## Retreat from Connaught

Ranelagh's tenure in Connaught ended in February 1643 when he and Coote evacuated the province, leading the English troops in a retreat eastward toward Leinster. The Confederate commander in Leinster, [[General Thomas Preston]], attempted to intercept this column at Rathconnell but was repulsed. (069-066)

> From the day Lord Ranelagh and the younger Coote deserted the Western province, the Confederate cause had rapidly advanced. (069-093)

Within months, on the 20th of June 1643, Willoughby surrendered Galway to the Confederates under Sir John Burke. (069-091) Galway, ranked alongside Cadiz and Bristol for its commercial wealth, remained under Confederate control for nine years. (069-096) The loss of the province Ranelagh had governed was a severe blow to the Crown's position.

## Role in the Williamite War

Ranelagh's direct involvement in Irish affairs diminished after his withdrawal from Connaught. The source does not record his participation in the later [[Williamite War in Ireland|Williamite campaigns]], but his family remained connected to the Protestant interest in Ireland throughout the turbulent decades that separated the Confederate period from the Williamite settlement.

The political realignments that followed the [[Restoration of Charles II]] and the [[Glorious Revolution]] brought new opportunities for Anglo-Irish figures who had demonstrated loyalty to the Crown during the Confederate Wars. Ranelagh's administrative experience in Connaught placed him among those courted by successive regimes.

## Later career & Paymaster-General

Following the Restoration, Ranelagh rose to prominence in English fiscal administration, eventually securing the office of Paymaster-General. This position, one of the most lucrative in the royal administration, placed him at the centre of Crown finance during the reigns of [[Charles II]], [[James II]], and [[William III]].

The Paymastership carried immense patronage power and financial opportunity, though it also exposed its holders to parliamentary scrutiny and allegations of corruption. Ranelagh navigated these hazards through the shifting political landscape of the late Stuart period, maintaining his position across three reigns.

His later years exemplified the career trajectory of Anglo-Irish Protestant administrators who leveraged service in Ireland into positions of influence at the English court. Though his Irish presidency ended in retreat, his subsequent career at Westminster and Whitehall secured his place in the governing elite of the Restoration era.
