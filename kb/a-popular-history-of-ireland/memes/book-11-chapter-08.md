CHAPTER VIII.

[095-001]GRATTAN'S LEADERSHIP—LEGISLATIVE AND JUDICIAL INDEPENDENCE ESTABLISHED.

[095-002]The task which Mr. Grattan felt called upon to undertake, was not

[095-003]_revolutionary_, in the usually accepted sense of the term. He was a

[095-004]Monarchist and a Whig in general politics; but he was an Irishman,

[095-005]proud and fond of his country, and a sincere lover of the largest

[095-006]religious liberty. With the independence of the judiciary and the

[095-007]legislature, with freedom of commerce and of conscience, he would be

[095-008]well content to stand by the British connection. "The sea," he said, in

[095-009]his lofty figurative language, "protests against union—the ocean

[095-010]against separation." But still, within certain legal limits, his task

[095-011]_was_ revolutionary, and was undertaken under all the discouragements

[095-012]incident to the early stages of great constitutional reforms.

[095-013]Without awaiting the action of the English Parliament, in relation to

[095-014]free trade, a public-spirited citizen of Dublin, Alderman James Horan,

[095-015]demanded an entry at the custom house, for some parcels of Irish

[095-016]woollens, which he proposed exporting to Rotterdam, contrary to the

[095-017]prohibitory enactment, the 10th and 11th of William III. The

[095-018]commissioners of customs applied for instructions to the Castle, and

[095-019]the Castle to the Secretary of State, Franklin's friend, Lord

[095-020]Hillsborough. For the moment a collision similar to that which had

[095-021]taken place at Boston, on a not dissimilar issue, seemed imminent. A

[095-022]frigate was stationed off Howth, with instructions, it was said, to

[095-023]intercept the prohibited woollens, but Alderman Horan, by the advice of

[095-024]his friends, allowed his application to remain on the custom house

[095-025]files. It had served its purpose of bringing home practically to the

[095-026]people, the value of the principle involved in the demand for freedom

[095-027]of exports and imports. At the same time that this practical argument

[095-028]was discussed in every circle, Mr. Grattan moved in the House of

[095-029]Commons, in amendment to the supply bill, that, "At this time it is

[095-030]inexpedient to grant new taxes." The government divided the House, but

[095-031]to their mortification found only 47 supporters; for Grattan's

[095-032]amendment there were 170. A subsequent amendment against granting

[095-033]duties for the support of the loan fund, was also carried by 138 to

[095-034]100.

[095-035]These adverse votes were communicated with great trepidation, by the

[095-036]Lord Lieutenant, to the British administration. At length Lord North

[095-037]thought it essential to make some concessions, and with this view he

[095-038]brought in resolutions, declaring the trade with the British colonies

[095-039]in America and Africa, and the free export of glass and woollens, open

[095-040]to the Irish merchant. A week later, similar resolutions were passed in

[095-041]the Irish Commons, and in February, 1780, "a free trade" in the sense

[095-042]in which it had been demanded, was established by law, placing Ireland

[095-043]in most respects, as to foreign and colonial commerce, on an equality

[095-044]with England.

[095-045]In February, the Viceroy again alarmed the British administration, with

[095-046]the reported movement for the repeal of "Poyning's law,"—the statute

[095-047]which required heads of bills to be transmitted to, and approved in

[095-048]England, before they could be legislated upon. He received in reply,

[095-049]the royal commands to resist by every means in his power, any attempted

[095-050]"change in the constitution," and he succeeded in eliciting from the

[095-051]House of Lords, an address, strongly condemnatory of "the misguided

[095-052]men," who sought to raise such "groundless jealousies," between the two

[095-053]kingdoms. But the Patriot Commoners were not to be so deterred. They

[095-054]declared the repeal of Poyning's act, and the 6th of George I., to be

[095-055]their ultimatum, and notices of motion to that effect were immediately

[095-056]placed on the journals of the House of Commons.

[095-057]In the early days of April, Grattan, who, more than any of our orators,

[095-058]except perhaps Burke, was sensitive to the aspects of external nature,

[095-059]and imbued with the poetry of her works, retired from the city, to his

[095-060]uncle Dean Marlay's house, Cellbridge Abbey, formerly the residence of

[095-061]Swift's ill-fated Vannessa. "Along the banks of that river," he said,

[095-062]many years afterwards, "amid the groves and bowers of Swift and

[095-063]Vannessa, I grew convinced that I was right; arguments, unanswerable,

[095-064]came to my mind, and what I then presaged, confirmed me in my

[095-065]determination to persevere." With an enthusiasm intensified and

[095-066]restrained—but wonderful in the fire and grandeur of its utterance—he

[095-067]rose in his place, on the 19th of the month, to move that "the King,

[095-068]Lords, and Commons of Ireland, are the only power competent to enact

[095-069]laws to bind Ireland." He was supported by Hussey Burgh, Yelverton, and

[095-070]Forbes; Flood favoured postponement, and laid the foundation of his

[095-071]future estrangement from Grattan; Daly was also for delay; Fitzgibbon,

[095-072]afterwards Lord Clare, Provost Hutchinson, and John Foster, afterwards

[095-073]Lord Oriel, resisted the motion. The Castle party moved in amendment

[095-074]that "there being an equivalent resolution already on the journals of

[095-075]the House"—alluding to one of the resolutions against Stafford's

[095-076]tyranny in 1641—a new resolution was unnecessary. This amendment was

[095-077]carried by 136 to 79, thus affirming the formula of independence

[095-078]adopted in 1641, but depriving Grattan of the honour of putting it, in

[095-079]his own words, on the record. The substantial result, however, was the

[095-080]same; the 19th of April was truly what Grattan described it, "a great

[095-081]day for Ireland." "It is with the utmost concern," writes the Viceroy

[095-082]next day to Lord Hillsborough, "I must acquaint your Lordship that

[095-083]although so many gentlemen expressed their concern that the subject had

[095-084]been introduced, the sense of the House _against_ the obligation of

[095-085]_any statutes_ of the Parliament of Great Britain, within this kingdom,

[095-086]is represented to me to have been almost unanimous."

[095-088]Ten days later, a motion of Mr. Yelverton's to repeal Poyning's law, as

[095-089]far as related to the Irish privy council's supervision of heads of

[095-090]bills, was negatived by 130 to 105.

[095-091]During the remainder of the session the battle of independence was

[095-092]fought on the Mutiny Bill. The Viceroy and the Chief Secretary, playing

[095-093]the game of power, were resolved that the influence of the crown should

[095-094]not be diminished, so far as the military establishments were

[095-095]concerned. Two justices of the peace in Sligo and Mayo, having issued

[095-096]writs of _habeas corpus_ in favour of deserters from the army, on the

[095-097]ground that neither the British Mutiny Act, nor any other British

[095-098]statute, was binding on Ireland, unless confirmed by an act of its own

[095-099]legislature, brought up anew the whole question. Lord North, who, with

[095-100]all his proverbial tact and good humour, in the House of Commons,

[095-101]always pursued the most arbitrary policy throughout the empire,

[095-102]proposed a perpetual Mutiny Bill for Ireland, instead of the Annual

[095-103]Bill, in force in England. It was introduced in the Irish House of

[095-104]Commons by Mr. Gervase Parker Bushe, and, by a vote of two to one,

[095-105]postponed for a fortnight. During the interval, the British authorities

[095-106]remained obdurate to argument and remonstrance. In vain, the majority

[095-107]of the Irish privy counsellors advised concession; in vain, Flood, who

[095-108]was consulted, pointed out the futility of attempting to force such a

[095-109]measure; it was forced, and, under the cry of loyalty, a draft bill was

[095-110]carried through both Houses, and remitted to England in June. Early in

[095-111]August it was returned; on the 12th it was read a first time; on the

[095-112]16th, a second; and it was carried through Committee by 114 to 62. It

[095-113]was at this emergency the Volunteers performed the second act of their

[095-114]great drama of Ireland's liberation. A series of reviews were held, and

[095-115]significant addresses presented to Lord Camden (then on a visit to the

[095-116]country), Lord Charlemont, Mr. Flood, and Mr. Grattan. On the

[095-117]re-assembling of Parliament in August, when the bill was referred to,

[095-118]Mr. Grattan declared that he would resist it to the last; that if

[095-119]passed into law, he and his friends would _secede_, and would appeal to

[095-120]the people in "a formal instrument." A new series of corporation and

[095-121]county meetings was convened by the Patriot party, which warmly

[095-122]condemned the Perpetual Mutiny Act, and as warmly approved the repeal

[095-123]of Poyning's Act, and the 6th of George I.: questions which were all

[095-124]conceived to be intermixed together, and to flow from the assertion of

[095-125]a common principle. Parliament being prorogued in September, only threw

[095-126]the whole controversy back again into the furnace of popular agitation.

[095-127]The British Government tried a lavish distribution of titles and a

[095-128]change of Viceroys,—Lord Carlisle being substituted in December for

[095-129]Lord Buckingham—but the spirit abroad was too general and too earnest,

[095-130]to be quelled by the desertion of individuals, however numerous or

[095-131]influential. With Lord Carlisle, came, as Chief Secretary, Mr. Eden,

[095-132]afterwards Lord Auckland; he had been, with his chief, a peace

[095-133]commissioner to America, two years before, and had failed; he was an

[095-134]intriguing and accomplished man, but he proved himself as unequal as

[095-135]Heron or Rigby to combat the movement for Irish independence.

[095-136]Parliament was not again called together till the month of October,

[095-137]1781; the interval being busily occupied on both sides with endeavours

[095-138]to create and sustain a party. Soon after the meeting, Mr. Grattan,

[095-139]seconded by Mr. Flood, moved for a limitation of the Mutiny Bill, which

[095-140]was lost; a little later, Mr. Flood himself introduced a somewhat

[095-141]similar motion, which was also outvoted two to one; and again, during

[095-142]the session, Mr. Yelverton, having abandoned his promised motion

[095-143]against Poyning's law, on news of Lord Cornwallis's surrender reaching

[095-144]Dublin, Flood took it up, moved it, and was defeated. A further measure

[095-145]of relief for Roman Catholics, introduced by Mr. Gardiner, author of

[095-146]the act of 1778, and warmly supported by Grattan, was resisted by Flood

[095-147]in the one House, and Lord Charlemont in the other. It miscarried, and

[095-148]left another deposit of disagreement between the actual and the former

[095-149]leader of the Patriot party.

[095-150]Still no open rupture had taken place between the two Patriot orators.

[095-151]When the convention of the volunteers was called at Dungannon for the

[095-152]15th of February, 1782, they consulted at Charlemont House as to the

[095-153]resolutions to be passed. They were agreed on the constitutional

[095-154]question; Grattan, of his own generous free will, added the resolution

[095-155]in favour of emancipation. Two hundred and forty-two delegates,

[095-156]representing 143 corps, unanimously adopted the resolutions so drafted,

[095-157]as their own, and, from the old head-quarters of Hugh O'Neil, sent

[095-158]forth anew an unequivocal demand for civil and religious liberty. The

[095-159]example of Ulster soon spread through Ireland. A meeting of the

[095-160]Leinster volunteers, Mr. Flood in the chair, echoed it from Dublin; the

[095-161]Munster corps endorsed it unanimously at Cork; Lord Clanrickarde

[095-162]summoned together those of the western counties at Portumna—an historic

[095-163]spot, suggestive of striking associations. Strengthened by these

[095-164]demonstrations of public opinion, Mr. Grattan brought forward, on the

[095-165]22nd of February, his motion declaratory of the rights of Ireland. An

[095-166]amendment in favour of a six months' postponement of the question was

[095-167]carried; but on the 16th of April, just two years from his first effort

[095-168]on the subject (the administration of Lord North having fallen in the

[095-169]meantime), the orator had the satisfaction of carrying his address

[095-170]declaratory of Irish legislative independence. It was on this occasion

[095-171]that he exclaimed: "I found Ireland on her knees; I watched over her

[095-172]with a paternal solicitude; I have traced her progress from injury to

[095-173]arms, and from arms to liberty. Spirit of Swift! Spirit of Molyneux!

[095-174]your genius has prevailed! Ireland is now a nation! in that new

[095-175]character I hail her! and bowing to her august presence, I say, _Esto

[095-176]perpetua!_"

[095-177]Never was a new nation more nobly heralded into existence! Never was an

[095-178]old nation more reverently and tenderly lifted up and restored! The

[095-179]Houses adjourned to give England time to consider Ireland's

[095-180]_ultimatum_. Within a month it was accepted by the new British

[095-181]administration, and on the 27th of May, the new Whig Viceroy, the Duke

[095-182]of Portland, was authorized to announce from the throne the

[095-183]establishment of the judicial and legislative independence of Ireland.
