CHAPTER III.

[066-001]LORD STRAFFORD'S IMPEACHMENT AND EXECUTION—PARLIAMENT OF 1639-'41—THE

[066-002]INSURRECTION OF 1641—THE IRISH ABROAD.

[066-003]The tragic end of the despot, whose administration we have sketched,

[066-004]was now rapidly approaching. When he deserted the popular ranks in the

[066-005]English House of Commons for a Peerage and the government of Ireland,

[066-006]the fearless Pym prophetically remarked, "Though you have left us, I

[066-007]will not leave you while your head is on your shoulders." Yet, although

[066-008]conscious of having left able and vigilant enemies behind him in

[066-009]England, Strafford proceeded in his Irish administration as if he

[066-010]scorned to conciliate the feelings or interests of any order of men. By

[066-011]the highest nobility, as well as the humblest of the mechanic class,

[066-012]his will was to be received as law; so that neither in Church, nor in

[066-013]State, might any man express even the most guarded doubt as to its

[066-014]infallibility. Lord Mountnorris, for example, having dropped a casual,

[066-015]and altogether innocent remark at the Chancellor's table on the private

[066-016]habits of the Deputy, was brought to trial by court martial on a charge

[066-017]of mutiny, and sentenced to military execution. Though he was not

[066-018]actually put to death, he underwent a long and rigorous imprisonment,

[066-019]and at length was liberated without apology or satisfaction. If they

[066-020]were not so fully authenticated, the particulars of this outrageous

[066-021]case would hardly be credible.

[066-022]The examples of resistance to arbitrary power, which for some years had

[066-023]been shown by both England and Scotland, were not thrown away upon the

[066-024]still worse used Irish. During the seven years of Strafford's iron

[066-025]rule, Hampden had resisted the collection of ship money, Cromwell had

[066-026]begun to figure in the House of Commons, the Solemn League and Covenant

[066-027]was established in Scotland, and the Scots had twice entered England in

[066-028]arms to seal with their blood, if need were, their opposition to an

[066-029]episcopal establishment of religion. It was in 1640, upon the occasion

[066-030]of their second invasion, that Strafford was recalled from Ireland to

[066-031]assume command of the royal forces in the North of England. After a

[066-032]single indecisive campaign, the King entertained the overtures of the

[066-033]Covenanters, and the memorable Long Parliament having met in November,

[066-034]one of its first acts was the impeachment of Strafford for high crimes

[066-035]and misdemeanors. The chief articles against him related to his

[066-036]administration of Irish affairs, and were sustained by delegates from

[066-037]the Irish House of Commons, sent over for that purpose: the whole of

[066-038]the trial deserves to be closely examined by every one interested in

[066-039]the constitutional history of England and Ireland.

[066-040]A third Parliament, known as the 14th, 15th and 16th Charles I., met at

[066-041]Dublin on the 20th March, 1639, was prorogued till June, and adjourned

[066-042]till October. Yielding the point so successfully resisted in 1613, its

[066-043]sittings were held in the Castle, surrounded by the viceregal guard.

[066-044]With one exception, the acts passed in its first session were of little

[066-045]importance, relating only to the allotment of glebe lands and the

[066-046]payment of twentieths. The exception, which followed the voting of four

[066-047]entire subsidies to the King, was an Act ordaining "that this

[066-048]Parliament shall not determine by his Majesty's assent to this and

[066-049]other Bills." A similar statute had been passed in 1635, but was wholly

[066-050]disregarded by Strafford, who no doubt meant to take precisely the same

[066-051]course in the present instance. The members of this Assembly have been

[066-052]severely condemned by modern writers for passing a high eulogium upon

[066-053]Strafford in their first session and reversing it after his fall. But

[066-054]this censure is not well founded. The eulogium was introduced by the

[066-055]Castle party in the Lords, as part of the preamble to the Supply Bill,

[066-056]which, on being returned to the Commons, could only be rejected _in

[066-057]toto_, not amended—a proceeding in the last degree revolutionary. But

[066-058]those who dissented from that ingenious device, at the next session of

[066-059]the House, took care to have their protest entered on the journals and

[066-060]a copy of it despatched to the King. This second proceeding took place

[066-061]in February, 1640, and as the Lord Lieutenant was not arraigned till

[066-062]the month of November following, the usual denunciations of the Irish

[066-063]members are altogether undeserved. At no period of his fortune was the

[066-064]Earl more formidable as an enemy than at the very moment the Protest

[066-065]against "his manner of government" was ordered "to be entered among the

[066-066]Ordinances" of the Commons of Ireland. Nor did this Parliament confine

[066-067]itself to mere protestations against the abuses of executive power. At

[066-068]the very opening of the second session, on the 20th of January, they

[066-069]appointed a committee to wait on the King in England, with instructions

[066-070]to solicit a bill in explanation of Poyning's law, another enabling

[066-071]them to originate bills in Committee of their own House, a right taken

[066-072]away by that law, and to ask the King's consent to the regulation of

[066-073]the courts of law, the collecting of the revenue, and the quartering of

[066-074]soldiers by statute instead of by Orders in Council. On the 16th of

[066-075]February the House submitted a set of queries to the Judges, the nature

[066-076]of which may be inferred from the first question, viz.: "Whether the

[066-077]subjects of this Kingdom be a free people, and to be governed only by

[066-078]the common law of England, and statutes passed in this Kingdom ?" When

[066-079]the answers received were deemed insufficient, the House itself,

[066-080]turning the queries into the form of resolutions, proceeded to vote on

[066-081]them, one by one, affirming in every point the rights, the liberties,

[066-082]and the privileges of their constituents.

[066-084]The impeachment and attainder of Strafford occupied the great part of

[066-085]March and April, 1641, and throughout those months the delegates from

[066-086]Ireland assisted at the pleadings in Westminster Hall and the debates

[066-087]in the English Parliament. The Houses at Dublin were themselves

[066-088]occupied in a similar manner. Towards the end of February articles of

[066-089]impeachment were drawn up against the Lord Chancellor, Bolton, Dr.

[066-090]Bramhall, Bishop of Derry, Chief-Justice Lowther, and Sir George

[066-091]Radcliffe, for conspiring with Strafford to subvert the constitution,

[066-092]and laws, and to introduce an arbitrary and tyrannical government. In

[066-093]March, the King's letter for the continuance of Parliament was laid

[066-094]before the Commons, and on the 3rd of April, his further letter,

[066-095]declaring that all his Majesty's subjects of Ireland "shall, from

[066-096]henceforth, enjoy the benefit of the said graces [of 1628] according to

[066-097]the true intent thereof." By the end of May the Judges, not under

[066-098]impeachment, sent in their answers to the Queries of the Commons, which

[066-099]answers were voted insufficient, and Mr. Patrick Darcy, Member for

[066-100]Navan, was appointed to serve as Proculator at a Conference with the

[066-101]Lords, held on the 9th of June, "in the dining-room of the Castle," in

[066-102]order to set forth the insufficiency of such replies. The learned and

[066-103]elaborate argument of Darcy was ordered to be printed by the House; and

[066-104]on the 26th day of July, previous to their prorogation, they resolved

[066-105]unanimously, that the subjects of Ireland "were a free people, to be

[066-106]governed only by the common law of England, and statutes made and

[066-107]established in the kingdom of Ireland, and according to the lawful

[066-108]custom used in the same." This was the last act of this memorable

[066-109]session; the great northern insurrection in October having, of course,

[066-110]prevented subsequent sessions from being held. Constitutional agitators

[066-111]in modern times have been apt to select their examples of a wise and

[066-112]patriotic parliamentary conduct from the opposition to the Act of Union

[066-113]and the famous struggles of the last century; but whoever has looked

[066-114]into such records as remain to us of the 15th and 16th of Charles

[066-115]First, and the debates on the impeachment of Lord Chancellor Bolton,

[066-116]will, in my opinion, be prepared to admit, that at no period whatever

[066-117]was constitutional law more ably expounded in Ireland than in the

[066-118]sessions of 1640 and 1641; and that not only the principles of Swift

[066-119]and of Molyneux had a triumph in 1782, but the older doctrines also of

[066-120]Sir Ralph Kelly, Audley Mervin, and Patrick Darcy.

[066-121]Strafford's Deputy, Sir Christopher Wandesford, having died before the

[066-122]close of 1640, the King appointed Robert, Lord Dillon, a liberal

[066-123]Protestant, and Sir William Parsons, Lords Justices. But the pressure

[066-124]of Puritan influence in England compelled him in a short time to remove

[066-125]Dillon and substitute Sir John Borlace, Master of the Ordnance—a mere

[066-126]soldier—in point of fanaticism a fitting colleague for Parsons. The

[066-127]prorogation of Parliament soon gave these administrators opportunities

[066-128]to exhibit the spirit in which they proposed to carry on the

[066-129]government. When at a public entertainment in the capital, Parsons

[066-130]openly declared that in twelve months more no Catholics should be seen

[066-131]in Ireland, it was naturally inferred that the Lord Justice spoke not

[066-132]merely for himself but for the growing party of the English Puritans

[066-133]and Scottish Covenanters. The latter had repeatedly avowed that they

[066-134]never would lay down their arms until they had wrought the extirpation

[066-135]of Popery, and Mr. Pym, the Puritan leader in England, had openly

[066-136]declared that his party intended not to leave a priest in Ireland. The

[066-137]infatuation of the unfortunate Charles in entrusting at such a moment

[066-138]the supreme power, civil and military, to two of the devoted partizans

[066-139]of his deadliest enemies, could not fail to arouse the fears of all who

[066-140]felt themselves obnoxious to the fanatical party, either by race or by

[066-141]religion.

[066-142]The aspirations of the chief men among the old Irish for entire freedom

[066-143]of worship, their hopes of recovering at least a portion of their

[066-144]estates, the example of the Scots, who had successfully upheld both

[066-145]their Church and nation against all attempts at English supremacy, the

[066-146]dangers that pressed, and the fears that overhung them, drove many of

[066-147]the very first abilities and noblest characters into the conspiracy

[066-148]which exploded with such terrific energy on the 23rd of October, 1641.

[066-149]The project, though matured on Irish soil, was first conceived among

[066-150]the exiled Catholics, who were to be found at that day in all the

[066-151]schools and camps of Spain, Italy, France and the Netherlands. Philip

[066-152]III. had an Irish legion, under the command of Henry O'Neil, son of

[066-153]Tyrone, which, after his death was transferred to his brother John. In

[066-154]this legion, Owen Roe O'Neil, nephew of Tyrone, learned the art of war,

[066-155]and rose to the rank of Lieutenant-Colonel. The number of Irish serving

[066-156]abroad had steadily increased after 1628, when a license of enlistment

[066-157]was granted by King James. An English emissary, evidently

[066-158]well-informed, was enabled to report, about the year 1630, that there

[066-159]were in the service of the Archduchess Isabella, in the Spanish

[066-160]Netherlands alone, "100 Irish officers able to command companies, and

[066-161]20 fit to be colonels." The names of many others are given as men of

[066-162]noted courage, good engineers, and "well-beloved" captains, both

[066-163]Milesians and Anglo-Irish, residing at Lisbon, Florence, Milan and

[066-164]Naples. The emissary adds that they had long been providing arms for an

[066-165]attempt upon Ireland, "and had in readiness 5,000 or 6,000 arms laid up

[066-166]in Antwerp for that purpose, _bought out of the deduction of their

[066-167]monthly pay_." After the death of the Archduchess, in 1633, an attempt

[066-168]was made by the Franco-Dutch, under Prince Maurice and Marshal

[066-169]Chatillon, to separate the Belgian Provinces from Spain. In the

[066-170]sanguinary battle at Avien victory declared for the French, and on

[066-171]their junction with Prince Maurice, town after town surrendered to

[066-172]their arms. The first successful stand against them was made at

[066-173]Louvain, defended by 4,000 Belgians, Walloons, Spaniards and Irish; the

[066-174]Irish, 1,000 strong, under the command of Colonel Preston, of the

[066-175]Gormanstown family, greatly distinguished themselves. The siege was

[066-176]raised on the 4th of July, 1635, and Belgium was saved for that time to

[066-177]Philip IV. At the capture of Breda, in 1637, the Irish were again

[066-178]honourably conspicuous, and yet more so in the successful defence of

[066-179]Arras, the capital of Artois, three years later. Not yet strengthened

[066-180]by the citadel of Vauban, this ancient Burgundian city, famous for its

[066-181]cathedral and its manufactures, dear to the Spaniards as one of the

[066-182]conquests of Charles V., was a vital point in the campaign of 1640.

[066-183]Besieged by the French, under Marshal Millerie, it held out for several

[066-184]weeks under the command of Colonel Owen Roe O'Neil. The King of France

[066-185]lying at Amiens, within convenient distance, took care that the

[066-186]besiegers wanted for nothing; while the Prince-Cardinal, Ferdinand, the

[066-187]successor of the Archduchess in the government, marched to its relief

[066-188]at the head of his main force with the Imperialists, under Launboy, and

[066-189]the troops of the Duke of Lorrain, commanded by that Prince in person.

[066-190]In an attack on the French lines the Allies were beaten off with loss,

[066-191]and the brave commander was left again unsuccoured in the face of his

[066-192]powerful assailant. Subsequently Don Philip de Silva, General of the

[066-193]Horse to the Prince Cardinal, was despatched to its relief, but failed

[066-194]to effect anything; a failure for which he was court-martialed, but

[066-195]acquitted. The defenders, after exhausting every resource, finally

[066-196]surrendered the place on honourable terms, and marched out covered with

[066-197]glory. These stirring events, chronicled in prose and verse at home,

[066-198]rekindled the martial ardour which had slumbered since the disastrous

[066-199]day of Kinsale.

[066-200]In the ecclesiastics who shared their banishment, the military exiles

[066-201]had a voluntary diplomatic _corps_ who lost no opportunity of advancing

[066-202]the common cause. At Rome, their chief agent was Father Luke Wadding,

[066-203]founder of Saint Isidore's, one of the most eminent theologians and

[066-204]scholars of his age. Through the friendship of Gregory XV. and Urban

[066-205]VIII., many Catholic princes became deeply interested in the religious

[066-206]wars which the Irish of the previous ages had so bravely waged, and

[066-207]which their descendants were now so anxious to renew. Cardinal

[066-208]Richelieu—who wielded a power greater than that of Kings—had favourably

[066-209]entertained a project of invasion submitted to him by the son of Hugh

[066-210]O'Neil, a chief who, while living, was naturally regarded by the exiles

[066-211]as their future leader.

[066-212]To prepare the country for such an invasion (if the return of men to

[066-213]their own country can be called by that name), it was necessary to find

[066-214]an agent with talents for organization, and an undoubted title to

[066-215]credibility and confidence. This agent was fortunately found in the

[066-216]person of Rory or Roger O'Moore, the representative of the ancient

[066-217]chiefs of Leix, who had grown up at the Spanish Court as the friend and

[066-218]companion of the O'Neils. O'Moore was then in the prime of life, of

[066-219]handsome person, and most seductive manners; his knowledge of character

[066-220]was profound; his zeal for the Catholic cause, intense; his personal

[066-221]probity, honour, and courage, undoubted. The precise date of O'Moore's

[066-222]arrival in Ireland is not given in any of the cotemporary accounts, but

[066-223]he seems to have been resident in the country some time previous to his

[066-224]appearance in public life, as he is familiarly spoken of by his English

[066-225]cotemporaries as "Mr. Roger Moore of Ballynagh." During the

[066-226]Parliamentary session of 1640, he took lodgings in Dublin, where he

[066-227]succeeded in enlisting in his plans Conor Maguire, Lord Enniskillen,

[066-228]Philip O'Reilly, one of the members for the county of Cavan, Costelloe

[066-229]McMahon, and Thorlogh O'Neil, all persons of great influence in Ulster.

[066-230]During the ensuing assizes in the Northern Province he visited several

[066-231]country towns, where in the crowd of suitors and defendants he could,

[066-232]without attracting special notice, meet and converse with those he

[066-233]desired to gain over. On this tour he received the important accession

[066-234]of Sir Phelim O'Neil of Kinnaird, in Tyrone, Sir Con Magennis of Down,

[066-235]Colonel Hugh McMahon of Monaghan, and Dr. Heber McMahon, Administrator

[066-236]of Clogher. Sir Phelim O'Neil, the most considerable man of his name

[066-237]tolerated in Ulster, was looked upon as the greatest acquisition, and

[066-238]at his castle of Kinnaird his associates from the neighbouring

[066-239]counties, under a variety of pretexts, contrived frequently to meet.

[066-240]From Ulster, the indefatigable O'Moore carried the threads of the

[066-241]conspiracy into Connaught with equal success, finding both among the

[066-242]nobility and clergy many adherents. In Leinster, among the Anglo-Irish,

[066-243]he experienced the greatest timidity and indifference, but an

[066-244]unforeseen circumstance threw into his hands a powerful lever, to move

[066-245]that province. This was the permission granted by the King to the

[066-246]native regiments, embodied by Strafford, to enter into the Spanish

[066-247]service, if they so desired. His English Parliament made no demur to

[066-248]the arrangement, which would rid the island of some thousands of

[066-249]disciplined Catholics, but several of their officers, under the

[066-250]inspiration of O'Moore, kept their companies together, delaying their

[066-251]departure from month to month. Among these were Sir James Dillon,

[066-252]Colonel Plunkett, Colonel Byrne, and Captain Fox, who, with O'Moore,

[066-253]formed the first directing body of the Confederates in Leinster.

[066-254]In May, 1641, Captain Neil O'Neil arrived from the Netherlands with an

[066-255]urgent request from John, Earl of Tyrone, to all his clansmen to

[066-256]prepare for a general insurrection. He also brought them the cheering

[066-257]news that Cardinal Richelieu—then at the summit of his greatness—had

[066-258]promised the exiles arms, money, and means of transport. He was sent

[066-259]back, almost immediately, with the reply of Sir Phelim, O'Moore and

[066-260]their friends, that they would be prepared to take the field a few days

[066-261]before or after the festival of All Hallows—the 1st of November. The

[066-262]death of Earl John, the last surviving son of the illustrious Tyrone,

[066-263]shortly afterwards, though it grieved the Confederates, wrought no

[066-264]change in their plans. In his cousin-germain, the distinguished

[066-265]defender of Arras, they reposed equal confidence, and their confidence

[066-266]could not have been more worthily bestowed.
