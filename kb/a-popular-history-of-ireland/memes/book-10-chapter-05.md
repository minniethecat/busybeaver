CHAPTER V.

[080-001]KING JAMES IN IRELAND—IRISH PARLIAMENT OF 1689.

[080-002]A few days after his arrival in France, James despatched a messenger to

[080-003]Tyrconnell, with instructions expressing great anxiety as to the state

[080-004]of affairs in Ireland. "I am sure," wrote the fugitive monarch, "you

[080-005]will hold out to the utmost of your power, and I hope this king will so

[080-006]press the Hollanders, that the Prince of Orange will not have men to

[080-007]spare to attack you." All the aid he could obtain from Louis at the

[080-008]moment was 7,000 or 8,000 muskets, which were sent accordingly.

[080-009]Events succeeded each other during the first half of the year 1689 with

[080-010]revolutionary rapidity. The conventions of England and Scotland, though

[080-011]far from being unanimous, declared by immense majorities, that James

[080-012]had abdicated, and that William and Mary should be offered the crowns

[080-013]of both kingdoms. In February, they were proclaimed as king and queen

[080-014]of "England, France, and Ireland," and in May, the Scottish

[080-015]commissioners brought them the tender of the crown of Scotland. The

[080-016]double heritage of the Stuart kings was thus, after nearly a century of

[080-017]possession, transferred by election to a kindred prince, to the

[080-018]exclusion of the direct descendants of the great champion of "the right

[080-019]divine," who first united under his sceptre the three kingdoms.

[080-020]James, at the Court of France, was duly informed of all that passed at

[080-021]London and Edinburgh. He knew that he had powerful partizans in both

[080-022]conventions. The first fever of popular excitement once allayed, he

[080-023]marked with exultation the symptoms of reaction. There was much in the

[080-024]circumstances attending his flight to awaken popular sympathy, and to

[080-025]cast a veil over his errors. The pathetic picture drawn of parental

[080-026]suffering by the great dramatist in the character of King _Lear_,

[080-027]seemed realized to the life in the person of King James. Message

[080-028]followed message from the three kingdoms, urging him to return and

[080-029]place himself at the head of his faithful subjects in a war against the

[080-030]usurper. The French king approved of these recommendations, for in

[080-031]fighting James's battle he was fighting his own, and a squadron was

[080-032]prepared at Brest to carry the fugitive back to his dominions.

[080-033]Accompanied by his natural sons, the Duke of Berwick and the Grand

[080-034]Prior Fitzjames, by Lieutenant-Generals de Rosen and de Maumont,

[080-035]Majors-General de Pusignan and de Lery (or Geraldine), about a hundred

[080-036]officers of all ranks, and 1,200 veterans, James sailed from Brest,

[080-037]with a fleet of 33 vessels, and landed at Kinsale on the 12th day of

[080-038]March (_old style_). His reception by the Southern population was

[080-039]enthusiastic in the extreme. From Kinsale to Cork, from Cork to Dublin,

[080-040]his progress was accompanied by Gaelic songs and dances, by Latin

[080-041]orations, loyal addresses, and all the decorations with which a popular

[080-042]favourite can be welcomed. Nothing was remembered by that easily

[080-043]pacified people but his great misfortunes and his steady fidelity to

[080-044]his and their religion. Fifteen chaplains, nearly all Irish,

[080-045]accompanied him, and added to the delight of the populace; while many a

[080-046]long-absent soldier, now came back in the following of the king, to

[080-047]bless the sight of some aged parent or faithful lover. The royal entry

[080-048]into Dublin was the crowning pageant of this delusive restoration. With

[080-049]the tact and taste for such demonstrations hereditary in the citizens,

[080-050]the trades and arts were marshalled before him. Two venerable harpers

[080-051]played on their national instruments near the gate by which he entered;

[080-052]a number of religious in their robes, with a huge cross at their head,

[080-053]chanted as they went; forty young girls, dressed in white, danced the

[080-054]ancient _Rinka_, scattering flowers as they danced. The Earl of

[080-055]Tyrconnell, lately raised to a dukedom, the judges, the mayor and

[080-056]corporation, completed the procession, which marched over newly sanded

[080-057]streets, beneath arches of evergreens and windows hung with "tapestry

[080-058]and cloth of Arras." Arrived at the castle the sword of state was

[080-059]presented to him by the deputy, and the keys of the city by the

[080-060]recorder. At the inner entrance, the primate, Dr. Dominick Macguire,

[080-061]waited in his robes to conduct him to the chapel, lately erected by

[080-062]Tyrconnell, where _Te Deum_ was solemnly sung. But of all the incidents

[080-063]of that striking ceremonial, nothing more powerfully impressed the

[080-064]popular imagination than the green flag floating from the main tower of

[080-065]the castle, bearing the significant inscription—"_Now or Never—Now and

[080-066]Forever_."

[080-067]A fortnight was devoted by James in Dublin to daily and nightly

[080-068]councils and receptions. The chief advisers who formed his court were

[080-069]the Count d'Avaux, Ambassador of France, the Earl of Melfort, principal

[080-070]Secretary of State, the Duke of Tyrconnell, Lieutenant-General Lord

[080-071]Mountcashel, Chief Justice Nugent, and the superior officers of the

[080-072]army, French and Irish. One of the first things resolved upon at Dublin

[080-073]was the appointment of the gallant Viscount Dundee as

[080-074]Lieutenant-General in Scotland—and the despatch to his assistance of an

[080-075]Irish auxiliary force, which served under that renowned chief with as

[080-076]much honour as their predecessors had served under Montrose.

[080-077]Communications were also opened through the Bishop of Chester with the

[080-078]west of England Jacobites, always numerous in Cheshire, Shropshire, and

[080-079]other counties nearest to Ireland. Certain changes were then made in

[080-080]the Privy Council; Chief Justice Keating's attendance was dispensed

[080-081]with as one opposed to the new policy, but his judicial functions were

[080-082]left untouched. Dr. Cartwright, Bishop of Chester, and the French

[080-083]Ambassador were sworn in, and writs were issued convoking the Irish

[080-084]Parliament for the 7th day of May following.

[080-085]Intermitting, for the present, the military events which marked the

[080-086]early months of the year, we will follow the acts and deliberations of

[080-087]King James's Parliament of 1689. The Houses met, according to summons,

[080-088]at the appointed time, in the building known as "the Inns of Court,"

[080-089]within a stone's throw of the castle. There were present 228 Commoners,

[080-090]and 46 members of the Upper House. In the Lords several Protestant

[080-091]noblemen and prelates took their seats, and some Catholic peers of

[080-092]ancient date, whose attainders had been reversed, were seen for the

[080-093]first time in that generation in the front rank of their order. In the

[080-094]Lower House the University and a few other constituencies were

[080-095]represented by Protestants, but the overwhelming majority were

[080-096]Catholics, either of Norman or Milesian origin. The King made a

[080-097]judicious opening speech, declaring his intention to uphold the rights

[080-098]of property, and to establish liberty of conscience alike for

[080-099]Protestant and Catholic. He referred to the distressed state of trade

[080-100]and manufactures, and recommended to the attention of the Houses, those

[080-101]who had been unjustly deprived of their estates under the "Act of

[080-102]Settlement."

[080-103]Three measures passed by this Parliament entitle its members to be

[080-104]enrolled among the chief assertors of civil and religious liberty. One

[080-105]was the "Act for establishing Liberty of Conscience," followed by the

[080-106]supplemental act that all persons should pay tithes only to the clergy

[080-107]of their own communion. An act abolishing writs of error and appeal

[080-108]into England, established the judicial independence of Ireland; but a

[080-109]still more necessary measure repealing Poyning's Law, was defeated

[080-110]through the personal hostility of the King. An act repealing the Act of

[080-111]Settlement was also passed, under protest from the Protestant Lords,

[080-112]and received the royal sanction. A bill to establish Inns of Court, for

[080-113]the education of Irish law students, was, however, rejected by the

[080-114]King, and lost; an "Act of Attainder," against persons in arms against

[080-115]the Sovereign, whose estates lay in Ireland, was adopted. Whatever may

[080-116]be the bias of historians, it cannot be denied that this Parliament

[080-117]showed a spirit worthy of the representatives of a free people. "Though

[080-118]Papists," says Mr. Grattan, our highest parliamentary authority, "they

[080-119]were not slaves; they wrung a constitution from King James before they

[080-120]accompanied him to the field."

[080-122]The King, unfortunately, had not abandoned the arbitrary principles of

[080-123]his family, even in his worst adversity. His interference with the

[080-124]discussions on Poyning's Law, and the Inns of Court bill, had shocked

[080-125]some of his most devoted adherents. But he proceeded from obstructive

[080-126]to active despotism. He doubled, by his mere proclamation, the enormous

[080-127]subsidy of 20,000 pounds monthly voted him by the Houses. He

[080-128]established, by the same authority, a bank, and decreed in his own name

[080-129]a bank restriction act. He debased the coinage, and established a fixed

[080-130]scale of prices to be observed by all merchants and traders. In one

[080-131]respect—but in one only—he grossly violated his own professed purpose

[080-132]of establishing liberty of conscience, by endeavouring to force fellows

[080-133]and scholars on the University of Dublin contrary to its statutes. He

[080-134]even went so far as to appoint a provost and librarian without consent

[080-135]of the senate. However we may condemn the exclusiveness of the College,

[080-136]this was not the way to correct it; bigotry on the one hand, will not

[080-137]justify despotism on the other.

[080-138]More justifiable was the interference of the King for the restoration

[080-139]of rural schools and churches, and the decent maintenance of the clergy

[080-140]and bishops. His appointments to the bench were also, with one or two

[080-141]exceptions, men of the very highest character. "The administration of

[080-142]justice during this brief period," says Dr. Cooke Taylor, "deserves the

[080-143]highest praise. With the exception of Nugent and Fritton, the Irish

[080-144]judges would have been an honour to any bench."
