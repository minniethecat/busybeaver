## Overview

Roland FitzEustace, 1st Baron Portlester, was one of the most powerful Anglo-Irish statesmen of the fifteenth century. Serving as both Chancellor and Lord Treasurer of Ireland, he was the principal legal officer of the Irish administration and the closest political ally — and father-in-law — of Gerald FitzGerald, the 8th Earl of Kildare, known as the "Great Earl." His career spanned the turbulent decades of the Wars of the Roses, during which the Anglo-Irish nobility navigated between the rival houses of York and Lancaster.

## Rise to Power

Portlester belonged to the FitzEustace family, one of the prominent Anglo-Norman houses of the Pale. His rise to the highest offices of the Irish state coincided with the ascendancy of the Kildare Geraldines. The Earl of Kildare, who dominated Irish affairs for over thirty years, cemented his alliance with Portlester through marriage: he "married one of his daughters to Preston, Baron of Naas, and another to the first Lord Portlester" (042-226). This family connection made Portlester not only Kildare's political ally but a member of his inner circle.

As Lord Treasurer under Edward IV, Portlester controlled the finances of the Irish administration. His position gave him leverage over the distribution of Crown revenues and the patronage that sustained the Kildare interest. The office was one of the three great appointments of the medieval Irish government, alongside the Lord Deputy and the Lord Chancellor.

## The Parliament at Naas

Portlester's most dramatic political intervention came during the contest between the Earl of Kildare and Lord Grey, the English-born Deputy sent by Henry VII to curb the Geraldine ascendancy. When Grey arrived in Ireland to assert royal authority, Kildare refused to surrender the Great Seal and called his own Parliament at Naas.

> Lord Portlester also, the Chancellor, and father-in-law to Kildare, joined that Earl in his Parliament at Naas with the great seal of England. (045-089)

This was an act of open defiance against the Crown. By taking the Great Seal to Kildare's rival assembly, Portlester lent legal legitimacy to what was effectively a rebellion against the King's appointed Deputy. The existence of two Parliaments — Grey's in Dublin and Kildare's at Naas — created a constitutional crisis that exposed the limits of Tudor authority in Ireland.

The contest was eventually resolved by compromise: "Acts of both Parliaments — Grey's and Kildare's — were by compromise confirmed" (045-098), and Kildare was acknowledged as Lord Deputy. Portlester's loyalty had been crucial to Kildare's survival, and he retained the Chancellorship.

## Removal from Office

Portlester's long tenure was not without reverses. The accession of Henry VII and the shifting fortunes of the Yorkist cause brought new dangers. The King, suspicious of the Kildare-Geraldine faction and its Yorkist sympathies, moved to curtail their power. McGee records that "he removed Lord Portlester from the Treasurership, which he conferred upon a Butler" (045-264). This act was part of a broader Tudor strategy of balancing the great Anglo-Irish families — Geraldines against Butlers — to prevent any one faction from dominating the Irish government.

The loss of the Treasurership, however, did not destroy Portlester's influence. He retained the Chancellorship for several more years, and his son, Lord Thomas Fitzgerald, briefly held the same office before resigning "the Chancellorship to take the command" (045-152) of an expedition to England in support of the Yorkist pretender Lambert Simnel.

## The Yorkist Connection and Simnel Affair

Portlester and his Kildare allies were deeply implicated in the Yorkist cause. In 1487, the Kildare faction — "the Deputy, Chancellor, and Treasurer were present" (045-146) — attended the coronation of Lambert Simnel as King Edward VI in Christ Church Cathedral, Dublin. A Parliament was convoked in Simnel's name, and an expeditionary force was assembled for an invasion of England. Portlester, as Chancellor, was at the centre of this extraordinary episode in which the Anglo-Irish nobility attempted to overthrow the Tudor dynasty.

The Simnel invasion ended in defeat at the Battle of Stoke Field (1487), but the Kildare faction survived. Henry VII, lacking the resources to govern Ireland directly, pardoned the leading rebels and confirmed Kildare as Deputy. Portlester likewise retained his position, a testament to the indispensability of the local magnates in governing the medieval Irish lordship.

## Legacy

Lord Portlester represents the apogee of Anglo-Irish noble power in the fifteenth century — a period when great families like the Geraldines, Butlers, and Burkes governed Ireland with minimal interference from England. As Chancellor and Treasurer, he embodied the legal and financial autonomy of the Dublin administration. His alliance with the Earl of Kildare, cemented by marriage, created a political dynasty that dominated Irish affairs until the Tudor reconquest of the sixteenth century. The FitzEustace family, though less famous than their Geraldine relatives, remained a significant force in the Pale into the early modern period.
