Randal MacDonnell, 1st Earl of Antrim, was a prominent Catholic peer of the seventeenth century, deeply involved in the tumultuous events of the Confederate Wars and the Williamite War. A descendant of the Gaelic MacDonnells of the Glens, his family had long controlled the northeastern coast of Ulster, including the formidable stronghold of Dunluce Castle. (068-127)

McGee records Lord Antrim in connection with several critical episodes: the Confederation of Kilkenny, the arrival of Scottish forces under General Munro, and the closing of the gates of Derry against his regiment in December 1688. (079-161)

## The Confederate Wars and Dunluce

During the Confederate Wars of the 1640s, Antrim's position was complex. His brother, Sir Alexander McDonnell — known by the Gaelic war-name Colkitto — gained fame as a commander in the Irish and Scottish campaigns. McGee records the presence of Colkitto with the northern forces, attributing to him a share in the military successes of the Confederate armies. (068-127)

> "to Lord Antrim, the famous Colkitto of the Irish and Scottish wars." [068-127]

Antrim's own fortunes suffered a severe blow when General Robert Munro, the Scottish commander, took Dunluce Castle from him by a stratagem similar to that employed by Sir Phelim O'Neill at Charlemont. Munro invited himself as a guest and arrested his host at his own table. The loss of Dunluce was a significant reverse for the Confederate interest in the north. (068-254)

> "Monroe took Dunluce from Lord Antrim by the same stratagem by which Sir Phelim took Charlemont—inviting himself as a guest, and arresting his host at his own table." [068-254]

McGee notes that a want of cordial co-operation between the Scottish commander Munro and the northern Irish leader [[Owen Roe O'Neill|Owen Roe O'Neill]] prevented them from following up these advantages. The mutual suspicion between the Gaelic Irish and the Scottish Presbyterians undermined the Confederate military position in Ulster. (068-256)

## The Closing of the Gates of Derry

The most dramatic episode associated with Lord Antrim occurred in December 1688. James II, aiming to secure Derry for the Jacobite cause, ordered Antrim's regiment of Highlanders to garrison the city. Antrim marched his men toward Derry, but the citizens, overwhelmingly Protestant and sympathetic to William of Orange, refused to admit them. (079-161)

> "on the 7th of December the gates of Derry were shut by 'the youthhood' against the Earl of Antrim and his Highlanders." [079-161]

The "youthhood" of Derry — the young apprentices and townsmen — took the decisive action of closing the Watergate against Antrim's regiment. This event marked the beginning of the famous Siege of Derry, one of the most celebrated episodes of the Williamite War. Within the walls, the citizens established an improvised government including eighteen clergymen and the town democracy. (081-042)

> "It was now four months since 'the youthhood' of Derry had shut the Watergate against Lord Antrim's regiment, and established within their walls a strange sort of government, including eighteen clergymen and the town democracy." [081-042]

Enniskillen was seized by a similar impulse of the popular will. An association quickly formed throughout Ulster in imitation of the English Convention Parliament. The closing of the gates of Derry became an emblem of Protestant resistance to James II's authority. (079-162)

## Historical Significance

Lord Antrim occupies a distinctive place in Irish history as a Catholic peer caught between the demands of his faith and the shifting political landscape of the seventeenth century. His family's ancient seat at Dunluce, his brother Colkitto's martial reputation, and his own role in the events that triggered the Siege of Derry make him a figure of enduring interest. The MacDonnells of Antrim exemplified the complex allegiances of the Gaelic aristocracy in an era of profound political and religious transformation. (081-044)
