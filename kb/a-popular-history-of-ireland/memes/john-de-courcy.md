## Overview

John de Courcy (c. 1150–c. 1219) was an Anglo-Norman knight who conquered the kingdom of Ulidia (eastern [[ulster|Ulster]]) and became the first Earl of Ulster. Among all the Norman adventurers who followed the initial invasion of Ireland, de Courcy stood out as the most daring and romantic figure—the "gallant giant," as the chroniclers described him, whose personal courage and military prowess made him the model of knighthood in his age. (028-087) (029-086)

Arriving in Ireland in 1176 as part of the second wave of Norman settlement, de Courcy carved out a lordship in the north of Ireland through a combination of audacious military campaigns and strategic castle-building. His conquest of Down and his plantation of Norman settlers there altered the political geography of [[ulster|Ulster]] for centuries. (027-176) (028-207)

## Biography

### Arrival and Early Campaigns

John de Courcy arrived in Ireland with the second wave of Norman adventurers, following in the wake of the initial conquest. Alongside [[hugh-de-lacy|Hugh de Lacy]], [[hugh-de-lacy|Lord of Meath]], and other Norman barons, de Courcy was granted extensive liberties by [[henry-ii|Henry II]] to carve out territories for himself—if he could conquer them. The king's patent for creating [[hugh-de-lacy|Hugh de Lacy]], [[hugh-de-lacy|Lord of Meath]], or John de Courcy, Earl of Ulster, shows the deliberate policy of encouraging private conquest under royal sanction. (026-144) (027-176)

### The Conquest of Ulster

De Courcy's most celebrated achievement was the conquest of the kingdom of Ulidia, the eastern portion of [[ulster|Ulster]]. This enterprise established de Courcy as a figure of legend. Baron of Stoke Courcy in Somerset, he gathered a small force of knights and men-at-arms and marched north from [[dublin|Dublin]] into the territories of the Irish lords of Down and Antrim. (028-087)

> "This was John de Courcy, Baron of Stoke Courcy, in Somersetshire, whose name is associated with the conquest of a large portion of Ulster." (028-087)

His companions in this northern enterprise included Jourdain de Courcy, Sir Robert de la Poer, Sir Geoffrey and Walter de Marisco, and other knights whose names became attached to the Norman settlement of [[ulster|Ulster]]. The bearings of de Courcy, mounted on a white horse, became famous throughout the Norman world—the arms of the de Courcy's, described as mounted on a white horse which de Courcy himself rode into battle, emblazoned the banners of the conqueror of [[ulster|Ulster]]. (028-093) (028-102)

In the year following their first success, de Courcy and his men fought the combined forces of Down, Louth, and the surrounding districts. De Courcy's companions stuck to their conquests with the tenacity of Norman settlers everywhere, and the plantation of Down took root. (028-113) (028-128)

### The Campaign against Connaught

De Courcy's ambition extended beyond [[ulster|Ulster]]. In the same year he took up the enterprise against Connaught, in which Milo de Cogan had so signally failed and from which even de Lacy had, for reasons of his own, refrained. The feuds of the O'Conor family were again the pretext and the ground of hope for the invaders. (029-099) (029-101)

However, the campaign proved disastrous. Donald More O'Brien, victorious on the Suir and the Shannon, carried his strong succours to Conor Moinmoy, and both powers combined marched against de Courcy. Unprepared for this junction, the Norman retreated towards Sligo, and had reached Ballysadare when Flaherty, Lord of Tyrconnell, came against them from the opposite point. Thus placed between two fires, they were forced to fly through the rugged passes of the Curlieu mountains, skirmishing as they went. (029-105) (029-109)

> "Thus placed between two fires, they were forced to fly through the rugged passes of the Curlieu mountains, skirmishing as they went." (029-109)

### Piety and Legend

De Courcy was renowned not only for his military prowess but also for his deep personal piety. We are told that he always carried about his person a copy of the prophecies attributed to Columbcille, and when in the year 1186 the relics of the three great saints—Patrick, Brigid, and Columbcille—whose dust sanctifies Downpatrick, were supposed to be discovered by the Bishop of Down in a dream, de Courcy caused them to be translated to the altar-side with all suitable reverence. (029-088) (029-093)

Yet all his devotions and pilgrimages did not prevent him from pushing on the work of conquest whenever occasion offered. His plantation in Down had time to take root from the unexpected death of Donald, Prince of Aileach, in an encounter with the garrison of one of de Courcy's new castles near Newry in 1188. (029-095) (029-097)

### Service under Prince John and Decline

When Prince John Lackland arrived in Ireland in 1185, John de Courcy was appointed his lieutenant—a position that brought him into close contact with the future king. De Courcy and the principal Norman nobles hastened to receive the young Prince at Waterford, and de Courcy served as the military commander during John's ill-fated expedition. (029-048) (029-056)

The title of Earl of Ulster was created for de Courcy in 1181, formally recognizing his conquest. However, his later years were marked by conflict with the sons of [[hugh-de-lacy|Hugh de Lacy]]. Warring with the de Lacys and seeking refuge among the clansmen of Tyrone, de Courcy eventually disappeared from the stage of Irish affairs. He is said to have passed on to England and ended his days in prison, a victim to the caprice or jealousy of King John. The title of Earl of Ulster was transferred in 1205, by royal patent, to Walter de Lacy. (029-194) (029-182) (030-160)

## Legacy

### The de Courcy Legend

Many tales are told of de Courcy's matchless intrepidity. His indirect descendants, the Barons of Kinsale, claim the right to wear their hats before the King in consequence of one of these legends, which represents him as the champion knight of England, taken from a dungeon to uphold the nation's honour against a French challenger. Other tales, as ill authenticated, are founded on his career, which, in its literal truth, is unexcelled for hardihood and adventure except perhaps by the cotemporaneous story of the Lion-Hearted Richard, whom de Courcy closely resembled. (029-186) (029-193)

### His Heirs and Ulster

The title of Earl of Ulster, created for de Courcy in 1181, passed through a complex succession—first to the de Lacy family, then in 1264 to Walter de Burgh, lord of Connaught, through his marriage with Maud de Lacy. From the de Burghs, the title passed in 1354, by the marriage of the fourth female descendant of de Burgh with Lionel, Duke of Clarence, into the royal family of England. Thus the conquests of John de Courcy—the gallant giant who carved out a kingdom in [[ulster|Ulster]] with his own sword—became part of the inheritance of the English Crown. (029-194) (029-198)
