## Overview

James Caulfeild, 1st Earl of Charlemont (1728–1799), was an Irish statesman and the commander-in-chief of the Irish Volunteers during the era of legislative independence. He was a central figure in the Patriot Party and a close associate of both Henry Grattan and Henry Flood. (095-116)

As President of the Volunteer Convention at Dungannon, Lord Charlemont presided over the great extra-parliamentary movement that secured legislative independence for the Irish Parliament in 1782. However, his conservative instincts on the question of Catholic relief limited his legacy. (096-250)

## Early Life and Political Rise

Born into the Anglo-Irish aristocracy, Charlemont came of age during the mid-18th century when the Protestant Ascendancy governed Ireland through a Parliament subordinate to Westminster. His early political education was shaped by the grievances of the Irish Protestant elite, who resented the restrictions placed on Irish trade and legislative autonomy by the British government. (095-116)

Charlemont became a patron of the arts and letters, and his early friendship with Henry Grattan proved formative. Grattan, who later became the pre-eminent parliamentary orator of his age, was described as Charlemont's "early friend and protégé." (105-412)

## The Volunteer Movement

When the American War of Independence drew British troops from Ireland, the Protestant middle class formed the Volunteer companies to defend the country against possible French invasion. Charlemont was elected commander-in-chief of this national force, which soon numbered tens of thousands of armed men. (095-116)

The Volunteers became more than a defensive militia. They transformed into a powerful political movement demanding free trade and legislative independence for the Irish Parliament. Charlemont, alongside Flood and Grattan, received significant addresses from the Volunteer corps throughout the country. (095-116)

## President of the Convention

In 1782, delegates from the Volunteer corps assembled at Dungannon for the great Convention that ratified the demand for Irish legislative independence. Charlemont presided over this assembly as its President, giving his authority and social standing to the cause. (096-250)

The Convention provided the extra-parliamentary pressure that enabled Grattan to secure the repeal of Poynings' Law and the Declaratory Act, establishing the independence of the Irish Parliament. When the British government conceded, Grattan's Parliament, the "Grattan Parliament," was hailed throughout the country. (095-116)

> Lord Charlemont, Mr. Flood, and Mr. Grattan — the three leading figures of the Patriot Party — received significant addresses presented to Lord Camden on the celebrated question of Irish parliamentary independence. [095-116]

## The Catholic Question and Conservative Turn

Charlemont's liberalism had clear limits. When parliamentary reform and the extension of political rights to Catholics were proposed, he aligned with the conservative wing of the Patriot Party. The bill to extend the franchise, supported by Grattan, was resisted by Flood in the Commons and by Lord Charlemont in the Lords. It miscarried, leaving another deposit of disagreement between the actual and the former leaders of the national party. (095-147)

> The reform bill, warmly supported by Grattan, was resisted by Flood in the one House, and Lord Charlemont in the other. It miscarried, and left another deposit of disagreement between the actual and the former leaders. [095-147]

At the Volunteer Convention of 1783, Charlemont used his influence as President to dexterously defeat a motion to re-assemble. Though the Convention had received Father Arthur O'Leary with military honours and contained many warm friends of Catholic rights, the majority — including Charlemont — remained intolerant of religious freedom. (096-250) (096-231) (096-233)

## Later Years and the Act of Union

In the years following the French Revolution, Charlemont watched with alarm as the United Irishmen movement, inspired by French republican principles, gained ground among both Catholics and Presbyterians. He opposed the radical turn of Irish politics and the rebellion of 1798. (105-412)

When Charlemont died in 1799, the Irish Parliament he had helped to liberate was within months of its own extinction through the Act of Union. His early friend Grattan returned to public life in the Imperial Parliament at Westminster to continue the unfinished work of Catholic emancipation. (105-412)

> The return to the kingdom and to public life of Lord Charlemont's early friend and protégé, Henry Grattan, marked the closing chapter of a generation that had briefly achieved Irish legislative independence. [105-412]

## Legacy

Lord Charlemont embodies both the achievements and the limitations of the Protestant Patriot tradition. He was indispensable to the winning of legislative independence in 1782, but his unwillingness to extend political rights to Catholics contributed to the fragility of that settlement. (095-147)

The fort of Charlemont in Ulster, named after the first Earl, became a strategic stronghold in the wars of the 17th century and featured prominently in the rebellion of 1641 — an ironic legacy for a man of the 18th-century Enlightenment. (061-184)
