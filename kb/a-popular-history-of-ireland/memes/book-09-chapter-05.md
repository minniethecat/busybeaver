CHAPTER V.

[068-001]THE CATHOLIC CONFEDERATION—ITS CIVIL GOVERNMENT AND MILITARY

[068-002]ESTABLISHMENT.

[068-003]How a tumultuous insurrection grew into a national organization, with a

[068-004]senate, executive, treasury, army, ships, and diplomacy, we are now to

[068-005]describe. It may, however, be assumed throughout the narrative, that

[068-006]the success of the new Confederacy was quite as much to be attributed

[068-007]to the perverse policy of its enemies as to the counsels of its best

[068-008]leaders. The rising in the midland and Munster counties, and the formal

[068-009]adhesion of the Lords of the Pale, were two of the principal steps

[068-010]towards the end. A third was taken by the Bishops of the Province of

[068-011]Armagh, assembled in Provincial Synod at Kells, on the 22nd of March,

[068-012]1642, where, with the exception of Dease of Meath, they unanimously

[068-013]pronounced "the war just and lawful." After solemnly condemning all

[068-014]acts of private vengeance, and all those who usurped other men's

[068-015]estates, this provincial meeting invited a national synod to meet at

[068-016]Kilkenny on the 10th day of May following. On that day accordingly, all

[068-017]the Prelates then in the country, with the exception of Bishop Dease,

[068-018]met at Kilkenny. There were present O'Reilly, Archbishop of Armagh;

[068-019]Butler, Archbishop of Cashel; O'Kealy, Archbishop of Tuam; David Rothe,

[068-020]the venerable Bishop of Ossory; the Bishops of Clonfert, Elphin,

[068-021]Waterford, Lismore, Kildare, and Down and Conor; the proctors of

[068-022]Dublin, Limerick, and Killaloe, with sixteen other dignitaries and

[068-023]heads of religious orders—in all, twenty-nine prelates and superiors,

[068-024]or their representatives. The most remarkable attendants were,

[068-025]considering the circumstances of their Province, the prelates of

[068-026]Connaught. Strafford's reign of terror was still painfully remembered

[068-027]west of the Shannon, and the immense family influence of Ulick Burke,

[068-028]then Earl, and afterwards Marquis of Clanrickarde, was exerted to

[068-029]prevent the adhesion of the western population to the Confederacy. But

[068-030]the zeal of the Archbishop of Tuam, and the violence of the Governor of

[068-031]Galway, Sir Francis Willoughby, proved more than a counterpoise for the

[068-032]authority of Clanrickarde and the recollection of Strafford: Connaught,

[068-033]though the last to come into the Confederation, was also the last to

[068-034]abandon it.

[068-035]The Synod of Kilkenny proceeded with the utmost solemnity and anxiety

[068-036]to consider the circumstances of their own and the neighbouring

[068-037]kingdoms. No equal number of men could have been found in Ireland, at

[068-038]that day, with an equal amount of knowledge of foreign and domestic

[068-039]politics. Many of them had spent years upon the Continent, while the

[068-040]French Huguenots held their one hundred "cautionary towns," and

[068-041]"leagues" and "associations" were the ordinary instruments of popular

[068-042]resistance in the Netherlands and Germany. Nor were the events

[068-043]transpiring in the neighbouring island unknown or unweighed by that

[068-044]grave assembly. The true meaning and intent of the Scottish and English

[068-045]insurrections were by this time apparent to every one. The previous

[068-046]months had been especially fertile in events, calculated to rouse their

[068-047]most serious apprehensions. In March, the King fled from London to

[068-048]York; in April, the gates of Hull were shut in his face by Hotham, its

[068-049]governor; and in May, the Long Parliament voted a levy of 16,000

[068-050]without the royal authority. The Earl of Warwick had been appointed the

[068-051]Parliamentary commander of the fleet, and the Earl of Essex, their Lord

[068-052]General, with Cromwell as one of his captains. From that hour it was

[068-053]evident the sword alone could decide between Charles and his subjects.

[068-054]In Scotland, too, events were occurring in which Irish Catholics were

[068-055]vitally interested. The contest for the leadership of the Scottish

[068-056]royalists between the Marquises of Hamilton and Montrose had occupied

[068-057]the early months of the year, and given their enemies of the Kirk and

[068-058]the Assembly full time to carry on their correspondence with the

[068-059]English Puritans. In April, all parties in Scotland agreed in

[068-060]despatching a force of 2,500 men, under "the memorable Major Monroe,"

[068-061]for the protection of the Scottish settlers in Ulster. On the 15th of

[068-062]that month this officer landed at Carrickfergus, which was "given up to

[068-063]him by agreement," with the royalist Colonel Chichester; the fortress,

[068-064]which was by much the strongest in that quarter, continued for six

[068-065]years the head-quarters of the Scottish general, with whom we shall

[068-066]have occasion to meet again.

[068-068]The state of Anglo-Irish affairs was for some months one of

[068-069]disorganization and confusion. In January and February the King had

[068-070]been frequently induced to denounce by proclamation his "Irish rebels."

[068-071]He had offered the Parliament to lead their reinforcements in person,

[068-072]had urged the sending of arms and men, and had repeatedly declared that

[068-073]he would never consent to tolerate Popery in that country. He had

[068-074]failed to satisfy his enemies, by these profuse professions had

[068-075]dishonoured himself, and disgusted many who were far from being hostile

[068-076]to his person or family. Parsons and Borlase were still continued in

[068-077]the government, and Coote was entrusted by them, on all possible

[068-078]occasions, with a command distinct from that of Ormond. Having

[068-079]proclaimed the Lords of the Pale rebels for refusing to trust their

[068-080]persons within the walls of Dublin, Coote was employed during January

[068-081]to destroy Swords, their place of rendezvous, and to ravage the estates

[068-082]of their adherents in that neighbourhood. In the same month 1,100

[068-083]veterans arrived at Dublin under Sir Simon Harcourt; early in February

[068-084]arrived Sir Richard Grenville with 400 horse, and soon after

[068-085]Lieutenant-Colonel George Monk, afterwards Duke of Albemarle, with Lord

[068-086]Leicester's regiment, 1,500 strong. Up to this period Ormond had been

[068-087]restrained by the Justices, who were as timid as they were cruel, to

[068-088]operations within an easy march of Dublin. He had driven the O'Moores

[068-089]and their Allies out of Naas; had reinforced some garrisons in Kildare;

[068-090]he had broken up, though not without much loss, an entrenched camp of

[068-091]the O'Byrnes at Kilsalgen wood, on the borders of Dublin; at last the

[068-092]Justices felt secure enough, at the beginning of March, to allow him to

[068-093]march to the relief of Drogheda. Sir Phelim O'Neil had invested the

[068-094]place for more than three months, had been twice repulsed from its

[068-095]walls, made a last desperate attempt, towards the end of February, but

[068-096]with no better success. After many lives were lost the impetuous

[068-097]lawyer-soldier was obliged to retire, and on the 8th of March, hearing

[068-098]of Ormond's approach at the head of 4,000 fresh troops, he hastily

[068-099]retreated northward. On receiving this report, the Justices recalled

[068-100]Ormond to the capital; Sir Henry Tichburne and Lord Moore were

[068-101]despatched with a strong force, on the rear of the Ulster forces, and

[068-102]drove them out of Ardee and Dundalk—the latter after a sharp action.

[068-103]The march of Ormond into Meath had, however, been productive of offers

[068-104]of submission from many of the gentry of the Pale, who attended the

[068-105]meetings at Crofty and Tara. Lord Dunsany and Sir John Netterville

[068-106]actually surrendered on the Earl's guarantee, and were sent to Dublin;

[068-107]Lords Gormanstown, Netterville, and Slane, offered by letter to follow

[068-108]their example; but the two former were, on reaching the city, thrust

[068-109]into the dungeons of the Castle, by order of the Justices; and the

[068-110]proposals of the latter were rejected with contumely. About the same

[068-111]time the Long Parliament passed an act declaring 2,500,000 acres of the

[068-112]property of Irish recusants forfeited to the State, and guaranteeing to

[068-113]all English "adventurers" contributing to the expenses of the war, and

[068-114]all soldiers serving in it, grants of land in proportion to their

[068-115]service and contribution. This act, and a letter from Lord Essex, the

[068-116]Parliamentarian Commander-in-Chief, recommending the transportation of

[068-117]captured recusants to the West Indian Colonies, effectually put a stop

[068-118]to these negotiations. In Ulster, by the end of April, there were

[068-119]19,000 troops, regulars and volunteers, in the garrison or in the

[068-120]field. Newry was taken by Monroe and Chichester, where 80 men and women

[068-121]and 2 priests were put to death. Magennis was obliged to abandon Down,

[068-122]and McMahon Monaghan; Sir Philem was driven to burn Armagh and

[068-123]Dungannon, and to take his last stand at Charlemont. In a severe action

[068-124]with Sir Robert and Sir William Stewart, he had displayed his usual

[068-125]courage with better than his usual fortune, which, perhaps, we may

[068-126]attribute to the presence with him of Sir Alexander McDonnell, brother

[068-127]to Lord Antrim, the famous _Colkitto_ of the Irish and Scottish wars.

[068-128]But the severest defeat which the Confederates had was in the heart of

[068-129]Leinster, at the hamlet of Kilrush, within four miles of Athy. Lord

[068-130]Ormond, returning from a second reinforcement of Naas and other Kildare

[068-131]forts, at the head, by English account, of 4,000 men, found on the 13th

[068-132]of April the Catholics of the midland counties, under Lords

[068-133]Mountgarrett, Ikerrin, and Dunboyne, Sir Morgan Cavenagh, Rory O'Moore,

[068-134]and Hugh O'Byrne, drawn up, by his report, 8,000 strong, to dispute his

[068-135]passage. With Ormond were the Lord Dillon, Lord Brabazon, Sir Richard

[068-136]Grenville, Sir Charles Coote, and Sir T. Lucas. The combat was short

[068-137]but murderous. The Confederates left 700 men, including Sir Morgan

[068-138]Cavenagh, and some other officers, dead on the field; the remainder

[068-139]retreated in disorder, and Ormond, with an inconsiderable diminution of

[068-140]numbers, returned in triumph to Dublin. For this victory the Long

[068-141]Parliament, in a moment of enthusiasm, voted the Lieutenant-General a

[068-142]jewel worth 500 pounds. If any satisfaction could be derived from such

[068-143]an incident, the violent death of their most ruthless enemy, Sir

[068-144]Charles Coote, might have afforded the Catholics some consolation. That

[068-145]merciless saberer, after the combat at Kilrush, had been employed in

[068-146]reinforcing Birr, and relieving the Castle of Geashill, which the Lady

[068-147]Letitia of Offally held against the neighbouring tribe of O'Dempsey. On

[068-148]his return from this service he made a foray against a Catholic force,

[068-149]which had mustered in the neighbourhood of Trim; here, on the night of

[068-150]the 7th of May, heading a sally of his troop, he fell by a musket

[068-151]shot—not without suspicion of being fired from his own ranks. His son

[068-152]and namesake, who imitated him in all things, was ennobled at the

[068-153]restoration by the title of the Earl of Mountrath. In Munster the

[068-154]President St. Leger, though lately reinforced by 1,000 men from

[068-155]England, did not consider himself strong enough for other than

[068-156]occasional forays into the neighbouring county, and little was effected

[068-157]in that Province.

[068-158]Such was the condition of affairs at home and abroad when the National

[068-159]Synod assembled at Kilkenny. As the most popular tribunal invested with

[068-160]the highest moral power in the kingdom, it was their arduous task to

[068-161]establish order and authority among the chaotic elements of the

[068-162]revolution. By the admission of those most opposed to them they

[068-163]conducted their deliberations for nearly three weeks with equal

[068-164]prudence and energy. They first, on the motion of the venerable Bishop

[068-165]Rothe, framed an oath of association to be publicly taken by all their

[068-166]adherents, by the first part of which they were bound to bear "true

[068-167]faith and allegiance" to King Charles and his lawful successors, "to

[068-168]maintain the fundamental laws of Ireland, the free exercise of the

[068-169]Roman Catholic faith and religion." By the second part of this oath all

[068-170]Confederate Catholics—for so they were to be called—as solemnly bound

[068-171]themselves never to accept or submit to any peace "without the consent

[068-172]and approbation of the general assembly of the said Confederate

[068-173]Catholics." They then proceeded to make certain constitutions,

[068-174]declaring the war just and lawful; condemning emulations and

[068-175]distinctions founded on distinctions of race, such as "new" and "old

[068-176]Irish;" ordaining an elective council for each Province; and a Supreme

[068-177]or National Council for the whole kingdom; condemning as excommunicate

[068-178]all who should, having taken the oath, violate it, or who should be

[068-179]guilty of murder, violence to persons, or plunder under pretence of the

[068-180]war. Although the attendance of the lay leaders of the movement at

[068-181]Kilkenny was far from general, the exigencies of the case compelled

[068-182]them, to nominate, with the concurrence of the Bishops, the first

[068-183]Supreme Council of which Lord Mountgarrett was chosen President, and

[068-184]Mr. Richard Belling, an accomplished writer and lawyer, Secretary. By

[068-185]this body a General Assembly of the entire Nation was summoned to meet

[068-186]at the same city, on the 23rd of October following—the anniversary of

[068-187]the Ulster rising, commonly called by the English party "Lord Maguire's

[068-188]day." The choice of such an occasion by men of Mountgarrett's and

[068-189]Selling's moderation and judgment, six months after the date of the

[068-190]alleged "massacre," would form another proof, if any were now needed,

[068-191]that none of the alleged atrocities were yet associated with the memory

[068-192]of that particular day.

[068-193]The events of the five months, which intervened between the adjournment

[068-194]of the National Synod at the end of May, and the meeting of the General

[068-195]Assembly on the 23rd of October, may best be summed up under the head

[068-196]of the respective provinces. I. The oath of Confederation was taken

[068-197]with enthusiasm in Munster, a Provincial Council elected, and General

[068-198]Barry chosen Commander-in-Chief. Barry made an attempt upon Cork, which

[068-199]was repulsed, but a few days later the not less important city of

[068-200]Limerick opened its gates to the Confederates, and on the 21st of June

[068-201]the citadel was breached and surrendered by Courtenay, the Governor. On

[068-202]the 2nd of July St. Leger died at Cork (it was said of vexation for the

[068-203]loss of Limerick), and the command devolved on his son-in-law, Lord

[068-204]Inchiquin, a pupil of the school of Wards, and a soldier of the school

[068-205]of Sir Charles Coote. With Inchiquin was associated the Earl of

[068-206]Barrymore for the civil administration, but on Barrymore's death in

[068-207]September both powers remained for twelve months in the hands of the

[068-208]survivor. The gain of Limerick was followed by the taking of Loughgar

[068-209]and Askeaton, but was counterbalanced by the defeat of Liscarroll, when

[068-210]the Irish loss was 800 men, with several colours; Inchiquin reported

[068-211]only 20 killed, including the young lord Kinalmeaky, one of the five

[068-212]sons whom the Earl of Cork gave to this war. II. In Connaught, Lord

[068-213]Clanrickarde was still enabled to avert a general outbreak. In vain the

[068-214]western Prelates besought him in a pathetic remonstrance to place

[068-215]himself at the head of its injured inhabitants, and take the command of

[068-216]the Province. He continued to play a middle part between the President,

[068-217]Lord Ranelagh, Sir Charles Coote the younger, and Willoughby, Governor

[068-218]of Galway, until the popular impatience burst all control. The chief of

[068-219]the O'Flahertys seized Clanrickarde's castle, of Aughrenure, and the

[068-220]young men of Galway, with a skill and decision quite equal to that of

[068-221]the Derry apprentices of an after day, seized an English ship

[068-222]containing arms and supplies, lying in the bay, marched to the Church

[068-223]of Saint Nicholas, took the Confederate oath, and shut Willoughby up in

[068-224]the citadel. Clanrickarde hastened to extinguish this spark of

[068-225]resistance, and induced the townsmen to capitulate on his personal

[068-226]guarantee. But Willoughby, on the arrival of reinforcements, under the

[068-227]fanatical Lord Forbes, at once set the truce made by Clanrickarde at

[068-228]defiance, burned the suburbs, sacked the Churches, and during August

[068-229]and September, exercised a reign of terror in the town. About the same

[068-230]time local risings took place in Sligo, Mayo, and Roscommon, at first

[068-231]with such success that the President of the Province, Lord Ranelagh,

[068-232]shut himself up in the castle of Athlone, where he was closely

[068-233]besieged. III. In Leinster, no military movement of much importance was

[068-234]made, in consequence of the jealousy the Justices entertained of

[068-235]Ormond, and the emptiness of the treasury. In June, the Long Parliament

[068-236]remitted over the paltry sum of 11,500 pounds to the Justices, and

[068-237]2,000 of the troops, which had all but mutinied for their pay, were

[068-238]despatched under Ormond to the relief of Athlone. Commissioners arrived

[068-239]during the summer, appointed by the Parliament to report on the affairs

[068-240]of Ireland, to whom the Justices submitted a penal code worthy of the

[068-241]brain of Draco or Domitian; Ormond was raised to the rank of Marquis,

[068-242]by the King; while the army he commanded grew more and more divided, by

[068-243]intrigues emanating from the castle and beyond the channel. Before the

[068-244]month of October, James Touchet, Earl of Castlehaven, an adventurous

[068-245]nobleman, possessed of large estates both in Ireland and England,

[068-246]effected his escape from Dublin Castle, where he had been imprisoned on

[068-247]suspicion by Parsons and Borlase, and joined the Confederation at

[068-248]Kilkenny. In September, Colonel Thomas Preston, the brave defender of

[068-249]Louvain, uncle to Lord Gormanstown, landed at Wexford, with three

[068-250]frigates and several transports, containing a few siege guns, field

[068-251]pieces, and other stores, 500 officers, and a number of engineers. IV.

[068-252]In Ulster, where the first blow was struck, and the first hopes were

[068-253]excited, the prospect had become suddenly overclouded. Monroe took

[068-254]Dunluce from Lord Antrim by the same stratagem by which Sir Phelim took

[068-255]Charlemont—inviting himself as a guest, and arresting his host at his

[068-256]own table. A want of cordial co-operation between the Scotch commander

[068-257]and "the Undertakers" alone prevented them extinguishing, in one

[068-258]vigorous campaign, the northern insurrection. So weak and disorganized

[068-259]were now the thousands who had risen at a bound one short year before,

[068-260]that the garrisons of Enniskillen, Derry, Newry, and Drogheda, scoured

[068-261]almost unopposed the neighbouring counties. The troops of Cole,

[068-262]Hamilton, the Stewarts, Chichesters, and Conways, found little

[068-263]opposition, and gave no quarter. Sir William Cole, among his claims of

[068-264]service rendered to the State, enumerated "7,000 of the rebels famished

[068-265]to death," within a circuit of a few miles from Enniskillen. The

[068-266]disheartened and disorganized natives were seriously deliberating a

[068-267]wholesale emigration to the Scottish highlands, when a word of magic

[068-268]effect was whispered from the sea coast to the interior. On the 6th of

[068-269]July, Colonel Owen Roe O'Neil arrived off Donegal with a single ship, a

[068-270]single company of veterans, 100 officers, and a considerable quantity

[068-271]of ammunition. He landed at Doe Castle, and was escorted by his

[068-272]kinsman, Sir Phelim, to the fort of Charlemont. A general meeting of

[068-273]the northern clans was quickly called at Clones, in Monaghan, and

[068-274]there, on an early day after his arrival, Owen O'Neil was elected

[068-275]"General-in-Chief of the Catholic Army" of the North, Sir Phelim

[068-276]resigning in his favour, and taking instead the barren title of

[068-277]"President of Ulster." At the same moment Lord Lieven arrived from

[068-278]Scotland with the remainder of the 10,000 voted by the Parliament of

[068-279]that kingdom. He had known O'Neil abroad, had a high opinion of his

[068-280]abilities, and wrote to express his surprise "that a man of his

[068-281]reputation should be engaged in so bad a cause;" to which O'Neil

[068-282]replied that "he had a better right to come to the relief of his own

[068-283]country than his lordship had to march into England against his lawful

[068-284]King." Lieven, before returning home, urged Monroe to act with

[068-285]promptitude, for that he might expect a severe lesson if the new

[068-286]commander once succeeded in collecting an army. But Monroe proved deaf

[068-287]to this advice, and while the Scottish and English forces in the

[068-288]Province would have amounted, if united, to 20,000 foot and 1,000

[068-289]horse, they gave O'Neil time enough to embody, officer, drill, and arm

[068-290](at least provisionally), a force not to be despised by even twice

[068-291]their numbers.
