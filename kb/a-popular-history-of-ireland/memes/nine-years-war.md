# Nine Years' War

The **Nine Years' War** (1594–1603), also known as Tyrone's Rebellion, was the largest military conflict of the Elizabethan period in Ireland. Led by [[hugh-oneill|Hugh O'Neil]]l, Earl of [[Tyrone]], the war united several Gaelic Irish lords in a concerted resistance to the extension of English rule, culminating in a Spanish intervention and the decisive English victory at Kinsale.

## Background

The war arose from the encroachment of English authority upon the traditional autonomy of the Gaelic lords of [[ulster|Ulster]]. [[hugh-oneill|Hugh O'Neil]]l, who had been raised in English custody and knighted by the crown, initially cooperated with the [[dublin|Dublin]] administration. However, the appointment of English sheriffs in his territory and the aggressive policies of the English Deputy provoked him into rebellion.

[[hugh-oneill|The O'Neill]]s of Tyrone had long been one of the most powerful families in Gaelic Ireland, their lordship extending over much of central and eastern [[ulster|Ulster]].

## The Course of the War

O'Neill secured alliances with other [[ulster|Ulster]] lords, notably the O'Donnells of Tyrconnell, and sought Spanish support against the English. The war was characterised by O'Neill's skilled use of terrain and guerrilla tactics, which frustrated English armies sent against him.

## The Battle of Kinsale

The turning point of the war came in 1601, when a Spanish expeditionary force landed at Kinsale in the south of Ireland. O'Neill marched his army the length of Ireland to join the Spanish, but the English forces under Mountjoy defeated the combined Irish and Spanish force at the [[Battle of Kinsale]] in December 1601.

The defeat at Kinsale broke the back of the rebellion. O'Neill retreated to [[ulster|Ulster]] and held out for another year before surrendering in 1603, just days after the death of [[elizabeth-i|Queen Elizabeth]].

## Aftermath: The Flight of the Earls

The Treaty of Mellifont (1603) allowed O'Neill to retain his lands and title, but under increased English supervision. Growing pressure from English officials led O'Neill and other [[ulster|Ulster]] lords to depart Ireland in 1607 in what became known as the [[Flight of the Earls]]. Their departure opened the way for the [[Plantation of Ulster]].

The Nine Years' War marked the end of the old Gaelic order. The defeat of O'Neill and the subsequent plantation transformed [[ulster|Ulster]] from the most Gaelic of the provinces into the centre of English and Scottish settlement, a change with profound consequences for Irish history.
