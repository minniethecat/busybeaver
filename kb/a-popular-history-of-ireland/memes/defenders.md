The **Defenders** were a Catholic secret society that emerged in the 1780s in County [[Armagh]], [[Ulster]], as a defensive response to attacks by Protestant gangs—the so-called "Peep o' Day Boys" who raided Catholic homes to seize arms. The movement rapidly spread beyond [[Ulster]] into [[Leinster]] and [[Munster]], evolving from a local agrarian defense organization into a politicized revolutionary movement. By the mid-1790s, the Defenders had forged an alliance with the [[United Irishmen]], contributing their extensive network of popular support to the [[Rebellion of 1798]].

## Origins in Sectarian Conflict

The Defenders arose from the sectarian tensions of late 18th-century [[Armagh]], where competition for land between Catholic and Protestant tenants, combined with the relaxation of the [[Penal Laws]], fueled communal violence. The "Peep o' Day Boys" conducted dawn raids on Catholic homes, and the Defenders organized in response to protect Catholic communities:

> "Defenders, and a dozen other denominations of secret associations, sprang from the social disorganization of the eighteenth century." (093-094)

## Organization and Ideology

Unlike earlier agrarian movements such as the [[Whiteboys]], the Defenders developed an elaborate organizational structure with oaths, passwords, and a hierarchical command system. Their ideology combined Catholic sectarian identity with emerging democratic and republican ideas influenced by the French Revolution. By the 1790s, their rhetoric increasingly adopted the language of national liberation:

> "The system, and as patrons of the Defenders; the telling argument used everywhere was that the Catholics had nothing to hope from an Irish Parliament." (098-151)

## Alliance with the United Irishmen

The Defenders' extensive rural network proved invaluable to the [[United Irishmen]] as they prepared for insurrection in the 1790s. The two organizations, though differing in their social composition (the [[united-irishmen|United Irishmen]] were predominantly middle-class and Protestant), found common ground in their opposition to the [[protestant-ascendancy|Protestant Ascendancy]] and their demand for parliamentary reform. The alliance was sealed in 1795, and Defender units fought alongside United Irish forces during the [[Rebellion of 1798]]:

> "The country between Orangemen and Defenders. Thus it came, that the rising of 1798 was as much a jacquerie as a political revolution." (099-207)

## Legacy

The Defenders movement bridged the gap between the older tradition of agrarian secret societies and the modern revolutionary nationalism of the 19th century. After the defeat of 1798, Defender cells persisted in rural areas, eventually being absorbed into the [[Ribbonmen]] and later the [[Fenian Brotherhood]]. Their alliance with the [[united-irishmen|United Irishmen]] demonstrated the potential of cross-sectarian cooperation that would inspire later nationalist movements.
