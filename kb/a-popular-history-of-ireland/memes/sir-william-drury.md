## Overview

Sir William Drury (d. 1579) was an English soldier and colonial administrator who served Elizabeth I in Ireland during a particularly violent phase of the Tudor conquest. He was appointed Lord Deputy in 1578 and died in the field the following year while attempting to suppress the Desmond Rebellion in Munster.

## Early Service as Lord Justice

Before his elevation to the deputyship, Drury served as Lord Justice of Ireland. In this capacity, he was involved in the coercive enforcement of the Protestant Reformation. (056-154)

His methods were severe. When acting as Lord Justice, he dealt harshly with those who resisted English authority, sending suspects to Kilmallock for interrogation and punishment. (056-163)

## Lord Deputyship and Religious Enforcement

Drury was appointed Lord Deputy in 1578, and his administration intensified the policy of religious conformity by force. He imposed penalties on the citizens of Kilkenny, binding several of them under a penalty of forty pounds each to attend the English Church service, and authorized the Anglican Bishop to levy a rate for the repair of the church. (063-005, 063-006, 063-007)

> Thus, when Sir William Drury was Deputy, in 1578, he bound several citizens of Kilkenny, under a penalty of 40 pounds each, to attend the English Church service, and authorized the Anglican Bishop "to make a rate for the repair of the Church, and to distrain for the payment of it." (063-005, 063-006, 063-007, 063-008)

The harshness of his regime is further underscored by the thirty-six executions he ordered in the same city. Among those put to death were "a blackamoor and two witches," condemned by what contemporaries described as the law of nature, since no positive statute against witchcraft yet existed in Ireland. (063-010, 063-011, 063-012, 063-013)

## The Desmond Campaign and Death

In the spring of 1579, with the Desmond Rebellion spreading through Munster, Drury fixed his headquarters at the walled town of Kilmallock. The town had been refortified after its destruction by Fitzmaurice eleven years earlier, and now stood with strong walls lined with earthworks and crowned by towers. (057-054, 057-055, 057-056)

He summoned the Queen's lieges in Munster and assembled a force of not less than a thousand English regulars, supplemented by perhaps twice that number under the Munster "Undertakers." (057-058, 057-059, 057-060)

Drury made an attempt to strike at the Geraldine encampment at Kilmore, but the operation ended disastrously. One division of his force was cut to pieces — three hundred men by the Irish account, two hundred by the English — along with their captains Herbert, Price, and Eustace. The remainder retreated in disorder to Athneasy. (057-061, 057-062, 057-063, 057-064)

For nine weeks Drury remained in the field without gaining any advantage, harassed day and night by his adversaries until his health collapsed under the strain. (057-066, 057-067, 057-068)

> For nine weeks Drury continued in the field, without gaining any advantage, yet so harassed day and night by his assailants that his health gave way under his anxieties. Despairing of recovery, he was removed by slow stages to Waterford — but died before reaching the first mentioned city. (057-066, 057-067, 057-068, 057-069, 057-070, 057-071)

## Legacy

Drury's death in the field exemplified the gruelling nature of Elizabethan warfare in Ireland, where disease, exhaustion, and guerrilla harassment proved deadlier than pitched battle. His successor, Sir John Perrott, continued his coercive approach to religious conformity, and was himself later recalled on charges of excessive severity against recusants. (063-016, 063-017)

Drury's brief tenure illustrates the Tudor administration's reliance on martial law and religious compulsion in Ireland — methods that deepened the alienation of the native population and helped fuel the very rebellion they were meant to quell.
