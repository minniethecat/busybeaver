## Overview

Maelmurra was the Prince of Leinster in the late 10th and early 11th centuries. His defection from the alliance of High King [[Brian Boru]] after a personal insult at Kinkora led directly to the great Danish invasion of Ireland and the [[Battle of Clontarf]] in 1014, where he fell fighting on the side of the Norse-Danish coalition.

## Biography

### The Chess Game at Kinkora

Maelmurra's fatal falling-out with Brian Boru's family occurred during a game of chess at the royal residence of Kinkora. While playing or advising on the game, Maelmurra made or recommended a false move.

> [014-112] hastened this memorable day. Maelmurra, Prince of Leinster, playing or advising on the game, made, or recommended, a false move, upon which Morrogh, son of Brian, observed, it was no wonder his friends, the Danes, (to whom he owed his elevation,) were beaten at Glen-Mama, if he gave them advice like that. (014-112–014-115)

> [014-116] Maelmurra, highly incensed by this allusion—all the more severe for its bitter truth—arose, ordered his horse, and rode away in haste. (014-116–014-118)

Brian attempted to reconcile by sending a messenger after the indignant guest, but Maelmurra was not to be pacified and refused to return (014-119–014-120).

### Alliance with the Danes

After leaving Kinkora, Maelmurra immediately began conspiring with Danish agents who were always open to such negotiations. These measures led to the great invasion of the Northmen in 1014 (014-121–014-122). Maelmurra mustered all the forces he could command for the expedition, as head of the powerful family of O'Byrne (014-150–014-152).

### The Battle of Clontarf

At the Battle of Clontarf on Good Friday, 1014, Maelmurra commanded the Leinster contingent fighting against Brian Boru's forces:

> [014-197] under Maelmurra, of Leinster, his own tribe, and their kinsmen of Offally and Cullen—the modern Kildare and Wicklow. (014-197–014-198)

> [014-223] Maelmurra of Leinster, with his lords, fell on one side, and Conaing, nephew of Brian, O'Kelly, O'Heyne, and the Stewart of Marr, on the other. Hardly a nobly-born man escaped. (014-223–014-225)

Maelmurra perished in the battle along with many of his lords.

### Aftermath

The consequences of Maelmurra's defeat were felt for generations. In 1018, Brian, son of Maelmurra, fell prisoner, and the Leinster tribes who had joined Maelmurra suffered lasting repercussions (014-240, 016-019, 016-029). By the time of the Norman invasion, the territory once held by Maelmurra had been forfeited (017-070).

## Legacy

Maelmurra is remembered as the "traitor Maelmurra" whose personal pride led to the greatest battle of the Danish era in Ireland. His defection demonstrated the fragility of Brian Boru's pan-Irish alliance and the persistent power of provincial rivalries. Though he fought and died on the losing side at Clontarf, the battle he helped precipitate marked the end of Norse power in Ireland.
