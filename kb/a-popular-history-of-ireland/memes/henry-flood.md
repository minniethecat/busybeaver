## Overview

Henry Flood (1732–1791) was an Irish statesman and one of the most celebrated orators of the 18th-century Irish Parliament. This gentleman, the son of a former Chief Justice, was a man destined to universal reputation in Irish political life. (092-019)

He was only in his 28th year when first elected member for Kilkenny, but in point of genius and acquirements he was already regarded as one of the foremost men of his generation. (092-022) Flood was the original leader of the Patriot Party, a position later contested by his great rival [[Henry Grattan]].

Intermarried into the powerful oligarchical family of the Beresfords, Flood was well connected to the Protestant Ascendancy that dominated Irish public life. (092-020)

## Rise to Political Prominence

Flood entered the Irish House of Commons in 1759 as member for Kilkenny. The son of a former Chief Justice of the King's Bench, he brought to the House both legal learning and an eloquence that quickly marked him out.

> Henry Flood, the son of a former Chief Justice, intermarried into the powerful oligarchical family of the Beresfords, was only in his 28th year when first elected member for Kilkenny; but, in point of genius and acquirements, he was even then the first man in Ireland, and one of the first in the empire. (092-019, 092-022)

For a session or two he silently observed the forms of the House, preparing himself for the great contest to come; but when at last he obtained the ear of his party he was heard to some purpose. (092-024, 092-026)

Flood became the leader of the Patriot Party, which sought to free the Irish Parliament from the constitutional subordination imposed by Poynings' Law and the Declaratory Act.

## Rivalry with Henry Grattan

The defining personal dynamic of Flood's career was his rivalry with [[Henry Grattan]]. Grattan entered Parliament in 1775 while Flood was briefly out of office, and quickly assumed the leadership of the Patriot cause that Flood had built.

> On that ground, Henry Flood and Henry Grattan parted, never to be reconciled. (096-052, 096-053)

The rivalry was personal as well as political, and it divided the Patriot Party at critical moments. The elder Patriot, whose conduct had been as energetic and consistent as that of Grattan himself, saw Grattan seize the prize that he had long prepared. (096-054)

When Grattan secured the great triumph of legislative independence in 1782, Flood was in a characteristically ambiguous position — he had laid the groundwork but was not present to claim the prize.

His subsequent criticisms of Grattan's settlement, particularly on the question of whether simple repeal of the Declaratory Act was sufficient, were regarded by some as the sour grapes of a displaced leader.

## Later Career and Retirement

In the later 1770s and 1780s, Flood became closely associated with the [[Irish Volunteers]], the citizen militia that had been formed to defend Ireland against French invasion during the American war. He saw in the Volunteers a vehicle for pressing constitutional demands on the British government.

After the session of 1785, in which he had been outvoted on every motion he proposed, he retired from the Irish Parliament, and allowed himself to be persuaded, at the age of fifty-three, to enter the English House of Commons. (096-256, 096-258) He was elected for Winchester, and made his first essay on the new scene on his favourite subject of representative reform. (096-259)

But his health was undermined; he failed to make the impression in London that he had made in Dublin. (096-261)

## Assessment

Henry Flood's legacy is that of the great precursor — the man who built the platform on which Grattan stood, but who could not bring himself to share the applause. His intellect and oratory were universally acknowledged; his political judgment, in the eyes of many contemporaries, less so.

The public life of Henry Flood illustrates both the possibilities and the limitations of 18th-century Irish patriotism. He was a Protestant patriot in a Protestant parliament, seeking to enlarge Irish liberty without challenging the fundamental structure of Ascendancy rule. Later generations of Irish nationalists would see both Flood and Grattan as figures of a necessarily incomplete revolution.
