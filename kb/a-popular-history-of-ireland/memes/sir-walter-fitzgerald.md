## Overview

Sir Walter Fitzgerald was a sixteenth-century Anglo-Irish knight connected by marriage to the powerful O'Byrne chieftain of Glenmalure. (058-016) He was drawn into the rebellion of [[James Eustace, Viscount Baltinglass]] in 1580 and thereafter took refuge among the O'Byrnes of Wicklow, becoming a figure of suspicion to the Dublin government throughout the final decades of Elizabeth's reign. (059-036)

## The Baltinglass Rebellion

In the summer of 1580, James Eustace, Viscount Baltinglass, took up arms against the Dublin administration. Baltinglass had suffered imprisonment in Dublin Castle for refusing to pay an illegal tax and was afterwards made the object of a special, vindictive enactment known as "the Statute of Baltinglass." (058-011) Surrounded by armed friends and retainers, he established himself on his keeping in the Wicklow highlands. (058-015)

> His friend, Sir Walter Fitzgerald, son-in-law to the chief of Glenmalure, and many of the clansmen of Leix, Offally and Idrone, repaired to him at Slieveroe, near the modern village of Blessington, from which they proceeded to form a junction with the followers of the dauntless Feagh McHugh O'Byrne of Ballincor. (058-016)

The gathering at Slieveroe united the discontented Anglo-Irish gentry with the Gaelic clans of the mountains. Sir Walter Fitzgerald's presence alongside Baltinglass signalled that the rebellion was not merely a native Irish rising but drew support from elements of the Old English community who chafed under the exactions of the Tudor state.

## The Battle of Glenmalure

Lord Grey of Wilton, arriving in Dublin in August 1580, determined to strike a decisive blow against this gathering before proceeding to the south. (058-020) All the chief captains in the Queen's service — the Malbys, Dudleys, Cosbys, Carews, and Moors — marched under his command into the Wicklow highlands. (058-023)

The Catholics were concentrated in the valley of Glenmalure, on one of the slopes of which Lord Grey constructed a strong camp and gave orders to attack. (058-027) Sir William Stanley, one of the officers in command, described the upshot in a letter to Secretary Walsingham:

> "When we entered the glen," he writes, "we were forced to slide, sometimes three or four fathoms, ere we could stay our feet; it was in depth, where we entered, at least a mile, full of stones, rocks, logs and wood; in the bottom thereof a river full of loose stones, which we were driven to cross divers times... the enemy charged us very hotly — it was the hottest piece of service that ever I saw, for the time, in any place." (058-031)

The assault was repulsed with heavy loss. Among the slain were Sir Peter Carew, Colonel Francis Cosby of Mullaghmast memory, Colonel Moor, and other distinguished officers. (058-039) The full extent of the defeat was concealed from Elizabeth in the official despatches, but private letters soon conveyed the painful intelligence to court. (058-042) The battle of Glenmalure, fought on the 25th of August, 1580, was one of the most complete victories the Irish ever won over an English army.

## A Fugitive in Glenmalure

After the suppression of the Baltinglass rising, Sir Walter Fitzgerald remained in the mountains. His connection to the O'Byrnes through marriage and his friendship for the fugitive Lord Baltinglass made him a marked man. In 1594, when the truce with Feagh Mac Hugh O'Byrne expired, the Dublin government struck:

> His son-in-law, Sir Walter Fitzgerald, had been driven by ill-usage, and his friendship for Lord Baltinglass, to the shelter of Glenmalure, and this was, of course, made a ground of charge against its chief. (059-036)

O'Byrne's sureties at Dublin — Geoffrey son of Hugh and his own son James — were committed to close custody in Dublin Castle, and the sheriff of Carlow reported warlike preparations in the Glen. (059-034) The aged chieftain Feagh Mac Hugh narrowly escaped capture in a night march on his house at Ballincor; his wife Rose O'Toole was wounded in the flight. (059-044)

Sir Walter Fitzgerald's career illustrates the divided loyalties of the Old English gentry in late Tudor Ireland. Connected by blood and marriage to both the Anglo-Irish Pale and the Gaelic clans of the mountains, he chose — when pressed — the traditional liberties of his class against the encroaching claims of the Tudor state.
