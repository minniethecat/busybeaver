CHAPTER VII.

[007-001]KINGS OF THE EIGHTH CENTURY.

[007-002]The kings of the eighth century are Congal II. (surnamed Kenmare), who

[007-003]reigned seven years; Feargal, who reigned ten years; Forgartah,

[007-004]Kenneth, Flaherty, respectively one, four, and seven years; Hugh V.

[007-005](surnamed Allan), nine years; Donald III., who reigned (A.D. 739-759)

[007-006]twenty years; Nial II. (surnamed Nial of the Showers), seven years; and

[007-007]Donogh I., who reigned thirty-one years, A.D. 766-797. The obituaries

[007-008]of these kings show that we have fallen on a comparatively peaceful

[007-009]age, since of the entire nine, but three perished in battle. One

[007-010]retired to Armagh and one to Iona, where both departed in the monastic

[007-011]habit; the others died either of sickness or old age.

[007-012]Yet the peaceful character of this century is but comparative, for in

[007-013]the first quarter (A.D. 722), we have the terrible battle of Almain,

[007-014]between Leinster and the Monarch, in which 30,000 men were stated to

[007-015]have engaged, and 7,000 to have fallen. The Monarch who had double the

[007-016]number of the Leinster Prince, was routed and slain, _apropos_ of which

[007-017]we have a Bardic tale told, which almost transports one to the far

[007-018]East, the simple lives and awful privileges of the Hindoo Brahmins. It

[007-019]seems that some of King FEARGAL's army, in foraging for their fellows,

[007-020]drove off the only cow of a hermit, who lived in seclusion near a

[007-021]solitary little chapel called Killin. The enraged recluse, at the very

[007-022]moment the armies were about to engage, appeared between them,

[007-023]regardless of personal danger, denouncing ruin and death to the

[007-024]monarch's forces. And in this case, as in others, to be found in every

[007-025]history, the prophecy, no doubt, helped to produce its own fulfilment.

[007-026]The malediction of men dedicated to the service of God, has often

[007-027]routed hosts as gallant as were marshalled on the field of Almain.

[007-028]FEARGAL'S two immediate successors met a similar fate—death in the

[007-029]field of battle—after very brief reigns, of which we have no great

[007-030]events to record.

[007-031]FLAHERTY, the next who succeeded, after a vigorous reign of seven

[007-032]years, withdrew from the splendid cares of a crown, and passed the long

[007-033]remainder of his life—thirty years—in the habit of a monk at Armagh.

[007-034]The heavy burthen which he had cheerfully laid down, was taken up by a

[007-035]Prince, who combined the twofold character of poet and hero. HUGH V.

[007-036](surnamed Allan), the son of FEARGAL, of whom we have just spoken, was

[007-037]the very opposite of his father, in his veneration for the privileges

[007-038]of holy persons and places. His first military achievement was

[007-039]undertaken in vindication of the rights of those who were unable by

[007-040]arms to vindicate their own. Hugh Roin, Prince of the troublesome

[007-041]little principality of Ulidia (Down), though well stricken in years and

[007-042]old enough to know better, in one of his excursions had forcibly

[007-043]compelled the clergy of the country through which he passed to give him

[007-044]free quarters, contrary to the law everywhere existing. Congus, the

[007-045]Primate, jealous of the exemptions of his order, complained of this

[007-046]sacrilege in a poetic message addressed to Hugh Allan, who, as a

[007-047]Christian and a Prince, was bound to espouse his quarrels. He marched

[007-048]into the territory of the offender, defeated him in battle, cut off his

[007-049]head on the threshold of the Church of Faughard, and marched back

[007-050]again, his host chanting a war song composed by their leader.

[007-051]In this reign died Saint Gerald of Mayo, an Anglo-Saxon Bishop, and

[007-052]apparently the head of a colony of his countrymen, from whom that

[007-053]district is ever since called "Mayo of the Saxons." The name, however,

[007-054]being a general one for strangers from Britain about that period, just

[007-055]as Dane became for foreigners from the Baltic in the next century, is

[007-056]supposed to be incorrectly applied: the colony being, it is said,

[007-057]really from Wales, of old British stock, who had migrated rather than

[007-058]live under the yoke of their victorious Anglo-Saxon Kings. The

[007-059]descendants of these Welshmen are still to be traced, though intimately

[007-060]intermingled with the original Belgic and later Milesian settlers in

[007-061]Mayo, Sligo, and Galway—thus giving a peculiar character to that

[007-062]section of the country, easily distinguishable from all the rest.

[007-063]Although Hugh Allan did not imitate his father's conduct towards

[007-064]ecclesiastics, he felt bound by all-ruling custom to avenge his

[007-065]father's death. In all ancient countries the kinsmen of a murdered man

[007-066]were both by law and custom the avengers of his blood. The members of

[007-067]the Greek _phratry_, of the Roman _fatria_, or _gens_, of the Germanic

[007-068]and Anglo-Saxon _guild_, and of the mediaeval sworn _commune_, were all

[007-069]solemnly bound to avenge the blood of any of their brethren, unlawfully

[007-070]slain. So that the repulsive repetition of reprisals, which so disgusts

[007-071]the modern reader in our old annals, is by no means a phenomenon

[007-072]peculiar to the Irish state of society. It was in the middle age and in

[007-073]early times common to all Europe, to Britain and Germany, as well as to

[007-074]Greece and Rome. It was, doubtless, under a sense of duty of this sort

[007-075]that Hugh V. led into Leinster a large army (A.D. 733), and the day of

[007-076]Ath-Senaid fully atoned for the day of Almain. Nine thousand of the men

[007-077]of Leinster were left on the field, including most of their chiefs; the

[007-078]victorious monarch losing a son, and other near kinsmen. Four years

[007-079]later, he himself fell in an obscure contest near Kells, in the plain

[007-080]of Meath. Some of his quartrains have come down to us, and they breathe

[007-081]a spirit at once religious and heroic—such as must have greatly

[007-082]endeared the Prince who possessed it to his companions in arms. We are

[007-083]not surprised, therefore, to find his reign a favourite epoch with

[007-084]subsequent Bards and Storytellers.

[007-085]The long and prosperous reign of Donald III. succeeded (A.D. 739 to

[007-086]759). He is almost the only one of this series of Kings of whom it can

[007-087]be said that he commanded in no notable battle. The annals of his reign

[007-088]are chiefly filled with ordinary accidents, and the obits of the

[007-089]learned. But its literary and religious record abounds with bright

[007-090]names and great achievements, as we shall find when we come to consider

[007-091]the educational and missionary fruits of Christianity in the eighth

[007-092]century. While on a pilgrimage to Durrow, a famous Columbian foundation

[007-093]in Meath, and present King's County, Donald III. departed this life,

[007-094]and in Durrow, by his own desire, his body was interred.

[007-095]Nial II. (surnamed of the Showers), son to FEARGAL and brother of the

[007-096]warrior-Bard, Hugh V., was next invested with the white wand of

[007-097]sovereignty. He was a prince less warlike and more pious than his elder

[007-098]brother. The _soubriquet_ attached to his name is accounted for by a

[007-099]Bardic tale, which represents him as another Moses, at whose prayer

[007-100]food fell from heaven in time of famine. Whatever "showers" fell or

[007-101]wonders were wrought in his reign, it is certain that after enjoying

[007-102]the kingly office for seven years, Nial resigned, and retired to Iona,

[007-103]there to pass the remainder of his days in penance and meditation.

[007-104]Eight years he led the life of a monk in that sacred Isle, where his

[007-105]grave is one of those of "the three Irish Kings," still pointed out in

[007-106]the cemetery of the Kings. He is but one among several Princes, his

[007-107]cotemporaries, who had made the same election. We learn in this same

[007-108]century, that Cellach, son of the King of Connaught, died in Holy

[007-109]Orders, and that Bec, Prince of Ulidia, and Ardgall, son of a later

[007-110]King of Connaught, had taken the "crostaff" of the pilgrim, either for

[007-111]Iona or Armagh, or some more distant shrine. Pilgrimages to Rome and to

[007-112]Jerusalem seem to have been begun even before this time, as we may

[007-113]infer from St. Adamnan's work on the situation of the Holy Places, of

[007-114]which Bede gives an abstract.

[007-115]The reign of Donogh I. is the longest and the last among the Kings of

[007-116]the eighth century (A.D. 776 to 797). The Kings of Ireland had now not

[007-117]only abandoned Tara, but one by one, the other royal residences in

[007-118]Meath as their usual place of abode. As a consequence a local

[007-119]sovereignty sprung up in the family of O'Melaghlin, a minor branch of

[007-120]the ruling race. This house developing its power so unexpectedly, and

[007-121]almost always certain to have the national forces under the command of

[007-122]a Patron Prince at their back, were soon involved in quarrels about

[007-123]boundaries, both with Leinster and Munster. King Donogh, at the outset

[007-124]of his reign, led his forces into both principalities, and without

[007-125]battle received their hostages. Giving hostages—generally the sons of

[007-126]the chiefs—was the usual form of ratifying any treaty. Generally also,

[007-127]the Bishop of the district, or its most distinguished ecclesiastic, was

[007-128]called in as witness of the terms, and both parties were solemnly sworn

[007-129]on the relics of Saints—the Gospels of the Monasteries or Cathedrals—or

[007-130]the croziers of their venerated founders. The breach of such a treaty

[007-131]was considered "a violation of the relics of the saint," whose name had

[007-132]been invoked, and awful penalties were expected to follow so heinous a

[007-133]crime. The hostages were then carried to the residence of the King, to

[007-134]whom they were entrusted, and while the peace lasted, enjoyed a parole

[007-135]freedom, and every consideration due to their rank. If of tender age

[007-136]they were educated with the same care as the children of the household.

[007-137]But when war broke out their situation was always precarious, and

[007-138]sometimes dangerous. In a few instances they had even been put to

[007-139]death, but this was considered a violation of all the laws both of

[007-140]hospitality and chivalry; usually they were removed to some strong

[007-141]secluded fort, and carefully guarded as pledges to be employed,

[007-142]according to the chances and changes of the war. That Donogh preferred

[007-143]negotiation to war, we may infer by his course towards Leinster and

[007-144]Munster, in the beginning of his reign, and his "kingly parlee" at a

[007-145]later period (A.D. 783) with FIACHNA, of Ulidia, son of that

[007-146]over-exacting Hugh Roin, whose head was taken from his shoulders at the

[007-147]Church door of Faughard. This "kingly parlee" was held on an island off

[007-148]the Methian shore, called afterwards "King's Island." But little good

[007-149]came of it. Both parties still held their own views, so that the

[007-150]satirical poets asked what was the use of the island, when one party

[007-151]"would not come upon the land, nor the other upon the sea?" However, we

[007-152]needs must agree with King Donogh, that war is the last resort, and is

[007-153]only to be tried when all other means have failed.

[007-155]Twice during this reign the whole island was stricken with panic, by

[007-156]extraordinary signs in the heavens, of huge serpents coiling themselves

[007-157]through the stars, of fiery bolts flying like shuttles from one side of

[007-158]the horizon to the other, or shooting downward directly to the earth.

[007-159]These atmospheric wonders were accompanied by thunder and lightning so

[007-160]loud and so prolonged that men hid themselves for fear in the caverns

[007-161]of the earth. The fairs and markets were deserted by buyers and

[007-162]sellers; the fields were abandoned by the farmers; steeples were rent

[007-163]by lightning, and fell to the ground; the shingled roofs of churches

[007-164]caught fire and burned whole buildings. Shocks of earthquake were also

[007-165]felt, and round towers and cyclopean masonry were strewn in fragments

[007-166]upon the ground. These visitations first occurred in the second year of

[007-167]Donogh, and returned again in 783. When, in the next decade, the first

[007-168]Danish descent was made on the coast of Ulster (A.D. 794), these signs

[007-169]and wonders were superstitiously supposed to have been the precursors

[007-170]of that far more terrible and more protracted visitation.

[007-171]The Danes at first attracted little notice, but in the last year of

[007-172]Donogh (A.D. 797) they returned in greater force, and swept rapidly

[007-173]along the coast of Meath; it was reserved for his successors of the

[007-174]following centuries to face the full brunt of this new national danger.

[007-175]But before encountering the fierce nations of the north, and the stormy

[007-176]period they occupy, let us cast back a loving glance over the

[007-177]world-famous schools and scholars of the last two centuries. Hitherto

[007-178]we have only spoken of certain saints, in connection with high affairs

[007-179]of state. We must now follow them to the college and the cloister, we

[007-180]must consider them as founders at home, and as missionaries abroad;

[007-181]otherwise how could we estimate all that is at stake for Erin and for

[007-182]Christendom, in the approaching combat with the devotees of Odin,—the

[007-183]deadly enemies of all Christian institutions?
