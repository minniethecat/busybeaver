## Overview

Jonathan Swift (1667–1745) was the greatest English-language satirist of the eighteenth century and one of the most powerful voices in Irish public life. As Dean of [[saint-patrick|St. Patrick]]'s Cathedral, [[dublin|Dublin]], he used his position and his pen to defend Irish interests against English exploitation, earning him the enduring gratitude of the Irish people.

In his political writings, Swift directed his satirical genius against corruption and injustice. McGee, writing in the nineteenth century, recalls the force of Swift's pen: his "knotted lash" was never more worthily applied than to the political clique he immortalised with savage wit in his poem "The Legion Club." (086-172)

## Biography

### Early Life and Education

Jonathan Swift was born in [[dublin|Dublin]] in 1667 to English parents. His father died before his birth, and he was educated at Kilkenny College and Trinity College [[dublin|Dublin]]. The political upheavals of the Williamite period disrupted his studies, but he eventually completed his degree and moved to England, where he entered the service of Sir William Temple, a retired diplomat and distant relative.

### Literary Career

Swift's satirical masterpiece, _Gulliver's Travels_ (1726), secured his place among the greatest writers in the English language. Beyond his fiction, Swift was one of the most powerful political pamphleteers of his age. His _Drapier's Letters_ (1724–1725) rallied Irish public opinion against the debasement of Irish coinage by the English government, making him a national hero in Ireland.

### Swift and the Irish Parliament

Swift's satirical poem "The Legion Club" stands as a monument to his contempt for political corruption. McGee describes how the [[irish-parliament|Irish Parliament]] of Swift's day, by allowing the bold propositions of Molyneux to be condemned without protest, earned the full force of Swift's indignation. (086-170) (086-172)

The "Legion Club" to which Swift consigned an unenviable immortality was the [[irish-parliament|Irish House of Commons]], which he saw as supine before English interests and indifferent to the welfare of the Irish people. (086-173) (086-174)

### Dean of St. Patrick's

Swift was appointed Dean of [[saint-patrick|St. Patrick]]'s Cathedral, [[dublin|Dublin]], in 1713. Though he had hoped for a more prominent appointment in England, his service in [[dublin|Dublin]] placed him at the heart of Irish life during a period of increasing colonial subordination. From his deanery, Swift spoke out against the injustices of English rule, becoming the conscience of Protestant Ireland.

## Legacy

Jonathan Swift's legacy endures in both literature and politics. As a writer, he is recognised as a master of English prose and the greatest satirist in the language. As a political figure, he demonstrated that the pen could be a weapon of resistance against oppression, prefiguring the tradition of Irish literary nationalism that would flourish in the nineteenth and twentieth centuries.

His bequest of his estate to found [[saint-patrick|St. Patrick]]'s Hospital for the mentally ill in [[dublin|Dublin]] remains a living monument to his compassion and his commitment to the city he served.
