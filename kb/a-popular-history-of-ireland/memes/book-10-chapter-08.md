CHAPTER VIII.

[083-001]THE WINTER OF 1690-91.

[083-002]The Jacobite party in England were not slow to exaggerate the extent of

[083-003]William's losses before Athlone and Limerick. The national

[083-004]susceptibility was consoled by the ready reflection, that if the beaten

[083-005]troops were partly English, the commanders were mainly foreigners. A

[083-006]native hero was needed, and was found in the person of Marlborough, a

[083-007]captain, whose name was destined to eclipse every other English

[083-008]reputation of that age. At his suggestion an expedition was fitted out

[083-009]against Cork, Kinsale, and other ports of the south of Ireland, and the

[083-010]command, though not without some secret unwillingness on William's

[083-011]part, committed to him. On the 23rd of September, at the head of 8,000

[083-012]fresh troops, amply supplied with all necessary munitions, Marlborough

[083-013]assaulted Cork. After five days' bombardment, in which the Duke of

[083-014]Grafton, and other officers and men were slain, the Governor, McEligot,

[083-015]capitulated on conditions, which, in spite of all Marlborough's

[083-016]exertions, were flagrantly violated. The old town of Kinsale was at

[083-017]once abandoned as untenable the same day, and the new fort, at the

[083-018]entrance to the harbour, was surrendered after a fortnight's cannonade.

[083-019]Covered with glory from a five weeks' campaign, Marlborough returned to

[083-020]England to receive the acclamations of the people and the most gracious

[083-021]compliments of the prince.

[083-022]Berwick and Sarsfield on the one side and Ginkle and Lanier on the

[083-023]other, kept up the winter campaign till an advanced period, on both

[083-024]banks of the Shannon. About the middle of September, the former made a

[083-025]dash over the bridge of Banagher, against Birr, or Parsonstown, the

[083-026]family borough of the famous _Undertaker_. The English, in great force,

[083-027]under Lanier, Kirke, and Douglas, hastened to its relief, and the Irish

[083-028]fell back to Banagher. To destroy "that convenient pass" became now the

[083-029]object of one party, to protect it, of the other. After some

[083-030]skirmishing and manoeuvring on both sides, the disputed bridge was left

[083-031]in Irish possession, and the English fell back to the borough and

[083-032]castle of Sir Lawrence Parsons. During the siege of the new fort at

[083-033]Kinsale, Berwick and Sarsfield advanced as far as Kilmallock to its

[083-034]relief, but finding themselves so inferior in numbers to Marlborough,

[083-035]they were unwillingly compelled to leave its brave defenders to their

[083-036]fate,

[083-037]Although the Duke of Berwick was the nominal Commander-in-Chief, his

[083-038]youth, and the distractions incident to youth, left the more mature and

[083-039]popular Sarsfield the possession of real power, both civil and

[083-040]military. Every fortunate accident had combined to elevate that gallant

[083-041]cavalry officer into the position of national leadership.

[083-042]He was the son of a member of the Irish Commons, proscribed for his

[083-043]patriotism and religion in 1641, by Anna O'Moore, daughter of the

[083-044]organizer of the Catholic Confederation. He was a Catholic in religion,

[083-045]spoke Gaelic as easily as English, was brave, impulsive; handsome, and

[083-046]generous to a fault, like the men he led. In Tyrconnell's absence every

[083-047]sincere lover of the country came to him with intelligence, and looked

[083-048]to him for direction. Early in November he learned through his

[083-049]patriotic spies the intention of the Williamites to force the passage

[083-050]of the Shannon in the depth of winter. On the last day of December,

[083-051]accordingly, they marched in great force under Kirke and Lanier to

[083-052]Jonesboro', and under Douglas to Jamestown. At both points they found

[083-053]the indefatigable Sarsfield fully prepared for them, and after a

[083-054]fortnight's intense suffering from exposure to the weather, were glad

[083-055]to get back again to their snug quarters at Parsonstown.

[083-056]Early in February Tyrconnell landed at Limerick with a French fleet,

[083-057]escorted by three vessels of war, and laden with provisions, but

[083-058]bringing few arms and no reinforcements. He had brought over, however,

[083-059]14,000 golden louis, which were found of the utmost service in

[083-060]re-clothing the army, besides 10,000 more which he had deposited at

[083-061]Brest to purchase oatmeal for subsequent shipment. He also brought

[083-062]promises of military assistance on a scale far beyond anything France

[083-063]had yet afforded. It is almost needless to say he was received at

[083-064]Galway and Limerick with an enthusiasm which silenced, if it did not

[083-065]confute, his political enemies, both in Ireland and France.

[083-066]During his absence intrigues and factions had been rifer than ever in

[083-067]the Jacobite ranks. Sarsfield had discovered that the English movement

[083-068]on the Shannon in December was partly hastened by foolish or

[083-069]treacherous correspondence among his own associates. Lord Riverston and

[083-070]his brother were removed from the Senate, or Council of Sixteen—four

[083-071]from each province—and Judge Daly, ancestor of the Dunsandle family,

[083-072]was placed under arrest at Galway. The youthful Berwick sometimes

[083-073]complained that he was tutored and overruled by Sarsfield; but though

[083-074]the impetuous soldier may occasionally have forgotten the lessons

[083-075]learned in courts, his activity seems to have been the greatest, his

[083-076]information the best, his advice the most disinterested, and his

[083-077]fortitude the highest of any member of the council. By the time of

[083-078]Tyrconnell's return he had grown to a height of popularity and power,

[083-079]which could not well brook a superior either in the cabinet or the

[083-080]camp.

[083-081]On the arrival of the Lord Lieutenant, who was also Commander-in-Chief,

[083-082]the ambition of Sarsfield was gratified by the rank of Earl of Lucan, a

[083-083]title drawn from that pleasant hamlet, in the valley of the Liffey,

[083-084]where he had learned to lisp the catechism of a patriot at the knee of

[083-085]Anna O'Moore. But his real power was much diminished. Tyrconnell,

[083-086]Berwick, Sir Richard Nagle, who had succeeded the Earl of Melfort as

[083-087]chief secretary for King James, all ranked before him at the board, and

[083-088]when Saint Ruth arrived to take command-in-chief, he might fairly have

[083-089]complained that he was deprived of the chief reward to which he had

[083-090]looked forward.

[083-091]The weary winter and the drenching spring months wore away, and the

[083-092]Williamite troops, sorely afflicted by disease, hugged their tents and

[083-093]huts. Some relief was sent by sea to the Jacobite garrison of Sligo,

[083-094]commanded by the stout old Sir Teague O'Regan, the former defender of

[083-095]Charlemont. Athlone, too, received some succours, and the line of the

[083-096]Shannon was still unbroken from Slieve-an-iron to the sea. But still

[083-097]the promised French assistance was delayed. Men were beginning to doubt

[083-098]both King Louis and King James, when, at length at the beginning of

[083-099]May, the French ships were signalled from the cliffs of Kerry. On the

[083-100]8th, the Sieur de Saint Ruth, with Generals D'Usson and De Tesse,

[083-101]landed at Limerick, and assisted at a solemn _Te Deum_ in St. Mary's

[083-102]Cathedral. They brought considerable supplies of clothes, provisions,

[083-103]and ammunitions, but neither veterans to swell the ranks, nor money to

[083-104]replenish the chest. Saint Ruth entered eagerly upon the discharge of

[083-105]his duties as generalissimo, while Sarsfield continued the nominal

[083-106]second in command.
