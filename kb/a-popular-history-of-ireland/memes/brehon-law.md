## Overview

Brehon Law (from the Irish *breitheamh*, "judge") was the native legal system of Ireland, administered by a professional class of jurists known as Brehons. It was one of the oldest surviving bodies of law in Europe, dating back to pre-Christian times and continuing in use in parts of Gaelic Ireland until the early seventeenth century.

The Brehon law system was distinctive in its approach to crime, property, inheritance, and social relations. Unlike the common law systems of England and continental Europe, it emphasized compensation and restitution rather than punishment, and it recognized a complex hierarchy of social statuses governing rights and obligations.

## Background

### Origins

The Brehon laws originated in the customary law of the early Irish, passed down orally by the filid (poet-seers) and later by professional Brehon families. The laws were said to have been collected and expurgated of their pagan elements under the direction of [[saint-patrick|Saint Patrick]] in the fifth century. The resulting code, contained in the *Senchas Már* (Great Tradition) and other legal tracts, combined pre-Christian custom with Christian principles. (003-172) (003-189)

### The Book of Rights

A related body of law was contained in the *[[book-of-rights|Lebor na Cert]]* ([[book-of-rights|Book of Rights]]), which set out the tributes, privileges, and obligations of the various kings and sub-kings of Ireland. This book, in its printed form, fills some 250 octavo pages and may be said to contain the original institutes of Erin under her Celtic kings.

### The Brehon Profession

The Brehons were a hereditary professional class who memorized and interpreted the law. They served as judges, arbitrators, and legal advisors to kings and chieftains. The most famous Brehon families—the O'Davorens, MacEgans, and O'Breslins—maintained law schools that preserved and transmitted the legal tradition from generation to generation.

## Key Features

### Compensation and Restitution

The most distinctive feature of Brehon law was its emphasis on compensation (*eric*) rather than punishment. Almost every offense, including homicide, could be resolved through the payment of a fine calculated according to the victim's social status (*honor-price* or *lóg n-enech*). This contrasts with the English common law's emphasis on capital and corporal punishment.

### Social Hierarchy

Brehon law recognized a complex hierarchy of social grades, each with its own rights, obligations, and honor-price. The grades ranged from the *Ard-Righ* (High King) through various ranks of kings, nobles, freemen (the *bó-aire* or "cow-lord" class), and various categories of the unfree.

### Kinship and Inheritance

The kinship group (*fine*) was the fundamental social and legal unit. Land was held collectively by the kin group, and inheritance was determined by complex rules of succession. The concept of individual land ownership, central to English common law, was alien to the Brehon system.

### Women's Rights

Brehon law recognized significant rights for women, including the right to own property, to divorce, and to retain their own property after marriage. These provisions were notably more favorable to women than contemporary English common law.

## Decline

The Anglo-Norman invasion of 1169 introduced English common law to Ireland, but Brehon law continued to be used in Gaelic areas. Over the centuries, English statutes repeatedly attempted to suppress the native legal system. The final blow came with the Flight of the Earls (1607) and the subsequent [[plantation-of-ulster|Plantation of Ulster]], which extinguished the Gaelic political order and with it the Brehon law system.

The laws survived in manuscript form, however, and were published in the nineteenth century, becoming a subject of scholarly study and a source of pride in Ireland's distinctive legal heritage.

## Legacy

The Brehon laws represent one of Ireland's most remarkable contributions to European legal culture. Their emphasis on compensation, their recognition of women's rights, and their sophisticated social classification system distinguish them from contemporary legal codes. They survive as a testament to the sophistication of pre-Norman Irish civilization.
