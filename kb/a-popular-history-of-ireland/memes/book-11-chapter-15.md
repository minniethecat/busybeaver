CHAPTER XV.

[102-001]THE INSURRECTION OF 1798.

[102-002]It is no longer matter of assertion merely, but simple matter of fact,

[102-003]that the English and Irish ministers of George III. regarded the

[102-004]insurrectionary movement of the United Irishmen as at once a pretext

[102-005]and a means for effecting a legislative union between the two

[102-006]countries. Lord Camden, the Viceroy who succeeded Lord Fitzwilliam in

[102-007]March, '95—with Mr. Pelham as his Chief Secretary, in a letter to his

[102-008]relative, the Hon. Robert Stewart, afterwards Lord Castlereagh,

[102-009]announced this policy, in unmistakable terms, so early as 1793; and all

[102-010]the official correspondence published of late years, concerning that

[102-011]period of British and Irish history, establishes the fact beyond the

[102-012]possibility of denial.

[102-013]Such being the design, it was neither the wish nor the interest of the

[102-014]Government, that the insurrection should be suppressed, unless the

[102-015]Irish constitution could be extinguished with it. To that end they

[102-016]proceeded in the coercive legislation described in a previous chapter;

[102-017]to that end they armed with irresponsible power the military officers

[102-018]and the oligarchical magistracy; with that view they quartered those

[102-019]yeomanry regiments, which were known to be composed of Orangemen, on

[102-020]the wretched peasantry of the most Catholic counties, while the corps

[102-021]in which Catholics or United Irishmen were most numerous, were sent

[102-022]over to England, in exchange for Scotch fencibles and Welsh cavalry.

[102-023]The outrages committed by all these volunteer troops, but above all by

[102-024]the Orange yeomanry of the country, were so monstrous, that the gallant

[102-025]and humane Sir John Moore exclaimed, "If I were an Irishman, I would be

[102-026]a rebel!"

[102-028]It was, indeed, impossible for any man, however obscure, or however

[102-029]eminent, to live longer in the country, without taking sides. Yet the

[102-030]choice was at best a hard and unhappy one. On the one side was the

[102-031]Castle, hardly concealing its intention of goading on the people, in

[102-032]order to rob them of their Parliament; on the other was the injured

[102-033]multitude, bound together by a secret system which proved in reality no

[102-034]safeguard against traitors in their own ranks, and which had been

[102-035]placed by its Protestant chiefs under the auspices of an infidel

[102-036]republic. Between the two courses men made election according to their

[102-037]bias or their necessities, or as they took local or general, political

[102-038]or theological views of the situation. Both Houses of the legislature

[102-039]unanimously sustained the government against the insurrection; as did

[102-040]the judges, the bar, and the Anglican clergy and bishops. The

[102-041]Presbyterian body were in the beginning all but unanimous for a

[102-042]republican revolution and the French alliance; the great majority of

[102-043]the Catholic peasantry were, as the crisis increased, driven into the

[102-044]same position, while all their bishops and a majority of the Catholic

[102-045]aristocracy, adhered to that which they, with the natural tendency of

[102-046]their respective orders, considered the side of religion and authority.

[102-047]Thus was the nation sub-divided within itself; Protestant civilian from

[102-048]Protestant ecclesiastic, Catholic layman from Catholic priest, tenant

[102-049]from lord, neighbour from neighbour, father from son, and friend from

[102-050]friend.

[102-051]During the whole of '97, the opposing parties were in a ferment of

[102-052]movement and apprehension. As the year wore on, the administration,

[102-053]both English and Irish, began to feel that the danger was more

[102-054]formidable than they had foreseen. The timely storm which had blown

[102-055]Grouchy out of Bantry Bay, the previous Christmas, could hardly be

[102-056]reckoned on again, though the settled hostility of the French

[102-057]government knew no change. Thoroughly well informed by their legion of

[102-058]spies both on the Continent and in Ireland, every possible military

[102-059]precaution was taken. The Lord Lieutenant's proclamation for disarming

[102-060]the people, issued in May, was rigorously enforced by General Johnstone

[102-061]in the South, General Hutchinson in the West, and Lord Lake in the

[102-062]North. Two hundred thousand pikes and pike-heads were said to have been

[102-063]discovered or surrendered during the year, and several thousand

[102-064]firelocks. The yeomanry, and English and Scotch corps amounted to

[102-065]35,000 men, while the regular troops were increased to 50,000 and

[102-066]subsequently to 80,000, including three regiments of the Guards. The

[102-067]defensive works at Cork, and other vulnerable points were strengthened

[102-068]at an immense cost; the "Pigeon House" fort, near Dublin, was enlarged,

[102-069]for the city itself was pronounced by General Vallancy, Colonel

[102-070]Packenham, and other engineer authorities dangerously weak, if not

[102-071]wholly untenable. A system of telegraphic signals was established from

[102-072]all points of the coast with the Capital, and every precaution taken

[102-073]against the surprise of another French invasion.

[102-074]During the summer assize, almost every considerable town and circuit

[102-075]had its state trial. The sheriffs had been carefully selected

[102-076]beforehand by the Castle, and the juries were certain to be of "the

[102-077]right sort," under the auspices of such sheriffs. Immense sums in the

[102-078]aggregate were contributed by the United Irish for the defence of their

[102-079]associates; at the Down assizes alone, not less than seven hundred or

[102-080]eight hundred guineas were spent in fees and retainers; but at the

[102-081]close of the term, Mr. Beresford was able to boast to his friend Lord

[102-082]Auckland, that but one of all the accused had escaped the penalty of

[102-083]death or banishment! The military tribunals, however, did not wait for

[102-084]the idle formalities of the civil courts. Soldiers and civilians,

[102-085]yeomen and townsmen, against whom the informer pointed his finger, were

[102-086]taken out, and summarily executed. Ghastly forms hung upon the

[102-087]thick-set gibbets, not only in the market places of country towns, and

[102-088]before the public prisons, but on all the bridges of the metropolis.

[102-089]Many of the soldiers, in every military district were shot weekly and

[102-090]almost daily for real or alleged complicity with the rebels. The horrid

[102-091]torture of picketing, and the blood-stained lash, were constantly

[102-092]resorted to, to extort accusations or confessions. Over all these

[102-093]atrocities the furious and implacable spirit of Lord Clare presided in

[102-094]Council, and the equally furious and implacable Luttrel, Lord

[102-095]Carhampton, as Commander-in-Chief. All moderate councils were denounced

[102-096]as nothing short of treason, and even the elder Beresford, the Privy

[102-097]Counsellor, was compelled to complain of the violence of his noble

[102-098]associates, and his inability to restrain the ferocity of his own

[102-099]nearest relatives—meaning probably his son John Claudius, and his

[102-100]son-in-law, Sir George Hill.

[102-101]It was while this spirit was abroad, a spirit as destructive as ever

[102-102]animated the Councils of Sylla or Marius in Old Rome, or prompted the

[102-103]decrees of Robespierre or Marat in France, that the genius and courage

[102-104]of one man redeemed the lost reputation of the law, and upheld against

[102-105]all odds the sacred claims of personal liberty. This man was John

[102-106]Philpot Curran, the most dauntless of advocates, one of the truest and

[102-107]bravest of his race. Although a politician of the school of Grattan,

[102-108]and wholly untainted with French principles, he identified himself

[102-109]absolutely with his unhappy clients, "predoomed to death." The genius

[102-110]of patriotic resistance which seemed to have withdrawn from the Island

[102-111]with Grattan's secession from Parliament, now re-appeared in the last

[102-112]place where it might have been expected—in those courts of death,

[102-113]rather than of justice—before those predetermined juries, besides the

[102-114]hopeless inmates of the crowded dock, personified in the person of

[102-115]Curran. Often at midnight, amid the clash of arms, his wonderful

[102-116]pleadings were delivered; sometimes, as in Dublin, where the court

[102-117]rooms adjoined the prisons, the condemned, or the confined, could hear,

[102-118]in their cells, his piercing accents breaking the stillness of the

[102-119]early morning, pleading for justice and mercy—pleading always with

[102-120]superhuman perseverance, but almost always in vain. Neither menaces of

[102-121]arrest, nor threats of assassination, had power to intimidate that

[102-122]all-daring spirit; nor, it may be safely said, can the whole library of

[102-123]human history present us a form of heroism superior in kind or degree

[102-124]to that which this illustrious advocate exhibited during nearly two

[102-125]years, when he went forth daily, with his life in his hand, in the holy

[102-126]hope to snatch some human victim from the clutch of the destroyer

[102-127]thirsting for his blood.

[102-128]In November, '97, some said from fear of personal consequences, some

[102-129]from official pressure in a high quarter, Lord Carhampton resigned the

[102-130]command of the forces, and Sir Ralph Abercromby was appointed in his

[102-131]stead. There could not be a more striking illustration of the system of

[102-132]terror patronized by government than was furnished in the case of Sir

[102-133]Ralph as Commander-in-Chief. That distinguished soldier, with his half

[102-134]century of services at his back, had not been a week in Dublin before

[102-135]he discovered the weakness of the Viceroy, and the violence of his

[102-136]principal advisers, the Chancellor, the Speaker, Lord Castlereagh and

[102-137]the Beresfords. Writing in confidence to his son, he says, "The abuses

[102-138]of all kinds I found here can scarcely be believed or enumerated." The

[102-139]instances he cites of such abuses are sufficiently horrible to justify

[102-140]the strong language which brought down on his head so much hostility,

[102-141]when he declared in his proclamation of February '98, that the Irish

[102-142]army was "formidable to every one but the enemy." These well-known

[102-143]opinions were so repugnant to the Castle policy, that that party held a

[102-144]caucus in the Speaker's Chambers, at which it was proposed to pass a

[102-145]vote of censure in Parliament on the General, whom they denounced as "a

[102-146]sulky mule," "a Scotch beast," and by other similar names. Though the

[102-147]Parliamentary censure dropped, they actually compelled Lord Camden to

[102-148]call on him to retract his magnanimous order. To this humiliation the

[102-149]veteran stooped "for the sake of the King's service," but at the same

[102-150]time he proffered his resignation. After two months' correspondence, it

[102-151]was finally accepted, and the soldier who was found too jealous of the

[102-152]rights of the people to be a fit instrument of their destruction,

[102-153]escaped from his high position, not without a profound sentiment of

[102-154]relief. His verdict upon the barbarous policy pursued in his time was

[102-155]always expressed, frankly and decisively. His entire correspondence,

[102-156]private and public, bears one and the same burthen—the violence,

[102-157]cruelty, and tyranny of Lord Camden's chief advisers, and the pitiful

[102-158]weakness of the Viceroy himself. Against the infamous plan of letting

[102-159]loose a lustful and brutal soldiery to live at "free quarters" on a

[102-160]defenceless and disarmed people—an outrage against which Englishmen had

[102-161]taken perpetual security at _their_ revolution, as may be seen in "the

[102-162]Bill of Rights," he struggled during his six months' command, but with

[102-163]no great success. The plan, with all its horrors, was upheld by the

[102-164]Lord-Lieutenant, and more than any other cause, precipitated the

[102-165]rebellion which exploded at last, just as Sir Ralph was allowed to

[102-166]retire from the country. His temporary successor, Lord Lake, was

[102-167]troubled with no such scruples as the gallant old Scotsman.

[102-168]Events followed each other in the first months of 1798, fast and

[102-169]furiously. Towards the end of February, Arthur O'Conor, Father James

[102-170]Quigley, the brothers John and Benjamin Binns, were arrested at Margate

[102-171]on their way to France; on the 6th of March, the _Press_ newspaper, the

[102-172]Dublin organ of the party, as the _Star_ had been the Ulster organ, was

[102-173]seized by Government, Lord Edward Fitzgerald and William Sampson being

[102-174]at the time in the office. On the 12th of March, on the information of

[102-175]the traitor, Thomas Reynolds, the Leinster delegates were seized in

[102-176]conclave, with all their papers, at the house of Oliver Bond, in Bridge

[102-177]Street, Dublin. On the same information. Addis Emmet and Dr. McNevin

[102-178]were taken in their own houses, and Sampson in the north of England: of

[102-179]all the executive, Lord Edward alone escaping those sent in search of

[102-180]him. This was, as Tone notes in his journal, on the ill news reaching

[102-181]France, "a terrible blow." O'Conor's arrest in Kent, Sampson's in

[102-182]Carlisle, and the other arrests in Belfast and Dublin, proved too truly

[102-183]that treason was at work, and that the much-prized oath of secrecy was

[102-184]no protection whatever against the devices of the Castle and the

[102-185]depravity of its secret agents. The extent to which that treason

[102-186]extended, the number of associates who were in the pay of their deadly

[102-187]enemies, was never known to the United Irish leaders; time has,

[102-188]however, long since "revealed the secrets of the prison-house," and we

[102-189]know now, that men they trusted with all their plans and hopes, such as

[102-190]McNally and McGucken, were quite as deep in the conspiracy to destroy

[102-191]them as Mr. Reynolds and Captain Armstrong.

[102-192]The most influential members of the Dublin Society remaining at large

[102-193]contrived to correspond with each other, or to meet by stealth after

[102-194]the arrest at Bond's. The vacancies in the Executive were filled up by

[102-195]the brothers John and Henry Sheares, both barristers, sons of a wealthy

[102-196]Cork banker, and former member of Parliament, and by Mr. Lawless, a

[102-197]surgeon. For two months longer these gentlemen continued to act in

[102-198]concert with Lord Edward, who remained undetected, notwithstanding all

[102-199]the efforts of Government, from the 12th of March till the 19th of May

[102-200]following. During those two months the new directors devoted themselves

[102-201]with the utmost energy to hurrying on the armament of the people, and

[102-202]especially to making proselytes among the militia, where the gain of

[102-203]one man armed and disciplined was justly accounted equal to the

[102-204]enlistment of three or four ordinary adherents. This part of their plan

[102-205]brought the brothers Sheares into contact, among others, with Captain

[102-206]John Warneford Armstrong, of the Queen's County Yeomanry, whom they

[102-207]supposed they had won over, but who was, in reality, a better-class

[102-208]spy, acting under Lord Castlereagh's instructions. Armstrong cultivated

[102-209]them sedulously, dined at their table, echoed their opinions, and led

[102-210]the credulous brothers on to their destruction. All at last was

[102-211]determined on; the day of the rising was fixed—the 23rd day of May—and

[102-212]the signal was to be the simultaneous stoppage of the mail coaches,

[102-213]which started nightly from the Dublin post-office, to every quarter of

[102-214]the kingdom. But the counterplot anticipated the plot. Lord Edward,

[102-215]betrayed by a person called Higgins, proprietor of the _Freeman's

[102-216]Journal_, was taken on the 19th of May, after a desperate struggle with

[102-217]Majors Swan and Sirr, and Captain Ryan, in his hiding-place in Thomas

[102-218]Street; the brothers Sheares were arrested in their own house on the

[102-219]morning of the 21st, while Surgeon Lawless escaped from the city, and

[102-220]finally from the country, to France. Thus, for the second time, was the

[102-221]insurrection left without a head; but the organization had proceeded

[102-222]too far to be any longer restrained, and the Castle, moreover, to use

[102-223]the expression of Lord Castlereagh, "took means to make it explode."

[102-225]The first intelligence of the rebellion was received in Dublin on the

[102-226]morning of the 24th of May. At Rathfarnham, within three miles of the

[102-227]city, 500 insurgents attacked Lord Ely's yeomanry corps with some

[102-228]success, till Lord Roden's dragoons, hastily despatched from the city,

[102-229]compelled them to retreat, with the loss of some prisoners and two men

[102-230]killed, whom Mr. Beresford saw the next day, literally "_cut to

[102-231]pieces_—a horrid sight." At Dunboyne the insurgents piked an escort of

[102-232]the Reay Fencibles (Scotch) passing through their village, and carried

[102-233]off their baggage. At Naas, a large popular force attacked the

[102-234]garrison, consisting of regulars, Ancient Britons (Welsh), part of a

[102-235]regiment of dragoons, and the Armagh Militia; the attack was renewed

[102-236]three times with great bravery, but finally, discipline, as it always

[102-237]will, prevailed over mere numbers, and the assailants were repulsed

[102-238]with the loss of 140 of their comrades. At Prosperous, where they cut

[102-239]off to a man a strong garrison composed of North Cork Militia, under

[102-240]Captain Swayne, the rising was more successful. The commander in this

[102-241]exploit was Dr. Esmonde, brother of the Wexford baronet, who, being

[102-242]betrayed by one of his own subalterns, was the next morning arrested at

[102-243]breakfast in the neighbourhood, and suffered death at Dublin on the

[102-244]14th of the following month.

[102-245]There could hardly be found a more unfavourable field for a peasant war

[102-246]than the generally level and easily accessible county of Kildare, every

[102-247]parish of which is within a day's march of Dublin. From having been the

[102-248]residence of Lord Edward, it was, perhaps, one of the most highly

[102-249]organized parts of Leinster, but as it had the misfortune to be

[102-250]represented by Thomas Reynolds, as county delegate, it laboured under

[102-251]the disadvantage of having its organization better known to the

[102-252]government than any other. We need hardly be surprised, therefore, to

[102-253]find that the military operations in this county were all over in ten

[102-254]days or a fortnight; when those who had neither surrendered nor fallen,

[102-255]fell back into Meath or Connaught, or effected a junction with the

[102-256]Wicklow rebels in their mountain fastnesses. Their struggle, though so

[102-257]brief, had been creditable for personal bravery. Attacked by a numerous

[102-258]cavalry and militia under General Wilford, by 2,500 men, chiefly

[102-259]regulars, under General Dundas, and by 800 regulars brought up by

[102-260]forced marches from Limerick, under Sir James Duff, they showed

[102-261]qualities, which, if well directed, would have established for their

[102-262]possessors a high military reputation. At Monastereven they were

[102-263]repulsed with loss, the defenders of the town being in part Catholic

[102-264]loyalists, under Captain Cassidy; at Rathangan, they were more

[102-265]successful, taking and holding the town for several days; at Clane, the

[102-266]captors of Prosperous were repulsed; while at Old Killcullen, their

[102-267]associates drove back General Dundas' advance, with the loss of 22

[102-268]regulars and Captain Erskine killed. Sir James Duff's wanton cruelty in

[102-269]sabring and shooting down an unarmed multitude on the Curragh, won him

[102-270]the warm approval of the extermination party in the Capital, while

[102-271]Generals Wilford and Dundas narrowly escaped being reprimanded for

[102-272]granting a truce to the insurgents under Aylmer, and accepting of the

[102-273]surrender of that leader and his companions. By the beginning of June

[102-274]the six Kildare encampments of insurgents were totally dispersed, and

[102-275]their most active officers in prison or fugitives west or south.

[102-276]By a preconcerted arrangement, the local chiefs of the insurrection in

[102-277]Dublin and Meath, gathered with their men on the third day after the

[102-278]outbreak, at the historic hill of Tara. Here they expected to be joined

[102-279]by the men of Cavan, Longford, Louth and Monaghan; but before the

[102-280]northerners reached the trysting place, three companies of the Reay

[102-281]Fencibles, under Captain McClean, the Kells and Navan Yeomanry, under

[102-282]Captain Preston, (afterwards Lord Tara,) and a troop of cavalry under

[102-283]Lord Fingal, surrounded the royal hill. The insurgents, commanded by

[102-284]Gilshine and other leaders, intrenched themselves in the graveyard

[102-285]which occupies the summit of Tara, and stoutly defended their position.

[102-286]Twenty-six of the Highlanders and six of the Yeomanry fell in the

[102-287]assault, but the bullet reached farther than the pike, and the

[102-288]defenders were driven, after a sharp action, over the brow of the

[102-289]eminence, and many of them shot or sabred down as they fled.

[102-290]Southward from the Capital the long pent-up flame of disaffection broke

[102-291]out on the same memorable day, May 23rd. At Dunlavin, an abortive

[102-292]attempt on the barrack revealed the fact that many of the Yeomanry were

[102-293]thoroughly with the insurgents. Hardly had the danger from without

[102-294]passed over, when a military inquiry was improvised. By this tribunal,

[102-295]nineteen Wexford, and nine Kildare Yeomanry, were ordered to be shot,

[102-296]and the execution of the sentence followed immediately on its rending.

[102-297]At Blessington, the town was seized, but a nocturnal attack on Carlow

[102-298]was repulsed with great loss. In this last affair, the rebels had

[102-299]_rendezvoused_ in the domain of Sir Edward Crosbie, within two miles of

[102-300]the town. Here arms were distributed and orders given by their leader,

[102-301]named Roche. Silently and quickly they reached the town they hoped to

[102-302]surprise. But the regular troops, of which the garrison was chiefly

[102-303]composed, were on the alert, though their preparations were made full

[102-304]as silently. When the peasantry emerged from Tullow Street, into an

[102-305]exposed space, a deadly fire was opened upon them from the houses on

[102-306]all sides. The regulars, in perfect security themselves, and abundantly

[102-307]supplied with ammunition, shot them down with deadly unerring aim. The

[102-308]people soon found there was nothing for it but retreat, and carrying

[102-309]off as best they could their killed and wounded, they retired sorely

[102-310]discomfited. For alleged complicity in this attack, Sir Edward Crosbie

[102-311]was shortly afterward arrested, tried and executed. There was not a

[102-312]shadow of proof against him; but he was known to sympathize with the

[102-313]sufferings of his countrymen, to have condemned in strong language the

[102-314]policy of provocation, and that was sufficient. He paid with the

[102-315]penalty of his head for the kindness and generosity of his heart.
