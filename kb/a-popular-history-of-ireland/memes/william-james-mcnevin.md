## Overview

William James McNevin (1763–1841) was a Connaught Catholic physician who became a leader of the United Irishmen. Educated in Austria and practising medicine in Dublin, he joined the revolutionary conspiracy early and survived the government crackdown of 1798. (098-207) (100-137)

He later emigrated to the United States, where he remained an active advocate for Irish Catholic Emancipation into the 1820s. His long career bridged the revolutionary generation of the 1790s and the constitutional Catholic movement under Daniel O'Connell. (108-014) (114-131)

## Early Life and Education

McNevin was born in 1763 in Connaught, of Catholic background. Unlike most Irish professionals of his generation, he received his medical education abroad, in Austria. This was a consequence of the Penal Laws, which barred Catholics from taking degrees at Trinity College Dublin. (100-137)

Returning to Dublin, McNevin established himself as a physician. He briefly served as member for Phillipstown and became known in the capital's professional and political circles. (100-137)

> And ex-member for Phillipstown, and Dr. William James McNevin, a Connaught Catholic, educated in Austria, then practising his profession in Dublin. [100-137]

## The United Irishmen

McNevin was among those who joined the United Irishmen as the society transformed from an open reform club into a secret revolutionary organization. Along with McCormick, he "joined the United Irishmen, and the two latter were placed on the Directory." He was thus one of a handful of men who directed the entire United Irish organization during the critical years leading up to 1798. (098-207)

In 1796, McNevin was present at a secret meeting with the French general Hoche, who had been dispatched to assess the prospects for a French landing in Ireland. "From this meeting — the secret of which he kept to himself — the young general returned in the highest spirits," convinced that a French expedition could succeed if supported by a well-organized Irish rising. (101-046)

## Arrest and Imprisonment (1798)

On the 12th of March 1798, following information supplied by the informer Thomas Reynolds, McNevin was arrested at his own house in Dublin. The arrests occurred on the same day that the Leinster delegates were seized at Oliver Bond's house in Bridge Street, decapitating the United Irish leadership on the eve of the rebellion. (102-177)

McNevin was subsequently examined before the Committees of Parliament along with Thomas Addis Emmet and Arthur O'Conor. Their testimony, however, "did not altogether serve the purposes of government." The prisoners managed to withhold the most damaging information about the conspiracy. (104-309)

> Before the Committees of Parliament by Emmet, McNevin, and O'Conor, did not altogether serve the purposes of government. [104-309]

## Emigration to the United States

After the suppression of the rebellion, McNevin accepted exile. "Emmet and McNevin emigrated to the United States," part of a larger exodus of Irish radicals. (108-014)

In New York, McNevin was reunited with other United Irish exiles. "Emmet, McNevin, Sampson, and the family of Tone were reunited in New York." He resumed his medical practice and became a leading figure in the Irish emigrant community. (104-322)

## Advocate for Catholic Emancipation

McNevin remained politically engaged long after the revolutionary generation had passed. By the 1820s, the cause of Catholic Emancipation had been taken up by Daniel O'Connell through constitutional agitation. From New York, McNevin became one of the most prominent American voices in support. (114-128)

In January 1828, "the veteran McNevin wrote to Mr. O'Connell" conveying the depth of American support. His letter declared: "Public opinion in America is deep, and strong, and universal, in your behalf." His correspondence linked the revolutionary generation of 1798 with the constitutional movement that would achieve Emancipation in 1829. (114-131)

> January, 1828, the veteran McNevin wrote to Mr. O'Connell — "Public opinion in America is deep, and strong, and universal, in your behalf." [114-131]

## Legacy

William James McNevin's career spanned two continents and two eras of Irish nationalism. As a United Irishman, he sat on the Directory and met with French generals. As an emigrant physician in New York, he helped build American support for Catholic Emancipation. (098-207) (108-014)

His letter to O'Connell in 1828, written when he was sixty-five, stands as a testament to the endurance of the ideals of his youth. Among the United Irish leaders, he was unusual in surviving the revolutionary period and living to see a partial realization of the cause for which he had risked his life. (114-131)
