CHAPTER XI.

[098-001]THE ERA OF INDEPENDENCE—THIRD PERIOD—CATHOLIC RELIEF BILL OF 1793.

[098-002]Before relating the consequences which attended the spread of French

[098-003]revolutionary opinions in Ireland, it is necessary to exhibit the new

[098-004]and very important position assumed by the Roman Catholic population at

[098-005]that period.

[098-006]The relief bills in 1774 and 1778, by throwing open to Catholics the

[098-007]ordinary means of acquiring property, whether moveable or immoveable,

[098-008]had enabled many of them to acquire fortunes, both in land and in

[098-009]trade. Of this class were the most efficient leaders in the formation

[098-010]of the Catholic Committee of 1790—John Keogh, Edward Byrne, and Richard

[098-011]McCormick. They were all men who had acquired fortunes, and who felt

[098-012]and cherished the independence of self-made men. They were not simply

[098-013]Catholic agitators claiming an equality of civil and religious rights

[098-014]with their Protestant fellow-countrymen; they were nationalists, in the

[098-015]broadest and most generous meaning of the term. They had contributed to

[098-016]the ranks and expenses of the Volunteers; they had swelled the chorus

[098-017]of Grattan's triumph, and borne their share of the cost in many a

[098-018]popular contest. The new generation of Protestant patriots—such men as

[098-019]the Hon. Simon Butler, Wolfe Tone, and Thomas Addis Emmet, were their

[098-020]intimate associates, shared their opinions, and regarded their

[098-021]exclusion from the pale of the constitution as a public calamity.

[098-022]There was another and a smaller, but not less important class—the

[098-023]remnant of the ancient Catholic peerage and landed gentry, who, through

[098-024]four generations, had preferred civil death to religious apostasy. It

[098-025]was impossible not to revere the heroic constancy of that class, and

[098-026]the personal virtues of many among them. But they were, perhaps,

[098-027]constitutionally, too timid and too punctilious to conduct a popular

[098-028]movement to a successful issue. They had, after much persuasion, lent

[098-029]their presence to the Committee, but on some alarm, which at that time

[098-030]seems to have been premature, of the introduction of French

[098-031]revolutionary principles among their associates, they seceded in a

[098-032]mass. A formal remonstrance against what remained, pretending to act

[098-033]for the Catholic body, was signed by Lord Kenmare and sixty-seven

[098-034]others, who withdrew. As a corrective, it was inadequate; as a

[098-035]preventive, useless. It no doubt hastened in the end the evil it

[098-036]deprecated in the beginning; it separated the Catholic gentry from the

[098-037]Catholic democracy, and thrust the latter more and more towards those

[098-038]liberal Protestants, mainly men of the middle class like themselves,

[098-039]who began about this time to club together at Belfast and Dublin, under

[098-040]the attractive title of "United Irishmen." Whatever they were

[098-041]individually, the union of so many hereditary Catholic names had been

[098-042]of very great service to the committee. So long as they stood aloof,

[098-043]the committee could not venture to speak for _all_ the Catholics; it

[098-044]could only speak for a part, though that part might be nine-tenths of

[098-045]the whole: this gave for a time a doubtful and hesitating appearance to

[098-046]their proceedings. So low was their political influence, in 1791, that

[098-047]they could not get a single member of Parliament to present their

[098-048]annual petition. When at last it was presented, it was laid on the

[098-049]table and never noticed afterwards. To their further embarrassment, Mr.

[098-050]McKenna and some others formed "the Catholic Society," with the nominal

[098-051]object of spreading a knowledge of Catholic principles, through the

[098-052]press, but covertly, to raise up a rival organization, under the

[098-053]control of the seceders. At this period John Keogh's talents for

[098-054]negotiation and diplomacy saved the Catholic body from another term of

[098-055]anarchical imbecility.

[098-056]A deputation of twelve having waited this year on the Chief Secretary

[098-057]with a list of the existing penal laws, found no intention, at the

[098-058]Castle, of further concession. They were "dismissed without an answer."

[098-059]Under these circumstances, the Committee met at Allen's Court. "It was

[098-060]their determination," says Keogh, "to give up the cause as desperate,

[098-061]lest a perseverance in what they considered an idle pursuit might not

[098-062]only prove ineffectual, but draw down a train of persecution on the

[098-063]body." Keogh endeavoured to rally them; proposed a delegation to

[098-064]London, to be sent at the expense of the Committee; offered, at last,

[098-065]to go at his own charge, if they authorized him. This proposal was

[098-066]accepted, and Keogh went. "I arrived in London," he adds, "without any

[098-067]introduction from this country, without any support, any assistance,

[098-068]any instructions." He remained three months, converted Mr. Dundas,

[098-069]brought back with him the son of Burke as Secretary, and a promise of

[098-070]four concessions: 1st. The magistracy. 2nd. The grand juries. 3rd. The

[098-071]sheriffs of counties. 4th. The bar. It was in this interview that

[098-072]Keogh, after obtaining Mr. Dundas's express permission and promise not

[098-073]to be offended, said to him, according to Charles Butler's account,

[098-074]"Since you give me this permission, and your deliberate promise not to

[098-075]be offended, I beg leave to repeat, that there _is_ one thing which you

[098-076]ought to know, but which you don't suspect: you, Mr. Dundas, know

[098-077]nothing of Ireland." Mr. Dundas, as may be supposed, was greatly

[098-078]surprised; but, with perfect good humour, told Mr. Keogh that he

[098-079]believed this was not the case; it was true that he never had been in

[098-080]Ireland, but he had conversed with many Irishmen. "I have drunk," he

[098-081]said, "many a good bottle of wine with Lord Hillsborough, Lord Clare,

[098-082]and the Beresfords." "Yes, sir," said Mr. Keogh, "I believe you have;

[098-083]and that you drank many a good bottle of wine with them before you went

[098-084]to war with America."

[098-085]On the return of Keogh to Dublin, a numerous meeting was held to hear

[098-086]his report. At this meeting, the fair promises of the English ministers

[098-087]were contrasted with the hostility of the Castle. The necessity of a

[098-088]strong organization, to overcome the one and hasten the other, was felt

[098-089]by all: it was then decided to form the Committee into a Convention. By

[098-090]this plan, the Catholics in each county and borough were called on to

[098-091]choose, in a private manner, certain electors, who were to elect two or

[098-092]more delegates, to represent the town or county in the general meeting

[098-093]at Dublin, on the 3rd day of December following. A circular, signed by

[098-094]Edward Byrne, Chairman, and Richard McCormick, Secretary, explaining

[098-095]the plan and the mode of election, was issued on the 14th of January,

[098-096]and the Catholics everywhere prepared to obey it.

[098-097]The corporations of Dublin and other cities, the grand juries of Derry,

[098-098]Donegal, Leitrim, Roscommon, Limerick, Cork, and other counties, at

[098-099]once pronounced most strongly against the proposed Convention. They

[098-100]declared it "unconstitutional," "alarming," "most dangerous;" they

[098-101]denounced it as a copy of the National Assembly of France; they

[098-102]declared that they would "resist it to the utmost of their power;" they

[098-103]pledged "their lives and fortunes" to suppress it. The only answer of

[098-104]the Catholics was the legal opinion of Butler and Burton, two eminent

[098-105]lawyers, Protestants and King's counsellors, that the measure was

[098-106]entirely legal. They proceeded with their selection of delegates, and

[098-107]on the appointed day the Convention met. From the place of meeting,

[098-108]this Convention was popularly called "the Back Lane Parliament." Above

[098-109]200 members were present.

[098-110]The Convention proceeded (Mr. Byrne in the chair) to declare itself the

[098-111]only body competent to speak for the Catholics of Ireland. They next

[098-112]discussed the substance of the proposed petition to the King. The

[098-113]debate on this subject, full of life and colour, has been preserved for

[098-114]us in the memoirs of Tone, who, although a Protestant, had been elected

[098-115]Secretary to the Catholic Committee. Great firmness was exhibited by

[098-116]Teeling of Antrim, Bellew of Galway, McDermott of Sligo, Devereux of

[098-117]Wexford, Sir Thomas French, and John Keogh. These gentlemen contended,

[098-118]and finally carried, without a division, though not without a two-days'

[098-119]debate, a petition, asking complete and unrestricted emancipation. With

[098-120]the addition of the Chairman and Secretary, they were appointed as

[098-121]deputies to proceed to London, there to place the Catholic ultimatum in

[098-122]the hands of King George.

[098-123]The deputies, whether by design or accident, took Belfast on their way

[098-124]to England. This great manufacturing town, at the head of the staple

[098-125]industry of the north, had been in succession the head-quarters of the

[098-126]Volunteers, the Northern Whigs, and the United Irishmen. Belfast had

[098-127]demanded in vain, for nearly a generation, that its 20,000 inhabitants

[098-128]should no longer be disfranchised, while a dozen burgesses—creatures of

[098-129]Lord Donegal—controlled the representation. Community of

[098-130]disfranchisement had made the Belfastians liberal; the Catholic

[098-131]deputies were publicly received with bonfires and ringing of bells,

[098-132]their expenses were paid by the citizens, and their carriage drawn

[098-133]along in triumph, on the road to Port-Patrick.

[098-134]Arrived at London, after much negotiation and delay with ministers, a

[098-135]day was fixed for their introduction to the King. It was Wednesday, the

[098-136]2nd of January, 1793; they were presented by Edmund Burke and the Home

[098-137]Secretary to George III., who "received them very graciously;" they

[098-138]placed in his hands the petition of their co-religionists, and, after

[098-139]some compliments, withdrew. In a few days, they were assured their case

[098-140]would be recommended to the attention of Parliament in the next royal

[098-141]speech, and so, leaving one of their number behind as "charge

[098-142]d'affaires," they returned to Dublin highly elated.

[098-144]The Viceroy, on their return, was all attention to the Catholics; the

[098-145]Secretary, who, a year before, would not listen to a petition, now

[098-146]laboured to fix a limit to concession. The demand of complete

[098-147]emancipation, was not maintained in this negotiation as firmly as in

[098-148]the December debates of "the Back Lane Parliament." The shock of the

[098-149]execution of the King of France; the efforts of the secret committee of

[098-150]the House of Lords to inculpate certain Catholic leaders in the

[098-151]United-Irish system, and as patrons of the Defenders; the telling

[098-152]argument, that to press all was to risk all,—these causes combined to

[098-153]induce the sub-committee to consent to less than the Convention had

[098-154]decided to insist upon. Negotiation was the strong ground of the

[098-155]government, and they kept it. Finally, the bill was introduced by the

[098-156]Chief Secretary, and warmly supported by Grattan, Curran, Ponsonby,

[098-157]Forbes, and Hutchinson, Provost of Trinity College. It was resisted in

[098-158]the Lower House by Mr. Speaker Foster, Mr. Ogle, and Dr. Duigenan, an

[098-159]apostate, who exhibited all the bitterness of his class; and in the

[098-160]Upper House, by the Chancellor, the son of an apostate, and the

[098-161]majority of the lords spiritual. On the 9th day of April, 1793, it

[098-162]became the law of Ireland. "By one comprehensive clause," says Tone,

[098-163]"all penalties, forfeitures, disabilities, and incapacities are

[098-164]removed; the property of the Catholic is completely discharged from the

[098-165]restraints and limitations of the penal laws, and their liberty, in a

[098-166]great measure, restored, by the restoration of the right of elective

[098-167]franchise, so long withheld, so ardently pursued. The right of

[098-168]self-defence is established by the restoration of the privilege to

[098-169]carry arms, subject to a restraint, which does not seem unreasonable,

[098-170]as excluding none but the very lowest orders. The unjust and

[098-171]unreasonable distinctions affecting Catholics, as to service on grand

[098-172]and petty juries, are done away; the army, navy, and all other offices

[098-173]and places of trust are opened to them, subject to exceptions hereafter

[098-174]mentioned. Catholics may be masters or fellows of any college hereafter

[098-175]to be founded, subject to two conditions, that such college be a member

[098-176]of the University, and that it be not founded exclusively for the

[098-177]education of Catholics. They may be members of any lay body corporate,

[098-178]except Trinity College, any law, statute, or bye-law of such

[098-179]corporation to the contrary notwithstanding. They may obtain degrees in

[098-180]the University of Dublin. These, and some lesser immunities and

[098-181]privileges, constitute the grant of the bill, the value of which will

[098-182]be best ascertained by referring to the petition."

[098-183]It is true, Catholics were still excluded from the high offices of Lord

[098-184]Lieutenant, Lord Deputy, and Lord Chancellor. What was much more

[098-185]important, they were excluded from sitting in Parliament—from

[098-186]exercising legislative and judicial functions, Still the franchise, the

[098-187]juries, the professions, and the University, were important

[098-188]concessions. Their first fruits were Daniel O'Connell and Thomas Moore!

[098-189]The Committee having met to return thanks to the parliamentary

[098-190]supporters of the bill, their own future operations came also under

[098-191]debate. Some members advised that they should add reform to their

[098-192]programme, as the remnant of the penal laws were not sufficient to

[098-193]interest and attract the people. Some would have gone much further than

[098-194]reform; some were well content to rest on their laurels. There were

[098-195]ultras, moderate men, and conservatives, even in the twelve. The latter

[098-196]were more numerous than Wolfe Tone liked or expected. That ardent

[098-197]revolutionist had, indeed, at bottom, a strong dislike of the Catholic

[098-198]religion; he united himself with that body because he needed a party;

[098-199]he remained with them because it gave him importance; but he chiefly

[098-200]valued the position as it enabled him to further an ulterior design—an

[098-201]Irish revolution and a republic on the French plan. The example of

[098-202]France had, however, grown by this time rather a terror than an

[098-203]attraction to more cautious men than Tone. Edward Byrne, Sir Thomas

[098-204]French, and other leading Catholics, were openly hostile to any

[098-205]imitation of it, and the dinner at Daly's, to celebrate the passage of

[098-206]the act, was strongly anti-Gallican in spirit and sentiment. Keogh,

[098-207]McCormick, and McNevin, however, joined the United Irishmen, and the

[098-208]two latter were placed on the Directory. Keogh withdrew, when, in 1795,

[098-209]that organization became a secret society.

[098-210]The Bishops, who had cheered on, rather than participated in the late

[098-211]struggle, were well satisfied with the new measure. They were, by

[098-212]education and conviction, conservatives. Dr. Plunkett of Meath, Dr.

[098-213]Egan of Waterford, Dr. Troy of Dublin, and Dr. Moylan of Cork, were the

[098-214]most remarkable for influence and ability at this period. Dr. Butler of

[098-215]Cashel, and his opponent, Dr. Burke of Ossory, the head of the resolute

[098-216]old ultramontane minority, were both recently deceased. With the

[098-217]exception of Dr. James Butler, Bishop of Cloyne and Ross, who deserted

[098-218]his faith and order on becoming unexpectedly heir to an earldom, the

[098-219]Irish prelates of the reign of George III. were a most zealous and

[098-220]devoted body. Lord Dunboyne's fall was the only cause of a reproach

[098-221]within their own ranks. That unhappy prelate made, many years

[098-222]afterwards, a death-bed repentance, was reconciled to his church, and

[098-223]bequeathed a large part of his inherited wealth to sustain the new

[098-224]national college, the founding of which, ever since the outbreak of the

[098-225]French revolution, the far-seeing Burke was urging upon Pitt and all

[098-226]his Irish correspondents.

[098-227]In 1794, the Irish Bishops, having applied for a "royal license" to

[098-228]establish academies and seminaries, were graciously received, and Lord

[098-229]Fitzwilliam's government the next session brought in the Act of

[098-230]Incorporation. It became law on the 5th of June, 1795, and the college

[098-231]was opened the following October with fifty students. Dr Hussey,

[098-232]afterwards Bishop of Waterford, the friend of Burke, who stood by his

[098-233]deathbed, was first President; some refugee French divines were

[098-234]appointed to professorships; and the Irish Parliament voted the very

[098-235]handsome sum of 8,000 pounds a year to the new foundation. Maynooth,

[098-236]whatever its after lot, was the creation in the first instance of the

[098-237]Irish Parliament. We have thus, in the third century after the

[098-238]reformation, after three great religious wars, after four

[098-239]confiscations, after the most ingenious, cruel, and unchristian methods

[098-240]of oppression and proselytism, had been tried and had failed, the grand

[098-241]spectacle of the Catholics of Ireland restored, if not fully, yet to

[098-242]the most precious of the civil and religious liberties of a people! So

[098-243]powerless against conscience is and ever must be coercion!
