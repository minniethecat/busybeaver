## Overview

Thorlogh O'Brien was King of Munster and the principal rival of [[Thorlogh More O'Conor]] during the mid-12th century contest for the Irish high-kingship. As successor to Conor of the fortresses, he commanded a powerful army and sought to maintain the O'Brien dynasty's pre-eminence that had been established by his ancestor [[Brian Boru]].

His reign culminated in the Battle of Moanmore in 1151, a catastrophic defeat at the hands of Thorlogh More O'Conor that effectively ended O'Brien hegemony in the south of Ireland. (019-013, 019-022)

## Rise to Power

Thorlogh O'Brien succeeded to the kingship of Munster and the throne of Cashel in the generation after the death of Brian Boru. The O'Brien dynasty had dominated the Irish high-kingship for over a century, but by the early 12th century, the rising power of the O'Conors of Connacht under Thorlogh More posed an existential challenge:

> [018-002] Four years before the death of Thorlogh O'Brien, a Prince destined to be the life-long rival of his great son, had succeeded to the kingship. (018-002)

This "Prince" was Thorlogh More O'Conor, who would become the dominant figure of the era. The rivalry between the O'Briens of Munster and the O'Conors of Connacht defined the politics of 12th-century Ireland.

In his early reign, Thorlogh O'Brien was a loyal ally of the ruling high-king. His father had owed a deep debt to his northern patrons, and Thorlogh O'Brien as King of Cashel faithfully repaid that obligation. (017-136) He also demonstrated his piety through dealings with the great monastic foundation of Clonmacnoise. (017-172)

## The Battle of Moanmore (1151)

The great crisis of Thorlogh O'Brien's reign came in 1151, the fifteenth year of Thorlogh More O'Conor's reign. Determined to throw off the overlordship of Connacht, Thorlogh O'Brien raised the largest army Munster had fielded in a generation:

> [019-014] Thorlogh O'Brien, King of Munster, successor to Conor of the fortresses, had on foot, in that year, an army of three battalions (or caths), each battalion consisting of 3,000 men, with which force he overawed some and compelled others of the southern chiefs to withdraw their homage from O'Conor. (019-014)

In response, Thorlogh More united his own Connacht forces with those of Meath and Leinster and marched southward. The two armies met at Moanmore:

> [019-022] He encountered, at Moanmore, the army of the south, and defeated them, with the enormous loss of 7,000 men—a slaughter unparalleled throughout the war of succession. Every leading house in North Munster mourned the loss of either its chief or its tanist; some great families lost three, five, or seven brothers on that sanguinary day. (019-022, 019-025)

> [019-027] The household of Kinkora was left without an heir, and many a near kinsman's seat was vacant in its hospitable hall. (019-027)

[[Dermid McMurrogh]], King of Leinster, fought on the side of the victor at Moanmore, earning the enduring enmity of Thorlogh O'Brien. (019-032)

## The Partition of Munster

Following his victory, Thorlogh More O'Conor exercised the victor's prerogative by partitioning Munster. Earlier in 1151, the high-king had divided Munster into three parts:

> [019-048] King Thorlogh had divided Munster into three parts, giving Desmond to MacCarthy, Ormond to Thaddeus O'Brien, who had fought under him at Moanmore, and leaving the remainder to the O'Brien. (019-048)

> [019-054] By these subdivisions the politic monarch expected to weaken to a great degree the power of the rival families of Meath and Munster. It was an arbitrary policy which could originate only on the field of battle, and could be enforced only by the sanction of victory. (019-052, 019-054)

Thorlogh O'Brien refused to accept a diminished kingdom of a mere third of Munster:

> [019-056] Thorlogh O'Brien, once King of all Munster, refused to accept a mere third, and carrying away his jewels and valuables, including the drinking horn of the great Brian, he threw himself again on the protection of Murkertach of Aileach. (019-055, 019-056)

This act of defiance preserved the symbolic treasures of the O'Brien dynasty but marked the effective end of O'Brien supremacy in the south.

## Later Years and Death

In his later years, Thorlogh O'Brien sought to regain his former power. He formed an alliance with Dermid McMurrogh of Leinster, and together they attempted to resist the ascendancy of the O'Conors. (018-170) His final illness and death occurred around 1165, four years before the Norman invasion of Ireland that would transform the political landscape of the entire country. (017-167)

> [017-180] Thus were God and Saint Kieran vindicated. Thorlogh O'Brien slowly regained his strength, though the authors he favoured recorded events with a partisan pen. (017-180)

## Legacy

Thorlogh O'Brien was the last O'Brien king who could realistically claim the high-kingship of Ireland. His defeat at Moanmore in 1151 marked the definitive transfer of national power from the descendants of Brian Boru in Munster to the O'Conor dynasty of Connacht. The partition of Munster that followed his defeat fragmented O'Brien power for generations.

His career illustrates the destructive nature of the prolonged war of succession that weakened the Irish monarchy in the century before the Anglo-Norman invasion. The chronic infighting between O'Brien and O'Conor, Munster and Connacht, left Ireland divided and vulnerable when the Normans landed in 1169, just a few years after Thorlogh O'Brien's death.
