CHAPTER X.

[047-001]STATE OF RELIGION AND LEARNING DURING THE FOURTEENTH AND FIFTEENTH

[047-002]CENTURIES.

[047-003]Although the English and Irish professed the same religion during these

[047-004]ages, yet in the appointment of Bishops, the administration of

[047-005]ecclesiastical property, and in all their views of the relation of the

[047-006]Church to the State, the two nations differed almost as widely as in

[047-007]their laws, language, and customs. The Plantagenet princes and their

[047-008]Parliaments had always exhibited a jealousy of the See of Rome, and

[047-009]statute upon, statute was passed, from the reign of Henry II. to that

[047-010]of Richard II., in order to diminish the power of the Supreme Pontiffs

[047-011]in nominating to English benefices. In the second Richard's reign, so

[047-012]eventful for the English interest in Ireland, it had been enacted that

[047-013]any of the clergy procuring appointments directly from Rome, or

[047-014]exercising powers so conferred, should incur the penalty of a

[047-015]praemunire—that is, the forfeiture of their lands and chattels, beside

[047-016]being liable to imprisonment during the King's pleasure. This statute

[047-017]was held to apply equally to Ireland, being confirmed by some of those

[047-018]petty conventions of "the Pale," which the Dublin Governors of the

[047-019]fourteenth century dignified with the name of Parliaments.

[047-020]The ancient Irish method of promotion to a vacant see, or abbacy,

[047-021]though modelled on the electoral principle which penetrated all Celtic

[047-022]usages, was undoubtedly open to the charge of favouring nepotism, down

[047-023]to the time of Saint Malachy, the restorer of the Irish Church. After

[047-024]that period, the Prelates elect were ever careful to obtain the

[047-025]sanction of the Holy See, before consecration. Such habitual submission

[047-026]to Rome was seldom found, except in cases of disputed election, to

[047-027]interfere with the choice of the clergy, and the custom grew more and

[047-028]more into favour, as the English method of nomination by the crown was

[047-029]attempted to be enforced, not only throughout "the Pale," but, by means

[047-030]of English agents at Rome and Avignon, in the appointment to sees,

[047-031]within the provinces of Armagh, Cashel, and Tuam. The ancient usage of

[047-032]farming the church lands, under the charge of a lay steward, or

[047-033]_Erenach_, elected by the clan, and the division of all the revenues

[047-034]into four parts—for the Bishop, the Vicar and his priests, for the

[047-035]poor, and for repairs of the sacred edifice, was equally opposed to the

[047-036]pretensions of Princes, who looked on their Bishops as Barons, and

[047-037]Church temporalities, like all other fiefs, as held originally of the

[047-038]crown. Even if there had not been those differences of origin,

[047-039]interest, and government which necessarily brought the two populations

[047-040]into collision, these distinct systems of ecclesiastical polity could

[047-041]not well have existed on the same soil without frequently clashing, one

[047-042]with the other.

[047-044]In our notice of the association promoted among the clergy, at the end

[047-045]of the thirteenth century, by the patriotic McMaelisa, ("follower of

[047-046]Jesus"), and in our own comments on the memorable letter of Prince

[047-047]Donald O'Neil to Pope John XXII., written in the year 1317 or '18, we

[047-048]have seen how wide and deep was the gulf then existing between the

[047-049]English and Irish churchmen. In the year 1324, an attempt to heal this

[047-050]unchristian breach was made by Philip of Slane, the Dominican who

[047-051]presided at the trial of the Knights Templars, who afterwards became

[047-052]Bishop of Cork, and rose into high favour with the Queen-Mother,

[047-053]Isabella. As her Ambassador, or in the name of King Edward III., still

[047-054]a minor, he is reported to have submitted to Pope John certain

[047-055]propositions for the promotion of peace in the Irish Church, some of

[047-056]which were certainly well calculated to promote that end. He suggested

[047-057]that the smaller Bishoprics, yielding under sixty pounds per annum,

[047-058]should be united to more eminent sees, and that Irish Abbots and Priors

[047-059]should admit English lay brothers to their houses, and English

[047-060]Superiors Irish brothers, in like manner. The third proposition,

[047-061]however, savours more of the politician than of the peacemaker; it was

[047-062]to bring under the bann of excommunication, with all its rigorous

[047-063]consequences in that age, those "disturbers of the peace" who invaded

[047-064]the authority of the English King in Ireland. As a consequence of this

[047-065]mission, a Concordat for Ireland seems to have been concluded at

[047-066]Avignon, embracing the two first points, but omitting the third, which

[047-067]was, no doubt, with the English Court, the main object of Friar

[047-068]Philip's embassy.

[047-069]During the fourteenth century, and down to the election of Martin V.

[047-070](A.D. 1417), the Popes sat mainly at Avignon, in France. In the last

[047-071]forty years of that melancholy period, other Prelates sitting at Rome,

[047-072]or elsewhere in Italy, claimed the Apostolic primacy. It was in the

[047-073]midst of these troubles and trials of the Church that the powerful

[047-074]Kings of England, who were also sovereigns of a great part of France,

[047-075]contrived to extort from the embarrassed pontiffs concessions which,

[047-076]however gratifying to royal pride, were abhorrent to the more Catholic

[047-077]spirit of the Irish people. A constant struggle was maintained during

[047-078]the entire period of the captivity of the Popes in France between Roman

[047-079]and English influence in Ireland. There were often two sets of Bishops

[047-080]elected in such border sees as Meath and Louth, which were districts

[047-081]under a divided influence. The Bishops of Limerick, Cork, and

[047-082]Waterford, liable to have their revenues cut off, and their personal

[047-083]liberty endangered by sea, were almost invariably nominees of the

[047-084]English Court; those of the Province of Dublin were necessarily so; but

[047-085]the prelates of Ulster, of Connaught, and of Munster—the southern

[047-086]seaports excepted—were almost invariably native ecclesiastics, elected

[047-087]in the old mode, by the assembled clergy, and receiving letters of

[047-088]confirmation direct from Avignon or Italy.

[047-089]A few incidents in the history of the Church of Cashel will better

[047-090]illustrate the character of the contest between the native episcopacy

[047-091]and the foreign power. Towards the end of the thirteenth century,

[047-092]Archbishop McCarwill maintained with great courage the independence of

[047-093]his jurisdiction against Henry III. and Edward I. Having inducted

[047-094]certain Bishops into their sees without waiting for the royal letters,

[047-095]he sustained a long litigation in the Anglo-Irish courts, and was much

[047-096]harassed in his goods and person. Seizing from a usurer 400 pounds, he

[047-097]successfully resisted the feudal claim of Edward I., as lord paramount,

[047-098]to pay over the money to the royal exchequer. Edward having undertaken

[047-099]to erect a prison—or fortress in disguise—in his episcopal city, the

[047-100]bold Prelate publicly excommunicated the Lord Justice who undertook the

[047-101]work, the escheator who supplied the funds, and all those engaged in

[047-102]its construction, nor did he desist from his opposition until the

[047-103]obnoxious building was demolished. Ralph O'Kelly, who filled the same

[047-104]see from 1345 to 1361, exhibited an equally dauntless spirit. An

[047-105]Anglo-Irish Parliament having levied a subsidy on all property, lay and

[047-106]ecclesiastical, within their jurisdiction, to carry on the war of races

[047-107]before described, he not only opposed its collection within the

[047-108]Province of Cashel, but publicly excommunicated Epworth, Clerk of the

[047-109]Council, who had undertaken that task. For this offence an information

[047-110]was exhibited against him, laying the King's damages at a thousand

[047-111]pounds; but he pleaded the liberties of the Church, and successfully

[047-112]traversed the indictment. Richard O'Hedian, Archbishop from 1406 to

[047-113]1440, was a Prelate of similar spirit to his predecessors. At a

[047-114]Parliament held in Dublin in 1421, it was formally alleged, among other

[047-115]enormities, that he made very much of the Irish and loved none of the

[047-116]English; that he presented no Englishman to a benefice, and advised

[047-117]other Prelates to do likewise; and that he made himself King of

[047-118]Munster—alluding, probably, to some revival at this time of the old

[047-119]title of Prince-Bishop, which had anciently belonged to the Prelates of

[047-120]Cashel. O'Hedian retained his authority, however, till his death, after

[047-121]which the see remained twelve years vacant, the temporalities being

[047-122]farmed by the Earl of Ormond.

[047-123]From this conflict of interests, frequently resulting in disputed

[047-124]possession and intrusive jurisdiction, religion must have suffered

[047-125]much, at least in its discipline and decorum. The English Archbishops

[047-126]of Dublin would not yield in public processions to the Irish

[047-127]Archbishops of Armagh, nor permit the crozier of St. Patrick to be

[047-128]borne publicly through their city; the English Bishop of Waterford was

[047-129]the public accuser of the Irish Archbishop of Cashel, last mentioned,

[047-130]before a lay tribunal—the knights and burgesses of "the Pale." The

[047-131]annual expeditions sent out from Dublin, to harass the nearest native

[047-132]clans, were seldom without a Bishop or Abbot, or Prior of the Temple or

[047-133]Hospital, in their midst. Scandals must have ensued; hatreds must have

[047-134]sprung up; prejudices, fatal to charity and unity, must have been

[047-135]engendered, both on the one side and the other. The spirit of party

[047-136]carried into the Church can be cherished in the presence of the Altar

[047-137]and Cross only by doing violence to the teachings of the Cross and the

[047-138]sanctity of the Altar.

[047-139]While such was the troubled state of the Church, as exemplified in its

[047-140]twofold hierarchy, the religious orders continued to spread, with

[047-141]amazing energy, among both races. The orders of Saint Francis and Saint

[047-142]Dominick, those twin giants of the thirteenth century, already rivalled

[047-143]the mighty brotherhood which Saint Bernard had consecrated, and Saint

[047-144]Malachy had introduced into the Irish Church. It is observable that the

[047-145]Dominicans, at least at first, were most favoured by the English and

[047-146]the Anglo-Irish; while the Franciscans were more popular with the

[047-147]native population. Exceptions may be found on both sides: but as a

[047-148]general rule this distinction can be traced in the strongholds of

[047-149]either order, and in the names of their most conspicuous members, down

[047-150]to that dark and trying hour when the tempest of "the Reformation"

[047-151]involved both in a common danger, and demonstrated their equal heroism.

[047-152]As elsewhere in Christendom, the sudden aggrandizement of these

[047-153]mendicant institutes excited jealousy and hostility among certain of

[047-154]the secular clergy and Bishops. This feeling was even stronger in

[047-155]England during the reigns of Edward III. and Richard II., when,

[047-156]according to the popular superstition, the Devil appeared at various

[047-157]places "in the form of a grey friar." The great champion of the secular

[047-158]clergy, in the controversy which ensued, was Richard, son of Ralph, a

[047-159]native of Dundalk, the Erasmus of his age. Having graduated at Oxford,

[047-160]where the Irish were then classed as one of "the four nations" of

[047-161]students, Fitz-Ralph achieved distinction after distinction, till he

[047-162]rose to the rank of Chancellor of the University, in 1333. Fourteen

[047-163]years afterwards he was consecrated, by provision of Pope Clement VI.,

[047-164]Archbishop of Armagh, and is by some writers styled "Cardinal of

[047-165]Armagh." Inducted into the chief see of his native Province and

[047-166]country, he soon commenced those sermons and writings against the

[047-167]mendicant orders which rendered him so conspicuous in the Church

[047-168]history of the fourteenth century. Summoned to Avignon, in 1350, to be

[047-169]examined on his doctrine, he maintained before the Consistory the

[047-170]following propositions: 1st, that our Lord Jesus Christ, as a man, was

[047-171]very poor, not that He loved poverty for itself; 2nd, that our Lord had

[047-172]never begged; 3rd, that He never taught men to beg; 4th, that, on the

[047-173]contrary, He taught men not to beg; 5th, that man cannot, with prudence

[047-174]and holiness, confine himself by vow to a life of constant mendicity;

[047-175]6th, that minor brothers are not obliged by their rule to beg; 7th,

[047-176]that the bull of Alexander IV., which condemns the Book of Masters,

[047-177]does not invalidate any of the aforesaid conclusions; 8th, that by

[047-178]those who, wishing to confess, exclude certain churches, their parish

[047-179]one should be preferred to the oratories of monks; and 9th, that, for

[047-180]auricular confession, the diocesan, bishop should be chosen in

[047-181]preference to friars.

[047-182]In a "defence of Parish Priests," and many other tracts, in several

[047-183]sermons, preached at London, Litchfield, Drogheda, Dundalk, and Armagh,

[047-184]he maintained the thesis until the year 1357, when the Superior of the

[047-185]Franciscans at Armagh, seconded by the influence of his own and the

[047-186]Dominican order, caused him to be summoned a second time before the

[047-187]Pope. Fitz-Ralph promptly obeyed the summons, but before the cause

[047-188]could be finally decided he died at Avignon in 1361. His body was

[047-189]removed from thence to Dundalk in 1370 by Stephen de Valle, Bishop of

[047-190]Meath. Miracles were said to have been wrought at his tomb; a process

[047-191]of inquiry into their validity was instituted by order of Boniface IX.,

[047-192]but abandoned without any result being arrived at. The bitter

[047-193]controversy between the mendicant and other orders was revived towards

[047-194]the end of the century by Henry, a Cistercian monk of Baltinglass, who

[047-195]maintained opinions still more extreme than those of Fitz-Ralph; but he

[047-196]was compelled publicly and solemnly to retract them before

[047-197]Commissioners appointed for that purpose in the year 1382.

[047-198]The range of mental culture in Europe during the fourteenth century

[047-199]included only the scholastic philosophy and theology with the physics,

[047-200]taught in the schools of the Spanish Arabs. The fifteenth century saw

[047-201]the revival of Greek literature in Italy, and the general restoration

[047-202]of classical learning. The former century is especially barren of

[047-203]original _belles lettres_ writings; but the next succeeding ages

[047-204]produced Italian poetry, French chronicles, Spanish ballads, and all

[047-205]that wonderful efflorescence of popular literature, which, in our far

[047-206]advanced cultivation, we still so much envy and admire. In the last

[047-207]days of Scholasticism, Irish intelligence asserted its ancient equality

[047-208]with the best minds of Europe; but in the new era of national

[047-209]literature, unless there are buried treasures yet to be dug out of

[047-210]their Gaelic tombs, the country fell altogether behind England, and

[047-211]even Scotland, not to speak of Italy or France. Archbishop Fitz-Ralph,

[047-212]John Scotus of Down, William of Drogheda, Professor of both laws at

[047-213]Oxford, are respectable representatives among the last and greatest

[047-214]group of the School-men. Another illustrious name remains to be added

[047-215]to the roll of Irish Scholastics, that of Maurice O'Fihely, Archbishop

[047-216]of Tuam. He was a thorough Scotist in philosophy, which he taught at

[047-217]Padua, in discourses long afterwards printed at Venice. His

[047-218]Commentaries on _Scotus_, his Dictionary of the Sacred Scriptures, and

[047-219]other numerous writings, go far to justify the compliments of his

[047-220]cotemporaries, though the fond appellation of the "flower of the earth"

[047-221]given him by some of them sounds extravagant and absurd. Soon after

[047-222]arriving from Rome to take possession of his see he died at Tuam in

[047-223]1513, in the fiftieth year of his age—an early age to have won so

[047-224]colossal a reputation.

[047-225]Beyond some meagre annals, compiled in monastic houses, and a few

[047-226]rhymed panegyrics, the muses of history and of poetry seem to have

[047-227]abandoned the island to the theologians, jurists, and men of science.

[047-228]The Bardic order was still one of the recognized estates, and found

[047-229]patrons worthy of their harps in the lady Margaret O'Carroll of

[047-230]Offally, William O'Kelley of Galway, and Henry Avery O'Neil. Full

[047-231]collections of the original Irish poetry of the Middle Ages are yet to

[047-232]be made public, but it is scarcely possible that if any composition of

[047-233]eminent merit existed, we should not have had editions and translations

[047-234]of it before now.
