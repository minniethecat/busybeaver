## Overview

John Jeffreys Pratt, 2nd Earl Camden (1759–1840), served as Lord Lieutenant of Ireland from March 1795 to June 1798. Appointed to replace the reform-minded Lord Fitzwilliam, Camden pursued systematic coercion.

His policy of repression helped drive Ireland into the Rebellion of 1798. Camden's administration embodied the Ascendancy's determination to resist Catholic emancipation and maintain exclusive Protestant control.

## Appointment and Instructions

Lord Camden was appointed immediately after Fitzwilliam's recall. "Lord Fitzwilliam was immediately replaced by Lord Camden" (099-198), an appointment that signaled the triumph of the anti-reform faction at court.

> Lord Camden, the Viceroy who succeeded Lord Fitzwilliam in March, '95 — with Mr. Pelham as his Chief Secretary, in a letter to his relative, the Hon. Robert Stewart, afterwards Lord Castlereagh, articulated the government's strategy. (102-006)

Camden's "Viceroyalty extended into the middle of the year 1798" (099-199), encompassing the final years of the independent Irish Parliament. The King directed him "to support the old English interest as well as the Protestant religion" (105-020).

## Policy of Repression

Camden's administration pursued harsh methods from the outset. "The policy of torture and provocation pursued under Lord Camden" (104-013) became notorious. Military outrages, whipping, torture, and suppression of the press characterized the period (100-145).

"In the latter part of Lord Camden's administration" (104-006) the United Irishmen grew rapidly. Constitutional avenues for reform had been systematically closed, driving thousands into the revolutionary underground.

> The cruelty and tyranny of Lord Camden's chief advisers, and the pitiable condition of the country, were widely condemned even by those who supported the government. (102-157)

The insurrectionary movement provided both "a pretext and a means for effecting a legislative union between the two countries" (102-005). Camden's policies, whether by design or consequence, created the conditions that justified abolishing Ireland's Parliament.

At an earlier moment, Camden had been among those presented with loyal addresses of a quite different character. McGee notes that "significant addresses presented to Lord Camden" (095-115) showed that at times he received expressions of support from elements of Irish society. But his administration's direction alienated even moderate opinion.

## The Crisis of 1798

By spring 1798, Camden's policies had brought Ireland to the brink. The arrest of the Leinster directory in March temporarily disrupted the United Irishmen. But the insurrection broke out in May nonetheless.

Camden's hardline approach had demonstrably failed. He was replaced by Lord Cornwallis in June 1798. The new viceroy immediately adopted a contrasting policy of clemency and reconciliation.

## Later Career

After leaving Ireland, Camden served as Lord President of the Council and held other cabinet positions. He was raised in the peerage to Earl Camden and later Marquess Camden, continuing a long career in British public life.

## Legacy

Lord Camden's Irish administration remains a textbook example of how repressive policies radicalize populations. His three-year term transformed the movement for emancipation into a revolutionary conspiracy, leading directly to the Rebellion of 1798.

The contrast between Fitzwilliam's brief reformist viceroyalty and Camden's coercive administration illustrated the choice that confronted British policy. Camden's name became permanently associated with the failed repression that preceded the Act of Union.
