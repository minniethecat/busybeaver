## Overview

Con O'Neil (c. 1460–c. 1548) was Lord of Tyrone and Prince of the O'Neill dynasty during a transformative period in Irish history. A brother-in-law of the powerful Earl of Kildare, Con O'Neil navigated the shifting alliances between Gaelic lords and the Tudor crown.

He became one of the first Irish chiefs to accept an English title under Henry VIII's policy of surrender and regrant — only to be imprisoned by his own son and die of grief during his confinement.

## Lord of Tyrone

Con O'Neil succeeded to the lordship of Tyrone in 1483, ruling from the O'Neill strongholds at Dungannon and extending his authority over the eastern shores of Lough Neagh.

In the year 1480 — or shortly before his accession — Con O'Neil married his cousin-germain, Lady Alice Fitzgerald, daughter of the Earl of Kildare. This marriage forged a dynastic alliance between the O'Neills of Ulster and the Geraldines, the most powerful Anglo-Irish family of the Pale. (048-047)

> In the year 1480, Con O'Neil, lord of Tyrone, married his cousin-germain, Lady Alice Fitzgerald, daughter of the Earl of Kildare. (048-047)

The O'Neill connection with Kildare was both political and personal. In 1491, Con O'Neil and O'Donnell mutually submitted their disputes to the Earl of Kildare's decision at his Castle of Maynooth, demonstrating the influence the Geraldines exercised over the northern Gaelic lords. (045-243)

## Naturalization and the Pale

One of the most remarkable episodes of Con O'Neil's career was his inclusion in a legislative effort to extend legal rights to the Gaelic Irish. The Parliament of the Pale, at the urging of the Earl of Kildare, passed acts extending to Con O'Neil, Prince of Tyrone, and brother-in-law of Kildare, the rights of a naturalized subject within the Pale. (045-102)

This was an exceptional measure. At a time when many statutes prohibited intercourse with the mere Irish, Con O'Neil's personal connection to Kildare enabled him to bridge the legal and cultural divide between Gaelic Ulster and the Anglo-Irish administration. (045-101)

## Surrender and Regrant

The reign of Henry VIII brought a new policy to Ireland — surrender and regrant — by which Gaelic chiefs would surrender their lands and receive them back with an English title. In August 1541, Con O'Neil, lord of Tyrone, gave in his adhesion to this policy alongside other northern lords including Manus O'Donnell and Murrogh O'Brien. (051-008)

Under the Act of Election, Con O'Neil was created Earl of Tyrone, becoming the first of his ancient line to accept an English peerage. The Earldom of Tyrone was originally conferred upon him and was intended to pass to his legitimate heirs according to English primogeniture rather than Gaelic tanistry. (057-171)

## Imprisonment and Death

The surrender and regrant policy provoked fierce opposition from within the Gaelic lordships. Con O'Neil's acceptance of an English title was seen by many of his clansmen as a betrayal of Gaelic tradition and independence.

> Con O'Neil, the first of his race who had accepted an English title, was imprisoned by his son, John the Proud, and died of grief during his confinement. (051-128)

His son John (known as John the Proud) led the opposition, rejecting the English succession system that would have passed the earldom to Con's legitimate heir — the younger son Matthew — rather than to John himself by Gaelic custom. The family conflict that resulted would culminate generations later in the Nine Years' War under Con's grandson, Hugh O'Neill. (051-129) (052-195)

## Legacy

Con O'Neil's career encapsulates the dilemmas faced by the Gaelic aristocracy during the Tudor reconquest of Ireland. His decision to accept English law and titles brought immediate benefits but set in motion a dynastic conflict that would culminate in the destruction of the O'Neill lordship.

The Earldom of Tyrone, which he was the first to hold, became a contested title — fought over by his descendants and ultimately forfeited to the Crown after the Nine Years' War and the Flight of the Earls.
