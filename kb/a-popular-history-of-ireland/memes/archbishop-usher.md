**James Ussher** (1581–1656) was [[Archbishop of Armagh]] and [[Primate of All Ireland]], one of the most influential scholars of the seventeenth century. He was among the first three students admitted to [[Trinity College Dublin]] upon its foundation in 1593. (063-041)

A prolific writer in Latin and a rigorous ecclesiastical historian, Ussher is best remembered for his biblical chronology dating Creation to 4004 BC, and for the honour of being buried in [[Westminster Abbey]] at the command of [[Oliver Cromwell]]. (074-143) His Calvinistic teachings shaped the doctrinal character of the [[Church of Ireland]] for generations. (075-146)

## Early Life and Education

James Ussher was born in Dublin in 1581 into a prominent Protestant family. His uncle was [[Richard Stanihurst]], the scholar and historian who converted to Catholicism and died as chaplain to the Archdukes in Brussels. (063-184)

Ussher's scholarly promise was evident from an early age. When [[Trinity College Dublin]] received its royal charter in January 1593, he was among the very first three students to enter the new university. (063-041) The first Provost, [[Archbishop Loftus]], oversaw the fledgling institution that would produce Ireland's most celebrated Protestant divine. (063-040)

## Rise to Prominence as Preacher

While still a young man, Ussher gained renown as a preacher of intense conviction. In 1601, aged only 21, he mounted the pulpit of [[Christ Church Cathedral]] in Dublin to denounce the conditional toleration extended to Catholics by the Lord Deputy [[Charles Blount, Lord Mountjoy|Mountjoy]]. (063-064) He condemned the policy in forceful terms:

> [063-065] an unhallowed compromise with antichrist.

Ussher preached his most famous early sermon from the text of the forty days in Ezekiel. His rhetorical warning was later treated by his followers as fulfilled prophecy after the outbreak of the northern insurrection in 1641. (063-067) (063-072) The sermon declared:

> [063-069] "From this year," cried the youthful zealot, "will I reckon the sin of Ireland, that those whom you now embrace shall be your ruin, and you shall bear their iniquity."

During this period Ussher also engaged in religious controversy. When the Jesuit [[Henry Fitzsimons]] was imprisoned in Dublin Castle, Ussher visited him and commenced a theological dispute which was never concluded. (063-080)

## Bishop of Meath and Archbishop of Armagh

Ussher's ecclesiastical ascent was steady. As [[Bishop of Meath]], he preached before the Lord Deputy, Viscount Falkland, from the text "He beareth not the sword in vain." (065-059) His allusions to the Catholic body were so violent that he was compelled to issue a public apology. (065-059)

Nevertheless, Ussher remained firmly aligned with the extreme Protestants. He joined the principal bishops and leading settlers, including [[Richard Boyle, 1st Earl of Cork|Boyle]], [[Arthur Chichester, 1st Baron Chichester|Chichester]], and [[William Parsons|Parsons]], in signing a declaration that any toleration of Catholic worship was "a grievous sin." (065-173)

By the 1640s, Ussher had been elevated to [[Archbishop of Armagh]] and [[Primate of All Ireland]]. During the [[Confederate War]], when both Catholic and Protestant deputations travelled to King [[Charles I]] at Oxford in 1644, Ussher served as the advocate for the Irish Protestant interest. (070-066) McGee describes him at this juncture as:

> [070-067] the most puritanical of his prelates,

whose presence and countenance were essential to the King's policy of balancing confessional factions. (070-067) (070-068)

## Scholarship and Chronology

Ussher's enduring fame rests as much on his scholarship as on his ecclesiastical office. He wrote exclusively in Latin, as did nearly all Irish authors of his era. (063-192) His great tomes stand alongside those of [[Luke Wadding]], [[John Colgan]], [[Philip O'Sullivan Beare|O'Sullivan]], and [[Peter White]] as monuments of seventeenth-century Irish learning. (078-013)

Though he differed in religion from the other scholars of his generation, McGee acknowledges his pre-eminence:

> [078-019] [078-022] Though Usher differed in religion, and in his theory of the early connection of the Irish with the Roman Church, from all the rest, yet he stands pre-eminent among them for labour and research.

His most celebrated work, the _Annales Veteris Testamenti_, established the biblical chronology for which he is best known, dating the creation of the world to 4004 BC. He also wrote extensively on the early history of the Irish church, seeking to demonstrate its primitive independence from Rome.

His learning earned him recognition across Europe: King James made Usher an archbishop, and he was called upon to instruct the [[Westminster Assembly]] of divines. (078-025) Earlier scholars from Archbishop Usher to [[Thomas Moore]] praised his deep engagement with the early Irish manuscripts. (008-232)

McGee ranks Ussher, along with [[Sir James Ware|Ware]] and [[Roger Boyle, 1st Earl of Orrery|Orrery]], as the only graduates of Trinity College in the entire seventeenth century to achieve "national, not to say, European reputation." (078-155)

## Role in the Confederate Period

Throughout the [[Confederate War]] (1641–1653), Ussher remained a key figure in the Protestant interest. His advocacy at the Oxford court in 1644 helped ensure that King [[Charles I]] resisted Catholic demands for legal recognition while also refusing to crush them utterly. (070-066) (070-068)

Ussher's moral authority made this delicate balancing act possible. Though his sympathies lay firmly with the Puritan cause, his stature as a scholar commanded respect across confessional lines. His [[Calvinism|Calvinistic teachings]] left a lasting imprint on the Irish established church, a tincture deepened later by the Cromwellian settlement. (075-146)

## Death and Legacy

Ussher died in 1656 at the age of 75. In a striking gesture of respect across political and religious divides, [[Oliver Cromwell]] ordered that he be interred in [[Westminster Abbey]]. (074-143)

This honour, granted to the Primate of the Irish church that Cromwell's armies had fought to subdue, testifies to the extraordinary prestige Ussher had attained as a man of learning. His biblical chronology remained printed in English Bibles well into the nineteenth century, and his historical method influenced both Protestant and Catholic antiquarians of later generations.
