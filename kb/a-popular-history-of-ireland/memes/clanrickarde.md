## Overview

The Earls of Clanrickarde were among the most powerful of the Anglo-Irish noble houses, descended from the de Burgo (Burke) family that had established itself in Connaught in the thirteenth century. The title was created in 1543 when Ulick "McWilliam" Burke surrendered his Gaelic title and received an earldom from [[Henry VIII|Henry VIII]]. For the next two centuries, the Clanrickardes navigated the turbulent waters between Gaelic tradition, Crown authority, and Catholic loyalty, playing roles in the Tudor conquest, the Confederate Wars, and the Williamite settlement. (051-010) (051-020) (045-256) (054-209)

## Origins and the De Burgo Inheritance

The Burkes of Clanrickarde traced their lineage to William de Burgo, the Anglo-Norman adventurer who received vast grants in Connaught during the thirteenth century. Over time, the family adopted Gaelic customs, intermarried with Irish families, and produced cadet branches including the MacWilliam Burkes of Mayo and Clanrickarde. By the fifteenth century, Ulick III, Lord of Clanrickarde, had established himself as one of the most formidable magnates in the west of Ireland. (045-354) (045-361)

> "It so happened that Clanrickarde's enemies, encouraged by the Lord Deputy, had prepared for a trial of strength." (045-361)

## The Tudor Creation

The pivotal moment in the family's history came in the 1540s when the Tudor policy of "Surrender and Regrant" reached Connaught. Ulick Burke, lord of Clanrickarde, submitted to Henry VIII in 1542 and 1543, surrendering his Gaelic lordship and receiving in return the title Earl of Clanrickarde and Baron of Dunkellin. At the same time, he was granted "all the parsonages and vicarages of Clanrickarde" — a significant endowment that placed the revenues of the dissolved monasteries in the new earl's hands. (051-010) (051-020) (051-049)

> "Ulick Burke, lord of Clanrickarde, surrendered his Gaelic title and received the earldom of Clanrickarde and barony of Dunkellin in 1543." (051-010)

The new Earl, however, found himself caught between the demands of the English administration and his traditional Gaelic allies. During the Geraldine rebellion, the Lord Deputy Sidney chose the new Earl of Clanrickarde as a counterweight in the West to Con O'Neill. (051-128)

## Through the Elizabethan and Stuart Eras

The Clanrickardes navigated the dangerous waters of Elizabethan and early Stuart politics with considerable skill. The family's position in Connaught enabled them to serve as intermediaries between the Crown and the Gaelic Irish. During the Tudor conquest, their territory was repeatedly traversed by English armies. They participated in the campaign led by the Earl of Essex that swept "the vales and plains of Clare, and of Clanrickarde." (061-135)

However, the family's balancing act became increasingly difficult. Strafford's plantation schemes threatened their estates in the 1630s. The new Lord Deputy even detained Clanrickarde himself "who had been detained a prisoner" and threatened the family's traditional holdings. The memory of "Strafford's reign of terror" was still painfully remembered throughout Connaught during the crisis of 1641. (055-271) (068-026) (068-032)

## The Confederate Period

During the Confederate Wars of the 1640s, the Clanrickarde interest was divided. Ulick Burke, the fifth Earl, maintained a position of loyalty to the Crown while his sons "though released from the custody of the Deputy" took part in the Confederate movement. The Earl of Clanrickarde was described as "well intentioned, but controlled wholly by his sons" — a predicament shared by many Anglo-Irish nobles of the period. (055-030) (055-263)

Clanrickarde forces participated in the assembly of Confederate troops that included the O'Neills, O'Donnells, and other Gaelic lords, and Clanrickarde was among the Anglo-Irish lords who formed the coalition that fought the English Parliament. (060-040)

## Legacy

The house of Clanrickarde illustrates the trajectory of the Anglo-Irish nobility: from Norman conquerors to Gaelicised magnates to Crown peers, and ultimately to participants in the long struggle to preserve Catholic and Irish interests within the framework of loyalty to the Stuart monarchy. Their regional power in Connaught endured well into the eighteenth century, and their story is inseparable from the history of that province. (059-306)
