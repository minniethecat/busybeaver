## Overview

Gilbert de Clare was a twelfth-century Norman baron, descended from Richard of Normandy, who held the powerful lordship of Pembroke and the stronghold of Chepstow. (023-082) He served as Governor of Pembroke for nearly forty years and was the father of [[Strongbow|Richard de Clare]], the celebrated Earl of Pembroke who led the first Norman invasion of Ireland in 1169. (023-085) Though Gilbert never set foot in Ireland himself, the power and ambition he bequeathed to his son set in motion the chain of events that brought the Normans across the Irish Sea.

## The Norman Settlement of Wales

Gilbert de Clare rose to prominence during the Norman penetration of Wales under Henry I. The King adopted the device of partitioning Welsh territory among the most enterprising nobles, accepting their homage in advance of possession and authorizing them to maintain troops at their own charges for making good grants of what had never belonged to the Crown. (022-118)

> Gilbert de Clare for Pembroke: the best portions of North Wales were partitioned between the Mortimers, Latimers, De Lacys, Fitz-Alans, and Montgomerys. (022-124)

Among these ambitious lords — Robert Fitz-Aymon for Glamorgan, Bernard Newmarch for Brecknock, Roger de Montgomery for Cardigan — Gilbert de Clare received the lordship of Pembroke, the finest of the South Welsh territories. (022-122) This was the foundation upon which the de Clare family built its fortunes over the succeeding century.

## Governor of Pembroke

For nearly forty years, spanning the reign of Henry I and the stormy reign of King Stephen, Gilbert served as Governor of Pembroke. Like all the great barons of the age, he played his game chiefly to his own advantage, building a formidable power base in the Welsh marches.

> His father, Gilbert de Clare, was descended from Richard of Normandy, and stood no farther removed in degree from that Duke than the reigning Prince. For nearly forty years under Henry I. and during the stormy reign of King Stephen, he had been Governor of Pembroke, and like all the great Barons played his game chiefly to his own advantage. (023-082)

His lineage traced directly to Richard of Normandy, placing him no farther from the ducal house than the reigning prince himself — a proximity to royal blood that marked the de Clares as among the highest ranks of the Anglo-Norman nobility. (023-083)

## Chepstow Castle

Gilbert's castle at Chepstow was one of the strongest fortifications in the west of Britain. (023-087) The power and prestige he accumulated there — together with the extensive lands of Pembroke — constituted an inheritance that excited the apprehensions of the astute and suspicious Henry II.

> His castle at Chepstow was one of the strongest in the west, and the power he bequeathed to his able and ambitious son excited the apprehensions of the astute and suspicious Henry II. (023-087)

## Legacy and the Road to Ireland

The power Gilbert bequeathed to his son Richard proved a double-edged legacy. Henry II, wary of the de Clare strength on the Welsh marches, deliberately withheld favour from the younger de Clare. Fourteen years of the King's reign passed, and Earl Richard had received no great employments, no new grants of land, no personal favours from his sovereign. (023-090)

> Fourteen years of this King's reign had passed away, and Earl Richard had received no great employments, no new grants of land, no personal favours from his Sovereign. He was now a widower, past middle age, condemned to a life of inaction such as no true Norman could long endure. (023-090)

It was this enforced idleness, this royal disdain, that drove Richard de Clare — Gilbert's son, known to history as Strongbow — to seize the opportunity presented by the exiled King [[Dermid McMurrogh]] of Leinster. The Norman adventure in Ireland, which would reshape the island's history for eight centuries, was in large measure the unintended consequence of Henry II's suspicion of the power Gilbert de Clare had built in Pembroke and Chepstow.

The de Clare family's Irish connection continued long after Gilbert's death. His grandson Thomas de Clare received the grant of Thomond, and the de Clares remained a force in Munster affairs until their line was extinguished in battle against [[O'Brien|the O'Briens]] in 1318. (031-016) (031-222)
