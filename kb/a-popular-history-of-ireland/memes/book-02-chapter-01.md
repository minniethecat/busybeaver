CHAPTER I.

[009-001]THE DANISH INVASION.

[009-002]Hugh VI., surnamed Ornie, succeeded to the throne vacant by the death

[009-003]of Donogh I. (A.D. 797), and reigned twenty-two years; Conor II.

[009-004]succeeded (A.D. 819), and reigned fourteen years; Nial III. (called

[009-005]from the place of his death Nial of Callan), reigned thirteen years;

[009-006]Malachy I. succeeded (A.D. 845), and reigned fifteen years; Hugh VII.

[009-007]succeeded and reigned sixteen years (dying A.D. 877); Flan (surnamed

[009-008]Flan of the Shannon) succeeded at the latter date, and reigned for

[009-009]thirty-eight years, far into the tenth century. Of these six kings,

[009-010]whose reigns average twenty years each, we may remark that not one died

[009-011]by violence, if we except perhaps Nial of Callan, drowned in the river

[009-012]of that name in a generous effort to save the life of one of his own

[009-013]servants. Though no former princes had ever encountered dangers equal

[009-014]to these—yet in no previous century was the person of the ruler so

[009-015]religiously respected. If this was evident in one or two instances

[009-016]only, it would be idle to lay much stress upon it; but when we find the

[009-017]same truth holding good of several successive reigns, it is not too

[009-018]much to attribute it to that wide diffusion of Christian morals, which

[009-019]we have pointed out as the characteristic of the two preceding

[009-020]centuries. The kings of this age owed their best protection to the

[009-021]purer ethics which overflowed from Armagh and Bangor and Lismore; and

[009-022]if we find hereafter the regicide habits of former times partially

[009-023]revived, it will only be after the new Paganism—the Paganism of

[009-024]interminable anti-Christian invasions—had recovered the land, and

[009-025]extinguished the beacon lights of the three first Christian centuries.

[009-026]The enemy, who were now to assault the religious and civil institutions

[009-027]of the Irish, must be admitted to possess many great military

[009-028]qualities. They certainly exhibit, in the very highest degree, the

[009-029]first of all military virtues—unconquerable courage. Let us say

[009-030]cheerfully, that history does not present in all its volumes a braver

[009-031]race of men than the Scandinavians of the ninth century. In most

[009-032]respects they closely resembled the Gothic tribes, who, whether

[009-033]starting into historic life on the Euxine or the Danube, or faintly

[009-034]heard of by the Latins from the far off Baltic, filled with constant

[009-035]alarm the Roman statesmen of the fourth century; nor can the invasions

[009-036]of what we may call the maritime Goths be better introduced to the

[009-037]reader than by a rapid sketch of the previous triumphs of their kindred

[009-038]tribes over the Roman Empire.

[009-039]It was in the year of our Lord 378 that these long-dreaded barbarians

[009-040]defeated the Emperor Valens in the plain of Adrianople, and as early as

[009-041]404—twenty-six years after their first victory in Eastern Europe—they

[009-042]had taken and burned great Rome herself. Again and again—in 410, in

[009-043]455, and in 472—they captured and plundered the Imperial City. In the

[009-044]same century they had established themselves in Burgundy, in Spain, and

[009-045]in Northern Africa; in the next, another branch of the Gothic stock

[009-046]twice took Rome; and yet another founded the Lombard Kingdom in

[009-047]Northern Italy. With these Goths thus for a time masters of the Roman

[009-048]Empire, whose genius and temper has entered so deeply into all

[009-049]subsequent civilization, war was considered the only pursuit worthy of

[009-050]men. According to their ideas of human freedom, that sacred principle

[009-051]was supposed to exist only in force and by force; they had not the

[009-052]faintest conception, and at first received with unbounded scorn the

[009-053]Christian doctrine of the unity of the human race, the privileges and

[009-054]duties annexed to Christian baptism, and the sublime ideal of the

[009-055]Christian republic. But they were very far from being so cruel or so

[009-056]faithless as their enemies represented them; they were even better than

[009-057]they cared to represent themselves. And they had amongst them men of

[009-058]the highest capacity and energy, well worthy to be the founders of new

[009-059]nations. Alaric, Attila, and Genseric, were fierce and unmerciful it is

[009-060]true; but their acts are not all written in blood; they had their

[009-061]better moments and higher purposes in the intervals of battle; and the

[009-062]genius for civil government of the Gothic race was in the very

[009-063]beginning demonstrated by such rulers as Theodoric in Italy and Clovis

[009-064]in Gaul. The rear guard of this irresistible barbaric invasion was now

[009-065]about to break in upon Europe by a new route; instead of the long land

[009-066]marches by which they had formerly concentrated from the distant Baltic

[009-067]and from the tributaries of the Danube, on the capital of the Roman

[009-068]empire; instead of the tedious expeditions striking across the

[009-069]Continent, hewing their paths through dense forests, arrested by rapid

[009-070]rivers and difficult mountains, the last northern invaders of Europe

[009-071]had sufficiently advanced in the arts of shipbuilding and navigation to

[009-072]strike boldly into the open sea and commence their new conquests among

[009-073]the Christian islands of the West. The defenders of Roman power and

[009-074]Christian civilization in the fifth and sixth centuries, were arrayed

[009-075]against a warlike but pastoral people encumbered with their women and

[009-076]children; the defenders of the same civilization, in the British

[009-077]Islands in the ninth and tenth centuries, were contending with kindred

[009-078]tribes, who had substituted maritime arts and habits for the pastoral

[009-079]arts and habits of the companions of Attila and Theodoric. The Gothic

[009-080]invasion of Roman territory in the earlier period was, with the single

[009-081]exception of the naval expeditions of Genseric from his new African

[009-082]Kingdom, a continental war; and notwithstanding the partiality of

[009-083]Genseric for his fleet, as an arm of offence and defence, his

[009-084]companions and successors abandoned the ocean as an uncongenial

[009-085]element. The only parallel for the new invasion, of which we are now to

[009-086]speak, is to be found in the history and fortunes of the Saxons of the

[009-087]fifth century, first the allies and afterwards the conquerors of part

[009-088]of Britain. But even their descendants in England had not kept pace,

[009-089]either in the arts of navigation or in thirst for adventure, with their

[009-090]distant relatives, who remained two centuries later among the friths

[009-091]and rocks of Scandinavia.

[009-093]The first appearance of these invaders on the Irish and British coasts

[009-094]occurred in 794. Their first descent on Ireland was at Rathlin island,

[009-095]which may be called the outpost of Erin, towards the north; their

[009-096]second attempt (A.D. 797) was at a point much more likely to arouse

[009-097]attention—at Skerries, off the coast of Meath (now Dublin); in 803, and

[009-098]again in 806, they attacked and plundered the holy Iona; but it was not

[009-099]until a dozen years later they became really formidable. In 818 they

[009-100]landed at Howth; and the same year, and probably the same party, sacked

[009-101]the sacred edifices in the estuary of the Slaney, by them afterwards

[009-102]called Wexford; in 820 they plundered Cork, and in 824—most startling

[009-103]blow of all—they sacked and burned the schools of Bangor. The same year

[009-104]they revisited Iona; and put to death many of its inmates; destroyed

[009-105]Moville; received a severe check in Lecale, near Strangford lough (one

[009-106]of their favourite stations). Another party fared better in a land

[009-107]foray into Ossory, where they defeated those who endeavoured to arrest

[009-108]their progress, and carried off a rich booty. In 830 and 831, their

[009-109]ravages were equally felt in Leinster, in Meath, and in Ulster, and

[009-110]besides many prisoners of princely rank, they plundered the primatial

[009-111]city of Armagh for the first time, in the year 832. The names of their

[009-112]chief captains, at this period, are carefully preserved by those who

[009-113]had so many reasons to remember them; and we now begin to hear of the

[009-114]Ivars, Olafs, and Sitricks, strangely intermingled with the Hughs,

[009-115]Nials, Connors, and Felims, who contended with them in battle or in

[009-116]diplomacy. It was not till the middle of this century (A.D. 837) that

[009-117]they undertook to fortify Dublin, Limerick, and some other harbours

[009-118]which they had seized, to winter in Ireland, and declare their purpose

[009-119]to be the complete conquest of the country.

[009-120]The earliest of these expeditions seem to have been annual visitations;

[009-121]and as the northern winter sets in about October, and the Baltic is

[009-122]seldom navigable before May, the summer was the season of their

[009-123]depredations. Awaiting the breaking up of the ice, the intrepid

[009-124]adventurers assembled annually upon the islands in the Cattegat or on

[009-125]the coast of Norway, awaiting the favourable moment of departure. Here

[009-126]they beguiled their time between the heathen rites they rendered to

[009-127]their gods, their wild bacchanal festivals, and the equipment of their

[009-128]galleys. The largest ship built in Norway, and probably in the north,

[009-129]before the eleventh century, had 34 banks of oars. The largest class of

[009-130]vessel carried from 100 to 120 men. The great fleet which invaded

[009-131]Ireland in 837 counted 120 vessels, which, if of average size for such

[009-132]long voyages, would give a total force of some 6,000 men. As the whole

[009-133]population of Denmark, in the reign of Canute who died in 1035, is

[009-134]estimated at 800,000 souls, we may judge from their fleets how large a

[009-135]portion of the men were engaged in these piratical pursuits. The ships

[009-136]on which they prided themselves so highly were flat-bottomed craft,

[009-137]with little or no keel, the sides of wicker work, covered with strong

[009-138]hides. They were impelled either by sails or oars as the changes of the

[009-139]weather allowed; with favourable winds they often made the voyage in

[009-140]three days. As if to favour their designs, the north and north-west

[009-141]blast blows for a hundred days of the year over the sea they had to

[009-142]traverse. When land was made, in some safe estuary, their galleys were

[009-143]drawn up on shore, a convenient distance beyond highwater mark, where

[009-144]they formed a rude camp, watch-fires were lighted, sentinels set, and

[009-145]the fearless adventurers slept as soundly as if under their own roofs,

[009-146]in their own country. Their revels after victory, or on returning to

[009-147]their homes, were as boisterous as their lives. In food they looked

[009-148]more to quantity than quality, and one of their most determined

[009-149]prejudices against Christianity was that it did not sanction the eating

[009-150]of horse flesh. An exhilarating beer, made from heath, or from the

[009-151]spruce tree, was their principal beverage, and the recital of their own

[009-152]adventures, or the national songs of the Scalds, were their most

[009-153]cherished amusement. Many of the Vikings were themselves Scalds, and

[009-154]excelled, as might be expected, in the composition of war songs.

[009-155]The Pagan belief of this formidable race was in harmony with all their

[009-156]thoughts and habits, and the exact opposite of Christianity. In the

[009-157]beginning of time, according to their tradition, there was neither

[009-158]heaven nor earth, but only universal chaos and a bottomless abyss,

[009-159]where dwelt Surtur in an element of unquenchable fire. The generation

[009-160]of their gods proceeded amid the darkness and void, from the union of

[009-161]heat and moisture, until Odin and the other children of Asa-Thor, or

[009-162]the Earth, slew Ymer, or the Evil One, and created the material

[009-163]universe out of his lifeless remains. These heroic conquerors also

[009-164]collected the sparks of eternal fire flying about in the abyss, and

[009-165]fixed them as stars in the firmament. In addition, they erected in the

[009-166]far East, Asgard, the City of the Gods; on the extreme shore of the

[009-167]ocean stood Utgard, the City of Nor and his giants, and the wars of

[009-168]these two cities, of their gods and giants, fill the first and most

[009-169]obscure ages of the Scandinavian legend. The human race had as yet no

[009-170]existence until Odin created a man and woman, Ask and Embla, out of two

[009-171]pieces of wood (ash and elm), thrown upon the beach by the waves of the

[009-172]sea.

[009-173]Of all the gods of Asgard, Odin was the first in place and power; from

[009-174]his throne he saw everything that happened on the earth; and lest

[009-175]anything should escape his knowledge, two ravens, Spirit and Memory,

[009-176]sat on his shoulders, and whispered in his ears whatever they had seen

[009-177]in their daily excursions round the world. Night was a divinity and the

[009-178]father of Day, who travelled alternately throughout space, with two

[009-179]celebrated steeds called Shining-mane and Frost-mane. Friga was the

[009-180]daughter and wife of Odin; the mother of Thor, the Mars, and of the

[009-181]beautiful Balder, the Apollo, of Asgard. The other gods were of

[009-182]inferior rank to these, and answered to the lesser divinities of Greece

[009-183]and Rome. Niord was the Neptune, and Frega, daughter of Niord, was the

[009-184]Venus of the North. Heimdall, the watchman of Asgard, whose duty it was

[009-185]to prevent the rebellious giants scaling by surprise the walls of the

[009-186]celestial city, dwelt under the end of the rainbow; his vision was so

[009-187]perfect he could discern objects 100 leagues distant, either by night

[009-188]or day, and his ear was so fine he could hear the wool growing on the

[009-189]sheep, and the grass springing in the meadows.

[009-190]The hall of Odin, which had 540 gates, was the abode of heroes who had

[009-191]fought bravest in battle. Here they were fed with the lard of a wild

[009-192]boar, which became whole every night, though devoured every day, and

[009-193]drank endless cups of hydromel, drawn from the udder of an

[009-194]inexhaustible she-goat, and served out to them by the Nymphs, who had

[009-195]counted the slain, in cups which were made of the skulls of their

[009-196]enemies. When they were wearied of such enjoyments, the sprites of the

[009-197]Brave exercised themselves in single combat, hacked each other to

[009-198]pieces on the floor of Valhalla, resumed their former shape, and

[009-199]returned to their lard and their hydromel.

[009-200]Believing firmly in this system—looking forward with undoubting faith

[009-201]to such an eternity—the Scandinavians were zealous to serve their gods

[009-202]according to their creed. Their rude hill altars gave way as they

[009-203]increased in numbers and wealth, to spacious temples at Upsala, Ledra,

[009-204]Tronheim, and other towns and ports. They had three great festivals,

[009-205]one at the beginning of February, in honour of Thor, one in Spring, in

[009-206]honour of Odin, and one in Summer, in honour of the fruitful daughter

[009-207]of Niord. The ordinary sacrifices were animals and birds; but every

[009-208]ninth year there was a great festival at Upsala, at which the kings and

[009-209]nobles were obliged to appear in person, and to make valuable

[009-210]offerings. Wizards and sorcerers, male and female, haunted the temples,

[009-211]and good and ill winds, length of life, and success in war, were

[009-212]spiritual commodities bought and sold. Ninety-nine human victims were

[009-213]offered at the great Upsala festival, and in all emergencies such

[009-214]sacrifices were considered most acceptable to the gods. Captives and

[009-215]slaves were at first selected; but, in many cases, princes did not

[009-216]spare their subjects, nor fathers their own children. The power of a

[009-217]Priesthood, who could always enforce such a system, must have been

[009-218]unbounded and irresistible.

[009-219]The active pursuits of such a population were necessarily maritime. In

[009-220]their short summer, such crops as they planted ripened rapidly, but

[009-221]their chief sustenance was animal food and the fish that abounded in

[009-222]their waters. The artizans in highest repute among them were the

[009-223]shipwrights and smiths. The hammer and anvil were held in the highest

[009-224]honour; and of this class, the armorers held the first place. The kings

[009-225]of the North had no standing armies, but their lieges were summoned to

[009-226]war by an arrow in Pagan times, and a cross after their conversion.

[009-227]Their chief dependence was in infantry, which they formed into

[009-228]wedge-like columns, and so, clashing their shields and singing hymns to

[009-229]Odin, they advanced against their enemies. Different divisions were

[009-230]differently armed; some with a short two-edged sword and a heavy

[009-231]battle-axe; others with the sling, the javelin, and the bow. The shield

[009-232]was long and light, commonly of wood and leather, but for the chiefs,

[009-233]ornamented with brass, with silver, and even with gold. Locking the

[009-234]shields together formed a rampart which it was not easy to break; in

[009-235]bad weather the concave shield seems to have served the purpose of our

[009-236]umbrella; in sea-fights the vanquished often escaped by swimming ashore

[009-237]on their shields. Armour many of them wore; the Berserkers, or

[009-238]champions, were so called from always engaging, _bare_ of defensive

[009-239]armour.

[009-240]Such were the men, the arms, and the creed, against which the Irish of

[009-241]the ninth age, after three centuries of exemption from foreign war,

[009-242]were called upon to combat. A people, one-third of whose youth and

[009-243]manhood had embraced the ecclesiastical state, and all whose tribes now

[009-244]professed the religion of peace, mercy, and forgiveness, were called to

[009-245]wrestle with a race whose religion was one of blood, and whose

[009-246]beatitude was to be in proportion to the slaughter they made while on

[009-247]earth. The Northman hated Christianity as a rival religion, and

[009-248]despised it as an effeminate one. He was the soldier of Odin, the elect

[009-249]of Valhalla; and he felt that the offering most acceptable to his

[009-250]sanguinary gods was the blood of those religionists who denied their

[009-251]existence and execrated their revelation. The points of attack,

[009-252]therefore, were almost invariably the great seats of learning and

[009-253]religion. There, too, was to be found the largest bulk of the portable

[009-254]wealth of the country, in richly adorned altars, jewelled chalices, and

[009-255]shrines of saints. The ecclesiastical map is the map of their campaigns

[009-256]in Ireland. And it is to avenge or save these innumerable sacred

[009-257]places—as countless as the Saints of the last three centuries—that the

[009-258]Christian population have to rouse themselves year after year, hurrying

[009-259]to a hundred points at the same time. To the better and nobler spirits

[009-260]the war becomes a veritable crusade, and many of those slain in

[009-261]single-hearted defence of their altars may well be accounted

[009-262]martyrs—but a war so protracted and so devastating will be found, in

[009-263]the sequel, to foster and strengthen many of the worst vices as well as

[009-264]some of the best virtues of our humanity.

[009-265]The early events are few and ill-known. During the reign of Hugh VI.,

[009-266]who died in 819, their hostile visits were few and far between; his

[009-267]successors, Conor II. and Nial III., were destined to be less fortunate

[009-268]in this respect. During the reign of Conor, Cork, Lismore, Dundalk,

[009-269]Bangor and Armagh, were all surprised, plundered, and abandoned by "the

[009-270]Gentiles," as they are usually called in Irish annals; and with the

[009-271]exception of two skirmishes in which they were worsted on the coasts of

[009-272]Down and Wexford, they seem to have escaped with impunity. At Bangor

[009-273]they shook the bones of the revered founder out of the costly shrine

[009-274]before carrying it off; on their first visit to Kildare they contented

[009-275]themselves with taking the gold and silver ornaments of the tomb of St.

[009-276]Bridget, without desecrating the relics; their main attraction at

[009-277]Armagh was the same, but there the relics seemed to have escaped. When,

[009-278]in 830, the brotherhood of Iona apprehended their return, they carried

[009-279]into Ireland, for greater safety, the relics of St. Columbkill. Hence

[009-280]it came that most of the memorials of SS. Patrick, Bridget, and

[009-281]Columbkill, were afterwards united at Downpatrick.

[009-283]While these deplorable sacrileges, too rapidly executed perhaps to be

[009-284]often either prevented or punished, were taking place, Conor the King

[009-285]had on his hand a war of succession, waged by the ablest of his

[009-286]contemporaries, Felim, King of Munster, who continued during this and

[009-287]the subsequent reign to maintain a species of rival monarchy in

[009-288]Munster. It seems clear enough that the abandonment of Tara, as the

[009-289]seat of authority, greatly aggravated the internal weakness of the

[009-290]Milesian constitution. While over-centralization is to be dreaded as

[009-291]the worst tendency of imperial power, it is certain that the want of a

[009-292]sufficient centralization has proved as fatal, on the other hand, to

[009-293]the independence of many nations. And anarchical usages once admitted,

[009-294]we see from the experience of the German Empire, and the Italian

[009-295]republics, how almost impossible it is to apply a remedy. In the case

[009-296]before us, when the Irish Kings abandoned the old mensal domain and

[009-297]betook themselves to their own patrimony, it was inevitable that their

[009-298]influence and authority over the southern tribes should diminish and

[009-299]disappear. Aileach, in the far North, could never be to them what Tara

[009-300]had been. The charm of conservatism, the halo of ancient glory, could

[009-301]not be transferred. Whenever, therefore, ambitious and able Princes

[009-302]arose in the South, they found the border tribes rife for backing their

[009-303]pretensions against the Northern dynasty. The Bards, too, plied their

[009-304]craft, reviving the memory of former times, when Heber the Fair divided

[009-305]Erin equally with Heremon, and when Eugene More divided it a second

[009-306]time with Con of the Hundred Battles. Felim, the son of Crimthan, the

[009-307]contemporary of Conor II. and Nial III., during the whole term of their

[009-308]rule, was the resolute assertor of these pretensions, and the Bards of

[009-309]his own Province do not hesitate to confer on him the high title of

[009-310]_Ard-Righ_. As a punishment for adhering to the Hy-Nial dynasty, or for

[009-311]some other offence, this Christian king, in rivalry with "the

[009-312]Gentiles," plundered Kildare, Burrow, and Clonmacnoise—the latter

[009-313]perhaps for siding with Connaught in the dispute as to whether the

[009-314]present county of Clare belonged to Connaught or Munster. Twice he met

[009-315]in conference with the monarch at Birr and at Cloncurry—at another time

[009-316]he swept the plain of Meath, and held temporary court in the royal rath

[009-317]of Tara. With all his vices lie united an extraordinary energy, and

[009-318]during his time, no Danish settlement was established on the Southern

[009-319]rivers. Shortly before his decease (A.D. 846) he resigned his crown and

[009-320]retired from the world, devoting the short remainder of his days to

[009-321]penance and mortification. What we know of his ambition and ability

[009-322]makes us regret that he ever appeared upon the scene, or that he had

[009-323]not been born of that dominant family, who alone were accustomed to

[009-324]give kings to the whole country.

[009-325]King Conor died (A.D. 833), and was succeeded by Nial III., surnamed

[009-326]Nial of Callan. The military events of this last reign are so

[009-327]intimately bound up with the more brilliant career of the next

[009-328]ruler—Melaghlin, or Malachy I.—that we must reserve them for the

[009-329]introduction to the next chapter.
