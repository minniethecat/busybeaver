CHAPTER XI.

[062-001]THE CONQUEST OF MUNSTER—DEATH OF ELIZABETH, AND SUBMISSION OF

[062-002]O'NEIL—"THE ARTICLES OF MELLIFONT."

[062-003]The days of Queen Elizabeth were now literally numbered. The death of

[062-004]Essex, the intrigues of the King of Scotland, and the successes of

[062-005]Tyrone, preyed upon her spirits. The Irish chief was seldom out of her

[062-006]mind, and, as she often predicted, she was not to live to receive his

[062-007]submission. She was accustomed to send for her godson, Harrington, who

[062-008]had served in Ireland, to ask him questions concerning Tyrone; the

[062-009]French ambassador considered Tyrone's war one of the causes that

[062-010]totally destroyed her peace of mind in her latter days. She received

[062-011]the news of the victory of Kinsale with pleasure, but, even then, she

[062-012]was not destined to receive the submission of Tyrone.

[062-013]The events of the year, so inauspiciously begun for the Irish arms,

[062-014]continued of the same disastrous character. Castlehaven was surrendered

[062-015]by its Spanish guard, according to Del Aguila's agreement. Baltimore,

[062-016]after a momentary resistance, was also given up, but O'Sullivan, who

[062-017]considered the Spanish capitulation nothing short of treason, threw a

[062-018]body of native troops, probably drawn from Tyrrell's men, into Dunboy,

[062-019]under Captain Richard Mageoghegan, and Taylor, an Englishman, connected

[062-020]by marriage with Tyrrell. Another party of the same troops took

[062-021]possession of Clear Island, but were obliged to abandon it as

[062-022]untenable. The entire strength of the Dunboy garrison amounted to 143

[062-023]men; towards the end of April—the last of the Spaniards having sailed

[062-024]in March—Carew left Cork at the head of 3,000 men to besiege Dunboy.

[062-025]Sir Charles Wilmot moved on the same point from Kerry, with a force of

[062-026]1,000 men, to join Carew. In the pass near Mangerton Wilmot was

[062-027]encountered by Donald O'Sullivan and Tyrrell, at the head of then

[062-028]remaining followers, but forced a passage and united with his superior

[062-029]on the shores of Berehaven. On the 1st of June the English landed on

[062-030]Bear Island, and on the 6th opened their cannonade. They were 4,000

[062-031]men, with every military equipment necessary, against 143. After eleven

[062-032]days' bombardment the place was shattered to pieces; the garrison

[062-033]offered to surrender, if allowed to retain their arms, but their

[062-034]messenger was hanged, and an instant assault ordered. Over fifty of

[062-035]this band of Christian Spartans had fallen in the defence, thirty

[062-036]attempted to escape in boats, or by swimming, but were killed to a man

[062-037]while in the water. The remainder retreated with Mageoghegan, who was

[062-038]severely wounded, to a cellar approached by a narrow stair, where the

[062-039]command was assumed by Taylor. All day the assault had been carried on

[062-040]till night closed upon the scene of carnage. Placing a strong guard on

[062-041]the approach to the crypt, Carew returned to the charge with the

[062-042]returning light. Cannon were first discharged into the narrow chamber

[062-043]which held the last defenders of Dunboy, and then a body of the

[062-044]assailants rushing in, despatched the wounded Mageoghegan with their

[062-045]swords, having found him, candle in hand, dragging himself towards the

[062-046]gunpowder. Taylor and fifty-seven others were led out to execution; of

[062-047]all the heroic band, not a soul escaped alive.

[062-048]The remaining fragments of Dunboy were blown into the air by Carew on

[062-049]the 22nd of June. Dursey Castle, another island fortress of

[062-050]O'Sullivan's, had fallen even earlier; so that no roof remained to the

[062-051]lord of Berehaven. Still he held his men well together in the glens of

[062-052]Kerry, during the months of Summer, but the ill-news from Spain in

[062-053]September threw a gloom over those mountains deeper than was ever cast

[062-054]by equinoctial storm. Tyrrell was obliged to separate from him in the

[062-055]Autumn, probably from the difficulty of providing for so many mouths,

[062-056]and O'Sullivan himself prepared to bid a sad farewell to the land of

[062-057]his inheritance. On the last day of December he left Glengariffe, with

[062-058]400 fighting men, and 600 women, children, and servants, to seek a

[062-059]refuge in the distant north. After a retreat almost unparalleled, the

[062-060]survivors of this exodus succeeded in reaching the friendly roof of

[062-061]O'Ruarc, at Dromahaire, not far from Sligo. Their entire march, from

[062-062]the extreme south to the almost extreme north-west of the island, a

[062-063]distance, as they travelled it, of not less than 200 miles, was one

[062-064]scene of warfare and suffering. They were compelled to kill their

[062-065]horses, on reaching the Shannon, in order to make boats of the hides,

[062-066]to ferry them to the western bank. At Aughrim they were attacked by a

[062-067]superior force under Lord Clanrickarde's brother, and Captain Henry

[062-068]Malby, but they fought with the courage of despair, routed the enemy,

[062-069]slaying Malby, and other officers. Of the ten hundred who left the

[062-070]shores of Glengariffe, but 35 souls reached the Leitrim chieftain's

[062-071]mansion. Among these were the chief himself, with Dermid, father of the

[062-072]historian, who at the date of this march had reached the age of

[062-073]seventy. The conquest of Munster, at least, was now complete. In the

[062-074]ensuing January, Owen McEgan, Bishop of Ross, was slain in the midst of

[062-075]a guerilla party, in the mountains of Carberry, and his chaplain, being

[062-076]taken, was hanged with the other prisoners. The policy of extermination

[062-077]recommended by Carew was zealously carried out by strong detachments

[062-078]under Wilmot, Harvey, and Flower; Mr. Boyle and the other "Undertakers"

[062-079]zealously assisting as volunteers.

[062-081]Mountjoy, after transacting some civil business at Dublin, proceeded in

[062-082]person to the north, while Dowcra, marching out of Derry, pressed

[062-083]O'Neil from the north and north-east. In June, Mountjoy was at

[062-084]Charlemont, which he placed under the custody of Captain Toby Caufield,

[062-085]the founder of an illustrious title taken from that fort. He advanced

[062-086]on Dungannon, but discovered it from the distance, as Norris had once

[062-087]before done, in flames, kindled by the hand of its straitened

[062-088]proprietor. On Lough Neagh he erected a new fort called Mountjoy, so

[062-089]that his communications on the south now stretched from that great lake

[062-090]round to Omagh, while those of Dowcra, at Augher, Donegal, and Lifford,

[062-091]nearly completed the circle. Almost the only outlet from this chain of

[062-092]posts was into the mountains of O'Cane's country, the north-east angle

[062-093]of the present county of Derry. The extensive tract so enclosed and

[062-094]guarded had still some natural advantages for carrying on a defensive

[062-095]war. The primitive woods were standing in masses at no great distance

[062-096]from each other; the nearly parallel vales of Faughan, Moyala, and the

[062-097]river Roe, with the intermediate leagues of moor and mountain, were

[062-098]favourable to the movements of native forces familiar with every ford

[062-099]and footpath. There was also, while this central tract was held, a

[062-100]possibility of communication with other unbroken tribes, such as those

[062-101]of Clandeboy and the Antrim glens on the east, and Breffni O'Ruarc on

[062-102]the west. Never did the genius of Hugh O'Neil shine out brighter than

[062-103]in these last defensive operations. In July, Mountjoy writes

[062-104]apologetically to the Council, that "notwithstanding her Majesty's

[062-105]great forces, O'Neil doth still live." He bitterly complains of his

[062-106]consummate caution, his "pestilent judgment to spread and to nourish

[062-107]his own infection," and of the reverence entertained for his person by

[062-108]the native population. Early in August, Mountjoy had arranged what he

[062-109]hoped might prove the finishing stroke in the struggle. Dowcra from

[062-110]Derry, Chichester from Carrickfergus, Danvers from Armagh, and all who

[062-111]could be spared from Mountjoy, Charlemont, and Mount Norris, were

[062-112]gathered under his command, to the number of 8,000 men, for a foray

[062-113]into the interior of Tyrone. Inisloghlin, on the borders of Down and

[062-114]Antrim, which contained a great quantity of valuables, belonging to

[062-115]O'Neil, was captured. Magherlowney and Tulloghoge were next taken. At

[062-116]the latter place stood the ancient stone chair on which the O'Neils

[062-117]were inaugurated time out of mind; it was now broken into atoms by

[062-118]Mountjoy's orders. But the most effective warfare was made on the

[062-119]growing crops. The 8,000 men spread themselves over the fertile fields

[062-120]along the valleys of the Bann and the Roe, destroying the standing

[062-121]grain with fire, where it would burn, or with the _praca_, a peculiar

[062-122]kind of harrow, tearing it up by the roots. The horsemen trampled crops

[062-123]into the earth which had generously nourished them; the infantry shore

[062-124]them down with their sabres, and the sword, though in a very different

[062-125]sense from that of Holy Scripture, was, indeed, converted into a

[062-126]sickle. The harvest month never shone upon such fields in any Christian

[062-127]land. In September, Mountjoy reported to Cecil, "that between

[062-128]Tulloghoge and Toome there lay unburied a thousand dead," and that

[062-129]since his arrival on the Blackwater—a period of a couple of

[062-130]months—"there were about 3,000 starved in Tyrone." In O'Cane's country,

[062-131]the misery of his clansmen drove the chief to surrender to Dowcra, and

[062-132]the news of Hugh Roe's death having reached Donegal, his brother

[062-133]repaired to Athlone, and made his submission to Mountjoy, early in

[062-134]December. O'Neil, unable to maintain himself on the river, Roe, retired

[062-135]with 600 foot and 60 horse, to Glencancean, near Lough Neagh, the most

[062-136]secure of his fastnesses. His brother Cormac McMahon, and Art O'Neil,

[062-137]of Clandeboy, shared with him the wintry hardships of that last asylum,

[062-138]while Tyrone, Clandeboy, and Monaghan, were given up to horrors,

[062-139]surpassing any that had been known or dreamt of in former wars.

[062-140]Moryson, secretary to Mountjoy, in his account of this campaign,

[062-141]observes, "that no spectacle was more frequent in the ditches of towns,

[062-142]and especially in wasted countries, than to see multitudes of these

[062-143]poor people dead, with their mouths all coloured green, by eating

[062-144]nettles, docks, and all things they could rend above ground."

[062-145]The new year, opening without hope, it began to be rumoured that O'Neil

[062-146]was disposed to surrender on honourable terms. Mountjoy and the English

[062-147]Council long urged the aged Queen to grant such terms, but without

[062-148]effect. Her pride as a sovereign had been too deeply wounded by the

[062-149]revolted Earl to allow her easily to forgive or forget his offences.

[062-150]Her advisers urged that Spain had followed her own course towards the

[062-151]Netherlands, in Ireland; that the war consumed three-fourths of her

[062-152]annual revenue, and had obliged her to keep up an Irish army of 20,000

[062-153]men for several years past. At length she yielded her reluctant

[062-154]consent, and Mountjoy was authorized to treat with the arch-rebel upon

[062-155]honourable terms. The agents employed by the Lord Deputy in this

[062-156]negotiation were Sir William Godolphin and Sir Garrett Moore, of

[062-157]Mellifont, ancestor of the Marquis of Drogheda—the latter, a warm

[062-158]personal friend, though no partizan of O'Neil's. They found him in his

[062-159]retreat near Lough Neagh early in March, and obtained his promise to

[062-160]give the Deputy an early meeting at Mellifont. Elizabeth's serious

[062-161]illness, concealed from O'Neil, though well known to Mountjoy, hastened

[062-162]the negotiations. On the 27th of March he had intelligence of her

[062-163]decease at London on the 24th, but carefully concealed it till the 5th

[062-164]of April following. On the 31st of March, he received Tyrone's

[062-165]submission at Moore's residence, the ancient Cistercian Abbey, and not

[062-166]until a week later did O'Neil learn that he had made his peace with a

[062-167]dead sovereign.

[062-168]The honourable terms on which this memorable religious war was

[062-169]concluded were these: O'Neil abjured all foreign allegiance, especially

[062-170]that of the King of Spain; renounced the title of O'Neil; agreed to

[062-171]give up his correspondence with the Spaniards, and to recall his son,

[062-172]Henry, who was a page at the Spanish Court, and to live in peace with

[062-173]the sons of John the Proud. Mountjoy granted him an amnesty for himself

[062-174]and his allies; agreed that he should be restored to his estates as he

[062-175]had held them before the war, and that the Catholics should have the

[062-176]free exercise of their religion. That the restoration of his ordinary

[062-177]chieftain rights, which did not conflict with the royal prerogative,

[062-178]was also included, we have the best possible evidence: Sir Henry Dowcra

[062-179]having complained to Lord Mountjoy that O'Neil quartered men on O'Cane,

[062-180]who had surrendered to himself, Mountjoy made answer—"My Lord of Tyrone

[062-181]is taken in with promise to be restored, as well to all his lands as to

[062-182]his honour and dignity, and O'Cane's country is his, and must be

[062-183]obedient to his commands." That the article concerning religion was

[062-184]understood by the Catholics to concede full freedom of worship, is

[062-185]evident from subsequent events. In Dublin, sixteen of the principal

[062-186]citizens suffered fine and imprisonment for refusing to comply with the

[062-187]act of uniformity; in Kilkenny the Catholics took possession of the

[062-188]Black Abbey, which had been converted into a lay fee; in Waterford they

[062-189]did the same by St. Patrick's Church, where a Dominican preacher was

[062-190]reported to have said, among other imprudent things, that "Jesabel was

[062-191]dead"—alluding to the late Queen. In Cork, Limerick, and Cashel, the

[062-192]cross was carried publicly in procession, the old Churches restored to

[062-193]their ancient rites, and enthusiastic proclamation made of the public

[062-194]restoration of religion. These events having obliged the Lord Deputy to

[062-195]make a progress through the towns and cities, he was met at Waterford

[062-196]by a vast procession, headed by religious in the habits of their order,

[062-197]who boldly declared to him "that the citizens of Waterford could not,

[062-198]in conscience, obey any prince that persecuted the Catholic religion."

[062-199]When such was the spirit of the town populations, we are not surprised

[062-200]to learn that, in the rural districts, almost exclusively Catholic, the

[062-201]people entered upon the use of many of their old Churches, and repaired

[062-202]several Abbeys—among the number, Buttevant, Kilcrea, and Timoleague in

[062-203]Cork; Quin Abbey in Clare; Kilconnell in Galway; Rosnariell in Mayo,

[062-204]and Multifarnham in West-Meath. So confident were they that the days of

[062-205]persecution were past, that King James prefaces his proclamation of

[062-206]July, 1605, with the statement—"Whereas we have been informed that our

[062-207]subjects in the kingdom of Ireland, since the death of our beloved

[062-208]sister, have been deceived by a false rumour, to wit, that we would

[062-209]allow them liberty of conscience," and so forth. How cruelly they were

[062-210]then undeceived belongs to the history of the next reign; here we need

[062-211]only remark that the Articles of Limerick were not more shamefully

[062-212]violated by the statute 6th and 7th, William III., than the Articles of

[062-213]Mellifont were violated by this Proclamation of the third year of James

[062-214]I.
