## Overview

Henry VIII (1491–1547) was King of England from 1509 and, from 1542, the first English monarch to style himself King of Ireland rather than merely Lord of Ireland. His reign transformed the relationship between the English Crown and Ireland through the twin instruments of the [[reformation|Reformation]] and the policy of "surrender and regrant." (041-061) (051-013)

For Ireland, Henry's reign represented an unprecedented assertion of Crown authority over the Gaelic and Anglo-Irish lords. His break with Rome in the 1530s introduced religious division into Irish politics for the first time, a schism that would shape Irish history for the next four centuries. (051-095)

## Biography

### Early Policy Toward Ireland

In the early years of his reign, Henry largely continued the policy of his father Henry VII, relying on the [[Earl of Kildare]] to govern Ireland on the Crown's behalf. The Geraldine family, under the 8th and 9th [[earl-of-kildare|Earls of Kildare]], had become virtually independent rulers of Ireland, wielding power that rivaled the Crown's own. (041-061)

Henry's chief minister, Cardinal Wolsey, received detailed suggestions for extending royal power in Ireland, but it was not until the rebellion of "Silken Thomas" FitzGerald, the 10th [[earl-of-kildare|Earl of Kildare]], in 1534 that Henry was forced to intervene directly. (041-061) (046-184)

### The Kildare Rebellion and Its Aftermath

The rebellion of Silken Thomas led to the destruction of the [[earl-of-kildare|Kildare Geraldines]]' power. Maynooth Castle, the Geraldine stronghold, was taken and its garrison executed. The [[earl-of-kildare|Earl of Kildare]] was summoned to London to answer charges, and the family's hegemony in Ireland was broken. (046-184) (049-004)

With the fall of Kildare, Henry was faced with the challenge of governing Ireland directly. His solution was twofold: to extend the [[reformation|Reformation]] to Ireland, and to bring the Irish lords under Crown authority through negotiation rather than conquest. (050-044)

### The King of Ireland

In 1541, the [[irish-parliament|Irish Parliament]] passed an act declaring Henry King of Ireland, replacing the old title of Lord of Ireland which dated from the time of [[Henry II]]. This was far from unanimous—many questioned whether Henry would be classed by Irish historians as a legitimate king—but it marked a formal change in the constitutional relationship. (051-013) (051-242)

Henry had summoned the Irish chieftains to London, where he suggested a policy of "surrender and regrant": the chiefs would surrender their lands to the Crown and receive them back as English-style titles. Some accepted, taking refuge at the English Court in 1531; others resisted. (050-044) (050-139)

### The Reformation in Ireland

The religious changes of Henry's reign were neither few nor weak in their impact on Ireland. Henry VIII nominated George Dowdal, a native of Louth, as Archbishop of [[armagh|Armagh]], while the Pope appointed Robert Waucop to the same dignity. The resulting schism divided the Irish Church and planted the seeds of religious conflict that would dominate the Tudor era. (051-175) (051-095)

The dissolution of the monasteries under Henry, while less thorough in Ireland than in England, nevertheless transferred substantial Church property to lay hands and weakened the institutional power of the Catholic Church in Ireland. (052-183) (053-079)

### Later Years

In the latter days of Henry VIII, the religious settlement remained incomplete. Two ambassadors were received on behalf of the Catholic powers, and the [[reformation|Reformation]] in Ireland hung in the balance. The King's death in 1547 left the Irish question unresolved, and his successors—[[edward-vi|Edward VI]], Mary I, and [[elizabeth-i|Elizabeth I]]—would each attempt their own solutions to the Tudor conquest of Ireland. (052-183) (052-196)

The Tudors subsequent to Henry VIII showed plainly how the recent origin of the Royal Supremacy created ongoing constitutional and religious tensions in Ireland. The struggle between Crown authority and Irish autonomy that Henry had attempted to resolve through reform would instead deepen into religious war. (051-294)

## Legacy

Henry VIII's reign fundamentally altered the Irish political landscape. By breaking with Rome and declaring himself King of Ireland, he transformed what had been a feudal lordship into a kingdom and set the terms of political and religious conflict for the next century. His successors—particularly [[James I]] and [[Oliver Cromwell]]—would complete the process of English domination in Ireland that Henry had dramatically advanced. (055-061) (064-344)

The principle established under Henry—that the Crown, not the Pope, was the supreme authority in Ireland—became the foundation of the [[protestant-ascendancy|Protestant Ascendancy]] and the source of centuries of conflict between the Catholic majority and the Protestant state. Later reforms, including acts repealing statutes of Elizabeth and Henry VIII, would attempt to undo this legacy, but its effects proved enduring. (064-344)
