## Overview

Sir Richard Nagle (c. 1636–1699) was an Irish Jacobite lawyer, politician, and author who served as Attorney-General for Ireland and later as Secretary of State under King James II. He is best remembered as the author of the celebrated "Coventry Letter" (1687), a comprehensive legal and historical defence of Irish Catholic claims to their ancestral estates. Nagle was one of the most able legal minds in the Jacobite administration and played a central role in the Irish parliament of 1689 and the subsequent Williamite War.

## Early Career and the "Coventry Letter"

Richard Nagle was born into a Catholic family of Old English descent in County Cork. He studied law and was called to the bar, quickly gaining a reputation as an able advocate. His most famous contribution to Irish political literature was the "Coventry Letter," so named because it was addressed to a correspondent in Coventry, England, and designed for circulation among English Catholics and sympathisers.

McGee records "Sir Richard Nagle, author of the famous 'Coventry Letter'" (076-074) alongside Sir Nicholas Plunkett and Nugent (afterwards Lord Riverston) as among the ablest advocates for the Catholic interest in the Restoration period. The letter set forth the historical basis for Irish Catholic land claims, arguing that the confiscations of the Cromwellian period were unjust and should be reversed upon the restoration of a Catholic monarch.

> they had the services of Sir Nicholas Plunkett, an old confederate, of Sir Richard Nagle, author of the famous "Coventry Letter," of Nugent, afterwards Lord Riverston, and other able men. (076-073–076-075)

## Under James II

The accession of the Catholic James II in 1685 transformed Nagle's prospects. He was appointed Attorney-General for Ireland and became one of the King's most trusted Irish advisers. In the Irish parliament summoned by James in 1689 — the so-called "Patriot Parliament" — Nagle was a leading figure.

Following James's flight to Ireland after the Glorious Revolution, Nagle served in the Jacobite administration. After the Earl of Melfort fell from favour, "Sir Richard Nagle, who had succeeded the Earl of Melfort as chief secretary for King James" (083-086) became one of the principal figures in the Jacobite council, alongside the Duke of Berwick (James's illegitimate son) and the Duke of Tyrconnell. However, his influence was constrained: "Berwick, Sir Richard Nagle, who had succeeded the Earl of Melfort as chief secretary for King James, all ranked before him at the board" (083-085–083-086) — a reference to internal rivalries within the Jacobite leadership.

## The Jacobite Council

Nagle's position at the heart of the Jacobite administration placed him among some of the most distinguished Catholic figures of the age. He served alongside "Bishop French, Sir Richard Nagle, Sir Richard Belling, Lord Orrery" (078-142) and other prominent figures. The presence of Orrery in this list — the son of the Cromwellian Broghill — demonstrates the complex cross-currents of Restoration politics.

During the Williamite War (1689–1691), Nagle remained with the Jacobite court and council. He participated in the deliberations that shaped Irish Jacobite strategy, including the controversial decision to hold Limerick against William III's forces. His legal expertise was particularly valuable in the drafting of the terms ultimately agreed at the Treaty of Limerick.

> Berwick, Sir Richard Nagle, who had succeeded the Earl of Melfort as chief secretary for King James, all ranked before him at the board. (083-085–083-087)

## Exile and Death

Following the Jacobite defeat and the Treaty of Limerick (1691), Nagle followed James II into exile in France. Like many of the Irish Jacobite leaders — Patrick Sarsfield, the Earl of Clanrickarde, and others — he ended his days at the exiled Stuart court at Saint-Germain-en-Laye. He died in 1699, having witnessed the failure of the Jacobite cause but having left behind a body of legal argument that would inspire later generations of Irish Catholics seeking redress of their grievances through constitutional means.

## Legacy

Sir Richard Nagle's "Coventry Letter" remained an influential text in Irish Catholic political thought for more than a century. His arguments about the injustice of the land confiscations and the legitimate claims of the ancient Catholic families to their patrimonies were echoed by later advocates for Catholic relief, from the Catholic Committee of the 1760s through to Daniel O'Connell's leadership. As a legal mind, he brought rigour and eloquence to the Jacobite cause, and his career illustrates the brief window of opportunity that opened for Irish Catholics under James II — and the lasting consequences of its closure.
