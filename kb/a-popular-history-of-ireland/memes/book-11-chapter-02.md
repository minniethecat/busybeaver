CHAPTER II.

[089-001]REIGN OF GEORGE II.—GROWTH OF PUBLIC SPIRIT—THE "PATRIOT" PARTY—LORD

[089-002]CHESTERFIELD'S ADMINISTRATION.

[089-003]The accession of King George II. in 1727, led to no considerable

[089-004]changes, either in England or Ireland. Sir Robert Walpole continued

[089-005]supreme in the one country, and Primate Boulter in the other. The

[089-006]Jacobites, disheartened by their ill success in 1715, and repelled

[089-007]rather than attracted by the austere character of him they called King

[089-008]James III., made no sign. The new King's first act was to make public

[089-009]the declaration he had addressed to the Privy Council, of his firm

[089-010]resolution to uphold the existing constitution "in church and state."

[089-011]The Catholic population, beginning once more to raise their heads,

[089-012]thought this a suitable occasion to present a humble and loyal address

[089-013]of congratulation to the Lords Justices, in the absence of the Viceroy.

[089-014]Lord Delvin and several of their number accordingly appeared at the

[089-015]Castle, and delivered their address, which they begged might be

[089-016]forwarded to the foot of the throne. No notice whatever was taken of

[089-017]this document, either at Dublin or London, nor were the class who

[089-018]signed it permitted by law to "testify their allegiance" to the

[089-019]sovereign, for fifty years later—down to 1778.

[089-020]The Duke of Dorset, who succeeded Lord Carteret as Viceroy in 1731,

[089-021]unlike his immediate predecessor, refrained from suggesting additional

[089-022]severities against the Catholics. His first term of office—two

[089-023]years—was almost entirely occupied with the fiercest controversy which

[089-024]had ever waged in Ireland between the Established Church and the

[089-025]Protestant Dissenters. The ground of the dispute was the sacramental

[089-026]test, imposed by law upon the members of both Houses, and all burgesses

[089-027]and councillors of corporate towns. By the operations of this law, when

[089-028]rigidly enforced, Presbyterians and other dissenters were as

[089-029]effectually excluded from political and municipal offices as Catholics

[089-030]themselves. Against this exclusion it was natural that a body so

[089-031]numerous, and possessed of so much property, especially in Ulster,

[089-032]should make a vigorous resistance. Relying on the great share they had

[089-033]in the revolution, they endeavoured, though ineffectually, to obtain

[089-034]under King William the repeal of the Test Act of King Charles II. Under

[089-035]Queen Anne they were equally unsuccessful, as we may still read with

[089-036]interest in the pages of Swift, De Foe, Tennison, Boyse, and King.

[089-037]Swift, especially, brought to the controversy not only the zeal of a

[089-038]churchman, but the prejudices of an Anglo-Irishman, against the

[089-039]new-comers in the north. He upbraids them in 1708, as glad to leave

[089-040]their barren hills of Lochaber for the fruitful vales of Down and

[089-041]Antrim, for their parsimony and their clannishness. He denied to them,

[089-042]with bitter scorn, the title they had assumed of "Brother Protestants,"

[089-043]and as to the Papists, whom they affected to despise, they were, in his

[089-044]opinion, as much superior to the Dissenters, as a lion, though chained

[089-045]and clipped of its claws, is a stronger and nobler animal than an angry

[089-046]cat, at liberty to fly at the throats of true churchmen. The language

[089-047]of the Presbyterian champions was equally bold, denunciatory, and

[089-048]explicit. They broadly intimated, in a memorial to Parliament, that

[089-049]under the operation of the test, they would be unable to take up arms

[089-050]again, as they had done in 1688, for the maintenance of the Protestant

[089-051]succession; a covert menace of insurrection, which Swift and their

[089-052]other opponents did not fail to make the most of. Still farther to

[089-053]embarrass them, Swift got up a paper making out a much stronger case in

[089-054]favour of the Catholics than of "their brethren, the Dissenters," and

[089-055]the controversy closed, for that age, in the complete triumph of the

[089-056]established clergy.

[089-057]This iniquitous deprivation of equal civil rights, accompanied with the

[089-058]onerous burthen of tithes falling heaviest on the cultivators of the

[089-059]soil, produced the first great Irish exodus to the North American

[089-060]colonies. The tithe of agistment or pasturage, lately abolished, had

[089-061]made the tithe of tillage more unjust and unequal. Outraged in their

[089-062]dearest civil and religious rights, thousands of the Scoto-Irish of

[089-063]Ulster, and the Milesian and Anglo-Irish of the other provinces,

[089-064]preferred to encounter the perils of an Atlantic flitting rather than

[089-065]abide under the yoke and lash of such an oligarchy. In the year 1729,

[089-066]five thousand six hundred Irish landed at the single port of

[089-067]Philadelphia; in the next ten years they furnished to the Carolinas and

[089-068]Georgia the majority of their immigrants; before the end of this reign,

[089-069]several thousands of heads of families, all bred and married in

[089-070]Ireland, were rearing up a free posterity along the slopes of the Blue

[089-071]Ridge in Virginia and Maryland, and even as far north as the valleys of

[089-072]the Hudson and the Merrimac. In the ranks of the thirteen United

[089-073]Colonies, the descendants of those Nonconformists were to repeat, for

[089-074]the benefit of George III., the lesson and example their ancestors had

[089-075]taught to James II. at Enniskillen and at Derry.

[089-076]Swift, with all his services to his own order, disliked, and was

[089-077]disliked by them. Of the bishops he has recorded his utter contempt in

[089-078]some of the most cutting couplets that even he ever wrote. Boulter he

[089-079]detested; Narcissus Marsh he despised; with Dr. King of Dublin, Dr.

[089-080]Bolton of Cashel, and Dr. Horte of Tuam, he barely kept up appearances.

[089-081]Except Sterne, Bishop of Clogher, Berkely, Bishop of Cloyne, and

[089-082]Stopford, his successor, he entertained neither friendship nor respect

[089-083]for one of that order. And on their part, the right reverend prelates

[089-084]cordially reciprocated his antipathy. They resisted his being made a

[089-085]member of the Linen Board, a Justice of the Peace, or a Visitor of

[089-086]Trinity College. Had he appeared amongst them in Parliament as their

[089-087]peer, they would have been compelled to accept him as a master, or

[089-088]combine against him as an enemy. No wonder, then, that successive

[089-089]Viceroys shrank from nominating him to any of the mitres which death

[089-090]had emptied; "the original sin of his birth" was aggravated in their

[089-091]eyes by the actual sin of his patriotism. No wonder the sheets of paper

[089-092]that littered his desk, before he sunk into his last sad scene of

[089-093]dotage, were found scribbled all over with his favourite lines—

[089-094]"Better we all were in our graves,

[089-095]Than live in slavery to slaves."

[089-096]But the seeds of manly thought he had so broadly sown, though for a

[089-097]season hidden even from the sight of the sower, were not dead, nor

[089-098]undergoing decay. With something of the prudence of the founder, "the

[089-099]Patriot party," as the opposition to the Castle party began to be

[089-100]called, occupied themselves at first with questions of taxation and

[089-101]expenditure. In 1729, the Castle attempted to make it appear that there

[089-102]was a deficit—that in short "the country owed the government"—the large

[089-103]sum of 274,000 pounds! The Patriots met this claim, by a motion for

[089-104]reducing the cost of all public establishments. This was the chosen

[089-105]ground of both parties, and a more popularly intelligible ground could

[089-106]not be taken. Between retrenchment and extravagance, between high taxes

[089-107]and low, even the least educated of the people could easily decide; and

[089-108]thenceforward for upwards of twenty years, no session was held without

[089-109]a spirited debate on the supplies, and the whole subject of the public

[089-110]expenditure.

[089-111]The Duke of Devonshire, who succeeded the Duke of Dorset as Viceroy in

[089-112]1737, contributed by his private munificence and lavish hospitalities

[089-113]to throw a factitious popularity round his administration. No Dublin

[089-114]tradesman could find it in his heart to vote against the nominee of so

[089-115]liberal a nobleman, and the public opinion of Dublin was as yet the

[089-116]public opinion of Ireland. But the Patriot party, though unable to stem

[089-117]successfully the tide of corruption and seduction thus let loose, held

[089-118]their difficult position in the legislature with great gallantry and

[089-119]ability. New men had arisen during the dotage of Swift, who revered his

[089-120]maxims, and imitated his prudence. Henry Boyle, speaker of the House of

[089-121]Commons, afterwards Earl of Shannon; Anthony Malone—son of the

[089-122]_confrere_ of Sir Toby Butler, and afterwards Chancellor of the

[089-123]Exchequer, Sir Edward O'Brien, member for Clare, and his son, Sir

[089-124]Lucius, member for Ennis, were the pillars of the party. Out of doors,

[089-125]the most active spirit among the Patriots was Charles Lucas, a native

[089-126]of Clare, who, from his apothecary's shop in Dublin, attempted, not

[089-127]without both talents, zeal and energy, to play the part of Swift, at

[089-128]the press and among the people. His public writings, commenced in 1741,

[089-129]brought him at first persecution and exile, but they afterwards

[089-130]conducted him to the representation of the capital, and an honourable

[089-131]niche in his country's history.

[089-133]The great event which may be said to divide into two epochs the reign

[089-134]of George II. was the daring invasion of Scotland in 1745, by "the

[089-135]young Pretender"—Charles Edward. This brave and unfortunate Prince,

[089-136]whose adventures will live for ever in Scottish song and romance, was

[089-137]accompanied from France by Sir Thomas Sheridan, Colonel O'Sullivan, and

[089-138]other Irish refugees, still fondly attached to the house of Stuart. It

[089-139]is not to be supposed that these gentlemen would be without

[089-140]correspondents in Ireland, nor that the state of that country could be

[089-141]a matter of indifference to the astute advisers of King George. In

[089-142]reality, Ireland was almost as much their difficulty as Scotland, and

[089-143]their choice of a Viceroy, at this critical moment, showed at once

[089-144]their estimate of the importance of the position, and the talents of

[089-145]the man.

[089-146]Philip Dormer Stanhope, Earl of Chesterfield, a great name in the world

[089-147]of fashion, in letters, and in diplomacy, is especially memorable to us

[089-148]for his eight months' viceroyalty over Ireland. That office had been

[089-149]long the object of his ambition, and he could hardly have attained it

[089-150]at a time better calculated to draw out his eminent administrative

[089-151]abilities. By temper and conviction opposed to persecution, he connived

[089-152]at Catholic worship under the very walls of the Castle. The sour and

[089-153]jaundiced bigotry of the local oligarchy he encountered with _bon mots_

[089-154]and raillery. The only "dangerous Papist" he had seen in Ireland, he

[089-155]declared to the King on his return, was a celebrated beauty of that

[089-156]religion—Miss Palmer. Relying on the magical effect of doing justice to

[089-157]all classes, and seeing justice done, he was enabled to spare four

[089-158]regiments of troops for the war in Scotland, instead of demanding

[089-159]additions to the Irish garrisons. But whether to diminish the influence

[089-160]which his brilliant administration had created in England, or through

[089-161]the machinations of the oligarchy, still powerful at Dublin, within ten

[089-162]days from the decisive battle of Culloden, he was recalled. The fruits

[089-163]of his policy might be already observed, as he walked on foot, his

[089-164]countess on his arm, to the place of embarkation, amid the acclamations

[089-165]of all ranks and classes of the people, and their affectionate prayers

[089-166]for his speedy return.
