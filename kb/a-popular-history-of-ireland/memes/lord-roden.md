## Overview

Robert Jocelyn, 2nd Earl of Roden (1756–1820), was an Irish peer and military commander prominent during the turbulent period of the Rebellion of 1798 and its aftermath. As colonel of a regiment of dragoons and a steadfast supporter of the Protestant Ascendancy, Lord Roden's forces were repeatedly deployed against insurgent and agrarian movements, making him one of the most visible military figures in the counter-insurgency campaigns of the era.

## The Rebellion of 1798

Lord Roden first came to prominence during the 1798 Rebellion, when his regiment of dragoons was stationed in Dublin and the surrounding counties. In the initial outbreak of the rebellion in County Kildare, a force of insurgents attacked a yeomanry corps near the city. McGee records that "500 insurgents attacked Lord Ely's yeomanry corps with some success, till Lord Roden's dragoons, hastily despatched from the city, compelled them to retreat, with the loss of some prisoners and two men killed" (102-228–102-229). The report by John Beresford noted that the two dead rebels were found "literally cut to pieces" by cavalry sabres — a grim testament to the brutality of counter-insurgency warfare.

The rapid deployment of Roden's dragoons from Dublin reflected the strategic advantage held by government forces.

> 500 insurgents attacked Lord Ely's yeomanry corps with some success, till Lord Roden's dragoons, hastily despatched from the city, compelled them to retreat, with the loss of some prisoners and two men killed. (102-228–102-230)

Throughout the summer of 1798, Roden's men were repeatedly engaged in pursuit of scattered rebel bands in Kildare, Meath, and Wicklow.

## The Emmet Insurrection of 1803

Lord Roden appears again in the historical record during Robert Emmet's insurrection of July 1803. In the confused street fighting that followed Emmet's abortive rising, "one man, maddened by liquor, encountered Lord Roden hand to hand in the retreat, but who, while pinning him against the wall, was shot dead by one of Roden's companions" (103-378). This vignette captures the chaotic, close-quarters violence that characterized the suppression of the rising — a mixture of martial enforcement and summary justice.

> one man, maddened by liquor, encountered Lord Roden hand to hand in the retreat, but who, while pinning him against the wall, was shot dead by one of Roden's companions. (103-378)

Roden's presence on the streets of Dublin during the Emmet rising indicates his continued role as a key figure in the military establishment of the capital. Though the rising itself was small and quickly crushed — Emmet had barely a hundred followers in the streets — the government's response was characteristically severe, with martial law declared and extensive searches conducted across the city and surrounding counties.

## The Military Commission

In the aftermath of the Emmet rising, the government established a military commission to try captured insurgents. McGee records that a "Military Commission composed of Sir James Duff, Lord Roden, Colonels" (104-078) presided over the trials. The commission operated alongside the civil courts and handed down numerous death sentences against those captured in arms or accused of aiding the insurgents. The speed and severity of the military tribunals reflected the government's determination to make a swift example of the rebels and deter any future outbreaks.

## Political Role

As an Irish peer, Lord Roden sat in the Irish House of Lords until its abolition by the Act of Union in 1800. Like most of the Protestant peerage, he supported the Union, seeing it as a necessary measure to secure the Protestant interest against the growing Catholic majority. After the Union, he continued to exercise influence through his military connections and his extensive estates.

The Roden family's political alignment was staunchly conservative and anti-Catholic. In later decades, the 3rd Earl of Roden became notorious for his role in the sectarian violence at Dolly's Brae (1849), but the 2nd Earl's career belongs to the earlier period of the 1798–1803 insurrections and the imposition of the Union.

## Legacy

Lord Roden represents the military arm of the Protestant Ascendancy at its most active — the peer who exchanged the council chamber for the cavalry saddle when rebellion threatened. His dragoons appear at critical moments in both 1798 and 1803, always on the side of the government and always deploying deadly force. The "hand to hand" encounter in the Dublin streets and the "cut to pieces" rebels on the Kildare road are small but telling episodes in the larger story of how the Ascendancy maintained its grip on power through a combination of military force, legal coercion, and political manoeuvre.
