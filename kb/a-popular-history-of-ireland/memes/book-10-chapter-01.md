CHAPTER I.

[076-001]REIGN OF CHARLES II.

[076-002]Hope is dear to the heart of man, and of all her votaries none have

[076-003]been more constant than the Irish. Half a century of the Stuarts had

[076-004]not extinguished their blind partiality for the descendants of the old

[076-005]Scoto-Irish kings. The restoration of that royal house was, therefore,

[076-006]an event which penetrated to the remotest wilds of Connaught, lighting

[076-007]up with cheering expectation the most desolate hovels of the

[076-008]proscribed. To the Puritans settled in Ireland, most of whom, from the

[076-009]mean condition of menial servants, common soldiers and subaltern

[076-010]officers, had become rich proprietors, the same tidings brought

[076-011]apprehension and alarm. But their leaders, the Protestant gentry of an

[076-012]earlier date, wealthy, astute and energetic, uniting all their

[076-013]influence for the common protection, turned this event, which seemed at

[076-014]one time to threaten their ruin, to their advantage and greater

[076-015]security. The chief of these greater leaders was the accomplished Lord

[076-016]Broghill, whom we are to know during this reign under his more famous

[076-017]title of Earl of Orrery.

[076-018]The position of the Irish as compared with the English Puritans, was

[076-019]essentially different in the eyes of Ormond, Clarendon, and the other

[076-020]counsellors of the king. Though the former represented dissent as

[076-021]against the church, they also represented the English as against the

[076-022]Irish interest, in Ireland. As dissenters they were disliked and

[076-023]ridiculed, but as colonists they could not be disturbed. When national

[076-024]antipathy was placed in one scale and religious animosity in the other,

[076-025]the intensely national feeling of England for the Cromwellians, as

[076-026]Englishmen settled in a hostile country, prevailed over every other

[076-027]consideration. In this, as in all other conjunctures, it has been the

[076-028]singular infelicity of the one island to be subjected to a policy

[076-029]directly opposite to that pursued in the other. While in England it was

[076-030]considered wise and just to break down the Puritans as a party—through

[076-031]the court, the pulpit, and the press; to drive the violent into exile,

[076-032]and to win the lukewarm to conformity; in Ireland it was decided to

[076-033]confirm them in their possessions, to leave the government of the

[076-034]kingdom in their hands, and to strengthen their position by the Acts of

[076-035]Settlement and Explanation. These acts were hailed as "the Magna Charta

[076-036]of Irish Protestantism," but so far as the vast majority of the people

[076-037]were concerned, they were as cruelly unjust as the revocation of the

[076-038]Edict of Nantes, or the edicts which banished the Moors and Jews from

[076-039]the Spanish peninsula.

[076-041]The struggle for possession of the soil inaugurated by the

[076-042]confiscations of Elizabeth and James was continued against great odds

[076-043]by the Catholic Irish throughout this reign. Though the royal

[076-044]declaration of Breda, which preceded the restoration, had not mentioned

[076-045]them expressly, they still claimed under it not only the "liberty to

[076-046]tender consciences," but that "just satisfaction" to those unfairly

[076-047]deprived of their estates, promised in that declaration. Accordingly,

[076-048]several of the old gentry returned from Connaught, or places abroad,

[076-049]took possession of their old homes, or made their way at once to Dublin

[076-050]or London, to urge their claims to their former estates. To their

[076-051]dismay, they found in Dublin, Coote and Broghill established as Lords

[076-052]Justices, and the new Parliament—the first that sat for twenty

[076-053]years—composed of an overwhelming majority of Undertakers, adventurers,

[076-054]and Puritan representatives of boroughs, from which all the Catholic

[076-055]electors had been long excluded. The Protestant interest, or

[076-056]"ascendancy party," as it now began to be commonly called, counted in

[076-057]the Commons 198 members to 64 Catholics; in the House of Lords, 72

[076-058]Protestant to 21 Catholic peers. The former elected Sir Audley Mervyn

[076-059]their Speaker, and the able but curiously intricate and quaint

[076-060]discourses of the ancient colleague of Kelly and Darcy in the assertion

[076-061]of Irish legislative independence, shows how different was the spirit

[076-062]of Irish Protestantism in 1661 as compared with 1641. The Lords chose

[076-063]Bramhall, the long-exiled Bishop of Derry, now Archbishop of Armagh, as

[076-064]their Speaker, and attempted to compel their members "to take the

[076-065]sacrament" according to the Anglican ritual. The majority of both

[076-066]Houses, to secure the good-will of Ormond, voted him the sum of 30,000

[076-067]pounds, and then proceeded to consider "the Bill of Settlement," in

[076-068]relation to landed property. The Catholic bar, which had been

[076-069]apparently restored to its freedom, presented a striking array of

[076-070]talent, from which their co-religionists selected those by whom they

[076-071]desired to be heard at the bar of the House. The venerable Darcy and

[076-072]the accomplished Belling were no longer their oracles of the law; but

[076-073]they had the services of Sir Nicholas Plunkett, an old confederate, of

[076-074]Sir Richard Nagle, author of the famous "Coventry Letter," of Nugent,

[076-075]afterwards Lord Riverston, and other able men. In the House of Lords

[076-076]they had an intrepid ally in the Earl of Kildare, and in England an

[076-077]agent equally intrepid, in Colonel Richard Talbot, afterwards Earl of

[076-078]Tyrconnell. The diplomatic and parliamentary struggle between the two

[076-079]interests, the disinherited and the new proprietory, was too

[076-080]protracted, and the details are too involved for elucidation in every

[076-081]part; but the result tells its own story. In 1675—in the fifteenth year

[076-082]of the restoration—the new settlers possessed above 4,500,000 acres, to

[076-083]about 2,250,000 still retained by the old owners. These relative

[076-084]proportions were exactly the reverse of those existing before the

[076-085]Cromwellian settlement; a single generation had seen this great

[076-086]revolution accomplished in landed property.

[076-087]The Irish Parliament having sent over to England the heads of their

[076-088]bill, according to the constitutional rule established by Poyning's

[076-089]Act, the Irish Catholics sent over Sir Nicholas Plunkett to obtain

[076-090]modifications of its provisions. But Plunkett was met in England with

[076-091]such an outcry from the mob and the press as to the alleged atrocities

[076-092]of the Confederate war, and his own former negotiations on the

[076-093]continent, that he was unable to effect anything; while Colonel Talbot,

[076-094]for his too warm expostulations with Ormond, was sent to the Tower. An

[076-095]order of Council, forbidding Plunkett the presence, and declaring that

[076-096]"no petition or further address be made from the Roman Catholics of

[076-097]Ireland, as to the Bill of Settlement," closed the controversy, and the

[076-098]Act soon after received the royal assent.

[076-099]Under this act, a court was established at Dublin, to try the claims of

[076-100]"nocent" and "innocent." Notwithstanding every influence which could be

[076-101]brought to bear on them, the judges, who were Englishmen, declared in

[076-102]their first session, one hundred and sixty-eight innocent to nineteen

[076-103]nocent. Proceeding in this spirit "to the great loss and

[076-104]dissatisfaction of the Protestants," the latter, greatly alarmed,

[076-105]procured the interference of Ormond, now Lord Lieutenant (1662), in

[076-106]effecting a modification of the commission, appointing the court, by

[076-107]which its duration was limited to an early day. The consequence was,

[076-108]that while less than 800 claims were decided on when the fatal day

[076-109]arrived, over 3,000 were left unheard, at least a third of whom were

[076-110]admitted even by their enemies to be innocent. About 500 others had

[076-111]been restored by name in the Act of Settlement itself; but, by the Act

[076-112]of Explanation (1665), "no Papist who had not been adjudged innocent"

[076-113]under the former act could be so adjudged thereafter, "or entitled to

[076-114]claim any lands or settlements." Thus, even the inheritance of hope,

[076-115]and the reversion of expectation, were extinguished for ever for the

[076-116]sons and daughters of the ancient gentry of the kingdom.

[076-117]The religious liberties of this people, so crippled in property and

[076-118]political power, were equally at the mercy of the mob and of the

[076-119]monarch. To combat the war of calumny waged against them by the Puritan

[076-120]press and pulpit, the leading Catholics resolved to join in an official

[076-121]and authentic declaration of their true principles, as to the spiritual

[076-122]power of the Pope, their allegiance to the prince, and their relations

[076-123]to their fellow subjects of other denominations. With this intention a

[076-124]meeting was held at the house of the Marquis of Clanrickarde, in

[076-125]Dublin, at which Lords Clancarty, Carlingford, Fingal, Castlehaven, and

[076-126]Inchiquin, and the leading commoners of their faith, were present. At

[076-127]this meeting, Father Peter Walsh, a Franciscan, and an old courtier of

[076-128]Ormond's, as "Procurator of all the Clergy of Ireland," secular and

[076-129]regular, produced credentials signed by the surviving bishops or their

[076-130]vicars—including the Primate O'Reilly, the Bishops of Meath, Ardagh,

[076-131]Kilmore, and Ferns. Richard Belling, the secretary to the first

[076-132]Confederate Council, and Envoy to Rome, submitted the celebrated

[076-133]document known as "The Remonstrance," deeply imbued with the spirit of

[076-134]the Gallican church of that day. It was signed by about seventy

[076-135]Catholic peers and commoners, by the Bishop of Kilmore, by Procurator

[076-136]Walsh, and by the townsmen of Wexford—almost the only urban community

[076-137]of Catholics remaining in the country. But the propositions it

[076-138]contained as to the total independency of the temporal on the spiritual

[076-139]power, and the ecclesiastical patronage of princes, were condemned at

[076-140]the Sorbonne, at Louvain, and at Rome. The regular orders, by their

[076-141]several superiors, utterly rejected it; the exiled bishops withdrew

[076-142]their proxies from Father Walsh, and disclaimed his conduct; the

[076-143]Internuncio at Brussels, charged with the affairs of the British Isles,

[076-144]denounced it as contrary to the canons; and the elated Procurator found

[076-145]himself involved in a controversy from which he never afterwards

[076-146]escaped, and with which his memory is still angrily associated.

[076-147]The conduct of Ormond in relation to this whole business of the

[076-148]Remonstrance, was the least creditable part of his administration.

[076-149]Writhing under the eloquent pamphlets of the exiled Bishop of Ferns,

[076-150]keenly remembering his own personal wrongs against the former

[076-151]generation of bishops, of whom but three or four were yet living, he

[076-152]resolved "to work that division among the Romish clergy," which he had

[076-153]long meditated. With this view, he connived at a meeting of the

[076-154]surviving prelates and the superiors of regular orders, at Dublin, in

[076-155]1666. To this synod safe conduct was permitted to the Primate O'Reilly,

[076-156]banished to Belgium nine years before; to Peter Talbot, Archbishop of

[076-157]Dublin, John Burke, Archbishop of Tuam, Patrick Plunkett, Bishop of

[076-158]Ardagh, the vicars-general of other prelates, and the superiors of the

[076-159]regulars. This venerable body deliberated anxiously for an entire week,

[076-160]Father Walsh acting as ambassador between them and the Viceroy; at

[076-161]length, in spite of all politic considerations, they unanimously

[076-162]rejected the servile doctrine of the "Remonstrance," substituting

[076-163]instead a declaration of their own dictation. Ormond now cast off all

[076-164]affectation of liberality; Primate O'Reilly was sent back to his

[076-165]banishment, the other prelates and clergy were driven back to their

[076-166]hiding-places, or into exile abroad, and the wise, experienced,

[076-167]high-spirited duke, did not hesitate to avail himself of "the Popish

[076-168]plot" mania, which soon after broke out, to avenge himself upon an

[076-169]order of men whom he could neither break nor bend to his purposes! Of

[076-170]1,100 secular priests, and 750 regulars, still left, only sixty-nine

[076-171]had signed the Clanrickarde House Remonstrance.

[076-172]An incident of this same year—1666—illustrates more forcibly than

[076-173]description could do, the malignant feeling which had been excited in

[076-174]England against everything Irish. The importation of Irish cattle had

[076-175]long been considered an English grievance, it was now declared by law

[076-176]"a nuisance." The occasion taken to pass this statute was as ungracious

[076-177]as the act itself was despicable. In consequence of "the great fire,"

[076-178]which still glows for us in the immortal verse of Dryden, the Irish had

[076-179]sent over to the distressed, a contribution of 15,000 bullocks. This

[076-180]was considered by the generous recipients a mere pretence to preserve

[076-181]the trade in cattle between the two kingdoms, and accordingly both

[076-182]Houses, after some sharp resistance in the Lords', gravely enacted that

[076-183]the importation of Irish beef into England was "a nuisance," to be

[076-184]abated. From this period most probably dates the famous English sarcasm

[076-185]against Irish bulls.

[076-186]The act prohibiting the export of cattle from Ireland, and the equally

[076-187]exclusive and unjust Navigation Act—originally devised by Cromwell—so

[076-188]paralyzed every Irish industry, that the Puritan party became almost as

[076-189]dissatisfied as the Catholics. They maintained a close correspondence

[076-190]with their brethren in England, and began to speculate on the

[076-191]possibilities of another revolution. Ormond, to satisfy their demands,

[076-192]distributed 20,000 stand of arms among them, and reviewed the Leinster

[076-193]Militia, on the Curragh, in 1667. The next year he was recalled, and

[076-194]Lords Robarts, Berkely, and Essex, successively appointed to the

[076-195]government. The first, a Puritan, and almost a regicide, held office

[076-196]but a few months; the second, a cavalier and a friend of toleration,

[076-197]for two years; while Essex, one of those fair-minded but yielding

[076-198]characters, known in the next reign as "Trimmers," petitioned for his

[076-199]own recall and Ormond's restoration, in 1676. The only events which

[076-200]marked these last nine years—from Ormond's removal till his

[076-201]reappointment—were the surprise of Carrickfergus by a party of unpaid

[076-202]soldiers, and their desperate defence of that ancient stronghold; the

[076-203]embassies to and from the Irish Catholics and the court, of Colonel

[076-204]Richard Talbot; and the establishment of extensive woollen

[076-205]manufactories at Thomastown, Callan, and Kilkenny, under the patronage

[076-206]of Ormond.
