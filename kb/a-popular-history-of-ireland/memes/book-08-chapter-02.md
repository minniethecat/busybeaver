CHAPTER II.

[053-001]EVENTS OF THE REIGN OF PHILIP AND MARY.

[053-002]The death of Edward VI. and the accession of the lady Mary were known

[053-003]in Dublin by the middle of July, 1553, and soon spread all over the

[053-004]kingdom. On the 20th of that month, the form of proclamation was

[053-005]received from London, in which the new Queen was forbidden to be styled

[053-006]"head of the church," and this was quickly followed by another

[053-007]ordinance, authorizing all who would to publicly attend Mass, but not

[053-008]compelling thereto any who were unwilling. A curious legal difficulty

[053-009]existed in relation to Mary's title to the Crown of Ireland. By the

[053-010]Irish Statute, 38. Hen. VIII., the Irish crown was entailed by name on

[053-011]the Lady Elizabeth, and that act had not been repealed. It was,

[053-012]however, held to have been superseded by the English Statute, 35. Hen.

[053-013]VIII., which followed the election of 1541, and declared the Crown of

[053-014]Ireland "united and knit to the Imperial Crown of the Realm of

[053-015]England." Read in the light of the latter statute, the Irish

[053-016]sovereignty might be regarded a mere appurtenance of that of England,

[053-017]but Mary did not so consider it. At her coronation, a separate crown

[053-018]was used for Ireland, nor did she feel assured of the validity of her

[053-019]claim to wear it till she had obtained a formal dispensation to that

[053-020]effect from the Pope.

[053-022]The intelligence of the new Queen's accession, and the public

[053-023]restoration of the old religion, diffused a general joy throughout

[053-024]Ireland. Festivals and pageants were held in the streets, and eloquent

[053-025]sermons poured from all the pulpits. Archbishop Dowdal was called from

[053-026]exile, and the Primacy was restored to Armagh. Sir Anthony St. Leger,

[053-027]his ancient antagonist, had now conformed to the Court fashion, and was

[053-028]sent over to direct the establishment of that religion which he had

[053-029]been so many years engaged in pulling down. In 1554, Browne, Staples,

[053-030]Lancaster, and Travers, were formally deprived of their sees; Bale and

[053-031]Casey of Limerick fled beyond seas, without awaiting judgment. Married

[053-032]clergymen were invariably silenced, and the children of Browne were

[053-033]declared by statute illegitimate.

[053-034]What, however, gratified the public even more than these retributions

[053-035]was the liberation of the aged Chief of Offally from the Tower of

[053-036]London, at the earnest supplication of his heroic daughter, Margaret,

[053-037]who found her way to the Queen's presence to beg that boon; and the

[053-038]simultaneous restoration of the Earldom of Kildare, in the person of

[053-039]that Gerald, who had been so young a fugitive among the glens of

[053-040]Muskerry and Donegal, and had since undergone so many continental

[053-041]adventures. With O'Conor and young Gerald, the heirs of the houses of

[053-042]Ormond and of Upper Ossory were also allowed to return to their homes,

[053-043]to the great delight of the southern half of the kingdom. The

[053-044]subsequent marriage of Mary with Philip II. of Spain gave an additional

[053-045]security to the Irish Catholics for the future freedom of their

[053-046]religion.

[053-047]Great as was the change in this respect, it is not to be inferred that

[053-048]the national relations of Ireland and England were materially affected

[053-049]by such a change of sovereign. The maxims of conquest were not to be

[053-050]abandoned at the dictates of religion. The supreme power continued to

[053-051]be entrusted only to Englishmen; while the same Parliament (3rd and 4th

[053-052]Philip and Mary) which abolished the title of head of the Church, and

[053-053]restored the Roman jurisdiction in matters spiritual, divided Leix and

[053-054]Offally, Glenmalier and Slewmargy, into shire ground, subject to

[053-055]English law, under the name of King's and Queen's County. The new forts

[053-056]of Maryborough and Philipstown, as well as the county names, served to

[053-057]teach the people of Leinster that the work of conquest could be as

[053-058]industriously prosecuted by Catholic as by Protestant rulers. Nor were

[053-059]these forts established and maintained without many a struggle. St.

[053-060]Leger, and his still abler successor, the Earl of Sussex, and the new

[053-061]Lord Treasurer, Sir Henry Sidney, were forced to lead many an

[053-062]expedition to the relief of those garrisons, and the dispersion of

[053-063]their assailants. It was not in Irish human nature to submit to the

[053-064]constant pressure of a foreign power without seizing every possible

[053-065]opportunity for its expulsion.

[053-066]The new principle of primogeniture introduced at the commutation of

[053-067]chieftainries into earldoms was productive in this reign of much

[053-068]commotion and bloodshed. The seniors of the O'Briens resisted its

[053-069]establishment in Thomond, on the death of the first Earl; Calvagh

[053-070]O'Donnell took arms against his father, to defeat its introduction into

[053-071]Tyrconnell; John the Proud, as we have seen in the reign of Edward, had

[053-072]been one of its earliest opponents in Ulster. Being accused in the last

[053-073]year of Queen Mary of procuring the death of his illegitimate brother,

[053-074]the Baron of Dungannon, in order to remove him from his path, he was

[053-075]summoned to account for those circumstances before Sir Henry Sidney,

[053-076]then acting as Lord Justice. His plea has been preserved to us, and no

[053-077]doubt represents the prevailing opinion of the Gaelic-speaking

[053-078]population towards the new system. He answered, "that the surrender

[053-079]which his father had made to Henry VIII., and the restoration which

[053-080]Henry made to his father again were of no force; inasmuch as his father

[053-081]had no right to the lands which he surrendered to the King, except

[053-082]during his own life; that he (John) himself was the O'Neil by the law

[053-083]of Tanistry, and by popular election; and that he assumed no

[053-084]superiority over the chieftains of the North except what belonged to

[053-085]his ancestors." To these views he adhered to the last, accepting no

[053-086]English honours, though quite willing to live at peace with English

[053-087]sovereigns. When the title of Earl of Tyrone was revived, it was in

[053-088]favour of the son of the Baron, the celebrated Hugh O'Neil, the ally of

[053-089]Spain, and the most formidable antagonist of Queen Elizabeth.

[053-090]In the Irish Parliament already referred to (3rd and 4th Philip and

[053-091]Mary) an Act was passed declaring it a felony to introduce armed

[053-092]Scotchmen into Ireland, or to intermarry with them without a license

[053-093]under the great seal. This statute was directed against those

[053-094]multitudes of Islesmen and Highlanders who annually crossed the narrow

[053-095]strait which separates Antrim from Argyle to harass the English

[053-096]garrisons alongshore, or to enlist as auxiliaries in Irish quarrels. In

[053-097]1556, under one of their principal leaders, James, son of Conal, they

[053-098]laid siege to Carrickfergus and occupied Lord Sussex some six weeks in

[053-099]the glens of Antrim. Their leader finally entered into conditions, the

[053-100]nature of which may be inferred from the fact that he received the

[053-101]honour of knighthood on their acceptance. John O'Neil had usually in

[053-102]his service a number of these mercenary troops, from among whom he

[053-103]selected sixty body-guards, the same number supplied by his own clan.

[053-104]In his first attempt to subject Tyrconnell to his supremacy in 1557,

[053-105]his camp near Raphoe was surprised at night by Calvagh O'Donnell, and

[053-106]his native and foreign guards were put to the sword, while he himself

[053-107]barely escaped by swimming the Mourne and the Finn. O'Donnell had

[053-108]frequently employed a similar force, in his own defence; and we read of

[053-109]the Lord of Clanrickarde driving back a host of them engaged in the

[053-110]service of his rivals, from the banks of the Moy, in 1558.

[053-111]Although the memory of Queen Mary has been held up to execration during

[053-112]three centuries as a bloody-minded and malignant persecutor of all who

[053-113]differed from her in religion, it is certain that in Ireland, where, if

[053-114]anywhere, the Protestant. minority might have been extinguished by such

[053-115]severities as are imputed to her, no persecution for conscience' sake

[053-116]took place. Married Bishops were deprived, and married priests were

[053-117]silenced, but beyond this no coercion was employed. It has been said

[053-118]there was not time to bring the machinery to bear; but surely if there

[053-119]was time to do so in England, within the space of five years, there was

[053-120]time in Ireland also. The consoling truth—honourable to human nature

[053-121]and to Christian charity, is—that many families out of England,

[053-122]apprehending danger in their own country, sought and found a refuge

[053-123]from their fears in the western island. The families of Agar, Ellis,

[053-124]and Harvey, are descended from emigrants, who were accompanied from

[053-125]Cheshire by a clergyman of their own choice, whose ministrations they

[053-126]freely enjoyed during the remainder of this reign at Dublin. The story

[053-127]about Dr. Cole having been despatched to Ireland with a commission to

[053-128]punish heretics, and, losing it on the way, is unworthy of serious

[053-129]notice. If there had been any such determination formed there was ample

[053-130]time to put it into execution between 1553 and 1558.
