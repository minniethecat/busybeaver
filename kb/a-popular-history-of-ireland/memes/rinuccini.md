## Overview

Giovanni Battista Rinuccini (1592–1653), Archbishop of Fermo in the Marches of Ancona, was the Papal Nuncio sent by Pope Innocent X to the Irish Confederate Catholics in 1645. His mission was to secure the full restoration of the Catholic religion in Ireland, and his arrival transformed the political dynamics of the Confederate period. (071-036)

> "This personage was John Baptist Rinuccini, Archbishop of Fermo, in the Marches of Ancona, which see he had preferred to the more exalted" station of a cardinalate. (071-036)

Rinuccini brought arms, ammunition, and money to the Confederate cause, but his insistence on the complete restoration of Church property and his opposition to any compromise with Protestants made him a divisive figure. (071-036)

## Arrival in Ireland

When Rinuccini landed in Ireland in 1645, the Confederate Catholics had been at war for four years. They had established a functioning government at Kilkenny but were divided between a moderate party that sought a negotiated settlement with King Charles I and a clerical party that demanded full Catholic restoration. Rinuccini immediately aligned himself with the latter. (071-036)

The Nuncio's timing was propitious. The Confederate general [[Owen Roe ONeill]] had just won the Battle of Benburb (1646), and Rinuccini believed that total victory was within reach. He urged the Confederates to reject any truce or treaty that fell short of restoring the Catholic Church to its pre-Reformation position. (071-187)

## The Ormond Peace Crisis

The defining political crisis of Rinuccini's mission came in 1646, when the Confederate Supreme Council negotiated a peace treaty with the Marquess of Ormond, the King's Lord Lieutenant. The treaty would have granted Catholics limited toleration but the Nuncio found it wholly inadequate.

> "Rinuccini and a majority of the Prelates attached importance" to the treaty's omissions, and the Nuncio denounced it as a betrayal of Catholic interests. (071-187)

Notwithstanding the dissuasions of Rinuccini to the contrary, the Supreme Council was determined to proceed with the Ormond settlement. The Nuncio responded by excommunicating those who supported it and by throwing the full weight of the Church against the moderate party. (071-195)

> "Notwithstanding the dissuasions of Rinuccini to the contrary, it was to be kept secret from the world, though some of its obligations were" already being acted upon. (071-195)

This intervention split the Confederation at the very moment when unity was most needed.

## Departure and Legacy

By 1648 the Confederates, desperate for any settlement, turned back to Ormond. The Nuncio's position became untenable. "Rinuccini, despairing, perhaps, of a cause so distracted, sailed in his" own galley from Galway in February 1649, never to return. Within months [[Oliver Cromwell]] would land at Dublin to complete the conquest of Ireland. (072-177)

Rinuccini's legacy remains contested. To Catholic historians, he was a principled defender of the Faith who saw clearly that half-measures would lead to complete defeat.

To his critics, his intransigence helped destroy the best chance the Confederate Catholics had for a negotiated settlement. His mission marked the last time a Papal representative would exercise direct political influence in Ireland until the modern era.
