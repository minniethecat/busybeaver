CHAPTER IV.

[042-001]SUBSEQUENT PROCEEDINGS OF RICHARD II.—LIEUTENANCY AND DEATH OF THE EARL

[042-002]OF MARCH—SECOND EXPEDITION OF RICHARD AGAINST ART McMURROGH—CHANGE OF

[042-003]DYNASTY IN ENGLAND.

[042-004]At Dublin, Richard prepared to celebrate the festival of Christmas,

[042-005]with all the splendour of which he was so fond. He had received letters

[042-006]from his council in England warmly congratulating him on the results of

[042-007]his "noble voyage" and his successes against "his rebel Make Murgh."

[042-008]Several lords and chiefs were hospitably entertained by him during the

[042-009]holidays—but the greater magnates did not yet present themselves—unless

[042-010]we suppose them to have continued his guests at Dublin, from Christmas

[042-011]till Easter, which is hardly credible.

[042-012]The supplies which he had provided were soon devoured by so vast a

[042-013]following. His army, however, were paid their wages weekly, and were

[042-014]well satisfied. But whatever the King or his flatterers might pretend,

[042-015]the real object of all the mighty preparations made was still in the

[042-016]distance, and fresh supplies were needed for the projected campaign of

[042-017]1395. To raise the requisite funds, he determined to send to England

[042-018]his uncle, the Duke of Gloucester. Gloucester carried a letter to the

[042-019]regent, the Duke of York, countersigned "Lincolne," and dated from

[042-020]Dublin, "Feb. 1, 1395." The council, consisting of the Earls of Derby,

[042-021]Arundel, de Ware, Salisbury, Northumberland, and others, was convened,

[042-022]and they "readily voted a tenth off the clergy, and a fifteenth off the

[042-023]laity, for the King's supply." This they sent with a document, signed

[042-024]by them all, exhorting him to a vigorous prosecution of the war, and

[042-025]the demolition of all forts belonging to "MacMourgh [or] le grand

[042-026]O'Nel." They also addressed him another letter, complimentary of his

[042-027]valour and discretion in all things.

[042-028]While awaiting supplies from England, Richard made a progress as far

[042-029]northward as Drogheda, where he took up his abode in the Dominican

[042-030]Convent of St. Mary Magdalen. On the eve of St. Patrick's Day, O'Neil,

[042-031]O'Donnell, O'Reilly, O'Hanlon, and MacMahon, visited and exchanged

[042-032]professions of friendship with him. It is said they made "submission"

[042-033]to him as their sovereign lord, but until the Indentures, which have

[042-034]been spoken of, but never published, are exhibited, it will be

[042-035]impossible to determine what, in their minds and in his, were the exact

[042-036]relations subsisting between the native Irish princes and the King of

[042-037]England at that time. O'Neil, and other lords of Ulster, accompanied

[042-038]him back to Dublin, where they found O'Brien, O'Conor, and McMurrogh,

[042-039]lately arrived. They were all lodged in a fair mansion, according to

[042-040]the notion of Master Castide, Froissart's informant, and were under the

[042-041]care of the Earl of Ormond and Castide himself, both of whom spoke

[042-042]familiarly the Irish language.

[042-043]The glimpse we get through Norman spectacles of the manners and customs

[042-044]of these chieftains is eminently instructive, both as regards the

[042-045]observers and the observed. They would have, it seems, very much to the

[042-046]disedification of the English esquire, "their minstrels and principal

[042-047]servants sit at the same table and eat from the same dish." The

[042-048]interpreters employed all their eloquence in vain to dissuade them from

[042-049]this lewd habit, which they perversely called "a praiseworthy custom,"

[042-050]till at last, to get rid of importunities, they consented to have it

[042-051]ordered otherwise, during their stay as King Richard's guests.

[042-052]On the 24th of March the Cathedral of Christ's Church beheld the four

[042-053]kings devoutly keeping the vigil preparatory to knighthood. They had

[042-054]been induced to accept that honour from Richard's hand. They had

[042-055]apologized at first, saying they were all knighted at the age of seven.

[042-056]But the ceremony, as performed in the rest of Christendom, was

[042-057]represented to them as a great and religious custom, which made the

[042-058]simplest knight the equal of his sovereign, which added new lustre to

[042-059]the crowned head, and fresh honour to the victorious sword. On the

[042-060]Feast of the Annunciation they went through the imposing ceremony,

[042-061]according to the custom obtaining among their entertainers.

[042-063]While the native Princes of the four Provinces were thus lodged

[042-064]together in one house, it was inevitable that plans of co-operation for

[042-065]the future should be discussed between them. Soon after the Earl of

[042-066]Ormond, who knew their language, appeared before Richard as the accuser

[042-067]of McMurrogh, who was, on his statement, committed to close confinement

[042-068]in the Castle. He was, however, soon after set at liberty, though

[042-069]O'Moore, O'Byrne, and John O'Mullain were retained in custody, probably

[042-070]as hostages, for the fulfilment of the terms of his release. By this

[042-071]time the expected supplies had arrived from England, and the festival

[042-072]of Easter was happily passed. Before breaking up from his winter

[042-073]quarters Richard celebrated with great pomp the festival of his

[042-074]namesake, St. Richard, Bishop of Chichester, and then summoned a

[042-075]parliament to meet him at Kilkenny on the 12th of the month. The acts

[042-076]of this parliament have not seen the light; an obscurity which they

[042-077]share in common with all the documents of this Prince's progress in

[042-078]Ireland. The same remark was made three centuries ago by the English

[042-079]chronicler, Grafton, who adds with much simplicity, that as Richard's

[042-080]voyage into Ireland "was nothing profitable nor honourable to him,

[042-081]therefore the writers think it scant worth the noting."

[042-082]Early in May a deputation, at the head of which was the celebrated

[042-083]William of Wyckham, arrived from England, invoking the personal

[042-084]presence of the King to quiet the disturbances caused by the progress

[042-085]of Lollardism. With this invitation he decided at once to comply, but

[042-086]first he appointed the youthful Earl of March his lieutenant in

[042-087]Ireland, and confirmed the ordinance of Edward III., empowering the

[042-088]chief governor in council to convene parliament by writ, which writ

[042-089]should be of equal obligation with the King's writ in England. He

[042-090]ordained that a fine of not less than fifty marks, and not more than

[042-091]one hundred, should be exacted of every representative of a town or

[042-092]shire, who, being elected as such, neglected or refused to attend. He

[042-093]reformed the royal courts, and appointed Walter de Hankerford and

[042-094]William Sturmey, two Englishmen, "well learned in the law" as judges,

[042-095]whose annual salaries were to be forty pounds each. Having made these

[042-096]arrangements, he took an affectionate leave of his heir and cousin, and

[042-097]sailed for England, whither he was accompanied by most of the great

[042-098]nobles who had passed over with him to the Irish wars. Little dreamt

[042-099]they of the fate which impended over many of their heads. Three short

[042-100]years and Gloucester would die by the assassin's hand, Arundel by the

[042-101]executioner's axe, and Mowbray, Earl Marshal, the ambassador at

[042-102]Ballygorry, would pine to death in Italian banishment. Even a greater

[042-103]change than any of these—a change of dynasty—was soon to come over

[042-104]England.

[042-105]The young Earl of March, now left in the supreme direction of affairs,

[042-106]so far as we know, had no better title to govern than that he was heir

[042-107]to the English throne, unless it may have been considered an additional

[042-108]recommendation that he was sixth in descent from the Lady Eva

[042-109]McMurrogh. To his English title, he added that of Earl of Ulster and

[042-110]Lord of Connaught, derived from his mother, the daughter of Lionel,

[042-111]Duke of Clarence, and those of Lord of Trim and Clare, from other

[042-112]relations. The counsellors with whom he was surrounded included the

[042-113]wisest statesmen and most experienced soldiers of "the Pale." Among

[042-114]them were Almaric, Baron Grace, who, contrary to the statute of

[042-115]Kilkenny, had married an O'Meagher of Ikerrin, and whose family had

[042-116]intermarried with the McMurroghs; the third Earl of Ormond, an

[042-117]indomitable soldier, who had acted as Lord Deputy, in former years of

[042-118]this reign; Cranley, Archbishop of Dublin, and Roche, the Cistercian

[042-119]Abbot of St. Mary's, lately created Lord Treasurer of Ireland; Stephen

[042-120]Bray, Chief Justice; and Gerald, fifth Earl of Kildare. Among his

[042-121]advisers of English birth were Roger Grey, his successor; the new

[042-122]Judges Hankerford and Sturmey, and others of less pacific reputation.

[042-123]With the dignitaries of the Church, and the innumerable priors and

[042-124]abbots, in and about Dublin, the court of the Heir-Presumptive must

[042-125]have been a crowded and imposing one for those times, and had its

[042-126]external prospects been peaceful, much ease and pleasure might have

[042-127]been enjoyed within its walls.

[042-128]In the three years of this administration, the struggle between the

[042-129]natives, the naturalized, and the English interest knew no cessation in

[042-130]Leinster. Some form of submission had been wrung from McMurrogh before

[042-131]his release from Dublin Castle, in the spring of 1395, but this

[042-132]engagement extorted under duress, from a guest towards whom every rite

[042-133]of hospitality had been violated, he did not feel bound by after his

[042-134]enlargement. In the same year an attempt was made to entrap him at a

[042-135]banquet given in one of the castles of the frontier, but warned by his

[042-136]bard, he made good his escape "by the strength of his arm, and by

[042-137]bravery." After this double violation of what among his countrymen,

[042-138]even of the fiercest tribes, was always held sacred, the privileged

[042-139]character of a guest, he never again placed himself at the mercy of

[042-140]prince or peer, but prosecuted the war with unfaltering determination.

[042-141]In 1396, his neighbour, the chief of Imayle, carried off from an

[042-142]engagement near Dublin, six score heads of the foreigners: and the next

[042-143]year—an exploit hardly second in its kind to the taking of Ross—the

[042-144]strong castle and town of Carlow were captured by McMurrogh himself. In

[042-145]the campaign of 1398, on the 20th of July, was fought the eventful

[042-146]battle of Kenlis, or Kells, on the banks of the stream called "the

[042-147]King's river," in the barony of Kells, and county of Kilkenny. Here

[042-148]fell the Heir-Presumptive to the English crown, whose premature removal

[042-149]was one of the causes which contributed to the revolution in England, a

[042-150]year or two later. The tidings of this event filled "the Pale" with

[042-151]consternation, and thoroughly aroused the vindictive temper of Richard.

[042-152]He at once despatched to Dublin his half-brother, Thomas Holland, Earl

[042-153]of Kent, recently created Duke of Surrey. To this duke he made a gift

[042-154]of Carlow castle and town, to be held (if taken) by knights' service.

[042-155]He then, as much, perhaps, to give occupation to the minds of his

[042-156]people, as to prosecute his old project of subduing Ireland, began to

[042-157]make preparations for his second expedition thither. Death again

[042-158]delayed him. John of Ghent, Duke of Lancaster, his uncle, and one of

[042-159]the most famous soldiers of the time, suddenly sickened, and died. As

[042-160]Henry, his son, was in banishment, the King, under pretence of

[042-161]appropriating his vast wealth to the service of the nation, seized it

[042-162]into his own hands, and despite the warnings of his wisest counsellors

[042-163]as to the disturbed state of the kingdom, again took up his march for

[042-164]Milford Haven.

[042-165]A French knight, named Creton, had obtained leave with a

[042-166]brother-in-arms to accompany this expedition, and has left us a very

[042-167]vivid account of its progress. Quitting Paris they reached London just

[042-168]as King Richard was about "to cross the sea on account of the injuries

[042-169]and grievances that his mortal enemies had committed against him in

[042-170]Ireland, where they had put to death many of his faithful friends."

[042-171]Wherefore they were further told, "he would take no rest until he had

[042-172]avenged himself upon MacMore, who called himself most excellent King

[042-173]and Lord of great Ireland; where he had but little territory of any

[042-174]kind."

[042-175]They at once set out for Milford, where, "waiting for the north wind,"

[042-176]they remained "ten whole days." Here they found King Richard with a

[042-177]great army, and a corresponding fleet. The clergy were taxed to supply

[042-178]horses, waggons, and money—the nobles, shires, and towns, their

[042-179]knights, men-at-arms, and archers—the seaports, from Whitehaven to

[042-180]Penzance, were obliged, by an order in council, dated February 7th, to

[042-181]send vessels rated at twenty-five tons and upwards to Milford, by the

[042-182]octave of Easter. King's letters were issued whenever the usual

[042-183]ordinances failed, and even the press-gang was resorted to, to raise

[042-184]the required number of mariners. Minstrels of all kinds crowded to the

[042-185]camp, enlivening it by their strains, and enriching themselves the

[042-186]while. The wind coming fair, the vessels "took in their lading of

[042-187]bread, wine, cows and calves, salt meat and plenty of water," and the

[042-188]King taking leave of his ladies, they set sail.

[042-189]In two days they saw "the tower of Waterford." The condition to which

[042-190]the people of this English stronghold had been reduced by the war was

[042-191]pitiable in the extreme. Some were in rags, others girt with ropes, and

[042-192]their dwellings seemed to the voyagers but huts and holes. They rushed

[042-193]into the tide up to their waists, for the speedy unloading of the

[042-194]ships, especially attending to those that bore the supplies of the

[042-195]army. Little did the proud cavaliers and well-fed yeomen, who then

[042-196]looked on, imagine, as they pitied the poor wretches of Waterford, that

[042-197]before many weeks were over, they would themselves be reduced to the

[042-198]like necessity—even to rushing into the sea to contend for a morsel of

[042-199]food.

[042-200]Six days after his arrival, which was on the 1st of June, King Richard

[042-201]marched from Waterford "in close order to Kilkenny." He had now the

[042-202]advantage of long days and warm nights, which in his first expedition

[042-203]he had not. His forces were rather less than in 1394; some say twenty,

[042-204]some twenty-four thousand in all. The Earl of Rutland, with a

[042-205]reinforcement in one hundred ships, was to have followed him, but this

[042-206]unfaithful courtier did not greatly hasten his preparations to overtake

[042-207]his master. With the King were the Lord Steward of England, Sir Thomas

[042-208]Percy; the Duke of Exeter; De Spencer, Earl of Gloucester; the Lord

[042-209]Henry of Lancaster, afterwards King Henry V.; the son of the late Duke

[042-210]of Gloucester; the son of the Countess of Salisbury; the Bishop of

[042-211]Exeter and London; the Abbot of Westminster, and a gallant Welsh

[042-212]gentleman, afterwards known to fame as Owen Glendower. He dropped the

[042-213]subterfuge of bearing Edward the Confessor's banner, and advanced his

[042-214]own standard, which bore leopards and flower de luces. In this order,

[042-215]"riding boldly," they reached Kilkenny, where Richard remained a

[042-216]fortnight awaiting news of the Earl of Rutland from Waterford. No news,

[042-217]however, came. But while he waited, he received intelligence from

[042-218]Kildare which gratified his thirst for vengeance. Jenico d'Artois, a

[042-219]Gascon knight of great discretion and valour, who had come over the

[042-220]preceding year with the Duke of Surrey, marching towards Kilkenny, had

[042-221]encountered some bands of the Irish in Kildare (bound on a like errand

[042-222]to their prince), whom he fought and put to flight, leaving two hundred

[042-223]of them dead upon the field. This Jenico, relishing Irish warfare more

[042-224]than most foreign soldiers of his age, continued long after to serve in

[042-225]Ireland—married one of his daughters to Preston, Baron of Naas, and

[042-226]another to the first Lord Portlester.

[042-227]On the 23rd of June, "the very vigil of St. John," a saint to whom the

[042-228]King was very much devoted, Richard, resolving to delay no longer, left

[042-229]Kilkenny, and marched directly towards Catherlough. He sent a message

[042-230]in advance to McMurrogh, "who would neither submit nor obey him in

[042-231]anyway; but affirmed that he was the rightful King of Ireland, and that

[042-232]he would never cease from war and the defence of his country until his

[042-233]death; and said that the wish to deprive him of it by conquest was

[042-234]unlawful."

[042-235]Art McMurrogh, now some years beyond middle age, had with him in arms

[042-236]"three thousand hardy men," "who did not appear," says our French

[042-237]knight, "to be much afraid of the English." The cattle and corn, the

[042-238]women and the helpless, he had removed into the interior of the

[042-239]fastnesses, while he himself awaited, in Idrone, the approach of the

[042-240]enemy.

[042-242]This district, which lies north and south between the rivers Slaney and

[042-243]Barrow, is of a diversified and broken soil, watered with several small

[042-244]streams, and patched with tracts of morass and marsh. It was then half

[042-245]covered with wood, except in the neighbourhood of Old Leighlin, and a

[042-246]few other places where villages had grown up around the castles, raths,

[042-247]and monasteries of earlier days. On reaching the border of the forest,

[042-248]King Richard ordered all the habitations in sight to be set on fire;

[042-249]and then "two thousand five hundred of the well affected people," or,

[042-250]as others say, prisoners, "began to hew a highway into the woods."

[042-251]When the first space was cleared, Richard, ever fond of pageantry,

[042-252]ordered his standard to be planted on the new ground, and pennons and

[042-253]banners arrayed on every side. Then he sent for the sons of the Dukes

[042-254]of Gloucester and Lancaster, his cousins, and the son of the Countess

[042-255]of Salisbury and other bachelors-in-arms, and there knighted them with

[042-256]all due solemnity. To young Lancaster, he said, "My fair cousin,

[042-257]henceforth, be preux and valiant, for you have some valiant blood to

[042-258]conquer." The youth to whom he made this address was little more than a

[042-259]boy, but tall of his age, and very vigorous. He had been a hard student

[042-260]at Oxford, and was now as unbridled as a colt new loosed into a meadow.

[042-261]He was fond of music, and afterwards became illustrious as the Fifth

[042-262]Henry of English history. Who could have foreseen, when first he put on

[042-263]his spurs by the wood's side, in Catherlough, that he would one day

[042-264]inherit the throne of England and make good the pretensions of all his

[042-265]predecessors to the throne of France?

[042-266]Richard's advance was slow and wearisome in the forests of Idrone. His

[042-267]route was towards the eastern coast. McMurrogh retreated before him,

[042-268]harassing him dreadfully, carrying off everything fit for food for man

[042-269]or beast, surprising and slaying his foragers, and filling his camp

[042-270]nightly with alarm and blood. The English archers got occasional shots

[042-271]at his men, "so that they did not all escape;" and they in turn often

[042-272]attacked the rear-guard, "and threw their darts with such force that

[042-273]they pierced haubergeon and plates through and through." The Leinster

[042-274]King would risk no open battle so long as he could thus cut off the

[042-275]enemy in detail. Many brave knights fell, many men-at-arms and archers;

[042-276]and a deep disrelish for the service began to manifest itself in the

[042-277]English camp.

[042-278]A party of Wexford settlers, however, brought one day to his camp

[042-279]Malachy McMurrogh, uncle to Art, a timid, treaty-making man. According

[042-280]to the custom of that century—observed by the defenders of Stirling and

[042-281]the burgesses of Calais—he submitted with a _wythe_ about his neck,

[042-282]rendering up a naked sword. His retinue, bareheaded and barefoot,

[042-283]followed him into the presence of Richard, who received them

[042-284]graciously. "Friends," said he to them, "as to the evils and wrongs

[042-285]that you have committed against me, I pardon you on condition that each

[042-286]of you will swear to be faithful to me for the time to come." Of this

[042-287]circumstance he made the most, as our guide goes on to tell in these

[042-288]words: "Then every one readily complied with his demand; and took the

[042-289]oath. When this was done he sent word to MacMore, who called himself

[042-290]Lord and King of Ireland, (_that country_,) where he has many a wood

[042-291]but little cultivated land, that if he would come straightways to him

[042-292]with a rope about _his_ neck, as his uncle had done, he would admit him

[042-293]to mercy, and elsewhere give him castles and lands in abundance." The

[042-294]answer of King Art is thus reported: "MacMore told the King's people he

[042-295]would do no such thing for all the treasures of the sea or on this

[042-296]side, (the sea,) but would continue to fight and harass him."

[042-297]For eleven days longer Richard continued his route in the direction of

[042-298]Dublin, McMurrogh and his allies falling back towards the hills and

[042-299]glens of Wicklow. The English could find nothing by the way but "a few

[042-300]green oats" for the horses, which being exposed night and day, and so

[042-301]badly fed, perished in great numbers. The general discontent now made

[042-302]itself audible even to the ears of the King. For many days five or six

[042-303]men had but a "single loaf." Even gentlemen, knights and squires,

[042-304]fasted in succession; and our chivalrous guide, for his part, "would

[042-305]have been heartily glad to have been penniless at Poitiers or Paris."

[042-306]Daily deaths made the camp a scene of continued mourning, and all the

[042-307]minstrels that had come across the sea to amuse their victor

[042-308]countrymen, like the poet who went with Edward II. to Bannockburn to

[042-309]celebrate the conquest of the Scots, found their gay imaginings turned

[042-310]to a sorrowful reverse.

[042-311]At last, however, they came in sight of the sea-coast, where vessels

[042-312]laden with provisions, sent from Dublin, were awaiting them. So eager

[042-313]were the famished men for food, that "they rushed into the sea as

[042-314]eagerly as they would into their straw." All their money was poured

[042-315]into the hands of the merchants; some of them even fought in the water

[042-316]about a morsel of food, while in their thirst they drank all the wine

[042-317]they could lay hands on. Our guide saw full a thousand men drunk that

[042-318]day on "the wine of Ossey and Spain." The scene of this extraordinary

[042-319]incident is conjectured to have been at or near Arklow, where the beach

[042-320]is sandy and flat, such as it is not at any point of Wicklow north of

[042-321]that place.

[042-322]The morning after the arrival of these stores, King Richard again set

[042-323]forward for Dublin, determining to penetrate Wicklow by the valleys

[042-324]that lead from the Meeting of the Waters to Bray. He had not proceeded

[042-325]far on his march, when a Franciscan friar reached his camp as

[042-326]Ambassador from the Leinster King. This unnamed messenger, whose cowl

[042-327]history cannot raise, expressed the willingness of his lord to treat

[042-328]with the King, through some accredited agent—"some lord who might be

[042-329]relied upon"—"so that _their_ anger (Richard's and his own), that had

[042-330]long been cruel, might now be extinguished." The announcement spread

[042-331]"great joy" in the English camp. A halt was ordered, and a council

[042-332]called. After a consultation, it was resolved that de Spencer, Earl of

[042-333]Gloucester, should be empowered to confer with Art. This nobleman, now

[042-334]but 26 years of age, had served in the campaign of 1394. He was one of

[042-335]the most powerful peers of England, and had married Constance, daughter

[042-336]of the Duke of York, Richard's cousin. From his possessions in Wales,

[042-337]he probably knew something of the Gaelic customs and speech. He was

[042-338]captain of the rearguard on this expedition, and now, with 200 lances,

[042-339]and 1,000 archers, all of whom were chosen men, he set out for the

[042-340]conference. The French knight also went with him, as he himself relates

[042-341]in these words:

[042-342]"Between two woods, at some distance from the sea, I beheld MacMore and

[042-343]a body of the Irish, more than I can number, descend the mountain. He

[042-344]had a horse, without housing or saddle, which was so fine and good,

[042-345]that it had cost him, they said, four hundred cows; for there is little

[042-346]money in the country, wherefore their usual traffic is only with

[042-347]cattle. In coming down, it galloped so hard, that, in my opinion, I

[042-348]never saw hare, deer, sheep, or any other animal, I declare to you for

[042-349]a certainty, run with such speed as it did. In his right hand he bore a

[042-350]great long dart, which he cast with much skill. * * * * His people drew

[042-351]up in front of the wood. These two (Gloucester and the King), like an

[042-352]out-post, met near a little brook. There MacMore stopped. He was a fine

[042-353]large man—wondrously active. To look at him, he seemed very stern and

[042-354]savage, and an able man. He and the Earl spake of their doings,

[042-355]recounting the evil and injury that MacMore had done towards the King

[042-356]at sundry times; and how they all foreswore their fidelity when

[042-357]wrongfully, without judgment or law, they most mischievously put to

[042-358]death the courteous Earl of March. Then they exchanged much discourse,

[042-359]but did not come to agreement; they took short leave, and hastily

[042-392]When Richard arrived in Dublin, still galled by the memory of his

[042-424]It is beyond our province to follow the after-fate of the monarch,

[042-425]whose Irish campaigns we have endeavoured to restore to their relative

[042-426]importance. His deposition and cruel death, in the prison of

[042-427]Pontefract, are familiar to readers of English history. The

[042-428]unsuccessful insurrections suppressed during his rival's reign, and the

[042-429]glory won by the son of that rival, as Henry V., seem to have

[042-430]established the house of Lancaster firmly on the throne; but the long

[042-431]minority of Henry VI.—who inherited the royal dignity at nine months

[042-432]old—and the factions among the other members of that family, opened

[042-433]opportunities, too tempting to be resisted, to the rival dynasty of

[042-434]York. During the first sixty years of the century on which we are next

[042-435]to enter, we shall find the English interest in Ireland controlled by

[042-436]the house of Lancaster; in the succeeding twenty-five years the

[042-437]partizans of the house of York are in the ascendant; until at length,

[042-438]after the victory of Bosworth field (A.D. 1485), the wars of the roses

[042-439]are terminated by the coronation of the Earl of Richmond as Henry VII.,

[042-440]and his politic marriage with the Princess Elizabeth—the representative

[042-441]of the Yorkist dynasty. It will be seen how these rival houses had

[042-442]their respective factions among the Anglo-Irish; how these factions

[042-443]retarded two centuries the establishment of English power in Ireland;

[042-444]how the native lords and chiefs took advantage of the disunion among

[042-445]the foreigners to circumscribe more and more the narrow limits of the

[042-446]Pale; and lastly, how the absence of national unity alone preserved the

[042-447]power so reduced from utter extinction. In considering all these far

[042-448]extending consequences of the deposition of Richard II., and the

[042-449]substitution of Henry of Lancaster in his stead, we must give due

[042-450]weight to his unsuccessful Irish wars as proximate causes of that

[042-451]revolution. The death of the Heir-Presumptive in the battle of Kells;

[042-452]the exactions and ill-success of Richard in his wars; the seizure of

[042-453]John of Ghent's estates and treasures; the absence of the sovereign at

[042-454]the critical moment: all these are causes which operated powerfully to

[042-455]that end. And of these all that relate to Irish affairs were mainly

[042-456]brought about by the heroic constancy, in the face of enormous odds,

[042-457]the unwearied energy, and high military skill exhibited by one man—Art

[042-458]McMurrogh.
