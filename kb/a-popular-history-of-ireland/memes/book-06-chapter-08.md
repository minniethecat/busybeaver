CHAPTER VIII.

[045-001]THE AGE AND RULE OF GERALD, EIGHTH EARL OF KILDARE—THE TIDE BEGINS TO

[045-002]TURN FOR THE ENGLISH INTEREST—THE YORKIST PRETENDERS, SIMNEL AND

[045-003]WARBECK—POYNING'S PARLIAMENT—BATTLES OF KNOCKDOE AND MONABRAHER.

[045-004]Perhaps no preface could better introduce to the reader the singular

[045-005]events which marked the times of Gerald, eighth Earl of Kildare, than a

[045-006]brief account of one of his principal partizans—Sir James Keating,

[045-007]Prior of the Knights of St. John. The family of Keating, of

[045-008]Norman-Irish origin, were most numerous in the fifteenth century in

[045-009]Kildare, from which they afterwards spread into Tipperary and Limerick.

[045-010]Sir James Keating, "a mere Irishman," became Prior of Kilmainham about

[045-011]the year 1461, at which time Sir Robert Dowdal, deputy to the Lord

[045-012]Treasurer, complained in Parliament, that being on a pilgrimage to one

[045-013]of the shrines of the Pale, he was assaulted near Cloniff, by the

[045-014]Prior, with a drawn sword, and thereby put in danger of his life. It

[045-015]was accordingly decreed that Keating should pay to the King a hundred

[045-016]pounds fine, and to Sir Robert a hundred marks; but, from certain

[045-017]technical errors in the proceedings, he successfully evaded both these

[045-018]penalties. When in the year 1478 the Lord Grey of Codner was sent over

[045-019]to supersede Kildare, he took the decided step of refusing to surrender

[045-020]to that nobleman the Castle of Dublin, of which he was Constable. Being

[045-021]threatened with an assault, he broke down the bridge and prepared his

[045-022]defence, while his friend, the Earl of Kildare, called a Parliament at

[045-023]Naas, in opposition to Lord Grey's Assembly at Dublin. In 1480, after

[045-024]two years of rival parties and viceroys, Lord Grey was feign to resign

[045-025]his office, and Kildare was regularly appointed Deputy to Richard, Duke

[045-026]of Gloucester, afterwards Richard III. Two years later, Keating was

[045-027]deprived of his rank by Peter d'Aubusson, Grand Master of Rhodes, who

[045-028]appointed Sir Marmaduke Lumley, an English knight, in his stead. Sir

[045-029]Marmaduke landed soon after at Clontarf, where he was taken prisoner by

[045-030]Keating, and kept in close confinement until he had surrendered all the

[045-031]instruments of his election and confirmation. He was then enlarged, and

[045-032]appointed to the commandery of Kilseran, near Castlebellingham, in

[045-033]Louth. In the year 1488, Keating was one of those who took an active

[045-034]part in favour of the pretender Lambert Simnel, and although his pardon

[045-035]had been sternly refused by Henry VII., he retained possession of the

[045-036]Hospital until 1491, when he was ejected by force, "and ended his

[045-037]turbulent life," as we are told, "in the most abject poverty and

[045-038]disgrace." All whom he had appointed to office were removed; an Act of

[045-039]Parliament was passed, prohibiting the reception of any "mere Irishman"

[045-040]into the Order for the future, and enacting that whoever was recognized

[045-041]as Prior by the Grand Master should be of English birth, and one having

[045-042]such a connection with the Order there as might strengthen the force

[045-043]and interest of the Kings of England in Ireland.

[045-044]The fact most indicative of the spirit of the times is, that a man of

[045-045]Prior Keating's disposition could, for thirty years, have played such a

[045-046]daring part as we have described in the city of Dublin. During the

[045-047]greater part of that period, he held the office of Constable of the

[045-048]Castle and Prior of Kilmainham, in defiance of English Deputies and

[045-049]English Kings; than which no farther evidence may be adduced to show

[045-050]how completely the English, interest was extinguished, even within the

[045-051]walls of Dublin, during the reign of the last of the Plantagenet

[045-052]Princes, and the first years of Henry VII.

[045-053]In 1485, Henry, Earl of Richmond, grandson of Queen Catherine and Owen

[045-054]ap Tudor, returned from his fourteen years' exile in France, and, by

[045-055]the victory of Bosworth, took possession of the throne. The Earl of

[045-056]Kildare, undisputed Deputy during the last years of Edward IV., had

[045-057]been continued by Richard, and was not removed by Henry VII. Though a

[045-058]staunch Yorkist, he showed no outward opposition to the change of

[045-059]dynasty, for which he found a graceful apology soon afterwards. Being

[045-060]at Mass, in Christ's Church Cathedral, on the 2nd of February, 1486, he

[045-061]received intelligence of Henry's marriage with Elizabeth of York, which

[045-062]he at once communicated to the Archbishop of Dublin, and ordered an

[045-063]additional Mass for the King and Queen. Yet, from the hour of that

[045-064]union of the houses of York and Lancaster, it needed no extraordinary

[045-065]wisdom to foresee that the exemption of the Anglo-Irish nobles from the

[045-066]supremacy of their nominal King must come to an end, and the freedom of

[045-067]the old Irish from any formidable external danger must also close. The

[045-068]union of the Roses, so full of the promise of peace for England, was to

[045-069]form the date of a new era in her relations with Ireland. The tide of

[045-070]English power was at that hour at its lowest ebb; it had left far in

[045-071]the interior the landmarks of its first irresistible rush; it might be

[045-072]said, without exaggeration, that Gaelic children now gathered shells

[045-073]and pebbles where that tide once rolled, charged with all its thunders;

[045-074]it was now about to turn; the first murmuring menace of new

[045-075]encroachments began to be heard under Henry VII.; as we listen they

[045-076]grow louder on the ear; the waves advance with a steady, deliberate

[045-077]march, unlike the first impetuous onslaught of the Normans; they

[045-078]advance and do not recede, till they recover all the ground they had

[045-079]abandoned. The era which we dated from the Red Earl's death, in 1333,

[045-080]has exhausted its resources of aggression and assimilation; a new era

[045-081]opens with the reign of Henry VII.—or more distinctly still, with that

[045-082]of his successor, Henry VIII. We must close our account with the old

[045-083]era, before entering upon the new.

[045-085]The contest between the Earl of Kildare and Lord Grey for the

[045-086]government (1478-1480) marks the lowest ebb of the English power. We

[045-087]have already related how Prior Keating shut the Castle gates on the

[045-088]English deputy, and threatened to fire on his guard if he attempted to

[045-089]force them. Lord Portlester also, the Chancellor, and father-in-law to

[045-090]Kildare, joined that Earl in his Parliament at Naas with the great

[045-091]seal. Lord Grey, in his Dublin Assembly, declared the great seal

[045-092]cancelled, and ordered a new one to be struck, but after a two years'

[045-093]contest he was obliged to succumb to the greater influence of the

[045-094]Geraldines. Kildare was regularly acknowledged Lord Deputy, under the

[045-095]King's privy seal. It was ordained that thereafter there should be but

[045-096]one Parliament convoked during the year; that but one subsidy should be

[045-097]demanded, annually, the sum "not to exceed a thousand marks." Certain

[045-098]Acts of both Parliaments—Grey's and Kildare's—were by compromise

[045-099]confirmed. Of these were two which do not seem to collate very well

[045-100]with each other; one prohibiting the inhabitants of the Pale from

[045-101]holding any intercourse whatsoever with the mere Irish; the other

[045-102]extending to Con O'Neil, Prince of Tyrone, and brother-in-law of

[045-103]Kildare, the rights of a naturalized subject within the Pale. The

[045-104]former was probably Lord Grey's; the latter was Lord Kildare's

[045-105]legislation.

[045-106]Although Henry VII. had neither disturbed the Earl in his governments,

[045-107]nor his brother, Lord Thomas, as Chancellor, it was not to be expected

[045-108]that he could place entire confidence in the leading Yorkist family

[045-109]among the Anglo-Irish. The restoration of the Ormond estates, in favour

[045-110]of Thomas, seventh Earl, was both politic and just, and could hardly be

[045-111]objectionable to Kildare, who had just married one of his daughters to

[045-112]Pierce Butler, nephew and heir to Thomas. The want of confidence

[045-113]between the new King and his Deputy was first exhibited in 1486, when

[045-114]the Earl, being summoned to attend on his Majesty, called a Parliament

[045-115]at Trim, which voted him an address, representing that in the affairs

[045-116]about to be discussed, his presence was absolutely necessary. Henry

[045-117]affected to accept the excuse as valid, but every arrival of Court news

[045-118]contained some fresh indication of his deep-seated mistrust of the Lord

[045-119]Deputy, who, however, he dared not yet dismiss.

[045-120]The only surviving Yorkists who could put forward pretensions to the

[045-121]throne were the Earl of Lincoln, Richard's declared heir, and the young

[045-122]Earl of Warwick, son of that Duke of Clarence who was born in Dublin

[045-123]Castle in 1449. Lincoln, with Lord Lovell and others of his friends,

[045-124]was in exile at the court of the dowager Duchess of Burgundy, sister to

[045-125]Edward IV.; and the son of Clarence—a lad of fifteen years of age—was a

[045-126]prisoner in the Tower. In the year 1486, a report spread of the escape

[045-127]of this Prince, and soon afterwards Richard Symon, a Priest of Oxford,

[045-128]landed in Dublin with a youth of the same age, of prepossessing

[045-129]appearance and address, who could relate with the minutest detail the

[045-130]incidents of his previous imprisonment. He was at once recognized as

[045-131]the son of Clarence by the Earl of Kildare and his party, and

[045-132]preparations were made for his coronation by the title of Edward VI.

[045-133]Henry, alarmed, produced from the Tower the genuine Warwick, whom he

[045-134]publicly paraded through London, in order to prove that the pretender

[045-135]in Dublin was an impostor. The Duchess of Burgundy, however, fitted out

[045-136]a fleet, containing 2,000 veteran troops, under the command of Martin

[045-137]Swart, who, sailing up the channel, reached Dublin without

[045-138]interruption. With this fleet came the Earl of Lincoln, Lord Lovell,

[045-139]and the other English refugees, who all recognized the _protege_ of

[045-140]Father Symon as the true Prince. Octavius, the Italian Archbishop of

[045-141]Armagh, then residing at Dublin, the Bishop of Clogher, the Butlers,

[045-142]and the Baron of Howth, were incredulous or hostile. The great majority

[045-143]of the Anglo-Irish lords, spiritual and temporal, favoured his cause,

[045-144]and he was accordingly crowned in Christ Church Cathedral, with a

[045-145]diadem taken from an image of our Lady, on the 24th of May, 1487; the

[045-146]Deputy, Chancellor, and Treasurer were present; the sermon was preached

[045-147]by Pain, Bishop of Meath. A Parliament was next convoked in his name,

[045-148]in which the Butlers and citizens of Waterford were proscribed as

[045-149]traitors. A herald from the latter city, who had spoken over boldly,

[045-150]was hanged by the Dubliners as a proof of their loyalty. The Council

[045-151]ordered a force to be equipped for the service of his new Majesty in

[045-152]England, and Lord Thomas Fitzgerald resigned the Chancellorship to take

[045-153]the command. This expedition—the last which invaded England from the

[045-154]side of Ireland—sailed from Dublin about the first of June, and landing

[045-155]on the Lancashire shore, at the pile of Foudray, marched to Ulverstone,

[045-156]where they were joined by Sir Thomas Broughton and other devoted

[045-157]Yorkists. From Ulverstone the whole force, about 8,000 strong, marched

[045-158]into Yorkshire, and from Yorkshire southwards into Nottingham. Henry,

[045-159]who had been engaged in making a progress through the southern

[045-160]counties, hastened to meet him, and both armies met at

[045-161]Stoke-upon-Trent, near Newark, on the 16th day of June, 1487. The

[045-162]battle was contested with the utmost obstinacy, but the English

[045-163]prevailed. The Earl of Lincoln, the Lords Thomas and Maurice

[045-164]Fitzgerald, Plunkett, son of Lord Killeen, Martin Swart, and Sir Thomas

[045-165]Broughton were slain; Lord Lovell escaped, but was never heard of

[045-166]afterwards; the pretended Edward VI. was captured, and spared by Henry

[045-167]only to be made a scullion in his kitchen. Father Symon was cast into

[045-168]prison, where he died, after having confessed that his _protege_ was

[045-169]Lambert Simnel, the son of a joiner at Oxford.

[045-170]Nothing shows the strength of the Kildare party, and the weakness of

[045-171]the English interest, more than that the deputy and his partizans were

[045-172]still continued in office. They despatched a joint letter to the King,

[045-173]deprecating his anger, which he was prudent enough to conceal. He sent

[045-174]over, the following spring, Sir Richard Edgecombe, Comptroller of his

[045-175]household, accompanied by a guard of 500 men. Sir Richard first touched

[045-176]at Kinsale, where he received the homage of the Lords Barry and de

[045-177]Courcy; he then sailed to Waterford, where he delivered to the Mayor

[045-178]royal letters confirming the city in its privileges, and authorizing

[045-179]its merchants to seize and distress those of Dublin, unless they made

[045-180]their submission. After leaving Waterford, he landed at Malahide,

[045-181]passing by Dublin, to which he proceeded by land, accompanied with his

[045-182]guard. The Earl of Kildare was absent on a pilgrimage, from which he

[045-183]did not return for several days. His first interviews with Edgecombe

[045-184]were cold and formal, but finally on the 21st of July, after eight or

[045-185]ten days' disputation, the Earl and the other lords of his party did

[045-186]homage to King Henry, in the great chamber of his town-house in Thomas

[045-187]Court, and thence proceeding to the chapel, took the oath of allegiance

[045-188]on the consecrated host. With this submission Henry was fain to be

[045-189]content; Kildare, Portlester, and Plunkett were continued in office.

[045-190]The only one to whom the King's pardon was persistently refused was Sir

[045-191]James Keating, Prior of Kilmainham.

[045-192]In the subsequent attempts of Perkin Warbeck (1492-1499), in the

[045-193]character of Richard, Duke of York, one of the Princes murdered in the

[045-194]tower by Richard III., the Anglo-Irish took a less active part. Warbeck

[045-195]landed at Cork from Lisbon, and despatched letters to the Earls of

[045-196]Kildare and Desmond, to which they returned civil but evasive replies.

[045-197]At Cork he received an invitation from the King of France to visit that

[045-198]country, where he remained till the conclusion of peace between France

[045-199]and England. He then retired to Burgundy, where he was cordially

[045-200]received by the Duchess; after an unsuccessful descent on the coast of

[045-201]Kent, he took refuge in Scotland, where he married a lady closely

[045-202]allied to the crown. In 1497 he again tried his fortune in the South of

[045-203]Ireland, was joined by Maurice, tenth Earl of Desmond, the Lord Barry,

[045-204]and the citizens of Cork. Having laid siege to Waterford, he was

[045-205]compelled to retire with loss, and Desmond having made his peace with

[045-206]Henry, Warbeck was forced again to fly into Scotland. In 1497 and '8,

[045-207]he made new attempts to excite insurrection in his favour in the north

[045-208]of England and in Cornwall. He was finally taken and put to death on

[045-209]the 16th of November, 1499. With him suffered his first and most

[045-210]faithful adherent, John Waters, who had been Mayor of Cork at his first

[045-211]landing from Lisbon, in 1492, and who is ignorantly or designedly

[045-212]called by Henry's partizan "O'Water." History has not yet positively

[045-213]established the fraudulency of this pretender. A late eminently

[045-214]cautious writer, with all the evidence which modern research has

[045-215]accumulated, speaks of him as "one of the most mysterious persons in

[045-216]English history;" and in mystery we must leave him.

[045-217]We have somewhat anticipated events, in other quarters, in order to

[045-218]dispose of both the Yorkist pretenders at the same time. The situation

[045-219]of the Earls of Kildare in this and the next reign, though full of

[045-220]grandeur, was also full of peril. Within the Pale they had one part to

[045-221]play, without the Pale another. Within the Pale they held one language,

[045-222]without it another. At Dublin they were English Earls, beyond the Boyne

[045-223]or the Barrow, they were Irish chiefs. They had to tread their

[045-224]cautious, and not always consistent way, through the endless

[045-225]complications which must arise between two nations occupying the same

[045-226]soil, with conflicting allegiance, language, laws, customs, and

[045-227]interests. While we frequently feel indignant at the tone they take

[045-228]towards the "Irish enemy" in their despatches to London—the pretended

[045-229]enemies being at that very time their confidants and allies—on farther

[045-230]reflection we feel disposed to make some allowance on the score of

[045-231]circumstance and necessity, for a duplicity which, in the end, brought

[045-232]about, as duplicity in public affairs ever does, its own punishment.

[045-233]In Ulster as well as in Leinster, the ascendency of the Earl of Kildare

[045-234]over the native population was widespread and long sustained. Con

[045-235]O'Neil, Lord of Tyrone, from 1483 to 1491, and Turlogh, Con and Art,

[045-236]his sons and successors (from 1498 to 1548), maintained the most

[045-237]intimate relations with this Earl and his successors. To the former he

[045-238]was brother-in-law, and to the latter, of course, uncle; to all he

[045-239]seems to have been strongly attached. Hugh Roe O'Donnell, Lord of

[045-240]Tyrconnell (1450-1505), and his son and successor, Hugh Dhu O'Donnell,

[045-241](1505-1530), were also closely connected with Kildare both by

[045-242]friendship and intermarriage. In 1491, O'Neil and O'Donnell mutually

[045-243]submitted their disputes to his decision, at his Castle of Maynooth,

[045-244]and though he found it impossible to reconcile them at the moment, we

[045-245]find both of these houses cordially united with him afterwards. In

[045-246]1498, he took Dungannon and Omagh, "with great guns," from the

[045-247]insurgents against the authority of his grandson, Turlogh O'Neil, and

[045-248]restored them to Turlogh; the next year he visited O'Donnell, and

[045-249]brought his son Henry to be fostered among the kindly Irish of

[045-250]Tyrconnell. In the year 1500 he also placed the Castle of Kinnaird in

[045-251]the custody of Turlogh O'Neil. In Leinster, the Geraldine interest was

[045-252]still more entirely bound up with that of the native population. His

[045-253]son, Sir Oliver of Killeigh, married an O'Conor of Offally; the

[045-254]daughter of another son, Sir James of Leixlip, (sometimes called the

[045-255]Knight of the Valley) became the wife of the chief of Imayle. The Earl

[045-256]of Ormond, and Ulick Burke of Clanrickarde, were also sons-in-law of

[045-257]the eighth Earl, but in both these cases the old family feuds survived

[045-258]in despite of the new family alliances.

[045-259]In the fourth year after his accession, Henry VII., proceeding by slow

[045-260]degrees to undermine Kildare's enormous power, summoned the chief

[045-261]Anglo-Irish nobles to his Court at Greenwich, where he reproached them

[045-262]with their support of Simnel, who, to their extreme confusion, he

[045-263]caused to wait on them as butler, at dinner. A year or two afterwards,

[045-264]he removed Lord Portlester, from the Treasurership, which he conferred

[045-265]on Sir James Butler, the bastard of Ormond. Plunkett, the

[045-266]Chief-Justice, was promoted to the Chancellorship, and Kildare himself

[045-267]was removed to make way for Fitzsymons, Archbishop of Dublin. This,

[045-268]however, was but a government _ad interim_, for in the year 1494, a

[045-269]wholly English administration was appointed. Sir Edward Poynings, with

[045-270]a picked force of 1,000 men, was appointed Lord Deputy; the Bishop of

[045-271]Bangor was appointed Chancellor, Sir Hugh Conway, an Englishman, was to

[045-272]be Treasurer; and these officials were accompanied by an entirely new

[045-273]bench of judges, all English, whom they were instructed to instal

[045-274]immediately on their arrival. Kildare had resisted the first changes

[045-275]with vigour, and a bloody feud had taken place between his retainers

[045-276]and those of Sir James of Ormond, on the green of Oxmantown—now

[045-277]Smithfield, in Dublin. On the arrival of Poynings, however, he

[045-278]submitted with the best possible grace, and accompanied that deputy to

[045-279]Drogheda, where he had summoned a Parliament to meet him. From

[045-280]Drogheda, they made an incursion into O'Hanlon's country (Orior in

[045-281]Armagh). On returning from Drogheda, Poynings, on a real or pretended

[045-282]discovery of a secret understanding between O'Hanlon and Kildare,

[045-283]arrested the latter, in Dublin, and at once placed him on board a

[045-284]barque "kept waiting for that purpose," and despatched him to England.

[045-285]On reaching London, he was imprisoned in the Tower, for two years,

[045-286]during which time his party in Ireland were left headless and

[045-287]dispirited.

[045-289]The government of Sir Edward Poynings, which lasted from 1494 till

[045-290]Kildare's restoration, in August, 1496, is most memorable for the

[045-291]character of its legislation. He assembled a Parliament at Drogheda, in

[045-292]November, 1495, at which were passed the statutes so celebrated in our

[045-293]Parliamentary history as the "10th Henry VII." These statutes were the

[045-294]first enacted in Ireland in which the English language was employed.

[045-295]They confirmed the Provisions of the Statute of Kilkenny, except that

[045-296]prohibiting the use of the Irish language, which had now become so

[045-297]deeply rooted, even within the Pale, as to make its immediate abolition

[045-298]impracticable. The hospitable law passed in the time of Richard, Duke

[045-299]of York, against the arrest of refugees by virtue of writs issued in

[045-300]England, was repealed. The English acts, against provisors to

[045-301]Rome—ecclesiastics who applied for or accepted preferment directly from

[045-302]Rome—were adopted. It was also enacted that all offices should be held

[045-303]at the King's pleasure; that the Lords of Parliament should appear in

[045-304]their robes as the Lords did in England; that no one should presume to

[045-305]make peace or war except with license of the Governor; that no great

[045-306]guns should be kept in the fortresses except by similar license; and

[045-307]that men of English _birth_ only should be appointed Constables of the

[045-308]Castles of Dublin, Trim, Leixlip, Athlone, Wicklow, Greencastle,

[045-309]Carlingford, and Carrickfergus. But the most important measure of all

[045-310]was one which provided that thereafter no legislation whatever should

[045-311]be proceeded with in Ireland, unless the bills to be proposed were

[045-312]first submitted to the King and Council in England, and were returned,

[045-313]certified under the great seal of the realm. This is what is usually

[045-314]and specially called in our Parliamentary history "Poyning's Act," and

[045-315]next to the Statute of Kilkenny, it may be considered the most

[045-316]important enactment ever passed at any Parliament of the English

[045-317]settlers.

[045-318]The liberation of the Earl of Kildare from the Tower, and his

[045-319]restoration as Deputy, seems to have been hastened by the movements of

[045-320]Perkin Warbeck, and by the visit of Hugh Roe O'Donnell to James IV.,

[045-321]King of Scotland. O'Donnell had arrived at Ayr in the month of August,

[045-322]1495, a few weeks after Warbeck had reached that court. He was received

[045-323]with great splendour and cordiality by the accomplished Prince, then

[045-324]lately come of age, and filled with projects natural to his youth and

[045-325]temperament. With O'Donnell, according to the Four Masters, he formed a

[045-326]league, by which they bound themselves "mutually to assist each other

[045-327]in all their exigencies." The knowledge of this alliance, and of

[045-328]Warbeck's favour at the Scottish Court, no doubt decided Henry to avail

[045-329]himself, if possible, of the assistance of his most powerful Irish

[045-330]subject. There was, moreover, another influence at work. The first

[045-331]countess had died soon after her husband's arrest, and he now married,

[045-332]in England, Elizabeth St. John, cousin to the King. Fortified in his

[045-333]allegiance and court favour by this alliance, he returned in triumph to

[045-334]Dublin, where he was welcomed with enthusiasm.

[045-335]In his subsequent conduct as Lord Deputy, an office which he continued

[045-336]to hold till his death in 1513, this powerful nobleman seems to have

[045-337]steadily upheld the English interest, which was now in harmony with his

[045-338]own. Having driven off Warbeck in his last visit to Ireland (1497), he

[045-339]received extensive estates in England, as a reward for his zeal, and

[045-340]after the victory of Knock-doe (1505), he was installed by proxy at

[045-341]Windsor as Knight of the Garter. This long-continued reign—for such in

[045-342]truth it may be called—left him without a rival in his latter years. He

[045-343]marched to whatever end of the island he would, pulling down and

[045-344]setting up chiefs and castles; his garrisons were to be found from

[045-345]Belfast to Cork, and along the valley of the Shannon, from Athleague to

[045-346]Limerick.

[045-347]The last event of national importance connected with the name of Geroit

[045-348]More arose out of the battle of KNOCK-DOE, ("battle-axe hill"), fought

[045-349]within seven or eight miles of Galway town, on the 19th of August,

[045-350]1504. Few of the cardinal facts in our history have been more entirely

[045-351]misapprehended and misrepresented than this. It is usually described as

[045-352]a pitched battle between English and Irish—the turning point in the war

[045-353]of races—and the second foundation of English power. The simple

[045-354]circumstances are these: Ulick III., Lord of Clanrickarde, had married

[045-355]and misused the lady Eustacia Fitzgerald, who seems to have fled to her

[045-356]father, leaving her children behind. This led to an embittered family

[045-357]dispute, which was expanded into a public quarrel by the complaint of

[045-358]William O'Kelly, whose Castles of Garbally, Monivea, and Gallagh, Burke

[045-375]Artillery and musketry, first introduced from Germany some twenty years

[045-376]before (1487), were freely used, and the ploughshare of the peasant has

[045-377]often turned up bullets, large and small, upon the hillside where the

[045-378]battle was fought. The most credible account sets down the number of

[045-379]the slain at 2,000 men—the most exaggerated at 9,000. The victory was

[045-380]with Kildare, who, after encamping on the field for twenty-four hours,

[045-381]by the advice of O'Donnell, marched next day to Galway, where he found

[045-382]the children of Clanrickarde, whom he restored to their injured mother.

[045-383]Athenry opened its gates to receive the conquerors, and after

[045-384]celebrating their victory in the stronghold of the vanquished, the

[045-385]Ulster chiefs returned to the North, and Kildare to Dublin.

[045-386]Less known is the battle of Monabraher, which may be considered the

[045-387]offset of Knock-doe. It was fought in 1510—the first year of Henry

[045-388]VIII., who had just confirmed Lord Kildare in the government. The

[045-389]younger O'Donnell joined him in Munster, and after taking the Castles

[045-390]of Kanturk, Pallis, and Castelmaine, they marched to Limerick, where

[045-391]the Earl of Desmond, the McCarthys of both branches, and "the Irish of

[045-392]Meath and Leinster," in alliance with Kildare, joined them with their

[045-393]forces. The old allies, Turlogh O'Brien, Clanrickarde, and the

[045-394]McNamaras, attacked them at the bridge of Portrush, near Castleconnell,

[045-395]and drove them through Monabraher ("the friar's bog"), with the loss of

[045-396]the Barons Barnwall and Kent, and many of their forces; the survivors

[045-397]were feign to take refuge within the walls of Limerick.

[045-398]Three years later, Earl Gerald set out to besiege Leap Castle, in

[045-399]O'Moore's country; but it happened that as he was watering his horse in

[045-400]the little river Greese, at Kilkea, he was shot by one of the O'Moores:

[045-401]he was immediately carried to Athy, where shortly afterwards he

[045-402]expired. If we except the first Hugh de Lacy and the Red Earl of

[045-403]Ulster, the Normans in Ireland had not produced a more illustrious man

[045-404]than Gerald, eighth Earl of Kildare. He was, says Stainhurst, "of tall

[045-405]stature and goodly presence; very liberal and merciful; of strict

[045-406]piety; mild in his government; passionate, but easily appeased." And

[045-407]our justice-loving _Four Masters_ have described him as "a knight in

[045-408]valour, and princely and religious in his words and judgments."
