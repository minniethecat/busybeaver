## Overview

Henry Ireton (1611–1651) was the Puritan commander of the Parliamentarian forces in Ireland during the Cromwellian conquest, and the son-in-law of [[Oliver Cromwell]]. After Cromwell's departure from Ireland in 1650, Ireton assumed command as Lord Deputy and continued the subjugation of the country, completing the most destructive phase of the conquest. (074-010)

His marriage to Cromwell's daughter, contracted about two years before his Irish command, cemented his place in the inner circle of the Protectorate. As the executor of Cromwell's Irish policy, Ireton presided over the confiscation and settlement that would permanently reshape Irish society. (074-010) (074-011)

## Biography

### Early Career

Henry Ireton was born in 1611 in Nottinghamshire, England, to a Puritan gentry family. He studied at Trinity College, Oxford, and later at the Middle Temple, though the outbreak of the English Civil War drew him into military service rather than the law. He rose rapidly in the Parliamentarian army, distinguishing himself at the Battle of Naseby in 1645.

### Marriage and Alliance with Cromwell

Ireton's marriage to Bridget Cromwell, [[oliver-cromwell|Oliver Cromwell]]'s eldest daughter, in 1646 forged a family alliance that placed him at the very centre of the Protectorate's power. The marriage, contracted about two years before he assumed command in Ireland, made Ireton not merely a general but a member of the ruling dynasty of the English Commonwealth. (074-010) (074-011)

### The Irish Command

When Cromwell departed Ireland in May 1650, he left Ireton as Lord Deputy with the task of completing the conquest. The Puritan commander now faced the remnants of the Catholic Confederate and Royalist forces, who continued to resist despite the fall of their major strongholds.

Ireton's campaign was methodical and ruthless. He prosecuted the siege of Limerick, which fell in October 1651 after a protracted defence. However, the hardships of the campaign took their toll on Ireton himself. He died of fever at the siege of Limerick in November 1651, only weeks after the city's surrender.

### Death and Legacy of Conquest

Ireton's death did not halt the Cromwellian project in Ireland. His successors continued the policy of confiscation and transplantation that Ireton had helped to design. The Act of Settlement, passed in 1652, formalised the dispossession of Catholic landowners that Ireton's military operations had made possible.

McGee records that the policy of the Protector could have devolved upon few persons more capable of executing it than Henry Ireton — a grim tribute to the efficiency with which he carried out the subjugation of Ireland. (074-012)

## Legacy

Henry Ireton's legacy in Ireland is almost entirely negative. As the architect of the [[cromwellian-settlement|Cromwellian Settlement]], he bears responsibility for the largest single transfer of land from Catholic to Protestant hands in Irish history. The bitterness engendered by his campaign endured for centuries, becoming a central grievance in the Irish nationalist narrative.

His tomb in Westminster Abbey did not escape the judgment of history: after the [[restoration|Restoration of Charles II]] in 1660, Ireton's body was exhumed, hanged at Tyburn, and disposed of — a posthumous punishment for his role in the regicide of Charles I and the conquest of Ireland.
