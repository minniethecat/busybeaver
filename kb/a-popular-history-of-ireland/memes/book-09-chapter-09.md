CHAPTER IX.

[072-001]FROM THE BATTLE OF BENBURB TILL THE LANDING OF CROMWELL AT DUBLIN.

[072-002]The Nuncio, elated by the great victory of O'Neil, to which he felt he

[072-003]had personally contributed by his seasonable supplies, provoked and

[072-004]irritated by Ormond's intrigues and the King's insincerity, rushed with

[072-005]all the ardour of his character into making the war an uncompromising

[072-006]Catholic crusade. In this line of conduct, he was supported by the

[072-007]Archbishops of Dublin and Cashel, by ten of the Bishops, including the

[072-008]eminent Prelates of Limerick, Killalla, Ferns, and Clogher; the

[072-009]Procurator of Armagh; nine Vicars-general, and the Superiors of the

[072-010]Jesuits, Dominicans, Franciscans, and Augustinians. The peace party, on

[072-011]the other hand, were not without clerical adherents, but they were

[072-012]inconsiderable, as to influence and numbers. They were now become as

[072-013]anxious to publish the Thirty Articles agreed upon at the end of March,

[072-014]as they then were to keep them secret. Accordingly, with Ormond's

[072-015]consent, copies of the treaty were sent early in August to the sheriffs

[072-016]of counties, mayors of cities, and other leading persons, with

[072-017]instructions to proclaim it publicly in due form; upon hearing which,

[072-018]the Nuncio and his supporters of the clergy, secular and regular,

[072-019]assembled in council at Waterford, on the 12th of August, solemnly

[072-020]declared that they gave no consent, and would not, "to any peace," that

[072-021]did not grant "further, surer, and safer considerations for their

[072-022]religion, king, and country," according to the original oath of the

[072-023]Confederacy.

[072-024]The rupture between the clergy and the laymen of the Council was now

[072-025]complete. The prelates who signed the decree of Waterford, of course,

[072-026]thereby withdrew from the body whose action they condemned. In vain the

[072-027]learned Darcy and the eloquent Plunkett went to and fro between the two

[072-028]bodies: concord and confidence were at an end. The synod decided to

[072-029]address Lord Mountgarrett in future as President of "the _late_ Supreme

[072-030]Council." The heralds who attempted to publish the Thirty Articles in

[072-031]Clonmel and Waterford were hooted or stoned; while in Limerick the

[072-032]mayor, endeavouring to protect them, shared this rough usage. Ormond,

[072-033]who was at Kilkenny at the critical moment of the breach, did his

[072-034]utmost to sustain the resolution of those who were stigmatized by his

[072-035]name; while the Nuncio, suspicious of Preston, wrote urgently to O'Neil

[072-036]to lead his army into Leinster, and remove the remnant of the late

[072-037]council from Kilkenny. All that those who held a middle course between

[072-038]the extremes could do, was to advocate an early meeting of the General

[072-039]Assembly; but various exigencies delayed this much-desired meeting,

[072-040]till the 10th day of January, 1647.

[072-041]The five intervening months were months of triumph for Rinuccini. Lord

[072-042]Digby appeared at Dublin as a special agent from the King, to declare

[072-043]his consent to Glamorgan's original terms; but Ormond still insisted

[072-044]that he had no authority to go beyond the Thirty Articles. Charles

[072-045]himself wrote privately to Rinuccini, promising to confirm everything

[072-046]which Glamorgan had proposed, as soon as he should come into "the

[072-047]Nuncio's hands." Ormond, after a fruitless attempt to convert O'Neil to

[072-048]his views, had marched southward with a guard of 1,500 foot, and 500

[072-049]horse, to endeavour to conciliate the towns, and to win over the Earl

[072-050]of Inchiquin. In both these objects he failed. He found O'Neil before

[072-051]him in his county palatinate of Tipperary, and the Mayor of Cashel

[072-052]informed him that he dared not allow him into that city, for fear of

[072-053]displeasing the northern general. Finding himself thus unexpectedly

[072-054]within a few miles of "the Catholic Army," 10,000 strong, the Viceroy

[072-055]retreated precipitately through Kilkenny, Carlow, and Kildare, to

[072-056]Dublin. Lord Digby, who had accompanied him, after an unsuccessful

[072-057]attempt to cajole the Synod of Waterford, made the best of his way back

[072-058]to France; the Marquis of Clanrickarde, who had also been of the

[072-059]expedition, shared the flight of Ormond. Towards the middle of

[072-060]September, O'Neil's army, after capturing Roscrea Castle, marched to

[072-061]Kilkenny, and encamped near that city. His forces had now augmented to

[072-062]12,000 foot, and 1,500 horse; on the 18th of the month, he escorted the

[072-063]Nuncio in triumph into Kilkenny, where the Ormondist members of the old

[072-064]council were committed to close custody in the castle. A new council,

[072-065]of four bishops and eight laymen, was established on the 26th, with the

[072-066]Nuncio as president; Glamorgan succeeded Castlehaven, who had gone over

[072-067]to Ormond, as commander in Munster; while O'Neil and Preston were

[072-068]ordered to unite their forces for the siege of Dublin. The sanguine

[072-069]Italian dreamt of nothing less, for the moment, than the creation of

[072-070]Viceroys, the deliverance of the King, and the complete restoration of

[072-071]the ancient religion.

[072-072]O'Neil and Preston, by different routes, on which they were delayed in

[072-073]taking several garrisoned posts, united at Lucan in the valley of the

[072-074]Liffey, seven miles west of Dublin, on the 9th of November. Their joint

[072-075]forces are represented at 16,000 foot, and 1,600 horse—of which Preston

[072-076]had about one-third, and O'Neil the remainder. Preston's head-quarters

[072-077]were fixed at Leixlip, and O'Neil's at Newcastle—points equi-distant,

[072-078]and each within two hours' march of the capital. Within the walls of

[072-079]that city there reigned the utmost consternation. Many of the

[072-080]inhabitants fled beyond seas, terrified by the fancied cruelty of the

[072-081]Ulstermen. But Ormond retained all his presence of mind, and readiness

[072-082]of resources. He entered, at first covertly, into arrangements with the

[072-083]Parliamentarians, who sent him a supply of powder; he wrote urgently to

[072-084]Monroe to make a diversion in his favour; he demolished the mills and

[072-085]suburbs which might cover the approaches of the enemy; he employed

[072-086]soldiers, civilians, and even women, upon the fortifications,—Lady

[072-087]Ormond setting an example to her sex, in rendering her feeble

[072-088]assistance. Clanrickarde, in Preston's tent, was doing the work of

[072-089]stimulating the old antipathy of that general towards O'Neil, which led

[072-090]to conflicting advices in Council, and some irritating personal

[072-091]altercations. To add to the Confederate embarrassment, the winter was

[072-092]the most severe known for many years; from twenty to thirty sentinels

[072-093]being frozen at night at their posts. On the 13th of November, while

[072-094]the plan of the Confederate attack was still undecided, commissioners

[072-095]of the Parliament arrived, with ample stores, in Dublin Bay. On the

[072-096]next day they landed at Ringsend, and entered into negotiations with

[072-097]Ormond; on the 16th the siege was raised, and on the 23rd Ormond broke

[072-098]off the treaty, having unconsciously saved Dublin from the

[072-099]Confederates, by the incorrect reports of supplies being received,

[072-100]which were finally carried northward to Monroe.

[072-101]The month of January brought the meeting of the General Assembly. The

[072-102]attendance in the great gallery of Ormond Castle was as large, and the

[072-103]circumstances upon the whole as auspicious as could be desired, in the

[072-104]seventh year of such a struggle. The members of the old council,

[072-105]liberated from arrest, were in their places. O'Neil and Preston,

[072-106]publicly reconciled, had signed a solemn engagement to assist and

[072-107]sustain each other. The Nuncio, the Primate of Ireland, and eleven

[072-108]bishops took their seats; the peers of oldest title in the kingdom were

[072-109]present; two hundred and twenty-four members represented the Commons of

[072-110]Ireland, and among the spectators sat the ambassadors of France and

[072-111]Spain, and of King Charles. The main subject of discussion was the

[072-112]sufficiency of the Thirty Articles, and the propriety of the

[072-113]ecclesiastical censure promulgated against those who had signed them.

[072-114]The debate embraced all that may be said on the question of clerical

[072-115]interference in political affairs, on conditional and unconditional

[072-116]allegiance, on the power of the Pontiff speaking _ex cathedra_, and the

[072-117]prerogatives of the temporal sovereign. It was protracted through an

[072-118]entire month, and ended with a compromise, which declared that the

[072-119]Commissioners had acted in good faith in signing the articles, while it

[072-120]justified the Synod of Waterford for having, as judges of the nature

[072-121]and intent of the oath of Confederation, declared them insufficient and

[072-122]unacceptable. A new oath of Confederacy, solemnly binding the

[072-123]associates not to lay down their arms till they had established the

[072-124]free and public exercise of religion as it had existed in the reign of

[072-125]Henry VII., was framed and taken by the entire General Assembly; the

[072-126]Thirty Articles were declared insufficient and unacceptable by all but

[072-127]a minority of twelve votes; a new Supreme Council of twenty-four was

[072-128]chosen, in whom there were not known to be above four or five partisans

[072-129]of Ormond's policy. The church plate throughout the kingdom was ordered

[072-130]to be coined into money, and a formal proposal to co-operate with the

[072-131]Viceroy on the basis of the new oath was made, but instantly rejected;

[072-132]among other grounds, on this, that the Marquis had, at that moment, his

[072-133]son and and other sureties with the Puritans who, in the last resort,

[072-134]he infinitely preferred to the Roman Catholics.

[072-136]The military events of the year 1647 were much more decisive than its

[072-137]politics. Glamorgan still commanded in Munster, Preston in Leinster,

[072-138]and O'Neil in both Ulster and Connaught. The first was confronted by

[072-139]Inchiquin, at the head of a corps of 5,000 foot and 1,500 horse,

[072-140]equipped and supplied by the English Puritans; the second saw the

[072-141]garrisons of Dundalk, Drogheda, and Dublin, reinforced by fresh

[072-142]regiments of Covenanters, and fed by Parliamentary supplies from the

[072-143]sea; the latter was in the heart of Connaught, organizing and

[072-144]recruiting and attempting all things within his reach, but hampered for

[072-145]money, clothing and ammunition. In Connaught, O'Neil was soon joined by

[072-146]the Nuncio, who, as difficulties thickened, began to lean more and more

[072-147]on the strong arm of the victor of Benburb; in Munster, the army

[072-148]refused to follow the lead of Glamorgan, and clamoured for their old

[072-149]chief, Lord Muskerry; finally, that division of the national troops was

[072-150]committed by the Council to Lord Taafe, a politician of the school of

[072-151]Ormond and Clanrickarde, wholly destitute of military experience. The

[072-152]vigorous Inchiquin had little difficulty in dealing with such an

[072-153]antagonist; Cashel was taken without a blow in its defence, and a

[072-154]slaughter unparalleled till the days of Drogheda and Wexford, deluged

[072-155]its streets and churches. At Knocknos, later in the autumn (Nov. 12th),

[072-156]Taafe was utterly routed; the gallant _Colkitto_, serving under him,

[072-157]lamentably sacrificed after surrendering his sword; and Inchiquin

[072-158]enabled to dictate a cessation covering Munster—far less favourable to

[072-159]Catholics than the truce of Castlemartin—to the Supreme Council. This

[072-160]truce was signed at Dungarvan, on the 20th of May, 1648, and on the

[072-161]27th the Nuncio published his solemn decree of excommunication against

[072-162]all its aiders and abettors, and himself made the best of his way from

[072-163]Kilkenny to Maryboro', where O'Neil then lay.

[072-164]The military and political situation of O'Neil, during the latter

[072-165]months of 1647 and the whole of 1648, was one of the most extraordinary

[072-166]in which any general had ever been placed. His late sworn colleague,

[072-167]Preston, was now combined with Inchiquin against him; the royalist

[072-168]Clanrickarde, in the western counties, pressed upon his rear, and

[072-169]captured his garrison in Athlone; the Parliamentary general, Michael

[072-170]Jones, to whom Ormond had finally surrendered Dublin, observed rather

[072-171]than impeded his movements in Leinster; the lay majority of the Supreme

[072-172]Council proclaimed him a traitor—a compliment which he fully returned;

[072-173]the Nuncio threw himself wholly into his hands; finally, at the close

[072-174]of '48, Ormond, returning from France to Ireland, concluded, on the

[072-175]17th of January, a formal alliance with the lay members, under the

[072-176]title of "Commissioners of Trust," for the King and Kingdom; and

[072-177]Rinuccini, despairing, perhaps, of a cause so distracted, sailed in his

[072-178]own frigate, from Galway, on the 23rd of February. Thus did the actors

[072-179]change their parts, alternately triumphing and fleeing for safety. The

[072-180]verdict of history may condemn the Nuncio, of whom we have now seen the

[072-181]last, for his imperious self-will, and his too ready recourse to

[072-182]ecclesiastical censures; but of his zeal, his probity, and his

[072-183]disinterestedness, there can be, we think, no second opinion.

[072-184]Under the treaty of 1649—which conceded full civil and religious

[072-185]equality to the Roman Catholics—Ormond was once more placed at the head

[072-186]of the government and in command of the royal troops. A few days after

[072-187]the signing of that treaty, news of the execution of Charles I. having

[072-188]reached Ireland, the Viceroy proclaimed the Prince of Wales by the

[072-189]title of Charles II., at Cork and Youghal. Prince Rupert, whose fleet

[072-190]had entered Kinsale, caused the same ceremony to be gone through in

[072-191]that ancient borough. With Ormond were now cordially united Preston,

[072-192]Inchiquin, Clanrickarde, and Muskerry, on whom the lead of the Supreme

[072-193]Council devolved, in consequence of the advanced age of Lord

[072-194]Mountgarrett, and the remainder of the twelve Commissioners of Trust.

[072-195]The cause of the young Prince, an exile, the son of that Catholic queen

[072-196]from whom they had expected so much, was far from unpopular in the

[072-197]southern half of the island. The Anglican interest was strong and

[072-198]widely diffused through both Leinster and Munster; and, except a

[072-199]resolute prelate, like Dr. French, Bishop of Ferns, or a brave band of

[072-200]townsmen like those of Waterford, Limerick, and Galway, or some remnant

[072-201]of mountain tribes, in Wicklow and Tipperary, the national, or "old

[072-202]Irish policy," had decidedly lost ground from the hour of the Nuncio's

[072-203]departure.

[072-204]Owen O'Neil and the Bishops still adhered to that national policy. The

[072-205]former made a three-months' truce with General Monck, who had succeeded

[072-206]Monroe in the command of all the Parliamentary troops in his province.

[072-207]The singular spectacle was even exhibited of Monck forwarding supplies

[072-208]to O'Neil, to be used against Inchiquin and Ormond, and O'Neil coining

[072-209]to the rescue of Coote, and raising for him the siege of Londonderry.

[072-210]Inchiquin, in rapid succession, took Drogheda, Trim, Dundalk, Newry,

[072-211]and then rapidly countermarched to join Ormond in besieging Dublin. At

[072-212]Rathmines, near the city, both generals were surprised and defeated by

[072-213]the Parliamentarians under Michael Jones. Between desertions, and

[072-214]killed and wounded, they lost, by their own account, nearly 3,000, and

[072-215]by the Puritan accounts, above 5,000 men. This action was the virtual

[072-216]close of Ormond's military career; he never after made head against the

[072-217]Parliamentary forces in open field. The Catholic cities of Limerick and

[072-218]Galway refused to admit his garrisons; a synod of the Bishops,

[072-219]assembled at Jamestown (in Roscommon), strongly recommended his

[072-220]withdrawal from the kingdom; and Cromwell had arrived, resolved to

[072-221]finish the war in a single campaign. Ormond sailed again for France,

[072-222]before the end of 1649, to return no more until the restoration of the

[072-223]monarchy, on the death of the great Protector.
