CHAPTER XVI.

[103-001]THE INSURRECTION OF 1798—THE WEXFORD INSURRECTION.

[103-002]The most formidable insurrection, indeed the only really formidable

[103-003]one, broke out in the county of Wexford, a county in which it was

[103-004]stated there were not 200 sworn United Irishmen, and which Lord Edward

[103-005]Fitzgerald had altogether omitted from his official list of counties

[103-006]organized in the month of February. In that brief interval, the

[103-007]Government policy of provocation had the desired effect, though the

[103-008]explosion was of a nature to startle those who occasioned it.

[103-009]Wexford, geographically, is a peculiar county, and its people are a

[103-010]peculiar people. The county fills up the south-eastern corner of the

[103-011]island, with the sea south-east, the river Barrow to the west, and the

[103-012]woods and mountains of Carlow and Wicklow to the north. It is about

[103-013]forty miles long by twenty-four broad; the surface undulating and

[103-014]rising into numerous groups of detached hills, two or more of which are

[103-015]generally visible from each conspicuous summit. Almost in the midst

[103-016]flows the river Slaney, springing from a lofty Wicklow peak, which

[103-017]sends down on its northern slope the better known river Liffey. On the

[103-018]estuary of the Slaney, some seventy miles south of Dublin, stands the

[103-019]county town, the traveller journeying to which by the usual route then

[103-020]taken, passed in succession through Arklow, Gorey, Ferns, Enniscorthy,

[103-021]and other places of less consequence, though familiar enough in the

[103-022]fiery records of 1798. North-westward, the only road in those days from

[103-023]Carlow and Kilkenny, crossed the Blackstairs at Scollagh-gap, entering

[103-024]the county at Newtownbarry, the ancient Bunclody; westward, some twenty

[103-025]miles, on the river Barrow, stands New Ross, often mentioned in this

[103-026]history, the road from which to the county town passes through

[103-027]Scullabogue and Taghmon (_Ta'mun_), the former at the foot of

[103-028]Carrickbyrne rock, the latter at the base of what is rather

[103-029]hyperbolically called "the _mountain_ of Forth." South and west of the

[103-030]town, towards the estuary of Waterford, lie the baronies of Forth and

[103-031]Bargy, a great part of the population of which, even within our own

[103-032]time, spoke the language Chaucer and Spenser wrote, and retained many

[103-033]of the characteristics of their Saxon, Flemish, and Cambrian ancestors.

[103-034]Through this singular district lay the road towards Duncannon fort, on

[103-035]Waterford harbour, with branches running off to Bannow, Ballyhack, and

[103-036]Dunbrody. We shall, therefore, speak of all the localities we may have

[103-037]occasion to mention as on or near one of the four main roads of the

[103-038]county, the Dublin, Carlow, Boss, and Waterford roads.

[103-039]The population of this territory was variously estimated in 1798, at

[103-040]150,000, 180,000, and 200,000. They were, generally speaking, a

[103-041]comfortable and contented peasantry, for the Wexford landlords were

[103-042]seldom absentees, and the farmers held under them by long leases and

[103-043]reasonable rents. There were in the country few great lords, but there

[103-044]was little poverty and no pauperism. In such a soil, the secret

[103-045]societies were almost certain to fail, and if it had not been for the

[103-046]diabolical experiments of Lord Kingsborough's North Cork Militia, it is

[103-047]very probable that that orderly and thrifty population would have seen

[103-048]the eventful year we are describing pass over their homes without

[103-049]experiencing any of the terrible trials which accompanied it. But it

[103-050]was impossible for human nature to endure the provocations inflicted

[103-051]upon this patient and prosperous people. The pitch-cap and the triangle

[103-052]were resorted to on the slightest and most frivolous pretexts. "A

[103-053]sergeant of the North Cork Militia," says Mr. Hay, the county

[103-054]historian, "nicknamed, _Tom the Devil_, was most ingenious in devising

[103-055]new modes of torture. Moistened gunpowder was frequently rubbed into

[103-056]the hair cut close and then set on fire; some, while shearing for this

[103-057]purpose, had the tips of their ears snipt off; sometimes an entire ear,

[103-058]and often both ears were completely cut off; and many lost part of

[103-059]their noses during the like preparation. But, strange to tell," adds

[103-060]Mr. Hay, "these atrocities were publicly practised without the least

[103-061]reserve in open day, and no magistrate or officer ever interfered, but

[103-062]shamefully connived at this extraordinary mode of quieting the people!

[103-063]Some of the miserable sufferers on these shocking occasions, or some of

[103-064]their relations or friends, actuated by a principle of retaliation, if

[103-065]not of revenge, cut short the hair of several persons whom they either

[103-066]considered as enemies or suspected of having pointed them out as

[103-067]objects for such desperate treatment. This was done with a view that

[103-068]those active citizens should fall in for a little experience of the

[103-069]like discipline, or to make the fashion of short hair so general that

[103-070]it might no longer be a mark of party distinction." This was the origin

[103-071]of the nickname "Croppy," by which, during the remainder of the

[103-072]insurrection, it was customary to designate all who were suspected or

[103-073]proved to be hostile to, the government.

[103-074]Among the magistracy of the county were several persons who, whatever

[103-075]might have been their conduct in ordinary times, now showed themselves

[103-076]utterly unfit to be entrusted with those large discretionary powers

[103-077]which Parliament had recently conferred upon all justices of the peace.

[103-078]One of these magistrates, surrounded by his troops, perambulated the

[103-079]county with an executioner, armed with all the equipments of his

[103-080]office; another carried away the lopped hands and fingers of his

[103-081]victims, with which he stirred his punch in the carousals that followed

[103-082]every expedition. At Carnew, midway between the Dublin and Carlow

[103-083]roads, on the second day of the insurrection, twenty-eight prisoners

[103-084]were brought out to be shot at as targets in the public ball alley; on

[103-085]the same day Enniscorthy witnessed its first execution for treason, and

[103-086]the neighbourhood of Ballaghkeen was harried by Mr. Jacob, one of the

[103-087]magistrates whose method of preserving the peace of the county has been

[103-088]just referred to. The majority of the bench, either weakly or

[103-089]willingly, sanctioned these atrocities, but some others, among them a

[103-090]few of the first men in the county, did not hesitate to resist and

[103-091]condemn them. Among these were Mr. Beauchamp Bagenal Harvey of Bargy

[103-092]Castle, Mr. Fitzgerald of Newpark, and Mr. John Henry Colclough of

[103-093]Tintern Abbey; but all these gentlemen were arrested on Saturday, the

[103-094]26th of May—the same day, or more strictly speaking, the eve of the day

[103-095]on which the Wexford outbreak occurred.

[103-097]On the day succeeding these arrests, being Whitsunday, Father John

[103-098]Murphy, parish priest of Kilcormick, the son of a small farmer of the

[103-099]neighbourhood, educated in Spain, on coming to his little wayside

[103-100]chapel, found it laid in ashes. To his flock, as they surrounded him in

[103-101]the open air, he boldly preached that it would be much better for them

[103-102]to die in a fair field than to await the tortures inflicted by such

[103-103]magistrates as Archibald Jacob, Hunter Gowan, and Hawtrey White. He

[103-104]declared his readiness to share their fate, whatever it might be, and

[103-105]in response, about 2,000 of the country people gathered in a few hours

[103-106]upon Oulart Hill, situated about half-way between Enniscorthy and the

[103-107]sea, and eleven miles north of Wexford. Here they were attacked on the

[103-108]afternoon of the same day by the North Cork Militia, Colonel Foote, the

[103-109]Shilmalier Yeoman cavalry, Colonel Le Hunte, and the Wexford cavalry.

[103-110]The rebels, strong in their position, and more generally accustomed to

[103-111]the use of arms than persons in their condition in other parts of the

[103-112]country, made a brave and successful stand. Major Lambert, the Hon.

[103-113]Captain De Courcy (brother of Lord Kinsale), and some other officers,

[103-114]fell before the long-shore guns of the Shilmalier fowlers; of the North

[103-115]Cork detachment, only the colonel, a sergeant, and two or three

[103-116]privates escaped; the cavalry, at the top of their speed, galloped back

[103-117]to the county town.

[103-118]The people were soon thoroughly aroused. Another popular priest of the

[103-119]diocese, Michael Murphy, on reaching Gorey, finding his chapel also

[103-120]rifled, and the altar desecrated, turned his horse's head and joined

[103-121]the insurgents, who had gathered on Kilthomas hill, near Carnew. Signal

[103-122]fires burned that night on all the eminences of the county, which

[103-123]seemed as if they had been designed for so many watch-towers; horns

[103-124]resounded; horsemen galloped far and near; on the morrow of Whitsunday

[103-125]all Wexford arose, animated with the passions and purposes of civil

[103-126]war.

[103-127]On the 28th, Ferns, Camolin, and Enniscorthy were taken by the

[103-128]insurgents; the latter, after an action of four hours, in which a

[103-129]captain, two lieutenants, and eighty of the local yeomanry fell. The

[103-130]survivors fled to Wexford, which was as rapidly as possible placed in a

[103-131]state of defence. The old walls and gates were still in good repair,

[103-132]and 300 North Cork, 200 Donegal, and 700 local militia ought to have

[103-133]formed a strong garrison within such ramparts, against a mere

[103-134]tumultuous peasantry. The yeomen, however, thought otherwise, and two

[103-135]of the three imprisoned popular magistrates were sent to Enniscorthy to

[103-136]exhort and endeavour to disperse the insurgents. One of them only

[103-137]returned, the other, Mr. Fitzgerald, joined the rebels, who, continuing

[103-138]their march, were allowed to take possession of the county town without

[103-139]striking a blow. Mr. Bagenal Harvey, the magistrate still in prison,

[103-140]they insisted on making their Commander-in-Chief; a gentleman of

[103-141]considerable property, by no means destitute of courage, but in every

[103-142]other respect quite unequal to the task imposed upon him. After a trial

[103-143]of his generalship at the battle of Ross, he was transferred to the

[103-144]more pacific office of President of the Council, which continued to sit

[103-145]and direct operations from Wexford, with the co-operation of a

[103-146]sub-committee at Enniscorthy. Captain Matthew Keogh, a retired officer

[103-147]of the regular army, aged but active, was made governor of the town, in

[103-148]which a couple of hundred armed men were left as his guards. An attempt

[103-149]to relieve the place from Duncannon had utterly failed. General

[103-150]Fawcett, commanding that important fortress, set out on his march with

[103-151]this object on the 30th of May—his advanced guard of 70 Meathian

[103-152]yeomanry, having in charge three howitzers, whose slower movements it

[103-153]was expected the main force would overtake long before reaching the

[103-154]neighbourhood of danger. At Taghmon this force was joined by Captain

[103-155]Adams with his command, and thus reinforced they continued their march

[103-156]to Wexford. Within three miles of the town the road wound round the

[103-157]base of the "three rock" mountain; evening fell as the royalists

[103-158]approached this neighbourhood, where the victors of Oulart,

[103-159]Enniscorthy, and Wexford had just improvised a new camp. A sharp volley

[103-160]from the long-shore-men's guns, and a furious onslaught of pikes threw

[103-161]the royal detachment into the utmost disorder. Three officers of the

[103-162]Meathian cavalry, and nearly one hundred men were placed _hors de

[103-163]combat_; the three howitzers, eleven gunners, and several prisoners

[103-164]taken; making the third considerable success of the insurgents within a

[103-165]week.

[103-166]Wexford county now became the theatre of operations, on which all eyes

[103-167]were fixed. The populace gathered as if by instinct into three great

[103-168]encampments, on Vinegar Hill, above Enniscorthy; on Carrickbyrne, on

[103-169]the road leading to Ross, and on the hill of Corrigrua, seven miles

[103-170]from Gorey. The principal leaders of the first division were Fathers

[103-171]Kearns and Clinch, and Messrs. Fitzgerald, Doyle, and Redmond; of the

[103-172]second, Bagenal Harvey, and Father Philip Roche; of the last, Anthony

[103-173]Perry of Inch, Esmond Kyan, and the two Fathers Murphy, Michael, and

[103-174]John. The general plan of operations was that the third division should

[103-175]move by way of Arklow and Wicklow on the Capital; the second to open

[103-176]communication with Carlow, Kilkenny, and Kildare by Newtownbarry and

[103-177]Scollagh-gap; while the first was to attack New Ross, and endeavour to

[103-178]hasten the rising in Munster.

[103-179]On the 1st of June, the advance of the northern division marching upon

[103-180]Gorey, then occupied in force by General Loftus, were encountered four

[103-181]miles from the town, and driven back with the loss of about a hundred

[103-182]killed and wounded. On the 4th of June, Loftus, at the instance of

[103-183]Colonel Walpole, aid-de-camp to the Lord Lieutenant, who had lately

[103-184]joined him with considerable reinforcements, resolved to beat up the

[103-185]rebel quarters at Corrigrua. It was to be a combined movement; Lord

[103-186]Ancram, posted with his militia and dragoons at the bridge of

[103-187]Scaramalsh, where the poetic Banna joins the Slaney, was to prevent the

[103-188]arrival of succours from Vinegar Hill; Captain McManus, with a couple

[103-189]of companies of yeomanry, stationed at another exposed point from which

[103-190]intelligence could be obtained and communicated; while the General and

[103-191]Colonel Walpole, marched to the attack by roads some distance apart,

[103-192]which ran into one within two miles of Corrigrua camp. The main body of

[103-193]the King's troops were committed to the lead of Walpole, who had also

[103-194]two six-pounders and a howitzer. After an hour-and-a-half's march he

[103-195]found the country changed its character near the village of Clogh

[103-196](_clo'_), where the road descending from the level arable land, dips

[103-197]suddenly into the narrow and winding pass of Tubberneering. The sides

[103-198]of the pass were lined with a bushy shrubbery, and the roadway at the

[103-199]bottom embanked with ditch and dike. On came the confident Walpole,

[103-200]never dreaming that these silent thickets were so soon to re-echo the

[103-201]cries of the onslaught. The 4th dragoon guards, the Ancient Britons,

[103-202]under Sir Watkyn Wynne, the Antrim militia, under Colonel Cope, had all

[103-203]entered the defile before the ambuscade was discovered. Then, at the

[103-204]first volley, Walpole fell, with several of those immediately about his

[103-205]person; out from the shrubbery rushed the pikemen, clearing ditch and

[103-206]dike at a bound; dragoons and fencibles went down like the sward before

[103-207]the scythe of the mower; the three guns were captured, and turned on

[103-208]the flying survivors; the regimental flags taken, with all the other

[103-209]spoils pertaining to such a retreat. It was, in truth, an immense

[103-210]victory for a mob of peasants, marshalled by men who that day saw their

[103-211]first, or, at most, their second action. Before forty-eight hours they

[103-212]were masters of Gorey, and talked of nothing less than the capture of

[103-213]Dublin within another week or fortnight!

[103-214]From Vinegar Hill the concerted movement was made against Newtonbarry,

[103-215]on the 2nd of June, the rebels advancing by both banks of the Slaney,

[103-216]under cover of a six-pounder—the only gun they had with them. The

[103-217]detachment in command of the beautiful little town, half hidden in its

[103-218]leafy valley, was from 600 to 800 strong, with a troop of dragoons, and

[103-219]two battalion guns, under command of Colonel L'Estrange; these, after a

[103-220]sharp fusilade on both sides, were driven out, but the assailants,

[103-221]instead of following up the blow, dispersed for plunder or refreshment,

[103-222]were attacked in turn, and compelled to retreat, with a reported loss

[103-223]of 400 killed. Three days later, however, a still more important

[103-224]action, and a yet more disastrous repulse from the self-same cause,

[103-225]took place at New Ross, on the Barrow.

[103-226]The garrison of Ross, on the morning of the 5th of June, when General

[103-227]Harvey appeared before it, consisted of 1,400 men—Dublin, Meath,

[103-228]Donegal, and Clare militia, Mid-Lothian fencibles, and English

[103-229]artillery. General Johnson, a veteran soldier, was in command, and the

[103-230]place, strong in its well preserved old walls, had not heard a shot

[103-231]fired in anger since the time of Cromwell. Harvey was reported to have

[103-232]with him 20,000 men; but if we allow for the exaggeration of numbers

[103-233]common to all such movements, we may, perhaps, deduct one-half, and

[103-234]still leave him at the head of a formidable force—10,000 men, with

[103-235]three field-pieces. Mr. Furlong, a favourite officer, being sent

[103-236]forward to summon the town, was shot down by a sentinel, and the attack

[103-237]began. The main point of assault was the gate known as "three bullet

[103-238]gate," and the hour, five o'clock of the lovely summer's morning. The

[103-239]obstinacy with which the town was contested, may be judged from the

[103-240]fact, that the fighting continued for nearly ten hours, with the

[103-241]interruption of an hour or two at noon. This was the fatal interruption

[103-242]for the rebels. They had, at a heavy cost, driven out the royalists,

[103-243]with the loss of a colonel (Lord Mountjoy), three captains, and above

[103-244]200 men killed: but of their friends and comrades treble the number had

[103-245]fallen. Still the town, an object of the first importance, was theirs,

[103-246]when worn out with heat, fatigue, and fasting since sunrise, they

[103-247]indulged themselves in the luxury of a deep unmeasured carouse. The

[103-248]fugitive garrison finding themselves unpursued, halted to breathe on

[103-249]the Kilkenny bank of the river, were rallied by the veteran Johnson,

[103-250]and led back again across the bridge, taking the surprised revellers

[103-251]completely unprepared. A cry was raised that this was a fresh force

[103-252]from Waterford; the disorganised multitude endeavoured to rally in

[103-253]turn, but before the leaders could collect their men, the town was once

[103-254]more in possession of the Bang's troops. The rebels, in their turn,

[103-255]unpursued by their exhausted enemies, fell back upon their camping

[103-256]ground of the night before, at Corbet hill and Slieve-kielter. At the

[103-257]latter, Father Philip Roche, dissatisfied with Harvey's management,

[103-258]established a separate command, which he transferred to a layman of his

[103-259]own name, Edward Roche, with whom he continued to act and advise during

[103-260]the remainder of this memorable month.

[103-261]The summer of 1798 was, for an Irish summer, remarkably dry and warm.

[103-262]The heavy Atlantic rains which at all seasons are poured out upon that

[103-263]soil, seemed suspended in favour of the insurgent multitudes, amounting

[103-264]to 30,000, or 40,000 at the highest, who, on the different hill

[103-265]summits, posted their nightly sentinels, and threw themselves down on

[103-266]turf and heather to snatch a short repose. The kindling of a beacon,

[103-267]the lowing of cattle, or the hurried arrival of scout or messenger,

[103-268]hardly interfered with slumbers which the fatigues of the day, and,

[103-269]unhappily also, the potations of the night rendered doubly deep. An

[103-270]early morning mass mustered all the Catholics, unless the very

[103-271]depraved, to the chaplain's tent—for several of the officers, and the

[103-272]chaplains always were supplied with tents; and then a hasty meal was

[103-273]snatched before the sun was fairly above the horizon, and the day's

[103-274]work commenced. The endurance exhibited by the rebels, their personal

[103-275]strength, swiftness and agility; their tenacity of life, and the ease

[103-276]with which their worst wounds were healed, excited the astonishment of

[103-277]the surgeons and officers of the regular army. The truth is, that the

[103-278]virtuous lives led by that peaceful peasantry before the outbreak,

[103-279]enabled them to withstand privations and hardships under which the

[103-280]better fed and better clad Irish yeomen and English guardsmen would

[103-281]have sunk prostrate in a week.

[103-283]Several signs now marked the turning of the tide against the men of

[103-284]Wexford. Waterford did not rise after the battle of Ross; while

[103-285]Munster, generally, was left to undecided councils, or held back in

[103-286]hopes of another French expedition. The first week of June had passed

[103-287]over, and neither northward nor westward was there any movement

[103-288]formidable enough to draw off from the devoted county the combined

[103-289]armies which were now directed against its camps. A gunboat fleet lined

[103-290]the coast from Bannow round to Wicklow, which soon after appeared off

[103-291]Wexford bar, and forced an entrance into the harbour. A few days

[103-292]earlier, General Needham marched from Dublin, and took up his position

[103-293]at Arklow, at the head of a force variously stated at 1,500 to 2,000

[103-294]men, composed of 120 cavalry under Sir Watkyn Wynne, two brigades of

[103-295]militia under Colonels Cope and Maxwell, and a brigade of English and

[103-296]Scotch fencibles under Colonel Skerrett. There were also at Arklow

[103-297]about 300 of the Wexford and Wicklow mounted yeomanry raised by Lord

[103-298]Wicklow, Lord Mountnorris, and other gentlemen of the neighbourhood.

[103-299]Early on the morning of the 9th of June the northern division of the

[103-300]rebels left Gorey in two columns, in order if possible to drive this

[103-301]force from Arklow. One body proceeding by the coast road hoped to turn

[103-302]the English position by way of the strand, the other taking the inner

[103-303]line of the Dublin road, was to assail the town at its upper or inland

[103-304]suburb. But General Needham had made the most of his two days'

[103-305]possession; barricades were erected across the road, and at the

[103-306]entrance to the main street; the graveyard and bridge commanding the

[103-307]approach by the shore road were mounted with ordnance; the cavalry were

[103-308]posted where they could best operate, near the strand; the barrack wall

[103-309]was lined with a _banquette_ or stage, from which the musketeers could

[103-310]pour their fire with the greatest advantage, and every other precaution

[103-311]taken to give the rebels a warm reception. The action commenced early

[103-312]in the afternoon, and lasted till eight in the evening—five or six

[103-313]hours. The inland column suffered most severely from the marksmen on

[103-314]the _banquette_, and the gallant Father Michael Murphy, whom his

[103-315]followers believed to be invulnerable, fell leading them on to the

[103-316]charge for the third time. On the side of the sea, Esmond Kyan was

[103-317]badly wounded in the arm, which he was subsequently obliged to have

[103-318]amputated, and though the fearless Shilmaliers drove the cavalry into

[103-319]and over the Avoca, discipline and ordnance prevailed once again over

[103-320]numbers and courage. As night fell, the assailants retired slowly

[103-321]towards Coolgreney, carrying off nine carloads of their wounded, and

[103-322]leaving, perhaps, as many more on the field; their loss was variously

[103-323]reported from 700 to 1,000, and even 1,500. The opposite force returned

[103-324]less than 100 killed, including Captain Knox, and about as many

[103-325]wounded. The repulse was even more than that at Ross, dispiriting to

[103-326]the rebels, who, as a last resort, now decided to concentrate all their

[103-327]strength on the favourite position at Vinegar Hill.

[103-328]Against this encampment, therefore, the entire available force of

[103-329]regulars and militia within fifty miles of the spot were concentrated

[103-330]by orders of Lord Lake, the Commander-in-Chief. General Dundas from

[103-331]Wicklow was to join General Loftus at Carnew on the 18th; General

[103-332]Needham was to advance simultaneously to Gorey; General Sir Henry

[103-333]Johnson to unite at Old Ross with Sir James Duff from Carlow; Sir

[103-334]Charles Asgill was to occupy Gore's bridge and Borris; Sir John Moore

[103-335]was to land at Ballyhack ferry, march to Foulke's Mill, and united with

[103-336]Johnson and Duff, to assail the rebel camp on Carrickbyrne. These

[103-337]various movements ordered on the 16th, were to be completed by the

[103-338]20th, on which day, from their various new positions, the entire force,

[103-339]led by these six general officers, was to surround Vinegar Hill, and

[103-340]make a simultaneous attack upon the last stronghold of the Wexford

[103-341]rebellion.

[103-342]This elaborate plan failed of complete execution in two points.

[103-343]_First_, the camp on Carrickbyrne, instead of waiting the attack, sent

[103-344]down its fighting men to Foulke's Mill, where, in the afternoon of the

[103-345]20th they beat up Sir John Moore's quarters, and maintained from 3

[103-346]o'clock till dark, what that officer calls "a pretty sharp action."

[103-347]Several times they were repulsed and again formed behind the ditches

[103-348]and renewed the conflict; but the arrival of two fresh regiments, under

[103-349]Lord Dalhousie, taught them that there was no farther chance of

[103-350]victory. By this affair, however, though at a heavy cost, they had

[103-351]prevented the junction of all the troops, and, not without

[103-352]satisfaction, they now followed the two Roches, the priest and the

[103-353]layman, to the original position of the mountain of Forth; Sir John

[103-354]Moore, on his part, taking the same direction, until he halted within

[103-355]sight of the walls of Wexford. The other departure from Lord Lake's

[103-356]plan was on the side of General Needham, who was ordered to approach

[103-357]the point of attack by the circuitous route of Oulart, but who did not

[103-359]On the morning of the appointed day, about 13,000 royal troops were in

[103-360]movement against the 20,000 rebels whom they intended to dislodge. Sir

[103-361]James Duff obtained possession of an eminence which commanded the lower

[103-362]line of the rebel encampment, and from this point a brisk cannonade was

[103-363]opened against the opposite force; at the same time the columns of

[103-364]Lake, Wilford, Dundas, and Johnson, pushed up the south-eastern,

[103-365]northern and western sides of the eminence, partially covered by the

[103-366]fire of these guns, so advantageously placed. After an hour and a

[103-367]half's desperate fighting, the rebels broke and fled by the unguarded

[103-368]side of the hill. Their rout was complete, and many were cut down by

[103-369]the cavalry, as they pressed in dense masses on each other, over the

[103-370]level fields and out on the open highways. Still this action was far

[103-371]from being one of the most fatal as to loss of life, fought in that

[103-372]county; the rebel dead were numbered only at 400, and the royalists

[103-373]killed and wounded at less than half that number.

[103-374]It was the last considerable action of the Wexford rising, and all the

[103-375]consequences which followed being attributed arbitrarily to this cause,

[103-376]helped to invest it with a disproportionate importance. The only leader

[103-377]lost on the rebel side was Father Clinch of Enniscorthy, who

[103-378]encountered Lord Roden hand to hand in the retreat, but who, while

[103-379]engaged with his lordship whom he wounded, was shot down by a trooper.

[103-380]The disorganization, however, which followed on the dispersion, was

[103-381]irreparable. One column had taken the road by Gorey to the mountains of

[103-382]Wicklow—another to Wexford, where they split into two parts, a portion

[103-383]crossing the Slaney into the sea-coast parishes, and facing northward

[103-384]by the shore road, the other falling back on "the three rocks"

[103-385]encampment, where the Messrs. Roche held together a fragment of their

[103-386]former command. Wexford town, on the 22nd, was abandoned to Lord Lake,

[103-387]who established himself in the house of Governor Keogh, the owner being

[103-388]lodged in the common jail. Within the week, Bagenal Harvey, Father

[103-389]Philip Roche, and Kelly of Killane, had surrendered in despair, while

[103-390]Messrs. Grogan and Colclough, who had secreted themselves in a cave in

[103-391]the great Saltee Island, were discovered, and conducted to the same

[103-392]prison. Notwithstanding the capitulation agreed to by Lord

[103-393]Kingsborough, the execution and decapitation of all these gentlemen

[103-394]speedily followed, and their ghastly faces looked down for many a day

[103-395]from the iron spikes above the entrance of Wexford Court House. Mr.

[103-396]Esmond Kyan, the popular hero of the district, as merciful as brave,

[103-397]was discovered some time subsequently paying a stealthy visit to his

[103-398]family; he was put to death on the spot, and his body, weighted with

[103-399]heavy stones, thrown into the harbour. A few mornings afterwards the

[103-400]incoming tide deposited it close by the dwelling of his father-in-law,

[103-401]and the rites of Christian burial, so dear to all his race, were

[103-402]hurriedly rendered to the beloved remains.

[103-403]The insurrection in this county, while it abounded in instances of

[103-404]individual and general heroism, was stained also, on both sides, by

[103-405]many acts of diabolical cruelty. The aggressors, both in time and in

[103-406]crime were the yeomanry and military; but the popular movement dragged

[103-407]wretches to the surface who delighted in repaying torture with torture,

[103-408]and death with death. The butcheries of Dunlavin and Carnew were repaid

[103-409]by the massacres at Scullabogue and Wexford bridge, in the former of

[103-410]which 110, and in the latter 35 or 40 persons were put to death in cold

[103-411]blood, by the monsters who absented themselves from the battles of Ross

[103-412]and Vinegar Hill. The executions at Wexford bridge would probably have

[103-413]been swelled to double the number, had not Father Corrin, one of the

[103-414]priests of the town, rushing in between his Protestant neighbours and

[103-415]the ferocious Captain Dixon, and summoning all present to pray, invoked

[103-416]the Almighty "to show them the same mercy" they showed their prisoners.

[103-417]This awful supplication calmed even that savage rabble, and no further

[103-418]execution took place. Nearly forty years afterwards, Captain Kellet, of

[103-419]Clonard, ancestor of the Arctic discoverer, and others whom he had

[103-420]rescued from the very grasp of the executioner, followed to the grave

[103-421]that revered and devoted minister of mercy!

[103-422]It would be a profitless task to draw out a parallel of the crimes

[103-423]committed on both sides. Two facts only need be recorded: that although

[103-424]from 1798 to 1800, not less than _sixty-five_ places of Catholic

[103-425]worship were demolished or burned in Leinster, (twenty-two of which

[103-426]were in Wexford county), only _one_ Protestant Church, that of Old

[103-427]Ross, was destroyed in retaliation; and that although towards men,

[103-428]especially men in arms, the rebels acted on the fierce Mosaic maxim of

[103-429]"an eye for an eye and a tooth for a tooth," no outrage upon women is

[103-430]laid to their charge, even by their most exasperated enemies.
