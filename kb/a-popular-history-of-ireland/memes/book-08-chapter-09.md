CHAPTER IX.

[060-001]ESSEX'S CAMPAIGN OF 1599—BATTLE OF THE CURLIEU MOUNTAINS—O'NEIL'S

[060-002]NEGOTIATIONS WITH SPAIN—MOUNTJOY, LORD DEPUTY.

[060-003]The last favourite of the many who enjoyed the foolish, if not guilty,

[060-004]favours of Elizabeth was Robert Devereux, Earl of Essex, son of that

[060-005]unfortunate nobleman spoken of in a previous chapter as the

[060-006]"undertaker" of Farney and Clandeboy. Born in 1567, the Earl had barely

[060-007]reached the age of manhood when he won the heart of his royal mistress,

[060-008]already verging on threescore. Gifted by nature with a handsome person,

[060-009]undoubted courage, and many generous qualities, he exhibited, in the

[060-010]most important transactions of life, the recklessness of a madman and

[060-011]the levity of a spoiled child; it was apparent to the world that

[060-012]nothing short of the personal fascination which he exercised over the

[060-013]Queen could so long have preserved him from the consequences of his

[060-014]continual caprices and quarrels. Such was the character of the young

[060-015]nobleman, who, as was afterwards said, at the instigation of his

[060-016]enemies, was sent over to restore the ascendancy of the English arms in

[060-017]the revolted provinces. His appointment was to last during the Queen's

[060-018]pleasure; he was provided with an army of 20,000 foot and 2,000 horse;

[060-019]three-fourths of the ordinary annual revenue of England (340,000 pounds

[060-020]out of 450,000 pounds) was placed at his disposal, and the largest

[060-021]administrative powers, civil and military, were conferred on him. A new

[060-022]plan of campaign in Ulster was decided upon at the royal council table,

[060-023]and Sir Samuel Bagnal, brother of the late Marshal, and other

[060-024]experienced officers, were to precede or accompany him to carry it into

[060-025]execution. The main feature of this plan was to get possession by sea

[060-026]and strongly fortify Ballyshannon, Donegal, Derry, and the entrance to

[060-027]the Foyle, so as to operate at once in the rear of the northern chiefs,

[060-028]as well as along the old familiar base of Newry, Monaghan, and Armagh.

[060-029]Essex, on being sworn into office at Dublin, on the 15th of April,

[060-030]1599, immediately issued a proclamation offering pardon and restoration

[060-031]of property to such of the Irish as would lay down their arms by a

[060-032]given day, but very few persons responded to this invitation. He next

[060-033]despatched reinforcements to the garrisons of Wicklow and Naas, menaced

[060-034]by the O'Moores and O'Byrnes, and to those of Drogheda, Dundalk, Newry,

[060-035]and Carrickfergus, the only northern strongholds remaining in

[060-036]possession of the Queen. The principal operations, it had been agreed

[060-037]before he left England, were to be directed against Ulster, but with

[060-038]the waywardness which always accompanied him, he disregarded that

[060-039]arrangement, and set forth, at the head of 7,000 men, for the opposite

[060-040]quarter. He was accompanied in this march by the Earls of Clanrickarde

[060-041]and Thomond, Sir Conyers Clifford, Governor of Connaught, and O'Conor

[060-042]of Sligo, the only native chief who remained in the English ranks. In

[060-043]Ormond he received the submission of Lord Mountgarrett, son-in-law to

[060-044]Tyrone, and took the strong castle of Cahir from another of the

[060-045]insurgent Butlers. After a halt at Limerick, he set out against the

[060-046]Geraldines, who the previous year had joined the Northern league, at

[060-047]the instance of Tyrrell and O'Moore. Although the only heir of the Earl

[060-048]of Desmond was a prisoner, or ward of Elizabeth in England, James

[060-049]Fitzgerald, son of Thomas Roe, son of the fifteenth Earl by that

[060-050]marriage which had been pronounced invalid, assumed the title at the

[060-051]suggestion of O'Neil, and was recognized as the Desmond by the greater

[060-052]portion of the relatives of that family. Fitzmaurice, Lord of Lixnaw,

[060-053]the Knight of Glynn, the White Knight, the Lord Roche, Pierce Lacy of

[060-054]Buree and Bruff, the last descendant of Hugh de Lacy and the daughter

[060-055]of Roderick O'Conor, with the McCarthys, O'Donohoes, O'Sullivans,

[060-056]Condons, and other powerful tribes, were all astir to the number, as

[060-057]Carew supposes, of 8,000 men, all emulous of their compatriots in the

[060-058]North. Issuing from Limerick, Essex marched southward to strengthen the

[060-059]stronghold of Askeaton, into which he succeeded, after a severe

[060-060]skirmish by the way, in throwing supplies. Proceeding to victual Adare,

[060-061]he experienced a similar check, losing among others Sir Henry Norris,

[060-062]the third of those brave brothers who had fallen a victim to these

[060-063]Irish wars. In returning to Dublin, by way of Waterford and Kildare, he

[060-064]was assailed by O'Moore at a difficult defile, which, to this day, is

[060-065]known in Irish as "the pass of the plumes" or feathers. The Earl forced

[060-066]a passage with the loss of 500 lives, and so returned with little glory

[060-067]to Dublin.

[060-068]The next military incident of the year transpired in the West. We have

[060-069]spoken of O'Conor Sligo as the only native chief who followed Essex to

[060-070]the South. He had been lately at the English Court, where he was

[060-071]treated with the highest distinction, in order that he might be used to

[060-072]impede O'Donnell's growing power in lower Connaught. On returning home

[060-073]he was promptly besieged by the Donegal chief in his remaining castle

[060-074]at Colooney, within five miles of Sligo. Essex, on learning this fact,

[060-075]ordered Sir Conyers Clifford to march to the relief of O'Conor with all

[060-076]the power he could muster. Clifford despatched from Galway, by sea,

[060-077]stores and materials for the refortification of Sligo town, and set out

[060-078]himself at the head of 2,100 men, drafted from both sides of the

[060-079]Shannon, under twenty-five ensigns. He had under him Sir Alexander

[060-080]Radcliffe, Sir Griffin Markham, and other experienced officers. Their

[060-081]rendezvous, as usual, was the old monastic town of Boyle, about a day's

[060-082]march to the south of Sligo. From Boyle, the highway led into the

[060-083]Curlieu mountains, which divide Sligo on the south-east from Roscommon.

[060-084]Here, in the strong pass of Ballaghboy, O'Donnell with the main body of

[060-085]his followers awaited their approach. He had left the remainder, under

[060-086]his cousin and brother-in-law, Nial Garve (or the _rough_), to maintain

[060-087]the siege of Colooney Castle. O'Ruarc and the men of Breffni joined him

[060-088]during the battle, but their entire force is nowhere stated. It was the

[060-089]eve of the Assumption of the Blessed Virgin, and the first anniversary

[060-090]of the great victory of the Yellow Ford. The night was spent by the

[060-091]Irish in fasting and prayer, the early morning in hearing Mass, and

[060-092]receiving the Holy Communion. The day was far advanced when the head of

[060-093]Clifford's column appeared in the defile, driving in a barricade

[060-094]erected at its entrance. The defenders, according to orders, discharged

[060-095]their javelins and muskets, and fell back farther into the gorge. The

[060-096]English advanced twelve abreast, through a piece of woodland, after

[060-097]which the road crossed a patch of bog. Here the thick of the battle was

[060-098]fought. Sir Alexander Radcliffe, who led the vanguard, fell early in

[060-099]the action, and his division falling back on the centre threw them all

[060-100]into confusion. O'Ruarc arriving with his men at the critical moment

[060-101]completed the rout, and pursued the fugitives to the gates of Boyle.

[060-102]The gallant Clifford, scorning to fly, was found among the slain, and

[060-103]honourably interred by his generous enemies in the monastery of Lough

[060-104]Key. On his head being shown to O'Conor at Colooney, he at once

[060-105]surrendered to O'Donnell, and entered into the Northern Confederacy.

[060-106]Theobald Burke, the commander of the vessels sent round from Galway to

[060-107]fortify Sligo, also submitted to O'Donnell, and was permitted to return

[060-108]to the port from which he had lately sailed, with very different

[060-109]intentions.

[060-110]Essex, whose mind was a prey to apprehension from his enemies in

[060-111]England had demanded reinforcements before he could undertake anything

[060-112]against Ulster. It seems hardly credible that the 15,000 regular troops

[060-113]in the country at his coming should be mostly taken up with garrison

[060-114]duty, yet we cannot otherwise account for their disappearance from the

[060-115]field. He asked for 2,000 fresh troops, and while awaiting their

[060-116]arrival, sent a detachment of 600 men into Wicklow, who were repulsed

[060-117]with loss by Phelim, son of Feagh, the new Chief of the O'Byrnes. Essex

[060-118]was thrown into transports of rage at this new loss. The officers who

[060-119]retreated were tried by court-martial, and, contrary to his usually

[060-120]generous temper, the surviving men were inhumanly decimated.

[060-121]Early in September, the reinforcement he had asked for arrived with a

[060-122]bitterly reproachful letter from the Queen. He now hastened to make a

[060-123]demonstration against Tyrone, although, from some cause unexplained, he

[060-124]does not seem to have drawn out the whole force at his disposal. From

[060-125]Newry he proceeded northward towards Carrickfergus, with only 1,300

[060-126]foot and 300 horse. On the high ground to the north of the river Lagan,

[060-127]overlooking Anaghclart Bridge, he found the host of O'Neil encamped,

[060-128]and received a courteous message from their leader, soliciting a

[060-129]personal interview. Essex at first declined, but afterwards accepted

[060-130]the invitation, and at an appointed hour the two commanders rode down

[060-131]to the opposite banks of the river, wholly unattended, the advanced

[060-132]guard of each looking curiously on from the uplands. O'Neil spurred his

[060-133]horse into the stream up to the saddle girth, and thus for an hour,

[060-134]exposed to the generous but impulsive Englishman, the grievances of

[060-135]himself and his compatriots. With all the art, for which he was

[060-136]distinguished, he played upon his knowledge of the Earl's character: he

[060-137]named those enemies of his own whom he also knew to be hostile to

[060-138]Essex, he showed his provocations in the strongest light, and declared

[060-139]his readiness to submit to her Majesty, on condition of obtaining

[060-140]complete liberty of conscience, an act of indemnity to include his

[060-141]allies in all the four Provinces; that the principal officers of state,

[060-142]the judges, and one half the army should in future be Irish by birth.

[060-143]This was, in effect, a demand for national independence, though the

[060-144]Lord Lieutenant may not have seen it in that light. He promised,

[060-145]however, to transmit the propositions to England, and within presence

[060-146]of six principal officers of each side, agreed to a truce till the 1st

[060-147]of May following. Another upbraiding letter from Elizabeth, which

[060-148]awaited him on his return to Dublin, drove Essex to the desperate

[060-149]resolution of presenting himself before her, without permission. The

[060-150]short remainder of his troubled career, his execution in the Tower in

[060-151]February, 1601, and Elizabeth's frantic lamentations, are familiar to

[060-152]readers of English history.

[060-153]In presenting so comprehensive an ultimatum to Essex, O'Neil was

[060-154]emboldened by the latest intelligence received from Spain. Philip II.,

[060-155]the life-long friend of the Catholics, had, indeed, died the previous

[060-156]September, but one of the first acts of his successor, Philip III., was

[060-157]to send envoys into Ireland, assuring its chiefs that he would continue

[060-158]to them the friendship and alliance of his father. Shortly before the

[060-159]conference at Anaghclart, a third Armada, under the Adelantado of

[060-160]Castile, was awaiting orders in the port of Corunna, and England, for

[060-161]the third time in ten years, was placed in a posture of defence. The

[060-162]Spaniards sailed, but soon divided into two squadrons, one of which

[060-163]passed down the British Channel unobserved, and anchored in the waters

[060-164]of the Sluys, while the other sailed for the Canaries to intercept the

[060-165]Hollanders. At the same time, however, most positive assurances were

[060-166]renewed that an auxiliary force might shortly be expected to land in

[060-167]Ireland in aid of the Catholics. The non-arrival of this force during

[060-168]the fortunate campaign of 1599 was not much felt by the Catholics; and

[060-169]was satisfactorily explained by Philip's envoys—but the mere fact of

[060-170]the existence of the Spanish alliance gave additional confidence and

[060-171]influence to the confederates. That fact was placed beyond all question

[060-172]by the arrival of two Spanish ships laden with stores for O'Neil,

[060-173]immediately after the interview with Essex. In the summer or autumn

[060-174]ensuing, Mathew of Oviedo, a Spaniard, consecrated at Rome, Archbishop

[060-175]of Dublin, brought over 22,000 crowns towards the pay of the Irish

[060-176]troops, and a year afterwards, Don Martin de la Cerda was sent to

[060-177]reside as envoy with Tyrone.

[060-179]The year 1600 was employed by Hugh O'Neil, after the manner of his

[060-180]ancestors, who were candidates for the Kingship of Tara, in a

[060-181]visitation of the Provinces. Having first planted strong garrisons on

[060-182]the southern passes leading into Ulster, he marched at the head of

[060-183]3,000 men into West-Meath, where he obliged Lord Delvin and Sir

[060-184]Theobald Dillon to join the Confederation. From Meath he marched to

[060-185]Ely, whose chief he punished for a late act of treachery to some Ulster

[060-186]soldiers invited to his assistance. From Ely he turned aside to

[060-187]venerate the relic of the Holy Cross, at Thurles, and being there he

[060-188]granted his protection to the great Monastery built by Donald More

[060-189]O'Brien. At Cashel he was joined by the Geraldine, whom he caused to be

[060-190]recognized as Earl of Desmond. Desmond and his supporters accompanied

[060-191]him through Limerick into Cork, quartering their retainers on the lands

[060-192]of their enemies, but sparing their friends; the Earl of Ormond with a

[060-193]corps of observation moving on a parallel line of march, but carefully

[060-194]avoiding a collision. In the beginning of March the Catholic army

[060-195]halted at Inniscarra, upon the river Lee, about five miles west of

[060-196]Cork. Here O'Neil remained three weeks in camp consolidating the

[060-197]Catholic party in South Munster. During that time he was visited by the

[060-198]chiefs of the ancient Eugenian clans—O'Donohoe, O'Donovan, and

[060-199]O'Mahoney: thither also came two of the most remarkable men of the

[060-200]southern Province, Florence McCarthy, Lord of Carberry, and Donald

[060-201]O'Sullivan, Lord of Bearehaven. McCarthy "like Saul, higher by the head

[060-202]and shoulders than any of his house," had brain in proportion to his

[060-203]brawn; O'Sullivan, as was afterwards shown, was possessed of military

[060-204]virtues of a high order. Florence was inaugurated with O'Neil's

[060-205]sanction as McCarthy More, and although the rival house of Muskerry

[060-206]fiercely resisted his claim to superiority at first, a wiser choice

[060-207]could not have been made had the times tended to confirm it.

[060-208]While at Inniscarra, O'Neil lost in single combat one of his most

[060-209]accomplished officers, the chief of Fermanagh. Maguire, accompanied

[060-210]only by a Priest and two horsemen, was making observations nearer to

[060-211]the city than the camp, when Sir Warham St. Leger, Marshal of Munster,

[060-212]issued out of Cork with a company of soldiers, probably on a similar

[060-213]mission. Both were in advance of their attendants when they came

[060-214]unexpectedly face to face. Both were famous as horsemen and for the use

[060-215]of their weapons, and neither would retrace his steps. The Irish chief,

[060-216]poising his spear, dashed forward against his opponent, but received a

[060-217]pistol shot which proved mortal the same day. He, however, had strength

[060-218]enough left to drive his spear through the neck of St. Leger, and to

[060-219]effect his escape from the English cavalry. Saint Leger was carried

[060-220]back to Cork where he expired; Maguire, on reaching the camp, had

[060-221]barely time left to make his last confession, when he breathed his

[060-222]last. This untoward event, the necessity of preventing possible

[060-223]dissensions in Fermanagh, and still more, the menacing movements of the

[060-224]new Deputy, lately sworn in at Dublin, obliged O'Neil to return home

[060-225]earlier than he intended. Soon after reaching Dungannon he had the

[060-226]gratification of receiving a most gracious letter from Pope Clement

[060-227]VIII., together with a crown of phoenix feathers, symbolical of the

[060-228]consideration with which he was regarded by the Sovereign Pontiff.

[060-229]A new Deputy had landed at Howth on the 24th of February, 1600, and was

[060-230]sworn in at Dublin the day following. This was Charles Blount, Lord

[060-231]Mountjoy, afterwards Earl of Devonshire, a nobleman now in his 37th

[060-232]year. He had been the rival, the enemy, and the devoted friend of the

[060-233]unfortunate Essex, whom he equalled in personal gifts, in courage, and

[060-234]in gallantry, but far exceeded in judgment, firmness, and foresight. He

[060-235]was one of a class of soldier-statesmen, peculiar to the second half of

[060-236]Elizabeth's reign, who affected authorship and the patronage of letters

[060-237]as a necessary complement to the manners of a courtier and commander.

[060-238]On the 2nd of April, Mountjoy, still at Dublin, wrote to her Majesty

[060-239]that the army had taken heart since his arrival, that he had no fear of

[060-240]the loss of the country, but was more anxious for Connaught than any

[060-241]other Province. He deplored the capture of Lord Ormond by the O'Moores,

[060-242]but hoped, if God prospered her arms during the summer, either "to bow

[060-243]or to break the crooked humours of these people." The three succeeding

[060-244]years of peace granted to England—interrupted only by the mad _emeute_

[060-245]of Essex, and the silly intrigues of the King of Scotland—enabled

[060-246]Elizabeth to direct all the energies of the State, which had so

[060-247]immensely increased in wealth during her reign, for the subjugation of

[060-248]the Irish revolt.

[060-249]The capture of Ormond by the O'Moores took place in the month of April,

[060-250]at a place called Corroneduff, in an interview between the Earl, the

[060-251]President of Munster, and Lord Thomond, on the one part, and the

[060-252]Leinster Chief on the other. Ormond, who stood out from his party, had

[060-253]asked to see the famous Jesuit, Father Archer, then with O'Moore. The

[060-254]Priest advanced leaning on his staff, which, in the heat of a

[060-255]discussion that arose, he raised once or twice in the air. The

[060-256]clansmen, suspecting danger to the Jesuit, rushed forward and dragged

[060-257]the Earl from his horse. Lord Thomond and the President, taking the

[060-258]alarm, plied their spurs, and were but too glad to escape. Ormond

[060-259]remained a prisoner from April to June, during which interval he was

[060-260]received by Archer into the Church, to which he firmly adhered till the

[060-261]day of his death. On his liberation he entered into bonds for 3,000

[060-262]pounds not to make reprisals, but Mountjoy took vengeance for him. The

[060-263]fair, well-fenced, and well-cultivated land of Leix was cruelly ravaged

[060-264]immediately after Ormond's release—the common soldiers cut down with

[060-265]their swords "corn to the value of 10,000 pounds and upwards," and the

[060-266]brave chief, Owny, son of Rory, having incautiously exposed himself in

[060-267]an attack on Maryborough, was, on the 17th of August, killed by a

[060-268]musket shot.
