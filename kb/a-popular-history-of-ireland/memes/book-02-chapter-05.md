CHAPTER V.

[013-001]REIGN OF MALACHY II. AND RIVALRY OF BRIAN.

[013-002]Melaghlin, or Malachy II., fifth in direct descent from Malachy I. (the

[013-003]founder of the Southern Hy-Nial dynasty), was in his thirtieth year

[013-004]when (A.D. 980) he succeeded to the monarchy. He had just achieved the

[013-005]mighty victory of Tara when the death of his predecessor opened his way

[013-006]to the throne; and seldom did more brilliant dawn usher in a more

[013-007]eventful day than that which Fate held in store for this victor-king.

[013-008]None of his predecessors, not even his ancestor and namesake, had ever

[013-009]been able to use the high language of his "noble Proclamation," when he

[013-010]announced on his accession—"Let all the Irish who are suffering

[013-011]servitude in the land of the stranger return home to their respective

[013-012]houses and enjoy themselves in gladness and in peace." In obedience to

[013-013]this edict, and the power to enforce it established by the victory at

[013-014]Tara, 2,000 captives, including the King of Leinster and the Prince of

[013-015]Aileach, were returned to their homes.

[013-016]The hardest task of every Ard-Righ of this and the previous century had

[013-017]been to circumscribe the ambition of the kings of Cashel within

[013-018]Provincial bounds. Whoever ascended the southern throne—whether the

[013-019]warlike Felim or the learned Cormac—we have seen the same policy

[013-020]adopted by them all. The descendants of Heber had tired of the long

[013-021]ascendancy of the race of Heremon, and the desertion of Tara, by making

[013-022]that ascendancy still more strikingly Provincial, had increased their

[013-023]antipathy. It was a struggle for supremacy between north and south; a

[013-024]contest of two geographical parties; an effort to efface the real or

[013-025]fancied dependency of one-half the island on the will of the other. The

[013-026]Southern Hy-Nial dynasty, springing up as a third power upon the

[013-027]Methian bank of the Shannon, and balancing itself between the

[013-028]contending parties, might perhaps have given a new centre to the whole

[013-029]system; Malachy II. was in the most favourable position possible to

[013-030]have done so, had he not had to contend with a rival, his equal in

[013-031]battle and superior in council, in the person of Brian, the son of

[013-032]Kennedy, of Kincorra.

[013-033]The rise to sovereign rank of the house of Kincorra (the O'Briens), is

[013-034]one of the most striking episodes of the tenth century. Descending,

[013-035]like most of the leading families of the South, from Olild, the Clan

[013-036]Dalgais had long been excluded from the throne of Cashel, by successive

[013-037]coalitions of their elder brethren, the Eugenians. Lactna and Lorcan,

[013-038]the grandfather and father of Kennedy, intrepid and able men, had

[013-039]strengthened their tribe by wise and vigorous measures, so that the

[013-040]former was able to claim the succession, apparently with success.

[013-041]Kennedy had himself been a claimant for the same honour, the alternate

[013-042]provision in the will of Olild, against Kellachan Cashel (A.D. 940-2),

[013-043]but at the Convention held at Glanworth, on the river Funcheon, for the

[013-044]selection of king, the aged mother of Kellachan addressed his rival in

[013-045]a quatrain, beginning—

[013-046]"Kennedi Cas revere the law!"

[013-047]which induced him to abandon his pretensions. This Prince, usually

[013-048]spoken of by the Bards as "the chaste Kennedy," died in the year 950,

[013-049]leaving behind him four or five out of twelve sons, with whom he had

[013-050]been blessed. Most of the others had fallen in Danish battles—three in

[013-051]the same campaign (943), and probably in the same field. There appear

[013-052]in after scenes, Mahon, who became King of Cashel; Echtierna, who was

[013-053]chief of Thomond, under Mahon; Marcan, an ecclesiastic, and Brian, born

[013-054]in 941, the Benjamin of the household. Mahon proved himself, as Prince

[013-055]and Captain, every way worthy of his inheritance. He advanced from

[013-056]victory to victory over his enemies, foreign and domestic. In 960 he

[013-057]claimed the throne of Munster, which claim he enforced by royal

[013-058]visitation five years later. In the latter year, he rescued

[013-059]Clonmacnoise from the Danes, and in 968 defeated the same enemy, with a

[013-060]loss of several thousand men at Sulchoid. This great blow he followed

[013-061]up by the sack of Limerick, from which "he bore off a large quantity of

[013-062]gold, and silver, and jewels." In these, and all his expeditions, from

[013-063]a very early age, he was attended by Brian, to whom he acted not only

[013-064]as a brother and prince, but as a tutor in arms. Fortune had

[013-065]accompanied him in all his undertakings. He had expelled his most

[013-066]intractable rival—Molloy, son of Bran, lord of Desmond; his rule was

[013-067]acknowledged by the Northmen of Dublin and Cork, who opened their

[013-068]fortresses to him, and served under his banner; he carried "all the

[013-069]hostages of Munster to his house," which had never before worn so

[013-070]triumphant an aspect. But family greatness begets family pride, and

[013-071]pride begets envy and hatred. The Eugenian families who now found

[013-072]themselves overshadowed by the brilliant career of the sons of Kennedy,

[013-073]conspired against the life of Mahon, who, from his too confiding

[013-074]nature, fell easily into their trap. Molloy, son of Bran, by the advice

[013-075]of Ivar, the Danish lord of Limerick, proposed to meet Mahon in

[013-076]friendly conference at the house of Donovan, an Eugenian chief, whose

[013-077]rath was at Bruree, on the river Maigue. The safety of each person was

[013-078]guaranteed by the Bishop of Cork, the mediator on the occasion. Mahon

[013-079]proceeded unsuspiciously to the conference, where he was suddenly

[013-080]seized by order of his treacherous host, and carried into the

[013-081]neighbouring mountains of Knocinreorin. Here a small force, placed for

[013-082]the purpose by the conspirators, had orders promptly to despatch their

[013-083]victim. But the foul deed was not done unwitnessed. Two priests of the

[013-084]Bishop of Cork followed the Prince, who, when arrested, snatched up

[013-085]"the Gospel of St. Barry," on which Molloy was to have sworn his

[013-086]fealty. As the swords of the assassins were aimed at his heart, he held

[013-087]up the Gospel for a protection, and his blood spouting out, stained the

[013-088]Sacred Scriptures. The priests, taking up the blood-stained volume,

[013-089]fled to their Bishop, spreading the horrid story as they went. The

[013-090]venerable successor of St. Barry "wept bitterly, and uttered a prophecy

[013-091]concerning the future fate of the murderers;" a prophecy which was very

[013-092]speedily fulfilled.

[013-093]This was in the year 976, three or four years before the battle of Tara

[013-094]and the accession of Malachy. When the news of his noble-hearted

[013-095]brother's murder was brought to Brian, at Kinkora, he was seized with

[013-096]the most violent grief. His favourite harp was taken down, and he sang

[013-097]the death-song of Mahon, recounting all the glorious actions of his

[013-098]life. His anger flashed out through his tears, as he wildly chanted

[013-099]"My heart shall burst within my breast,

[013-100]Unless I avenge this great king;

[013-101]They shall forfeit life for this foul deed

[013-102]Or I must perish by a violent death."

[013-103]But the climax of his lament was, that Mahon "had not fallen in battle

[013-104]behind the shelter of his shield, rather than trust in the treacherous

[013-105]words of Donovan." Brian was now in his thirty-fifth year, was married,

[013-106]and had several children. Morrogh, his eldest, was able to bear arms,

[013-107]and shared in his ardour and ambition. "His first effort," says an old

[013-108]Chronicle, "was directed against Donovan's allies, the Danes of

[013-109]Limerick, and he slew Ivar their king, and two of his sons." These

[013-110]conspirators, foreseeing their fate, had retired into the holy isle of

[013-111]Scattery, but Brian slew them between "the horns of the altar." For

[013-112]this violation of the sanctuary, considering his provocation, he was

[013-113]little blamed. He next turned his rage against Donovan, who had called

[013-114]to his aid the Danish townsmen of Desmond. "Brian," says the Annalist

[013-115]of Innisfallen, "gave them battle where Auliffe and his Danes, and

[013-116]Donovan and his Irish forces, were all cut off." After that battle,

[013-117]Brian sent a challenge to Molloy, of Desmond, according to the custom

[013-118]of that age, to meet him in arms near Macroom, where the usual

[013-119]coalition, Danes and Irish, were against him. He completely routed the

[013-120]enemy, and his son Morrogh, then but a lad, "killed the murderer of his

[013-121]uncle Mahon with his own hand." Molloy was buried on the north side of

[013-122]the mountain where Mahon was murdered and interred; on Mahon the

[013-123]southward sun shone full and fair; but on the grave of his assassin,

[013-124]the black shadow of the northern sky rested always. Such was the

[013-125]tradition which all Munster piously believed. After this victory over

[013-126]Molloy, son of Bran (A.D. 978), Brian was universally acknowledged King

[013-127]of Munster, and until Malachy had won the battle of Tara, was justly

[013-128]considered the first Irish captain of his age.

[013-130]Malachy, in the first year of his reign, having received the hostages

[013-131]of the Danes of Dublin, having liberated the Irish prisoners and

[013-132]secured the unity of his own territory, had his attention drawn,

[013-133]naturally enough, towards Brian's movements. Whether Brian had refused

[013-134]him homage, or that his revival of the old claim to the half-kingdom

[013-135]was his offence, or from whatever immediate cause, Malachy marched

[013-136]southwards, enforcing homage as he went. Entering Thomond he plundered

[013-137]the Dalcassians, and marching to the mound at Adair, where, under an

[013-138]old oak, the kings of Thomond had long been inaugurated, he caused it

[013-139]to be "dug from the earth with its roots," and cut into pieces. This

[013-140]act of Malachy's certainly bespeaks an embittered and aggressive

[013-141]spirit, and the provocation must, indeed, have been grievous to

[013-142]palliate so barbarous an action. But we are not informed what the

[013-143]provocation was. At the time Brian was in Ossory enforcing his tribute;

[013-144]the next year we find him seizing the person of Gilla-Patrick, Lord of

[013-145]Ossory, and soon after he burst into Meath, avenging with fire and

[013-146]sword the wanton destruction of his ancestral oak.

[013-147]Thus were these two powerful Princes openly embroiled with each other.

[013-148]We have no desire to dwell on all the details of their struggle, which

[013-149]continued for fully twenty years. About the year 987, Brian was

[013-150]practically king of half Ireland, and having the power, (though not the

[013-151]title,) he did not suffer any part of it to lie waste. His activity was

[013-152]incapable of exhaustion; in Ossory, in Leinster, in Connaught, his

[013-153]voice and his arm were felt everywhere. But a divided authority was of

[013-154]necessity so favourable to invasion, that the Danish power began to

[013-155]loom up to its old proportions. Sitrick, "with the silken beard," one

[013-156]of the ablest of Danish leaders, was then at Dublin, and his occasional

[013-157]incursions were so formidable, that they produced (what probably

[013-158]nothing else could have done) an alliance between Brian and Malachy,

[013-159]which lasted for three years, and was productive of the best

[013-160]consequences. Thus, in 997, they imposed their yoke on Dublin, taking

[013-161]"hostages and jewels" from the foreigners. Reinforcements arriving from

[013-162]the North, the indomitable Danes proceeded to plunder Leinster, but

[013-163]were routed by Brian and Malachy at Glen-Mama, in Wicklow, with the

[013-164]loss of 6,000 men and all their chief captains. Immediately after this

[013-165]victory the two kings, according to the Annals, "entered into Dublin,

[013-166]and the fort thereof, and there remained seven nights, and at their

[013-167]departure took all the gold, silver, hangings, and other precious

[013-168]things that were there with them, burnt the town, broke down the fort,

[013-169]and banished Sitrick from thence" (A.D. 999).

[013-170]The next three years of Brian's life are the most complex in his

[013-171]career. After resting a night in Meath, with Malachy, he proceeded with

[013-172]his forces towards Armagh, nominally on a pilgrimage, but really, as it

[013-173]would seem, to extend his party. He remained in the sacred city a week,

[013-174]and presented ten ounces of gold, at the Cathedral altar. The

[013-175]Archbishop Marian received him with the distinction due to so eminent a

[013-176]guest, and a record of his visit, in which he is styled "Imperator of

[013-177]the Irish," was entered in the book of St. Patrick. He, however, got no

[013-178]hostages in the North, but on his march southward, he learned that the

[013-179]Danes had returned to Dublin, were rebuilding the City and Fort, and

[013-180]were ready to offer submission and hostages to him, while refusing both

[013-181]to Malachy. Here Brian's eagerness for supremacy misled him. He

[013-182]accepted the hostages, joined the foreign forces to his own, and even

[013-183]gave his daughter in marriage to Sitrick of "the silken beard."

[013-184]Immediately he broke with Malachy, and with his new allies and

[013-185]son-in-law, marched into Meath in hostile array. Malachy, however,

[013-186]stood to his defence; attacked and defeated Brian's advance guard of

[013-187]Danish horse, and the latter, unwilling apparently to push matters to

[013-188]extremities, retired as he came, without "battle, or hostage, or spoil

[013-189]of any kind."

[013-190]But his design of securing the monarchy was not for an instant

[013-191]abandoned, and, by combined diplomacy and force, he effected his end.

[013-192]His whole career would have been incomplete without that last and

[013-193]highest conquest over every rival. Patiently but surely he had gathered

[013-194]influence and authority, by arms, by gifts, by connections on all

[013-195]sides. He had propitiated the chief families of Connaught by his first

[013-196]marriage with More, daughter of O'Heyne, and his second marriage with

[013-197]Duvchalvay, daughter of O'Conor. He had obtained one of the daughters

[013-198]of Godwin, the powerful Earl of Kent, for his second son; had given a

[013-199]daughter to the Prince of Scots, and another to the Danish King of

[013-200]Dublin.

[013-201]Malachy, in diplomatic skill, in foresight, and in tenacity of purpose,

[013-202]was greatly inferior to Brian, though in personal gallantry and other

[013-203]princely qualities, every way his equal. He was of a hospitable,

[013-204]out-spoken, enjoying disposition, as we gather from many characteristic

[013-205]anecdotes. He is spoken of as "being generally computed the best

[013-206]horseman in those parts of Europe;" and as one who "delighted to ride a

[013-207]horse that was never broken, handled, or ridden, until the age of seven

[013-208]years." From an ancient story, which represents him as giving his

[013-209]revenues for a year to one of the Court Poets and then fighting him

[013-210]with a "headless staff" to compel the Poet to return them, it would

[013-211]appear that his good humour and profusion were equal to his

[013-212]horsemanship. Finding Brian's influence still on the increase west of

[013-213]the Shannon, Malachy, in the year of our Lord 1000, threw two bridges

[013-214]across the Shannon, one at Athlone, the other at the present

[013-215]Lanesborough. This he did with the consent and assistance of O'Conor,

[013-216]but the issue was as usual—he made the bridges, and Brian profited by

[013-217]them. While Malachy was at Athlone superintending the work, Brian

[013-218]arrived with a great force recruited from all quarters (except Ulster),

[013-219]including Danish men-in-armour. At Athlone was held the conference so

[013-220]memorable in our annals, in which Brian gave his rival the alternative

[013-221]of a pitched battle, within a stated time, or abdication. According to

[013-222]the Southern Annalists, first a month, and afterwards a year, were

[013-223]allowed the Monarch to make his choice. At the expiration of the time

[013-224]Brian marched into Meath, and encamped at Tara, where Malachy, having

[013-225]vainly endeavoured to secure the alliance of the Northern Hy-Nial in

[013-226]the interval, came and submitted to Brian without safeguard or surety.

[013-227]The unmade monarch was accompanied by a guard "of twelve score

[013-228]horsemen," and on his arrival, proceeded straight to the tent of his

[013-229]successor. Here the rivals contended in courtesy, as they had often

[013-230]done in arms, and when they separated, Brian, as Lord Paramount,

[013-231]presented Malachy as many horses as he had horsemen in his train when

[013-232]he came to visit him. This event happened in the year 1001, when Brian

[013-233]was in his 60th and Malachy in his 53rd year. There were present at the

[013-234]Assembly all the princes and chiefs of the Irish, except the Prince of

[013-235]Aileach, and the Lords of Oriel, Ulidia, Tyrowen and Tyrconnell, who

[013-236]were equally unwilling to assist Malachy or to acknowledge Brian. What

[013-237]is still more remarkable is, the presence in this national assembly of

[013-238]the Danish Lords of Dublin, Carmen (Wexford), Waterford and Cork, whom

[013-239]Brian, at this time, was trying hard to conciliate by gifts and

[013-240]alliances.
