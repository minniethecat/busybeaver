## Overview

James Napper Tandy (1740–1803) was a Dublin merchant and one of the most colourful figures among the United Irishmen. He served as the first Secretary of the Dublin Society of United Irishmen and later commanded a French expeditionary force that landed in Ireland during the aftermath of the 1798 Rebellion. (100-051) (104-246)

Tandy's career combined merchant respectability with a passionate commitment to republican principles. His active middle age was devoted to the cause of Irish independence, for which he ultimately suffered exile and death in a foreign land. (100-051) (104-253)

## The Dublin Society of United Irishmen

When the United Irishmen first organized in Dublin, Tandy was chosen as Secretary of the society. The Chairman was Simon Butler, a leading barrister, and together they constituted the public face of the movement in its earliest phase. (100-050) (100-051)

> Simon Butler, a leading barrister, was chosen Chairman, and Mr. Napper Tandy, an active middle-aged merchant, with strong republican principles, was Secretary. [100-050] [100-051]

The solemn declaration or oath of the United Irishmen was administered to new members under Tandy's authority as Secretary. The oath bound every member "to forward a brotherhood of affection, an identity of interests, and a communion of rights" among Irishmen of every religious persuasion. (100-052) (100-053)

## The Back Lane Parliament and the National Guard

As the United Irish movement grew more militant, Tandy became a central figure in the so-called "Back Lane Parliament" — the great popular gatherings held in Dublin where republican sentiment was openly expressed. Here Tandy addressed his passionate harangues to the assembled crowds. (100-084) (100-085)

> Here Tandy, in the uniform of his new National Guard, whose standard bore the harp without the crown, addressed his passionate harangues to the delegates and spectators who thronged the meetings of the Back Lane Parliament. [100-085] [100-086]

The National Guard, under Tandy's command, adopted the harp without the crown as its standard — a deliberate republican repudiation of the British monarchy and a powerful visual symbol of the separatist aspirations of the United Irishmen. (100-085) (100-086)

## The French Expedition of 1798

The year 1798 was illustrated not only by General Humbert's more famous landing in Mayo but also by a weaker demonstration under General Reay and Napper Tandy. With a single armed brig, these daring allies made a landing on the coast of Donegal in September. (104-245) (104-246) (104-247)

> The year '98, was illustrated not only by Humbert's unauthorized adventure, but by a still weaker demonstration under General Reay and Napper Tandy, about the same time. With a single armed brig these daring allies made good their landing upon the northern coast. [104-245] [104-246] [104-247]

The expedition was too small and too late to alter the course of the rebellion, which had already been crushed in Wexford and Antrim. Tandy and his comrades, finding no general rising to support them, were forced to re-embark and return to France. (104-246)

## Exile and Death

After the failure of the 1798 enterprise, Tandy settled among the refugee community in France. He continued in old age the dashing career of his manhood, expiating in exile the crime of preferring the country of his birth to the country of his allegiance. (104-253) (104-254)

> Tandy, to continue in old age the dashing career of his manhood, and to expiate in exile the crime of preferring the country of his birth to the country of his allegiance. [104-253] [104-254]

His name appears among the principal refugees at Paris and Hamburg, alongside Tone, Lewines, Deane Swift, Major Plunkett, and other leading United Irishmen proscribed by the British government. Tandy died in France in 1803, the same year that Robert Emmet launched his ill-fated rising in Dublin. (105-105) (105-106)

## Legacy

Napper Tandy is remembered as a symbol of the colourful, quixotic patriotism of the United Irish era. His name has entered Irish folk memory through ballads and popular tradition, representing the merchant radical who sacrificed comfort and country for the cause of Irish liberty. (104-253)

His career illustrates the interconnectedness of the Irish revolutionary movement with revolutionary France, and the ultimate dependence of the United Irishmen on French military assistance — a dependence that proved fatal when that assistance arrived too little and too late. (104-245) (104-246)
