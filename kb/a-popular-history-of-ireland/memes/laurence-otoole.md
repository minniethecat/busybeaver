## Overview

Laurence O'Toole (1128–1180), or Lorcán Ua Tuathail, was the Archbishop of [[dublin|Dublin]] during the most critical period of the Anglo-Norman invasion of Ireland. As ecclesiastical leader, statesman, and saint, he mediated between the Irish High King [[Rory OConor]] and the forces of King [[Henry VIII|Henry II]] of England. His role as "Chancellor of the Irish King" and his diplomatic missions to the English court placed him at the centre of the political and ecclesiastical transformation of late twelfth-century Ireland. (028-034) (028-215)

O'Toole was canonised as a saint, and his life exemplified the transition from the old Gaelic ecclesiastical order to the new Anglo-Norman church establishment. His deathbed concerns reflected the uncertain prospects of his native land under the new regime. (028-082) (028-097)

## Biography

### Early Life and Ecclesiastical Rise

Laurence O'Toole was born into the prominent O'Toole dynasty of [[leinster|Leinster]]. Rising through the ranks of the Irish Church, he became Archbishop of [[dublin|Dublin]], one of the most important ecclesiastical sees in the country. His reputation for piety and diplomatic skill made him a key figure in the complex political landscape of pre-invasion Ireland. (028-034)

By the 1170s, as the Anglo-Norman adventurers consolidated their foothold, O'Toole had emerged as a trusted counsellor to the High King Roderick O'Conor and a respected voice among the Irish clergy and nobility.

### The Windsor Negotiations

In 1175, with King Roderick O'Conor harassed by the rebellion of his eldest son and by the advancing Normans, Laurence O'Toole was chosen as one of the three ambassadors to the Court of Windsor. Alongside Catholicus, Archbishop of Tuam, and Concors, Abbot of St. Brendan's, Laurence was tasked with negotiating an alliance with [[henry-ii|Henry II]] that would leave Roderick free to combat his domestic enemies. (028-034)

> "Roderick despatched to the Court of Windsor Catholicus, Archbishop of Tuam, Concors, Abbot of St. Brendan's, and Laurence, Archbishop of Dublin, whose is styled in these proceedings, 'Chancellor of the Irish King,' to negotiate an alliance with Henry, which would leave him free to combat against his domestic enemies." (028-034)

The resulting Treaty of Windsor (1175) recognized Roderick's sovereignty over Ireland—excluding those cantreds and cities actually possessed by Henry's subjects—and subinfeudated his authority to Henry after the manner adopted towards William, King of Scotland. An annual tribute of one merchantable hide for every tenth hide of cattle was agreed upon. Though the treaty proceeded on "the wild assumption that the feudal system was of force among the free clans of Erin," it was solemnly approved by a special Council and formed the basis of Henry's later grant of the Lordship of Ireland to his son John. (028-042)

### The Battle of Howth and the St. Laurence Lineage

In 1177, when [[john-de-courcy|John de Courcy]] resolved to invade [[ulster|Ulster]], one of his closest companions was Sir Almaric of St. Laurence, a relative of the Archbishop. As de Courcy and his knights marched out of [[dublin|Dublin]] with five hundred men-at-arms, they were attacked by a native force near Howth. There, Saint Laurence "laid in victory the foundation of that title still possessed by his posterity." The family name and the peerage of Howth both trace their origin to this engagement. (028-092) (028-097)

> "His inseparable brother-in-arms, Sir Almaric of St. Laurence, his relative... Hardly had they got beyond sight of the city, when they were attacked by a native force, near Howth, where Saint Laurence laid in victory the foundation of that title still possessed by his posterity." (028-092) (028-097)

### Death and Canonisation

By the late 1170s, the Norman conquest was accelerating under commanders like [[john-de-courcy|John de Courcy]], who pushed the outposts of his race into the heart of [[ulster|Ulster]]. Fitz-Aldelm, the commander at [[dublin|Dublin]], had shown no great capacity for following up the conquest. It was in this context that Laurence O'Toole lay on his deathbed, his mind troubled by the fate of his country. (028-081)

> "The prospects of his native land were, at that moment, of a cast which might well disturb the death-bed of the sainted Laurence." (028-082)

## Statesmanship and Legacy

Laurence O'Toole was more than a churchman—he was a statesman whose diplomatic skill was recognised by both Irish and Norman authorities. That Roderick O'Conor was capable of conceiving the complex diplomatic strategy represented by the Treaty of Windsor can be inferred from "the rank of Chancellor which he conferred, and the other negotiations which he entrusted to that great man." (028-215)

His successors in the see of [[dublin|Dublin]] reflected the changed political order. When Prince John—later King John of England—was sent to Ireland in 1185, he was preceded by "Archbishop Cuming, the English successor of St. Laurence." The shift from an Irish to an English Archbishop of [[dublin|Dublin]] symbolised the broader transformation O'Toole had struggled to manage during his lifetime. (029-047)

The St. Laurence family name continued in Irish history for centuries. Sir Armoric St. Laurence, founder of the Howth family, fell in battle in 1190 at the head of a numerous division. Centuries later, a decisive battle was fought on "St. Laurence's day—the 10th of August," at Athenry, where the steel-clad Norman battalion once more triumphed over the clansmen of the west. (029-140) (037-083)
