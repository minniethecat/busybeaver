CHAPTER III.

[011-001]REIGN OF FLAN "OF THE SHANNON" (A.D. 879 TO 916).

[011-002]Midway in the reign we are called upon to contemplate, falls the

[011-003]centenary of the first invasion of Ireland by the Northmen. Let us

[011-004]admit that the scenes of that century are stirring and stimulating; two

[011-005]gallant races of men, in all points strongly contrasted, contend for

[011-006]the most part in the open field, for the possession of a beautiful and

[011-007]fertile island. Let us admit that the Milesian-Irish, themselves

[011-008]invaders and conquerors of an older date, may have had no right to

[011-009]declare the era of colonization closed for their country, while its

[011-010]best harbours were without ships, and leagues of its best land were

[011-011]without inhabitants; yet what gives to the contest its lofty and

[011-012]fearful interest, is, that the foreigners who come so far and fight so

[011-013]bravely for the prize, are a Pagan people, drunk with the evil spirit

[011-014]of one of the most anti-Christian forms of human error. And what is

[011-015]still worse, and still more to be lamented, it is becoming, after the

[011-016]experience of a century, plainer and plainer, that the Christian

[011-017]natives, while defending with unfaltering courage their beloved

[011-018]country, are yet descending more and more to the moral level of their

[011-019]assailants, without the apology of their Paganism. Degenerate

[011-020]civilisation may be a worse element for truth to work in than original

[011-021]barbarism; and, therefore, as we enter on the second century of this

[011-022]struggle, we begin to fear for the Christian Irish, _not_ from the arms

[011-023]or the valour, but from the contact and example of the unbelievers.

[011-024]This, it is necessary to premise, before presenting to the reader a

[011-025]succession of Bishops who lead armies to battle, of Abbots whose voice

[011-026]is still for war, of treacherous tactics and savage punishments; of the

[011-027]almost total disruption of the last links of that federal bond, which,

[011-028]"though light as air were strong as iron," before the charm of

[011-029]inviolability had been taken away from the ancient constitution.

[011-030]We begin to discern in this reign that royal marriages have much to do

[011-031]with war and politics. Hugh, the late king, left a widow, named

[011-032]Maelmara ("follower of Mary"), daughter to Kenneth M'Alpine, King of

[011-033]the Caledonian Scots: this lady Flan married. The mother of Flan was

[011-034]the daughter of Dungal, Prince of Ossory, so that to the cotemporary

[011-035]lords of that borderland the monarch stood in the relation of cousin. A

[011-036]compact seems to have been entered into in the past reign, that the

[011-037]_Roydamna_, or successor, should be chosen alternately from the

[011-038]Northern and Southern Hy-Nial; and, subsequently, when Nial, son of his

[011-039]predecessor, assumed that onerous rank, Flan gave him his daughter

[011-040]Gormley, celebrated for her beauty, her talents, and her heartlessness,

[011-041]in marriage. From these several family ties, uniting him so closely

[011-042]with Ossory, with the Scots, and with his successor, much of the wars

[011-043]and politics of Flan Siona's reign take their cast and complexion. A

[011-044]still more fruitful source of new complications was the co-equal power,

[011-045]acquired through a long series of aggressions, by the kings of Cashel.

[011-046]Their rivalry with the monarchy, from the beginning of the eighth till

[011-047]the end of the tenth century, was a constant cause of intrigues,

[011-048]coalitions, and wars, reminding us of the constant rivalry of Athens

[011-049]with Sparta, of Genoa with Venice. This kingship of Cashel, according

[011-050]to the Munster law of succession, "the will of Olild," ought to have

[011-051]alternated regularly between the descendants of his sons, Eugene More

[011-052]and Cormac Cas—the Eugenians and Dalcassians. But the families of the

[011-053]former kindred were for many centuries the more powerful of the two,

[011-054]and frequently set at nought the testamentary law of their common

[011-055]ancestor, leaving the tribe of Cas but the border-land of Thomond, from

[011-056]which they had sometimes to pay tribute to Cruachan, and at others to

[011-057]Cashel. In the ninth century the competition among the Eugenian

[011-058]houses—of which too many were of too nearly equal strength—seems to

[011-059]have suggested a new expedient, with the view of permanently setting

[011-060]aside the will of Olild. This was, to confer the kingship when vacant,

[011-061]on whoever happened to be Bishop of Emly or of Cashel, or on some other

[011-062]leading ecclesiastical dignitary, always provided that he was of

[011-063]Eugenian descent; a qualification easily to be met with, since the

[011-064]great sees and abbacies were now filled, for the most part, by the sons

[011-065]of the neighbouring chiefs. In this way we find Cenfalad, Felim, and

[011-066]Olcobar, in this century, styled Prince-Bishops or Prince-Abbots. The

[011-067]principal domestic difficulty of Flan Siona's reign followed from the

[011-068]elevation of Cormac, son of Cuillenan, from the see of Emly to the

[011-069]throne of Cashel.

[011-070]Cormac, a scholar, and, as became his calling, a man of peace, was

[011-071]thus, by virtue of his accession, the representative of the old quarrel

[011-072]between his predecessors and the dominant race of kings. All Munster

[011-073]asserted that it was never the intention of their common ancestors to

[011-074]subject the southern half of Erin to the sway of the north; that Eber

[011-075]and Owen More had resisted such pretensions when advanced by Eremhon

[011-076]and Conn of the Hundred Battles; that the _esker_ from Dublin to Galway

[011-077]was the true division, and that, even admitting the title of the

[011-078]Hy-Nial king as Ard-Righ, all the tribes south of the _esker_, whether

[011-079]in Leinster or Connaught, still owed tribute by ancient right to

[011-080]Cashel. Their antiquaries had their own version in of "the Book of

[011-081]Rights," which countenanced these claims to co-equal dominion, and

[011-082]their Bards drew inspiration from the same high pretensions. Party

[011-083]spirit ran so high that tales and prophecies were invented to show how

[011-084]St. Patrick had laid his curse on Tara, and promised dominion to Cashel

[011-085]and to Dublin in its stead. All Leinster, except the lordship of

[011-086]Ossory—identical with the present diocese of the same name-was held by

[011-087]the _Brehons_ of Cashel to be tributary to their king; and this

[011-088]_Borooa_ or tribute, abandoned by the monarchs at the intercession of

[011-089]Saint Moling, was claimed for the Munster rulers as an inseparable

[011-090]adjunct of their southern kingdom.

[011-091]The first act of Flan Siona, on his accession, was to dash into

[011-092]Munster, demanding hostages at the point of the sword, and sweeping

[011-093]over both Thomond and Desmond with irresistible force, from Clare to

[011-094]Cork. With equal promptitude he marched through every territory of

[011-095]Ulster, securing, by the pledges of their heirs and _Tanists_, the

[011-096]chiefs of the elder tribes of the Hy-Nial. So effectually did he

[011-097]consider his power established over the provinces, that he is said to

[011-098]have boasted to one of his hostages, that he would, with no other

[011-099]attendants than his own servants, play a game of chess on Thurles

[011-100]Green, without fear of interruption. Carrying out this foolish wager,

[011-101]he accordingly went to his game at Thurles, and was very properly taken

[011-102]prisoner for his temerity, and made to pay a smart ransom to his

[011-103]captors. So runs the tale, which, whether true or fictitious, is not

[011-104]without its moral. Flan experienced greater difficulty with the tribes

[011-105]of Connaught, nor was it till the thirteenth year of his reign (892)

[011-106]that Cathal, their Prince, "came into his house," in Meath, "under the

[011-107]protection of the clergy" of Clonmacnoise, and made peace with him. A

[011-108]brief interval of repose seems to have been vouchsafed to this Prince,

[011-109]in the last years of the century; but a storm was gathering over

[011-110]Cashel, and the high pretensions of the Eugenian line were again to be

[011-111]put to the hazard of battle.

[011-112]Cormac, the Prince-Bishop, began his rule over Munster in the year 900

[011-113]of our common era, and passed some years in peace, after his accession.

[011-114]If we believe his panegyrists, the land over which he bore sway, "was

[011-115]filled with divine grace and worldly prosperity," and with order so

[011-116]unbroken, "that the cattle needed no cowherd, and the flocks no

[011-117]shepherd, so long as he was king." Himself an antiquary and a lover of

[011-118]learning, it seems but natural that "many books were written, and many

[011-119]schools opened," by his liberality. During this enviable interval,

[011-120]councillors of less pacific mood than their studious master were not

[011-121]wanting to stimulate his sense of kingly duty, by urging him to assert

[011-122]the claim of Munster to the tribute of the southern half of Erin. As an

[011-123]antiquary himself, Cormac must have been bred up in undoubting belief

[011-124]in the justice of that claim, and must have given judgment in favour of

[011-125]its antiquity and validity, before his accession. These _dicta_ of his

[011-126]own were now quoted with emphasis, and he was besought to enforce, by

[011-127]all the means within his reach, the learned judgments he himself had

[011-128]delivered. The most active advocate of a recourse to arms was Flaherty,

[011-129]Abbot of Scattery, in the Shannon, himself an Eugenian, and the kinsman

[011-130]of Cormac. After many objections, the peaceful Prince-Bishop allowed

[011-131]himself to be persuaded, and in the year 907 he took up his line of

[011-132]march, "in the fortnight of the harvest," from Cashel toward Gowran, at

[011-133]the head of all the armament of Munster. Lorcan, son of Lactna, and

[011-134]grandfather of Brian, commanded the Dalcassians, under Cormac; and

[011-135]Oliol, lord of Desies, and the warlike Abbot of Scattery, led on the

[011-136]other divisions. The monarch marched southward to meet his assailants,

[011-137]with his own proper troops, and the contingents of Connaught under

[011-138]Cathel, Prince of that Province, and those of Leinster under the lead

[011-139]of Kerball, their king. Both armies met at Ballaghmoon, in the southern

[011-140]corner of Kildare, not far from the present town of Carlow, and both

[011-141]fought with most heroic bravery. The Munster forces were utterly

[011-142]defeated; the Lords of Desies, of Fermoy, of Kinalmeaky, and of Kerry,

[011-143]the Abbots of Cork and Kennity, and Cormac himself, with 6,000 men,

[011-144]fell on the ensanguined field. The losses of the victors are not

[011-145]specified, but the 6,000, we may hope, included the total of the slain

[011-146]on both sides. Flan at once improved the opportunity of victory by

[011-147]advancing into Ossory, and establishing his cousin Dermid, son of

[011-148]Kerball, over that territory. This Dermid, who appears to have been

[011-149]banished by Munster intrigues, had long resided with his royal cousin,

[011-150]previous to the battle, from which he was probably the only one that

[011-151]derived any solid advantage. As to the Abbot Flaherty, the instigator

[011-152]of this ill-fated expedition, he escaped from the conquerors, and, safe

[011-153]in his island sanctuary, gave himself up for a while to penitential

[011-154]rigours. The worldly spirit, however, was not dead in his breast, and

[011-155]after the decease of Cormac's next successor, he emerged from his cell,

[011-156]and was elevated to the kingship of Cashel.

[011-158]In the earlier and middle years of this long reign, the invasions from

[011-159]the Baltic had diminished both in force and in frequency. This is to be

[011-160]accounted for from the fact, that during its entire length it was

[011-161]contemporaneous with the reign of Harold, "the Fair-haired" King of

[011-162]Norway, the scourge of the sea-kings. This more fortunate Charles XII.,

[011-163]born in 853, died at the age of 81, after sixty years of almost

[011-164]unbroken successes, over all his Danish, Swedish, and insular enemies.

[011-165]It is easy to comprehend, by reference to his exploits upon the Baltic,

[011-166]the absence of the usual northern force from the Irish waters, during

[011-167]his lifetime, and that of his cotemporary, Flan of the Shannon. Yet the

[011-168]race of the sea-kings was not extinguished by the fair-haired Harold's

[011-169]victories over them, at home. Several of them permanently abandoned

[011-170]their native coasts never to return, and recruited their colonies,

[011-171]already so numerous, in the Orkneys, Scotland, England, Ireland, and

[011-172]the Isle of Man. In 885, Flan was repulsed in an attack on Dublin, in

[011-173]which repulse the Abbots of Kildare and Kildalkey were slain; in the

[011-174]year 890, Aileach was surprised and plundered by Danes, for the first

[011-175]time, and Armagh shared its fate; in 887, 888, and 891, three minor

[011-176]victories were gained over separate hordes, in Mayo, at Waterford, and

[011-177]in Ulidia (Down). In 897, Dublin was taken for the first time in sixty

[011-178]years, its chiefs put to death, while its garrison fled in their ships

[011-179]beyond sea. But in the first quarter of the tenth century, better

[011-180]fortune begins to attend the Danish cause. A new generation enters on

[011-181]the scene, who dread no more the long arm of the age-stricken Harold,

[011-182]nor respect the treaties which bound their predecessors in Britain to

[011-183]the great Alfred. In 912, Waterford received from sea a strong

[011-184]reinforcement, and about the same date, or still earlier, Dublin, from

[011-185]which they had been expelled in 897, was again in their possession. In

[011-186]913, and for several subsequent years, the southern garrisons continued

[011-187]their ravages in Munster, where the warlike Abbot of Scattery found a

[011-188]more suitable object for the employment of his valour than that which

[011-189]brought him, with the studious Cormac, to the fatal field of

[011-190]Ballaghmoon.

[011-191]The closing days of Flan of the Shannon were embittered and darkened by

[011-192]the unnatural rebellion of his sons, Connor and Donogh, and his

[011-193]successor, Nial, surnamed _Black-Knee_ (_Glundubh_), the husband of his

[011-194]daughter, Gormley. These children were by his second marriage with

[011-195]Gormley, daughter of that son of Conaing, whose name has already

[011-196]appeared in connection with the plundered sepulchres upon the Boyne. At

[011-197]the age of three score and upwards Flan is frequently obliged to

[011-198]protect by recourse to arms his mensal lands in Meath—their favourite

[011-199]point of attack—or to defend some faithful adherent whom these

[011-200]unnatural Princes sought to oppress. The daughter of Flan, thus wedded

[011-201]to a husband in arms against her father, seems to have been as little

[011-202]dutiful as his sons. We have elegiac stanzas by her on the death of two

[011-203]of her husbands and of one of her sons, but none on the death of her

[011-204]father: although this form of tribute to the departed, by those skilled

[011-205]in such compositions, seems to have been as usual as the ordinary

[011-206]prayers for the dead.

[011-207]At length, in the 37th year of his reign, and the 68th of his age, King

[011-208]Flan was at the end of his sorrows. As became the prevailing character

[011-209]of his life, he died peacefully, in a religious house at Kyneigh, in

[011-210]Kildare, on the 8th of June, in the year 916, of the common era. The

[011-211]Bards praise his "fine shape" and "august mien," as well as his

[011-212]"pleasant and hospitable" private habits. Like all the kings of his

[011-213]race he seems to have been brave enough: but he was no lover of war for

[011-214]war's-sake, and the only great engagement in his long reign was brought

[011-215]on by enemies who left him no option but to fight. His munificence

[011-216]rebuilt the Cathedral of Clonmacnoise, with the co-operation of Colman,

[011-217]the Abbot, the year after the battle of Ballaghmoon (908); for which

[011-218]age, it was the largest and finest stone Church in Ireland. His charity

[011-219]and chivalry both revolted at the cruel excesses of war, and when the

[011-220]head of Cormac of Cashel was presented to him after his victory, he

[011-221]rebuked those who rejoiced over his rival's fall, kissed reverently the

[011-222]lips of the dead, and ordered the relics to be delivered, as Cormac had

[011-223]himself willed it, to the Church of Castledermot, for Christian burial.

[011-224]These traits of character, not less than his family afflictions, and

[011-225]the generally peaceful tenor of his long life, have endeared to many

[011-226]the memory of Flan of the Shannon.
