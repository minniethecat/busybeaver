CHAPTER X.

[073-001]CROMWELL'S CAMPAIGN—-1649-1650.

[073-002]An actor was now to descend upon the scene, whose character has excited

[073-003]more controversy than that of any other personage of those times.

[073-004]Honoured as a saint, or reprobated as a hypocrite, worshipped for his

[073-005]extraordinary successes, or anathematized for the unworthy artifices by

[073-006]which he rose—who shall deal out, with equal hand, praise and blame to

[073-007]Oliver Cromwell? Not for the popular writer of Irish history, is that

[073-008]difficult judicial task. Not for us to re-echo cries of hatred which

[073-009]convince not the indifferent, nor correct the errors of the educated or

[073-010]cultivated: the simple, and, as far as possible, the unimpassioned

[073-011]narrative of facts, will constitute the whole of our duty towards the

[073-012]Protector's campaign in Ireland.

[073-013]Cromwell left London in great state, early in July, "in a coach drawn

[073-014]by six gallant Flanders mares," and made a sort of royal procession

[073-015]across the country to Bristol. From that famous port, where Strongbow

[073-016]confederated with Dermid McMurrogh, and from which Dublin drew its

[073-017]first Anglo-Norman colony, he went on to Milford Haven, at which he

[073-018]embarked, arriving in Dublin on the 15th of August. He entered the city

[073-019]in procession, and addressed the townsfolk from "a convenient place."

[073-020]He had with him two hundred thousand pounds in money, eight regiments

[073-021]of foot, six of horse, and some troops of dragoons; besides the

[073-022]divisions of Jones and Monck, already in the country, and subject to

[073-023]his command. Among the officers were names of memorable interest—Henry

[073-024]Cromwell, second son of the Protector, and future Lord Deputy; Monck,

[073-025]Blake, Jones, Ireton, Ludlow, Hardress Waller, Sankey, and others

[073-026]equally prominent in accomplishing the King's death, or in raising up

[073-027]the English commonwealth.

[073-028]Cromwell's command in Ireland extends from the middle of August, 1649,

[073-029]to the end of May, 1650, about nine months in all, and is remarkable

[073-030]for the number of sieges of walled towns crowded into that brief

[073-031]period. There was, during the whole time, no great action in the field,

[073-032]like Marston Moor, or Benburb, or Dunbar; it was a campaign of

[073-033]seventeenth century cannon against mediaeval masonry; what else was

[073-034]done, was the supplemental work of mutual bravery on both sides.

[073-035]Drogheda, Dundalk, Newry, and Carlingford fell in September; Arklow,

[073-036]Enniscorthy, and Wexford in October; Ross, one of the first seaports in

[073-037]point of commerce, surrendered the same month; Waterford was attempted

[073-038]and abandoned in November; Dungarvan, Kinsale, Bandon, and Cork were

[073-039]won over by Lord Broghill in December; Fethard, Callan, and Cashel in

[073-040]January and February; Carrick and Kilkenny in March; and Clonmel, early

[073-041]in May. Immediately after this last capitulation, Cromwell was recalled

[073-042]to lead the armies of the Parliament into Scotland: during the nine

[073-043]months he had commanded in Ireland, he had captured five or six county

[073-044]capitals, and a great number of less considerable places. The terror of

[073-045]his siege-trains and Ironsides was spread over the greater part of

[073-046]three Provinces, and his well-reported successes had proved so many

[073-047]steps to the assumption of that sovereign power at which he already

[073-048]aimed.

[073-049]Of the spirit in which these several sieges were conducted, it is

[073-050]impossible to speak without a shudder. It was, in truth, a spirit of

[073-051]hatred and fanaticism, altogether beyond the control of the

[073-052]revolutionary leader. At Drogheda, the work of slaughter occupied five

[073-053]entire days. Of the brave garrison of 3,000 men, not thirty were

[073-054]spared, and these, "were in hands for the Barbadoes;" old men, women,

[073-055]children and priests, were unsparingly put to the sword. Wexford was

[073-056]basely betrayed by Captain James Stafford, commander of the castle,

[073-057]whose midnight interview with Cromwell, at a petty rivulet without the

[073-058]walls, tradition still recounts with horror and detestation. This port

[073-059]was particularly obnoxious to the Parliament, as from its advantageous

[073-060]position on the Bristol channel, its cruisers greatly annoyed and

[073-061]embarrassed their commerce. "There are," Cromwell writes to Speaker

[073-062]Lenthall, "great quantities of iron, hides, tallow, salt, pipe and

[073-063]barrel staves, which are under commissioners' hands to be secured. We

[073-064]believe there are near a hundred cannon in the fort and elsewhere in

[073-065]and about the town. Here is likewise some very good shipping; here are

[073-066]three vessels, one of them of thirty-four guns, which a week's time

[073-067]would fit for sea; there is another of about twenty guns, very nearly

[073-068]ready likewise." He also reports two other frigates, one on the stocks,

[073-069]which "for her handsomeness' sake" he intended to have finished for the

[073-070]Parliament, and another "most excellent vessel for sailing," taken

[073-071]within the fort, at the harbour's mouth. By the treachery of Captain

[073-072]Stafford, this strong and wealthy town was at the mercy of those

[073-073]"soldiers of the Lord and of Gideon," who had followed Oliver to his

[073-074]Irish wars. The consequences were the same as at Drogheda—merciless

[073-075]execution on the garrison and the inhabitants.

[073-076]In the third month of Cromwell's campaign, the report of Owen O'Neil's

[073-077]death went abroad, palsying the Catholic arms. By common consent of

[073-078]friend and foe, he was considered the ablest civil and military leader

[073-079]that had appeared in Ireland during the reigns of the Stuart kings.

[073-080]Whether in native ability he was capable of coping with Cromwell, was

[073-081]for a long time a subject of discussion; but the consciousness of

[073-082]irreparable national loss, perhaps, never struck deeper than amid the

[073-083]crash of that irresistible cannonade of the walled towns and cities of

[073-084]Leinster and Munster. O'Neil had lately, despairing of binding the

[073-085]Scots or the English, distrustful alike of Coote and of Monck, been

[073-086]reconciled to Ormond, and was marching southward to his aid at the head

[073-087]of 6,000 chosen men. Lord Chancellor Clarendon assures us that Ormond

[073-088]had the highest hopes from this junction, and the utmost confidence in

[073-089]O'Neil's abilities. But at a ball at Derry, towards the end of August,

[073-090]he received his death, it is said, in a pair of poisoned russet leather

[073-091]slippers presented to him by one Plunkett; marching southward, borne in

[073-092]a litter, he expired at Clough Oughter Castle, near his old Belturbet

[073-093]camp, on the 6th of November, 1649. His last act was to order one of

[073-094]his nephews—Hugh O'Neil—to form a junction with Ormond in Munster

[073-095]without delay. In the chancel of the Franciscan Abbey of Cavan, now

[073-096]grass-grown and trodden by the hoofs of cattle, his body was interred;

[073-097]his nephew and successor did honour to his memory at Clonmel and

[073-098]Limerick. It was now remembered, even by his enemies, with astonishment

[073-099]and admiration, how for seven long years he had subsisted and kept

[073-100]together an army, the creature of his genius; without a government at

[073-101]his back, without regular supplies, enforcing obedience, establishing

[073-102]discipline, winning great victories, maintaining, even at the worst, a

[073-103]native power in the heart of the kingdom. When the archives of those

[073-104]years are recovered (if they ever are), no name more illustrious for

[073-105]the combination of great qualities will be found preserved there than

[073-106]the name of this last national leader of the illustrious lineage of

[073-107]O'Neil.

[073-109]The unexpected death of the Ulster general favoured still farther

[073-110]Cromwell's southern movements. The gallant, but impetuous Bishop of

[073-111]Clogher, Heber McMahon, was the only northern leader who could command

[073-112]confidence enough to keep O'Neil's force together, and on him,

[073-113]therefore, the command devolved. O'Ferrall, one of Owen's favourite

[073-114]officers, was despatched to Waterford, and mainly contributed to

[073-115]Cromwell's repulse before that city; Hugh O'Neil covered himself with

[073-116]glory at Clonmel and Limerick; Daniel O'Neil, another nephew of Owen,

[073-117]remained attached to Ormond, and accompanied him to France; but within

[073-118]six months from the loss of their Fabian chief, who knew as well when

[073-119]to strike as to delay, the brave Bishop of Clogher sacrificed the

[073-120]remnant of "the Catholic Army" at the pass of Scariffhollis, in

[073-121]Donegal, and, two days after, his own life by a martyr's death, at

[073-122]Omagh. At the date of Cromwell's departure—when Ireton took command of

[073-123]the southern army—there remained to the Confederates only some remote

[073-124]glens and highlands of the North and West, the cities of Limerick and

[073-125]Galway, with the county of Clare, and some detached districts of the

[073-126]province of Connaught.

[073-127]The last act of Cromwell's proper campaign was the siege of Clonmel,

[073-128]where he met the stoutest resistance he had anywhere encountered. The

[073-129]Puritans, after effecting a breach, made an attempt to enter, chanting

[073-130]one of their scriptural battle-songs. They were, by their own account,

[073-131]"obliged to give back a while," and finally night settled down upon the

[073-132]scene. The following day, finding the place no longer tenable, the

[073-133]garrison silently withdrew to Waterford, and subsequently to Limerick.

[073-134]The inhabitants demanded a parley, which was granted; and Cromwell

[073-135]takes credit, and deserves it, when we consider the men he had to

[073-136]humour, for having kept conditions with them.

[073-137]From before Clonmel he returned at once to England, where he was

[073-138]received with royal honours. All London turned out to meet the

[073-139]Conqueror who had wiped out the humiliation of Benburb, and humbled the

[073-140]pride of the detested Papists. He was lodged in the palace of the king,

[073-141]and chosen "Captain-general of all the forces raised, or to be raised,

[073-142]by the authority of the Parliament of England."
