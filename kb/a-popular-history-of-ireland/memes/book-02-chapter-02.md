CHAPTER II.

[010-001]KINGS OF THE NINTH CENTURY (CONTINUED)—NIAL III.—MALACHY I.—HUGH VII.

[010-002]When, in the year 833, Nial III. received the usual homage and

[010-003]hostages, which ratified his title of _Ard-Righ_, the northern invasion

[010-004]had clearly become the greatest danger that ever yet had threatened the

[010-005]institutions of Erin. Attacks at first predatory and provincial had so

[010-006]encouraged the Gentile leaders of the second generation that they began

[010-007]to concert measures and combine plans for conquest and colonization. To

[010-008]the Vikings of Norway the fertile Island with which they were now so

[010-009]familiar, whose woods were bent with the autumnal load of acorns, mast,

[010-010]and nuts, and filled with numerous herds of swine—their favourite

[010-011]food—whose pleasant meadows were well stored with beeves and oxen,

[010-012]whose winter was often as mild as their northern summer, and whose

[010-013]waters were as fruitful in fish as their own Lofoden friths; to these

[010-014]men, this was a prize worth fighting for; and for it they fought long

[010-015]and desperately.

[010-016]King Nial inherited a disputed sovereignty from his predecessor, and

[010-017]the Southern annalists say he did homage to Felim of Munster, while

[010-018]those of the North—and with them the majority of historians—reject this

[010-019]statement as exaggerated and untrue. He certainly experienced continual

[010-020]difficulty in maintaining his supremacy, not only from the Prince of

[010-021]Cashel, but from lords of lesser grade—like those of Ossory and Ulidia;

[010-022]so that we may say, while he had the title of King of Ireland, he was,

[010-023]in fact, King of no more than Leath-Con, or the Northern half. The

[010-024]central Province, Meath, long deserted by the monarchs, had run wild

[010-025]into independence, and was parcelled out between two or three chiefs,

[010-026]descendants of the same common ancestor as the kings, but distinguished

[010-027]from them by the tribe-name of "the _Southern_ Hy-Nial." Of these heads

[010-028]of new houses, by far the ablest and most famous was Melaghlin, who

[010-029]dwelt near Mullingar, and lorded it over western Meath; a name with

[010-030]which we shall become better acquainted presently. It does not clearly

[010-031]appear that Melaghlin was one of those who actively resisted the

[010-032]prerogatives of this monarch, though others of the Southern Hy-Nial did

[010-033]at first reject his authority, and were severely punished for their

[010-034]insubordination, the year after his assumption of power.

[010-035]In the fourth year of Nial III. (A.D. 837), arrived the great Norwegian

[010-036]fleet of 120 sail, whose commanders first attempted, on a combined

[010-037]plan, the conquest of Erin. Sixty of the ships entered the Boyne; the

[010-038]other sixty the Liffey. This formidable force, according to all Irish

[010-039]accounts, was soon after united under one leader, who is known in our

[010-040]Annals as _Turgeis_ or _Turgesius_, but of whom no trace can be found,

[010-041]under that name, in the chronicles of the Northmen. Every effort to

[010-042]identify him in the records of his native land has hitherto failed—so

[010-043]that we are forced to conclude that he must have been one of those

[010-044]wandering sea-kings, whose fame was won abroad, and whose story, ending

[010-045]in defeat, yet entailing no dynastic consequences on his native land,

[010-046]possessed no national interest for the authors of the old Norse Sagas.

[010-047]To do all the Scandinavian chroniclers justice, in cases which come

[010-048]directly under their notice, they acknowledge defeat as frankly as they

[010-049]claim victory proudly. Equal praise may be given to the Irish annalists

[010-050]in recording the same events, whether at first or second-hand. In

[010-051]relation to the campaigns and sway of Turgesius, the difficulty we

[010-052]experience in separating what is true from what is exaggerated or

[010-053]false, is not created for us by the annalists, but by the bards and

[010-054]story-tellers, some of whose inventions, adopted by _Cambrensis_, have

[010-055]been too readily received by subsequent writers. For all the acts of

[010-056]national importance with which his name can be intelligibly associated,

[010-057]we prefer to follow in this as in other cases, the same sober

[010-058]historians who condense the events of years and generations into the

[010-059]shortest space and the most matter of fact expression.

[010-060]If we were to receive the chronology while rejecting the embellishments

[010-061]of the Bards, Turgesius must have first come to Ireland with one of the

[010-062]expeditions of the year 820, since they speak of him as having been

[010-063]"the scourge of the country for seventeen years," before he assumed the

[010-064]command of the forces landed from the fleet of 837. Nor is it

[010-065]unreasonable to suppose that an accurate knowledge of the country,

[010-066]acquired by years of previous warfare with its inhabitants, may have

[010-067]been one of the grounds upon which the chief command was conferred on

[010-068]Turgesius. This knowledge was soon put to account; Dublin was taken

[010-069]possession of, and a strong fort, according to the Scandinavian method,

[010-070]was erected on the hill where now stands the Castle. This fort and the

[010-071]harbour beneath it were to be the _rendezvous_ and arsenal for all

[010-072]future operations against Leinster, and the foundation of foreign power

[010-073]then laid, continued in foreign hands, with two or three brief

[010-074]intervals, until transferred to the Anglo-Norman chivalry, three

[010-075]centuries and a half later. Similar lodgment was made at Waterford, and

[010-076]a third was attempted at Limerick, but at this period without success;

[010-077]the Danish fort at the latter point is not thought older than the year

[010-078]855. But Turgesius—if, indeed, the independent acts of cotemporary and

[010-079]even rival chiefs be not too often attributed to him—was not content

[010-080]with fortifying the estuaries of some principal rivers; he established

[010-081]inland centres of operation, of which the cardinal one was on Lough

[010-082]Ree, the expansion of the Shannon, north of Athlone; another was at a

[010-083]point called Lyndwachill, on Lough Neagh. On both these waters were

[010-084]stationed fleets of boats, constructed for that service, and

[010-085]communicating with the forts on shore. On the eastern border of Lough

[010-086]Ree, in the midst of its meadows, stood Clonmacnoise, rich with the

[010-087]offerings and endowments of successive generations. Here, three

[010-088]centuries before, in the heart of the desert, St. Kieran had erected

[010-089]with his own hands a rude sylvan cell, where, according to the allegory

[010-090]of tradition, "the first monks who joined him," were the fox, the wolf,

[010-091]and the bear; but time had wrought wonders on that hallowed ground, and

[010-092]a group of churches—at one time, as many as ten in number—were gathered

[010-093]within two or three acres, round its famous schools, and presiding

[010-094]Cathedral. Here it was Turgesius made his usual home, and from the high

[010-095]altar of the Cathedral his unbelieving Queen was accustomed to issue

[010-096]her imperious mandates in his absence. Here, for nearly seven years,

[010-097]this conqueror and his consort exercised their far-spread and terrible

[010-098]power. According to the custom of their own country—a custom attributed

[010-099]to Odin as its author—they exacted from every inhabitant subject to

[010-100]their sway—a piece of money annually, the forfeit for the non-payment

[010-101]of which was the loss of the nose, hence called "nose-money." Their

[010-102]other exactions were a union of their own northern imposts, with those

[010-103]levied by the chiefs whose authority they had superseded, but whose

[010-104]prerogatives they asserted for themselves. Free quarters for their

[010-105]soldiery, and a system of inspection extending to every private

[010-106]relation of life, were the natural expedients of a tyranny so odious.

[010-107]On the ecclesiastical order especially their yoke bore with peculiar

[010-108]weight, since, although avowed Pagans, they permitted no religious

[010-109]house to stand, unless under an Abbot, or at least an _Erenach_ (or

[010-110]Treasurer) of their approval. Such is the complete scheme of oppression

[010-111]presented to us, that it can only be likened to a monstrous spider-web

[010-112]spread from the centre of the Island over its fairest and most populous

[010-113]districts. Glendalough, Ferns, Castle-Dermid, and Kildare in the east;

[010-114]Lismore, Cork, Clonfert, in the southern country; Dundalk, Bangor,

[010-115]Derry, and Armagh in the north; all groaned under this triumphant

[010-116]despot, or his colleagues. In the meanwhile King Nial seems to have

[010-117]struggled resolutely with the difficulties of his lot, and in every

[010-118]interval of insubordination to have struck boldly at the common enemy.

[010-119]But the tide of success for the first few years after 837 ran strongly

[010-120]against him. The joint hosts from the Liffey and the Boyne swept the

[010-121]rich plains of Meath, and in an engagement at Invernabark (the present

[010-122]Bray) gave such a complete defeat to the southern Hy-Nial clans as

[010-123]prevented them making head again in the field, until some summers were

[010-124]past and gone. In this campaign Saxolve, who is called "the chief of

[010-125]the foreigners," was slain; and to him, therefore, if to any

[010-126]commander-in-chief, Turgesius must have succeeded. The shores of all

[010-127]the inland lakes were favourite sites for Raths and Churches, and the

[010-128]beautiful country around Lough Erne shared the fiery ordeal which

[010-129]blazed on Lough Ree and Lough Neagh. In 839 the men of Connaught also

[010-130]suffered a defeat equal to that experienced by those of Meath in the

[010-131]previous campaign; but more unfortunate than the Methians, they lost

[010-132]their leader and other chiefs on the field. In 840, Ferns and Cork were

[010-133]given to the flames, and the fort at Lyndwachill, or Magheralin, poured

[010-134]out its ravages in every direction over the adjacent country, sweeping

[010-135]off flocks, herds, and prisoners, laymen and ecclesiastics, to their

[010-136]ships. The northern depredators counted among their captives "several

[010-137]Bishops and learned men," of whom the Abbot of Clogher and the Lord of

[010-138]Galtrim are mentioned by name. Their equally active colleagues of

[010-139]Dublin and Waterford took captive, Hugh, Abbot of Clonenagh, and

[010-140]Foranan, Archbishop of Armagh, who had fled southwards with many of the

[010-141]relics of the Metropolitan Church, escaping from one danger only to

[010-142]fall into another a little farther off. These prisoners were carried

[010-143]into Munster, where Abbot Hugh suffered martyrdom at their hands, but

[010-144]the Archbishop, after being carried to their fleet at Limerick, seems

[010-145]to have been rescued or ransomed, as we find him dying in peace at

[010-146]Armagh in the next reign. The martyrs of these melancholy times were

[010-147]very numerous, but the exact particulars being so often unrecorded it

[010-148]is impossible to present the reader with an intelligible account of

[010-149]their persons and sufferings. When the Anglo-Normans taunted the Irish

[010-150]that their Church had no martyrs to boast of, they must have forgotten

[010-151]the exploits of their Norse kinsmen about the middle of this century.

[010-153]But the hour of retribution was fast coming round, and the native

[010-154]tribes, unbound, divided, confused, and long unused to foreign war,

[010-155]were fast recovering their old martial experience, and something like a

[010-156]politic sense of the folly of their border feuds. Nothing perhaps so

[010-157]much tended to arouse and combine them together as the capture of the

[010-158]successor of Saint Patrick, with all his relics, and his imprisonment

[010-159]among a Pagan host, in Irish waters. National humiliation could not

[010-160]much farther go, and as we read we pause, prepared for either

[010-161]alternative—mute submission or a brave uprising. King Nial seems to

[010-162]have been in this memorable year, 843, defending as well as he might

[010-163]his ancestral province—Ulster—against the ravagers of Lough Neagh, and

[010-164]still another party whose ships flocked into Lough Swilly. In the

[010-165]ancient plain of Moynith, watered by the little river Finn, (the

[010-166]present barony of Raphoe,) he encountered the enemy, and according to

[010-167]the Annals, "a countless number fell"—victory being with Nial. In the

[010-168]same year, or the next, Turgesius was captured by Melaghlin, Lord of

[010-169]Westmeath, apparently by stratagem, and put to death by the rather

[010-170]novel process of drowning. The Bardic tale told to _Cambrensis_, or

[010-171]parodied by him from an old Greek legend, of the death by which

[010-172]Turgesius died, is of no historical authority. According to this tale,

[010-173]the tyrant of Lough Ree conceived a passion for the fair daughter of

[010-174]Melaghlin, and demanded her of her father, who, fearing to refuse,

[010-175]affected to grant the infamous request, but despatched in her stead, to

[010-176]the place of assignation, twelve beardless youths, habited as maidens,

[010-177]to represent his daughter and her attendants; by these maskers the

[010-178]Norwegian and his boon companions were assassinated, after they had

[010-179]drank to excess and laid aside their arms and armour. For all this

[010-180]superstructure of romance there is neither ground-work nor license in

[010-181]the facts themselves, beyond this, that Turgesius was evidently

[010-182]captured by some clever stratagem. We hear of no battle in Meath or

[010-183]elsewhere against him immediately preceding the event; nor, is it

[010-184]likely that a secondary Prince, as Melaghlin then was, could have

[010-185]hazarded an engagement with the powerful master of Lough Ree. If the

[010-186]local traditions of Westmeath may be trusted, where _Cambrensis_ is

[010-187]rejected, the Norwegian and Irish principals in the tragedy of Lough

[010-188]Owel were on visiting terms just before the denouement, and many

[010-189]curious particulars of their peaceful but suspicious intercourse used

[010-190]to be related by the modern story-tellers around Castle-pollard. The

[010-191]anecdote of the rookery, of which Melaghlin complained, and the remedy

[010-192]for which his visitor suggested to be "to cut down the trees and the

[010-193]rooks would fly," has a suspicious look of the "tall poppies" of the

[010-194]Roman and Grecian legend; two things only do we know for certain about

[010-195]the matter: _firstly_, that Turgesius was taken and drowned in Lough

[010-196]Owel in the year 843 or 844; and _secondly_, that this catastrophe was

[010-197]brought about by the agency and order of his neighbour, Melaghlin.

[010-198]The victory of Moynith and the death of Turgesius were followed by some

[010-199]local successes against other fleets and garrisons of the enemy. Those

[010-200]of Lough Ree seem to have abandoned their fort, and fought their way

[010-201](gaining in their retreat the only military advantage of that year)

[010-202]towards Sligo, where some of their vessels had collected to bear them

[010-203]away. Their colleagues of Dublin, undeterred by recent reverses, made

[010-204]their annual foray southward into Ossory, in 844, and immediately we

[010-205]find King Nial moving up from the north to the same scene of action. In

[010-206]that district he met his death in an effort to save the life of a

[010-207]_gilla_, or common servant. The river of Callan being greatly swollen,

[010-208]the _gilla_, in attempting to find a ford, was swept away in its turbid

[010-209]torrent. The King entreated some one to go to his rescue, but as no one

[010-210]obeyed he generously plunged in himself and sacrificed his own life in

[010-211]endeavouring to preserve one of his humblest followers. He was in the

[010-212]55th year of his age and the 13th of his reign, and in some traits of

[010-213]character reminded men of his grandfather, the devout Nial "of the

[010-214]Showers." The Bards have celebrated the justice of his judgments, the

[010-215]goodness of his heart, and the comeliness of his "brunette-bright

[010-216]face." He left a son of age to succeed him, (and who ultimately did

[010-217]become _Ard-Righ_,) yet the present popularity of Melaghlin of Meath

[010-218]triumphed over every other interest, and he was raised to the

[010-219]monarchy—the first of his family who had yet attained that honour.

[010-220]Hugh, the son of Nial, sank for a time into the rank of a Provincial

[010-221]Prince, before the ascendant star of the captor of Turgesius, and is

[010-222]usually spoken of during this reign as "Hugh of Aileach." He is found

[010-223]towards its close, as if impatient of the succession, employing the

[010-224]arms of the common enemy to ravage the ancient mensal land of the kings

[010-225]of Erin, and otherwise harassing the last days of his successful rival.

[010-226]Melaghlin, or Malachy I. (sometimes called "of the Shannon," from his

[010-227]patrimony along that river), brought back again the sovereignty to the

[010-228]centre, and in happier days might have become the second founder of

[010-229]Tara. But it was plain enough then, and it is tolerably so still, that

[010-230]this was not to be an age of restoration. The kings of Ireland after

[010-231]this time, says the quaint old translator of the Annals of

[010-232]Clonmacnoise, "had little good of it," down to the days of King Brian.

[010-233]It was, in fact, a perpetual struggle for self-preservation—the first

[010-234]duty of all governments, as well as the first law of all nature. The

[010-235]powerful action of the Gentile forces, upon an originally

[010-236]ill-centralized and recently much abused Constitution, seemed to render

[010-237]it possible that every new Ard-Righ would prove the last. Under the

[010-238]pressure of such a deluge all ancient institutions were shaken to their

[010-239]foundations; and the venerable authority of Religion itself, like a

[010-240]Hermit in a mountain torrent, was contending for the hope of escape or

[010-241]existence. We must not, therefore, amid the din of the conflicts

[010-242]through which we are to pass, condemn without stint or qualification

[010-243]those Princes who were occasionally driven—as some of them _were_

[010-244]driven—to that last resort, the employment of foreign mercenaries (and

[010-245]those mercenaries often anti-Christians,) to preserve some show of

[010-246]native government and kingly authority. Grant that in some of them the

[010-247]use of such allies and agents cannot be justified on any plea or

[010-248]pretext of state necessity; where base ends or unpatriotic motives are

[010-249]clear or credible, such treason to country cannot be too heartily

[010-250]condemned; but it is indeed far from certain that such were the motives

[010-251]in _all_ cases, or that such ought to be our conclusion in any, in the

[010-252]absence of sufficient evidence to that effect.

[010-253]Though the Gentile power had experienced towards the close of the last

[010-254]reign such severe reverses, yet it was not in the nature of the men of

[010-255]Norway to abandon a prize which was once so nearly being their own. The

[010-256]fugitives who escaped, as well as those who remained within the strong

[010-257]ramparts of Waterford and Dublin, urged the fitting out of new

[010-258]expeditions, to avenge their slaughtered countrymen and prosecute the

[010-259]conquest. But defeat still followed on defeat; in the first year of

[010-260]Malachy, they lost 1,200 men in a disastrous action near Castle Dermot,

[010-261]with Olcobar the Prince-bishop of Cashel; and in the same or the next

[010-262]season they were defeated with the loss of 700 men, by Malachy, at

[010-263]Fore, in Meath. In the third year of Malachy, however, a new northern

[010-264]expedition arrived in 140 vessels, which, according to the average

[010-265]capacity of the long-ships of that age, must have carried with them

[010-266]from 7,000 to 10,000 men. Fortunately for the assailed, this fleet was

[010-267]composed of what they called _Black_-Gentiles, or Danes, as

[010-268]distinguished from their predecessors, the _Fair_-Gentiles, or

[010-269]Norwegians. A quarrel arose between the adventurers of the two nations

[010-270]as to the possession of the few remaining fortresses, especially of

[010-271]Dublin; and an engagement was fought along the Liffey, which "lasted

[010-272]for three days;" the Danes finally prevailed, driving the Norwegians

[010-273]from their stronghold, and cutting them off from their ships. The new

[010-274]Northern leaders are named Anlaf, or Olaf, Sitrick (Sigurd?) and Ivar;

[010-275]the first of the Danish Earls, who established themselves at Dublin,

[010-276]Waterford and Limerick respectively. Though the immediate result of the

[010-277]arrival of the great fleet of 847 relieved for the moment the worst

[010-278]apprehensions of the invaded, and enabled them to rally their means of

[010-279]defence, yet as Denmark had more than double the population of Norway,

[010-280]it brought them into direct collision with a more formidable power than

[010-281]that from which they had been so lately delivered. The tactics of both

[010-282]nations were the same. No sooner had they established themselves on the

[010-283]ruins of their predecessors in Dublin, than the Danish forces entered

[010-284]East-Meath, under the guidance of Kenneth, a local lord, and overran

[010-285]the ancient mensal, from the sea to the Shannon. One of their first

[010-286]exploits was burning alive 260 prisoners in the tower of Treoit, in the

[010-287]island of Lough Gower, near Dunshaughlin. The next year, his allies

[010-288]having withdrawn from the neighbourhood, Kenneth was taken by King

[010-289]Malachy's men, and the traitor himself drowned in a sack, in the little

[010-290]river Nanny, which divides the two baronies of Duleek. This

[010-291]death-penalty by drowning seems to have been one of the useful hints

[010-292]which the Irish picked up from their invaders.

[010-293]During the remainder of this reign the Gentile war resumed much of its

[010-294]old local and guerrilla character, the Provincial chiefs, and the

[010-295]Ard-Righ, occasionally employing bands of one nation of the invaders to

[010-296]combat the other, and even to suppress their native rivals. The only

[010-297]pitched battle of which we hear is that of "the Two Plains" (near

[010-298]Coolestown, King's County), in the second last year of Malachy (A.D.

[010-299]859), in which his usual good fortune attended the king. The greater

[010-300]part of his reign was occupied, as always must be the case with the

[010-301]founder of a new line, in coercing into obedience his former peers. On

[010-302]this business he made two expeditions into Munster, and took hostages

[010-303]from all the tribes of the Eugenian race. With the same object he held

[010-304]a conference with all the chiefs of Ulster, Hugh of Aileach only being

[010-305]absent, at Armagh, in the fourth year of his reign, and a General

[010-306]_Feis_, or Assembly of all the Orders of Ireland, at Rathugh, in

[010-307]West-Meath, in his thirteenth year (A.D. 857). He found,

[010-308]notwithstanding his victories and his early popularity, that there are

[010-309]always those ready to turn from the setting to the rising sun, and

[010-310]towards the end of his reign he was obliged to defend his camp, near

[010-311]Armagh, by force, from a night assault of the discontented Prince of

[010-312]Aileach; who also ravaged his patrimony, almost at the moment he lay on

[010-313]his death-bed. Malachy I. departed this life on the 13th day of

[010-314]November, (A.D. 860), having reigned sixteen years. "Mournful is the

[010-315]news to the Gael!" exclaims the elegiac Bard! "Red wine is spilled into

[010-316]the valley! Erin's monarch has died!" And the lament contrasts his

[010-317]stately form as "he rode the white stallion," with the striking reverse

[010-318]when, "his only horse this day"—that is the bier on which his body was

[010-319]borne to the churchyard—"is drawn behind two oxen."

[010-320]The restless Prince of Aileach now succeeded as Hugh VII., and

[010-321]possessed the perilous honour he so much coveted for sixteen years, the

[010-322]same span that had been allotted to his predecessor. The beginning of

[010-323]this reign was remarkable for the novel design of the Danes, who

[010-324]marched out in great force, and set themselves busily to breaking open

[010-325]the ancient mounds in the cemetery of the Pagan kings, beside the

[010-326]Boyne, in hope of finding buried treasure. The three Earls, Olaf,

[010-327]Sitrick, and Ivar, are said to have been present, while their

[010-328]gold-hunters broke into in succession the mound-covered cave of the

[010-329]wife of Goban, at Drogheda, the cave of "the Shepherd of Elcmar," at

[010-330]Dowth, the cave of the field of Aldai, at New Grange, and the similar

[010-331]cave at Knowth. What they found in these huge cairns of the old

[010-332]_Tuatha_ is not related; but Roman coins of Valentinian and Theodosius,

[010-333]and torques and armlets of gold, have been discovered by accident

[010-334]within their precincts, and an enlightened modern curiosity has not

[010-335]explored them in vain, in the higher interests of history and science.

[010-337]In the first two years of his reign, Hugh VII. was occupied in securing

[010-338]the hostages of his suffragans; in the third he swept the remaining

[010-339]Danish and Norwegian garrisons out of Ulster, and defeated a newly

[010-340]arrived force on the borders of Lough Foyle; the next the Danish Earls

[010-341]went on a foray into Scotland, and no exploit is to be recorded; in his

[010-342]sixth year, Hugh, with 1,000 chosen men of his own tribe and the aid of

[010-343]the Sil-Murray (O'Conor's) of Connaught, attacked and defeated a force

[010-344]of 5,000 Danes with their Leinster allies, near Dublin at a place

[010-345]supposed to be identical with Killaderry. Earl Olaf lost his son, and

[010-346]Erin her _Roydamna_, or heir-apparent, on this field, which was much

[010-347]celebrated by the Bards of Ulster and of Connaught. Amongst those who

[010-348]fell was Flan, son of Conaing, chief of the district which included the

[010-349]plundered cemeteries, fighting on the side of the plunderers. The

[010-350]mother of Flan was one of those who composed quatrains on the event of

[010-351]the battle, and her lines are a natural and affecting alternation from
