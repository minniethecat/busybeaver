CHAPTER VI.

[069-001]THE CONFEDERATE WAR—CAMPAIGN OF 1643—THE CESSATION.

[069-002]The city of Kilkenny, which had become the capital of the Confederacy,

[069-003]was favourably placed for the direction of the war in Leinster and

[069-004]Munster. Nearly equidistant from Dublin, Cork, and Limerick, a meeting

[069-005]place for most of the southern and south-western roads, important in

[069-006]itself both as a place of trade, and as the residence of the Duke of

[069-007]Ormond and the Bishop of Ossory, a better choice could not, perhaps,

[069-008]have been made, so far as regarded the ancient southern "Half-Kingdom."

[069-009]But it seems rather surprising that the difficulty of directing the war

[069-010]in the North and North-West, from a point so far south, did not occur

[069-011]to the statesmen of the Confederacy. In the defective communications of

[069-012]those days, especially during a war, partaking even partially of the

[069-013]character of civil strife, it was hard, if not impossible to expect,

[069-014]that a supervision could be exercised over a general or an army on the

[069-015]Erne or the Bann, which might be quite possible and proper on the Suir

[069-016]or the Shannon. A similar necessity in England necessitated the

[069-017]creation of the Presidency of the North, with its council and

[069-018]head-quarters in the city of York; nor need we be surprised to find

[069-019]that, from the first, the Confederate movements combined themselves

[069-020]into two groups—the northern and the southern—those which revolved

[069-021]round the centre of Kilkenny, and those which took their law from the

[069-022]head-quarters of Owen O'Neil, at Belturbet, or wherever else his camp

[069-023]happened to be situated.

[069-024]The General Assembly met, according to agreement, on the 23rd of

[069-025]October, 1642, at Kilkenny. Eleven bishops and fourteen lay lords

[069-026]represented the Irish peerage; two hundred and twenty-six commoners,

[069-027]the large majority of the constituencies. Both bodies sat in the same

[069-028]chamber, divided only by a raised dais. The celebrated lawyer, Patrick

[069-029]Darcy, a member of the Commons' House, was chosen as chancellor, and

[069-030]everything was conducted with the gravity and deliberation befitting so

[069-031]venerable an Assembly, and so great an occasion. The business most

[069-032]pressing, and most delicate, was felt to be the consideration of a form

[069-033]of supreme executive government. The committee on this subject, who

[069-034]reported after the interval of a week, was composed of Lords

[069-035]Gormanstown and Castlehaven, Sir Phelim O'Neil, Sir Richard Belling,

[069-036]and Mr. Darcy. A "Supreme Council" of six members for each province was

[069-037]recommended, approved, and elected. The Archbishops of Armagh, Dublin,

[069-038]and Tuam, the Bishops of Down and of Clonfert, the Lords Gormanstown,

[069-039]Mountgarrett, Roche, and Mayo, with fifteen of the most eminent

[069-040]commoners, composed this council. It was provided that the vote of

[069-041]two-thirds should be necessary to any act affecting the basis of the

[069-042]Confederacy, but a quorum of nine was sufficient for the transaction of

[069-043]ordinary business. A guard of honour of 500 foot and 200 horse was

[069-044]allowed for their greater security. The venerable Mountgarrett, the

[069-045]head of the Catholic Butlers, (son-in-law of the illustrious Tyrone,

[069-046]who, in the last years of Elizabeth, had devoted his youthful sword to

[069-047]the same good cause,) was elected president of this council; and Sir

[069-048]Richard Belling, a lawyer, and a man of letters, the continuator of Sir

[069-049]Philip Sydney's _Arcadia_, was appointed secretary.

[069-050]The first act of this Supreme Council was to appoint General O'Neil as

[069-051]Commander-in-Chief in Ulster; General Preston, in Leinster; General

[069-052]Barry, in Munster; and Sir John Burke as Lieutenant-General in

[069-053]Connaught; the supreme command in the West being held over for

[069-054]Clanrickarde, who, it was still hoped, might be led or driven into the

[069-055]Confederacy. We shall endeavour to indicate in turn the operations of

[069-056]these commanders, thus chosen or confirmed; leaving the civil and

[069-057]diplomatic business transacted by the General Assembly, or delegated to

[069-058]the Supreme Council, for future mention.

[069-059]Contrary to the custom of that age, the Confederate troops were not

[069-060]withdrawn into winter quarters. In November, General Preston, at the

[069-061]head of 6,000 foot and 600 horse, encountered Monk at Tymahoe and

[069-062]Ballinakil, with some loss; but before the close of December he had

[069-063]reduced Birr, Banagher, Burris, and Fort Falkland, and found himself

[069-064]master of King's county, from the Shannon to the Barrow. In February,

[069-065]however, he sustained a serious check at Rathconnell, in endeavouring

[069-066]to intercept the retreat of the English troops from Connaught, under

[069-067]the command of Lord Ranelagh, and the younger Coote; and in March,

[069-068]equal ill success attended his attempt to intercept Ormond, in his

[069-069]retreat from the unsuccessful siege of the town of Ross. Lord

[069-070]Castlehaven, who was Preston's second in command, attributes both these

[069-071]reverses to the impetuosity of the general, whose imprudence seems to

[069-072]have been almost as great as his activity was conspicuous. In April and

[069-073]May, Preston and Castlehaven took several strongholds in Carlow,

[069-074]Kildare, and West-Meath, and the General Assembly, which met for its

[069-075]second session, on the 20th of May, 1643, at Kilkenny, had, on the

[069-076]whole, good grounds to be satisfied with the success of the war in

[069-077]Leinster.

[069-078]In the Southern Province, considerable military successes might also be

[069-079]claimed by the Confederates. The Munster troops, under Purcell, the

[069-080]second in command, a capable soldier, who had learned the art of war in

[069-081]the armies of the German Empire, relieved Ross, when besieged by

[069-082]Ormond; General Barry had successfully repulsed an attack on his

[069-083]head-quarters, the famous old Desmond town of Killmallock. In June,

[069-084]Barry, Purcell, and Castlehaven drove the enemy before them across the

[069-085]Funcheon, and at Kilworth brought their main body, under Sir Charles

[069-086]Vavasour, to action. Vavasour's force was badly beaten, himself

[069-087]captured, with his cannon and colours, and many of his officers and

[069-088]men. Inchiquin, who had endeavoured to form a junction with Vavasour,

[069-089]escaped to one of the few remaining garrisons open to him—probably

[069-090]Youghal.

[069-091]In Connaught, the surrender of Galway, on the 20th of June, eclipsed

[069-092]all the previous successes, and they were not a few, of

[069-093]Lieutenant-General Burke. From the day Lord Ranelagh and the younger

[069-094]Coote deserted the Western province, the Confederate cause had rapidly

[069-095]advanced. The surrender of "the second fort in the Kingdom"—a sea-port

[069-096]in that age, not unworthy to be ranked with Cadiz and Bristol, for its

[069-097]commercial wealth and reputation—was a military event of the first

[069-098]importance. An English fleet appeared three days after the surrender of

[069-099]Willoughby, in Galway harbour; but nine long years elapsed before the

[069-100]Confederate colours were lowered from the towers of the Connaught

[069-101]citadel.

[069-102]In the North, O'Neil, who, without injustice to any of his

[069-103]contemporaries, may certainly be said to have made, during his seven

[069-104]years' command, the highest European reputation among the Confederate

[069-105]generals, gathered his recruits into a rugged district, which forms a

[069-106]sort of natural camp in the north-west corner of the island. The

[069-107]mountain plateau of Leitrim, which sends its spurs downwards to the

[069-108]Atlantic, towards Lough Erne, and into Longford, accessible only by

[069-109]four or five lines of road, leading over narrow bridges and through

[069-110]deep defiles, was the nursery selected by this cautious leader, in

[069-111]which to collect and organize his forces. In the beginning of May—seven

[069-112]months after the date of his commission, and ten from his solitary

[069-113]landing at Doe Castle—we find him a long march from his mountain

[069-114]fortress in Leitrim, at Charlemont, which he had strengthened and

[069-115]garrisoned, and now saved from a surprise attempted by Monroe, from

[069-116]Carrickfergus. Having effected that immediate object, he again retired

[069-117]towards the Leitrim highlands, fighting by the way a smart cavalry

[069-118]action at Clonish, with a superior force, under Colonels Stewart,

[069-119]Balfour, and Mervyn. In this affair O'Neil was only too happy to have

[069-120]carried off his troop with credit; but a fortnight brought him

[069-121]consolation for Clonish in the brilliant affair of Portlester. He had

[069-122]descended in force from his hills and taken possession of the greater

[069-123]part of the ancient Meath. General Monk and Lord Moore were despatched

[069-124]against him, but reinforced by a considerable body of Meathian

[069-125]Confederates, under Sir James Dillon, he resolved to risk his first

[069-126]regular engagement in the field. Taking advantage of the situation of

[069-127]the ground, about five miles from Trim, he threw up some field works,

[069-128]placed sixty men in Portlester mill, and patiently awaited the advance

[069-129]of the enemy. Their assault was overconfident, their rout complete.

[069-130]Lord Moore, and a large portion of the assailants were slain, and Monk

[069-131]fled back to Dublin. O'Neil, gathering fresh strength from these

[069-132]movements, abandoned his mountain stronghold, and established his

[069-133]head-quarters on the river Erne between Lough Oughter (memorable in his

[069-134]life and death) and the upper waters of Lough Erne. At this point stood

[069-135]the town of Belturbet, which, in "the Plantation" of James I., had been

[069-136]turned over exclusively to British settlers, whose "cagework" houses,

[069-137]and four acres of garden ground each, had elicited the approval of the

[069-138]surveyor Pynnar, twenty years before. The surrounding country was

[069-139]covered with the fortified castles and loop-holed lawns of the chief

[069-140]_Undertakers_—but few were found of sufficient strength to resist the

[069-141]arms of O'Neil. At Belturbet, he was within a few days' march of the

[069-142]vital points of four other counties, and in case of the worst, within

[069-143]the same distance of his protective fastness. Here, towards the end of

[069-144]September, busied with present duties and future projects, he heard,

[069-145]for the first time, with astonishment and grief, that the requisite

[069-146]majority of "the Supreme Council" had concluded, on the 13th of that

[069-147]month, a twelve-months' truce with Ormond, thus putting in peril all

[069-148]the advantages already acquired by the bravery of the Confederate

[069-149]troops, and the skill of their generals.

[069-150]The war had lasted nearly two years, and this was the first time the

[069-151]Catholics had consented to negotiate. The moment chosen was a critical

[069-152]one for all the three Kingdoms, and the interests involved were

[069-153]complicated in the extreme. The Anglo-Irish, who formed the majority of

[069-154]the Supreme Council, connected by blood and language with England, had

[069-155]entered into the war, purely as one of religious liberty. Nationally,

[069-156]they had, apart from the civil disabilities imposed on religious

[069-157]grounds, no antipathy, no interest, hostile to the general body of

[069-158]English loyalists, represented in Ireland by the King's lieutenant,

[069-159]Ormond. On his side, that nobleman gave all his thoughts to, and

[069-160]governed all his actions by the exigencies of the royal cause,

[069-161]throughout the three Kingdoms. When Charles seemed strong in England,

[069-162]Ormond rated the Catholics at a low figure; but when reverses increased

[069-163]he estimated their alliance more highly. After the drawn battle of

[069-164]Edgehill, fought on the very day of the first meeting of the General

[069-165]Assembly at Kilkenny, the King had established his head-quarters at

[069-166]Oxford, in the heart of four or five of the most loyal counties in

[069-167]England. Here he at first negotiated with the Parliament, but finally

[069-168]the sword was again invoked, and while the King proclaimed the

[069-169]Parliament rebels, "the solemn league and covenant" was entered into,

[069-170]at first separately, and afterwards jointly, by the Puritans of England

[069-171]and Presbyterians of Scotland. The military events during that year,

[069-172]and in the first half of the next, were upon the whole not unfavourable

[069-173]to the royal cause. The great battle of Marston Moor, (July 2nd, 1644,)

[069-174]which "extinguished the hopes of the Royalists in the Northern

[069-175]counties," was the first Parliamentary victory of national importance.

[069-176]It was won mainly by the energy and obstinacy of Lieutenant-General

[069-177]Cromwell, from that day forth the foremost English figure in the Civil

[069-178]War. From his court at Oxford, where he had seen the utter failure of

[069-179]endeavouring to conciliate his English and Scottish enemies, the King

[069-180]had instructed Ormond—lately created a Marquis—to treat with the Irish

[069-181]Catholics, and to obtain from them men and money. The overtures thus

[069-182]made were brought to maturity in September; the Cessation was to last

[069-183]twelve months; each party was to remain in possession of its own

[069-184]quarters, as they were held at the date of the treaty; the forces of

[069-185]each were to unite to punish any infraction of the terms agreed on; the

[069-186]agents of the Confederates, during the cessation, were to have free

[069-187]access and safe conduct to the King; and for these advantages, the

[069-188]Supreme Council were to present his Majesty immediately with 15,000

[069-189]pounds in money, and provisions to the value of 15,000 pounds more.

[069-191]Such was "the truce of Castlemartin," condemned by O'Neil, by the Papal

[069-192]Nuncio, Scarampi, and by the great majority of the old Irish, lay and

[069-193]clerical; still more violently denounced by the Puritan Parliament as

[069-194]favouring Popery, and negotiated by Popish agents; beneficial to Ormond

[069-195]and the Undertakers, as relieving Dublin, freeing the channel from

[069-196]Irish privateers, and securing them in the garrisons throughout the

[069-197]Kingdom which they still held; in one sense advantageous to Charles,

[069-198]from the immediate supplies it afforded, and the favourable impression

[069-199]it created of his liberality, at the courts of his Catholic allies; but

[069-200]on the other hand disadvantageous to him in England and Scotland, from

[069-201]the pretexts it furnished his enemies, of renewing the cry of his

[069-202]connivance with Popery, a cry neither easily answered, nor, of itself,

[069-203]liable quickly to wear out.
