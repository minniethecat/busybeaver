CHAPTER XVIII.

[105-001]ADMINISTRATION OF LORD CORNWALLIS—BEFORE THE UNION.

[105-002]"Nothing strengthens a dynasty," said the first Napoleon, "more than an

[105-003]unsuccessful rebellion." The partial uprising; of the Irish people in

[105-004]1798 was a rebellion of this class, and the use of such a failure to an

[105-005]able and unscrupulous administration, was illustrated in the extinction

[105-006]of the ancient legislature of the kingdom, before the recurrence of the

[105-007]third anniversary of the insurrection.

[105-008]This project, the favourite and long-cherished design of Mr. Pitt, was

[105-009]cordially approved by his principal colleagues, the Duke of Portland,

[105-010]Lord Grenville, and Mr. Dundas; indeed, it may be questioned whether it

[105-011]was not as much Lord Grenville's design as Pitt's, and as much George

[105-012]the Third's personal project as that of any of his ministers. The old

[105-013]King's Irish policy was always of the most narrow and illiberal

[105-014]description. In his memorandum on the recall of Lord Fitzwilliam, he

[105-015]explains his views with the business-like brevity which characterized

[105-016]all his communications with his ministers while he retained possession

[105-017]of his faculties; he was totally opposed to Lord Fitzwilliam's

[105-018]emancipation policy, which he thought adopted "in implicit obedience to

[105-019]the heated imagination of Mr. Burke." To Lord Camden his instructions

[105-020]were, "to support the old English interest as well as the Protestant

[105-021]religion," and to Lord Cornwallis, that no further "indulgence could be

[105-022]granted to Catholics," but that he should steadily pursue the object of

[105-023]effecting the union of Ireland and England.

[105-024]The new Viceroy entered heartily into the views of his Sovereign.

[105-025]Though unwilling to exchange his English position as a Cabinet Minister

[105-026]and Master-General of Ordnance for the troubled life of a

[105-027]Lord-Lieutenant of Ireland, he at length allowed himself to be

[105-028]persuaded into the acceptance of that office, with a view mainly to

[105-029]carrying the Union. He was ambitious to connect his name with that

[105-030]great imperial measure, so often projected, but never formally

[105-031]proposed. If he could only succeed in incorporating the Irish with the

[105-032]British legislature, he declared he would feel satisfied to retire from

[105-033]all other public employments; that he would look on his day as

[105-034]finished, and his evening of ease and dignity fully earned. He was not

[105-035]wholly unacquainted with the kingdom against which he cherished these

[105-036]ulterior views; for he had been, nearly thirty years before, when he

[105-037]fell under the lash of _Junius_, one of the Vice-Treasurers of Ireland.

[105-038]For the rest he was a man of great information, tact, and firmness;

[105-039]indefatigable in business; tolerant by temperament and conviction; but

[105-040]both as a general and a politician it was his lot to be identified in

[105-041]India and in Ireland with successes which might better have been

[105-042]failures, and in America, with failures which were much more beneficial

[105-043]to mankind than his successes.

[105-044]In his new sphere of action his two principal agents were Lord Clare

[105-045]and Lord Castlereagh, both Irishmen; the Chancellor, the son of what in

[105-046]that country is called a "spoiled priest," and the Secretary, the son

[105-047]of an ex-volunteer, and member of Flood's Reform Convention. It is not

[105-048]possible to regard the conduct of these high officials in undermining

[105-049]and destroying the ancient national legislature of their own country,

[105-050]in the same light as that of Lord Cornwallis, or Mr. Pitt, or Lord

[105-051]Grenville. It was but natural, that as Englishmen, these ministers

[105-052]should consider the empire in the first place; that they should desire

[105-053]to centralize all the resources and all the authority of both Islands

[105-054]in London; that to them the existence of an independent Parliament at

[105-055]Dublin, with its ample control over the courts, the revenues, the

[105-056]defences, and the trade of that kingdom, should appear an obstacle and

[105-057]a hindrance to the unity of the imperial system. From their point of

[105-058]view they were quite right, and had they pursued their end, complete

[105-059]centralization, by honourable means, no stigma could attach to them

[105-060]even in the eyes of Irishmen; but with Lords Clare and Castlereagh the

[105-061]case was wholly different. Born in the land, deriving income as well as

[105-062]existence from the soil, elected to its Parliament by the confidence of

[105-063]their countrymen, attaining to posts of honour in consequence of such

[105-064]election, that they should voluntarily offer their services to

[105-065]establish an alien and a hostile policy on the ruins of their own

[105-066]national constitution, which, with all its defects, was national, and

[105-067]was corrigible; this betrayal of their own, at the dictate of another

[105-068]State, will always place the names of Clare and Castlereagh on the

[105-069]detested list of public traitors. Yet though in such treason, united

[105-070]and identified, no two men could be more unlike in all other respects.

[105-071]Lord Clare was fiery, dogmatic, and uncompromising to the last degree;

[105-072]while Lord Castlereagh was stealthy, imperturbable, insidious, bland,

[105-073]and adroit. The Chancellor endeavoured to carry everything with a high

[105-074]hand, with a bold, defiant, confident swagger; the Secretary, on the

[105-075]contrary, trusted to management, expediency, and silent tenacity of

[105-076]purpose. The one had faith in violence, the other in corruption; they

[105-077]were no inapt personifications of the two chief agencies by which the

[105-078]union was effected—Force and Fraud.

[105-079]The Irish Parliament, which had been of necessity adjourned during the

[105-080]greater part of the time the insurrection lasted, assembled within a

[105-081]week of Lord Cornwallis' arrival. Both Houses voted highly loyal

[105-082]addresses to the King and Lord-Lieutenant, the latter seconded in the

[105-083]Commons by Charles Kendal Bushe, the college companion of Wolfe Tone! A

[105-084]vote of 100,000 pounds to indemnify those who had suffered from the

[105-085]rebels—subsequently increased to above 1,000,000 pounds—was passed _una

[105-086]voce_; another, placing on the Irish establishment certain English

[105-087]militia regiments, passed with equal promptitude. In July, five

[105-088]consecutive acts—a complete code of penalties and proscription—were

[105-089]introduced, and, after various debates and delays, received the royal

[105-090]sanction on the 6th of October, the last day of the session of 1798.

[105-091]These acts were: 1. The Amnesty Act, the exceptions to which were so

[105-092]numerous "that few of those who took any active part in the rebellion,"

[105-093]were, according to the Cornwallis' correspondence, "benefited by it."

[105-094]2. An Act of Indemnity, by which all magistrates who had "exercised a

[105-095]vigour beyond the law" against the rebels, were protected from the

[105-096]legal consequences of such acts. 3. An act for attainting Lord Edward

[105-097]Fitzgerald, Mr. Harvey, and Mr. Grogan, against which Curran, taking

[105-098]"his instructions from the grave," pleaded at the bar of the House of

[105-099]Lords, but pleaded in vain. (This act was finally reversed by the

[105-100]Imperial Parliament in 1819.) 4. An act forbidding communication

[105-101]between persons in Ireland and those enumerated in the Banishment Act,

[105-102]and making the return to Ireland, after sentence of banishment by a

[105-103]court-martial, a transportable felony. 5. An act to compel fifty-one

[105-104]persons therein named to surrender before 1st of December, 1798, under

[105-105]pain of high treason. Among the fifty-one were the principal refugees

[105-106]at Paris and Hamburg: Tone, Lewines, Tandy, Deane Swift, Major

[105-107]Plunkett, Anthony McCann, Harvey Morres, etc. On the same day in which

[105-108]the session terminated, and the royal sanction was given to these acts,

[105-109]the name of Henry Grattan was, a significant coincidence, formally

[105-110]struck, by the King's commands, from the roll of the Irish Privy

[105-111]Council!

[105-113]This legislation of the session of 1798, was fatal to the Irish

[105-114]Parliament. The partisans of the Union, who had used the rebellion to

[105-115]discredit the constitution, now used the Parliament to discredit

[105-116]itself. Under the influence of a fierce reactionary spirit, when all

[105-117]merciful and moderate councils were denounced as treasonable, it was

[105-118]not difficult to procure the passage of sweeping measures of

[105-119]proscription. But with their passage vanished the former popularity of

[105-120]the domestic legislature. And what followed? The constitution of '82

[105-121]could only be upheld in the hearts of the people; and, with all its

[105-122]defects, it had been popular before the sudden spread of French

[105-123]revolutionary notions distracted and dissipated the public opinion

[105-124]which had grown up within the era of independence. To make the once

[105-125]cherished authority, which liberated trade in '79, and half emancipated

[105-126]the Catholics in '93, the last executioner of the vengeance of the

[105-127]Castle against the people, was to place a gulf between it and the

[105-128]affections of that people in the day of trial. To make the

[105-129]anti-unionists in Parliament, such as the Speaker, Sir Lawrence

[105-130]Parsons, Plunkett, Ponsonby and Bushe, personally responsible for this

[105-131]vindictive code, was to disarm them of the power, and almost of the

[105-132]right, to call on the people whom they turned over, bound hand and

[105-133]foot, to the mercy of the minister in '98, to aid them against the

[105-134]machinations of that same minister in '99. The last months of the year

[105-135]were marked besides by events already referred to, and by negotiations

[105-136]incessantly carried on, both in England and Ireland, in favour of the

[105-137]Union. Members of both Houses were personally courted and canvassed by

[105-138]the Prime Minister, the Secretaries of State, the Viceroy and the Irish

[105-139]Secretary. Titles, pensions and offices were freely promised. Vast sums

[105-140]of secret service money, afterwards added as a charge to the public

[105-141]debt of Ireland, were remitted from Whitehall. An army of pamphleteers,

[105-142]marshalled by Under-Secretary Cooke, and confidentially directed by the

[105-143]able but anti-national Bishop of Meath, (Dr. O'Beirne,) and by Lord

[105-144]Castlereagh personally, plied their pens in favour of "the

[105-145]consolidation of the empire." The Lord Chancellor, the Chief Secretary

[105-146]and Mr. Beresford, made journeys to England, to assist the Prime

[105-147]Minister with their local information, and to receive his imperial

[105-148]confidence in return. The Orangemen were neutralized by securing a

[105-149]majority of their leaders; the Catholics, by the establishment of

[105-150]familiar communication with the bishops. The Viceroy complimented Dr.

[105-151]Troy at Dublin; the Duke of Portland lavished personal attentions on

[105-152]Dr. Moylan, in England. The Protestant clergy were satisfied with the

[105-153]assurance that the maintenance of their establishment would be made a

[105-154]fundamental article of the Union, while the Catholic bishops were given

[105-155]to understand that complete Emancipation would be one of the first

[105-156]measures submitted to the Imperial Parliament. The oligarchy were to be

[105-157]indemnified for their boroughs, while the advocates of Reform were

[105-158]shown how hopeless it was to expect a House constituted of _their_

[105-159]nominees, ever to enlarge or amend its own exclusive constitution. Thus

[105-160]for every description of people a particular set of appeals and

[105-161]arguments was found, and for those who discarded the affectation of

[105-162]reasoning on the surrender of their national existence, there were the

[105-163]more convincing arguments of titles, employments, and direct pecuniary

[105-164]purchase. At the close of the year of the rebellion, Lord Cornwallis

[105-165]was able to report to Mr. Pitt that the prospects of carrying the

[105-166]measure were better than could have been expected, and on this report

[105-167]he was authorized to open the matter formally to Parliament in his

[105-168]speech at the opening of the following session.

[105-169]On the 22nd of January, 1799, the Irish legislature met under

[105-170]circumstances of great interest and excitement. The city of Dublin,

[105-171]always keenly alive to its metropolitan interests, sent its eager

[105-172]thousands by every avenue towards College Green. The Viceroy went down

[105-173]to the Houses with a more than ordinary guard, and being seated on the

[105-174]throne in the House of Lords, the Commons were summoned to the bar. The

[105-175]House was considered a full one, 217 members being present. The

[105-176]viceregal speech congratulated both Houses on the suppression of the

[105-177]late rebellion, on the defeat of Bompart's squadron, and the recent

[105-178]French victories of Lord Nelson; then came, amid profound expectation,

[105-179]this concluding sentence:—"The unremitting industry," said the Viceroy,

[105-180]"with which our enemies persevere in their avowed design of

[105-181]endeavouring to effect a separation of this kingdom from Great Britain,

[105-182]must have engaged your attention, and his Majesty commands me to

[105-183]express his anxious hope that this consideration, joined to the

[105-184]sentiment of mutual affection and common interest, may dispose the

[105-185]Parliaments in both kingdoms to provide the most effectual means of

[105-186]maintaining and improving a connection essential to their common

[105-187]security, and of consolidating, as far as possible, into one firm and

[105-188]lasting fabric, the strength, the power, and the resources of the

[105-189]British empire." On the paragraph of the address, re-echoing this

[105-190]sentiment, which was carried by a large majority in the Lords, a debate

[105-191]ensued in the Commons, which lasted till one o'clock of the following

[105-192]day, above twenty consecutive hours. Against the suggestion of a Union

[105-193]spoke Ponsonby, Parsons, Fitzgerald, Barrington, Plunkett, Lee,

[105-194]O'Donnell and Bushe; in its favour, Lord Castlereagh, the Knight of

[105-195]Kerry, Corry, Fox, Osborne, Duigenan, and some other members little

[105-196]known. The galleries and lobbies were crowded all night by the first

[105-197]people of the city, of both sexes, and when the division was being

[105-198]taken, the most intense anxiety was manifested, within doors and

[105-199]without. At length the tellers made their report to the Speaker,

[105-200]himself an ardent anti-Unionist, and it was announced that the numbers

[105-201]were—"for the address 105, for the amendment 106," so the paragraph in

[105-202]favour of "consolidating the empire" was lost by one vote! The

[105-203]remainder of the address, tainted with the association of the expunged

[105-204]paragraph, was barely carried by 107 to 105. Mr. Ponsonby had attempted

[105-205]to follow his victory by a solemn pledge binding the majority never

[105-206]again to entertain the question, but to this several members objected,

[105-207]and the motion was withdrawn. The ministry found some consolation in

[105-208]this withdrawal, which they characterized as "a retreat after a

[105-209]victory," but to the public at large, unused to place much stress on

[105-210]the minor tactics of debate, nothing appeared but the broad, general

[105-211]fact, that the first overture for a Union had been rejected. It was a

[105-212]day of immense rejoicing in Dublin; the leading anti-Unionists were

[105-213]escorted in triumph to their homes, while the Unionists were protected

[105-214]by strong military escorts from the popular indignation. At night the

[105-215]city was illuminated, and the patrols were doubled as a protection to

[105-216]the obnoxious minority.

[105-217]Mr. Ponsonby's amendment, affirmed by the House of Commons, was in

[105-218]these words:—"That the House would be ready to enter into any measure

[105-219]short of surrendering their free, resident and independent legislature

[105-220]as established in 1782." This was the _ultimatum_ of the great party

[105-221]which rallied in January, 1799, to the defence of the established

[105-222]constitution of their country. The arguments with which they sustained

[105-223]their position were few, bold, and intelligible to every capacity.

[105-224]There was the argument from Ireland's geographical situation, and the

[105-225]policy incident to it; the historical argument; the argument for a

[105-226]resident gentry occupied and retained in the country by their public

[105-227]duties; the commercial argument; the revenue argument; but above all,

[105-228]the argument of the incompetency of Parliament to put an end to its own

[105-229]existence. "Yourselves," exclaimed the eloquent Plunkett, "you may

[105-230]extinguish, but Parliament you cannot extinguish. It is enthroned in

[105-231]the hearts of the people—it is enshrined in the sanctuary of the

[105-232]constitution—it is as immortal as the island that protects it. As well

[105-233]might the frantic suicide imagine that the act which destroys his

[105-234]miserable body should also extinguish his eternal soul. Again,

[105-235]therefore, I warn you. Do not dare to lay your hands on the

[105-236]Constitution—it is above your powers!"

[105-237]These arguments were combated on the grounds that the islands were

[105-238]already united under one crown—that that species of union was uncertain

[105-239]and precarious—that the Irish Parliament was never in reality a

[105-240]national legislature; that it existed only as an instrument of class

[105-241]legislation; that the Union would benefit Ireland materially as it had

[105-242]benefited Scotland; that she would come in for a full share of imperial

[105-243]honours, expenditure and trade; that such a Union would discourage all

[105-244]future hostile attempts by France or any other foreign power against

[105-245]the connection, and other similar arguments. But the division which

[105-246]followed the first introduction of the subject showed clearly to the

[105-247]Unionists that they could not hope to succeed with the House of Commons

[105-248]as then constituted; that more time and more preparation were

[105-249]necessary. Accordingly, Lord Castlereagh was authorized in March, to

[105-250]state formally in his place, that it was not the intention of the

[105-251]government to bring up the question again during that session; an

[105-252]announcement which was hailed with a new outburst of rejoicing in the

[105-253]city.

[105-254]But those who imagined the measure was abandoned were sadly deceived.

[105-255]Steps were immediately taken by the Castle to deplete the House of its

[105-256]majority, and to supply their places before another session with forty

[105-257]or fifty new members, who would be entirely at the beck of the Chief

[105-258]Secretary. With this view, thirty-two new county judgeships were

[105-259]created; a great number of additional inspectorships and commissioners

[105-260]were also placed at the Minister's disposal; thirteen members had

[105-261]peerages for themselves or for their wives, with remainder to their

[105-262]children, and nineteen others were presented to various lucrative

[105-263]offices. The "Escheatorship of Munster"—a sort of Chiltern Hundreds

[105-264]office—was accepted by those who agreed to withdraw from opposition,

[105-265]for such considerations, but who could not be got to reverse their

[105-266]votes. By these means, and a lavish expenditure of secret service

[105-267]money, it was hoped that Mr. Pitt's stipulated majority of "not less

[105-268]than fifty" could be secured during the year.

[105-269]The other events of the session of '99, though interesting in

[105-270]themselves, are of little importance compared to the union debates. In

[105-271]the English Parliament, which met on the same day as the Irish, a

[105-272]paragraph identical with that employed by Lord Cornwallis in

[105-273]introducing the subject of the Union, was inserted in the King's

[105-274]speech. To this paragraph, repeated in the address, an amendment was

[105-275]moved by the celebrated Richard Brinsley Sheridan, and resisted with an

[105-276]eloquence scarcely inferior to his own, by his former _protege_ and

[105-277]countryman, George Canning. Canning, like Sheridan, had sprung from a

[105-278]line of Irish literateurs and actors; he had much of the wit and genius

[105-279]of his illustrious friend, with more worldly wisdom, and a higher

[105-280]sentiment of personal pride. In very early life, distinguished by great

[105-281]oratorical talents, he had deliberately attached himself to Mr. Pitt,

[105-282]while Sheridan remained steadfast to the last, in the ranks of the Whig

[105-283]or liberal party. For the land of their ancestors both had, at bottom,

[105-284]very warm, good wishes; but Canning looked down upon her politics from

[105-285]the heights of empire, while Sheridan felt for her honour and her

[105-286]interests with the affection of an expatriated son. We can well credit

[105-287]his statement to Grattan, years afterwards, when referring to his

[105-288]persistent opposition to the Union, he said, he would "have waded in

[105-289]blood to his knees," to preserve the Constitution of Ireland. In taking

[105-290]this course he had with him a few eminent friends: General Fitzpatrick,

[105-291]the former Irish Secretary, Mr. Tierney, Mr. Hobhouse, Dr. Lawrence,

[105-292]the executor of Edmund Burke, and Mr., afterwards Earl Grey. Throughout

[105-293]the entire discussion these just minded Englishmen stood boldly forward

[105-294]for the rights of Ireland, and this highly honourable conduct was long

[105-295]remembered as one of Ireland's real obligations to the Whig party.

[105-296]The resolutions intended to serve as "the basis of union," were

[105-297]introduced by Mr. Pitt, on the 21st of January, and after another

[105-298]powerful speech in opposition, from Mr. Grey, who was ably sustained by

[105-299]Mr. Sheridan, Dr. Lawrence, and some twenty others, were put and

[105-300]carried. The following are the resolutions:—

[105-309]2nd. "It would be fit to propose as the first article, to serve as a

[105-311]Ireland shall, on a day to be agreed upon, be united into one kingdom,

[105-313]3rd. "For the same purpose it would be fit to propose, that the

[105-314]succession to the monarchy and the imperial crown of the said United

[105-316]imperial crown of the said Great Britain and Ireland now stands limited

[105-401]This prorogation was originally till August, but in August it was

[105-402]extended till January, 1800. In this long interval of eight months, the

[105-404]incessantly employed, through the press, in social intercourse, in the

[105-405]grand jury room, in county and city meetings, by correspondence,

[105-407]zeal and warmth of men who felt that on one side they were labouring

[105-408]for the country, on the other for the empire. Two incidents of this
