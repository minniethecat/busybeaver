# Impulse

Impulse (*l'impulsion*), in Bergson's *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]*, is the **inner [[motion|movement]]** by which consciousness passes from one state to another. Unlike mechanical [[causality|causation]], which links externally related terms, impulse is a **[[quality|qualitative]] thrust** of the [[self|whole self]] — the [[ego|self]] does not choose among discrete options but is carried forward by its own organic momentum. Impulse is Bergson's way of describing how conscious change is **[[ego|self]]-propelled** rather than externally determined.

## Bergson's Argument

### Impulse as Inner Transition

> [003-383] …from one state to another by an inner impulse, a kind of effort.

Bergson describes the transition "from one state to another by an inner impulse, a kind of effort." Impulse is the **[[quality|qualitative]] motor** of conscious change — not a cause that pushes from behind but the [[ego|self]]'s own tendency toward expression. The word "inner" is crucial: impulse is not imposed from without but arises from the **depths** of the [[ego|self]].

> [003-200] …their medium is the same as the impulse which leads us to live in common…

The impulse that structures [[consciousness|conscious states]] shares its "medium" with "the impulse which leads us to live in common" — impulse has a **social dimension** grounded in the same organic tendency that organizes individual consciousness. [[language|Language]], social life, and pr[[action|act]]ical [[mind|reason]] all spring from the same impulsive source: the [[ego|self]]'s need to externalize it[[ego|self]] for [[action|act]]ion.

### Impulse and the Spatializing Tendency

> [001-214] …the same impulse to picture externality as to…

Bergson identifies "the same impulse to picture externality" — the drive to spatialize experience is it[[ego|self]] an impulse. This is Bergson's reflexive move: even the tendency to think spatially (which obscures duration) is it[[ego|self]] a **tendency of duration**. The spatializing [[intelligence|intellect]] is not an external imposition on consciousness; it is consciousness's own impulse toward pr[[action|act]]ical [[action|act]]ion.

### Impulse, Effort, and the Will

Impulse is closely related to [[effort]] and the [[will]]. [[effort|Effort]] is impulse **felt from within** — the [[quality|qualitative]] [[sensation]] of the [[ego|self]] mobilizing it[[ego|self]]. The will is impulse **organized and directed** — the impulse of the [[self|whole self]], not of a single isolated tendency. A free [[action|act]] is not one that lacks impulse; it is one in which the impulse expresses the **entire personality**.

## Key Distinctions

### Impulse vs. Cause

A mechanical cause is **external** to its effect — it pushes from outside. An impulse is **internal** to the [[becoming|process]] it drives — it is the [[ego|self]]'s own momentum, not a force [[action|act]]ing upon it. To explain [[action|act]]ion by impulse is not to reduce it to [[causality|causation]] but to describe its [[quality|qualitative]] char[[action|act]]er as a **living [[motion|movement]]**.

### Impulse and Desire

[[Desire]] is impulse directed toward a specific object. But in Bergson's account, desire is not an atomic mental state; it is a **phase** of the [[ego|self]]'s ongoing impulsive life. [[desire|Desire]] crystallizes impulse around a particular aim without breaking the continuity of the impulsive flow.
