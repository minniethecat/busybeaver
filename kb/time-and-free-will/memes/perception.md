# Perception

Perception, in Bergson's *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]*, is not the passive reception of external data but an **[[action|act]]ive, [[quality|qualitative]] [[action|act]]** of the [[self|whole self]]. Against the [[psychophysics]] of [[Fechner]] and [[Weber]], which treated perception as a measurable response to physical stimuli, Bergson argues that perception is always permeated by the entire history of the perceiving consciousness and cannot be decomposed into [[quantity|quantitative]] elements.

## Bergson's Argument

### Perception as Qualitative, Not Quantitative

Bergson's discussion of perception is woven through his analysis of [[intensity]]. The psychophysicists assumed that the intensity of a perception corresponds to the magnitude of the physical stimulus. Bergson replies that what we call the "intensity" of a perception is [[action|act]]ually its **quality** — the way it colors and is colored by the whole of consciousness:

> [001-024] …shall see further on, with the perception of a movement or of an external…

The perception of a [[motion|movement]] is not the sum of the perceptions of the points traversed. It is a single, [[quality|qualitative]] [[action|act]] — a **synthesis** that cannot be decomposed. This is the fundamental f[[action|act]] about perception that [[quantity|quantitative]] psychology misses.

> [001-056] …external cause or to involve the perception of muscular contraction…

Perception is sometimes [[mind|thought]] to involve muscular [[sensation]]s — we perceive the effort of perceiving. Bergson argues that even this introspective account smuggles in spatial assumptions. The "effort" of perception is a [[quality|qualitative]] state, not a [[magnitude|measurable quantity]].

### Perception and the Whole Self

Every perception is colored by the [[self|whole self]] — by memories, expectations, and the unique history of the perceiver:

> [001-028] …tered the shade of a thousand perceptions or memories, and that in this…

A perception is never isolated. It arrives already tinged by the "shade of a thousand perceptions or memories." This interpenetration of past and present in perception is what distinguishes Bergson's account from the atomistic psychology of the associationists.

> [001-033] …in cases of extreme joy, our perceptions and memories become tinged with…

In states of extreme [[feeling|emotion]], the interpenetration of perception with the [[self|whole self]] becomes particularly visible. Joy or sorrow does not merely accompany perceptions; it **transforms** them [[quality|qualitative]]ly.

### The Perception of Ease and Effort

> [001-037] …trace. At first it is only the perception of a certain ease, a certain…

Bergson analyzes the perception of ease in [[motion|movement]] as an example of qualitative perception. The [[emotion|feeling]] of ease is not a [[rhythm|measure]]ment of effort expended; it is a **global quality** of the whole experience:

> [001-038] Thus the perception of ease in motion passes over…

The perception of ease passes over into the perception of the [[motion|movement]] it[[ego|self]]; the two are not separable components but phases of a single [[quality|qualitative]] [[action|act]].

### Perception and the Oscillation of Attention

> [001-044] …motive, causes our faculty of perception to oscillate between the same…

[[attention|Attention]] is not a fixed beam but an oscillation. The faculty of perception oscillates between different aspects of the object, and this oscillation is it[[ego|self]] [[quality|qualitative]] — it depends on interest, fatigue, and the whole state of the [[ego|self]]:

> [001-067] …but in what does our perception of its intensity exactly consist?

The question of what the intensity of a perception consists in cannot be answered by pointing to a measurable stimulus. The intensity is the **quality** of the perceptual [[action|act]], which varies with the whole state of consciousness.

| Aspect | Quantitative Account (Psychophysics) | Bergson's Qualitative Account |
|--------|--------------------------------------|------------------------------|
| Perception of intensity | Proportional to stimulus magnitude | Qualitative transformation of whole consciousness |
| Perception of [[motion|movement]] | Sum of point-perceptions | Single synthetic [[action|act]] |
| Role of memory | Separate from perception | Permeates every perception |
| Measurement | Possible (psychophysical law) | Impossible ([[quality|qualitative]]) |
| [[attention|Attention]] | Fixed capacity | Oscillating, [[quality|qualitative]]ly variable |

## Key Distinctions

### Perception vs. Sensation

Bergson distinguishes between [[sensation]] (the immediate [[quality|qualitative]] state) and perception (the [[action|act]] that synthesizes [[sensation]]s into an object). But this is a distinction within the [[quality|qualitative]], not between quality and quantity. Both [[sensation]] and perception are **[[quality|qualitative]] [[action|act]]s** that cannot be decomposed into measurable parts.

### Perception vs. Memory

Perception is always permeated by [[memory]]. The present perception is not a snapshot of the present moment; it is a synthesis in which the entire past of the [[ego|self]] is [[action|act]]ive. Bergson develops this theme more fully in *[[matter|Matter]] and [[memory|Memory]]* (1896), but it is already present in *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]*.

### Perception and Space

Our perception of external objects necessarily involves [[space]] — the [[homogeneity|homogeneous medium]] in which objects are distributed. But Bergson insists that the **[[action|act]]** of perceiving, as a conscious event, occurs in [[duration]], not in space. The spatial framework of perceived objects should not be confused with the temporal char[[action|act]]er of the perceiving [[action|act]].

## Historical Context

### Fechner and Psychophysics

[[Fechner]]'s *Elements of Psychophysics* (1860) claimed to establish a mathematical law relating stimulus intensity to [[sensation]] intensity. Bergson's critique of perception is a sustained argument against this project: perception is [[quality|qualitative]], not [[quantity|quantitative]], and no mathematical law can capture its nature.

### The Associationist Account

The [[association|associationist psychology]] of [[Mill]] and [[Spencer]] treated perception as the association of atomic [[sensation]]s. Bergson counters that perception is never atomic; it is always already a synthesis, and the "atoms" are products of subsequent analysis, not original data.
