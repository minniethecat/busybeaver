# Instinct

Instinct, in Bergson's *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]*, is a form of knowledge and [[action|act]]ion that operates **within** life rather than outside it. Unlike the [[intelligence|intellect]], which breaks experience into discrete, spatialized elements for [[analysis]], instinct grasps its object as a whole and [[action|act]]s on it immediately. Bergson's treatment of instinct is brief in this early work but lays the foundation for the fuller theory in *Creative [[evolution|Evolution]]* (1907): instinct is the mode of cognition proper to [[duration]], and [[intuition]] is instinct made [[ego|self]]-conscious.

## Bergson's Argument

### Instinctive Distinctions

> [001-047] …sides degrees of intensity we instinctively distinguish degrees of depth…

Bergson notes that we "instinctively" distinguish not only degrees of intensity but also degrees of **depth** in [[consciousness|conscious states]]. This instinctive distinction is more faithful to experience than the [[intelligence|intellect]]ual one: depth is a [[quality|qualitative]] dimension that resists spatialization, whereas intensity, when [[intelligence|intellect]]ualized, becomes a [[quantity|measurable magnitude]]. Instinct knows what the [[intelligence|intellect]] must rediscover through effort.

> [001-048] …reater number of emotions are instinct with a thousand sensations, feelings…

Bergson uses "instinct" here in an older sense — meaning "filled with" or "permeated by" — to describe how [[feeling|emotion]]s are saturated with a "thousand [[sensation]]s." This double meaning is deliberate: [[feeling|emotion]]s are **instinct with** [[sensation]]s in the way that instinct is instinct with knowledge — immediately, without the mediation of concepts.

### Confused Instinct and Spatialization

> [003-175] …ate gives place to a confused instinct, capable, like all instincts,…

As the [[intelligence|intellect]] relaxes its hold, "a confused instinct" emerges — a mode of awareness that does not separate subject and object, quality and quantity, space and time. This instinct is "confused" not because it is inferior to clear [[intelligence|intellect]]ual knowledge but because it has not yet been **spatialized**. Like all instincts, it is capable of guiding [[action|act]]ion with precision, but it does so from within the situation, not from an external standpoint.

### The Instinct to Solidify

> [003-182] … and individual existence. We instinctively tend to solidify our impressions…

Bergson identifies a paradox: one instinct — the instinct to "solidify" impressions — works **against** the deeper instinct that grasps duration. We "instinctively tend to solidify our impressions" in order to [[action|act]] on them and communicate them. This solidifying instinct serves pr[[action|act]]ical life but obscures the nature of consciousness. Philosophy must resist this instinct with another: the **instinct for duration**, which Bergson will later call [[intuition]].

### The Instincts of the Intellect

> [003-192] …ows how our intellect has its instincts—and what can an instinct of …

Bergson's striking phrase — "our [[intelligence|intellect]] has its instincts" — undermines the sharp opposition between instinct and [[intelligence|intellect]]. The [[intelligence|intellect]]'s "instincts" are its habitual spatializing operations: [[number|counting]], distinguishing, comparing. These feel natural and immediate, but they are acquired — habits solidified into instincts. Recognizing that the [[intelligence|intellect]]'s operations are **themselves instinctive** is the first step toward suspending them.

### The Instinctive Belief of Mankind

> [003-235] … cannot bite into it; and the instinctive, though vague, belief of mankind…

Bergson allies him[[ego|self]] with the "instinctive, though vague, belief of mankind" in [[free-will|freedom]]. Ordinary consciousness knows, instinctively, that it is free — this is not a philosophical conclusion but an **immediate datum**. Philosophy's task is not to prove [[free-will|freedom]] but to remove the [[intelligence|intellect]]ual obstacles (determinism, spatialization) that make this instinctive belief seem naive.

| Form of Instinct | What It Does | Philosophical Significance |
|-----------------|-------------|--------------------------|
| Instinctive distinction | Distinguishes depth from intensity | More faithful to duration than [[intelligence|intellect]]ual analysis |
| Confused instinct | Grasps whole without separating terms | Pre-spatialized awareness; precursor of [[intuition]] |
| Solidifying instinct | Makes impressions fixed and communicable | Serves pr[[action|act]]ical life but obscures duration |
| Intellect's instincts | Spatializing operations ([[number|counting]], comparing) | [[habit|Habit]]s mistaken for natural operations |
| Instinctive belief in [[free-will|freedom]] | Immediate sense of being free | The datum philosophy must recover, not prove |

### Instinct and the Organic

> [003-152] …motion there is a natural and instinctive separation between the spatial…

Instinctive separation is "natural" — it belongs to the living being's engagement with the world, not to the [[intelligence|intellect]]'s abstr[[action|act]] analysis. The instinctive distinction between the spatial and the temporal is more trustworthy than the philosopher's conceptual distinction because it is rooted in the **organic functioning** of consciousness, not in a theoretical scheme.

## Key Distinctions

### Instinct vs. Intellect

The [[intelligence|intellect]] analyzes, breaks down, spatializes; instinct synthesizes, unifies, lives within duration. But Bergson does not simply value instinct over [[intelligence|intellect]] — the [[intelligence|intellect]] has its proper domain (pr[[action|act]]ical life, science). The error is letting the [[intelligence|intellect]]'s spatial habits **colonize** the domain of consciousness, where instinct (disciplined as [[intuition]]) is the appropriate guide.

### Instinct vs. Intuition

[[Intuition]], in Bergson's mature philosophy, is **instinct become disinterested and [[ego|self]]-conscious**. Instinct guides [[action|act]]ion; [[intuition]] guides knowledge. The "confused instinct" of *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]* is [[intuition]] in embryo — it grasps duration but cannot articulate what it grasps. Philosophical [[reflection]] transforms instinct into [[intuition]] without losing its immediate cont[[action|act]] with [[reality|the real]].

### Instinct vs. Habit

[[Habit]] is an **acquired** automaticity; instinct is a **constitutional** automaticity. [[habit|Habit]] can be unlearned; instinct can only be disciplined. The spatializing tendency is habitual (sedimented by language and pr[[action|act]]ical life); the instinct for duration is constitutional (rooted in the nature of consciousness). Philosophy must undo the habit to liberate the instinct.
