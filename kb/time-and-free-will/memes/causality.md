# Causality

Causality, in Bergson's *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]*, is a concept whose legitimate domain is the **material world** — the world of objects distributed in [[space]]. When applied to conscious life, causality becomes incoherent, because causal connection presupposes the mutual externality of cause and effect, and in [[duration]], [[consciousness|conscious states]] are not external to one another. Bergson's critique of causality is the foundation of his argument that [[determinism]] is a **badly posed** problem.

## Bergson's Argument

### Causality and the Mutual Externality of Terms

The fundamental assumption behind causality is that cause and effect are **distinct, separable items** that can be linked by a law:

> [001-182] …causality as regular succession…

Whether we conceive causality as necessary connection (rationalism) or as [[succession|regular succession]] (empiricism), we presuppose that cause and effect are **mutually external** — that they are two separate events whose relation we then investigate. But in [[duration]], [[consciousness|conscious states]] are never mutually external; they interpenetrate.

> [001-140] …law of causality…

The "law" of causality requires terms to which it can apply. In space, objects are distinct and can be linked by laws. In consciousness, states melt into one another; there are no distinct terms to be linked, and therefore no causal law can apply in the same sense.

### Causality in the Physical World vs. Consciousness

> [001-211] …gave causality same meaning in inner and outer…

Bergson's target is the assumption that causality means the same thing in the inner world (consciousness) as in the outer world (physical nature). When we say a billiard ball causes another to move, cause and effect are **separate objects** in space. When we say a [[mind|thought]] causes a [[emotion|feeling]], the "cause" and "effect" are not separate items at all; they are phases of a single continuous [[becoming|process]].

> [001-216] …of causality…

The attempt to apply physical causality to consciousness is the error that makes determinism seem plausible. Once we treat motives as causes and [[action|act]]ions as effects — as if they were distinct objects in space — the problem of [[will|free will]] becomes insoluble.

| Aspect | Physical Causality | Conscious "Causality" |
|--------|-------------------|----------------------|
| Terms | Distinct objects in [[space]] | Interpenetrating states in [[duration]] |
| Relation | External (law-like connection) | Internal (organic growth) |
| Necessity | [[mechanical|Mechanical]] determination | Qualitative coherence |
| Prediction | Possible in principle | Impossible (novelty is [[reality|real]]) |
| Domain | Material world | Mistaken application |

### Two Conceptions of Causality

> [001-149] …two conceptions of, in causality…

Bergson identifies two [[idea|conception]]s of causality. The first is the scientific [[idea|conception]]: causality as a relation between **distinct phenomena** that can be formulated mathematically. This is legitimate within its domain (the physical world). The second is the psychological [[idea|conception]]: causality as the **determination of inner states** by prior inner states. This is the illegitimate [[extensity|extension]] that Bergson critiques.

> [001-198] …attempt to replace causality by…

Some philosophers, recognizing the difficulty of applying causality to consciousness, attempt to replace causality with other concepts (identity, correlation). Bergson argues that these replacements still presuppose the spatial schema of distinct terms and therefore fail to escape the problem.

### Causality and Identity

> [001-228] …coincidence of causality with identity…

Bergson notes a striking f[[action|act]]: in the history of philosophy, causality has often been identified with **identity** — the cause is said to contain the effect, or the effect to be the cause in another form. This coincidence suggests, Bergson argues, that causality is fundamentally a **spatial** concept: it handles change by breaking it into parts (cause, effect) and then reuniting them by a law. Real duration does not need this operation, because in duration, change is continuous and does not break into parts.

## Key Distinctions

### Causality vs. Succession

[[Hume]]'s great contribution was to show that we never directly perceive causal connection — we see only regular [[succession]]. Bergson radicalizes this insight: even "[[succession|regular succession]]" presupposes spatialization. To say A regularly succeeds B, we must treat A and B as **distinct items** whose sequence we can observe. But in duration, states are not distinct items in a sequence; they are phases of a continuous whole.

### Causality vs. Free Will

The determinism–free-will debate is, at bottom, a debate about causality. The determinist says: every conscious state has a sufficient cause in prior states. The libertarian says: some [[consciousness|conscious states]] are uncaused. Bergson says: **both are wrong** because both treat [[consciousness|conscious states]] as if they were objects to which causal relations could apply. The free [[action|act]] is not uncaused; it is **non-causal** — it grows out of the [[self|whole self]] in a way that is not reducible to a causal [[mechanical|mechanism]].

### Causality and Determinism

[[Determinism]] is causality universalized. If every event has a sufficient cause, then the entire course of the universe is determined. Bergson's critique of determinism is a critique of this universalization: causality is a spatial concept, legitimate within its domain (physical nature), but it cannot be extended to consciousness and duration.

## Historical Context

### Hume's Critique

[[Hume]] argued that causal necessity is not given in experience — we see only constant conjunction, not necessary connection. Bergson takes this further: even "constant conjunction" presupposes the **spatial distinctness** of the conjoined terms. The very framework of Hume's analysis — treating cause and effect as separate events — already concedes the spatial assumption Bergson questions.

### Kant's Response to Hume

[[Kant]] responded to Hume by making causality an *a priori* cat[[self|ego]]ry of the [[mind|understanding]] — a necessary condition of experience. Bergson's response is subtler: causality may be necessary for the experience of **objects in space**, but it is not necessary for the experience of **consciousness in duration**. [[kant|Kant]] mistook the conditions of spatial experience for the conditions of all experience.

### The Nineteenth-Century Debate

> [001-217] …conception of causality as leading to…

Nineteenth-century philosophy was consumed by debates about causality: [[mechanical|mechanism]] vs. [[dynamism]], determinism vs. [[free-will|freedom]], material cause vs. final cause. Bergson's intervention is to argue that these debates are all **badly posed** because they all accept the spatial schema that makes causality seem applicable to consciousness.
