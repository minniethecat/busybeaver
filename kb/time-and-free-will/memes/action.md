# Action

Action, in Bergson's *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]*, is the **lived expression** of the [[ego|self]] — not a mere bodily [[motion|movement]] or an effect of prior causes, but the outward manifestation of the whole of one's conscious duration. The problem of [[free-will]] is, at bottom, a problem about the nature of action: is an action determined by prior motives, or does it flow freely from the [[self]]? Bergson's answer is that the question it[[ego|self]] is **badly posed** — it treats motives and actions as separate items in [[space]], when in [[reality|real]]ity they are phases of a single continuous [[becoming|process]].

## Bergson's Argument

### Action as Expression of the Whole Self

> [001-016] …the least appreciation of the number of causes, their mode of action or their extent.

The determinist treats action as the **resultant** of a set of prior causes — motives, character traits, external circumstances — that can in principle be enumerated and weighed. Bergson's central claim is that this model applies only to the **[[self|superficial self]]**. The [[self|deep self]] does not "have" motives that "produce" actions; it **expresses** it[[ego|self]] in action as a whole:

> [001-033] …diminished by this attraction, our ideas and sensations…

Motives are not external forces acting on the [[ego|self]] — they are **aspects** of the [[ego|self]] at a given moment. When we act, we do not add up motives and reach a [[choice|decision]]; we live through a [[quality|qualitative]] [[becoming|process]] whose outcome is our action. The action is not caused by motives; it is the [[ego|self]]'s way of **being** at that moment.

### Action and Muscular Contraction

> [001-028] …increasing number of muscular contractions which take place on the surface…

Bergson notes an important fact: every conscious state has a **bodily accompaniment** — muscular contractions, changes in breathing, physiological adjustments. The [[emotion|feeling]] of effort, for example, is largely the perception of these bodily changes. But this does not mean the action is **reducible** to the bodily changes. The bodily accompaniment is the spatial expression of a [[quality|qualitative]] inner [[becoming|process]].

| View | Nature of Action | Role of Motives |
|------|-----------------|----------------|
| [[determinism|Determinism]] | Effect of prior causes | Sufficient conditions |
| Libertarianism | Uncaused event | Overridden or absent |
| Bergson | Expression of [[self]] | Aspects of the [[self|whole self]] |

### The Free Act

The free act, for Bergson, is not an act without **[[mind|reason]]s** — it is an act whose [[mind|reason]]s cannot be separated from the [[ego|self]] that acts. When an action expresses the whole [[self|deep self]] in its full durational continuity, it is free. When it expresses only a part of the [[ego|self]] (a habit, a social [[habit|convention]], an isolated impulse), it is relatively determined:

> [001-056] …the perception of muscular contraction. But such states are rare.

Truly free acts are **rare**, Bergson admits, because most of our actions express only a segment of the [[ego|self]] — the [[self|superficial self]] of habit, language, and social [[habit|convention]]. Freedom is not a constant property of all actions but an **achievement** — the alignment of action with the whole of one's lived duration.

## Key Distinctions

### Action vs. Choice

[[Choice]] is the deliberative [[becoming|process]] that the determinist-libetarian debate focuses on — weighing motives, deciding between alternatives. Bergson argues that choice, understood this way, is already spatialized (it treats motives as separate objects to be compared). The free act is not the outcome of a choice among options; it is the **spontaneous expression** of the [[self|whole self]].

### Action vs. Effort

[[Effort]] is the felt experience of action — the muscular and [[tension|psychic tension]] that accompanies the transition from motive to [[motion|movement]]. The [[emotion|feeling]] of effort reveals the **continuity** between inner state and outer action — a continuity that determinism breaks by separating cause and effect.

### Action vs. Habit

[[Habit]] is action that expresses only a part of the [[ego|self]] — a sedimented pattern that operates without engaging the whole duration. Most everyday actions are habitual and therefore relatively unfree. The free act is precisely the act that breaks with habit.

## Historical Context

Bergson's theory of action engages with **Aristotle's** practical syllogism (action as the conclusion of a practical [[reason|reasoning]] [[becoming|process]]), **[[kant|Kant]]'s** [[idea|conception]] of [[will|free will]] as rational [[ego|self]]-determination, and the **nineteenth-century** debate about whether [[will|volition]] is a distinct [[mind|mental faculty]]. Bergson's innovation is to relocate the problem from the **structure** of [[choice|decision]] to the **temporality** of the [[ego|self]] that acts. Freedom is not about the logic of choice but about the mode of being in [[duration]].
