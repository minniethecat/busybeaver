# Attention

Attention (*attention*) occupies a pivotal position in Bergson's analysis of psychic states. It stands **between** pure [[sensation]] and violent [[emotion]], sharing features of both: like [[sensation]], it involves the focusing of a sense organ; like [[feeling|emotion]], it involves a coordination of the whole [[matter|body]] through muscular contr[[action|act]]ion.

Its apparent intensity, Bergson argues, is nothing other than the **extent and quality of the muscular accompaniment**.

## Bergson's Argument

### Attention as an Intermediate State

Bergson classifies attention among the "intermediate states" — psychic phenomena that are neither pure [[sensation]]s nor deep [[feeling|emotion]]s:

> …in the first case there is intellectual effort or attention; in the second we have the emotions which may be called violent or acute… [001-074]

Attention and violent [[feeling|emotion]] share a common structure: both are **[[idea|ideas]] coordinated with muscular systems**. The difference is the nature of the coordinating idea:

| State | Coordinating [[idea|Idea]] | Bodily Expression |
|-------|------------------|-------------------|
| Attention | Reflective idea of **knowing** | Muscular tension in sense organs, brow, posture |
| Violent [[emotion|Emotion]] | Unreflective idea of **[[action|act]]ing** | Whole-[[matter|body]] muscular contr[[action|act]]ion, preparation for [[motion|movement]] |

### The Muscular Basis of Attention

Bergson draws on the work of [[fechner|Fechner]] and [[ribot|Ribot]] to establish that attention is inseparable from bodily [[motion|movement]]:

> Fechner had already reduced the effort of attention in a sense-organ to the muscular feeling "produced by putting in motion, by a sort of reflex action, the muscles which are correlated with the different sense organs." [001-076]

> …with many adults eager attention gives rise to a protrusion of the lips… [001-077]

> …movement will always enter into voluntary attention, even if it be nothing more than an inhibition of movement. [001-078]

Attention is not a purely [[intelligence|intellect]]ual phenomenon that happens to have bodily side-effects. The [[motion|movement]]s **are part of** the phenomenon; they "express it in terms of space." To attend is to mobilize the [[matter|body]] in a particular way.

### Attention and the Aesthetic

Bergson's analysis of the [[feeling|aesthetic feeling]] involves attention in a crucial role. Art works by redistributing our attention — putting to sleep our "[[action|act]]ive and resistant powers" and making us responsive to suggestion:

> …causing our attention to swing to and fro between former sensations and ideas… [001-044]

> …our attention is distributed equally over a whole surface… [001-045]

> …sometimes it draws our attention from them, but not so that they become lost to sight… [001-047]

The three stages of aesthetic engagement correspond to three distributions of attention — from peripheral interruption, to focused awareness, to total absorption.

### Attention and the Estimation of Intensity

Bergson uses attention to expose a general fallacy in the estimation of [[intensity|psychic intensity]]. When we focus attention on a [[sensation]], the [[sensation]] seems to grow in intensity. But what [[action|act]]ually happens is that attention **adds new elements** — the muscular [[sensation]]s of the attentive [[action|act]] it[[ego|self]] become fused with the original [[sensation]]:

> …as your attention was concentrated on your closed hand, you failed to notice distinctly all these concomitant sensations… [001-071]

The apparent "increase" in the [[sensation]] is [[reality|real]]ly the entry of [[quality|qualitative]]ly new elements from the attentive [[action|act]]. What we call "paying closer attention" is not turning up the volume on a fixed signal; it is enriching the experience with new [[quality|qualitative]] content.

### Attention as a Fact of Consciousness

Bergson emphasizes that attention is not a "faculty" in the traditional sense. It is a **f[[action|act]] of consciousness** — the way consciousness organizes it[[ego|self]] around an object:

> …it is just because the state which engrosses our attention… supplants all the others… [002-031]

When we attend to something, our whole psychic field reorganizes around it. This reorganization is [[quality|qualitative]], not [[quantity|quantitative]] — it changes the char[[action|act]]er of the experience, not its [[quantity|measurable magnitude]].

## Key Distinctions

### Attention vs. Effort

The "effort of attention" is frequently discussed in *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]* as a specific kind of [[effort]] — [[intelligence|intellect]]ual rather than muscular:

> …we do not see any essential difference between the effort of attention and what may be called the effort of psychic tension… [001-080]

Both kinds of effort are reducible to systems of muscular contr[[action|act]]ion coordinated by an idea. The "effort" we feel when concentrating is the muscular [[sensation]] of maintaining the attentive posture, not a purely mental [[effort|exertion]].

### Attention and Perception

Attention is the **[[action|act]]ive dimension** of [[perception]]. While perception registers what is present to the senses, attention selects, focuses, and amplifies — but the amplification is always through the addition of new [[quality|qualitative]] elements (muscular [[sensation]]s, associated memories), never through the increase of a homogeneous magnitude.

### Attention and Duration

In [[duration]], attention is what allows consciousness to **deepen** rather than **spread**. When we attend to our own inner states without spatializing them, we discover the [[interpenetration|qualitative multiplicity]] that concepts obscure. Attention, in this sense, is akin to [[intuition]] — it is consciousness turning toward its own [[quality|qualitative]] richness.
