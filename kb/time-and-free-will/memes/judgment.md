# Judgment

Judgment, in Bergson's *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]*, is a **mediated mental operation** that stands at a distance from immediate conscious experience. Unlike [[intuition]], which grasps [[duration]] directly, judgment belongs to the domain of [[memory]], spatial comparison, and retrospective evaluation. Bergson's treatment of judgment is critical: wherever philosophical psychology relies on judgment — as in [[psychophysics]], which asks subjects to judge the intensity of [[sensation]]s — it imports spatial cat[[self|ego]]ries into the analysis of consciousness.

## Bergson's Argument

### Judgment and the Senses

> [001-015] …auses, and thus to revise the judgment of our senses, which at first…

Bergson opens Chapter I by noting that science "revises the judgment of our senses" — it corrects our immediate sensory judgments by discovering external causes. But this correction is it[[ego|self]] a judgment, and it operates at a **further remove** from immediate experience. The philosopher's task is not to add another layer of judgment but to return to the immediate data of consciousness before any judgment has been passed.

### Psychophysical Judgment

> [003-018] …psychophysicist, and that its judgment is worth here just what psych…

Bergson is sharply critical of the role of judgment in [[psychophysics]]. When a subject judges that one [[sensation]] is twice as intense as another, this judgment is taken as a [[rhythm|measure]]ment of a psychic state. But Bergson argues that the judgment is not a **direct report** of conscious experience; it is a cognitive [[action|act]] that compares, cat[[self|ego]]rizes, and spatializes what was originally [[quality|qualitative]].

The judgment "this [[sensation]] is twice as intense" does not describe the [[sensation]] it[[ego|self]] — it describes a **relation constructed by the judging mind**. The psychophysicist treats this constructed relation as if it were a property of the [[sensation]], committing the fundamental error of substituting the symbol for [[reality|the real]]ity.

### Judgment, Memory, and Character

> [003-268] …the memory rather than to the judgment. Here will be found, within t…

Bergson distinguishes judgment from [[memory]] in an important passage about char[[action|act]]er. When we grasp someone's char[[action|act]]er, we do not "judge" it in the sense of comparing it to a standard; we **remember** the person's [[action|act]]s and let the char[[action|act]]er emerge from the totality of memory. Judgment fragments; memory unifies. The [[mind|understanding]] of a person through memory is closer to [[intuition]] than to judgment.

### Judgment of Character

> [003-309] …ct of our friend as to pass a judgment on his present character, tha…

When we "pass judgment" on a friend's char[[action|act]]er, we are doing something that feels immediate but is in f[[action|act]] the **synthesis of countless memories**. Bergson does not deny that such judgments can be accurate; he denies that they are elementary mental operations. Judgment is always a **construction**, never a direct datum of consciousness.

|-----------------|--------|------|---------------------|
| [[Intuition]] | Immediate experience | Direct grasp | In duration |
| Judgment | Mediated cognition | Comparison and evaluation | Retrospective, spatialized |
| [[Memory]] | Retained past | [[organic|Organic]] synthesis | Bears duration within it |
| [[Analysis]] | Intellectual dissection | Breaking into parts | External to duration |

### Judgment vs. Immediate Consciousness

Throughout *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]*, Bergson draws a sharp distinction between what is **given immediately** in consciousness and what is added by judgment. The [[sensation]] of red is immediate; the judgment "this red is brighter than that red" is a **comparative [[action|act]]** that introduces spatial relations. The successive notes of a [[melody]] are experienced in duration; the judgment "note A preceded note B" spatializes this succession into a timeline.

Judgment, for Bergson, is the operation by which the mind **steps outside duration** and treats [[consciousness|conscious states]] as objects to be compared. This makes judgment useful for pr[[action|act]]ical life but misleading for philosophy.

## Key Distinctions

### Judgment vs. Intuition

[[Intuition]] is Bergson's term for the direct, non-conceptual grasp of duration. Judgment is the opposite: a mediated, conceptual, comparative operation. The method of *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]* is to suspend judgment and recover [[intuition]] — to set aside the spatial constructions that judgment introduces and attend to consciousness as it is immediately given.

### Judgment vs. Memory

[[memory|Memory]], for Bergson, is not a filing cabinet of past judgments but the **survival of the past in the present**. When I remember a friend's past [[action|act]]s, I do not retrieve judgments about those [[action|act]]s; I carry the past forward into the present, where it colors my perception of the friend. Judgment separates past from present; memory unites them in [[duration]].

### Judgment and Reflection

[[Reflection]] is philosophical judgment properly disciplined — it recognizes its own mediated char[[action|act]]er and works to overcome it. Bergson's "strenuous effort of reflection" at [003-450] is not an effort to judge better but an effort to **suspend judgment** and return to the immediate. [[reflection|Reflection]], paradoxically, must undo the work of judgment.

## Historical Context

Bergson's critique of judgment as a source of philosophical knowledge aligns him with a tradition running from **Pascal** (the distinction between *esprit de géométrie* and *esprit de finesse*) through **Rousseau** (the critique of reflection as a corruption of natural [[feeling|sentiment]]) to the Romantic re[[action|act]]ion against Enlightenment rationalism. But Bergson is not an irrationalist: he does not reject judgment in its proper domain (pr[[action|act]]ical life, science). He only rejects the **philosophical use** of judgment to analyze consciousness, because judgment spatializes what it touches.
