# Anger

## Overview / Definition

In *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]*, Bergson examines anger (and rage) as a paradigmatic case of the "violent or acute" [[feeling|emotion]]s [001-074], using it to advance his central thesis that [[intensity|psychic intensity]] is [[quality|qualitative]], not [[quantity|quantitative]]. Anger is not a state whose intensity can be [[rhythm|measure]]d on a numerical scale; rather, its intensity *is* the extent and depth of the [[organic|organic disturbance]] that accompanies it — "the deeper and deeper disturbance of the [[body|organism]], a disturbance which consciousness has no difficulty in measuring by the number and extent of the bodily surfaces concerned" [001-082].

## Bergson's Argument

Bergson's analysis of anger unfolds through a critique of physiological reductionism and a defense of the irreducibly psychic element within [[feeling|emotion]]:

**Anger as effort of [[tension|psychic tension]].** Bergson classifies anger among the states that involve "the effort of [[tension|psychic tension]]: acute desire, uncontrolled anger, [[desire|passion]]ate love, violent hatred" [001-080]. Each of these may be reduced, in part, to "a system of muscular contr[[action|act]]ions co-ordinated by an idea" [001-080]. But while [[attention]] is coordinated by the reflective idea of knowing, [[feeling|emotion]] is coordinated by "the unreflective idea of [[action|act]]ing" [001-080].

**The physiological account and its limit.** Drawing on [[darwin|Darwin]]'s description of the physiological symptoms of rage — accelerated heartbeat, flushed or pallid face, labored breathing, trembling, clenched teeth, violent gestures [001-081] — Bergson acknowledges the bodily dimension of anger. However, he resists the [[james|James]]ian reduction of [[feeling|emotion]] to organic [[sensation]]s alone. "There will always be an irreducible psychic element in anger, if this be only the idea of striking or fighting, of which [[darwin|Darwin]] speaks, and which gives a common [[tendency|direction]] to so many diverse [[motion|movement]]s" [001-081]. The idea is what unifies the diverse physiological symptoms into *anger* rather than a mere collection of bodily events.

**Intensity as organic involvement.** Bergson's key claim is that "the growing intensity of the state it[[ego|self]] is, we believe, nothing but the deeper and deeper disturbance of the [[body|organism]]" [001-082]. Intensity is not an additive magnitude — it is a [[quality|qualitative]] spreading of the disturbance across more and more of the bodily surface. The distinction between a mild irritation and a violent rage is not one of degree on a single scale, but of how much of the [[body|organism]] is implicated.

**Restrained rage and the paradox of intensity.** Bergson anticipates and refutes the objection that restrained rage can be more intense than expressed rage. "The [[mind|reason]] is that, where [[feeling|emotion]] has free play, consciousness does not dwell on the details of the accompanying [[motion|movement]]s, but it does dwell upon them and is concentrated upon them when its object is to conceal them" [001-082]. The [[emotion|feeling]] of greater intensity in suppression is a function of heightened [[attention]] to the bodily state, not an increase in some measurable [[feeling|emotion]]al magnitude.

**The eliminative argument.** Bergson's most radical move is eliminative: "Eliminate, in short, all trace of [[organic|organic disturbance]], all tendency towards muscular contr[[action|act]]ion, and all that will be left of anger will be the idea, or, if you still insist on making it an [[feeling|emotion]], you will be unable to assign it any intensity" [001-082]. Anger without bodily involvement is not anger — it is a bare [[intelligence|intellect]]ual representation. This is Bergson's proof that [[feeling|emotion]]al intensity is not a [[quantity|quantitative]] property added to an idea, but the [[quality|qualitative]] f[[action|act]] of organic participation.

## Anger and the Deep Self

Beyond its role in the theory of intensity, anger reappears in Bergson's discussion of the [[self|deep self]] in Chapter 3. A "violent anger roused by some accidental circumstance, an hereditary vice suddenly emerging from the obscure depths of the [[body|organism]] to the surface of consciousness, will [[action|act]] almost like a hypnotic suggestion" [003-268]. Such an anger is not integrated into the continuous fabric of the [[ego|self]]; it "usurps the [[personality|whole personality]] when its time comes" [003-268], operating as an autonomous element rather than an expression of the whole person. This distinction between surface anger (re[[action|act]]ive, isolated) and deep anger (expressive of the [[self|whole self]]) parallels Bergson's broader distinction between the superficial and deep selves, and has implications for his theory of [[will|free will]]: an [[action|act]] is free only when it expresses the entire personality, not when it is dictated by an isolated [[desire|passion]].

## Key Distinctions

| Aspect | Physiological Account ([[james|James]]) | Bergson's Account |
|--------|------------------------------|-------------------|
| Nature of [[feeling|emotion]] | Sum of organic [[sensation]]s | [[organic|Organic]] disturbance + irreducible psychic idea |
| Intensity | Correlate of physiological magnitude | Qualitative extent of organic involvement |
| Restrained rage | Less intense (fewer symptoms) | More intensely *felt* (heightened [[attention]]) |
| Anger without [[matter|body]] | Impossible ([[feeling|emotion]] = bodily change) | Impossible — only the idea remains, without intensity |
