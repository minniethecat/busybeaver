# Association

Association (*l'association*), in Bergson's *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]*, is the target of one of his most sustained critiques. The **associationist psychology** — from Locke and Hume through [[james|James]] [[mill|Mill]] and Taine — holds that mental states are linked by external laws (contiguity, resemblance, contrast). Bergson's decisive move is not to deny that association occurs but to show that it applies **only to the [[self|superficial self]]** — the [[ego|self]] that has been spatialized by language and pr[[action|act]]ical life. In the [[self|deep self]], states do not associate; they **interpenetrate**.

## Bergson's Argument

### The Limits of Association

> [003-191] …conscious states obey the laws of association. Deeper down they interpenetrate…

Bergson's key distinction: [[consciousness|conscious states]] "obey the laws of association" only at the surface. "Deeper down they interpenetrate" — association is the spatialized appearance of a deeper [[quality|qualitative]] unity. The associationist mistakes the **effect** (the spatial arrangement of states for pr[[action|act]]ical purposes) for the **cause** (the [[action|act]]ual organization of conscious life).

> [003-192] …we shall inevitably fall into the mistakes of associationism.

Bergson warns that unless we are vigilant, "we shall inevitably fall into the mistakes of associationism." The pull toward spatializing [[mind|thought]] is so strong that even philosophers who critique associationism tend to reproduce its assumptions. The associationist [[idea|conception]] is not a theory we choose; it is a **default mode of [[mind|thought]]** built into language and pr[[action|act]]ical [[mind|reason]].

### Associationism and the Superficial Self

> [003-195] …the associationist theory applies. External to each other, the states are linked…

The associationist theory applies only where states are "external to each other" — in the domain of the [[self|superficial self]] that lives in [[space]] and [[language]]. But this externality is not given; it is **produced** by the spatializing [[intelligence|intellect]]. The associationist takes a product of analysis (separated states) and treats it as the original [[reality|real]]ity, then invents laws to reconnect what was never genuinely separate.

> [003-201] …the concrete and living self as an association of terms which are distinct from each other…

Bergson rejects the representation of "the concrete and living [[ego|self]] as an association of terms which are distinct from each other." The [[ego|self]] is not a collection of mental atoms held together by associative bonds; it is an **[[organic|organic whole]]** whose states grow into one another. Association is the shadow that this [[organic|organic unity]] casts when projected into space.

### Associationist Determinism

> [003-229] This is the origin of associationist determinism, an hypothesis…

Bergson traces [[determinism]] to associationism: if mental states are linked by external laws, then one state necessarily calls forth the next, and the [[ego|self]] becomes a **chain** rather than a living growth. "Associationist determinism" is the fatal consequence of spatializing the [[ego|self]] — once states are laid out side by side, [[causality|causal law]]s inevitably appear to connect them.

> [003-239] …psychological determinism depends on associationist conception of mind.

"Psychological determinism depends on associationist [[idea|conception]] of mind" — Bergson's critique of determinism is inseparable from his critique of associationism. To defeat determinism, it is not enough to argue for [[freedom]] in the abstr[[action|act]]; one must dismantle the associationist picture of mind that makes determinism seem inevitable.

### Association vs. Interpenetration

> [003-024] …themselves, independently of all association of ideas…

Genuine conscious [[becoming|process]]es occur "independently of all association of ideas." The interpenetration of states in [[duration]] is not a form of association; it is a **different kind of relation** altogether — [[quality|qualitative]] rather than mechanical, organic rather than additive. The language of association can never capture this relation because language it[[ego|self]] spatializes.

| Associationist Claim | Bergson's Response |
|---------------------|-------------------|
| Mental states are discrete atoms | States are phases of an [[organic|organic whole]] |
| States are linked by external laws (contiguity, resemblance) | States interpenetrate [[quality|qualitative]]ly |
| The [[ego|self]] is a bundle of associated states | The [[ego|self]] is an [[organic|organic unity]] expressing it[[ego|self]] through states |
| [[determinism|Determinism]] follows from associative links | [[determinism|Determinism]] rests on a spatialized picture of mind |
| Association applies to all mental life | Association applies only to the superficial, spatialized [[ego|self]] |

## Key Distinctions

### Association vs. Interpenetration

[[Interpenetration]] is the mode of relation proper to the [[self|deep self]] — states grow into one another, each colored by the whole. Association is the **spatialized appearance** of interpenetration — states laid out side by side and reconnected by external links. Associationism is the philosophical mistake of taking the appearance for [[reality|the real]]ity.

### Association and Memory

[[Memory]] in Bergson is not the association of present [[idea|ideas]] with past traces. It is the **survival of the past** in the present — the past permeates the present, giving it its [[quality|qualitative]] char[[action|act]]er. Associationism treats memory as retrieval; Bergson treats it as organic continuity.

### Association and Habit

[[Habit]] is what makes association seem [[reality|real]]. When a pattern of response becomes habitual, it crystallizes into a quasi-mechanical link that looks like an association. But habit is the **fossilization** of organic [[becoming|process]], not the fundamental law of mental life.
