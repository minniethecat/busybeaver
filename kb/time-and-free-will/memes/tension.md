# Tension

Tension (*la tension*), in Bergson's *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]*, is a concept that spans the bodily and the psychic without reducing either to the other. A muscular tension is **felt** as a [[quality|qualitative]] state, not [[rhythm|measure]]d as a quantity; a "tension of soul" is a dynamic concentration of the [[self|whole self]], not a force that could be plotted on a graph. Tension is Bergson's preferred term for describing the **[[intensity|qualitative intensity]]** of conscious life — how consciousness changes not in magnitude but in its mode of organization.

## Bergson's Argument

### Muscular Tension as Qualitative State

Bergson opens his analysis of tension with the [[matter|body]]:

> [001-076] …he very distinct sensation of tension and contraction of the scalp…

The "very distinct [[sensation]] of tension and contr[[action|act]]ion" is not a [[rhythm|measure]]ment but a **felt quality**. When we say a muscle is tense, we are not reporting a quantity of contr[[action|act]]ion; we are describing a [[quality|qualitative]] state that has its own char[[action|act]]er and nuance. This is Bergson's first move: reclaim tension from the physiologist and return it to conscious experience.

> [001-079] … violent emotions as muscular tension.

Bergson identifies "violent [[feeling|emotion]]s" with "muscular tension" — but this is not a reduction of [[feeling|emotion]] to physiology. It is an insistence that [[feeling|emotion]] is **lived** in the [[matter|body]] as tension. The psychophysicist separates the [[feeling|emotion]] (psychic) from the tension (physical); Bergson insists they are **one organic event** experienced under two aspects.

### Psychic Tension and Effort

> [001-078] … still conscious of a growing tension of soul, of an immaterial effort…

The "growing tension of soul" is "an immaterial effort" — something we are "still conscious of" even when we abstr[[action|act]] from the [[matter|body]]. Bergson does not deny that we can distinguish psychic tension from muscular tension; he denies that they are **separate [[reality|real]]ities**. The "tension of soul" and the muscular tension are two poles of a single experience, not cause and effect.

> [001-080] … called the effort of psychic tension: acute desire, uncontrolled anger…

Bergson enumerates the varieties of "psychic tension": acute desire, uncontrolled anger, [[desire|passion]]ate love, violent hatred. Each is a distinct **quality** of the [[ego|self]]'s organization, not a different quantity of the same force. "Psychic tension" is Bergson's term for the intensive dimension of consciousness — how consciousness varies not in amount but in its mode of being.

### The Undivided Tension of the Spring

> [003-433] …se, the dynamic and undivided tension of the spring. Thus, by a real…

The culminating image of *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]*: the free [[ego|self]] is like "the dynamic and undivided tension of the spring." The spring's tension is not in one part of the spring; it is **undivided** — the whole spring is tensed. Similarly, the free [[action|act]] is not the result of one motive overpowering others; it is the [[self|whole self]] [[action|act]]ing, its entire history coiled into the [[action|act]]. The tension is "dynamic" — it is not a static state but a **tendency toward [[action|act]]ion**.

> [001-260] …e_109); spreads out undivided tension of spring, [228](221710457380…

The "undivided tension of spring" spreads out — it unfolds in time. This is the temporal char[[action|act]]er of tension: it is not a state but a **[[becoming|process]]**, a coiling that tends toward uncoiling, a concentration that tends toward expression. [[Freedom]] is this tendency [[action|act]]ualized.

### Tension of the Vocal Chords

> [002-038] …o notice that the greater the tension of the vocal chords in the change…

Bergson uses the "tension of the vocal chords" to illustrate how effort is felt as a [[change|qualitative change]], not a [[quantity|quantitative]] one. The singer who increases pitch feels a **[[change|qualitative change]]** in the tension of the vocal chords — not a measurable increment but a different mode of vocal organization. Psychophysics mistakes this [[change|qualitative change]] for a [[quantity|quantitative]] increase, transforming the singer's felt experience into a number on a scale.

| Form of Tension | Domain | Char[[action|act]]er | Misinterpretation |
|----------------|--------|-----------|------------------|
| Muscular tension | [[body|Body]] | Felt as [[quality|qualitative]] state | Measured as quantity (psychophysics) |
| Psychic tension | [[soul|Soul]]/consciousness | [[dynamic|Dynamic]] concentration of the [[ego|self]] | Reduced to physiology |
| Tension of the spring | Freedom | Undivided tendency of the [[self|whole self]] | Analyzed into separate motives |
| Vocal tension | [[effort|Effort]] | Qualitative change in mode of [[action|act]]ion | Treated as [[quantity|quantitative]] increase |

### Tension and Attention

> [001-110] Attention, and muscular tension…

Bergson links [[attention]] to muscular tension: to attend is to **tense** the [[body|organism]] toward an object. But this tension is not a measurable force; it is a [[quality|qualitative]] orientation of the [[self|whole self]]. The [[association|associationist psychology]] that treats [[attention]] as a [[choice|selection]] among pre-existing [[idea|ideas]] misses the organic, tensional char[[action|act]]er of attending — the way [[attention]] organizes the whole field of consciousness, not just picks out one item.

### Tension and Extension

Bergson frequently contrasts tension with **[[extensity|extension]]**:

> [001-158] …tml#Page_154); illegitimate extension of…

> [001-314] …des of thought and modes of extension…

> [003-107] … and even if we assume that extension is nothing but a relation…

[[extension|Extension]] belongs to [[space]] — the domain of mutually external parts. Tension belongs to [[duration]] — the domain of interpenetrating qualities. The spatializing [[intelligence|intellect]] transforms tension into [[extensity|extension]], the undivided into the divisible, the [[quality|qualitative]] into the [[quantity|quantitative]]. Philosophy's task is the reverse: to recover tension from [[extensity|extension]].

## Key Distinctions

### Tension vs. Intensity

[[Intensity]] is often [[mind|thought]] of as a **magnitude** — a "how much" of [[sensation]]. Tension is a **quality** — a "how" of organization. Bergson's analysis of intensity in Chapter I shows that what we call "intensity" is [[action|act]]ually tension: a [[quality|qualitative]] mode of conscious organization, not a [[magnitude|measurable quantity]]. The "intensity" of a [[sensation]] is the tension it introduces into the [[organic|organic whole]] of consciousness.

### Tension vs. Effort

[[Effort]] is tension **felt as [[action|act]]ion** — the [[quality|qualitative]] experience of the [[ego|self]] mobilizing it[[ego|self]]. When Bergson speaks of "the effort of psychic tension" at [001-080], he is describing how tension is experienced not as a passive state but as an [[action|act]]ive mobilization. [[effort|Effort]] is tension in the mode of doing.

### Tension and the Will

The [[will]] is the [[ego|self]] as a dynamic tension tending toward expression. Bergson's spring metaphor captures this: the spring is not choosing among [[tendency|direction]]s; it is **coiled**, and its uncoiling is the [[action|act]]. The will is the tension of the [[self|whole self]] tending toward [[action|act]]ualization.

### Tension and Duration

In [[duration]], tension is the **[[quality|qualitative]] register** of temporal depth. A moment of high tension is one in which the whole past is gathered and concentrated; a moment of low tension is one in which the past is diffuse. The variations of tension across conscious life are what give duration its texture — its rhythms, its intensities, its dramatic arc.
