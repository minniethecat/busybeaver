## Overview

William James (1842–1910) is the contemporary figure with whom Bergson has the deepest [[intelligence|intellect]]ual affinity in *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]*. Their relationship extended beyond the text—James was among the first to introduce Bergson's philosophy to the English-speaking world, publishing "The Philosophy of Bergson" in the *Hibbert Journal* (1909) and *A Pluralistic Universe* (1909), both listed in Bergson's bibliography [004-029]. Within the argument of *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]*, James's experimental investigations into the [[emotion|feeling]] of effort provide Bergson with a decisive empirical counterexample to the psychophysical axiom that conscious effort is the direct perception of outgoing nervous energy. Bergson embraces James's findings while extending them into a more radical philosophical conclusion.

## Key Ideas

### The Centripetal Theory of Effort

James's central contribution to Bergson's argument is his experimental demonstration that the [[emotion|feeling]] of effort is centripetal (incoming, sensory) rather than centrifugal (outgoing, motor). The traditional view, held by [[helmholtz|Helmholtz]] and [[wundt|Wundt]] among others, was that when we will a [[motion|movement]], we are directly conscious of the nervous impulse being discharged from the brain to the muscles. James's experiments with ocular paralysis showed otherwise:

> "But, replies Professor James, no account has been taken of what goes on in the other eye. This remains covered during the experiments; nevertheless it moves and there is not much trouble in convincing oneself that the movement of the other eye is what produces the sensation of effort together with the impression that the objects perceived by the right eye are moving." [001-063]

### Effort as Peripheral Sensation

James's findings led him to conclude that what we call the [[emotion|feeling]] of effort is [[action|act]]ually the complex of sensory feedback from muscles, tendons, and joints already in motion—not the initiation of that motion. Bergson quotes this view with approval:

> "These and similar observations lead Professor James to assert that the feeling of effort is centripetal and not centrifugal. We are not conscious of a force which we are supposed to launch upon our organism: our feeling of muscular energy at work 'is a complex afferent sensation, which comes from contracted muscles, stretched ligaments, compressed joints, etc.'" [001-064]

## Bergson's Engagement

### Extending James's Insight

Bergson builds explicitly on James's findings while pushing beyond them. Where James demonstrated that the [[emotion|feeling]] of effort is sensory rather than motor, Bergson asks a further question: in what does our *perception of the intensity* of effort consist?

> "It is sufficient to observe oneself attentively to reach a conclusion on this point which Professor James has not formulated, but which seems to us quite in accord with the spirit of his teaching." [001-067]

Bergson's [[extensity|extension]] of James's insight is that the perceived intensity of effort corresponds not to a quantity of force discharged but to the *extent of the [[body|organism]] involved*—the number and spread of muscular [[sensation]]s recruited into consciousness. This transforms James's empirical finding into a philosophical principle: [[intensity|psychic intensity]] is [[quality|qualitative]] [[extensity|extension]], not [[quantity|quantitative]] magnitude.

### The Qualitative Nature of Muscular Sensation

Bergson's use of James goes beyond the specific debate about effort. It exemplifies Bergson's broader method: showing that phenomena which appear to admit of [[quantity|quantitative]] [[rhythm|measure]]ment (how much effort? how intense?) are in f[[action|act]] [[quality|qualitative]] through and through. James's centripetal theory strips away the illusion that we have direct introspective access to a quantity of nervous energy; what we [[action|act]]ually experience is a quality of bodily [[sensation]] whose "intensity" names not a [[quantity|measurable magnitude]] but a felt degree of organic involvement.

### Intellectual Kinship and Divergence

Bergson's relationship with James is marked by deep respect and productive difference. James's pragmatism and radical empiricism share with Bergson's philosophy a commitment to the primacy of lived experience over abstr[[action|act]] conceptualization. Bergson's bibliography lists three works by James [004-029], and James's early advocacy was instrumental in establishing Bergson's international reputation. Yet the two thinkers diverged on the ultimate implications of their shared starting point—James remaining closer to empirical psychology, Bergson moving toward a thoroughgoing metaphysics of duration.
