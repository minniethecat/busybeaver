# Dream

## Overview / Definition

In *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]*, Bergson invokes the dream state as a privileged illustration of how consciousness operates when freed from the demands of pr[[action|act]]ical life. The dream is not treated as a separate phenomenon to be explained, but as evidence for the [[quality|qualitative]] nature of duration — a state in which "we no longer [[rhythm|measure]] duration, but we feel it; from quantity it returns to the state of quality" [003-175]. The dreamer, cut off from external objects, experiences the interpenetration of images and [[idea|ideas]] that char[[action|act]]erizes consciousness at its deepest level.

## Bergson's Argument

Bergson's treatment of dreams serves three interconnected purposes in his broader argument:

**Dreams as access to deep consciousness.** Bergson observes that "the further we penetrate into the depths of consciousness, the less right we have to treat psychic phenomena as objects placed side by side" [001-028]. Dreams provide a natural experiment in which the superficial, spatializing layer of consciousness is suspended. What remains is the interpenetration of states — "we experience something of the kind in certain dreams, in which we do not imagine anything out of the ordinary, and yet through which there resounds an indescribable note of originality" [001-028].

**Dreams and the return from quantity to quality.** The most explicit treatment appears in Chapter 3, where Bergson identifies sleep as the condition that alters "the communicating surface between the [[self|ego]] and external objects" [003-175]. In waking life, the [[self|ego]] maintains a "balance-wheel" of outer psychic states oriented toward [[action|act]]ion in space. Sleep relaxes this [[mechanical|mechanism]]: "Here we no longer [[rhythm|measure]] duration, but we feel it; from quantity it returns to the state of quality; we no longer estimate the time that has passed, except by [[emotion|feeling]] it as a quality" [003-175].

**Dreams as a model for conceptual interweaving.** Bergson uses the strangeness of dream imagery to illuminate the nature of conceptual [[mind|thought]] it[[ego|self]]. "The strangest dreams, in which two images overlie one another and show us at the same time two different persons, who yet make only one, will hardly give us an idea of the interweaving of concepts which goes on when we are awake" [003-196]. The dreamer's imagination "imitates with mere images, and parodies in its own way, the [[becoming|process]] which constantly goes on with regard to [[idea|ideas]] in the deeper regions of the [[intelligence|intellect]]ual life" [003-196]. This is a striking inversion: dreams do not distort waking [[mind|thought]]; rather, waking [[mind|thought]] is it[[ego|self]] a more sophisticated version of the blending and interpenetration visible in dreams.

## Key Distinctions

| Aspect | Waking (Spatialized) State | Dream State |
|--------|---------------------------|-------------|
| Relation to external objects | Active communication, pr[[action|act]]ical orientation | Relaxed cont[[action|act]], "balance-wheel" removed |
| Experience of time | Measured, [[quantity|quantitative]] | Felt, [[quality|qualitative]] |
| Organization of states | Juxtaposed in space | Interpenetrating, blending |
| [[self|Self]]-awareness | Reflective, [[ego|self]]-possessed | [[self|Self]]-forgetful, immersed |

## Aesthetic Dimension

Bergson also draws on the dream as a metaphor for [[aesthetic|aesthetic experience]]. The rhythm of poetry "lulls the soul into [[ego|self]]-forgetfulness, and, as in a dream, thinks and sees with the poet" [001-044]. Art, like dreaming, suspends the pr[[action|act]]ical orientation of consciousness and allows a more direct cont[[action|act]] with the [[quality|qualitative]] flow of experience. Similarly, Bergson notes that "we find more charm in hope than in possession, in dreams than in [[reality|real]]ity" [001-030] — a remark that connects the dream state to the affective richness of possibility over [[action|act]]uality, anticipating his broader argument about [[free-will|freedom]] and the open future.
