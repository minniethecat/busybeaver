# Consciousness (conscience)

Consciousness (*conscience*) in Bergson's philosophy is not a theater where discrete mental states appear and pass away. It is the **continuous, [[quality|qualitative]] field** where states [[interpenetration|interpenetrate]] — each moment colored by the whole of the past and anticipating the future.

For Bergson, consciousness *is* [[duration]]: to be conscious is to endure, and to endure is to be conscious. This identification is the pivot of his arguments against determinism and for [[free-will]].

## Definition

In Bergson's usage, consciousness has two fundamental aspects:

| Aspect | Immediate Consciousness | Reflective Consciousness |
|--------|------------------------|--------------------------|
| Mode | Direct, lived, [[quality|qualitative]] | [[symbol|Symbol]]ic, spatialized, [[quantity|quantitative]] |
| Relation to [[ego|self]] | Immersed in duration | Observes states as objects |
| States | Interpenetrate, form a whole | Separated, placed side by side |
| Freedom | Free [[action|act]]s possible | [[determinism|Determinism]] appears necessary |
| Grasped by | [[intuition|Intuition]] | [[analysis|Analysis]] / language |

> (001-199) …reflective consciousness has two fundamental…

The reflective consciousness is necessary for pr[[action|act]]ical life and science, but it distorts [[reality|the real]]ity of immediate experience by projecting it onto [[extensity]] ([[extension|spatial extension]]).

Bergson's method is to recover the data of immediate consciousness beneath the spatialized overlay.

## Bergson's Argument

### Consciousness as Duration

Bergson's central claim is that consciousness is duration — not a succession of separate states but an **[[organic|organic whole]]** where past, present, and future interpenetrate:

> (001-002) …states of consciousness, sensations, feelings, passions…

These states are not discrete atoms; they form a continuous, flowing multiplicity. What we call a "[[choice|decision]]" or a "motive" is an abstr[[action|act]]ion from this flowing whole.

### The Surface and the Depths

Bergson distinguishes between the surface of consciousness — states connected to [[perception|external perception]] and bodily [[action|act]]ion — and its depths, where states [[interpenetration|interpenetrate]] without spatial separation:

> (001-028) …penetrate into the depths of consciousness, the less right we have to treat…

> (001-024) …take place on the surface of consciousness, and which are always connected with external perception or with bodily states…

The surface of consciousness is where the spatializing habit operates: states appear separate because they are tied to distinct bodily locations or external objects. The depths are where the true nature of consciousness — **[[interpenetration|qualitative multiplicity]]** — is revealed.

### Consciousness and Free Will

Bergson's argument for [[will|free will]] turns on this distinction. [[determinism|Determinism]] operates on the surface of consciousness — where states can be isolated, labeled, and treated as causes. But the free [[action|act]] emerges from the depths, where the [[self|whole self]] is expressed:

> (001-044) …goes back without ceasing to the consciousness of our personality…

The free [[action|act]] is not uncaused; it is caused by the **whole of the [[ego|self]]** — the entire accumulated duration of one's conscious history. This totality cannot be reduced to any finite set of antecedent causes.

### The Psychophysical Error

Bergson critiques the psychophysical attempt to [[rhythm|measure]] consciousness. Psychophysicists like [[Fechner]] and [[Wundt]] treat conscious states as if they corresponded one-to-one with physical stimuli:

> (001-060) …nervous force which consciousness perceives. Wundt also speaks…

> (001-017) …the sensation which is given in consciousness, and not the movement.

But consciousness is not a measuring instrument. The [[quality|qualitative]] char[[action|act]]er of a [[sensation]] — its [[intensity]], its place in the flowing whole — cannot be captured by correlating it with an external stimulus.

## Key Distinctions

### Immediate vs. Reflective Consciousness

Immediate consciousness is **lived experience** — duration as it is [[action|act]]ually felt. Reflective consciousness is the [[intelligence|intellect]]'s attempt to grasp this experience by spatializing it, breaking it into parts, and representing it symbolically. Philosophy's task is to recover the former beneath the latter.

### Consciousness vs. the Brain

Bergson denies that consciousness is an "epiphenomenon" of brain [[becoming|process]]es:

> (001-160) Epiphenomenon, consciousness as…

Consciousness has its own [[reality|real]]ity and temporality that cannot be explained by or reduced to neural events. The brain is the organ of [[attention]] to life, not the generator of consciousness.

### Consciousness vs. Space

While [[Kant]] treated space and time as forms of [[intuition]], Bergson argues that space is the form of **external** perception, while duration is the form of **consciousness**. Consciousness, properly understood, is non-spatial:

> (001-081) …the organism, a disturbance which consciousness has no difficulty in measuring…

The measuring that consciousness performs is [[quality|qualitative]], not [[quantity|quantitative]] — it feels changes as transformations of the whole, not as additions of parts.

## Historical Context

Bergson's [[idea|conception]] of consciousness engages with several traditions:

- **[[kant|Kant]]ianism**: [[kant|Kant]] distinguished between phenomena (objects as they appear) and noumena (things in themselves). Bergson reinterprets this: the immediate data of consciousness are not merely phenomena but the direct grasp of [[reality|real]]ity as duration.

- **Psychophysics**: [[Fechner]] and [[Weber]] sought mathematical laws relating stimulus to [[sensation]]. Bergson argues that the "[[sensation]]" they [[rhythm|measure]] is already spatialized — an abstr[[action|act]]ion from living consciousness.

- **[[association|Association]]ism**: [[Spencer]] and [[Mill]] treated consciousness as a chain of associated [[idea|ideas]]. Bergson argues that association is a surface phenomenon; in the depths, states [[interpenetration|interpenetrate]] and form an indivisible whole.

- **[[wundt|Wundt]]ian Psychology**: [[Wundt]] attempted to [[rhythm|measure]] the "speed of [[mind|thought]]" and the [[attention|effort of attention]]. Bergson shows that these [[rhythm|measure]]ments track bodily accompaniments, not consciousness it[[ego|self]].
