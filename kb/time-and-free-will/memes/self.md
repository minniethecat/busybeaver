# Self

The [[ego|self]], in Bergson's *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]*, is not a single entity but a **two-level structure**. The **superficial self** lives in [[space]] and language — it projects [[consciousness|conscious states]] onto a [[homogeneity|homogeneous medium]], separates them into distinct items, and names them. The **deep self** lives in [[duration]] — its states interpenetrate, form an [[organic|organic whole]], and resist analysis into parts. The free [[action|act]] is nothing other than the expression of the whole deep self.

## Bergson's Argument

### The Two Selves

> [001-024] …rightly or wrongly, to be self-sufficient, such as deep joy…

Bergson distinguishes between two levels of [[ego|self]]hood. The superficial self is the [[ego|self]] of **social life** — the [[ego|self]] that uses [[language|words]], makes distinctions, and treats its own states as objects. The deep self is the [[ego|self]] of **inner life** — the [[ego|self]] whose states melt into one another, forming a continuously changing but indivisible whole.

> [001-019] …mathematical relation, precise in itself though confusedly perceived…

The deep self is not a mystery inaccessible to reflection. It is the [[ego|self]] we [[action|act]]ually live, moment by moment, before language and spatial [[mind|thought]] dissect it into discrete [[emotion|feeling]]s, [[idea|ideas]], and motives. The difficulty is not in being the deep self but in **describing** it without spatializing it.

### The Deep Self and Freedom

Freedom, for Bergson, is not a property that the [[ego|self]] possesses or lacks. It is the **mode of being** of the deep self. When an [[action|act]]ion flows from the whole deep self — from the entirety of one's lived duration — it is free. When an [[action|act]]ion is determined by an isolated motive, habit, or external pressure, it is the superficial self [[action|act]]ing:

| Self Level | States | Mode of [[action|Action]] | Freedom |
|-----------|--------|---------------|---------|
| Deep [[ego|self]] | Interpenetrating, [[quality|qualitative]] | Expression of the whole | **Free** |
| Superficial [[ego|self]] | Separate, named, spatialized | Determination by part | Determined |

### Why the Two Selves Are Often Confused

[[language|Language]] is the great obstacle to knowing the deep self. Words are general, fixed, and discrete — they name states as if they were separate objects. When we say "I am angry," we convert a living, changing quality into a **fixed thing** with a name. Over time, the superficial self — the [[ego|self]] of language — comes to seem more [[reality|real]] than the deep self.

## Key Distinctions

### Deep Self vs. Superficial Self

The deep self is [[quality|qualitative]], durational, and free. The superficial self is [[quantity|quantitative]], spatial, and determined. But they are not two separate entities — they are two **aspects** of one [[ego|self]], two ways in which the same person can be known. Social life requires the superficial self; [[free-will|freedom]] requires the deep self.

### Self vs. Ego

The [[ego]], as discussed in psychophysics and [[association|associationist psychology]], is the **superficial self** — the [[ego|self]] as a collection of states, [[emotion|feeling]]s, and [[idea|ideas]] that can be counted and causally linked. Bergson does not deny the existence of this [[ego|self]]; he denies that it is the **whole** [[ego|self]].

### Self and Personality

The deep self is not a static substance — it is a **becoming**. [[personality|Personality]] is not a fixed char[[action|act]]er that determines [[action|act]]ions; it is the continuously evolving whole of one's duration. The free [[action|act]] both expresses this personality and changes it.

## Historical Context

Bergson's two-[[ego|self]] model responds to **[[kant|Kant]]'s** distinction between the empirical and noumenal [[ego|self]], but Bergson rejects [[kant|Kant]]'s conclusion that the noumenal [[ego|self]] is unknowable. For Bergson, the deep self is directly **lived** — the problem is not knowledge but expression. His view also engages with **[[will|Will]]iam [[james|James]]'s** distinction between the "I" ([[ego|self]]-as-knower) and the "me" ([[ego|self]]-as-known), and anticipates later phenomenological accounts of the lived [[matter|body]] and pre-reflective [[ego|self]]-awareness.
