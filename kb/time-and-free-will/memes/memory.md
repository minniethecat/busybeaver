# Memory

Memory, in Bergson's *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]*, is not a faculty for storing and retrieving representations but the very **mode of being** of conscious life. In [[duration]], the past does not disappear; it survives, permeating the present and giving each moment its unique [[quality|qualitative]] char[[action|act]]er. Bergson's account of memory — developed more fully in *[[matter|Matter]] and Memory* (1896) — is already present in germ in his early analysis of how [[consciousness|conscious states]] interpenetrate.

## Bergson's Argument

### Memory as the Survival of the Past

Bergson's central claim is that in consciousness, nothing is ever truly lost. The past survives, not as a separate representation stored in a mental archive, but as a **living presence** that colors every new experience:

> [003-146] …organized them as a whole in memory…

When we recall something, we do not retrieve a fixed image from sto[[anger|rage]]. Memory organizes experiences as a **whole**, and each [[action|act]] of recollection is a fresh synthesis in which the present [[ego|self]] meets its accumulated past.

> [003-257] …of childhood come back to my memory…

The sudden return of childhood memories illustrates Bergson's point: the past is not "gone" — it is latent, and when conditions are right, it returns not as a faded copy but as a living quality that transforms the present.

### Memory and the Interpenetration of States

The survival of the past in memory is what makes [[consciousness|conscious states]] interpenetrate. Each moment is colored by everything that preceded it:

> [002-065] …our imagination, aided by our memory, inserts between A and Β…

When we imagine a transition between two [[consciousness|conscious states]], we rely on memory to **fill** the gap. But this filling is not an addition of missing links; it is the recognition that in duration, there are no gaps — the transition is continuous because memory sustains the past in the present.

### Memory and the Illusion of Discrete States

The view of the mind as a series of discrete states — an assumption shared by determinists and libertarians alike — arises when we ignore the role of memory:

> [003-272] …owing to the solidification in memory of such and such sensations…

What we call a "state" of consciousness is [[action|act]]ually a **solidification** produced by memory. [[language|Language]] and pr[[action|act]]ical life require us to isolate moments, to give them names, to treat them as separate. But this solidification is the work of memory operating under the demands of [[action|act]]ion; it is not the original [[reality|real]]ity.

> [003-275] …and carefully scrutinize our memory, we shall see that we had our…

If we carefully scrutinize memory it[[ego|self]], we discover that what we took to be discrete states were [[action|act]]ually **continuous transitions**. The [[ego|self]] does not jump from state to state; it matures, and memory is the medium of this maturation.

### Memory vs. Habit

> [003-268] …education which appeals to the memory rather than to the judgment…

Bergson distinguishes between genuine memory (the survival of the unique [[quality|qualitative]] past) and **habit-memory** (the mechanization of repeated [[action|act]]ions). An education that appeals to memory rather than judgment produces the latter — rote learning — not the living memory that sustains the [[ego|self]] in duration.

> [003-300] …merely gives the stereotyped memory of the process…

What passes for "memory" in deterministic accounts is [[action|act]]ually the **stereotyped memory** of a [[becoming|process]] — the habit-memory that treats past [[action|act]]ions as fixed templates. The [[reality|real]] memory of duration is not a template; it is the continuous creation of the [[quality|qualitative]]ly new.

## Key Distinctions

### Memory vs. Perception

Bergson does not separate memory and [[perception]] as distinct faculties. [[perception|Perception]] is always **memory-laden**; the present moment is always infused with the past. This is what gives each perception its [[quality|qualitative]] uniqueness — it is not a snapshot of the present but a synthesis in which the whole history of the [[ego|self]] is [[action|act]]ive.

### Memory vs. Storage

Bergson's most radical claim is that memory is not sto[[anger|rage]]. The brain does not "contain" memories the way a computer contains files. Memory is the **continuity of duration it[[ego|self]]** — the f[[action|act]] that the past survives in the present without being stored anywhere. This claim is developed more fully in *[[matter|Matter]] and Memory*.

### Memory and the Self

The [[ego|self]], for Bergson, **is** its memory. There is no "[[ego|self]]" above and beyond the accumulated past; the [[ego|self]] is nothing but the continuous interpenetration of all its lived experience. This is why the free [[action|act]] — the [[action|act]] that expresses the [[self|whole self]] — is impossible to predict: the [[ego|self]] at each moment is the unique product of a unique history.

## Historical Context

### The Associationist Theory of Memory

The [[association|associationist psychology]] of the nineteenth century treated memory as the [[association|association of ideas]] — a mechanical [[becoming|process]] by which one idea calls up another through laws of similarity, contiguity, and contrast. Bergson's account is a direct challenge to this mechanistic picture: memory is not a [[mechanical|mechanism]] but the very texture of conscious life.

### Bergson's Later Development

While *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]* contains only scattered references to memory, the concept is central to Bergson's later work. *[[matter|Matter]] and Memory* (1896) develops a full theory of memory as the intersection of [[soul|spirit]] and matter, distinguishing between pure memory (the survival of the past in duration) and habit-memory (the motor [[mechanical|mechanism]]s of the [[matter|body]]).
