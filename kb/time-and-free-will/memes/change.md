# Change

Change, in Bergson's *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]*, is not one thing but two. There is **qualitative change** — the transformation of a conscious state into something genuinely new — and **[[quantity|quantitative]] change** — an increase or decrease along a homogeneous scale. The central thesis of Bergson's first chapter is that what we call an "increase in intensity" is [[action|act]]ually a qualitative change: the [[sensation]] does not get "more" of the same quality; it **becomes a different quality**.

## Bergson's Argument

### The Two Kinds of Change

Bergson opens *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]* by examining what we mean when we say a [[sensation]] becomes "more intense":

> [001-018] …extensive differences between the changes taking place behind them…

Behind what we call [[quantity|quantitative]] changes in [[sensation]], there are extensive differences in the physical stimulus. But the [[sensation]] it[[ego|self]] undergoes qualitative change, not [[quantity|quantitative]] increase.

> [001-027] …surroundings seems now to have changed radically.

When our [[feeling|emotion]]al state changes, our entire perception of the world changes [[quality|qualitative]]ly — not by degrees but in kind. This is the model for [[mind|understanding]] all conscious change.

### Change of Quality vs. Change of Magnitude

> [001-029] But that is a change of quality rather than of magnitude…

Bergson insists repeatedly: what we call an increase in the magnitude of a [[sensation]] is [[action|act]]ually a change in its **quality**. A brighter light is not "more" of the same light-experience; it is a **different** light-experience altogether.

> [001-031] …correspond to qualitative changes in the whole of our psychic…

Every apparent [[quantity|quantitative]] change in consciousness corresponds to a qualitative change in the **whole** of the psychic state. This is because [[consciousness|conscious states]] interpenetrate; to change one aspect is to change the entire quality of the experience.

> [001-035] …which is thus supposed to change in magnitude…

The supposition that a [[sensation]] changes in magnitude is an [[intelligence|intellect]]ual construction. In immediate experience, there is no "magnitude" of [[sensation]] — only [[quality|qualitative]] transformations.

### The Curve of Change

> [001-037] …while a curved line changes its direction at every moment…

Bergson uses the image of a curved line to illustrate [[becoming|continuous change]]. A curve changes its [[tendency|direction]] at every point without ever breaking. Duration is like this: it changes continuously, but each change is [[quality|qualitative]], not [[quantity|quantitative]]. The [[ego|self]] is the curve; its [[tendency|direction]] is its whole [[quality|qualitative]] char[[action|act]]er at each moment.

| Kind of Change | Domain | Nature | Example |
|---------------|--------|--------|---------|
| Quantitative | [[space]] / physical world | More or less of the same | A longer line, a louder sound (physically) |
| Qualitative | [[duration]] / consciousness | Transformation into something new | A deeper sorrow, a brighter joy |

## Key Distinctions

### Change vs. Succession

[[Succession]] is the form change takes in duration. States do not merely succeed one another; each successive state is a [[quality|qualitative]] **transformation** of the preceding one. Change is succession understood [[quality|qualitative]]ly, not as a series of discrete steps but as continuous metamorphosis.

### Change vs. Motion

[[Motion]] is change in space — a change of position measurable by distance and time. Bergson's analysis shows that the **perception** of motion is a qualitative change in consciousness, not a [[quantity|quantitative]] [[rhythm|measure]]ment. The two senses of motion correspond to the two senses of change.

### Change and the Self

The [[ego|self]] is not a thing that undergoes change; the [[ego|self]] **is** change — continuous [[quality|qualitative]] transformation. This is the deepest implication of Bergson's philosophy: there is no unchanging substrate beneath the [[becoming|flux]]; the [[becoming|flux]] it[[ego|self]] is [[reality|the real]]ity. The free [[action|act]] is simply the [[action|act]] that expresses this continuously changing [[self|whole self]].

## Historical Context

### Heraclitus and the Flux

Heraclitus taught that all things are in [[becoming|flux]] (*panta rhei*). Bergson radicalizes this: not only are things in [[becoming|flux]], but the [[becoming|flux]] is **[[quality|qualitative]]** — each moment is genuinely new, not merely a rearrangement of eternal elements. This distinguishes Bergson's philosophy of change from both ancient and modern versions of the [[becoming|flux]] doctrine.

### The Evolutionary Context

Bergson wrote in the wake of [[darwin|Darwin]], who had made change — specifically, the transformation of species over time — a central theme of nineteenth-century [[mind|thought]]. Bergson extends the evolutionary insight from biology to metaphysics: not only species but **consciousness it[[ego|self]]** is essentially change.
