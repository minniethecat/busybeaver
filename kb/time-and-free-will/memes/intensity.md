# Intensity (intensité)

Intensity (*intensité*) is the central concept of Chapter I of *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]*, where Bergson argues that the intensity of [[consciousness|conscious states]] — [[sensation]]s, [[feeling|emotion]]s, efforts — is fundamentally **[[quality|qualitative]]** rather than [[quantity|quantitative]].

What we call an "increase in intensity" is, upon analysis, not the addition of more of the same, but a **change in quality** that pervades and transforms the whole of [[consciousness]]. This argument is the foundation for Bergson's later claims about [[duration]] and [[free-will]].

## Definition

Bergson defines intensity in opposition to [[extension|extensive magnitude]]. While an [[extension|extensive magnitude]] (like length) can be divided into homogeneous parts and [[rhythm|measure]]d, an intensive state is a **simple, indivisible quality**:

| Aspect | Extensive [[magnitude|Magnitude]] | Intensive State |
|--------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Nature | Quantitative, divisible | Qualitative, indivisible |
| Measurement | Objective, numerical | Subjective, comparative |
| [[change|Change]] | Addition of parts | Qualitative transformation |
| Locus | [[space|Space]] (external) | Consciousness (internal) |
| Examples | Length, area, volume | Joy, sorrow, effort, [[feeling|aesthetic feeling]] |

> (001-009) …s back to the inquiry, why an intensity can be assimilated to a magnitude…

The key error Bergson identifies is the tendency to treat intensity *as if* it were a magnitude — a con[[interpenetration|fusion]] rooted in the spatialization of consciousness.

## Bergson's Argument

Bergson's argument proceeds through a detailed examination of different kinds of [[consciousness|conscious states]]. He shows, case by case, that what appears to be a [[quantity|quantitative]] increase in intensity is [[action|act]]ually a **[[quality|qualitative]] progress**:

### Deep-seated Feelings

For [[feeling|emotion]]s like joy, sorrow, or [[moral|pity]], Bergson argues that increasing intensity is not the addition of more of the same [[emotion|feeling]], but the **progressive reorganization** of the entire psychic field:

> (001-047) …to us to admit of degrees of intensity, and also of degrees of elevation…

> (001-051) …Pity. Its increasing intensity is a qualitative progress.

A deepening joy is not the same joy amplified — it is a different state, permeated by new elements, anticipations, and memories. The [[change|qualitative change]] of the whole is what we misdescribe as a [[quantity|quantitative]] "more."

### Muscular Effort

Bergson extends the analysis to [[effort|muscular effort]], where the [[quantity|quantitative]] illusion seems strongest. When we exert "more" effort, our consciousness registers a greater number of muscles involved and a wider surface of the [[matter|body]] affected:

> (001-074) We are thus led to define the intensity of a superficial effort in th…

> (001-072) …our consciousness of an increase of muscular effort is nothing but the perception of a larger surface of the body…

What we call "greater intensity" of effort is [[action|act]]ually the **spread of [[sensation]] across a larger bodily surface** — a change in extensity, not a pure increase of intensity.

### Sensations

Even simple [[sensation]]s resist quantification. The intensity of a sound or light is judged not by any internal [[rhythm|measure]] but by the extent of associated bodily effects:

> (001-019) …that we assert the higher intensity of a particular sound? Without…

> (001-025) Pure intensity ought to be more easily defin…

The very attempt to define "pure intensity" reveals that we can only point to [[quality|qualitative difference]]s — one [[sensation]] differs *in kind* from another, not merely in degree.

## Key Distinctions

### Intensity vs. Extensity

The most critical distinction in Bergson's analysis. [[extensity|Extensity]] is the property of occupying space — being spread out, divisible, measurable. Intensity, by contrast, is the quality of a conscious state as lived:

> (001-057) …appears to spread outwards, as if intensity were being developed into extensity…

When an intense state "spreads" to involve the [[matter|body]], intensity appears to transform into extensity — but this is a symptom of the spatializing habit of consciousness, not a [[reality|real]] equivalence.

### Intensity vs. Magnitude

Bergson argues that philosophers have illegitimately treated intensity as a magnitude — that is, as something that can be greater or less than another of the same kind. This is the "psychophysical" error:

> (001-012) …setting up a pure intensity as a magnitude, just as if it were extended…

[[magnitude|Magnitude]]s belong to space; intensities belong to consciousness. Confusing them is the root error of [[psychophysics]].

### Intensity vs. Quantity

The distinction between intensity and quantity parallels Bergson's broader distinction between [[quality]] and quantity. A quantity is homogeneous and additive; an intensity is heterogeneous and transformative — it involves the [[interpenetration]] of states rather than their juxtaposition.

## Historical Context

Bergson's analysis of intensity engages critically with **psychophysics** — the 19th-century scientific [[motion|movement]] led by [[Fechner]] and [[Weber]] that attempted to [[rhythm|measure]] the relationship between physical stimuli and psychological [[sensation]]s. Bergson argues that psychophysics assumes precisely what it needs to prove: that [[consciousness|conscious states]] can be [[rhythm|measure]]d [[quantity|quantitative]]ly.

Bergson also engages with [[Kant]], who distinguished between intensive and [[extension|extensive magnitude]] in the *Critique of Pure [[reason|Reason]]*. While [[kant|Kant]] treated intensity as a magnitude (albeit one grasped in an instant), Bergson denies that intensity is a magnitude at all.

The concept of intensity also connects to the **[[association|associationist psychology]]** of [[Spencer]] and [[Mill]], which Bergson critiques for treating [[consciousness|conscious states]] as separable atoms that can be combined.
