# Habit

Habit, in Bergson's *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]*, is not merely a psychological phenomenon but a **philosophical obstacle**. It is the deeply ingrained pr[[action|act]]ice of setting up distinctions in [[space]] and then projecting them onto [[duration]]. Habit is the [[mechanical|mechanism]] by which the spatializing [[tendency]] of the mind becomes **sedimented** into common sense, language, and philosophical doctrine. To grasp [[duration|real duration]], we must first unlearn the habits of spatial [[mind|thought]].

## Bergson's Argument

### Convention, Habit, and Immediate Experience

Bergson opens the Preface by opposing habit and convention to genuine philosophical [[intuition]]:

> [P01-007] …does away with convention and habit and gets back to its natural …

The philosopher's task is to do away with "convention and habit" and return to the "natural" — that is, to immediate conscious experience before it has been reshaped by spatial habits. This is not a rejection of all habitual [[action|act]]ion but a methodological suspension: we must set aside the habits of [[mind|thought]] that prevent us from seeing [[duration]] as it is.

### The Habit of Counting

> [003-078] …on this point seems to be the habit we have fallen into of counting…

The "habit of [[number|counting]]" is one of Bergson's central examples. We have become so accustomed to treating everything as numerable — [[sensation]]s, [[feeling|emotion]]s, even time it[[ego|self]] — that we forget [[number|counting]] is a spatial operation. To count, items must be **juxtaposed in space**; in [[duration|pure duration]], there is no juxtaposition and therefore nothing to count. The habit of [[number|counting]] is the habit of substituting [[space]] for [[duration]].

### The Habit of Spatial Distinctions

> [003-146] …one another, we get into the habit of setting up the same distinction…

Bergson identifies a pattern: whenever we encounter a [[quality|qualitative difference]] in consciousness, "we get into the habit of setting up the same distinction" that we would make in space — as if [[consciousness|conscious states]] were **mutually external objects**. This habit transforms [[quality|qualitative difference]]s into [[quantity|quantitative]] ones, generating the con[[interpenetration|fusion]]s that dominate [[psychophysics]] and [[association|associationist psychology]].

> [003-168] …betrayed the deeply ingrained habit of setting out time in space…

The habit of "setting out time in space" is so deeply ingrained that even philosophers who explicitly reject it fall back into it. Bergson traces this habit to the demands of pr[[action|act]]ical life and social communication — we need [[time|spatialized time]] to coordinate [[action|act]]ion, but this pr[[action|act]]ical necessity becomes a theoretical obstacle.

### Habit and the Association of Images

> [003-381] …es it, the reason is that the habit of associating the two images…

The [[association]] of images — the core [[mechanical|mechanism]] of empiricist psychology — is it[[ego|self]] a product of habit. We habitually associate two images because we habitually place them side by side in an imaginary space. The association is not a natural operation of the mind but an artif[[action|act]] of spatial habit.

> [003-390] …nsequence. Unfortunately, the habit has grown up of taking the process…

The "habit of taking the [[becoming|process]]" for the thing it[[ego|self]] — the spatial representation for the temporal [[reality|real]]ity — is Bergson's diagnosis of what goes wrong in philosophical psychology. The habit "has grown up" historically; it is not innate but acquired, and what is acquired can be unlearned.

### The Amalgamating Habit

> [003-391] …termination, has got into the habit, so to speak, of amalgamating…

Bergson uses "amalgamating" to describe how habit fuses distinct operations into an apparently simple one. The mind "has got into the habit" of blending duration and space, quality and quantity, until the blend seems natural and the distinction invisible. Philosophical [[reflection]] must undo this amalgamation.

## Key Distinctions

### Habit vs. Tendency

[[Tendency]] is a **constitutional [[tendency|direction]]** of consciousness — it belongs to the nature of mind. Habit is an **acquired pr[[action|act]]ice** — it can be suspended or unlearned. The spatializing tendency is constitutional; the specific habits of [[number|counting]], distinguishing, and associating are the forms that tendency takes in daily life and philosophical pr[[action|act]]ice.

### Habit vs. Duration

Habit is the **antithesis of duration**. In duration, each moment is novel, carrying the whole past forward into an unforeseeable future. Habit, by contrast, is repetition: the same spatial operation applied again and again until it seems natural. Bergson's philosophy of [[free-will|freedom]] requires breaking the hold of habit on [[mind|thought]].

### Habit and Language

> [003-253] This erroneous tendency aided by language.

[[Language]] reinforces spatial habits by providing ready-made [[language|words]] that carve continuous experience into discrete units. Every word is a habit of [[mind|thought]] solidified. Bergson's stylistic [[effort|struggle]] — his straining against ordinary language — reflects his recognition that language it[[ego|self]] is a vehicle of spatial habit.

## Historical Context

Bergson's critique of habit has roots in **Maine de Biran**'s French [[soul|spirit]]ualist tradition, which treated *l'habitude* as a fundamental cat[[self|ego]]ry of mental life. But where Biran saw habit as a positive force that organizes experience, Bergson sees it as an **epistemological obstacle** — something that must be overcome for genuine philosophical knowledge. This inversion anticipates themes in later phenomenology (Heidegger's critique of "the They," Merleau-Ponty's recovery of pre-objective experience) and in pragmatism (Dewey's insistence that habits are instruments, not foundations).
