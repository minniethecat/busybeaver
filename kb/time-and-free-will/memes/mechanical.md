# Mechanical

The mechanical (*le mécanique*), in Bergson's *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]*, is more than a scientific theory — it is a **mode of [[mind|thought]]** that reduces all relations to external determination, all [[becoming|process]]es to spatial rearrangement, and all qualities to measurable quantities. The mechanical is the natural language of the reflective [[consciousness]]: it operates by breaking wholes into parts, translating succession into juxtaposition, and substituting **static symbols** for [[dynamic]] [[becoming|process]]es. Bergson's critique of mechanism is not a rejection of science but a demonstration of its **limits** — mechanism is valid for matter in [[space]] but systematically falsifies [[reality|the real]]ity of [[duration]] and [[free-will]].

## Bergson's Argument

### Mechanical Theories and Conscious Work

Bergson distinguishes mechanical work from conscious effort:

> [001-018] We know that mechanical, and especially kinetic, theories have aimed at explaining the visible and tangible properties of bodies by well-defined movements of their ultimate parts…

Mechanical theories explain the "visible and tangible properties of bodies" through "well-defined [[motion|movement]]s of their ultimate parts" — they reduce quality to motion, and motion to mathematical description. This method is perfectly legitimate for material bodies. But when applied to consciousness, it produces not explanation but **substitution** — the mechanical symbol replaces the lived [[reality|real]]ity.

> [001-019] …in consciousness, and not this mechanical work.

Bergson insists that what matters in conscious life is not "mechanical work" — the measurable output — but the [[intensity|qualitative intensity]] of the experience it[[ego|self]]. A slight pain that fills consciousness may involve less "mechanical work" than an intense [[sensation]] attended to distr[[action|act]]edly, but the former is **[[quality|qualitative]]ly greater**.

### The Mechanical Conception of the Self

The most dangerous [[extensity|extension]] of mechanism is the mechanical conception of the [[ego|self]]:

> [001-140] The associationist theory of psychic states leads to a mechanical conception of the self.

The [[association|associationist psychology]] of Bergson's time treated psychic states as discrete elements connected by external [[association|laws of association]]. This "leads to a mechanical conception of the [[ego|self]]" — the [[ego|self]] becomes a **collection of atoms** of consciousness, related only by external contiguity, rather than a living whole whose every state expresses its entire history.

> [003-278] …another and is thus led to a mechanical conception of the self. He will see in the self only a collection of psychic states…

The determinist "is led to a mechanical conception of the [[ego|self]]" — seeing in it "only a collection of psychic states." Once the [[ego|self]] is conceived mechanically — as a bundle of discrete elements subject to external determination — [[free-will]] becomes unintelligible. But this unintelligibility is not a discovery about the [[ego|self]]; it is an artif[[action|act]] of the mechanical model.

### Mechanical Explanation and Its Limits

Bergson identifies a pattern in mechanical explanation:

> [003-217] …is closely bound up with mechanical or rather kinetic theories of matter.

Physical determinism "is closely bound up with mechanical or rather kinetic theories of matter." The model of [[causality|causation]] that determinism applies to consciousness is borrowed from the physics of matter — it assumes that the relation between a motive and an [[action|act]] is of the same type as the relation between one physical state and another.

> [003-223] Wherever we succeed in giving a mechanical explanation, we observe a fairly close relation between the phenomena…

"Wherever we succeed in giving a mechanical explanation" — and Bergson does not deny these successes — the price is the elimination of **duration**. Mechanical explanation succeeds precisely to the extent that it treats time as a reversible parameter rather than as lived, irreversible becoming.

### The Mechanical Oscillation Model of Deliberation

Bergson diagnoses a mechanical picture at the heart of the free-will debate:

> [003-299] …deliberation is preceded by a kind of mechanical oscillation between two points…

[[deliberation|Deliberation]], in the mechanical picture, is "preceded by a kind of mechanical oscillation between two points" — the [[ego|self]] oscillates between alternatives like a pendulum, and the "stronger" motive wins. But this picture spatializes what is [[action|act]]ually a **[[dynamic|dynamic progress]]** in which the [[ego|self]] is "in a constant state of becoming." The oscillation model confuses the **symbol** for [[reality|the real]]ity.

> [003-302] …we give a mechanical explanation of a fact, and then substitute the explanation for the fact itself.

This is Bergson's fundamental diagnosis of the mechanical fallacy: "we give a mechanical explanation of a f[[action|act]], and then substitute the explanation for the f[[action|act]] it[[ego|self]]." The mechanical model is a **tool**; we mistake it for the **thing it[[ego|self]]**.

### Mechanical Symbolism

The mechanical mode of [[mind|thought]] operates through spatial symbols:

> [003-307] …Hence a symbolism of a mechanical kind, equally incapable of proving or disproving freedom.

A "symbolism of a mechanical kind" is "equally incapable of proving or disproving [[free-will|freedom]]" — because it has already translated the terms of the question into a language in which [[free-will|freedom]] cannot be expressed. The mechanical symbol treats the [[ego|self]] as a **system of forces**, and within that symbol, "[[free-will|freedom]]" can only mean an uncaused cause — a logical absurdity. The [[reality|real]] alternative is to abandon the mechanical symbol and return to the immediate data of consciousness.

### Mechanical Determination vs. Dynamic Relation

Bergson contrasts mechanical determination with dynamic relation:

> [003-392] …The wholly mechanical determination of two external phenomena by one another now assumes in our eyes the same form as the dynamic relation of our exertion of force to the act which springs from it…

Through [[endosmosis]], "the wholly mechanical determination of two external phenomena" comes to assume "the same form as the dynamic relation" of effort to [[action|act]] — and vice versa. The dynamic relation is **contaminated** by the mechanical form, and we end up [[thought|thinking]] of our own [[action|free action]]s as if they were cases of mechanical determination.

> [003-367] …the more the progress of mechanical explanations enables us to develop the system of necessary relations…

The "progress of mechanical explanations" — the genuine advance of physical science — "enables us to develop the system of necessary relations." But this system is valid only for the **spatialized** representation of [[reality|real]]ity, not for [[reality|real]]ity as lived. Bergson's point is not that mechanism is false but that it is **partial** — it captures the spatial dimension of [[reality|real]]ity at the cost of the temporal.

| Aspect of Mechanism | What It Does | Bergson's Response |
|--------------------|-------------|-------------------|
| Reduction to parts | Breaks conscious wholes into discrete elements | Conscious states interpenetrate; parts are abstr[[action|act]]ions |
| External determination | Relates elements by external laws | Inner relations are dynamic, [[quality|qualitative]] |
| Spatial symbolism | Represents [[becoming|process]] as static configuration | The symbol is not [[reality|the real]]ity |
| Reversibility | Treats time as a parameter | Duration is irreversible becoming |
| Substitution | Replaces lived experience with model | Return to immediate data of consciousness |

## Key Distinctions

### Mechanical vs. Dynamic

The [[dynamic]] is the [[quality|qualitative]], temporal mode of conscious life — [[becoming|process]], becoming, interpenetration. The mechanical is the [[quantity|quantitative]], spatial mode of material explanation — structure, external determination, separation. Bergson's philosophy does not deny the validity of the mechanical within its proper domain; it denies the **universality** of the mechanical and exposes the error of applying it to consciousness.

### Mechanical and Determinism

[[Determinism]] is, for Bergson, the metaphysical expression of the mechanical mode of [[mind|thought]]. Once the [[ego|self]] is conceived mechanically — as a collection of discrete states subject to external laws — determinism follows as a matter of logical consistency. But the mechanical conception is not forced on us by the f[[action|act]]s; it is a **choice** of representational framework, and a misleading one.

### Mechanical and Science

[[Science]], particularly mathematical physics, operates through mechanical explanations. Bergson's critique of mechanism is not a critique of science as such but of **scientism** — the metaphysical claim that mechanical explanation exhausts [[reality|real]]ity. Science is valid for matter; it becomes invalid when it claims to explain consciousness in material terms.
