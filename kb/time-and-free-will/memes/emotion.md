# Emotion

Emotion (*émotion*) in Bergson's analysis is the paradigmatic case of a **[[quality|qualitative]] psychic state** — one that appears to admit of degrees of intensity but which, on closer examination, reveals it[[ego|self]] as a succession of **different states altogether**.

The "increase" in an [[feeling|emotion]] is always the entry of new [[quality|qualitative]] elements that transform the whole, never the addition of a homogeneous quantity to an existing magnitude.

## Bergson's Argument

### Emotions as Self-Sufficient States

Bergson distinguishes [[feeling|emotion]]s from [[sensation]]s tied to external objects. Deep [[feeling|emotion]]s — joy, sorrow, [[feeling|aesthetic feeling]] — seem to be purely internal, making them the ideal test case for the thesis that [[intensity|psychic intensity]] is [[quality|qualitative]]:

> …certain states of the soul seem to us, rightly or wrongly, to be self-sufficient, such as deep joy or sorrow, a reflective passion or an aesthetic emotion. Pure intensity ought to be more easily definable in these simple cases, where no extensive element seems to be involved. [001-024]

If even these "pure" [[feeling|emotion]]s turn out to have no measurable intensity — if their apparent magnitude is [[reality|real]]ly a [[quality|qualitative]] transformation — then Bergson's thesis holds for the whole of psychic life.

### Joy and Sorrow: Progressive Qualitative Change

Bergson's analysis of joy and sorrow is a centerpiece of his argument. An [[feeling|emotion]] does not start small and grow by addition; it **transforms** the entire psychic field:

> Neither inner joy nor passion is an isolated inner state which at first occupies a corner of the soul and gradually spreads. At its lowest level it is very like a turning of our states of consciousness towards the future. [001-031]

> The emotions of joy and sorrow. Their successive stages correspond to qualitative changes in the whole of our psychic states. [001-031]

Joy at its lowest intensity is an **orientation** toward the future — [[idea|ideas]] and [[sensation]]s flow more rapidly, [[motion|movement]]s cost less effort. At higher intensities, the whole of consciousness is reorganized. Each stage is not "more joy" but a **different kind of joy**.

### The Aesthetic Emotion: New Elements Alter the Nature

The [[feeling|aesthetic feeling]]s provide the clearest demonstration that increasing intensity is [[reality|real]]ly [[change|qualitative change]]:

> …this progressive stepping in of new elements, which can be detected in the fundamental emotion and which seem to increase its magnitude, although in reality they do nothing more than alter its nature. [001-037]

When we say a work of art produces a "more intense" [[aesthetic|aesthetic emotion]], what we mean is that it introduces new elements — associations, resonances, perceptual nuances — that transform the quality of the feeling. The "intensity" is a metaphor for **richness of [[quality|qualitative]] content**.

### Stages in the Aesthetic Emotion

Bergson develops a subtle theory of aesthetic suggestion:

> It follows from this analysis that the feeling of the beautiful is no specific feeling, but that every feeling experienced by us will assume an aesthetic character, provided that it has been *suggested,* and not *caused.* [001-047]

The [[aesthetic|aesthetic emotion]] admits of **degrees of elevation** as well as intensity — from a feeling that barely interrupts our ordinary psychic flow, to one that draws our [[attention]] but leaves us aware of our surroundings, to one that completely engrosses us:

| Stage | Char[[action|act]]er | Relation to [[self|Self]] |
|-------|-----------|-----------------|
| Low | Scarcely breaks the texture of psychic phenomena | Peripheral |
| Middle | Draws [[attention]] but does not eclipse other states | Focused |
| High | Puts it[[ego|self]] in place of all other states, engrosses us | Total absorption |

### Violent Emotions and Muscular Tension

The "violent" [[feeling|emotion]]s — [[anger|rage]], [[desire|passion]]ate love, acute desire — seem to have a measurable intensity because of their **bodily accompaniment**:

> The intensity of these violent emotions is thus likely to be nothing but the muscular tension which accompanies them. [001-080]

[[darwin|Darwin]]'s description of the physiological symptoms of [[anger|rage]] — clenched fists, accelerated breathing, muscular rigidity — points to the same conclusion. What we call the "intensity" of an [[feeling|emotion]] is the **quantity of bodily surface** involved in the muscular response, not a magnitude of the [[feeling|emotion]] it[[ego|self]].

### The Richness of Emotion

Bergson acknowledges that most [[feeling|emotion]]s are not simple:

> …the greater number of emotions are instinct with a thousand sensations, feelings, and ideas… [001-048]

Each [[feeling|emotion]] is a **[[interpenetration|qualitative multiplicity]]** — an interpenetrating whole of [[sensation]]s, [[idea|ideas]], and tendencies that cannot be decomposed without destroying its char[[action|act]]er. This is why the artist, rather than the psychologist, best captures [[feeling|emotion]]al [[reality|real]]ity: the artist suggests the whole, while analysis dissects it into dead parts.

## Key Distinctions

### Emotion vs. Sensation

| Aspect | [[sensation|Sensation]] | Emotion |
|--------|-----------|---------|
| Locus | Tied to external cause or [[matter|body]] part | [[self|Self]]-sufficient, involves the [[self|whole self]] |
| Apparent intensity | From extent of [[body|organism]] affected | From [[quality|qualitative]] transformation of the whole |
| Example | Pressure on hand, light on eye | Joy, sorrow, [[feeling|aesthetic feeling]], [[anger|rage]] |

### Emotion and the Self

Emotions are not additions to a stable [[ego|self]]; they **transform the [[ego|self]]**. Each new [[feeling|emotion]]al state colors the entire psychic field in [[duration]], so that the [[ego|self]] after an [[feeling|emotion]] is [[quality|qualitative]]ly different from the [[ego|self]] before it. This is why determinism's attempt to predict [[feeling|emotion]]al responses from past states fails: the [[ego|self]] that responds is never the same [[ego|self]].

### Emotion and Intensity

The concept of [[feeling|emotion]]al intensity is, for Bergson, a con[[interpenetration|fusion]].

When we say an [[feeling|emotion]] is "more intense," we are registering one of three things: (a) the entry of new [[quality|qualitative]] elements that transform the [[feeling|emotion]]'s nature, (b) the extent of bodily involvement ([[tension|muscular tension]], organic [[sensation]]s), or (c) the degree to which the [[feeling|emotion]] monopolizes the whole of [[consciousness]]. None of these is a [[quantity|quantitative]] magnitude.
