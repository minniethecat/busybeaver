# Motion

Motion, in Bergson's *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]*, is analyzed through the lens of his central distinction between [[quality]] and [[quantity]]. Physical motion — the displacement of an object in [[space]] — is [[quantity|quantitative]], measurable, and infinitely divisible. But the **perception** of motion is [[quality|qualitative]], indivisible, and occurs in [[duration]]. The failure to distinguish these two senses of motion is, Bergson argues, one more instance of the spatialization of consciousness.

## Bergson's Argument

### The Perception of Motion as Indivisible Synthesis

> [001-024] …the perception of a movement or of an external…

When we perceive a movement, we do not perceive the infinity of points the moving object traverses. We perceive a **single, [[quality|qualitative]] [[action|act]]** — a synthesis that cannot be decomposed into parts. The moving object may trace a line in space, but the perception of its movement is not it[[ego|self]] spatial.

> [001-037] …detected in the fundamental emotion and which seem to increase it…

The perception of ease in motion is a global quality, not a [[magnitude|measurable quantity]]. When a movement feels "easy," this is not a [[rhythm|measure]]ment of effort expended but a **[[quality|qualitative]] char[[action|act]]er** of the whole experience.

### Motion and the Fundamental Emotion

> [001-025] …which make up the fundamental emotion.

Bergson connects the perception of motion to the "fundamental [[feeling|emotion]]" that underlies [[aesthetic|aesthetic experience]]. The [[emotion|feeling]] of grace in a dancer's movement is not analyzable into spatial components; it is a single [[quality|qualitative]] impression:

> [001-038] …the perception of ease in motion passes over into the pleasure…

The perception of ease in motion passes over into aesthetic pleasure — not as a separate effect but as a continuation of the same [[quality|qualitative]] [[action|act]].

### The Two Motions

| Aspect | Physical Motion | Perceived Motion |
|--------|----------------|-----------------|
| Nature | Quantitative (distance/time) | Qualitative (lived experience) |
| Divisibility | Infinitely divisible (Zeno's paradox) | Indivisible (single synthesis) |
| Measure | Speed, velocity | Grace, ease, effort |
| Domain | [[space]] | [[duration]] |

> [001-031] The emotions of joy and sorrow. Their successive…

The [[feeling|emotion]]s of joy and sorrow are themselves motions of consciousness — [[quality|qualitative]] movements in duration, not spatial displacements. Bergson's analysis of motion thus extends from physical movement to the **inner motions** of the [[ego|self]].

### Motion and the Marionette

> [001-040] …by means of which we set in motion this imaginary puppet…

Bergson uses the image of an "imaginary puppet" — the spatialized [[ego|self]] we construct for pr[[action|act]]ical purposes. We set this puppet in motion through a kind of physical sympathy, projecting spatial movements onto our inner life. But [[reality|the real]] [[ego|self]] moves [[quality|qualitative]]ly, in duration.

## Key Distinctions

### Motion in Space vs. Motion in Duration

In space, motion is a change of position measurable by distance and time. In duration, motion is a [[quality|qualitative]] passage — not from point A to point B but from one quality of experience to another. The motion of a [[mind|thought]], the motion of a [[emotion|feeling]] — these are [[reality|real]] motions, but they are not spatial.

### Zeno's Paradox

Zeno's paradox (the arrow never reaches its target because it must traverse infinitely many points) arises, Bergson would argue, from confusing **physical motion** (infinitely divisible in space) with the **perception of motion** (indivisible in duration). The paradox dissolves when we recognize that the [[action|act]] of perceiving motion is not a sum of point-perceptions.

## Historical Context

### The Greek Problem of Motion

From Zeno to Aristotle, Greek philosophy [[effort|struggle]]d with the problem of motion — how can something change and yet remain it[[ego|self]]? Bergson's solution is to relocate motion from space to duration: change is not a problem for a being whose very substance is temporal.
