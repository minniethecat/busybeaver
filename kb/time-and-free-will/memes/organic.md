# Organic

The organic (*l'organique*), in Bergson's *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]*, is the **antithesis of the mechanical**. An organic whole is one whose parts **interpenetrate** rather than remaining mutually external; each part bears the char[[action|act]]er of the whole, and the whole is more than the sum of its parts. This concept is the bridge between Bergson's analysis of [[consciousness]] (where states form an organic whole in [[duration]]) and his critique of [[psychophysics]] (which treats organic disturbances as if they were [[quantity|measurable magnitude]]s).

## Bergson's Argument

### Organic Disturbance and Emotion

> [002-004] Affective sensations and organic disturbance.

Bergson titles a key section "Affective [[sensation]]s and organic disturbance," signaling that the organic is the **medium of [[feeling|emotion]]**. An [[feeling|emotion]] is not a mental state floating free of the [[matter|body]]; it is "the conscious expression of an organic disturbance, the inward echo" of bodily changes. But — and this is crucial — the organic disturbance is not a **cause** of the [[feeling|emotion]]; it is the **bodily aspect** of a unified psychophysical event.

> [001-082] …inate, in short, all trace of organic disturbance, all tendency towards…

Bergson describes how we can abstr[[action|act]] from the organic dimension of [[feeling|emotion]] to focus on its "psychic" aspect, but this abstr[[action|act]]ion is artificial. In [[reality|real]]ity, the organic and the psychic are **inseparable phases** of a single [[becoming|process]]. The error of psychophysics is to treat the organic as the measurable, physical side and the psychic as an epiphenomenon — when both are aspects of an organic whole that cannot be decomposed.

> [002-005] …he conscious expression of an organic disturbance, the inward echo…

The "inward echo" metaphor captures Bergson's organicism: the psychic is not caused by the organic but **echoes** it — the two are in resonance, not in a causal chain. This is why measuring the organic (heart rate, [[tension|muscular tension]]) can never yield the intensity of the [[feeling|emotion]]: the organic is a [[quality|qualitative]] expression, not a [[quantity|quantitative]] cause.

### The Organic Whole of Consciousness

> [003-178] …to one another and forming an organic whole. But we are generally content…

Bergson's key claim: [[consciousness|conscious states]] are "forming an organic whole." They do not sit side by side like objects in [[space]]; they grow into one another, each permeated by the whole. This organic unity is the essence of [[duration]] — the past survives in the present, the whole is present in each part, and the future is genuinely open because organic wholes produce genuine novelty.

> [003-204] …the co-existing terms form an organic whole, they will never lead us…

When terms form an organic whole, "they will never lead us" to the kind of deterministic laws that apply to mechanical systems. An organic whole does not obey [[causality|causal law]]s because its parts are not mutually external items that could be linked by laws. The organic is the domain of **[[free-will|freedom]]** — not because it is lawless, but because its mode of connection is [[quality|qualitative]], not causal.

### Organic Elements and Sensation

> [003-111] …uence of the diversity of the organic elements which they affect, the…

Bergson notes that different [[sensation]]s affect different "organic elements." A sound affects the whole [[body|organism]] differently than a color does. This diversity is [[quality|qualitative]], not [[quantity|quantitative]] — it concerns the **kind** of organic involvement, not the measurable intensity. Psychophysics goes wrong by ignoring this [[quality|qualitative]] diversity and treating all organic involvement as a single measurable dimension.

### Organic Functions and the Self

> [003-273] …hey play the same part as our organic functions in relation to the self…

Bergson draws an analogy: just as "organic functions" (breathing, circulation) sustain the life of the [[matter|body]] without being the [[matter|body]] it[[ego|self]], so the superficial states of consciousness sustain the [[ego|self]]'s engagement with the world without being the [[ego|self]]. The deeper [[ego|self]] lies beneath these functions, as the living [[matter|body]] lies beneath its organic [[becoming|process]]es.

> [003-123] …nd the present states into an organic whole, as happens when we recall…

[[memory|Memory]] fuses "present states into an organic whole" — past and present interpenetrate, forming a unity that is not a sum. This organic [[interpenetration|fusion]] is what [[memory]] does: it does not store the past as a discrete item and retrieve it; it **carries** the past forward, letting it color and transform the present.

|----------------|-------------------|----------------------|
| Organic disturbance | Bodily aspect of [[feeling|emotion]] (inseparable from psychic side) | Measurable physiological change |
| Organic whole | Interpenetrating unity of [[consciousness|conscious states]] | Aggregation of discrete items |
| Organic elements | Qualitatively diverse bodily involvements | Quantitatively comparable magnitudes |

### Relaxing the Organic Functions

> [003-175] …, by relaxing the play of the organic functions, alters the communication…

When the "organic functions" relax — in reverie, in [[aesthetic|aesthetic experience]], in deep [[reflection]] — the "communication" between states changes. The spatializing, pr[[action|act]]ical orientation of consciousness is an organic function; when it relaxes, the deeper, durational char[[action|act]]er of consciousness emerges. This is why Bergson's method requires a kind of **relaxation** — not [[intelligence|intellect]]ual effort in the usual sense, but the suspension of the organic functions that sustain spatialization.

## Key Distinctions

### Organic vs. Mechanical

A mechanical system is an **aggregation** of mutually external parts linked by external laws. An organic system is a **unity** of interpenetrating parts linked by internal relations. Bergson's entire philosophy can be seen as the project of [[thought|thinking]] the organic without reducing it to the mechanical — [[thought|thinking]] consciousness, duration, and [[free-will|freedom]] in properly organic terms.

### Organic vs. Duration

[[Duration]] is the **temporal form** of organic unity. An organic whole is not static; it is a [[becoming|process]] unfolding in time. Duration is organic unity spread out in its temporal dimension — each moment grows out of the whole, just as each part of an [[body|organism]] expresses the whole.

### Organic and the Body

The [[body]] is the primary site of organic unity. Bergson's treatment of organic disturbance in [[feeling|emotion]] shows that the [[matter|body]] is not a machine housing a ghost but an **organic whole** with psychic and physical aspects that cannot be separated. This challenges both materialist reduction (the psychic is nothing but the organic) and dualist separation (the psychic is independent of the organic).

## Historical Context

Bergson's organicism draws on a long tradition — from Aristotle's *De Anima* (the soul as the form of an organic [[matter|body]]) through the Romantic re[[action|act]]ion against [[mechanical|mechanism]] (Schelling, Goethe) to the French [[soul|spirit]]ualist tradition (Maine de Biran, Ravaisson). Bergson's contribution is to give the organic a **temporal** interpretation: what makes a whole organic is not its static structure but its mode of existing in [[duration]]. This temporal organicism is Bergson's distinctive philosophical achievement.
