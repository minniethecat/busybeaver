# Illusion

## Overview / Definition

"Illusion" in *[[time|Time]] and [[free-will|Free Will]]* is not a casual error but a **structural tendency of [[consciousness|reflective consciousness]]** to mistake spatial representations for temporal [[reality|real]]ities. Bergson identifies "the two fundamental illusions of the [[consciousness|reflective consciousness]]" [003-319] and a third that arises from them [003-322]. These illusions are not mere mistakes to be corrected by better [[reason|reasoning]]; they are built into the structure of conceptual [[mind|thought]] it[[ego|self]] and can only be dispelled by a direct [[intuition]] of duration.

## Bergson's Argument

**The illusion of common sense.** Bergson identifies how common sense "strengthens the illusion" [001-060] regarding the nature of [[consciousness|conscious states]]. Similarly, "the same illusion of consciousness is likely to" [001-074] mislead us about the nature of intensity. These illusions are not arbitrary — they arise from the natural tendency of the mind to spatialize.

**The two fundamental illusions.** Bergson explicitly names "the two fundamental illusions of the [[consciousness|reflective consciousness]]" [003-319]: the con[[interpenetration|fusion]] of succession with simultaneity, and the con[[interpenetration|fusion]] of quality with quantity. These are the root errors from which other philosophical mistakes grow. "Now, in these two illusions themselves a third one is involved" [003-322] — the illusion that time can be treated as a [[homogeneity|homogeneous medium]].

**Illusion and habit.** Bergson compares illusion to the effects of aging on perception: "This is not a mere illusion; for if to-day's impression were absolutely identical with that of yesterday, what difference would there be between perceiving and recognizing?" [003-182]. The illusion of sameness across time has [[reality|real]] experiential consequences.

**Dispelling the last illusion.** Bergson holds that "imagination will dispel this last illusion" [003-141] — not rational argument but a shift in the mode of [[attention]]. "We should also understand the illusion which makes the one party think" [003-466] that determinism and [[will|free will]] are incompatible in the way they imagine. The "illusion through which we confuse succession" [003-467] with simultaneity is the deepest source of philosophical error.

**Gain through illusion.** Bergson notes that we are "going to gain by keeping up the illusion through which we make them share" [003-445] in spatial properties — a pragmatic acknowledgment that illusion has pr[[action|act]]ical utility even as it distorts theoretical [[mind|understanding]].
