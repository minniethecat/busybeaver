# Duration (durée)

Duration (*durée*) is Henri Bergson's most fundamental concept — the [[quality|qualitative]], heterogeneous, and continuous flow of conscious experience that cannot be divided into discrete units without distortion. It stands in opposition to the spatialized, mathematical [[idea|conception]] of time that Bergson argues has dominated philosophy and science.

## Definition

Bergson introduces duration in contrast to [[time|homogeneous time]]. While [[time|clock time]] can be divided into equal, measurable instants, duration is char[[action|act]]erized by the interpenetration of [[consciousness|conscious states]] — past, present, and future are not separate points but a continuous [[organic|organic whole]].

As Bergson writes in the Author's Preface:

> (P02-002) ...a previous confusion of duration with extensity, of succession with simultaneity, of quality with quantity...

This con[[interpenetration|fusion]], Bergson argues, is the root of many philosophical problems, including the [[will|free will]] debate.

## Duration vs. Spatialized Time

The key distinction Bergson draws is between two [[idea|conception]]s of time:

- **Homogeneous time**: [[time|Time]] as a measurable, divisible medium — the time of clocks, physics, and mathematics. This is time spatialized, treated as a fourth dimension of space.

- **Pure duration**: The [[succession|qualitative succession]] of [[consciousness|conscious states]] as [[action|act]]ually lived. In duration, states do not follow one another like points on a line; they interpenetrate, each colored by the previous and anticipating the next.

The "spatialization of time" is Bergson's diagnosis of why philosophy has [[effort|struggle]]d with problems of [[will|free will]]. When we project our inner experience onto space, we treat [[consciousness|conscious states]] as separable objects, which leads inevitably to determinism.

## Role in the Free Will Argument

Duration is the foundation of Bergson's argument for [[will|free will]]. He contends that determinism arises from treating [[consciousness|conscious states]] as if they were objects in space, with causal relations between them. When we recover the experience of duration — of consciousness as a living, interpenetrating whole — the problem of [[will|free will]] dissolves.

> (001-165) ...confusion with duration raises problem of freedom...

The free [[action|act]], for Bergson, is one that expresses the whole of the [[ego|self]] — the entire accumulated duration of one's conscious history — rather than being determined by any single motive or cause.

## Quality vs. Quantity

Duration is fundamentally [[quality|qualitative]], not [[quantity|quantitative]]. Unlike spatial magnitudes which can be added and divided, the moments of duration interpenetrate:

> (001-119) Change, but not duration, attributed to external things...

External objects change position in space, but only consciousness experiences duration — the [[quality|qualitative]] passage of time that cannot be reduced to mere change of position.
